# Niddah 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Zeira Hybrid

### Context & Zeira

We conclude the first cycle of *Shas* with the final week of *Masechet Niddah*, picking up from the last three words of 66a. At the close of that *daf*, we encountered the critical ruling of Rabbi Zeira — a statement that has reshaped nearly all of *Masechet Niddah* for contemporary practice. Rabbi Zeira's innovation creates a hybrid of *niddah* and *zavah*: even the tiniest drop of blood, regardless of what time of the month it appears, renders a woman a *zavah gedolah*. The hybrid borrows from the law of *niddah* the triggering mechanism — a single drop of blood suffices, rather than the three consecutive days required for a *zavah* — while importing from the law of *zavah* the consequence: seven clean days.

### One Drop = Zavah

This construction — one drop of blood producing a *zavah gedolah* status with its attendant seven clean days — is the practical foundation upon which all subsequent discussion in this *daf* rests. It is worth pausing to appreciate how striking this is: the classical *niddah* cycle required no clean days whatsoever beyond the fixed seven days of impurity, while the *zavah* cycle required three consecutive days of flow before the seven-clean-day obligation arose. Rabbi Zeira's ruling collapses this distinction entirely, imposing the most stringent consequence upon the most minimal act.

### Rav Pappa vs Rava

There is a significant exchange between Rav Pappa and Rava that we saw yesterday and will return to momentarily. Rav Pappa pressed Rava: how could one rule normative *halacha* without reckoning with Rabbi Zeira's statement? Rava initially resisted, characterizing Rabbi Zeira's position as mere *minhag* rather than binding law. Yet a moment later, Rava himself ruled regarding *dam chimud* — blood that may appear when a woman is first propositioned for marriage — and he, too, required seven clean days. So even as Rava pushed back against universalizing Rabbi Zeira's *chumra*, his own practical ruling appeared to concede the point.

### Dam Chimud

The concept of *dam chimud* is the concern that a woman who unexpectedly receives a marriage proposal may experience a spotting of blood, even if no blood is visible to her. Rava's ruling that such a situation triggers seven clean days reveals that by this point he has substantially accepted the framework of Rabbi Zeira — that the *zavah*'s seven-clean-day requirement attaches even in borderline or uncertain cases.

### Chimud Relevance

In practical terms, *dam chimud* has largely ceased to be relevant today, since the scenario it addresses — a genuine surprise proposal for marriage — is exceedingly rare. The responsa literature does contain interesting discussions of edge cases, such as when an arrangement for one woman falls through and a substitute is proposed: does the second proposal constitute a new cause for *dam chimud*, or is it subsumed under the original expectation? But these are largely theoretical questions.

### Mikvah Timing

One area where the concept does retain some practical echo is the widespread custom that a woman does not go to the *mikvah* more than four days before the wedding night. This practice is understood to connect, at least in part, to the concerns surrounding the proximity of the immersion to the occasion of *dam chimud* — ensuring that the immersion is sufficiently close in time to the wedding night.

### Separation Before Wedding

A related and counterintuitive point deserves mention. The widespread practice in many communities of the *chattan* and *kallah* not seeing each other for a week before the wedding actually runs directly contrary to the logic of *dam chimud*. The concern underlying *dam chimud* is precisely that surprise and heightened excitement surrounding marriage may produce bleeding. If so, one would expect the *halacha* to discourage creating a dramatic buildup of tension and anticipation in the days preceding the wedding night — not to institutionalize it through a formal separation. If anything, the couple should maintain a calm, natural relationship in the final days before the wedding to minimize any sudden emotional shock.

It is worth noting, as documented in the *Jewish Observer* many years ago, that this practice of pre-wedding separation has no authentic Jewish origin; it was adopted from non-Jewish custom. One could argue it is problematic for two independent reasons: first, it runs counter to the concern of *dam chimud*; second, it may constitute *chukot hagoyim*. Yet it has been absorbed into certain communities as a mark of *frumkeit* — a reminder of how practices can migrate across cultural boundaries and acquire religious valence they do not inherently possess.

## Chafifah Washing

### Neser & Chol

Turning to Daf 67 proper, Rava states: *isha lo tachof* — a woman should not wash her hair *lo beneser velo bechol*, neither with *neser* nor with *chol*. *Neser* is a kind of harsh cleanser that causes hair to fall out, creating tangles with neighboring hairs; *chol*, a type of sand used as a cleansing agent, causes the hair strands to stick to one another. The prohibition on these substances is thus not a rejection of the obligation to wash hair before immersion, but rather a specification of how to do it properly — using materials that actually cleanse without generating new problems.

### Chafifah Requirement

This ruling presupposes a well-known background: the *takkanah* of Ezra that women wash their hair before going to the *mikvah*. This practice is called *chafifah*, and it encompasses the washing and combing of hair in order to remove any possible *chatzitzot* — obstructions that would invalidate the immersion. The reason the emphasis falls specifically on hair is transparent: women tend to have long hair, and hair is the place where dirt, tangles, and external matter are most likely to accumulate and remain undetected.

There is a debate among the *rishonim* about whether the *takkanah* of *chafifah* extends beyond the hair to the entire body, or whether it was specifically limited to the hair. The emphasis in the *Gemara* on the hair-specific concern is suggestive. *Tosafot* maintains that strictly speaking there may be no rabbinic requirement to bathe the body at all; only the hair carries the formal obligation. The body must obviously be free of *chatzitzot*, but whether there is a mandated bathing of the whole body — as distinct from a mandated inspection — is a genuine question. That said, as we will see shortly, the *minhag* to bathe the entire body before immersion is well established and strongly recommended, even if its formal rabbinic basis is debated.

### Hot Water Requirement

Amemar said in the name of Rava: *isha lo tachof ela bechamim* — a woman should wash her hair only in hot water, not cold. Even water heated by the sun — *chamei chama* — is sufficient; it need not be intensely hot. But cold water is excluded.

### Hot Water Benefits

The reason cold water is inadequate is that it is *kariri* — cold — and as Rashi explains, it causes the hair to be stiff and hard, and therefore ineffective at dislodging dirt and obstructions. Hot water, by contrast, renders hair pliable and soft, enabling thorough cleaning. The parallel to ordinary experience is apt: hot water is simply more effective at removing dirt than cold. This is thus a practical specification of the *chafifah* requirement — not only must the right cleansers be used, but the water must be of sufficient temperature to actually accomplish the task.

## Body Cleansing

### Rinse Folds

Rava adds another ruling: *le'olam yilamed adam betoch beiso shetehei isha madicha beis kemateha bemayim* — a man should always instruct in his household that a woman rinse the folds of her body with water before going to the *mikvah*. The *beis kemateha* refers to the folds of skin on the body — the underarms, the abdominal folds, the area between the thighs — places where skin folds over itself and where dirt may collect. Rashi specifically emphasizes the underarms and pubic area. The instruction is that these folds be deliberately rinsed so that any accumulated dirt or obstruction is removed before immersion.

### Folds vs Hidden

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from a distinction between two categories: *beit hakematzim* (folds) and *beit hastamim* (hidden places). The *beit hastamim* refers to inner cavities of the body that are still open to the outside world — the mouth, the inside of the ear, the vagina — places that are internal but accessible. The *beit hakematzim* are the folds of skin on the exterior of the body. The *Gemara* states that neither of these requires water to actually enter: *ein tzrichim l'vias mayim* — you do not need the water of the *mikvah* to physically reach inside the mouth, nor does it need to penetrate beneath every fold of skin. The water must cover the naturally exposed surfaces of the body; it does not need to reach areas that are naturally concealed.

If that is so, one might ask: why must these places be rinsed at all? If water does not need to reach them, why does it matter whether they are clean?

### Rauy Levilah

The *Gemara* answers: *nehi d'vias mayim lo ba'inan, makom harauy l'vias mayim ba'inan* — it is true that water does not need to actually penetrate these areas, but they must be capable of receiving water. A *chatzitzah* in such a place still invalidates the *tevilah*, even though the water would not have reached there anyway.

The principle invoked here is drawn from Rabbi Zeira's ruling in a different context: *kol harauy l'vilah ein vilah me'akevet bo* — anything that is fit to be mixed does not require actual mixing. In the context of a *mincha* offering, the flour must be mixed with oil; but the *Gemara* rules that if one failed to mix it, the offering remains valid — provided the quantity was small enough that mixing was theoretically possible. The key criterion is fitness for the act, not the performance of the act itself. The inverse is also stated: *she'ein rauy l'vilah, vilah me'akevet bo* — if the mixture is too large to be practically mixed with oil at all, then the failure to mix is disqualifying, because the capacity for the act was never present.

Applying this principle to *mikvah*: the water need not actually reach the inner folds or cavities of the body, but those areas must be capable of receiving water — meaning there must be no *chatzitzah* blocking access. Hence the requirement to rinse these areas before immersion, so that the capacity for contact is unimpeded.

This generates an important ancillary question. If the requirement that these hidden areas be free of *chatzitzot* is derived from this principle of *rauy levilah*, some *rishonim* argue that this requirement is only *derabbanan*. Their reasoning: if it were a biblical obligation, one would need the *mikvah* water to actually penetrate those areas. Since we accept that the water need not reach there, the concern about *chatzitzot* in such places must be rabbinic in character. This has significant practical ramifications — for instance, in cases involving braces, earplugs used for medical ear infections, and similar situations. Both Rav Moshe Feinstein and Rav Ovadia Yosef address these cases, each with distinctive approaches.

Rav Ovadia Yosef leans toward ruling leniently in cases of medical necessity in *beit hastamim* areas, reasoning that the *chatzitzah* concern there is at most rabbinic, and adding additional grounds of *eino makpid* (discussed below). Rav Moshe develops a more elaborate conceptual distinction: he differentiates between a *chatzitzah* that is physically on the body and something that merely prevents water from reaching the body without itself being attached to the body. In the case of cotton packed into an ear with Vaseline to prevent water infiltration during immersion: the Vaseline, being water-soluble, does not constitute a *chatzitzah*; and the cotton, not being affixed to the body itself but merely resting within the ear canal, is not technically a *chatzitzah* in the strict sense — it is simply blocking water access. But since water does not need to reach the *beit hastamim* in the first place, the mere prevention of water entry is not disqualifying. This is a subtle but important conceptual move.

### Body Washing

The discussion of the folds raises a broader question: does the *takkanah* of *chafifah* require washing the entire body, or is it limited to the hair? One reading of Rava's statement — that the folds must be rinsed — implies that body washing is assumed and that Rava is adding only a specific detail about the folds. On this reading, the *chiddush* is that folds require particular attention even beyond a general body wash.

*Tosafot*, however, reads it differently: there may simply be no general rabbinic requirement to wash the entire body. The *Gemara* mentions the folds specifically because that is where dirt accumulates; the rest of the body surface, being smooth and exposed, does not require formal rabbinic washing. Most *rishonim* adopt a position closer to *Tosafot* — that the only formal rabbinic requirement is to wash the hair. The body must be inspected and free of *chatzitzot*, but formal bathing of the body may not have been mandated.

That said, the *minhag* to bathe the entire body before immersion is both widespread and practically sensible. If a woman bathes her entire body immediately before going to the *mikvah* and subsequently discovers a *chatzitzah*, she can presume it appeared after her immersion. If she did not bathe her body, she cannot make that assumption with confidence, and may be required to immerse again. This practical calculus — regardless of whether the formal obligation exists — strongly supports the practice of bathing the whole body.

## Chatzitzah Inner

### Chicken Bone

The *Gemara* cites a story: *maaseh bishifchato shel Rebbe* — an incident involving the maidservant of Rebbe. She immersed in the *mikvah*, emerged, and then discovered a small bone — apparently a chicken bone — lodged between her teeth. Rebbe ruled that she must perform another *tevilah*. This case serves as evidence that a *chatzitzah* in the *beit hastamim* — specifically, inside the mouth — does indeed invalidate the immersion. Even though water does not need to enter the gums, a *chatzitzah* within them is nonetheless problematic. If she had not eaten anything after her immersion, the inference is reasonable that the bone was present during the *tevilah* itself.

### After Tevilah

Rava generalizes from this case: if a woman immersed and upon emerging discovered something that could constitute a *chatzitzah*, the question of whether she must repeat the immersion depends on the timing of her *chafifah* relative to her immersion.

### Samuch Lachafifah

If she immersed *samuch l'chafifah* — immediately after her preparatory washing — then she need not repeat: *eina tzricha lachzor v'litvol*. The reasoning is that she thoroughly washed and checked right before entering the *mikvah*, so one may presume the *chatzitzah* appeared only afterward. If, however, she did not wash immediately before immersion — *v'im lav, tzricha lachzor v'litvol* — she must immerse again, because during the interval between the *chafifah* and the *mikvah* she may have handled food, been outside on a warm day, or otherwise acquired a *chatzitzah* without realizing it.

This ruling has important implications for the scope of *chafifah* itself. If *chafifah* refers only to washing the hair, then the *samuch l'chafifah* rule applies specifically to *chatzitzot* found in the hair. If it encompasses the entire body, it applies more broadly. This is one of the reasons *rishonim* emphasize that even if the formal *takkanah* of *chafifah* covered only the hair, the strong *minhag* to wash the entire body remains prudent: if a *chatzitzah* is found on the body after immersion, the woman needs to have done a general washing in order to claim it appeared post-immersion.

### Same Day Rule

An alternative formulation is cited: *im b'oto yom shechafefa tavlah, eina tzricha lachzor v'litvol* — if she performed her *chafifah* on the same day as the immersion, even if not immediately before, she need not repeat. If it was not the same day — *v'im lav, tzricha lachzor v'litvol* — she must repeat.

The *Gemara* asks what practical difference exists between the two versions. The answer is *nafka mina* in two directions: first, whether she must do her *chafifah* immediately before immersion; and second, the case of doing *chafifah* during the day and immersing at night. If she performed her *chafifah* at, say, half an hour before nightfall, and then immersed just after nightfall, the *chafifah* was immediately prior — but it was not on the same day. On the "same day" formulation, this would be a problem; on the "immediately before" formulation, it would not.

This question has direct practical relevance for the widespread custom in which a woman performs a thorough *chafifah* at home during the daytime — carefully bathing and combing in the unhurried comfort of her own home — and then does a brief check or comb-through at the *mikvah* itself just before immersion. This practice captures the benefits of both approaches: the thorough home washing provides quality, while the brief pre-immersion check at the *mikvah* ensures the *samuch* requirement is satisfied as well.

## Mikvah Rules

### No Vessels

Rava rules: *isha lo ta'amod al gabei klei cheres v'titvol* — a woman should not stand on top of pottery vessels inside the *mikvah* while immersing.

### Bathhouse Feel

Rav Kahana initially proposed that the reason for this prohibition is *mishum gezeiras merchatzos* — a rabbinic concern that it will make the *mikvah* feel like a bathhouse. Bathhouses, which use drawn water (*she'uvim*) and are typically not valid *mikvaot*, contain stools, platforms, and furniture. If a woman stands on upturned vessels in a *mikvah*, it begins to resemble a bathhouse, and one might eventually come to think that a bathhouse itself is a valid substitute. Rav Kahana then reasoned that if this is the concern, a simple wooden plank — *silt'fa* — which does not look like furniture, should be permitted.

Rav Chama from Neharde'a disagrees with Rav Kahana's reasoning. He maintains the actual reason is *mishum di'vu't* — fear. Standing on a vessel inside the *mikvah* is precarious, and the woman's anxiety about tipping over will prevent her from immersing properly. If that is the reason, then a plank of wood presents the same difficulty: it too is unstable, and she will be similarly afraid. Both are therefore prohibited.

### Port Concern

Rav Shaman bar Abba rules that a woman should not immerse in a *namal* — a port. Rashi explains the concern as follows: even if the woman inspects herself upon emerging and finds no mud, it is possible that mud adhered to her body during immersion and was rubbed off as she moved through the water or walked out, meaning the *chatzitzah* was present during the actual *tevilah*. *Tosafot* questions whether this explanation belongs in the original text of the *Gemara*, and suggests instead that the problem at a port is the presence of people nearby: the woman will feel embarrassed and rush, and therefore will not immerse properly.

### Seasonal Mikvaot

The father of Shmuel made *mikvaot* for his daughters in *Nisan* and prepared mats for them in *Tishrei*. The mats in *Tishrei* were provided because his daughters would immerse in a river, and Rashi explains the mats were to prevent mud from accumulating on their feet (a potential *chatzitzah*), while *Tosafot* understands the mats as privacy screens.

### Rainwater Problem

The reason he constructed actual *mikvaot* in *Nisan* is more significant. In *Nisan*, the spring thaw brings large quantities of rainwater and snowmelt into rivers. Rivers are valid for immersion as naturally flowing bodies of water — *mayim chayim*. However, if the majority of the water in a river at a given moment consists not of natural spring-fed river water but of rainwater and snowmelt, the river loses its status as a naturally running body of water, because most of its water is not inherently flowing. Therefore, during *Nisan*, one cannot rely on the river, and a separate *mikvah* must be constructed.

Rashi's explanation is that the problem is the loss of flowing-water status when rainwater predominates. However, a Geonic interpretation reads the issue differently: the problem is not the loss of *running water* status but the loss of *mayim chayim* — live water — status. The Torah explicitly requires a *zav* to immerse in *mayim chayim*; some *Geonim* extended this requirement to a *zavah* as well. If all women are now treated as *safek zavot*, they would require *mayim chayim*, meaning only natural springs or rivers — not man-made *mikvaot* — would suffice.

Rashi rejects this Geonic reading explicitly. If it were correct, constructing artificial *mikvaot* would not solve the problem — the solution would need to provide *mayim chayim*. One possible response is that the father of Shmuel dug wells, which are indeed *mayim chayim*. But all *rishonim* emphatically reject the Geonic position. A *zavah* does not require *mayim chayim*, and the entire edifice of man-made *mikvaot* — which has sustained Jewish family purity law throughout the centuries — rests on this rejection. It is important to be aware, however, that this alternative view existed and was explicitly considered and dismissed.

## Food Handling

### Cooked Food

Rav Gidel in the name of Rav rules: if a woman gave some cooked food to her son immediately before going to the *mikvah*, the *tevilah* is not valid — *lo alsah lah tvilah*.

### Invisible Food

The woman might protest that she inspected herself and found no food on her body. The *Gemara* responds: *eimar b'ardaguni nafal* — perhaps it fell off as she rubbed her hands together or as she entered the water. The food may have been on her during the actual immersion and washed off in the process of emerging, leaving no trace. The practical implication is clear: even relatively innocuous activities — handling sticky or moist foods — in close proximity to immersion create a concern that residue remained on the body at the moment of *tevilah*, even if no *chatzitzah* is visible afterward. This reinforces the importance of washing and checking immediately before immersion.

## Specific Chatzitzot

### Scab Timeline

Ami bar Abba rules regarding a scab over a wound: *ad tlata yomei lo havei* — for the first three days, it is not a *chatzitzah*, because the scab remains soft and is considered integral to the wound and the skin beneath it. *Mikan v'eilach havei* — from three days onward, when the scab hardens and extends beyond the boundaries of the wound, it is a *chatzitzah* and must be removed before immersion.

### Eye Mucus

Mar Ukva rules regarding *lifluf she'ba'ayin* — the mucous discharge found in the corners of the eyes. If it is *lach* — moist — it is *eino chotzetz*, not a *chatzitzah*, because it remains fluid and permeable. If it is *yavesh* — dry — it is a *chatzitzah*. When does it qualify as dry? From the moment it begins to turn greenish in color, *mishe'matchil le'yarek*.

### Eye Makeup

Shmuel rules that *kachol* — a substance applied to the eyes as both cosmetic and medicinal salve — is not a *chatzitzah* when applied inside the eye, because the interior of the eye remains moist and the substance does not form a barrier. When applied on the outside of the eye, however, it is a *chatzitzah*, because it dries and hardens on the surface. An exception applies if the woman's eyelids are constantly fluttering: in that case, even the external application remains loose and powdery, and therefore does not constitute a *chatzitzah*.

### Eye Position

Rabbi Yochanan rules: if a woman opens her eyes excessively wide or squeezes them shut too tightly during immersion, the *tevilah* is invalid — *lo alta la tvilah*. The reason is that artificially distorting the position of the eyelids causes skin areas that are normally exposed to become folded over, while areas that are normally concealed become unnaturally exposed. The *mikvah* water must contact what is naturally exposed; it need not reach what is naturally concealed. But if areas are artificially caused to be concealed by extreme positioning, and the water therefore did not reach them, this is problematic.

### Natural Posture

Reish Lakish states the general principle: *ha'isha lo titvol ela k'derech gdilata* — a woman should immerse only in her natural posture. She should not contort her body in an attempt to expose more of the fold areas to the water, because doing so will simultaneously cause other areas to become unnaturally concealed.

### Negaim Analogy

This principle is grounded in an analogy to the laws of *nega'im* — *tzara'at*. The *pasuk* states that the *kohen* examines a *nega* *lechol mareh einei hakohen* — to the full extent of the *kohen*'s vision. The *halacha* of *tzara'at* distinguishes between areas that are naturally exposed and areas that are naturally concealed: a mark in a normally hidden area does not require the same treatment. The standard for "naturally exposed" is determined by typical body positions during ordinary activities.

For a man, the examples given are hoeing (*ader*) and picking olives (*mosek zeitim*) — activities that involve natural extension of the limbs without excessive stretching. For a woman, the examples are knitting (*oreget*) and nursing her child (*menika et b'nah*). Rashi explains that the two female examples address different areas: knitting speaks to the degree of exposure of the underarms when the arms are slightly extended, while nursing in a squatting position speaks to exposure of the side and lower body. The upshot is that the posture during immersion should mirror the natural positions of everyday life, no more and no less.

After this extended catalogue of specific *chatzitzah* laws, the *Gemara* contains a line — cited in the version of *Tosafot* and attributed in the name of Rabbeinu Chananel — stating that the *halacha* does not follow all of these rulings as stated: *v'leit hilchasa k'chol hanei shma'atata*. These stringencies, according to this tradition, were stated in the context of *taharot* — the additional strictures observed when handling *terumah* or similar sacred matters — and do not apply to the ordinary *mikvah* immersion of a *niddah*. This is a significant qualification, as it places the entire foregoing discussion in a different halachic register and raises important questions about which of these rulings are actually *pasken* for standard practice.

## Rov & Makpid

### Knotted Hair

Rabbah bar bar Chana rules: *nima achas k'shura chotzetza* — one hair knotted upon itself is a *chatzitzah*. Although the body ordinarily cannot be a *chatzitzah* upon itself — a principle that also bears on questions like whether overgrown nails or hair constitute a *chatzitzah* (they do not, because they are part of the body) — a knotted hair is different: water genuinely cannot penetrate the interior of a tight knot. The problem is therefore not precisely a body-upon-body *chatzitzah* in the classical sense, but a practical reality that the knot creates an impenetrable space.

Three hairs knotted together — *shalosh* — is *eino chotzetza*, not a *chatzitzah*, because three hairs together cannot form a sufficiently tight knot. As for two hairs — *shtayim* — the ruling is uncertain: *eini yodeia*.

### Single Hair

Rabbi Yochanan is more lenient: *ein lanu ela achas* — only a single knotted hair is a problem. Anything beyond one hair does not constitute a *chatzitzah*.

### Biblical Chatzitzah

Rav Yitzchak states the foundational principle: *devar Torah rubo hamakpid alav chotzetz* — biblically, a *chatzitzah* requires two elements: it must cover the majority of the body, and the person must be *makpid* — must object to its presence, not wanting it on the body. Both conditions must be present simultaneously. A biblical *chatzitzah* is therefore nearly impossible to achieve in ordinary circumstances. What could cover the majority of the body while also being something the person objects to? Perhaps falling into a vat of adhesive material. The practical point is that virtually all real-world *chatzitzah* concerns are rabbinic.

### Rabbinic Safeguard

The rabbis extended the prohibition in two directions. First: *rubo she'eino makpid* — a majority covering of the body even without *makpid* is rabbinically prohibited, because it resembles the biblical case too closely (the only missing element being the *makpid*). Second: *mi'uto hamakpid* — a minority coverage where the person is *makpid* is also rabbinically prohibited, because the *makpid* element is present, which is the more subjectively significant of the two criteria.

### Minority Exception

The one case that escapes rabbinic prohibition is *mi'uto she'eino makpid* — a minority coverage where the person does not object. This is not a *chatzitzah* even rabbinically. The *Gemara* asks: should we not extend the prohibition to this case as well, as a *gezeira* against the *mi'uto hamakpid* case? The answer is *hi gufa gezeira* — the *mi'uto hamakpid* rule is itself already a rabbinic safeguard. To then build a further safeguard upon a safeguard — *gezeira legezeira* — is not done, at least in this context. And so *mi'uto she'eino makpid* remains permissible.

### Makpid Definition

This brings us to the crucial practical question: what constitutes *makpid*? The *makpid* criterion is subjective: something that a person genuinely objects to and wishes were not on the body. Something one always wears and considers part of one's regular state — a wedding ring worn continuously, for instance — is clearly *eino makpid* (not *makpid*). But many real cases fall in between. A cast on a broken arm: one wants it on now for medical necessity, but in an ideal world one would not want it. Braces: one wishes every minute they were off, yet one has them placed deliberately for a future benefit. Is such ambivalence *makpid* or not?

### Medical Applications

The practical stakes of this question are enormous. Rav Ovadia Yosef takes a relatively lenient approach: items that are medically necessary are generally considered *eino makpid*, because the person genuinely wants them on for therapeutic reasons. He applies this to casts, and with additional reasoning about *beit hastamim*, to devices placed in the ear for medical reasons. Rav Moshe Feinstein is more circumspect, reasoning that because at some ideal level the person would prefer not to have the item — for instance, wishing the cast were already off — this may constitute *makpid*, and the issue therefore warrants case-by-case analysis.

Nail polish presents its own interesting variation. Some *poskim* require its removal before immersion. Others permit it provided it is intact and freshly applied, because a woman who applies nail polish and leaves it smooth is clearly *eino makpid*. If the nail polish is chipped, however, she is presumably *makpid* — she would prefer to either have it whole or have it off — and in that case it is a *chatzitzah*. There is a logic to the position that freshly reapplied nail polish, which the woman clearly wants in that state, is not a *chatzitzah* even if it covers a majority of the nails.

## Hair Rov

### Hair Calculation

A distinctive question arises regarding hair in the context of *rov*. Some *rishonim* hold that hair is evaluated separately from the rest of the body for purposes of *chatzitzah* analysis. Rather than asking whether the *chatzitzah* covers a majority of the entire body, one asks whether it covers a majority of the hair. If the majority of the hair is affected — even without *makpid* — it may be rabbinically prohibited, because of the particular emphasis the tradition places on the hair and its vulnerability to *chatzitzot*. This would explain why a single knotted hair can be problematic: on the body-wide *rov* analysis, one hair is clearly a minority and *eino makpid*, yet on the hair-specific analysis, if the majority of hairs are involved, the calculus changes.

### Braids & Dye

This framework applies to braided hair and to hair dye. Tightly braided hair may involve a *rov* of the hair itself and thus warrant concern. Hair dye, however, is generally treated as permissible by most *poskim*, on the grounds that dye is absorbed into the hair shaft itself and does not sit as a surface layer upon the hair; it therefore does not constitute a *chatzitzah* on the hair. This is the more common and accepted ruling.

## Tevilah Timing

### Night Requirement

Rav rules: *niddah bizmanah eina tovelet ela balaylah* — a *niddah* immersing at her proper time can only do so at night. This follows directly from the structure of classical *niddah* law: the woman is *tamei* throughout day seven and becomes permitted to immerse only when night falls, marking the beginning of the eighth day.

### Late Immersion

*She-lo bizmanah* — if she is immersing late, past the seventh night, going on day eight or later — *tovelet bein bi-yom bein bi-layla*: she may immerse either during the day or at night. Having missed her window, she is no longer constrained to nighttime.

### Serach Bita

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *bein bizmanah bein she-lo bizmanah, eina tovelet ela ba-layla* — whether at the proper time or late, she may only immerse at night. The reason is *mishum serach bitah* — because of the daughter's following of her mother. This is a concern about the transmission of practice across generations. In an era when daughters learned *hilchos niddah* by observing their mothers' practice — rather than through formal instruction — a daughter who observed her mother immersing during the daytime would remember and replicate that practice. But she might go on day seven, which under the original *niddah* law is categorically prohibited, as the woman remains *tamei* through day seven and cannot immerse until nightfall.

### Imitation Risk

The mechanism of *serach bitah* reflects a broader principle of *lo palug* — the rabbis do not make fine-grained distinctions when establishing protective rulings. Even though the specific woman immersing on day eight is herself not transgressing anything, a blanket rule is established for all cases to prevent the scenario in which an imitating daughter goes on day seven. The *Gemara* adds that even Rav, who had originally permitted daytime immersion when late, reversed his position: *af Rav hadar beih*, as taught in the name of Rav by Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi, that even when not at the proper time, immersion is only at night, *mishum serach bitah*.

## Day Immersion

### Rav Idi Lions

Despite the general rule, local rabbinic authorities permitted daytime immersion on day eight in the face of genuine danger. Rav Idi in Narash permitted *tevilah* during the day of the eighth — not day seven, which remains biblically prohibited — *mishum aryavata*, because of lions that roamed at night and made nighttime travel to the *mikvah* dangerous.

### Rav Acha Robbers

Rav Acha bar Yaakov in Papunya permitted daytime immersion on day eight *mishum ganvi* — because of robbers and other dangerous individuals who made nighttime passage unsafe.

### Rav Yehuda Cold

Rav Yehuda in Pumbedita permitted daytime immersion on day eight *mishum tzina* — because of extreme cold at night that posed a real danger to women traveling to the *mikvah*.

### Rava City Guard

Rava in Mechuza permitted daytime immersion on day eight *mishum avulei* — understood by some to mean the city guards, who restricted movement after certain hours, preventing women from reaching the *mikvah* at night or returning home safely afterward.

The pattern is clear: the rabbinic prohibition against daytime immersion is waived in the face of concrete, community-specific dangers. Each authority made a *takkanah* for his locale, permitting day eight immersion in the daytime, but not day seven — because the prohibition against day seven immersion (in the classical framework) is biblical, not merely rabbinic.

## Universal Zavah

### Safek Zavot

Rav Pappa now returns to challenge Rava (or Abaye), and here we see him again playing the role he played at the outset — pushing to universalize Rabbi Zeira's *chumra*. His formulation is telling: *hashta, kulhu safek zavot shavinhhu rabbanan* — nowadays, the rabbis have declared all women to be *safek zavot*, possible *zavot*. He no longer frames this as the voluntary stringency that *benot Yisrael* took upon themselves; it has become a universalized rabbinic status. It is Rav Pappa who is credited with this crucial move, transforming Rabbi Zeira's *chumra* from a personal or communal stringency into a binding rabbinic framework applying to all Jewish women.

### Day Seven Clean

Given that all women are now treated as *safek zavot*, they observe seven clean days. And a *zavah* — unlike a *niddah* in the classical sense — may in principle immerse on day seven of her clean days, by virtue of the principle *miktzat hayom ke-kulo*: part of the day counts as the whole. So one might think that a woman could immerse during the daytime of the seventh clean day.

### Retroactive Bleeding

Rav Pappa rejects this, however, *mishum de-Rabbi Shimon* — because of a ruling of Rabbi Shimon cited in a *baraita*. The relevant verse states *achar tithar* — afterwards she shall become *tahorah*. Rabbi Shimon interprets *achar* to mean: after she has gone to the *mikvah*, which would permit immersion on day seven of the clean days — since going to the *mikvah* is itself the concluding act. But the rabbis ruled that one should not do so. Why? *Shema tavo lidei safek* — lest she encounter doubt. The concern is the following: if she immerses on day seven and subsequently has marital relations, and then menstrual bleeding appears later on that same day seven, the bleeding retroactively uproots the seven clean days — meaning she will have had relations while she was a *zavah*, which is prohibited. To prevent this scenario, the rabbis prohibited immersion on day seven entirely.

### Night Seven

The practical result is that contemporary women immerse at the night of the seventh clean day — the night between day seven and day eight. The conceptual framing of this practice has shifted significantly from how the classical *niddah* law was understood. In the original framework, a *niddah* was *tamei* through day seven and could only immerse at nightfall, marking the beginning of day eight. In the post-Rabbi Zeira framework, women are *zavot* waiting seven clean days, and as *zavot* they could theoretically immerse on day seven — but the rabbinic prohibition arising from the concern of retroactive bleeding pushes the immersion to the night of day seven. The practical destination is the same, but the legal journey that leads there is entirely different.

### Rabbeinu Tam

This analysis opens a significant contemporary question. When the *Gemara* earlier discussed permitting daytime immersion in extenuating circumstances, the alternatives offered were always day eight — never day seven. That was because in the classical *niddah* framework, day seven immersion is biblically invalid. But in the post-Rabbi Zeira framework, where all women are *zavot* keeping seven clean days, the prohibition against day seven immersion is rabbinic — arising from the concern of retroactive bleeding — not biblical. In extenuating circumstances, might one therefore permit immersion on day seven itself?

Rabbeinu Tam adds a further dimension: since the concern underlying the prohibition is specifically that she might have relations on day seven and then bleed, what if she immerses on day seven in such a way that she will not be alone with her husband — not in *yichud* — until after nightfall? If the risk of relations that same day is eliminated, perhaps the rabbinic prohibition does not apply. This leads to important halachic discussions in the *poskim* about whether a woman may immerse on day seven in circumstances where marital relations before nightfall are genuinely precluded. These questions remain live and practically significant.
