# Gittin 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Perek Hasholeach Intro

### Perek Overview

With Daf 32 we begin the fourth *perek* of Masechet Gittin, *Hasholeach*. While the *perek* opens with a discussion of *get*, what follows it moves into a range of topics that at first glance might appear unrelated to the laws of divorce. The unifying theme, however, is *tikkun olam* — the betterment of the world — a concept that motivates a series of rabbinic enactments throughout this *perek* and the ones that follow. Understanding what precisely is meant by *tikkun olam* here is itself one of the central tasks ahead of us.

The *perek* is also significant because it demonstrates the scope of rabbinic power. We will see how the rabbis could use their authority to shape, and in certain circumstances even to contravene, *d'Oraita* law. This is not a digression; it is the very point.

### Core Topics Preview

Beyond the question of *get*, the *perek* introduces core conceptual issues in a number of distinct areas. The most famous is *pruzbul*, the mechanism by which Hillel addressed the annulment of debts on *shemita*. To appreciate *pruzbul* properly, one must understand the underlying mechanics of how debts function during the *shemita* year — and this *perek* gives us that opportunity. Cases involving slaves and other varied and foundational topics will also arise, making this *perek* one of the richest in the masechet.

## Annulling Get Mishnah

### Get Nullification Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, וְהִגִּיעַ בַּשָּׁלִיחַ אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁלַח אַחֲרָיו שָׁלִיחַ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״גֵּט שֶׁנָּתַתִּי לְךָ, בָּטֵל הוּא״ – הֲרֵי זֶה בָּטֵל. קִידֵּם אֵצֶל אִשְׁתּוֹ אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁלַח אֶצְלָהּ שָׁלִיחַ, וְאָמַר לָהּ: ״גֵּט שֶׁשָּׁלַחְתִּי לִךְ, בָּטֵל הוּא״ – הֲרֵי זֶה בָּטֵל. אִם מִשֶּׁהִגִּיעַ גֵּט לְיָדָהּ – שׁוּב אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְבַטְּלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who sends a bill of divorce to his wife** with an agent, **and he reached the agent, or where he sent** another **agent after him, and he said to** the agent delivering the bill of divorce: **The bill of divorce that I gave you, it is void,** then **this** bill of divorce **is hereby void.** Similarly, if the husband reached **his wife before** the bill of divorce reached her, **or** in a case **where he sent an agent to her, and he said,** or had the agent say, **to** his wife: **The bill of divorce that I sent to you, it is void,** then **this** bill of divorce **is hereby void.** However, **if** he stated this **once the bill of divorce had entered her possession, he can no longer render it void,** as the divorce had already taken effect.

The *Mishnah* opens at the top of daf 32: *Hasholeach get l'ishto* — a man sends a *get* to his wife. We are still within the framework of *shlichut* established in the first *perek*, where the mechanics of delivering a *get* through an agent were laid out. What is new here, and immediately striking, is the concept of *bitul* — annulling the *get*.

When we hear that a husband can annul the *get* he sent, this demands some explanation. What does it mean to annul a *get*? One could straightforwardly annul the *shlichut* — tell the agent that he is no longer acting on your behalf — but the *Mishnah*'s emphasis seems to be on something more: changing the very status of this document, this piece of paper.

Given all that has been emphasized until this point about *lishmah* — that the *sofer* must write with the specific intent and under the direction of the husband, that the *get* must be a full and genuine expression of the husband's will — a certain logic emerges. The *get* is not merely a legal document the way an ordinary *shtar* might be; it is an expression of the husband's will. The text *harei at muteret lechol adam* is the verbal crystallization of that will. If the husband genuinely and formally retracts that will before the *get* has taken effect, does the document not cease to be what it was? Does it not become merely a piece of paper?

This idea is sometimes expressed in the *acharonim* literature in terms of the husband being *mevatel* the *lishmah* of the *get*. That formulation requires a moment's unpacking. The *lishmah* — the intent — is a fact; it was written with proper names and proper intent. But what it really means is that the *get* is an expression of the husband's ongoing will. The *acharonim* are saying: if that will is genuinely and formally retracted, the document no longer embodies it.

Consider the contrast with other *shtarot*. A *shtar kiddushin* — *harei at mekudeshet li* — is also an expression of one's will, and one might ask whether the same logic applies there. But a *shtar mecher* — *sadi mechurah lach* or *sadi netunah lach* — does not require *lishmah* in the same way. The *sofer* could theoretically pre-fill such a document, and the prohibition on pre-filling a *get* is by rabbinic *gezeirah* specific to *Gittin*. It is the *get* that demands this heightened sense of *lishmah* as an expression of the husband's will, and it is for that very reason that there seems to be this possibility of retracting it.

The *Mishnah* assumes, at least on its surface, that a verbal annulment suffices. One need not write a formal document of revocation. The verbal withdrawal of will, communicated to the right parties, is enough. The mechanics of how this works, and its limits, will be developed throughout the *Gemara*.

### Four Annulment Scenarios

The *Mishnah* describes four distinct scenarios of annulment. In the first, the husband himself catches up with the *shaliach* — *v'higia bashaliach* — and tells him: *get shenatati lecha batel, harei hu batel* — the *get* I gave you is annulled, behold it is annulled. In the second scenario, *o sheshalach acharav shaliach* — he sends a second *shaliach* after the first — and this second agent conveys the message of annulment to the first.

In the third scenario, *kadam etzlo ishto* — the husband races ahead and gets to his wife before the *shaliach* does — and tells her directly: *get sheshalachti lach batel* — the *get* I sent you is annulled, before she has received it. In the fourth, *o sheshalach etzlah shaliach* — he sends a second *shaliach* directly to his wife — and that second agent conveys the annulment to her before the *get* arrives.

All four scenarios are presented as valid forms of *bitul*. The *Gemara* will examine each in turn to explain why each one needed to be stated explicitly, since each one might have been thought ineffective for a different reason.

### Cutoff Point Rule

Before turning to the *Gemara*'s analysis of those scenarios, the *Mishnah* states the obvious but essential limit: *im meshehigia get leyadah, shuv eino yachol levatlo* — once the *get* has been placed in the wife's hand, he can no longer annul it. She is divorced. The document has taken effect and its work is done.

### Rabban Gamliel Takana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה בֵּית דִּין מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר וּמְבַטְּלוֹ, הִתְקִין רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הַזָּקֵן שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ עוֹשִׂין כֵּן, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna relates that **initially,** a husband who wished to render the bill of divorce void **would convene a court elsewhere and render** the bill of divorce **void** in the presence of the court before it reached his wife. **Rabban Gamliel the Elder instituted** an ordinance **that one should not do this, for the betterment of the world.** The Gemara will explain what this means.

The *Mishnah* then records that *barishonah* — originally, in the earlier practice — a husband could convene a *beis din* in his own hometown and annul the *get* there, without notifying the *shaliach* or the wife. The *shaliach* would continue on his way thinking the *get* was valid; the wife would receive it thinking she was divorced; and no one in the relevant location would have any reason to doubt it — while back home, the husband and his small *beis din* knew otherwise.

The problem this creates is obvious. *Hitkin Rabban Gamliel HaZaken shelo yehu osin ken mipnei tikkun olam* — Rabban Gamliel the Elder enacted a *takana* that this practice is forbidden, for the sake of *tikkun olam*. A woman who believed herself divorced and remarried on the basis of a *get* that had been secretly annulled would be in a catastrophic halachic situation. The *takana* therefore requires that any annulment be communicated to the relevant parties — the *shaliach* or the wife — so that they are aware of it and the *get* cannot be acted upon in error.

The *Gemara* will raise the question: if someone defied Rabban Gamliel's *takana* and annulled the *get* secretly before a hidden *beis din*, is the *bitul* nonetheless effective — *batel o eino mevutal* — or does violating the *takana* invalidate the annulment itself?

## Gemara Analysis Early

### Higia Intentionality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ ״הִגִּיעוֹ״ לָא קָתָנֵי, אֶלָּא ״הִגִּיעַ״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ מִמֵּילָא, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן לְצַעוֹרַהּ הוּא דְּקָא מִיכַּוֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that if one sends a bill of divorce with an agent and then meets the agent and renders void the bill of divorce in his presence, then it is void. The Gemara points out: The mishna **does not teach: He reached** the agent after pursuing **him; rather: He reached** the agent, meaning **and even** if he reached him **incidentally,** without intent, he renders the bill of divorce void with his statement. **And we do not say** that in that case **he intends** only **to vex** his wife and does not actually intend to render the bill of divorce void.

The *Gemara* opens by noting that the *Mishnah* does not use the plural *higu* — they reached — but rather the singular *higia* — he reached. This is a reading about intentionality. *Higia* suggests that the husband simply happened to encounter the *shaliach*, perhaps coincidentally, without necessarily having set out to catch him. The *Gemara* raises the question of whether such an unintentional encounter could support a valid *bitul*. One might reason that since the husband was not focused on annulling the *get* — he just happened upon the *shaliach* — perhaps his subsequent words of *bitul* were not serious, or perhaps we should be concerned that he was not genuinely retracting his will but rather seizing an opportunity. The *Gemara* teaches: *afielu mimaila* — even if the encounter was unintentional — the *bitul* is valid. We do not say that the husband was acting on a whim or merely exploiting the moment to cause his wife difficulty.

### Second Shaliach Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁלַח אַחֲרָיו שָׁלִיחַ״ – לְמָה לִי? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לָא אַלִּימָא שְׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ דְּבָתְרָא מִשְּׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ דְּקַמָּא – דִּלְבַטְּלֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the mishna to state that the bill of divorce is void when he reached the agent, **or** in a case **where he sent** another **agent after him?** The legal status of a person's agent is like that of himself, so it seems obvious that just as the husband can nullify the agency of the first agent, so too can the second agent nullify the agency of the first agent. The Gemara answers: This principle was stated **lest you say** that **the agency of the latter,** the second agent, **is not stronger than the agency of the former,** and **that** the latter agent cannot **nullify** the agency of the first agent and only the husband can nullify it. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that the second agent can nullify the agency of the first agent.

The second scenario — sending a second *shaliach* — requires explicit mention because one might have thought that *shaliach* number two lacks the standing to override *shaliach* number one. *Shaliach* number one carries the actual document; he is the embodiment of the husband's original will, and there is something to the idea that physical possession of the *get* invests that original *shlichus* with greater weight. Perhaps a mere verbal message carried by a second agent — himself not in possession of the document — cannot override the status conferred by the first *shaliach*'s role.

*Kamashmalan* — the *Mishnah* teaches that it can. The second *shaliach* effectively represents the husband's current will, and that current will takes precedence.

### Reaching Wife Directly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קָדַם הוּא אֵצֶל אִשְׁתּוֹ״ – לְמָה לִי? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כִּי לָא אָמְרִינַן לְצַעוֹרַהּ קָא מִיכַּוֵּין – הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְשָׁלִיחַ, אֲבָל לְדִידַהּ וַדַּאי לְצַעוֹרַהּ קָא מִיכַּוֵּין, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues and asks: **Why do I** need the mishna to teach a case where a husband reached **his wife before** the bill of divorce reached her? It is obvious that a husband can render void the bill of divorce before it reaches his wife. The Gemara explains: This principle was stated **lest you say** that **when we don't say** that he **intends** only **to vex her** like in the case above, and the bill of divorce is in fact void, **this matter applies** only when he said **to the agent** that the bill of divorce is void; **however,** if he said that **to her, he certainly intends** only **to vex her,** and he does not actually intend to render the bill of divorce void. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that even in this case the bill of divorce is void.

The third scenario — the husband himself races to his wife — also requires separate mention. Here, the concern cuts differently. When the husband goes directly to the woman who has been waiting for the *get*, and he appears before her and tells her that the *get* she was expecting is annulled, we might be deeply suspicious of his motives. *Vadai letzura d'achamchaman* — certainly we might suspect he is doing this purely to cause her anguish, not as a genuine retraction of his will. When he annuls through a messenger to the first *shaliach*, there is a degree of remove; but here he is confronting her directly, and the opportunity for cruelty is patent.

Nevertheless, the *Mishnah* teaches that we do not make that assumption. The *bitul* is valid regardless of how it was received emotionally.

### Second Shaliach to Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁלַח אֶצְלָהּ שָׁלִיחַ״ – לְמָה לִי? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אִיהוּ הוּא דְּלָא טָרַח אַדַּעְתָּא לְצַעוֹרַהּ, אֲבָל שָׁלִיחַ, דְּלָא אִיכְפַּת לֵיהּ כִּי טָרַח – וַדַּאי לְצַעוֹרַהּ קָא מִיכַּוֵּין, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues and asks: **Why do I** need the mishna to state: **Or where he sent an agent to her,** which, as stated above, means that the legal status of a person's agent is like that of himself? The Gemara answers: It is necessary **lest you say** that only **he would not exert** himself **with the** sole **intent to vex her,** by informing her falsely that the bill of divorce is void; **however,** with regard to the **agent, as** the husband **does not care** if **he exerts** himself for no reason, and the husband **certainly intends** only **to vex her** when he sends an agent and not actually to render the bill of divorce void. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that in this case as well the bill of divorce is void.

The fourth scenario — sending a second *shaliach* to the wife — requires mention for yet another distinct reason. One might have thought that hiring a *shaliach* to deliver the message of *bitul* to the wife suggests that the husband is not troubled by the effort and cost of causing her anguish at arm's length. He sits comfortably at home while someone else carries the distressing message. *Vadai letzura d'achamchaman* — perhaps in this configuration, where the husband incurs no personal inconvenience, we should certainly suspect his motives. The *Mishnah* teaches that we still do not invalidate the *bitul* on this basis.

### Running After Shaliach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם מִשֶּׁהִגִּיעַ גֵּט לְיָדָהּ – אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְבַטְּלוֹ. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא דְּמַהְדַּר עֲלֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא לְבַטּוֹלֵי; מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אִיגַּלַּאי מִלְּתָא לְמַפְרֵעַ דְּבַטּוֹלֵי בַּטְּלֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states further: **If** he stated this **once the bill of divorce had entered her possession, he can no longer render it void,** as the divorce had already taken effect. The Gemara asks: **Isn't** this **obvious?** Once the bill of divorce has entered her possession, they are divorced. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** for the mishna to state that even in a case **where he was going around searching for** the bill of divorce **from the beginning** in order **to render it void** before it reached his wife, once it enters her possession it is too late. **Lest you say:** Once he renders the bill of divorce void, even after it had entered her possession, **it has become clear retroactively that he rendered it void** from the beginning, before it reached his wife, therefore the mishna **teaches us** that since the bill of divorce was rendered void only after it had entered her possession, they are divorced.

Following the analysis of all four scenarios, the *Gemara* addresses a further case: *iglaei milta lemafreia d'bitulei batlei* — what if we discover in hindsight that the husband was clearly trying to annul the *get*? Specifically, the case imagined is one where the husband was visibly running through the marketplace after the *shaliach*, clearly trying to catch him, but the *shaliach* delivered the *get* to the wife a moment before the husband arrived. Should we reason that since it is now manifest what the husband's intent was — he was plainly trying to be *mevatel* — perhaps his running itself should count retroactively as an act of *bitul*?

The *Gemara* says: *ka mashma lan* — no. This does not work. And the reasons are multiple. First, we ordinarily say *devarim shebelev einam devarim* — unarticulated thoughts carry no halachic weight. The husband said nothing; he was thinking and running, but those inner intentions do not constitute a valid speech act. Second, even if one could somehow treat the running as equivalent to an articulation of will, the husband still did not communicate that will to the *shaliach*, the wife, or a *beis din*. He was shouting, so to speak, in the empty air of the marketplace. The *Mishnah*'s structure throughout is that valid *bitul* requires communication to the relevant parties. Without that, there is no *bitul*.

### Tosafot Devarim Shebelev

*Tosafot* engages this case with particular depth. *Tosafot* begins from the question of why *devarim shebelev einam devarim* — why do unarticulated thoughts carry no weight? The conventional answer is systemic: a legal framework cannot function if people can later claim "this is what I was thinking." Halachic acts require articulated, measurable, knowable expression.

But *Tosafot* notes a second rationale that appears in certain contexts: the reason unarticulated thoughts don't count is that if a person really meant them, they would have expressed them. The failure to articulate is itself evidence that the commitment was not firm enough to have legal weight.

*Tosafot* then points out that this second rationale fails to apply to the running-after-the-*shaliach* case. No one doubts that the husband was serious. He was running. He was making every effort. The failure to articulate was not a sign of ambivalence but a failure of logistics — he simply did not reach the *shaliach* in time. If the only problem were lack of seriousness, it would not apply here.

*Tosafot* further imagines a scenario in which the *shaliach* actually saw the husband running. At the time, the *shaliach* may have interpreted it as the husband urging him on — *hurry up and deliver it*. Only in hindsight, once the husband explains himself, does the *shaliach* understand that the running was an attempt at *bitul*. If *devarim shebelev havei devarim* in such a case — if we can treat the running as a form of articulation — should we not retroactively interpret what the *shaliach* witnessed as a communicated act of *bitul*?

*Tosafot* toys with this possibility seriously. Yet in the end, *Tosafot* concludes that the rabbis treated such a case as equivalent to *devarim shebelev* — even if the running was unambiguously sincere. *Shekeivan d'ktzas dami l'devarim shebelev lo chashiv akimas dibur min haTorah* — since it resembles *devarim shebelev* closely enough, it is not treated as a fully constituted speech act under Torah law. And *Tosafot* is explicit that this amounts to a rabbinic override: the rabbis effectively prevented what might technically have been a valid *bitul* from taking effect. As *Tosafot* notes — and this is a theme that will recur throughout the *perek* — the rabbis sometimes use their power to prevent something that would otherwise work under *d'Oraita* law, provided the contravention is not so blatant as to undermine the Torah structure.

The bottom line is that this does not constitute valid *bitul*, for a convergence of reasons: *devarim shebelev einam devarim*; even if it were treated as articulation, it was not communicated to the right parties at the right time; and even if the *shaliach* witnessed the running, a retroactive reinterpretation of that moment — based on later clarification — does not convert it into a contemporaneous speech act. Valid *bitul* requires explicit, timely communication to the *shaliach*, the wife, or a *beis din*.

## Annulment Formulas

### Valid Invalid Formulas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בָּטֵל הוּא״; ״אִי אֶיפְשִׁי בּוֹ״ – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין. ״פָּסוּל הוּא״; ״אֵינוֹ גֵּט״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** If a husband made one of the following statements with regard to a bill of divorce that he sent: **It is void [*batel hu*],** or: **I do not desire it,** then **his statement takes effect** and the bill of divorce is void. However, if he said: **It is invalid,** or: **It is not a bill of divorce,** then it is as though **he said nothing,** as the bill of divorce has nothing disqualifying it.

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Baraita* that addresses the question of what language suffices for valid *bitul*. A student had earlier asked whether there is a required formula. The *Baraita* teaches: if he said *batel hu* — it is annulled — or *ee efshi bo* — I do not desire it — *devarav kayamim*, it is effective. If he said *pasul hu* — it is invalid — or *eino get* — it is not a *get* — *lo amar klum*, he has said nothing.

The distinction is conceptually interesting. *Ee efshi bo* — I no longer desire it — does not explicitly void the document; it simply withdraws the will behind it. Yet that is enough. *Pasul hu*, on the other hand, is a statement of alleged fact — you are asserting that the *get* is halachically invalid. But by what grounds is it invalid? You are not making a declarative act of retraction; you are making a false assertion about its current legal status. That carries no weight.

### Batel Status vs Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּ״בָּטֵל״ לִישָּׁנָא דְּלִבְּטִיל מַשְׁמַע?! וְהָאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אַיְבוּ, אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת; וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: מְקַבֵּל מַתָּנָה, שֶׁאָמַר לְאַחַר שֶׁבָּאתָה מַתָּנָה לְיָדוֹ: ״מַתָּנָה זוֹ מְבוּטֶּלֶת״; ״תִּיבַּטֵּל״; ״אִי אֶיפְשִׁי בָּהּ״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. ״בְּטֵלָה הִיא״; ״אֵינָהּ מַתָּנָה״ – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין. אַלְמָא ״בָּטֵל״ מֵעִיקָּרָא מַשְׁמַע!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that** the formulation: ***Batel*, is a** prescriptive **formulation that means: Let it become void,** and not a descriptive formulation that means that the bill of divorce is already void? **But didn't Rabba bar Aivu say** that **Rav Sheshet said, and some say** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** With regard to **one who receives a gift, who, after the gift had entered his possession, said: This gift is rendered void;** or if he said: **Let it become void;** or if he said: **I do not desire it,** it is as though **he said nothing.** He has already acquired the gift, and he cannot undo his acquisition. However, if he said: **It is void [*betela he*],** or: **It is not a gift, his statement is effective,** as these formulations indicate that he had never agreed to acquire the gift in the first place. **Apparently,** the formulation: ***Batel*, means** that it is void **from the beginning,** and not that it should become void, in opposition to the *baraita*.

The more subtle case is *batel hu* itself. The *Gemara* asks: *lemimra de-batel lishna de-livatel mashma* — does saying "it is *batel*" mean it shall become *batel*, i.e., I am hereby annulling it? Or is it a description of current status — it already is *batel*? Consider the language of *kiddushin*: *harei at mekudeshet li* — behold you are betrothed to me. Is that a description of a fact or the performance of an act? Obviously the latter. But the grammar is identical to a description. The question of whether language is constitutive or merely descriptive is not always resolved by the words themselves.

### Matana Gift Comparison

The *Gemara* brings a ruling of Rav Sheshet (also attributed to Rabba bar Abba or Rabba bar Avuh) concerning *matana* — a gift. If someone received a gift and thereafter said *matana zo mevutelet* — this gift that I have received shall be annulled — or *ee efshi bah* — I do not desire it — *lo amar klum*. Once you have received the gift, the gift is yours. If you wish to divest yourself of it, go give it away; you cannot retroactively annul the receipt. The language of *shall be annulled* is simply inapplicable to something already completed.

However, if the recipient said *beteila hi* — it is annulled — or *eina matana* — it is not a gift — *devarav kayamin*, that is effective. Here, the logic is essentially the reverse of the *get* case. By saying "it is not a gift, it was never a gift," you are articulating that you never accepted it as such; you are describing the status from its inception, not trying to effect a change now.

*Alma batel me'ikara mashma* — so when *batel* is used effectively in the *matana* context, it means "it was never valid from the outset." The *Gemara* thus faces a tension: by the *get*, *batel hu* seems to mean "I am hereby annulling it" — a future-oriented performative act. By the *matana*, *beteila hi* seems to mean "it was never valid" — a backward-looking description. Does *batel* mean one thing or two different things?

### Abaye Context Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״בָּטֵל״ שְׁתֵּי לְשׁוֹנוֹת מַשְׁמַע – מַשְׁמַע דְּבָטֵל, וּמַשְׁמַע דְּלִיבְּטִיל. גַּבֵּי גֵּט לִישָּׁנָא דְּמַהֲנֵי בֵּיהּ קָאָמַר, גַּבֵּי מַתָּנָה לִישָּׁנָא דְּמַהֲנֵי בַּהּ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The formulation: ***Batel*, has two** potential **meanings,** depending on the context. **It means that** it was **void** already, **and it** also **means that it will become void** in the future. **With regard to a bill of divorce, he stated the formulation that is effective with regard to it,** and since his statement can be understood to mean that the bill of divorce should be void from that point onward, it is interpreted as such. **With regard to a gift, he stated the formulation that is effective with regard to it,** and since one cannot nullify the acquisition of a gift after taking possession of it, his intention was that the gift was void from the outset, and his statement is interpreted as such.

*Abaye* resolves this: *batel shtei leshonot mashma* — *batel* carries both meanings, and which meaning obtains depends entirely on context. *Le-gabei get lishna de-bahani beh ka-amar* — by a *get*, the clear purpose of the statement is to effect an annulment, so we interpret it accordingly; *batel hu* means it shall hereby be annulled. *De-gabei matana lishna de-bahani beh ka-amar* — by a *matana* already received, the only way the language can function effectively is if it refers to original status; so *beteila hi* means it was never a valid gift.

The straightforward explanation is simply that words are often ambiguous, and context disambiguates them. This is entirely parallel to the concept of *yadayim mokhichot* — when context is clear, it determines the meaning of an otherwise ambiguous expression. Under the *chuppah*, *harei at mekudeshet li* is unmistakably performative. Said in a different context, the same words might describe a fact. Here too: the intent behind the speech act in its context tells us which reading of *batel* is operative.

## Ravina Formula Questions

### Batel Without Hu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק דִּתְלֵי וְקָאֵי בְּעִיבְרָא דְּדַשָּׁא, וְקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: ״בָּטֵל״ מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Ravina found Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak as he was leaning on the bar of a door** deep in thought, **and** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak **was considering** the following **dilemma: What is** the *halakha* if a husband said only: This bill of divorce is **void,** but did not say: This bill of divorce, it is void? Does he mean to render the bill of divorce void from that point onward, which he has the ability to do, or is he merely noting the fact that this bill of divorce is void, in which case his statement does not affect the validity of the document? No answer is given to this question and therefore the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

*Ravina* found Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak leaning in a doorway and posed him the following question: what if the husband simply said *batel* — without *hu*? He did not say *harei hu batel* or *batel hu*; he just uttered *batel*. Is that an effective formula? *Rashi* explains that the problem is the absence of *hu* — he did not even form a complete sentence.

The *Gemara* answers: *teiku* — the question stands unresolved. We do not know whether this stripped-down utterance suffices or not.

### Rav Sheshet Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: ״גֵּט זֶה לֹא יוֹעִיל״; ״לֹא יַתִּיר״; ״לֹא יַעֲזִיב״; ״לֹא יְשַׁלַּח״; ״לֹא יְגָרֵשׁ״; ״יְהֵא חֶרֶס״; ״יְהֵא כְּחֶרֶס״ – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין. ״אֵינוֹ מוֹעִיל״; ״אֵינוֹ מַתִּיר״; ״אֵינוֹ מַעֲזִיב״; ״אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּחַ״; ״אֵינוֹ מְגָרֵשׁ״; ״חֶרֶס הוּא״; ״כְּחֶרֶס הוּא״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says, and some say** that **it was taught in a *baraita*,** that if the husband said one of these phrases: **This bill of divorce shall not be effective, shall not release, shall not cause to leave, shall not send away, shall not divorce, shall be pottery,** or **shall be like pottery,** in all of these cases **his statement is effective,** and the bill of divorce is rendered void. If, however, the husband used one of the following formulations: This bill of divorce **has no effect, does not release, does not cause to leave, does not send away, does not divorce, it is pottery,** or **it is like pottery,** then it is as though **he said nothing.** The husband has the authority only to render the bill of divorce void. However, his descriptive statements with regard to the legal standing of the bill of divorce are meaningless.

A *Baraita* is cited in the name of Rav Sheshet — some say it was taught in a *matnitta*: if he said *get zeh lo yoil* — this *get* shall not work — *lo yattir* — shall not render her permitted — *lo yaaziv* — shall not send her forth — *lo yishalach*, *lo yigoresh* — shall not divorce her — or *yehe kacheres* / *tehe kacheres* — it shall be like pottery — *devarav kayamim*, all of these are effective formulas.

What these formulations share is that they are forward-looking: they say what the *get* shall no longer do. They constitute a speech act of annulment. *Eino mo'il, eino matir, eino ma'aziv, eino meshaleach, eino megaresh* — it does not work, does not permit, does not send her forth, does not divorce — *lo amar klum*, these are not effective. These formulations merely describe what the *get* currently does not do — they are statements of alleged fact about its present status rather than acts of voiding it.

### Harei Hu Cheres

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״הֲרֵי הוּא חֶרֶס״, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מַאי שְׁנָא מֵ״הֲרֵי הוּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ״; ״הֲרֵי הוּא הֶפְקֵר״?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* if he said: **It is hereby pottery?** Is his intention to make a descriptive statement, that the bill of divorce is not valid, or does he intend to render it void? **Ravina said to Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, and some say** that **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** In **what** way **is** this formulation **different from** saying about one's property: **It is hereby consecrated** property, or: **It is hereby ownerless** property, where it is clear that his intention is to designate the items as consecrated or ownerless property? With regard to bills of divorce as well, his statement is effective and it renders the bill of divorce void.

An ambiguous case is then raised: what of *harei hu cheres* — behold, it is pottery? Is this a performative declaration — I hereby declare it to be mere pottery, stripping it of its status as a *get* — or is it a description of current status — I am asserting that it already is nothing more than pottery? *Mahu* — what is the ruling?

One *amora* said to another: *mai shena me-harei hu hekdesh, harei hu hefker* — what is the difference from "behold it is *hekdesh*" or "behold it is *hefker*"? In those cases, the declaration clearly effects a change in status; *harei hu hekdesh* is the paradigmatic performative speech act by which something becomes consecrated. Why should *harei hu cheres* not be read the same way?

*Tosafot* notes the difficulty with this comparison. The reason *harei hu hekdesh* or *harei hu hefker* are unmistakably performative is that *hekdesh* and *hefker* are statuses that exist exclusively through speech acts. When you say something "is *hekdesh*," the context makes clear that you mean you are making it *hekdesh*, because that is the only way *hekdesh* comes into being. *Cheres*, however — pottery — is a material description. Saying something "is pottery" does not inherently invoke a speech act that changes status; it might simply be identifying what the object is made of. Moreover, *Tosafot* points out, the parallel does not map cleanly: saying *kikar zeh hekdesh* — this loaf of bread is *hekdesh* — would clearly be effective, whereas *be-get zeh cheres* — this *get* is pottery — might certainly be saying nothing at all. One cannot mechanically substitute one predicate for another and assume the performative force transfers. The specific word matters enormously to the sense of the sentence, and *Tosafot* here does not arrive at a definitive resolution.

## Reversing Annulment

### Chozer Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹזֵר וּמְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ, אוֹ אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר וּמְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ? רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: חוֹזֵר וּמְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ; וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר וּמְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: If the husband rendered void a bill of divorce, can he **go back and divorce** his wife **with it,** since perhaps he did not actually render void the bill of divorce but only nullified the agency for its delivery, so it can be used again in the future; **or may he not go back and divorce with it,** as the bill of divorce itself was rendered void? **Rav Naḥman says: He may go back and divorce with it, and Rav Sheshet says: He may not go back and divorce with it.** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Naḥman,** that the bill of divorce may be used.

The *Gemara* now raises what is perhaps the most conceptually arresting question of the entire *sugiyah*: *chozer umegaresh bo o eino chozer umegaresh bo?* — once the husband has annulled the *get*, can he then reverse that annulment and use the *get* to divorce his wife, or has he effectively destroyed the *get* beyond recovery?

This is the equivalent of asking whether stopping a check and then un-stopping it would allow the check to be cashed. Can the *bitul* be undone?

### Nachman Sheshet Dispute

Rav Nachman says *chozer umegaresh bo* — he can reuse it; the *bitul* can be reversed and the *get* used. Rav Sheshet says *eino chozer umegaresh bo* — he cannot; the *get* has been rendered permanently void. *Vehilchata kevateih de-Rav Nachman* — the law follows Rav Nachman.

This ruling is striking. We have spent considerable time reading through formulas of *bitul* — *batel hu, harei hu cheres, lo yoil, lo yattir* — all of which are directed emphatically at the efficacy of the *get*, at stripping it of its status as an instrument of divorce. And yet the *halacha* says it can be un-done?

### Kiddushin Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא קַיְימָא לַן הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: חוֹזֶרֶת. הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם – דִּיבּוּר וְדִיבּוּר הוּא, אָתֵי דִּיבּוּר וּמְבַטֵּל דִּיבּוּר; וְהָכָא – נְהִי דְּבַטְּלֵיהּ לִשְׁלִיחוּתָא דְּשָׁלִיחַ, גִּיטָּא גּוּפֵיהּ מִי קָא בָטֵיל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But don't we maintain** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said:** If a man gives a woman money for betrothal and says that the betrothal will take effect after thirty days, the woman **can retract** her agreement within the thirty days and decide that she does not wish to be betrothed. Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that as long as the change in status had not yet taken effect, she may nullify her earlier agreement by stating a retraction. Therefore, here too, when the husband states that the bill of divorce should be rendered void, since the divorce had not taken effect, it should be rendered void. The Gemara rejects this argument: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of betrothal, **it is speech** that accepts the betrothal **and speech** that retracts her acceptance; therefore, her **speech comes and nullifies** her previous **speech,** as the woman first stated that she agreed and then stated afterward that she retracted her agreement. **But here,** even **though** it is true **that** the husband **rendered void the agency of the agent, does** he also **render void the bill of divorce itself?** Since the bill of divorce is a tangible object, it cannot be made void through speech alone.

The *Gemara* draws a comparison to a case in *Kiddushin*. If a man says to a woman *harei at mekudeshet li be-tabaat zu le-achar sheloshim yom* — you are betrothed to me with this ring in thirty days — and then during those thirty days one of the parties retracts, *Rabbi Yochanan* rules that this retraction is effective. *Vekayma lan hilchata kevateih de-Rabbi Yochanan be-chozeret* — and the established ruling follows Rabbi Yochanan that she can retract. Furthermore, the kiddushin, once reversed in this way, is fully undone. The initial act, which created a pending kiddushin, can be annulled before it takes effect, and the annulment is complete.

The *Gemara* adduces this to support the idea that annulment before an act has fully taken effect is possible and reversible: just as the retraction of *kiddushin* before the thirty days expire is clean and complete, so too should a *bitul* of a *get* before delivery be reversible.

### Dibbur Umaseh Analysis

The *Gemara* immediately rejects this comparison. *Hatam dibbur u-dibbur hu* — there, in the *kiddushin* case, it is speech against speech. The *kiddushin* was created by a speech act conditioned on time, and it is undone by another speech act before the condition matures. *Ati dibbur u-mevatel dibbur* — one speech act cancels another. Nothing concrete has happened; no status has been effected in the world; the act is entirely in the realm of verbal declaration, and a subsequent verbal declaration can undo it.

*Hacha*, here, by contrast, the situation is different. *Nehi de-bitul li-shlichutei de-shaliach* — granted, when he said *batel hu*, he did annul the *shlichus* of the *shaliach*, the agency. But *gita gufiah mi ka batel?* — since when did he annul the *get* itself? This is the crucial move. The *Gemara* is suggesting that what the husband's words actually accomplished was the cancellation of the agency — nothing more. The *get* as a legal document was not changed. A valid document sitting in an agent's hand is not rendered legally void by the principal's subsequent verbal statement of displeasure with it. The words canceled the *shlichus*; they did not alter the status of the document.

And if all that was canceled was the *shlichus*, then the *shlichus* can simply be re-established. The document remains valid; appoint a new *shaliach* or take the document yourself, and deliver it. *Chozer umegaresh bo* — yes, he can reuse it — because nothing happened to the *get* itself.

### Tosafot Annul Get

*Tosafot* presses on this reading carefully. From the language of the *Gemara* — *gita gufiah mi ka batel* — *Tosafot* draws the inference that the *Gemara* is stating it is simply impossible to annul the *get* itself through speech. *Mitoch ha-lashon mashma de-afilu bitlo be-perush eino mevutal* — even if the husband had explicitly stated "I am not annulling the *shlichus*, I am annulling the *get* itself," it would be ineffective. *Ve-dibbur li-shlichuto hu de-batel* — whatever he said, we will interpret it as a cancellation of agency, not a change in the document's status.

This is significant because it means that all the rich analysis of formulas — *batel hu*, *harei hu cheres*, *lo yoil* — was really about the annulment of *shlichus*, not about changing the inherent legal status of the *get* as a document. The *get*, as a validly executed document, is beyond the reach of the husband's subsequent speech. *Ve-ka-amar gita gufiah mi ka batel — mashma de-ein lo koach levatel* — the language implies he simply does not have the power to annul it.

*Tosafot* also notes, however, that a parallel *sugiyah* reads slightly differently: *nehi de-batlei li-shlichutei, midei de-gita lo batlei* — granted he annulled the *shlichus*, but from the *get* itself he did not annul anything. *Tosafot* notes that this formulation only says he *did not* annul the *get*, not that he *could not*. Based on this, *Tosafot* argues that in a case where the *bitul* was explicit and unambiguous — where the husband clearly declared the *get* itself void — *Rav Nachman* would actually concede that it is *batel* and the *get* cannot be reused. Furthermore, *Tosafot* maintains that one necessarily must say that the *get* is not yet an irretractable completed act as long as it has not reached the woman's hand — otherwise, various other principles would be in tension. The upshot is that the *sugiyah* leaves genuine ambiguity: does Rav Nachman's ruling that *chozer umegaresh bo* rest on the view that annulment of the *get* itself is categorically impossible, or merely that in the ordinary case we interpret the husband's words as canceling *shlichus* rather than the *get*, while a fully explicit annulment could in principle still work?

## Original Beis Din

### Barishonah Original

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה. אִיתְּמַר, בִּפְנֵי כַּמָּה הוּא מְבַטְּלוֹ? רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם; רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that **initially** a husband who wished to render void the bill of divorce **would convene** a court, even if he had already sent the document with an agent, and render the bill of divorce void in the presence of the court. **It was stated:** When the husband would state that the bill of divorce should be void, **in the presence of how many** people **must he render it void? Rav Naḥman says:** He must render it void **in the presence of two** people. **Rav Sheshet says:** He must render it void **in the presence of three** people.

The *Gemara* now takes up the second part of the *Mishnah* — the *barishonah* clause. Originally, before Rabban Gamliel's *takana*, a husband could annul the *get* by convening a *beis din* back in his hometown while the *shaliach* was en route, with no notification to the *shaliach* or the wife. This has now been prohibited. But the *Gemara* asks: in that original state, when this was permissible, how many people were required to constitute the *beis din* before which the *bitul* was made?

### Two Three Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה – ״בֵּית דִּין״ קָתָנֵי. וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם – לְבֵי תְרֵי נָמֵי ״בֵּית דִּין״ קָרֵי לְהוּ. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּתְנַן: ״מוֹסְרַנִי לִפְנֵיכֶם פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי הַדַּיָּינִים שֶׁבְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the reasoning of each *amora*: **Rav Sheshet said** that he must do so **in the presence of three** people, because the mishna **teaches** that this takes place in the presence of a **court,** and a court consists of three judges. **And Rav Naḥman said** that it may be done **in the presence of two** people, as **two** people **are also called a court,** and in exigent circumstances one may rely on this. **Rav Naḥman said: From where do I say** that two people are also called a court? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 10:4): When one creates a document that prevents the Sabbatical Year from abrogating an outstanding debt [*prosbol*], he states: **I transfer** to you **in your presence, so-and-so and so-and-so the judges, in such and such a place,** in order that I will collect any debt that I am owed by so-and-so whenever I wish.

Rav Nachman says *bifnei shnayim* — before two. Rav Sheshet says *bifnei gimel* — before three, a full *beis din*. Rav Sheshet's position is straightforward: the *Mishnah* says *beis din*, and a *beis din* is three. Rav Nachman counters that two are also called a *beis din* in certain contexts — *beis terei nami beis din kari lehu*.

*Tosafot* notes that Rav Nachman holds a position in Sanhedrin that *shnayim she-danu, dineihem din* — two who adjudicated, their ruling stands retroactively — but points out that other *amoraim* who agree with Rav Nachman here do not hold that position in Sanhedrin, suggesting that Rav Nachman's ruling here reflects a broader definitional claim about the term *beis din* in certain contexts, not merely a leniency about judicial quorums.

### Agency vs Document

The deeper question behind the dispute between Rav Nachman and Rav Sheshet over two versus three is conceptual, and it connects elegantly to the earlier dispute between them. Why did the original procedure require a *beis din* at all? If all the husband is doing is retracting his will — annulling the *shlichus* — why not simply do it privately? The only reason to require witnesses is to establish proof that it occurred. In that case, two witnesses suffice, just as any other fact requiring proof requires two witnesses, not three judges. This is Rav Nachman's position: the *bitul* is fundamentally a retraction of *shlichus*, an act of the husband's will, which requires only *eidim* to authenticate it. The use of the term *beis din* is loose; two witnesses are all that is really needed.

Rav Sheshet, who holds that the *bitul* effects a genuine change in the status of the *get* itself — and who correspondingly holds that once done it cannot be reversed — sees this as a fundamentally judicial act. Something that changes the legal status of a document cannot be accomplished by a private expression of will, however witnessed. It requires a formal juridical proceeding, a *beis din* of three. The two positions — Rav Nachman and Rav Sheshet — thus map perfectly: *chozer umegaresh bo* / *eino chozer umegaresh bo*, and *bifnei shnayim* / *bifnei gimel*, are two expressions of a single underlying conceptual disagreement about the nature of *bitul*.
