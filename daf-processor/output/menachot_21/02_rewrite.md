# Menachot 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Salt Requirements

### What Gets Salted

The daf opens with a discussion of which offerings require salting on the *mizbe'ach*. The basic conclusion already established is that one salts things that are burnt on the *mizbe'ach*, but not blood, and there is a question regarding wood — specifically, whether wood is offered as a *mincha* in its own right, or simply used as fuel that is burnt directly. The *gemara* opens with the words *appik eitzei d'ayalu l'nesachim* — "take out wood" (which is debatable, since according to Rebbi wood would be salted as a proper *mincha*) "and put in *nesachim*," the wine libations, which are not burnt but poured down a conduit along one of the *karnot* of the *mizbe'ach*, and therefore do not require salting.

### Four Exceptions

A *braita* is then cited listing four things that do not require salting: *aval hayayin vehadam veha'eitzim vehaktoret ein te'unim melach* — "but the wine, the blood, the wood, and the *ketoreth* do not require salt." Wine and blood are readily understood as things that are not burnt. Wood is back on the list — if one follows the position that it is not a *korban* in its own right but simply serves as fuel, it is excluded. And then there is *ketoreth*, the incense, which is strange, because *ketoreth* is indeed burnt — yet it is burnt on the *mizbe'ach hazahav*, the inner golden altar.

The *gemara* asks: whose opinion does this *braita* reflect? If it is Rebbi's view, then the inclusion of wood is difficult, since Rebbi holds wood requires salting. If it is the *chachamim*, then the inclusion of *ketoreth* is difficult, since *ketoreth* is something that is burnt.

### Ishmael's Criteria

The *gemara* resolves this by attributing the *braita* to a specific *tanna*: *Rabbi Yishmael b'no shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka omer*. He derives the rule from an explicit verse and establishes three criteria for what requires salting: (1) it must be *mekabel tumah* — capable of becoming ritually impure; (2) it must be *oleh l'ishim* — burnt on the altar fire; and (3) it must be placed on the outer *mizbe'ach*. Each criterion excludes one of the problematic items on the list.

### Blood & Wine

The criterion of *oleh l'ishim* — that the item be burnt — excludes blood and wine. Blood is not burnt; nor is wine. Everyone, regardless of position on wood, would agree that since neither blood nor wine is burnt, neither requires salting. (It is worth noting that there is an opinion in the *gemara* that one would sprinkle the wine toward the fire and allow it to be partially burnt; according to that view, salting the *nesachim* might indeed be required.)

### Incense Exception

The criterion of the outer *mizbe'ach* excludes *ketoreth*. The incense is burnt every morning and afternoon, but on the *mizbe'ach hazahav*, the inner golden altar, not on the outer *mizbe'ach*. Hence, despite the fact that *ketoreth* is burnt, it does not meet this third criterion and does not require salting.

### Wood Status

The criterion of *mekabel tumah* — susceptibility to ritual impurity — excludes wood, which is neither food nor a vessel and therefore does not ordinarily become *tamei*. The *gemara* acknowledges a complication: there is a principle of *chibas hakodesh*, by which items dedicated to the *Mikdash* and placed on the *mizbe'ach* attain a heightened significance that renders them susceptible to *tumah*. The *gemara* explicitly includes wood in that category elsewhere. How then can the *mishnah* say wood is not *mekabel tumah*?

Rashi resolves this by saying that *chibas hakodesh* is only a *derabanan* concept; *de'oraisa*, wood is simply not *mekabel tumah*. Tosafot disagrees and considers *chibas hakodesh* to be *de'oraisa*, and therefore offers two alternative explanations. First, Tosafot suggests that whether wood qualifies under *chibas hakodesh* may depend on the very dispute between Rebbi and the *chachamim*: if, like Rebbi, one treats wood as a genuine *korban mincha* — food for the *mizbe'ach*, as it were — then it functions similarly to other offerings and is *mekabel tumah*. If, like the *chachamim*, wood is merely used as fuel and not as an offering in its own right, it does not qualify for *chibas hakodesh* and remains not *mekabel tumah*. Second, Tosafot suggests that *ein mekabel tumah* in this context may simply mean that in ordinary, non-*Mikdash* contexts, wood is not *mekabel tumah*, and that is enough to exclude it from the salting requirement, even if within the *Mikdash* it might technically qualify.

## Cooked Blood

### Blood Exclusion

The *gemara* now draws on the exclusion of blood from the *pasuk* itself: *minchascha velo damecha* — "your *mincha*," but not your blood. Were blood not explicitly excluded by the verse, one might have thought it should be salted. This prompts a deeper question: even granting the theoretical possibility of salting blood, once you salt blood it is no longer considered blood in the halakhic sense, and therefore cannot be used for *kaparah*. How could there ever have been a *hava amina* to salt it?

### Cooked Blood

The *gemara* cites the teaching of Ze'iri in the name of Rabbi Chanina: *dam shebishlo eino over alav* — if you cooked blood and then ate it, you do not transgress the prohibition of blood. There is no *kares*, and possibly no *de'oraisa* violation at all. The reason appears to be rooted in the connection the *Torah* draws between the prohibition of eating blood and its use on the *mizbe'ach*: it is the *dam shehaneshama yotzeis bo* — the lifeblood that exits at the moment of death — which both carries the prohibition and which is valid for *zerikah*. Once the blood is no longer in its natural state, it is no longer in that category.

### Salt Like Heat

Building on this, Rav Yehuda says in the name of Ze'iri: *dam shemalcho eino over alav* — not only cooked blood, but even salted blood exempts one from the prohibition. This is presumably based on the principle of *maliach harei hu ke-rosei'ach* — salting is equivalent to roasting. This principle is most familiar from the laws of *kashrus*, where the same logic explains how salt draws out blood from meat (as heat would draw it out on a spit), and how a heavily salted food can transfer flavor to other foods, creating concerns about *basar bechalav* or *treifus*. Here, the *gemara* applies the same logic in the other direction: just as cooking transforms blood such that one is no longer *chayav* for eating it, salting also transforms it and removes the prohibition.

### Salted Blood Problem

This creates a genuine difficulty. If salted blood is no longer considered halakhic blood, it would follow that it cannot be used for *zerikah* either. The assumption underlying both rules — the prohibition against eating blood and its use on the *mizbe'ach* — is that they apply to the same substance. If one is exempt from the prohibition of eating, one also cannot use that blood for the *korban*. So how could the *gemara* have entertained any *hava amina* that one should salt the blood at all, given that the act of salting would itself disqualify it for its essential temple use?

The connection is further illuminated by considering *yayin mevushal*: cooked wine cannot be used on the *mizbe'ach*, and significantly, it is also excluded from many of the restrictions of *stam yeinam*, because cooked wine was not relevant to the world of idolatrous worship. The theme is consistent: substances that exist in their natural state — wine, blood — are subject to both their respective prohibitions and their *korban* uses. Cooking or transformation removes them from both categories simultaneously. There is, of course, the entire tractate of Menachot devoted to the *mincha*, which is baked — but the *mincha* is by nature something processed. For blood, wine, and similar items, *bishul* removes them from their fundamental category.

### Salt Quantity

The *gemara*'s resolution is elegant: *mishtaba b'mashehu l'mitzvah d'alma* — the theoretical *hava amina* was that one might have applied just the tiniest amount of salt, a *mashehu*, not enough to constitute a full act of *melicha*, not enough to invoke *maliach ke-rosei'ach*, and thereby perform the *mitzvah* of salting while leaving the blood legally intact. The verse comes to exclude even this minimal approach, because blood — as something not burnt on the *mizbe'ach* — is excluded from the salting requirement entirely.

## Congealed Blood

### Congealed Challenge

The *gemara* now turns to the broader teaching of Ze'iri. Rava was sitting and reviewing this teaching when Abaye challenged him from a *braita*: *hikfeh et hadam ve'achalo* — if one congealed the blood and ate it; *o shehimchehu et hachelev u'gma'o* — or if one liquified forbidden fat and drank it. In both cases, the normal mode of consumption has been altered — blood was eaten rather than drunk, *chelev* was drunk rather than eaten. The *braita* rules that one is nevertheless *chayav*. This seems to contradict Ze'iri's position, since congealing blood would appear to be a form of transformation analogous to cooking.

### Fire vs Sun

The *gemara* answers: *lo kashya: kan shehikfeh be'ur, kan shehikfeh bechama* — there is no contradiction, because the two cases involve different mechanisms of congealing. If the blood was congealed by fire, one is exempt; if it was congealed by the sun, one is *chayav*. The *gemara* states the principle explicitly: *be'ur lo hadar, bechamah hadar* — what is done by fire does not revert to its original state; what is done by the sun does revert.

The underlying logic here is worth examining carefully. Ordinarily, the distinction between fire and the sun is invoked in contexts where the nature of the *act* matters — as on *Shabbat*, where one is *chayav* for cooking with fire, the standard human method, but not for leaving something in the sun, which is considered an unusual method. Here, however, the focus is not on the act but on the state of the object. Why should it matter whether it was fire or sunlight?

Rashi explains that the critical variable is irreversibility. Fire produces a genuine, irreversible transformation — the blood changes permanently and is no longer the same substance. The sun, being natural, does not produce that kind of transformation. The blood hardens temporarily, but it can revert to its prior liquid state. It has not truly changed; it is merely in a practically altered condition. The distinction may also reflect whether the process is human-made and thus "manufactured" or natural — what is produced by a natural process remains, in a sense, part of the natural order and can be restored to it.

### Mani's Question

The *gemara* now introduces a related question. Rabbi Mani asked Rabbi Yochanan: *dam shekarash ve'achalo mahu* — if blood congealed and one ate it in its hardened state, is one *chayav*? This appears equivalent to the earlier case of *hikpah et hadam ve'achalo*. Rabbi Yochanan's answer: *ho'il venidchah yidacheh* — since it was already pushed off from eligibility for the *mizbe'ach* in its congealed state, it remains pushed off. One would therefore not be *chayav* for eating it.

### Dichuy Principle

This invokes the principle of *dichuy* — once an item is disqualified from its sacred purpose, it remains disqualified even if it later returns to a fit state. In the context of blood, the argument runs as follows: while the blood is congealed, it cannot be used for *zerikah* and is therefore *dachuy* — pushed off — from the *mizbe'ach*. The assumption underlying the prohibition against eating blood is that it is precisely the blood used on the *mizbe'ach*. If the blood was in a state where it could never have been used on the *mizbe'ach*, perhaps one should not be *chayav* for eating it. Rabbi Yochanan's ruling adopts this reasoning.

This *dichuy* framework also resonates with the broader question of *nosar*: blood that is left over does not lose its physical character as blood, yet it cannot be used for *zerikah*. In that case, however, the blood was never disqualified by a change of state — it simply became *nosar*. The comparison is instructive precisely because it highlights what is distinctive about a state-change disqualification.

### Inner vs Outer

Abaye, challenged by Rabbi Yochanan's silence in the face of the contradicting *braita*, offers a distinction: perhaps the difference lies between blood of the outer *chataot* — *chataos hachitzoniyos* — and the inner *chataos* — *chataos hapnimiyos*. Rav Chisda is then cited for the following rule: congealed blood of an outer *chatas*, if eaten, makes one *chayav*, because the outer *chatas* requires *lekicha vinesinah* — taking the blood and placing it on the *mizbe'ach* — and even congealed blood can be smeared onto the altar. You can take a piece of hardened blood and press it onto the *mizbe'ach*; therefore the blood remains in the halakhic category of valid *korban* blood, and one who eats it is *chayav*. However, blood of an inner *chatas*, if congealed, makes one *patur* if eaten, because the inner *chatas* requires *tevila vahaza'ah* — dipping and sprinkling — and congealed blood cannot be dipped or sprinkled. In that case, the blood is practically disqualified from its required purpose, and therefore eating it does not incur liability.

### Heat Transforms

Rashi and Tosafot are in essential agreement on the underlying structure, though Tosafot's version of Rashi differs from the text of Rashi before us, leading Tosafot to think he is arguing with Rashi when in fact they may agree. The position of our Rashi is as follows: anything transformed by fire — whether cooking or salting — is a complete and permanent transformation. The blood is categorically different, it is *patur* everywhere and for anyone, whether the blood of an inner *chatas*, an outer *chatas*, or ordinary blood from an animal slaughtered in a private context. Sunlight, by contrast, does not transform at all — it merely creates a temporary practical obstacle. The blood is not really different; it is just currently hard and cannot be sprinkled. Given this, the distinction between inner and outer *chatas* applies only to sun-congealed blood: only for the inner *chatas*, where the temporary hardening creates a complete practical disqualification for its specific mode of application, does the *dichuy* argument apply. For sun-hardened blood of the outer *chatas*, and for all ordinary blood (whether from a *korban* or not), the blood remains in its halakhic category and one is *chayav* for eating it.

This reading is critically important: it confines Ze'iri's teaching about cooked blood — and by extension salted blood — to fire-based transformation. The entire exemption of *dam shebishlo* and *dam shemalcho* applies only where fire or salt produces an irreversible state-change. The separate discussion of sun-congealed blood and the inner versus outer *chatas* is a distinct analysis with no bearing on the cooking or salting question.

## Halachic Application

### Tosafot Leniency

Tosafot seizes on Ze'iri's teaching and extends it into a broad practical principle: whenever meat is cooked without having been properly salted — as when a piece of meat went directly into the pot — the violation is at most *issur d'rabbanan*. The phrase *eino over alav* could technically mean only that there is no *kares*, while a *lav de'oraisa* might still apply. But Tosafot reads the *gemara* as eliminating even the *de'oraisa* prohibition entirely, leaving only a rabbinic prohibition. This has significant *halakhic* implications in cases of *safek* and in assessing the status of a pot in which unsalted meat was cooked.

### Rambam View

The Rambam never ruled this way. Reading the Rambam in *hilchos* *shechita*, the approach is striking. He rules that if one cooked a heart without first slitting it open to release the blood, one is not *chayav kares* for a bird's heart — because there is not a *kezayis* of blood in the heart of a bird. But for the heart of a large animal: *chayav kares sheharei yesh bo kezayis midam shebetoch halev shechayavim alav kares* — one is *chayav kares*, because there is a *kezayis* of blood that carries the *kares* liability. The Rambam thus essentially ignores the *gemara* of *dam shebishlo* entirely. Perhaps the Rambam held this *sugya* is not *le'halacha*, or perhaps he understood *eino over alav* as referring only to the absence of *kares* while a *de'oraisa lav* still applies. Either way, the Rambam treats cooked blood as carrying a full *de'oraisa* violation, and the extensive practice of generations of women carefully salting meat reflected exactly this understanding.

### Practical Case

The practical stakes of this dispute are real. A common scenario: one purchases a chicken and places it in the oven, only to discover afterward that the giblets — liver and other organs that require special salting or broiling — were left inside in their bag, unsalted, and cooked along with the chicken. The question then becomes whether the chicken and the pot are prohibited, how much leniency can be applied, whether *bitul* or *shishim* is relevant. If one holds that *dam shebishlo* is only *d'rabbanan*, that opens more latitude than if one treats it as a full *de'oraisa* with potential *kares*. As a practical matter, the experience of those who kept meticulous *hilchos dam* treated the prohibition as entirely *de'oraisa*, and the work of properly salting meat was taken with complete seriousness even if, reading this *gemara* carefully, Tosafot would characterize any residual blood after cooking as only *d'rabbanan*.

## Congealed Blood (II)

### Rava's View

The *gemara* now records Rava's independent position: *afillu b'chatas hapnimiyos ve'achalo chayav* — even if one ate blood of an inner *chatas* in its congealed state, one is *chayav*. Rava's reasoning: *ho'il u'k'negdo ra'uy b'chatas hachitzoniyos* — since this same type of blood, if it were blood of an outer *chatas*, could be used even in its congealed state (by smearing), it remains within the category of valid *korban* blood. Even the blood of an inner *chatas*, where congealed blood cannot be dipped and sprinkled, is not pushed out of the category of *korban* blood because the outer *chatas* validates congealed blood as a halakhically viable form of *korban* blood. One may not be *yotzei* the *korban* obligation by using it this way, but the person who ate it is nonetheless *chayav*.

### Donkey Blood

Rav Papa draws the logical conclusion: *hilkach, dam chamor shekarash ve'achalo chayav* — therefore, even donkey blood that congealed, if eaten, makes one *chayav*. Donkey blood cannot be used on the *mizbe'ach* at all, and it is congealed. Yet one is still *chayav* for eating it. The reasoning follows from Rava's principle: since blood of the same type — were it *kosher* animal blood — could be used on the outer *chatas* even in congealed form, it remains within the category of blood that carries the prohibition. Non-*kosher* animal blood, and even congealed blood, does not fall out of this category.

The implication for the overall discussion is significant. If the *gemara*'s discussion of congealed blood is understood as applying even to fire-congealed blood (and not only sun-congealed blood), then the entire discussion becomes moot — Rava and Rav Papa push back all congealed blood into the *chayav* category, and the only way to maintain Ze'iri's teaching as practically operative is to confine it exclusively to blood transformed by cooking or salting, which represents a complete and irreversible transformation in a way that mere congealing does not.

## Blood Chatzitza

### Blood Chatzitza

The *gemara* transitions to a related topic. Rav Gidel says in the name of Rav Iri: *dam bein lach bein yavesh chotzetz* — blood, whether dry or wet, creates a *chatzitza* for immersion in a *mikvah*. This would apply both to a person immersing and to a *kohen* performing *avoda* who has blood on his body.

### Dried vs Wet

A *mishnah* in Mikvaot is then cited which appears to contradict this: *hadam vehadio veha'dvash veha'chalav — yaveshim chotzetzim, lachim einam chotzetzim* — blood, ink, honey, and milk — when dry, they are a *chatzitza*; when wet, they are not, because in their wet state they are permeable to water and do not block it from reaching the skin.

### Adhesiveness

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — ha d'sarich, ha d'la sarich* — it depends on whether the substance is adhesive. Blood that is wet but sticky is a *chatzitza* because it blocks the water; blood that is wet but flows freely allows water through and is not a *chatzitza*. The principle is that adhesiveness, not mere wetness or dryness, determines whether a substance interposes between the body and the water of the *mikvah*. Honey presents a similar case — loose honey flows off, but thick or adhesive honey would remain and block the water.

## Salt Mitzvah

### Timlach Usage

The *gemara* now turns to the *mitzvah* of salting offerings and examines the precise language of the verse: *korban mincha b'melach timlach* — "every offering of your *mincha* you shall salt with salt." The word *melach* already indicates salt; what does *timlach* — the verbal form "you shall salt" — add?

### Sodomite Salt

A *braita* is cited: *b'melach* — with salt — *yachol tedunehu* — perhaps you should saturate it with salt — *talmud lomar timlach* — therefore the verse says "you shall salt it," meaning in the normal manner that people salt things. Continuing: *ei timlach* — if salted — *yachol b'mei melach* — perhaps even salt water would suffice — *talmud lomar b'melach* — no, only actual salt. Then: *v'lo tashbit melach* — "do not let salt rest from your *korban*" — *havei melach she'eina shovetet* — bring salt that does not rest. This means *melach Sedomit*, the salt of Sodom. This is sea salt — Sodom sits at the Dead Sea — and the sea is always producing salt, even on *Shabbat*, which is the reason it is called *she'eina shovetet*: it does not rest. The Aruch explains the phrase precisely this way: sea salt continues to wash up on shores even on *Shabbat*, unlike mineral salt, which requires human labor and therefore would not be mined on *Shabbat*.

### Salt Types

If *melach Sedomit* — sea salt — is the ideal, the *gemara* asks: how do we know that if sea salt is unavailable, one may substitute mineral salt — *melach istrokanit*? From the word *takriv* in the verse, which the *gemara* interprets as *takriv mikol makom* — bring it from wherever available — and *takriv kol shehu* — any type of salt will do. The word *takriv* also teaches: *va'afilu beShabbat* — even on *Shabbat*, the salting of offerings is required; *va'afilu betumah* — even in a state of *tumah*, as is true for any *korban tzibbur* brought in conditions of communal *tumah*, the salting requirement applies equally. Rashi adds that *takriv mikol makom* further means the salt need not come from *Eretz Yisrael* specifically.

### Tavunuhu Debate

The *braita* also states: *yachol tedunehu*, which the *gemara* now attempts to parse. What does *tedunehu* mean? Rabbah bar bar Chana suggests it comes from the word *teven* — straw — meaning one might have thought you should pack the offering in salt the way one packs straw into plaster, an enormous quantity. The verse then comes to teach the normal mode of salting. Abaye objects that the grammar would require *yitbonenu* if the root were *teven*, not *tedunehu*. Abaye himself suggests *tedunehu* means to build up salt layer upon layer, like a structure — *kevinyan*. Rava in turn objects that the grammar for that meaning should be *yivnenahu*. 

### Binah Metaphor

Rav Ashi offers a different reading: *yachol yitein bo ta'am kevinah* — perhaps one should add just a small amount of salt, enough to give a hint of flavor, like *binah* — wisdom — which takes what is dull and gives it depth and refinement, just as a small amount of wisdom elevates a person without overwhelming them. On this reading, the *hava amina* was to use only a tiny, symbolic amount of salt. The verse teaches that the normal manner of salting is required — neither too little nor excessively much.

The Aruch, cited in the margin of the *gemara*, offers yet another interpretation of *tedunehu*: even a small amount, enough to taste the salt — *ad kedei lehavin sheyesh bo ta'am melach*. The verse then teaches that the required amount is the normal way people salt things, which is more substantial.

### Salting Method

The *gemara* clarifies the practical method: *meivi ha'eiver venoten alav melach, vechozer vehofcho, venoten alav melach, umaleihu* — one brings the limb, places salt on it, flips it, places salt on the other side, and then places it on the *mizbe'ach*. One full layer of salt on each side, nothing perfunctory but nothing excessively heavy.

### Pot Comparison

Abaye then adds: *v'chen lakdeira* — and so too for the pot. These two words are of great halakhic significance, and there is a disputed *girsa*: some texts read *v'chen litzli* — and so too for roasting. The difference is substantial. For roasting on an open fire, the blood flows out easily and one does not strictly need salt. If the *girsa* is *v'chen litzli*, then the method described — one layer on each side — is a minimal, essentially perfunctory application, and the same light touch would apply to *korbanot* on the *mizbe'ach*. If the *girsa* is *v'chen lakdeira*, salting for the pot, then the method is more meaningful: the salt must have real force to extract blood without the assistance of heat. On this reading, the method of salting for *korbanot* requires genuine salting, not merely a token gesture — but the verse equally teaches that it should not be excessive, since the *braita* describes a defined and moderate method. The same moderate, effective salt application then informs the halakha of *melicha* for *kashrut*. Sefer Rabbeinu Gershom preserves the *girsa* of *v'chen litzli*, which Tosafot notes creates a certain tension with what precedes.

## Salt & Me'ilah

### Me'ilah Status

A *braita* rules: *melach she'al gabei ha'eiver mo'alin bo* — salt that has been placed on the offering itself is subject to *me'ilah*. But salt *she'al gabei hakeves v'sheberosh hamizbe'ach* — salt on the ramp or at the top of the *mizbe'ach*, before it has been placed on the actual *korban* — is not subject to *me'ilah*. Once the salt is applied to the *korban*, it becomes like the *korban* itself and carries the full sanctity. Before that moment, it is available for use and has not yet achieved the status of *me'ilah*.

### Pesuk Source

Rav Papa asks: what is the *pasuk* source? *Hikravtem lifnei Hashem ve'hishlichu hakohanim aleihem melach, vehe'alu otam olah laHashem* — "you shall bring them before God, and the *kohanim* shall cast salt upon them, and bring them up as an offering to God." The *gemara* reads *vehe'alu* — "bring them up" — in the plural, encompassing the salt together with the offering, indicating that once the salt is placed, it is drawn into the sanctity of the *korban* and subject to *me'ilah*.

### Shekalim Source

The *gemara* cites a *braita* from the Talmud Yerushalmi of Shekalim: one of the stipulations of the *Beit Din* was that the *kohanim* were permitted to make use of the wood and salt in the *Mikdash* — *al hamelach v'al ha'eitzim she'hayu hakohanim ne'eisim bahem*. This means that *kohanim* could benefit from these materials without incurring *me'ilah*. For example, a *kohen* working at the top of the *mizbe'ach* on a cold day could warm himself by the fire without concern for *me'ilah*. The *gemara* will now apply this principle specifically to salt.

## Salt Locations

### Chamber Salt

A *braita* identifies three locations in the *Mikdash* where salt was stored, each serving a distinct purpose. The first is *lishkat hamelach*, the chamber of salt. Salt from this room was used to salt the hides — *oros kodshim* — of the offerings. Processing the leather of an *olah* or a *chatas* was necessary to make it useful, and the salt from the chamber served this function.

### Ramp Salt

The second location is the ramp, the *keves*. Salt kept there was used to salt the *eivarim* — the limbs of the animals — and the internal organs, all the meat that would be placed on the *mizbe'ach*. This salting was done on the ramp, as the *kohanim* carried the limbs upward toward the altar.

### Top Salt

The third location is the top of the *mizbe'ach*, *rosh hamizbe'ach*. Salt there was used for the non-meat items: *hakometz vehalevona* — the *kometz* and the *levonah*; *haktoret* — the *ketoreth*; *minchas kohanim* and *minchas kohen mashiach* — the *mincha* of the *kohanim* and of the *kohen mashuach*, which are wholly burnt; *minchas nesachim* — the *mincha* accompanying the wine libations; and *olas ha'of* — the bird *olah*. The appearance of *ketoreth* on this list requires comment: *ketoreth* is burnt on the *mizbe'ach hazahav*, the inner golden altar, not on the outer *mizbe'ach*. Why is it salted at the top of the outer *mizbe'ach*?

### Cleanliness Reason

Tosafot explains that the separation between locations reflects a concern for cleanliness. The area where animal limbs are salted is bloody; the area where non-meat items are salted should be kept free of that. Therefore, items like *ketoreth* — even though they will ultimately be burnt on the inner *mizbe'ach* — are salted in the clean space at the top of the outer *mizbe'ach*, rather than in the bloodied salting area on the ramp. The *kohen* would salt the *ketoreth* at the top of the outer *mizbe'ach*, then descend and carry it to the *mizbe'ach hazahav* inside the *heichal*. This is admittedly an indirect route, but it reflects the deliberate organizational logic of the three distinct salting zones.

## Salt Usage Rules

### Shmuel View

The *gemara* now addresses what use *kohanim* may make of the *Mikdash* salt under the *Beit Din*'s stipulation. Shmuel rules: *lo shanu ela l'korbanan* — the permission applies only *l'korbanan*, for use in connection with their *korban*. But *achal lo* — for eating, they may not use the *Mikdash* salt. This requires clarification: what does it mean to use the salt "for their *korban*"? And what does "for eating" mean?

### Permitted Uses

The initial reading of Shmuel's statement is that *kohanim* may use the salt to salt the *korban* for burning on the *mizbe'ach*, but not to salt the meat of the *korban* for their own eating. However, this creates an immediate problem.

### Prohibited Uses

The *gemara* objects: if *kohanim* are permitted to use *Mikdash* salt to salt the *oros kodshim* — the hides of the *korbanot* — for their personal use and benefit, surely they should be permitted to use it to salt the *korban* meat they are eating, which is itself a *mitzvah*. Eating *kodashim* is a halakhically mandated act; it can hardly be less permitted than tanning leather. How then can Shmuel restrict the salt?

### Use Distinction

The *gemara* therefore reinterprets Shmuel. *Mai l'korbanan* — what does "for their *korban*" mean? It means *l'achilat korbanan* — for eating the meat of their *korban*. And *mai l'achila* — what does "eating" that is prohibited mean? *Achila d'chulin* — eating non-*korban* food. *Kohanim* were instructed to eat a quantity of ordinary food, or *terumah*, before eating the *korban* itself, so that they would come to the *korban* in a state of *sova* — fullness — and eat it with dignity rather than hunger. One might have thought that since this ordinary eating serves the purpose of *korban* consumption, the *Mikdash* salt could be used for it too. Shmuel teaches: no. Even though that eating serves a *korban* purpose, the salt from the *Mikdash* may not be used for food that is not itself *kodashim*. The distinction is between direct use on *kodashim* — permitted — and serving a *korban* purpose only indirectly — prohibited. Using the salt to preserve the leather of a *korban*, to salt the meat of a *korban*, or to salt any item directly categorized as *kodashim* is permitted. Using it for *chulin*, even *chulin* consumed as part of the process of eating a *korban*, is not.
