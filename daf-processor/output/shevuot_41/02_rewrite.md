# Shevuot 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Modeh B'miktzas

### Shmuel's Cases

The daf begins approximately twenty lines before the bottom of 40b, at the passage commencing with the words *Rav Anan*. The Gemara there picks up a series of statements transmitted in the name of Shmuel concerning the precise requirements for *modeh b'miktzas* — the partial admission that triggers a biblical oath obligation — and, in particular, what counts as an admission that is *mimin hata'ana*, of the same type as the original claim.

### Wheat & Barley

The first case: *tano chitim vekodam vehodo lo bese'orim* — the claimant said "you owe me wheat," and was about to add "and barley," but before he could finish, the defendant pre-empted him and said, "No, I owe you barley." Shmuel's earlier statement had established that had the claimant actually said "wheat and barley" and the defendant admitted to the barley, that would constitute a valid *modeh b'miktzas* — it would be *mimin hata'ana*, of the same category as the claim — and the defendant would be obligated in a *shvua*. But in this case, only "wheat" came out of the claimant's mouth, and the defendant responded "barley." He has not admitted to any part of the stated claim, and on the face of it he appears to be a *kofer hakol*, a total denier, and would be exempt from the *shvua*.

### Intent & Scheming

The Gemara resolves the ambiguity as follows: *im kimarim chayav ve'im b'miskaven patur* — if the defendant was acting as a schemer, meaning he knew the claimant was about to include barley and deliberately cut him off in order to prevent that word from being spoken, then he is obligated in a *shvua*. But if he was acting innocently — he heard "wheat," he said "no, barley," with no strategic intent — then he is exempt.

The precise nature of the obligation in the first case is itself unclear. One possibility is that it is only a rabbinic obligation: technically, on a biblical level, the defendant has escaped the *modeh b'miktzas* structure, but the rabbis impose a *shvua* where manipulation is detected. Alternatively, the very definition of what constitutes the *ta'ana*, the claim, may be contextually determined: if the defendant demonstrably knew that "barley" was about to be part of the claim and was effectively responding to it, then the claim did include barley, and the admission falls squarely within *mimin hata'ana* even at the biblical level.

### Two Vessels

The Gemara continues: *tano shenei machtim vehodo lo b'achas mehen chayav* — if the claimant demanded two needles and the defendant admitted to one of them, he is obligated. This spells out a principle discussed earlier: *keilim*, vessels or discrete objects, operate under a distinct standard for *modeh b'miktzas*. The normal threshold requires a claim of at least two *ma'ah* — a substantial monetary amount — but in the case of *keilim*, the two-item structure is itself sufficient, regardless of market value. The Gemara phrases this as *lefikach yatzo keilim lemah shehen* — that is why *keilim* are treated as their own category, whatever their monetary worth. Tosafot qualifies this by insisting that each needle must still be worth at least a *pruta* in order to have the requisite significance, but the two-*shekel* threshold does not apply.

### Rav Pappa's Case

Rav Pappa introduces a further refinement: *tano keilim uperuta vehodo b'keilim vekhafar b'peruta patur* — if the claimant demanded two needles and a *pruta* and the defendant admitted to the needles but denied the *pruta*, he is exempt. Conversely, *hodo b'peruta vekhafar b'keilim chayav* — if he admitted to the *pruta* and denied the needles, he is obligated.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

The Gemara explains this outcome elegantly: *chada k'Rav vechada k'Shmuel* — one of these rulings follows Rav and one follows Shmuel. Rav had maintained that *kefiras ta'anas shte kessef* — not only must the claim reach the two-*ma'ah* threshold, but the denial itself must be of sufficient magnitude, either two *ma'ah* in value or two *keilim* in kind. In Rav Pappa's case, the claim is certainly sufficient — it includes two needles and a *pruta*. The question is whether the denial reaches the required standard. If the defendant denied the *pruta* and admitted the needles, the denial is too small, and he is exempt — this is *k'Rav*, following Rav's standard for the denial. If, however, he admitted the *pruta* and denied the two needles, the denial is substantial — two discrete *keilim* — and he is obligated. That ruling, in turn, follows Shmuel's principle that if two distinct types of items are claimed (*chitim use'orim*) and the defendant admits one category while denying the other, the partial admission constitutes *modeh b'miktzas* even though what was denied is entirely separate from what was conceded. Notably, Rav did not dispute Shmuel on this point. The conclusion thus synthesizes both positions: the denial must be of sufficient magnitude (Rav), and admission to one of two distinct categories of items suffices as a partial admission (Shmuel).

## Kofer HaKol

### Rav Nachman's Oath

The Gemara now returns, with the two-dot marker, to the Mishnah. The Mishnah states that if someone demands a hundred *zuz* and the other party responds "I owe you nothing," the denier is exempt — *kofer hakol patur*. The natural reaction to this is astonishment: is a total denial truly without any consequence whatsoever? The Gemara immediately addresses this. *Amar Rav Nachman mashbi'in oto shvuat hesset* — Rav Nachman rules that we do make the denier take a *shvuat hesset*.

### Presumption

What is a *shvuat hesset*? The word *hesset* likely means something like "persuasion" — Rashi understands it as a *shvua* that rabbinic authority effectively compelled; the individual technically has no biblical obligation, yet the rabbis pressed the point with considerable force. More substantively, the *shvuat hesset* is a generic rabbinic *shvua* distinct from the small, carefully defined set of cases of *nishba'in venotlin* — those specific situations where a claimant with strong circumstantial support is allowed to swear and collect. The *shvuat hesset* is far broader: it applies essentially any time a denial is made without corroboration on either side.

The rationale for imposing it on the *kofer hakol* is stated plainly: *chazakah ein adam tove'a ela im ken yesh lo alav* — there is a presumption that a person does not press a monetary claim unless there is some legitimate basis for it. If the claimant says you owe me a hundred *zuz*, the mere fact of that demand raises enough suspicion that we require the defendant to back his denial with a *shvua* before we let him walk away.

### Stalling vs. Lying

The Gemara immediately raises a counterpoint: *chazakah ein adam me'iz panav bifnei ba'al hovo* — there is also a presumption that a person would not have the audacity to deny a debt to the face of a creditor. If he denies it, perhaps he is telling the truth and the presumption favors him. The Gemara rejects this: *ishtamutei hu deka mishtamit lei* — he is merely stalling. The debtor may know perfectly well that he owes the money but is buying time: *savar ad hava lei parana lei*, he thinks, "let me get the funds together and I'll pay him later." The social pressure of total denial is therefore insufficient evidence of innocence, and the *shvuat hesset* remains appropriate.

### Loan vs. Deposit

The Gemara then cites a ruling of Rav Idi bar Avin in the name of Rav Chisda that illuminates the nature of this stalling behavior: *hakofer b'milveh kasher l'eidus, b'pikadon pasul l'eidus* — one who falsely denies a loan remains valid as a witness, whereas one who falsely denies holding a deposit is disqualified as a witness.

What distinguishes the two? When someone denies a loan, even if witnesses subsequently demonstrate that the denial was false, the denial is understood as a form of stalling — the debtor was not intending to permanently deprive the lender of his money, only to delay. Such a lie, motivated by temporary embarrassment or financial difficulty rather than an intent to steal, does not constitute the kind of moral corruption that disqualifies a witness. A deposit is different. If someone has an object in his possession and denies holding it at all, that denial can only be understood as an intent to steal — there is no temporal reprieve logic available. That constitutes a fundamental dishonesty that does invalidate witness testimony.

The implication for *shvuat hesset* is that the denial of a loan, even if false, reflects a kind of defensible human weakness rather than genuine wickedness — which is precisely why the mere fact of denial is not itself determinative. We therefore require a *shvua* to supplement the denial, rather than simply believing or disbelieving the defendant outright.

### Both Cases

The Gemara notes that *Rav Chavivi matni a'seifa* — Rav Chavivi taught Rav Nachman's ruling in connection with the second part of the Mishnah rather than the first. The second part of the Mishnah deals with a case where the defendant had initially admitted the debt — "yes, you are owed a hundred *zuz*" — and then the following day claimed to have already paid it. There, the existence of the debt is established; the question is only whether it was discharged. Rav Nachman imposes the *shvuat hesset* on this denier as well.

The Gemara then observes: *man d'matni a'reisha kol sheken a'seifa* — whoever holds that the *shvuat hesset* applies even in the first case, where there is no prior evidence of any debt at all, would certainly apply it in the second case, where the debt's prior existence is known. The *shvuat hesset* in the first version is therefore the more expansive ruling, and the second case is virtually self-evident by comparison.

## Drora D'Mamona

### Connection Req'd

According to the second version of Rav Nachman's statement — the one attributed to Rav Chavivi — the *shvuat hesset* requires *drora d'mamona*: an established monetary connection between the parties. The case of the second part of the Mishnah satisfies this because the debt was already admitted. *Aval hatam d'leika drora d'mamona* — but in a case where no such basis exists, where the claimant's demand emerges entirely without prior foundation, the *shvuat hesset* would not apply. The term *drora d'mamona* combines *mamona*, money, with *drora*, understood by the *rishonim* as something like *shaychus* — a connection or linkage — indicating that the claimant has some established, demonstrable nexus to the money being claimed, not merely a bare assertion.

### Tosafot Ruling

The question then arises: what is the *halacha*? Tosafot, citing Rabbeinu Tam, rules as follows: *hashta d'lo itmar hilcheta k'chad meihanei lishni* — since neither version of Rav Nachman's statement has been definitively adopted, we are left with two competing positions. In a case where there is no *drora d'mamona*, should we nonetheless impose the *shvuat hesset*? Tosafot applies the principle of *hamotzi meichavero alav hare'aya* — the burden of proof rests on the claimant — to the *shvua* context as well: just as we cannot extract money from a defendant without proof, we cannot extract a *shvua* from him without sufficient basis. Therefore, where there is doubt whether the *shvuat hesset* applies, we do not impose it.

At the same time, Rabbeinu Tam acknowledges that he did not object to judges who regularly applied the *shvuat hesset* even without *drora d'mamona* — judges found it useful for promoting candor and giving their verdicts greater weight. Tosafot further notes that there is something to be said for the first version of Rav Nachman's ruling — that the *shvuat hesset* applies even without any prior basis — since among the two versions of a Talmudic statement, the first formulation is typically considered the primary one and carries greater weight. However, Rabbeinu Tam adds that when there is an *ika d'amri* — an alternate formulation — we follow the more lenient view, and since imposing a *shvua* without *drora d'mamona* is the more demanding position, we refrain from it.

### Shepherd Case

Tosafot then discusses what constitutes *drora d'mamona* by reference to a case in the first chapter of Bava Metzia involving a shepherd. In that case, there was an established pattern: each day, a flock would be entrusted to a shepherd's care. On one particular day, the owners claimed to have handed over sheep without witnesses, and the shepherd denied receiving them. The court imposed a *shvuat hesset*. The question is whether this constitutes a case with *drora d'mamona* or not. On that specific day, there is no evidence of a transaction. But the pattern of daily dealings over an extended period creates a strong contextual probability that the handover occurred. Rabbeinu Tam holds that this suffices as *drora d'mamona* — the established course of dealings provides the necessary nexus to the claimed money, even in the absence of witnesses to that day's transaction.

## Shevua D'Oraita

### Mehapaich Shvua

With the nature of the *shvuat hesset* established, the Gemara turns to the structural differences between a *shvua d'Oraita* and a *shvua d'Rabbanan*. The question posed is: *mai ika bein shvua d'Oraita lishvua d'Rabbanan?* — what precisely distinguishes them? The first answer offered is *mehapaich shvua* — the reversibility of the oath obligation. This refers to the option of a party who is required to take an oath instead turning to the other side and saying: "I do not wish to swear; you swear, and I will pay."

### Mar bar Rav Ashi

The Gemara initially states that by a *shvua d'Oraita*, the oath cannot be reversed — *lo mehaphinan*. If the *nitva* (defendant) refuses to swear, he simply pays; he cannot transfer the obligation to the *tove'a* (claimant). By a *shvua d'Rabbanan*, however, the oath can be reversed — *mehaphinan*. The *nitva* may decline and place the *shvua* on the other party, who must then swear or forgo collection. Mar bar Rav Ashi disputes this, holding that even a *shvua d'Oraita* may be reversed. According to his position, if a defendant in a *modeh b'miktzas* situation refuses to swear and instead demands that the claimant swear, the claimant is indeed presented with that option. If the claimant also declines, both parties would presumably walk away.

### Seizure Question

If Mar bar Rav Ashi holds that reversal is available even for a *d'Oraita* oath, what then distinguishes a *shvua d'Oraita* from a *shvua d'Rabbanan*? The Gemara answers: *mechas l'nechsei* — whether the court proceeds to seize the defendant's property. By a *d'Oraita* obligation, if the defendant refuses to swear and refuses to reverse the oath, the court will send the equivalent of the sheriff to seize his assets. By a *d'Rabbanan* obligation — specifically a *shvuat hesset* — under one view, the court acknowledges the obligation to pay but will not back it up through seizure of property.

### Rav Yosef View

Rav Yosef, however, disputes this asymmetry. He holds that even rabbinic monetary obligations are backed by court enforcement. His position is derived from a broader principle: the Gemara cites the case of a *cheresh, shoteh, veikatan* — a deaf-mute, imbecile, or minor — who finds a lost object. There is a view that the object is "theirs" only *mipnei darchei shalom*, for the sake of civil order, without conferring true legal ownership; accordingly, if someone takes it from them, the court would not intervene. Rav Yosef, however, holds that the rabbis declared such a finder a *gezel gamur* in the rabbinic sense — a genuine owner by rabbinic decree — and therefore the court would compel restitution if someone takes the object away. The Gemara reads this disagreement as touching a global principle: whenever a rabbinic ruling creates an obligation or a right, does the court actively enforce it through seizure? The *chachamim* say no; Rav Yosef says yes. Applied to *shvuat hesset*, if the defendant refuses to swear, Rav Yosef would hold that the court treats him as owing the money and would seize assets accordingly.

### Chashud

A further distinction between *d'Oraita* and *d'Rabbanan* concerns the *chashud al hashvua* — a person suspected of swearing falsely, such as a known gambler or one previously found to have taken a false oath, who consequently cannot be asked to swear at all. By a *shvua d'Oraita*, when the defendant is disqualified from taking the oath, the court reverses the obligation: *afchinan lei shvua veshadina aidach* — the oath is transferred to the claimant, who swears and collects. By a *shvuat hesset*, however, this further step is not taken — *takanta letakanta lo avdinan* — we do not stack one rabbinic remedy atop another. The result is that a defendant known to swear falsely is, paradoxically, released from liability entirely when the underlying obligation is only rabbinic. He cannot be made to swear, and the *tove'a* cannot swear in his place.

## Refusing Oath

### Cherem Imposed

Returning to the view that by a *shvuat hesset* the court does not proceed to seizure of property, the Gemara asks: *mai avdinan lei?* — what do we do with someone who refuses to take the *shvuat hesset*? The answer is: *meshamtinan lei* — we place him in *cherem*, rabbinic excommunication. The social and communal pressure of *cherem* functions as a substitute for the coercive power of physical enforcement.

### Crude Force

Ravina challenges Rav Ashi: is *cherem* really so different from outright coercion? If *cherem* effectively forces the person to comply — Rashi notes the passage here employs a crude expression, describing the process as grabbing the man by a part of his anatomy in order to compel him to surrender his cloak — then the pressure exerted is functionally indistinguishable from sending in the sheriff.

## Refusing Oath…

### 30-Day Cherem

Rav Ashi responds: *meshamtinan lei ad d'mati zman negdei* — the *cherem* is maintained for thirty days, after which, under the *halacha*, one who remains under *cherem* without rectifying the matter receives rabbinic lashes, and is then released. The structure is therefore: thirty days of *cherem*, followed by lashes, followed by release. It functions like a contempt-of-court sentence of fixed duration. After that period, if the person is willing to undergo *cherem* and lashes rather than pay, he is in principle released. The practical reality is that this does represent a meaningful difference from a *d'Oraita* obligation, where property seizure would occur regardless of the defendant's willingness to endure communal sanction.

## Shtar Rules

### Debt Document

The Gemara now introduces a structurally inverse case. Rather than a claimant appearing with nothing more than a bare assertion, here the claimant produces a *shtar* — a written debt instrument with witnesses — establishing that David owes him a hundred *zuz*. David responds that the debt has been fully paid. *Lav kol kminach, zil shalim* — the court tells David he is not believed; go pay. A defendant's bare claim of repayment is insufficient to overcome a *shtar* in the other party's possession.

### Debtor Reversal

But suppose David then says: I will pay, but I want the creditor to take a *shvua* first that the debt is not yet satisfied. *Aminan lei ishtava lei* — the Gemara says we tell the creditor: go ahead and swear to him. So even when the claimant has the full weight of a *shtar* behind him, the defendant may nonetheless demand a *shvuat hesset* before paying.

### Pogem Shtaro

Rav Acha the son of Rav Yosef raises an objection to Rav Ashi: *u'mah bein zeh lepogem et shtaro?* — how is this different from the established rabbinic case of *pogem shtaro*, the creditor who himself concedes that part of the debt recorded in the *shtar* has been repaid? In that case, the rabbis specifically ordained that the creditor must take a *shvua* before collecting the remainder, precisely because his admission that part was paid introduces doubt about the whole. But if a *shvua* can be demanded even when the *shtar* is entirely intact and uncontested, why was it necessary to institute a special rule for *pogem shtaro*?

The Gemara responds: *hasam af al gav delo ta'in ihu taninan lei* — in the case of *pogem shtaro*, even if the defendant does not raise the demand himself, the court proactively imposes the *shvua* on the creditor. *Taninan lei anan* — we make the argument on the defendant's behalf. Here, by contrast — *hacha amrinan lei zil shalim lei* — the court tells the defendant to go pay; we do not suggest the *shvua* unprompted. It is only *v'i ta'in v'amar ishtava li* — if David expressly demands it — that the court then responds, *zil ishtava lei*, go and swear. The distinction is between a court-initiated oath and a defendant-initiated demand.

### Tzurva Exception

The Gemara introduces a further qualification: *v'i tzurva meRabanan hu lo mashbi'inan lei* — if the claimant is a *tzurva meRabanan*, a Torah scholar, we do not require him to take the *shvua* even upon the defendant's demand. The rationale is that demanding a *shvua* from a scholar implies a suspicion of dishonesty that is incompatible with his standing. Notably, this exemption is not mentioned in ordinary cases of *shvuat hesset* — a *tzurva meRabanan* presumably takes a *shvuat hesset* like everyone else — but here, where the scholar has the additional backing of a *shtar*, the combination is held sufficient to waive the *shvua* demand entirely.

### Objection

Rav Yeimar objects to Rav Ashi: *tzurva meRabanan mashlach glima d'inshi?* — does a Torah scholar have the right to strip people of their cloaks? If we exempt him from the *shvua* that any other creditor would have to take, we are effectively granting him an immunity that allows him to collect without any check on his claim — that is, to potentially rob people with impunity. This is reminiscent of the earlier discussion about whether a *tzurva meRabanan* should sit or stand in court relative to the other litigant. Favoritism that compromises the integrity of the judicial process cannot be tolerated.

### Case Delayed

The Gemara's resolution is striking: *lo mizdakkinan lei l'dinei* — the court simply never hears his case. Unable to ask the scholar to take a *shvua* (which would impugn his integrity) and equally unable to exempt him from it without creating an unjust favoritism, the court resolves the tension by indefinitely deferring his claim. A *tzurva meRabanan* with a *shtar* in hand — even a substantial debt document — will find his case perpetually delayed. This is, as the lecturer observes, a paradoxical outcome: the defendant's bad faith in demanding a *shvua* is accommodated at the scholar's expense, and the scholar is in effect penalized for his status. Yet that appears to be the Gemara's conclusion.

## Witnesses Required

### Rav Yehuda

The Gemara introduces a ruling in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Rav Assi: *hamalveh et chaveiro b'eidim tzarich l'poro b'eidim* — one who lends money to another in the presence of witnesses must be repaid in the presence of witnesses. Implicit in this ruling is that if the borrower cannot produce witnesses to the repayment, his claim of having paid will not be credited.

### Shmuel Position

When Rav Yehuda presented this ruling before Shmuel, Shmuel responded that the borrower may say: *paraticha bifnei ploni u'ploni vehalchu lahem limdinat hayam* — I paid you in the presence of so-and-so and so-and-so, and they have since gone overseas. In other words, Shmuel holds that even if repayment required witnesses, the borrower is not actually obligated to produce those witnesses. He may claim they were present and have since become unavailable. The practical upshot is that, for Shmuel, even a loan made with witnesses does not in the end subject the borrower to a strict production requirement; the bare claim of witnessed repayment is accepted.

### Mishnah Conflict

The Gemara notes a tension with the Mishnah. The Mishnah's earlier case — where the defendant initially admitted the debt and the following day claimed to have paid — appears to address a situation where the acknowledgment was made in the presence of witnesses. That, says the Gemara, is analogous to a loan made with witnesses (*k'man d'ozfay b'eidim dami*), and yet the Mishnah states that a claim of repayment is believed — *patur*. This seems to support Shmuel's lenient view. The Gemara identifies this as a potential *tyuvta* against Rav Assi's stricter position.

### Rav Assi

Rav Assi deflects: *ana ki amri heicha d'me'ikara ozfay b'eidim d'lo leideihimnei* — I only said that witnesses must be produced if the loan was originally made with witnesses specifically *because* the lender signaled from the outset that he did not trust the borrower and expected all transactions to be fully documented. In the Mishnah's case, the original loan was not made with witnesses; it was only the subsequent admission of debt that occurred in their presence. That circumstance does not carry the same implication of distrust and does not trigger the witness-production requirement. Therefore, the Mishnah's ruling that the borrower is believed is consistent with Rav Assi's position.

### Rav Assi

Rav Yosef teaches a variant version of the exchange. In this version, the initial ruling of Rav Assi is actually the lenient one: *hamalveh et chaveiro b'eidim eino tzarich l'poro b'eidim* — even if the loan was made with witnesses, no witnesses are required for repayment, absent an explicit stipulation. Only if the lender expressly stipulated — *al tifreini ela b'eidim*, do not pay me without witnesses — is the borrower then obligated to repay with witnesses, and presumably to produce them as proof.

### Baraita Debate

When Rav Yehuda then presented Shmuel's claim to Shmuel himself in this formulation, Shmuel responded identically: even if the lender stipulated that repayment must occur before witnesses, the borrower may still claim *paraticha bifnei ploni u'ploni vehalchu lahem limdinat hayam* — that he paid in the presence of witnesses who are no longer available. For Shmuel, the condition is interpreted narrowly: it governs the modality of repayment, not the evidentiary standard for proving it later. Unless the lender spelled out explicitly both that witnesses must be present *and* that no claim of repayment will be credited without their production, Shmuel holds that the condition does not compel the borrower to produce them.

The Gemara then examines a *baraita* that appears to contradict Shmuel. The *baraita* states: if the lender says "I lent you with witnesses, repay me with witnesses" — *o yiten o yavi ra'aya she-nasati* — then the borrower must either pay now or bring proof that he already paid, implying that the witnesses must be produced. This supports the stricter reading. However, Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira in the same *baraita* endorses Shmuel's position, holding that the borrower may satisfy the condition by claiming the witnesses went overseas.

### Rabbi Yehuda

The *baraita* therefore represents a genuine dispute among *tanna'im*: one view holds that a stipulation requiring witnesses means the witnesses must be produced for the claim of repayment to be accepted; the other, endorsed by Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira and paralleling Shmuel's position, holds that the stipulation governs the act of repayment but not the evidentiary burden.

### Rav Pappa Ruling

Rav Pappa rules in the name of Rava: *hamalveh chaveiro b'eidim tzarich l'poro b'eidim* — if the loan was made with witnesses, repayment must be made with witnesses. This is the most demanding of the positions and represents the formal ruling. However, Rav Pappa also clarifies: if there were no witnesses at the time of the loan and no explicit stipulation was made, then no witnesses are required for repayment — *ein tzarich l'poro b'eidim*. This is the generally accepted *psak* throughout the *Shas*. But if the lender stipulated *al tifreini ela b'eidim* — do not pay me without witnesses — then witnesses are required.

### Unresolved

The final and most contested question — whether the borrower who claims *paraticha bifnei ploni u'ploni vehalchu lahem limdinat hayam* is believed — remains unresolved. The text as standardly understood reads *ne'eman* — believed, following Shmuel — but the *girsa* of the Rambam reads *eino ne'eman*, not believed. The straightforward implication of the Mishnah, and the logical force of demanding witnesses at all, points strongly to the stricter position: if a stipulation requiring witnesses was made, the borrower should have to produce them, and cannot escape the requirement by claiming unavailability. Yet Shmuel — supported by Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira — holds otherwise. The Gemara does not definitively resolve which version governs, leaving the ruling dependent on one's textual tradition.
