# Eruvin 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Me'abrin Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד מְעַבְּרִין אֶת הֶעָרִים? בַּיִת נִכְנָס בַּיִת יוֹצֵא, פִּגּוּם נִכְנָס פִּגּוּם יוֹצֵא, הָיוּ שָׁם גְּדוּדִיּוֹת גְּבוֹהוֹת עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How does one extend** the boundaries of **cities** in order to ensure that all its protrusions are included within the borders of the city? He extends a straight line across the edge of the city, and if **a house is recessed and** another **house protrudes,** or **a turret [*pagum*] is recessed and** another **turret protrudes** from that line, and similarly, if **there were remnants** of walls **ten handbreadths high,**

We open on 53b, the beginning of a new *perek*. The *mishnah* that opens this chapter will serve primarily as a jumping-off point for several pages of *aggadeta*. The *mishnah* introduces the concept of *keitzad me'abrin* — how do we extend the boundaries of a city — not to be confused with *me'arvin*, which relates to *eiruv*. The term *me'abrin* derives from *ibbur*, like a pregnant woman, because the process involves squaring off the protrusions of the city to generate additional space, so that the city, as it were, "juts out" beyond its natural perimeter. That is the conceptual backdrop against which the *gemara* proceeds.

### Alef vs. Ayin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל: חַד תָּנֵי ״מְעַבְּרִין״ וְחַד תָּנֵי ״מְאַבְּרִין״. מַאן דְּתָנֵי ״מְאַבְּרִין״ — אֵבֶר אֵבֶר, וּמַאן דְּתָנֵי ״מְעַבְּרִין״ — כְּאִשָּׁה עוּבָּרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara cites a dispute with regard to the mishna's terminology. **Rav and Shmuel** disagreed: **One taught** that the term in the mishna is ***me'abberin*,** with the letter *ayin*, **and one taught** that the term in the mishna is ***me'abberin*,** with the letter *alef*. The Gemara explains: **The one who taught *me'abberin*** with an *alef* explained the term in the sense of **limb** [***ever***] by **limb.** Determination of the city's borders involves the addition of limbs to the core section of the city. **And the one who taught *me'abberin*** with an *ayin* explained the term in the sense of **a pregnant woman** [***ubbera***] whose belly protrudes. In similar fashion, all the city's protrusions are incorporated in its Shabbat limit.

The *gemara* opens with a dispute between Rav and Shmuel. One of them (*chad tani*) has the version of the *mishnah* spelling *me'abrin* with an *ayin* — *ibbur*, the root connoting pregnancy — while the other (*ve'chad tani*) reads *me'avrin* with an *alef*, connoting *eiver*, a limb, since the process adds limbs to the city's outline. The *gemara* explains both readings: the *alef* version relates to the extension of the city limb by limb, while the *ayin* version compares the process to a pregnant woman, whose form extends outward in an irregular shape that is then squared off.

### Guttural Loss

It is worth noting here that other *gemarot* reflect the phenomenon that in the more Hellenized, Greek-Roman cities of the Land of Israel, the distinction between *ayin* and *alef* was being lost — a direct consequence of the difficulty speakers of a non-Semitic language have with guttural consonants. In a fully oral culture, one would expect that the audible difference between *ayin* and *alef* would be immediately apparent. Yet the very fact that this dispute arises suggests that in certain communities, the guttural capacity was eroding — making these two letters effectively indistinguishable in pronunciation. They were, as one might quip, the first *Ashkenazim*.

## Me'arat HaMachpelah

### Machpelah Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעָרַת הַמַּכְפֵּלָה, רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: שְׁנֵי בָתִּים זֶה לִפְנִים מִזֶּה, וְחַד אָמַר: בַּיִת וַעֲלִיָּיה עַל גַּבָּיו. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר זֶה עַל גַּב זֶה — הַיְינוּ ״מַכְפֵּלָה״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁנֵי בָתִּים זֶה לִפְנִים מִזֶּה, מַאי ״מַכְפֵּלָה״? שֶׁכְּפוּלָה בְּזוּגוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos this dispute, the Gemara cites similar disputes between Rav and Shmuel. With regard to **the Machpelah Cave,** in which the Patriarchs and Matriarchs are buried, **Rav and Shmuel** disagreed. **One said:** The cave consists of **two rooms, one** farther **in** than **the other. And one said:** It consists of **a room and** a second **story above it.** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** this is understandable **according to the one who said** the cave consists of **one** room **above the other,** as **that is** the meaning of **Machpelah, double. However, according to the one who said** it consists of **two rooms, one** farther **in** than **the other,** in **what** sense is it **Machpelah?** Even ordinary houses contain two rooms. Rather, it is called Machpelah in the sense **that it is doubled with** the Patriarchs and Matriarchs, who are buried there **in pairs.**

The *gemara* proceeds to a series of related disputes between Rav and Shmuel touching on biblical geography and etymology. The first concerns *Me'arat HaMachpelah*: what does the name *Machpelah* — "the doubled one" — actually mean? One opinion holds that *Machpelah* refers to the cave's physical structure: two chambers, one behind the other (*shenei batim zeh lifnim mizeh*). The other holds that it refers to two stories, a house built atop another (*bayit al gabav*).

The *gemara* notes that the two-story interpretation explains the name *Machpelah* straightforwardly: it is doubled because it has two roofs, and in *halachah*, a house is defined by its roof. But for the view that it is two chambers side by side, the doubling of roofs does not apply — so what is the meaning of *Machpelah*? The answer given is *she'kfulah b'zugot*: the cave is "doubled" in the sense that it contains multiple pairs (*zugot*) of the buried — Abraham and Sarah, Isaac and Rebecca, Jacob and Leah, and Adam and Eve. The *Maharsha* notes, however, that from a *pshat* perspective this is clearly a *drash*, since the *pesukim* refer to the field as *S'dei HaMachpelah*, suggesting that *Machpelah* is simply the name of the place rather than a description of the cave's configuration — unless one says the field was named after the cave. In any case, the *gemara* treats this as an aggadic interpretation.

### Four Couples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מַמְרֵא קִרְיַת אַרְבַּע״, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, קִרְיַת הָאַרְבַּע זוּגוֹת: אָדָם וְחַוָּה, אַבְרָהָם וְשָׂרָה, יִצְחָק וְרִבְקָה, יַעֲקֹב וְלֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is similar to the homiletic interpretation of the alternative name for Hebron mentioned in the Torah: **"Mamre** of **Kiryat Ha'Arba,** which is Hebron" (Genesis 35:27). **Rabbi Yitzḥak said:** The city is called Kiryat Ha'Arba, the city of four, because it is **the city of the four couples** buried there: **Adam and Eve, Abraham and Sarah, Isaac and Rebecca,** and **Jacob and Leah.**

The same theme of four pairs leads the *gemara* to address *Kiryat Arba* — the city of Hebron. Rav Yitzchak is quoted: *Kiryat Ha'arba* means the city of four pairs — Adam and Eve, Abraham and Sarah, Isaac and Rebecca, and Jacob and Leah (*arba zugot*). The *Maharsha* observes that in *Sefer Yehoshua*, Arba appears as a proper name, but that in one passage in *Bereishit* the text reads *Kiryat Ha'arba* — with the definite article *heh* — and a *heh hayedi'ah* would not be attached to a proper noun. This grammatical observation grounds the *gemara*'s *drasha* that *arba* here is a number, not a name.

### Amraphel/Nimrod

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי בִּימֵי אַמְרָפֶל״, רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: נִמְרוֹד שְׁמוֹ. וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ אַמְרָפֶל? שֶׁאָמַר וְהִפִּיל לְאַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ בְּתוֹךְ כִּבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ, וְחַד אָמַר: אַמְרָפֶל שְׁמוֹ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ נִמְרוֹד? שֶׁהִמְרִיד אֶת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ עָלָיו בְּמַלְכוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** They disagreed about this verse as well: **"And it came to pass in the days of Amraphel"** (Genesis 14:1). **Rav and Shmuel** both identified Amraphel with Nimrod. However, **one said: Nimrod was his name. And why was his name called Amraphel?** It is a contraction of two Hebrew words: **As he said [*amar*]** the command **and cast [*hippil*] our father Abraham into the fiery furnace,** when Abraham rebelled against and challenged his proclaimed divinity. **And one said: Amraphel was his name. And why was his name called Nimrod? Because he caused the entire world to rebel [*himrid*]** against **God during his reign.**

A further dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerns *Amraphel*, king of Shinar, mentioned at the opening of the war of the four kings against five. The *midrash* identifies Amraphel with Nimrod, since Nimrod is described as *gibor tziyad lifnei Hashem* and the first major war in the *Torah* is fought under Amraphel's leadership. The dispute is over which name is primary and which is the descriptive epithet. One view holds that his real name was Nimrod, and Amraphel was descriptive: *she'amar ve'hipil* — "he said and caused to fall" — referring to his decree that Abraham be cast into the furnace (*kivshan ha'esh*). The other holds that Amraphel was his actual name, while Nimrod was the epithet: *she'himrid et kol ha'olam b'malchuto* — that he caused the entire world to rebel against God, associating him with the leadership of the *Migdal Bavel* insurrection, consistent with his role as *Melech Shinar*.

## New King Egypt

### New King Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּקׇם מֶלֶךְ חָדָשׁ עַל מִצְרָיִם״, רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: חָדָשׁ מַמָּשׁ, וְחַד אָמַר: שֶׁנִּתְחַדְּשׁוּ גְּזֵירוֹתָיו. מַאן דְּאָמַר חָדָשׁ מַמָּשׁ — דִּכְתִיב ״חָדָשׁ״, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁנִּתְחַדְּשׁוּ גְּזֵירוֹתָיו — מִדְּלָא כְּתִיב ״וַיָּמׇת וַיִּמְלוֹךְ״. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁנִּתְחַדְּשׁוּ גְּזֵירוֹתָיו, הָא כְתִיב ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא יָדַע אֶת יוֹסֵף״? מַאי ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא יָדַע אֶת יוֹסֵף״ — דַּהֲוָה דָּמֵי כְּמַאן דְּלָא יָדַע לֵיהּ לְיוֹסֵף כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** They also disagreed about this verse: **"There arose a new king over Egypt,** who knew not Joseph" (Exodus 1:8). **Rav and Shmuel** disagreed. **One said:** He was **actually** a **new** king, **and one said:** He was in fact the old king, but **his decrees were new.** The Gemara explains. **The one who said** he was **actually** a **new** king based his opinion on the fact **that it is written** in the verse that he was **new. And the one who said that his decrees were new** derived his opinion **from** the fact **that it is not written: And** the king **died, and** his successor **reigned,** as it is written, for example, with regard to the kings of Edom (Genesis 36). The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who said that his decrees were new, isn't it written: "Who knew not Joseph"?** If it were the same king, how could he not know Joseph? The Gemara explains: **What is** the meaning of the phrase: **"Who knew not Joseph"?** It means **that he** conducted himself **like one who did not know Joseph at all.**

The *gemara* then turns to another famous dispute — one that *Rashi* cites in his commentary on the *Torah*. The verse states *vayakom melech chadash al Mitzrayim*, "a new king arose over Egypt." Rav and Shmuel disagree: one holds *chadash mamash* — he was literally a new king — while the other holds *she'nitchadesh gezeirotav* — the same king renewed his decrees and changed his policies toward Israel.

The *gemara* supports the "literally new" position from the text itself: the *Torah* says *chadash*. And it supports the "renewed decrees" position from what is absent: the text does not say *vayamot vayimloch* — it never tells us that the previous king died and a new one reigned. The *gemara* then raises an objection against the latter view: the verse says *asher lo yada et Yosef* — "who did not know Joseph" — which implies he was a different person entirely, since surely the same king who had ruled during Joseph's time would have known him. The *gemara* resolves this: the phrase means he acted *as if* he did not know Joseph — he deliberately chose to ignore Joseph's legacy and behave as though it were unknown to him.

## Rabbi Yochanan/Oshaya

### Eighteen Days Learning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁמוֹנָה עָשָׂר יָמִים גִּידַּלְתִּי אֵצֶל רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא בְּרִיבִּי וְלֹא לָמַדְתִּי מִמֶּנּוּ אֶלָּא דָּבָר אֶחָד בְּמִשְׁנָתֵינוּ: כֵּיצַד מְאַבְּרִין אֶת הֶעָרִים — בְּאָלֶף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: I spent eighteen days with Rabbi Oshaya the Distinguished [*Beribbi*], and I learned from him only one matter in our Mishna.** In the phrase: **How does one extend cities,** the word *me'abberin* is spelled **with an *alef*.**

Rabbi Yochanan makes a statement that at first appears to be a digression, but will circle back directly to the question of *me'abrin* with *alef* or *ayin*. He says: *yud-chet yamim gidalti etzel Rabbi Oshaya b'rebi* — I spent eighteen days in the presence of Rabbi Oshaya the Great. And note the verb *gidalti* — "I grew" — an unusual formulation for merely spending time. *Ulo lamadti mimenu ela davar echad b'mishnatenu* — and the only thing I learned from him relating to the *mishnah* was: *keitzad me'avrin et ha'arim b'aleph* — that the word in the *mishnah* is spelled with an *alef*.

This sounds, on its surface, like a rather deflating statement — both about the value of the eighteen days spent and about Rabbi Oshaya himself. One might ask: is Rabbi Yochanan diminishing the importance of Rabbi Oshaya by suggesting that after eighteen days, only a single textual point could be learned from him? And is the point itself — merely an orthographic variant with no halakhic consequence — so trivial as to make the whole encounter seem underwhelming?

It is worth noting that this is entirely consistent with Rabbi Oshaya's broader scholarly enterprise. Rabbi Oshaya, together with Rabbi Chiya, was instrumental in establishing the authoritative texts of the *Tannaic* literature. As the *gemara* elsewhere states, a *baraita* that was not taught in the academy of Rabbi Oshaya or Rabbi Chiya cannot be fully relied upon — they performed for the *baraitot* and *Tosefta* the same editorial work that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi performed for the *mishnah*. Precision of text was precisely Rabbi Oshaya's domain. So it is perhaps not surprising that what Rabbi Yochanan learned from him in connection with the *mishnah* was a textual clarification.

### Lev and Chochmah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר תַּלְמִידִים הָיוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא בְּרִיבִּי, וּשְׁמוֹנָה עָשָׂר יָמִים גִּידַּלְתִּי בֵּינֵיהֶן, וְלָמַדְתִּי לֵב כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד, וְחׇכְמַת כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד? לֵב כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד וְחׇכְמַת כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד גְּמַר, גְּמָרָא — לָא גְּמַר. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִנַּיְיהוּ דִּידְהוּ — גְּמַר, מִינֵּיהּ דִּידֵיהּ — לָא גְּמַר. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דָּבָר אֶחָד בְּמִשְׁנָתֵינוּ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this so? Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: Rabbi Oshaya the Distinguished had twelve students, and I spent eighteen days among them, and I learned the heart of each and every one,** i.e., the nature and character of each student, **and the** extent of the **wisdom of each and every one?** How could Rabbi Yoḥanan say that he learned only one matter? The Gemara answers: It is possible that **he learned the heart of each and every one and the wisdom of each and every one,** but **he did not learn** substantive **tradition.** And **if you wish, say** instead: **From** the students **themselves he learned** many things; **from** Rabbi Oshaya **himself he did not learn** anything beyond that one matter. **And if you wish, say** instead: Rabbi Yoḥanan meant to **say** that he learned only one matter **in our Mishna** from Rabbi Oshaya, but he learned other matters from him based on *baraitot* and other sources.

The *gemara* then questions the statement itself: *ini?* Can this really be so? It immediately contrasts it with another statement of Rabbi Yochanan: there were twelve prize students of Rabbi Oshaya, and Rabbi Yochanan spent eighteen days among them — *v'limadti lev kol echad ve'echad* — he came to understand the heart of each one of them, *ve'chachmat kol echad ve'echad* — and the wisdom of each one. *Rashi* explains that this means he grasped their individual methods of learning, their ways of thinking, their particular approaches to *derech* and analysis, as well as their accumulated knowledge.

So was it really the case that he only learned one thing? The *gemara* qualifies. *Lev kol echad ve'echad ve'chachmat kol echad ve'echad gemar* — he did indeed learn their approaches and methodologies — *gemara lo gemar* — but he did not actually acquire new material from them. A first resolution: *mineihu dideihu gemar* — what he learned, he learned from the students; *minei didei lo gemar* — he did not learn new material from Rabbi Oshaya himself. A second resolution: *davar echad b'mishnatenu gemar* — he may have learned other things, but in relation to the *mishnah* specifically, only one thing was clarified for him by Rabbi Oshaya. Perhaps he learned much else, but the single contribution Rabbi Oshaya made to his understanding of the *mishnah* was this orthographic point.

This tension — between learning methodology on the one hand and acquiring new material on the other — is instructive. Learning a *rebbe*'s *derech*, understanding how he thinks, grasping what each student carries — that is arguably more valuable than any single piece of information. The adage about teaching a man to fish rather than giving him a fish captures something of this distinction. And yet the *gemara* acknowledges both dimensions as real and distinct.

### Four in One Amah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּשֶׁהָיִינוּ לוֹמְדִין תּוֹרָה אֵצֶל רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, הָיִינוּ יוֹשְׁבִין אַרְבָּעָה אַרְבָּעָה בְּאַמָּה. אָמַר רַבִּי: כְּשֶׁהָיִינוּ לוֹמְדִין תּוֹרָה אֵצֶל רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ, הָיִינוּ יוֹשְׁבִין שִׁשָּׁה שִׁשָּׁה בְּאַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said** about that period: **When we were studying Torah with Rabbi Oshaya,** it was so crowded with students that **we would sit four in each** square **cubit.** Similarly, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: When we were studying Torah with Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua, we would sit six in each** square **cubit.**

Rabbi Yochanan adds: when we were learning *Torah* by Rabbi Oshaya, *hayinu yoshvim arba arba be'ama* — four of us would sit, pressed together, within a single *amah*. If an *amah* is roughly a cubit — something on the order of a foot and a half — this represents an extraordinarily tight fit. *Rashi* explains that everyone was crowding around Rabbi Oshaya to hear what he said. This implies that the full student body was considerably larger than twelve; the twelve were the prize students, while the others pressed in as best they could.

The *mishnah* records a parallel: *amar Rebbi*, when we were learning *Torah* by Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua — the famous student of Rabbi Akiva and teacher of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — *hayinu yoshvim shisha shisha be'ama*, six of us would squeeze into a single *amah*. The *Maharsha* observes that this is either miraculous or hyperbolic, but either way, both accounts convey the same essential image: the pressing, crowded, almost physical intensity of learning in the presence of a true master.

There is an additional passage that sheds light on Rabbi Oshaya's stature. Rabbi Yochanan elsewhere states: Rabbi Oshaya in his generation was like Rabbi Meir in his generation — *mah Rabbi Meir bedoro lo yachlu chaveirav la'amod al sof da'ato*, just as no one in Rabbi Meir's time could fully follow him to the depth of his thinking, so too with Rabbi Oshaya. Rabbi Yochanan's remark that he learned only one *mishnaic* point from Rabbi Oshaya may in fact reflect this: Rabbi Oshaya's analysis was of a depth that Rabbi Yochanan himself could not fully access, and so the one thing he could confirm and carry away was a textual correction.

## Yeridas HaDoros

### Opening Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לִבָּן שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹנִים כְּפִתְחוֹ שֶׁל אוּלָם, וְשֶׁל אַחֲרוֹנִים כְּפִתְחוֹ שֶׁל הֵיכָל, וְאָנוּ — כִּמְלֹא נֶקֶב מַחַט סִידְקִית.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The hearts,** i.e., the wisdom, **of** the **early** Sages were **like the doorway to the Entrance Hall** of the Temple, which was twenty by forty cubits, **and** the hearts **of** the **later** Sages **were like the doorway to the Sanctuary,** which was ten by twenty cubits. **And we,** i.e., our hearts, **are like** the **eye of a fine needle.**

The discussion of the great *gedolim* of preceding generations leads Rabbi Yochanan to one of the most celebrated passages in this section. *Liban shel rishonim kepitcho shel ulam* — the heart, meaning the intellectual capacity and depth of understanding, of the earlier generations was like the opening of the *ulam*, the great hall that preceded the *heichal* of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *ulam* was twenty *amot* wide. *Ve'shel acharonim kepitcho shel heichal* — the later generations had a capacity only like the opening of the *heichal* itself, which was ten *amot* wide — half as large. This goes back to the very first *perek* of *Eruvin*, where these measurements were established.

The *gemara* specifies who these generations are. According to one view, the *rishonim* with the understanding of the *ulam*'s opening refers to Rabbi Akiva, and the *acharonim* with the *heichal*'s opening refers to Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua, his student. According to another view, the *rishonim* already includes Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua, and the decline began only with Rabbi Oshaya. Either way, the generation from Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi onward represents a further and steeper drop — represented by the image of a needle's point.

One student raised an intriguing counterpoint: since the *acharonim* are closer to the *heichal*, might the spatial metaphor also suggest proximity to the sacred core — that later generations, while diminished in raw capacity, are in some sense drawing nearer? This is a beautiful thought, and resonates with the well-known image of dwarfs standing on the shoulders of giants. Later generations may understand less in absolute terms, but they build on everything that preceded them and can therefore sometimes see further. Whether this is the *pshat* of the metaphor is uncertain, but it is a striking observation.

### Needle/Wax Metaphors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וַאֲנַן, כִּי סִיכְּתָא בְּגוּדָּא לִגְמָרָא. אָמַר רָבָא: וַאֲנַן, כִּי אֶצְבַּעְתָּא בְּקִירָא לִסְבָרָא. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲנַן, כִּי אֶצְבַּעְתָּא בְּבֵירָא לְשִׁכְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of the steady decline of the generations, **Abaye said: And we,** as far as our capabilities are concerned, **are like a peg in the wall with regard to** Torah **study.** Just as a peg enters a wall with difficulty, our studies penetrate our minds only with difficulty. **Rava said: And we are like a finger in wax [*kira*] with regard to logical reasoning.** A finger is not easily pushed into wax, and it extracts nothing from the wax. **Rav Ashi said: We are like a finger in a pit with regard to forgetfulness.** Just as a finger easily enters a large pit, similarly, we quickly forget our studies.

*Ve'anu*, continues Rabbi Yochanan — and we, the *Amoraim* as compared to the *Tannaim* — *kemale nekev machat sidkit* — our capacity is like the opening of a very fine needle. The drop from twenty *amot* to ten is significant; the drop from ten *amot* to a needle's eye is catastrophic. This may also serve as the *gemara*'s own internal explanation for the principle that an *amora* cannot argue with a *tanna*. Why not? Because the *Tannaim*, even at their latest stage, operated with the understanding of the *heichal*'s opening; the *Amoraim* are left with a needle's point. The transitional generation of Rav — about whom the *gemara* says *Rav tanna hu u'palig* — is the exception that proves the rule.

Abaye, speaking from the later *Amoraic* period, adds: *ke'sikhtsa be'guda le'gemara* — we are like a peg being driven into a stone wall when it comes to *gemara*, meaning the mastery and transmission of received material. The further one gets from the source, the greater the loss of the chain of transmission, and the harder it becomes to drive that peg through. Rava adds a parallel formulation: *ke'etzbeta be'kira le'sevara* — we are like a finger in wax when it comes to *sevara*, analytical reasoning. Abaye speaks of *gemara* — the acquisition of tradition; Rava speaks of *sevara* — independent intellectual capacity. Both are diminished, but perhaps in different ways. If one pressed the wax metaphor slightly, one might note that a finger pressed into soft wax does leave some impression; it does not pass all the way through, but something sticks. Nonetheless, the *gemara*'s intent is clearly to describe limitation. And Rav Ashi, the latest of these voices, concludes: *ke'etzbeta be'bira le'shikhcha* — we are like a finger plunged into a well when it comes to forgetting. There is no resistance at all; information passes through us without leaving a trace.

As the total body of *Torah she'be'al peh* grows with each generation, so too does the challenge of retaining it. More information, less retention — and in an oral culture, the combination is particularly acute.

### Rabbinic Authority

This extended treatment of *Yeridas HaDoros* deserves some broader reflection, though only along the lines that the *gemara* itself suggests. The theology of declining generations — while certainly rooted in statements like the ones here, as well as *im rishonim kemalachim anachnu kivnei adam, im rishonim kivnei adam anachnu kachamorim* — is not the only posture that *chazal* adopt. The principle of *halacha k'basrai*, going by the later authorities, reflects a competing intuition: that later generations, having the benefit of all prior learning, can in some respects see further than their predecessors, like the dwarfs on the shoulders of giants.

The book *Maimonides and the Descent of Generations* by Menachem Kellner addresses precisely this tension and argues, among other things, that the *Rambam* took a fundamentally different view of the nature of rabbinic authority. For the *Rambam*, the authority of the *Talmud Bavli* — and of *halachic* norms generally — rests not on the assumption of superior wisdom or infallibility, but on canonization: the acceptance by *Klal Yisrael* of a body of literature and a system of practice. The *Bavli* is more authoritative than the *Yerushalmi* not because the *Bavli*'s sages were wiser, but because it was the *Bavli* that was canonized and accepted throughout the Jewish world. Similarly, the *Kesef Mishneh* argues that the rule against *Amoraim* disputing *Tannaim* derives not from an assumption about intrinsic superiority but from an implicit consensus — a canonization of the *Tannaic* corpus that occurred after the closing of the *Mishnah*.

This distinction has significant practical implications. If rabbinic authority rests on claims of intrinsic wisdom and near-infallibility, then any acknowledgment of error in *chazal* is profoundly threatening. If it rests on canonization — on *Klal Yisrael*'s acceptance — then it is the fact of acceptance itself that confers authority, independently of questions about superior knowledge. The *Chazon Ish* famously took this latter approach: *rishonim* whose works were not part of the *mesorah* of *Klal Yisrael* — those discovered late and never incorporated into the living tradition — carry no *halachic* weight, not because he doubted their intelligence or their *pshat*, but because what gives a work *halachic* authority is its integration into the accepted system. By extension, as the *Meiri* and similar works become increasingly cited by *poskim*, they can begin to acquire that standing organically.

Those who hold the alternative view — that authority flows from intrinsic truth — tend to account for the *Shulchan Aruch*'s authority through a different lens: the fact that *Klal Yisrael* accepted it demonstrates *siyata d'shmaya*, Divine assistance, which would not have been granted unless the *Shulchan Aruch* embodied the true *halachah*. The question of manuscript variants, textual corruptions through censorship, and the *halachic* weight of newly discovered *rishonim* all turn, in part, on which of these two foundational positions one adopts.

## Judea vs. Galilee

### Judean Precision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה שֶׁהִקְפִּידוּ עַל לְשׁוֹנָם, נִתְקַיְּימָה תּוֹרָתָם בְּיָדָם. בְּנֵי גָלִיל שֶׁלֹּא הִקְפִּידוּ עַל לְשׁוֹנָם, לֹא נִתְקַיְּימָה תּוֹרָתָם בְּיָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the discussion relating to study and comprehension, and cites that which **Rav Yehuda said that Rav said:** With regard to **the people of Judea, who were particular in their speech** and always made certain that it was both precise and refined, **their Torah** knowledge **endured for them;** with regard to **the people of the Galilee, who were not particular in their speech, their Torah** knowledge **did not endure for them.**

The *gemara* transitions back toward the theme of *me'abrin* with *alef* or *ayin* through a teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *bnei Yehuda she'hikpidu al leshonam, nitkayma Toratam be'yadam* — the people of Judea, who were precise and exacting in their language, held on to their *Torah*. The *gemara* then contrasts this with *bnei Galil she'lo hikpidu al leshonam, lo nitkayma Toratam be'yadam* — the Galileans, who were careless in speech, did not hold on to their *Torah*.

### Galilean Looseness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי בִּקְפֵידָא תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא? אֶלָּא: בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה דְּדָיְיקִי לִישָּׁנָא, וּמַתְנְחִי לְהוּ סִימָנָא, נִתְקַיְּימָה תּוֹרָתָן בְּיָדָן. בְּנֵי גָלִיל דְּלָא דָּיְיקִי לִישָּׁנָא, וְלָא מַתְנְחִי לְהוּ סִימָנָא, לֹא נִתְקַיְּימָה תּוֹרָתָן בְּיָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is** this **matter at all dependent on** being **particular** with one's language? **Rather,** with regard to **the people of Judea, who were precise in their language and** who **would formulate mnemonics** for their studies, **their Torah** knowledge **endured for them;** with regard to **the people of the Galilee, who were not precise in their language and** who **would not formulate mnemonics, their Torah** knowledge **did not endure for them.**

The *gemara* asks: *midi be'kfeida talya milta?* Is the preservation of *Torah* really dependent on carefulness of speech? The explanation is as follows. *Bnei Yehuda de'dayki lishna* — those who are precise in language, when they learn a *mishnah* in an oral culture, establish a fixed, exact text. *U'matni lehu simana* — and with a precise, fixed text, one can devise *simanim*, mnemonics, to remember the material. *Nitkayma Toratam be'yadam* — the tradition is thus preserved. *Bnei Galil de'lo dayki lishna* — those who are imprecise have a moving target: the text shifts from person to person and occasion to occasion. *Ve'lo matni lehu simana* — without a fixed text, you cannot build reliable mnemonics. *Lo nitkayma Toratam be'yadam* — and so the tradition is lost.

*Rashi* adds a further dimension: it is not only each individual *mishnah* that requires precision, but also the order in which one *mishnah* follows another. If that order is carefully maintained, one can use the sequence itself as a mnemonic — the opening of one *mishnah* triggers the memory of the next. But if the ordering is loose or inconsistent, that entire associative chain collapses.

### One vs. Many Teachers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה גְּמַרוּ מֵחַד רַבָּה, נִתְקַיְּימָה תּוֹרָתָן בְּיָדָם. בְּנֵי גָלִיל דְּלָא גָּמְרִי מֵחַד רַבָּה, לֹא נִתְקַיְּימָה תּוֹרָתָן בְּיָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, with regard to **the people of Judea,** who **studied from one teacher, their Torah** knowledge **endured for them,** as their teacher provided them with a consistent approach; however, with regard to **the people of the Galilee, who did not study from one teacher,** but rather from several teachers, **their Torah** knowledge **did not endure for them,** as it was a combination of the approaches and opinions of a variety of Sages.

The *gemara* continues with a further contrast. *Bnei Yehuda gamru mi'chad raba, nitkayma Toratam be'yadam* — the Judeans learned from a single *rebbe*, and their *Torah* was preserved. *Bnei Galil de'lo gamru mi'chad raba, lo nitkayma Toratam be'yadam* — the Galileans learned from many different teachers, and their tradition was not preserved. *Tosafot* clarifies: this is specifically about the transmission of material. When you learn a *mishnah* from one teacher, you receive one precise *girsa* — one version of the text. If you go from teacher to teacher, each gives you a slightly different version, and the result is confusion and instability.

However — and *Tosafot* is careful to note this — for the purposes of analytical learning, *sevara* and methodology, learning from many teachers is in fact a *mitzvah*. Already in the first *perek* the *gemara* states that one who learns *Torah* before only a single *rebbe* will never see a sign of blessing — the implication being that exposure to multiple approaches is essential for intellectual development. The resolution is that these are two distinct tasks: mastering the text requires one stable *girsa* from one consistent source, while mastering analysis requires exposure to many minds and approaches.

### Text Analysis

A related insight: Chaim Soloveitchik once observed that *Tosafot* as a genre was only possible after *Rashi*. Not because the *Baalei Tosafot* could not understand the *gemara* without *Rashi*, but because *Rashi* established a default interpretation — a fixed reading — that became the standard starting point. Before *Rashi*, the *gemara* was, in a sense, a moving target: with multiple possible readings of every line, how does one formulate a precise critical question? Once *Rashi* establishes what a passage means, one has a fixed text against which to push. The question becomes sharp and the analysis becomes possible. This is precisely the model the *gemara* is articulating: first establish a single, precise text; then, from that stable foundation, undertake the broader analytical work.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה דְּגַלּוֹ מַסֶּכְתָּא, נִתְקַיְּימָה תּוֹרָתָן בְּיָדָם. בְּנֵי גָלִיל דְּלָא גַּלּוּ מַסֶּכְתָּא, לֹא נִתְקַיְּימָה תּוֹרָתָן בְּיָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** With regard to **the people of Judea, who would** publicly **disclose the tractate** to be studied in the coming term so that everyone could prepare and study it in advance (*ge'onim*), **their Torah** knowledge **endured for them;** with regard to **the people of the Galilee, who would not disclose the tractate** to be studied in the coming term, **their Torah** knowledge **did not endure for them.**

*Ravina* adds a final point: *bnei Yehuda degalu masechta, nitkayma Toratam be'yadam* — the Judeans revealed a *masechta*, meaning, according to *Rashi*, either that they taught it to others or that they analyzed it in depth. *Bnei Galil delo galu masechta, lo nitkayma Toratam be'yadam*. This is a fitting counterpart to what came before: if precision of text and learning from a single teacher are about retention, then going deep into the material and revealing it — whether through teaching or through analysis — is about understanding. And understanding, in turn, reinforces memory. Memorization and comprehension are not separate enterprises; each supports the other.

## David/Shaul Paradigm

### David/Shaul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּוִד גַּלִּי מַסֶּכְתָּא, שָׁאוּל לָא גַּלִּי מַסֶּכְתָּא. דָּוִד דְּגַלִּי מַסֶּכְתָּא, כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״יְרֵאֶיךָ יִרְאוּנִי וְיִשְׂמָחוּ״. שָׁאוּל דְּלָא גַּלִּי מַסֶּכְתָּא, כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״(אֶל כׇּל) אֲשֶׁר יִפְנֶה יַרְשִׁיעַ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that King **David would disclose the tractate** to be studied in advance, whereas **Saul would not disclose the tractate** to be studied. **With regard to David, who would disclose the tractate, it is written: "Those who fear You will see me and be glad"** (Psalms 119:74), since all were prepared and could enjoy his Torah. **With regard to Saul, who would not disclose the tractate** to be studied, **it is written: "And wherever he turned himself he did them mischief"** (i Samuel 14:47).

This observation about *galui masechta* — revealing a *masechta* — is then illustrated through the contrast between David and Shaul. *David degali masechta* — David revealed his *masechet*; *Shaul lo gali masechta* — Shaul did not. By David it is written: *yereiecha yiruni veyismachu* — those who fear You will see me and rejoice. The *gemara* reads this as those who come to David with *halachic* questions are happy with his answers, knowing that he understands what he is talking about. This connects with the *midrashim* in *Berachot* portraying David as a *posek*, immersed in questions of *Tannaic* law, going to *Mephibosheth* with his rulings.

### Shaul's Evil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִין שֶׁמָּחַל לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עַל אוֹתוֹ עָוֹן — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מָחָר אַתָּה וּבָנֶיךָ עִמִּי״. עִמִּי — בִּמְחִיצָתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes the mention of Saul on a positive note. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: From where** is it derived **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, forgave him for that sin,** the massacre of Nov, the city of priests? **As it is stated** that the spirit of Samuel said to him: "And the Lord will also deliver Israel with you into the hand of the Philistines, **and tomorrow shall you and your sons be with me"** (i Samuel 28:19); the phrase **"with me"** means **within my partition** together with me in heaven, i.e., on the same level as the righteous prophet Samuel.

By contrast, of Shaul it is written: *uv'chol asher yifneh yarshia* — wherever he turned, he would do wrong, or cause evil. The *gemara* reinterprets this: it does not mean that Shaul was a wicked person in the conventional sense, but rather that he would *pasken* incorrectly. Because he had not fully revealed his *masechta* — had not fully analyzed and transmitted his learning — his *halachic* judgments were unreliable, and harm would follow from them. This is, notably, a significant softening of what the verse says on its surface. The entire weight of Shaul's moral failure is recast as a failure of *halachic* competence.

### Shaul's Forgiveness

Consistent with this more sympathetic reading, Rabbi Yochanan asks: *minayin she'machal lo HaKadosh Baruch Hu al oto avon?* How do we know that God forgave Shaul for that sin — the sin of destroying Nov, the city of the priests? The proof is from Shmuel's message to Shaul when he was summoned from the dead: *machar atah u'vanecha imi* — tomorrow you and your sons will be with me. The word *imi*, with me *bimchitzati*, in my portion — meaning Shaul would share in *Shmuel*'s *mechitzah* in the *olam haba*. The implication is that Shaul merited a share in the world to come alongside *Shmuel*, which the *gemara* reads as Divine forgiveness.

## Asking Judeans

### Which Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: אִי אִיכָּא דִּמְשַׁאֵיל לְהוּ לִבְנֵי יְהוּדָה דְּדָיְיקִי לִשָּׁנֵי — מְאַבְּרִין תְּנַן אוֹ מְעַבְּרִין תְּנַן, אַכּוּזוֹ תְּנַן אוֹ עַכּוּזוֹ תְּנַן, יָדְעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the earlier question with regard to the correct reading of the word *me'abberin*. **Rabbi Abba said: If there is anyone who can ask the people of Judea, who are precise in their language,** whether the term in the mishna that **we learned** is ***me'abberin*** with an *alef* **or *me'abberin*** with an *ayin*, he should ask them. Similarly, with regard to the blemishes of a firstborn animal, **was** the term meaning its hindquarters that **we learned** in the mishna ***akkuzo*** with an *alef*, **or did we learn *akkuzo*** with an *ayin*? **They would know.**

Having established the superior precision of *bnei Yehuda* in matters of language and textual transmission, the *gemara* now makes the connection explicit. Rabbi Abba says: if there is someone who can ask the *bnei Yehuda*, those who are careful with their language, they should be asked: *ma'avrin tnan o me'abrin tnan?* Is the *mishnah*'s word spelled with an *alef* or with an *ayin?* Similarly, with respect to a *mishnah* in the laws of *mumim* in a *bechor* — where the text describes an animal sitting on its haunches — is it *achuzu* with an *alef* or *achuzu* with an *ayin?* In both cases, the question hinges precisely on the distinction between *alef* and *ayin*, which in the more Hellenized communities of the Galilee had become blurred. The Judeans, being *daykei lishna*, would know the difference.

It is worth noting parenthetically that the Galilee was the more cosmopolitan, Hellenistically influenced region, while Judea was more rural. The loss of guttural consonants — *ayin*, *alef*, *chet* — was precisely a feature of the Greek-influenced urban centers. So *bnei Yehuda*'s precision was not merely a cultural disposition; it was tied to the fact that their language had not been assimilated into a non-Semitic phonological framework. The instruction to ask the *bnei Yehuda* is therefore specifically appropriate for questions that turn on *alef* versus *ayin*.

### Both Versions Exist

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁאֵילִינְהוּ וְאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: אִיכָּא דְּתָנֵי מְאַבְּרִין, וְאִיכָּא דְּתָנֵי מְעַבְּרִין. אִיכָּא דְּתָנֵי אַכּוּזוֹ וְאִיכָּא דְּתָנֵי עַכּוּזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **One asked** the people of Judea, **and they said to him: Some teach *me'abberin*** with an *alef*, **and some teach *me'abberin*** with an *ayin*. **Some teach *akkuzo*** with an *alef*, **and some teach *akkuzo*** with an *ayin*. Both versions are well founded and neither one is erroneous.

*Shailunhu* — they were asked. *Va'amru lehu* — and they replied: *ika de'tanni me'avrin ve'ika de'tanni me'abrin* — there are manuscripts with one version and manuscripts with the other. Similarly, *ika de'tanni achuzu ve'ika de'tanni achuzu*. Both variants exist.

## Green Textile

### Tardin Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה דָּיְיקִי לִישָּׁנָא מַאי הִיא? דְּהָהוּא בַּר יְהוּדָה דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: טַלִּית יֵשׁ לִי לִמְכּוֹר. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאי גּווֹן טַלִּיתְךָ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: כִּתְרָדִין עֲלֵי אֲדָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned that **the people of Judea are precise in their speech,** the Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of this? The Gemara answers with an example: **As** in the case of **a certain person from Judea who said to those** within earshot: **I have a cloak to sell. They said to him: What color is your cloak? He said to them: Like beets on the ground,** providing an exceedingly precise description of the exact shade of the cloak, the green tint of beet greens when they first sprout.

What is a concrete example of *bnei Yehuda*'s linguistic precision? A certain Judean was going about telling people: *talit yesh li limkor* — I have a *talit* to sell. They asked him: *mai gavan talitecha?* What color is it? He replied: *ke'tardin alei adama* — like the leaves of beets growing from the ground. He gave not just a color but a precise shade and hue — the particular green of beet leaves as distinct from the beet itself.

The *Maharsha* explains the point more sharply: why not simply say *yarok*, green? Because in the *mishnah*, *yarok* spans a range from green to yellow — it covers what we would call the green-yellow continuum — and is therefore ambiguous. Rather than risk misunderstanding, the Judean merchant described the color by comparison to a specific, recognizable natural object. That is precision of language.

## Galilean Errors

### Chamar Confusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי גָלִיל דְּלָא דָּיְיקִי לִישָּׁנָא מַאי הִיא? (דְּתַנְיָא) דְּהָהוּא בַּר גָּלִילָא [דַּהֲוָה קָאָזֵיל] וַאֲמַר לְהוּ: ״אֲמַר לְמַאן, אֲמַר לְמַאן?״ אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: גָּלִילָאָה שׁוֹטֶה, חֲמַר לְמִירְכַּב אוֹ חֲמַר לְמִישְׁתֵּי? עֲמַר לְמִילְבַּשׁ אוֹ אִימַּר לְאִיתְכַּסָּאָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to **the people of the Galilee, who are not precise in their speech. What is** the meaning of this? The Gemara cites examples: **As it was taught** in a *baraita* **that** there was **a certain person from the Galilee who would walk and say** to people: **Who has *amar*? Who has *amar*? They said to him: Foolish Galilean,** what do you mean? Galileans did not pronounce the guttural letters properly, so it was unclear whether he sought **a donkey [*ḥamor*] to ride, or wine [*ḥamar*] to drink, wool [*amar*] to wear, or a lamb [*imar*] to slaughter.** This is an example of the lack of precision in the Galileans' speech.

What is an example of *bnei Galil*'s imprecision? A Galilean was going about asking: *chamar leman, amar leman?* — looking for someone who had *chamar* or *amar*. Those who heard him responded: *galila shota* — stupid Galilean, what are you asking for? Are you asking for a *chamor lemirkav*, a donkey to ride? Or *chamar limishti*, wine to drink? And continuing: *amar lemilbash*, wool to wear? Or *imar le'iksa*, a lamb to slaughter? All four words — donkey, wine, wool, lamb — are rendered nearly identical by someone who cannot distinguish between *alef*, *ayin*, and *chet*. The story illustrates precisely how the loss of gutturals produces communicative chaos.

### Woman's Slips

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִתְּתָא דְּבָעֲיָא לְמֵימַר לַחֲבֶרְתַּהּ: ״תָּאִי דְּאוֹכְלִיךְ חֲלָבָא״, אֲמַרָה לַהּ: ״שְׁלוּכָתִי, תּוֹכְלִיךָ לָבִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another example of the lack of linguistic precision of the Galileans: There was **a certain woman who wanted to say to her friend:** My neighbor, **come and I will feed you milk [*ta'i de'okhlikh ḥelba*];** however, due to the imprecise articulation of her words, **she said to her: My neighbor,** may a **lioness eat you [*tokhlikh lavya*].**

A woman wanted to say to her friend: *ta de'ochlich chalba* — come, let me feed you milk. But she garbled it. First she said *shiluchti* — a mispronunciation of *shaluvti*, a term of endearment meaning something like "my companion" or "my friend." And then, instead of *de'ochlich chalba*, she said *tochlich lavi* — which means "a lion should eat you." A Freudian slip of the most dramatic kind, and again, one rooted in the imprecise handling of guttural consonants.

### Judge's Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהִיא אִתְּתָא דְּאָתְיָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּדַיָּינָא, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: ״מָרִי כִּירִי, תַּפְלָא הֲוָית לִי וְגַנְבוּךְ מִין. וְכַדּוּ הֲווֹת, דְּכַד שָׁדְרוּ לָךְ עִילָּוַיהּ — לָא מָטֵי כַּרְעָיךְ אַאַרְעָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another example of the ignorance and incivility of the Galileans: There was **a certain woman who came before a judge** intending to say: Master, sir [*Mari kiri*, spelled with a *kuf*], I had a board, and they stole it from me [*tavla havet li ugenavuha mimeni*]. But instead **she said to him: Master, servant [*Mari kiri*,** spelled with a *kaf*], **I had a beam and they stole you from me** [***tafla havet li ugenavukh min***]. **And it was so** large, **that when they would hang you upon it, your feet would not reach the ground.**

A woman came before a judge. Instead of addressing him respectfully with *kiri* spelled with a *kuf* — an honorific — she said *kiri* with a *chaf*, meaning slave. She wanted to say she had a *tavla* — a plank or tablet — but said *tifla*, a senseless thing, a beam perhaps. She said it had been stolen from her — *vigenovach min* — but the phrasing meant that it was *the judge* who had been stolen. And then, to make it worse, she explained that if the tablet were laid out flat, a person's legs would not reach the floor — an image of the judge being stretched across it. The last part was not a mispronunciation but simply crude and inappropriate — and yet somehow the entire speech collapsed into something deeply disrespectful despite her apparent intention to make a legitimate claim. The combination of mispronunciation and poor word choice produced results the speaker never intended.

## Wise Speech

### Maidservant's Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְהֲתָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי כִּי הֲוָה מִשְׁתַּעְיָא בִּלְשׁוֹן חׇכְמָה, אָמְרָה הָכִי: עֶלֶת נְקַפַת בְּכַד, יִדְאוֹן נִישְׁרַיָּא לְקִינֵּיהוֹן. וְכַד הֲוָה בָּעֵי דְּלִיתְּבוּן, הֲוָה אָמְרָה לְהוּ: יִעְדֵּי בָּתַר חֲבֶרְתַּהּ מִינַּהּ, וְתִתְקְפֵי עֶלֶת בְּכַד, כְּאִילְפָא דְּאָזְלָא בְּיַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** In contrast to the speech of the Galileans, which indicates ignorance and loutishness, the Gemara cites examples of the clever phraseology of the inhabitants of Judea and the Sages: **The maidservant in the house of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **when she would speak enigmatically,** employing euphemistic terminology or in riddles, **she would say as follows: The ladle** used for drawing wine from the jug **is** already **knocking against** the bottom of **the jug,** i.e., the wine jug is almost empty. **Let the eagles fly to their nests,** i.e., let the students return home, as there is nothing left for them to drink. **And when** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **wanted them to sit, she would say to them: Let us remove** the stopper **from another** jug, **and let the ladle** float **in the jug like a ship sailing in the sea.**

Having illustrated the failures of imprecise speech, the *gemara* turns to positive exemplars of *leshon chachma* — wise, refined, and circumspect expression. The first is the maidservant of Rabbi's household. When she wished to speak *bileshon chachma* — in a tasteful, indirect way — she would say: *alat nakfast bechad* — the ladle has struck the bottom of the barrel, meaning there is no wine left. *Yadu nishra lekineihem* — let the eagles fly back to their nests — time for the scholars gathered there to go home, as there is nothing left to drink.

And when she wished to convey the opposite — that they should stay, that there was wine aplenty — she would say: *ya'adi batar chaverta* — let it be removed from its companion, meaning let the stopper be pulled from the barrel. *Ve'tishkufi alat bekad* — and let the ladle float in the barrel. *Ke'ifa de'azla beyama* — like a ship sailing on the sea. Both messages — come and go — are delivered with dignity, metaphor, and a kind of poetic restraint appropriate to her station. It was not her place to directly invite or dismiss guests; so she expressed herself through images that conveyed the message without overstepping.

### Riddles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אָסְיָין כִּי הֲוָה מִשְׁתַּעֵי בִּלְשׁוֹן חׇכְמָה, אֲמַר: עֲשׂוּ לִי שׁוֹר בְּמִשְׁפָּט בְּטוּר מִסְכֵּן. וְכַד הֲוָה שָׁאֵיל בְּאוּשְׁפִּיזָא, אָמַר הָכִי: גְּבַר פּוּם דֵּין חַי, מַה זּוֹ טוֹבָה יֵשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara also relates that **when Rabbi Yosei bar Asyan would speak enigmatically, he would say: Prepare for me an ox in judgment on a poor mountain.** His method was to construct words by combining words from Aramaic translations of Hebrew words or Hebrew translations of Aramaic words. Ox is *tor* in Aramaic; judgment is *din*. Combined they form *teradin*, beets. Mountain in Hebrew is *har*, which they pronounced *ḥar*; poor is *dal*. Together it spells *ḥardal*, mustard. Thus, Rabbi Yosei bar Asyan was requesting beets in mustard. **And when he would inquire about an inn, he would say as follows: This man here is raw; what is this good that there is?** The phrase "this man here is raw" is used in a similar syllable-by-syllable translation: man in Hebrew is *ish*; here is *po*; this is *zeh*; and raw is *na*. All together, they sound like *ushpazikhna*, i.e., an innkeeper (Rabbeinu Ḥananel). In other words, Rabbi Yosei bar Asyan was asking after the innkeeper.

Rabbi Yossi bar Asyan, when he wished to speak in this same refined and indirect mode, would use riddles. Wanting beets with mustard, he would say: *asu li shor bamishpat, be'tur misken* — make me an ox in judgment, in a poor mountain. Translated: *shor bamishpat* in Aramaic becomes *tor din*, i.e., *tordan* — *tardin*, beets. *Tur misken*, a poor mountain, is *har dal* — *chardal*, mustard. Beets with mustard.

When he wished to inquire about an innkeeper, he would ask: *gvar pum din chai* — this man, mouth, judgment, live. *Gvar* is *ish*, *pum* is *peh*, *din* is *zeh*, *chai* is *na*: *ushpizachna* — my innkeeper.

### Abbahu's Riddle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ כִּי הֲוָה מִשְׁתַּעֵי בִּלְשׁוֹן חׇכְמָה, הֲוָה אָמַר הָכִי: אַתְרִיגוּ לְפֶחָמִין, אַרְקִיעוּ לִזְהָבִין, וַעֲשׂוּ לִי שְׁנֵי מַגִּידֵי בַּעֲלָטָה. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: וְיַעֲשׂוּ לִי בָּהֶן שְׁנֵי מַגִּידֵי בַּעֲלָטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rabbi Abbahu would speak enigmatically, he would say as follows: Make the coals the color of an *etrog*; beat the golden ones,** i.e., spread out the coals, which redden like gold when they glow; **and make me two speakers-in-the-dark,** i.e., roosters, which announce the dawn when it is still dark. **Some say** a slightly different version: **And they shall make me in them,** on the coals, i.e., roast for me on top of the coals, **two speakers-in-the-dark.**

Rabbi Abbahu's riddle language: wanting coals to be lit, he would say *asrigu lepachamim* — turn them into coals resembling *esrogim*, reddish-yellow. *Arkiu lezehavim* — spread them out like gold. *Ve'asu li shney magidei ba'alata* — and make for me two speakers in the night — two roosters that crow as the night ends. That is: light the coals, spread them out, and put up two roosters as an alarm. Some say the phrasing is *veyasu li bahen shney magidei ba'alata* — make with them two speakers of the night — a slight variant that does not substantially change the meaning.

## Word Games

### Hide-and-Seek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: הַצְפִּינֵנוּ הֵיכָן רַבִּי אִלְעַאי צָפוּן. אָמַר לָהֶן: עָלַץ בְּנַעֲרָה אַהֲרוֹנִית אַחֲרוֹנִית עֵירָנִית וְהִנְעִירַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In a similarly clever manner, **the Sages said to Rabbi Abbahu: Show us [*hatzpinenu*] where Rabbi Elai is hiding [*tzafun*],** as we do not know his whereabouts. **He said to them: He rejoiced with the latter [*aḥaronit*] Aharonic [*Aharonit*] girl; she is lively [*eiranit*] and kept him awake [*vehiniratu*].**

The rabbis said to Rabbi Abbahu: *hatzpinenu heichan Rabbi Elai tzafun* — play on words: *hatzpinenu* (show us, or perhaps conceal us) and *tzafun* (hidden) — tell us where Rabbi Elai is hiding. The *gemara* presents this as a kind of game they were playing among themselves, in which questions about one another's whereabouts were posed in riddle form — a pastime of wit and wordplay among people for whom precise and elegant language was itself a form of recreation.

### South Journey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לַהּ אִשָּׁה. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ מַסֶּכְתָּא. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אִלְעַאי: הַצְפִּינֵנוּ הֵיכָן רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ [צָפוּן]. אָמַר לָהֶן: נִתְיָיעֵץ בַּמַּכְתִּיר וְהִנְגִּיב לִמְפִיבֹשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** There are two ways to understand this cryptic statement: **Some say** it refers to **a woman,** i.e., he married a young girl from a priestly family [Aharonic], who is his second [latter] wife, from a village [*eiranit*], and he is sleeping now because she kept him awake during the night. **And some say** it refers to **a tractate.** The term girl refers to the tractate; Aharonic indicates that it is a tractate from the order of *Kodashim*, which deals with the priestly service. The phrase the latter means that it is his latest course of study, and lively alludes to the challenging nature of the subject matter. Since he was awake all night studying, he is presently sleeping. The Gemara continues: **They said to Rabbi Elai: Show us where Rabbi Abbahu is hiding,** as we do not know where he is. **He said to them: He has taken counsel with the one who crowns,** i.e., the *Nasi*, who appoints the Sages, **and has gone south** [***hingiv***] **to Mephibosheth,** i.e., he has headed to the Sages of the south, referred to here as Mephibosheth, who was King Saul's grandson and a great Sage of his time.

Rabbi Abbahu responded with a carefully composed riddle: *alatz bena'arah — aharonit, acharonit, iranit — vehinehartehu*. He rejoiced in a young woman — one of Aaron, or the last, or the lively one — and she kept him awake. The play runs through *aharonit* (of Aaron, suggesting priesthood or sanctity), *acharonit* (last, perhaps a later wife), *iranit* (wakeful, lively), and *vehinehartehu* (she illumined him or kept him up). One reading of this is the literal one — he was up late with his wife. The other reading, which some prefer, is metaphorical: the *masechta* was his "other woman," a well-established metaphor for *Torah* as an intimate companion, and he spent the night absorbed in learning. Rabbi Abbahu's riddle is considerably more artful than the reply Rabbi Elai gave in turn, when asked about Rabbi Abbahu's whereabouts: *nisya'etz be'machtir* — he took counsel from the crown-giver, meaning he received *semichah* from the *Nasi* — *ve'henguiv l'Mefivoshet* — and he went south to Mephibosheth, meaning to Eretz Yisrael to learn *Torah*, since Mephibosheth is held up as the *rebbe* of David. It is a functional answer, but it lacks the elegance of what came before.

## Three Encounters

### Woman/Pe'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָה: מִיָּמַי לֹא נִצְּחַנִי אָדָם חוּץ מֵאִשָּׁה תִּינוֹק וְתִינוֹקֶת. אִשָּׁה מַאי הִיא? פַּעַם אַחַת נִתְאָרַחְתִּי אֵצֶל אַכְסַנְיָא אַחַת, עָשְׂתָה לִי פּוֹלִין בְּיוֹם רִאשׁוֹן — אֲכַלְתִּים וְלֹא שִׁיַּירְתִּי מֵהֶן כְּלוּם. שְׁנִיָּיה, וְלֹא שִׁיַּירְתִּי מֵהֶן כְּלוּם. בְּיוֹם שְׁלִישִׁי הִקְדִּיחָתַן בְּמֶלַח, כֵּיוָן שֶׁטָּעַמְתִּי — מָשַׁכְתִּי יָדַי מֵהֶן. אָמְרָה לִי: רַבִּי, מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵינְךָ סוֹעֵד? אָמַרְתִּי לָהּ: כְּבָר סָעַדְתִּי מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם. אָמְרָה לִי: הָיָה לְךָ לִמְשׁוֹךְ יָדֶיךָ מִן הַפַּת! אָמְרָה לִי: רַבִּי, שֶׁמָּא לֹא הִנַּחְתָּ פֵּאָה בָּרִאשׁוֹנִים? וְלֹא כָּךְ אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אֵין מְשַׁיְּירִין פֵּאָה בָּאִלְפָּס, אֲבָל מְשַׁיְּירִין פֵּאָה בַּקְּעָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Having discussed wise speech and the wisdom of Jewish women, the Gemara cites the following story: **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya said** as follows: **In** all **my days, no person defeated me** in a verbal encounter **except for a woman, a young boy, and a young girl. What is** the encounter in which **a woman** got the better of me? **One time I was staying at a certain inn** and the hostess **prepared me beans. On the first day I ate them and left nothing over,** although proper etiquette dictates that one should leave over something on his plate. On the **second** day I again ate **and left nothing over.** On the third day **she over-salted them** so that they were inedible. **As soon as I tasted** them, **I withdrew my hands from them.** **She said to me: My Rabbi, why aren't you eating** beans as on the previous days? Not wishing to offend her, **I said to her: I have already eaten during the daytime. She said to me: You should have withdrawn your hand from bread** and left room for some beans. **She** then **said to me: My Rabbi, perhaps you did not leave a remainder** of food on your plate **on the first** days, which is why you are leaving over food today. **Isn't this what the Sages said: One need not leave a remainder in the pot [*ilpas*], but one must leave a remainder on the plate** as an expression of etiquette (*Tosafot*). This is the incident in which a woman got the better of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya is quoted: *miyamai lo nitzachani adam chutz me'ishah, tinok, ve'tinoket* — in all my days, no one has ever bested me except for a woman, a young boy, and a young girl.

The woman: once I lodged with a certain innkeeper. On the first day she made me lentils, and I ate them completely — I left nothing over. The second day, same dish, and again I finished everything on my plate. On the third day, she added excessive salt, ruining the dish. When I tasted it, I pushed it aside and could not eat. She asked: *Rebbi, why are you not eating?* I said I had already eaten earlier and was not hungry. She replied: if you truly were not hungry, you should have left over some bread and saved your appetite for the main dish. That explanation does not hold.

But then she pressed the real point: *shema lo hinachta pe'ah barishonim* — perhaps you failed to leave *pe'ah* on your plate on the previous days? There was a *minhag*, referred to here by the same term as the agricultural *pe'ah*, of leaving a small portion of food on one's plate. *Rashi* explains the purpose: so that the servant who clears the plates has something to eat. The *chachamim* said: *ein meshayrin pe'ah be'ilpas* — one does not leave it in the serving pot — *aval meshayrin pe'ah be'kara* — but one does leave it on the plate. Rabbi Yehoshua had not done this. The woman's pedagogical method — deliberately salting the dish too heavily on the third day in order to create the occasion for the lesson — was itself a form of wisdom.

### Girl/Field Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינוֹקֶת מַאי הִיא? פַּעַם אַחַת הָיִיתִי מְהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וְהָיְתָה דֶּרֶךְ עוֹבֶרֶת בַּשָּׂדֶה, וְהָיִיתִי מְהַלֵּךְ בָּהּ. אָמְרָה לִי תִּינוֹקֶת אַחַת: רַבִּי, לֹא שָׂדֶה הִיא זוֹ? אָמַרְתִּי לָהּ: לֹא, דֶּרֶךְ כְּבוּשָׁה הִיא. אָמְרָה לִי: לִיסְטִים כְּמוֹתְךָ כְּבָשׁוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the incident with **a young girl? One time I was walking along the path, and the path passed through a field, and I was walking on it. A certain young girl said to me: My Rabbi, isn't this a field?** One should not walk through a field, so as not to damage the crops growing there. **I said to her: Isn't it a well-trodden path** in the field, across which one is permitted to walk? **She said to me: Robbers like you have trodden it.** In other words, it previously had been prohibited to walk through this field, and it is only due to people such as you, who paid no attention to the prohibition, that a path has been cut across it. Thus, the young girl defeated Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya in a debate.

The young girl: once I was walking along a road, and there was a path that cut through a field. I was walking on it. A young girl said to me: *Rebbi, lo sadeh hi zo?* Is this not a field? I replied: is this not an established path? She said back: *listim k'moscha kevashuha* — robbers like you made it into a path. Each person who crosses the field thinking it is permissible because others have done so before them participates in the creation of an illicit path. The path was made by trespassers, and the fact that it is now well-trodden does not legitimate the trespass. The observation is as practically acute today as it was then — many a shortcut through someone's property becomes a sidewalk in precisely this fashion, each person justifying himself by the path already worn.

### Boy/Crossroads

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינוֹק מַאי הִיא? פַּעַם אַחַת הָיִיתִי מְהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וְרָאִיתִי תִּינוֹק יוֹשֵׁב עַל פָּרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים. וְאָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: בְּאֵיזֶה דֶּרֶךְ נֵלֵךְ לָעִיר? אָמַר לִי: זוֹ קְצָרָה וַאֲרוּכָּה, וְזוֹ אֲרוּכָּה וּקְצָרָה. וְהָלַכְתִּי בִּקְצָרָה וַאֲרוּכָּה, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגַּעְתִּי לָעִיר מָצָאתִי שֶׁמַּקִּיפִין אוֹתָהּ גַּנּוֹת וּפַרְדֵּיסִין. חָזַרְתִּי לַאֲחוֹרַי. אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: בְּנִי, הֲלֹא אָמַרְתָּ לִי קְצָרָה? אָמַר לִי: וְלֹא אָמַרְתִּי לְךָ אֲרוּכָּה? נְשַׁקְתִּיו עַל רֹאשׁוֹ, וְאָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: אַשְׁרֵיכֶם יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁכּוּלְּכֶם חֲכָמִים גְּדוֹלִים אַתֶּם, מִגְּדוֹלְכֶם וְעַד קְטַנְּכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the incident with **a young boy? One time I was walking along the path, and I saw a young boy sitting at the crossroads. And I said to him: On which path shall we walk** in order to get **to the city? He said to me: This** path **is short and long, and that** path **is long and short. I walked on the** path that was **short and long. When I approached the city I found that gardens and orchards surrounded it,** and I did not know the trails leading through them to the city. **I went back** and met the young boy again and **said to him: My son, didn't you tell me** that this way is **short? He said to me: And didn't I tell you** that it is also **long? I kissed him on his head and said to him: Happy are you, O Israel, for you are all exceedingly wise, from your old to your young.**

The young boy: once I was walking and I came to a crossroads. A young boy was sitting there. I asked him: which road leads to the city? He replied: *zo ktzara va'arucha* — this one is short and long — *ve'zo arucha u'ktzara* — and this one is long and short. I took the road he described as short and long. When I reached the city, I found it ringed by gardens and orchards, and it took me a very long time to work my way around them and inside. I went back to the boy and said: did you not tell me it was short? He replied: did I not also tell you it was long? *Nashakti al rosho* — I kissed him on his head. *Ashreichem Yisrael* — praised are you, Israel — *she'kulchem chachamim gedolim atem, migdolchem ve'ad ketanchem* — for you are all great and wise, from your greatest to your smallest.

The image of *arucha she'hi ktzara, ktzara she'hi arucha* — the long road that proves to be short, the short road that proves to be long — is a metaphor with wide resonance, deployed elsewhere in *chazal* as well. What appears to be the direct path may in fact cost you more; what appears to be the longer route may in the end bring you there faster and more securely. The boy said exactly what he meant, with complete precision, and Rabbi Yehoshua's haste to take the seemingly shorter path led him astray. The lesson in *Derech Eretz* — not to rush, to listen carefully, to understand what is being told to you before acting — is delivered by a child.
