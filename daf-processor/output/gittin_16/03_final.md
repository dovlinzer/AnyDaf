# Gittin 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Half-Pure Hands

### Half-Pure Hands Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי אִילְפָא: יָדַיִם – טְהוֹרוֹת לַחֲצָאִין, אוֹ אֵין טְהוֹרוֹת לַחֲצָאִין?
>
> **Translation:** **Ilfa raised a dilemma:** With regard to ritual washing, can one's **hands** be **ritually pure in halves, or** can they **not** be **ritually pure in halves?**

The daf opens with a query posed by Ilfa: *yadayim tehorot lachata'in o ein tehorot lachata'in* — can hands be half *tahor*, or is that simply not a meaningful category? The question is, on its face, analogous to asking whether someone can be a little bit pregnant. To understand what the question is actually asking, the Gemara walks through a series of candidate cases, systematically eliminating each one before arriving at the genuine *sha'alah*.

### Eliminating Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּקָא מָשׁוּ בֵּי תְרֵי מֵרְבִיעִית, וְהָא תְּנַן: מֵרְבִיעִית נוֹטְלִין לַיָּדַיִם, לְאֶחָד וַאֲפִילּוּ לִשְׁנַיִם! וְאֶלָּא דְּקָא מָשֵׁי חֲדָא חֲדָא יְדֵיהּ, וְהָתְנַן: הַנּוֹטֵל יָדוֹ אַחַת בִּנְטִילָה, וְאַחַת בִּשְׁטִיפָה, יָדָיו טְהוֹרוֹת! וְאֶלָּא דְּקָא מָשֵׁי פַּלְגָא פַּלְגָא דְּיָדֵיהּ, וְהָאָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: יָדַיִם – אֵין טְהוֹרוֹת לַחֲצָאִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If we say that two** people **wash with** the requisite one **quarter-***log* of water, and therefore in actuality each one of them washes with only half of a quarter-*log*, **but didn't we learn** explicitly in a mishna (*Yadayim* 1:1): **With** the amount of **a quarter-***log* **one can wash the hands of one** person **and even of two?** A quarter-*log* of water suffices for one person to wash his hands before eating bread, and even two may wash their hands simultaneously with this amount, if they do so in the correct manner. **But rather,** Ilfa is referring to a case **where** one **washed his** two **hands one by one,** not both hands at the same time. The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Yadayim* 2:1): With regard to **one who** purifies **one hand by washing** with a vessel **and one** hand **by immersing** it in a river, **his hands are ritually pure?** This mishna indicates that there is no need for both hands to be washed simultaneously. **But rather,** Ilfa's dilemma refers to a case **where he washes his hand in two halves,** i.e., he first washes one half of his hand and next washes the second half of that same hand. The Gemara asks: **But didn't** the Sages **from the school of Rabbi Yannai say: Hands cannot** be rendered **ritually pure in halves?**

The Gemara begins by asking *heichi dami* — what kind of case could this be? It first proposes that perhaps the question concerns two people washing from a single *kli* containing one *reviit*. But this is immediately dismissed: we have already learned explicitly (*umatnan*) that a *reviit* suffices for the *netilat yadayim* of one person, or even two. There is nothing unresolved there.

A second possibility is raised: perhaps he washes one hand at a time, sequentially. Again, this is set aside, because we have likewise already learned that if one hand is washed with *netila* and the other with *shtifa* — that is, dunking it in a body of water rather than having water poured from a vessel — both hands are nonetheless *tehorot*. Certainly if both hands are washed with proper *netila*, sequential washing would be fine. And a third possibility — washing half the hand at a time, in stages, with full drying in between — is dismissed by the statement of the school of Rabbi Yannai: *yadayim ein tehorot lachata'in*, hands simply cannot be purified in halves.

### Moisture Remaining Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִיכָּא מַשְׁקֶה טוֹפֵחַ. הָא נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: טוֹפֵחַ לְהַטְפִּיחַ – חִיבּוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** this dilemma is **necessary** in a case **where there is** liquid that rendered an item **moist.** In other words, the half of the hand that was washed is not merely slightly moist, it is moist enough to render something else moist. The Gemara asks: **We** already **learned this as well:** With regard to liquid that rendered an item **moist** enough **to render** another item **moist,** it **connects** with other liquid. What, then, is Ilfa's dilemma?

The Gemara therefore narrows the question: *lo tzericha d'ika mashkeh tofeach* — the case requires that there be residual moisture on the first portion of the hand when the second portion is washed. In other words, the person washed the hand in stages but did not fully dry the first section before washing the second. The moistness of the first section remains.

One might ask why that would change anything. After all, we have learned that *nitzok*, *katrafes*, and mere *mashkeh tofeach* are not considered *chibur* — connection — neither for *tuma* nor for *tahara*. A thin stream of liquid connecting two vessels, for instance, does not allow *tuma* to travel from one to the other, and similarly does not confer *tahara* upon the *tamei* one. So the residual moisture ought to be irrelevant.

But the Gemara refines the case further: the question is specifically where the moisture remaining on the first portion is *tofeach lehatfiach* — wet enough that if placed against another surface, it would render that surface wet as well. This is a higher threshold than mere dampness. And indeed, regarding *netilat yadayim*, we do have a tradition that *tofeach lehatfiach* constitutes *chibur*. So this particular category remains genuinely unresolved.

### Chibur & Mikvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא לְעִנְיַן מִקְוָאוֹת, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא. דִּתְנַן: מִקְוֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה מְכוּוָּנוֹת, וְיָרְדוּ שְׁנַיִם וְטָבְלוּ (שְׁנֵיהֶם בְּבַת אַחַת – טְהוֹרִים), בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה – רִאשׁוֹן טָהוֹר, וְהַשֵּׁנִי טָמֵא. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיוּ רַגְלָיו שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן נוֹגְעוֹת בַּמַּיִם – אַף הַשֵּׁנִי טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Perhaps** the *halakha* that liquid rendered an item moist enough to render another item moist joins with other liquid is stated only **with regard to the issue of ritual baths, and** this **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda. As we learned** in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 7:6): In the case of **a ritual bath that contains precisely forty *se'a*** of water, **and two** people **descended and immersed** in it, if **both of them** immersed **at the same time they are ritually pure.** If they immersed **sequentially,** then the **first** person is **ritually pure,** as he immersed in a ritual bath that contains the requisite amount of water, **but the second** person is **impure,** because some of the water must certainly have clung to the first individual as he left the ritual bath. Consequently, after the first person's immersion the ritual bath contained slightly less than the requisite forty *se'a* of water. **Rabbi Yehuda says: If** the **first** person's **feet were** still **touching the water** in the ritual bath when the second person immersed, then **the second** person is **also ritually pure.**

The Gemara then raises another possible dismissal: perhaps the *chibur* principle of *tofeach lehatfiach* is known only in the context of a *mikvah*, not for *netilat yadayim*. If that is so, there is no established ruling for the hand-washing case at all, and the question stands. This framing — that the *chibur* idea may belong exclusively to *mikvah* discussions — sets up the next step.

### Rabbi Yehuda Chibur

The *mikvah* source for *chibur* is introduced from a Mishnah: if a *mikvah* contains exactly forty *seah* — precisely the minimum required — and two people immerse, one after the other, some water will cling to the first person and leave the *mikvah* slightly deficient for the second. The *tanna kamma* rules that the first is *tahor* but the second is *tamei*. Rabbi Yehuda, however, rules that if the first person's feet are still touching the water — still in contact with the *mikvah* as he exits — then the water on his body is considered connected to the *mikvah* through *chibur*, and the second person's immersion is also valid.

The relevance to the *yadayim* question is this: the *chibur* principle in the hand-washing case may simply be an extension of Rabbi Yehuda's position regarding *mikvah*. If so, it is Rabbi Yehuda's view, not a universally accepted principle.

### Unresolved Question

The question is ultimately left unresolved. What we can say is that the answer likely follows party lines: Rabbi Yehuda, consistent with his *mikvah* position, would likely say the partially-washed hand is *tahor*, while the *tanna kamma* would not. As a practical note, the proper custom is to ensure a full *reviit* covers each hand completely — not to divide the water — precisely to avoid any such ambiguity, though the underlying legal question remains without a definitive answer in the Gemara.

## Drawn Water Decrees

### Head in Drawn Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ: הַבָּא רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ בְּמַיִם שְׁאוּבִין, וְטָהוֹר שֶׁנָּפְלוּ עַל רֹאשׁוֹ וְעַל רוּבּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה לוּגִּין מַיִם שְׁאוּבִין – טָמֵא; בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: חֶצְיוֹ בְּבִיאָה וְחֶצְיוֹ בִּנְפִילָה, מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya says: They said** in a mishna (*Zavim* 5:12): In the case of **one whose head and most of his** body **enter** into **drawn water** after he immersed himself in a ritual bath to purify himself, **and a ritually pure person** concerning **whom three *log*** of **drawn water fell on his head and most of his** body, both of these people are **ritually impure.** With regard to this mishna, **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** If **half of** a person became purified **by entering** into drawn water **and half of him** became purified **by** having water **fall** on him, **what** is the *halakha*? Do these acts join together to render him ritually impure or not? No answer is found, and therefore the Gemara says that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rabbi Yirmeyah introduces two related rulings. First: one who immerses *rosho ve-rubo* — head and majority of the body — in *mayim she'uvim*, drawn water, is *tamei*. Second: a *tahor* person upon whom three *lugin* of drawn water are poured is likewise *tamei*. The question immediately arises: why should this be? In the first case, the person may have already been *tahor* — either they had just immersed in a valid *mikvah* or had never become *tamei* in the first place. Why would immersing in drawn water undo that?

### Rashi's Explanation

Rashi explains that such a person is *tamei l'terumah* — considered *tamei* with respect to *terumah* — until he immerses again in a valid *mikvah*. Rashi further notes that this is one of the eighteen things decreed during the famous confrontation between the disciples of Beit Shammai and the disciples of Beit Hillel, recorded at the beginning of Masechet Shabbat. These eighteen decrees were exceptional in that, notwithstanding the general principle that one does not issue a *gezeirah* upon the public unless the majority can uphold it (*ein gozerin gezeirah al ha-tzibur ela im ken rov tzibur yecholin la'amod bah*), these decrees were imposed because of the extraordinary intensity of feeling behind them at the time.

### Filthy Mikvah Caves

The Gemara explains the historical circumstance that gave rise to this *gezeirah*: *she-hayah lahem mei me'arot sruchim l'tevilah* — they used to immerse in the water of caves, which was foul and turgid. One can imagine the conditions: cave water, depending on what resided in or passed through those caves, could be thoroughly unpleasant. After immersing in such a *mikvah*, people would naturally cleanse themselves with drawn water — in effect, bathing after the immersion.

### Bathing Prohibition

This created a serious perceptual problem. Bystanders seeing this sequence of events might conclude that it was not the *mikvah* immersion but the subsequent bathing that effected *tahara* — *atu elu meta'arin elu meta'arin*, "is it these that purify, or these?" The concern was that people would simply skip the *mikvah* and rely on the drawn water alone. To prevent this, the Rabbis decreed that immersing in drawn water, or bathing with drawn water after a *mikvah*, renders one *tamei*, thereby preserving the *mikvah*'s exclusive role as the vehicle of *tahara*.

As a practical aside, this decree has contemporary relevance primarily in the context of ascending the Temple Mount or for a convert undergoing *tevilah* for conversion. For the purpose of marital *tahara*, where the *mikvah* is used for *niddah* and *zavah* restrictions rather than classic *tumah ve-tahara*, the question of a subsequent shower does not affect the woman's status vis-à-vis her husband. Rashi's formulation — *tamei l'terumah* — reflects that this *tuma* has practical significance only for *terumah* and *kodshim*, neither of which applies today outside of the Temple Mount context.

### Three Lugin Extension

The same *gezeirah* was extended to the case where three *lugin* of drawn water are poured over the head and majority of a person's body. The Gemara states: *de-ha u-me-ha chada hilcheta hi* — these two cases are governed by the same legal principle. Whether one enters a pool of drawn water or has it poured upon them, the rabbinic consequence is identical.

### Half Immersion Half Pour

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: חֶצְיוֹ בִּטְבִילָה וְחֶצְיוֹ בִּנְתִינָה, מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Pappa says: They said** in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 3:4): In the case of **a sick** person **who experienced a seminal emission, who had nine *kav* of** drawn **water poured over him,** this is sufficient to render him **ritually pure.** He need not immerse himself in a ritual bath. **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma:** If **half of him** is purified **by immersion and half of him through** this act of **pouring, what** is the *halakha*? No answer is found for this dilemma either, and the Gemara says that it **shall stand** unresolved.

Rami bar Abba poses a question about a hybrid case: *chatzyo be-shfichah ve-chatzyo be-vi'ah* — what if half was from immersion and half was from pouring? Does the *gezeirah* apply to a combination of the two modalities, each of which alone constitutes only half the triggering condition? The Gemara concludes *teiku* — the question stands unresolved.

## Takanat Ezra

### Nine Kabin Remedy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ: בַּעַל קֶרִי חוֹלֶה, שֶׁנָּתְנוּ עָלָיו תִּשְׁעָה קַבִּין מַיִם – טָהוֹר;
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Pappa says: They said** in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 3:4): In the case of **a sick** person **who experienced a seminal emission, who had nine *kav* of** drawn **water poured over him,** this is sufficient to render him **ritually pure.**

Rav Papa then introduces an analogous ruling: *ba'al keri choleh she-natnu alav tish'ah kabin mayim tahor* — a sick person who has had a seminal emission upon whom nine *kabin* of water are poured is rendered *tahor* in the relevant sense. This ruling is ancient and well established.

### Ezra's Decree

Stepping back to the broader context: Ezra enacted a *takkanah* requiring a *ba'al keri* to immerse in a *mikvah* before engaging in Torah study or prayer, as Rashi notes — *Ezra tiken tevilah le-ba'alei kerin k'dei shelo yehoyu k'tarnegalim*, so that they would not approach Torah with the casual, thoughtless attitude of roosters. The goal was to ensure that Torah study is approached with gravity and intentionality.

### Sick Exception

The exception allows a *ba'al keri* who is ill and unable to go to the *mikvah* to instead have nine *kabin* of water poured over him — even drawn water — and thereby fulfill the requirement sufficiently. The practical effect is essentially a shower. This *heter* applies only to the sick; a healthy *ba'al keri* would still be required to immerse in a proper *mikvah*.

### Modern Application

The *takkanah* of Ezra is generally not observed today. The underlying rationale was to ensure that Torah study is approached with the proper seriousness and composure that follows *tevilah*, as opposed to the levity that may have preceded the *keri*. The sad commentary embedded in this abandonment is that, since we do not in general approach Torah with the sustained gravity that would make the contrast meaningful, the *gezeirah* no longer has its intended effect and has effectively lapsed.

### Ritva Clarification

The Ritva adds an important clarification: the nine *kabin* do not actually purify the *ba'al keri* in the full *halachic* sense of *tahara*. Rather, they remove specifically the prohibition enacted by Ezra against Torah study and prayer. For purposes requiring actual *tahara* — such as going to the *Beit Ha-Mikdash* or its modern analog, ascending the Temple Mount — a full *mikvah* immersion remains necessary.

### Temple Mount Immersion

This point has direct practical implications. The only contexts today in which *mikvah* immersion for men carries concrete *halachic* weight are, first, ascending the Temple Mount, and second, conversion immersion for a convert. The nine *kabin* option of Ezra's *takkanah* would not suffice for these purposes, since the Ritva's distinction between removing a rabbinic prohibition and achieving actual *tahara* is operative there. For men who customarily immerse before *chag*, there is a view that *b'diavad* a shower might suffice based on this *takkanah*, though this remains a matter requiring consultation.

### Rav Papa Extension

Rav Papa extends the same logical question that Rami bar Abba asked about drawn water: *chetzyo be-tvila ve-chetzyo be-netina mah* — what if half the required quantity came from immersion and half from pouring, in the context of the nine *kabin* for a sick *ba'al keri*? Just as before, the Gemara answers *teiku* — the question is left without resolution.

## Get Witnesses

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב״ וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר כּוּ׳: אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין הַגֵּט יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יְדֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם, אֲבָל גֵּט יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יְדֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that if **one says: It was written in my presence, and one says:** It was signed in my presence, the bill of divorce is invalid. **Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They taught** that the document is invalid **only if the bill of divorce** was **not produced by both of them** in court. In other words, this *halakha* applies only if they were not both agents for bringing this bill of divorce. One person is the primary carrier; the other merely happened to be present and can attest to only part of the required declaration. Rashi explains: *mitachat yadei she-shneihem adukim bo u-shneihem shlichim* — if both are holding the *get* and both are designated as messengers, the situation is different. **However,** if the **bill of divorce** was **produced by both of them,** it is **valid.**

The Gemara returns to the *Mishnah* governing a *get* brought from outside of Israel (*medinat ha-yam*). The *takkanah* requires the messenger to declare *bifanai nictav uv'fanai nechtam* — "it was written in my presence and sealed in my presence" — a single person attesting to both the writing and the sealing. The Mishnah presents several cases involving multiple witnesses, each attesting to only one part of this declaration, and asks whether the *get* is valid.

### Three Cases

The Mishnah presents three configurations. In the first, one witness says *bifanai nictav* — it was written in my presence — and a different witness says *bifanai nechtam* — it was sealed in my presence. The *get* is *pasul*, invalid. In the second case, two witnesses attest to the writing and one attests to the sealing. The *tanna kamma* rules this *pasul*, while Rabbi Yehuda rules it *kasher*. In the third case, one attests to the writing and two attest to the sealing, and all opinions agree the *get* is *kasher*. These three cases form the framework for the ensuing analysis.

### Get Under Both Hands

Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda reports in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *lo shanu ela she-ein ha-get yotzei mitachat yedei shneihem* — the Mishnah's ruling of *pasul* in the first case applies only where the *get* does not exit from under the hands of both witnesses, meaning the two individuals were not both appointed as *shlichim*, messengers. One person is the primary carrier; the other merely happened to be present and can attest to only part of the required declaration. Rashi explains: *mitachat yadei she-shneihem adukim bo u-shneihem shlichim* — if both are holding the *get* and both are designated as messengers, the situation is different.

### Foreign Get Takana

The *takkanah* for a *get* brought from outside Israel was specifically designed to require that one person testify to both the writing and the sealing together. The reason, as we shall explore, relates to either the *lishma* concern or the *edim metzuyim* concern — the two competing rationales that will be debated. The key structural point here is that the *takkanah* was crafted around a single person bearing full testimony, not a composite of partial attestations from multiple individuals.

### Two Messengers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קָסָבַר שְׁנַיִם שֶׁהֵבִיאוּ גֵּט מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, אֵין צְרִיכִין שֶׁיֹּאמְרוּ: ״בְּפָנֵינוּ נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנֵינוּ נֶחְתַּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Apparently,** Rabbi Yoḥanan **holds** that **two** people **who brought a bill of divorce from a country overseas are not required to say: It was written in our presence and it was signed in our presence.**

If, however, both witnesses are formally appointed as messengers — *shneyhem shlichim*, both designated to carry and deliver the *get* — then the *get* is valid even according to the Mishnah's first case framing. *Amar kasavar shnayim she-heviu get mimedinas hayam ein tzrichim she-yomru bifanai nechtav uv'fanai nechtam* — when two people bring the *get* together as co-messengers, the *takkanah* requiring the combined declaration does not apply to them in the same way. The logic of why this should be the case, and how far the principle extends, is the subject of the dispute between Rava and Rabbah.

## Rava's Edim Principle

### Rava-Rabbah Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בְּקִיאִין לִשְׁמָהּ, וּמָר סָבַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עֵדִים מְצוּיִין לְקַיְּימוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do** the *tanna'im* **disagree?** The Gemara answers: One **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that the reason for the declaration: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, is **because** the people who live overseas **are not experts** in writing a bill of divorce **for her sake.** Consequently, it is necessary for them to testify about both the writing and the signing for her sake, in accordance with the rabbinic decree, even when two people bring the bill of divorce. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that the reason for the declaration is **because there are no witnesses available to ratify it,** and when two people bring a bill of divorce there are people available to ratify it, and therefore the declaration is unnecessary.

The Gemara identifies two competing rationales for the *takkanah* requiring a messenger to declare *bifanai nictav uv'fanai nechtam*: the position of Rabbah and the position of Rava. This dispute, first introduced at the beginning of the tractate, is now directly applied to the Mishnah's cases. Rabbah holds that the *takkanah* was instituted because people outside of Israel were not expert in the requirement that a *get* be written *lishma* — for the specific purpose of the woman's divorce. Rava holds that the concern was *edim metzuyim l'kaymo* — the absence of available witnesses to authenticate the document through its signatures, since people in one country are not familiar with the signatures of witnesses from another.

### Edim Metzuyim

Rava's position — *lefi she-ein edim metzuyim l'kaymo* — generates a specific analytical framework. If the underlying concern is the availability of witnesses to establish the *get*'s authenticity, then when two people bring the *get* together as co-messengers, there are in fact two witnesses present and available. The *edim metzuyim* problem is solved. This explains why, under Rava's reasoning, two co-messengers need not make the declaration: they themselves constitute the available witnesses.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, סֵיפָא דְּקָתָנֵי: שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״בְּפָנֵינוּ נִכְתַּב״ וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בְּפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״ – פָּסוּל, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר; טַעְמָא דְּאֵין הַגֵּט יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יְדֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם, הָא גֵּט יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יְדֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם – מַכְשְׁרִי רַבָּנַן?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: If that is so,** consider **the latter clause** of the mishna, **which teaches:** If **two** people **say: It was written in our presence, and one** person **says: It was signed in my presence,** then the document is **invalid. And Rabbi Yehuda deems** it **valid.** Is it true to say that **even** when **the bill of divorce was produced by both of them the Rabbis deem** it **invalid?** Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda **said to him: Yes.**

Abaye challenges Rava by directing attention to the second case in the Mishnah: *shnayim omrim bifanaynu nictav ve-echad omer bifanai nechtam* — two say it was written in their presence, one says it was sealed in his presence. Under this configuration, you have three witnesses total, and certainly *edim metzuyim* are present. Yet the *tanna kamma* still rules *pasul*. How does Rava account for this? If *edim metzuyim l'kaymo* is the operative concern and that concern is satisfied here, why is the *get* still invalid?

### Confusion Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְמַעוֹטֵי ״בְּפָנַי נֶחְתַּם אֲבָל לֹא בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב״ – סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְלָא גָּזַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יַחְזוֹר דָּבָר לְקִלְקוּלוֹ; דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאִחַלּוֹפֵי בְּקִיּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת דְּעָלְמָא, בְּעֵד אֶחָד – נָמֵי לָא גָּזַר; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** this serves **to exclude** a case where only one witness testified: **It was signed in my presence but it was not written in my presence.** The Gemara asks: Why is it necessary to exclude this case? It might **enter your mind to say: Since Rabbi Yehuda did not issue** a rabbinic **decree** that it is invalid **lest the matter return to its corrupt state, perhaps he also did not decree** that it is invalid lest people **will come to confuse** this case **with the typical** situation of **ratification of** legal **documents,** which cannot be performed **with one witness.** Consequently, the *baraita* **teaches us** that although Rabbi Yehuda is not concerned about people forgetting the *halakha* and therefore holds that there is no longer any reason to be worried about the document not being written for her sake *ab initio*, nevertheless he is still concerned that a bill of divorce might be confused with other legal documents.

Rava resolves Abaye's challenge by clarifying the nature of the *takkanah*: the concern is not simply whether you have a sufficient number of witnesses in the abstract, but whether the particular configuration will lead to confusion with the authentication of regular documents (*kiyum shetarot d'alma*). Regular documents require two witnesses who together attest to the document's authenticity. If we permit this split-testimony arrangement for a *get* — where one group attests to writing and a different individual attests to sealing — people will begin to confuse that with the ordinary rules for authenticating *shetarot*, thinking perhaps that one witness can establish a document in general contexts. The *gezeirah* extends to prevent that confusion even where multiple people are present.

### Appointed vs. Present

The critical distinction in Rava's framework is between witnesses who are formally appointed as *shlichim* — designated messengers — and witnesses who are merely present and happen to be able to testify to one element or another. When both individuals are appointed as co-messengers, the *get* exits from under both their hands; they collectively bear the responsibility and function of the *shliach*, and the *takkanah* does not require them to make the composite declaration. But where they are not both formally appointed, even if their combined testimony covers all the required elements, the configuration is structurally flawed: no single person is standing fully behind the document, and the *gezeirah* against confusion with regular *kiyum shetarot* applies.

## Rabbah Lishma

### Lishma Common Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּה אָמַר לָךְ: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בָּעֵינַן לִשְׁמָהּ, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – לְאַחַר שֶׁלָּמְדוּ; וּבִגְזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יַחְזוֹר הַדָּבָר לְקִלְקוּלוֹ קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר גָּזְרִינַן, וּמָר סָבַר לָא גָּזְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba** could have **said to you,** in accordance with the second formulation of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement, i.e., two people who bring a bill of divorce are also required to say: It was written in our presence and it was signed in our presence, **that everyone** agrees that **we require** them to issue the declaration because people are not experts in the *halakha* that it must be written **for her sake. And with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with the period **after they learned** to write a bill of divorce for her sake even overseas. **And they disagree with regard to** whether there is a rabbinic **decree** that the bill of divorce is invalid **lest the matter return to its corrupt state,** i.e., the residents overseas will forget that a bill of divorce must be written for the woman's sake. **As** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds:** The Sages **decree** that it is invalid for this reason, and therefore the declaration is still required even after they learned. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds:** The Sages **do not decree** that it is invalid lest they forget the *halakha*.

Rabbah advances a different framework: *d'chulei alma ba'inan lishma* — both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda agree that the *takkanah* was instituted because of the *lishma* concern, that people were not expert in the requirement that a *get* be written specifically for the sake of this woman's divorce. This is Rabbah's operating principle, shared across all positions in the Mishnah.

### Gezeirah Lest Revert

Given that shared premise, Rabbah explains the disagreement: *v'hacha b'mai askinan le'achar she-lamdu u-gezeirah shema yachzor davar l'kilkulo* — the debate concerns a later stage, after knowledge of the *lishma* requirement has spread, and specifically whether we issue a *gezeirah* lest things revert (*davar ha-chozer l'kilkulo*) to the original state of ignorance. Originally, the *takkanah* was enacted because people did not know the *get* had to be written *lishma*. Eventually, this became widely understood. The question is whether, even after the principle became common knowledge, we maintain the original safeguards to prevent a future regression.

### Rabbi Yehuda Lenient

Rabbi Yehuda is lenient: since everyone already understands the *lishma* requirement, we need not worry that the situation will revert. The knowledge is sufficiently embedded that allowing some flexibility in how the testimony is arranged poses no risk of causing people to forget or disregard *lishma*. Rabbi Yehuda therefore permits the second case — two attesting to the writing and one to the sealing — because the concern that originally gave rise to the *takkanah* is no longer pressing.

### Tanna Kamma Stringent

The *tanna kamma*, by contrast, is *gazrinan* — we do issue the *gezeirah* against reversion. Even though everyone currently knows about *lishma*, the *tanna kamma* fears that if we relax the requirement, the structure that enforces awareness of *lishma* will erode, and future generations will lose sight of it. The *tanna kamma* therefore insists that the declaration of *bifanai nictav uv'fanai nechtam* by a single person must be maintained in all configurations, precisely because that unified declaration is the mechanism that keeps the *lishma* concern visible and enforced.

## Baraita Resolution

### Baraita Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיפְלוֹג נָמֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵישָׁא! הָא אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר עוּלָּא: חָלוּק הָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַף בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה. מֵתִיב רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא לְעוּלָּא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר בְּזוֹ וְלֹא בְּאַחֶרֶת. מַאי, לָאו לְמַעוֹטֵי אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב״ וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בְּפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **let Rabbi Yehuda disagree also with regard to the first clause** of the mishna, concerning the case where one agent says: It was written in my presence, and the other agent says: It was signed in my presence. Why does he disagree only about a case where two say that the document was written in their presence? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yehuda disagrees even with regard to the first clause, as **it was** already **stated with regard to** the mishna that **Ulla said: Rabbi Yehuda was in disagreement even with regard to the first clause.** **Rav Oshaya raises an objection to** the opinion of **Ulla** from the following *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:2): **Rabbi Yehuda deems** the bill of divorce **valid in this** case **but not in another** case. **What, is it not** correct to say that this serves **to exclude** a case where **one says: It was written in my presence, and one says: It was signed in my presence?**

Ulla's teaching is introduced in response to a logical pressure: if Rabbi Yehuda is not worried about *davar ha-chozer l'kilkulo*, he should be lenient not only in the second case but in the first case as well. *Ve-liflog nami Rabbi Yehuda b'reisha* — why does Rabbi Yehuda not disagree in the first case too? If the *lishma* concern is no longer operative, even one person saying *bifanai nictav* and another saying *bifanai nechtam* should be acceptable under Rabbi Yehuda's logic.

### Ulla Interpretation

Ulla resolves this: *chaluk hayah Rabbi Yehuda af b'rishona* — Rabbi Yehuda in fact does disagree in the first case as well. The Mishnah's formulation, which seems to show Rabbi Yehuda disagreeing only in the second case, is to be read as recording his leniency in that specific configuration while leaving his first-case position implicit. But this is complicated by a *Baraita* that was taught to Ulla by Rav Oshaya: *Rabbi Yehuda machshir b'zu ve-lo b'acheret* — Rabbi Yehuda validates *this* case and not *that* one. This *Baraita* explicitly limits Rabbi Yehuda's leniency, stating that there is one case he permits and another he does not.

### One Sealing Only

The question then becomes: what is the case that Rabbi Yehuda does *not* permit? If he disagrees in the first Mishnah case (one attests to writing, one to sealing) and permits the second (two attest to writing, one to sealing), then what is the "other" case the *Baraita* refers to that he rejects? The Gemara concludes that the missing case — not discussed in the Mishnah at all — is where a single witness says only *bifanai nechtam*, attesting solely to the sealing without any attestation to the writing. This configuration: zero witnesses to writing, one to sealing, is universally *pasul*. The *Baraita* is informing us that this is the case Rabbi Yehuda excludes, not one of the Mishnah's own cases.

### Salka Daatach

The Gemara explains why we might have thought otherwise: *salka daatach amina* — one might have assumed that since Rabbi Yehuda is not worried about *davar ha-chozer l'kilkulo*, he would extend his leniency all the way, validating even a case where only one person attests to only one element. After all, if the *lishma* concern is no longer pressing, and if we are not going to confuse this with regular *kiyum shetarot* — why not be maximally lenient?

### Rabbi Yehuda Limits

The answer is that Rabbi Yehuda, despite not being worried about *davar ha-chozer l'kilkulo*, still holds that there must be *some* substantive testimony to both elements of the declaration. He cannot simply accept half a job. The *takkanah* may have relaxed in one direction — Rabbi Yehuda does not require a single person to deliver the entire composite declaration — but it does not relax to the point of permitting a *get* where only one element is attested to at all. Attestation to sealing alone, without any testimony to writing, remains invalid even for Rabbi Yehuda.

### Final Resolution

The final resolution is thus clear. Rabbi Yehuda's leniency is structurally limited: it extends to cases where both persons delivering the testimony are appointed as *shlichim* — co-messengers formally charged with bringing the *get*. In that configuration, the *get* exits from under both their hands, and the functional requirements of the *takkanah* are satisfied through their shared appointment even if their testimonies are formally divided. What Rabbi Yehuda will not permit is a case where only one element is testified to in total, or where the individuals are not designated messengers and are merely bystanders attesting to fragments of what the *takkanah* requires as a unified whole.
