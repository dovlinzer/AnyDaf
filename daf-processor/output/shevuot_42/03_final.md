# Shevuot 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shmuel's Position

### Shmuel's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ בְּעֵדִים – אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְפוֹרְעוֹ בְּעֵדִים. וְאִם אָמַר: ״אַל תִּפְרָעֵנִי אֶלָּא בְּעֵדִים״ – צָרִיךְ לְפוֹרְעוֹ בְּעֵדִים. כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר לִי: יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ: ״פְּרַעְתִּיךָ בִּפְנֵי פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי, וְהָלְכוּ לָהֶם לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who lends** money to **another in** the presence of **witnesses,** the latter **need not repay him in** the presence of **witnesses. But if** the creditor **said: Repay me only in** the presence of **witnesses,** the debtor is **required to repay him in** the presence of **witnesses.** Rav Yehuda continued: **When I said this in the presence of Shmuel, he said to me** that the debtor **can say to** the claimant: **I repaid you in the presence of so-and-so and so-and-so, and they went overseas.**

The daf opens by continuing from 41b, where the *gemara* concluded that according to Shmuel, one who lends money in the presence of witnesses is not required to be repaid in the presence of witnesses. The practical implication for a creditor is clear: either obtain a *shtar* at the time of the loan, or stipulate explicitly that repayment must occur in the presence of witnesses.

### Stipulation Options

When such a stipulation is made, a further question arises. Does the condition require merely that witnesses be present at the time of repayment, or does it require the debtor to actually produce those witnesses in court to prove that payment occurred? The question, in other words, is whether the debtor may claim that witnesses were present but have since traveled abroad — *medinat hayam* — and are therefore unavailable. Is such a claim sufficient, following Shmuel's permissive position, or must the witnesses be physically produced?

### Conclusion Ambiguity

The *gemara*'s conclusion on this point is not fully clear, as it depends on one's *girsa* — the textual variant — in the closing lines of the discussion. Whether we rule with Shmuel, and whether the debtor is believed to claim that witnesses existed but are now in *medinat hayam*, thus remains unresolved as the *gemara* moves forward into a series of concrete cases.

## Designated Witnesses

### Creditor's Stipulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״כִּי (פָּרְעַתַּן) [פָּרְעַתְּ לִי], פִּרְעַן (לִי) בְּאַפֵּי רְאוּבֵן וְשִׁמְעוֹן״. אֲזַל וּפַרְעֵיהּ בְּאַפֵּי תְרֵי מֵעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** creditor **who said to** the debtor: **When you repay me, repay me in the presence of Reuven and Shimon.** The debtor **went and repaid him in the presence of two** other witnesses **from the general public.**

The *gemara* presents its first case. A creditor said to his debtor: when you repay me, repay me specifically in front of Reuven and Shimon. The creditor thus not only stipulated that witnesses must be present, but named the particular individuals he required.

### Different Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּאַפֵּי בֵּי תְרֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ, בְּאַפֵּי בֵּי תְרֵי פַּרְעֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: לְהָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ ״בְּאַפֵּי רְאוּבֵן וְשִׁמְעוֹן״ – כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא נִדְחֲיֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The creditor **said to** the debtor to repay him **in the presence of two** people who would serve as witnesses, and **he repaid him in the presence of two** people. Therefore, the creditor has no further claim. **Rava said to him:** It is **for this** reason that the creditor **said to him** to repay him **in the presence of Reuven and Shimon: So that he will not** be able to **dismiss him** by saying that he repaid him in the presence of other witnesses.

The debtor, ignoring this condition, repaid the creditor in front of two other witnesses — Levi and Yehuda. According to the reading of Tosafot, those witnesses subsequently traveled to *medinat hayam* and are no longer available. The debtor now claims before the court that he repaid the debt in front of Levi and Yehuda, who are abroad, and that this should suffice.

### Abaye's Position

Abaye rules in favor of the debtor. When the creditor said "repay me in front of Reuven and Shimon," he effectively meant "repay me in front of two witnesses." There is no meaningful legal distinction between one pair of valid witnesses and another; the naming of Reuven and Shimon was merely a way of expressing the requirement of witnesses generally. Since the debtor paid in front of two witnesses — and following Shmuel's position that one may claim witnesses went to *medinat hayam* — the debtor is believed and has discharged his obligation.

### Rava's Position

Rava disagrees sharply. If the creditor had simply meant "two witnesses," he would have said "two witnesses." The deliberate naming of Reuven and Shimon was purposeful: *lehachi amar lei be'appei Reuven veShimon* — precisely to foreclose the *medinat hayam* argument. The creditor knew Reuven and Shimon to be reliably available, and specified them so that the debtor could not later invoke the Shmuel defense by naming two witnesses who happen to be away on a lengthy journey. By specifying Reuven and Shimon, the creditor closed off that avenue entirely.

### Tosafot's Interpretation

Tosafot's reading of Rava's position has important consequences for a further scenario. What if the debtor did not merely claim that Levi and Yehuda went to *medinat hayam*, but instead produced Levi and Yehuda in court, and they testified that the debt was repaid? According to Tosafot, this would be entirely effective. The creditor's stipulation naming Reuven and Shimon was designed solely to prevent the *medinat hayam* evasion — to ensure that the debtor could not name absent witnesses. But if actual witnesses appear and testify to repayment, the creditor cannot invoke the stipulation to invalidate their testimony. The debt was objectively repaid and can be proven in court; the creditor has no power to stipulate away valid testimony that stands before the court. The creditor's condition related to *evidence*, not to the legal reality of repayment itself.

### Rashi's View

Rashi reads the case differently and reaches a more far-reaching conclusion. According to Rashi, by stipulating that repayment must occur in front of Reuven and Shimon, the creditor effectively established — and the debtor implicitly accepted — that any other testimony would be treated as invalid. When the debtor received the money on those terms, he agreed that the only witnesses whose testimony would be accepted in this matter are Reuven and Shimon. Thus, even if Levi and Yehuda actually witnessed the repayment and stand ready to testify, their testimony is legally null by force of the prior agreement. For *halacha* purposes, it is as though the debt was never repaid.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

The debate between Rashi and Tosafot thus turns on how far the creditor's stipulatory power reaches. For Tosafot, a creditor may stipulate regarding the *mode of proof* — requiring that witnesses be producible rather than merely claimed as absent — but may not stipulate to *invalidate* testimony that is actually present. For Rashi, the parties may contractually agree to treat specific named witnesses as the exclusive valid testimony in this matter, such that even actual testimony from others is disregarded. One might ask whether, under Rashi's reading, the appearance of Levi and Yehuda might at least trigger a *shvuat hesset* — a floating oath — rather than leaving the creditor with no remedy at all. Perhaps so, but the core point is that for formal collection purposes, Rashi holds that their testimony has been rendered legally inoperative by the prior agreement.

## Quality Witnesses

### Halacha Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״כִּי פָּרְעַתְּ לִי, פִּרְעַן (לִי) בְּאַפֵּי בֵּי תְרֵי דְּתָנוּ הִלְכְתָא״. אֲזַל פַּרְעֵיהּ בֵּין דִּילֵיהּ לְדִילֵיהּ, אִיתְּנִיסוּ הָנָךְ זוּזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** creditor **who said to** the debtor: **When you repay me, repay me in the presence of two** people **who** have **learned *halakha*.** The debtor **went** and **repaid him between the two of them,** i.e., in the absence of witnesses. **Those dinars were** subsequently taken from the creditor due to **circumstances beyond his control.**

The second case involves a creditor who stipulated not named individuals but a qualitative standard: when you repay me, pay me in front of two witnesses who know *halacha*. The concern, perhaps, was that witnesses unfamiliar with the legal context might not appreciate the significance of what they were observing.

### Private Payment

The debtor disregarded this condition entirely and repaid the creditor in private, without any witnesses at all. More specifically, the creditor admits that he received the money — no dispute about that fact. But then a misfortune occurred: immediately after the debtor handed over the money, someone ran up and seized it from the creditor's hands. The parties came before Rav Nachman to adjudicate.

### Deposit Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אִין, קַבּוֹלֵי קַבֵּלְתִּינְהוּ מִינֵּיהּ – דֶּרֶךְ פִּקָּדוֹן; וְאָמֵינָא לֶיהֱוֵי גַּבַּאי פִּקָּדוֹן, עַד דְּמִתְרְמוּ בֵּי תְרֵי דְּתָנוּ הִלְכְתָא וּמְקַיֵּים תְּנָאֵיהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The creditor **came before Rav Naḥman** for judgment, and **said to him: Yes, I received** the money **from him;** but since he did not repay me as stipulated, I accepted it only **as a deposit.** I accepted it as an unpaid bailee, not as payment, **and said** to myself: **Let it be with me as a deposit until two** people **who** have **learned *halakha* happen** to arrive, **and** the debtor **will fulfill his condition.** Since the money was taken from me due to circumstances beyond my control, and I had it in my possession only as an unpaid bailee, I am not responsible for it, and the debtor is still liable to repay me.

The creditor argued as follows: yes, he concedes that he accepted the money from the debtor — *in, kiburei kabaltinhu* — but since the stipulation required repayment in front of witnesses who know *halacha*, when he actually accepted the money in private, he was not accepting it *as repayment of the debt*. Since the precondition for repayment was not met, the money was taken *derech pikadon* — in the capacity of a deposit. The creditor was holding the debtor's hundred *zuzi* temporarily, intending to return it until such time as the repayment could be properly conducted in front of qualified witnesses. Since he held it merely as a *shomer chinam*, and someone then stole it, he bears no liability to the debtor — and the debt itself remains outstanding.

### Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כֵּיוָן דְּקָא מוֹדֵית דְּוַדַּאי שְׁקַלְתִּינְהוּ מִינֵּיהּ – פֵּרָעוֹן מְעַלְּיָא הָוֵי; אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְקַיּוֹמֵי תְּנָאֵיהּ – זִיל אַיְיתִינְהוּ. דְּהָא אֲנָא וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, דִּתְנֵינָא הִלְכְתָא וְסִפְרָא וְסִפְרֵי וְתוֹסֶפְתָּא וְכוֹלָּא תַּלְמוּדָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Since you admit that you certainly took** the money **from him, it is a proper repayment. If you say** that the debtor is still required **to fulfill his condition, go bring** the money now, **as Rav Sheshet and I** have **learned *halakha*, and *Sifra*, and *Sifrei*, and *Tosefta*, and the entire Talmud.** Let him give you the money in our presence, and the condition will thereby be fulfilled. Since he gave you the money intending to repay the debt, and did not agree to entrust it to you as an unpaid bailee, your claim is not valid.

Rav Nachman rejected this argument outright. Since the creditor conceded that he definitely received the money from the debtor — *keivan de-ka modit de-vadai shakaltih mineih* — it constitutes full repayment, *piraon malya hvei*. If the creditor truly wished to enforce his stipulation about witnesses who know *halacha*, the remedy was simple: *zil aitinhu* — go bring such witnesses. Rav Nachman added, with apparent wit, that he and Rav Sheshet themselves — who had mastered *halacha*, *sifra*, *sifrei*, *tosefta*, and the entire *gemara* — could serve as those witnesses. The debtor could hand back the money and receive it in their presence, thereby satisfying the condition.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot notes that Rav Nachman's offer was *bdichuta be'alma* — essentially a joke — not a serious ruling that the stipulation could still be fulfilled. The deeper point Tosafot draws out is this: once the creditor admits the money was received, no stipulation can reframe that accepted payment as something other than repayment of the debt. A creditor may stipulate regarding what constitutes *evidence* — he may limit what proofs will be accepted in court. But he cannot stipulate that when the debtor actually hands him money and he takes it, it will be treated legally as something other than repayment. At the point of admitted receipt, the law of repayment applies regardless of any prior condition.

### Rashi's Distinction

Rashi introduces a more nuanced point. His reasoning suggests that the creditor's argument fails not because such a stipulation is categorically impossible, but because in this case the creditor said nothing at the moment of receiving the money. Had the creditor explicitly declared at that moment — "I am accepting this not as payment of the debt, but only as a *pikadon*" — that declaration might have been effective. What defeats him here is *devarim shebelev einam devarim*: the unspoken intent to treat the payment as a deposit carries no legal weight. It was a thought in his heart, never expressed.

### R. Akiva Eiger

Rabbi Akiva Eiger in his *Gilyon HaShas* takes a more radical position. Even if the creditor had declared explicitly at the moment of receipt that he was accepting the money only as a *pikadon* and not as payment — and even if the debtor said nothing to the contrary — Rabbi Akiva Eiger holds that this would still not work. If the debtor handed over the money and declared that he was paying it as repayment of the debt, then that is what it is. The creditor, no matter how loudly he protests that he is accepting it only as a deposit, cannot override the debtor's act of repayment through his own unilateral declaration. The debtor owed a hundred *zuzi*, he gave a hundred *zuzi*, he said he was repaying the debt — the debt is paid, and no verbal formula by the creditor changes that legal reality.

## Denial Then Proof

### Debtor's Denial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי מְאָה זוּזֵי דְּמַסֵּיקְנָא בָּךְ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לֹא הָיוּ דְבָרִים מֵעוֹלָם״. אֲזַל אַיְיתִי סָהֲדִי דְּאוֹזְפֵיהּ וּפַרְעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person **who said to another: Give me** the **hundred dinars that I claim from you.** The latter **said to him:** This **matter never happened;** you did not lend me money. The creditor **went** and **brought witnesses who** testified that **he lent** the money to **him and** that the debtor had **repaid him.**

The third case: a creditor demands repayment of a hundred *zuzi*. The debtor responds: *lo hayu devarim me'olam* — nothing of the sort ever occurred; I have no idea what you are talking about.

### Witness Testimony

The debtor then produces witnesses. Those witnesses testify both that the debtor borrowed the money *and* that he repaid it.

### Abaye's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַאי נִיעְבּוּד? אִינְהוּ אָמְרִי אוֹזְפֵיהּ, אִינְהוּ אָמְרִי פַּרְעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: What** is there for the court to **do** in this case? The same witnesses said both statements; **they said** that the creditor **lent him** the money, and **they** also **said** that the debtor **repaid him.**

Abaye says: *mai ne'avid* — what can we do to compel the debtor to pay? If we accept the witnesses' testimony in its entirety, they say he borrowed and they say he repaid — the result is that the debt is considered discharged. If we ignore the witnesses, the debtor has denied the loan outright, which is *kofer hakol*, and without a *shtar* he cannot be compelled to pay. Either way, the debtor walks free. The witnesses effectively establish repayment regardless of the denial.

### Rava's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״לֹא לָוִיתִי״ – כְּאוֹמֵר ״לֹא פָּרַעְתִּי״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Anyone who says: I did not borrow, is** considered **like** one who **says: I did not repay.** Since there is testimony that he borrowed the money, and he admits that he did not repay it, he is liable to repay the debt.

Rava reaches a strikingly different conclusion. When the debtor declared *lo hayu devarim me'olam*, he was not merely denying the loan — he was implicitly testifying that he never repaid anything. The phrase is unambiguous: nothing of this matter ever happened. That cannot be read as "I borrowed and repaid"; it can only mean he is asserting that the entire transaction, including any repayment, never occurred. That implicit testimony — that he never paid — now stands as his own admission against himself. The principle of *hoda'at ba'al din k'mei'ah edim dami* means that one's own admission against oneself carries even greater weight than witness testimony. Therefore, we take the witnesses' testimony that he borrowed, combine it with the debtor's own implicit testimony that he never repaid, and the result is that the debt is outstanding and he must pay.

### Testimony Analysis

This is a striking position. In effect, Rava splits the witness testimony — accepting the half that establishes the loan while the other half, which establishes repayment, is overridden by the debtor's own words. Tosafot observes that had the debtor said something more ambiguous — "I did not borrow" rather than the absolutist *lo hayu devarim me'olam* — we might have interpreted the denial charitably as meaning "I no longer owe you, because I repaid." But the phrase *lo hayu devarim me'olam* cannot be read that way. It is a categorical denial of any transaction whatsoever, and by that very categorical denial the debtor has implicitly testified against his own eventual claim to have repaid.

## Witnesses Deny Repayment

### Debtor's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי מְאָה זוּזֵי דְּמַסֵּיקְנָא בָּךְ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לָא פְּרַעְתִּיךָ בִּפְנֵי פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי?״ אֲתוֹ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי אָמְרִי: ״לֹא הָיוּ דְבָרִים מֵעוֹלָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person **who said to another: Give me** the **hundred dinars that I claim from you.** The latter **said to him: Didn't I repay you in the presence of so-and-so and so-and-so?** The two people he mentioned, **so-and-so and so-and-so, came** and **said:** This **matter never happened.**

The fourth case: the creditor demands payment of a hundred *zuzi*. The debtor responds that he already paid — specifically, that he paid in front of Ploni and Ploni, naming two individuals as his witnesses.

### Contradiction

The named witnesses are brought to court. Ploni and Ploni testify: *lo hayu devarim me'olam* — no such payment ever took place. They contradict the debtor directly.

### Rav Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת לְמֵימַר: הוּחְזַק כַּפְרָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: כֹּל מִילְּתָא דְלָא רַמְיָא עֲלֵיהּ דְּאִינָשׁ – לָאו אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet thought to say** that based on the testimony of the witnesses, the debtor **assumes the presumptive status of one who** falsely **denies** his debts; his claim that he repaid the debt is no longer accepted, and he is liable to pay. **Rava said to him: Anything that is not incumbent upon a person is not on his mind,** i.e., he is apt to forget it.

Rav Sheshet considered ruling that the debtor's credibility has been undermined — *huchzak kafan* — in this specific case. Ordinarily, a debtor who denies owing money would be believed, perhaps subject to a *shvuat hesset*. But here, the debtor identified specific witnesses to the alleged payment, and those very witnesses denied it. His credibility in this particular dispute has been destroyed, and even if he were now willing to take an oath, the court would not believe him. He must pay. (This designation of *huchzak kafan* applies only to this specific claim; his general standing in future matters is unaffected.)

### Rava

Rava disagrees and invokes the principle: *kol milta dalo ramya aleh de'inshi lav adaiteh* — for matters that people do not generally pay careful attention to, an error does not constitute a lie, and credibility is not thereby undermined. The debtor knew he had to pay back the money, but he may not have been careful to note the precise identities of the bystanders who happened to witness the transaction.

### Knowledge Standard

Rava's *chiddush* is grounded in the general *halacha* that a debtor without a *shtar* outstanding against him is believed to claim repayment — potentially with a *shvua*, but believed nonetheless. Because the debtor knew he did not strictly need to produce witnesses to be believed, he may not have paid careful attention to exactly who Ploni and Ploni were. He believed them to be those individuals, but since he was never truly relying on them as formal witnesses — his own word being sufficient — the identification error does not rise to the level of a deliberate falsehood. *Lo ramya aleh* — it was not his legal responsibility to track the witnesses' identities — and therefore his credibility is not undermined.

## Gallnuts Price

### Gallnuts Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי שֵׁית מְאָה זוּזֵי דְּמַסֵּיקְנָא בָּךְ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״וְלָא פְּרַעְתָּיךָ מְאָה קַבֵּי עַפְצֵי דְּקָיְימִי בְּשִׁיתָּא שִׁיתָּא?״
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person **who said to another: Give me** the **six hundred dinars that I claim from you.** The latter **said to him: But didn't I repay you** with **one hundred *kav*** **of gallnuts [*aftzei*] that were** worth **six** dinars for **each** *kav* at the time?

The fifth case: the creditor demands 600 *zuzi*. The debtor replies: did I not repay you with 100 measures of gallnuts, valued at six *zuzi* per measure? Six times one hundred equals six hundred — the debt is fully paid.

### Price Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לָאו בְּאַרְבְּעָה אַרְבָּעָה הֲווֹ קָיְימִי?״ אֲתוֹ תְּרֵי סָהֲדִי וַאֲמַרוּ: ״אִין, בְּאַרְבְּעָה אַרְבָּעָה הֲווֹ קָיְימִי״. אָמַר רָבָא: הוּחְזַק כַּפְרָן. אֲמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הָא אָמְרַתְּ כֹּל מִילְּתָא דְּלָא רַמְיָא עֲלֵיהּ דְּאִינִישׁ לָאו אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: קִצּוּתָא דְּתַרְעָא מִידְכָּר דְּכִירִי אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The creditor **said to him: Weren't they** worth **four** dinars for **each** *kav* at the time? **Two witnesses came and said: Yes, they were** worth **four** dinars per *kav*. **Rava said** that the debtor **assumes the presumptive status of one who** falsely **denies** his debts. **Rami bar Ḥama said: But didn't you say** that **anything that is not incumbent upon a person is not on his mind?** Perhaps he merely forgot what the price of gallnuts was at the time that he paid. **Rava said to him: People remember the** standard, **set** market **price.**

The creditor disputes the valuation. He says the gallnuts were not going at six per *zuz* that day; they were going at four per *zuz*. The debtor therefore paid the equivalent of only 400 *zuzi* and still owes 200.

### Credibility Loss

Two witnesses then testify that on the relevant day the market price — the *sha'ar* — for gallnuts was indeed four *zuzi* per measure, confirming the creditor's account. Rava rules that the debtor's credibility has been undermined: *huchzak kafan*. If the debtor now claims to have paid the remaining 200 in cash, or makes any further argument, he is no longer believed.

### Challenge

Rami bar Chama challenges this: did Rava himself not say just moments ago that matters a person does not closely track — *kol milta dalo ramya aleh de'inshi lav adaiteh* — do not undermine credibility? Perhaps the debtor simply misremembered the price.

### Rava's Answer

Rava responds with a distinction. The price of goods — the *kitzutza detara*, the market rate — is something people consciously know. When a debtor chooses to repay a large loan in gallnuts, he is making a deliberate commercial decision and necessarily knows the current rate. Unlike the identity of bystanders who happen to be present, which is not something a person ordinarily tracks, the exchange rate for the commodity he is using as payment is precisely the kind of information he is responsible for knowing. Claiming that gallnuts were worth six when witnesses establish they were worth four is therefore a conscious misrepresentation, not an innocent error, and it does undermine his credibility.

## Different Debt Claim

### Creditor's Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי מְאָה זוּזֵי דְּמַסֵּיקְנָא בָּךְ, וְהָא שְׁטָרָא״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״פְּרַעְתִּיךָ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הָנְהוּ סִיטְרָאֵי נִינְהוּ״. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אִיתְּרַע שְׁטָרָא. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: לָא אִיתְּרַע שְׁטָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person **who said to another: Give me** the **hundred dinars that I claim from you, and this is the** promissory **note** attesting to the debt. The latter **said to him: I** already **repaid you.** The creditor **said to him: That** money you gave me **was** for **a different** debt. **Rav Naḥman said** that **the** promissory **note is undermined** by the fact that the creditor admits that he received payment equal to the amount specified in the note, and his claim that there was an additional debt is unsubstantiated. **Rav Pappa said** that **the** promissory **note is not undermined.**

The sixth case: the creditor presents a *shtar* and demands a hundred *zuzi*. The debtor claims he paid. The creditor then does something unexpected — he concedes that he received a hundred *zuzi* from the debtor.

### Other Debt Claim

But the creditor asserts: *hani sitrai ninhu* — those funds were for a *different* debt. He claims there was a separate oral loan between them, and the hundred *zuzi* he received discharged that other obligation. The *shtar* debt, he insists, remains outstanding.

### Rav Nachman

Rav Nachman rules: *itra shtara* — the *shtar* has been undermined. Once the creditor admits to having received a hundred *zuzi* and cannot prove the existence of any other loan, we presume that payment was for the *shtar* debt, and the *shtar* can no longer be used to collect. According to Rashi, this means the *shtar* is entirely unusable.

### Rav Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב פָּפָּא – מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי מְאָה זוּזֵי דְּמַסֵּיקְנָא בָּךְ, וְהָא שְׁטָרָא״; אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לָאו אַתּוֹרֵי יְהַבְתְּ לִי, וַאֲתֵית וְאִיתֵּיבְתְּ אַמְּסַחְתָּא – וְקַבֵּילְתְּ זוּזָךְ?״ וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הָנְהוּ סִיטְרָאֵי נִינְהוּ״; וַאֲמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִיתְּרַע שְׁטָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to Rav Pappa,** in **what** way **is** this case **different from** the incident where **a certain** person **said to another: Give me** the **hundred dinars that I claim from you, and this is the** promissory **note.** The latter **said to him: Didn't you give me** that money as an investment to be used **for** buying **oxen** to be slaughtered? **And you came and sat in the slaughterhouse and received your money,** including your share of the profits, from the sale of the slaughtered oxen. **And** the creditor **said to him: That** money you gave me **was** for **a different** debt. **And Rav Pappa said** that in that case **the** promissory **note is undermined.**

Rav Pappa disagrees: *lo itra shtara* — the *shtar* is not undermined. The creditor still holds the *shtar*, which is prima facie evidence of an outstanding debt. The debtor has not independently proven that the money he paid was for this particular debt. The creditor's claim that it was for a different obligation is plausible, and he is believed. Tosafot, citing Rabbeinu Tam, offers a middle position: the *shtar* is not nullified outright, but if the creditor wishes to collect on it, he must now support it with a *shvua* attesting that it has not been paid.

### Contextual Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּקָאָמַר ״אַתּוֹרֵי יְהַבְתְּ לִי וּמִתּוֹרֵי שָׁקְלַתְּ״ – אִיתְּרַע שְׁטָרָא; הָכָא, אֵימוֹר סִיטְרָאֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There, since** the debtor **said: You gave me** the money **for oxen and you took** payment **from oxen,** and the creditor admitted that this had happened, **the** promissory **note is undermined** because there is no support for his claim that there was another debt in addition to the acknowledged transaction for the oxen. **Here, say** that the payment **was** in fact for **a different** debt.

The *gemara* then introduces a variant that illuminates the limits of Rav Pappa's position. Suppose the creditor lent the debtor money specifically for cattle trading — and the debtor repaid a hundred *zuzi* at the cattle market. The creditor again claims *hani sitrai ninhu* — that was for a different debt. Here, Rav Pappa himself concedes: *itra shtara*. The *shtar* is undermined. Why? Because the circumstances create an overwhelming contextual fit between the debt and the payment. The loan was for cattle; the repayment happened at the cattle market. The creditor's claim that this was for some other unrelated debt is implausible in that setting, and he is not believed.

In the original case, by contrast, there is no such contextual connection. The payment and the loan are linked only by the amount. In that circumstance, the creditor retains sufficient credibility to claim the payment was for a separate oral debt, and the *shtar* stands.

### No Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי, דְּפַרְעֵיהּ בְּאַפֵּי סָהֲדִי וְלָא אִידְּכַר לֵיהּ שְׁטָרָא; אֲבָל פַּרְעֵיהּ בֵּין דִּידֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ – מִיגּוֹ דְּיָכוֹל לְמֵימַר ״לֹא הָיוּ דְבָרִים מֵעוֹלָם״, יָכוֹל נָמֵי לְמֵימַר ״סִיטְרָאֵי נִינְהוּ״, וּכְדַאֲבִימִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this statement** applies in a case **where he repaid him in the presence of witnesses and did not mention the** promissory **note to** the creditor; **but** in a case where **he repaid him** privately, **between the two of them,** in the absence of witnesses, **since [*miggo*]** the creditor **can say to him:** This **matter never happened,** i.e., he could deny that he received any payment, **he can also say** that **this** money was for **a different** debt. **And** this is **like** the case involving **Avimi, son of Rabbi Abbahu,** who repaid a debt in the absence of witnesses, and the creditor then claimed that the payment was for another debt (see *Ketubot* 85a).

A clarification: the entire debate over *itra* or *lo itra shtara* concerns a case where there were actually witnesses to the payment — two people who saw the debtor hand over the hundred *zuzi* — but those witnesses said nothing about the *shtar*. The debtor did not ask for the *shtar* to be returned or cancelled in their presence. That is where the dispute applies, and it is the larger *chiddush* of the position that says *lo itra shtara*: even with witnesses to a payment, the creditor's holding of the *shtar* combined with a claim of alternative debt may be sufficient to maintain the *shtar*'s validity.

### Final Rule

A third scenario resolves more simply. If there are no witnesses to the payment at all — the creditor simply admits on his own that he received the money — then everyone agrees the *shtar* is valid and the creditor is believed to say it was for another debt. The principle of *migo* applies: since the creditor could have simply denied receiving any payment at all, the fact that he admitted it and attributed it to a different loan makes his claim credible. The *gemara* notes that this parallels the case cited in the name of Abimi son of Rabbi Abbahu in Ketubot. The *halacha* is ruled as *itra shtara* in the contested case — the *shtar* is undermined when witnesses saw the payment even if the *shtar* was not mentioned — while in the case of the creditor's own unaided admission, he retains the benefit of *migo*.

## Stipulated Belief

### Stipulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״מְהֵימְנַתְּ לִי כֹּל אֵימַת דְּאָמְרַתְּ לִי לָא פָּרַעְנָא״. אֲזַל פַּרְעֵיהּ בְּאַפֵּי סָהֲדִי. אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הָא הֵימְנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person **who said to another** who had lent him money: **I deem you credible whenever you say to me** that **I did not repay** the debt. He then **went** and **repaid** the debt **in the presence of witnesses,** and the creditor later denied that he had been repaid. **Abaye and Rava both say** that the witnesses are not deemed credible and the creditor can collect payment, as the debtor **deemed him credible** at the outset.

The seventh case involves a debtor who explicitly stipulates, as a condition of receiving the loan, that the creditor will always be believed to claim that the debt has not been repaid. This arrangement is made to reassure the creditor — perhaps the creditor feared the debtor might later claim *medinat hayam* or produce witnesses in dubious circumstances — and the debtor formally grants the creditor enhanced credibility: *mehemnat li kol eimat d'amart li lo parana* — you are believed to me whenever you say I have not repaid.

### Oath Demanded

The debtor later pays in front of witnesses. The creditor denies the repayment and, invoking the stipulation, demands that his word prevail. The debtor points to the witnesses.

### Abaye and Rava

Abaye and Rava both rule that the stipulation stands: since the debtor agreed that the creditor would be believed, that agreement carries more weight than the testimony of witnesses. The creditor is believed.

### Rav Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: נְהִי דְּהֵימְנֵיהּ טְפֵי מִנַּפְשֵׁיהּ, טְפֵי מִסָּהֲדֵי מִי הֵימְנֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this** and says: **Although he deemed him more credible than himself** concerning the possibility that the debtor would claim that he repaid the debt and the creditor would deny having been repaid, **did he deem him more credible than witnesses?** Therefore, he is exempt.

Rav Pappa challenges this. When the debtor said the creditor would be believed, he meant *nee d'hemnai tfei m'nafshei* — that the creditor would be believed over the debtor's own claims. The debtor was granting the creditor enhanced standing compared to himself — foreclosing a simple *kofer hakol* defense — but he was not saying the creditor would be believed over witnesses. The phrase "you are believed" means more than me, not more than external testimony.

## Multiple Witness Equival…

### Two or Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״מְהֵימְנַתְּ לִי כְּבֵי תְרֵי, כׇּל אֵימַת דְּאָמְרַתְּ לָא פָּרַעְנָא״. אֲזַל פַּרְעֵיהּ בְּאַפֵּי תְּלָתָא. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּבֵי תְרֵי הֵימְנֵיהּ, כְּבֵי תְלָתָא לָא הֵימְנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person **who said to another** who had lent him money: **I deem you credible like two** witnesses **whenever you say** that **I did not repay. He went and repaid** the debt **in the presence of three** witnesses. **Rav Pappa said** that the creditor cannot deny their testimony, as the debtor **deemed him credible like two** witnesses; **he did not deem him credible like three** witnesses.

This leads the *gemara* to a more precise version of the stipulation: the debtor now says explicitly that the creditor is believed *k'bei trei* — like two witnesses — whenever he claims non-payment. The debtor subsequently pays in front of three witnesses. The question is whether the creditor's stipulated weight of two neutralizes or is overridden by three.

### Number Question

The further extension: what if the debtor said the creditor is believed like three witnesses, and the debtor then pays in front of four?

### Rav Pappa's Rule

Rav Pappa holds that the stipulation is limited to the number stated. If the creditor was granted the weight of two witnesses and the debtor paid in front of three, the three override the two. The creditor's stipulated credibility did not include being treated as more than two.

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַב פָּפָּא: אֵימוֹר דְּאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן דְּאָזְלִינַן בָּתַר רוֹב דֵּעוֹת – הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן אוּמְדָּנָא, דְּכַמָּה דִּנְפִישִׁי בְּקִיאִי טְפֵי; אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן עֵדוּת – מְאָה כִּתְרֵי, וּתְרֵי כִּמְאָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rav Pappa: Say** that although **the Sages say that we follow the majority of opinions,** and the opinion of three people is therefore accepted against the opinion of two, **this statement** applies **with regard to assessing** value, **as the more** people there are, **the more knowledgeable they are. But with regard to testimony, one hundred** witnesses **are like two, and two are like one hundred.** Therefore, in this case there is no distinction between two witnesses and three witnesses.

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua challenges Rav Pappa. He invokes the well-known principle: *trei k'mei'a u'mei'a k'trei* — in matters of testimony, two witnesses are the equivalent of a hundred. When does the count of witnesses actually matter? Only in the context of *umdena* — assessments, such as when appraisers are brought to value property for seizure in payment of debt. There, more assessors means greater expertise, and the majority view prevails. But in formal legal testimony, two is identical in weight to a hundred. Therefore, if the creditor was granted the status of two witnesses, he equivalently has the status of any number of witnesses, and he neutralizes even three.

### Quantity Counting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״כְּבֵי תְלָתָא״, וַאֲזַל פַּרְעֵיהּ בְּאַפֵּי בֵּי אַרְבְּעָה – כֵּיוָן דִּנְחֵית לְדֵעוֹת, נְחֵית לְדֵעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another extension: **But if** the debtor **said to** the creditor that he deems him credible **like three** witnesses, **and** then **went** and **repaid him in the presence of four,** then **once he entered,** by mentioning a larger number than what is necessary for testimony, the realm of **opinions,** in which three people carry greater weight than two, **he has entered** the realm of **opinions,** and four witnesses are deemed more credible than three. Therefore, the creditor is not deemed credible against them.

The *gemara* then addresses the case where the debtor stipulated "three witnesses" rather than "two." Here, Rav Huna concedes that four witnesses would indeed override the stipulation. Why? Because once the debtor specified three rather than two, the only purpose of mentioning a larger number is to invoke the logic of counting — *keivan de'neichis le'de'ot neichis le'de'ot*. Since there is no qualitative difference between two and three witnesses in terms of testimonial weight, specifying three can only be meaningful if the debtor intended the *quantity* of witnesses to matter. Having imported numerical counting into the stipulation, four witnesses are then sufficient to override a stipulated weight of three.

### Agunah Application

This legal device — stipulating that one party will be believed against a certain weight of testimony — has practical relevance beyond the monetary cases under discussion. It is invoked in attempts to address the *agunah* problem. One proposed solution involves *kiddushin al tnai* — conditional betrothal — but such arrangements are vulnerable to the husband's ability to nullify the condition during the marriage by declaring before witnesses that he is *mevatel* the *tnai*. To foreclose this, the arrangement can include a stipulation in which the husband grants the wife the status of being believed — even against witnesses the husband might later produce — to establish that no such nullification took place. The mechanism analyzed in this *gemara*, by which one party's credibility can be contractually calibrated against a specified number of witnesses, is thus the formal underpinning of such solutions.

## No Oath Rules

### Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עַל טַעֲנַת חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, וְאֵין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אֶת הַקָּטָן. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי יִתֵּן אִישׁ אֶל רֵעֵהוּ כֶּסֶף אוֹ כֵלִים לִשְׁמוֹר״, וְאֵין נְתִינַת קָטָן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One does not take an oath concerning the claim of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor, and** the court **does not administer an oath to a minor.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason?** The Gemara answers that in the passage from which the *halakhot* of admission to part of a claim are derived, **the verse states: "If a man delivers to his neighbor silver or vessels to safeguard"** (Exodus 22:6). The word "man" indicates that the reference is only to adults, and **delivery by a minor is nothing,** i.e., it is not recognized as a halakhically significant act, as a minor is not halakhically competent.

The *gemara* turns to a new *mishnah*: one does not take a *shvua* — specifically a *shvuat modeh bemiktzat* — when the claim is made against one by a *cheireish*, *shoteh*, or *katan*. Equally, one does not administer an oath to a *katan*. Rashi notes parenthetically that he had forgotten to explain this *mishnah* when it was first cited and is explaining it here — *shachachti milifaresh hamishnah vehineni mefaresh kan* — a remarkably candid admission given that Rashi is known to have produced multiple editions of his commentary.

### Ki Yitein Ish

The *gemara* derives the rule from the verse *ki yitein ish el re'ehu* — "when a man gives to his fellow." The giving must be the act of an *ish*, a legally competent adult. The giving of a *katan* does not satisfy that standard. This does not mean that a minor cannot create a debt — legally, a minor can have property and a debt can arise from a transaction with him. But the Talmudic mechanism that generates the oath requirement is tied to the quality of the original giving, and that quality is absent when the lender was a *katan*.

### Exception Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל נִשְׁבָּעִין לְקָטָן וּלְהֶקְדֵּשׁ. וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ רֵישָׁא: אֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עַל טַעֲנַת שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **But one does take an oath to a minor, or to** a representative of **the Temple** treasury with regard to consecrated property. The Gemara asks: **But didn't you say** in **the first clause** that **one does not take an oath concerning the claim of** a deaf-mute, **an imbecile, or a minor?**

The *mishnah* continues: nevertheless, one does take an oath *to* a *katan* or to *hekdesh*. The *gemara* questions the apparent contradiction: if the rule is that oaths are not required when the claimant is a *katan*, how can one be required to swear *to* a *katan*?

### Rav Bibi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: בְּבָא בְּטַעֲנַת אָבִיו, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב הִיא; דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: פְּעָמִים שֶׁאָדָם נִשְׁבָּע עַל טַעֲנַת עַצְמוֹ. כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: ״מָנֶה לְאָבִיךָ בְּיָדִי וְהֶאֱכַלְתִּיו פְּרָס״ – הֲרֵי זֶה נִשְׁבָּע, וְזֶהוּ שֶׁנִּשְׁבַּע עַל טַעֲנַת עַצְמוֹ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא כְּמֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה, וּפָטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said:** The *halakha* that one takes an oath concerning the claim of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor is **with regard to** one who **comes** to court **with a claim** for a debt owed **to his** late **father, and it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** There are **times when** although no one claimed of a person that he owes money, that **person takes an oath on** the basis of **his own claim. How** so? If one **said to** another: **Your father had one hundred dinars in my possession, but I provided him** with repayment of **half** that amount, he is liable to **take an oath** that he repaid half; **and that is** the case of one **who takes an oath on** the basis of **his own claim. And the Rabbis say:** In that case **he is only like one returning a lost item,** as the son did not claim the money at all, **and** is **exempt** from taking an oath.

Rav Bibi resolves the contradiction: the case in which an oath is administered to a *katan* is where the money originated from the father. The *katan* comes to court and says: my father lent you a hundred *zuzi*, and the defendant admits to fifty. The oath is triggered not because the *katan* made the loan, but because the original transaction was between adults — *ki yitein ish* is satisfied by the father. The *katan* now appears as the heir pressing the father's claim. The relevant question, the *gemara* establishes, is not who is currently pressing the claim in court, but whether the original giving was by a competent adult.

## Self-Claim Oath

### Own Claim Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב – לֵית לֵיהּ מֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה פָּטוּר?! אָמַר רַב: בְּשֶׁטְּעָנוֹ קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov not of** the opinion that **one who returns a lost item** is **exempt** from taking an oath attesting to the fact that he did not take anything from it? **Rav said:** The *baraita* is referring to a case **where a minor advanced a claim** against **him.** The creditor's minor son claimed that the debtor did not repay any part of the loan to his father. The debtor's partial admission came in response to that claim. Therefore, his admission is not comparable to the act of returning a lost item.

The *gemara* identifies the position of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov: *pe'amim she'adam nishba al ta'anat atzmo* — there are instances in which a person takes an oath based on a claim that, in a sense, originates from himself rather than from a fully standing opposing party.

### Orphan Example

The example: a person approaches an orphan and volunteers that he borrowed a hundred *zuzi* from the orphan's father and has repaid fifty — *amar lo maneh la'avicha beyadi veHe'echaltiv pras*. He thus creates a partial admission — *modeh bemiktzat* — before a party who made no formal demand. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov rules that this person must take an oath. The *gemara* asks: if no claim was made against him, is he not simply like one who returns a lost object — *meshiv aveida* — a person who volunteers information he was under no obligation to disclose and whom we simply believe? Why impose a *shvua*?

The answer: this is not a case of pure voluntary disclosure. The *katan* — or more precisely, as the *gemara* will resolve, the orphan — did make some form of demand. It is that demand, combined with the partial admission, that triggers the oath. Rav states: *beta'ano katan* — the minor pressed a claim against him. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds that even the demand of a minor suffices to establish the claim side of the equation, so that the defendant's partial admission becomes a *modeh bemiktzat* requiring an oath.

### Adult Orphan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָטָן?! וְהָאָמְרַתְּ: אֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עַל טַעֲנַת חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן! לְעוֹלָם גָּדוֹל, וְאַמַּאי קָרוּ לֵיהּ קָטָן? דִּלְגַבֵּי מִילֵּי דַּאֲבוּהּ – קָטָן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can the *baraita* be referring to the claim of **a minor? But didn't you say** in the mishna that **one does not take an oath concerning the claim of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor?** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the reference is to **an adult** son; **and why did** Rav **call him a minor?** It was due to the fact **that with regard to his father's matters,** one **is** like **a minor,** as he is uncertain about the particulars of his father's dealings. Here, too, Rav explains that the *halakha* in the mishna that one takes an oath to a minor is referring to an adult claiming a debt owed to his late father.

The *gemara* pushes back: if the oath is triggered by the *katan*'s demand, why does the *mishna* call this *ta'anat atzmo* — a claim from himself — if there is actually a *katan* demanding? Moreover, why do the *Rabbanan* say he is merely *meshiv aveida* if there is a claimant? The *gemara* resolves: *le'olam gadol* — the orphan in question is actually an adult. When the *mishna* calls him a *katan*, it means he is a "minor" only in relation to the father's dealings — he is the heir, not the original party to the loan. For purposes of the Talmudic analysis, the case is one of an adult orphan pressing his late father's claim. This is why the *Rabbanan* can argue that the admission is merely *meshiv aveida*, and why Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's position must be grounded in something other than the ordinary mechanics of *ta'anat* — a claim — from a fully standing party.

## Chutzpah Principle

### Chutzpah Lack

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בִּדְרַבָּה קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה מוֹדֶה מִקְצָת הַטַּעֲנָה יִשָּׁבַע? חֲזָקָה אֵין אָדָם מֵעִיז פָּנָיו בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל חוֹבוֹ. וְהַאי – בְּכוּלֵּיהּ בָּעֵי דְּלִיכְפְּרֵיהּ, וְהַאי דְּלָא כַּפְרֵיהּ – מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵינוֹ מֵעִיז פָּנָיו בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל חוֹבוֹ;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov and the Rabbis **disagree with regard to** the statement **of Rabba, as Rabba says: For what** reason **did the Torah say** that one who **admits to a part of the claim must take an oath?** It is because there is **a presumption** that **a person does not exhibit insolence** by lying **in the presence of his creditor,** who did him a favor by lending money to him. **And this** person who denies part of the claim actually **wants to deny all of** the debt, so as to be exempt, **and this** fact, i.e., **that he does not deny** all of **it,** is **because a person does not exhibit insolence in the presence of his creditor.**

The *gemara* sets out Rabbah's foundational principle explaining why *modeh bemiktzat* requires a *shvua* at all: *chazakah ein adam me'iz panav bifnei ba'al chovo* — it is an established presumption that a person will not have the *chutzpah* to deny everything in the face of his creditor.

### Partial Admission

If a debtor is lying and trying to avoid paying, he would *want* to deny everything — *be-kulei bei d-lichpariah*. But he cannot bring himself to do so in front of the very person who lent him the money, because *eino me'iz panav bifnei ba'al chovo* — he lacks the brazenness to deny it entirely to his creditor's face. This is why the partial admission cannot simply be taken at face value. The debtor cannot invoke *migo* — the argument that since he could have denied everything and been believed, he should be believed when he partially admits. He could *not* have denied everything, because he would not have had the *chutzpah* to do so.

### Oath Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכוּלֵּיהּ בָּעֵי דְּלוֹדֵי לֵיהּ, וְהַאי דְּלָא אוֹדִי לֵיהּ – אִישְׁתְּמוֹטֵי הוּא דְּקָא מִשְׁתְּמִיט מִינֵּיהּ, סָבַר: עַד דְּהָוֵי לִי זוּזֵי וּפָרַעְנָא לֵיהּ; וְרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר רְמִי שְׁבוּעָה עִילָּוֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלוֹדֵי לֵיהּ בְּכוּלֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba continues: **And** in order not to exhibit insolence, he **wants to admit to** the creditor **with regard to all of** the debt; **and this** fact, i.e., **that he did not admit** the entire debt **to him,** is because he may be temporarily **avoiding** paying **him.** He **rationalizes** doing so by saying to himself: I am avoiding him only **until** the time **that I have** enough **money, and** then **I will repay him. And** therefore, **the Merciful One says** in the Torah: **Impose an oath on him in order** to induce the debtor **to admit the entire** debt **to him.**

Yet if the debtor truly could not deny it all, why does he not admit the full amount? Because — *ishtamutei hu de-ka mishtamit mineih* — he is merely stalling. He intends to pay eventually but needs time to gather the funds. He is not a true thief; he is a person under financial pressure trying to buy time. The *shvua* is therefore imposed to induce full admission — *rami shvua ileih ki heichi de-lodei leh* — since he is not truly dishonest, the weight of an oath will prompt him to acknowledge what he actually owes.

## Chutzpah Principle…

### Father vs. Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב סָבַר: לָא שְׁנָא בּוֹ וְלָא שְׁנָא בִּבְנוֹ – אֵינוֹ מֵעֵיז, וְהִלְכָּךְ לָאו מֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה הוּא. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: בְּפָנָיו הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ מֵעֵיז, אֲבָל בִּפְנֵי בְּנוֹ מֵעֵיז; וּמִדְּלֹא מֵעֵיז – מֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this principle, **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov maintains:** It **is no different with regard to** the creditor **himself, and** it **is no different with regard to his son;** the debtor **would not exhibit insolence** and deny the debt. **And therefore, he is not** deemed as **one returning a lost item** on his own initiative; rather, this is an ordinary case where one admits to a part of a claim and is therefore required to take an oath. **And the Rabbis maintain: It is in the presence of** the original creditor **that** one **would not exhibit insolence; but in the presence of his son,** who did not lend him the money, he would **exhibit insolence** and deny the claim entirely. **And since** this debtor **is not exhibiting insolence,** as he could have denied the loan completely but instead is opting to admit to part of the claim, **he is** deemed as **one returning a lost item,** and his claim is accepted without his taking an oath.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds that the *chutzpah* principle operates equally whether the creditor is the father himself or the father's son — *lo shena bo ve-lo shena bno eino me'iz*. One lacks the brazenness to deny a debt even to the heir of the person who lent the money. Therefore, there is no *migo* — the debtor could not simply have denied it all — and a *shvua* is required.

### Sages' View

The *Rabbanan* disagree. They hold that while a person will not have the *chutzpah* to deny the debt to the creditor himself — *be-vo hu de-eino me'iz* — he would have no such inhibition before the creditor's son — *aval bifnei bno me'iz*. If the debtor were lying and wanted to deny the debt, he would have had no difficulty doing so when facing the son rather than the father. Therefore, the *migo* argument does apply: since the debtor could have denied the entire debt to the son and been believed, the fact that he instead admitted to half establishes his credibility, making him *meshiv aveida* — a person returning a lost object — who needs no oath.

## Self-Claim Oath…

### Claim Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי מָצֵית מוֹקְמַתְּ לַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב?! הָא קָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא: ״מָנֶה לְאַבָּא בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא חֲמִשִּׁים דִּינָר״ – פָּטוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמֵּשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה הוּא! הָתָם דְּלָא אָמַר ״בָּרִי לִי״, הָכָא דְּאָמַר ״בָּרִי לִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Can you interpret** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov? Isn't it taught** in **the former clause** that if the claimant said: My late **father had one hundred dinars in your possession,** and the defendant responded: **You have only fifty dinars in my possession,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath, **as he is** like **one returning a lost item?** The Gemara answers: **There,** it is referring to a case **where** the claimant **did not say: I am certain** that you owe my father this money, but rather made an uncertain claim. In such a case, Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov agrees that the defendant is like one returning a lost item. **Here,** by contrast, it is a case **where he said: I am certain** that you owe him.

The foregoing analysis crystallizes the structural dispute between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *Rabbanan*. For Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, the adult orphan pressing his father's claim generates a sufficiently weighty *ta'anah* — combined with the *chutzpah* principle that operates even vis-à-vis the son — to produce a full-fledged *modeh bemiktzat* requiring an oath. For the *Rabbanan*, because one *would* have had the *chutzpah* to deny it all to the son, the partial admission before him is credible on its own terms and the person is simply *meshiv aveida*, exempt from any oath.
