# Beitzah 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Melila Methods

### Melila Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקִּשְׁרֵי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו וּלְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **from the joints of his fingers and above,** i.e., he does not place the kernels mixed with chaff in his palm but on his fingers, which is an unusual way to hold them.

The daf begins five lines from the bottom of 13b, in the midst of a discussion that arose in connection with the question of separating *terumah* on *Yom Tov*. That inquiry led to an extended treatment of removing husks from kernels of grain and legumes on *Yom Tov*. The *Gemara* had already established that such removal — *melila* — is permitted in principle, with one concern relating to quantity or the use of specialized vessels. Rashi explains: *keitzad molel b'Yom Tov* — how does one perform *melila* on *Yom Tov*? Before examining the *Gemara*'s answer, Rashi makes a foundational point: *v'havaday shinuy ba-ei* — it is assumed that even when using one's bare hands, the *melila* must be performed with some degree of *shinuy*, some departure from the ordinary weekday manner. The reason, Rashi explains, is *shinuy bo mi-uvda d'chol* — the act must be distinguished from what would constitute a weekday appearance. Not every permitted *melacha* on *Yom Tov* requires a *shinuy*; cooking certainly does not. But certain food-preparation activities, particularly those that are sometimes performed not in the immediate kitchen context but as part of a prior, large-scale pre-processing stage, carry with them a concern of *uvda d'chol*. For those, the *Chachamim* required some modification even on *Yom Tov*.

It is worth noting parenthetically that Tosafot confirms this discussion concerns *Yom Tov*: *u'veShabbat assur limlol* — on *Shabbat*, *melila* is simply forbidden. The Rif, somewhat remarkably, holds that *melila* may be performed even on *Shabbat* with a *shinuy*, and most *Rishonim* reject this outright. It is difficult to understand how a *melacha* could be permitted on *Shabbat* merely through a *shinuy*. And yet there are isolated food-preparation *melachot* that *Chazal* permitted on *Shabbat* with a *shinuy* performed in small quantities — *lisha* being the classic example. Anyone who has attempted to make oatmeal on *Shabbat* will appreciate the complexity: mixing water with a granular substance to produce a dough-like consistency is precisely the concern, yet some permit it with a *shinuy*, such as reversing the order of mixing or using the back of a spoon. The Rif apparently extends this framework to *melila*, though the consensus is that we are dealing exclusively with *Yom Tov*.

### Finger Techniques

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד מוֹלֵל? אַבָּיֵי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: חֲדָא אַחֲדָא. וְרַב אַוְיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: חֲדָא אַתַּרְתֵּי. רָבָא אָמַר: כֵּיוָן דִּמְשַׁנֵּי — אֲפִילּוּ חֲדָא אַכּוּלְּהוּ נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **How may one husk** grain on a Festival? **Abaye said in the name of Rav Yosef: One** finger **on another,** i.e., one may place the kernels between two fingers and rub. **And Rav Avya said in the name of Rav Yosef:** One may even do so **one on two,** i.e., between the thumb and two fingers. **Rava said: Since he alters** the manner in which he performs the activity, **he may even** do so with **one** finger **on all** the rest.

The *Gemara* asks: *keitzad molel?* — what is the proper technique? Abaye in the name of Rav Yosef says: *chada achada* — one finger against one finger, rubbing the husk off between two fingers. Rav Avya in the name of Rav Yosef says: *chada atartei* — one finger against two fingers, a slightly easier method. Rava says: *keivan d'meshani* — since in any case you are doing it differently from the most convenient manner, even using the thumb against all the fingers at once is acceptable. All of these presumably constitute a departure from whatever the standard large-scale method would be — perhaps using the palms, or a vessel altogether.

### Winnowing Technique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַחֲכוּ עֲלַהּ בְּמַעְרְבָא: כֵּיוָן דִּמְשַׁנֵּי — אֲפִילּוּ בְּכוּלַּהּ יְדָא נָמֵי. אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מְנַפֵּחַ בְּיָדוֹ אַחַת, וּבְכָל כֹּחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **They laughed at this** explanation **in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, saying: **Since he alters** his behavior from the usual method, doing so **even with** his **entire hand** should be permitted **as well. Rather,** the *halakha* is as **Rav Elazar said: One may blow** while holding the grain **with one hand** but not two, **and** he may even do so **with all his strength,** as this is not considered similar to a prohibited labor at all.

Having removed the husk, one must now separate the loosened chaff from the kernel. This is a form of *borer*. Rav Adda bar Abba said in the name of Rav: *minapeiach mikishrei etzba'otav u-l'ma'ala* — one blows or waves the mixture while holding it at the tips of the fingers, not cupped in the palms. In Eretz Yisrael they laughed at this restriction: *keivan d'meshani* — once you are doing it differently in some respect, even waving with the whole hand is fine. The final ruling comes from Rav Abba bar Alazar: *minapeiach b'yado achat u-v'chol kocho* — one holds it in one hand and fans with full force, as opposed to placing it in a sifter, which would constitute the kind of specialized vessel associated with large-scale, pre-kitchen processing.

This establishes the principle that will animate the discussions to follow: certain food-preparation acts on *Yom Tov* require some degree of *shinuy* because of the concern of *uvda d'chol*, and Rashi explicitly attributes this concern to the appearance of large-scale production. What exactly falls into this category — and why — will continue to be worked out as the *Gemara* progresses.

## Grinding Spices

### Beit Shammai Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: תַּבְלִין נִדּוֹכִין בְּמָדוֹךְ שֶׁל עֵץ, וְהַמֶּלַח בְּפַךְ וּבְעֵץ הַפָּרוּר. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: תַּבְלִין נִדּוֹכִין כְּדַרְכָּן בְּמָדוֹךְ שֶׁל אֶבֶן, וְהַמֶּלַח בְּמָדוֹךְ שֶׁל עֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Beit Shammai say: Spices may be pounded** on a Festival in a slightly unusual manner, **with a wooden pestle, and salt** may be pounded only **with** an earthenware **flask or with a wooden pot ladle,** in a manner very different from that of a weekday. **And Beit Hillel say: Spices may be pounded in their** usual **manner,** even **with a stone pestle, and** as for **salt,** although it must be pounded in an irregular manner, a slight modification such as pounding it **with a wooden pestle** is enough to render the act permitted.

The *Mishnah* transitions directly into the question of grinding spices on *Yom Tov*, a natural continuation of the discussion about *shinuy* and *uvda d'chol*. Beit Shammai rules: *tavlin* — ordinary spices — must be ground with a wooden pestle rather than the normal stone one, a modest *shinuy*. Salt requires an even greater departure: it must be ground either in a pottery vessel or with the back of a wooden spoon — *etz haparur* — a substantially more modified technique.

### Beit Hillel Position

Beit Hillel disagrees with the first ruling. *Tavlin nidochin kedarkan b'madoch shel even* — ordinary spices may be ground in the normal stone mortar and pestle without any *shinuy* at all. Since these are *tzorech ochel nefesh* — freshly ground spices needed for food preparation on *Yom Tov* — no modification is required. Salt, however, even Beit Hillel agrees requires some departure: *b'madoch shel etz* — a wooden rather than stone mortar. So while Beit Hillel reduces Beit Shammai's two-level stringency for salt to one level, the basic structure — that salt requires a *shinuy* while other spices do not — is preserved.

### Salt vs. Spices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהַת מֶלַח בָּעֲיָא שִׁנּוּי. מַאי טַעְמָא? רַב הוּנָא וְרַב חִסְדָּא, חַד אָמַר: כׇּל הַקְּדֵרוֹת כּוּלָּן צְרִיכוֹת מֶלַח, וְאֵין כׇּל הַקְּדֵרוֹת צְרִיכוֹת תַּבְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **In any event, everyone,** both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, **agrees** that the pounding of **salt requires a change;** it may not be performed in the regular weekday manner. **What is the reason** for this? **Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda** disputed this issue. **One** of them **said:** Everyone knows that **all dishes require salt,** and therefore one should prepare salt the day before the Festival. Since he failed to do so, this task may be performed on the Festival only in an unusual manner. **But not all dishes require spices,** and therefore it is possible that on the day prior to the Festival, one was not aware that he would require spices on the Festival.

The *Gemara* asks: *d'kulei alma miha melach ba'ya shinuy* — everyone agrees salt requires *some* form of *shinuy*. But why? It is *Yom Tov*, it is *melachat ochel nefesh* — food preparation — so what is the basis for any special restriction? Rav Huna and Rav Chisda debate this question, each offering a different rationale.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַד אָמַר: כׇּל הַתַּבְלִין מְפִיגִין טַעְמָן, וּמֶלַח אֵינָהּ מְפִיגָה טַעְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the other **one said** a different reason: **All spices lose their flavor** and cannot be prepared ahead of time, **and salt does not lose its flavor,** which means one could have prepared it the day before. Since he neglected to do so, he may prepare salt on the Festival only in an unusual manner.

One of them says: *kol hakedeirot kulan tzrichin melach v'ein kol hakedeirot tzrichin tavlin* — all dishes require salt, while not all dishes require other spices. Salt is universally needed in the kitchen, and therefore one should have anticipated this need before *Yom Tov*. Having failed to prepare it in advance, one is penalized with the requirement of a *shinuy* on *Yom Tov* itself. Other spices, by contrast, are not so universally anticipated, and one cannot be faulted for not grinding them ahead of time.

The other opinion holds: *kol hatavlin mifihei ta'ama* — all other spices, if ground in advance, lose some of their pungency and sharpness. Freshly ground pepper or garlic has a quality that diminishes over time. Salt, however, *eina mifiha ta'ama* — does not lose its character if ground beforehand. There is therefore no justification for waiting until *Yom Tov* to grind it, and having done so, one must perform it with a *shinuy*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּיָדַע מַאי קְדֵרָה בָּעֵי לְבַשּׁוֹלֵי, אִי נָמֵי בְּמוֹרִיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical **difference between** these two reasons? The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them is** in a case **where one knew** beforehand **which** type of **dish he wants to cook** on the Festival. Since he knew which spices he would require, he could have prepared them the day before, in which case spices are no different from salt, and one should be required to prepare them in an unusual manner. However, if the reason is that spices lose their flavor, the fact that he knew which dishes he planned to prepare is of no relevance. **Alternatively,** there is a practical difference in the case **of saffron,** whose flavor does not dissipate over the course of a single day. Consequently, one who knows what dish he will prepare on the Festival could have prepared the saffron the day before.

The *Gemara* proceeds to identify the practical differences between these two explanations. If the reasoning is one of anticipation — you should have known you would need it — then any spice for a meal one had already planned in advance would likewise require a *shinuy*: *d'yada mai kdeira ba'ei l'mishlei* — if you knew in advance what dish you intended to cook, you had ample opportunity to prepare its spices beforehand. Under the pungency rationale, however, this would be irrelevant; the justification for grinding on *Yom Tov* is precisely the freshness advantage, regardless of whether the meal was anticipated.

A second difference involves saffron — *demorika*. Rashi notes that saffron apparently does not lose its pungency when ground in advance. Under the pungency rationale, one should therefore have ground saffron ahead of time and, having failed to do so, would require a *shinuy* on *Yom Tov*. Under the anticipation rationale, the question would simply be whether one knew one would need it. These differences matter significantly for practical *halacha*, because which rationale we adopt will determine which spices require a *shinuy* and which can always be ground normally.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הַנִּדּוֹכִין נִדּוֹכִין כְּדַרְכָּן, וַאֲפִילּוּ מֶלַח. וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ מֶלַח בָּעֲיָא שִׁנּוּי! הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל הַנִּדּוֹכִין שֶׁנִּדּוֹכִין כְּדַרְכָּן וּמֶלַח עִמָּהֶן,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: All** foods **that must be pounded** before being eaten may be **pounded in their** usual **manner, and** this applies **even** to **salt.** The Gemara challenges this: **But didn't you say** that everyone agrees that **salt requires a change** in its manner of preparation? The Gemara answers: He **said** this *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir said: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree in** the case of foods **that are** regularly **pounded.** They both maintain **that they may be pounded in their** usual **manner, and salt** can be pounded together **with them.**

Before the *Gemara* settles this question, Rav Yehuda cites a sweeping ruling in the name of Shmuel: *kol hanidochin nidochin kedarkon va'afilu melach* — all spices may be ground in the normal manner on *Yom Tov*, and this includes even salt. The *Gemara* challenges this immediately: but the *Mishnah* itself states that salt requires a *shinuy*! Shmuel, the *Gemara* explains, holds in accordance with a particular *tanna*: Rabbi Meir, in a *braita*, states that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not dispute the principle that all spices — salt included — may be ground normally when they are ground together as part of a single session.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא לְדוּכָהּ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מֶלַח בְּפַךְ וּבְעֵץ הַפָּרוּר לְצָלִי, אֲבָל לֹא לִקְדֵרָה. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: בְּכׇל דָּבָר. בְּכׇל דָּבָר סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: לְכׇל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagreed only** about whether it is permitted **to pound** salt **by itself, as Beit Shammai say: Salt may be** pounded **with a flask and with a wooden pot ladle for roasting,** i.e., in small quantities, as one does not require much salt for roasting meat, **but** it may **not** be pounded in the large quantities required to salt meat **for** a cooked **pot. And Beit Hillel say:** It may be pounded **by anything.** The Gemara expresses surprise at this last statement: **By anything? Can this enter your mind?** How can Beit Hillel say that one may pound salt in any manner, when it has been established that everyone agrees that this may be performed only in an unusual manner? **Rather, say:** Salt may be pounded **for anything,** whether it is a small quantity for roasting or a large quantity for salting meat.

The phrase *u'melach imahen* — "and salt with them" — does not necessarily mean grinding them simultaneously in the same vessel; it means that when one sits down to grind the spices needed for the meal and salt is among them, the salt is carried along with the general permission and requires no special modification.

The dispute, Rabbi Meir clarifies, concerns only the case where one sits down exclusively to grind salt — *al hadocha bifnei atzma*. In that specific case, Beit Shammai requires a pottery vessel and the back of a spoon, and limits the quantity to that needed for roasting (*litzli*) rather than for salting meat in a pot (*l'kdeira*). Beit Hillel, in the *braita*, extends the permission broadly — *bechol davar* — for any purpose and in any amount. The *Gemara* notes that the literal reading, "with any thing," would be puzzling — could one use a *muktzah* object? — and therefore emends it to *l'chol davar*, meaning for any culinary purpose.

The upshot of Shmuel's position is therefore a significant simplification: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel debate only the standalone grinding of salt, and Beit Hillel permits even that without restriction. The multi-tiered distinctions that emerged from the earlier explanations of Rav Huna and Rav Chisda — spices that one anticipated needing, spices that do not lose pungency, and so forth — would under Shmuel's view be irrelevant, since the *Mishnah*'s basic rule is simply that normal spices need no *shinuy* and even salt is at most a minor issue.

### Practical Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּרְדְּלָא לִבְרֵיהּ: כִּי דָיְיכַת אַצְלִי אַצְלוֹיֵי וְדוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa Bardela said to his son: When you pound** salt, **tilt it** a little **to the side and** then **pound,** so that it will at least be performed in a slightly different manner on a Festival.

Rav Acha Bardala gave his son a practical instruction: *ki daichat atzli atzluyei* — when you grind salt on *Yom Tov*, lean the vessel at an angle. This minimal modification — tilting the mortar rather than using it perfectly upright — constitutes a sufficient *shinuy*. There is no need to switch vessels or use an entirely different implement; the slight change in orientation satisfies the concern.

### Rav Sheshet Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת שְׁמַע קָל בּוּכְנָא, אֲמַר: הַאי לָאו מִגַּוֵּויהּ דְּבֵיתַאי הוּא. וְדִלְמָא אַצְלוֹיֵי אַצְלִי? דְּשַׁמְעֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה צְלִיל קָלֵיהּ. וְדִלְמָא תַּבְלִין הֲווֹ? תַּבְלִין נַבּוֹחֵי מְנַבַּח קָלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara similarly relates: **Rav Sheshet heard the sound of a pestle** pounding salt on a Festival. **He said** to himself: **This** sound **is not** coming **from inside my house,** as I have instructed the members of my household not to do so. The Gemara asks: **But perhaps they tilted it** and pounded in a permitted manner? The Gemara answers: This could not have been the case, **as** Rav Sheshet **heard that it was a clear sound,** unlike the one produced when a pestle is tilted. The Gemara asks: **But perhaps they were** pounding **spices,** which may be pounded in the regular fashion on a Festival? The Gemara answers: **The sound** produced by pounding **spices** is distinctive, **like a bark,** which he would have recognized.

The *Gemara* relates that on *Yom Tov*, Rav Sheshet heard the sound of a mortar and pestle and declared: *hai lav migabai d'veitai hu* — that sound is certainly not coming from my house. He had established in his household that no grinding is permitted on *Yom Tov*. The *Gemara* challenges: perhaps it was coming from his house, and they were grinding salt at an angle as Rav Acha Bardala had instructed, which is permitted? No — *deshomei dehavei tzalil kalei* — the sound was too pure, too clear, the resonance of a pestle striking straight down, not angled. Perhaps it was other spices, which require no restriction at all? No — *tavlin ivuchei minavach kalei* — spices produce a different quality of sound when pounded, something more like a popping or cracking, whereas salt produces a crushing sound. Rav Sheshet, from the acoustics alone, could identify that it was salt being ground in the ordinary, perpendicular manner, and he knew his household would never do so.

The narrative illustrates the direction the *Gemara* has been moving: following the thrust of Shmuel's position, normal spices present no issue whatsoever, consistent with the *Mishnah*. For salt, the maximum requirement is the modest *shinuy* of tilting the vessel — otherwise even the choice of vessel can remain unchanged.

### Tosafot Rules

Tosafot addresses the practical question of how we rule. The two explanations of Rav Huna and Rav Chisda — anticipation versus pungency — each generate their own leniencies and stringencies. Under the first rationale (*lishna kama*), spices one could not have anticipated require no *shinuy*, while those one could have anticipated do. Under the second rationale (*lishna batra*), spices that lose their pungency may be ground normally, while those that do not — because there would have been no downside to grinding them beforehand — require a *shinuy*.

The Ri rules stringently on both counts: *she'yesh le'esor bechol inyan* — one must be strict in every case. One might think that since these are rabbinic matters, one should follow the lenient position. But the Ri argues that since each opinion has its own leniencies and stringencies, there is no single lenient position to follow. Adopting leniency across the board would mean rejecting both opinions entirely. Since one cannot simply discard all the *poskim*, one must be *machmir* in both directions. He invokes a parallel from the laws of the *Seder*, where the *Gemara* is uncertain whether to lean during the first or last two cups of wine, and concludes that since leniency would eliminate leaning altogether, one must lean for all four — the *Ra'ah* makes this explicit.

Tosafot then partially retreats. For garlic — *shum* — he permits grinding on *Yom Tov* without a *shinuy*, because garlic loses its pungency dramatically and would certainly fall under the lenient category even according to the stringent position.

But then Tosafot draws a further conclusion that governs the practical *halacha*: *de'ha kaima lan ke'Shmuel* — in the end, we rule like Shmuel, that all spices may be ground normally — *kol hanidochin nidochin kedarkon* — and the only possible residual restriction is for salt alone, which requires a minor *shinuy* of tilting the vessel. The Rif rules this way, as does Rav Yehuda Hai Gaon, and Tosafot confirms that this is the established practice: *vechen yesh minhag halacha l'ma'aseh d'hach milta taluy b'ashlei ravrevei* — this is the custom in practice and it rests on major authorities.

### Halacha L'Maaseh

At this point one might conclude that freshly ground pepper on *Yom Tov* is entirely unproblematic. Tosafot introduces a caveat: *assur l'itchon pilpilin b'reichayim shelahem* — it is forbidden to grind pepper in *their* grinder, the kind referred to as a *molinta* — because this constitutes *uvda d'chol*. However, *aval b'madoch shel even mutar l'duchan b'lo shinuy* — grinding in a normal stone mortar and pestle is fully permitted without any *shinuy*. The *melacha* itself — *tochen* — is not the concern, since it is permitted on *Yom Tov* for food preparation. The concern is the specific type of vessel.

This distinction is important and requires clarification. One contemporary approach, reflected throughout *Shemiras Shabbos Kehilchasa*, effectively translates *uvda d'chol* as "modern labor-saving appliances," so that virtually any kitchen device is categorically prohibited. Rav Moshe completely rejects this definition. His position is that what makes something *uvda d'chol* is not that it saves labor, but that it evokes the kind of processing that takes place not in the home kitchen but at a larger, pre-kitchen, industrial stage. The *systra* that Rashi cited earlier — the large sieve — is forbidden precisely because it is the type of vessel used in large-scale grain processing, not household cooking. A home mortar and pestle, by contrast, is the domestic instrument for preparing spices in the kitchen, and its use is entirely consonant with the spirit of *Yom Tov* food preparation.

Notably, using a mortar and pestle requires considerably more effort than a hand grinder. The concern with *uvda d'chol* is therefore not about how much work is involved. If anything, it runs in the opposite direction from a "labor-saving devices" definition. The question is whether the vessel belongs to the world of domestic food preparation or to the world of commercial, large-scale processing. A small home pepper grinder — the handheld kind one finds on dining tables — would therefore seem to be fully permitted, as it is the household analog to the mortar and pestle, not an industrial device. The practical conclusion is that grinding spices on *Yom Tov* is permitted, and one may use a home grinder. The sole residual question is salt, for which a slight *shinuy* — tilting the vessel — is the standard practice.

## Wheat Dishes

### Tisni Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין עוֹשִׂין טִיסְנִי, וְאֵין כּוֹתְשִׁין בְּמַכְתֶּשֶׁת. תַּרְתֵּי?! הָכִי קָאָמַר: מַה טַּעַם אֵין עוֹשִׂין טִיסְנִי — לְפִי שֶׁאֵין כּוֹתְשִׁין בְּמַכְתֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: On a Festival **one may not prepare groats [*tisanei*],** a dish comprised of grains of wheat crushed into quarters, which involves great effort, **nor may one grind** grain **with a mortar and pestle.** The Gemara expresses puzzlement: These are **two** contradictory rulings. The *tanna* first stated that groats alone may not be prepared, as this involves hard work, implying that other items may be ground. He subsequently states that one may not grind with a mortar and pestle at all. The Gemara answers: **This is what he said:** The *baraita* should be amended to read as follows: **What is the reason** that **one may not prepare groats? Because one may not grind with a mortar and pestle.**

The *Gemara* cites a *Tanu Rabbanan*: *ein osin Tisni b'Yom Tov* — one may not prepare *Tisni* on *Yom Tov*. *Tisni* is a grain-based dish made from wheat kernels that are split into quarters — something resembling grits — and its preparation involves considerable effort. Even though *ochel nefesh* preparation is permitted on *Yom Tov*, the *Chachamim* sought to limit excessive *tircha*, and *Tisni*, with its laborious process, crosses that line.

### Grinding Wheat

The *Gemara* continues: *ve'ein kotzin b'machteshes* — and one may not use a grinder for the wheat. This might seem to imply a categorical prohibition on grinding wheat at all on *Yom Tov* — not merely a restriction on the specific dish. One might wonder: grinding spices involves *tochen* and is permitted on *Yom Tov*; why should grinding wheat be treated differently? The *Gemara* observes that the *braita* seems internally inconsistent, prohibiting both the dish and the grinding, as though they are independent concerns. The *Gemara* resolves: *hachi ka'amar* — the two clauses are not parallel prohibitions but rather explanatory. The reason one cannot make *Tisni* is because fundamentally one cannot grind with this *machteshes* on *Yom Tov* — the dish is unavailable because its prerequisite is unavailable.

Why cannot one use a wheat grinder? Presumably, as with the earlier discussions, large-scale wheat grinding evokes *uvda d'chol*. Wheat flour production is not home-based in the immediate kitchen sense; it belongs to the world of mills, not kitchens.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא: אֵין כּוֹתְשִׁין בְּמַכְתֶּשֶׁת! אִי תָּנֵי אֵין כּוֹתְשִׁין בְּמַכְתֶּשֶׁת, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּמַכְתֶּשֶׁת גְּדוֹלָה, אֲבָל בְּמַכְתֶּשֶׁת קְטַנָּה — אֵימָא שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And let** the *tanna* simply **say: One may not grind with a mortar and pestle,** from which it can be inferred that groats may not be prepared. The Gemara explains that **if he teaches** only: **One may not grind with a mortar and pestle, I would have said** that **this applies only to a large mortar and pestle,** whose use has the appearance of a weekday activity; **but with a small mortar and pestle,** one might **say it seems well,** and one may prepare even groats with this mortar and pestle. The *tanna* therefore **teaches us** that groats may not be prepared in any manner.

The *Gemara* then presses: if the rule is simply *ein kotzin b'machteshes*, why mention the specific dish at all? Because without the concrete example of *Tisni*, one might think the prohibition applies only to a large grinder — the *machteshes gedola* — while a small, home-based grinder would be permitted. By coupling the prohibition with the specific dish, the *braita* signals that even a small wheat grinder is covered, leaving no opening to permit *Tisni* production even with modest equipment.

### Small Grinders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין כּוֹתְשִׁין בְּמַכְתֶּשֶׁת גְּדוֹלָה, אֲבָל כּוֹתְשִׁין בְּמַכְתֶּשֶׁת קְטַנָּה! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כִּי תַּנְיָא נָמֵי מַתְנִיתָא — מַכְתֶּשֶׁת גְּדוֹלָה תַּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: **But isn't it taught** explicitly in a *baraita* that **one may not grind with a large mortar and pestle, but one may grind with a small mortar and pestle? Abaye said: Also, when** the first ***baraita* was taught, it was taught** with regard to **a large mortar and pestle,** not a small one. In other words, the *baraita* is stating two *halakhot*, not one, as claimed previously. The *tanna* first rules that one may not prepare groats even with a small mortar and pestle, and he subsequently states that one may not use a large mortar and pestle for any purpose.

A *braita*, however, contradicts this: *ein kotzin b'machteshes gedola* — one may not use a large grinder — *aval kotzin b'machteshes ktana* — but one may use a small wheat grinder. Rashi explains the small grinder's leniency: *de'havei ke'lochar yad de'eina ela l'tavlin* — a small grinder is ordinarily used for spices, so using it for wheat constitutes a *shinuy* of sorts. In any case, the large grinder is the *uvda d'chol* concern; the small one does not carry that association.

Abaye resolves the contradiction: *ki tanya matnisin machteshes gedola tanya* — when the *braita* prohibited *Tisni* and all grinding, it was speaking of a large grinder. The reconciled position is: do not make *Tisni* at all — even with a small grinder, because of the *tircha* involved — and do not use a large grinder for anything. But a small wheat grinder for other purposes is permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא לַן, וְהָא לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This is **not difficult;** the *baraitot* do not contradict one another. **This** ruling, which permits a small mortar and pestle, **is for us,** the residents of Babylonia, who do not have servants, **and that** statement **is for them,** the inhabitants of Eretz Yisrael, who have many servants. Since servants might treat the prohibition lightly, by using a large mortar and pestle and claiming they had used only a small one, they are prohibited from pounding in all cases.

Rava offers an alternative resolution: *lo kashya ha lan v'ha lehu* — this is not a contradiction but a geographic difference between Bavel and Eretz Yisrael. In Eretz Yisrael, where household servants were less scrupulous about *halacha*, if one permitted small grinders, people would claim to have used the small one while actually using the large one. Therefore the stringent position that prohibits even small grinders was established there. In Bavel, where such concerns were less acute, the small grinder is permitted.

### Oatmeal Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פַּפֵּי אִקְּלַע לְבֵי מָר שְׁמוּאֵל. אַיְיתִי לֵיהּ דַּיְיסָא וְלָא אֲכַל. וְדִלְמָא בְּמַכְתֶּשֶׁת קְטַנָּה עַבְדוּהּ? דְּחַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה דְּיִיק טְפֵי. וְדִלְמָא מֵאֶתְמוֹל עַבְדוּהּ? דְּחַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה קְלִיף צַהֲרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Pappi happened to come** on a Festival **to the house** of **Mar Shmuel. He brought to him porridge,** made from wheat groats, **but he did not eat** it, out of concern that the wheat might have been pounded on the same day. The Gemara asks: **And perhaps they performed** the pounding **with a small mortar and pestle,** in a permitted manner? The Gemara answers: This cannot be the case, **as he observed that it was well ground,** which cannot be achieved by means of a small mortar and pestle. The Gemara asks: **And perhaps they prepared it the day before,** on the Festival eve? The Gemara answers: This cannot be the case, **as he observed that the husks** of the groats **were bright,** an indication that they had been prepared recently.

A narrative illustrates the application of these rules. Rav Papi came to the house of Mar Shmuel on *Yom Tov*, and they served him freshly ground oatmeal. He refused to eat it. The *Gemara* probes: perhaps it was prepared with a small grinder, which is permitted even by the stricter view in Bavel? No — *de-chaza de-hava dayek tfei* — Rav Papi observed that the grain had been ground to a fineness achievable only with a large grinder. Perhaps it was prepared the day before? No — *de-chaza de-hava klif tzaharei* — the flakes were visibly bright and fresh, unmistakably ground that very day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: שָׁאנֵי בֵּי מָר שְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאִיכָּא פְּרִיצוּתָא דְעַבְדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that he refrained from eating for another reason, because **the house** of **Mar Shmuel is different, as** he had slaves, and **slaves** are **lax** with regard to prohibitions. Therefore, Mar Shmuel ought to have prohibited all forms of grinding in his house, to ensure that no one grind anything in an inappropriate manner.

An alternative explanation is also offered: *shani bei Mar Shmuel de-ika pritzuta de-avdei* — the house of Mar Shmuel is exceptional because the servants there were known to be not scrupulous, making it analogous to the situation in Eretz Yisrael. In such a household, even a small grinder should not be permitted, because one cannot trust that the line between small and large will be observed. But the basic *halacha* for ordinary households in Bavel remains: a small grinder is permitted, a large one is not — and the underlying concern with the large grinder is, once again, *uvda d'chol*.

The conceptual organizing principle for this entire series of discussions deserves emphasis. The *Gemara* elsewhere, in a *Yerushalmi* cited by Tosafot, raises the principle of *lisha va'eilach* — that only *melachot* from kneading onwards are permitted on *Yom Tov*, meaning that threshing, grinding, sifting and winnowing are excluded. Yet the *Gemara* here clearly permits these activities in various forms. The most coherent resolution is not a categorical list of *melachot* but a distinction based on *locus*: acts that belong to the domestic, immediate food-preparation context of the home kitchen are permitted; those that evoke the pre-kitchen, field-or-mill stage of processing are not. This explains both the permission for spice grinding and the restrictions on wheat grinding, and it likewise explains the *uvda d'chol* concern — the prohibited vessel is prohibited precisely because it evokes that pre-domestic, large-scale world.

## Borer Rules

### Shammai Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַבּוֹרֵר קִטְנִיּוֹת בְּיוֹם טוֹב, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: בּוֹרֵר אוֹכֶל וְאוֹכֵל. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: בּוֹרֵר כְּדַרְכּוֹ בְּחֵיקוֹ בַּקָּנוֹן וּבַתַּמְחוּי, אֲבָל לֹא בַּטַּבְלָא וְלֹא בַּנָּפָה וְלֹא בַּכְּבָרָה. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף מֵדִיחַ וְשׁוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who selects legumes on a Festival** by separating the edible and inedible portions, **Beit Shammai say: He may select food and eat** it immediately, while leaving the refuse. **And Beit Hillel say: He may select in his** usual **manner, in his lap, with a tray, or with a large vessel, but he may not** do so **with a tablet, nor with a winnow, nor with a sieve,** as these vessels are specially designed for selecting, which gives his action the appearance of a weekday activity. **Rabban Gamliel says: One may even wash** the legumes in water **and skim off** the refuse floating on top.

The *Mishnah* turns to *borer* — sorting and selection — completing the sequence of *melachot* associated with grain: *dasha*, *tochen*, and now *borer*. The question is what *borer* looks like on *Yom Tov*, where food preparation is in principle permitted. Beit Shammai rules: *borer ochel ve-ochel* — one sorts food and eats it. The formulation mirrors the rules of *borer* on *Shabbat*: one performs *ochel mitoch psolet*, focusing on the food itself, and consumes it immediately. For Beit Shammai, it is unclear that *Yom Tov* confers any additional latitude beyond what *Shabbat* allows in this domain; the model remains *derech achila*, taking the food directly.

### Hillel Lenient

Beit Hillel is more permissive: *borer kederko* — one may sort in the normal manner. This apparently includes removing the *psolet* rather than the food — the inverse of the *Shabbat* rule. In terms of technique, one may do it *b'cheiko* — in one's lap — *b'kanon uvetamcho* — using a vessel or funnel. What remains prohibited, however, is *tavla*, *nafah*, and *kivara* — the large sieve, strainer, and colander. As has been the pattern throughout this *daf*, the prohibited implements are precisely those associated with large-scale, professionalized processing rather than home-based food preparation. Beit Hillel's ruling thus reflects the same underlying framework: *borer* itself presents no fundamental *halachic* problem on *Yom Tov*; the concerns are *tircha* and the use of *uvda d'chol* vessels.

### Minority Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — כְּשֶׁהָאוֹכֶל מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפְּסוֹלֶת, אֲבָל פְּסוֹלֶת מְרוּבָּה עַל הָאוֹכֶל — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל נוֹטֵל אֶת הָאוֹכֵל וּמַנִּיחַ אֶת הַפְּסוֹלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabban Gamliel said: In what** case **is this statement said?** Under what circumstances did Beit Hillel permit the removal of the refuse in the weekday manner of selecting? In a case **where the** quantity of the **food is greater than the** quantity of the **refuse. However, if** the quantity of **refuse is greater than the** quantity of **food, everyone agrees** that **one must remove the food and leave the refuse.**

A *braita* cites Rabban Gamliel's clarification: *bamei devarim amurim* — when does Beit Hillel's rule permitting removal of the *psolet* apply? When *ochel merubeh al hapsolet* — the food outnumbers the waste. In that case, removing the *psolet* — the minority — is the efficient method, and that is what Beit Hillel permits. But when *psolet merubeh al ha-ochel* — the waste exceeds the food — *divrei hakol notel et ha-ochel* — all agree that one removes the food.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּסוֹלֶת מְרוּבָּה עַל הָאוֹכֶל מִי אִיכָּא מַאן דְּשָׁרֵי? לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּנְפִישׁ בְּטִרְחָא וְזוּטַר בְּשִׁיעוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If the quantity of **refuse is greater than the** quantity of **food, is there an opinion that permits** it? Since the smaller amount of food is nullified by the refuse, the entire mixture is considered *muktze* and may not be moved. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* in a case **where** the removal of the refuse **is greater in** terms of **effort, and** yet it is **smaller in size.** In other words, there is actually more food than refuse, but since greater effort is required to remove the refuse, it is preferable to remove the food.

One might think this is because the mixture, being predominantly waste, is *muktzah* as a unit. But the *Gemara* rejects this: *lo tzricha* — the ruling is needed for a different case entirely.

The case is: *de-nafis betircha vezutar beshiva* — the *psolet* is numerically a minority, but the effort required to remove it is greater than the effort to remove the food. In this situation, one removes whichever is less effort, regardless of which is smaller in quantity. This is the key principle: *denafish betircha* — on *Yom Tov*, *borer* is fundamentally permitted, and the governing concern is not the definition of *borer* as it would be on *Shabbat*, but *tircha* — excessive effort. One must always choose the method that minimizes labor. This also means that on *Yom Tov*, cutting vegetables and dealing with seeds and scraps in the kitchen does not trigger the *borer* concerns that operate on *Shabbat*; what matters is simply doing it in a home-based, effort-minimizing manner without professionalized sifting equipment.

The distinction between *Yom Tov* and *Shabbat* in this context is fundamental. On *Shabbat*, the question of *borer* is entirely about halachic definition — whether the act constitutes *ochel mitoch psolet* and *derech achila*. On *Yom Tov*, since the *melacha* is permitted for food purposes, the question is purely one of *tircha* and *uvda d'chol* vessels. These are the two recurrent concerns throughout the *daf*.

### Water Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף מֵדִיחַ וְשׁוֹלֶה. תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק: כָּךְ הָיָה מִנְהָגָן שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, שֶׁהָיוּ מְבִיאִין דְּלִי מָלֵא עֲדָשִׁים וּמְצִיפִין עָלָיו מַיִם, וְנִמְצָא אוֹכֶל לְמַטָּה וּפְסוֹלֶת לְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that **Rabban Gamliel says: One may even wash** the legumes in water **and skim off** the refuse floating on top. **It is taught** (*Tosefta*, *Beitza* 1): **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, said: This was the custom of the house of Rabban Gamliel, that they would bring a bucket full with lentils and pour water in it, and then** the **food** would sink **down and the refuse** would float **up,** which spared them from having to separate the lentils from the refuse manually.

Rabban Gamliel adds his own method: *af madiach veshola* — one may even submerge the legumes in water and scoop from the surface, exploiting the difference in buoyancy between the food and the chaff. A *braita* relates that this was the actual practice in the house of Rabban Gamliel: they would bring buckets of lentils, pour water over them, and the chaff would separate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא אִיפְּכָא? לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּעַפְרָא, הָא בְּגִילֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't the opposite taught** in another *baraita*, that the food rose to the top while the refuse sunk below? The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which says the refuse sinks down, is referring **to** refuse in the form of **earth,** which sinks below the lentils; **that** *baraita*, where it is stated that the refuse rises, speaks **of straw,** which floats on water.

A second *braita* appears to contradict the first regarding which component rises and which sinks, but the *Gemara* resolves the apparent discrepancy: *ha b'afra ha b'gilei* — the two *baraitot* describe different types of impurity. Dirt and heavier debris sink; light chaff floats. The two statements simply describe different situations.

## Gift Sending

### Food Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מְשַׁלְּחִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב אֶלָּא מָנוֹת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מְשַׁלְּחִין בְּהֵמָה חַיָּה וָעוֹף, בֵּין חַיִּים בֵּין שְׁחוּטִין. מְשַׁלְּחִין יֵינוֹת שְׁמָנִים וּסְלָתוֹת וְקִטְנִיּוֹת, אֲבָל לֹא תְּבוּאָה. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר בִּתְבוּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Beit Shammai say: One may send only portions** of prepared food **on a Festival,** but not any other gifts. **And Beit Hillel say: One may** even **send** gifts of **domesticated animals, undomesticated animals, and fowl, whether alive or slaughtered.** Similarly, **one may send wines, oils, and** vessels of **flour, and** even **legumes, but not grain,** which is unfit for use, as one may not grind it on the Festival. **And Rabbi Shimon permits** sending gifts even in the case **of grain,** as it can be made into porridge without being ground.

With the discussion of food-preparation *melachot* concluded, the *Mishnah* shifts to the laws of sending gifts on *Yom Tov*. Beit Shammai rules: *ein meshallchin b'Yom Tov ela manos* — one may send on *Yom Tov* only portions that are ready to eat. Something that requires further preparation — even preparation technically permitted on *Yom Tov* — may not be sent. The apparent concern is that sending a recipient something raw or unready, which they had not planned to cook, is either a *tircha* or *muktzah* problem for the recipient, or more broadly, it fails to constitute a genuine *Yom Tov* gift of ready-to-enjoy food.

Beit Hillel takes a substantially broader view: *meshallchin beheima chaya v'of* — one may send livestock, whether alive or slaughtered. *Meshallchin yeinos shmanim u'soltos* — one may send wine, oil, and flour, the last of which still requires kneading and baking. *U'kitniyos* — legumes are also permitted. The principle is that anything which could in theory be used on *Yom Tov* — even if it requires effort — may be sent. *Aval lo tevuah* — but unground grain may not be sent, since under normal circumstances there is no practical way to use whole grain on *Yom Tov*.

### Livestock Gifts

Beit Hillel's inclusion of livestock is expansive: since one can perform *shechita* on *Yom Tov* for the purpose of eating, a live animal is in principle usable on *Yom Tov* and may therefore be sent.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין עוֹשִׂין טִיסְנִי, וְאֵין כּוֹתְשִׁין בְּמַכְתֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: On a Festival **one may not prepare groats [*tisanei*],** a dish comprised of grains of wheat crushed into quarters, which involves great effort, **nor may one grind** grain **with a mortar and pestle.**

Rabbi Shimon goes further and permits sending even unground grain: *Rabbi Shimon matir bitvuah*. Rashi explains this by reference to the discussion above — since one may use a small grinder on *Yom Tov* in certain circumstances, grain is at least theoretically usable, and Rabbi Shimon regards this theoretical usability as sufficient.

### Procession Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב יְחִיאֵל: וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲשֶׂנּוּ בְּשׁוּרָה. תָּנָא: אֵין שׁוּרָה פְּחוּתָה מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם. בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: תְּלָתָא גַּבְרֵי וּתְלָתָא מִינֵי מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yeḥiel teaches: And** one may send these items **provided that he does not do it by** means of **a convoy** of people, as they would look as though they were transporting merchandise to the marketplace. It is **taught** in a *baraita*: **A convoy** consists of **no less than three people;** if there are fewer than three, it is not called a convoy and is permitted. **Rav Ashi raised a dilemma:** In a case of **three people and three** different **types** of food, **what is** the *halakha*? Is this considered a convoy, or does the fact that there are three different types of food mean that each of them is considered a separate item? The Gemara answers: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved, as no answer was found.

Even Beit Hillel's broad permission is limited by Rav Chiya's teaching: *u'vilvad shelo ya'asenu b'shurah* — one may not arrange the gifts in a procession, a *shurah*. Sending a long line of bearers carrying successive gifts is forbidden. Rashi explains that this resembles going to sell goods in the marketplace — *kemolichin lemakor hashuk* — and is therefore inappropriate on *Yom Tov*. The concern likely reflects the Roman culture of gift-giving and patronage, in which a formal procession of gift-bearers was a recognizable commercial or social spectacle.

A *shurah* requires a minimum of three people, as the *Gemara* specifies. Rav Ashi raises a further question: what if three people are each sent with a different type of gift — does the diversity of items break up what would otherwise constitute a *shurah*, since each bearer is carrying something distinct? *Teiku* — this remains unresolved.

## Non-Food Gifts

### General Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מְשַׁלְּחִין כֵּלִים בֵּין תְּפוּרִין בֵּין שֶׁאֵינָן תְּפוּרִין, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן כִּלְאַיִם וְהֵן לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד. אֲבָל לֹא סַנְדָּל הַמְסוּמָּר וְלֹא מִנְעָל שֶׁאֵינוֹ תָּפוּר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף לֹא מִנְעָל לָבָן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁצָּרִיךְ אוּמָּן. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁנֵּאוֹתִין בּוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב — מְשַׁלְּחִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may send clothes, whether** they are **sewn or whether they are unsewn, and even if they contain diverse kinds,** a prohibited mixture of wool and linen [*sha'atnez*]. **But** one may do so only if **they** serve **the purposes of the Festival. However, one may not** send **a spiked sandal,** which has nails fixed to it, as the Sages decreed that one may not wear a sandal of this kind on a Shabbat or Festival, **nor an unsewn shoe,** which is not suitable for a Festival. **Rabbi Yehuda says: One may not even** send **a white shoe,** which people do not usually wear, **because** it **requires an artisan** to paint it black. **This is the** general **principle:** Anything **that one may use on a Festival, one may send it.**

The *Mishnah* extends the discussion to non-consumable items: *meshallchin keilim* — vessels and clothing may be sent on *Yom Tov*, whether sewn or unsewn — this being according to Beit Hillel's broader framework — provided they can be used on *Yom Tov*.

### Shatnez Garments

Remarkably, the *Mishnah* includes: *ve'af al pi she'yesh bahen kilayim* — even garments containing *shatnez* may be sent. This seems counterintuitive — what possible use could one have for *shatnez* on *Yom Tov*, given that wearing it is forbidden? The *Mishnah* states: *ve'hein letzorech hamoed* — they must be for use on *Yom Tov*. An alternate textual reading has *she'ein letzorech hamoed* — even if they are not for *Yom Tov* — meaning that even if the practical intention is for after the holiday, the mere theoretical possibility of *Yom Tov* use suffices. The challenge, of course, is identifying what that theoretical use could be.

### Excluded Items

The *Mishnah* lists items that may not be sent: *aval lo sandal hamesumar* — a nailed sandal, which the Rabbis forbade wearing on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* entirely due to a historical incident. *Velo min'al she'eino tafur* — an unsewn shoe, which has no use whatsoever until the leather is properly sewn together, and therefore lacks even hypothetical *Yom Tov* utility. Rabbi Yehuda adds: *af lo min'al lavan* — even a white shoe, meaning an untanned or unblackened leather shoe, which requires a craftsman to finish and which no one would wear publicly as-is. Since it has no practical or even theoretical use in its current state, it may not be sent.

The operative principle is stated explicitly: *zeh haklal kol she'nehenin bo beyom tov meshallchin oto* — the rule is that anything one could theoretically benefit from on *Yom Tov* may be sent, even if that is not the primary intention in sending it.

### Shatnez Cushion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא תְּפוּרִין — חֲזוּ לְמַלְבּוּשׁ, שֶׁאֵין תְּפוּרִין נָמֵי — חֲזוּ לְכַסּוֹיֵי, אֶלָּא כִּלְאַיִם לְמַאי חֲזוּ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks a question: **Granted,** it is permitted to send **sewn** clothes, as they are **suitable for wearing.** With regard to clothes **that are not sewn as well, they are** at least **suitable for covering** oneself. **But** in the case of clothes than contain **diverse kinds, for what** use **are they suited?** Since these clothes may not be used on the Festival, why is it permitted to send them?

The *Gemara* traces through the logic for each permitted category. Sewn garments may clearly be worn — *chazu lemalbush*. Unsewn garments may be used as blankets or coverings — *chazu lechisuyei*. But *shatnez* — what possible use is there?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵימָא חֲזוּ לְמֵימַךְ תּוּתֵיהּ, וְהָתַנְיָא: ״לֹא יַעֲלֶה עָלֶיךָ״, אֲבָל אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַצִּיעוֹ תַּחְתֶּיךָ. אֲבָל אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אָסוּר לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן, שֶׁמָּא תִּכָּרֵךְ לוֹ נִימָא עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that clothes of diverse kinds **are suited for one to place underneath himself, but isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **"Neither shall there come upon you** a garment of diverse kinds" (Leviticus 19:19)? This verse teaches that one may not wear this garment, **but** by Torah law **you may spread it beneath you. However, the Sages said: It is prohibited to do so, lest a thread [*nima*] wind itself around his flesh.** This shows that the Sages prohibited even spreading underneath oneself a cloth that contains a mixture of wool and linen.

Perhaps, the *Gemara* suggests, one could use it as a mat to sit upon — *chazu lemitmachtei* — without wearing it. But a *braita* is cited: *lo ya'aleh alecha* — the prohibition on *shatnez* extends beyond wearing; the *Rabbis* forbade even reclining on it, lest a strand come into contact with one's body — *shema tichroch nima al besaro*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּמַפְסֵיק מִידֵּי בֵּינֵי בֵּינֵי, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן שָׁאוּל, אָמַר רַבִּי מִשּׁוּם קְהָלָא קַדִּישָׁא דְּבִירוּשָׁלַיִם: אֲפִילּוּ עֶשֶׂר מַצָּעוֹת זוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי זוֹ וְכִלְאַיִם תַּחְתֵּיהֶן — אָסוּר לִישַׁן עֲלֵיהֶם (מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יַעֲלֶה עָלֶיךָ״).
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say that** the allowance of cloth of diverse kinds is referring to a case where **there is something separating between them,** i.e., one may place a garment of diverse kinds beneath another object, **but didn't Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi say** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said** that **Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul said** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said, in the name of the sacred congregation in Jerusalem: Even** in a case of **ten mattresses** placed **one on top of the other and** a cloth of **diverse kinds underneath them, it is prohibited to sleep on them, as it is stated: "Neither shall there come upon you"** (Leviticus 19:19).

What if one places a pillow between oneself and the *shatnez* mat, creating an interposition? Perhaps that should be sufficient to allay the concern about bodily contact.

### Jerusalem Stringency

Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi, citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, citing Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul, citing Rebbi in the name of the holy community of Jerusalem — *kehila kadisha de-Yerushalayim* — rules: *afilu eser matzot zo al gav zo vekilayim tachtehem assur lishon aleihem* — even ten mats stacked one atop the other with the *shatnez* at the very bottom, like the princess and the pea, it is forbidden to sleep upon them. Once the Rabbis prohibited reclining on *shatnez*, the prohibition was absolute, regardless of how many intervening layers separated the person from the forbidden garment.

This leaves the *Gemara* — and us — in suspense: if one cannot wear *shatnez*, cannot sit on it, and cannot use it even as the bottommost layer under ten cushions, what theoretical use could there possibly be for *shatnez* on *Yom Tov* that would permit sending it as a gift?
