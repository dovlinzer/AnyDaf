# Shabbat 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fire Prohibitions

### Roasting Restrictions

The *mishna* at the bottom of 19b transitions from a series of debates between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* — concerning processes begun before *Shabbat* that continue into *Shabbat*, whether carried out independently or involving a non-Jew — to a case on which all agree to be strict. Presumably even *Beit Hillel* concurs with this prohibition.

The *mishna* opens: *ein tzolin basar batzal u'veitza ela kedei she-yitzolu mibe'od yom* — one may not roast meat, an onion, or an egg on *erev Shabbat* unless there is sufficient time for the food to complete its roasting while it is still day. The prohibition, then, is against having the roasting continue into *Shabbat* itself.

### Bread & Crust

The *mishna* continues: *ein notnin pat le-tanur im chasheicha* — one may not place bread into an oven as *Shabbat* approaches, *velo charara al gabei gechalim* — nor a wafer or biscuit on top of coals — *ela kedei she-yikremu paneiha mibe'od yom* — unless there is sufficient time for the crust to harden from while it is still day. Once the crust forms, the bread is considered sufficiently cooked that no concern remains.

Rabbi Eliezer qualifies this: *kedei she-yikrem hatachton shela* — it suffices for the bottom crust alone to form. The *gemara* raises the question of which side constitutes "the bottom" in Rabbi Eliezer's view. Rashi understands that the *bavli* envisions bread being baked in the manner of *esh tanur* pita — slapped against the interior wall of the oven. The side pressed against the wall forms its crust last, while the side facing the fire crusts over first. Does Rabbi Eliezer intend the more lenient reading — the face toward the fire — or the more stringent one — the face against the wall?

A *baraita* resolves this: *Rabbi Eliezer omer kedei she-yakrimu paneiha ha-midubakim ba-tanur* — Rabbi Eliezer requires the side adhering to the oven wall to form its crust, meaning the later and more demanding stage. Only then are we not concerned that one might continue fussing with the fire once *Shabbat* has begun.

### Pesach Exception

The *mishna* introduces a notable exception: *meshalshelin et ha-pesach ba-tanur im chasheicha* — one may lower the *Korban Pesach* into the oven as *Shabbat* descends. This rule presupposes the scenario in which *Pesach* falls on *Shabbat*, as on a normal *erev Pesach* one is permitted to cook on *Yom Tov*. The manner of roasting is also a *chiddush* — one might have assumed the *Korban Pesach* was roasted over an open fire, but the *mishna* indicates it was lowered into an oven, with the fire burning inside and the spit holding the animal over it.

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason this is permitted? The answer: *mishum di-vnei chaburah zerizin hein* — because the members of the group are particularly careful. A recurring principle throughout the tractate is that communal groups maintain a heightened level of vigilance; at least one member will be alert enough to prevent any inadvertent *chillul Shabbat*.

A difficulty arises from an earlier teaching that a whole roasting goat — the oven lid being sealed to avoid damaging the meat — creates no concern about stirring the coals even for an individual. The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between the *Korban Pesach*, which is roasted whole and thus less susceptible to exposure, and the earlier case in which the animal had been cut into pieces, greatly increasing the surface area exposed to air. The sealed oven there had a different function, and the cases are accordingly not analogous.

### Fire Vigilance

The *mishna*'s sequence is noteworthy. The laws governing *erev Shabbat* in this *perek* began with the prohibition against reading by firelight — and the underlying concern throughout is unmistakably fire. Unlike most *gezerot* where the worry is that a person will deliberately violate *Shabbat*, the concern here is that one will become preoccupied and *forget* that *Shabbat* has arrived. Fire, in particular, demands constant attention: one fusses with it, adjusts it, and tends to it. It is easy to lose track of time in the process.

This is why even *Beit Hillel*, who generally permit beginning a process before *Shabbat* and allowing it to continue, agree that fire-related activities require extra caution — both that the fire be burning well and that the food be well along in its cooking before *Shabbat* begins.

It is worth noting that the *Mechilta* records a view that the *mishna* of *ein tzolin basar* reflects the position of *Beit Shammai* alone, consistent with their general stringency regarding processes begun before *Shabbat*, and that *Beit Hillel* would disagree. However, that is not how the *gemara* reads it, nor how *Rebbi* understood it. The universally accepted reading is that fire-related concerns are treated with heightened caution by all, arising from a well-established pattern — first visible in the prohibition on reading by candlelight — that with fire, the likelihood of distraction and inadvertent violation is simply too great.

### Fire-Starting Rules

Having addressed roasting and bread, the *mishna* turns to lighting a fire itself. *U-ma'achizin et ha-ur bi-mdurat Beit HaMoked* — one may begin kindling the fire in the *midurat Beit HaMoked*, the chamber within the *Beit HaMikdash* where the *kohanim*, who were required to go barefoot on the cold stone floors, would warm themselves. This fire may be started on *erev Shabbat* even without waiting for it to fully take hold. By contrast, at the top of 20a: *u-va-gevulin kedei she-tochoz ha-ur be-rubah* — outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, the fire must seize most of the wood pile before *Shabbat* arrives.

This reflects the reality of fire-making before modern conveniences. Building and maintaining a fire required sustained effort, and there was genuine concern that a person would continue tending to it and forget the arrival of *Shabbat*. Outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, once the fire has seized most of the wood, it is considered self-sustaining and no fiddling is expected.

Rabbi Yehuda adds: *be-fechamim kol she-hu* — for charcoal, any minimal amount of ignition is sufficient on *erev Shabbat*, since charcoal, once it catches, burns reliably and no further intervention is necessary.

## Ma'achal Ben Drusai

### Roasting Standard

The *gemara* asks: *ve-chama* — how well roasted must the meat be? Rabbi Elazar said in the name of Rav: *kedei she-yitzolu mibe'od yom ke-ma'achal Ben Drusai* — it must be cooked to the standard of *ma'achal Ben Drusai*. Ben Drusai was reportedly a bandit who, pressed for time, could not afford to cook his meat fully — hence his preference for very rare meat. Rashi identifies him accordingly. There is a debate in the *rishonim* whether *ma'achal Ben Drusai* denotes a third cooked or a half cooked, and it is not entirely clear whether these fractions refer to proportional cooking time or to observable degrees of doneness — what we might call rare versus medium-rare.

This principle is significant well beyond the present context, as it will become central to the discussion of the third *perek*, which addresses the permissibility of leaving food on a fire before *Shabbat*. The *Ashkenazic* ruling is that *ma'achal Ben Drusai* — whether a third or a half cooked — suffices. The *Sephardic* ruling requires the food to be fully cooked. Importantly, however, that dispute pertains to the case of cooking, not to roasting. This *mishna* treats roasting separately from the third *perek*, which deals exclusively with food left cooking on a stove.

Several differences between roasting and cooking are worth noting. Since roasting proceeds more quickly than cooking, all opinions agree that once meat has reached the *ma'achal Ben Drusai* threshold, one need not be concerned. Moreover, the *kula* that applies to raw food left on the stove before *Shabbat* — because raw food will not be ready until the next morning and one naturally forgets about it — does not apply to roasting. Even raw meat being roasted poses a problem, since roasting proceeds rapidly and one remains attentive to it. On the other hand, by the time roasted meat reaches the *ma'achal Ben Drusai* stage, it is so close to completion that no concern about fiddling remains, and no *blech* is required.

### Bishulei Nochrim

A related teaching is cited by analogy: Rav Assi said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, *kol she-hu ke-ma'achal Ben Drusai ein bo mishum bishulei nochrim* — food that has been cooked to the *ma'achal Ben Drusai* standard is not subject to the prohibition of *bishulei nochrim*. The prohibition against food cooked entirely by non-Jews applies to two categories: food that cannot be eaten raw, and food that is *ra'ui la'alot al shulchan melachim* — fit to be served at a royal table. One of the exceptions is where a Jew initiated the cooking. The *gemara* defines the threshold for that Jewish initiation: once the food has reached the *ma'achal Ben Drusai* stage — cooked to a third or half — it is considered to have been adequately begun by a Jew, and if a non-Jew completes the cooking, the food is permitted. The contemporary *Ashkenazic* leniency of a Jew merely lighting the flame is a later development; in the *gemara* itself the standard is *ma'achal Ben Drusai*. The analogy reinforces the principle: if that stage constitutes sufficient "cooking" for *bishulei nochrim*, it likewise constitutes sufficient readiness to permit leaving roasted meat on the fire into *Shabbat*.

### Chananya Cooking

A *baraita* is then cited: *Chananya omer, kol she-hu ke-ma'achal Ben Drusai mutar le-hashehoto al gabei kirah, v'af al pi she-eino grufah u-ktumah* — Chananya holds that any food cooked to the *ma'achal Ben Drusai* standard may be left on a *kirah* (a stove or oven), even without the stove being *grufah u-ktumah* — swept of its coals or covered with ashes.

This is a direct borrowing from the subject matter of the third *perek*. The concept of *shehiyah* — leaving food on the fire over *Shabbat* — and *grufah u-ktumah* — the swept-and-ashed condition that serves as the Talmudic antecedent of the *blech* — are the central concerns of that later chapter. Chananya's ruling bridges the two discussions: for him, whether one is roasting or cooking, *ma'achal Ben Drusai* is the operative threshold, and once reached it eliminates the need for a *blech*. As noted, others disagree regarding cooking: for cooked food left on the stove, some require it to be fully cooked, on pain of needing a *blech*. But for roasting, *ma'achal Ben Drusai* is universally accepted as sufficient.

### Roast vs. Cook

The structural separation of the present *mishna* — which deals with roasting — from the third *perek* — which deals with cooking — is itself significant. Roasting proceeds quickly; the concern that one will fiddle with it dissipates once a certain threshold of doneness is reached. Cooking is a slower and more uncertain process, and accordingly opinions differ as to how far along it must be before one is confident the person will not intervene. Chananya's position is that both roasting and cooking follow the same rule, while others maintain a stricter standard for cooking. The *gemara* makes this connection explicit here, drawing the two discussions together even before the third *perek* formally opens.

## Fire-Starting Rules

### Beit HaMoked

Returning to the *mishna*'s ruling that one may begin the fire in the *Beit HaMoked* on *erev Shabbat* even without it seizing most of the wood, the *gemara* asks: *minan milei* — what is the source that one is permitted to be more lenient in the *Beit HaMikdash*?

Rav Huna derives it from the *pasuk*: *lo teva'aru esh be-chol moshvoteichem be-yom ha-Shabbat* — do not kindle a fire in any of your dwelling places on *Shabbat*. The *derasha* is: the prohibition is on kindling fire *be-chol moshvoteichem*, in your dwelling places. But the *Beit HaMikdash* — the *Mishkan*, the sanctuary — is not a dwelling place in this sense; it is the place of the Divine Presence. Therefore, the prohibition does not apply there, and one may kindle fire in the *midurat Beit HaMoked*.

### Pasuk Derivation

The *pasuk* of *lo teva'aru esh* thus provides the conceptual basis for distinguishing between the *Beit HaMikdash* and *gevulin* — the territories outside it. Outside, the prohibition on kindling fire without adequate seizure applies; inside, it does not in the same way.

### Shabbat Fire Issue

Rav Chisda challenges this: *i hachi, afilu be-Shabbat nami* — if the *pasuk* truly exempts the *Beit HaMikdash* from the fire prohibition, one should be permitted to kindle a fire there even on *Shabbat* itself. Yet this cannot be right: while *melachah* is permitted in the *Beit HaMikdash* on *Shabbat*, it is only for the purpose of *korbanot* that are *be-mo'ado* — obligatory in their time. Optional *korbanot* may not be brought, and *melachot* that do not directly serve the *korban* are prohibited even for the *kohanim*. Warming the *kohanim* does not meet this standard. So how can the *pasuk* be invoked?

### Fats & Kohanim

Rav Chisda himself resolves this: *kra ki ata le-mishrei evarim u-fedarim hu de-ata* — the *pasuk* comes to permit the burning of fats and limbs on *Shabbat*. This refers either to the fats of *korbanot* slaughtered on the previous day — a substantial *chiddush* that their burning may continue into *Shabbat* — or to the fats of the *Tamid* and *Musaf* offerings, which need not technically be burned on *Shabbat* but which the *pasuk* permits. *Tosafot* notes some ambiguity about which scenario is intended, going back and forth on the question.

The fire in the *Beit HaMoked* for the warming of the *kohanim*, however, is not covered by this *pasuk*. Its leniency on *erev Shabbat* — that one need not wait for the fire to seize most of the wood — rests on two other foundations. First: *Kohanim zerizim hem* — *kohanim* are conscientious, as *benei chaburah zerizim hem* established earlier. Second: *ein shvut ba-Mikdash* — rabbinic restrictions are generally relaxed within the *Beit HaMikdash* to give the *kohanim* the latitude they need to perform the *avodah*. The upshot is that while we would not permit a *kohen* to kindle fire *le-chatchila* on *Shabbat* itself for their comfort, before *Shabbat* on *erev Shabbat*, we trust the *kohanim* not to tend to it, and therefore we need not require that the fire fully seize the wood.

### Outside Beit Mikdash

For the rest of Israel — *u-va-gevulin* — the standard is more demanding. *Kedei she-tochoz ha-ur be-rubah* — the fire must seize most of the wood pile before *Shabbat* begins.

## Fire Seizure Standards

### Rav's Position

The *gemara* asks: *mai rubah* — what does it mean for the fire to seize "most" of the wood? Rav answers: *rov kol echad ve-echad* — most of each individual stick must be aflame.

### Shmuel's Position

Shmuel offers a more functional and intuitive standard: *kedei she-lo yomru havu etzim ve-naniyach tachteihem* — it is sufficient when one would no longer say, "bring more twigs to lay beneath them." That is, once the fire is self-sustaining to the point that no one would think to add kindling, the threshold is met. Anyone who has built a bonfire will recognize this moment: there is a clear transition from the stage where one anxiously feeds the fire to the stage where it is simply going on its own.

### Menorah Analogy

A *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Chiyya is cited in support of Shmuel: *kedei she-tehei shalhevet olah me-eleha* — until the flame rises on its own. This is drawn from the procedure for lighting the *menorah* in the *Beit HaMikdash*: one would light it until the flame rose independently, without any need for further adjustment. The analogy is not about *Shabbat* concerns per se, but the principle is identical — once the flame sustains itself, the act of lighting is complete. As the *baraita* adds: *ve-lo she-tehei shalhevet olah al yedei davar acher* — it is not sufficient to leave the flame in a state where it still requires external assistance to rise.

### Single Stick Rule

For a single isolated stick — *etz yechidi* — the question of how much must catch fire is debated. Rav says *rov avyo* — most of its thickness. Others say *be-rov heikeifo* — most of its circumference. Rav Pappa rules: *hilchach, beinan rov avyo u-veinan rov heikeifo* — since the matter is unresolved, we require both: most of the thickness and most of the circumference.

### Tannaim Debate

This dispute mirrors a *tannaitic* disagreement. Rabbi Chiyya holds: *kedei she-yishacheit ha-etz mi-mlechet ha-uman* — the stick must be so consumed that it can no longer serve any craftsman's purpose. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira holds: *kedei she-tochoz ha-eish mi-shnei tzedadin* — the fire must take hold on both sides of the stick. Although, as the *gemara* notes, there is no definitive proof for the latter view, there is a textual allusion (*zeicher la-davar*) from Yechezkel: *et shnei ktzotav achlah ha-eish ve-tocho nachar, ha-yitzlach li-mlacha* — the fire consumed both its edges and its middle was charred; is it fit for any work? Rashi reads this passage as aligning Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's position with the *rov heikeifo* standard (burning on both sides corresponding to most of the circumference), and Rabbi Chiyya's with *rov avyo* (the degree of destruction that removes the wood from useful service). The precise correspondence is not entirely transparent, but that is Rashi's reading.

## Ach Digression

### Ach Meaning

The *gemara* briefly departs from its discussion of fire seizure to address a lexical curiosity. The *pasuk* in Yirmiyahu describing Yehoyakim burning the *Megilla* of Eichah reads: *ve-ha-ach lefanav mevo'aret* — the *ach* was burning before him. Yehoyakim, unwilling to accept the prophecy of Yerushalayim's destruction, cast the scroll into the fire. The *gemara* asks: *mai ach* — what is an *ach*?

Rav says: *achvana* — and as Rashi explains, this means *arava*, willow. Shmuel offers a different interpretation: *eitzim she-nidliku be-achvana* — sticks of wood that burned in a bundle together, each lighting the next — *kol ha-eitzim nidlakim zeh mi-zeh katan madlik ha-gadol* — the smaller pieces kindling the larger ones. The image is of a small bonfire where the pieces feed one another.

### Aravas Anecdote

The discussion concludes with an anecdote that confirms the identification of *achvana* with *arava*: *hahu de-amar lehu man ba'ei achvana* — there was someone going around calling out "who wants *achvana*?" — *ve-ishtakach arvasa* — and it turned out he was selling *aravas*, willows. This confirms that *ach* or at least *achvana* refers to the willow.

## Fuel Types

### Twigs vs. Bundles

Rav Huna rules: *kanim ein tzrichim rov* — loose twigs do not require the fire to seize most of them; *agdan tzrichim rov* — but if bundled together, most must be aflame. Twigs burn easily and reliably when separate; bundled together, the fire has more difficulty penetrating between them, and therefore a higher threshold is needed. Similarly for date pits: *garinim ein tzrichim rov* — loose date pits do not require a majority to have caught fire, given their surface area and flammability; *netanam be-chutalot tzrichim rov* — but if placed in baskets, where they are packed together and less surface is exposed, most must be alight.

### Chisda Opposition

Rav Chisda challenges this directly: *ipcha mistabra* — the opposite is more logical. Regarding twigs: *mevadran* — when scattered and separate, they do not kindle one another; if one begins to go out there is nothing adjacent to reignite it. *Agdan lo mevadran* — when bundled, they support each other, each relighting the others. So bundled twigs should be the easier case. The same reversal applies to date pits: loose, they are scattered and each risks going out independently; packed in a basket, they sustain one another's combustion. Rav Chisda thus maintains the exact opposite of Rav Huna. The *gemara* presents both positions without resolution, and the implicit appeal to empirical testing goes unexplored.

### Kahana Position

Rav Kahana, according to the text as we have it, takes a split position: *kanim she-agadan tzrichim rov* — bundled sticks require a majority; *lo agadan ein tzrichim rov* — unbundled, they do not. This corresponds to Rav Chisda's view for sticks. For date pits, the ruling is reversed: *garinim tzrichim rov* — pits alone require a majority; *netanam be-chutalot ein tzrichim rov* — in baskets they do not. There are significant textual variants (*girsaot*) in this passage that yield considerably different arrangements of the rulings, and different manuscripts come out all different ways in terms of what the *baraita* is saying.

### Special Fuels

Rav Yosef teaches: *arba medurot ein tzrichim rov* — there are four categories of fire that do not require the fire to seize most of the material. These are: *shel zefet* — tar or pitch, which is highly flammable; *shel gofrit* — sulfur; a third material that according to our text reads *shel gvina* — cheese — which is puzzling, since cheese is not an obvious fuel. The *Gra* emends the reading to *kira*, gourds, though whether gourds make for particularly reliable fuel is also unclear; and *shel revav* — some type of liquid flammable substance, which Rashi identifies as melted wax or oil. These are all materials that, once ignited, burn reliably and need no further tending — the *erev Shabbat* concern about fiddling does not apply.

A *baraita* adds: *af shel kash ve-shel gavava* — even stubble and straw fall into this category. These burn so rapidly and so easily that there is no concern one will need to adjust them — indeed, as was noted, they may burn almost too well.

### Babylonian Wood

Rabbi Yochanan states: *eitzim shel Bavel ein tzrichim rov* — the wood in Bavel does not require a majority to have caught fire before *Shabbat*. This may be because it is particularly oily or especially dry and thus burns readily.

Rav Yosef challenges this: *mai hi* — what wood are you referring to? If you mean *silty* — thin strips of wood — that cannot be easier to handle than a wick. For Ulla will teach at the opening of the next *perek* that when lighting an oil lamp before *Shabbat*, one must ensure that most of the wick extending from the lamp's mouth is actually burning. A wick is specifically designed to draw oil and sustain a flame; if even a wick requires most of its exposed portion to be lit, how could a thin strip of wood be treated more leniently?

Rav Yosef therefore concludes: *shucha de-arza* — the reference is not to ordinary thin wood but to the fibrous, woolly material found between the bark and the trunk of the cedar tree. This substance is highly flammable and, once ignited, burns reliably — which is why it requires no *rov*. The same material, we will see, reappears shortly in the opening of the next *perek*, where it is identified as one of the substances excluded from use as a wick because it burns too readily.

## Perek 2 Intro

### Fiddle Concern

The conclusion of the first *perek* and the opening of the second flow together seamlessly. The concern that dominated the first *perek* — that one will fiddle with a fire on *Shabbat* — provides the direct rationale for the *mishna* of *bameh madlikin*, which asks what types of wicks and oils may be used for *Shabbat* candles. If the wick or oil is of poor quality, the flame will sputter and burn unevenly, creating precisely the anxiety that leads a person to adjust it. Even without the specific concern of reading by candlelight, an unreliable flame tempts intervention.

### Candle History

The straightforward reading of the *mishna* suggests that the lighting of candles on *erev Shabbat* was, at its origin, simply a practical necessity: without oil lamps burning from before *Shabbat*, there would be no light for the Friday night meal. During the week, people largely ate before dark; on *Shabbat*, they ate after dark and could not kindle a flame, so they lit before *Shabbat* began. The *mishna* treats this as a practical reality and asks: given that you must light these lamps, what materials may you use?

That said, it becomes evident within the *gemara* and crystallizes in the *rishonim* that candle lighting eventually acquired the status of a *mitzvah*. Some historians argue that even at the time of the *mishna* it was already perceived as ritually significant — not merely as turning on the lights — pointing to references in non-Jewish and pagan sources that take note of the Jewish practice of lighting candles before *Shabbat* as a distinctively religious act. Lighting candles carried connotations of ceremony and sanctity broadly in the ancient world; the practice may have become ritualized even before it was formally classified as a *mitzvah* with a *bracha*. The question of when exactly that crystallization occurred will unfold as the *perek* proceeds.

### Karaite Context

The Karaites interpreted *lo teva'aru esh be-chol moshvoteichem be-yom ha-Shabbat* to forbid any fire from burning on *Shabbat*, even if kindled beforehand. This led them to spend *Shabbat* in cold and darkness. Maintaining fire from *erev Shabbat* — the *cholent* left burning on the fire, the candles kindled before nightfall — became for *Rabbinic* Jews an important institutional counterstatement against Karaite practice.

It is not entirely clear whether the Karaite position was already known in the *Talmudic* period; the first explicit evidence of the dispute comes from the *Geonic* period. It was in that era that the practice developed of reciting *Bameh Madlikin* on *erev Shabbat* as a deliberate affirmation of the permissibility — indeed, the obligatory nature — of burning lights into *Shabbat*. Manuscripts discovered in the *Genizah* contain a remarkable *bracha* that preceded *Bameh Madlikin* in the *Geonic* liturgy, beginning: *Baruch Atah Hashem Elokeinu Melech Ha-olam asher bachar ba-chachamim ve-talmideihem ve-natan lahem Torah me-Har Sinai al yedei Moshe Rabbeinu* — and proceeding to enumerate the chain of tradition from Moshe through Yehoshua, the seventy elders, the prophets, the sages, and their students, culminating in the commandment to observe *Shabbat* and to kindle the *Shabbat* lamp, before going directly into *Bameh Madlikin*. The polemical thrust is unmistakable: the *Rabbinic* tradition from Sinai is the authentic one, and it mandates lighting candles that remain burning on *Shabbat*.

Whatever the earlier history, by the *Geonic* period the *mitzvah* of *hadlakat ner Shabbat* was unambiguously established, complete with a *bracha* — the first description of such a *bracha* appears precisely in this period.

## Prohibited Wicks

### Lachesh

The second *perek* opens: *bameh madlikin u-bameh ein madlikin* — with what may one light, and with what may one not light? The *mishna* begins, as it sometimes does, with the prohibited category. The first prohibited wick is *lachesh* (some have the *girsa* *legesh*). The *gemara* identifies this as *shucha de-arza* — the fibrous, woolly material found between the bark and trunk of the cedar tree, the very same substance that was just discussed at the close of the first *perek* as a fuel that burns so readily it requires no *rov*. The *gemara* registers a moment of apparent confusion: surely *shucha de-arza* is just wood; who would think to use a stick as a wick? The resolution is that the reference is not to the stick itself but to the woolly fiber within it — *be-amranitta de-it bah*, the fibrous substance embedded in it. And precisely because this material burns so easily and rapidly, it is disqualified from use as a wick: it will not sustain a steady, even flame.

### Chosen Flax

The next prohibited item is *chosen* (some versions read *chossen*). Rav Yosef initially identifies it as *ne'oret shel pishtan* — the combings of flax, the fibers removed in the combing process. Abaye challenges this, citing a *pasuk*: *ve-haya ha-chason la-ne'oret* — indicating that *chason* and *ne'oret* are distinct things. Abaye therefore redefines *chosen* as *kitna de-dayek ve-lo nafitz* — flax that has been beaten but not yet combed, so that the husks of the flax have not yet been removed. Flax in this state burns unevenly because of the remaining husks, making it unreliable as a wick. To use flax as a wick properly, it would first need to be fully combed.

### Klach Silk

The third prohibited item is *klach*, which is generally assumed to refer to silk. Shmuel remarks: *she'alti le-chol nechutei yama ve-amru li kulcha shemei* — I asked all those who travel by sea, and they told me its name is *kulcha*. This is essentially a straight Aramaic equivalence rather than a substantive explanation. Rav Yitzchak bar Ze'ira calls it *gushkera*, which Steinsaltz identifies with the cocoon of the silkworm, while Rashi reads *hu beit ha-tola'at* — the house of the worm — the same idea. The implication is that *klach* is not fine silk but a byproduct or remnant of the silk-making process, perhaps the discarded casing or dross, which is why one might think to use it as a wick at all. Who would use expensive silk for a wick? It is the discarded material from silk production that raises the question.

Ravin and Abaye were once sitting in the presence of Ravna Nechemya, the brother of the *Resh Galuta* — the lay leader of Babylonian Jewry. They noticed he was wearing *metaksa*, a fine silk garment. Ravin said to Abaye: that must be the *klach* mentioned in our *mishna*. Abaye replied: what we call *shira parnida* is altogether different — that is fine, expensive silk, not the material of the *mishna*. This suggests that the exchange was as much about identifying a substance as about distinguishing grades of material.

### Silk Types

The *gemara* presses on this distinction. A *baraita* states that *ha-shirayim ve-ha-klach ve-ha-sivikin* — various types of silk fabrics, including *sivikin* (which Rashi identifies as a corded silk and ArtScroll translates as "corded silk," made from the softer portions of silk worked into cord) — *chayavin be-tzitzit*, are obligated in *tzitzit*. The *gemara* notes this as a *tiyuvta* — a difficulty — suggesting that *shirayim* and *klach* are indeed listed as separate items. The resolution proposed is: *shira lachud ve-shira parnida lachud* — plain *shira* and *shira parnida* are distinct categories. Under Rashi's reading, *shira parnida* is to be identified with *klach*, while under *Tosafot*'s reading, *shira parnida* is finer than *klach* and thus different from it. The upshot in either case is that *klach* refers to some form of silk — most plausibly a byproduct or lower-grade remnant of the silk-making process — and is disqualified as a wick because it burns too quickly and will not sustain a reliable flame.

### Idan & Desert

The string of the *idan* (*petilat ha-idan*) is identified by the *gemara* as *achvina* — the same word that just appeared in the digression about the *pasuk* in Yirmiyahu, which Rashi there identified as *arava*, willow. A wick must not only burn but must draw oil consistently; the fibrous material from a willow, whatever its burning properties, does not function well as an oil-drawing wick.

*Petilat ha-midbar* — the fiber of the desert — is identified by Rashi as a type of tall grass, perhaps a grass that grows in arid regions and can be braided into a rope-like form. Though it may seem a plausible material, it too fails to draw oil reliably.

### Moss Problem

*Yaroka she-al pnei ha-mayim* — the green growth on the surface of water — refers presumably to moss or algae. The *gemara* asks what exactly this is. The initial suggestion — *uchnisa de-charitzai*, the moss or algae of ponds — is rejected: *ifuhei mapharchi*, it crumbles and is too brittle; no one could seriously attempt to use it as a wick. Rav Pappa therefore identifies it as *uchnisa de-arva* — the moss that accumulates on the hulls of boats.

This identification was confirmed in a well-known anecdote: Ravin and Abaye — the same pair who appeared earlier identifying the *klach* — were traveling through the valley of Tamrurita when they observed what grew on the water there. Ravin said to Abaye: this must be the *yaroka* of the *mishna*. It is notable that the same pair of scholars appears repeatedly in this *perek* attempting to identify the obscure materials listed in the *mishna* against actual specimens encountered in the field.

### Wool & Hair

The *mishna* then states that the list of prohibited wicks was subsequently expanded (*hosifu aleihen*) to include wool (*tzemer*) and animal hair (*sa'ar*). The *tanna* of the *mishna* did not bother including these originally because they were self-evident: wool, when used as a wick, *michzi kavish* — shrivels up and collapses — and will not sustain a flame. Hair *ichruchei michrich* — singes — and cannot draw oil at all. These were so obviously unsuitable that no one would have thought to try them, and therefore the *tanna* required no explicit prohibition.

## Prohibited Oils

### Wick-Oil Shift

A *baraita* explicitly demarcates the boundary in the *mishna*: *ad kan psul petilot* — up to this point, the list concerns prohibited wicks; *mi-kan ve-eilach psul shmanim* — from here onward, the list concerns prohibited oils. The transition is marked by the mention of *zefet* (pitch or tar) and *sha'avah* (wax).

### Wax Debate

The *gemara* observes: *pshita* — it is obvious that pitch and wax cannot be used as oils, apparently because their burning properties, smell, or consistency make them clearly unsuitable as fuel. The *gemara* clarifies, however, that the case of wax (*sha'avah*) required explicit mention: *mahu de-teima li-psila nami lo chazya* — one might have thought that wax cannot even be used as a wick, and the *mishna* therefore tells us *ka mashma lan* — that wax is valid as a wick, just not as a fuel.

This is counterintuitive to the modern reader, since wax candles function precisely through the wax serving as fuel. The distinction in the *Talmudic* world reflects the fact that oil lamps — not wax candles — were the standard technology. Wax as a wick material, without functioning as the liquid fuel drawn up by capillary action, may have been used by applying wax to a fiber wick to help it stay lit — analogous to how modern wax-coated wicks maintain their flame more reliably than bare fiber. Whether one could also use melted wax as a fuel in a lamp-style vessel remains unclear from the *gemara*'s discussion.

### Dross Materials

Rami bar Avin provides lexical clarifications: *atrna psulta de-zifta* — the substance called *atrna* (elsewhere *itran*) is the dross or residue of *zefet*, the material left over after pitch is extracted from the tree. *Sha'avah psulta de-duvsha* — wax is the dross of honey; that is, beeswax is the byproduct of the honeycomb after the honey is extracted, confirming that the *sha'avah* of the *mishna* is indeed beeswax.

### Commercial Use

The *gemara* asks: *le-mai nafka mina* — what is the practical consequence of these identifications? *Le-mikach u-mimkar* — for commercial transactions. If a buyer contracts to purchase *itran* or *sha'avah*, the identification of exactly what those terms denote — dross of pitch, dross of honey — establishes what the seller is obligated to deliver. The *gemara*'s lexical exercise thus has direct application to the law of commerce and trade contracts.

*Tosafot*, in addressing the ruling that wax cannot be used as fuel, notes the practice in Narvona — contemporaneous to *Tosafot* — of using wax candles for *Shabbat*. *Tosafot* records the debate: does the *gemara*'s prohibition on wax as fuel extend to the wax candles of his era? The argument goes back and forth. The ultimate ruling of *Tosafot* — *ela vadai lo shanu ela bi-shemen, ki lo hiniach bi-shemen nami sharei* — suggests that the prohibition was formulated in the context of oil lamps, and that without the oil-lamp framework, the use of wax candles may be permitted. This became the *Ashkenazic* practice; wax and later paraffin candles are taken for granted for *Shabbat* lighting, though the theoretical basis for reconciling this with the *gemara*'s ruling is a matter discussed by the *rishonim*.
