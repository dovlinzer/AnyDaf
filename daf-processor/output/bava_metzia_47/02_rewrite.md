# Bava Metzia 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Money vs. Meshicha

### Kinyan Overview

The daf opens in the middle of the celebrated debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish regarding the mechanism of *kinyan* in commercial transactions. Rabbi Yochanan holds that by Torah law it is *kesef* — the payment of money — that effects the *kinyan*, but *Chazal* instituted a *takanah* requiring *meshicha*, the physical act of taking the object into one's possession, in order to finalize the acquisition. Reish Lakish disagrees fundamentally, maintaining that *meshicha* is not a rabbinic override but is itself the Torah's mechanism for *kinyan*. Running alongside this debate is a related set of questions: when is a coin considered money and when is it considered *peiros* (produce or commodity), and whether *chalipin* — the formal act of exchange — can be performed using a coin, or whether a coin can itself be transferred through *chalipin*.

### Handful of Coins

The gemara picks up with a ruling transmitted by Rava in the name of Rav Huna: if Reuven says to Shimon, "*mechor li be'elu*" — "sell me [your cow] with these [coins]," gesturing to whatever handful of coins he holds without specifying the amount — and Shimon agrees, the *kinyan* takes effect, and there is *ona'ah*, the law of overcharging or undercharging, that applies to the transaction.

This formulation raises an immediate tension. On one hand, the applicability of *ona'ah* implies that this is a normal sale for a price — the transaction is understood as a monetary purchase, and the question of whether the price was fair is therefore legally relevant. On the other hand, since no specific amount was named, the money is being treated to some degree as an object rather than a currency, making the transaction function more like *chalipin* — a swap. And if it is like *chalipin*, why was no *meshicha* of the cow required to finalize the deal?

### Ona'ah & Chalipin

The tension in Rav Huna's ruling becomes sharper when contrasted with the analysis that follows. The gemara notes that Reuven used the word *mechor* — "sell" — not "swap." The language of sale is what allows *ona'ah* to apply, because the parties understand the cow is being exchanged for a monetary value. At the same time, the failure to specify the exact amount allows the coins to be treated somewhat like an object, which is why the transaction can function as *chalipin* and does not require physical *meshicha* of the cow. The difficulty is that these two characterizations seem to pull in opposite directions: *ona'ah* belongs to the world of sales, while the dispensation from *meshicha* belongs to the world of swaps.

### Money as Object

A contrasting version of Rav Huna's ruling is cited in the name of Rav Abba: *mechor li be'elu kana ve'ein lo alav ona'ah* — if one says "sell me with these," the *kinyan* takes effect but there is no *ona'ah*. In this reading, the transaction is understood entirely as a swap. The coins function as an object — item number one — Shimon takes possession of them, and Reuven thereby acquires the cow. There is no *ona'ah* because the parties are not conducting a sale at a fixed price but exchanging one object for another, like a cow for a donkey. While this reading is internally more consistent — treating the money purely as an object coheres with the absence of *ona'ah* — it still must contend with the general principle that a coin (*matbe'a*) cannot serve as the instrument of *chalipin*.

The gemara addresses this directly: *lema savar Rav Huna matbe'a na'aseh chalipin* — do we think Rav Huna holds that a coin can effect *chalipin*? The answer is no. Rather, Rav Huna holds like Rabbi Yochanan, who maintains that by Torah law money itself is *koneh*. What is happening in this case is therefore not *chalipin* at all in the technical sense, but a genuine sale effected by money operating on Torah terms. The reason this is permitted — while the usual rabbinic requirement of *meshicha* is set aside — is the principle *milsa dishchicha gazar bah rabanan, milsa delo shchicha lo gazar bah rabanan*: the rabbis enacted their override for common cases, but in unusual or exceptional circumstances they allowed the Torah rule to operate. What makes this case exceptional is the degree to which the money was being treated as an object — the unspecified handful — which takes it out of the standard transaction and allows the Torah's mechanism of *kesef* to function.

This resolves the internal tension in the first version of Rav Huna's ruling as well. It truly is a *mecher* — a sale — which is why *ona'ah* applies. The Torah mechanism of *kesef* is operative, which is why no *meshicha* is required. The quasi-objectlike treatment of the coins is not what effects the *kinyan*, but it is what creates the unusual character of the transaction sufficient to carve it out from the rabbinic *takanah*.

This is confirmed by a subsequent clarification: Mar Huna the son of Rav Nachman told Rav Ashi that in their tradition, Rav Huna had explicitly ruled that a coin cannot effect *chalipin* — *ein matbe'a na'aseh chalipin* — and was counted among the *amoraim* who stated this position unambiguously. The earlier reading of his ruling as possibly endorsing *chalipin* with a coin was thus only a hypothetical. For those who know Rav Huna's position clearly, the only explanation for why the transaction works at all must be the Rabbi Yochanan principle: *min hatorah kesef koneh*.

## Chalipin

### Price-Aware Swaps

Before returning to the main debate, the gemara poses an elegant parallel question. We have just seen that when money is treated somewhat like an object — when no exact amount is specified — it can function quasi-like *chalipin* and allow the Torah's mechanism of *kesef* to operate. The reverse question now presents itself: if parties conducting what is formally a *chalipin* swap also take careful account of the value of the objects being exchanged, does that degree of price-consciousness transform the swap into a sale?

### Donkey/Cow Case

Rav Adda bar Ahava brings a *braita* as potential evidence. The case: Reuven stands in the market with his cow. Shimon approaches and asks why he brought the cow; Reuven replies that he is looking to acquire a donkey. Shimon offers his donkey in exchange for the cow, and the two parties then proceed to assess the value of each animal — "*paratcha bekama, bekach vekach; chamorecha bekama, bekach vekach*" — determining what each is worth. Having established the values, Shimon (the owner of the donkey) then takes possession of the cow. Before Reuven can take possession of the donkey, however, the donkey dies. The ruling: *lo kana ba'al hachamor et hapara* — Shimon does not acquire the cow.

The apparent implication is significant. Since the parties set a dollar value on the animals, the transaction was effectively transformed from a swap into a sale. In a sale, one object is the currency and the other is the commodity. If Reuven came to the market to acquire a donkey and offered his cow as payment, then the cow functions as the "money" and the donkey as the "object." Shimon took possession of the cow — analogous to receiving payment — but Reuven had not yet taken possession of the donkey — analogous to *meshicha* of the sold object. Absent *meshicha*, the sale is not final, and Reuven may withdraw. This seems to confirm that price-awareness converts *chalipin* into a sale.

### Rava's Defense

Rava rejects this reading with characteristic sharpness: *atu chalipin beshotni askinan dela kapdi?* — are we really discussing *chalipin* conducted by fools who pay no attention to value? Of course parties to a swap assess whether they are getting equal or fair value; that does not transform the transaction into a sale. People who swap a donkey for a cow are not oblivious to relative worth, and *chalipin* has always contemplated that parties will attend to value. Accordingly, Rava states the affirmative rule: *kol chalipin mikpad kapid vekaney* — in every *chalipin*, one may be careful about value and the *kinyan* is nonetheless valid as a swap.

### Multiple Objects

If value-consciousness does not explain the *braita*'s ruling, what does? Rava reinterprets the case entirely. The scenario being described is one in which the parties discovered, upon assessing values, that the donkey and cow were not of equal worth — say the donkey was worth one hundred and the cow only sixty. To equalize the exchange, Reuven offered not only his cow but also his sheep (worth forty), making the swap a donkey for a cow and a sheep. The *chalipin* remains a genuine swap, not a sale — but now the "package" on Reuven's side consists of two objects. When Shimon takes possession of the cow but not yet the sheep, that is not a complete *meshicha* of Reuven's side of the deal: *delo haveya leh meshicha ma'alya* — it is not a full *meshicha*. Because the entire set of objects Reuven is giving has not been taken into possession, the transaction is not yet finalized, and when the donkey dies Reuven may withdraw.

This reading preserves the integrity of *chalipin* even when value is assessed. The gemara thus determines that price-awareness does not convert a swap into a sale. What the *braita* illustrates is rather that when value-equalization leads to a multi-object package on one side, *meshicha* of that entire package must occur before the *kinyan* is complete.

The gemara notes in passing an interesting open question: what if, rather than adding a sheep, one party said "donkey for cow, and I'll owe you forty"? Would the outstanding monetary obligation assimilate the transaction to a sale? The gemara does not address this case, leaving it as a point requiring further analysis.

## Money vs. Meshicha…

### Rav Huna's View

The gemara now returns to reconcile the positions that have emerged. The key move is the identification of Rav Huna's ruling with the position of Rabbi Yochanan — *min hatorah kesef koneh* — and the principle that the rabbinic override (*gezeirah*) requiring *meshicha* was enacted for ordinary cases but not extended to exceptional ones.

The reason *Chazal* instituted *meshicha* in place of *kesef* is the well-known *gezeirah*: *shema yomar lo nisrefu chitecha ba'aliyah* — lest the seller say to the buyer, "your grain burned up in my loft." If the payment of money were to finalize the sale, the grain would henceforth belong to the buyer while remaining physically in the seller's domain. The seller, now a *shomer chinam* with no personal stake in the grain's safety, would have little motivation to exert himself to save it in a fire. *Chazal* therefore required that the sale not be final until the buyer took physical possession, ensuring that the person who stands to lose from negligence is also the one in a position to prevent it.

The gemara probes whether this concern can be dismissed: if someone causes the fire, let him pay the buyer for the loss. The response: the *gezeirah* addresses cases of *ones* — an accidental fire, such as one caused by lightning, for which no one is liable. In such a case, absent the *takanah*, the seller would have no incentive to risk himself to save what is already, technically, the buyer's property. With the *takanah* in place — the sale not being final until *meshicha* — the seller knows the grain is still his until possession transfers, and *masser nafshia tarach umatzil*, he will exert himself to save it.

Given this rationale, the principle that *milsa delo shchicha lo gazru bah rabanan* — the rabbis did not extend their decree to unusual cases — becomes intelligible in context. What makes the unusual case exceptional is not mere rarity alone; it is that the particular features of the transaction, such as the quasi-objectlike treatment of the coins, remove it from the paradigm the *gezeirah* was designed to address.

This also illuminates what is admittedly still somewhat strange: having one system that operates differently in standard versus exceptional cases. Yet the gemara notes that we already know *meshicha* works in some situations, *hagbahah* in others, *mesira* in others still, so a certain plurality of operative mechanisms is familiar. The philosophical question of why the Torah would have constructed the *kinyan* around *kesef* in the first place — if, in practice, *Chazal* found it necessary to replace it with *meshicha* — is left as a matter for deeper reflection.

## Objects in Chalipin

### Rav vs. Levi

Having concluded the discussion of when money can operate as *kesef*, the gemara now addresses the detailed mechanics of *chalipin*: which objects may be used to effect it, and whose object is employed. This is introduced with the question *bameh konin* — with what do we acquire?

Rav rules *b'kelov shel koneh* — with the object belonging to the purchaser. The buyer gives his handkerchief to the seller; the seller takes it, and the buyer thereby acquires whatever is being sold. This follows naturally from the swap model: the buyer gives something and receives something in return. It is also, in fact, the *halakha* as ruled. Levi disagrees: *b'kelov shel makneh* — the object belongs to the one transferring, the seller. Shimon who is selling his cow gives his handkerchief to Reuven; Reuven takes it, and thereby acquires the cow. Everything moves in one direction, which seems counterintuitive.

### Gemurit Da'at

The gemara explains the rationale behind Rav's ruling in terms of *gemurit da'at* — the finalization of intent. In any *kinyan*, the party whose *da'at* is most critical to establish is the *makneh*, the one transferring the object out of his possession. He must reach a point of genuine, settled resolve to part with what he owns. The mechanism of *chalipin*, on this reading, is designed to bring about that finalization of intent in the *makneh*. Therefore, *d'nikha leh l'koneh d'lehavi makneh koneh* — it is in the buyer's interest that the *makneh* be the one who takes possession of the buyer's object, *ki heichi d'ligmar v'likni leh* — so that the seller will reach *gemurit da'at* and transfer the property. The seller receiving something from the buyer is what crystallizes his resolve to give.

### Land & Chattel

A pointed challenge is raised against Levi's position by Rav Huna mi-Diskarta. If *chalipin* operates through the seller giving his own handkerchief to the buyer, and the buyer acquires both the handkerchief and the land being sold, then the buyer is acquiring land by taking possession of a garment. But the established rule runs in precisely the opposite direction: *nechasim she'ein lahem achrayut niknin im nechasim she'yesh lahem achrayut* — chattel can be acquired together with real estate, meaning that taking possession of land brings chattel along with it. The reverse — that chattel drags real estate along with it — is not the rule. Yet on Levi's model, taking the handkerchief would seem to transfer the land, which is exactly that impermissible reversal.

### Benefit Theory

Rava's response on Levi's behalf is striking. He says, with characteristic vigor, that Levi would have been furious at the challenge: *aval maruka lo itzterich* — the question rests on a misunderstanding of the conceptual model. It is not that the buyer acquires the land *through* taking possession of the handkerchief, as if the handkerchief were dragging the land with it. Rather, the mechanism is entirely different: *b'hai hana'ah d'kamakabbel minei gemar v'akni lei* — it is the benefit that the seller (the *makneh*) receives from the fact that the buyer agreed to accept a gift from him. When Reuven accepts Shimon's handkerchief, Shimon experiences a kind of benefit — the honor and goodwill of having one's gift received — and it is that benefit which generates the *gemar da'at* in Shimon, allowing him to finalize his intent and transfer the land. There is no chattel acquiring land; the handkerchief is not a vehicle of acquisition at all. The vehicle is the *benefit* flowing back to the seller from the act of acceptance.

While this formulation avoids the technical objection about *nechasim she'ein lahem achrayut*, it is admittedly a more convoluted mechanism than the simple swap model underlying Rav's position. Why not simply use the natural direction of the buyer giving to the seller? Nevertheless, this remains Levi's position.

### Ruth's Shoe

The debate between Rav and Levi is shown to be rooted in a *tannaitic* dispute about the verse in Ruth: *v'zos l'fanim b'Yisrael al hage'ulah v'al hatemurah l'kayem kol davar, shalaf ish na'alo v'natan l'rei'eihu* — "And this was the custom in Israel concerning redemption and exchange, to confirm every matter: a man drew off his shoe and gave it to his fellow." The gemara identifies *ge'ulah* as referring to sale and *temurah* as referring to exchange — *chalipin* — and notes that *l'kayem kol davar* encompasses anything one wishes to establish, whether a sale, a swap, or an obligation.

The critical question in the verse is *mi natan l'mi* — who gave to whom? The *tanna kamma* says Boaz gave to the redeemer, meaning the *koneh* (Boaz, who was acquiring the right of redemption and Ruth) gave his object to the *makneh* — the position of *kelov shel koneh*, which is Rav's view. Rabbi Yehuda says the redeemer gave to Boaz, meaning the *makneh* gave his object to the *koneh* — the position of *kelov shel makneh*, which is Levi's view.

It is worth noting that the *ketubah* ceremony provides a vivid illustration of *l'kayem kol davar* used not as a swap and not as a sale, but as an act of *hitchayvut* — obligation. The rabbi representing the *kallah* gives the handkerchief to the *chatan*, who accepts it, and through that acceptance obligates himself to the *kallah* in all the terms of the *ketubah*. This usage — establishing an obligation rather than transferring ownership — is arguably a natural reading of *l'kayem kol davar*, though the gemara itself does not develop this point.

## Valid Objects

### Any Vessel

A *braita* teaches: *konin bikli af al pi she'ein bo shaveh pruta* — the *kinyan* is effected with a *kli*, a vessel or functional object, even if it is not worth a *perutah*. A handkerchief, a pen, any functional object — regardless of monetary value — suffices. The requirement is that it be a *kli*, an object, not that it have a specific worth.

### Vessels Only

Rav Nachman rules: *lo shanu ella bikli, aval befeirot lo* — only a vessel qualifies; fruit does not. As Tosafot explains and as the context makes clear, this is not a minimum-value requirement but a categorical exclusion: produce and food items, being consumable rather than functional, cannot serve as the instrument of *chalipin* regardless of their worth.

### Produce Allowed

Rav Sheshet disagrees: *afilu befeirot* — even produce can effect *chalipin*. The dispute turns on competing readings of the verse. Rav Nachman derives from *na'alo* — his shoe — that the object must be something like a shoe: a man-made, functional item. Rav Sheshet derives from *l'kayem kol davar* — to establish any matter — that the object used need not be a *kli* specifically; it can be anything, including food, provided it is a complete, discrete item and not half a pomegranate or half a walnut. The word *na'alo*, Rav Sheshet would say, teaches only that the object must be a recognizable whole — a *davar mesuyam* — not that it must be a manufactured vessel.

Each *amora* must also account for the textual element emphasized by the other. For Rav Nachman, *l'kayem kol davar* means one can acquire anything one wishes through *chalipin*, not that one can use anything as the instrument. For Rav Sheshet, *na'alo* teaches that the object must be a complete and discrete thing, not that it must be a *kli*.

### Ketuba Language

The standard formula used in halakhic documents, including the *ketubah*, encodes these disputes: *b'mana d'chasher l'miknya bei* — "with a vessel that is fit to effect *kinyan* with." Each word carries halakhic weight. *B'mana* — "with a vessel" — excludes the position of Rav Sheshet that produce suffices; the formula insists on a *kli*. *D'chasher* — "that is fit" or "appropriate" — excludes the position of Shmuel that *maroka* (a vessel fashioned from dung or date-pits, something considered degraded and inappropriate) may be used. Even if something qualifies technically as a *kli*, it must be fit and seemly. *L'miknya bei* — "to acquire with" — excludes the position of Levi that the *makneh*'s vessel is used; *l'miknya* is the Aramaic for "to take possession," indicating that it is the *koneh* who uses the vessel to acquire, not the *makneh* who uses it to transfer.

### What's Excluded

Three further exclusions emerge from the formula. Rav Pappa derives from the word *bei* — "with it" — that a coin (*matbe'a*) is excluded. A coin, regardless of how it is framed, cannot serve as the *kli* for *chalipin*. Rav Zevid (or, according to some, Rav Ashi) derives from *d'chasher* — "appropriate" — that *issurei hana'ah*, objects from which benefit is prohibited (such as something fashioned from materials of *avodah zarah*), are excluded. Even if such an object is technically a *kli*, it lacks the quality of fitness required. The two exclusions are complementary rather than contradictory: *bei* excludes the coin (on account of its monetary character), and *d'chasher* excludes objects of prohibited benefit (on account of their legal status).

As for *maroka*, the gemara states *lo itzterich* — no special derivation is needed; it is so obviously beneath the dignity of a formal legal act that its exclusion goes without saying. The requirement of *d'chasher* captures not only technical fitness but a standard of appropriateness and dignity.

In summary: the *halakha* follows Rav — *kli shel koneh*; *chalipin* cannot be performed with a *matbe'a*; produce is invalid; and the object used must be fit, seemly, and not one from which benefit is prohibited.

### Rabbeinu Tam

An important qualification to these rules emerges from the position of Rabbeinu Tam, discussed in Tosafot. Rabbeinu Tam draws a sharp distinction between two fundamentally different modes of *chalipin*. The first, which corresponds to *al hage'ulah* (sale) and *l'kayem kol davar* (obligation), is the formal, ritual act of *chalipin* — the handkerchief given as a legal mechanism to seal a sale or create an obligation. It is to this mode that all the formal requirements apply: *kli shel koneh*, only a *kli* and not produce, the requirements of fitness and dignity. The second mode corresponds to *al hatemurah* — an actual exchange, like a donkey for a cow. Here, Rabbeinu Tam maintains, everyone would agree that the restrictions of *kli hara'uy liknot bo* do not apply. In a genuine swap, the parties are exchanging actual items, and the rules of *chalipin* as a formal legal act are simply not operative in the same way. Even produce, Rabbeinu Tam holds, would be valid in the context of an actual exchange — a point on which there would be universal agreement once the two modes are properly distinguished. The question of whether animals fall in the category of *kli* or of produce (since they are not man-made but are also not consumable) remains a point of analysis within this framework.

### Asimon

The gemara introduces the concept of *asimon*, which occupies an intermediate category between money and commodity. The *mishna* states: *asimon koneh et hamatbe'a* — the *asimon* acquires the standard coin. That is, the *asimon* is treated as the object (*peiros*) while the standard coin is the currency; when the buyer takes possession of the *asimon*, that *meshicha* finalizes the *kinyan*.

Rav initially explains *asimon* as coins used as chits at a bathhouse — tokens given to indicate something (perhaps counting patrons, or entitling one to a towel), but not functioning as genuine currency. The gemara challenges this: a *braita* lists both *asimon* and bathhouse chits as separate categories, making them clearly distinct.

Rabbi Yochanan provides the authoritative definition: *asimon* is a *pulsa* — and as Rashi explains, this means an unstamped coin (*mechusar tzurah*), a piece of precious metal that has inherent value by weight but has not yet been impressed with an official image. Such a piece cannot function as ordinary currency — it cannot be exchanged at face value in the market — and is therefore treated as a commodity rather than money.

Rabbi Yochanan adds that his reading of *asimon* is consistent with the position of Rabbi Dosa and Rabbi Yishmael, who apply a broader standard in *ma'aser sheni* redemption. The verse *v'tzarta hakesef b'yadecha* — "you shall bind the money in your hand" — is interpreted by Rabbi Yishmael as including anything that can be bound in one's hand, making unstamped metal eligible for *ma'aser sheni* redemption. Rabbi Akiva restricts this to *davar sheyesh alav tzurah* — only something bearing a stamped image. Rabbi Yochanan sees Rabbi Dosa (who permits *asimon* for *ma'aser sheni*) as sharing the inclusive approach of Rabbi Yishmael, and his own definition of *asimon* as unstamped metal flows directly from that logic.

## Yochanan/Reish

### Yochanan: Money

With the conclusion of the *mishnah*'s ruling that *meshicha* finalizes a sale while money alone does not — and that a party who accepted payment but has not yet delivered the object is morally censured but not legally bound — the gemara now presents the original location of the Rabbi Yochanan–Reish Lakish debate. Everything cited earlier was anticipatory; here is the debate as it stands in the text.

Rabbi Yochanan states: *devar Torah ma'ot konot* — by Torah law, it is the payment of money that effects the acquisition. The Torah system is built around *kesef*.

### Gezeirah

Why, then, did the rabbis require *meshicha*? *Gezeirah shema yomar lo nisrefu chitecha ba'aliyah* — lest the seller say to the buyer, "your grain burned in my loft." If money finalizes the sale, the grain is now the buyer's property even while sitting in the seller's domain. The seller, now a *shomer chinam* with no personal stake, has no strong incentive to risk himself to save it. *Chazal* therefore required the buyer to take physical possession before the sale is complete, ensuring that the party with the incentive to protect the property is also the one who controls it.

The gemara tests whether the concern is trivial: if someone causes the fire, let the arsonist pay. The response: the *gezeirah* targets cases of *ones*, where a fire breaks out through no one's fault — lightning, for instance — and no one is legally liable. In such a case, if the grain is already the buyer's by operation of Torah law, *lo masser nafshia tarach u'matzil* — the seller will not exert himself to save it. But if the sale is not final until *meshicha*, the seller knows his own property is at risk, and *masser nafshia tarach u'matzil* — he will go to great lengths to protect it.

This *gezeirah* is a striking feature of halakhic reasoning: *Chazal* overrode a Torah-level mechanism — *kesef* as *kinyan* — in order to produce better incentives and protect the practical interests of buyers. The philosophical question of why the Torah would have established *kesef* as the primary mechanism in the first place, if it produces such problematic incentive structures, is left open.

### Reish: Meshicha

Reish Lakish takes a categorically different position: *meshicha meforashes min hatorah* — *meshicha* is the Torah's own mechanism for *kinyan*, not a rabbinic enactment. The Torah itself establishes that physical acquisition is what transfers ownership, and money plays no direct role in effecting the *kinyan* at the Torah level.

### Textual Proof

Reish Lakish derives this from the verse: *vechi simkeru mimkar la'amisecha o kano miyad amisecha* — "When you sell a sale to your fellow or purchase from the hand of your fellow." The phrase *kano miyad* — "purchase from the hand" — indicates that acquisition occurs specifically through the transfer from one hand to another, i.e., through physical possession changing hands. *Davar hanikneh miyad l'yad* — it is an act that is effected hand to hand, which is the language of *meshicha*.

Rabbi Yochanan reads *miyad* differently: it comes to exclude land (*karka*) from the laws of *ona'ah*. The verse discusses *ona'ah*, and *miyad* serves to limit those laws to chattel — *l'mi'utei karka deleis bei ona'ah* — since the *halakhot* of overcharging do not apply to real estate. This is itself a striking interpretive point: the verse speaks explicitly about land, yet *Chazal* apply its central teachings — *ona'ah* and, on Reish Lakish's reading, *meshicha* — to chattel.

### Ona'ah Dispute

Reish Lakish presses further. If *miyad* is merely coming to exclude land from *ona'ah*, why does the verse need to mention *o kano* — "or purchase" — at all? The verse could simply have said "you shall sell to your fellow from his hand, do not oppress." The inclusion of the purchaser's act (*kano*) must teach something additional. Reish Lakish maintains it teaches two things: first, that the prohibition of *ona'ah* applies to the purchaser (i.e., underpaying is also *ona'ah*, not only overcharging), and second, that *kano miyad* tells us the acquisition occurs only through the hand-to-hand transfer.

Rabbi Yochanan accounts for *o kano* differently: *talmud lomar o kano al tonu* — the verse must say "or if you purchase, do not oppress" in order to teach that the prohibition of *ona'ah* applies whether it is the *lokei'ach* (buyer) who is cheated by being overcharged or the *mocher* (seller) who is cheated by being underpaid. Without this phrase, one might have thought only the buyer's being defrauded triggers the prohibition. *O kano* extends *ona'ah* to the seller's disadvantage as well.

Reish Lakish responds: *tartei shma mina* — one can derive both from the same phrase. *O kano al tonu* teaches that *ona'ah* applies to the seller; *kano miyad* teaches that acquisition is through hand-to-hand transfer — *meshicha*. Both lessons are contained in the same words, and there is no need to choose between them.

## Rabbi Shimon

### Upper Hand

The *mishnah* contains a ruling by Rabbi Shimon: *kol shehakesef beyado yado al ha'elyona* — whoever holds the money has the upper hand. This cryptic formulation requires unpacking.

### Back-Out Rights

The *mishnah* goes on to explain: *mocher hu demati hadar bei* — it is the seller, who now holds the buyer's money, who has the right to withdraw from the transaction. *Lokei'ach lo mati hadar bei* — the buyer, who has given his money but has not yet taken possession of the object, cannot back out. The situation, then, is asymmetric: money has changed hands but *meshicha* has not yet occurred. Rabbi Shimon holds that the seller — the one with the money in hand — has the upper hand and retains the right to retract, while the buyer, having given his money, is bound.

### Reish Challenges

This ruling is then deployed as a challenge in the ongoing debate. The gemara asks: *ei amart bishlama ma'ot konot* — if you accept Rabbi Yochanan's position that money effects *kinyan* by Torah law and *meshicha* is only rabbinic, then Rabbi Shimon's position becomes understandable. One can conceive of a system where the rabbinic override of *meshicha* is applied differently to the two parties. But *ela im amart ma'ot einan konot* — if you accept Reish Lakish's position that money is halakhically inert at the Torah level, then nothing has happened at all when money is exchanged without *meshicha*. How could Rabbi Shimon give significance to the money as a basis for distinguishing the rights of seller and buyer?

Reish Lakish responds directly: *aliba deRabbi Shimon lo kamina* — I am not claiming that Rabbi Shimon's view fits with my position. Reish Lakish concedes that Rabbi Shimon, who gives decisive weight to the transfer of money even prior to *meshicha*, must be operating on the assumption that *kesef koneh min hatorah* — money effects *kinyan* by Torah law, a position Reish Lakish himself rejects. Rabbi Shimon's view simply belongs to the opposing camp on that foundational question. *Ki kamina aliba deRabbanan* — Reish Lakish's argument applies to the majority Rabbinic position, not to Rabbi Shimon's. For the Rabbis (who on Reish Lakish's view hold *meshicha* is the Torah-level mechanism), the transfer of money is irrelevant to who may or may not withdraw.

### Rav Chisda

For Rabbi Yochanan, however, a further question remains. Both Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis accept that *kesef* is the Torah mechanism and *meshicha* is rabbinic. What then is the substance of their debate? The gemara answers: *ika beinaihu deRav Chisda* — the dispute between them corresponds to the principle of Rav Chisda. Rav Chisda rules: *keshem shetiknu meshicha bemokhrin kach tiknu meshicha belokkhin* — just as the rabbis established *meshicha* as operative from the seller's side, so too did they establish it as operative from the buyer's side. The override is symmetric and applies equally to both parties: until *meshicha* occurs, neither the seller nor the buyer is bound, regardless of which direction the money has flowed.

### Final Framework

The Rabbis hold of Rav Chisda's principle; Rabbi Shimon does not. For the Rabbis, because the rabbinic enactment of *meshicha* applies equally to seller and buyer, it does not matter who holds the money — until *meshicha* takes place, both parties are free to withdraw. For Rabbi Shimon, who rejects the symmetric application of *meshicha*, the Torah mechanism of *kesef* continues to operate — at least from one direction. The buyer, having given his money, is bound by the Torah-level *kinyan* of *kesef* and cannot withdraw. The seller, however, benefits from the rabbinic protection of *meshicha*: since the object has not yet transferred to the buyer's possession, the seller retains the right to retract.

Rashi's explanation of exactly why the rabbinic enactment would protect the seller but not the buyer is admittedly convoluted, and Tosafot engages in extended analysis of the point, including the question of whether a *mi shepara* curse applies when a party withdraws after money was given but before *meshicha*, particularly in cases where a genuine accident (like a fire) provides a compelling reason for withdrawal. The gemara does not fully resolve the underlying logic of Rabbi Shimon's asymmetric position, but the structural framework — the Rabbis accept Rav Chisda's symmetric application of *meshicha*, Rabbi Shimon does not — remains the operative distinction. This debate of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, and its rippling implications, will continue to be developed in the sugyot ahead.
