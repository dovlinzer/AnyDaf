# Shabbat 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Context

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְתִינוֹק. בְּצִיבּוּר נָמֵי, חֲזֵי לְגַמֵּעַ לְתִינוֹק עָנִי? וְתוּ, הָא דְּתַנְיָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּטַלְטְלִין אֶת הַשּׁוֹפָר כָּךְ מְטַלְטְלִין אֶת הַחֲצוֹצְרוֹת. מַנִּי? אֶלָּא לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הָא רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **to a child.** Because the mouth of a *shofar* is bent, one can pour a little water at a time. If so, a *shofar* belonging **to the community is also suitable to feed** water **to a poor infant** whose sustenance is provided by the community. **And furthermore, that** *halakha* **which was taught** in a *baraita*: **Just as one may move the *shofar*, so too one may move** the **trumpets,** is contrary to that which was taught previously that there is a difference between moving the *shofar* and moving the trumpet. In accordance with **whose** opinion is that *baraita*? **Rather,** this is **not difficult,** as it can be explained that these three *baraitot* correspond to the three opinions with regard to these *halakhot*. **This** *baraita*, which permits moving the *shofar* but not the trumpet, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that the laws of set-aside apply to these items on Shabbat and one may not move a utensil whose only function is prohibited. Since a trumpet has no permitted use on Shabbat, it may not be moved. On the other hand, one is permitted to move a *shofar*, which can be used to feed a child. And **that** *baraita*, which permits moving both a *shofar* and a trumpet, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that the *halakhot* of set-aside do not apply to utensils of this kind on Shabbat. Whereas **this** other *baraita*, which prohibits moving both a *shofar* and a trumpet, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya,** who holds that one may not use a utensil whose primary function is prohibited on Shabbat, even for a permissible purpose.

Daf 36 marks the transition from the second *perek* to the third. The shiur picks up from the bottom of 35b, completing the final discussion of the second *perek* before moving on to the new chapter's opening *mishnah*.

### Shofar Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שׁוֹפָר — נָמֵי חֲצוֹצְרוֹת. כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא. דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָנֵי תְּלָת מִילֵּי אִישְׁתַּנִּי שְׁמַיְיהוּ מִכִּי חֲרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ: ״חֲצוֹצַרְתָּא״ — ״שׁוֹפָרָא״, ״שׁוֹפָרָא״ — ״חֲצוֹצַרְתָּא״. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינָּה? לְשׁוֹפָר שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** However, this explanation raises a slight difficulty with regard to the statement that one may move neither a *shofar* nor a trumpet. There was no need to mention the trumpet. If one may not move a *shofar*, certainly he may not move a trumpet. However, it can be explained as follows: **What is** the ***shofar*** mentioned in this *baraita*? It refers to **trumpets, in accordance with** the statement of **Rav Ḥisda, as Rav Ḥisda said: These three objects, their names changed since the Holy Temple was destroyed.** That which was called **trumpet** was called ***shofar*** in later generations, and that which was called ***shofar*** was called **trumpet** in later generations. The *baraita* that was cited employed the style that switches trumpet and *shofar*, and they were mentioned in that order. Incidentally, the Gemara asks: **What is the** practical halakhic **difference** whether a *shofar* is called *shofar* or trumpet? The Gemara answers: It is significant with regard **to** the *halakhot* of ***shofar* of Rosh HaShana.** On Rosh HaShana one fulfills his obligation only by sounding a *shofar*. If one comes today and asks what instrument he should use to sound the requisite blasts, he should be told to use a trumpet.

The passage under discussion concerns the *chazan haknesset* — the synagogue sexton — who would sound the *shofar* blasts announcing the arrival of *Shabbat*. After the final blast, the question arose: what does he do with his *shofar*? The Gemara answers that he had a designated hidden place at the top of his roof where he would deposit it immediately, because *Shabbat* was entering at that very moment. Even within a *reshut hayachid*, a *shofar* and *chatzotzrot* (trumpet) are *muktzah* and may not be handled once *Shabbat* begins. This detail sets up the Gemara's subsequent analysis of the precise *muktzah* status of these instruments.

---

## Shofar/Trumpet Muktzah

### Hide Shofar

The Gemara establishes that the *chazan* had to deposit his *shofar* immediately upon the onset of *Shabbat* because *shofar* and *chatzotzrot* carry *muktzah* status. The term *m'taltalin* — normally associated with carrying between domains — here means something more fundamental: the object is *muktzah* and may not be handled at all. Even within a fully enclosed *reshut hayachid*, neither instrument may be moved.

### Braitot Contradiction

The Gemara then surfaces a striking set of contradictions among *braitot*. One *braita* states that neither *shofar* nor *chatzotzrot* may be carried on *Shabbat* — both are *muktzah*. A second *braita* draws a distinction: a *shofar* may be carried, but a *chatzotzrot* may not. Rav Yosef attempts to resolve the apparent contradiction between these two sources by distinguishing between a private *shofar* and a communal one. Abaye challenges this immediately: even a private *shofar*, what renders it permissible? The Gemara answers that a *shofar* is fit for a legitimate use — it can serve as a kind of funnel through which water is given to a child (*tinok*), drawing the liquid from the wide end through the narrow mouthpiece. A *chatzotzrot*, being straight, cannot serve this function as naturally. Yet Abaye presses further: even a communal *shofar* — the very one used to announce *Shabbat* — is fit for that same use when a poor child in the congregation needs it. The *beit knesset* bears responsibility for the poor, and therefore the communal *shofar* too is potentially usable. The distinction between private and public thus collapses. A third *braita* then enters: just as a *shofar* may be carried, so too may *chatzotzrot* — neither is *muktzah* at all. The three *braitot* now represent three distinct positions: both forbidden, one permitted and one forbidden, and both permitted.

### Three Tannaim

The Gemara resolves the contradictions cleanly: each *braita* represents a different *Tanna*. The *braita* permitting only the *shofar* reflects the view of Rabbi Yehuda. The *braita* permitting both reflects Rabbi Shimon. The *braita* forbidding both reflects Rabbi Nechemya. The three positions map onto a spectrum of how broadly or narrowly each *Tanna* defines *muktzah*.

---

## Muktzah Categories

### R. Yehuda View

Rabbi Yehuda occupies the middle position in this spectrum. He holds of *muktzah* in its standard formulation and distinguishes between the *shofar* and the *chatzotzrot* on the basis of their alternative uses. His view is that a *shofar*, because it can be used as a funnel for a child, has a sufficient legitimate use that affects its classification, while a *chatzotzrot* lacks any comparable alternative function. At one extreme, Rabbi Shimon rejects most categories of *muktzah* — he acknowledges only very narrow cases such as *muktzah machmat mi'us* — and therefore has no difficulty with either instrument. At the other extreme, Rabbi Nechemya holds that even a *kli* that might theoretically have a permitted use may only be moved for its fundamental designated purpose. Since a *shofar*'s fundamental purpose is to be blown as a musical instrument — which is itself prohibited on *Shabbat* — Rabbi Nechemya holds that even the *shofar* may not be moved.

### Rashi Shofar

Rashi's explanation of Rabbi Yehuda's position deserves close attention. Rashi states explicitly that the *chatzotzrot* is a *davar shemelachto l'issur* — an implement whose primary function is a prohibited one — and is therefore forbidden as *muktzah*. The *shofar*, by contrast, Rashi treats as having been recategorized: because it can be used to give water to a child, it is classified not merely as a *kli shemelachto l'issur* used for a permitted purpose, but as a *kli shemelachto l'heter* outright. This is a significant claim. The standard rule is that a *kli shemelachto l'issur* — a hammer, for example — may be moved on *Shabbat* if one needs to use it for a permitted purpose, such as cracking nuts. But the hammer remains classified as a *kli shemelachto l'issur*; it does not thereby become a *kli shemelachto l'heter*. Rashi appears to be saying that the *shofar* is different: its legitimate alternative use is substantial enough to actually reclassify the object. The question naturally arises — why does the same logic not apply to the *chatzotzrot*? Rashi's answer, at least implicitly, is that the *chatzotzrot* has no such legitimate use that could reframe its classification.

### Tosafot Trumpet

Tosafot take a fundamentally different approach. Tosafot accept the general rule that a *kli shemelachto l'issur* may be moved for a *tzorech heter* — a permitted need — without any reclassification of the object itself. On this reading, both *shofar* and *chatzotzrot* are *kli shemelachto l'issur*, and both could in principle be moved for a permitted purpose. Why then does Rabbi Yehuda distinguish between them? Tosafot's answer is found toward the end of their commentary on 35b. The *chatzotzrot* is in a category worse than *kli shemelachto l'issur*: it is something one would never think of using for any other purpose whatsoever. Tosafot invoke the analogy of a bed designated for holding coins — an object that has been so thoroughly set aside from ordinary use that it enters a state of complete exclusion. The *chatzotzrot* is like that: no one would ever consider picking up a trumpet to crack a nut or perform any other incidental task. It has been entirely removed from the realm of alternative usage, placing it in a more stringent category than a standard *kli shemelachto l'issur*.

### Rashi v. Tosafot

The contrast between Rashi and Tosafot thus comes into sharp relief. Rashi resolves the asymmetry by upgrading the *shofar* — reclassifying it as a *kli shemelachto l'heter* on the basis of its alternative use. Tosafot resolve the asymmetry by downgrading the *chatzotzrot* — placing it in a more severe category than *kli shemelachto l'issur* because it has been fully set aside from any alternative use. The two approaches move in opposite directions: Rashi adjusts the *shofar* upward, Tosafot pushes the *chatzotzrot* downward.

It is important to note that this debate between Rashi and Tosafot does not appear to reflect a broader disagreement about the general rule of *kli shemelachto l'issur*. The *gemara* is clear elsewhere that a *kli shemelachto l'issur* may be moved *l'tzorech gufo* — for use of the object itself in a permitted manner. Rashi presumably does not contest this. The key to understanding Rashi's position in this *sugya* is the specific case under discussion: the *chazan* who deposited his *shofar* the moment *Shabbat* entered was not using it for a permitted purpose — he was protecting it. Moving an object to protect it from damage or theft is what is called *mechama l'tzel*, moving from sun to shade, and that is not permitted even for a *kli shemelachto l'issur*. On this reading, Rashi's concern is precisely with that scenario: since the movement here is protective rather than functional, he must find a way to permit the *shofar* through reclassification. The *chatzotzrot*, not being reclassifiable as *l'heter*, remains forbidden even for protective movement. Tosafot, by contrast, appear to understand the case as involving movement for a permitted purpose — and therefore must find a different way to distinguish the *chatzotzrot*, namely by treating it as completely set aside.

### Moving Objects

To summarize the underlying principles: a *kli shemelachto l'issur* may be moved if one needs to use it (for a permitted purpose) or if one needs its place. It may not be moved purely to protect it from damage — that is the prohibition of *mechama l'tzel*. A *kli shemelachto l'heter* may be moved even to protect it. According to Tosafot, Rabbi Shimon categorizes both *shofar* and *chatzotzrot* as *kli shemelachto l'issur* — he does not acknowledge the more severe category into which Tosafot place the *chatzotzrot* — and therefore both may be moved for a *tzorech heter*. They may not be moved *mechama l'tzel*, but for any permitted use they are available. Rabbi Nechemya, at the other extreme, holds that objects may only be moved for their designated purpose, and since the designated purpose of both instruments is prohibited on *Shabbat*, neither may be moved at all. Tosafot add a clarification: even Rabbi Shimon, who permits moving both instruments for a permitted use, would still forbid moving them *mechama l'tzel* — to protect them for their own sake.

## Name Changes

### Shofar Names

Having completed the *muktzah* analysis, the Gemara pivots to a terminological observation. The opening *braita* stated that one may not carry "*lo et hashofar v'lo et hachatzotzrot*" — neither the *shofar* nor the *chatzotzrot*. Rashi notes that the order of the listing is puzzling: if the *shofar* is the more usable and presumably less *muktzah* of the two, why mention the prohibition on *shofar* first, since the prohibition on *chatzotzrot* is even more obvious? This apparent inversion provides the occasion for the Gemara to introduce a significant historical fact: the names of these two instruments were reversed in popular usage after the destruction of the *Beit Hamikdash*. What was originally called a *shofar* — the animal horn — began to be called *chatzotzrot*, and what was originally called *chatzotzrot* — the fashioned metal trumpet — began to be called *shofar*. Rav Chisda states this explicitly: among the three things whose names were interchanged after the *churban*, the first pair is *shofar* and *chatzotzrot*.

This phenomenon is not merely antiquarian. The *Sheiltos*, a Gaonic work composed approximately in the seventh century, demonstrates the real-world effect of this shift. When discussing the *mitzvah* of *tekiat shofar*, the *Sheiltos* writes that Jews are obligated to sound the *chatzotzrot* on Rosh Hashanah — using the post-*churban* vernacular in which the word *chatzotzrot* had come to denote what we call the *shofar*.

### Rosh Hashanah

The Gemara asks *l'mai nafka mina* — what practical difference does this name reversal make? The answer: for the *shofar* of Rosh Hashanah. If someone asks what instrument to obtain for the *mitzvah*, one must be careful to use the vernacular correctly. If speaking in the biblical idiom and telling someone to obtain a *chatzotzrot*, he may go and purchase a metal trumpet — and fulfill no *mitzvah* at all. Knowing what the current vernacular means is essential for directing people to the correct instrument.

## Other Name Shifts

### Lulav Plants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עֲרָבָה״ — ״צַפְצָפָה״, ״צַפְצָפָה״ — ״עֲרָבָה״. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינָּה? לְלוּלָב.
>
> **Translation:** The second object whose name was changed: That which was called **willow [*arava*]** was called in later generations ***tzaftzafa*,** and that which was called ***tzaftzafa* was** called **willow.** Here too the Gemara asks: **What is the** practical halakhic **difference** that emerges from the name change? The Gemara answers: With regard **to** the mitzva of the four species, referred to by the name of one of the species, as taking the **palm branch,** as one of the four species is a willow branch, not a *tzaftzafa*.

The Gemara continues with two additional examples from Rav Chisda's list of inverted names. The *aravah* — the willow used for the *lulav* set, the *arvei nachal* — was subsequently called *tziptzafah*, and the *tziptzafah* — a plant that is not valid for the *lulav* — was subsequently called *aravah*. Here too the *nafka mina* is practical: if someone asks which plant to use for his *lulav*, one must communicate in the current vernacular. Telling him to use an *aravah*, if that term now refers to the invalid species, will cause him to perform the *mitzvah* improperly.

### Furniture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״פָּתוּרָה״ — ״פָּתוּרְתָּא״, ״פָּתוּרְתָּא״ — ״פָּתוּרָה״. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינָּה? לְמִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The third item whose name was changed: That which was called ***petora***, originally meaning a large table, was called in later generations ***petorata*, and that which was called *petorata*, orginally meaning a small table, was called *petora*** in later generations. The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical halakhic **difference** that emerges from the change of name? The Gemara answers: With regard **to** the laws of **buying and selling.** A person who orders a *petora* should know that he ordered a small table and not a large one.

The third item in Rav Chisda's list involves furniture. The words *psora* and *psorta* — denoting a large table and a small table respectively — were reversed in common usage. The *nafka mina* here is for *mikach u'mimkar*, commercial transactions. If a buyer orders a *psora* and the seller understands it to mean the opposite size, the transaction is compromised. One must know the current vernacular to conduct business accurately.

### Stomach Parts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אַף אָנוּ נֹאמַר: ״הוּבְלִילָא״ — ״בֵּי כָסֵי״, ״בֵּי כָסֵי״ — ״הוּבְלִילָא״. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינָּה — לְמַחַט שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת בְּעוֹבִי בֵּית הַכּוֹסוֹת, דְּמִצַּד אֶחָד כְּשֵׁירָה, וּמִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִים טְרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: We too shall speak** and comment on changes in the meaning of terms in our generation. What was called ***huvlila*,** the first stomach of animals that chew their cud, is, in recent generations, called ***bei kasei*,** the name of the animal's second stomach. Similarly, what was once called in the past ***bei kasei*** is called ***huvlila*** in recent generations. **What is the** practical halakhic **difference** that emerges from this change of names? **With regard to a needle that is found in the thick** wall **of the second stomach.** In the *halakhot* of *tereifot*, one is prohibited to eat animals with a life expectancy of less than a year. It was established that if a needle punctured the wall of the second stomach **from** only **one side,** the animal is **kosher.** If the needle penetrated through the wall in a manner visible **from both sides,** the animal assumes the halakhic status of a ***tereifa*.** In the first stomach, even if the needle penetrated only one side of the wall, the animal assumes the halakhic status of a *tereifa*. Therefore, it is crucial to distinguish between the first and the second stomachs.

Abaye adds a further case to the list. The *hovlila* — one of the cow's stomachs, associated with the grinding of food and characterized by relatively thin walls — and the *beit hakosot* — a later stomach with thick, doubled walls separated by a layer of fat — had their names exchanged. The *nafka mina* here is for *treifot*. Specifically, the Gemara asks: what difference does it make if a needle is found *b'ovi beit hakosot*, in the thickness of the wall of the *beit hakosot*? The ruling is: if the needle has penetrated only one side of the double wall, the animal is *kesherah*, because the fat layer between the walls seals the puncture. Only if the needle has pierced through both walls entirely is the animal *treifah*. In the thinner-walled grinding stomach, by contrast, even a single penetration is a problem. The practical danger of the name reversal is obvious: a *posek* who confuses the two stomachs may declare an animal *treifah* when it is *kesherah*, or vice versa. Precision in terminology is thus a matter of *halachic* consequence.

### Geography

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אַף אָנוּ נֹאמַר: ״בָּבֶל״ — ״בּוֹרְסִיף״, ״בּוֹרְסִיף״ — ״בָּבֶל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: We too shall speak** of matters whose name changed over the generations. The city that, in biblical times, was called **Babylon** was called **Bursif** in later generations, and **Bursif** was called **Babylon** in later generations.

Rav Ashi appends a final case: *Bavel* and *Borsif* — two geographic names that were interchanged. What had been called *Bavel* came to be called *Borsif*, and vice versa. The *nafka mina* is for *gittei nashim*, bills of divorce. The opening *mishnah* of *Gittin* establishes that an agent bringing a *get* from *medinat hayam* — distant lands across the sea — must declare "*bifnai nichtav u'bifnai nechtam*," that the document was written and signed in his presence, because we cannot assume that proper *halachic* procedure was followed in distant locales. However, the Gemara elsewhere clarifies that *Bavel* is exempt from this requirement because its scholars were sufficiently expert in *hilchot gitin*. Knowing which territory is considered *Bavel* and which is not is therefore crucial: an agent arriving from what is now called *Borsif* must know whether that name corresponds to the *Bavel* of the exemption or not.

Rashi offers a second explanation as well: since a *get* must record the names of places, one must use accurate contemporary terminology. Tosafot, however, take strong exception to this second explanation. Tosafot argue that *Bavel* is not a city but a country, and the name of a country (*shem medina*) is not written in a *get* — only the city is recorded. This raises a broader question relevant to *get* practice across the generations: whether the name of a state or province constitutes a *shem medina* that should be omitted, or whether it serves as legitimate identifying information. That question will require attention when the tractates of *Gittin* and *Ketubot* are reached.

With this digression concluded, the Gemara declares *hadran alach bama madlikin* — the second *perek* is complete.

## Third Perek Intro

### Perek Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כִּירָה שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהָ בְּקַשׁ וּבִגְבָבָא — נוֹתְנִים עָלֶיהָ תַּבְשִׁיל. בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעֵצִים — לֹא יִתֵּן עַד שֶׁיִּגְרוֹף, אוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן אֶת הָאֵפֶר. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: חַמִּין, אֲבָל לֹא תַּבְשִׁיל. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: חַמִּין וְתַבְשִׁיל. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נוֹטְלִין, אֲבָל לֹא מַחֲזִירִין. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף מַחֲזִירִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a stove that was lit** on Shabbat eve **with straw** or **with rakings,** scraps collected from the field, one may **place** a pot of **cooked food atop it** on Shabbat. The fire in this stove was certainly extinguished while it was still day, as both straw and rakings are materials that burn quickly. However, if the stove was lit **with pomace,** pulp that remains from sesame seeds, olives, and the like after the oil is squeezed from them, **and** if it was lit **with wood,** one may **not place** a pot atop it on Shabbat **until he sweeps** the coals from the stove while it is still day **or until he places ashes** on the coals, so that the fire will not ignite on Shabbat. **Beit Shammai say:** Even after one has swept away the coals, it is only permitted to place **hot water** on it, as it is sufficiently hot and does not require additional cooking, **but not cooked food.** Since, in general, one prefers that food will cook more, there is concern lest he come to ignite the fire by stoking the coals. **And Beit Hillel say:** Both **hot water and cooked food** may be placed. **Beit Shammai say: One may remove** a pot from the stove on Shabbat **but** may **not return** it. **And Beit Hillel say:** One may **even return it.**

The transition to the third *perek* follows naturally from the preceding material. The second *perek* dealt with the sequence of *erev Shabbat* preparations — the lighting of candles and the *shofar* blasts heralding *Shabbat*. One of the *braitot* cited there described how, at the third blast, people would remove food from the fire and light the candles — indicating that fire management and candle lighting occurred at approximately the same moment. The third *perek* picks up that thread and addresses the laws governing food left on or returned to the fire as *Shabbat* enters.

### Fire Management

This chapter also connects back to a discussion at the end of the first *perek*, where Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai debated the permissibility of various *erev Shabbat* activities that extend into *Shabbat* itself. While Beit Hillel generally permits work begun before *Shabbat* to continue into *Shabbat*, food on the fire presents a special concern: the anxiety over whether food is cooking properly may lead a person to stir the coals — an act that constitutes a prohibited *melachah*. The third *perek* will develop this concern in detail and ultimately arrive at the *halachic* framework underlying the contemporary practice of using a *blech*.

---

## Kirah & Fire

### Kirah Vessel

The *mishnah* opens: *kira she-hisikuha b'kash u've-givah* — a *kirah* that was heated with straw and stubble. The *kirah* was a large pottery vessel, fixed in place, that functioned as a cooking apparatus. Fire was built inside the vessel, and a cooking pot was placed on the rim, suspended over the heat. The concentrated space directed heat upward efficiently. Rashi confirms this understanding of the vessel's structure.

### Straw Fuel

When the *kirah* is fueled with straw (*kash*) and stubble (*givah*), the fuel burns quickly and leaves no lasting coals. Since the entire concern of this *mishnah* is that a person may come to stir the coals on *Shabbat*, a fire that produces no sustained coals eliminates the concern entirely. Therefore, in such a case, *notnin aleha tavshil* — one may place a cooked dish upon it before *Shabbat* without any additional condition.

### Wood Fuel

When, however, the *kirah* is fueled with *gefes* — which Rashi identifies as a remnant of something like pressed seeds — or with actual wood (*eitzim*), the situation changes. These fuels produce genuine, lasting coals that retain significant heat and continue to burn well into the cooking period. In this case, the concern that a person will stir the coals on *Shabbat* is real.

### Ash Coverage

Faced with lasting coals, the *mishnah* imposes a requirement: *lo yiten ad she-yigrof o ad she-yiten et ha-eifer* — one may not place food on the *kirah* until one either sweeps out the coals entirely or covers them with ashes. Sweeping out leaves only residual heat with no live coals to stir. Covering with ashes — *ketumah* — effectively suppresses the fire and removes the practical temptation to stir. It is worth noting that the *mishnah*'s plain language might suggest that the fire must be entirely eliminated. This would pose an obvious difficulty for the contemporary practice of maintaining a burner covered by a *blech*. The Gemara will address this: the *eifer* covering need not extinguish the fire completely. What it must do is serve as a *heker* — a marker — that modifies the cooking context. This in turn provides the conceptual basis for treating the *blech* as a functional equivalent of the ash covering: it somewhat reduces heat, it serves as a visual reminder, and it is sufficient even though the flame beneath continues to burn.

---

## Shammai v. Hillel

### Shammai Water

The *mishnah* now introduces the first of two debates between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. Even once the conditions of sweeping out or covering with ashes have been met, Beit Shammai holds: *chamin aval lo tavshil* — hot water may be left on the *kirah*, but not a *tavshil*, a cooked dish. The scope of Beit Shammai's leniency is limited to water alone.

### Anxiety Concern

The rationale underlying Beit Shammai's distinction is the degree of anxiety that different foods produce. Water is simple: it heats, it boils, and there is no ongoing cooking process to monitor. One is not tempted to intervene, check, or adjust. A *tavshil* — any dish that is actually cooking — involves a prolonged, anxiety-laden process. Is it cooking evenly? Has it burned? Is it too watery? These concerns draw a person's attention and may lead him to stir the coals to raise the heat. It is this anticipatory anxiety that Beit Shammai seeks to preempt.

### Hillel Both

Beit Hillel disagrees: *chamin v'tavshil* — both hot water and cooked dishes may be left on the *kirah* under these conditions. The *halachah* follows Beit Hillel. Beit Hillel's position does not mean that any food in any state of preparation may simply be left on a roaring fire; the prior conditions of sweeping or covering still apply. But once those conditions are met, Beit Hillel imposes no further distinction based on what is being cooked.

---

## Remove & Return

### Shammai Return

The *mishnah* then records a second debate: *Beit Shammai omrim notlin aval lo machzirin* — Beit Shammai says one may remove food from the fire on *Shabbat* but may not return it. Even the hot water that Beit Shammai permitted to remain on the *kirah* before *Shabbat* — once removed, it may not be replaced.

### Hillel Return

Beit Hillel responds: *af machzirin* — one may even return food to the fire. This position, too, follows Beit Hillel, and it becomes the *halachic* foundation for the contemporary practice of removing and replacing a pot of *cholent* on *Shabbat* — subject, as the Gemara will make clear, to a series of carefully defined conditions.

## Sheiya v. Chazara

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הַאי ״לֹא יִתֵּן״ — לֹא יַחֲזִיר הוּא, אֲבָל לְשַׁהוֹת — מְשַׁהִין, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ גָּרוּף וְאֵינוֹ קָטוּם. וּמַנִּי — חֲנַנְיָה הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, חֲנַנְיָה אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא כְּמַאֲכָל בֶּן דְּרוּסַאי — מוּתָּר לְשַׁהוֹתוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי כִּירָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ גָּרוּף וְאֵינוֹ קָטוּם. אוֹ דִילְמָא — לְשַׁהוֹת תְּנַן, וְאִי גָרוּף וְקָטוּם — אֵין, אִי לָא — לָא, וְכָל שֶׁכֵּן לְהַחֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The students **raised a dilemma** with regard to the explanation of the mishna. **That** which we learned in the mishna: One may **not place, does it** mean that **one may not return** a pot that he took off the fire and wishes to return on Shabbat; **however, to leave** the pot from Shabbat eve into Shabbat, **one may leave** it **even though** this stove **is not swept** of its coals **and** its coals **are not covered with ashes? And,** according to this, **whose** opinion **is it** in this mishna? **It** is the opinion of **Ḥananya. As it was taught** in a *baraita*, **Ḥananya says: Any** food **that** has already been cooked to the extent **of the food of ben Drosai,** who would only cook his food the minimum amount necessary, **one is permitted to leave it atop a stove** on Shabbat **even though** the stove **is not swept and not covered with ashes. Or perhaps,** that which **we learned** in the mishna: One may not place, means one may not **leave** it on the fire from Shabbat eve. **And if** the coals in the stove were **swept** or **covered with ashes, yes,** one may leave the pot on the stove. **And if not, no,** one may not leave it, **and all the more so** one may not **return** it to the stove on Shabbat under any circumstances.

The Gemara now poses its central question regarding the *mishnah*'s opening clause. When the *mishnah* says that one may not place food on the *kirah* unless it has been swept out or covered — does this refer to *sheiya* (leaving food on the fire from before *Shabbat*) or to *chazara* (returning food to the fire on *Shabbat* after it has been removed)? The question is not merely philological. It determines how stringent the requirement of *grufa u'ketuma* actually is and — critically — whether there exists a category of *sheiya* that is entirely exempt from that requirement.

### Chananya View

The Gemara introduces the opinion of Chananya: *kol she-hu k'ma'achal Ben Drusai mutar le-shahoto al gabei kirah af al pi she-eino garuf ve-katum* — any food that has reached the stage of *ma'achal Ben Drusai* may be left on the *kirah* even if the fire has not been swept out and even if no ashes have been placed. *Ma'achal Ben Drusai* refers to food that has been cooked to approximately a third or half of full doneness — the name derives from a historical figure notorious for eating food in a partially cooked state. The reasoning behind Chananya's leniency is that once food has reached this stage, it is already technically edible. The cook's anxiety diminishes substantially: the food will not be ruined if left alone, and there is far less temptation to intervene. Chananya therefore holds that the concern for stirring does not apply at this stage, and no suppression of the fire is required for *sheiya*.

If Chananya is correct, the *mishnah*'s demand for *grufa u'ketuma* applies only to *chazara* — returning the food to the fire — not to *sheiya*. The Gemara asks whether that is indeed how the *mishnah* should be read.

### Ashkenazi/Sefardi

Tosafot, at the top of 37a, state their position directly: *mishum d'kaima lan k'Chananya* — because we rule like Chananya, the Gemara is compelled to read the *mishnah* in a way consistent with his view, even though the plain sense of the *mishnah* clearly speaks of *sheiya*. Tosafot acknowledge that this reading of the *mishnah* is forced (*dochik*), but maintain it because the *halachah* follows Chananya.

This is the hinge point of the Ashkenazi-Sefardi divide on the *blech*. Ashkenazim rule like Chananya. The practical consequence is that once food is already a third or half cooked before *Shabbat*, one does not require a *blech* merely to leave it on the fire — *sheiya* is permitted without *grufa u'ketuma* at that stage. A *blech* is required only for *chazara* — if one intends to remove the food and return it during *Shabbat*. Sefardim do not rule like Chananya and therefore require *grufa u'ketuma* — implemented as a *blech* — for *sheiya* as well.

### Personal Experience

The practical consequence of the Ashkenazi position is that for food which is well on its way to being cooked before *Shabbat*, the *blech* is strictly speaking not required for *sheiya*. The *Rama* notes that it is preferable to be stringent *l'chatchilah*, but the underlying Ashkenazi *halachah* does not require it. The requirement of a *blech* for Ashkenazim is primarily the *chazara* concern — placing food back on the fire during *Shabbat* — which carries a higher level of scrutiny for independent reasons.

## Chazara Stringency

### Stirring Risk

Why does *chazara* generate a stricter standard than *sheiya*, even according to Chananya who is relatively lenient? Two explanations are offered.

The first: the act of handling the pot heightens the risk of stirring. When food has simply been sitting on the fire since before *Shabbat*, one is in a hands-off posture — there is no reason to touch anything. But once a person picks up the pot, moves it, sets it down, and is in the process of placing it back on the fire, he is already actively engaged with the cooking apparatus. In that moment of active handling, it is far easier to think — the flame could be a little higher — and to reach for the coals. The physical engagement creates an elevated likelihood of the prohibited act.

### Cooking Appearance

The second explanation: returning food to a fire is experientially indistinguishable from the act of cooking. The Gemara uses the phrase *mechzi k'mevashel* — it appears like cooking. Regardless of one's subjective intent, the observable act of placing a pot onto a fire on *Shabbat* looks, feels, and functions like an act of cooking, and that appearance itself is something the Rabbis sought to prevent.

### Modern Devices

These two rationales yield different practical outcomes in modern contexts. Consider a hotplate or stovetop with adjustable knobs. If the concern behind the stringency of *chazara* is the risk of stirring — or in modern terms, the risk of adjusting the flame — then even a covered knob that cannot be easily accessed would address that concern, since there is no realistic avenue to inadvertently adjust the heat. If, however, the concern is *mechzi k'mevashel* — the appearance of cooking — then the question shifts: does anyone cook on a particular device? A hot plate, for example, might not evoke the appearance of cooking in the same way a stovetop does. Conversely, consider a crock pot with its knobs fully covered. The adjustment concern is neutralized, but a crock pot is a device people routinely use for cooking, so the *mechzi k'mevashel* concern may well apply. The two rationales thus generate a genuine *nafka minah* in assessing these modern instruments.

## Gemara Reading

### Chazara Primary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְהַחֲזִיר תְּנַן, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: כִּירָה שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהָ בְּקַשׁ וּבִגְבָבָא — מַחֲזִירִין עָלֶיהָ תַּבְשִׁיל. בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעֵצִים — לֹא יַחֲזִיר עַד שֶׁיִּגְרוֹף אוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן אֵפֶר. וּמָה הֵן מַחֲזִירִין? — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: חַמִּין, אֲבָל לֹא תַּבְשִׁיל. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: חַמִּין וְתַבְשִׁיל. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נוֹטְלִין אֲבָל לֹא מַחֲזִירִין. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף מַחֲזִירִין. הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **However, if you say** that when **we learned** in the mishna that one may not place, it means that it is prohibited **to return** it, then the mishna **is teaching as follows: A stove that was lit with straw** or **with rakings, one may return cooked food onto it.** If it was lit **with pomace** or **with wood, one may not return** cooked food to it **until one sweeps** the coals **out** while it is still day **or until one places ashes** on them. **And what may they return? Beit Shammai say: Hot water but not cooked food. And Beit Hillel say:** Both **hot water and cooked food. Beit Shammai say: One may remove but may not return. And Beit Hillel say: One may even return.** If in the first section the question of what may be returned was already addressed, **why do I need this additional** dispute in the second section? The gist of Beit Shammai's statement that cooked food may not be returned to the stove is that one may remove but may not return. Apparently, the mishna can only be understood in accordance with the first explanation. The first clause discusses leaving and the latter clause discusses returning.

The Gemara now works through the alternative reading of the *mishnah* — namely, that its opening clause about *grufa u'ketuma* refers primarily to *chazara*. This is the reading that the Gemara pursues in order to be consistent with Chananya.

### Missing Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ לְהַחֲזִיר תְּנַן, וְחַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: כִּירָה שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהָ בְּקַשׁ וּבִגְבָבָא — מַחֲזִירִין עָלֶיהָ תַּבְשִׁיל, בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעֵצִים — לֹא יַחֲזִיר עַד שֶׁיִּגְרוֹף אוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן אֶת הָאֵפֶר. אֲבָל לְשַׁהוֹת — מְשַׁהִין אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ גָּרוּף וְאֵינוֹ קָטוּם. וּמָה הֵן מְשַׁהִין? — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: חַמִּין, אֲבָל לֹא תַּבְשִׁיל. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: חַמִּין וְתַבְשִׁיל. וְהָךְ חֲזָרָה דַּאֲמַרִי לָךְ, לָאו דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל הִיא, אֶלָּא מַחֲלוֹקֶת בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נוֹטְלִין וְלֹא מַחֲזִירִין. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף מַחֲזִירִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof. **Actually, you can say** that in the first clause of the mishna **we learned to return and** the mishna is **incomplete.** A clause must be added to the mishna, **and it teaches the following:** With regard to **a stove that was lit with straw** or **rakings, one may return** a pot of **cooked food to it.** If it was lit **with pomace** or **with wood, one may not return** a pot to it **until one sweeps** the coals out while it is still day **or until one covers** the coals **with ashes. However, to leave** the pot on the flame on Shabbat, **one may leave it, even though it is not swept and not covered with ashes.** Through this addition, the continuation of the dispute can be understood as follows: **And what may they leave? Beit Shammai say: Hot water but not cooked food. And Beit Hillel say:** Both **hot water and cooked food.** Furthermore: **And that return that I said to you** at the start of the mishna **is not according to everyone. Rather,** it too **is** subject to **a dispute** between **Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, as Beit Shammai say: One may remove but not return. And Beit Hillel say: One may even return.** The dilemma with regard to the interpretation of the mishna has not been resolved.

To sustain this reading, the Gemara invokes the principle of *chasurai mechasra* — the *mishnah* is missing words and must be supplemented. The *mishnah* as it stands does not make the *sheiya* permission explicit; it must be inserted as an implicit middle section of the text. Specifically, the permission to leave food on the fire without sweeping — Chananya's leniency — is treated as an unstated assumption embedded in the *mishnah* between its first and final sections.

### Three Stages

Under this reading, the *mishnah* unfolds in three stages. First, the *mishnah* establishes the conditions for *chazara*: if the *kirah* was fueled with straw and stubble, one may return food to it; if with wood, one may not return food until the fire has been swept out or covered. Second — the inserted section — the *mishnah* implies a separate rule about *sheiya*: leaving food on is permitted even without sweeping, provided the food has reached the level of Chananya's *ma'achal Ben Drusai*. What may one leave on? That is a debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel: water only, or also cooked foods. Third, the *mishnah* closes by clarifying that the permission for *chazara* stated in the first section is itself contested: Beit Shammai holds one may remove but not return; Beit Hillel holds one may even return. On this reading, the middle section — the debate about water versus *tavshil* — concerns *sheiya* without sweeping, while the final debate of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel concerns *chazara*.

### Complexity

This is admittedly a strained reading. The plain sense of the *mishnah* proceeds naturally from conditions for leaving food on (*sheiya*), to what may be left on (the Beit Shammai/Beit Hillel debate about water versus *tavshil*), to whether removed food may be returned (*chazara*). Tosafot acknowledge this openly: the Gemara is pressing the text into an unnatural shape *mishum d'kaima lan k'Chananya* — because the *halachah* has been established in accordance with Chananya, and the *mishnah* must be reconciled with that ruling. The reading is technically possible but requires the interpolation of unstated content, which is why the Gemara frames it under the rubric of *chasurai mechasra*.
