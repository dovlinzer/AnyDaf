# Makkot 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol Death

### Intro & Gmar Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ מֵת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל – הֲרֵי זֶה אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה. אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ מֵת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל וּמִינּוּ אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו וּלְאַחַר מִכֵּן נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ – חוֹזֵר בְּמִיתָתוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁנִי. נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ בְּלֹא כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְהַהוֹרֵג כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁהָרַג – אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא מִשָּׁם לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If, after the unintentional murderer's verdict was decided and he was sentenced to exile, the High Priest died, he is not exiled, as the death of the High Priest exempts him from exile. If it was before his verdict was decided that the High Priest died and they appointed another in his place, and thereafter his verdict was decided, he returns from exile with the death of the second High Priest. If the verdict of a murderer was decided at a time when there was no High Priest, and likewise in the cases of one who unintentionally killed a High Priest and in the case of a High Priest who killed unintentionally, the unintentional murderer never leaves the city of refuge.

The *gemara* on daf 12a picks up immediately following the *mishna* on 11b, which addresses the question of who counts as the *kohen gadol* for purposes of releasing the *rotzeach* from the *ir miklat*. The basic rule established by the *mishna* is that the determining moment is *gmar din* — the moment of final sentencing. Whoever holds the office of *kohen gadol* at the time the judgment is passed is the *kohen gadol* whose death eventually frees the exile. This holds even if the *kohen gadol* dies before the convicted person has actually traveled to the *ir miklat*: the connection is forged at *gmar din*, not at the moment of arrival. Conversely, if there is no *kohen gadol* at the moment of *gmar din*, the exile is permanent — the person never goes free. And if there are multiple changes in the office, what matters is who held it at *gmar din*.

### Kal Vachomer & Kapara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: קַל וָחוֹמֶר, וּמָה מִי שֶׁגָּלָה כְּבָר – יָצָא עַכְשָׁיו, מִי שֶׁלֹּא גָּלָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יִגְלֶה? וְדִלְמָא: הַאי דִּגְלָה אִיכַּפַּר לֵיהּ, הַאי דְּלָא גְּלָה לָא! מִידֵּי גָּלוּת קָא מְכַפְּרָא? מִיתַת כֹּהֵן הוּא דִּמְכַפְּרָא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: If after the unintentional murderer's verdict was decided and he was sentenced to exile, the High Priest died, the unintentional murderer is not exiled. The Gemara asks: What is the reason for this? Abaye says: It is derived through an *a fortiori* inference: If one who was already exiled now emerges with the death of this High Priest, with regard to one who was not yet exiled, is it not right that he should not be exiled? The Gemara rejects this reasoning: And perhaps with regard to this one, who was already exiled, his sin was atoned for by his exile, and therefore the death of the High Priest facilitates his return, but that one, who was not yet exiled, no, his sin was not atoned for and the death of the High Priest should not prevent his exile. The Gemara rebuts: Is it his exile that atones for his sin? It is the death of the High Priest that atones for his sin, and the High Priest died.

The *gemara* opens by asking *mai ta'ama* — what is the underlying rationale for the ruling that if the *kohen gadol* dies even before the convicted person has entered the *ir miklat*, he nonetheless goes free? Abaye proposes a *kal vachomer*: if someone who has already been in exile goes free upon the *kohen gadol*'s death, surely someone who has not yet entered exile at all — who is in a sense already partway out — should likewise go free.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this *kal vachomer*. One might argue precisely the reverse: the person who went into exile received some measure of *kapara* through the experience of exile itself, whereas the person who never entered *galus* has not yet undergone that atonement. On this reading, the exile who has already experienced *galus* has earned his release, but the person who has not yet entered cannot benefit from the same logic.

The *gemara* then delivers what appears to be a decisive clarification, almost as a throwaway remark: *midi galus kamchapra? Misas kohen hi de'mechaphra* — do you think it is the *galus* that atones? No. It is the death of the *kohen* that atones. This statement raises a significant difficulty, because earlier in the *masechet* the *gemara* itself speaks explicitly about exile as a form of *kapara*, stating that the unworthy go into exile so that it serves as atonement for them.

### Tosafot Kapara

*Tosafot* addresses this tension directly, citing the passage at the beginning of the *masechet* that treats *galus* as *kapara*: *alma galus mechaper*. *Tosafot* asks: surely the exile itself does atone — how then can the *gemara* dismiss this? *Tosafot* answers by distinguishing between two different forms of *kapara*. There is the *kapara* that atones for the underlying sin of negligent homicide — and that is indeed the experience of exile. But there is a separate question of what serves as the release mechanism, what "atones" in the sense of enabling the person to be freed from the obligation of exile altogether. That function is performed by the death of the *kohen gadol*. *Tosafot* phrases this as *kapara liftur migalus* — the atonement that frees one from exile — and says it is the *misas kohen gadol* that accomplishes this, even for someone who never entered exile at all.

The result is a striking and somewhat ironic situation: the person who goes free before ever entering the *ir miklat* is released from the *chiyuv* of exile through the *kohen gadol*'s death, yet never undergoes the experiential *kapara* of *galus* that would atone for the original act of negligence. The *gemara* says: *de'misas kohen gadol to'il liptoro migalus m'kal vachomer* — the death of the *kohen gadol* suffices to free him from exile by means of *kal vachomer*. These are two distinct processes operating in parallel.

### Kohen Appointed Mid-Trial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ וְכוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיָשַׁב בָּהּ עַד מוֹת הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל אֲשֶׁר מָשַׁח אֹתוֹ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַקֹּדֶשׁ״, וְכִי הוּא מוֹשְׁחוֹ? אֶלָּא: זֶה שֶׁנִּמְשַׁח בְּיָמָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: If it was before his verdict was decided that the High Priest died and they appointed another in his place, and thereafter his verdict was decided, he returns with the death of the second High Priest. The Gemara asks: From where are these matters derived? Rav Kahana said that they are derived from a verse, as the verse states: "And he shall dwell there until the death of the High Priest, whom he anointed with the sacred oil" (Numbers 35:25). Now is it the unintentional murderer who anoints the High Priest? Rather, the reference is to that High Priest who was anointed during his days, after he committed the unintentional murder.

The *mishna* addresses a further case: what if a new *kohen gadol* was appointed during the course of the trial proceedings themselves? The rule is that it is the *kohen gadol* who holds office at the moment of *gmar din* who determines the exile — even if he was appointed only partway through the proceedings.

### Anointed in His Day

Rav Kahana derives this from a verse: *v'yashav bah ad mos hakohen hagadol asher mashach oso b'shemen hakodesh* — he shall dwell in the *ir miklat* until the death of the *kohen gadol* who anointed him with the holy oil. The *gemara* asks: *v'chi hu meshacho?* Did the convicted person actually anoint the *kohen gadol*? Obviously not. Rather, says the *gemara*, *asher mashach* means *shenimshach b'yamav* — who was anointed during his time, that is, during the period of the exile's lifetime or, more precisely, during the relevant period of proceedings. This reading encompasses even a *kohen gadol* who was anointed partway through the trial, since the verse attaches the release to whoever held anointed office at the moment of *gmar din*.

### Kohen Responsibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֶעְבַּד? הָיָה לוֹ לְבַקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים שֶׁיִּגְמוֹר דִּינוֹ לִזְכוּת, וְלֹא בִּיקֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why is his return home dependent on the death of the second High Priest? Earlier (11a), the Gemara explained that the High Priest bears a share of the responsibility for unintentional murderers, as he should have pleaded for mercy for his generation and he did not do so. In this case, as the High Priest in question was appointed only after the murder transpired, what could he have done to prevent the unintentional murder? The Gemara answers: He should have pleaded for mercy that the verdict of the unintentional murderer would be decided by the court favorably, so that he would not be sentenced to exile, and he did not plead.

The *gemara* then raises a conceptual difficulty regarding this newly appointed *kohen gadol*. Part of the underlying logic of the institution — that the *kohen gadol*'s death frees the exile — has to do with the *kohen gadol* bearing some degree of responsibility for preventable deaths in the society under his watch. Leaders of the religious establishment are responsible for cultivating a society in which such tragedies do not occur. But if this *kohen gadol* was only appointed during the trial proceedings, what responsibility does he bear for the original death?

The *gemara* answers: *mah hava lei l'meivad? Haya lo l'vakesh rachamim sheyigmor dino l'zechut v'lo vikesh* — what should he have done? He should have prayed that the judgment be concluded in the person's favor, and he failed to do so. This is admittedly difficult to parse. Even if the convicted person genuinely deserved to be exiled, the *kohen gadol*'s responsibility is framed as his failure to pray on his behalf. The *gemara* never fully resolves the philosophical tension here. It is perhaps best understood in light of the broader principle, noted by a participant in the discussion, of *mitat tzadikim mechaperet* — the death of the righteous atones — and the *kohen gadol*'s unique status as the head of the religious establishment, such that his death carries atoning power regardless of the specific circumstances of his appointment.

## Burial & Bones (II)

### Death Pre-Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נָקְטִינַן: נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ וָמֵת – מוֹלִיכִין אֶת עַצְמוֹתָיו לְשָׁם, דִּכְתִיב: ״לָשׁוּב לָשֶׁבֶת בָּאָרֶץ עַד מוֹת הַכֹּהֵן״, וְאֵיזֶהוּ יְשִׁיבָה שֶׁהִיא בָּאָרֶץ – הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זוֹ קְבוּרָה. תָּנָא: מֵת קוֹדֶם שֶׁמֵּת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, מוֹלִיכִין עַצְמוֹתָיו עַל קִבְרֵי אֲבוֹתָיו, דִּכְתִיב: ״יָשׁוּב הָרֹצֵחַ אֶל אֶרֶץ אֲחֻזָּתוֹ״, אֵיזֶהוּ יְשִׁיבָה שֶׁהִיא בְּאֶרֶץ אֲחוּזָּתוֹ הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זוֹ קְבוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said: We have a tradition that with regard to an unintentional murderer whose verdict was decided and who was sentenced to exile, and he died before he was exiled to the city of refuge, one transports his bones to the city of refuge, and buries him there, as it is written: "To return and dwell in the land until the death of the priest" (Numbers 35:32). And what is the dwelling that is in the land? You must say it is referring to his burial. A Sage taught: If an unintentional murderer died in a city of refuge before the High Priest died, one transports his bones to the graves of his ancestors after the High Priest dies, as it is written: "The murderer shall return to his ancestral land" (Numbers 35:28). What is the dwelling that is taking place in his ancestral land? You must say it is his burial.

Abaye states: *naktinan*, we take it as an established principle, that if a person received *gmar din* and then died before going to the *ir miklat*, the question arises of what is to be done with his body.

### Burial in Ir Miklat

Abaye rules: *molichin et atzmotav l'sham* — his bones are brought to an *ir miklat* and he is buried there. The source is the verse *lashuv lashevet ba'aretz ad mot hakohen* — to return to dwell in the land until the *kohen* dies. The *gemara* reads *lashevet ba'aretz* — literally "to dwell in the land," meaning under the land — as a reference to burial. The *mishna* itself (readable in the narrow line within the *gemara* text) states explicitly: *sham tei dirato, sham tei misato, sham tei kevurato* — there is his dwelling, there is his death, there is his burial. Burial in the *ir miklat* is thus not incidental but part of the *din* of exile itself. Even a person who never lived in the *ir miklat* is subject to this aspect of the *din* that was passed upon him.

### Disinterment & Return

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: if the exile died while residing in the *ir miklat* — before the *kohen gadol* died — and was buried there, and subsequently the *kohen gadol* died, the verse states *yashuv harotzeach el eretz achuzato* — the murderer shall return to the land of his inheritance. The *gemara* interprets *eizehu yeshuv she'hi eretz achuzato* through the same lens: *hevei omer zu kevurah* — this "return" refers to burial in his ancestral plot.

The Rambam's reading of this passage is notably different from a more surface-level reading. Rather than reading the phrase about carrying the bones *al kivrei avotav* as a brief symbolic visit followed by reburial in the *ir miklat*, the Rambam understands the sequence as follows: the person was buried in the *ir miklat* during the period of exile; when the *kohen gadol* died, the exile ended retroactively, and at that point the bones are disinterred and reinterred with his fathers in his ancestral land. Just as a living exile would physically return upon the *kohen gadol*'s death, his bones are accorded the same return. The verse *yashuv harotzeach* applies equally to bones as to a living person, enabling his remains ultimately to rest in his ancestral burial place.

## Pasul Kohen

### Ben Grusha Discovered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ, וְנַעֲשָׂה כֹּהֵן בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא. חַד אוֹמֵר: מֵתָה כְּהוּנָּה. וְחַד אוֹמֵר: בָּטְלָה כְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a dispute with regard to a case where the murderer's verdict was decided, i.e., he was sentenced to exile, and the High Priest filling the position at the time was deemed the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*, and the High Priest was thereby disqualified from the priesthood. Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa disagree with regard to this matter. One says: The priesthood died, i.e., it is as though the High Priest died, and all exiles return home from the city of refuge. And the other one says: The priesthood is voided, i.e., it is as though there was no High Priest filling the position during that period, and therefore, the exiles may never leave the city of refuge.

A new case is introduced: *nigmar dino*, a person received his *gmar din*, with a particular individual serving as *kohen gadol* — call him Reuven. Reuven's death is thus the event that will eventually free this exile. Subsequently, it is discovered that Reuven's mother was in fact a *grusha*, rendering him a *chalal* — a *pasul kohen* who was never halachically fit for the *kehunah* in the first place. The question is whether, in retrospect, there was any valid *kohen gadol* at the moment of *gmar din*, and what the consequences are for the exile.

### Misas/Batla Kehunah

Rabbi Ammi and Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha debate the matter. One holds *meita kehunah*: the discovery of his *pesul* is treated as the death of his *kehunah*, equivalent to the death of a *kohen gadol*, and the exile goes free. The other holds *batla kehunah*: retroactively his *kehunah* is nullified, meaning there was no valid *kohen gadol* at *gmar din*, and the exile remains permanently confined to the *ir miklat*.

### Korban Validity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ קָא מִיפַּלְגִי. דִּתְנַן: הָיָה עוֹמֵד וּמַקְרִיב עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וְנוֹדַע שֶׁהוּא בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל קׇרְבָּנוֹת שֶׁהִקְרִיב פְּסוּלִין, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מַכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: Let us say that these *amora'im*, Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa, disagree with regard to the issue that is the subject of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, as we learned in a mishna (*Terumot* 8:1): If a priest was standing and sacrificing offerings upon the altar, and it became known that he was the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*, and he was disqualified from the Temple service, Rabbi Eliezer says: All offerings that he sacrificed up to that point are not valid, as it is apparent that he is not and never was fit for Temple service, and Rabbi Yehoshua deems all offerings that he already sacrificed as valid.

The *gemara* wonders whether this debate maps onto an earlier tannaitic dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. The *mishna* (in a different context) describes a case where a *kohen* is found to be a *ben grusha* or *ben chalutzah* in the midst of bringing *korbanot* on the *mizbe'ach*. Rabbi Eliezer rules: *kol korbanot she'hikriv pesulin* — all the *korbanot* he ever brought are retroactively invalid, because he was never a *kohen* at all. Rabbi Yehoshua rules *machshir* — he validates them, holding that his invalidity takes effect only from the moment of discovery, not retroactively.

The connection to the *ir miklat* debate seems straightforward: the opinion that holds *meita kehunah* — treating discovery as equivalent to the *kohen gadol*'s death, freeing the exile — parallels Rabbi Yehoshua's position that the *kohen*'s status was valid until the moment of discovery. The opinion that holds *batla kehunah* — that retroactively there was never a valid *kohen gadol* — parallels Rabbi Eliezer's position that everything is retroactively annulled.

### Gemara Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְלִיגִי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מַאן דְּאָמַר מֵתָה – כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר בָּטְלָה – עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הָתָם דִּכְתִיב: ״בָּרֵךְ ה׳ חֵילוֹ וּפֹעַל יָדָיו תִּרְצֶה״ – אֲפִילּוּ חֲלָלִין שֶׁבּוֹ, אֲבָל הָכָא, אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מוֹדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this parallel: According to the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, everyone agrees that he holds that the priesthood is voided. When they disagree, it is according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua: The one who says the priesthood is dead holds in accordance with the straightforward understanding of the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua. And the one who says the priesthood is voided can also hold in accordance with his opinion and explain that Rabbi Yehoshua states his opinion only there, with regard to Temple service, as it is written: "Bless, God, his property [*ḥeilo*], and accept the work of his hands" (Deuteronomy 33:11). The term *ḥeilo* is interpreted homiletically to mean that even the offerings of those disqualified from Temple service due to flawed lineage [*ḥalalin*] are accepted after the fact. But here, with regard to the status of the priest, even Rabbi Yehoshua concedes that the priesthood is voided retroactively.

The *gemara* rejects this mapping, however, and offers a more refined resolution at the top of 12a. *Aliba de'Rabbi Eliezer kulei alma lo pligi* — according to Rabbi Eliezer, there is no debate in the *ir miklat* case at all. If Rabbi Eliezer holds that the *pesul* is fully retroactive, then clearly there was no *kohen gadol* at *gmar din*, and the exile never goes free. The debate between the two opinions is only *aliba de'Rabbi Yehoshua* — according to Rabbi Yehoshua, who holds that the *kohen*'s status was valid until discovery. Even granting that, one could argue that the discovery acts like a *misas kehunah* and frees the exile — that is the first opinion. The second opinion, however, holds *batla kehunah* even within Rabbi Yehoshua's framework, and would say that even Rabbi Yehoshua's leniency does not extend to this case.

### Korban Exception

The reason the second opinion can hold *batla kehunah* even according to Rabbi Yehoshua is that Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling in the *korbanot* case rests on a specific verse: *bareich Hashem cheilo ufo'al yadav tirtzeh* — God should bless his *cheil* (related to *chullin* or *chalal*) and the acts of his hands shall be accepted. The *drasha* is that even if he turns out to be a *chalal*, as long as this was not known at the time, God accepts the *korbanot*. This is a specific *halacha* applying to *korbanot* — a practically necessary rule to prevent the cascade of invalidation that would otherwise require enormous numbers of people to bring replacement offerings. But *ad kan* — this is the limit of Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling. It is a *halacha* confined to *korbanot*. In the context of *ir miklat*, where no such verse applies, even Rabbi Yehoshua would agree that if the *kohen gadol* was a *chalal*, there was never a valid *kohen gadol* at *gmar din*, and the exile remains permanently confined. Thus the debate within Rabbi Yehoshua's position comes down to whether the discovery itself acts as a species of *misas kehunah* capable of freeing the exile, or whether — absent the special *halacha* of *korbanot* — the retroactive nullification leaves the exile with no path to freedom.

### Yoav ben Tzeruiah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שְׁתֵּי טָעִיּוֹת טָעָה יוֹאָב בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּנׇס יוֹאָב אֶל אֹהֶל ה׳ וַיַּחֲזֵק בְּקַרְנוֹת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״, טָעָה – שֶׁאֵינוֹ קוֹלֵט אֶלָּא גַּגּוֹ, וְהוּא תָּפַס בְּקַרְנוֹתָיו. טָעָה – שֶׁאֵינוֹ קוֹלֵט אֶלָּא מִזְבַּח בֵּית עוֹלָמִים, וְהוּא תָּפַס מִזְבֵּחַ שֶׁל שִׁילֹה. אַבָּיֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּהָא נָמֵי מִיטְעָא טְעָה: טָעָה – שֶׁאֵינוֹ קוֹלֵט אֶלָּא כֹּהֵן וַעֲבוֹדָה בְּיָדוֹ, וְהוּא זָר הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the verdict of a murderer was decided at a time when there was no High Priest, and likewise in the cases of one who unintentionally killed a High Priest and in the case of a High Priest who killed unintentionally, the unintentional murderer never leaves the city of refuge. And one who is exiled may not leave the city at all; even if the Jewish people require his services, and even if he is the general of the army of the Jewish people like Joab ben Zeruiah, he does not leave the city of refuge ever. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Joab made two errors at that moment,** when he fled from Solomon, **as it is written: "And Joab fled to the Tent of God and grasped the horns of the altar"** (I Kings 2:28). **He erred** in **that only the top of** the altar **provides refuge, and he grasped its corners.** Moreover, **he erred** in **that only the altar of the eternal House,** i.e., the Temple. **provides refuge, and he grasped the altar at Shiloh. Abaye said:** It is **with regard to this** that Joab **also erred, as** the altar **provides refuge only** for **a priest** who grasps the roof of the altar **and his service is in his hand, and** Joab **was a non-priest.**

A further digression in the *gemara* concerns the case of Yoav ben Tzeruiah, who is cited in the context of a *kohen gadol* or a figure whose presence in the *ir miklat* is required by the nation at large. The question of *pikuach nefesh* is raised — what happens if the person confined to the *ir miklat* is someone the nation needs urgently, such as a military commander? The *gemara* does not develop this point directly.

Regarding Yoav ben Tzeruiah himself: David on his deathbed instructed Shlomo to ensure that Yoav would not die in peace, because Yoav had shed blood in a time of peace by killing two major Israelite generals. Yoav, understanding the principle of sanctuary, fled to the *mizbe'ach* and grasped its horns. Shlomo sent Benaiahu ben Yehoyada, who killed him there. The *gemara* (cited by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav) teaches that Yoav made two fundamental errors — and Abaye adds a third. First, he erred in thinking the altar provided sanctuary while grasping its horns: the verse says *me'im mizbechi*, which the *gemara* reads as referring to the roof or top surface of the altar, not the horns. Second, he erred because the sanctuary applies only to the *mizbe'ach beit olamim* — the altar of the permanent *Beis Hamikdash* — whereas Yoav grasped the temporary altar erected by David before the *Beis Hamikdash* was built. Third, according to Abaye, he erred because the sanctuary of the altar protects only a *kohen* who is actively engaged in the *avodah*, and Yoav was a *zar*.

It is striking that the *gemara* does not list a fourth error: that Yoav killed *b'meizid*, intentionally, and the verse *v'chi yazid ish al re'ehu l'horgo b'ormah me'im mizbechi tikachenu lamus* explicitly states that an intentional killer is dragged from the altar to be executed. The Rambam addresses this omission in a remarkable passage. He rules that the altar sanctuary applies to someone who killed *b'shogeg*, and also — under different conditions — to someone fleeing the king's power or a *beit din* acting in *hora'at sha'ah* emergency capacity, even without a standard *din*. For such a person, the Rambam rules, the sanctuary is absolute and they may never be removed, even if they are a *zar*, even if they held on to the horns rather than the rooftop. This would encompass Yoav's case, since Shlomo's mandate to execute him did not come from a formal *beit din* process. The Rambam's source may be the *Yerushalmi*, as the reading diverges significantly from our *gemara*'s analysis.

### Reish Lakish on Rome

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: שָׁלֹשׁ טָעִיּוֹת עָתִיד שָׂרוֹ שֶׁל רוֹמִי לִטְעוֹת, דִּכְתִיב: ״מִי זֶה בָּא מֵאֱדוֹם חֲמוּץ בְּגָדִים מִבׇּצְרָה״, טוֹעֶה – שֶׁאֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת אֶלָּא בֶּצֶר, וְהוּא גּוֹלֶה לְבׇצְרָה. טוֹעֶה – שֶׁאֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת אֶלָּא שׁוֹגֵג, וְהוּא מֵזִיד הָיָה. טוֹעֶה – שֶׁאֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת אֶלָּא אָדָם, וְהוּא מַלְאָךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos errors, the Gemara cites that **Reish Lakish says: The angel of Rome is destined to make three errors, as it is written: "Who is this who comes from Edom, with crimsoned garments from Bozrah?"** (Isaiah 63:1), which is a parable for God's arrival after killing the angel of Rome in Bozrah. The angel of Rome will **err** in **that** it is **only** the city of **Bezer** that **provides refuge and he exiled** himself **to Bozrah;** he will **err** in **that it provides refuge only** to **an unintentional** murderer **and he was** an **intentional** murderer; and he will **err** in **that it provides refuge only** to **a person and he is an angel.**

The *gemara* concludes this section with a *midrashic* application by Reish Lakish. Since the discussion has touched on the three errors of Yoav, Reish Lakish observes that the *sar shel Romi* — the divine power overseeing Rome — will make three parallel errors in the eschatological era. The verse in Yeshayahu, *mi zeh ba me-Edom chamutz begadim mi-Botzrah*, depicts God arriving blood-soaked from Edom after slaying that power. Three errors: the angel fled to *Botzrah* rather than *Betzer*, one of the actual *arei miklat* — confusing the names; the *ir miklat* sanctuary only protects a *shogeg*, and this angel was a *meizid*; and the sanctuary protects only human beings, and this is an angel.

## Burial & Bones (II)

### Levites' Cemetery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: עָרֵי מִקְלָט לֹא נִתְּנוּ לִקְבוּרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמִגְרְשֵׁיהֶם יִהְיוּ לִבְהֶמְתָּם וְלִרְכֻשָׁם וּלְכֹל חַיָּתָם״ – לְחַיִּים נִתְּנוּ וְלֹא לִקְבוּרָה. מֵיתִיבִי, ״שָׁמָּה״ – שָׁם תְּהֵא דִּירָתוֹ, שָׁם תְּהֵא מִיתָתוֹ, שָׁם תְּהֵא קְבוּרָתוֹ! רוֹצֵחַ שָׁאנֵי, דְּגַלִּי בֵּיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara resumes its analysis of the mishna. **Rabbi Abbahu says: Cities of refuge were not given for** the purpose of **burial** of unintentional murderers within them, **as it is written** with regard to the Levite cities: **"And their open land shall be for their cattle, and for their property, and for all their beasts [*ḥayyatam*]"** (Numbers 35:3), from which it is derived: **For life [*leḥayyim*] they are given, but not for burial.** Even Levites who reside in these cities are buried beyond the open land surrounding the city. The Gemara **raises an objection** to this from the mishna's interpretation of the term: "That he fled **there"** (Numbers 35:25), from which it is derived: **There shall be his dwelling, there shall be his death, there shall be his burial.** The Gemara answers: **A murderer is different, as the Merciful One revealed concerning him** that he is to be buried there. That does not apply to the other residents of the city.

Rabbi Abbahu rules: *ir miklat lo nitnu l'kevura* — the cities of refuge were not designated for burial. He derives this from the verse *u-migrasheihem yiheyu lahem livhemtam u-l'rekhusham v'sham l'khol chayatam* — the open lands around the cities were designated for living creatures. *L'chayim nitnu v'lo l'kevura* — they were given for the living, not for burial.

This seems to contradict the *halacha* established earlier that exiles are buried in the *ir miklat*. The *gemara* resolves this cleanly: *rotzeach shani*, the *rotzeach* is different, because the *Torah* explicitly specifies that the exile dwells there, dies there, and is buried there. That explicit ruling overrides the general principle. The general principle — that these cities are not for burial — applies to the *Leviyim* themselves, the permanent residents. The *Leviyim*' cemetery is located outside the boundaries of the *ir miklat*, not within it.

### Tchum Refuge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהָעִיר קוֹלֶטֶת וְכוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״וְיָשַׁב בָּהּ״ – בָּהּ וְלֹא בִּתְחוּמָהּ! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן – לִקְלוֹט, כָּאן – לָדוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **Just as** an unintentional murderer **is admitted** to **the city** of refuge, so is he admitted to its outskirts, located within the Shabbat boundary. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita* with regard to that which is written concerning the unintentional murderer: **"And he shall dwell in it"** (Numbers 35:25), from which it is inferred: **"In it," but not within its boundary. Abaye said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** the mishna is referring to the unintentional murderer being **admitted** to the city, which will provide refuge from the blood redeemer, who may not kill him there. **There,** the *baraita* is referring to the place where it is permitted for the murderer **to dwell,** i.e., within the city itself and not on its outskirts.

The *mishna* states that just as the city itself provides sanctuary, so too does the *tchum* — the surrounding area of two thousand *amah* — serve as a refuge zone. The basis is the general structure of the *arei ha-Leviyim*: the *Torah* speaks of a *migrash* of one thousand *amah* and a *sadeh* extending to two thousand *amah* around the city, and both zones together constitute the effective *tchum*.

A question is raised from the verse *v'yashav bah* — he shall dwell *in it*, implying only within the city itself, not in its environs. Abaye resolves this: *kan liklot kan ladur* — these are two separate provisions. The verse *v'yashav bah* concerns where the exile must live — his permanent dwelling must be inside the city. But the *tchumim* surrounding the city retain their protective function as sanctuary even without being his residence. He must reside in the city; he is protected throughout the *tchum*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לָדוּר״? תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּאֵין עוֹשִׂין שָׂדֶה מִגְרָשׁ וְלֹא מִגְרָשׁ שָׂדֶה, לֹא מִגְרָשׁ עִיר וְלֹא עִיר מִגְרָשׁ. אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לִמְחִילּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why is it necessary to state that the murderer may not **dwell** on the outskirts of the city? **Let him derive** that *halakha* from the fact **that one may not render the field** of a Levite city **an open space, nor an open space a field, nor an open space** part of the **city, nor** the **city an open space.** Apparently, the outskirts of the city, whose status is that of an open space, may not be utilized for residential purposes. **Rav Sheshet said: It is necessary** to state this *halakha* **only for tunnels.** If a murderer excavated a tunnel on the outskirts of the city, although he did not violate the prohibition against ruining the fields of the city, he may not reside there based on the *halakha* by Torah law that he must reside inside the city.

A further complication: there is a general *halacha* of *arei ha-Leviyim* prohibiting building in the *migrash* area, meaning the exile cannot construct a dwelling outside the city boundaries even if he wished to. Rav Sheshet resolves this by noting that the prohibition on dwelling outside applies specifically to building structures. One could potentially inhabit caves or subterranean spaces in the *tchum* without violating the prohibition. The practical upshot is that the exile must live in the city proper but is protected throughout the *tchum*.

## Goel HaDam

### Yose vs. Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רוֹצֵחַ שֶׁיָּצָא חוּץ לַתְּחוּם וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְרָצַח גֹּאֵל הַדָּם אֶת הָרֹצֵחַ״ – מִצְוָה בְּיַד גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם, אֵין גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם – רְשׁוּת בְּיַד כׇּל אָדָם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: רְשׁוּת בְּיַד גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם, וְכׇל אָדָם חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches a dispute between Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and Rabbi Akiva with regard to a case **where** the unintentional **murderer emerged beyond the** Shabbat **boundary** of the city of refuge, and the blood redeemer found him there. **The Sages taught** that it is written: "And the blood redeemer finds him outside the border of his city of refuge **and the blood redeemer murders the murderer,** he has no blood" (Numbers 35:27): It is **a mitzva for the blood redeemer** to kill him, and if **there is no blood redeemer** available to fulfill this mitzva, **it is optional for any person** to do so; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Rabbi Akiva says:** It is **optional for the blood redeemer** to kill him, **and any** other **person is liable for** killing **him.**

The *gemara* now turns to what happens if the *rotzeach* exits the *ir miklat*. Rabbi Yose HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva debate the status of the *goel hadam* in such a case. Rabbi Yose HaGlili holds that it is a *mitzvah* for the *goel hadam* to kill the *rotzeach* who has left the bounds of the *ir miklat*, and if there is no *goel hadam* available, then *reshus b'yad kol adam* — any person has permission to do so. Rabbi Akiva holds that the *goel hadam* has *reshus* — permission, not an obligation — and any other person who kills the *rotzeach* would be liable for murder. The crux of the debate turns on the reading of the verse *v'ratzach goel hadam et ha-rotzeach*: Rabbi Yose HaGlili reads *v'ratzach* as prescriptive — he shall kill; Rabbi Akiva notes that it does not say *yirtzach* and reads it as descriptive — if he kills. The verb is genuinely ambiguous and neither position has a decisive grammatical advantage.

Most people would instinctively identify with Rabbi Akiva's position; the idea of *mitzvah* for the *goel hadam* strikes most as difficult to embrace, even if the verse permits it. The Torah's framework here is plausibly understood as accommodating the pre-existing reality of blood vengeance while channeling and limiting it — the *ir miklat* system is an attempt to insert legal structure into what would otherwise be pure vigilante justice.

### Tannaim Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי? מִי כְּתִיב: ״אִם רֹצֵחַ״? וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִי כְּתִיב: ״יִרְצַח״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the mishna: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili?** He says: **Is it written: If** the blood redeemer **murders** the unintentional murderer, he has no blood? It states: "And the blood redeemer murders the murderer," indicating that it is a mitzva. **And Rabbi Akiva** says: **Is it written:** The blood redeemer **shall murder,** in the imperative? It merely states: "And the blood redeemer murders," which is merely relating the scenario under discussion.

A *baraita* supports the analysis. The *Torah* states *v'ratzach goel hadam et ha-rotzeach — ein lo damim* — the *goel hadam* kills the *rotzeach* and bears no blood guilt. The *baraita* in accordance with Rabbi Yose HaGlili reads this as: it is a *mitzvah* for the *goel hadam* to do so; if no *goel hadam* is available, the permission passes to any person. In accordance with Rabbi Akiva: the *goel hadam* alone has *reshus*, and any other person would be guilty.

### Mar Zutra Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה אָמַר רַב: רוֹצֵחַ שֶׁיֵּצֵא חוּץ לַתְּחוּם, וּמְצָאוֹ גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם וַהֲרָגוֹ – נֶהֱרָג עָלָיו. כְּמַאן? לָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, וְלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא!
>
> **Translation:** On a related note, the Gemara cites that **Mar Zutra bar Toviyya says** that **Rav says:** In the case of **a murderer who emerged beyond the** Shabbat **boundary** of the city of refuge, **and the blood redeemer found him** there **and killed him,** the blood redeemer is **executed for** killing **him.** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion did Rav issue this ruling? He issued it **neither in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** who maintains that there is a mitzva for the blood redeemer to kill him, **nor in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** who says the blood redeemer has the option of killing him.

Mar Zutra bar Tuvia in the name of Rav takes a position that departs from both *tanna'im*: a *rotzeach* who exits the *tchum* and is killed by the *goel hadam* — the *goel hadam* himself is liable for murder. This is the most restrictive possible reading and, as the *gemara* notes, contradicts both Rabbi Yose HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva, since both agree the *goel hadam* is at minimum permitted. The *gemara* asks: *k'man* — like whom? And answers that Mar Zutra follows a third *tanna* whose opinion we have not yet seen.

### Standing Before Eidah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״עַד עׇמְדוֹ לִפְנֵי הָעֵדָה לַמִּשְׁפָּט״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְרָצַח גֹּאֵל הַדָּם אֶת הָרֹצֵחַ״, יָכוֹל מִיָּד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַד עׇמְדוֹ לִפְנֵי הָעֵדָה לַמִּשְׁפָּט״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav **states** this ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says** with regard to the verse: "And the murderer shall not die, **until he stands before the congregation for judgment"** (Numbers 35:12): **Why** must **the verse state** this? It is necessary **since it is stated:** "And the blood redeemer finds him…**and the blood redeemer murders the murderer"** (Numbers 35:27). One **might** have thought that the blood redeemer may murder him **immediately;** therefore, **the verse states: "Until he stands before the congregation for judgment,"** from which it is derived that the blood redeemer may kill the murderer only after he is convicted in court. Mar Zutra bar Toviyya says that Rav says that the blood redeemer is liable if he kills the murderer before he is convicted.

That third *tanna* is derived from a *braita* in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: *ad omdo lifnei ha-eidah l'mishpat* — the verse says he shall not be killed until he stands before the court for judgment. Rabbi Eliezer applies this principle not only to the initial trial but also to the case of someone who has left the *ir miklat*. When the *Torah* states *v'ratzach goel hadam et ha-rotzeach* — he kills the *rotzeach* who left — one might think this grants the *goel hadam* immediate, independent authority to act. *Talmud lomar*: *ad omdo lifnei ha-eidah l'mishpat* — no. Even now, the killing may only occur after the person has stood before a court.

This is a remarkable step in the direction of fully judicializing the *goel hadam* function. The *goel hadam* is not acting as a private avenger but as an instrument of the court. The *rotzeach* who leaves the *ir miklat* has, by that act, made himself liable to death — but the execution of that judgment requires judicial process. One possible reading: the *goel hadam* must bring the person before a *beit din*, the *beit din* finds him liable, and only then may the *goel hadam* carry out the killing. As the discussion notes, this transforms the entire *goel hadam* institution into something closely analogous to a *shaliach beis din*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הַאי ״עַד עׇמְדוֹ לִפְנֵי הָעֵדָה לַמִּשְׁפָּט״ מַאי דָּרְשִׁי בֵּיהּ? הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְסַנְהֶדְרִין שֶׁרָאוּ אֶחָד שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ שֶׁאֵין מְמִיתִין אוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁיַּעֲמוֹד בְּבֵית דִּין אַחֵר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַד עׇמְדוֹ לִפְנֵי הָעֵדָה לַמִּשְׁפָּט״ – עַד שֶׁיַּעֲמוֹד בְּבֵית דִּין אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Yosei** HaGelili **and Rabbi Akiva,** with regard to **this** verse: **"Until he stands before the congregation for judgment," what do** they **derive from it?** The Gemara answers: **That** verse **is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says: From where** is it derived in the case of **a Sanhedrin that saw one kill a person that** they **may not execute him until he stands** trial **in a different court?** It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "Until he stands before the congregation for judgment,"** meaning: **Until he stands** before **a different court.** Since they themselves witnessed the murder, they are no longer capable of considering the possibility that he may be innocent.

Rabbi Yose HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva, the *gemara* notes, do not use *ad omdo lifnei ha-eidah l'mishpat* for this purpose. They have a different *drasha* from that verse — specifically, Rabbi Akiva uses it to derive that if an entire *sanhedrin* witnessed a murder, they may not themselves adjudicate the case; the witnesses must bring their testimony to a separate *beit din*, because *ad omdo lifnei ha-eidah l'mishpat* requires the defendant to be able to "stand before" the court in a genuine sense, which is impossible if the court itself observed the crime.

### Intentional Exit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִם יָצֹא יֵצֵא הָרֹצֵחַ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּמֵזִיד, בְּשׁוֹגֵג מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִם יָצֹא יֵצֵא״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. וְהָתַנְיָא: וְהַהוֹרְגוֹ, בְּמֵזִיד – נֶהֱרָג, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – גּוֹלֶה! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: אָמְרִינַן דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה כִלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם, הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: לָא אָמְרִינַן דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה כִלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"And if the murderer emerges [*yatzo yetze*]** …and the blood redeemer murders the murderer, he has no blood" (Numbers 35:26–27): **I have** derived **only** that the blood redeemer may kill the unintentional murderer if the unintentional murderer emerges from the city **intentionally. From where** is it derived that the same applies if he emerges **unwittingly?** It is derived from this verse, as **the verse states: "If *yatzo yetze*";** the doubled form of the verb serves to teach that this *halakha* applies **in any case** where the unintentional murderer emerges from the city of refuge. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to an unintentional murderer who emerged from the city of refuge unwittingly: **And one who kills him intentionally is executed,** and one who kills him **unintentionally is exiled?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as **this** second *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that **we say: The Torah spoke in the language of people,** and no *halakha* is derived from the doubled form of the verb: *Yatzo yetze*, as it is merely a rhetorical flourish, and **that** first *baraita* **is in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that **we do not say: The Torah spoke in the language of people,** and the compound verb was employed in order to derive that the blood redeemer may kill the unintentional murderer even if he emerged from the city of refuge unwittingly.

A *baraita* asks: *im yatzo yetzei ha-rotzeach* — the verse says "if he leaves, he leaves." Does this expose the *rotzeach* to being killed regardless of how he came to be outside the *ir miklat*? What if he left *b'shogeg* — accidentally, without intent? *Talmud lomar*: the double language *yatzo yetzei* could imply *mi-kol makom*, by any means. Yet another *braita* distinguishes: *b'meizid neherag, b'shogeg goleh* — if he left intentionally he is liable to be killed; if unintentionally, he simply returns to the *ir miklat*. The two *baraitot* thus conflict, and the *gemara* resolves that the question turns on the principle of *dibrah Torah ki-leshon b'nei adam* — whether one gives interpretive weight to the doubled expression or reads it as ordinary language.

### End Not Harsher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִסְתַּבְּרָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה כִּלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא סוֹפוֹ חָמוּר מִתְּחִלָּתוֹ: מָה תְּחִלָּתוֹ, בְּמֵזִיד – נֶהֱרָג, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – גּוֹלֶה, אַף סוֹפוֹ, בְּמֵזִיד – נֶהֱרָג, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – גּוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: It stands to reason** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says: The Torah spoke in the language of people,** and the blood redeemer is liable for killing an unintentional murderer who emerged from the city of refuge unwittingly, in order to ensure **that** the **ultimate** punishment of the unintentional murderer, when he emerges from the city of refuge, **will not be more severe than his initial** punishment, when he is sentenced in court. **Just as** with regard to **his initial** punishment for murder, if he killed **intentionally he is executed,** and if he killed **unintentionally he is exiled, so too,** with regard to **his ultimate** punishment, if he emerges from the city of refuge **intentionally he is killed** by the blood redeemer, and if he emerges **unwittingly he is** returned to **exile** in the city of refuge.

Abaye weighs in decisively with a logical argument: *mistabra* — it stands to reason — to adopt the position that reads *yatzo yetzei* as ordinary language and restricts the *goel hadam*'s license to the case of intentional exit. His reason: *shelo yehei sofo chamur mi-tchilato* — the end should not be harsher than the beginning. At the beginning, when the original killing occurred: *b'meizid neherag, b'shogeg goleh* — intentional killing incurs death, unintentional killing incurs exile. The same logic should apply at the end: if he left the *ir miklat* intentionally, *neherag*; if unintentionally, *goleh* — he simply returns. It would be incoherent for the regime surrounding the *ir miklat* to be harsher than the original sentencing framework that put him there.

This argument also reinforces the broader pattern visible throughout this sugya: the entire institution is being progressively reframed in terms of *din* — legal judgment with the categories of *shogeg* and *meizid*, intentionality, court authorization — rather than raw blood vengeance. The language of *neherag*, "he is killed," rather than "the *goel hadam* kills him," reflects this judicial framing.

## Father & Son

### Son's Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: אָב שֶׁהָרַג – בְּנוֹ נַעֲשָׂה לוֹ גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵין בְּנוֹ נַעֲשָׂה לוֹ גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם. לֵימָא הָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי וְהָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא?
>
> **Translation:** § It **is taught** in **one** *baraita*: In the case of **a father who killed** his son, **his** surviving **son becomes his blood redeemer** and may kill him. **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **His son does not become his blood redeemer.** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **this** *baraita*, which states that his son does become his blood redeemer, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.** Since there is a mitzva for the blood redeemer to kill the murderer, this mitzva applies equally to a son. **And that** *baraita*, which states that a son does not become a blood redeemer to kill his father, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** who holds that the blood redeemer has only the option, not a mitzva, to kill the murderer.

A *baraita* rules: *av she-harag bno na'asch lo goel hadam* — a father who accidentally killed his son: the surviving brother may serve as *goel hadam* against the father to avenge his brother's blood. Another *baraita* contradicts this: *ein bno na'asch lo goel hadam* — a son may not serve as *goel hadam* against his father.

The *gemara* considers whether this maps onto the debate between Rabbi Yose HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva — the former expanding the scope of the *goel hadam* institution, the latter restricting it.

### No Shaliach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרַאּ? בֵּין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִצְוָה בֵּין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר רְשׁוּת, מִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: לַכֹּל אֵין הַבֵּן נַעֲשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ לְאָבִיו לְהַכּוֹתוֹ וּלְקִלְלָתוֹ, חוּץ מִמֵּסִית, שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה ״לֹא תַחְמֹל וְלֹא תְכַסֶּה עָלָיו״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that understanding. **And** how can **you understand** it in that manner? **Both according to the one who says** that there is **a mitzva** for the blood redeemer to kill the unintentional murderer **and according to the one who says** that it is **optional, is it permitted** for a son to do so? **But doesn't Rabba bar Rav Huna say, and likewise the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: With regard to all** transgressions of the Torah, even if the father is liable to receive lashes or be ostracized, **a son does not become an agent** of the court **to flog his father or to curse him, apart from** the case of a father who acted as **one who incites** others to engage in idol worship, **as the Torah states** in his regard: **"You shall neither spare nor conceal him"** (Deuteronomy 13:9)?

The *gemara* rejects this mapping: *v'tisbra* — does it even make sense, regardless of whether the *goel hadam*'s function is a *mitzvah* or a *reshus*, to permit a son to act against his father in this way? The principle is established by Rabbah bar Rav Huna and confirmed by the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *ein haben na'asch shaliach l'aviv lehakoto ulekallelo* — a son cannot serve as the *beit din*'s agent to administer corporal punishment or impose *cherem* on his own father. The son must recuse himself from any judicial function directed against his father. The one exception is *mesit* — one who seduces others to idolatry — where the *Torah* explicitly commands *lo tachmol alav v'lo techaseh alav*, leaving no room for compassion.

The very framing of this ruling — that the *goel hadam* cannot act against a father because a son cannot be a *shaliach beit din* against his father — once again encodes the *goel hadam* as a judicial agent rather than a private avenger.

### Grandson Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בִּבְנוֹ, וְהָא בְּבֶן בְּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara suggests that the apparent contradiction between the two *baraitot* is **not difficult,** as **this** *baraita*, which says that the son does not become a blood redeemer to kill his father, is referring **to his son, and that** *baraita*, which says that the son does become a blood redeemer, is referring **to the son of his son,** who can become a blood redeemer to kill his grandfather, as the grandson is not required to honor his grandfather as he is required to honor his father.

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction between the two *baraitot*: *lo kashya, ha bno v'ha ben bno* — there is no contradiction. The *baraita* that prohibits it refers to the son acting against his father. The *baraita* that permits it refers to the grandson — the son of the man who was killed — acting against his grandfather, the killer. That is permitted. The father-son relationship creates the disqualification; the grandfather-grandson relationship does not.

## Tree Boundary

### Crown Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אִילָן שֶׁהוּא עוֹמֵד בְּתוֹךְ הַתְּחוּם, וְנוֹפוֹ נוֹטֶה חוּץ לַתְּחוּם, אוֹ עוֹמֵד חוּץ לַתְּחוּם וְנוֹפוֹ נוֹטֶה בְּתוֹךְ הַתְּחוּם – הַכֹּל הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַנּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The previous mishna teaches that the halakhic status of the outskirts of the city is like that of the city itself in terms of the unintentional murderer being provided refuge there. The mishna adds: With regard to **a tree that stands within the** Shabbat **boundary** of a city of refuge, **whose boughs extend outside the boundary, or** a tree that **stands outside the boundary and its boughs extend inside the boundary,** the status of the tree, whether it is considered inside or outside the boundary, in **all** cases **follows the boughs.**

The final *mishna* of the *daf* addresses a classic question in halachic boundary law: a tree that stands astride the boundary of the *ir miklat*. If the trunk (*ikar*) stands within the *tchum* and the crown (*nof*) extends outside, or the trunk is outside and the crown extends inside — where is the person in the tree considered to be? The *mishna* rules: *hakol holech achar hanof* — everything follows the crown. This reverses the usual presumption, often formulated as *shadinan nofo batar ikaro* — we treat the crown as following the trunk — and says instead that the crown determines the person's location.

### Maaser Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּרְמִינְהִי: אִילָן שֶׁהוּא עוֹמֵד בְּתוֹךְ הַפָּנִים וְנוֹטֶה לַחוּץ, אוֹ עוֹמֵד בַּחוּץ וְנוֹטֶה לִפְנִים, מִכְּנֶגֶד הַחוֹמָה וְלִפְנִים – כְּלִפְנִים, מִכְּנֶגֶד הַחוֹמָה וְלַחוּץ – כְּלַחוּץ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Ma'aser Sheni* 3:7) taught with regard to second tithe. Second-tithe produce must be consumed within Jerusalem or redeemed outside of Jerusalem: With regard to **a tree that stands within** Jerusalem, **and** whose boughs **extend outside** the city wall, **or** a tree that **stands outside** the city wall **and** whose boughs **extend inside** the wall, the principle is: The halakhic status of any part of the tree that is **above the wall and inward** is that of an area **within** the wall and the halakhic status of any part of the tree that is **above the wall and outward** is that of an area **outside** the wall. Apparently, the trunk does not follow the boughs and the boughs do not follow the trunk. The status of each part of the tree is determined by its position relative to the wall.

The *gemara* immediately raises a *reminehu* — a contradiction from a *baraita* dealing with *Maaser Sheni* in Yerushalayim. The relevant law is that *Maaser Sheni* must be eaten within the walls of Yerushalayim. The *baraita* states: a tree whose trunk is inside Yerushalayim and whose crown extends outside, or whose trunk is outside and crown extends inside — *miknegd ha-chomah velifnim ke-lifnim, miknegd ha-chomah velachutz ke-lachutz* — the dividing line is the wall projected straight upward. You are wherever the vertical line of the wall places you. This makes no reference to crown or trunk; it simply asks whether you are in the airspace inside or outside the wall.

This seems to contradict the *mishna* in our *masechet*, which says to follow the crown.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֵׂר אַעָרֵי מִקְלָט קָא רָמֵית? מַעֲשֵׂר בְּחוֹמָה תְּלָה רַחֲמָנָא, עָרֵי מִקְלָט בְּדִירָה תְּלָה רַחֲמָנָא – בְּנוֹפוֹ מִתְּדַר לֵיהּ, בְּעִיקָּרוֹ לָא מִתְּדַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the parallel between the cases. **Are you raising a contradiction** between the *halakha* of second **tithe and** the *halakha* of **cities of refuge?** With regard to the *halakha* of second **tithe, the Merciful One made** the status of the tree **dependent on the wall,** and with regard to **cities of refuge, the Merciful One made** the status of the tree **dependent on dwelling. One can dwell in its boughs,** but **one cannot dwell in its trunk.** Therefore, with regard to cities of refuge, the halakhic status of the tree is determined by the boughs.

The *gemara* offers an initial resolution: these two cases are not comparable because the governing principle differs. By *Maaser Sheni*, the *Torah* attached the law to the *chomah* — the physical walls of Yerushalayim — so the relevant question is always one of airspace relative to the projected wall. By *ir miklat*, the *Torah* attached the law to *dirah* — dwelling — as in the verse *ki be-ir miklato yeishev*. The question is not airspace but where one is considered to be dwelling. *Benofo midayer, be-ikaro lo midayer* — one can conceivably build a dwelling in the crown of a tree; nobody dwells in a trunk. Therefore by *ir miklat* the crown determines the locus of "dwelling."

### Yehuda Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִי מַעֲשֵׂר אַמַּעֲשֵׂר, דְּתַנְיָא: בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם – הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַנּוֹף, בְּעָרֵי מִקְלָט – הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַנּוֹף. אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְהָא רַבָּנַן, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בִּמְעָרָה – הַולֵּךְ אַחַר פִּתְחָהּ, בְּאִילָן – הַולֵּךְ אַחַר נוֹפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** between the previous *baraita* with regard to the *halakha* of second **tithe and** another *baraita* with regard to the *halakha* of second **tithe, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (see *Ma'asrot* 3:10): **In Jerusalem, follow the boughs** in determining the status of the tree with regard to consuming second-tithe produce, and likewise, **with regard to cities of refuge, follow the boughs** in determining the status of the tree with regard to providing refuge for an unintentional murderer. **Rav Kahana said:** This apparent contradiction is **not difficult,** as **this** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and that** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to partaking of second tithe in Jerusalem: **Rabbi Yehuda says: With regard to a cave, follow its entrance;** if the entrance is inside the city, the status of the entire cave is that of part of the city, and one may partake of tithes in it. **With regard to a tree, follow its boughs.**

The *gemara* then complicates matters further: *rami Maaser a-Maaser* — there is a contradiction even within the *Maaser Sheni* sources themselves. A *mishna* in *Maasrot* states that by Yerushalayim one follows the *nof* (crown) for determining whether one may eat *Maaser Sheni*. This contradicts the *baraita* that said to follow the projected wall.

Rav Kahana resolves: *lo kashya, ha Rabbi Yehuda ve-ha Rabbanan* — the two sources reflect a tannaitic debate. The *baraita* recorded in a *baraita*: Rabbi Yehuda says that by a cave, one follows the opening (*achar pitchah*), and by a tree, one follows the crown (*achar nofo*). This is Rabbi Yehuda's position — both for *Maaser Sheni* in Yerushalayim and for *ir miklat*, follow the crown. The opposing position (the *Rabbanan*) would say you follow where you actually are in the airspace, consistent with the *baraita* about the projected wall.

### Lechumra Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה גַּבֵּי מַעֲשֵׂר לְחוּמְרָא, עִיקָּרוֹ בַּחוּץ וְנוֹפוֹ בִּפְנִים, כִּי הֵיכִי דִּבְנוֹפוֹ לָא מָצֵי פָּרֵיק – בְּעִיקָּרוֹ נָמֵי לָא מָצֵי פָּרֵיק. עִיקָּרוֹ מִבִּפְנִים וְנוֹפוֹ מִבַּחוּץ, כִּי הֵיכִי דִּבְנוֹפוֹ לָא מָצֵי אָכֵיל בְּלֹא פְּדִיָּיה – בְּעִיקָּרוֹ נָמֵי לָא מָצֵי אָכֵיל בְּלֹא פְּדִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that explanation. **Say that you heard Rabbi Yehuda** express this opinion **with regard to** second-**tithe** produce in a situation where his ruling is **a stringency,** as in the case where the **trunk** of a tree **is outside** Jerusalem **and its boughs are inside** Jerusalem, **just as among its boughs, one may not redeem** second-tithe produce, and he must partake of it in Jerusalem, **so too at its trunk he may not redeem** second-tithe produce, even though it stands outside of Jerusalem. So too in a case where the **trunk** of a tree **is inside and its boughs outside,** there is a stringency: **Just as among its boughs, one may not partake** of second-tithe produce **without redemption, so too at its trunk he may not partake** of second-tithe produce **without redemption,** even though it stands inside Jerusalem.

The *gemara*, however, does not accept that Rabbi Yehuda's ruling applies fully and symmetrically in all directions. There is a general principle — cited by *Rashi* — of *shadinan nofo batar ikaro*: normally the crown follows the trunk, not the reverse. Given this background assumption, the *gemara* reasons: one cannot say that Rabbi Yehuda intends to fully override this principle in both directions. Rather, *d'shamat lei l'Rabbi Yehuda gabei Maaser l'chumra* — Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that one follows the crown should be understood as applying only *l'chumra*, only in the stringent direction.

What does *l'chumra* mean by *Maaser Sheni*? There are two relevant restrictions: eating *Maaser Sheni* requires being inside Yerushalayim; *pidyon* (redemption) of *Maaser Sheni* may only be done outside. To be *machmir* both ways means: if the trunk is inside and the crown is outside, we follow the crown (*l'chumra*) and say this person may not eat *Maaser Sheni* there, because the crown is outside. But we also do not allow *pidyon*, because the trunk is inside and the general principle is to follow the trunk — so *l'chumra* for that as well, we say he may not redeem. If the trunk is outside and the crown is inside, we follow the crown (*l'chumra*) and say he may not redeem without *pidyon* because he is considered inside; but we also say he may not eat without *pidyon* because the trunk is outside. The result is that Rabbi Yehuda's rule about following the crown is always applied to impose the maximum stringency — never to grant leniency.

### Rava Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא גַּבֵּי עָרֵי מִקְלָט, בִּשְׁלָמָא עִיקָּרוֹ בַּחוּץ וְנוֹפוֹ בִּפְנִים, כִּי הֵיכִי דִּבְנוֹפוֹ לָא מָצֵי קָטֵיל לֵיהּ, בְּעִיקָּרוֹ נָמֵי לָא מָצֵי קָטֵיל לֵיהּ. אֶלָּא עִיקָּרוֹ בִּפְנִים וְנוֹפוֹ בַּחוּץ, כִּי הֵיכִי דִּבְנוֹפוֹ מָצֵי קָטֵיל לֵיהּ, בְּעִיקָּרוֹ נָמֵי מָצֵי קָטֵיל לֵיהּ?! הָא גַּוַּאי קָאֵי! אָמַר רָבָא: בְּעִיקָּרוֹ – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּלָא מָצֵי קָטֵיל. קָאֵי בְּנוֹפוֹ, וְיָכוֹל לְהוֹרְגוֹ בְּחִצִּים וּבִצְרוֹרוֹת – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּמָצֵי קָטֵיל לֵיהּ. כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בְּמֶהֱוֵי עִיקָּרוֹ דַּרְגָּא לְנוֹפוֹ. מָר סָבַר: הָוֵי עִיקָּרוֹ דַּרְגָּא לְנוֹפוֹ. וּמָר סָבַר: לָא הָוֵי עִיקָּרוֹ דַּרְגָּא לְנוֹפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **But with regard to cities of refuge,** it may be otherwise: **Granted,** if **its trunk** is **outside** the boundary **and its boughs** are **inside, just as among its boughs,** the blood redeemer **may not kill** the unintentional murderer, **so too at its trunk, he may not kill him. But** if **its trunk** was **inside and its boughs outside,** would one say that **just as among its boughs,** the blood redeemer **may kill him, at its trunk, he may also kill him? Isn't** the unintentional murderer **standing inside** the city of refuge? How could one say that it is permitted for the blood redeemer to kill him inside the city? **Rava said** that it can be explained as follows: In the case where its trunk is inside the boundary and its boughs outside, and the unintentional murderer was standing **at its trunk, everyone agrees that** the blood redeemer **may not kill** him, and when Rabbi Yehuda said that the trunk follows the boughs, he did not intend to include that case. If the murderer **is standing among the boughs** of the tree, **and** the blood redeemer **is able to kill him with arrows and pebbles, everyone,** including the Rabbis, **agrees that** the blood redeemer **may kill him,** as the boughs are outside the city. **When they disagree is with regard to** whether **its trunk can become a step for its boughs,** enabling the blood redeemer to gain access to the unintentional murderer there. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds: Its trunk can become a step for its boughs;** the blood redeemer may gain access to the boughs extending outside the boundary and kill the unintentional murderer by climbing the trunk inside the city. It was in that context that Rabbi Yehuda says that the trunk follows the boughs. **And** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds: Its trunk cannot become a step for its boughs.**

Rava observes that this *l'chumra* reading works cleanly by *Maaser Sheni*, but creates a problem by *ir miklat*. By *ir miklat*, *l'chumra* in one direction is clear: if the trunk is outside and the crown inside, we treat the person as inside and say the *goel hadam* cannot kill him — that is a stringency in the sense of protecting the *rotzeach*. But what about the reverse case: trunk inside, crown outside? To follow the crown *l'chumra* here would mean treating him as outside the *ir miklat* and allowing the *goel hadam* to kill him — even though he is physically standing inside the *ir miklat* at the trunk of the tree. *Ha gavra kai* — the man is standing inside! Treating him as exposed to death on the grounds that his crown is outside is not a *chumra* in any meaningful sense; it is an absurdity. You would be using a legal fiction to allow someone to be killed who is physically inside the protected zone.

### Rav Ashi Crown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מַאי ״אַחַר הַנּוֹף״ – אַף אַחַר הַנּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: What** is the meaning of Rabbi Yehuda's statement: **Follow its boughs?** It does not mean that the location of the boughs is the only determining factor; rather, it means that in addition to the trunk, **follow the boughs as well** in a case where it is a stringency. Therefore, with regard to a city of refuge a tree whose trunk is inside the boundary and its boughs extend beyond the boundary, the halakhic status of the boughs is the same as what it would be were they inside the boundary.

Rav Ashi offers the *gemara*'s final resolution. *Mai acharei nof, af acharei nof* — when the *mishna* says one follows the *nof*, this means: we follow the *nof* to protect the person, to extend the mantle of the *ir miklat* in his favor. The rule is asymmetric by design. If the person is at the trunk inside the *ir miklat* and the crown extends outside, he is inside — obviously, since he is standing inside. If the person is at the trunk outside but the crown extends inside, we follow the *nof* and consider him inside the *ir miklat*, thereby protecting him. The crown is used to expand the zone of protection, never to contract it.

This reading also clarifies Rava's earlier point about the cases: *be-ikaro dechulei alma lo peligi delo matzi katil* — if the person is at the trunk inside the *ir miklat*, everyone agrees he cannot be killed. *Kai benofo* — if he is physically up in the crown in the outside zone, everyone agrees he can be killed. The debate that remains — per Rava — is the narrow case of whether the *goel hadam* may enter the *ir miklat*, use the trunk as a ladder (*darga lenof*), climb up to the crown, and kill the person there. That is a distinct question about the *goel hadam*'s ability to pass through protected territory to reach an exposed location, and the *tanna'im* debate it. Rav Ashi's formulation resolves the broader structural question: the *mishna*'s rule that one follows the crown is a rule for the benefit of the *rotzeach* — the crown of the tree, wherever it reaches, casts its protective shadow.
