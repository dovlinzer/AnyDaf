# Zevachim 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pigul Exemptions

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶם מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל – הַקּוֹמֶץ, וְהַקְּטֹרֶת, וְהַלְּבוֹנָה,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these are** the **items for which one is not liable** to receive *karet* **due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*.** One is liable to receive *karet* only if he partakes of an item that was permitted for consumption or for the altar by another item. As for the items listed here, either nothing else renders them permitted for consumption or for the altar, or they themselves render other items permitted. They are as follows: **The handful** of flour, which permits consumption of the meal offering; **the incense,** which is burned in its entirety, without another item rendering it permitted for the altar; **the frankincense,** which is burned together with the handful of the meal offering;

Daf 43 of Zevachim opens on 43a, picking up in the middle of a *mishna* that addresses which items, when consumed, carry *kares* liability due to *pigul*. The *mishna* is built around a fundamental structural principle: an offering can only become *pigul* if there exists the relationship of a *matir* — something that renders the primary item permitted — and a *nittar* — the remainder that becomes permitted through that *matir*. Where this relationship does not obtain, the status of *pigul* cannot take hold.

### Matir & Nittar

The governing principle underlying *pigul* is that every offering subject to this disqualification must have two components: the *matir*, which is the element that enables the other part to become permitted — either for human consumption or for being burned on the *mizbeach* — and the *nittar*, the thing thereby rendered permitted. If something is offered straight, with no other element serving as its *matir*, and it does not itself render something else permitted, it falls outside the framework of *pigul* entirely. Items that lack this two-stage structure are exempt from *pigul* liability.

### Mishna Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים, וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ, וְהַדָּם, וְהַנְּסָכִים הַבָּאִין בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַף הַבָּאִין עִם הַבְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **the meal offering of priests,** from which no handful of flour is removed and which is burned in its entirety (see Leviticus 6:16); **the meal offering of** the **anointed priest,** which is sacrificed by the High Priest each day, half in the morning and half in the evening; **the blood,** which permits all the offerings; **and the libations that are brought by themselves** as a separate offering and do not accompany an animal offering; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** The same *halakha* applies **even** with regard to libations **that are brought with an animal** offering.

The *mishna* opens its list of things exempt from *pigul* with the following: *ve'elu devarim she'ein chayavin aleihem mishum pigul* — the following things are not subject to *pigul* liability. One who eats them has not consumed *pigul* in the halachically consequential sense.

### Kometz & Frankincense

The *mishna* begins by listing: *hakometz*, the handful of flour taken from the *mincha* offering and burned on the *mizbeach*. This *kometz* functions as the *matir* that renders the *shirayim* — the remainder of the *mincha* — permitted to the *kohanim*. Since the *kometz* is itself the *matir*, it does not become *mutar* through something else; it has no *matir* of its own. Thus, even if the *mincha* becomes *pigul* and one who eats the *shirayim* incurs *kares*, eating the *kometz* itself does not carry *pigul* liability.

Similarly, *vehaktoret* — the burning of the *ketoret* — is burned straight and does not render anything else permitted in the two-stage sense. *Vehalevona* — the frankincense — functions identically: it is burned directly on the *mizbeach* without being rendered *mutar* through something else. *Uminchat kohanim* — any *mincha* brought by a *kohen* — is burned entirely on the *mizbeach*, and since the whole offering is consumed by the altar, there is no bifurcation of *matir* and *nittar*. *Uminchat kohen mashiach* — the daily *mincha* of the *kohen gadol*, half in the morning and half in the evening — likewise follows the rule that the entire offering is burned, so it too lacks the structure required for *pigul*.

### Blood & Nesachim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל; שֶׁדַּם הָאָשָׁם מַתִּירוֹ, וְכׇל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין בֵּין לָאָדָם בֵּין לַמִּזְבֵּחַ – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the *log* of oil** that accompanies the guilt offering **of** a recovered **leper, Rabbi Shimon says: One is not liable for** consuming **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** because it is not permitted by any other item. **And Rabbi Meir says: One is liable for** consuming **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*, as the blood of the guilt offering** of the leper **permits its** use, as only after the blood's sacrifice is the oil sprinkled and given to the priests. **And** the principle is: With regard to **any** item **that has permitting factors, either for** consumption by **a person or for** burning on **the altar, one is liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*.**

*Vehadam* — the blood of a *korban* — is also listed as exempt from *pigul* liability. Blood is the *matir* of the offering: it is through the sprinkling of the blood that the meat becomes permitted. But the blood itself never becomes *mutar* through anything else. Therefore, even if one's improper intention during the blood service rendered the *korban* into *pigul*, and even though one incurs *kares* for eating the meat, eating the blood of that offering does not carry *pigul* liability. This is separate from the prohibition against eating blood altogether, which is a distinct *issur*.

The *mishna* then introduces a dispute. *Vehanesachim haba'im bifnei atzman divrei Rabbi Meir* — wine libations brought independently, i.e., when someone takes a vow to bring wine to the *mizbeach*, are exempt from *pigul* according to Rabbi Meir. His view implies, however, that *nesachim* brought together with a *korban* are subject to *pigul*, because the blood of the *korban* serves as the *matir* — it is through the sprinkling of the blood that the meat, the accompanying *mincha*, and the *nesachim* all become permitted. Thus, when brought together with the animal, the *nesachim* fall within the *matir*-*nittar* framework, and Rabbi Meir holds they are subject to *pigul*.

*Vachachamim omrim af haba'im im habehemah* — the *chachamim* dispute this and maintain that even *nesachim* brought on the same day as the animal are not subject to *pigul*. Their reasoning is that the *halacha* permits one to bring the accompanying *nesachim* on a later day, after the *korban* has already been offered. Since it is possible to bring the *nesachim* the following day, the *korban*'s blood is not the *matir* for the *nesachim* — the *nesachim* stand on their own. And if the mechanism is that the *korban* does not make the *nesachim mutar*, then even when they are brought on the same day, the logic is identical. One may be obligated to bring the *nesachim* because of the *korban*, but the *korban* does not render the *nesachim mutar*. Consequently, the *nesachim* remain independent and do not fall within *pigul*.

A parallel dispute exists regarding the *log shemen shel metzora*. A *metzora* undergoing *tahara* on the eighth day brings an *asham* and, alongside it, a *log* — approximately twelve ounces — of *shemen*. This oil is applied to the *metzora*'s ear, thumb, and other places as part of the purification process, and the *gemara* elsewhere establishes that the remainder of the *log* is eaten by the *kohanim*. The question is whether the blood of the *asham* serves as a *matir* for the *log shemen*, rendering it subject to *pigul*.

*Rabbi Shimon omer ein chayavin alav mishum pigul* — Rabbi Shimon holds that one is not liable because of *pigul* for eating the *log shemen*. His reasoning mirrors that of the *chachamim* regarding the *nesachim*: since it is possible to bring the *log shemen* on a later day after the *asham* has been offered, the blood of the *asham* is evidently not the *matir* for the *log*. Therefore, even when brought on the same day, the *korban* is not rendering the *log mutar*, and *pigul* does not apply.

*Rabbi Meir omer chayavin alav mishum pigul shedahm ha'asham matiro* — Rabbi Meir maintains that one is liable, because when the *log* is brought on the same day as the *asham*, the blood of the *asham* does serve as a *matir* for it, just as he holds that the blood of any *korban* serves as a *matir* for *nesachim* brought on the same day.

### General Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין בֵּין לָאָדָם בֵּין לַמִּזְבֵּחַ – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** And** the principle is: With regard to **any** item **that has permitting factors, either for** consumption by **a person or for** burning on **the altar, one is liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*.**

The *mishna* concludes by articulating the positive formulation of the rule: *vechol sheyesh lo matirin bein le'adam bein lemizbe'ach chayavin alav mishum pigul* — anything that has a *matir*, whether it is rendered permitted for human consumption or for being placed on the *mizbeach*, is subject to *pigul* liability.

## Matir Examples

### Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָעוֹלָה – דָּמָהּ מַתִּיר אֶת בְּשָׂרָהּ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְעוֹרָהּ לַכֹּהֲנִים. עוֹלַת הָעוֹף – דָּמָהּ מַתִּיר אֶת בְּשָׂרָהּ לְמִזְבֵּחַ. חַטַּאת הָעוֹף – דָּמָהּ מַתִּיר אֶת בְּשָׂרָהּ לַכֹּהֲנִים. פָּרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים וּשְׂעִירִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים – דָּמָן מַתִּיר אֶת אֵימוּרֵיהֶן לִיקָרֵב.
>
> **Translation:** The burnt offering, **its blood permits its flesh to** be burned on **the altar and its hide to** be used **by the priests. The bird burnt offering, its blood permits its flesh** and its skin **to** be burned on **the altar. The bird sin offering, its blood permits its meat for** consumption by **the priests. Bulls that are burned,** e.g., the bull sacrificed for an unwitting communal transgression, **and goats that are burned,** e.g., the goat sacrificed for an unwitting communal sin of idol worship, **their blood permits their sacrificial portions to be sacrificed** on the altar.

The *mishna* now proceeds to enumerate cases where the *matir*-*nittar* relationship does obtain, establishing that *pigul* applies. Regarding the *olah*: *damah matir et besarah lemizbeach ve'orah lakohanim* — the blood of the *olah* makes the meat fit to be placed on the *mizbeach* (since the meat of an *olah* is not consumed by people but burned entirely on the altar), and it simultaneously renders the hide permissible to the *kohanim* as a benefit. Because the blood functions as a *matir* for both the meat on the *mizbeach* and the hide for the *kohanim*, one who eats from the meat of a *korban* that became *pigul* would incur *kares*. The *olat ha'of* follows the same logic: *damah matir et besarah lemizbeach* — the blood renders the bird's meat fit for the *mizbeach*, and since there is no hide involved with a bird, the equation is simpler but structurally identical.

### Chatat HaOf

The *mishna* proceeds to the *chatat ha'of*: *damah matir et besarah lakohanim* — its blood renders the meat permissible to the *kohanim*, who eat it. The same principle could have been stated about a regular *chatat* of a *behemah*, where the blood similarly renders the meat permitted to the *kohanim*. The *mishna* states the rule in terms of the bird offering, but the logic extends to the animal *chatat* as well.

### Parim HaNisrafim

The *mishna* addresses the *parim hanisrafim* and *seirim hanisrafim* — the bulls and goats whose blood is sprinkled inside the *heichal* and whose meat is then taken outside and burned. One might have thought that in these cases there is nothing being rendered *mutar*, since the meat is not consumed either by people or by the *mizbeach* in the usual sense. Yet the *mishna* teaches: *daman matir et emureihem lekarev* — even in these cases, the blood renders the *eimurim*, the innards and fats of the offering, fit to be burned on the *mizbeach*. The *eimurim* cannot be placed on the *mizbeach* until after the blood has been sprinkled. Therefore, the blood functions as a *matir* for the *eimurim*, and one who eats the *eimurim* of such a *korban* that became *pigul* incurs *kares*.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן כִּשְׁלָמִים, אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** Those bulls and goats are not subject to *piggul* because their blood is presented in the Sanctuary, and in the case of **any** offering **whose** blood **is not** presented **on the external altar like** that of **a peace offering,** with regard to which the *halakha* of *piggul* was stated in the Torah, **one is not liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*.**

*Rabbi Shimon omer kol she'eino kemizbeach hachitzon kishlamim ein chayavin alav mishum pigul* — Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the application of *pigul* to the *parim hanisrafim* and *seirim hanisrafim*. His position, which appears earlier in the tractate as well, is that the *Torah* discusses *pigul* in multiple places in the context of *shelamim*, and from this he derives that only offerings processed on the outer *mizbeach* are subject to *pigul*. Since the blood of the *parim hanisrafim* is sprinkled on the inner *mizbeach*, inside the *heichal*, the critical act of the *matir* — the sprinkling of the blood — takes place at the inner altar. Even though the *eimurim* are ultimately burned on the outer *mizbeach*, because the *matir* itself is performed at the inner altar, Rabbi Shimon excludes these offerings from the category of *pigul* entirely.

## Ulla on Kometz

### Pigul Removed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר עוּלָּא: קוֹמֶץ פִּיגּוּל שֶׁהֶעֱלוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – פָּקַע פִּיגּוּלוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ;
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Ulla says:** With regard to **a handful** of a meal offering that is ***piggul* that was offered up on the altar, its *piggul*** status **has left it.**

*Amar Ulla* — Ulla states: *kometz pigul she-he'alo al gabei mizbeach paka pigulo mimenu* — if a *kometz* of a *mincha* was offered with a *pigul* intention, rendering the *mincha* disqualified as *pigul*, and that *kometz* was then placed on the *mizbeach*, its *pigul* status departs from it. The *gemara* will interrogate both the logic and the precise halachic implication of this statement.

### Matir Not Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֲחֵרִים מֵבִיא לִידֵי פִּיגּוּל, הוּא עַצְמוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?! מַאי קָאָמַר? הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם אֵינוֹ מִתְקַבֵּל, הֵיאַךְ מֵבִיא אֲחֵרִים לִידֵי פִּיגּוּל?
>
> **Translation:** His reasoning is as follows: **If** this handful **brings other** items **to** a status of ***piggul*,** with regard to **itself** is it **not all the more so?** The Gemara asks: **What is** Ulla **saying?** This consideration does not explain why the status of *piggul* should leave the handful. The Gemara answers that **this** is what **he is saying: If** the handful **is not accepted,** i.e., if its sacrifice is disqualified, **how can it bring other** items **to** a status of ***piggul*?** A meal offering is considered *piggul* only if its handful is properly sacrificed.

Before identifying the specific halachic ruling Ulla is communicating, the *gemara* parses his underlying reasoning. Ulla frames his point as follows: *im acherim meivi lidei pigul, hu atzmo lo kol shekein* — if the *kometz* can bring other things to *pigul*, is it not all the more so the case that it itself must be regarded as not *pigul*?

This initially seems paradoxical: the argument appears to be a *kal vachomer* in the direction of making the *kometz* itself *pigul*, yet Ulla is claiming the opposite. The *gemara* therefore clarifies what Ulla means: *im eino mitkabel, heich mevi acherim lidei pigul?* — if the *kometz* were itself regarded as having been offered improperly (as *pigul*), then the *korban* would not have been properly brought, and the *shirayim* would not have become *pigul*. For an item becomes *pigul* only once all its *matirim* have been properly offered — *hikrivu kol matirav*. The fact that the *kometz* does make the *shirayim* into *pigul* demonstrates that at some level the *kometz* itself is viewed as having been properly offered, i.e., as if it were not itself *pigul*. The *kal vachomer* thus runs in the reverse direction from what might first appear: *if* it brings the *shirayim* to *pigul*, *then* we must be treating the *kometz* as not itself *pigul*, otherwise the mechanism could not operate.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? אִי דְּאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל – תְּנֵינָא, אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶם מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל: הַקּוֹמֶץ, וְהַקְּטֹרֶת, וְהַלְּבוֹנָה, וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים, וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ, וּמִנְחַת נְסָכִים, וְהַדָּם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Ulla **teaching us? If** he is teaching us **that one is not liable for** eating the handful **due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*, we learn** this in the mishna: **These are** the **items for which one is not liable due to *piggul*: The handful, the incense, the frankincense, the meal offering of priests, the meal offering of** the **anointed priest, the meal offering** brought with the **libations** that accompany animal offerings, **and the blood.** If so, one is not liable for eating the handful even if it was not offered up on the altar.

Having established the logic, the *gemara* asks: *mai kamashmalan?* — what is Ulla actually informing us? If his intention is to rule that one does not incur *kares* for eating the *kometz* because it is regarded as not itself *pigul*, the *gemara* responds: *tanina* — we already know this from our *mishna*. The *mishna* at the bottom of 42b explicitly lists the *kometz* among the things for which one does not incur *pigul* liability: *hakometz vehaktoret vehalevona minchat kohanim uminchat kohen mashiach uminchat nesachim vehadam*. It is worth noting that the logic of the *mishna* is somewhat different from Ulla's logic. The *mishna*'s reasoning is simply that *pigul* applies to the *nittar* and not to the *matir*. Ulla's reasoning is that we regard the *matir* as if it were not itself *pigul*, because otherwise the whole mechanism of *pigul* in the *nittar* could not function. Perhaps Ulla's reasoning serves as the explanation for the *mishna*'s ruling, but in any case the practical ruling is already stated in the *mishna*, so the *gemara* presses for what new information Ulla is conveying.

### If Removed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּאִם עָלוּ לֹא יֵרְדוּ – תְּנֵינָא: הַלָּן, וְהַיּוֹצֵא, וְהַטָּמֵא, וְשֶׁנִּשְׁחַט חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – אִם עָלוּ לֹא יֵרְדוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Ulla is teaching **that if** items with *piggul* status **ascended** the altar, **they shall not descend.** But this too, **we learn** in a mishna (84a): With regard to sacrificial flesh **that** is **left** overnight, **or that emerges** from the Temple courtyard, **or that is ritually impure, or** an offering **that was slaughtered** with the intention of eating the meat **beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area,** thereby acquiring the status of *piggul*, **if** any one of these **ascended** the altar **they shall not descend.**

The *gemara* then proposes: *ela de-im alu lo yardu* — perhaps Ulla is teaching that if this *pigul kometz* was placed on the *mizbeach*, the ruling is that it need not be removed, because we regard it as if the *kometz* itself were not *pigul*. The *gemara* again replies: *tanina* — this too is already taught. A later *mishna* in the tractate establishes that items such as *lan* (meat or blood left overnight), *yotzei* (carried outside its proper boundaries), *tamei* (ritually impure), and items offered *chutz lizmano* (with out-of-time intent, i.e., *pigul*) or *chutz limkomo* (with out-of-place intent) — all of these, though they should not be placed on the *mizbeach*, if they were placed on the *mizbeach*, one does not remove them. And as the *gemara* notes, that rule has nothing to do with Ulla's specific logic about the *matir* not being *pigul*; it applies to the full range of *pesulim* as a general principle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא דְּאִם יֵרְדוּ יַעֲלוּ – הָא נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאִם עָלוּ לֹא יֵרְדוּ, כָּךְ אִם יֵרְדוּ לֹא יַעֲלוּ! לָא צְרִיכָא, שֶׁמָּשְׁלָה בָּהֶן הָאוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Ulla is teaching **that if** items with *piggul* status **descended** from the altar after having been brought up there, **they ascend** once again. But **we also learn** in that same mishna (84b) that **this** is not so: **Just as if they ascended** the altar, **they shall not descend, so** too, **if they descended** from the altar **they shall not ascend.** The Gemara answers: **No,** Ulla's ruling is **necessary** in a case **where the fire has** already **taken hold of it,** i.e., the handful began to burn before it came down from the altar. Ulla teaches that in such a case the priests should return the handful to the altar, as its *piggul* status has already left it.

The *gemara* then suggests: *ve-iba'is eima de-im yardu ya'alu* — perhaps the novelty is that if such a *pigul kometz* was removed from the *mizbeach*, it should be put back, since we regard it as if it were not *pigul* at all, and therefore the proactive obligation to offer it remains. But this too the *gemara* rejects with another *tanina*: a *mishna* explicitly teaches *keshem she'im alu lo yardu, kach im yardu lo ya'alu* — just as if they went up they are not taken down, so too if they were taken down, they are not put back up. This principle explicitly includes *pigul*. So that conceptually appealing inference — that if the *kometz* is regarded as not *pigul*, it should be restored to the *mizbeach* — is directly contradicted by an established *halacha*.

### Fire Takes Hold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי אַמְרַהּ עוּלָּא חֲדָא זִימְנָא, דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא מָשְׁלָה בָּהֶן הָאוּר, אֲבָל מָשְׁלָה בָּהֶן הָאוּר – יַעֲלוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: But **this** *halakha* **too, Ulla** already **said it** on **another occasion. As Ulla says:** The mishna **taught** that items that descended from the altar shall not ascend again **only where the fire has not taken hold of them, but where the fire has** already **taken hold of them, they shall ascend.** The Gemara explains: Even so, there is a novelty in Ulla's ruling:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי אֵבֶר – דִּמְחַבַּר; אֲבָל קוֹמֶץ, דְּמִיפְּרַת – אֵימָא לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Lest you say** that **this matter** applies only to **a limb** of an offering, **which is** all **connected** together so that it forms a single unit, and one can say that if fire took hold of part of it, all of it is considered the food of the altar, and therefore it is returned to the altar if it came down; **but** with regard to **a handful, which** consists of **separate** pieces, perhaps only the part that the fire took hold of is returned to the altar, but as for the rest you might **say** that it does **not** ascend again. Therefore, Ulla **teaches us** that the same *halakha* applies to the handful as to a limb, i.e., if it descended from the altar after fire already took hold of any part of it, it ascends once again in its entirety to the altar.

The *gemara* resolves the matter: *lo tzericha she-mashlah bahen ha-ur* — Ulla is speaking specifically about a case where the fire of the *mizbeach* has taken hold of the *kometz*. Rashi explains this with the phrase *na'aseh lachmo shel mizbeach* — once the fire has truly seized the item, it is transformed, and is now regarded as belonging to and consumed by the *mizbeach*. Once that transformation occurs, even if the item is taken down, it is put back up, because it is no longer viewed as the same disqualified object it was before.

The *gemara* notes that even this principle — that once the fire takes hold, the item is restored even if removed — is not new from Ulla. Ulla himself had already stated elsewhere: *lo shanu ela she-lo mashlah bahen ha-ur* — the rule that disqualified items taken down are not replaced applies only before the fire has taken hold; *aval mashlah bahen ha-ur ya'alu* — once the fire has taken hold, they are put back up even if removed. That statement applied to all categories of *pesulim*.

What, then, is Ulla's novelty here? The *gemara* concludes that Ulla is teaching a specific refinement regarding the *kometz* of a *mincha*. One might have thought that the principle of *mashlah bahen ha-ur* — fire taking hold — applies to whole units, such as a limb of a *korban*, where once the fire consumes the majority of the limb, the entire limb is regarded as transformed. But a *kometz* of flour and oil is not a single firm unit; it can be separated into pieces. One might therefore have thought that even if the fire took hold of the majority of the *kometz*, the remaining portion that was not yet burned is to be regarded as a separate entity, and the transformation does not extend to it. *Ulla* teaches otherwise: since the *kometz* was placed on the *mizbeach* as a single unit, we always regard it conceptually as a single unit. Once the fire has consumed the majority of it, the entire *kometz* is regarded as transformed — *lachmo shel mizbeach* — and if any part of it is taken down, even the unburned portion, it is to be restored to the *mizbeach*. The *chiddush* is that the scattered or fractured nature of the *kometz* does not defeat the unifying logic of *mashlah bahen ha-ur berubo*.

It is important to emphasize that this conclusion has nothing to do with Ulla's earlier reasoning about the *matir* not being itself *pigul*. The principle of *mashlah bahen ha-ur* is a general principle applicable to all *pesulim* — a *mincha* that is *tamei*, a *mincha* that is *yotzei*, any category of disqualification — and its extension to a fractured *kometz* is a halachic point about the unity of an offering placed on the *mizbeach*, not about the unique logical status of a *matir* in the *pigul* framework.

### Rav Achai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחַאי: הִלְכָּךְ, הַאי קוֹמֶץ פִּיגּוּל, דְּפַלְגֵיהּ מַחְמִין אַאַרְעָא וּפַלְגֵיהּ אַסְּקַנְהוּ אַמַּעֲרָכָה, וּמָשְׁלַת בּוֹ הָאוּר – מַסְּקִינַן לֵיהּ לְכוּלֵּיהּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥai says:** Since the handful is considered one unit, **therefore,** in the case of **this handful of *piggul*, half of which lies on the ground and half of which was brought up to the** wood **arrangement** on the altar **and the fire took hold of it, one brings all of it up** to the altar ***ab initio*.**

*Amar Rav Achai* — Rav Achai (whose statements are sometimes understood as of Geonic provenance) draws a further inference from Ulla's ruling. He states: *hai kometz pigul de-palgei machis ara u-palgei askei amaaracha* — consider a case where a *pigul kometz* was divided in two, with one half left on the ground of the *Beis HaMikdash* and the other, larger half (say, sixty percent) placed on the *mizbeach* and burned. *Umashlaba beih or* — and the fire has now consumed that portion. Since Ulla teaches that even when the *kometz* is in separate pieces we regard it as a single unit once the fire has taken hold of the majority, Rav Achai reasons that *maskinei lei lekulei lechatchila* — we should place the remaining half on the *mizbeach* proactively, even though it had never been on the *mizbeach* at all.

This is a somewhat difficult ruling to follow. It is one thing to say that when the *kometz* is placed as a unit on the *mizbeach* and then breaks apart, we continue to regard it as one unit, so that the fire's transformation of the majority extends to the parts that come down. But if a portion was never placed on the *mizbeach* at all, it is harder to understand why it should be regarded as part of the transformed unit. Moreover, there is a practical puzzle: if the entire *kometz* has not been offered, then strictly speaking the *matir* of the *korban* has not been fully brought, and the *korban* may not have fully acquired *pigul* status. Would it not be wiser simply not to offer the remaining portion? This line of Rav Achai does not fit smoothly within the logical framework of the *sugya*, which is perhaps why some treat his statements as Geonic additions that occasionally stand somewhat independently of the preceding *amoraic* analysis.

## Status Change

### Status Removed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַפִּיגּוּל וְהַנּוֹתָר וְהַטָּמֵא שֶׁהֶעֱלָן לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ – פָּקַע אִיסּוּר מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to ***piggul*, *notar*,** i.e., offerings that remain after the time allotted for their consumption, **and ritually impure** flesh, **where one brought them up to the altar, their prohibition has left them.**

The *gemara* transitions to a related but broader statement. *Amar Rabbi Yitzchak amar Rabbi Yochanan: hapigul vehanotar vehatamei she-he'alo legabei mizbeach paka isur meihen* — Rabbi Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states that if *pigul*, *notar* (leftover sacrificial meat), or something *tamei* was placed on the *mizbeach*, the *issur* departs from them. This is a significantly more sweeping claim than what Ulla said specifically about the *kometz*: it applies to all three disqualifications and states that placing the item on the *mizbeach* actually removes its prohibited status.

### Rav Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מָרֵי דֵּיכִי; מִזְבֵּחַ – מִקְוֵה טָהֳרָה?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said** in astonishment: **Teacher of this** *halakha*, is the **altar a bath of ritual purification** that can render an impure item pure?

*Amar Rav Chisda* — Rav Chisda challenges this sharply: *mari de-hai, mizbeach mikveh tahara?* — the master of this teaching, is the *mizbeach* a *mikveh* for ritual purification? How does placing *tamei* meat on the *mizbeach* cause it to stop being *tamei*? What is the mechanism by which the *mizbeach* removes a status of *tumah*?

### Fire Transforms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: שֶׁמָּשְׁלָה בָּהֶן הָאוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira says:** This is referring to a case **where the fire took hold of them,** and therefore the item belongs to the altar and the prohibition lapses.

*Amar Rav Zeira: she-mashlah bahen ha-or* — the answer, says Rav Zeira, is again the principle of *mashlah bahen ha-ur*. Once the fire of the *mizbeach* truly takes hold of the item, it is transformed. It is no longer viewed as *tamei* meat or *pigul* eimurim; it has become something else entirely — an object consumed by the *mizbeach*. For the same reason that an item once transformed by fire is restored to the *mizbeach* even if taken down, so too does it no longer carry the *issur* of *tumah* or *pigul* as an eating prohibition. One who eats from it would not be liable for having consumed *tamei* meat, because the object has changed its identity.

### Tamei Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר בִּיסְנָא: אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: ״וְטֻמְאָתוֹ עָלָיו״ – מִי שֶׁטּוּמְאָה פּוֹרַחַת מִמֶּנּוּ; יָצָא בָּשָׂר – שֶׁאֵין טוּמְאָה פּוֹרַחַת מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Bisna raises an objection** from a *baraita*. **Others say:** The verse states: "But the soul that eats of the meat of the sacrifice of peace offerings, which pertain to the Lord, **having his impurity upon him,** that soul shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 7:20). Although this can also be read as: Having its impurity upon it, referring to the meat of the peace offerings, the verse in fact is referring to **one whose impurity can depart from him,** i.e., a person who is currently impure, but can attain a state of ritual purity by immersing in a ritual bath. This serves to **exclude** the impure **flesh** of offerings, **as its impurity cannot depart from it,** since ritually impure flesh cannot be purified.

*Metiv Rav Yitzchak bar Bisna* — Rav Yitzchak bar Bisna challenges this from a *braita*. *Acherim omrim: v'tumaso alav, mi she-tumah porachat mimenu; yatza basar she-ein tumah porachat mimenu* — others say in a *braita* that the verse *v'tumaso alav* (which we will analyze at length in the next section) must be referring to a person, because it implies the *tumah* is temporary and can depart; this excludes meat, because *tumah* does not depart from meat. But if the position of Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yochanan and Rav Zeira is correct, that fire does cause the *tumah* to depart from the meat, then the *braita*'s exclusion collapses — *tumah* would after all be capable of departing from meat, just through a different mechanism.

## Tumah Pesukim

### Tzav Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: ״וְטֻמְאָתוֹ עָלָיו״ – בְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara proceeds to analyze **the** matter **itself.** In the *baraita* the Rabbis attempt to prove that the verse: "But the soul that eats of the meat of the sacrifice of peace offerings, which pertain to the Lord, having his impurity upon him, that soul shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 7:20), is referring to a ritually impure person, and not to impure flesh. The *baraita* states: **"Having his impurity upon him"; the verse speaks of impurity of the body** of the person, not the impurity of the flesh of the offering.

The discussion of *tumah* departing from meat leads the *gemara* into a sustained analysis of a *pasuk* in Tzav (Vayikra 7). The verses there read as follows: *v'habasar asher yiga bechol tamei lo yei'achel ba'esh yisaref* — meat that touches anything *tamei* may not be eaten and must be burned; *v'habasar kol tahor yochal basar* — only someone *tahor* may eat the meat. Then: *v'hanefesh asher tochal basar mizevach hashelamim asher l'Hashem, v'tumaso alav, v'nichrasa hanefesh hahi mei-ameha* — the soul that eats from the *shelamim* offering of God, and its/his *tumah* is upon it/him, that soul shall be cut off from its people.

### Tumah On Whom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף; אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּטוּמְאַת בָּשָׂר? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן: ״טֻמְאָתוֹ״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן: ״טֻמְאָתוֹ (עָלָיו) [בּוֹ]״ – מָה לְהַלָּן בְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אַף כָּאן בְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The question may be raised: **Do you say** that it is dealing **with impurity of the body? Or is it** speaking **only of impurity of the flesh,** as is suggested by the fact that the term for "meat" [*basar*] is masculine, matching the masculine pronominal suffix attached to the word "impurity," whereas the word for "soul" [*nefesh*] is feminine? The answer is that **here it is stated:** "Having **his impurity** upon him," **and there it is stated:** "Whoever touches the dead, the body of any man that has died, and does not purify himself, he has defiled the Tabernacle of the Lord; and that soul shall be cut off from Israel, because the water of sprinkling was not sprinkled upon him, he shall be impure, **his impurity is** yet **upon him"** (Numbers 19:13). **Just as there, the verse is speaking of impurity of the body, so too here, the verse is speaking of impurity of the body.**

The question is: does *v'tumaso alav* refer to the *tumah* being on the person eating, or on the meat itself? The *halacha* is that *kares* is incurred when the *person* is *tamei* and eats *shelamim*, whereas eating *tamei* meat carries only a negative prohibition without *kares*. But how do we know that *v'tumaso alav* is not referring to the meat? Indeed, Rashi notes that there is a grammatical argument in favor of reading it as referring to the meat: the subject *nefesh* is feminine, and the passage uses feminine verb forms — *tochal*, not *yochal*; *v'nichrasa*, not *v'nichrat*. Yet *v'tumaso alav* uses masculine forms — *tumaso* and *alav* — which grammatically correspond to *basar* (meat), a masculine noun, rather than to *nefesh*, a feminine noun. So the grammar actually seems to support that *v'tumaso alav* refers to the *tumah* of the meat, not of the person.

How then does the *halacha* learn that this verse is speaking of the person being *tamei*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֶמְרוּ קָדָשִׁים בִּלְשׁוֹן רַבִּים, וְנֶאֶמְרָה טוּמְאָה בִּלְשׁוֹן יָחִיד – בְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״וְאָכַל״ – בְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says** there is a different proof: **Since** in this verse (Leviticus 7:20) the **sacrificial** animals **are mentioned in the plural** form, i.e., "peace offerings," **but** the **impurity is mentioned in the singular:** "Upon him," evidently **the verse is speaking of impurity of the body,** and not impurity of the peace offerings. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** there is yet another proof: Since the next verse states: "And when anyone shall touch any impure item…**and eat** of the meat of the sacrifice of peace offerings" (Leviticus 7:21), this indicates that **the verse is speaking of impurity of the body,** as will be explained.

The *braita* presents several answers. The first is a *gezeira shava*: *ne'emar kan tumaso v'ne'emar lehalan tumaso* — the word *tumaso* appears here and also in another context, where a *tamei* person enters the *Beis HaMikdash* and the verse says *o tumaso bo*, clearly referring to the person being *tamei*. By *gezeira shava*, *tumaso* here likewise refers to the person.

*Rabbi Yosi* offers a different reading: *ho'il v'ne'emru kodashim bilshon rabim v'ne'emar tumah bilshon yachid, bitumas haguf hakatuv medaber* — since *shelamim* is expressed in the plural while *tumah* is expressed in the singular, the *tumah* cannot be referring to the *shelamim*, which would require a plural form; therefore it must refer to the person. This is admittedly a difficult explanation to follow — *shelamim* as a word does carry a plural-looking ending, but it refers to a single sacrifice, and in any case the subject of the *tumah* would be the meat (*basar*), not the *shelamim* as such.

*Rabbi* (Rebbi) then states: *v'achal bitumas haguf hakatuv medaber* — the word *v'achal* in the immediately following verse indicates that this is about the person being *tamei*. The *gemara* will interrogate what exactly Rebbi means by this.

### Acherim View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחֵרִים אָמְרוּ: ״וְטֻמְאָתוֹ עָלָיו״ – מִי שֶׁטּוּמְאָה פּוֹרַחַת מִמֶּנּוּ; יָצָא בָּשָׂר, שֶׁאֵין טוּמְאָה פּוֹרַחַת מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Others say** that the phrase **"upon him"** proves that the reference here is to **one whose impurity can depart from him,** i.e., a person. This serves to **exclude** the impure **flesh** of offerings, **as** its **impurity cannot depart from it.** This concludes the *baraita*.

*Acherim omrim* — others offer yet another derivation: *v'tumaso alav, mi she-tumah porachat mimenu; yatza basar she-ein tumah porachat mimenu* — the phrase *alav* (upon him/it) implies that the *tumah* is a temporary condition, something that can depart while the subject remains intact. This can only describe a person, since a person can go to the *mikveh* and be purified while remaining the same person. Meat, by contrast, cannot be rendered *tahor* — food that has become *tamei* has no mechanism for purification while remaining itself. Therefore, *v'tumaso alav* must refer to the person, not the meat.

It is at this juncture that Rav Yitzchak bar Bisna's challenge to Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yochanan's statement gains its force: if the fire of the *mizbeach* causes *tumah* to depart from meat, then the *Acherim*'s exclusion of meat (on the grounds that *tumah* does not leave it) would be undermined.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: עַל יְדֵי מִקְוֶה קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** When the *baraita* speaks of an item whose impurity cannot depart from it, **we say** it is referring to purification **by means of a ritual bath,** not through any other means.

The *gemara* first records Rava's response: *al yedei mikveh ka'aminan* — Rava says that *Acherim* are referring specifically to removal through *mikveh*. The *mikveh* can remove *tumah* from a person but not from food; fire operating on meat placed on the *mizbeach* is a different mechanism.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי מִקְוֶה כְּתִיב?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Is** the term **ritual bath written** in the *baraita*?

The *gemara* challenges this immediately: *midi mikveh ksiv?* — where does the verse mention *mikveh*? The derivation from *alav* is that the *tumah* is a temporary, removable status. If one accepts that the fire of the *mizbeach* removes the *tumah*, then meat would also qualify as something from which *tumah* can depart, and the exclusion would fail.

### Ravina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: ״וְטֻמְאָתוֹ עָלָיו״ – מִי שֶׁטּוּמְאָה פּוֹרַחַת מִמֶּנּוּ כְּשֶׁהוּא שָׁלֵם; יָצָא בָּשָׂר – דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין טוּמְאָה פּוֹרַחַת כְּשֶׁהוּא שָׁלֵם, אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁהוּא חָסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says** there is a different answer: Even if the impurity of flesh leaves it when it is brought up to the altar, this verse cannot be referring to the impure meat of peace offerings, as the phrase **"having his impurity upon him"** is referring to **one whose impurity departs from him when he is whole;** the term "upon him" indicates that he is in a state of wholeness. This serves to **exclude** sacrificial **flesh,** which is **an item whose impurity does not depart from it when it is whole, but** only **when it is deficient,** i.e., when fire takes hold of it on the altar.

*Ravina amar* — Ravina offers a more precise formulation. *V'tumaso alav, mi she-tumah porachat mimenu ke-she-hu shalem; yatza basar, davar she-ein tumah porachat ke-she-hu shalem, ela ke-she-hu chaseir* — the word *alav* implies that the *tumah* is a temporary condition on this entity, and the entity itself remains the same entity after the *tumah* departs. That is, the *tumah* can leave it while it remains *shalem* — whole and intact. This fits a person perfectly: a *tamei* person goes to the *mikveh*, the *tumah* leaves, and the same person stands there, now *tahor*. But for meat, *tumah* does not depart from the meat while it remains the same meat. If anything departs from it, it is only when the meat has been transformed — when it has changed identity, as when it is consumed by the fire of the *mizbeach*. The *tumah* does not leave the meat as it is; only when the meat becomes something else does the question of its prior *tumah* cease to be relevant. Therefore, the verse's use of *alav* cannot be referring to meat, because meat lacks the property of being something from which *tumah* can depart while the thing itself remains *shalem*. The verse must be referring to the person.

This answer is elegant and also helps clarify the underlying logic of Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yochanan's original statement. When he said the *issur* departs from *tamei* items placed on the *mizbeach*, the mechanism is not that the *tumah* left the meat in the sense *Acherim* are discussing — it is that the item transformed. Ravina's formulation is thus consistent with both the *gezeira shava* of *Acherim* and the principle of *mashlah bahen ha-ur*.

## Braita Analysis

### Emor Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתָא – דְּתַנְיָא: אִם נֶאֶמְרוּ קַלּוֹת לָמָּה נֶאֶמְרוּ חֲמוּרוֹת, וְאִם (נאמר) [נֶאֶמְרוּ] חֲמוּרוֹת לָמָּה (נאמר) [נֶאֶמְרוּ] קַלּוֹת? אִם (נאמר) [נֶאֶמְרוּ] קַלּוֹת וְלֹא חֲמוּרוֹת – הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: עַל הַקַּלּוֹת בְּלָאו, וְעַל הַחֲמוּרוֹת בְּמִיתָה; לְכָךְ (נאמר) [נֶאֶמְרוּ] חֲמוּרוֹת. וְאִם (נאמר) [נֶאֶמְרוּ] חֲמוּרוֹת וְלֹא נֶאֶמְרוּ קַלּוֹת – הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: עַל הַחֲמוּרוֹת יְהֵא חַיָּיב, וְעַל הַקַּלּוֹת יְהֵא פָּטוּר; לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר קַלּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the ***baraita*,** alluded to by Rava, through which the interpretative prowess of Rav Ze'eiri is demonstrated. This *baraita* also discusses the topic of eating consecrated food while in a state of ritual impurity. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **If** the **lenient are stated, why are** the **stringent stated; and if** the **stringent** are **stated, why** are the **lenient stated? If** the **lenient were stated and not** the **stringent, I would say: For the lenient,** one is liable to receive lashes **for** violating **a prohibition, and for the stringent,** one is liable **to** be punished with **death** at the hand of Heaven. **Therefore,** the **stringent** are **stated. And if** the **stringent were stated and** the **lenient were not stated, I would say:** Only **for the stringent should one be liable** to receive a punishment; **but for the lenient, one should be** entirely **exempt. Therefore,** the **lenient** are **stated.** This concludes the *baraita*, the meaning of which is opaque.

The *gemara* now turns to a parallel passage in *Emor* (Vayikra 22), which addresses *tumah* and eating *kodashim* more broadly. The relevant verses establish that a *kohen* who is *tamei* — whether from *tzaraat*, a *zav* condition, contact with a *tamei* person, or contact with a *sheretz* — may not eat from the *kodashim* until he has immersed and the sun has set. The opening verse of this section states: *kol ish asher yikrav mikol zar'achem el hakodashim asher yakdishu vnei Yisrael l'Hashem v'tumaso alav, v'nichrasa hanefesh hahi milfanai ani Hashem* — any person of your seed who approaches the holy things that Israel has consecrated to God while his *tumah* is upon him, that soul shall be cut off before Me; I am God. *Chazal* understand the *kodashim* here to include not only *korbanot* but also *ma'aser sheni* and *terumah*.

### Braita Intro

The *gemara* presents a *braita* that is, as Rava previewed, quite difficult to understand without the explanation of Rav Zeira. The *braita* reads: *im ne'emru kalim, lama ne'emru chamuros; ve-im ne'emru chamuros, lama ne'emru kalos* — if the light cases were stated, why were the weighty cases needed? And if the weighty cases were stated, why were the light ones needed? The *braita* continues: had only the light cases been stated, I would have said that for the light ones there is a negative prohibition and for the weighty ones there is *misa* (death at the hands of Heaven). The *Torah* therefore stated the weighty cases to teach that they too carry only a negative prohibition, not *misa*. Conversely, had only the weighty cases been stated, I would have said that for the weighty ones there is a negative prohibition but for the light ones there is no prohibition at all. The *Torah* therefore stated the light ones as well.

### Light & Heavy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קַלּוֹת וּמַאי חֲמוּרוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** are the **lenient, and what** are the **stringent?**

The *gemara* asks: *mai kalos u-mai chamuros* — what are the light cases and what are the heavy cases? The *braita*'s structure requires that the conclusion be: whether light or heavy, the punishment is only a negative prohibition and not *misa*. This will prove to be the key constraint on interpreting the *braita*.

### Maaser vs Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵימָא קַלּוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר, חֲמוּרוֹת תְּרוּמָה – הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר בְּמִיתָה?! הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי הָא בְּמִיתָה!
>
> **Translation:** It is known that the *baraita* is discussing the broad topic of eating consecrated food in a state of impurity, but its precise meaning requires elucidation. **If we say** that the **lenient** is referring to the consumption of second **tithe** while one is impure, and the **stringent** is referring to the partaking of ***teruma*,** the portion of the produce designated for the priest, in a state of impurity, how can the *baraita* say that had the Torah stated only the prohibition against eating second tithe **I would** incorrectly **have said** that one who partakes of *teruma* is liable to be punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven? **Now too, this** is the *halakha*; one who partakes of *teruma* when he is in a state of impurity is indeed liable **to** be punished with **death** at the hand of Heaven.

The first suggestion is that *kalos* refers to being *tamei* and eating *ma'aser*, and *chamuros* refers to being *tamei* and eating *terumah*. The *braita* would then be saying: had the *Torah* only stated the light case (*ma'aser*) with a negative prohibition, I might have thought that *terumah* (the weightier *kedushah*) would carry *misa*; the *Torah* therefore tells you that *terumah* also carries only a negative prohibition. But this is impossible: the *halacha* is that a *tamei* person who eats *terumah* does incur *misa bidei shamayim*. So the *braita* cannot be saying the punishment for *terumah* is only a *lav*. Furthermore, even the logical structure fails: if I started from *ma'aser* with a *lav*, how would I ever have thought to reach *misa* for *terumah*? The rule of *dayo lavo min hadin lihyos kenidon* — a *kal vachomer* cannot produce a conclusion more severe than its premise — would prevent me from deriving *misa* from a case that itself carries only a *lav*. So this interpretation is doubly untenable.

### Sheretz vs Meis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא קַלּוֹת – טוּמְאַת שֶׁרֶץ, חֲמוּרוֹת – טוּמְאַת מֵת. וּבְמַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** when the *baraita* refers to the **lenient** it is referring to **the impurity of** the carcass of **a creeping animal,** while the **stringent** is referring to **the impurity** imparted by **a corpse,** as the Torah discusses both these cases in the context of eating consecrated foods in a state of ritual impurity: "And whoever touches anything that is impure by the dead, or a man whose semen goes from him, or whoever touches any creeping animal" (Leviticus 22:4–5). The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **to what** food is this referring?

The second suggestion is that *kalos* and *chamuros* refer to the degrees of *tumah* rather than the food consumed: *kalos* is *tumas sheretz* (the lighter *tumah* from contact with a creeping thing) and *chamuros* is *tumas meis* (the weightier *tumah* from a corpse). The *braita* would then be teaching that whether you have a light *tumah* or a heavy *tumah*, the punishment is only a *lav*.

The *gemara* then asks: with which food are we discussing this? If the *braita* is speaking about *terumah*, then again both cases actually carry *misa*, not merely a *lav*, which contradicts the *braita*'s conclusion. And if we are speaking about *ma'aser*, the logical structure again breaks down: my starting point would be *tumas sheretz* eating *ma'aser* — a *lav*. How would I have thought that *tumas meis* eating *ma'aser* could produce *misa*? The *dayo* principle would prevent me from constructing a *kal vachomer* that reaches beyond the original case of a simple *lav*.

### Rav Zeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר זְעֵירִי: קַלּוֹת – טוּמְאַת שֶׁרֶץ, חֲמוּרוֹת – טוּמְאַת מֵת. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר טוּמְאַת שֶׁרֶץ וְנֶאֱמַר מַעֲשֵׂר וּתְרוּמָה, וְלֹא נֶאֶמְרָה טוּמְאַת מֵת – הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: [קַלּוֹת] עַל הַקַּלּוֹת בְּלָאו, וְעַל הַחֲמוּרוֹת בְּמִיתָה;
>
> **Translation:** **Ze'eiri says:** Indeed, the **lenient** case is referring to **the impurity of a creeping animal,** and the **stringent** case is referring to **impurity** imparted by **a corpse. And this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: If the impurity of a creeping animal was stated, and it was stated** that one who eats second **tithe** while impure with such impurity has violated a prohibition **and** one who partakes of ***teruma*** in that state is liable to be punished with death at the hand of Heaven, **and the impurity** imparted by **a corpse was not stated** in this context, **I would say** as follows: The **lenient** level of impurity, that of a creeping animal, **with regard to** food with **lenient** *halakhot*, second tithe, **involves** the violation of **a prohibition, and with regard to** food with **stringent** *halakhot*, *teruma*, it involves liability to be punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven.

Here the *gemara* delivers on Rava's earlier promise: *amar Zeiri* — it is Rav Zeira who now provides the brilliant resolution. *Kalos tumas sheretz, chamuros tumas meis* — the degrees of *tumah* are indeed the axis of *kalos* and *chamuros*, as the second proposal suggested. But here is the precise reconstruction of the *braita*'s reasoning.

Had the *Torah* stated only *tumas sheretz* — the lighter *tumah* — in connection with both *ma'aser* and *terumah* (establishing that *tumas sheretz* with *ma'aser* produces a *lav* and *tumas sheretz* with *terumah* produces *misa*), I would have had a point of departure for a *kal vachomer*. The reasoning would run as follows: a *light* form of *tumah* (*tumas sheretz*) combined with a *heavy* form of eating (*terumah*) produces *misa*. By analogy, perhaps a *heavy* form of *tumah* (*tumas meis*) combined with a *light* form of eating (*ma'aser*) should also produce *misa*, since in each case one of the two variables is at its maximum weight. The *Torah* therefore needed to state the case of *tumas meis* explicitly, to teach that even in that case — even with the heaviest *tumah* eating the lightest *kedushahma'aser* — the punishment is only a *lav*, not *misa*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּקַלּוֹת עַל הַחֲמוּרוֹת בְּמִיתָה – חֲמוּרוֹת נָמֵי עַל הַקַּלּוֹת בְּמִיתָה; לְכָךְ נֶאֶמְרוּ חֲמוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Ze'eiri continues his explanation of the *baraita*: **And from** the fact **that** the **lenient** level of impurity, that of a creeping animal, **with regard to** food with **stringent** *halakhot*, *teruma*, involves liability to be punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven, it may be inferred that **also** in the analogous case of the **stringent** level of impurity, imparted by a corpse, **with regard to** food with **lenient** *halakhot*, there is liability to be punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven. **Therefore, the stringent** level of impurity, imparted by a corpse, **was stated** with regard to second tithe, which has lenient *halakhot*, to teach that even if one contracted impurity from a corpse, he has violated only a prohibition for eating second tithe, and is not liable to be punished with death at the hand of Heaven.

Conversely, had the *Torah* stated only *tumas meis* (the heavier *tumah*) with *ma'aser* and established that as a *lav*, I would have thought that *tumas sheretz* (the lighter *tumah*) with *ma'aser* might carry no prohibition at all — the *kal vachomer* reasoning that a lighter *tumah* is less severe and perhaps not prohibited at all when eating *ma'aser*. The *Torah* therefore stated the lighter case of *tumas sheretz* eating *ma'aser* as well, to establish that even the lighter *tumah* carries a *lav* for eating *ma'aser*.

This is a genuinely elegant solution. The *braita* is comparing two different combinations of *tumah* and food: the *kal* combination is heavy-*tumah* with light-food (*tumas meis* eating *ma'aser*), and the *chamur* combination in the background is light-*tumah* with heavy-food (*tumas sheretz* eating *terumah*, which carries *misa*). The *chiddush* of stating the *kal* combination explicitly is that I might have thought the crossing of a heavy *tumah* with a light food would also escalate to *misa*, by analogy with the crossing of a light *tumah* with heavy food. The *Torah* tells us no: it is only a *lav*. And the *chiddush* of stating the *kalos* case (*tumas sheretz* with *ma'aser*) is that I might have thought the light combination carries nothing at all. The two explicit statements are thus mutually necessary, each preventing an erroneous inference that the other alone would leave open. The daf concludes here.
