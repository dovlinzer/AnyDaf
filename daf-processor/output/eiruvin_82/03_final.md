# Eiruvin 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Techumin vs. Chatzeiros

### Storekeeper Money Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדִים בִּשְׁאָר כׇּל הָאָדָם, שֶׁזָּכוּ לוֹ מְעוֹתָיו. שֶׁאֵין מְעָרְבִין לָאָדָם אֶלָּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Rabbis **concede with regard to all** other **people,** apart from grocers and bakers, **that** if one gave them money for the food of an *eiruv*, **his money confers possession upon him, as one** may **establish an *eiruv* for a person only with his knowledge** and at his bidding.

The daf opens by reviewing the *mishna* discussed previously, which addressed the case of a person who gave money to a storekeeper to purchase bread for an *eiruv*. The *mishna* ruled that even though the person clearly intended to participate in the *eiruv*, his participation is ineffective. The reasoning is twofold: he never actually acquired the bread through purchase, and moreover, even if the bread was subsequently used by another person to make an *eiruv* on his behalf, it does not work for him, because he had specified that he wanted to do it through purchase — not through someone else's gift of a portion.

This seems like an extraordinarily technical disqualification. After all, the person's desire to create an *eiruv* is manifest. Nevertheless, the *halacha* does not bend to the broad intent when the specific modality chosen by the participant was not realized.

### L'da'ato Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים בְּעֵירוּבֵי תְחוּמִין, אֲבָל בְּעֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת — מְעָרְבִין לְדַעְתּוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לְדַעְתּוֹ, לְפִי שֶׁזָּכִין לָאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו, וְאֵין חָבִין לָאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: In what case is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to a joining of** Shabbat **boundaries, but with regard to a joining of courtyards, one** may **establish an *eiruv*** for a person either **with his knowledge or without his knowledge.** The reason is **because one may act for a person's benefit in his absence, but one may not act to a person's disadvantage in his absence.**

To this point, Rabbi Yehuda offered what the *gemara* examines as either a clarification or a dispute. Rabbi Yehuda distinguishes between *eiruv techumin* and *eiruv chatzeiros*. An *eiruv techumin* involves an inherent trade-off: whatever a person gains by extending his boundary in one direction, he loses in the other. Because of this zero-sum character, the *eiruv techumin* must conform precisely to the person's own wishes. Even if he broadly signaled his desire to create one, the technical requirements must be met exactly as he intended. By contrast, *eiruv chatzeiros* is exclusively to the participant's benefit, and therefore it need not be made *l'da'ato* — with that person's explicit knowledge or consent.

This distinction is eminently sensible. The *gemara* at the close of the previous discussion explored whether anyone disputes Rabbi Yehuda's position and would require that *eiruv chatzeiros* also be made *l'da'ato*. As the *gemara* noted, it is difficult to understand why anyone would raise such a requirement when one is using one's own food to be *mezakeh* — to transfer possession to — another person, since receiving a benefit requires no consent. The debate, it seems, concerns more specifically the case where it is the other person's food being used on his behalf: can one take that person's food to make an *eiruv* for him, even without his knowledge? Either way, the ruling follows Rabbi Yehuda: *eiruv chatzeiros* is a *zechus*, a pure benefit, and may therefore be established even *shelo l'da'as* — without the participant's awareness. Whether this applies even when using the participant's own food remains somewhat unclear.

### Eimasai vs. Bameh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? הָא לֹא מָשַׁךְ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara poses a question: **What is the reason for Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion that one who gave money to a grocer or a baker has acquired possession of the food for the *eiruv*? This ruling is difficult, as **he did not** perform a transaction by **pulling** the food into his possession, and one can acquire an object only by performing a valid act of acquisition.

The *gemara* continues with the *gufa* — picking up the discussion two lines from the top — which touches on a broader principle of Talmudic interpretation. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi states: whenever Rabbi Yehuda employs the word *eimasai* ("when?") in a *mishna*, he is not disputing the *chachamim* but merely clarifying the conditions of their ruling. Rabbi Yochanan, however, draws a distinction: *eimasai* is indeed a clarification, but *bameh* ("in what cases?") signals an actual dispute — Rabbi Yehuda is restricting the application of the ruling to certain circumstances, thereby excluding others.

Both formulations qualify the preceding statement, but in different ways. The *eimasai* formulation says "this is when the rule applies" without contesting the underlying position; the *bameh* formulation carves out a narrower scope, implicitly rejecting the broader application. The practical significance of this distinction reverberates throughout the *gemara*'s reading of various *mishnayos*.

### Pasul Le'edut Gamblers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ בְּבָרַיְיתָא, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בֵּין שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אוּמָּנוּת אֶלָּא הִיא, וּבֵין שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אוּמָּנוּת שֶׁלֹּא הִיא — הֲרֵי זֶה פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its difficulty: According to the above principle with regard to statements introduced with the term when, Rabbi Yehuda's statement should be understood merely as an explanation of the previous opinion. However, **a *baraita* was taught about** the mishna: **And the Rabbis say: Whether he has no occupation other than this** one, **or whether he has a** fitting **occupation other than this, this** person **is disqualified** from giving testimony.

The *gemara* now tests the above principle with a concrete case. The *mishna* in another tractate lists those who are disqualified from giving testimony: one who plays dice (*ha-mesachek be-kubya*), one who lends with interest (*ha-malveh be-ribis*), one who races pigeons (*mafrichei yonim*), and those who trade in *shevi'is* produce. The common thread is a willingness to pursue improper monetary gain, which creates grounds to suspect that such a person might also testify falsely for money.

Rabbi Yehuda then says *eimasai* — "when is this true?" — and limits the disqualification of the gambler to cases where gambling is the person's sole occupation. If the person has another legitimate profession, he is *kasher* — qualified to testify.

## Asmachta Kanya Debate

### Asmachta Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** opinion in the *baraita*, with regard to those disqualified from providing testimony, is not the opinion of the Rabbis of the mishna. Rather, **it is** that **of Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintained that **Rabbi Tarfon said** this opinion.

The *gemara* now enters a sustained discussion of *asmachta* — the question of whether a conditional agreement, specifically one entered into with an unrealized expectation, constitutes a binding *kinyan*. In the context of gambling, when two parties wager, each one only commits to pay because he assumes he will win. The loser never genuinely anticipated losing, and therefore, it is argued, never truly resolved in his mind to hand over the money. This is the essence of *asmachta*: one relies (*nismach*) on an outcome that may not materialize, and because the commitment was never fully internalized, there is a question whether it generates binding legal obligation. The formal discussion of *asmachta kanya* or *lo kanya* — whether such a conditional agreement acquires or does not — is developed at length in tractate Sanhedrin.

The first opinion holds that *asmachta lo kanya* — the agreement is not binding. Accordingly, when the loser pays and the winner accepts, the winner is in effect receiving money that he was never truly owed. The loser hands it over because he mistakenly believes an obligation exists, but no genuine obligation was created. This makes the winner a *gazlan* of sorts — not an outright thief in the conventional sense, since the money was given voluntarily, but technically taking money that does not rightfully belong to him. It is not a gift either, because the loser is not giving it as a gift; he gives it only because he thinks he owes it. On this view, gamblers are disqualified from testimony because they are effectively *gazlanim*.

### Rashi on Asmachta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: לְעוֹלָם אֵין אֶחָד מֵהֶן נָזִיר, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין נְזִירוּת אֶלָּא לְהַפְלָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita* with regard to naziriteship: **Rabbi Yehuda said in the name of Rabbi Tarfon:** In a case where two people accepted a bet, with each undertaking to become a nazirite if he lost the wager, and a doubt arose as to who won, **neither one** of them **can possibly be a nazirite, as there is no** acceptance of **naziriteship without** clear and definitive **pronunciation.**

Rabbi Yehuda, however, maintains that *asmachta kanya* — the bet is in fact a binding agreement. When Rashi explains Rabbi Yehuda's position, he cites the language: *de-kasvar Rabbi Yehuda asmachta she-hu nismach al da'ato she-hu yenatzechenu ve-al oto asmachta hu matneh kanya* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the conditional commitment one makes while relying on the expectation of victory is nonetheless binding, and even in the event of loss, one genuinely was committing to pay. Therefore, on Rabbi Yehuda's view, gamblers are not *gazlanim* at all.

### Lack of Honest Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא כֵּיוָן דִּמְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ אִי נָזִיר אִי לָא נָזִיר הוּא — לָא מְשַׁעְבֵּיד נַפְשֵׁיהּ. הָכָא נָמֵי: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא יָדַע אִי קָנֵי אִי לָא קָנֵי — לָא גָּמַר וּמַקְנֵה.
>
> **Translation:** **Consequently,** it can be inferred that **since he is in doubt** as to **whether he is a nazirite or he is not a nazirite, he does not submit himself** to and accept his vow of naziriteship. **Here, too,** Rabbi Yehuda disqualifies those who play games of chance from delivering testimony due to the fact that they are robbers. **Since** the player **does not know whether he will** win and **acquire** the money **or whether he will** lose and **not acquire** it, **he does not fully transfer ownership** of the money with which he plays to others, which means that the one who gains from these games receives money that was not wholeheartedly given to him.

If gamblers are not *gazlanim* according to Rabbi Yehuda, why then are they disqualified — even if only when gambling is their sole profession? Rashi explains: *u-psulay ninhu lefi she-ein oskym be-yishuvo shel olam* — they are disqualified because they do not engage in the productive sustenance of the world. Rashi elaborates: *vekivan de-ein makkirim be-ki'am be-torach ve-tza'ar benei adam* — since they have no experience with the genuine effort and hardship involved in earning a living, *einam hasim al havreihem le-hafsidam mammon* — they do not take seriously the financial harm they may cause others. A person who has never done an honest day's work cannot appreciate what money truly means to those who have, and therefore cannot be relied upon to resist the temptation to testify falsely in monetary cases.

This raises an interesting conceptual question: what of a person who spends all his time learning in a *yeshiva*, or one who trades in financial markets without engaging in conventional labor? The *gemara*'s concern is specifically directed at those who have neither honest work nor appreciation for the weight of others' earnings.

### Chachamim Debate

The *gemara* then notes that a *braita* records the *chachamim* as saying: *bein she-ein lo umnus ela hi bein she-yesh lo umnus she-lo hi harei zeh pasul* — whether gambling is one's sole profession or only an ancillary one, the person remains disqualified. This appears to contradict Rabbi Yehuda's clarification in the *mishna*, suggesting he was in fact disputing rather than clarifying.

### Multiple Opinions

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: the *chachamim* in the *braita* represent a distinct position, not the same *chachamim* of the *mishna* whom Rabbi Yehuda was clarifying. The *mishna* itself addressed only the case where gambling was the person's sole profession, and Rabbi Yehuda with his *eimasai* was clarifying that baseline — he was not disputing anyone in the *mishna*. The *braita*'s stricter ruling is attributed to Rabbi Yehuda himself, but cited in the name of Rabbi Tarfon, and it represents a separately recorded opinion. The *gemara* thus preserves Rabbi Yoshua ben Levi's rule that *eimasai* in a *mishna* denotes clarification, not dispute.

### Nezirut Analogy

The *braita* cited in this resolution contains a remarkable analogical argument. Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Tarfon states: if two people are sitting together and someone passes before them, and one says "I am a *nazir* if he is a *nazir*" while the other says "I am a *nazir* if he is not a *nazir*," neither of them is a *nazir*. Rabbi Yehuda says: *le-olam ein echad mehem nazir* — not one of them has become a *nazir*.

The reason is rooted in the concept of *hafla'ah* — the technical category of verbal declarations that create a halachic change of status, whether becoming a *nazir*, consecrating an object (*hekdesh*), and the like. Rashi explains: *lehafla'ah she-hi nidor meforash al ha-vadai ve-lo al ha-tzad safek* — such a declaration must be absolute, not contingent on an uncertain condition. The declaration must be fully explicit and certain; if it is conditional upon something that may or may not be the case, the *hafla'ah* does not take effect.

The *gemara* then makes a bold conceptual leap. It reasons: *alma, keivan de-masfka lei i nazir i lav nazir hu, lo meshabeid nafsheih* — since the person declaring *nezirus* does not know whether he will actually become a *nazir*, we conclude that he has not truly committed himself. And from this the *gemara* draws an analogy that extends far beyond the laws of *nezirus*: *hacha nami, keivan de-lo yadi'i i kani i lo kani, lo gamar u-makni* — here too, in the gambling context, since the bettor does not know whether he will win or lose, he has not truly resolved in his mind to pay in the event of loss, and therefore no genuine commitment is formed.

The *gemara* thereby converts what might have been a narrow technical requirement confined to the laws of *hafla'ah* and *nezirus* into a broader principle of *gemar da'as* — finality of intent — applicable across many legal categories. The question is no longer merely about the formalities of *nezirus* but about whether any binding commitment can be formed under conditions of genuine uncertainty regarding one's own position.

### Tosafos Analysis

Tosafos engages the *asmachta* question with a careful tripartite analysis. He distinguishes among three categories of wagers based on the degree to which skill or chance governs the outcome.

At one extreme are games of pure chance, such as a lottery. Here, Tosafos argues, *asmachta kanya* — the commitment is binding — because the participant enters knowing full well that he may lose. There is no illusion of control; the outcome is entirely random, and both parties understand this from the outset. Since the bettor genuinely reckons with the possibility of loss, his commitment to pay in that eventuality is real.

At the other extreme are commitments that are entirely within one's control — the classic *gemara* case of a penalty clause, such as "if I fail to plow your field, I will pay you a hundred thousand dollars." Even though the sum is disproportionate, since the performance is entirely up to the obligor, Tosafos suggests there may be grounds to say the commitment is genuine and therefore binding, even if the disproportion might otherwise suggest the person never truly intended to commit.

It is the middle category — partially skill, partially chance, such as games like cards or dice — that presents the most difficulty. Here the player genuinely believes in his own skill, expects to win, and therefore never truly commits in his mind to paying in the event of loss. This is the paradigm case of *asmachta*, and it is this category that generates the unresolved debate over *asmachta kanya* or *lo kanya*.

### Lottery Ticket Case

Tosafos raises an additional point at the end of his discussion. He observes that concerning those who make bets — *ve-osam ha-mesachakim be-amana* — Rabbi Yitzchak holds that such a bet is not legally binding. The question is: *be-mai yikneh* — by what act does the bet become binding? The problem has two dimensions. First, for those who hold *asmachta lo kanya*, no amount of formal procedure will create a binding obligation: *afilu be-kinyan* and even in the presence of a *beis din chashuv*, the bet will not take effect. Second, and separately, even for those who hold *asmachta kanya*, a verbal bet alone — *be-devarim belo kinyan* — lacks any formal *ma'aseh kinyan*, the act of acquisition required to create binding legal obligation. Tosafos thus concludes that for a bet to be halakhically enforceable, one must both resolve the *asmachta* question favorably and complete a formal *kinyan*.

This leads naturally to an anecdote involving a *shiur* delivered by Rav Lichtenstein on the question of purchasing a lottery ticket and whether it constitutes an *asmachta*. Rav Lichtenstein spent considerable time surveying the different *shittos* regarding *asmachta kanya* or *lo kanya* and the definitions of the category — and then concluded at the end that none of it was relevant. His reasoning: the buyer of a lottery ticket is not making a bet at all. He is purchasing an object — a ticket. Just as one buys an apple or any commodity, the buyer acquires the ticket, and if the ticket happens to win, so be it. The transactional framework, in his view, displaces the contractual betting framework entirely.

One might press the point, however: the concern underlying *asmachta* is not about the buyer's act of acquiring the ticket, but about what happens when the ticket wins. If *asmachta lo kanya* governs, then the lottery operator's promise to pay the winner was never truly binding — meaning the winner, in accepting the payout, may be taking money that is not genuinely owed. Who determines, at the moment of payment, what legal framework governs? Rav Lichtenstein's response, ultimately, was pragmatic: the lottery operator wants people to buy tickets, and therefore has every motivation to pay out. The halakhic enforceability of the underlying obligation, in this context, may be secondary to the commercial reality. The discussion remained open-ended, but the underlying *tosafos* point remains: if the lottery is pure chance, it may fall into the category where *asmachta kanya* naturally applies.

With that, the *perek* concludes. *Hadran alach Chalon*, and the new *perek* begins.

## Eruv Techumin Basics

### Making Eruv Techumin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד מִשְׁתַּתְּפִין בִּתְחוּמִין? מַנִּיחַ אֶת הֶחָבִית, וְאוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי זֶה לְכׇל בְּנֵי עִירִי, לְכׇל מִי שֶׁיֵּלֵךְ לְבֵית הָאֵבֶל אוֹ לְבֵית הַמִּשְׁתֶּה. וְכׇל שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם — מוּתָּר, מִשֶּׁתֶּחְשַׁךְ — אָסוּר. שֶׁאֵין מְעָרְבִין מִשֶּׁתֶּחְשַׁךְ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How does one participate in** the joining of Shabbat **boundaries?** One who wishes to establish a joining of Shabbat boundaries for himself and others **places a barrel** of food in the location he designates as their place of residence, **and says: This is for all the residents of my town, for anyone who** wishes to **go** on Shabbat **to a house of mourning or to a house of** a wedding **feast** situated beyond the Shabbat limit. **Anyone who accepted upon himself while it was still day,** i.e., before the onset of Shabbat, that he will rely on the *eiruv*, **is permitted** to rely upon it; but if one did so only **after nightfall,** he is **prohibited** to rely upon it, as the principle is that **one may not establish an *eiruv* after nightfall.**

Returning now to the core topic of *eiruv techumin*, the new *perek* opens with the question: *keitzad mishtatfin bitechumin* — how does one create a joint *eiruv techumin*? The *mishna* describes the following procedure: one takes a barrel filled with food — unlike *eiruv chatzeiros*, which must consist of bread, *eiruv techumin* may use any food — and declares: *harei zeh le-chol benei iri* — "this is for all the people of my city."

Since an *eiruv techumin* is not an unambiguous benefit, the person making it specifies the conditions: it applies to whoever wishes to go to a house of mourning (*beis ha-avel*) to offer consolation, or to a *sheva brachos* celebration in a neighboring town. These examples — *nichum aveilim* and *simchat chatan ve-kalla* — are *mitzvah* occasions, a point to which the *gemara* will return.

### Pre-Shabbat Acceptance

The *mishna* then states: *ve-chol she-kibel alav mi-be-od yom mutar* — anyone who, before the onset of *Shabbat*, explicitly accepted that he wished to avail himself of this *eiruv*, is permitted to travel in that direction. *Mishetachshach* — if one decided only after nightfall — *assur*, it is forbidden. The principle is that an *eiruv* must take effect from the preceding day; it cannot be established retroactively after *Shabbat* has begun.

This requirement is central to the structure of *eiruv techumin*. Because the *eiruv* involves a trade-off — extending one's boundary in one direction while forfeiting it in the other — a person cannot simply be swept into it without his own deliberate choice. The contrast with *eiruv chatzeiros* is instructive: there, the arrangement is wholly beneficial, and one may be included without prior awareness. Here, the participant must actively elect to join before *Shabbat* begins.

### No Mezakeh Required

A further striking distinction emerges from the *mishna*'s formulation. When making an *eiruv chatzeiros* for the residents of a courtyard, one is required to be *mezakeh* — to formally transfer possession of the *eiruv* food to the participants via a third party. The *mishna* here, however, makes no mention of any such act. The *gemara* observes: we are not performing the *mezakeh* procedure here. This was in fact the position of Shmuel, who held that *mezakeh* is required for *eiruv chatzeiros* but not for *eiruv techumin*. For *eiruv techumin*, it is sufficient that the person places food and the participants opt in; no formal transfer of ownership through a third party is necessary. This is quite remarkable: even if the food belongs to the person who established the *eiruv* rather than to the participants, and even without a formal *mezakeh* procedure, the *eiruv techumin* takes effect — provided the participant has elected to join before *Shabbat*.

### Dvar Mitzvah Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אֵין מְעָרְבִין אֶלָּא לִדְבַר מִצְוָה. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: לְכׇל מִי שֶׁיֵּלֵךְ לְבֵית הָאֵבֶל אוֹ לְבֵית הַמִּשְׁתֶּה!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yosef said: One may establish a joining** of Shabbat boundaries **only for the purpose of a mitzva,** i.e., to enable the fulfillment of a religious obligation, but not for an optional activity. The Gemara asks: **What** novel element **is he teaching us** by this? **We** explicitly **learned** this idea in the mishna from the phrase: **For anyone who** wishes to **go** on Shabbat **to a house of mourning or to a house of a** wedding **feast.**

Rav Yosef states: *ein me'arvin ela li-dvar mitzvah* — an *eiruv techumin* may only be established for the purpose of performing a *mitzvah*. The *gemara* immediately challenges: surely this is already evident from the *mishna*, which gave only *mitzvah*-related examples — going to a house of mourning (*nichum aveilim*) or to a celebration (*simchat chatan ve-kalla*)? The *gemara* answers: one might have thought the *mishna* was merely citing typical or convenient illustrations, not intending to limit the *eiruv* to *mitzvah* purposes. Rav Yosef's statement teaches that the limitation is in fact categorical: *eiruv techumin* is permitted by the rabbis only when the travel serves a genuine *mitzvah* purpose.

This requirement is striking in its asymmetry with *eiruv chatzeiros*, which carries no such condition. A plausible explanation lies in the nature of what each *eiruv* accomplishes. The *eiruv chatzeiros* serves the intrinsic communal good of bringing residents together into a shared *Shabbat* domain — it is, in a sense, itself an expression of *Shabbat* community. The rabbis naturally encouraged it unreservedly. The *eiruv techumin*, by contrast, involves leaving one's community — extending one's reach outward rather than consolidating inward. The *gemara* consistently reads this as something that requires a compelling justification. There is also the consideration that *eiruv techumin* operates against the backdrop of the verse *shvu ish tachtav* — "let every man remain in his place" — and pushing against even a rabbinic boundary in that direction calls for a legitimating *mitzvah* purpose. The *gemara* throughout the tractate treats this assumption as essentially axiomatic, though it is not explicitly stated in every *mishnah* of the *perek*.

## Breira Doctrine

### Ein Breira Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ אֵין בְּרֵירָה, דְּאִי יֵשׁ בְּרֵירָה — תִּיגַּלֵּי מִילְּתָא לְמַפְרֵעַ דְּמִבְּעוֹד יוֹם הֲוָה נִיחָא לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **Anyone who accepted upon himself while it was still day** that he will rely upon the *eiruv* is permitted to rely upon it on Shabbat. The Gemara comments: Apparently, **learn from here** that **there is no** halakhic principle of retroactive **clarification.** That is to say, there is no halakhic assumption that an uncertain state of affairs can be retroactively clarified.

The *gemara* now raises a fundamental question bearing on the *mishna*'s requirement of pre-*Shabbat* acceptance. The principle of *breira* — retroactive clarification — holds that in certain circumstances, a decision or action made now can retroactively determine the legal status of something from an earlier point. If *breira* applies here, why must one accept the *eiruv* before *Shabbat*? Even if a person decides on *Shabbat* morning that he wishes to travel to the neighboring town, perhaps we could say retroactively that it has become clear (*tiga-lei milta le-mafrea*) that he already wanted this from *erev Shabbat*, and that the *eiruv* food was his from that earlier moment.

The *gemara* responds: *shema mina ein breira* — evidently the *mishna* holds that *breira* does not apply here. The very fact that a prior acceptance is required demonstrates that retroactive clarification cannot substitute for actual prior intent.

This is a conceptually interesting application of the *breira* debate. Tosafos points out that even according to those who generally accept *yesh breira*, there may be a distinction when the uncertainty concerns a decision dependent entirely on one's own future state of mind — *toleh be-da'as atzmo*. Just because I am now deciding to go somewhere does not mean I had already decided this on the previous day; I may simply have changed my mind. *Breira* is perhaps better suited to cases where the uncertainty concerns external facts rather than the contingency of one's own future choices.

### Rav Ashi Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: ״הוֹדִיעוּהוּ״ וְ״לֹא הוֹדִיעוּהוּ״ קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that the mishna **teaches:** While it was still day. This phrase does not require one to make the decision to rely on the *eiruv* before Shabbat. Rather, the criterion for using the *eiruv* on Shabbat is whether **they informed him or they did not inform him** of the existence of the *eiruv* prior to Shabbat.

Rav Ashi offers a resolution that technically alters the reading of the *mishna*. The *mishna*'s phrase *kibel alav mi-be-od yom* need not mean that the person must actually accept the *eiruv* before *Shabbat*; it means only that he must be *informed* before *Shabbat* begins. *Hodiu ve-lo hodiu katanai* — the *mishna* speaks of whether one was told, not whether one had formally opted in. This is admittedly not the simple reading of the *mishna*, but the *gemara* accepts it as the operative interpretation.

### Breira Application

On Rav Ashi's reading, *breira* is in fact operative in the following way: if a person was informed before *Shabbat* that the *eiruv* was available to him — that he could join in and extend his boundary in the specified direction — then even if he does not actually decide to use it until after *Shabbat* begins, that later decision retroactively establishes that he was part of the *eiruv* from the start. The prior notification is the critical step; it creates the legal framework within which *breira* can operate. Without that notification, there was no opportunity to buy in, and retroactive clarification has nothing to work with. The *gemara* thus adopts a lenient position on *breira* specifically in the domain of *eruvin*, where the *gemara* consistently inclines toward leniency, while simultaneously explaining why the prior notification requirement exists.

## Minors & Dependencies

### Minor with Mother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: קָטָן בֶּן שֵׁשׁ יוֹצֵא בְּעֵירוּב אִמּוֹ. מֵיתִיבִי: קָטָן שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לְאִמּוֹ — יוֹצֵא בְּעֵירוּב אִמּוֹ, וְשֶׁאֵין צָרִיךְ לְאִמּוֹ — אֵין יוֹצֵא בְּעֵירוּב אִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi said: A six-year-old child may go out by** means of **his mother's *eiruv*.** As he is subordinate to her, he is included in her *eiruv* and does not require his own *eiruv*. **The Gemara raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **A child who needs his mother may go out by** means of **his mother's *eiruv*, but one who does not need his mother may not go out by** means of **his mother's *eiruv*.**

The *gemara* turns to a statement of Rav Assi: *katan ben sheish yotzei be-eruv imo* — a minor up through the age of six is included in his mother's *eiruv*. The underlying question is not whether someone may make an *eiruv* on behalf of a minor — that will be addressed shortly — but something more elemental: is the minor considered sufficiently an extension of his mother that her *eiruv*, made purely for herself, automatically encompasses him? Until what age is the child so identified with his mother that he is, for *eiruv* purposes, simply part of her?

### Independence Age

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן נָמֵי גַּבֵּי סוּכָּה כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא: קָטָן שֶׁאֵין צָרִיךְ לְאִמּוֹ — חַיָּיב בְּסוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And we also learned a similar** *halakha* in a mishna **with regard to a *sukka*: A child who does not need his mother is obligated in** the mitzva of ***sukka*** by rabbinical law, so that he will be trained in the observance of mitzvot.

The *gemara* challenges Rav Assi's ruling from a *braita*: *katan she-tzarich le-imo yotzei be-eruv imo, u-she-ein tzarich le-imo ein yotzei be-eruv imo* — a minor who is still dependent on his mother goes along with her *eiruv*, while one who is no longer dependent does not. The *braita* also invokes a parallel from the laws of *sukkah*: once a child no longer needs his mother — *she-ein tzarich le-imo* — he is obligated in *sukkah* for purposes of *chinuch*. The threshold, then, is not a fixed age of six, but the developmental milestone of independence from maternal dependence.

### Wake Without Calling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: וְאֵיזֶהוּ קָטָן שֶׁאֵין צָרִיךְ לְאִמּוֹ? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כׇּל שֶׁנִּפְנֶה וְאֵין אִמּוֹ מְקַנַּחְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And we discussed** this mishna and raised a question: **But who is** the **child who does not need his mother? The** Sages of the **school of Rabbi Yannai said:** This is referring to **any** child **who defecates and his mother does not wipe him.**

The *gemara* asks: what exactly constitutes *tzarich le-imo* — dependent on one's mother? The school of Rabbi Yannai answers: any child who goes to the bathroom and still requires his mother to clean him afterward. This would suggest independence arrives at roughly age three or four. Reish Lakish offers a different criterion: any child who wakes from sleep and does not call out for his mother. The *gemara* objects: even adults call for their mothers when they wake in the night! The *gemara* revises the criterion: a child who wakes and does not repeatedly call *ima ima* — "Mommy, Mommy!" — continuing to call until the mother appears.

### Age Discrepancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְכַמָּה? כְּבַר אַרְבַּע, כְּבַר חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues. The Sages who discussed the mishna asked: **And** at **what** age is a child no longer considered to be in need of his mother? Such a child is **one about four** years **old** or **about five** years **old,** as some children become independent of their mothers earlier than others.

By this criterion, independence arrives at around age four or five, depending on the child — yet Rav Assi had said six. This creates an apparent tension: if four or five marks the end of dependence on the mother, how can Rav Assi maintain that a six-year-old is still included in the mother's *eiruv*?

### Dependence vs. Preference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: כִּי קָאָמַר רַב אַסִּי — כְּגוֹן שֶׁעֵירַב עָלָיו אָבִיו לַצָּפוֹן וְאִמּוֹ לַדָּרוֹם, דַּאֲפִילּוּ בַּר שֵׁשׁ נָמֵי בְּצַוְותָּא דְאִמֵּיהּ נִיחָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehoshua, son of Rav Idi, said** in answer: **When Rav Asi said** that this child may go out by means of his mother's *eiruv*, he was referring to a case **where his father established an *eiruv* on his behalf to the north, and his mother** prepared an *eiruv* on his behalf **to the south, as even a six-year-old prefers the company of his mother** to that of his father.

The resolution is offered by Rabbi Yehoshua son of Rav Idi. He reframes the question entirely. Rav Assi was not speaking about whether the child is literally dependent on his mother in the developmental sense. Rather, Rav Assi was addressing a case where the child's father made an *eiruv* extending northward and his mother made an *eiruv* extending southward — and the question is which *eiruv* should be deemed to apply to the child. In such a scenario, *d'afilu bar sheish nami be-tzavta de-imei nicha leih* — even a six-year-old would rather be with his mother than with his father. So at age six, the child genuinely prefers the mother's company; the *eiruv* in her direction should be assumed to be the one he would choose.

The distinction Rav Assi introduces, on this reading, is between actual dependence — which ends at four or five — and preference — which persists until at least six. For purposes of assuming where the child wants to be when two competing *eruvin* pull in opposite directions, even a child who is no longer physically dependent on his mother will still instinctively incline toward her, and the law reflects this.

### Realistic Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: קָטָן שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לְאִמּוֹ יוֹצֵא בְּעֵירוּב אִמּוֹ — עַד בֶּן שֵׁשׁ. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בַּר רַב אִידִי תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **A child who needs his mother may go out by** means of **his mother's *eiruv*** that she established for herself. Until what age may he do so? He can be **up to the age of six.** This *baraita* **is a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rav Yehoshua bar Rav Idi,** who maintains that a six-year-old may go out by means of his mother's *eiruv* only if she established an *eiruv* on his behalf as well?

The *gemara* then considers a challenge: a subsequent *braita* states explicitly that *katan she-tzarich le-imo yotzei be-eruv imo ad ben sheish* — a minor who is dependent on his mother goes along with her *eiruv* until the age of six. This appears to say that actual dependence persists until six — not just preference — which would contradict Rabbi Yehoshua bar Rav Idi's resolution and reinstall the difficulty with the four-or-five figure derived from the *sukkah* discussion. The *gemara* declares this a *tiyuvta* of Rabbi Yehoshua bar Rav Idi — a valid refutation.

### Father's Presence Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — דְּאִיתֵיהּ אֲבוּהִי בְּמָתָא, הָא — דְּלָא אִיתֵיהּ אֲבוּהִי בְּמָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Let us say** that this **is** also **a refutation of** the opinion of **Rabbi Yannai and Reish Lakish,** who maintain that a child in need of his mother is one who is up to age four or five. The Gemara explains: This is **not difficult. Here** Rabbi Yannai and Reish Lakish are referring to a situation **when** the child's **father is in town.** As the child can go out with his father, he is no longer in need of his mother, even though he is younger than six. In contrast, **there** the *baraita* is referring to a case **when his father is not in town.**

Turning to the question of whether this *braita* now also refutes Rav Assi — since the *braita* says *until* six while Rav Assi said six — the *gemara* defends Rav Assi: *ad ve-ad bikhlal*, the phrase "until six" in the *braita* means up to and including six. So Rav Assi and the *braita* are in agreement.

The remaining tension — between the four-or-five figure from *sukkah* and the six-year figure here — is resolved by the *gemara* with a striking observation: *ha de-ita avuhi be-mata ha de-leita avuhi be-mata* — the outcome depends on whether the father is present in the town or not. Rashi explains: when the father is regularly at home, the child interacts with both parents, develops a sense of independence from the mother earlier, and separates psychologically from exclusive maternal dependence by around age four or five. But when the father is absent — when the mother is effectively functioning as a single parent — the child remains in a far more dependent relationship with the mother and does not achieve that independence until the age of six or later.

The *gemara*'s observation here is both psychologically acute and halakhically consequential. The same developmental milestone — independence from maternal dependence — arrives at different chronological ages depending on the structure of the child's household. This has direct halachic implications for both *eiruv* and *sukkah*, and it is precisely the dynamic that informs custody law as well. Indeed, the *gemara* in tractate Ketubot addresses the question of child custody in directly analogous terms: sons above a certain age go with the father, younger children and daughters of younger ages remain with the mother. The *eiruv* discussion here is not merely an analogy to custody law but draws on the same underlying framework.

## Who Controls Eruv?

### Dependents Eruv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְעָרֵב אָדָם עַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים, עַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הַכְּנַעֲנִים, בֵּין לְדַעְתָּן בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעְתָּן. אֲבָל אֵינוֹ מְעָרֵב לֹא עַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הָעִבְרִים, [וְלֹא] עַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים, וְלֹא עַל יְדֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ אֶלָּא מִדַּעְתָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Our Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A person may establish an *eiruv* on behalf of his minor son or daughter,** and **on behalf of his Canaanite servant or maidservant, either with their knowledge or without their knowledge. However, he may not establish an *eiruv*, neither on behalf of his Hebrew servant or maidservant, nor on behalf of his adult son or daughter, nor on behalf of his wife, except with their knowledge.**

A *braita* is now cited that addresses the authority to make an *eiruv* on behalf of others. The *braita* states: *me-arev adam al yedei beno u-vito ha-ketanim ve-al yedei avdo ve-shifkhato ha-kenanim* — a person may make an *eiruv* on behalf of his minor children and his Canaanite (non-Jewish) slaves. This authority applies *bein le-daatan bein she-lo le-daatan* — whether or not they are aware of it and whether or not they have consented. The rationale is that these individuals are entirely under the authority of the head of the household. Minor children are legally subject to their parents, and non-Jewish slaves are the property of their owner. In both cases, the *halakha* treats them as extensions of the householder's domain, and his decision-making authority is absolute.

### Adult Autonomy

By contrast, the *braita* continues: one may not make an *eiruv* for one's adult children, one's Hebrew (Jewish) slaves, or one's wife, except *mi-da'atam* — with their knowledge and agreement. These individuals, though they may share a household and stand in a relationship of authority or obligation to the householder, retain legal personhood and cannot be swept into arrangements without their consent. Jewish slaves, though they serve their master, are not his property; adult children, though they honor their father, make their own decisions; and the wife, though in a patriarchal framework she generally defers to her husband, is her own person with independent standing.

### Master Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁעֵירְבוּ וְעֵירַב עֲלֵיהֶם רַבָּן — יוֹצְאִין בְּשֶׁל רַבָּן, חוּץ מִן הָאִשָּׁה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיְּכוֹלָה לִמְחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And all of these who established an *eiruv*** for themselves, **and** at the same time **their master established an *eiruv* for them** in a different direction, **they may go out** by means of **their master's** *eiruv*, **except for a wife, because she can object** by saying that she does not want her husband's *eiruv*.

The *braita* adds a further dimension regarding the first category. Not only may a person make an *eiruv* for his minor children and non-Jewish slaves without their awareness — he may also override whatever *eiruv* they themselves may have already established. *Ve-chulan she-irvu ve-eirev aleihen rabban, yotzim b'shel rabban* — if they had made their own *eiruv* and their master (or parent) subsequently made one for them, it is the master's *eiruv* that is operative. The authority is not merely additive; it is exclusive. The minor or slave has no standing to make a binding independent choice, even if they go through the motions of doing so.

### Wife Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִשָּׁה מַאי שְׁנָא? אָמַר רַבָּה: אִשָּׁה וְכׇל דְּדָמֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara begins its analysis of the *baraitot* by posing a question: **A wife,** in **what** way **is** she **different** from an adult son or daughter? Why can't they object? They are also of age and under their own authority. **Rabba said:** The reference is not only to a wife; the *tanna* is speaking of **a wife and all who are similar to her** in this regard, such as a Hebrew servant or an adult son.

The *braita* concludes with an exception: *chutz min ha-isha mipnei she-yechola limchot* — except for the wife, because she has the capacity to protest (*limchot*). The *gemara* immediately presses the question: why single out the wife? The same should be true of adult children and Jewish slaves — all of whom were in the category of those who require *mi-da'atam* and who can make their own choices. The *gemara* acknowledges that the *braita* intends the wife as representative of the entire category: all those listed together with her — adult children, Jewish slaves — share this capacity to assert their own will.

But the phrasing of the exception generates a more subtle question. The *braita* says she can oppose — *yechola limchot*. Does this mean that if she does *not* actively oppose, the husband's *eiruv* for her takes effect? The implication seems to be yes: silence equals acquiescence. Yet the opening of the same *braita* said that for all these individuals — wife, adult children, Jewish slaves — the *eiruv* requires *mi-da'atam*, their active awareness and agreement. If silence suffices at the end, why does the beginning require active consent?

### Making Own Eruv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כֵּיוָן שֶׁעֵירְבוּ, אֵין לְךָ מִיחוּי גָּדוֹל מִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Since they established an *eiruv*** in a different direction, **you have no protest greater than this.** They need not register any other objection, as their actions prove that they do not wish to be part of their master's *eiruv*.

Rava resolves the apparent contradiction within the *braita*. What does the ending mean when it says the wife can protest? It refers to a case where she went ahead and made her own *eiruv* in a different direction. Rava states: *kivan she-irvu, ein lach michui gadol mizeh* — once she has made her own *eiruv*, there is no more powerful demonstration of her non-acquiescence. The act of establishing her own independent *eiruv* is itself the protest; no explicit verbal objection is required. Her action speaks definitively.

### Realistic Position

The *gemara*'s overall resolution reflects what one might call a realistic middle position — calibrated to the social realities of its time. The *gemara* does not treat the wife, adult children, and Jewish slaves as fully independent agents who must affirmatively opt in before anything can be done on their behalf. Nor does it treat them as simply extensions of the householder's will. Rather, it establishes a layered default: as a baseline, the householder's arrangement is presumed to reflect what the household will do together, and those in the household are presumed to go along with it. However, this presumption is defeasible — the individuals concerned retain the right and the standing to do otherwise, and if they exercise that right (whether by explicit protest or by making their own independent *eiruv*), their choice prevails.

## Who Controls Eruv?…

### Information Requirement

A remaining practical question is left partially unresolved by the *gemara*: does silence constitute acquiescence only when the person was actually *informed* of the husband's *eiruv* and chose not to object, or does the husband's *eiruv* take effect even when the wife was never told about it at all — with silence meaning simply the absence of protest rather than informed non-objection? The *gemara*'s use of the term *de-ishtiku* — "they were silent" — suggests that prior notification may be the underlying assumption: one can read silence as meaningful only if the person had the information and still said nothing. Whether an uninformed wife's silence equally constitutes acquiescence is a question the *gemara* leaves without a definitive answer. This connects back to the broader tension noted throughout: the degree to which individuals within a household are presumed to cede decision-making to the head of the household versus the degree to which they retain meaningful independent standing.

## Shiur of Eruv

### Two Meals Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כַּמָּה הוּא שִׁיעוּרוֹ? מְזוֹן שְׁתֵּי סְעוּדוֹת לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד. מְזוֹנוֹ לַחוֹל וְלֹא לַשַּׁבָּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לַשַּׁבָּת וְלֹא לַחוֹל, וְזֶה וָזֶה מִתְכַּוְּונִים לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **What is the measure** for a joining of Shabbat boundaries? It consists of a quantity of **food** sufficient for **two meals for each and every one** of those included in the *eiruv*. The *tanna'im* disagree with regard to the size of these two meals. It is referring to **one's food** that he eats **on a weekday and not on Shabbat;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is referring to the amount he eats **on Shabbat and not on a weekday. And** both **this** Sage, Rabbi Meir, **and that** Sage, Rabbi Yehuda, **intended to be lenient,** as Rabbi Meir maintains that people eat more food on Shabbat, whereas Rabbi Yehuda believes that they consume more on a weekday.

The new *mishna* turns to the question of the *shiur* — the requisite quantity of food — for an *eiruv techumin*. The *mishna* establishes: *mazon shtei seudos le-chol echad ve-echad* — food sufficient for two meals for each and every participant. This stands in sharp contrast to the *eiruv chatzeiros* standard. For *eiruv chatzeiros*, the quantity of a *grogeret* — a pressed fig — per person suffices, and once the aggregate reaches the equivalent of two meals, even a thousand people may be included. Here, each individual must contribute or be assigned two full meals' worth of food, with no ceiling or shared threshold.

### Higher Shiur Rationale

Two considerations explain the elevated requirement. First, as noted throughout the *perek*, *eiruv techumin* involves a departure from one's community rather than the consolidation of community that *eiruv chatzeiros* achieves. The rabbis wished to make *eiruv chatzeiros* easy and attractive, while *eiruv techumin* — which is in tension with the spirit of remaining in one's place on *Shabbat* — is not subject to the same facilitative approach. Second, *eiruv chatzeiros* is inherently communal: everyone's food goes into a shared loaf, symbolizing collective unity, and the total amount represents the entire community. *Eiruv techumin* is by nature individual — one does not leave town as a community but as a person — and the law reflects this individuality by requiring a distinct, personal quantity for each participant.

### Practical Details

A practical question arises: if the *eiruv* is made for anyone in the city who wishes to use it, and the food must amount to two meals per person, how does the person making the *eiruv* know how many people will join? The *gemara* clarifies that the participants do not need to formally sign up. Crucially, the food need not be consumed — it must only exist at the onset of *Shabbat*. Even if the food is subsequently destroyed, the *eiruv* has already taken effect as of the moment *Shabbat* began, and the participants may travel accordingly. The question of how many people the food covers in advance is therefore a practical matter of ensuring sufficient quantity relative to the expected number of participants, but the act of acquisition does not require a formal enumeration.

### Meir vs. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: מִכִּכָּר בְּפוּנְדְּיוֹן, מֵאַרְבַּע סְאִין בְּסֶלַע. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׁתֵּי יָדוֹת לַכִּכָּר מִשָּׁלֹשׁ לַקַּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka says:** Food for two meals is the size **of a loaf** bought **with a *pundeyon*,** which is one-forty-eighth of a *sela*, **when four *se'a*** of wheat are sold **for a *sela*. Rabbi Shimon says:** Food for two meals is **two** of three **parts of a loaf, when three** loaves are prepared **from a *kav*** of wheat.

The *mishna* then records a dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda about the size of these two meals. Rabbi Meir says the measure is *mazono le-chol ve-lo le-Shabbat* — two weekday meals, not two *Shabbat* meals. Rabbi Yehuda reverses this: the meals are measured by *Shabbat* meals, not weekday meals. Both tannaim intended their position to be the lenient one — and the disagreement turns on an empirical question about human eating habits: are *Shabbat* meals larger or smaller than weekday meals?

One might instinctively assume *Shabbat* meals are larger: one makes a special effort, the food is nicer, the occasion is celebratory. On that assumption, the two-meal requirement measured by *Shabbat* meals would demand more food, making Rabbi Yehuda's standard stricter. Rabbi Meir's weekday standard would then be more lenient. Conversely, one could argue that on *Shabbat* one must eat *shalosh seudos* — three meals — and therefore each individual meal is actually smaller, so that *Shabbat* portions are less than weekday portions, and Rabbi Yehuda's standard is actually the lenient one. The debate is precisely about which intuition is correct.

### Specific Measures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְכַמָּה מְזוֹן שְׁתֵּי סְעוּדוֹת? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: תַּרְתֵּי רִיפָתָא אִיכָּרְיָיתָא. רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר: תַּרְתֵּי רִיפָתָא נְהַר פַּפָּיָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And how much** is **food for two meals** according to the measures of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Two farmers'** [***ikaryata***] **loaves of bread. Rav Adda bar Ahava said: Two** standard **loaves of bread** baked in the region of **the Pappa River.**

Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka offers a concrete quantification. The standard, he says, is a loaf of bread costing one *pundyon* at a time when four *se'ah* of flour can be purchased for a *sela*. The *gemara* works through the arithmetic: a *sela* is equivalent to forty-eight *pundyon*, and four *se'ah* equals twenty-four *kav* (since there are six *kav* in a *se'ah*). Therefore, for one *pundyon* one can purchase one-half *kav* of flour. The loaf of bread costing one *pundyon* — ignoring the labor costs of baking, to which the *gemara* will return — is made from half a *kav* of flour. This loaf represents two meals' worth of food according to Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka.

### Shimon's Measure

Rabbi Shimon offers an alternative, smaller measure. He says: *shtei yadot la-kikar mi-shalosh le-kav* — two-thirds of a loaf when three loaves equal one *kav*. If three loaves equal one *kav*, then one loaf equals one-third of a *kav*, and two-thirds of a loaf — *shtei yadot* — equals two-ninths of a *kav*. This is a considerably smaller amount than the half-*kav* standard of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka.

### Half-Measure Terms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֶצְיָיהּ, לַבַּיִת הַמְנוּגָּע. וַחֲצִי חֶצְיָיהּ, לִפְסוֹל אֶת הַגְּוִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** Having discussed measures with regard to a loaf of bread, the mishna states that **half** of this loaf is the amount called a half [*peras*], a measure relevant **for** the *halakhot* of **a leprous house.** If one enters a house afflicted with leprosy and remains there long enough to eat this amount of food, the clothes he is wearing become ritually impure. **And half of its half,** a quarter of this loaf, is the amount of ritually impure food that **disqualifies the body.**

The *gemara* notes that half of this standard loaf — meaning half of the two-meal amount, which is one meal's worth — is the measurement known as a *pras*. The word *pras* literally means "half," referring to half the two-meal loaf. This *pras* measurement is the standard unit in the concept of *kedai achilas pras* — the time it takes to eat a *pras*, which is the unit used for combining separate acts of eating into one continuous act for purposes of liability (as on *Yom Kippur*, for instance).

The *pras* also appears in the laws of *tzaraas* (*Vayikra* 14): if a person enters a house afflicted with *tzaraas* (*beis ha-menuga*), he becomes *tamei*. The rabbis interpret the time-based element of this *tum'ah* as the duration required to eat a meal — specifically, one *pras*, half the two-meal loaf. Remaining in the afflicted house for *kedai achilas pras* establishes the *tum'ah*, and this same duration makes one's clothing *tamei*.

Finally, half of the *pras* — one-quarter of the two-meal loaf — is the measure relevant to *psul gviyah*, the rabbinic law by which eating *tamei* food in that quantity disqualifies the body. Eating *tamei* food does not ordinarily render a person *tamei* through contact alone, but the rabbis enacted that eating *tamei* food in the amount of half a *pras* makes the person's body itself ritually unfit in a derivative sense — the concern being that one who eats *tamei* food may then consume *terumah*, and the *tamei* food will contact the *terumah* in the mouth.

### Common Loaf Size

To give these measures practical meaning, Rav Yehuda cites Rav: the two-meal standard corresponds to *tarti rifta de-achra'ei asya* — two loaves of the sort that cowherds would take with them into the fields for a day's sustenance. The Babylonian location of Nahar Papisa (cited by Mevat bar Abba) had its own standard size of loaf that was locally understood. Both are attempts to ground the abstract *shiur* in a familiar, practical unit — roughly equivalent to two standard bread rolls, the kind of portion a working person would eat for two regular meals.

### Rav Yosef Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף לְרַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: אֲבוּךְ כְּמַאן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ? כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סְבִירָא לֵיהּ. אֲנָא נָמֵי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סְבִירָא לֵיהּ. דְּאִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, קַשְׁיָא הָא דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: רַוְוחָא לִבְסִימָא שְׁכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said to Rav Yosef, son of Rava: Your father, in accordance with whose** opinion **does he hold,** that of Rabbi Yehuda or Rabbi Meir? He replied: **He holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.** Rav Yosef added: **I too agree with** the ruling of **Rabbi Meir, because if** the *halakha* is **in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda, a difficulty** would arise **from the popular saying: There is** always **room for sweets.**

The *gemara* concludes the daf with an exchange in which Rav Yosef asks Rav Yosef son of Rava (or, in an alternate reading, Rabba): whose position did your father hold in the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda? The son reports: his father held like Rabbi Meir — that the two meals are measured by weekday portions, not *Shabbat* portions. Rav Yosef responds: I also hold like Rabbi Meir, and he explains why. If one were to hold like Rabbi Yehuda — that *Shabbat* meals are the standard — it would present a difficulty, because as people say: *ravcha le-vasima shachi'ach* — there is always room for something that tastes good. Since *Shabbat* food tastes better, people eat more of it, meaning *Shabbat* meals are larger. If the two-meal measure is calibrated by *Shabbat* meals, the requirement becomes heavier, and the lenient position is actually Rabbi Meir's weekday standard. Therefore Rav Yosef, siding with Rabbi Meir, prefers the smaller weekday measure as the operative *shiur* for the *eiruv techumin*. The mathematical reconciliation of the various measures with the *se'ah*-based calculations of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka and Rabbi Shimon awaits the continuation on the next daf.
