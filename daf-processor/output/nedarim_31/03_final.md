# Nedarim 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Daf 31 Overview

### Perek 3 Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַנּוֹדֵר מִשּׁוֹבְתֵי שַׁבָּת — אָסוּר בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל וְאָסוּר בַּכּוּתִים. מֵאוֹכְלֵי שׁוּם — אָסוּר בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל וְאָסוּר בַּכּוּתִים. מֵעוֹלֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם — אָסוּר בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל וּמוּתָּר בַּכּוּתִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who takes a vow** that deriving benefit **from** those **who rest on Shabbat** is forbidden to him is **prohibited** from deriving benefit **from a Jew, and** he is also **prohibited** from deriving benefit **from Samaritans [*Kutim*]** because they are also Shabbat observers. One who takes a vow that deriving benefit **from those who eat garlic** on Shabbat night is forbidden to him is **prohibited** from deriving benefit **from a Jew, and** he is also **prohibited** from benefiting **from Samaritans.** However, if one takes a vow that deriving benefit **from those who ascend** to **Jerusalem** is forbidden to him, he is **prohibited** from deriving benefit **from a Jew, but** he is **permitted** to benefit **from Samaritans** because they do not ascend to Jerusalem, but rather, to Mount Gerizim.

Daf 31 marks the lighter conclusion of the third *perek*. The opening section of the *perek* was weighty and dense — concerned with the question of *piv ulibo shavin*, whether a person's mouth and heart are aligned — and the middle *mishnah* opened a fascinating discussion about the status of a *neder* that renders an object like *hekdesh*, requiring a form of *pidyon*. Now we arrive at a series of *mishnayot* that carry us to the end of the *perek*, each addressing a relatively contained question: when a person declares a certain group of people forbidden, who exactly is included in that group and who is not?

### Topic Progression

The *mishnayot* of this concluding section move through a progression of such group-definition problems — from *shabbat* observers and garlic eaters, to *bnei Noach* and *zera Avraham*, to Jews as a category of forbidden beneficiaries, and finally to the terminology of circumcised and uncircumcised. The *perek* then closes with an extended *aggadic* discussion of *brit milah* that, while formally belonging to the *mishnah*, stands out as quite striking for this genre.

## Shabbat & Garlic

### Shabbat Observers

The *mishnah* opens: *HaNoder MiShovsei Shabbos* — one who says "I will not derive benefit from those who rest on *shabbat*" — is forbidden from benefiting from Jews and also from Samaritans, who likewise observe *shabbat*. The *mishnah* then adds the parallel case: *MeOchlei Shum*, those who eat garlic — again, forbidden from both Jews and Samaritans.

### Garlic Custom

The relevance of garlic requires explanation. As recorded in the *Gemara* in Bava Kama and Ketubot, and spelled out more fully in the *meforesh* here than in the *Ran*, Jews customarily ate garlic on Friday night on account of *ona* — the *mitzvah* of conjugal relations — since men would fulfill this obligation *me'erev shabbos le'erev shabbos*, and garlic was understood to increase male potency, as the *Gemara* elsewhere connects the verse *asher piryo yitein be'ito* to *talmidei chachamim* who fulfill this duty every Friday night. There is an obvious irony in this — one might wonder whether a partner would appreciate the garlic — but the *Gemara* records that there was specifically a practice to eat garlic on Friday night for this reason. Accordingly, "garlic eaters" functions as a marker for Jews and Samaritans, who apparently shared this custom.

### Gemara Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״שׁוֹבְתֵי שַׁבָּת״? אִילֵּימָא מִמְּקַיְּימֵי שַׁבָּת, מַאי אִירְיָא בַּכּוּתִים? אֲפִילּוּ גּוֹיִם נָמֵי! אֶלָּא מִמְּצֻוִּוים עַל הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the expression in the mishna: **Those who rest on Shabbat? If we say** that the one who took the vow intended to render forbidden deriving benefit **from those who uphold Shabbat,** i.e., who actually observe it, **why** mention **specifically** that he is prohibited **from** deriving benefit from **Samaritans; even** benefit from other **gentiles** who are Shabbat observers should **also** be prohibited? **Rather,** the intention of the *tanna* was to refer to a case where one took a vow that deriving benefit **from** those **who are commanded about** observing **Shabbat** is forbidden, and this *tanna* holds that the Samaritans are considered true converts, commanded to observe Shabbat.

The *Gemara* then asks: *Mai Shovsei Shabbos*? What exactly does *shabbat* observer mean? If it simply means anyone who happens to keep *shabbat*, then why limit this to Samaritans? Non-Jews who observe *shabbat* would also be included — and there is a known phenomenon of non-Jews who kept *shabbat* without having converted. In the Roman world, the class of *yirei Hashem* — God-fearers who were attracted to Jewish practice — was real and recognized; the phrase appears in *Hallel* as a distinct group alongside *beit Aharon*, and one tradition identifies it with non-Jewish worshipers of God in the *mishnaic* era. Indeed, it is worth noting that the New Testament itself, in Acts chapter 15, speaks of Gentiles observing *shabbat* before the Council of Nicaea shifted Christian practice to Sunday.

The *Gemara* therefore proposes that *shovsei shabbat* means not those who merely happen to observe it, but *mi metzuvah v'oseh* — those who are commanded and who perform it. This would imply that Samaritans are considered Jews for this purpose, since the whole question of their status turned on whether they were to be treated as Jews.

### Jerusalem Pilgrims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: ״מֵעוֹלֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם״ — אָסוּר בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל וּמוּתָּר בַּכּוּתִים, אַמַּאי? וְהָא מְצֻוִּוים נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, say the latter clause** of the mishna: If he takes a vow that deriving benefit **from those who ascend to Jerusalem** is forbidden to him, he is **prohibited** from deriving benefit **from a Jew but permitted** to derive benefit **from Samaritans. Why? But aren't** Samaritans **commanded** to ascend just like other Jews?

The *Gemara* then challenges this from the *seifa* of the *mishnah*: *Me'olei Yerushalayim* — those who make pilgrimage to Jerusalem — are forbidden from Jews but permitted from Samaritans. If Samaritans are commanded on *shabbat*, they are equally commanded on *aliyah l'regel*. Why then are they excluded from this case?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה קָתָנֵי. בְּתַרְתֵּי בָּבֵי קַמָּיָיתָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל וְכוּתִים — מְצֻוִּוין וְעוֹשִׂין, גּוֹיִם הָהוּא דְּעָבְדִי — עוֹשִׂין וְאֵינָם מְצֻוִּוין. בְּעוֹלֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם: יִשְׂרָאֵל — מְצֻוִּוין וְעוֹשִׂין, כּוּתִים — מְצֻוִּוין וְאֵינָם עוֹשִׂין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** It **is teaching** about those who are **commanded and** actually **perform** a mitzva, and the mishna is to be understood as follows: **In the first two clauses** of the mishna, which concern Shabbat observance and eating garlic, both **Jews and Samaritans** are included because they are **commanded and** actually **perform** the mitzva. However, with regard to **gentiles, those who perform** these mitzvot have the status of those who **perform** the mitzva **but are not commanded** to do so. Therefore, the one who took the vow is permitted to derive benefit from them. Concerning the case **of those who ascend to Jerusalem, a Jew is commanded** to keep this mitzva **and performs** it, while **Samaritans** are **commanded but do not perform** it, so he is permitted to derive benefit from them.

Abaye resolves this: the *mishnah* means *metzuvah v'oseh* — commanded and performing. In the first two cases of *shabbat* and garlic, Jews and Samaritans are both commanded and both in practice observe *shabbat*. Non-Jews who happen to keep *shabbat* are performing but not commanded. With respect to *olei Yerushalayim*, Jews are commanded and perform, while Samaritans are commanded but do not perform it — they make *aliyah* to Har Gerizim, not to Jerusalem. There remains an interesting question about the framing: is it that we consider them commanded, or that they consider themselves commanded? The *Gemara* appears to take the former position, though in the end the operative principle in *nedarim* is always *da'at hanoder* — the intent of the one who vowed.

## Noahides & Seed

### Noahides Neder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאֵינִי נֶהֱנֶה לִבְנֵי נֹחַ״ — מוּתָּר בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל וְאָסוּר בְּאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says: The property **of** the **descendants of Noah is *konam*** for me, and for **that** reason **I will not benefit** from it, he is **permitted** to derive benefit **from a Jew but prohibited** from deriving benefit **from the nations of the world.**

The next *mishnah*: *Konam she'eini nehene l'Vnei Noach* — one who vows not to benefit from *bnei Noach* — is permitted to benefit from Jews but forbidden from non-Jews. All non-Jews are *bnei Noach*.

### Jews & Noach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מִי נָפֵיק מִכְּלָל בְּנֵי נֹחַ? כֵּיוָן דְּאִיקַּדַּשׁ אַבְרָהָם, אִיתְקְרוֹ עַל שְׁמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And is a Jew excluded from the category** of the **descendants of Noah?** They are also descendants of Noah. The Gemara answers: **Since Abraham was sanctified** and designated to possess a unique role in the world, all his descendants **are called by his name** and are no longer termed the descendants of Noah.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: but Jews are also *bnei Noach* — they too descend from Noah. The *Gemara* answers: *keivan d'ikdish Avraham itkaru ashmei* — once Abraham was sanctified and they received the identity of being Jewish, they are no longer identified under the rubric of *bnei Noach*. When one says *bnei Noach*, one means Gentiles, not Jews — even though technically Jews are biological descendants of Noah. This could simply be a matter of vernacular usage, which is always available in the laws of *nedarim* through the principle of *halach achar leshon bnei adam*. In the common idiom, "Noahides" refers to non-Jews, and so that is how the term is read.

### Philosophical Status

Yet the question carries a deeper dimension as well. There is a *Gemara* in Sanhedrin that states there can be no prohibition that applies to *bnei Noach* but is permitted to Jews. When that question arises — whether parameters of theft, *ever min hachai*, or other categories of *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach* apply to Jews — the *Gemara's* framing seems to suggest that technically Jews could be included, but we would not allow for such a conclusion in practice because it would make it appear as though Jews have fewer religious obligations in certain areas than non-Jews do. One might instead suggest that it is because Jews are simultaneously Jews *and bnei Noach*, so that any human-universal obligations apply to them as well.

However, this reading does not seem to fit the *halacha*. In practice, non-Jews are *chayav mita* for violating many of the *sheva mitzvot* where Jews are not. This strongly suggests that even as a *halachic* category, Jews are not subsumed within *bnei Noach* — at least not for purposes of obligation and liability. The passing remark in *Nedarim* may not itself elaborate on this, but the question surfaces naturally here, and is treated more fully in Sanhedrin and, to some extent, in Avodah Zarah.

### Zera Avraham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״שֶׁאֵינִי נֶהֱנֶה לְזֶרַע אַבְרָהָם״ — אָסוּר בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל וּמוּתָּר בְּאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says: The property **of the offspring of Abraham** is forbidden to me, and for **that** reason **I will not benefit** from it, **he is prohibited** from deriving benefit **from a Jew but permitted** to derive benefit **from the nations of the world.**

The *Gemara* continues: *she'eini ne'eneh le'zera Avraham* — one who vows not to benefit from the seed of Abraham — is forbidden from Jews but permitted from non-Jews. The *Gemara* asks: but Ishmaelites are also descendants of Abraham biologically. The answer is *ki ve'Yitzchak yikarei lecha zara* — through Isaac shall your seed be called — so that biologically Ishmael descends from Abraham, but in terms of how that lineage is identified, only the line of Isaac carries the designation. And what of Esau, who is also a descendant? The verse says *be'Yitzchak* — within Isaac — *velo kol Yitzchak*, not all of Isaac. So Esau is equally excluded. One might add *bnei Ketura* as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָאִיכָּא יִשְׁמָעֵאל! ״כִּי בְיִצְחָק יִקָּרֵא לְךָ זָרַע״ כְּתִיב. וְהָאִיכָּא עֵשָׂו! ״בְּיִצְחָק״, וְלֹא כׇּל יִצְחָק.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Concerning the mishna's ruling that the one who takes such a vow is permitted to derive benefit from the nations of the world, the Gemara asks: **But isn't there Ishmael** and his descendants, who are also Abraham's offspring? Why isn't deriving benefit from them forbidden as well? The Gemara answers: **It is written** with regard to Abraham: **"For in Isaac shall seed be called to you"** (Genesis 21:12), which demonstrates that the descendants of Ishmael are not termed the offspring of Abraham. The Gemara asks: **But isn't there Esau** and his descendants; they are also offspring of Abraham, since they are descendants of Isaac? The Gemara answers that the words **"in Isaac"** mean that some of Isaac's descendants, i.e., the children of Jacob, are included in the offspring of Abraham, **but not all** the descendants of **Isaac.**

What is notable here is the *Gemara's* approach. It could simply have invoked *halach achar leshon bnei adam* — in common vernacular, *zera Avraham* means Jews, not non-Jews — and left it at that. But the *Gemara* instead demonstrates that even at the level of the *pesukim* themselves, the label *zera Avraham* does not apply to all of Abraham's biological descendants. It was always a more restricted designation, limited to those identified as Abraham's line in the covenantal sense. This makes the ruling more principled: it is not merely a concession to popular usage, but a reflection of how the *pesukim* themselves define the category.

## Benefit from Jews

### Overpaying Buyer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״שֶׁאֵינִי נֶהֱנֶה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל״ — לוֹקֵחַ בְּיוֹתֵר וּמוֹכֵר בְּפָחוֹת. ״שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל נֶהֱנִין לִי״ — לוֹקֵחַ בְּפָחוֹת וּמוֹכֵר בְּיוֹתֵר, וְאֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ. ״שֶׁאֵינִי נֶהֱנֶה לָהֶן וְהֵן לִי״ — יֵהָנֶה לְאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says: The property of **a Jew is** forbidden to me, and for **that** reason **I will not benefit** from it, **he may purchase** items from a Jew **for more** than the market price **and may sell** items to a Jew **for less** than the market price, so that he does not derive benefit from the transactions. If one says: **Benefit from me is** forbidden **to a Jew, he may purchase** items from a Jew **for less** than the market price **and may sell** items to a Jew **for more** than the market price, so that he does not derive benefit from the transactions. **But** although this would be permitted, **they do not listen to him,** i.e., people will generally not agree to deal with him in a manner that causes them a loss in every transaction. If one says: The property of a Jew is forbidden to me, and for **that** reason **I will not benefit from them, and** my property is forbidden to a Jew and **they** will not benefit **from me,** in this case **he may benefit from the nations of the world** but not from a Jew, and a Jew may not benefit from him.

The *mishnah* now turns to a case with direct practical consequences: *she'eini ne'eneh mi'Yisrael* — I will not benefit from Jews. How does one then conduct commerce? The *mishnah* answers: *lokeach beyoter umocheir bepachot* — one must overpay when buying and undersell when selling. If I go into Michael's store and I have vowed not to benefit from Jews, then I must overpay for whatever he sells me. The logic is that if I am paying more than the item is worth, I am not truly receiving a benefit — I am receiving less than my money's worth, so he has not done anything for me in any meaningful sense. The *chiddush* here is that merely being available to sell me milk so I need not travel elsewhere is not conceptualized as benefit. If, however, I am paying five dollars for a three-dollar gallon of milk, then whatever transaction occurred, I have not come out ahead.

This naturally raises the question of *ona'ah* — the prohibition against fraudulent overcharging. If one knowingly overpays and accepts that arrangement willingly, the rules of *ona'ah* may not apply in the ordinary sense, since *ona'ah* depends on the parties not having consented to the deviation. There is also a question of degree: does one dollar over market price suffice, or must the overpayment be more substantial? The analysis here touches on the *sugya* of *al menat she'ein bo ona'ah*, and the question of whether one must actually take a loss or merely receive less than market value. This is a genuinely important question that the *Gemara* does not resolve in full here.

### Underselling Seller

The reverse case: *sheYisrael ne'enin li* — Jews shall not benefit from me. In that case, *lokeach bepachot umocheir beyoter* — one must underpay when buying and overcharge when selling. If I run a store and declare that no Jew may benefit from me, then when Michael walks in I must charge five dollars for three-dollar milk. Michael, of course, will simply go elsewhere. The *mishnah* acknowledges this: *ve'ein shom'in lo* — no one will agree to these terms — though some versions read *im shom'in lo*, if anyone does agree.

### Non-Jews Only

The final and combined case: *she'eini ne'eneh lahen vehen lo yehenu li* — I will not benefit from them and they will not benefit from me. One cannot overpay and cannot undersell. What then? *Yehenu le'ovdei kochavim* — one must do all business exclusively with non-Jews.

One might think such a *neder* is automatically *battel* — like a vow never to sleep for a week, which is simply impossible to fulfill. But the *Ran* rejects this: it is not automatically void because it is possible to fulfill it. One need only conduct all interactions with non-Jews. The arrangement is admittedly *takanah rechokah*, a remote and difficult solution given how much of Tractate Avodah Zarah is devoted to navigating the constraints of commercial interaction even with non-Jews. But difficult is not the same as impossible, and the *neder* therefore stands.

### Ran's Analysis

A crucial conceptual point emerges here that the *Ran* makes explicit and that will have direct bearing on the next *perek*. One might ask: why does the *mishnah* need to spell out the remedy of overpaying or underselling? If I had vowed *nikhasai alecha* — my property is forbidden to you — then it would make no difference whether you overpay or underpay: any transfer of my property would be prohibited. Why then does overpaying resolve anything?

The answer is that in the cases of our *mishnah*, the object being forbidden is not property — it is persons. I am the forbidden object, or you are. When I say I will not benefit from Jews, I am declaring that Jews — as persons — are the forbidden entity, and the question is what types of interactions with them constitute deriving benefit from them. Selling something to me at fair market value is a form of benefit I receive through our interaction. But if I overpay, I have not come out ahead in that interaction, and therefore I have not derived benefit from you as a person.

Conversely, if my property were the forbidden object, no transfer of my property could occur regardless of price. But since I am the forbidden object — not my property — the question is whether any given transaction constitutes someone deriving benefit from me. If the terms are sufficiently skewed against the beneficiary, it does not. This distinction between the person as the forbidden object and the property as the forbidden object is, as the *Ran* stresses, a foundational conceptual shift. It will remain essential as the next *perek* turns to analyzing exactly what types of acts constitute benefit.

## Shmuel's Principle

### Test Drive Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַלּוֹקֵחַ כְּלִי מִן הָאוּמָּן לְבַקְּרוֹ וְנֶאֱנַס בְּיָדוֹ — חַיָּיב. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר הֲנָאַת לוֹקֵחַ הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel said:** In the case of **one who takes a vessel from a craftsman to examine it, and an accident occurs** to it while it is **in his hand,** e.g. it broke, the one who examined it is **liable** to pay for the damages. Since the one examining the item could have completed the sale at any time, he is treated like a borrower while he examines it, as all the benefit is his. The Gemara comments: **Apparently,** Shmuel **holds** that in every sale **the** primary **benefit** belongs to **the buyer.** The buyer benefits much more than the seller, and therefore he must pay for accidents.

*Amar Shmuel*: one who takes a vessel from a craftsman *le'vakro* — to inspect it before purchasing — and it is damaged by *oness* while in his possession, is liable. The paradigm is something like a test drive: you have not yet agreed to buy the item, you are taking it to evaluate it, and an accident occurs that is entirely not your fault. Are you liable?

The answer turns on whether you are considered a *sho'el* — a borrower. A *sho'el* is liable even for *oness*, accidents beyond his control, because he is the exclusive beneficiary of the arrangement: he gets use of the item at no cost, while the owner receives nothing. If the potential purchaser is in the same position — sole beneficiary — then he too bears *sho'el* liability.

Intuitively, one might resist this. The seller is also getting something out of the arrangement: he is hoping to make a sale and presumably earning a commission. The interaction seems mutually beneficial. But *Shmuel* holds otherwise: in such a transaction, the purchaser is the genuine beneficiary. The seller is not really gaining from letting you take the item — his gain depends entirely on whether you buy it, and if you return it without buying, he has received nothing.

### Both Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: ״שֶׁאֵינִי נֶהֱנֶה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל״ — מוֹכֵר בְּפָחוֹת, אֲבָל שָׁוֶה בְּשָׁוֶה — לָא. וְאִי הֲנָאַת לוֹקֵחַ הִיא — אֲפִילּוּ שָׁוֶה בְּשָׁוֶה! מַתְנִיתִין בִּזְבִינָא דִּרְמֵי עַל אַפֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with Shmuel's statement: **We learned** in the mishna that if one says: The property of **a Jew is** forbidden to me, and for **that** reason **I will not benefit** from it, **he may sell** items to a Jew **for less** than the market price. The Gemara infers: He may sell at a lesser price, **but** selling the items at a price **equal** to the market price is **not** permitted. **But if the** primary **benefit** of the sale is to the **buyer,** then **even** selling the items at a price **equal** to the market price should be permitted. The Gemara answers: **The mishna** is referring **to a sale that lies in his face,** i.e., an item that arouses no interest among potential buyers. In that case, the seller benefits from the sale even if the item is sold at market value, and this is prohibited.

This principle — that the purchaser is the real beneficiary in a normal transaction — is, frankly, a recurring *Gemara* assumption that is difficult to fully absorb. It seems to reflect the social and economic world of the *Gemara*, which was primarily agricultural rather than commercial. In a commercial craftsman's context, one would expect the seller to be equally invested in making sales. Yet the *Gemara* often frames the purchaser as the one who wins.

The classic expression of this sensibility appears in the *midrash* on *ki lekach tov natati lachem torati al taazovu*: the verse describes *Torah* as a *lekach*, a purchase, to underscore that God is not like a human seller. In a human transaction, the *midrash* says, *mocher atzev veloke'ach samei'ach* — the seller is sad and the buyer is happy. The seller experiences loss — he is letting go of something he made or owned — even if he is in the business of selling it. A craftsman has invested himself in what he produced, and parting with it involves a psychological loss, however rational the transaction.

### Hot Items Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן אֵימָא רֵישָׁא: לוֹקֵחַ בְּיוֹתֵר. וְעוֹד, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: ״שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל נֶהֱנִין לִי״ — [לוֹקֵחַ בְּפָחוֹת] וּמוֹכֵר בְּיוֹתֵר. וְאִי בִּזְבִינָא דִּרְמֵי עַל אַפֵּיהּ — אֲפִילּוּ שָׁוֶה בְּשָׁוֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, say the first clause** of that *halakha*: **He may purchase** items from a Jew **for more** than the market price. If the mishna deals with a case where the seller is glad to sell, why does the buyer need to pay more? He should be permitted to pay the market value. **Furthermore, say the latter clause** of the mishna: If one says: **Benefit from me is** forbidden **to a Jew, he may purchase** items from a Jew **for less** than the market price **and may sell** items to a Jew **for more** than the market price. **But if** it is referring **to a sale that lies in his face,** then **even** if he sells **at the** price **equal** to the purchase price he has more benefit than the buyer, and it should be permitted.

The *Gemara* challenges *Shmuel's* ruling from our *mishnah*. If the purchaser is always the sole beneficiary in a transaction, then even in a normal sale at market price, the seller should not be considered to benefit. But our *mishnah* implies that at market price both parties benefit — which is precisely why one must deviate from market price in order to ensure that only one side benefits. If only the purchaser ever benefits, the *mishnah's* structure makes no sense.

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve this by suggesting that our *mishnah* concerns *zvina deramei al apeih* — wares that the seller is desperate to unload, goods that are not moving and that the seller urgently wants to sell. In such a case, even a normal sale benefits the seller. But this creates new difficulties: the *reisha* of the *mishnah* says that the buyer must overpay, which would also be strange if these are wares the seller is desperate to sell — in that case, even a normal-priced purchase would benefit the seller, so why must the buyer overpay?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא בִּזְבִינָא חֲרִיפָא. אִי הָכִי לוֹקֵחַ בְּפָחוֹת? אֲפִילּוּ שָׁוֶה בְּשָׁוֶה! אֶלָּא
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The latter clause** is referring **to** the opposite case, in **a keen [*ḥarifa*] sale,** i.e., one in which the merchandise arouses keen interest among potential buyers. Therefore, the buyer benefits if he pays the market price. The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that the latter clause is referring to such a case, why should the one who took the vow **purchase** it **for less** than the market price? **Even at the** price **equal** to the purchase price it should be permitted, since the merchandise is selling well and the seller derives no benefit from it. **Rather,**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין בִּזְבִינָא מִיצְעָא, וְדִשְׁמוּאֵל בִּזְבִינָא חֲרִיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** it must be that **the mishna** is dealing **with an average sale,** which is neither of particularly low quality and difficult to sell nor of particularly high quality and in high demand. Therefore, when it is sold at the fixed price, it cannot be said that either the buyer or seller benefits. Consequently, the one taking the vow must lower the price when selling to those forbidden by the vow and add to the price when buying from them. **And** by contrast, the case **of Shmuel** is referring **to a keen sale,** in which a sale at the fixed price is considered to be primarily beneficial to the buyer.

After working through several such difficulties, the *Gemara* settles on the obvious conclusion: *matnisin bizvina mitza* — our *mishnah* speaks about normal, mid-range goods. For such items, a transaction at fair market price benefits both parties. The seller is content to have made the sale; the buyer is content to have acquired the item at a fair price. That is the assumed baseline.

Shmuel, accordingly, must be limited. His ruling that the purchaser is the sole beneficiary — and therefore bears *sho'el* liability — does not apply to all transactions. *Udishmuel, bizvina charifa* — Shmuel is speaking specifically of a hot item, something in high demand where the seller has no shortage of willing buyers. In that case, the seller loses nothing by declining to sell to you; he can sell to the next person in line. You are the one who benefits from the opportunity to inspect it. The seller is not dependent on your purchase in any meaningful sense. Limit Shmuel to that case, and our *mishnah* is coherent: it speaks of normal goods where both sides genuinely benefit from a fair transaction.

### Jewelry to In-Laws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: הַלּוֹקֵחַ כֵּלִים מִן הַתַּגָּר לְשַׁגְּרָן לְבֵית חָמִיו, וְאָמַר לוֹ: אִם מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן מִמֶּנִּי — אֲנִי נוֹתֵן לְךָ דְּמֵיהֶם, וְאִם לָאו — אֲנִי נוֹתֵן לְךָ לְפִי טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה שֶׁבָּהֶן. נֶאֶנְסוּ בַּהֲלִיכָה — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel** that taking an item from the seller to inspect it before purchase is considered like borrowing it. In the case of **one who takes utensils from a merchant** in order **to send them to his father-in-law's house** as a gift **and says to** the merchant: **If they accept them from me I** will **give you their value, and if** they do **not** want them, **I** will **give you** a sum of money **according to** the value of **the financial benefit that** I received **from them,** i.e., I will pay something for the benefit that I received from showing my father-in-law that I want to honor him, then if **an accident occurs to** the utensils **on the way** to the house of the father-in-law and they are broken, the buyer is **liable** to pay because he has the status of a borrower.

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* in support of *Shmuel*'s position: one who purchases vessels from a merchant to send to his in-laws — essentially, one sends a gift on approval, meaning he has not yet paid but will do so if the gift is accepted — and an accident befalls the items, is liable *behalicha* (on the way there) but exempt *uvechazarah* (on the way back).

The paradigm is sending jewelry to one's prospective in-laws before the purchase is finalized. While bringing it to them, one is a hopeful purchaser: one is the sole beneficiary of having the opportunity to show it, one has paid nothing, and one is seen as a *sho'el*. If the item is damaged by *oness* on the way there, one is liable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּחֲזָרָה — פָּטוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּנוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** But if the father-in-law did not want them and returned them to the seller, and an accident occurred **on the return** trip, the buyer **is exempt because he is like a paid bailee.** Since the father-in-law decided not to accept them, and the prospective buyer no longer benefits from them, he is not considered to be a borrower, but rather, a paid bailee of these utensils, and a paid bailee is exempt in the case of an accident.

On the return trip, however, the situation has changed. The prospective in-laws declined the gift. One is no longer a hopeful purchaser. One will now pay the merchant a rental fee for having had use of the item — which places the arrangement in the category of *nosei sachar*, a paid custodian, who is exempt from *oness* but liable for theft, loss, and negligence. *Uvechazarah patur* — exempt on the return — because the relationship is now mutually beneficial: one pays the rental fee, the merchant receives it.

This *baraita* directly supports *Shmuel*: the sole beneficiary — the hopeful purchaser — bears *sho'el* liability. Once the relationship becomes bilateral, that liability is reduced to the level of a *shomer sachar*.

### Safsira Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא סַפְסִירָא דִּשְׁקַל חֲמָרָא לְזַבּוֹנֵי וְלָא אִיזַּבַּן. בַּהֲדֵי דַּהֲדַר, אִיתְּנִיס חַמְרָא, חַיְּיבֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְשַׁלּוֹמֵי. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: נֶאֶנְסוּ בַּהֲלִיכָה חַיָּיב, בַּחֲזָרָה פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain middleman [*safseira*] who took a donkey to sell but it was not sold,** i.e., he was unsuccessful in finding a buyer. **While he was in the midst of returning** the donkey to its owner, **an accident occurred** to the **donkey. Rav Naḥman** then **obligated him to pay** for it. **Rava raised an objection to Rav Naḥman** from this *baraita*: **If an accident occurred while on the way, he is liable** to pay; if it occurred **on the return** trip **he is exempt.** Since the case involving Rav Naḥman occurred on the return trip, why did Rav Naḥman obligate him to pay?

A *safsira* is a middleman who takes goods on a particular kind of consignment. Unlike a standard consignment arrangement where the owner retains title and the middleman takes a percentage of the sale, the *safsira* agrees to pay the owner a fixed price if he finds a buyer. Conceptually, the *safsira* is a hopeful purchaser of the goods — not selling the owner's item, but agreeing to buy it from the owner if he can locate a customer to whom he will then sell his own item at a markup.

A *safsira* took a donkey (*chamra*) to sell — notably, the *Ran* clarifies that this is a *beheimah*, an animal, not a barrel of wine, as *chamra* could in another vocalization suggest — and was unable to find a buyer. As he was returning, the donkey suffered an accident and died, entirely through *oness*. Rav Nachman ruled that he was liable to pay.

Rava challenged this from the *baraita* about the jewelry: one returning the jewelry to the merchant after it was declined is *patur* from *oness*. Why should the *safsira* be different when he too is returning?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲזָרָה דְסַפְסִירָא — הוֹלָכָה הִיא. דְּאִילּוּ מַשְׁכַּח לְזַבּוֹנֵי — אֲפִילּוּ אַבָּבָא דְבֵיתֵיהּ מִי לָא מְזַבֵּין לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him: The return** trip **of a middleman is** like **the trip** there, and an item is not considered returned until he actually gives it to its owner. This is **because were he to find** someone **to sell** the donkey to **even at the door of his house, would he not sell it?** Therefore, he retains the status of a borrower. However, in the case of bringing a gift to a particular person who does not accept it, the sale is nullified, and the prospective buyer has only to take care of the item until it is returned to its owner, which gives him the status of a paid bailee.

Rav Nachman replied: *chazara de'safsira holacha hi* — the return journey of a *safsira* is, legally speaking, still a "going." The *safsira* has not truly abandoned his hope of selling. *De'ilu mashkach lezvunei afilu abava deveitei, mi lo mazbin leih* — if he found a buyer at his own doorstep, he would of course have sold immediately. As long as he retains the item and remains a potential purchaser, he is still in the posture of the sole beneficiary and retains *sho'el* liability. Only when he actually returns the item to the owner and formally relinquishes his stake in the transaction does that liability dissolve.

## Circumcision Markers

### Uncircumcised Neder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאֲנִי נֶהֱנֶה לָעֲרֵלִים״ — מוּתָּר בְּעַרְלֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאָסוּר בְּמוּלֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says: **Benefiting from those who are uncircumcised is *konam* for me,** he is **permitted** to derive benefit **from uncircumcised Jews** because they are not regarded as uncircumcised, **but he is prohibited** from deriving benefit **from the circumcised of the nations of the world.**

The *mishnah* now introduces the terminology of circumcised and uncircumcised as group identifiers. *Konam she'ani neheneh la'areilim* — one who vows not to benefit from the uncircumcised — is *mutar be'areilei Yisrael* (permitted to benefit from uncircumcised Jews) and *assur bemulei ovdei kochavim* (forbidden to benefit from circumcised non-Jews).

This is striking. Many non-Jews are circumcised — Muslims, many Arabs, and as a matter of common practice in twentieth-century America, many non-Jewish men as well. Conversely, there are Jews who, for whatever reason, are uncircumcised. Yet none of this matters: the terms *arel* and *nimul* as used in the language of *nedarim* function as identity markers for non-Jews and Jews respectively, not as descriptions of physical status.

### Circumcised Neder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שֶׁאֲנִי נֶהֱנֶה לַמּוּלִים״ — אָסוּר בְּעַרְלֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, וּמוּתָּר בְּמוּלֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם. שֶׁאֵין הָעׇרְלָה קְרוּיָה אֶלָּא לְשֵׁם אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי כׇל הַגּוֹיִם עֲרֵלִים וְכׇל בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל עַרְלֵי לֵב״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה הַפְּלִשְׁתִּי הֶעָרֵל הַזֶּה״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״פֶּן תִּשְׂמַחְנָה בְּנוֹת פְּלִשְׁתִּים פֶּן תַּעֲלֹזְנָה בְּנוֹת הָעֲרֵלִים״.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, if he said: **Benefiting from those who are circumcised** is *konam* **for me, he is prohibited** from deriving benefit even **from uncircumcised Jews and** he is **permitted** to derive benefit **from the circumcised of the nations of the world, as** the term **uncircumcised is used only to name the nations of the world, as it is stated: "For all the nations are uncircumcised, but all the house of Israel are uncircumcised in the heart"** (Jeremiah 9:25), **and it says: "And this uncircumcised Philistine shall be"** (I Samuel 17:36), **and it says: "Lest the daughters of the Philistines rejoice, lest the daughters of the uncircumcised triumph"** (II Samuel 1:20). These verses indicate that ordinary gentiles are referred to as uncircumcised, regardless of whether they are actually circumcised.

The reverse: *she'ani ne'eneh le'mulim* — one who vows not to benefit from the circumcised — is *assur be'areilei Yisrael* (forbidden from uncircumcised Jews) and *mutar bemulei ovdei kochavim* (permitted to benefit from circumcised non-Jews). Here too, uncircumcised Jewish men are still called *mulim* — circumcised — in identity terms, and circumcised non-Jews are still called *areilim* — uncircumcised — as a matter of designation.

### Identity Markers

The *mishnah* explains the principle: *she'ein ha'arla keruyah ela leshem ovdei kochavim* — the foreskin is a term that refers specifically to non-Jews.

### Biblical Sources

The *mishnah* grounds this in two *pesukim*. *Ki kol hagoyim areilim* — all the nations are uncircumcised (Jeremiah 9:25) — and *vechol beit Yisrael arle lev* — yet the entire house of Israel are uncircumcised of heart. The verse acknowledges that Israel may be spiritually deficient in some respects, but they are not called uncircumcised in the default, physical sense. The term *arel* unqualified (*stam*) refers to non-Jews.

The *mishnah* adds: *ve'haya haplishtiti ha'arel hazeh* — and this Philistine, this uncircumcised one (I Samuel 17:26). The use of the term there is obviously derogatory — it would not have been remarkable simply to note that a Philistine was uncircumcised — and so it clearly functions as a marker of otherness and contempt. A further *pasuk*: *pen tismachna bnos plishtim pen ta'aloznah bnos ha'areilim* — lest the daughters of the Philistines rejoice, lest the daughters of the uncircumcised triumph (II Samuel 1:20). Here too, *areilim* is used as a general designation for the enemy, not a physiological description.

### Derogatory Usage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: מְאוּסָה הִיא הָעׇרְלָה, שֶׁנִּתְגַּנּוּ בָּהּ רְשָׁעִים שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי כׇל הַגּוֹיִם עֲרֵלִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says: The foreskin is repulsive, as** is evident from the fact that **the wicked are disgraced through it, as it is stated:** "Behold, the days come, says the Lord, that I will punish all them that are circumcised in their uncircumcision: Egypt, and Judah, and Edom, and the children of Ammon, and Moab, and all that have the corners of their hair polled, that dwell in the wilderness; **for all the nations are uncircumcised,** but all the house of Israel are uncircumcised in the heart" (Jeremiah 9:25), which indicates that there is an element of disgrace associated with the foreskin.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya adds: *me'usa hi ha'arla* — the foreskin is repugnant — in that it was employed as a way of deprecating the wicked, *shenitganu ba reshaim*, as the verse says *ki kol hagoyim areilim*. This is not merely a neutral label. The *mishnah* is quite candid: the term carries a strongly negative connotation, rooted in a categorical identification of non-Jews as *reshaim*. This reflects a pronounced *anachnu v'hem* — us-and-them — orientation, in which the foreskin becomes not merely a physical distinction but an identity and moral marker. And correspondingly, *milah* — circumcision — becomes not just a ritual act but the defining marker of Jewish identity, to the point where the label attaches even when the physical act was not performed.

## Brit Milah Aggada

### Brit Milah Greatness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: גְּדוֹלָה מִילָה, שֶׁנִּכְרְתוּ עָלֶיהָ שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה בְּרִיתוֹת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: גְּדוֹלָה מִילָה שֶׁדּוֹחָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת חֲמוּרָה. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: גְּדוֹלָה מִילָה — שֶׁלֹּא נִתְלָה לוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה הַצַּדִּיק עָלֶיהָ מְלֹא שָׁעָה. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: גְּדוֹלָה מִילָה — שֶׁדּוֹחָה אֶת הַנְּגָעִים. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: גְּדוֹלָה מִילָה, שֶׁכׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת שֶׁעָשָׂה אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ לֹא נִקְרָא שָׁלֵם עַד שֶׁמָּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִתְהַלֵּךְ לְפָנַי וֶהְיֵה תָמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael says:** So **great is** the mitzva of **circumcision that thirteen covenants were sealed with regard to it,** for the word covenant appears thirteen times in the biblical passage that discusses circumcision (Genesis, chapter 17). **Rabbi Yosei says:** So **great is** the mitzva of **circumcision that it overrides the strict** *halakhot* of **Shabbat,** as circumcision is performed even if the eighth day following the birth of a son occurs on Shabbat, despite the fact that circumcision violates the prohibition of labor on Shabbat. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: Great is** the mitzva of **circumcision,** as is evident from the fact **that** the punishment of **Moses the righteous for** not circumcising his son when he was capable of doing so **was not postponed** for even **a full hour** (see Exodus 4:24–26). **Rabbi Neḥemya says:** So **great is** the mitzva of **circumcision that it overrides** the prohibitions associated with **leprosy.** If leprosy is found on the foreskin of an infant, although it is generally prohibited to cut the afflicted area, it is permitted to do so to perform the mitzva of circumcision. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** So **great is** the mitzva of **circumcision that** despite **all the mitzvot that Abraham our Patriarch did,** he **was not called wholehearted until he circumcised** himself, **as it is stated** at the time that the mitzva was given to him: **"Walk before Me and you should be wholehearted"** (Genesis 17:1).

The *mishnah* then opens into an extended *aggadic* collection on the greatness of *brit milah*, a passage notable for its placement within a *mishnah* on *nedarim*. The *Albeck* edition notes that this material appears to have been carried over from the *Tosefta*, where it reads as an expansion. Yet even the Kaufmann manuscript — generally considered the best manuscript of the *Mishnah* — already contains it within the body of the *mishnah*. That itself is striking.

The opinions are stacked one upon another. Rabbi Yishmael: *gedola mila shenichretu aleha shelosh esrei britos* — the greatness of *milah* is that thirteen covenants were cut over it, as the word *brit* appears thirteen times in the passage in which God commands Abraham regarding circumcision. Rabbi Yosi: *gedola mila shedocha es hashabbos hachamura* — great is *milah* in that it overrides *shabbat*, severe as *shabbat* is; the *brit milah* of the eighth day is performed on *shabbat* when it falls on that day. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: great is *milah* in that not even the righteous Moses — and it is notable that the *mishnah* uses *Moshe HaTzadik*, "Moses the Righteous," a designation one rarely encounters; the usual formulation is *Moshe Rabbeinu* — was given a moment's latitude when he was lax about performing the *milah* for his son at the *malon*. Rabbi Nechemyah: great is *milah* in that it overrides the laws of *nega'im* — a spot of *tzara'at* on the foreskin would be excised. *Rebbi*: with all the *mitzvot* that Abraham performed, he was not called *tamim* — whole, complete — until he circumcised himself: *His'halech lefanai ve'heye tamim*, walk before me and be perfect, followed immediately by the commandment of *milah*.

### Ultimate Importance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: גְּדוֹלָה מִילָה — שֶׁאִלְמָלֵא הִיא, לֹא בָּרָא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֶת עוֹלָמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ אִם לֹא בְרִיתִי יוֹמָם וָלָיְלָה חֻקּוֹת שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ לֹא שָׂמְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** so **great is** the mitzva of **circumcision that if not for it the Holy One, Blessed be He, would not have created His world, as it is stated: "Thus says the Lord: If My covenant be not with day and night, I would not have appointed the ordinances of heaven and earth"** (Jeremiah 33:25), and the covenant that exists day and night is the covenant of circumcision, as it is always found on the person's body.

The final opinion reaches the highest level of claim: *gedola mila she'ilmale hi lo bara HaKadosh Baruch Hu es ha'olam* — were it not for *brit milah*, God would not have created the world. The source: *koh amar Hashem im lo brisi yomam va'laili chukos shamayim va'aretz lo samti* — so says God: if not for My covenant day and night, I would not have established the laws of heaven and earth. The word *brit* in this verse, usually taken to refer to *Torah*, is here read as referring to *milah*.

### Milah Wordplay

One might ask — and this question has no clean answer — why this collection appears here, in *Masechet Nedarim*, in the middle of a *mishnah* concerned with group-definition rules in *nedarim*. One might also ask why the discussion of *brit milah* does not appear in the *sugya* of Rabbi Eliezer in *Shabbat*, where the *halachic* details of performing a *brit milah* are analyzed at length, and where such *aggadic* material might seem more at home.

One thought — admittedly somewhat speculative — is the wordplay on *milah* itself. *Milah* means both circumcision and word. The entire third *perek* has revolved around the power of speech: what words create a *neder*, whether mouth and heart are aligned, what linguistic categories people use when they invoke a group's identity. At the conclusion of that *perek*, a reflection on *milah* in its dual sense may be intentional. And yet, there is a deep tension here: a *neder* takes a neutral world and gives it *halachic* status through words alone, with no physical change; whereas the entire weight of the *brit milah* discussion — especially *Rebbi's* opinion that Abraham was not *tamim* until he underwent *milah* — emphasizes the centrality of the physical act. Indeed, one might note the irony that a Jewish male who was never circumcised is nevertheless called a *nimul* in the language of the *mishnah*, suggesting that the *identity label* can function independently of the *physical reality*. There is something deeply interesting at the intersection of those ideas, even if the *mishnah* does not develop it explicitly.

On a different note: one might also observe that the *brit milah* discussion of Hatafat dam brit — as noted in connection with a convert who is already circumcised — demonstrates that the act is more than purely physical. The drop of covenantal blood required even of one already circumcised reflects a ritual and covenantal dimension irreducible to surgery.

### Moshe's Dilemma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: גְּדוֹלָה מִילָה, שֶׁכׇּל זְכִיּוֹת שֶׁעָשָׂה מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ לֹא עָמְדוּ לוֹ כְּשֶׁנִּתְרַשֵּׁל מִן הַמִּילָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּפְגְּשֵׁהוּ ה׳ וַיְבַקֵּשׁ הֲמִיתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says:** So **great is** the mitzva of **circumcision that all the merits that Moses our teacher** accrued when he **performed** mitzvot **did not protect him when he was negligent about** performing **the** mitzva of **circumcision, as it is stated: "And the Lord met him and sought to kill him"** (Exodus 4:24).

The bottom of the page brings a *baraita* keyed to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's ruling that Moses was not given any grace period when he was lax about his son's *milah*. The *baraita* asks: but was Moses truly lax? Surely he had a reason. Indeed he did. He reasoned: if I perform the *brit milah* and then immediately set out on the journey God commanded — *lech shuv Mitzrayim*, return to Egypt — the danger to a newborn in the first three days after *brit milah* is well documented, as the verse says *vayihi bayom hashlishi biheyotam ko'avim*. Traveling with an infant three days post-circumcision is a genuine *sakana*. On the other hand, if I wait the three days, God has explicitly commanded me to go immediately. Moses was thus caught between two obligations, each of which made the other impossible to fulfill immediately.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי: חַס וְשָׁלוֹם שֶׁמּשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ נִתְרַשֵּׁל מִן הַמִּילָה, אֶלָּא כָּךְ אָמַר: אָמוּל וְאֵצֵא — סַכָּנָה הִיא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי בַיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי בִּהְיוֹתָם כֹּאֲבִים וְגוֹ׳״. אָמוּל וְאֶשְׁהֶא שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אָמַר לִי ״לֵךְ שֻׁב מִצְרָיִם״. אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי מָה נֶעֱנַשׁ מֹשֶׁה —
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: Heaven forbid that Moses our teacher was neglectful of the** mitzva of **circumcision. Rather, this** is what **he said:** If **I circumcise** the child now **and depart** to begin my journey, it **is a danger** for the child, **as it is stated: "And it came to pass on the third day, when they were in pain"** (Genesis 34:25), which indicates that the pain of circumcision lasts for several days and the child may be in danger while in pain. If **I circumcise** him immediately **and wait three days** and only then embark on the journey, this is problematic, as **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to me: "Go, return into Egypt"** (Exodus 4:19), i.e., go immediately. For these reasons Moses did not circumcise the child immediately, but no neglect existed on his part. **But** according to this explanation, **for what reason was Moses punished?**

Why then was Moses punished at all? The *baraita* answers: *mipnei shenitasek b'malon techilah* — because upon arriving in the town, Moses first attended to his lodging arrangements before performing the *brit milah*. Had circumcision been his absolute first priority upon reaching the *malon*, the threat would not have materialized. But by addressing accommodations first — even understandably, even reasonably — he demonstrated that *brit milah* was not at the very top of his hierarchy in that moment. And for that, the verse says *vayifgeshehu Hashem vayivakesh hamito* — God encountered him and sought to kill him. The lesson, conveyed through the *aggadic* reading, is that even a man of Moses's stature and righteousness — and even when the delay is logically explicable — the *brit milah* admits of no postponement once the moment for it has arrived.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּתְעַסֵּק בַּמָּלוֹן תְּחִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וַיְהִי בַּדֶּרֶךְ בַּמָּלוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Because he was occupied with lodging first** and did not immediately perform the mitzva of circumcision, **as it is stated: "And it came to pass on the way at the lodging-place"** (Exodus 4:24).
