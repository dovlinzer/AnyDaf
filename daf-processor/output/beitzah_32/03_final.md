# Beitzah 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lamp Obstructions

### Pochet Haneir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין אֶת הַנֵּר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה כֶּלִי, וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין פֶּחָמִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאֵין חוֹתְכִין אֶת הַפְּתִילָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חוֹתְכָהּ בָּאוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: On a Festival, **one may not hollow out** a piece of clay to form **a lamp** into which he will place oil and a wick **because he** thereby **creates a vessel. And** similarly, **one may not produce charcoal** at all **on a Festival** because this is not labor for sustenance. **And** similarly, **one may not cut the wick,** as this is considered mending a vessel. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If one requires a wick of a particular length, **he may cut it** by burning it **in a fire** but not by cutting it with a knife.

The Gemara continues on 32a, approximately ten lines from the bottom. The *mishna* rules that one may not perform *pochet haneir* — removing the stuffing from a lamp that has undergone only its first firing and has not yet finished hardening — because doing so constitutes an act of completing a vessel. Tosafot understands the *mishna* in precisely these terms: the removal of the obstruction before the second firing transforms the lamp into a functional vessel, and that act of completion is prohibited on *Yom Tov*.

### Vessel Completion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן תְּנָא דִּפְחִיתַת נֵר מָנָא הוּא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: כְּלִי חֶרֶס, מֵאֵימָתַי מְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה — מִשֶּׁנִּגְמְרָה מְלַאכְתּוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיִּצְרְפוֹ בַּכִּבְשָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that **hollowing out a lamp is** considered creating **a vessel,** even if it is not fired in a furnace? **Rav Yosef said: It is Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity: **From when is an earthenware vessel susceptible to ritual impurity?** It is **from when its work is completed,** i.e., when the clay has been made into the form of a vessel; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is **from when** the vessel **is fired in the furnace.**

The Gemara seeks to identify which *tanna* holds that removing something from a partially completed vessel — even before the hardening process is finished — renders it a vessel. It first compares the lamp case to a general tannaitic statement about pottery, which Tosafot understood as addressing exactly this scenario. The Gemara then turns to a parallel dispute involving frying pans. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that even while the cover remains sealed on, the frying pan already possesses vessel status — it is considered a vessel at an earlier stage. Rabbi Eliezer berabbi Tzadok disagrees, arguing *lefi shelo nigmar melachtan* — their work is not yet complete — which is understood to mean that the cover has not yet been removed. Once the cover is removed, however, even before the firing and hardening process is complete, the frying pan attains vessel status. According to Rabbi Eliezer berabbi Tzadok, then, it is precisely the removal of the obstruction that makes it a vessel, even prior to the completion of the manufacturing process.

### Village Frying Pans

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר הָתָם אֶלָּא דַּחֲזֵי לְקַבּוֹלֵי בֵּיהּ מִידֵּי, אֲבָל הָכָא לְמַאי חֲזִי? לְקַבּוֹלֵי בֵּיהּ פְּשִׁיטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: From where** do you conclude that this is the same opinion? **Perhaps Rabbi Meir stated** his opinion **only there,** with regard to vessels, **since they are fit to contain something.** Although they cannot hold liquids before being fired in a furnace, they can contain other items. **But here, for what** use **is** this lamp **fit?** The Gemara responds: It can be used **to contain** small **coins.**

Abaye challenges this analogy. He suggests that perhaps Rabbi Eliezer berabbi Tzadok's ruling applies only there — to the frying pan — because once the cover is removed, even if the pan has not yet hardened fully, it is still *chazi lekippulei bei midi*, fit to receive and store objects. But this small lamp, he asks, *lemai chazi* — what use could it possibly serve? The Gemara responds: *lekippulei bei peshiti* — one can place small coins or objects inside it. Therefore, even the partially processed lamp has a functional use sufficient to confer vessel status, and the *mishna*'s prohibition on removing its obstruction is justified on those grounds.

In sum, the Gemara understands the *mishna* as prohibiting the removal of an obstruction that is part of the vessel-making process, on the grounds that the act of removal itself — even before full hardening — completes the vessel. Two tannaitic disputes in the domain of *tumah ve'taharah* are invoked to support this understanding, since both address the question of at what stage a partially manufactured vessel becomes susceptible to impurity.

### Function vs. Form

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין אֶת הַנֵּר, וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין אִלְפָּסִין חִרָנִיּוֹת בְּיוֹם טוֹב. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מַתִּיר בְּאִלְפָּסִין חִרָנִיּוֹת. מַאי חִרָנִיּוֹת? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: עִרָנִיּוֹת. מַאי עִרָנִיּוֹת? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: צָעֵי חַקְלָיָיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One may not hollow out a lamp, and one may not create *ḥaraniyyot* pots on a Festival. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel permits** creating ***ḥaraniyyot* pots.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of ***ḥaraniyyot*? Rav Yehuda said:** They are pots **of a city [*iraniyyot*].** The Gemara again inquires: **What is** the meaning of ***iraniyyot*? Abaye said:** It means **villagers' bowls** who will use even clay vessels that have not been fully formed or fired, as they are not particular with regard to half-finished utensils.

The Gemara briefly revisits the *charniyes* frying pans. The *baraita* quoted earlier defines these as *iraniot* — village frying pans — and Abaye explains that the term refers to *tzachei chaklaita*, field platters. Frying pans with the cover still sealed could still serve as platters, and in villages where people are less fastidious about such things, they might be willing to use them at that stage. There is a deeper conceptual tension underlying this entire discussion: one mode of thought holds that functionality determines vessel status — if it can be used, it is a vessel; another holds that formal completion of the manufacturing process is what matters, regardless of interim functional utility. Both considerations are at work in the tannaitic debate, intertwined rather than neatly separated.

## Charcoal & Bathhouses

### Charniyes Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין פֶּחָמִין. פְּשִׁיטָא, לְמַאי חָזֵי? תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְמוֹסְרָן לְאוֹלְיָירִין לְבוֹ בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the mishna: **And one may not produce charcoal** on a Festival. The Gemara challenges: It is **obvious** that one may perform only labor for sustenance. **For what is** charcoal **fit;** what purpose does it have for the sake of sustenance? **Rabbi Ḥiyya taught: It is necessary** to teach this *halakha* **only** in the case of **handing** the charcoal **over to bathhouse attendants [*olyarin*]** who heat up the bathhouse **for that day.** One might have thought that since charcoal is used for the sake of washing on a Festival, producing it should be permitted.

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: *ein pochtin et haneir ve'ein osin ilpas ilpasin charaniot beYom Tov* — one may not remove the stuffing from a lamp, and one may not make *ilpasin charaniot*, village frying pans, on *Yom Tov*. Interestingly, this *baraita* combines the lamp discussion from the *mishna* with the *Yom Tov* context, applying to both the same principle that had been debated in *tumah ve'taharah*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel permits the village frying pans, apparently taking a different position on when vessel status is attained.

### Charcoal Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבוֹ בַּיּוֹם מִי שְׁרֵי? כִּדְאָמַר רָבָא: לְהַזִּיעַ, וְקוֹדֶם גְּזֵרָה. הָכָא נָמֵי: לְהַזִּיעַ, וְקוֹדֶם גְּזֵרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this: **And on that day is it permitted** to heat up water for washing? The Sages decreed that one may not wash in a bathhouse on a Festival, even if the water was heated up the day before, and certainly it is prohibited if the water was heated on the Festival itself. The Gemara replies: **As Rava said** with regard to a different issue, this decree does not refer to actual washing but to entering a bathhouse merely in order **to sweat, and** it was stated **before** the enactment of the **decree** against sweating in a bathhouse. **Here, too,** one can explain that the statement of Rabbi Ḥiyya is referring to a case where one entered **to sweat, and** he entered at a time **before** the **decree** was enacted.

The Gemara states: *ve'ein osin pechamin* — one may not make charcoal on *Yom Tov*. The Gemara immediately asks: *p'shita* — is this not obvious? And then: *lemai chazi* — what would one use it for? This is a striking formulation. One might have thought the sole question is whether the act is prohibited, not whether the charcoal has a use. The implicit reasoning is as follows: making a fire on *Yom Tov* is permitted. If charcoal were used for cooking, one might argue — by analogy to permitted fire-making — that producing it should also be allowed. But charcoal, as Rashi explains, is used primarily for metalwork and smelting, not for cooking. Hence the prohibition is obvious and requires no further analysis.

### Charcoal Definition

But what then is the *hava amina*, the initial assumption that the *mishna* needs to dispel? The Gemara answers through a *baraita* of Rabbi Chiyya: *lo nitzrecha ela lemosran le'ulyarin bo bayom* — the ruling is needed for a case where one intended to deliver the charcoal to *ulyarin*, the bathhouse workers who heat the water. One might have thought: heating water for bathing is *ochel nefesh*, or at least *tzorech kol gufo*, a whole-body need — so producing charcoal for that purpose should be permitted. It is to exclude this possibility that the *baraita* speaks.

### Bathhouse Ban

The Gemara immediately challenges this: *bo bayom nishtero* — since when is it permitted to use a heated bathhouse on *Yom Tov* at all? This leads to an important *halacha*. The Sages decreed — *gzeirat merchatzaot* — that one may not enter a heated bathhouse on *Shabbos*, even if the water was heated before *Shabbos*, out of concern that one might come to heat water on *Shabbos* itself. The question is whether this decree extended to *Yom Tov* as well. On *Yom Tov*, heating water for one's face, hands, and feet would in any case be a legitimate act; and one might suppose that heating water for the whole body, as a *tzorech kol gufo*, would also be permitted, making bathhouse use permissible. Nevertheless, the Gemara makes clear that the *gzeirat merchatzaot* applied to *Yom Tov* as well — bathhouses were entirely off-limits — even if under certain circumstances heating the water itself might have been technically permissible. Since bathhouse use is prohibited on *Yom Tov*, the argument that charcoal may be produced for the *ulyarin* collapses.

### Steam Before Decree

The Gemara then cites Rava: *lehazia* — one could, however, have used the charcoal for the steam room, the sauna portion of the bathhouse. Rava teaches that when the *gzeira* was first enacted against bathhouses, the steam room was not yet included in the prohibition. At that earlier stage, using a steam room would have been a *tzorech kol gufo* and rabbinically unobjectionable. The *mishna* therefore needs to teach that even under those circumstances — when steam use was legitimate and constituted a valid bodily need — one still may not produce charcoal on *Yom Tov* to fuel it.

### Vessel Ban Hierarchy

This point carries significant halakhic weight. Even if the purpose is genuinely *tzorech kol haguf*, and even if Rabbi Yehuda — who appears in the *mishna* and who permits *machshirei ochel nefesh* — does not contest this prohibition, the production of charcoal remains forbidden. The reason is that charcoal, whatever its use, is being fashioned into something — it was a piece of wood and is now a processed, functional material with a degree of permanence. Making it involves a quality of *tikkun kli*, of making a vessel, that places it in a different category from mere fire-related acts. The Gemara's treatment implies that Rabbi Yehuda himself would concede this point: even he, who permits preparatory acts for *ochel nefesh*, would not permit the making of something that has this character of vessel-production, even if the ultimate use is legitimate. The question of whether charcoal truly fits our intuitive category of *kli* is itself interesting — charcoal is used by being destroyed, unlike most vessels — but the Gemara treats it as sufficiently vessel-like to trigger the prohibition.

### Yom Tov Bathing

This discussion of *gzeirat merchatzaot* has direct contemporary relevance for the question of bathing on *Yom Tov*. The Gemara elsewhere records a dispute between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel: Beis Hillel permits heating water on *Yom Tov* for *panav yadav ve'raglav* — one's face, hands, and feet — but not for *kol gufo*, one's whole body. Tosafot explains that heating water for one's whole body was not considered a universal need — not everyone, in that era, required a full hot bath. Many *poskim* reason, however, that in our times a hot shower or bath is entirely universal, and therefore constitutes a genuine *tzorech kol haguf*, making it permissible to heat water for bathing on *Yom Tov*.

Yet even if one accepts that a hot shower is permitted on *Yom Tov* — and many *poskim* do permit it — a hot bath remains forbidden. The *gzeirat merchatzaot* specifically targeted immersion in a bathhouse, and that prohibition remains in force rabbinically even today. The practical upshot is that a hot shower may be taken on *Yom Tov*, as a *tzorech kol haguf* falling within the *ochel nefesh* framework of permitted *Yom Tov* acts, while a hot bath remains off-limits by virtue of the original *gzeira*. The two acts, though closely related, are subject to distinct halakhic analyses.

## Wick Modification

### Cutting Wicks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין חוֹתְכִין אֶת הַפְּתִילָה לִשְׁנַיִם. מַאי שְׁנָא בְּסַכִּין דְּלָא —
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: **And one may not cut a wick into two,** but Rabbi Yehuda permits cutting it by means of fire. The Gemara asks: **What is different** about cutting a wick with fire as opposed to with a knife, **that one may not** cut a wick **with a knife?**

The Gemara teaches: *ve'ein chotzin et haptilah lishnayim* — one may not cut a wick into two pieces on *Yom Tov*. This too falls under the prohibition of making a vessel. One might initially think: where is the act of creation here? Before cutting, the wick was usable; after cutting, it is still usable, merely more appropriately sized. Yet the Gemara treats the cutting as an act of *tikkun* — fashioning the wick into its more functional, properly sized form. This connects to the broader question raised by the cutting of paper towels, tin foil, and similar materials on *Yom Tov*: even if the item was usable before, cutting it to its optimal size constitutes an act that is at minimum rabbinically problematic, and potentially involves the biblical prohibition of *mechatech*, cutting to a precise measurement. The Gemara here establishes that creating the wick in its more appropriate form is indeed a concern.

Rabbi Yehuda, however, permits dividing the wick by means of fire. The Gemara asks: *mai shena besakin delo* — why is a knife forbidden? Because *kematakin mana* — one is fixing a vessel. Whether this phrase denotes a biblical violation of *makeh bepatish*, the final blow in vessel-making, or only a rabbinic prohibition resembling vessel-completion, is debated among the *Rishonim*. Either way, it is forbidden. But if so, the Gemara asks, *or nami kematakin mana* — using fire also makes it a vessel; why does Rabbi Yehuda permit it?

### Fire Cutting Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָמְתַקֵּן מָנָא, בָּאוּר נָמֵי קָא מְתַקֵּן מָנָא! תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: חוֹתְכָהּ בָּאוּר בְּפִי שְׁתֵּי נֵרוֹת. אָמַר רַב נָתָן בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: מוֹחֲטִין אֶת הַפְּתִילָה בְּיוֹם טוֹב. מַאי ״מוֹחֲטִין״? אָמַר רַב חֲנִינָא בַּר שֶׁלֶמְיָא (מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב): לְעַדּוֹיֵי חוּשְׁכָא.
>
> **Translation:** Is it because **he** thereby **mends a vessel?** If so, when one cuts it **in the fire, he is also preparing a vessel** for use. **Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** in explanation: **He cuts it by fire in the mouth of two candles.** In other words, he does not simply cut a wick, but rather inserts a long wick into two lamps, which he subsequently lights in the middle. This indeed leads to the formation of two separate wicks, but only as a result of kindling two lamps. **Rav Natan bar Abba said** that **Rav said: One may *moḥet* a wick on a Festival.** The term *moḥet* was unknown, and the Gemara therefore asks: **What is** the meaning of the word ***moḥet*? Rav Ḥanina bar Shelemya said in the name of Rav: To remove the dark;** in other words, it is permitted to remove the burnt, charcoaled section to make the lamp shine more brightly.

Rabbi Chiyya's *baraita* resolves this: *chotcho ba'or befi shtei nerot* — one divides it by fire at the mouth of two lamps. The image is of two lamps positioned side by side, with a single wick running between them. Rather than cutting the wick and then lighting it, one lights the wick in the middle, between the two lamps, so that the flame naturally divides it into two functional wicks, each serving one lamp. The act is primarily one of lighting the lamp; the division of the wick is incidental to it.

As Rashi explains: *delo mochiacha deletikunei mana mechatech* — it is not apparent that one's purpose is to fashion a vessel. At first glance, one appears simply to be lighting a lamp. This reasoning works most cleanly if the underlying prohibition is rabbinic in nature — if the problem is not a genuine biblical act of vessel-making but a rabbinic enactment against acts that resemble vessel-making. Where the experiential character of the act is primarily that of lamp-lighting, and the division is a byproduct, the rabbinic concern does not apply. This is not a matter of deceiving anyone; it is that the nature of the act, as experienced and performed, does not fall within the category the Sages prohibited.

### Wick Needling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי בַּר קַפָּרָא: שִׁשָּׁה דְּבָרִים נֶאֶמְרוּ בִּפְתִילָה. שְׁלֹשָׁה לְהַחְמִיר, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה לְהָקֵל. לְהַחְמִיר: אֵין גּוֹדְלִין אוֹתָהּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאֵין מְהַבְהֲבִין אוֹתָהּ בָּאוּר, וְאֵין חוֹתְכִין אוֹתָהּ לִשְׁנַיִם. לְהָקֵל: מְמַעֲכָהּ בַּיָּד, וְשׁוֹרָהּ בְּשֶׁמֶן, וְחוֹתְכָהּ בָּאוּר בְּפִי שְׁתֵּי נֵרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Bar Kappara taught: Six matters were stated with regard to** the *halakhot* of **a wick** on a Festival, **three** of which **are to be stringent and three** of which **are to be lenient.** The three *halakhot* **to be stringent** are: **One may not spin** or twist **it *ab initio* on a Festival, and one may not singe it in fire** before lighting it so that it will burn well, **and one may not cut it into two.** The three *halakhot* **to be lenient** are: **One may crush it by hand,** as although it is prohibited to twist it into a wick, one may adjust its shape in an unusual manner; **and one may soak it in oil** so that it will later burn well; **and one may cut it by fire in the mouth of two candles.**

Rav Natan bar Abba said in the name of Rav: *mochtin et haptilah beyom tov* — one may needle a wick on *Yom Tov*. Rav Chanina bar Shemaya explains: *le'aduyei chashocha* — to remove the blackness, meaning one takes a sharp instrument and scrapes the charred residue from a previously used wick, straightening the threads and preparing it for reuse. This raises the question: is this not also a form of *tikkun kli*, repairing a vessel? The answer is analogous to the permitted sharpening of a dull knife, which was discussed earlier in the *masechta*, particularly according to Rabbi Yehuda's more lenient approach. One is not creating something new; one is restoring what had already been functional. That is permitted.

### Six Wick Rules

Bar Kappara taught a *baraita* summarizing six rulings regarding wicks — *sheisha devarim ne'emru b'ptila* — three stringent and three lenient.

The three stringent rulings are: one may not braid a wick (*ein gozdin oto l'chat-chila b'yom tov*) — taking strands and twisting them into a wick from scratch is a genuine act of making something; one may not char the tip of a wick in fire (*ve'ein mehavvin oto ba'ur*) — pre-charring, which appears in the language of *Bameh Madlikin*, facilitates lighting and constitutes fashioning the wick; and one may not cut a wick into two (*ve'ein chotchin oto lishnayim*) — as discussed above.

The three lenient rulings are: *memacha b'yad* — one may roll or flatten the wick in one's hand to help it absorb oil better. Rashi notes that this is a *tikkun kal al yad* — a light adjustment made by hand, without precision or craftsmanship, and thus not within the scope of vessel-making. The absence of any artisanal character, and the fact that the wick was already functional beforehand, distinguish this from the prohibited acts. *Ve'shora bashemen* — one may soak the wick in oil, pre-saturating it; this too is closer to beginning to use the wick than to making it. And *ve'chotcha ba'ur* — one may divide the wick by fire between two lamps, ruling in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda's position and the method described in Rabbi Chiyya's *baraita*.

## Compassion & Identity

### Shabsai Incident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נָתָן בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: עַתִּירֵי בָּבֶל, יוֹרְדֵי גֵיהִנָּם הֵם. כִּי הָא דְּשַׁבְּתַאי בַּר מָרִינוּס אִקְּלַע לְבָבֶל, בְּעָא מִנַּיְיהוּ עִסְקָא — וְלָא יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ. מְזוֹנֵי מֵיזָן — נָמֵי לָא זַיְנוּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § After citing one teaching in the name of Rav Natan bar Abba, the Gemara quotes a few more statements attributed to the same scholar. Since he is not mentioned often, Rav Natan's teachings are arranged together, so that they can be remembered more easily. **Rav Natan bar Abba said** that **Rav said: The wealthy** Jews **of Babylonia will descend to Gehenna** because they do not have compassion on others. This is illustrated by incidents **such as this: Shabbetai bar Marinus happened to come to Babylonia. He requested** their participation in **a business venture,** to lend him money and receive half the profits in return, **and they did not give** it to **him.** Furthermore, when he asked them **to sustain him with food, they likewise refused to sustain him.**

Rav Natan bar Abba — the same *amora* who discussed the needling of wicks — said in the name of Rav: *atirei Bavel yordei Gehennam hen* — the wealthy people of Babylonia are destined for Gehinnom. The reason is illustrated by the case of Shabsai bar Marinos, who arrived in Babylonia. He was not himself wealthy; rather, the story exposes a culture of indifference among the Babylonian rich. Shabsai sought to engage in some business dealings with the local community — not a handout, but the opportunity to participate in commerce. They refused him. When reduced to asking for food, they refused that as well. Shabsai declared: *hanei me'eirev rav ka'asu* — these people must be descended from the *Eirev Rav*, the mixed multitude that left Egypt with the Israelites but were not truly of them. They are not real Jews.

### Rachamim Lineage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: הָנֵי מֵעֵרֶב רַב קָא אָתוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנָתַן לְךָ רַחֲמִים וְרִחַמְךָ״, כׇּל הַמְרַחֵם עַל הַבְּרִיּוֹת — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהוּא מִזַּרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ, וְכׇל מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְרַחֵם עַל הַבְּרִיּוֹת — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִזַּרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He said: These** wealthy people are not descendants of our forefathers, **but they came from the mixed multitude, as it is written: "And show you compassion, and have compassion upon you,** and multiply you, as He has sworn to your fathers" (Deuteronomy 13:18), from which it is derived: **Anyone who has compassion** for God's **creatures, it is known that he is of the descendants of Abraham, our father, and anyone who does not have compassion** for God's **creatures, it is known that he is not of the descendants of Abraham, our father.** Since these wealthy Babylonians do not have compassion on people, clearly they are not descended from Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

The basis for this declaration is a *pasuk*: *ve'nasan lecha rachamim ve'richamcha... ka'asher nishba la'avosecha* — God will give you compassion and have compassion on you, as He swore to your forefathers. The conventional reading of this verse — which appears after the commandment to destroy an *Ir Hanidachas* — is that God will restore His mercy to you after you have had to perform this harsh act. But the Gemara reads it differently, and several *mefarshim* on the verse share this reading: *ve'nasan lecha rachamim* means that God will have implanted within you the capacity for compassion — and the question of whether He has done so, whether you truly carry the compassion of your forefathers, is demonstrated by your behavior toward others.

From this the Gemara derives: *kol hamerachem al habriyot, beyadua shehu mizar'o shel Avraham Avinu* — anyone who has compassion upon God's creatures is known to be of the descendants of Abraham our father. *Ve'chol she'eino merachem al habriyot, beyadua she'eino mizar'o shel Avraham Avinu* — and one who lacks compassion is known not to be of his descendants. The wealth of Babylonia, then, is not the problem in itself; it is the culture it apparently produced — indifference to those in need, unwillingness to extend even basic sustenance to a fellow Jew in distress — that constitutes a severance from the Abrahamic inheritance.

### Three Miserable Lives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נָתָן בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַמְצַפֶּה עַל שֻׁלְחַן אֲחֵרִים — עוֹלָם חָשַׁךְ בַּעֲדוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נוֹדֵד הוּא לַלֶּחֶם אַיֵּה יָדַע כִּי נָכוֹן בְּיָדוֹ יוֹם חֹשֶׁךְ״. רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: אַף חַיָּיו אֵינָן חַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** This is another teaching that **Rav Natan bar Abba said** that **Rav said: Whoever looks to the table of others** for his sustenance, **the world is dark for him.** Everything looks bleak and hopeless to him, **for it is stated: "He wanders abroad for bread: Where is it? He knows the day of darkness is ready at his hand"** (Job 15:23). **Rav Ḥisda said: Even his life is no life,** as he receives no satisfaction from it.

From this *aggadic* passage, the Gemara transitions to a related statement of Rav Natan bar Abba in the name of Rav: *kol hametzapeh al shulchan acherim olam chashoch ba'ado* — whoever is always looking toward another's table, the whole world grows dark before him. Rashi connects this to the verse: *noded hu lalechem aye, yada ki nachon beyado yom choshech* — he wanders seeking bread, though he should know that a day of darkness is ready at hand. The person who is never content, always measuring his portion against someone else's, cannot experience the life he actually has. Rav Chisda adds: *af chayav einam chayim* — such a person's life is not truly a life.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה חַיֵּיהֶן אֵינָם חַיִּים, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: הַמְצַפֶּה לְשֻׁלְחַן חֲבֵירוֹ, וּמִי שֶׁאִשְׁתּוֹ מוֹשֶׁלֶת עָלָיו, וּמִי שֶׁיִּסּוּרִין מוֹשְׁלִין בְּגוּפוֹ. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף מִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא חָלוּק אֶחָד. וְתַנָּא קַמָּא: אֶפְשָׁר דִּמְעַיֵּין בְּמָנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In support of this last claim, the Gemara cites a *baraita* in which **the Sages taught:** There are **three** whose **lives are not lives, and they are as follows: One who looks to the table of others** for his sustenance; **and one whose wife rules** over **him; and one whose body is ruled by suffering. And some say: Even one who has only one robe.** Since he cannot wash it properly, he suffers from lice and dirt. The Gemara comments: **And the first *tanna*,** who did not include such a person, maintains: **It is possible** for him **to examine his clothes** and remove the lice, which would alleviate his suffering.

This leads to a *baraita*: *gimel chayav einam chayim* — three people whose lives are not real lives. The first is one who constantly gazes at another's table, as just described. The second is *mi she'ishto moshelet alav* — a man over whom his wife rules. The third is one *she'yisurin moshlin begufo* — whose body is racked by suffering; overwhelmed by physical pain, such a person cannot feel truly alive. A fourth opinion adds: *af mi she'ein lo ela chaluk echad* — even one who has only a single shirt, because that garment will accumulate filth and lice, making his existence miserable. The *Tanna Kamma*, who omits this last case, responds that one can de-louse a shirt while wearing it — an anti-climactic but characteristically Talmudic precision.

## Pottery & Paper

### Breaking Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין שׁוֹבְרִין אֶת הַחֶרֶס, וְאֵין חוֹתְכִין הַנְּיָיר לִצְלוֹת בּוֹ מָלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not break earthenware** on a Festival. **And one may not cut paper in order to roast salted** fish **on it.** Earthenware shards or pieces of paper that have been soaked in water were placed on the metal surface or in the oven in which the fish was roasted, so that it would not be burned by the heat.

The *mishna* continues with related prohibitions. *Ein shovrin et hacheres* — one may not break pottery on *Yom Tov*, even to use the shard as a scoop, a scraper, or a surface on which to cook a fish over fire. *Ve'ein chotchin haniyar litzlot bo maliach* — one may not cut papyrus to roast a salted fish upon it. These acts — breaking pottery or cutting papyrus to create a usable cooking surface — constitute the making of a vessel even in the direct context of food preparation.

### Oven Sweeping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין גּוֹרְפִין תַּנּוּר וְכִירַיִם, אֲבָל מְכַבְּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not sweep out** anything that has fallen into **an oven or stove** that interferes with the baking, such as plaster. **But one may press down** and flatten any accumulated dust and ashes at the bottom of the oven, which might prevent it from lighting properly.

*Ve'ein gorfin et hatannur vehakiyrayim* — one may not sweep out an oven or stove. The seemingly simple act of removing ashes is, according to Rashi, actually a form of repair: he understands the case to involve a partially damaged oven, where some plaster or structural material has fallen in, and the sweeping constitutes a meaningful act of restoration. However, one is permitted to *mechabshin* — pat down the ashes against the walls and floor of the oven — thereby making the oven usable without crossing into the prohibited act of repair.

Rashi adds a practical dimension: if the ashes or debris are excessive, they will adhere to the bread or food being cooked, ruining it. Patting the ashes down prevents this without constituting a forbidden repair.

### Barrel Ovens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מַקִּיפִין שְׁתֵּי חָבִיּוֹת לִשְׁפּוֹת עֲלֵיהֶן אֶת הַקְּדֵרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not draw two barrels together** in order **to place a pot on them,** so that its contents will be cooked by a fire lit between the barrels.

*Ve'ein makkivin shtei chaviyos lispot aleihen kdeira* — one may not position two large barrels side by side to create an impromptu oven, with a fire burning between them and a pot resting across their tops. This is striking: no physical change is made to the barrels themselves. They are simply moved into proximity with one another. Yet the act is prohibited, and the Gemara will address the reason.

### Wood Support Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין סוֹמְכִין אֶת הַקְּדֵרָה בַּבְּקַעַת, וְכֵן בַּדֶּלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not prop a pot** that does not stand straight **with a piece of wood,** in order to prevent it from falling. **And similarly, with a door.**

*Ve'ein somchin es hakdeira bevika'at* — one may not prop up a cooking pot with a piece of wood or a beam. *Ve'chein badeles* — nor with a door, which apparently refers to a detached door being used as a support. Rashi explains that the underlying assumption of this *mishna* is that wood is categorically treated as fuel for fire; it is not considered a *kli* even if one wishes to use it for another purpose. Hence using a beam or a detached door to support a pot is prohibited.

### Stick & Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מַנְהִיגִין אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה בַּמַּקֵּל בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not lead an animal with a stick** in the public domain **on a Festival; and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, permits** it.

*Ve'ein manhigin es habeheima bemakel* — one may not lead an animal with a stick on *Yom Tov*. Whether this prohibition stems from *muktzeh*, from *uvdin dechol* (weekday activities), or from some other concern remains to be resolved in the Gemara. Rabbi Eliezer bar Shimon permits it. Rashi connects the final several cases of this *mishna* — the wood support and the stick used with animals — under the rubric of the assumption that wood has the status of fuel and nothing more.

## Oven Sweeping Analysis

### Vessel-Making Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מְתַקֵּן מָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the issue of breaking earthenware and cutting paper, the Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this prohibition? **Because one** is thereby **preparing a vessel** for use.

The Gemara explains the prohibition on breaking pottery and cutting papyrus for cooking: *mishum dekamatakin mana* — because one is fashioning a vessel, and this is forbidden even in the direct context of *ochel nefesh*. This is a significant ruling. Most of the earlier debate in the *masechta* concerned whether *machshirei ochel nefesh* — preparatory acts for food — are permitted or forbidden. But here, even acts that directly serve food preparation are prohibited when they involve *tikkun kli*, making or completing a vessel.

### Brick Incident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין גּוֹרְפִין תַּנּוּר וְכִירַיִם. תָּנֵי רַב חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: וְאִם אִי אֶפְשָׁר לֶאֱפוֹת אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן גּוֹרְפוֹ — מוּתָּר. דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא נְפַל לַהּ אֲרִיחָא בְּתַנּוּרָא בְּיוֹמָא טָבָא, אֲמַר לַהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא: חֲזַי, דַּאֲנָא רִפְתָּא מְעַלַּיְיתָא בָּעֵינָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ: טְוִי לִי בַּר אֲווֹזָא, וְאִזְדְּהַר מֵחֲרוֹכָא.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: **And one may not sweep out an oven or stove. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef taught before Rav Naḥman: And if it is not possible to bake unless one sweeps it out, it is permitted.** The Gemara relates an incident with regard **to the wife of Rabbi Ḥiyya: A part of a brick fell into** her **oven on a Festival,** preventing her from baking. **Rav Ḥiyya said to her: See,** you should know that **I want good-quality bread.** He thereby stated that it would be impossible unless she removed the brick, making it permissible for her to do so. Similarly, **Rava said to his attendant: Roast for me a duck** in an oven, **and be careful not to singe** it. He thereby implied that the attendant may remove all impediments from the oven in order to fulfill this requirement because otherwise it would not be possible to cook without singeing.

The same reasoning is applied to sweeping the oven: *ve'ein gorfin tanur vekirayim* — one may not sweep out the oven, again on the grounds of vessel repair. However, Rabbi Chiyya bar Yosef taught before Rav Nachman: *ve'im iy efshar le'ofos ela im kein gorfo, mutar* — if it is impossible to bake without sweeping the oven, it is permitted. This exception is telling. It implies that the prohibition on sweeping, of all the items in this *mishna*, is not at the level of a biblical act of *tikkun kli*. Indeed, sweeping ashes from an oven is the least convincing candidate for a full prohibit of vessel-making — far less so than breaking pottery or cutting papyrus. Some *Rishonim* maintain that all these prohibitions are biblical; others hold they are rabbinic in nature, their status being one of resembling vessel-completion rather than genuinely constituting it. The halakhic ambiguity is reflected in the Gemara's willingness to grant an exception for necessity in this particular case, while not extending the same exception to the other categories. The category of *efshar la'asos mibod yom* — the principle that acts which could have been performed before *Yom Tov* are forbidden even if they relate to *machshirei ochel nefesh* — is relevant here as well, though the Gemara grounds the prohibition primarily in *tikkun kli* rather than in that consideration.

### Roasting Goose

Rava said to his attendant: *tavi li bar avza* — roast a goose for me — *ve'izdaharei me'charucha* — and be careful that it does not come into contact with the charcoal, which would blacken and ruin it. This statement illustrates precisely the practical concern that underlies the oven-related rulings: contact with charcoal and ash ruins food. The patting-down of ashes, and in cases of necessity the sweeping of the oven, address this concern. Whether Rava's instruction yields a specific new *halacha* or merely contextualizes the practical stakes is not made explicit.

### Sealing Ovens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אָמַר לַן רַב אַחָא מֵהוּצָל, דְּמָר שָׁרְקִין לֵיהּ תַּנּוּרָא בְּיוֹמָא טָבָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנַן אַרַקְתָּא דִפְרָת סָמְכִינַן, וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי הוּא דְּצַיְּירֵיהּ מֵאֶתְמוֹל. אָמַר רָבִינָא: וְקִטְמָא — שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** In a related case, **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Rav Aḥa from Hutzal said to us that the master,** Rav Ashi, allows his attendants **to plaster** the mouth of **the oven for him on a Festival.** This was done in order to ensure that the roasted or cooked dish would be fully prepared. Why does this not constitute the prohibited labor of kneading on a Festival? **He said to him: We rely on the bank of the Euphrates** River. We avoid the labor of kneading by taking sufficiently kneaded mud from the riverbank. The Gemara comments: **And this applies only when one wrapped** or made some mark on the mud **the day before,** so that it not be *muktze*. **Ravina said: And** as for kneading with **ashes** for this purpose, it is **permitted,** since the labor of kneading does not apply to ashes.

Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Rav Acha from Hutzal told us that you seal your oven on *Yom Tov* — *de'mar sharik leh tanura be'yoma tava*. The practice of sealing an oven door with a plaster-like substance creates an airtight heat environment optimal for roasting. But is this not a form of *lisha*, kneading, when one prepares the sealing material? Is it permissible?

Rav Ashi replied: *anan arkata de'Frat samchinan* — we rely on the banks of the Euphrates. That is, he does not prepare plaster; he uses mud taken directly from the banks of the Euphrates, which is already in a workable state without any kneading or preparation. However, he adds: *ve'hani mili hu de'tzayirei me'etmol* — this is only permitted if one designated the mud before *Yom Tov*. One must have marked off a portion of the mud — creating a small indentation or boundary around it — on *Erev Yom Tov*, so that it is not *muktzeh* and is considered designated for use. Under those conditions, applying pre-formed riverside mud to seal the oven door involves no prohibited act.

Ravina adds: *u've'kitma shari* — using ashes would also be acceptable. Tosafot disagrees with Rashi's reading on this point: Rashi understands this as ashes mixed with water, which Tosafot would consider *lisha*. Tosafot instead explains that one simply packs dry ashes into the crevices around the oven door to seal it, which involves no kneading and is straightforwardly permitted.
