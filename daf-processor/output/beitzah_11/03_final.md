# Beitzah 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pestle as Cutting Board

### Intro: Pestle & Eli

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין נוֹטְלִים אֶת הָעֱלִי לְקַצֵּב עָלָיו בָּשָׂר, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Beit Shammai say: One may not take a large pestle** from a mortar, which is normally used for crushing wheat in the preparation of porridge, for any other purpose on a Festival, e.g., **to cut meat on it; and Beit Hillel permit** it.

The *mishnah* on 11a opens a new cluster of debates between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* concerning *Yom Tov*, shifting from the earlier discussions about birds to more general questions that arise from slaughtering and preparing meat. The first case involves the *eli* — the pestle used together with a mortar to grind spices or grain. The pestle is a heavy piece of wood, and because it is flat and broad enough to work as a surface, one might want to repurpose it on *Yom Tov* as an improvised cutting board for meat.

### Beit Shammai Position

*Beit Shammai* prohibits this: *ein notlin et ha-ali lakatzev alav basar* — one may not take the pestle and use it to chop meat upon it. The reason is that the *eli* is a *kli she'melachto l'issur* — a vessel whose primary function is associated with a forbidden activity. Even on *Yom Tov*, one may not grind spices in the ordinary manner using a mortar and pestle; doing so would constitute the prohibited labor of grinding. Since the vessel's normal use is thus forbidden, *Beit Shammai* holds that one may not use it even for a permitted purpose such as cutting meat.

### Beit Hillel Allows

*Beit Hillel* is more lenient: *u'Beit Hillel matirin* — they permit using the pestle as a cutting board. On *Yom Tov*, since one is using it to prepare food for the meal, it falls under the category of *l'tzorech ochel nefesh*, and *Beit Hillel* does not apply the full stringency of *muktzah* in such circumstances.

### Tosafos & Chisaron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין נוֹתְנִין אֶת הָעוֹר לִפְנֵי הַדּוֹרְסָן. וְלֹא יַגְבִּיהֶנּוּ, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יֵשׁ עִמּוֹ כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **Beit Shammai say: One may not place** an unprocessed **hide before those who** will **tread on it,** as this constitutes the prohibited labor of tanning on a Festival. **And one may not lift** the hide from its place, as it is considered *muktze*, **unless there is an olive-bulk of meat on it,** in which case it may be carried on account of its meat; **and Beit Hillel permit** it in both cases.

*Tosafos* finds *Beit Shammai*'s position here troubling. Looking back at earlier discussions, *Beit Shammai* was in certain respects notably lenient — as seen in the very first *mishnah* of the tractate. More broadly, we have not generally found that even *Beit Shammai* forbids using a *kli she'melachto l'issur* for a permitted purpose; the classic example of using a hammer to crack nuts is universally acknowledged as permissible. *Tosafos* therefore proposes that perhaps *Beit Shammai*'s concern here is not a standard *muktzah* argument but rather a *chisaron kis* concern — the pestle is a valuable item, and one fears it may be damaged if pressed into service as a cutting board. The concern about potential financial loss, rather than the vessel's categorization as *kli she'melachto l'issur*, may be driving the prohibition.

The *mishnah* continues with two further debates. *Beit Shammai* rules that one may not place the skin of a slaughtered animal before people who will tread upon it, and that the skin cannot be moved at all unless a *k'zayit* of meat remains attached to it — in which case the meat renders it movable. *Beit Hillel* permits both. The underlying logic of *Beit Hillel*'s leniency is the principle later termed *sof mishum techilasan* — the rabbis allowed the "end" in order to enable the "beginning." If a person who slaughters an animal on *Yom Tov* cannot take any steps to prevent the skin from rotting — since tanning (*ibud*) is forbidden — he may forgo the slaughter entirely, and there would be no meat for *Yom Tov*. To prevent this, *Beit Hillel* permits placing the skin where people will walk over it inadvertently. The people treading on it are themselves oblivious to what is occurring, making this something closer to a *grama* than a direct act of *ibud*. Moreover, it is also worth noting that having people walk on a skin laid at an entranceway does not look like tanning; it simply looks like a mat, so there is no visual impropriety.

The *mishnah* then turns to *trisim* — the large wooden shutters of shops. In those days, a shop's shutters, when removed, would fold down to form a counter or display surface for commerce. *Beit Shammai* forbids removing them on *Yom Tov*. *Beit Hillel* not only permits removing them but even permits replacing them afterward.

### Pestle vs. Block

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: וְשָׁוִין שֶׁאִם קִצֵּב עָלָיו בָּשָׂר — שֶׁאָסוּר לְטַלְטְלוֹ. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בֶּעֱלִי, אֲבָל בְּתָבְרָא גַּרְמֵי — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר. פְּשִׁיטָא, עֱלִי תְּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sage **taught** in a *baraita*: **And** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel **agree that if one** already **cut** the **meat** he needs for the Festival **on** the pestle, **it is prohibited to move** the pestle farther on the Festival. The reason is that the vessel is *muktze* as a utensil whose primary function is a prohibited use, and therefore it is permitted to handle it only when one requires it. **Abaye said:** This **dispute** applies specifically **in** the case of **a pestle; however, in** the case of a wooden anvil used **for breaking bones, everyone agrees** that **it is permitted.** The Gemara asks: This is **obvious; we learned** in the mishna: **A pestle.** Why would one think that an object not even mentioned in the mishna is prohibited?

The *Gemara* then addresses the scope of the opening debate. *Abaye* states: *machlokes b'ali* — the disagreement between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* concerns the pestle specifically. But regarding the *tavra garmi* — the butcher block, a heavy wooden surface used primarily for chopping meat and bones — *divrei hakol mutar*: everyone agrees it is permitted. This is intuitive, since the butcher block's normal function is cutting meat, which is a wholly permitted activity on *Yom Tov*.

The *Gemara* pushes back: *pshita* — this is obvious, since the *mishnah* explicitly says *ali*, pestle. Why does *Abaye* need to state this? The *Gemara* answers that without *Abaye*'s clarification, one might have supposed that *Beit Shammai* would forbid even the butcher block, and the *mishnah* chose the pestle specifically to illustrate the extent of *Beit Hillel*'s leniency — that even a *kli she'melachto l'issur* is permitted according to *Beit Hillel*. *Abaye* comes to teach us that this reading is wrong: *Beit Shammai* never entertained restricting the use of a standard butcher block.

### Abaye: New Block

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ תָּבְרָא גַּרְמֵי נָמֵי. וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי עֱלִי, לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחָן דְּבֵית הִלֵּל, דַּאֲפִילּוּ דָּבָר שֶׁמְּלַאכְתּוֹ לֶאֱסוֹר נָמֵי שָׁרוּ — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ תָּבְרָא גַּרְמֵי חַדְתִּי, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מִמְּלֵךְ וְלָא תָּבַר עֲלַהּ — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Abaye's statement is necessary, **lest you say: The same is true,** i.e., Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree, **even** with regard to a wooden anvil used **for breaking bones; and that which** the mishna specifically **teaches: A pestle,** is **to convey the far-reaching nature of** the opinion of **Beit Hillel,** that **they permitted** moving **even an object whose** primary **function** is **for a prohibited** use. Abaye therefore **teaches us** that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree with regard to a wooden anvil used for breaking bones. **Some say** a different version of the previous discussion. **Abaye said: It is necessary** to say **only: Even a new** wooden anvil used **for breaking bones** is also permitted. **Lest you say:** Perhaps **one will reconsider and not break** bones **on it,** but rather set it aside for a different purpose, Abaye therefore **teaches us** that this is not a concern.

A second version of *Abaye*'s statement refines the point further. *Abaye* says: *lo nitzrecha ela afilu tavra garmi chadta* — the novel teaching concerns even a brand-new butcher block. One might have thought that even *Beit Hillel* would be hesitant to allow a brand-new butcher block, since the owner might reconsider (*mimlach v'lo tavar alei*) — fearing to mar the pristine surface, he might decide not to use it after all, and his act of moving it will have served no purpose. The novelty is that even in this case, we are not concerned that he will change his mind.

This introduces a theme that recurs throughout this passage: the concern that a person will change his mind and thereby render a prior action purposeless. When it comes to the butcher block, however, the *Gemara* will explain why this concern does not apply. Once the animal has been slaughtered, it must be butchered — *keevan de-shachat l'tvira kaimei* — and so the likelihood that one will suddenly decide not to use the available butcher block is negligible.

### Rashi: Effort

What exactly is the problem if one does change one's mind? *Rashi* explains that the concern is *tirchah* — unnecessary effort. One moved the block, then changed one's mind, and now must return it: *amloch v'lo tavach v'ishtakach d'tarach b'chidi* — he deliberated, did not slaughter, and it turns out he exerted effort for nothing. This is admittedly a striking *Gemara*. The issue is not that moving the block constituted a violation of *muktzah* — it is not *muktzah* in the relevant sense. Rather, the concern is simply that moving a heavy object and then putting it back achieves nothing. How much *tirchah* is truly entailed by this is not entirely clear from the *Gemara*'s language, but *Rashi*'s reading takes it as a meaningful enough concern to warrant caution.

## Tools & Animals

### Baraita: Butcher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי לָא חָיְישִׁי לְאִמְּלוֹכֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מוֹלִיכִין טַבָּח וְסַכִּין אֵצֶל בְּהֵמָה, וְלֹא בְּהֵמָה אֵצֶל טַבָּח וְסַכִּין. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מוֹלִיכִין זֶה אֵצֶל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is that so? **Are Beit Shammai not concerned** about the possibility **that** one might **reconsider? But isn't it taught** (*Tosefta*, *Beitza* 1:13): **Beit Shammai say:** On a Festival, **one may not lead a butcher with a knife** in hand **to an animal** located far from him, so that he can slaughter it; **nor** may one lead **an animal to a butcher with a knife,** lest he reconsider, in which case he will have handled the knife unnecessarily, which is prohibited; **and Beit Hillel say: One may lead** them **from one to the other,** as they are not concerned about unnecessary use of the knife.

The *Gemara* now raises a challenge to the conclusion that *Beit Shammai* is not concerned about a person changing his mind. A *baraita* is cited in which *Beit Shammai* rules: *ein molichin tabach v'sakin eitzel behemah* — one may not bring the butcher and the knife to the animal, and likewise *v'lo behemah eitzel tabach v'sakin* — one may not bring the animal to the butcher and the knife. This seems to demonstrate that *Beit Shammai* very much worries about the possibility of a change of mind.

### Reshus HaRabim

The *Ran* suggests that the *baraita*'s prohibition may specifically target cases where the butcher and animal must be conveyed across a *reshus harabim* — a public domain. In that scenario, if the person ultimately changes his mind and does not slaughter the animal, it will emerge that he carried the knife across the public domain for no permissible purpose on *Yom Tov*. Whether the *baraita*'s concern is about this carrying violation or simply about the *tirchah* of a fruitless journey is not definitively resolved, but either way *Beit Shammai* clearly takes the possibility of a change of mind seriously.

### Beit Hillel Allows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מוֹלִיכִין תַּבְלִין וּמָדוֹךְ אֵצֶל מְדוֹכָה, וְלֹא מְדוֹכָה אֵצֶל תַּבְלִין וּמָדוֹךְ. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מוֹלִיכִין זֶה אֵצֶל זֶה!
>
> **Translation:** By the same reasoning, **Beit Shammai say: One may not bring spices or a pestle to a mortar, nor a mortar to spices and a pestle,** as he might change his mind and will have handled these utensils on the Festival for no purpose. **And Beit Hillel say: One may bring one to the other,** as there is no concern that he may reconsider.

*Beit Hillel* rules: *molichin zeh eitzel zeh* — one may bring the butcher to the animal and the animal to the butcher. *Beit Hillel* is unconcerned about a potential change of mind and permits all necessary preparations for the slaughter.

### Spices & Mortar

A parallel case in the same *baraita* concerns spices: *ein molichin tavlin u'madoch eitzel maducha* — *Beit Shammai* forbids bringing spices and the pestle to the mortar, and equally forbids bringing the mortar to the place where the spices and pestle are located. *Beit Hillel* again permits it: *molichin zeh eitzel zeh*. Whether *molichin* in this case implies traversing a *reshus harabim* or simply a significant *shlep* is similarly ambiguous.

### Gemara: Change Mind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּה?! בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּהֵמָה אָתֵי לְאִמְּלוֹכֵי, דְּאָמַר: נִשְׁבֹּק הַאי בְּהֵמָה כְּחוּשָׁה, וּמַיְיתֵינָא בְּהֵמָה אַחֲרִיתִי דְּשַׁמִּינָה מִינַּהּ. קְדֵרָה נָמֵי אָתֵי לְאִימְּלוֹכֵי, דְּאָמַר: נִשְׁבֹּק הַאי קְדֵרָה דְּבָעֲיָא תַּבְלִין, וּמַיְיתֵינָא אַחֲרִיתִי דְּלָא בָּעֲיָא תַּבְלִין. הָכָא מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? מִמְּלֵךְ וְלָא תָּבַר? כֵּיוָן דְּשַׁחְטַהּ — לִתְבִירָא קָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this: **How can** these cases **be compared? Granted,** in the case of **an animal, one** is liable to **come to reconsider, as** he might **say: Let us leave** aside **this animal,** as it is **thin, and we will bring a different animal, fatter than it.** With regard to **a pot, too, one** is liable **to come to reconsider, as** he might **say: Let us leave** aside **this pot** of cooked food, **as** it **requires spices** and would take great effort to prepare, **and I will bring a different one that does not require spices** and can be cooked as it is. However, **here,** with regard to a wooden anvil used for breaking bones, **what is there to say?** Will one **reconsider and not break** the bones? **Since he has slaughtered** an animal, it **stands** ready for its bones **to be broken,** as it cannot be eaten in any other way.

The *Gemara* then asks: if *Beit Shammai* is concerned about a change of mind in these cases, why did *Abaye* say that *Beit Shammai* concedes the butcher block is permitted even when new? The *Gemara* resolves the tension. *Bishlama behemah* — the concern about changing one's mind regarding an animal makes sense. There are obvious reasons a person might reconsider: *d'amar nishbak hai behemah kichusha u'maisina behemah achrisa d'shmena* — he might decide that this animal is too scrawny and opt to slaughter a fatter one instead. Regarding the spices, there is similarly good reason to change one's mind: *nishbak hai kedeirah d'ba'ya tavlin u'maisina achrisa d'lo ba'ya tavlin* — he might abandon the recipe that requires these spices and make a different dish that requires no grinding at all. In both cases the prospect of changing one's mind is realistic and its consequences significant.

### Animals vs. Vessels

By contrast, in the case of the butcher block, the question is whether a person who has already slaughtered an animal will truly decide not to use the available butcher block. *Keevan de-shachat l'tvira kaimei* — once the animal is slaughtered, it requires butchering; there is no option of simply not doing it. One may not be ready to mar a brand-new block, but one will have to chop the meat on something. The likelihood of a meaningful change of mind is negligible, and therefore even *Beit Shammai* does not restrict the use of a butcher block. The *tirchah* concern is real in the animal and spice cases but not here.

## Salting & Preservation

### Agreement: After Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: וְשָׁוִין שֶׁמּוֹלְחִין עָלָיו בָּשָׂר לְצָלִי. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְצָלִי, אֲבָל לִקְדֵרָה — לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sage **taught** (*Tosefta*, *Beitza* 1:13): **And** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel **agree that one may salt meat for roasting on** this hide, and there is no concern that some of the salt will fall on the hide, which would be similar to tanning the hide by salting. **Abaye said: They taught** that one may salt meat **only for roasting,** in which case it is not salted a great deal. **However,** in the case of meat **for a pot,** i.e., for cooking, the Sages did **not** say that one may salt it on the hide, as meat must be well-salted on all sides before cooking, and a large amount of salt will inevitably spill onto the hide.

A *tanna* teaches that despite their disagreement about using the pestle, *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* agree on one point: *she'im katzev alav basar she'asur l'taltlo* — once one has finished chopping meat on the pestle, it becomes forbidden to move it. Even according to *Beit Hillel*, whose leniency was predicated on an active, legitimate use, once that use is complete the pestle reverts to *muktzah* status. Whether this is because it is a *kli she'melachto l'issur*, or because of the *chisaron kis* rationale advanced by *Tosafos*, or perhaps even because, after being used for raw meat, the object becomes *muktzah machmas miyus*, the conclusion is the same: a *Beit Hillel* leniency tied to active use lapses the moment that use ends.

The *mishnah* then records a further point of agreement: *v'shavin shemolchin alav basar l'tzli* — both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* agree that one may salt the meat while it is still attached to the skin, for the purpose of roasting. This is permitted even though some of the salt inevitably contacts and partially affects the skin, which might otherwise constitute *ibud* — tanning. Several considerations together explain why this is tolerated. First, the salt is applied for the sake of roasting the meat, not for the sake of the skin. Second, the amount of salt used for roasting is minimal — *l'tzli* — far less than the quantity used when salting meat *l'kedeira* (for cooking in a pot, to draw out blood). Third, whatever effect the incidental salt has on the skin may not rise to the halachic threshold of *ibud*, since *ibud* is a process, not a single drop of salt striking a surface. Even so, the permission is somewhat surprising, since the person does intend — at least indirectly — that the skin benefit from the salting.

### Salt with Skin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא — לְצָלִי תְּנַן! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְצָלִי, כְּעֵין קְדֵרָה — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: It is **obvious** the one may not salt meat **for** cooking in a pot, as **we** explicitly **learned** in the *Tosefta* just cited: **For roasting,** and not for cooking. The Gemara answers: **This** comes to **teach us that even** the permission to salt meat **for roasting** applies only if one does so in the usual manner. However, if one salts it **in a manner of** meat salted to be cooked in **a pot,** which requires more salt than is necessary for roasting, it is **prohibited.**

*Abaye* sharpens this ruling: *lo shanu ela l'tzli* — the permission is specifically and only for the minimal amount of salt appropriate for roasting.

The *Gemara* objects: *pshita* — this is obvious, since the *mishnah* itself says *l'tzli*. The *Gemara* answers that the novel teaching is this: *d'afilu l'tzli ke'ein kedeira assur* — even if one is technically salting for roasting, if the quantity of salt applied approaches the amount typically used *l'kedeira* — because, say, one happens to like heavily salted meat — that too is forbidden. The permission is truly limited to the barest minimum; any excessive quantity, regardless of stated intent, is prohibited.

### Fats Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מוֹלְחִין אֶת הַחֲלָבִים וְאֵין מְהַפְּכִין בָּהֶן. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אֲמַרוּ: שׁוֹטְחָן בָּרוּחַ עַל גַּבֵּי יְתֵדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** On a Festival, **one may not salt the fats** of an animal, which is done so that they will not decompose and emit a foul odor. This is true even if the animal was slaughtered on the Festival. **And one may not turn them over.** The fats are unfit for use on the Festival, and therefore they are *muktze*. **They said in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua: One may spread** the fats **out in the wind on pegs** to prevent them from decaying.

The *baraita* then addresses the *chalabim* — the fats of the slaughtered animal. *Ein molchin es hachallavin* — one may not salt the fats, and *v'ein mehapchim bahem* — one may not even turn them over. The *Ran* explains that these fats are not ordinary food: they are not what most people would typically eat, and because they occupy an ambiguous position between food and non-food, heavily salting or manipulating them could constitute *ibud* in a way that salting normal meat would not. The principle *ein ibud be'ochlin* — there is no *ibud* by food — does not fully apply here. Indeed, *Rashi* treats the prohibition of *ibud* by these fats as a biblical-level concern. The prohibition against turning them over seems to reflect a similar logic, treating excessive handling as falling outside the latitude normally granted for food.

*Rabbi Yehoshua* offers a compromise: *shotchan baruach al gabi yatedot* — one may spread the fats out in the wind, hanging them on pegs. This accomplishes something toward preservation without crossing into the forbidden territory of salting or repeated manipulation.

### Rule or Not

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ — אִצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא יָחִיד וְרַבִּים הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּים, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן הֲלָכָה כְּיָחִיד. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין הֲלָכָה — פְּשִׁיטָא, יָחִיד וְרַבִּים הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּים! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מִסְתַּבַּר טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ דְּאִי לָא שָׁרֵית לֵיהּ מִמְּנַע וְלָא שָׁחֵיט, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mattana said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua. Some say** that **Rav Mattana said: The *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua.** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who said** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** this statement **is necessary.** Otherwise, **it might enter your mind to say** that since this is a dispute between **an individual and** the **many,** one should apply the principal that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of the **many.** Rav Mattana therefore **teaches us** that, in this case, the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **the individual.** **However, according to the one who said** that the ***halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua, this is **obvious.** In a case involving **an individual and** the **many, the *halakha* is in accordance with** the **many.** The Gemara answers: This ruling is nevertheless necessary, **lest you say: Rabbi Yehoshua's opinion is** more **reasonable, for if you do not permit him** to air out the fats, **he will refrain and not slaughter** an animal at all. Rav Mattana therefore **teaches us** that this factor is not taken into consideration.

*Rav Masna* is cited in two opposing traditions. One version states *halachah k'Rabbi Yehoshua* — we rule in accordance with *Rabbi Yehoshua*, following the lenient position. The reasoning: since *Rabbi Yehoshua* is a *da'as yachid* — a single opinion against the majority who would permit nothing — one would normally assume the ruling follows the majority. *Rav Masna* must therefore go out of his way to specify that we follow the lone opinion.

A second version states *ein halachah k'Rabbi Yehoshua* — we do not rule like him. Here too the explicit statement is necessary. One might think that since *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s reasoning has intrinsic force — *de'ee lo sharis leh mimmna v'lo shachat*, if you give the person no latitude at all he will simply forgo slaughtering the animal — we might follow his view despite his minority status. *Rav Masna* comes to teach that even so, the *halachah* does not follow him.

### Skin vs. Fats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵעוֹר לִפְנֵי הַדּוֹרְסָן? הָתָם לָא מוֹכְחָא מִלְּתָא, מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲזֵי לְמִזְגֵּא עֲלֵיהּ. הָכָא אָתֵי לְמֵימַר: מַאי טַעְמָא שָׁרוּ לִי רַבָּנַן — כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִסְרַח. מָה לִי לְמִשְׁטְחִינְהוּ, מָה לִי לְמִמְלְחִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in **what** way **is** this case **different from** placing **a hide before those who will tread on it,** which Beit Hillel, whose ruling is accepted as *halakha*, permit for the very reason that, if one is not allowed to do so, he will refrain from slaughtering animals? The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of spreading out the hide, **the matter is not** so **evident** that it is spread out for tanning **because** in its current state, **it is fit to recline on,** and therefore it can be said that one placed it for this purpose. However, **here,** with regard to fats, he himself might **come to say: What is the reason** that **the Sages permitted** it **to me? So that it will not emit a foul odor.** If so, **what** is the difference **to me** if I **spread them out,** and **what** is the difference **to me** if I **salt them?** This reasoning will lead one to salt hides, which is a prohibited labor.

The *Gemara* then asks a probing question: if we give latitude for the skin — allowing it to be placed before people who will tread on it, a clear if indirect form of preservation — why do we not similarly give latitude for the fats?

The *Gemara* answers: *hasam lo mochecha milsa* — in the case of the skin, the activity is not visually self-evident. *Mishum d'chazi l'migzar u'lmiskal alei* — a skin placed at an entrance could plausibly be a mat; it is something people could reasonably sit or lie on. A passerby does not look at it and think, "He is tanning leather on *Yom Tov*." But fats hanging on a clothesline present a different picture entirely. If one may hang the fats to prevent them from spoiling, a person will naturally reason: *mai ta'ama sharu lee rabbanan ki heichi d'lo linsrach* — the rabbis only allowed me to hang them so they will not rot. Once that logic is internalized, the next step almost follows automatically: *ma li l'mishtichinhu u'ma li l'mimlechanhu* — if the goal is preservation, what difference does it make whether I hang them or simply salt them? And salting for preservation would be forbidden *ibud*. The visual transparency of hanging fats signals too clearly that one is engaged in preservation, making it far more likely that the person will slip into the forbidden activity. By contrast, the ambiguity of a skin mat provides a natural buffer against that logical slide.

## Multiple Pieces

### Rav Yehuda: Salting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מוֹלֵחַ אָדָם כַּמָּה חֲתִיכוֹת בָּשָׂר בְּבַת אַחַת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ אֶלָּא לַחֲתִיכָה אַחַת. רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה מַעֲרִים וּמָלַח גַּרְמָא גַּרְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: A person may salt** on a Festival **several pieces of meat at one time, although he requires** only **one piece,** as it is all one act of salting. **Rav Adda bar Ahava** would **employ artifice and salt bone** by **bone.** After salting one bone, he would say: I prefer this one instead, and would thereby salt all the meat in his possession.

*Rav Yehuda* teaches in the name of *Shmuel*: *molei'ach adam kama chatichos basar b'vas achas af al pi she'eino tzarich ela l'chatichah achas* — a person may salt many pieces of meat at once on *Yom Tov*, even if he ultimately needs only a single piece. The breadth of this allowance reflects the desire to give people genuine latitude in preparing meat for the *Yom Tov* meal.

### Rav Adda: One Piece

*Rav Adda bar Abba* disagrees: *ma'arimin umalchin garma garma* — one must employ a form of *ha'aramah*, a permitted circumvention, and salt only piece by piece. The procedure is: take a piece of meat, declare it one's intended piece for *Yom Tov*, salt it; then reconsider, set it aside, take the next piece, and repeat. One does not receive a blanket license to salt ten pieces simultaneously on the stated premise that one of them will be eaten.

### Ha'aramah Concept

The use of *ha'aramah* — deliberate circumvention of a halachic boundary — raises a principled question. Is it not troubling to encourage finding loopholes in halacha? This question looms large in many contexts, perhaps most visibly in the sale of *chametz* before *Pesach*, which many *poskim* historically resisted as a manipulation of the system. Yet the *Gemara* here not only acknowledges *ha'aramah* but prescribes it. Other fundamental institutions — the *eruv* being a notable example — have a similar character.

### Benefits & Boundaries

The deeper value of the *ha'aramah* in this case is instructive. Even as a person moves from piece to piece with the knowledge that he is working around the system, each individual act of salting requires him to articulate — at least formally — that this piece is being prepared for the *Yom Tov* meal. Each iteration reinforces the very parameter being stretched: that salting on *Yom Tov* is permissible only in the context of *Yom Tov* eating. A blanket license to salt ten pieces at once conveys a different message — it begins to resemble salting for general preservation, which would dissolve the boundary altogether. The *ha'aramah*, by contrast, keeps the boundary conceptually present even while working around it.

*Rashi*'s understanding of the underlying prohibition is also noteworthy. He does not frame the problem as *ibud* — there is no concern about tanning when dealing with ordinary meat. Rather, the issue is *tirchah*: salting a large quantity of meat for stock that will not be eaten on *Yom Tov* constitutes unnecessary effort expended for the sake of post-*Yom Tov* benefit. This is distinct from a formal *hachana* violation, though it occupies similar conceptual territory. Preventing spoilage, one might argue, is not *hachana* for the weekday but simply prudent preservation. Yet *Rashi* sees the problem as the sheer effort involved when directed primarily at future use rather than at *simchas Yom Tov*.

## Store Shutters

### What Are Trisim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מְסַלְּקִין אֶת הַתְּרִיסִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין אַף לְהַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Beit Shammai say: One may not remove** the **shutters [*terisin*]** of a store **on a Festival,** due to the prohibition against building and demolishing. **And Beit Hillel permit** one not only to open the shutters, but **even to replace** them.

The next *mishnah* takes up the *trisim* — the shutters of stores. In the era of the *Gemara*, shops opened directly onto the street, and the shutters, when removed, folded down to create a counter or display for merchandise. The ability to open one's store on *Yom Tov* was significant because *Chazal* gave broad latitude for *Yom Tov* commerce in food: a person who needed an item for the *Yom Tov* meal could take it from a Jewish shopkeeper, with payment deferred until after the holiday. The *Torah*'s own allowance — *ach asher yeachel lechol nefesh* — set the precedent for permitting cooking and fire on *Yom Tov*, and the rabbis extended this spirit to give people meaningful access to food preparation. Opening a store involves, however, the physical act of removing and potentially replacing large wooden shutters.

### Shammai vs. Hillel

*Beit Shammai* rules: *ein mesalkin es hatrisin b'Yom Tov* — one may not remove the shutters on *Yom Tov*. *Beit Hillel* is not only lenient about removing them but explicitly permits replacing them afterward: *matirin af lehachazir*. The logic of *Beit Hillel*'s position on replacement will be developed in the *Gemara*.

### Sof Mishum Techila

*Beit Hillel* allows the replacement precisely because if replacement were forbidden, people would refrain from opening the store at all — the shutters would be ruined or lost if left unprotected in the street. By permitting the replacement (*sof*), the rabbis made the initial removal (*techila*) viable. This is the principle *Ulla* will shortly formulate explicitly.

## Three Permissions

### Ulla's Three Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר עוּלָּא: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים הִתִּירוּ סוֹפָן מִשּׁוּם תְּחִלָּתָן, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: עוֹר לִפְנֵי הַדּוֹרְסָן, וּתְרִיסֵי חֲנוּיוֹת, וַחֲזָרַת רְטִיָּה בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Ulla said:** With regard to **three matters,** the Sages **permitted** an action **whose result** is undesirable **in order** to encourage a desirable **initial** action. **And these are** the three matters: First, they permitted spreading out the **hide** of an animal slaughtered on a Festival **before those** who will **tread on it,** a stage in its tanning. This was permitted because the Sages wish to encourage slaughtering the animal to enable celebration on the Festival. **And** second, the Sages permitted the replacement of **shutters of shops** on a Festival, so that storeowners could supply the Festival requirements for those in need. **And** the third permitted action is **the replacement of a bandage in the Temple.** If a priest had an injury on his hand, he would have to remove the bandage while performing the Temple service, as it is prohibited for any item to interpose between his hand and whatever he must handle as part of the rite. After concluding his Temple service, he was allowed to replace the bandage on Shabbat, despite the fact that this is ordinarily prohibited, so as not to discourage him from engaging in Temple service.

*Ulla* formulates a general principle: *shlosha devarim hitiru sofan mishum techilasan* — there are three things the rabbis permitted at the end in order to enable the beginning. These three cases share a structural logic: in each, an act that would otherwise be restricted is permitted because prohibiting it would discourage a desirable prior act.

### Skin Case

The first case is *oro lifnei hadorsan* — placing the skin before people who will tread on it. The "end" that is permitted is the semi-preservation of the skin through being walked on; the "beginning" it enables is the willingness to slaughter an animal on *Yom Tov*. Without this latitude, the financial loss of a ruined skin would deter slaughter.

### Shutters Case

The second case is *trisei chanuyos* — the shutters of stores. The "end" permitted is replacing the shutters after the day's commerce; the "beginning" it enables is the removal of the shutters to open the store in the first place.

### Bandage Case

The third case is *chazaras retzu'ah bemikdash* — replacing a bandage in the *Beit Hamikdash*. A *kohen* who has a wound covered by a bandage cannot perform the *avodah* with the bandage in place, since it constitutes a *chatzitza*. On *Shabbat*, removing the bandage to perform the service and then replacing it would normally raise concern about *memari'ach* — smoothing a salve over a wound. The rabbis permitted replacing the bandage afterward in order to enable the *kohen* to remove it in the first place and perform the *avodah*. Without the assurance that he could replace the bandage, he would refuse to take it off.

## Basis of Allowance

### Skin: Fundamental?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹר לִפְנֵי הַדּוֹרְסָן, תְּנֵינָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּבֵית הִלֵּל מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲזֵי לְמִזְגֵּא עֲלַיְיהוּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב נָמֵי — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן הִתִּירוּ סוֹפָן מִשּׁוּם תְּחִלָּתָן: דְּיוֹם טוֹב — אִין, דְּעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Ulla's statement, the Gemara asks: **We** already **learned** the *halakha* that one may spread out an animal's **hide before those** who will **tread on it.** Why did Ulla find it necessary to restate an explicit teaching of a mishna? The Gemara explains: **Lest you say** that **the reason of Beit Hillel** is **because** the hide **is fit for reclining on it, and** therefore **even** if the animal was slaughtered **on the eve of the Festival,** it would **also** be permitted to spread out its hide on the Festival. Ulla therefore **teaches us** that the reason for the leniency is that the Sages **permitted** an action **whose result** was undesirable **in order** to encourage a desirable **initial** action. Consequently, in the case of an animal slaughtered **on a Festival, yes,** this *halakha* applies; but with regard to one that was slaughtered **on the eve of a Festival, no,** one may not spread out its hide.

The *Gemara* now interrogates each of *Ulla*'s three cases to determine whether the leniency is truly a special allowance — granted only to enable the prior act — or whether it reflects a more fundamental position that the activity is simply not problematic.

Regarding the skin: one might have argued that *Beit Hillel* permits placing the skin before the treaders not as a special concession, but because the skin is genuinely fit to be used as a mat and is therefore not *muktzah* in any meaningful sense — *mishum d'chazi l'mizga aleihu*. If that were the reason, the permission would apply even if the animal was slaughtered on *erev Yom Tov* rather than on *Yom Tov* itself. The *Gemara* establishes that this is not the case. The permission is specifically *sof mishum techilasan*: *Yom Tov in, erev Yom Tov lo* — it applies only when the animal was slaughtered on *Yom Tov* itself, when the encouragement of *shechita* is at stake. Had the animal been slaughtered before *Yom Tov*, there was no obstacle to processing the skin beforehand, and no reason to extend a special *Yom Tov* leniency.

### Shutters: Fundamental?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרִיסִי חֲנוּיוֹת נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא [וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין אַף לְהַחְזִיר]! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּבֵית הִלֵּל מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין בִּנְיָן בְּכֵלִים וְאֵין סְתִירָה בְּכֵלִים, וַאֲפִילּוּ דְּבָתִּים נָמֵי — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן הִתִּירוּ סוֹפָן מִשּׁוּם תְּחִלָּתָן: דַּחֲנוּיוֹת — אִין, דְּבָתִּים — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **We** already **learned** the *halakha* of the **shutters of shops as well,** as the mishna states that **Beit Hillel permit** one **even to replace them.** The Gemara explains: This, too, is necessary. **Lest you say: Beit Hillel's reason** for being lenient **is that there is no** prohibition of **building** with regard **to vessels and no** prohibition of **dismantling** with regard **to vessels.** Since these shops are not attached to the ground, they are vessels rather than houses, and it is therefore permitted to replace their shutters; **and** as a result, the dismantlement and replacement of shutters of large vessels, **even of** those found in **houses,** should **also** be permitted. To counter this logic, Ulla therefore **teaches us** that the reason the Sages allowed the replacement of shutters of shops on a Festival is because they **permitted** an action **whose result** is undesirable **in order** to encourage a desirable **initial** action. Consequently, in the case of the shutters **of shops, yes,** they permitted their replacement; in the case of those **of houses, no,** they did not allow it.

Regarding the shutters: one might have argued that *Beit Hillel* permits both removing and replacing the shutters not as a concession, but because there is fundamentally *ein binyan bikeilim v'ein stirah bikeilim* — no biblical prohibition of *binyan* (construction) or *stirah* (deconstruction) when the object in question is a vessel rather than a fixed structure. The general principle is that *binyan* applies to buildings and permanent structures; assembling or disassembling a movable vessel does not constitute *binyan* at a Torah level. If this were *Beit Hillel*'s reasoning, one might extend the allowance to household vessels generally — for example, replacing a table leg that had fallen off, or refixing a panel that had come loose from a cabinet.

The *Gemara* rejects this expansion. The permission is *sof mishum techilasan*, limited to the specific context of store shutters: *chanuyos in, debattim lo* — store shutters yes, household vessels no. *Tosafos* and the *Rashba* both address the logical implication: it cannot be that *Beit Hillel* rules *yeish binyan bikeilim* — that there is a Torah-level prohibition — since *Beit Hillel* would never permit an actual *melachah*. Rather, they explain that the operative ruling is indeed *ein binyan bikeilim* at the Torah level, but rabbinically there remains a concern, and that rabbinic restriction is what *Beit Hillel* waives in the case of store shutters alone, for the sake of *Yom Tov* commerce. For ordinary household vessels, the rabbinic restriction remains in place. This distinction also bears on the broader question of the relationship between *sof mishum techilasan* and other halachic contexts — such as the well-known ruling permitting doctors to drive home after being called to a hospital on *Shabbat* — where the question of whether the principle operates only for rabbinic prohibitions is critically important.

### Bandage: Service Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲזָרַת רְטִיָּה בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: מַחְזִירִין רְטִיָּה בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ אֲבָל לֹא בַּמְּדִינָה! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא טַעְמָא מַאי — מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין שְׁבוּת בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, אֲפִילּוּ כֹּהֵן דְּלָאו בַּר עֲבוֹדָה הוּא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן הִתִּירוּ סוֹפָן מִשּׁוּם תְּחִלָּתָן: דְּבַר עֲבוֹדָה — אִין, דְּלָאו בַּר עֲבוֹדָה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **We** already **learned** the *halakha* of **the replacement of a bandage in the Temple as well: One may replace a bandage in the Temple but not in** the rest of **the country.** The Gemara explains that this *halakha* is necessary. **Lest you say: What is the reason** that a bandage may be replaced? It is **because rabbinic decrees prohibiting labor** do **not** apply **in the Temple.** Since the prohibition against applying a bandage is by rabbinic law, this leniency should apply to all who are in the Temple, **even** to **a priest who is not a candidate** to perform the Temple **service.** Ulla **teaches us** that this is not the case; rather, it is an instance where the Sages **permitted a result for the sake of an initial action: If one is a candidate for service, yes,** he may replace his bandage; **if one is not a candidate for service, no,** he may not replace his bandage.

Regarding the bandage in the *Mikdash*: one might have argued that the reason for the allowance is the general principle *ein shevus ba-mikdash* — rabbinic restrictions are broadly set aside within the Temple precincts. If that were the basis, then even a *kohen ba'al mum* — a priest with a physical disqualification who is not currently performing the *avodah* — would be permitted to replace his bandage in the *Mikdash* precincts. The *Gemara* rejects this reading. The principle of *ein shevus ba-mikdash* is not an unlimited waiver of all rabbinic prohibitions for everyone present in the *Mikdash*. Rather, the allowance here is again *sof mishum techilasan*: *de-bar avodah in, de-lav bar avodah lo* — only a *kohen* who is actively performing the *avodah* may replace the bandage, and only because the allowance is granted specifically to enable him to remove it for the *avodah*. A *kohen* who is not performing the service has no such license.

### Wine & Dough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַחֲבָא אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אַף הַפּוֹתֵחַ חָבִיתוֹ, וּמַתְחִיל בְּעִיסָּתוֹ עַל גַּב הָרֶגֶל — וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר יִגְמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Raḥava** said that **Rabbi Yehuda said:** There is **also** one other matter, i.e., another instance where the Sages permitted an action whose result was undesirable in order to encourage a desirable initial action. This concerns a *ḥaver*, a member of a group that is meticulous with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity, **who opens his barrel** of wine or prepares **and begins** to sell **his dough** to pilgrims **for the sake of the Festival. And** this is **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who said: He may finish** selling all the bread made from that dough and all the wine in the barrel.

The *Gemara* then asks why *Ulla* did not include a fourth case in his list — the teaching of *Rachaba* in the name of *Rebbe Yehuda*, which states that one who opens a barrel of wine or begins a loaf of dough during the *regel*, in circumstances where an *am ha'aretz* then touches it, retains the leniency of treating that wine or dough as *tahor* even after the *regel* ends — *af al gav delo hischil nami* being the rejected extension. The answer is that *Ulla* does not enumerate cases that are subjects of an unresolved debate among *tannaim*, and this fourth case is precisely such a debate: *Rebbe Yehuda* permits completing the use of the opened wine or dough after the *regel*, while the *Chachamim* do not. By contrast, the three cases *Ulla* chose involve debates only between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* — and *Beit Shammai bemakom Beit Hillel einah mishna*: in the era of the *Amoraim*, *Beit Shammai*'s dissenting position is not treated as a live halachic alternative in the way a *tannaic* dispute between later authorities would be.

## Rabbi Shimon Ben Elazar

### Agree: Removal OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כִי הַאי תַּנָּא דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: מוֹדִים בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל שֶׁמְּסַלְּקִין אֶת הַתְּרִיסִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב, לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא לְהַחֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar said: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that one may remove shutters on a Festival. They disagree only** as to whether it is permitted **to replace** them, as well.

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* in which *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* offers a reformulation of the shutter debate that makes the structure of the argument more transparent. *Modi'im Beit Shammai ve'Beit Hillel shemesalkin et hatrisin b'Yom Tov* — both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* actually agree that removing the shutters is permitted. This is analogous to the skin case, where *Beit Shammai* concedes that slaughtering the animal is permitted; the dispute concerns only what comes after.

### Debate: Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מַחְזִירִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף מַחְזִירִין.
>
> **Translation:** as **Beit Shammai say: One may not replace** them, **and Beit Hillel say: One may even replace** them.

*Lo nechelku ela lehachzir* — they disagree only about replacing the shutters. *Beit Shammai omrim ein machzirin* — *Beit Shammai* holds the shutters may not be replaced. *Beit Hillel omrim af machzirin* — *Beit Hillel* permits replacement as well. This formulation is more parallel to the other *sof mishum techilasan* cases and makes the logic of *Beit Hillel*'s allowance cleaner: precisely because replacement is permitted, removal becomes viable.

### Condition: Hinge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן צִיר, אֲבָל אֵין לָהֶן צִיר — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And in what** case **is this statement said? When** these shutters **have a hinge** that can be inserted into a slot in the side of the vessel. **However,** if **they do not have a hinge, everyone agrees** that **it is permitted,** as this is merely replacement of a board, and it is not similar to building.

*Bamei devarim amurim* — under what conditions does this debate apply? *Besheyesh lahem tzir* — when the shutters have a hinge. A hinge that fits into a socket, or a peg that must be inserted into a hole, involves a more substantial act of assembly, and it is this type of construction that constitutes the subject of the debate.

### No Hinge: All OK

*Aval ein lahem tzir, divrei hakol mutar* — if there is no hinge at all, everyone agrees it is permitted. When the shutters can be removed and replaced without any insertion of pins, pegs, or hinges into sockets — simply by fitting pieces together — there is no meaningful act of *binyan* even at the rabbinic level, and no debate is necessary.

## Hinge Analysis

### Side Hinge Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּשֶׁאֵין לָהֶן צִיר, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לָהֶן צִיר — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן צִיר מִן הַצַּד — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this claim: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **In what** case **is this statement said?** What is the situation in which Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree? They disagree **when** the shutters **do not have a hinge; however, if they have a hinge, everyone agrees** that it is **prohibited. Abaye said** that the two sources can be reconciled: **When they have a hinge on the side, everyone agrees** that it is **prohibited,** as the placement of a hinge in the side is a complicated endeavor that resembles building.

*Abaye* further refines the analysis by distinguishing among different types of hinges. *Besheyesh lahem tzir min hatzad* — if the hinge is on the side, meaning it is the kind of fitting in which a pin or peg goes into a hole at the edge, everyone agrees this is forbidden. This is the strongest form of assembly, most analogous to construction, and even *Beit Hillel* would not waive the concern here.

### No Hinge OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לָהֶן צִיר כׇּל עִיקָּר — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** If **they have no hinge at all, everyone agrees** that **it is permitted,** as it is considered merely the replacement of a board.

*Ein lahem tzir kol ikkar* — when there is literally no hinge of any kind, and the pieces simply fit tightly together by design, everyone agrees this is permitted. There is no rabbinic concern of *binyan* when no actual insertion or fitting of a pin into a socket occurs.

### Middle Hinge Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן צִיר בָּאֶמְצַע. מָר סָבַר: גָּזְרִינַן צִיר בָּאֶמְצַע אַטּוּ צִיר מִן הַצַּד, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא גָּזְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **When they disagree,** it is with regard to a case **where they have a hinge in the middle** rather than on the side. One **Sage,** Beit Shammai, **holds** that **we issue a decree** and prohibit **a hinge in the middle due to a hinge on the side; and** one **Sage,** Beit Hillel, **holds** that **we do not issue** this **decree.** Since placing a hinge of this type does not constitute the prohibited labor of building, it is permitted.

*Ki pligi* — the actual debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* concerns the intermediate case: *besheyesh lahem tzir ba'emtza* — when the hinge is in the middle. This might be imagined as a horizontal peg running across the center of the shutter panel rather than inserted into a side socket — a less pronounced form of fitting, not as tight or permanent as the side hinge but more involved than mere contact.

### Abaye: Two Views

*Abaye* explains that the two positions reflect different approaches to rabbinic decree. *Mar savar gazrinan tzir ba'emtza atu tzir min hatzad* — one view holds that we must prohibit the middle hinge as well, lest people confuse it with the forbidden side hinge and come to reassemble that more serious type as well. The other view — *u'mar savar lo gazrinan* — holds that no such decree is needed, and in fact the middle-hinge shutters should be permitted for the sake of *simchat Yom Tov*: people should be able to fully open and restore their stores on the holiday without restriction. Whether the underlying concern in the forbidden cases rises to a *de'oraita* prohibition of *binyan* or remains solely a rabbinic issue — *ein binyan bikeilim* being the Torah-level ruling — remains a point of ambiguity, though the analysis of *Tosafos* and the *Rashba* suggests the concern operative in these debates is rabbinic throughout, with *Beit Hillel* waiving it specifically in the context of store commerce on *Yom Tov*.
