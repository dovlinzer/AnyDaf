# Shabbat 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shammai & Hillel vs. Beis

### Hand Impurity Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיָדַיִם תַּלְמִידֵי שַׁמַּאי וְהִלֵּל גְּזוּר? שַׁמַּאי וְהִלֵּל גְּזוּר! דְּתַנְיָא: יוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה וְיוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹחָנָן אִישׁ יְרוּשָׁלַיִם גְּזַרוּ טוּמְאָה עַל אֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים וְעַל כְּלֵי זְכוּכִית. שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח תִּיקֵּן כְּתוּבָּה לָאִשָּׁה, וְגָזַר טוּמְאָה עַל כְּלֵי מַתָּכוֹת. שַׁמַּאי וְהִלֵּל גָּזְרוּ טוּמְאָה עַל הַיָּדַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** Among the list of items in the mishna with regard to which the disciples of Shammai and Hillel instituted decrees, were the hands of any person who did not purify himself for the sake of purity of *teruma*. If he came into contact with *teruma*, the Sages decreed it impure. The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **hands,** was it **the disciples of Shammai and Hillel** who **issued the decree** of impurity? **Shammai and Hillel** themselves **issued the decree. As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Yosei ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida and Yosei ben Yoḥanan of Jerusalem decreed impurity on the land of the nations,** that the land outside Eretz Yisrael transmits impurity; **and** they decreed impurity **on glass vessels,** even though glass is not listed in the Torah among the vessels that can become impure. **Shimon ben Shataḥ instituted** the formula of **a woman's marriage contract and** also **decreed** special **impurity on metal vessels. Shammai and Hillel decreed impurity on the hands.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא שַׁמַּאי וְסִיעָתוֹ וְהִלֵּל וְסִיעָתוֹ, וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁמֹנָה עָשָׂר דָּבָר גָּזְרוּ, וּבִשְׁמֹנָה עָשָׂר נֶחְלְקוּ. וְאִילּוּ הִלֵּל וְשַׁמַּאי לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת נֶחְלְקוּ וְתוּ לָא. וְכִי תֵימָא: אֲתוֹ אִינְהוּ גְּזוּר לִתְלוֹת, וַאֲתוֹ תַּלְמִידַיְיהוּ וּגְזַרוּ לִשְׂרוֹף. וְהָאָמַר אִילְפָא: יָדַיִם תְּחִלַּת גְּזֵירָתָן לִשְׂרֵיפָה! אֶלָּא אֲתוֹ אִינְהוּ גְּזוּר וְלָא קַבִּלוּ מִינַּיְיהוּ, וַאֲתוֹ תַּלְמִידַיְיהוּ גְּזַרוּ וְקַבִּלוּ מִינַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that the *baraita* is referring to **Shammai and his faction and Hillel and his faction, didn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Shmuel said: With regard to eighteen matters they issued decrees** that day, **and with regard to** those **eighteen** matters **they disagreed** prior to that? The eighteen disputes were only between the disciples of Shammai and Hillel, **whereas Hillel and Shammai** themselves **argued only in three places.** Clearly, they were neither party to the disputes nor the decrees. **As Rav Huna said:** Shammai and Hillel **disagreed in** only **three places and no more. And if you say** that Hillel and Shammai **came** and **decreed** that *teruma* that came into contact with hands **would be in abeyance, and their students came and decreed to burn** *teruma* that came into contact with hands, then the following difficulty arises. **Didn't Ilfa,** one of the Sages, **say:** With regard to **hands,** from **the beginning their decree** was that *teruma* that comes into contact with them is **to be burned?** According to Ilfa, there is no uncertainty. *Teruma* that came into contact with definite impurity is burned. *Teruma* that is in abeyance may not be destroyed. One must wait until it becomes definitely impure or decomposes on its own. **Rather,** the explanation is that **they came and issued a decree and** the people **did not accept** the decree **from them, and their disciples came and issued a decree and they accepted** it **from them.**

The *daf* begins at the bottom of 14b, with the *gemara* addressing a tension in the attribution of the ruling that hands are a *sheni l'tumah* capable of rendering *terumah tamei*. One tradition traces this ruling to the *gezeiros* of *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* as part of the eighteen things. Yet another tradition attributes it to *Shammai* and *Hillel* themselves, not their respective schools. The *gemara* attempts to reconcile these by suggesting that when we say *Shammai* and *Hillel*, we really mean *Shammai* and his *beis midrash*, *Hillel* and his *beis midrash* — meaning they were the same as *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* and all part of the *yud ches davar*.

This reconciliation is immediately challenged. Rav Yehuda cites Shmuel: *yud ches davar gazru b'yud ches nechelku* — on eighteen things they issued an edict, and on eighteen things they were divided. The implication in the *Bavli* is that these were the same eighteen: the things that became the *yud ches davar* were originally matters of dispute. The problem, however, is that *Hillel* and *Shammai* are known to have debated in only three areas, and *tumah* of *yadayim* was not among them. Since the *yud ches davar* were things that had been debated, the *takkanah* of *yadayim* cannot be attributed to *Shammai* and *Hillel* as part of that same group.

### Two Stages of Strictness

A second attempt at reconciliation is then offered. Perhaps *Shammai* and *Hillel* and *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* represent two distinct *stages* of the *gezeirah*, not two separate bodies. Under this reading, *Shammai* and *Hillel* ruled that hands that touched *terumah* render it *toleh* — meaning one does not immediately burn it, since after all the *tumah* is only *d'rabbanan*, but one does not eat it either. One waits for the *terumah* to become invalid naturally, and only then burns it. The students, *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel*, then came and extended the ruling: they declared that such *terumah* must actually be burned outright, treating the hands as conferring full *tumah*.

### Ilfa's Challenge

This two-stage reconciliation is rejected on the basis of a ruling by *Ilfa*: *yadayim techilas gezeirasam l'sreifah* — from the very outset, the *takkanah* regarding hands stipulated that *terumah* touched by impure hands must be burned. There was never a preliminary stage in which one merely suspended the *terumah*. The two-stage model therefore cannot apply to *yadayim*.

### Accepted vs. Rejected

The *gemara* therefore concludes with a different resolution entirely. *Shammai* and *Hillel* did indeed attempt to enact the *gezeirah* of *yadayim* — but it was not accepted. One of the foundational principles governing *gezeiros* is that a *gezeirah* does not become binding unless it is accepted by *klal Yisrael*: *ein gozrin gezeirah al hatzibur ela im ken rov hatzibbur yachol la'amod bah* — a decree may not be imposed on the public unless a majority of the public is capable of upholding it, and correspondingly, a decree that fails to achieve acceptance does not take hold. *Shammai* and *Hillel* tried, but the society was not receptive. Then their students came — *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* — and renewed the *gezeirah*, and this time it was accepted. This is a striking resolution: the same ruling, issued by teacher and student alike, succeeded the second time around, apparently because the communal climate had shifted and people had become more receptive.

### Solomon's Takkanah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי, שְׁלֹמֹה גְּזַר! דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁתִּיקֵּן שְׁלֹמֹה עֵירוּבִין וּנְטִילַת יָדַיִם, יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: ״בְּנִי אִם חָכַם לִבֶּךָ יִשְׂמַח לִבִּי גַם אָנִי״, ״חֲכַם בְּנִי וְשַׂמַּח לִבִּי וְאָשִׁיבָה חוֹרְפִי דָבָר״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **Still,** the matter is not clear, as the decree of hands was **issued** by King **Solomon. As Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: At the time that Solomon instituted** the ordinances of ***eiruv* and** of **washing hands** to purify them from their impurity, **a Divine Voice emerged and said** in his praise: **"My son, if your heart is wise my heart will be glad, even mine"** (Proverbs 23:15), and so too: **"My son, be wise and make my heart glad, that I may respond to those who taunt me"** (Proverbs 27: 11).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹמֹה גְּזַר לְקׇדָשִׁים, וַאֲתוֹ אִינְהוּ וּגְזוּר אַף לִתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Solomon** and **decreed** impurity on hands **to** prohibit contact with **consecrated items,** and Shammai, Hillel, and their disciples **came and decreed** impurity on hands even **to** prohibit contact with ***teruma*.**

The *gemara* then raises yet another tradition that complicates the picture further. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel teaches: *b'sha'ah she'tikein Shlomo eruvin v'netilas yadayim* — when *Shlomo HaMelech* enacted *eruvin* and *netilas yadayim*, a *bas kol* went forth and declared: *beni im hacham libecha yismach libi gam ani* — "My son, if your heart is wise, my heart too will be glad." The verse signals divine approval for a human rabbinic initiative. If *Shlomo* himself enacted *netilas yadayim*, how can we attribute it to *Shammai* and *Hillel* or to their schools?

The logic underlying *netilas yadayim* runs as follows: hands are deemed *tamei* as a *sheni l'tumah*, and a *sheni* can render *terumah tamei*. Since eating bread is analogous to the *kohanim* eating *terumah*, the requirement of *netilas yadayim* was extended universally to anyone eating bread. This chain of reasoning is traced back to *Shlomo*. It is noteworthy that two *takkanot* in particular — *eruvin* and *netilas yadayim* — are projected all the way back to *Shlomo HaMelech*.

### Solomon's Kodashim

The *gemara* resolves the tension by distinguishing the scope of *Shlomo*'s original *takkanah*. *Shlomo gazar l'kodashim* — *Shlomo* decreed that hands are *tamei* only in relation to *kodashim*, not *terumah*. If the ruling applied only to *kodashim*, then *netilas yadayim* as we understand it — the washing before eating ordinary bread — would not follow from *Shlomo*'s enactment at all, since the connection to bread eating operates through *terumah*, not *kodashim*. *Shlomo*'s *netilas yadayim* must therefore have been intended specifically for *kohanim* eating *kodashim*, or for individuals who had brought a *korban* and were entitled to eat from it — not the universal practice we observe today.

### Extension to Terumah

It was then *Shammai* and *Hillel* — and ultimately their students — who extended the ruling to *terumah* as well. *Shammai* and *Hillel* attempted this extension but it did not hold. Their *talmidim* then renewed the *gezeirah* and this time it was accepted and became binding. The picture that emerges is a layered legislative history: *Shlomo* enacted the *takkanah* for *kodashim*; *Shammai* and *Hillel* tried to extend it to *terumah* and failed; *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* succeeded in establishing the full extension, culminating in the universal practice of *netilas yadayim* before eating bread.

## Eighteen Things

### Three Sets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁמֹנָה עָשָׂר גָּזְרוּ וּבִשְׁמֹנָה עָשָׂר נֶחְלְקוּ. וְהָתַנְיָא הוּשְׁווּ! בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם נֶחְלְקוּ, וּלְמָחָר הוּשְׁווּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As to the matter itself** that was mentioned above in passing, **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: With regard to eighteen matters they issued decrees** that day, **and with regard to** those **eighteen** matters **they disagreed** prior to that. The Gemara asks: **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **they reached a consensus** in their opinions with regard to the eighteen decrees? They answer: **On that day they disagreed, and the following day,** after the matter was decided in a vote, **they reached a consensus** in their opinions.

The *gemara* returns to the earlier statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel — *yud ches gazru v'yud ches nechelku* — and addresses the apparent contradiction with the teaching that *hushvu*, that they reached consensus. The *Bavli* seems to conflate all of these into a single set of eighteen, understanding that the items debated and the items decreed were one and the same group. The *Yerushalmi*, however, presents a considerably more structured picture: three entirely separate sets of eighteen.

### Same Day Debate

According to the *Yerushalmi*, one set of eighteen were things that *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* actively debated, with *Beis Shammai* ultimately prevailing and winning the vote — these are the *yud ches gazru*. A second set were things on which there was always consensus, never a dispute — the *hushvu*. A third set were things that remained as standing *machlokes* and were never resolved — the *nechelku*. The *Bavli*'s reading, by contrast, takes the debate and the decree as the same eighteen, understanding the sequence as: they debated, then *Beis Shammai* won, then the next day *Beis Hillel* conceded and they were *hushvu*. The *Yerushalmi*'s reading is arguably more natural given the order of the words: the *Bavli* has *gazru* before *nechelku*, which would be puzzling if the decree followed from the debate, whereas the *Yerushalmi* treats them as independent categories.

### Rambam's Framework

The *Rambam* engages directly with the *Yerushalmi*'s framework and identifies all three sets. The eighteen things that *Beis Shammai* won by vote — the *yud ches gazru* — are the *tumah v'taharah* rulings discussed in the *gemara* here, the long list of *sheni l'tumah* concerns. These were things *Beis Shammai* was confident they had the votes to win, so they brought them to a vote. The eighteen things that were always consensus — the *hushvu* — the *Rambam* identifies as the *mishnayos* that preceded the current *mishna*, the cases of the *baal habayis* and the *ani*, the carrying before *Shabbos*, the sitting down for a haircut, and so on. And the eighteen things that remained as standing disputes — the *nechelku* — are, according to the *Rambam*, the *mishnayos* that follow in the remainder of the first *perek*. These are the cases *Beis Shammai* chose not to bring to a vote, precisely because they were uncertain of the outcome — they feared that some members of their school might switch sides, and so those debates were left unresolved.

## Shammai & Hillel Debates

### Challah Amounts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת נֶחְלְקוּ שַׁמַּאי וְהִלֵּל. שַׁמַּאי אוֹמֵר: מִקַּב חַלָּה. וְהִלֵּל אוֹמֵר: מִקַּבַּיִים. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה וְלֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה, אֶלָּא קַב וּמֶחֱצָה חַיָּיב בַּחַלָּה. מִשֶּׁהִגְדִּילוּ הַמִּדּוֹת, אָמְרוּ חֲמֵשֶׁת רְבָעִים קֶמַח חַיָּיבִין בַּחַלָּה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: חֲמִשָּׁה פְּטוּרִין, חֲמִשָּׁה וְעוֹד חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As to the matter itself** that was mentioned above in passing, **Rav Huna said: Shammai and Hillel disagreed in three places.** The Gemara cites the disputes. One, **Shammai says: From a *kav*** of dough, one is required to separate ***ḥalla*,** the portion of the dough given to a priest. From any less than that measure there is no obligation to separate *ḥalla,* as that is not the measure alluded to in the verse: "The first of your dough" (Numbers 15:20), written with regard to the mitzva of separating *ḥalla*. **And Hillel says:** One must separate *ḥalla* only **from two *kav*. And the Rabbis say:** The *halakha* is **neither in accordance with the statement of this** one, who is stringent, **nor in accordance with the statement of that** one, who is lenient. **Rather, one and a half *kav*** is the measure from which one **is obligated** to separate ***ḥalla*. Once the measures increased** and the Sages recalculated the volume of a *kav* to be greater, **they said** that based on the measure of the new *kav*, **five quarters** of a *kav* of **flour** is the measure from which one **is obligated** to separate ***ḥalla*. Rabbi Yosei says: Five** quarters **are exempt;** only from dough the size of **five** quarters **and** a bit **more is** one **obligated** to separate *ḥalla*.

Rav Huna teaches that *Shammai* and *Hillel* disputed in three areas. The first is the measure of dough that obligates the separation of *challah*. *Shammai* says: from one *kav* of flour. *Hillel* says: from two *kavim*. The *chachamim* say: neither — rather, one and a half *kavim* is the threshold of obligation.

### Measure Conversions

The derivation of these measures requires working through a series of unit conversions. The *Torah* connects *challah* to the concept of *reishis arisoseichem*, which the *gemara* associates with the *omer* of *manna* in the wilderness — the daily portion was an *asiris ha'eifah*, one tenth of an *eifah*. An *eifah* is seventy-two *log*, so a tenth of that is 7.2 *log*. Now, a *kav* is four *log*. However, there was a well-known conversion between the measures used in the *midbar* and the later rabbinic measures: what had been six units in the period of the *Torah* became five units in the later system (*shisha midbariot* equal *chamisha yerushalmiyot*), meaning the later measures were proportionally larger. Multiplying by five-sixths, the 7.2 *log* of the *midbar* becomes six *log* in the later system — which is one and a half *kavim*. This is the position of the *chachamim*. *Hillel* and *Shammai* apply the arithmetic somewhat differently and arrive at two *kavim* and one *kav* respectively.

After a subsequent re-standardization of measures — *mishe'higdilu hamiddot* — the threshold shifted again. Rabbi Yosi rules that five *reva'im* of flour are exempt and five *reva'im* plus any additional amount are obligated. Rashi explains this as a function of Rabbi Yosi's egg-volume calculation being slightly larger than that of the *chachamim*. *Tosafot* offers a different reading: Rabbi Yosi agrees with the base figure of five *kavim* but holds that five *kavim* must remain *after* the *challah* is taken, not that one begins with five *kavim*. Therefore one needs five *kavim* plus a minimal additional amount — a *mashu* — at the outset.

### Mikvah Water Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, הִלֵּל אוֹמֵר: מְלֹא הִין מַיִם שְׁאוּבִים פּוֹסְלִים אֶת הַמִּקְוֶה — שֶׁחַיָּיב אָדָם לוֹמַר בִּלְשׁוֹן רַבּוֹ. שַׁמַּאי אוֹמֵר: תִּשְׁעָה קַבִּין. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה וְלֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה. עַד שֶׁבָּאוּ שְׁנֵי גַרְדִּיִּים מִשַּׁעַר הָאַשְׁפָּה שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם וְהֵעִידוּ מִשּׁוּם שְׁמַעְיָה וְאַבְטַלְיוֹן שֶׁשְּׁלֹשָׁה לוּגִּין מַיִם שְׁאוּבִים פּוֹסְלִים אֶת הַמִּקְוֶה, וְקִיְּימוּ חֲכָמִים אֶת דִּבְרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And another** dispute between Hillel and Shammai is that **Hillel says: A full *hin*,** twelve *log*, **of drawn water** poured into a ritual bath in which there was not yet a full measure of forty *se'a* **disqualifies the** water of the **ritual bath** and accords even the water that had been there previously the status of drawn water. Even if water fit for a ritual bath is subsequently added to complete the measure of forty *se'a*, the ritual bath remains unfit for immersion. Hillel used the biblical measure, *hin*, **because,** when quoting one's teacher, **a person must speak** employing **the language of his teacher. Shammai says: Nine *kav*** of water is enough to disqualify the ritual bath. **And the Rabbis say:** The *halakha* is **neither in accordance with the statement of this** one **nor in accordance with the statement of that** one. The Sages did not determine a measure for the water disqualifying a ritual bath **until two weavers came from the Dung Gate in Jerusalem and testified in the name of Shemaya and Avtalyon that three *log* of drawn water disqualify the ritual bath, and the Rabbis upheld their statement** against the opinions of the great Sages of Israel, Hillel and Shammai. The Gemara emphasized their occupation and the place that they lived to underscore that, despite the fact that their occupation was despised and their place was contemptible, there is no preferential treatment when it comes to Torah.

The second debate between *Shammai* and *Hillel* concerns the amount of drawn water (*mayim she'uvim*) that can invalidate a *mikvah*. *Hillel* says: a full *hin* of drawn water invalidates the *mikvah*. A *hin* is twelve *log*, which works out to roughly two two-liter bottles of liquid. *Hillel* adds the notable statement *she-chayav adam lomar bilshon rabbo* — a person is obligated to quote the language of his teacher — the significance of which will be addressed shortly. *Shammai* says: nine *kavim*, which is thirty-six *log*, three times the amount stipulated by *Hillel*, making *Shammai* considerably more lenient on this point. The *chachamim* rule neither like *Shammai* nor like *Hillel*: they waited until two weavers from the Dung Gate in Jerusalem came and testified in the name of *Shemaya v'Avtalyon* that just three *log* of drawn water invalidates the *mikvah* — a fraction of what either *Shammai* or *Hillel* had said — and the *chachamim* upheld that position.

### Weavers' Evidence

The mention of the weavers carries an important lesson: *le'olam lo yimna adam atzmo mibeis hamidrash* — no one should ever exclude himself from the study hall. Weaving was a profession held in low regard, and yet these two weavers possessed a tradition transmitted directly from *Shemaya v'Avtalyon* that proved decisive. The *gemara* connects this to the tractate *Eduyot*, which was taught at the session when the doors of the *beis midrash* were opened to all comers — the policy of inclusion that allowed previously overlooked testimony to be gathered and preserved.

What makes this particularly striking is the *Hillel* dimension. *Hillel* himself was a student of *Shemaya v'Avtalyon*, and his statement *she-chayav adam lomar bilshon rabbo* — I am quoting the exact language of my teacher — is an explicit invocation of that chain of transmission. Hillel is not presenting a personal opinion; he is claiming to directly transmit what he heard from *Shemaya v'Avtalyon*. And yet the *chachamim* backed the weavers' testimony over *Hillel*'s. The text offers no explanation of why. A priori, one would expect the weight to fall on *Hillel*'s side. One can only infer that the *chachamim* found the weavers' tradition more consistent with what they knew — or perhaps that three *log* simply made more internal sense — and that in this particular case they judged that testimony more reliable.

### Gra on Pronunciation

The *Gra* offers an illuminating explanation of why *Hillel* said *mlo hin* rather than simply *hin mayim she'uvim poslin es hamikvah*. The reason *Shemaya v'Avtalyon* used the phrase *mlo hin*, the *Gra* explains, is that they were *geirim*, and as such they struggled with the guttural pronunciation of the letter *hei*. Had they said *hin* with an imprecise guttural, it could have been heard as *ein* — *ein mayim she'uvim poslin es hamikvah*, meaning drawn water does *not* invalidate the *mikvah*, the precise opposite of what they intended. By prefacing the measure with *mlo* — a full *hin* — the meaning could not be misunderstood regardless of how the *hei* was pronounced. *Hillel* himself could have said *hin* clearly and been understood, but he preserved the exact formulation of his teachers — *bilshon rabbo* — including the seemingly redundant *mlo*, because of the obligation to transmit Torah in the precise language in which it was received.

### Blood Stain Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, שַׁמַּאי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים דַּיָּין שְׁעָתָן. וְהִלֵּל אוֹמֵר: מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְיָמִים הַרְבֵּה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה וְלֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה, אֶלָּא: מֵעֵת לְעֵת מְמַעֵט עַל יָד מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה, וּמִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה מְמַעֵט עַל יָד מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And another** dispute between Hillel and Shammai is that **Shammai says: All women, their time is sufficient,** i.e., a woman who notices that she saw blood of menstruation but did not feel the flow beforehand, need not worry that perhaps the flow of blood began before she saw it, and it is sufficient if she assumes ritual impurity status beginning at that moment. **Hillel says: From examination to examination,** i.e., a woman who saw blood, if she does not know when the menstrual flow began, she is considered impure retroactive to the last time she examined herself and found herself to be ritually pure, **and even** if the examination took place **several days** earlier. Anything that she touched in the interim becomes ritually impure. **And the Rabbis say:** The *halakha* is **neither in accordance with the statement of this** one **nor in accordance with the statement of that** one; **rather,** the principle is: **A full day,** twenty-four hours, **reduces** the time **from examination to examination,** i.e., if her final self-examination took place a long time before, she need only concern herself with ritual impurity for the twenty-four hour period prior to noticing the blood. **And from examination to examination reduces** the time **from a full day,** i.e., if she examined herself in the course of the previous day and discovered no blood, she was certainly ritually pure prior to the examination.

The third debate between *Shammai* and *Hillel* concerns the retroactive *tumah* of a woman who discovers a blood stain. *Shammai* says *dayan sha'atan* — we assess it from the current moment, assuming the stain occurred just now. Anything she touched prior to discovery is therefore *tahor*. *Hillel* says *mipkidah lipkidah* — we trace back to the last time she checked herself; if she was last examined three days ago and found clean, everything she touched in the intervening three days may be considered *tamei*. Notably, in two of the three *Shammai*-*Hillel* debates, *Hillel* takes the more stringent position.

### Sages' Compromise

The *chachamim* again introduce a compromise: *me'et le'et mema'et al yad mipkidah lipkidah umipkidah lipkidah mema'et al yad me'et le'et* — twenty-four hours limits the last examination, and the last examination limits twenty-four hours, meaning one applies whichever is the lesser of the two. If she last checked three days ago, she is retroactively *tamei* only for the previous twenty-four hours. If she last checked five hours ago, she is retroactively *tamei* only for those five hours. This is the operative ruling.

### Uncounted Debates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ לֵיכָּא? וְהָאִיכָּא הִלֵּל אוֹמֵר לִסְמוֹךְ, וְשַׁמַּאי אוֹמֵר שֶׁלֹּא לִסְמוֹךְ. כִּי קָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא, הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבְּווֹתָא בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And are there no more** disputes between them? **Isn't there** what we learned that **Hillel says** that it is permitted **to lay hands** on the heads of offerings sacrificed on a Festival, and one performs no prohibited labor and does not desecrate the Festival by doing so; **and Shammai says not to lay hands?** The Gemara answers: **When Rav Huna said** his statement, he was referring to disputes **where there is no dispute** between the great **Sages** who predated them **concomitant with theirs.** The dispute with regard to laying hands on the Festival is ancient, and their predecessors, Sages dating back to the beginning of the era of the pairs, already disputed it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא הַבּוֹצֵר לַגַּת — שַׁמַּאי אוֹמֵר: הוּכְשַׁר, וְהִלֵּל אוֹמֵר: לֹא הוּכְשַׁר. בַּר מִינֵּיהּ דְּהַהִיא, דְּהָתָם קָא שָׁתֵיק לֵיהּ הִלֵּל לְשַׁמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **Isn't there** also the dispute with regard to **one who harvests** grapes in order to take them **to the press** and stomp them as to whether or not the liquid that seeps out of the grapes is considered as having seeped out willfully and renders the grapes susceptible to impurity? **Shammai says: It has become susceptible, and Hillel says: It has not become susceptible.** The Gemara rejects this: **Except for that one, as there,** although they originally disagreed, ultimately **Hillel was silent** and did not respond **to Shammai** and ultimately accepted his opinion.

The *gemara* then asks whether there are no other debates between *Shammai* and *Hillel*. It raises the famous dispute over *semicha* on *Yom Tov* — whether one may perform the laying of hands on an animal offering brought as a *nedarim u'nedavos* on *Yom Tov* — where *Hillel* permits and *Shammai* prohibits. Rav Huna's rule, however, limits the count to disputes that were unique to *Shammai* and *Hillel* alone: *heicha d'leika plukta d'revusa* — only where there was no prior standing dispute. The *semicha* debate had been contested across multiple generations of *Zugos* long before *Shammai* and *Hillel*; it was not a debate that originated with them and is therefore not included in the three.

A further case is raised: *habotzer lagat* — one who harvests grapes intended for pressing. *Shammai* says the juice that drips from the grapes during harvest renders them *huchshar*, susceptible to *tumah*, because there are circumstances in which one would want that juice. *Hillel* says they are not *huchshar*. This might appear to be a fourth debate. But the *gemara* notes that *Hillel* was silent in the face of *Shammai*'s arguments — meaning he conceded — and a debate that was conceded does not count as a standing dispute between them.

The very fact that *Shammai* and *Hillel* debated in only three areas should give one pause. We are so conditioned by the ubiquity of *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* disputes that we tend to project the same pattern back onto *Shammai* and *Hillel* themselves. In reality, we have very few halakhic teachings from *Shammai* and *Hillel* individually, and almost no *machlokes* between them — a striking contrast with the hundreds of disputes attributed to their schools.

## Early Enactments

### Two Yoseis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה וְיוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹחָנָן אִישׁ יְרוּשָׁלַיִם גָּזְרוּ טוּמְאָה עַל אֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים וְעַל כְּלֵי זְכוּכִית. וְהָא רַבָּנַן דִּשְׁמֹנִים שָׁנָה גְּזוּר! דְּאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: כְּשֶׁחָלָה רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, שָׁלְחוּ לוֹ: רַבִּי, אֱמוֹר לָנוּ שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלוֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַרְתָּ לָנוּ מִשּׁוּם אָבִיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** Earlier it was mentioned that **Yosei ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida and Yosei ben Yoḥanan of Jerusalem decreed impurity upon the land of the nations and upon glass vessels.** The Gemara asks: Was it these two Sages, who were among the first Sages in the era of the pairs, who issued these decrees? **Wasn't it the** Sages who lived in the final **eighty years** of the Second Temple period who **issued** these **decrees?** As **Rav Kahana said: When Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, fell ill,** the Sages **sent to him: Rabbi, tell us two or three statements that you** once **told** us **in the name of your father.**

Having discussed the attribution of *yadayim*, the *gemara* turns its attention to the earlier *ברייתא* that cited *Yosei ben Yoezer ish Tzreidah* and *Yosei ben Yochanan ish Yerushalayim* — figures known primarily from *Pirkei Avos* as among the earliest of the *Zugos* — as having enacted *tumah al eretz ha'amim*, declaring the land of *chutz la'aretz* *tamei*, and *tumah al klei zechuchis*, declaring glassware susceptible to *tumah*. In the *Torah*, the categories subject to *tumah* include metal, pottery, leather, and wood, but glassware is not explicitly mentioned. From the time glassware began to be manufactured and used, *chazal* eventually ruled that it too could become *tamei* — and this ruling is traced here to the earliest *Zugos*.

### Historical Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלַח לָהֶם, כָּךְ אָמַר אַבָּא: מֵאָה וּשְׁמֹנִים שָׁנָה עַד שֶׁלֹּא חָרַב הַבַּיִת, פָּשְׁטָה מַלְכוּת הָרְשָׁעָה עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל. שְׁמֹנִים שָׁנָה עַד שֶׁלֹּא חָרַב הַבַּיִת גָּזְרוּ טוּמְאָה עַל אֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים וְעַל כְּלֵי זְכוּכִית. אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה עַד שֶׁלֹּא חָרַב הַבַּיִת גָּלְתָה לָהּ סַנְהֶדְרִין וְיָשְׁבָה לָהּ בַּחֲנוּיוֹת. לְמַאי הִילְכְתָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: לוֹמַר שֶׁלֹּא דָּנוּ דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת. דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: שֶׁלֹּא דָּנוּ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **He sent to them: This** is what my **father said: One hundred and eighty years before the Temple was destroyed, the evil kingdom** of Rome **invaded Israel. Eighty years before the Temple was destroyed, they decreed impurity on the land of the nations and on glass vessels. Forty years before the Temple was destroyed, the Sanhedrin was exiled** from the Chamber of Hewn Stones **and sat in the stores** on the Temple Mount. With regard to the last statement, the Gemara asks: **What** are the **halakhic** ramifications of this statement? **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Avdimi said: To say that they no** longer **judged cases of fines.** The Gemara wonders: **Does it enter your mind** that they no longer judged **cases of fines?** Even several generations after the Temple was destroyed they continued to judge cases of fines in Eretz Yisrael. **Rather,** emend and **say: That they no longer judged capital cases.** The authority to impose the death penalty was stripped from the Sanhedrin, and therefore they willingly left the Chamber of Hewn Stone. Since the Sanhedrin no longer convenes in its designated place, the *halakha* is that it no longer has the authority to judge capital cases (*Tosafot*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵימָא בִּשְׁמֹנִים שָׁנָה נָמֵי אִינְהוּ הֲווֹ, וְהָתַנְיָא: הִלֵּל וְשִׁמְעוֹן, גַּמְלִיאֵל וְשִׁמְעוֹן, נָהֲגוּ נְשִׂיאוּתָן לִפְנֵי הַבַּיִת מֵאָה שָׁנָה. וְאִילּוּ יוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה וְיוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹחָנָן הֲווֹ קָדְמִי טוּבָא!
>
> **Translation:** In any case, we learned that the Sages of the last eighty years before the destruction are the ones who decreed impurity on the land of the nations. **And if you say** that Yosei ben Yo'ezer and Yosei ben Yoḥanan **were also there during** those **eighty years, wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Hillel, and** his son **Shimon,** and his grandson **Gamliel, and** his great-grandson **Shimon filled their** position of ***Nasi* before the House,** while the Temple was standing, for **a hundred years, while Yosei ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida and Yosei ben Yoḥanan were much earlier** than Hillel?

The *gemara* challenges this attribution by introducing another tradition: Rav Kahana transmits that when Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosi fell ill, the Sages sent to him requesting that he share a few teachings in his father's name before he died. He responded with a chronological tradition: 180 years before the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash* — approximately 110 BCE — *pashta malchus ha'resha'ah al Yisrael*, the evil empire (Rome) extended its dominion over Israel. Eighty years before the *churban* — approximately 10 BCE — they enacted *tumah al eretz ha'amim v'al klei zechuchis*, the very two enactments attributed to *Yosei ben Yoezer* and *Yosei ben Yochanan*. And forty years before the *churban* — approximately 30 CE — the *Sanhedrin* relocated from *Har HaBayis* and took up residence in the market stalls (*chanuyos*) of Jerusalem.

The *gemara* asks what the halakhic significance of the *Sanhedrin*'s relocation was. Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi says it was to note that *dinei nefashos*, capital cases, could no longer be adjudicated. *Tosafot* is troubled by this: we find that the location requirement — that the *Beis Din* must sit specifically on *Har HaBayis* — applies only to the case of *zaken mamre*, the rebellious elder, where the verse says *el hamakom asher yivchar Hashem Elokecha bo*, and the *gemara* derives *melamed shehamakom gorem* — that the physical location is itself constitutive of the authority invoked against the *zaken mamre*. Elsewhere, *Tosafot* notes, we find no source that the *Beis Din*'s presence on *Har HaBayis* is required for capital cases. *Tosafot* does not resolve the difficulty.

The chronological problem remains: if the enactment of *eretz ha'amim* impurity was 80 years before the *churban*, and if we have a tradition that *Hillel* led his dynasty for 100 years before the *churban* — with *Yosei ben Yoezer* and *Yosei ben Yochanan* several generations earlier still — then the dates plainly do not match. *Yosei ben Yoezer* could not have been active merely 80 years before the *churban*.

## Foreign Land Decree

### Ground Suspension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: אֲתוֹ אִינְהוּ גְּזוּר אַגּוּשָּׁא לִשְׂרוֹף וְאַאֲוִירָא וְלֹא כְלוּם, וַאֲתוֹ רַבָּנַן דִּשְׁמֹנִים שָׁנָה גְּזוּר אַאֲוִירָא לִתְלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this decree was issued in stages. First, Yosei ben Yo'ezer and Yosei ben Yoḥanan **came and issued a decree** that *teruma* that comes into contact with **a clump** of earth of the land of the nations is **to** be **burned**, **and** they decreed **nothing** with regard to *teruma* that enters into **the air** space of the land of the nations. **The Sages of** the final **eighty years** prior to the destruction of the Temple **came** and **issued a decree** with regard to *teruma* that enters into **the air** space of the land of the nations that its legal status is **in abeyance,** and it is not burned.

The *gemara* resolves the chronological tension by positing that the *gezeirah* of *eretz ha'amim* unfolded in multiple stages, not all at once. The earliest stage, attributed to *Yosei ben Yoezer* and *Yosei ben Yochanan*, was: *gazru agusha litlos* — if one physically touches the ground of *chutz la'aretz*, one is in a state of suspended *tumah* (*toleh*) with regard to *terumah*, meaning one does not eat the *terumah* but does not burn it either. As for the air of *chutz la'aretz* — traveling through it without touching the ground — *v'lo klum*, there was no decree at all at this stage.

### Air Suspension

The second stage was enacted by the Sages of eighty years before the *churban*. They added the airspace: both the ground and the air of *chutz la'aretz* now render one in a state of *toleh*, suspended *tumah* requiring one to hold the *terumah* but not yet to burn it. So now ground and air both produce suspension, but not burning.

### Six Usha Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי, בְּאוּשָׁא גְּזוּר! דִּתְנַן: עַל שִׁשָּׁה סְפֵקוֹת שׂוֹרְפִין אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה: עַל סְפֵק בֵּית הַפְּרָס, וְעַל סְפֵק עָפָר הַבָּא מֵאֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים, וְעַל סְפֵק בִּגְדֵי עַם הָאָרֶץ, וְעַל סְפֵק כֵּלִים הַנִּמְצָאִין, וְעַל סְפֵק הָרוּקִּין, וְעַל סְפֵק מֵי רַגְלֵי אָדָם שֶׁכְּנֶגֶד מֵי רַגְלֵי בְּהֵמָה — עַל וַדַּאי מַגָּעָן וְעַל סְפֵק טוּמְאָתָן שׂוֹרְפִין אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asked further: **And still** is the matter clear? Didn't **the Sages** issue this **decree in Usha,** many years after the destruction of the Temple? **As we learned** in a mishna: **For six** cases of **uncertain** impurity **one burns the *teruma*** which came into contact with them: **For the uncertain** case **of *beit haperas*,** meaning *teruma* that entered a field where a grave was plowed and the location of the bones of the corpse is unknown, and it is uncertain whether or not the *teruma* became impure; **And for the uncertain** case **of earth that comes from the land of the nations,** whose impurity itself has the status of uncertain impurity. Therefore, *teruma* that came into contact with it also has the status of uncertain impurity; **And for the uncertain** case **of the clothes of an *am ha'aretz*.** Since an *am ha'aretz* is not careful with regard to purity, we are concerned lest a menstruating woman touch his clothes. Due to that uncertainty, his clothes are considered impure with a severe degree of impurity. If *teruma* came into contact with them there is uncertainty with regard to whether or not they became impure;  **And for the uncertain** case **of vessels** that are not his **that are found.** Since he does not know whether or not those vessels are impure, if *teruma* came into contact with them, there is uncertainty whether or not they are impure; **And for the uncertain** case **of spittle,** as perhaps it is the spittle of a *zav* and transmits impurity by Torah law. If *teruma* came into contact with it there is uncertainty whether or not it is impure; **And for the uncertain** case **of a person's urine,** even though it was **adjacent to the urine of an animal,** there is room for concern that perhaps it is the urine of a *zav*, and impure by Torah law. If *teruma* came into contact with it, there is uncertainty whether or not it is impure. In all of these cases, the Sages established that **for their definite contact,** when it is clear that these came into contact with *teruma*, **and although** there is **uncertainty with regard to their** essential **impurity,** i.e., it is uncertain whether or not these items are impure, **one burns the *teruma*** that came into contact with them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל סְפֵק מַגָּעָן בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — שׂוֹרְפִין. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד תּוֹלִין, בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — טְהוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: Even in a** case of **uncertain contact;** if it was **in the private domain one burns** *teruma* that came into contact with it, as with regard to impurity by Torah law an uncertainty that developed in a private domain is also ruled impure. According to Rabbi Yosei, these decrees, even though they are fundamentally cases of uncertainty, are sufficiently stringent that the Sages applied Torah law to them. **And the Rabbis say:** Since these cases are only impure by rabbinic decree, in a case of uncertain contact **in the private domain, one** does not burn the *teruma* but rather **places** it **in abeyance.** While **in the public domain, they are ritually pure.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר עוּלָּא: אֵלּוּ שִׁשָּׁה סְפֵיקוֹת בְּאוּשָׁא הִתְקִינוּ! אֶלָּא אֲתוֹ אִינְהוּ גְּזוּר אַגּוּשָּׁא לִתְלוֹת וְאַאֲוִירָא וְלֹא כְלוּם, וַאֲתוֹ רַבָּנַן דִּשְׁמֹנִים שָׁנָה גְּזוּר אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי לִתְלוֹת, וַאֲתוֹ בְּאוּשָׁא גְּזוּר אַגּוּשָּׁא לִשְׂרוֹף, וְאַאֲוִירָא — כִּדְקָאֵי קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Ulla said** with regard to **these six uncertain** cases: **In Usha** they instituted how one must act in terms of practical *halakha*. If so, a clump of earth from the land of the nations transmits impurity from the time of the Usha ordinances and not from eighty years prior to the destruction of the Temple. Rather, Yosei ben Yo'ezer and Yosei ben Yoḥanan **came and decreed** that if *teruma* came into contact with **a clump** of earth from the land of the nations, its legal status is **in abeyance** and one does not burn it, **and upon** *teruma* that entered the **air** space of the land of the nations they decreed **nothing. And the Sages of** the last **eighty years** of the Temple **came** along and **issued a decree upon this,** earth, **and upon that,** air, that in both cases the *teruma* **is in abeyance. And** the Sages of the city of **Usha came** along and **decreed** that *teruma* that came into contact with **a clump** of earth from the land of the nations is **burned. And** *teruma* that entered the **air** space of the land of the nations, **as it stood, it** continues to **stand** in abeyance. They did not impose any greater stringency in this matter.

The third stage, which the *gemara* identifies as *takkanat Usha*, is then introduced. The *mishna* teaches: *al shesh sefeikos sorfin es ha'terumah* — for six categories of doubt, one burns *terumah*. These six are: doubt about *beis hapras* — a field through which a grave was plowed and bone fragments may be scattered; doubt about soil imported from *chutz la'aretz*; doubt about the garments of an *am ha'aretz* — since an *am ha'aretz* is presumed to be careless about *tumah v'taharah*, he may be a *zav* without knowing it, and his clothes may carry *tumah*; doubt about vessels found without known provenance; doubt about human saliva — since it might be the saliva of a *zav* or *zavah*; and doubt about human urine found alongside animal urine.

The last case requires clarification. Rashi reads it as follows: even where it is not certain whether the liquid is human urine or animal urine, the mere possibility that it is human urine is sufficient to require stringency. All the other five cases involve certainty about what the substance is — human saliva, human clothing — but uncertainty about whether the person was *tamei*. Here, by contrast, there is uncertainty about the very identity of the substance, and Rashi says we are nonetheless stringent. *Tosafot* reads it in precisely the opposite direction: when urine appears in isolation one may assume it is animal urine and be lenient, but when human urine appears *alongside* animal urine, the contrast makes it apparent which is which, and one can identify the human urine with certainty — and it is only then that one must be concerned.

The six cases also differ from the *Beis Shammai*-*Beis Hillel* items in that they are not necessarily about *sheni l'tumah*. If the spit or urine truly comes from a *zav*, it would be an *av hatumah*, and one who touches it becomes a *rishon*. The emphasis of *sorfin* here is not about the degree of *tumah* but about the communal commitment to treat even doubtful cases with full stringency — to burn rather than merely suspend.

The *mishna* distinguishes between *vadai magaan* — certain contact, uncertain *tumah* status — in which case one burns, and *safek magaan* — uncertain contact. Rabbi Yosi rules that in a *reshus hayachid* even doubtful contact obligates burning, whereas the *chachamim* say that in a *reshus hayachid* uncertain contact produces only *toleh*, and in a *reshus harabbim* it is *tahor* entirely.

*Ulla* confirms that these six things were all enacted at *Usha* after the *churban*.

### Usha Burning Rule

With this in place, the three-stage history of the *eretz ha'amim* decree becomes clear. Stage one (*Yosei ben Yoezer*): ground produces *toleh*, air produces nothing. Stage two (eighty years before the *churban*): ground produces *toleh*, air also produces *toleh*. Stage three (*Usha*): ground now produces *sreifah* — one burns *terumah* — and air remains at *toleh*, exactly as it was in stage two. The *Usha* innovation was not about the airspace; it was specifically about the ground, and specifically about escalating the consequence from suspension to burning.

## Glassware Status

### Glass-Pottery Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלֵי זְכוּכִית — מַאי טַעְמָא גְּזוּר בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן טוּמְאָה? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הוֹאִיל וּתְחִלַּת בְּרִיָּיתָן מִן הַחוֹל שַׁוִּינְהוּ רַבָּנַן כִּכְלֵי חֶרֶס. אֶלָּא, מֵעַתָּה לֹא תְּהֵא לָהֶן טׇהֳרָה בְּמִקְוֶה. אַלְּמָה תְּנַן: וְאֵלּוּ חוֹצְצִין בְּכֵלִים — הַזֶּפֶת וְהַמּוֹר בִּכְלֵי זְכוּכִית!
>
> **Translation:** One of the matters mentioned above was the decree of impurity on glass vessels. With regard to **glass vessels, what is the reason that the Sages decreed impurity upon them? Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **Reish Lakish said: Since the beginning of the manufacture** of glass vessels **is from sand, the Sages equated them to earthenware vessels.** The Gemara asks: **But if** what you say is **so,** if the Sages truly equated the impurity of glass vessels to the impurity of earthenware vessels, **there should not be purification in the ritual bath for** glass vessels, just as there is no purification for earthenware vessels. **Why,** then, **did we learn** in a mishna with regard to the *halakhot* of immersing vessels: **And these** materials **interpose in vessels,** i.e., if they were stuck to the vessel when it was immersed the vessel is not purified: **The pitch and the myrrh** that were stuck **on glass vessels** obstruct their immersion. Apparently, glass vessels are purified in a ritual bath.

The *gemara* turns to the second of the two enactments attributed to the early period: *tumah* of *klei zechuchis*. Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Reish Lakish explains the reasoning: *ho'il u'techilas bri'asan min hachol* — since glassware originates from sand, the Sages treated it like pottery (*klei cheres*). Pottery derives from earth; glass derives from sand — and so *rabbanan* applied the same rules of *tumah* to glass as to pottery.

### Lead-Filled Glass

The analogy to pottery immediately raises a problem. If glass is to be treated like pottery, then just as pottery cannot be purified in a *mikvah* — the way to purify pottery is to break it — glass should likewise be incapable of *tahara* through immersion. Yet the *mishna* in the laws of *chatzitza* implies that glass vessels can be immersed in a *mikvah*, since it lists substances like tar and myrrh that constitute an impediment (*chatzitza*) to proper immersion of glassware. Why is immersion possible if glass is like pottery?

### Rabbi Meir's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן? — כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּיקְּבוּ וְהִטִּיף לְתוֹכָן אֲבָר. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר הַכֹּל הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַמַּעֲמִיד. דְּתַנְיָא: כְּלֵי זְכוּכִית שֶׁנִּקְּבוּ וְהִטִּיף לְתוֹכָן אֲבָר, אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְטַמֵּא וַחֲכָמִים מְטַהֲרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that glass cannot usually be purified in a ritual bath. However, **with what are we dealing here? With** a special **case where** the glass vessels **were perforated and he dripped** molten **lead into them** to seal the hole. This *halakha* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said: Everything follows** the nature of **the facilitator,** i.e., if an object that is not fit for use on its own is reinforced with a different material that facilitates its use, the entire object assumes the legal status of that material. Therefore, since the substance that sealed the holes in these glass vessels is lead, which can be purified through immersion like other metals, these glass vessels can also be purified in a ritual bath. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Glass vessels that were perforated and one dripped lead into them; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said** that **Rabbi Meir deems them ritually impure and the Rabbis deem them ritually pure.**

The *gemara* limits the permissibility of *mikvah* immersion for glass to a specific and unusual case: a glass vessel that developed a hole and was filled with lead. This is the position of Rabbi Meir, who holds *hakol holech achar hama'amid* — the defining identity of an object follows whatever it is that gives it its structural integrity. A glass vessel with a hole is no longer a functional vessel; its hole renders it incapable of containing anything. What restored it to vessel-status was the lead filling. Therefore its current identity is that of a lead vessel, i.e., a metal vessel. Metal vessels, unlike pottery, can be purified through *mikvah* immersion — and so the lead-filled glass vessel can be immersed.

### Meir vs. Sages

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel records the dispute: Rabbi Meir says the lead-filled glass vessel is *tamei*, the *chachamim* say it is *tahor*. The *gemara*'s understanding is that Rabbi Meir, by applying *hakol holech achar hama'amid*, reclassifies the vessel as a metal vessel — which is susceptible to *tumah* biblically (*de'oraita*). The *chachamim* disagree and declare it *tahor*. *Tosafot* raises the question: even if one does not accept Rabbi Meir's reclassification as a metal vessel, should the vessel not at least be *tamei* as a glass vessel on a *rabbinic* level? The answer implied is that perhaps yes — but Rabbi Meir's unique contribution is the claim that it rises to the level of full biblical *tumah* as a metal vessel.

The practical upshot for the main discussion is that the *mikvah* immersion of glass is permissible only in this special case of a hole filled with lead. For standard glass, the analogy to pottery holds: it can become *tamei*, but it cannot be purified through *mikvah*. The *gemara* will continue with this topic on the following *daf*.
