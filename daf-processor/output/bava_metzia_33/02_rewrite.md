# Bava Metzia 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzaar Ba'alei Chayim

### Intro: Tzaar Ba'alei

The daf begins in the middle of a sustained discussion concerning whether *tzaar ba'alei chayim* — the prohibition against causing suffering to animals — is a principle of Torah law (*de'oraita*) or merely rabbinic enactment (*derabbanan*). In other Talmudic contexts, this question bears on whether one may violate a rabbinic prohibition on Shabbat in order to relieve an animal's pain. Here, however, the Gemara frames the question differently: is *tzaar ba'alei chayim* a Torah *value* — operative and weighty enough to shape how we understand the obligations of *prika u'te'inah* (unloading and loading a collapsed animal) and how we interpret the relevant *pesukim*? This is a significant methodological point: sometimes our grasp of the underlying values determines how we *darshan* a verse, rather than the *drasha* being a purely mechanical exercise. The Gemara proceeds to marshal arguments in both directions.

### Proof 1: Mishnah

The Gemara opens with *teda* — "know," or "here is a proof." It cites the Mishnah's ruling that if the owner of the collapsed animal walks away, sits down, and says to the passerby, "You have a *mitzvah* to unload it — go ahead," the passerby is *patur* (exempt) from doing so, as derived from the verse *azov ta'azov imo* — "you shall surely help *with him*," implying the owner must participate.

The Gemara asks: if *tzaar ba'alei chayim* is *de'oraita*, what difference should it make whether the owner is present and helping or not? The animal is suffering regardless. *Mai li itai le'marei bahadei u'mai li leika le'marei bahadei* — why should the presence or absence of the owner affect the obligation? If the concern is genuinely the suffering creature before you, the owner's irresponsibility ought to be irrelevant.

### Response: Paid vs Free

The Gemara responds with a crucial distinction: *mi savrat patur patur legamrei v'dilma patur bechinam ve'chayav besachar* — perhaps "exempt" does not mean fully exempt, but only exempt from doing it *for free*. One may charge for the labor when the owner is being irresponsible. The Gemara then restates the Mishnah accordingly: *itai le'marei bahadei avid gabei bechinam* — if the owner is there helping, you help him at no cost; *leika le'marei bahadei avid gabei besachar* — if the owner abandons his animal, you must still tend to the suffering animal, but you may collect payment. Thus, *tzaar ba'alei chayim* remains *de'oraita*, and the animal must be relieved of its suffering — only the question of compensation shifts based on the owner's conduct.

### Proof 2: Non-Jew's Animal

The Gemara now attempts to *support* the position that *tzaar ba'alei chayim* is *de'oraita* by citing a Baraita: *behemat oved kochavim metapel bah ke'vehemat Yisrael* — one must tend to the animal of a non-Jew just as one would tend to the animal of a Jew. This seems to be a compelling proof. All the brotherly obligations — *tzedakah*, going out of one's way for a neighbor — are generally understood to apply specifically between Jews. If the obligation of *prika u'te'inah* were about protecting the *owner's* property or preventing the owner from being stranded, one would expect it to apply only to Jewish owners. But if it is about the *animal's* suffering — which is the same regardless of who holds the deed — it makes perfect sense that it would apply equally to a non-Jew's animal.

### Resolution: Eivah

The Gemara rejects this proof, however, with the counterproposal: *hatam mishum eivah* — there, the reason one tends to the non-Jew's animal is not because of *tzaar ba'alei chayim* at all, but simply to avoid *eivah* (enmity). Leaving a non-Jew's animal to suffer creates ill-will between Jews and non-Jews, and it is that social concern — not the Torah's animal welfare principle — which motivates the ruling.

The Gemara reinforces this interpretation by noting: *hachi nami mistavra* — and indeed, this reading is more reasonable. For the Baraita continues: *im hayta te'una yayin assur ein zakuk lah* — if the animal was carrying *yayin nesech* (wine used for idolatrous libations), one need not help at all. If the motive were genuinely *tzaar ba'alei chayim*, the nature of the cargo would be irrelevant — the animal is suffering regardless. But if the motive is *eivah*, one can distinguish: concerning wine of *avoda zara*, one may plausibly explain to the non-Jew that religious law forbids handling it, and non-Jews, the Gemara elsewhere assumes, will accept such a religious justification. This cleanly explains why helping would be required in the ordinary case but not when the animal carries forbidden wine. The proof from the non-Jew's animal thus dissolves.

### Proof 3: Non-Jew Animal…

The Gemara introduces a further case: *behemat oved kochavim u'masoy Yisrael* — a non-Jew's animal carrying a Jew's wares. The verse *ve'chadalta* is invoked in the sense of permission to refrain — suggesting that in this configuration, one is not obligated to help. Now the question sharpens considerably: if *tzaar ba'alei chayim* is *de'oraita*, how can one ever refrain from helping a suffering animal? And if the obligation hinges on the wares' owner rather than the animal's owner, what is the underlying logic?

The Gemara responds: *le'olam tza'ar ba'alei chayim de'oraita* — *tzaar ba'alei chayim* remains a Torah principle, but the case here is *te'inah* (reloading), not *prika* (unloading). Reloading is about helping the *person* — getting their cargo to its destination — not about relieving the animal's pain. Thus it is a different category of obligation entirely. Unloading, driven by *tzaar ba'alei chayim*, one would always be obligated to perform. Reloading, which involves assisting the human being standing before you, can legitimately turn on who that person is.

### Loading vs Unloading

The Gemara then tests this reading against the second half of the Baraita: *behemat Yisrael u'masoy oved kochavim* — a Jew's animal carrying a non-Jew's wares — in which case *azov ta'azov* applies and one must help. If this is about reloading, asks the Gemara, why does one focus on who *owns the animal* rather than who *owns the wares*? If reloading is about helping the person who needs to get his goods somewhere, should not the identity of the cargo's owner be decisive?

The Gemara explains: *stama d'milsa inish basar chamrei azil* — ordinarily, the person driving the donkey is the person who owns it. So the owner of the animal is, as a practical matter, the person physically present before you. It is *that* person — the one you face — who is Jewish or non-Jewish, and that is what determines whether the interpersonal obligation of *te'inah* applies. If the driver before you is Jewish (because he owns the donkey), you reload; if he is a non-Jew (because the non-Jew owns the donkey), you are not obligated to reload.

This explains the Baraita's logic when working with the assumption that *tzaar ba'alei chayim* is *de'oraita*: *prika* (unloading) one would always perform, since the animal's suffering is the concern; *te'inah* (reloading) follows the identity of the person present before you. But it is worth pausing to note what the Baraita would have looked like under the contrary assumption — that *tzaar ba'alei chayim* is *not de'oraita*. In that case, *prika* itself would be about protecting *property* — the animal as a possession — and the question would simply be: whose donkey is it? A Jew's donkey, you unload; a non-Jew's, you do not. The distinction between the owner of the animal and the owner of the wares would then be self-explanatory, with no need to recast the Baraita as discussing *te'inah* at all.

### Proof 4: Verse Citations

The Gemara now presses the point further: *v'ha v'chadalta m'azov lo, azov ta'azov b'prika hu d'chtiv* — the trouble is, the verses being cited by the Baraita are the verses about *prika* (unloading), not *te'inah* (reloading). One cannot read this Baraita as discussing reloading when its proof-texts are explicitly about unloading. The Gemara is backed into a corner.

### Rabbi Yosei HaGelili

Faced with a Baraita whose plain meaning cannot be reconciled with the position that *tzaar ba'alei chayim* is *de'oraita*, the Gemara invokes a familiar move: *ella ha mani Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili hi, d'amar tzaar ba'alei chayim lav de'oraita* — this Baraita follows Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili, who holds that *tzaar ba'alei chayim* is not a Torah principle at all.

Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili maintains that if an owner has overloaded his animal, you have no obligation to unload it. This position is coherent: if the operative concern were the animal's suffering, the owner's negligence would be irrelevant — the animal suffers whether or not it was overloaded. But for Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili, whose framework is not about the animal's pain but about assisting the owner in his distress, an owner who has acted irresponsibly forfeits his claim on your assistance. A Baraita that limits the obligation based on whose animal it is, and disregards the suffering of the animal as an independent factor, is therefore consistent with Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili and need not reflect the majority view.

The Gemara then brings a fourth proof: *ohev lifrok ve'sonei liton* — if one encounters two situations simultaneously, the animal of a person one likes being crushed under its load (*prika*), and the animal of a person one dislikes needing to be loaded up (*te'inah*), which takes precedence? The ruling is: *mitzvah b'sonei kedei lachof et yitzro* — the *mitzvah* favors tending first to the enemy's animal in order to subdue one's evil inclination and overcome the enmity.

The Gemara asks: if *tzaar ba'alei chayim* is *de'oraita*, should not the suffering animal — the one requiring *prika* — take precedence, regardless of interpersonal dynamics? The Gemara responds: *afilu hachi kedei lachof et yitzro adif* — even so, overcoming one's evil inclination is itself a Torah value, rooted in *ahavat Yisrael* and *lo tisna et achikha bilvavekha*. Multiple *de'oraita* principles can coexist and compete, and subduing one's *yetzer* may, in this particular configuration, outweigh the animal-suffering concern without disproving it.

A fifth proof is then brought: *sonei she'amru sonei Yisrael velo sonei oved kochavim* — when the verse speaks of an enemy's animal, it means an Israelite enemy, not a non-Jewish one. If the concern were the animal's suffering, the nationality of the owner would again be irrelevant. The Gemara replies: *mi savrat sonei dekra ka'amar* — this is not speaking of the *pasuk*'s case of *chamor soneicha* at all. It refers to the Mishnah's discussion of the interpersonal case we just discussed. And that ruling — favoring the enemy's animal to subdue one's *yetzer* — applies only between Jews, since it is rooted in obligations of mutual Jewish solidarity. No proof against *tzaar ba'alei chayim de'oraita* can be extracted from it.

### Proof 5: Unloading Limits

The Gemara brings yet another proof from a Baraita: *rovetz velo ravtzan* — the animal must be crouching under its load, but not one that habitually crouches; *rovetz velo omed tachat masa'o* — it must be actively crouching, not merely straining while still standing; and *tachat masa'o, masui sheyachol la'amod bo* — only under a burden it is capable of bearing. If the animal was given an excessive load, one is not obligated.

The Gemara's challenge is immediate: *ve'ei amrat tzaar ba'alei chayim de'oraita mah li rovetz mah li ravtzan u'mah li omed* — if the concern is the animal's suffering, why should any of these distinctions matter? A habitually crouching animal still suffers. An animal straining while standing still suffers. An overloaded animal suffers even more. Under a *tzaar ba'alei chayim de'oraita* framework, none of these limitations should exist.

The Gemara again attributes this Baraita to Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili: *ha manni Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili hi de'amar tzaar ba'alei chayim derabbanan* — this entire Baraita reflects his position. The Gemara adds that this attribution is internally confirmed: *dekatani tachat masa'o masui sheyachol la'amod bo* — the clause about "an appropriate burden" is the signature of Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili, for he is the one who holds that the owner's negligence removes your obligation. *Man shamat leh d'it leh hai sevara Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili* — who is it that holds this position? Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili. So the entire Baraita must follow him.

### Charging for Reload

The Gemara raises a complication: the same Baraita also contains the phrase *velo mefurak* — "and not if it is already unloaded" — suggesting no obligation to reload. But surely there *is* a *mitzvah* to reload, as written explicitly: *hakeim takim imo*. The Gemara therefore clarifies that *lo mefurak bechinam ela besachar* — "not unloaded for free, but rather for pay." The obligation to reload exists, but unlike unloading, it may be performed for compensation.

This ruling about charging for reloading reflects the position of the *Rabbanan* in the Mishnah, not Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili specifically. The Gemara resolves the apparent inconsistency: *le'olam Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili hu, u've'ha savar lah ke'Rabbanan* — the Baraita overall follows Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili, but on this particular point about reloading for pay, he agrees with the *Rabbanan*. These are independent debates, and holding one position does not dictate the other. Even without *tzaar ba'alei chayim* as the operative principle, one can still recognize a meaningful distinction between *prika* — where the urgency of a collapsing animal creates a stronger imperative — and *te'inah*, where the owner simply needs assistance getting his load back on, and for which payment may reasonably be expected.

The evidence across all these proofs weighed, if anything, somewhat in the direction of *tzaar ba'alei chayim* not being the operative principle in *prika u'te'inah* — or at least not the only operative principle. The Gemara elsewhere concludes more definitively that *tzaar ba'alei chayim* is indeed *de'oraita*, but here the debate concludes without explicit resolution.

## Distance & Payment

### Distance: One Ris

A new Baraita is introduced that defines the scope of the obligation. The verse *ki tir'eh* — "when you see" — might have implied that the obligation to help a fallen animal applies even at great distance; conversely, *ki tifga* — "when you encounter" — might have suggested only direct physical proximity. The Gemara resolves the tension: one is obligated when one *sees* the animal close enough that it constitutes an *encounter*. The Sages measured this as one out of seven and a half in a *mil* — that is, 2/15ths of a *mil*, which works out to approximately 266 *ammot*, roughly the length of a football field, about 100 yards. This distance is called a *ris*.

### Accompaniment: One Parsah

After unloading and reloading the animal, the obligation does not end there. A Tanna taught: *u'medado imo ad parsah* — one must accompany the owner for a *parsah* (four *mil*, approximately 12,000 feet) after reloading, to ensure the animal does not collapse again under its burden. This is a substantial demand — at the Gemara's standard walking pace of approximately 18 minutes per *mil*, this accompaniment would take around 72 minutes.

### Charge for Parsah

Because of the significant burden of this extended accompaniment, Rabbah bar bar Chanah rules: *u'notel sachar* — for traveling that full *parsah*, one may charge. This is consistent with the broader principle we saw earlier that particularly demanding assistance may be performed for compensation.

## Precedence: Self/Father/…

### Your Object First

The Gemara now moves to a celebrated Mishnah addressing *aveida* — the recovery of lost objects — and the question of competing obligations. The general ethical principle in Jewish law is that obligation intensifies with proximity: obligations to those closest to you take precedence. Accordingly: *aveida shelo va'avedat aviv, shelo kodemet* — your own lost object comes before your father's. Even a child is not required to sacrifice his own property for his father's benefit when both are at stake simultaneously.

### Father Over Teacher

By the same logic: *aveida shelo va'avedat rabo, shelo kodemet* — your own lost object also comes before your *rebbi*'s. But now the hierarchy shifts: *avedat aviv va'avedat rabo, shel rabo kodemet* — between your father's lost object and your *rebbi*'s, your *rebbi* takes precedence. The Mishnah provides the rationale: *she'aviv hevi'o la'olam ha-zeh, ve'rabo she'limdo chochma mevi'o le'chayei ha'olam ha'ba* — your father brought you into this world, but your *rebbi* who taught you *chochma* brings you into life in the *olam ha'ba*. One's religious and intellectual formation, the Mishnah holds, constitutes a form of relationship even deeper than biological paternity.

This ruling may seem to serve the interests of the very Sages who authored it, and one might raise that objection. But the underlying logic is not self-serving: it reflects a sincerely held conviction that Torah learning and the religious life it cultivates constitute the deepest and most enduring good one person can confer upon another. The Mishnah is not devaluing the father; it is elevating the significance of Torah to the highest possible station.

### Father Is Sage

There is, however, an important qualification: *ve'im aviv chacham, shel aviv kodemet* — if the father is himself a *chacham*, his lost object takes precedence over the *rebbi*'s. The Mishnah does not require that the father have taught as much as the *rebbi*, or that he specifically be responsible for the son's Torah education. Once the father is in the *parsha* — once he himself represents Torah learning and scholarship — he reasserts his priority. The precise threshold of what it means for a father to be a *chacham* in this context is left somewhat ambiguous in the Mishnah.

### Burdens & Captivity

The same hierarchy applies across analogous cases. *Haya aviv ve'rabo nosim masoy* — if both one's father and one's *rebbi* are carrying burdens and need to be relieved, one relieves the *rebbi* first and then the father. *Haya aviv ve'rabo be'veit ha'shevi* — if both are in captivity, one ransoms the *rebbi* first and then the father. Given the Torah's powerful emphasis on *kabeid et avicha ve'et imecha* and the deep bonds of family, the Mishnah's insistence that the *rebbi* consistently precedes the father is striking. Only when the father is a *chacham* does the calculus reverse — and again even then without requiring the father's Torah contribution to equal the *rebbi*'s.

### Verse Source

The Gemara asks: *mena hanei mili* — what is the Scriptural basis for the principle that one's own needs come first? Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav derives it from the verse *efes ki lo yihiyeh becha evyon* — "however there shall be no poor person *in you*." The emphasis on "in you" is read as *shelcha kodem le'shel kol adam* — your own welfare takes precedence over that of everyone else. This is the principle of *chayecha ve'chayei achikha* — your life before your fellow's.

However, Rav Yehuda adds an important caution in the name of Rav: *kol ha'mekayem be'atzmo kach sof ba liyedei kach* — one who always insists on this principle, always ensuring his own needs are met before extending himself for others, will ultimately end up impoverished. The right one has to prioritize oneself is real, but constant self-focus is self-defeating. One must, when possible, transcend this prerogative.

### Who Is a Rabbi?

The Mishnah gave priority to the *rebbi* without defining who qualifies. A Baraita addresses this directly. Rabbi Meir holds: *rabo she'limdo chochma, ve'lo rabo she'limdo mikra u'mishna* — only the teacher who taught you *chochma* counts, meaning deep analytical learning — *gemara* in the broad sense — not mere memorization of *mikra* or *Mishnah*. Rabbi Yehuda holds: *kol she'rov chochmato heimenu* — whoever provided the majority of one's Torah knowledge, regardless of its form. This is more subjective and less elitist than Rabbi Meir's position: if someone has only ever learned *mikra*, the teacher who gave him most of that *mikra* is his *rebbi*; similarly for *Mishnah*. But it also means only one teacher can occupy this role. Rabbi Yosei holds the broadest view: *afilu lo heir einav ela be'mishna achat, zu hi rabo* — even if he illuminated your eyes in only a single *mishnah*, that person is your *rebbi*.

Rava offers a personal illustration of Rabbi Yosei's position: Rav Schora explained to him the meaning of *zuhama listron* in tractate Keilim — a type of ladle used to skim froth from a pot. That single clarification, Rava implies, would suffice to confer the status of *rebbi*. Similarly, Shmuel performed *keriah* (the mourning rending of garments) for a certain rabbinic student — not a major scholar — who had explained to him a particular detail about how the *Kohanim* would open the doors of the *Heichal*, one reaching in to armpit-depth to draw one door open and then pushing the second directly. A single illuminating explanation was enough for Shmuel to regard that person as one of his teachers and respond to his death with the mourning rite appropriate for a *rebbi*.

### Honor vs Precedence

Ulla sharpens an important distinction. He observes that *talmidei chachamim* in Bavel would stand for one another and perform *keriah* for one another — treating each other, in terms of honor, as *rebbeim*. This is the standard of *kavod* owed to Torah scholars as a class.

But *le'inyan aveida bi'mkom aviv*, when it comes to the question of whose lost object takes priority over a father's — that is a different and more demanding standard. For that purpose, only one's *rabo muvhak*, one's primary and singular teacher, suffices. The honors of standing and *keriah* may be extended broadly; but the displacement of parental priority belongs exclusively to the *rabo muvhak*. This sounds like the approach of Rabbi Yehuda.

### Rav Chisda's Question

Rav Chisda posed a question to Rav Huna: *talmid ve'tzarich lo rabo mai* — what is the status of a student whose *rebbi* himself relies on him, perhaps to recall certain memorized material? Would that reciprocal dependence alter the nature of the relationship?

Rav Huna heard the question as a personal affront — as if Rav Chisda were implying that Rav Huna needed *him*, Rav Chisda. He replied pointedly: *Chisda Chisda, la tzerichna lach, at tzerichat li* — "Chisda, Chisda, I don't need you; you need me." From that exchange, the two remained estranged from one another for forty years, *arba'in shanin ikpad ahadadei*, and did not visit one another. Rav Chisda was offended that his purely theoretical question was taken as a personal implication; Rav Huna perhaps felt guilty for having suspected him, yet remained unable to overcome the wound. Rav Chisda sat forty fasts (*arba'in ta'anita*) for having caused Rav Huna distress — even without intent. Rav Huna, in turn, sat forty fasts for having wrongly suspected Rav Chisda of bad faith. The pathos of the episode is not lost: guilt, it seems, did not translate into reconciliation.

### Ruling on Rabbi

The Gemara reports two competing rulings. Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *halacha ke'Rabbi Yehuda* — we follow Rabbi Yehuda, meaning the *rabo muvhak*, the primary teacher from whom one received most of one's Torah. Rav Acha bar Rav Huna in the name of Rav Sheshet: *halacha ke'Rabbi Yosi* — we follow Rabbi Yosei, meaning even one who illuminated a single *mishnah*.

The Gemara then challenges the ruling attributed to Rabbi Yochanan: Rabbi Yochanan's own general principle is that the *halacha* follows the *stam mishna* — the anonymously stated ruling in the Mishnah — and the Mishnah states *rabo she'limdo chochma*, which sounds more like Rabbi Meir than Rabbi Yehuda. The Gemara resolves the tension: *mai chochma rov chochmato* — the word *chochma* in the Mishnah should be read as "the majority of one's learning," not exclusively analytical *gemara*-style study. Read this way, the Mishnah's anonymous ruling aligns with Rabbi Yehuda's position, and Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is consistent with his general principle.

## Torah Study Hierarchy

### Study Classification

Having discussed what constitutes a *rebbi* — with the debate centering heavily on the distinction between *mikra*, *Mishnah*, and *gemara* — the Gemara now addresses these categories directly. A Baraita lays out an explicit hierarchy: *ha'oskin be'mikra, mida ve'einah mida* — those who engage with *mikra* (Scripture) possess a quality, but it is not of the highest order. *Be'Mishnah, mida ve'notlin aleha sachar* — those who study *Mishnah* have an authentic virtue for which they receive reward, since *Mishnah* leads toward practical *halacha* and the deeper learning of *gemara*. *Gemara ein lach mida gedolah mizu* — but those who study *gemara*, there is no greater level than this.

### Contradiction

The same Baraita then states: *le'olam havei ratz le'Mishnah yoter min ha'gemara* — yet one should always run to study *Mishnah* more than *gemara*. The Gemara immediately notes the internal contradiction: *amarta be'gemara ein lach mida gedolah mizu, ve'hadar amarta le'olam havei ratz le'Mishnah yoter min ha'gemara* — first we are told nothing surpasses *gemara*, and then we are told to prioritize *Mishnah* over *gemara*.

### Rabbi's Reform

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the contradiction historically: *bimei Rebbe, nishneit mishnah zu* — this Baraita was composed in the time of Rebbe (Rabbi Yehuda ha-Nasi), during his great editorial project of organizing and codifying the Mishnah. What Rebbe did was gather the tannaitic traditions, adjudicate between contradictory versions, determine which rulings were primary and which secondary, and produce the structured, authoritative *Mishnah* we possess. In doing so, he transformed rote memorization of traditions into genuine understanding.

His conclusion was that *gemara* — analytical engagement — was of supreme importance: *gemara ein lach mida gedolah mizu*. But the unintended consequence was dramatic: *shavku kulei alma matnitin ve'azlu batar gemara* — everyone abandoned *Mishnah* entirely and rushed off to do *gemara*. The problem is that analysis without command of the material is impossible. *Svara* cannot operate in a vacuum; one cannot analyze facts one does not know. Seeing this, Rebbe reversed course and proclaimed: *le'olam havei ratz le'Mishnah yoter min ha'gemara* — go back to *Mishnah*, prioritize acquiring the body of knowledge, because without it the analytical work collapses. The two rulings in the Baraita represent these two different moments in Rebbe's pedagogical campaign, each responding to the distortions of the other.

The underlying logic of *gemara*'s supremacy seems to be its connection to practical *halacha*. Learning *pesukim* alone does not yield legal rulings; *gemara* — the process of working through the law — does. *Gemara* is intellectually demanding and practically consequential. This explains why it ranks above *mikra* in the hierarchy. Of course, the study of *gemara* encompasses tractates like *Kodashim* and *Taharot* that have no immediate practical application, so intellectual intensity and rigor are also part of the calculus. The *Rashbam*, in his introduction to his commentary on the Torah, pushes back: because *Chazal* characterized *mikra* study as *mida ve'einah mida*, Jews became ignorant of *Tanakh*, which is the very foundation of our tradition — and in the medieval period, that ignorance was weaponized against them by those who sought to contest Jewish interpretation. A proper Jewish education, this view holds, requires the full range.

### Gemara: Errors = Sins

The Gemara supplies the *derasha* underlying *gemara*'s supreme status. Rabbi Yehuda bar Ila'i expounded: *hagged le'ammi pish'am u'leveit Yaakov chattatam* — "tell my people their transgression, and the house of Jacob their sin." *Leammi* — "my people," the favored ones — refers to *talmidei chachamim*, for *pesha* denotes intentional sin. *Elu talmidei chachamim she'shegagot na'asot lahem ke'zedonot* — for Torah scholars, errors are treated as intentional transgressions, because their responsibility is to know the law and rule correctly. Negligence in study that leads to incorrect *psak* is not excused as an innocent mistake; the scholar is held to a higher standard. This is why rigorous *gemara* study is not merely preferable but essential — the consequences of error are of a different category entirely for those who bear the mantle of authority.

This interpretation is corroborated by a Mishnah: *Rabbi Yehuda omer, havei zahir be'talmud, she'shiggas talmud ola le'zadon* — be exceedingly careful in your learning, for an error arising from study rises to the level of intentional sin.

### Learner Categories

Rabbi Yehuda bar Ila'i further expounds another verse: *shim'u devar Hashem ha'charedim el devaro* — "hear the word of God, you who tremble at His word." These tremblers, he says, are the *talmidei chachamim*, who are acutely conscious that their errors carry weighty consequences. Then comes a social portrait embedded in the verse's continuation: *amru achichem* — "your brothers" — refers to *ba'alei mikra*, those who study Scripture. They are your brothers, committed to Torah in their way, but not in the same category as *talmidei chachamim*. *Soneichem* — "those who hate you" — refers to *ba'alei Mishnah*. This startling identification reflects a well-known Talmudic tension: those who know only *Mishnah* believe they possess sufficient knowledge to rule on *halacha*, yet they frequently err, and the Gemara elsewhere calls such people *mavlei olam* — those who destroy the world. The closer a group is to the *talmidei chachamim* in status, the sharper the perceived threat from their partial and potentially misleading knowledge. *Menadeichem* — "those who cast you out" or treat you as *niddui* — are the *ammei ha'aretz*, the unlearned, between whom and *Chazal* there was considerable historical tension as well.

### All Israel Rejoices

Despite these tensions and internal fractures, the Gemara closes on a note of reconciliation. Lest one conclude that the hope of the various groups — *ba'alei mikra*, *ba'alei Mishnah*, *ammei ha'aretz* — is forfeit because of these enmities, the verse continues: *ve'nira be'simchatchem* — you will yet see in their rejoicing. All Israel, despite its divisions, shares in the ultimate joy. And lest one fear that Israel itself will be shamed by these internal conflicts, the verse reassures: *ve'hem yevoshu* — it is the nations, *ovdei kochavim*, who will be put to shame, *ve'Yisrael yismechu* — while Israel rejoices. This closing note perhaps echoes the tension we saw earlier in the daf between the obligations owed to Jews and non-Jews in the context of *prika u'te'inah* — a thread that quietly surfaces here in the final resolution.

*Hadran alach Elu Metzios.* With this, the second chapter of the tractate concludes.

## Shomrim: Paid Loss

### Four Watchmen

The Gemara now opens the third chapter of Bava Metzia, turning from lost objects to the laws of *shomrim* — guardians entrusted with another's property. There are four types of *shomrim*: the *shomer chinam* (unpaid watchman), the *shomer sachar* (paid watchman), the *socher* (renter), and the *sho'el* (borrower). Each bears a different level of liability. The chapter opens with the case of the *shomer chinam*.

### Voluntary Payment

The Mishnah addresses a specific scenario: *ha'mapkid etzel chaveiro behema o keilim* — one deposits with a friend an animal or vessels. The friend watches without pay. The objects are stolen or lost. A *shomer chinam* who was not negligent may take an oath asserting this and be fully exempt from payment — *shomer chinam nishba ve'yotzei*. But in the Mishnah's case, the *shomer chinam* does not wish to swear. Perhaps he harbors a general aversion to oaths, a sensibility deeply embedded in Jewish ethical practice over centuries; perhaps he simply feels a residual sense of moral responsibility, however technically unwarranted. Whatever his reason, he chooses to pay rather than swear.

### Thief Found: Kefel

Having voluntarily paid, what happens if the thief is subsequently found? *Nimtza ha'ganav meshalem tashlumei kefel* — the thief pays the double penalty. *Tavach u'machar, meshalem tashlumei arba'ah va'chamisha* — if the theft involved a sheep or ox that was slaughtered or sold, the thief pays four or five times the value. The question is: to whom does this payment go? *L'mi she'ha'pikadon etzlo* — to the one in whose possession the deposited object resided, meaning the *shomer*, not the original owner. The *shomer*, having elected to pay when he was legally exempt, acquires standing to receive any future penalty extracted from the thief.

The incentive structure here is worth noting. The owner's primary interest is in recovering his object, not in receiving the oath of his *shomer*. Granting the *shomer* the benefit of *kefel* or four-and-five creates an inducement for *shomrim* to pay up rather than exempt themselves through a *shevua* — which means the owner is more likely to receive actual restitution if the thief is found, and the *shomer* has something to gain from taking on the liability.

### Swears Not Pays

The Mishnah then presents the contrasting case: the *shomer chinam* takes the oath and does not pay — the standard outcome. If the thief is subsequently found, *meshalem tashlumei kefel, tavach u'machar meshalem tashlumei arba'ah va'chamisha, l'mi meshalem, l'va'al ha'bayit* — the thief's doubled or multiplied payment goes to the owner of the object. This is the ordinary case: the *shomer* who exercised his legal right to swear and exit his liability retains no stake in the object and therefore acquires no claim to whatever the thief eventually pays.

### Animals & Vessels

The Gemara asks a pointed question: *lama li lemitna behema, ve'lama li lemitna keilim* — why does the Mishnah need to specify both *behema* (animal) and *keilim* (vessels)? Both appear in the relevant *pesukim*, but is the Mishnah merely echoing the verses, or does each term add something substantive?

The Gemara answers: both are necessary. Had the Mishnah mentioned only *behema*, one might have thought the *shomer*'s entitlement to *kefel* applies specifically to animals — because watching an animal involves considerable effort (*nafish tircha le'iyula ule'afoka*, taking it out to graze, bringing it in, tending to it), so the bonus of *kefel* is a fitting reward for that labor. Mere vessels, which require almost no effort to watch, might not have generated the same *takanah*. Conversely, had the Mishnah mentioned only *keilim*, one might have thought the *kefel* principle applies specifically there — because *keilim* are typically of modest value, so the *kefel* is not a large sum and the Sages were comfortable granting it. But animals carry the potential of *arba'ah va'chamisha* — four and five times the value — a far more substantial payout, and one might have thought the Sages hesitated to extend the *shomer*'s entitlement that far. Each term is therefore necessary to preclude a different erroneous inference: *tzricha*.

The *Rashbam* adds a structural observation: the Torah itself mentions *keilim* in the context of *shomer chinam* and *behema* in the context of *shomer sachar*, and this is not accidental. *Keilim* are typically watched without pay, since the effort is minimal. *Behema* demands significant effort and so typically generates paid guardianship. The Mishnah's pairing of both reflects the Torah's own awareness of this natural distinction.

### Rami's Challenge

The Gemara closes the daf with a sharp challenge from Rami bar Chama. The entire mechanism by which the *shomer* acquires the *kefel* has been described using the language of *makne* — the owner "conveys" to the *shomer* his right to the *kefel*. But Rami bar Chama objects: *v'ha ein adam makne davar shelo ba la'olam* — one cannot transfer ownership of something that does not yet exist. At the moment the *shomer* agrees to pay, the *kefel* does not exist. There is no thief yet identified, no conviction, no double payment to speak of. How can the owner convey a non-existent entitlement?

Even Rabbi Meir — who is attributed with the position that a person *can* transfer ownership of something not yet in existence — limits this leniency to cases like pre-selling the fruit of a tree that has not yet borne fruit. There, the future fruit is naturally anticipated; it is virtually present, as it were. *Aval hacha* — but here, at the top of 34a — *mi yeimar de'mignava* — who says the item will even be stolen? *Mi yeimar de'mishtakach ganav* — if stolen, who says the thief will be found? *Mi yeimar de'mishalem* — if found, who says he will pay? *Dilma modei u'miftar* — perhaps he will simply confess and thereby be exempted from the doubled payment. The *kefel* is not merely unborn — it is entirely speculative, dependent on a cascade of contingencies none of which is guaranteed. Under these circumstances, how can any form of *kinyan* — transfer of ownership — be operative?

Rava's resolution to Rami bar Chama's challenge awaits us at the beginning of 34a.
