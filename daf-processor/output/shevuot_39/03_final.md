# Shevuot 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oath Model & Requirements

### Oath Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכִי מַשְׁבְּעִינַן לֵיהּ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתוֹ בִּשְׁבוּעָה הָאֲמוּרָה בְּתוֹרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ בַּה׳ אֱלֹהֵי הַשָּׁמַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **How** does the court **administer an oath to** someone who is liable to take an oath? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** The court **administers to him the oath stated in the Torah, as it is written** in Abraham's instruction to his servant: **"And I will make you swear by the Lord, the God of heaven"** (Genesis 24:3).

The *gemara* opens not in direct response to any particular point in the *mishna*, but to the broader phenomenon of *shevuat hadayanim* — the oath administered by the judges. The question posed is: *heichi mashbinan lei?* How do we actually impose the *shevua*? Rav Yehuda citing Rav answers that we use as our model the oath that Avraham administered to his servant, as it is written: *v'ashbiacha bashem elokei hashamayim v'elokei ha'aretz asher lo sikach isha livni mibnos hakna'ani asher anochi yoshev bekirbo* — "And I will make you swear by the Lord, God of heaven and God of earth, that you will not take a wife for my son from the daughters of the Canaanites among whom I dwell."

The question immediately arises: what precisely are we deriving from this Abrahamic model? The *gemara* might have assumed that the emphasis lies on the words *bashem* — that the oath must invoke God's name specifically. Ravina raised this point with Rav Ashi, asking whether this represents the position of Rabbi Chanina bar Idi, who requires the use of *shem hameyuchad*, the distinctive and explicit Divine Name. Rav Ashi replied that the ruling holds even according to the *rabbanan*, who permit the use of a secondary divine appellation such as *El* rather than the Tetragrammaton. The derivation from the Abrahamic model therefore concerns something else entirely.

### Holy Object Req.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי בְּכִינּוּי; וְנָפְקָא מִינַּהּ צְרִיךְ לְאַתְפּוֹשֵׂי חֶפְצָא בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: You** may **even say** it is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** that one is merely required to take an oath **using an appellation** of God. **And** the practical **ramification** of Rav's statement is that just as in the aforementioned verse, Abraham said: "Please put your hand under my thigh, and I will make you swear" (Genesis 24:2–3), instructing his servant to grasp his circumcised penis, which is considered sacred to some degree, so too, in oaths administered by the court, **one must grasp** a sacred **item in his hand** while taking the oath.

The practical derivation from Avraham's oath is the requirement that the oath-taker hold a holy object in hand during the *shevua*. This follows from the instruction Avraham gave his servant: *sim na yadecha tachas yerechi* — "place your hand under my thigh." The understanding is that the servant grasped the *bris milah* of Avraham, which served as a tangible symbol of a *mitzva* and of covenantal obligation to God. From this we learn that the administration of a *shevua* requires the oath-taker to hold onto something sacred — in practice, a *sefer torah*.

### Rava: No Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְרָבָא – דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הַאי דַּיָּינָא דְּאַשְׁבַּע ״בַּה׳ אֱלֹהֵי הַשָּׁמַיִם״ – נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁטָּעָה בִּדְבַר מִשְׁנָה, וְחוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava; as Rava said: This judge who administers an oath "by the Lord, the God of heaven,"** without instructing the litigant to grasp a sacred item, **is considered like one who made a mistake with regard to a matter** that appears in the **Mishna;** in which case his ruling is **revoked,** and the litigant must repeat the oath.

The *gemara* then invokes the ruling of Rava: a judge who administers an oath invoking *Hashem elokei hashamayim* but does not require the oath-taker to hold a holy object is regarded as one who has erred in a matter of *mishna* — *to'eh bidvar mishna* — and the ruling must be reversed. The implication is that the requirement to hold a holy object is so well-established that failing to enforce it constitutes an inexcusable halakhic error, with the consequences that flow from such errors in the context of judicial rulings discussed in tractate *Sanhedrin*.

### Rav Pappa: Sefer Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי דַּיָּינָא דְּאַשְׁבַּע בִּתְפִלִּין – נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁטָּעָה בִּדְבַר מִשְׁנָה, וְחוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Pappa said: This judge who administers an oath in** which the litigant grasps **phylacteries,** not a Torah scroll, **is considered like one who made a mistake with regard to a matter** that appears in the **Mishna;** in which case his ruling is **revoked,** and the litigant must repeat the oath.

Rav Pappa goes further still: a judge who administers an oath using *tefillin* as the held object — rather than a *sefer torah* — is also considered to have erred in a matter of *mishna*, and the ruling is likewise reversed. The holding of *tefillin* alone does not suffice; a *sefer torah* is required. One might ask whether this standard was ever met in the case of Avraham's servant, who obviously had no *sefer torah* available. The *gemara* implicitly acknowledges that Avraham simply had no alternative.

### Halakha Decision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא. הִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרָבָא – דְּהָא לָא נָקֵיט חֶפְצָא בִּידֵיהּ; וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא – דְּהָא נָקֵיט חֶפְצָא בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava, but the *halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Pappa. The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava** that if the litigant merely took an oath using the ineffable name of God, he is required to take another oath, **as he was not grasping** any sacred **item in his hand; but the *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Pappa,** that if the litigant was grasping phylacteries he is required to take another oath, **as he was grasping** a sacred **item in his hand,** even though it was not a Torah scroll.

The *gemara* then rules on both positions. The *halakha* follows Rava: if no holy object whatsoever was used, it is a clear and unacceptable error, and the oath is invalidated. However, the *halakha* does not follow Rav Pappa. The ruling is: *velo hilchesa kevashei deRav Pappa deha nakat cheftza beyadei dehalo sefer torah* — since the oath-taker was holding some holy object, even if it was only *tefillin* rather than a *sefer torah*, the *shevua* is valid after the fact.

### Standing Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁבוּעָה מְעוּמָּד; תַּלְמִיד חָכָם – מְיוּשָּׁב. שְׁבוּעָה בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה; תַּלְמִיד חָכָם – לְכַתְּחִלָּה בִּתְפִלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** One takes **an oath while standing,** but **a Torah scholar** takes an oath **while sitting.** One takes **an oath** grasping **a Torah scroll** *ab initio*, but **a Torah scholar** may take an oath grasping **phylacteries *ab initio*.**

A *shevua* must be taken while standing — *shevua be'umad*. A *talmid chacham*, however, may be seated — *talmid chacham beyoshev*. This reflects a broader principle of deference to Torah scholars within the court system. The logic behind these various requirements — standing, use of a *sefer torah*, and so forth — appears to be that they are designed to impress upon the oath-taker the gravity of what a *shevua* is and the enormity of the consequences of a false one. A *talmid chacham* does not require the same theatrical weight to internalize that gravity, which is why the rules are relaxed for him: he may sit, and *tefillin* suffices for him *lechatchila* rather than a *sefer torah*.

### Any Language Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁבוּעַת הַדַּיָּינִין, אַף הִיא בִּלְשׁוֹנָהּ נֶאֶמְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **An oath** imposed **by the judges may also** be **recited in its language,** i.e., in any language spoken by the one taking the oath. It is not required that the oath be in Hebrew.

The *Tosefta* cited from the context of tractate *Sota* establishes that *shevuat hadayanim* may be administered in any language — *shevuat hadayanim af hi bilshonam ne'emra*. The significance of this ruling will be unpacked further below in connection with the relevant *mishna* in *Sota*.

## Fear & Consequences

### World's Trembling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: הֱוֵי יוֹדֵעַ, שֶׁכׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ נִזְדַּעְזַע בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּסִינַי ״לֹא תִשָּׂא אֶת שֵׁם ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ לַשָּׁוְא״.
>
> **Translation:** Before he takes the oath, the judges **say to him: Be aware that the entire world trembled when the Holy One, Blessed be He, said at** Mount **Sinai: "You shall not take the name of the Lord, your God, in vain,** for the Lord will not hold guiltless one who takes His name in vain" (Exodus 20:7).

When the oath is about to be administered, those present address the oath-taker with the following: *hevei yodea shekol ha'olam kulo nizdazeia besha'a she'amar hakadosh baruch hu beSinai* — be aware that the entire world trembled at the moment God proclaimed at Sinai: *lo sisa et shem Hashem elokecha lashav*, "You shall not take the name of the Lord your God in vain." The purpose is to impress upon the person about to take the oath the full weight of what he is doing.

### No Forgiveness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״וְנַקֵּה״, וְכָאן נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא יְנַקֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** be aware that **with regard to all of the** other **transgressions in the Torah it is stated: "And will…clear the guilty [*venakkeh*]"** (Exodus 34:7); **whereas here,** with regard to taking a false oath, **it is stated: "Will not hold guiltless [*lo yenakkeh*]."**

The *braisa* goes on to explain what makes this prohibition singular. For all other sins in the Torah, the verse employs the language of *venakeh* — God will cleanse. But here the Torah states *ki lo yenakeh Hashem et asher yisa shemo lashav* — God will not cleanse one who takes His name in vain. The *gemara* reads the phrase *nakeh lo yenakeh* elsewhere in the Torah as meaning: God cleanses those who repent — *nakeh lashavim* — but does not cleanse those who do not repent — *velo yenakeh le'eina shavim*. There is at least some measure of *nakeh* in all other cases. But by *shevuat shav*, the Torah employs *ki lo yenakeh* without any qualifying *nakeh*, signaling that even the possibility of full cleansing is categorically denied.

### Family Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה נִפְרָעִין מִמֶּנּוּ, וְכָאן מִמֶּנּוּ וּמִמִּשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַל תִּתֵּן אֶת פִּיךָ לַחֲטִיא אֶת בְּשָׂרֶךָ״ – וְאֵין בְּשָׂרוֹ אֶלָּא קְרוֹבוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִבְּשָׂרְךָ לֹא תִּתְעַלָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** be aware that with regard to **all of the** other **transgressions in the Torah, punishment is exacted** only **from** the transgressor, whereas **here,** punishment is exacted **from him and from his family, as it is stated: "Do not allow your mouth to bring your flesh into guilt"** (Ecclesiastes 5:5). The verse indicates that one who sins with his mouth, by taking a false oath, causes his flesh to be punished as well; **and one's flesh is nothing other than his relative, as it is stated: "And that you not hide yourself from your own flesh"** (Isaiah 58:7).

Beyond the absence of forgiveness, the *braisa* identifies a second distinctive feature of false oaths: whereas for all other sins in the Torah punishment is exacted from the sinner alone, for a false oath punishment extends to his family as well — *mimenu umimishpachto*. The proof text is: *al titein et picha lachati et besarecha* — do not allow your mouth to bring sin upon your flesh. The word *basar* — flesh — is interpreted as referring to one's relatives, as the verse states: *umibesarcha lo titalem*, do not hide yourself from your own flesh.

### Hosea's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה נִפְרָעִין מִמֶּנּוּ, וְכָאן מִמֶּנּוּ וּמִכׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָלֹה וְכַחֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** be aware that with regard to **all of the** other **transgressions in the Torah, punishment is exacted** only **from** the transgressor; whereas **here,** punishment is exacted **from him and from the entire world, as it is stated: "Swearing, and lying,** and murdering, and stealing, and committing adultery, they break all bounds…Therefore, the land mourns, and everyone who dwells therein languishes" (Hosea 4:2–3).

Further still, the *braisa* identifies a third and even more expansive consequence: whereas for all other sins only the sinner bears punishment, a false oath brings suffering upon the entire world. This is the meaning of the world's trembling at the utterance of *lo tisa* — because swearing falsely in God's name represents a fundamental violation of the trust placed in God as witness.

The proof text is drawn from *Hoshea* 4:2: *alah vechachesh veratzoach veganov vena'of paratzu udamim bedamim naga'u* — false swearing, dishonesty, murder, theft, and adultery have burst forth and blood touches upon blood. The verse continues: *al ken te'evel ha'aretz ve'umlal kol yoshev bah bechayat hasadeh uve'of hashamayim vegam degei hayam ye'aseifu* — therefore the land mourns and all who dwell therein are bereaved, including the beasts of the field, the birds of the sky, and even the fish of the sea.

One might object that this verse lists a whole litany of sins, not only false oaths. The *gemara* addresses this by pointing to a parallel verse in *Yirmiyahu*: *mipnei alah avelah ha'aretz* — because of the oath the land mourns. This verse employs the exact same language of mourning as the verse in *Hoshea*, thereby drawing a direct line connecting the cosmic mourning of the land specifically to the sin of false swearing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא עַד דְּעָבֵיד לְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ; דִּכְתִיב: ״מִפְּנֵי אָלָה אָבְלָה הָאָרֶץ״, וּכְתִיב: ״עַל כֵּן תֶּאֱבַל הָאָרֶץ וְאֻמְלַל כׇּל יוֹשֵׁב בָּהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And** why not **say** that punishment is not exacted from the entire world **unless he commits all of** the sins mentioned in the verse? The Gemara answers: This should **not enter your mind, as it is written: "Because of swearing the land mourns"** (Jeremiah 23:10), indicating that taking a false oath is sufficient to cause the land to mourn. **And it is** similarly **written** in the verse in Hosea: **"Therefore, the land mourns, and everyone who dwells therein languishes."** Both verses employ a term of mourning.

### Immediate Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה – אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ זְכוּת, תּוֹלִין לוֹ שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת; וְכָאן נִפְרָעִין מִמֶּנּוּ לְאַלְתַּר – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹצֵאתִיהָ נְאֻם ה׳ צְבָאוֹת, וּבָאָה אֶל בֵּית הַגַּנָּב וְאֶל בֵּית הַנִּשְׁבָּע בִּשְׁמִי לַשָּׁקֶר, וְלָנֶה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ וְכִלַּתּוּ וְאֶת עֵצָיו וְאֶת אֲבָנָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues with the judges' forewarning: **And** be aware that with regard to **all the** other **transgressions in the Torah, if** the transgressor **has merit,** God **suspends his** punishment for **two or three generations,** and only if his descendants follow in his ways are they punished. Whereas **here, punishment is exacted from him immediately, as it is stated:** "This is the curse that goes forth over the face of the whole land…**I cause it to go forth, says the Lord of hosts, and it shall enter into the house of the thief, and into the house of he who swears falsely by My name; and it shall abide in the midst of his house and shall consume it, with its timber and its stones"** (Zechariah 5:3–4).

For all other sins, the *braisa* states, if the sinner possesses merit, punishment may be deferred for two or three generations. But for a false oath, punishment comes immediately — *nifra'im mimenu le'altar*. The proof text is from *Zechariah*: *hotzeitia neum Hashem tzevaot uvah el beit haganav ve'el beit hanishba bishmi lashaker* — I will eject it, says the Lord of Hosts, and it will come to the house of the thief and to the house of one who swears falsely in My name. *Hotzeitia* — I will eject — connotes immediacy, *le'altar*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הוֹצֵאתִיהָ״ – לְאַלְתַּר. ״וּבָאָה אֶל בֵּית הַגַּנָּב״ – זֶה הַגּוֹנֵב דַּעַת הַבְּרִיּוֹת; שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מָמוֹן אֵצֶל חֲבֵירוֹ, וְטוֹעֲנוֹ וּמַשְׁבִּיעוֹ. ״וְאֶל בֵּית הַנִּשְׁבָּע בִּשְׁמִי לַשָּׁקֶר״ – כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ. ״וְלָנֶה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ וְכִלַּתּוּ וְאֶת עֵצָיו וְאֶת אֲבָנָיו״ – הָא לָמַדְתָּ, דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין אֵשׁ וּמַיִם מְכַלִּין אוֹתָן, שְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר מְכַלָּה אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* analyzes the verse: **"I cause it to go forth"** means **immediately. "And it shall enter into the house of the thief"; this** is referring to **one who deceives people,** e.g., one **who has no money in the possession of another, but claims** money from **him and administers an oath to him** in court, thereby causing an oath to be taken in vain. **"And into the house of he who swears falsely by My name"** is **as it indicates,** in accordance with its straightforward meaning. From the end of the verse: **"And it shall abide in the midst of his house and shall consume it, with its timber and its stones," you have therefore learned** that **a false oath consumes things that** even **fire and water do not consume,** such as stones.

The *gemara* interprets *beit haganav* — the house of the thief — as referring to the one who practices *geneivat da'at*, that is, deception of others. The classic illustration here is of someone who makes a financial claim against another, who, uncertain of the facts, finds himself compelled to swear in order to extricate himself from the demanded payment. The one making the false claim has engaged in *geneivat da'at* in the most consequential sense — misrepresenting facts for financial gain and weaponizing the *shevua* to that end.

### Metaphor of Fire

The verse concludes: *velanah betoch beito vechilat'o ve'et etzav ve'et avanav* — and the sin will dwell within his house and consume it along with its timber and its stones. The *gemara* draws a remarkable lesson from this: *devarim she'ein esh ochaltan shvuat sheker mechala otan* — things that even fire cannot consume, a false oath will consume them. Even stone and timber, which resist ordinary destruction, are not immune to the devastation wrought by a false oath. The *avon* — the sin — dwells within the house and destroys everything within it.

## Oath Administration

### Right to Refuse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אָמַר: ״אֵינִי נִשְׁבָּע״ – פּוֹטְרִין אוֹתוֹ מִיָּד. וְאִם אָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי נִשְׁבָּע״ – הָעוֹמְדִין שָׁם אוֹמְרִים זֶה לָזֶה: ״סוּרוּ נָא מֵעַל אׇהֳלֵי הָאֲנָשִׁים הָרְשָׁעִים הָאֵלֶּה וְגוֹ׳״. וּכְשֶׁמַּשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתוֹ אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: הֱוֵי יוֹדֵעַ, שֶׁלֹּא עַל דַּעְתְּךָ אָנוּ מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתְךָ, אֶלָּא עַל דַּעַת הַמָּקוֹם וְעַל דַּעַת בֵּית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **If** the defendant **says** at this point: **I will not take an oath,** the court **dismisses him immediately,** and rules him liable to pay. **And if he says: I will take an oath, the** people **standing there say to each other: "Depart, I pray you, from the tents of these wicked men,** and touch nothing of theirs, lest you be swept away in all their sins" (Numbers 16:26). **And when** the judges **administer the oath to him, they say to him: Be aware that we administer an oath to you not according to your understanding** of the oath, **but according to the** objective **understanding of the Omnipresent and according to the understanding of the court,** i.e., the judges' intention.

After instilling all of this fear and awe in the oath-taker, if the person says *eini nishba* — I will not take the oath — the court immediately releases him: *potrin oso mi-yad*. There is no penalty for refusing. Indeed, the procedure is to wrap up the matter quickly at that point. *Rashi* and *Tosafos* discuss whether he could retract his refusal, but the practice is to conclude proceedings swiftly so that no such reversal occurs.

### Distancing from Evil

If, having heard all of this, the person nevertheless declares *ha-reini nishba* — I will proceed with the oath — those present respond by distancing themselves, reciting: *suru na me-al ohalei ha-anashim ha-reshaim ha-eleh* — turn away from the tents of these evil men. This is striking: even the one who demands the oath is characterized alongside the oath-taker as belonging to the category of *resha'im*. The very act of taking a *shevua* — even if it may well be truthful — carries so enormous a moral and spiritual weight that all those in proximity must recoil. The longstanding Jewish practice of avoiding *shevuos* and *nedarim* flows from precisely this sensibility.

### Judicial Warning

When the oath is actually administered, the court declares: *hevei yodea she-lo al datcha anu mashbi-in oscha ela al da'as ha-Makom ve-al da'as Beit Din* — be aware that we are not imposing this oath according to your own private understanding, but according to the intent of God and the intent of the court. The concern is that the oath-taker might frame his words so that they sound like one thing while technically meaning something else in his own mind.

### No Reservations

This warning forecloses all attempts at mental reservation or self-serving reinterpretation. Whatever semantic sleight of hand the oath-taker might attempt to perform internally — whatever qualifying gloss he places on the words in his own mind — is irrelevant. The oath is constituted by what God means and what the court means by those words, not by whatever the oath-taker privately intends.

### Moses Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, כְּשֶׁהִשְׁבִּיעַ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל, אָמַר לָהֶן: דְּעוּ, שֶׁלֹּא עַל דַּעְתְּכֶם אֲנִי מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֶתְכֶם, אֶלָּא עַל דַּעַת הַמָּקוֹם וְעַל דַּעְתִּי; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא אִתְּכֶם לְבַדְּכֶם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as we** have **found** written **with regard to Moses, our teacher. When he administered an oath to the Jewish people** in the plains of Moab so that they would accept the Torah upon themselves, **he said to them: Know that** it is **not according to your understanding that I administer an oath to you, but according to the understanding of the Omnipresent and according to my understanding. As it is stated: "Neither with you only** do I make this covenant and this oath" (Deuteronomy 29:13), which is homiletically interpreted to mean: Not only according to your intention.

The *braisa* cites a precedent for this practice in the behavior of Moshe Rabbeinu. When Moshe administered the oath to Yisrael at *Arvot Moav*, he similarly declared: *de'u she-lo al datchaschem ani mashbi-a eschem ela al da'as ha-Makom ve-al da'ati* — know that it is not according to your own understanding that I am administering this oath, but according to the intent of God and my own intent. The proof text is: *ve-lo itchem levadchem anochi koreis es ha-bris ha-zos ki es asher yeshno poh* — and it is not with you alone that I am making this covenant, but with all those who stand here. The phrase *ve-lo itchem* — not with you — is read as signaling that what you privately have in mind does not determine the content of the covenant.

### Future Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי אֶת אֲשֶׁר יֶשְׁנוֹ פֹּה״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אוֹתָן הָעוֹמְדִין עַל הַר סִינַי; דּוֹרוֹת הַבָּאִים וְגֵרִים הָעֲתִידִין לְהִתְגַּיֵּיר – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאֵת אֲשֶׁר אֵינֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** Having quoted a verse, the *baraita* tangentially interprets the subsequent verse. From the phrase: **"But with he who stands here** with us this day" (Deuteronomy 29:14), **I have** derived **only** that **those who stood at Mount Sinai** were included in this covenant. **From where** do I derive that **the subsequent generations, and the converts who will convert in the future,** were also included? **The verse states: "And also with he who is not here** with us this day" (Deuteronomy 29:14).

The *gemara* then continues to expound the verse. *Ki es asher yeshno poh* — this might be taken to mean only those physically present at the covenant ceremony. But from where do we know that it includes future generations? The verse continues: *ve-es asher eineno* — and even those who are not here — establishing that all future generations were included in the *kabbalat* *mitzvos* at Sinai and *Arvot Moav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא מִצְוָה שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ עֲלֵיהֶם מֵהַר סִינַי; מִצְווֹת הָעֲתִידוֹת לְהִתְחַדֵּשׁ, כְּגוֹן מִקְרָא מְגִילָּה – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״קִיְּמוּ וְקִבְּלוּ״ – קִיְּימוּ מַה שֶּׁקִּבְּלוּ כְּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And I have** derived **only** that the **mitzvot that** the Jewish people **accepted upon themselves at Mount Sinai** were included in the oath. **From where** is it derived that **mitzvot that were to be initiated in the future, for example, the reading of the Megilla,** the Scroll of Esther, on Purim, were also included? **The verse states:** "The Jews **ordained and took** upon themselves…that they would keep these two days" (Esther 9:27), which is homiletically interpreted to mean: **They ordained,** in the generation of Esther, mitzvot **that they had already accepted** upon themselves by oath in the plains of Moab.

The *gemara* further asks: from where do we know that the concept of *kabbalat mitzvos* — the covenantal acceptance — extended even to commandments that would only come into existence later, such as *mikra megilla*? The answer is derived from the phrase *kimu vekiblu* — they confirmed and accepted. At the time of the *megilla*, Yisrael reaffirmed and confirmed what had previously been accepted, extending the scope of covenantal obligation even to God's presence as experienced in a more hidden mode, and to all future *mitzvos* that would subsequently emerge.

## Language & Severity

### Language Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אַף הִיא בִּלְשׁוֹנָהּ נֶאֶמְרָה״?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **What** is the precise meaning of the statement: An oath imposed by the judges **may also** be **recited in its language?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדִתְנַן: אֵלּוּ נֶאֱמָרִין בְּכׇל לְשׁוֹן – פָּרָשַׁת סוֹטָה, וִידּוּי מַעֲשֵׂר, קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, וּתְפִלָּה, וּבִרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן, וּשְׁבוּעַת הָעֵדוּת, וּשְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן. וְקָאָמַר נָמֵי: שְׁבוּעַת הַדַּיָּינִין – אַף הִיא בִּלְשׁוֹנָהּ נֶאֶמְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Sota* 32a): **These are recited in any language** and it is not required that they be recited in Hebrew: **The portion of** the warning and the oath administered by the priest to **a woman suspected by her husband of having been unfaithful [*sota*]; the declaration of tithes,** which occurs after the third and the sixth year of the seven-year Sabbatical cycle, when one declares that he has given his tithes appropriately; **the recitation of *Shema*; and** the *Amida* **prayer; and Grace after Meals; and the oath of testimony,** where one takes an oath that he does not have any testimony to provide on a given issue; **and the oath on a deposit,** where one takes an oath that he does not have possession of another's deposit. All these may be recited in any language. **And** the *baraita* **also states,** as an addendum to this *halakha*, that **an oath** imposed **by the judges may also** be **recited in its language,** i.e., in any language.

The *gemara* now returns to examine the opening phrase of the *braisa*: *shevuat hadayanim af hi bilshonam ne'emra* — the oath of the judges may also be said in any language. The word *af* — also — implies a comparison to something else. The *gemara* explains that this is a reference to the *mishna* in *Sota*, which lists those things that may be said in any language, as opposed to those which must specifically be said in Hebrew. Among the items on that list the *mishna* in *Sota* includes *parshat sotah*, *viduy ma'aser*, *krias shema*, *tefilla*, *birchat hamazon*, *shvuat ha-eidus*, and *shvuat hapikadon*. The *mishna* there did not include *shevuat hadayanim*, and the *braisa* therefore comes to add it — *af* — she too may be said in any language.

### Relative Severity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר, אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: הֱוֵי יוֹדֵעַ, שֶׁכׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ נִזְדַּעְזַע בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: ״לֹא תִשָּׂא אֶת שֵׁם ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ לַשָּׁוְא״. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִתְיְהֵב בְּסִינַי – עֶשֶׂר דִּבְּרוֹת נָמֵי אִתְיְהֵב!
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in the *baraita* above that the judges **say to him: Be aware that the entire world trembled when the Holy One, Blessed be He, said: "You shall not take the name of the Lord, your God, in vain." What is the reason** that the entire world trembled? **If we say** it was **because** this prohibition **was given at** Mount **Sinai,** this is difficult, as when the entire world trembled, the rest of the **Ten Commandments were also given** at Mount Sinai. This quality is not unique to this specific prohibition.

The *gemara* then addresses the underlying reason why the world trembled at the utterance of *lo tisa*. It might seem that the trembling was simply because this commandment was given at Sinai — but the *gemara* notes that the entire *Aseret HaDibrot* were given at Sinai, and yet we do not say that the world trembled at each of them. Alternatively, perhaps the trembling reflects the particular severity of *lo tisa*. But here the *gemara* points to a *mishna* that categorizes the transgressions: the lighter ones are positive and negative commandments excluding *lo tisa*, while the weightier category includes *krisos*, *misos beit din*, and *lo tisa* alongside them. So *lo tisa* is certainly weightier than ordinary negative commandments, yet it is not weightier than *karet* and *misas beit din*. There are many sins carrying those penalties, and yet the world did not tremble specifically at their utterance. What, then, accounts for the trembling?

## Language & Severity (II)

### Why Trembling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא – וְכׇל עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״וְנַקֵּה״, וְכָאן נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא יְנַקֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the reason** the world trembled particularly when this prohibition was given is **as it is taught** subsequently in the *baraita*: **And with regard to all of the** other **transgressions in the Torah it is stated: "And will…clear the guilty," whereas here, it is stated: "Will not hold guiltless."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה לֹא נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״לֹא יְנַקֶּה״?! וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְנַקֵּה לֹא יְנַקֶּה״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is it not stated with regard to all of the** other **transgressions in the Torah** that God **"will not hold guiltless [*lo yenakkeh*]"** one who transgresses? **But isn't it written: "And Who will by no means clear the guilty [*venakkeh lo yenakkeh*]"** (Exodus 34:7)?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹמַר ״וְנַקֵּה״ – שֶׁכְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא יְנַקֶּה״; אִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹמַר ״לֹא יְנַקֶּה״ – שֶׁכְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״וְנַקֵּה״. הָא כֵּיצַד? מְנַקֶּה הוּא לַשָּׁבִים, וְאֵינוֹ מְנַקֶּה לְשֶׁאֵינָן שָׁבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** verse is **necessary for** that which is derived through the homiletic interpretation **of Rabbi Elazar, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Elazar says: It is not possible to say: And will clear the guilty [*venakkeh*],** about all transgressions, **since: Will not clear the guilty [*lo yenakkeh*], is already stated.** And **it is not possible to say: Will not clear the guilty [*lo yenakkeh*], since: And will clear the guilty [*venakkeh*], is already stated. How** can **these** texts be reconciled? The Holy One, Blessed be **He, clears those** guilty ones **who repent and does not clear those who do not repent.**

The *gemara* resolves the question by returning to what the *braisa* itself says: the reason the world trembled is not that *lo tisa* is the weightiest transgression in the Torah — it is not, since *karet* and *misas beit din* are weightier in their prescribed punishment — but rather because there is categorically no *kapara* for it. The verse says *lo yenakeh*, and that absence of any qualifying *nakeh* sets *shevuat shav* apart from every other transgression. Rabbi Elazar addresses the tension in the text: it is impossible to read *venakeh* as meaning God fully cleanses, since the same verse also says *lo yenakeh*; and it is impossible to read *lo yenakeh* as meaning God never forgives at all, since the same verse also says *venakeh*. The resolution is: *menakeh hu lashavim ve'eino menakeh leshainam shavim* — God forgives those who repent but does not forgive those who do not. For all other transgressions there is some measure of *nakeh* even without complete *teshuva*. Here, there is none without it. That is why the world trembled.

### Family vs. Accessory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה נִפְרָעִין מִמֶּנּוּ, וְכָאן מִמֶּנּוּ וּמִמִּשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ. וְכׇל עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה מִמִּשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ לָא?!
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated in the *baraita* that with regard to **all of the transgressions in the Torah, punishment is exacted** only **from** the transgressor, whereas **here,** punishment is exacted **from him and from his family.** The Gemara asks: **And** is punishment **not** exacted **from** the transgressor's **family** with regard to **all of the** other **transgressions in the Torah?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְשַׂמְתִּי אֲנִי אֶת פָּנַי בָּאִישׁ הַהוּא וּבְמִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ״; וְתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אִם הוּא חָטָא, מִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ מָה חָטָאת? לוֹמַר לְךָ: אֵין לְךָ מִשְׁפָּחָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ מוֹכֵס – שֶׁאֵין כּוּלָּהּ מוֹכְסִין; וְשֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ לִסְטִים – שֶׁאֵין כּוּלָּהּ לִסְטִים; מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּחַפִּין עָלָיו!
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it written** in the Torah with regard to one who worships Molech: **"Then I will set My face against that man, and against his family,** and I will cut him off" (Leviticus 20:5)? **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon said: If he sinned, how did his family sin?** Why are they punished? This serves **to tell you** that **there is no family that has** an unauthorized **tax collector among them** in **which all of** the family members **are not** regarded as unauthorized **tax collectors, and** similarly, there is no family **that has a bandit [*listim*] among them** in **which all of** the family members **are not** regarded as **bandits.** This is **because they cover for him.** Evidently, punishment is exacted from the transgressor's family with regard to transgressions other than taking a false oath.

The *gemara* challenges this: is it really true that families are never punished for other sins? The verse in connection with *avodah zarah* and the sin of giving one's child to *Molech* states: *ve-samti ani es panai ba-ish hahu u-v'mishpachto* — I will set My face against that man and his family.

### Family Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם בְּדִינָא אַחֲרִינָא, הָכָא בְּדִינָא דִּידֵיהּ; כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״וְהִכְרַתִּי אוֹתוֹ״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשַׂמְתִּי אֲנִי אֶת פָּנַי״ – יָכוֹל כׇּל הַמִּשְׁפָּחָה כּוּלָּהּ בְּהִיכָּרֵת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹתוֹ״ – אוֹתוֹ בְּהִיכָּרֵת, וְלֹא כׇּל הַמִּשְׁפָּחָה כּוּלָּהּ בְּהִיכָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** with regard to other transgressions, the transgressor's family is punished **with another punishment,** less severe than the one the transgressor receives, whereas **here,** with regard to a false oath, the transgressor's family is punished **with his punishment. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Why** must **the verse state** with regard to one who worships Molech: **"And I will cut him off"? Since it is stated** earlier in the verse: **"Then I will set My face** against that man, and against his family," one **might** have thought that **the entire family** is liable **to** be punished with ***karet*.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And I will cut **him** off," indicating that only **he** is liable **to** be punished with ***karet*,** whereas **his entire family is not** liable **to** be punished with ***karet*.**

A *braisa* is cited in which Rabbi Shimon asks: if he sinned, what did the family sin? The answer: there is no family that has a *mocheis* — a tax collector engaged in extortion — in which the entire household is not implicated. Family members cover for him, make excuses on his behalf, assist him actively or by their silence. The *gemara* applies the parallel principle here: there is no family that harbors a bandit in which all members are not in some way implicated, *mipnei shemechaphin alav* — because they cover for him.

The *gemara* then draws the critical distinction. In those other cases — *avodah zarah* and similar transgressions — the family is punished in a different and lesser capacity: as accessories, bearing a derivative punishment commensurate with their complicity. But in the case of a false oath, the family receives the same punishment as the sinner himself, not merely an accessory penalty. This is the distinctive feature: not that the family is punished at all, but that it is punished equally.

A *braisa* is cited in which Rebbe expounds the verse *ve-hikrasti oso* — and I will destroy him. Why does the Torah need to add this given the prior mention of the family? Because the prior verse, *u-v'mishpachto*, might suggest that the entire family receives *karet*. Therefore the Torah adds *oso* — him specifically receives *karet*, but not the entire family. The upshot is that in other *aveiros* the family receives only an accessory and lesser punishment, while in the case of a false oath the punishment that falls on the family is equal to that of the sinner himself, even as *karet* remains the exclusive penalty of the individual sinner alone.

## Fear & Consequences (II)

### Cleanse Doctrine

The textual tension embedded in the phrase *nakeh lo yenakeh* — which appears to simultaneously assert both that God cleanses and that God does not cleanse — is resolved, as noted above, through the doctrine of *teshuva*. Rabbi Elazar's teaching establishes that neither reading can stand alone: *venakeh* cannot mean unconditional forgiveness, nor can *lo yenakeh* mean absolute non-forgiveness in all circumstances. The reconciliation is that God cleanses those who repent — *menakeh hu lashavim* — and does not cleanse those who do not — *ve'eino menakeh leshainam shavim*. What sets *shevuat shav* apart from all other transgressions is not that repentance is powerless — for no sin is beyond the reach of genuine *teshuva* — but rather that without *teshuva*, there is no cleansing whatsoever, no partial mitigation, no accumulated merit that can defer or soften the consequence. That is the meaning of the unreserved *lo yenakeh*, and that is why the world trembled.
