# Sanhedrin 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fox & Wolf Parables

### Intro & Rashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת מִשְׁלוֹת שׁוּעָלִים הָיוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְאָנוּ אֵין לָנוּ אֶלָּא שָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **three hundred parables of foxes, and we have only three.**

The Gemara at the top of 39a concludes its discussion of Rabbi Meir by noting that although he composed three hundred parables involving foxes, we possess only three. These three parables are introduced cryptically — without context or narrative — and a careful reading of Rashi is necessary before any of them makes sense.

Rashi on the opening words — *avot yochlu boser* — explains that the three parables are embedded within a single story: the fox tricked the wolf into entering a Jewish quarter on *erev Shabbat* in order to help prepare the Shabbat meal and eat with them. When the wolf arrived and the townspeople set upon him with clubs, he turned to the fox in alarm. The fox reassured him: "They are not beating you for your own sins. They are beating you because of your father, who once came here, began helping them prepare the meal, and then ate all the choice portions himself, leaving nothing." The wolf protested — *uvishvil aba ani loke?* — "For my father's sin I am being beaten?" The fox replied simply: yes. This is the first parable: *avot yochlu boser v'shinei banim tikhenah* — "the fathers ate sour fruit and the teeth of the sons became dull." The phenomenon by which a parent's actions visit consequences upon a child is ancient and deeply human, and here it is given a parable's concise, unsettling form.

### Sour Fruit Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָבוֹת יֹאכְלוּ בֹסֶר וְשִׁנֵּי בָנִים תִּקְהֶינָה״, ״מֹאזְנֵי צֶדֶק אַבְנֵי צֶדֶק״, ״צַדִּיק מִצָּרָה נֶחֱלָץ וַיָּבֹא רָשָׁע תַּחְתָּיו״.
>
> **Translation:** And they are the parables concerning the following verses: **"The fathers have eaten sour grapes, and the children's teeth are set on edge"** (Ezekiel 18:2); **"Just balances, just weights**…shall you have" (Leviticus 19:36); and **"The righteous is delivered out of trouble, and the wicked comes in his stead"** (Proverbs 11:8).

Having delivered this explanation, the fox continues: come with me, I will show you a place where you can eat and be satisfied. This transition leads directly into the second and third parables, both of which are contained within the same escapade at the well.

The identification of the wolf as the consuming, ungrateful figure and the fox as the clever one is precisely the conceit our rabbis are drawing on. Yet even accepting this, the reader is left with a question: how exactly did the fox manage this trick, and how does it connect to the remaining two *psukim*?

### Well Bucket Story

The fox leads the wolf to a well. Over the mouth of the well lies a piece of wood from which a rope is suspended, with a bucket tied to each end — the classic two-bucket pulley system, in which the weight of a descending bucket draws the other upward. The fox climbs into the upper bucket. His weight carries him down. As he descends, the other bucket rises. He calls up to the wolf: there is meat and cheese down here — eat and be satisfied! To make the claim convincing, he points to the reflection of the moon in the water below, which shimmers like a round of cheese.

The wolf asks how he can descend. Simple, says the fox: climb into the upper bucket. The moment the wolf — heavier than the fox — steps in, his weight carries him down and the fox rises up. Now the wolf is trapped at the bottom of the well. He calls out: how do I get back up? And the fox, now safely at the top, quotes him the *pasuk* from Mishlei: *tzadik mitzara nechelatz v'yavo rasha tachtav* — "the righteous man is rescued from trouble, and the evildoer comes under him." *Lav kach ktiv?* Is that not what is written? This is the third parable.

The second parable — *moznei tzedek avnei tzedek*, scales of justice and weights of justice — is also woven into this moment, referring to the precise calibration by which the scheme was arranged and executed.

### Maharsha Stones

The Maharsha, writing in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, raises a practical difficulty with the story: the fox weighs less than the wolf. Left to his own weight alone, would the fox even succeed in pulling the bucket down far enough to go to the bottom? And once down, if the wolf chooses not to get in, how does the fox get back out? The fox would be trapped regardless.

The Maharsha answers that the fox placed two stones in his bucket before descending — stones whose combined weight with the fox exceeded the fox's weight alone, but was still less than the wolf's weight. This is the *moznei tzedek*, the precise calibration of weights. If the wolf cooperated, his weight would bring the fox up. If the wolf refused, the fox could remove one of the stones, and the remaining stone — heavier than the fox himself — would tip the balance and raise the fox out. It is a clever and self-insuring arrangement, and the Maharsha's observation makes the ingenuity of the scheme complete.

## Heretic Challenges

### God Thief Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ קֵיסָר לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: אֱלֹהֵיכֶם גַּנָּב הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּפֵּל ה׳ אֱלֹהִים תַּרְדֵּמָה עַל הָאָדָם וַיִּישָׁן״. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ בְּרַתֵּיה: שִׁבְקֵיהּ, דַּאֲנָא מַהְדַּרְנָא לֵיהּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: תְּנוּ לִי דּוּכּוֹס אֶחָד. אֲמַר לַהּ: לְמָה לִיךְ? לִיסְטִין בָּאוּ עָלֵינוּ הַלַּיְלָה וְנָטְלוּ מִמֶּנּוּ קִיתוֹן שֶׁל כֶּסֶף וְהִנִּיחוּ לָנוּ קִיתוֹן שֶׁל זָהָב. אָמַר לָהּ: וּלְוַואי שֶׁיָּבֹאוּ עָלֵינוּ בְּכׇל יוֹם. וְלֹא יָפֶה הָיָה לוֹ לְאָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן, שֶׁנָּטְלוּ מִמֶּנּוּ צֵלָע אַחַת וְנָתְנוּ לוֹ שִׁפְחָה לְשַׁמְּשׁוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **The** Roman **emperor said to Rabban Gamliel: Your God is a thief, as it is written: "And the Lord God caused a deep sleep to fall upon the man and he slept;** and He took one of his sides, and closed up the place with flesh instead" (Genesis 2:21). **The daughter of** the emperor **said to** Rabban Gamliel: **Leave him, as I will respond to him. She said** to her father: **Provide one commander [*dukhus*] for me** to avenge someone's wrongdoing. The emperor **said to her: Why do you need** him? She said to him: **Armed bandits came to us this** past **night, and took a silver jug [*kiton*] from us, and left a golden jug for us.** The emperor **said to her:** If so, **would it be that** armed bandits such as these **would come to us every day.** She said to him: **And was it not** similarly **good for Adam the first** man **that** God **took a side from him and gave him a maidservant to serve him?**

We now move squarely onto 39a, which presents ten exchanges between a *kofer* — understood by the commentators as the emperor or Roman ruler — and various *Tanna'im* and *Amora'im*. Each exchange reflects a genuine theological challenge rooted in a misreading or provocative reading of a *pasuk*, and each response turns the challenge on its head.

The first challenge is addressed to Rabban Gamliel. A *kofer* declares: your God is a thief. The proof text: *vayapel Hashem Elokim tardemah al ha'adam vayishan* — God caused a deep sleep to fall on Adam and he slept. While Adam slept, God took his rib and fashioned woman from it. Stealing, the challenger insists, while the victim is unconscious.

Before Rabban Gamliel can respond, his interlocutor's own daughter intervenes. She says to Rabban Gamliel: sit, I will answer him. She then demands a judge from her father, alarming him. Why do you need a judge? Because, she says, robbers came to us last night. They took away a vessel of silver and left behind a vessel of gold. Her father replies: *ulevai sheyavo aleinu bechol yom* — would that such robbers came to us every day! She turns to him: was it not the same for Adam? They took one rib and gave him a *shifchah leshamsho*, a partner to serve him. He lost nothing and gained everything.

The emperor is not fully satisfied. *Hachi kamina*, he says — I understand the point, but the issue is not the exchange itself; it is that it was done covertly. Why did God not do this openly while Adam was awake?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לַהּ: הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא, אֶלָּא לִשְׁקְלַיהּ בְּהֶדְיָא? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אַיְיתוֹ לִי אוּמְצָא דְּבִישְׂרָא. אַיְיתוֹ לַהּ. אוֹתְבַהּ תּוּתֵי בַּחְשָׁא, אַפִּיקְתַּהּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אֱכוֹל (מֵהַאי). אֲמַר לַהּ: מְאִיסָא לִי. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: וְאָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן נָמֵי, אִי הֲוָת שְׁקִילָה בְּהֶדְיָא, הֲוָה מְאִיסָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The emperor **said to her: This is what I was saying: But** if it is good for Adam, **let** God **take** his side from him **in the open,** not during the time of his deep sleep, like a thief. **She said to him: Bring me** a slice of **raw meat. They brought it to her. She placed it under the embers,** and **removed it** after it was roasted. **She said to him: Eat from this** meat. The emperor **said to her: It is repulsive to me.** Although he knew that this is how meat is prepared, seeing the raw meat made it repulsive to him. **She said to him:** With regard to **Adam the first** man **as well, had** God **taken her** from him **in the open, she would have been repulsive to him.** Therefore God acted while Adam was asleep.

The daughter's response is as elegant as it is practical. She asks that a piece of raw meat be brought. She prepares it over the fire and coals, and presents it to her father. He recoils: *me'isa li* — it is revolting to me. She says: had Adam watched the entire process — the taking of the rib, the fashioning of flesh and bone — it would have been equally repulsive to him. It was better that it was done in concealment. This answer effectively closes the challenge.

### God Location Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ קֵיסָר לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: יָדַעְנָא אֱלָהַיְיכוּ מַאי קָא עָבֵיד וְהֵיכָן יָתֵיב. אִיתְנְגִיד וְאִיתְּנַח. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בֵּן אֶחָד יֵשׁ לִי בִּכְרַכֵּי הַיָּם וְיֵשׁ לִי גִּיעֲגוּעִים עָלָיו. בָּעֵינָא דְּמַחְוֵית לֵיהּ נִיהֲלִי. אָמַר: מִי יָדַעְנָא הֵיכָא נִיהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּאִיכָּא בְּאַרְעָא לָא יָדְעַתְּ, דְּאִיכָּא בִּשְׁמַיָּא יָדְעַתְּ?
>
> **Translation:** **The emperor said to Rabban Gamliel: I know your God, what He does and where He sits.** Meanwhile, the emperor **was moaning and groaning.** Rabban Gamliel **said to him: What** is **this?** Why are you in distress? The emperor **said to him: I have one son in the cities overseas and I miss him.** Rabban Gamliel said to him: **I want you to show him to me.** The emperor **said: Do I know where he is?** Rabban Gamliel **said to him:** If **you do not know that which is on earth,** is it possible that **you do know that which is in the heavens?**

The second challenge, again to Rabban Gamliel. The emperor says: I know what your God is doing — and where He sits. Having made this boast, he then becomes visibly restless and sighs. Rabban Gamliel asks: what is the matter? The emperor replies: I have one son somewhere across the sea, and I miss him greatly. Rabban Gamliel says: show him to me. The emperor replies: how would I know where he is? He is somewhere in the world. Rabban Gamliel responds: *de'ika be'ara lo yad'at, de'ika bishmaya yad'at?* — what is happening on the earth you do not know, and yet you claim to know what is happening in the heavens?

### Stars Counting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ קֵיסָר לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: כְּתִיב ״מוֹנֶה מִסְפָּר לַכּוֹכָבִים״, מַאי רְבוּתֵיהּ? אֲנָא מָצֵינָא לְמִימְנֵי כּוֹכְבֵי! אַיְיתִי חֲבוּשֵׁי, שְׁדִינְהוּ בְּאַרְבֵּילָא, וְקָא מְהַדַּר לְהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְנִינְהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אוֹקְמִינְהוּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רָקִיעַ נָמֵי הָכִי הָדְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The emperor said to Rabban Gamliel: It is written** in praise of the Lord: **"He counts the number of the stars;** He gives them all their names" (Psalms 147:4). **What is His greatness? I can** also **count the stars.** Rabban Gamliel **brought quinces, put them in a sieve, and spun them. He said** to the emperor: **Count them.** The emperor **said to him: Stand them still** so that I can count them. Rabban Gamliel **said to him:** The **firmament also revolves like this,** therefore you cannot count the stars in it.

Third challenge. The emperor says to Rabban Gamliel: it is written that your God can count the stars. What is so remarkable about that? *Ana mtzana lemeiman kochvei* — I too can count the stars. The practical difficulty with refuting this claim is obvious: if someone tells you there are twenty-three billion stars, you cannot immediately contradict them. You cannot count them on the spot.

Rabban Gamliel responds with a demonstration rather than an argument. He produces quinces and throws them into a spinning mixer, then turns to the emperor and says: *minyaneihu* — count them. The emperor says: stop the mixer and I will count them. Rabban Gamliel replies: *rakia nami hachi hadra* — the sky too is turning while you are counting. If you cannot count fruit whirling in a basin before your eyes, how do you propose to count the stars as the heavens revolve?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, הָכִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְנִי לִי כּוֹכְבֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵימָא לִי כַּכָּיךְ וְשִׁינָּיךְ כַּמָּה הָוֵי? שְׁדָא יְדֵיהּ לְפוּמֵּיהּ וְקָא מָנֵי לְהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּאִיכָּא בְּפוּמָּיךְ לָא יָדְעַתְּ, דְּאִיכָּא בִּרְקִיעָא יָדְעַתְּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **this is** what the emperor **said to him: I have counted the stars.** Rabban Gamliel **said to him: Tell me how many teeth and incisors you** have. The emperor **put his hand in his mouth and was counting them.** Rabban Gamliel **said to him: You do not know what is in your mouth,** but **you do know what is in the firmament?**

An alternative version of this exchange is recorded: the emperor challenges Rabban Gamliel to count the stars. Rabban Gamliel turns it around and asks: tell me first how many teeth and molars are in your mouth. The emperor immediately puts his hand to his mouth and begins counting. Rabban Gamliel says: *deika b'pumeich lo yad'at, deika b'rakia yad'at?* — what is inside your own mouth you do not know, and yet you claim to know what is in the heavens?

### Creators Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ קֵיסָר לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: מִי שֶׁבָּרָא הָרִים לֹא בָּרָא רוּחַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״כִּי הִנֵּה יוֹצֵר הָרִים וּבֹרֵא רוּחַ״. אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, גַּבֵּי אָדָם דִּכְתִיב ״וַיִּבְרָא״ ״וַיִּיצֶר״, הָכִי נָמֵי מִי שֶׁבָּרָא זֶה לֹא בָּרָא זֶה?
>
> **Translation:** **The emperor said to Rabban Gamliel: He Who created mountains did not create wind,** rather two separate gods created them, **as it is stated: "For, lo, He forms mountains and creates wind"** (Amos 4:13); one is described with the verb "forms," and the other with the verb "creates." Rabban Gamliel said to him: **If that is so,** then **with regard to Adam, as it is written** concerning him: **"And** God **created"** (Genesis 1:27), and also: **"And** the Lord God **formed"** (Genesis 2:7), **so too** should one say that **He who created this did not create that?**

The fourth challenge introduces a more philosophically layered claim. The emperor says to Rabban Gamliel: the one who created the mountains did not create the wind. The proof text comes from the prophet Amos: *ki hinei yotzer harim u'vorei ruach* — "for behold, He who forms the mountains and creates the wind." Two different verbs are used — *yatzar* and *bara* — and the challenger reads this as evidence of two distinct creators: one who fashioned the mountains and one who created the wind.

The distinction between these verbs is significant. *Yetzira* — formation — describes shaping something from pre-existing material. *Bri'ah* — creation — describes bringing something into existence from nothing, an act uniquely available to God. People can be *yotzrim*, artisans and creators in a derived sense, but they are not called *bor'im*. That capacity belongs to God alone. The challenger's point rests on reading the two verbs as marking two separate agents rather than two distinct modes of the same divine creative act.

Rabban Gamliel responds: if this is so, then consider what is written about the creation of the human being — both *vayivra* and *vayitzer* appear. Does that mean two different creators fashioned Adam? The Gemara understands the *bri'ah* as producing the raw material — something from nothing — and the *yetzira* as shaping that material into human form. But the challenger's logic, if pressed, would require multiple creators for the human being.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טֶפַח עַל טֶפַח יֵשׁ בּוֹ בָּאָדָם, וּשְׁנֵי נְקָבִים יֵשׁ בּוֹ. מִי שֶׁבָּרָא זֶה לֹא בָּרָא זֶה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֲנֹטַע אֹזֶן הֲלֹא יִשְׁמָע וְאִם יֹצֵר עַיִן הֲלֹא יַבִּיט״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּבִשְׁעַת מִיתָה כּוּלָּן נִתְפַּיְּיסוּ?
>
> **Translation:** If you will claim that different gods created different parts of Adam, that will not suffice. **A person has one handbreadth by one handbreadth** of facial countenance, with **two** types of **orifices in it,** eyes and ears. Should one say that **He who created this did not create that; as it is stated: "He that planted the ear, shall He not hear? He that formed the eye, shall He not see?"** (Psalms 94:9)? The verse employs two verbs for the eyes and ears alone. The emperor **said to him: Yes,** different gods created different parts of the face. Rabban Gamliel **said to him: And at the moment of death, are they all appeased?** Do all these gods agree as one that the time arrived for the person to die?

Rabban Gamliel presses the absurdity further: *tefach al tefach yesh bo b'adam u'shnei nekavim yesh bo* — a human being is constructed composite, from part upon part, with multiple openings. Do you really mean that each was created by a separate being? He brings a *pasuk* from Tehillim: *hanotea ozen halo yishma, v'im yotzer ayin halo yabit* — "He who plants the ear, does He not hear? He who forms the eye, does He not see?" Does this mean the one who planted the ear and the one who formed the eye are different persons? The emperor confirms: yes, that is what I mean.

Rabban Gamliel then delivers the decisive line: *u'vishkat mita kulan nitpaisu?* — when the time comes for a human being to die, did all these various creators make peace with one another and agree that now is the moment? If there are multiple creators each responsible for a different organ or faculty, what happens at death? The composite would collapse incoherently, with each part departing on its own schedule. Yet people die as whole beings. This observation — delivered in one line — refutes both the Greco-Roman polytheism with its pantheon of non-omnipotent gods and the emerging Christian theology of distinct powerful divine figures, all in a single argument.

### Dualism Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא אַמְגּוּשָׁא לְאַמֵּימָר: מִפַּלְגָךְ לְעִילַּאי דְּהוֹרְמִיז, מִפַּלְגָךְ לְתַתַּאי דְּאַהוֹרְמִיז. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, הֵיכִי שָׁבֵיק לֵיהּ אַהוֹרְמִיז לְהוֹרְמִיז לְעַבּוֹרֵי מַיָּא בְּאַרְעֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** A certain magus said to Ameimar: From your midpoint and up** is in the domain **of Hurmiz,** the god of good, who created the significant and important parts of the body, and **from your midpoint and down** is in the domain **of Ahurmiz,** the god of bad. Ameimar **said to him: If so, how does Ahurmiz allow Hurmiz to urinate in his territory?** A person drinks with his mouth, which is in his upper half, and urinates from below.

A *magush* — Rashi glosses this as a *mechashef*, a sorcerer, and some understand it as a Persian priest — says to Ameimar: from your waist upward, you belong to Ormazd; from your waist downward, you belong to Ahurmiz. This is a Zoroastrian dualism in which two distinct divine powers govern different domains. Applied to the human body, the upper half belongs to one god and the lower half to another.

Ameimar's refutation is immediate and practical: *i hachi, heichi shabkeih Ahurmiz le-Hurmiz l'avorei maya be-ar'eih?* — if so, how does Ahurmiz permit Ormazd's territory to pass water through its domain? One god controls the intake, the other controls the output, and they must share the body's infrastructure. This is not a tenable arrangement. The argument dissolves the dualist claim by pointing to the body's functional unity.

### Unity Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ קֵיסָר לְרַבִּי תַּנְחוּם: תָּא לִיהְווֹ כּוּלָּן לְעַמָּא חַד. אֲמַר: לְחַיֵּי, אֲנַן דְּמָהֲלִינַן לָא מָצֵינַן מִיהְוֵי כְּוָותַיְיכוּ. אַתּוּן מָהֲלִיתוּ וֶהֱווֹ כְּוָותַן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵימָר שַׁפִּיר קָאָמְרַתְּ, מִיהוּ כֹּל דְּזָכֵי לְמַלְכָּא לִשְׁדְּיוּהּ לְבֵיבָר. שַׁדְיוּהּ לְבֵיבָר וְלָא אַכְלוּהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא: הַאי דְּלָא אַכְלוּהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא כְּפוּן הוּא. שַׁדְיוּהּ לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ וְאַכְלוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The emperor said to Rabbi Tanḥum: Come, let us all be one people.** Rabbi Tanḥum **said: Very well.** But **we, who are circumcised, cannot become** uncircumcised **as you** are; **you** all **circumcise** yourselves **and become like us.** The emperor **said to** Rabbi Tanḥum: In terms of the logic of your **statement, you are saying well, but anyone who bests the king** in a debate **is thrown to the enclosure [*labeivar*]** of wild animals. **They threw him to the enclosure but** the animals **did not eat him,** as God protected him. **A certain heretic said to** the emperor: **This** incident, **that they did not eat him,** happened **because they are not hungry. They** then **threw the** heretic into the enclosure **and** the animals **ate him.**

The emperor says to Rabban Tanchum: come, let us all be one people. The proposal sounds generous. Rabban Tanchum replies with a smile: we would gladly oblige, but there is one small obstacle — *anan de-mahalinan lo matzinan mihvei kevataichu*. We practice circumcision and cannot simply become like you. However, you are welcome to become like us: *atu mahlitu ve-hevu kevatan*.

The emperor pronounces this an excellent answer — and then adds that anyone who bests the king in argument will be thrown to the animals. Rabban Tanchum is cast into the arena, and the animals do not eat him. A *min* who is watching says: the animals are simply not hungry — that is why he was spared. So they throw the *min* in, and the animals devour him. The parallel to the story of Abraham and to the story of Daniel is not lost on the Gemara.

### Shekhinah Minyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ קֵיסָר לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: אָמְרִיתוּ כֹּל בֵּי עַשְׂרָה שְׁכִינְתָּא שָׁרְיָא, כַּמָּה שְׁכִינְתָּא אִיכָּא? קַרְיֵיהּ לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ, מְחָא בֵּיהּ בְּאַפָּתְקָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי עָל שִׁמְשָׁא בְּבֵיתֵיהּ דְּקֵיסָר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שִׁמְשָׁא אַכּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא נָיְחָא. וּמָה שִׁמְשָׁא דְּחַד מִן אֶלֶף אַלְפֵי רִבּוֹא שַׁמָּשֵׁי דְּקַמֵּי קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא נָיְחָא אַכּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, שְׁכִינְתָּא דְּקוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The emperor said to Rabban Gamliel: You say** that **the Divine Presence dwells** in **any place where there are ten** adult male Jews. He asked, sarcastically: **How many Divine Presences are there?** Rabban Gamliel **summoned the servant** of the emperor and **hit him on his neck [*be'appatka*].** Rabban Gamliel **said to him: Why** did you allow **the sun** to **enter the house of the emperor? The emperor said to him: The sun rests upon all the world;** no one can prevent it from shining. Rabban Gamliel said to him: **And if the sun, which is one of ten thousand attendants that are before the Holy One, Blessed be He, rests upon all the world, the Divine Presence of the Holy One, Blessed be He, all the more so** rests upon the world.

A *kofer* challenges Rabban Gamliel: you say that wherever ten people assemble — wherever there is a *minyan* — the *shekhinah* dwells. How many *shekhinah*s can there be? If every *minyan* that gathers has its own *shekhinah*, the number becomes impossibly large.

Rabban Gamliel calls over the emperor's servant and strikes him across the back. The emperor demands an explanation. Rabban Gamliel says: I am upset because you let the sun into the emperor's house. The emperor replies: the sun shines across the entire world — how could we keep it out? Exactly, says Rabban Gamliel. If the sun — which is but one among thousands upon thousands of creations of the *kadosh baruch hu* — can illuminate the whole world simultaneously, then *al achat kama ve-kama*: all the more so can the *shekhinah* of the *kadosh baruch hu*, which is not a physical object, be present in every place at once. The argument also makes a subtler point: Rabban Gamliel demonstrates that even a physical entity can be present everywhere, which makes the omnipresence of a non-physical divine presence that much more comprehensible.

### Yechezkel Suffering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אֱלֹהֵיכֶם גַּחֲכָן הוּא, דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ לִיחֶזְקֵאל ״שְׁכַב עַל צִדְּךָ הַשְּׂמָאלִי״, וּכְתִיב ״וְשָׁכַבְתָּ עַל צִדְּךָ הַיְמָנִי״! אֲתָא הָהוּא תַּלְמִידָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא דִּשְׁבִיעֲתָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַשְׁתָּה אָמֵינָא לְכוּ מִילְּתָא דְּשָׁוְיָא לְתַרְוַיְיכוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain heretic said to Rabbi Abbahu: Your God is a jester, as He said to Ezekiel** the prophet: **"Lie on your left side"** (Ezekiel 4:4), **and** it **is** also **written: "Lie on your right side"** (Ezekiel 4:6); God had Ezekiel turn from side to side, apparently for comic effect. In the meantime, **a certain student came** before Rabbi Abbahu and **said to him: What is the reason for** the mitzva of **the Sabbatical** Year? Rabbi Abbahu **said to them: Now I will tell you something that is fit for the two of you.**

A *min* challenges Rabbi Abbahu: your God is inconsistent — He says to Yechezkel "lie on your left side," yet elsewhere it is written "you shall lie on your right side." What does He want?

At the same moment, a student arrives and asks Rabbi Abbahu: what is the reason for *shemitah*? Rabbi Abbahu responds: I will now give you an answer that addresses both questions — though he notes in advance that it will connect Yechezkel to *shemitah* while still leaving much unsaid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: זִרְעוּ שֵׁשׁ וְהַשְׁמִיטוּ שֶׁבַע, כְּדֵי שֶׁתֵּדְעוּ שֶׁהָאָרֶץ שֶׁלִּי הִיא. וְהֵן לֹא עָשׂוּ כֵּן, אֶלָּא חָטְאוּ וְגָלוּ. מִנְהָגוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם: מֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁסָּרְחָה עָלָיו מְדִינָה – אִם אַכְזָרִי הוּא, הוֹרֵג אֶת כּוּלָּן; אִם רַחְמָן הוּא, הוֹרֵג חֶצְיָם; אִם רַחְמָן מָלֵא רַחֲמִים הוּא, מְיַיסֵּר הַגְּדוֹלִים שֶׁבָּהֶן בְּיִסּוּרִין. אַף כָּךְ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְיַיסֵּר אֶת יְחֶזְקֵאל כְּדֵי לְמָרֵק עֲוֹנוֹתֵיהֶם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu continued: **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to the Jewish people: Sow** for **six** years, **and withhold** sowing during the **seventh** year, **so that that you will know that the land is Mine. But** the Jewish people **did not do so; rather, they sinned and were** consequently **exiled. The manner of the world** is that in the case of **a flesh-and-blood king whose province sinned against him, if he is cruel, he kills them all; if he is compassionate, he kills** only **half of them;** and **if he is compassionate** and **is full of compassion, he afflicts the leaders among them with suffering.** Rabbi Abbahu continues: **So** too in **this** case, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, afflicts Ezekiel in order to cleanse the sins of the Jewish people.** God instructed him to lie down and suffer the same number of days as the number of years that the Jewish people did not observe the *halakhot* of the Sabbatical Year.

Here is the answer. God said to Israel: sow for six years and let the land rest on the seventh, so that you will know that the land is Mine. They did not do so; they sinned and were exiled. Now consider how an earthly king responds to a rebellious province. If he is cruel, he kills everyone. If he is merciful, he kills half. If he is full of compassion, he singles out the great ones among them and inflicts suffering upon them alone. That is what the *kadosh baruch hu* did: all of Israel had transgressed in the matter of *shemitah*, but He afflicted only Yechezkel — *meyaser et Yechezkel* — in order to cleanse the transgressions of Israel.

This raises a profound and difficult question. Is this the trope of the suffering servant — a single righteous person bearing the burden of collective sin? The concept exists in our tradition: the suffering of the righteous can carry atoning weight for the many. But there are important distinctions. In this reading, Yechezkel is not an innocent party sacrificed in the people's place; he is the *gadol* among them, the leader of the *galut*, arguably the most responsible figure among those who failed to uphold *shemitah*, and his suffering reflects that responsibility. This differs from the Christian theological claim in which the atoning figure is entirely innocent and wholly outside the community of sinners. Here the connection between Yechezkel's leadership and the nation's failure is precisely what makes his suffering meaningful rather than arbitrary. The restlessness and discomfort described in the account of Yechezkel may itself be a reflection of that burden — the leader of exile carrying the weight of what exile represents.

### Priest Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אֱלֹהֵיכֶם כֹּהֵן הוּא, דִּכְתִיב ״וְיִקְחוּ לִי תְּרוּמָה״. כִּי קַבְרֵיהּ לְמֹשֶׁה, בְּמַאי טְבֵיל? וְכִי תֵּימָא בְּמַיָּא, וְהָכְתִיב ״מִי מָדַד בְּשׇׁעֳלוֹ מַיִם״?
>
> **Translation:** **A certain heretic said to Rabbi Abbahu: Your God is a priest, as it is written: "That they take for Me an offering [*teruma*]"** (Exodus 25:2), and *teruma* is given to the priests. He asked, sarcastically: **When He buried Moses, in what** ritual bath **did He immerse?** A priest who contracts impurity from a corpse must immerse in order to be able to partake of *teruma*. **And if you would say** that He immersed **in water, but isn't it written: "Who has measured the waters in the hollow of His hand"** (Isaiah 40:12), that all waters of the world fit in the palm of God, so He could not immerse in them.

A *min* says to Rabbi Abbahu: your God is a *kohen*. The proof: *v'yikchu li terumah* — "and they shall take for Me *terumah*." If God receives *terumah*, He must be a *kohen*. But a *kohen* who buries a corpse becomes *tamei* and must immerse. Since God buried Moshe — *ki kavrei l'Moshe* — what *tevilah* did He perform afterward? And if you say He immersed in water, the *pasuk* from Yeshayahu refutes that: *mi madad b'sha'alo mayim* — God is so vast that the waters of the entire world would barely cover His hand. There is no body of water sufficient for such an immersion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּנוּרָא טְבֵיל, דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי הִנֵּה ה׳ בָּאֵשׁ יָבוֹא״. וּמִי סָלְקָא טְבִילוּתָא בְּנוּרָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְּרַבָּה, עִיקַּר טְבִילוּתָא בְּנוּרָא הוּא, דִּכְתִיב ״וְכׇל אֲשֶׁר לֹא יָבֹא בָּאֵשׁ תַּעֲבִירוּ בַמָּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu **said to him: He immersed in fire, as it is written: "For, behold, the Lord will come in fire"** (Isaiah 66:15). The heretic said to him: **But is immersion in fire effective?** Rabbi Abbahu **said to him: On the contrary, the main** form of **immersion is in fire, as it is written** with regard to the removal of non-kosher substances absorbed in a vessel: **"And all that abides not the fire you shall make to go through the water"** (Numbers 31:23), indicating that fire purifies more than water does.

Rabbi Abbahu responds: *b'nura tavil* — He immersed in fire. The proof text: *ki hinei Hashem ba'esh yavo* — "for behold, God comes in fire."

The *min* objects: can fire really serve as a valid medium for immersion? Rabbi Abbahu replies: *adraba* — on the contrary. *Ikar tviluta b'nura* — immersion in fire is the superior form. The proof comes from Bamidbar, where the Israelites are instructed on purifying the vessels they acquired from Midian: *v'chol asher lo yavo ba'esh ta'aviru va-mayim* — anything that cannot pass through fire shall pass through water. The implication is clear: fire is the preferred and primary medium; water is the fallback for what cannot withstand fire. Rabbi Abbahu does not reject the premise that God is a *kohen* or that He buried Moshe. He simply explains how the necessary purification was accomplished.

### Nations Uniqueness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי אֲבִינָא: כְּתִיב ״וּמִי כְעַמְּךָ כְּיִשְׂרָאֵל גּוֹי אֶחָד בָּאָרֶץ״, מַאי רְבוּתַיְיהוּ? אַתּוּן נָמֵי עָרְבִיתוּ בַּהֲדַן, דִּכְתִיב ״כׇּל הַגּוֹיִם כְּאַיִן נֶגְדּוֹ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִדִּידְכוּ אַסְהִידוּ עֲלַן, דִּכְתִיב
>
> **Translation:** **A certain heretic said to Rabbi Avina: It is written: "And who is like Your people, Israel, one nation in the earth"** (II Samuel 7:23). The heretic asked: **What is your greatness? You are also mixed together with us, as it is written: "All nations before Him are as nothing;** they are counted by Him less than nothing and vanity" (Isaiah 40:17). Rabbi Avina **said to him: One of yours,** the gentile prophet Balaam, **has** already **testified for us, as it is written:**

A *min* says to Rabbi Avina: it is written *mi k'amcha Yisrael goy echad ba'aretz* — "who is like your people Israel, one nation in the land?" What is so distinctive about you? On one hand, you are a *goy* like any other nation. On the other hand, the *pasuk* says *kol hagoyim k'ayin negdo* — all nations are as nothing before Him. Which is it? Are you elevated or are you, together with all nations, simply nothing?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבַגּוֹיִם לֹא יִתְחַשָּׁב״.
>
> **Translation:** "It is a people that shall dwell alone, **and shall not be reckoned among the nations"** (Numbers 23:9), teaching that where the verse mentions "the nations," the Jewish people are not included.

One of the most striking features of this exchange is Rabbi Avina's response, or rather the response embedded in the Gemara's resolution. The answer is not to choose one side of the tension but to hold both. Israel is a *goy* — a people, a nation — and in that sense shares a category with all nations. And Israel is *goy echad*, uniquely one, set apart in a way that Bilam himself proclaimed: *uvagoyim lo yitchashav* — it shall not be reckoned among the nations. The power of the Jewish teaching, as is observed here, lies precisely in this refusal to collapse the tension. If the answer were only one way, something would be lost. Because it is both, something is preserved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״טוֹב ה׳ לַכֹּל״, וּכְתִיב ״טוֹב ה׳ לְקֹוָויו״. מָשָׁל לְאָדָם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ פַּרְדֵּס: כְּשֶׁהוּא מַשְׁקֶה – מַשְׁקֶה אֶת כּוּלּוֹ, כְּשֶׁהוּא עוֹדֵר – אֵינוֹ עוֹדֵר אֶלָּא טוֹבִים שֶׁבָּהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related statement: **Rabbi Elazar raises a contradiction: It is written** in one verse: **"The Lord is good to all** and His compassion is over all His works" (Psalms 145:9), **and it is written** in a different verse: **"The Lord is good to those who wait for Him,** to the soul that seeks Him" (Lamentations 3:25). He explains: This can be **compared to a person who has an orchard. When he waters** it, **he waters all of it.** Perforce, the water reaches all parts of the orchard, and he cannot discriminate between trees. This is the meaning of the verse: "The Lord is good to all." By contrast, **when he hoes** around the trees, **he hoes only** around **the good** trees **among them.** Similarly, when God bestows goodness to the world, all receive it. When He protects people from harm, not all are protected.

Rabbi Elazar then juxtaposes two *psukim* that seem to contradict each other. One says *tov Hashem lakol* — God is good to all. Another says *tov Hashem lekovav* — God is good to those who hope in Him. The *shoresh* *kuf-vav-heh* carries the connotations of gathering, hope, and waiting — *tikvah*, hope, and *mikveh*, a gathering of waters, share this root. The parable offered to resolve this: a person who has an orchard. When he waters, he waters the whole orchard — everyone benefits equally. But when he tills and tends to each tree individually, he does not tend to all of them indiscriminately; he gives particular care only to the good ones, the ones that will bear fruit. So too with God: His general sustenance extends to all creation, but His intimate, individualized attention is directed toward those who seek and hope in Him.

## Ahab Downfall

### Witness Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ כָּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד כּוּ׳. ״וַיַּעֲבֹר הָרִנָּה בַּמַּחֲנֶה״. אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: ״בַּאֲבֹד רְשָׁעִים רִנָּה״ – בַּאֲבוֹד אַחְאָב בֶּן עָמְרִי רִנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Therefore,** since all humanity descends from one person, **each and every** person is obligated to say: The world was created for me. The mishna then teaches that the court says to the witnesses: And perhaps you will say: Why would we want to be responsible for the blood of this person? But be aware, as is it not already stated: "When the wicked perish, there is song" (Proverbs 11:10)? The Gemara teaches: A separate verse relates that after the evil king Ahab was killed: **"And the song went throughout the camp"** (I Kings 22:36). **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina says:** The fact that the verse states "the song," and not a song, indicates that it is referring to a song mentioned elsewhere. Accordingly, the verse: **"When the wicked perish, there is song,"** alludes to this episode: **When Ahab ben Omri perished,** there was **song.**

The Mishnah at 37a closes the chapter with the statement: *lefikach kol echad ve'echad chayav lomar bishvili nivra ha'olam* — therefore every single person is obligated to say, for my sake the world was created. This is connected to the testimony requirement in capital cases: witnesses might think, what is all this trouble to us? But the Torah says you must testify. And witnesses might also think, why should we be responsible for this person's blood? The Mishnah addresses this by citing the *pasuk* from Mishlei: *ba'avod resha'im rinah* — when evildoers are lost, there is joy. The implied message to potential witnesses is that when testimony leads to the conviction and death of a genuine evildoer, this is not a cause for guilt but for *rinah*.

This *pasuk* now becomes the entry point for the Gemara on 39b, which picks up the word *rinah* and subjects it to close analysis.

### No Joy Rejoicing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי חָדֵי קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא בְּמַפַּלְתָּן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים? הָכְתִיב: ״בְּצֵאת לִפְנֵי הֶחָלוּץ וְאֹמְרִים הוֹדוּ לַה׳ כִּי לְעוֹלָם חַסְדּוֹ״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא נֶאֱמַר בְּהוֹדָאָה זוֹ ״כִּי טוֹב״? לְפִי שֶׁאֵין הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שָׂמֵחַ בְּמַפַּלְתָּן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is the Holy One, Blessed be He, gladdened by the downfall of the wicked? Isn't it written** in the verse describing the victory of the Jewish people in battle: "He appointed them that should sing unto the Lord, and praise in the beauty of holiness, **as they went out before the army, and say: Give thanks to the Lord, for His mercy endures forever"** (II Chronicles 20:21). **And Rabbi Yonatan says: For what** reason **is it not stated in this praise:** "Give thanks to the Lord **for He is good,** for His mercy endures forever," as is stated elsewhere, e.g., Psalms 118:1? This is **because the Holy One, Blessed be He, is not gladdened by the downfall of the wicked.**

The Gemara notes a similar word in the account of King Ahab's death in the book of Melachim: *vaya'avor harinah bamachaneh* — a proclamation of joy passed through the camp. But the text does not say *vaya'avor rinah*; it says *vaya'avor ha-rinah*, with the definite article *heh*. For our commentators, a *heh* here is significant — it can function as a reference to *Hashem*.

Rabbi Avicha bar Chanina teaches: *ba'avod resha'im rinah* — when evildoers are lost, there is joy — and the example given is the loss of Ahab ben Omri. One might then ask: is this plural *resha'im* justified when we are speaking of a single person? Some explain that it includes Ahab's wife Izevel and the rest of his household, and extends — as we learned in the context of Kayin and Hevel — to his offspring as well.

But a serious problem immediately arises. *U'mi chadei Kudsha Brich Hu bemapalatan shel resha'im?* — Does God actually rejoice at the downfall of evildoers? This is theologically fraught. The Gemara brings a *pasuk* from Divrei Hayamim: *betzet lifnei hachalutz ve'omrim hodu Lashem ki le'olam chasdo* — when the armies went out, they said, give thanks to *Hashem*, for His kindness endures forever. The formulation conspicuously omits the usual phrase *ki tov* — "for He is good." Rabbi Yochanan explains: *mipnei mah lo ne'emar behoda'ah zu ki tov?* — Why was *ki tov* not said in this thanksgiving? Because *ein Hakadosh Baruch Hu same'ach bemapalatan shel resha'im* — the *kadosh baruch hu* does not rejoice at the downfall of evildoers.

### Angels Song

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וְלֹא קָרַב זֶה אֶל זֶה כׇּל הַלָּיְלָה״? בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה בִּקְּשׁוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לוֹמַר שִׁירָה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא. אָמַר לָהֶן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: מַעֲשֵׂה יָדַי טוֹבְעִין בַּיָּם וְאַתֶּם אוֹמְרִים שִׁירָה לְפָנַי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **As Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥman says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written** in the passage describing the splitting of the Red Sea: **"And the one came not near the other all the night"** (Exodus 14:20)? **At that time the ministering angels desired to recite a song before the Holy One, Blessed be He. The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to them: My handiwork,** i.e., the Egyptians, **are drowning in the sea, and you are reciting a song before Me?** Apparently, God is not gladdened by the downfall of the wicked.

The same principle is illustrated in one of the most famous *aggadot* in the Talmud, cited here by Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. At the crossing of the sea, it is written: *velo karav zeh el zeh kol halaila* — the forces did not come close to one another all night. During that night of separation, the *mal'achei hasharet* came before the *kadosh baruch hu* to sing a song of praise. God said to them: *ma'aseh yadai tove'im bayam ve'atem omrim shira lefanai?* — My creations are drowning in the sea and you wish to sing a song before Me?

There is considerable discussion in the commentaries about which *shirah* is meant here — whether it is the *Az Yashir* that Israel sang at the sea, or whether it refers to the *Hallel*, and which contexts of incomplete *Hallel* trace back to this moment. These are rich threads, but the core teaching is clear: God's sorrow at the death of His creations — even the Egyptians who oppressed Israel — precluded a posture of celebration.

### Hif'il Grammar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: הוּא אֵינוֹ שָׂשׂ, אֲבָל אֲחֵרִים מֵשִׂישׂ. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דִּכְתִיב ״יָשִׂישׂ״ וְלָא כְּתִיב ״יָשׂוּשׂ״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says: He,** i.e., God, **does not rejoice** in their downfall, **but He** does **cause others** to feel **joy.** The Gemara comments: Accordingly, the language of the verse **is also precise, as it is written:** "And it shall come to pass, that as the Lord rejoiced over you to do you good, and to multiply you; so the Lord **will cause rejoicing [*yasis*]** over you to cause you to perish" (Deuteronomy 28:63), **and it is not written: Will have joy [*yasus*].** The term "*yasis*" connotes causing joy to others, not that God will experience joy Himself. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** this inference that it is so.

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina adds a grammatical precision that sharpens the point. He teaches: *hu eino sas, aval acherim masis* — He Himself does not rejoice, but He causes others to rejoice. The proof lies in the verb form. The text uses *yasis* — the *hif'il*, causative stem — rather than *yasus*, the simple *kal* form. In the *hif'il*, the subject causes something in others rather than experiencing it directly. God is in the causative position: He brings about joy in others at the passing of evildoers, but He does not Himself rejoice.

This creates a carefully calibrated theological position. The Mishnah is correct that there is *rinah* — joy — when evildoers are lost. The Gemara in Pirkei Avot is also correct that one should not rejoice when one's enemy falls. Both teachings are true simultaneously. As is observed here: whenever one asks whether a Jewish teaching points this way or that way, the answer is almost invariably yes. The capacity to hold two truths in tension without forcing a resolution is one of the most characteristic features of Jewish thought, and this discussion is a striking illustration of that disposition.

### Ahab Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַזֹּנוֹת רָחָצוּ וְגוֹ׳״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לְמָרֵק שְׁתֵּי חֶזְיוֹנוֹת, אַחַת שֶׁל מִיכָיְהוּ וְאַחַת שֶׁל אֵלִיָּהוּ. בְּמִיכָיְהוּ כְּתִיב: ״אִם שׁוֹב תָּשׁוּב בְּשָׁלוֹם לֹא דִבֶּר ה׳ בִּי״; בְּאֵלִיָּהוּ כְּתִיב: ״בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר לָקְקוּ הַכְּלָבִים אֶת דַּם נָבוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the death of Ahab, the Gemara cites another verse from that passage. The verse states: "And they washed the chariot by the pool of Samaria, and the dogs licked up his blood; **and the harlots also washed** themselves there, according to the word of the Lord that He spoke" (I Kings 22:38). **Rabbi Elazar says:** "The harlots [*hazzonot*]" can be read as visions [*ḥezyonot*], and the verse means: **To cleanse,** i.e., to fulfill, **two visions, one of Micaiah and one of Elijah.** This fulfills a vision **of Micaiah,** as it **is written: "If you return at all in peace, the Lord has not spoken by me"** (I Kings 22:28). Once Ahab died, Micaiah's vision was fulfilled. This also fulfills a vision **of Elijah,** as it **is written: "In the place where the dogs licked the blood of Naboth** shall the dogs lick your blood, even yours" (I Kings 21:19).

Since the Gemara has introduced Ahab in this context, it now turns to explore a peculiarity in the narrative of his death. The text in Melachim tells us that Ahab was killed by a stray arrow — *v'ish mashach b'keshet l'tumo vayakeh* — someone drew his bow randomly and the arrow found Ahab. The word *l'tumo* is ambiguous. Rabbi Elazar reads it simply: *lefi tumo* — innocently, without aim or intention. But Rava reads it differently: *letamem* — to complete, to bring to wholeness — because *tam* carries the sense of completion, as in *Yaakov ish tam*.

Rava's reading connects to a deeper problem: at the time of Ahab's death, two prophecies about him appeared to contradict one another. Michayahu had prophesied that Ahab would fall somewhere other than where Eliyahu had prophesied — Eliyahu had designated the place where the dogs had licked Navot's blood, while Michayahu's prophecy pointed elsewhere. Geographically, one points toward the Gilad region to the southeast of the Kinneret, and the other toward Shomron, the capital of Ahab's kingdom. Rava reads the stray arrow — *l'tumo* — as the instrument by which both prophecies were simultaneously fulfilled.

### Zonot Interpretations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: זוֹנוֹת מַמָּשׁ. אַחְאָב אִישׁ מְצוּנָּן הָיָה, וְעָשְׂתָה לוֹ אִיזֶבֶל שְׁתֵּי צוּרֵי זוֹנוֹת בְּמֶרְכַּבְתּוֹ, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּרְאֶה אוֹתָן וְיִתְחַמֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The verse is speaking of **actual harlots. Ahab was a frigid man,** who did not lust for women. **And Jezebel,** his wife, **fashioned two forms of harlots for him in his chariot, in order that he see them** while traveling **and become aroused.** Accordingly, one would understand the verse as stating that the harlots were washed, not that they washed themselves.

The verse describing Ahab's death includes the phrase *v'hazonot rachatzu* — the *zonot* washed. The surface meaning (*peshat*) is that harlots washed in the pool where Ahab's blood-drenched chariot was cleansed, signifying a degraded and ignominious end for the king: his blood was licked by dogs and washed away by prostitutes.

Rabbi Eliezer, however, reads *zonot* not as prostitutes but as *hezyonot* — visions, prophecies. His reading: the washing at the pool of Shomron served to cleanse, to reconcile, the two contradictory *hezyonot* — the vision of Michayahu and the vision of Eliyahu. The chariot carried his blood from the place where he fell to Shomron, allowing the blood to be licked by dogs at the location Eliyahu had designated while also arriving at the Shomron pool that Michayahu's prophecy had pointed to. Both prophecies were thus upheld.

Rabba, by contrast, maintains the plain sense: *zonot mamash* — real prostitutes. He explains that Ahab, whose name in later Hebrew is associated with being *metsunan* — cold, frigid — had little interest in intimate relations. Izevel, his wife, had placed images of harlots on his chariot in the hope of arousing him. These are the *zonot* of the verse.

## Ovadiah Stories

### Blessing Lineage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּקְרָא אַחְאָב אֶל עֹבַדְיָהוּ אֲשֶׁר עַל הַבָּיִת וְעֹבַדְיָהוּ הָיָה יָרֵא [אֶת] ה׳ מְאֹד״. מַאי קָאָמַר קְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** § Before continuing its discussion of Ahab, the Gemara records **a mnemonic** for the forthcoming statements: **Called; and merited; of Edom.** It **is written: "And Ahab called Obadiah, who was over the household; now Obadiah feared the Lord greatly"** (I Kings 18:3). The Gemara asks: **What is the verse saying?** What is the connection between Obadiah's fear of the Lord and the call of Ahab?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אָמַר לֵיהּ, בְּיַעֲקֹב כְּתִיב ״נִחַשְׁתִּי וַיְבָרְכֵנִי ה׳ בִּגְלָלֶךָ״, בְּיוֹסֵף כְּתִיב ״וַיְבָרֶךְ ה׳ אֶת בֵּית הַמִּצְרִי בִּגְלַל יוֹסֵף״. בֵּיתָא דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא לָא הֲוָה מִיבְּרִיךְ. שֶׁמָּא לֹא יְרֵא אֱלֹהִים אַתָּה? יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: ״וְעֹבַדְיָהוּ הָיָה יָרֵא אֶת ה׳ מְאֹד״, אֲבָל בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁל אַחְאָב אֵינוֹ מְזוּמָּן לִבְרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says** that Ahab **said to** Obadiah: It **is written with regard to Jacob:** "And Laban said to him: **I have observed the signs, and the Lord has blessed me for your sake"** (Genesis 30:27). It **is written with regard to Joseph: "The Lord blessed the Egyptian's house for Joseph's sake"** (Genesis 39:5). **The house of that man,** i.e., my house, **was not blessed. Perhaps you do not fear God?** Immediately, **a Divine Voice emerged and said: "Now Obadiah feared the Lord greatly," but the house of Ahab is not fit for blessing** like the houses of those masters.

Since the Gemara has been discussing Ahab, it turns now to an episode that illuminates his relationship with his servant Ovadiah. The *pasuk* reads: *vayikra Achav el Ovadyahu asher al habayit ve'Ovadyahu haya yere Hashem me'od* — Ahab called to Ovadiah who was over his household, and Ovadiah feared God greatly. The word *me'od* — greatly — will carry significant weight.

The question the Gemara raises is: what is the narrative connection between the fact that Ahab calls Ovadiah and the fact that Ovadiah fears God greatly? They appear as two unrelated facts placed side by side without explanation.

Rabbi Yitzchak supplies the subtext of what Ahab said to Ovadiah. Ahab was invoking a lineage of blessing. In the story of Yaakov, Lavan acknowledged: *nichashti vayivarchani Hashem biglalecha* — I have divined that *Hashem* blesses me on your account; because you are with me I am blessed. In the story of Yosef, it is written: *vayivarech Hashem et beit hamitzri biglal Yosef* — *Hashem* blessed the Egyptian household because of Yosef. Ahab was therefore saying to Ovadiah: you fear God greatly, so the house of anyone who shelters you should be blessed as Lavan's was blessed through Yaakov and as Potipha's house was blessed through Yosef. But my house is not being blessed — *beiteih dehai gufra lo hava mivarech*. Perhaps you are not truly fearing God as I thought.

Ahab's construction of this lineage is deliberate. The kingdom of Israel rested over the territories of Ephraim and Menashe, the sons of Yosef. Ahab was positioning himself as the successor to the Yaakov–Yosef line rather than to the Davidic line from Yehudah, establishing a claim to dynastic blessing through Ovadiah's presence in his house.

The *bat kol* responded: *ve'Ovadyahu haya yere et Hashem me'od* — Ovadiah indeed feared *Hashem* greatly. The blessing, however, would not reach Ahab's house. *Aval beito shel Achav eino mezuman livracha* — Ahab's household is simply not positioned to receive blessing. No amount of Ovadiah's righteousness could overcome the weight of Ahab's own conduct.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: גָּדוֹל שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בְּעוֹבַדְיָהוּ יוֹתֵר מִמַּה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר בְּאַבְרָהָם, דְּאִילּוּ בְּאַבְרָהָם לָא כְּתִיב ״מְאֹד״, וּבְעוֹבַדְיָהוּ כְּתִיב ״מְאֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba says:** The praise **that is stated with regard to Obadiah** is **greater than that which is stated with regard to Abraham. As with regard to Abraham** the verse states: "For now I know that you fear God" (Genesis 22:12), and the term **"greatly" is not written, and about Obadiah** the term **"greatly" is written.**

Rava adds a further dimension: the greatness attributed to Ovadiah in this verse actually exceeds what was said of Avraham. In the verse describing Avraham's fear of God, the word *me'od* does not appear; in the verse about Ovadiah it does. Yet even this extraordinary level of *yirat Hashem* was insufficient to unlock blessing for the house of Ahab.

### Ovadiah Prophets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִפְּנֵי מָה זָכָה עוֹבַדְיָהוּ לִנְבִיאוּת? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֶחְבִּיא מֵאָה נְבִיאִים בַּמְּעָרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי בְּהַכְרִית אִיזֶבֶל אֵת נְבִיאֵי ה׳ וַיִּקַּח עֹבַדְיָהוּ מֵאָה נְבִיאִים וַיַּחְבִּיאֵם חֲמִשִּׁים אִישׁ בַּמְּעָרָה וְגוֹ״. מַאי שְׁנָא חֲמִשִּׁים אִישׁ? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִיַּעֲקֹב לָמַד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה הַמַּחֲנֶה הַנִּשְׁאָר לִפְלֵיטָה״. רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מְעָרָה מַחְזֶקֶת יוֹתֵר מֵחֲמִשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: For what** reason **did Obadiah merit prophecy?** It is **because he concealed one hundred prophets in a cave, as it is stated: "It was so, when Jezebel cut off the prophets of the Lord, that Obadiah took one hundred prophets, and hid them, fifty men in a cave,** and fed them with bread and water" (I Kings 18:4). The Gemara asks: **What is different,** i.e., why did he decide, to conceal **fifty men** in each of two caves and not conceal them all together in one cave? **Rabbi Elazar says: He learned from** the behavior of **Jacob** to do so, **as it is stated:** "And he said: If Esau comes to the one camp and smites it, **then the camp that is left shall escape"** (Genesis 32:9). Obadiah learned from this to divide the prophets and thereby lessen the danger. **Rabbi Abbahu says:** It is **because there is no cave** big enough **to contain more than fifty** people.

The Gemara now examines the role Ovadiah played during Izevel's campaign against the prophets of *Hashem*. The *pasuk* recounts: when Izevel was cutting down *Hashem*'s prophets, Ovadiah took a hundred prophets, hid them fifty to a cave, and provided them with bread and water.

Why fifty per cave rather than all one hundred in a single place? Rabbi Elazar answers: *mi'Yaakov lamad* — he learned this from Yaakov. When Yaakov approached danger from Esav, he divided his camp into two, so that if one was struck the other might survive: *vehayah hamachaneh hanishar lifleitah*. By dividing the prophets, Ovadiah ensured that the capture of one group would not doom the other.

Rabbi Abbahu offers a more prosaic explanation: a cave simply cannot hold more than fifty people. Practicality, not spiritual strategy, dictated the division.

### Convert Prophet

The question then arises: why did Ovadiah, specifically, merit the gift of prophecy? According to tradition, Ovadiah was a convert — an Edomite by origin. There is a principle rooted in the *pasuk* that God's covenant extends to Israel and to their offspring after them, and this has been understood by some as limiting prophecy to those of Israelite lineage. A convert lacks that genealogical chain. How, then, did Ovadiah become a prophet?

One answer given is precisely his act of protecting the hundred prophets. By risking his position in Ahab's court — and his life — to conceal them in the caves, Ovadiah demonstrated a fidelity to *Hashem*'s prophets that merited prophetic standing in return.

### Edom Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חֲזוֹן עֹבַדְיָהוּ כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ אֱלֹהִים לֶאֱדוֹם וְגוֹ׳״. מַאי שְׁנָא עוֹבַדְיָה לֶאֱדוֹם?
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"The vision of Obadiah. So says the Lord God concerning Edom:** We have heard a message from the Lord, and an ambassador is sent among the nations: Arise, and let us rise up against her in battle" (Obadiah 1:1). The Gemara asks: **What is different,** i.e., what is the reason, that specifically **Obadiah** prophesied **concerning Edom?**

The opening words of the book of Ovadiah read: *chazon Ovadyahu, koh amar Hashem Elokim le'Edom* — the vision of Obadiah; thus says *Hashem* God to Edom. Why should Ovadiah, of all prophets, be the one to deliver a prophecy against Edom?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, יָבֹא עוֹבַדְיָהוּ הַדָּר בֵּין שְׁנֵי רְשָׁעִים וְלֹא לָמַד מִמַּעֲשֵׂיהֶם, וְיִנָּבֵא עַל עֵשָׂו הָרָשָׁע שֶׁדָּר בֵּין שְׁנֵי צַדִּיקִים וְלֹא לָמַד מִמַּעֲשֵׂיהֶם. אָמַר אֶפְרַיִם מִקְשָׁאָה תַּלְמִידוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: עוֹבַדְיָהוּ גֵּר אֲדוֹמִי הָיָה. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: מִנֵּיהּ וּבֵיהּ אִבָּא נֵיזִיל בֵּיהּ נַרְגָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: Let Obadiah come, who dwells among two wicked** ones, Ahab and Jezebel, **but did not learn from their actions; and he will prophesy concerning Esau the wicked,** the progenitor of Edom, **who dwelled among two righteous** ones, Isaac and Rebekkah, **but did not learn from their actions. Efrayim Miksha'a, a student of Rabbi Meir, said in the name of Rabbi Meir: Obadiah was an Edomite convert.** Consequently, he prophesied with regard to Edom. **And this is as people say: From and within the forest comes the ax to it,** as the handle for the ax that chops the tree is from the forest itself.

Rabbi Yitzchak explains in the name of the *kadosh baruch hu*: let Ovadiah — who lived between two evildoers, Ahab and Izevel, and did not learn from their conduct — come and prophesy against Esav the evildoer, who lived between two righteous ones, Yitzchak and Rivka, and did not learn from their conduct. The contrast is precise and structurally elegant: Ovadiah withstood malign influence from within the palace of the wicked; Esav could not absorb righteous influence even from within the home of the righteous. Each is measured by the environment they inhabited and how they responded to it.

Ephraim Maksha'ah, a student of Rabbi Meir, transmits in Rabbi Meir's name that Ovadiah was indeed of Edomite origin, a *ger Edomite*. This brings the Gemara to the proverb: *mineih uveih abba nizal beih narga* — from it and within it, the father, the axe handle goes into it. The axe handle is made from the very forest it is used to cut down. Destruction sometimes comes from within: the means of a thing's undoing is drawn from its own substance. In Ovadiah's case, an Edomite becomes the instrument of Edom's prophetic condemnation.

## David Moab Tension

### David Moab War

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּךְ אֶת מוֹאָב וַיְמַדְּדֵם בַּחֶבֶל הַשְׁכֵּב אוֹתָם אַרְצָה״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: מִינֵּיהּ וּבֵיהּ אִבָּא לֵיזִיל בֵּיהּ נַרְגָּא. כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: יָרֵךְ מִתּוֹכָהּ מַסְרַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states concerning King David: **"And he smote Moab and measured them with the line, making them lie down on the ground** and he measured two lines to put to death" (II Samuel 8:2). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: This is as people say: From and within the forest comes the ax to it,** as King David was a descendant of Ruth the Moabite. **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael **he stated** something similar to this: **A thigh rots from within,** i.e., when a piece of meat rots, the rotting begins from the inner part of it. Here too, the punishment is administered by one descended from that very nation.

The Gemara brings this proverb again in the name of Rabbi Yochanan citing Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, now applied to a different case. The *pasuk* from Melachim recounts: *vayach et Mo'av vayimadedem bechevel hashkev otam artzah* — King David struck Moav and measured them with a rope, laying them on the ground. David waged war against Moav. What makes this notable is that David himself traces his lineage through Moav — his ancestor Rut was a Moabite. Here too, the axe handle comes from the forest. The instrument of Moav's judgment was formed from within Moav's own line.

### Customs Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּקַּח אֶת בְּנוֹ הַבְּכוֹר אֲשֶׁר יִמְלֹךְ תַּחְתָּיו וַיַּעֲלֵהוּ עֹלָה עַל הַחֹמָה״. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם, וְחַד אָמַר: לְשֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a verse concerning the king of Moab: **"Then he took his eldest son, who should have reigned in his stead, and sacrificed him for a burnt offering upon the wall;** and there was great indignation against Israel, and they departed from him and returned to their own land" (II Kings 3:27). **Rav and Shmuel** engage in a dispute concerning this. **One says:** The king sacrificed his son **for the sake of Heaven, and one says:** The king sacrificed his son **for the sake of idol worship.**

The Gemara then presents another episode and a further biblical tension. A king takes his firstborn son — the one who was to reign after him — and offers him as a burnt-offering upon the wall. Rav and Shmuel disagree about the motivation: one says it was *leshem shamayim* — for the sake of heaven; the other says it was for the sake of *avodah zarah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״וַיְהִי קֶצֶף גָּדוֹל עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְשֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אַמַּאי ״וַיְהִי קֶצֶף״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says** that he sacrificed him **for the sake of Heaven, this is as it is written: "And there was great indignation against Israel,"** as the gentile attempted to honor God according to his understanding, while the Jewish people were straying from worshipping God, leading to God's anger against them. **But according to the one who says** that he sacrificed him **for the sake of idol worship, why** would the verse state: **"And there was…indignation"?**

Both positions bring a proof text. The one who says it was for the sake of heaven cites the verse *vayehi ketzef gadol al Yisrael* — a great anger came upon Israel — and explains this through a teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who juxtaposes two apparently contradictory verses. One verse says: *u'chemishpetei hagoyim asher svivoteichem lo asitem* — you have not done like the laws and customs of the nations around you. Another verse says: *ki chemishpetei hagoyim asher svivoteichem asitem* — for you have done like the laws and customs of the nations around you. Which is it? Did Israel follow the ways of the nations or not?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״וּכְמִשְׁפְּטֵי הַגּוֹיִם אֲשֶׁר סְבִיבוֹתֵיכֶם לֹא עֲשִׂיתֶם״, וּכְתִיב ״וּכְמִשְׁפְּטֵי הַגּוֹיִם אֲשֶׁר סְבִיבוֹתֵיכֶם עֲשִׂיתֶם״. כַּמְתוּקָּנִין שֶׁבָּהֶם – לֹא עֲשִׂיתֶם, כַּמְקוּלְקָלִין שֶׁבָּהֶם – עֲשִׂיתֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This can be understood **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. As Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi raises a contradiction: It is written** concerning the Jewish people: "You have not walked in My statutes, neither have you kept My ordinances; **nor have you done according to the statutes of the nations that are round about you"** (Ezekiel 5:7), **and it is** also **written: "You have done according to the statutes of the nations that are round about you"** (Ezekiel 11:12). Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi explains: **You did not act like the proper ones among** the nations, but **you did act like the flawed ones among them.** Here as well, the Jewish people learned from the king of Moab to engage in human sacrifice.

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *kamukanim shebahem lo asitem, kemkulkalim shebahem asitem* — the good customs of the surrounding nations, you did not adopt; the corrupted ones, you did. Israel selectively absorbed the worst of their neighbors while failing to learn from the best.

### Evildoers Depths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּסְעוּ מֵעָלָיו וַיָּשׁוּבוּ לָאָרֶץ״. אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא: בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה יָרְדוּ שׂוֹנְאֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַדְרֵיגָה הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the end of that verse: **"And they departed from him and returned to their own land [*la'aretz*]"** (II Kings 3:27). **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa says: At that time the enemies of the Jewish people,** a euphemism for the Jewish people themselves, **descended to the lowest level.** He interprets "*la'aretz*" to mean: To the earth, teaching that the Jewish people were as low as the dust of the earth.

Following the departure of the nations from the king who sacrificed his son — *vayisu me'alav vayashuvu la'aretz* — Rabbi Chanina bar Papa teaches: *be'otah sha'ah yardu resha'im shel Yisrael l'madregah tachtona* — at that moment, the evildoers of Israel descended to the lowest level. The *pasuk* cited describes the *na'arah* as *yafah ad me'od* — beautiful up to very much.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַנַּעֲרָה יָפָה עַד מְאֹד״. אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא: עֲדַיִן לֹא הִגִּיעָה לַחֲצִי יוֹפִי שֶׁל שָׂרָה, דִּכְתִיב ״עַד מְאֹד״ – וְלֹא מְאֹד בַּכְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** The chapter closes with the explication of a verse stated with regard to Abishag, who attended King David before his death: **"And the damsel was very fair [*yafa ad me'od* ]"** (I Kings 1:4). **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa says: She still did not reach half the beauty of Sarah, as it is written:** "*Ad me'od*," which can be translated as **"up to very** fair," **but not including** the praise of **"very** fair." By contrast, it is written concerning Sarah: "She was very fair" (Genesis 12:14).

Rabbi Chanina bar Papa adds a closing qualification: *adayin lo higgia l'chatzi yafyah shel Sarah* — they still did not reach even half the beauty of Sarah. The word *ad me'od* — "up to very much" — means they reached *until* the threshold of *me'od*, but did not cross into it. *Me'od bichlal* they did not attain. In descending to the lowest depths, the evildoers of Israel still, perhaps by some mercy, stopped short of the absolute limit. That the chapter closes on this note — finding even in the most extreme descent a margin of restraint — is itself a kind of commentary on the unbounded quality of divine measure.

*Hadran alach echad dinei mamonot.* We conclude the fourth chapter of Sanhedrin.
