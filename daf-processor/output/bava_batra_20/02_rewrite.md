# Bava Batra 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Maysivay to Shmuel

### Shmuel's Wafer Principle

We pick up four lines from the top of 20a with a *maysivay* challenge to a ruling attributed to Shmuel. On the previous *daf*, Rav Tuvi bar Kisna reported in the name of Shmuel that a wafer placed in a window does not reduce the size of that window. The *halachic* context is the laws of *ohel* and *tumah*: when a corpse lies in one room, *tumah* passes into an adjacent room only if the connecting window is at least a *tefach* in height and width. If the window measures exactly a *tefach* but a wafer sitting in it would reduce the effective opening to less than a *tefach*, one might think the window is now too small to transmit *tumah*. Shmuel's ruling is that the wafer does not count toward reducing the window's size, because the wafer is intended to be eaten and will be removed. The principle underlying Shmuel's position is that only something one genuinely intends to leave in place permanently can be considered part of the wall structure; an object one plans to remove does not diminish the window's legal dimensions.

Tosafot notes that this discussion is brought here because it will connect to the broader conversation about vegetation near walls — whether certain growth benefits or damages a wall — which ties into our *mishnah*'s topic of required distancing from walls.

### Tosefta Objects List

The *maysivay* challenge to Shmuel comes from a *Tosefta* in *Ohalos*. The *Tosefta* lists a series of objects that, if placed in a window, *do* diminish its size — meaning they are treated as part of the wall for purposes of measuring the *tefach* threshold. These are:

Grass (*asavim*) that was uprooted and placed in the window, or grass that grew on its own through gaps in the stones. Small rags (*metlanyot*) that lack three by three fingerbreadths in area — it is important to note that the measurement here is *shalosh al shalosh etzbaot*, fingerbreadths, not *tefachim*; we will encounter a four by four *tefachim* standard shortly and the grammatical distinction is critical. A rag smaller than three by three fingerbreadths is not *mekabel tumah*, susceptible to ritual impurity, and that is precisely the qualification at work throughout this list. A hanging limb or flesh (*ha'eiver vehabasar hamedulalin bivheimah uvchayah*) — a limb that has been partially torn from an animal but is still partially attached. A live bird resting in the window (*of sheshachan bechalon*). A non-Jew seated in the window: though a non-Jew is rabbinically a source of *tumah*, he is not *mekabel tumah* on a Torah level. A baby born in the eighth month (*ben shmonah*): the *Gemara* held that such an infant would not survive, and so it has the status of a stone — while still alive it is not classified as a person who is *mekabel tumah*. Salt (*melach*), since salt is not a food in itself and therefore not *mekabel tumah*. A pottery vessel (*kli cheres*) — ordinarily pottery is *mekabel tumah*, but only through its opening, so the case here requires that the opening faces away from the room containing the corpse. And a *Sefer Torah*, which is striking: a *Sefer Torah* is not *mekabel tumah* simply because it is a book, and a book does not meet any of the criteria of a *kli* that renders an object susceptible to impurity.

The *Tosefta* concludes: *kulam mematin bechalon* — all of these diminish the size of the window. Because none of them is *mekabel tumah*, they are treated as part of the wall, and the *tefach* is measured from the top of these objects to the top of the window opening.

## Temporary Objects

### Why They Don't Minimize

The *Tosefta* continues with a contrasting list: *aval hasheleg vehabarad vehagalid vehakefor vehamayim* — snow, hail, frost, and water. For all of these, *kulam ein mematin bechalon*: they do not diminish the size of the window.

The reason is not that these substances are *mekabel tumah* — that consideration would not apply straightforwardly, since random rainwater not set aside for drinking has not been designated as food and therefore lacks that status. Rather, the reason these items do not count is that they will not remain. They melt, flow away, or otherwise naturally dissipate. Even though they are not *mekabel tumah*, they fail the other requirement: physical permanence. An object in a window can only reduce the effective opening if it will stay there.

This now creates the central tension with Shmuel's position. The *Tosefta* seems entirely unconcerned with whether a person *plans* to remove an object — it only asks whether the object is *mekabel tumah* and whether it will physically remain on its own. By contrast, Shmuel's principle was precisely about intent to remove: the wafer does not count because you will take it to eat it. Yet the *Tosefta*'s list includes many objects that one would naturally want to remove, and it rules that they *do* minimize the window. The *Gemara* must now work through this tension, going through the list case by case.

## Maysivay to Shmuel (II)

### Grass through Living Cre…

The *Gemara* takes up each item in the *Tosefta* and asks why, given Shmuel's principle, we would not expect the owner to remove it.

Beginning with grass: *veha'asavim chazu livheimah* — grass is fit to feed to one's animal, so one would presumably want to take it out of the window. The *Gemara* answers: *be'afrazta* — we are speaking of a poisonous variety that cannot be fed to animals. As for grass that grew up on its own through the wall, the *Gemara* notes that since such growth damages the wall — *keivan dekashi lekotel* — the owner would certainly want to remove it. This, incidentally, is why the *Tosefta* is being brought here: the damage that grass causes to walls is the very issue our *mishnah* addresses. Rava answers that we are speaking of a wall in a desolate, ownerless area (*bekotel churva*) where nobody is concerned about maintaining the wall and nobody plans to remove the grass. Rav Pappa goes further and says it applies even to a wall in a settled area (*afilu bekotel yishuv*), because the grass is growing from the ground three *tefachim* away — just as our *mishnah* requires — and is simply bending over and falling into the window from that safe distance, causing no damage, and therefore giving no reason to remove it.

Moving to rags: a rag smaller than three by three fingerbreadths can still serve as a patch for a torn garment, so why would someone leave it in the window? The *Gemara* answers: *b'smichta* — it is a thick, heavy rag that would not work well as a patch. But it could still be used by a blood-letter to wipe away blood after bloodletting. The *Gemara* answers: *b'riska* — it is rough and scratchy material, unsuitable for use on skin. The *Gemara* then raises a further challenge: if we are dealing with sackcloth (*briska*), then the threshold for being *mekabel tumah* is not three by three fingerbreadths but four by four *tefachim* — a much larger standard. So the *Tosefta* cannot be discussing sackcloth. The *Gemara* resolves: *k'ein briska* — not sackcloth itself, but a scratchy material like it, made of ordinary cloth. The upshot is that we have a tiny scrap of rough cloth with no practical use whatsoever, which is why it will be left in the window.

The *Gemara* then turns to the hanging limb: *v'ha'eiver v'habasar hamiduldalin bivheimah uvchayah* — if a limb is hanging from an animal that is standing in the window, surely the animal itself will wander off. The *Gemara* answers: *bikshurah* — the animal is tied down. But even so, one would eventually slaughter the animal. The *Gemara* answers: *bitmeiah* — it is a non-kosher animal, a mule for instance. But one would sell it to a non-Jew. The *Gemara* answers: *bichshusha* — it is so weak and emaciated that no one would want to buy it. But why not simply cut off the hanging limb and throw it to a dog as food? The *Gemara* answers: *keivan d'ika tza'ar ba'alei chayim lo avid* — since doing so would cause the animal pain, one would not do it. It is worth noting that the *Gemara* does not say it is forbidden; the general ruling of the *poskim*, beginning with the *Trumas Hadeshen*, is that *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* is permitted when it serves a human purpose. Here the point is simply that this would not be one's practical intention. The result is a highly contrived scenario: an emaciated, non-kosher animal tied in a window with a partially dangling limb, where no plan for removal exists.

For the bird: *parach v'azil* — it will fly away. *Bikshur* — it is tied down. *Shachit lei* — but one would slaughter and eat it. *Betame* — it is a non-kosher bird. *Mazbenei lei lenochri* — one would sell it to a non-Jew. *Bekalanisa* — we are dealing with a *kalanisa*, a bird that Rashi identifies as very thin and of no culinary value, so no one would want to buy it. *Yahvei lei lyanuka* — but one might give it to a child as a pet. *Bimsarit* — it is a scratchy, abrasive bird and no one would want it as a pet. The *Gemara* then notes that a *kalanisa* by definition does not scratch — *kalanisa lo masreta* — so the resolution is: *keein kalanisa*, something like a *kalanisa*, a similarly worthless but scratchy bird. One is left with a tied-up, unsellable, uneatable, too-scratchy-to-be-a-pet bird sitting in the window.

The *Gemara* turns to the non-Jew: *kai ve'azil* — he will get up and leave. *Bekafut* — he is bound. *Atei chavreia vesharei lei* — his friend will come and untie him. *Bimetzora* — he has a communicable disease and no one will approach him. *Atei metzora chavreia vesharei lei* — even so, a fellow sufferer might come and free him. *Ela bechavushei malchut* — the final resolution: he is being held prisoner by the government and is chained there under governmental authority, so no one may free him. We have arrived at the scenario of a government prisoner in a window.

### Salt to Sefer Torah

Turning to salt: *chazi lei* — salt is useful, so one would remove it. *Bemarirata* — it is bitter salt with no culinary use. *Chazi le'oros* — one could still use it for tanning leather. *De'it bei kotzei* — it is filled with thistles and therefore unusable for that purpose as well. *Keivan dekashei lekotel shaklei lei* — but salt damages a wall, so one would remove it to protect the wall. *Deyaspa chaspa* — it is sitting on a pottery shard and not in direct contact with the wall, so it does no damage.

The *Gemara* then asks: but the pottery shard itself — *chaspa gufa teichatz* — would also be a partition between the salt and the wall, and so it should independently count as something that diminishes the window size. The *Gemara* answers: *deleis bei shiura* — the pottery shard is so small it has no *halachic* significance. The *Gemara* cites the *mishnah* in *Shabbos*: the minimum significant size of a pottery shard is one large enough to be placed between stacked beams to keep them from warping. This shard is smaller than that. Tosafot raises a difficulty here: we already encountered a rag smaller than three by three fingerbreadths — also not normally significant for *Shabbos* purposes — that nonetheless *does* diminish the window. Why should the pottery shard be any different? Tosafot resolves this by suggesting the pottery shard is so thin in profile that it could not itself reduce the opening to less than a *tefach*, which is why the *Tosefta* does not mention it. The *mishnah* in *Shabbos* is cited only to explain why one would not bother removing the shard — and since neither the shard nor the salt has any use, neither will be removed.

Moving to the pottery vessel (*kli cheres*): *chazi lei* — it is fit for use. *Demitneif* — it has become soiled and is disgusting. *Chazi leumna* — a blood-letter could still use it as a vessel for collecting blood. *Deminkav* — it has a hole in it, rendering it useless even for that purpose.

Finally, the *Sefer Torah*: surely of all things, one would remove it from a window. *Chazi lemikra* — it is fit for reading. *Beviloi* — it is completely worn out. *Veha ba'ei gnizah* — but even a worn-out *Sefer Torah* requires proper burial in a *genizah*; one cannot simply leave it in a window. The *Gemara* answers: *shama gnizaso* — that location in the window is itself its place of resting, effectively its *genizah*. The details of what constitutes a valid *genizah* and whether an open window truly qualifies are questions one could explore at length, but that is the *Gemara*'s resolution.

Stepping back, what is remarkable about the *Gemara*'s treatment of this entire *Tosefta* is that virtually every item on the list would naturally be removed, and in each case the *Gemara* has constructed an elaborate and highly unusual circumstance to explain why removal is not planned. The *pshat* of the *Tosefta* runs squarely against Shmuel's position — intent to remove seems irrelevant to whether something diminishes the window — yet the *Gemara* expends considerable effort constructing these *okimtot* to preserve Shmuel's ruling.

## Salt/Fat Mechitza

### Rav's Mechitza Rule

Rav taught: *bechol osin mechitza chutz memelach verevav* — one may construct a *mechitza* for *Shabbos* purposes from any material, except salt and fat (*revav*). The context here is not the *mechitzos* familiar from *beit knesset* usage but rather *mechitzos* that create a *reshus hayachid* or serve purposes of *eiruv* or *sukkah*. Salt and fat are excluded because they lack permanence: a pile of salt will scatter and fat will dissolve. Even if they currently have a recognizable form, they will not maintain it, and so they cannot function *halachically* as a wall.

### Shmuel vs. Salt Types

Shmuel disagrees: *afilu melach* — even salt may be used for a *mechitza*. Rav Pappa resolves the apparent dispute: *lo peligi* — they do not actually disagree. *Ha b'melach Sedomis ha b'melach Istronknis* — one is speaking of *melach Sedomis*, mineral salt, which is hard and retains its form, especially when cut from the ground; the other is speaking of *melach Istronknis*, sea salt, which is granular and does not hold its shape. Rav's prohibition applies to sea salt, while Shmuel's permission applies to mineral salt.

### Salt Mounds & Beam

The *Gemara* adds a further nuance in the name of Rabbah: *oseh adam shenei tziburei melach umaniyach aleihem korah* — a person may construct two mounds of salt and place a beam across them. The principle at work is mutual reinforcement: the salt supports the beam, and the beam by its weight presses down and keeps the salt in place. In this configuration even sea salt (*melach Istronknis*) can serve as the posts of a *tzuras hapethach* for an *eiruv* or *mavoi*. Thus the final resolution: *ha d'ika korah ha d'leika korah* — the distinction between the positions is simply whether a beam is resting on top of the salt or not.

This discussion of salt and walls flows naturally from the earlier treatment of salt in the window context, which is what prompted the digression.

## Mills & Ovens

### Normal & Donkey Mills

The *mishnah* now turns to objects that must be kept at a distance from a wall: *marchikin es harechayim gimel min hashechev shehein daled min harechev* — one must distance a mill three *tefachim* from the *shechev*, the base of the mill, which translates to four *tefachim* from the *rechev*, the grinding component. The base protrudes more widely than the grinding part, so something three *tefachim* from the wider base is four *tefachim* from the narrower grinding element above it.

Similarly: *v'shel chamor gimel min ha'istrobiel shehein daled min hakelat* — a donkey mill, so called because donkeys were sometimes employed to turn it, though it is a smaller and more portable device, must likewise be kept three *tefachim* from its base (*istrobiel*), which is four *tefachim* from its grinding component (*kelat*). The structural geometry is parallel.

### Vibrations vs. Sound

The *Gemara* asks *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for distancing a mill from the wall? *Mishum tiraya* — because of the vibrations. When a heavy mill operates close to a wall, the vibrations destabilize it. The *Gemara* then challenges: but by the donkey mill, the same law applies — *mai tiraya ika?* — yet a lighter, more portable mill would not produce significant vibrations. *Ella mishum kala* — rather, the reason for the donkey mill is noise. The sound waves emanating from a mill operating very close to a wall cause damage to the wall. The two rationales — vibration for the large mill, sound for the small one — both lead to the same required distance.

### Tannur & Kirah

*Vechen tannur shalosh min hakalya shehein arba min hasafa* — and similarly, an oven (*tannur*) must be three *tefachim* from its base (*kalya*), which is four *tefachim* from its mouth (*safa*). The *tannur* was shaped roughly like a large pot, wider at the base and narrower at the opening. Rashi understands this as a vessel wide at the bottom and narrow at the top, like an inverted beaker, where the three *tefachim* are measured from the wide base and the four from the narrower mouth. Tosafot suggests a pot-belly shape — narrow at top and bottom, widest in the middle — where the three *tefachim* are from the protruding middle section and the four from the mouth.

### Abaye's Inference

Abaye draws a practical inference: *shema mina kalya de-tannur tefach* — we learn from these measurements that the base (or for Tosafot, the protruding middle section) of a *tannur* extends one *tefach* beyond the mouth. The arithmetic is straightforward: three *tefachim* from one point equals four from another, meaning the difference of one *tefach* represents the protrusion of the base. Abaye notes the practical implication: *nafka mina le-mekkach u-mimkar* — this matters for commercial transactions. If one orders or purchases an oven and it is delivered without the full extent of that base protrusion, the buyer has grounds for complaint.

## Home Oven Safety

### First Story Oven

The *mishnah* now addresses oven safety within a home: *lo ya'amid adam tannur be-toch ha-bayit ella im ken yesh al gabav govah arba amot* — a person may not install an oven inside a house unless there are four *amot* — approximately six feet — between the top of the oven and the ceiling. The concern operates on two levels: fire safety in general, and specifically the damage that rising heat and steam can cause to the ceiling, which is simultaneously the floor of the story above where a neighbor may be living.

### Second Story Oven

*Haya ma'amido be-aliya* — if the oven is being installed on an upper story, *tzarich she-yehei tachtav ma'aziva shelosha tefachim* — there must be three *tefachim* of plaster (*ma'aziva*) in the floor beneath it. This serves to insulate the lower neighbor's ceiling from the heat of the oven above, preventing structural damage or fire. The four-*amot* clearance above the oven remains applicable on the upper story as well.

### Kirah Requirement

*U've-kirah tefach* — for a *kirah*, a smaller oven, it is sufficient to have one *tefach* of plaster beneath it. Rashi explains that because the *kirah* is smaller, it does not produce as large a fire, and the reduced requirement follows accordingly. Though one might ask whether this should also affect the four-*amot* clearance required above — the *mishnah* does not say so explicitly.

### Liability Debate

*Ve-im hizzik meshalem mah she-hizzik* — even if one has complied with all of these specifications, if damage results one remains liable. *Rabbi Shimon omer*: the specifications were given precisely in order to define exemption — *lo amru kol ha-shi'urim ha-elu ella she-im hizzik patur mi-leshalem* — if one followed all the required measures and damage still occurred, one is exempt from payment.

This disagreement touches on a fundamental question in *Bava Kamma*. Normally there is an obligation of *shemirah* — watchfulness — over one's fire, and liability follows from a failure of that watchfulness. Rabbi Shimon's position is natural: compliance with the prescribed standards constitutes adequate *shemirah*, and therefore exempts the owner. The *Tanna Kamma*'s position is more nuanced. One way to understand it is that these specifications, while legally sufficient to permit operating the oven, do not actually constitute full *shemirah* against all possible damage. True *shemirah* might require far greater clearance — twenty feet, or more — but demanding that would make ordinary domestic life impossible. So the *halacha* adopts a middle position: the standards define what is sufficient for the purpose of permitted use, but they do not remove the underlying liability that attaches to fire. A parallel exists in *Bava Kamma* with respect to animals in *reshus harabim*: several *Rishonim* explain that an animal's natural conduct in a public domain does not trigger liability precisely because imposing liability would make ordinary use of public space untenable. Here the tension is similar but unresolved in the permissive direction — we allow the oven but do not eliminate the liability.

## Shops Under Storage

### Bakery & Dye Shop Ban

*Lo yiftach adam chanut shel nachtomim veshel tzeva'im tachas otzaro shel chavero* — a person may not open a bakery or a dyeing shop beneath another's storehouse. The fumes, smoke, and heat rising from these operations will damage whatever the upper neighbor is storing — grain, straw, fruit, and similar goods.

### Cowshed Prohibition

*Velo refet bakar* — nor may one operate a cowshed beneath a storehouse. The odor from cattle is sufficiently penetrating and damaging that it is always forbidden, regardless of what is stored above.

### Wine Exception

*Be'emet beyayin hitiru aval lo refet bakar* — in truth, when the storehouse contains wine, the prohibition on bakeries and dye shops is lifted, because the warmth and fumes from those operations actually improve wine rather than harming it. The cowshed prohibition, however, remains in force even for wine storage, since the smell from cattle spoils the wine.

A *tanna* adds: *beyayin hitiru mipnei she-mashpicho* — the bakery fumes benefit wine. But *velo refes bakar mipnei she-masricho* — the cowshed still ruins it.

### Priority Baraita

A *braita* addresses the question of priority: *im hayta refet kodemet le-otzar muttar* — if the cowshed was there first, and the upper neighbor subsequently decided to use the space as a storehouse, the cowshed owner need not relocate. First occupancy confers rights, and the one who arrived later with the storehouse cannot compel the prior occupant to move.

### Abaye's Questions

Abaye poses a series of questions about the boundary of this priority rule. *Kibbed veribetz le-otzar mahu* — what if the upper-story owner had begun sweeping and preparing the space for use as a storehouse, making his intention clear, but had not yet actually stored anything there, when the lower-story person moved in a cowshed? Is the storehouse considered to have priority because of the preparatory activity? *Reiba be-chalonos mahu* — what if he went further and made structural alterations, opening up additional windows to provide air and light for storage — is that sufficient to establish priority? *Bana aliya gabei bayis mahu* — or what if the upper story itself was built explicitly for storage purposes but has not yet been put to use? The *Gemara* leaves all of these unresolved: *teiku*.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua poses a further question: *tamrei veriminei mai* — what about a storehouse containing dates or pomegranates, which are presumably less sensitive to the smells from below? Does the same prohibition apply? This too is left as *teiku*.

### Yosef & Sheshes

The discussion of wine generates two further rulings from later Amoraim. Rav Yosef observes: *hai didan afilu kutra de-sharga nami kasha lei* — our wine in Babylonia is particularly sensitive, and even the smoke from a single candle is damaging to it. There is therefore no relaxation of the protective rules for wine; if anything, greater care is required.

Rav Sheshes adds: *aspasta ke-refes bakar damya* — *aspasta*, vetch, a coarse fodder used for animal feed, produces an odor so pungent that storing it below a storehouse is treated exactly like operating a cowshed. The prohibition applies with full force.
