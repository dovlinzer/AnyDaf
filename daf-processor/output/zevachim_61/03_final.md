# Zevachim 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mizbeiach Whole

### Shechita Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁעַת סִילּוּק מַסָּעוֹת – קָדָשִׁים נִפְסָלִין, וְזָבִים וּמְצוֹרָעִים מִשְׁתַּלְּחִים חוּץ לַמְּחִיצָה; וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בִּשְׁנֵי מְקוֹמוֹת קֳדָשִׁים נֶאֱכָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **At the time** when the Jewish people would dismantle the Tabernacle in order to **depart** on their **journeys** in the wilderness, **sacrificial** food was **disqualified** from being consumed, since the altar was not in place. Nevertheless, ***zavim* and lepers** were **sent out of** the relevant **partition;** a *zav* was sent out of the Levite camp and a leper was sent out of the Israelite camp. **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **Sacrificial** food could be **consumed in two locations,** i.e., one could eat it while the Tabernacle was in place and one could continue eating it after the Tabernacle had been disassembled and transported.

The daf opens at the bottom of 61b with a ruling that had been established: one must perform *shechita* while the *mizbeiach* is whole. A parallel question had been raised regarding the *ketores* — whether the incense offering also requires an intact *mizbeiach* — but the conclusion was that it does not. The *shechita* requirement, however, is firm. And further: just as *shechita* requires an intact *mizbeiach*, so too does the consumption of the meat, since the meat is linked to the blood. The same logic extends to the *mincha*: one must take the *komets* with the *mizbeiach* intact, and presumably one must eat the remainder under those same conditions as well.

### Kodshei vs. Kodshim Kalim

All of the above is straightforward with respect to *kodshei kodashim*, because the eating of *kodshei kodashim* takes place in the *azarah* and is therefore inherently referenced to the *mizbeiach* — it can be understood as something analogous to being burned on the altar. The question becomes more pressing with regard to *kodshim kalim*. Since *kodshim kalim* is eaten anywhere in Yerushalayim, and is consumed by the owners rather than the *kohanim*, there is a compelling reason to think it bears no intrinsic connection to the *mizbeiach* whatsoever.

### Rabbi Yishmael Bechor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, הָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **This** first *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** who derives that meat of a firstborn offering, which is an offering of lesser sanctity, cannot be consumed if the altar is damaged or absent, based upon the *halakha* pertaining to the blood of the firstborn. **That** second *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Sages,** who disagree with Rabbi Yishmael.

The initial position was that, yes, even the meat of *kodshim kalim* requires the *mizbeiach* to be whole — derived from a teaching of Rabbi Yishmael concerning the *bechor*. Rabbi Yishmael linked the blood of the *bechor* to its meat: just as the blood of the *bechor* requires a *mizbeiach*, so too does the eating of its meat. This linkage was then read as extending even to *maaser sheni*, raising deeper questions about whether the operative factor is the *kedushah* of the *makom*, the *kedushah* of the *mikdash* as an institution, or the physical structure of the *mizbeiach* itself — a fascinating set of distinctions that we bracket here for its own discussion.

## Rabbi Yirmiyah Dispute

### Babylonians Criticized

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סְלֵיק רָבִין, אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה. אָמַר: בַּבְלָאֵי טַפְשָׁאֵי – אַמְּטוּל דְּיָתְבִי בְּאַרְעָא חֲשׁוֹכָא, אָמְרִי שְׁמַעְתָּא דִּמְחַשְּׁכָא! לָא שְׁמִיעַ לְהוּ הָא דְּתַנְיָא:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When Ravin ascended** from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael, **he stated this *halakha*,** that even items of lesser sanctity are disqualified if the altar is damaged or missing, **in the presence of Rabbi Yirmeya.** Rabbi Yirmeya **said: Foolish Babylonians! Because they dwell in a dark land, they state *halakhot* that are dim. Have they not heard that which is taught** in a *baraita*:

When this teaching was reported to Rabbi Yirmiyah — *ki salik* Ravin and presented the discussion before him — his response was strikingly harsh. He said: *bavlayi tipshayi* — the foolish Babylonians — because they sit in a land of darkness they speak dark, unilluminated ideas. Could they really think that the eating of *kodshim kalim* requires the *mizbeiach* to be whole? Did they not hear the following *braita*?

### Camp Transit Pesul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁעַת סִילּוּק מַסָּעוֹת – קָדָשִׁים נִפְסָלִין, וְזָבִים וּמְצוֹרָעִים מִשְׁתַּלְּחִים חוּץ לַמְּחִיצָה;
>
> **Translation:** **At the time** when the Jewish people would dismantle the Tabernacle in order to **depart** on their **journeys** in the wilderness, **sacrificial** food was **disqualified** from being consumed, since the altar was not in place. Nevertheless, ***zavim* and lepers** were **sent out of** the relevant **partition;** a *zav* was sent out of the Levite camp and a leper was sent out of the Israelite camp.

The *braita* Rabbi Yirmiyah cites states: *bishat siluk masa'ot* — at the time the camp decamps and moves — *kodshim nifsalim*, sacrifices become invalidated. At the same time, *zavim* and *metzora'im* are still sent outside their respective boundaries, which demonstrates that the concept of *machaneh* as such continues to exist even in transit. The question therefore becomes: if the *machaneh* still exists in transit — and the proof is that *teme'im* are expelled from it — why should *kodshim* be invalidated?

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

This is where Rashi and Tosafot part ways in a genuinely illuminating dispute. Rashi holds that this *braita* supports precisely what we had been saying about the *mizbeiach*. Even in transit, the concept of *machaneh shechinah* continues to exist — the *machaneh* is intact — but the *Mishkan* has been deconstructed, and therefore the *kodshim* are invalidated because there is no *mizbeiach*. The *machaneh* is present; it is the *mizbeiach* that is missing.

Tosafot disagrees fundamentally. For Tosafot, even in transit one can speak of *machaneh Yisrael* and *machaneh leviyah* — the *Bnei Yisrael* marching in formation, the *Leviyim* in their procession — and it is precisely those camps that generate the expulsion of *zavim* and *metzora'im* from their respective zones. But what one cannot speak of during transit, says Tosafot, is *machaneh shechinah*. For *machaneh shechinah* to exist, the camp must be encamped and the *Mishkan* must be erected. When the *Mishkan* has been taken apart and is being transported, there is no *machaneh shechinah*. The *kodshim* are therefore invalidated not merely because there is no *mizbeiach*, but because the situation is analogous to the meat having been taken outside the bounds of the *azarah* — as if the *Beit HaMikdash* itself were destroyed.

The conceptual stakes are sharply drawn: one can have *Har HaBayit* and *Yerushalayim* without having *makom hamikdash*. So too here, one can have *machaneh Yisrael* and *machaneh leviyah* without having *machaneh shechinah*. For Rashi, *machaneh shechinah* persists in transit and the problem is solely the absence of the *mizbeiach*. For Tosafot, *machaneh shechinah* itself is absent in transit, and that absence is the primary disqualification.

### Two Eating Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בִּשְׁנֵי מְקוֹמוֹת קֳדָשִׁים נֶאֱכָלִים. מַאי, לָאו הָא בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, הָא בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים?
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **Sacrificial** food could be **consumed in two locations,** i.e., one could eat it while the Tabernacle was in place and one could continue eating it after the Tabernacle had been disassembled and transported. **What, is it not** that **this** first *baraita* is referring **to offerings of the most sacred order,** and **that** second *baraita* is referring **to offerings of lesser sanctity?** Accordingly, Abaye's statement that offerings of lesser sanctity may not be consumed if the altar is damaged is incorrect.

A second *braita* is then cited: *bishnei mekomos kodshim ne'echalin* — *kodshim* may be eaten in two contexts. The assumption is that these two contexts are: when the camp is encamped, and when it is in transit. But this creates an immediate difficulty: the first *braita* said that *kodshim* are invalidated in transit, and now this *braita* says they can be eaten in transit. How are these reconciled?

The initial suggestion is: *mai lav ha bekodshei kodashim veha bekodshim kalim* — perhaps the first *braita* refers to *kodshei kodashim* and the second to *kodshim kalim*. The logic would be: in transit there is no *mizbeiach* and no *machaneh shechinah* (for Tosafot), so *kodshei kodashim* are invalidated. But *kodshim kalim*, which may be eaten throughout all of *machaneh Yisrael* — i.e., throughout Yerushalayim — do not require a *mizbeiach* and do not require *machaneh shechinah*; they require only *machaneh Yisrael*, which continues to exist even in transit. This reading, says the *Gemara*, supports Rabbi Yirmiyah's position: at least one *braita* shows that *kodshim kalim* does not need a *mizbeiach*.

### Ravina Reconciles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים; וְלָא קַשְׁיָא – הָא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, הָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said** that there is an alternative reconciliation of the two *baraitot*: Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are referring **to offerings of lesser sanctity, and** it is **not difficult:** **This** first *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** who derives that meat of a firstborn offering, which is an offering of lesser sanctity, cannot be consumed if the altar is damaged or absent, based upon the *halakha* pertaining to the blood of the firstborn. **That** second *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Sages,** who disagree with Rabbi Yishmael.

Ravina intervenes to defend the position that *kodshim kalim* does require a *mizbeiach*. He argues: *idi ve'idi bekodshim kalim* — both *braitot* are actually speaking about *kodshim kalim*. The first *braita* rules that even *kodshim kalim* cannot be eaten in transit, because *kodshim kalim* also requires a *mizbeiach*. So how does one explain the second *braita*, which seems to permit eating in transit?

*Velo kashya ha Rabbi Yishmael ha rabbanan* — this is a *machloket*. The second *braita* reflects the view that *kodshim kalim* does not require a *mizbeiach*, which is one opinion. Rabbi Yishmael's derivation from the *bechor* — linking the meat to the blood and requiring the *mizbeiach* for both — is what generated the position that *kodshim kalim* does require a *mizbeiach*. Rabbi Yirmiyah's citation proves only that there exists an opposing view; it does not demonstrate that Rabbi Yishmael's position is simply wrong. The Babylonians were not in error to follow Rabbi Yishmael — they were following a legitimate opinion.

### Two Places

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבַּעְיָא אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים; וּמַאי בִּשְׁנֵי מְקוֹמוֹת – קוֹדֶם שֶׁיַּעֲמִידוּ לְוִיִּם אֶת הַמִּשְׁכָּן, וּלְאַחַר שֶׁיְּפָרְקוּ הַלְוִיִּם אֶת הַמִּשְׁכָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** there is a different resolution of the two *baraitot*: Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are referring **to offerings of the most sacred order. And what** does the second *baraita* mean when it says the food may be consumed **in two locations?** It is referring to when the Israelites arrive at a new camp, **before the Levites erect the Tabernacle,** **and,** when they are leaving the camp, **after the Levites dismantle the Tabernacle** but before they remove the altar. Since the altar has not yet been moved, it is still permitted to consume the sacrificial food.

There is a further resolution offered: *ve'ibais eima ha veha bekodshei kodashim* — both *braitot* can be read as speaking about *kodshei kodashim*, and the phrase *bishnei mekomos* does not refer to "while erect" versus "in transit" at all, but rather to two distinct partial-transit scenarios.

Specifically, *kodem she'ya'amidu Leviyim es hamishkan* — before the *Leviyim* erected the *Mishkan* — and *le'achar she'yifreku Leviyim es hamishkan* — after the *Leviyim* dismantled it. But this requires clarification, since taken literally it would mean they ate *kodshim* before the *Mishkan* was ever standing, which cannot be the intent.

### Shechinah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אִיפְּסִיל לְהוּ בְּיוֹצֵא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְנָסַע אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנָּסַע, אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: It was necessary to state this *halakha* **lest you say** that once the partitions surrounding the courtyard have been taken down, the sacrificial food **has been disqualified because** it is considered to have **left** the courtyard of the Tabernacle. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that the food is permitted for consumption as long as the altar remains in place. The Gemara challenges: **And say** it is **indeed** so, that the sacrificial food should be disqualified because it is no longer within the partitions surrounding the courtyard. The Gemara explains: **The verse states: "Then the Tent of Meeting shall travel"** (Numbers 2:17). This verse indicates that **even though it traveled it is** still considered **the Tent of Meeting.** Therefore, the sacrificial food is not considered to have left its designated area.

Rashi explains that the phrase "before they erected it" is *lav davka* — it is simply structurally parallel phrasing. The actual two scenarios are: first, the normal case where the *Mishkan* is fully erected; and second, a moment early in the dismantling process, when the curtains have already been taken down but the *mizbeiach* has not yet been moved.

To understand this concretely: when the camp would prepare to travel, the *Leviyim* responsible for the curtains and structural elements would take those down first. The *Bnei Kehat* would then come and transport the sacred vessels. So there was a window — after the curtains came down but before the *mizbeiach* was removed — during which the altar remained in place. Rashi holds that even in that transitional state, *machaneh shechinah* continues to exist, and since the *mizbeiach* is still in its position, *kodshei kodashim* can still be eaten in reference to it. That is the second of the two permitted contexts.

Tosafot disagrees. For Tosafot, once you are in any state of transit, there is no *machaneh shechinah* — you may have *machaneh leviyah* and *machaneh Yisrael*, but not *machaneh shechinah*. However, Tosafot reads the *Mishkan* very precisely: if one dismantles only the *Mishkan* proper — the inner structure housing the *aron* and related vessels — but the *kela'im*, the curtains forming the *chatzer*, remain standing, and the *mizbeiach* remains in place, that is the second permitted scenario. The *chatzer* with its curtains is what constitutes *machaneh shechinah*, not the inner *Mishkan* alone. So: curtains down, *mizbeiach* gone — you have lost *machaneh shechinah*. But: inner *Mishkan* dismantled, curtains still up, *mizbeiach* still present — *machaneh shechinah* persists and *kodshei kodashim* may be eaten.

This is a striking structural difference. For Rashi, the *chiddush* is that even fully in transit — even after the entire apparatus has been packed up — *machaneh shechinah* persists, and as long as the *mizbeiach* remains, one may eat. For Tosafot, once the curtains are removed and the camp is moving, *machaneh shechinah* is gone — but the inner *Mishkan* can be dismantled while the curtains and *mizbeiach* remain, and that still qualifies.

There is an additional subtlety here for Tosafot. Recall the earlier discussion about *petach ohel moed* — the *shechita* had to be performed *lifnei* the *Ohel Moed*, but the eating did not have to be specifically at the entrance. In the scenario Tosafot describes, there is no *Ohel Moed* per se — the inner structure is dismantled — and yet the *chatzer* with its curtains retains enough sanctity to constitute *machaneh shechinah* for the purposes of eating. That is Tosafot's position, and the debate between Rashi and Tosafot here is genuinely consequential.

### Ohel Moed Transit

The *Gemara* then raises a further challenge: *eima hachi nami* — perhaps one should say that once in transit the *kodshei kodashim* are invalidated like *psul b'yotze*, as if the meat had been taken outside the permitted area. For Tosafot this would have been answered by pointing to the curtains still being up; but the *Gemara*'s actual answer is a verse: *venasa ohel moed* — the *Ohel Moed* will travel. The verse teaches: *af al pi shenasa, ohel moed hu* — even while in transit and on the move, it retains the status of *Ohel Moed*. The simple reading of this verse lends itself naturally to Rashi's position — in transit there is still *machaneh shechinah*, so there is no *psul b'yotze* — and Tosafot would presumably read it as: the *Ohel Moed* is indeed in transit and dismantled, but the *ohel moed* status persists, while still requiring the curtains to remain in place to define *machaneh shechinah*.

## Mizbeach History

### Shiloh Stone Altar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מִזְבֵּחַ שֶׁל שִׁילֹה – שֶׁל אֲבָנִים הָיָה. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֲבָנִים״ ״אֲבָנִים״ ״אֲבָנִים״ שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים? אֶחָד שֶׁל שִׁילֹה, וְאֶחָד שֶׁל נוֹב וְגִבְעוֹן, וּבֵית עוֹלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara raises another discussion concerning the altar: **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says: The altar in Shiloh was** fashioned **of stones,** unlike the portable altar constructed in the time of Moses, which was fashioned from copper. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says: Why** must **the verses state** that the altar must be fashioned from **stones** (Exodus 20:22), and state again that the altar must be fashioned from **stones** (Deuteronomy 27:5), and mention yet again the word **stones** (Deuteronomy 27:6), with regard to the altar, for a total of **three times?** These allude to three different stone altars: **One in Shiloh, and one in Nov and Gibeon, and** one in the **Eternal House,** i.e., the Temple.

The *Gemara* now transitions to a historical survey of the *mizbeiach* from Moshe through Shlomo and into the Second *Beit HaMikdash*. Rav Huna states in the name of Rav: *mizbe'ach shel Shilo shel avanim haya* — the altar at Shiloh, where the *Mishkan* was established after entering *Eretz Yisrael*, was made of stone. The copper altar of Moshe's era was replaced with a large stone altar.

### Three Avanim

A *braita* is cited to support this: Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov asks, *ma talmud lomar avanim avanim avanim* — why does the verse about building the altar upon entering *Eretz Yisrael* repeat the word *avanim* three times? *Avanim shelemot tivneh et mizbe'ach Hashem Elokecha, lo tanif aleihem barzel* — you shall build the altar of God from whole stones, you shall not lift iron upon them. From the triple repetition, Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov derives that there were three distinct stone altars: *echad shel Shilo* — one at Shiloh, where, even though the curtain-and-curtain *Mishkan* structure continued from Moshe's era, a new stone altar was built to accommodate the increased volume of the nation entering the land, and perhaps also to express a sense of greater permanence; *ve'echad shel Nov veGivon* — one at Nov and Givon, where even without the full *Mishkan* structure there was a *bama gedola*, a major altar, also of stone; and *u'veit olamim* — and the third is the *Beit HaMikdash* itself. By the time the nation entered *Eretz Yisrael*, the central altars were already stone.

### Fire on Copper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב אַחָא בַּר אַמֵּי: אֵשׁ שֶׁיָּרְדָה מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם בִּימֵי מֹשֶׁה – לֹא נִסְתַּלְּקָה מֵעַל מִזְבַּח הַנְּחוֹשֶׁת אֶלָּא בִּימֵי שְׁלֹמֹה, וְאֵשׁ שֶׁיָּרְדָה בִּימֵי שְׁלֹמֹה – לֹא נִסְתַּלְּקָה עַד שֶׁבָּא מְנַשֶּׁה וְסִילְּקָהּ. וְאִם אִיתָא, מֵעִיקָּרָא הוּא דְּאִיסְתַּלַּק לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ami raises an objection** based on a *baraita*: The **fire that descended from Heaven** upon the altar **in the days of Moses** (see Leviticus 9:24) **departed from atop the copper altar only in the days of Solomon,** when he replaced the copper altar with a stone altar, **and** the **fire that descended** upon the altar **in the days of Solomon did not depart until Manasseh came and removed it** by destroying the altar. **And if it is so** that the altar in Shiloh was fashioned of stones, **it** emerges **that** the fire **departed** the copper altar **earlier,** when the stone altar in Shiloh replaced the copper altar of Moses, many years before King Solomon.

Rav Acha bar Ami challenges this. He cites a *braita* from *Seder Olam*: *esh sheyarda min hashamayim bimei Moshe lo nistalka me'al mizbe'ach hanechoshet ela bimei Shlomo* — the divine fire that descended in the time of Moshe (the fire we read about in *parshat Shmini*, which consumed the sacrifices at the inauguration) never departed from the copper altar until the time of Shlomo. And then: the divine fire that descended in the time of Shlomo similarly did not depart until *Menashe* the evil king caused it to be removed. If, as Rav Huna says, they replaced the copper altar with a stone altar as soon as they arrived at Shiloh, the divine fire should have left the copper altar at that point — or at minimum, the source should have described it as transferring. But it says explicitly that it remained on the copper altar. This implies the copper altar remained in active use straight through until the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Hybrid Altar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר – כְּרַבִּי נָתָן; דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: מִזְבֵּחַ שֶׁל שִׁילֹה – שֶׁל נְחוֹשֶׁת הָיָה, חָלוּל וּמָלֵא אֲבָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Rav Huna **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Natan, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says: The altar in Shiloh was** fashioned **of copper;** it was **hollow and full of stones.**

The *Gemara* responds: *hu de'amar k'Rabbi Natan* — Rav Huna is following the position of Rabbi Natan. As the *braita* records: *Rabbi Natan omer, mizbe'ach sheb'Shilo shel nechoshet haya, chalul umalei avanim* — the altar at Shiloh was made of copper but was hollow and filled with stones. It was not the same altar as Moshe's solid copper altar; a new one was fashioned, with a copper exterior shell and stones filling the interior. This is an interesting compromise — presumably because the rest of the structure retained the appearance of the *Mishkan*, they wished to preserve some visual continuity with the copper altar, while at the same time incorporating stone, perhaps as a marker that this was not yet the final resting place. According to this view, two *braitot* reflect two genuine opinions about the Shiloh altar.

### Coexisting Altars

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: מַאי לֹא נִסְתַּלְּקָה – לֹא נִסְתַּלְּקָה לְבַטָּלָה. מַאי הִיא? רַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: שְׁבִיבָא הֲוָה מְשַׁדְּרָא. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: אוּשְׁפִּיזָא הֲוָה נָקֵט, וְזִימְנִין הָכָא וְזִימְנִין הָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says** there is an alternative answer: **What** is the meaning of the statement in the *baraita* that the fire **did not depart** until the days of Solomon? It means that **it did not depart** in a manner in which it was **nullified;** it was still somewhat present in Shiloh on the copper altar, which stood together with the stone altar. The Gemara asks: **What is it,** i.e., what does it mean that the fire did not depart in a manner in which it was nullified? The Gemara answers: **The Rabbis say:** The fire on the copper altar **would emit sparks** toward the stone altar when the priests would sacrifice offerings on the stone altar. **Rav Pappa says:** The fire **was** as **a guest; sometimes** it was **here,** on the copper altar, **and sometimes** it was **there,** on the stone altar.

Rav Adda bar Yitzchak offers a more elegant resolution that avoids positing a *machloket*. His answer: *mai lo nistalka? lo nistalka levatala* — when the source says the divine fire did not depart from the copper altar, it does not mean the copper altar remained the functional altar of use. Rather: *rabbanan amri shaviva hava meshadra* — the fire would send out bolts and sparks. The picture Rashi draws is striking: in Shiloh there were, in fact, two altars. The large stone altar was the one actively used for sacrifices. But the small copper altar from Moshe's era stood alongside it, and the divine fire resided on the copper altar. When sacrifices were offered on the stone altar, the divine fire would send out a bolt — a spark — from the copper to the stone, connecting the two.

This image carries a weight beyond the technical. The new, larger altar is where the work of *avodah* happens; but the genuine divine fire, the original heavenly presence, remains tethered to the older altar. The nation is doing its own thing, in a sense, but borrowing from the original source of sacred fire. It is a picture of continuity and transition simultaneously.

### Fire Rounds

Rav Pappa offers yet another reading: *ushpiza havei nakit, zimnin hacha zimnin hacha* — the fire would do its rounds like a host visiting different places. Sometimes it would be on the copper altar, sometimes on the stone. The contrast with Rav Adda's view is subtle but meaningful. Rav Adda pictures the fire permanently on the copper altar, sending out occasional bolts to the stone. Rav Pappa pictures the fire alternating, fully present at one and then the other. If Rav Adda's image suggests the old is the permanent and genuine source from which the new borrows, Rav Pappa's image of alternation conveys something different: a moment of genuine transition, where neither the old nor the new has exclusive claim on the divine presence — a hovering between one era and the next, appropriate to the liminal period before the *Beit HaMikdash* established a final and permanent home.

## Expansion Reasons

### Footprint Extended

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: וּכְשֶׁעָלוּ בְּנֵי הַגּוֹלָה – הוֹסִיפוּ עָלָיו אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת מִן הַדָּרוֹם וְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת מִן הַמַּעֲרָב, כְּמִין גַּמָּא. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא סָפַק.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues discussing the altar: **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Middot* 35b): The altar in the First Temple was twenty-eight by twenty-eight cubits. **When the members of the exile ascended** to Jerusalem in the beginning of the Second Temple period, **they added four cubits to it on the south and four cubits on the west** sides of the altar, **like** the shape of the Greek letter **gamma,** i.e., the additions made a right angle. As a result, the altar in the Second Temple was thirty-two by thirty-two cubits. The Gemara asks: **What was the reason** for this expansion? **Rav Yosef said: Because** the size of the altar from the First Temple **was not sufficient.**

The *Gemara* cites a *mishnah* from tractate *Middot*: *uchshe'alu Bnei Gola* — when the returnees from exile came up in the time of Ezra — *hosifu alav arba amot min hadarom ve'arba amot min hama'arav, k'min gamma* — they added four *amot* from the south and four *amot* from the west, in the shape of a gamma.

To understand the significance of these numbers, one must recall the earlier discussion of dimensions. The *mizbeiach* of Shlomo had a base of 28×28 *amot*. Going inward one *amah* for the *yesod*, one *amah* for the *sovev*, and then allowing for the *karnot* and the walkway — a total of four *amot* inward on each side — a 28×28 base yields a top surface of 20×20. The *Bnei Gola* extended the base: four *amot* added to the south and four to the west, bringing the footprint to 32×32. On a 32×32 base, the same four-*amah* reduction on each side yields a top surface of 24×24. This was not an addition to an existing structure — obviously one cannot simply attach to an existing *mizbeiach* — but the new *mizbeiach* was built with this larger footprint as its foundation.

### Population Reason

Rav Yosef offers an explanation for why the *Bnei Gola* enlarged the *mizbeiach*: *mishum d'lo sipaik* — the older dimensions were insufficient; they needed greater surface area to handle the volume of offerings.

### Abaye Counters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הַשְׁתָּא מִקְדָּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״יְהוּדָה וְיִשְׂרָאֵל רַבִּים כַּחוֹל אֲשֶׁר עַל שְׂפַת הַיָּם״ – סְפֵק; מִקְדָּשׁ שֵׁנִי, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״כׇּל הַקָּהָל כְּאֶחָד אַרְבַּע רִבּוֹא״ – לֹא סָפֵק?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם אֵשׁ שֶׁל שָׁמַיִם מְסַיַּיעְתָּן, הָכָא אֵין אֵשׁ שֶׁל שָׁמַיִם מְסַיַּיעְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: Now,** if in **the First Temple** era, **about which it is written: "Judah and Israel were many as the sand that is by the sea"** (I Kings 4:20), the altar **was sufficient,** how could it be that in **the Second Temple** era, **about which it is written: "The whole congregation together was forty** and two **thousand** three hundred and sixty" (Ezra 2:64), the altar **was not sufficient?** Rav Yosef **said to** Abaye: **There,** in the First Temple, a **heavenly fire** would **assist them** and consume the offerings. **Here,** in the Second Temple, there was **no heavenly fire** that would **assist them.** Therefore, they needed a larger area in which to burn the offerings.

Abaye immediately challenges this. He points to the verse about the First Temple period, which describes the population of *Yehudah veYisrael rabim k'chol asher al hayam* — as numerous as the sands of the sea. With that enormous population, the 28×28 base was sufficient. The Second Temple began with *kol kahal ke'echad arba ribu* — the entire assembled community numbered a mere 40,000 people who made *aliyah*. How could a far smaller population require a larger altar?

### Divine Fire Speed

The response resolves the paradox: *hasam eish shel shamayim mesayasa, hacha ein eish shel shamayim mesayasa* — there the heavenly fire helped; here the heavenly fire did not help. The divine fire consumed offerings miraculously and instantaneously in the First Temple period — there was no waiting for things to burn naturally — so the surface area of the altar was effectively unlimited in its throughput. As soon as one sacrifice was placed, it was consumed, and the next could begin. In the Second Temple, the burning had to proceed naturally, requiring sacrifices to occupy the altar surface for extended periods. A note must be added here: Rashi, drawing on another *Gemara*, clarifies that there was indeed a heavenly fire in the Second Temple period — to say otherwise would raise severe theological difficulties about the validity of the *avodah* entirely — but that fire did not accelerate the consuming. It was present as a *siman* of divine presence but did not perform the miraculous rapid consumption of the First Temple's heavenly fire. Hence the need for greater surface area.

### Wine Libation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי מִשּׁוּם בַּר קַפָּרָא: שִׁיתִין הוֹסִיפוּ. מֵעִיקָּרָא סְבוּר: ״מִזְבַּח אֲדָמָה״ – שֶׁהוּא אָטוּם בָּאֲדָמָה. וּלְבַסּוֹף סְבוּר: שְׁתִיָּה כַּאֲכִילָה, וּמַאי ״מִזְבַּח אֲדָמָה״ – שֶׁהוּא מְחוּבָּר בַּאֲדָמָה;
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he reported that which **Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi says in the name of Bar Kappara** with regard to the expansion of the altar: **They expanded** the altar to extend over the underground **cavities** into which the libations flowed. **Initially,** in the First Temple era, **they held** that when the verse states: **"An altar of earth** you shall make for Me" (Exodus 20:21), it means **that it** should be completely **filled with earth. But ultimately,** in the Second Temple era, **they maintained** that the altar's **drinking is like** its **eating,** i.e., just as the offerings are burned upon the altar, so too, the libations must be poured onto the altar itself and not down its side. Consequently, they expanded the altar to cover the underground cavities, and created holes in the altar so that the libations could be poured on top of the altar and flow into the underground cavities. **And** according to this, **what** is the meaning of the phrase **"an altar of earth"?** It teaches **that** the altar must **be attached to the earth,**

A second reason for the expansion is then offered: *shissin hosifu* — the addition was made in order to accommodate the *shittin*, the underground cavern that received the wine *nesachim*. The mechanism requires explanation.

### Hole Position

Rashi describes the situation as follows. In the First Temple, when wine *nesachim* were poured at the southwest corner of the altar, the wine would run down the sides of the altar and flow into an exterior pit — a *bor* — situated just outside and below the corner. In the Second Temple period, a different practice was desired: the wine should be absorbed into the altar itself, descending through an interior channel built into the structure and flowing down into the same underground well. However, for this to work, the base of the altar had to be positioned directly over the well, so that the corner of the altar — where the *shittin* opening was located — would be situated above it. Since the well was in a position slightly removed from where the First Temple footprint had placed the corner, the solution was to extend the base of the new altar southward and westward by four *amot* in each direction. This repositioned the southwest corner over the well, allowing an interior channel — a funnel — to carry the wine from the corner of the *mizbeiach* down into the underground *shittin*.

### Solid to Drinking

This practical adjustment rested on a conceptual shift. The earlier understanding of *mizbe'ach adamah* — the verse's requirement that the altar be of earth — had been interpreted to mean that the altar must be *atum be'adamah*, completely solid on the inside, with no hollow spaces. Under this interpretation, an interior channel was impossible. But a new interpretation prevailed: *shtiyah ke'achilah* — drinking is analogous to eating. Just as the sacrificial meat is consumed by the altar's fire, so too must the wine *nesachim* be consumed — not simply poured on the exterior and allowed to run away, but taken in by the altar itself. This conceptual equation of the altar's "drinking" with its "eating" justified — and indeed required — the interior cavity.

One might note a certain irony here: much of the earlier discussion had been oriented toward de-emphasizing the anthropomorphic dimensions of the *avodah*, stressing that it is God's fire that consumes, that the rising smoke is not literally food for God. And yet here the *Gemara* embraces the image wholeheartedly — the altar eats and the altar drinks. Whatever discomfort that imagery may occasion, the halachic point stands.

And then the *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: if *mizbe'ach adamah* prohibits any hollow interior, how could the *shittin* — the underground well — exist at all? After all, the *Gemara* in tractate Sukkah famously recounts that David built the *shittin*, drilling deep into the earth until he cracked open the *tehom*. Tosafot there addresses the question directly in a brief but pointed comment: *bor shel shittin lo hayah chashuv mechilah kivan de'tzorech mizbe'ach* — the pit of the *shittin* does not count as a *mechilah*, a forbidden void beneath the altar, because it serves a function of the altar itself. The prohibition is against constructing the altar over something extraneous — a vaulted room used for other purposes below. When the underground space is functionally integrated into the altar's *avodah*, it is part of the structure, not a disqualifying void beneath it.

## Ezra Dimensions

### Proper Dimensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, לָאו הַיְינוּ דְּתַנְיָא: ״וַיָּכִינוּ אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ עַל מְכוֹנָתוֹ״ – שֶׁהִגִּיעוּ לְסוֹף מִדּוֹתָיו?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that after reconsidering the reason for the expansion of the altar, **Rav Yosef said: Is this not as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"And they set the altar upon its bases"** (Ezra 3:3), which teaches **that** in the Second Temple the size of the altar **reached its full measure,** i.e., that it was the ideal size, whereas in the First Temple it was not the ideal size?

Rav Yosef then introduces a *braita*: *vayachinu hamizbe'ach al mechonav* — the verse about Ezra's generation describes how they established the *mizbeiach* on its foundation. The *braita* teaches: *she'higiyu lesof midotav* — that in the time of Ezra they arrived at the proper, correct dimensions of the *mizbeiach*, dimensions that had not been fully known in the time of Shlomo. This is why they built it differently — not merely for practical reasons, but because they genuinely understood something about the *mizbeiach*'s true dimensions that Shlomo had not grasped.

### Better Than Shlomo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״הַכֹּל בִּכְתָב מִיַּד ה׳ עָלַי הִשְׂכִּיל״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written** with regard to the instructions David gave Solomon about how to build the Temple: **"All this in writing, as the Lord has made me wise by His hand upon me,** even all the works of this pattern" (I Chronicles 28:19), indicating that the design of the First Temple was dictated by God?

This is a striking *Gemara*. Shlomo is famously described as *hachacham mikol adam* — wiser than any person. Moreover, the verse in *Divrei HaYamim* records David himself saying *hakol bichsav miyad Hashem alai hiskil* — everything was revealed to me in writing from God. If the very dimensions of the *Beit HaMikdash* and its altar were communicated by divine revelation, how could Ezra's generation claim to know better? How can those who returned from Babylonian exile — a far smaller and less distinguished generation — have a more accurate understanding of the proper altar dimensions than Shlomo, who received direct divine communication?

### Verse Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: קְרָא אַשְׁכַּח וּדְרַשׁ – ״וַיֹּאמֶר דָּוִיד: זֶה הוּא בֵּית ה׳ הָאֱלֹהִים, וְזֶה מִּזְבֵּחַ לְעֹלָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל״ – כִּי בַּיִת; מָה בַּיִת שִׁשִּׁים אַמָּה, אַף מִזְבֵּחַ שִׁשִּׁים אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef said:** The size of the altar in the First Temple was ideal, but in the Second Temple era there was a need to expand the altar, and they **found a verse and interpreted** it as follows. The verse states: **"Then David said: This is the House of the Lord God, and this is the altar of burnt offering for Israel"** (I Chronicles 22:1). The verse juxtaposes the House, i.e., the Temple, with the altar, which indicates that the altar is **like** the **Temple: Just as** the **House** was **sixty cubits** (see I Kings 6:2), **so too,** the **altar** may be extended up to a length of **sixty cubits.**

Rav Yosef resolves this: *kra ashkach ve'daraish* — they found a verse and interpreted it. This very phrase is used in a parallel context elsewhere, regarding changes made in the Second Temple. Ezra's generation did not simply innovate or claim to know better through personal wisdom. They engaged in *drash* — they looked carefully at a verse and derived from it a new understanding.

### House Analogy

The verse is from *Divrei HaYamim*, where David says: *zeh hu Beis Hashem Elokim ve'zeh mizbe'ach le'olah le'Yisrael* — this is the house of God, and this is the *mizbeiach* for the *olah* for Israel. The word *ki bayit* — "like a house" — then provides the basis for a *gezerah shavah* or analogical derivation: *ma bayit* — what is the house, meaning David's palace — *shishim amah* — was 60 *amot* in one dimension; *af mizbe'ach shishim amah* — so too the *mizbeiach* has a dimension of 60 *amot*. Rashi clarifies that this does not mean the *mizbeiach* must be 60×60; it means the *mizbeiach* may be built within a zone of up to 60×60 *amot*. The 28×28 of Shlomo's altar was not invalid — but it was not the only legitimate possibility. The verse reveals that within a 60×60 space, there is halakhic room to expand.

What Ezra's generation discovered, then, was not that Shlomo had erred. They discovered that the existing dimensions had not been the only valid option. They were permitted — and, given the circumstances, wise — to build larger. The practical need for more surface area (absent the accelerating divine fire) and the desire to position the *shittin* properly under the altar both pointed in the same direction, and the verse confirmed that expanding within the 60×60 limit was entirely legitimate.

### Oral Torah Space

This conclusion resonates far beyond the technical question of altar dimensions. The entire episode is, in essence, a parable for the relationship between *Torah Shebe'al Peh* and the received tradition. The instinct would be to reconstruct the altar exactly as it had been — Shlomo built it as 28×28; who are we, returning from exile with a fraction of the population and none of the grandeur of the First Temple period, to do otherwise? The answer is *kra ashkach ve'daraish*: we looked at a verse and we interpreted. And that interpretation — a genuine act of *drash*, not mere innovation — revealed that there is more space than the received practice had utilized.

The keyword is *darash*. It is not a simple, literal reading of a verse. It is an insight, an act of interpretation that unlocks latent meaning. And that interpretive act reveals that the tradition, while binding, is not so rigid as to admit only one possible configuration. There is halakhic space, room to move, room to respond to changed circumstances. The First Temple had heavenly fire that made the smaller surface sufficient; the Second Temple does not, so more surface is needed. The First Temple poured wine on the exterior; the Second Temple wants the altar to drink it internally. These are genuine differences, and the tradition — properly interpreted — provides room for them.

*Af mizbe'ach shishim amah* — the *mizbeiach* can be up to 60 *amot*. That is the outer boundary. Within it, each generation works with what it has, interprets as it must, and builds accordingly.
