# Yoma 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Surplus Animals

### R. Yehuda vs. Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח שָׂעִיר, פַּר מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? גְּזֵירָה פַּר אַטּוּ שָׂעִיר. וּמִשּׁוּם גְּזֵירָה יָמוּתוּ?
>
> **Translation:** This works out **well** with regard to the Yom Kippur **goat.** However, with regard to the High Priest's **bull** which he pays for himself and which is considered an individual offering, **what is there to say?** The Gemara answers: There is **a** rabbinic **decree** with regard to the **bull due to the goat.** The Gemara asks: Is it right that **due to a** rabbinic **decree** the offerings **should** be left to **die** instead of being left to graze?

The Gemara opens with a question that has been under discussion: what happens when one of the paired animals — the goat or the ox — is lost or dies and must be replaced, leaving the original animal unused? A debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the Chachamim governs this situation. The Chachamim hold that a communal *chatas* in this circumstance is sent out to pasture — even if a replacement was ultimately used — while Rabbi Yehuda holds that it must be left to die. The Gemara's question presses on Rabbi Yehuda's position: given that he elsewhere rules that money set aside for the *shekel* and later superseded may be carried forward to the following year, why not simply hold over the unused animal for next year's service? You sanctified the animal for this year; surely it could serve next year instead.

### Goat Age Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, כְּרַבִּי. דְּתַנְיָא: ״שָׁנָה תְּמִימָה״, מוֹנֶה שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת וְשִׁשִּׁים וַחֲמִשָּׁה יוֹם כְּמִנְיָן יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מוֹנֶה שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **This is not difficult,** as it is possible that the goat will not be disqualified, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to redeeming houses in a walled city the Torah states: "And if it not be redeemed within the space of **a full year"** (Leviticus 25:30), which indicates that **he counts 365 days, in accordance with the number** of **days** in a **solar year;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: He counts twelve months from day to day.**

The Gemara has been working through a series of answers, some applying to the ox, some to the goat, each falling short of a fully satisfying resolution. The most recent and seemingly obvious answer is that by the following year the goat would be more than a year old, and the goat offering must come from within its first year of life. So straightforward is this answer that one wonders why the Gemara did not propose it at the outset.

The Gemara then adds a further wrinkle: it invokes a *gezeira* based on the possibility that the goat will not yet be a year old. The Gemara appeals here to the position of Rebbi, who rules in the context of the sale of a house in a walled city — where the Torah specifies *shana temima*, a full year — that one follows the solar year rather than the lunar year. It is thus theoretically possible, however rarely, that by the following year's *Yom Kippur* the goat would still fall within its first solar year. To cover even that edge case, the Gemara invokes the principle of *lo pelug* — we do not distinguish within the rule. This is admittedly strange on two counts: first, why legislate for such a far-fetched scenario? Second, Rebbi's solar-year calculation was stated specifically in the context of walled-city sales, not animal age. Nevertheless, this line of reasoning explains why the goat cannot simply be held over.

### Ox Gezeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח שָׂעִיר, פַּר מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? גְּזֵירָה פַּר אַטּוּ שָׂעִיר. וּמִשּׁוּם גְּזֵירָה יָמוּתוּ?
>
> **Translation:** This works out **well** with regard to the **goat,** but with regard to the **bull** of the High Priest, which is not chosen by lottery, **what is there to say?** The Gemara answers: There is **a** rabbinic **decree** with regard to the **bull due to** the **goat.** The Gemara challenges further: Is it right that **due to a** rabbinic **decree** the offerings **should** be left to **die** instead of being left to graze?

The Gemara is not yet satisfied. It asks: *hateinach se'ir* — granted, this explains the goat; *par mai ika lemeimar* — but what prevents holding the ox over to next year? The Gemara's answer is *gezeira par atu se'ir*: we decree that the ox may not be held over lest one come to hold the goat over as well, even though the goat would then be too old. The ox, by contrast, has considerably more latitude with respect to age — a range of roughly two to four years depending on the opinion, with Rabbi Meir's position that three years and above is always acceptable — so age is not itself an obstacle for the ox. The concern is one of conflation: permitting the ox to be held over might lead to permitting the goat as well, where the age issue does pose a genuine problem.

But the Gemara remains dissatisfied. Is this *gezeira* — this somewhat remote concern — really sufficient reason to mandate that the animal die? The word *umishum gezeira yamut* captures the Gemara's incredulity.

### Resh Lakish Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, חַטָּאת שֶׁעִבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ — לִרְעִיָּה אָזְלָא! דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: חַטָּאת שֶׁעִבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ, רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ הִיא עוֹמֶדֶת בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת וְרוֹעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** a **sin-offering whose year has passed** is not left to die but rather **goes to graze. As Reish Lakish said: We consider a sin-offering whose year has passed as though it stands in a cemetery,** and the priest cannot take it out in order to sacrifice it because he is not permitted to become ritually defiled. Therefore, **it grazes** until it becomes unfit and is then sold.

The Gemara now introduces a further complication. Even if one accepts the age argument as a reason not to hold the goat over, why does it follow that the animal must die? Resh Lakish rules that a *chatas* whose year has passed — meaning it can no longer be offered because it is over-age — does not die. Rather, *ro'in osah ke'ilu hi omedes b'veis hakvaros*, we regard it as if it were standing in a cemetery: inaccessible, but not intrinsically invalid. It goes out to pasture. Thus even granting that the goat cannot be used next year due to its age, the proper disposition should be to let it graze — not to let it die. So the Gemara must still explain Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that the animal dies.

## Takala Rationale

### Rava's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם תַּקָּלָה. אֵין מַקְדִּישִׁין, וְאֵין מַעֲרִיכִין, וְאֵין מַחְרִימִין בַּזְּמַן הַזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The bull and goat of Yom Kippur may not be left from one year to the next by rabbinic **decree due to** a concern that a **mishap** may occur. As it was taught in a *baraita*: **One may neither consecrate** objects, **nor valuate,** i.e., take a vow to donate one's value to the Temple treasury, **nor dedicate** items **for sacred use at this time,** when the Temple no longer exists.

At this point the Gemara offers the resolution that had in fact been anticipated in prior discussion: *ella amar Rava*, here is Rava's answer. The reason you may not hold these animals over until the following year is *gezeira mishum takala* — a concern of stumbling, meaning a fear that one will come to accidentally derive benefit from or misuse the animal during the intervening period. An animal set aside and awaiting use a year hence sits in your mind as something that will eventually be brought; and precisely because it remains a viable candidate for the altar, you remain engaged with it, and the risk of inadvertent transgression is real.

### Hekdesh Disposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הִקְדִּישׁ וְהֶעֱרִיךְ וְהֶחְרִים, בְּהֵמָה — תֵּיעָקֵר. פֵּירוֹת כְּסוּת וְכֵלִים — יֵרָקְבוּ, מָעוֹת וּכְלֵי מַתָּכוֹת — יוֹלִיךְ הֲנָאָה לְיָם הַמֶּלַח. וְאֵי זֶה הוּא עִיקּוּר — נוֹעֵל דֶּלֶת לְפָנֶיהָ, וְהִיא מֵתָה מֵאֵלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if one did consecrate, valuate, or dedicate** items for sacred use: If he dedicated **an animal it is uprooted,** i.e., he arranges for it to die quickly. If he dedicated agricultural **produce, garments, or vessels** made from materials that decompose, he should store them until **they decompose.** And if he dedicated **money or metal vessels, he** should redeem them and **transport** the value of their **benefit to the Dead Sea.** This *baraita* indicates that animals that cannot be sacrificed at this time must be left to die so that they are not used improperly. The Gemara explains: **And what** constitutes **uprooting? He locks the door before it, and it dies on its own** from hunger.

The Gemara brings a *baraita* to anchor this concern: *ein makdishin ve'ein maarichin ve'ein machrimin bazman hazeh* — nowadays one does not sanctify animals for *hekdesh*, does not undertake *ha'arakhah* (pledging the assessed value of a person or object to the Temple treasury), and does not declare *cherem* (another form of consecration to the treasury). The reason is the very concern of *takala* — that with no functioning Temple to absorb these sanctified items, they will simply sit in one's possession and create opportunities for misuse.

If one has nevertheless done so, the law prescribes specific modes of disposal. An animal — *behema* — is subject to *te'aker*. Fruit, clothing, and ordinary vessels — *peirot, ksut, vekeilim* — are left to rot, *yirakvu*. Money and metal objects — *ma'ot uchlei matachot* — are cast into the Dead Sea, *yolichem leyam hamelach*. The phrase used for money is *yolich hana'ah*, which is slightly unusual — it could suggest transferring the monetary value rather than the object itself, though in context it may simply mean throwing the coins themselves into the sea.

Tosafot raises a pointed question here: why not simply transfer the *kedusha* to a nominal sum of money and dispose of that? There is even a teaching that nowadays, since no actual Temple treasury will benefit, one may transfer *kedusha* at a steep discount — sanctifying something worth a thousand and transferring it to an object worth one. Why not use that mechanism? Tosafot acknowledges that in some cases this is indeed done, but explains that here the law deliberately does not make it so easy. The goal is not merely to solve the technical problem but to introduce a degree of consequence — not so much punitive as preventive — that discourages people from developing the habit of casual sanctification in a world without a Temple.

### Te'aker Definition

The Gemara asks: *ve'eizo hi ikur* — what exactly is *te'aker*? Literally the word suggests something like hamstringing, but the Gemara clarifies: *no'el delet befaneha umeta me'eleha* — you lock the door in front of it, you enclose it in a pen, and you let it die on its own. You do not actively destroy it, because to do so would constitute *bizayon hekdesh* — a degradation of consecrated property. But you do take a definitive step to render it inaccessible and to ensure that it will go to waste. The underlying point is that consecrated property may not simply sit available; it must be placed in a condition where its destruction is assured and its misuse is impossible.

### Takala Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּקָּלָה דְּמַאי? אִי תַּקָּלָה דְּהַקְרָבָה, אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל רְעִיּוֹת נָמֵי! אִי תַּקָּלָה דְּגִיזָּה וַעֲבוֹדָה, אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל רְעִיּוֹת נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What mishap** might occur if the bull and goat are left until the next year? **If** you say it is a **mishap of offering** the animal as a different offering, a similar concern should exist **even** with regard to **all** animals that have been disqualified for use as offerings that one leaves to **graze. If** it is a **mishap of shearing** the animal's wool **and working** the animal, which would constitute unlawful use of consecrated property, a similar concern should exist **even** with regard to **all** animals that have been disqualified for use as offerings that are left to **graze.** Why are these animals in particular left to die?

The Gemara now asks a more granular question: *takala demai* — what specific form of stumbling is the concern? Two possibilities are considered. The first is that we fear the person will actually come to offer the animal as a sacrifice, even though it is not currently fit for that purpose — either because its year has not yet come around, or because it was sanctified for a different service. The second possibility is a lesser concern: not that it will be brought to the altar, but that one will come to derive mundane benefit from it, such as shearing its wool or using it for labor.

The Gemara raises an objection to both possibilities: *afillu kol re'uyot nami* — if we are worried about either form of misuse, we should apply the same concern to all animals that are put out to pasture awaiting a blemish so that their value can be redeemed. Why are we comfortable letting those animals graze without worrying about misuse?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם תַּקָּלָה דְּהַקְרָבָה, וְהָנָךְ דְּלָאו בְּנֵי הַקְרָבָה נִינְהוּ — לָא טְרִיד בְּהוּ, הָךְ דְּבַת הַקְרָבָה הִיא — טְרִיד בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the concern is indeed that **the animal might be accidentally brought as an offering. And those** other disqualified animals that are left to graze, **which are not fit for sacrifice, he is not preoccupied with,** and will not accidentally sacrifice them. **This** bull and goat, **which are fit for sacrifice** next year, **he is preoccupied with** them. Therefore, there is a greater concern that one may sacrifice them as offerings, and they may not be left to graze.

The Gemara resolves this by drawing a critical distinction: *le'olam takala de-hakrava* — the concern is indeed that the animal might be accidentally brought as an offering. *Henakh de-lav bar hakrava ninhu lo tarid behu* — those animals that can never be brought to the altar themselves — those that must graze until they develop a blemish and whose value is then redeemed — nobody is thinking of actually bringing the animal itself. Its path to the altar is categorically foreclosed, and so people put it out of their minds. *Ha de-var hakrava hu tarid bah* — but this animal, which one is holding over because it will in fact be eligible to be brought next *Yom Kippur*, remains constantly on one's mind. One is actively anticipating using it, and that mental engagement creates the risk that before the appointed time arrives, one might inadvertently misuse it.

### Animal Categories

The Gemara's answer, it must be admitted, is not entirely satisfying in all directions. Taking stock of where we stand: the case of the ox is now explained — in theory, it could be held over and used next year, but because one's mind remains fixed on it as a future offering, we prohibit holding it over on grounds of *takala* and require that it die. That is somewhat counterintuitive — one might observe that the very seriousness with which someone intends to bring it as a *korban* is being used as the reason to destroy it — but that is the Gemara's logic.

The case of the goat, however, remains partially unresolved. The goat will not be fit for use next year due to its age; it is thus in the same category as an animal that will never be brought to the altar itself. According to the Gemara's own logic, animals in that category are not subject to *takala* concerns — people don't think about them — and accordingly should be left to graze, not die. Furthermore, Resh Lakish's position that an over-age *chatas* goes to pasture and does not die has not been fully addressed. So the answer works cleanly for the ox but leaves something to be desired for the goat — and notably for those who accept Resh Lakish, the question of why the animal must die in either case remains somewhat open.

One might also raise the concern of *tzaar ba'alei chayim* — the prohibition against causing unnecessary animal suffering. It is striking that the Gemara institutes what amounts to a slow death by confinement in order to forestall an admittedly remote risk of human inadvertence. Some Rishonim do cite cases like *te'aker* as evidence that the prohibition of *tzaar ba'alei chayim* can be overridden for legitimate human needs — analogizing, for instance, to the use of animals for medical purposes. But in this context the "human need" is ritual observance and the prevention of transgression, and the suffering imposed on the animal serves no benefit to it whatsoever. The dissonance is real, especially given the Torah's own solicitude for animals — the obligation to assist a struggling donkey, for instance. There is no fully satisfying answer here.

### Takala Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַקָּלָה עַצְמָהּ תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא חֲדָא: פֶּסַח שֶׁלֹּא קָרַב בְּרִאשׁוֹן — יִקְרַב בַּשֵּׁנִי, בַּשֵּׁנִי — יִקְרַב לְשָׁנָה הַבָּאָה. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: לֹא יִקְרַב. מַאי לָאו, בְּתַקָּלָה פְּלִיגִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This issue **itself,** i.e., whether decrees are instituted due to a concern about a possible **mishap, is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*. As it was taught** in **one** *baraita*: **A Paschal lamb that was not sacrificed on the first** *Pesaḥ* is sold to someone who was impure on the first *Pesaḥ* or who was distant from Jerusalem, so that it may be **sacrificed on the second** *Pesaḥ*. If it was not sacrificed **on the second** *Pesaḥ*, **it is sacrificed the following year. And it was taught** in **another** *baraita*: If it was not sacrificed on the second *Pesaḥ*, **it is not sacrificed** the following year. **What, is it not that they disagree about** the question of whether a decree was issued prohibiting keeping the animal for an entire year due to a concern about the possibility of a **mishap?**

The Gemara adds a further observation: *u-v-takala atzma tannai hi* — the underlying question of whether we are concerned about *takala* in cases of animals held over for future use is itself a dispute among Tannaim.

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: *pesach she-lo karev be-Rishon yikarev be-Sheni* — if one sanctified an animal for *korban Pesach* and did not bring it on the first *Pesach*, give it to someone who will be bringing on *Pesach Sheni* in Iyar; and if it was not brought then either, hold it over for the following year. A second *baraita* flatly contradicts this: *lo yikarev* — do not bring it the following year.

The Gemara asks: is this not a dispute about *takala* — whether holding a *korban* over for an entire year creates an unacceptable risk of misuse? One *baraita* permits it, unconcerned; the other forbids it, apparently concerned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא [לָא] חָיְישִׁינַן לְתַקָּלָה, וְהָכָא בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי וְרַבָּנַן קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי, הָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** these sources do not prove that the *tanna'im* disputed this issue. It is possible **that everyone agrees** that **we are not concerned about a mishap, and here they disagree about the dispute between Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and the Rabbis** with regard to whether it is possible that the lamb will still be fit for sacrifice the following year. **And** the apparent contradiction between the *baraitot* is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which says that the lamb is brought the following year, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **That** *baraita*, which says that the lamb may not be sacrificed the following year, is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that by the following year, the lamb will certainly be more than a year old and will therefore be unfit as a Paschal offering.

The Gemara considers but then partially deflects this reading. The debate over the *Pesach* animal itself may simply turn on whether one follows Rebbi's solar year — the same question about whether the animal can still be within its first year by the time of the next *Pesach*, not a principled dispute about *takala* at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: וְכֵן הַמָּעוֹת! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ — בְּתַקָּלָה פְּלִיגִי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this rejection: **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **And, so too, money** that was dedicated for purchasing a Paschal lamb is subject to dispute about whether it may be kept for the following year. In the case of money, the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis is irrelevant. **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from this that they disagree with regard to** whether a decree is issued due to a concern about **a mishap?** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** that it is so.

However, the Gemara then cites yet another *baraita*: *ve-chen ma'ot* — and similarly with money sanctified for a *korban Pesach*. Here too there is a debate: hold the money over for next year, or do not. And here the age question is entirely irrelevant — money does not become over-age. Therefore, *shma mina*, this dispute must be about *takala* itself: do we fear that holding sanctified money over for a year will lead to misuse?

This is the critical distinction the Gemara has been building toward. At one extreme: an animal put out to pasture to await a blemish, with no expectation of ever being brought itself — no *takala* concern, all opinions agree. At the other extreme: sanctified objects in the present era with no Temple in sight, to be held indefinitely — clearly a *takala* concern, all opinions agree, and the law mandates destruction. The middle case is the one genuinely in dispute: an object sanctified for a *korban* that cannot be used now but will be eligible in a year's time. Is that a *takala* concern or not? That is the debate between the Tannaim, and it is precisely the debate underlying Rabbi Yehuda's ruling about the goat and the ox.

## Scapegoat Viduy

### Viduy Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בָּא לוֹ אֵצֶל שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, וְסוֹמֵךְ שְׁתֵּי יָדָיו עָלָיו וּמִתְוַדֶּה. וְכָךְ הָיָה אוֹמֵר: אָנָא הַשֵּׁם! חָטְאוּ, עָווּ, פָּשְׁעוּ לְפָנֶיךָ עַמְּךָ בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל. אָנָא הַשֵּׁם! כַּפֶּר נָא לַחֲטָאִים וְלָעֲוֹנוֹת וְלַפְּשָׁעִים שֶׁחָטְאוּ וְשֶׁעָווּ וְשֶׁפָּשְׁעוּ לְפָנֶיךָ עַמְּךָ בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל, כַּכָּתוּב בְּתוֹרַת מֹשֶׁה עַבְדֶּךָ לֵאמֹר: ״כִּי בַיּוֹם הַזֶּה יְכַפֵּר עֲלֵיכֶם לְטַהֵר אֶתְכֶם מִכֹּל חַטֹּאתֵיכֶם לִפְנֵי ה׳ תִּטְהָרוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Yom Kippur service continues: The High Priest **comes over to the scapegoat, places both his hands upon it, and confesses. And he would say as follows: Please, God, Your people, the house of Israel, have sinned, and done wrong, and rebelled before You. Please, God, grant atonement, please, for the sins, and for the wrongs, and for the rebellions that they have sinned, and done wrong, and rebelled before You, Your people, the house of Israel, as it is written in the Torah of Moses Your servant, saying: "For on this day atonement shall be made for you to cleanse you of all your sins; before the Lord you shall be purified"** (Leviticus 16:30).

With the questions surrounding surplus animals resolved — at least partially — the Gemara returns to the *Mishnah* and to the actual *avodah* of *Yom Kippur*. By this point in the service, the *Kohen Gadol* has completed the blood rites of the ox and the inner goat: the *ketores* in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, the sprinkling of the blood in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, then on the *Parochet*, then on the outer *mizbe'ach*, and finally the pouring of the blood at the base of the altar. Now the Mishnah turns to the *se'ir hamishtalei'ach*, the goat that will be sent away.

*Ba lo eitzel sa'ir hamishtalei'ach* — he comes to the goat that is to be dispatched. *Ve-someich shtei yadav alav* — he places both his hands upon it, and he recites the *viduy*, the confession.

### Viduy Text

The text of the *viduy* is as follows: *Anna Hashem, chatu avu pash'u lifanecha amcha beis Yisrael* — please, God, your people Israel have sinned, transgressed, and rebelled before You. The ordering of these three categories — *chet*, *avon*, and *pesha* — reflecting unintentional sin, deliberate iniquity, and willful rebellion respectively — is a matter that has already been discussed in the Gemara; the *girsa* in the printed *mishnayot* differs from what appears here, and that ordering is significant in its own right.

The *viduy* continues: *Anna Hashem, kaper na lachatayim vela'avonot veli-fsha'im shechatu ve-shavu she-pash'u lifanecha amcha beis Yisrael, kakatuv be-Toras Moshe avdecha leimor: ki vayom hazeh yechaper aleichem letaher eschem mikol chatoseichem lifnei Hashem titharu* — please, God, atone now for the sins, iniquities, and rebellions that Your people Israel have committed before You, as it is written in the Torah of your servant Moshe: for on this day atonement shall be made for you to purify you; from all your sins before God you shall be purified.

### Response Ritual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַכֹּהֲנִים וְהָעָם הָעוֹמְדִים בָּעֲזָרָה כְּשֶׁהָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִים שֵׁם הַמְּפוֹרָשׁ שֶׁהוּא יוֹצֵא מִפִּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, הָיוּ כּוֹרְעִים וּמִשְׁתַּחֲוִים וְנוֹפְלִים עַל פְּנֵיהֶם, וְאוֹמְרִים: ״בָּרוּךְ שֵׁם כְּבוֹד מַלְכוּתוֹ לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the priests and the people standing in the** Temple **courtyard, when they would hear the Explicit Name emerging from the mouth of the High Priest,** when the High Priest did not use one of the substitute names for God, **they would kneel and prostrate** themselves **and fall on their faces, and say: Blessed is the name of His glorious kingdom forever and ever.**

*Ve-hakohanim ve-ha'am ha'omdim ba'azarah, ke-she-hayu shom'im shem ha-meforash she-hu yotzei mipi Kohen Gadol, hayu kor'im u-mishtachavim ve-noflim al pneihem ve-omrim: baruch shem kevod malchuto le-olam va'ed* — the priests and the people standing in the *azarah*, when they heard the explicit divine name emerging from the mouth of the *Kohen Gadol*, would bow and prostrate themselves and fall upon their faces, saying: blessed is the name of His glorious kingdom forever and ever.

It is worth noting that this formulation differs from the dramatic liturgical climax familiar from our *machzor*. In the prayer service, the sequence builds to a moment where the *Kohen Gadol*, after naming God explicitly, pauses while the people prostrate themselves, and then finishes the verse with *titharu* — "you shall be purified" — in a soaring declaration of communal purity. The *Mishnah* here contains the same basic elements but does not stage them with quite that degree of theatrical buildup, and the word *titharu* does not appear as a climactic final pronouncement in the same way.

## Scapegoat Dispatch

### Handoff

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסָרוֹ לְמִי שֶׁהָיָה מוֹלִיכוֹ. הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִים לְהוֹלִיכוֹ, אֶלָּא שֶׁעָשׂוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים קֶבַע, וְלֹא הָיוּ מַנִּיחִין אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהוֹלִיכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Following the *viduy*, the priest **passed** the goat **to the one who was to lead it** to the wilderness. According to the *halakha*, **everyone is eligible to lead it, but the High Priests established** a **fixed** custom **and did not allow an Israelite to lead it.**

Following the *viduy*, the *Mishnah* states: *masru lemi she-hayu molicho* — he handed the goat over to the person who would carry it, running with it through the city and out to the cliff beyond the city limits. The *Mishnah* then establishes: *hakol kesherim leholicho* — anyone is eligible to perform this task.

### Kohein Monopoly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מַעֲשֶׂה וְהוֹלִיכוֹ עַרְסְלָא, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל הָיָה. וְכֶבֶשׁ עָשׂוּ לוֹ מִפְּנֵי הַבָּבְלִיִּים, שֶׁהָיוּ מְתַלְּשִׁים בִּשְׂעָרוֹ וְאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: טוֹל וָצֵא טוֹל וָצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said:** That was not always the case. There was **an incident where** a person named **Arsela led** the goat to the wilderness, **and he was an Israelite. And they made a ramp for** the goat **due to the Babylonian** Jews who were in Jerusalem, **who would pluck at** the goat's **hair and** would **say to** the goat: **Take** our sins **and go, take** our sins **and go,** and do not leave them with us.

Despite the theoretical eligibility of all, the *Mishnah* records: *ella she-asu hakohanim* — the *Kohanim* established a practice, *teva*, that they would not permit *Yisraelim* to perform this role. Even though the law allowed it, the custodians of the Temple service reserved this function for their own. Rabbi Yose notes an exception: there was a case in which Arselah of Tzippori performed this task, and he was a *Yisrael*. But the general practice remained in *Kohen* hands.

### Ramp Purpose

The *Mishnah* further records that a ramp was constructed for the person carrying out the goat, *mipnei haBavliyim* — on account of the Babylonians, who would pull at his hair and call out to him: *tol vatzei, tol vatzei* — take it and get out, take it and get out — apparently expressing a kind of frenzied urgency to be rid of the communal sins. The ramp provided a protected corridor so that the designated carrier could exit without interference. There is likely also a geographic dimension: the Temple Mount abuts the Kidron Valley, and a ramp spanning from the Temple precincts toward the Mount of Olives — where, notably, the *Parah Adumah* rite was also performed — would enable the carrier to traverse the topography more quickly and directly, just as such a ramp was used to convey the red heifer across the valley without descending all the way to the valley floor.

## Scapegoat Viduy (II)

### Viduy Recipients

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְאִילּוּ ״בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן עַם קְדוֹשֶׁךָ״ לָא קָאָמַר. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא אָמַר: יֵשׁ לָהֶם כַּפָּרָה בְּשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: In the confession over the scapegoat, the High Priest confessed the sins of the Jewish people, **whereas he did not say: The children of Aaron, Your sacred people,** in order to confess the sins of the priests. The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* that **taught** this mishna? **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** The mishna **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, for if** it were **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, didn't** Rabbi Yehuda **say:** The priests receive **atonement through the scapegoat,** which indicates that their sins must be mentioned in the confession over the scapegoat?

The *Mishnah* having completed its description of the *viduy* on the *se'ir hamishtalei'ach*, the Gemara now raises a pointed question: *ve-ilu bnei Aharon kedoshecha lo ka'amar* — in this *viduy*, the *Kohen Gadol* made no mention of *Bnei Aharon*, the priests. He spoke only of the people of Israel. *Man tanna* — who is the Tanna that holds this position?

Rabbi Yirmiyah says: this is not according to Rabbi Yehuda, because Rabbi Yehuda explicitly holds that the *Kohanim* do receive atonement through the *se'ir hamishtalei'ach* — *yesh lahem kappara be-sa'ir hamishtalei'ach* — and therefore he would expect the *Kohanim* to be named in the *viduy*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אַטּוּ כֹּהֲנִים לָאו בִּכְלַל ״עַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל״ נִינְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, is that to say that priests are not included among: Your people,** the house of **Israel?** Nothing can be proven from the fact that the High Priest did not list every segment of the Jewish people separately.

Abaye responds: even according to Rabbi Yehuda, the *Mishnah* can be explained. *Atu Kohanim lav bi-khlal amcha Yisrael ninhu?* — are *Kohanim* not included within "your people Israel"? Perhaps the very reason not to single them out is to emphasize their unity with the entire people. For issues of *tumat Mikdash* — the impurity of entering the Temple in a state of *tumah* — different groups bear distinct legal identities and are addressed separately. But for all other sins, the intent of the *viduy* may be precisely to underscore that *Kohanim* and *Yisraelim* stand together before God, and singling them out would undercut that message.

### Kohein vs. People

To understand the stakes of this debate, it is helpful to map the various atonements of *Yom Kippur*. There is the *par bifnim* — the ox whose blood is brought into the *Kodesh Kodashim* — and the *sa'ir bifnim* — the inner goat. These rites accomplish atonement specifically related to *tumat Mikdash*: the sin of entering or desecrating the Temple while in a state of *tumah*. According to the Gemara, the *par* atones for the *Kohanim* in this regard, and the inner *sa'ir* for the *Yisraelim*. Then there is the *se'ir hamishtalei'ach*, which, on the simpler reading, covers all other sins for all of *Klal Yisrael* — *Kohanim* included in *Yisrael* — the other six hundred and twelve commandments not addressed by the blood rites inside.

A more complex view, however, holds that the *se'ir hamishtalei'ach* with its *viduy* atones for the other sins of the *Yisraelim*, while the *Kohanim*'s atonement for all of their sins — not just *tumat Mikdash* but everything else — flows from a separate track. The *viduy* that the *Kohen Gadol* recited earlier over his own *par*, and the blood brought inside, account for the *Kohanim*'s other sins as well. Under this reading, the *viduy* alone — without any blood rite accompanying it — is what achieves *kappara* for the *Kohanim*'s non-*Mikdash* sins, which is a striking theological point: the power of the confession itself, independent of the sacrificial act, accomplishes atonement.

This connects to the observation we noted earlier: the *se'ir hamishtalei'ach* could, in principle, fall and die immediately after the *viduy* is recited, and the atonement would still be effective. According to one opinion, even without the *viduy*, the mere act of the *goral* — the lottery — suffices. What emerges is that the Yom Kippur service increasingly foregrounds the *viduy* as the locus of atonement, a move that one might read as pointing toward the post-Temple reality in which confession, rather than blood rite, becomes the central act.

The other dimension of the more complex view — that the *Kohanim*'s atonement is entirely separate from and different from that of the people — raises questions of its own. What does it mean that the *Kohanim*, even in their capacity as ordinary human beings rather than Temple functionaries, receive a categorically distinct *kappara*? The simpler reading, which sees the *tumat Mikdash* rites as the only point of differentiation and the *se'ir hamishtalei'ach* as covering everyone equally for all other sins, is in many ways more theologically comfortable. Both readings, however, are present in the Gemara.

## Scapegoat Dispatch (II)

### Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִישׁ״ — לְהַכְשִׁיר אֶת הַזָּר, ״עִתִּי״ — וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת, ״עִתִּי״ — וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught,** with regard to the verse: "And he shall send it away with an appointed man into the wilderness" (Leviticus 16:21), that the halakhic midrash interprets the word **man** as mentioned in order **to qualify a non-priest** for this task. The word **appointed** indicates that the scapegoat is always sent away at the appointed time, **and even on Shabbat.** Similarly, the word **appointed** indicates that the scapegoat is always sent away at the appointed time, **and even** when the appointed man is **in** a state of ritual **impurity.**

The Gemara now takes up a *baraita* that expounds the verse *ve-shillacho be-yad ish itti* — and he shall send it by the hand of a ready man. From this verse the *baraita* derives three separate rules. The word *ish* — man — teaches *lehachshir et ha-zar*, that even a non-*Kohen* is eligible. The word *itti* — timely, designated — teaches that the sending must occur in its proper time, *va-afilu be-Shabbat*, even on *Shabbat*, and *va-afilu be-tumah*, even in a state of *tumah*.

### Non-Kohein

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אִישׁ״ — לְהַכְשִׁיר אֶת הַזָּר. פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״כַּפָּרָה״ כְּתִיבָא בֵּיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* stated that the word **man** is mentioned to **qualify a non-priest.** The Gemara expresses surprise: It is **obvious** that a non-priest is qualified for this service; why would one have thought otherwise? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say:** The term **atonement is written with regard to it,** and atonement is achieved only through services performed by priests. Therefore, **it teaches us** that this atonement is not achieved through a sacrificial offering, and consequently the service may be performed even by an Israelite.

The derivation that a non-*Kohen* may perform this task is, the Gemara notes, not immediately obvious. *Pesita* — it might seem self-evident, since this is not an *avodah* performed within the Temple precincts — but one might have thought that since the verse connects this act to *kappara*, to atonement, perhaps it requires a *Kohen* as performer. The verse therefore specifies *ish* — any man — to preclude that inference.

### Shabbat Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עִתִּי״ וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לוֹמַר, שֶׁאִם הָיָה חוֹלֶה — מַרְכִּיבוֹ עַל כְּתֵפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* stated that the word **appointed** indicates that the service is performed **even on Shabbat.** The Gemara asks: With regard **to what *halakha*** is this stated? There is no apparent desecration of Shabbat by escorting the goat, since the *halakha* of Shabbat boundaries is merely Rabbinic. **Rav Sheshet said:** It is mentioned in order **to state that if** the goat **were ill** and could not walk the whole way, the one who escorts the goat **carries it on his shoulder.**

The ruling that this may be performed even on *Shabbat* requires explanation: what *Shabbat* violation might arise? The answer given is *techumin de-rabbanan* — the rabbinic restriction on traveling beyond the *Shabbat* boundary — but Rav Sheshet goes further: the verse is needed to permit carrying the goat on one's shoulders if it is sick. Specifically, if the goat became ill and could not walk on its own, may one carry it — an act that would ordinarily be a biblical *melacha* on *Shabbat*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן, דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי נָתָן. דְּאִי רַבִּי נָתָן, הָאָמַר: חַי נוֹשֵׂא אֶת עַצְמוֹ: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי נָתָן, חָלָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **In accordance with whose** opinion was this stated? It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan, as, if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Natan, didn't** he **say** that **a living** being **carries itself?** Because a living being is lighter than dead weight, the living being is considered to be aiding the one carrying it, and therefore carrying a living being is not considered an act of prohibited labor according to Torah law. The Gemara rejects this: **Even** if **you say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Natan,** a living being that is **ill is different.** Since the goat cannot walk on its own strength, despite the fact that it is alive, all agree that the one who carries it is performing a prohibited labor.

The Gemara notes that according to Rabbi Natan, carrying a live being does not constitute a biblical violation of *Shabbat*, because *chai nosei et atzmo* — a living creature partially supports its own weight. If so, no verse would be needed to permit carrying the sick goat. But the Gemara resolves this: *choleh shani* — a sick animal is different. If the animal is ill, it cannot adequately bear its own weight, and the principle of *chai nosei et atzmo* does not apply. There is therefore a genuine biblical concern of carrying, and the verse is needed to override it. This has broader implications for questions about carrying infants or incapacitated individuals on *Shabbat*, but those discussions belong elsewhere.

### Yom Kippur Carrying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַפְרָם, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: עֵירוּב וְהוֹצָאָה לְשַׁבָּת, וְאֵין עֵירוּב וְהוֹצָאָה לְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Based on the fact that the word appointed indicates that the scapegoat is sent away even on Shabbat, **Rafram said: That is to say** that the concept of ***eiruv* and** the prohibition against **carrying out** apply **to Shabbat,** but ***eiruv* and carrying out do not** apply **to Yom Kippur.** If these *halakhot* applied equally to Yom Kippur, and nevertheless the Torah commanded that the scapegoat be sent away, it would be unnecessary to derive that the same is true even if Yom Kippur occurs on Shabbat.

Rafram draws an inference from the fact that the verse had to specify *Shabbat* in particular: *ve-ein eiruv ve-hotza'ah le-Yom HaKippurim* — if *Shabbat* carrying needed explicit biblical permission, it must be because *Yom Kippur* does not carry a biblical prohibition of carrying. Since no verse was needed to permit this act on *Yom Kippur* alone, *Yom Kippur* must be like *Yom Tov* in this regard: no biblical restriction on *hotza'ah*, hence no need for *eiruv* either. The *chiddush* of the verse is therefore only for *Shabbat*.

### Tosafos Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַפְרָם, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: עֵירוּב וְהוֹצָאָה לְשַׁבָּת, וְאֵין עֵירוּב וְהוֹצָאָה לְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Based on the fact that the word appointed indicates that the scapegoat is sent away even on Shabbat, **Rafram said: That is to say** that the concept of ***eiruv* and** the prohibition against **carrying out** apply **to Shabbat,** but ***eiruv* and carrying out do not** apply **to Yom Kippur.**

Tosafot flatly rejects this inference, citing the Gemara in Kerisot, which dismisses Rafram's statement as *bedusa* — effectively, a position not worthy of serious consideration. The correct analysis, Tosafot argues, is that the verse does not prove anything about *Yom Kippur* carrying. Rather, there is an obvious reason why no verse is needed for *Yom Kippur*: on *Yom Kippur* itself, one has blanket license to do whatever the *avodah* requires, in the same way one may perform *shechita* and other biblically-prohibited acts on *Yom Kippur* for the purpose of the service. The need for a verse addressing *Shabbat* is thus entirely unremarkable from the standpoint of *Yom Kippur* carrying: one already had permission to carry on *Yom Kippur* for the *avodah* — not because there is no prohibition, but because the *avodah* overrides it. The *chiddush* of the verse is that even on *Shabbat* — where one might have thought that the license extended only to *Yom Kippur* obligations strictly speaking — the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* can override *Shabbat* restrictions as well, including carrying when the animal is sick. Do not be misled: there is a biblical prohibition of *hotza'ah* on *Yom Kippur* just as on *Shabbat*.

### Tumah Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עִתִּי״ וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּטוּמְאָה. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לוֹמַר שֶׁאִם נִטְמָא מְשַׁלְּחוֹ — נִכְנָס טָמֵא לָעֲזָרָה, וּמְשַׁלְּחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* stated that the word **appointed** indicates that the service is performed **even in** a state of ritual **impurity.** The Gemara asks: With regard **to what *halakha*** is this stated? **Rav Sheshet said:** The verse comes **to tell** you **that if the one sending** the goat away **became impure, he** nevertheless **enters the** Temple **courtyard** while he is **impure and sends it** away.

The *baraita* also teaches that the *se'ir hamishtalei'ach* overrides *tumah*. The Gemara asks: *le-mai hilchata* — in what practical scenario? Rav Sheshet explains: *lomar she-nitma meshallcho* — if the person who had been designated to carry out the goat became *tamei*, he may nevertheless enter the *azarah* in his state of *tumah* and carry out the dispatch. Ordinarily we would simply find a replacement; the *Baraita* teaches that the designated individual, *itti*, has a special status by virtue of having been appointed, and he may perform his role even *be-tumah*, overriding the prohibition of a *tamei* person entering the *azarah*. Whether this follows from the general principle of *tumah hutra be-tzibbur* — that public communal needs override *tumah* concerns — or whether the *itti* designation itself confers elevated status on the specific individual, may be debated.

## R. Eliezer Stories

### Sick Goat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: חָלָה, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּרְכִּיבֵהוּ עַל כְּתֵפוֹ? אָמַר לָהֶם: יָכוֹל הוּא לְהַרְכִּיב אֲנִי וְאַתֶּם. חָלָה מְשַׁלְּחוֹ, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּשַׁלְּחֶנּוּ בְּיַד אַחֵר? אָמַר לָהֶם: אֱהֵא בְּשָׁלוֹם אֲנִי וְאַתֶּם.
>
> **Translation:** The students once **asked Rabbi Eliezer:** If the goat **became ill, what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether the escort may **carry it on his shoulder? He said to them: That** goat **can carry me and you,** meaning the goat designated healthy was unlikely to become ill. Rabbi Eliezer thereby avoided the question. They asked him: If **the one sending** the goat away **became ill, what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **they send it with someone else? He said to them** dismissively: **I and you shall be in peace,** i.e., this would never happen.

The *Mishnah* records a series of questions posed to Rabbi Eliezer, all relating to the dispatch of the *se'ir hamishtalei'ach*, and all met with cryptic non-answers. First: *choleh mahu she-yarkivennu al ketefo* — if the goat became sick, may one carry it on his shoulders? Rabbi Eliezer replied: *yachol le-harkiv ani ve-atem* — it can carry me and you. This response, as Rashi reads it, is simply an evasive deflection, refusing to engage with the question.

The Chachamim, by contrast, answer directly: yes, if the animal is sick, one carries it on his shoulders.

### Sick Messenger

Second question: *choleh meshallcho mahu she-yishlachenu be-yad acher* — if the designated carrier fell ill, may another person be sent in his place? Rabbi Eliezer replied: *yehei be-shalom ani ve-atem* — you and I should all be well. Again, no answer.

The Chachamim say: yes, appoint a substitute.

### Goat Doesn't Die

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּחָפוֹ וְלֹא מֵת, מַהוּ שֶׁיֵּרֵד אַחֲרָיו וִימִיתֶנּוּ? אָמַר לָהֶם: ״כֵּן יֹאבְדוּ כׇל אוֹיְבֶיךָ ה׳״. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: חָלָה — מַרְכִּיבוֹ עַל כְּתֵפוֹ, חָלָה מְשַׁלְּחוֹ — יְשַׁלְּחֶנּוּ בְּיַד אַחֵר. דְּחָפוֹ וְלֹא מֵת — יֵרֵד אַחֲרָיו וִימִיתֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The students continued to question Rabbi Eliezer: **If he pushed** the goat **and it did not die** upon its fall, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **he should follow it down and kill it? He said to them: "So may all your enemies perish, Lord"** (Judges 5:31). In other words, the goat will certainly die on its own. Rabbi Eliezer did not wish to answer these questions, as will be explained below. However, **the Sages say:** If the goat **became ill,** the escort **carries it on his shoulder. If the one sending out** the goat **became ill, he sends** the goat **with someone else. If he pushes it and it does not die, he follows it down and kills it.**

Third question: *dachfo ve-lo met, mahu she-yered achrav vi-yemitennu* — if the goat was pushed off the cliff but did not die, may one descend after it and kill it? Rabbi Eliezer replied with a verse: *ken yovedu kol oyvecha Hashem* — thus shall all Your enemies perish, O God — as if to say, it will most certainly die; the scenario of surviving the fall is not a real possibility. Once again, no direct answer.

The Chachamim say: yes, go after it and kill it.

Why does the Gemara record these deflections? Apparently, to illustrate how far Rabbi Eliezer would go to avoid answering something he had not received as *mesorah* from his *rebbe* — a principle to which we shall turn directly.

The *Maharsha* offers an alternative reading in which Rabbi Eliezer is actually taking a substantive position in each case, disputing the *Chachamim* rather than simply evading. On the first question, *yachol le-harkiv ani ve-atem* means: if the goat is healthy enough to carry both of us, then you may carry it — but if it is genuinely sick and truly cannot support its own weight, then carrying it would be a biblical violation of *Shabbat* and one may not do so. This would represent a disagreement with the Gemara's earlier conclusion that the verse overrides *Shabbat*. On the second question, the *Maharsha* emends the *girsa*: instead of *yehei be-shalom ani ve-ata*, read *yehei be-shilluach ani ve-ata* — you or I can take care of it. On this reading, Rabbi Eliezer holds that neither the sick *shaliach* himself nor anyone he appoints can be his substitute; it is for the *Kohen Gadol* or the Temple authorities to designate a new person entirely. The *Chachamim* hold that the designated *shaliach* himself may appoint his own substitute, which raises interesting questions about whether this role is the *shaliach*'s own to transfer or whether it reverts to the *Kohen Gadol* when he cannot perform it.

### No New Teachings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא אָמַר דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא שָׁמַע מִפִּי רַבּוֹ מֵעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **because he never said anything that he did not hear from the mouth of his teacher.**

The Gemara now states the principle underlying Rabbi Eliezer's behavior: *lo amar davar she-lo shama me-rabbo me'olam* — he never in his life said something he had not heard directly from his *rebbe*. Rashi explains that even when Rabbi Eliezer knew the *halacha*, if he had not received it as an explicit teaching from his teacher, he would not transmit it. This commitment to *mesorah* as exclusively transmitted teaching — rather than derived reasoning — explains all three evasive responses. This same trait is also documented in Tractate Sukkah.

### David Stories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: פְּלוֹנִי, מַהוּ לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא? אָמַר לָהֶם לֹא שְׁאֶלְתּוּנִי אֶלָּא עַל פְּלוֹנִי. מַהוּ לְהַצִּיל רוֹעֶה כִּבְשָׂה מִן הָאֲרִי? אָמַר לָהֶם: לֹא שְׁאֶלְתּוּנִי אֶלָּא עַל הַכִּבְשָׂה. מַהוּ לְהַצִּיל הָרוֹעֶה מִן הָאֲרִי? אָמַר לָהֶם: לֹא שְׁאֶלְתּוּנִי אֶלָּא עַל הָרוֹעֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites more questions that the students asked Rabbi Eliezer, which he refused to answer. **They asked Rabbi Eliezer: What is** the fate of **so-and-so,** a certain man who was known to be wicked, with regard **to the World-to-Come? He** evaded the question and **said to them: You have only asked me about so-and-so,** and not a different individual whom you believe to be righteous? They asked him: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **a shepherd may save a ewe from a lion** on Shabbat (*Me'iri*)? **He said to them: You have only asked me about the ewe?** They asked him: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **saving the shepherd from the lion** on Shabbat? **He said to them: You have only asked me about the shepherd?**

The Gemara records a further series of questions posed to Rabbi Eliezer, all of which he deflected in the same manner. Someone asked: *Ploni, mahu l'Olam HaBa* — will so-and-so have a portion in the World to Come? Rabbi Eliezer responded: why are you asking me about that one; ask me about the other one instead. When they asked about the second, he said: why ask about that one; ask about the first.

Tosafot cites Rabbeinu Chananel with a brilliant interpretation. The first question was about Avshalom: did he have a portion in the World to Come, given that he had violated his father's *pilegesh*? Does that constitute full adultery or not, given various *halachic* subtleties? Rabbi Eliezer did not want to rule. They then shifted to asking about Shlomo HaMelech, who was entangled in questions about his many wives — and Rabbi Eliezer sent them back to the question about Avshalom.

The second exchange — about whether the shepherd could save the lamb from the lion, and then whether the shepherd could be saved from the lion — is, according to Rabbeinu Chananel, a coded discussion about the story of Bat Sheva, Uriah, and David. *Ha-ari* — the lion — is David. *Ha-kivsa* — the lamb — is Bat Sheva. *Ha-ro'eh* — the shepherd — is Uriah. The question of whether the lion took the lamb is a question about whether David's act with Bat Sheva constituted adultery — a matter *Chazal* tried to mitigate by reading Uriah as having given a conditional *get*. The question of whether the lion killed the shepherd is a question about whether David was culpable for Uriah's death — a matter *Chazal* tried to mitigate by characterizing Uriah as a *mored b'malchut*, one who was flouting the king's authority. Rabbi Eliezer evaded both by sending the questioners from one to the other.

### Mamzer Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַמְזֵר, מַה הוּא לִירַשׁ? מַהוּ לְיַבֵּם? מַהוּ לָסוּד אֶת בֵּיתוֹ? מַהוּ לָסוּד אֶת קִבְרוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** They asked him: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **a *mamzer* inherits** from his parents? Rabbi Eliezer responded with a question: Did you not ask me **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether he may **perform levirate marriage?** They asked him: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to whether it is permitted **to plaster one's house** after the destruction of the Temple? Rabbi Eliezer responded: **What is** the *halakha* with regard **to plastering one's grave?**

Another series of questions was posed to Rabbi Eliezer: *mamzer mahu l'yarash* — can a *mamzer* inherit his father? This question might seem obvious given that according to *Chazal* a *mamzer* is the halachically recognized child of his father — he can even be bound in a relationship of *yibbum* with his father's widowed *co-wife*, though marriage itself is forbidden — and thus should in principle inherit. Rabbi Eliezer responded: why ask me about inheritance; ask me about plastering the grave. To which Rabbi Eliezer in turn deflected again: why ask about plastering the grave; ask about plastering the house. Rashi understands the house question as asking about the requirement of *zecher le-churban* — the prohibition on fully plastering one's home as a reminder of the Temple's destruction. The *Bach* understands the grave question as relating back to the *mamzer*: does one plaster a *mamzer*'s grave in the usual honorific fashion, or does one leave it unmarked to indicate his status and warn his descendants?

### Woman Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלָה אִשָּׁה חֲכָמָה אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מֵאַחַר שֶׁמַּעֲשֵׂה הָעֵגֶל שָׁוִין, מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין מִיתָתָן שָׁוָה? אָמַר לָהּ: אֵין חׇכְמָה לָאִשָּׁה אֶלָּא בְּפֶלֶךְ, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְכׇל אִשָּׁה חַכְמַת לֵב בְּיָדֶיהָ טָווּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another question posed to Rabbi Eliezer. **A wise woman asked Rabbi Eliezer: Since** all bore **equal** responsibility for the **incident of the** Golden **Calf, due to what** factor were **their deaths not equal?** Some of the people were killed by the sword of Moses and the Levites, some were killed in a plague, and others were struck with an intestinal illness. **He said to her: There is no wisdom in a woman except** weaving **with a spindle, and so it states: "And any woman who was wise-hearted spun with her hands"** (Exodus 35:25).

A *chachama*, a wise woman, asked Rabbi Eliezer: since everyone who worshipped the Golden Calf sinned equally, *mipnei mah ein mitatam shava* — why did they receive different deaths? The Torah describes three modes: those slain by the sword at the hands of the Levites; those who died by divine plague; and those who died from drinking the water into which Moshe had ground the Calf — which *Chazal* read as analogous to the *sotah* trial, with the guilty party's body rupturing.

### Women's Role

Rabbi Eliezer's response was infamous: *ein chochma le-isha ella be-falechah* — a woman has no wisdom except for the spindle. He supports this with the verse *ve-chol isha chachamas lev be-yadeha tavu* — all the wise-hearted women spun with their hands — implying that women's proper domain is weaving, not Torah inquiry. This reflects a position Rabbi Eliezer expresses elsewhere with even greater force — the statement in Tractate Sotah that it is better for words of Torah to be burned than transmitted to women. His relationship with his own wife Imma Shalom provides a richer biographical context to this attitude, but that discussion extends beyond our passage.

## Calf Punishments

### Rav/Levi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַב וְלֵוִי. חַד אָמַר: זִיבֵּחַ וְקִיטֵּר בְּסַיִּף. גִּפֵּף וְנִישֵּׁק — בְּמִיתָה. שָׂמַח בִּלְבָבוֹ — בְּהִדְרוֹקָן. וְחַד אָמַר: עֵדִים וְהַתְרָאָה — בְּסַיִּף, עֵדִים בְּלֹא הַתְרָאָה — בְּמִיתָה, לֹא עֵדִים וְלֹא הַתְרָאָה — בְּהִדְרוֹקָן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this issue, **it was stated** that the *amora'im* **Rav and Levi** disagreed: **One** of them **said:** One who **sacrificed and burned** incense to the calf, which are idolatrous practices that incur capital punishment, was punished **by the sword.** One who **embraced and kissed** it, which are not forms of idolatrous worship that incur capital punishment, was subject to a divine punishment of **death** by a plague. One **who rejoiced inwardly** but performed no act was killed **by** the intestinal illness known as ***hidrokan*. And one** of them **said:** One who served the calf in the presence of **witnesses and** after a **warning** was punished **by the sword.** One who served the calf in the presence of **witnesses** but **without warning** was subject to **death** by a plague. One who served **without witnesses and without warning** was killed **by *hidrokan*.**

The Gemara, having declined to answer the wise woman through Rabbi Eliezer, now answers her question through its own discussion. Rav and Levi each offer a classification. One holds: *zivach ve-kitter b'sayif* — one who actually slaughtered animals and offered incense to the Calf, performing genuine acts of *avodah zara*, was executed by sword, the standard capital punishment. *Gibeph ve-nishak* — one who hugged and kissed the Calf, short of a full act of worship — died by divine death, *mita bi-dei shamayim*. *Samach be-libo* — one who merely rejoiced inwardly but took no outward action — died by *hidrokan*, the stomach-exploding affliction associated with drinking the ground-up Calf waters.

The other opinion classifies by juridical criteria rather than severity of act. *Edim ve-hatra'ah* — witnesses and warning — yields execution by sword: the case fit for formal court proceedings. *Edim belo hatra'ah* — witnesses but no warning — yields divine death. *Lo edim ve-lo hatra'ah* — no witnesses and no warning — yields *hidrokan*. The analogy here is to the *sotah*, whose violation was committed with no witnesses and who undergoes a quasi-judicial process involving the water ordeal. Those who sinned with the Calf entirely in private, beyond any possibility of formal court action, are dealt with through the same divine mechanism.

### Levi Innocent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁבְטוֹ שֶׁל לֵוִי לֹא עָבַד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעֲמוֹד מֹשֶׁה בְּשַׁעַר הַמַּחֲנֶה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: The** entire **tribe of Levi did not engage in idol worship, as it is stated: "Then Moses stood in the gate of the camp,** and said: Who is for God, let him come to me; and all the children of Levi gathered to him" (Exodus 32:26).

The Gemara then records Rav Yehuda's statement: *shivto shel Levi lo avad avodah kochavim* — the tribe of Levi did not worship the Calf at all. This is derived from the verse in which Moshe stands at the gate of the camp and calls out *mi la-Hashem eilai*, and all of Levi gathered to him — implying that the entire tribe had already stood apart from the sin.

### Half-Sibling Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רָבִינָא וְקָאָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ בְּנֵי רַב פָּפָּא בַּר אַבָּא לְרָבִינָא: ״הָאוֹמֵר לְאָבִיו וּלְאִמּוֹ לֹא רְאִיתִיו״. ״אָבִיו״ — אֲבִי אִמּוֹ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, ״אֶחָיו״ — אֶחָיו מֵאִמּוֹ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, ״בָּנָיו״ — בְּנֵי בִתּוֹ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina sat and related this *halakha*** with regard to the tribe of Levi. **The sons of Rav Pappa bar Abba raised an objection to Ravina:** The verse states in praise of the tribe of Levi: **"Who said of his father and of his mother: I have not seen him,** neither did he acknowledge his brothers, nor did he know his sons" (Deuteronomy 33:9). This indicates that some of them did engage in idol worship and were killed by their relatives. Ravina answered them: **"His father"** does not refer to his actual father, but rather **his mother's father,** who was an **Israelite.** Similarly, the term **"his brothers"** is referring to **his** half-**brothers from his mother,** who were fathered **by an Israelite. "His sons"** is referring to **his daughter's sons from an Israelite,** who are considered Israelites. In fact, however, no one from the tribe of Levi worshipped the calf.

This claim is immediately challenged. The Torah in *Ha'azinu* praises Levi by saying that Levi showed no favor to *aviv* — his father — and to *imo* — his mother — and did not recognize *banav*, his children. If all of Levi was innocent, against whom is this praise directed? Who among Levi's tribe was tempted to show such favoritism?

The Gemara resolves this through careful parsing. *Aviv* in the verse does not refer to his father but to *avi imo mi-Yisrael* — his maternal grandfather, who was not a Levite. *Echav* — his brothers — refers to *achav me-imo mi-Yisrael* — his half-brother on his mother's side, born of a non-Levite father. *Banav* — his children — refers to *benei bito mi-Yisrael* — his grandchildren through his daughter, who had married a non-Levite. In each case, the relatives the Levite refused to spare were relatives by maternal lineage whose fathers were *Yisraelim* — members of other tribes who had worshipped the Calf. The Levites themselves, on their father's side, remained entirely innocent. Thus the praise of Levi is preserved without contradiction, and the tribe's complete fidelity to God at the moment of the Golden Calf stands intact.
