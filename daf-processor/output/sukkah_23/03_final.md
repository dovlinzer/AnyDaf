# Sukkah 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Schach Layering

### Rashi: Sunlight

Before turning to the new material on 23a, it is worth revisiting a point from the previous session that was left incomplete. The *mishna* had discussed the case of *schach* arranged at two separate layers, with the question being whether the principle of *lavud* — or *chavut rami* — could virtually combine those two layers into a single valid covering. Rashi, in his commentary there, emphasized that the fundamental problem is one of sunlight: with the *schach* split across two distinct levels, too much light penetrates, and the *chiddush* is that even when we virtually combine the layers through *lavud*, even though in physical reality the *schach* is not fully providing shade, we treat it as sufficient — because in a virtual, halachic sense, it is providing shade.

### Tosafot: Sun & Roof

Tosafot offers two important points that add considerable nuance to this discussion. The first is that when assessing whether *schach* provides adequate shade, we judge from the position of the sun directly overhead — not at an angle. With the sun at its zenith, even *schach* spread across two layers allows less light through than one might imagine. Tosafot therefore argues that shade per se is not actually the issue here; the *schach*, even in its two-layered configuration, is in fact providing shade.

This leads to Tosafot's second and more fundamental point. The real question, on this reading, is not shade but structure: can these two layers be considered a single *schach*, a single roof — or are they two separate roofs, neither of which is independently sufficient? It is precisely on this question that *lavud* and *chavut rami* become relevant. These principles derive from the laws of *Ohalot* — the laws governing tent-structures and their boundaries — and their application here is direct and natural. We are not asking whether shade is provided; we are asking whether a single *ohel* has been formed. Tosafot's framing thus makes the *gemara*'s invocation of these *Ohalot* principles considerably more coherent: there is no shadow of a problem with shade; the only issue is whether one unified roof exists.

## Boat Sukkah

### Gamliel vs. Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַנִּי מַתְנִיתִין — רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: הָעוֹשֶׂה סוּכָּתוֹ בְּרֹאשׁ הַסְּפִינָה — רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל פּוֹסֵל, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מַכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara comments: In accordance with **whose** opinion is **the mishna?** It **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who establishes his *sukka* at the top of the ship, Rabban Gamliel deems** it **unfit and Rabbi Akiva deems** it **fit.**

The *gemara* on 23a opens by asking whose opinion underlies our *mishna*, which validated a *sukkah* built atop a wagon or a boat. The *gemara* answers: it is Rabbi Akiva's position, as we learn from the following *baraita*.

The *baraita* states: if one builds a *sukkah* at the top of a boat (*berosh hasfina*), Rabban Gamliel declares it invalid (*posel*), while Rabbi Akiva declares it valid (*machshir*).

### Wind Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא שֶׁהָיוּ בָּאִים בִּסְפִינָה, עָמַד רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְעָשָׂה סוּכָּה בְּרֹאשׁ הַסְּפִינָה. לְמָחָר נָשְׁבָה רוּחַ וַעֲקָרַתָּה. אָמַר לוֹ רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: עֲקִיבָא! הֵיכָן סוּכָּתְךָ?
>
> **Translation:** There was **an incident involving Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Akiva, who were coming on a ship. Rabbi Akiva arose and established a *sukka* at the top of the ship. The next day the wind blew and uprooted it. Rabban Gamliel said to him: Akiva, where is your *sukka*?** It was unfit from the start.

The *baraita* then recounts a famous incident. Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Akiva were traveling together on a boat during the festival of *Sukkot*. Rabbi Akiva arose and built a *sukkah* at the top of the vessel. The next day, a wind blew and tore the *sukkah* down entirely. Rabban Gamliel then turned to him and said: "Akiva, where is your *sukkah*?" — a pointed remark that is hard to miss.

One naturally wonders: did neither of them dwell in a *sukkah* that night? Presumably Rabban Gamliel reasoned that since he regarded such a structure as invalid from the outset, there was no point in building one — and being travelers at sea (*holchei drachim*), there may well be grounds for exemption in any case. That question will be addressed elsewhere in the tractate.

### Wind Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, הֵיכָא דְּאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לַעֲמוֹד בְּרוּחַ מְצוּיָה דְּיַבָּשָׁה — לֹא כְּלוּם הִיא. יְכוֹלָה לַעֲמוֹד בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ מְצוּיָה דְּיַבָּשָׁה, כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּכְשֵׁרָה. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּדִיכוֹלָה לַעֲמוֹד בְּרוּחַ מְצוּיָה דְּיַבָּשָׁה וְאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לַעֲמוֹד (בְּרוּחַ שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְצוּיָה דְּיַבָּשָׁה). רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: סוּכָּה דִּירַת קֶבַע בָּעֵינַן, וְכֵיוָן דְּאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לַעֲמוֹד בְּרוּחַ מְצוּיָה דְּיָם — לֹא כְּלוּם הִיא. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: סוּכָּה דִּירַת עֲרַאי בָּעֵינַן, וְכֵיוָן דִּיכוֹלָה לַעֲמוֹד בְּרוּחַ מְצוּיָה דְּיַבָּשָׁה — כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Everyone agrees that** in a case **where** the *sukka* **is unable to withstand a typical land wind,** the *sukka* **is of no consequence** and it is not even a temporary residence. If **it is able to withstand** even **an atypical land wind, everyone agrees that** the *sukka* **is fit. Where they disagree** is **in** a case **where** the *sukka* **is able to withstand a typical land wind but is unable to withstand an atypical land wind,** which is the equivalent of a typical sea wind. **Rabban Gamliel holds:** In order to fulfill the mitzva of ***sukka*, we require a permanent residence, and since it is not able to withstand** an atypical land wind, which is like **a typical sea wind, it is of no consequence** and is not a *sukka* at all. **Rabbi Akiva holds:** In order to fulfill the mitzva of ***sukka*, we require a temporary residence, and since it is able to withstand a typical land wind,** it **is fit,** although it is unable to withstand a typical sea wind.

The *gemara* now turns to Abaye, who analyzes the underlying dispute and maps out the full range of cases. Abaye establishes two poles of agreement: if a *sukkah* cannot withstand a *ruach metzuya* — a normal, ordinary wind on dry land — it is not considered a *dirah* at all, and everyone agrees it is invalid. Conversely, if it can withstand even an unusual, powerful wind on dry land, everyone agrees it is valid. These two extremes are uncontested.

The debate falls in the middle: a *sukkah* that can withstand a normal wind on dry land but cannot withstand either an unusual wind on dry land — or, in an alternative textual reading, a normal wind at sea (*ruach metzuya deyam*). The assumption underlying this analysis is that the winds at sea are significantly stronger than those on land, such that a normal wind on the ocean is roughly equivalent in force to an unusual wind on dry land. The question is whether a *sukkah* that cannot withstand that level of force is valid.

### Gamliel: Permanent

Rabban Gamliel holds that a *sukkah* must constitute a *dirat keva* — a permanent-style dwelling. Consequently, if the *sukkah* cannot withstand a normal wind at sea (*ruach metzuya deyam*), it lacks the requisite permanence and is invalid even before any wind has actually blown. The mere inability to endure those conditions disqualifies it from the outset.

### Akiva: Temporary

Rabbi Akiva holds that a *sukkah* need only be a *dirat arai* — a temporary dwelling — and as long as it can withstand a normal wind on dry land, it is valid.

This position is somewhat striking when examined closely. On dry land, the debate between *dirat keva* and *dirat arai* is intuitive: one might argue that a structure which could not survive an unusual wind is insufficiently permanent — a reasonable position even if not the definitive one. But Rabbi Akiva's position becomes puzzling when the *sukkah* is actually situated on a boat in the middle of the ocean. In that context, the relevant winds are the ocean's, and the *sukkah* cannot withstand them. Shouldn't the contextual reality govern? Shouldn't we say that a structure which will not survive even a day in its actual environment cannot be called a *dirat arai*?

Rabbi Akiva apparently maintains that the structural evaluation is performed against a fixed, land-based standard. As long as the *sukkah* would qualify as a dwelling were it on dry land, it retains that status even aboard a vessel at sea. One might compare this, loosely, to the situation in certain coastal regions with extreme seasonal weather — where what counts as a normal wind at that time of year is already quite powerful — though the case of a *sukkah* at sea where ordinary winds are disqualifying remains, on its own terms, a genuinely difficult position to understand.

## Animal Sukkah

### Meir vs. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי גָּמָל כּוּ׳. מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי — רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: הָעוֹשֶׂה סוּכָּתוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה — רַבִּי מֵאִיר מַכְשִׁיר, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹסֵל. מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר קְרָא: ״חַג הַסּוּכּוֹת תַּעֲשֶׂה לְךָ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״. סוּכָּה הָרְאוּיָה לְשִׁבְעָה — שְׁמָהּ סוּכָּה. סוּכָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לְשִׁבְעָה — לֹא שְׁמָהּ סוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **Or** if one establishes his *sukka* **atop a camel,** the *sukka* is fit. The Gemara asks: **Who is** the *tanna* of **the mishna? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who establishes his *sukka* atop an animal, Rabbi Meir deems** it **fit and Rabbi Yehuda deems** it **unfit.** The Gemara asks: **What is the rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda?** The Gemara answers that it is as **the verse states: "You shall prepare for yourself the festival of *Sukkot* for seven days"** (Deuteronomy 16:13), from which Rabbi Yehuda derives: **A *sukka* that is suitable for seven** days **is called a *sukka*,** while **a *sukka* that is not suitable for seven** days **is not called a *sukka*.** It is prohibited to climb upon an animal on the first day of the festival of *Sukkot*, and therefore a *sukka* atop an animal is unfit, as it cannot be used all seven days.

The *gemara* continues: who is the author of the *mishna*'s ruling concerning a *sukkah* built atop an animal or a tree — where the *sukkah* is valid but may not be ascended on *Yom Tov*? The *gemara* answers: it is Rabbi Meir's position. A *baraita* is cited: if one builds a *sukkah* on top of an animal (*al gabei behema*), Rabbi Meir declares it valid (*machshir*) and Rabbi Yehuda declares it invalid (*posel*).

### Yehuda: Seven Days

The *gemara* asks for Rabbi Yehuda's reason. The verse states: *chag ha-sukkot ta'aseh lecha shiv'at yamim* — you shall make the festival of *Sukkot* for yourself for seven days. Rabbi Yehuda derives from this that only a *sukkah* fit for use (*re'uya*) for all seven days bears the name *sukkah* in the halachic sense. A structure that cannot be used on *Yom Tov* — because it is built atop an animal one may not mount on *Yom Tov* — is not fit for all seven days, and therefore does not qualify.

It is worth noting a subtlety: if one built a *sukkah* during *Chol Ha-moed*, it would also not have been available for the full seven days, yet we have already encountered that case elsewhere in the tractate. Here the principle applies because the structure, by its very nature, cannot be used on *Yom Tov*.

### Meir: Torah vs. Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, הָא נָמֵי — מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא מִחְזֵה חַזְיָא, וְרַבָּנַן הוּא דִּגְזַרוּ בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Meir,** who holds that the *sukka* is fit, would say: **By Torah law, this** *sukka* **is also suitable** for use on a Festival and on Shabbat, as there is no Torah prohibition against using an animal on those days, **and it is the Sages who issued a decree** prohibiting **it.** The fact that it is prohibited by rabbinic decree does not render the *sukka* unfit.

Rabbi Meir does not fundamentally dispute the requirement that the *sukkah* must be fit for seven days. Rather, he argues that biblically (*mi-d'oraita*), there is no prohibition against ascending a tree or riding an animal on *Yom Tov*. The restriction is rabbinic (*rabbanan hu de-gazru bah*). Consequently, the *sukkah* is in principle fit for all seven days from the Torah's perspective; it is only a rabbinic enactment that prevents its use. Rabbi Meir maintains that a rabbinic restriction does not retroactively render the *sukkah* unfit in the biblical sense — the *sukkah* itself remains fully valid; it is merely the rabbis who prevent one from accessing it.

Both authorities thus agree on the underlying principle that the *sukkah* must be fit for seven days. Their dispute is whether a rabbinically-imposed barrier to access constitutes a deficiency in the *sukkah* itself.

### Animal as Dofen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂאָהּ לִבְהֵמָה דּוֹפֶן לְסוּכָּה — רַבִּי מֵאִיר פּוֹסֵל וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ רוּחַ חַיִּים, אֵין עוֹשִׂין אוֹתוֹ לֹא דּוֹפֶן לְסוּכָּה, וְלֹא לֶחִי לְמָבוֹי, וְלֹא פַּסִּין לְבֵירָאוֹת, וְלֹא גּוֹלֵל לְקֶבֶר. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אָמְרוּ: אַף אֵין כּוֹתְבִין עָלָיו גִּיטֵּי נָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** However, if **one utilized** his **animal** as **a wall for a *sukka*** and did not establish the entire *sukka* atop the animal, **Rabbi Meir deems** it **unfit and Rabbi Yehuda deems** it **fit, as Rabbi Meir would say:** With regard to **any animate object, one** may **neither establish it as a wall for** the ***sukka*, nor** as **a side post** placed at the entrance to **an alleyway** to render it permitted to carry in the alleyway on Shabbat, **nor** as one of the upright **boards** placed around **wells** to render the area a private domain and permit one to draw water from the well on Shabbat, **nor** as the **covering for a grave. In the name of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** the Sages **said: Nor** may **one write bills of divorce on it.**

The *gemara* now introduces a distinct case: not a *sukkah* built entirely atop an animal, but a *sukkah* in which the animal is used as one of the *dofen* — one of the walls — while the *sukkah* otherwise stands independently. The positions here are reversed: Rabbi Meir declares it invalid (*posel*) and Rabbi Yehuda declares it valid (*machshir*).

A *baraita* elaborates Rabbi Meir's position. Rabbi Meir stated a general principle: anything that is alive (*kol davar she-yesh bo ruach chayim*) may not be made into a *dofen* of a *sukkah*, a *lechi* for a *mavoi*, the corner-posts (*pasin*) for a well in a public domain (*pasi vira'ot*), or a *golel* for a grave. And in the name of Rabbi Yose Ha-Glili it was further said: one may not even write a *get* upon a living creature.

The list is suggestive from a philosophical standpoint — there is something intuitive about the notion that living beings should not be reduced to the role of inanimate halachic objects. Yet the *gemara* recognizes that a philosophical rationale alone cannot adequately explain the specific boundaries of this list, which is neither exhaustive nor fully systematic. The *gemara* therefore proceeds to identify the concrete halachic concerns that motivate each of these exclusions.

### Death vs. Flight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: שֶׁמָּא תָּמוּת. רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: שֶׁמָּא תִּבְרַח. בְּפִיל קָשׁוּר כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, דְּאִי נָמֵי מָיֵית — יֵשׁ בְּנִבְלָתוֹ עֲשָׂרָה. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּפִיל שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָשׁוּר. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁמָּא תָּמוּת — לָא חָיְישִׁינַן. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא תִּבְרַח — חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Meir,** who rules that an animal is unfit for use as a partition in areas of *halakha* where a partition is required? **Abaye said:** It is due to the concern **lest** the animal **die,** leaving the *sukka* without a wall. **Rabbi Zeira said:** It is due to the concern **lest it flee.** The Gemara explains the practical halakhic differences between the two opinions. **In** the case where one established a wall with **a tied elephant, everyone agrees** that the *sukka* is fit, **as even if it dies** and falls, **its carcass** still **has** a height of **ten** handbreadths and is fit for the wall of a *sukka*. **Where they disagree** is **in** the case of **an elephant that is not tied. According to the one who said:** It is due to the concern **lest** the animal **die, we are not concerned** in this case, as the carcass would remain a fit wall. **According to the one who said:** It is due to **a decree lest it flee, we** remain **concerned.**

What is Rabbi Meir's operative reason? Abaye says: *shema tamut* — we are concerned that the animal might die. If the animal dies, the wall it was constituting collapses, and the *sukkah* (or *mavoi*, or whatever structure depended on it) loses its required configuration — the ten *tefachim* of wall height, or the *lechi*, or the corner-post.

Rabbi Zeira offers a different rationale: *shema tivrach* — we are concerned that the animal will run away.

### Elephant Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּאָמַר גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא תָּמוּת, נֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא תִּבְרַח! אֶלָּא: בְּפִיל שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָשׁוּר, כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְלִיגִי. כִּי פְלִיגִי בִּבְהֵמָה קְשׁוּרָה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא תָּמוּת — חָיְישִׁינַן. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא תִּבְרַח — לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **According to the one who said:** It is due to **a decree lest it die, let us** also **be concerned lest it flee,** as that too is a reasonable concern. **Rather,** this is the explanation: **In** the case where one established a wall with **an elephant that is not tied, everyone agrees** that the *sukka* is unfit lest it flee. **Where they disagree** is **in** the case of **a tied animal. According to the one who said:** It is due to **a decree lest** the animal **die, we are concerned,** as although it cannot flee, it might die, and the carcass of a typical animal is not ten handbreadths high. **And according to the one who said:** It is due to **a decree lest it flee, we are not concerned.**

The *gemara* identifies a case that differentiates between these two rationales. Consider a tied elephant (*pil kashur*). Such a case is unproblematic for everyone: it cannot run away because it is tied, and even if it dies, an elephant's carcass still provides ten *tefachim* of height. There is thus no concern on either ground, and all agree it may be used.

What about an untied elephant (*pil she-eino kashur*)? Initially one might think this differentiates the two positions: Abaye, who is concerned only about death, should have no concern here — since the elephant's carcass will provide ten *tefachim* even if it dies — while Rabbi Zeira, who is concerned about flight, would still worry.

The *gemara* rejects this framing, however. Even Abaye, who formulated the concern as *shema tamut*, must obviously also be concerned about an untied animal running away. The rationale of death does not preclude the rationale of flight; it merely adds to it. An untied animal presents the concern of flight regardless of which authority one follows, and therefore all agree an untied elephant is problematic.

### Tied Animal Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּאָמַר גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא תִּבְרַח, נֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא תָּמוּת? מִיתָה לָא שְׁכִיחָא. וְהָאִיכָּא רַוְוחָא דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי? דְּעָבֵיד לֵיהּ בְּהוּצָא וְדַפְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the one who said:** It is due to **a decree lest it flee, let us** also **be concerned lest it die.** The Gemara answers: That is not a concern because **death is not common.** The Sages do not issue decrees with regard to uncommon circumstances. The Gemara asks: **But** according to all opinions, **isn't there the space between** its legs, which is like a breach in a wall? How can one establish a partition whose breached segment exceeds its standing segment? The Gemara answers: **He establishes** a partition **for it** by filling the gaps **with hard palm leaves and laurel leaves,** sealing the breach.

The actual *machlokes* between Abaye and Rabbi Zeira, the *gemara* concludes, concerns a normal-sized tied animal (*behema kshura*). A tied animal cannot run away — that concern is off the table. The question is whether the residual concern of death alone is sufficient to prohibit its use. Abaye says yes: even a tied animal might die, and that concern is sufficient to render it invalid. Rabbi Zeira says no: flight was the operative concern, and since a tied animal cannot flee, it may be used.

### Death Uncommon

The *gemara* presses further. If Abaye is concerned about death for a tied normal animal, why is Rabbi Zeira not also concerned about death? The *gemara*'s answer is direct: *mitah lo shechicha* — death is not common. A tied animal left over *Shabbat* or *Sukkot* is extremely unlikely to die during that period. Flight, by contrast, is the natural behavior of an unsecured animal — it simply walks away. Since death is not a realistic concern during the relevant timeframe, Rabbi Zeira sees no reason to prohibit use of a tied animal.

One might ask whether the entire discussion is purely theoretical — did people actually tie animals to their *sukkot*? Whether or not this occurred in practice, the *gemara* uses the scenario as a conceptual test case: the "concern" about death or flight is serving as a halachic litmus test for whether the structure is considered stable and reliable, rather than a purely empirical inquiry into animal behavior.

### Rope Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא תִּבְרַח, נֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא תָּמוּת? מִיתָה לָא שְׁכִיחָא. וְהָאִיכָּא רַוְוחָא דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי? דְּעָבֵיד לֵיהּ בְּהוּצָא וְדַפְנָא. וּדִלְמָא רָבְעָה? דִּמְתִיחָה בְּאַשְׁלֵי מִלְּעֵיל. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא תָּמוּת נָמֵי, הָא מְתִיחָה בְּאַשְׁלֵי מִלְּעֵיל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the one who said:** It is due to **a decree lest it flee, let us** also **be concerned lest it die.** The Gemara answers: That is not a concern because **death is not common.** The Sages do not issue decrees with regard to uncommon circumstances. The Gemara asks: **But** according to all opinions, **isn't there the space between** its legs, which is like a breach in a wall? How can one establish a partition whose breached segment exceeds its standing segment? The Gemara answers: **He establishes** a partition **for it** by filling the gaps **with hard palm leaves and laurel leaves,** sealing the breach. The Gemara asks further: **And** even though there is no concern lest the animal die, **perhaps it will crouch,** leaving a wall that is less than ten handbreadths? The Gemara answers: It is referring to a case **where** the animal **is tied with ropes from above** so that it cannot crouch. Based on that explanation, the Gemara asks: **And according to the one who said:** It is due to **a decree lest it die,** there is **also** no concern since **it is tied with ropes from above.**

The *gemara* then raises a practical difficulty with the animal-as-*dofen* scenario. Even setting aside death and flight: what about the gap beneath the animal's body, between its legs? That open space would surely disqualify the *sukkah*'s wall. The *gemara* answers that one fills that space with branches or reeds. But then — perhaps the animal will crouch down? The *gemara* answers that the animal is suspended by ropes from above, preventing it from crouching. So the full setup involves an animal tied against flight, suspended by ropes against crouching, with the gap beneath its body filled in with vegetation.

### Shrinkage Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִמְנִין דְּמוֹקֵים בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה סָמוּךְ לַסְּכָךְ, וְכֵיוָן דְּמָיְיתָא — כָּוְוצָא וְלָאו אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Sometimes** the ten-handbreadth wall consists of the animal that is a bit higher than seven handbreadths **established adjacent to the roofing, less than three** handbreadths away. And once it dies, it contracts to be more than three handbreadths from the roofing, and it does not enter his mind to fix it because it is not noticeable. In that case, the principle of *lavud* would not apply, and the result would be a wall that is less than the minimum requisite height.

With all of these measures in place, the *gemara* revisits the concern of death for Abaye's position. If the animal is already suspended by ropes, why should its death matter? The *gemara* responds with a specific scenario: sometimes the animal is positioned just within three *tefachim* of the *schach* overhead, so that we invoke *lavud* to close that gap — and together with the animal's body height, one achieves the required ten *tefachim* of wall. If the animal dies and its body shrinks even slightly, it may move out of the three-*tefachim* range for *lavud*, invalidating the measurement — and one may not even notice.

The *gemara*'s use of the word *velav adatei* — "and he will not be aware" — is telling. The concern is not primarily that the *sukkah* is inherently unstable. It is, rather, that one might continue using the *sukkah* after it has become *pasul* without realizing it. This is characteristic of a *gezeirah* framing: the concern is practical inadvertent violation, not abstract structural inadequacy. This framing is reinforced by the specific scenario chosen — once the animal is suspended by ropes and the gap is filled, the post-death change is subtle enough that a person would not notice it had occurred.

## Death Concern

### Abaye Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר אַבָּיֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר חָיֵשׁ לְמִיתָה וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא חָיֵשׁ? וְהָתְנַן: בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְכֹהֵן וְהָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם — אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה, בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁהוּא קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Abaye** actually **say** that **Rabbi Meir is concerned about** potential **death** with regard to the *sukka* walls **and** that **Rabbi Yehuda is not concerned? Didn't we learn** in a mishna: With regard to the **daughter of an Israelite who married a priest and her husband went to a country overseas,** she may continue to **partake of *teruma*** as the wife of a priest, as **the presumptive status** of her husband is **that he is alive?** Apparently, in the absence of evidence to the contrary, the presumption is that one who is alive remains alive.

The *gemara* now steps back and challenges the entire framework Abaye has constructed. We have positioned Rabbi Meir as the authority concerned about death (*chayish le-mita*), and Rabbi Yehuda as the one unconcerned. But is this accurate?

### Terumah Widow

A *mishna* is cited: a Jewish woman married to a *kohen* who has eaten *terumah* on account of her husband's status — when her husband travels overseas (*halach ba'alah li-medinat ha-yam*), she may continue eating *terumah* on the presumption that he is still alive (*b'chezkat shehu kayam*). The principle of *chezkat chai* — the presumption of continued life that obtained at the time of departure — allows her to continue in that status. Death is not assumed in the absence of contrary information.

### Conditional Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָמֵינַן עֲלַהּ: הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ שָׁעָה אַחַת קוֹדֶם מִיתָתִי — אֲסוּרָה לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה מִיָּד!
>
> **Translation:** **And we raised a contradiction** from a different mishna: If one is leaving his place of residence, and in order to preclude a situation where his wife would have the status of a deserted wife he gives her a conditional bill of divorce and stipulates: **This is your bill of divorce** that will take effect **one hour prior to my death, it is prohibited for her to partake of *teruma* immediately** due to the concern lest he die in the next hour. Apparently, there is concern lest one die at any point.

The *gemara* then adduces a contradictory case. A man says to his wife: "This is your *get*, effective one hour before my death." He thereby attempts to ensure she becomes a divorcee rather than a widow at the moment of his death — relieving her of the obligation of *yibum*, for instance. The ruling: she is immediately forbidden to eat *terumah*. Why? Because at any given moment, the following hour might be his last — and the preceding moment, retroactively, would be one hour before his death. Since any instant might turn out to have been the critical moment, she can never eat *terumah* from the moment the conditional *get* is given. This case treats death as an imminent realistic possibility to be reckoned with at all times.

The tension is stark: in one case, we presume the husband alive and she eats *terumah*; in the other, we treat death as a moment-to-moment possibility and she cannot eat *terumah*.

### Initial Harmony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּלָא חָיֵשׁ לְמִיתָה, הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּחָיֵשׁ לְמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye said** in resolving the contradiction: This is **not difficult. This** mishna, where the presumption is that one who is alive remains alive, **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who is not concerned about** potential **death. That** mishna, where there is concern lest one die at any point, **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who is concerned about** potential **death.**

Abaye initially proposes a clean resolution: the *mishna* permitting her to eat *terumah* reflects Rabbi Meir's position — Rabbi Meir does not worry about death. The case of the conditional *get* that forbids her reflects Rabbi Yehuda's position — Rabbi Yehuda is concerned about death. There is no contradiction; the two cases simply represent the two sides of a *machlokes* between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda.

### Wine Flask

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הַלּוֹקֵחַ יַיִן מִבֵּין הַכּוּתִים, אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי לוּגִּין שֶׁאֲנִי עָתִיד לְהַפְרִישׁ הֲרֵי הֵן תְּרוּמָה, עֲשָׂרָה מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן, תִּשְׁעָה מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, וּמֵיחֵל וְשׁוֹתֶה מִיָּד, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites proof that these are the opinions of those *tanna'im*. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who purchases wine from among the Samaritans** and there is reason to suspect that *teruma* and tithes were not taken, and he is not in a position to separate *teruma*, he acts as follows. If there are one hundred *log* of wine in the barrels, **he says: Two *log* that I will separate in the future are *teruma*,** as the mandated average measure of *teruma* is one-fiftieth; **ten** *log* are **first tithe;** and a tenth of the remainder, which is **nine** *log*, are **second tithe. And he deconsecrates** the second tithe that he will separate in the future, transferring its sanctity to money, **and** he may **drink** the wine **immediately,** relying on the separation that he will perform later. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The evidence for this attribution of positions comes from a *baraita* concerning wine purchased from Samaritans. Since Samaritans are suspected of not separating *terumot u-ma'asrot*, the buyer is obligated to separate. If *Shabbat* is about to begin and there is no time to perform the separation properly, a leniency is offered — attributed to Rabbi Meir — allowing one to declare: "The two *log* that I will eventually separate shall be *terumah*; ten units shall be *maaser rishon*; nine shall be *maaser sheni*" — and one may then begin drinking immediately. The designated portions are not identified now but will be retroactively determined by what one separates after *Shabbat*.

This leniency depends on the principle of *breira* — that a present designation whose referent is unspecified can be validated retroactively by future action. Rabbi Meir holds *yesh breira*: such retroactive determination is valid. Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yosi, and Rabbi Shimon forbid this practice.

### Ipcha Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אַדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר! אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מִיתָה שְׁכִיחָא, בְּקִיעַת הַנּוֹד לָא שְׁכִיחָא, אֶפְשָׁר דְּמָסַר לֵיהּ לְשׁוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This is **difficult,** as there is a contradiction between the statement **of Rabbi Meir** with regard to *sukka*, where he is concerned about potential death, and the other statement **of Rabbi Meir** with regard to separation of *teruma* and tithes, where he is not concerned lest the wineskin burst. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Meir** could have **said to you: Death is common,** as every living being will eventually die; however, **the bursting of the wineskin is not common** because **it is possible that he gave** the wineskin **to a guard** for protection so that it does not burst until he has the opportunity to separate the required *teruma* and tithes.

The *gemara* identifies a problem, however. The same Abaye who assigned the "not concerned about death" position to Rabbi Meir must now confront the *sukkah* case: it is Rabbi Meir who invalidates the use of an animal as a *dofen*, and Abaye's own explanation was that Rabbi Meir is concerned the animal will die. That would make Rabbi Meir the one *who is* concerned about death — the opposite of what Abaye just said.

The *gemara* therefore inverts the attribution: *ipcha* — reverse it. Rabbi Meir is concerned about death (*chayish le-mita*), and Rabbi Yehuda is not concerned about death. This is consistent with the *sukkah* case.

### Meir Defense

But now a new contradiction arises regarding Rabbi Meir. In the wine case, Rabbi Meir permits drinking without worrying about whether the wine flasks will break before *Shabbat* is out — suggesting he is unconcerned about unlikely negative events. Yet in the *sukkah* case, Rabbi Meir prohibits using the animal because he is concerned it will die — treating an unlikely event as a serious worry. Is Rabbi Meir contradicting himself?

The *gemara* answers in Rabbi Meir's voice: *mita shechicha, pkiyat nod lo shechicha* — death is common; a wine flask breaking is not common. Moreover, a flask breaking is something that can be guarded against (*efshar de-masrei lei le-shomer* — one can hand it to a watchman), whereas death cannot be prevented. Rabbi Meir's position is thus internally consistent: he is concerned about death precisely because it is a real and unavoidable occurrence, while he is unbothered by the prospect of a flask breaking because that is both rare and preventable.

### Yehuda Breira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This is **difficult,** as there is a contradiction between the statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** with regard to *sukka*, where he is not concerned about potential death, and the other statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** with regard to separation of *teruma* and tithes, where he is concerned lest the wineskin burst.

The *gemara* then raises a parallel challenge regarding Rabbi Yehuda. If Rabbi Yehuda is not concerned about the animal dying, how does he forbid the wine scheme? The *gemara*'s answer: Rabbi Yehuda's objection to the wine leniency has nothing to do with the flask breaking. His real objection is that he does not hold of *breira* at all (*leis lei breira*). The retroactive designation mechanism is fundamentally invalid in his view. The flask-breaking argument was actually Rabbi Yehuda's way of challenging Rabbi Meir *on Rabbi Meir's own terms* — even granting *breira*, shouldn't you worry about the flask? To which Rabbi Meir responds: when it breaks, I'll worry about it.

### Debate Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּחָיֵשׁ לִבְקִיעַת נוֹד, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵית לֵיהּ ״בְּרֵירָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda** with regard to tithes is **not due to** the fact **that he is concerned about the** potential **bursting of** the **wineskin; rather,** he rules that one may not drink the wine **because he is not of** the opinion that there is a principle of retroactive **clarification.** The procedure prescribed by Rabbi Meir is based on a fundamental assumption that when the separation is actually performed, the produce that he separates for *teruma* and tithes at that point is determined retroactively to have been *teruma* and tithes from the outset. Rabbi Yehuda does not accept this principle. Therefore, one's subsequent actions do not retroactively determine the original status of the produce.

The *gemara* thus separates two distinct debates that had initially appeared conflated. The dispute over the wine and the retroactive designation is fundamentally a *machlokes* about *breira*, not about whether unlikely events — death or breakage — are halachically cognizable concerns. The dispute over the animal *sukkah*, by contrast, is genuinely about whether death is a concern sufficient to disqualify the structure. These are independent questions.

What remains to be resolved is the case of the *kohen*'s wife. Abaye had originally assigned the permissive *terumah* position to Rabbi Meir (not concerned about death) and the restrictive conditional-*get* position to Rabbi Yehuda (concerned about death). Now that the positions have been inverted — Rabbi Meir is the one concerned about death, Rabbi Yehuda the one who is not — the earlier harmonization must be revisited in reverse. The *gemara* has bracketed that question for the moment, having resolved the wine-flask issue as a *breira* dispute rather than a death-concern dispute, and the resolution of the *kohen* case will need to follow the newly established framework: Rabbi Meir concerned about death, Rabbi Yehuda not.
