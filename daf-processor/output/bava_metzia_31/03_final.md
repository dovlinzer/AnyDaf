# Bava Metzia 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zaken Ve'eino Lefi Kevodo

### Personal Precedence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יִהְיֶה בְּךָ אֶבְיוֹן״ – שֶׁלְּךָ קוֹדֵם לְשֶׁל כׇּל אָדָם, אֶלָּא לְזָקֵן וְאֵינָהּ לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **"There shall be no needy among you"** (Deuteronomy 15:4). This verse can be understood as a command, indicating that it is incumbent upon each individual to ensure that he will not become needy. Therefore, **your** assets **take precedence over** the assets **of any** other **person.** **Rather,** the verse is necessary **to** derive the exemption from returning the lost item in the case where he was **an elderly person and it is not in keeping with his dignity** to tend to the item.

Today's *daf* is 30b. We are in the middle of a very important discussion of the principle of *zaken ve'eino lefi kevodo* — the limits of what you are not obligated to do in returning a lost object (*aveidah*). The discussion begins three lines from the top of 30b.

The Gemara has already established that there are times when one need not return a lost object — for instance, if doing so would require entering a cemetery, which the Gemara treats as obvious. But beyond ritual considerations, there is a broader ethical principle at work: a person's own needs take precedence over another's. This does not mean that one places one's desires above another's needs, but rather that one's own needs carry greater moral weight than someone else's needs. Accordingly, before protecting another's property, one must first protect one's own. No special *pasuk* of *ve'hitalamta mehem* was needed to establish that. What we do learn from *ve'hitalamta mehem* — read not rhetorically but as an affirmative statement that one is sometimes permitted to refrain from returning a lost object — is when doing so would violate one's dignity. This is not a case of protecting one's own property over another's, but of protecting one's dignity over another's property.

### Kavod Habriyot

This is the principle of *kavod habriyot*. *Tosafot* discusses at some length how, when there is a significant affront to dignity — a body left in the street, or, as *Tosafot* also mentions, going about naked — the concern for human dignity can override even ritual obligations. Certainly at a lesser degree, it can permit one to forgo financial responsibilities toward another's property.

### Rava: Engagement Obligates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: הִכִּישָׁהּ – חַיָּיב בָּהּ. אַבָּיֵי הֲוָה יָתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה, חֲזָא לְהָנָךְ עִיזֵּי דְּקָיְימוּ, שְׁקַל קָלָא וּשְׁדָא בְּהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיחַיַּיבְתְּ בְּהוּ, קוּם אַהְדְּרִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba says:** If there was a lost animal and the elderly person began the process of returning it, e.g., if he **struck it** even once to guide it in a certain direction, he is **obligated** to tend **to it** and return it. The Gemara relates: **Abaye was sitting before Rabba** and **saw these goats standing** nearby. **He picked up a clod of dirt and threw it at them,** causing them to move. Rabba **said to him: You have** thereby **obligated yourself to** return **them. Arise and return them** to their owner.

*Amar Rava*: *hishicha chayav bah* — even though you are exempt from returning a lost object when doing so is beneath your dignity, if you have already engaged it, if you struck or drove the animal, you are now obligated and cannot disengage. At first glance this principle might seem to apply generally: once I stop to pick up any *aveidah*, I can no longer claim my exemption.

### Livestock Exception

However, another *gemara* clarifies that this rule applies specifically to livestock. Once you have struck or driven an animal, it will follow you, and you cannot simply restore it to its prior state. Putting it back where you found it will only cause it to stray further. That is why engaging livestock in particular creates an immediate and inescapable obligation. By contrast, if one picks up, say, a wallet that it would be beneath one's dignity to return, another *gemara* indicates — though the present passage does not resolve the point — that one could still put it back where one found it and withdraw. The present *gemara* leaves that case for elsewhere.

### Abaye and the Goats

The Gemara illustrates this with a brief narrative: *Abaye hava yasiv kamei de'Rava* — Abaye was sitting before Rava. He saw some goats standing there, apparently wandering, and he threw a clump of dirt at them — *shakal kala veshada behu*. Whether he intended to shoo them away or to herd them is unclear from the text, but Rava immediately said to him: *ichayavtes behu kum ahadrinhu* — you have now made yourself obligated, go return them. Even an ambiguous act of engagement — not necessarily an attempt to take custody — is sufficient to create the obligation, because by his action Abaye may have caused the animals to stray further and he must now ensure their return to their rightful owner.

## Field vs. City Dignity

### Unresolved Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: דַּרְכּוֹ לְהַחְזִיר בַּשָּׂדֶה, וְאֵין דַּרְכּוֹ לְהַחְזִיר בָּעִיר, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן הֲשָׁבָה מְעַלְּיָא בָּעֵינַן, וְכֵיוָן דְּלָאו דַּרְכֵּיהּ לְהַחְזִיר בָּעִיר – לָא לִחַיַּיב? אוֹ דִלְמָא בַּשָּׂדֶה מִיהַת הוּא דְּאִיחַיַּיב לֵיהּ, וְכֵיוָן דְּאִיחַיַּיב עֲלֵיהּ בַּשָּׂדֶה – אִיחַיַּיב לֵיהּ בָּעִיר? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: In a case of a person for whom it **is his** typical **manner to return** an item of that type **in the field,** where there are fewer onlookers, **but it is not his** typical **manner to return** an item of that type **in the city, what is** the *halakha*? Do **we say** that for one to be obligated to return a lost item **we need an unequivocal** obligation to **return** it that applies in all cases, **and since it is not his** typical **manner to return** an item of that sort **in the city, let him not be obligated** to return such an item at all? **Or perhaps, he is obligated in any event** to return the item **in the field, and once he is obligated** to return **it in the field, he is** also **obligated in the city.** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The Gemara now raises a *ibaya*: how does one determine whether a particular act of returning is beneath one's dignity? Consider a case where finding the animal in the field (*basadeh*) presents no indignity — there are few passersby before whom one's standing matters — but bringing the animal into the city (*ba'ir*) would be beneath one's dignity. And yet, the animal was found in the field, where one could not locate anyone who knows the owner, and so the animal will eventually have to be brought into the city and one will need to inquire there.

The question has two sides. On one hand, *mi amrinan hashavah ma'alya be'inan* — perhaps the obligation of returning a lost object must be complete and encompasses all stages, and since the full act of return will involve something beneath one's dignity, the obligation never takes hold. *Ve'ein darko le'hachzir ba'ir lo mechayev* — since it is not within one's dignity to return it in the city, one is simply not obligated at all.

On the other hand, perhaps *besadeh miha ha ichayev lei* — since the obligation attached in the field, where it was fully appropriate for this person to act, the obligation is now live. And once one becomes obligated, *ichayev lei ba'ir* — even the portion of the return that must take place in the city falls within that obligation, and one must see it through entirely.

The Gemara concludes: *teiku* — this question remains unresolved.

One might ask: what does *hachrazah* — the public announcement — look like in practical terms? In the time of the *Beit HaMikdash*, the *Even HaToen* served as the designated site for announcements during the *regalim*. But outside that context, returning a lost object means going to *shuls* and *batei midrash*, posting announcements, doing whatever the local context requires. It is implausible that in ordinary times one would simply wait three months for the *regel*. Local inquiries would naturally have been initiated immediately.

### Rava's Self-Reference Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כֹּל שֶׁבְּשֶׁלּוֹ מַחְזִיר, בְּשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ נָמֵי מַחְזִיר. וְכֹל שֶׁבְּשֶׁלּוֹ פּוֹרֵק וְטוֹעֵן, בְּשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ נָמֵי פּוֹרֵק וְטוֹעֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** In **any** case **where he would recover his own** item and would consider it to be in keeping with his dignity, he is **also** obligated to **return another's** item. **And any** case where **he unloads and loads his own** animal's burden, he is **also** obligated to **unload and load** the burden of **another's** animal.

*Amar Rava*: *kol she-bishelo machazir be-shel chaveiro nami machazir* — here is a useful way of determining whether an act of return is consistent with one's dignity: would you bother doing this act if it were your own object? If I drop a few pennies in a public square and would not stoop to pick them up, I would not do so for someone else's either. The thought experiment works well when the question is simply whether one would bend down to retrieve something. It is somewhat harder to apply when the question involves the process of returning — going door to door, making inquiries — but the general idea is to ask: if this were my object and these actions were required of me to recover it, would I perform them?

### Context-Dependent Dignity

This criterion, however, is not always a perfect measure. As a student pointed out in discussion, what is appropriate to one's dignity may depend on *context* and *beneficiary*. A wealthy man might not stoop to pick up a coin for himself, yet there is something qualitatively different about doing so in service of another whose needs are genuine. The context — that I am doing it not for myself but for someone else — may itself bear on whether the act is compatible with my dignity. Rava's self-referential thought experiment, while helpful, cannot always resolve the question, and the discussion acknowledged this limitation explicitly.

## Field vs. City Dignity…

### Prika and Te'ina

The same principle extends to the related *mitzvah* of *prika u'te'ina* — unloading and reloading an animal collapsing under its burden. There too, if the act would be beneath one's dignity, one is exempt. The Gemara states: *ve'hakol she-bishelo porek ve'tonen uv'shel chaveiro eino porek ve'tonen* — if it is not fitting for him to unload and reload his own animal, he is not obligated to do so for another's. And the same diagnostic applies: ask yourself whether you would do it for your own animal. The caveat raised regarding *hashavat aveidah* applies here as well — context and beneficiary may render the self-referential test imperfect, though it remains the starting point for honest self-assessment.

## Lifnim Mishurat Hadin

### Bundle of Sticks Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הֲוָה קָאָזֵיל בְּאוֹרְחָא, פְּגַע בֵּיהּ הָהוּא גַּבְרָא, הֲוָה דָּרֵי פִּתְכָּא דְאוֹפֵי, אוֹתְבִינְהוּ וְקָא מִיתְּפַח, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּלִי לִי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כַּמָּה שָׁוִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פַּלְגָא דְזוּזָא, יְהַיב לֵיהּ פַּלְגָא דְזוּזָא וְאַפְקְרֵהּ. הֲדַר זְכָה בְּהוּ, הֲדַר יְהַיב לֵיהּ פַּלְגָא דְזוּזָא וְאַפְקְרֵהּ. חַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה קָא בָּעֵי לְמֶיהְדַּר לְמִזְכֵּיה בְּהוּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אַפְקַרְתִּינְהוּ וְלָךְ לָא אַפְקַרְתִּינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, was walking on the road. A certain man encountered him,** and that man **was carrying a burden** that consisted of sticks **of wood. He set down** the wood **and was resting.** The man **said to him: Lift** them **for me** and place them upon me. Since it was not in keeping with the dignity of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, to lift the wood, Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: How much are they worth?** The man **said to him: A half-dinar.** Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, **gave him a half-dinar,** took possession of the wood, **and declared** the wood **ownerless.** The man **then reacquired** the wood **and** again requested that Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, lift the wood for him. Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, **again gave him a half-dinar,** again took possession of the wood, **and** again **declared** the wood **ownerless. He** then **saw that** the man **desired to reacquire** the sticks of wood. Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, **said to him: I declared** the sticks of wood **ownerless with regard to everyone** else, **but I did not declare them ownerless with regard to you.**

The Gemara now introduces a famous episode. *Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yosi hava ka'azil b'orcha* — Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yosi was walking along the road when he encountered a certain man carrying a bundle of sticks. The man set them down and asked Rabbi Yishmael to help him reload them. Since Rabbi Yishmael was a distinguished elder, helping with these dirty, cumbersome sticks was clearly beneath his dignity. Rather than simply declining, he asked the man the value of the bundle. The man said it was worth half a *zuz*. Rabbi Yishmael purchased the bundle for half a *zuz*, then declared it *hefker* — ownerless. The man immediately reclaimed the bundle, at which point Rabbi Yishmael bought it again for another half a *zuz*, and again declared it *hefker*. When he saw the man was about to reclaim it a third time, he said: *l'chulei alma afkrinehu, lach lo afkrinehu* — it is *hefker* for everyone in the world, but not for you. Someone else may take it.

### Why Not Exempt?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי זָקֵן וְאֵינָהּ לְפִי כְבוֹדוֹ הֲוָה! רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי לִפְנִים מִשּׁוּרַת הַדִּין הוּא דַּעֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, an elderly person and it was not in keeping with his dignity** to tend to the item? Why did he purchase the wood and render it ownerless in order to absolve himself of the obligation to lift the burden if he had no obligation to do so in the first place? The Gemara answers: In the case of **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, he conducted** himself **beyond the letter of the law,** and he could have simply refused the request for help.

The Gemara immediately raises the obvious question: *v'ha Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yosi zaken v'eino l'fi k'vodo hava* — Rabbi Yishmael was precisely the sort of elder for whom this act was beneath his dignity. Why did he not simply invoke his exemption and decline to help altogether?

### Rabbi Yishmael's Model

The Gemara answers: *Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yosi lifnim mishurat hadin hu d'avad* — he was acting beyond the letter of the law, a principle we have encountered repeatedly in this *perek*. Though he was fully entitled to claim his exemption, he chose not to exercise every exemption available to him. *Tosafot* notes that there are different ways of conceptualizing *lifnim mishurat hadin*: sometimes it means refraining from claiming an exemption one legitimately holds, because the underlying obligation applies to everyone else and the right thing is to act accordingly. Other times, it means performing an act that is genuinely beyond what the law requires of *anyone*, not merely declining to exploit a personal exemption. Here, the former characterization applies: the general obligation to help with another's burden exists for everyone; Rabbi Yishmael had a personal exemption, and *lifnim mishurat hadin* meant not invoking it.

A brief parenthetical: does the *hefker* created in this story even take legal effect? The Gemara raises the question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי הָוֵי הֶפְקֵר כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא? וְהָתְנַן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: הֶפְקֵר לַעֲנִיִּים – הֶפְקֵר. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ הֶפְקֵר עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא הֶפְקֵר לַעֲנִיִּים וְלַעֲשִׁירִים, כִּשְׁמִיטָּה. אֶלָּא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אַפְקְרִינְהוּ, וּבְמִלְּתָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּאוֹקְמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is** property **rendered ownerless in a case like this? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Pe'a* 6:1) that **Beit Shammai say:** Property **declared ownerless for the poor is** thereby rendered **ownerless. And Beit Hillel say: It is not ownerless, until** the property **will be ownerless for the poor and for the rich, like** produce during **the Sabbatical Year,** which is available for all. As the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel, how could Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, declare the wood ownerless selectively, excluding the prior owner of the wood? **Rather, Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei,** actually **declared** the wood **ownerless to everyone** without exception, **and** it **was with a mere statement that he prevented him** from reacquiring the wood, i.e., he told the man not to reacquire the wood even though there was no legal impediment to that reacquisition.

*Beit Hillel* holds that *hefker* is valid only when it is made available to everyone — rich and poor alike — as with *shmita*. One cannot declare something *hefker* selectively. The Gemara resolves this by saying that Rabbi Yishmael did in fact declare it *hefker* for the entire world: *l'chulei alma afkrinehu*. His statement to the man that it was not *hefker* for him was merely a social deterrent — *uv'milta b'alma hu d'ukmeih* — not a legal limitation on the *hefker*. Technically, the man could have reacquired it; Rabbi Yishmael was simply discouraging him from doing so.

### Rav Yosef's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: ״וְהוֹדַעְתָּ לָהֶם״ – זֶה בֵּית חַיֵּיהֶם, ״אֶת הַדֶּרֶךְ״ – זוֹ גְּמִילוּת חֲסָדִים, ״אֲשֶׁר יֵלְכוּ״ – זֶה בִּיקּוּר חוֹלִים, ״בָּהּ״ – זוֹ קְבוּרָה, ״וְאֶת הַמַּעֲשֶׂה״ – זֶה הַדִּין, ״אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשׂוּן״ – זוֹ לִפְנִים מִשּׁוּרַת הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a source for going beyond the letter of the law in the performance of mitzvot. **As Rav Yosef taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "And you shall teach them the statutes and the laws, and shall show them the path wherein they shall walk and the action that they must perform" (Exodus 18:20). The *baraita* parses the various directives in the verse. **"And you shall teach them," that** is referring to **the structure of their livelihood,** i.e., teach the Jewish people trades so that they may earn a living; **"the path," that** is referring to **acts of kindness; "they shall walk," that** is referring to **visiting the ill; "wherein," that** is referring to **burial; "and the action," that** is referring to acting in accordance with the letter of the **law; "that they must perform," that** is referring to acting **beyond the letter of the law.**

The Gemara grounds this discussion in a *baraita* taught by Rav Yosef, which derives a set of life principles from Yisro's advice to Moshe. The verse reads: *v'hodata lahem es haderech yelchu vah v'es hama'aseh asher ya'asun* — "and you shall make known to them the way they should walk in it and the deed that they should do" (Exodus 18:20). *Chazal* understand that Yisro is counseling Moshe not merely to teach *halacha* and *mitzvot*, but to transmit a broader way of living — one that is not fully captured by technical legal obligation. Strikingly, this advice comes from a non-Jew, and before *Matan Torah*, suggesting that it draws on a larger human wisdom about how a life is properly lived.

### Five Life Principles

Rav Yosef's *baraita* derives five distinct principles from this verse. *Zeh beis chayeihem* — "this is the way of their life," referring to *parnassa*, the pursuit of livelihood. Teaching people *halacha* alone is insufficient; they must also learn how to make their way in the world. *Es haderech* — "the way" — *zo g'milus chasadim*: this is the practice of acts of kindness, a way of life that transcends halakhic mandates. *Yelchu* — "they shall walk" — *ze bikkur cholim*: visiting the sick. *Bah* — "in it" — *zu k'vurah*: burying the dead. And *v'es hama'aseh asher ya'asun* — "and the deed that they should do" — *zu lifnim mishurat hadin*: going beyond the letter of the law.

The structure here is instructive. There is a broader morality — *mentshlekhkeyt*, *g'milus chasadim*, building a livelihood — that is not even specifically about the spirit of any particular law. Then there is the law itself. And then there is the spirit of the law, the ideal one should be striving toward: *lifnim mishurat hadin*. Rabbi Yishmael's behavior was an expression of this third category.

## Bikkur Cholim & Kevura

### Ben Gilo Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״אֲשֶׁר יֵלְכוּ״ – זֶה בִּיקּוּר חוֹלִים, הַיְינוּ גְּמִילוּת חֲסָדִים! לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְבֶן גִּילוֹ, דְּאָמַר מָר: בֶּן גִּילוֹ נוֹטֵל אֶחָד מִשִּׁשִּׁים בְּחׇלְיוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמֵיזַל לְגַבֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **The Master said:** With regard to the phrase **"they shall walk," that** is referring to **visiting the ill.** The Gemara asks: **That is** a detail of **acts of kindness;** why does the *baraita* list it separately? The Gemara answers: The reference to visiting the ill is **necessary only for the contemporary of** the ill person, **as the Master said:** When **one who is a contemporary** of an ill person visits him, he **takes one-sixtieth of his illness.** Since visiting an ill contemporary involves contracting a bit of his illness, a special derivation is necessary to teach that **even so, he is required to go** and visit **him.**

The Gemara now returns to parse the individual elements of Rav Yosef's *baraita*. Regarding *bikkur cholim*: isn't visiting the sick simply a form of *g'milus chasadim*? Why specify it separately? The Gemara answers: *lo nitzrecha ela le'ben gilo* — the *chiddush* is specifically with respect to one's *ben gilo*, a person born under the same constellation or astrological grouping. The Gemara cites a teaching that when one visits one's *ben gilo* who is ill, one actually removes one sixtieth of his illness — but also takes on that portion of the sickness oneself. One might therefore think that the obligation of *bikkur cholim* does not apply in this case, since the visitor will be harmed. The Gemara rules: *ve'afilu hachi mibayei lei lemizal legabeih* — even so, one is obligated to go and visit.

This raises a broader point about *bikkur cholim* and personal risk. Certainly one's primary moral responsibility is to oneself — as the *daf* established earlier — and if there is genuine danger of infection or serious harm, the obligation of *bikkur cholim* does not override that. But in the Gemara's framework, a minor diminishment — one-sixtieth of another's illness — does not rise to that level, and the obligation stands.

### Burial Dignity Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בָּהּ״ – זוֹ קְבוּרָה. הַיְינוּ גְּמִילוּת חֲסָדִים! לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְזָקֵן וְאֵינָהּ לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the *baraita*: With regard to the phrase **"wherein," that** is referring to **burial.** The Gemara asks: **That is** a detail of **acts of kindness;** why does the *baraita* list it separately? The Gemara answers: The reference to burial is **necessary only to** teach the *halakha* of **an elderly person, and** it is in a circumstance where **it is not in keeping with his dignity** to bury the dead. Therefore, a special derivation is necessary to teach that even so, he is required to participate in the burial.

Regarding *k'vurah*: isn't burying the dead also subsumed under *g'milus chasadim*? The Gemara answers here as well: *lo nitzrecha ela le'zaken ve'eino lefi kevodo* — the *chiddush* is that even one for whom this act would be beneath his dignity is still obligated to attend to burial of the dead.

This is a striking *gemara*. Within a religious society that honors the mitzvot of caring for the dead, how can burying the dead be considered *eino lefi kevodo*? Yet the Gemara acknowledges the reality. The more interesting question is whether the obligation here is a strict *din* or whether it represents *lifnim mishurat hadin*. One could argue that since the dignity of the deceased — *kavod habriyot* — is itself at stake, the competing *kavod habriyot* of the one who would be embarrassed to perform the burial is overridden. It is not merely money versus one person's dignity; it is one person's dignity weighed against another's. This would make it a strict legal obligation. Alternatively, this may be more in the nature of *lifnim mishurat hadin* — an aspirational standard that goes beyond the letter of the law.

Notably, the marginal notes (*hagahot*) in the printed text cite the *halacha* of *bikkur cholim* even in the *ben gilo* case, but there is no corresponding citation bringing down a practical *halacha* for *k'vurah* in a case of *eino lefi kevodo*. This asymmetry invites reflection on whether this case is indeed a binding *din* or something more aspirational. One might also observe — as was raised in discussion — that *Hashem* Himself buried Moshe, suggesting that perhaps the entire framework of *eino lefi kevodo* should not even be acknowledged as a valid consideration when it comes to burial of the dead.

### Jerusalem's Destruction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשׂוּן זוֹ לִפְנִים מִשּׁוּרַת הַדִּין. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא חָרְבָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם אֶלָּא עַל שֶׁדָּנוּ בָּהּ דִּין תּוֹרָה. אֶלָּא דִּינֵי דְּמָגִיזְתָּא לְדַיְּינוּ?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: שֶׁהֶעֱמִידוּ דִּינֵיהֶם עַל דִּין תּוֹרָה וְלָא עֲבַדוּ לִפְנִים מִשּׁוּרַת הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the *baraita*: **"That they must perform"; that** is referring to acting **beyond the letter of the law, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Jerusalem was destroyed only for** the fact **that they adjudicated** cases on the basis of **Torah law in** the city. The Gemara asks: **Rather,** what else should they have done? **Should they rather have adjudicated** cases on the basis of **arbitrary decisions [*demagizeta*]? Rather, say: That they established their rulings on** the basis of **Torah law and did not go beyond the letter of the law.**

The Gemara cites a remarkable statement of Rabbi Yochanan: *lo charva Yerushalayim ela she-danu bah din Torah* — Jerusalem was destroyed only because they judged by the law of the Torah. The obvious objection is raised: should they have used some other standard of law? The Gemara clarifies: *ela eima she-he'emidu dineihem al din Torah v'lo avdu lifnim mishurat hadin* — they were destroyed because they insisted on the precise letter of the law in every case and never acted beyond it. "I fulfilled my *halachic* obligation, I am done." There was no broader conversation about what the right thing to do was, no spirit of the law, no *lifnim mishurat hadin*. That orientation — reducing all of Jewish life to technical compliance — is what ultimately brought about the destruction of Jerusalem.

How this connects mechanically to the destruction is a broader discussion; the *Netziv* in his introduction to *Sefer Bereishit* addresses related themes at length.

## Defining Aveidah

### Grazing on Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵי זוֹ הִיא אֲבֵידָה? מָצָא חֲמוֹר אוֹ פָּרָה רוֹעִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ – אֵין זוֹ אֲבֵידָה. חֲמוֹר וְכֵלָיו הֲפוּכִין, פָּרָה רָצָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים – הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲבֵידָה. הֶחְזִירָהּ וּבָרְחָה, הֶחְזִירָהּ וּבָרְחָה, אֲפִילּוּ אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִישָׁה פְּעָמִים – חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִירָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָשֵׁב תְּשִׁיבֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Which is** the item that is considered **lost property?** If **one found a donkey or a cow grazing on the path, that is not lost property,** as presumably the owners are nearby and are aware of the animals' whereabouts. If one found **a donkey with its accoutrements overturned, or a cow** that **ran through the vineyards, that is lost property.** In a case where **one returned** the lost animal **and it fled,** and he again **returned it and it fled, even** if this scenario repeats itself **four or five times,** he is **obligated to return it** each time, as it **is stated:** "You shall not see your brother's ox or his sheep wandering and disregard them; you shall return them to your brother" (Deuteronomy 22:1).

The Gemara now turns to the next *mishnah*, which asks: *eizo hi aveidah* — what constitutes a lost object? The question might seem strange after all the discussion to this point, but the Gemara (through Rav Yehuda) explains that the *mishnah* is identifying the clearest paradigm: *matza chamor o parah ro'in baderech ein zo aveidah* — if one finds a donkey or a cow grazing on the path, that is not an *aveidah* and one is not obligated to return it.

### Signs of Loss

By contrast, *chamor ve-chelav hafuchin parah ratzah bein hakramim* — a donkey with its saddlebags overturned, or a cow running through the vineyards — *harei zo aveidah*, that is clearly a lost object. These are things no owner would allow to occur, and they indicate that the animal has gone astray without the owner's knowledge. The Gemara distills two criteria: the animal's behavior (grazing versus running) and its location (a normal path versus a vineyard). For an animal to clearly constitute an *aveidah*, both problematic criteria apply; if only one is present, the analysis becomes more complex.

### Three-Day Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְעוֹלָם?! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: עַד שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי בְּלֵילָוָתָא – אֲפִילּוּ חֲדָא שַׁעְתָּא נָמֵי, אִי בִּימָמָא – אֲפִילּוּ טוּבָא נָמֵי לָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּהֲוָה חָזֵי לַהּ בְּקַדְמְתָא וּבַחֲשֶׁכְתָּא, תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי אָמְרִינַן: אִיתְרְמוֹיֵי אִתְרְמִי לַהּ וְנָפְקָא, טְפֵי וַדַּאי אֲבֵידָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the ruling in the mishna that a donkey and cow grazing on the path are not considered lost property, the Gemara asks: **And** is that the case even if they graze there untended **forever? Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Until three days** pass they are not lost. Thereafter, they are considered lost. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** the animal is found grazing **at night, even** if it is untended for **even one hour** it can be presumed to be lost, as an owner never grazes his animals untended at night. **If** the animal is found grazing **during the day, even** if it is untended for **more** than three days, it is **also not** presumed to be lost. The Gemara answers: **No,** the measure of three days **is necessary** only in a case **where one saw** the animal grazing **in the early** hours in the morning **and in the dark** of nightfall. For the first **three days, we say: It happened** that the animal **went out** a bit earlier or a bit later than usual, but nevertheless, it was with the owner's knowledge. Once this is observed for **more** than three days, it is **certainly a lost item.**

But what if an animal is simply grazing on the path — normally not an *aveidah* — and one observes this day after day, with no owner in sight? At some point the evidence of abandonment becomes overwhelming. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: ad shlosha yamim* — after three days without an owner appearing, one treats it as an *aveidah* and is obligated to act.

### Early-Late Observations

The Gemara probes what this means in practice. If the animal is seen grazing at night (*b'leilyusa*), even a single observation suffices — owners do not send animals to graze at night. If one observes it only during daylight hours (*b'yimama*), even many days might not indicate abandonment, since the owner may be collecting the animal at night.

The operative case is the one where one sees the animal before dawn and again into the first hour of night, day after day — *lo tzricha d'havei chazi lei b'kadmusa u'v'chashachta*. Could such early and late grazing simply reflect an unusually diligent or negligent owner? Possibly, for one or two days. But *ad tlosha yomei amrinan ishtrumay ishtrumay lei* — for up to three days we say the owner's schedule might simply account for it. *V'nafak tfei* — beyond three days, it is certainly an *aveidah* and one must take the animal in.

## Aveidah Extended

### Flood Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאִסְרַטְיָא וּפָרָה רָצָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים – הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲבֵידָה. טַלִּית בְּצַד גָּדֵר, קַרְדּוֹם בְּצַד גָּדֵר, וּפָרָה רוֹעָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים – אֵין זוֹ אֲבֵידָה. שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים זֶה אַחַר זֶה – הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲבֵידָה. רָאָה מַיִם שֶׁשּׁוֹטְפִין וּבָאִים – הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹדֵר בִּפְנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **on a main thoroughfare [*be'isratiyya*], or a cow running through the vineyards, that is lost property.** If one found **a cloak alongside a fence, an ax alongside a fence, or a cow grazing among the vineyards, that is not lost property.** If one sees these items for **three consecutive days, that is lost property.** If **one saw water that is flowing and coming** to inundate another's field, **he must establish a barrier before** the water in order to preserve the field.

The *mishnah* introduces a new case: *ra'ah mayim sheshofin u-va'in harei zeh goder bifneihem* — if one sees water coming to flood a field, one is obligated to build a barrier to stop it. This extends the *mitzvah* of *hashavat aveidah* in two significant directions: to land rather than movable objects, and to *protecting* against an impending loss rather than simply *returning* something already lost.

### Rava's Abstraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: ״לְכׇל אֲבֵידַת אָחִיךָ״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֲבֵידַת קַרְקַע. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חֲנַנְיָא לְרָבָא: תַּנְיָא דִּמְסַיַּיע לָךְ, רָאָה מַיִם שֶׁשּׁוֹטְפִין וּבָאִים הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹדֵר בִּפְנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that the verse: "And so shall you do **with every lost item of your brother"** (Deuteronomy 22:3), serves **to include** an obligation to protect your brother from the **loss of** his **land. Rav Ḥananya said to Rava:** There is a *baraita* **that is taught that supports your** opinion. If **one saw water that is flowing and coming** to inundate another's field, **he must establish a barrier before** the water in order to preserve the field.

*Amar Rava le-chol aveidat achikha le-rabbot aveidat karka* — Rava derives from the verse "all the loss of your brother" that the *mitzvah* of *hashavat aveidah* encompasses even damage to land. The *mitzvah* is not limited to picking up a lost object and finding its owner; it includes protecting another's property from harm even when the property is immovable and the owner is present.

### Objects vs. Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי מִשּׁוּם הָא לָא (תְּסַיְּיעַי) [תְּסַיְּיעַן], הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בִּדְאִיכָּא עוּמְרִין. אִי דְּאִיכָּא עוּמְרִין, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא – לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִית בַּהּ עוּמְרִין דִּצְרִיכִי לְאַרְעָא, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דִּצְרִיכִי לְאַרְעָא, כִּי גּוּפַהּ דְּאַרְעָא דָּמַיִין, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rav Ḥananya: **If** you are attempting to bring support for my ruling **due to that** *baraita*, **do not support my** ruling. **With what are we dealing here** in the *baraita*? We are dealing with a field **in which there are sheaves** of grain on the land. The *tanna* of the *baraita* is referring to preservation of the sheaves, not of the land itself. The Gemara asks: **If** the *baraita* is referring to a field **in which there are sheaves** of grain, **what** is the purpose **of stating** it? Isn't it obvious that one is obligated to preserve the sheaves as he would any other item? **No,** it is **necessary** to state the *halakha* only in a case where **there are sheaves that need the land** in order to dry. **Lest you say: Since they still need the land, their** legal status **is like** that of **the land itself** and he is not obligated to return them, the *baraita* **teaches us** that the sheaves are independent of the land and must be preserved.

Rav Chananya offered a supporting *baraita* from the flood-protection case. Rava, however, was not fully satisfied, noting that the case could be read as protecting the bundles of wheat (*omerin*) in the field rather than the land itself — making it still a case of protecting a movable object. The Gemara engages this suggestion: if there are bundles that are closely associated with the land (*de-tzrikhei le-ar'a*), one might have thought they are treated as part of the land and thus exempt from *hashavat aveidah*; the *baraita* teaches otherwise. Whether the extension to land itself is established remains an open question — the Gemara notes that land (*karka*) is formally excluded from some categories of obligation, as in the laws of theft and robbery. But what is clear in either reading is that protecting a concrete object from imminent destruction — not just returning one already lost — falls within *hashavat aveidah*.

### Pikuach Nefesh

This expanded understanding of *hashavat aveidah* carries a striking implication. One of the sources for the *mitzvah* of *pikuach nefesh* — the obligation to protect a person's life or health when at risk — is precisely this broadened conception of *hashavat aveidah*. If I am obligated to protect your property against an impending loss, then *kal va-chomer* I am obligated to protect you against injury. As the Gemara reasons: if *hashavat aveidah* means protecting against loss and not only recovering what is lost, then it encompasses protecting a person's body as well as a person's property. This is a remarkably broad interpersonal mandate — not merely about lost objects, but about protecting people and their possessions.

## Mishna Contradiction

### Contradiction Identified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצָא חֲמוֹר אוּפָרָה [וְכוּ׳]. הָא גּוּפָהּ קַשְׁיָא! אָמְרַתְּ: מָצָא חֲמוֹר אוּפָרָה רוֹעִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ – אֵין זוֹ אֲבֵידָה, רוֹעִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ הוּא דְּלָא הָווּ אֲבֵידָה, הָא רָצָה בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְרוֹעָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים הָוְיָא אֲבֵידָה. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: חֲמוֹר וְכֵלָיו הֲפוּכִים וּפָרָה רָצָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים – הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲבֵידָה. רָצָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים הוּא דְּהָוְיָא אֲבֵידָה, הָא רָצָה בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְרוֹעָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים – אֵין זוֹ אֲבֵידָה!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **one found a donkey or a cow** grazing on the path, that is not deemed lost property. The Gemara asks: **This itself** is **difficult.** On the one hand **you said:** If **one found a donkey or a cow grazing on the path, that is not lost property,** from which it may be inferred that only if it is **grazing on the path it is not lost property, but** if it was **running on the path or grazing among the vineyards, it is a lost item.** On the other hand, **say the latter clause** of the mishna: If one found **a donkey with its accoutrements overturned, or a cow** that **ran through the vineyards,** that **is lost property.** From this wording it may be inferred that only if the animal is **running through the vineyards is it lost property, but** if it is **running on the path or grazing among the vineyards, it is not lost property.**

The Gemara now identifies an internal contradiction within the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* states: *matza chamor o parah ro'in baderech ein zo aveidah* — grazing on the path is not an *aveidah*. The implication is that *ro'in baderech* alone fails the test, but running (*ratzah*) on the path, or grazing in a vineyard (*ro'eh bein hakramim*), would constitute an *aveidah*. Yet later the *mishnah* states that *parah ratzah bein hakramim* — a cow running in the vineyards — is an *aveidah*, implying that only when *both* problematic criteria are present (running *and* in the vineyard) is it clearly an *aveidah*. These two readings appear to conflict.

### Context Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״יַגִּיד עָלָיו רֵעוֹ״; תְּנָא רוֹעָה בַּדֶּרֶךְ דְּלָא הָוְיָא אֲבֵידָה, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְרוֹעָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים. תְּנָא רָצָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים דְּהָוְיָא אֲבֵידָה, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְרָצָה בַּדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that the *tanna* employs the style of: **Its counterpart tells about it** (see Job 36:33), and the mishna distinguishes between grazing and running. The *tanna* **taught** a case of **grazing on the path, where** the animal is **not** considered **lost property, and the same is true** of a case where the animal is **grazing among the vineyards.** And the *tanna* **taught** a case of **running through the vineyards, where** the animal **is** considered **lost property, and the same is true of** a case where the animal is **running on the path.**

*Amar Abaye*: *yagid alav re'o* — let the context of each clause define what it means. When the *mishnah* says grazing on the path is not an *aveidah*, it implicitly includes all types of grazing — even grazing in a vineyard — since vineyards are mentioned explicitly later, and the contrast being drawn here is purely between grazing and running. Similarly, when the *mishnah* says running in the vineyard is an *aveidah*, path and vineyard are not the operative distinction; it is running versus grazing that determines the outcome. The location (path vs. vineyard) is incidental to the case being described; the *mishnah* is not generating rules about location.

### Direction Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי ״יַגִּיד עָלָיו רֵעוֹ״ – לִיתְנֵי קִילְּתָא, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן חֲמִירְתָּא. לִיתְנֵי רָצָה בַּדֶּרֶךְ דְּהָוְיָא אֲבֵידָה, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן רָצָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים. וְלִתְנֵי רוֹעָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים, דְּלָא הָוְיָא אֲבֵידָה, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן רוֹעָה בַּדֶּרֶךְ. אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: רָצָה אַרָצָה לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּאַפַּהּ לְגַבֵּי דַּבְרָא, הָא דְּאַפַּהּ לְגַבֵּי מָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: If** the *tanna* employs the style of: **Its counterpart tells about it, let him teach the lenient** case **and all the more so** it would apply to **the stringent** case. The Gemara elaborates: **Let** the *tanna* **teach** that when the animal is **running on the path it is lost property and all the more so** it is lost property when it is **running through the vineyards. And let** the *tanna* **teach** that when the animal is **grazing among the vineyards it is not lost property, and all the more so** it is not lost property **when** it is **grazing on the path. Rather, Rava said:** The apparent contradiction **between** the inference from the first clause with regard to **running** on the path and the inference from the latter clause with regard to **running** on the path is **not difficult. This** inference from the first clause that an animal running on the path is lost property is referring to a case **where its face is** directed **toward the field,** and it is running away from the city. **That** inference from the latter clause that an animal running on the path is not lost property is referring to a case **where its face is** directed **toward the city.**

Rava, however, is not fully satisfied with Abaye's resolution, and he offers his own: the *mishnah* need not rely on reading location as incidental. Instead, direction of movement is what matters. *Ha de'appa legabei dabra* — if the animal is running *away* from the city, that is an *aveidah*, whether on a path or in a vineyard, because an animal heading away from inhabited areas without an owner is clearly lost. *Ha de'appa legabei mata* — if it is running *toward* the city, that is not an *aveidah*, because perhaps it is heading home of its own accord. The old Chassidic teaching applies: it is not which rung of the ladder you stand on, but which direction you are moving.

### Two Aveidah Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רוֹעָה אַרוֹעָה נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בַּאֲבֵידַת גּוּפָהּ, כָּאן בַּאֲבֵידַת קַרְקַע. כִּי קָתָנֵי רוֹעָה בַּדֶּרֶךְ לָא הָוְיָא אֲבֵידָה, הָא רוֹעָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים הָוְיָא אֲבֵידָה בַּאֲבֵידַת קַרְקַע. וְכִי קָתָנֵי רָצָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים הָוְיָא אֲבֵידָה, הָא רוֹעָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים לָא הָוְיָא אֲבֵידָה בַּאֲבֵידַת גּוּפָהּ, דְּרָצָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים מִסְתַּקְּבָא, וְרוֹעָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים לָא מִסְתַּקְּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: The apparent contradiction **between** the inference from the first clause with regard to **grazing** among the vineyards and the inference from the latter clause with regard to **grazing** among the vineyards is **also not difficult. Here,** the inference from the latter clause that an animal grazing among the vineyards is not lost property is **with regard to loss of** the animal **itself. There,** the inference from the first clause that the *halakhot* of lost property apply in the case of an animal grazing among the vineyards is referring to **loss** in the sense of damage **to the land.** The Gemara elaborates: **When** the *tanna* **teaches** that in the case of an animal **grazing on the path,** the *halakhot* of **lost property do not** apply, from which it is inferred: **But** in the case of an animal **grazing among the vineyards** the *halakhot* of **lost property do** apply, it is referring to preventing **loss** in the sense of damage **to the land** caused by the animal. **And when** the *tanna* **teaches** that in the case of an animal **running among the vineyards** the *halakhot* of **lost property do** apply, from which it is inferred: **But** in the case of an animal **grazing among the vineyards** the *halakhot* of **lost property do not** apply, it is referring **to loss of** the animal **itself, as** an animal **running among the vineyards** is typically **wounded** with lacerations from the vines, **but** an animal **grazing among the vineyards** is **not** typically **wounded.**

Regarding the apparent contradiction about grazing in the vineyards — in one place it seems to be an *aveidah*, in another not — Rava offers a resolution that is conceptually significant: *kan be'aveidat gufa kan be'aveidat karka* — in one case we are speaking of *aveidat gufa*, loss of the animal itself, and in the other case of *aveidat karka*, damage to the land. As to the animal: grazing in a vineyard does not necessarily indicate that the animal is lost (*aveidat gufa*), because the owner may simply be allowing it to graze there. As to the vineyard: grazing in a vineyard is absolutely a problem of *aveidat karka*, because the vines are being damaged, and one must intervene to protect the landowner's property even if the animal itself is not technically "lost."

### Running in Vineyards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרוֹעָה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים, נְהִי דְּלָא מִסְתַּקְּבָא תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם אֲבֵידַת קַרְקַע! בִּדְגוֹי. וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם אֲבֵידַת גּוּפָהּ, דְּדִלְמָא קָטְלוּ לַהּ! בְּאַתְרָא דְּמַתְרוּ וַהֲדַר קָטְלִי. וְדִלְמָא אַתְרוֹ בָּהּ! אִי אַתְרוֹ בַּהּ וְלָא אִזְדְּהַרוּ בַּהּ, וַדַּאי אֲבֵידָה מִדַּעַת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to the inference from the latter clause that in the case of an animal **grazing among the vineyards** the *halakhot* of lost property do not apply, **although it is not wounded,** why not **derive** that the *halakhot* of lost property do apply **due to loss** in the sense of damage **to the land** caused by the animal? The Gemara answers: It is stated **with regard to** the land **of a gentile,** which one is not obligated to return or preserve. The Gemara questions this explanation: **But** why not **derive** that one is obligated to return it **due to loss of** the animal **itself, as perhaps** the gentiles will **kill it?** The Gemara answers: It is stated **with regard to a place where they forewarn** the owner **and** only **then kill** the animal. The Gemara challenges: **And perhaps they** already **forewarned** the owner **with regard to** the animal. The Gemara explains: **If they** already **forewarned** the owner **with regard to** the animal **and** the owner **did not heed** the warning, **this is certainly** a case of **deliberate loss,** where there is no obligation to return it.

Why is *running* in a vineyard different? *Deratzah bein hakramim miskiva* — when an animal runs through a vineyard, the thorns and undergrowth injure the animal itself. No owner would voluntarily allow this; it is therefore certain (*aveidat gufa*) that the animal is lost. *Vero'eh bein hakramim lo miskiva* — when the animal merely grazes in a vineyard, it is not injured, so the owner may have permitted it. The obligation to intervene arises not from *aveidat gufa* but from *aveidat karka* — the damage being done to the vineyard.

The Gemara adds: but if the vineyard is not owned by a Jew, there is no *aveidat karka* obligation (according to strict *halacha*, though *lifnim mishurat hadin* might still counsel otherwise). And might not the animal itself be at risk — since the vineyard's owner might kill an animal trespassing on non-Jewish property? The Gemara addresses this: in a locale where the practice is to give warning before taking action against the animal, the first incursion does not yet present a real danger. And if the owner has already been warned and allowed the animal to graze there again, *harei aveida mida'ato* — the owner has implicitly accepted the risk and it is treated as an *aveidah* he has brought upon himself.

## Returning Multiple Times

### Doubling Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶחְזִירָהּ וּבָרְחָה הֶחְזִירָהּ וּבָרְחָה [וְכוּ׳]. אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן לְרָבָא, אֵימָא: ״הָשֵׁב״ חֲדָא זִמְנָא, ״תְּשִׁיבֵם״ תְּרֵי זִמְנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: In a case where **one returned** the lost animal **and it fled,** and he again **returned it and it fled,** even if this scenario repeats itself four or five times, he is obligated to return it each time, as it is stated: "You shall not see your brother's ox or his sheep wandering and disregard them; you shall return them [*hashev teshivem*] to your brother" (Deuteronomy 22:1). The Gemara understands that from the use of the compound form of the verb, "*hashev teshivem*," the mishna derives that one must return the lost animal multiple times if it flees. The Gemara asks: **A certain** one **of the Sages said to Rava: Say** that from **"*hashev*"** one derives the obligation to return the animal **once,** and from **"*teshivem*"** one derives the obligation to return the animal **twice,** and beyond that there is no obligation.

The *mishnah* states that if one returns a lost animal and it runs away again, one must continue returning it — *afilu me'ah pe'amim*, even a hundred times — as the verse states *hashev teshivem*. One of the *rabanan* raised the following challenge to Rava: perhaps *hashev teshivem* means only twice — *hashev* once, *teshivem* a second time. On what basis does the *mishnah* derive a hundred times?

### First Word Indicates Many

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הָשֵׁב״ אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה פְּעָמִים מַשְׁמַע, ״תְּשִׁיבֵם״ אֵין לִי אֶלָּא לְבֵיתוֹ, לְגִינָּתוֹ וּלְחוּרְבָּתוֹ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תְּשִׁיבֵם״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּמִינַּטְרָא, פְּשִׁיטָא. אִי דְּלָא מִינַּטְרָא, אַמַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: "*Hashev*" indicates** that there is an absolute obligation to return the animal, **even** if it flees **one hundred times. "*Teshivem*"** teaches another matter: **I have** derived **only** that one may return the animal **to** the owner's **house. From where** is the *halakha* derived that one may return the animal **to his garden or to his** building in **ruins? The verse states: "*Teshivem*,"** to teach that **in any case,** wherever one returns the lost animal, he fulfills the mitzva of returning it. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** those areas are **protected,** it is **obvious** that one who returns the animal there fulfills his obligation. **If** they are **not protected, why** is he considered to have returned the lost animal? It will just flee again.

Rava's response is elegant and precise: *hashev afilu me'ah pe'amim mashma* — the word *hashev* alone, by its grammatical form, carries the ongoing, iterative sense of "it shall be returned" rather than the simple "you shall return." The doubling is not what generates the obligation to return many times; even the first word alone implies as many times as necessary.

### Teshivem Extension

If so, what does *teshivem* add? The Gemara derives a different *chiddush*: *ein li ela le-veito, ginato u-charto minayin? Talmud lomar teshivem mikol makom* — I might have thought the obligation of return is fulfilled only by placing the animal directly in the owner's house. How do I know that returning it to his garden or his yard also counts? *Teshivem mikol makom* — the double language broadens the forms of valid return, encompassing any protected location belonging to the owner.

The Gemara clarifies: this refers specifically to a *guarded* location. If the garden or yard is not properly enclosed, returning an animal there would not constitute a valid return. The *chiddush* applies only where the alternate location offers real protection — equivalent to the owner's house.

### Return Without Notification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּמִינַּטְרָא, וְהָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: דְּלָא בָּעֵינַן דַּעַת בְּעָלִים. וְכִדְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דְּאָמַר: הַכֹּל צְרִיכִין דַּעַת בְּעָלִים, חוּץ מֵהֲשָׁבַת אֲבֵידָה, שֶׁהַתּוֹרָה רִיבְּתָה הֲשָׁבוֹת הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** it is a case **where** the property is **protected. And this teaches us that we do not require the owner's knowledge** in order to return the lost item to him. **And** this ruling **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who says: Every** instance involving return of an item to its owner, e.g., by a bailee or by a thief, **requires the owner's knowledge** that it is being returned, **except for the return of a lost item, as the Torah amplified** the *halakha* to permit **multiple** forms of **return** by means of the compound verb "*hashev teshivem*," among them return without the owner's knowledge.

Having established that one may return an animal to the owner's yard rather than his house, the Gemara raises a further question: does a valid return require *da'at ba'alim* — that the owner be informed and aware? Perhaps one must knock on the door and notify the owner directly.

The answer is no: *hashavat aveidah* does not require notifying the owner. This is derived from Rabbi Elazar's ruling: *de'amar hakol tzrichin da'at ba'alim chutz me'hashavat aveidah she'hatorah ribta hashavot harbeh* — virtually all forms of repayment or restoration require the awareness of the recipient, but returning a lost object is an exception, because the Torah's multiple formulations (*ribta hashavot harbeh*) indicate that many forms of return are valid — including return without the owner's active knowledge. One may simply place the animal in the owner's pen and go home.

## Double Language Pattern

### Shiluach Hakan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שַׁלֵּחַ תְּשַׁלַּח״, אֵימָא: ״שַׁלֵּחַ״ חֲדָא זִימְנָא, ״תְּשַׁלַּח״ תְּרֵי זִמְנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites additional mitzvot where the Torah employs the compound verb form, and the Sages derived additional *halakhot* from the phrasing of the verse. With regard to the mitzva of dispatch of the mother bird from the nest before taking its eggs or fledglings, the verse states: **"You shall dispatch [*shalle'aḥ teshallaḥ*]** the mother, but the young take for yourself; that it may be well with you, and that you may prolong your days" (Deuteronomy 22:7). The Gemara understands that from the use of the compound form of the verb, "*shalle'aḥ teshallaḥ*," the Sages derive that one must dispatch the mother bird multiple times if it returns. The Gemara asks: **Say** that from **"*shalle'aḥ*"** one derives the obligation to dispatch the mother **once,** and from **"*teshallaḥ*"** one derives the obligation to dispatch the mother **twice,** and beyond that there is no obligation.

The Gemara now traces the same interpretive pattern through several other *mitzvot* that employ double language. *Shale'ach tishalach et ha'em* — the *mitzvah* of sending away the mother bird before taking her young. The *gemara* in *Chulin* teaches that if the mother bird returns, one must send her away again — even a hundred times. Here too, one of the *rabanan* asked Rava: perhaps *shale'ach* means once and *tishalach* means a second time — why assume a hundred?

### First Word Indicates Many

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״שַׁלֵּחַ״ – אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה פְּעָמִים מַשְׁמַע. ״תְּשַׁלַּח״ אֵין לִי אֶלָּא לִדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת, לִדְבַר מִצְוָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תְּשַׁלַּח״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: "*Shalle'aḥ*" indicates** that one must dispatch the mother **even one hundred times. "*Teshallaḥ*"** teaches another matter: **I have** derived **only** the obligation to dispatch the mother bird in a case where one takes the eggs or the fledglings and wants to take the mother bird **for a non-compulsory matter,** e.g., to eat it. In a case where one takes the eggs or the fledglings and needs the mother bird **for a matter** involving **a mitzva,** e.g., the purification of a leper, **from where** is the *halakha* that he must dispatch the mother derived? **The verse states: "*Teshallaḥ*,"** to teach that one must dispatch the mother bird.

Rava responds with the same structure: *shale'ach afilu me'ah pe'amim mashma* — the word *shale'ach* alone, in its iterative grammatical sense, implies as many times as the situation requires. What, then, does *tishalach* add? *Ein li ela lidvar hareshut* — I would have known the obligation applies when taking the birds for an ordinary permitted purpose. *Lidvar mitzvah minayin* — how do I know it applies when one is taking the birds for a *mitzvah* purpose, such as the birds used in the purification of a *metzora*? *Tishalach mikol makom* — the doubling extends the obligation across all cases, including those where the taking itself serves a religious purpose.

## Double Language Patter…

### Hochacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן לְרָבָא: וְאֵימָא ״הוֹכֵחַ״ חֲדָא זִימְנָא, ״תּוֹכִיחַ״ תְּרֵי זִמְנֵי?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the mitzva of rebuke, the verse states: "You shall not hate your brother in your heart; you shall rebuke [*hokhe'aḥ tokhiaḥ*] your neighbor, and not bear sin because of him" (Leviticus 19:17). The Gemara understands that from the use of the compound form of the verb, "*hokhe'aḥ tokhiaḥ*," the Sages derive that one must rebuke another multiple times if necessary. **A certain** one **of the Sages said to Rava: Say** that from **"*hokhe'aḥ*"** one derives the obligation to rebuke another **once,** and from **"*tokhiaḥ*"** one derives the obligation to rebuke another **twice,** and beyond that there is no obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הוֹכֵחַ״ – אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה פְּעָמִים מַשְׁמַע. ״תּוֹכִיחַ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא הָרַב לְתַלְמִיד. תַּלְמִיד לְרַב מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״הוֹכֵחַ תּוֹכִיחַ״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: "*Hokhe'aḥ*" indicates** that one must rebuke another **even one hundred times. "*Tokhiaḥ*"** teaches another matter: **I have** derived **only** the obligation of **a teacher to** rebuke **a student.** With regard to the obligation for **a student to** rebuke **a teacher, from where** is it derived? **The verse states: "*Hokhe'aḥ tokhiaḥ*"** to teach that one is obligated to rebuke another **in any case** that warrants rebuke.

The same question is posed regarding *hocheach tochiach* — the obligation to rebuke a fellow Jew. Does this mean only twice, or many times? Rava answers: *hocheach afilu me'ah pe'amim mashma* — the first word, *hocheach*, already implies an ongoing obligation, as many times as necessary. *Tochiach* then extends the obligation's scope: *ein li ela rav le-talmid* — I might have thought only a teacher may rebuke a student. *Talmid le-rav minayin* — how do I know a student may, or is even obligated to, remonstrate a teacher? *Hocheach tochiach mikol makom* — the double language extends the obligation regardless of relative standing, provided, of course, that it is done respectfully.

### Prika and Te'ina Revisited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עָזֹב תַּעֲזֹב עִמּוֹ״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּעָלָיו עִמּוֹ. שֶׁאֵין בְּעָלָיו עִמּוֹ, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עָזֹב תַּעֲזֹב״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. ״הָקֵם תָּקִים עִמּוֹ״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּעָלָיו עִמּוֹ. שֶׁאֵין בְּעָלָיו עִמּוֹ, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הָקֵם תָּקִים״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites additional derivations from compound verb forms. "If you see the donkey of him that hates you collapsed under its burden, you shall forgo passing him by; **you shall release it [*azov ta'azov*] with him"** (Exodus 23:5). **I have** derived **only** that one is obligated to help unload the fallen animal in a case where **its owner is with it. From where** is the obligation to unload it in a case **where its owner is not with it** derived? **The verse states: "*Azov ta'azov*,"** indicating that there is an obligation to unload it **in any case.** The verse states: "You shall not see your brother's donkey or his ox fallen by the wayside, and hide yourself from them; **you shall lift them [*hakem takim*] with him"** (Deuteronomy 22:4). **I have** derived **only** that one is obligated to help load the animal in a case where **its owner is with it. From where** is the obligation to load it in a case **where its owner is not with it** derived? **The verse states: "*Hakem takim*,"** to teach that there is an obligation to load it **in any case.**

The Gemara applies the same analysis to *azov ta'azov imo* and *hakem takim* — the obligations to unload and reload a collapsing animal. The verse regarding unloading says *azov ta'azov imo* — "you shall surely release with him," implying the owner's participation. *Ein li ela be'alav imo* — I would only know the obligation applies when the owner is present and helping. *She'ein be'alav imo minayin* — how do I know I must unload even when the owner is not present, is unable to help, or it is beneath his dignity? *Talmud lomar azov ta'azov mikol makom* — the double language extends the obligation across all circumstances. Similarly for *hakem takim* — the reloading obligation: the double language teaches that even when the owner cannot assist, the obligation remains. In both cases, the first word alone conveys the iterative, ongoing character of the obligation, and the second word broadens the contexts to which it applies.

## Both Mitzvot Needed

### Unloading Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמָה לֵיהּ לְמִכְתַּב פְּרִיקָה, וּלְמָה לֵיהּ לְמִכְתַּב טְעִינָה? צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא פְּרִיקָה – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא צַעַר בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים וְאִיכָּא חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס, אֲבָל טְעִינָה, דְּלָאו צַעַר בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים אִיכָּא וְלָא חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס אִיכָּא, אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן טְעִינָה מִשּׁוּם דִּבְשָׂכָר, אֲבָל פְּרִיקָה דִּבְחִנָּם, אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why does** the Torah need **to write** the compound verb form to teach the obligation in the owner's absence with regard to **unloading and why does** the Torah need **to write** the compound verb form to teach the obligation in the owner's absence with regard to **loading** the animal? The Gemara answers: They **are** both **necessary, as had the Merciful One written** this *halakha* only with regard to **unloading, I would say** that one is obligated to unload the animal even when the owner is not present, **due to** the fact **that** in the failure to unload the animal **there is** potential **suffering of animals and there is** potential **monetary loss,** as the burden might be damaged or the animal might die. **But in** the case of **loading,** where **there is no** potential **suffering of animals and there is no** potential **monetary loss,** I would **say no,** there is no obligation to load the animal when the owner is not present. The Gemara continues its answer: **And had** the Torah **taught us** the obligation in the owner's absence with regard to **loading,** I would say that it is **due to** the fact **that** his action is rewarded **with remuneration,** as one is paid for loading an animal. **But** with regard to **unloading, which is** performed **for free,** I would **say no,** there is no obligation to unload the animal when the owner is not present. Due to the unique element in each, both are **necessary.**

The Gemara now asks a deeper conceptual question: *lamma lei le-michtav prika ve-lamma lei le-michtav te'ina?* Why did the Torah need to specify both the obligation to unload and the obligation to reload? Given that we have already abstracted *hashavat aveidah* to encompass all forms of protecting property from loss, why not derive one from the other?

The answer is *tzrichi* — both are necessary. Had the Torah stated only the obligation to unload, one would have understood it as flowing from two convergent concerns: *mishum tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — because the animal is suffering, and *mishum chissaron kis* — because the owner faces significant financial loss (the animal may die or the goods will not reach their destination). The case is so compelling on both grounds that the obligation is obvious. But *te'ina* — reloading — involves neither animal suffering nor so immediate a financial risk. The animal is fine; the owner can potentially find someone else to reload for him. Without an explicit *pasuk*, one would have thought reloading falls outside the scope of obligation.

### Aveidah Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי לְמִכְתַּב הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי, וּלְמָה לִי לְמִכְתַּב אֲבֵידָה? צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי – מִשּׁוּם דְּצַעֲרָא דְּמָרַהּ אִיתָא, צַעֲרָא דִּידַהּ אִיתָא. אֲבָל אֲבֵידָה, דְּצַעֲרָא דְּמָרַהּ אִיתָא וְצַעֲרָא דִּידַהּ לֵיתָא, אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן אֲבֵידָה – מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיתֵאּ לְמָרַהּ בַּהֲדַהּ, אֲבָל הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי דְּאִיתֵאּ לְמָרַהּ בַּהֲדַהּ, אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the Torah **to write these two** mitzvot of unloading and loading, **and why do I** need the Torah **to write** the obligation to return **a lost item?** Write one of them, and derive the other from it, as they are all mitzvot to preserve another's property. The Gemara answers: Both **are necessary, as had the Merciful One written** only **these two** mitzvot of unloading and loading, one would say that it is **due to** the fact that in those cases **there is** the **suffering of its owner and there is** the **suffering of** the animal **itself. But** in the case of **a lost item, where there is** the **suffering of its owner but there is no suffering of** the lost item, I might **say no,** there is no obligation to return the lost item. **And had** the Torah **taught us** only the obligation to return **a lost item,** one would say that is **due to** the fact that **its owner is not with it** to care for it; **but** in the case of **these two** mitzvot of unloading and loading, **where its owner is with it,** I might **say no,** there is no need to assist him. Therefore, it was **necessary** for the Torah to write both.

Conversely, had the Torah told us only about *te'ina*, one would understand it as addressing cases where *the owner is absent* and *detza'ara demarei ita* — the owner's distress is acute. But unloading and reloading (*prika u'te'ina*) occur when *the owner is present*: *de'ita le-marei bahadah*. One might have thought: the owner is right here — why is this my responsibility? Let him handle his own animal. Without an explicit *pasuk* for *prika u'te'ina*, one would not have known that the obligation exists even when the owner is present and capable.

### Reloading Paid

An additional asymmetry between *prika* and *te'ina* further necessitates explicit instruction for each. The Gemara notes that *te'ina* — reloading — may be performed for pay. It is more like the work of a hired laborer assisting in the owner's commercial enterprise than like the emergency intervention of *prika*. Had the Torah told us only about *te'ina*, we might have known it is obligatory (because one can be compensated), but we would not have known that *prika* — which must be done without compensation — is equally obligatory.

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר: אַף טְעִינָה בְּחִנָּם, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא מְסַיְּימִי קְרָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Shimon, who says** that **even loading** must be performed **for free, what is there to say** to explain why it was necessary to repeat the obligation with regard to unloading? The Gemara answers: **According to Rabbi Shimon,** it **is not** clearly **defined** which of **the verses** is referring to loading and which is referring to unloading. Had the Torah written one verse, it would have been interpreted to be referring to unloading and one might assume that he need not load an animal in the absence of the owner.

Rabbi Shimon goes further, ruling that *af te'ina bechinam* — even reloading must be performed without compensation. From Rabbi Shimon's perspective, there is an even stronger reason why the Torah must specify both: since both unloading and reloading are done without pay, it is not obvious from one that the other is required. And there is a further complication: the verbs used in the *pesukim* — *azov ta'azov* and *hakem takim* — are not so transparently referring to loading or unloading respectively. Had the Torah mentioned only one, it would have been read as referring solely to unloading, and the obligation to reload would have remained entirely unaddressed. Only by specifying both twice does the Torah make clear that there are two distinct obligations, one for unloading and one for reloading.

The broader conceptual takeaway is that *hashavat aveidah* — properly understood — is not merely about recovering a lost object and reuniting it with its owner. It is a *mitzvah* that applies even without *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*, and even when the owner is present. It encompasses protecting people's property against imminent harm, and by *kal va-chomer*, protecting people themselves. This is the sweeping interpersonal mandate that the Gemara, over the course of this *daf*, has carefully elaborated.
