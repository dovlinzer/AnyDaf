# Zevachim 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Avodos & Pigul

### Opening & Four Avodos

The daf begins in the middle of a substantive conceptual debate — a welcome breath of fresh air after the extended *drashot* of the earlier *pesukim*. The Gemara on the bottom of 13b opens with *leima k'tana'ei* and we are squarely in the world of the four *avodos* of sacrifice: *shechita* (slaughtering the animal), *kabbalas hadam* (receiving the blood), *holacha* (transporting it to the altar), and *zerika* (sprinkling it on the altar). A *machshava* — a disqualifying thought — during any one of these *avodos* carries legal consequences. For ordinary sacrifices, a *shelo lishma* thought means the sacrifice does not fulfill the owner's obligation. For a *chatas* or a *pesach*, such a thought renders the sacrifice entirely invalid. Similarly, a *pigul* thought — intending to eat the sacrifice outside its proper time — arises during these same *avodos* and is a regular subject of the Gemara's discussion.

### Piggul & Shelo Lishma

Against this backdrop, the Gemara raises a focused question: what is the status of dipping the finger (*tvillas etzba*) into the blood of the *chatas*? This act pertains both to the inner *chatas* (whose blood is applied on the inner altar, as on *Yom Kippur*) and to the outer *chatas* (whose blood is applied at the corners of the outer altar). Can a *machshava* during *tvillas etzba* invalidate — either by generating *pigul* or *shelo lishma*? And does a *pigul* thought directed at *tvillas etzba* render the act the content of the invalid intent? The Gemara frames this as a question of whether *tvillas etzba* possesses the halachic status of an *avodah*.

### Tvillas Etzba as Avodah

The Gemara categorizes *tvillas etzba* as a form of *holacha* — part of the process of conveying blood toward the altar — rather than as *zerika* (the actual placement). One might intuitively assign it to *zerika*, since blood is being moved toward the altar. The Gemara acknowledges this and seems to prefer *holacha* because the act is not the final placement but a necessary step in getting the blood ready for placement. More significantly, the framing as *holacha* is appropriate because *holacha* is precisely the *avodah* about which there exists a debate between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon: whether thought during *holacha* invalidates. Since *tvillas etzba* is a form of *holacha* that cannot be dispensed with — unlike ordinary *holacha*, which in principle can be avoided by slaughtering adjacent to the altar — it should arguably carry greater weight as a necessary *avodah* even by Rabbi Shimon's logic. A parallel question arises from *mincha*: the *kometz* of the *mincha* parallels *shechita*, placing it in a *kli* parallels *kabbala*, and burning it on the altar parallels *zerika*. But *tvillas etzba* has no exact parallel in the *mincha* framework, which raises the additional question of whether one can recognize an entirely new *avodah*, or incorporate a non-standard act into an existing category, even when it is explicitly mentioned in the *pasuk* and is halachically necessary.

## Tvillas Etzba Tannaim

### Baraita on Tvillas Etzba

The Gemara cites two conflicting *Baraisos*. The first states: *t'vilas etzba m'paggeles b'chatas* — dipping the finger can cause *pigul* in a *chatas*, meaning it is treated as a genuine *avodah* during which intent matters. The second *Baraita* states the opposite: *lo m'paggeles v'lo mitpaggeles* — it neither causes *pigul* nor is it subject to being the object of a *pigul* thought during other *avodos*. The *v'lo mitpaggeles* clause is of less interest here; what commands attention is the dispute over whether *tvillas etzba* is itself an *avodah* that can generate *pigul*. The Gemara initially proposes that this is a straightforward *machlokes Tannaim* — a dispute as to whether *tvillas etzba* counts as one of the recognized *avodos*.

### Rabbanan vs. R. Shimon

The Gemara then reframes the dispute. Rather than being a debate specifically about *tvillas etzba*, it may reflect the broader *machlokes* between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon regarding *holacha*. For the *Rabbanan*, *holacha* is a full *avodah* during which *machshava* invalidates; for Rabbi Shimon, since *holacha* is an *avodah* one can in principle avoid, *machshava* during it does not invalidate. One might object that Rabbi Shimon is a *Tanna*, making this indeed a *machlokes Tannaim*. But the Gemara's point is that one need not say it is a debate specifically about *tvillas etzba*. Rather, everyone may agree that *tvillas etzba* counts as *holacha*, and the dispute is really about the broader question of *holacha* — which is a known debate.

### Inner/Outer Chatas

The Gemara then offers a resolution that avoids positing any *machlokes* at all. *V'lo kashya* — the two *Baraisos* are not in conflict. Each addresses a different case: one speaks of the inner *chatas*, the other of the outer *chatas*. By the inner *chatas*, the *pasuk* explicitly states *v'taval et etzba'o badam* — "he shall dip his finger in the blood." Since the act is both necessary and explicitly labeled by the Torah, it carries the full status of *holacha*, and *machshava* during *tvillas etzba* invalidates according to all opinions. By the outer *chatas*, while the act of dipping is halachically required, the *pasuk* does not explicitly say *v'taval*. Instead, it says *velakach b'etzba'o min hadam* — "he shall take from the blood with his finger." One might have supposed that since *velakach* implies taking with the finger — which is essentially dipping — it is equivalent to an explicit *pasuk*, and *machshava* should invalidate here as well. After all, if a monkey came and placed blood on the *kohen*'s finger, the *kohen* would still be required to dip his finger back into the blood (*ba'ei l'mitbal zimna achriti*), demonstrating that the act of dipping is genuinely required even by the outer *chatas*, not merely incidental.

### Monkey Example

The monkey case thus establishes that even for the outer *chatas*, the act of dipping is substantively obligatory and not merely the consequence of whichever method happens to get blood onto the finger. One might therefore conclude — since the required act is functionally identical to the inner *chatas* — that *tvillas etzba* should count as an *avodah* for the outer *chatas* as well, rendering it *k'man de'ksiv v'taval*, as if the *pasuk* had explicitly stated "dip."

---

## Scripture & Avodah

### Why Not Vtaval

The Gemara rejects this conclusion. The outer *chatas* is categorically different because the *pasuk* does not explicitly say *v'taval*. The word *velakach* carries dual interpretive weight: *Rashi* notes that one reading of *velakach* focuses not on the finger at all but on the blood itself — "*take the blood*," meaning the act of *kabbala*, catching the blood in a vessel from the neck of the slaughtered animal. Thus the word *etzba* is somewhat bracketed in that reading. The fact that the Torah chose *velakach* rather than *v'taval* signals that this act, though halachically required, is not being designated by the Torah as a central *avodah* of the *chatas* ritual.

### No Explicit = No Piggul

The Gemara states this as an operative principle: *lo ksiv v'taval — ka mashma lan*. The Torah deliberately avoided writing *v'taval* to indicate that while the act must be performed, it does not carry the status of an *avodah* for purposes of invalidation. If the *pasuk* had said *v'taval* explicitly, we would have treated *machshava* during *tvillas etzba* of the outer *chatas* as invalidating — *k'man de'ksiv v'taval dami*, as if it had been explicitly stated. But since the *pasuk* is ambiguous — *d'mashma hachi u'mashma hachi* — and does not explicitly designate this act, it remains a required step without the halachic weight that would make *machshava* during it operative.

### Inner/Outer Summary

The upshot is a clean distinction: for the inner *chatas*, where the *pasuk* explicitly labels *tvillas etzba*, the act is treated as a form of *holacha*, and *machshava* during it invalidates according to all views (including the *Rabbanan*). For the outer *chatas*, while the dipping is halachically required — and failure to dip renders the sacrifice invalid — it is not treated as a designated *avodah* for purposes of *machshava*, and thought during that moment does not invalidate. Both *Baraisos* are therefore consistent: one addresses the inner *chatas*, the other the outer *chatas*, and no *machlokes Tannaim* need be posited.

## R. Shimon & Holacha

### R. Shimon's Principle

Having resolved the tension between the two *Baraisos*, the Gemara now turns explicitly to Rabbi Shimon's position on *holacha*. The principle is stated clearly: *avodah she'efshar levatlah, lav avodah hi* — an *avodah* that can in principle be dispensed with does not carry the halachic weight of an *avodah* for purposes of *machshava*. Since one could in principle slaughter an animal right next to the altar and thereby eliminate the need for *holacha*, Rabbi Shimon holds that *machshava* during *holacha* does not invalidate.

### Inner Chatas Exception

The Gemara records that Rabbi Shimon himself acknowledges an exception: *modeh Rabbi Shimon beholachas chatas hapnimis shemachshava poseles bo*. For the inner *chatas*, even Rabbi Shimon concedes that *machshava* during *holacha* invalidates. The reason is *hoil ve'avodah she'eino yachol levatlah* — it is an *avodah* that cannot be dispensed with. Since the animal cannot be slaughtered inside the *heichal* or the *ulam*, it must be slaughtered outside, and the blood must then be transported inside. That *holacha* is therefore genuinely necessary — not merely incidental to where one happens to position oneself — and Rabbi Shimon agrees that *machshava* during a truly necessary *holacha* does invalidate.

### Temple Layout Diagram

Understanding why the *holacha* is unavoidable requires attention to the physical layout of the *Beis HaMikdash*. The outer altar stands in the *azarah*. Adjacent is the *ulam*, the vestibule, and beyond it the *heichal*, the inner sanctuary. The inner altar, on which the blood of the inner *chatas* is applied, stands within the *heichal*. The animal must be slaughtered to the north of the outer altar — in the *azarah* — since slaughtering inside the *ulam* or *heichal* is not halachically acceptable (and the Gemara implies it would be invalid, even though *Rashi* describes it only as inappropriate; for the Gemara's analysis to hold, the invalidity must be operative). The blood therefore must travel from the *azarah* into the *heichal*, making *holacha* genuinely unavoidable. This is why even Rabbi Shimon agrees that *machshava* during *holacha* of the inner *chatas* invalidates.

## Piggul/Shelo Lishma

### Piggul Problem

A difficulty now surfaces. If we are saying that *machshava* during *holacha* of the inner *chatas* invalidates — specifically a *pigul* thought — we encounter Rabbi Shimon's independent ruling that *kol she'ein al mizbeach hachitzon kishlamim, ein chayavin alav mishum pigul*: anything not offered on the outer altar as a *shelamim* is excluded from the laws of *pigul*. The inner *chatas* is by definition not brought on the outer altar. Therefore, according to Rabbi Shimon, *pigul* does not apply to the inner *chatas* at all. How then can we say that a *machshava* during *holacha* of the inner *chatas* would generate *pigul*? *Rashi* notes that the simpler answer would have been to say we are not talking about *pigul* at all — the *Mishnah* is fundamentally discussing *shelo lishma*, and a *shelo lishma* thought during *holacha* of the inner *chatas* would of course invalidate since it is a necessary *avodah*. However, since the Gemara framed the question around *pigul*, it answers within that framework.

### Piggul on Meat Only

The resolution is offered by Rebbi Yosi b'Rebbi Chanina: when Rabbi Shimon said *pigul* does not apply to things not brought on the outer altar, he meant specifically that the *status* of *pigul* does not attach to the *meat*. The significance of the *pigul* status on the meat is that one who eats it incurs *kares*. The meat of an inner *chatas* is already forbidden; one who eats it violates a *lav*, but even without *pigul* status one would not incur *kares* for eating it, since there is no *pigul* designation applicable. What Rabbi Shimon excluded, therefore, is specifically *kares* for eating the meat. He did not exclude the invalidation of the *korban* itself. A *machshava* of *chutz lizmano* during *holacha* of the inner *chatas* does invalidate the sacrifice — it simply does not confer the additional *kares* penalty on the meat. The same applies to *shelo lishma*: it invalidates the *korban*.

### Kal Vachomer

This resolution leads the Gemara to a *kal vachomer*. If *shelo lishma* — which does not invalidate a *shelamim* — nonetheless invalidates a *chatas*, then *chutz lizmano* — which *does* invalidate a *shelamim* (with *kares*) — should certainly invalidate a *chatas* as well. This is Rebbi Yosi b'Rebbi Chanina's logic: *umah shelo lishman shehuchshru bishlamim pasul bachatat, chutz lizmano shepasul bishlamim eino din sheyifsol bachatat*. The *chatas* is structurally easier to invalidate than the *shelamim*, and so whatever invalidates the *shelamim* certainly invalidates the *chatas*.

The Gemara then presses further: granted that *chutz lizmano* invalidates the inner *chatas* via this *kal vachomer*, what is the source for *chutz limkomo* — a thought of the wrong *place* — also invalidating the inner *chatas*? One might attempt a parallel *kal vachomer*: just as *chutz lizmano* invalidates, so does *chutz limkomo*. But the Gemara objects — *chutz lizmano* is weightier because it carries *kares*, and one cannot simply transfer that logic to *chutz limkomo*. Alternatively, one might derive *chutz limkomo* from *shelo lishma*: if the weaker (*shelo lishma*, which has no application to *shelamim*) invalidates a *chatas*, certainly *chutz limkomo* (which does invalidate a *shelamim*) should. But the Gemara objects here too: *shelo lishma* has broader scope in one sense — it applies even on a *bama* (a private altar during periods when *bamot* were permitted). *Chutz limkomo* is irrelevant on a *bama* because there is no fixed *makom*; it is not that *chutz limkomo* is halachically inapplicable, simply that the concept has no referent. But *shelo lishma* similarly has no real application on a *bama*, since only free-will offerings were brought on *bamot*, and *pesach* and *chatas* — the sacrifices where *shelo lishma* actually invalidates — were never brought on a *bama*. So neither can demonstrate the broader scope of the other, and the *kal vachomer* from *shelo lishma* to *chutz limkomo* is valid.

The ultimate source is a *hekesh* — an analogy derived from the same *pasuk*. The *pasuk* states: *im he'achol ye'achel bayom hashlishi pigul hu lo yeratzeh* — if it will be eaten on the third day, it is *pigul*, it will not be accepted. *Shlishi* — the third day — is *chutz lizmano*, a thought to eat it at the wrong time. *Pigul hu lo yeratzeh* would be redundant if it referred to the same thing; the Gemara reads *lo yeratzeh* as including *chutz limkomo* — the wrong place — thereby linking both categories in a single verse. Since the *pasuk* packages *chutz lizmano* and *chutz limkomo* together, whatever *din* applies to one applies to the other. Even according to Rabbi Shimon, therefore, a thought of the wrong time or the wrong place invalidates the inner *chatas*.

## Necessary Holacha

### Shechita Location

Having established that Rabbi Shimon agrees *machshava* during *holacha* of the inner *chatas* invalidates because the *holacha* is unavoidable, the Gemara now explores precisely *how much* of that *holacha* is truly necessary. This is where the discussion becomes particularly intricate. Rava opens with: *im timtzi lomar* — if you say that Rabbi Shimon holds like his son, who rules that the space between the *ulam* and the outer altar counts as "north of the altar." The requirement to slaughter the inner *chatas* to the north of the altar means, on the broadest reading, that *shechita* must occur directly adjacent to the altar's northern face. From that point, all the walking to the *heichal* would be necessary *holacha*.

### North Space

But Rabbi Shimon's son holds that even the airspace between the *ulam* and the outer altar — the open courtyard area that lies between them — counts as north of the altar. If so, one could slaughter the inner *chatas* anywhere in that space, including right at the door of the *ulam*. In that case, the only truly necessary *holacha* — the only portion during which *machshava* would invalidate — is from the door of the *ulam* inward. Walking prior to that point is not halachically required, because in principle one could have slaughtered right at the *ulam* doorstep. *Ein machshava mo'eles b'holachas chatas hapnimiyos ela mipisach ulam v'lifnim* — *machshava* is operative only from the door of the *ulam* and inward.

### Ulam Door Threshold

The Gemara continues with further refinements, now shifting to a different case — *siluk bezichin*, removing the incense (*levona*) from the *lechem hapanim* (the showbread) on the *shulchan*. This incense had to be brought out and burned on the altar, an act that functionally releases the bread for consumption. The question of necessary *holacha* applies here as well. If one holds that the floor between the *ulam* and the outer altar has the sanctity of the altar itself (a position attributed to Rabbi Yehuda — *toch azarah mekadesh*), then in principle one could burn the incense on that floor as soon as one exits the *heichal*. The only *holacha* that is truly necessary is from the door of the *heichal* outward. Any further walking, since the incense could have theoretically been deposited just outside the *heichal* door, is not required. In this scenario, *machshava* would be operative only from the *heichal* door and outward — *mipisach heichal velachutz*.

But why from the *heichal* door and not from where the inner altar actually stands? Because the inner altar could in principle be positioned right up against the *heichal* doorway, and from that position the incense could be burned on the floor immediately upon exiting. Any earlier movement of the altar within the *heichal* is not halachically mandated.

### Arm Stretch Only

The refinements continue. If one holds that the *heichal* and the *ulam* share a single level of sanctity (*kedushas heichal ve'ulam chada hi*), then the inner altar could theoretically be positioned anywhere within the combined space of *heichal* and *ulam*, up to the outer door of the *ulam*. Since the floor of the *azarah* already has the sanctity of the altar (in this framework), one could in theory place the *shulchan* right against the outer edge of the *ulam* doorway, take the incense, and burn it by merely extending one's arm to deposit it on the floor just outside. The doorway of the *ulam* had significant physical thickness — like a thick wall through which one walks. If the doorway itself (*toch petach*) shares the sanctity of the interior, then theoretically the *shulchan* could be at the very outer edge of that doorway, and the only necessary act would be stretching the arm to place the incense on the floor. Not even a full step would be required — only *bichedei lehoshit yado*, the distance of extending one's arm.

Taking this to its logical extreme: *ein machshava mo'eles afilu pesiah achas* — not even one step of *holacha* would carry *machshava* weight. The only operative moment would be the arm's extension from the threshold to the floor. And this introduces the next question directly: if the only "transport" is the stretching of an arm with no bodily movement of the feet, does that even qualify as *holacha* in a halachic sense?

---

## Holacha Beregel

### Hand-to-Hand Transfer

This brings the Gemara to a new question: *holacha shelo beregel, shemah holacha o lo shemah holacha* — if the blood is transported without any walking — for example, passed hand to hand among a line of *kohanim*, each standing in place — does that constitute a valid act of *holacha*?

The question is two-directional. If such passing does *not* constitute *holacha*, then the blood must be returned and transported again properly; but on the upside, since no *avodah* occurred, any *machshava* during that hand-to-hand transfer does not invalidate. Conversely, if hand-to-hand transfer *does* constitute *holacha*, then it was a valid *avodah* — but any disqualifying *machshava* during it would invalidate the blood. Similarly, if a *zar* performed this transfer: if it counts as *holacha* and *holacha* requires a *kohen*, the *zar*'s act would have invalidated the sacrifice; if it does not count as *holacha*, no act of consequence occurred and the blood may simply be transported again properly.

## Non-Kohen Holacha

### Abaye's Question

Abaye's question to the *amora* of Rav Chisda — relayed so that it could be posed to Rav Chisda at the next opportunity — is direct: *holacha bezar mahu*, what is the *halacha* if a non-*kohen* performs *holacha*? The background assumption is that ordinarily, a *zar* who performs an *avodah* invalidates it. But *shechita* is the well-known exception: a non-*kohen* may perform *shechita* and the sacrifice remains valid. The question is whether *holacha*, which follows *kabbala* (an act that unambiguously requires a *kohen*), is more like *kabbala* and *zerika* or more like *shechita*.

### Rav Chisda's Source

Rav Chisda responds that *holacha* performed by a *zar* is *keshera* — valid — and he supports this from a *pasuk* in *Divrei Hayamim*: *vayishchatu es hapesach vayizreku hakohanim es hadam miyadam* — the people slaughtered the *pesach* offering, and the *kohanim* sprinkled the blood *from their hands* — meaning from the hands of the Israelites who slaughtered. The implication is that after the *kohen* received the blood, a non-*kohen* carried it to the *kohen* who performed the *zerika*. *Rashi* notes that a *kohen* must have performed the initial *kabbala*, but once the blood was caught, a non-*kohen* brought it to the *kohanim* who threw it. This sequence suggests that the *holacha* portion was performed by a non-*kohen* without invalidating the *korban*.

### Rav Sheshes Counter

Rav Sheshes objects from an explicit *Baraita*: *zar ve'onen, shikor u'vaal mum — bekabbala, beholacha uvizrika pasul*. A non-*kohen*, a mourner (*onen*), someone intoxicated, or someone with a physical blemish — all of these disqualify the sacrifice if they performed *kabbala*, *holacha*, or *zerika*. The only exception is *shechita*. This *Baraita* is unambiguous. It goes on to note: *vechen yoshev vechen smol* — similarly, performing these *avodos* while seated or with the left hand is invalid, because service in the *Beis HaMikdash* must be performed while standing, as derived from *laamod leshares*. The contradiction between this *Baraita* and Rav Chisda's reading of the *Divrei Hayamim* passage requires resolution.

### Pedestal Solution

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: the *Divrei Hayamim* passage is describing a situation where the non-*kohen* was not performing an act of *holacha* at all. Rather, *de'avid maaseh itztaba* — he acted as a pedestal. The *kohen* caught the blood, placed the vessel on the non-*kohen*'s outstretched hands (without the non-*kohen* moving), and another *kohen* then took it from the non-*kohen*'s hands and carried it to the *zerika*. The non-*kohen* in this scenario is functionally an inanimate stand — he holds the vessel without transporting it. The *pasuk*'s phrasing — the *kohanim* took the blood *from their hands* — supports this reading: the Israelites did not hand it to the *kohanim*; rather, the *kohanim* retrieved it from stationary Israelites. This was not an act of *holacha* by a *zar* but merely the use of a human pedestal.

### Machlokes R. Shimon

Rabba and Rav Yosef then offer an alternative framing: *holacha bezar machlokes Rabbi Shimon verabbanan* — the question of whether a *zar* can perform *holacha* is actually tied to the *machlokes* about the status of *holacha* as an *avodah*. For Rabbi Shimon, who holds *avodah she'efshar levatlah lav avodah hi* — that an *avodah* one can in principle dispense with is not treated as a full *avodah* — *holacha* is downgraded and can therefore be performed by a *zar*. For the *Rabbanan*, who maintain that *holacha* is a full *avodah*, a *zar* who performs it invalidates. The proposed correlation is thus: the weight assigned to *holacha* as an *avodah* determines both whether *machshava* during it invalidates and whether a *zar* performing it invalidates.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye challenges this correlation. *Shechita* is an *avodah she'i efshar levatlah* — one that cannot be dispensed with; *shechita* is absolutely necessary for the sacrifice. Yet *shechita keshera bezar* — a non-*kohen* may perform it. So the proposed correlation — that an *avodah* one cannot avoid requires a *kohen*, while one that can be avoided does not — breaks down. The inability to dispense with an *avodah* does not entail that a *kohen* is required.

### Shechita Non-Avodah

The Gemara's response is pointed: *shechita lav avoda hi* — *shechita* is simply not an *avodah* in the technical sense. It is necessary, *machshava* during it can invalidate, slaughtering for *avoda zara* carries capital liability, and slaughtering outside the *Beis HaMikdash* carries liability for *shechutei chutz* — yet it is not classified as an *avodah* in the halachic sense that would require a *kohen*. The marker of its non-*avodah* status is precisely that a non-*kohen* may perform it. The Gemara acknowledges that the question of what exactly constitutes an *avodah* — and what cluster of *halachos* attach to that designation — is not fully resolved by a single criterion. Within the recognized *avodos* from *kabbala* onward, however, the correlation between *avodah* status and the requirement of a *kohen* does hold. *Shechita* is in a category of its own and should not be imported into this analysis.

An attempt is made to demonstrate that *shechita* is nonetheless an *avodah*, based on the ruling of Rav (cited by Rabbi Zeira) that *shechitas parah bezar psula* — slaughtering the *parah aduma* by a non-*kohen* is invalid. If the requirement of a *kohen* signals *avodah* status, and *shechita* of the *parah aduma* requires a *kohen*, then *shechita* must be an *avodah*. The Gemara even proposes a *kal vachomer*: if by *kadshai bedek habayis* — sanctified items that are not even sacrifices — *shechita* is treated as an *avodah* requiring a *kohen*, how much more so by *kadshai mizbeach*. But this is rejected: *shani parah, d'kadshai bedek habayis hi* — the *parah aduma* is anomalous, as it is not a *korban* at all and is not brought in the *Beis HaMikdash*. Its particular laws cannot be the basis for a *kal vachomer* about sacrificial *shechita*. Rav Sheshes the son of Rav Idi adds: the need for a *kohen* is not in itself a mark of *avodah* status, since inspecting *nega'im* (leprous afflictions) requires a *kohen* and is clearly not an *avodah*. So the inference fails, *shechita* remains a non-*avodah*, and the challenge from *shechita* to the correlation does not stand.

### Holacha Always Pasul

The Gemara ultimately concludes via Ulla in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *holacha bezar pesulah afilu Rabbi Shimon* — even according to Rabbi Shimon, a *zar* who performs *holacha* of the blood invalidates it. The Gemara's conclusion therefore assumes that even Rabbi Shimon's downgrading of *holacha* for purposes of *machshava* does not reduce *holacha* to such a degree that a *zar* may perform it. *Holacha* retains its status as an *avodah* — a *zar* performing it renders the sacrifice invalid — even if, according to Rabbi Shimon, *machshava* during a non-necessary *holacha* does not invalidate. The downgrading is specific and limited: it affects *machshava* for avoidable *holacha*, but not the fundamental requirement that the *avodah* be performed by a *kohen*.

## Holacha Beregel…

### Sitting/Left Hand

The Gemara now returns to the unresolved question of *holacha shelo beregel* — whether *holacha* requires actual bodily movement, i.e., traversing the distance with one's feet. The first source adduced is the *Baraita* cited earlier: *vechen yoshev vechen smol* — sitting or using the left hand invalidates *holacha*. If sitting invalidates *holacha*, this seemingly implies that standing while performing the same action (passing the vessel) is valid. That in turn would suggest that stationary hand-to-hand transfer — done while standing — constitutes valid *holacha*.

But the Gemara is not satisfied with this inference. Perhaps *yoshev de'kashif meishaf* — one who is sitting might be scooting along the ground, performing a form of bodily movement. If so, the invalidity of sitting is because sitting while moving is an inappropriate mode of service (*laamod leshares* requires standing); and the corresponding valid form — standing — would also involve some bodily movement (*ha'omed dumya deyoshev denayad purta*), a slight shifting while moving. On this reading, the *Baraita* does not tell us anything about whether pure hand-to-hand transfer without any movement constitutes *holacha*.

### Pesach Narrative

The Gemara then turns to the *pesach* narrative — precisely the passage raised earlier. *Shachat Yisrael, vekibel hakohen, nasan lechavero vechavero lechavero* — the Israelite slaughtered, the *kohen* caught the blood, and it was passed from one *kohen* to the next, until it reached the *kohen* who performed the *zerika*. This chain of hand-to-hand passing is familiar from the Gemara's earlier resolution of Rav Chisda's source, where it concluded that the Israelites functioned as pedestals. But here the question is different: does the chain of *kohanim* passing the blood to one another — without each necessarily walking — constitute a valid *holacha*? The narrative seems to present it as the proper procedure, suggesting it is valid. The Gemara responds again: *hatam nami denayad purta* — perhaps each *kohen* took a small step as he passed the vessel to the next, incorporating some bodily movement, however minimal, into the act of transfer.

### Pasul Recipient

This leads to a further nuance. The *Baraita* states: *kibel hakosher venasan* — if a valid (*kosher*) *kohen* received the blood and passed it to an invalid (*pasul*) *kohen* (or a *zar*), then *yachzir lakasher* — it must be returned to a *kosher* kohen. Now, this ruling might imply that the initial *kosher* *kohen*'s act of handing the blood to the *pasul* recipient was itself an invalid *holacha* — meaning no *avodah* took place, so the blood may be recovered and the *holacha* performed again. But it might also be read otherwise: another *kohen* can come and retrieve it from the *pasul*'s hands — *eima yachzor hakasher veykablah* — meaning the initial act of the valid *kohen* stretching his arm to hand the blood was a *holacha*, but since it landed in the hands of a *pasul*, it needs a valid *kohen* to retrieve it and continue. The ambiguity in the *Baraita*'s language — does *yachzir lakasher* mean "hand it back" or "let a valid *kohen* take it forward" — is genuine and unresolved.

### Unresolved Question

In the end, the Gemara does not reach a definitive resolution on whether *holacha shelo beregel* constitutes valid *holacha*. The question — whether the act of transporting the blood requires that the *kohen* physically traverse the distance with his feet, or whether stationary hand-to-hand passing suffices — remains open (*teiku*). Each attempted source either admits an alternative reading that preserves the possibility of bodily movement, or is ambiguous enough not to settle the matter. This unresolved question stands as the conclusion of the daf.
