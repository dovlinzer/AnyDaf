# Bava Kamma 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shevet Overview

### Opening & Context

The discussion on this daf picks up approximately fifteen lines from the bottom of 85b, at the words *amar Rava*, and concerns the calculation of *shevet* — the compensation owed to an injured party for lost wages or reduced earning capacity during the period of recovery. The framework established thus far is that a *shevet* assessment does not simply reimburse a person for the income they lost; rather, the injured party is evaluated according to what kind of work they remain capable of performing given the nature of the injury, and the tortfeasor pays the difference between that diminished earning capacity and the person's original capacity.

### Injury Examples

Several concrete examples illustrate this principle. A person whose hand has been severed can no longer perform most forms of manual labor requiring both hands; accordingly, the *shevet* assessment imagines them employed as a *shomer kishu'in*, a watchman of a pumpkin patch, which requires walking around but not the use of the hands. A person whose leg has been broken is assessed as a *shomer* at the city gate — a toll collector who sits in one place and need not move about at all. One who has been blinded is assessed as someone capable of grinding wheat at the millstone, *matchina bareichayim*, since that work requires neither movement nor sight. These examples demonstrate that *shevet* is calculated by identifying the highest-paying work the injured person can still perform, and compensating for the gap.

## Rava's Multiple Inju (II)

### Successive Injuries

The *gemara* introduces a question of Rava: *keta yado velo imduhu* — suppose a person severed someone's hand and the *beit din* had not yet had an opportunity to assess the damage. Then he went and broke the victim's leg — again without assessment. Then he blinded him, again without assessment. And finally he made him deaf. The question is: *mahu*? What is the law in such a case?

One possibility is that since the *beit din* never assessed each injury individually as it occurred, only a single assessment at the end is conducted, and the tortfeasor simply pays *dmei kulo* — the full value — in one lump sum. The alternative is that each injury must be assessed independently, resulting in four separate payments.

The practical consequence of this question — the *nafka mina* — turns on *tzaar* and *boshet*. For *nezek*, *shevet*, and *ripui*, the cumulative amounts would be essentially equivalent whether assessed together or separately, because each incremental injury reduces the slave-market valuation by a further step, and those steps add up to the same total. But *tzaar* — pain and suffering — and *boshet* — embarrassment — are categorically different. Each individual injury generates its own distinct pain experience and its own occasion for public embarrassment. Consequently, if each injury is assessed independently, the aggregate *tzaar* and *boshet* will be substantially greater than if all injuries are assessed in a single undifferentiated evaluation. As the *gemara* puts it, *dehai d'nezek veripuy veshevet kol chada vechada lo havina lei* — for *nezek*, *ripui*, and *shevet*, we do not assess separately — *dekeivan deyahiv lei dmei kulo keman dekatleh damei* — since paying the full value is, in a sense, like having killed the person in terms of earning capacity. But *tzaar* and *boshet* are not subsumed in that calculation.

---

## Shevet Overview (II)

### Tzaar & Boshet

It is worth clarifying the conceptual distinction at stake. *Nezek*, *shevet*, and *ripui* all relate to the economic consequences of the injury — permanent devaluation, lost wages, and medical costs respectively. *Tzaar* and *boshet*, by contrast, are experiential categories: one measures subjective suffering and the other measures public degradation. Because each injury is a discrete occasion of pain and a discrete occasion of embarrassment, the question of whether they are assessed together or separately is not merely procedural but substantively changes the amount owed.

---

## Rava's Multiple Inju (II)

### Assessment Method

Rava's question extends further. Suppose the injuries were assessed individually — that is, *beit din* evaluated each injury as it occurred — but the tortfeasor had not yet paid. Does the fact that the assessment occurred separately mean the payments must now be rendered separately, preserving the compounded *tzaar* and *boshet*? Or does the fact that payment has not yet been made allow the injuries to be collapsed into one consolidated payment, with *tzaar* and *boshet* evaluated as if they were one undifferentiated event?

The structure of the question thus has two layers. First: where there was no intervening assessment at all, do we assess each injury individually or all together? Second: where there was an assessment after each injury but no payment, does the assessment lock in the separation, or can the payments still be consolidated?

### Teiku Resolution

The *gemara* concludes: *teiku* — the question is left unresolved. All of Rava's formulations of this problem remain without a definitive answer. In monetary cases, the practical consequence of a *teiku* is generally that we cannot compel the tortfeasor to pay the disputed amount, but if the claimant has already taken possession of the funds — *ei tafas lo mafkinan* — we do not remove it from him, since he has arguable grounds for his claim.

### Rishonim Disagreement

What is striking is that the Rosh, at the end of *siman* 5 in his commentary, does not simply accept the indeterminacy of the *teiku*. He *poseks* — he rules — that assessing each injury individually makes more sense. The Tosafot Yom Tov and the *Pilpula Charifta* take issue with this approach: how can the Rosh simply decide a question that the *gemara* explicitly leaves unresolved? A *teiku* signals that the question is genuinely open; finding one resolution more intuitively compelling does not grant license to rule definitively. This tension reflects a broader methodological dispute among *rishonim* about what it means for a question to be left as *teiku* — whether it forecloses any ruling or merely reflects the *gemara*'s own uncertainty while leaving room for later decisors to reason their way to a conclusion.

## Shevet Permanent Injury

### Temporary Injury Shevet

Rava raises a further question — *ba'ei Rava: shevet hapocheto bidamim mahu*? What is the rule for *shevet* in a case where the injury reduces the victim's earning power but the injury itself will heal? Rashi explains: *hikino makah shehu tzarich leshevet mimalachto vesofo lihyot shalem kevarishonah* — he struck him with a blow that requires him to be off work, but his ultimate prognosis is a full recovery. The question cuts to the conceptual foundation of *shevet*: is *shevet* compensation for a permanent, lifelong reduction in earning capacity, or does it cover any period of lost wages, including temporary ones?

### Hand Bruise Case

The concrete case the *gemara* offers is *kegon shehikahu al yado vetzamtah yado* — someone struck another on the hand and it became swollen or bruised, rendering the hand temporarily unusable, *vesofo lachzor* — but it will eventually heal. The question bifurcates into two possibilities. On one view, *keivan desofo lachzor lo yahiv leh velo midi* — since the hand will heal and the person will return to their original employment, perhaps no *shevet* is owed at all. On the other view, *o dilma hashta mihat apchateh* — even so, right now you have reduced his earning power, and the time lost during recovery must be compensated regardless of what eventually happens.

This question, the *gemara* acknowledges, goes to the heart of what the Torah's phrase *rak shivto yiten* actually demands. All prior examples in the *gemara* had involved permanent injuries — a severed hand, a broken leg that in Talmudic medicine would heal improperly, blindness — all of which irreversibly alter the victim's earning capacity. But is *shevet* intrinsically tied to permanence, or can it attach to a purely temporary incapacitation?

### Parent Injury Braita

A *ta shema* is offered. The *braita* states: *hamakeh aviv ve'imo velo asa bahen chaburah* — if one strikes one's parents without leaving a bruise — and then it discusses *hachovel bechavero beyom hakippurim* — injuring someone on Yom Kippur — and rules *chayav bekulam*, obligated in all five categories of payment. The relevance is this: striking one's parent in a way that does leave a wound is a capital crime, and the principle of *kim leh d'rabah mineh* — that a greater penalty exempts from the lesser — would eliminate the monetary obligation. But striking one's parent without leaving a bruise, or injuring someone on Yom Kippur (which carries *karet* rather than execution), does not trigger *kim leh d'rabah mineh*, and therefore all five payments apply. The implication is that an injury without a permanent mark still gives rise to *shevet*.

The *gemara* presses: what does it mean to have struck one's parent without leaving a bruise? The immediate suggestion is that this refers to a case like striking the hand in such a way that eventually heals — *lav kegon shehikahu al yado vesofo lachzor* — and yet it says *chayav bekulam*, obligated in all five. That would seem to answer Rava's question directly, proving that *shevet* applies even to a temporary injury. But the *gemara* deflects: *kegon shechersho* — the case is where one rendered the parent deaf. And indeed Rava himself had stated that causing one's father deafness is a capital offense — *hacheresho le'aviv neherag* — because it is impossible for deafness of this kind to occur without a drop of blood entering the ear (*tipat dam nafla leh be'ozneh*), and that internal bleeding constitutes a wound even if no external bruise is visible. So the case of "no bruise" in the *braita* is not a case of a healing injury — it is a case of internal injury causing permanent damage. That reading does not resolve Rava's question.

The *gemara* then proposes a different case: *kegon she-gilcho* — perhaps the case is where one shaved the parent's head. But hair grows back — *mehadar hadar* — so that too seems temporary and self-correcting.

### Baldness & Job Loss

The *gemara* ultimately resolves the case by explaining: *amrei hacha bemai askinan kigon she-sacho nashra* — the case involves applying a depilatory resin or cream that destroys the hair follicles permanently, effecting lasting hair removal analogous to what we today would recognize as permanent depilation. With that, all five categories can be identified. *Tzaar* — *de-it lei kartufnei be-reishei* — because the victim had fissures or cracks in his scalp, and applying the caustic substance to those wounds caused genuine pain. *Ripui* — *de-baei asuyei* — because those wounds require medical treatment. *Shevet* — *de-hava markid bei kuvei* — because this person was a juggler or jester who earned his living by performing at taverns, and *de-baei mechavei gunei a-reisha velo mechavei me-hanei kartufnei* — because his act required vigorous head movement that he can no longer perform on account of the wounds to his scalp. And *boshet* — *ein lach boshet gadol mi-zeh* — there is no greater embarrassment than being forcibly and permanently stripped of one's hair. Some *acharonim* note that the *boshet* from baldness is not inherent to baldness itself — many people lose their hair naturally and gracefully — but rather derives from the sudden, involuntary, and premature nature of the loss, compounded by whatever cultural context one inhabits. In *Chazal*'s milieu, at least, such a forced disfigurement was considered a grave public humiliation.

The *shevet* here is notably subjective and occupation-dependent: this person's *shevet* is calculated not based on the generic "watchman of the cucumber patch" standard but based on the gap between his skilled, specialized employment and whatever lower-paying work he can perform in the interim. The case makes vivid that *shevet* carries an inherently variable, context-sensitive quality that the more abstract examples can obscure.

## Abaye vs Rava

### Abaye's Two Shevet

The *gemara* observes that what was a genuine unresolved question for Rabbah was obvious to Abaye and Rava in opposite directions — a phenomenon entirely recognizable in a *yeshiva* setting, where a *rosh yeshiva*'s open question can become the settled position of two leading students who each embrace a different side. The case before them: *hikehu al yado vetzimtah vesofo lachzor* — someone struck another on the hand, causing a severe bruise or swelling that rendered the hand unusable, but the hand will eventually heal.

Abaye rules: *noten lo shevet gedola ushevet ketana* — he pays both a *shevet gedola* and a *shevet ketana*. Rashi is essential here. *Shevet gedola*, according to Rashi, is *d'mei yado* — the value of the hand — meaning the lifelong, permanent-type assessment of the kind we have been calling *nezek*. *Shevet ketana* — *kol yamim sheyipol lemishkav vero'in oto ke'ilu hu shomer kishu'in* — is the day-by-day compensation for the period of incapacitation, assessed at the minimum-wage baseline corresponding to the injury.

Abaye's conceptual move is striking: he reconceptualizes what the *mishnah* calls *nezek* as a form of *shevet*, a lifelong reduction in earning power. For Abaye, both the permanent dimension and the temporary dimension of an injury's economic consequences are properly categorized under *shevet*, one large and one small. This reflects a deeper view that the underlying principle of *shevet* — compensation for impaired earning capacity — encompasses both temporal scales.

### Rava's Daily Shevet

Rava disagrees sharply: *eino noten lo ela d'mei shivto shebechol yom vayom* — one pays only the day-by-day *shevet* for each day of incapacitation. For Rava, there is no such thing as *shevet gedola*. *Nezek* and *shevet* are categorically distinct: *nezek* applies only when there is permanent damage, and *shevet* compensates only for temporary lost wages. Since the injury here will heal, *nezek* does not apply at all; only the temporary *shevet ketana* is owed.

### Eved Ivri Case

The dispute is then applied to a case involving an *eved Ivri* — a Hebrew indentured servant — whose hand has been severed by a third party. An *eved Ivri* is not owned in the full sense; he is a free person who has contracted his labor to a master. The question becomes: when a third party injures the *eved* in a way that impairs his ability to work, who receives the *nezikin* payment, and for which components?

### Eved Ivri Payments

Abaye rules: *noten lo shevet gedola la'eved veshevet ketana larav* — the *shevet gedola* goes to the *eved* himself, since it pertains to the permanent value of his person, while the *shevet ketana* — the temporary loss of labor during recovery — goes to the master, since the master holds the contractual right to the *eved*'s labor during the term of service.

Rava rules: *hakol yinaten la'eved* — everything goes to the *eved*. The assessment of *nezikin* is not about who purchased the rights to someone's labor; it is about who suffered the injury. Furthermore, *veyilakach bahen karka* — the *eved* should use the money to purchase land — *veharav ochel peirot* — and the master is entitled to the produce of that land. This reflects the *halakhic* structure of the master-*eved* relationship more precisely: the master does not own the *eved*'s labor outright, but rather functions as *ochel peirot* — he benefits from the fruits of the labor. This is structurally parallel to the relationship between a husband and wife regarding landed property in a marriage. Therefore, the *nezikin* payment goes to the *eved*, the land is purchased with it, and the master draws his entitled benefit through the produce.

The *gemara* raises a challenge against Rava: *peshita pichat eitzel atzmo ve'eitzel rabbo lo pichat* — obviously the *eved* himself was devalued; but was he devalued relative to his master? And it clarifies that the dispute between Abaye and Rava is confined to cases where the injury actually affected the *eved*'s capacity to work — *pichat eitzel rabbo* — whereas if the injury was to a peripheral location such as an earlobe or nostril (*lerish udna o lerish nechira*), that injury affects his personal valuation but not his productive output, and all agree that the *nezikin* payment goes to the *eved* himself, who buys land with it and from which the master then takes *peirot*.

## Boshet Mishnah

### Assessment Standard

The *mishnah* rules: *boshet hakol lefi hamivayesh vehamitbayesh* — embarrassment is assessed entirely according to both the one who caused the embarrassment and the one who was embarrassed. The standard is thus thoroughly subjective and bilateral.

### Tanna Identity

The *gemara* immediately asks: *mani matnitin*? Whose position does the *mishnah* represent? It cannot be Rabbi Meir, it cannot be Rabbi Yehuda; it must be Rabbi Shimon.

### Rabbi Meir Standard

The *braita* records Rabbi Meir's position: *vekulan ro'in otam ke'ilu hem bnei chorin sheyardu minechaseihem shehem bnei Avraham Yitzchak veYaakov* — we assess every Jewish person's *boshet* as if they were free people of noble lineage who have simply lost their wealth, because all Jews are descendants of Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, and that ancestry confers equal stature on all. Rabbi Meir applies a single, flat, universal standard. This clearly cannot fit the *mishnah*, which explicitly varies the assessment based on the identity of the parties.

### Rabbi Yehuda Standard

Rabbi Yehuda takes the opposite approach, fully subjective: *hagadol lefi godlo vehakatan lefi katno* — the more prominent or wealthy person is assessed at the higher level, the less prominent at the lower. Greater prominence means greater *boshet* when embarrassed. This too cannot fit the *mishnah*, as the next *mishnah* — to be addressed shortly — rules that one is liable for embarrassing a blind person, whereas Rabbi Yehuda holds that a blind person has no *boshet*.

### Rabbi Shimon Standard

Rabbi Shimon occupies a middle position: *ashirim ro'in otam ke'ilu hem bnei chorin sheyardu minechaseihem* — wealthy people are assessed as free people who have lost their money, meaning we reduce even the wealthy to a modest baseline and do not take full account of their prominence. *Aniyim kepchutim shebahem* — poor people are assessed at the lowest level of poverty. Two tiers, both bumped downward. The rationale connects to the procedural principle of *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'aya* — the burden of proof falls on the one seeking to extract money, and courts must therefore be conservative in imposing liability. The *gemara* concludes that the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Shimon.

The *gemara* considers whether the *braita* describing wrong-target embarrassment — *nitkaven livayesh et hakatan uvayesh et hagadol* — can be attributed to any of the three *tannaim*. It concludes after extended analysis that it fits Rabbi Yehuda, provided one reads *ein la'avadim boshet* narrowly: Rabbi Yehuda means that slaves do not have the *right* to collect their *boshet* — *lemishkal minei* — but they can still serve as the basis for a *boshet* assessment — *le-meisham sha'iminan behu*. Hence if one intended to embarrass a slave and instead embarrassed a free person, the free person collects the amount that would have been assessed for embarrassing the slave. Alternatively, the *gemara* suggests *gadol* and *katan* in the *braita* may simply refer to an adult and a child respectively, not to wealthy and poor, in which case the *braita* could even fit Rabbi Meir, since Rabbi Meir's equalization of standing among adults would not necessarily extend to equalizing adults with children.

## Intention Requirement

### Sleeping Person

The *mishnah* had stated: *veyashen she'biyyesh patur* — a person who is asleep and who while asleep embarrasses someone else is exempt from paying *boshet*. The exemption turns on intention: *boshet* requires *kavana livayesh*, an intention to cause embarrassment. Unlike the general rule of *adam muad le'olam* — that a person is always liable for the physical damage they cause regardless of intent — *boshet* does not follow this principle. Without purposeful intent, no *boshet* liability attaches.

### Wrong Target Case

The *gemara* asks: whose position is the rule that *nitkaven livayesh et hakatan uvayesh et hagadol noten lagadol miboshto shel katan* — if one intended to embarrass a child and ended up embarrassing an adult, one pays the adult the amount assessed for embarrassing the child? And similarly *livayesh et ha'eved uvayesh ben chorin* — intended to embarrass a slave and embarrassed a free person, one pays the free person the assessment for embarrassing the slave. The *gemara* works through the three positions of the *tannaim* to determine whose view this represents.

### Murder Comparison

The *gemara* draws an illuminating comparison: *mai ta'ama de-katla mah katla ad de-michavein lei* — why should *boshet* require intention directed at the specific victim? Because it is analogous to the law of killing. The difference between murder and manslaughter turns entirely on premeditation and directed intent — *dich'tiv ve-arav lo ve-kam alav*, the verse speaks of lying in wait *for him*, meaning you had to have had that specific person in mind. Just as murder requires intent directed at the actual victim, *boshet* requires that the embarrassment was directed at the person who ends up collecting. This is a striking comparison that *Chazal* develop elsewhere as well: *hamalbein penei chaveiro barabim chayav mitah* — publicly embarrassing someone is compared to taking their life, *zeh shofech damim*, because the blood drains from a person's face in embarrassment just as it drains from a body in death.

### Struggle Source

The *scriptural* source for the *kavanah* requirement in *boshet* is *dich'tiv ve-shalcha yadah ve-hechezika bi-mivushav* — the passage describing a woman who extends her hand during a brawl to grab the attacker in the genital area in defense of her husband. The verse emphasizes the deliberate extension of her hand — *ad de-michavein lei* — she had to have specifically intended to do that act. From here the *gemara* derives that *boshet* liability requires intentional, purposive action directed at the specific form of embarrassment caused.

## Special Cases Mishnah

### Liable Cases

The *mishnah* states: *hamevayesh et ha'arum* — one who embarrasses a naked person is liable; *hamevayesh et hasuma* — one who embarrasses a blind person is liable; *vehamevayesh et hayashen* — one who embarrasses a sleeping person is liable.

### Sleeping Exemption

*Veyashen she'biyyesh patur* — but a sleeping person who embarrasses others is exempt, since the sleeping person lacked any intention to cause embarrassment.

### Roof Fall Rule

*Nafal min hagag vehizzik uviyyesh* — a person who fell from a roof and thereby caused both damage and embarrassment is *chayav al hanezek upatur al haboshet ad sheyehe mitkavven* — liable for the *nezek* but exempt from *boshet* unless there was intention. This follows directly from the principle already established: *adam muad le'olam* applies to physical damage and requires no intent, but *boshet* demands *kavanah*. The *gemara* notes that this rule had already appeared in the discussion of *nezek* at 27a and resurfaces here in the context of *boshet*.

## Comparative Embarrassment

### Naked Less Serious

The *braita* teaches: *biyyesho arum hayyav, ve'eno domeh biyyesho arum le-biyyesho lavush* — one who embarrasses a naked person is liable, yet there is no comparison between embarrassing a naked person and embarrassing a clothed person. Embarrassing someone who is already unclothed is less serious than embarrassing someone who is clothed and then revealed. If a person is partially exposed already and then exposed further, the *boshet* is real but diminished compared to having one's clothing removed when one is fully dressed.

### Location Difference

Similarly: *biyyesho beveit hamerhatz hayyav, ve'eno domeh biyyesho beveit hamerhatz le-biyyesho bashuk* — one is liable for embarrassing someone in the bathhouse, but the embarrassment in the bathhouse is nowhere near as serious as the same embarrassment in the marketplace. In a setting like a bathhouse or locker room, partial or full undress is expected and normalized; the same public exposure in a marketplace or open street carries far greater humiliation. This gradation reflects the *gemara*'s attentiveness to social context as a genuine variable in calculating *boshet*.

The discussion of bathhouse embarrassment occasions a *sugya* on *kevod haberiyot*, which is only briefly touched upon here. The *halakhic* principle that *kevod haberiyot* overrides a rabbinic prohibition is often misapplied. The more precise reading, as many *rishonim* explain, is that *kevod haberiyot doheh lo ta'aseh sheba-torah* refers to one specific biblical prohibition — the *lo ta'aseh* of *lo tasur*, the obligation to follow rabbinic enactments — meaning that *kevod haberiyot* can suspend a rabbinic rule in the narrow situations where *Chazal* applied it: clothing oneself in the street, basic bodily needs on *Shabbat*, and comparable cases of intimate human dignity. The category is not a broad license to override rabbinic law whenever something is inconvenient or embarrassing.

### Wind Case

Rav Pappa addresses the ruling that embarrassing a naked person still incurs liability, raising the obvious question: *arum bar boshet hu*? Is a naked person really someone who can be embarrassed? He answers: *mai arum? da'ati zika vekarchei lemanei* — what does it mean that the person was "naked"? The wind came and blew up his garment, since men of that era wore long robe-like smocks without undergarments. Then *ve'ata hu vedalineihu tefei uveisho* — someone came along and pulled the garment up further, compounding what the wind had begun. The person was not freely walking around naked; he was caught in an embarrassing moment by the wind and then made worse by another person's deliberate act. That is when liability for *boshet* attaches even to a "naked" person.

### Bathhouse Clarification

Similarly, Rav Pappa explains that *biyyesho beveit hamerhatz* does not mean embarrassing someone in a closed, private bathhouse — where everyone is undressed and there is no particular *boshet* to speak of — but rather *shebeisho al gav hanahar*, embarrassing someone who was bathing by the river in a semi-public setting. The riverbank is neither fully private nor fully public, and there a person retains some expectation of dignity. Exposure or embarrassment in that context is humiliating precisely because of that intermediate status.

## Asleep Then Died

### Death Question

Rabbi Abba bar Memel poses a question: *beisho yashen umet* — what if someone was embarrassed while asleep and then died before waking? The person never experienced the embarrassment themselves. Is the tortfeasor nonetheless obligated to pay *boshet* to the deceased's heirs?

### Two Frameworks

Rav Zvid explains that the question hinges on the underlying rationale of *boshet*. Is *boshet* grounded in *kisufa* — the victim's own subjective emotional experience of humiliation? Rashi glosses *kisufa* as *chapiyat panim*, the personal distress and internal hurt felt by the person who was embarrassed. Or is *boshet* grounded in *ziluta* — something broader, the societal degradation of the person's standing, independent of whether they personally felt anything? Rashi glosses *ziluta* as *demizalzel bei bifnei rabbim* — the act of publicly diminishing someone in the eyes of others.

### Dead Person Analysis

If *boshet* is rooted in *kisufa*, the dead person's claim is weak: *veha mit lei deleit lei kisufa* — once a person dies, they can no longer experience the emotional hurt. But if *boshet* is rooted in *ziluta*, the claim survives: *veha ozlei* — you still diminished this person's standing while they were alive, regardless of whether they were aware of it, and that degradation already occurred as a social fact.

## Deaf Child Fool

### Rights Assignment

A *braita* is introduced: *Rabbi Meir omer cheresh vekatan yesh lahem boshet* — a deaf person and a child have *boshet* rights, meaning one is liable for embarrassing them. *Shoteh ein lo boshet* — but a *shoteh*, a person with severe mental disability characterized by total unawareness of social reality, does not have *boshet* rights. The *gemara* is careful to note that the *halakhic* category of *shoteh* — one who acts in a completely irrational way and has no awareness of their place in society — is quite distinct from what we today recognize as mental disability or special needs. Most people with disabilities or special needs retain a robust sense of their own identity and social standing and can manifestly be embarrassed; they would not fall under the *gemara*'s definition of *shoteh*.

### Ziluta Test

The *gemara* tests the *ziluta* framework against the *braita*. If *boshet* is about societal standing, why does the *braita* specifically mention *katan*? A child's position in society is already of lesser significance than an adult's, so it makes sense that the *braita* would need to affirmatively teach that children too have *boshet* — because without that teaching, one might have thought the child's diminished social standing means no *boshet* is owed. That fits *ziluta* well: the *braita* teaches that even with a lower social profile, children still have a protected standing that others are not permitted to degrade.

### Maximum Embarrassment

But the *ziluta* framework runs into difficulty with the exclusion of *shoteh*. If *boshet* is about societal standing, then even the family of a *shoteh* has a standing that could be further damaged by someone embarrassing the *shoteh* in public. The *gemara* responds: *shoteh ein lach boshet gadol mizo* — being a *shoteh* is already the maximum possible embarrassment for the family. There is no incremental *boshet* to be calculated, since the family's standing has already been reduced to the lowest point by virtue of having a *shoteh* in the household. Any additional embarrassment would be subsumed within that already-existing condition.

### Rav Pappa Kisufa

Rav Pappa then offers a *kisufa*-based account of why children have *boshet*: *demachlimu lei umichlam* — we are talking about cases where you say something to a child and the child actually feels embarrassed, where the child actually experiences the hurt. This is a contextual and developmentally sensitive standard. A two-year-old is different from an eight-year-old, who is different from a twelve-year-old; what rolls off one child's back may genuinely wound another. The *boshet* of a child is calibrated to whether the particular child, at their particular age and development, actually experiences the humiliation.

## Boshet Basis

### Kisufa vs Family

Rav Pappa raises what the *gemara* presents as his own open question: *mishum kisufa didei* — is the basis of *boshet* the victim's own personal embarrassment, the inner hurt they experience? *Vahamis lei* — but what if the person has died? Or is it *mishum boshet mishpacha* — for the embarrassment of the family unit, a wider circle of those whose standing is affected by the degradation?

### Child Test

The *gemara* tests the family-embarrassment thesis against the case of *katan*. If *boshet* is for the family, it makes sense to include children, because embarrassing a child embarrasses the family even if the child does not personally process the humiliation in the way an adult would.

### Fool Test

But the *mishpacha* thesis again runs into the *shoteh*. If *boshet* is for the family, then embarrassing a *shoteh* should embarrass the *shoteh*'s family — and the *gemara* gives the same answer as before: *shoteh ein lach boshet gadol mizo* — the family already endures the maximum possible embarrassment from having a *shoteh*, and there is no further degradation that can be added.

### Rav Pappa Reconsidered

The *gemara* concludes by returning to Rav Pappa's own position: *amar Rav Pappa, ain, demachlimu lei umichlam* — even at the personal *kisufa* level, children can have *boshet*, because it is assessed contextually based on whether this particular child, at this stage of development, would actually feel the hurt. A child's *boshet* need not be explained through the broader *mishpacha* framework; it can be grounded in the child's own experiential capacity for embarrassment, provided that capacity is present. The assessment of what a given child can feel scales with age and circumstance, creating a sliding, developmentally sensitive standard. The *gemara* leaves the ultimate resolution of Rav Pappa's framing open, closing with the observation that this is how far we have reached on 86a, with the continuation to follow on the next *daf*.
