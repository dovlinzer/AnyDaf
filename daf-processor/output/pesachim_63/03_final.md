# Pesachim 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shelo L'Ochlav

### Intro: Shelo L'Ochlav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: הִקְדִּים מוּלִים לַעֲרֵלִים — כָּשֵׁר, עֲרֵלִים לְמוּלִים — פָּסוּל. מַאי שְׁנָא מוּלִין לַעֲרֵלִים דְּכָשֵׁר — דְּכוּלַּהּ עׇרְלָה בָּעֵינַן, וְלֵיכָּא? עֲרֵלִים לְמוּלִין נָמֵי, כּוּלַּהּ עׇרְלָה בָּעֵינַן, וְלֵיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that ***Aḥerim* say:** If one sacrifices a Paschal lamb for both circumcised and uncircumcised people and had in mind **first the circumcised people and then the uncircumcised people,** the offering is **valid.** But if he had in mind first **the uncircumcised people** and then **the circumcised people, it is disqualified.** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about having in mind first **the circumcised people** and then **the uncircumcised people,** such **that** the offering **is valid?** One might say **that** in order to disqualify the sacrifice, **we require that all the people** he has in mind **be uncircumcised, and** this **is not** the case here, as some of them are circumcised. But if this is so, when he has in mind first **the uncircumcised people** and then **the circumcised people,** we should **also** say **that** in order to disqualify the sacrifice **we require that all the people** he has in mind **be uncircumcised, and** this **is not** the case here. What, then, is the difference between the two cases?

We pick up at the very bottom of 62b and turn to the issue of *shelo l'ochlav* — the invalidity created when one *shechts* the *korban Pesach* with the intention of offering it for the sake of people who are owners yet are unable to eat. This is a *pasul* unique to the *korban Pesach*, and it applies specifically at the moment of *shechita*.

### Arelim v. Mulim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא קָסָבְרִי אֲחֵרִים אֵינָהּ לִשְׁחִיטָה אֶלָּא בַּסּוֹף, וְכִדְרָבָא. דְּאָמַר רָבָא: עֲדַיִין הִיא מַחֲלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let us say that *Aḥerim* hold that slaughter is not** legally significant **until the end,** meaning that the slaughter is considered to take place only at the end of the rite and not during the time it takes to perform the act of slaughter, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava** in a different context. **As Rava said: It is still a dispute,** meaning that the disagreement whether one is held accountable only for his first expression or also for the conclusion of his statement applies even when it is one's clear intention that the status he wishes to confer should apply only at a particular time, e.g., if one says that an animal should be consecrated as a burnt-offering until midday and from then on as a peace-offering (Rabbeinu Ḥananel). The opinion of Rabbi Meir, referred to here as *Aḥerim*, is that one is held accountable only for his first expression.

The *gemara* resumes four lines from the bottom of 62b with a *tanya*. The *baraita* states in the name of *Acherim* — understood to be Rabbi Meir — that if one first mentions *mulim* (circumcised) and then *arelim* (uncircumcised), the *shechita* is *kasher*. But if one says *arelim* before *mulim*, it is *pasul*. The *gemara* sets out to unpack this ruling.

### Mixed Thought Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִילְכָּךְ: הִקְדִּים מוּלִין לַעֲרֵלִים — מוּלִין חָיְילִי, עֲרֵלִים לָא חָיְילִי. הִקְדִּים עֲרֵלִים לְמוּלִין — עֲרֵלִים חָיְילִי, מוּלִין לָא חָיְילִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** based on these two assumptions, if **one put the circumcised people before the uncircumcised people,** his statement with regard to **circumcised people applies** but his statement with regard to **uncircumcised people does not apply.** If the slaughter is legally considered to take effect in a single instant, and if one said that his intention is for circumcised people at that moment, his statement takes effect. In the reverse case, if **one put the uncircumcised people before the circumcised people,** his statement with regard to **uncircumcised people applies,** but his statement with regard to **circumcised people does not apply.**

The *gemara* asks: *mai shna mulim l'arelim de-kasher* — why is it valid if one says *mulim* and *arelim*? Because, as the preceding discussion established, *shelo l'ochlav* is only invalid when the entire intended group is an ineligible group. A mixed group — partly *mulim*, partly *arelim* — does not constitute that kind of fully invalid designation. But that reasoning applies equally when the words are said in reverse order. Whether one says *arelim l'mulim* or *mulim l'arelim*, one is still referring to a mixed group. So why does the order matter? The *gemara* turns to the top of 63a to resolve this.

### Yeshna L'Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: לָא, לְעוֹלָם קָסָבְרִי אֲחֵרִים יֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׁחִיטָה מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁגָּמַר בְּלִבּוֹ לְתַרְוַויְיהוּ, בֵּין לְמוּלִין בֵּין לַעֲרֵלִים, וְהוֹצִיא בְּפִיו ״לַעֲרֵלִים״, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לוֹמַר ״לְמוּלִין״ עַד שֶׁנִּגְמְרָה שְׁחִיטָה בַּ״עֲרֵלִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: No,** we should not say this. **Actually, *Aḥerim* hold that slaughter is** legally significant **from beginning to end, and with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where one decided in his mind** to slaughter the offering **for both of them, both circumcised and uncircumcised people, and he verbally expressed** his intention with the phrase: **For uncircumcised people, but did not have a chance to say: For circumcised people, before the slaughter was** already **finished** as he was saying: **For uncircumcised people.**

The *gemara* proposes: *leima kasavri Acherim — eina lishchita ela besof*, and invokes a ruling of Rava, noting that it remains a standing dispute. An enormous amount is packed into these few words, as they reference two distinct and expansive *sugyot* in *Shas*.

The first is the question of *yeshna lishchita mitchila v'ad sof* versus *eina lishchita ela l'besof* — whether the act of *shechita* is considered to be performed continuously throughout the entire cutting process, or whether it is treated as taking legal effect only at its conclusion. The second is the debate of *tchilas devarav adam nitpas* versus *besof devarav adam nitpas* — whether, when a person makes a statement whose components are in tension, we hold him to the first part he uttered or the last.

### Process vs. Moment

The question of *yeshna lishchita mitchila v'ad sof* is fundamentally about whether *shechita* should be understood as a continuous process or as a discrete point in time. It is not simply a question of identifying at which precise instant the act legally occurs — everyone agrees that the cut must be completed — but rather whether the entire duration of the cutting constitutes the act, or whether the act is defined solely by its final moment, with that final moment then incorporating everything that preceded it.

This question has wide application across the laws of *korbanot*. An invalidating *machshava* — whether *shelo lishmah*, *shelo l'shem ba'alim*, or *shelo l'ochlav* — does not become irrelevant simply because one stops thinking it at the precise last instant. The question is rather: when did that thought take legal effect? If *shechita* is a process, then a thought present at any point during the cutting took effect during the act of *shechita* itself. If *shechita* is only at its end, then the question becomes what thoughts are operative at that final moment, which then absorbs all that preceded.

### Timeline Model

To make this concrete: imagine the *shechita* begins at one point and concludes when both the trachea and esophagus have been cut. If *yeshno lishchita mitchila v'ad sof* — that the entire process constitutes *shechita* — then at some point during the cutting one might have thought *mulim*, and a split second later *arelim*, or vice versa. Since both thoughts are present during the act of *shechita*, the designation is mixed, and a mixed designation is *kasher*. On this view, both orderings should be equally valid.

If, on the other hand, *eino lishchita ela basof* — only the final moment counts — then one would think that both thoughts, having been expressed sequentially, are effectively absorbed into that single final moment and are simultaneously operative. This should also produce a mixed designation, which would also be *kasher*. So on either view, the order should not matter. What changes everything is the introduction of the second *sugya*.

### Rav Papa's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְהָא פְּלִיגִי, דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: לָא בָּעֵינַן פִּיו וְלִבּוֹ שָׁוִים. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי בָּעֵינַן פִּיו וְלִבּוֹ שָׁוִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is with regard to this** point **that they disagree: Rabbi Meir,** who is *Aḥerim*, **holds that we do not require that one's mouth and heart be the same;** what is legally significant is his verbal expression. Since he said: For uncircumcised people, he has disqualified the offering. **And the Rabbis hold that we require that his mouth and heart be the same.** Since he wanted to express his intent for both circumcised and uncircumcised people, he has not disqualified the offering.

If *eino lishchita ela basof* — if everything takes legal effect at the end — then the two statements of *arelim* and *mulim*, although uttered sequentially, are combined into a single moment of effect. This is where the debate of *tchilas devarav adam nitpas* becomes operative. Rabbi Yosi holds *batchilat devarav adam nitpas* — one is bound by the first part of a compound or seemingly contradictory statement. If both *arelim* and *mulim* take effect simultaneously at the conclusion, it is as though they were said at once, and according to that principle, only the first part would govern.

Rava addresses a case where this principle is applied not retroactively but prospectively — if one says "I want this animal to be an *olah-shelamim* starting at noon." Here one cannot argue that the person first said *olah* and then changed their mind, since both are being designated for a future moment. Abaye holds that Rabbi Yosi would not apply *tchilas devarav* in such a case, since there is no sequence of reconsideration. Rabbah, however, says *adayin hi machloket* — the debate persists even then. Even when the two designations are intended to take effect together at a later point, Rabbi Yosi would still hold that only the first governs.

### Tchilas Devarav

The *gemara* now reads this into the case of *arelim* and *mulim*. The *shochet* is performing the cut and intends to say *l'mulim v'l'arelim*, but the *shechita* takes effect at the final moment, meaning both statements are combined into that single point. Rabbah says *adayin hi machloket*, meaning: even in such a case, the debate about *tchilas devarav adam nitpas* still applies. According to the position that one is bound by the first thing said, the entire designation reduces to whichever word came first. Hence the *gemara*'s conclusion follows:

*Hilcha hikdim mulim l'arelim* — if *mulim* was said first, *mulim chaili arelim lo chaili*: only *mulim* takes legal effect at that final moment, not *arelim*, and the *shechita* is valid. *Heicha d'amar arelim l'mulim* — if *arelim* was said first, *arelim chaili mulim lo chaili*: only *arelim* takes effect, and it is *pasul*.

This is then stated explicitly in the text: *hilcha hikdim mulim l'arelim* — if *mulim* was said first — *mulim chaili arelim lo chaili*, and it is valid. Reversed, *arelim chaili mulim lo chaili*, and it is invalid. The key word *chaili* means the designation *chal* — takes legal effect.

Now Rabbah (some have the *girsa* of Rava; the *gemara* will address this) responds: *le'olam kasavar Acharin yeshna lishchita mit'tchila v'ad sof*. One need not invoke the *eino lishchita ela basof* position at all. Even if we say *shechita* is a continuous process throughout, there is still a reason the order matters — but for a completely different reason.

### Mulim First Case

Rabbah's answer redirects the analysis entirely. *Dehacha b'mai askinan* — the case under discussion is one where the *shochet* intended in his mind to designate the *korban* for both *mulim* and *arelim*, but what he actually articulated during the *shechita* was only one of them — and the second word came out only after the *shechita* was complete.

If he said *mulim* first, then while the *shechita* was ongoing, he had said *l'mulim*, and he finished the *shechita* before he could complete the phrase *v'l'arelim*. At the critical moment of *shechita*, what was said was *l'mulim*, and the *arelim* was added only afterward. The designation at the time of the act was therefore for *mulim*, which is valid.

### Arelim First Case

Conversely, if he said *arelim* first and then completed the *shechita* before getting to *mulim*, then at the critical moment of the *shechita* only *arelim* had been articulated. The subsequent utterance of *mulim* does not help, since it came after the act was already complete. What matters is what was said during the *shechita*, and what was said was only *arelim*, which is *pasul*.

This is the ruling as the *gemara* states it: *vegamar belivo l'travaiyu* — in his mind he intended both — *vehotzi b'piv l'arelim* — but what he said aloud was *arelim*, *velo hispik lomar l'mulim ad shenigmara shchita* — and he did not manage to say *mulim* before the *shechita* was complete.

### Piv v'Libo Shavin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר לָא בָּעֵינַן פִּיו וְלִבּוֹ שָׁוִין? וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַמִּתְכַּוֵּין לוֹמַר ״תְּרוּמָה״, וְאָמַר ״מַעֲשֵׂר״; ״מַעֲשֵׂר״, וְאָמַר ״תְּרוּמָה״. אוֹ: ״שֶׁאֵינִי נִכְנָס לְבַיִת זֶה״, וְאָמַר ״לָזֶה״; ״שֶׁאֵינִי נֶהֱנֶה לָזֶה״, וְאָמַר ״לָזֶה״ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ פִּיו וְלִבּוֹ שָׁוִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: **But does Rabbi Meir hold that we require that his mouth and heart be the same?** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** based on a mishna in tractate *Terumot* that states: With regard to **one who intended to say** that the produce he has designated should be ***teruma*, but he** mistakenly **said** the word **tithe;** or he intended to say **tithe but** mistakenly **said *teruma*;** or he intended to vow: **I will not enter this house, but** mistakenly **said: That** house, i.e., he mistakenly referred to a different house; or he intended to vow: **I will not derive benefit from this person, but he said: From that** person, i.e., he mistakenly referred to someone else; **he has not said anything until his mouth and heart are the same.** This is an unattributed mishna, and unattributed *mishnayot* are presumed to be authored by Rabbi Meir.

The *gemara* then raises a fundamental objection from a different direction. If the reason we dismiss the second half of the statement is that it came too late, the implicit assumption is that we go by what was said — and specifically what was said at the critical moment — even if it does not reflect what was in the speaker's mind. After all, in his mind he was thinking of both *mulim* and *arelim* throughout. But one might ask: does Rabbi Meir really hold that one's speech need not reflect one's inner intention?

The *gemara* challenges this from the following ruling: if a person intended to say *terumah* and accidentally said *maaser*, or intended to make a *neder* about one house but misspoke and said another — *lo amar klum*, the statement has no effect, *ad sheyehei piv uleevo shavin* — until one's mouth and heart are aligned. This appears to be a universal principle. Why then would Rabbi Meir not require *piv v'libo shavin* here?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רֵישָׁא, דְּאָמַר ״סִימָן רִאשׁוֹן לְמוּלִין, וְסִימָן שֵׁנִי אַף לַעֲרֵלִים״ — דִּבְסִימָן שֵׁנִי נָמֵי פְּתִיכִי בֵּיהּ מוּלִין. סֵיפָא, דְּאָמַר ״סִימָן רִאשׁוֹן לַעֲרֵלִים, סִימָן שֵׁנִי לְמוּלִים״ — דִּבְסִימָן רִאשׁוֹן הָא לָא פְּתִיכִי בֵּיהּ מוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** the following explanation: We are dealing with a case where the person expressed two different intentions within the act of slaughter itself, as valid slaughter involves cutting most of both the windpipe and the esophagus of the animal. **The first clause** is referring to a case where **he said:** I cut **the first** organ **for circumcised people and the second** organ **even for uncircumcised people, so that in the second** organ **even circumcised people are included.** Consequently, he had circumcised people in mind at each stage of the slaughtering. **The latter clause** is referring to a case where **he said:** I cut **the first** organ **for uncircumcised people** and **the second** organ **for circumcised people, so that in the first** organ **circumcised people are not included,** and his intent during that stage is solely for uncircumcised people.

The *gemara* answers: *deRabbi Meir savar lo ba'inan piv v'libo shavin* — Rabbi Meir holds that we do not require the mouth to match the heart. *Rabbanan savri ba'inan piv v'libo shavin* — the Rabbis hold that we do. The Rabbis would therefore say: since the *shochet* was thinking of *mulim* and *arelim* as a mixed group throughout, and his speech at the critical moment was only a partial expression of that full intention and did not match what he was thinking in its entirety, we cannot reduce his designation to just the first word.

### Articulated Thought

This leads to a conclusion of broad importance. We have been speaking throughout this *sugya* and the preceding ones as though the *psulei machshava* of *shelo lishmah*, *shelo l'shem ba'alim*, and *shelo l'ochlav* are purely about what one thinks. Here the *gemara* makes clear that the operative category is what one articulates. Rashi states explicitly — commenting on *ve-hacha bemai askinan* — that *kol machshava de-kodshim motzi befiv*: whenever the *gemara* speaks of an invalid *machshava* by *kodshim*, it means an expressed, articulated thought — not a silent intention held in the mind alone.

Tosafot makes the same point even more directly. From the *gemara*'s discussion here, Tosafot derives that what is called *machshava* throughout the *sugya* in all cases of *pigul* and *shelo lishmah* does not mean pure mental intention — *eino pigul ad she-yotzi befiv* — it is not *pigul* unless one has expressed it verbally.

The reason Tosafot gives for distinguishing this from the principle of *devarim shebalev einam devarim* is significant. The principle that unexpressed thoughts have no legal force applies when a person harbored a silent condition or intention that they simply never stated — in that case, since you did not say it, we cannot give it legal effect. But here, the *shochet* did speak. The problem is not that he was silent about part of his intention but that he misspoke, or that the second part of what he was saying came out too late. When speech does not reflect intent because of error or mistiming, we can set aside the spoken word. When speech reflects a deliberate silent reservation one chose not to articulate, we cannot import that reservation.

### Two Siman Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: מְפַגְּלִין בַּחֲצִי מַתִּיר. וְרַבָּנַן לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּאָמְרִי: אֵין מְפַגְּלִין בַּחֲצִי מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Meir follows** his own line of **reasoning, as he says** that ***piggul* status can be conferred** upon an offering **at half of what renders it permitted.** In other words, *piggul* status applies not only when one has a disqualifying intent during the entire act that renders the animal permitted, e.g., while slaughtering both the windpipe and the esophagus, but even if one has the disqualifying intent during half of the act that renders the animal permitted, e.g., while slaughtering one of those two organs. **And the Rabbis** also **follow** their regular line of **reasoning, as they say** that ***piggul* status cannot be conferred at half of what permits** it. Since he expressed his intent for both uncircumcised and circumcised people over the course of the entire act of slaughter, the offering is not disqualified.

Abaye offers a different resolution entirely. *Reisha d'amar siman rishon lemulin vesiman sheini af lareilin* — the case where the order is *mulim* first is one where the *shochet* said, while cutting the first *siman* (the trachea), that this is for *mulim*, and when cutting the second *siman* (the esophagus), he added that it is also for *arelim*. Since the *arelim* designation was attached to the second *siman* and came after the *mulim* had already been stated, the *mulim* is embedded in the context of the second *siman* as well — *nami ptichi be-mulin*, it opens into the *mulim* — and the thought is therefore mixed throughout.

In the reverse case — *siman rishon lareilin siman sheini lemulin* — he designated the first *siman* for *arelim* alone. Even though he later said *lemulin* during the second *siman*, the first *siman* stood entirely on its own as a designation for *arelim*, with no admixture of *mulim* at that point. That isolated designation of *arelim* for the first *siman* creates a *psul machshava* that cannot be remedied by what was added later.

### Mefalgin Bachatzi

Abaye's resolution leads the *gemara* to an explicit connection: *veRabbi Meir letaimei de-amar mefalgin be-chatzi matir, verabbanan letaimei de-amri ein mefalgin be-chatzi matir*. Can one produce *pigul* — or any *psul machshava* — by means of an invalid thought applied to only half of the *matir*, i.e., only one of the two *simanim*? Rabbi Meir holds *mefalgin be-chatzi matir*: an invalid thought attaching to half the *matir* is sufficient to create the *pasul*. Therefore, when one says *arelim* during the first *siman* alone, that creates a *psul* independent of what was said during the second. The Rabbis hold *ein mefalgin be-chatzi matir*: you cannot generate an invalid designation from only half the *matir*, so we assess the thought only when both *simanim* are complete — at which point the designation is mixed, and therefore *kasher*.

This debate of *mefalgin be-chatzi matir* is larger than the case at hand. It applies, for example, to a *mincha* where both the *kometz* and the *levonah* must be offered, and one has an invalid thought by only one of them. The question of whether one can create *pigul* with half the *matir* is a broad *sugya* in *Shas*, and here the *gemara* roots the *arelim-mulim* dispute directly within it. There is also a conceptual relationship to the question of *yeshna lishchita mitchila v'ad sof*, since treating *shechita* as two discrete acts — each of the two *simanim* — presupposes that we are willing to analyze each act separately, which is itself the underlying logic of *yeshna lishchita*.

## Lo Tishchat Al Chametz

### Intro: Chametz Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַפֶּסַח עַל הֶחָמֵץ — עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַתָּמִיד. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַפֶּסַח בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר לִשְׁמוֹ — חַיָּיב, וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ — פָּטוּר. וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים, בֵּין לִשְׁמָן וּבֵין שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who slaughters the Paschal lamb with leavened bread** still in his possession **violates a negative commandment,** as the Torah states: "You shall not offer the blood of My sacrifice with leavened bread; neither shall the sacrifice of the festival of Passover be left until the morning" (Exodus 34:25). **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** one who slaughters **the daily** afternoon **offering** on Passover eve with leaven in his possession violates the commandment. **Rabbi Shimon says:** One who slaughters **the Paschal lamb on the fourteenth** of Nisan **for its** own **purpose** with leaven in his possession **is liable;** but if he slaughtered it **for a different purpose he is exempt. And** for **all other offerings** that one slaughters on Passover eve, when owning leaven is prohibited, **whether** he slaughtered them **for their** own **purpose or** he slaughtered them **for a different purpose, he is exempt.**

We now shift to a distinct but related topic — the prohibition of *lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi*, the prohibition against *shechting* the *Pesach* while *chametz* is present. The *pasuk* does not say *Pesach* explicitly, but says *dam zivchi* — the blood of my sacrifices. As we will see, this generates significant debate about the scope of the prohibition and about who must own the *chametz* for the violation to apply.

### Mishna & R. Yehuda

The *mishna* opens: *hashochet et hapesach al hachametz over belo taaseh* — one who *shechts* the *korban Pesach* while *chametz* is present transgresses a negative commandment. Rabbi Yehuda adds: *af hatamid* — even the afternoon *tamid* on *erev Pesach*. He understands the prohibition to extend beyond the *korban Pesach* itself, applying to other *korbanot* offered during the *zman shechitat chametz* as well.

### R. Shimon Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַמּוֹעֵד, לִשְׁמוֹ — פָּטוּר. שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ — חַיָּיב. וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים, בֵּין לִשְׁמָן בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן — חַיָּיב. חוּץ מִן הַחַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And during the festival** of Passover, if one slaughtered the Paschal lamb **for its** own **purpose he is exempt.** Since a Paschal lamb that is slaughtered for its own purpose at an improper time is disqualified, it is not an offering at all and there is no violation of the commandment: "You shall not offer the blood of My sacrifice with leavened bread." However, if he slaughtered it **for a different purpose** and thereby validated the sacrifice as a peace-offering, **he is liable** for having sacrificed it with leaven in his possession. **And** for **all other offerings** that one slaughters on Passover, when it is prohibited to slaughter with leaven in one's possession, **whether** he slaughtered them **for their** own **purpose** or he slaughtered them **for a different purpose, he is liable.** This is **with the exception of a sin-offering that he slaughtered for a different purpose** with leaven in his possession. Unlike other offerings, a sin-offering is disqualified if it is slaughtered for a different purpose, and therefore one does not violate the prohibition of "You shall not offer the blood of My sacrifice with leavened bread."

Rabbi Shimon's position introduces a major new principle. *Hapesach bearba asar lishmo* — if one *shechts* the *korban Pesach* on the fourteenth properly in its name, and one has *chametz*, *chayav* — one has transgressed. *Veshelo lishmo* — if the *shechita* was done *shelo lishmo*, *patur* — one is exempt. The reason, according to Rabbi Shimon, is the principle of *shchita she'eina reu'ya lo kriya shchita* — a *shechita* that did not achieve the effect of rendering the meat permissible or of constituting a valid *korban* is not called an act of *shechita* at all. It is as though the animal was simply stabbed. Since *shelo lishmo* rendered the *Pesach* invalid, there was no act of *shechita* in the relevant sense, and therefore the prohibition of *lo tishchat* was not violated.

### Pesach Still Kosher

A striking ruling emerges from the Tosafot Riva: *depesach kashar* — the *korban Pesach* itself remains *kasher* even if one transgressed the prohibition of *lo tishchat al chametz*. The *Tosefta* in the *Mechilta* states explicitly: *hashochet et hapesach al hachametz be-yud-daled over belo taaseh, vehapesach atzmo kashar, veyotzei bo yedei chovato bapesach* — one violates the *lav* but fulfills the *mitzvah* of eating the *korban Pesach*. The transgression of *lo tishchat al chametz* does not invalidate the *Pesach*. This is distinct from the *shelo lishmo* case, which addresses the validity of the *korban*, not the *lo tishchat* prohibition.

### Chattat Exception

The continuation of the mishna addresses the *chag* — the intermediate days of Passover. This further complicates the picture. During the *chag*, a *Pesach* that was slaughtered *lishmo* on those days (when the proper time has passed) is invalid as a *Pesach*, and therefore *patur* — one is not liable for the prohibition. But if it was slaughtered *shelo lishmo* — rendering it valid as a peace-offering — one is *chayav*. All other offerings brought during the *chag* are presumed *kasher* regardless of intent, and therefore *chayav*, with one exception: the *chatat sheshachto shelo lishmah*, which cannot be valid for any other purpose and therefore remains invalid, and thus *patur*.

### Invalid Shechita Logic

The logic driving all of Rabbi Shimon's exemptions is consistent: *shechita she'eina reu'ya lo kriya shchita*. Wherever the *shechita* did not produce a valid *korban*, it does not legally qualify as an act of *shechita*, and the prohibition of *lo tishchat* does not apply. Where the *shechita* produced a valid *korban* — even if it was not the intended *korban Pesach* — the prohibition does apply. This principle is at work throughout Rabbi Shimon's position and will recur in subsequent analysis.

### Ownership Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיְּהֶא הֶחָמֵץ לַשּׁוֹחֵט אוֹ לַזּוֹרֵק אוֹ לְאֶחָד מִבְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה, וְעַד שֶׁיְּהֵא עִמּוֹ בָּעֲזָרָה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין עִמּוֹ בַּעֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: One is never liable** for having violated the commandment: "You shall not offer the blood of My sacrifice with leavened bread" **unless the leavened bread belongs to the one who slaughters** the Paschal lamb, **or to the one who sprinkles** its blood, **or to one of the members of the group;** and he is liable **only if** the leaven **is with him in the Temple courtyard** itself. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** He is liable **even if** the leaven **is not with him in the Temple courtyard.**

The *gemara* turns to the *amora'im* to address the question of whose *chametz* is relevant. Reish Lakish states: one does not transgress the prohibition unless the *chametz* belongs either to the *shochet* or to the *zorek* — the one who performs the sprinkling of the blood — or to one of the members of the *chaburah*, the group of owners who will eat the *korban*. This is a critical structural distinction: the *shochet* is the one who transgresses, but the relevant *chametz* need not be his own — it suffices that any member of the *chaburah* owns *chametz*. Conversely, owning *chametz* does not in itself cause a member of the *chaburah* to transgress the *lo tishchat* prohibition. Only the one performing the *shechita* or the *zerika* can transgress, and only if someone within the relevant circle owns *chametz*. Additionally, the *chametz* must be *im'o ba'azarah* — present with him in the *azarah*. This last requirement flows from the language of *al chametz*, which Reish Lakish understands to require physical proximity.

That the *zorek* is included at all is not obvious from the *pasuk*, which says *lo tishchat*. Rashi refers to a *Mechilta* from which we learn that *zrikat hadam* is also included, derived from a related *pasuk* of *vedam zvachecha yishapech*.

### Al Samuch Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? אִילֵּימָא בְּ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ סָבַר: ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: לָא בָּעֵינַן ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ. וְהָא אִיפְּלִגוּ בַּהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא!
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to what** principle do **they disagree? If you say** that **they disagree** with regard to whether the expression **"with"** indicates **next to,** namely, **that Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish holds that "with"** always indicates **next to,** and therefore "You shall not offer the blood of My sacrifice with leavened bread" means that the leaven must not be next to the one slaughtering the sacrifice, in the Temple courtyard itself, **and Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that** when the verse says **"with," we do not require** that the leaven be **next to** the slaughterer in order to transgress, then this is difficult, because **they have already disagreed about this once** before.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees with Reish Lakish's requirement of physical proximity: *af al pi she'eino imo ba'azarah* — even if the *chametz* is not present in the *azarah*, the prohibition applies. The *gemara* asks: *bemai kamiflagi* — what exactly do they dispute? The most natural reading is that they disagree about whether the word *al* in *lo tishchat al chametz* requires physical proximity — *al besamuch* — or merely conceptual connection. Reish Lakish holds *al besamuch*; Rabbi Yochanan holds *lo beinan al besamuch*.

The *gemara* notes that this same pair has apparently debated this question in another context, and wonders whether their dispute here is the same one restated, or whether there is an additional dimension — specifically, the question of *hatra'at safeik*, a doubtful warning. If the *chametz* is not in the *azarah*, the *kohein* performing the *shechita* cannot see it and may well doubt that the warning being given — do not *shecht*, one of the owners has *chametz* — is accurate. Reish Lakish holds, in another context, that *hatra'at safeik lav shma hatraa* — a doubtful warning is not a valid warning, and without a valid warning one cannot receive *malkot*. That would provide an independent reason for exemption separate from any question about *al besamuch*.

### Toda Bread Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: הַשּׁוֹחֵט תּוֹדָה לִפְנִים וְלַחְמָהּ חוּץ לַחוֹמָה — לֹא קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **one who slaughters a thanks-offering inside** the Temple courtyard while **its bread,** namely the forty loaves that are brought together with the offering, **is outside the wall, the bread has not become sanctified,** as the verse states: "And he shall offer with the thanks-offering unleavened cakes." (Leviticus 7:12).

The *gemara* identifies the prior context in which Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish already argued about *al besamuch*: *hashochet todah lifnim velachmah lachutz* — one who *shechts* a *korban toda* inside the *azarah* while the accompanying bread is outside. The *toda* is accompanied by bread, and the *pasuk* connects them with the word *al* — *al zevach hatoda* — implying that the *shechita* of the *toda* sanctifies the bread when they are properly connected. If the bread is outside the *azarah* when the *toda* is *shechted*, does the bread become sanctified?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חוּץ לַחוֹמָה? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חוּץ לְחוֹמַת בֵּית פָּאגֵי, אֲבָל חוּץ לְחוֹמַת הָעֲזָרָה — קָדֵישׁ, וְלָא בָּעֵינַן ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ חוּץ לְחוֹמַת עֲזָרָה לָא קָדֵישׁ. אַלְמָא בָּעֵינַן ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** A question was raised with regard to this mishna: **What** is the meaning of the phrase **outside the wall? Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It means **outside the wall of Beit Pagei,** the outermost wall around Jerusalem, **but** if the bread was merely **outside the wall of the Temple courtyard, it has been sanctified, as we do not require** that the bread, described as **"with"** the offering, be **next to** it in order to be sanctified. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** disagreed and **said: Even** if the bread was merely **outside the wall of the Temple courtyard, it has not been sanctified. Apparently,** he holds that **we require** that the bread described as **"with"** the offering be **next to** it in order to be sanctified. Since Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish have already disputed this issue, they presumably did not repeat this same dispute in other contexts.

Rabbi Yochanan holds: *chutz lachoma Beit Pagi* means outside the wall of Jerusalem — if the bread is outside Jerusalem, it is not sanctified, but if it is merely outside the *azarah* but still within Jerusalem, it is sanctified. *Al besamuch* is not required for the bread. Reish Lakish holds: *afilu chutz lechomat azara lo kadish* — even one step outside the *azarah* and the bread is not sanctified. *Al besamuch* is required.

### Issura v. Kedushat Lechem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לְעוֹלָם בְּ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אִיפְּלִיגוּ לְעִנְיַן חָמֵץ, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בְּהָהוּא הוּא דְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּלָא בָּעֵינַן ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ — מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיסּוּר הוּא, וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ — אִיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They say** in answer to this: **Actually,** we can explain that **they disagree about** whether **"with"** indicates **next to, and it is necessary** to teach that they disagree about the case involving the leaven in addition to the case of the loaves of the thanks-offering, because the cases are not entirely comparable. **As, if they disagreed** only **with regard to leaven, I would have said** that **it is** only **with regard to that** case **that Rabbi Yoḥanan said that** when the verse says **"with," we do not require** that the slaughterer have the leaven **next to** him in order to transgress, **because it is a prohibition, and wherever it is, it is.** On the eve of Passover in the afternoon one is prohibited to possess leaven anywhere.

The *gemara* concludes that both disputes are necessary, despite their apparent overlap. *U-tzricha* — both cases must be stated, because each one alone might have led to a different inference about the other.

Had the dispute been stated only in the context of *chametz*, one might have thought: Rabbi Yochanan only says *lo beinan al besamuch* there because it involves a *transgression* — wherever the *chametz* is and however it is connected, the *issur* is present in full. But when it comes to *sanctifying the bread*, one might have supposed that even Rabbi Yochanan agrees with Reish Lakish that the bread must be within the *azarah*, since the *shechita*'s power to sanctify it is analogous to the power of a *kli shareit* — and a *kli shareit* can only sanctify what is placed within it. Rashi understands the *azarah* itself as functioning in a manner analogous to a *kli shareit*: what is within it is amenable to sanctification, and what is outside is not. Tosafot makes a more technical version of this point: a *kli shareit* can only sanctify things placed into it when the *kli shareit* itself is in the *azarah*; similarly, the sanctifying power of the *shechita* extends only to what is present within the *azarah*. Therefore, had the dispute appeared only by *chametz*, we might have said Rabbi Yochanan would agree to Reish Lakish for the bread case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן מִקְדָּשׁ לֶחֶם — לָא קָדֵישׁ אֶלָּא בִּפְנִים, אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ דְּבָעֵינַן ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ, דְּאִי אִיתֵיהּ גַּוַּאי — קָדֵישׁ, אִי לָא — לָא קָדֵישׁ, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַכְּלֵי שָׁרֵת. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **However, with regard to sanctification of the bread, it becomes sanctified only inside** the Temple courtyard. Consequently, **say that** in that case Rabbi Yochanan **concedes to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish that we require** that the bread described as **"with"** the offering be **next to** it. Therefore, **if it is inside, it is sanctified; and if not, it is not sanctified.** This is **just as it is** with regard to **a vessel used in the** Temple **service,** which sanctifies a meal-offering only when the meal is inside it and not when the meal is outside of it. Therefore, **it is necessary** to teach that the dispute applies in both cases.

Conversely, had the dispute appeared only by the bread, one might have thought: Reish Lakish insists on *al besamuch* there because sanctification requires physical proximity — *de-i ika gavai kadish, i lav lo kadish* — but for *chametz*, which is about a prohibition and not about conferring sanctity, even Reish Lakish might agree with Rabbi Yochanan that physical proximity is not required. Therefore, both cases must be stated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן לְעִנְיַן מִקְדָּשׁ לֶחֶם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בְּהָךְ קָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ דְּבָעֵינַן ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ, דְּאִי אִיתֵיהּ גַּוַּאי — קָדֵישׁ, אִי לָא — לָא קָדֵישׁ, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן חָמֵץ מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּלָא בָּעֵינַן ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ, דְּאִיסּוּרָא הוּא, וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ — אִיתֵיהּ, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had we been taught** the dispute only **with regard to sanctification of the bread, I would have said that** only **in this** case **did Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish say that we require** that the bread described as **"with"** the sacrifice be **next to it,** such **that if it is inside** the Temple courtyard, **it is sanctified; and if not, it is not sanctified. But with regard to leavened bread, he concedes to Rabbi Yoḥanan that** when the verse says **"with," we do not require** that the slaughterer have the leaven **next to** him in order to transgress, **for it is a prohibition and wherever it is, it is.** Therefore, **it is necessary** to say that they argued in both cases.

It is also worth noting what all opinions agree upon: physical proximity alone, even if necessary according to Reish Lakish, is never sufficient. The *chametz* must be owned by the *shochet*, the *zorek*, or a member of the *chaburah*. Stranger's *chametz* in the *azarah* creates no liability whatsoever.

### Ownership Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא מֵרַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֵין לוֹ לַשּׁוֹחֵט, וְיֵשׁ לוֹ לְאֶחָד מִבְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Oshaya asked Rabbi Ami** the following question: If **the slaughterer does not have** leaven in his possession **but one of the members of the group does have, what is** the *halakha*?

Rav Oshaya posed a question to Rabbi Ami: *ein lo la-shochet ve-yesh lo le-achad mi-benei ha-chaburah mahu* — what if the *shochet* himself has no *chametz*, but one of the members of the *chaburah* does? Is the *shochet* liable?

### Lo Yalin Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי כְּתִיב ״לֹא תִשְׁחַט עַל חֲמֵצְךָ״? ״לֹא תִשְׁחַט עַל חָמֵץ״ כְּתִיב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ לְאֶחָד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם נָמֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָמַר קְרָא ״לֹא תִשְׁחַט ... וְלֹא יָלִין״ — ״לֹא תִשְׁחַט עַל חָמֵץ״ — הָנָךְ דְּקָיְימִי עֲלֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא יָלִין״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ami **said to him: Is it written: You shall not offer** the blood of My sacrifice **with your leavened bread,** meaning you may not slaughter the Paschal lamb with the leaven of the slaughterer? **It is written: "You shall not offer with leavened bread"** (Exodus 34:25), meaning you may not slaughter it with anyone's leaven and not necessarily leaven that belongs to the slaughterer. Rav Oshaya **said to** Rav Ashi: **If so, even if someone at the end of the world** has leaven it should **also** be a violation. Since there is no limitation to this prohibition, it should apply even if the leaven belongs to someone who is not associated in any way with this Paschal lamb. Rav Ashi **said to him** that **the verse says: "You shall not offer,"** and the end of the verse states: **"Neither shall** the sacrifice of the festival of Passover **be left** until the morning." The verse equates the two prohibitions, from which the following may be derived: **"You shall not offer with leavened bread"** applies to **those who are governed** by the prohibition of **"neither shall be left over,"** namely, the members of the group that registered for this Paschal lamb. People who are not part of this group are not obligated to ensure that the Paschal lamb does not remain until the morning; similarly, they are not taken into account with regard to the prohibition against sacrificing the offering while in possession of leaven.

Rabbi Ami responded: it does not say *lo tishchat al chamtzecha* — do not *shecht* on your *chametz*. The *pasuk* says *lo tishchat al chametz* — with *chametz*, meaning *chametz* that is in some relevant sense connected. The connection is established not by ownership specifically, but by the juxtaposition within the *pasuk* itself: *lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi velo yalin chelev chagi ad boker*. The prohibition of *lo tishchat* is linked to *lo yalin* — the prohibition against leaving the fat unburned overnight. Those who are subject to the obligation of *lo yalin* with respect to this *korban* — i.e., those whose connection to the *korban* is such that its overnight neglect would concern them — are the ones whose ownership of *chametz* renders the *shochet* liable. This definitively includes the members of the *chaburah*.

### Rav Papa Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הִילְכָּךְ כֹּהֵן הַמַּקְטִיר אֶת הַחֵלֶב — עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, הוֹאִיל וְיֶשְׁנוֹ בִּכְלַל הֲלָנַת אֵמוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Therefore,** based on this reason, **the priest who burns the fats** of the Paschal lamb **transgresses the negative commandment** of "You shall not offer" if he has leaven in his possession. **Since he is included in** the prohibition of **leaving over sacrificial parts** of the offering, he is also included in the prohibition against sacrificing the offering with leaven, although he is not one of the people who will eat this Paschal lamb.

Rav Papa draws an additional inference. If the category of those subject to *lo yalin* is what determines whose *chametz* is relevant, then: *kohein hamaktir et hachelev* — a *kohein* who burns the fats of the *korban Pesach* — *over belo taaseh* — transgresses the *lav* of *lo tishchat* — *hoil veyeshno bichlal halanat imurin* — because he is within the category of those subject to *halanat imurin*, the prohibition against leaving the *eimurin* unburned overnight.

This is a significant analytical leap. Until this point, the *lo yalin* connection was used to identify whose *chametz* ownership is relevant for the *shochet's* liability. Rav Papa now uses it to create liability for an entirely new act — *haktarat imurin* — that is nowhere mentioned in the *pasuk*. We already knew from Reish Lakish that *zerika* is also an act that triggers liability, derived from a separate *pasuk*. Rav Papa adds *haktarat imurin* on the basis of the *kohein*'s relationship to *lo yalin*, reasoning that one subject to *lo yalin* who performs an *avoda* on this *korban* is brought within the scope of the prohibition.

### Braita: Who Transgresses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַפֶּסַח עַל הֶחָמֵץ — עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא לַשּׁוֹחֵט אוֹ לַזּוֹרֵק אוֹ לְאֶחָד מִבְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה. הָיָה לְאֶחָד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם — אֵין זָקוּק לוֹ. וְאֶחָד הַשּׁוֹחֵט וְאֶחָד הַזּוֹרֵק וְאֶחָד הַמַּקְטִיר — חַיָּיב. אֲבָל הַמּוֹלֵק אֶת הָעוֹף בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר — אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out that **it was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Pappa: One who slaughters the Paschal lamb with leavened bread** in his possession **transgresses a negative commandment. When? When** the leaven belongs **to the slaughterer, or to the one who sprinkles** the blood, **or to one of the members of the group.** If leaven belonged **to someone at the end of the world, he is not bound to him,** meaning that the slaughterer need not take him into account. **And** whether **he slaughters** the animal, **or he sprinkles** the blood, **or he burns** the fats, he is **liable. But one who pinches** the neck of **a bird,** which is not a Paschal offering but a burnt-offering or sin-offering, if he does so with leaven in his possession **on the fourteenth** of Nisan, after the prohibition against owning leaven has taken effect, **he does not transgress anything.**

The *gemara* cites a *braita* in support of Rav Papa. The *braita* states: *hashochet et hapesach al hachametz* — one who *shechts* the *Pesach* with *chametz* present — *over belo taaseh* — when it is: *lashochet, o lazorek, o leachad mibnei chaburah* — belonging to the *shochet*, the *zorek*, or one of the members of the *chaburah*. If the *chametz* belongs to some stranger — *hayah leachad besof haolam, ein zakuk lo* — it does not matter. The *braita* then confirms the scope of Rav Papa's ruling: *ve'echad hashochet ve'echad hazorek ve'echad hamaktir chayav* — whether one performed the *shechita*, the *zerika*, or the *haktara*, one is liable.

### Melikat Exception

The *braita* adds one further ruling: *aval hamolek et ha'of bearba'ah asar eino over belo klum* — one who performs *melikat ha'of* — the neckpinching that substitutes for *shechita* in bird-offerings — on the fourteenth of Nisan is not liable at all. The surface reading might suggest the reason is that *melika* is not an act of *shechita*, and therefore falls outside the prohibition of *lo tishchat*. However, Rashi explains that the actual reason is different: on *erev Pesach*, the prohibition only applies to *shechting* a *korban Pesach*, and *melika* is done for a bird, not a *korban Pesach*. The most common reason to bring a bird at this time is for *mechuserei kippurim* — those completing the *taharah* process — who would bring such a bird even after the *tamid* in order to become *tahor* in time to eat the *korban Pesach*. Since the bird is not a *korban Pesach*, the prohibition of *lo tishchat al chametz* simply does not apply to it on the fourteenth.
