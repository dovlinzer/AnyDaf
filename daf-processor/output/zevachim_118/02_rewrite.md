# Zevachim 118 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prior Debate Summary

### R. Yehuda Position

The discussion on this *daf* — the third-to-last of the *masechet* — continues the running analysis of the various historical periods in which sacrifices could or could not be offered on *bamot*, and under what conditions. To orient the discussion, it is worth briefly recapping the debate that was worked through previously.

Rabbi Yehuda took a permissive stance regarding the *bamah ketanah*, the private altar. The question had been whether private *bamot* were limited to *olah* and *shelamim*, or whether they extended to a *minchah* or to obligatory sacrifices that arose from voluntary vows — such as the *olah* of a *nazir*. The conclusion was that fundamentally the private *bamah* was limited to *olah* and *shelamim*, and perhaps not even all of those, specifically excluding obligatory ones. Turning to the *bamah gedolah* in Gilgal, the public central *bamah*, the *tzibur* could bring everything, and the question became what an individual could bring there. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the individual could bring everything as well — the public *bamah gedolah* functioned very similarly to the *Beis Hamikdash* or to Shilo. The only meaningful distinction for Rabbi Yehuda between Gilgal and Shilo was whether private *bamot* were permissible at all. But within the public *bamah* at Gilgal, every individual could bring whatever he or she wished.

### R. Meir/Chachamim

Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* disagreed. Their view was that even when you come to the public central *bamah*, the individual remains limited to *olah* and *shelamim*, just as on the private *bamah*. The reasoning is conceptual: if one is operating as a free agent — as someone who worships privately wherever he chooses — then even when he comes to the central location, he cannot plug into the obligatory sacrificial structure. The freedom to worship privately is characterized as an expression of free will, not an entitlement to obligatory categories. The *tzibur*, by contrast, does bring all the normal communal sacrifices. This distinction between the *tzibur* and the *yachid* — restricted even at the public *bamah* — generates a troubling consequence: throughout the entire period of Gilgal, Nov, and Givon, no individual could bring a *chatas* to atone for sin. Moreover, anyone in a state of *tumah* — a *yoledes*, a *zav*, a *zava* — could not bring a *chatas* to achieve purification, and would therefore be unable to eat the *Korban Pesach* or any of the year's *korbanos*. Rabbi Yehuda's approach, being far more expansive, avoids this troubling situation.

### Aron & Bamah Status

One question that deserves explicit treatment is why Gilgal — which did have a single central public *bamah* — was not treated like Shilo. Why did the existence of that central location not trigger a prohibition on *bamot* elsewhere, the way Shilo did?

The answer lies in the *aron*. In places like Gilgal, Nov, and Givon, the *aron* was absent. At the time of Nov and Givon particularly, the *aron* had been taken captive by the *Plishtim* and was elsewhere entirely. Without the *aron*, there was no central locus of God's presence. The structure in Gilgal was not truly a *Mishkan* in the full sense — it was a *bamah gedolah*, a large communal *bamah*, but it was not the particular dwelling-place of the *Shechinah*. It was therefore conceptually not much different from a *bamah ketanah* — just a larger, communally shared one.

### Presence & Restriction

This brings into focus a powerful theological principle. If God is present in one particular place — if the *Shechinah* in some meaningful sense dwells more particularly there than anywhere else — then it makes sense that one cannot simply worship everywhere. The uniqueness of the location demands exclusivity. But if God is not more present in Gilgal than anywhere else, then there is no compelling reason to restrict worship to that one spot. God is equally accessible everywhere, and both the *bamah gedolah* and the *bamah ketanah* reflect that equal availability. It is precisely this principle — that restriction to one place follows from the concentration of divine presence in that place — that explains why the *issur bamot* applies in Shilo and the *Beis Hamikdash* but not in Gilgal.

## R. Shimon Model

### R. Shimon Position

Having laid out the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir with the *Chachamim*, the *Gemara* now turns to the position of Rabbi Shimon, who is even more restrictive than Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Shimon holds that even the *tzibur* in a place like Gilgal could only bring the *Korban Pesach* and sacrifices with fixed, scheduled obligations. Where Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* restricted the *yachid* at the *bamah gedolah*, Rabbi Shimon restricts the *tzibur* itself.

### Pesach Derivation

The textual basis for Rabbi Shimon's position is a verse from Yehoshua: *vaya'asu bnei Yisrael et haPesach beGilgal* — the children of Israel made the Passover offering in Gilgal. The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: isn't it obvious that they brought the *Korban Pesach*? The *pesukim* are telling a story — why would the text need to tell us this? The answer is that the verse is teaching something non-obvious: *dechovos ke'ein Pesach hu dekarav, hala ke'ein Pesach lo karav* — only obligatory sacrifices *like* the *Pesach* were offered; things that were not like the *Pesach* were not offered.

This, Rabbi Shimon's view holds, reflects the conceptual reality that even the *bamah gedolah* in Gilgal was not fundamentally different from a *bamah ketanah*. It was merely an expanded version of the private *bamah* — somewhat more capacious, but conceptually rooted in the same model. The *Korban Pesach* is permitted for several converging reasons: it is the central communal sacrifice reconnecting the people to their founding narrative; it also has a quasi-private character, since individuals bring it for themselves; and it functions somewhat like a *shelamim*. These features allow it to stand as an exception.

The *Gemara* at this point introduces an alternative understanding of the verse. The *Chachamim* who hold a permissive view — that the *tzibur* can bring everything at the *bamah gedolah* — need this verse for the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yose ben Nazir: that an *arel*, an uncircumcised man, can receive the *hazayah*, the sprinkling of the ashes of the *Parah Adumah*, for purification from *tumas meis*.

The reasoning is as follows. Before the first *Korban Pesach* in Gilgal, the people performed a mass *bris milah*. Based on the chronology in the *pesukim* — they crossed the Yarden on the 10th of Nisan, recovered for a day, and could not have performed *bris milah* before the 11th — there was a potential problem. Everyone who had been wandering in the wilderness for forty years was presumably *tamei meis*, since people had been dying throughout. Purification from *tumas meis* requires the sprinkling of *Parah Adumah* ashes on day three and day seven of a seven-day process. If one cannot receive this sprinkling while still an *arel*, then no sprinkling could have begun until after the *bris milah* on day 11. That would push the final sprinkling to day 17 at the earliest, which is the 17th of Nisan — well after the 14th when *Korban Pesach* is brought. The fact that they did eat the *Korban Pesach* proves they must have been *tahor* in time, which means the sprinkling must have been performed while they were still *arelim*. Hence Rabbi Yochanan derives from this narrative that an *arel* can indeed have the *Parah Adumah* ashes sprinkled upon him.

So the verse serves two functions: for Rabbi Shimon's school, it establishes the narrow range of what was permitted at the *bamah gedolah* in Gilgal; for the *Chachamim*, who already permit a broader range at the *bamah gedolah*, the verse teaches this *halacha* about the *arel* and *Parah Adumah*.

### Gemara Challenge

A *tanna* taught before Rav Ada bar Ahava: *ein bein bamah gedolah l'bamah ketanah ela Pesach v'chovos shekavua lahem zman* — the only difference between a *bamah gedolah* and a *bamah ketanah* is the *Korban Pesach* and fixed-time obligatory offerings. This is Rabbi Shimon's position — the *bamah gedolah* is essentially a *bamah ketanah* with a small increment.

The *Gemara* is troubled by this. The formulation assumes that the *bamah gedolah* is conceptually rooted in the *bamah ketanah* and merely extends it slightly. If that is the case, then whatever is permitted at the *bamah gedolah* must have a counterpart at the *bamah ketanah*. The *Korban Pesach* passes this test, for the reasons already noted. But if Rabbi Shimon means to include fixed-time *chovot* that encompass a *chatas* — such as the *chatas* brought every *regel* or on *Yom Kippur* — then that is categorically different from anything done on a private *bamah*, where no *chatas* is offered at all. The logic of the model breaks down: you cannot build off a *bamah ketanah* to arrive at something the *bamah ketanah* has no analogue for.

### Olot vs. Chatas

The *Gemara* resolves this by a narrow reading: Rabbi Shimon's reference to fixed obligatory offerings means only obligatory *olot*, not obligatory *chatat* offerings. The *olat tamid* — the twice-daily burnt offering — and the *olah* component of the *musafin* on *Shabbos* and the *regalim* all have a direct counterpart in the *bamah ketanah*, where a private individual may bring an *olah*. So obligatory *olot* can be brought at the *bamah gedolah* as an expansion of the private *bamah*'s *olah*, but the *chatas* of the *regel* or of *Yom Kippur* cannot, because there is no private *chatas* at the *bamah ketanah* to build upon.

The *Gemara* briefly raises the possibility of including the *minchat chovah* — for instance, the daily *minchah* of the *Kohen Gadol* — since an individual can bring a private *minchah* on his personal *bamah*, and therefore a communal obligatory *minchah* could serve as its parallel. The *Gemara* rejects this on the basis of the view that there is no *minchah* at a *bamah* at all.

The practical implications of Rabbi Shimon's model are significant, even if theoretically the *bamah gedolah* is grounded in the *bamah ketanah*. In practice it still meant that the *olat tamid* was brought daily, and the *musafin* on *Shabbos* and the *regalim* were brought — just without the accompanying *chatas*. The absence of the *Yom Kippur avodah* entirely is noted by the *Tosafot* as a striking consequence — though conceptually this makes a certain amount of sense, since the *Yom Kippur avodah* is fundamentally about cleansing the *Mikdash* and the place of the *Shechinah*, and in a location like Gilgal, which lacks that concentrated divine presence, such an *avodah* would be out of place.

## Move to Shilo

### Shilo Structure

The *Gemara* now moves on: *ba'u leShilo* — they came to Shilo. Shilo was the first location that functioned as a quasi-*Beis Hamikdash*: private *bamot* were forbidden during its entire period, and the laws of *bamot* only became permissible again after Shilo was destroyed. Once the *Mikdash* in Jerusalem was built, of course, the prohibition on *bamot* became permanent.

The *Gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — what is the source for the particular structure of the Mishkan in Shilo? Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: there is an apparent contradiction between verses. One verse says *vatevi'ehu Beis Hashem Shilo* — "they brought him to the house of God in Shilo," using the word *beis*, which connotes a permanent stone structure. Another verse says *vayitosh Mishkan Shilo* — "God abandoned the *Mishkan* of Shilo," using the word *Mishkan*, which evokes the tent-like tabernacle of the wilderness. A third verse says *vayimas b'ohel Yosef uveshevet Ephraim lo vachar* — "He despised the tent of Yosef and did not choose the tribe of Ephraim" — using *ohel*, a tent. How are all three to be reconciled?

The answer is that the structure in Shilo was both: *lo haysa sham tikra ela avanim milmata viyeri'os milmala* — there was no permanent roof; instead, there were stone walls below and curtains above. The word *beis* refers to the stone walls; the word *ohel* refers to the curtain-like roofing, which functioned like the original *Mishkan*'s covering. This was the unique hybrid structure of the Mishkan in Shilo.

### Menucha Status

The *Gemara* notes that this structure made Shilo a *menucha* — a resting place — but not a *nachala*, a permanent inheritance. The term *menucha* indicates that it was a significant and settled dwelling of the *Shechinah*, but it was still temporary in character. After Shilo's destruction, private *bamot* became permissible again — something that did not happen after the destruction of Jerusalem. This impermanence is reflected in the physical structure itself: the temporary roofing over permanent walls captures the intermediate status of Shilo between the mobile *Mishkan* of the wilderness and the eternal *Beis Hamikdash* of Jerusalem. This hybrid character is echoed in the festival of *Sukkot*, where the *halachot* governing what makes a *sukkah* a temporary dwelling focus particularly on the roof — the *sechach* — while the walls may in principle be permanent. The temporary roof over stable walls is precisely the Shilo model.

### Kodashim Eating Rules

The discussion turns to the rules governing where one could eat *kodshei kodashim* and *kodashim kalim* at Shilo. *Kodshei kodashim* were eaten within the curtained enclosure in the usual manner. But *kodashim kalim* required a more extended zone — analogous to the walls of Jerusalem in the *Beis Hamikdash* period. The question is: what defined that zone in Shilo?

One might expect the answer to be the city of Shilo itself, serving as the equivalent of Jerusalem. But this was not the *halacha*. The city of Shilo had no special sanctified status comparable to Jerusalem's. Jerusalem carries a profound historical and theological weight; Shilo did not. Accordingly, the zone for eating *kodashim kalim* was not defined by the city limits of Shilo but by the line of sight to the *Mikdash* — from any place from which one could see the Mishkan, one could eat the *korbanos*. This could be miles away, provided there was no obstruction. It was not a geographical designation but a relational one: connection to the *Mikdash* through direct sight.

## Line of Sight

### Restriction Verse

The source for this principle is a verse cited by Rabbi Oshaya: *hishamer lecha pen ta'aleh olotecha bechol makom asher tireh* — "guard yourself lest you bring your *olot* in every place that you see." This verse, speaking of the prohibition of *bamot* when a central place exists, says you may not bring sacrifices wherever you see fit. The *Gemara* derives from this verse a positive analogue: *aval ata ochel bechol makom she'ata ro'eh* — but you may eat your sacrifices wherever you can see, meaning wherever you can see Shilo.

The *Gemara* anticipates an objection: perhaps the verse means that wherever you can see Shilo, you may slaughter (*zove'ach*) — not merely eat — your sacrifice? Rabbi Yannai resolves this from another verse: *sham ta'aleh, vesham ta'aseh* — "there you shall offer up, and there you shall perform." The offering and the *avodah* must occur in the single central place. The *avodah* is fully confined to the *Mikdash* in Shilo; only the *eating* extends to wherever sight permits.

### Yosef Midrash

Rav Dimi bar Rav Chisda brings another verse: *velo te'anach Shilo* — a verse describing the border of Yosef that goes eastward toward Shilo. Rashi candidly notes that he searched through all of *Tanach* and could not find this verse as quoted, but he did find a similar formulation: *venasav hagvul mizrachah Ta'anach Shilo* — the boundary turns eastward toward *Ta'anach Shilo*. The *Gemara* reads *Ta'anach Shilo* not as a place-name but midrashically: *makom shekol haroeh oso mit'ane'ach alav* — the place over which everyone who sees it sighs, remembering that *korbanos* were once eaten throughout the entire region within sight of that place.

Rabbi Abahu derives the same teaching from the verse about Yosef: *ben porat Yosef, ben porat alei ayin* — "a fruitful son is Yosef, a fruitful son by the fountain, whose branches run over the wall." The *Gemara* reads *alei ayin* as a reference to the eye: *ayin shelo ratztah lehannos midavar she'eino shelo* — the eye that refused to benefit from that which was not its own. Yosef refused to look upon what was not his — specifically, he refused to sin with the wife of Potiphar despite her persistence. Because Yosef guarded his eye and refused to take what was not his, his eye is rewarded: *tizkeh vesochalah kemlo eineha* — it merits to eat as far as the eye can see. The territory in which Yosef's descendants benefited from proximity to the Mishkan in Shilo extended as far as the eye could see.

Rabbi Yosi berabbi Chanina offers a parallel derivation from the verse in *Devarim*: *uretzon shocheni sneh* — the desire of the one who dwells in the thornbush, understood as God. Here again, the eye of Yosef that refused to benefit from what was not its own is rewarded: *tizkeh vesochalah bein hasnu'in* — it merits to eat even among those who hated him. The *Gemara* reads *sneh* not as thornbush but as *senu'in*, those who hated, referencing the brothers who despised Yosef — *vayisnu oso*. The reward is that even someone sitting in the territory of another tribe, one of those brothers' tribes, on a high place from which Shilo is visible, could eat the sacrifices by virtue of that connection to the Mishkan. That is how far Yosef's reward extended.

### Visibility Rules

A *Baraita* is cited that specifies the requirements: *roeh* — seeing. The question of exactly what "seeing" requires is then refined. The *Baraita* initially seems to imply *roeh kullo* — seeing all of it. But the essential qualification is *velo hamafsik beino leveino* — there must be no obstruction between the person and Shilo. This is the critical criterion: not distance, but unobstructed line of sight. One could be many miles away and still qualify; one could be quite close but disqualified by an intervening hill or structure.

Rabbi Shimon ben Yakam illustrates this for Elazar with a concrete example: the synagogue of *Ma'on* — a location presumably built on a high place where, despite the distance, Shilo was visible, and therefore it was a place where one could eat the *korbanos*.

### Partial Sight

Rav Pappa refines the ruling: *roeh she-amru lo roeh kullah* — when the *Sages* said "seeing," they did not require seeing the whole of Shilo. *Ela roeh miktzatah* — even seeing a portion of Shilo suffices. This clarifies the earlier formulation and may also explain why some versions of the *Baraita* text differ, with one version appearing to say *roeh kullo* ("seeing it all") while the *Gemara* now rules that partial sight is enough.

It is worth noting that throughout this discussion, "seeing Shilo" most naturally refers to seeing the *Mishkan* itself — seeing the actual location of the sacred structure. However, the plain meaning of the language could equally suggest that it was the town of Shilo that needed to be visible. This would be somewhat paradoxical, since it was established earlier that the city of Shilo has no independent sanctified status equivalent to Jerusalem. Nevertheless, if the relevant criterion is seeing the town rather than the *Mishkan* specifically, that does give the actual city some degree of legal significance.

### Edge Cases

Rav Pappa poses a further question: *omed v'roeh, yoshev v'eino roeh, mai* — if when standing one can see Shilo, but when sitting one cannot, what is the ruling? Does the ability to see while standing mean the location qualifies — so that one may even eat while seated — or does the inability to see while seated mean the location is disqualified entirely? The question cuts both ways and no resolution is given.

Rabbi Yirmiah, who characteristically enjoyed pressing such edge cases, posed an analogous question about a *nachal* — a wadi or ravine. If one stands at the edge of the wadi and can see Shilo, but descends into the wadi and can no longer see it, what is the ruling? Perhaps the wadi and its rim are considered one unified location and must be governed by a single rule; or perhaps the sight-line at each physical point determines the rule for that point. The *Gemara* leaves both questions unresolved: *teiku*.

## Divine Presence

### Rav Dimi Teaching

Rav Dimi, upon returning from *Eretz Yisrael*, cited a teaching in the name of Rabbi: *b'shlosha mekomot sharta Shechinah b'Yisrael* — the *Shechinah* dwelt in three locations among Israel: in Shilo, in Nov and Givon, and in the *Beis Olamim* — the eternal *Beis Hamikdash*. The larger claim is that all of these locations were exclusively within the portion of Binyamin: *u'v'chulan lo sharta ela b'chelko shel Binyamin*. This is derived from the verse *chofef alav kol hayom* — God hovers over Binyamin the entire day — which the teaching interprets to mean that every location of divine hovering was always within Binyamin's territory.

The mention of Nov and Givon as locations of the *Shechinah* is itself striking, since as was noted, it was precisely during the period of Nov and Givon that the *aron* was absent. Yet Rav Dimi calls them places of the dwelling of the divine presence. The *Gemara* does not fully unpack this tension.

### Shilo Location

When Abaye repeated this teaching before Rav Yosef, Rav Yosef reacted sharply — characterizing the transmission as muddled. His objection was textual: *vayitosh Mishkan Shilo, vayimas b'ohel Yosef uveshevet Ephraim lo vachar* — the verses explicitly say God abandoned Shilo in connection with Yosef and Ephraim, not Binyamin. Shilo is in the territory of Yosef, not Binyamin. These are different tribes — Binyamin is Yosef's brother, a fellow son of Rachel, but a distinct tribe with a distinct territorial allotment.

Rav Adda responds: *mai kakasheilei* — why is that a difficulty? Perhaps the same arrangement obtained at Shilo as is well-known from Jerusalem: the *Shechinah* — the location of the *aron* and the *mizbeach* — was in Binyamin's portion, while the *Sanhedrin* convened in Yosef's portion. Just as in Jerusalem the *Shechinah* was in Binyamin and the *Sanhedrin* was in Yehuda, so too in Shilo the *Shechinah* proper may have been in Binyamin while the *Sanhedrin* occupied the section that fell within Yosef's territory. The *Gemara* finds it fascinating to imagine that the *Sanhedrin* dwelt in the Mishkan complex in Shilo as well, paralleling its role in the *Beis Hamikdash*.

Rav Yosef challenges this analogy: *hachi hashta?* — Is the comparison valid? In Jerusalem, Yehuda and Binyamin are genuinely adjacent; their portions are contiguous, and a shared border makes the overlap at the *Mikdash* natural. But Ephraim and Binyamin — are their portions juxtaposed in the same way?

The *Gemara* responds: yes, they can be considered juxtaposed — *hachi nami mikravin*. Just as Rabbi Chama berabbi Chanina taught that a strip (*retzuah*) went out from Yehuda's portion into Binyamin's, giving Yehuda a share in the *mizbeach* — and over this strip Binyamin the righteous yearned, desiring to absorb it — so too here, a strip could go out from Yosef's portion into Binyamin's, situating the sacred core of Shilo in Binyamin even while a significant section of the complex fell within Ephraim. The verse *ta'anat Shiloh*, understood in this context as indicating an extension, would then reflect this strip of Yosef extending into the area of Shilo situated in Binyamin.

The *Gemara* is candid that if one places Shilo according to its known archaeological and geographical location, the neat parallel with Jerusalem's border between Yehuda and Binyamin does not work perfectly. But the conceptual point the *Gemara* wishes to establish is that the arrangement in Shilo could have mirrored that of Jerusalem: the *Shechinah* concentrated in Binyamin's portion, with Yosef/Ephraim contributing a strip that encompassed major aspects of the compound — just as in Jerusalem, Yehuda contributed the strip where the *mizbeach* stood.

The deeper background to all of this involves the recurring tension between the legacy of Yosef and the legacy of Yehuda — between *Bnei Rachel* and *Bnei Leah*, between the earlier focus of kingship and sacred space in Yosef's descendants (as exemplified by Shaul, who was from Binyamin, but more broadly in the centrality of Ephraim during the early period), and the eventual shift of both kingship and *Mikdash* to Yehuda. Binyamin functions throughout as the mediating presence, always adjacent to the center, captured by the phrase *chofef alav kol hayom* — God hovers over him always, making Binyamin the perpetual locus of divine nearness regardless of which surrounding tribe holds the larger territorial claim.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan offers a formulation that parallels Rav Dimi's teaching in its conclusion. His language — *Shechinah hovers over Binyamin perpetually in this world* — encompasses all periods of history, including Shilo.

### Tannaitic Dispute

The *Gemara* then reports that this question is in fact the subject of a *Tannaitic* dispute — *k'tannai*. The verse *chofef alav kol hayom, uvein kteifav shachen* is parsed by the first *Tannaim* to refer to discrete periods: *chofef alav* refers to the first *Beis Hamikdash*; *kol hayom* refers to the second *Beis Hamikdash*; and *uvein kteifav shachen* refers to the messianic period. On this reading, God's hovering presence in Binyamin was limited to the periods of the *Mikdash* — it did not encompass the period of Shilo. The implication is that Shilo was not within Binyamin, consistent with the plain reading of the verses about Yosef and Ephraim.

Rabbi, by contrast, reads the verse more expansively: *chofef alav* — *zeh olam hazeh* — in all of recorded history, God was always present in Binyamin. *Kol hayom* — *eilu yemot hamashiach* — refers to the messianic period. *Uvein kteifav shachen* — *zeh ha'olam habah* — refers to the world to come, which in the *Gemara*'s usage typically means not the afterlife of individual souls but the renewed world of the post-messianic era. According to Rabbi, even the period of Shilo falls within God's perpetual hovering over Binyamin, meaning Shilo was indeed considered to be within Binyamin's portion.

## Shilo Chronology

### 480-Year Framework

The *Gemara* now turns to a precise historical chronology, working out how long the Mishkan stood in Shilo. The framework is provided by the verse in *Melachim* stating that the *Beis Hamikdash* was built 480 years after the exodus from Egypt. The approach is essentially one of subtraction: identify all the other periods — the wilderness, Gilgal, Nov and Givon — subtract their totals from 480, and the remainder is the years Shilo stood.

### Final Subtraction

The known figures are as follows. The *Ohel Moed* in the wilderness stood for 39 years. The period in Gilgal was 14 years. The period in Nov and Givon was 57 years. Together these total 111 years (40 + 14 + 57, where the 40 accounts for the wilderness period in round terms). Subtracting 111 from 480 yields 369 years — *nishtayru le'Shilo gimel me'ot v'shiv'im chaser achas*, 370 minus 1 — for the period of Shilo. This is a remarkably long time. We tend to think of Shilo as a minor episode in the history of sacred space, but its 369 years compare closely with the 420 years of the first *Beis Hamikdash* — a difference of only about 50 years.

### Wilderness: 39 Years

How is the figure of 39 years derived for the wilderness period? As the tradition states: *shana rishona asa Moshe et haMishkan* — the first year was spent constructing the *Mishkan*; *shniya hukam haMishkan* — an explicit verse establishes that it was erected on *Rosh Chodesh Nisan* of the second year. Since the *Mishkan* was standing from year two through year forty, that is 39 years.

### Gilgal: 14 Years

The 14 years of Gilgal are derived from the testimony of Calev. He declares: *ben arba'im shana anochi b'shloach Moshe eved Hashem miKadesh Barnea l'ragel et ha'aretz* — I was 40 years old when Moshe sent the spies from Kadesh Barnea. The spies were sent in year two, so Calev was 40 in year two. They then entered *Eretz Yisrael* in year 40 of the wilderness, making Calev 78 at the crossing of the Yarden. He later declares *ve'ata hinei anochi hayom ben chamish v'shmonim shana* — I am 85 today. From 78 to 85 is 7 years — the seven years of conquest. The seven years of dividing the land are added to this. How do we know it took seven years to divide? One possibility offered is simply *sevara be'alma* — a hypothesis that since it took seven years to conquer, it took seven to divide. The *Gemara* offers a second derivation based on Yechezkel's statement that his prophecy came fourteen years after the destruction of Jerusalem, combined with calculations about the *Shemitta* and *Yovel* cycles: since the counting of *Yovel* begins only when every tribe is settled in its proper location, and various computations converge on the conclusion that this process could not have begun until the 14th year after entry, this confirms the 14-year total for the period in Gilgal.

### Nov/Givon: 57 Years

The 57-year figure for Nov and Givon requires a more complex derivation built up from several smaller units, and the overall structure is established through a key verse and several corroborating computations.

### Eli & Aron Capture

The *Gemara* anchors the transition from Shilo to Nov at the death of Eli. The verse records that when the *aron* of God was mentioned in his presence — that it had been taken captive by the *Plishtim* — Eli fell backwards and died. A *Tanna* teaches: *keshemet Eli HaKohen charav Shilo* — when Eli the priest died, Shilo was destroyed. This marks the end of the Shilo period and the beginning of what follows. The *Mishkan* operation moved to Nov, functioning without the *aron*. When Shmuel died, Nov in turn was destroyed (*charav Nov*), and the center moved to Givon.

### Aron Kiryat Ye'arim

A separate verse tracks the fate of the *aron* itself: *vayehi miyom shevet ha-aron beKiryat Ye'arim, vayirbu hayamim vayihyu esrim shana* — from the day the *aron* rested in Kiryat Ye'arim, the days multiplied and were twenty years. The *aron* had been returned by the *Plishtim* relatively quickly after its capture, and it then resided in Kiryat Ye'arim for twenty years before David brought it to Jerusalem. These twenty years are then broken down: ten years during which Shmuel ruled alone; one year during which Shmuel and Shaul overlapped; two additional years of Shaul's reign after Shmuel's death; and seven years of David's reign in Hevron before he moved to Jerusalem. Together these account for the twenty years during which the *aron* sat in Kiryat Ye'arim.

### David & Solomon

The remaining 37 years needed to reach the total of 57 are derived from the reigns of David and Solomon. The verse states that David reigned over Israel for forty years: seven in Hevron and thirty-three in Jerusalem. So after the *aron* arrived in Jerusalem, David reigned another 33 years. Adding these 33 years to the 20 years of Kiryat Ye'arim yields 53 years. Solomon then built the *Beis Hamikdash* not in year one of his reign but in year four: *vayachel livnot bishnat arba lemalchuto*. Adding those four years brings the total to 57 years — the full period from the destruction of Shilo through to the construction of the *Beis Hamikdash*.

Expressed differently: David's 40 years plus Shmuel and Shaul's combined 13 years plus Solomon's initial 4 years equals 57. This is the total period of Nov and Givon, and when subtracted along with the wilderness years and the Gilgal years from the 480-year framework, it leaves precisely 369 years for Shilo — a duration that commands reflection, reminding us that Shilo was not a brief transitional moment but a centuries-long center of Jewish sacred life.
