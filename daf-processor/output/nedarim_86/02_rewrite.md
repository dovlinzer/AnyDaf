# Nedarim 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Specificity in Annulment

### Mistaken Identity Cases

The *mishnah* on 86b continues the discussion of a husband's power of *hafarah*, shifting focus from the content of vows — what constitutes *inuy nefesh* or *devarim shebeino l'veina* — to the mechanics of the act of annulment itself. The *mishnah* presents a series of cases in which a husband attempted to annul or affirm a vow under some form of mistaken identity or misclassification. *Nedar ishto vesavar shenadar bito* — his wife took a vow but he thought it was his daughter, or *nedar bito vesavar shenadar ishto* — his daughter took a vow but he thought it was his wife. *Nadra benazir vesavar shenadra bekorban* — she vowed to be a *nazir* but he thought she used *korban* language, or the reverse. *Nadra mite'einim vesavar shenadra min ha'anavim* — she vowed not to eat figs but he thought the vow was about grapes, or vice versa.

In each case, the husband was mistaken either about the identity of the woman whose vow he was acting upon, the subject matter of the vow, or the type of the vow. The *mishnah* rules: *harei zeh yachzor veyafer* — he must go back and annul properly if he wants the annulment to take effect.

### Three Requirements

The principle emerging from the *mishnah* is that for an act of *hafarah* — and as the *gemara* will clarify, *kiyum* as well — to be valid, the husband must know for whom he is acting, what object the vow concerns, and what type of vow it is. The *mishnah* is not primarily concerned with the subjective reasons a husband might have for preferring to annul one vow over another, though one could argue that the reason he might annul a *nazir* vow differs from his reason for annulling a *korban* vow, and similarly that what he wishes to annul for his wife may differ from what he wishes to annul for his daughter. The *mishnah* treats these as formal requirements of knowledge rather than merely motivational ones.

### Timing from Hearing

One might ask whether a mistaken annulment resets the clock of *beyom shamo* — the requirement that the husband act on the day he hears the vow. The answer appears to be no. Since in all these cases the husband did know he had the power to annul and did know he was dealing with a *neder* he could address, the original time period is not reset. The clock runs from the day he actually heard the vow. The *mishnah* does not treat this category of error as analogous to cases — discussed in upcoming *mishnayot* — where the husband did not realize he had the power to annul or did not recognize the vow as one he could address.

### Mode of Hearing

One question that arises is how a husband could mistake his wife for his daughter or vice versa. The simplest reading is that he heard from behind a door and confused their voices. The *Ran*, however, writes that *she'amru lo shebito nadra umitoch kach hefer o kiyem* — it was reported to him incorrectly, and on the basis of that report he annulled or affirmed. This is somewhat puzzling, since an earlier discussion established that the husband must hear the vow personally, which was the basis for ruling that he cannot appoint a *shaliach* to hear on his behalf. How the *Ran*'s formulation fits that requirement is a question that deserves further attention.

### Otah Analysis

The *gemara* takes up the *mishnah* with the observation: *l'memra d'otah davka* — the word *otah* ("her") in the relevant verse is specific. He must know precisely who the woman is whose vow he is annulling.

### Knowing the Vow

The *Rosh* here explains: *dichtiv v'ini otah d'mashma she-makkir ha-ishah ha-noderes* — the word *otah* implies recognition of the woman who made the vow, *umashma nami hadichtiv v'heifer et nidrah* — and the phrase "her vow" implies that he must also recognize the specific vow. *Tosafot*, by contrast, understands the word *otah* in *v'ini otah* to refer not to the woman but to the vow itself. We noted earlier that the plain meaning of the verse is that *v'ini otah* means "prevent her" — the woman — while *nidrah* refers to the vow. Thus the *Rosh* draws on both to derive both requirements, while *Tosafot* reads *otah* as referring to the vow directly. Either way, the conclusion is the same: the husband must know both the woman and the vow.

## Explicit vs. Internal

### Keriah Wrong Person

Having established that the husband must have specific knowledge, the *gemara* raises a challenge from *keriah* — the rending of garments in mourning. The verse speaks of David rending his garments *al Shaul v'al Yehonatan beno*, explicitly naming those for whom the rending was performed. One would therefore expect that rending garments for the wrong person ought not to count, just as annulling the wrong person's vow does not count.

### Baraita Contradiction

A *beraisa* is cited: *amru lo met aviv ve-kara* — someone was told his father had died and he rent his garments, *ve-achar kach nimtza beno* — and it turned out it was actually his son who had died. The *beraisa* rules *yatza yedei keriah* — he has fulfilled his obligation of *keriah*, even though the act was performed under a mistaken identity. This seems directly analogous to our case, and yet the ruling goes the other way. The verse contains *al* — just as specific as *v'ini otah* — and still we do not require that the *keriah* be performed for the correct person.

A second *beraisa* appears to contradict the first: *amru lo met aviv ve-kara, ve-achar kach nimtza beno, lo yatza yedei keriah* — he did not fulfill the obligation. The *gemara* resolves this apparent contradiction: *lo kashya, ha bistam ve-ha bemeforesh* — it is not difficult; the two *baraitot* address different cases. The distinction turns on whether the act was explicitly labeled as being for a particular person or whether the mistaken identity was only internal.

### Explicit Mislabel

The *Ran* explains the resolution more precisely. The first *beraisa*, which rules that *keriah* counts even though it was done for the wrong person, is not describing a case where they explicitly told him his father had died. Rather, *be-she'amru lo met* — they told him only that someone had died, *ve-savar aviv ve-nimtza beno* — and he assumed it was his father, when in fact it was his son. Since the act was not explicitly labeled as a rending for the father, *keriah* performed in the context of a family member's death is valid even though his internal assumption was wrong. The second *beraisa*, which rules that *keriah* does not count, describes the case where they told him explicitly that his father died — that is, the act was explicitly labeled as being for the father — and then it emerged that it was the son. An explicitly mislabeled act cannot be reassigned.

This principle — that what matters is not internal intention but external, explicit labeling — is striking. One might have thought that *keriah* could tolerate internal error because it is an outward, physical demonstration in a real-world context of loss, whereas an act of *hafarah* or *hakamah* requires much more specific engagement with the substance of the vow, and *kiyum* in particular requires no verbal act at all. That the same principle of external labeling would govern all of these is somewhat counterintuitive, though the *gemara*'s development of the *keriah* analogy pushes in precisely that direction.

### Apply to Annul

Translating this principle back to *hafarah* and *hakamah*: as long as the husband has not explicitly labeled his act as an annulment of a specific person's specific vow — as long as he simply heard a vow and declared *meifer ani* — the act is not mislabeled. Even if internally he was thinking about the wrong woman or the wrong vow, the act stands: there was a vow, he had the power to annul, and he performed the act of *hafarah*. It is only where he explicitly designated the act — "I am annulling my daughter's vow" — and it turned out to be his wife's vow, that the *hafarah* fails.

This is admittedly a surprising extension of the *keriah* analogy. The sense in which an act of mourning has objective meaning in the context of a real death — regardless of what was in the mourner's mind — is not identical to the sense in which a husband's act of annulment has meaning regardless of whom he was thinking of. But the *gemara*'s logic treats the two as parallel.

## Toch Kedei Dibur

### Rav Ashi Correction

*Rav Ashi* goes further: *kan betoch kedei dibbur kan le'achar kedei dibbur* — the difference between the two *baraitot* on *keriah* is not a question of explicit versus internal labeling, but of timing. Even if the act was explicitly mislabeled — even if they told him his father had died and he rent his garments — if the discovery that it was actually his son occurred *betoch kedei dibbur*, within the time window of *toch kedei dibbur*, the act can be redefined and the *keriah* counts.

### Simultaneity Concept

The concept of *toch kedei dibbur* — roughly, "within the time of speech," understood as approximately the time it takes to greet a teacher (*shalom alecha rabbi*) — normally applies to verbal acts, where a speaker can reconsider and amend a declaration within an immediate window. It is less intuitive when applied to a physical act like *keriah*, where there is no speech to extend or amend. What *Rav Ashi* seems to be saying is that the discovery itself, occurring *toch kedei dibbur*, allows the earlier act to be retrospectively redefined — not by redoing anything, but simply by virtue of the temporal proximity of the new awareness.

### Keriah Discovery

The *gemara* supports this with a *beraisa*: *mi sheyesh lo choleh b'toch beito* — someone has a gravely ill person at home, *ve-chedumeh shemet* — and the person appeared to have died (stopped breathing), *ve-kara* — and he rent his garments, *ve-achar met* — and then the person actually died shortly afterward. *Lo yatza yedei keriah* — he did not fulfill the obligation. Then comes a gloss attributed to *Rav Shimon ben Pazi* in the name of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* in the name of *Bar Kappara*: *lo shanu ela shemet achar k'dei dibbur* — this ruling applies only if the person died after *k'dei dibbur* had elapsed; *aval b'toch k'dei dibbur k'dibbur dami* — but if the person died within *toch kedei dibbur*, it counts, because within that window, the moment of the act and the moment of the death are treated as contemporaneous.

The combined teaching of this section is therefore: first, *keriah* need not be intentionally directed at the correct person — it must only not be explicitly mislabeled; second, even explicit mislabeling can be corrected if the correction or discovery occurs *toch kedei dibbur*; third, the same principles apply by analogy to *hafarah* and *hakamah*.

## Explicit vs. Internal…

### Keriah Before Death

The most extreme and philosophically difficult implication of *Rav Ashi*'s ruling is the *beraisa* just cited: the man rent his garments before the person had actually died, and yet it counts as valid *keriah* provided the death followed *toch kedei dibbur*. This is a genuinely difficult case. It is one thing to say that an act performed in the correct context — someone has died, a family member rends garments — is valid even if the mourner's internal assumptions were wrong. But here the precondition for *keriah* — actual death — had not yet obtained at the moment the act was performed. How can an act be valid for an event that had not yet occurred?

## Toch Kedei Dibur…

### Pre-Condition Problem

This question is genuinely difficult. For *toch kedei dibbur* to apply to a matter of intent or a verbal act, one can say that the speaker's mind or declaration remains open and continuous within that window, allowing a later moment within it to redefine an earlier one. But here the difficulty is ontological: you are trying to pull a future event — the actual death — backward to give meaning to a prior act. It is not merely a question of redefining one's frame of mind; the very condition that would make the act halachically meaningful did not exist yet.

### Time Granularity

One possible resolution is that *halachah* measures time in *toch kedei dibbur* increments, not in seconds. The clock of *halachah*, so to speak, does not tick in units finer than *toch kedei dibbur*. Just as the principle of *i efshar letzamtzem* addresses the impossibility of perfect precision, one could suggest that *halachah* treats the entire span of *toch kedei dibbur* as a single temporal unit — anything occurring within it is halachically simultaneous with anything else in that window. Under this reading, the *keriah* and the death occur in the same halachic moment, even if, measured by an ordinary clock, the death came fractionally later.

This interpretation does have limits. The *gemara* rules elsewhere that certain acts cannot be remedied even within *toch kedei dibbur*. For example, if a husband betroths a woman who at that same moment receives a *get* from a previous husband, the *kiddushin* does not take effect. So the principle of halachic simultaneity does not resolve every case. The temporal-granularity approach may explain the *keriah* case — where one is merely relabeling an act in the context of a real death — but the full philosophical implications of *toch kedei dibbur* as applied to preconditions that do not yet exist remain genuinely challenging.

## Exceptions to Toch

### Four Exceptions

The *gemara* concludes with the operative *halachah*: *v'hilchasa, toch kedei dibbur k'dibbur dami* — the rule of *toch kedei dibbur* applies as a matter of law, *chutz mimigadef v'oved avodas kochavim u'mekadesh u'mgaresh* — except for blasphemy (*megadef*), idolatry (*oved avodah zarah*), betrothal (*kiddushin*), and divorce (*gerushin*). In these four cases, a retraction or correction within *toch kedei dibbur* has no legal effect.

All the *rishonim* note that this entire framework — the general principle and its exceptions — appears to be *d'oraisa* in its application. The *mishnah* we are analyzing concerns *hafarah* and *hakamah*, which are themselves *d'oraisa* matters. This creates a significant difficulty for the view of *Rabbeinu Tam*, who held that *toch kedei dibbur* is a rabbinic *takkanah* designed to ease commercial transactions by giving parties a brief window to reconsider. The *gemara* here applies *toch kedei dibbur* across a range of *d'oraisa* contexts without any indication that it is a rabbinic overlay.

### Weighty Acts

No explicit verse is cited as the source for excluding the four cases. The *rishonim* explain it on the basis of *sevara* — logic. The *Ran* writes: *bishar mili d'lav chamiri kulei hai, k'she-adam oseh otam lo b'gemar da'ato hu oseh* — in ordinary matters of life, a person acts without having fully made up his mind, always holding a small reservation, *da'ato she-yuchal lachazor bo toch k'dei dibbur* — expecting that he can still reconsider within that brief window. The act is undertaken with a kind of built-in tentativeness, which is what *toch kedei dibbur* accommodates.

### Intent Finality

But *hani kivan d'chamiri kulei hai, ein adam oseh otam ela b'haskamah gemurah* — since these four acts are so weighty, a person never undertakes them without complete and unreserved intention. There is no residual tentativeness to be accommodated. Therefore, *mishum hachi chazarah afilu toch k'dei dibbur lo mahani* — any attempt to retract is legally null, because the *da'as* at the moment of the act is considered to have been full and final.

One could alternatively formulate the principle as follows: rather than asking about the actor's internal state, one could say that an act of such weight is itself final by virtue of what it is. The act speaks for itself and cannot be relabeled or retracted, regardless of whether the actor's frame of mind was technically tentative or not. Whether the focus is on the finality of *da'as* or the finality of the act itself, the functional conclusion is the same: for *megadef*, *oved avodah zarah*, *kiddushin*, and *gerushin*, *toch kedei dibbur* provides no relief.

## Partial Acts Debate

### Figs Grapes Vow

The *mishnah* introduces a new scenario. A woman declared: *konam te'einim v'anavim eilu she-ani to'emet* — these figs and grapes are forbidden to me regarding my tasting of them. The husband then acted on only one part of the vow.

### Partial Effects

The *mishnah* rules: *kiyam le'te'einim, kulo kiyam* — if he affirmed the vow regarding figs, the entire vow is affirmed. *Heifer le'te'einim, eino mufar ad she-yafer af la-anavim* — but if he annulled regarding figs, it is not annulled at all until he annuls the grapes as well. The principle: a partial *hakamah* works for the whole *neder*, but a partial *hafarah* works for nothing. The *neder* already exists as a reality; to affirm what is already there, even a partial affirmation is sufficient to root the entire *neder* in place. To annul, however, requires rejecting the whole, and a partial annulment accomplishes nothing.

The *Ran* suggests a different reading of the *mishnah* — namely, that *hafarah* on part does work for that part, and the phrase *eino mufar* until he annuls the grapes means only that the grapes remain bound until he addresses them separately. But this is not the *pshat* of the *mishnah*, and the *Ran* himself appears to acknowledge as much. The plain reading is all-or-nothing.

### Separate Language

A second case in the *mishnah*: *amar konam te'einah she-ani to'emet ve-anavah she-ani to'emet* — she used the phrase *she-ani to'emet* separately for each item, applying it to the fig and to the grape individually. *Harei hu shenei nedarim* — this constitutes two separate vows. The consequence is that a *kiyum* on one does not establish the other, and a *hafarah* on one is effective for that one. The repetition of the verbal formula *she-ani to'emet* suffices to break the single vow into two independent vows, even without repeating the word *konam* for each item.

### Yishmael All/Nothing

The *gemara* identifies the *mishnah* with *Rabbi Yishmael*. A *beraisa* is cited to spell out his position: *isha yekimenu ve-isha yeferenu* — the husband shall establish it and the husband shall annul it. If she said *konam te'einim v'anavim eilu she-ani to'emet* — figs and grapes that I shall not taste — *kiyem le'te'einim, kulo kiyem* — if he affirmed for figs, it is all affirmed; *heifer le'te'einim, eino mufar ad she-yafer af la-anavim* — if he annulled for figs only, it is nothing until he annuls the grapes as well. These are the words of *Rabbi Yishmael*. The *mishnah* is thus fully aligned with his position.

### Akiva Linked

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees. He reads the juxtaposition in the verse — *isha yekimenu ve-isha yeferenu* — as linking *hakamah* and *hafarah* so that they operate with equal efficacy. The *gemara* then asks: where does the concept of partial *hakamah* producing full effect come from? The *drasha* is from the word *yekimenu*: the *mem* suffix is read as *mimmenu* — "from it" or "of it" — yielding *yakim mimmenu*, meaning that affirming even a part of the vow constitutes a full *hakamah*. From this, *Rabbi Akiva* derives by *hekesh* — analogy — that *af yesirenu mimmenu*: just as a partial *hakamah* is a full one, a partial *hafarah* is also effective. Both work partially and completely in parallel.

*Rabbi Yishmael* would counter: *mi ketiv yafer mimmenu?* — the word *mimmenu* appears only by *yekimenu*, not by *yeferenu*. You cannot derive the partial *hafarah* from the partial *hakamah* without a specific textual basis. *Rabbi Akiva* responds that the verse *hekesh*'s the two — they are explicitly linked — so *ma hakamah mimmenu af hafarah mimmenu*. The debate is thus clear: both agree that partial *hakamah* operates fully; they disagree on whether the same follows for *hafarah*.

### Sages Position

*Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* introduces a third opinion, that of the *Chachamim*. According to the standard *girsa*: *ma hafarah ma sheheifer heifer af hakamah ma shekiyam kiyam* — just as *hafarah* works for what was annulled (and only for that), so too *hakamah* works only for what was affirmed (and only for that). This reading rejects the all-or-nothing framework entirely: partial *hafarah* is effective for the part addressed, and partial *hakamah* is effective only for the part affirmed.

The *Ran*, however, presents an alternative *girsa* found in *k'tzat sefarim*: *aval chachamim omrim mah hafarah mah shehefir lo hefir af hakama mah shekiyam lo kiyam* — just as a partial *hafarah* does nothing, so too a partial *hakamah* does nothing. The *Ran* further notes that this is the *girsa* of *Rabbeinu Moshe bar Nachman* in his *halachot*. Under this reading, the *Chachamim* represent the third logical possibility in an all-or-nothing framework: Rabbi Yishmael holds that partial *hakamah* is everything and partial *hafarah* is nothing; Rabbi Akiva holds that both are everything; and the *Chachamim* hold that both are nothing. Three logically coherent positions given an all-or-nothing premise.

### Rambam Ruling

The *Rambam*, in chapter 13 of *Hilchot Nedarim*, halachah 10, rules as follows: *asah te'einim va'anavim bein bishvuah bein she'asar atzmo bechol hamin vekiyam lite'einim vehefir la'anavim o shekiyam la'anavim vehefir lite'einim mah shekiyam kiyam mah shehufar mufar* — if the figs and grapes were treated as separate matters, and he affirmed the figs and annulled the grapes, or vice versa, what he affirmed is affirmed and what he annulled is annulled. The *Rambam* thus follows the standard *girsa* of the *Chachamim*, ruling that partial *hafarah* and partial *hakamah* each take effect for their respective objects.

## Oaths Parallel

### Rava Shimon

*Rava* observes: *masnitin Rabbi Shimon hi* — the *mishnah* reflects the position of *Rabbi Shimon*. The reference is to the debate regarding multiple oaths: *de'amar ad sheyomar shevuah lechol echad ve'echad* — Rabbi Shimon holds that separate liability for each oath requires that each be designated separately. In the case of one who borrowed from five people and denied each debt, a single blanket denial-oath produces only one *korban*, whereas separate declarations of *shevuah* for each person produce separate obligations.

### Separation Needed

The parallel to our *mishnah* is that combining the figs and grapes in a single vow creates a single *neder*, just as combining the oaths in a single declaration creates a single oath. The repetition of *she-ani to'emet* for each item functions analogously to repeating the word *shevuah* for each creditor — it creates the necessary separation. *Rava*'s point is that our *mishnah*'s requirement of explicit separate language to divide a compound vow into independent *nedarim* mirrors *Rabbi Shimon*'s requirement in the realm of oaths.
