# Ketubot 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Moredet Overview

### Onah to Moredet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹרֶדֶת עַל בַּעְלָהּ — פּוֹחֲתִין לָהּ מִכְּתוּבָּתָהּ שִׁבְעָה דִּינָרִין בַּשַּׁבָּת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שִׁבְעָה טַרְפְּעִיקִין. עַד מָתַי הוּא פּוֹחֵת — עַד כְּנֶגֶד כְּתוּבָּתָהּ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם הוּא פּוֹחֵת וְהוֹלֵךְ, עַד שֶׁאִם תִּפּוֹל לָהּ יְרוּשָּׁה מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר, גּוֹבָה הֵימֶנָּה. וְכֵן, הַמּוֹרֵד עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ — מוֹסִיפִין עַל כְּתוּבָּתָהּ שְׁלֹשָׁה דִּינָרִין בְּשַׁבָּת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשָׁה טַרְפְּעִיקִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A woman **who rebels against her husband** is fined; **her marriage contract is reduced** by **seven dinars** each **week. Rabbi Yehuda says: Seven half-dinars [*terapa'ikin*]** each week. **Until When does he reduce** her marriage contract? **Until** the reductions are **equivalent to her marriage contract,** i.e., until he no longer owes her any money, at which point he divorces her without any payment. **Rabbi Yosei says: He can always continue to deduct** from the sum, even beyond that which is owed to her due to her marriage contract, **so that if she will receive an inheritance from another source, he can collect** the extra amount **from her. And similarly,** if a man **rebels against his wife,** he is fined and an extra **three dinars a week are added to her marriage contract. Rabbi Yehuda says: Three *terapa'ikin*.**

Today's daf begins towards the bottom of 63a, where the Mishnah transitions from the topic of *onah* — the husband's obligation to have marital relations at regular intervals — into the subject of the *moredet*, a wife who is not living up to her responsibilities in the marriage. The earlier Mishnah had framed *onah* in terms of frequency, and the preceding Gemara had explored the tension between *talmud Torah* and a man's obligations at home. Now the Mishnah turns to examine the range of mutual responsibilities between husband and wife, and what happens when one party fails to meet them.

The term *moredet* — "one who rebels" — is the central concept here, along with its less common counterpart, *mored*, referring to a husband who similarly fails his obligations. This passage carries enormous practical weight, both for questions of *agunah* and for broader questions about how rabbinic courts should approach a marriage that is no longer functioning as it should.

### Mishna Penalties

The Mishnah states: *hamoredet al ba'alah* — a woman who rebels against her husband — *pofsin lach mi-ktuvatah*, we reduce the value of her *ketubah*. The mechanism is a progressive monetary fine: for each week that she remains in a state of rebellion, the total value of her *ketubah* is reduced. The *ketubah*'s total value encompasses the base amount — 100 or 200 *zuz* — plus whatever *tosefet* the husband added, plus the value of the dowry she brought into the marriage. All of this is slowly diminished on a weekly basis, so that even if she subsequently reconciles herself to her obligations, she will be unable to collect the full value of the *ketubah* upon divorce or the husband's death.

The Mishnah specifies the rate of reduction: *shiva dinarim b'shabbos*, seven *dinar* per week. Rabbi Yehudah says the rate is seven *tarpeikin*. The Gemara will clarify the precise monetary values involved and what they represent relative to the 200 *zuz* base amount. As to how long this process continues: *ad keneged ktuvatah*, until the total *ketubah* is reduced to zero. Rabbi Yosei disagrees and says *le'olam hu pofes v'holech* — one continues to reduce even into negative territory, so that she actually incurs a debt to her husband. The practical significance of this is that *she'im tipol lah yerusha mimakom acher* — if she ever comes into money from another source — *goveh mimena*, he can collect from her.

### Mored Reciprocal

The Mishnah then turns to the reciprocal case: *v'chein hamored al ishto*, a husband who rebels against his wife. Here the remedy runs in the opposite direction: *mosifin al kesuvatah*, we increase the amount of her *ketubah*. The practical impact, however, is similarly deferred — it is only felt when the *ketubah* becomes payable. The rate of increase is *sheloshah dinarim b'shabbos*, three *dinar* per week — less than half the rate applied to the wife. Rabbi Yehudah sets his rate at three *tarpeikin*. Why the disparity between the fine imposed on her and the one imposed on him? The Gemara will address this, but the asymmetry is already striking.

## Sex vs. Labor Debate

### Rav Huna: Sex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מוֹרֶדֶת מִמַּאי? רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: מִתַּשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: מִמְּלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Against what** does **she rebel;** what is the nature of the rebellion discussed in the mishna? **Rav Huna said: Against** engaging in **marital relations. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: Against** the **tasks** she is obligated to perform for her husband.

Before the Gemara addresses the monetary mechanics, it raises a prior question: *moredet mimah* — in what domain is the wife rebelling? Rav Huna says: *mi-tashmish hamitah* — she is refusing sexual relations. This is noteworthy, because the earlier Mishnah never explicitly stated that she has an obligation to have sex with her husband; it was simply assumed. If the Mishnah already detailed his obligation to have sex with her at regular intervals, it implicitly took her reciprocal obligation for granted. What she is now refusing, on Rav Huna's reading, is precisely that.

### R. Yose bar Chanina: Labor

Rabbi Yose bar Chanina says: *mim'lachah* — she is refusing to perform her domestic duties, the very obligations detailed at length in the preceding Mishnah, such as cooking, baking, and laundry.

### Tosafot Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: וְכֵן הַמּוֹרֵד עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִתַּשְׁמִישׁ, לְחַיֵּי. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִמְּלָאכָה, מִי מְשׁוּעְבָּד לַהּ? אִין, בְּאוֹמֵר ״אֵינִי זָן וְאֵינִי מְפַרְנֵס״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this dispute. **The mishna states: Similarly, if** a man **rebels against his wife. Granted, according to the one who says** that the rebellion is **against marital relations, it is well,** as this type of rebellion can apply equally to a husband. **However, according to the one who says** that she rebels **against** performing **tasks, is he subjugated to her** to perform tasks? The Gemara answers: **Yes,** he is, as the mishna is discussing **someone who says: I will not sustain and I will not support** my wife.

Tosafot raises an important question: why not simply say that she can be a *moredet* with respect to either responsibility? The answer, Tosafot suggests, may lie in a position encountered earlier: *yacholah ishah shetomar l'baalah eini nizonas v'eini osah* — a woman is entitled to say to her husband, "I will not be supported by you, and I will not perform domestic labor for you." If that is her right, then refusing her *ma'aseh yadayim* is not a rebellion at all — she is simply exercising a recognized option. On this reading, Rav Huna holds that the only thing she can genuinely rebel against is sex, since refusing *melacha* while waiving *mezonot* is a legitimate choice, not a transgression. Rabbi Yose bar Chanina, by contrast, holds that this option belongs to the husband, not the wife — it is his right to say he will not feed her and she need not work, but she cannot unilaterally declare that arrangement. Even if she offers to waive her *mezonot*, she is still obligated to perform her domestic duties, and refusing them constitutes a genuine *moredet*.

## Mored Parallel Case

### If Sex: Parallel Sex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב: הָאוֹמֵר ״אֵינִי זָן וְאֵינִי מְפַרְנֵס״ — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה! וְלָאו לְאִמְּלוֹכֵי בֵּיהּ בָּעֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rav say: One who says: I will not sustain and I will not support** my wife **must** immediately **divorce** her **and give her** the payment for her **marriage contract?** What relevance is there to a discussion of a weekly fine? The Gemara answers: **Shouldn't he be consulted** to investigate whether he will retract his decision? In the interim, while the court discusses the issue with him and explains that he must divorce his wife if he does not retract his decision, he is fined by the addition of three dinars per week to her marriage contract.

The Gemara now asks how the reciprocal case — the *mored* husband — fits into each position. *Bishlama l'man d'amar mi-tashmish* — if we are speaking about sex refusal, the parallel case is straightforward: the husband is refusing to have sex with his wife. That makes perfect sense as a mirror image.

### If Labor: Parallel Support

*Ela l'man d'amar mimelocha* — if the *moredet* is one who refuses domestic labor, what is the parallel for the husband? He has no comparable domestic labor obligation to her. The Gemara answers: *be-omer eini zan v'eini mefarnes* — the case is one where he says he will refuse to feed her and support her. What does she receive in exchange for her *ma'aseh yadayim*? His *mezonot*. So if she rebels by withholding her labor, the parallel is his withholding her sustenance.

### Rav: Immediate Divorce

*Ha-omer eini zan v'eini mefarnes yotzi v'yitein kesuvah* — a husband who says he will refuse to provide food and support must immediately divorce his wife and pay the *ketubah*. If that is so, there would seem to be no room for a drawn-out process of weekly increases to the *ketubah* — the obligation is immediate.

### Gemara Resolution

*Lo l'mimluchi beih bayinan* — we still need to discuss the matter with him. Even if the goal is for him to give a *get*, the practical process of bringing that about may take some weeks. During that period, while the beit din is working toward compelling the divorce, the *ketubah* amount continues to increase. If he changes his behavior in the interim, all the better, but the orientation is not to invest a year or two hoping he will reform — it is to move toward the *get* expeditiously, while leaving a short window during which his behavior might change.

This question — how quickly a rabbinic court should move to dissolve a troubled marriage versus how long it should work to preserve it — is not merely an academic one. It maps directly onto contemporary disputes about the proper role of *batei din* when one party is clearly not meeting their obligations. Too often, courts invoke the rubric of *shalom bayit* to delay resolution in situations that are plainly untenable, when a more direct reading of the Gemara would counsel moving promptly toward the *get*.

One might also ask what exactly is meant by *eini zan v'eini mefarnes*. Rashi understands it to mean the husband is refusing to pay the food bills. Tosafot raises the question of whether it might also mean he refuses to go out and earn a living at all. Tosafot cites Rabbeinu Eliyahu, who argues that a husband is indeed obligated to hire himself out — even to work as a schoolteacher, which was considered a low-prestige occupation — in order to provide his wife's *mezonot*: *mikan mochi'ach Rabbeinu Eliyahu shechayav adam l'haskir atzmo u'l'lamed tinokos o la'asos melacha acheres kedei lazon es ishto*. Rashi, however, understands the obligation only as paying the bills from whatever resources he has. Tosafot then cites Rabbeinu Tam, who limits the work obligation to farming one's own land — *avodas hakarka*, *eflach* as in plowing and tending fields — but stops short of requiring a man to hire himself out to someone else: *aval l'haskir atzmo eino chayav*. The distinction reflects a society in which working one's own land and hiring oneself out for labor were understood as categorically different in terms of dignity and social standing. The underlying question — whether a wife can demand that her husband stop being a deadbeat and go find employment — is very much embedded in the language of the *ketubah* contract itself, with its phrase *v'ana eflach*, "and I will work."

## Rabbis' Enactment

### Four-Week Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבּוֹתֵינוּ חָזְרוּ וְנִמְנוּ, שֶׁיְּהוּ מַכְרִיזִין עָלֶיהָ אַרְבַּע שַׁבָּתוֹת זוֹ אַחַר זוֹ, וְשׁוֹלְחִין לָהּ בֵּית דִּין: הֱוִי יוֹדַעַת שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ כְּתוּבָּתִיךְ מֵאָה מָנֶה הִפְסַדְתְּ. אַחַת לִי אֲרוּסָה וּנְשׂוּאָה, אֲפִילּוּ נִדָּה, אֲפִילּוּ חוֹלָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם.
>
> **Translation:** Our Sages went back and were counted again,** meaning they voted and decided that instead of deducting a small amount from her marriage contract each week, **they would make** public **announcements about her for four consecutive *Shabbatot*. And** they decided **that the court would send** messengers **to her** to inform her: **Be aware that even if your marriage contract is worth ten thousand dinars, you will lose** it all if you continue your rebellion. If she does not retract her rebellion, she forfeits her entire marriage contract. With regard to this enactment, **it is the same to me,** meaning the *halakha* does not change, if she is **a betrothed woman or a married woman,** and **even** if she is **a menstruating woman, and even** if she is **ill, and even** if she is **a widow awaiting her *yavam*** to perform levirate marriage.

The Gemara now introduces a later ruling by the Sages. *Raboseinu chazru v'nimnu* — the rabbis reconsidered and voted — to replace the gradual week-by-week reduction of the *ketubah* with a different mechanism: *machrizin aleiha arba shabbatot zu achar zu*, they make public announcements for four consecutive weeks. At the end of that process, the woman loses the entire *ketubah* at once, however large it may be. The beit din sends her a formal notification: *hevei yoda'at she'afilu kesuvatech me'ah maneh hafsadta* — know that even if your *ketubah* is worth a hundred *maneh*, you have forfeited it. The ברייתא, *braita*, quoted here applies this ruling to a *moredet* who is an *arusah*, a *nesua*, even a *niddah*, even one who is ill, and even a *shomeret yavam*.

This shift from the Mishnah's gradual process to the four-week announcement reflects a decisional judgment — whether strategic, role-based, or diagnostic — that a drawn-out progressive fine is not the right tool. Whether the reasoning is that the rabbis do not see marriage counseling as their function, or that they believe a sufficiently severe and time-limited ultimatum is more effective, or that a marriage which has reached this point is unlikely to recover in any case, the result is a binary choice: change within four weeks, or lose everything and the *get* follows.

The announcements are made in public — *ein machrizin aleha ella bebatei knesiyot uvebatei midrashot* — in synagogues and study halls, plainly using social pressure and public shame as part of the mechanism. One notes the asymmetry: it is not clear from the sources that equivalent public announcements are made when the husband is the *mored*. As Rav Ameimar bar Chama notes, the choice of synagogues is deliberate: since the announcements are to be made on four consecutive *Shabbatot*, and *Shabbos* is when the public is gathered in those spaces, the two facts cohere — the four *shabbatot* of the *braita* are actual Sabbaths, not merely weeks, precisely because that is when the communities assemble.

### When to Engage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: פַּעֲמַיִם שׁוֹלְחִין לָהּ מִבֵּית דִּין, אַחַת קוֹדֶם הַכְרָזָה, וְאַחַת לְאַחַר הַכְרָזָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said: The court sends** people to talk **with her twice, once before the announcement and once after the announcement.**

A dispute arises regarding when during this four-week process the beit din should directly engage the woman. Rav Ami bar Chama teaches that the beit din sends her two notifications: *achas kodem hachraza v'achas achar hachraza* — one before the announcement process begins, and one after it concludes. This represents a middle position: the court is not merely making passive public announcements and waiting; it actively reaches out to her at both ends of the process. Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda publicly declared that the *halacha* follows *raboseinu* — which, on Tosafot's reading, means we reach out only at the end, not at the beginning.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבּוֹתֵינוּ. אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי בּוּרְכָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאי בּוּרְכָתֵיהּ? אֲנָא אַמְרִיתַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, וּמִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּגַבְרָא רַבָּה אַמְרִיתַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, וּמַנּוּ, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא. וְאִיהוּ כְּמַאן סָבַר? כִּי הָא דְּאִתְּמַר: רָבָא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הֲלָכָה, נִמְלָכִין בָּהּ. רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הֲלָכָה, אֵין נִמְלָכִין בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Rav Ḥisda taught** with regard to this: **The *halakha* is in accordance with** the decision of **our Sages. Rava said: This is an absurdity [*burkha*]. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said to him: What is absurd about this? I said this to him, and I said it to him in the name of a great man. And who is** the great man who ruled this way? **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina. And in accordance with whose** opinion does Rava **hold?** The Gemara answers: **In accordance with that which was stated: Rava** said that **Rav Sheshet said:** The ***halakha* is** that **she is consulted** in an attempt to convince her to retract her decision, and while doing so deductions are made from her marriage contract. But **Rav Huna bar Yehuda** said that **Rav Sheshet said: The *halakha* is** that **we do not consult with her.** According to both versions of Rav Sheshet's ruling, she does not lose her marriage contract immediately but rather it is reduced every week. This is the source for Rava's opinion.

Rava objects to Rav Nachman's ruling, calling it *burcha* — foolish. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak responds that the ruling was not original to Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda but was transmitted from Rabbi Yose bar Chanina, a figure of considerable standing. The underlying dispute is then clarified through a further citation: *Rava amar Rav Sheshet, halacha nimlachim bah* — the *halacha* is that we do engage her, that we actively deliberate with her during the process. *Rav Huna bar Yehuda amar Rav Sheshet, halacha ein nimlachim bah* — no, we do not engage her at all; we make the public announcements, give her fair warning through those announcements, and then the *get* is given. There is no direct conversation with her from the beit din.

The result is three distinct positions along a spectrum: the Mishnah's drawn-out progressive fine over potentially many months or years; the four-week process with active engagement from the beit din at the start; and the four-week process with no direct engagement at all — announcements only, followed by loss of *ketubah* and *get*. Each reflects a different understanding of the court's role and its assessment of whether behavioral change in such cases is achievable.

## Ma'is Alai Case

### Ameimar Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמְיָא מוֹרֶדֶת? אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: דְּאָמְרָה ״בָּעֵינָא לֵיהּ וּמְצַעַרְנָא לֵיהּ״. אֲבָל אָמְרָה ״מְאִיס עֲלַי״ — לָא כָּיְיפִינַן לַהּ. מָר זוּטְרָא אָמַר: כָּיְיפִינַן לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to this *halakha*, the Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which the *halakha* of **a rebellious** woman applies? **Ameimar said:** The case is where **she says: I want** to be married to **him, but** I am currently refusing him because **I want to cause him anguish** due to a dispute between us. **However,** if **she said: I am disgusted with him, we do not compel her** to remain with him, as one should not be compelled to live with someone who disgusts her. **Mar Zutra said: We** do **compel her** to stay with him.

Having established the various procedural approaches to the *moredet*, the Gemara now introduces a critical distinction made by Ameimar. The cases discussed so far all involve a woman who is refusing relations or domestic labor but who has not said that the marriage itself is intolerable — she is using refusal as leverage, whether out of anger or some marital dispute. Ameimar describes this as *de-amra be'ina lei u-metza'arna lei* — she is saying, in effect, "I do want to be with him, but I am making him suffer." In such a case, there is reason to believe that her behavior can change — she is angry, she has not abandoned the marriage in principle, and therefore the processes described above make sense.

### Rav Sheshet vs. Mar Zutra

Ameimar distinguishes this from the case of *ma'is alai* — where the woman says she finds her husband repulsive and will never be able to live with him again. In this case, *lo kaifinan lah* — we do not employ financial penalties to try to force a change in her behavior. Mar Zutra, however, disagrees: *amar Mar Zutra, kaifinan lah* — even in the case of *ma'is alai*, we impose financial penalties and attempt to compel her to continue the marriage.

It is worth pausing to appreciate how disturbing Mar Zutra's position is. We are speaking of a woman who has declared that she finds her husband completely intolerable, that she cannot live with him, that she will never have sexual relations with him again. Mar Zutra's position is that we should apply financial pressure to coerce her back into the marriage — and into the marital bed — simply because sufficient financial threat might break her refusal. Even granting the most charitable interpretation — that Mar Zutra believes her attitude, not merely her behavior, might genuinely change — the posture of compelling a woman to have sexual relations with a man she explicitly finds repulsive is profoundly troubling. The language of Rashi's understanding of *lo kaifinan lah* is telling: *lo kaifinan lah lish'osei ela notnin lah get ve-yotzei be-lo ketubah* — we do not force her to remain with him; rather, the *get* is given and she leaves without her *ketubah*. That is the position the Gemara ultimately accepts.

### Mar Zutra Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא וְאַכְפְּיַהּ מָר זוּטְרָא, וּנְפַק מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא מִסּוּרָא. וְלָא הִיא: הָתָם סִיַּיעְתָּא דִשְׁמַיָּא הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is related: **There was an incident** in which a woman rebelled, claiming that she was disgusted with her husband, **and Mar Zutra compelled her** to stay with him. **And from this** couple **issued Rabbi Ḥanina of Sura.** This demonstrates that even such coercion can cause a blessing. However, the Gemara concludes: **That is not so.** That case should not serve as a precedent, as **there** the positive outcome **was** due to **heavenly assistance.** Ordinarily, nothing good results from conjugal relations that the wife does not desire.

The Gemara records that Mar Zutra actually acted on his ruling: *hava uvda ve-achfa Mar Zutra* — there was a case and Mar Zutra compelled the woman to remain with her husband even in the case of *ma'is alai*. The outcome was that *ve-nafik minei Rabbi Chanina mi-Sura* — the great Rabbi Chanina from Sura was born from her having been compelled to continue that marriage. The implication is that perhaps we should always force couples to remain together in such cases, since great things can emerge from it.

The Gemara immediately dismisses this: *ve-lav hi, hatam siyata di-shmaya hava* — that cannot be used as proof; what happened there was a specially ordained divine intervention. The Gemara's rejection is striking in its reasoning. Whether one reads a case as evidence for a general rule or as a miraculous exception depends entirely on one's prior orientation. If one begins from the premise that we should keep marriages together at all costs, the story of Rabbi Chanina from Sura looks like compelling evidence. If one begins from the premise that a woman who says *ma'is alai* is describing an intolerable situation, the same story is read as an inexplicable exception from which no normative conclusions may be drawn. The Gemara chooses the latter reading.

### Agunah Implications

The more consequential question that the *ma'is alai* discussion opens up is not merely whether we impose financial penalties on the woman, but whether, once beit din declares *lo kaifinan lah*, we also compel the husband to give a *get*. The Gemara itself does not address this directly. The situation it describes — we will not try to force her to change, you are entitled to divorce her without paying the *ketubah* — leaves open the scenario in which the husband responds: I do not want to divorce her. I am content to remain married even without sexual relations. I know she will lose her *ketubah* if I eventually choose to give the *get*, but I choose not to.

Tosafot addresses this gap directly. One opinion holds that *kofin oto lehotzi* — we compel him to give the *get*. Rabbeinu Tam rejects this: *denichos shema einah nassan b'acherin* — perhaps she is only claiming *ma'is alai* because she has her eye on someone else. Allowing her declaration alone to trigger compelled divorce would fundamentally shift the balance of marital power, enabling a woman to exit any marriage she chose simply by declaring it intolerable, forfeiting the *ketubah* but gaining freedom. Rabbeinu Tam views this as an unacceptable result.

The Rambam, however, rules that if a woman says *ma'is alai*, we do compel the husband to give a *get*. The Rambam employs the formulation drawn from the Gemara: *ein adam dar bekhfifah im hanachash* — one cannot be forced to live under the same roof as a serpent. If a person finds their partner genuinely impossible to live with, beit din does not force cohabitation. On the Rambam's ruling, the woman's declaration of *ma'is alai* therefore generates a compellable obligation on the husband to divorce her.

The *Rishonei Ashkenaz*, led by Rabbeinu Tam, disagree, and in Ashkenazic practice the Rambam's position has not been accepted. The practical consequences are enormous: the rejection of the Rambam's ruling is one of the pillars of the contemporary *agunah* crisis. If a woman could say *ma'is alai* and thereby trigger a beit din's compulsion of the *get*, the *agunah* problem — in which a husband refuses indefinitely to give a *get* — would be substantially resolved. The Ashkenazic response invokes *shalom bayit* and the concern of *shema natna eineha b'acher*, that she may simply want out of the marriage for her own reasons, as grounds for withholding that power from the woman.

Some historians have argued that the Rambam's ruling was partly shaped by the social reality he faced: women in his milieu were converting to Islam in order to escape intolerable marriages, since the Muslim legal system did not recognize their prior marriage and allowed them to remarry. Faced with women resorting to apostasy as the only practical exit, the Rambam may have been additionally motivated to establish a legitimate halakhic solution. If a beit din would actually compel the *get* upon a declaration of *ma'is alai*, conversion would be unnecessary. The observation connects the medieval context to a perennial question about rabbinic jurisprudence: how much does the practical urgency of the situation on the ground inform the willingness to employ halakhic tools that are formally available but rarely used?

What is clear from the Gemara itself is that the entire range of questions — how long to work with the *moredet*, how quickly to move toward *get*, whether to engage her directly or only through announcements, and what to do when she declares the marriage intolerable — reflects a deep and unresolved tension between the court's protective role, its interest in preserving marriages, and its responsibility to the individuals trapped in untenable situations.

## Ketubah Loss Details

### Clothes & Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּלְּתֵיהּ דְּרַב זְבִיד אִימְּרַדָא, הֲוָה תְּפִיסָא חַד שֵׁירָא. יְתֵיב אַמֵּימָר וּמָר זוּטְרָא וְרַב אָשֵׁי, וִיתֵיב רַב גַּמָּדָא גַּבַּיְיהוּ. יָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: מָרְדָה — הִפְסִידָה בְּלָאוֹתֶיהָ קַיָּימִין. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב גַּמָּדָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב זְבִיד גַּבְרָא רַבָּה, מַחְנִיפִיתוּ לֵיהּ? וְהָאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מִיבַּעְיָא בָּעֵי רָבָא, וְלָא פְּשִׁיט? אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, יָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי, מָרְדָה — לֹא הִפְסִידָה בְּלָאוֹתֶיהָ קַיָּימִין. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב גַּמָּדָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב זְבִיד גַּבְרָא רַבָּה הוּא, אָפְכִיתוּ לֵיהּ לְדִינָא עִילָּוֵיהּ? הָאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מִיבַּעְיָא בָּעֵי לַהּ רָבָא, וְלָא פְּשִׁיט?! הַשְׁתָּא דְּלָא אִתְּמַר לָא הָכִי וְלָא הָכִי, תְּפַסָה — לָא מַפְּקִינַן מִינַּהּ, לָא תְּפַסָה — לָא יָהֲבִינַן לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **the daughter-in-law of Rav Zevid rebelled** against her husband. **She was holding a certain garment** in her hands. **Ameimar, Mar Zutra, and Rav Ashi were sitting, and Rav Gamda was sitting with them. They sat and said:** If a woman **rebelled, she lost** her right to **her worn clothes,** meaning she has forfeited the clothes she brought with her for her dowry even if they are still in existence. **Rav Gamda said to them: Because Rav Zevid is a great man,** are **you** willing to **flatter him** with regard to this *halakha*? **Didn't Rav Kahana say: Rava raises a dilemma** about this issue with regard to worn clothes, **and he did not resolve it?** Yet you reached a decision out of respect for Rav Zevid. This is inappropriate. **There are** those **who say** that this incident happened differently, as these three Sages **sat and said:** If she **rebelled, she did not lose** her right to **her worn clothes. Rav Gamda said to them: Because Rav Zevid is a great man,** and due to his piety and humility he would not challenge the ruling, **you twist the judgment against him? Didn't Rav Kahana say: Rava raises a dilemma** with regard to this issue **and did not resolve** it, so how did you rule that she may retain her worn clothes? The Gemara summarizes: **Now that it was not stated** and concluded **this way or that way, if she seized** an item of her possessions, **we do not take it away from her,** but if she **did not seize** it, **we do not give** it to **her.**

The Gemara now turns to a practical question arising from the case of Rav Zevid's daughter-in-law, who rebelled against her husband and was in possession of a garment at the time. Ameimar, Mar Zutra, and Rav Ashi were convened as a beit din, with Rav Gamda sitting among them. The question before them: when a *moredet* loses her entire *ketubah*, does that include the worn garments that are in her possession — *belosei ha-kayamim*, literally the existing worn clothes? On one version of the text, they ruled that she does lose even those. On an alternate version, they ruled she does not lose the worn clothes in her closet. In both versions, Rav Gamda challenges the panel, suggesting in the first that they are ruling too harshly against her out of deference to the eminence of Rav Zevid, and in the second that they are ruling too leniently in his favor for the same reason.

The underlying question — whether the worn clothes in her closet are part of the *ketubah* settlement she forfeits — was one that Rava raised but did not resolve. The practical consequence of that unresolved question: *tafsa lo mafkinan minah* — if she has those clothes in her possession, we do not take them from her, since she may be entitled to them; *lo tafsa lo yahavinan lah* — if she does not have them in hand, we do not give them to her. The default is determined by the status quo of physical possession.

The background to this dispute involves the nature of the dowry clothes. Rashi explains that these are garments she brought into the marriage as part of her trousseau, the value of which was recorded in the *ketubah*. He takes possession of them upon marriage, but she is entitled to their residual value upon dissolution. If she loses the entire *ketubah*, she loses that claim as well.

### Alternative Timelines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְשַׁהֵינַן לַהּ תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא אַגִּיטָּא, וּבְהָנָךְ תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא לֵית לַהּ מְזוֹנֵי מִבַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds another *halakha* with regard to a rebellious woman: **And we delay her bill of divorce** for **twelve months** of **the year** and do not give her a bill of divorce until then. **And during those twelve months** of **the year she does not** receive **sustenance from** her **husband.**

A further variant ruling is recorded: *umashinan lah tresar yarchei shata agita* — we actually extend the process for twelve months rather than four weeks. During those twelve months, however, *leis lah mezoni miba'al* — she receives no *mezonot* from her husband. This represents yet another point on the spectrum of possible approaches: a longer time horizon to allow for change, but with an additional financial disincentive layered on, removing her food support for the duration.

### Post-Talmud Practice

Tosafot, at the beginning of 63b, records a significant post-Talmudic development. Rabbeinu Yehudah found in the writings of Rav Sherira Gaon that the Geonim enacted *shetekenu beyamav latet get le'ishah la'altar* — they ruled in his time to give the *get* to the woman immediately, without waiting four weeks or twelve months. This mirrors what was said earlier about the husband who is *mored* — immediate *get* without extended process. Tosafot further records that the *Rabbanan Savorai* — the transitional authorities between the Talmudic period and the Geonic era — enacted that we also reclaim whatever clothing she had taken from her closet and give her the *get* at once.

The comparison raises an interesting question about which approach is ultimately more favorable to the woman. The drawn-out process of the Mishnah or the four-week Talmudic process, especially if she is engaged at the beginning, gives her time to reconsider and an opportunity to preserve the marriage if she chooses. The immediate *get* of the Geonic enactment is faster, but it also means she exits with nothing, no *ketubah*, no clothes, no transitional period. The four-week process with beit din engagement — particularly where she is reached out to at the start — may in fact represent the most balanced of the available approaches.

## Arusah & Yavam

### Arusah vs. Yavam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב טוֹבִי בַּר קִיסְנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כּוֹתְבִין אִגֶּרֶת מָרֶד עַל אֲרוּסָה, וְאֵין כּוֹתְבִין אִגֶּרֶת מָרֶד עַל שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Tuvi bar Kisna said** that **Shmuel said:** The court **writes a letter of rebellion about a betrothed woman** who is rebelling against her husband. This letter is a court order to deduct value from the marriage contract. **But it does not write a letter of rebellion about a widow awaiting her *yavam*** who does not want to enter into levirate marriage.

Rav Tuvi bar Kisna, citing Shmuel, rules: *kotevin iggeret mered al arusah ve-ein kotevin iggeret mered al shomeret yavam* — we write a writ of rebellion against an *arusah* but not against a *shomeret yavam*. The reasoning is that an *arusah*, even though she is not yet in a stage where she would be obligated to have sexual relations, has made a declaration that she will refuse to do so — or refuse to proceed to *nisuim* — when the time comes, and this is sufficient to trigger the process. A *shomeret yavam*, by contrast, is not yet in a stage where these obligations apply, and we therefore do not initiate the process against her.

### Braitah Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אַחַת לִי אֲרוּסָה וּנְשׂוּאָה, אֲפִילּוּ נִדָּה, אֲפִילּוּ חוֹלָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **It is the same to me** if she is **a betrothed woman or a married woman,** and **even** if she is **a menstruating woman,** and **even** if she is **ill, and even** if she is **a widow awaiting her *yavam*.**

The Gemara raises a challenge: the *braita* cited earlier specifically listed the *shomeret yavam* as one of the cases to which the *moredet* process applies — *achat le-arusah u-le-nesuah va-afilu niddah va-afilu cholah va-afilu shomeret yavam*. That seems to contradict Shmuel's ruling.

### Who Demands?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן שֶׁתָּבַע הוּא, כָּאן שֶׁתָּבְעָה הִיא. דְּאָמַר רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר אֲבִימִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תָּבַע הוּא — נִזְקָקִין לוֹ. תָּבְעָה הִיא — אֵין נִזְקָקִין לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** is **not difficult,** as the contradiction can be resolved in the following way: **Here,** where there is no distinction between a betrothed woman and a widow awaiting her *yavam*, it refers to a case where **he asked** to marry **her** and she is refusing; **there,** where there is a distinction, the case is where **she asked** to marry **him** and he is refusing. **As Rav Taḥalifa bar Avimi said** that **Shmuel said: If he asked,** the court **responds to his** request and gives her the status of a rebellious woman, **but if she asked, it does not respond to her** request and does not add to her marriage contract.

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya, kan she-tava hu, kan she-tav'ah hi* — there is no difficulty; the cases differ based on who is making the demand. When the man is the one asserting his right and the woman refuses, that is the case of the *braita* — we write the writ against the *shomeret yavam*. When she is the one demanding and he is refusing, that is Shmuel's case — we do not write the writ on her behalf.

This is confirmed by Rav Tachlifa bar Avimi citing Shmuel: *tava hu, nizkakim lo* — if he demands and she refuses, beit din attends to him and applies financial pressure on her; *tav'ah hi, ein nizkakim lo* — if she demands and he refuses, we do not attend to her demand. The Gemara's adjustment of Shmuel's formulation — reading *le-arusah* in the sense of the *braita* being written for her rather than against her — resolves the textual difficulty while preserving the substantive ruling.

## Yibum vs. Chalitzah

### No Yibum Compulsion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם דְּלָא — דְּאָמְרִינַן לַהּ: זִיל, לָא מִפַּקְּדַתְּ, אֲרוּסָה נָמֵי, נֵימָא לַהּ: זִיל, לָא מִפַּקְּדַתְּ! אֶלָּא בְּבָאָה מֵחֲמַת טַעֲנָה, דְּאָמְרָה: בָּעֵינָא חוּטְרָא לִידָא וּמָרָה לִקְבוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **a widow awaiting her *yavam*,** for whom a letter of rebellion is not written against her husband? **Because we say to her: Go away; you are not commanded** to procreate. Therefore, although she cannot get married, he cannot be compelled to perform an act that the Torah does not specifically command him to perform. The Gemara challenges this answer: If this is the reasoning, then in the case of **a betrothed woman, too, let us say to her: Go away; you are not commanded. Rather,** the case where a letter of rebellion is issued must be referring to a woman who **comes with a claim, saying: I want a staff in** my **hand and a hoe for burial,** i.e., I want children who will support me in my old age and attend to my burial after my death. This claim is valid, and therefore the court issues a letter of rebellion against the husband.

Having established that the question of who is making the demand determines whether beit din intervenes, the Gemara turns to the broader question of *yibum* versus *chalitzah*. The Gemara asks: why do we not compel a *shomeret yavam* to accept *yibum* when the *yavam* demands it? The answer: *de-amrinan lah, zil lo mifkadet* — we tell her, go, you are not commanded in *peru u-revu*. He has a *mitzvah* she does not share, and therefore his claim to compel her cooperation is stronger than hers would be in the reverse situation. She, for her part, can simply allow the relationship to remain unresolved or proceed to *chalitzah*.

### Chalitzah Claim

But if a woman cannot compel *yibum*, can she at least compel *chalitzah* when she demands it and he refuses? Here the Gemara introduces a striking argument: *de-amra, ba'ina chutra leyada u-mara likvura* — she says, "I want a staff for my hand and a shovel for my grave," meaning she wants children to care for her in old age and to bury her when she dies. She does not need to be commanded in *peru u-revu* to have a legitimate claim to children. This gives her standing to demand resolution of her situation — whether through *yibum* or *chalitzah* — even without the formal obligation that applies to him. The Gemara affirms that this same claim applies to the *shomeret yavam*: *hachi nami shomeret yavam de-ba machmas ta'ana* — when she comes with a legitimate claim, we do attend to her.

### Historical Preference Shift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי פְּדָת אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תָּבַע לַחְלוֹץ — נִזְקָקִין לוֹ. תָּבַע לְיַיבֵּם — אֵין נִזְקָקִין לוֹ. מַאי שְׁנָא לְיַיבֵּם דְּלָא, דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: זִיל וּנְסֵיב אִיתְּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי. לַחְלוֹץ נָמֵי נֵימָא לֵיהּ: זִיל וּנְסֵיב אִיתְּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי! אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר: ״כֵּיוָן דַּאֲגִידָא בִּי, לָא קָא יָהֲבוּ לִי אַחֲרִיתִי״. הָכָא נָמֵי: ״כֵּיוָן דַּאֲגִידָא בִּי, לָא קָא יָהֲבוּ לִי אַחֲרִיתִי״! אֶלָּא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי שֶׁתָּבַע לְיַיבֵּם, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — כְּמִשְׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, כָּאן — כְּמִשְׁנָה אַחֲרוֹנָה. דִּתְנַן: מִצְוַת יִבּוּם קוֹדֶמֶת לְמִצְוַת חֲלִיצָה — בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, שֶׁהָיוּ מִתְכַּוְּונִין לְשׁוּם מִצְוָה. עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוְּונִין לְשׁוּם מִצְוָה, אָמְרוּ: מִצְוַת חֲלִיצָה קוֹדֶמֶת לְמִצְוַת יִבּוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Pedat said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: If** the *yavam* **asked her to perform *ḥalitza*** and she refused, the court **responds to him. If he asked to perform levirate marriage,** the court **does not respond to him. What is different** with regard to the request **to consummate the levirate marriage, that** if a woman refuses the court **does not** write a letter of rebellion against her? **Because we say to him: Go and marry another woman.** He is not required to marry her specifically, if she does not agree to the marriage. Therefore, her refusal is not deemed rebellion. The Gemara challenges that answer: If so, with regard to a request **to perform *ḥalitza* also, let us say to him: Go and marry another woman.** The difference between the two cases is still not clear. **Rather,** it must be that the reason is because **he says: Since she is attached to me they will not give me another** wife. As long as he has not performed *ḥalitza*, he may have a problem finding another wife, as a potential wife will be concerned that he has a woman attached to him and may eventually enter levirate marriage with him. This is a valid claim, and therefore the court writes a letter of rebellion against her if she refuses *ḥalitza*. The Gemara asks: If so, **here too,** when she refuses a request to consummate the levirate marriage, he may say: **Since she is attached to me they will not give me another.** Why then doesn't the court write a letter of rebellion in this case? **Rather,** one must say that **this and that** are both discussing a case **where he asked to consummate the levirate marriage. And it** is **not difficult. Here,** in the *baraita*, where the court writes a letter of rebellion, it is **in accordance** with **the first mishna. There,** in Shmuel's statement, where it doesn't write one, it is **in accordance** with **the ultimate** version of the **mishna. As we learned** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 13a): **The mitzva of levirate marriage precedes the mitzva of *ḥalitza*.** This *halakha* **originally** applied **when** people **would intend** to perform the levirate marriage **for the sake of the mitzva.** At that time, it was customary to compel a woman to enter levirate marriage. If she refused, the court wrote a letter of rebellion about her. However, **now that** people **do not intend** to enter levirate marriage **for the sake of the mitzva,** but may have other intentions, the Sages **said: The mitzva of *ḥalitza* precedes the mitzva of levirate marriage.** Shmuel's statement that the court does not write a letter of rebellion about a widow awaiting her *yavam* is in accordance with the ultimate version of the mishna.

The Gemara then introduces a crucial historical distinction, cited from Rav Pada in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *tava lachalotz nizkakim lo* — if the *yavam* demands *chalitzah* and she refuses, beit din compels her; *tava le-yabbem, ein nizkakim lo* — if he demands *yibum* and she refuses, beit din does not compel her. The reason for preferring *chalitzah* and not compelling *yibum* is given through the teaching of the Mishnah: *mitzvas yibum kodemes lemitzvas chalitzah* — originally, *yibum* took precedence over *chalitzah*, because *be-rishona she-hayu miskavnim leshum mitzvah* — in earlier times, *yibum* was performed for the sake of the *mitzvah* itself. *Achshav she-ein miskavnim leshum mitzvah, amru mitzvas chalitzah kodemes* — now that it is no longer performed with that sincere motivation, we rule that *chalitzah* takes precedence. And since we favor *chalitzah*, when he demands *yibum* against her will, we do not compel her compliance.

### Enforcement Measures

The Gemara addresses the apparent inconsistency: if she can refuse *yibum* and he is told to go marry someone else, why can she not similarly refuse *chalitzah* and he be told the same thing? He responds: *kivan de-agida bi, lo ka yahvi li achriti* — since she is bound to him, no one will agree to marry him; the unresolved status of the *shomeret yavam* effectively bars him from remarriage. The same argument should apply whether she refuses *yibum* or *chalitzah*. The Gemara ultimately resolves this by distinguishing between the earlier and later Mishnah positions on which is preferred: under the initial ruling, *yibum* was favored and we would have compelled her; under the later ruling, we prefer *chalitzah* and therefore we do not compel *yibum* even when he demands it, while we do compel *chalitzah* when he demands it.

The final *Tosafot* of the sugya draws the parallel explicitly: just as the discussion of *ma'is alai* raised the question of what measures beit din may use to compel a husband to give a *get* when the wife declares the marriage intolerable, the analogous question here is what measures may be used to compel a *yavam* to give *chalitzah* when the woman demands it and he refuses — or worse, when he refuses both *yibum* and *chalitzah* and simply leaves her in limbo. The woman is then precisely in the position of an *agunah*: bound to him, unable to remarry, with no resolution in sight. The tools available to the court in both scenarios — and the willingness to deploy them — remain very much an open and contested question.
