# Zevachim 85 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Imurim Before Zrika

### Ulla's Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: אֵימוּרֵי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים שֶׁהֶעֱלָן לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמָן – לֹא יֵרְדוּ, נַעֲשׂוּ לַחְמוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § Concerning items that were inappropriately placed on the altar, **Ulla says: Sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity that** one **offered up** upon the altar **before the sprinkling of their blood,** which is the act that sanctifies such portions for the altar, **shall not descend,** as **they have become the bread of** the **altar.**

The daf opens in the midst of the broader discussion of *hamizbeach mekadeshan* — the principle that the altar consecrates whatever is placed upon it, such that even invalid offerings, once on the altar, need not be removed. The preceding material had explored the scope of this rule: what kinds of connection to the altar suffice, and how invalid must something be before the principle ceases to apply. The central dividing line that emerged was *psulo bakodesh* — whether the invalidating defect arose within the sacred context of the Temple service itself, as opposed to something that could have affected the animal while it was still alive.

The Gemara now continues on 85a, six lines from the top, with a statement of Ulla. *Imurei kodshim kalim she'he'elu lifnei zrikas hadam lo yerdu, na'asu lachmo shel mizbeach* — the *imurim* (the sacrificial portions, the fats and organs designated for the altar) of *kodshim kalim* that were placed on the altar before the blood-throwing (*zrikas hadam*) do not come down; they have become the bread of the altar. It is worth noting that Ulla chooses this distinctive phrase — *lachmo shel mizbeach*, the bread of the altar — rather than the standard formulation *hamizbeach mekadeshan*. This shift in language might have suggested that the fire must already have begun consuming them, but Ulla does not say that; that qualification will arise separately later.

### Chiddush: Partial Design…

The *chiddush* here is subtle but significant. One might have thought that just as the *kometz* of a *mincha* before it is placed in a *klei sharet* is not yet fully designated for the altar — and therefore falls outside the protective scope of *hamizbeach mekadeshan* — so too the *imurim* of *kodshim kalim* before *zrikas hadam* should be considered insufficiently designated. And indeed, the analogy is apt in a particular way: by *kodshim kalim*, there is not yet *me'ila* (the prohibition against misappropriating consecrated property) in the *imurim* prior to the blood-throwing, because *kodshim kalim* are fundamentally under the owner's control — the owner will be eating the meat, and the *imurim* only pass fully into God's domain, as it were, after the blood is thrown. Nevertheless, says Ulla, they are sufficiently connected to the altar that once placed upon it, they do not come down. The rule of *hamizbeach mekadeshan* applies even here.

## Rav Zeira's Kal Vachomer

### Rav Zeira's Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: שֶׁנִּשְׁפַּךְ דָּמָהּ, וְשֶׁיָּצָא דָּמָהּ חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים. וּמָה הָתָם – דְּאִם בָּא לִזְרוֹק אֵין לוֹ לִזְרוֹק, אָמַרְתָּ: אִם עָלוּ לֹא יֵרְדוּ; הָכָא דְּאִם בָּא לִזְרוֹק זוֹרֵק – לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** in support of Ulla: **We learn** in the mishna **as well:** The sacrificial portions of an offering **whose blood was spilled, or whose blood emerged outside the curtains,** shall not descend if they ascended. From this one can infer: **And if there, where** the blood was spilled, **if he comes to sprinkle** it **he cannot sprinkle** it, as there is no blood with which to do so, and **you said** that **if they ascended** the altar **they shall not descend,** then **here,** where the blood is intact such **that if he comes to sprinkle** it **he sprinkles** it, which renders the sacrificial portions permitted for sacrifice upon the altar, is it **not all the more so** that if they ascended they shall not descend?

Rav Zeira now seeks to find support for Ulla's ruling from an already-established Mishna. The Mishna records a case — according to Rabbi Shimon, not Rabbi Yehuda — where the blood was spilled or removed outside the Temple precincts and can therefore never be thrown. Even in that scenario, if the *imurim* were placed on the altar, they remain there. Rav Zeira constructs a *kal vachomer*: *uma hatam de'im ba lizrok ein lo lizrok* — in that case, if one wished to throw the blood, one could not do so at all, the animal is entirely beyond repair in this regard, and yet *amarta im alu lo yerdu* — we say that if they were placed on the altar they do not come down. Presumably this applies across all categories of offerings, not distinguishing *kodshei kodashim* from *kodshim kalim*. If so, *hacha de'im ba lizrok zorek* — in Ulla's case, where the blood has simply not yet been thrown but still can be, should the *imurim* not remain on the altar all the more certainly?

### Inference Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ אַקׇּדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the inference of Rabbi Zeira: **Interpret** the mishna as referring specifically **to offerings of the most sacred order,** whose sacrificial portions are sanctified for the sake of the altar before their blood is presented. Therefore, such sacrificial portions are considered fit for the altar and shall not descend if they ascended. It may be that the sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity are entirely unfit for the altar before the presentation of their blood.

The Gemara rejects this inference. *Targema akodshei kodashim* — perhaps the Mishna is speaking exclusively of *kodshei kodashim*. This is important, because by *kodshei kodashim* the *imurim* are fully under divine dominion from the outset — there is *me'ila* immediately — and so the principle of the altar's hold over them is readily understood. By *kodshim kalim*, where the *imurim* are not yet fully God's until *zrikas hadam*, the question remains open. The Mishna cannot be used to prove Ulla's point.

### Pesach Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי פֶּסַח – דְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים הוּא! תַּרְגְּמַהּ בְּשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But there is **a Paschal offering, which is an offering of lesser sanctity,** and it is mentioned in the same list of items that shall not descend if they ascended. The Gemara explains: **Interpret** the case of a Paschal offering as referring **to** where it was slaughtered **not for its sake,** but not to where its blood was spilled or emerged outside the curtains.

The Gemara attempts to demonstrate that the Mishna does address *kodshim kalim*. After all, it explicitly mentions the *pesach* offered *shelo lishmo* as one of its cases, and a *pesach* is *kodshim kalim* — so the Mishna must be dealing with that category as well. The Gemara deflects this: *targema beshelo lishmo* — the *pesach* case refers only to *shelo lishmo*, a disqualification particular to that specific offering. The other cases in the Mishna regarding spilled blood remain limited to *kodshei kodashim*. One cannot fully prove from the Mishna that *kodshim kalim* are covered in the general rule, and Ulla's statement therefore retains its novelty.

## Living Creatures Rule

### All Living Animals Down

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: וְכוּלָּן שֶׁעָלוּ חַיִּין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ – יֵרְדוּ. הָא שְׁחוּטִין – לֹא יֵרְדוּ; מַאי, לָאו לָא שְׁנָא קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים לָא שְׁנָא קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים?
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **And all of them that** if they ascend they do not descend, if **they ascended to** the top of **the altar alive, they descend. But** it may be inferred from here that **slaughtered** animals **shall not descend,** even if their blood was not presented. **What, is it not** that this inference applies to all offerings, and the *halakha* **is not different** with regard to **offerings of the most sacred order, and it is not different** with regard to **offerings of lesser sanctity?**

The Gemara attempts yet another angle. The Mishna concludes with a general statement: *vechulan she'alu legabei mizbeach yerdu* — all of these animals, if brought to the top of the altar while still alive, must be brought down and slaughtered below. The word *vechulan*, "all of them," appears after the mention of the *pesach*, suggesting that the Mishna is speaking broadly, encompassing *kodshim kalim* as well. If so, one might infer from this that if they were slaughtered at the top of the altar, they would stay — including *kodshim kalim* — even with various disqualifications.

### Living vs Slaughtered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; הָא שְׁחוּטִין – מֵהֶן יֵרְדוּ מֵהֶן לֹא יֵרְדוּ. וְהָא ״כּוּלָּן״ קָתָנֵי! ״כּוּלָּם״ אַחַיִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** it is possible to infer as follows: **But** with regard to **slaughtered** animals, some **of them shall descend** and some **of them shall not descend.** The Gemara challenges: **But** the mishna **teaches** that **all of them** shall descend if they ascended alive, in which case it must be inferred that if they were slaughtered they shall not descend. The Gemara explains: When the mishna states: **All of them** shall descend, it is referring only **to** animals that ascended upon the altar while still **alive,** in order to emphasize that all fit animals that ascended while still alive shall descend and are not slaughtered atop the altar. The mishna did not intend to teach the *halakha* with regard to all slaughtered animals.

The Gemara draws a careful distinction. The Mishna's rule about bringing living animals down applies to all animals — *kodshei kodashim* and *kodshim kalim* alike — but this says nothing about slaughtered animals. With slaughtered animals, the rule is not uniform: some would come down and some would not.

### No Inference

The Gemara is explicit: *lo, hashchutin mehem yerdu umehem lo yerdu* — do not read any inference about slaughtered animals into the Mishna's statement about living animals. The Mishna is speaking only about the scenario where the animal is still alive and must be brought down. When it says "all of them," it means all living animals must descend, regardless of whether they are *kodshei kodashim* or *kodshim kalim*. No conclusion about slaughtered animals of *kodshim kalim* with various defects follows from this.

## Minor Blemish Chiddush

### Why Down if Alive?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! לְעוֹלָם אַחַיִּים – וּבְדוּקִּים שֶׁבָּעַיִן; וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: אִם עָלוּ לֹא יֵרְדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that live animals that ascended upon the altar shall descend? The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna intends to teach the *halakha* **with regard to living** animals **but** is referring specifically **to** animals blemished on **the cornea of the eye, and** it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva, who says** that in the case of such a small blemish, **if they ascended** the altar **they shall not descend.** The phrase: All of them shall descend, teaches that even Rabbi Akiva agrees that such animals that ascended while still alive shall descend.

Once the Gemara has established that the Mishna's rule about living animals is not providing an inference about slaughtered animals, it raises the obvious question: *pshita* — is this not obvious? If a living animal is brought to the top of the altar, of course it must be brought down and slaughtered below. Why does the Mishna need to state this?

The Gemara answers: *le'olam achayin ubedukin sheba'ayin* — we are speaking of living animals, and the *chiddush* concerns animals bearing small blemishes of the eye, *dukin sheba'ayin*, according to Rabbi Akiva. The case must involve an invalidity that attaches while the animal is still alive but that, had the animal been slaughtered at the top of the altar, would have been tolerated there — meaning it would have been allowed to remain. The only such invalidity that fits this profile is the minor blemish addressed by Rabbi Akiva's position, which we will encounter explicitly later in the Mishna. A more serious disqualification — such as an animal used for a prohibited act — would require bringing the animal down even if it were already slaughtered, and so the contrast the Mishna draws between alive and slaughtered would be meaningless in such a case.

### Chiddush Explained

With this framing, the *chiddush* of the Mishna becomes clear. You have a living animal with a minor blemish. According to Rabbi Akiva, had this animal been slaughtered at the top of the altar, it would be permitted to remain there — the *hamizbeach mekadeshan* principle applies. One might therefore reason: leave it on the altar alive, because if it is slaughtered there, it can legitimately stay. The Mishna teaches otherwise: even so, while it is still alive, bring it down. And this carries a real consequence. Once the animal is brought down, if it is then slaughtered below — even though it was not supposed to be slaughtered, being a *ba'al mum* — there is no way to return it to the altar. An invalid offering not already on the altar cannot be placed there. So the act of bringing the animal down forecloses the possibility of the altar's protection entirely. The *chiddush* is that despite this loss, the Torah requires bringing it down.

To a student's question about whether leaving a sacrifice on the altar discharges the owner's obligation: the answer is no. The principle of *hamizbeach mekadeshan* allows the offering to burn on the altar, but it does not discharge the owner's obligation. Where the invalidity is in the blood itself, the obligation is never fulfilled regardless. Where the invalidity is in other aspects of the animal — as in this case of a minor blemish — it remains a question of what may remain on the altar, not whether the owner has fulfilled his *korban*.

## Olah Flaying Case

### Flaying at Top

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתַּהּ – בִּפְסוּלִין? אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וְכֵן עוֹלָה שֶׁעָלְתָה חַיָּה לְרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – תֵּרֵד, שְׁחָטָהּ בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – יַפְשִׁיט וִינַתֵּחַ בִּמְקוֹמָהּ. וְאִי פְּסוּלָה – בַּת הֶפְשֵׁט וְנִתּוּחַ הִיא?! ״וְנִתַּח אוֹתָהּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, ״אוֹתָהּ״ – כְּשֵׁרָה וְלָא פְּסוּלָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **To what** case **did you interpret** this *halakha* in the mishna to be referring? You interpreted it as referring **to disqualified** offerings. **Say the latter clause: And likewise, a burnt offering that ascended to the top of the altar alive shall descend.** But if **one slaughtered** the animal **at the top of the altar, he should flay** it **and cut** it **into pieces in its place,** and it is not removed from the altar. **And if** the mishna is referring to **a disqualified** offering one must ask: Is a disqualified offering **fit for flaying and cutting? The Merciful One states:** "And he shall flay the burnt offering, **and cut it** into its pieces" (Leviticus 1:6), and the word **"it"** indicates an exclusion: Only **fit** offerings are flayed and cut, **and not** those that are **disqualified.**

The Mishna continues: *ve'chen olah she'altah chayah le'rosh hamizbeach tiered* — just as a living animal must come down, so too if an *olah* was brought alive to the top of the altar, it must descend. *Shachatah be'rosh hamizbeach yafshit viyenate'ach bimkomah* — but if it was slaughtered at the top of the altar, it should be flayed and dismembered right there.

This immediately raises a difficulty. We have just established that the Mishna's *chiddush* about living animals concerns those bearing a minor blemish. If that is the backdrop of the Mishna, then when it proceeds to discuss an *olah* that was slaughtered at the top of the altar, it would seemingly refer to the same scenario — an animal with a minor blemish. But in that case, how can the Mishna say to flay and dismember it? The Torah explicitly states — *osah*, referring to a valid offering — that only a valid animal is subject to the procedure of *hefshet venituach*, flaying and dismemberment. An invalid animal is excluded.

### Flaying Invalid?

The Gemara's first attempt to resolve this: *seifa atan leksherah* — the second half of the Mishna shifts to a different case entirely, a fully valid *olah* that was slaughtered at the top of the altar. There are thus two distinct teachings: the first, about living animals with a minor blemish that must come down; the second, about a valid *olah* slaughtered at the altar top, which should be flayed there. But the Gemara is dissatisfied: *u'mai ka mashma lan* — what is the novel teaching? If an animal is valid and was slaughtered at the top of the altar, it is obvious that one should flay it there. Tosafot addresses this directly, noting that everyone agrees to the plain meaning of the Mishna — that what is slaughtered at the top is flayed there. The debate the Gemara references concerns whether one may *lechatchilah* bring a carcass up to the altar top or slaughter there intentionally from the outset. If one holds that flaying at the altar top is always permitted, the Mishna teaches this as an extension. If one holds it is not always permitted, the Mishna's *chiddush* remains unclear. The Gemara is pressing: given the straightforwardness of the case, what exactly is being taught?

### Sha'at Hakesher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לִכְשֵׁרָה. וּמַאי קָמַשְׁמַע לַן? דְּיֵשׁ הֶפְשֵׁט וְנִתּוּחַ בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In **the latter clause we come to** refer to **a fit** offering. The Gemara asks: **And what does** this **teach us?** The Gemara answers: It teaches **that there is flaying and cutting at the top of the altar,** and it is not considered degrading to the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין הֶפְשֵׁט וְנִתּוּחַ בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר וְנִפְסְלָה; וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּזְרַק הַדָּם וְהוּרְצָה בָּשָׂר שָׁעָה אַחַת – יַפְשִׁיטֶנָּה, וְעוֹרָהּ לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to the one who says** that **there is no flaying and cutting at the top of the altar, what can be said?** The Gemara answers: According to that opinion, **what are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the animal that ascended while still alive **had a time of fitness,** during which it was slaughtered atop the altar and its blood was presented, **and** thereafter it **became disqualified. And** the last clause of the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, who says: Since the blood was sprinkled,** thereby **effecting acceptance of the flesh** of the offering for **a moment,** even if the offering then became disqualified, **he shall flay it and its hide** is given **to the priests,** and its flesh is burned.

The Gemara resolves the difficulty: *hacha bemai askinan kegon shehayta lo sha'at hakesher venifsela* — we are dealing with a case where the animal had a moment of validity, *sha'at hakesher*, but subsequently became invalid. The living-animal portion of the Mishna discusses an animal with a minor blemish, whose *chiddush* is that it must come down even though slaughtering it at the top would have allowed it to remain. The flaying portion of the Mishna discusses a different invalidity — not a blemish, but something that arose after slaughter: for instance, the blood spilled after the slaughter, or the meat became *tamei* after the blood was thrown.

This is the position of Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon: *keivan shenizrak hadam vehurtzah basar sha'ah achat, yafshetenah* — once the blood was thrown and the meat became permissible for even a single moment, even if the meat subsequently became *tamei* or otherwise invalid, one may still proceed to flay the animal. The rationale is practical: the purpose of flaying an *olah* at the altar top is not the burning of the meat alone — it is also to allow the *kohanim* to receive the hide, which is valuable. Even if the meat can no longer be burned on the altar by reason of the invalidity, flaying it preserves the hide for the *kohanim*. The *chiddush* of the Mishna is thus that one proceeds with *hefshet venituach* even after the invalidity arose, because there was a *sha'at hakesher* — a valid moment.

## Braita Procedure

### Organs Down & Rinse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא דְּקָתָנֵי: כֵּיצַד עוֹשֶׂה – מוֹרִיד אֶת הַקְּרָבַיִם לְמַטָּה וּמַדִּיחָן; לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the mishna is referring to an offering that became disqualified, then with regard to **that which is taught** in a *baraita* concerning the mishna: **What shall** he **do** with such an offering? He **takes the innards down** from the altar, placing them **below** it, **and** thereafter **rinses them, why do I** need to do so? Since the innards of a disqualified offering may not be returned to the altar once they have been removed from it, why should they be removed from the altar?

The Gemara now cites a *braita* that describes the procedure in detail. When an *olah* was slaughtered at the top of the altar: *morid et hakirbayim lemata umadichan* — one brings the inner organs down to the bottom and rinses them. The inner organs, primarily the small intestine, contain refuse and cannot be burned as they are.

### Why Rinse Invalid?

The Gemara presses: if we are dealing with a *pasul olah*, as we have established, what is the point of rinsing the organs? The rinsing is presumably preparatory to returning them to the altar. But once something invalid has been removed from the altar, it cannot be returned — the protection of *hamizbeach mekadeshan* applies only while it is already there. So why bother rinsing?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד? נַקְרְבִינְהוּ בְּפִרְתַּיְיהוּ?! ״הַקְרִיבֵהוּ נָא לְפֶחָתֶךָ הֲיִרְצְךָ אוֹ הֲיִשָּׂא פָנֶיךָ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rather, **what should we do? Should we offer them with their dung?** Doesn't the verse state: **"Present it now to your governor; will he be pleased with you? Or will he accept your person?"** (Malachi 1:8), and isn't it derived from this verse that items considered repulsive to people are not fit for use as an offering for the Most High?

One might suggest: burn the organs as they are, with the refuse. To this the Gemara objects by quoting the verse in Malachi — *hakrivehu na lefachatecha hayirtzecha o hayisa fanecha* — "Bring it now to your governor; will he be pleased with you, or will he show you favor?" Something plainly disgusting cannot be offered. So burning refuse-filled intestines on the altar is ruled out. But the Gemara's actual question is not how to burn them — it is why rinse them at all if they cannot go back up? *Madichan lama li?*

### Respect for Sacred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲנַן הָכִי קָאָמְרִינַן: מַדִּיחָן לְמָה לִי? דְּאִי מִיתְרְמֵי כֹּהֵן אַחֲרִינָא וְלָא יָדַע – נַסְּקִינְהוּ. וַאֲנַן נֵיקוּ נֶעְבֵּיד לְהוּ לְכֹהֲנִים מִילְּתָא דְּאָתוּ בַּהּ לִידֵי תַקָּלָה?! אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי עֲדִיפָא, שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ קׇדְשֵׁי שָׁמַיִם מוּטָלִים כִּנְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **This is what we are saying:** Since one removes the innards of a disqualified offering from atop the altar and they may not be returned, **why do I** need to **rinse them?** The concern is **that if another priest chances** upon these innards **and does not know** that they are disqualified for the altar, **he will sacrifice them** upon the altar with their dung. The Gemara asks: **And shall we stand** and **do something for the priests through which they** shall **come to** encounter **a stumbling block?** Were these innards to remain unwashed, no priest would mistakenly sacrifice them upon the altar. The Gemara answers: **Even so,** rinsing disqualified innards is **preferable, so that the sanctified offerings of Heaven shall not be lying as a carcass.**

The Gemara's first answer is somewhat surprising: the rinsing is done so that should an uninformed *kohen* come across the organs and not realize they are from an invalid offering, he might return them to the altar — and once on the altar, they would be allowed to remain. This answer itself triggers a further objection: *v'anan neiku v'na'avid lehu l'kohanim milta d'atu bah lidei takala* — are we to deliberately arrange a situation for the *kohanim* that could lead them into transgression? One may not set the stage so that an unwitting violation achieves a permitted result. This is reminiscent of debates in the laws of *kashrut* about whether one may passively arrange for an invalid item to become nullified through someone else's inadvertent act.

The Gemara concludes with a different rationale entirely: *afilu hachi adifa shelo yehei kodshei shamayim mutalim k'neveilah* — even granting all of the above, it is preferable that consecrated property not lie about like a carcass. The organs are rinsed out of respect for sanctified items, even if they can no longer be placed on the altar. Ultimately these materials will need to be burned — not on the altar, but in their designated place for *pasul* offerings — and one would not want the refuse to remain in them in the interim. This is the Gemara's operative reason for the rinsing: basic dignity for sanctified property, not any hope of returning it to the altar.

The *pshat* of the *braita*, however, clearly envisioned rinsing in preparation for returning the organs to the altar — which is why the Gemara had to work so hard to justify it under the assumption that the *olah* was invalid. The Gemara's conclusion is that the Mishna's final sections address various scenarios of *psulim*, and therefore the Mishna need not be read as addressing *kodshim kalim* in the way required to make Ulla's teaching obvious. Ulla's statement — and by implication Reish Lakish's related position — retains its character as a genuine *chiddush* not explicitly derivable from the Mishna.

## Rabbi Yochanan's Question

### Yerida Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, בָּעֵי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵימוּרֵי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים שֶׁהֶעֱלָן לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמָן, יֵרְדוּ אוֹ לֹא יֵרְדוּ?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the matter discussed by Ulla (85a), **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan raises a dilemma:** In the case of **sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity that** one **offered up before the sprinkling of their blood,** which is the act which sanctifies such portions for the altar, **shall they descend or shall they not descend?**

The Gemara now presents a related discussion attributed to Rabbi Yochanan — one that parallels and intersects with what Reish Lakish ruled. As reported by Rabbi Chiya bar Abba: *baei Rabbi Yochanan, eimurei kodshim kalim she'he'elum lifnei zrikat daman, yerdu o lo yerdu* — Rabbi Yochanan posed the question of whether the *imurim* of *kodshim kalim* placed on the altar before *zrikas hadam* must come down or may remain. This is precisely the question Reish Lakish had resolved decisively — they stay up — but Rabbi Yochanan apparently held it as an open question.

### Me'ila Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי: וְתִיבְּעֵי לָךְ מְעִילָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְעִילָה לָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לִי – דִּזְרִיקָה הוּא דְּקָבְעָה לְהוּ בִּמְעִילָה; כִּי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ – יְרִידָה. וּפָשֵׁיט: לֹא יֵרְדוּ, וְאֵין בָּהֶן מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: Instead of raising the dilemma of whether or not such portions shall descend, **you should raise the dilemma** of whether their ascension upon the altar sanctifies them with regard to the *halakhot* of **misuse** of consecrated property. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** With regard to the **misuse** of consecrated property **I do not raise the dilemma, as** certainly the rite of **sprinkling** and not their ascension to the altar **determines** their status **with regard to** the **misuse** of consecrated property. **When** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised the dilemma** it was with regard to their **descent** from the altar. **And** Rabbi Yoḥanan **resolved** his dilemma and ruled: If they ascended **they shall not descend, and they are not subject to** the prohibition of **misuse** of consecrated property.

Rabbi Ami responds: *v'tiba'i lach me'ilah* — why not ask the parallel question about *me'ila*? The underlying issue in both questions is the same. Since *kodshim kalim* are fundamentally under the owner's control until *zrikas hadam*, the *imurim* are not yet fully God's domain. This generates uncertainty both about whether they must descend from the altar and about whether the laws of *me'ila* apply to them at this stage.

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *me'ila lo kamiba'i li, d'zrika hu d'kava'ah lehu bime'ila* — the *me'ila* question does not trouble him, because it is clear that *zrika* is what establishes the *imurim* in the realm of *me'ila*. Until the blood is thrown, the *imurim* of *kodshim kalim* do not carry *me'ila*. What he does not know is *yerida* — the question of taking down — because *yerida* operates on a different logic. Many things remain on the altar even when they arguably should not. The question is whether this *imurim*, despite lacking *me'ila*, are sufficiently connected to the altar to stay there. The Gemara resolves this in accordance with Reish Lakish: *lo yerdu v'ein bahen me'ila* — they do not come down, and yet there is no *me'ila* in them. They are close enough to the altar to benefit from *hamizbeach mekadeshan*, even though they are not yet fully consecrated to the point of *me'ila*.

## Me'ila Version

### Me'ila Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק מַתְנֵי הָכִי: אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, בָּעֵי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵימוּרֵי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים שֶׁהֶעֱלָן לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמָן, יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מְעִילָה אוֹ לָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי: וְתִיבְּעֵי לָךְ יְרִידָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יְרִידָה לָא קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לִי – דְּנַעֲשׂוּ לַחְמוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ; כִּי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לִי – מְעִילָה. וּפָשֵׁיט הָכִי: לֹא יֵרְדוּ, וְאֵין בָּהֶן מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak teaches** the discussion in **this** manner: **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan raises a dilemma:** In the case of **sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity that** one **offered up** on the altar **before the sprinkling of their blood, are they subject to** the *halakhot* of **misuse or not? Rabbi Ami said to him: And you should raise the dilemma** with regard to their **descent** from upon the altar. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** With regard to their **descent I do not raise the dilemma, because** after these sacrificial portions ascend the altar **they become the bread of** the **altar** and shall not descend. **When I raise the dilemma** it is with regard to the **misuse** of consecrated property. **And** Rabbi Yoḥanan **resolved** his dilemma in **this** manner: **They shall not descend, and they are not subject to** the prohibition of **misuse** of consecrated property.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak transmits an alternative version of this same exchange. Here, Rabbi Yochanan's original question was about *me'ila*: *eimurei kodshim kalim she'he'elum lifnei zrikat daman yesh bahen me'ila o lav* — do the *imurim* of *kodshim kalim* placed on the altar before *zrikas hadam* carry *me'ila* or not?

### Counter Question

Rabbi Ami pushes back in the opposite direction from the first version: *v'tiba'i lach yerida* — why not ask instead whether these *imurim* must come down from the altar? If the question is the extent to which these *imurim* are consecrated, the *yerida* question is equally pressing.

### Conclusion Both Versions

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *yerida lo kamiba'i li, d'nasu lehu lachmo shel mizbeach* — the *yerida* question does not trouble him. Using Reish Lakish's own terminology, he considers it obvious that once placed on the altar, the *imurim* become *lachmo shel mizbeach* — the bread of the altar — and the altar holds them firmly. What remains uncertain is whether they carry *me'ila*. The Gemara resolves both versions with the identical conclusion: *lo yerdu v'ein bahen me'ila* — they stay on the altar, and there is no *me'ila* in them prior to *zrikas hadam*. This is the ruling of Reish Lakish, now confirmed through the parallel tradition of Rabbi Yochanan.

## Psulo Lo Bakodesh

### Not in Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ לֹא הָיָה פְּסוּלָן בַּקֹּדֶשׁ
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And these are** the offerings **whose disqualification** did **not** occur in sanctity: An animal that copulated with a person, or an animal that was the object of bestiality…or blemished animals. Such offerings shall descend from the altar if they ascended. Rabbi Akiva deems blemished animals fit in the sense that if they ascended upon the altar they shall not descend.

The Gemara moves to the final section of the Mishna: *v'eilu lo hayah psulan bakodesh* — and these are the cases whose invalidity is not *bakodesh*, listing various disqualifications that attached to the animal while it was still alive, prior to any sacred context. These fall outside the protective rule of *hamizbeach mekadeshan*.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

Rabbi Akiva adds: even a *ba'al mum* — an animal with a physical blemish — if slaughtered at the top of the altar, remains there and is not brought down.

### Dukin Sheba'ayin Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא הִכְשִׁיר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֶלָּא בְּדוּקִּים שֶׁבָּעַיִן, הוֹאִיל וּכְשֵׁרִים בָּעוֹפוֹת. וְהוּא שֶׁקָּדַם הֶקְדֵּשָׁן אֶת מוּמָן. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בְּעוֹלַת נְקֵבָה, דִּכְשֶׁקָּדַם מוּמָהּ לְהֶקְדֵּשָׁהּ דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Akiva deemed fit only** those animals with small blemishes, such as **on the cornea of the eye, as** such blemishes **are fit with regard to bird** offerings *ab initio*. And **this** is the *halakha* only **when their consecration preceded their blemish,** since such an animal was fit for the altar at the time of its consecration. But if their blemish preceded their consecration, they shall descend, as they were never fit for the altar. **And Rabbi Akiva concedes with regard to a female burnt offering** that it shall descend from the altar. Since only a male animal may be used for a burnt offering, **it is like** a case **where** the animal's **blemish preceded its consecration.**

The Gemara immediately qualifies this: *lo hechshir Rabbi Akiva ela b'dukin sheba'ayin* — Rabbi Akiva's lenient position applies only to *dukin sheba'ayin*, a minor cataract-like blemish of the eye. This is how Rabbi Yochanan explains Rabbi Akiva, and it reflects a genuine exegetical problem: why should a *ba'al mum* be treated more favorably by the altar than other disqualifications that are less explicit in the Torah? Major blemishes are explicitly prohibited by the Torah, while other disqualifications — such as an animal used for bestiality — derive from more indirect sources. Yet those more obscure disqualifications seem to be handled more stringently. The absence of a clear logical basis for treating *baal mum* favorably forces the Gemara to restrict Rabbi Akiva's position to only the most marginal category of blemish.

## Eye Blemish Logic

### Birds Permit

The rationale offered for why *dukin sheba'ayin* would be valid at all — and thus within the scope of *hamizbeach mekadeshan* according to Rabbi Akiva — is: *ho'il veksherim b'ofot*, since such blemishes are valid in bird-offerings. Birds are only disqualified for missing a limb; all other physical irregularities are acceptable in a bird-offering. Since a minor eye blemish is *kasher* in a bird, there is some basis to treat it as less disqualifying in general, which provides the conceptual foothold for Rabbi Akiva's position.

### Rashi: Unseemly

A difficulty arises, however. If the rule for birds is that anything short of a missing limb is acceptable, why limit the comparison to the most minimal of blemishes? One could argue: as long as the animal is not missing a limb, it is analogous to what is valid in a bird. Why does Rabbi Akiva's position extend only to the tiniest eye blemishes rather than to any blemish less severe than a missing limb?

Rashi addresses this directly. Rashi notes that the verse — *tmimim zachar babakar bakshavim uva'izim* — requires both *tmimut* (being unblemished) and *zachrut* (being male) only by cattle and sheep; birds are exempt from both requirements. Accordingly, birds are broadly valid despite blemishes. However, Rashi continues, *mechusar eivar lo* — a bird missing a limb is disqualified — citing the very same verse from Malachi: *hakrivehu na lefachatecha* — bringing something to the governor in such a condition would not find favor. What disqualifies in this case is not simply the technical category of "missing limb" but the unseemly appearance it creates.

### Major Blemishes

Rashi's framing is deliberate and consequential. By grounding the limb-disqualification in the concept of *meguneh* — unseemly, repulsive appearance — rather than in a specific legal category, Rashi expands the principle: *vekol mum meguneh* — any major, conspicuous blemish falls within the same prohibition by birds. It is not limited to missing limbs per se; any blemish that is strikingly ugly or obviously disfiguring would be equally disqualifying. This is a significant *chiddush* in Rashi's reading. We are generally taught that birds are only disqualified for missing limbs, but Rashi is saying that in reality, birds are disqualified for missing limbs *because* they are unseemly — and therefore any sufficiently prominent, visible blemish would similarly disqualify. This explains why Rabbi Akiva's position is limited to the small eye-blemishes of *dukin sheba'ayin*: only those inconspicuous, barely noticeable blemishes avoid the category of *meguneh*. A major blemish, even if not a missing limb, would be *meguneh* even in a bird and therefore cannot serve as a basis for leniency. It is only the most minor, visually negligible blemishes that remain in a gray area and thus benefit from Rabbi Akiva's ruling.
