# Bava Batra 117 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bnot Tzelofchad Setup

### Nachala Concept

The *mishna* on this *daf* concerns *bnot Tzelofchad*, and it affords us a rich opportunity to examine how the *nachala* — the inheritance of the Land of Israel — was conceptually structured for the generation that entered *Eretz Yisrael*. The central question animating the entire sugya is deceptively simple: when the land was divided, was it distributed to *yotzei Mitzrayim* — those who left Egypt — or to *ba'ei ha'aretz* — those who actually entered the land? The practical stakes of this question are considerable, and the *mishna* itself implicitly takes a position.

### Three Portions

The *mishna* teaches that the daughters of Tzelofchad received three portions of land, and to understand why, one must reconstruct the family tree. Chefer was Tzelofchad's father. Chefer had Tzelofchad and several brothers. Tzelofchad died in the *midbar*, and his daughters then petitioned for his share. According to the *mishna*'s underlying assumption — namely, that portions were assigned to those who left Egypt, who then passed them on to their descendants — Chefer himself received a portion as one who left Egypt, plus an additional portion as a *bechor*, for a total of two portions derived from Chefer's own father. Tzelofchad, as Chefer's firstborn son, inherited both of these portions. His daughters therefore stood to receive not just the ordinary portion Tzelofchad would have taken as one of Chefer's sons, but also the *bechor* portion. This accounts for the three portions the *mishna* credits to them.

The *mishna* thereby teaches several distinct *chiddushim*. First, that the land was distributed conceptually to *yotzei Mitzrayim*. Second, as the *Rashbam* explains, that daughters may replace their father and receive together with the father's brothers, even though brothers are present in that generation. Third, it is not self-evident that heirs of a deceased *bechor* are entitled to the *bechor* portion at all, since the father himself never lived to hold it — yet the *mishna* affirms they do. Fourth, the ordinary rule is that a *bechor* does not receive a double portion unless the asset was already in the father's possession before death — the requirement of *muchzak* — yet here the father never actually held land in *Eretz Yisrael*, and the *mishna* rules nevertheless that the portion passes as though he had been entitled to it.

## Yotzei vs. Ba'ei

### Yotzei Mitzrayim

The *Gemara* opens by observing that the *mishna* proceeds according to the opinion that the land was divided to *yotzei Mitzrayim*: *tnan Gemara keman de'amar leyotzei Mitzrayim nitchalkah ha'aretz*. The practical significance of this choice cannot be overstated. Imagine two brothers, both *yotzei Mitzrayim*. One has a single child entering the land; the other has ten. If distribution is pegged to the *yotzei Mitzrayim* generation, each brother receives an equal share, which then descends to his children. The brother with one child passes down one full share; the brother with ten children passes down one share divided among ten. The ten cousins each receive one-tenth of what their single cousin receives. By contrast, if the land is distributed directly to *ba'ei ha'aretz*, then portions are sized according to the number of people actually entering, and all eleven children receive equal portions relative to one another. The stakes of the debate are therefore not abstract but bear directly on the relative allocation of land among families.

A *baraita* is then cited: *Rabbi Yoshia says the land was divided to yotzei Mitzrayim*, as derived from the verse *lishmot matot avotam yinchalu* — they shall inherit according to the names of their fathers' tribes. The straightforward reading of *lematot avotam* would be merely that the land is divided by tribe. Rabbi Yoshia, however, reads the verse as indicating that the inheritance is conceived as flowing *from* the fathers — meaning the *yotzei Mitzrayim* generation received it notionally and transmitted it downward.

### Ka'eleh Exclusion

The *Gemara* then asks how Rabbi Yoshia handles the verse *la'eleh techalek ha'aretz benachala* — "to these shall the land be divided as an inheritance" — which plainly speaks to the generation standing on the cusp of entry. He answers: *le'eleh ka'eleh* — "to these as these" — meaning the people actually entering the land must resemble the *yotzei Mitzrayim* in one critical respect, specifically to the exclusion of *tefelim*, the underaged. The *Rashbam* explains *tefelim* as derived from *taf*, young children. This creates a striking consequence: a man who left Egypt at age nineteen would not himself receive a portion, because the *yotzei Mitzrayim* entitlement applied only to those counted in the census, which required being twenty years of age or older. Not only does that man fail to receive a portion, but his own children entering the land also receive nothing — because distribution goes to the *yotzei Mitzrayim* generation, not to *ba'ei ha'aretz*, and he was not eligible as one of the former. It is theoretically possible, then, for an entire family line to have no portion in *Eretz Yisrael* — specifically if a man left Egypt under the age of twenty and his father had already died. As the *Rashbam* states, without a *morish* who left Egypt with sons, *az lo natlu chelek ba'aretz*. The one saving circumstance is that if the underage person's father was alive and had left Egypt above the required age, the portion would still descend through the father.

### Metim Yorshim

Rabbi Yonatan disagrees. He holds that the land was given to *ba'ei ha'aretz*, which represents the simpler reading of *la'eleh techalek ha'aretz*. When confronted with the verse about fathers' tribes, he does not dissolve it but rather draws a sweeping conceptual distinction: *meshunah nachala zo mikol nachalos sheba'olam* — this inheritance is unlike all other inheritances in the world. In all other inheritances, *chaim yorshin es hameisim* — the living inherit from the dead. Here, *meisim yorshin es hachaim* — the dead inherit from the living. The verse about the fathers' tribes thus reflects a genuine inversion: the generation that entered the land passed their portions back up to the *yotzei Mitzrayim* fathers who had already died in the *midbar*, and those fathers' estates then redistributed downward to their heirs.

### Two Brothers Parable

To illustrate this remarkable mechanism, the *Gemara* offers a *mashal*. Two brothers, both *kohanim*, live in the same city. One has a single son; the other has two sons. When the time comes to collect *terumah* from the threshing floor, the brothers reason that rather than going themselves — and receiving one portion each, for a total of two — they should send their children. The *ba'al habayis* gives one equal portion to each *kohen* who comes, so three children going yields three portions. The children collect the three portions and return them to their fathers. The fathers then divide equally: each receives one and a half portions. The children have effectively acted as agents on behalf of their fathers, the dead collecting through the living.

### Family Fairness

The *mashal* raises an obvious question: if the three portions are ultimately divided equally between the two brothers, why does it matter whether the scheme runs through the children or directly between the fathers? The answer emerges when one considers not just these two brothers but their relationship to other families. If the land had been distributed directly to *yotzei Mitzrayim*, each *yotzei Mitzrayim* individual would have received one equal share, which would then descend to his children. Relative to another family — say, one with a single *ba'ei ha'aretz* member — each family's patriarch would have received an identical share. But under the *ba'ei ha'aretz* framework as Rabbi Yonatan describes it, the children go in, collect portions proportional to their number, and pass those up to their fathers. A family with nine children entering the land and a family with one child both had fathers who were *yotzei Mitzrayim*, but the nine-child family now sends nine portions back to its father, giving that family five times as much as the one-child family once the fathers divide the pooled resources among themselves. The mechanism therefore produces significantly different outcomes across families, with larger *ba'ei ha'aretz* families commanding far greater inherited wealth than smaller ones, even if the *yotzei Mitzrayim* ancestors were originally equal. The verse *lishmot matot avotam yinchalu* is thus not a mere formality but an operative inheritance mechanism with real distributional consequences.

## Third Opinion

### Both Get It

Rav Shimon ben Elazar offers a third position: *la'elu u'le'elu nitchalkah ha'aretz* — the land was divided to both generations simultaneously, so as to fulfill both verses. There is no need to force a choice between *yotzei Mitzrayim* and *ba'ei ha'aretz*; each generation receives its own portion in parallel. Practically, this means tallying the entire population of *yotzei Mitzrayim* — some 600,000 — alongside the entire population of *ba'ei ha'aretz* — also approximately 600,000 — and creating a total pool of roughly 1.2 million portions, divided tribe by tribe. Those eligible as *yotzei Mitzrayim* receive from that pool and then, having died in the *midbar*, pass their portions down to their children. Those entering the land receive their own portions as *ba'ei ha'aretz* directly. The result is that a child entering the land might receive both: his own portion as *ba'ei ha'aretz* and, through inheritance, his father's portion as *yotzei Mitzrayim*.

### Yehoshua Calev

The most striking potential beneficiaries of this dual system are Yehoshua and Calev — the only two men who were both *yotzei Mitzrayim* and *ba'ei ha'aretz*. They would seemingly be entitled to a portion from each pool. *Tosafot* raises the complication that both men were over sixty years old when they entered the land, and there is a question whether the *ba'ei ha'aretz* entitlement was capped at those under sixty, consistent with the census counts. The *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* suggest that even if Yehoshua and Calev cannot serve as the paradigm case, the *mikan umikan* principle still operates for others: a man who was a *yotzei Mitzrayim* has his father's portion descend to him, and he also receives his own portion as *ba'ei ha'aretz*, yielding two portions through two distinct legal channels. The dual system also helps address the problem created by the age-twenty requirement: even if a man entered the land too young to qualify as *yotzei Mitzrayim*, he at least receives as *ba'ei ha'aretz*; and if a man was ineligible as *ba'ei ha'aretz* for some reason, he might still receive through descent from a *yotzei Mitzrayim* father.

## Wicked Groups

### Meraglim Lost

The *Gemara* now takes up the question of those whose wickedness affected their inheritance rights. Beginning with the *meraglim* — the spies — the *Gemara* establishes that Yehoshua and Calev received the portions of the *meraglim*: *lo b'shel Meraglim Yehoshua v'Calev natlu chelkam*. The source is a *drashah* from the verse *chayyu min ha'anashim ha'eleh* — they survived from these men — which is read to mean that Yehoshua and Calev were enriched from the *meraglim*, inheriting what had been assigned to the spies. The *meraglim* had been *yotzei Mitzrayim* and therefore had been assigned portions; those assigned portions were then transferred to Yehoshua and Calev rather than descending to the *meraglim*'s own children. An additional source from the *Sifrei* derives from *ka'eleh* that those who were *reshaim* were excluded from receiving portions. This means that even under Rav Shimon ben Elazar's dual approach — where having been a *ba'ei ha'aretz* might have allowed the *meraglim*'s children to receive on their own merit — the specific legal transfer of the *meraglim*'s assigned portions to Yehoshua and Calev was an affirmative redistribution, not merely a lapse.

### Korach's Children

The *mitlonenim* — the complainers — are identified by the *Gemara* as the members of *adat Korach*, specifically the 250 men who joined Korach's rebellion. Unlike the *meraglim*, these men *lo hayah lahem chelek ba'aretz* — they had no portion in the land at all. Yet the *Gemara* teaches that their children received portions — *bizchut avi avihem* — by virtue of their grandfathers. The verse from *bnot Tzelofchad*'s own petition is cited in support: *avinu meit bamidbar v'hu lo hayah b'toch ha'eida hano'adim al Hashem ba'adat Korach* — our father died in the *midbar* but he was not among the congregation that gathered against God in *adat Korach*. The implicit logic is that had Tzelofchad been part of *adat Korach*, he would indeed have had no portion, confirming that *adat Korach* was entirely excluded. The grandchildren's salvation comes through the grandfather: if a grandfather was a *yotzei Mitzrayim* in good standing, and his son was a member of *adat Korach* and thus forfeited any entitlement, the grandson could still receive through the grandfather's portion descending past the disqualified middle generation. It is possible for a father and son both to have been over twenty when they left Egypt, so the grandfather's eligibility as *yotzei Mitzrayim* remains intact even if the father is struck from the list.

The *Gemara* also notes an additional dimension: *habanim natlu bizchut avi avihen u'vizchut avi imoteihen* — the children received by virtue of both their paternal and maternal grandfathers. Women would not receive portions on their own merit as *yotzei Mitzrayim*, but once the innovation of *bnot Tzelofchad*'s ruling was established, inheritance could flow through a maternal grandfather as well. Thus even grandchildren whose mothers were the sole link to a *yotzei Mitzrayim* grandfather could receive through him.

### Assigned vs. Unassigned

There is a meaningful distinction between the *meraglim* and *adat Korach* with respect to what they lost. The *meraglim* had been assigned specific portions as *yotzei Mitzrayim*, and those assigned portions were affirmatively transferred to Yehoshua and Calev. *Adat Korach*, by contrast, were never assigned portions at all — they were, so to speak, struck from the distribution list entirely, and their would-be shares were absorbed back into the general pool, effectively redistributed proportionally among everyone else. This distinction matters for how we conceptualize the forfeiture: the *meraglim* had something concrete taken from them and given to specific individuals, while *adat Korach* were simply excluded from receiving anything, with no specific successor to their shares.

## God's Promise

### Yerusha Promise

The *Gemara* then challenges the assumption embedded in the *lishmot matot avotam* derivation, asking whether that verse simply means the land is divided by the twelve tribal groupings descending from the sons of Yaakov — the natural reading of *matot* as *shevatim* — rather than specifically evoking the *yotzei Mitzrayim* generation. The *Gemara*'s response is striking: it cites the verse *venasati osah lachem morashah ani Hashem* — I shall give it to you as a heritage — which Moshe declares to *bnei Yisrael* at the outset of the Exodus. The *Gemara* then cites the related phrase *yerushah hi lachem me-avoseichem* — it is an inheritance for you from your fathers. This was said directly to *yotzei Mitzrayim*, as the *Gemara* states: *le-yotzei Mitzrayim ka'amar*. God's promise was made specifically to that generation. All three opinions in the *Gemara* therefore share the premise that *yotzei Mitzrayim* must receive some portion of the land, because God explicitly promised it to them. The debate is only about whether *ba'ei ha'aretz* also receive portions, not about whether *yotzei Mitzrayim* do.

### All via Fathers

The verse carries an additional layer of meaning. The promise was not framed as a direct grant but as a *yerushah* — an inheritance — *me-avoseichem*, from their forefathers. This means that even the *yotzei Mitzrayim*, who received God's explicit promise, did not receive the land as conquerors earning it by right of valor alone. They received it as an inheritance from the *Avot*. And this principle cascades: those who enter as *ba'ei ha'aretz* receive it as a *yerushah* from *yotzei Mitzrayim*, who themselves received it as a *yerushah* from the *Avot*. In this way, every generation's relationship to *Eretz Yisrael* is mediated by *yerushah* — inheritance — never by pure independent entitlement. Even the generation that physically conquered the land cannot regard its acquisition as *kochi ve-otzem yadi*. The *Gemara*'s insistence that *ba'ei ha'aretz* receive their portions through *yotzei Mitzrayim* rather than directly is therefore not merely a technical legal mechanism but a theological statement about the nature of every generation's claim to the land.

## Growth Verses

### Larov Tarbeh

Rav Papa then mounts a challenge from the verse *larov tarbeh nachalaso ve-lama'at tam'it nachalaso* — for the many you shall increase the inheritance and for the few you shall diminish it. He argues that this verse works more naturally if the land was distributed to *yotzei Mitzrayim*. His challenge to the *ba'ei ha'aretz* position is formulated as *kashya*: if distribution is to those entering the land, the verse is simply saying give more land to a large group and less to a small group — which is unremarkable and requires no special formulation. But why, under that reading, does the verse use the language of *tarbeh* and *tam'it* — increase and diminish — language that implies changing something from what it otherwise would have been?

### Changing Shares

The *yotzei Mitzrayim* reading makes better sense of this language. Under that approach, the verse is directing that the portions going to *ba'ei ha'aretz* should be adjusted based on the *preceding* generation — the *yotzei Mitzrayim*. If the *yotzei Mitzrayim* family was large, then even if the *ba'ei ha'aretz* generation is relatively small, their inherited share should be increased — *tarbeh* — beyond what their own numbers alone would warrant. Conversely, if the *yotzei Mitzrayim* family was small, the *ba'ei ha'aretz* descendants receive a diminished — *tam'it* — share, even if they are numerically many. The active language of changing reflects the fact that the size of the inheritance is being adjusted relative to the incoming generation's own headcount. The *Gemara* itself acknowledges the difficulty with *kashya*, implying that the point is subtle.

### Tosafot Girsa

*Tosafot* preserves a different *girsa* of the *Gemara* — a variant textual reading. Rather than the verse *larov tarbeh nachalaso*, the operative text in *Tosafot*'s version is *bein rav lama'at*, drawn from the verse *la'eleh techalek ha'aretz bein rav lama'at* — "to these shall the land be divided, whether many or few." Under this *girsa*, the *Gemara*'s argument is more direct: the phrase *bein rav lama'at* — "whether many or few" — makes sense only if the distribution is keyed to *yotzei Mitzrayim*, because it instructs the apportioners to disregard the size of the incoming *ba'ei ha'aretz* generation entirely and look instead to the previous generation. This directly contradicts the *ba'ei ha'aretz* reading, under which the size of the entering group is precisely what drives the allocation. The *Rashbam* on the corresponding page notes that he does not have the *bein rav lama'at* reading in his text, suggesting that manuscript traditions diverge here, and that the *girsa* known to *Tosafot* clarifies the *Gemara*'s intent more sharply.

## Daughters' Complaint

### Complaint Logic

Rav Papa presses a final challenge directly from the narrative of *bnot Tzelofchad* themselves. If the land was given to *ba'ei ha'aretz*, he asks, *amai tzavachan* — why were the daughters complaining? *Halo leitnehu delishkalu* — they themselves could simply receive portions as *ba'ei ha'aretz*! The daughters' complaint, as recorded in the Torah, was not that they deserved portions on their own independent merit. They argued specifically that their father was being left without a portion — that they should be permitted to step into their father's place. That argument presupposes a framework in which the land was initially distributed to *yotzei Mitzrayim*, leaving a portion notionally belonging to Tzelofchad which, upon his death, risked passing away from his family. Under a pure *ba'ei ha'aretz* system, there would be no such prior paternal entitlement to claim. The daughters would have had to argue for an entirely different right — a direct personal entitlement — and that is not the argument they made.

### Uncles' Portion

The *Gemara* does not leave this unanswered. Even under the *ba'ei ha'aretz* framework, it turns out that the daughters had a coherent and forceful argument — though a somewhat different one from what is initially intuited. The *Gemara* states: *ella lachazara u-litol be-nichsei Chefer* — rather, they were seeking to reclaim through Chefer's estate. The *Rashbam* confirms this reading explicitly: *she-hem achei avinu nachalu lehachzir oto lachevel avihem* — the brothers of our father inherited in order to return it to the ancestral portion.

The argument works as follows. Under the *ba'ei ha'aretz* system, the uncles of Tzelofchad — Chefer's other sons — were themselves *ba'ei ha'aretz* and would receive portions. According to Rabbi Yonatan's mechanism, those entering the land pass their portions back up to the *yotzei Mitzrayim* fathers, and Chefer then divides the pooled family inheritance among all his sons, including the portion that would have been Tzelofchad's share. The daughters are thus arguing: our uncles are *ba'ei ha'aretz* and are entitled to portions; those portions would flow through Chefer and be divided among all of Chefer's sons, including our father; our father's share in that redistribution would then descend to us. By being daughters rather than sons, are we to be told that our father forfeits the portion he would have received through his brothers? That argument is entirely within the *ba'ei ha'aretz* framework and does not require invoking any direct *yotzei Mitzrayim* entitlement.

This reading makes the daughters' complaint stronger and more precise than the first impression suggests. They are not claiming a direct personal right. They are not even claiming, in the primary instance, that had they been sons they themselves would have entered and generated a portion for their father. They are pointing to an existing mechanism — their uncles' *ba'ei ha'aretz* portions flowing upward through Chefer — and asking only that they not be cut off from the share of that flow that would have been their father's. It is, in the end, still an argument grounded in their father's entitlement, which is why the *Gemara*'s initial intuition — that the complaint only makes sense under *yotzei Mitzrayim* — required refinement rather than outright rejection. And it is precisely because our *mishna* ultimately awards them three portions rather than this more limited claim that the *mishna* is understood to proceed according to the *yotzei Mitzrayim* framework throughout.
