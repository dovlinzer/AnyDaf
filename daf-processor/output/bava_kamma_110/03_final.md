# Bava Kamma 110 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gezel Hager Money

### L'Hashem L'Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לַה׳ לַכֹּהֵן״ – קְנָאוֹ הַשֵּׁם, וּנְתָנוֹ לַכֹּהֵן שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ מִשְׁמָר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לַכֹּהֵן שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ מִשְׁמָר; אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְכׇל כֹּהֵן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״מִלְּבַד אֵיל הַכִּפֻּרִים אֲשֶׁר יְכַפֶּר בּוֹ עָלָיו״ – הֲרֵי לַכֹּהֵן שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ מִשְׁמָר הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the payment to priests in the case of one who robbed a convert who then died without heirs, **the Sages taught:** The verse states: "Restitution for guilt that is made **shall be the Lord's, even the priest's"** (Numbers 5:8), teaching that **the Name,** i.e., God, **acquired it and gave it to the priest who is in that priestly watch.** Do **you say** that it is given only **to the priest who is on that priestly watch? Or** perhaps that is **not** the case, and **rather** he may give it **to any priest that he wishes? When it says** in that verse: **"Besides the ram of the atonement, whereby atonement shall be made for him,"** this teaches that **the verse speaks of** giving it **to the priest who is on that priestly watch.** Just as the ram that the robber brings is given only to the priest on duty who sacrifices it, so too, the money is also given only to the priest on duty.

Today's daf is 110, and we pick up in the middle of 109b, roughly fifteen lines down from the top, with a *baraita* introduced by *tanu rabbanan*. The passage opens with a quotation of the *pasuk*: *l'Hashem l'Kohen* — "to God, to the *kohen*." This is the *mitzvah* of *gezel hager*, which is about to become the subject of the next *mishna*. Much of the surrounding *gemara* has been a digression anticipating that discussion: what happens when someone steals from a *ger* who subsequently dies without heirs, swears falsely about it, and must return the principal and the *chomesh* — and bring an *asham* — to the *kohanim*?

The *baraita* explains the phrase *l'Hashem l'Kohen* as follows: *kina'o Hashem u'netano l'Kohen* — God has, as it were, acquired it, and then awards it to the *kohen*. The structure mirrors the *korban* system: one brings an offering to God, and the *kohanim* then eat from the meat. Here too, the money is first consecrated to God and then distributed to the *kohanim*, rendering the act a type of *kaparah*.

### Which Kohen Gets It

The *baraita* then specifies: *she'b'oso mishmar* — it goes to the *kohanim* of that particular *mishmar*, the weekly rotation of *kohanim* serving in the *Beis Hamikdash*. The question is posed whether the money must go specifically to that *mishmar*, or whether the giver may choose any *kohen* he wishes, even one not currently serving. The *baraita* resolves this from the *pasuk* that refers to the *asham* being returned *milvad eil hakippurim* — in addition to the ram of atonement. Just as the *korban asham* is shared by the *kohanim* working at the time of its offering, so too the money of *gezel hager* is shared equally by the *kohanim* of the serving *mishmar*. The emphasis on the weekly *mishmar* rather than the daily *beis av* reflects that the primary division of *kohanim* was by week, and the distribution is understood to be equal across the entire *mishmar*.

### Kohen Steals from Ger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה גּוֹזֵל כֹּהֵן, מִנַּיִן שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמַר: הוֹאִיל וְיוֹצֵא לַכֹּהֲנִים, וַהֲרֵי הוּא תַּחַת יָדִי – יְהֵא שֶׁלִּי? וְדִין הוּא – אִי בְּשֶׁל אֲחֵרִים הוּא זוֹכֶה, בְּשֶׁל עַצְמוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** Concerning this payment, **the Sages taught:** In a case **where** the one who **robs** the convert **was a priest, from where** is it derived **that he shall not say: Since** the payment usually **is removed** from the robber and given **to the priests and it is** now **in my possession, it should be mine. And** there is **a logical derivation** to support this reasoning, as follows: **If** a priest **has the right to acquire** the payment **belonging to others** who stole from a convert, then with regard to payment **belonging to himself,** which is already in his possession, is it **not all the more so** that he should keep it?

The *baraita* continues with a striking case: *harei she'hayah gozel Kohen* — what if the one who stole from the *ger* was himself a *kohen*? He may be tempted to argue as follows: *hovil v'yotzei l'Kohanim v'harei tachas yadi yehei sheli* — since the money is going to the *kohanim* anyway, and it is currently in my possession, why shouldn't I simply keep it? This argument has an interesting wrinkle: one might have expected the *gemara* to reject it immediately on the basis of what was established on the previous *amud* — namely, that the *mitzvah* of *hashavah* requires an affirmative act of returning the object, independent of any monetary entitlement. Yet the *gemara* does not take that route here, and instead engages with the logical argument on its own terms.

### Kal Vachomer Refutation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר בְּלָשׁוֹן אַחֵר: וּמָה דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק בּוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּכָּנֵס בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ – כְּשֶׁיִּכָּנֵס לִרְשׁוּתוֹ אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ מִיָּדוֹ; דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ חֵלֶק בּוֹ עַד שֶׁלֹּא יִכָּנֵס בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ – מִשֶּׁנִּכְנַס לִרְשׁוּתוֹ אֵינוֹ דִּין דְּאֵין אַחֵר יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ מִיָּדוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Natan states** this logical inference **with different wording: And just as** with regard to **a matter in which** a priest **has no portion until it enters his possession,** e.g., *teruma*, which can be given to any priest, **once it enters his possession** another priest **cannot remove it from his possession;** so too, with regard to **a matter in which** a priest **has a portion** even **before it enters his possession,** e.g., an item stolen from a convert, in which he has a portion as one of the priests on the priestly watch, **isn't it logical that another** priest **cannot remove it from his possession** once it is in his possession?

The *gemara* considers the *kohen*'s potential *kal vachomer*: *im b'shel acherim hu zocheh, b'shel atzmo lo kol shekein* — if I receive a share when a stranger brings *gezel hager* during my *mishmar*'s week, surely I should receive a share — or more — in money I myself am bringing, money that is already in my possession. The *gemara* acknowledges the force of this argument but notes its limitation: even if valid, it could only establish a right to one's proportional share, not to the entire sum. It does not prove the *kohen* may retain the whole amount.

### Rabbi Natan's Version

Rabbi Natan formulates the argument more precisely: *u'mah davar she-ein lo chelek bo ad she-yikanes birshuto, kesheyikanes birshuto eino yachol lehotzi miyado* — something I have no rights to whatsoever until it is placed in my possession, like *trumah*, once given to me belongs entirely to me and no other *kohen* has any claim. If that is true of something I had no prior claim to, then *davar she-yesh lo chelek bo* — something to which I already have a rightful claim, since any *gezel hager* brought during my *mishmar*'s week would entitle me to a portion — should certainly be fully mine once it comes into my control.

### Davar She-Yesh Lo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא; אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק בּוֹ – שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק בּוֹ כָּךְ אֵין לַאֲחֵרִים חֵלֶק בּוֹ, תֹּאמַר בְּגָזֵל – שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ חֵלֶק בּוֹ, כָּךְ יֵשׁ לַאֲחֵרִים חֵלֶק בּוֹ?! אֶלָּא גְּזֵילוֹ יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ, וּמִתְחַלֵּק לְכׇל אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues, countering that this logical inference is not correct: **No, if you said** this **concerning a matter in which** a priest **has no portion,** where the reason why once it enters his possession another priest cannot remove it from his possession is **that just as he has no portion in it, so too, others have no portion in it; would you say** the same **concerning** an item **robbed** from a convert? As there it could be said **that just as** the priest who robbed him **has a portion in it** as one of the priests on the priestly watch, **so too, other** priests on that watch **have a portion in it. Rather,** the *halakha* is that **the item he stole is removed from his possession and is distributed to all of his brethren, the priests.**

The *gemara* rejects this *kal vachomer* on precise logical grounds. With *trumah*, *she-kesheim she-ein lo chelek bo, kach ein la-acheirim chelek bo* — just as I have no claim to it before it is given to me, no other *kohen* has any claim either. Therefore, once it is given to me, I alone own it. But with *gezel hager*, *she-kesheim she-yesh lo chelek bo, kach yesh la-acheirim chelek bo* — just as I have a rightful claim to it, so too do all the other *kohanim* serving this week. The symmetry that drove the *kal vachomer* for *trumah* actually undermines it for *gezel hager*: because everyone started with an equal claim, no single *kohen* can assert exclusive ownership by virtue of possession. The conclusion is therefore: *gezeilah yotzei'ah mitachat yado u'mitchalek l'chol echav ha-kohanim* — the stolen property must be relinquished and divided among all the brother *kohanim*.

### Ve-Ish Et Kodashav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְאִישׁ אֶת קֳדָשָׁיו לוֹ יִהְיוּ״! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּכֹהֵן טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **But isn't it written: "And every man's hallowed things shall be his;** whatsoever any man gives to the priest, it shall be his" (Numbers 5:10), indicating that a priest is not required to give to the other priests the offerings he sacrifices? Therefore, just as he has the right to the flesh of the guilt-offering that he brings to atone for robbing the convert, shouldn't he also have the right to the payment? The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here,** in this case? We are dealing **with a ritually impure priest,** who is unfit to sacrifice the guilt-offering and therefore does not have rights to the flesh of the offering. In this case, he will not be able to keep the payment for himself.

At this point the *gemara* raises a more formidable challenge: *ve-haktiv ve-ish et kodashav lo yihyu* — does the verse not say that a man's sacred offerings belong to him? The *gemara* is about to enter a digression, but the argument is this: we know that a *kohen* who comes on any day of the year, even outside his assigned *mishmar*, has the right to perform the *avodah* on his own personal *korban* and to benefit from it. If so, and if this *kohen* is bringing the *asham* of *gezel hager*, he has the right to perform the *avodah* on his own *korban asham*, and since the *pasuk* teaches us that the money is distributed in tandem with the *korban* — *ha-asham ha'mushav la'Hashem la'kohen milvad eil ha'kippurim* — perhaps he should also be entitled to keep the money, since the distribution of the money is linked to the distribution of the *korban*, and he alone gets the *korban* he brings.

Rashi's reading of *ve-ish et kodashav lo yihyu* in context of *matanot kehunah* concerns *trumah* and *ma'aser*, not *korbanot*; but the *gemara* here applies the verse to the *kohen*'s right to perform the *avodah* on and benefit from his own *korban*.

### Kohen Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בְּכֹהֵן טָמֵא, ״דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ חֵלֶק בּוֹ״ מִי אִית לֵיהּ?! אֶלָּא אָתְיָא ״לַכֹּהֵן״–״לַכֹּהֵן״ מִשְּׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** the *baraita* states its ruling specifically **with regard to an impure priest,** how can it describe the payment as **a matter in which** the priest **has a portion? Does he have** a portion in it? Therefore, the *baraita* must not be referring to an impure priest. **Rather,** there is another reason why he has no right to the payment. This *halakha* is **derived** through a verbal analogy between the phrase **"even the priest's [*lakohen*]"** (Numbers 5:8) written in this context, and the phrase **"to the priest [*lakohen*]"** (Leviticus 27:21) **from** the verse stated concerning **an ancestral field,** which, if consecrated and then redeemed by another does not revert to its original owner in the Jubilee Year, but is given to the priests, as the *baraita* will now explain.

The *gemara* resolves the challenge: *hacha b'mai askinan b'kohen tamei* — we are dealing with the case of a *kohen* who is ritually impure. A *tamei kohen* cannot bring his own *korban*, and therefore the verse *ve-ish et kodashav lo yihyu* does not apply to him. Since he has no right to the *korban*, he likewise has no special claim to the money.

The *gemara* then presses the point: if the entire discussion concerns a *kohen tamei*, how could he have formulated the *davar she-yesh lo chelek bo* argument to begin with? If he is *tamei*, does he even have a claim to the *gezel hager* being brought during his *mishmar*'s week? The *gemara* concedes that the earlier argument assumed he did have such a claim — to the money, if not to the *korban*. But the *gemara* shifts its reasoning: the source that actually establishes that the *kohen* cannot retain the *gezel hager* exclusively is a *gezera shava* from *sdeh achuza* — the case of an ancestral field that has been consecrated and redeemed by a *kohen*.

When the *Yovel* arrives, consecrated fields redeemed from the *Beis Hamikdash* do not revert to their original owners but are distributed among the *kohanim* of the serving *mishmar*. If a *kohen* himself redeemed such a field during the week his *mishmar* serves, one might argue — by the same logic as above — that since it is already under his control and would anyway go to *kohanim*, he should keep it entirely. The *baraita* on *sdeh achuza* rules otherwise: *achuzah shelo v'ein zu shelo* — it is his *achuzah* in the sense that he has a portion, but not entirely his. The word *achuzato* in the verse is read to mean he holds a share in it, not exclusive ownership. Therefore *yotzei mitachat yado u'mit'chalek l'chol ha-Kohanim* — even though the field is under his control, it is divided equally among all the *kohanim*. By *gezera shava* — *l'Kohen* in *gezel hager* is linked to *l'Kohen* in *sdeh achuza* — the same rule applies: the *gezel hager* must be divided equally regardless of the fact that it is in the *kohen*'s possession.

## Kohen Korban Rights

### Kohen Bring Anytime

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן לְכֹהֵן שֶׁבָּא מַקְרִיב קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו בְּכׇל עֵת וּבְכׇל שָׁעָה שֶׁיִּרְצֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּבָא בְּכׇל אַוַּת נַפְשׁוֹ״, ״וְשֵׁרֵת״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara records another *baraita* concerning the rights of a priest to offerings he sacrifices. **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta*, *Menaḥot* 13:17): **From where** is it derived **that a priest** may **come and sacrifice his offerings at any time and at any hour that he wishes** and does not have to wait for his priestly watch to serve in the Temple? **The verse states:** "And if a Levite comes from any of your gates out of all Israel, where he sojourns, **and comes with all the desire of his soul** unto the place that the Lord shall choose; **then he shall minister** in the name of the Lord his God" (Deuteronomy 18:6–7).

As a digression arising from the previous discussion, the *gemara* now examines in full the *halacha* that a *kohen* may bring his own *korban* at any time. A *baraita* asks: *minayin l'Kohen she'ba makriv korbanotav b'chol eit u'v'chol sha'ah she'yirtzeh* — from where do we know that a *kohen* may come and offer his *korbanot* at any time he wishes?

### Derivation Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִין שֶׁעֲבוֹדָתָהּ וְעוֹרָהּ שֶׁלּוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִישׁ אֶת קֳדָשָׁיו לוֹ יִהְיוּ״. הָא כֵּיצַד? אִם הָיָה בַּעַל מוּם – נוֹתְנָהּ לְכֹהֵן שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ מִשְׁמָר, וַעֲבוֹדָתָהּ וְעוֹרָהּ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And from where** is it derived **that the performance of** the offering's **service,** i.e., eating the meat of the animal he sacrifices, **and** the offering's **hide, belong to him? The verse states: "And every man's hallowed things shall be his"** (Numbers 5:10). **How so? If** this priest **was blemished, he gives** his offering **to** another **priest that is on the same priestly watch** as him to sacrifice it, **but the performance of its service and its hide belong to him.**

The source for the right to bring is *u'va b'chol avat nafsho v'shiret* — he comes whenever his soul desires and he serves. The source for the right to benefit from the *korban* — to keep the meat and the hide — is *ve-ish et kodashav lo yihyu*: a person retains his sacred offerings. These two verses together establish the compound right: to perform the *avodah* and to receive its benefits.

### Ba'al Mum Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיָה זָקֵן אוֹ חוֹלֶה – נוֹתְנָהּ לְכׇל כֹּהֵן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה, וַעֲבוֹדָתָהּ וְעוֹרָהּ לְאַנְשֵׁי מִשְׁמָר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And if he was old or sick,** so that he cannot perform the Temple service or eat from the offering, **he gives it to any priest he wishes** to sacrifice it, even to one not on his priestly watch, **and performance of its service and its hide** are given **to the members of the priestly watch.**

The *baraita* then addresses the more complex case of a *ba'al mum*: *im haya ba'al mum notnah l'Kohen she'ba'otah mishmar* — if the *kohen* has a disqualifying physical blemish, he cannot perform the *avodah* himself, so he assigns the *avodah* to the *kohen* of the serving *mishmar*. Two distinct questions arise: first, can he designate a specific *kohen* to perform the *avodah*, or must it be assigned generally to the *mishmar*? Second, does he retain the right to the meat and the hide?

On the question of designating who performs the *avodah*: since the *ba'al mum* cannot himself perform it, he cannot assign it to a specific *kohen* of his choosing. The right of designation derives from the right of personal performance, and since he has lost the latter, he loses the former as well. It goes to the *mishmar* generally.

On the question of the meat: here two versions of the *baraita* diverge sharply. According to one version, *ve'avodato ve'oro shelo* — the meat and the hide remain his, since a *kohen ba'al mum* retains the right to eat *kodashim* even though he cannot perform the *avodah*. His right to eat is independent of his right to perform. Rashi, however, has a different *girsa*: *ve'avodato ve'oro l'anshei mishmar* — the meat and the hide go to the *mishmar* equally, because his right to eat the meat is considered derivative of his right to perform the *avodah*, and since he has lost the latter, the meat is distributed equally. These two *girsa'ot* represent a fundamental dispute about whether the right to perform and the right to eat are independent or linked.

The *baraita* then addresses the mirror case: a *kohen* who is elderly or ill — able in principle to perform the *avodah* but unable to eat the meat. Such a *kohen*, since he retains the theoretical capacity to perform the *avodah*, can still assign it to whichever *kohen* he chooses. But because he cannot eat the meat, that right is distributed equally: *v'avodah savrah l'anshei mishmar*.

The *gemara* asks: *hai zaken o choleh heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If he is capable of performing the *avodah*, *avodah savrah nami k'vidi didei* — surely the same should apply to the meat, and he should be able to assign both. And if he is not capable of performing the *avodah*, how can he assign it to anyone else?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שֶׁיָּכוֹל לַעֲשׂוֹת עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק. עֲבוֹדָה – דְּכִי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק עֲבוֹדָה הִיא, וּמְשַׁוֵּי שָׁלִיחַ; אֲכִילָה – דְּכִי אָכֵיל עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק אֲכִילָה גַּסָּה הִיא, וַאֲכִילָה גַּסָּה לָאו כְּלוּם הוּא; מִשּׁוּם הָכִי עֲבוֹדָתָהּ וְעוֹרָהּ לְאַנְשֵׁי מִשְׁמָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** The *baraita* is referring to a case **where he is able to do** it **with difficulty.** With regard to the Temple **service, where** the *halakha* is that if **he performs it with difficulty it is** still considered performance of the Temple **service,** he is therefore able to **appoint an agent** to do it for him. With regard to **eating** the offering, **where** the *halakha* says that **if he eats** it **with difficulty it is excessive eating, and excessive eating is nothing,** i.e., he does not thereby fulfill the mitzva to eat the sacrificial portion, he is not able to appoint an agent to eat it for him. **Due to that** reason, **performance of its service and its hide** are given **to the members of the priestly watch.**

Rav Papa resolves this: *sheyachol la'asot al yedei ha'dachak* — he can manage to perform the *avodah* with great effort. When it comes to the *avodah*, *d'chi avid al yedei ha'dachak avodah hi* — struggling through the performance with great difficulty still constitutes valid *avodah*, so he retains the capacity to assign it. But when it comes to eating, *d'chi achil al yedei ha'dachak achila gassa hi* — forcing oneself to eat when one's body resists is categorized as *achila gassa*, which does not constitute a valid act of eating. Tosafos notes that there are gradations of *gassa*: a mild reluctance — as when one pushes through to dessert — differs from genuine bodily resistance that truly prevents meaningful consumption. In this case the level is the latter, and *achila gassa lav klum hu*. Therefore, because the *avodah* is within his capacity (however difficult), he may assign it; because eating is not within his capacity in any halachically meaningful sense, that right falls to the *mishmar* equally.

The broader principle emerging from both cases is that the right to perform the *avodah* and the right to eat the meat are, at least to some degree, independent entitlements.

### Tamei Korban Tzibur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אִם הָיָה כֹּהֵן טָמֵא בְּקׇרְבַּן צִבּוּר – נוֹתְנָהּ לְכׇל מִי שֶׁיִּרְצֶה, וַעֲבוֹדָתָהּ וְעוֹרָהּ לְאַנְשֵׁי מִשְׁמָר. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאִיכָּא טְהוֹרִים, טְמֵאִים מִי מָצוּ עָבְדִי?! וְאִי דְּלֵיכָּא טְהוֹרִים, עֲבוֹדָתָהּ וְעוֹרָהּ לְאַנְשֵׁי מִשְׁמָר?! הָא טְמֵאִים נִינְהוּ, וְלָא מָצוּ אָכְלִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says: If a priest** of the priestly watch **was ritually impure,** then **with regard to a communal offering he gives it to any** priest **he wishes, and performance of its service and its hide** are given **to the members of the priestly watch.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** where this *halakha* applies? **If there are ritually pure** priests available, then **can impure ones perform** the Temple service, and by extension appoint an agent to perform it in their stead? **And if there are no ritually pure** priests there, as all members of the priestly watch are impure, is the **performance of its service and its hide** given **to the members of the priestly watch?** Even though communal offerings are sacrificed in such a circumstance, the priests **are impure and are not able** to **eat** the offering, even though they may sacrifice it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, אֵימָא: לְבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין טְהוֹרִין שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ מִשְׁמָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in explanation: **Say** that they are given **to blemished** but **ritually pure** priests **who are on that priestly watch.** Even though blemished priests are disqualified from performing the Temple service and therefore the offering must be sacrificed by impure priests, the blemished priests are permitted to eat the sacrifice, as they are ritually pure.

Rav Sheshes addresses the case of a *kohen tamei* bringing a *korban tzibbur*. Rashi explains the scenario: the *mishmar* is impure and it falls to them to bring a *korban tzibbur* for which they are responsible. In such a case, the *halacha* is well-established that *tumah* is overridden for a *korban tzibbur* — the *korban* may be brought even in a state of *tumah* — but it is not eaten in a state of *tumah*. So a *tamei kohen* who has the right to bring the *korban* may assign the *avodah* to whichever *kohen* he chooses — *nosnoh l'chol mi she'yirtzeh*. But since he cannot eat it in his state of *tumah*, he cannot assign the eating, and *ve'avodasav ve'oro l'anshei mishmar*.

The *gemara* challenges: *heichi dami* — what is the precise scenario? If there are *tahor kohanim* present, how does a *tamei kohen* have the right to bring the *korban* in the first place? *Tahor kohanim* should be the ones bringing it. And if everyone is *tamei*, then even though the *kohen tamei* brings it, nobody can eat it — so who exactly are the *anshei mishmar* who are eating?

Rava resolves this: *eima l'ba'alei mumim tehorim sheba'oto mishmar* — all the *kohanim* without blemishes are *tamei*, so the *korban* must be brought by the *tamei kohanim*. The *ba'alei mumim*, however, are *tahor* — and while they cannot bring the *korban*, since they are *tahor* they can eat it. So the *tamei kohen* assigns the *avodah* to another *tamei kohen*, and the eating is done by the *ba'alei mumim tehorim*. The right to perform and the right to eat fall to entirely different groups.

### Kohen Gadol Onen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אִם הָיָה כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל אוֹנֵן – נוֹתְנָהּ לְכׇל כֹּהֵן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה, וַעֲבוֹדָתָהּ וְעוֹרָהּ לְאַנְשֵׁי מִשְׁמָר. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מַקְרִיב אוֹנֵן, וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק לֶאֱכוֹל לָעֶרֶב!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: If a High Priest was an acute mourner,** i.e., one whose immediate relative died on that day but had yet to be buried, and he had an offering to sacrifice on his own behalf, **he gives** the offering **to any priest that he wishes** to sacrifice it, **and performance of its service and its hide** are given **to the members of the priestly watch.** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rav Ashi's statement **teaching us? We** already **learned** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Zevaḥim* 11:3): **A High Priest may sacrifice** an offering even when he is **an acute mourner, but he does not eat** it during that day while he is an acute mourner **and does not** receive **a portion** of the sacrifice **to eat at night** after his acute mourning is finished. It follows from this *baraita* that since he sacrifices it by himself he is able to appoint another priest in his stead, and since he may not eat it, it is given to the priestly watch to eat. What, then, was the novelty of Rav Ashi's statement?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: כִּי חָס רַחֲמָנָא עֲלֵיהּ דְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל – לְקָרוֹבֵי הוּא, אֲבָל לְשַׁוּוֹיֵי שָׁלִיחַ – לָא מָצֵי מְשַׁוֵּי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It might **enter your mind to say** that **when the Merciful One had compassion on the High Priest,** permitting him to continue serving in the Temple even while in a state of acute mourning, it was for **him to sacrifice; but** with regard **to appointing an agent, he is not able** to **appoint** one. Therefore, Rav Ashi **teaches us** that he is able to appoint an agent, since he himself is permitted to perform the Temple service.

Rav Ashi addresses the case of a *Kohen Gadol* who is an *onen* — in the period of acute mourning following the death of a close relative. The verse *min ha'Mikdash lo yetzei v'lo yechalel et Mikdash Elokav* establishes that even in a state of *aninus*, the *Kohen Gadol* continues to perform the *avodah* but does not eat the *korbanot*. If such a *Kohen Gadol* brings his own *korban*, he may assign the *avodah* to whichever *kohen* he chooses — *nosno l'chol kohen she'yirtzeh* — but the meat goes to the *mishmar* equally, since he has no right to eat it.

The *gemara* asks: *mai kamashma lan* — what is the novelty here? We already know from a *mishna* that *Kohen Gadol makriv onen v'eino ochel*, and moreover, *v'eino cholek l'echol ba'erev* — he has no right even to a portion of the meat to be eaten later in the evening when his *aninus* ends. Clearly he cannot assign the eating.

The *gemara* answers: *salka da'atach amina ki chas Rachmana aleih d'Kohen Gadol l'kruvei hu* — one might have thought that the Torah's special dispensation allowing the *Kohen Gadol* to bring a *korban* even as an *onen* was a privilege specifically for his own personal performance of the *hakravah*, not a transferable right. If that were so, he could not assign it to another. The novelty of Rav Ashi's ruling is *lo matzi mashvei* — we might have thought he cannot assign it, but in fact he can: once he himself is entitled to perform the *hakravah*, that entitlement is assignable. The right to eat, by contrast, is entirely absent, and so it goes to the *mishmar*.

## Gezel Hager Death

### Gazlan Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹזֵל אֶת הַגֵּר, וְנִשְׁבַּע לוֹ, וָמֵת – הֲרֵי זֶה מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ לַכֹּהֲנִים, וְאָשָׁם לַמִּזְבֵּחַ; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם אֵין לָאִישׁ גּוֹאֵל לְהָשִׁיב הָאָשָׁם אֵלָיו, הָאָשָׁם הַמּוּשָׁב לַה׳ לַכֹּהֵן, מִלְּבַד אֵיל הַכִּפֻּרִים אֲשֶׁר יְכַפֶּר בּוֹ עָלָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who robs a convert and takes a** false **oath** denying having done so, **and** then the convert **dies,** the robber, in order to achieve repentance, **pays** the **principal,** i.e., the stolen item or, if it is no longer extant, its monetary value, **and** an additional **one-fifth** of its value **to the priests, and** presents **a guilt-offering to the altar, as it is stated: "But if the man has no kinsman to whom restitution may be made for the guilt, the restitution for guilt that is made shall be the Lord's, even the priest's; besides the ram of the atonement, whereby atonement shall be made for him"** (Numbers 5:8).

We return now to the case anticipated by the coming *mishna*: *ha'gozel es ha'ger v'nishba lo u'meis* — one who steals from a *ger*, swears falsely, and dies before making restitution. The *mishna* states: *harei zeh meshalem keren v'chomesh* — he pays the principal and the fifth — *l'Kohanim*, and *asham l'mizbe'ach* — the *korban asham* goes to the altar.

The verse providing the basis is *v'im ein la'ish go'el l'hashiv ha'asham eilav, ha'asham ha'mushav la'Hashem la'Kohen, milvad eil ha'kippurim asher yechalel bo alav*: if the *ger* has no redeemer to whom the *asham* — meaning the principal — can be returned, that *asham* which is returned goes to God and then to the *kohen*, in addition to the ram of atonement which is the *korban asham* itself.

### Money to Sons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה מַעֲלֶה אֶת הַכֶּסֶף וְאֶת הָאָשָׁם, וָמֵת – הַכֶּסֶף יִנָּתֵן לְבָנָיו, וְהָאָשָׁם יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב – וְיִמָּכֵר, וְיִפְּלוּ דָּמָיו לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If the robber **was bringing the money and the guilt-offering up** to Jerusalem **and he died** before paying the priests and bringing his offering, **the money shall be given to** the robber's **children, and the** animal designated for the **guilt-offering shall graze until it becomes blemished** and consequently disqualified from being sacrificed. **And** the animal **shall** then **be sold and** the **money** received **for it shall be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings.

Now consider what happens when the *gazlan* himself dies while carrying the money and the *korban* to the *Beis Hamikdash*: *hakesef yinasein l'vanav* — the money goes to his sons, not to the *kohanim*. This ruling reflects a fundamental conceptual point about the nature of the money.

### Asham After Death

The standard *halacha* for an *asham* whose owner has died: *yireh ad she'yista'eiv v'yimacheir v'yiplu damav l'nedava* — the animal grazes until it develops a blemish, is sold, and the proceeds go toward a communal *olah*.

### Money as Kaparah

The decisive insight is in understanding why the money goes to the sons. At first one might state the principle this way: the money is treated purely as a *kaparah* obligation rather than a monetary debt, and since there is no *kaparah* to perform for a dead man, the obligation dissolves and the sons keep the money. But this framing needs careful correction.

If the money were simply a monetary obligation — if the *kohanim* stood in the place of the *ger* as his assigned heirs — then even after the *gazlan*'s death, the money should be transferred to the *kohanim*, just as a stolen object would have to be returned to the estate of a deceased *nigzal*. The fact that it is not transferred to the *kohanim* is precisely what reveals that the money is viewed as a type of *korban*, a component of *kaparah*. And since one does not perform *kaparah* for a person after his death, no *korban* is brought, and by the same token, no money is given. The sons keep it.

Rashi makes this explicit: *she'gazlo d'zacha bei meesas ha'ger* — from a monetary standpoint the object already legally belonged to the *gazlan* from the moment the *ger* died without heirs, *ella d'va'ei l'me'evad hashavah ki heichi d'tehavi leih kapara* — but he was required to perform an act of return as part of his *kaparah*. *V'hashta tu leika kapara keivan d'mais leih* — now that he is dead, there is no longer any *kaparah* to be achieved, so the money stays with the sons.

This also raises a question — not explored by the *gemara* here — about the *chomesh* in an ordinary case of *gezeila* (not *gezel hager*). If the *gazlan* dies, the principal must be returned to the *nigzal* because it is a monetary obligation. But is the *chomesh* similarly a monetary obligation, or is it purely a *kaparah* component that would therefore not need to be paid after the *gazlan*'s death? The *gemara* leaves this question open.

### Money Given First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן הַכֶּסֶף לְאַנְשֵׁי מִשְׁמָר, וָמֵת – אֵין הַיּוֹרְשִׁין יְכוֹלִין לְהוֹצִיא מִיָּדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִתֵּן לַכֹּהֵן – לוֹ יִהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** If the robber **gave the money to the members of the priestly watch and** then **died** before they sacrificed his guilt-offering, **the heirs cannot remove** the money **from** the priests' **possession, as it is stated:** "And every man's hallowed things shall be his; **whatsoever any man gives to the priest, it shall be his"** (Numbers 5:10).

A related case: *nasan ha'kesef l'anshei mishmar u'mais* — the *gazlan* gave the money to the *kohanim* and then died before bringing the *korban*. The heirs claim the money back, arguing that since the *kaparah* was never completed, the transfer should be undone. The ruling is *ein ha'yorshim yecholim lehotzi miyadam* — the heirs cannot recover the money, since the verse *v'ish asher yitnen la'kohen lo yihyeh* establishes that once given to a *kohen*, it belongs to him.

### Order of Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן הַכֶּסֶף לִיהוֹיָרִיב, וְאָשָׁם לִידַעְיָה – יָצָא. אָשָׁם לִיהוֹיָרִיב וְכֶסֶף לִידַעְיָה – אִם קַיָּים הָאָשָׁם, יַקְרִיבוּהוּ בְּנֵי יְדַעְיָה; וְאִם לֹא, יַחֲזִיר וְיָבִיא אָשָׁם אַחֵר. שֶׁהַמֵּבִיא גְּזֵילוֹ עַד שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא אֲשָׁמוֹ – יָצָא; הֵבִיא אֲשָׁמוֹ עַד שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא גְּזֵילוֹ – לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If the robber **gave the money to** the priestly watch of **Joiarib and** then gave the **guilt-offering to** the priestly watch of **Jedaiah,** the following priestly watch, to sacrifice on his behalf, he has **fulfilled** his obligation. By contrast, if he first gave the **guilt-offering to** the priestly watch of **Joiarib and** then gave the **money to** the priestly watch of **Jedaiah, if** the animal designated for **the guilt-offering** is **extant,** then **members of** the priestly watch of **Jedaiah,** who received the money, **should sacrifice it. But if** it is **no** longer extant because the priestly watch of Joiarib had already sacrificed it, he **should return and bring another guilt-offering; for one who brings his stolen item** to the priests **before** he **brings his guilt-offering** has **fulfilled** his obligation, but one who **brings his guilt-offering before** he **brings his stolen item** has **not fulfilled** his obligation.

The *mishna* addresses the question of whether the order in which the money and the *korban* are given matters. The verse *ha-asham ha'mushav la'Hashem la'kohen milvad eil ha'kippurim* suggests money first, then *korban*. The *mishna* confirms: if you gave the money to one *mishmar* (represented by *Yehoyariv*, the first *mishmar*) and the *korban* to the next (represented by *Yedaya*), you are *yotzei*. But if you brought the *korban* first (*asham l'Yehoyariv*) and the money second (*hakesef l'Yedaya*): if the *korban* has not yet been offered, the *mishmar* of *Yedaya* may offer it, and you are *yotzei*. But if the *korban* was already offered before the money was given back, you were not *yotzei*, and you must return the money and bring a new *asham*. The logic is analogous to the general principle that one must obtain forgiveness from the person one has wronged before seeking atonement from God — the return must precede the *korban*.

### Keren Without Chomesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְלֹא נָתַן אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ – אֵין הַחוֹמֶשׁ מְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** Although he cannot sacrifice the offering before paying the principal, if **he gave the principal but did not** yet **give the** additional **one-fifth** payment, the lack of having given **the** additional **one-fifth** payment **does not preclude** sacrificing the offering.

Finally: *nasan et ha'keren v'lo nasan et ha'chomesh, ein ha'chomesh me'akeiv* — if one gave back the principal without the fifth, the fifth does not prevent the fulfillment of the obligation. One has still achieved *kaparah*, though one remains obligated to give the *chomesh*. What the *mishna* establishes overall is that the money of *gezel hager*, unlike the money owed in an ordinary case of *gezeila*, is a component of *kaparah* and not a monetary debt — and all the consequences that flow from that distinction follow accordingly.

## Money as Asham

### Asham = Keren

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אָשָׁם״ – זֶה קֶרֶן, ״הַמּוּשָׁב״ – זֶה חוֹמֶשׁ. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא: ״אָשָׁם״ – זֶה אַיִל?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in explanation of the verse cited in the mishna: "But if the man has no kinsman…the restitution for guilt that is made shall be the Lord's, even the priest's" (Numbers 5:8): With regard to the word **"guilt," this** is referring to the **principal,** i.e., the stolen item itself or its equivalent value; **"the restitution…that is made," this** is referring to the additional **one-fifth** payment. **Or** perhaps this is **not** the proper interpretation of the verse. **Rather,** it should be interpreted: **"Guilt," this** is referring to the **ram** of the guilt-offering.

A *baraita* derives the precise reference of each term in the key verse: *asham zeh keren, ha'mushav zeh chomesh* — the word *asham* in the phrase *ha'asham ha'mushav* refers to the principal, while *ha'mushav* — the returned portion — refers to the fifth. The *korban* is separately designated as the *eil ha'kippurim*. Another interpretation had suggested that *asham* refers to the *korban* itself — the ram — but the subsequent phrase *milvad eil ha'kippurim* rules that out. Similarly, one might read *ha'mushav* as referring to *kefel* — the double payment — making the *pasuk* a case of *geneivah*, not *gezeila*. But the verse *v'heshiv et ashamo b'rosho va'chamishito yosef alav* clarifies: we are speaking of an *asham* that is returned "at the head," meaning the principal, to which a fifth is added — not a doubling.

### Why Call It Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר שֶׁהֶחְזִירוֹ בַּלַּיְלָה – לֹא יָצָא. הֶחְזִירוֹ חֲצָאִין – לֹא יָצָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״אָשָׁם״ קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** Before continuing the *baraita* the Gemara interrupts to clarify: **And for what purpose** does the *baraita* distinguish between the two interpretations of guilt, as in any event, both the principal and the guilt-offering must be brought? The Gemara explains: **To exclude that which Rava** holds, **as Rava says:** With regard to the **stolen item of a convert that** the robber **returned at night,** the robber **did not fulfill** his obligation. And similarly, if **he returned it to him** in **halves,** he **did not fulfill** his obligation. **What is the reason? The Merciful One labeled** the stolen item with the term **"guilt,"** teaching that just as a guilt-offering cannot be offered at night or in halves, so too, the stolen item cannot be returned at night or in halves.

The halachic significance of the Torah designating the principal as an *asham* is now developed. Since the principal carries the status of an *asham* — a *korban* — it is subject to the rules governing *korbanot*.

### Cannot Return Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״מִלְּבַד אֵיל הַכִּפֻּרִים״, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: ״אָשָׁם״ – זֶה קֶרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **When it says** in that verse: **"Besides the ram of the atonement"** (Numbers 5:8), referring to the offering, **you must say** concerning the word **"guilt"** written earlier in the verse that **this** is referring to the **principal.**

Rava derives: *gezel hager she'hechziro ba'laila lo yatza* — if one returned the principal of *gezel hager* at night, one has not fulfilled the obligation. Just as a *korban* must be offered during the day, so too the return of this money must take place during daylight hours. Similarly, *hechziro chatziyo lo yatza* — returning only half does not suffice. The operative principle: *asham karya Rachmana* — the Torah called it an *asham*. One might further reason that delivering the money outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, or to a *kohen* outside the context of *avodah*, would also not fulfill the obligation, precisely because of this *korban*-like status.

This raises a natural comparison to the ordinary case of *gezeila* (not involving a *ger*). There too, returning the stolen object is a necessary precondition for *kaparah*. But in the ordinary case, the return is simply a monetary act — the *nigzal* is a private individual, the exchange takes place outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, no *kohen* is involved, and no day-restriction applies. Only in *gezel hager*, where the money is given to the *kohanim* with the structure of a *korban*, does the money itself acquire the full legal character of an *asham*.

### Pruta Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״אָשָׁם״ – זֶה קֶרֶן, ״הַמּוּשָׁב״ – זֶה חוֹמֶשׁ. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא: ״אָשָׁם״ – זֶה חוֹמֶשׁ? לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִמַּתְנִיתִין, דִּתְנַן: נָתַן לוֹ אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְלֹא נָתַן לוֹ אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ – אֵין הַחוֹמֶשׁ מְעַכֵּב. אַדְּרַבָּה, חוֹמֶשׁ מְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: With regard to the word **"guilt," this** is referring to the **principal; "the restitution…that is made," this** is referring to the additional **one-fifth** payment. **Or** perhaps this is **not** the proper interpretation of the verse. **Rather,** it should be interpreted: **"Guilt," this** is referring to the additional **one-fifth** payment. Before continuing the *baraita*, the Gemara interrupts to clarify: **For what purpose** does the *baraita* distinguish between the two interpretations? The Gemara explains: **To exclude** that which **the mishna** teaches, **as we learned** in the mishna: If he **gave him the principal but did not** yet **give him the** additional **one-fifth** payment, the lack of having given **the** additional **one-fifth** payment **does not preclude** sacrificing the offering. If "guilt" is referring to the additional one-fifth payment, then, **on the contrary,** it would follow that the lack of having given the additional **one-fifth** payment **precludes** sacrificing the offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת אֲשָׁמוֹ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ, וַחֲמִישִׁתוֹ״, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: ״אָשָׁם״ – זֶה קֶרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **When it says** in the previous verse: **"And he shall make restitution for his guilt in full, and** add unto it **the fifth part thereof"** (Numbers 5:7), **you must say** concerning the word **"guilt"** that **this** is referring to the **principal.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״אָשָׁם״ – זֶה קֶרֶן, ״הַמּוּשָׁב״ – זֶה חוֹמֶשׁ; וּבְגֶזֶל הַגֵּר הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא: ״הַמּוּשָׁב״ – זֶה כֶּפֶל, וּבִגְנֵיבַת הַגֵּר הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת אֲשָׁמוֹ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ, וַחֲמִישִׁתוֹ״ – הֲרֵי בְּמָמוֹן הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּם בָּרֹאשׁ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: With regard to the word **"guilt," this** is referring to the **principal; "the restitution…that is made," this** is referring to the additional **one-fifth** payment, **and the verse is speaking of robbery of a convert. Or** perhaps this is **not** the proper interpretation of the verse. **Rather,** it should be interpreted: **"The restitution…that is made," this** is referring to **double payment** that a thief must pay, **and the verse is speaking of theft from a convert. When it says** in the previous verse: **"And he shall make restitution for his guilt in full, and** add unto it **the fifth part thereof"** (Numbers 5:7), **the verse is speaking of money that is paid** exactly **according to the principal,** and not double payment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר רָבָא: גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר שֶׁהֶחְזִירוֹ בַּלַּיְלָה – לֹא יָצָא. הֶחְזִירוּהוּ חֲצָאִין – לֹא יָצָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״אָשָׁם״ קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** § Having quoted Rava's statement, the Gemara returns to discuss **the** matter **itself. Rava says:** With regard to the **stolen item of a convert that** the robber **returned at night,** the robber **did not fulfill** his obligation. And similarly, if **he returned it to him** in **halves,** he **did not fulfill** his obligation. **What is the reason? The Merciful One labeled** the stolen item with the term **"guilt,"** teaching that just as a guilt-offering cannot be offered at night or in halves, so too, the stolen item cannot be returned at night or in halves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה לְכׇל כֹּהֵן וְכֹהֵן – לֹא יָצָא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״הָאָשָׁם הַמּוּשָׁב״ – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא הֲשָׁבָה לְכׇל כֹּהֵן וְכֹהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** With regard to the **stolen item of a convert that does not have the value of one *peruta* for each and every priest** on the priestly watch, the robber **did not fulfill his obligation** by giving it to the priestly watch. **What is the reason? As it is written: "The restitution for guilt that is made,"** meaning that the robber has not fulfilled his obligation to return the stolen item **until there will be** halakhically significant **restitution made to each and every priest,** minimally one *peruta*. If the stolen item was of less value than can be distributed with each priest in the watch receiving at least one *peruta*, the robber must add to the payment so that each priest receives one *peruta*.

Rava further rules: *gezel hager she'ein bo shaveh pruta l'chol kohen v'kohen lo yatza* — if the amount of *gezel hager* is insufficient to provide a *pruta*'s worth to each individual *kohen* in the serving *mishmar*, the obligation is not fulfilled. This is derived from the *pasuk* *ha'asham ha'mushav* — the *asham* must be returned in a way that constitutes a valid distribution to *each kohen*. Since we have established that the money is divided equally among all members of the *mishmar*, each member must receive at least a *pruta*.

This creates a genuinely difficult practical problem. If someone stole a small amount from a *ger* and the serving *mishmar* is large, he may find it impossible to fulfill his obligation. The *gemara* does not resolve what such a person is to do, and simply notes the *halacha* without offering a remedy.

### Different Mishmar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: אֵין בּוֹ לְמִשְׁמֶרֶת יְהוֹיָרִיב וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ לְמִשְׁמֶרֶת יְדַעְיָה, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Based on this *halakha*, **Rava raises a dilemma:** If the stolen item **does not have** the value of one *peruta* **for** each priest on **the priestly watch of Joiarib,** which had many priests, **but it has** the value of one *peruta* **for** each priest on the **priestly watch of Jedaiah,** which had fewer priests, **what is** the *halakha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּיַהֲבֵיהּ לִידַעְיָה בְּמִשְׁמֶרֶת יְדַעְיָה – הָא אִית בֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** with regard to which Rava raised his dilemma? **If we say** that the dilemma is raised in a case **where he gave** the money **to** the priestly watch of **Jedaiah, during** the time of the Temple service of the **priestly watch of Jedaiah,** there would be no dilemma. **There is in** this payment enough value for each priest to receive one *peruta*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּיַהֲבֵיהּ לִידַעְיָה בְּמִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ דִּיהוֹיָרִיב. מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּלָאו מִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ הוּא – וְלָא כְּלוּם הוּא; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא – לִידַעְיָה קָאֵי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** to raise the dilemma in a case **where he gave it to** the **Jedaiah** priestly watch **during** the time of the Temple service of the **priestly watch of Joiarib;** in that case, **what** is the *halakha*? The Gemara explains the two possibilities: **Do we say** that **since it is not** during Jedaiah's **priestly watch, it is nothing,** i.e., it is not a fulfillment of the mitzva to return the stolen item? **Or perhaps** we say that **since it was not fit for** the Joiarib priestly watch, as it was of insufficient value, **from the outset it stands** ready **for** the **Jedaiah** priestly watch, and by giving it to them he fulfilled the mitzva? The Gemara comments: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

Rava raises a further question: what if the amount is insufficient for each member of the current *mishmar* (say, *Yehoyariv*'s week) to receive a *pruta*, but the following *mishmar* (*Yedaya*) is smaller and division there would yield a *pruta* per *kohen*? What if the *gazlan* gives the money to a member of *Yedaya* during *Yehoyariv*'s week?

The *gemara* first clarifies the precise scenario. If he waited until *Yedaya*'s week and gave it then, that is straightforward — each *kohen* received a *pruta* and he is clearly *yotzei*. The question is where he gave the money to *Yedaya* during *Yehoyariv*'s week. On one hand, since *Yehoyariv* cannot facilitate a valid distribution (no one would receive a *pruta*), perhaps the right to collect effectively passes to *Yedaya*, and the transfer to *Yedaya* during *Yehoyariv*'s week is a valid *hashavah*. On the other hand, since it is not *Yedaya*'s week, and the money has the status of a *korban*, perhaps giving it to a *mishmar* outside its serving week is not a valid act of return. The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teyku*.

## Money Fungibility

### Pool All Gezel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: כֹּהֲנִים שֶׁיַּחְלְקוּ גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר כְּנֶגֶד גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises** another **dilemma:** With regard to **priests, what is** the *halakha* concerning **whether they may divide** among themselves the restitution for **robbery of a convert,** with some priests taking a larger share **in exchange for** taking a smaller share in the restitution for another **robbery of a convert?** In other words, can the priests arrange that one priest or several priests will receive the restitution for one robbery and another priest or several priests will receive the restitution for a different robbery another time?

Rava poses a further question: *kohanim she'yichalku gezel ha'ger k'neged gezel ha'ger* — suppose many people came this week to return *gezel hager*, each in different amounts. May the *kohanim* pool all the money together and divide the total equally among themselves, so that each *kohen* receives the same total sum regardless of which *gezel hager* contributed to it? In other words, can *gezel hager* be treated as fungible money, allowing a kind of communal pooling?

### Korban Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן: ״אָשָׁם״ קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא – מָה אָשָׁם אֵין חוֹלְקִין אָשָׁם כְּנֶגֶד אָשָׁם, אַף גֶּזֶל אֵין חוֹלְקִין גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר כְּנֶגֶד גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר; אוֹ דִּלְמָא, גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר מָמוֹנָא הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** He explains the two possibilities: **Do we say** that **the Merciful One labeled** the stolen item with the term **"guilt,"** and therefore, **just as** with **a guilt-offering** the priests **may not divide** portions of **a guilt-offering,** with some priests taking a larger share **in exchange for** taking a smaller share in other portions of **a guilt-offering,** but rather all priests of the watch share in the sacrificial flesh, **so too** with the restitution for **robbery:** The priests **may not divide** the restitution for **robbery of a convert,** with some priests taking a larger share **in exchange for** taking a smaller share in the restitution for another **robbery of a convert? Or perhaps** the restitution for **robbery of a convert** paid to priests is not in fact an offering, but it **is monetary restitution,** and monetary restitution may be divided in this manner among the priests?

The comparison is to *korbanot*: one cannot take all the various *korbanot* brought in a given week, pool the meat, and distribute equal portions to each *kohen*. Each *korban* must be divided individually. Does the same apply to *gezel hager*?

### Separate Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ: ״אָשָׁם״ קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא. רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ בְּהֶדְיָא – אָמַר רָבָא: כֹּהֲנִים אֵין חוֹלְקִין גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר כְּנֶגֶד גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״אָשָׁם״ קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **then resolves it** himself: **The Merciful One labeled** the stolen item with the term **"guilt,"** so it may not be divided in this manner. **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, teaches it explicitly** as a ruling, and not as a dilemma and solution, that **Rava says: Priests may not divide** the restitution for **robbery of a convert** with some priests taking a larger share **in exchange for** taking a smaller share in the restitution for another **robbery of a convert. What is the reason? The Merciful One labeled** the stolen item with the term **"guilt."**

The *gemara* initially presents this as an open question, but then records that Rava concluded — and according to Rav Aha son of Rava, Rava taught it straightforwardly without hesitation: *asham kara Rachmana* — the Torah called it an *asham*. Just as *korbanot* cannot be pooled, each unit of *gezel hager* must be divided separately among the *kohanim*. Money, though fungible in ordinary commercial life, loses that fungibility here by virtue of its *asham* status.

What emerges from the entire sugya is a coherent and demanding legal framework. The money of *gezel hager* is not merely a monetary obligation redirected to the *kohanim* as surrogate heirs of the *ger*. It is an act of *kaparah*, carrying the full formal structure of a *korban*: it must be given during the day; it must yield a *pruta* to each *kohen*; it must be divided individually, not pooled; it must precede the *korban asham*; and if the *gazlan* dies, the obligation dissolves along with any possibility of *kaparah*, leaving the money with his heirs. The *gemara* will continue to explore the further implications of this framework.
