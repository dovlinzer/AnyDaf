# Sanhedrin 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishkav Zachar Ages

### Tanur Rabanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: זְכוּר – לֹא עָשׂוּ בּוֹ קָטָן כְּגָדוֹל, בְּהֵמָה – עָשׂוּ בָּהּ קְטַנָּה כִּגְדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to intercourse with **a male,** the Torah **does not deem a younger boy** to be **like an older boy;** but with regard to intercourse with **an animal,** the Torah **does deem a young** animal to be **like an old** animal.

The daf opens on Sanhedrin 54b, six lines from the bottom, with the passage *tanu rabbanan*. The *baraita* addresses male-on-male intercourse and notes, with reference to the word *zachor*—specifically as it appears with the *vav*, pointing to the one being penetrated—that *lo asu bo katan kegadol*: a minor is not treated the same as an adult. There is a meaningful distinction based on the age of the *nishkav*, the penetrated party.

To appreciate this distinction, it helps first to recall the framework for heterosexual *arayot*. In a heterosexual context, only one of the two parties need be an adult for that adult to be *chayav*; however, the other party must be of an age at which the act is legally considered intercourse at all. For a minor male in such a context, the threshold is nine years and a day—below that age, the act is not legally defined as intercourse, so the adult woman would bear no liability. For a minor female, the threshold is three years and a day, based on *Chazal*'s understanding of whether the hymen would regenerate and whether the act constitutes legally cognizable intercourse.

The question now concerns *mishkav zachar* specifically: if a minor male is the *nishkav* and an adult is the *shochev*, what age of the *nishkav* transforms the act into legally cognizable intercourse?

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״לֹא עָשׂוּ בּוֹ קָטָן כְּגָדוֹל״? אָמַר רַב: לֹא עָשׂוּ בִּיאַת פָּחוֹת מִבֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים כְּבֶן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לֹא עָשׂוּ בִּיאַת פָּחוֹת מִבֶּן שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים כְּבֶן שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** does it mean that the Torah **does not deem a younger boy** to be **like an older boy? Rav says:** It means that the Torah **does not deem the intercourse of one who is less than nine years old** to be **like** the intercourse of **one who is** at least **nine years old,** as for a male's act of intercourse to have the legal status of full-fledged intercourse the minimum age is nine years. **And Shmuel says:** The Torah **does not deem the intercourse of** a child who is **less than three years old** to be **like** that of **one who is three years old.**

On this question, *Rav* and *Shmuel* dispute. *Rav* holds: *lo asu biat pachat miben tesha shanim keben tesha shanim*—the threshold is nine years and a day. Below nine years and a day, the act is not defined as intercourse; above it, it is. *Shmuel* holds: *lo asu biat pachat miben shalosh shanim keben shalosh shanim*—the threshold is three years and a day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַב סָבַר: כֹּל דְּאִיתֵיהּ בְּשׁוֹכֵב – אִיתֵיהּ בְּנִשְׁכָּב, וְכֹל דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בְּשׁוֹכֵב – לֵיתֵיהּ בְּנִשְׁכָּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do** Rav and Shmuel **disagree?** The Gemara answers: **Rav holds** that **any** *halakha* **that applies to one who engages in intercourse** actively **applies to one who engages in intercourse** passively, **and any** *halakha* **that does not apply to one who engages in intercourse** actively **does not apply to one who engages in intercourse** passively. Therefore, just as one who engages in intercourse actively is not liable if he is less than nine years old, as the intercourse of such a child does not have the halakhic status of intercourse, so too, if a child who is less than nine years old engages in homosexual intercourse passively, the one who engages in intercourse with him is not liable.

The *Gemara* probes the conceptual basis of their disagreement. *Rav* reasons: *kol d'ita b'shochev ita b'nishkav, v'kol d'leita b'shochev leita b'nishkav*—the *shochev* and the *nishkav* are linked. Since a male is considered capable of the act of intercourse as the penetrating party at nine years and a day, the same age defines him as a meaningful participant when he is the penetrated party. *Shmuel*, by contrast, reasons from the Torah's own language: *mishkevei ishah ketiv*—the verse speaks of "the lyings of a woman." The Torah frames the role of the *nishkav* by analogy to a woman, and therefore the threshold is three years and a day, the same age that governs a female's participation in intercourse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל סָבַר: ״מִשְׁכְּבֵי אִשָּׁה״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel holds: It is written:** "And you shall not lie with a male **as with a woman,"** indicating that the *halakha* of a male who engages in intercourse passively is like that of a woman; just as the intercourse of a woman has the halakhic status of intercourse from when she is three years old, the same is true with regard to a male who engages in intercourse passively. Consequently, in Shmuel's opinion, one who engages in intercourse with a male who is older than three is liable.

### Baraita kVetat Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַב: זָכָר בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav:** One who engages in homosexual intercourse with **a male aged nine years and one day,**

The *Gemara* then cites a *baraita* that supports *Rav*: *zachar ben tesha shanim v'yom echad*—when it comes to *mishkav zachar*, the relevant threshold is nine years and a day. *Rashi* explains that this must be addressing the *nishkav*, because the nine-year threshold for the *shochev* was never in dispute—any time a male is doing the penetrating, nine years and a day is the operative standard. Since the *baraita* explicitly specifies *mishkav zachar* and declares nine years and a day, it is telling us that the same age applies to the *nishkav* as well—in accordance with *Rav*'s position.

## Bestiality Acts

### Baraita Behema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַבָּא עַל הַבְּהֵמָה, בֵּין כְּדַרְכָּהּ בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ, וְהָאִשָּׁה הַמְּבִיאָה אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה עָלֶיהָ, בֵּין כְּדַרְכָּהּ בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** or **one who engages in intercourse with an animal, whether in a typical manner or in an atypical manner,** i.e., anal intercourse, **and** similarly **a woman who engages in intercourse with an animal, whether in a typical manner or in an atypical manner,** is **liable.** This *baraita* sets the minimum age for the passive male at nine years and one day.

The *baraita* continues with the laws of bestiality. *Haba al ha-behemah bein kedarka bein shelo kedarka*—one who has intercourse with a female animal, whether vaginally or anally, is *chayav*. Likewise, *v'ishah ha-mevi'ah behemah aleha bein kedarka bein shelo kedarka*—a woman who has intercourse with a male animal, whether it penetrates her vaginally or anally, is *chayav*. No distinction is drawn between the modes of penetration in either case.

### Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא: בְּאִשָּׁה שְׁנֵי מִשְׁכָּבוֹת, וּבִבְהֵמָה מִשְׁכָּב אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Rav Ḥisda taught: With regard to a woman** there are **two** manners of **lying.** A woman who engages in intercourse with an animal, whether it is vaginal or anal intercourse, is liable. **But with regard to** a man who engages in intercourse with **an animal** there is only **one** manner of **lying,** i.e., vaginal intercourse.

At the opening of *amud aleph*, *Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda* offers an *explication*: *be'ishah shnei mishkavot u've-vehemah mishkav echad*—by a woman there are two *mishkavot*, vaginal and anal, as derived from the term *mishkevei ishah*, but by an animal there is only one, since the Torah does not use the plural *mishkevei* in the context of an animal. On this reading, when a man penetrates a female animal, the prohibition applies only to vaginal intercourse. The *baraita* that treated both modes as equivalent is thereby challenged.

### Rav Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: אַדְּרַבָּה, אִשָּׁה דְּאוֹרְחַהּ הִיא אַמִּשְׁכָּב מִיחַיַּיב, אַמִּידַּעַם אַחֲרִינָא לָא מִיחַיַּיב. בְּהֵמָה דְּלָאו אוֹרְחָא הִיא, לִחַיַּיב עֲלַהּ עַל כׇּל נֶקֶב וָנֶקֶב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this** opinion: **On the contrary, a woman, whose typical manner** of intercourse **is** vaginal, **is rendered liable for lying** with an animal only in that manner; she **is not rendered liable for something else,** i.e., for engaging in anal intercourse with an animal. With regard to a man who engages in intercourse with **an animal,** by contrast, **since it is not its typical** conduct to engage in intercourse with a man, he **should be rendered liable for** engaging in intercourse with **it through each and every orifice.**

*Rav Pappa* presses back: one could argue the exact opposite. *Ishah de'orchah hi mishkav mechayev, amida achrina lo mechayev*—for a woman, since intercourse is the natural and expected form of sexual activity, one might reason that only the natural mode (vaginal) makes one liable, not the alternative (anal). But *be-vehemah delav orchah hi*—since intercourse with an animal is in no sense a natural act, the Torah's prohibition could be said to extend to every orifice regardless, since no particular mode is more "natural" than another. He acknowledges the slight tension in this reasoning—penetration of a woman by an animal is also not natural—but the challenge stands: the absence of the plural *mishkevei* by an animal does not necessarily restrict the prohibition; it might expand it.

### Tanya - No Chiyuv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא (דְּלָא כְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ): זָכָר בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד הַבָּא עַל הַבְּהֵמָה, בֵּין כְּדַרְכָּהּ בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ, וְהָאִשָּׁה הַמְּבִיאָה אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה עָלֶיהָ, בֵּין כְּדַרְכָּהּ בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with neither of their** opinions: One who engages in homosexual intercourse with **a male aged nine years and one day,** or **one who engages in intercourse with an animal, whether in a typical manner, or in an atypical manner,** i.e., anal intercourse, **and** similarly, **a woman who engages in intercourse with an animal, whether in a typical manner or in an atypical manner,** are **liable.** Evidently, there is no difference between the nature of the transgression of a woman who engages in bestiality and a man who engages in bestiality in this regard.

A *baraita* is then cited—in some versions with the notation *tanya d'lo kevateivhu*, that this *baraita* sides with neither position: *zachar ben tesha shanim v'yom echad*, and then, *haba b'vehemah bein kedarka bein shelo kedarka v'ishah ha-mevi'ah behemah aleha bein kedarka bein shelo kedarka, chayav*. The *baraita* makes no distinction whatsoever in the bestiality cases—neither in the case of a man penetrating a female animal nor in the case of a male animal penetrating a woman—between vaginal and anal intercourse. Both modes are equally prohibited and equally carry liability.

## Me'arah Definition

### Me'arah Bevirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: הַמְעָרֶה בִּזְכוּר, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rava:** With regard to **one who performs the initial stage of intercourse with** another **male,** i.e., insertion of the penis, without completing the intercourse, **what is** the *halakha*? Is he liable for engaging in homosexual intercourse?

*Ravina* poses a question to *Rava*: *ha-me'areh be-zachar mahu?* In a heterosexual context, the legally operative moment of intercourse is *me'arah*—initial penetration, when the tip of the penis enters the vagina. A prior stage, *neshikah*, involves contact without entry and is not legally sufficient; and *gmar bi'ah* is full penetration. The question is whether this same threshold of *me'arah* applies to *mishkav zachar*.

### Mishkevei Ishah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְעָרֶה בִּזְכוּר – מִשְׁכְּבֵי אִשָּׁה כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ. אֶלָּא, הַמְעָרֶה בִּבְהֵמָה, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: With regard to **one who performs the initial stage of intercourse with a male,** what is the question? The expression **"as with a woman"** (Leviticus 18:22) **is written with regard to him,** which indicates that any act that is considered an act of intercourse with a woman is also considered an act of intercourse with a man. **Rather,** the question is as follows: With regard to **one who performs the initial stage of intercourse with an animal, what is** the *halakha*?

The *Gemara* responds almost dismissively: *mishkevei ishah ketiv bah*—the Torah itself defines the act of male-on-male intercourse through the language of "the lyings of a woman." Since the prohibition is explicitly framed by reference to heterosexual intercourse, including its anal variant, the same definition of intercourse—*me'arah*, initial penetration—carries over. What is notable, and conceptually significant, is that the *Gemara* does not reach this conclusion by appeal to a general *hekesh* linking all *arayot* to one another. The *arayot* framework does not automatically encompass *mishkav zachar*; rather, it is specifically the phrase *mishkevei ishah* that does the work of importing the standard definition of intercourse into this case.

### Me'arah Behema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְהַעֲרָאָה דִּכְתִיבָא גַּבֵּי אֲחוֹת אָבִיו וַאֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ, דְּלָא צְרִיכָא, דְּהָא אִיתַּקַּשׁ לְהַעֲרָאָה דְּנִדָּה – תְּנֵיהוּ עִנְיָן לְהַעֲרָאָה דִבְהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him:** In the verse: "And you shall not uncover the nakedness of your mother's sister nor of your father's sister; for he has made naked [*he'era*] his relative" (Leviticus 20:19), the word *he'era* alludes to the initial stage of intercourse [*ha'ara*]. **If** the word *he'era* **is not** needed for the **matter of the initial stage of intercourse** in the context **where it is written,** i.e., for the prohibition of intercourse with one's **father's sister and** one's **mother's sister, as it is not necessary** in that context since the *halakha* of the initial stage of intercourse with regard to all forbidden sexual relations is **compared** by the Torah **to** the *halakha* of **the initial stage of intercourse** mentioned **with regard to a menstruating woman, apply it** instead **to** the **matter of the initial stage of intercourse with an animal.** The superfluous expression that appears in the verse concerning one's aunt teaches that the initial stage of intercourse is considered intercourse even with regard to an animal.

The next question is whether *me'arah* also defines intercourse in the context of bestiality. *Amar lei*—the *Gemara* answers through the interpretive principle of *im eino inyan*. In *Vayikra*, the word *he'erah*—"he uncovered her source"—appears in two places in the context of *arayot*: once regarding a *niddah*, and once regarding an aunt (*achot aviv* or *achot imo*). From the *niddah* passage, we derive the definition of *me'arah* for all *arayot*, since all forbidden sexual relationships are linked to one another through a *hekesh*. Given that derivation, why does the Torah need to repeat *he'erah* in the context of an aunt? Since the verse about the aunt is redundant for the standard *arayot* application, the principle of *im eino inyan* directs us to apply it elsewhere—specifically to bestiality, a case not linked by *hekesh* to the other *arayot* and not covered by *mishkevei ishah* as *mishkav zachar* was. Thus, *me'arah* governs the act of intercourse in bestiality as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי, בְּהֵמָה מֵחַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין הִיא, לְמָה לִי דִּכְתִיב לְהַעֲרָאָה דִּידַהּ גַּבֵּי חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת? לִכְתְּבֵיהּ גַּבֵּי חַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין, וְלִיגְמוֹר חַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין מֵחַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Since** one who engages in intercourse with **an animal is among those who are liable** to receive **a** court-imposed **death** penalty, **why do I** need the *halakha* of one who performs the **initial stage of intercourse with** an animal **to be written** in a verse **concerning those who are liable** to receive ***karet*,** i.e., the case of one who engages in intercourse with his aunt? **Let** the Torah **write** this *halakha* **with regard to those who are liable** to receive **a** court-imposed **death** penalty, **and** one would then **derive** this *halakha* concerning **those who are liable** to receive **a** court-imposed **death** penalty **from** the *halakha* of **those who are liable** to receive **a** court-imposed **death** penalty, and not from the less relevant *halakha* of one who engages in intercourse with his aunt, who is punished with *karet*.

### Why Aunt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹאִיל וְכוּלֵּיהּ קְרָא לִדְרָשָׁא הוּא דְּאָתֵי, כְּתִיבָא נָמֵי מִילְּתָא דִּדְרָשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Since the entire verse** about the punishment of one who engages in intercourse with the sister of his father or mother is superfluous, and **comes for** the sake of **exposition,** as this *halakha* is already stated in other verses (see Leviticus 18:12–13), this **matter,** i.e., the fact that the initial stage of intercourse is halakhically defined as intercourse, **is also written** in this verse for the purpose **of an exposition,** i.e., in order to indicate that this principle holds true with regard to bestiality as well.

One might ask: if the Torah wanted to use this extra mention of *he'erah* to teach us about bestiality, why did it place it in the context of an aunt, a case of *chayvei keritot*, rather than in a case of *chayvei mitot beit din*, which would be a more natural extension to bestiality (itself a capital offense)? The *Gemara* answers: *ho'il ve'chulei kra le-drasha hu de'ata*—the entire verse concerning *achot av* and *achot em* is already replete with *drashot*. As we will see in *Yevamot*, the prohibition appears in *Acharei Mot* and the punishment in *Kedoshim*, and the verse in *Kedoshim* is in any case largely extra, making it a rich source for multiple *derashot*. Since the verse is packed with exegetical content, it accommodates one more—the extension of *me'arah* to the case of bestiality. The result is that the same definition of intercourse, *me'arah*, applies equally to heterosexual *arayot*, to *mishkav zachar*, and to bestiality.

## Self-Penetration

### Ha-Me'arah Be-Atzmo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב אַחָדְבוּי בַּר אַמֵּי מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הַמְעָרֶה בְּעַצְמוֹ, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קְבַסְתַּן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥadevoi bar Ami asked Rav Sheshet:** With regard to **one who performs the initial stage of** homosexual **intercourse on himself, what is** the *halakha*? Is he liable for homosexual intercourse? Rav Sheshet **said to him: You disgust me** with your question; such an act is not possible.

*Rav Achdivoi bar Ami* poses to *Rav Sheshet* a question that the *Gemara* itself treats as bizarre: *ha-me'areh be-atzmo mahu?* If a man has an exceptionally long penis and is physically capable of bending it to insert it into his own anus, is he *chayav* for *mishkav zachar*? The underlying assumption of the question—that an act of intercourse might be committed by a single person against himself—is startling, since one would naturally expect that *mishkav zachar* requires two parties. *Rav Sheshet*'s response, *kabastan*, conveys something like "you are troubling me with an absurd question."

### Meshamesh Meit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַאי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ? בְּקוֹשִׁי לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, כִּי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בִּמְשַׁמֵּשׁ מֵת. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְשַׁמֵּשׁ מֵת בַּעֲרָיוֹת פָּטוּר, הָכָא פָּטוּר. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר חַיָּיב, הָכָא מִיחַיַּיב תַּרְתֵּי: מִיחַיַּיב אַשּׁוֹכֵב, וּמִיחַיַּיב אַנִּשְׁכָּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: What is your dilemma?** With regard to doing so **with an erect** penis, **you cannot find** such a case. **You can find it** only **when** one **performs** this **act of intercourse** with **a flaccid** penis. And the *halakha* is subject to a dispute: **According to the one who says** that a man **who engages in intercourse** with **a flaccid** penis, **with** one of **those with whom relations are forbidden,** is **exempt,** as that is not considered intercourse, **here** too, when one does so to himself, he is **exempt. And according to the one who says** that he is **liable,** he is **rendered liable here** for transgressing **two** prohibitions according to Rabbi Yishmael; he is **rendered liable for engaging in** homosexual **intercourse** actively, **and** he is **rendered liable for engaging in** homosexual **intercourse** passively.

*Rav Ashi* engages the question more analytically. *Bekoshi lo mishkachat la*—in any case where the man actually has an erection, this act is physically impossible. *Ki mishkachat la bi-mshamesh meit*—the only scenario in which it could occur is if the penis is non-erect, a state called *meshamesh meit*. The answer then follows from an existing dispute: *le-man d'amar meshamesh meit ba-arayot patur*, if one holds that intercourse with a non-erect penis is exempt in the context of *arayot*, then here too the man would be exempt. But *le-man d'amar chayav*, if one holds that even *meshamesh meit* constitutes cognizable intercourse, then *hacha nechayev tartei*—he would be liable twice: once as the *shochev* and once as the *nishkav*.

This conclusion is, as the *Gemara* presents it, quite striking. The more intuitive response would be that an act of intercourse is categorically defined as occurring between two parties, and that a person cannot constitute both simultaneously. The *Gemara* passes over that objection entirely, resolving the case instead on the axis of *meshamesh meit*. One is reminded of a well-known *Rosh Yeshiva* story in which students debate whether an *androginos* could betroth himself—after an elaborate discussion, the answer given is simply that he cannot because she is his sister. The punch line cuts through the wrong analytical frame entirely. Here too, the *Gemara*'s answer operates on a different axis than one might expect, and the result—double liability for self-penetration—remains genuinely difficult.

## Non-Jew & Animal

### Takala v. Kalon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: גּוֹי הַבָּא עַל הַבְּהֵמָה מַהוּ? תַּקָּלָה וְקָלוֹן בָּעֵינַן, וְהָכָא תַּקָּלָה אִיכָּא, קָלוֹן לֵיכָּא? אוֹ דִילְמָא תַּקָּלָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין קָלוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** The students **asked Rav Sheshet:** With regard to **a gentile who engages in intercourse with an animal, what is** the *halakha*? Must the animal be killed? The Gemara elaborates: **Do we need** two reasons to kill the animal, namely that it caused **a calamity and** that it caused **shame, and** therefore **here** the animal is not killed, as while **there is a calamity,** as it caused a person to sin and be executed, **there is no shame,** as the Jewish court is not responsible for the shame of a gentile? **Or perhaps** one reason is enough, and an animal is killed because of the **calamity** it caused **even if there is no shame?**

The sugya now shifts focus from the liability of the person engaged in bestiality to the legal status of the animal itself. The *Mishnah* (on daf 58a) records that an animal involved in a capital sexual offense is stoned, and provides two explanations. The first: *im adam chata behemah be-mah chat'ah? ella le-pi she-ba l'adam takalah al yadah*—if a man sinned, what did the animal sin? Rather, because the animal caused the man to stumble, it is stoned. The second (*davar acher*): *shelo te-hei overet ba-shuk ve-yomru zu hi she-niskal ploni al yadah*—so that the animal should not be walking through the marketplace and people say, "This is the animal on account of which so-and-so was stoned." The first reason is *takalah*—the animal was a stumbling block to sin; the second is *kalon*—public shame and embarrassment.

With this background, *Rav Sheshet* is asked: if a non-Jew (*oved kochavim*) has intercourse with an animal that is subject to Jewish jurisdiction, what is done with the animal? The question is whether both *takalah* and *kalon* are required to trigger the stoning of the animal. *V'hacha takalah ika kalon leika*—there is clearly *takalah*, since bestiality is one of the seven *mitzvot benei Noach*, but there may be no *kalon*. *Rashi* offers two explanations for why shame may be absent: either because such conduct is, sadly, less aberrant among non-Jews and therefore less socially scandalous, or, less pejoratively, because the Torah's concern for protecting a person's honor extends only to Jews, not to non-Jews. The question therefore is: does *takalah* alone suffice, or must both elements be present?

### Rav Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: תְּנֵיתוּהָ, מָה אִילָנוֹת שֶׁאֵין אוֹכְלִין וְאֵין שׁוֹתִין וְאֵין מְרִיחִין, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: הַשְׁחֵת, שְׂרוֹף וְכַלֵּה, הוֹאִיל וּבָאת לְאָדָם תַּקָּלָה עַל יָדָן. הַמַּתְעֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ מִדַּרְכֵי חַיִּים לְדַרְכֵי מִיתָה – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: You learned** the answer to this question in a *baraita*: **If** with regard to **trees, which neither eat nor drink nor smell,** and nevertheless, if they are used in idolatrous rites, **the Torah says: Destroy, burn, and demolish** them (see Deuteronomy, chapters 7, 12), and the reason is **since a calamity was caused to people by them,** then with regard to one **who leads another astray from the ways of life to the ways of death, all the more so** he is liable to be destroyed. It can be derived from here that any item used for a transgression that renders one liable to be executed should be destroyed.

*Rav Sheshet* responds: *tanituah*—the answer is taught in a *baraita*. The *baraita* concerns the obligation to destroy trees used as *asherot* (idolatrous trees). *Ma ilanos she-ein ochlim ve-ein shotim ve-ein merichim*—these trees do not eat, drink, or smell. Yet the Torah commands: *hashcheit srof ve-chaleh*—destroy, burn, and eliminate them, because *ba'u al yadam takalah*—they caused people to sin and served as the object of sin. *Kal va-chomer*, the *baraita* continues, one who actively causes another person to stray from the path of life to the path of destruction is certainly deserving of destruction. The operative logic is that *takalah* alone—having been the object through which a sin was committed—suffices as grounds for destruction. If it suffices for the *asherot*, it should suffice for the animal of a non-Jew as well.

### Ela Me'ata

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, גּוֹי הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לִבְהֶמְתּוֹ תִּיתְּסַר וּמִקַּטְלָא? מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דִּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל לָא אָסַר וּלְגוֹי אָסַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this ruling: **If that is so,** in the case of **a gentile who bows down to his animal,** the animal **should be forbidden,** i.e., it should be prohibited to derive benefit from it, **and it should be killed.** The Gemara answers: **Is there anything that is not forbidden to a Jew, but is forbidden to a gentile?** Since a Jew who bows down to an animal does not render it forbidden (see *Temura* 29b), a gentile who does so does not render it forbidden either.

The *Gemara* challenges this reasoning immediately: *ela me'ata*, if *takalah* alone is truly sufficient, then *oved kochavim ha-mishtachaveh le-vehemto titasar maktala?*—if a non-Jew bows down to his own animal in an act of *avodah zarah*, should that animal not also become forbidden and subject to death? The same logic of *takalah* would seem to apply. But this conclusion is untenable, because *de-Yisrael lo asur oved kochavim asur?*—it cannot be that the law is stricter for a non-Jew than for a Jew. In the case of a Jew who bows down to an animal, the animal does not thereby become forbidden or subject to stoning. It would be incoherent to rule that a non-Jew's act of worship does the same. Moreover, even setting aside the Jewish-non-Jewish comparison, there is a broader problem: if *takalah* from *avodah zarah* genuinely drives the logic behind destroying the *asherot*, one would expect the same reasoning to apply to a Jew's animal that was worshipped—and it does not. The *Gemara* thus concludes that something about this *avodah zarah* framework is out of sync with the *takalah* logic, and the derivation from *asherot* cannot stand as stated.

### Abaye - Shame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִשְׂרָאֵל גּוּפֵיהּ לִיתְּסַר, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַרְבִיעָה? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: זֶה קְלוֹנוֹ מְרוּבֶּה, וְזֶה קְלוֹנוֹ מוּעָט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges that assumption: With regard to **a Jew himself** who bows down to his animal, **let** the animal **be forbidden, just as it is in** a case of **bestiality,** when the animal is forbidden and is killed. **Abaye says:** The cases are not the same. In **this** case, where one commits bestiality, **his shame is great; but** in **that** case, where one worships an animal, **his shame is slight,** and he will not be so disgraced if the animal is left alive.

*Abaye* offers a resolution. *Ze klono meruba ve-ze klono mu'at*—there is a meaningful difference in the degree of shame involved. Intercourse with an animal (*bi'ah*) involves an extreme degree of degradation and shame; bowing down to an animal (*hishtachavayah*) does not carry the same level of disgrace. This is why, in the case of a Jew, we stone the animal for bestiality but not for *avodah zarah*—the threshold for killing a living animal is high, and that threshold is reached only when the act carries a particularly severe *kalon*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי אִילָנוֹת, דְּאֵין קְלוֹנָן מְרוּבֶּה, וְאָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: הַשְׁחֵת, שְׂרוֹף וְכַלֵּה! בְּבַעֲלֵי חַיִּים קָאָמְרִינַן, דְּחָס רַחֲמָנָא עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to **trees** that are used in idolatrous rites, in **which** case **the shame of** the idol worshippers **is not great, the Torah** nevertheless **says: Destroy, burn, and demolish** them. The Gemara answers: **We are speaking of living animals.** The *halakha* is different there, **as the Merciful One has pity on them.** Therefore, if the shame of the person is not great, the animal is not killed.

One might then object: what of the *asherot*, which are destroyed even though bowing down to them is not as shameful as bestiality, and they are certainly not living? *Abaye* responds: *beva'alei chayim ka'aminan, dechas Rachmana aleihu*—we are speaking of living animals, and the Torah has particular compassion for them. Destroy inanimate objects of *avodah zarah*, yes, but to kill a living animal requires a much higher threshold. That threshold, for *Abaye*, is an elevated degree of shame—which is present in bestiality but not in *avodah zarah* contexts. As to how *Abaye*'s framework applies to the question about the non-Jew, *Rashi* preserves two possibilities: either shame is indeed required and is absent in the non-Jew's case (so the animal would not be stoned), or shame is present even in that case. *Abaye*'s primary contribution is to explain the existing evidence—why bestiality triggers stoning of the animal while *avodah zarah* does not—before the non-Jewish case is even resolved.

### Rava - Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה, בְּהֵמָה נֶהֱנֵית מֵעֲבֵירָה – תֵּיהָרֵג.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that there is a different reason for the distinction between an animal that was worshipped and an animal with which one committed bestiality: **The Torah states** that because the **animal enjoyed** the **transgression, it must be killed.** This cannot be said about an animal that was worshipped.

*Rava* offers an alternative explanation. *Amar Torah: behemah nehenis me'aveirah, tehareig*—the Torah's principle is that an animal that derived benefit or pleasure from the sinful act is executed. In the case of bestiality, the animal is regarded as an active and benefiting participant in the act; in the case of *avodah zarah*, the animal is merely an object of worship and derives no such benefit. By contrast, *va-harei ilanos de-ein nehenin me-aveirah*—but the trees of *asherot* did not benefit from the act either, yet they are destroyed. *Rava* gives the same answer as *Abaye* on this point: *beva'alei chayim kamina, dechas Rachmana aleihu*—we are discussing living creatures, for whom the Torah maintains a higher threshold. Trees do not implicate that threshold. For *Rava*, the operative distinction between bestiality and *avodah zarah* in the case of living animals is the animal's benefit or pleasure rather than the degree of shame. Applying this to the non-Jew: since the animal still derived benefit from the act regardless of the nationality of the human party, *Rava*'s logic would presumably lead to stoning the animal even in the case of the non-Jew—*takalah* plus the animal's benefit together suffice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי אִילָנוֹת, דְּאֵין נֶהֱנִין מֵעֲבֵירָה, וְאָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: הַשְׁחֵת, שְׂרוֹף וְכַלֵּה! בְּבַעֲלֵי חַיִּים קָאָמְרִינַן, דְּחָס רַחֲמָנָא עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to **trees** that are used in idolatrous rites, **which do not enjoy** the **transgression,** nevertheless **the Torah says: Destroy, burn, and demolish.** The Gemara answers: **We are speaking of living animals.** The *halakha* is different there, **as the Merciful One has pity on them.** Therefore, an animal is killed only if it enjoyed the transgression.

## Mishnah Reasons

### Seifa - Public

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דָּבָר אַחֵר, שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא בְּהֵמָה עוֹבֶרֶת בַּשּׁוּק וְיֹאמְרוּ: ״זוֹ הִיא שֶׁנִּסְקַל פְּלוֹנִי עַל יָדָהּ.״ מַאי לָאו,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution from the mishna to the dilemma concerning an animal with which a gentile committed bestiality: **Alternatively,** it is **so that** this **animal will not pass through the marketplace, and** those who see it **will say: This is** the animal **because of which so-and-so was stoned,** and its existence would shame his memory. **What, is it not**

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Mishnah* itself to try to resolve the open question. The second reason given in the *Mishnah*—*davar acher: shelo te-hei behemah overet ba-shuk ve-yomru zu hi she-niskal ploni al yadah*—mentions that the animal caused someone to be stoned (*niskal al yadah*). The *Gemara* reads this second formulation as indicating both elements: the public shaming (*kalon*) of the animal walking through the marketplace, and the *takalah*, since the mention of *niskal al yadah* evokes the stumbling that brought about capital punishment. So the *seifa* encodes both *takalah* and *kalon*.

### Reisha - Takala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּסֵיפָא תַּקָּלָה וְקָלוֹן, רֵישָׁא תַּקָּלָה בְּלֹא קָלוֹן. וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי? גּוֹי הַבָּא עַל הַבְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** evident **from** the fact **that the latter clause** of the mishna includes two reasons for the killing of the animal, namely both the **calamity and** the **shame** caused by the animal, that **the first clause,** the first reason stated in the mishna, is referring to a case of **a calamity without shame? And what are the circumstances** of a calamity without shame? It is the case of **a gentile who engages in intercourse with an animal.** In that case there is a calamity, as the gentile is executed, but his shame is not the concern of the Jewish court.

With the *seifa* carrying both elements, attention turns to the *reisha*—the first reason: *im adam chata behemah be-mah chat'ah? ella le-pi she-ba l'adam takalah al yadah*—"because a stumbling came about through it." *Mai lav*—is it not the case that since the *seifa* has both *takalah* and *kalon*, the *reisha* must be referring to just one of the two? And specifically, might the *reisha*'s mention of *takalah* be read not as a stumbling-block-to-sin but as a stumbling of shame—conveying that *kalon* alone suffices? If so, the case of a Jew who committed bestiality *be-shogeg* (without awareness that it was forbidden) would be a case of *kalon* without *takalah*, and the animal would still be stoned.

Alternatively, reading the *reisha* straightforwardly—*takalah* means a stumbling-block to sin—it would indicate that *takalah* alone suffices, and the applicable scenario would be the case of a non-Jew with an animal: *takalah* without *kalon*.

### Ketana Mezida

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: תָּא שְׁמַע, בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת בְּבִיאָה, וְאִם בָּא עָלֶיהָ יָבָם קְנָאָהּ, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says: Come** and **hear** a resolution from a mishna (*Nidda* 44b): A girl who is **three years and one day old** whose father arranged her betrothal **is betrothed with intercourse,** as the legal status of intercourse with her is that of full-fledged intercourse. **And** in a case where the childless husband of a girl who is three years and one day old dies, **if** his brother, the ***yavam*, engages in intercourse with her, he acquires her** as his wife; **and** if she is married, a man other than her husband is **liable for** engaging in intercourse with **her due to** the prohibition of intercourse with **a married woman.**

To test this, *Rav Yosef* introduces a famous *Mishnah* from *Niddah* enumerating the halachic ramifications of a girl who is three years and one day old. *Bas shalosh shanim ve-yom echad miskadeshes be-vi'ah*—if a girl of this age has intercourse (arranged by her father as the act of *kiddushin*), it is legally cognizable intercourse and she is halachically betrothed. *Ve-im ba aleha yabam kena'ah*—if the girl had been married and her husband died childless, and the *yabam* (the brother-in-law) performs *yibum* with her at this age, the act of *yibum* is legally effective and she becomes his wife. *Ve-chayavin aleha mishum eshet ish*—and any man who subsequently has intercourse with her is guilty of adultery, since she is a fully married woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְטַמְּאָה אֶת בּוֹעֲלָהּ, לְטַמֵּא מִשְׁכָּב תַּחְתּוֹן כְּעֶלְיוֹן. נִיסֵּת לְכֹהֵן – אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה. בָּא עָלֶיהָ אֶחָד מִן הַפְּסוּלִים – פְּסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And** if she is impure due to menstruation, **she transmits impurity to one who engages in intercourse with her,** who then **renders** all the items designated for **lying beneath** him **impure like** the items designated for lying **above** him. If **she marries a priest, she may partake of *teruma*** like any other wife of a priest. If she is unmarried and **one** of the men **who are unfit** for the priesthood, e.g., a *mamzer* or *ḥalal*, **engages in intercourse with her, he has disqualified her from** marrying into **the priesthood,** and if she is the daughter of a priest, she is disqualified from partaking of *teruma*.

The *Mishnah* continues: *u-metame et bo'alah le-tame mishkav tachton ke-elyon*—she renders the man who has intercourse with her *tamei* in the manner of a *niddah*. If she is a *niddah* and this man has intercourse with her, he becomes an *av ha-tumah*: anything he subsequently sits upon, even if ten cushions are stacked beneath him, each one has the status of a *mishkav*—though, because he himself is a *bo'el niddah* rather than a *niddah* herself, those cushions become a *rishon* rather than an *av*.

*Niset le-kohen, ochelet be-trumah*—if she is married to a *kohen*, she may eat *terumah*. Normally, by rabbinic law, a *kohen*'s wife eats *terumah* only after actual *nisuin* (cohabitation); and *nisuin* is considered to have occurred only when the couple is in a context where intercourse is possible. Once she is three years and a day, the marriage is understood to be a fully cohabitating marriage and she may eat *terumah*.

*Ba aleha echad min ha-psulim, pasla min ha-kehunah*—if a *pasul* (an invalid person, such as a *mamzer* or *chalal*) has intercourse with her, she is rendered invalid (*chalalah*), disqualified from marrying a *kohen*. And: *ve-im ba aleha echad mi-kol ha-arayot ha-amurot ba-Torah, mumatin al yadah, ve-hi peturah*—if any person in a forbidden *arayot* relationship has intercourse with her, that adult is executed; she, being a minor, is exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם בָּא עָלֶיהָ אֶחָד מִכׇּל הָעֲרָיוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בְּתוֹרָה, מוּמָתִין עַל יָדָהּ, וְהִיא פְּטוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And if one of any of those with whom relations are forbidden, who are stated in the Torah, engaged in intercourse with her,** e.g., her father or father-in-law, **they are executed** by the court **for** engaging in intercourse with **her, and she is exempt** because she is a minor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד מִכׇּל עֲרָיוֹת, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּהֵמָה. וְהָא הָכָא דְּקָלוֹן אִיכָּא, תַּקָּלָה לֵיכָּא, וְקָתָנֵי: מוּמָתִין עַל יָדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **One of any of those with whom relations are forbidden** apparently includes **even an animal. And here, there is shame** but **there is no calamity,** as she is not executed due to her status as a minor, **and** yet the mishna **teaches: They are executed for** engaging in intercourse with **her.** Evidently, the animal is killed.

The *Gemara* notes: *achat mi-kol ha-arayot, ve-afilu behemah*—presumably including an animal that had intercourse with her. The animal would be stoned. And *v'hacha kalon ika, takalah leika*—there is shame in the act, but the girl, as a minor, is not considered to have sinned in a legally meaningful sense, so there is no *takalah*. And yet the animal is stoned. This seems to prove that *kalon* alone suffices.

### Keivan Dezid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דִּמְזִידָה הִיא, תַּקָּלָה נָמֵי אִיכָּא, וְרַחֲמָנָא הוּא דְּחָס עֲלַהּ. עֲלַהּ דִּידַהּ חָס, אַבְּהֵמָה לָא חָס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Since she** committed this transgression **intentionally, there is a calamity as well, and it is the Merciful One who has pity on her** due to her young age, and exempts her from punishment. And although the Merciful One **has pity on her, He does not have pity on the animal.** Therefore, it cannot be proven from here that shame without a calamity is sufficient cause for the animal to be put to death, because calamity is present in this case.

The *Gemara* rejects this inference: *keivan de-mezidah hi, takalah nami ika, ve-Rachmana hu de-chas alah*. Even a three-year-old minor, if she willfully engages in this act—however qualified "willful" must be for a child of that age—is considered to have sinned, even if she is exempt from punishment. The Torah had compassion on her (*chas aleha*) and exempted her from the penalty, but she was not entirely outside the category of sin. By contrast, the animal is not spared: *a-behemah lo chas*—the Torah's compassion did not extend to it. The *takalah* is therefore still present—the minor willfully caused or participated in a sinful act, and the animal was the instrument of that sin.

This is a striking position. One might have expected that an adult acting *be-shogeg*—without awareness that the act is forbidden—would constitute a lesser *takalah* than a willful act, even one performed by a minor who is technically exempt. The *Gemara* is asserting the opposite: willful sin that carries an exemption due to minority status still constitutes *takalah*, whereas *shogeg* by an adult—lacking the element of willfulness—does not rise to the same level of *takalah*. This is counterintuitive but is the *Gemara*'s position.

### Ben Tesha Shanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד הַבָּא עַל יְבִמְתּוֹ – קְנָאָהּ, וְאֵינוֹ נוֹתֵן גֵּט עַד שֶׁיַּגְדִּיל. וּמְטַמֵּא כְּנִדָּה לְטַמֵּא מִשְׁכָּב תַּחְתּוֹן כְּעֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Come** and **hear** a proof from the subsequent mishna (*Nidda* 45a): With regard to a boy who is **nine years and one day old,** whose brother died childless, and **who engaged in intercourse with his *yevama*,** his brother's widow, the status of the intercourse is that of full-fledged intercourse and **he has acquired her** as his wife, **but** if he chooses to end the marriage, **he cannot give** her **a bill of divorce until he reaches majority. And he becomes ritually impure like a menstruating woman** after engaging in intercourse with her, and then **renders** all the items designated for **lying beneath** him **impure like** the items designated for lying **above** him.

*Rava* attempts the same inference from the parallel case of the nine-year-and-one-day-old boy. *Ben tesha shanim ve-yom echad ha-ba al yivimto kena'ah*—a boy of this age performing *yibum* effectuates the levirate marriage, even though *mi-de-oraita* this may only be *de-rabbanan*. *Ve-eino noten get ad she-yagdil*—he cannot give a *get* until he becomes an adult. *U-metamei k'niddah le-tamei mishkav tachton ke-elyon*—if he has intercourse with a *niddah*, he becomes an *av ha-tumah* and renders the cushions beneath him a *mishkav*, though at the level of *rishon*. *Posel ve-eino ma'achil*—if he is *pasul* (a *mamzer* or similar) and has intercourse with a woman, he disqualifies her, but if he is a *kohen* and has not yet reached the age of full intercourse, his wife cannot eat *terumah* since they are not yet in a state of *nisuin*. *U-posel et ha-behemah me-al gabei ha-mizbe'ach*—if he has intercourse with an animal, it cannot be brought as a *korban*, even if there are insufficient witnesses to stone it; ownership or a single witness admitting to the act would disqualify it from the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פּוֹסֵל וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל, וּפוֹסֵל אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה מֵעַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וְנִסְקֶלֶת עַל יָדוֹ. וְאִם בָּא עַל אַחַת מִכׇּל הָעֲרָיוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בְּתוֹרָה, מוּמָתִים עַל יָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If he is disqualified from the priesthood and engages in intercourse with the daughter of a priest, **he disqualifies** her from partaking of *teruma*; **but** if he is a priest who marries an Israelite woman, **he does not enable** her **to partake** of *teruma*. **And** if he engages in intercourse with an animal, **he disqualifies the animal from** being sacrificed **upon the altar, and** the animal **is stoned on** the basis of intercourse with **him. And if he engaged in intercourse with one of any of those with whom relations are forbidden, who are stated in the Torah, they are executed** by the court **on** the basis of intercourse with **him,** but he is exempt.

*U-nisqelet al yado*—and the animal is stoned. Here the case is explicit: a nine-year-and-one-day-old boy has intercourse with an animal, and the animal is stoned. The same inference is drawn: *kalon ika, takalah leika*—there is shame, but he is a minor. Yet the animal is stoned. Does this not prove *kalon* alone suffices?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא קָלוֹן אִיכָּא, תַּקָּלָה לֵיכָּא, וְקָתָנֵי: נִסְקֶלֶת עַל יָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **And here,** in a case where he engages in intercourse with an animal, **there is shame,** but **there is no calamity, and** yet the mishna **teaches** that the animal **is stoned on** the basis of intercourse with **him,** indicating that shame is sufficient for the animal to be killed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּמֵזִיד הוּא, תַּקָּלָה נָמֵי אִיכָּא, וְרַחֲמָנָא הוּא דְּחָס עִילָּוֵיהּ. עֲלֵיהּ דִּידֵיהּ חָס רַחֲמָנָא, אַבְּהֵמָה לָא חָס רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Since he** committed this transgression **intentionally, there is a calamity as well, and it is the Merciful One who has pity on him** due to his minority. Although **the Merciful One has pity on him, the Merciful One does not have pity on the animal.** Therefore, it cannot be proven from here that shame without a calamity is sufficient cause for the animal to be put to death, because calamity is present in this case.

The *Gemara* gives the same answer: *keivan de-mezid hu, takalah nami ika, ve-Rachmana hu de-chasi aleih, aleih dideih chas, a-behemah lo chas*—since he acted willfully, there is *takalah*; the Torah exempted him from punishment as a minor, not the animal. We cannot prove that *kalon* alone is sufficient.

### Final Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דָּבָר אַחֵר, שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא בְּהֵמָה עוֹבֶרֶת בַּשּׁוּק וְיֹאמְרוּ: ״זוֹ הִיא שֶׁנִּסְקַל פְּלוֹנִי עַל יָדָהּ״. מַאי לָאו, מִדְּסֵיפָא תַּקָּלָה וְקָלוֹן, רֵישָׁא קָלוֹן בְּלֹא תַּקָּלָה? וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי? יִשְׂרָאֵל הַבָּא עַל הַבְּהֵמָה בְּשׁוֹגֵג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution from the mishna (54a): **Alternatively,** it is **so that** this **animal will not pass through the marketplace, and** those who see it **will say: This is** the animal **because of which so-and-so was stoned. What, is it not** evident **from** the fact **that the latter clause** of the mishna includes two reasons for the killing of the animal, namely both the **calamity and** the **shame** caused by the animal, that **the first clause,** the first reason stated in the mishna, is referring to a case of **shame without a calamity? And what are the circumstances** of shame without a calamity? It is the case of **a Jew who unwittingly engages in intercourse with an animal.**

One final attempt is made by returning to the *Mishnah*'s *davar acher*: *shelo te-hei behemah overet ba-shuk ve-yomru zu hi she-niskal ploni al yadah*. If the *seifa* contains both *takalah* and *kalon*, perhaps the *reisha*—the first reason, which mentions only that a *takalah* came about through the animal—should be read as demonstrating that *kalon* without *takalah* suffices, specifically as a reference to the case of a Jew who committed bestiality *be-shogeg*: shame is present, but genuine *takalah* is not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, סֵיפָא תַּקָּלָה וְקָלוֹן, רֵישָׁא תַּקָּלָה בְּלֹא קָלוֹן. וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי? גּוֹי הַבָּא עַל הַבְּהֵמָה, וּכְדִבְעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** perhaps **the latter clause** is referring to a case where there is both **a calamity and shame,** while **the first clause** is referring to a case where there is **a calamity without shame. And what are the circumstances** of a calamity without shame? It is the case of **a gentile who engages in intercourse with an animal, as** the students **asked Rav Sheshet.** The dilemma is left unresolved. No conclusive answer can be inferred from the mishna for either this dilemma or for the dilemma raised by the students of Rav Sheshet.

The *Gemara* counters: no, read the *reisha* straightforwardly—it demonstrates that *takalah* without *kalon* suffices, exemplified by the case of a non-Jew (*oved kochavim*) with an animal, exactly as *Rav Sheshet* originally inquired. The *Mishnah*'s first and second reasons can each stand alone. The *seifa* encodes both; the *reisha* shows that one suffices—but we cannot determine from the *Mishnah* alone which one, since the *reisha* is naturally read as pure *takalah* and the inference of *kalon* alone from it is forced. The *Gemara* leaves us here: both the case of the non-Jew with an animal (*takalah* without *kalon*) and the case of the Jew acting *be-shogeg* (*kalon* without *takalah*) remain unresolved as to whether they independently trigger the stoning of the animal.
