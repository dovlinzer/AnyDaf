# Arakhin 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Jews & Erech

### Non-Jews in Erech

The *daf* opens by continuing a discussion that began at the bottom of 5b: can non-Jews participate in the category of *erech*? Can they obligate themselves to pay an *erech*, and can they serve as the object of one? The *Gemara* had been inclined to suggest that if an exclusion is to be drawn, it should fall specifically on the giving of *erech* — and the basis for this inclination is a story and verse from the book of *Ezra*, in which non-Jewish contributions to the *Beit HaMikdash* were rejected at the outset of the building process out of concern that it would undermine the enterprise as a distinctly Jewish undertaking. As *Tosafot* notes, this does not warrant a sweeping exclusion: the *Mishnah* already states that non-Jews may give a *neder*, and whatever limitation exists pertains specifically within the domain of *erchin*.

### Ezra-Nehemiah Story

The story from *Ezra-Nechemia* is the textual anchor for this entire discussion. When the rebuilding of the Second *Beit HaMikdash* began, those who sought to contribute from outside the Jewish community were rebuffed. The concern was not merely theological abstraction — it was the vulnerability of the enterprise itself. The text describes the surrounding peoples working to weaken the hands of those who were building. In that charged climate, accepting outside contributions risked importing with them outside interests, outside influence, and ultimately outside ownership of what was meant to be an exclusively Jewish national undertaking. The *Gemara* will ultimately need to grapple with how much general policy can be derived from this singular historical episode.

### R. Yehuda: Erech Valid,…

The *Gemara* asks: since *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that a non-Jew can be *maarich* — can give an *erech* that takes effect — what does he do with the verse from *Ezra-Nechemia* that seems to prohibit accepting non-Jewish contributions to the *Beit HaMikdash*? The answer given is that of *Rav Chisda* in the name of *Rav Avimi*: *ve'erko nignaz* — even *Rabbi Yehuda* would agree that as a matter of policy the contribution should not be accepted. Nevertheless, if a non-Jew does set aside the money for an *erech*, the money becomes sanctified. The question then becomes: what does one do with it? The answer: it must be buried.

A note on terminology is appropriate here. The *Gemara*'s use of *ovdei kochavim* is almost invariably a later self-censorship; the original text would have read *goy* or *nochri*. In context, the concern is not limited to idolaters per se — it applies to all non-Jews, and the driving concern is the integrity of the *Beit HaMikdash* as a Jewish enterprise.

This is an interesting way of incorporating the policy concern within *Rabbi Yehuda*'s framework. He agrees that sanctity attaches when the money is set aside, but agrees as well that the practical outcome is burial rather than use. This is admittedly a waste. And it applies only to *erchin* — the *Mishnah*, as noted, permits *nedarim* from non-Jews.

### Me'ila Objection

The *Gemara* immediately presses this position. If the money is to be buried and will never be used by the *Beit HaMikdash*, there ought to be no violation of *meila* — the prohibition against misusing sanctified property. The underlying principle is that if something is sanctified but can never in any way be put to use, there is no *meila*: *lo nehenin velo moalin*. This is established in the *Mishnah* itself, which teaches that items such as the five *chatatot* that must be left to die, or money that is sanctified but must be thrown into the Dead Sea, are governed by this rule — they are rabbinically prohibited from benefit, yet no transgression of *meila* attaches to them.

If the *erech* money of a non-Jew is destined for burial, the same should apply. But this is contradicted by a *braita*: *bameh devarim amurim bekodshei mizbeach* — when does the rule of no *meila* apply to things sanctified by a non-Jew? Only for *kodshei mizbeach*, sacrificial offerings. *Aval bekodshei bedek habayit moalin bo* — if a non-Jew sanctified something for the Temple treasury, *meila* does apply. Since *Rabbi Meir* holds that non-Jews cannot even sanctify effectively, this *braita* must be reflecting *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position — and it states explicitly that *meila* applies. This refutes the burial conclusion.

### Rava: Unique Circumstance

*Rava* concedes the point. *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is that non-Jews can give an *erech*, the money becomes sanctified, and it would be used — which is in fact the straightforward reading of *Rabbi Yehuda*. What, then, does *Rabbi Yehuda* do with the *Ezra* story? *Rava* answers: *mishum rifyon yadayim hu* — that story reflects entirely unique circumstances. It cannot serve as the basis for general policy. The verse cited is explicit: *vayehi am ha'aretz merappim yedei am Yehuda umevahhalim otam livnot* — the surrounding peoples were actively working to weaken the hands of the Jews who were building the *Beit HaMikdash*. The entire venture was extraordinarily fragile, and those who offered to contribute may well have harbored ulterior motives — seeking to insert themselves into the process and use their contribution as leverage for manipulation. That concern was specific to those circumstances; it does not translate into a standing general rule.

With this, *Tosafot*'s observation becomes all the more pointed: even the opinion that does derive some policy from the *Ezra* story applies that policy narrowly — only to *erchin*, not to *nedarim*. The *Mishnah* is explicit that non-Jews may give a *neder*. So if one presses the question of what precisely distinguishes *erchin*, *Tosafot*'s answer is essentially that there is nothing uniquely disqualifying about *erchin* — but given that there are grounds for some concern, if any limitation is to be drawn, this is where it lands. That said, the simple reading of the *Gemara* we have just concluded, along with what follows at the top of 6a, suggests that the policy implications extend more broadly than *erchin* alone.

## Contributions: Beginning…

### Two Baraitot on Neder

At the top of 6a, the *Gemara* turns to a different type of contribution — not *erech* but *nedavah*, a vow or gift to the Temple treasury — which according to our *Mishnah* should present no problem at all. Yet even here, policy concerns emerge. One *braita* states that if non-Jews gave a *nedavah* to *bedek habayit*, the Temple maintenance fund, one accepts it. A second *braita* states that one does not accept it. How are these reconciled?

### Beginning vs. End

*Rabbi Ila* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *lo kashya — ha bitechilah ha besof* — there is no contradiction. It depends on whether the contribution comes at the beginning or the end of the process. The straightforward explanation, rooted directly in the *Ezra-Nechemia* narrative, is this: at the very beginning, when the foundation is being laid, the Jewish community must establish itself unambiguously as the owner and driver of the enterprise. The question of who is investing in the *Beit HaMikdash*, who owns the process, who is responsible for it — all of that must be settled firmly through Jewish commitment alone. Once the project is well underway, once the Jewish community's investment is beyond question, outside contributions can be absorbed without threatening that sense of ownership.

### Stringent at Beginning

The stringency at the beginning is sweeping. *De'amar Rabbi Assi amar Rabbi Yochanan: bitechilah, afilu mayim u'melach ein mekablin* — at the beginning of the process, even the most basic items — water and salt — are not accepted from non-Jews. It matters not at all how trivial or how essential the contribution might be; the principle of exclusive Jewish ownership of the enterprise is absolute at the outset.

### End: Specific/Generic

Once the process is well along, distinctions emerge. *Davar hamesuyam ein mekablin, davar she'eino mesuyam mekablin* — a specific, identified item is not accepted, but a generic contribution — money that is absorbed into the general fund — is acceptable. The logic is clear: a generic donation disappears into the collective and carries no identifying mark. A specific item, however, remains associated with its donor and introduces the very concern that drove the original rejection: an outside party now has a visible, named stake in the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Ama Chalya Oreiv

*Rav Yosef* offers an example of a *davar mesuyam*: the *ama khalya oreiv* — the spiked device, one *amah* by one *amah*, installed on the Temple roof to prevent birds from landing and contaminating the altar with its burning sacrifices. The spikes frightened away ravens and other birds that would otherwise be drawn by the smell of burning meat. The *Gemara* presumably selects this example to forestall a mistaken inference: one might have thought that only prestigious items — like the *Aron Kodesh* — would carry identifying weight. The *ama khalya oreiv* is relatively minor, yet it would still be identified as a non-Jewish contribution. Any specific item, regardless of its prominence, is in the same category.

### Reclamation Concern

*Rabbeinu Gershom* adds a further dimension to the rationale for rejecting specific items: the concern is not only about identification and prestige, but about reclamation. When a non-Jew donates something specific, there is a risk that he might later seek to reclaim it — either because he has a change of heart, because relations deteriorate, or simply out of self-interest. A generic monetary donation cannot be demanded back once absorbed into general funds, but a specific, identifiable object presents an ongoing vulnerability. The *Beit HaMikdash* should not be in the position of having to refuse such a demand, or worse, of losing something it has already integrated into its operations. This is also why, at the end of the process, even relatively minor specific items are rejected: the concern about future complications is independent of the item's importance.

### Royal Contribution

*Rav Yosef* raises a challenge: the *Gemara* in *Nechemia* records a letter from the king to *Asaf*, the keeper of the royal forest, directing him to supply timber for the construction of the *Beit HaMikdash*. This is a contribution at the very beginning of the building process — yet it was accepted. *Tosafot* notes the broader difficulty: the entire reconstruction under *Koresh* was carried out under royal directive and with royal support. *Tosafot* suggests that *Koresh* himself was in a special category, already singled out by prophecy in *Yeshayahu*. But here the contributor was not *Koresh* directly; it was *Asaf*, acting on royal command. How could such a contribution be accepted at the beginning of the process?

### Royal Cannot Reverse

*Abaye* resolves this: *shani malchusa d'lo hadra beih* — royal action is categorically different, because a king does not reverse himself. *Shmuel* stated this principle explicitly: if the king says he will uproot mountains, the mountains will be uprooted — *i amar malchusa akarna tura, akar tura v'lo hadra beih*. The maxim is well known: *Shmuel* was the *amora* who articulated *dina d'malchusa dina*, the rule that governmental law is binding law. *Shmuel* was also personally close to the Persian royal court.

What exactly is the concern that is being neutralized? *Rashi* indicates that the worry is not only about dramatic manipulation but about something more mundane: will this contribution turn out to be sincere and final, or will the donor later seek to take it back, creating complications and conflict? When the contribution comes by royal decree, there is no realistic concern that it will be reversed or reclaimed. Royal declarations carry the weight of institutional permanence. A king does not say "I changed my mind" and ask for the lumber back. That assurance removes the concern that makes outside contributions at the beginning of the process so fraught.

One might ask: does this not leave open a different concern — that the government, precisely because it has contributed, will now seek to influence or control the enterprise? The point is a sharp one, and it applies with equal or greater force to a royal contributor than to a private one. If anything, a government can manipulate a situation regardless of whether it has made a formal donation. The *Gemara*'s resolution, focused on the concern about reversal, does not address the manipulation concern directly — though it may be that royal contribution at the outset, made under the authority of a decree, is simply taken as a given that falls outside the ordinary policy framework.

## Non-Jew Terumah

### Non-Jew Separates

*Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Rav*: if a non-Jew separates *terumah* from his own grain grown in *Eretz Yisrael*, what is the status of that separation? The question presupposes the view that non-Jewish ownership of land in *Eretz Yisrael* does not remove its *kedusha* — a position with significant practical implications, including for the question of selling land to a non-Jew in a *shemita* year. Given that the grain carries inherent sanctity, is a non-Jew capable of actualizing it by performing the separation?

### Intent Investigation

The *Gemara* rules that the answer depends on an investigation: *bodkin oto* — one must examine what the non-Jew had in mind. *Im bedaat Yisrael hifrishah* — if he performed the separation with Jewish intent — meaning either that he did so at the direction of a Jew, or, as *Rashi* offers alternatively, that he intended it to work precisely as *terumah* works for Jews — then *tinaten lekohen*: it is genuine *terumah* and must be given to a *kohen*. A non-Jew, if the grain carries sanctity and he intends the separation in the Jewish sense, can effectively make *terumah* from his own produce.

### Ambiguous Terumah

If, however, his intent cannot be so confirmed, the situation becomes complicated. *Chayshinan shema belibo lashamayim* — we are concerned that when he said *terumah*, he may have meant something different. The word *terumah* appears in the Torah not only as the name for the priestly portion separated from grain, but also as the name for gifts given for the construction of the *Beit HaMikdash* — *zot haterumah*, the portion from which the *Mishkan* materials were gathered. If the non-Jew meant to give his grain as a donation to the Temple treasury, it is now *kodshei bedek habayit* rather than *terumah* in the agricultural sense. Since one cannot be certain which he intended, one faces an impasse: if it is *terumah*, it cannot be used for the Temple. If it is *kodshei bedek habayit*, it cannot be given to *kohanim* to eat. There is no way to use it appropriately in either direction, and so it must be buried.

One might wonder whether some creative solution could avoid the need for burial — perhaps having a *kohen* redeem it on a small coin, or finding some other mechanism to release it. The *Gemara* does not pursue such a path, and the ruling is that in the absence of clear intent, burial is required.

### Non-Jew Sanctity

This entire discussion raises a broader question about whether non-Jews can create *kedusha* that operates meaningfully within a Jewish legal framework. The natural assumption might be that someone outside the system cannot generate such effects. But the *Gemara*'s line of reasoning suggests otherwise. Non-Jews can bring *korbanot*, which requires sanctifying the animal. They can donate to the Temple treasury in ways that take effect. Once those capacities are established, the possibility that they can also separate *terumah* — particularly where the *kedusha* is already inherent in the grain and the non-Jew is in some sense merely actualizing it — is not a radical extension.

This is distinct from the question of whether such actions constitute *mitzvot* for the non-Jew, or whether the non-Jew accrues merit. The *Rambam* holds that even non-Jews who perform *mitzvot* are performing a meaningful act, if not at the same level as one who is commanded. But the question here is not about spiritual accounting — it is about legal efficacy. Can non-Jews generate *kedusha* that imposes real obligations and restrictions within *halacha*? The answer emerging from this *sugya* is yes, within defined parameters.

## Beam with God's Name

### Donation Conditions

The *Gemara* raises a case: a non-Jew donates a beam for the Temple — or, as *Rashi* indicates the *braita* implies, in a time when there is no Temple, for the synagogue — and on the beam he has written God's name, indicating that he is donating it for religious purposes.

### Cut Name, Use Rest

*Bodkin oto* — again an investigation is conducted. *Im amar bedaas Yisrael hifrashtiah* — if he says that he donated it in the manner that Jews donate things, intending it as a straightforward religious donation rather than as a formal act of sanctification, then *yigod* — one cuts out the section of the beam bearing the divine name, which carries *kedushat Hashem* and must be buried as *sheimos*, while the remainder of the beam may be used.

The fact that the text requires cutting out the name even in this case is itself significant: even a divine name written by a non-Jew apparently carries real *kedushat Hashem*, not merely a matter of propriety or respect. This is the apparent implication of the *braita*, and we will return to this point.

### Unclear Intent Buried

*Ve'im lav* — if he cannot confirm that he intended it the Jewish way — *teuna genizah* — the entire beam must be buried. The concern is *chayshinan shema belibo lashamayim*: perhaps he meant to formally sanctify the beam for religious use, in which case the beam carries *kedusha* of *hekdesh*. In the present era, when there is no functioning Temple, *hekdesh* that cannot be used — as we learned at the end of *Bechorot* — must be put away. There is nothing that can be done with it. If it might be *hekdesh*, it cannot be used for the synagogue; and since there is no Temple to which it could be brought, it must be buried.

This is particularly intelligible in the context of the ancient pagan world, where the standard form of religious donation was often not an animal sacrifice but the dedication of precious objects — literally placing valuables in the temple treasury as an act of religious devotion. We know, for example, of temple treasuries found in various ancient religious sites containing enormous accumulations of gold and other precious goods — dedicated, never to be sold or used, simply offered to the deity. In that world, a non-Jew who donates a beam without specifying that he means to do so "the Jewish way" very plausibly intended precisely such a formal, irrevocable sanctification. That is why the absence of a clear statement of Jewish-style intent triggers the concern and requires burial.

### Limited Sanctification

The *Gemara* then draws a further inference. The fact that the *braita* specifies a beam with God's name written on it implies that if there were no name on the beam, burial would not be required — the default assumption would not be that the non-Jew intended formal *hekdesh*. But the *Gemara* immediately pushes back: *hu hadin de'af al gav de'ein shem katav aleha nami bay genizah* — even without God's name, if the non-Jew cannot confirm Jewish intent, we would still be concerned that the donation might constitute *hekdesh* and burial would be required.

What, then, is the *chiddush* of specifying that God's name was written on the beam? *De'af al gav deshem katav aleha* — even with God's name on it, if the non-Jew explicitly states that he meant to give it in the Jewish manner, then the beam may be used — provided the section bearing the name is cut out. The name sanctifies only its immediate location: *shem she'lo bimkomo lav kadosh* — a divine name does not extend its sanctity beyond the space it physically occupies. Only that section requires burial; the rest of the beam is fully usable.

A supporting *braita* from *Masechet Sofrim* establishes this principle: if God's name was written on the handles of vessels or the legs of a bed, one cuts out and buries those specific parts. The vessel or bed itself is not sanctified throughout.

### Pagan vs. Modern

The question arises whether these concerns about a non-Jew's writing of *Shem Hashem* — and the possibility that an unclear donation constitutes *hekdesh* — remain relevant outside the context of the ancient pagan world. The parallel to a *Sefer Torah* written by a non-Jew, where *Shem Hashem* is treated as genuine *sheimos*, suggests that there is real *kedusha* at stake, not merely a question of respect. But when it comes to the concern about formal sanctification, the pagan context matters considerably. A non-Jew in the contemporary world who donates his car to a synagogue does not inhabit a world in which the default religious act is the irrevocable dedication of objects to a deity's treasury. The concern that his donation might constitute formal *hekdesh* is correspondingly attenuated. Whether contemporary *poskim* have addressed this distinction directly is a question worth pursuing.

## Tzedakah Money

### Mutar Lishnosah

*Rav Nachman* said in the name of *Rabbah bar Avuha*: *ha'omer sela zu litzedakah mutar lishnosah* — if one says "this coin is for *tzedakah*," one may switch it out, meaning one may use the coin for another purpose and give a different coin to *tzedakah*. The coin does not become sanctified to the point where it must be this specific coin; the obligation is to give the money, not to give this particular piece of currency.

### Self vs. Other

The *Gemara* initially considered whether this latitude extended only to the donor's own use — that perhaps one could use it for oneself but could not lend it to a third party. The reasoning behind this hypothetical distinction, as *Rashi* explains, is that one might naturally reserve in one's own mind the option to use the money oneself, while never having contemplated that others might draw on it.

### Borrow & Repay

This restriction was rejected. *Amar Rabbi Ami amar Rabbi Yochanan: bein la'atzmo bein la'acher* — it makes no difference whether it is used by the donor or lent to a third party. The fundamental principle is that designating money for *tzedakah* does not sanctify it the way *hekdesh* sanctifies Temple property. The obligation is to give the money, but the money itself retains a degree of flexibility. Whether one said *alai* — creating a personal obligation — or *harei zu* — setting aside the specific coin — the ruling is ultimately the same: one may borrow it and repay, as long as the *tzedakah* ultimately receives what is owed.

### Rava's Reasoning

*Rava* challenges a proposed distinction between the two formulations — *alai* versus *harei zu* — on policy grounds. *Adrabba ipecha mistabra* — the opposite of the proposed distinction makes more sense. If one says *harei zu*, designating this specific coin, one should actually be encouraged to go ahead and use it. Why? *Ki heichi d'lichayev b'achrayusa* — so that one will bear personal financial responsibility for it. If the coin remains as designated but the donor has not accepted a personal obligation, and the coin is lost, the *tzedakah* loses out entirely. But if the donor uses the coin and now owes the money, the debt remains regardless of what happens to the original coin. The *tzedakah* is thereby better protected. *Ella lo shna* — ultimately, the *Gemara* collapses the distinction: it does not matter which formulation was used, and the latitude to borrow and repay applies throughout.

The deeper question is what this ruling implies about the status of money designated for *tzedakah*. If it had no status at all, the various concerns and distinctions the *Gemara* entertains would not arise. But if it had the full status of *hekdesh*, one could not use it at all. The *Gemara* seems to describe something intermediate: the money has a status — an aspect of *neder*, a quality of being earmarked — yet that status comes with considerably more latitude than *hekdesh*. The *Rambam* famously emphasizes that *tzedakah* is a *neder* and that the designated money carries something like a *kedusha*, while also recognizing that it may be borrowed and repaid. Reconciling the status with the latitude is the ongoing challenge of the *sugya*.

### Neder Not Hekdesh

A *braita* is cited in support of *Rava*'s overall position: *neder tzedakah ve'ein hekdesh tzedakah* — *tzedakah* is like a *neder* but not like *hekdesh*. What does this mean? The *Gemara* clarifies: *tzedakah harei hi beval ta'acher* — a commitment to give *tzedakah* carries the force of a vow, such that failing to fulfill it within three *regalim* constitutes a transgression of *bal ta'acher*, just as delaying the fulfillment of a vow to bring a *korban* does. This has real practical implications: when a person commits to give at a communal appeal, the laws of *bal ta'acher* apply to that commitment. The exact conditions under which such a pledge constitutes a binding *neder* — as opposed to a mere expression of intention — is a subject for the tractate of *Nedarim*, but the principle that *tzedakah* can carry *neder* status is established here.

At the same time, *ve'eino kehekdesh* — it is not like *hekdesh*. *Hekdesh* places the object entirely off-limits: *assur lehishtamesh bo*. *Tzedakah*, by contrast, *shari lehishtamesh bah* — may be used, in the sense of borrowing. This unique hybrid — carrying the obligation structure of a *neder* while lacking the use-prohibition of *hekdesh* — captures the peculiar character of *tzedakah* money. It is perhaps best understood as a *neder* whose fulfillment is measured not by the fate of the specific object but by whether the *aniyim* ultimately receive what they are owed.

### Gabbai Control

*Tanu rabanan*: if one says "this *sela* is for *tzedakah*," the latitude to borrow and repay exists only *ad shelo ba'ah leyad gabbai* — until the money reaches the hands of the *gabbai*, the communal official responsible for collecting and distributing *tzedakah*. Once it is in the *gabbai*'s hands, *assur lishnotah* — it cannot be switched or used for another purpose. The reason is that when the *gabbai* receives the money, he acquires it on behalf of the *aniyim*. At that point it is their money. Whatever latitude existed before, based on the donor's continuing personal ownership and obligation, disappears once a third party has taken possession on behalf of the intended beneficiaries.

### R. Yannai Exception

The *Gemara* challenges this from practice: *Rav Yannai*, who served as a *gabbai*, would himself borrow from the *tzedakah* funds and later repay them. How could he do so once the money was in the *gabbai*'s hands?

The *Gemara* answers: *shani Rav Yannai denicha lehu la'aniyim* — the situation of *Rav Yannai* was different, because it was actually beneficial for the poor that he borrow the money. The mechanism is as follows: when the *tzedakah* coffers were depleted because *Rav Yannai* had borrowed from them, he bore personal responsibility to ensure the poor were fed. Faced with empty coffers and an outstanding obligation, he would go out and compel additional donations from the community — thereby generating more funds than would otherwise have been collected. The poor ultimately received more, not less, as a result of his borrowing. When the borrowing is genuinely in the interest of the *aniyim*, that implicit permission may be attributed to them, and the act is permitted.

This reasoning underscores how the entire framework of *tzedakah* money is calibrated around the welfare of the poor rather than around abstract categories of sanctity. The status of the money, whatever it is, exists to ensure that the *aniyim* are served. When a departure from the ordinary rules serves that same purpose better than rigid adherence would, the departure is endorsed.

## Synagogue Donations

### Jewish Donation

The *Gemara* now turns to specific donations made to a synagogue. *Yisrael she-nitnadev menora o ner le-beit haknesset* — if a Jew donated a candelabrum or a lamp to a synagogue, *assur lishnosah* — the synagogue may not reuse it for a different purpose. It is as though the item carries a form of *kedushat haguf* — intrinsic sanctity attached to the object itself — rather than merely *kedushat damim*, monetary value earmarked for a particular use.

### Mitzvah Reuse

*Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba* initially understood this prohibition to be absolute, applying even if the synagogue wished to repurpose the item for a different *mitzvah* purpose. *Rav Ami* corrected him in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: the prohibition applies only to *dvar reshut*, to uses unrelated to any *mitzvah*. For a *dvar mitzvah*, repurposing is permitted. The practical question of what constitutes a *dvar reshut* versus a *dvar mitzvah* in a synagogue context is left with some ambiguity, though Torah study or another religious purpose would clearly qualify.

### Non-Jew Limited

When the donor is a non-Jew, additional considerations apply. *Rabbi Assi* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: if a non-Jew donated a lamp or candelabrum to a synagogue — and here, notably, the *Gemara* implicitly accepts that such a donation was received, unlike the *Beit HaMikdash* context — *ad she-lo nishtaka sheim ba'aleha, assur lishnosah*. As long as the donor's name is still remembered in connection with the object, it may not be repurposed even for a *mitzvah* purpose.

### Name Forgotten

*Mishenishtaka sheim ba'aleha, mutar lishnosah* — once the donor's name has been forgotten, the synagogue may sell the item and apply the proceeds to other purposes. The operative concern is not intrinsic sanctity but rather the risk of the donor's complaint: as long as people remember that this is "John's candelabrum," using it differently would constitute a public insult to the donor. The donor — the non-Jew — may appear and ask what happened to his gift. Even if the donor himself always remembers, once the communal memory fades and the item is no longer identified with him in the public consciousness, the risk of offense has sufficiently passed.

The parallel to communal life is vivid and genuine. A synagogue that accepts a specific donated item enters into an ongoing relationship with the donor's identity. The familiar scenario — a major donor wishes to give a new *aron kodesh* to replace the one already bearing another family's name — is precisely the tension this *halacha* addresses. The *Gemara*, strikingly, assumes this concern applies only to non-Jewish donors. For Jewish donors, *lo payei* — the assumption is that a Jew gives wholeheartedly and would not complain. Whether that assumption is realistic in every communal context is another matter.

### Shazrik Case

The *Gemara* then presents a concrete case. *Shazrik the Taya* — a non-Jewish merchant — donated a lamp to a synagogue associated with *Rav Yehuda*. *Rechava* subsequently repurposed it for some other use. *Rava* was upset, holding that the repurposing was impermissible. A second version has the versions reversed — *Rava* made the change and *Rechava* objected. A third version has the synagogue's *chazanim* make the change, with both *Rechava* and *Rava* objecting.

The substantive dispute in the case is this: the one who permitted the change reasoned that since *Shazrik* was a traveling merchant, he was almost never in the city — *d'lo shechiah* — and the likelihood of his appearing and noticing the change was remote. The one who forbade it — or in the versions where multiple authorities objected, the shared ground — is that *zimnin d'mikla v'asi*, merchants do occasionally pass through, and one cannot count on him never seeing the change. The *halacha* therefore requires that as long as the donor could plausibly appear and recognize what was done with his gift, the donation must remain as it was received.

The section thus concludes with a subtle observation: whereas at the beginning of the *sugya* the *Beit HaMikdash* context led to a rule against accepting specific named items from non-Jews at all, the synagogue context apparently operates differently — donations are accepted, and the consequence is a set of ongoing obligations regarding their use. The concern about reclamation or complaint, managed in the *Beit HaMikdash* context by refusal, is managed in the synagogue context by ongoing attention to the donor's association with the gift.

## Goses & Execution

### Mishna Rule

The *Gemara* now arrives at a new *Mishnah*. *HaGoses v'hayotze lehareg lo nidar v'lo ne'erach* — a *goses*, a person who is actively dying and imminently terminal, and a *yotzei lehareg*, a person condemned to execution by a Jewish court, cannot be the object of a vow or an *erech*. As an object of a vow — *nidar* — this makes immediate sense: they have no market value; if sold as slaves they would fetch nothing. The *chiddush* lies in the ruling that they also cannot be the object of an *erech*, since *erech* is a fixed sum determined by age and gender, entirely independent of market value.

### R. Chanina View

*Rabbi Chanina ben Akavya* disagrees on the point of *erech*: he holds that they are *ne'erach*. His reasoning is straightforward — *mipnei shedamav ketzuvim* — the *erech* value is fixed by the Torah chart, and that chart does not require market value. The *goses* and the condemned are still men or women of a certain age; their place on the chart exists regardless of their condition or fate.

### R. Yosi View

*Rabbi Yosi* goes further: *noder u'maarich u'makdish, ve'im hizik chayav* — a *goses* and a condemned person can themselves make vows and commit to pay *erech*, can sanctify objects, and are liable for damages they cause. In other words, while they may not serve as objects of the vow or *erech* — since they lack the requisite status as subjects of valuation — they retain their full legal agency as actors. They can still do legally binding things. *Rabbi Yosi*'s position is not in direct conflict with the opening ruling: the *Mishnah* says they cannot be the object, and *Rabbi Yosi* confirms they can be the subject.

### Goses Analysis

The *Gemara* works through each case. For the *goses*: *bishlama goses lo nidar delav bar damim hu* — his exclusion from being the object of a monetary vow is clear, since he has no market value. *Velo ne'erach delav bar ha'amada veha'aracha hu* — and his exclusion from *erech* is derived from the verse at the opening of the tractate, which implies that the person must be capable of being stood up for valuation. Since a *goses* cannot stand, the technical condition for *erech* is not met.

### Condemned Analysis

For the condemned person: *bishlama lo nidar delav bar damim hu* — no market value, same as the *goses*. *Ela lo ne'erach amai lo* — but why can he not be the object of an *erech*? He can stand; the technical condition is met. The answer is from a *braita*: *minayin leyotze lehareg ve'amar erki alai shelo amar klum* — from where do we know that if a condemned person declares his own *erech* upon himself, it is not binding? *Talmud lomar kol cherem lo yipadeh* — "anything under the ban shall not be redeemed." The verse is read to mean that a person who is sentenced to destruction cannot be redeemed through any payment mechanism, and since *erech* is conceptually akin to a form of redemption, it does not apply.

One might think this exclusion applies even before the final verdict is rendered. *Talmud lomar min ha'adam* — the verse teaches *from people*, implying *not all people*. Only after the court issues its final judgment does the person lose the capacity to be the object of *erech*; prior to that, *erech* applies normally.

*Rabbi Chanina ben Akavya*, who holds that the condemned person *is* an object of *erech*, must find a different reading for *kol cherem lo yipadeh*. The *Gemara* provides one, drawn from *Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka*: since we find that some categories of heavenly death — those imposed by divine judgment rather than court verdict — can be mitigated through payment of *kofer*, atonement money (as in the case of the goring ox whose owner bears liability for the victim's death), one might have thought that the same principle applies to court-imposed execution. The condemned person might pay money to redeem himself from his sentence. *Kol cherem lo yipadeh* then teaches that no — a condemned person cannot pay *kofer* to escape execution. On this reading, the verse is about the prohibition of monetary redemption from the death penalty, not about *erech* at all, leaving *Rabbi Chanina ben Akavya* free to hold that *erech* applies.

### Atonement Principle

The *Gemara* refines this further. Perhaps the prohibition against *kofer* applies only to *mitot chamurot shelo nitna shegagatan lechapara* — the most severe categories of execution, for which even an unintentional violation carries no possibility of *korban* atonement. For less severe capital offenses — *mitot kalot shenitna shegagatan lechapara* — where an unintentional violation would be atoned for by a *korban*, perhaps there is a concept of monetary atonement even for the intentional offense, and *kofer* might be permitted. *Talmud lomar kol cherem* — "anything under the ban" — the word *kol*, "all," extends the prohibition to every form of court-imposed execution, regardless of its severity.

In the end, the substantive dispute between the *tanna'im* — whether the condemned person can be an *erech* — may rest less on the interpretation of *kol cherem* and more on a prior disagreement about whether a person's fixed value on the Torah chart can be set aside by any external circumstance. The one who says he cannot be an *erech* reads *kol cherem* as speaking directly to *erech* and redemption. The one who says he can be an *erech* reads it as speaking to *kofer* money, and finds the *erech* chart simply unaffected by the verdict of the court.
