# Kiddushin 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Me'ilah & Ma'aser Sheni

### Recap Me'ilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין לוֹקְחִים בְּהֵמָה בִּמְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי,
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta*, *Ma'aser Sheni* 1:15): **One may not purchase an animal with second-tithe money** outside of Jerusalem,

We pick up at the very bottom of 55b, where a *tanu rabbanan* opens a new section of the *gemara*. The preceding discussion had centered on a case of an animal found outside of Jerusalem whose identity as a particular type of *korban* was uncertain. That uncertainty served as an occasion to explore the position of Rabbi Meir, who holds that *me'ilah be'mezid* — intentional misappropriation of consecrated property — is halachically effective. The *gemara* had raised the possibility that according to Rabbi Meir, *me'ilah be'mezid* could operate even against something possessing *kedushat haguf*, intrinsic sanctity, since the power of a willful act might be sufficient to divest even a *korban* of its consecrated status. Under the ordinary rule, *me'ilah* does not remove *kedusha* from an object with intrinsic sanctity. The *gemara* further noted that this question would apply regardless of whether the animal in question was *kodshei kodashim* or *kodshei kallim*, with arguments running in both directions.

### Ma'aser Sheni Status

To step back and provide the broader framing: several days earlier, the *gemara* had addressed the foundational dispute over the status of *ma'aser sheni* — whether it is *mammon gavoah*, God's property, or *mammon hedyot*, the individual's own property encumbered with *kedusha*. The *gemara* concluded that by *ma'aser sheni* we rule like Rabbi Meir, who holds it is *mammon gavoah*, and that by *me'ilah* we rule against Rabbi Meir, rejecting the view that *me'ilah be'mezid* is halachically operative. Both issues, however, were explored in their own right regardless of the practical ruling. Now the *gemara* returns to the *ma'aser sheni* question and proceeds to examine several consequential and intricate *halachot* that arise from it.

---

## Animal Purchase Ban

### Rule: No Animal Purchase

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *ain lokchin behema b'dmei ma'aser sheni* — one may not purchase an animal with *ma'aser sheni* money. This prohibition applies outside of Jerusalem. Within Jerusalem, one may certainly purchase an animal and either slaughter it for its meat or bring it as a *korban*, since *ma'aser sheni* money is designated for food. The question is why one cannot purchase an animal outside of Jerusalem with that money.

### Rashi Explanation 1

Rashi offers two explanations. His first is that the only things one may bring to Jerusalem are the original fruit itself or the money to which it was transferred; one may not purchase other items outside and transport them in. According to this view, the animal purchase is invalid because the mechanism of *ma'aser sheni* transfer requires either the original produce or its monetary equivalent, not a further exchange.

### Tosafot Challenge

Tosafot challenges Rashi's first explanation by pointing out that it is in fact permissible to transfer *ma'aser sheni* to money and then use that money to purchase food, which one then brings to Jerusalem in place of the original fruit. It is not the case that only the original fruit or the original money may be brought — other food purchased with that money is equally valid. If so, Tosafot cannot understand why purchasing an animal outside of Jerusalem should be inherently problematic, given that one could simply slaughter it or bring it as a *korban* upon arrival. Rashi's first rationale, Tosafot argues, is thus without basis.

### Rashi Explanation 2

Rashi therefore offers a second explanation: the problem is that an animal driven on foot to Jerusalem will lose value in transit. Unlike other goods that can be transported without deterioration, a sheep or cow walked along the road will become less healthy, less fat, and therefore worth less upon arrival. The *ma'aser sheni* money thus suffers a loss. This is a rabbinic concern — the practical devaluation of the sacred funds.

### Tosafot Explanation

Tosafot adds a third explanation, also a secondary rabbinic concern. If one purchases an animal with *ma'aser sheni* money and then delays bringing it to Jerusalem, the animal may give birth, and its offspring would also carry the status of *ma'aser sheni*. One could quickly find oneself responsible for entire flocks encumbered with *kedushat ma'aser sheni*, which creates obvious practical difficulties and potential for abuse. For one reason or another, then — whether the animal's devaluation during transport or the proliferation of *ma'aser sheni* offspring — the Sages prohibited purchasing animals outside Jerusalem with *ma'aser sheni* funds.

## Unwitting Purchase

### Shogeg: Sale Reversed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לָקַח בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יַחְזְרוּ דָּמִים לִמְקוֹמָם.
>
> **Translation:** **and if one purchased** an animal **unwittingly,** without realizing he was using second tithe money, **the money returns to its place,** meaning that it is considered to be an erroneous transaction and is void, because the purchaser would prefer to bring the second tithe money to Jerusalem rather than to travel with an animal to bring as an offering.

The *gemara* moves to the top of 56a: *ve'im lakach bishogeg* — if one purchased an animal unwittingly, not realizing he was using *ma'aser sheni* money, then *yachzeru damim limkomam*, the sale is reversed. The money is returned to the seller, the animal is returned to the buyer, and the transaction is undone.

### Mekach Taos Principle

Rashi explains that this reversal is grounded in the principle associated with Rabbi Yehuda: had the buyer known he was using *ma'aser sheni* money, he certainly would not have wanted to violate this rabbinic restriction. Since, had he known, he would not have agreed to the transaction, the sale is treated as a *mekach ta'os* — an erroneous transaction — and is therefore void. This principle was already encountered earlier in the *sugya*, and here it applies straightforwardly.

---

## Mezid: Intent & Transfer

### Mezid Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמֵזִיד – תַּעֲלֶה וְתֵאָכֵל בַּמָּקוֹם. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּמִתְכַּוֵּין וְלָקַח תְּחִילָּה לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים,
>
> **Translation:** If he purchased it **intentionally,** the animal **is brought up and eaten in** its proper **place,** i.e., in Jerusalem. **Rabbi Yehuda said: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to** one who acted **with intent, and purchased** the animal from **the outset for the sake of a peace-offering,**

The *gemara* now addresses the case of intentional purchase: *bemezid ta'aleh vetachel bamakom* — if he knew what he was doing and violated the prohibition anyway, the animal acquires *kedushat ma'aser sheni*, and he should bring it to Jerusalem and consume it there with that *kedusha*. What is done is done; the appropriate response is to move forward and treat the animal as having assumed the sanctity of the money used to purchase it.

### Rabbi Yehuda Limit

Rabbi Yehuda, however, qualifies this rule. He asks: *bameh devarim amurim* — when does this ruling apply? Only when the buyer was *bimitkaven velakach techila leshem shelamim* — when he intentionally purchased the animal with the conscious purpose of having its *kedusha* transfer, and with the plan to bring it to Jerusalem and eat it there with *kedushat ma'aser sheni*.

### Shelamim Use Case

The natural and obvious case Rabbi Yehuda envisions is the following. A person buys a sheep with *ma'aser sheni* money; the sheep takes on *kedushat ma'aser sheni*. He could simply slaughter it and eat the meat as *ma'aser sheni* food in Jerusalem. But since he is going to Jerusalem anyway, he takes the opportunity to bring it as a *shelamim*, thereby fulfilling his obligation to bring a *korban* while still retaining the ability to eat the meat — since a *shelamim* cannot be an *olah*, which would require burning the entire animal and preclude eating it. This double purpose is entirely appropriate, and in such a case Rabbi Yehuda's rule applies: proceed, bring the animal up, eat it in Jerusalem.

## Profane Transfer Concern

### Problem Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בְּמִתְכַּוֵּין לְהוֹצִיא מְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי לְחוּלִּין, בֵּין שׁוֹגֵג בֵּין מֵזִיד – יַחְזְרוּ דָּמִים לִמְקוֹמָם.
>
> **Translation:** **But with regard to** one who acted **with intent to withdraw the second-tithe money** from its consecrated state **to a desacralized** state, **whether** his act was **unwitting,** in which case it is an erroneous transaction, or **intentional, the money returns to its place.**

Rabbi Yehuda continues: *aval bimitkavein lehotzi ma'os ma'aser sheni lechulin* — but what if the buyer's intention was not to transfer the *kedusha* to the animal but rather to spend the *ma'aser sheni* money and obtain a *chulin* animal in exchange? In this scenario, the buyer wishes to have steaks in his backyard rather than travel to Jerusalem, and he "sticks" the seller with the *ma'aser sheni* money. The seller, for his part, has agreed to this arrangement — perhaps because he is being offered more than he would ordinarily receive for the animal.

### Tosafot Reading

Tosafot's reading of this passage — which is clearer than Rashi's — presents a contrast between two intentional scenarios. In the first, the buyer knowingly transfers the *kedusha* of the money to the animal; he intends to bring a *shelamim* or eat the animal in Jerusalem with *kedushat ma'aser sheni*, and the transaction functions as an intentional and legitimate transfer of *kedusha*. In the second, the buyer knowingly spends the *ma'aser sheni* money — *lehotzi*, to spend it — in exchange for a *chulin* animal, deliberately avoiding the *kedusha* obligation and leaving the seller holding the *ma'aser sheni* money without a corresponding transfer of *kedusha* to the purchased object.

The phrase *lehotzi ma'os ma'aser sheni lechulin* thus means: to spend the *ma'aser sheni* funds in a transaction designed to yield *chulin* goods, while the money retains its *kedusha* in the seller's possession. The buyer intends to avoid the *kedusha* obligations entirely, and the seller may be complicit or simply uninformed.

### Misuse Risk

This scenario is deeply problematic. The *ma'aser sheni* money belongs in Jerusalem — it must be spent on food there, or its *kedusha* must transfer to something that will serve that purpose. By structuring the transaction as the buyer did, the money is diverted from its intended purpose. There is a real concern of *zilzul ma'aser sheni* — a degradation or misuse of the sacred funds. The seller may take the money and, thinking or hoping that the *kedusha* has been lifted, use it for ordinary purposes. The buyer has no intention of bringing anything to Jerusalem.

### Reversal Solution

Accordingly, in this scenario — *bein shogeg bein mezid*, whether the buyer was *shogeg* or *mezid* in a relevant sense — the sale must be reversed. The *gemara* notes that *shogeg* here refers to a buyer who did not even realize he was using *ma'aser sheni* money at all, in which case *mekach ta'os* applies directly. The *mezid* case refers to a buyer who knew it was *ma'aser sheni* money and intentionally structured the deal to keep the animal as *chulin* while leaving the seller with the encumbered funds. In both cases, *yachazir damim limkomam* — the sale is reversed. The Rabbis do not approve of this arrangement, and the reversal of the sale is the remedy. The concern is clear: the money is being diverted from its sacred purpose, and the seller may well misuse it.

This resolution is halachically sufficient, and the practical enforceability is a separate matter. The general principle is that by firmly establishing the halachic ruling, communal pressure and individual conscience play their role. Moreover, it is rarely a case of pure, calculating wickedness — most situations are more ambiguous, and the ruling creates the framework within which people are expected to act.

## Kiddushin Exception

### Kiddushin Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּמֵזִיד – קִידֵּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this statement: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If the second tithe was used **intentionally, he has betrothed** the woman with it, and the betrothal, which is a type of acquisition, is effective?

The *gemara* now raises a comparison to the *mishnah* before us: *Rabbi Yehuda omer bemezid kideish* — if one intentionally used *ma'aser sheni* money to betroth a woman, the *kiddushin* is valid. Here, too, the *ma'aser sheni* money retains its *kedusha* in the woman's hands — there is nothing for the *kedusha* to transfer to, since a woman is not a food item that can be eaten in Jerusalem.

### Why No Reversal?

This raises a pointed question. The woman is in an analogous position to the seller of the animal: she has received *ma'aser sheni* money, and in the back of her mind she might rationalize — as the animal seller might — that the *kedusha* has left the money and she can use it freely. The same concern of *zilzul ma'aser sheni* should apply. So why does the *gemara* not demand that the *kiddushin* be reversed, just as the animal sale is reversed? It is clearly a less drastic remedy to reverse a sale than to void a *kiddushin* — the latter would leave a woman thinking she is unmarried when she may not be, with all the attendant consequences for *ishut*. And yet, there is still a conceptual question: if the same misuse concern exists, why does it not generate at least a demand for reversal?

### Woman Knows Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אִשָּׁה יוֹדַעַת שֶׁאֵין מְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי מִתְחַלְּלִין עַל יָדָהּ, וְעוֹלָה וְאוֹכַלְתּוֹ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said:** The *halakha* in the mishna is based upon the assumption that **a woman knows that second-tithe money is not desacralized by her** acceptance of the betrothal, **and** it is as though she has agreed that **she** would **ascend and eat it in Jerusalem.**

Rabbi Elazar answers: *isha yoda'at she'ein me'os ma'aser sheni mischallin al yadah, v'ola v'ochla b'Yerushalayim* — the woman knows that *ma'aser sheni* money does not lose its *kedusha* through her receipt of it, and she therefore intends to go up to Jerusalem and spend it properly there. But this is not simply because she is a woman receiving *kiddushin* money — the key factor is that she is a *chavera*, a member of that scrupulous, learned subgroup that is expert in precisely these laws. The term *chaver* or *chavera* does not denote scholarly achievement per se but rather membership in a community distinguished by its careful practice of these *halachot*. Because she is a *chavera* and is *yoda'at* — she knows — we trust that the money will be used properly and do not demand reversal.

This answer is, in a sense, a limiting principle: the case of *kiddushin* in the *mishnah* is not a general rule but a specific case premised on the woman's knowledge. The real rule, applicable across all scenarios, is that wherever we fear the money will be misused, we demand reversal. The *kiddushin* case is bracketed off as exceptional not primarily because of the severity of undoing a marriage — though that is surely also a factor — but because of the presumption that a *chavera* will handle the funds properly.

## Non-Food Purchases

### Challenge Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: וַהֲרֵי בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה, עֲבָדִים וְקַרְקָעוֹת, דְּאָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין מְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי מִתְחַלְּלִין עֲלֵיהֶן,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya objects to this: But** there are cases of **a non-kosher animal, slaves, or land,** with regard to which **a person knows that second-tithe money is not desacralized by** being used to purchase **them,

Rav Yirmiyah now challenges this framework. He raises the case of purchasing non-food items with *ma'aser sheni* money: *beheima tmei'a, avadim, v'karka'os* — a non-kosher animal, slaves, or land. In all of these cases, the *kedusha* of the *ma'aser sheni* money obviously cannot transfer, since *kedusha* can only transfer to food items. The money therefore retains its *kedushat ma'aser sheni* in the seller's hands.

### Presumption of Knowledge

Rav Yirmiyah's point is this: if the buyer knows that *kedusha* cannot transfer to non-food items — and this should be common knowledge, since the *halacha* that *kedusha* transfers only to food is well established — then by the same logic we applied to the *chavera* who received *kiddushin* money, the seller should be presumed to know that the money retains its *kedusha* and should handle it accordingly. If that presumption of knowledge justified not reversing the *kiddushin*, it should equally justify not reversing the sale of a non-food item.

### Gemara Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן: אֵין לוֹקְחִים בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה עֲבָדִים וְקַרְקָעוֹת בִּמְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי אֲפִילּוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. וְאִם לָקַח – יֹאכַל כְּנֶגְדָּן!
>
> **Translation:** and we learned** in a mishna (*Ma'aser Sheni* 1:7): **One may not purchase a non-kosher animal, slaves, or land with second-tithe money, even in Jerusalem. And if one did purchase** them, **he must eat** other food **corresponding to their** value.

The *gemara* responds that in fact one may not purchase these non-food items with *ma'aser sheni* money — *ein lokchim beheima tmei'a avadim v'karka'os b'me'os ma'aser sheni afilu b'Yerushalayim*, not even in Jerusalem. However, if one did purchase such items, there is an alternative remedy rather than reversal: *v'im lakach yochal k'negdan*.

## Yochel K'Negdan

### Non-Food Remedy

The buyer must spend an equivalent sum on food and eat it in Jerusalem. He is not required to reverse the sale; instead, he personally undertakes the obligation that the *ma'aser sheni* money was supposed to fulfill. This alternative remedy applies when the sale of a non-food item cannot be reversed.

### Interpretation Debate

There is a *machlokes* among the *ba'alei haTosafos* regarding the precise mechanics of *yochal k'negdan*. One view holds that when the buyer spends his own money in Jerusalem on food, the *kedusha* of the original *ma'aser sheni* money — still sitting with the seller — transfers onto the buyer's new expenditure, and the seller's money thereby becomes *chulin*. The other view holds that the seller's money remains *kodesh* and must still find its way to proper use; the buyer's *yochal k'negdan* expenditure is a *knas* visited upon him but does not automatically discharge the seller's *ma'aser sheni* obligation. Either way, the buyer must spend equivalent money and eat it in Jerusalem; the question of whether this action discharges the *kedusha* on the seller's funds remains the subject of dispute.

---

## Decision Framework

### Solutions Differ?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? יַחְזְרוּ דָּמָיו לִמְקוֹמָם, כִּי הָתָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But why** should he do so? Why not say that **the money must return to its place,** just **as** was the ruling **there,**

At this point, the *gemara* confronts an apparent inconsistency. We seem to have different legal solutions for different types of objects: reversal for a kosher animal, *yochal k'negdan* for non-food items, and nothing at all for *kiddushin*. The *gemara* asks why these distinct solutions apply to these distinct object types.

### Seller Presence Key

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּשֶׁבָּרַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says:****The case here is** **where** the seller **fled** with the money, and there is no way of voiding the sale and returning the money to the purchaser.

Shmuel answers: *keshebarach* — the distinguishing factor is not the type of object at all, but whether the seller is present. If the seller has fled or is otherwise unavailable, it is impossible to reverse the sale. In that case, *yochal k'negdan* is the only available remedy. If the seller is present, reversal is possible and is the preferred remedy.

### Decision Chart

The *gemara*'s framework thus yields the following decision structure. The first question is: does the recipient know that the *ma'aser sheni* money retains its *kedusha* and will use it properly? If yes — as in the case of the *chavera* who receives *kiddushin* money — no remedy is necessary and the transaction stands. If no — the normal case, in which we fear the money will be misused — then we proceed to the second question: is the seller present? If present, we reverse the sale. If not present, we apply *yochal k'negdan*. The differences between the *mishnah*'s cases involving a kosher animal, a non-kosher animal, and *kiddushin* are not intrinsic to those categories but reflect these underlying circumstantial differences that happen to characterize each typical scenario.

---

## Why Reverse?

### Default Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא דְּבָרַח, הָא לֹא בָּרַח – קָנְסִינַן לְמוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And the reason is that he fled, but had he not fled, we would penalize the seller** by requiring him to return the money.

The *gemara* presses further. Given that both reversal and *yochal k'negdan* are available remedies when the seller is present, why is reversal treated as the default? Why not simply apply *yochal k'negdan* in all cases?

### Fine the Seller

The *gemara* answers: *kansinan lemocher* — we impose a penalty on the seller. Reversal is the means by which this penalty is administered. When the seller is present, he is held accountable, and reversal — forcing him to undo a transaction he sought to complete — is the appropriate sanction.

### Seller More Eager

Tosafot explains why the seller is the one penalized rather than the buyer. If the remedy were *yochal k'negdan* rather than reversal, the seller would clearly prefer that outcome: he has already sold his animal or property, and the buyer is the one burdened with the obligation to spend equivalent money in Jerusalem. *Yochal k'negdan* is a *knas* on the buyer. Reversal, by contrast, is a *knas* on the seller, who is forced to undo the very transaction he was eager to complete. Moreover, there is a general presumption that the seller is always more eager to sell than the buyer is to buy — the seller wanted to be rid of his goods, and reversing the sale frustrates that interest. Thus, when the seller is present, reversal is the appropriate mechanism.

## Rectify Location

### Seller as Enabler

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו עַכְבְּרָא גַּנָּב אֶלָּא חוֹרָא גַּנָּב.
>
> **Translation:** It is **not the mouse** that **steals, but the hole** that **steals.**

At this point the *gemara* introduces the famous principle: *lav achbara ganav ela chura ganav* — it is not the rat that steals, it is the hole it escapes through. The seller, by being available and willing to transact with *ma'aser sheni* money, provided the very opportunity that enabled the misuse. Without the seller's participation, the buyer could not have diverted the sacred funds. In this sense, the seller is the enabling condition, the "hole," and bears responsibility accordingly.

### Buyer More Culpable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא עַכְבְּרָא, חוֹרָא מַאי קָעָבֵיד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this logic: **But if not** for the **mouse, what has the hole done?**

The *gemara* itself, however, pushes back against this reasoning. After all, *ve'ee lav achbara chura mai ka'avad* — what good would the hole have done without the rat? The buyer is the one who actually possessed the *ma'aser sheni* money, who chose to spend it in this improper manner, who sought to obtain *chulin* goods while leaving the seller with encumbered funds. He is, if anything, the more culpable party — the one whose act we would most naturally identify as the *aveira*. Why then do we penalize the seller rather than the buyer?

### Rectify Not Punish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא אִיסּוּרָא – הָתָם קָנְסִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It stands to reason** that **anywhere that the forbidden** item **is,** i.e., anywhere that the money is, and in this case it is with the seller, **there we** should **penalize.**

The *gemara* resolves this with the word *mistavra* — it stands to reason. Both the buyer and the seller are complicit; both parties participated in the problematic transaction, and arguments can be made for penalizing either. But the *gemara* shifts the frame: *kol heicha de'ika issura hatam kansinan* — wherever the problem currently resides, that is where we focus our intervention. And where is the problem? The *ma'aser sheni* money is now with the seller. That money has *kedusha*; it does not belong where it is. The goal is not to punish individuals but to rectify the situation — to return the money to a position where it can fulfill its sacred purpose. Since the money is in the seller's hands, we address that situation directly by reversing the sale and returning the money to the buyer, from whom it must then properly make its way to Jerusalem. The asymmetry of penalty is thus not really about culpability at all; it is about where the money is and what needs to be done about it.

## Issurei Hana'ah

### Six Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּעׇרְלָה, בְּכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם, בְּשׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל, וּבְעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה, בְּצִפּוֹרֵי מְצוֹרָע, וּבִשְׂעַר נָזִיר, וּפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר, וּבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב, וְחוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with *orla*,** i.e., fruit grown during a tree's first three years, or **with diverse kinds in a vineyard,** i.e., grain and grapes planted together, or **with an ox that is** sentenced to be **stoned, or with a heifer whose neck is broken,** or **with** the **leper's birds** which are designated for his offering, **or with a nazirite's hair, or** with **a firstborn donkey, or** with **meat** cooked **in milk, or** with **non-sacred** animals **that were slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard,** if he betroths her with any of these items, the woman **is not betrothed,**

With the *ma'aser sheni* and *me'ilah* discussion concluded, the *gemara* now transitions to a new *mishnah*. We move from objects that are *kodesh* — sanctified — to a different category: *issurei hana'ah*, objects from which one is forbidden to derive any benefit. These are not objects possessing *kedusha* in the manner of *ma'aser sheni* or *hekdesh*, but objects that the Torah has placed entirely beyond the realm of permissible use.

### Object Examples

The *mishnah* enumerates several categories. *Orla* is fruit from the first three years of a tree's growth. *Kilai hakerem* refers specifically to the mixture of grape vine with other grain crops; a *kilayim* of two grain species without the vine would not render the produce forbidden, but the combination of vine with grain does. The *shor haniskal* is an ox sentenced to stoning because it gored a human being. The *eglah arufah* is the heifer whose neck is broken in the ritual performed when a murder victim is found outside a city. *Tziporei metzora* are the birds used in the purification process of the *metzora*. *Se'ar nazir* is the hair of the *nazir* that is shaved upon the completion of the *nezirut* period. *Peter chamor* is the firstborn of a donkey. *Basar bechalav* is meat and milk cooked together. And *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah* are non-sanctified animals slaughtered in the *Beit HaMikdash*. All of these are *assurim behana'ah*.

### Kiddushin Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְכָרָן וְקִידֵּשׁ בִּדְמֵיהֶן – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if **one sold them and betrothed** a woman **with** the **money** received from **their** sale, **she is betrothed,** as in these cases one may derive benefit from the money he receives in exchange for the forbidden item.

If one attempts to betroth a woman using any of these objects — *hamikadesh be'orla eino mekudeshet* — the *kiddushin* is invalid. The reason is that these objects are legally valueless: since one may not derive benefit from them in any recognized way, they carry no legal market value, and *kiddushin* requires transfer of an object worth at least a *perutah*.

### No Status Transfer

A crucial distinction marks off *issurei hana'ah* from *kedushat ma'aser sheni*: *kedusha* can transfer. If I sell a *ma'aser sheni* item, the money I receive takes on the *kedusha*. With *issurei hana'ah*, no such transfer occurs. If one transgresses and sells a cheeseburger to another person for ten dollars, having violated the prohibition against deriving benefit from *basar bechalav*, the ten dollars received is entirely *mutar* — plain, ordinary money, free of any forbidden status. The *issur hana'ah* does not follow the proceeds. Therefore, if one uses that ten dollars to betroth a woman, the *kiddushin* is valid: the money is perfectly ordinary, and the *isur* on the original item does not encumber the sale proceeds.

## Tosafot Discussion

### Market Value Q

Tosafot raises a significant question on the *mishnah*'s rule that *kiddushin* with *issurei hana'ah* is invalid. The assumption is that such objects are valueless. But Tosafot points out that *chazal* permit deriving benefit from *issurei hana'ah* in a non-standard manner — *shelo k'derech hana'aso*. For instance, olives that have not yet ripened can be used for medicinal purposes in a non-food application. Since there is some category of permissible use, however narrow, someone might be willing to pay something for the item. If so, the object does carry some market value, and *kiddushin* with it should in principle be valid.

### Value Answer

Tosafot's first answer is that the quantity in question simply does not reach *shaveh perutah*. Even if theoretically someone would pay something for the object because of its limited non-standard use, the amount of the object being used in the *kiddushin* is insufficient to constitute a *perutah*'s worth of value via that restricted use. Only if one had enough of the substance to generate a full *perutah* of value through that unusual channel would *kiddushin* be potentially valid.

### Awareness Answer

Tosafot's second answer introduces a different principle. Even granting that enough of the substance is present to be worth a *perutah* through non-standard use, if the woman is unaware that her use of the object is restricted to this unusual mode — if she assumed she was receiving something she could use normally — then the *kiddushin* is void as a *mekach ta'os*. She never would have agreed to the *kiddushin* had she known she was receiving something with such a limited and atypical form of permissible use. This answer effectively transforms the *mishnah*'s ruling from a categorical legal statement about the valuelessness of *issurei hana'ah* into a circumstantial rule dependent on the woman's awareness.

### Rashba: Lawful Valuation

The Rashba offers what is likely the most straightforward *pshat* in the *mishnah*, and it cuts through both of Tosafot's answers. Tosafot's two answers effectively concede that if there is enough of the substance and if the woman is aware of the restricted use, one could marry a woman with *issurei hana'ah* — which is plainly not what the *mishnah* is saying. The Rashba therefore takes a more fundamental position: it is irrelevant what someone might actually pay for an *issur hana'ah* object. The law defines these objects as valueless. Since the Torah forbids any benefit that we would legally recognize as *hana'ah*, there is no legally cognizable value. Whatever a buyer might actually pay for such an object reflects a transaction between private parties that we do not prohibit — but it does not alter the legal classification of the object's value. As a matter of *halacha*, the object's value is zero. *Kiddushin* performed with a legally valueless object is invalid, regardless of what private market conditions might dictate.

## Sources & Proofs

### Orlah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּעׇרְלָה מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״עֲרֵלִים לֹא יֵאָכֵל״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִיסּוּר אֲכִילָה, הֲנָאָה מִנַּיִן? שֶׁלֹּא יֵהָנֶה מִמֶּנּוּ, וְלֹא יִצְבַּע בּוֹ, וְלֹא יַדְלִיק בּוֹ אֶת הַנֵּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וַעֲרַלְתֶּם אֶת עׇרְלָתוֹ״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת כּוּלָּם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara explains the source of the *halakha* that it is prohibited to derive benefit from the items mentioned in the mishna. **With *orla*: From where do we** derive that one is prohibited from deriving benefit from *orla*? The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: "And shall have planted all manner of trees for food, then you shall count the fruit thereof as forbidden; three years shall it be as **forbidden [*arelim*]** to you; **it shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 19:23). From this verse, **I have** derived **only a prohibition** against **eating** it. **From where** do I derive that one may not even derive **benefit** from it? From where do we derive **that one may not** derive **benefit from it** in another manner, **and one may not paint with** the dye that can be extracted from the fruit, **and one may not light a lamp with** its oil? **The verse states: "Then you shall count the fruit thereof [*orlato*] as forbidden [*araltem*]"** (Leviticus 19:23), **to include all these** types of benefit in the prohibition.

The *gemara* now undertakes to trace the biblical sources for each of the *issurei hana'ah* listed in the *mishnah*. Beginning with *orla*: *ba'orlah minalan* — from where do we derive that *orla* is forbidden in benefit? A *beraita* is cited: the *pasuk* says *orlah lo ye'achel* — the uncircumcised fruit shall not be eaten. From this verse alone, I would know only that eating it is forbidden. How do I know that benefit is also prohibited — that one cannot use it for dyeing, or for fuel for a candle? The answer comes from the continuation of the verse: *va'araltem arlato* — make it entirely off-limits, as though it were uncircumcised. This language of total exclusion — *lirabot et kulam* — extends the prohibition to all forms of benefit.

### Kilai Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם מְנָלַן? אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: אָמַר קְרָא: ״פֶּן תִּקְדַּשׁ״ – פֶּן תּוּקַד אֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna stated that one may not betroth a woman **with diverse kinds in a vineyard. From where do we** derive that one is prohibited from deriving benefit from diverse kinds in a vineyard? **Ḥizkiyya said: The verse states:** "You shall not sow your vineyard with diverse kinds; lest the growth of the seed that you have sown be forbidden [*pen tikdash*], together with the yield of the vineyard" (Deuteronomy 22:9). Ḥizkiyya expounds **"lest** the growth of the seed that you have sown **be forbidden [*pen tikdash*]"** as: **Lest it be burned [*pen tukad esh*],** indicating that the seed of diverse kinds must be destroyed by fire, so that no benefit is derived from it.

For *kilai hakerem*: *bekilai hakerem minalan* — from where do we know this is forbidden in benefit? Chizkiyah cites the verse *pen tikdash* — lest it become sanctified. Chizkiyah reads this not as *tikdash* (become holy) but as *pen tukad esh* — lest it be deserving of burning by fire. This reading implies total prohibition: an object deserving to be burned is one from which no benefit may be derived.

### Rav Ashi Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: ״פֶּן יִהְיֶה קֹדֶשׁ״. אִי מָה קֹדֶשׁ תּוֹפֵס אֶת דָּמָיו וְיוֹצֵא לְחוּלִּין – אַף כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם תּוֹפֵס אֶת דָּמָיו וְיוֹצֵא לְחוּלִּין! אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְחִזְקִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says** that the phrase "*pen tikdash*" indicates that it is comparable to consecrated property, as if it said: **Lest it become consecrated [*kodesh*],** and one is therefore prohibited from deriving benefit from it, just as one may not derive benefit from consecrated property. The Gemara questions Rav Ashi's interpretation: **If** diverse kinds in a vineyard is compared to consecrated property, then **just as consecrated** property **transfers** its sanctity to the **money** with which **it** is redeemed **and becomes desacralized, so too, diverse kinds in a vineyard** should **transfer** their sanctity to the **money** with which **they** are redeemed **and become desacralized.** Contrary to this logic, the mishna taught that if one sells diverse kinds in a vineyard, the proceeds can be used for betrothal, indicating that it is not forbidden to derive benefit from this money. **Rather, it is clear** that the correct derivation is **as** that **of Ḥizkiyya.**

Rav Ashi proposes an alternative reading of *pen tikdash*: take it literally as *pen yihyeh kodesh* — lest it become like something holy. And since holy things — *hekdesh* — are forbidden in benefit, the same should apply to *kilai hakerem*.

### Objection

The *gemara* objects to Rav Ashi's reading. *Kedusha* is not a perfect analogy, because an object possessing *kedusha* can have that *kedusha* transferred to money, whereupon the original object becomes *chulin*. If *kilai hakerem* were truly analogous to *kodesh*, one might think that transferring its "status" to money would render the original produce permissible — which is clearly not the case.

### Resolution

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela k'deChizkiyah* — better to adopt Chizkiyah's *drasha* of *pen tukad esh* rather than the *kedusha* analogy. An object deserving to be burned is absolutely off-limits, and this resolves the difficulty raised against Rav Ashi's reading.

## Shor Haniskal

### Neveilah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל״ מִנַּיִן? דְּתַנְיָא: מִמַּשְׁמָע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״סָקוֹל יִסָּקֵל הַשּׁוֹר״ – אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁנְּבֵילָה הִיא, וּנְבֵילָה אֲסוּרָה בַּאֲכִילָה? מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא יֵאָכֵל אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ״? מַגִּיד לָךְ שֶׁאִם שְׁחָטוֹ לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ – אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its analysis of the mishna: **From where** is it derived that one is prohibited from deriving benefit from **an ox that is** sentenced to be **stoned?** The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: It is taught **by inference from** that **which is stated** with regard to an ox that killed a person: "And if an ox gore a man or a woman, that they die, **the ox shall be stoned,** and its flesh shall not be eaten; but the owner of the ox shall be clear" (Exodus 21:28). **Don't I know** from this verse **that** the stoning makes it an unslaughtered **animal carcass, and it is prohibited to eat** an unslaughtered **animal carcass? What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "Its flesh shall not be eaten"?** The verse **is telling you that** even **if one slaughtered** the ox **after its verdict had been reached** but before it was stoned, it is still **prohibited to eat** it.

For the *shor haniskal*: *shor haniskal minalan* — from where do we know the stoned ox is forbidden in benefit? A *beraita* begins by noting that the verse commands *sakol yisakel hashor* — the ox shall certainly be stoned. Once stoned, the ox is a *neveilah*, an animal that did not undergo proper *shechita*. And a *neveilah* is already forbidden in eating.

### Redundancy Q

If so, why does the Torah add *velo ye'achel et besaro* — and its flesh shall not be eaten? We already know from the *neveilah* status that eating is forbidden. What does this additional prohibition accomplish?

### Slaughter Teaching

The *beraita* explains: *she'im shechato le'achar shenigmar dino assur ba'achilah* — to teach that even if one slaughtered the ox properly after the death sentence was pronounced — thereby circumventing the *neveilah* status and producing what would otherwise be ordinary slaughtered meat — it is nevertheless forbidden to eat. The ruling of *shor haniskal* takes effect at the moment the *gmar din* is rendered; from that point forward, all the *halachot* of *shor haniskal* apply, including the eating prohibition, regardless of how the animal subsequently dies.

### Benefit Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּהֲנָאָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּבַעַל הַשּׁוֹר נָקִי״. מַאי מַשְׁמַע? שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן זוֹמָא אוֹמֵר: כְּאָדָם שֶׁאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: ״יָצָא פְּלוֹנִי נָקִי מִנְּכָסָיו וְאֵין לוֹ בָּהֶם הֲנָאָה שֶׁל כְּלוּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From where** is it derived that one is prohibited **from** deriving **benefit** from the ox as well? **The verse states: "But the owner of the ox shall be clear."** The Gemara asks: **From where may** it **be inferred** that one may not derive benefit from this ox? **Shimon ben Zoma says:** This is **like a person who says to another: So-and-so was left clear [*naki*] of his property, and he has no benefit from it at all.** Similarly, "But the owner of the ox shall be clear" means that he may not derive any benefit from the ox.

But that addresses the eating prohibition. For the forbidden benefit aspect: *talmud lomar u'va'al hashor naki* — the verse adds that the owner of the ox is *naki*, clean. The *gemara* cites Shimon ben Zoma's *drasha*: this is like the expression *yatza ploni naki minichasav* — that person was cleaned out of his possessions, he had no benefit from them at all. The phrase *ba'al hashor naki* thus teaches that the owner is "cleaned out" — he retains no benefit whatsoever from the ox, meaning all *hana'ah* from it is forbidden.

## Lo Ye'achel Rule

### Standard Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יֹאכַל״ ״לֹא תֹאכַל״ וְ״לֹא תֹאכְלוּ״ – אֶחָד אִיסּוּר אֲכִילָה וְאֶחָד אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה, עַד שֶׁיִּפְרֹט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁפָּרַט לְךָ בִּנְבֵילָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the question: **As Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Elazar says** that **wherever it is stated: "It shall not be eaten";** or **"you,"** singular, **"shall not eat"; or "you,"** plural, **"shall not eat"; both a prohibition** against **eating and a prohibition** against deriving **benefit** are indicated. This is so **unless the verse specifies for you** that one may derive benefit, **in the manner that it specified for you with regard to an animal carcass,** from which the verse explicitly permits one to derive benefit, as it states: "You may sell it to a foreigner" (Deuteronomy 14:21). Here, too, with regard to the ox that is stoned, the phrase: "Its flesh shall not be eaten," may serve to teach that one may not derive benefit from the stoned ox.

Rabbi Eliezer articulates a general principle: *kol makom shene'emar lo ye'achel, lo tochal, lo tochlu* — whenever the Torah uses the formulation "shall not eat" in any of its conjugations, the verse includes *both* the prohibition on eating and the prohibition on deriving benefit: *echad issur achilah ve'echad issur hana'ah bemashma*. The standard biblical prohibition to not eat encompasses the full prohibition on benefit.

### Exception Case

The only exception is where the *pasuk* itself explicitly permits benefit. The example is *neveilah*: the Torah says one may not eat a *neveilah*, but then adds *lager asher bish'arecha t'tnena va'achalhah o machor l'nochri* — you may give it to the resident alien or sell it to a foreigner. This explicit permission to sell demonstrates that benefit is permitted. Absent such explicit permission, *lo ye'achel* includes the prohibition on *hana'ah*.

### Shor Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּנָפְקָא לַן אִיסּוּר אֲכִילָה מִ״לֹּא יֹאכַל״, הָכָא אִיסּוּר אֲכִילָה מִ״סָּקוֹל יִסָּקֵל״ נָפְקָא. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתְּךָ לְאִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה הוּא דִּכְתִיב, נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא: ״לֹא יֵהָנֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This statement** of Rabbi Abbahu **applies only** to a case **where we derive the prohibition** against **eating from the words: "It may not be eaten,"** which is interpreted to include any manner of consumption. But **here, the prohibition** against **eating is derived from: "It shall be stoned,"** from which it is understood that since the ox becomes an unslaughtered animal carcass, it may not be eaten. **As if it enters your mind that** the phrase: "Its flesh may not be eaten," **is written to prohibit** one from deriving **benefit, let the verse state: "One may not derive benefit."** Why does it refer to eating if that prohibition has already been derived?

The *gemara* now applies this principle back to the *shor haniskal* and raises a difficulty. Given Rabbi Eliezer's rule that *lo ye'achel* always implies *issur hana'ah*, why did the *gemara* need the *drasha* of *ba'al hashor naki* to derive that *hana'ah* is forbidden? Let the extra *lo ye'achel et besaro* itself teach us the prohibition on *hana'ah*!

The resolution: Rabbi Eliezer's principle applies when the eating prohibition itself was derived from the *lo ye'achel* of that very verse. But for the *shor haniskal*, the eating prohibition did not derive from *lo ye'achel et besaro* — it was already established from the *neveilah* status, which flows from *sakol yisakel*. Since the eating prohibition was independently derived from a different source, the *lo ye'achel* cannot serve double duty by additionally teaching *issur hana'ah*; the extra *lo ye'achel et besaro* must therefore be doing something else entirely. And what does it teach? *Af al gav deshachatei ke'ein basar assar* — even if the ox was slaughtered and has the character of ordinary meat, it remains forbidden. That is the *chiddush* of the phrase *et besaro*. The source for *issur hana'ah* must therefore come from elsewhere — and that source is *ba'al hashor naki*, which teaches that the owner is entirely cleaned out and retains no benefit whatsoever.
