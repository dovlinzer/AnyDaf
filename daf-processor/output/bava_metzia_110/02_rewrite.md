# Bava Metzia 110 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dried Vines Dispute

### Vines Drying Out

The *daf* opens by returning to a case introduced at the bottom of 109b, marked by the asterisk approximately twelve lines from the bottom. The case involves a *mashkanta de-Sura* — a mortgage arrangement common in Sura whereby the creditor takes possession of a field or vineyard and consumes its produce for a fixed number of years, after which the loan is considered repaid. In the specific instance before us, someone mortgaged a *pardes* — which Rashi identifies here as a vineyard — for ten years, giving the creditor the right to all produce during that period. The complication is that within five years, some of the vines dried out and died. Since the creditor is entitled to ten years of produce, the question arises: does the wood of the dried-out vines count as a form of produce that the creditor may keep, or must it be treated as principal — as part of the vineyard itself — requiring reinvestment?

### Abaye: Dried Wood as Fru…

*Abaye amar peira havei*: Abaye rules that the dried wood is a form of *peira*, fruit. Since the vines dried out at the expected time — this is when they were naturally meant to cease producing — the drying and its resulting wood should be seen as what those vines produce at the end of their productive life. The creditor accordingly takes the wood as he would take any other fruit of the vineyard.

### Rava: Vines as Principal

*Rava amar karna havei*: Rava disagrees and holds that the dried vines remain part of the principal, the *keren*. They were part of the vineyard when the mortgage was arranged, and their having dried out does not transform them into produce. What must be done, says Rava, is to sell the dried wood and use the proceeds to purchase land — *veyilakach bo karka vehu ochel peiros* — so that the creditor may continue to derive *peiros* from that new asset over the remainder of the mortgage period. He cannot simply pocket the wood as if it were fruit.

### Baraita Proof

The *gemara* cited a *baraita* in support of Rava: *yavesh ha-ilan o niktzas, sheneihem asurin bo* — if a tree dries out or is cut down, both the creditor and the debtor are forbidden to benefit from it. Instead, *yimkru le-eitzim ve-yilakach bahen karka vehu ochel peiros* — it must be sold as wood, land purchased with the proceeds, and the creditor continues to eat the produce of that land. This is precisely Rava's position.

The *gemara* then asks: *mai lav yavesh dumya de-niktzas* — just as "cut down" implies the cutting was planned (i.e., at its natural time), so too "dried out" must refer to drying at its natural time. And the *baraita* rules that even then, it is treated as principal. This seems a strong proof for Rava.

### Abaye's Rebuttal

Abaye, however, pushes back: *niktzas dumya de-yavesh* — read the *baraita* the other way around. Perhaps both cases refer to situations where it was *not* the expected time. In that case the drying out or cutting down was anomalous, and Abaye would himself concede that in such cases the wood is treated as principal — because the vine was never supposed to die during the mortgage period, it remains part of the vineyard in every sense. Abaye's position that the wood counts as *peira* applies only when the vine dries at its natural and anticipated time, since in that scenario the vine is simply completing its normal productive lifecycle and the wood is its final yield.

### Inherited Trees Case

The *gemara* continues with a further attempt to resolve the dispute. The case involves a woman whose husband has usufruct rights over her property — a parallel structure to the *mashkanta de-Sura*, in that one party holds title while another has the right to the *peiros*. The scenario is: *naflu lah gefanim ve-zeisim zekeinim* — she inherited vineyards and olive trees that were already old and dried out. The rule stated is *yimkru le-eitzim ve-yilakach bahen karka vehu ochel peiros* — sell them as wood, buy land, and the husband takes the produce of that land. This again implies that dried vines and olive trees are treated as principal, not as fruit — supporting Rava.

### Abaye's Concession

Abaye offers two responses. First: *eima ve-hizkinu* — perhaps "old" does not mean naturally old at the end of their lifespan, but rather that they became old prematurely. If so, Abaye would concede that this is principal, since he holds that unexpected drying always counts as *keren*. The case therefore does not necessarily speak to naturally aged trees.

Second — *ve-iba'it eima* — Abaye offers an alternative explanation entirely: this case was already established elsewhere as referring to trees that fell to her on land that she does not own. She inherited the trees alone, not the underlying land. In that situation, there can be no question of whether the trees are the "fruit" of the land, because she owns only the trees themselves, and those trees are inherently principal. Under such circumstances, Abaye would agree with Rava. His own view — that naturally dried wood is *peira* — applies only when the owner holds both the land and the trees, so that one can conceptually distinguish the trees' produce from the land's principal value.

## Disputed Years Doctrine

### Lost Document Setup

The *gemara* opens a new topic. There was a document of *mashkanta de-Sura* in which the number of years was written simply as *shanin stama* — "years," with no numeral specified. The document was dated and established that the creditor had the right to consume the produce for "years," after which the loan would be considered repaid. The number of years, however, was left unspecified — a significant omission.

### Three vs. Two Years

*Malveh amar shalosh, loveh amar shtayim*: the creditor claims he was entitled to three years of produce; the debtor maintains it was only two. One might initially think this resembles a case of *modeh be-miktzat* — an admission to part of a claim — but the *gemara* develops it differently.

The scenario intensifies: *ve-kadim malveh ve-achlinhu le-peira* — the creditor anticipated the dispute and already consumed the produce of the third year before the case came before *beis din*. Now the debtor seeks to recover the value of that fruit. *Mi ne'eman?* Who is believed?

### Karka Be-Chezkat

*Rav Yehuda amar karka be-chezkat be'aleha kayama*: Rav Yehuda holds that land is always considered to be in the presumptive possession of its owner. This is the principle of *karka be-chezkat be'aleha*. Even though the creditor has already consumed the fruit, the analysis must go back to how the situation stood when the issue first arose. Had the dispute come to *beis din* at the beginning of the third year, the land would have been considered in the debtor's possession, and the creditor — seeking to claim the fruit of that year — would have been the *motzi me-chaveiro*, the one extracting from another, bearing the burden of proof. Rav Yehuda holds that this classification does not change simply because the creditor has now pocketed the money. The burden of proof remains on the creditor, and if he cannot prove his entitlement to three years, he must return the value of the fruit to the debtor.

### Rav Kahana Position

Rav Kahana disagrees: *peiros be-chezkat ochleihen* — the fruit is in the presumptive possession of whoever consumed it. In Rav Kahana's view, the relevant question for *hamotzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'ayah* is not the abstract ownership-status of the land as it stood earlier, but the concrete status quo as it exists now, when *beis din* must act. Currently, the creditor has already consumed the fruit. The debtor, seeking to recover that value, is now the *motzi me-chaveiro* and bears the burden of proof. Without proof, the debtor cannot recover anything.

The *gemara* records: *hilchesa ke-vasei de-Rav Kahana* — the law follows Rav Kahana.

### Rav Nachman Contradiction

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty. We have an established ruling that the law follows Rav Nachman, who stated *karka be-chezkat be'aleha omedet* — the land stands in the presumptive possession of its owner. That ruling emerged from a case involving a rental dispute: a house was rented for twelve months at a rate that also implied a monthly figure, and then it turned out to be a leap year with a thirteenth month. Rav Nachman ruled that the tenant bears the burden of proof. The landlord, as owner of the land, is the *muchzak*, and even if the tenant has already benefited from the extra month, the tenant must pay — because the land's ownership-presumption had already established who bore the burden when the dispute first arose. This appears to mirror Rav Yehuda's position and contradict the ruling in favor of Rav Kahana.

### Evidence Distinction

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction with a principled distinction: *hasam milsa de-lo avida le-igluyei hi, hacha milsa de-avida le-igluyei* — there, the dispute can never be resolved by new evidence, whereas here, it can.

In the rental case, the document said "twelve months" and also implied a monthly rate — a self-contradiction with no way to resolve it. No further witnesses or evidence can emerge. The dispute is inherently unresolvable.

In the mortgage case, the document said only "years" without a number. Witnesses might yet come forward to testify as to what number of years was originally agreed upon. New evidence could surface.

### No Court Reversals

*Ve-atruchei beis dina trei zimni lo matrchinnen* — we do not burden *beis din* with having to reconvene twice. If *beis din* were to rule now — relying on the *karka be-chezkat be'aleha* principle — that the debtor is the *muchzak* and the creditor must return the fruit's value, and then witnesses later emerged proving the arrangement was indeed for three years, *beis din* would have to reverse its ruling and require the debtor to pay back the creditor. To avoid this risk of reversal, the *gemara* applies the *hamotzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'ayah* rule in its pragmatic sense: we will not disturb the current state of affairs unless proof is provided. Since the creditor presently holds the consumed fruit's value, the debtor must bring proof to dislodge him.

Where no new evidence can ever emerge — as in the rental case — there is no risk of reversal, and *beis din* can safely apply the conceptual *karka be-chezkat be'aleha* reasoning. In that case, we rule according to Rav Nachman, treating the landlord as the *muchzak* regardless of who currently holds the money.

## Lost Document Credibility

### Five vs. Three Years

A related but distinct scenario is now introduced. Here, the *malveh* claims five years of entitlement and the *loveh* claims only three. This time the loan document has been lost before being used in any proceeding. The *loveh* tells the *malveh*: go bring the document and we'll see how many years it specifies. The *malveh* responds: *shtra irkas li* — I lost it.

What makes this case structurally different from the previous one is that the creditor has already been in possession of the land for three years — that much is undisputed. The debtor insists the mortgage period has now concluded; the creditor claims two additional years remain.

### Rav Yehuda Migo

*Amar Rav Yehuda: malveh ne'eman* — Rav Yehuda rules that the creditor is believed. His reasoning is a *migo*: *migo de'ei ba'ei amar lekucha hi be-yadi* — the creditor could have made the stronger claim that he purchased the land outright. The *sugya* in *Bava Batra* establishes that if someone has been in uncontested possession of land for three years without the owner registering a protest, that person may claim he purchased it and be believed — since one does not preserve a bill of sale indefinitely. Since the creditor could have deployed this stronger claim and been believed, he should certainly be believed in the lesser claim of entitlement to two more years under the mortgage.

This *migo* is especially noteworthy given that ordinarily, under *karka be-chezkat be'aleha*, the debtor's ownership-presumption would place the burden of proof on the creditor. The *migo* is doing significant work here to overcome that presumption.

### Rejection of Migo

*Rav Zvid and Rav Avira* reject Rav Yehuda's *migo* entirely. They argue that the premise is flawed: the creditor did not lose the document.

### Careful with Document

*Mai ta'ama? — hai shtra, keivan de-leguvayna kai, mizdehar zahir bei* — this document is used to collect the loan. Unlike a bill of sale, which one might not bother preserving after three years of unchallenged possession, a loan document is kept precisely because it is the instrument of collection. A creditor always knows he may need it to prove the debt if the debtor ever denies it. Therefore, *umichbash hu de-chavshei le-shtarei* — he has not lost it; he has hidden it. The inference is that if the document were produced, it would support the debtor's version of events. The creditor has concealed it to claim two extra years of produce.

Since we suspect the document exists and contradicts him, his claim is not merely unsubstantiated — it is affirmatively undermined. A *migo* does not function when there is such conspicuous counter-evidence against the claimed statement. Moreover, the *migo* here is being deployed *le-hotzi* — to extract from the debtor's possession — making it an even weaker tool. Rav Zvid and Rav Avira accordingly refuse to credit the *migo* in this context.

### Abuse Concern

*Ravina* raises a broader structural concern to *Rav Ashi*. If we accept that a creditor in a *mashkanta de-Sura* could claim *lekucha hi be-yadi* after three years of uncontested possession, the entire institution of the *mashkanta de-Sura* is exposed to systematic abuse. Every creditor, after three years, could simply conceal the document and declare he purchased the property. *Ve-chi matkini rabanan milta de-ati bah lidei pesida* — would the Rabbis have instituted an arrangement susceptible to such exploitation?

### Tax and Trench Test

The response is: *lo — hatam tikinu leh rabanan de-marei ara yaheiv tiska ve-charei charya* — no, the Rabbis structured the *mashkanta de-Sura* so that the owner of the land continues to pay the property tax and to dig the required trenches each year. These acts of public, official ownership make it impossible for the creditor to credibly claim he purchased the land. The ongoing obligations of ownership are visibly performed by the debtor, not the creditor.

### Annual Protest Required

The *gemara* then asks: *ara de-leisa bei charya ve-lo yaheiv taska mai* — what of land that requires neither digging nor property tax? How is the owner protected there? *Amar lei ibei lei lemichuyei* — the owner must register a *machaah*, a formal protest.

One might have thought a *machaah* would be irrelevant here, since the classic *machaah* is a protest against someone who is on your land without authorization — the very outrage of unauthorized presence provokes the owner to object, and silence implies consent. But here the creditor's presence is entirely authorized under the mortgage arrangement, so the owner would have no natural reason to protest. Nevertheless, the *gemara* rules: if the institution of *mashkanta* is to be viable, owners must understand that they must make an annual *machaah* to prevent any future claim of purchase. *Lo imchi, mai? Ihu de-afsid anafshei* — if the owner failed to protest, he has brought the loss upon himself.

## Sharecropper Dispute

### Half vs. Third Share

A new dispute: *aris omer le-machetza yaradeti u-va'al ha-bayit omer li-shlish horadeti* — the sharecropper (*aris*) claims the agreement was for him to receive half the produce, and the landowner (*ba'al ha-bayit*) claims the agreement was for one-third. *Mi ne'eman?*

### Rav Yehuda Position

Rav Yehuda rules that the *ba'al ha-bayit* is believed — applying again the principle *karka be-chezkat be'aleha*: the land is in the presumptive possession of its owner, so the *aris*, who is seeking to extract a greater share, is the *hamotzi me-chaveiro* and bears the burden of proof.

### Rav Nachman Custom

Rav Nachman says: *ha-kol ke-minhag ha-medina* — everything depends on local custom. Whatever the prevailing sharecropping arrangement is in that locale is presumed to govern, and that standard operates even against the usual *hamotzi me-chaveiro* calculus.

### Local Variation

The *gemara* initially attempts to reconcile these views by suggesting they are not actually in dispute: where the local custom is for the *aris* to receive half, the *aris* is believed; where the custom is for one-third, the *ba'al ha-bayit* is believed. On this reading, both Rav Yehuda and Rav Nachman are simply describing different locales, and the question of *mi ne'eman* is answered in each place by *minhag ha-medina*.

### Abaye Overrides Custom

However, Rav Mari the son of the daughter of Shmuel reports that Abaye rejected this harmonization: *afilu be-asra de-shakeil arisa palga pligi* — even in a place where the local custom is for the *aris* to receive half, they still dispute. Rav Yehuda maintains that *karka be-chezkat be'aleha* overrides even *minhag ha-medina*: the *ba'al ha-bayit* remains the *muchzak* and the *aris* bears the burden of proof regardless of local custom. This reflects how strongly Rav Yehuda holds to the ownership-presumption principle.

### Landowner's Migo

The *gemara* then adds another basis for the *ba'al ha-bayit*'s credibility, apparently independent of the *karka be-chezkat be'aleha* principle. *Rav Yehuda amar ba'al ha-bayit ne'eman de-i ba'ei amar sechiri u-lekiti hu* — the *ba'al ha-bayit* could have claimed that these workers are mere day laborers whom he pays by the day, not sharecroppers at all, and he would owe them only the daily wage. Since he could have made this stronger claim and been believed — particularly given the absence of documentation — the *migo* grants him credibility when he makes the lesser claim of a one-third sharecropping arrangement. One might ask whether this *migo* functions even in cases where the sharecropping relationship is publicly known; that question is left open.

## Orphans vs. Creditor

### Improvements Question

The *gemara* now introduces a case involving orphans and a creditor of their late father. The father borrowed money, and a lien was placed on a specific piece of property. After the father's death, the land — now in the hands of the orphans — has been improved. Its value at the time of the loan was, say, a thousand dollars; it is now worth twelve hundred. The question is: who made the improvements? If the father made them before his death, they fall under the creditor's lien and he may collect the improved value. If the orphans made the improvements after inheriting, the lien never attached to those improvements and the creditor has no claim on them.

### Rav Chanina View

*Savar Rav Chanina le-meimar: ara be-chezkat yatmei kayma u-va'al chov alav ra'ayah* — Rav Chanina initially inclined to say that the land is in the presumptive possession of the orphans, and it is therefore the creditor who must bring proof that the improvements were made during the father's lifetime.

### Rabbi Yochanan Position

A certain elder corrected this, citing Rabbi Yochanan: *al ha-yesomim le-havi ra'ayah* — on the contrary, it is the orphans who bear the burden of proof. *Mai ta'ama? Ara, keivan de-leguvayna kaimei, ke-man de-gavya dami* — since this land is subject to a lien and will certainly be collected upon, it is considered as though it has already been collected. The creditor, by virtue of his lien, is regarded as the effective *ba'al*, the possessor, and the orphans must prove that the improvements are theirs.

### Apotiki Lien Status

The *gemara* notes — and Rashi and *Tosafot* introduce this point earlier as well — that this principle applies specifically to a case of *apotiki*, a specific lien: *de-amar lei lo yehei lach pira'on ella mi-zu* — the creditor explicitly designated this single property as the exclusive source of collection for the loan. Because the creditor has no recourse to any other asset, this land is treated as though it already belongs to him in a meaningful sense. It is that heightened degree of lien which shifts the *chazakah* and places the burden of proof on the orphans.

### Tree Analogy

Abaye finds support for this ruling in a *tanna'itic* source: *safek zeh kadam, safek zeh kadam, kotzetz ve-eino nosen damim* — if a tree was planted within seventy-five feet of a city's perimeter and it is uncertain whether the city or the tree came first, the tree is cut down without payment. The reason: *keivan de-lemikatz kai* — since the tree will be cut down regardless of which came first, the only question is whether payment is owed. We therefore do not treat the tree as in the owner's possession such that the city must prove its case and then pay; rather, since cutting is inevitable, the owner must prove his entitlement to payment. *Amina lei: ayisi ra'ayah u-shkol* — bring proof and collect.

So too here: *hai shtra, keivan de-leguvayna kai, ke-man de-gavya dami* — since the document will certainly be used to collect from this property, it is as though already collected, and the orphans must prove the improvements are theirs to avoid the creditor taking them.

## Improvements Valuation

### Land Division View

Returning to the case where the orphans have successfully brought proof that they made the improvements: the question now is how the creditor collects. If the land was worth a thousand dollars — the amount of the lien — and the orphans' improvements added two hundred, the creditor is entitled to collect a thousand, not twelve hundred. But how does he do so? *Savar Rabbi Chanina le-meimar: ki mesalkinan lehu be-ara mesalkinan lehu* — Rav Chanina initially thought: the creditor takes his proportional share of the land itself. He is only owed a thousand out of twelve hundred, so he takes five-sixths of the plot and leaves one-sixth — worth two hundred dollars — to the orphans.

### Cash Payment View

*Ve-lo hi* — this is not the *halacha*. *Bi-dmei mesalkinan lehu* — the creditor takes the entire plot of land and pays the orphans two hundred dollars in cash. He is not compelled to divide the land.

### Three Evaluators

This ruling derives from Rav Nachman citing Shmuel: *shelosha shamin lahem et ha-shevach u-ma'alin otam bi-damim* — three appraisers evaluate the improvement and the party who takes the whole land pays for the *shevach* in money. The principle is that we do not compel a litigant to break up a piece of land; instead, whoever holds the superior claim takes the whole and compensates the other party in cash.

### Inheritance Example

Shmuel's ruling enumerated three categories. The first is *bechor le-pashut* — a firstborn son vis-à-vis a regular son. If there are two sons — call them Reuven, the firstborn, and Shimon — Reuven is entitled to two thirds of the estate and Shimon to one third. Suppose that before the estate is divided, Shimon makes capital improvements on the undivided property. When the division occurs, Reuven takes his two thirds, which now includes a portion of Shimon's improvements. Shimon cannot demand that Reuven return a portion of the land corresponding to those improvements; rather, Reuven takes his full two thirds and pays Shimon in cash for the improvement embedded in that share.

### Uniform Treatment

The same rule applies in the second category — *ba'al chov u-ketuvat isha le-yesomim*: a creditor or a wife collecting her *ketubah* from orphans. Even when the orphans have proved their improvements, the creditor or widow takes the entire plot and compensates the orphans in cash. The third category — *ba'al chov le-lakuchos* — covers the case where a creditor exercises a lien against a purchaser who has improved the land. Again, the creditor takes the whole and pays the purchaser for the improvement in cash.

### Valuation Threshold

However, the *gemara* introduces a crucial distinction that governs whether the creditor must pay for the *shevach* at all.

### Improvements Within Loan

*Ha de-masik bei ke-shi'ur ara u-shvacha* — if the loan amount equals the combined value of the land plus the improvements, the creditor collects everything and pays nothing. The purchaser (or orphan) who made improvements took a risk; the creditor's lien covers the full current value, and the improver has no claim against him.

### Improvements Exceed Loan

*Ha de-lo masik bei ke-shi'ur ara u-shvacha* — but when the loan amount equals only the original value of the land, and the improvements push the total value beyond the loan, the creditor takes the whole plot and pays the improver the excess in cash. He has no right to benefit from improvements beyond what his lien entitles him to collect.

### Cash Alternative

The *gemara* then raises a problem with this second scenario. *Ravina* asks *Rav Ashi*: you are saying the creditor takes the whole land and pays the purchaser two hundred dollars in cash. But consider the view that a purchaser who has the money can pay off the creditor and prevent him from seizing the land entirely — *ei it lei zuzei le-lokeiach, lo matzi mesalik lei le-va'al chov* states the opposing view. If the purchaser *could* have tendered the full loan amount and kept all the land, why can he not, when the creditor is collecting and offering him two hundred dollars, say: with that two hundred dollars I want to retain a two-hundred-dollar piece of the land? If cash can stop the creditor from taking everything, a fortiori it should give the purchaser a right to retain a corresponding slice.

### Apotiki Exception

The answer: *hacha be-mai askinan ke-gon de-shavyei nihalei apotiki, de-amar lei lo yehei lach pira'on ella mi-zu* — the case throughout this discussion presupposes a specific *apotiki* lien, in which the creditor designated this single property as the exclusive source of repayment. In such a case, even a purchaser who has the money cannot simply pay off the creditor and hold on to the land — the creditor has a vested right to collect specifically from this property. It is precisely because of the *apotiki* designation that the purchaser cannot leverage the cash payment into a retained slice of land. Rashi and *Tosafot* note that this *apotiki* framing actually underlies and explains the entire discussion of the creditor's enhanced standing from the earlier sections, though the *gemara* makes it explicit only here.

## Rental Agreements

### Seven-Year Period

The *mishnah* now introduces rules about the terms of rental agreements. *Ha-mekabel sadeh me-chaveiro le-shavua echad, be-sheva me'ot zuz, ha-shevi'it min ha-minyan* — one who rents a field for *one shavua* — a seven-year period — for seven hundred *zuz*, the *shemitah* year counts within that seven. Since the renter contracted for a *shavua*, a biblical "week" of years, *shemitah* is inherently included.

### Seven Planting Years

*Kibel heimenu sheva shanim be-sheva me'ot zuz, ein ha-shevi'it min ha-minyan* — but if he said he would rent for *seven years* of farming for seven hundred *zuz*, then *shemitah* is not included in the count. In this formulation, "years" plainly refers to productive agricultural years, each of which involves actual planting, and *shemitah* — a year in which the land must lie fallow — does not qualify.

## Worker Payment Laws

### Torah Sources

The *mishnah* now shifts to the laws governing the obligation to pay hired workers promptly. The Torah provides two *pesukim* addressing this. One states *lo tavo alav ha-shemesh* — *beyomo titein secharo* — do not let the sun set on his wages, pay him during his day. The other states *lo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker* — do not let the wages of a hired worker sleep over with you until morning. *Chazal* understand that these two cannot both address the same type of worker, since one cannot simultaneously fulfill both. The first must refer to a worker who labored at night — his payment window is the following day, and one transgresses by letting the sun set without paying. The second must refer to a worker who labored during the day — the following night is his window, and one transgresses by letting his wages remain unpaid until morning.

### Day Workers

*Sechir yom goveh kol ha-laila* — a daytime hired worker can collect throughout the entire following night. If not paid, the employer transgresses *lo talin pe'ulat sachir*.

### Night Workers

*Ve-sechir laila goveh kol ha-yom* — a nighttime hired worker can collect throughout the entire following day. If not paid, the employer transgresses *lo tavo alav ha-shemesh*.

### Hourly Workers

*Sechir sha'ot goveh kol ha-laila ve-kol ha-yom* — an hourly worker, who may have worked spanning both day and night, can collect the entire remainder of the night plus the entire following day. The precise reasoning here is debated further in the *gemara*, but at the *mishnah* level the rule is stated as a compound window.

### Long-Term Workers

The *mishnah* extends the same framework to workers hired for longer periods — *sechir Shabbat, sechir chodesh, sechir shanah, sechir shavua* — weekly, monthly, yearly, or seven-year workers. *Yatza ba-yom, goveh kol ha-yom; yatza ba-laila, goveh kol ha-laila ve-kol ha-yom* — if the engagement concludes during the day, the entire remaining day is the payment window; if it concludes at night, the entire night plus the following day is the window. The same asymmetry applies regardless of the length of the overall engagement. For example, if Yaakov finished his seven years of labor for Lavan at midday, Lavan was obligated to pay before sunset. If the term concluded at seven in the evening, payment was due before sunset the following day — a window of nearly twenty-four hours.

Two explanations are given for this asymmetry. One is that *lo talin* is only transgressed if a full night has elapsed since the work was completed without payment; if the work ended partway through the night, no full night has elapsed by daybreak, so the first morning does not trigger *lo talin*, and the operative prohibition is *lo tavo alav ha-shemesh* — sunset of the next day. The other explanation follows the structure that Rashi implies: halachically, the night and its following day form a single unit, so a half-night of uncollected wages extends naturally into the full following day without the worker losing his window.

### One-Time Transgression

The *baraita* makes a striking point: *mamashma she-ne'emar lo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha — eini yode'a she-ad boker?* If the Torah says "do not let it sleep over," of course that means until morning — what does specifying *ad boker* add? *Melamed she-eino over ela ad boker rishon bilvad* — it teaches that the employer transgresses *lo talin* only once — at that first morning. He does not continue to accrue a fresh transgression at each subsequent morning by virtue of having still not paid. This is a striking *din*, since once the initial transgression has occurred, there is no further *lo talin* violation to incentivize payment — leaving the employer without ongoing halachic pressure.

### Bal Teshahe

*Mikan ve-eilach mai?* — what does the employer transgress from that point on? *Amar Rav: over mishum bal teshahe* — he transgresses the prohibition of *bal teshahe*, delaying wages. Rav Yosef asks: where is the *pasuk* for this? The answer is drawn from *Mishlei*: *al tomar le-re'acha lech va-shuv u-machar eten ve-yesh itach* — do not tell your neighbor "go and come back, I'll give it to you tomorrow," when you have the means to pay him now. This constitutes a separate prohibition against delay, apparently of rabbinic character rather than *de-oraita*.

### Money Availability

The critical qualifier is the phrase *ve-yesh itach* — *and you have it with you*. If the employer genuinely does not have the funds on hand — if he himself must collect money to make payment — he has not let wages "sleep over" in his possession and does not transgress *lo talin*. The same caveat applies to *bal teshahe*: one only transgresses by delaying payment when the money is available and one simply chooses not to pay.
