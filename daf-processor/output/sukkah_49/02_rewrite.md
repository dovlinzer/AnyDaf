# Sukkah 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sadducee & Broken Horn

### Sadducee Water Incident

The gemara opens by returning to the context of the *nisuach hamayim*, the water libation performed on Sukkot. As discussed previously, the *kohen* would ascend the ramp, make a left turn and then a right turn, arriving at the two funnels atop the altar — the eastern funnel for the wine libation and the western funnel for the water. The people would demand that the *kohen* raise his hand so they could verify he was performing the libation correctly into the funnel, because on a prior occasion a *kohen* who was a *tzaduki* — a Sadducee who did not believe in the obligation of *nisuach hamayim* — had deliberately poured the water on his feet rather than into the funnel, whereupon the entire assembly pelted him with their *esrogim*.

The *tanna'im* now revisit this incident explicitly: *maaseh b'tzaduki echad shenisach al gabei raglav* — there was an incident with a certain Sadducee who poured the water libation on his feet, *veragmuhu kol ha'am b'esrogim* — and the entire people stoned him with their *esrogim*. Although this episode was already visible in the *mishnah*, the *baraita* here adds the explicit identification of the officiant as a *tzaduki*.

### Horn Damage & Salt Fix

The *baraita* continues: *oso hayom nifgemah keren hamizbeach* — that very day, the horn of the altar was chipped. To appreciate what this means, one must recall the physical structure of the altar. At each of the four upper corners of the *mizbeach* stood a projecting cube, one *amah* by one *amah* by one *amah*, known as the *keren*, the horn. The Sadducee had been pouring near this corner, and when the crowd hurled their *esrogim* at him, some struck the horn, chipping it.

In response, *hiviu gush shel melach l'sasmuhu* — they brought a clump of salt to fill in the chip. The precise mechanics of this repair are admittedly puzzling, given that these altar components were constructed of plaster rather than raw stone. Whatever the method, the text is unambiguous that the repair was not halachically effective: *lo mipnei shehuchshar l'avoda* — not because this rendered the altar fit for *avodah*; the salt patch did not constitute a genuine structural restoration. Rather, *ella mipnei shelo yiraeh mizbeach pagum* — it was done solely so that the altar would not appear damaged or lacking. There is a value in ensuring that the altar presents itself with dignity even while awaiting proper repair, without resorting to the visual equivalent of scaffolding bearing an apologetic notice. But one could not actually continue performing the *avodah* on an altar in this condition.

### Altar Requirements

The gemara explains why the damaged horn renders the altar unfit. At the top of 49a: *shekol mizbeach she'ein lo lo kevesh v'lo keren v'lo yesod v'lo ribua pasul l'avoda* — any altar lacking a ramp (*kevesh*), a horn (*keren*), a foundation (*yesod*) — that lowest projecting base — or proper squareness (*ribua*) is invalid for the *avodah*. Rabbi Yosi bar Yehuda adds that even the *sovev* — the encircling ledge that runs around the altar — is indispensable.

It is worth noting in passing that the concern for *ribua* — the precise square shape — and the disqualifying effect of chipping appear in another halachic context as well: *tefillin*, where the cube-with-base structure and the requirement of perfect squareness are strikingly analogous.

### Rashi's Torah Links

The natural question is why these specific components carry disqualifying force. Rashi explains the underlying principle: *kol makom shene'emar hamizbeach le'akev* — wherever the Torah uses the definite article *hamizbeach*, "the altar," it implies that the altar must conform precisely to its prescribed form. *De'hachi mashma hamizbeach ze hu hamizbeach ha'asuy kach* — the phrasing "the altar" indicates it must be exactly as specified. Rashi then traces each component to its biblical source: the horns are derived from the phrase *el karnot hamizbeach*; the ramp from *el penei hamizbeach*, since the ramp is, as Rashi explains, *panav shel mizbeach* — the face of the altar, its entrance and ascent; the foundation from *el yesod hamizbeach*; and the squareness from the explicit formulation *ravu'a yihiyeh hamizbeach*. Rabbi Yosi bar Yehuda's inclusion of the *sovev* rests on his identification of *karkov hamizbeach* with the *sovev*, and the appearance of the definite article in connection with it.

### Tosafot Materials

The question remains: what exactly renders a chip on the horn disqualifying? If one still has a horn, merely chipped, why is that not sufficient? This becomes a question about the threshold at which a component ceases to exist in the halachically required sense.

There is an additional complexity. If the altar were constructed of stone, a separate principle would apply: *avanim shleimot* — the stones must be whole — and *lo tanif aleihem barzel* — iron may not be used upon them. This creates an apparent paradox: you cannot shape the stones with iron tools, yet the stones must fit together precisely. One interpretation of *shleimot* therefore shifts from "complete" in the sense of unbroken to "smooth and unchipped" in the sense of unblemished. This is the background for the tradition of the *shamir*, the creature whose secretions could cut stone without iron, or alternatively, as Tosafot suggests, one might break stones without metal — though such breakage would itself compromise *shleimut*.

In practice, however, as Tosafot clarifies, the *mizbeach* in the Temple was not built from hewn field stones in this manner. The sheer practical impossibility of achieving precise dimensions with the constraints described makes plain that the altar was constructed using plaster — essentially a form of early concrete, with small pebbles and filler material poured into molds, which allowed for precision of shape far more tractably. Given this construction method, a chip in itself is not automatically problematic in the way that a damaged stone would be. The disqualifying question is rather one of degree: at what point does damage to the horn render it no longer a horn in the halachically cognizable sense? Or how much loss of right-angle shape compromises *ribua*? The gemara does not spell this out precisely, but it was evidently the case that the damage inflicted by the *esrogim* on that day crossed that threshold, such that the *avodah* could not be resumed until a proper repair was effected.

## Shitin Cavities

### Yochanan's View

The gemara now transitions to a famous passage concerning the *shitin* — the cavities into which the wine libation descended after being poured into the funnel atop the altar. There are two fundamental positions about what the *shitin* actually were and where the wine ultimately went.

One opinion holds that the wine descended through the interior of the altar via a kind of internal pipe or channel and collected in a cavity within the altar's base. The other holds that it passed through the altar entirely, broke through the tiled floor of the Temple court, and descended into natural underground caverns reaching to the very depths of the earth. Both positions agree on the name *shitin* for the destination; they differ on whether that destination was inside the altar or beneath it.

Rabbi Yochanan expresses himself on this matter: *Shitin misheshet yemei bereishit nivreu* — the *shitin* are natural underground caverns beneath the Temple Mount, and they were fashioned during the six days of Creation. His support comes from a verse in Shir HaShirim: *chamukei yereichayich kemo chala'im maaseh yedei omman* — "the hidden roundness of your thighs is like *chala'im*, the handiwork of a craftsman." The gemara reads *chamukei* as deriving from a root meaning hidden or concealed, and *yereichayich* as referring to the sides (*yerech*) of the altar — a usage well-attested in the Torah itself, which describes the northern side of the altar as *al yerech hamizbeach tzafona*. Thus *chamukei yereichayich elu hashitin* — the hidden parts of the altar's sides are the *shitin*. And *kemo chala'im shemechulalin veyoredim ad hatehom* — they are like hollow cavities that descend all the way to the depths. *Maaseh yedei omman zeh maaseh yedei umnuto shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — the "handiwork of a craftsman" in the verse refers to the handiwork of the Holy One Himself; these were created by God as natural formations.

### Chamukei Yereichayich

The gemara continues with a *derashah* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *al tikrei Bereishit ella bara shit* — do not read the first word of the Torah as merely "In the beginning," but rather as "He created the *shit*" — that is, God created the *shitin* in the very act of creation. This reading reinforces Rabbi Yochanan's naturalistic account: the underground caverns were not constructed by human hands but were part of the primordial structure of the world, placed there in anticipation of their eventual role in the Temple service.

A *baraita* corroborates this view: *Rabbi Yoshiya omer shitin mechulalin veyoredim ad hatehom* — Rabbi Yoshiya teaches that the *shitin* are hollowed out and descend to the depths of the earth.

### Isaiah Vineyard

Rabbi Yoshiya then cites a striking midrashic reading of the opening verses of Isaiah 5 — the parable of the beloved's vineyard — as support. The passage reads: *ashira na lididi shirat dodi lecharmo* — let me sing to my beloved a song of his vineyard. *Kerem hayah lididi bekeren ben shamen* — a vineyard belonged to my beloved on a richly fertile hill. *Vayazkehu vayeskalehu* — he dug it out and cleared it of stones. *Vayitaehu sorek* — he planted it with choice vines. *Vayiven migdal betocho* — he built a tower in its midst. *Vegam yekev chatzav bo* — and he even dug a wine vat within it. The verse then laments how the vineyard, despite all this care, yielded only sour grapes — the prophetic metaphor for Israel's betrayal of God. But the opening description of investment and cultivation yields a layered midrashic reading.

The gemara — according to Rashi's version — reads the verse as follows: *vayitaehu sorek ze Beit HaMikdash* — "he planted the choice vine" refers to the Temple itself; *vayiven migdal betocho ze Mizbeach* — "he built a tower in its midst" refers to the altar, a tall, imposing structure; *vegam yekev chatzav bo elu hashitin* — "he dug a wine vat within it" refers to the *shitin*, since a vat is precisely the receptacle into which wine flows.

### Cavern vs. Cavity

Tosafot's version of the text reads somewhat differently: *vayiven migdal betocho ze Heichal* — "he built a tower" refers to the *Heichal*, the great hall of the Temple, which was indeed the towering structure; *vegam yekev chatzav bo ze Mizbeach* — "he dug a wine vat" refers to the altar itself, since the altar is where the wine is first poured before descending, making it the vat. And the *shitin* are derived not from *yekev* directly but from the seemingly superfluous word *vegam* — "and also" — indicating an extension beyond the altar proper into what lies beneath it. This version is consistent with what Tosafot says is the reading of the *Tosefta*.

This textual divergence between Rashi and Tosafot reflects and maps onto the underlying dispute about the nature of the *shitin*. According to Rashi, the straightforward reading identifies the wine vat with the *shitin* — the underground cavern is where the wine ends up. According to the Tosafot version, the vat is the altar itself, and the *shitin* are a further extension beneath it, derived from the word *vegam*. Crucially, those who read the Isaiah passage in the Tosafot fashion understand the construction of the *Heichal* and *Mizbeach* as referring to human building activity — and therefore, even though they agree that there are underground caverns, they do not hold that these caverns were naturally created from the six days of Creation. Rather, they were excavated as part of the construction of the *Beit HaMikdash*. This stands in tension with Rabbi Yochanan's position, which attributes the *shitin* to divine creation at the dawn of time.

## Cleaning Shitin

### Trapdoor Location

The gemara introduces a *baraita* from Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok: *lul katan haya bein kevesh leMizbeach bamaaravo shel kevesh* — there was a small trapdoor between the ramp and the altar, on the western side of the ramp. The location of this trapdoor on the western side makes sense given the geography of the libations: the wine was poured at the northwestern corner of the altar, and whatever collected in the cavity would be accessible from the west.

### 70-Year Cleaning

*Ve'achat leshiv'im shana pirchei kehuna yordin lesham* — once every seventy years, the young *kohanim* — *pirchei kehuna*, literally those whose facial hair was just beginning to appear, small enough to fit through the trapdoor — would descend through this opening to clean out the accumulated wine. *Umelaketin misham yayin karush shedomeh leigulei deveila* — they would collect from within the cavity congealed wine that had solidified into forms resembling pressed fig cakes. *Uba'in vesorfin oto bikdusha* — and they would bring this material out and burn it in holiness, as befitting a sacred substance. The gemara derives this requirement to burn it in holiness through a *gezeirah shavah* connecting the word *kodesh* in the verse *bakodesh hasech nesech sheichar* — "in the holy place perform the libation, a libation of intoxicating drink to God" — with the burning of *nosar*, leftovers of offerings, where the Torah says *lo yei'acheil ki kodesh hu* — "it shall not be eaten for it is holy." Just as *nosar* must be burned in its sanctity, so must this congealed wine be disposed of in a manner consistent with its holy status.

### Ramp-Altar Gap

This picture of the trapdoor located between the ramp and the altar prompts reflection on the spatial relationship between those two structures. The ramp did not actually touch the top of the altar; there was an intentional gap between them. This gap existed because of the halachic requirement for *zrikah* — throwing. As Rashi notes by citing the verse *veasita olotecha habasar vehadam*, and drawing the inference *mah dam b'zrikah af basar b'zrikah* — just as the blood is cast upon the altar with a throwing motion from below, so too the placement of the limbs (*evarim*) upon the altar must involve a throwing action. A *kohen* standing at the top of the ramp would therefore throw the limbs across the gap onto the altar surface. This gap, while halachically necessary, would have made the top of the ramp a place requiring considerable care.

## Me'ilah Status

### Mishnah Me'ilah

The gemara now addresses the question of *me'ilah* — the prohibition against deriving unauthorized benefit from consecrated objects — in relation to the libation wine. The *mishnah* teaches: *nisachim b'tchilah moalin bahem* — with wines designated for libation, *me'ilah* applies initially; *yardu l'shittin ayn moalin bahem* — once they have descended into the *shitin*, *me'ilah* no longer applies.

### Sage Positions

This raises the question of which rabbinic opinion this *mishnah* aligns with. The gemara first suggests: *laima Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Tzadok* — perhaps this *mishnah* follows the position of Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok, who holds that the wine collected in an internal cavity of the altar rather than descending to underground depths. For according to the rabbis, who hold that the wine descended all the way to the abyss, how would there ever be a practical circumstance in which someone could misuse the wine once it entered the *shitin*? If it has gone to the depths of the earth, it is entirely inaccessible, and the *mishnah*'s statement that *me'ilah* no longer applies would be obvious and unremarkable.

The gemara responds: even according to the rabbis, the *mishnah* can be understood — *bediklava* — in the case where someone caught the wine as it was passing through the altar's interior cavity on its way down to the underground cavern. Such a person might have a reason to think *me'ilah* still applies since the mitzvah has not yet fully been discharged by the wine's arrival at its final destination.

The gemara then proposes the inverse: *ve'ika de'amri* — others say the reasoning should be reversed. Perhaps it is specifically according to the rabbis — who hold the wine goes underground — that this *mishnah* makes sense, while it does not easily fit with Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok. For according to Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok, once the wine has collected in the internal cavity, its holiness remains intact — *kedushah bikdushatei kai* — and one might think *me'ilah* still applies, since after all the wine must still be burned in holiness.

### Pappa Resolution

The gemara resolves this: *afilu teima Rabbi Elazar* — even according to Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok, *ein lecha davar shena'aseit mitzvatah umoalin bah* — there is no object in all of halacha that, once its designated mitzvah has been performed with it, remains subject to *me'ilah*. The fact that the wine retains some residual sanctity — enough to require burning — does not mean it remains subject to *me'ilah*. These are distinct categories. Once the mitzvah of *nisuach* has been accomplished by pouring the wine into the funnel, the wine has fulfilled its halachic role, and *me'ilah* no longer applies to it, regardless of what happens subsequently.

## Stopping Drains

### Reish Lakish

The gemara introduces a further teaching about the mechanics of the libation: *amar Reish Lakish* — Reish Lakish taught that *bizman shemenaschin yayin al gabei hamizbeach, pokekin et hashitin* — when they would pour the wine libation atop the altar, they would first stop up the *shitin*. According to Rashi, they would plug the openings near the top of the cavity. The wine would then not immediately run down into the depths. This practice is grounded in the verse: *bakodesh hasech nesech sheichar LaHashem* — "in the holy place pour a libation, a libation of *sheichar* to God."

### Pappa Sheichar

The connection between stopping the drains and this verse requires explanation. Rav Pappa provides it: *sheichar lashon shetiah* — the word *sheichar* connotes drinking; *lashon seviah* — it denotes a drinking that satisfies; *lashon shichrut* — it suggests the kind of drinking that actually produces intoxication. What does this have to do with plugging a drain?

The gemara proceeds to draw a physiological analogy: *ki sava inish chamra migronei sava* — when a person truly feels satisfied and full from wine, that satisfaction comes from the throat, not merely from the stomach. Wine that rushes straight down without pausing in the mouth and throat does not produce the sensation of *seviah*, of true satiation. Holding wine in the mouth before swallowing — allowing it to fill the mouth and linger in the throat — produces the experience of genuinely satisfied drinking, the kind that has intoxicating effect.

Applied to the libation: if the wine were poured in and immediately ran straight down through the *shitin* without any pause, it would be like swallowing a drink without savoring it. By stopping up the *shitin*, one allows the wine to fill the cavity at the top — the "mouth" of the altar, as it were — before releasing. This constitutes a *sheichar* — a truly satisfied drinking, a libation that is complete in the sense that the wine has fully occupied its designated space before descending.

### Rava's Teaching

Rava applies this physiological insight to a practical teaching: *tzurba miRabanan dela nafish lei chamra* — a Torah student who cannot afford much wine — perhaps he has access to some on *Shabbos* but it is limited — *ligma gimei* — he should take large swallows and hold them before swallowing. This is more effective at producing satisfaction than sipping small amounts repeatedly. Taking big gulps and holding the wine in the throat before swallowing will derive the full benefit from a small quantity of wine.

### Blessing Cup

In connection with this, the gemara notes that Rava himself, with the *kassa de'birchasa* — the cup over which he recited *Kiddush* or *Birkat HaMazon* — would similarly *agmah gimuaye*, take a substantial gulp and hold it before swallowing. For Rava, this was an expression of the honor due the *kos shel berachah*: just as the altar paused to receive the wine fully, Rava paused to honor the cup of blessing.

## Mah Yafu Footsteps

### Rava Ascent

The gemara records a *derashah* of Rava on the verse *mah yafu pe'amayich bane'alim bat nadiv* — "how beautiful are your footsteps in sandals, O daughter of the generous one." Rava interprets: *mah yafu pe'amoteihem shel Yisrael besha'ah she'olim laregel* — how beautiful are the footsteps of the Jewish people when they go up to Jerusalem for the *regel*, the pilgrimage festival. The word *pe'amayich*, footsteps, evokes the *aliyah laregel*, the ascending pilgrimage; and *ne'alim*, sandals, carries also the connotation of *oleh*, ascending.

### Abraham Convert

*Bat nadiv* — daughter of the generous one — is interpreted as *bito shel Avraham Avinu* — the daughter of our father Abraham, *shenikra nadiv* — who was called the generous one. The proof text is *nedivei amim ne'esafu am Elohei Avraham* — "the generous ones of the nations have gathered, the people of Abraham's God." This verse speaks of the people as belonging to the God of Abraham specifically — *velo Elohei Yitzchak veYaakov?* Why Abraham and not also Isaac and Jacob? The gemara answers: *ella Elohei Avraham shehayah techilah lagerim* — because Abraham was the first of the converts, the first to come near to God of his own volition.

Rashi notes that this is the point of the word *nadiv* applied to Abraham. *Nadiv* suggests not merely generosity with wealth but an internal motivating force — the kind of heart-driven desire to give and to act, *kol nediv lev*. Abraham's heart drove him toward God, toward knowledge of God, and this is why he is the archetypal *ger*, the father of all future converts whose hearts similarly move them to draw near. When Israel goes up to Jerusalem on *aliyah laregel*, they are enacting this same impulse — leaving the familiar, moving toward God — and in that sense they are *bat nadiv*, children of Abraham's quality of heart-driven approach. The *lech lecha* — the command to leave one's land and birthplace — resonates each time a person makes that personal journey toward *kedushah*.

## Torah Modesty

### Torah Hidden

The gemara returns to the phrase *chamukei yereichayich* — which it had earlier interpreted in relation to the *shitin* of the altar — and now applies it in a completely different direction: as a metaphor for Torah study. *Tanna devei Rav Anani* asks: *lamah nimshalu divrei Torah k'yerech?* — Why is Torah compared to a thigh? *Lomar lecha mah yerech beseter, af divrei Torah beseter* — to teach you that just as the thigh is kept covered and private, so too words of Torah belong in a private, modest setting. Torah study is not meant to be ostentatious. You do not perform it at a mountaintop to be seen; it has an intimacy to it — a *rebbe* and students, earnest engagement rather than display.

Rashi explains that the connection between Torah and *chala'im* — the other word in the verse, which in its plain sense means jewelry or precious stones — reflects the tradition that the Tablets of the Law were fashioned from precious stone.

### Humility Teaching

*Vehaynu de'amar Rabbi Elazar* — and this is what Rabbi Elazar teaches when he asks: *mai d'ichtiv higid lecha adam mah tov, umah Hashem doresh mimcha* — "He has told you, O man, what is good; what does the Lord require of you?" *Ki im asot mishpat ve'ahavat chesed vehatzne'a lechet im Elohecha* — "but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God."

The gemara unpacks each phrase: *asot mishpat zeh hadin* — doing justice means building a society based on law. *Ve'ahavat chesed zu gemilut chasadim* — loving kindness means performing acts of loving-kindness for one another. *Vehatzne'a lechet im Elohecha* — walking humbly with your God: *zu hotza'at hamet vehachnasat kallah lachuppah* — this refers to accompanying the dead to burial and bringing a bride to the *chuppah*.

### Hidden Mitzvot

Why are these two particular *mitzvot* associated with *hatzne'a lechet*, walking humbly? Rashi points to the verse *tov lalechet lebeit ha'avel milalechet el beit mishteh* — "it is better to go to a house of mourning than to a house of feasting" — where the word *lechet*, going, appears. The *beit mishteh* evokes the setting of a wedding celebration. These two acts — accompanying the dead and rejoicing with a bride — are paradigmatically public occasions; the proper honor of the deceased calls for a public *levayah*, and the joy of *chatan vakallah* is heightened by communal participation.

Yet the Torah says: *hatzne'a lechet* — do them with modesty. This is striking. The *kal vachomer* the gemara draws is: *kal vachomer* — if the Torah requires modesty in acts that are by their nature public, how much more so must modesty govern acts that are by nature private? And this naturally connects back to Torah study, which is inherently more intimate, making modesty in that domain all the more expected.

### Modest Display

What does modesty mean in the context of a wedding or a funeral, which are inherently public? Rashi offers two explanations, both of powerful practical relevance.

The first: do not allow the externals to overshadow the essence. A wedding must not become an exercise in conspicuous consumption where the sheer expense and spectacle distract from the genuine *simchah* of *chatan vakallah*. The *hatzne'a lechet* is a call to keep one's focus on what is actually meaningful — the couple, the celebration, the sacred moment — rather than on the apparatus of display. And this applies not only to the organizers but to every guest: how does one comport oneself at a wedding? Does one participate in the *simchah* or primarily attend to the food and social spectacle?

The second explanation Rashi gives is equally demanding. When one contributes financially to make a wedding or a funeral happen — and *hachnasat kallah* includes the communal collection of *tzedakah* for those who cannot afford their own weddings — the fact that many people will inevitably know how much things cost, who paid what, how the bills were settled, does not license one to broadcast one's generosity. *Hatzne'a lechet* requires that even when the act of giving is visible by necessity, one refrain from calling attention to it, from saying, "You know how much that wedding cost? I was the one who made it happen." That restraint in speech, that refusal to capitalize on the visibility of one's contribution, is itself the *tzniut* demanded by this verse.

## Tzedakah Hierarchy

### Elazar Hierarchy

The gemara presents a hierarchy of virtues articulated by Rabbi Elazar. First: *gadol ha'oseh tzedakah yoter mikol hakorbanot* — one who performs *tzedakah* is greater than one who offers all the sacrifices. This is a striking statement given how central the *korbanot* are to Torah life and how keenly their absence is felt. The source is the verse *asoh tzedakah umishpat nivchar laHashem mizevach* — "doing charity and justice is more desired by God than sacrifice."

Rabbi Elazar then adds: *gedolah gemilut chasadim yoter min hatzedakah* — *gemilut chasadim* is greater even than *tzedakah*. The proof text is *ziru lachem litzedakah vekitzru lefi chesed* — "plant for yourselves in *tzedakah* and harvest according to *chesed*." Planting involves uncertainty: *im adam zorea safek ochel safek eino ochel* — when you plant, you are not yet guaranteed a yield. But *adam kotzer vaday ochel* — when you harvest, you eat with certainty. *Chesed* is the harvest — the more assured and complete form of the good.

### Reciprocal Kindness

It is important not to reduce *gemilut chasadim* to charity performed toward the unfortunate. The word *gomel* itself, as in *gomel chesed*, ordinarily means to repay or reciprocate. There is an inherent reciprocity in *gemilut chasadim* that distinguishes it from top-down giving. Going to a friend's wedding is *gemilut chasadim*. Going to a funeral is *gemilut chasadim*. Being there for someone in a moment of need is *gemilut chasadim*. These are acts that flow between equals, between community members who are there for one another across all of life's occasions — not merely from those who have to those who lack.

This reciprocal quality is captured by the image of the community *gemach* — a loan society in which members share resources: a ladder, a lawn mower, any item one household has and another temporarily needs. The insight is that *gemilut chasadim* is not the province of the wealthy helping the poor; it is the fabric of mutual care that holds any community together, and in which anyone, regardless of means, can be a full participant as both giver and recipient.

### Tzedakah Reward

Rabbi Elazar adds a third teaching: *ein tzedakah mishtalemet ela lefi chesed she-bah* — *tzedakah* is rewarded only in proportion to the *chesed* that is within it. The same verse is cited: *ziru lachem litzedakah vekitzru lefi chesed* — you plant in *tzedakah*, but you harvest according to *chesed*. This is not merely a ranking of two separate virtues; it is an interweaving of them. Even when you are giving *tzedakah*, how you give it determines its ultimate value.

### Rashi Effort

Rashi's comment here is particularly illuminating. He explains: *gemilut chasadim she-bah, ha-netinah hi ha-tzedakah, veha-torach hu ha-chesed* — the monetary giving is the *tzedakah*; the effort you invest is the *chesed*. What kind of effort? *Kigun molicho le-beito* — walking the recipient to his home, accompanying him. Or *o torach she-taaleh lo le-harbeh* — working to ensure the money actually increases for him, helping him invest or use it wisely. Or *kigun noten lo pat afuyah o beged lilvosh o maot be-et tzarah metzuyah* — giving him bread already baked, or a garment to wear, or money at the moment when it can be obtained cheaply. *Shelo yotzi ma'ot le'ibud shenoten libo vedaato letovato shel ani* — ensuring the money is not wasted, because you genuinely think through what will serve this person's actual good. That attentiveness — that investment of thought and effort — is the *chesed* within the *tzedakah*.

### Three Distinctions

The *baraita* then formalizes the superiority of *gemilut chasadim* over *tzedakah* along three axes. *Tzedakah bimmono* — *tzedakah* is performed with one's money alone. *Gemilut chasadim bein begufo bein bimmono* — *gemilut chasadim* is performed with one's person as well as one's money; one can give of one's time, one's physical presence, one's personal assistance — or one can provide a loan rather than a gift. *Tzedakah la'aniyim* — *tzedakah* is directed only toward the poor. *Gemilut chasadim bein la'aniyim bein la'ashirim* — *gemilut chasadim* extends to the wealthy as well, for everyone stands in need of someone else at some point. And *tzedakah lachayim* — *tzedakah* is for the living. *Gemilut chasadim bein lachayim bein lametim* — *gemilut chasadim* extends even to the dead, as when one participates in a *levayah* and honors the deceased. This is the *chesed shel emet*, the truest act of kindness, for there is no possibility of the recipient repaying it. And yet, understood within the framework of a community, it is precisely this act that embodies the deepest reciprocity — honoring what this person meant, being present for a family in grief, participating in the continuity of human solidarity.

## Justice & Kindness

### Tzedakah Mishpat

Rabbi Elazar expands the teaching further: *kol ha'oseh tzedakah umishpat ke'ilu milei kol ha'olam kulo chesed* — anyone who performs *tzedakah* and *mishpat* is as though he has filled the entire world with *chesed*. The verse is *ohev tzedakah umishpat chesed Hashem mala'ah ha'aretz* — "He who loves *tzedakah* and *mishpat*, the *chesed* of God fills the earth." This formulation links individual human conduct to cosmic *chesed*.

The insight here is that a society genuinely built on justice — where law is applied equally, where systemic conditions allow for the flourishing of all members, and where those who fall through the cracks are caught by *tzedakah* — constitutes a form of *chesed* for the world. It does not achieve the full quality of personal *chesed*, but *ke'ilu* — as if — one has filled the world with *chesed*. The *chesed* of God, which pervades creation, finds its human analog in the construction of a just and caring society.

### Rare Kindness

The gemara immediately adds a qualification. *Shema tomar kol haba likpotz kofetz* — lest you say that anyone who wishes can simply commit to acts of *tzedakah* and *chesed* and succeed: *talmud lomar mah yakar chasdecha Elokim* — "how rare, how precious is Your kindness, O God." Rashi interprets this as a caution: good intentions do not automatically produce effective results. To genuinely do *tzedakah* and *mishpat* and *chesed* in ways that actually help, rather than harm or miss the mark, requires forethought, discernment, and genuine engagement. The verse acknowledges that even truly valuable *chesed* is precious partly because it is not easy to achieve.

### God's Grace

Yet the difficulty is not insurmountable for those who approach it with the right orientation: *yachol af yere shamayim ken* — perhaps even a God-fearing person faces this difficulty? *Talmud lomar vechesed Hashem meolam vead olam al yerayav* — "and the *chesed* of God is from eternity to eternity upon those who fear Him." The opportunities for true *chesed*, and the *siyata dishmaya* to perform it well, are more accessible to the genuinely *yere shamayim*, perhaps because a hidden agenda — even one of which the person is themselves unaware — can quietly distort even well-meaning acts of generosity. The *yere shamayim* is more likely to be free of such distortions.

Rabbi Chama bar Pappa adds: *kol adam sheyesh lo chen beyadu'a shehu yere shamayim* — anyone who possesses *chen* — that quality of grace and favor in others' eyes — one can know that person is God-fearing. The source is again the verse connecting *chesed* with those who fear God. The inference is that *chesed* generates *chen*, and therefore *chen* is a marker of genuine *yirat shamayim*.

## Torah Lishmah

### Woman Wisdom

The gemara concludes with a verse from Proverbs: *piha patcha be'chochma vetorat chesed al leshona* — "she opens her mouth with wisdom, and the Torah of kindness is on her tongue." Rabbi Elazar asks: *veki yesh Torah shel chesed veyesh Torah she'eina shel chesed?* — is there then a Torah of kindness and a Torah that is not of kindness? The question is serious: all Torah is presumably one — what distinguishes these two?

### Lishmah View

The first answer: *ella Torah lishmah zu hi Torah shel chesed* — Torah studied for its own sake is Torah of *chesed*. When a person learns Torah purely because it is the word of God, because Torah itself is the beloved object and the relationship with the Divine is the purpose, there is no intervening agenda. This is *chesed* toward God, as it were — a relationship of pure devotion. *Veshelo lishmah, zu hi Torah she'eina shel chesed* — Torah studied with other agendas — prestige, livelihood, status — lacks this quality of *chesed*.

### Teaching View

The second opinion says: *Torah lelamda zu hi Torah shel chesed* — Torah studied with the intent to teach it to others is Torah of *chesed*, because the learning opens outward, it is given over to others, it multiplies. *Shelo lelamda* — Torah studied purely for oneself, to accumulate knowledge and standing as a *talmid chacham* without any orientation toward transmitting it, *zu hi Torah she'eina shel chesed* — this is Torah that is not of *chesed*, because it remains entirely self-directed and does not flow outward to others.
