# Zevachim 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shalmei Tzibur Location

### North from Shavuot

The daf opens with the Gemara's question: *minalan d'ba'ei tzafon* — what is the source for the requirement that *shalmei tzibur* be slaughtered in the north? The Gemara introduces a *baraita* taught by Rabba bar Rav Chanan before Rava. The verse in the Shavuot passage reads: *v'asitem se'ir izim echad l'chatas u'shnei kevasim b'nei shanah l'zevach shelamim* — bring one goat as a *chatas* and two lambs as *shelamim*. Since the *chatas* and the *shalmei tzibur* are mentioned together in the same verse, one might draw an analogy: just as a *chatas* requires the north, so too *shalmei tzibur* requires the north.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava immediately objects: but where did the *chatas* itself learn that it requires the north? From the *olah*. And here is a principle the Gemara has established in prior dapim: *davar halamad b'hekesh eino chozer u'melamed b'hekesh* — something derived through a *hekesh* cannot itself serve as a source for a further *hekesh*. Once the *chatas* learned its law from the *olah* through *hekesh*, you cannot use that *chatas* as the basis for yet another *hekesh* to the *shalmei tzibur*. This objection — sometimes called *pirku* — invalidates the initial derivation entirely.

### Rav Mari Source

The Gemara therefore turns to an alternative source, one taught by Rav Mari son of Rav Kahana. The verse states: *al oloteichem v'al zivchei shalmeichem* — upon your *olot* and your *zivchei shalmim*. This verse, as Rashi explains, is understood by *Chazal* to refer to the communal *korbanot*, specifically the *zivchei shalmei tzibur* brought on Shavuot, based on the context of blowing the *chatzotzrot*, a practice reserved for *korbanot tzibur* rather than *korbanot yachid*. The verse thus links the *olah* directly to the *zivchei shalmei tzibur*. From this *hekesh* we derive two things: just as an *olah* is *kodshei kodashim*, so too *zivchei shalmei tzibur* are *kodshei kodashim*; and just as an *olah* requires the north, so too *zivchei shalmei tzibur* require the north. Crucially, this is a direct *hekesh* from the *olah* itself — no double linkage problem.

### Male Kohanim Rule

But now the Gemara asks: if the *hekesh* to the *olah* already supplies everything we need, what was the purpose of the original *hekesh* to the *chatas* mentioned in the Shavuot passage? The Gemara answers: *k'chatas* — to teach us that *zivchei shalmei tzibur* may only be eaten by *zichrei kehunah*, male *kohanim*. A *chatas* is not eaten by anyone except male *kohanim*, and this quality is imported by the *hekesh* to the *chatas*. One cannot derive this from the *olah* because an *olah* is not eaten at all — there is nothing to teach about who may eat it. The two *drashot* thus complement each other: the *hekesh* to the *olah* establishes the north and *kodshei kodashim* status, while the *hekesh* to the *chatas* teaches the restriction to male *kohanim*.

## Ayil Nazir Debate

### Abaye Question

Abaye raises a challenge. The passage in the Torah concerning the *nazir* reads: *v'hikriv et korbano l'Hashem keves ben shnato tamim echad l'olah v'chavsa achas bat shnata temima l'chatas v'ayil echad tamim l'shlamim* — one lamb for an *olah*, one ewe for a *chatas*, and one ram as a *shelamim*. Since the *ayil nazir* — the ram brought as the *nazir*'s *shelamim* — is explicitly linked in the same verse to the *chatas*, should we not apply the same reasoning? Just as the *chatas* is eaten only by male *kohanim*, perhaps the *ayil nazir* too should be restricted to male *kohanim*. The *ayil nazir* is a private *shelamim*, so why should it take on the stringencies of *kodshei kodashim*?

### Owners Portion

The Gemara dismisses this concern decisively. The Torah states that the *kohen* receives *ha'zeroa beshela min ha'ayil* — the cooked foreleg of the ram. The very fact that the Torah explicitly designates one specific portion for the *kohen* carries an implicit corollary: *dechulei be'alim achlei lei* — the rest is eaten by the owners. This is the hallmark of *kodshim kalim*, not *kodshei kodashim*. Since the verse itself presupposes that the owners eat the bulk of the meat, it is impossible to say that the *ayil nazir* is limited to male *kohanim*.

The Gemara acknowledges a more refined version of the question: perhaps at least the *kohen*'s designated portion — the cooked foreleg — should be treated like *kodshei kodashim* and limited to male *kohanim*? The Gemara concedes this is *kashia* — difficult to resolve cleanly. But practically speaking, no such hybrid category exists anywhere in *halacha*: a *korban* is either entirely *kodshei kodashim* — eaten exclusively by male *kohanim* on the Temple grounds — or it is *kodshim kalim*, in which case even the *kohen*'s portion is eaten by the *kohen*'s entire household, wives, children, and slaves included. The absence of any such intermediate category makes the practical force of Abaye's question moot.

### Ayil Nazir Status

The Gemara adds a further reason why the *ayil nazir* is definitively not in the category of *kodshei kodashim*: it is never called *kodshei kodashim* in the Torah. At most the relevant verse uses the word *kodesh*; *zivchei shalmei tzibur*, by contrast, uses the phrase *kodesh l'Hashem*. Rashi notes this distinction in the language of the respective *psukim*.

This being so, the Gemara asks: if the linkage of the *ayil nazir* to the *chatas* is not meant to import the male *kohanim* restriction, what does the *hekesh* come to teach? Rava — some texts read Ravina — answers: *she'im gilach al achad me'shloshtan yatza* — it teaches that if the *nazir* shaved his head in conjunction with any one of the three *korbanot* in his passage (the *olah*, the *chatas*, or the *shelamim*), he fulfills his obligation of ending his *nezirut*. This is the link's direction: not to import laws from the *chatas* onto the *shelamim*, but to establish that the *shelamim* of the *nazir* — around which the entire shaving ritual is structured, since he places his shorn hair beneath the pot cooking the *zeroa beshela* — is so central to *nezirut* that shaving over any of the three *korbanot* suffices. This is a striking *chiddush*: the completion of *nezirut* is not exclusively dependent on the *ayil nazir* itself.

## Todah & Ayil Nazir

### Intro: Todah

Having concluded the *kodshei kodashim*, the Mishnah now turns to *kodshim kalim*, beginning with two categories that are, in a sense, subspecies of *shelamim* but with distinctive features. The first is the *todah* — the thanksgiving offering brought in four circumstances enumerated by *Chazal*. It is a type of *shelamim* but is accompanied by forty loaves of bread: thirty of *matzah* (in three varieties) and ten of *chametz*. The *kohen* receives one loaf from each group of ten, giving him one of each of the four types of bread in addition to the normal *kohen*'s portions of the animal.

The second is the *ayil nazir*, which we have just been discussing — the ram brought by the *nazir*, a private *shelamim* with the added priestly portion of the cooked foreleg beyond the usual *chazeh* and *shok*.

### Ayil Nazir Rules

Both the *todah* and the *ayil nazir* are *kodshim kalim*, and the Mishnah states: *shechitatan bechol makom ba'azarah* — they may be slaughtered anywhere in the *azarah*, not specifically in the north. This distinguishes them from *kodshei kodashim*. The blood requires *shtei matanot she'hen arba* — two applications that fulfill the requirement of four. They are eaten *bechol ha'ir* — throughout the city of Jerusalem — by anyone, *l'chol adam*, prepared in any manner, *bechol ma'achal*. 

One significant difference from generic *shelamim* is the time limit: *leyom valaylah*, eaten only for the day of the offering and the following night. The Torah states this explicitly in the passage of the *todah*, and the *ayil nazir* is linked by *Chazal* to the *todah*'s timeframe. The *d'oraita* rule extends through the entire night, but *Chazal* decreed *ad chatzot* — until midnight — as a protective measure, *harchakat adam min ha'aveira*, to prevent people from falling asleep and inadvertently eating after the time limit.

### Terumah Portion

Regarding the *hamuram mehem* — the *terumah* portions given to the *kohen*, namely the *shok haterumah* and *chazeh hatenufah* — these follow the same *halachot* as the rest: eaten in Jerusalem, within the same time restriction. However, there is a critical difference. Whereas the body of the *todah* and *ayil nazir* is eaten *l'chol adam*, the *hamuram* — the portions lifted for the *kohen* — may be eaten *lakohanim lenasheihem velivneihem vela'avdeihem*: by the *kohanim* and their wives, their children, and their slaves. This is not the stringency of *kodshei kodashim* — it is not limited to male *kohanim* alone — but neither is it available to all of Israel. Its holiness is comparable to *terumah*, which the Torah itself permits to the *kohen*'s household including his daughters, as the verse explicitly states *ata u'vanecha u'vnotecha itcha*.

There is nonetheless a meaningful distinction from actual *terumah*: *terumah* is called *kodshei hagvul* and may be eaten anywhere within the boundaries of the Land of Israel. The *hamuram* here, while sharing *terumah*'s level of *kedusha* in terms of who may consume it, is geographically restricted to Jerusalem. It cannot be taken out of *Yerushalayim*. The Gemara also notes that the *bechol ma'achal* phrase — that it may be prepared in any manner — is retained in the Mishnah for purposes of comprehensive cataloguing, since the subsequent Mishnah concerning *Korban Pesach* will specify that the *Pesach* may only be eaten *tzali* (roasted), and the contrast requires that the other categories be explicitly stated.

The Gemara then cites the *baraita* of *Tanu Rabbanan* to establish from where we know that this *hamuram* is eaten outside the *azarah* yet only within Jerusalem. The verse in *Shemini* reads: *ve'es chazeh hatenufah ve'es shok haterumah tochlu bemakom tahor ata u'vanecha u'vnotecha itacha* — eat the breast of the wave-offering and the thigh of the *terumah* in a pure place, you and your sons and daughters with you.

Rabbi Nechemiah asks: does this imply that earlier offerings were eaten in *tumah*? Clearly not. Rather, *tahor* here is being contrasted with *kadosh*, not asserting an absolute standard of purity. The preceding verse uses the phrase *bemakom kadosh* for the *mincha* — a *kodshei kodashim* — and it adds the phrase *kodesh kodashim hi*. Here, by contrast, the verse uses only *tahor*, without calling it *kadesh kodashim*, and includes daughters alongside sons. The term *tahor miklal she'hu tamei* — pure but implying some degree of impurity — reflects the Gemara's answer that *Yerushalayim* is called *makom tahor* in a relative sense: it excludes *metzoraim*, who must leave all three camps, but it permits the presence of a *zav*, a *tamei meis*, and other *tumos*. Jerusalem is therefore *tahor* relative to the *metzora*'s exclusion, yet *tamei* relative to *zav* status — *tahor mitumas metzora vetamei mitumas zav*.

Abaye then reads the verses in a two-step derivation. The verse states *otah bemakom kadosh* — this one (*chatas*) in a holy place — implying by contrast that the *shelamim*'s *hamuram* need not be in a holy place, i.e., not in the *azarah* proper. That alone would tell us it could even be in *Machaneh Leviyah*, the area corresponding to *Har HaBayit*. The Torah then adds *bemakom tahor*, which takes it further out still, all the way to *Machaneh Yisrael* — that is, all of Jerusalem.

Rava offers an alternative reading: *otah bemakom kadosh* implies that without further instruction one would have thought the *shelamim* could be eaten anywhere at all — even outside Jerusalem — since the limiting word *otah* emphasizes only that the *chatas* is *bemakom kadosh*, not the others. Then *bemakom tahor* pulls it back in to *Machaneh Yisrael*. The Gemara challenges Rava: if *tahor* is pulling something back inward, why does it land specifically at *Machaneh Yisrael* and not one step closer at *Machaneh Leviyah*? The Gemara answers: *l'chada mailinan l'tartei lo mailinan* — we bring it in only one degree by one textual indicator. Rava's derivation is then further challenged: the initial *hava amina* that a *korban* could be eaten outside Jerusalem is untenable, since the verse *lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha* already teaches that *kodshim* cannot be eaten in ordinary cities. The Gemara concludes: *michavrta k'Abaye* — Abaye's reading is the cleaner derivation.

## Generic Shlamim

### Shlamim Features

The Mishnah now turns to the broadest category of *kodshim kalim*: *shelamim* in general. The Mishnah states: *shelamim kodshim kalim shechitatam bechol makom ba'azarah* — they may be slaughtered anywhere in the *azarah*, with the blood requiring *shtei matanot she'hen arba*. They are eaten *bechol ha'ir* — throughout Jerusalem — *l'chol adam bechol ma'achal* — by any person in any form of preparation. The time limit is *l'shnei yamim v'laila echad* — two days and the intervening night. Unlike the *todah* and *ayil nazir*, where *Chazal* restricted eating to midnight as a precaution, here the full two-day period applies and there is no need for such a protective decree.

### Shlamim Terumah

Similarly, *hamuram mehem kayotze bahem* — the *terumah* portions follow the same rules — except that *ha'hamuram ne'echal l'kohanim u'lnesheihem u'livneihem u'la'avdeihem*: the *chazeh* and *shok* designated for the *kohanim* are eaten in Jerusalem but only by the *kohen*'s household, not by all of Israel.

The Gemara now uses the *shelamim* Mishnah as an opportunity to examine the broader question of how we know that *shelamim* may be slaughtered anywhere in the *azarah*. The underlying answer is straightforward: the Torah never specifies north for *shelamim*. Instead, whenever the Torah discusses *shelamim* in the early chapters of *Vayikra*, it uses the phrase *petach Ohel Moed* — the opening of the Tent of Meeting — a broad and directionally neutral designation. Three such *psukim* employ this language, and together they will serve to validate *shechita* in all directions, as the next section explores.

---

## Petach Ohel Moed

### Tanna Kamma

A *baraita* is cited: the verses *v'shachatato petach Ohel Moed* and *v'shachato oto lifnei Ohel Moed* appear multiple times in the *shelamim* passages. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that three such *psukim* exist, and they serve three functions: one establishes the basic requirement that there be an operative *petach* — an open doorway; a second validates all directions, meaning any orientation within the *azarah*; and the third excludes *tzidei tzedadim* — side-of-side locations such as enclosed chambers or rooms off the *azarah*, which despite being physically proximate to *Ohel Moed* are not considered *lifnei Ohel Moed*. Given these three functions, the north requires no additional *pasuk* because there was never a reason to exclude it: the *Tanna Kamma* does not assume the north is reserved for *kodshei kodashim*, so a *kal vachomer* from *kodshei kodashim* straightforwardly validates the north for *kodshim kalim* as well.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees. In his view, one would have a *hava amina* that *kodshim kalim* cannot be slaughtered in the north. The reasoning is an inverted *kal vachomer*: *kodshim kalim* are permitted in all directions where *kodshei kodashim* are not permitted; therefore, perhaps where *kodshei kodashim* are permitted — the north — *kodshim kalim* are not. Rashi elaborates: there is no intrinsic *kedusha* to the north; it is an arbitrary divine designation. If that is so, perhaps the Torah intended the north to be an exclusive zone reserved for *kodshei kodashim*, in which case *kodshim kalim* encroach on that designated space. This is a cogent argument. Therefore, Rabbi Eliezer requires one of the three *psukim* specifically to validate the north for *kodshim kalim*. The second *pasuk* teaches that the sides are valid even though they are not directly opposite the door. The third *pasuk*, in Rabbi Eliezer's reading, excludes *tzidei tzedadim* as a matter of logical inference without requiring explicit textual exclusion — if someone is in a closed-off room, that is obviously not *lifnei Ohel Moed* and requires no verse to dismiss.

### Debate Resolution

Both *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer agree on the practical *halacha*: *kodshim kalim* may be slaughtered anywhere in the *azarah* including the north, and enclosed side-rooms do not qualify as *petach Ohel Moed*. Their dispute is purely methodological — a disagreement over which textual function each of the three *psukim* serves and whether the north requires an affirmative verse or a *kal vachomer*.

## Door Opening Rule

### Door Status Halacha

The word *petach* in *petach Ohel Moed* carries a further *halacha* regarding temporal validity. Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel rules: *shelamim sheshachatam kodem sheyiptechu daltot ha'heichal — psulin* — *shelamim* slaughtered before the doors of the *heichal* are opened in the morning are invalid. The same ruling is cited in the name of Mar Ukva bar Chama citing Rav Yossi bar Chanina, and again in the name of Rav Yaakov bar Acha citing Rav Ashi — three independent transmissions of the same principle. The verse *v'shachatato petach Ohel Moed* is read: *bizman she'petach Ohel Moed patuach* — only when the opening of the *Ohel Moed* is actually open, *v'lo bizman shehu naul* — not when it is locked.

This rule reflects a sensible intuition: before the doors of the King's palace are opened, one does not commence the sacred service in the courtyard. The Mishnayot elsewhere describe the process: a *kohen* would enter through a small passageway to unlock and swing open the great doors of the *Ulam* and then the *Heichal*, after which the day's service could begin.

The same principle applies to the *Mishkan*: if the *Leviim* had not yet erected the *Mishkan* — and thus no *petach* yet existed — or if they had already dismantled it, slaughter performed during those intervals would similarly be invalid. The *petach* must actually exist and must actually be open.

### Door vs Curtain

The Gemara now probes the definition of *patuach*. It is obvious, the Gemara states, that a door that is merely *mugaf* — pushed closed without being locked — is considered *kenaul dami*, equivalent to locked, since a *petach* requires the door to be genuinely open, not merely unlocked.

What about a *vilon* — a curtain hanging in the doorway? Rabbi Zeira rules: a curtain is equivalent to an open doorway. Rashi explains that a curtain is there for modesty, not to block entry; it does not constitute a physical barrier. This ruling is, in fact, what one would expect from first principles: in the *Mishkan* itself there were no doors — only curtains — and those curtains were always understood as constituting *petach Ohel Moed*. A closed door is invalid; a curtain is treated as equivalent to an open door.

### Obstructions

The Gemara raises the question *govah mai* — what if there is some physical obstruction in front of the doorway? *Tosafot* notes the irony here: the most obvious obstruction in the *azarah* was the *mizbeach* itself, and yet the *halacha* self-evidently permits *shechita* throughout the *azarah*. Perhaps the question is directed at an obstruction pressed directly up against the door itself — something that does not prevent the door from being open, but does block direct access or visibility. The question is left as a *teiku* of sorts, to be resolved by the subsequent architectural discussion.

## Back Door Access

### Beit Chalipot

The Gemara cites a *baraita* from Rabbi Yossi son of Rabbi Yehuda: *shenei pishpeshin hayu b'Veit HaChalifot* — there were two small doorways in the *Beit HaChalifot*. The *Beit HaChalifot* was the area along the sides of the *Ulam*, the great porch that in the *Beit HaMikdash* extended fifteen *amot* beyond the width of the *Heichal* on each side. In the walls of the *Ulam* were niches — *chalifot* meaning knife-holders — where the *kohanim* stored their knives. These small doorways were *shemoneh amot* high, *kedei lehachshir et ha'azarah kulah* — in order to make the entire *azarah* qualify as *petach Ohel Moed* — *l'shchitat kodshim kalim*, for the *shechita* of *kodshim kalim*.

The reasoning is as follows: for areas of the *azarah* that lie behind the *Ulam* — on the far side of the building relative to the main courtyard — one cannot directly face the door of the *Heichal*. However, since these small back doorways exist, and through them one has access into the *Ohel Moed*, the entire space behind the *Ulam* acquires the status of *petach Ohel Moed*. The Gemara notes parenthetically that the Mishnah's mention of eating *kodshei kodashim* in this context is imprecise — the requirement of *petach Ohel Moed* was never applied to eating *kodshei kodashim*, which must simply be within the *azarah*, a *makom kadosh*, without the specific *petach* condition. The *petach Ohel Moed* requirement pertains only to *shechita*.

The Gemara briefly considers whether the statement that these doorways were eight *amot* high might instead be read as saying there was an eight-*amot* mound in front of them — which would make the doorways relevant to the obstruction question raised earlier. The Gemara dismisses this reading: the plain meaning is simply that the doorways themselves were eight *amot* tall. One might note that eight *amot* is a striking height for what are described as small back doors, though the Gemara notes that ordinary *shearim* of the *Mikdash* were twenty *amot* high and ten *amot* wide — *pishpeshin* are categorically different.

### Corner Doors

The placement of these *pishpeshin* is deliberate. The Gemara notes that they were positioned at the corners — *keren zavis* — of the *Ulam*'s back wall: one at the corner between the southern wall and the western wall, one at the corner between the northern wall and the western wall. By positioning them at the corners rather than centered on the back wall, both doors partially face sideways as well as rearward. This design means that the space alongside the *Ulam* — which is neither directly in front of the main entrance of the *Heichal* nor squarely behind it — is nonetheless *lifnei* one of the two corner doors, and therefore counts as *petach Ohel Moed*.

### Back Window

The corner doors resolve the status of the area behind and alongside the *Ulam*, but a further question remains: what about the space directly behind the *Kodesh HaKodashim* itself — *achorei Beit HaKappores*? This area lies behind the *aron* and is accessible from neither the front doors nor the corner *pishpeshin*. 

The Gemara cites Ami bar Yud in the name of Rav: *lul katan hayah achorei Beit HaKappores* — there was a small opening, like a window, behind the *Beit HaKappores*, eight *amot* tall and eight *amot* wide. Its purpose, stated explicitly in the Gemara, was *l'hachshir et ha'azarah* — to render the entire *azarah* eligible for *kodshim kalim* slaughter by ensuring that every point in the *azarah* was in some sense *lifnei* an opening of the *Ohel Moed*. 

This is a striking *chidush*. The *Kodesh HaKodashim* was the most restricted space in the world — accessible only to the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur* — and yet there was apparently an opening of significant dimensions at its back wall. One might wonder whether this opening was visible from the *azarah* — whether one standing behind the building could see through into the inner sanctum. Perhaps some kind of curtain, *vilon*, served to maintain the sanctity and privacy of the space while preserving the legal status of an opening. This would harmonize with what was established earlier: a curtain qualifies as *petach patuach*.

### Guards & Dimensions

The Gemara connects this back opening to a cryptic verse: *v'hainu d'chtiv shenayim laparbar* — two were for the *parbar*. Rabbah bar Rav Shila explains *parbar* as *klapei bar* — meaning outward-facing. Two guards were stationed for this rear opening of the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, their function being to protect the sanctity of the space from the outside.

The daf closes with a transition to a new topic. Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel rules: *ein chayavin mishum tumah ela al orech me'ah ushmonim v'shiva v'rochav me'ah ushloshim v'chamesh* — one is liable for *tumah* only within a space 187 by 135 *amot*. This is the precise measurement of the *azarah* proper — exclusive of the *Ezrat Nashim*, which bears the status of *Machaneh Leviyah* rather than *Machaneh Shechinah*. It is in this bounded space — 187 by 135 *amot* — that all of the laws discussed throughout this daf apply: eating *kodshei kodashim*, *shechita* of *kodshim kalim*, and liability for *tumah* in the *azarah*. The elaboration of those dimensions will continue on the following daf.
