# Zevachim 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shalmei Tzibur Location

### North from Shavuot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא לַן דְּבָעֵי צָפוֹן? דְּתָנֵי רַבָּה בַּר רַב חָנָן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא: ״וַעֲשִׂיתֶם שְׂעִיר עִזִּים אֶחָד לְחַטָּאת״ – מָה חַטָּאת בַּצָּפוֹן, אַף שַׁלְמֵי צִבּוּר בַּצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **From where do we** derive **that** the communal peace offerings, i.e., the two sheep brought on *Shavuot*, **require** slaughter in the **north** of the Temple courtyard? The Gemara answers: **As Rabba bar Rav Ḥanan taught before Rava:** The passage in the Torah discussing the offerings of *Shavuot* states: **"And you shall offer one he-goat for a sin offering,** and two male sheep in their first year for a sacrifice of peace offerings" (Leviticus 23:19). The peace offerings are juxtaposed with the sin offering. **Just as** the **sin offering** must be slaughtered **in the north** of the Temple courtyard, **so too, communal peace offerings** must be slaughtered **in the north** of the Temple courtyard.

The daf opens with the Gemara's question: *minalan d'ba'ei tzafon* — what is the source for the requirement that *shalmei tzibur* be slaughtered in the north? The Gemara introduces a *baraita* taught by Rabba bar Rav Chanan before Rava. The verse in the Shavuot passage reads: *v'asitem se'ir izim echad l'chatas u'shnei kevasim b'nei shanah l'zevach shelamim* — bring one goat as a *chatas* and two lambs as *shelamim*. Since the *chatas* and the *shalmei tzibur* are mentioned together in the same verse, one might draw an analogy: just as a *chatas* requires the north, so too *shalmei tzibur* requires the north.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְכִי חַטָּאת מֵהֵיכָן לָמְדָה – מֵעוֹלָה; דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: But from where** is the requirement to slaughter **a sin offering** in the north of the Temple courtyard **derived?** It is derived **from** its juxtaposition with **a burnt offering,** as the verse states: "Speak to Aaron and to his sons, saying: This is the law of the sin offering: In the place where the burnt offering is slaughtered shall the sin offering be slaughtered before the Lord; it is most holy" (Leviticus 6:18). In the realm of consecrated matters, can **a matter derived via a juxtaposition then teach** its *halakha* **via a juxtaposition?** The Gemara (49b) demonstrates that it cannot.

Rava immediately objects: but where did the *chatas* itself learn that it requires the north? From the *olah*. And here is a principle the Gemara has established in prior dapim: *davar halamad b'hekesh eino chozer u'melamed b'hekesh* — something derived through a *hekesh* cannot itself serve as a source for a further *hekesh*. Once the *chatas* learned its law from the *olah* through *hekesh*, you cannot use that *chatas* as the basis for yet another *hekesh* to the *shalmei tzibur*. This objection — sometimes called *pirku* — invalidates the initial derivation entirely.

### Rav Mari Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִדְּתָנֵי רַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא: ״עַל עֹלֹתֵיכֶם וְעַל זִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵיכֶם״ – מָה עוֹלָה קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, אַף זִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵי צִבּוּר קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים; מָה עוֹלָה בַּצָּפוֹן, אַף זִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵי צִבּוּר בַּצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains: **Rather,** the source that communal peace offerings must be slaughtered in the north of the Temple courtyard is derived **from** that **which Rav Mari, son of Rav Kahana, teaches:** The verse states: "Also in the day of your gladness, and in your appointed seasons, and in your New Moons, you shall blow with the trumpets **over your burnt offerings, and over your peace offerings;** and they shall be to you for a memorial before your God: I am the Lord your God" (Numbers 10:10). This verse directly juxtaposes burnt offerings with communal peace offerings. **Just as burnt offerings** are **offerings of the most sacred order, so too, communal peace offerings** are **offerings of the most sacred order.** Furthermore, **just as a burnt offering** must be slaughtered **in the north** of the Temple courtyard, **so too, communal peace offerings** must be slaughtered **in the north** of the Temple courtyard.

The Gemara therefore turns to an alternative source, one taught by Rav Mari son of Rav Kahana. The verse states: *al oloteichem v'al zivchei shalmeichem* — upon your *olot* and your *zivchei shalmim*. This verse, as Rashi explains, is understood by *Chazal* to refer to the communal *korbanot*, specifically the *zivchei shalmei tzibur* brought on Shavuot, based on the context of blowing the *chatzotzrot*, a practice reserved for *korbanot tzibur* rather than *korbanot yachid*. The verse thus links the *olah* directly to the *zivchei shalmei tzibur*. From this *hekesh* we derive two things: just as an *olah* is *kodshei kodashim*, so too *zivchei shalmei tzibur* are *kodshei kodashim*; and just as an *olah* requires the north, so too *zivchei shalmei tzibur* require the north. Crucially, this is a direct *hekesh* from the *olah* itself — no double linkage problem.

### Male Kohanim Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הֶיקֵּשָׁא קַמָּא לְמַאי אֲתָא? כִּי חַטָּאת – מָה חַטָּאת אֵינָהּ נֶאֱכֶלֶת אֶלָּא לְזִכְרֵי כְהוּנָּה, אַף זִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵי צִבּוּר לְזִכְרֵי כְהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, what** *halakha* does **the first juxtaposition** between communal peace offerings and the he-goat sin offering **come to** teach? The Gemara answers: It teaches that the *halakhot* of communal peace offerings are **like** the *halakhot* of **a sin offering. Just as a sin offering is eaten only by male priests, so too, communal peace offerings** are eaten only **by male priests.**

But now the Gemara asks: if the *hekesh* to the *olah* already supplies everything we need, what was the purpose of the original *hekesh* to the *chatas* mentioned in the Shavuot passage? The Gemara answers: *k'chatas* — to teach us that *zivchei shalmei tzibur* may only be eaten by *zichrei kehunah*, male *kohanim*. A *chatas* is not eaten by anyone except male *kohanim*, and this quality is imported by the *hekesh* to the *chatas*. One cannot derive this from the *olah* because an *olah* is not eaten at all — there is nothing to teach about who may eat it. The two *drashot* thus complement each other: the *hekesh* to the *olah* establishes the north and *kodshei kodashim* status, while the *hekesh* to the *chatas* teaches the restriction to male *kohanim*.

## Ayil Nazir Debate

### Abaye Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִי הָכִי, גַּבֵּי אֵיל נָזִיר דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִקְרִיב אֶת קׇרְבָּנוֹ לַה׳ כֶּבֶשׂ בֶּן שְׁנָתוֹ תָמִים אֶחָד לְעֹלָה, וְכַבְשָׂה אַחַת בַּת שְׁנָתָהּ [תְּמִימָה] לְחַטָּאת, וְאַיִל אֶחָד [תָּמִים] לִשְׁלָמִים״ – הָכִי נָמֵי נֵימָא דְּהִקִּישׁ הַכָּתוּב לְחַטָּאת: מָה חַטָּאת אֵינָהּ נֶאֱכֶלֶת אֶלָּא לְזִכְרֵי כְהוּנָּה, אַף אֵיל נָזִיר נָמֵי אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא לְזִכְרֵי כְהוּנָּה?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: **If so,** then **with regard to a nazirite's ram,** concerning **which it is written** in the verse: **"And he shall present his offering to the Lord, one he-lamb in its first year without blemish for a burnt offering, and one ewe-lamb in its first year without blemish for a sin offering, and one ram without blemish for a peace offering"** (Numbers 6:14), **so too shall we say that the Torah juxtaposed** the nazirite's ram **with a sin offering** of a nazirite to teach that **just as a sin offering is eaten only by male priests, so too, a nazirite's ram is eaten only by male priests?** As the next mishna states, a nazirite's ram may be eaten by every ritually pure Jew.

Abaye raises a challenge. The passage in the Torah concerning the *nazir* reads: *v'hikriv et korbano l'Hashem keves ben shnato tamim echad l'olah v'chavsa achas bat shnata temima l'chatas v'ayil echad tamim l'shlamim* — one lamb for an *olah*, one ewe for a *chatas*, and one ram as a *shelamim*. Since the *ayil nazir* — the ram brought as the *nazir*'s *shelamim* — is explicitly linked in the same verse to the *chatas*, should we not apply the same reasoning? Just as the *chatas* is eaten only by male *kohanim*, perhaps the *ayil nazir* too should be restricted to male *kohanim*. The *ayil nazir* is a private *shelamim*, so why should it take on the stringencies of *kodshei kodashim*?

### Owners Portion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דִּכְתִיב ״וְלָקַח הַכֹּהֵן אֶת הַזְּרֹעַ בְּשֵׁלָה מִן הָאַיִל״ – מִכְּלָל דְּכוּלֵּיהּ בְּעָלִים אָכְלִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava answers: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** with regard to the nazirite's ram, **since it is written: "And the priest shall take the foreleg of the ram when it is cooked"** (Numbers 6:19), **by inference** one can deduce **that the owner may eat all of** the remaining portions of the animal.

The Gemara dismisses this concern decisively. The Torah states that the *kohen* receives *ha'zeroa beshela min ha'ayil* — the cooked foreleg of the ram. The very fact that the Torah explicitly designates one specific portion for the *kohen* carries an implicit corollary: *dechulei be'alim achlei lei* — the rest is eaten by the owners. This is the hallmark of *kodshim kalim*, not *kodshei kodashim*. Since the verse itself presupposes that the owners eat the bulk of the meat, it is impossible to say that the *ayil nazir* is limited to male *kohanim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְרוֹעַ בְּשֵׁלָה מִיהָא לָא לִיתְאֲכִיל אֶלָּא לְזִכְרֵי כְהוּנָּה! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **in any event,** the **cooked foreleg** of the nazirite's ram **should be eaten only by male priests.** The mishna states that the cooked foreleg may be eaten by priests, their wives, and their slaves. The Gemara concedes: This is **difficult.** Based on its juxtaposition with the sin offering of a nazirite, the *halakha* should be that only the male priests may eat it, yet that is not the case.

The Gemara acknowledges a more refined version of the question: perhaps at least the *kohen*'s designated portion — the cooked foreleg — should be treated like *kodshei kodashim* and limited to male *kohanim*? The Gemara concedes this is *kashia* — difficult to resolve cleanly. But practically speaking, no such hybrid category exists anywhere in *halacha*: a *korban* is either entirely *kodshei kodashim* — eaten exclusively by male *kohanim* on the Temple grounds — or it is *kodshim kalim*, in which case even the *kohen*'s portion is eaten by the *kohen*'s entire household, wives, children, and slaves included. The absence of any such intermediate category makes the practical force of Abaye's question moot.

### Ayil Nazir Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: קׇדְשֵׁי אִקְּרִי, קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים לָא אִיקְּרוּ. וְאֶלָּא לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא אִיתַּקַּשׁ? אָמַר רָבָא: שֶׁאִם גִּלַּח עַל אֶחָד מִשְּׁלׇשְׁתָּן – יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers another explanation: **And if you wish, say** that the foreleg of the nazirite's ram **is called sacred, but it is not called an offering of the most sacred order.** Therefore, it cannot be juxtaposed with the sin offering of a nazirite in this regard. The Gemara asks: **But rather, with regard to what *halakha* is** the nazirite's ram **juxtaposed** with the sin offering of a nazirite? **Rava says:** It is juxtaposed to teach **that if** the nazirite **shaved after** the sacrifice of any **one of the three** offerings, he has **fulfilled** his obligation after the fact.

The Gemara adds a further reason why the *ayil nazir* is definitively not in the category of *kodshei kodashim*: it is never called *kodshei kodashim* in the Torah. At most the relevant verse uses the word *kodesh*; *zivchei shalmei tzibur*, by contrast, uses the phrase *kodesh l'Hashem*. Rashi notes this distinction in the language of the respective *psukim*.

This being so, the Gemara asks: if the linkage of the *ayil nazir* to the *chatas* is not meant to import the male *kohanim* restriction, what does the *hekesh* come to teach? Rava — some texts read Ravina — answers: *she'im gilach al achad me'shloshtan yatza* — it teaches that if the *nazir* shaved his head in conjunction with any one of the three *korbanot* in his passage (the *olah*, the *chatas*, or the *shelamim*), he fulfills his obligation of ending his *nezirut*. This is the link's direction: not to import laws from the *chatas* onto the *shelamim*, but to establish that the *shelamim* of the *nazir* — around which the entire shaving ritual is structured, since he places his shorn hair beneath the pot cooking the *zeroa beshela* — is so central to *nezirut* that shaving over any of the three *korbanot* suffices. This is a striking *chiddush*: the completion of *nezirut* is not exclusively dependent on the *ayil nazir* itself.

## Todah & Ayil Nazir

### Intro: Todah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' הַתּוֹדָה וְאֵיל נָזִיר, קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, שְׁחִיטָתָן בְּכׇל מָקוֹם בָּעֲזָרָה, וְדָמָן טָעוּן שְׁתֵּי מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע, וְנֶאֱכָלִין בְּכׇל הָעִיר, לְכׇל אָדָם, בְּכׇל מַאֲכָל, לְיוֹם וָלַיְלָה עַד חֲצוֹת. הַמּוּרָם מֵהֶם כְּיוֹצֵא בָּהֶן, אֶלָּא שֶׁהַמּוּרָם נֶאֱכָל לַכֹּהֲנִים, לִנְשֵׁיהֶם וְלִבְנֵיהֶם וּלְעַבְדֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The thanks offering and nazirite's ram** are **offerings of lesser sanctity. Their slaughter** is **anywhere in the** Temple **courtyard, and their blood requires two placements that are four, and they are eaten throughout the city** of Jerusalem, **by every person,** i.e., any ritually pure Jew, prepared **in any** manner of **food** preparation, **on** the **day** the offering was sacrificed **and** during the **night** that follows, **until midnight.** The status of the portion **that is separated from them** and given to the priests **is similar to theirs; but** the portion **that is separated is eaten by the priests, by their wives, and by their children, and by their slaves.**

Having concluded the *kodshei kodashim*, the Mishnah now turns to *kodshim kalim*, beginning with two categories that are, in a sense, subspecies of *shelamim* but with distinctive features. The first is the *todah* — the thanksgiving offering brought in four circumstances enumerated by *Chazal*. It is a type of *shelamim* but is accompanied by forty loaves of bread: thirty of *matzah* (in three varieties) and ten of *chametz*. The *kohen* receives one loaf from each group of ten, giving him one of each of the four types of bread in addition to the normal *kohen*'s portions of the animal.

The second is the *ayil nazir*, which we have just been discussing — the ram brought by the *nazir*, a private *shelamim* with the added priestly portion of the cooked foreleg beyond the usual *chazeh* and *shok*.

### Ayil Nazir Rules

Both the *todah* and the *ayil nazir* are *kodshim kalim*, and the Mishnah states: *shechitatan bechol makom ba'azarah* — they may be slaughtered anywhere in the *azarah*, not specifically in the north. This distinguishes them from *kodshei kodashim*. The blood requires *shtei matanot she'hen arba* — two applications that fulfill the requirement of four. They are eaten *bechol ha'ir* — throughout the city of Jerusalem — by anyone, *l'chol adam*, prepared in any manner, *bechol ma'achal*. 

One significant difference from generic *shelamim* is the time limit: *leyom valaylah*, eaten only for the day of the offering and the following night. The Torah states this explicitly in the passage of the *todah*, and the *ayil nazir* is linked by *Chazal* to the *todah*'s timeframe. The *d'oraita* rule extends through the entire night, but *Chazal* decreed *ad chatzot* — until midnight — as a protective measure, *harchakat adam min ha'aveira*, to prevent people from falling asleep and inadvertently eating after the time limit.

### Terumah Portion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֶת חֲזֵה הַתְּנוּפָה וְאֵת שׁוֹק הַתְּרוּמָה תֹּאכְלוּ בְּמָקוֹם טָהוֹר״ – אָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: וְכִי רִאשׁוֹנִים בְּטוּמְאָה אֲכָלוּם?!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the priests: **"And the breast of waving and the thigh of heaving you shall eat in a pure place;** you, and your sons, and your daughters with you; for they are given as your due, and your sons' due, out of the peace offerings of the children of Israel" (Leviticus 10:14). **Rabbi Neḥemya said: But did they eat** the **first** offerings, mentioned in the previous two verses, **in** a place of **impurity?** The previous verses make reference to the remainder of the meal offering, which also had to be eaten in a pure place.

Regarding the *hamuram mehem* — the *terumah* portions given to the *kohen*, namely the *shok haterumah* and *chazeh hatenufah* — these follow the same *halachot* as the rest: eaten in Jerusalem, within the same time restriction. However, there is a critical difference. Whereas the body of the *todah* and *ayil nazir* is eaten *l'chol adam*, the *hamuram* — the portions lifted for the *kohen* — may be eaten *lakohanim lenasheihem velivneihem vela'avdeihem*: by the *kohanim* and their wives, their children, and their slaves. This is not the stringency of *kodshei kodashim* — it is not limited to male *kohanim* alone — but neither is it available to all of Israel. Its holiness is comparable to *terumah*, which the Torah itself permits to the *kohen*'s household including his daughters, as the verse explicitly states *ata u'vanecha u'vnotecha itcha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טָהוֹר – מִכְּלָל שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא; טָהוֹר מִטּוּמְאַת מְצוֹרָע, וְטָמֵא מִטּוּמְאַת זָב. וְאֵיזֶה זֶה? זֶה מַחֲנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, by inference,** the word **"pure"** in this context is referring to a place **that is** to some degree **ritually impure,** but pure in some respects. The priest must eat in a place that is **pure due to the impurity of a leper, but** it may be a place that is **impure due to the impurity of a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*]. And which** place **is this? This** is **the Israelites' camp** in the wilderness, and, once the Temple was built in Jerusalem, the entire city of Jerusalem. A *zav* could remain in these places, but not a leper. This is the source for the *halakha* in the mishna that these offerings may be eaten anywhere in Jerusalem.

There is nonetheless a meaningful distinction from actual *terumah*: *terumah* is called *kodshei hagvul* and may be eaten anywhere within the boundaries of the Land of Israel. The *hamuram* here, while sharing *terumah*'s level of *kedusha* in terms of who may consume it, is geographically restricted to Jerusalem. It cannot be taken out of *Yerushalayim*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא טָהוֹר מִטּוּמְאַת זָב וְטָמֵא מִטּוּמְאַת מֵת – וְאֵיזֶה זֶה, זֶה מַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that the word "pure" is referring to a place which is **pure from the impurity of a *zav* and impure due to ritual impurity** imparted by **a corpse? And which** place **is this? This** is **the Levites' camp** in the wilderness, and, once the Temple was built in Jerusalem, the Temple Mount.

The Gemara also notes that the *bechol ma'achal* phrase — that it may be prepared in any manner — is retained in the Mishnah for purposes of comprehensive cataloguing, since the subsequent Mishnah concerning *Korban Pesach* will specify that the *Pesach* may only be eaten *tzali* (roasted), and the contrast requires that the other categories be explicitly stated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וַאֲכַלְתֶּם אֹתָהּ בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ״ – אוֹתָהּ בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ וְלֹא אַחֶרֶת בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ, אַפְּקַהּ לְמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה; הֲדַר כְּתִיב: ״בְּמָקוֹם טָהוֹר״ – אַפְּקַהּ לְמַחֲנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says** that **the verse states** with regard to the meal offering: **"And you shall eat it in a sacred place"** (Leviticus 10:13). The word "it" is a restriction, teaching that one must eat **"it,"** i.e., the meal offering, **in a sacred place,** i.e., in the camp of the Divine Presence, **but** one is **not** required to eat **another** offering, i.e., the breast of waving and the thigh of heaving, **in a sacred place.** This **removes** the portions from the camp of the Divine Presence, **to** be eaten in **the Levites' camp. Then it is written:** "You shall eat **in a pure place,"** which **removes** the portions from the Levites' camp, **to** be eaten in **the Israelites' camp.**

The Gemara then cites the *baraita* of *Tanu Rabbanan* to establish from where we know that this *hamuram* is eaten outside the *azarah* yet only within Jerusalem. The verse in *Shemini* reads: *ve'es chazeh hatenufah ve'es shok haterumah tochlu bemakom tahor ata u'vanecha u'vnotecha itacha* — eat the breast of the wave-offering and the thigh of the *terumah* in a pure place, you and your sons and daughters with you.

Rabbi Nechemiah asks: does this imply that earlier offerings were eaten in *tumah*? Clearly not. Rather, *tahor* here is being contrasted with *kadosh*, not asserting an absolute standard of purity. The preceding verse uses the phrase *bemakom kadosh* for the *mincha* — a *kodshei kodashim* — and it adds the phrase *kodesh kodashim hi*. Here, by contrast, the verse uses only *tahor*, without calling it *kadesh kodashim*, and includes daughters alongside sons. The term *tahor miklal she'hu tamei* — pure but implying some degree of impurity — reflects the Gemara's answer that *Yerushalayim* is called *makom tahor* in a relative sense: it excludes *metzoraim*, who must leave all three camps, but it permits the presence of a *zav*, a *tamei meis*, and other *tumos*. Jerusalem is therefore *tahor* relative to the *metzora*'s exclusion, yet *tamei* relative to *zav* status — *tahor mitumas metzora vetamei mitumas zav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: ״אוֹתָהּ בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ״ – וְלֹא אַחֶרֶת בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ, אַפְּקַהּ לִגְמָרֵי. הֲדַר כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״תֹּאכְלוּ בִּמְקוֹם טָהוֹר״ – עָיְילָא לְמַחֲנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** a different explanation: **"It,"** i.e., the meal offering, must be eaten **in a sacred place, but** one is **not** required to eat **another** offering, i.e., the breast of waving and the thigh of heaving, **in a sacred place.** This verse **removes** the portions **completely** from any pure place, and according to this stage of Rava's interpretation, these portions may be eaten anywhere. **Then the Merciful One wrote: "You shall eat in a pure place,"** which **brings it into the Israelites' camp,** which is a pure place, relative to everywhere outside it.

Abaye then reads the verses in a two-step derivation. The verse states *otah bemakom kadosh* — this one (*chatas*) in a holy place — implying by contrast that the *shelamim*'s *hamuram* need not be in a holy place, i.e., not in the *azarah* proper. That alone would tell us it could even be in *Machaneh Leviyah*, the area corresponding to *Har HaBayit*. The Torah then adds *bemakom tahor*, which takes it further out still, all the way to *Machaneh Yisrael* — that is, all of Jerusalem.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא עַיְּילַאּ לְמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה! לַחֲדָא מְעַיְּילִינַן, לְתַרְתֵּי לָא מְעַיְּילִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that the verse **brings it into the Levites' camp,** which is also referred to as pure? The Gemara answers: **We bring it into one** camp, adjacent to the area outside the encampment of the Jewish people, but **we do not bring it into two** camps.

Rava offers an alternative reading: *otah bemakom kadosh* implies that without further instruction one would have thought the *shelamim* could be eaten anywhere at all — even outside Jerusalem — since the limiting word *otah* emphasizes only that the *chatas* is *bemakom kadosh*, not the others. Then *bemakom tahor* pulls it back in to *Machaneh Yisrael*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אַפּוֹקֵי נָמֵי – לַחֲדָא מַפְּקִינַן, לְתַרְתֵּי לָא מַפְּקִינַן! וְעוֹד, ״לֹא תוּכַל לֶאֱכוֹל בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״ כְּתִיב! אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְאַבָּיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, also** with regard to **removing it,** why not say that **we remove it to one** place, but **we do not remove it from two** places? Accordingly, the initial derivation should be to eat it in the Israelites' camp. **And furthermore, it is written: "You may not eat within your gates** the tithe of your grain, or of your wine, or of your oil, or the firstborn of your herd or of your flock, nor any of your vows which you vow, nor your gift offerings, nor the offering of your hand" (Deuteronomy 12:17). This verse negates the possibility that an offering may be eaten "in your gates," i.e., outside of Jerusalem. **Rather, it is clear** that the source for the ruling of the *baraita* is **in accordance with** the explanation **of Abaye.**

The Gemara challenges Rava: if *tahor* is pulling something back inward, why does it land specifically at *Machaneh Yisrael* and not one step closer at *Machaneh Leviyah*? The Gemara answers: *l'chada mailinan l'tartei lo mailinan* — we bring it in only one degree by one textual indicator. Rava's derivation is then further challenged: the initial *hava amina* that a *korban* could be eaten outside Jerusalem is untenable, since the verse *lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha* already teaches that *kodshim* cannot be eaten in ordinary cities. The Gemara concludes: *michavrta k'Abaye* — Abaye's reading is the cleaner derivation.

## Generic Shlamim

### Shlamim Features

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלָמִים – קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים – שְׁחִיטָתָן בְּכׇל מָקוֹם בָּעֲזָרָה, וְדָמָן טָעוּן שְׁתֵּי מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע, וְנֶאֱכָלִין בְּכׇל הָעִיר, לְכׇל אָדָם, בְּכׇל מַאֲכָל, לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד; הַמּוּרָם מֵהֶם כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶם, אֶלָּא שֶׁהַמּוּרָם נֶאֱכָל לַכֹּהֲנִים, לִנְשֵׁיהֶם, וְלִבְנֵיהֶם, וּלְעַבְדֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Peace offerings** are **offerings of lesser sanctity. Their slaughter** is **anywhere in the** Temple **courtyard, and their blood requires two placements that are four, and they are eaten throughout the city** of Jerusalem, **by every person,** i.e., any ritually pure Jew, prepared **in any** manner of **food** preparation, **for two days and one night,** i.e., the day on which they are slaughtered, the following day, and the intervening night. The status of the portion **that is separated from them** and given to the priests **is similar to theirs; but** the portion **that is separated is eaten by the priests, by their wives, and by their children, and by their slaves.**

The Mishnah now turns to the broadest category of *kodshim kalim*: *shelamim* in general. The Mishnah states: *shelamim kodshim kalim shechitatam bechol makom ba'azarah* — they may be slaughtered anywhere in the *azarah*, with the blood requiring *shtei matanot she'hen arba*. They are eaten *bechol ha'ir* — throughout Jerusalem — *l'chol adam bechol ma'achal* — by any person in any form of preparation. The time limit is *l'shnei yamim v'laila echad* — two days and the intervening night. Unlike the *todah* and *ayil nazir*, where *Chazal* restricted eating to midnight as a precaution, here the full two-day period applies and there is no need for such a protective decree.

### Shlamim Terumah

Similarly, *hamuram mehem kayotze bahem* — the *terumah* portions follow the same rules — except that *ha'hamuram ne'echal l'kohanim u'lnesheihem u'livneihem u'la'avdeihem*: the *chazeh* and *shok* designated for the *kohanim* are eaten in Jerusalem but only by the *kohen*'s household, not by all of Israel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּשְׁחָטוֹ פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״; ״וְשָׁחַט אֹתוֹ לִפְנֵי אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״; ״וְשָׁחַט אוֹתוֹ לִפְנֵי אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ –
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to cattle peace offerings, the verse states: "And he shall lay his hand upon the head of his offering, **and slaughter it at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 3:2). With regard to sheep peace offerings the verse states: "And he shall lay his hand upon the head of his offering, **and slaughter it before the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 3:8). With regard to goat peace offerings the verse states: "And he shall lay his hand upon the head of it, **and slaughter it before the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 3:13).

The Gemara now uses the *shelamim* Mishnah as an opportunity to examine the broader question of how we know that *shelamim* may be slaughtered anywhere in the *azarah*. The underlying answer is straightforward: the Torah never specifies north for *shelamim*. Instead, whenever the Torah discusses *shelamim* in the early chapters of *Vayikra*, it uses the phrase *petach Ohel Moed* — the opening of the Tent of Meeting — a broad and directionally neutral designation. Three such *psukim* employ this language, and together they will serve to validate *shechita* in all directions, as the next section explores.

## Petach Ohel Moed

### Tanna Kamma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהַכְשִׁיר אֶת כׇּל הָרוּחוֹת בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים. קַל וָחוֹמֶר לַצָּפוֹן: וּמָה קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים, שֶׁלֹּא הוּכְשְׁרוּ בְּכׇל הָרוּחוֹת – הוּכְשְׁרוּ בַּצָּפוֹן; קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, שֶׁהוּכְשְׁרוּ בְּכׇל הָרוּחוֹת – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁהוּכְשְׁרוּ בַּצָּפוֹן?!
>
> **Translation:** This repetition serves **to render fit all directions** of the Temple courtyard **for** the slaughter of **offerings of lesser sanctity.** From here one can derive **an *a fortiori*** inference **to** permit slaughter in **the north** of the Temple courtyard: **Just as** the rites of slaughtering **offerings of the most sacred order, which are not rendered fit in all directions, are rendered fit in the north,** with regard to the rites of slaughtering **offerings of lesser sanctity, which are rendered fit in all directions, is it not logical that they are rendered fit in the north?**

A *baraita* is cited: the verses *v'shachatato petach Ohel Moed* and *v'shachato oto lifnei Ohel Moed* appear multiple times in the *shelamim* passages. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that three such *psukim* exist, and they serve three functions: one establishes the basic requirement that there be an operative *petach* — an open doorway; a second validates all directions, meaning any orientation within the *azarah*; and the third excludes *tzidei tzedadim* — side-of-side locations such as enclosed chambers or rooms off the *azarah*, which despite being physically proximate to *Ohel Moed* are not considered *lifnei Ohel Moed*. Given these three functions, the north requires no additional *pasuk* because there was never a reason to exclude it: the *Tanna Kamma* does not assume the north is reserved for *kodshei kodashim*, so a *kal vachomer* from *kodshei kodashim* straightforwardly validates the north for *kodshim kalim* as well.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לֹא נֶאֱמַר הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לְהַכְשִׁיר צָפוֹן; שֶׁיָּכוֹל וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, שֶׁהוּכְשְׁרוּ בְּכׇל הָרוּחוֹת – לֹא הוּכְשַׁר מְקוֹמָן אֵצֶל קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים; קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים, שֶׁלֹּא הוּכְשְׁרוּ אֶלָּא בַּצָּפוֹן – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא הוּכְשַׁר מְקוֹמָן אֵצֶל קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים?! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The repetition of **the verse is stated only to render fit** slaughter in the **north. As** one **might** have thought: **And could this not** be derived through **logical inference? Just as** with regard to **offerings of lesser sanctity, whose** slaughter is **rendered fit in any direction,** nevertheless **their place is not rendered fit for** the slaughter of **offerings of the most sacred order,** with regard to **offerings of the most sacred order, whose** slaughter is **rendered fit only in the north, is it not logical that their place should not be rendered fit for** the slaughter of **offerings of lesser sanctity?** Therefore, **the verse states: "Tent of Meeting,"** to teach that offerings of lesser sanctity may be slaughtered in the north.

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees. In his view, one would have a *hava amina* that *kodshim kalim* cannot be slaughtered in the north. The reasoning is an inverted *kal vachomer*: *kodshim kalim* are permitted in all directions where *kodshei kodashim* are not permitted; therefore, perhaps where *kodshei kodashim* are permitted — the north — *kodshim kalim* are not. Rashi elaborates: there is no intrinsic *kedusha* to the north; it is an arbitrary divine designation. If that is so, perhaps the Torah intended the north to be an exclusive zone reserved for *kodshei kodashim*, in which case *kodshim kalim* encroach on that designated space. This is a cogent argument. Therefore, Rabbi Eliezer requires one of the three *psukim* specifically to validate the north for *kodshim kalim*. The second *pasuk* teaches that the sides are valid even though they are not directly opposite the door. The third *pasuk*, in Rabbi Eliezer's reading, excludes *tzidei tzedadim* as a matter of logical inference without requiring explicit textual exclusion — if someone is in a closed-off room, that is obviously not *lifnei Ohel Moed* and requires no verse to dismiss.

### Debate Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: תְּלָתָא קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי; חַד לְגוּפֵיהּ – דְּנִיבְעֵי פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד, וְחַד לְהַכְשִׁיר צְדָדִין, וְחַד לִפְסוֹל צִידֵּי צְדָדִין. וְצָפוֹן לָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **With regard to what do they disagree? The first *tanna* holds** that **three verses are written. One** verse serves **to** teach the *halakha* **itself, that we require** that the slaughter take place at the **entrance of the Tent of Meeting. And one** verse serves **to render fit** slaughter at the **sides,** i.e., the entire length of the Temple courtyard eastward from the entrance, and not only that part which is directly in front of the entrance. **And one** verse serves **to disqualify the sides of the sides,** i.e., those parts of the courtyard that are not in front of the entrance at all. **And it was not necessary** to have **a verse** teach the fitness of slaughtering in the **north,** as it can be derived via an *a fortiori* inference.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: חַד לְגוּפֵיהּ – דְּנִיבְעֵי פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד, וְחַד לְהַכְשִׁיר צָפוֹן, וְחַד לְהַכְשִׁיר צְדָדִין. וְצִידֵּי צְדָדִין לָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Eliezer holds** that **one** verse is necessary **to** teach the *halakha* **itself, that we require** slaughter at the **entrance of the Tent of Meeting. And one** verse serves **to render fit** slaughter in the **north** of the courtyard. **And one** verse serves **to render fit** slaughter at the **sides** of the courtyard. **But** according to him, **it was not necessary** to have **a verse** teach that the **sides of the sides** are not fit for the slaughter of the offering.

Both *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer agree on the practical *halacha*: *kodshim kalim* may be slaughtered anywhere in the *azarah* including the north, and enclosed side-rooms do not qualify as *petach Ohel Moed*. Their dispute is purely methodological — a disagreement over which textual function each of the three *psukim* serves and whether the north requires an affirmative verse or a *kal vachomer*.

## Door Opening Rule

### Door Status Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא – דִּכְתִיב: ״פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם – דִּכְתִיב: ״לִפְנֵי אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל; דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁלָמִים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן קוֹדֶם שֶׁיִּפָּתְחוּ דַּלְתוֹת הַהֵיכָל – פְּסוּלִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּשְׁחָטוֹ פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ – בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא פָּתוּחַ, וְלֹא בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא נָעוּל.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **What is different here that it is written: "At the entrance to the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 3:2), **and what is different there that it is written: "Before the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 3:8, 13)? The Gemara answers: This **teaches us** a matter **in accordance with that** which **Rav Yehuda** says that **Shmuel says. As Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: Peace offerings that one slaughtered** in the Temple **before the doors of the Sanctuary were opened are disqualified, as it is stated** with regard to peace offerings: **"And he shall slaughter it at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 3:2), which teaches that it must be slaughtered **when** the entrance **is open** and serves as an actual entrance, **and not when it is locked.** As long as the entrance to the Tent of Meeting, or in the Temple the doors to the Sanctuary, remain closed, one may not sacrifice the peace offerings, and if they are sacrificed, they are disqualified.

The word *petach* in *petach Ohel Moed* carries a further *halacha* regarding temporal validity. Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel rules: *shelamim sheshachatam kodem sheyiptechu daltot ha'heichal — psulin* — *shelamim* slaughtered before the doors of the *heichal* are opened in the morning are invalid. The same ruling is cited in the name of Mar Ukva bar Chama citing Rav Yossi bar Chanina, and again in the name of Rav Yaakov bar Acha citing Rav Ashi — three independent transmissions of the same principle. The verse *v'shachatato petach Ohel Moed* is read: *bizman she'petach Ohel Moed patuach* — only when the opening of the *Ohel Moed* is actually open, *v'lo bizman shehu naul* — not when it is locked.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שְׁלָמִים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן קוֹדֶם שֶׁיִּפָּתְחוּ דַּלְתוֹת הַהֵיכָל – פְּסוּלִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּשְׁחָטוֹ פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ – בִּזְמַן שֶׁפֶּתַח הַהֵיכָל פָּתוּחַ, וְלֹא בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא נָעוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated: Mar Ukva bar Ḥama says** that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: Peace offerings that one slaughtered before the doors of the Sanctuary were opened are disqualified, as it is stated** in the verse: **"He shall slaughter it at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 3:2). This means **when the entrance of the Sanctuary is open, and not when it is locked.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* בְּמַעְרְבָא מַתְנֵי הָכִי – אָמַר רַב יַעֲקֹב בַּר אַחָא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: שְׁלָמִים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן קוֹדֶם שֶׁיִּפָּתְחוּ דַּלְתוֹת הַהֵיכָל – פְּסוּלִים. וּבַמִּשְׁכָּן – קוֹדֶם שֶׁיַּעֲמִידוּ לְוִיִּם אֶת הַמִּשְׁכָּן, וּלְאַחַר שֶׁיְּפָרְקוּ לְוִיִּם אֶת הַמִּשְׁכָּן – פְּסוּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they teach** this *halakha* **like this: Rav Ya'akov bar Aḥa says** that **Rav Ashi says: Peace offerings that one slaughtered before the doors of the Sanctuary were opened are disqualified. And in the Tabernacle,** which had no doors, peace offerings that were slaughtered **before** the **Levites erected the Tabernacle or after** the **Levites dismantled the Tabernacle are disqualified.**

This rule reflects a sensible intuition: before the doors of the King's palace are opened, one does not commence the sacred service in the courtyard. The Mishnayot elsewhere describe the process: a *kohen* would enter through a small passageway to unlock and swing open the great doors of the *Ulam* and then the *Heichal*, after which the day's service could begin.

The same principle applies to the *Mishkan*: if the *Leviim* had not yet erected the *Mishkan* — and thus no *petach* yet existed — or if they had already dismantled it, slaughter performed during those intervals would similarly be invalid. The *petach* must actually exist and must actually be open.

### Door vs Curtain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* פְּשִׁיטָא – מוּגָף, כְּנָעוּל דָּמֵי. וִילוֹן מַאי? אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: הוּא עַצְמוֹ אֵין נַעֲשֶׂה אֶלָּא כְּפֶתַח פָּתוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It is **obvious** that if the door is **closed** but not locked it has the same status **as if it were locked.** The Gemara asks: If there is **a curtain** covering the entrance, **what** is the *halakha*? **Rabbi Zeira says:** A curtain **itself is made only** to be used **like an open entrance,** and therefore it is not considered as if the entrance is closed.

The Gemara now probes the definition of *patuach*. It is obvious, the Gemara states, that a door that is merely *mugaf* — pushed closed without being locked — is considered *kenaul dami*, equivalent to locked, since a *petach* requires the door to be genuinely open, not merely unlocked.

What about a *vilon* — a curtain hanging in the doorway? Rabbi Zeira rules: a curtain is equivalent to an open doorway. Rashi explains that a curtain is there for modesty, not to block entry; it does not constitute a physical barrier. This ruling is, in fact, what one would expect from first principles: in the *Mishkan* itself there were no doors — only curtains — and those curtains were always understood as constituting *petach Ohel Moed*. A closed door is invalid; a curtain is treated as equivalent to an open door.

### Obstructions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* גּוּבְהָא מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי פִּשְׁפָּשִׁין הָיוּ בְּבֵית הַחֲלִיפוֹת, וְגוֹבְהָן שְׁמוֹנָה; כְּדֵי לְהַכְשִׁיר אֶת הָעֲזָרָה כּוּלָּהּ לַאֲכִילַת קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, וְלִשְׁחִיטַת קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the *halakha* if **a tall** item is blocking the entrance? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof that a tall item does not render an entrance as closed, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: There were two wickets,** i.e., openings, **in the Chamber of Knives, and their height** was **eight** cubits. The function of these openings was **in order to render fit the entire** Temple **courtyard for the eating of offerings of the most sacred order, and** to render fit all of the Temple courtyard **for the slaughter of offerings of lesser sanctity.** Due to the openings, the western area of the courtyard was considered to be "before the Tent of Meeting."

The Gemara raises the question *govah mai* — what if there is some physical obstruction in front of the doorway? *Tosafot* notes the irony here: the most obvious obstruction in the *azarah* was the *mizbeach* itself, and yet the *halacha* self-evidently permits *shechita* throughout the *azarah*. Perhaps the question is directed at an obstruction pressed directly up against the door itself — something that does not prevent the door from being open, but does block direct access or visibility. The question is left as a *teiku* of sorts, to be resolved by the subsequent architectural discussion.

## Back Door Access

### Beit Chalipot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַאי, לָאו דְּאִיכָּא קַמַּיְיהוּ שְׁמוֹנֶה? לָא; דְּגָבְהוּ נִינְהוּ שְׁמוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the inference: **What, is** the reference to eight cubits **not** referring to the fact **that in front of** the wickets there was an obstruction **eight** cubits high? Nevertheless, the area outside of it is considered open to the Chamber of Knives. The Gemara answers: **No, their height** was **eight** cubits, i.e., the wickets themselves were eight cubits high.

The Gemara cites a *baraita* from Rabbi Yossi son of Rabbi Yehuda: *shenei pishpeshin hayu b'Veit HaChalifot* — there were two small doorways in the *Beit HaChalifot*. The *Beit HaChalifot* was the area along the sides of the *Ulam*, the great porch that in the *Beit HaMikdash* extended fifteen *amot* beyond the width of the *Heichal* on each side. In the walls of the *Ulam* were niches — *chalifot* meaning knife-holders — where the *kohanim* stored their knives. These small doorways were *shemoneh amot* high, *kedei lehachshir et ha'azarah kulah* — in order to make the entire *azarah* qualify as *petach Ohel Moed* — *l'shchitat kodshim kalim*, for the *shechita* of *kodshim kalim*.

The reasoning is as follows: for areas of the *azarah* that lie behind the *Ulam* — on the far side of the building relative to the main courtyard — one cannot directly face the door of the *Heichal*. However, since these small back doorways exist, and through them one has access into the *Ohel Moed*, the entire space behind the *Ulam* acquires the status of *petach Ohel Moed*. The Gemara notes parenthetically that the Mishnah's mention of eating *kodshei kodashim* in this context is imprecise — the requirement of *petach Ohel Moed* was never applied to eating *kodshei kodashim*, which must simply be within the *azarah*, a *makom kadosh*, without the specific *petach* condition. The *petach Ohel Moed* requirement pertains only to *shechita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מֵיתִיבִי: כׇּל הַשְּׁעָרִים שֶׁהָיוּ שָׁם – גּוֹבְהָן עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה, וְרוֹחְבָּן עֶשֶׂר אַמָּה! פִּשְׁפָּשִׁין שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this explanation from a mishna (*Middot* 35a), which teaches: **All the gates that were there,** in the Temple, were **twenty cubits high and ten cubits wide.** If so, how could these wickets be only eight cubits high? The Gemara answers: The **wickets are different,** and they are not considered gates.

The Gemara briefly considers whether the statement that these doorways were eight *amot* high might instead be read as saying there was an eight-*amot* mound in front of them — which would make the doorways relevant to the obstruction question raised earlier. The Gemara dismisses this reading: the plain meaning is simply that the doorways themselves were eight cubits high. One might note that eight *amot* is a striking height for what are described as small back doors, though the Gemara notes that ordinary *shearim* of the *Mikdash* were twenty *amot* high and ten *amot* wide — *pishpeshin* are categorically different.

### Corner Doors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָאִיכָּא צְדָדִין! דִּמְעַיֵּיל לְהוּ בְּקֶרֶן זָוִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the ruling of the *baraita*. How did the wickets render fit the entire Temple courtyard? **But there are** the **sides,** i.e., to the north and south of the Chamber of Knives, and the wickets faced only to the west. The Gemara answers **that they inserted** the wickets **in** the **corner** of the Chamber, so that one opened to the northwest, and one to the northeast.

The placement of these *pishpeshin* is deliberate. The Gemara notes that they were positioned at the corners — *keren zavis* — of the *Ulam*'s back wall: one at the corner between the southern wall and the western wall, one at the corner between the northern wall and the western wall. By positioning them at the corners rather than centered on the back wall, both doors partially face sideways as well as rearward. This design means that the space alongside the *Ulam* — which is neither directly in front of the main entrance of the *Heichal* nor squarely behind it — is nonetheless *lifnei* one of the two corner doors, and therefore counts as *petach Ohel Moed*.

### Back Window

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲחוֹרֵי בֵּית הַכַּפּוֹרֶת מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לוּל קָטָן הָיָה אֲחוֹרֵי בֵּית הַכַּפּוֹרֶת, גָּבוֹהַּ שְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת; כְּדֵי לְהַכְשִׁיר אֶת הָעֲזָרָה לַאֲכִילַת קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, וְלִשְׁחִיטַת קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״שְׁנַיִם לַפַּרְבָּר״. מַאי ״לַפַּרְבָּר״? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא, כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: כְּלַפֵּי בַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the *halakha* with regard to the area **behind the Hall of the Ark Cover,** i.e., behind the Sanctuary, from where one could not see the entrance of the Temple courtyard or the two wickets? The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear, as Rami bar Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: There was a small niche behind the Hall of the Ark Cover,** which was **eight cubits high.** The function of this niche was **in order to render fit the entire** Temple **courtyard for the eating of offerings of the most sacred order, and** to render fit all of the Temple courtyard **for the slaughter of offerings of lesser sanctity. And this is as it is written:** "For the precinct westward, four at the causeway, and **two at the precinct"** (I Chronicles 26:18). **What** is the meaning of the term: **"For the precinct [*laparbar*]"? Rabba bar Rav Sheila said:** It is **like one who says: Facing outward [*kelappei bar*].**

The corner doors resolve the status of the area behind and alongside the *Ulam*, but a further question remains: what about the space directly behind the *Kodesh HaKodashim* itself — *achorei Beit HaKappores*? This area lies behind the *aron* and is accessible from neither the front doors nor the corner *pishpeshin*. 

The Gemara cites Ami bar Yud in the name of Rav: *lul katan hayah achorei Beit HaKappores* — there was a small opening, like a window, behind the *Beit HaKappores*, eight *amot* tall and eight *amot* wide. Its purpose, stated explicitly in the Gemara, was *l'hachshir et ha'azarah* — to render the entire *azarah* eligible for *kodshim kalim* slaughter by ensuring that every point in the *azarah* was in some sense *lifnei* an opening of the *Ohel Moed*. 

This is a striking *chidush*. The *Kodesh HaKodashim* was the most restricted space in the world — accessible only to the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur* — and yet there was apparently an opening of significant dimensions at its back wall. One might wonder whether this opening was visible from the *azarah* — whether one standing behind the building could see through into the inner sanctum. Perhaps some kind of curtain, *vilon*, served to maintain the sanctity and privacy of the space while preserving the legal status of an opening. This would harmonize with what was established earlier: a curtain qualifies as *petach patuach*.

### Guards & Dimensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין חַיָּיבִין מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה, אֶלָּא עַל אוֹרֶךְ מֵאָה וּשְׁמוֹנִים וָשֶׁבַע עַל רוֹחַב מֵאָה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים וְחָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** One is **liable for** entering the Temple courtyard in a state of **ritual impurity only with regard to** an area whose **length** is **187** cubits **by 135** cubits in **width.**

The Gemara connects this back opening to a cryptic verse: *v'hainu d'chtiv shenayim laparbar* — two were for the *parbar*. Rabbah bar Rav Shila explains *parbar* as *klapei bar* — meaning outward-facing. Two guards were stationed for this rear opening of the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, their function being to protect the sanctity of the space from the outside.

The daf closes with a transition to a new topic. Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel rules: *ein chayavin mishum tumah ela al orech me'ah ushmonim v'shiva v'rochav me'ah ushloshim v'chamesh* — one is liable for *tumah* only within a space 187 by 135 *amot*. This is the precise measurement of the *azarah* proper — exclusive of the *Ezrat Nashim*, which bears the status of *Machaneh Leviyah* rather than *Machaneh Shechinah*. It is in this bounded space — 187 by 135 *amot* — that all of the laws discussed throughout this daf apply: eating *kodshei kodashim*, *shechita* of *kodshim kalim*, and liability for *tumah* in the *azarah*. The elaboration of those dimensions will continue on the following daf.
