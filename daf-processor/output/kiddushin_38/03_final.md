# Kiddushin 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chadash Overview

### Winter vs. Spring Wheat

Today's *daf* is 38, continuing the discussion of *mitzvot hatluyot ba'aretz* and picking up where we left off on the topic of *chadash*. By way of introduction, it is worth clarifying the practical application of *chadash* to the wheat situation in the United States. Winter wheat is planted in the fall, grows through the winter, and begins to be harvested around spring. Because it takes root over the winter — before the *omer* is brought — it never presents a *chadash* problem: by the time it is ready to come to market, the *omer* has already been brought, and the *omer* is effective from the moment the grain has taken root. The problem arises specifically with spring wheat, which is planted in the spring after the *omer* is brought, takes root after the *omer* is brought, and comes to market around *Rosh Hashanah*. That spring wheat remains *asur* until the following *pesach*, until the next *omer* is brought.

As for the practical proportion: approximately 70 to 80 percent of wheat grown in the United States is winter wheat, so the argument that the *rov* is *yashan* is correct. However, different wheat varieties have different qualities — gluten content and rising characteristics differ — and so it is not always possible to assume that a given flour is entirely from one type. Some all-purpose flours may be a blend. Pizza dough, by contrast, is almost invariably winter wheat, chosen specifically for its rising qualities, which is why pizza establishments can readily rely on *yashan*. The winter wheat is what gets one out of the *chadash* problem.

### Contradiction Issue

One might ask why no one seems to address the apparent contradiction between all these *sugyot* and the verse in *Kohelet*, which states *ein chadash* — there is nothing new. The response offered is simply: *ain chadash* — there you go.

### Mixing Chadash/Yashan

A practical question arises about mixing: if flour that is *chadash* is blended with *yashan* flour by a producer, can one rely on *bitul*? The answer is that one cannot simply rely on *bitul* here, because *chadash* is a *davar sheyesh lo matirin* — something that will eventually become permitted on its own, namely after the next *omer* is brought. A *ta'arovet* of *chadash* with *yashan* therefore does not permit the mixture. One might try to argue along the lines of *kol deparish meruba parish* — treating whatever comes to hand as presumptively from the majority — but the matter is complicated.

## Rabbi Yishmael & Binyan…

### Tanna Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״מוֹשָׁב״ – כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתֶּם יוֹשְׁבִים מַשְׁמַע, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״וַיֹּאכְלוּ מֵעֲבוּר הָאָרֶץ מִמׇּחֳרַת הַפֶּסַח״ – מִמָּחֳרַת הַפֶּסַח אֲכוּל, מֵעִיקָּרָא – לָא אֲכוּל. אַלְמָא אַקְרוּב עוֹמֶר וַהֲדַר אֲכוּל. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְאַחַר יְרוּשָּׁה וִישִׁיבָה, נֵיכוֹל לְאַלְתַּר! לָא הֲווֹ צְרִיכִי, דִּכְתִיב ״וּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אָכְלוּ אֶת הַמָּן אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה עַד בֹּאָם אֶל אֶרֶץ נוֹשָׁבֶת, אֶת הַמָּן אָכְלוּ עַד בֹּאָם אֶל קְצֵה אֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן״. אִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹמַר ״עַד בֹּאָם אֶל אֶרֶץ נוֹשָׁבֶת״, שֶׁכְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״אֶל קְצֵה אֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן״. וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹמַר ״אֶל קְצֵה אֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן״, שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״עַד בֹּאָם אֶל אֶרֶץ נוֹשָׁבֶת״. הָא כֵּיצַד? בְּשִׁבְעָה בַּאֲדָר מֵת מֹשֶׁה וּפָסַק מָן מִלֵּירֵד, וְהָיוּ מִסְתַּפְּקִין מִמָּן שֶׁבִּכְלֵיהֶם עַד שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּנִיסָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about the term dwelling, written in connection to the prohibition of the new crop. **Granted, according to the one who says** that the term **dwelling indicates wherever you dwell, this** explains the fact **that it is written** with regard to the Jewish people immediately after they entered Eretz Yisrael: **"And they ate the produce of the land on the morrow after the Passover"** (Joshua 5:11). This means that **on the day after the Passover** they **ate** the produce of Eretz Yisrael, but **initially,** before that date, they **did not eat** it. **Apparently, they sacrificed the *omer* and** only **afterward** did they **eat. But** according to the one who says that the term dwelling indicates that the prohibition of the new crop applies only **after inheritance and settlement** of Eretz Yisrael, **let** the Jews **eat** the produce of the land **immediately. The Gemara answers: They did not need** to eat the new produce, as they still had manna. Although they did not eat the new crop of Eretz Yisrael, the reason was not that it was prohibited. **As it is written: "And the children of Israel ate the manna forty years until they came to a settled land; they ate the manna until they came to the borders of the land of Canaan"** (Exodus 16:35). The Gemara analyzes this verse: **One cannot say** they ate **"until they came to a settled land,"** i.e., that they were still eating the manna when they entered Eretz Yisrael, **as it is already stated: "To the borders of the land of Canaan,"** which indicates that they stopped eating manna before entering Eretz Yisrael, on the plains of Moab. **And one cannot say** that they ate manna only until they reached **"to the borders of the land of Canaan," as it is already stated: "Until they came to a settled land." How** can **these** clauses be reconciled? **Moses died on the seventh of Adar and the manna ceased falling, and they ate the manna that was** left **in their vessels until the sixteenth of Nisan,** even after they entered Eretz Yisrael.

The *Gemara* picks up on 37b. By *chadash* the *pasuk* states: *lechem vekali vekarmel lo tochlu ad etzem hayom hazeh ad havi'achem es korban elokeichem, chukas olam ledoroseichem bechol moshavoseichem* — bread, parched grain, and fresh ears you shall not eat until this very day, until you bring the offering of your God, an eternal statute throughout your generations in all your dwelling places. The question is what *bechol moshavoseichem* means. Does it mean wherever you dwell — even in *chutz la'aretz* — as Rabbi Eliezer holds? Or does it mean after you have settled the land, as Rabbi Yishmael holds regarding *nesachim*, linking *moshavoseichem* to the concept of having taken possession and settled?

*Abaye* notes that a *tanna* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael contradicts another *tanna* from that same school. The teaching he introduces runs as follows: *ho'il vene'emru biyot batorah stam* — since the *Torah* frequently speaks of *ki savo el ha'aretz* without further specification — *ufaret lecha hakatuv be'achat mehen le'achar yerusha veyeshiva* — and in one place it specifies that the obligation applies only after taking possession and settling — *af kol le'achar yerusha veyeshiva* — we derive that all instances of *ki savo*, even without the added phrase *veyarashta veyashavta*, mean after you have settled the land. In other words, in one place the *Torah* explicitly links entry to settlement, and that serves as a paradigm for every other usage of *ki savo* throughout the *Torah*.

### Binyan Av Hermeneutic

The hermeneutical mechanism here is *binyan av*: one clear case in the *Torah* — where entry is explicitly conditioned on *yerusha veyeshiva*, possession and settlement — serves as a model for all other cases, establishing that *ki savo* never means the mere act of crossing the border but always implies the completed process of conquest and settlement after the fourteen-year period. The specific examples are the *pasuk* by the king — *ki savo el ha'aretz veyarashta veyashavta bah* — and the *pasuk* by *bikkurim* — similarly, *ki savo el ha'aretz veyarashta veyashavta*, followed by *velakachta mereishit kol pri ha'adamah*. Both specify that the relevant obligation applies only after taking possession and settling.

### Two Verses Together

The opposing *tanna* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael disagrees. His argument is that you cannot use this as a *binyan av* because the condition *yerusha veyeshiva* is spelled out explicitly in two separate *pesukim* — by the king and by *bikkurim* — and the rule is that *shnei ketuvim haba'im ke'echad ein melamdin*: when the *Torah* makes two cases explicit, those two cases are to be understood as exceptions, not as paradigms from which to generalize. According to this view, the default meaning of *ki savo* is immediate entry, and both the king and *bikkurim* are spelled out precisely because they would otherwise have been understood as applying immediately.

The first *tanna* responds with *tzrichi* — both *pesukim* are necessary and neither is superfluous. He argues that had the *Torah* written only the case of the king without *bikkurim*, one might have concluded that while the general rule requires settlement, *bikkurim* is an exception — since the fruits of the land are immediately available upon entry, one might have thought *bikkurim* should apply at once. Therefore the *Torah* had to clarify that *bikkurim* too follows the general rule and applies only after settlement. And had the *Torah* written only *bikkurim* without the king, one might have assumed that a king is needed from the outset — since waging the wars of conquest requires a king — and therefore the obligation to appoint a king applies immediately. Therefore the *Torah* had to clarify that even appointing a king is only after settlement. Each case is necessary, but they are not exceptions; they are confirmations that the rule of settlement applies even in contexts where one might have thought otherwise.

### Challah Comparison

The *tanna* who holds that the two explicit *pesukim* create exceptions then pushes back further. He says: the *Torah* could have written only the case of the king, and I could have inferred *bikkurim* on my own — *umah Melech d'lichbosh acharei yerusha veyeshiva, Bikkurim lo kol shekein* — if even appointing a king, who is normally needed to wage war, is only after settlement, how much more so *bikkurim*, which is simply bringing the first fruits. The fact that *bikkurim* was spelled out again signals that it is an exception, confirming that the general rule of *ki savo* means immediately upon entry.

The first *tanna* replies that this inference fails because of *challah*. By *challah*, the *Torah* uses the word *bevo'achem* — when you come — and the *Gemara* elsewhere derives that *challah* applies immediately upon entry into the land, without waiting for settlement. Since *bikkurim* and *challah* are closely analogous — both are considered a form of *terumah*, both are *matanot* given to the *kohen*, and both are called *reishit* — I would have naturally equated them and concluded that *bikkurim*, like *challah*, applies immediately. Therefore the *Torah* had to specify separately that *bikkurim* too requires prior settlement, and this specification is not to mark *bikkurim* as an exception but to prevent me from placing it in the category of *challah*. The general rule remains that *ki savo* means settlement, and *bikkurim* is clarified as part of that rule, not as a departure from it.

What remains relevant to us regarding *chadash* is that in both approaches, *moshavoseichem* means after you have settled — as opposed to the entirely different reading that *moshavoseichem* means wherever you dwell, which would entail that *chadash* applies immediately in *chutz la'aretz*. Regardless of which hermeneutical approach one takes within the debate above, the question of whether *chadash* applies outside the land depends on which meaning of *moshavoseichem* one adopts.

## Moshvoteichem Meaning

### Shabbat Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ חוֹבַת הַגּוּף נוֹהֲגֶת בֵּין בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל בֵּין בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, ״מוֹשָׁב״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי שַׁבָּת, לְמָה לִי? אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּבְעִנְיָנָא דְמוֹעֲדוֹת כְּתִיבָא, תִּיבְּעֵי קִידּוּשׁ כִּי מוֹעֲדוֹת, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And now that you said** that **an obligation of the body applies both in Eretz** Yisrael **and outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **why do I** need the term **"dwelling" that the Merciful One writes with regard to Shabbat** (Leviticus 23:3)? The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** to say this, as **it might enter your mind to say: Since** the mitzva of Shabbat **was written in the context of the Festivals, it should require sanctification** by the court, **like the Festivals,** which rely on the sanctification of the New Moon by the court. Therefore the verse **teaches us** that this is not required.

The *Gemara* now transitions: *hashta de'amarta chovat haguf noheget bein ba'aretz bein bechutz la'aretz* — now that we have established that personal obligations apply in *chutz la'aretz* — *moshvoteichem dichtav rachmana gabei Shabbat lama li?* Why does the *Torah* say *bechol moshvoseichem* by *Shabbat*? The *pasuk* reads *Shabbat hi l'Hashem bechol moshvoseichem*. If *moshvoseichem* normally signifies after you've settled the land, then writing it by *Shabbat* is completely inappropriate — *Shabbat* obviously cannot be a condition on settlement. And if it means wherever you dwell, that too is obvious regarding *Shabbat*, since everyone knows it applies everywhere.

The *Gemara* answers: *itzterich* — it is necessary. *Salka da'atach amina* — one might have thought, since *Shabbat* is written in *Parshat Emor* in the same passage as the other *mo'adim*, that perhaps it would require a process of sanctification parallel to the other festivals, which depend on *kiddush hachodesh* — the declaration of the new month — to determine when they occur. Perhaps *Shabbat* would similarly require some form of declaration to make it holy. *Ka mashma lan* — the *Torah* therefore teaches, through the phrase *bechol moshvoseichem*, that *Shabbat* has no dependence on what takes place in the land of Israel. It applies automatically, without any aspect of sanctification — emphasizing that it is entirely independent of any centralized process. This is why even though it is obvious that *Shabbat* applies everywhere, the *Torah* still had to write *bechol moshvoseichem*.

### Chelev Vedam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מוֹשָׁב״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי חֵלֶב וָדָם, לְמָה לִי? אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּבְעִנְיָנָא דְקׇרְבָּנוֹת כְּתִיבִי, בִּזְמַן דְּאִיכָּא קׇרְבָּן, נִיתְּסַר חֵלֶב וָדָם, בִּזְמַן דְּלֵיכָּא קׇרְבָּן – לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **Why do I** need the term **dwelling, which the Merciful One writes with regard to** the consumption of forbidden **fat and blood** in the verse: "A perpetual statute throughout your generations in all your dwellings, that you shall eat neither forbidden fat nor blood" (Leviticus 3:17)? The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** to state this, as **it might enter your mind to say: Since** these prohibitions **are written in the context of the offerings,** then **when there is an offering** and the priests sacrifice fat and blood upon the altar, i.e., when the Temple is standing, that is when forbidden **fat and blood are forbidden** for consumption. But **when there is no offering,** forbidden fat and blood are **not** forbidden. The verse therefore **teaches us** that they are always forbidden.

The *Gemara* continues to other *mitzvot* where the *Torah* uses the phrase *bechol moshvoseichem*, examining in each case why that phrase is necessary. By *chelev vedam* — fat and blood — the *Torah* says *bechol moshvoseichem*. Why is this necessary? The prohibition on *chelev vedam* is clearly not land-based; of course it applies everywhere. The *Gemara* answers: *salka da'atach amina* — since the prohibition of *chelev vedam* appears in the context of *korbanot*, one might have thought that *chelev* and *dam* are only forbidden because they belong on the *mizbe'ach*, and that therefore the prohibition would apply only when there are *korbanot*. And as for the *chelev* of a *chaya* — a non-domesticated animal — which cannot be sacrificed on the *mizbe'ach*, one might have thought it is entirely permitted. *Ka mashma lan* — the *Torah* therefore teaches through *bechol moshvoseichem* that the prohibition applies in all places and at all times, entirely independent of the context of the *Mikdash*.

### Matzah Umaror

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מוֹשָׁב״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי מַצָּה וּמָרוֹר, לְמָה לִי? אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב ״עַל מַצּוֹת וּמְרֹרִים יֹאכְלֻהוּ״, בִּזְמַן דְּאִיכָּא פֶּסַח – אִין, בִּזְמַן דְּלֵיכָּא פֶּסַח – לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, the Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the term **dwelling, which the Merciful One writes with regard to *matza* and bitter herbs,** in the verse: "In all your dwellings you shall eat *matza*" (Exodus 12:20)? The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** to state this, as **it might enter your mind to say** that **since it is written: "They shall eat** the Paschal offering **with *matza* and bitter herbs"** (Numbers 9:11), **when there is a Paschal offering, yes,** one must eat it with *matza* and bitter herbs, but **when there is no Paschal offering,** i.e., when the Temple is not standing, there is **no** obligation to eat *matza* and bitter herbs. The verse therefore **teaches us** that these mitzvot apply every Passover, whether or not the Temple is standing.

Similarly, by *matzah umaror* the *Torah* states *bechol moshvoseichem tochlu matzot*. Why is this necessary? *Salka da'atach amina* — the *pasuk* itself establishes *matzah* and *maror* in the context of the *korban pesach* — *al matzot umerorim yochelohu* — and one might have concluded that *matzah* and *maror* are obligatory only when the *Pesach* offering is brought, and not otherwise. *Ka mashma lan* — the phrase *bechol moshvoseichem* teaches that *matzah* applies even in exile, even after the *churban Beis HaMikdash*, even when one is not in the land of Israel and even when there is no *korban pesach*. This is of course directly relevant to our own practice. The irony is not lost that *maror* in fact does not apply today independently of the *korban pesach*, and the *Gemara* elsewhere derives the continuing obligation of *matzah* from a separate *pasuk*. But the principle established by *bechol moshvoseichem* is that *matzah* is not contingent on the context of the *Mikdash*.

### Tefillin & Peter Chamor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בִּיאָה״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי תְּפִילִּין וּפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר, לְמָה לִי? הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: עֲשֵׂה מִצְוָה זוֹ, שֶׁבִּשְׁבִילָהּ תִּיכָּנֵס לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **Why do I** need the term **coming, which the Merciful One writes with regard to phylacteries and the firstborn donkey,** at the start of the Torah passage which discusses these mitzvot: "And it shall be when the Lord will bring you into the land of the Canaanite" (Exodus 13:11)? The Gemara answers: **That verse is necessary for that which** the *tanna* of **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Perform this mitzva** of donning phylacteries, **on account of which** you shall be rewarded and **enter Eretz** Yisrael.

By *tefillin* and *peter chamor*, the *Torah* uses the phrase *ki savo el ha'aretz* — when you come to the land — followed by the instruction regarding the firstborn donkey and the commandment of *tefillin*. These *mitzvot* are plainly not limited to coming into the land. So why does the *Torah* phrase them in terms of entry? The *Gemara* answers that we need the phrase for what the school of Rabbi Yishmael teaches: *asei mitzvah zo she'bishvilah tikanes la'aretz* — perform this *mitzvah*, for in its merit you will enter the land. The reference to entry is not to say that *tefillin* and *peter chamor* apply only after entry; rather, it is a reversal of the usual dependency. These *mitzvot* are the merit by virtue of which one merits entry into the land.

## Chadash in Israel

### Omer Necessity

The *Gemara* now returns to *chadash* directly. *Bishlama lema'an de'amar moshav kol makom she'atem yoshvim mashma* — according to the view that *moshav* means wherever you dwell, and *chadash* therefore applies in *chutz la'aretz* like Rabbi Eliezer holds, one can understand the *pasuk* in *Yehoshua*: *vayochlu me'avur ha'aretz mimocharat haPesach* — they ate from the produce of the land from the day following *Pesach*. On the reading that *chadash* applies everywhere, they could not eat the new grain of the land until the *omer* was brought — *mei'ikara lo achol* — and only after that could they eat. *Alma akrivu omer ve'hadar achol* — this implies the *omer* was in fact brought and the eating followed. According to *Chazal*, something happened on the sixteenth of *Nissan* — evidently the *omer* was brought in the traveling *Mishkan* — and that permitted eating from the new grain. This reading coheres with the position that *chadash* applies in *chutz la'aretz* and applies in Israel immediately upon entry, requiring the *omer* before any new grain may be consumed.

### Settlement Timing

But consider the alternative: *ela lema'an de'amar le'achar yerusha veyeshiva* — according to the view that *bechol moshvoseichem* by *chadash* means after you've settled the land. In that case, when *Yehoshua* and the Jewish people had just entered the land, the *issur chadash* had not yet taken effect. So *neichlu le'altar* — why did they not simply eat the new grain immediately upon crossing the Jordan? The *Gemara* answers: *lav mitzterichi* — they simply did not need it. It was coincidence that they waited until the sixteenth of *Nissan*, and this had nothing to do with the *omer*.

Why did they not need the grain? Because *dichtiv uvnei Yisrael achlu es haman arbaim shana ad bo'am el eretz noshevet* — they ate the *man* for forty years until they came to the settled land. But a problem arises: one *pasuk* says they ate the *man* until they came *el eretz noshevet* — implying they ate it until after they entered — while another says they ate it *ad bo'am el ketzei eretz Canaan* — until they reached the edge of the land of *Canaan*, suggesting they stopped before entering. *Ha keitzad* — how do we reconcile these?

The *Gemara* resolves: *beshiva be'Adar meis Moshe* — Moshe died on the seventh of *Adar*. The *man* stopped falling when Moshe died, when the people were at the cusp of entering. But they continued eating from that last supply of *man* — one day's portion, which sustained them — for the following thirty-seven days until they actually entered the land. Both *pesukim* are thus true: the *man* stopped falling at the edge of *Canaan*, and yet they were still eating it when they came *el eretz noshevet*. This is the resolution to the apparent contradiction.

The relevance for our question is as follows: if *chadash* applies *bechol moshvoseichem* everywhere and immediately — including in the land of Israel from the moment of entry — then one must explain why they did not eat the new grain immediately upon crossing. The answer is that they still had *man* and did not need it. The *omer* was then brought on the sixteenth, and only after that did they eat from the new grain. But if *moshavoseichem* means only after settlement, they were already permitted to eat the new grain upon entry, and they simply happened not to need it until the sixteenth because they were still sustained by the *man*.

### Mahn Narrative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״וּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אָכְלוּ אֶת הַמָּן אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה״. וְכִי אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה אָכְלוּ? וַהֲלֹא אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה חָסֵר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם אָכְלוּ! אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לְךָ: עוּגוֹת שֶׁהוֹצִיאוּ מִמִּצְרַיִם, טָעֲמוּ בָּהֶם טַעַם מָן.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"And the children of Israel ate the manna forty years"** (Exodus 16:35). **But did they** really **eat** it for **forty years? But didn't they eat** it for **forty years less thirty days?** The manna began to fall on the sixteenth of Iyyar in the first year in the wilderness, and they stopped eating it on the sixteenth of Nisan in the fortieth year. **Rather, this** verse comes **to tell you** that **they tasted the taste of manna** in the unleavened **cakes that they took out from Egypt** on the fifteenth of Nisan in their first year, and this sustained them until the manna fell.

A separate *braisa* addresses the chronology of the *man* itself. *Uvnei Yisrael achlu es haman arbaim shana* — they ate the *man* for forty years. Did they really eat it for a full forty years? In fact it was forty years minus thirty days, since the *man* did not begin to fall on the day they left Egypt but only a month later. How then can the *Torah* say forty years? *Ela lomar lach* — the *Torah* is telling us that the cakes they had taken out of Egypt — *ugah shehotziu miMitzrayim ta'amu bahem ta'am man* — tasted like the *man*. So it was as if they had been eating the *man* for forty years, because even in the first month, before the *man* fell, the cakes they were eating had the taste of *man*.

### Chronology Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בְּשִׁבְעָה בַּאֲדָר מֵת מֹשֶׁה, וּבְשִׁבְעָה בַּאֲדָר נוֹלַד. מִנַּיִן שֶׁבְּשִׁבְעָה בַּאֲדָר מֵת – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּמׇת שָׁם מֹשֶׁה עֶבֶד ה׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּבְכּוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת מֹשֶׁה בְּעַרְבֹת מוֹאָב שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי אַחֲרֵי מוֹת מֹשֶׁה עֶבֶד ה׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״מֹשֶׁה עַבְדִּי מֵת וְעַתָּה קוּם עֲבֹר״ וּכְתִיב: ״עִבְרוּ בְּקֶרֶב הַמַּחֲנֶה וְצַוּוּ אֶת הָעָם לֵאמֹר הָכִינוּ לָכֶם צֵדָה כִּי בְּעוֹד שְׁלֹשֶׁת יָמִים תַּעַבְרוּ אֶת הַיַּרְדֵּן״ וּכְתִיב: ״וְהָעָם עָלוּ מִן הַיַּרְדֵּן בֶּעָשׂוֹר לַחֹדֶשׁ הָרִאשׁוֹן״, צֵא מֵהֶן שְׁלֹשִׁים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים לְמַפְרֵעַ, הָא לָמַדְתָּ שֶׁבְּשִׁבְעָה בַּאֲדָר מֵת מֹשֶׁה. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁבְּשִׁבְעָה בַּאֲדָר נוֹלַד מֹשֶׁה – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵהֶם בֶּן מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה אָנֹכִי הַיּוֹם לֹא אוּכַל עוֹד לָצֵאת וְלָבוֹא״, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַיּוֹם״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַיּוֹם״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא יוֹשֵׁב וּמְמַלֵּא שְׁנוֹתֵיהֶם שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם וּמֵחֹדֶשׁ לְחֹדֶשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת מִסְפַּר יָמֶיךָ אֲמַלֵּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **Moses died on the seventh of Adar, and he was** likewise **born on the seventh of Adar. From where** is it derived **that** Moses **died on the seventh of Adar? As it is stated: "So Moses, the servant of the Lord, died there"** (Deuteronomy 34:5), **and it is written: "And the children of Israel wept for Moses in the plains of Moab thirty days"** (Deuteronomy 34:8). **And it is written: "Now it came to pass after the death of Moses, the servant of the Lord"** (Joshua 1:1), **and it is written: "Moses, My servant, is dead; now arise, cross** this Jordan" (Joshua 1:2). **And it is written: "Pass through the midst of the camp and command the people, saying: Prepare your victuals, for within three days you are to cross the Jordan"** (Joshua 1:11). **And it is written: "And the people came up out of the Jordan on the tenth day of the first month"** (Joshua 4:19). **Subtract retroactively from** that date, the tenth of Nisan, **the last thirty-three days,** i.e., the thirty days of mourning for Moses and the three days of preparation before crossing the Jordan, and you **learn** from here **that Moses died on the seventh of Adar. And from where** is it derived that **Moses was born on the seventh of Adar?** It is **as it is stated: "And he said to them, I am one hundred and twenty years old today; I can no more go out and come in"** (Deuteronomy 31:2). **As** there is **no** need for **the verse to state "today,"** since Moses could have said simply: I am one hundred and twenty years old. **What** is the meaning when **the verse states "today"?** One can learn from it that Moses was born on that date, i.e., he was exactly one hundred and twenty years old. **This teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, sits and completes the years of the righteous from day to day and from month to month, as it is stated: "The number of your days I will fulfill"** (Exodus 23:26).

Another *braisa* establishes the precise chronology. *Beshiva be'Adar meis Moshe, beshiva be'Adar nolad* — Moshe was born and died on the seventh of *Adar*. How do we know he died on the seventh of *Adar*? From the *pasuk* stating that Moshe died in the plains of *Moav*, combined with the *pasuk* that the people mourned him for thirty days in the plains of *Moav*. Then the book of *Yehoshua* opens with God commanding Yehoshua following Moshe's death: *moshe avdi meis ve'atah kum avor* — Moshe my servant has died, now arise and cross. And soon after: *ivru bekerev hamachane vetzivu es ha'am — hachinu lachem tzeidah ki be'od shlosha yamim ta'avru es hayardein* — prepare yourselves provisions, for in three days you will cross the Jordan. And then: *veha'am alu min hayardein be'asor lachodesh harishon* — the people came up from the Jordan on the tenth of the first month. Working backwards from the tenth of *Nissan*: subtract thirty days of mourning and three days of preparation, and one arrives at the seventh of *Adar*. That is how we know Moshe died on the seventh of *Adar*. And how do we know he was born on that date? Because Moshe said *ben me'ah ve'esrim shana anochi hayom* — I am one hundred and twenty years old today — and the principle is that the righteous live out the full measure of their years, filling out the year entirely, as the *pasuk* states *et mispar yamecha amaleh* — the number of your days I will fill. Therefore Moshe died on the same day he was born.

## Three Mitzvot Debate

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: שָׁלֹשׁ מִצְוֹת נִצְטַוּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּכְנִיסָתָן לָאָרֶץ, וְנוֹהֲגוֹת בֵּין בָּאָרֶץ בֵּין בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ. וּמָה חָדָשׁ, שֶׁאֵין אִיסּוּרוֹ אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם, וְאֵין אִיסּוּרוֹ אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה, וְיֵשׁ הֶיתֵּר לְאִיסּוּרוֹ, נוֹהֵג בֵּין בָּאָרֶץ בֵּין בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ – כִּלְאַיִם, שֶׁאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם, וְאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה, וְאֵין הֶיתֵּר לְאִיסּוּרָן – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיִּנְהֲגוּ בֵּין בָּאָרֶץ בֵּין בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ? וְהוּא הַדִּין לְעׇרְלָה בִּשְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: The Jewish people were commanded** to perform **three mitzvot** immediately **upon entering Eretz** Yisrael: The new crop, diverse kinds, and *orla*, **and** these **apply both in Eretz** Yisrael **and outside of Eretz** Yisrael. **If** the prohibition of the **new crop, whose prohibition is not a permanent prohibition,** as it does not apply to grain that grows after the *omer* has been sacrificed on the sixteenth of Nisan, **and whose prohibition is not a prohibition against** deriving **benefit,** since usages other than eating are permitted, **and there is dissolution for its prohibition,** i.e., grain that grew before the sixteenth of Nisan becomes permitted after the *omer* offering has been brought, **applies both in Eretz** Yisrael **and outside of Eretz** Yisrael, the same *halakha* should certainly apply to diverse kinds. With regard to **diverse kinds, whose prohibition is a permanent prohibition,** as it never expires, **and whose prohibition is a prohibition against** deriving **benefit,** since one may not benefit in any manner from diverse kinds of the vine, **and there is no dissolution for its prohibition, is it not logical that it should apply both in Eretz** Yisrael **and outside of Eretz** Yisrael? **And the same is true of *orla* with regard to two** of those points. *Orla* is not a permanent prohibition, as it applies only for the first three years that a tree produces fruit, but one may not derive benefit from it, and produce from the first three years never becomes permitted.

*Tanya Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai omer* — Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai teaches: *shalosh mitzvot nitztavu Yisrael bichnisatan la'aretz venoheget bein ba'aretz bein b'chutz la'aretz* — three *mitzvot* were commanded to Israel upon their entry into the land, and yet they apply both in the land and outside the land. Those three are *chadash*, *orlah*, and *kilayim*. They began with entry into the land, but once they began to apply, they apply everywhere — including *chutz la'aretz*. He thus agrees with Rabbi Eliezer that *chadash* applies in *chutz la'aretz*.

He then reasons as follows: *umah chadash she'ein issuro issur olam ve'ein issuro issur hana'ah veyesh heter l'issuro noheg bein ba'aretz bein b'chutz la'aretz* — *chadash* is the weakest of these three prohibitions. Why? Three reasons. First, *ein issuro issur olam* — the prohibition is not permanent; the grain that begins as *chadash* becomes *mutar* once the *omer* is brought. Second, *ein issuro issur hana'ah* — *chadash* is not forbidden in *hana'ah*; you may not eat it but you may derive benefit from it. Third, *veyesh heter l'issuro* — it has a mechanism of permission: not only does the *issur* end with time, but if the *omer* is brought on the sixteenth it becomes *mutar* even on the sixteenth itself — in effect, half a day early. Rashi understands *yesh heter l'issuro* as essentially a stronger formulation of the first point — that the *omer* creates a *heter* even within the *issur* period. *Tosafot* offers several alternative explanations. In any case, even with all these leniencies, *chadash* applies in *chutz la'aretz*.

*Kilayim she'issuran issur olam* — *kilayim*, by contrast, is always forbidden; it does not become permitted with time. *Ve'issuran issur hana'ah* — it is forbidden even in *hana'ah*. *Ve'ein heter l'issuran* — there is no mechanism of permission. *Eino din she'yanogu bein ba'aretz bein b'chutz la'aretz* — how much more so should *kilayim* apply in *chutz la'aretz*. And *orlah* has two of these three stringencies — it is not *issur olam* in the same sense, since while the fruit of those first three years remains permanently forbidden, the tree itself eventually yields permitted fruit — and it too therefore applies in *chutz la'aretz*.

Importantly, Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai appears to be saying not merely that *chadash*, *orlah*, and *kilayim* apply rabbinically in *chutz la'aretz*, but that they apply *de'oraita*. This is a significant point and stands in contrast to the positions that will emerge from the *Mishnah* later.

*Tosafot* raises a pointed objection from this line of reasoning. If *chadash* applies *de'oraita* in *chutz la'aretz* and is demonstrably the weakest of these three prohibitions, then by a straightforward *kal vachomer* all the other *mitzvot hatluyot ba'aretz* — *tevel*, *terumah*, *ma'aser*, and so on — should also apply *de'oraita* outside the land. *Tosafot* answers that the *kal vachomer* can be challenged: *chadash* is unique in that it cannot be annulled through a *she'eilah* — a *halakhic* inquiry dissolving the status — whereas *terumah* and *ma'aser* can in principle be reversed through such inquiry, since they acquire their status through a human act of designation. But the question is in some ways more striking than any particular answer: if one takes Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's position fully seriously, it opens the door to an expansive view of which agricultural *mitzvot* apply everywhere.

### Rabbi Elazar View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מִצְוָה שֶׁנִּצְטַוּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל קוֹדֶם כְּנִיסָתָן לָאָרֶץ – נוֹהֶגֶת בֵּין בָּאָרֶץ בֵּין בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ. לְאַחַר כְּנִיסָתָן לָאָרֶץ – אֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא בָּאָרֶץ, חוּץ מִן הַשְׁמָטַת כְּסָפִים וְשִׁילּוּחַ עֲבָדִים, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּצְטַוּוּ עֲלֵיהֶם לְאַחַר כְּנִיסָתָן לָאָרֶץ – נוֹהֶגֶת בֵּין בָּאָרֶץ בֵּין בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: Any mitzva that the Jewish people were commanded** to perform **before entering Eretz** Yisrael, i.e., it was not linked to entry into the land, **applies both in Eretz** Yisrael **and outside of Eretz** Yisrael. Conversely, any mitzva that they were commanded to perform **after they entered Eretz** Yisrael **applies only in Eretz** Yisrael, **except for** the **abrogation of monetary** debts in the Sabbatical Year (see Deuteronomy 15:1–2), **and** the **emancipation of slaves** in the Jubilee Year (see Leviticus 25:39–41). **Even though** the Jews **were commanded with regard to these** mitzvot that they were to perform them only **after their entry into Eretz** Yisrael, these mitzvot **apply both in Eretz** Yisrael **and outside of Eretz** Yisrael.

Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Shimon takes the opposing position: *kol mitzvah shenitztavu Yisrael kodem knisatam la'aretz noheget bein ba'aretz bein b'chutz la'aretz* — any *mitzvah* that applied before they entered the land is not land-dependent and therefore applies outside the land as well. But *le'achar knisatam la'aretz* — anything that applied only upon entering the land, such as *chadash*, *orlah*, and *kilayim* — *einah noheget ela ba'aretz* — applies only in the land of Israel. He thus disagrees with his father and takes the position that these three are categorically limited to the land of Israel, since they are precisely the type of obligations that came into effect only with entry and are fundamentally land-dependent.

### Exceptions: Loans & Slav…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁמָטַת כְּסָפִים חוֹבַת הַגּוּף הִיא! לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְכִדְתַנְיָא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״וְזֶה דְּבַר הַשְּׁמִטָּה שָׁמוֹט״ – בִּשְׁתֵּי שְׁמִיטוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר: אַחַת שְׁמִיטַּת קַרְקַע, וְאַחַת שְׁמִיטַּת כְּסָפִים. בִּזְמַן שֶׁאַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט קַרְקַע – אַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט כְּסָפִים, בִּזְמַן שֶׁאִי אַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט קַרְקַע – אִי אַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט כְּסָפִים. וְאֵימָא: בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט קַרְקַע – אַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט כְּסָפִים, וּבִמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין אַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט קַרְקַע – אֵין אַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט כְּסָפִים! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי קָרָא שְׁמִטָּה לַה׳״ מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the need for this ruling: **The abrogation of monetary** debts **is an obligation of the body.** Since this mitzva is not referring to the land, what is the novelty of the ruling that it applies even outside of Eretz Yisrael? The Gemara answers: **It is necessary** to mention the cancellation of debts **only for that which is taught** in a *baraita*. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The verse states in the context of the cancellation of debts: **"And this is the manner of the abrogation: He shall abrogate"** (Deuteronomy 15:2). **The verse speaks of two** types of **abrogation: One** is **the release of land and one** is the **abrogation of monetary** debts. Since the two are juxtaposed, one can learn the following: **At a time when you release land,** when the Jubilee Year is practiced, **you abrogate monetary** debts; **at a time when you do not release land,** such as the present time, when the Jubilee Year is no longer practiced, **you** also **do not abrogate monetary** debts. The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that one can learn the following from this juxtaposition: **In a place** where **you release land,** i.e., in Eretz Yisrael, **you abrogate monetary** debts, **and in a place** where **you do not release land, you do not abrogate monetary** debts. If so, the abrogation of debts would apply only in Eretz Yisrael, despite the fact that this obligation is not related to the land. Therefore, **the verse states: "Because the Lord's abrogation has been proclaimed"** (Deuteronomy 15:2), to indicate that this obligation applies **in any case,** even outside of Eretz Yisrael. This is the novelty of the statement of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon.

Rabbi Elazar does, however, identify two exceptions: *chutz min hashmattat kesafim veshiluach avadim* — the annulment of loans in the *shemittah* year and the freeing of slaves in the *yovel* — *she'af al pi shenitztavu le'achar knisatam la'aretz, noheget bein ba'aretz bein b'chutz la'aretz* — even though these were commanded with reference to entry into the land, they apply both inside and outside. The question, which the *Gemara* will now address, is: why are these two specifically carved out as exceptions? If they are personal obligations with no inherent connection to the soil, why would it even occur to anyone to limit them to the land?

## Shemittah & Release

### Loans Dependency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁילּוּחַ עֲבָדִים חוֹבַת הַגּוּף הִיא! סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב ״וּקְרָאתֶם דְּרוֹר בָּאָרֶץ״, בָּאָרֶץ – אִין, בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ – לָא, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יוֹבֵל הִיא״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בָּאָרֶץ״? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַדְּרוֹר נוֹהֵג בָּאָרֶץ – נוֹהֵג בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, אֵין דְּרוֹר נוֹהֵג בָּאָרֶץ – אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the need for the second ruling of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon: **The emancipation of slaves is** also **an obligation of the body,** not one that applies to the land. What is novel about this ruling? The Gemara answers that **it might enter your mind to say: Since it is written: "And proclaim liberty throughout the land** unto all the inhabitants thereof" (Leviticus 25:10), one could say: **"Throughout the land," yes,** this mitzva applies, but **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **no,** the emancipation does not take effect. Therefore **the** same **verse** also **states** the seemingly superfluous phrase: **"It shall be a Jubilee"** (Leviticus 25:10), to indicate that it applies **in any case,** in all places. The Gemara asks: **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "Throughout the land"?** The Gemara answers that this phrase teaches: **When the liberty** of slaves **applies in Eretz** Yisrael, it also **applies outside of Eretz** Yisrael, and when **the liberty** of slaves **does not apply in Eretz** Yisrael, **it does not apply outside** of **Eretz** Yisrael. The mitzva depends on whether the Jubilee Year is in effect, not on the place in question.

The *Gemara* asks: *hashmattat kesafim chovat haguf hi* — annulling loans is a personal obligation, so of course it applies outside the land. Why does Rabbi Elazar need to spell this out as an exception? The *Gemara* answers: *lo nitzrecha ela l'chidetanya* — the ruling is necessary because of what we learn in a *braisa*. *Rabbi omer*: *v'zeh devar hashemittah shamot* — this is the matter of *shemittah*, release — *bishtei shemittot hakatuv medaber* — the *Torah* here is speaking of two types of *shemittah*. *Achat shemitat karka ve'achat shemitat kesafim* — one is the release of the land from agricultural labor, and the other is the cancellation of loans. The principle established is: *bizman she'atah meshamet karka, atah meshamet kesafim* — when *shemitat karka* applies, *shemitat kesafim* also applies. *Bizman she'ei atah meshamet karka, ein atah meshamet kesafim* — when *shemitat karka* does not apply, neither does *shemitat kesafim*.

From this dependency, one might have reasoned: *bemakom she'atah meshamet karka atah meshamet kesafim uvemakom she'ein atah meshamet karka ein atah meshamet kesafim* — perhaps the annulment of loans applies only where *shemitat karka* is geographically practiced, namely in the land of Israel. Therefore it would be *mutar* to maintain loans in *chutz la'aretz* even during a *shemittah* year, since *shemitat karka* does not operate there. This is not an unreasonable reading; indeed, a number of *Rishonim* held that *shemitat kesafim* outside the land is not even *de'rabbanan* in a full sense but merely a *minhag chasidut*, and historically people in the Rishonic period sometimes barely bothered with writing a *pruzbul*.

Against this, the *Gemara* answers: *talmud lomar ki kara shemittah l'Hashem mikol makom* — the *Torah* teaches that *shemittah* is declared for God from every place. The word *mikol makom* signifies that the cancellation of loans operates regardless of geographic location — even in *chutz la'aretz* where *shemitat karka* does not apply. What *shemitat kesafim* does require is that *shemitat karka* is being practiced in Israel; the dependency is temporal, not geographic. When *shemittah* is operative in Israel, the annulment of loans applies everywhere. When *shemittah* is not operative in Israel — for instance, because *yovel* is not practiced, which in turn requires that *kol yoshveiha aleha* — all inhabitants dwell upon it — the annulment of loans does not apply even in Israel, let alone outside it. This condition has not been met since *Sancheriv* exiled the ten tribes, toward the end of the era of the first *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Slaves Release

Similarly, *shiluach avadim chovat haguf hi* — freeing slaves is a personal obligation, so of course it applies in *chutz la'aretz*. But the *Gemara* notes the *salka da'atach amina*: the *Torah* states *ukratem dror ba'aretz lechol yoshveiha* — you shall proclaim liberty throughout the land to all its inhabitants thereof. *Ba'aretz in, bechutz la'aretz lo* — one might have read this as limiting the freeing of slaves to the land of Israel. *Talmud lomar yovel hi mikol makom* — the *Torah* teaches that it is the *yovel*, regardless of location. *Im ken mah talmud lomar ba'aretz?* What then is the function of the word *ba'aretz*? *Bizman shehadrur noheg ba'aretz, noheg bechutz la'aretz* — when freedom is proclaimed in Israel, it applies outside Israel as well. *Bizman she'ein dror noheg ba'aretz, eino noheg bechutz la'aretz* — if *yovel* is not operative in Israel for whatever reason, it does not apply outside of Israel either. The structure is identical to *shemitat kesafim*: the obligation is not geographically confined to Israel, but it is triggered by and dependent on the practice in Israel.

### Theological Framework

This pattern invites a broader theological reflection. The *Gemara* is operating throughout with a lens that divides *mitzvot* into soil-based obligations, on one hand, and personal obligations, on the other — assuming that the former are naturally limited to Israel while the latter apply everywhere. But this framing is itself worth questioning. As Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon's formulation makes clear — and as the cases of *shemitat kesafim* and *shiluach avadim* illustrate — there is a category of *mitzvot* that are not literally soil-based but are deeply national in character, tied to an experience of Israel that generates obligations even in *chutz la'aretz*, yet only when that experience is actively taking place in Israel.

This points toward what the *Ramban* articulates: the *Torah* does not fundamentally conceive of Judaism as a universal religion whose *mitzvot* are practiced wherever one happens to live. Rather, the *Torah* and its *mitzvot* are the laws of the Israelite people living in the land of Israel. Any other state of being — exile — is not a natural or normative condition but an aberration. The *chiddush* in this framework is not that some *mitzvot* are limited to the land; it is that any *mitzvot* apply in *chutz la'aretz* at all. The question of which *mitzvot* are soil-based and therefore limited to Israel is, in a sense, the wrong starting point. The prior question is why anything should apply outside Israel.

## Status Categories

### Mishna Statuses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הֶחָדָשׁ אָסוּר מִן הַתּוֹרָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, עׇרְלָה הֲלָכָה, וְהַכִּלְאַיִם מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים. מַאי הֲלָכָה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הִלְכְתָא מְדִינָה. עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Orla* 3:9): **The new crop is forbidden by Torah law everywhere; *orla*** is forbidden outside of Eretz Yisrael according to **a *halakha*,** as the Gemara will immediately explain; **and diverse kinds** are forbidden outside the land **by rabbinic law. What** is this ***halakha*,** mentioned with regard to *orla*? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** It is the **local *halakha*,** i.e., this was the practice of Jews in places where they settled. **Ulla** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Mishnah* in *Orlah*. *Hachadash assur min hatorah bechol makom* — *chadash* is forbidden by Torah law everywhere. This *stam Mishnah* reflects the position of Rabbi Eliezer, and it is the operative *halakhic* question whether we rule in accordance with this *stam Mishnah* and apply *chadash de'oraita* in *chutz la'aretz*. *Orlah halakhah* — *orlah* is a *halakhah*, the precise meaning of which the *Gemara* will now debate. *Vekilayim midivrei soferim* — *kilayim* is rabbinic.

### Halachot Medinah

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* — Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel says that the word *halakhah* by *orlah* means *hilchot medinah* — a very interesting formulation that Rashi interprets as meaning something like an accepted practice, something the people took upon themselves in *chutz la'aretz* rather than a legislated rabbinic enactment from above. The word *noheg* captures this — it is the practice, the custom, rather than a formal decree. This would make *orlah* in *chutz la'aretz* weaker even than a standard *de'rabbanan*.

### Halacha L'Moshe

*Ulla amar Rabbi Yochanan* — Ulla citing Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *halakhah* here means *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — a tradition handed down orally from Sinai. This is weightier than a rabbinic enactment; it carries the authority of an ancient tradition even though it is not written explicitly in the *Torah*.

### Safek Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ עוּלָּא לְרַב יְהוּדָה: בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי, דְּאָמֵינָא הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי – הַיְינוּ דְּשָׁנֵי לַן בֵּין סְפֵק עׇרְלָה לִסְפֵק כִּלְאַיִם. דִּתְנַן: סְפֵק עׇרְלָה, בָּאָרֶץ – אָסוּר, בְּסוּרְיָא – מוּתָּר. בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ יוֹרֵד וְלוֹקֵחַ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִרְאֶנּוּ לוֹקֵט. וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי כִלְאַיִם תְּנַן: כֶּרֶם הַנָּטוּעַ יָרָק, וְיָרָק נִמְכָּר חוּצָה לוֹ, בָּאָרֶץ – אָסוּר, בְּסוּרְיָא – מוּתָּר, בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ – יוֹרֵד וְלוֹקֵט. וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִלְקוֹט בַּיָּד. אֲמַר לֵיהּ עוּלָּא לְרַב יְהוּדָה: בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי, דְּאָמֵינָא הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי – הַיְינוּ דְּשָׁנֵי לַן בֵּין סְפֵק עׇרְלָה לִסְפֵק כִּלְאַיִם. אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, דְּאָמֵינָא הִלְכְתָא מְדִינָה בִּשְׁנֵיהֶם, מָה שָׁנָא סְפֵק עׇרְלָה לִסְפֵק כִּלְאַיִם?
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said to Rav Yehuda: Granted, according to my** opinion, **as I say** in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan that the mishna means that this is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai, this is** the reason **why we distinguish between** fruit whose status as ***orla*** is **uncertain,** which is forbidden outside of Eretz Yisrael according to the *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **and** produce whose status as **diverse kinds** is **uncertain,** which is permitted outside of Eretz Yisrael, as the prohibition of diverse kinds applies outside of Eretz Yisrael only by rabbinic law. This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Orla* 3:9): Fruit whose status as ***orla*** is **uncertain is forbidden in Eretz** Yisrael, and **it is permitted in Syria** with no concern about its uncertain status. **Outside of Eretz** Yisrael, a Jew may **go down** into the gentile's field **and purchase** fruit that is *orla* from the gentile, **provided that** the Jew **does not see him gather** it. **Nevertheless, we learned** in a mishna **with regard to** the *halakhot* of **diverse kinds** (*Orla* 3:9): If **a vineyard has vegetables planted in it, and** there are **vegetables being sold outside** the vineyard, but there is no proof that these vegetables came from the vineyard, **in Eretz** Yisrael they are **forbidden.** The reason is that as it is possible that the vegetables came from the vineyard, the *halakha* is stringent in a case involving a prohibition by Torah law. **In Syria** these vegetables are **permitted. Outside of Eretz** Yisrael, if the gentile owner of a field containing diverse kinds **goes down** into his field **and gathers** produce, then, **provided that** the Jew **does not gather** it **by hand,** he may purchase the produce from the gentile. This indicates that outside of Eretz Yisrael there is a difference between *orla* of uncertain status and diverse kinds of uncertain status. This is understandable if *orla* is forbidden by a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai, while diverse kinds are forbidden by rabbinic law. **But according to your** opinion, that neither *orla* nor diverse kinds is forbidden by Torah law, **what is the difference between** fruit whose status as ***orla*** is **uncertain** and produce whose status as **diverse kinds** is **uncertain?**

The practical difference between these two positions is illustrated by comparing the laws of *safek orlah* and *safek kilayim* in *chutz la'aretz*. *Safek orlah* in the land of Israel is forbidden — *safek de'oraita lechumra*. In *Surya*, which has a certain intermediate status due to the conquests of *David HaMelech*, *safek orlah* is *mutar*. And in *chutz la'aretz*, not only is *safek* permitted but one may even go down and purchase from the orchard owner even knowing that some of the trees are *orlah* and some are not — *yored velokeach* — as long as one does not directly witness the seller picking specifically from the *orlah* tree. This represents a significant leniency, almost resembling a form of willful ignorance.

Regarding *kilayim* in *chutz la'aretz*, the *Mishnah* in *Kilayim* describes a case of a vineyard planted with vegetables, and a vegetable stand selling from what appears to come from inside the vineyard, right at the edge of the public road. In the land of Israel this is forbidden. In *Surya* it is *mutar*. In *chutz la'aretz*, one may even observe the seller picking from the mixed planting directly, as long as one does not pick it oneself — *yored veloket ubilvad shelo yilkot beyad*. This is a striking leniency: even witnessing the act of picking directly from what is almost certainly *kilayim* does not prevent purchase, as long as there remains some degree of personal remove.

Ulla's argument is: *bishlama l'didi de'amina halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — if *orlah* is *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* and *kilayim* is *de'rabbanan*, it makes sense that there would be different thresholds for leniency in cases of *safek*. A *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* is weightier than a *de'rabbanan*, and the levels of stringency in the *safek* cases reflect this difference. *Ela l'didach* — but according to Rav Yehuda, who says *orlah* is *hilchot medinah* — a practice even weaker than a *de'rabbanan* — why would there be any different standards at all? Both should be at the same level of leniency. The fact that there is a discernible difference in *halachic* stringency between *safek orlah* and *safek kilayim* in *chutz la'aretz* thus supports Ulla's position that *orlah* is *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* and not merely an accepted regional practice. This point will be developed further in the next *daf*.
