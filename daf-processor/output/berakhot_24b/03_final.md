# Berakhot 24b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Chanina & Conduct

### Rabbi's Bodily Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֲנִי רָאִיתִי אֶת רַבִּי שֶׁגִּיהֵק וּפִיהֵק וְנִתְעַטֵּשׁ וְרָק וּמְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בְּבִגְדוֹ, אֲבָל לֹא הָיָה מִתְעַטֵּף. וּכְשֶׁהוּא מְפַהֵק הָיָה מַנִּיחַ יָדוֹ עַל סַנְטֵרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina said: I saw Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, while he was praying, **belch, yawn, sneeze, spit, and if he was stung by a louse, he may feel** for it and remove it **with his garment, but he would not wrap himself** in his prayer shawl if it fell during prayer. **And when he would yawn he would place his hand on his chin** so that his open mouth would not be visible.

The daf opens with a remarkable report from Rabbi Chanina: *ani ra'iti et Rebbe* — I saw Rebbe himself engage in a series of behaviors that might appear jarring in the context of prayer. Specifically, Rebbe would *gihek u-pihek* — burp and yawn — *nitatesh*, sneeze, *raq*, spit, and *mimashmesh b'vgadav*, feel along his garments — which Rashi understands as checking for lice. All of this occurred during *davening*. Yet there is one thing Rebbe would not do: if his *tallis* fell off, he would not pick it up and re-wrap it, as that would have constituted an interruption. Tosafos clarifies that *mimashmesh b'vgadav* refers to straightening the *tallis*, but not picking it up once it had fallen entirely. And when Rebbe would yawn, he would place his hand over his mouth — a gesture of proper *derech eretz* before the One he was addressing.

### Burping vs. Sneezing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַמַּשְׁמִיעַ קוֹלוֹ בִּתְפִלָּתוֹ — הֲרֵי זֶה מִקְּטַנֵּי אֲמָנָה. הַמַּגְבִּיהַּ קוֹלוֹ בִּתְפִלָּתוֹ הֲרֵי זֶה מִנְּבִיאֵי הַשֶּׁקֶר. מְגַהֵק וּמְפַהֵק — הֲרֵי זֶה מִגַּסֵּי הָרוּחַ. הַמִּתְעַטֵּשׁ בִּתְפִלָּתוֹ — סִימָן רַע לוֹ. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: נִיכָּר שֶׁהוּא מְכוֹעָר. הָרָק בִּתְפִלָּתוֹ — כְּאִילּוּ רָק בִּפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on a *baraita*: **One who sounds his voice during his** *Amida* **prayer is among those of little faith,** as he seems to believe that the Lord cannot hear his prayer when it is uttered silently. **One who raises his voice during prayer is** considered to be **among the false prophets,** as they too were wont to cry out and shout to their gods. Furthermore, one who **belches and yawns** while praying **is surely among the uncouth. One who sneezes during his prayer, for him it is a bad omen. And some say: It is clear that he is repulsive.** Also, **one who spits during prayer, it is tantamount to spitting in the face of the king.** In light of all this, how could Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi have done all that while praying?

This account immediately raises a difficulty, for the *Gemara* has a *baraita* that enumerates a series of prohibitions during prayer. *Hamashmia kolo b'tefilato harei zeh mikatnei amana* — one who makes his voice heard during prayer is as one who lacks sufficient faith, implying that God requires audible speech to hear one's petition. Going further: *hamagbi'a kolo b'tefilato harei zeh minevi'ei ha-sheker* — one who actually raises his voice is likened to false prophets, as Rashi notes by citing the verse *Vayiqre'u be-qol gadol*, the great shouting of the false prophets. The same *baraita* states that *migahek u-mifahek* — burping and yawning during prayer — constitutes arrogance, as if one were indifferent to standing in God's presence. *Harak b'tefilato k'ilu rak bifnei hamelech* — spitting during prayer is like spitting before the king. The question presses itself: how could Rebbe have done all of these things?

### Burping vs. Sneezing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מְגַהֵק וּמְפַהֵק לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן לְאוֹנְסוֹ, כָּאן לִרְצוֹנוֹ. אֶלָּא מִתְעַטֵּשׁ אַמִּתְעַטֵּשׁ קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Granted,** with regard to **one who belches and yawns, it is not difficult: Here,** in the case where Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi did so, **it was involuntary** and therefore permissible; **here,** where it is considered uncouth, is in a case where **it is deliberate.** However, the contradiction **between sneezing** in the case where Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi did so **and sneezing** where it is considered a bad omen **is difficult.**

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction in stages. Regarding burping and yawning, it says *bishlama migahek u-mifahek lo kashya, kan l'ons'o kan l'retzono* — there is no difficulty, because one case refers to voluntary burping and yawning and the other to involuntary. If one cannot control it, Rebbe's behavior was entirely appropriate; the prohibition applies only where one has a choice.

The harder case is sneezing. The *Gemara* assumes that a sneeze, unlike a burp, is typically something one cannot hold back. How, then, can the *baraita* treat *mitatesh* as problematic? The answer: *mitatesh a-mitatesh nami lo kashya, kan l'mala kan l'mata* — the two instances of *mitatesh* refer to different things entirely. One refers to sneezing from above — through the nose — and the other to passing gas from below. Even though passing gas may be just as involuntary as sneezing, it is still characterized as a *siman ra* or as *mechuar* — repulsive. This is not a moral condemnation of what cannot be controlled, but a recognition that the experience is still unseemly and carries a negative connotation. This becomes a significant practical issue for those who struggle, particularly in morning prayers, with flatulence, and the *Gemara* will develop practical guidance accordingly.

### Sneezing Good Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִתְעַטֵּשׁ אַמִּתְעַטֵּשׁ נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן מִלְּמַעְלָה, כָּאן מִלְּמַטָּה. דְּאָמַר רַב זֵירָא: הָא מִילְּתָא אִבַּלְעָא לִי בֵּי רַב הַמְנוּנָא וּתְקִילָא לִי כִּי כּוּלֵּי תַּלְמוּדַאי: הַמִּתְעַטֵּשׁ בִּתְפִלָּתוֹ סִימָן יָפֶה לוֹ, כְּשֵׁם שֶׁעוֹשִׂים לוֹ נַחַת רוּחַ מִלְּמַטָּה, כָּךְ עוֹשִׂים לוֹ נַחַת רוּחַ מִלְּמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The contradiction **between sneezing** in one case **and sneezing** in the other case **is also not difficult: Here,** in the case of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, it is referring to sneezing **from above,** his nose; **here,** where it is a bad omen, is referring to sneezing **from below,** flatulence. **As Rav Zeira said: In the school of Rav Hamnuna I absorbed this matter** in passing, **and it is equal** in significance **to all the rest of my learning: One who sneezes in the midst of prayer, it is a good omen for him. Just as** the sneeze soothes his irritation, **giving him pleasure below,** it is a sign that **they are similarly giving him pleasure above.** Since Rav Zeira sneezed often, he was extremely pleased to hear this.

Rav Zeira offers a striking counterpoint. He says that this teaching was something he either received directly or heard discussed in the academy of Rav Hamnuna, and he gave it the weight of his entire learning: *hamisatesh bitfilaso siman yafe lo* — one who sneezes during prayer, this is a good sign for him. Just as one is made to feel a pleasant relief below, so too are they granted a measure of divine favor from above. The sneezing, invigorating and cleansing as it is, signals that one's prayers are being received well. This confirms that the negative usage of *mitatesh* in the earlier *baraita* referred to passing gas, not sneezing, and that even the involuntary nature of passing gas does not remove its status as problematic.

### Spitting Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רָק אַרָק קַשְׁיָא! רָק אַרָק נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא, אֶפְשָׁר כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָיָה עוֹמֵד בִּתְפִילָּה וְנִזְדַּמֵּן לוֹ רוֹק — מַבְלִיעוֹ בְּטַלִּיתוֹ. וְאִם טַלִּית נָאֶה הוּא — מַבְלִיעוֹ בַּאֲפַרְקְסוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** The contradiction **between spitting** in the case where Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi did so **and spitting** where it is deemed tantamount to spitting in the face of the king **is difficult.** The Gemara replies: The contradiction **between spitting** in one case **and spitting** in the other case **is also not difficult,** as **it is possible** to resolve it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda,** as **Rav Yehuda said: One who was standing in prayer, and saliva happened** to accumulate in **his** mouth, **he absorbs it in his garment. And if it was a fine garment** and he does not want it to become sullied, **he may cover it up in his head covering.** That way, one is permitted to spit.

The *Gemara* now turns to spitting. Unlike burping, yawning, or passing gas, spitting is clearly voluntary — one can choose what to do with saliva. The *baraita* states unequivocally that spitting during prayer is *k'ilu rak bifnei hamelech*, like spitting before a king. How, then, did Rebbe spit? The *Gemara* answers: *efshar k'deRav Yehuda* — it was possible to handle it as Rav Yehuda instructed. Rav Yehuda taught that if one is standing in *davening* and has saliva collecting in the mouth that must be addressed, *mavli'o b'talito* — one absorbs it into one's cloak. If the cloak is a fine garment one does not wish to soil, one may use the *afarkasuto*, a type of headscarf, instead. The *baraita*'s condemnation presumably applies when this solution was available and ignored; Rebbe's spitting must have occurred when neither option was viable. The point is also that swallowing accumulated saliva can itself be objectionable, so the act of spitting is not entirely a matter of free choice.

### Ravina's Spitting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא הֲוָה קָאֵי אֲחוֹרֵי רַב אָשֵׁי, נִזְדַּמֵּן לוֹ רוֹק, פַּתְקֵיהּ לַאֲחוֹרֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר לְהָא דְּרַב יְהוּדָה מַבְלִיעוֹ בַּאֲפַרְקְסוּתוֹ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא אֲנִינָא דַּעְתַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** That Way, one is permitted to spit. The Gemara relates: **Ravina was standing behind Rav Ashi** during prayer when **saliva happened** to accumulate in **his** mouth, **so he discharged it behind him.** Rav Ashi **said to him:** And **does the Master not hold** in accordance with **that** statement of **Rav Yehuda, who said** that **one absorbs it in his head covering? He said to him: I am delicate,** and the mere knowledge that there is spittle in my head covering disturbs my prayer.

A related incident involves Ravina, who was standing behind Rav Ashi during prayer. When saliva collected, Ravina spat behind himself rather than into a garment. Rav Ashi asked: was Rav Yehuda's ruling not available to him — to absorb it in the *afarkasuto*? Ravina replied: *ana anina da'atai* — I am fastidious; I am not willing to spit into any of the garments I am wearing. This fastidiousness may well have been Rebbe's reason as well. One might note, parenthetically, that the sources speak of cloths such as a *sudar karuch arasha* — a head-cloth wound around the head — but not of handkerchiefs as a separate object, which appears not to have been standard equipment.

## Loud Prayer & Faith

### Voice in Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּשְׁמִיעַ קוֹלוֹ בִּתְפִלָּתוֹ הֲרֵי זֶה מִקְּטַנֵּי אֲמָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in a *baraita*: **One who sounds his voice during his** *Amida* **prayer is among those of little faith,** as he seems to believe that the Lord cannot hear his prayer when it is uttered silently.

The *Gemara* returns to the *baraita*: *Hamashmia kolo b'tefilato harei ze m'katnei amana* — one who makes his voice audible in prayer is characterized as lacking faith. The implication is that voicing one's prayer loudly suggests a belief that God can only hear what is spoken aloud.

### Whispering Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ, אֶלָּא שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְכַוֵּון אֶת לִבּוֹ בְּלַחַשׁ, אֲבָל אֵין יָכוֹל לְכַוֵּון אֶת לִבּוֹ בְּלַחַשׁ — מוּתָּר. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי, בְּיָחִיד, אֲבָל בְּצִיבּוּר — אָתֵי לְמִיטְרַד צִיבּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: This was only taught** in a case **where one is able to focus his heart** while praying silently, **but** if he **is unable to focus his heart** while praying **silently, he is permitted** to sound his voice. **This applies only to** one praying **alone,** but when he is praying **in a congregation** his voice **will come to disturb the congregation** and it is prohibited.

Rav Huna qualifies this sharply: *lo shanu ella sheyachol l'kaven es libo b'lachash* — the prohibition was only stated for one who is capable of achieving proper concentration while praying in a complete whisper. But *eino yachol l'kaven es libo b'lachash* — if someone genuinely cannot focus unless they can hear their own words, then making one's voice slightly audible is *mutar*, permitted.

This distinction between *yachid* and *tzibur* is worth reflecting on carefully. It is not simply a matter of whether one physically disturbs the person standing nearby. The dividing line is whether one is praying as *yachid* or as *tzibur* — and the gap between them is more than numerical. It may reflect a deeper conceptual difference about what kind of experience *tefillah* is meant to be. If the emphasis is on *tefillah* as a deeply personal outpouring — *kavana*, concentration, interior focus — then accommodating personal needs for vocalization makes sense in private. But if *tefillah* in *shul* is understood as a collective, shared experience — a communal standing before God together — then certain external forms and decorum of that communal experience sometimes take precedence over what any individual might do to improve their personal *kavana*. The person who wants to sway intensely or vocalize loudly to concentrate better is welcome to do so at home; the *shul* creates a collective experience whose integrity must be maintained. This is a theme that will develop further as the *Gemara* continues.

## Forced Redemption

### Rav Yehuda Anti-Aliyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַבָּא הֲוָה קָא מִשְׁתְּמִיט מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה דַּהֲוָה קָא בָּעֵי לְמִיסַּק לְאַרְעָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כׇּל הָעוֹלֶה מִבָּבֶל לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל — עוֹבֵר בַּעֲשֵׂה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּבֶלָה יוּבָאוּ וְשָׁמָּה יִהְיוּ עַד יוֹם פׇּקְדִי אוֹתָם נְאֻם ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Abba was avoiding** being seen **by** his teacher **Rav Yehuda,** as Rabbi Abba **sought to ascend to Eretz Yisrael** and his teacher disapproved, **as Rav Yehuda said: Anyone who ascends from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael transgresses a positive commandment, as it is stated: "They shall be taken to Babylonia and there they shall remain until the day that I recall them, said the Lord"** (Jeremiah 27:22).

The *Gemara* introduces a narrative that opens with a halachic controversy of significant scope. Rabbi Abba was deliberately avoiding Rav Yehuda, his *rebbe*, because he wished to make *aliyah* to Eretz Yisrael and did not want to be confronted. Rav Yehuda had taken an extreme position: *kol ha'oleh miBavel le'Eretz Yisrael over be'aseh* — anyone who goes up from Babylon to the Land of Israel transgresses a positive commandment. His source is the verse *Bavela yuva'u, veshama yihyu, ad yom pokdi otam, ne'um Hashem* — they will be brought to Babylon, and there they will remain, until the day I redeem them, says God. The position holds that divine redemption must come on its own terms, through miraculous intervention, and any attempt to force one's return constitutes a violation of God's design.

This view is the ancient ancestor of later positions such as the *Neturei Karta*, who more typically cite the *Gemara* in Ketubot regarding the *Shalosh Shevuot* — the three oaths — one of which is that Israel would not force its way back to the land. The same underlying concern surfaced even at the time of the Maccabees, when a group called the *Chassidim* refused to take up arms, waiting for divine deliverance alone. The contemporary form of this argument is well-known; the point here is simply that this tension has deep roots in the tradition itself.

### Rabbi Abba's Escape

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: אֵיזִיל וְאֶשְׁמַע מִינֵּיהּ מִילְּתָא מִבֵּית וַועֲדָא, וַהֲדַר אֶפֹּיק.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abba did not want to discuss his desire to emigrate with Rav Yehuda. Nevertheless **he said: I will go and hear something from him at the hall** where the Sages **assemble,** without being seen, **and afterwards I will leave** Babylonia.

Wanting to learn *Torah* without being seen, Rabbi Abba devised a plan: *eizil ve'eshma minei milta mibei va'ada* — let me go and overhear teaching from the *beit midrash*, the house of study, without being detected. He would eavesdrop at the door, hear what was being taught, and then depart to catch his passage to Israel. What he heard there was a *tanna* — one of the professional memorizers of *braisos* and *meimros* — reciting a teaching before Rav Yehuda.

### Four Cubits Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְתַנָּא דְּקָתָנֵי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: הָיָה עוֹמֵד בִּתְפִלָּה וְנִתְעַטֵּשׁ — מַמְתִּין עַד שֶׁיִּכְלֶה הָרוּחַ וְחוֹזֵר וּמִתְפַּלֵּל. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: הָיָה עוֹמֵד בִּתְפִלָּה וּבִיקֵּשׁ לְהִתְעַטֵּשׁ — מַרְחִיק לְאַחֲרָיו אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וּמִתְעַטֵּשׁ, וּמַמְתִּין עַד שֶׁיִּכְלֶה הָרוּחַ, וְחוֹזֵר וּמִתְפַּלֵּל, וְאוֹמֵר: ״רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, יְצַרְתָּנוּ נְקָבִים נְקָבִים חֲלוּלִים חֲלוּלִים, גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לְפָנֶיךָ חֶרְפָּתֵנוּ וּכְלִימָּתֵנוּ בְּחַיֵּינוּ וּבְאַחֲרִיתֵנוּ רִמָּה וְתוֹלֵעָה״, וּמַתְחִיל מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁפָּסַק.
>
> **Translation:** **He went and found the *tanna*,** who recites the tannaitic sources before the study hall, **reciting** the following *baraita* **before Rav Yehuda: One who was standing in prayer and sneezed** from below **waits until the odor dissipates and resumes praying. Some say: One who was standing in prayer when he felt the need to sneeze** from below, **retreats four cubits, sneezes, waits until the odor dissipates and resumes praying. And** before resuming his prayer, **he says: Master of the universe, You have formed us with many orifices and cavities; our disgrace and shame in life are clear and evident before You, as is our destiny with maggots and worms,** and so we should not be judged harshly. **And he resumes** his prayer **from where he stopped.**

The teaching addressed directly what we discussed earlier: *ha'omed bitefillato venitathesh* — if one is standing in *davening* and passes gas. Given that this is a *siman ra* yet cannot always be controlled, what is the practical *halacha*? The first ruling: *mamtin ad sheyikhleh haruach, vechozer umitpallel* — one waits until the smell dissipates, and then resumes *davening*.

A second opinion addresses a slightly different case: *ha'omed bitefillah uvikeish lehit'athesh* — if one is in the middle of *davening* and senses that flatulence is imminent, before it has actually occurred. In this case, *marchik l'acharo arba amos* — one steps back four *amos* from one's place, allows it to pass, waits for the smell to dissipate, and then returns. This is a genuine *halacha* that many are unfamiliar with: although during *Shemoneh Esrei* one ordinarily does not move, there are circumstances under which movement is permitted. The four *amos* represent the boundaries of one's prayer space; stepping out is akin to excusing oneself from that space and then re-entering it. This principle — that one can exit and re-enter one's *arba amos* under appropriate circumstances — has broader applications as well, including, for instance, the case where a cell phone belonging to someone in the midst of *Shemoneh Esrei* goes off with the device across the room; there is reason to think one may move to silence it and then resume.

### Embarrassment Prayer

Upon returning to prayer, the *Gemara* states, *ve'omer leyad be'emtzah tefillato* — one says, right there in the middle of *Shemoneh Esrei*, before resuming: *Ribbono Shel Olam, yatzartanu nekavim nekavim chalulim chalulim* — Master of the Universe, You have fashioned us with many openings and chambers — language drawn from the *berachah* recited after leaving the bathroom — *galui veyadua lefanecha boshtenu uchlimatenu bechayenu uv'acharitenu lerimah vetole'ah* — it is known before You, our shame and our embarrassment in life, and in death we become worms and maggots. The prayer acknowledges the full weight of human bodily reality: this is not merely an apology but a recognition that these functions are part of how we were created, and one then resumes from the place where one left off.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִילּוּ לֹא בָּאתִי אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוֹעַ דָּבָר זֶה — דַּיִּי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abba **said to him: Had I only come** to the assembly of the Sages **to hear this teaching, it would have been sufficient for me.**

Rav Yehuda's reaction upon hearing this through the door was unambiguous: *ilu lo bati ela lishmoah davar zeh dayi* — if I had come to the *beit midrash* only to hear this one teaching, it would have been worth it entirely. And then he went to Israel.

## Proper Dress

### Sleeping Under Cloak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיָה יָשֵׁן בְּטַלִּיתוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ מִפְּנֵי הַצִּנָּה — חוֹצֵץ בְּטַלִּיתוֹ עַל צַוָּארוֹ, וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: עַל לִבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** One who **was sleeping** unclothed, but was covered **with his garment, and he is unable to stick his head out** from under the garment **because of the cold,** may **form a barrier with his garment at his neck and recite *Shema*** in bed. **And some say:** He must form a barrier with his garment **at his heart.**

The *Gemara* continues with a *baraita* on the proper conditions for reciting *Kriyat Shema*: *haya yashen betalito* — one who is sleeping wrapped in one's cloak, using it as a blanket, and *eino yachol lehotzi et rosho mipnei hatzinah* — cannot bear to take one's head out from under the covers because of the bitter cold. The person is naked beneath the garment. Were their head outside and the cloak draped over the body like a toga, there would be no issue of nakedness. But fully beneath the covering, one is naked, and the question is whether *Shema* may be recited.

The solution offered: *chotzetz betalito al tzavaro vekoreh* — one bunches up the cloak around the neck so as to create a division between the head and the body, even while remaining entirely under the covering, thereby forming two distinct sections. One may then recite *Kriyat Shema*.

### Separation Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא הֲרֵי לִבּוֹ רוֹאֶה אֶת הָעֶרְוָה! קָסָבַר לִבּוֹ רוֹאֶה אֶת הָעֶרְוָה — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the first *tanna*,** shouldn't he be prohibited from reciting *Shema* because **his heart sees** his **nakedness,** as there is no barrier between them? The Gemara responds: Indeed, the first *tanna* **holds that** when **one's heart sees** his **nakedness, it is permitted** to recite *Shema*, and a barrier is only necessary to separate between his mouth and his nakedness.

A second opinion, *veyesh omrim al libo*, disagrees about where the separation must be formed. It is not enough to separate the head from the rest of the body; the division must be *al libo* — at the heart, between the heart and the lower regions. This is the concept of *libo roeh et ha'ervah* — the heart seeing the genitals — which underlies, among other things, the *halacha* of wearing a *gartel* for *davening*. Today the practical ruling is that even the elastic of one's undergarments constitutes a sufficient division. The *tanna kamma* who requires only a head-to-body separation is challenged: *harei libo roeh et ha'ervah* — the heart is still in the same space as the *ervah*. The *tanna kamma* responds, in effect, that *libo roeh et ha'ervah mutar* — this is not considered a problem.

### Heart Sees Genitals

Rav Huna elaborates on the specific applicability of the principle of *libo roeh et ha'ervah*. One might ask whether this concern applies only to *Kriyat Shema* or extends to *berachot* and *Shemoneh Esrei* as well. The assumption is that *libo roeh et ha'ervah* does indeed apply to *berachot* and *tefillah* as well, and it raises real questions in other contexts — for example, the woman immersing in a *mikvah* who recites the *berachah* with her head outside the water while her body is submerged, which involves related issues.

## Filthy Courtyards

### Hand Over Mouth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בִּמְבוֹאוֹת הַמְטוּנָּפוֹת — מַנִּיחַ יָדוֹ עַל פִּיו וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who was walking in alleyways filthy** with human excrement and he must recite *Shema*, **he places his hand over his mouth and recites *Shema*.**

The *Gemara* records a ruling in the name of Rav Huna citing Rabbi Yochanan: *hai mahlah b'mavo'ot hemetunafot* — one who is walking through filthy courtyards, meaning places with excrement or rancid smells — *maniach yado al piv v'koreh Kriyat Shema* — places one's hand over one's mouth and recites *Shema*. It is worth pausing to note something broader about these cases. We are dealing repeatedly with people reciting *Shema* in circumstances far removed from a synagogue setting — someone under a blanket still naked, someone walking through foul alleyways. This reflects the *Gemara*'s understanding that *Shema* is to be recited in the context of *uvelechticha vaderech* — as you walk on the road — and one does not always have the luxury of reciting it in ideal circumstances, especially if one is bound by the deadline of sunrise for the morning *Shema* and must say it wherever one happens to be. The cases do not appear to be the standard *Shema-Shemoneh Esrei* package at all. Regarding the act of covering one's mouth — it is not entirely clear whether this is about preventing the words of holiness from entering the defiled air or perhaps about the sense of smell, since much is indeed sensed through the mouth as well.

### Rav Chisda Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא: הָאֱלֹהִים! אִם אַמְרָהּ לִי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בְּפוּמֵּיהּ — לָא צָיֵיתְנָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: By God!** Even **if Rabbi Yoḥanan had said it to me** directly, **with his own mouth, I would not have obeyed him.**

Rav Chisda rejects this ruling in the most emphatic terms: *Ha-Elohim, im amar li Rabbi Yochanan befumei, lo tzeisina lei* — by God, even if Rabbi Yochanan had said this to me directly from his own mouth, I would not listen to it. This formulation appears twice, once in the name of Rabbi Yochanan and once in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — both with the same formula, and with the same rejection. It should be noted that the *Gemara*'s phrasing itself plays on the theme of speech and the mouth: *befumei* — from his own mouth — directly echoes the concern about covering one's mouth.

### Torah Scholars Banned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַב הוּנָא הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: תַּלְמִיד חָכָם אָסוּר לוֹ לַעֲמוֹד בִּמְקוֹם הַטִּנּוֹפֶת לְפִי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לַעֲמוֹד בְּלִי הִרְהוּר תּוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: **Did Rav Huna** really **say that? Didn't Rav Huna say: A Torah scholar is prohibited from standing in a place of filth, as he is unable to stand without contemplating Torah,** and uttering *Shema* orally is graver than mere contemplation.

The source of Rav Chisda's objection becomes clear from another ruling of Rav Huna: *talmid chacham asur lo la'amod bimkom hatinofet lefi she'efshar la'amod bli hirhur Torah* — a Torah scholar is forbidden even to stand in a place of filth, because it is impossible for such a person to stand anywhere without thoughts of Torah arising. If merely thinking Torah in such a place is problematic — and if even thinking is forbidden, let alone speaking — then how does covering one's mouth help at all?

### Standing vs. Walking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בְּעוֹמֵד, כָּאן בִּמְהַלֵּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** This **is not difficult; here,** Rav Huna prohibited contemplating Torah **in** a case where one is **standing** in a place of filth, while **here** he permitted to recite *Shema* **in** a case where one is **walking** through a place of filth.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *lo kashya, kan b'omeid kan bimhaleich* — there is no contradiction, because the cases are distinct. Standing in a filthy place is one thing: you are present there, you are identified with that space, and Torah thought is forbidden. Walking through is another: you are not defined by that space, you are merely passing, and in that case one may indeed recite *Shema* with one's hand over one's mouth.

This principle is then complicated by a story about Rabbi Abba following Rabbi Yochanan. Rabbi Abba was reciting *Shema* as they walked, and when they passed through filthy courtyards he fell silent. After they had passed through, he asked Rabbi Yochanan: *leheichan achazor* — where do I return to? Rabbi Yochanan replied: *im shehah k'dei ligmor et kullah chozer l'rosh* — if you waited the time it would have taken to complete the whole thing, go back to the beginning. But there is an implicit tension: Rabbi Yochanan had apparently permitted reciting *Shema* while walking through such places, yet here he is implicitly accepting that Rabbi Abba stopped. The *Gemara* explains: *l'didi lo svira li* — in my own view, I hold that you did not need to stop. But since you held that you must stop, I am addressing your question from within your own position.

### Sneezing Omen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּכָל מָקוֹם מוּתָּר לְהַרְהֵר בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה, חוּץ מִבֵּית הַמֶּרְחָץ וּמִבֵּית הַכִּסֵּא. וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכָא נָמֵי כָּאן בְּעוֹמֵד כָּאן בִּמְהַלֵּךְ — אִינִי, וְהָא רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ הֲוָה קָא אָזֵיל בָּתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וַהֲוָה קָא קָרֵי קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. כִּי מְטָא בִּמְבוֹאוֹת הַמְטוּנָּפוֹת אִשְׁתִּיק. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְהֵיכָן אֶהְדַּר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם שָׁהִיתָ כְּדֵי לִגְמוֹר אֶת כּוּלָּהּ — חֲזוֹר לָרֹאשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Did Rabbi Yoḥanan** really **say that? Didn't Rabba bar bar Ḥana say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One is permitted to contemplate matters of Torah everywhere, except the bathhouse and the bathroom?** Consequently, it is prohibited to even contemplate Torah in a place of filth. **And if you say: Here, too,** there is a distinction between the two cases, **here,** Rabbi Yoḥanan prohibited contemplating Torah **in** a case where one is **standing; here,** Rabbi Yoḥanan permitted to recite *Shema* **in** a case where one is **walking, is that so? Wasn't Rabbi Abbahu walking after Rabbi Yoḥanan and reciting *Shema*,** and **when he reached a filthy alleyway he fell silent** and stopped reciting *Shema*. When they emerged, Rabbi Abbahu **said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: To where** in *Shema* **should I return** and resume reciting it? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: If you delayed** continuing *Shema* for an interval **sufficient to complete the entire** *Shema*, **return to the beginning** and recite it from there.

The *Gemara* here also introduces an interpretation of Rabbi Yonatan, who reads certain *pesukim* to draw a connection between *dibur* — speech — and its proper context. One who continues reciting *Shema* in an inappropriate place is characterized as taking a *mitzvah* and making it a vehicle for transgression, invoking the verse *V'gam ani natati lahem chukim lo tovim u-mishpatim lo yichyu bahem* — I have given them laws that are not good and ordinances by which they shall not live. Rav Assi adds: *hoy moshchei he-avon b'chavlei ha-shav* — woe to those who draw iniquity upon themselves with ropes of nothingness. Rashi explains: the sin was so easily avoidable — simply cease reciting — and yet the person brought it upon themselves with almost no effort at all. The plays on the word *davar* — both God's word and speech — complete the theme: protect the *devar Hashem* and it will protect you; bring it into filth and you have denigrated it.

## Excrement Rules (III)

### Huna vs. Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא. תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא: הַמְהַלֵּךְ בִּמְבוֹאוֹת הַמְטוּנָּפוֹת — מַנִּיחַ יָדוֹ עַל פִּיו וְיִקְרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בִּמְבוֹאוֹת הַמְטוּנָּפוֹת — לֹא יִקְרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁאִם הָיָה קוֹרֵא וּבָא — פּוֹסֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites tannaitic sources to corroborate both the lenient and the stringent opinions. **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Huna, **and** it is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Ḥisda. It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Huna: One who was walking in filthy alleyways places his hand over his mouth and recites *Shema*. It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Ḥisda: One who was walking in filthy alleyways may not recite *Shema*. Furthermore, if he was in the course of reciting** *Shema* when he reached a filthy alleyway, **he stops** his recitation at that point.

The *Gemara* now presents two parallel *baraisos*, one supporting Rav Huna and one supporting Rav Chisda. The version supporting Rav Huna states: *haya mahalech bimvo'ot hametunafot, meniach yado al piv v'koreh Kriyat Shema* — one who is walking through filthy courtyards may place one's hand over one's mouth and recite *Shema*, which is exactly Rav Huna's position. The version supporting Rav Chisda states: *haya mahalech bimvo'ot hametunafot lo yikra Kriyat Shema* — walking through such courtyards, one may not recite *Shema* at all. Moreover, *v'lo od*, not only that, but if one was already in the middle of reciting *Shema* and entered such a place, *posek* — one must stop entirely. What if one did not stop? Rabbi Yashia bar brei d'Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi cites the verse *v'gam ani natati lahem chukim lo tovim u-mishpatim lo yichyu bahem* — I gave them laws not good and ordinances by which they shall not live — which carries the weight of a severe condemnation. Rav Adda bar Ahava adds: *ki dvar Hashem bazah* — that person has scorned the word of God by bringing it into that space. Conversely, the *Gemara* notes that one who interrupts is rewarded, as the verse states *uvadavar hazeh ta'arichu yamim* — with this *davar* you will have long days. The *Gemara* thus leaves us with the distinction between Rav Huna and Rav Chisda unresolved through a single definitive ruling, but with the clear implication that the more stringent position — which prohibits even walking through while reciting — is at least equally well-grounded.

Rav Huna also teaches separately that if one's cloak is bound at the waist, covering the lower body even without covering the torso, this suffices for *Kriyat Shema*, since nakedness in the minimal sense is covered. A *baraita* confirms this: whether cloth, leather, or sackcloth, if it is bound at the loins, one may recite *Shema*. However, for *tefillah* the standard is higher: *ad sheyechasah libo* — the heart must be covered as well. Rashi explains directly: *aval letfillah tzarich leharot et atzmo ke'omed lifnei hamelech vela'amod be'eimah* — for prayer one must present oneself as standing before a king, with awe. *Aval Kriyat Shema eino metzapeh lifnei hamelech* — for *Kriyat Shema*, one is not standing before the king in that same sense. In *Kriyat Shema* one is not in conversation with God; one is making a declaration of faith, speaking to oneself, accepting the yoke of heaven. This is the practical expression of *uvelechticha vaderech* — and as you walk on the road. The difference in required dress reflects a genuine conceptual difference between the two.

### Excrement Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יִקְרָא אָדָם קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, לֹא כְּנֶגֶד צוֹאַת אָדָם, וְלֹא כְּנֶגֶד צוֹאַת כְּלָבִים, וְלֹא כְּנֶגֶד צוֹאַת חֲזִירִים, וְלֹא כְּנֶגֶד צוֹאַת תַּרְנְגוֹלִים, וְלֹא כְּנֶגֶד צוֹאַת אַשְׁפָּה שֶׁרֵיחָהּ רָע. וְאִם הָיָה מָקוֹם גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים אוֹ נָמוּךְ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — יוֹשֵׁב בְּצִדּוֹ וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. וְאִם לָאו — מַרְחִיק מִמֶּנּוּ מְלוֹא עֵינָיו. וְכֵן לִתְפִלָּה. רֵיחַ רַע שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ עִיקָּר — מַרְחִיק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת מִמְּקוֹם הָרֵיחַ, וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The operative concern in this framing is not primarily the smell but the visual presence of the filth within one's space. Only then does the four-*amos* smell rule enter: **A person may not recite *Shema* opposite human excrement, dog excrement, pig excrement, chicken excrement, a foul-smelling dung-heap** or anything repulsive. **However, if** the filth **were in a place ten handbreadths above or ten handbreadths below** him, **he may sit alongside it and recite *Shema*,** as a height disparity of ten handbreadths renders it a separate domain. **And if** the filth were **not** ten handbreadths above or below him, **he must distance himself** until it remains beyond **his range of vision. And the same is true of prayer.** However, from **a foul odor with a** visible **source, he distances himself four cubits from the place** that **the odor** ceased **and recites *Shema*.**

The *baraita* rules: *lo yikra adam krias shema lo keneged tzoas adam, velo keneged tzoas klavim, velo keneged tzoas chazirim, velo keneged tzoas tarnigolim, velo keneged tzoas ashpa sherei'cha ra* — one may not recite *Shema* in front of human excrement, dog excrement, pig excrement, chicken excrement, or rotten garbage with a bad smell. If there is a raised area of ten *tefachim* or a depression of ten *tefachim*, one may sit to the side of it and it serves as a wall of separation, permitting *Shema*. If not, one must move until the excrement is entirely out of one's field of vision. This applies to *tefillah* equally.

A second *baraita*, which Rava rules is the operative *halacha*, omits chickens and qualifies the dog and pig excrement: *bizman shenasan oros lesochan* — only when leather has been placed in them for tanning, which creates an intensely foul odor, are dog and pig excrement prohibited. Rashi understands this to mean that dog and pig excrement on their own are not a problem; Tosafos focuses on the omission of chickens and raises the practical question that would have pressed on many Jewish households: if chicken excrement everywhere is forbidden, how does anyone living on a farm ever *daven*? Tosafos cites Rabbeinu Yehuda's ruling that the prohibition on chicken excrement applies specifically in the chicken coop — *belul shelahem* — where the concentration and smell are severe, but not to chickens walking freely through the house, since otherwise *davening* would have been practically impossible in much of the ancient world. The principle seems to be that it is the smell and concentration that determine the prohibition, and scattered droppings that are not particularly pungent do not reach that threshold.

## Tefillin Bathroom

### Covering Tefillin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: שָׁכַח וְנִכְנַס בִּתְפִילִּין לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא — מַנִּיחַ יָדוֹ עֲלֵיהֶן עַד שֶׁיִּגְמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Huna said: One** who **forgot and entered the bathroom while donning phylacteries places his hand on them until he finishes.**

The *Gemara* now states: *shachach venichnas betfillin leveit hakisei* — if one forgot one was wearing *tefillin* and entered the bathroom, what is to be done? One should place one's hand over the *tefillin* until one has finished. This protects them from exposure to the inappropriate space.

### First Thing Drops

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁיִּגְמוֹר סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא כִּדְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: עַד שֶׁיִּגְמוֹר עַמּוּד רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders: **Does it enter your mind** that he can do so **until he is finished? Rather, as Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Until he finishes** discharging **the first mass** of feces, at which point he can step out and remove his phylacteries.

The *Gemara* challenges: does this mean one can simply keep one's hand there indefinitely, for however long one is engaged? Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak clarifies: *ad sheyigmor amud rishon* — only until the first thing drops, at which point one must remove the *tefillin*.

### Health Interruption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִפְסוֹק לְאַלְתַּר וְלֵיקוּם? מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר, עַמּוּד הַחוֹזֵר — מֵבִיא אֶת הָאָדָם לִידֵי הִדְרוֹקָן, סִילוֹן הַחוֹזֵר — מֵבִיא אֶת הָאָדָם לִידֵי יֵרָקוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let him stop immediately** when he realizes that he is donning phylacteries **and stand** and step out. The Gemara replies: He cannot do so **because of** the statement of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **A mass** of feces **that is held back** without having been discharged **causes a person** to suffer **from dropsy [*hidrokan*], while a stream** of urine **that is held back causes a person** to suffer **from jaundice [*yerakon*].** Since there is potential danger, the Sages did not require him to step out.

The reason for permitting even this delay, rather than requiring an immediate interruption, is derived from Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's teaching: *amud hachozer meivi et ha'adam lidei hadrokan* — interrupting defecation and causing the waste to retract leads to *hadrokan*, a serious intestinal illness. Similarly, interrupting urination — *vesilon hachozer meivi et ha'adam lidei yerakon* — leads to jaundice. The *Gemara* therefore permits waiting until the process reaches a natural stopping point before removing the *tefillin*, in deference to the health concern.

## Excrement on Body

### Hand Excrement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: צוֹאָה עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ, אוֹ יָדוֹ מוּנַּחַת בְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: מוּתָּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: אָסוּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the Sages disagreed with regard to one who had **fecal matter on his skin or** whose **hand,** but not the rest of his body, **was placed inside the bathroom.** Under those circumstances, **Rav Huna said:** He is **permitted to recite *Shema*. Rav Ḥisda said: He is prohibited from reciting *Shema*.**

The *Gemara* now addresses a case related to the filthy-courtyard discussion but distinct from it: *tzoa o besaro o yado munachas beveit hakise* — excrement on one's flesh, or one's hand situated within the space of a *beit hakise*. Rav Huna rules *mutar likros krias shema* — one may still recite *Shema*. A small amount of excrement on one's hand, without surrounding oneself with the smells and sights of filth, does not prohibit *Shema*. This reflects something noteworthy about the relative standards of hygiene assumed by the *Gemara*. Rav Chisda disagrees: *assur likros krias shema* — it is forbidden.

### Souls Praise God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב הוּנָא — דִּכְתִיב: ״כֹּל הַנְּשָׁמָה תְּהַלֵּל יָהּ הַלְלוּיָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason for Rav Huna's** opinion? **As it is written: "Let every soul** [***neshama***] **praise the Lord; Halleluya"** (Psalms 150:6), which he interprets as "Let everything that has breath" [*neshima*]. As long as the mouth with which one recites praise is in a place of purity, the location of the other limbs of his body is irrelevant.

Rava explains Rav Huna's reasoning from a verse: *kol haneshama tehalel y-h* — all souls shall praise God. Rashi explains: *hapeh vehachotam bichlal hahilul* — the mouth and the nose are the instruments of praise. The concern is proximity of the organs of speech and smell to filth. If the filth is only on one's hand, it is not in contact with the organs through which *Shema* is recited, and therefore does not prohibit it.

### Bones Speak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: אָסוּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא — דִּכְתִיב: ״כׇּל עַצְמוֹתַי תֹּאמַרְנָה ה׳ מִי כָמוֹךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda said: He is prohibited from reciting *Shema*. What is the reason for Rav Ḥisda's** opinion? **As it is written: "All of my bones shall say: Lord, who is like You"** (Psalms 35:10). Since this praise is undertaken with one's entire body, he may not recite *Shema* even if just one limb is not appropriately clean.

Rava then explains Rav Chisda's reasoning from a different verse: *kol atzmosai tomarna Hashem mi kamocha* — all my bones declare: God, who is like You? The entire body is the instrument of praise, not merely mouth and nose. On this view, excrement on any part of the body implicates the whole, and *Shema* is forbidden. One might note that the application of both of these verses to *Shema* — rather than exclusively to *tefillah* — is itself striking, as it suggests that even for *Kriyat Shema*, the full engagement of the self is implicated.

## Smell Sources

### Smell from Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: רֵיחַ רַע שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ עִיקָּר — רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: מַרְחִיק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: מַרְחִיק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת מִמְּקוֹם שֶׁפָּסַק הָרֵיחַ, וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **It was said** that the Sages disagreed over a similar issue: What is the legal status of **a foul odor that** emanates from a visible **source? Rav Huna said: He distances himself four cubits** from the source of the odor **and recites *Shema*. And Rav Ḥisda said:** The source is irrelevant; **he distances himself four cubits from the place that the odor ceased and recites *Shema*.**

The *Gemara* turns to *reiach ra sheyesh lo ikkar* — a bad smell that has a source, meaning it emanates from a specific physical object such as excrement or rotten garbage. Rav Huna rules *marchik arba amos vekorei krias shema* — move four *amos* away from the object and then recite *Shema*. Rav Chisda disagrees: four *amos* from the object itself may not be enough, since one may still be smelling it. His ruling is *marchik arba amos mimakom shepasak hareiach* — four *amos* from the place where the smell ends. One must be beyond the reach of the odor before beginning *Shema*. The *Gemara* rules that a *baraita* accords with Rav Chisda's more stringent position.

## Excrement Rules (III)

### Bad Smell Distance

Rava cites as operative *halacha* also addresses the scenario of *rotten garbage or bad-smelling waste*: if there is a rise of ten *tefachim* or a depression of ten *tefachim*, sitting to its side is sufficient, as it functions as a wall. If not, one must move until the object is out of one's line of sight. The operative concern in this framing is not primarily the smell but the visual presence of the filth within one's space. Only then does the four-*amos* smell rule enter: for a bad smell with a source, one must move four *amos* from where the smell ends, and then recite *Shema*. Rava rules that the earlier *baraita* on this point does not represent the *halacha* — instead, it is the version he cites that governs.

## Smell Sources (II)

### Smell in Air

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: רֵיחַ רַע שֶׁאֵין לוֹ עִיקָּר, מַהוּ? אָמַר לְהוּ: אֲתוֹ חֲזוֹ הָנֵי צִיפֵּי דְבֵי רַב, דְּהָנֵי גָּנוּ וְהָנֵי גָּרְסִי. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה, אֲבָל בִּקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע — לָא. וְדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה נָמֵי לָא אֲמַרַן, אֶלָּא דְּחַבְרֵיהּ, אֲבָל דִּידֵיהּ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet: What is** the legal status of **a foul odor that has no** visible **source,** e.g., flatulence? **He said to them: Come and see these mats in the study hall, as these** students **are sleeping** on them **and these** other students **are studying,** and they are not concerned about foul odors. **However, this only applies to Torah study** because there is no alternative, **but not to the recitation of *Shema*. And with regard to Torah study we said** that it is permitted **only** when the odor originated **with another, but not** when it originated **with himself.**

The *Gemara* now asks: *reiach ra she'ein lo ikar* — what about a bad smell without a source? This refers specifically to the case of passed gas, where the odor is present in the air but is no longer emanating from an object. Earlier the *Gemara* stated one must wait for the smell to dissipate before returning to *davening*; but what if one wishes to learn Torah or recite *Shema* in such a space? Rav Sheshet's answer is indirect but telling: *ta chazi hanei tzivei debei Rav* — come and see the mattresses in Rav's *yeshiva*, where students would sleep and study in the same space. Given that people sleep in those rooms, there is inevitably passing gas and bodily odors present, and yet students sit and learn *Torah* there without concern.

However, the *Gemara* immediately qualifies: *v'hanei mili bidvrei Torah, aval b'kriat Shema lo* — this leniency applies to Torah study, but not to *Kriyat Shema*. This is another instance of the graduated scale the *Gemara* employs: *Torah* study, *Shema*, *tefillah*, and *berachot* are not all treated identically, and here *Shema* requires stricter conditions than Torah study. Furthermore, even for Torah: *lo amran ela dechavrei aval dedidei lo* — the leniency applies only to someone else's smell, not to one's own. The reason given is presumably that one is more closely associated with, or perhaps more strongly affected by, one's own flatulence.

## Passing Excrement

### Abaye Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: צוֹאָה עוֹבֶרֶת — אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: מוּתָּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. רָבָא אָמַר: אָסוּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the Sages disagreed over a parallel issue: What is the law with regard to **feces passing** before him, being moved from place to place? **Abaye stated: One is permitted to recite *Shema*** opposite it, **while Rava said: One is forbidden to recite *Shema*** opposite it.

The *Gemara* addresses *tzoah overet* — passing excrement, the case where a cart is transporting excrement through a space. Can one recite *Shema* as it passes? Abaye says *mutar likro Kriyat Shema* — it is permitted. His reasoning draws an analogy to the laws of *metzora*: *hatamei omed tachat ha'ilan vehatahor over tahor* — if the *metzora* is standing under a tree and a pure person merely passes beneath, the pure person is not made impure, because the tree is the *metzora*'s space, not the passerby's. But *hatahor omed tachat ha'ilan vehatamei over tahor* — if it is the pure person standing and the *metzora* passing, it is the pure person who defines the space, and the *metzora*'s transit through it does not contaminate. By analogy: you are standing in your place and the excrement is what is moving through; it is not establishing itself in your space.

### Rava Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ — דִּתְנַן: הַטָּמֵא עוֹמֵד תַּחַת הָאִילָן וְהַטָּהוֹר עוֹבֵר — טָמֵא. טָהוֹר עוֹמֵד תַּחַת הָאִילָן וְטָמֵא עוֹבֵר — טָהוֹר. וְאִם עָמַד — טָמֵא. וְכֵן בְּאֶבֶן הַמְנוּגַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: From where do I say this** *halakha*? I say this on the basis of what **we learned** in a mishna: One who is afflicted with biblical leprosy renders the area beneath any covering under which he is located ritually impure. In a case where **the ritually impure** leper **is standing under** the branches of **a tree and a ritually pure** person **passes** under the branches of that same tree, the pure person **is** rendered **impure,** as the entire area under that covering is impure. However, if the **pure person is standing under the tree and the impure** leper **passes, he** remains **pure. And if** the leper **stopped** under the tree, the pure person is immediately rendered **impure. The same is true with regard to a stone afflicted** with biblical leprosy (see Leviticus 14), in that if it is merely being moved from place to place, it does not cause impurity.

Rava disagrees: *amar lach hatam bikvi'uta talya milta* — the *metzora* case hinges on the concept of *keviut*, fixed dwelling. The verse *badad yeshev michutz lamachaneh moshavo* — he shall dwell alone, outside the camp, his dwelling — concerns where the *metzora* is established. That is a question of the *metzora*'s space. But here the operative principle is different: *v'haya machanecha kadosh amar Rachmana* — the Torah commands that your camp shall be holy. *V'ha leika* — and here that holiness is absent. When excrement is passing through your space, your camp is not holy at that moment, regardless of whether the filth is stationary or in transit. This is the broader principle: the requirement of *machane kadosh* applies to where you are, not merely to whether a source of impurity has taken up permanent residence.

## Excrement Rules (III)…

### Pig Mouth Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: פִּי חֲזִיר כְּצוֹאָה עוֹבֶרֶת דָּמֵי. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא אַף עַל גַּב דְּסָלֵיק מִנַּהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: The mouth of a pig is like passing feces.** The Gemara asks: **That is obvious.** The Gemara replies: **No,** this *halakha* is only **necessary** to teach that **even though** the pig **emerged from the river** and one might assume that its mouth was thereby cleansed, it never becomes completely clean.

Rav Papa adds: *pi chazir k'tzoa overes dami* — the mouth of a pig is treated as equivalent to passing excrement. Whatever one rules about passing excrement, a pig's mouth carries the same status at all times. The *Gemara* notes this seems obvious, but the ruling adds a crucial detail: *af al gav d'salik m'nahara* — even if the pig has just emerged from a river, having in effect bathed, its mouth still carries this status, because of what remains within the mouth or clinging to it even after washing.

## Doubt Standards

### Doubt Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: סְפֵק צוֹאָה — אֲסוּרָה. סְפֵק מֵי רַגְלַיִם — מוּתָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** If there is **uncertainty** as to the presence of **feces,** e.g., whether something is or is not feces, and therefore whether or not one is permitted to utter sacred matters in its presence, **it is prohibited** to do so. However, if there is **uncertainty** as to the presence of **urine, it is permitted** to do so.

Rav Yehuda rules on the case of *safeik* — uncertainty about whether forbidden matter is present. *Safeik tzoa asura* — if there is a doubt whether excrement is present, one may not recite *Shema*. *Safeik mei raglayim mutarin* — if there is a doubt whether urine is present, it is permitted. The asymmetry reflects the general pattern: excrement is a more severe concern, and doubt does not resolve in one's favor; urine is a lesser concern, and doubt permits.

### Location Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: סְפֵק צוֹאָה, בַּבַּיִת — מוּתֶּרֶת, בָּאַשְׁפָּה — אֲסוּרָה. סְפֵק מֵי רַגְלַיִם — אֲפִילּוּ בָּאַשְׁפָּה נָמֵי מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** an alternative version of this. **Rav Yehuda said:** If there is **uncertainty** as to the presence of **feces, in the home** one may assume that there is no feces present and **it is permitted** to speak sacred matters, but if there is doubt as to the presences of feces **in the dung-heap** it is **forbidden** to do so. If there is **uncertainty** as to the presence of **urine,** however, **even in the dung-heap it is permitted** to do so.

A second version of Rav Yehuda's ruling is more nuanced. *Safeik tzoa babayit mutar* — if there is doubt about excrement in a house, it is permitted, because the house is presumably kept clean and the doubt favors absence. *Ba'ashpah, asura* — if the doubt arises in a garbage heap, it is forbidden, because the base rate there is that filth is present. For urine, doubt is lenient even in a garbage heap.

The underlying framework: *lo asra Torah ella k'neged amud bilvad* — the Torah only prohibited recitation of *Shema* or *berachot* in front of a stream of urine itself, not in the presence of dried or collected urine. The Rabbis extended this, but their extension was only for *vadai* — certain presence. *Aval b'safeik lo gazru* — for cases of doubt, the Rabbinic prohibition was never enacted. Rabbi Yonatan derives the distinction between excrement and urine from the *pesukim* in *Devarim* concerning camp hygiene: *v'yad tihyeh l'cha michutz lamachaneh* and *v'chisisa es tzeisacha* — go outside the camp and cover your waste. The *Gemara* reads these to address *gedolim* and *ketanim* respectively: one covers excrement but not urine, indicating that the Torah's concern is primarily with excrement, while urine is prohibited only during the act itself.

## Urine Wetness

### Moist Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְוַדָּאָן עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּטְפִּיחִין. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּטְפִּיחִין. וְכֵן אָמַר עוּלָּא: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּטְפִּיחִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In a case of the certain** presence of urine, **until when** and in what state does its presence preclude one from uttering sacred matters? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: As long as it** is wet enough to **moisten** the hands of one who touches it. **And so too Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: As long as it moistens. And so too Ulla said: As long as it moistens.**

Having established that urine is less severe, the *Gemara* asks: for how long after urination does the urine on the ground constitute a prohibition? Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel, Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, and Ulla all rule consistently: *kol zman shematpichin* — as long as the spot is still moist enough to pat and feel wetness.

### Visible Mark

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּנִיבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אָמַר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁרִשּׁוּמָן נִיכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Geniva in the name of Rav said:** It is forbidden **as long as its mark is apparent** on the ground.

Gneiva in the name of Rav takes a stricter position: *kol zman sherishumo nikar* — as long as the mark of where the urine fell is still visible, even if it is no longer wet, it remains a problem.

### Rav Yosef Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: שְׁרָא לֵיהּ מָרֵיהּ לִגְנִיבָא, הַשְׁתָּה צוֹאָה אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב כֵּיוָן שֶׁקָּרְמוּ פָּנֶיהָ — מוּתָּר, מֵי רַגְלַיִם מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: May** God, **his Master, forgive Geniva,** as Rav could have said no such thing. **Now,** in the case of **feces, Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Once its surface has** dried sufficiently to form a **crust, one is permitted** to utter sacred matters opposite it; **is it necessary** to say that opposite **urine** it is permitted once it dries?

Rav Yosef rejects Gneiva's ruling sharply: *sharei lei marei leGneiva* — his Master should forgive Gneiva for that statement. The reasoning: we know that even regarding excrement, Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav is relatively lenient, ruling *yavesha keshekarmu paneha mutar* — when excrement has dried and formed a surface crust, it is permitted. If we are lenient to that degree with excrement, it would be strange to be strict about urine to the point that even a visible but dry mark constitutes a prohibition.

### Excrement Hardened

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מַאי חָזֵית דְּסָמְכַתְּ אַהָא, סְמוֹךְ אַהָא, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: צוֹאָה, אֲפִילּוּ כְּחֶרֶס — אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: What did you see that** led **you to rely on that** *halakha*? **Rely on this** *halakha*; **as Rabba bar Rav Huna said** that **Rav said:** Uttering sacred matters opposite **feces, even** if it is as dry **as earthenware, is prohibited.**

Against that, the *Gemara* introduces Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rav: *tzoa afilu kecheres asura* — excrement, even if it has hardened to the consistency of pottery, remains forbidden. What does *kecheres* — like pottery — mean exactly? If one throws it and it does not shatter, or according to another version, if one rolls it and it does not crumble. The two opinions differ about the precise threshold of hardness, but both agree that dried excrement never loses its forbidden status. The case of Ravina being asked by Rav Yehuda of Diphti to examine whether a piece of excrement on the ground had formed a surface crust — and then being permitted to *daven* when it had — reflects the more lenient ruling, a ruling that Abeye challenges by pointing to the Rabbah bar bar Chana tradition that excrement like pottery remains forbidden regardless.

## Final Halacha

### Rava's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכְתָא: צוֹאָה כְּחֶרֶס אֲסוּרָה, וּמֵי רַגְלַיִם כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּטְפִּיחִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that **the *halakha*** is: Opposite **feces** as dry **as earthenware it is prohibited, and** opposite **urine,** it is prohibited **as long as it moistens.**

Rava issues the definitive ruling: *hilchata tzoa kecheres asura* — the *halacha* is that excrement, even when dried to the consistency of pottery, remains forbidden for *Kriyat Shema* and *davening*. This is the stringent position. But for urine the ruling follows the lenient line: *kol zman shematpichin* — only as long as the spot is still moist is it a problem. Once it is no longer wet, even if a mark remains visible, it is permitted.

### Vessel Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלִי שֶׁנִּשְׁפְּכוּ מִמֶּנּוּ מֵי רַגְלַיִם, אָסוּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע כְּנֶגְדּוֹ. וּמֵי רַגְלַיִם עַצְמָן שֶׁנִּשְׁפְּכוּ, נִבְלְעוּ — מוּתָּר, לֹא נִבְלְעוּ — אָסוּר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּטְפִּיחִין.
>
> **Translation:** **It is forbidden to recite *Shema* opposite a vessel from which urine was poured. However, the urine itself that was poured,** if it **was absorbed it is permitted;** if it **was not absorbed, it is prohibited. Rabbi Yosei** disagrees and **says:** It is prohibited **as long as it moistens.**

A separate case: *kli shenishpachu mimeno mei raglayim asur likro krias shema k'negdo* — a vessel from which urine has been poured — a chamber pot — remains forbidden for *Kriyat Shema* even after the urine is gone, because the absorbed odors and residue remain. The urine itself that fell to the ground: if absorbed, *mutar*; if not yet absorbed, *assur*. The vessel carries a stricter status because it has, as it were, retained the urine in its walls.

### Rabbi Yossi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי "נִבְלְעוּ" וּמַאי "לֹא נִבְלְעוּ" דְּקָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא? אִילֵימָא נִבְלְעוּ דְּאֵין מַטְפִּיחִין, לֹא נִבְלְעוּ דְּמַטְפִּיחִין — וַאֲתָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְמֵימַר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּטְפִּיחִין הוּא דְּאָסוּר, הָא רִשּׁוּמָן נִיכָּר — שְׁרֵי, הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this dispute: **What is** the meaning of **absorbed and not absorbed in what the first *tanna* says? If you say** that **absorbed** means that it **does not moisten and not absorbed** means that it **moistens, and Rabbi Yosei came to say: As long as it moistens it is prohibited, but** if there is no moisture but its **mark is apparent, it is permitted.** If so, **that is** identical to the opinion of the **first *tanna*** and there is no dispute at all.

The *Gemara* notes an apparent dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yossi. The *tanna kamma* speaks of absorbed versus not absorbed; Rabbi Yossi says the criterion is *kol zman shematpichin* — wetness. Are they disagreeing? On the surface it seems so: the *tanna kamma* apparently uses visibility as the criterion, while Rabbi Yossi uses wetness.

### Wetness Degree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא נִבְלְעוּ דְּאֵין רִשּׁוּמָן נִיכָּר, לֹא נִבְלְעוּ — דְּרִשּׁוּמָן נִיכָּר. וַאֲתָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְמֵימַר כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּטְפִּיחִין הוּא דְּאָסוּר, הָא רִשּׁוּמָן נִיכָּר — שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, absorbed** means that **its mark is not apparent** and **not absorbed** means that **its mark is apparent. And Rabbi Yosei came to say: As long as it moistens, it is prohibited, but** if there is no moisture but its **mark is apparent, it is permitted,** in which case the dispute in our Gemara is parallel to this tannaitic dispute.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction. Both authorities agree that the operative criterion is wetness, not mere visibility — *ha rishumo nikar sharei*, a visible but dry mark is permitted. Their debate concerns something more subtle: the degree of wetness. The question is *tofe'ach al menat lehatpi'ach* — is the spot wet enough that if you were to touch it, your touch would then make something else wet? That is one standard of wetness. A lesser degree of wetness — where touching it makes your hand damp but does not then transfer that dampness to another object — is the other. The *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yosei dispute which degree of wetness triggers the prohibition. Both agree that total dryness or mere visible marking does not; the line they draw differs only in the gradation of remaining moisture.
