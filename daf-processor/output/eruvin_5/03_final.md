# Eruvin 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lowering the Beam

### Heights Over Twenty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה גָּבוֹהַּ מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה וּבָא לְמַעֲטוֹ, כַּמָּה מְמַעֵט? כַּמָּה מְמַעֵט?! כַּמָּה דִּצְרִיךְ לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** If the cross beam spanning the entrance to an alleyway **was higher than twenty cubits** from the ground **and one comes to diminish its** height, **how much must he diminish** it? The Gemara is surprised by the question: **How much must he diminish** it? **The amount that he needs** in order to render its height less than twenty cubits.

We begin today's *daf* by picking up at the bottom of 4b, returning to the *mishnah* that rules a *mavoi* opening higher than twenty *amos* to be invalid. The *gemara* opens with the question: *hai gavoha me'esrim amah u'va'i lema'utei, kama mema'it?* — if the opening exceeds twenty *amos* and one wishes to lower it, how much must one lower it?

### Lowering Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: רׇחְבּוֹ בְּכַמָּה? רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: טֶפַח, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the space between the cross beam and the ground must, of course, be reduced to twenty cubits. However, when one raises the alleyway, **how much** must **the width** of the raised section be in order to render the alleyway fit for carrying within it? **Rav Yosef said:** One **handbreadth. Abaye said: Four** handbreadths.

The *gemara*'s initial response is almost playful in its literalism: *kama d'tzarich* — as much as you need. If the opening is twenty-five *amos*, lower it five; if twenty-seven, lower it seven. The *gemara* then pivots to the more substantive question: *ela rochvo bekama?* — how wide, or rather how deep, must the adjustment be?

It is worth pausing here to clarify what "lowering" actually means in this context. *Rashi* and the other *rishonim* understand the operative solution to be not the obvious one of physically repositioning the beam downward, but rather of raising the floor beneath it, so that the space between the floor and the beam is less than twenty *amos*. The *Rif* does read the *gemara* as discussing the beam itself being lowered, but the subsequent discussion makes clear that the floor is the focus. The *Rosh* disagrees with the *Rif* on this reading without much elaboration. As for the *Shulchan Aruch*, it describes only the case of raising the floor, with no mention of lowering the beam — and the *Mishnah Berurah*, almost as an afterthought in half a sentence, notes that lowering the beam would also work. There is something slightly comic about that, since lowering the beam is the intuitive solution: one simply drives a peg into the wall at a lower point and rests the beam on it. One could argue that the beam cannot be lower than the top of the *mavoi* walls, which may be why the discussion focuses elsewhere — but the *Rosh* does not raise this concern explicitly.

### Raising Floor

So, the *gemara*'s real question is this: if one raises the floor beneath the *korah* such that the gap between the floor and the beam is now less than twenty *amos*, how far into the *mavoi* must that raised floor extend? The image to hold in mind is a *mavoi* with its beam across the opening, and a mound or platform of earth directly beneath the beam. The question is how deep into the *mavoi* — measured from the beam inward — that raised platform must reach in order to be halachically effective.

### Rav Yosef vs Abaye

*Rav Yosef* says: one *tefach*. *Abaye* says: four *tefachim*. The *gemara* asks whether this dispute may reflect a deeper disagreement about when one is permitted to use the space beneath the *korah*. Perhaps *Rav Yosef*, who requires only one *tefach*, holds that one is permitted to use the space under the beam — *mutar le'hishtamesh tachat hakorah* — while *Abaye*, requiring four *tefachim*, holds that one is not. This framing sets up one of the central conceptual discussions of the *daf*.

## Heker vs Mechitzah

### Two Models

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא קָסָבְרִי מוּתָּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ תַּחַת הַקּוֹרָה, וּבְהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר קוֹרָה מִשּׁוּם הֶיכֵּר, וּמָר סָבַר קוֹרָה מִשּׁוּם מְחִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **No, everyone agrees that it is permitted to utilize** the area **beneath the** cross **beam, and they disagree with regard to this:** This **Master,** Rav Yosef, **holds** that **a** cross **beam** functions in an alleyway **as a conspicuous marker** that demarcates the alleyway from the public domain, and consequently a mere handbreadth is sufficient, as even a handbreadth is sufficiently conspicuous. **And** this **Master,** Abaye, **holds** that **a** cross **beam** serves **as a partition,** and a partition is not effective for an area of less than four handbreadths. The principle that an outer edge descends and seals the alleyway does not apply if the beam is higher than twenty cubits. In order for it to be considered a partition, there must be at least four handbreadths that are less than twenty cubits beneath the cross beam.

To understand what is really at stake in this dispute, we need to step back and ask a more fundamental question: what is the *korah* actually doing? This is not a new question — it already surfaced in the earlier discussion of why a height above twenty *amos* is invalid. Two competing frameworks emerged there, and they resurface here with even greater clarity.

The first framework is *korah mishum heker*: the beam functions as a sign, a marker, something that the person entering the *mavoi* will notice and thereby be reminded of the transition from public to private domain. The second framework is *korah mishum mechitzah*: the beam functions as a type of partition, a quasi-wall, and its halachic validity is assessed in the more formal terms we would apply to any *mechitzah*. These two models generate meaningfully different analyses of the depth question.

### Mechitzah Model

If the *korah* functions as a *mechitzah*, the question of which edge of the beam — front or back — constitutes the operative partition becomes significant. Consider the beam spanning the entrance of the *mavoi*. If it is the front edge, the edge closest to the public domain, that constitutes the wall, then the space beneath the beam is within the *mavoi*. If it is the back edge, then the area directly beneath the beam is still considered outside the *mavoi's* walled space.

### Front vs Back Edge

This is reminiscent, in structure if not in substance, of the discussion about the thickness of the wall between the *heichal* and the *kodesh kodashim* — to which space does the wall's interior belong? Here too, if we are being formalistic about the *korah* as a wall, one must ask: does the wall descend from its front face or its back face? One might suggest that the entire thickness of the beam constitutes the wall — but that same question arises for any physical wall, and even walls have interior space that must be assigned to one domain or another.

If the front edge of the beam defines the wall, then the space beneath the beam is usable, and it suffices to raise the floor by even one *tefach* directly beneath the beam — because the moment the floor is elevated, the visible gap between the floor and the front of the beam is already less than twenty *amos*, and the beam is performing its function. But if the back edge defines the operative boundary, then the space beneath the beam is not really within the *mavoi*'s effective area, and raising the floor there accomplishes nothing for the usable space of the *mavoi*. One must raise the floor further in — which requires at minimum the next significant halachic dimension, namely four *tefachim*.

### Visibility vs Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא קוֹרָה מִשּׁוּם הֶיכֵּר, וְהָכָא — בְּהֶיכֵּר שֶׁל מַטָּה וּבְהֶיכֵּר שֶׁל מַעְלָה קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר אָמְרִינַן הֶיכֵּר שֶׁל מַטָּה כְּהֶיכֵּר שֶׁל מַעְלָה, וּמָר סָבַר לָא אָמְרִינַן הֶיכֵּר שֶׁל מַטָּה כְּהֶיכֵּר שֶׁל מַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes an alternative explanation: **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone agrees** that **a** cross **beam** serves **as a conspicuous marker, and here they disagree with regard to** the relationship between **a conspicuous marker below,** i.e., the raised area of the alleyway, **and a conspicuous marker above,** i.e., the cross beam. This **Master,** Rav Yosef, **holds** that **we say** that the *halakha* that governs **the conspicuous marker below is like** the *halakha* that applies to **the conspicuous marker above,** and one handbreadth suffices. **And** this **Master,** Abaye, **holds** that **we do not say** that the *halakha* that governs **the conspicuous marker below is like** the *halakha* that applies to **the conspicuous marker above.** The lower sign must be more prominent and extend four handbreadths.

The *gemara* now refines the argument: *lo*, perhaps all opinions agree that one may use the space beneath the beam. The dispute, rather, reflects the two fundamental models. *Mar savar korah mishum heker* — one holds the beam is about visibility — *umar savar korah mishum mechitzah* — and the other holds it is about partition.

How does this play out? If the purpose of the *korah* is *heker*, then so long as one standing beneath the beam can look up and see that the gap above is less than twenty *amos*, the *heker* is achieved. One *tefach* of raised floor directly beneath the beam suffices, because from that position the beam is visible and its marking function is fulfilled. If however the purpose is *mechitzah*, then we are thinking about the *korah* as creating a partition that encloses a space. A *mechitzah*-defined space has minimum dimensions: a *reshut hayachid*, for example, requires four *tefachim* by four *tefachim* surrounded by walls. If the *korah* is to serve as a wall, the space it encloses — the space between the raised floor and the beam — must meet this minimum. Four *tefachim* is the smallest space we recognize as having the character of a halachically significant enclosed area. Therefore, according to this view, the raised floor must extend four *tefachim* into the *mavoi*.

### Visibility Distance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא קוֹרָה מִשּׁוּם הֶיכֵּר, וְהָכָא בִּגְזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִפְחוֹת קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes yet another explanation of the amoraic dispute: **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone agrees** that **we say** that fundamentally, the *halakha* that governs **the conspicuous marker below is like** the *halakha* that applies to the **conspicuous marker above,** and even a handbreadth should suffice. **But here they disagree whether** the Sages **decreed** that four handbreadths are necessary, **lest** people treading upon it will erode and **diminish** the raised area. Rav Yosef is not concerned that it will be diminished and therefore holds that a raised area of a handbreadth is sufficient, whereas Abaye is concerned that the raised area of a handbreadth will erode to less than a handbreadth, rendering it inconspicuous, and the alleyway will come to be utilized in a prohibited manner.

The *gemara* then introduces a third possibility: *ve'iba'it eima*, perhaps all agree that the *korah* functions because of *heker*, but they disagree about what constitutes adequate visibility. *Mar savar amrinan heker shemata keheker shemala* — one holds that just as the *korah* need only be one *tefach* wide above, so too it is sufficient to be noticeable within one *tefach* below, meaning directly beneath the beam. The other — *lo amrinan heker shemata keheker shemala* — says no: although the beam need only be one *tefach* thick, the visibility requirement below is more demanding. It is not enough to perceive the beam only when standing immediately beneath it; one must be able to notice it from further away, as one approaches. That would require the gap to be less than twenty *amos* over a greater depth than a single *tefach*.

### Preventive Measure

A fourth possibility follows: *ve'iba'it eima*, perhaps all agree that *heker* below mirrors *heker* above — one *tefach* would theoretically suffice. The debate then concerns a precautionary *gezeirah*: *shema yifchos*, lest the raised platform erode or settle and fall below the required one *tefach*. Requiring four *tefachim* instead of one provides a buffer, ensuring the height adjustment will remain halachically effective. This last explanation, notably, operates entirely within the *heker* framework, with the dispute about depth driven by a secondary concern rather than by any disagreement about the nature of the *korah*'s function.

What is striking about this entire sugya is how elegantly it mirrors the earlier discussion about why the twenty-*amah* threshold exists. There, the two models were the doorway-definition approach (akin to *mechitzah*) and the visibility approach (akin to *heker*). Here, those same two models generate a concrete halachic dispute about how much floor must be raised, with multiple layers of nuance about what exactly *heker* requires in three-dimensional space.

## Digging Below Beam

### Pit Digging

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה פָּחוֹת מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים וְחָקַק בּוֹ לְהַשְׁלִימוֹ לַעֲשָׂרָה, כַּמָּה חוֹקֵק? כַּמָּה חוֹקֵק?! כַּמָּה דִּצְרִיךְ לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא מִשְׁכּוֹ בְּכַמָּה? רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: בְּאַרְבָּעָה, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara considers a new case: If the cross beam spanning the entrance to an alleyway **was less than ten handbreadths** above the ground, **and one hollowed out** the ground under the cross beam in order **to complete** the distance from the ground to the cross beam **to ten, how much must he hollow out?** The Gemara is surprised by the question: **How much must he hollow out? However much is necessary for it** to increase the height to at least ten handbreadths. **Rather,** the question is as follows: **How far must** the hollowed-out area **extend** into the alleyway in order to render it permitted to carry throughout the alleyway? **Rav Yosef said: Four** handbreadths. **Abaye said: Four cubits.**

The *gemara* now turns to the reverse case. If the *korah* is valid only from a height of ten *tefachim* and above, what happens when the *mavoi*'s walls are too short — when the beam is less than ten *tefachim* from the ground? The solution proposed is the inverse of what we just discussed: rather than raising a platform beneath an overly high beam, one digs a pit beneath an overly low beam, deepening the ground at the entrance so that the effective height from the floor to the beam reaches ten *tefachim*.

The *gemara* once again asks: *kama chokek*, how much must one dig — meaning how far into the *mavoi* must the pit extend? And again, the answer *kama d'tzarich* — as much as the height deficit requires — is taken as obvious; the real question is the depth, not the amount of digging downward.

### Pit Depth Debate

Here the debate shifts in an interesting way. *Rav Yosef* says: four *tefachim*. *Abaye* says: four *amos*. Notably, no one takes the one-*tefach* position from the earlier discussion. The *gemara* asks whether this dispute is connected to the positions of *Rabbi Ami* and *Rabbi Assi*.

### Broken Mavoi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בִּדְרַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּאִיתְּמַר: מָבוֹי שֶׁנִּפְרַץ מִצִּידּוֹ כְּלַפֵּי רֹאשׁוֹ, אִיתְּמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי: אִם יֵשׁ שָׁם פַּס אַרְבָּעָה — מַתִּיר בְּפִירְצָה עַד עֶשֶׂר. וְאִם לָאו, פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה מַתִּיר. שְׁלֹשָׁה אֵינוֹ מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that** these *amora'im* **disagree with regard to** the ruling of **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi. As it was stated** with regard to the following question: If **the side wall of an alleyway was breached toward its entrance,** i.e., close to where the alleyway opens into the public domain, what is the *halakha*? **It was stated in the name of Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi: If an** upright **board four** handbreadths wide remains of the original wall or is set up where the original wall had ended, the cross beam or side post at the entrance to the alleyway **renders it permitted** to carry in the alleyway even if there is **a breach** of **up to ten** cubits wide. **And if** there is **no** upright board there, the following distinction applies: If the breach is **less than three** handbreadths, the cross beam or side post **renders it permitted** to carry in the alleyway, based on the principle of *lavud*. If the breach is **three** or more handbreadths, the cross beam or side post **does not render it permitted** to carry.

It was stated in the name of *Rabbi Ami* and *Rabbi Assi*: if a *mavoi* is breached on its side near the opening (*nifratz mitzido kelapi rosho*), and there remains a partition of four *tefachim* at the front, one may permit the *mavoi* even if the breach extends up to ten *amos*. If there is no four-*tefach* partition at the front, then only if the gap is less than three *tefachim* — which we treat as continuous by the principle of *lavud* — is the wall considered unbroken.

To appreciate the ruling of *Rabbi Ami* and *Rabbi Assi*, one must picture the configuration. A *mavoi* has three walls and an open fourth side. The two long side walls run the length of the alleyway. If one of those walls is breached near the opening — near the fourth, open side — that breach is particularly problematic because the beam is placed at the opening, and the beam's effectiveness depends on the walls being seen as extending to that point. If there are four *tefachim* of intact wall at the front (nearest the opening), that is considered a sufficiently legitimate structure at the *mavoi*'s entrance, and the breach further back may be as large as ten *amos*, which is the general threshold for what counts as an opening rather than a breach.

Now, what does this have to do with the pit-digging dispute? If the *mavoi*'s walls are less than ten *tefachim* tall throughout, then digging a pit at the entrance creates walls of ten *tefachim* — but only in the section where the pit was dug. The rest of the *mavoi* retains walls that are less than ten *tefachim* and are therefore halachically deficient. So the question of how far in to dig is really the question of how much of the *mavoi* needs to have proper walls at the entrance in order for the configuration to function.

The *gemara* suggests: *Rav Yosef* holds like *Rabbi Ami* and *Rabbi Assi* — four *tefachim* at the front suffice — and therefore four *tefachim* of digging is enough. *Abaye* does not hold like *Rabbi Ami* and *Rabbi Assi*, or more precisely, *Abaye* distinguishes the cases. He says: *hatam sof mavoi, hacha techilat mavoi* — there, in *Rabbi Ami* and *Rabbi Assi*'s case, you began with a full, valid *mavoi* and something broke off. You are dealing with the remnants of legitimacy. Under such conditions, four *tefachim* at the front may suffice to preserve the *mavoi*'s status. Here, however, one is not preserving an existing status but creating one from scratch. For that, *Abaye* argues, you need four *amos* — because four *amos* is the minimum length required for a space to qualify as a *mavoi* at all.

This brings us directly to the next major topic: the minimum dimensions of a *mavoi*.

## Minimum Mavoi Size

### Courtyard Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ — דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין מָבוֹי נִיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ בָּתִּים וַחֲצֵרוֹת פְּתוּחִין לְתוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: From where do I say** that a length of at least four cubits is required? **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **An alleyway is permitted by means of a side post or** cross **beam only if it has** both **houses** opening into courtyards **and courtyards opening into it,** as only in that case can it be called an alleyway.

*Abaye* now presents his argument more fully. A *ברייתא* teaches: *ein mavoi nitar be'lechi ve'korah ad she'yiheyu batim va'chatzeirot ptuchim letocho* — a *mavoi* is not permitted with a *lechi* or *korah* unless it has houses and courtyards opening into it. The basic configuration is: individual houses open into a *chatzer*, and multiple *chatzeirot* open into the *mavoi*, which functions as a shared communal alleyway. This is the structure required for the space to carry the status of a *mavoi* and thus to be served by the minimal fourth-wall solution of a *lechi* or *korah* alone.

### Length Exceeds Width

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בְּאַרְבָּעָה הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לֵיהּ! וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּפָתַח לֵהּ בְּדוֹפֶן הָאֶמְצָעִי, וְהָאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: נְקִיטִינַן, אֵיזֶהוּ מָבוֹי שֶׁנִּיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה כֹּל שֶׁאׇרְכּוֹ יָתֵר עַל רׇחְבּוֹ, וּבָתִּים וַחֲצֵרוֹת פְּתוּחִין לְתוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the entire length of the alleyway is only **four** handbreadths, as is indicated by Rav Yosef's opinion, **how can you find this** case? Under what circumstances is it possible for such a short alleyway to have courtyards opening into it? Even if there are only two such courtyards, the entrance to each is at least four handbreadths wide. **And if you say that** the alleyway **opens** into the courtyards **through** its **middle wall,** i.e., the alleyway is only four handbreadths long but is wide enough to have two entrances opening into two courtyards, this is difficult. **Didn't Rav Naḥman say** that **we hold** on the authority of tradition: **Which is an alleyway that is permitted by a side post or a** cross **beam? Any** alleyway **whose length is greater than its width and has houses and courtyards opening into it.** Accordingly, if the alleyway is only four handbreadths long, its width must be even less than that. Consequently, argues Abaye, a length of at least four cubits is required.

Now, *Abaye* poses a sharp question: if you maintain that a *mavoi* can have a minimum length of four *tefachim*, how can the *chatzeirot* open into it? An opening is always at least four *tefachim* wide. If the entire length of the *mavoi* is four *tefachim*, and two *chatzeirot* must open into it — each opening requiring four *tefachim* — there will be no wall left anywhere. The *mavoi* would consist of nothing but openings, with no wall surface remaining on its long sides. Such a structure cannot function as a *mavoi*.

One might suggest that the *mavoi* is very wide and only four *tefachim* deep, with the *chatzeirot* opening through the back wall. But the *halacha* is that a *mavoi* is defined as a space in which the length exceeds the width: *kol she'orko yeter al rochbo*. This is not merely a geometric observation; it reflects the nature of a *mavoi* as an alleyway or passage rather than a courtyard. If width exceeds length, the space is classified differently and is subject to different requirements. So if the length is four *tefachim*, the width must be less — and there is certainly no room for *chatzeirot* to open in from the side.

### Corner Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף: דְּפָתַח לֵיה בְּקֶרֶן זָוִית.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef,** how would he respond to this? Rav Yosef explains that the *baraita* is dealing with a case **where** the courtyards **open** into the alleyway **at its corners.** In this way it is possible to have two openings, each of which is at least four handbreadths wide, although the length of the alleyway itself is no greater than four handbreadths, as the four handbreadths of the openings to the courtyards are divided between the width and the length of the alleyway.

*Rav Yosef* offers a solution: *de'patach lah bekeren zavit* — the *chatzeirot* open at an angle, through the corners. *Rashi* illustrates this geometrically: if the opening is not perpendicular to the wall but is set at a diagonal, the four *tefachim* of the opening span a corner, and a small remnant of wall is preserved at the bottom of the angle. The mathematics work — using the *pythagorean* relationship of a diagonal, one can achieve a four-*tefach* diagonal opening while leaving a small piece of wall intact, approximately a third of a *tefach* or so. This is technically possible, but it is an extraordinarily strained solution, and *Abaye*'s position that a *mavoi* must be at least four *amos* in length is beginning to sound far more compelling, both intuitively — whoever heard of an alleyway four *tefachim* long? — and structurally.

### Lechi Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ — דְּאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֶחִי הַבּוֹלֵט מִדּוֹפְנוֹ שֶׁל מָבוֹי, פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי, וְאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ. אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם מָבוֹי וְצָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye** further **said: From where do I say** that a length of at least four cubits is required? **As Rami bar Ḥama said** that **Rav Huna said:** With regard to **a side post that protrudes from the wall** on the side **of an alleyway** into the opening of the alleyway, if its protrusion was **less than four cubits, it is deemed a side post** that renders it permitted to carry in the alleyway, **and no other side post is required to render it permitted** to carry in it. However, if it protruded **four cubits,** that section **is deemed an alleyway,** as though there were an additional alleyway within an alleyway, **and another side post is required to render it permitted** to carry there. This shows that anything less than four cubits does not have the status of an alleyway, which supports the position of Abaye.

*Abaye* adduces an additional proof. He cites the ruling of *Rami bar Chama* in the name of *Rav Huna*: a *lechi* that protrudes from the wall of a *mavoi* is treated as a *lechi* — functioning as a valid side-post — only if it is less than four *amos* in length. Once it reaches four *amos*, it is no longer treated as a *lechi* but is reclassified as a wall of the *mavoi* itself (*nidon mishum mavoi*), and a separate *lechi* is then required to mark the entrance.

*Abaye*'s argument is this: why does the transition occur at four *amos*? Because once this protruding element reaches four *amos* in width, it could itself define the dimensions of a *mavoi* — and at that point it can no longer serve merely as a *lechi*. This implies that four *amos* is the minimum width (and therefore, given the length-exceeds-width rule, the minimum length) of a *mavoi*. *Rav Yosef*, by contrast, holds that even four *tefachim* can define a *mavoi* in the dimensional sense, but the reclassification from *lechi* to *mavoi* wall requires a significantly larger threshold before we change the element's identity. These are, *Rav Yosef* argues, two distinct questions: the minimum parameters of a *mavoi* and the point at which a *lechi* changes its functional classification.

## Lechi Stands Itself

### Lechi Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֶחִי הַבּוֹלֵט מִדׇּפְנוֹ שֶׁל מָבוֹי, פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ. אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם מָבוֹי, וְצָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines Rami bar Ḥama's statement cited in the course of the previous discussion. As to the matter **itself: Rami bar Ḥama said** that **Rav Huna said:** With regard to **a side post that protrudes from the wall** on the side **of an alleyway** into the entrance of the alleyway, if its protrusion is **less than four cubits, it is deemed a side post** that renders it permitted to carry in the alleyway, **and no other side post is required to render it permitted.** However, if it protrudes **four cubits,** that section **is deemed an alleyway, and another side post is required to render it permitted to** carry in it.

The *gemara* now takes up the ruling of *Rami bar Chama* in the name of *Rav Huna* in its own right — *gufa*, as the *gemara* marks it. A *lechi* that protrudes from the wall of the *mavoi* (*lechi habolet midofno shel mavoi*): if it is less than four *amos*, it is treated as a *lechi* (*nidon mishum lechi*) and no additional *lechi* is required to render the *mavoi* permissible. If it is four *amos* or more, it is treated as a wall of the *mavoi* (*nidon mishum mavoi*), and a separate *lechi* is needed.

This is connected to the famous dispute between *Abaye* and *Rava* about *lechi ha'omed me'eilav* — a *lechi* that stands on its own, meaning a structure that was never placed there for the purpose of serving as a *lechi* but can functionally fulfill that role. A telephone pole at the entrance of an alleyway is the classic modern example. *Abaye* holds that such a structure may serve as a *lechi*; *Rava* disagrees. This is one of the relatively rare cases in which the *halacha* follows *Abaye* rather than *Rava*.

The intuitive question is: why should a protruding element become a *problem* as it grows longer? Surely more wall is better than less wall. The answer lies in the *heker* dimension of the *lechi*'s function. Once the protruding element is long enough to be experienced as part of the *mavoi*'s wall structure rather than as a distinct marker at the entrance, it ceases to serve the marking function (*heker*) of a *lechi*. It is absorbed into the ambient wall, so to speak, and the entrance point of the *mavoi* needs a separate marker. This also illustrates — as *Tosafot* will make on the following *daf* — that even if one holds primarily that the *korah* functions as a *mechitzah*, there must be some residual *heker* component as well; and conversely, even if one holds primarily that it functions as a *heker*, there are formalistic elements that cannot be ignored.

### Lechi Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹתוֹ לֶחִי, הֵיכָן מַעֲמִידוֹ? אִי דְּמוֹקֵי לֵיהּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ, אוֹסֹפֵי הוּא דְּקָא מוֹסִיף עֲלֵיהּ. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּמוֹקֵי לֵיהּ לְאִידַּךְ גִּיסָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **That side post,** which is added in order to permit carrying within the alleyway that was formed by the four-cubit side post, **where does one position it** such that one may carry within the alleyway? The Gemara clarifies its difficulty: **If one positions it alongside** the first side post as an addition to it, it looks as if **he is** merely **extending** the original side post, and it is not noticeable that an extra side post is present. **Rav Pappa said: He should position it,** the extra side post, **on the other side** of the alleyway, near the opposite wall.

The practical question that follows is: where does one place the additional *lechi* once the protruding element has crossed into *mavoi*-wall status? If one places another *lechi* alongside the same protrusion, he has simply extended the wall further — *osei hu dikamosif lah* — making the protruding wall four *amos* and one *tefach*, which solves nothing. It remains a wall rather than a *lechi*.

*Rav Pappa* therefore says: *demokei lei le'idach gisa* — place the additional *lechi* against the opposite wall. That creates a genuine *lechi* marking the entrance on the other side.

### Omed Meruba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא דְּמוֹקֵי לֵהּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ דִּמְטַפֵּי בֵּיהּ, אוֹ דִּמְבַצַּר בֵּיהּ. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: לָא אֲמַרַן, אֶלָּא בְּמָבוֹי שְׁמוֹנָה. אֲבָל בְּמָבוֹי שִׁבְעָה — נִיתָּר בְּעוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: Even if you say that he positions it alongside** the first side post, it is valid, so long as **he adds to it or diminishes from it** in thickness or height, so that it will be noticeable that it is a side post of its own. The Gemara limits the application of Rami bar Ḥama's ruling: **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: We stated** this halakha with regard to a side post protruding four cubits into the alleyway **only in** the case of **an alleyway** that is at least **eight** cubits wide. **However, in** the case of **an alleyway** that is only **seven** cubits wide, it is **permitted** to carry within the alleyway without an additional side post, not because the original side post functions as a side post but rather because it sufficiently seals off the entrance to the alleyway such that the **standing** segment **is greater than the breached** segment. The alleyway is now closed off from all four sides, and the remaining opening to the public domain is regarded as an entrance, as most of that side is closed and only a small part of it is open.

*Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua* offers an alternative: even positioning the additional *lechi* alongside the protrusion can work, provided it differs in the other dimensions — either slightly higher or lower, or slightly deeper or shallower than the protrusion itself. This difference, however slight, provides the *heker* that distinguishes the new *lechi* from the wall.

*Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua* also introduces the broader principle that governs whether the four-*amah* protrusion is a problem in the first place: *lo amrin ela bemavoi shemoneh* — this entire concern applies only if the *mavoi* is eight *amos* wide or more. If the *mavoi* is less than eight *amos* wide, the four-*amah* protrusion seals off the majority of that side, and the principle of *omed meruba al haparutz* — that a side is considered closed when the majority of it is sealed — renders the entrance permissible even without an additional *lechi*.

## Mavoi vs Chatzer

### Wall Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵחָצֵר: וּמָה חָצֵר שֶׁאֵינָהּ נִיתֶּרֶת בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה — נִיתֶּרֶת בְּעוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ, מָבוֹי שֶׁנִּיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנִּיתָּר בְּעוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ?
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is derived by means of **an *a fortiori* inference from a courtyard: Just as** in **a courtyard, which is not** rendered a **permitted** domain **by** means of **a side post or a** cross **beam,** but actual partitions are required, it **is** nevertheless rendered a **permitted** domain even if there are gaps in the partitions, as long **as the standing** segment **is greater than the breached** segment in **an alleyway,** with regard to which the Sages were lenient, **as** it **is** rendered a **permitted** domain **by** means of **a side post or a** cross **beam, is it not right** that it **is** rendered a **permitted** domain **when the standing** segment of the partition is **greater than the breached** segment?

The *gemara* now sets out the structural distinction between *mavoi* and *chatzer* regarding the requirements for the fourth wall. A *mavoi* requires only a *lechi* (a vertical post) or a *korah* (a horizontal beam) — minimal markers. A *chatzer* requires a *pas arba'ah* — an actual partition of four *tefachim*. This is a more significant physical structure.

The distinction, as we have noted, is not purely a function of how the space is used but of its geometry. A space in which length exceeds width is classified as a *mavoi* and is subject to the lenient fourth-wall standard. A space in which width equals or exceeds length is classified as a *chatzer* and requires the more demanding *pas arba'ah*. The intuition behind this difference is connected to *heker*: in a long narrow alleyway, a small marker at the entrance is noticeable and effective; in a broader, squarer space, something more substantial is required to register as a boundary.

### Kal Vachomer

*Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua* now argues that the principle of *omed meruba al haparutz* applies to a *mavoi* via a *kal vachomer* from *chatzer*. The reasoning is as follows: a *chatzer* has a more demanding fourth-wall requirement — it needs a *pas arba'ah* rather than a mere *lechi* or *korah*. Yet we know that for a *chatzer*, when any wall is majority sealed (*omed meruba al haparutz*), that wall is considered closed. If this leniency applies to a *chatzer*, where the standards are stricter, it should certainly apply to a *mavoi*, which is in every other way more lenient.

### Break Sizes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לֶחָצֵר שֶׁכֵּן פִּרְצָתָהּ בְּעֶשֶׂר, תֹּאמַר בְּמָבוֹי שֶׁפִּרְצָתוֹ בְּאַרְבַּע!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this *a fortiori* inference that was based on the fact that the legal status of a courtyard is more lenient than that of an alleyway, as it is in fact more stringent than that of an alleyway in at least one respect. With regard to **what** is true of **a courtyard, that** as long as **its breach is** less than **ten** cubits it remains a permitted domain, can **you say** the same **of an alleyway,** which is more stringent, as in a case where **its breach is** only **four** handbreadths it is not permitted to carry in the alleyway? Therefore, the *halakha* of an alleyway cannot be derived from the *halakha* of a courtyard.

The *gemara* challenges this *kal vachomer* with a classic counter-move: *mah l'chatzer sheken pirtzata b'eser, tomar bemavoi shepirtzato b'arba?* Perhaps the *chatzer* is more lenient than we think in a different dimension: a breach in a *chatzer*'s wall is only considered a problematic break if it is ten *amos* wide, whereas by a *mavoi* even a break of four *amos* is considered a breach. If a *mavoi* is actually stricter than a *chatzer* in the area of breach thresholds, then the *kal vachomer* collapses — one cannot draw the inference that whatever works for the more demanding *chatzer* must certainly work for the more lenient *mavoi*, because in this respect the *mavoi* is not more lenient.

### Rav Huna Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מָבוֹי נָמֵי, פִּרְצָתוֹ בְּעֶשֶׂר. לְמַאן קָאָמְרִינַן? לְרַב הוּנָא, וְהָא רַב הוּנָא פִּרְצָתוֹ בְּאַרְבַּע סְבִירָא לֵיהּ?! רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ טַעְמָא דְנַפְשֵׁיהּ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, holds** that **the breach of an alleyway** is **also ten** cubits. The Gemara raises a difficulty: But **in accordance with whose** opinion **did we state** this *a fortiori* inference? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Huna. But doesn't Rav Huna** himself **hold that the breach** of an alleyway is **four** handbreadths. The Gemara answers: **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** did not come to explain Rav Huna's position. Rather, he **stated his own view,** and he does not accept Rav Huna's opinion with regard to the law of a breach in an alleyway.

The *gemara* resolves this by clarifying whose position is under discussion. *Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua* — who was *Rav Huna*'s student — was presenting the ruling about the eight-*amah* threshold as an elaboration of *Rav Huna*'s original statement. But *Rav Huna* himself holds that a breach of four *amos* constitutes a problematic break in a *mavoi*, which would indeed undermine the *kal vachomer* from *chatzer*. The resolution is: *Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua ta'ama d'nafshei ka'amar* — he was not explaining *Rav Huna*'s position but stating his own independent view. According to *Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua* himself, a *mavoi* also requires a breach of ten *amos* before it is considered broken, just as a *chatzer*. On that basis, the *mavoi* is in every respect more lenient than the *chatzer*, and the *kal vachomer* stands: if *omed meruba al haparutz* works for the more demanding *chatzer*, it certainly works for the *mavoi*.

## Rav Ashi Leniency

### Eight Amos Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּמָבוֹי שְׁמוֹנֶה נָמֵי לָא צְרִיךְ לֶחִי, מָה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי עוֹמֵד נְפִישׁ נִיתָּר בְּעוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ, וְאִי פָּרוּץ נְפִישׁ נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi** went further than Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, and **said: Even if you say** that the law with regard to a side post protruding four cubits into the alleyway applies **in** the case of **an alleyway** that is exactly **eight** cubits wide, in that it **too does not require an** additional **side post. Whichever** way **you** look at it, you are forced to arrive at this conclusion: **If** you say that the **standing** segment **is greater,** then the alleyway **is permitted,** because its **standing** segment **is greater than the breached** segment; **and if** you say that the **breached** segment **is greater,** then the protrusion is **deemed a side post,** as its width must be less than four cubits.

*Rav Ashi* now pushes the analysis one step further with a characteristically precise observation. *Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua* had said the four-*amah* protrusion becomes a problem only when the *mavoi* is eight *amos* wide or more — at which point the protrusion fails to close off the majority of the entrance. *Rav Ashi* says: *afilu teima bemavoi shemoneh nami lo tzarich lechi* — even if the *mavoi* is exactly eight *amos* wide, no additional *lechi* is required.

### Measurement Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אָמְרַתְּ, דְּשָׁווּ תַּרְוַיְיהוּ כִּי הֲדָדֵי — הָוֵה לֵיהּ סְפֵק דִּבְרֵיהֶן, וּסְפֵק דִּבְרֵיהֶן לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **What** might **you say** that would require an additional side post? Would you suggest that there is yet another possibility, **that the two are** exactly **equal,** the standing portion and the breach? **This is an uncertainty** with regard to **rabbinic law,** as carrying in an alleyway is forbidden only by rabbinic law, **and** the principle is that where there is **an uncertainty** with regard to **a rabbinic law,** one may assume the **lenient** position, as opposed to an uncertainty arising with respect to a Torah law, where one assumes the stringent position.

The reason is subtle. *Manafshach* — think it through either way. In practice, it is impossible to measure exactly. Either the sealed portion is slightly greater than the open portion, in which case *omed meruba al haparutz* applies and no *lechi* is needed; or the open portion is slightly greater, meaning the protruding element is actually slightly less than four *amos*, in which case it still qualifies as a *lechi* and no additional one is required. The only case that would be genuinely problematic is one in which the protrusion is measured to exactly four *amos* and the *mavoi* to exactly eight — positioned precisely in the no-man's-land where the element is too large to be a *lechi* but fails to close off a majority of the entrance.

*Rav Ashi* argues that such a perfectly precise measurement is practically impossible to achieve. Therefore, any given situation presents only a *safek* — a doubt — as to whether one is actually in that problematic configuration. And since the entire requirement for a *lechi* in this context is *derabbanan*, the principle of *safek divreihem l'hakel* applies: in cases of doubt regarding *rabbinic* enactments, we rule leniently. Therefore, even when the dimensions appear to be exactly eight *amos* and four *amos*, one may rely on the practical impossibility of such precision and need not place an additional *lechi*.
