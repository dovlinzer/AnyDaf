# Beitzah 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shabbat Koveia?

### Intro: Shabbat Koveia

The central question animating the opening of daf 35a concerns whether *shabbat* functions as *koveia* — that is, whether the onset of *shabbat* imposes a permanent, meal-like status upon fruit that has not yet undergone *gemar melacha*, the completion of its processing. The background *halacha* is as follows: produce whose *gemar melacha* has not been completed, or which has been completed but not yet brought indoors (*re'iyat pnei habayit*), may be eaten in a temporary, incidental manner (*derech arai*) without first separating *terumot u'ma'asrot*. One may snack, but not make a fixed meal (*derech keva*). The further principle under discussion is that *shabbat*, on account of the sanctity and importance of eating on that day, functions as *koveia* — rendering even incidental eating into something permanent and obligatory for *terumot u'ma'asrot*. The Gemara's question is whether this principle applies only where *gemar melacha* has already been completed and only *re'iyat pnei habayit* remains, or whether *shabbat* is *koveia* even when the fruit's processing is altogether unfinished — *lo nigmar melachto*.

### Mishna: Erev Shabbat

This question bears on the *mishna*, which addresses a case of standing over fruit on *erev shabbat* and on *erev shvi'it* during the *shemitah* year. The implication of that *mishna* is that when *shabbat* arrives, if the fruit were truly obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot*, one would find oneself in a bind. That implication sounds like *shabbat* is indeed *koveia*. However, the Gemara works to escape this inference. We now resume with that very question at the top of 35a.

The Gemara asks: *ve'halo* — is it not the case that in the *mishna*'s scenario of designating fruit, one would indeed be unable to snack from it? And either that is because *shabbat* is *koveia*, or, the Gemara had previously suggested, perhaps it is because the verbal designation itself — the declaration *mikan ani ochel*, "I will eat from this" — transforms the eating into something permanent (*keva*), even without invoking the principle of *shabbat koveia*.

### Challenge: Dibbur Koveya

The Gemara now challenges whether a verbal declaration alone can accomplish this transformation. *Ve'halo*, it asks, does not *motaro chozer* — the leftover returns? If one declares "I will eat from this section," one eats what one eats and simply leaves the rest in place. There is not even a need to return it; it remains undisturbed. If the physical act of removing a handful and then returning the remainder does not constitute *keva*, how much less so a mere verbal designation, where nothing has even been disturbed and everything that will not be eaten remains precisely where it was?

### R. Eliezer: Motaro Chozer

The Gemara invokes the known position of Rabbi Eliezer — the *tanna* at the center of the *mishna* — who holds that *kol heicha de'motaro chozer, lo kava*: wherever the remainder of what one takes will return to its original place, the eating is not considered permanent and one may eat in a temporary fashion. The Gemara then cites the relevant *mishna* to illustrate this principle.

### Olives Mishna

*De'tnan*: if one takes olives from the *ma'atan* — a vessel in which olives are placed prior to pressing, where gravity gradually causes their juices to begin flowing out — one may dip them one by one in salt and eat them. These olives are not *nigmar melachto*; they are in the process of becoming olive oil, and eating one or two is snacking. Indeed, the *torat* obligation of *gemar melacha* for olives is only completed when the oil is finished, as the Torah speaks of *dagan tirosh v'yitzhar* — and *yitzhar* is oil, not the olive itself. Hence one may snack without separating *terumot u'ma'asrot*.

However, if one dipped a whole group and placed them before oneself — sitting down with a full plate — that is *achilat keva* and one is *chayav*. Then the *mishna* introduces a dispute: *Rabbi Eliezer omer*, if one took from a *tahor* vessel, one is obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot*; if from a *tamei* vessel, one is exempt — *mipnei shehu machzir es ha'mutar*, because one returns what is left over.

The Gemara asks: *mai shna reisha u'mai shna seifa* — what difference does it make whether the vessel was *tahor* or *tamei*? Rabbi Abbahu explains: the first case (*reisha*) involves a *tahor* vessel and a *tamei* person. When one reaches in to take olives, the ones one touches become *tamei*, and one cannot return them without contaminating the rest. Hence *de'lo matzi mahadar leh* — one cannot return what is left over — and the taking is therefore *keva*. The second case (*seifa*) involves a *tamei* vessel and a *tamei* person; one can eat what one eats and return the rest without consequence, since everything was already *tamei*. There, *dematzi mahadar leh*, and so the eating remains *arai*.

The basic *halacha* at stake, then, is: if one removes a group of olives and sits down with them before *gemar melacha*, is that *keva* or *arai*? The *tanna kamma* says it is *keva*. Rabbi Eliezer says that if one intends to return what remains, that remains *arai*, and one may eat even before separating *terumot u'ma'asrot*.

### Keva Context Matters

This is the crux of the Gemara's challenge. We wished to say that in the *mishna* on *erev shabbat* and *shvi'it*, the verbal declaration *mikan ani ochel* is what makes the eating *keva*. But the Gemara objects: if physically removing a pile of olives and sitting down to eat them does not constitute *keva* when one intends to return the remainder — because the remainder is going back into the context of ongoing processing — then how could a verbal designation, where nothing has even been disturbed and everything remains where it was, possibly constitute *keva*? The leftover is not merely going back; it never left. It is already, in the most complete sense, back. The eating is purely incidental — one is merely snacking from within a process that is still ongoing.

The Gemara therefore presses: rather than saying the verbal declaration is *koveia*, we must say it is *shabbat* itself that is *koveia*. That must be what the *mishna* implies — that the reason one cannot snack on *shabbat* from unfinished produce is the principle of *shabbat koveia*.

This leads to the suggestion of Rav Shimi bar Ashi: one must be careful not to read too much into the *mishna*, because the *mishna* reflects the position of Rabbi Eliezer, and Rabbi Eliezer *letamei* — he follows his own reasoning. Rabbi Eliezer in general is more inclined to see various halachic events as transforming food into a state of *achilat keva*. Specifically, *de'amar teruma kav'a* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that the act of taking *teruma* from produce renders the remainder absolutely obligated as *tevel*, and *ve'chol sheken shabbat*, certainly *shabbat* would do so as well.

The relevant *mishna* from *Ma'asrot* is cited: *perot she'traman ad shelo nigmar melachtan* — fruit from which one separated *teruma* before their processing was complete. Rabbi Eliezer holds one may no longer eat from such fruit even *b'arai*, because having taken *teruma* — even voluntarily before the obligatory stage — locks the remainder into full *tevel* status. The *chachamim*, however, permit continued snacking. The point is that Rabbi Eliezer's view that *teruma* changes the status of unfinished produce is a more expansive conception of what constitutes halachic transformation. One who accepts that principle can readily accept that *shabbat* equally transforms the eating. But that is Rabbi Eliezer's singular view, and it has not been demonstrated that the *chachamim* agree.

The Gemara then turns to the *seifa* of the *mishna* — the position of the *chachamim* — where it says: *ve'ha'chachamim omrim ad sheyomar mikan ve'ad kan*, that one must verbally declare "from here to here" in order to address the *muktza* concern. The Gemara notes that both Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* implicitly agree that were this not *shemitah*, one would be forbidden to eat the fruit on *shabbat*. Their only dispute is how to resolve the *muktza* problem; neither doubts that without *shemitah*, the fruit would be inaccessible. The question, then, remains: why could one not simply snack?

The Gemara responds for the *chachamim*: *shani hasam*, the situation is different, *keivan de'amar mikan ve'ad kan ani ochel lemachar* — since the *chachamim* require one to declare "from here to here I will eat tomorrow," that verbal designation is itself what renders the eating *keva*. It is not *shabbat* that is *koveia*; it is the act of specific designation. One is caught, as it were, in a halachic bind: the designation is necessary to escape *muktza*, but the very act of designating transforms the food into *achilat keva*.

This resolution works for the *chachamim* in a way it did not for Rabbi Eliezer, because the *motaro chozer* objection applied only to Rabbi Eliezer. It was Rabbi Eliezer who held that returning the remainder preserves *arai* status. The *chachamim*, by contrast, do not recognize that principle — for them, even returning the remainder does not necessarily preserve *arai* status. Hence the verbal designation can indeed function as *koveia* according to the *chachamim* without being undercut by the *motaro chozer* reasoning.

The Gemara then asks: *ei hachi, mai irya shabbat, afilu bechol nami* — if the *chiddush* is merely that verbal designation constitutes *keva*, why is this being taught in the context of *shabbat* and *muktza*? Why not simply teach it in a weekday context regarding *terumot u'ma'asrot*? The Gemara responds: *ha kamashma lan de'tevel muchan hu eitzel shabbat* — the *mishna* is teaching an additional ruling, namely that even *tevel* — produce rabbinically forbidden to eat because *terumot u'ma'asrot* have not been separated — does not acquire *muktza* status on *shabbat*, provided that *de'im avar v'tikno metukan*, if someone *b'dieved* went ahead and separated *terumot u'ma'asrot* on *shabbat*, the produce is rendered permissible and is not treated as *muktza*. This is a genuinely striking *halacha*: ordinarily we might assume that anything forbidden when *shabbat* commenced, whether by biblical or rabbinic prohibition, ought to be treated as *muktza*. Yet because food occupies such a central and expected place in *shabbat* life, even rabbinically forbidden produce does not become *muktza* in the full sense — it retains, as it were, a latent fitness. The *muktza* problem in the *mishna* arises not from the *tevel* status itself but from the physical state of the fruit, which is still in the process of drying out on the rooftop, and it is that condition — not *tevel* — that creates the *muktza* concern.

This is the *mishna*'s actual *chiddush* being conveyed through the *shabbat* context: *lechatchila* one cannot designate *tevel* for eating on *shabbat*, but *b'dieved*, the fixing does not retroactively render it *muktza*.

### Grapes Mishna

The Gemara now raises a *ramei* — a contradiction — from another *mishna* in *Terumos*. The case: *haya ochel be'eshkol venichnas migina l'chatzer* — one was eating from a cluster of grapes (not *nigmar melachto*, since grapes are intended to become wine), and then walked from the garden into the courtyard. Entering the *chatzer* would normally be significant if the produce were *nigmar melachto*, but here it is not. Nevertheless, a dispute arises: Rabbi Yehoshua says one may not finish the snacking; Rabbi Eliezer says go ahead and finish.

Then comes the directly relevant case: *chashcha beleilei shabbat* — one was snacking on these grapes on Friday, and *shabbat* arrived. Rabbi Eliezer says one may finish eating; Rabbi Yehoshua says one may not. This appears to be a direct and explicit dispute about whether *shabbat* is *koveia* — exactly the question the Gemara has been circling around. Rabbi Yehoshua holds *shabbat* is *koveia*, Rabbi Eliezer holds it is not. Yet this contradicts what we established earlier — that according to Rabbi Eliezer, *shabbat* should be *koveia* (since he is even more expansive about such things, holding that *teruma* itself is *koveia*).

### Both Agree Shabbat

The Gemara resolves this through a teaching of Rabbi Natan: when Rabbi Eliezer said "finish eating" in the *chatzer* case, he did not mean to continue eating while inside the *chatzer* — he meant take the grapes *out* of the *chatzer* and finish them there. The *chatzer* does not permanently change the status of the grapes, but it does mean one may not eat from them while inside it; remove them, and the snacking is permissible again.

Similarly, when Rabbi Eliezer said "finish eating" in the *shabbat* case, he did not mean to continue eating on *shabbat* itself. He meant *mamtin lemotzaei shabbat veyigmor* — wait until after *shabbat* and finish the grapes then. The onset of *shabbat* does not permanently alter the status of the grapes such that they become forbidden even on Sunday; it only means that eating them on *shabbat* itself cannot be considered mere snacking.

This clarifies what *shabbat koveia* actually means. It does not mean that the grapes' halachic status is transformed permanently once *shabbat* passes over them. The rationale for *shabbat koveia* — *ve'karata la'shabbat oneg* — is that eating on *shabbat* is inherently weighty and significant. Hence eating on *shabbat* itself cannot be characterized as *arai*. But that applies to the act of eating on *shabbat*, not to the ontological status of the grapes. Once it is after *shabbat*, the grapes remain exactly as they were before, and the earlier, unfinished *gemar melacha* means one may still snack from them. (Rashi notes, however, that if one had specifically designated the grapes for *shabbat* — setting them aside and declaring their purpose — then even after *shabbat* the status may be different, but that is a separate question from where the grapes simply happen to be present when *shabbat* arrives.)

With this understanding, the contradiction within Rabbi Eliezer's position dissolves. In the *mishna* regarding *erev shabbat*, Rabbi Eliezer's implication that one could not snack reflects his view that *shabbat* is indeed *koveia* in the sense that eating on *shabbat* cannot be *arai*. In the grapes case, his ruling to "finish eating" means waiting until *motzaei shabbat* — he is not contradicting himself; he is consistent. Eating on *shabbat* is forbidden, but the grapes' status is not permanently changed.

What emerges is striking: both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua agree that one cannot eat the grapes on *shabbat* itself. Their dispute is only about whether after *shabbat* the grapes retain the restriction. Rabbi Eliezer says after *shabbat* one may resume; Rabbi Yehoshua says the restriction persists even afterward. Both, however, accept that *shabbat* is *koveia* in the sense that eating on *shabbat* from unfinished produce constitutes *achilat keva*. This makes the Gemara's earlier contortions to avoid that conclusion seem, in retrospect, puzzling — but such is the nature of this particular *sugya*.

### R. Yochanan: Gemar Melac…

Despite all of the foregoing evidence pushing toward the conclusion that *shabbat* is *koveia*, a definitive ruling arrives from Eretz Yisrael. *Ki asa Ravin amar Rabbi Yochanan*: whether it is *shabbat*, whether it is *teruma*, whether it is a *chatzer*, whether it is a *mekach* — a sale or purchase — none of these makes unfinished produce *keva*. *Kulam ein kovin ela bedavar shenigmerah melachtan*: all of them operate only where the produce has already reached *gemar melacha*. Before that point, no halachic event — not the arrival of *shabbat*, not the separation of *teruma*, not bringing the produce into a *chatzer*, not a commercial transaction — changes the produce's fundamental status. One may still snack from it. All of the readings of the *mishna* and the positions attributed to various *tannaim* that pointed toward *shabbat* or *teruma* being *koveia* are thereby set aside, at least as normative *halacha*.

## Rejecting Positions

### Shabbat Not Koveia

Having stated Rabbi Yochanan's overarching ruling, the Gemara proceeds to work through each of the positions being rejected, one by one.

Regarding *shabbat*: it is not *koveia* for produce that has not yet reached *gemar melacha*. This is stated as a positive ruling, and immediately the Gemara specifies against whom it is directed.

### Hillel Alone

*Ve'apukei mide'Hillel*: Rabbi Yochanan's ruling excludes the position of Hillel. A *baraisa* is cited: *hama'avir peiros mimakom lemakom lekatzetz* — one who is transporting fruit to be dried out (hence *lo nigmerah melachtan*), and *shabbat* arrives (*vekidesh aleihem hayom*). Amar Rabbi Yehuda: *Hillel le'atzmo oser* — Hillel alone held that this was now forbidden, that *shabbat* made the eating problematic. This is a peculiar state of affairs. The Gemara has just spent a sustained discussion showing that various *tannaim* — Rabbi Eliezer, Rabbi Yehoshua — seemed to accept that *shabbat* is *koveia*, at least for eating on *shabbat* itself. Yet now the Gemara identifies Hillel as the lone voice for that position and uses his isolation to marginalize it. The effect is to narrow the camp of *shabbat koveia* proponents to a single, minority *tanna*, and on that basis to rule that we do not *pasken* accordingly.

### Chatzer Not Koveia

Similarly, bringing unfinished produce into a *chatzer* does not make eating from it *keva*. This rejects the position of Rabbi Yaakov. *De'tnan*: the *mishna* in *Ma'asrot* teaches that one who transports figs (*hama'avir te'einim bechatzeiro lekatzetz*) — and members of his household may eat from them *b'arai* and are exempt from *ma'aser*. The *mishna* is explicit that the *chatzer* does not change the status of produce not yet at *gemar melacha*. *Ve'tanya alah*: a *baraisa* on that *mishna* teaches that Rabbi Yaakov holds them *chayav* — once the produce enters the *chatzer*, he regards it as *koveia*. Rabbi Yose be'Rabbi Yehuda exempts. Rabbi Yochanan's ruling sides with the exempting position, against Rabbi Yaakov.

### Teruma Not Koveia

Nor does the act of taking *teruma* from unprocessed produce lock the remainder into permanent *tevel* status that would preclude snacking. The relevant *mishna* states it plainly: *perot she'traman ad shelo nigmar melachtan* — fruit from which *teruma* was separated before completion of processing. *Rabbi Eliezer oser le'echol mehem arai, ve'chachamim matirin*. Rabbi Yochanan follows the *chachamim*. The taking of *teruma*, however significant a halachic act, does not alter the physical-halachic reality that the produce remains in an incomplete state and may still be eaten incidentally.

### Mekach Not Koveia

Nor does purchase. One might have thought that a commercial transaction transforms the identity of the produce and removes it from the category of "still in process." A *baraisa* disproves this.

### Figs Purchase Baraita

*Halokeach te'einim me'am ha'aretz* — one who purchases figs from an *am ha'aretz* in a locale where *rov bnei adam dorsim*, where the majority of people trample the figs into pressed rounds and do not eat them fresh, meaning the figs are not yet at *gemar melacha* for most purchasers: *ochel mehen arai, u'me'asran demai*. One may snack from them, and one treats them as *demai* — subject only to rabbinic doubt-obligation for *ma'asrot* — rather than as *vaday tevel*, which would be a biblical prohibition.

The Gemara notes that *shma mina tlas* — three teachings emerge. First and most directly relevant: *mekach eina kova'at* — the act of purchase does not make the unfinished produce *keva*; one may still eat from it *b'arai*. Second: *rov amei ha'aretz me'asrin hen* — the majority of *amei ha'aretz* do tithe, which explains why even when purchasing from an *am ha'aretz* the obligation is only *demai* and not *vaday*. Third, and most striking: *me'asrin demai me'am ha'aretz afilu bedavar shelo nigmar melacha* — one takes *demai* from an *am ha'aretz* even when the processing is not yet complete. This is a genuinely bold ruling. One might have thought that at this earlier stage — where there is both the general doubt attendant on *am ha'aretz* produce and the added consideration that most people have not yet tithed at this stage — the result should be a *vaday* obligation, not merely *demai*. Nevertheless, the Gemara teaches that we still assume the majority probably did tithe even at this earlier stage, and the obligation remains at the level of *demai*.

This *baraisa* stands in tension with another *mishna* (*ha'machlif peiros im chaveiro*) that implies *mekach* does render unfinished produce obligated. In that *mishna*, one exchanges one's fruit for another's — whether both are drying out, or one is fresh and one is drying — and in all cases one is *chayav*. The exchange constitutes a kind of sale, and the *mishna* appears to say that even where the produce is not yet at *gemar melacha*, the sale is *koveia*. Rabbi Yehuda, however, disputes the blanket ruling and holds *le'echol chayav u'le'katzos patur* — if the produce is designated for eating, the purchaser is *chayav*, but if it is designated for drying (not yet at *gemar melacha*), the sale is not *koveia* and one may still eat *b'arai*. Rabbi Yochanan rules in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda's narrower position.

### Only Gemar Melacha

The conclusion is unambiguous. We began the *sugya* with the question of whether *shabbat* is *koveia* for produce not yet at *gemar melacha*. Through an extended and at times convoluted discussion, the Gemara examined the *mishna* from multiple angles, explored the positions of Rabbi Eliezer, Rabbi Yehoshua, Hillel, Rabbi Yaakov, and others, and progressively dismantled each position that attributed *koveia* status to anything other than *gemar melacha* itself. The statement of Rabbi Yochanan is the definitive resolution: none of these factors — not *shabbat*, not *chatzer*, not *teruma*, not *mekach* — changes the fundamental status of produce that has not yet completed its processing. Only *gemar melacha* does that. Before that threshold is crossed, all of these events are halachically inert with respect to the obligation in *terumot u'ma'asrot*, and the produce may still be eaten in a temporary fashion.

## Yom Tov Food Handling

### Skylight Lowering

With *Hadran Alach Hamavi*, the Gemara transitions to the final *perek* of the tractate. Much of the preceding *perek* addressed questions around fire, cooking, and the prohibition against fashioning vessels even in the course of food preparation. This final *perek* will focus significantly on questions of *techum* — the *shabbat* boundary — and how *techum* restrictions intersect with food on *Yom Tov*. But the *mishna* opens with a different issue: the general question of physical effort (*tircha*) and its limits on *Yom Tov*.

*Mashilin peiros derech arubbah b'Yom Tov* — one may lower fruit through a skylight on *Yom Tov*. The scenario is that fruit has been drying on the rooftop; whether the *muktza* concern has been resolved through prior designation or because *gemar melacha* has been completed, the fruit is accessible. One wishes to bring it inside. The *mishna* permits lowering it through the skylight. *Aval lo beShabbat* — but not on *shabbat*. The difference is the principle being invoked. The *chiddush* of the *mishna* is permitting this act on *Yom Tov* despite the concern of *tircha yeteira* — excessive effort. There is room to argue whether this is better characterized as a *tircha* problem or as *uvda de'chol* — carrying on weekday-like labor — since hauling baskets of fruit down from a rooftop evokes the mundane work of ordinary weekdays. The skylight method, however, involves a *shinui* (deviation from the weekday manner) and does not convey the same sense of weekday labor, which is why it is permitted. On *shabbat*, however, even this is forbidden.

### Covering from Rain

*U'mechassin peirot bikeilim mipnei ha'delef* — one may cover fruit with vessels to protect them from rain. Similarly, pitchers of wine or oil may be covered if rain threatens to fall in and ruin their contents. These acts are permitted on *Yom Tov*. By contrast, the *Yerushalmi* adds — and the parallel is preserved in the *mishna*'s structure — that such covering is not done on *shabbat*.

### Vessel Under Drip

*Notnin kli tachat ha'delef beShabbat* — one may, however, place a vessel underneath a dripping hole in the roof even on *shabbat*. This is taught in *Masechet Shabbat* as well and is permitted despite the various restrictions that attach to *shabbat*.

### Rashi: Hefsed Mammon

Rashi's analysis of this *mishna* is instructive. He characterizes the problem in all these cases as *tircha* — excessive effort — rather than primarily *uvda de'chol*. More importantly, he explains why the *mishna* permits covering the fruit and pitchers on *Yom Tov* even though these acts involve some effort and are not being done for immediate benefit today: *mishum hefsed mamon*, because of financial loss. The produce or wine will be ruined if not protected, and the concern for *hefsed mamon* provides sufficient grounds for leniency.

This raises a conceptual question. The phrase Rashi uses — *shelo letzorech Yom Tov* — suggests that the problem is not merely the quantity of effort but whether the act is being performed for the benefit of *Yom Tov* itself. The general license of *ochel nefesh* on *Yom Tov* permits effort expended for the purpose of eating and benefiting on the day itself. Covering produce from rain, however, does not benefit one today — the benefit is preventing future loss. This is analogous to a common question about returning milk to a refrigerator toward the end of *shabbat*: if one is doing so to prevent spoilage rather than to prepare for a weekday use, this should not constitute *hachana*; one is protecting one's property, not preparing for afterwards. Similarly here, no one claims that covering the fruit is *hachana* for a weekday. But neither is it *ochel nefesh*. The question is how much latitude is given to protect property on *Yom Tov*, and Rashi's answer is that *hefsed mamon* provides that latitude.

## Textual Variants

### Four Verb Options

The Gemara records a dispute between Rav Yehuda and Rav Natan concerning the precise verb used in the *mishna*. One *tanna* read the *mishna* as *mashilin*, another as *mashchilin*. Both versions are transmitted. Mar Zutra states that neither reading is necessarily a corruption — *man de'tani mashilin lo mishtabash, u'man de'tani mashchilin lo mishtabash*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds further that a third version, *mashirin*, is equally defensible, and — though Rashi notes that no one actually claimed this as their text — a fourth form, *menashirin*, could also be legitimate. All four verbs share a rough semantic range having to do with dropping, falling off, or lowering, and each can be traced to a biblical or *mishnaic* precedent.

### Mashilin: Drop

*Man de'tani mashilin lo mishtabash* — the form *mashilin* is supported by the verse *ki yashal zeitecha*, "your olive tree will drop its fruit" (Devarim 28:40). The verb *yashal* means for fruit to fall from a tree, and *mashilin* would accordingly mean to cause fruit to drop — entirely appropriate for the act of sending fruit down through the skylight.

### Mashchilin: Dislocate

*U'man de'tani mashchilin lo mishtabash* — the form *mashchilin* is supported by the *mishnaic* terms *ha'shchul ve'ha'kasul*, two types of *mumim* (blemishes) that disqualify an animal. *Shchul shenishmeta yarecho* — the animal's thigh has been dislocated; *kasul she-echad mi'yarchotav gavoah me'chaverta* — one thigh is positioned higher than the other. In both cases, the root *shin-chet-lamed* conveys some form of dislocation or displacement from a normal position, and *mashchilin* could therefore similarly mean to move or shift fruit downward through the skylight.

### Mashirin: Fall

*Man de'tani mashirin lo mishtabash* — the form *mashirin* is supported by a *mishnaic* teaching regarding *nazir*: Rabbi Yishmael says a *nazir* may not wash his hair with earth, *mipnei she'meshir et ha'se'ar* — because doing so causes the hair to fall out, and a *nazir* is forbidden to cut his hair. The verb *meshir* thus means to cause to fall off, and *mashirin* would mean to cause the fruit to fall — an apt description of lowering them through the opening.

### Menashirin: Drop Off

*U'man de'tani menashirin lo mishtabash* — the form *menashirin* is supported by the *mishna* regarding *nazir*, through a different formulation: *mi she'nashru kelav ba'mayim*, one whose garments fell into the water, may walk in them and need not be concerned (people will not suspect he did laundry on *shabbat*; it is obvious the clothes accidentally fell in). The root *nun-shin-reish* carries the meaning of falling or dropping off. More directly, the Gemara adds: *eizu leket — ha'nosher bi'sh'at ketzirah* — *leket* is defined as grain that falls during the act of harvesting. The word *nosher* and its cognate *menashirin* thus share the meaning of grain or produce dropping off, which is relevant both to the agricultural imagery of the *mishna* and to the earlier discussion in the tractate of *gezeirat peirot ha'noshrin* — the rabbinic concern about fruit that falls from trees. All four forms, then, converge on the same basic meaning, and the Gemara is content to note that none of them represents a corrupt text.
