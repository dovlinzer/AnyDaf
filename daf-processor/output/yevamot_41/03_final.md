# Yevamot 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shniyot B'Chalutza

### Shniyot Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: גָּזְרוּ שְׁנִיּוֹת בַּחֲלוּצָה, אוֹ לֹא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: In addition to the Torah prohibition against engaging in relations with one's wife's close relatives, the Sages decreed that it is also prohibited to engage in relations with one's wife's secondary relatives, i.e., those who are less closely related and so are permitted by Torah law. Since the Sages decreed that a *ḥalutza* is to be regarded as though she had actually been married to the one who performed *ḥalitza*, such that her close relatives are forbidden to him, did **they** also **decree** that the prohibition of **secondary** forbidden **relationships** should also apply **in** the case of **a *ḥalutza*, or not?**

The Gemara on 41b opens with a focused question: *gazru shniyot b'chalutza o lo?* When the *chalutza* is treated like a *gerusha* and the relatives of the woman with whom *chalitza* was performed become forbidden to him, does that prohibition extend beyond the biblical *arayot* to include the rabbinic secondary prohibitions — the *shniyot l'arayot* — as well? The *mishna* sounds as though it does, but the Gemara subjects that assumption to careful scrutiny.

### D'Oraisa vs. D'Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעֶרְוָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן שְׁנִיּוֹת, בַּחֲלוּצָה — לָא גְּזַרוּ רַבָּנַן שְׁנִיּוֹת, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents the sides of the dilemma: Did **the Sages decree** that the prohibition of **secondary** forbidden **relationships** applies only **in** a case where there is a relative with whom **relations** are **forbidden by Torah** law, but **in** the case of a ***ḥalutza,** whose relatives are forbidden only by rabbinic decree, **they did not decree** that there should be a prohibition of **secondary** forbidden **relationships? Or perhaps** the case of a ***ḥalutza* is no different,** and since the basis of the rabbinic prohibition applying to her relatives is that it is considered as though she had actually been married to her *yavam*, the prohibition should extend to her secondary relatives as well?

The Gemara's initial answer draws a principled distinction: *d'oraisa gazru rabbanan shniyot; b'chalutza lo gazru rabbanan shniyot.* The secondary prohibitions are applied where the man was actually married to the woman — a biblical relationship — but not in the case of the *chalutza*, whose status as a quasi-wife is itself only rabbinic. Before proceeding, it is worth noting a broader point: the *chalutza* is treated as a *gerusha* for the purposes of a *kohen*, and there is some evidence in tannaic literature that this status was understood as *d'oraisa*, though the Gemara insists it is rabbinic. In any event, the prohibition on a *kohen* marrying a *chalutza* is a distinct question from whether the *arayot* framework — with all its secondary extensions — applies to the *zika* relationship created by *chalitza*.

### Ima v'Eim Ima

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הוּא אָסוּר בְּאִמָּהּ וּבְאֵם אִמָּהּ, וְאִילּוּ ״אֵם אֵם אִמָּהּ״ — לָא קָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: A *yavam* **is prohibited** from engaging in relations **with** the **mother of** his *ḥalutza* **and with her mother's mother. However,** the mishna **does not teach** that it is prohibited to engage in relations with **her mother's mother's mother,** which is one of the secondary relationships. This would suggest that the prohibition of secondary relationships does not apply in this case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּלָא תָּנֵי, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָבָעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי סֵיפָא ״וְהָאַחִין מוּתָּרִין״, וְאִי תָּנָא ״אֵם אֵם אִמָּהּ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָאַחִין מוּתָּרִין דַּוְקָא בְּאֵם אֵם אִמָּהּ, אֲבָל בְּאֵם אִמָּהּ וּבְאִמָּהּ — לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **Perhaps this is the reason that** the mishna **does not teach** that case, **because it needs to teach in the latter clause:** However, **the** other **brothers** who did not perform *ḥalitza* **are permitted** to her relatives. **And if it** had **taught** the prohibition of **her mother's mother's mother** in the first clause, **I would** mistakenly **say** that **the brothers are permitted specifically to her mother's mother's mother, but not to her mother's mother.** Therefore, no proof can be adduced from that case of the mishna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי ״אֵם אֵם אִמָּהּ״, וְלִיתְנֵי ״הָאַחִין מוּתָּרִין בְּכוּלָּן״! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** if he is forbidden to the great-grandmother, then **let it teach: Her mother's mother's mother** in the first clause, **and** then **let it teach: The brothers are permitted to all of them,** in the latter clause. This would avoid the concern stated above. The fact that the mishna did not do so would suggest that he is not forbidden to the great-grandmother, or any other secondary relatives. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, this is **difficult,** but still inconclusive.

To test this principle, the Gemara turns to the *mishna*'s specific examples. The *mishna* states that the man who performed *chalitza* is forbidden in his *chalutza*'s mother and grandmother — *ima u'veim ima* — but does not mention the great-grandmother. Since the great-grandmother would represent a rabbinic *shniya*, the omission seems to prove that *shniyot* do not apply to the *chalutza*. The Gemara deflects: perhaps the reason the great-grandmother is not mentioned is simply that the *mishna* then goes on to teach the leniency that the brothers are permitted with all these relatives, and had the *mishna* mentioned the great-grandmother, a reader might mistakenly think the brothers are permitted only with the great-grandmother but not with the grandmother and mother. By stopping at grandmother, the *mishna* prevents that misreading. The Gemara counters: but why not simply state explicitly that both the man is forbidden in the great-grandmother *and* the brothers are permitted with all of them? *Kashya* — this is difficult. So there remains a strong suggestion that the *mishna* stops at grandmother because *shniyot* are not applied to the *chalutza*.

### Aviv v'Avi Aviv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הִיא אֲסוּרָה בְּאָבִיו וּבַאֲבִי אָבִיו. קָתָנֵי מִיהָא אֲבִי אָבִיו, מַאי לָאו, מִשּׁוּם חוֹלֵץ דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ כַּלַּת בְּנוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof from the mishna: **She is prohibited** from engaging in relations **with his father, and with his father's father.** The Gemara explains the proof: **In any case,** the mishna **teaches** that she is forbidden to **his father's father. What, is it not** that she is forbidden to him **due to** the rabbinic decree that it is considered as though she had actually been married to **the one who performed *ḥalitza*** with her, and therefore she is forbidden to the grandfather because **she is considered to be** the grandfather's **son's daughter-in-law,** i.e., the wife of one of the grandfather's grandsons, which is a secondary relationship? If so, the mishna explicitly demonstrates that the prohibition of secondary relationships also applies in the case of a *ḥalutza*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, מִשּׁוּם מִיתָנָא, דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ כַּלַּת בְּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **No,** she is forbidden to his grandfather **due to** her previous marriage with **the deceased,** whose relatives are forbidden to her by Torah law, and it is due to that relationship **that she is considered** the grandfather's **son's daughter-in-law.** Therefore, no proof can be adduced from that case of the mishna.

The Gemara tries a different case: the *mishna* states the *chalutza* is forbidden with his father and his grandfather — *aviv u'vavi aviv*. This becomes more interesting when analyzed carefully. Why cannot she marry the father? Because from the man's perspective, she is his daughter-in-law — an explicit biblical *erva*. But the grandfather? She would be his son's daughter-in-law — a *shniya l'arayot*, a second-degree rabbinic prohibition. So it seems the *mishna* applies *shniyot* to the *chalutza* status.

The Gemara replies: *lo mishum mishna* — this is not because of the *chalitz* himself (the man who performed *chalitza*, here called *Shimon*), but rather *mishum mes* — because of the deceased brother (*Reuven*). In other words, the grandfather is also Reuven's grandfather, and she is his son's daughter-in-law through the biblical relationship with Reuven, not through the rabbinic *chalutza* relationship with Shimon. It is a *shniya l'arayot*, yes, but generated by the *d'oraisa* relationship with Reuven, not by the *d'rabbanan* relationship with Shimon.

### Reuven vs. Shimon

This logic drives the entire analysis of the subsequent cases in the *mishna*, and it is worth pausing to appreciate its structure. Throughout these cases, what looks like *shniyot* applied to the *chalutza* relationship turns out, on examination, to be *shniyot* applied to the underlying biblical relationship with the deceased husband. The prohibition attaches because of Reuven, not because of Shimon.

To illustrate: if Shimon performed *chalitza* with Rachel, and the question is whether she may marry Shimon's grandfather Yitzchak, the answer is no — but the reason is not that Yitzchak's relationship to Rachel through Shimon's *chalutza* status generates a *shniya*. The reason is that Yitzchak is also Reuven's grandfather, and Rachel is Reuven's daughter-in-law through the biblical marriage, making this a *shniya* derived from the *d'oraisa* relationship, not the *d'rabbanan* one.

This framing — that all these prohibitions trace to Reuven and not to Shimon — is how the Gemara defends the position that *shniyot* do not apply to the *chalutza* relationship per se.

### Rabi Chiya Arba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אַרְבַּע מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, וְאַרְבַּע מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים: אָב וּבְנוֹ, אָחִיו, וּבֶן אָחִיו — מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה. אֲבִי אָבִיו, וַאֲבִי אִמּוֹ, בֶּן בְּנוֹ, וּבֶן בִּתּוֹ — מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita* **that Rabbi Ḥiyya taught:** Of the relatives who are forbidden to a *ḥalutza*, **four** are forbidden **by Torah law and four by rabbinic law,** as follows: **The father** of the deceased husband, **and his son, his brother, and his brother's son** are forbidden **by Torah law; his father's father, and his mother's father, his son's son, and his daughter's son** are forbidden **by rabbinic law.**

This analysis receives its most explicit formulation in a *beraita* of Rabbi Chiyya: *d'tani Rabbi Chiyya arba midivrei Torah v'arba midivrei Soferim.* Among those forbidden to the *chalutza*, four categories are forbidden based on biblical relationships and four based on rabbinic ones. The four biblical categories are his father, his son, his brother, and his brother's son — all of whom, when analyzed from the perspective of the relative in question, stand in a relationship of biblical *erva* to Rachel through their connection to Reuven. For example, why is Shimon's brother's son forbidden? Because from that son's perspective, Rachel is his father's brother's wife — aunt — which is biblical.

The four rabbinic categories — grandfather (*avi aviv*), maternal grandfather (*avi imo*), grandson (*ben bno*), and daughter's son (*ben bito*) — are *midivrei Soferim*, and it is explicit that they represent *shniyot*. The Gemara then goes through each of these, attempting to demonstrate that even these four could be explained through the biblical Reuven relationship rather than the rabbinic Shimon relationship.

### Avi Imo Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֲבִי אִמּוֹ. מַאי לָאו, מִשּׁוּם חוֹלֵץ, דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ כַּלַּת בִּתּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to consider whether proof may be adduced from each of the other secondary relationships listed in the *baraita*: **Come** and **hear** a proof from that which the *baraita* teaches: She is forbidden to **his mother's father.** The Gemara explains the proof: **What, is it not** that she is forbidden to him **due to** the rabbinic decree that it is considered as though she had actually been married to **the one who performed *ḥalitza*** with her, and therefore she is forbidden to the grandfather because **she is considered to be** the grandfather's **daughter's daughter-in-law,** which is a secondary relationship? This would prove that the prohibition of secondary relationships applies in the case of a *ḥalutza*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, מִשּׁוּם מִיתָנָא, דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ כַּלַּת בִּתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **No,** she is forbidden **due to** her previous marriage with **the deceased,** whose relatives are forbidden to her by Torah law, and it is due to that relationship that **she is considered** the grandfather's **daughter's daughter-in-law.** Therefore, no proof can be adduced from that case of the *baraita*.

Take the case of *avi imo*, the maternal grandfather — in our terms, Lavan, the father of Rachel's mother Leah. The Gemara asks: is this forbidden because of Shimon's relationship to Rachel (*mishum choletz*), making this his daughter's daughter-in-law, a *shniya l'arayot*? Or is it because of Reuven (*mishum migareish*), making it Reuven's daughter's daughter-in-law? The Gemara gives the same answer — it is because of Reuven. But this requires a slight digression. The natural extension of *shniyot* follows within the same gender — a son-in-law is biblical, so a son's son-in-law is rabbinic. The extension to daughter's son-in-law, crossing genders, is less obvious but nevertheless how the system operates. The answer remains the same in structure: yes, it is a *shniya*, but it derives from the biblical relationship with Reuven, not from the rabbinic *chalutza* relationship with Shimon.

### Ben Beno Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וּבֶן בְּנוֹ. מַאי לָאו, מִשּׁוּם חוֹלֵץ, דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ אֵשֶׁת אֲבִי אָבִיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to seek proof for those who disagree with Ameimar: **Come** and **hear** a proof from that which the *baraita* teaches: **And** she is forbidden to **his son's son.** The Gemara explains the proof: **What, is it not** that she is forbidden to him **due to** the rabbinic decree that it is considered as though she had actually been married to the **one who performed *ḥalitza*** with her, and so she is forbidden to his grandson because **she is considered to be the wife** of that grandson's **father's father,** which is a secondary relationship? If so, the *baraita* explicitly demonstrates that the prohibition of secondary relationships applies in the case of a *ḥalutza*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, מִשּׁוּם מִיתָנָא, וְהָוְיָא לַהּ אֵשֶׁת אֲחִי אֲבִי אָבִיו. וְהָא אַמֵּימָר מַכְשִׁיר בְּאֵשֶׁת אֲחִי אֲבִי אָבִיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **No,** she is forbidden to his grandson **due to** her previous marriage with **the deceased,** whose relatives are forbidden to her by Torah law, and due to that relationship **she is considered to be the wife of a brother of** that grandson's **father's father,** which is also a forbidden secondary relationship. Therefore, no proof can be adduced from that case of the *baraita*. The Gemara asks: **But didn't Ameimar rule permissible** the marriage of a man **with the wife of a brother of his father's father?** Accordingly, the prohibition in the mishna cannot be due to that relationship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמֵּימָר מוֹקֵים לַהּ מִשּׁוּם חוֹלֵץ, וְקָסָבַר גָּזְרוּ שְׁנִיּוֹת בַּחֲלוּצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concedes: **Ameimar** would **establish** the mishna's case of: His son's son, **as** being **due to** the rabbinic decree that it is considered as though she had actually been married to **the one who performed *ḥalitza*** with her, **and** as such it is evident that **he holds** that the Sages **decreed** that the prohibition of **secondary** forbidden **relationships** should apply **in** the case of **a *ḥalutza*.**

The Gemara then turns to the case of *ben bno* — Shimon's grandson. From the grandson's perspective, Rachel would be his grandfather's wife — *eshes avi aviv* — a rabbinic *shniya*. The Gemara asks whether this prohibition flows from the *chalutza* status (*mishum choletz*). The Gemara replies: *lo mishum mifna* — no, because through Reuven's relationship, Rachel is the great-uncle's wife (*eshes achi avi aviv*), which is itself a *shniya l'arayot* derived from the biblical concept of uncle's wife.

At this point the Gemara introduces a critical complication. *Ameimar machshir b'eshes achi avi aviv* — Ameimar holds that a great-uncle's wife is not subject to *shniyot* at all, and therefore the Reuven-based explanation is unavailable for the *ben bno* case. The Gemara acknowledges that Ameimar's position cannot be rescued by pushing things up a generation, as was done in earlier cases, when working within Rabbi Chiyya's *beraita*. Therefore the Gemara concludes: *Ameimar mokim lah mishum choletz* — Ameimar is forced to ground the prohibition in Shimon's *chalutza* relationship to Rachel, *v'kasavar gazru shniyot b'chalutza* — and Ameimar would have to concede that *shniyot* do apply to the *chalutza* relationship. This proves the point for Ameimar's position, at least.

### Ben Bito

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וּבְבֶן בִּתּוֹ. מַאי לָאו, מִשּׁוּם חוֹלֵץ, דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ אֵשֶׁת אֲבִי אִמּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to seek proof for those who disagree with Ameimar: **Come** and **hear** a proof from that which the *baraita* teaches: **And** she is forbidden to **his daughter's son.** The Gemara explains the proof: **What, is it not** that she is forbidden to him **due to** the rabbinic decree that it is considered as though she had actually been married to the **one who performed *ḥalitza*** with her, and so **she is** forbidden to his grandson because **she is considered to be the wife** of that grandson's **mother's father,** which is a secondary relationship? If so, the *baraita* explicitly demonstrates that the prohibition of secondary relationships applies in the case of a *ḥalutza*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, מִשּׁוּם מִיתָנָא, דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ אֵשֶׁת אֲחִי אֲבִי אִמּוֹ. וְהָא גַּבֵּי שְׁנִיּוֹת דְּעֶרְוָה לֹא גָּזְרוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **No,** she is forbidden to his grandson **due to** her previous marriage with **the deceased,** whose relatives are forbidden to her by Torah law, and due to that relationship **she is considered to be the wife of a brother of** the grandson's **mother's father.** The Gemara challenges this: **But** the Sages did **not decree** that such a person is included **among** those relatives who are forbidden as **secondary** forbidden **relationships.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו, מִשּׁוּם חוֹלֵץ, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ גָּזְרוּ שְׁנִיּוֹת בַּחֲלוּצָה? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not,** as the Gemara initially assumed, that she is forbidden to him **due to** the rabbinic decree that it is considered as though she had actually been married to **the one who performed *ḥalitza*** with her, **and** therefore must one not **conclude from** this case that the Sages **decreed** that the prohibition of **secondary** forbidden **relationships** should apply **in** the case of **a *ḥalutza*?** The Gemara confirms: One should indeed **conclude from it** that this is so.

The Gemara presses further with the case of *ben bito* — the daughter's son. From the perspective of this descendant, Rachel would be his mother's father's wife. The Gemara attempts the same Reuven-based explanation: she is the wife of his mother's father's brother (*eshes achi avi imo*). But the Gemara then recognizes that a mother's father's brother's wife is simply not recognized as a *shniya l'arayot* — *shniyot de'erva lo gazru* for that relationship. Therefore the Reuven-based explanation fails entirely, and the prohibition can only be grounded in the *chalutza* relationship with Shimon. *Shema mina gazru shniyot b'chalutza shema mina* — this establishes definitively that *shniyot* do apply to the *chalutza* relationship.

## Tzaras Krovat Chalutza

### Mutar vs. Assur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּתָּר אָדָם בִּקְרוֹבַת צָרַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ. וְאָסוּר בְּצָרַת קְרוֹבַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states an additional principle: **A man is permitted** to engage in relations **with a relative of a rival wife of his *ḥalutza*,** i.e., his *yevama* with whom he performed *ḥalitza*. Since he did not perform *ḥalitza* with her, she is not regarded as though she had actually been married to him. However, **he is forbidden** to engage in relations **with a rival wife of a relative of his *ḥalutza*,** i.e., in addition to being forbidden to the relatives of his *ḥalutza*, he is also forbidden to their rival wives.

The *mishna* concludes with the rule: *mutar adam b'krovos tzaras chalutzaso* — a man is permitted to marry the relatives of the co-wife (*tzara*) of his *chalutza*. To understand this, consider the setup: Reuven was married to both Rachel and Devorah. Reuven dies. Shimon performs *chalitza* with Rachel. Rachel thereby acquires *chalutza* status with respect to Shimon, making her relatives forbidden to him. But Devorah, the co-wife, never underwent *chalitza* — the act of *chalitza* was performed only with Rachel. Since the *chalutza* status attaches only to the woman with whom the act was performed, Devorah is not Shimon's *chalutza*, and her relatives remain permissible to him.

### Haba al Tzarat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּתָּר אָדָם וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב טוֹבִי בַּר קִיסְנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַבָּא עַל צָרַת חֲלוּצָה — הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר. מַאי טַעְמָא? בְּאִיסּוּרַהּ קָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **A man is permitted** to engage in relations with a relative of a rival wife of his *ḥalutza*; however, he is prohibited from engaging in relations with a rival wife of a relative of his *ḥalutza*. The Gemara cites a ruling: **Rav Tovi bar Kisna said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to **one who engages in intercourse with a rival wife of** his ***ḥalutza*, the offspring** of that union **is a *mamzer*. What is the reason** for this ruling? It is that **she remains in her** original **prohibition.**

Against this background, Rav Tuvi bar Kisna citing Shmuel teaches: *haba al tzarat chalutza, havlad mamzer* — if Shimon goes ahead and has sexual relations with Devorah, the child is a *mamzer*. The reason: *be'issura kaima* — she retains her original forbidden status. Devorah was Reuven's wife. She would have been permitted to Shimon only through the act of *yibbum*. Since no act of *yibbum* was performed — and *chalitza* was performed to Rachel, not to Devorah — Devorah remains forbidden as a brother's wife, and relations with her produce a *mamzer*.

### Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: מוּתָּר אָדָם בִּקְרוֹבַת צָרַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא צָרָה אַבָּרַאי, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מוּתָּר בַּאֲחוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: We, too, learned** this through an inference from the mishna: **A man is permitted** to engage in relations **with a relative of a rival wife of his *ḥalutza*.** Rav Yosef explains the inference: **Granted, if you say** that **a rival wife is outside,** i.e., she is not represented in the *ḥalitza* of the *ḥalutza* and so she is not afforded a status similar to that of the *ḥalutza* herself, then it is **due to that** reason that **he is permitted** to engage in relations **with her sister** and other relatives, because since they are not the relatives of his *ḥalutza*, there is no reason for them to be forbidden.

Rav Yosef infers from the *mishna*'s ruling that the *chalutza* status applies exclusively to the woman who was party to the act. The *mishna* says he is permitted to marry Devorah's relatives. If Devorah herself were considered a *chalutza*, one could not explain why her relatives are permissible — they would be the relatives of a *chalutza* and therefore forbidden. It must be, then, that Devorah has no *chalutza* status whatsoever. This explains simultaneously two things: why he may marry Devorah's relatives (she is not his *chalutza*), and why relations with Devorah produce a *mamzer* (her status as brother's wife was never altered by the *chalitza* performed to Rachel).

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: בֵּין הוּא, בֵּין אַחִין — אֵין חַיָּיבִין לֹא עַל הַחֲלוּצָה כָּרֵת, וְלֹא עַל צָרָתָהּ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say,** then, that this mishna **is a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan? As** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said: Neither he** who performed the *ḥalitza* **nor his brothers are liable to** receive ***karet*;** they are **neither** liable to receive ***karet* on account of** engaging in relations with **the *ḥalutza*, nor** are they liable to receive ***karet* on account of** engaging in relations with **her rival wife.**

A tension arises when the Gemara considers the position of Rabbi Yochanan. Rabbi Yochanan holds that neither the man nor his brothers incur *karet* for relations with either the *chalutza* herself or with her co-wife — *ein chayavin lo al hachalutza karet v'lo al tzarata karet*. This implies that Devorah's status *does* change as a consequence of the *chalitza* performed to Rachel — she is no longer considered a biblical *eshet ach* — yet the *mishna* still permits the man to marry Devorah's relatives. This seems contradictory: if her status changes enough to remove *karet*, why does she not acquire *chalutza* status sufficient to forbid her relatives?

### D'Oraisa vs. D'Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְתִסְבְּרַאּ אֲחוֹת חֲלוּצָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? וְהָאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כָּאן שָׁנָה רַבִּי אָחוֹת גְּרוּשָׁה מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, אֲחוֹת חֲלוּצָה מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rabbi Yoḥanan** could have **said to you: But** how can **you understand** it that way? Why do you assume that if the rival wives are considered to be represented in the *ḥalitza* that perforce means that their relatives should be forbidden to the *yavam*? Is **a sister of** one's ***ḥalutza*** forbidden **by Torah** law**? Didn't Reish Lakish say** with regard to another mishna (41a): **Here, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **taught:** Engaging in relations with **a sister of** one's **divorcée** is forbidden **by Torah law,** whereas engaging in relations with **a sister of** one's ***ḥalutza* is** forbidden **by rabbinic law.** Since the prohibition with regard to the relatives of one's *ḥalutza* is rabbinic, it is reasonable to assume that it was applied to only the wife who actually performed *ḥalitza* and not extended to her rival wives, even if they are considered to be represented in her *ḥalitza* to the extent that they do not return to their original forbidden status.

Rabbi Yochanan's response is instructive: *v'chi tisbara? achot chalutza d'oraita?* Does anyone think the relative of a *chalutza* is a biblical prohibition? Reish Lakish already stated — *kan shana Rabbi: achot gerusha midivrei Torah, achot chalutza midivrei Soferim* — the sister of a *gerusha* is a biblical prohibition, whereas the sister of a *chalutza* is only rabbinic. This resolution hinges on distinguishing the *d'oraisa* plane from the *d'rabbanan* plane. On the biblical level, the *chalitza* performed to Rachel transforms the status of both Rachel and Devorah — neither is any longer *eshet ach* in the full biblical sense, and *karet* no longer applies. But the rabbinic enactment that forbids the relatives of a *chalutza* is a separate layer, and that rabbinic enactment is applied only to the woman who was actually party to the act, not to Devorah who was affected incidentally.

### Mai Shna Hai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא הַאי, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara questions the distinction indicated by the mishna: **What is different** about **this** woman, the relative of a rival wife of one's *ḥalutza*, that she is permitted, **and what is different** about **that** woman, the rival wife of a relative of one's *ḥalutza*, that she is forbidden?

The Gemara then addresses the final line of the *mishna*: not only is the man permitted with the relatives of his *chalutza*'s co-wife (*krovos tzaras chalutzaso*), but he is also forbidden with the co-wife of his *chalutza*'s relative (*tzara* of *krovat chalutzato*). The question is: *mai shna hai u'mai shna hai?* — why the asymmetry? One case is permitted, the other forbidden.

### Two Brothers

This is where Tosafot offers a fundamentally different reading of the *mishna*'s scenario. According to Rashi, the *mishna* is discussing a situation where Barak — a completely external party — was married to both Leah (the *chalutza*'s relative) and some second wife Devorah. But Tosafot finds this logically unsatisfying and the Gemara's answer strained under Rashi's reading. Tosafot instead reads the *mishna* as dealing with a case of two brothers married to two sisters — Levi, another brother of Shimon and Reuven, who was married to Leah. Under this reading, the scenario makes immediate sense.

### Tzarat Ervah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָךְ דְּאָזְלָא בַּהֲדַהּ לְבֵי דִינָא, גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן. הַאי דְּלָא אָזְלָא בַּהֲדַהּ לְבֵי דִּינָא, לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: With regard to **that** woman, the relative of the *ḥalutza*, **who** often **goes together with** the *ḥalutza* **to court,** since she is present in the court during the *ḥalitza*, people might mistakenly assume that it was she who actually performed *ḥalitza*. Were the *yavam* permitted to marry her rival wife, people might incorrectly conclude that it is permitted to marry the rival wife of one's *ḥalutza*. To preclude this possibility, **the Sages decreed with regard to her** that her rival wife should be forbidden. However, with regard to **this** woman, the rival wife of the *ḥalutza*, **who does not go together with** the *ḥalutza* **to court,** since she is never present in the court during the *ḥalitza*, there is no concern that people will mistake her for the *ḥalutza* herself. Consequently, **the Sages did not** see any reason to **decree with regard to her** that her relatives should be forbidden.

Once Shimon performed *chalitza* with Rachel, Rachel becomes like his ex-wife — *achos chalutza*, which carries the status of a quasi-*erva*. Leah, as Rachel's sister, is therefore a quasi-*erva* to Shimon as well. If Levi then dies, Leah falls before Shimon for *yibbum*. But Leah is a *tzarat erva* — the co-wife of a quasi-*erva* — and just as *tzarat erva* is exempt from *yibbum* on the biblical level, this rabbinic analog creates the same exemption. The answer to *mai shna hai u'mai shna hai* is therefore self-evident under Tosafot's reading: the woman who underwent the act of *chalitza* is the source of the *chalutza* status; *hach d'azla behada l'vei dina* — she who was brought to court and had the act performed with her acquires the full *chalutza* status, from which all subsequent prohibitions derive. The co-wife who never came to court, *d'lo azla behada l'vei dina*, acquires no *chalutza* status, so her relatives remain permissible — and yet, on the *d'oraisa* plane, her own status changes such that relations with her are no longer an *issur karet*.

## Shomeres Yavam

### Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַחוֹלֵץ לִיבִמְתּוֹ, וְנָשָׂא אָחִיו אֶת אֲחוֹתָהּ, וָמֵת — חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַבֶּמֶת. וְכֵן הַמְגָרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְנָשָׂא אָחִיו אֶת אֲחוֹתָהּ, וָמֵת — הֲרֵי זוֹ פְּטוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a *yavam* **who performed *ḥalitza* with his *yevama* and** then **his brother married her sister and died,** the sister **performs *ḥalitza*** with the *yavam*, **but she may not enter into levirate marriage** with him, since as a sister of his *ḥalutza* she is forbidden to him. **And similarly,** in the case of **one who divorced his wife and his brother married her sister and died, then that** woman **is exempt** both from *ḥalitza* and from consummating levirate marriage, since as the sister of his former wife she is forbidden to him.

The next *mishna* opens a new scenario that follows naturally from the Tosafot reading just discussed. *Hacholeitz l'yevimto v'nasa achiv et achoto* — a man performed *chalitza* with his *yevama*, and his brother then married her sister. So Shimon performed *chalitza* with Rachel, and Levi comes along and marries Leah, Rachel's sister. This is precisely the scenario that Tosafot identified in the previous *mishna*'s final case. Leah is forbidden to Shimon because she is his *chalutza*'s sister — a rabbinic *erva*. But Leah is permissible to Levi, because the *chalutza* status created by the *chalitza* act applies only between Shimon and Rachel, not between Levi and Rachel. Levi's ability to marry Leah is thus unaffected.

### Hamten

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ אָחִיו אֶת אֲחוֹתָהּ — מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא אָמְרוּ, אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: הַמְתֵּן עַד שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה אָחִיךָ מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a widow waiting for her *yavam*** to consummate levirate marriage or perform *ḥalitza* with her, and the **brother of** the *yavam* **betrothed her sister, they said in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira: They say to** the brother: **Wait** and do not marry the woman you betrothed **until your brother performs an act,** either of *ḥalitza* or of consummating the levirate marriage, as until he does there remains a levirate bond between the *yevama* and each of the brothers, and it is prohibited to marry the sister of a woman to whom one is bound by a levirate bond.

The complication arises when the *mishna* introduces the scenario of *shomeres yavam* — a woman awaiting *yibbum*. Here, Rachel was not yet a *chalutza* but a *shomeres yavam*, waiting for one of the brothers to act, while Levi went ahead and performed *kiddushin* with Leah. The problem: if Levi consummates with Leah, he will be having relations with *achot zikukato* — the sister of the woman to whom he has a *zika* (levirate bond). The ruling, in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, is *hamten* — wait. Levi must wait until Shimon performs either *yibbum* or *chalitza* with Rachel. Only once Shimon has done something to sever Levi's *zika* to Rachel may Levi proceed to consummate with Leah.

### Death Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָלַץ לָהּ אָחִיו אוֹ כְּנָסָהּ — יִכְנוֹס אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. מֵתָה הַיְּבָמָה — יִכְנוֹס אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. מֵת יָבָם — יוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט, וְאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו בַּחֲלִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **a brother of** the one who betrothed the sister of the *yevama* **performed *ḥalitza* with** the *yevama* **or consummated a levirate marriage with her,** since by doing so the levirate bond between the *yevama* and the one who betrothed her sister is dissolved, **he may** then **enter into marriage** with **his wife,** who until that point was only betrothed to him, as she is no longer the sister of a woman to whom he is bound by a levirate bond. Similarly, if **the *yevama* died,** since his levirate bond to her is dissolved upon her death, **he may** proceed to **enter into marriage** with **his** betrothed **wife.** However, if the ***yavam* died** without performing an act that would have dissolved the levirate bond, **he must divorce his wife with a bill of divorce,** as she is forbidden to him as the sister of a woman to whom he is bound by a levirate bond, **and his brother's wife** he must send out **with *ḥalitza*,** as she is forbidden to him as the sister of his divorcée.

Once Shimon performs *chalitza* or *yibbum* with Rachel, or if Rachel herself dies, Levi's relationship to Rachel is terminated and he may consummate with Leah. These are the uncomplicated resolutions. *Chalatz l'achiv o kinsah — yichnos et ishto; metah hayevama — yichnos et ishto.*

### Met Hayavam

The tragic scenario is when Shimon himself dies. Now Levi is stuck. Rachel falls before him as a second *yevama* through the now-deceased Shimon. What can Levi do? He cannot perform *yibbum*, because that would mean having relations with his wife's sister. He cannot do *chalitza* freely and then marry Rachel, because Rachel would then be his *chalutza* and Leah is Rachel's sister — he would be in the same trap. The ruling: *yotzi et ishto b'get v'eshet achiv b'chalitza* — he must divorce Leah and perform *chalitza* with Rachel. Both relationships must be dissolved; he cannot be with either woman.

### Wording

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״וְכֵן״? אֵימָא: ״אֲבָל הַמְגָרֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna considers two cases in which there is no possibility of consummating levirate marriage with a *yevama*. In the first case the reason is that she is the sister of one's *ḥalutza*, and in the second it is that she is the sister of one's divorcée. The mishna appears to compare the two cases by introducing the second case with: And similarly. The Gemara questions this: **What is** the meaning of: **And similarly?** The two cases are not similar; in the first case the *yevama* must perform *ḥalitza*, and in the second she is entirely exempt from the need to do anything. The Gemara answers: Emend the mishna and **say: However, one who divorces.**

The Gemara notes a textual issue with the *mishna*'s transition. When the *mishna* moves from the *chalutza* case to the case of a genuine *gerusha*, it uses the language *v'chen* — "and similarly." But the cases are not similar at all: if Reuven genuinely divorced Rachel (making her a *gerusha*), and Levi then married Leah, Leah would be a full biblical *erva* to Levi — a *d'oraisa* prohibition — and she would need no *chalitza* at all; she is entirely exempt. So *v'chen* is misleading. The Gemara says: read it as *aval* — "but" — indicating a contrast rather than a similarity.

### Divorcee vs. Chalutza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, כָּאן שָׁנָה רַבִּי: אֲחוֹת גְּרוּשָׁה מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, אֲחוֹת חֲלוּצָה מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the reason for the difference between the two cases: **Reish Lakish said: Here,** through this mishna, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **taught** that **a sister of** one's **divorcée** is forbidden to him **by Torah law,** and that is why she is entirely exempt, whereas **a sister of a *ḥalutza*** is forbidden to him only **by rabbinic law,** and therefore, since there is still a levirate bond between her and the *yavam*, she requires *ḥalitza* to release her from it.

This is the occasion for Reish Lakish's statement, already cited in an earlier context: *kan shana Rabbi: achas gerusha midivrei Torah, achas chalutza midivrei Soferim.* The sister of a *gerusha* is forbidden on a biblical level, and therefore Leah in the *gerusha* case requires no *chalitza* and is entirely exempt. The sister of a *chalutza* is forbidden only rabbinically, and therefore even though she may not do *yibbum*, she must nonetheless perform *chalitza*.

## Mesa Ishto Status

### Mahu B'Yevimto

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מֵתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ, מַהוּ בִּיבִמְתּוֹ? רַב וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מֵתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ — מוּתָּר בִּיבִמְתּוֹ. שְׁמוּאֵל וְרַב אַסִּי דְאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מֵתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ — אָסוּר בִּיבִמְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If a *yavam* is betrothed to the sister of his *yevama*, then since the *yevama* is his betrothed wife's sister, it is forbidden for him to consummate a levirate marriage with her. However, if **his** betrothed **wife died, what is** the *halakha* **with regard to his *yevama*;** is he then permitted to consummate a levirate marriage with her? It is **Rav and Rabbi Ḥanina who both say:** If **his wife died, he is permitted to** consummate a levirate marriage with **his *yevama*.** Their opinion is disputed by **Shmuel and Rav Asi, who both say:** If **his wife died, he is forbidden to his *yevama*.**

The Gemara now poses the key question arising from the *shomeres yavam* scenario: *mesa ishto mahu b'yevimto?* If, after Levi married Leah and created the forbidden status for Rachel, Leah then dies — what is the law regarding Rachel? We know that in the *mishna*'s case, Rachel was initially a *shomeres yavam* and therefore permissible to Levi before his marriage to Leah. Marriage to Leah then made Rachel forbidden. But now that Leah is dead, does Rachel revert to her originally permitted status, or does she remain permanently forbidden?

The case involves a sequence of three statuses: first permitted (as *shomeres yavam*), then forbidden (when Levi married Leah), then potentially permitted again (when Leah dies). The question is whether the intermediate period of prohibition permanently locks in her status.

### Rav Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב — מִשּׁוּם דַּהֲוַאי יְבָמָה שֶׁהוּתְּרָה וְנֶאֶסְרָה, וְחָזְרָה וְהוּתְּרָה, תַּחְזוֹר לְהֶיתֵּירָהּ הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is Rav's reasoning?** She is permitted **due to** the fact that **she is a *yevama* who,** when her husband died, **was permitted** to the *yavam*, **and** then when the *yavam* betrothed her sister **she became forbidden** to him as his wife's sister, **and then** when his wife died the *yevama* **reverted** to her original status **and was permitted** to him. As such, **she should** also **revert to her original permitted status** with regard to levirate marriage and be permitted to consummate a levirate marriage with the *yavam*.

Rav and Rabbi Chanina both hold: *mesa ishto, mutar b'yevimto* — if his wife dies, he may once again consummate with his *yevama*. Rava explains the reasoning: *mishum d'hava yevama she'hutrah, v'ne'esra, v'chazra v'hutrah* — she was a *yevama* who was permitted, became forbidden, and then became permitted again; *tachzor l'heiteira harishon* — she returns to her original status. The foundational identity of a *yevama* is determined at the moment of the husband's death. When Reuven died, Rachel was in no way an *erva* to Levi, and that basic, original status can reassert itself once the intervening obstacle is removed.

### Shmuel Position

Shmuel and Rav Assi disagree: *mesa ishto, assur b'yevimto.* Once forbidden, she remains forbidden — even though she was initially permitted and only became forbidden subsequently.

### Gimmel Achim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב הַמְנוּנָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם נְשׂוּאִין שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְאֶחָד מוּפְנֶה. מֵת אֶחָד מִבַּעֲלֵי אֲחָיוֹת, וְעָשָׂה בָּהּ מוּפְנֶה מַאֲמָר, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת אָחִיו הַשֵּׁנִי,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna raised an objection** from that which was taught in a *baraita*: With regard to a case of **three brothers, two** of whom **are married to two sisters and one** of whom **is a bachelor,** if **one of the sisters' husbands died and the bachelor performed levirate betrothal** with that sister, **and afterward the second brother** who was married to the other sister **died,** and so his wife also happened before the bachelor for levirate marriage, the bachelor is then bound to each of the sisters through a levirate bond. In such a case it is prohibited for the bachelor to consummate a levirate marriage with either one, as it is prohibited to marry the sister of a woman to whom one is bound through a levirate bond.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ אַחֲרָיו — אוֹתָהּ יְבָמָה חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if **afterward the wife** of the second brother **died after him,** i.e., after her husband had already died, which restored the original situation in which the bachelor was bound by a levirate bond only to the widow of the first brother, then in such a case **that *yevama*,** the widow of the first brother, **performs *ḥalitza* but may not enter into levirate marriage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? תֶּיהְוֵי כִּיבָמָה שֶׁהוּתְּרָה וְנֶאֶסְרָה וְחָזְרָה וְהוּתְּרָה, תַּחְזוֹר לְהֶיתֵּירָהּ הָרִאשׁוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Hamnuna explains the challenge from the *baraita*: **But why** may she not consummate the levirate marriage? **She should be like a *yevama* who was** initially **permitted** to her *yavam*, **and** then **became forbidden** to him, **and then reverted** to her original status **and was permitted, and so** in this case **she should** also **revert to her original permitted status** with regard to levirate marriage and be permitted to consummate a levirate marriage with him. The fact that the *baraita* rules that she is prohibited from consummating the marriage demonstrates that she does not revert to her original permitted status.

Rav Hamnuna challenges Rav's position with the following case: three brothers — Reuven, Shimon, and Levi — where Reuven and Shimon are married to two sisters, Rachel and Leah respectively, and Levi is single. Reuven dies. Levi performs a *ma'amar* with Rachel (a quasi-betrothal in the *yibbum* context). Then Shimon dies. Now Levi has a *zika* to Leah as well, while being quasi-married to Rachel. He cannot consummate with either, as one is his wife's sister and the other creates the symmetric problem. Then — and here is the crucial question — Leah dies. May Levi now consummate *yibbum* with Rachel?

### Shomeres Yavam

One might argue, using Rav's logic, that Rachel was initially permitted when she became a *yevama* through Reuven's death, that she became forbidden only because of Leah, and that now that Leah is gone, she should revert to her permitted status. But closer examination reveals the cases are not parallel. In the original case, Rachel was a *shomeres yavam* and thus permissible to Levi *before* he married Leah. It was only Levi's act of marrying Leah that then made Rachel forbidden. There was a genuine prior permitted state from Levi's perspective. In Rav Hamnuna's case, by contrast, when Leah became a *yevama* through Shimon's death, Levi had already performed a *ma'amar* with Rachel. At the very moment Leah entered *yevama* status, she was already the sister of someone who was quasi-his-wife. She was *never* permissible from the moment she became a *yevama*. Therefore — *otah yevamah choletzet v'lo mityabemet* — she may only perform *chalitza*. This seems to prove that even Rav would agree: a *yevama* who was never permitted cannot revert to a permitted state, and here there is nothing to revert *to*.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתִּיק. לְבָתַר דִּנְפַק אֲמַר: אַמַּאי לָא אֲמַרְתְּ לֵיהּ, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָמְדָה עָלָיו שָׁעָה אַחַת בְּאִסּוּר — נֶאֶסְרָה עָלָיו עוֹלָמִית?
>
> **Translation:** Rava **was silent,** as he had no immediate answer. **After** Rav Hamnuna **left** the study hall, Rava **said** to himself: **Why did you not say to him** that the *baraita* does not pose a challenge because **it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who said** in a mishna (108b) with regard to the case of a man who divorced his wife, remarried her, and then died childless, that the *yevama* may not consummate levirate marriage with her *yavam* because **once** a *yevama* **has stood** before her *yavam* **in a forbidden state,** even **for a single moment, she** remains **forbidden to him forever,** and in that case, during the period she was divorced from her husband she was forbidden to her *yavam*? The Rabbis, however, disagree with his opinion, and the *halakha* is ruled in accordance with their opinion.

After this exchange, Rav was silenced — *ishtik* — apparently conceding the point. But after leaving, he reflected and said: why didn't I invoke Rabbi Eliezer? *Amai lo amart lei, Rabbi Elazar hi* — that case in the *beraita* is the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds *keivan she-amdah alav sha'ah achat be'issur, ne'esrah alav le-olam* — once a woman stood before him in a forbidden status for even one moment, she is forbidden to him forever. He could have said: I do not rule like Rabbi Eliezer — I rule that she may revert to being permitted — and you have cited a *beraita* that follows Rabbi Eliezer's stringent position.

### Gerusha Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אֲמַר: אֵימוֹר דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר — הֵיכָא דְּלָא חַזְיָא בִּשְׁעַת נְפִילָה, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּחַזְיָא בִּשְׁעַת נְפִילָה, מִי אָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** **He then said** to himself: It is not certain that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar because one could **say that Rabbi Elazar said** his opinion only **where she was not suitable** for the *yavam*, i.e., she was forbidden to him, already from **the time** of her **happening** before her *yavam* for levirate marriage at the moment of her husband's death. **However, where she was suitable** for her *yavam* **at the time of** her **happening** before him and became forbidden to him only at some later point, as in the case of a *yavam* who betrothed the sister of his *yevama*, **did he** actually **say** that if the reason for the prohibition is removed then she does not return to her permissible state? If the *baraita* does not represent the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, then it must be unanimously agreed upon, and therefore it indeed poses a challenge.

Then he reconsidered further: could I actually have said it was Rabbi Eliezer? When does Rabbi Eliezer say once forbidden, always forbidden? The case of Rabbi Eliezer is as follows: Reuven married Rachel, then divorced her. At that point, Rachel became a biblical *erva* to Shimon — a divorced brother's wife, without any context of *yibbum* whatsoever. Reuven then remarried Rachel. Reuven dies. Does Rachel now come before Shimon as a *yevama*, or does her earlier status as his brother's *gerusha* — which occurred outside any *yibbum* framework — permanently bar her? Rabbi Eliezer says yes, once she was forbidden to him, she remains forever forbidden.

### Lo Chazya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אֲמַר: אִין, וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: מֵתָה יְבִמְתּוֹ — מוּתָּר בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ, מֵתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ — אוֹתָהּ יְבָמָה חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **He then said: Yes,** actually, Rabbi Elazar did state his opinion even in such a case, **and** so **it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a *yavam* who betrothed the sister of his *yevama*, **Rabbi Elazar says:** If **his *yevama* dies, he is permitted to his wife.** If **his wife dies, that *yevama* performs *ḥalitza* but may not enter into levirate marriage** with him. Therefore, the challenge from the *baraita* can be dismissed since it represents only Rabbi Elazar's opinion.

The critical limiting principle is: *heicha d'lo chazya bi'she'at nefilah* — Rabbi Eliezer's stringency applies where the woman was never in a permissible *yevama* status at the moment she became a *yevama*. In the *gerusha* case, when Reuven died and she became a *yevama*, her prior history of having been a divorced brother's wife outside the *yibbum* context meant she entered *yevama* status already tainted. But in Rav's case — the case of the *mishna* — when Reuven died and Rachel became a *shomeres yavam*, she was entirely permissible. The question is only whether a subsequent intervening prohibition (Levi's marriage to Leah) can permanently bar her.

### When Status Locks

So Rav Hamnuna's challenge ultimately resolves differently from what it first appeared. In the three-brothers case, Leah was never permissible *from the moment of her entry into yevama status* — and even Rav might agree she remains forbidden. In the original case, Rachel *was* permissible at the moment of entry into *yevama* status, and once Leah dies the question is whether she can revert. Rav says yes; Shmuel and Rav Assi say no. The *beraita* of Rabbi Eliezer cannot resolve this debate because Rabbi Eliezer's principle is limited to women who never had a permissible *yevama* status to begin with.

## Shlosh Chodashim

### Yevama

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַיְּבָמָה לֹא תַּחֲלוֹץ וְלֹא תִּתְיַיבֵּם עַד שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים. וְכֵן כׇּל שְׁאָר הַנָּשִׁים לֹא יִתְאָרְסוּ וְלֹא יִנָּשְׂאוּ עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ לָהֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים: אֶחָד בְּתוּלוֹת וְאֶחָד בְּעוּלוֹת, אֶחָד גְּרוּשׁוֹת וְאֶחָד אַלְמָנוֹת, אֶחָד נְשׂוּאוֹת וְאֶחָד אֲרוּסוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A *yevama* may neither perform *ḥalitza* nor enter into levirate marriage until she has** waited **three months** from the time of her husband's death. **And similarly, all other women may not be betrothed and may not marry until they have** waited **three months** since their previous marriage ended. This waiting period is necessary so that, should a woman give birth shortly after remarrying, it will be obvious who the father of the child is. This applies **both to virgins and non-virgins, both to divorcées and widows,** and **both to women** who were **married** to their previous husbands **and women** who were only **betrothed.** All of these women must wait three months before remarrying even though for some of them the reason for doing so does not apply.

The *mishna* introduces an entirely new topic: *ha-yevama lo tachlot v'lo tityabem ad she-yeish la shlosha chodashim* — the *yevama* may not perform *chalitza* nor may she consummate *yibbum* for three months after the death of the husband. The concern, in a world without pregnancy testing, is to establish who the father of any child will be.

### Kol Hanashim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַנְּשׂוּאוֹת — יִתְאָרְסוּ, וְהָאֲרוּסוֹת — יִנָּשְׂאוּ. חוּץ מִן הָאֲרוּסוֹת שֶׁבִּיהוּדָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלִּבּוֹ גַּס בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: The women** who were **married** to their previous husbands **may be betrothed, and the women** who were only **betrothed** to their previous husbands **may marry** without waiting three months. This is true **except for the betrothed women that are in** the area of **Judea, due to** the fact **that** the groom **is familiar with her.** The custom in Judea was for the couple to be secluded together before the marriage so that they would become familiar with each other. This led to the possibility that they might cohabit even during their betrothal period. Rabbi Yehuda holds that one does not need to wait three months whenever the reason for doing so does not apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים יִתְאָרְסוּ, חוּץ מִן הָאַלְמָנָה, מִפְּנֵי הָאִיבּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: All of the women may be betrothed** within three months even if they were previously married, **except for a widow, due to the mourning** period she must observe for her deceased husband.

This rule is extended to all women whose marriages have ended: *v'chen kol sh'ar nashim* — widows and divorcees alike must wait three months before entering any new marital relationship. *Lo ye'arsu v'lo yinasu ad she-yeish lahem shlosha chodashim* — they may neither perform *kiddushin* nor proceed to *nisuin* during this period.

The extension to *kiddushin* is notable, since *kiddushin* without *nisuin* involves no sexual relations and would seem to pose no risk of paternity confusion. This prohibition on *kiddushin* is a kind of *lo plug* — a blanket rule that does not differentiate — because once you permit *kiddushin* for some women immediately, you risk confusion about which women may and may not proceed.

The first *tanna* draws no distinctions among women: *echad betulot v'echad be'ulot, echad gerushos v'echad almanos, echad nesuas v'echad arusos* — whether a virgin or a non-virgin, whether divorced or widowed, whether the first marriage involved full *nisuin* or only *eirusin*, all must wait three months. Even a woman who was only an *arusa* to her first husband — with no sexual contact possible — and who is now only about to be an *arusa* to her second husband — still involving no sexual contact — must wait. This double extension to cases where the concern is entirely theoretical represents a deliberate *lo plug*.

### Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi

Rabbi Yehuda takes a more calibrated position. Women who had been *nesuous* to their first husbands — where sex was possible and the pregnancy concern is real — must wait before proceeding to *nisuin* with their second husbands, but they may at least enter *kiddushin* in the interim, since *kiddushin* alone does not permit sexual relations. Women who had only been *arusot* to their first husbands — where sex was not permitted — may proceed directly to *nisuin* with their second husbands, since there is no possibility they are pregnant. He does not accept the *lo plug* reasoning.

Rabbi Yehuda's single exception is the *arusa* from Yehuda — *mipnei shelibo gas bah* — because in Yehuda the practice was for the *arus* and *arusa* to spend private time together before *nisuin*, becoming comfortable with one another. This private time occasionally led to sexual relations, meaning *arusot* in Yehuda could in fact be pregnant. Therefore they must also wait, and may not even enter *kiddushin* with their next partner during the three-month period.

Rabbi Yosi agrees with Rabbi Yehuda's general framework — that *kiddushin* is permissible for all women during the three months — but adds one exception for a different reason entirely: *chutz min ha'almana mipnei ha'eivul* — a widow must wait because of the *aveilus* mourning period. Even setting aside the pregnancy concern, Rabbi Yosi holds that propriety requires a widow to complete her mourning before taking a new partner, and therefore she may not even perform *kiddushin* until the *avelius* has concluded.

## Shma Yehei Velad

### Ben Kayama

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא לֹא תִּתְיַיבֵּם: שֶׁמָּא יִהְיֶה הַוָּלָד בֶּן קַיָּימָא, וְקָא פָגַע בְּאִיסּוּר אֵשֶׁת אָח דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. אֶלָּא לֹא תַּחְלוֹץ, אַמַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara questions the mishna's opening clause: **Granted, she should not enter into levirate marriage,** as she might already be pregnant, which would still not be noticeable during the first three months, and **perhaps the baby will be viable.** In that case it would emerge that she was never subject to the mitzva of levirate marriage, **and** therefore, if the *yavam* consummates a levirate marriage **he will have encountered the Torah prohibition against** engaging in relations with one's **brother's wife. However, why should she not perform *ḥalitza*?**

The Gemara now analyzes the *mishna*'s reasoning. The most obvious question is: why may the *yevama* not do either *chalitza* or *yibbum* for three months? The concern is *shema yehei velad ben kayama* — perhaps there is a viable child in utero. If there is, no action is necessary or appropriate.

### Eshet Ach

For *yibbum*, the concern is particularly weighty. If the woman is pregnant and the child is viable, there is no *mitzvah* of *yibbum*, and performing *yibbum* would constitute a violation of the biblical prohibition of *eshet ach* — *vekapaga be'issur eshet ach de'oraisa*.

### Karoz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: חֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה. וְלָאו מִי אוֹתְבִינֵּיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן חֲדָא זִימְנָא?! לֵימָא מֵהָא נָמֵי תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this mishna, which states that a pregnant woman should not perform *ḥalitza*, **is a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said** that **a *ḥalitza*** performed **with a pregnant woman** who later miscarries **is considered** a valid ***ḥalitza*.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't they** already **conclusively refute** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan one time** previously from other sources? The Gemara rephrases the suggestion: **Let us say** that **from this** mishna **as well there is a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, הָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא: שֶׁמָּא יְהֵא וָלָד בֶּן קַיָּימָא, וְנִמְצֵאתָ אַתָּה מַצְרִיכָהּ כָּרוֹז לַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara deflects this suggestion: **No,** it is possible that in the mishna **here, this is the reason** that she may not perform *ḥalitza*: **Perhaps** the **offspring will be viable,** in which case although she performed the act of *ḥalitza*, it is entirely meaningless since it was completely unnecessary, and she would remain permitted to marry into the priesthood. However, this could lead to a problem: People might not realize that the *ḥalitza* she performed was meaningless, and they would think she is a *ḥalutza* who is prohibited from marrying a priest. **It would** therefore **emerge** that if you permit her to perform *ḥalitza* even though it is possible that she is pregnant, it could be that **you will** ultimately **require a public announcement** to be made **for her** to attest to the fact that the *ḥalitza* she performed was meaningless and she is in fact still permitted to marry **into the priesthood.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַצְרְכַהּ! דִּלְמָא אִיכָּא דְּהָוֵאי בַּחֲלִיצָה וְלָא הָוֵי בְּהַכְרָזָה, וּמַפְסְלוּהָ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders why this poses a problem: **But** why not let her perform *ḥalitza* even though it is possible she is pregnant, and then, if it becomes necessary, **require** a public announcement to be made **for her?** The Gemara explains why one should avoid having to rely on a public announcement: **Perhaps there will be some** people **who are** present **at the *ḥalitza* but were not** present **at the public announcement, and** those people **will** incorrectly **disqualify her from** marrying into **the priesthood.** To avoid this situation, the mishna rules that she should wait three months before performing *ḥalitza*.

But why should *chalitza* be delayed? Surely *chalitza* cannot violate a biblical prohibition even if a child exists. The *mishna* must be implying that *chalitzas me'uberes* — *chalitza* with a pregnant woman — does not take effect. This, indeed, was the subject of an earlier dispute, where Rabbi Yochanan held that *chalitzo me'uberes shema chalitza* — it does take effect. Our *mishna*'s prohibition on *chalitza* within three months would then seem to contradict Rabbi Yochanan's position. The Gemara notes that Rabbi Yochanan has already been challenged on this point before, and piles on this *mishna* as an additional proof against him.

The Gemara then offers Rabbi Yochanan a defense. Even if *chalitza* with a pregnant woman is technically valid, we still prohibit it *lechatchila* for a practical reason: *shema yehei velad ben kayama* — perhaps the child is viable, meaning *chalitza* was not needed at all — *venimtzesa ata matzricha karoz l'kehuna* — and then a retraction must be announced, correcting the public impression that she has become a *chalutza* (which would disqualify her from marrying a *kohen*). The Gemara proposes: just make the announcement. The Gemara responds: *dilma ika d'havai bachalitza v'lo havai bahachraza* — perhaps some who were present at the *chalitza* ceremony were not present at the retraction, and they will go about saying she is disqualified from marrying a *kohen* without having heard the correction. Like a retraction printed in small type the following day, the announcement does not reach everyone.

### Grusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח אַלְמָנָה. גְּרוּשָׁה — מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? מִשּׁוּם דְּמַפְסֵיד לַהּ מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This answer **works out well** with regard to **a widow** because as a widow she is currently still permitted to marry into the priesthood, but with regard to **a divorcée,** who is already prohibited from marrying into the priesthood, **what is there to say;** why shouldn't she perform *ḥalitza* immediately? The Gemara provides an alternative reason to delay the *ḥalitza*: The reason is **because** by performing *ḥalitza* immediately **she will forfeit the sustenance payments** from her deceased husband's estate, which she otherwise would have been entitled to for the first three months. Since she is not permitted to remarry during those three months, she will have no means of support. Therefore, she should wait until after three months have passed before performing *ḥalitza*.

A further question: even granting that concern, why does the three-month waiting period apply to a *grusha*? If the woman had already been divorced from a previous marriage before marrying Reuven, she is already disqualified from marrying a *kohen* regardless of any *chalitza* status. The concern about an uncorrected *karoz* therefore does not apply. So why cannot Shimon proceed to *chalitza* with her immediately?

### Mezonos

The Gemara's answer reframes the entire rationale: *mishum d'mapslu lah mimezonos* — because performing *chalitza* prematurely would deprive the woman of her *mezonos*, the financial support she is entitled to receive from the deceased husband's estate during the waiting period. Before *chalitza* is performed, she is supported by the estate. Once *chalitza* is performed, that entitlement ends. The Gemara is therefore saying: regardless of the *kohen* concern, there is an independent reason not to perform *chalitza* too early — it removes her from the estate's support before the appropriate time.

### Oleh Leyibum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח נְשׂוּאָה. אֲרוּסָה גְּרוּשָׁה — מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This answer **works out well** with regard to a **woman** who was **married** to her previous husband and who is therefore entitled to sustenance payments, but with regard to **a divorcée who** was only **betrothed** to her previous husband, **what is there to say?** She is neither entitled to receive sustenance payments nor is she permitted to marry into the priesthood. Why, then, shouldn't she perform *ḥalitza* immediately?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁבָּא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אָמַר לוֹ: מַהוּ לַחְלוֹץ בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה? אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא תַּחְלוֹץ. וְתַחְלוֹץ, וּמָה בְּכָךְ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara therefore provides an alternative reason to delay the *ḥalitza*: **Rather,** it is **due to that** which **Rabbi Yosei** stated, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: There was **an incident involving a certain man who came before Rabbi Yosei.** The man **said to him: What is** the *halakha* with regard **to performing *ḥalitza* within three** months of the husband's death? **He said to him: She may not perform *ḥalitza*.** He asked him: **Let her perform *ḥalitza*; what** would be the problem **with that?** Even if she is pregnant, no prohibition will have been transgressed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרָא עָלָיו מִקְרָא זֶה: ״וְאִם לֹא יַחְפֹּץ״, הָא אִם חָפֵץ — יִבֵּם. כׇּל הָעוֹלֶה לְיִבּוּם — עוֹלֶה לַחֲלִיצָה כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** To explain his ruling, Rabbi Yosei **recited this verse about him: "And if the man does not wish** to take his *yevama*" (Deuteronomy 25:7) in levirate marriage, then he must perform *ḥalitza*. By inference, the possibility of performing *ḥalitza* exists only in a case in which **if he wishes he may consummate the levirate marriage.** This teaches the principle that **one who is eligible for levirate marriage is eligible for *ḥalitza*,** and one who is ineligible for levirate marriage is ineligible for *ḥalitza*. This includes even a woman who is temporarily prohibited from marrying, e.g., a woman in the first three months following a previous marriage.

The Gemara presses further: this works for a *nesu'ah* — a woman who was in full *nisuin* and therefore entitled to *mezonos* from the estate. But what about an *arusa* who had only *kiddushin* and is therefore not entitled to *mezonos*, and who was also a *gerusha* from a previous marriage? The *kohen* concern does not apply (already disqualified), and the *mezonos* concern does not apply (she has no entitlement). Why must she wait?

The Gemara's final answer invokes the principle of Rabbi Yosi: *ella mishum d'Rabbi Yosi* — because of Rabbi Yosi's teaching. A *ma'aseh* is cited in which someone came before Rabbi Yosi and asked whether he could perform *chalitza* within the three months. Rabbi Yosi said no. The petitioner pressed: *v'sachloz umah bekach?* — what would be the harm? Rabbi Yosi responded by citing the verse — *im lo yachpotz*, "if he does not wish" — implying that the alternative of *yibbum* must be a genuine option: *ha im chafetz, yabem*. This leads to the principle: *kol ha'oleh leyibum oleh lechaltiza v'kol she'eino oleh leyibum eino oleh lechaltiza* — whoever is eligible for *yibbum* is eligible for *chalitza*, and whoever is ineligible for *yibbum* is ineligible for *chalitza*. We perform *chalitza* only where *yibbum* would also have been a valid option. Since during the first three months, *yibbum* is not a valid option — the child may be viable — *chalitza* is likewise not performed. Rabbi Yosi's principle thus provides a conceptual ground for the three-month restriction that applies even where the *mezonos* and *kohen* concerns are absent. As Rashi observes, this principle holds even in cases where *yibbum* is ultimately forbidden rabbinically, so long as *chalitza* is performed only when *yibbum* could theoretically be an option — that is, *lechatchila*, we confine *chalitza* to contexts where the *yibbum* option genuinely exists.
