# Beitzah 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Context Review

### Hillel vs. Shammai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חָל לִהְיוֹת אַחַר הַשַּׁבָּת — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מַטְבִּילִין אֶת הַכֹּל מִלִּפְנֵי הַשַּׁבָּת. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: כְּלִים מִלִּפְנֵי הַשַּׁבָּת, וְאָדָם בְּשַׁבָּת. וְשָׁוִין שֶׁמַּשִּׁיקִין אֶת הַמַּיִם בִּכְלִי אֶבֶן לְטַהֲרָן, אֲבָל לֹא מַטְבִּילִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a Festival **occurs** directly **after Shabbat,** i.e., on a Sunday, and one wishes to behave in a proper manner and purify himself and his vessels in honor of the Festival, **Beit Shammai say: One must immerse everything before Shabbat, and Beit Hillel say: Vessels** must be immersed **before Shabbat, but a person** may immerse himself even **on Shabbat.** **And** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel both **agree that one may bring** ritually impure **water into contact** with ritually pure water **in stone vessels** on Shabbat **in order to purify** the water. Impure water can be purified if it is placed into a vessel that does not contract ritual impurity, such as a stone vessel, and then lowered with the vessel into a ritual bath. The water becomes purified when it comes into contact with the water of the ritual bath. Although this is not considered proper immersion, water may nevertheless be purified in this manner. **However, one may not immerse** the impure water in a ritually impure vessel in order to purify the vessel at the same time.

Today's daf is 18, and we pick up from the very top, with two lines of context drawn from the bottom of 17b. The *mishnah* there records the dispute between Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai regarding what may be brought to the *mikvah* on *Shabbos* or *Yom Tov*. Crucially, both schools agree on one point: *kelim* — vessels — may not be immersed. The dispute concerns only whether a person may immerse.

### Rava: Gezeira Carrying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהַת — כְּלִי בְּשַׁבָּת לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבָּה: גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יִטְּלֶנּוּ בְּיָדוֹ, וְיַעֲבִירֶנּוּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **In any event, everyone agrees,** i.e., both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree, that one **may not** immerse **a vessel on Shabbat.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that one may not do so? Which type of prohibited labor does it involve? **Rabba said:** It is **a decree** issued by the Sages as a preventive measure, **lest one** come to **pick up** the vessel **in his hand and carry it four cubits in the public domain** to a ritual bath.

The *gemara* opens with Rava's explanation of why vessel immersion is universally forbidden. The prohibition is a *gezeirah shema yitlenu be-yado ve-yavirenu arba amot bi-reshut ha-rabbim* — a rabbinic decree lest one take the vessel in hand and carry it four *amot* in the public domain. This is precisely the same language Rava employs to explain why one may not take a *lulav* to the public domain on *Shabbos*, and one might ask whether he formulated this reasoning independently for both cases or whether the *gemara* applied a statement made in one context to the other.

### Abaye Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: יֵשׁ לוֹ בּוֹר בַּחֲצֵירוֹ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גְּזֵירָה בּוֹר בַּחֲצֵרוֹ אַטּוּ בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabba: If **one has a pit** full of water of a ritual bath **in his courtyard,** so that this decree should not apply, **what is there to say?** Rabba **said to him:** The Sages issued **a decree** against immersing vessels even in **a pit** of water **in one's** own **courtyard due to a pit** situated **in the public domain.**

Abaye presses: what of someone who has a well in his *chatzer*? A private courtyard well is effectively a *mikvah*, and the surrounding courtyard walls — the very infrastructure that made *eruv chatzerot* necessary — would prevent any carrying into a public domain. Since people routinely drew their water from such wells, this was hardly an unusual configuration.

### Rava Responds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתִינַח שַׁבָּת, בְּיוֹם טוֹב מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? גָּזְרוּ יוֹם טוֹב אַטּוּ שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye posed another challenge: This works out **well** with regard to **Shabbat,** but **with regard to a Festival,** when there is no prohibition against carrying from one domain to another, **what is there to say?** Rabba replied: The Sages **issued a decree** that it is prohibited to immerse a vessel on **a Festival, due to** the prohibition against immersing it **on Shabbat.**

Rava answers: *gezeirah bor be-chatzeiro atu bor bi-reshut ha-rabbim* — even when the well is in one's courtyard, we decree against immersion lest it lead to cases where the only available well is in the public domain, which would entail carrying. The *gemara* then presses further: granted, this reasoning holds for *Shabbos*, where carrying is forbidden. But on *Yom Tov*, when carrying is generally permitted, why maintain the prohibition? The *gemara* answers: *gazru Yom Tov atu Shabbos* — the Rabbis extended the prohibition to *Yom Tov* as a protective measure against its violation on *Shabbos*.

### Merchatza'ot Tangent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי גָּזְרִינַן? וְהָא תְּנַן: וְשָׁוִין שֶׁמַּשִּׁיקִין אֶת הַמַּיִם בִּכְלִי אֶבֶן לְטַהֲרָן, אֲבָל לֹא מַטְבִּילִין. וְאִי אִיתָא — נִגְזוֹר הַשָּׁקָה אַטּוּ הַטְבָּלָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do we issue a decree** in such a case? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **And** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel both **agree that one may bring** ritually impure **water into contact** with ritually pure water **in stone vessels in order to purify** the water. **However, one may not immerse** the impure water in a ritually impure vessel in order to purify the vessel at the same time. **And if it is so** that such a decree was issued, **let us** also **decree** here that **bringing** ritually impure water **into contact** with ritually pure water is prohibited **due to** the prohibition against **immersing** a ritually impure vessel in a ritual bath.

This logic — extending a *Shabbos* prohibition to *Yom Tov* — is admittedly counterintuitive given how many leniencies are unique to *Yom Tov*, including *ochel nefesh* and activities that are entirely permitted precisely because the relevant *melachot* are not forbidden on *Yom Tov*. A notable parallel is the *gezeirat merchatza'ot*, the prohibition against bathing in heated water even when the water was heated before *Shabbos*, lest one come to heat water on *Shabbos* itself. There is no compelling reason this concern should apply on *Yom Tov*, yet it was nonetheless extended there. In fact, a number of *poskim* — including the present lecturer's own practice — hold that one may take a hot shower on *Yom Tov*, provided one takes care not to squeeze out one's hair and uses liquid rather than bar soap. However, soaking in a hot bath remains forbidden on account of the *gezeirat merchatza'ot*, even though the underlying concern is not truly operative on *Yom Tov*. Occasionally, then, a *gezeirah* that originated in the context of *Shabbos* is extended to *Yom Tov*, even where the core concern is inapplicable.

## Gezeira L'Gezeira

### Hashakah Water Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא? אִי אִית לֵיהּ מַיִם יָפִים, הָנֵי לְמָה לִי לְמֶעְבַּד לְהוּ הַשָּׁקָה? אֶלָּא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ, וְכֵיוָן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ מִזְהָר זְהִיר בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this challenge: **And** how can **you understand** that there is substance to this question? **If he has** other **good,** ritually pure, **water** to drink, **why do I** need **to bring this** ritually impure water **into contact** with the ritually pure water? **Rather,** one must say **that he does not have** suitable drinking water, **and since he does not have** other water, **he is** particularly **careful about** this water, so that it not become impure. Therefore, perforce, this must be an exceptional case, as the water became impure despite the precautions that were taken; and the Sages did not apply their decrees to unusual cases.

The *gemara* now raises a pointed methodological objection. The prohibition as Rava has framed it involves considerable layering: we forbid immersing a vessel in one's courtyard because it might lead to carrying, and we further extend this to *Yom Tov* lest it lead to *Shabbos* violation. This amounts to *gezeirah l'gezeirah* — a safeguard built upon a safeguard. The *gemara* therefore challenges: if we are indeed comfortable making such layered decrees, why does the *mishnah* permit *hashakah* of water? *Hashakah* refers to lowering a vessel containing water into a *mikvah* so that the waters connect and are thereby purified, even though the vessel itself may not be immersed for its own purification. If we are so vigilant about *gezeirah l'gezeirah*, we ought to forbid *hashakah* as well, since it could too easily lead to the case of immersing the vessel itself.

### Lo Shchiach

The *gemara* responds with a question of its own: *ve-tisbra?* Does that concern really make sense? If one has perfectly good, pure water available, why would one bother with the involved process of *hashakah*? The only reason anyone performs *hashakah* is that they have no other water. And if they have no other water — *miz-dazar behu* — they will be careful. As Rashi explains, this is an application of the principle *milsa d'lo sh'chiach lo gazru rabbanan*: the Rabbis do not issue decrees against uncommon circumstances. The initial scenario requiring *hashakah* is itself rare. While *gezeirah l'gezeirah* typically addresses common circumstances where A might lead to B, the Rabbis decline to extend their protective decrees to cases where the very starting point is an unusual situation.

### Tosafot Water Q

Tosafot raises a sharper question. The *gemara* framed the concern as: why don't we worry that permitting *hashakah* of water will lead to immersing the *keli* itself? But there is a more straightforward concern: why not worry that permitting the bringing of water to the *mikvah* will lead to carrying the water? Setting aside entirely the question of the vessel, the act of transporting water to the *mikvah* itself could constitute a carrying violation if one exits the *chatzer*. Tosafot observes that one is far more pressed and urgent when needing to immerse a vessel — *shaholv* — and so the concern that haste might lead to a violation is more acute there. With water, where alternatives exist, the urgency is lower. While this explanation is not entirely airtight — particularly given that water must be carried in some vessel anyway — it reflects the *gemara*'s underlying assumption that the concern for violation tracks the urgency of the need.

### Drawing vs. Immersing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: מַדְלִין בִּדְלִי טָמֵא, וְהוּא טָהוֹר. וְאִי אִיתָא, נִגְזוֹר דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאַטְבּוֹלֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁלֹּא הוּתְּרָה לוֹ אֶלָּא עַל יְדֵי דׇּלְיוֹ — זָכוּר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection** from the following *baraita*: **One may draw water** from a spring or a ritual bath on a Festival **with a** ritually **impure pail, and** the pail becomes ritually **pure** because while being filled with water, the bucket is completely immersed in the ritual bath. **And if it is so** that the Sages issued such a decree, **let us** also **decree** here that it is prohibited to draw water on Festival with a ritually impure pail **lest one come to immerse** the pail **by itself.** Rabba answered: **It is different there; since it is permitted for him** to immerse the pail **only by drawing** water with it, **he remembers** that it is prohibited to immerse a vessel by itself, and therefore there is no reason to issue a decree.

The *gemara* then introduces a new challenge: *madlin bi-dli tamei ve-hu tahor* — one may draw water using an impure bucket, and in the process the bucket becomes pure. This seems to be another case where we allow an act closely resembling vessel immersion without concern that it will lead to the prohibited act itself.

### Rashi Category

Rashi explains the critical distinction. When one draws water from a well using a bucket, the fundamental category of the act is *she'ivat mayim* — drawing water. One is not formally performing *tevilat keli*; the purification of the vessel is entirely incidental to the purpose of the act. Because the act falls formally outside the category of *tevilah*, it is not governed by the decree against vessel immersion.

### Why Not Vessels

The *gemara* presses further: granted that this is formally an act of drawing water rather than *tevilah*, why are we not concerned that it will lead to carrying the bucket — not because it formally resembles *tevilah*, but simply because someone is going to a well with a *keli*?

### Category Boundaries

The *gemara* answers: *shani hatam, mitoch she-lo hutar lo ela al yedei dalyo, dalyo zachur hu* — because one is only permitted to perform this act through the bucket, and the entire permission is framed as an act of drawing water, one will remember and not come to perform a full act of *tevilah*. The fundamental logic here, as the lecturer notes, is internally consistent: *gezeirah l'gezeirah* is not a claim that we genuinely believe A will always lead to B will always lead to C. Rather, it is a formalistic principle. When we forbid a category, we forbid everything within the formal definition of that category. What falls outside the formal definition — drawing water, *hashakah*, wearing clothes into the *mikvah* — is also formally outside the prohibition and does not require its own protective layer. This is not an evasion; it is precisely what makes *gezeirah l'gezeirah* coherent. We forbid a category broadly, but that breadth is delimited by the formal contours of the category itself.

### Mikvah as Well

A brief but illuminating digression: it is worth noting that in the ancient world, and well into the medieval period, the *mikvah* and the well were often one and the same structure. One dug down to the water table, and that subterranean water — not collected rainwater — constitutes *mayim chayim*. This explains how the stringent position of certain *Geonim*, that a *zavah* requires *mayim chayim* just as a *zav* does, could ever have been practically maintained. If the community's *mikvah* was already a natural spring or well, the requirement for *mayim chayim* imposed no additional burden. Thankfully, this position was rejected by the *rishonim*, without which contemporary *mikvaot* would be entirely unusable.

### Tamei Timing Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: כְּלִי שֶׁנִּטְמָא מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב — אֵין מַטְבִּילִין אוֹתוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב, בְּיוֹם טוֹב — מַטְבִּילִין אוֹתוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב. וְאִם אִיתָא, נִגְזוֹר דְּיוֹם טוֹב אַטּוּ דְּעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב! טוּמְאָה בְּיוֹם טוֹב מִלְּתָא דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא הִיא, וּמִלְּתָא דְלָא שְׁכִיחָא לָא גְּזַרוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection** from a different *baraita*, in which it was taught: With regard to **a vessel that was rendered ritually impure on the eve of a Festival, one may not immerse it on the Festival;** however, if it became impure **on** the **Festival** itself, **one may immerse it on the Festival. And if it is so** that the Sages issued such a decree, **let us** also **decree** here **that** it is prohibited to immerse a vessel that became impure on **a Festival due to** the prohibition against immersing a vessel that became impure on the **eve of a Festival.** Rabba answered: Contracting ritual **impurity on a Festival,** when all are ritually pure, **is an uncommon occurrence, and** the general principle is that in the case of **an uncommon occurrence, the Sages did not issue a decree** as a preventive measure.

The *gemara* now raises a challenge from a different direction. A ruling is cited: if a vessel became *tamei* before *Yom Tov* — on *erev Yom Tov* — one may not immerse it on *Yom Tov* itself, because one should have attended to it beforehand. But if it became *tamei* on *Yom Tov* itself, one may immerse it then. This introduces a fine distinction that sits awkwardly with the sweeping categorical approach Rava has been advocating. If we are categorically forbidding all vessel immersion as a broad decree, why do we carve out an exception for vessels that became *tamei* on *Yom Tov* itself?

### Tumah Lo Shchiach

The *gemara* answers: *tumah be-Yom Tov milsa d'lo sh'chicha* — it is uncommon for a vessel to become *tamei* on *Yom Tov* itself. Why? Because everyone endeavors to be in a state of *taharah* for the *regel*, for the *aliyah la-regel*, and therefore vessels becoming *tamei* on *Yom Tov* is a rare occurrence. Since it is a rare circumstance, the Rabbis did not apply their decree to it — in keeping with the principle that *milsa d'lo sh'chicha lo gazru rabbanan*.

### Tumah Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: כְּלִי שֶׁנִּטְמָא בְּאַב הַטּוּמְאָה — אֵין מַטְבִּילִין אוֹתוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב, בִּוְלַד הַטּוּמְאָה — מַטְבִּילִין אוֹתוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב. וְאִם אִיתָא, נִגְזוֹר הָא אַטּוּ הָא! וְלַד הַטּוּמְאָה הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? גַּבֵּי כֹהֲנִים — כֹּהֲנִים זְרִיזִין הֵם.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised** yet another **objection** from the following *baraita*: With regard to **a vessel that was rendered** ritually **impure by a primary source of impurity, one may not immerse it on a Festival.** However, if it was rendered impure only **by a derivative source of impurity,** meaning that the vessel came into contact with an object that was rendered impure by a primary source of impurity, so that the object has the status of first-degree ritual impurity and confers upon the vessel the status of second-degree ritual impurity, a type of impurity that applies to vessels only by rabbinic decree; in such a case, **one may immerse** the vessel **on a Festival. And if it is so** that the Sages issued such a decree, **let us** also **decree** here that it is prohibited to immerse **this,** a vessel rendered impure by a derivative source of impurity, **due to** the prohibition to immerse **that,** a vessel rendered impure by a primary source of impurity. Rabba replied: **Where do you find** a case where people are particular about purifying a vessel that contracted ritual impurity from **a derivative source of impurity?** It is only in one situation, namely, **with regard to priests,** since they eat *teruma*, and *teruma* contracts ritual impurity even from a vessel that came into contact with only a derivative source of impurity. An ordinary person, who eats non-consecrated produce, does not go to the trouble of purifying such a vessel, since regular produce contracts ritual impurity only from a vessel that came into contact with a primary source of impurity, but not from a vessel that came into contact with a derivative source of impurity. And as for priests, the general principle is that **priests are vigilant;** they are careful not to allow their vessels to become impure. Therefore, impurity in the case of priests is considered a rare occurrence, with regard to which the Sages did not issue a decree.

Yet another distinction is introduced: *keli she-nitma be-av ha-tumah ein matbilin oto be-Yom Tov, be-vlad ha-tumah matbilin oto be-Yom Tov* — a vessel that became *tamei* through a primary source of impurity (*av ha-tumah*, making the vessel a *rishon*) may not be immersed on *Yom Tov*, but a vessel that became *tamei* through a secondary source (*vlad ha-tumah*, making the vessel a *sheni*) may be immersed. Rashi clarifies that this latter leniency applies even if the vessel became *tamei* on *erev Yom Tov* — an additional layer of permission beyond the rule regarding vessels that became *tamei* on *Yom Tov* itself. The logic is that *sheni* status for a vessel is only rabbinically significant, and only relevant in the context of *terumah*, since vessels technically cannot become *sheniyim* from the Torah. It is not considered a true *tumah* in the full sense, and therefore it was not subsumed within the categorical decree.

### Kohanim Careful

The *gemara* asks why we do not extend the decree to this case as well, given that a *sheni* vessel can render *terumah* into a *shlishi* — which is a real consequence in the context of *kohanim* eating *terumah*. The *gemara* answers: *kohanim zerizin heim* — *kohanim* are careful. The very people who would be affected by this concern are those who are most scrupulous in their conduct, and so the Rabbis did not feel the need to impose a protective decree in that case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: נִדָּה שֶׁאֵין לָהּ בְּגָדִים — מַעֲרֶמֶת וְטוֹבֶלֶת בִּבְגָדֶיהָ. וְאִם אִיתָא, נִגְזוֹר דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאַטְבּוֹלֵי בְּעֵינַיְיהוּ! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁלֹּא הוּתְּרָה לָהּ אֶלָּא עַל יְדֵי מַלְבּוּשׁ — זְכוּרָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a different proof, **as Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said: A menstruating woman who has no** ritually pure **clothes** to wear after she immerses herself in a ritual bath to purify herself, as all her clothes had become impure, and it is Shabbat or a Festival, when she is unable to immerse them, **may employ an artifice** to circumvent the prohibition **and immerse** herself **in her clothes.** She is permitted to purify herself, and when she immerses herself while wearing her garments, they become purified at the same time. **And if it is so** that the Sages issued such a decree, **let us** also **decree** that it is prohibited for the woman to immerse herself in her clothes **lest she come to immerse** the clothes by **themselves.** The Gemara answers: **It is different there; since it is permitted for her** to immerse the clothes **only by** wearing them as **garments, she remembers** that it is prohibited to immerse them by themselves and will not come to violate this prohibition.

The *gemara* then introduces the case of *niddah she-ein lah begadim* — a woman who is a *niddah* and has no *tahor* clothing to wear after immersion, only the *tamei* garments she has been wearing throughout. She may enter the *mikvah* while wearing her clothes, provided they are sufficiently loose-fitting not to constitute a *chatzitzah*. The *gemara* asks: if we are making so many layers of *gezeirah*, why do we not decree against this lest it lead to immersing garments independently of the body? The *gemara* answers: *shani hatam mitoch she-hutrah lah al yedei malbush zchurah hi* — because her permission is so specifically framed as immersing herself while clothed, the memory of that specific context will prevent any confusion with the forbidden act of immersing a garment on its own.

Tosafot raises a further difficulty: immersing clothing in water is itself an act of *shiriya*, and *amrinan shiriyato zu hi kibuso* — soaking is considered the beginning of laundering and would be forbidden on *Shabbos* as *mechabes u-melaben*. Tosafot answers that since the *mikvah* waters are not clean, and the woman's body is not clean, the soaking is *derech tinuf* rather than *derech libun*, and the act of immersing something in a manner that makes it dirty rather than clean does not constitute laundering. Tosafot extends this logic to the question of wiping hands on a towel on *Shabbos*, noting that even slight dampness might constitute *shiriya*, but the soiling quality of the act exempts it.

The *gemara*'s ultimate point here is clarified by the lecturer: the various answers to the challenge of *gezeirah l'gezeirah* fall into two broad categories. The first is the principle of *lo sh'chiach* — where the initial circumstances are sufficiently uncommon that the Rabbis simply did not extend their decrees there. The second is a formalistic argument — even within the framework of categorical *gezeirot*, what falls outside the formal definition of the category is not subject to the prohibition. These are not inconsistent answers; together they map the actual contours of how the Rabbis applied protective decrees.

## Alternative Reasons

### Rav Yosef Squeeze

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: גְּזֵרָה מִשּׁוּם סְחִיטָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: תִּינַח כְּלִים דִּבְנֵי סְחִיטָה נִינְהוּ. כְּלִים דְּלָאו בְּנֵי סְחִיטָה נִינְהוּ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גְּזֵרָה הָנֵי אַטּוּ הָנֵי. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ כֹּל הָנֵי תְּיוּבָתָא, וְשַׁנִּי לֵיהּ כִּדְשַׁנִּינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos Rabba's view that one may not immerse a vessel on Shabbat lest he come to carry it four cubits in the public domain, **Rav Yosef said** that it is prohibited to immerse a vessel on Shabbat for a different reason: It is **a decree** issued by the Sages as a preventive measure **due to** the prohibition against **wringing.** After immersing certain items, such as clothes, one might come to wring them, and this is prohibited on Shabbat and Festivals as a subcategory of the biblically prohibited labor of threshing. **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: This works out **well** with regard to **vessels that are fit for wringing,** such as clothes, but with regard to **vessels that are not fit for wringing, what is there to say?** Rav Yosef **said** to him: The Sages issued **a decree** against **these** vessels, which cannot be wrung, **due to those** vessels, which can be wrung. Abaye **raised** against Rav Yosef **all of these objections** that he had raised against Rabba, in an attempt to prove that the Sages did not issue such a decree, **and** Rav Yosef **answered him as we answered** in the name of Rabba.

With the foregoing analysis of Rava's first explanation complete, the *gemara* turns to alternative bases for the prohibition. Rav Yosef explains: *gezeirah mishum sechitah* — the decree is a protection against squeezing. If one immerses a vessel such as a garment in a *mikvah*, one may come to wring it out, which violates the prohibition of *sechitah* on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*.

### Abaye Non-Squeeze

Abaye challenges immediately: this reasoning is compelling for garments and similar *kelim* that are susceptible to squeezing, but what of vessels that cannot be wrung out — wooden bowls, metal utensils, and the like? Rav Yosef answers: *gezeirah hani atu hani* — we prohibit the latter category as a safeguard against the former. This, of course, introduces yet another layer of *gezeirah l'gezeirah*, and the *gemara* notes that once one invokes that principle, all the previously discussed challenges resurface. Rav Yosef would need to give the same answers we already provided to Rava's first explanation — whether based on *lo sh'chiach* or on formalistic category distinctions — and the *gemara* records that he does so.

### Rav Bibi Delay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב בִּיבִי אָמַר: גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יְשַׁהֶא. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב בִּיבִי: כְּלִי שֶׁנִּטְמָא מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב — אֵין מַטְבִּילִין אוֹתוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב, גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יְשַׁהֶא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai said** a different reason: The prohibition against immersing a vessel on a Festival is **a decree** issued by the Sages **lest one** come to **delay** the immersion of his impure vessels. Were the Sages to permit him to immerse vessels on a Festival, he might delay immersing all of his impure vessels until the Festival, when he has more free time; and were he to leave ritually impure vessels in his possession for a lengthy period, he might come to defile items that must be kept ritually pure, such as *teruma*. The Gemara comments: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Beivai:** With regard to **a vessel that became ritually impure on the eve of a Festival, one may not immerse it on a Festival,** due to **a decree lest he** come to **delay** and keep impure vessels in his home in order to immerse them on the Festival.

Rav Bibi offers a third explanation: *gezeirah shema yisheheh* — the decree is a protection against deliberate delay. If one is permitted to immerse vessels on *Shabbos* or *Yom Tov*, people will intentionally defer their *taharah* obligations to those days, when they have leisure time available. This concern is particularly acute on *Yom Tov*, when one wishes to be *tahor* for the occasion and may deliberately leave immersion to that day rather than attending to it earlier.

### Braita Tanaic

Rav Bibi's explanation is explicitly supported by a *tanaic* source. The *baraita* states: *keli she-nitma me-erev Yom Tov ein matbilin oto be-Yom Tov, gezeirah shema yisheheh* — a vessel that became *tamei* before *Yom Tov* may not be immersed on *Yom Tov* itself, and the *baraita* gives the explicit reason: lest one intentionally leave it over. This explanation also has the advantage of addressing both *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov* within a single unified concern, and it more directly accounts for the distinction between vessels that became *tamei* before versus on *Yom Tov* — a distinction that the earlier *gezeirah*-based explanations handled less elegantly.

### Rava Nireh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה כִּמְתַקֵּן כְּלִי. אִי הָכִי, אָדָם נָמֵי! אָדָם נִרְאֶה כְּמֵיקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** yet a different reason: It is prohibited to immerse a vessel on Shabbat **because it looks as if he is repairing the vessel.** Since the vessel was previously unfit for use, and the act of immersion renders it usable, this is similar to the repair of a vessel, which Torah law proscribes on Shabbat and Festivals. The Gemara challenges this understanding: **If so, a person** should **likewise** be prohibited to immerse himself, because it looks as if he is repairing himself through purification. The Gemara answers: **A person** undergoing immersion **looks as if he is cooling** himself.

Rava now offers his final and preferred explanation: *mipnei she-nireh k'metaken keli* — because immersing a vessel appears to be an act of fixing or improving it. By transitioning the vessel from *tamei* to *tahor* status, one is in effect rendering it serviceable in a new domain, and this looks like the rabbinic prohibition of *makeh ba-patish* — the finishing blow, the act of completing an object. This reasoning applies equally to *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov* without requiring any secondary *gezeirah*. It also explains the exceptions more naturally: the act of drawing water with a bucket, or performing *hashakah* on the water, does not appear as an act of *tikun* because the focus of the act is on the water or the drawing, not on the vessel. Similarly, the difference between a *vlad ha-tumah* and an *av ha-tumah* can be understood experientially — a vessel with only minor *tumah* does not look as dramatically improved by the immersion. Rava's explanation does not, however, satisfyingly distinguish between vessels that became *tamei* on *erev Yom Tov* versus on *Yom Tov* itself — for that distinction, we are left with a degree of leniency that is better explained by the *milsa d'lo sh'chicha* principle than by *nireh k'metaken*.

## Person vs Vessel

### Adam Nireh Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תִּינַח מַיִם יָפִים, מַיִם רָעִים מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: פְּעָמִים שֶׁאָדָם בָּא בַּשָּׁרָב, וְרוֹחֵץ אֲפִילּוּ בְּמֵי מִשְׁרָה. תִּינַח בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: פְּעָמִים שֶׁאָדָם בָּא מִן הַשָּׂדֶה מְלוּכְלָךְ בְּטִיט וּבְצוֹאָה, וְרוֹחֵץ אֲפִילּוּ בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation: This works out **well** in a case where he immerses himself in **good,** clean **water,** in which it would be a pleasure to bathe; but if he immerses himself in **bad,** murky **water,** as the water of a ritual bath is not always sufficiently clean, **what is there to say? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** Even in that case, his actions do not prove that his intention is to purify himself, as **sometimes a person comes** home **on a hot day and washes** himself **even in** putrid **water** in which flax **was soaked,** because the heat has made him so uncomfortable. The Gemara raises another objection: This works out **well in the summer season;** however, **in the rainy season,** when people do not usually immerse themselves in water in order to cool off, **what is there to say? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Sometimes a person comes** home **from the field soiled with mud and excrement and washes** himself **even in the rainy season.**

With Rava's explanation now established as primary, the *gemara* raises the natural question: if the problem is *nireh k'metaken* — that the act of immersion appears to be fixing — then why is a person permitted to immerse at all on *Shabbos* or *Yom Tov*? Surely a person transitioning from *tamei* to *tahor* also appears to be fixing themselves.

### Cooling Not Fixing

The *gemara* answers: *adam nireh ke-meiker* — a person going to the *mikvah* looks like they are simply cooling themselves off. The act is indistinguishable from ordinary bathing, and therefore it does not carry the appearance of a formal act of improvement or fixing.

### Winter Dirty Q

The *gemara* immediately challenges: this reasoning works for warm weather and clean water, but what about in winter, or when the *mikvah* water is dirty? In such cases, it is obviously not a cooling or pleasurable bath. One would not willingly immerse in cold, muddy water for comfort. The act then seems clearly to be performed for the purpose of *taharah* — and that looks like *metaken*.

### Rav Nachman Heat

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak responds with two answers. First: *pe'amim she-adam ba be-sharav ve-rocheitz afilu be-mei mishrah* — sometimes a person arrives from outdoors in intense heat and will bathe even in water used for soaking flax, water that is foul and dirty. In the summer, even unpleasant water offers relief, and so immersion in a dirty *mikvah* can still look like bathing. Second, regarding winter: *pe'amim she-adam ba min ha-sadeh u-muluchlach be-tit u-ve-tzoah ve-rocheitz afilu bi-ymot ha-geshamim* — sometimes a person comes from the field covered in mud and excrement, and in that case they will immerse even in cold water in winter, because the *mikvah* water, however cold and impure, is still cleaner than what they are covered with.

### Yom Kippur Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח בְּשַׁבָּת, בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אָמַר רָבָא: מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דִּבְשַׁבָּת שְׁרֵי וּבְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים אָסוּר? אֶלָּא הוֹאִיל וּבְשַׁבָּת שְׁרֵי — בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים נָמֵי שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues with another objection: This works out **well** and explains why it is permitted for one to immerse himself **on Shabbat,** when it is permitted to wash, and therefore it can be argued that it is not clearly evident that he is immersing in order to purify himself; but **on Yom Kippur,** when all bathing apart from ritual immersion is prohibited, **what is there to say?** His actions prove that his intention is to purify himself, and this should be prohibited because it looks as though he is repairing himself through purification. **Rava said:** It is impossible to prohibit immersion on Yom Kippur alone, as **is there anything that is permitted on Shabbat and** the very same act is **prohibited** as labor **on Yom Kippur?** In fact, the prohibition against labor is more stringent on Shabbat than on Yom Kippur. **Rather, since it is permitted on Shabbat, it is also permitted on Yom Kippur.** The Sages did not issue decrees with regard to Yom Kippur that would make it more stringent than Shabbat. Therefore, since they allowed a person to immerse on Shabbat because it looks as if he is doing so for pleasure, to escape the heat or to remove dirt, and not necessarily in order to purify himself, they permitted it on Yom Kippur as well, even though it is evident in that situation that his immersion is for the purpose of achieving purity.

The *gemara* then asks: granted, this reasoning accounts for *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*, since one can always construct a scenario where the immersion looks like bathing or cleaning. But what about *Yom Kippur*? On *Yom Kippur*, bathing is explicitly prohibited. One cannot claim that going to the *mikvah* on *Yom Kippur* resembles a normal bath; everyone knows that bathing on *Yom Kippur* is forbidden. So if a person immerses on *Yom Kippur*, it is obviously not for the purpose of cooling down or cleaning — it can only be for the sake of *taharah*. Should it not then be forbidden as *nireh k'metaken*?

### Ho'il Shabbat

Rava responds with the principle of *ho'il*: *mi ika midi de-be-Shabbos shari u-ve-Yom ha-Kippurim assur?* — Is there any matter that is permitted on *Shabbos* and yet forbidden on *Yom Kippur*? Rashi qualifies: in matters related to *melachot*, no such asymmetry exists. Eating, drinking, and bathing are forbidden on *Yom Kippur* but not because of *melachah*; they are a separate category of prohibition. But for anything that is permitted on *Shabbos* because it is not considered a forbidden labor, it should likewise be permitted on *Yom Kippur*. Therefore, since we permit person-immersion on *Shabbos* — because it does not rise to the level of *nireh k'metaken* when a person is the subject — that same logic carries over to *Yom Kippur*: *ho'il u-ve-Shabbos shari, be-Yom ha-Kippurim nami shari*.

### Vinegar Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרָבָא ״הוֹאִיל״? וְהָתְנַן: הַחוֹשֵׁשׁ בְּשִׁינָּיו — לֹא יְגַמֵּעַ בָּהֶן אֶת הַחוֹמֶץ, אֲבָל מְטַבֵּל הוּא כְּדַרְכּוֹ, וְאִם נִתְרַפֵּא — נִתְרַפֵּא. וְרָמֵינַן עֲלַהּ: לֹא יְגַמֵּעַ וּפוֹלֵט, אֲבָל מְגַמֵּעַ וּבוֹלֵעַ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Does Rava accept** the principle of: **Since? But didn't we learn** in a mishna: **One who is concerned** about pain **in his teeth may not sip vinegar through them** on Shabbat in order to alleviate his toothache; **however, he may dip** his food in vinegar **in his** usual **manner** during the meal and eat it, **and if he is healed** by the vinegar, **he is healed. And we raised a contradiction to this** mishna from the following *baraita*: **One may not sip** vinegar **and** immediately **spit** it **out,** as this is clearly done for medicinal purposes; **however, one may sip** the vinegar **and swallow** it, since it looks as though he is drinking it. This indicates that there is a permitted way to use vinegar even without dipping his food into it.

The *gemara* now tests whether Rava consistently holds this *ho'il* principle. The *mishnah* rules: if one has a toothache on *Shabbos*, one may not swallow vinegar directly over the gums for relief — doing so would constitute a transparent act of *refuah*, which is rabbinically forbidden on *Shabbos*. However, one may dip bread in vinegar in the normal manner and eat it; if the vinegar incidentally helps the toothache, *ve-im nitrapei nitrapei* — so be it.

### Vinegar Contradiction

A *baraita* appears to contradict the *mishnah*: *lo yigma u-falat* — one may not take vinegar and spit it out (clearly a medical application), *aval megama u-vole'a* — but one may take vinegar and swallow it. Here the *baraita* seems to permit swallowing vinegar directly, which the *mishnah* appears to forbid.

### Abaye Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: כִּי תְּנַן נָמֵי מַתְנִיתִין, מְגַמֵּעַ וּפוֹלֵט תְּנַן. וְרָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא מְגַמֵּעַ וּבוֹלֵעַ, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן קוֹדֶם טִבּוּל, כָּאן לְאַחַר טִבּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye said: Also when we learned** this ruling in **the mishna, we learned** it with regard to the case of **one who sips and spits** it **out. Rava said: Even** if **you say** that the mishna prohibits sipping vinegar even in a case where **one sips and swallows** it, it is still **not difficult: Here,** the *baraita* permits sipping vinegar **before dipping** one's food in it, as he appears to be doing so for pleasure. **There,** the mishna prohibits sipping the vinegar **after dipping** his food in it, when it is clear that one is doing so for medicinal purposes only.

Abaye resolves the contradiction: when the *mishnah* forbids taking vinegar, it refers specifically to *megama u-falat* — taking it in and spitting it out, which is an obvious medical procedure. Taking vinegar and swallowing it is permitted, and the *mishnah* was not addressing that case. Rava then offers his own resolution, one that accepts that swallowing itself is the subject of dispute. The two sources are reconciled on the basis of *timing*: *kan kodem tibul, kan le'achar tibul* — the distinction depends on where one is in the course of the meal. Before or during the phase of dipping — when sauces, vinegar, and condiments are present at the table — drinking vinegar directly is not so unusual. But after the dipping course is concluded, taking straight vinegar would be bizarre except as medicine. It is worth noting that the practice of reclining and multiple dippings at a formal meal was standard in the era when the *mishnah* was written, not the peculiar custom it became in later periods when the *rishonim* composed their works during *Pesach*.

### Rava Reverses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, נֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְקוֹדֶם טִבּוּל שְׁרֵי — לְאַחַר טִבּוּל נָמֵי שְׁרֵי! הֲדַר בֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵהַהִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara completes its question: **And if it is so** that Rava accepts the principle of: Since, **let him say: Since it is permitted** to sip vinegar **before dipping, it is also permitted** to do so **after dipping.** The Gemara answers: **Rava retracted that** statement with regard to vinegar and accepted Abaye's resolution of the difficulty, along with the principle of: Since.

But now the *gemara* turns Rava's *ho'il* principle against this very distinction. If we hold, as Rava does, that anything permitted on *Shabbos* must also be permitted on *Yom Kippur* — and by extension, anything permitted during one phase of *Shabbos* must be permitted throughout — then how can Rava draw a line between before and after the dipping? If swallowing vinegar is permitted before the dipping, *ho'il* would demand it be permitted after as well. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *hadar bei Rava me-hai* — Rava reversed himself from his distinction between before and after the dipping. In the end he accepts Abaye's explanation: the *mishnah* forbids only *megama u-falat*, while swallowing is always permitted.

### Braita Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּמֵהַהִיא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ, דִּלְמָא מֵהָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ? לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: כׇּל חַיָּיבֵי טְבִילוֹת טוֹבְלִין כְּדַרְכָּן, בֵּין בְּתִשְׁעָה בְּאָב בֵּין בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where** can it be determined **that he retracted that** teaching? **Perhaps he retracted this** statement with regard to immersion in a ritual bath on Yom Kippur. The Gemara answers: **This cannot enter your mind** that he retracted his view in that case, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **All** who are **obligated in immersions immerse in their** usual **manner, both on the Ninth of Av and on Yom Kippur,** even though it is prohibited to wash on these days. Rava would certainly have aligned his opinion with the explicit ruling of a *baraita*.

The *gemara* then raises the question of which position Rava ultimately retracted: was it the vinegar explanation or his broader *ho'il* principle applied to *Yom Kippur*? The *gemara* answers decisively: *lo salka da'atcha* — it is inconceivable that Rava retracted from his *Yom Kippur* position, because a *baraita* explicitly supports it. *Ve-tanya: kol chayavei tevilos tovilin ke-darkan bein be-Tisha be-Av bein be-Yom ha-Kippurim* — anyone who is obligated in immersion immerses normally, even on *Tisha be-Av* and on *Yom Kippur*. This *baraita* confirms that person-immersion is permitted on *Yom Kippur*, which means Rava must maintain his *ho'il* position there, and must therefore have retracted his before-and-after-dipping distinction in the vinegar case so as to be consistent.

## Ho'il Yom Kippur

### Tanaic Tisha Yom

The *baraita* just cited — that all those obligated in immersion may do so even on *Tisha be-Av* and *Yom Kippur* — deserves careful attention. The principle that immersion is permitted even on these days reflects the understanding that *tevilah* performed for the purpose of becoming *tahor*, rather than for the pleasure of bathing, does not fall within the prohibition of washing that applies on those days. Even on *Yom Kippur*, where bathing is explicitly prohibited, immersion *l'shem taharah* is categorically different. The prohibition of washing on *Yom Kippur* is directed at bathing *derech ta'anug* — in a manner of pleasure and comfort — and immersion in a *mikvah* for the purpose of *taharah* is not that. It may sit close to the line, but it is formally outside the category of the forbidden act. Hence the *baraita*'s ruling: one who is obligated immerses even on those days.

### Aveilus Principle

Tosafot addresses *Tisha be-Av* separately. Technically, the *baraita* permits immersion on that day as well, and Tosafot notes that in principle there is no prohibition that would bar it. Rather, the restraint on *Tisha be-Av* is not a formal *isur* but an expression of *aveilus* — mourning. Rebbi Chananya ben Antignos articulates the principle: *kedai hu beit Eloheinu le-abeid tevilah achas ba-shanah* — the mourning for the Temple is sufficient cause to forgo one immersion in the year. The Yerushalmi records that Rebbi Levi ruled in accordance with this view. So while one is technically permitted, the spirit of the day calls for foregoing the immersion as an expression of mourning.

### Yom Kippur Modern

Tosafot then addresses *Yom Kippur*. In the present era, Tosafot rules, the common practice is that women do not immerse on either *Yom Kippur* or *Tisha be-Av*. The reason, he explains, is tied to the conditions under which immersion would be practically necessary.

### Practical Need

Tosafot distinguishes between two very different motivations for immersion. In the era when *taharot* — the laws of ritual purity — were actively practiced, there were immediate and weighty consequences to remaining *tamei*, and immersion was therefore urgently required. Even without the principle of *tevilah bi-zmanah mitzvah* — that immersing at the proper time is itself a *mitzvah* — there was a pressing practical need that justified immersion even on those days. Today, however, the sole operative consequence of a woman remaining *tamei* is that she is forbidden to her husband. And since marital relations are forbidden on both *Tisha be-Av* and *Yom Kippur*, there is no immediate benefit to immersing on those days. The permission to immerse granted by the *baraita* was conditioned on there being an immediate and practical need; where that need is absent, the immersion is simply deferred. Tosafot further notes that a woman can arrange to wash her hair on *erev Yom Kippur* and then immerse on *motzai Yom Kippur* — which requires *chafifah* close to the immersion — thus satisfying her obligation without any need to immerse on the day itself. This practical arrangement confirms that no *halakhic* loss results from deferring. Tosafot applies the same reasoning to *Tisha be-Av*. The question of whether immersion would be permitted in a case of immediate need — for example, to permit non-sexual touch for a husband who is ill — is left open.

## Water Vessel

### Mishna Hashakah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין מַטְבִּילִין אֶת הַכְּלִי עַל גַּב מֵימָיו לְטַהֲרוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said: One may not immerse** an impure **vessel on account of its water** in order **to purify it on a Festival.** The Sages allowed impure water to be purified through contact with ritually pure water only in a stone vessel or in another vessel that does not contract impurity, but not in an impure vessel that would itself become purified through this immersion.

The *gemara* returns to examine the *mishnah*'s ruling directly. The *mishnah* permits *hashakah* — connecting the impure water to the *mikvah* via a stone vessel — but prohibits using an impure vessel as a means of immersing the vessel itself alongside the water. More precisely: one lowers a stone vessel containing impure water into the *mikvah*, and the waters connect and are purified; but one may not use a *tamei keli* for this purpose with the intent to purify the vessel in the same act.

### Attribution Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי מַתְנִיתִין? לָא רַבִּי וְלָא רַבָּנַן, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין מַטְבִּילִין אֶת הַכְּלִי עַל גַּב מֵימָיו לְטַהֲרוֹ, וְאֵין מַשִּׁיקִין אֶת הַמַּיִם בִּכְלִי אֶבֶן לְטַהֲרָן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מַטְבִּילִין כְּלִי עַל גַּב מֵימָיו לְטַהֲרוֹ, וּמַשִּׁיקִין אֶת הַמַּיִם בִּכְלִי אֶבֶן לְטַהֲרָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If that is its meaning, in accordance with **whose** opinion **is the mishna?** It is **not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **nor** with that of **the Rabbis. As it is taught** in the *Tosefta*: **One may not immerse an** impure **vessel on account of its water** in order **to purify** the vessel, **and one may not bring** impure **water into contact** with pure water **in a stone vessel** in order **to purify** the water; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: One may immerse** an impure **vessel on account of its water** in order **to purify** the vessel, **and one may bring** impure **water into contact** with pure water **in a stone vessel** in order **to purify** the water.

The *gemara* asks: *mani matnisin* — whose view does our *mishnah* reflect? It does not appear to match either Rebbi or the *Chachamim*, based on the positions recorded in a *baraita*.

### Braita Positions

The *baraita* reads as follows: Rebbi holds *ein matbilin et ha-keli al gabbei meimav le-taharo ve-ein mashikin et ha-mayim bi-khli even le-taharan* — one may neither immerse the vessel to purify it, nor may one perform *hashakah* on the water. Rebbi forbids both. The *Chachamim* say the opposite: *matbilin keli even al gabbei meimav le-taharo u-mashikin et ha-mayim bi-khli even le-taharan* — one may do both, both the immersion of the vessel and the *hashakah* of the water. Our *mishnah*, which permits *hashakah* but forbids immersing the vessel, matches neither position.

### Temporal Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? אִי רַבִּי — קַשְׁיָא הַשָּׁקָה, אִי רַבָּנַן — קַשְׁיָא הַטְבָּלָה! אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא רַבִּי, אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא רַבָּנַן. אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא רַבִּי: רֵישָׁא דְּבָרַיְיתָא בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְסֵיפָא בְּשַׁבָּת, וְכוּלַּהּ מַתְנִיתִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** If so, in accordance with **whose** opinion is the mishna? **If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, then the ruling with regard to **bringing** impure **water into contact** with pure water in a stone vessel is **difficult,** as in the *Tosefta* Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi prohibits doing so, whereas the mishna permits it; and **if** it is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** then the ruling with regard to the **immersion** of an impure vessel on account of its water is **difficult,** as in the *Tosefta* the Rabbis permit this, while the mishna, as explained by Shmuel, prohibits it. The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi; and **if you wish, say** instead that it is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** How so? **If you wish, say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi and explain that the **first clause of** that ***baraita*,** i.e., the first part of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's statement in the *Tosefta*, deals with **a Festival;** it is in that case that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi prohibits immersing an impure vessel on account of its water, but bringing impure water into contact with pure water in a stone vessel would be permitted. **And the latter clause,** which extends the prohibition to bringing impure water into contact with pure water, is referring **to** the more stringent case of **Shabbat. And the entire mishna** is referring to **a Festival,** when it is permitted to purify impure water by bringing it into contact with pure water in a stone vessel.

The *gemara* resolves the problem through a creative reading: *reisha de-Braitah be-Yom Tov, ve-seifa be-Shabbos* — the two clauses of the *baraita* are addressing different time contexts. The first clause, prohibiting vessel immersion, speaks of *Yom Tov*. The second clause, prohibiting *hashakah* of the water, speaks of *Shabbos*.

### Dispute Division

With this reading, both Rebbi and the *Chachamim* can be harmonized with the *mishnah*. According to Rebbi: on *Yom Tov*, one may not immerse the vessel — but the first clause's silence on water implies that *hashakah* of water is permitted on *Yom Tov*. On *Shabbos*, Rebbi goes further and prohibits even the *hashakah* of water. Thus Rebbi distinguishes between *Yom Tov* and *Shabbos* regarding the water, and our *mishnah*, which permits *hashakah* while forbidding vessel immersion, reflects Rebbi's position on *Yom Tov*. According to the *Chachamim*: on *Yom Tov*, they permit both vessel immersion and *hashakah* of water; but on *Shabbos*, they draw the distinction our *mishnah* reflects — permitting *hashakah* but not vessel immersion. Thus our *mishnah*, read as governing *Shabbos*, reflects the position of the *Chachamim*. Both readings are available: *ibba'it eima Rebbi ibba'it eima Rabbanan* — the *mishnah*'s water-vessel distinction can be attributed to either authority, depending on whether one reads the *mishnah* as speaking of *Yom Tov* or *Shabbos* respectively.
