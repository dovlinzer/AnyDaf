# Bava Kamma 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dayo & Kal Vachomer

### R. Tarfon & Dayo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְהֵא שֵׁן וָרֶגֶל חַיָּיב בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר – וּמָה קֶרֶן, שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא חֲצִי נֶזֶק – בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים חַיֶּיבֶת; שֵׁן וָרֶגֶל, שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים חַיָּיב?
>
> **Translation:** **And let** one be held **liable** for **Eating and Trampling** if these take place **in the public domain.** This can be inferred **via** an ***a fortiori*** inference, as follows: **And if** with regard to damage classified as **Goring, for which,** if it took place **on the property of the injured** party, according to the Rabbis one is liable to **pay only half** the cost of **the damage, yet** the owner of the animal is nevertheless **liable** for damage caused **in the public domain,** then with regard to damage classified as **Eating and Trampling,** whose *halakhot* are more strict, **as** one **pays the full** cost of the **damage** caused **on the property of the injured** party, **is it not right that** one should be **liable** for damage caused **in the public domain?**

The daf opens at the very bottom of 25b, beginning with the words *ve-tei shen ve-regel*. We are in the midst of the extended discussion of *dayo* as it applies to Rabbi Tarfon's position. By way of review: Rabbi Tarfon does not hold by *dayo* when invoking it would entirely undermine the *kal vachomer* — when the *kal vachomer* would yield nothing beyond what one already knew from the source case. In such a circumstance, he permits the *kal vachomer* to extend beyond the original case, drawing guidance from parallel cases as to how far the inference may reach. On this basis, the *chatzi nezek* of *keren* may be upgraded to *nezek shalem* in *reshut ha-nizak*, since *shen ve-regel* pay full damages there, and absent that extension, the *kal vachomer* would produce no new information.

### Shen/Regel in Reshut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: "וּבִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר" – וְלֹא בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **The verse states** with regard to Eating or Trampling (Exodus 22:4): **"And it consumed in the field of another,"** meaning that one's liability for this form of damage is limited to damage caused on the property of the injured party, but **not** for damage caused **in the public domain.**

With that review in place, the *gemara* now proceeds to explore what happens when one attempts to apply a *kal vachomer* to *shen ve-regel* in different domains. This new discussion operates even according to the *rabbanan* — Rabbi Tarfon is set aside for the moment.

The *gemara* asks: should *shen ve-regel* not be *chayav* in *reshut ha-rabim* by means of a *kal vachomer*? The argument is as follows. *Keren* in *reshut ha-nizak* pays only half damages, yet it is nonetheless *chayav* in *reshut ha-rabim* as well — also at half. *Shen ve-regel* in *reshut ha-nizak* pays full damages, making it weightier than *keren*. Should it not follow, then, that *shen ve-regel* is also *chayav* in *reshut ha-rabim* to at least some degree?

### Pasuk Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי כּוּלֵּיהּ קָאָמְרִינַן?! פַּלְגָא קָאָמְרִינַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Are we saying** that based on the *a fortiori* inference one should have to pay the **full** cost of the damage caused in the public domain for Eating and Trampling? That would be false, as the verse indicating one's liability to pay the full cost of the damage limits the application to damage caused in "the field of another." **We are saying** only that he should be liable for **half** the cost of the damage there, just as with regard to Goring.

The *gemara* answers that the *pasuk* forecloses this conclusion. The Torah states *u-vi'er bi-sdeh acher* — it consumed in another's field — specifying that the obligation of *shen ve-regel* applies only in a private domain, not in *reshut ha-rabim*. The *gemara*, however, pushes back: the *pasuk* establishes full payment in *reshut ha-nizak*, but perhaps *half* damages in *reshut ha-rabim* could still be inferred by *kal vachomer* — after all, *keren* pays half in *reshut ha-rabim*, so if *shen ve-regel* is weightier in *reshut ha-nizak*, should it not pay at least half in *reshut ha-rabim*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: "וְחָצוּ אֶת כַּסְפּוֹ" – כַּסְפּוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, וְלֹא כַּסְפּוֹ שֶׁל אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this as well: This is also incorrect, as **the verse states** with regard to the payment of half the damages: **"And divide its monetary value"** (Exodus 21:35). The use of the expression "its monetary value," and not "the monetary value," emphasizes that it is specifically **the price of this** ox that caused damage classified as Goring whose money will be divided, i.e., the owner of the ox will be obligated to pay half the cost of the damage, **but not the price of another,** i.e., not in other cases of damage caused by one's ox.

The *gemara* responds with a second *pasuk*: *ve-chatzu et kaspo* — they shall divide his money. This language, teaching that payment is divided and only half is owed, applies exclusively to *keren*: *kaspo shel zeh ve-lo kaspo shel acher* — the money of this one, meaning *keren*, and not of another. Half damages are a category unique to *keren*, and no *kal vachomer* can introduce them elsewhere.

It is worth noting in this context — as always when assessing *kal vachomer* arguments — that such inferences deliberately ignore the distinctive characteristics of each case that explain the underlying differences. Logically, one could argue that *shen ve-regel* is exempt in *reshut ha-rabim* precisely because these behaviors are common: animals must use public thoroughfares, and those who leave property in *reshut ha-rabim* bear contributory negligence (*pshia ha-nizak*). *Keren*, by contrast, is an atypical act of aggression, so there is no comparable policy rationale for exempting it in *reshut ha-rabim* and no *pshia ha-nizak* to invoke. Similarly, *shen ve-regel* paying full in *reshut ha-nizak* reflects the owner's high degree of negligence in allowing the animal there, while *keren*'s half-damages everywhere reflect the element of the unexpected. A *kal vachomer*, however, is a blunt instrument — it looks only at relative weight of obligation, not at the particular logic underlying each *halacha*. The *pesukim* therefore intervene to keep each *din* precisely where it belongs.

### Reverse Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא תְּהֵא שֵׁן וָרֶגֶל חַיֶּיבֶת בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק אֶלָּא חֲצִי נֶזֶק – מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר מִקֶּרֶן; וּמָה קֶרֶן, שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים חַיֶּיבֶת – בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק אֵינָהּ מְשַׁלֶּמֶת אֶלָּא חֲצִי נֶזֶק; שֵׁן וָרֶגֶל, שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים פְּטוּרָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a derivation from a different inference: **And let** one be held **liable** to pay **only half** the cost of **the damage** caused by **Eating and Trampling** even if the incident took place **on the property of the injured** party. This can be inferred **via** an ***a fortiori*** inference drawn **from Goring,** as follows: **And if** for damage classified as **Goring,** which is governed by a stricter *halakha*, as one is held **liable** for damage classified as Goring even if it occurs **in the public domain,** yet one nevertheless **pays only half** the cost of **the damage** caused **on the property of the injured** party, then with regard to damage classified as **Eating and Trampling,** which are governed by more lenient *halakhot*, as one is completely **exempt** from liability for damage caused **in the public domain, is it not right that** he should have to **pay** only **half** the cost of **the damage** caused **on the property of the injured** party?

The *gemara* now reverses direction and considers whether one might reduce *shen ve-regel*'s obligation in *reshut ha-nizak* to *chatzi nezek* by means of a *kal vachomer* drawn from *keren*. The argument runs as follows: *keren* is weightier — it is *chayav* in *reshut ha-rabim* — yet even in *reshut ha-nizak* it pays only half damages. *Shen ve-regel* is lighter, since it is exempt in *reshut ha-rabim* entirely. Should it not follow, by *kal vachomer*, that in *reshut ha-nizak shen ve-regel* also pays only half?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״יְשַׁלֵּם״ – תַּשְׁלוּמִין מְעַלְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The verse states** with regard to Eating and Trampling: "The best of his field and the best of his vineyard **he shall pay"** (Exodus 22:4). The intent of the verse is to emphasize that the owner of the ox pays **a proper,** meaning complete, amount of **payment,** and not half the cost of the damage.

The *gemara* rejects this as well. The *pasuk* states *mi-tav sadehu u-mi-tav karmo yishalem* — "he shall pay from the best of his field and the best of his vineyard." The word *yishalem*, meaning full compensation, establishes that *tashlumin maalya* — complete payment — is mandated. *Shen ve-regel* pays full in *reshut ha-nizak*, and that cannot be diminished by inference.

### Yechetzun & Half

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא תְּהֵא קֶרֶן בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים חַיָּיב – מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר; וּמָה שֵׁן וָרֶגֶל, שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם – בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים פְּטוּרָה; קֶרֶן, שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק חֲצִי נֶזֶק – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים פְּטוּרָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a derivation from a different inference: **And let** one **not** be held **liable** at all with regard to damage classified as **Goring in the public domain.** This can be inferred **via** an ***a fortiori*** inference, as follows: **And if** for damage classified as **Eating and Trampling, for which** one is liable to pay **the full** cost of **the damage** for incidents that took place **on the property of the injured** party, one is completely **exempt** for damage caused **in the public domain,** then with regard to damage classified as **Goring,** which is governed by a more lenient *halakha*, as one is held **liable** for only **half** the cost of **the damage** caused **on the property of the injured** party, **is it not right that** one should be **exempt in the public domain?**

The *gemara* attempts one further *kal vachomer*, this time in the opposite direction for *keren*: since *shen ve-regel*, the weightier case in *reshut ha-nizak*, is nonetheless exempt in *reshut ha-rabim*, should *keren*, which is less weighty in *reshut ha-nizak* (paying only half), not also be exempt in *reshut ha-rabim*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אָמַר קְרָא: "יֶחֱצוּן" – אֵין חֲצִי נֶזֶק חָלוּק לֹא בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְלֹא בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The verse states** in reference to an innocuous ox: "And the carcass **they shall** also **divide"** (Exodus 21:35), to indicate that **there is no difference** with regard to the payment of **half** the cost of **the damage, whether** the damage occurs **in a public domain or** whether it occurs **on private property.**

Rabbi Yochanan responds that the *pasuk* states *ve-gam et ha-meis yechetzun* — and also the dead animal they shall divide. The word *yechetzun* is redundant; since the text already spoke of dividing the money, why add this term? To teach that *chatzi nezek* is not divisible — it applies universally. *Ein chatzi nezek chaluk*: whether in *reshut ha-rabim* or *reshut ha-yachid*, *keren* always pays half. All attempted *kal vachomer* arguments are thus dispatched by *pesukim* that lock each *av nezikin* into its established pattern. *Keren* is half everywhere; *shen ve-regel* is full in *reshut ha-nizak* and zero in *reshut ha-rabim*.

## Murder & Kofer

### Murder Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וִיהֵא אָדָם חַיָּיב בְּכוֹפֶר – מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר; וּמָה שׁוֹר, שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים – חַיָּיב בְּכוֹפֶר; אָדָם, שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא חַיָּיב בְּכוֹפֶר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a derivation from a different inference: **And let a person** who inadvertently kills another be **liable to pay ransom.** This can be inferred **via** an ***a fortiori*** inference, as follows: **And if** the owner of an **ox,** who **is not liable** to pay the **four types of indemnity,** i.e., pain, medical costs, loss of livelihood, and humiliation, if his ox injures a person, is nevertheless **liable to pay ransom** if it killed someone, then with regard to **a person, who is liable** to pay the **four types of indemnity** if he injures another, **is it not right that he should be liable to pay ransom** if he were to kill him?

The *gemara* now ventures beyond the domain of animal damages entirely. Should a human murderer not be *chayav* in *kofer* by means of a *kal vachomer*? The argument: an ox that gores a person does not pay the *arba'ah devarim* — the four additional payments of *tza'ar*, *nezek*, *ripui*, *shevet*, and *boshet* — that apply only when a human injures another human. The ox is therefore the lighter case. Yet even an ox whose goring has become habitual, and which kills a human on a subsequent occasion, requires the owner to pay *kofer*. If the lighter case — the ox — incurs *kofer*, how much more so should a human being who commits murder, the weightier case, also be required to pay *kofer*?

One might note that this *hava amina* is somewhat surprising given the principle of *kam lei bi-drabba minei* — that one does not pay a monetary penalty alongside a capital crime. The *gemara*'s suggested answer is that the *kofer* in the case of murder is not conceived as an external penalty added on top of the capital punishment, but rather as an intrinsic component of the act itself — as if the act of murder naturally carries two dimensions: the capital sanction and a payment. Rashi explains the case as involving *meizid* without *hasra'ah*, meaning the person would not in practice be executed, yet the underlying offense is capital in character. Nevertheless, the question remains whether such an act ought to carry a *kofer*-like payment, and the *gemara* answers that it does not.

### Kofer Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: "כְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר יוּשַׁת עָלָיו"; "עָלָיו" – וְלֹא עַל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The verse states** with regard to an ox killing a person: "He shall give for the redemption of his life **whatever is imposed upon him"** (Exodus 21:30). **"Upon him":** This means upon the owner of an ox who kills a person, **but not upon a person** who kills another.

The *pasuk* states *k'chol asher yushas alav* — according to all that is placed upon him — referring to the obligation of *kofer* when the ox gores and kills. The *gemara* reads: *alav* — upon the ox or its owner — *ve-lo al adam* — and not upon a human who takes a life. The *kofer* obligation is thus textually confined to the world of animal goring and does not extend to a human murderer.

By way of further clarification: in the case of the *muad* ox, the *kofer* is best understood not as compensatory damages but as an atonement payment. The owner, through his negligence in allowing a habitually dangerous animal to remain in circulation, has become deserving at some virtual level of the death penalty, and the *kofer* is paid in its stead. When the possibility was raised of applying *kofer* to human murder, it would have operated differently — more like damages accompanying the capital crime — but the *pasuk* forecloses even that application.

### Reverse Ox Kal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וִיהֵא שׁוֹר חַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים – מִקַּל וְחוֹמֶר; וּמָה אָדָם, שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּכוֹפֶר – חַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים; שׁוֹר, שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּכוֹפֶר – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא חַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests the reverse derivation: **And let** the owner of **an ox** that injured a person be **liable** to pay the **four types of indemnity.** This can be inferred **via** an ***a fortiori*** inference, as follows: **And if a person, who is not obligated to pay ransom** if he kills someone, **is** nevertheless **liable** to pay **four types of indemnity** if he injures another, then with regard to the owner of **an ox,** who **is liable to pay ransom, is it not right that he should** also **be liable** to pay the **four types of indemnity?**

The *gemara* now reverses the inference. The ox is weightier in that it pays *kofer*; a human does not. Should one then argue that the ox, since it is weightier by virtue of *kofer*, must also be *chayav* in the *arba'ah devarim* when it injures a human? The *kal vachomer*: a human being, who does not pay *kofer*, is nonetheless *chayav* in the *arba'ah devarim* — *tza'ar, ripui, shevet*, and *boshet* — when injuring another human. Since an ox is weightier (it pays *kofer*), should it not also be *chayav* in these four payments when it causes injury?

### Arba'ah Devarim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: "אִישׁ בַּעֲמִיתוֹ" – וְלֹא שׁוֹר בַּעֲמִיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The verse states** with regard to this matter: "And if **a man** maims **another"** (Leviticus 24:19), from which it can be derived that this *halakha* applies when a man harms another person **but not** when **an ox** harms **another** person.

The *gemara* again turns to a *pasuk*: *ki yitein mum b'amito* — when a man places a blemish upon his fellow. From the verses teaching *ayin tachat ayin, shein tachat shein*, and the surrounding language, we derive the *arba'ah devarim*. The phrase *ish b'amito* — a man with his fellow — explicitly confines these payments to cases of a human injuring another human: *ish b'amito ve-lo shor b'amito*. Even if the *kal vachomer* would seem to demand it, the *pasuk* restricts these four payments exclusively to human-on-human harm, and the ox is excluded.

## Regel Kofer Puzzle

### Regel Kofer Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רֶגֶל שֶׁדָּרְסָה עַל גַּבֵּי תִּינוֹק בַּחֲצַר הַנִּיזָּק, מַהוּ שֶׁתְּשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר? מִי אָמְרִינַן: מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַקֶּרֶן – קֶרֶן, כֵּיוָן דַּעֲבַד תְּרֵי וּתְלָתָא זִמְנֵי – אוֹרְחֵיהּ הוּא, וּמְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר; הָכָא נָמֵי לָא שְׁנָא;
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **Trampling,** in the case of an animal **that tramples a child in the courtyard of the injured** party and kills the child, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the liability of the owner of the animal **to pay ransom?** The Gemara explains the different sides of the question: **Do we say** that this *halakha* is **just as it is with regard to Goring?** Accordingly, just as with regard to **Goring, once** an animal has gored **two or three times** this becomes defined as **its usual manner** and therefore it is deemed forewarned **and** the owner must **pay ransom** in the event that it kills a person by an act classified as Goring, **here too it is not different,** as with regard to the category of Trampling the owner is deemed forewarned from the start and he must therefore pay ransom.

The *gemara* now pivots to a question that may seem tangential but proves directly relevant to a ruling of Rabbi Tarfon. The question raised is: *regel she-darsah al gabei tinok b'chatzer ha-nizak mahu she-teshalem kofer* — if an animal tramples and kills an infant in the domain of the injured party, does it incur *kofer*?

The question turns on a conceptual uncertainty about the nature of *kofer* itself. When an *ox* gores and kills — having done so habitually — is the *kofer* assessed because of the profound negligence of the owner in permitting a dangerous animal to remain active, which led to a human death? If so, there would be a comparable degree of negligence when one allows a large animal to walk in another person's domain where it might step on an infant — an act habitual to *regel*. Or is *kofer* specifically tied to the quasi-intentional character of a goring act — something that resembles a kind of murder done by one's animal — and thus inapplicable to the incidental trampling characteristic of *regel*, even if that trampling caused death?

### R. Tarfon Tam Kofer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הִכְנִיס שׁוֹרוֹ לַחֲצַר בַּעַל הַבַּיִת שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, וּנְגָחוֹ לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת וָמֵת; הַשּׁוֹר – בִּסְקִילָה, וּבְעָלָיו – בֵּין תָּם בֵּין מוּעָד, מְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר שָׁלֵם; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a solution to this dilemma from a *baraita*: If one **brought his ox into the courtyard of a homeowner without** his **permission, and it gored the homeowner and he died, the ox** is killed **by stoning and the owner** of the ox is obligated to **pay** the **full** amount of the **ransom,** regardless of **whether** the animal was **innocuous or forewarned.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Tarfon.**

The *gemara* then cites a direct ruling bearing on the question: if a person brought his ox into another's domain without permission (*hichnis shoro l'chatzer ba'al ha-bayit she-lo bi-reshut*) and the ox gored the owner and the owner died, the ox is stoned and the owner — whether the animal is a *tam* or a *muad* — pays *kofer shalem*, full *kofer*. That is the position of Rabbi Tarfon.

This ruling is striking. We know Rabbi Tarfon holds that one pays full damages (*nezek shalem*) in *reshut ha-nizak*, but *kofer* is not simply an enhanced form of *nezek* — it is a categorically different obligation. And by Torah law, a *tam* that kills a human pays no *kofer* at all. So how does Rabbi Tarfon arrive at a *tam* paying full *kofer* in *reshut ha-nizak*? The *gemara* asks: *kofer shalem be-tam le-Rabbi Tarfon mena lei* — from where does Rabbi Tarfon derive this?

### R. Yosei HaGalili

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוֹפֶר שָׁלֵם בְּתָם, לְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן – מְנָא לֵיהּ? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּסָבַר לֵיהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, דְּאָמַר: תָּם מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי כוֹפֶר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – וּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר מֵרֶגֶל? אַלְמָא אִיכָּא כּוֹפֶר בְּרֶגֶל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to clarify: **From where** does **Rabbi Tarfon** derive that **with regard to an innocuous** ox the owner must also pay the **full** amount of the **ransom?** Is it **not because he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who says** that the owner of **an innocuous** ox that killed a person **pays half** the **ransom** if the incident took place **in the public domain? And he derived** this ruling **via** an ***a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakhot* of **Trampling:** And if in a case of Trampling, for which one is exempted entirely from liability when it occurs in the public domain, one must nevertheless pay the full ransom if the incident took place on the property of the injured party, with regard to Goring, for which one must pay half the ransom when it occurs in the public domain, is it not right that one should be obligated to pay full ransom for an incident that took place on the property of the injured party? **Evidently,** it is clear that **there is a ransom** payment **in** the case of **Trampling.**

The *gemara* answers that it must be because Rabbi Tarfon holds in accordance with Rabbi Yosei HaGalili, who rules that a *tam* pays *chatzi kofer* in *reshut ha-rabim*. With that as a starting point, Rabbi Tarfon can then apply his characteristic *kal vachomer*: *regel* pays full damages in *reshut ha-nizak* and nothing in *reshut ha-rabim*; a *tam*, which pays half *kofer* in *reshut ha-rabim*, should certainly — by *kal vachomer* — pay full *kofer* in *reshut ha-nizak*. The inference from *chatzi kofer* in *reshut ha-rabim* to *kofer shalem* in *reshut ha-nizak* follows the same logical structure as his *kal vachomer* for *nezek*.

But crucially, for this *kal vachomer* to be valid in the world of *kofer*, one must be able to say that *regel* too pays *kofer* in *reshut ha-nizak*. Only then is the comparison apples to apples — comparing *kofer* obligations in different domains. If the only basis for the *kal vachomer* is that *regel* pays full *nezek* in *reshut ha-nizak*, then one is comparing *nezek* and *kofer* across domains, which is a category error.

### Nezek/Kofer Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא: תַּנָּא – מִנְּזָקִין דְּרֶגֶל מַיְיתֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi of Neharde'a said:** It is possible to explain that **the *tanna* derived** his *a fortiori* inference **from damage** caused **by Trampling:** And if in a case of Trampling, for which one is completely exempt from liability when it happens in the public domain, one pays the full cost of the damage done on the property of the injured party, with regard to Goring, for which one must pay half the ransom payment if the ox kills a person in the public domain, is it not right that one would certainly be obligated to pay the full ransom if the person was killed on his own property? According to this reasoning there is no indication that one pays a ransom payment in the case of a child that was killed by Trampling.

Rav Shimi of Nehardea, citing Rav Ashi, initially suggests that the *kal vachomer* need not rely on *regel* paying *kofer* at all — it can be built on the fact that *regel* pays full *nezek* in *reshut ha-nizak*. The inference would then be: a *tam* pays half *kofer* in *reshut ha-rabim*, therefore it should pay full *kofer* in *reshut ha-nizak*, because *regel* goes from full *nezek* in *reshut ha-nizak* to zero in *reshut ha-rabim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִפְרוֹךְ: מָה לִנְזָקִין דְּרֶגֶל – שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן בְּאֵשׁ! מִטָּמוּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if this is the basis for Rabbi Tarfon's opinion, **let** the Gemara **refute** it in this way: **What** can be learned about ransom **from damage** caused **by Trampling? These** same *halakhot* **apply to Fire;** nevertheless, there is no obligation to pay ransom when a person is killed by Fire, as was stated explicitly in a *baraita* above (10a). Consequently, the attempt to derive an *a fortiori* inference about ransom from Trampling is obviously flawed. The Gemara answers: The *a fortiori* inference can be **based on** the damage to **concealed** articles caused by Trampling on the property of the injured party. One would be exempt for damage such as this if it were caused by Fire.

The *gemara*, however, presses back. One cannot make the leap from *nezek* of *regel* to *kofer*, because there exist counterexamples of cases that are *chayav* in *nezek* but *patur* in *kofer*. For instance: *eish* — fire damages property but does not generate *kofer*. One could therefore challenge the *kal vachomer* on the grounds that *nezikin de-regel*, the damages of *regel*, are found in cases like *eish* which carry no *kofer* — meaning *nezek* and *kofer* are not co-extensive, and one cannot infer from one to the other.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְטָמוּן – שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנוֹ בְּבוֹר! מִכֵּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds to this challenge with a different one: **What** is notable about damage **to concealed** articles caused by Trampling? It is notable **in that these** same *halakhot* **apply to** the category of **Pit,** but nevertheless there is no ransom paid if a person is killed by a pit. Consequently, an attempt to derive an *a fortiori* inference about ransom from this *halakha* is obviously flawed. The Gemara answers: The *a fortiori* inference can be **based on** damage caused to **vessels** by Trampling on the property of the injured party. One would be exempt for damage of this nature if it were caused by a pit.

The *gemara* then attempts to refine the argument by choosing a more specific case of *regel* — one that is *chayav* in *nezek* but that excludes the *eish* and *bor* counterexamples. The suggestion is to focus on *tamon* — hidden objects — since *eish* is not *chayav* for hidden objects. But then the counterexample of *bor* can be raised: *bor* pays for hidden objects. So one might still say there are cases *chayav* in *nezek* and *patur* in *kofer*. The *gemara* then shifts to *keilim* — vessels — since *bor* is not *chayav* for vessels (*shor ve-lo adam, chamor ve-lo keilim*). But *eish* is *chayav* for *keilim*, so the counterexample remains. The *gemara* then tries *keilim tmunim* — buried vessels — since *eish* does not pay for buried objects and *bor* does not pay for them either. Is this finally the case where no counterexample can be raised?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְכֵלִים – שֶׁיֶּשְׁנָן בְּאֵשׁ! מִכֵּלִים טְמוּנִים. מָה לְכֵלִים טְמוּנִים – שֶׁיֶּשְׁנָן בְּאָדָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this as well: **What** is notable about damage caused **to vessels** by Trampling? It is notable **in that these** same *halakhot* **apply to** the category of **Fire.** The Gemara answers: The *a fortiori* inference can be **based on** damage caused to **concealed vessels** by Trampling. In this case, one would be liable for Trampling but exempt from liability for both Fire and Pit, so this can be the basis for the ransom payment, via the *a fortiori* inference stated by Rav Shimi of Neharde'a. The Gemara rejects this as well: **What** is notable about damage caused **to concealed vessels** by Trampling? It is notable **in that these** same *halakhot* **apply to** the category of **Man,** as a person is liable for damage to these items but does not pay ransom if he inadvertently kills another person.

The *gemara* answers: *mah le-keilim tmunim sheken yeshnan be-adam* — when a human being damages buried vessels, he is *chayav*, yet a human being is *patur* in *kofer*. So there remains a case *chayav* in *nezek* of *regel* and *patur* in *kofer*, and thus the gap between *nezek* and *kofer* cannot be bridged by *kal vachomer*.

### Regel Pays Kofer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִכּוֹפֶר דְּרֶגֶל מַיְיתֵי לַהּ? אַלְמָא אִיכָּא כּוֹפֶר בְּרֶגֶל! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from it** that since the *halakhot* of the ransom payment with regard to Goring cannot be deduced from the *halakhot* of damages with regard to Trampling, the *tanna* **derived** his *a fortiori* inference **based on** the *halakhot* of **ransom** in a case **of Trampling,** and therefore it may be concluded that **apparently there is ransom** in a case **of Trampling?** The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from it** that this is so. Consequently, in the case of a child trampled to death by Trampling while on his parents' property, the owner of the animal must pay ransom.

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela lav shemama mina mikofer de-regel mayseela* — one must deduce that the *kal vachomer* is derived specifically from the *kofer* of *regel* itself. The only way the *kal vachomer* coheres is if *regel* itself pays *kofer* in *reshut ha-nizak*. Then the argument is direct and analogical: *regel* pays full *kofer* in *reshut ha-nizak*; a *tam* that pays half *kofer* in *reshut ha-rabim* should certainly pay full *kofer* in *reshut ha-nizak*. This is apples to apples.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא – דְּאִיכָּא כּוֹפֶר בְּרֶגֶל; דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לֵיכָּא כּוֹפֶר בְּרֶגֶל – וְתַנָּא מִנְּזָקִין דְּרֶגֶל מַיְיתֵי לַהּ, לִפְרוֹךְ: מָה לִנְזָקִין דְּרֶגֶל – שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן בְּרֶגֶל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: So too, it is reasonable** to say **that there is** an obligation to pay **ransom** in a case **of Trampling, as, if it enters your mind** to say that **there is no** obligation to pay **ransom** in a case **of Trampling, and the *tanna* derived** his *a fortiori* inference **from damage** caused **by Trampling, let** the Gemara **refute it** in this way: **What** is notable about **damage** caused **by Trampling?** It is notable **in that these** same *halakhot* **apply to Trampling,** while there is no obligation to pay ransom in a case of Trampling. In other words, it would be possible to derive the obligation to pay a full ransom where a person was killed by the Goring of an innocuous ox while on the property of the victim only if there is also an obligation to pay ransom where the person was killed by Trampling.

Rav Acha of Difti then sharpens the point for Ravina: had the *kal vachomer* truly been derived from the *nezek* of *regel* rather than from the *kofer* of *regel*, the *gemara* should have asked immediately — why not challenge the inference by noting that *regel* itself has no *kofer*? If *regel* doesn't pay *kofer*, how can you infer a *kofer* obligation for *keren* from the *nezek* pattern of *regel*? The fact that this obvious objection was never raised — and instead the *gemara* labored through cases of *eish*, *bor*, *tamon*, and *keilim tmunim* — proves that everyone understands the *kal vachomer* to rest on the *kofer* of *regel*, not on its *nezek*. And this, in turn, establishes that *regel* does pay *kofer* in *reshut ha-nizak* — a ruling that apparently even the *rabbanan*, who disagree with Rabbi Tarfon on other points, would accept. Rabbi Tarfon's further step — full *kofer* for a *tam* in *reshut ha-nizak* — builds on this foundation together with the ruling of Rabbi Yosei HaGalili regarding half *kofer* for a *tam* in *reshut ha-rabim*.

## Adam Muad L'Olam

### Mishnah Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָדָם מוּעָד לְעוֹלָם – בֵּין שׁוֹגֵג בֵּין מֵזִיד, בֵּין עֵר בֵּין יָשֵׁן. סִימֵּא אֶת עֵין חֲבֵירוֹ וְשִׁיבֵּר אֶת הַכֵּלִים – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The legal status of **a person** is **always** that of one **forewarned.** Therefore, **whether** the damage was **unintentional or intentional, whether** he was **awake** while he caused the damage **or asleep,** whether **he blinded another's eye or broke vessels, he must pay the full** cost of **the damage.**

With the discussion of Rabbi Tarfon and *dayo* complete, the *gemara* introduces the next *mishnah*, which states: *adam muad le-olam bein shogeg bein meizid, bein eir bein yashen* — a person is always *muad*, always obligated to pay full damages, whether the act was intentional or unintentional, whether the person was awake or asleep. If while asleep one blinded another person or broke a vessel, full damages are owed. This is the fifth and final entry in the list of cases that are *muad*.

One should note that the *Yerushalmi* qualifies the sleeping case: if the person went to sleep and someone placed a vessel near him after the fact, he is exempt — not because his strict liability is removed, but because the person who placed the vessel there has introduced countervailing *peshias ha-nizak*, contributory negligence that shifts responsibility. If, however, no person placed the vessel there and it came to be near the sleeper by wind or other neutral cause, and the sleeper rolled over and broke it, he would be liable under *adam muad le-olam*.

Tosafot and the Ramban understand the principle somewhat differently. The Ramban holds that *adam muad le-olam* is genuinely absolute — no negligence on the part of the actor is required whatsoever, and one is liable purely because one caused the damage. Tosafot, by contrast, understands the principle to mean that a reduced threshold of negligence is sufficient, but some minimal degree is still required; going to sleep near a vessel itself constitutes that minimum.

### Arba'ah Devarim Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ קָתָנֵי ״סִימֵּא אֶת עֵין חֲבֵירוֹ״ דּוּמְיָא דְּ״שִׁיבֵּר אֶת הַכֵּלִים״; מָה הָתָם – נֶזֶק אִין, אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים לָא; אַף סִימֵּא אֶת עֵין חֲבֵירוֹ – נֶזֶק אִין, אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers: It **teaches** in the mishna: **He blinded another's eye,** and presumably this is **similar to** the other example: **Broke vessels.** From this it can be inferred that **just as there,** in the case of the broken vessels, **yes,** one must pay for the **damage** he caused but he does **not** pay **the four types of indemnity, so too,** in a case where **he blinds another, yes,** he must pay for the **damage** he caused, but he does **not** pay **the four types of indemnity,** since he caused the injury while asleep or unintentionally.

The *gemara* now makes a critical qualification. The *mishnah* teaches that blinding someone is analogous to breaking a vessel — just as with a vessel there is only *nezek* and no *arba'ah devarim* (since those additional payments do not apply to objects), so too when one injures a person under these circumstances, there is *nezek* but no obligation for *tza'ar, ripui, shevet*, and *boshet*.

This is a fundamental distinction. *Adam muad le-olam* and its strict liability applies to the *nezek* component. The *arba'ah devarim*, however, require a substantially higher level of intentionality. Rashi explains: one is obligated in *arba'ah devarim* only when the act was *meizid* or at least *karov le-meizid* — intentional or close to intentional — on the model of the case in the *pasuk* where two men fight and one deliberately seizes the other's private parts, an act that, while the precise injury may not have been intended, reflects a high degree of willful, aggressive conduct. Mere carelessness — even carelessness sufficiently prominent to trigger the full strict liability of *nezek* — does not suffice for *arba'ah devarim*.

### Petza Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה, וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה, אָמַר קְרָא: ״פֶּצַע תַּחַת פָּצַע״ – לְחַיְּיבוֹ עַל הַשּׁוֹגֵג כְּמֵזִיד, וְעַל הָאוֹנֶס כְּרָצוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the *halakha* that one must pay the full cost of the damage in a case where there was no intent to cause damage, the Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Ḥizkiyya says, and similarly, the school of Ḥizkiyya taught: The verse states: "Wound for wound [*petza taḥat patza*]"** (Exodus 21:25). This phrase is superfluous, as the Torah states elsewhere (see Leviticus 24:19) that one is liable to pay compensation when injuring another. This verse serves **to render him liable** to pay **for the unintentional** damage just **as** he pays **for the intentional** damage; **and** he pays **for** damage caused by **accident** just **as** he pays **for** damage caused **willingly.**

The *gemara* asks: what is the textual basis for strict liability in *nezek*? Chizkiyah answers, citing a tradition taught in his *yeshiva*: the *pasuk* states *petza tachas petza* — a wound in the place of a wound. From this verse we derive two distinct *halachot*. First, it teaches *l'chayvo al ha-shogeg ke-meizid* — one is liable for unintentional harm as for intentional harm — and *al ha-ones ke-ratzon* — even for completely compelled circumstances, as if they were done willingly. Second, the same verse teaches *tza'ar bi-mkom nezek* — that even when paying *nezek*, one must also pay separately for the pain that accompanies the injury.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִיתֵּן צַעַר בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק! אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב קְרָא: ״פֶּצַע בְּפָצַע״; מַאי ״תַּחַת פָּצַע״ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **this** verse **is necessary** in order **to** indicate that one must **pay** compensation for **pain,** even **in a case** where he pays compensation for **damage** caused by the injury. Consequently, it seems that that verse cannot also be the source of the principle derived by the school of Ḥizkiyya. The Gemara answers: **If** it is **so** that the superfluous phrase is intended to teach only that, then **let the verse write: *Petza befatza*,** which carries the same meaning. **What,** then, is meant by the superfluous word *taḥat* in the phrase "*petza **taḥat patza*****"?** It indicates that we must **derive two** conclusions **from it:** That one is liable to pay for pain even in a case where he pays compensation for damage, and that he is liable for unintentional damage as he is for intentional damage, and for damage caused by accident as for damage caused willingly.

The *gemara* objects: perhaps the verse is needed entirely for the second ruling and cannot simultaneously teach strict liability? The *gemara* answers: had the Torah wished to convey only the *tza'ar* teaching, it could have written *petza be-vatza*. The use of *tachat* — "in the stead of" — is the additional word that teaches both *halachot* simultaneously: *shema mina tartei*, we derive two things. Significantly, Tosafot observes that although both are derived from the same verse, strict liability applies only to *nezek* and not to *tza'ar*. This is because the general orientation of the law is to limit unprecedented obligations to their clearest application, and therefore the rule of strict liability is confined to the *nezek* component that the verse explicitly addresses.

## Stone Unaware

### Stone Unknown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: הָיְתָה אֶבֶן מוּנַּחַת לוֹ בְּחֵיקוֹ וְלֹא הִכִּיר בָּהּ, וְעָמַד וְנָפְלָה; לְעִנְיַן נְזָקִין – חַיָּיב, לְעִנְיַן אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים – פָּטוּר, לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת – מְלֶאכֶת מַחְשֶׁבֶת אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה, לְעִנְיַן גָּלוּת – פָּטוּר,
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba says:** If **there was a stone lying in** one's **lap and** he **was unaware of it, and he arose and it fell** and caused damage, **with regard to damages** he is **liable** to pay the full cost of the damage caused by the stone. **With regard to the four types of indemnity,** he is **exempt. With regard to Shabbat,** if the falling stone caused him to violate one of the prohibited categories of labor; for example, if the stone fell from a private domain to the public domain, he is exempt. The reason is that **the Torah prohibited** only **planned,** constructive **labor** on Shabbat, and he did not plan to perform this labor. **With regard to exile,** the punishment prescribed for one who unintentionally but negligently kills another, were this stone to kill someone he is **exempt,** as the incident is deemed accidental.

Rabbah now presents a series of cases that test the interplay between strict liability, intent, and the specific requirements of each legal domain. The first case: a stone was placed on a person's chest while he slept, and he had no awareness of it; he then stood up, the stone fell, and broke something — or in another version, killed someone or struck a slave. *Le-inyan nezikin chayav* — for purposes of *nezikin*, he is *chayav*. This is a precise illustration of the strictest reading of *adam muad le-olam*: not only is there no intention, there is not even awareness that one is holding something. The stone fell through his *koach*, his physical force — and one might have thought that a completely unaware *koach* should be treated like *chatzi nezek tzerorot* and not attributed to the actor. Nevertheless, he is strictly liable for *nezek*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעִנְיַן עֶבֶד – פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה רַבּוֹ רוֹפֵא, וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״כְּחוֹל עֵינִי״, וְסִימְּאָהּ; ״חֲתוֹר לִי שִׁינִּי״, וְהִפִּילָהּ – שִׂיחֵק בָּאָדוֹן, וְיָצָא לְחֵרוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to** a Canaanite **slave** whose tooth was destroyed or eye was blinded by the stone, potentially enabling the slave to earn his freedom (see Exodus 21:26–27), this is the subject of **a dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:25): **If the master was a doctor and** the slave **said to him: Paint** the lid of **my eye** in order to heal it, **and** the master **blinded it** during the procedure, or if the slave requested from his master: **Scrape my tooth** in order to heal it, **and** the master **knocked out** the tooth while scraping it, the slave **has mocked the master, as he is emancipated** due to the act of the master himself.

For *arba'ah devarim*, he is *patur* — there is no element of intentionality or even negligence, let alone *karov le-meizid*. For *Shabbat*, he is *patur* as well — *melachah* requires *melechet machshevet*, intentional creative work, and this act was entirely unconscious. For *galut* — if the stone killed someone — he is also *patur*. The verse states *makeh nefesh bishgagah* — there must be some form of *shgagah*, some negligent awareness that one was doing something potentially dangerous. A completely blameless act without any prior awareness does not qualify. As for the slave: if the stone knocked out a slave's tooth or blinded the slave's eye, the question of whether the slave goes free is the subject of a dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the *rabbanan*. A *beraita* provides the classic case: a master who was acting as a doctor for his slave, or was trying to assist with a dental procedure, and in the process accidentally blinded or knocked out the tooth of the slave. The *tanna kamma* holds that the slave goes free regardless of intentionality — the master caused the injury, and the slave is freed. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel requires *ad she-yiskayvein le-shachosah* — the master must have intended to destroy it. All the cases of Rabbah where the act is unintentional thus resolve to this standing dispute.

## Stone Aware/Forgot

### Stone Forgot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִכִּיר בָּהּ וּשְׁכֵחָהּ, וְעָמַד וְנָפְלָה; לְעִנְיַן נְזָקִין – חַיָּיב. לְעִנְיַן אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים – פָּטוּר. לְעִנְיַן גָּלוּת – חַיָּיב, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״בִּשְׁגָגָה״ – מִכְּלָל דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ יְדִיעָה, וְהָא הַוְיָא לֵיהּ יְדִיעָה. לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת – פָּטוּר. לְעִנְיַן עֶבֶד – פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** If **he was** initially **aware of it but forgot about it and he arose and it fell, with regard to damages** he is certainly **liable,** being that he is liable even if he was unaware of the stone. **With regard to the four types of indemnity,** here too he is **exempt,** as he did not intend to cause injury. **With regard to exile** he is **liable, as the verse states:** "One who **unwittingly** strikes a person mortally" (Numbers 35:11), indicating **by inference that** the assailant **had** some previous **awareness, and** in this case **he was** in fact previously **aware** of the stone in his lap. The term "unwittingly" is employed to describe someone who possessed knowledge of the potential transgression then forgot about it. **With regard to Shabbat** he is **exempt,** as this was not a planned, constructive labor. **With regard to a slave, the** same **dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis** applies.

The second case: the person *knew* the stone was on his chest and then *forgot* about it, after which he stood up and the stone fell. *Le-inyan nezikin chayav* — obviously still *chayav* for *nezek*, since strict liability applies a fortiori to negligent cases. *Le-inyan arba'ah devarim patur* — for the *arba'ah devarim*, he remains *patur*. Even though there was a degree of negligence — he knew about the stone and should have remembered — this does not rise to the level of *meizid* or *karov le-meizid* that Rashi requires. Forgetting is not sufficient. *Le-inyan galut*, however — if the stone killed someone — he is now *chayav*. The verse requires *bishgagah*, some form of negligent awareness, and here that element is present: there was an initial awareness of the dangerous object, and through negligence he forgot it. *Ve-havei lei yediah* — he had knowledge. *Le-inyan Shabbat patur* — for *Shabbat* he remains *patur*, because *Shabbat* requires *melechet machshevet*, and even though there was prior awareness of the stone, there was no intentionality to perform the act of transporting it from one domain to another. For the slave, the debate of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the *rabbanan* continues to apply as before.

## Throw 2/Land 4

### Two/Four Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְכַּוֵּין לִזְרוֹק שְׁתַּיִם, וְזָרַק אַרְבַּע; לְעִנְיַן נְזָקִין – חַיָּיב. לְעִנְיַן אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים – פָּטוּר. לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת – מְלֶאכֶת מַחְשֶׁבֶת בָּעֵינַן. לְעִנְיַן גָּלוּת – ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא צָדָה״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, פְּרָט לְנִתְכַּוֵּין לִזְרוֹק שְׁתַּיִם וְזָרַק אַרְבַּע. לְעִנְיַן עֶבֶד – פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where **he intended to throw** the stone, and he intended to throw it for a distance of only **two** cubits **but** instead he **threw** it a distance of **four** cubits, as it went farther than he wanted it to go, **with regard to damages** he is **liable. With regard to the four types of indemnity** he is **exempt. With regard to Shabbat** he is exempt, as **we require planned,** constructive **labor** as a condition for liability. **With regard to exile** he is liable, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"If a man lie not in wait"** (Exodus 21:13), which serves **to exclude** from the death penalty a situation where one **intended to throw** the stone for **two** cubits **but** he actually **threw** it for **four** cubits, as he did not intend to kill, so he is exiled. **With regard to a slave, the** same **dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis** applies.

The third case: the person intended to throw a stone two *amot* but it traveled four. There was a person standing four *amot* away who was struck and killed or injured. *Le-inyan nezikin chayav* — strict liability for *nezek* applies. *Le-inyan arba'ah devarim patur* — still *patur*, because even though there was some negligence in the throw, the possibility that a stone aimed at two *amot* might travel four does not constitute *meizid* or *karov le-meizid*. It is, in this respect, structurally similar to the forgotten-stone case.

*Le-inyan Shabbat* — carrying four *amot* in *reshut ha-rabim* is the *melachah* of *hotza'ah*, but since the intent was to throw only two *amot*, which is not a *melachah*, there was no *melechet machshevet*, and therefore he is *patur* for *Shabbat* as well. *Le-inyan galut*, the analysis depends on which reading of Rashi one follows. On one reading, *asher lo tzadah* — the requirement that one did not lie in wait — means the person was not trying to hit the victim, and since here he was not, he is nevertheless *chayav galut* as a negligent killer. On the second reading, which fits the *gemara*'s language more naturally, *asher lo tzadah* excludes a case where the act is too deliberately dangerous — the person was after all throwing a stone, and when one throws a projectile in a direction where a person stands, even if the intent was to land it short, the act is too close to an aggressive one to be treated as pure accident qualifying for the protection of *ir miklat*. On this reading, he is *patur* from *galut* precisely because there is too much intentionality. For the slave, the dispute of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the *rabbanan* again applies.

## Throw 4/Land 8

### Four/Eight Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְכַּוֵּין לִזְרוֹק אַרְבַּע, וְזָרַק שְׁמֹנֶה; לְעִנְיַן נְזָקִין – חַיָּיב. לְעִנְיַן אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים – פָּטוּר. לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת – בְּאוֹמֵר ״כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁתִּרְצֶה תָּנוּחַ״, אִין; אִי לָא, לָא. לְעִנְיַן גָּלוּת – ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא צָדָה״, פְּרָט לְנִתְכַּוֵּין לִזְרוֹק אַרְבַּע וְזָרַק שְׁמֹנֶה. לְעִנְיַן עֶבֶד – פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** And if **he intended to throw** the stone **four** cubits **but** instead he **threw** it **eight** cubits, **with regard to damages** he is **liable. With regard to the four types of indemnity** he is **exempt. With regard to Shabbat, if he said** to himself when he threw the stone that he would be satisfied **wherever it may land,** then **yes,** he is liable, as he intended to throw it a distance of four cubits, which is the minimum necessary to violate the prohibited labor of carrying in the public domain. **If** he did **not** throw the stone aimlessly but rather had selected a target that was four cubits away, then he is **not** liable as he did not perform the precise planned, constructive labor that he had intended. **With regard to exile,** the Torah states: **"If a man lie not in wait"** (Exodus 21:13), which serves **to exclude** from the death penalty a situation where one **intended to throw** it **four** cubits **but** he actually **threw** it **eight** cubits, as he did not intend to kill, so he is exiled. **With regard to a slave, the** same **dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis** applies.

The fourth case: the person intended to throw the stone four *amot* and it traveled eight. For all purposes involving personal injury and *arba'ah devarim*, the analysis is identical to the two-and-four case — even here, the fact that a person stood in the stone's path and was struck, when the thrower was aiming four *amot* and the stone went eight, is still not considered *meizid* or *karov le-meizid* for *arba'ah devarim*. This may seem surprising, since the victim was closer than the intended landing point, but since the thrower was aiming beyond him, the law does not treat it as sufficiently intentional.

The case differs from the previous one only with respect to *Shabbat*. Since throwing four *amot* in *reshut ha-rabim* is itself a *melachah*, the actor here *intended* to perform a *melachah* — unlike in the two-and-four case where the intended throw was sub-*melachah*. The question is then whether his intent to perform the *melachah* of throwing four *amot* is satisfied even though the stone went eight. The *gemara* rules: *be-omer kol makom she-tirtze tanuach* — if one declares indifference to where the stone lands, then one is *chayav* for *Shabbat*, since the *melechet machshevet* encompasses wherever the object comes to rest. But if one is insistent — *davka* — that the stone land at four *amot*, then even though one intended a *melachah*, since the *melachah* was accomplished at a different point than intended, one is *patur*. For *galut*, the same reasoning as in the two-and-four case applies: on the second reading of Rashi, one is *patur* because the act is too intentionally dangerous to be treated as accidental. For the slave, the dispute continues unchanged.

## Vessel Roof Case

### Already Broken

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה: זָרַק כְּלִי מֵרֹאשׁ הַגָּג, וּבָא אַחֵר וּשְׁבָרוֹ בְּמַקֵּל – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? מָנָא תְּבִירָא תָּבַר.
>
> **Translation:** § Since the Gemara cited Rabba's comments about various actions for which the perpetrator is liable with regard to certain matters but exempt with regard to others, the Gemara cites similar rulings: **And Rabba says:** If one **threw a vessel,** such as an earthenware jug, **from a roof and another came** along **and broke it with a stick** during its descent, the latter is **exempt** from liability. **What is the reason?** It is because **he broke a broken vessel,** meaning that once the vessel was thrown from the roof it was clear that it would be broken upon landing, and therefore it is considered as if it were already broken and the one who broke it while it was still in the air is not liable.

Rabbah introduces two further cases involving vessels thrown from a rooftop. In the first: one threw a vessel off a roof and before it struck the ground, another person came and broke it with a stick. The person who broke it with a stick is *patur*. Why? *Mana sevira tevar* — he broke something already broken. Once the vessel was thrown off the roof and gravity would inevitably destroy it, it lost its value and was already considered shattered. The person with the stick did not break a vessel worth anything. It follows — at least from Rashi's understanding of this case — that the one who threw the vessel off the roof bears liability, since when he threw it the destruction was set in motion and the vessel would certainly have broken regardless of any intervening cause.

### Pillows Grama

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה: זָרַק כְּלִי מֵרֹאשׁ הַגָּג, וְהָיוּ תַּחְתָּיו כָּרִים אוֹ כְסָתוֹת; בָּא אַחֵר וְסִלְּקָן, אוֹ קָדַם [הוּא] וְסִלְּקָן – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? בְּעִידָּנָא דְּשַׁדְיֵיהּ – פַּסּוֹקֵי מְפַסְּקִי גִּירֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba says:** If one **threw a vessel from a roof and there were cushions or blankets below** so that if the vessel would land on them it would not break, and then **another came and removed** the cushions or blankets, **or** if the individual who threw the vessel went quickly **before** it landed and **removed** the cushions or blankets himself, and as a result the vessel shattered, the one who threw the vessel is **exempt** from liability even though the vessel broke as a consequence of his actions. **What is the reason? At the time that he threw** the vessel, **his arrows were stopped,** i.e., what he did at the time he threw the vessel, which is an act comparable to the shooting of an arrow, did not have the capacity to break the vessel. Therefore, he is not viewed as having broken the vessel, and is exempt.

The second case: one threw a vessel off a roof and there were pillows below, so that at the moment of throwing the vessel was not going to break — it would have landed safely. A second party then removed the pillows, or alternatively the thrower himself came down and removed the pillows, causing the vessel to shatter. Both parties are *patur*. The thrower is *patur* because *be-idna de-shadyai* — at the moment he threw it, his act was completed and caused no damage; the vessel was going to land safely. The one who removed the pillows is *patur* because removing the pillows is *grama* — indirect causation — and *grama be-nezikin patur*. One who merely removes the protective cushion has not actively broken anything. Even when the same person does both acts — throws the vessel and then removes the pillows — he remains *patur* for each component: *patur* for the throw because it was harmless at the time, and *patur* for removing the pillows because that is *grama*. One can infer from the structure of these cases that had the person himself broken the falling vessel with a stick — without the interposition of another actor — he would have been *chayav*, since his initial throw set the destruction in motion.

## Infant Thrown

### Who Is Liable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה: זָרַק תִּינוֹק מֵרֹאשׁ הַגָּג, וּבָא אַחֵר וְקִבְּלוֹ בְּסַיִיף – פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא וְרַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא: הִכּוּהוּ עֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם בַּעֲשָׂרָה מַקְלוֹת – בֵּין בְּבַת אַחַת בֵּין בָּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, כּוּלָּן
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba says:** If one **threw a child from a roof and another came** along **and impaled him on his sword** and the child died, the question of who is liable to receive the death penalty for killing the child is dependent upon **the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira and the Rabbis. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **ten people beat** a victim **with ten sticks, whether** they did so **simultaneously or sequentially, they are all**

Rabbah concludes with the most horrifying case: *zarak tinok me-rosh ha-gag* — a person threw an infant off a roof and before the infant struck the ground, another came and speared it with a sword. It is a scenario of almost incomprehensible cruelty, yet the *gemara* addresses it as a question of *retzicha* — who, if anyone, bears criminal liability for the death? This case is the subject of a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira and the *rabbanan*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּטוּרִין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא אוֹמֵר: בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה – הָאַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקֵּירַב מִיתָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **exempt,** as it is impossible to sentence any one of them to death since no one person killed the victim and there is no death penalty for partially killing a person. **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says:** If they beat him **sequentially the last one** to beat him is **liable** for the death of the victim, **because he brought** the victim's **death closer.** So too, in this case, although the first one threw the child, the one to impale him on his sword was the one to hasten his death, and therefore according to Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira he would be liable to receive the death penalty.

The *beraita* discusses the related case of ten people who struck a victim with ten sticks — whether simultaneously or one after another — and the victim died. According to the *rabbanan*, all ten are *patur*, since when multiple fatal causes operate concurrently, one cannot assign liability to any single actor. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira holds that when the blows were delivered sequentially, *ha-acharon chayav mipnei she-keirev mitato* — the last one is *chayav* because he was the one who brought the death to completion. The *gemara* leaves us at the top of 27a with Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's principle applied to Rabbah's case: the one who threw the infant set a fatal process in motion; the one who speared it was the final, completing act. Whether the first actor, the second actor, or both bear liability for the death — and how Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira and the *rabbanan* would each resolve this — is where the daf concludes.
