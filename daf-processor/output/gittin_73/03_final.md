# Gittin 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Huna's Get

### Explicit Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ מֵהַיּוֹם, אִם מַתִּי מֵחוֹלִי זֶה
>
> **Translation:** **This is your bill of divorce from today if I die from this illness**

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion about a husband who gives his wife a *get* on the condition that he will die from his current illness. The central question is how this arrangement is framed: if the language implies that the *get* takes effect *after* death, it is invalid as a *get le'achar mita*; but if it is framed as a true *tnai* — a condition — it may be perfectly valid. We pick up on 73b, approximately ten lines from the bottom, beginning with the case of *zehu gittech mehayom im mati mecholi zeh* — "This is your *get* from today if I die from this illness." Here the husband has formulated a complete and explicit *tnai*: the *get* takes effect today, contingent upon his dying from the present illness. The question that will occupy the *Gemara* is when, precisely, that condition is considered met or definitively not met.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעָמַד וְהָלַךְ בַּשּׁוּק, וְחָלָה וָמֵת; אוֹמְדִים אוֹתוֹ – אִם מֵחֲמַת חוֹלִי הָרִאשׁוֹן מֵת, הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט; אִם לָאו, אֵינוֹ גֵּט
>
> **Translation:** and he recovered, **and he arose and walked in the market, but** then **became ill** again **and died,** the court **assesses him. If he died because of the first illness** then **this is** a valid **bill of divorce,** but **if not** then it is **not** a valid **bill of divorce.**

The *mishna* addresses the case where the husband gets up and walks about in the marketplace — *amad vehalach bashuk* — but then relapses and dies. The *mishna* does not automatically say the condition has been nullified merely because he managed to walk around. Instead, it calls for an *omdana*, a medical-style assessment: if it is determined that his death resulted from the original illness and that illness had never truly left him, the *get* is valid; if the relapse represents an entirely new illness, it is not.

### Get vs. Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: גִּיטּוֹ כְּמַתְּנָתוֹ; מָה מַתְּנָתוֹ, אִם עָמַד – חוֹזֵר; אַף גִּיטּוֹ, אִם עָמַד – חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said:** With regard to a person on his deathbed, the *halakhot* of **his bill of divorce** are the same **as** the *halakhot* of **his gift.** The Sages instituted that when a person on his deathbed gives a gift, no formal act of acquisition is required. **Just as** with regard to **his gift, if he arose** and was cured from his illness the gift he granted while on his deathbed is **retracted,** as he gave it only based on the assumption that he was about to die, **so too,** with regard to **his bill of divorce, if he arose** and was cured from his illness, the bill of divorce is **retracted** and nullified, as he gave his wife her bill of divorce only because he thought he was about to die and he wanted to exempt her from a levirate bond.

At this point the *Gemara* introduces a statement of Rav Huna: *gito ke-matnaso* — the *get* of a *shechiv mera* (a person on his deathbed) is analogous to the gift of a *shechiv mera*, a *matnas shchiv mera*. Just as a *matnas shchiv mera* — property distributed verbally on the deathbed — is automatically revoked if the person recovers (*im amad chozer*), so too the *get* of a *shechiv mera* is revoked if he recovers. Rav Huna then extends the comparison in a second direction: just as the rule that writing a *get* (*kisvu*) in a context of mortal danger is interpreted to mean *kisvu u-tenu* — "write and give" — so too a verbal gift in that same context, even without a formal *kinyan*, is legally binding.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה גִּיטּוֹ – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא פָּרֵישׁ, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״כְּתוֹבוּ״ – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אָמַר ״תְּנוּ״; אַף מַתְּנָתוֹ, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״תְּנוּ״ – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא קְנוֹ מִינֵּיהּ
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna continues: **And just as** in the case of **his bill of divorce,** where, **even though he did not explicitly say** his full intention, **once he said: Write** the bill of divorce, his statement is interpreted to mean that the court should give the bill of divorce to his wife **even though he did not say: Give** the bill of divorce to my wife, which a healthy man would need to state, **so too,** this is the *halakha* with regard to **his gift. Once he said: Give** the gift, then **even though** the recipients **did not acquire** it **from him,** which finalizes a gift from a healthy person, because he is dying the *halakha* takes into account his intention without all the necessary legal requirements.

The second part of Rav Huna's statement — the *matnas shchiv mera* point — requires clarification. It is well known that a *matnas shchiv mera* does not require a *kinyan*; that is the entire premise of the institution. What, then, is Rav Huna's *chiddush*? Tosafot explains that Rav Huna is not simply restating the rule of *matnas shchiv mera*. Rather, he is extending it: the same contextual circumstances that lead us to interpret *kisvu* as *kisvu u-tenu* — whether one is on his deathbed, being led out to execution, or embarking on a distant journey — those same circumstances should qualify as the context for *matnas shchiv mera*. So if someone being taken out to be executed says "give my property to so-and-so," one might object that this person is not technically a *shchiv mera*. Rav Huna teaches that the operative principle is not the precise category of *shchiv mera* but the set of circumstances in which we read an implicit urgency of death into the person's words, and those circumstances equally validate a verbal gift as a *matnas shchiv mera*. That is the *chiddush* of the second part of Rav Huna's statement, important to clarify even though our primary focus here is the first part.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

The first part of Rav Huna's statement — *gito ke-matnaso, im amad chozer* — is the subject of a significant dispute between Rashi and Tosafot, and this dispute will color everything that follows in the *Gemara*.

### Rashi's View

Rashi reads Rav Huna's statement as introducing a sweeping *chiddush*: just as a *shchiv mera* who distributes property implicitly means "in case I die," so too a *shchiv mera* who gives a *get* without articulating any condition at all has nonetheless made an implicit *tnai* — it is as if he said *im yamut mecholi zeh*, "if I die from this illness." The explicit conditions laid out in the *mishna*, on Rashi's reading, only come into play when the husband actually bothers to articulate something — and that is precisely when he can trip himself up by framing it incorrectly. But if he says nothing, the condition is implicit in the situation itself, and the *get* is treated as conditional upon his death. This, according to Rashi, is not a rabbinic *chiddush* in the technical sense — it is simply the husband's implicit *da'as*, assumed from the circumstances.

### Tosafot's View

Tosafot disagrees. There is no implicit *tnai*, and an explicit condition is always required. On Tosafot's reading, all Rav Huna is teaching is what constitutes a failure to satisfy the condition *mecholi zeh* once it has been explicitly made. Specifically, Rav Huna is saying: the moment the husband is able to get up, it is no longer *mecholi zeh*, regardless of what happens afterward. This, as Tosafot notes, stands in direct tension with the *mishna*, which says that even after the husband gets up and walks around, a relapse and death may still be *mecholi zeh* depending on an assessment. The *Gemara* will press this tension directly.

## Recovery & Relapse

### Mishna Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: ״זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ מֵהַיּוֹם, אִם מַתִּי מֵחוֹלִי זֶה״, וְעָמַד וְהָלַךְ בַּשּׁוּק, וְחָלָה וָמֵת; אוֹמְדִים אוֹתוֹ – אִם מֵחֲמַת חוֹלִי הָרִאשׁוֹן מֵת, הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט; אִם לָאו, אֵינוֹ גֵּט. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ ״אִם עָמַד – חוֹזֵר״, לְמָה לִי אוּמְדָּנָא? הֲרֵי עָמַד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **We learned** in the mishna: If a man said: **This is your bill of divorce from today if I die from this illness,** and he recovered, **and he arose and walked in the market, but** then **became ill** again **and died,** the court **assesses him. If he died because of the first illness** then **this is** a valid **bill of divorce,** but **if not** then it is **not** a valid **bill of divorce. And if you say** that **if he arose** and was cured of his illness the bill of divorce is **retracted,** then **why do I** need **assessment** at all? **He arose** from his sickbed, so the bill of divorce should automatically be nullified.

The *mishna* states clearly: if the husband got up, went out to the marketplace, fell ill again, and died, we conduct an assessment — *omdin oso* — and if we determine the death resulted from the original illness (*im machmas choli harishon meis harei zeh get*), it is a valid *get*; otherwise, it is not.

### Rav Huna Challenge

This *mishna* seems to contradict Rav Huna, at least on Tosafot's reading of his position. According to Rav Huna, once the husband gets up — *amad* — it is definitively no longer *mecholi zeh*. Why, then, does the *mishna* entertain the possibility of an *omdana*? If *amad* constitutes a clean break, there would be nothing to assess.

### Mecholi L'Choli

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: שֶׁנִּיתַּק מֵחוֹלִי לְחוֹלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar, son of Rav Yosef, says in the name of Rava:** This is referring to a case **where,** instead of recovering completely, **he proceeded from** one **illness** immediately **to** another **illness,** and the assessment is to ascertain whether he died from the first illness or from the second one.

The *Gemara* resolves this through the answer of Mar son of Rav Yosef in the name of Rava: *shenitak mecholi l'choli* — the *mishna* is speaking of a case where the husband transitioned from one illness to another without achieving a full recovery. He did not walk around in perfect health; he was still contending with some underlying ailment — perhaps pneumonia gave way to an infection — and in that state he made his way outside. In that continuous chain of illness, it remains possible to argue that his ultimate death was still *machmas choli harishon*, still attributable to the original illness. Rav Huna's ruling, by contrast, applies when there was a genuine period of complete freedom from illness — a true *amad* — at which point anything that follows is, by definition, something new.

### Amad Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״עָמַד״ קָתָנֵי! עָמַד מֵחוֹלִי זֶה, וְנָפַל לְחוֹלִי אַחֵר. וְהָא ״הָלַךְ בַּשּׁוּק״ קָתָנֵי! הָלַךְ עַל מִשְׁעַנְתּוֹ;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But isn't it taught** in the mishna: **He arose** from his illness, which indicates that he was completely cured? The Gemara answers: No, it means that he **arose from this illness but fell into another illness.** The Gemara challenges: **But isn't it** also **taught** in the mishna: **He walked in the market?** The Gemara answers: This means that **he walked with his staff** for support, meaning that he was not fully recovered from his illness but was able to walk only with assistance.

The *Gemara* presses further. The *mishna* says he *amad vehalach bashuk* — he stood up and walked in the marketplace — which sounds like complete recovery. How can we reinterpret *amad* to mean he was still sick? The *Gemara* responds: *halach al mishanto* — he walked with a cane. He was not in full health; he was ambulatory but still dependent on support.

### Mishna Chiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּהָלַךְ עַל מִשְׁעַנְתּוֹ – הוּא דְּבָעֵינַן אוּמְדָּנָא, אִידַּךְ – אוּמְדָּנָא נָמֵי לָא בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And this teaches us that we require assessment** only in a case **where he walked with his staff.** But in **another** case, where he did not arise from his illness and walk but immediately became ill again, **we do not even require assessment,** as it is clear that his death from the second illness was a result of the first illness.

This raises an obvious question: if the *mishna* is describing someone who was plainly still unwell, walking on a cane from one illness to another, what is its *chiddush*? One might have assumed that such a person is obviously still in the grip of the original illness, and his subsequent relapse and death would clearly be *mecholi zeh* without any need for assessment. The *Gemara* answers: *de'halach al mishanto hu de'ba'inan omdana* — precisely because he was walking around on his cane, because there had been some visible improvement, we cannot simply assume the subsequent death was from the original illness; we must assess. The *chiddush* is that even a partial, cane-assisted recovery introduces enough ambiguity to require an *omdana*. If he had never even reached that level of improvement — never managed to walk even with support — no assessment would be needed; it would unambiguously be *mecholi zeh*. Conversely, had he achieved full recovery for a period, no *omdana* would help: it would definitively not be *mecholi zeh* according to Rav Huna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ, שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁנִּיתַּק מֵחוֹלִי לְחוֹלִי – מַתְּנָתוֹ מַתָּנָה? אִין, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁנִּיתַּק מֵחוֹלִי לְחוֹלִי – מַתְּנָתוֹ מַתָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Can **you conclude from it** that in the case of **a person on his deathbed who proceeded from** one **illness** immediately **to** another **illness, his gift** is a valid **gift,** as he ultimately died as a result of the first illness? The Gemara answers: **Yes, as Rabbi Elazar says in the name of Rav:** In the case of **a person on his deathbed who proceeded from** one **illness** immediately **to** another **illness, his gift** is a valid **gift.**

The *Gemara* then draws an inference from this discussion regarding *matnas shchiv mera*: a *shchiv mera* who transitioned *mecholi l'choli* without a complete break — his gifts remain valid, because the entire chain of illness is considered one continuous state. This principle is confirmed by a tradition cited in the name of Rabbi Elazar from Rav: *shchiv mera shenitak mecholi l'choli matnato matana*.

## Implicit Condition Concern

### Rava & Rabba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרָבָא לָא סְבִירָא לְהוּ הָא דְּרַב הוּנָא, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ יֵשׁ גֵּט לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Rabba and Rava do not hold** in accordance with **this** *halakha* stated **by Rav Huna,** that if the husband was healed of his illness then the bill of divorce is nullified even if he did not specify such a condition. They hold that there is a rabbinic **decree** in place **lest** people **say** that **there** can be a valid **bill of divorce** given **after death.** Since people will see that in this case the bill of divorce took effect only once the husband died, in the future they may mistakenly consider a bill of divorce to be valid even though the husband explicitly made a condition that it would take effect only after his death.

Having worked through the *amad* dimension of Rav Huna's statement, the *Gemara* now confronts the second dimension — Rashi's reading of the *chiddush*, namely that the *tnai* can be implicit. Rabba and Rava reject Rav Huna's position entirely: *lo sevira lehu ha de-Rav Huna*. They do not accept that getting up constitutes an automatic break, nor — more fundamentally on Rashi's reading — do they accept that there can be an implicit *tnai* in the giving of a *get*.

### Get After Death Fear

The *Gemara* provides a reason beyond a simple factual disagreement: *gezeira shema yomru yesh get le'achar mita* — we fear that people will conclude that a *get* can take effect after death. This requires unpacking, because it is not immediately obvious how the implicit-*tnai* mechanism would produce that misunderstanding.

### Rashi Explanation

Rashi's explanation is illuminating precisely because his reading of Rav Huna is what makes the concern intelligible. Recall that on Rashi's reading, Rav Huna held that when a husband gives a *get* on his deathbed without stating any condition, it is treated as if he made a condition that it takes effect only if he dies. Now consider what an outside observer sees: in scenario one, the husband gives the *get*, dies, and the *get* is valid. In scenario two, the husband gives the *get*, recovers, and the *get* is not valid. Since no explicit *tnai* was ever articulated, the observer has no reason to attribute the difference to a conditional structure. What the observer is likely to conclude instead is that the husband's intention was for the *get* to take effect *after* his death — which would make it a *get le'achar mita*, invalid for a different and more fundamental reason. People will say: "Ah, so a *get* given by a *shechiv mera* takes effect upon death." That is the concern: the implicit *tnai* framework, precisely because it is invisible, will be consistently misread as a timing mechanism rather than a conditional one.

### Can Rabbis Invalidate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי, דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא לָא הָוֵי גִּיטָּא, וּמִשּׁוּם גְּזֵירָה שָׁרֵינַן אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ לְעָלְמָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is there anything that by Torah law is not** a valid **bill of divorce, but due to a** rabbinic **decree we permit a married woman to** marry **anyone,** even though by Torah law she remains married to her husband? Both Rabba and Rava agree that by Torah law the bill of divorce is nullified once the husband is healed from his illness, yet they treat the bill of divorce as valid. How can this be?

This leads to a striking challenge from the *Gemara* itself. If it is true that *de'oraita* there is an implicit *tnai* here — if the husband's real *da'as* really is "I mean this only if I die" — then how can we simply override that *tnai* because of a rabbinic concern about public misunderstanding? The scenario is this: the husband gives a *get* with an implicit *tnai*; he recovers; *de'oraita*, she is still married to him. But Rabba and Rava, concerned about how it will look, want to say we ignore the implicit *tnai* and treat the *get* as unconditional. That would mean declaring a woman divorced — *mesharei eshet ish l'alma* — when *de'oraita* she is still married. How can a rabbinic *gezeira* accomplish that?

### Kiddushin Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, כֹּל דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ אַדַּעְתָּא דְּרַבָּנַן מְקַדֵּשׁ, וְאַפְקְעִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן לְקִדּוּשִׁין מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** the Sages have the ability to nullify even a marriage that took effect by Torah law, because **anyone who betroths** a woman **betroths** her contingent **upon the will of the Sages, and** when one fails to conform to their will in matters of marriage and divorce **the Sages expropriated his betrothal from him** retroactively. Consequently, it is permitted for the woman to remarry.

The *Gemara* answers with the standard principle: *kol de-mekadesh ada'ata de-rabbanan mekadesh, ve-afkinu rabbanan le-kiddushin minei* — whoever betroths a woman does so with the implicit understanding and agreement of the Rabbis, and therefore the Rabbis have the power to annul the marriage retroactively. The reasoning is that since the marriage itself was conducted within the rabbinic framework, the Rabbis retain the authority to declare the marriage nullified when circumstances warrant.

### Ravina vs. Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: תִּינַח דְּקַדֵּישׁ בְּכַסְפָּא, קַדֵּישׁ בְּבִיאָה מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁוְּיוּהּ רַבָּנַן לִבְעִילָתוֹ בְּעִילַת זְנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** This **works out well** in a case **where he betrothed** his wife **with money,** as it is possible to say that the Sages expropriated the money used for the betrothal from the possession of its owner, resulting in a retroactive cancellation of the betrothal. But if **he betrothed** her **by means of sexual intercourse** then **what is there to say?** Rav Ashi **said to him: The Sages declared his sexual intercourse** to be **licentious sexual intercourse,** which does not create a bond of betrothal.

Ravina raises a qualification to Ravina: this solution works if the *kiddushin* was accomplished through money — *kiddushei kessef* — because then the Rabbis can invoke *hefker beis din hefker* and retroactively declare the money was never legally his, thereby annulling the transaction. But what if the *kiddushin* was accomplished through an act of *bi'ah* — sexual relations? In that case, how can the Rabbis annul the marriage? Rav Ashi responds: *deshavyei le-vi'ato bi'at zenut* — the Rabbis have the power to redefine the character of the act. They declare that his act of *bi'ah* was not a *ma'aseh kiddushin* but simply an act outside of marriage, *bi'at zenut*, and thus no marriage was created in the first place.

The Ramban, notably, suggests that this is not meant as a description of what actually happens in practice. The Ramban's position is that *afkinu rabbanan le-kiddushin minei* is really a coercive threat: by declaring that the Rabbis *could* retroactively designate the man's entire marital life as *bi'at zenut*, the implicit pressure is brought to bear on the husband to conform his *da'as* to the result that the Rabbis require. In other words, since no rational person would want his years of marital life reclassified as prostitution, we can effectively say: he really did not want the implicit *tnai* to govern here; deep down he accepts the Rabbis' framework. This is, on the Ramban's reading, a more principled and less radical use of *hafka'as kiddushin* — what we are really doing is redefining the husband's *da'as*, not annihilating his marriage from thin air. Rabba and Rava's position, then, is coherent: we simply refuse to recognize the implicit *tnai*, we treat the *get* as an unconditional *get*, and because the Rabbis have the theoretical power to annul *kiddushin* in cases where a husband's conduct creates communal harm, that refusal is halachically legitimate.

## Ones Contradiction

### House Collapse Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ מֵהַיּוֹם, אִם מַתִּי מֵחוֹלִי זֶה״, וְנָפַל הַבַּיִת עָלָיו אוֹ הִכִּישׁוֹ נָחָשׁ – אֵינוֹ גֵּט. ״אִם לֹא אֶעֱמוֹד מֵחוֹלִי זֶה״, וְנָפַל עָלָיו בַּיִת אוֹ הִכִּישׁוֹ נָחָשׁ – הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 7:2): If a husband says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce from today if I die from this illness, and the house collapsed on him or a snake bit him,** then **it is not** a valid **bill of divorce.** But if he said: This is your bill of divorce **if I will not arise** healthy **from this illness, and the house collapsed on him or a snake bit him,** then **this is** a valid **bill of divorce.**

The *Gemara* now turns to a *braita* that introduces a new dimension of the problem. The case: the husband says *zeh gittech mehayom im mati mecholi zeh* — "this is your *get* from today if I die from this illness" — and then a house collapses on him (*venafal habayit alav*), or a snake bites him (*o hichisho nachash*). The *braita* rules: *eino get* — it is not a valid *get*, because he stipulated *mecholi zeh* and he did not die from the illness; the cause of death was something external.

### Recover Condition

The *braita* then presents a second case: the husband says *im lo amod mecholi zeh* — "if I do not recover from this illness" — and again a house collapses on him or a snake bites him. Here the *braita* rules: *harei zeh get* — it is a valid *get*, because he never recovered from the illness.

### Cases Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** in **the first clause** such that the bill of divorce is not valid **and what is different** in **the latter clause** that the bill of divorce is valid? In neither case did he die from the illness. The Gemara gives no answer to this question.

The *Gemara* is struck by this and asks: *mai shena reisha u-mai shena seifa* — what is the difference between the two cases? To us, reading the *braita* simply, the distinction seems clear: in the first case he said "if I die from this illness," and since he died from something else, the condition was not met; in the second case he said "if I do not recover," and indeed he did not recover, so the condition was met. Simple enough.

### During Illness Reading

But the *Gemara* does not read the *braita* that way. Rather than understanding *mecholi zeh* as "as a result of this illness," the *Gemara* reads both cases as saying "during this illness" — *mitoch choli zeh*. Under that reading, in both cases the husband died during the illness; in both cases the condition, so understood, has been technically met. The contradiction then becomes real: in the first case the *braita* says it is not a valid *get* even though he died during the illness, and in the second case it says it is valid even though the cause of death was external. The *Gemara* therefore sees the *braita* as internally contradictory, and the contradiction maps onto the question of *yesh ones b'gittin o ein ones b'gittin* — does a claim of *ones* (circumstances beyond one's control, acts of God) affect whether a condition in a *get* is treated as satisfied?

The *ones* framework in this context works as follows: a husband says "this *get* takes effect if I do not return within twelve months," and then on the last day he is running to return but a flooded river blocks his path. Technically the condition was met — he did not return. But *ones* is the argument that these were not the circumstances he had in mind; he intended a voluntary failure to return, not an involuntary one. If we accept *ones* claims in *gittin*, the condition is not met and the *get* is invalid despite having been technically satisfied.

In our *braita*, the first case — where the *braita* says *eino get* — implies that we accept the *ones* claim: the house collapsed, that was an act of God, and despite the technical satisfaction of the condition we say it was not the kind of death or non-recovery the husband had in mind. The second case — where the *braita* says *harei zeh get* — implies that we reject the *ones* claim: he didn't recover, and it doesn't matter why he didn't recover.

### Acts of God Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם: אֲכָלוֹ אֲרִי – אֵין לָנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They sent** a ruling to Babylonia **from there,** from Eretz Yisrael: If the husband said: This is your bill of divorce if I am not healed from this illness, and **a lion ate him,** then **we do not** need to be concerned about this bill of divorce, for it is certainly not valid.

*Shalchu mitam* — they sent from Eretz Yisrael: *achlu ari ein lanu* — "we do not entertain the case of being eaten by a lion." The response from Eretz Yisrael is that extraordinary acts of God — being devoured by a lion, having a house collapse, being bitten by a snake — do not satisfy the condition in the husband's formulation. Even if technically the condition *mecholi zeh* (read as "during this illness") was met, the husband never intended such cataclysmic, unprecedented circumstances. The *braita*'s second case (*im lo amod*) is therefore one where the *Gemara* of Eretz Yisrael presumably distinguishes between ordinary non-recovery and extraordinary acts of God.

The *Bavli*, however, does not find this resolution satisfying. The *braita* remains a genuine contradiction between *reisha* and *seifa*. Tosafot notes the difficulty of reconciling this *braita* with the earlier *Gemara* discussion of *ones*, and the *Bavli*'s conclusion is essentially that the *braita* is unresolved: one clause assumes *yesh ones b'gittin* and the other assumes *ein ones b'gittin*.

## Field Flooding Story

### Full Responsibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּזַבֵּין אַרְעָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ, קַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ כֹּל אוּנְסָא דְּמִתְיְלִיד.
>
> **Translation:** It is related: There was **a certain man who sold land to another,** and he **accepted upon himself** the responsibility for **any unavoidable accident that may happen** to the land.

The *Gemara* now moves to a legal narrative that illuminates the *ones* question in the domain of monetary law. Rav Papa and Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua purchased a large quantity of sesame seeds — a commercial transaction, not a household matter — by the bank of the Nahar Malka. They hired shippers to transport the goods and the shippers accepted full responsibility for any circumstance that might arise: *kibbelu aleihu kol onsa de-misyalad*.

### River Redirection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְסוֹף אַפִּיקוּ בַּהּ נַהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** In such a case he would reimburse the buyer for the damage. **In the end they diverted a river into it,** meaning the government decided to make a new canal through the land that he sold.

What happened? The Nahar Malka was dammed — its course was altered — and as a result the goods could not be delivered. Rav Papa and Rav Huna demanded that the shippers rent donkeys and transport the sesame seeds overland, arguing: you accepted responsibility for *kol onsa*, and this is exactly the kind of circumstance where you must make good.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבִינָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל שְׁפִי לֵיהּ, דְּהָא קַבֵּילְתְּ עֲלָךְ כׇּל אוּנְסָא דְּמִתְיְלִיד. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא לְרָבִינָא: אוּנְסָא דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The buyer **came before Ravina** to lodge a claim. Ravina **said to** the seller: **Go pacify him,** i.e., reimburse him, **as you accepted upon yourself** responsibility for **any unavoidable accident that might happen. Rav Aḥa bar Taḥalifa** disagreed and **said to Ravina: It is an uncommon, unavoidable accident,** and the condition of the sale should not apply in such a case.

In an earlier parallel case, someone sold a field and accepted responsibility for any mishap (*kol onsa de-misyalid*); subsequently, the course of a river was redirected and the field was flooded. The case came before Ravina, who ruled that the seller must compensate the buyer: *ha kableit alach kol onsa de-misyalid* — you said anything, go pay. The case then came before Rav Acha bar Tachlifa, who challenged Ravina: this is an *onsa de-lo shchiach* — a completely uncommon act of God; surely such an extraordinary event was never contemplated within the scope of "anything."

### Rav Acha Ruling

Rav Acha bar Tachlifa's position is that an *ones delo shchiach* — an act of God so unusual and unprecedented that it falls entirely outside normal expectation — does not fall within the scope of a general acceptance of *kol onsa*. Even when a person says "I accept responsibility for anything," there is an implicit boundary: we are talking about the kinds of things that normally go wrong, not unprecedented redirections of major rivers.

### Rava Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיגַּלְגַּל מִילְּתָא, וּמְטָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אוּנְסָא דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The matter was circulated,** as this ruling was never finalized, **and it came before Rava. He said to them: It is an uncommon, unavoidable accident** for the Malka River to be dammed, and the sailors did not accept responsibility for this case.

The case of the sesame seeds and the shippers came before Rava, who sided with the shippers: *onsa de-lo shchiach hu* — this was an extraordinary act of God, and they are not liable. Rava's ruling establishes that *ones delo shchiach* is categorically different from ordinary *ones*. Even a blanket acceptance of *kol onsa* does not encompass the truly unforeseeable.

### Rava's Criticism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: קָאקֵי חִיוָּרֵי מְשַׁלְּחִי גְּלִימֵי דְאִינָשֵׁי, אוּנְסָא דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The two Sages **came** to court **before Rava** and **he said to** them: **You white geese [*kakei ḥivarei*],** referring to their long, white beards, **who strip men of their cloaks.** You are acting unfairly with the sailors. **It is an uncommon, unavoidable accident** for the Malka River to be dammed, and the sailors did not accept responsibility for this case.

What makes this passage particularly striking is Rava's language when he rendered his ruling. He said to those who had ruled against the shippers: *kakei chivrei, meshatchei glimei de-inshei* — "white ducks who rip off people's shirts." As Rashi explains: *mafshitim bigdei anashim, klomar porskin aleihem din anas* — they are stripping people's garments, meaning they are issuing rulings that line their own pockets (or the pockets of those they favor) under the guise of halacha. Rava's rebuke is sharp: these rabbis are exploiting their authority to rule against honest businessmen on the basis of a contract clause that no rational person would have understood to include the unprecedented damming of a major river.

The broader principle that emerges is that *ones delo shchiach* does not create legal liability, and this holds even in the face of explicit contractual language accepting general responsibility. To be held responsible for an *ones delo shchiach*, one would need to say so explicitly — *kol onsa afilu onsa de-lo shchiach* — and even then the question of whether such extreme language is enforceable is a matter of halachic analysis.

## Woman's Status

### No Yichud Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא תִּתְיַיחֵד עִמּוֹ אֶלָּא בִּפְנֵי עֵדִים,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a woman's ill husband gave her a bill of divorce, and made a condition that it should take effect from today if he dies from his illness, then **she may be secluded with him only in the presence of two witnesses,** lest they end up engaging in sexual intercourse.

The *mishna* now addresses the practical situation of a wife during the period of uncertainty after a conditional *get* has been given: *lo tityached imo ela bifnei eidim* — she may not be secluded alone with her husband unless witnesses are present.

### New Kiddushin Risk

The reason is concern about *kiddushin*. The *get* was given on condition; it may retroactively take effect from that day if the husband dies. But if the couple continues living together as before, and the husband eventually does die and the *get* is retroactively validated, then she will have been living with him after the effective date of the *get*. Any intimacy during that period would be viewed as potentially constituting new *kiddushin* — not *get yashan*, since the *get* had already been given, but a new act of *kiddushin* through cohabitation.

### Witnesses Safeguard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ עַל פִּי עֶבֶד אֲפִילּוּ עַל פִּי שִׁפְחָה; חוּץ מִשִּׁפְחָתָהּ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלִּבָּהּ גַּס בָּהּ בְּשִׁפְחָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** This applies to being secluded in the presence of not only valid witnesses; it is permitted for her to be secluded with him **even in the presence of a slave** or **even in the presence of a maidservant, except for** the wife's personal **maidservant.** And it is prohibited for the wife to be secluded in the presence of the latter **because she is accustomed to her maidservant,** and there is concern that she will engage in sexual intercourse with her husband even though the maidservant is present.

If witnesses are present, the concern about *yichud* is eliminated: witnesses prevent intimacy, and therefore no new *kiddushin* can be inferred from their continued presence in the same space. The *mishna* further specifies that even a slave, a maidservant, or a non-household member qualifies as a witness sufficient to remove the concern — *afilu al pi eved afilu al pi shifcha* — with one exception: her own personal maidservant does not suffice, because the woman feels entirely uninhibited in the presence of her own servant and would not restrain herself on that account (*mipnei shelibah gas bah bishifchatah*).

### Woman's Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה הִיא בְּאוֹתָן הַיָּמִים? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ לְכׇל דְּבָרֶיהָ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת וְאֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the halakhic status of the wife **during these days** between when the bill of divorce was given but before the condition has been fulfilled with the death of the husband? **Rabbi Yehuda says:** She is **like a married woman with regard to all of her matters,** and she remains forbidden to other men. **Rabbi Yosei says:** It is uncertain whether **she is divorced or** whether **she is not divorced.**

The second question the *mishna* addresses is the status of the woman during the intervening period: *mah hi be'otan hayamim* — what is she during those days? Is she treated as a *safek migureshet* (possibly divorced) or as a definite *eshet ish*?

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda holds that she is treated *ke'eshet ish lachol devareha* — as a fully married woman for all purposes. This is the more stringent position, treating her as unambiguously married throughout the conditional period.

### Rashi Reinterpretation

Rashi, however, introduces a significant reinterpretation of the case under discussion here. Rashi understands the *Gemara* to be speaking not of the standard *im mati mecholi zeh* condition established in the earlier *mishna*, but of a husband who says the *get* should take effect *a minute before his death*. This formulation — analogous to the idea of doing *teshuva* the day before one dies, which means one must always do *teshuva* since one never knows which day is one's last — creates a distinctive analysis. In hindsight, on the actual day of death, the wife was definitively *migureshet* for the last minute of that day. But on every other day that preceded it, she was definitively an *eshet ish* — not a *safek*, but a *vadai eshet ish* — because the *get* had not yet taken effect. The peculiarity is the gap between foresight and hindsight: living through those days, any given day might turn out to be the last day, and therefore prospectively she is a *safek migureshet* on each day. But retrospectively, once the husband has died, every day except the last was clearly a period of full *ishus*. This has profound consequences for questions such as whether she would bring a *chatat* or an *asham* if she were to have relations with another man during this period — a question of whether we assess at the time of the act (*be-sha'at ma'aseh*) or retrospectively.

Rabbi Yose holds *megureshes ve-eina megureshes* — she is in a state of *safek*. Again, on Rashi's reinterpretation, this refers to the case of "a minute before death," and Rabbi Yose acknowledges the ambiguity of her status throughout the period.

## Sexuality & Witnesses

### Braita Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּתְיַיחֲדָה עִמּוֹ בַּאֲפֵילָה, אוֹ שֶׁיָּשְׁנָה עִמּוֹ תַּחַת מַרְגְּלוֹת הַמִּטָּה – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא נִתְעַסְּקוּ בְּדָבָר אַחֵר; וְחוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם זְנוּת וְאֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם קִדּוּשִׁין. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 7:4): If, after the giving of this bill of divorce witnesses **saw that she secluded herself with** her husband **in the dark, or that she slept with him under the foot of the bed, one is not concerned** that **perhaps they were engaged in another matter,** i.e., sexual intercourse. **And one is concerned due to** their action of **licentiousness but one is not concerned** that **due to** their actions they performed **a betrothal. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: One is also concerned** that **due to** their actions they performed **a betrothal.**

A *braita* elaborates on the specific circumstances under which we become concerned about new *kiddushin* during this conditional period. The case is: they were seen together in the dark (*nistayachada imo ba'afeila*), or she slept at the foot of his bed (*yashna imo tachas margelot ha-mita*).

### Zenut Concern

The *braita* states: *ein chosheshin shema nis'asku be-davar acher* — we are not concerned that they engaged in sexual relations in such circumstances. Yet it also states *chosheshin mishum zenut* — we are concerned about *zenut*, about sexual conduct outside the marriage framework. And simultaneously, *ein chosheshin mishum kiddushin* — we are not concerned that any such relations constituted new *kiddushin*. This formulation is genuinely puzzling, and the *Gemara* works through multiple readings.

### Witness Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ, הָכִי קָאָמַר: רָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה – חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם קִידּוּשִׁין. נָתַן לָהּ כְּסָפִים – חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם זְנוּת, דְּאָמְרִינַן: בְּאֶתְנַנָּהּ נָתַן לָהּ; וְאֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם קִידּוּשִׁין. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף בְּזוֹ חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the *baraita* **saying? Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: This is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** If **they saw that she engaged in sexual intercourse** with her husband, then **there one is concerned** that **due to** their actions there was **a betrothal** and perhaps through this act he intended to remarry her. If **he gave her money** immediately following the sexual intercourse **one is concerned due to licentiousness, wherein we say: He gave** this money **as hire for a prostitute, but one is not concerned** that **due to** their actions they performed **a betrothal. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: Even in this** case **one is concerned** that **due to** their actions they performed **a betrothal,** i.e., one is concerned that he gave her the money as betrothal.

The first reading, proposed by Rav Nachman citing Rabba bar Avuha, unpacks the *braita* as follows: *ra'uah she-niv'ala* — if witnesses actually observed them engaging in sexual relations, then *chosheshin mishum kiddushin*, we are concerned that this may have been a new act of *kiddushin*. In other words, direct witnessing of the sexual act is the threshold: below that threshold there is no concern at all.

### Money Reframing

Within that same reading: if after the observed sexual relations the husband gave her money, then *chosheshin mishum zenut* — we are concerned it was *zenut* (*de-amrinan esnan nasan lah*, we say he paid her as a fee), *ve-ein chosheshin mishum kiddushin* — and we are no longer concerned that it was *kiddushin*. The passage of money reframes the act: rather than reading it as a new betrothal, we read the money as a payment, which in turn leads us to read the sexual act as *zenut* rather than as *kiddushin*. Counterintuitive as it is — this is his own wife, after all — the logic is that if money changes hands after intimacy, we interpret the money as *esnan zenut*, and therefore the act cannot simultaneously be construed as *kiddushin*.

### Rabbi Yose

Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *af chosheshin mishum kiddushin* — even when money was given afterward, we are still concerned it may have been *kiddushin*. Perhaps the money was a gift; perhaps she needed cash for shopping. The mere presence of money does not suffice to reframe the act as *zenut* and thereby dissolve the *chashash kiddushin*.

### Abaye Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִידֵּי כְּסָפִים קָתָנֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye objects to this** understanding of the *baraita*, according to which the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, disagree about a case where he gave her money after they engaged in sexual intercourse: **Is anything** with regard to **money taught** in the *baraita*?

Abaye presses the textual point: *midi kaspah katani* — where does the *baraita* mention money at all? The reading of Rav Nachman that makes money the pivot of the *baraita* imports a concept nowhere explicit in the text. Abaye therefore proposes an alternative reading.

### Abaye Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: רָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה – חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם זְנוּת, וְאֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם קִידּוּשִׁין. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said: This is** what the *baraita* **is saying:** If **they saw that she engaged in sexual intercourse one is concerned due to licentiousness, but one is not concerned** that **due to** their actions there was **a betrothal. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: One is also concerned** that **due to** their actions there was **a betrothal.**

On Abaye's reading, the *baraita* is structured differently. The first opinion (*tanna kamma*) says: if witnesses saw them actually having sexual relations (*ra'uah she-niv'ala*), we are concerned about *zenut* but not about *kiddushin*. That is: we take seriously that something happened, but we interpret it as *zenut*, not as a new betrothal. The default interpretive frame, even for witnessed sex, is *zenut* rather than *kiddushin*. Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and says: *af chosheshin mishum kiddushin* — we are also concerned that witnessed sex may constitute new *kiddushin*. On this reading, the entire debate turns on whether observed sexual relations between the husband and his conditionally-divorced wife should be presumed *zenut* or *safek kiddushin*, and the money question is simply absent from the *braita* entirely.

### Rava Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: אִם כֵּן, מַאי ״אַף״? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף לֹא רָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה – חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava objects to this: If so, what** is the meaning of the expression used by Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda: There is **also** concern that due to their actions they performed a betrothal? If there is concern about betrothal there should be no concern with regard to licentiousness. **Rather, Rava said** that **this is** what the *baraita* **is saying: Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: Even** if **they did not see that she engaged in sexual intercourse, one is concerned** that **due to** their actions they performed **a betrothal.**

Rava offers a third reading, which produces the most expansive range of positions. On Rava's reading, the *tanna kamma* is saying: even if witnesses saw them having sexual relations, we are not concerned about *kiddushin* — it is *zenut*, period. And Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda, by saying *af chosheshin mishum kiddushin*, means: I disagree on two counts. First, if they were seen having sex, that is a *safek kiddushin*, not mere *zenut*. Second — and this is the force of *af*, "even" — even if they were not observed having sex at all, as long as they were together in *yichud*, we are concerned about *kiddushin*. On Rava's reading, Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda holds that *yichud* alone is sufficient to generate the concern of new *kiddushin*, without any direct observation of sexual relations.

### Spectrum Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַחֲלוֹקֶת – כְּשֶׁרָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה, אֲבָל לֹא רָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי?
>
> **Translation:** Based on this explanation of the *baraita*, **in accordance with whose** opinion **is that which Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says,** that **the dispute** between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel (81a) is relevant only **when they saw that she engaged in sexual intercourse** with her husband, but if **they did not see that she engaged in sexual intercourse** with him, **everyone agrees that she does not require a second bill of divorce from him?**

The *Gemara* then asks: *ke-man azla ha de-amar Rabba bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan* — according to which position does the statement of Rabbi Yochanan align? Rabbi Yochanan had ruled elsewhere that the debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding a husband who gives a *get* and then spends the night at the same inn as his wife applies only when witnesses observed them having sex; if no sex was observed, *divrei hakol ein tzricha heimenu get sheni* — universally, no second *get* is needed. On Abaye's reading, Rabbi Yochanan's statement aligns with *divrei hakol* — it fits both *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda. On Rava's reading, however, Rabbi Yochanan's statement fits only the *tanna kamma*, not Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda, since on Rava's reading Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda holds that *yichud* alone — without any observed sex — already generates a concern about *kiddushin*. The *Gemara's* conclusion is *ke-man de-lo ke-chad* — this statement of Rabbi Yochanan does not fit cleanly with any single position.

What emerges from the full discussion is a spectrum of positions regarding when living together during the conditional *get* period creates a *chashash kiddushin*. At one extreme is the position that witnessed sex reframes automatically as *zenut* and nothing is ever a concern. At the other extreme is the position of Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda as read by Rava: *yichud* alone, even without sex, creates a concern. Between these poles sits the question of what direct witnessing of sexual relations entails. The *Gemara* ends with this range of positions laid out, without a final adjudication — a rich and unsettled conclusion to the discussions of *Gittin* 73.
