# Rosh Hashanah 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lo Tehei Shemiah

### Lo Tehei Shemiah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין בִּלְבַד — יַעַמְדוּ שְׁנַיִם וְיָעִידוּ בִּפְנֵיהֶם, וְיֹאמְרוּ: ״מְקוּדָּשׁ מְקוּדָּשׁ״. רָאוּהוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה וְהֵן בֵּית דִּין — יַעַמְדוּ הַשְּׁנַיִם וְיוֹשִׁיבוּ מֵחֲבֵירֵיהֶם אֵצֶל הַיָּחִיד, וְיָעִידוּ בִּפְנֵיהֶם, וְיֹאמְרוּ: ״מְקוּדָּשׁ מְקוּדָּשׁ״, שֶׁאֵין הַיָּחִיד נֶאֱמָן עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **the court alone saw** the new moon, **two** members of the court **should stand and testify before the others, and** the court **should say: Sanctified, sanctified.** If **three** people **saw** the new moon, **and they are** themselves members of **a court** for this purpose, **two** of them **should stand and seat** two **of their colleagues next to the individual** who remains of the three, thereby forming a new court of three. The two standing judges **should** then **testify before** the three seated judges that they saw the new moon and the seated judges **say: Sanctified, sanctified.** This procedure is necessary **because an individual is not authorized** to declare the month sanctified **by himself.** Rather, a court of three is required.

The daf begins by wrapping up the issues of *kiddush hachodesh* and picking up at the bottom of 25b, approximately ten lines from the bottom. The *mishna* there assumes that even if the *beit din* saw the new moon themselves, they must still go through the formal process of testimony. The *gemara* challenges this assumption by invoking the principle of *lo tehei shemiah gedolah mereiyah* — hearing testimony should not be greater than seeing directly. If seeing the new moon is sufficient knowledge, why must members of the *beit din* break off and formally testify to the others?

### Nighttime Seeing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנֵא רָאוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל? אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל — אִיפַּרְסְמָא לַהּ וְלָא לִיעַבְּרוּהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְכֵיוָן דִּתְנָא לֵיהּ רָאוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל — נֶחְקְרוּ הָעֵדִים לְמָה לִי! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִי נָמֵי נֶחְקְרוּ הָעֵדִים וְלֹא הִסְפִּיקוּ לוֹמַר ״מְקוּדָּשׁ״ עַד שֶׁחָשֵׁיכָה — הֲרֵי זֶה מְעוּבָּר. וְכֵיוָן דִּתְנָא עַד שֶׁחָשֵׁיכָה הֲרֵי זֶה מְעוּבָּר — לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנְיַיהּ חֲקִירַת הָעֵדִים כְּלָל? אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: תֶּיהְוֵי חֲקִירַת עֵדִים כִּתְחִילַּת דִּין, וּ״מְקוּדָּשׁ מְקוּדָּשׁ״ כִּגְמַר דִּין, וּלְקַדְּשֵׁיהּ בְּלֵילְיָא, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַדִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, דִּתְנַן: דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת דָּנִין בַּיּוֹם וְגוֹמְרִין בַּלַּיְלָה — הָכָא נָמֵי מְקַדְּשִׁין בְּלֵילְיָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי חֹק לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא מִשְׁפָּט לֵאלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב״, אֵימַת הָוֵי ״חֹק״ — בִּגְמַר דִּין, וְקָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא ״מִשְׁפָּט״, מָה מִשְׁפָּט בַּיּוֹם — אַף הָכָא נָמֵי בַּיּוֹם. רָאוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין — יַעַמְדוּ שְׁנַיִם וְיָעִידוּ בִּפְנֵיהֶם. וְאַמַּאי? לֹא תְּהֵא שְׁמִיעָה גְּדוֹלָה מֵרְאִיָּיה! אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁרָאוּהוּ בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the mishna **to teach:** If **the court and all of** the **Jewish people saw** the new moon? Merely stating that the court saw the moon would have sufficed, since its sanctification depends on them. The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** for the mishna to teach that even in that case, the month is intercalated. As **it might enter your mind to say** that **since the court and all of** the **Jewish people saw** the new moon, **it was publicized** that it was the New Moon that day, **and let them** no longer **intercalate** the month. Therefore, the *tanna* of the mishna **teaches us** that even in the case where all the Jewish people saw the new moon, the New Moon must be declared by the court. The Gemara asks further: **But once** the mishna **states:** If **the court and all of** the **Jewish people saw** the new moon, **why do I** need it to say: And **the witnesses were interrogated?** Why are witnesses necessary if the new moon was already seen by the court? The Gemara answers that **this is what** the *tanna* **is saying: Alternatively,** if **the witnesses were interrogated, but** the court **had no time to say: Sanctified, before nightfall,** the previous month is **intercalated** and rendered a full month of thirty days. The Gemara raises another difficulty. **But once** the mishna **taught:** But the court did not manage to say: Sanctified, **before nightfall,** the previous month is rendered a full, thirty-day month, **why do I** need the mishna **to teach** about **the interrogation of the witnesses?** This *halakha* was already stated with regard to a case where the court itself saw the new moon. The Gemara explains: It **was necessary,** as it might **enter your mind to say: Let the interrogation of the witnesses be** regarded **as the beginning of the judicial** process, **and** let the declaration: **Sanctified, sanctified,** be regarded **as the conclusion of the judicial** process, **and let them sanctify** the month **at night,** because the process began during the day. This process would then be **just as it is in** cases of **monetary law, as we learned** in a mishna: In cases of **monetary law,** although they must be adjudicated during the day, the court may **judge** the majority of a case **during the day,** and **complete** the trial and issue the ruling **at night. Here too,** one might assume that the court may **sanctify** the month **at night,** as the process began during the day. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that the court may not do so. The Gemara raises another difficulty: **Why not say** that, **indeed,** the sanctification of the month should be treated like monetary cases? The Gemara answers: **The verse states** with regard to Rosh HaShana: **"For this is a statute for Israel, a judgment [*mishpat*] of the God of Jacob"** (Psalms 81:5). **When does** the sanctification of the month **become a statute? At the end of the judicial process, and the Merciful One calls it a judgment** as well, thereby teaching that **just as** the primary time of **a judgment is during the day, here too,** with regard to the sanctification of the New Moon, the process must take place **during the day,** and not at night. § The mishna continues: If **the court** alone **saw** the new moon, **two** members of the court **should stand and testify before the others.** The Gemara ponders: If the court saw the new moon, **why** is it necessary for two of its members to testify before the others? **Hearing** their testimony **should not be greater than** actually **seeing** the new moon. The Gemara responds that **Rabbi Zeira said:** The mishna is addressing a case **where** the court **saw** the new moon **at night.** Because they saw it at night, their testimony is inadmissible at that time, as testimonies are admissible only during the day. They must therefore wait until the following day and testify as any ordinary person would.

Rav Zeira resolves the difficulty: the case in the *mishna* is one where they saw the moon at night. Since the entire process of accepting witnesses — and indeed the pronouncement of *mekudash* — must take place during the day, their act of seeing at night does not carry the legal weight of a formal act of testimony. One might immediately object: even if their nighttime seeing cannot function as formal testimony, they still *know* what they saw. Why must they hear witnesses the following morning?

The *gemara*'s answer is precise and important. The principle of *lo tehei shemiah gedolah mereiyah* does not go so far as to say that mere *knowledge* eliminates the need for formal process. Rather, the principle holds that an act of *seeing* is equivalent in legal status to an act of *hearing testimony* — but just as hearing testimony must occur during the day, so too the act of seeing that would substitute for it must occur during the day. Since they saw it at night, that seeing cannot function as the formal equivalent of testimony, and a fresh formal process is required the next morning. The new moon's visibility at night makes this situation entirely natural: they see it after sunset, but cannot validate the month until the morning.

### Three Witnesses Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאוּהוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה וְהֵן בֵּית דִּין — יַעַמְדוּ הַשְּׁנַיִם וְיוֹשִׁיבוּ מֵחֲבֵירֵיהֶם אֵצֶל הַיָּחִיד. אַמַּאי? הָכָא נָמֵי נֵימָא: לֹא תְּהֵא שְׁמִיעָה גְּדוֹלָה מֵרְאִיָּיה! וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכָא נָמֵי — כְּגוֹן שֶׁרָאוּהוּ בַּלַּיְלָה הַיְינוּ הָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If **three** people **saw** the new moon, **and they are** themselves members of **a court** for this purpose, **two** of them **should stand and seat** two **of their colleagues next to the individual** who remains of the three. The Gemara asks: **Why** is this necessary? **Here too, let us say: Hearing** their testimony **should not be greater than** actually **seeing** the new moon. **And if you say** that **here too,** the mishna is addressing a case **where they saw** the new moon **at night, this** case **is** identical to **that** previous one, and there would be no need for two separate rulings.

The *mishna* then presents a second case: if only three judges saw the moon, and two cannot break off to testify because an insufficient number would remain on the *beit din*, two must step aside and be replaced by additional judges so that testimony can be formally accepted. The *gemara* asks the same question — why not invoke *lo tehei shemiah gedolah mereiyah* here as well? The *gemara* considers giving the same answer as before (they saw it at night), but then objects: *haynu hach* — that is identical to the first case and adds nothing new.

### Eid Naasah Dayan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, דְּאֵין הַיָּחִיד נֶאֱמָן עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ. דְּסָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּתְנַן דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, וְאִם הָיָה מוּמְחֶה לְרַבִּים דָּן אֲפִילּוּ בְּיָחִיד, הָכָא נָמֵי נִיקַדְּשֵׁיהּ בִּיחִידִי — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאֵימָא הָכָא נָמֵי? אֵין לְךָ מוּמְחֶה לְרַבִּים בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל יוֹתֵר מִמֹּשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: עַד דְּאִיכָּא אַהֲרֹן בַּהֲדָךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה וְאֶל אַהֲרֹן בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם לֵאמֹר. הַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה לָכֶם״. לְמֵימְרָא דְּעֵד נַעֲשֶׂה דַּיָּין? לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּתַנְיָא: סַנְהֶדְרִין שֶׁרָאוּ אֶחָד שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ — מִקְצָתָן נַעֲשׂוּ עֵדִים וּמִקְצָתָן נַעֲשׂוּ דַּיָּינִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּן נַעֲשִׂין עֵדִים, וְאֵין עֵד נַעֲשֶׂה דַּיָּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** to teach **the last clause,** which states: **Because an individual is not deemed credible** and authorized to declare the month sanctified **by himself. For** it might **enter your mind to say** that **since we learned** in a *baraita*: Cases of **monetary law** are adjudicated **by** a court of **three** judges, **but if** a person **was a publicly recognized expert, he may judge** monetary matters **even individually, then here too,** one judge **should** be authorized to **sanctify** the month **individually** if he is a recognized expert. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that this is not so, and that three judges are required for the sanctification of the month. The Gemara asks: **But why not say that here too,** a recognized expert can sanctify the month individually? The Gemara rejects this possibility: But certainly **there was no publicly recognized expert among** the **Jewish people greater than our teacher Moses,** and nevertheless **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him:** You may not sanctify the new month **until Aaron is with you, as it is written: "And the Lord spoke to Moses and Aaron in the land of Egypt, saying, this month shall be for you** the beginning of months" (Exodus 12:1–2), where the word "you" is in the plural form. And since, to avoid deadlock, a court cannot be composed of an even number of judges, another judge must be added. It is therefore apparent that three judges are required for the sanctification of the month by Torah law. The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that a witness becomes a judge,** i.e., that one who witnessed an event can himself serve as a judge concerning the matter? **Let us say that the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, for it was taught** in a *baraita*: If **the Sanhedrin saw someone kill** another **person,** **some of them are rendered witnesses** and testify before the others **and some of them become judges** in the case; **this is the statement of Rabbi Tarfon. Rabbi Akiva says: They are all rendered witnesses, and a witness cannot become a judge.** It seems therefore that the mishna contradicts Rabbi Akiva's opinion.

The *gemara* now raises the principle of *eid na'aseh dayan*. The *mishna* envisions a scenario where a member of the *beit din* might himself have been in a position to serve as a witness to the moon. The concern is not the case of *eid hameid na'aseh dayan* — where one testifies and then immediately turns to serve as the judge of one's own testimony — but rather the case of *eid sherah*: a *dayan* who personally saw the moon and is now listening to others testify. Does the fact that he could have been a witness disqualify him from serving as a judge?

The *gemara* raises the possibility that the *mishna* follows the view of the Rabbis and not that of Rabbi Akiva. As taught in a *baraita*: if a *sanhedrin* witnessed a murder, Rabbi Tarfon holds that some of them may serve as witnesses while others serve as judges. Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *kulam na'asu eidim ve-ein eid na'aseh dayan* — all of them are considered witnesses, and a witness cannot become a judge.

## Eid Naasah Dayan

### Rabbi Tarfon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הָתָם אֶלָּא בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״וְשָׁפְטוּ הָעֵדָה״ ״וְהִצִּילוּ הָעֵדָה״, וְכֵיוָן דְחַזְיוּהוּ דִּקְטַל נַפְשָׁא לָא מָצוּ חָזוּ לֵיהּ זְכוּתָא. אֲבָל הָכָא — אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מוֹדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **You** can **even say** that the mishna was taught in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** but we must distinguish between the cases. **Rabbi Akiva states** his position **there only with regard to** cases of **capital law, for the Merciful One says: "And the congregation shall judge…and the congregation shall save"** (Numbers 35:24–25), which requires a court to search for grounds to exonerate the defendant, **but once they** themselves **have seen him kill a person, they will be unable to find** grounds to **exonerate** him. **But here,** with regard to the sighting of the new moon, **even Rabbi Akiva concedes** that a witness can be rendered a judge.

Rabbi Tarfon's view, as stated in the *baraita*, is that when a *sanhedrin* has witnessed a capital crime, the group may divide its roles: some will testify while others, together with additional judges, will adjudicate. Seeing the event does not disqualify one from serving as a judge, provided one does not personally testify. The witnessing itself leaves room for a formal distinction between the role of *eid* and the role of *dayan*.

### Rabbi Akiva

Rabbi Akiva takes the more stringent position: *kulam na'asu eidim* — everyone who saw it is rendered a witness — and *ein eid na'aseh dayan*, a witness cannot subsequently serve as the judge in that very case. The act of witnessing the crime itself, not merely the act of testifying, is what triggers the disqualification.

### Capital Crimes Only

The *gemara* resolves that even according to Rabbi Akiva, the *mishna* regarding the new moon is unproblematic. Rabbi Akiva's rule applies exclusively to *dinei nefashot*, capital cases, where the Torah states *ve-shaftu ha-eda ve-hitzilu ha-eda* — the congregation shall judge and the congregation shall seek to save the accused. In capital cases, one who has seen the crime committed is so emotionally and cognitively invested that he cannot maintain the dispassionate distance needed to seek out arguments in the defendant's favor. The graphic reality of witnessing a killing registers in a way that forecloses the kind of impartial advocacy the Torah demands of judges in capital matters. For *kiddush hachodesh*, however, even Rabbi Akiva agrees that having seen the moon does not disqualify one from participating in the adjudicative process.

## Tosafot Classification

### Three Categories

Tosafot, in the *dibbur hamatchil* beginning with the word *uve-eid*, provides a crisp summary of three distinct legal contexts in which *eid na'aseh dayan* operates differently. In the case of testimony about the new moon, which is *de-oraita*, one who merely saw the moon — *eid haroeh* — may nonetheless serve as a judge; the seeing does not disqualify. In the case of *kiyum shetarot*, the validation of signatures on documents, which is Rabbinic in origin, even *eid hameid na'aseh dayan* — one who actually testified may turn around and serve as the adjudicating judge. And in *dinei nefashot*, capital cases, even *eid haroeh eino na'aseh dayan* — even one who merely saw the act, without ever testifying, is disqualified from serving as judge.

The result is a spectrum: at one extreme, *kiyum shetarot* is the most permissive (even the testifying witness may judge); in the middle, *kiddush hachodesh* permits the one who saw to judge; and at the other extreme, *dinei nefashot* disqualifies even the one who only saw but never testified. This leaves open, as a *she'elah*, where ordinary *dinei mamonot* and cases involving lashes — which are neither capital nor merely monetary — would fall along this spectrum.

## Transition to Shofar

### All Except Cow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַשּׁוֹפָרוֹת כְּשֵׁרִים, חוּץ מִשֶּׁל פָּרָה — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא קֶרֶן. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: וַהֲלֹא כׇּל הַשּׁוֹפָרוֹת נִקְרְאוּ קֶרֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בִּמְשׁוֹךְ בְּקֶרֶן הַיּוֹבֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna begins to discuss the primary mitzva of Rosh HaShana, sounding the *shofar*. **All *shofarot* are fit** for blowing **except for** the horn **of a cow, because it is a horn [*keren*]** and not a *shofar*. **Rabbi Yosei said: But aren't all *shofarot* called horn, as it is stated:** "And it shall come to pass, **that when they sound a long blast with the horn [*keren*] of a ram [*yovel*]"** (Joshua 6:5), and a ram's horn is a *shofar* fit for sounding on Rosh HaShana?

With the issues of *kiddush hachodesh* concluded, the *masechet* now transitions to its *shofar* section. The *mishna* states: *kol hashofarot ksheirim* — all horns are valid for use as a *shofar* — *chutz mishel parah*, except for a cow's horn — *mipnei shehu keren*, because it is a *keren*. This formulation immediately invites scrutiny: what precisely is the significance of calling it a *keren*, and why would that disqualify it?

### Keren Definition

Rabbi Yosi challenges the reasoning directly. He argues that one cannot exclude a cow's horn on the grounds that it is called a *keren*, because all *shofarot* are referred to as *keren* as well. His proof is from the verse in Yehoshua concerning the fall of the walls of Yericho: *bimshoch bkeren hayovel heimah ya'alu bahar* — when the *keren hayovel* is drawn out, they shall go up the mountain — and the verse continues, *b'shom'achem et kol hashofar*. Since a *yovel* — which as the *gemara* will establish means a ram — has its horn called both *keren* and *shofar*, the terminology of *keren* alone cannot be the basis for distinguishing a cow's horn.

## Cow Horn Debate

### Rabbi Yosi Proof

Rabbi Yosi's challenge stands. Even a ram's horn — which is unambiguously valid — is referred to in Scripture as a *keren*. If the *mishna*'s reason for invalidating a cow's horn were simply that it is called a *keren*, that reason would equally invalidate every *shofar*, since rams' horns are likewise called *keren*. The objection therefore demands a stronger account of the distinction.

### Rabbis Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבָּנַן: כׇּל הַשּׁוֹפָרוֹת אִקְּרוֹ שׁוֹפָר וְאִקְּרוֹ קֶרֶן. דְּפָרָה — קֶרֶן אִקְּרִי, שׁוֹפָר לָא אִקְּרִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּכוֹר שׁוֹרוֹ הָדָר לוֹ וְקַרְנֵי רְאֵם קַרְנָיו״. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמַר לָךְ: דְּפָרָה נָמֵי אִקְּרִי שׁוֹפָר, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְתִיטַב לַה׳ מִשּׁוֹר פָּר״, אִם שׁוֹר — לָמָּה פָּר, וְאִם פָּר — לָמָּה שׁוֹר? אֶלָּא: מַאי ״שׁוֹר פָּר״ — מִשּׁוֹפָר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Yosei is saying well,** i.e., presents a convincing argument. Why do the Rabbis not accept it? The Gemara explains that **the Rabbis** counter Rabbi Yosei's argument as follows: Indeed, **all** other ***shofarot* are called *shofar* and** they are also **called *keren*,** but that of **a cow is called *keren*,** but **it is not called *shofar*, as it is written: "His firstborn bull, grandeur is his, and his horns [*karnav*] are the horns of [*karnei*] a wild ox"** (Deuteronomy 33:17). It is therefore clear that the horns of a bull are called *keren*, and nowhere are they called *shofar*. **And** how would **Rabbi Yosei** counter this argument? **He** could have **said to you:** The horns **of a cow are also called *shofar*, as it is written: "And it shall please the Lord better than an ox bull [*shor par*]** that has horns and hoofs" (Psalms 69:32). The wording of the verse is strange: **If** it is **an ox [*shor*], why** is it also called **a bull [*par*], and if** it is **a bull [*par*], why** is it called **an ox [*shor*]? Rather, what is** the meaning of ***shor par*?** These two words must be read as if they were one single word: **Than a *shofar*.** According to this reading, even the horn of a cow is called a *shofar*.

The Rabbis respond by refining the claim. All other horns, they argue, are called both *shofar* and *keren* — *kol hashofarot ikru keren ve-ikru shofar*. The term *shofar* applies to rams' horns, as derived from the *pasuk* at Har Sinai: *bimshoch hayovel heimah ya'alu bahar*, followed by *vayehi kol hashofar holech vechazek me'od* — there the *yovel* (ram's horn) is explicitly called a *shofar*. A cow's horn, by contrast, is only ever called a *keren*, never a *shofar*. The proof is the verse: *bechor shoro hadar lo ve-karnei re'eim karnav* — horn language is associated with the bovine, while the word *shofar* never appears in that context.

### Shor Par

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? כִּדְרַב מַתְנָה. דְּאָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: מַאי ״שׁוֹר פָּר״ — שֶׁהוּא גָּדוֹל כְּפַר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how do **the Rabbis** explain the difficulty in this verse? They explain it **as** it was explained by **Rav Mattana, as Rav Mattana said: What is** the meaning of ***shor par*? An ox [*shor*] that is as large as a bull [*par*],** as the animal is called a *shor* from birth, but is called a *par* only from the age of three.

Rabbi Yosei counters that a cow's horn *is* called a *shofar*, based on the verse *ve-titev la'Hashem mishor par* — my offering will be more pleasing to God than a *shor par*. The *gemara* asks: if it is a *shor*, why say *par*, and if it is a *par*, why say *shor*? The answer: *shor par* is a contraction for *mi-shofar* — from the *shofar*. The double language signals that the animal's horn is itself called a *shofar*.

The Rabbis respond to this by invoking Rav Masna's interpretation: the verse refers to an animal that was a *shor* — that is, a one-day-old — yet was as large as a *par* (a fully grown animal), since at the time of creation animals were formed in their fully developed state. The word *par* thus describes the size, not the species, and the verse does not establish a general term *shofar* for a cow's horn.

### Golden Calf Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן, כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא. דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל נִכְנָס בְּבִגְדֵי זָהָב לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים לַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה — לְפִי שֶׁאֵין קָטֵיגוֹר נַעֲשָׂה סָנֵיגוֹר. וְלָא? וְהָא אִיכָּא דָּם פַּר! הוֹאִיל וְאִשְׁתַּנִּי — אִשְׁתַּנִּי. וְהָא אִיכָּא אָרוֹן וְכַפּוֹרֶת וּכְרוּב! חוֹטֵא בַּל יַקְרִיב קָאָמְרִינַן. וְהָא אִיכָּא כַּף וּמַחְתָּה! חוֹטֵא בַּל יִתְנָאֶה קָא אָמְרִינַן. וְהָא אִיכָּא בִּגְדֵי זָהָב מִבַּחוּץ! מִבִּפְנִים קָא אָמְרִינַן. שׁוֹפָר נָמֵי מִבַּחוּץ הוּא! כֵּיוָן דִּלְזִכָּרוֹן הוּא — כְּבִפְנִים דָּמֵי. וְהָא תַּנָּא ״מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא קֶרֶן״ קָאָמַר! חֲדָא וְעוֹד קָאָמַר: חֲדָא — דְּאֵין קָטֵיגוֹר נַעֲשָׂה סָנֵיגוֹר, וְעוֹד — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא קֶרֶן. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי? אָמַר לָךְ: דְּקָא אָמְרַתְּ אֵין קָטֵיגוֹר נַעֲשָׂה סָנֵיגוֹר — הָנֵי מִילֵּי מִבִּפְנִים, וְהַאי שׁוֹפָר מִבַּחוּץ הוּא. וּדְקָא אָמְרַתְּ מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא קֶרֶן — כׇּל הַשּׁוֹפָרוֹת נָמֵי אִקְּרוֹ קֶרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: This is the reasoning of the Rabbis,** who say that the horn of a cow is unfit for sounding on Rosh HaShana. They say this **in accordance with the opinion of Rav Ḥisda, as Rav Ḥisda said: For what reason does the High Priest not enter the innermost sanctum,** the Holy of Holies, **with** his **golden garments to perform the service** there on Yom Kippur? It is **because a prosecutor [*kateigor*] cannot become an advocate [*sanneigor*].** Since the Jewish people committed the sin of worshipping the Golden Calf, the High Priest may not enter the Holy of Holies to atone for the Jewish people wearing golden garments, as they would bring that sin to mind. The Gemara asks: **But** do we **not** use a cow in the Holy of Holies? **But there is the blood of the bull** that is brought there to be sprinkled on Yom Kippur, despite the fact that the Jewish people sinned with a calf. The Gemara answers: **Since it has changed,** i.e., it is not the bull itself, but only its blood, then **it has changed,** i.e., so it does not bring the sin of the Golden Calf to mind. The Gemara raises another difficulty: **But there is the Ark, the Ark cover, and the cherub,** all of which are plated with gold. If this is problematic, why are they in the Holy of Holies? The Gemara explains: **What we are saying is** that **a sinner** seeking atonement **should not bring** something made of gold **into** the Holy of Holies, as it would bring the sin of the Golden Calf to mind. The Gemara asks further: **But there is the spoon and coal pan** that are brought into the Holy of Holies, and they are made of gold. The Gemara answers: **What we are saying is** that **a sinner** seeking atonement **should not adorn** himself with ornaments of gold. The Gemara raises yet another question: **But there are the golden garments** the High Priest wears **outside** the Holy of Holies. The Gemara answers: **What we are saying is** that a sinner should not adorn himself with gold **inside** the Holy of Holies, but outside there is no concern. The Gemara continues this line of questioning: If so, **the *shofar* is also outside,** since it is not brought into the Holy of Holies. The Gemara answers: **Since** the *shofar* **is** sounded in order to evoke God's **remembrance, it is considered as if** it were sounded **inside** the Holy of Holies. The Gemara asks: Is this indeed the reason that the Rabbis disqualify the use of a cow's horn? **But** the *tanna* **taught:** The horn of a cow cannot be used **because it is a *keren*.** The Gemara answers: The *tanna* **stated one** reason **and** also **another** reason: **One** reason is **that a prosecutor cannot become an advocate, and another** is **because it is** called **a *keren*** and not a *shofar*. The Gemara asks: **And** how would **Rabbi Yosei** counter these arguments? The Gemara explains: **He** could have **said to you: That which you said,** that **a prosecutor cannot become an advocate,** is indeed true, but **this applies** only **inside** the Holy of Holies, **and the *shofar* is** sounded **outside. And** with regard to **that which you said, because it is a *keren*, all *shofarot* are also called *keren*.**

Ulla proposes what he presents as the real reason underlying the Rabbinic position: *hainu ta'ama de-Rabbanan ke-Rav Chisda*. Rav Chisda had asked why the *kohen gadol* does not enter the inner sanctum on Yom Kippur wearing his golden vestments when performing the *avodat pnim*. The answer: *ein kategor na'aseh sanegore* — the prosecutor cannot become the defense attorney. Gold evokes the *chet ha-eigel*, the sin of the Golden Calf, and therefore cannot be the instrument through which the *kohen gadol* approaches God in the innermost sanctum on the day of atonement. Similarly, a cow's horn evokes the *eigel* and cannot serve as the instrument through which Israel approaches God on Rosh Hashanah.

The *gemara* then subjects this parallel to sustained scrutiny. Does not the blood of the ox (*dam par*) enter the *kodesh kodashim* on Yom Kippur? The *gemara* answers: blood no longer appears as a cow — *ishtani ishtani*, it has been transformed. What about the *aron*, the *kapores*, and the *keruvim*, all of which are gold and reside permanently inside the inner sanctum? The *gemara* distinguishes: the principle of *ein kategor na'aseh sanegore* applies to what the *kohen gadol* himself brings in and uses — *chotei bal yakriv* — the sinner may not approach using objects that evoke the sin. The permanent furnishings of the sanctuary are not being actively wielded by the approaching worshipper. What about the *kaf* and *machtah*, the fire pan and incense ladle, which are gold and which the *kohen gadol* does carry in? The *gemara* answers with the principle of *chotei bal yisna'eh* — the sinner may not adorn or beautify himself with things that evoke his sin. Clothing, which integrates with one's identity and presents one's person, is categorically different from the vessels one happens to carry.

The *gemara* then presses: the *kohen gadol* does wear gold vestments outside the inner sanctum for the other services on Yom Kippur — why is that permitted? The answer: *mi-pnim* — only within the innermost sanctum is the prohibition operative.

This leads to the sharpest objection: the *shofar* is blown outside, not within the *kodesh kodashim* — *shofar mi-bachutz hu*. So why should the *kategor-sanegore* logic apply? The *gemara* responds with a striking statement: *keivan de-le-zikaron hu ke-pnim dami* — since the *shofar* functions to raise Israel's memory before God, it is as if it penetrates directly into the innermost sanctum. The *shofar* pierces through to God's presence, so its use is treated as though it were an act performed *lifnai ve-lifnim*.

Yet the *gemara* simultaneously notes that the *mishna* stated the reason for excluding the cow's horn as *mipnei shehu keren* — which sounds like a terminology-based argument, not the *kategor-sanegore* reasoning. The *gemara* resolves this: *chada ve-od ka'amar* — the *mishna* is making two points, not one. The primary reason is *ein kategor na'aseh sanegore*; the secondary, additional point is *mipnei shehu keren*. Rabbi Yosi, for his part, maintains that neither argument succeeds: the *kategor-sanegore* reasoning applies only within the *kodesh kodashim*, and the *keren* terminology applies equally to all *shofarot*.

### Multiple Segments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אֲמַר, הַיְינוּ טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן: שׁוֹפָר אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה שׁוֹפָרוֹת. וְהָא דְּפָרָה, כֵּיוָן דְּקָאֵי גִּילְדֵי גִּילְדֵי — מִיתְחֲזֵי כִּשְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה שׁוֹפָרוֹת. וְהָא תַּנָּא ״מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא קֶרֶן״ קָאָמַר! חֲדָא וְעוֹד קָאָמַר: חֲדָא — דְּשׁוֹפָר אֶחָד אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה שׁוֹפָרוֹת. וְעוֹד — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא קֶרֶן. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמַר לָךְ: דְּקָאָמְרַתְּ ״שׁוֹפָר אֶחָד אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה שׁוֹפָרוֹת״ — כֵּיוָן דִּמְחַבְּרִי אַהֲדָדֵי, חַד הוּא. וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ ״מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא קֶרֶן״ — כׇּל הַשּׁוֹפָרוֹת נָמֵי אִקְּרוֹ קֶרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: This is the reasoning of the Rabbis: The Merciful One says** to sound a single ***shofar*, and not two or three *shofarot*** together, **but this** horn **of a cow, since it is comprised of layers, looks like two or three *shofarot*.** The Gemara asks: **But** the *tanna* **taught:** The horn of a cow cannot be used **because it is a *keren*.** Since the Rabbis' reasoning appears clearly in the mishna, how can the reasoning provided by Abaye be correct? The Gemara replies: The *tanna* **stated one** reason **and** also **another** reason. **One** reason is **that the Merciful One says** to sound **a** single ***shofar*, and not two or three *shofarot*** together, **and another** is **because it is a *keren*.** **And Rabbi Yosei** could have **said to you: That which you said,** that **the Merciful One says** in the Torah to sound a single ***shofar*, and not two or three *shofarot*,** is not a difficulty. **Since** the layers of the cow's horn **are connected to each other,** it is considered **a single** *shofar*. **And** with regard to **that which you said: Because it is a *keren*, all *shofarot* are also called *keren*.**

Abaye offers a third independent rationale: *shofar echad amar Rachmana ve-lo shnayim u-shloshah shofarot* — the Torah specifies a single *shofar*, not two or three. And later in the *masechet* we will see that one may not insert one *shofar* inside another and blow. The problem with a cow's horn is that *keivan de-kaei gildei gildei* — it is visibly layered, growing in segments year by year — *michzei ke-shnayim u-shloshah shofarot*, it appears as though it were two or three *shofarot* joined together, in violation of the requirement for a single *shofar*.

The *gemara* once again invokes *chada ve-od ka'amar* — both this reason and the *keren* terminology are cited by the *mishna*, the former being the primary one. Rabbi Yosei responds: *keivan de-michubar ahadadi*, since the segments of a cow's horn are all organically connected, it constitutes a single unit — *chad hu*. And the *keren* terminology, as before, applies to all horns equally. The *gemara* thus records an impasse between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosei, with the *gemara* itself appearing somewhat frustrated by the back-and-forth over the *keren* terminology and generating successive new arguments precisely because the original *mishna*ic formulation seems inadequate on its own terms.

## Ramban Pshat

### Bone vs Marrow

The Ramban composed a famous *drasha le-Rosh Hashanah* that functions effectively as a running commentary on the *halakhot* of *shofar* and *tefillah* addressed in this *masechet*. One of the first topics he addresses is this very question about the cow's horn. He argues that the entire debate about whether a cow's horn is called a *shofar* or a *keren* misses the plain meaning of the *mishna*. The *mishna* does not say the cow's horn is excluded *mipnei she-nikra keren* — because it is *called* a *keren*. It says *mipnei she-hu keren* — because it *is* a *keren*, because of what it actually *is*.

The Ramban's explanation is structural. A cow's horn contains no marrow; it is solid bone throughout. To convert it into a functioning musical instrument, one must carve out and hollow the bone, which makes the resulting instrument an essentially manufactured object. By contrast, a ram's horn contains marrow that can be scooped out, leaving a naturally hollow vessel that is ready to function as a *shofar* with minimal intervention. The cow's horn must be *fashioned* into an instrument; the ram's horn *is* one naturally. The invalidation thus rests on the distinction between a natural *shofar* and a manufactured horn instrument — which is what *keren* properly denotes in this context.

This reading elegantly sidesteps the philological debate in the *gemara* and grounds the exclusion in the physical character of the instrument rather than in its nomenclature.

### Tosafot Hollow

Tosafot makes a related observation in addressing why the *mishna* does not also exclude the horn of the *re'eim* (wild ox), since the *re'eim*'s horn is likewise called *keren* in Scripture. Tosafot answers: *ve-shema einam chalulim* — perhaps the *re'eim*'s horns are not naturally hollow — *ve-einam re'uyim le-shofar*, and therefore they are not fit to serve as a *shofar* in any case, rendering it unnecessary for the *mishna* to exclude them explicitly. Tosafot does not state that this is the *reason* for the cow's horn's disqualification; he is explaining only why no explicit exclusion was needed for the *re'eim*. The Ramban, however, points out that Tosafot's observation reflects the very same physical insight — the relevance of whether a horn is naturally hollow — and that the Ramban's reading of the *mishna* essentially makes this the operative principle that the *mishna* itself is invoking.

## Yovel as Ram

### Rabbi Akiva Travels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי יוֹבֵלָא לִישָּׁנָא דְּדִכְרָא הוּא? דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: כְּשֶׁהָלַכְתִּי לַעֲרַבְיָא, הָיוּ קוֹרִין לְדִכְרָא ״יוֹבֵלָא״.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna that Rabbi Yosei proves from a verse that a the horn of a ram [*yovel*] is called a *keren*. The Gemara asks: **From where** may it **be inferred that** this term ***yovel* denotes** the horn **of a ram?** The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Akiva said: When I went to Arabia,** I heard that **they called a ram *yovla*,** and from this we can infer the meaning of the term *yovel* in the related language of Hebrew.

The *gemara* asks: *mai mashma de-hai yovla lishna de-dichra hu* — what is the source for understanding the word *yovel* to mean a ram? The word appears throughout the *sugyot* of *kiddush hachodesh* and *shofar*, and the assumption that it refers specifically to a ram's horn has been taken for granted. A *baraita* is cited: Rabbi Akiva testified that when he traveled to Arabia, *hayu korin le-dichra yovla* — the local population referred to a ram as a *yovel*. This vernacular usage provides the philological anchor for the term as it appears in the Torah.

### Vernacular Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: כְּשֶׁהָלַכְתִּי לְגַלְיָא, הָיוּ קוֹרִין לְנִדָּה גַּלְמוּדָה. מַאי גַּלְמוּדָה — גְּמוּלָה דָּא מִבַּעְלָהּ. וְאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: כְּשֶׁהָלַכְתִּי לְאַפְרִיקִי, הָיוּ קוֹרִין לְמָעָה קְשִׂיטָה. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְפָרוֹשֵׁי ״מֵאָה קְשִׂיטָה״ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מְאָה דַּנְקֵי. אָמַר רַבִּי: כְּשֶׁהָלַכְתִּי לִכְרַכֵּי הַיָּם, הָיוּ קוֹרִין לִמְכִירָה כִּירָה. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְפָרוֹשֵׁי ״אֲשֶׁר כָּרִיתִי לִי״: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כְּשֶׁהָלַכְתִּי לִתְחוּם קַן נִשְׁרַיָּיא, הָיוּ קוֹרִין לַכַּלָּה נִינְפִי, וְלַתַּרְנְגוֹל שֶׂכְוִי.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **And Rabbi Akiva said: When I went to Galia,** I heard that **they called a menstruating woman *galmuda*,** and this clarifies the meaning of that word in Scripture. It should be understood as follows: **What** does ***galmuda*** mean? **She is separated [*gemula da*] from her husband,** as all physical contact between a menstruating woman and her husband is forbidden. **And Rabbi Akiva said: When I went to Africa,** I heard that **they called a *ma'a*,** which is a certain coin, ***kesita*.** The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **significance of this?** The Gemara answers: **To explain** that the words **in the Torah** relating to Jacob's purchase of his field near Shechem: "And he bought the parcel of ground where he had spread his tent, from the sons of Hamor for **a hundred *kesita*"** (Genesis 33:19), denote **a hundred *dankei*,** i.e., a hundred *ma'a.* **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: When I went to the sea towns,** I heard that **they called a sale *kira*.** And the Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **significance of this?** The Gemara answers: **To explain** the verse relating to Jacob's burial plot: "In my grave **which I purchased [*kariti*] for myself,** there shall you bury me" (Genesis 50:5). **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: When I went to the district of Kan Nishrayya,** I heard that **they called a bride *ninfi* and a rooster *sekhvi*.**

This exchange introduces a broader methodological point. The *gemara* uses Rabbi Akiva's observation as an instance of a legitimate form of lexicographic inquiry: difficult or rare words in the Torah or in *mishnaic* literature can sometimes be illuminated by how those words, or cognates of them, are used in the living vernacular — whether by local populations, traveling merchants, or the everyday speech of those close to the rabbis. Just as modern scholars of ancient Semitic languages compare biblical vocabulary with contemporary Arabic or Aramaic usage to reconstruct original meanings, the *tanna'im* and *amora'im* employed an analogous method.

## Vernacular Examples

### Akiva Examples

The *gemara* presents several further instances of Rabbi Akiva learning the meaning of scriptural or *mishnaic* terms from vernacular usage encountered in his travels. When Rabbi Akiva went to *Galya* (perhaps Gaul), he found that the local population called a *niddah* a *galmuda* — Tosafot notes this illuminates a *pasuk*. When he went to Africa, he found the local population called a *ma'ah* (a small coin) a *kesitah*, which clarifies the meaning of *me'ah kesitah* in the Torah — it means one hundred *danki*, a known monetary unit. Rabbi (Yehuda HaNasi) similarly reported that when he traveled to the coastal islands, the people there called selling *kirah*, which explains the verse *asher karisi li* — meaning "which I acquired for myself." And Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish reports that in a place called *Kan Nishraya*, a bride was called *ginfi* (possibly from the Greek for bride) and a rooster was called *sikhvi* — the latter illuminating the verse *mi shat batuchot chochmah o mi natan la-sikhvi binah* (Job 38:36), where *sikhvi* refers to the rooster who knows when to crow at dawn.

### Levi Stories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵוִי אִיקְּלַע לְהָהוּא אַתְרָא. אֲתָא גַּבְרָא לְקַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קַבְעַן פְּלָנְיָא. לָא הֲוָה יָדַע מַאי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ. אֲתָא שְׁאֵיל בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גַּזְלַן אֲמַר לָךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״הֲיִקְבַּע אָדָם אֱלֹהִים וְגוֹ׳״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא מִבַּרְנִישׁ לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אִי הֲוַאי הָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לֵיהּ: הֵיכִי קַבְעָךְ? בְּמַאי קַבְעָךְ? וְאַמַּאי קַבְעָךְ? וּמִמֵּילָא הֲוָה יָדְעִינָא. וְאִיהוּ סָבַר: מִילְּתָא דְאִיסּוּרָא קָאָמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Incidental to the discussion of the meanings of certain words in foreign countries, it was related that **Levi** once **happened** to come **to a certain place,** where **a man came before him** to complain about what had been done to him. This man **said to him: So-and-so *keva'a* from me.** Levi **did not understand what** that man **was saying to him,** as he did not know the meaning of the word *kava*. So **he went** and **asked in the study hall. They said to him:** That man **said to you: He robbed me, as it is written: "Will a man rob [*hayikba*] God?"** (Malachi 3:8). **Rava from** a place called **Barnish said to Rav Ashi: Had I been there** in Levi's place I would have tried to uncover the meaning of the word in a different way, for **I would have said to him: How did he *keva'a* you? With what did he *keva'a* you? And why did he *keva'a* you? And** from his answers **I would have understood on my own** what was being said. The Gemara comments: **And** Levi, who did not do this, **thought** that the man **was talking about a matter of prohibition** and not a monetary matter, and so asking the man these questions would not have helped, as they are relevant only to monetary matters.

The *gemara* then narrates the story of Levi, who encountered a man who said to him *kavan ploni* — "I was *kove'a* so-and-so." Levi did not understand the term and had to consult the study hall, where his colleagues explained that the man meant he had stolen from that person, as in the verse *ha-yikba adam Elohim ki atem kove'im oti* — "will a man rob God, for you have robbed Me?" — said in the context of withholding *ma'aser* and *terumah*. Notably, the root appears in a *pasuk* and is not purely vernacular, which makes it surprising that Levi did not recognize it.

### Rava Approach

Rava from Barnish told Rav Ashi that had he been in Levi's position, he would not have needed to consult the study hall. Instead, he would have engaged the man directly with a series of questions: *heichi kav'ach*, *be-mai kav'ach*, *ve-amai kav'ach* — how did you *kove'a* him, with what, and why? From the man's answers, Rava would have reconstructed the meaning by context. This is offered as a clever method of lexicographic inquiry through targeted questioning — essentially the same method one uses when one has forgotten someone's name and keeps the conversation going in hopes of reconstructing the context.

The *gemara* adds that Levi did not take this approach because *midei de-issura ka'amar leih* — he sensed the man was describing something of a transgressive nature and did not want to probe further into the details.

### Maidservant Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי רַבָּנַן מַאי ״סֵירוּגִין״. שַׁמְעוּהָ לְאַמְּתָא דְבֵי רַבִּי דַּחֲזָתִנְהוּ רַבָּנַן דַּהֲווֹ עָיְילִי פִּסְקֵי פִּסְקֵי, אֲמַרָה לְהוּ: עַד מָתַי אַתֶּם נִכְנָסִין סֵירוּגִין סֵירוּגִין. לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי רַבָּנַן מַאי ״חֲלוֹגְלוֹגוֹת״. יוֹמָא חַד שַׁמְעוּהָ לְאַמְּתָא דְבֵי רַבִּי דַּחֲזָית לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּקָא מְבַדַּר פַּרְפְּחִינֵיהּ, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: עַד מָתַי אַתָּה מְפַזֵּר חֲלוֹגְלוֹגְךָ. לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי רַבָּנַן מַאי: ״סַלְסְלֶהָ וּתְרוֹמְמֶךָּ״. יוֹמָא חַד שַׁמְעוּהָ לְאַמְּתָא דְבֵי רַבִּי דַּהֲווֹת אָמְרָה לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה קָא מְהַפֵּךְ בְּשַׂעְרֵיהּ, אָמְרָה לֵיהּ: עַד מָתַי אַתָּה מְסַלְסֵל בִּשְׂעָרְךָ. לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי רַבָּנַן מַאי ״וְטֵאטֵאתִיהָ בְּמַטְאֲטֵא הַשְׁמֵד״. יוֹמָא חַד שַׁמְעוּהָ לְאַמְּתָא דְבֵי רַבִּי דַּהֲווֹת אָמְרָה לַחֲבֶירְתַּהּ: שְׁקוּלִי טָאטִיתָא וְטַאטִי בֵּיתָא. לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי רַבָּנַן מַאי ״הַשְׁלֵךְ עַל ה׳ יְהָבְךָ וְהוּא יְכַלְכְּלֶךָ״. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה אָזְלִינָא בַּהֲדֵי הָהוּא טַיָּיעָא, הֲוָה דָּרֵינָא טוּנָא וַאֲמַר לִי: שְׁקוֹל יַהְבָּיךְ וּשְׁדִי אַגַּמְלַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its discussion of unusual words: **The Sages did not know the meaning of** the word ***seirugin*,** which is found in a mishna. One day **they heard the maidservant in Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi's **house say to the Sages whom she saw entering** the house not all at once, but **intermittently: How long shall you enter *seirugin seirugin*?** and from this they understood that the word *seirugin* means: At intervals. It is similarly recounted that **the Sages did not know the meaning** of the word ***ḥaloglogot*,** which is mentioned in various *mishnayot* and *baraitot*. **One day they heard the maidservant in Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi's **house say to a certain man whom she saw scattering his purslane** plants: **How long shall you scatter your *ḥaloglogot*?** So they understood that *ḥaloglogot* means purslane. **The Sages also did not know the meaning** of the word *salseleha* in the verse: **"*Salseleha* and it will exalt you"** (Proverbs 4:8). **One day they heard the maidservant in Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi's **house say to a certain man who was curling his hair: How long shall you *mesalsel* your hair?** And from this they understood that the verse means: Turn wisdom about, and it will exalt you. It is further related that **the Sages did not know the meaning** of the words in the verse: **"And sweep it [*vetetetiha*] with the broom [*matatei*] of destruction"** (Isaiah 14:23). **One day they heard the maidservant in Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi's **house saying to her workmate: Take a broom [*tateita*] and sweep** [***ta'ati***] **the house,"** and they understood the meaning of these words. **The Sages** also **did not know the meaning** of the word *yehavkha* in the verse: **"Cast your load [*yehavkha*] upon the Lord and He will sustain you"** (Psalms 55:23). **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: One day I was traveling with a certain Arab [*Tayya'a*],** and **I was carrying a load, and he said to me: Take your *yehav* and cast it on my camel,** and from this I understood that *yehav* means a load.

The *gemara* then presents a series of cases in which the Rabbis — meaning the rabbinic students in the academy — did not know the meaning of certain words appearing in *mishnaic* or biblical texts, and learned them through the vernacular usage of the maidservant in Rabbi's household.

They did not know what *serugin* meant (a word that appears in a *mishna*), until they heard the maidservant chastise the students who were entering the study hall in clusters with pauses between them: *ad matay atem nichnasim serugin serugin* — "how long will you keep entering in *serugin serugin*?" From this they understood that *serugin* means intermittently, in broken intervals.

They did not know what *chaluglogot* were — a type of vegetable relevant to certain *halachot* of a *zav*. One day they saw the maidservant rebuke a man who was scattering his *parpachinei* (purslane) about, saying *ad matay atah mefazer chaluglogecha* — and they identified *chaluglogot* as purslane.

They did not know what *salselah* meant in the verse *salselah u-teromemeka* (Proverbs 4:8), meaning to cherish or adorn. They heard the maidservant say to a man who was constantly fussing with his hair, *ad matay atah mesalsel be-sa'arecha* — "how long will you keep *mesalsel* your hair?" — and understood the word to mean playing with or adorning.

They did not know what *ve-titateiha be-matate ha-shmad* (Isaiah 14:23) meant. They heard the maidservant say to her companion *shikuli tatita ve-tati beita* — "take your broom and sweep the house" — and understood *matate* to mean a broom.

Finally, they did not know what *yehavcha* meant in the verse *hashlech al Hashem yehavcha ve-hu yechalkelecha* (Psalms 55:23) — "cast upon God your burden and He will sustain you." Rabbah bar bar Chanah reported that one day he was traveling with a nomadic Arab who, seeing him carrying a load, said *shekol yehavcha u-shdi agmalai* — "take your *yehavach* and throw it on my camel." From this he understood that *yehav* means one's burden or load.

## Rosh Hashanah Shofar

### Description

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹפָר שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה שֶׁל יָעֵל פָּשׁוּט וּפִיו מְצוּפֶּה זָהָב, וּשְׁתֵּי חֲצוֹזְרוֹת מִן הַצְּדָדִין. שׁוֹפָר מַאֲרִיךְ וַחֲצוֹזְרוֹת מְקַצְּרוֹת, שֶׁמִּצְוַת הַיּוֹם בְּשׁוֹפָר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The *shofar*** that was used **on Rosh HaShana** in the Temple was made from the **straight** horn **of an ibex, and its mouth,** the mouthpiece into which one blows, **was plated with gold. And** there were **two trumpets,** one **on** each of **the** two **sides** of the person sounding the *shofar*. **The *shofar* would sound a long** blast, **whereas the trumpets would sound a short** blast, **because the mitzva of the day is with the *shofar*.**

The *mishna* now describes the *shofar* used in the *Beit HaMikdash* on Rosh Hashanah: *shofar shel Rosh Hashanah shel ya'el pashut* — it was made from the horn of a *ya'el* (ibex), and it was straight. *U-fiv metzupeh zahav* — and its mouthpiece was plated in gold. This description pertains specifically to the practice in the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Trumpets Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְתַעֲנִיּוֹת בְּשֶׁל זְכָרִים כְּפוּפִין, וּפִיהֶן מְצוּפֶּה כֶּסֶף, וּשְׁתֵּי חֲצוֹזְרוֹת בָּאֶמְצַע. שׁוֹפָר מְקַצֵּר וַחֲצוֹזְרוֹת מַאֲרִיכוֹת, שֶׁמִּצְוַת הַיּוֹם בַּחֲצוֹזְרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in contrast, the *shofarot* used **on** public **fast days** were made from **the curved** horns **of rams, and their mouths were plated with silver.** There were **two trumpets in the middle** between the *shofarot*, and **the *shofar* would sound a short blast, whereas the trumpets would sound a long** blast, **for the mitzva of the day is with the trumpets.**

*U-shtei chatzotzrot min ha-tzedadim* — two silver trumpets were positioned on the sides. All three instruments would sound together, but the respective durations were calibrated to signal the primary instrument of the day: *shofar maarich ve-chatzotzrot mekatzrot* — the *shofar* would sustain its sound while the *chatzotzrot* would cut off early. The reason: *she-mitzvat hayom ba-shofar* — the central *mitzvah* of the day is the *shofar*, and the *chatzotzrot* serve only as accompaniment. The primacy of the *shofar* is audibly demonstrated by the extended *shofar* tone sounding alone after the *chatzotzrot* fall silent.

### Fast Day Shofar

On fast days, the arrangement was the inverse. *U-ve-ta'aniyot be-shel zecharim kfufim* — the *shofarot* used were the twisted horns of rams — *u-fihen metzupeh kasef* — and their mouthpieces were plated in silver. *U-shtei chatzotzrot ba-emtza* — the two *chatzotzrot* were placed in the center, not the sides — *shofar mekatzrot ve-chatzotzrot maarichot* — the *shofar* cut off while the *chatzotzrot* sustained their sound. *She-mitzvat hayom ba-chatzotzrot* — on a fast day, the primary *mitzvah* is the *chatzotzrot*.

The context for these blowings on fast days is the additional six *berachot* recited in the *shemoneh esrei* on communal fast days for rain. Two of those six additions are *zichronot* and *shofrot*. After each of these *berachot* in the repetition, the *kohanim* (at least in the *Beit HaMikdash*) would sound the *shofar* and *chatzotzrot*. Outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, blowings were done as well, though without the full ceremonial apparatus of center-and-sides placement and precious metal plating.

The *chatzotzrot* as instruments of calling out to God have their own independent biblical basis — *ki tavo'u milchama be-artzechem al ha-tzar ha-tzorer etchem ve-hareiotem ba-chatzotzrot* — and the integration of *chatzotzrot* into the *tefillot* of *ta'anit* reflects this distinct scriptural mandate. The parallels between fast-day practice and the *shofar* of Rosh Hashanah are worth noting even if they are not identical in their sources.

### Rosh Hashanah vs Yovel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁוֶה הַיּוֹבֵל לְרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לַתְּקִיעָה וְלַבְּרָכוֹת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה תּוֹקְעִין בְּשֶׁל זְכָרִים וּבַיּוֹבְלוֹת בְּשֶׁל יְעֵלִים.
>
> **Translation:** Yom Kippur of **the Jubilee Year is the same as Rosh HaShana with regard to** both **the** *shofar* **blasts** that are sounded **and the** additional **blessings** that are recited in the *Amida* prayer. **Rabbi Yehuda** disagrees and **says:** There is a difference between the two days: **On Rosh HaShana one blows with** horns **of rams, whereas in Jubilee Years** one blows **with** horns **of ibexes.**

The *mishna* then states that *yovel* is equivalent to Rosh Hashanah with respect to the *tekiot* and the *berachot* — meaning the number of blasts and the structure of *malchuyot*, *zichronot*, and *shofrot*. This equation sets up the next section's debate about whether the horn types used on Rosh Hashanah and *yovel* must in fact be identical.

## Rabbi Yehuda View

### Yehuda Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: מִצְוָה שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וְשֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים בִּכְפוּפִין, וְשֶׁל כׇּל הַשָּׁנָה בִּפְשׁוּטִין. וְהָתְנַן: שׁוֹפָר שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה שֶׁל יָעֵל פָּשׁוּט! הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה הָיוּ תּוֹקְעִין בְּשֶׁל זְכָרִים כְּפוּפִין, וּבַיּוֹבְלוֹת בְּשֶׁל יְעֵלִים. וְלֵימָא הִלְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה! אִי אָמְרַתְּ הִלְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁל יוֹבֵל נָמֵי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Levi said: The mitzva of Rosh HaShana and of Yom Kippur** of the Jubilee Year is to blow **with curved** *shofarot*, **and** that **of the rest of the year,** on fast days, is to blow **with straight** *shofarot*. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But didn't we learn** differently in the mishna: **The *shofar*** that was used **on Rosh HaShana** was made from the **straight** horn **of an ibex?** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Levi **said** his statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*,** Rabbi Yehuda, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says: On Rosh HaShana they would blow with the curved** horns **of rams, and on** the Days of Atonement of **the Jubilee Years with** the horns **of ibexes.** The Gemara asks: If so, **let** it simply **say** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** Why was it necessary to quote the *baraita* in full, as if it provided new information? The Gemara explains: **If you had said** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, I would have said** that **he,** Rabbi Levi, **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda even with regard to** the *shofar* used **in the Jubilee Year,** i.e., that one must blow with the horn of ibexes at that time. Therefore, the Gemara **teaches us** that he agrees with Rabbi Yehuda only with regard to Rosh HaShana, and not with regard to any other matter.

Rabbi Yehuda disputes the first part of the *mishna* on two fronts. He holds that Rosh Hashanah and *yovel* are not equivalent in the type of horn used, and that the horn for Rosh Hashanah itself differs from what the *tanna kamma* describes. *Be-Rosh Hashanah tokiyim be-shel zecharim* — on Rosh Hashanah one blows with rams' horns — *u-ve-yovel be-shel yeilim* — and on *yovel* with ibex horns. So Rabbi Yehuda actually reverses the *tanna kamma*'s assignment: ibex (*ya'el*, straight) for *yovel*, ram (*zachar*, bent) for Rosh Hashanah. He explicitly maintains a distinction between the two occasions rather than treating them symmetrically.

### Levi Classification

Rabbi Levi adds a further dimension: *mitzvah shel Rosh Hashanah ve-shel Yom Kippurim bi-kfufim* — the ideal for both Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur is bent horns — *ve-shel kol ha-shanah bi-fshutim* — while for all other occasions (meaning the fast-day *shofarot*) straight horns are used. Rabbi Levi thus agrees with Rabbi Yehuda that Rosh Hashanah calls for a bent horn, but disagrees with Rabbi Yehuda's distinction between Rosh Hashanah and *yovel*: for Rabbi Levi, both Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur (*yovel*) are alike in requiring a bent horn.

The *gemara* reconciles Rabbi Levi's view with the *mishna* by noting that the *mishna* already records Rabbi Yehuda's opinion that *zecharim* (rams) are used on Rosh Hashanah, and a *baraita* makes explicit what the *mishna* only implies: *Rabbi Yehuda omer be-Rosh Hashanah yotzim be-shel zecharim kfufim* — rams are bent. Rabbi Levi is therefore understood as following this *tanna*. The *gemara* explains why Rabbi Levi did not simply rule *halakha ke-Rabbi Yehuda*: had he done so, one might have thought he also accepts Rabbi Yehuda's distinction between Rosh Hashanah and *yovel*, with *yovel* using a straight horn. By specifying that both Rosh Hashanah and *yovel* take bent horns, Rabbi Levi makes clear he agrees with Rabbi Yehuda only on the Rosh Hashanah side, not on the differentiation.

The resulting picture is that all three positions — *tanna kamma*, Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Levi — take distinct stances along two axes: (1) whether Rosh Hashanah and *yovel* are the same or different, and (2) whether the horn for Rosh Hashanah is straight (*pashut*) or bent (*kafuf*). *Tanna kamma* holds them the same and straight; Rabbi Yehuda holds them different (Rosh Hashanah bent, *yovel* straight); Rabbi Levi holds them the same and both bent.

## Bent vs Straight

### Core Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה — כַּמָּה דְּכָיֵיף אִינִישׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ, טְפֵי מְעַלֵּי; וּבְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים — כַּמָּה דְּפָשֵׁיט אִינִישׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ, טְפֵי מְעַלֵּי. וּמָר סָבַר: בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה — כַּמָּה דְּפָשֵׁיט אִינִישׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ, טְפֵי מְעַלֵּי; וּבְתַעֲנִיּוֹת — כַּמָּה דְּכָיֵיף אִינִישׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ, טְפֵי מְעַלֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle do these *tanna'im* **disagree? One Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that **on Rosh HaShana the more a person bends his mind** and humbles himself by bending in prayer, **the better.** Therefore, a curved *shofar* is sounded as an allusion to our bent minds and bodies. **But on Yom Kippur, the more a person straightens his mind** and prays with simplicity, **the better.** Therefore, a straight *shofar* is sounded. **The other Sage,** the anonymous *tanna* of the mishna, **maintains** the opposite: **On Rosh HaShana, the more a person straightens his mind** and avoids any crookedness, **the better. On fasts,** on the other hand, **the more a person bends his mind** and humbles himself, **the better.**

The *gemara* now asks *be-mai kamifligi* — what exactly is the underlying disagreement between the *tanna kamma* and the others regarding the Rosh Hashanah *shofar*? Both sides agree that the *shofar* must work; the dispute is about the preferred shape. The *gemara* frames the answer in terms of the symbolic meaning each shape carries for the day of Rosh Hashanah.

### Straight Symbolism

One view holds that *be-Rosh Hashanah kama de-pashit inish dateih tfei ma'alei* — on Rosh Hashanah, the more one's *da'at* is directed straight, the better. The idea is that Rosh Hashanah is fundamentally about approaching God directly — not about self-abnegation or prostration, but about bringing one's prayers and one's very self straight to God. A straight horn symbolizes and embodies that direct, forward-directed orientation.

### Bent Symbolism

The opposing view holds *kama de-khayif inish dateih tfei ma'alei* — the more one is bent over, the better. This conception of Rosh Hashanah emphasizes supplication, self-negation, a sense of being weighed down by one's inadequacy, the posture of one who comes before the King not in confidence but in humility and contrition. A bent horn embodies that downward, inward orientation of *teshuvah* and shame.

By contrast, those who hold that Rosh Hashanah calls for a straight horn would agree that *be-ta'aniyot kama de-khayif inish dateih tfei ma'alei* — on fast days, the bent posture is appropriate, since fast days are precisely about confronting sin and communal guilt. On Yom Kippur, meanwhile, the *yovel* aspect — the proclamation of freedom — calls for a straight, outward, announcing posture: *ve-Yom Kippurim kama de-pashit inish dateih tfei ma'alei*.

### Tanna Kamma

The *tanna kamma* thus holds that Rosh Hashanah is best approached with a *pashut*, straight, direct orientation toward God. This is not the day of self-flagellation and guilt — that belongs to the fast days. Rosh Hashanah is the day of coronation, of drawing close, of presenting oneself to the King in a posture of dignity and directness. The *tanna kamma*'s preference for a straight horn on Rosh Hashanah reflects this theological orientation.

## Shofar Direction

### Animal Symbolism

The discussion of straight versus bent horns intersects with the question of which animal the horn comes from — and each animal carries its own symbolic weight. A ram's horn evokes the *akeidah*, the binding of Yitzchak, and all that entails: self-sacrifice, surrender, the willingness to give everything up. It evokes a posture of submission and the negation of self before God. An ibex (mountain goat) horn, with its proud, outward-reaching form, projects a very different imagery — directness, confidence, even pride. The *gemara* focuses on the *pashut-kafuf* axis, but the animal dimension reinforces the same symbolic contrast.

### Direction Question

There is a deeper question underlying this entire discussion: to whom or toward what is the *shofar* directed? Is the *shofar* directed *to you* — meaning it is a call addressed to the listener, meant to awaken the hearer to repentance and fear — or is it directed *to God* — meaning it is part of Israel's prayer, functioning as an instrument through which we send our supplications upward? If the *shofar* is directed to you, then the bent posture makes perfect sense for Rosh Hashanah: the *shofar* breaks through your self-satisfaction, bends you, induces fear and reflection. But if the *shofar* is directed to God — if it is the mechanism of *zikaron* before God — then the straight, direct, upward-directed shape is more fitting. Rashi's commentary implies this second understanding.

### Tosafot Symbolic

Tosafot engages at length with the question of how we *poskin* — how we rule in practice on the question of bent versus straight. Tosafot concludes that this is presumably a matter of *lechatchila* only — a symbolic preference about the ideal form of the *shofar* — and not a question of strict *halakha* that would render one's obligation unfulfilled if not observed. Tosafot does note, however, that one could read the discussion as *halakha le-ma'aseh*, such that using the wrong shape would mean one has not properly fulfilled the *mitzvah* — which is hard to accept in light of the opening *mishna* of this section, which declares that all *shofarot* are *kasher*. The normative conclusion is therefore that the preference for one shape or another is a matter of *lechatchila* and symbolic significance, not a *me'akeiv* disqualification. Nevertheless, the underlying question of what the *shofar* is fundamentally about — a summons to *teshuvah* directed inward at the listener, or an instrument of prayer directed outward toward God — is far from merely symbolic. It goes to the heart of how one understands the *mitzvah* of *shofar* on Rosh Hashanah.
