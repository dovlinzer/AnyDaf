# Rosh Hashanah 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lo Tehei Shemiah

### Lo Tehei Shemiah

The daf begins by wrapping up the issues of *kiddush hachodesh* and picking up at the bottom of 25b, approximately ten lines from the bottom. The *mishna* there assumes that even if the *beit din* saw the new moon themselves, they must still go through the formal process of testimony. The *gemara* challenges this assumption by invoking the principle of *lo tehei shemiah gedolah mereiyah* — hearing testimony should not be greater than seeing directly. If seeing the new moon is sufficient knowledge, why must members of the *beit din* break off and formally testify to the others?

### Nighttime Seeing

Rav Zeira resolves the difficulty: the case in the *mishna* is one where they saw the moon at night. Since the entire process of accepting witnesses — and indeed the pronouncement of *mekudash* — must take place during the day, their act of seeing at night does not carry the legal weight of a formal act of testimony. One might immediately object: even if their nighttime seeing cannot function as formal testimony, they still *know* what they saw. Why must they hear witnesses the following morning?

The *gemara*'s answer is precise and important. The principle of *lo tehei shemiah gedolah mereiyah* does not go so far as to say that mere *knowledge* eliminates the need for formal process. Rather, the principle holds that an act of *seeing* is equivalent in legal status to an act of *hearing testimony* — but just as hearing testimony must occur during the day, so too the act of seeing that would substitute for it must occur during the day. Since they saw it at night, that seeing cannot function as the formal equivalent of testimony, and a fresh formal process is required the next morning. The new moon's visibility at night makes this situation entirely natural: they see it after sunset, but cannot validate the month until the morning.

### Three Witnesses Case

The *mishna* then presents a second case: if only three judges saw the moon, and two cannot break off to testify because an insufficient number would remain on the *beit din*, two must step aside and be replaced by additional judges so that testimony can be formally accepted. The *gemara* asks the same question — why not invoke *lo tehei shemiah gedolah mereiyah* here as well? The *gemara* considers giving the same answer as before (they saw it at night), but then objects: *haynu hach* — that is identical to the first case and adds nothing new.

The *gemara* therefore explains that the second case is needed for a novel point stated at its end: *ein hayachid ne'eman al yedei atzmo* — a single individual cannot constitute a *beit din*. One might have thought that since monetary cases may be adjudicated by a single *mumcheh*, a recognized expert, perhaps the new moon could similarly be sanctified by one such individual. The *mishna* therefore teaches that this is not so. God told Moshe — despite being the greatest *mumcheh* the Jewish people has ever had — that Aharon must be with him, as derived from the verse *vayomer Hashem el Moshe ve'el Aharon be'eretz Mitzrayim lemor hachodesh hazeh lachem*. The deeper reason is that the *beit din* here functions not merely to render a legal decision but to represent the Jewish people as a whole, which requires a collective body rather than a single individual.

### Eid Naasah Dayan

The *gemara* now raises the principle of *eid na'aseh dayan*. The *mishna* envisions a scenario where a member of the *beit din* might himself have been in a position to serve as a witness to the moon. The concern is not the case of *eid hameid na'aseh dayan* — where one testifies and then immediately turns to serve as the judge of one's own testimony — but rather the case of *eid sherah*: a *dayan* who personally saw the moon and is now listening to others testify. Does the fact that he could have been a witness disqualify him from serving as a judge?

The *gemara* raises the possibility that the *mishna* follows the view of the Rabbis and not that of Rabbi Akiva. As taught in a *baraita*: if a *sanhedrin* witnessed a murder, Rabbi Tarfon holds that some of them may serve as witnesses while others serve as judges. Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *kulam na'asu eidim ve-ein eid na'aseh dayan* — all of them are considered witnesses, and a witness cannot become a judge.

---

## Eid Naasah Dayan

### Rabbi Tarfon

Rabbi Tarfon's view, as stated in the *baraita*, is that when a *sanhedrin* has witnessed a capital crime, the group may divide its roles: some will testify while others, together with additional judges, will adjudicate. Seeing the event does not disqualify one from serving as a judge, provided one does not personally testify. The witnessing itself leaves room for a formal distinction between the role of *eid* and the role of *dayan*.

### Rabbi Akiva

Rabbi Akiva takes the more stringent position: *kulam na'asu eidim* — everyone who saw it is rendered a witness — and *ein eid na'aseh dayan*, a witness cannot subsequently serve as the judge in that very case. The act of witnessing the crime itself, not merely the act of testifying, is what triggers the disqualification.

### Capital Crimes Only

The *gemara* resolves that even according to Rabbi Akiva, the *mishna* regarding the new moon is unproblematic. Rabbi Akiva's rule applies exclusively to *dinei nefashot*, capital cases, where the Torah states *ve-shaftu ha-eda ve-hitzilu ha-eda* — the congregation shall judge and the congregation shall seek to save the accused. In capital cases, one who has seen the crime committed is so emotionally and cognitively invested that he cannot maintain the dispassionate distance needed to seek out arguments in the defendant's favor. The graphic reality of witnessing a killing registers in a way that forecloses the kind of impartial advocacy the Torah demands of judges in capital matters. For *kiddush hachodesh*, however, even Rabbi Akiva agrees that having seen the moon does not disqualify one from participating in the adjudicative process.

---

## Tosafot Classification

### Three Categories

Tosafot, in the *dibbur hamatchil* beginning with the word *uve-eid*, provides a crisp summary of three distinct legal contexts in which *eid na'aseh dayan* operates differently. In the case of testimony about the new moon, which is *de-oraita*, one who merely saw the moon — *eid haroeh* — may nonetheless serve as a judge; the seeing does not disqualify. In the case of *kiyum shetarot*, the validation of signatures on documents, which is Rabbinic in origin, even *eid hameid na'aseh dayan* — one who actually testified may turn around and serve as the adjudicating judge. And in *dinei nefashot*, capital cases, even *eid haroeh eino na'aseh dayan* — even one who merely saw the act, without ever testifying, is disqualified from serving as judge.

The result is a spectrum: at one extreme, *kiyum shetarot* is the most permissive (even the testifying witness may judge); in the middle, *kiddush hachodesh* permits the one who saw to judge; and at the other extreme, *dinei nefashot* disqualifies even the one who only saw but never testified. This leaves open, as a *she'elah*, where ordinary *dinei mamonot* and cases involving lashes — which are neither capital nor merely monetary — would fall along this spectrum.

## Transition to Shofar

### All Except Cow

With the issues of *kiddush hachodesh* concluded, the *masechet* now transitions to its *shofar* section. The *mishna* states: *kol hashofarot ksheirim* — all horns are valid for use as a *shofar* — *chutz mishel parah*, except for a cow's horn — *mipnei shehu keren*, because it is a *keren*. This formulation immediately invites scrutiny: what precisely is the significance of calling it a *keren*, and why would that disqualify it?

### Keren Definition

Rabbi Yosi challenges the reasoning directly. He argues that one cannot exclude a cow's horn on the grounds that it is called a *keren*, because all *shofarot* are referred to as *keren* as well. His proof is from the verse in Yehoshua concerning the fall of the walls of Yericho: *bimshoch bkeren hayovel heimah ya'alu bahar* — when the *keren hayovel* is drawn out, they shall go up the mountain — and the verse continues, *b'shom'achem et kol hashofar*. Since a *yovel* — which as the *gemara* will establish means a ram — has its horn called both *keren* and *shofar*, the terminology of *keren* alone cannot be the basis for distinguishing a cow's horn.

---

## Cow Horn Debate

### Rabbi Yosi Proof

Rabbi Yosi's challenge stands. Even a ram's horn — which is unambiguously valid — is referred to in Scripture as a *keren*. If the *mishna*'s reason for invalidating a cow's horn were simply that it is called a *keren*, that reason would equally invalidate every *shofar*, since rams' horns are likewise called *keren*. The objection therefore demands a stronger account of the distinction.

### Rabbis Response

The Rabbis respond by refining the claim. All other horns, they argue, are called both *shofar* and *keren* — *kol hashofarot ikru keren ve-ikru shofar*. The term *shofar* applies to rams' horns, as derived from the *pasuk* at Har Sinai: *bimshoch hayovel heimah ya'alu bahar*, followed by *vayehi kol hashofar holech vechazek me'od* — there the *yovel* (ram's horn) is explicitly called a *shofar*. A cow's horn, by contrast, is only ever called a *keren*, never a *shofar*. The proof is the verse: *bechor shoro hadar lo ve-karnei re'eim karnav* — horn language is associated with the bovine, while the word *shofar* never appears in that context.

### Shor Par

Rabbi Yosi counters that a cow's horn *is* called a *shofar*, based on the verse *ve-titev la'Hashem mishor par* — my offering will be more pleasing to God than a *shor par*. The *gemara* asks: if it is a *shor*, why say *par*, and if it is a *par*, why say *shor*? The answer: *shor par* is a contraction for *mi-shofar* — from the *shofar*. The double language signals that the animal's horn is itself called a *shofar*.

The Rabbis respond to this by invoking Rav Masna's interpretation: the verse refers to an animal that was a *shor* — that is, a one-day-old — yet was as large as a *par* (a fully grown animal), since at the time of creation animals were formed in their fully developed state. The word *par* thus describes the size, not the species, and the verse does not establish a general term *shofar* for a cow's horn.

The debate at this point is granular — trading *pesukim* to establish whether *shofar* ever applies to a bovine horn — and the *gemara* itself seems to recognize the unsatisfying character of this exchange. Both Ulla and Abaye will offer alternative explanations that move away from the philological argument entirely.

### Golden Calf Link

Ulla proposes what he presents as the real reason underlying the Rabbinic position: *hainu ta'ama de-Rabbanan ke-Rav Chisda*. Rav Chisda had asked why the *kohen gadol* does not enter the inner sanctum on Yom Kippur wearing his golden vestments when performing the *avodat pnim*. The answer: *ein kategor na'aseh sanegore* — the prosecutor cannot become the defense attorney. Gold evokes the *chet ha-eigel*, the sin of the Golden Calf, and therefore cannot be the instrument through which the *kohen gadol* approaches God in the innermost sanctum on the day of atonement. Similarly, a cow's horn evokes the *eigel* and cannot serve as the instrument through which Israel approaches God on Rosh Hashanah.

The *gemara* then subjects this parallel to sustained scrutiny. Does not the blood of the ox (*dam par*) enter the *kodesh kodashim* on Yom Kippur? The *gemara* answers: blood no longer appears as a cow — *ishtani ishtani*, it has been transformed. What about the *aron*, the *kapores*, and the *keruvim*, all of which are gold and reside permanently inside the inner sanctum? The *gemara* distinguishes: the principle of *ein kategor na'aseh sanegore* applies to what the *kohen gadol* himself brings in and uses — *chotei bal yakriv* — the sinner may not approach using objects that evoke the sin. The permanent furnishings of the sanctuary are not being actively wielded by the approaching worshipper. What about the *kaf* and *machtah*, the fire pan and incense ladle, which are gold and which the *kohen gadol* does carry in? The *gemara* answers with the principle of *chotei bal yisna'eh* — the sinner may not adorn or beautify himself with things that evoke his sin. Clothing, which integrates with one's identity and presents one's person, is categorically different from the vessels one happens to carry.

The *gemara* then presses: the *kohen gadol* does wear gold vestments outside the inner sanctum for the other services on Yom Kippur — why is that permitted? The answer: *mi-pnim* — only within the innermost sanctum is the prohibition operative.

This leads to the sharpest objection: the *shofar* is blown outside, not within the *kodesh kodashim* — *shofar mi-bachutz hu*. So why should the *kategor-sanegore* logic apply? The *gemara* responds with a striking statement: *keivan de-le-zikaron hu ke-pnim dami* — since the *shofar* functions to raise Israel's memory before God, it is as if it penetrates directly into the innermost sanctum. The *shofar* pierces through to God's presence, so its use is treated as though it were an act performed *lifnai ve-lifnim*.

Yet the *gemara* simultaneously notes that the *mishna* stated the reason for excluding the cow's horn as *mipnei shehu keren* — which sounds like a terminology-based argument, not the *kategor-sanegore* reasoning. The *gemara* resolves this: *chada ve-od ka'amar* — the *mishna* is making two points, not one. The primary reason is *ein kategor na'aseh sanegore*; the secondary, additional point is *mipnei shehu keren*. Rabbi Yosi, for his part, maintains that neither argument succeeds: the *kategor-sanegore* reasoning applies only within the *kodesh kodashim*, and the *keren* terminology applies equally to all *shofarot*.

### Multiple Segments

Abaye offers a third independent rationale: *shofar echad amar Rachmana ve-lo shnayim u-shloshah shofarot* — the Torah specifies a single *shofar*, not two or three. And later in the *masechet* we will see that one may not insert one *shofar* inside another and blow. The problem with a cow's horn is that *keivan de-kaei gildei gildei* — it is visibly layered, growing in segments year by year — *michzei ke-shnayim u-shloshah shofarot*, it appears as though it were two or three *shofarot* joined together, in violation of the requirement for a single *shofar*.

The *gemara* once again invokes *chada ve-od ka'amar* — both this reason and the *keren* terminology are cited by the *mishna*, the former being the primary one. Rabbi Yosi responds: *keivan de-michubar ahadadi*, since the segments of a cow's horn are all organically connected, it constitutes a single unit — *chad hu*. And the *keren* terminology, as before, applies to all horns equally. The *gemara* thus records an impasse between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosi, with the *gemara* itself appearing somewhat frustrated by the back-and-forth over the *keren* terminology and generating successive new arguments precisely because the original *mishna*ic formulation seems inadequate on its own terms.

## Ramban Pshat

### Bone vs Marrow

The Ramban composed a famous *drasha le-Rosh Hashanah* that functions effectively as a running commentary on the *halakhot* of *shofar* and *tefillah* addressed in this *masechet*. One of the first topics he addresses is this very question about the cow's horn. He argues that the entire debate about whether a cow's horn is called a *shofar* or a *keren* misses the plain meaning of the *mishna*. The *mishna* does not say the cow's horn is excluded *mipnei she-nikra keren* — because it is *called* a *keren*. It says *mipnei she-hu keren* — because it *is* a *keren*, because of what it actually *is*.

The Ramban's explanation is structural. A cow's horn contains no marrow; it is solid bone throughout. To convert it into a functioning musical instrument, one must carve out and hollow the bone, which makes the resulting instrument an essentially manufactured object. By contrast, a ram's horn contains marrow that can be scooped out, leaving a naturally hollow vessel that is ready to function as a *shofar* with minimal intervention. The cow's horn must be *fashioned* into an instrument; the ram's horn *is* one naturally. The invalidation thus rests on the distinction between a natural *shofar* and a manufactured horn instrument — which is what *keren* properly denotes in this context.

This reading elegantly sidesteps the philological debate in the *gemara* and grounds the exclusion in the physical character of the instrument rather than in its nomenclature.

### Tosafot Hollow

Tosafot makes a related observation in addressing why the *mishna* does not also exclude the horn of the *re'eim* (wild ox), since the *re'eim*'s horn is likewise called *keren* in Scripture. Tosafot answers: *ve-shema einam chalulim* — perhaps the *re'eim*'s horns are not naturally hollow — *ve-einam re'uyim le-shofar*, and therefore they are not fit to serve as a *shofar* in any case, rendering it unnecessary for the *mishna* to exclude them explicitly. Tosafot does not state that this is the *reason* for the cow's horn's disqualification; he is explaining only why no explicit exclusion was needed for the *re'eim*. The Ramban, however, points out that Tosafot's observation reflects the very same physical insight — the relevance of whether a horn is naturally hollow — and that the Ramban's reading of the *mishna* essentially makes this the operative principle that the *mishna* itself is invoking.

## Yovel as Ram

### Rabbi Akiva Travels

The *gemara* asks: *mai mashma de-hai yovla lishna de-dichra hu* — what is the source for understanding the word *yovel* to mean a ram? The word appears throughout the *sugyot* of *kiddush hachodesh* and *shofar*, and the assumption that it refers specifically to a ram's horn has been taken for granted. A *baraita* is cited: Rabbi Akiva testified that when he traveled to Arabia, *hayu korin le-dichra yovla* — the local population referred to a ram as a *yovel*. This vernacular usage provides the philological anchor for the term as it appears in the Torah.

### Vernacular Evidence

This exchange introduces a broader methodological point. The *gemara* uses Rabbi Akiva's observation as an instance of a legitimate form of lexicographic inquiry: difficult or rare words in the Torah or in *mishnaic* literature can sometimes be illuminated by how those words, or cognates of them, are used in the living vernacular — whether by local populations, traveling merchants, or the everyday speech of those close to the rabbis. Just as modern scholars of ancient Semitic languages compare biblical vocabulary with contemporary Arabic or Aramaic usage to reconstruct original meanings, the *tanna'im* and *amora'im* employed an analogous method.

---

## Vernacular Examples

### Akiva Examples

The *gemara* presents several further instances of Rabbi Akiva learning the meaning of scriptural or *mishnaic* terms from vernacular usage encountered in his travels. When Rabbi Akiva went to *Galya* (perhaps Gaul), he found that the local population called a *niddah* a *galmuda* — Tosafot notes this illuminates a *pasuk*. When he went to Africa, he found the local population called a *ma'ah* (a small coin) a *kesitah*, which clarifies the meaning of *me'ah kesitah* in the Torah — it means one hundred *danki*, a known monetary unit. Rabbi (Yehuda HaNasi) similarly reported that when he traveled to the coastal islands, the people there called selling *kirah*, which explains the verse *asher karisi li* — meaning "which I acquired for myself." And Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish reports that in a place called *Kan Nishraya*, a bride was called *ginfi* (possibly from the Greek for bride) and a rooster was called *sikhvi* — the latter illuminating the verse *mi shat batuchot chochmah o mi natan la-sikhvi binah* (Job 38:36), where *sikhvi* refers to the rooster who knows when to crow at dawn.

### Levi Stories

The *gemara* then narrates the story of Levi, who encountered a man who said to him *kavan ploni* — "I was *kove'a* so-and-so." Levi did not understand the term and had to consult the study hall, where his colleagues explained that the man meant he had stolen from that person, as in the verse *ha-yikba adam Elohim ki atem kove'im oti* — "will a man rob God, for you have robbed Me?" — said in the context of withholding *ma'aser* and *terumah*. Notably, the root appears in a *pasuk* and is not purely vernacular, which makes it surprising that Levi did not recognize it.

### Rava Approach

Rava from Barnish told Rav Ashi that had he been in Levi's position, he would not have needed to consult the study hall. Instead, he would have engaged the man directly with a series of questions: *heichi kav'ach*, *be-mai kav'ach*, *ve-amai kav'ach* — how did you *kove'a* him, with what, and why? From the man's answers, Rava would have reconstructed the meaning by context. This is offered as a clever method of lexicographic inquiry through targeted questioning — essentially the same method one uses when one has forgotten someone's name and keeps the conversation going in hopes of reconstructing the context.

The *gemara* adds that Levi did not take this approach because *midei de-issura ka'amar leih* — he sensed the man was describing something of a transgressive nature and did not want to probe further into the details.

### Maidservant Words

The *gemara* then presents a series of cases in which the Rabbis — meaning the rabbinic students in the academy — did not know the meaning of certain words appearing in *mishnaic* or biblical texts, and learned them through the vernacular usage of the maidservant in Rabbi's household.

They did not know what *serugin* meant (a word that appears in a *mishna*), until they heard the maidservant chastise the students who were entering the study hall in clusters with pauses between them: *ad matay atem nichnasim serugin serugin* — "how long will you keep entering in *serugin serugin*?" From this they understood that *serugin* means intermittently, in broken intervals.

They did not know what *chaluglogot* were — a type of vegetable relevant to certain *halachot* of a *zav*. One day they saw the maidservant rebuke a man who was scattering his *parpachinei* (purslane) about, saying *ad matay atah mefazer chaluglogecha* — and they identified *chaluglogot* as purslane.

They did not know what *salselah* meant in the verse *salselah u-teromemeka* (Proverbs 4:8), meaning to cherish or adorn. They heard the maidservant say to a man who was constantly fussing with his hair, *ad matay atah mesalsel be-sa'arecha* — "how long will you keep *mesalsel* your hair?" — and understood the word to mean playing with or adorning.

They did not know what *ve-titateiha be-matate ha-shmad* (Isaiah 14:23) meant. They heard the maidservant say to her companion *shikuli tatita ve-tati beita* — "take your broom and sweep the house" — and understood *matate* to mean a broom.

Finally, they did not know what *yehavcha* meant in the verse *hashlech al Hashem yehavcha ve-hu yechalkelecha* (Psalms 55:23) — "cast upon God your burden and He will sustain you." Rabbah bar bar Chanah reported that one day he was traveling with a nomadic Arab who, seeing him carrying a load, said *shekol yehavcha u-shdi agmalai* — "take your *yehavach* and throw it on my camel." From this he understood that *yehav* means one's burden or load.

## Rosh Hashanah Shofar

### Description

The *mishna* now describes the *shofar* used in the *Beit HaMikdash* on Rosh Hashanah: *shofar shel Rosh Hashanah shel ya'el pashut* — it was made from the horn of a *ya'el* (ibex), and it was straight. *U-fiv metzupeh zahav* — and its mouthpiece was plated in gold. This description pertains specifically to the practice in the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Trumpets Role

*U-shtei chatzotzrot min ha-tzedadim* — two silver trumpets were positioned on the sides. All three instruments would sound together, but the respective durations were calibrated to signal the primary instrument of the day: *shofar maarich ve-chatzotzrot mekatzrot* — the *shofar* would sustain its sound while the *chatzotzrot* would cut off early. The reason: *she-mitzvat hayom ba-shofar* — the central *mitzvah* of the day is the *shofar*, and the *chatzotzrot* serve only as accompaniment. The primacy of the *shofar* is audibly demonstrated by the extended *shofar* tone sounding alone after the *chatzotzrot* fall silent.

### Fast Day Shofar

On fast days, the arrangement was the inverse. *U-ve-ta'aniyot be-shel zecharim kfufim* — the *shofarot* used were the twisted horns of rams — *u-fihen metzupeh kasef* — and their mouthpieces were plated in silver. *U-shtei chatzotzrot ba-emtza* — the two *chatzotzrot* were placed in the center, not the sides — *shofar mekatzrot ve-chatzotzrot maarichot* — the *shofar* cut off while the *chatzotzrot* sustained their sound. *She-mitzvat hayom ba-chatzotzrot* — on a fast day, the primary *mitzvah* is the *chatzotzrot*.

The context for these blowings on fast days is the additional six *berachot* recited in the *shemoneh esrei* on communal fast days for rain. Two of those six additions are *zichronot* and *shofrot*. After each of these *berachot* in the repetition, the *kohanim* (at least in the *Beit HaMikdash*) would sound the *shofar* and *chatzotzrot*. Outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, blowings were done as well, though without the full ceremonial apparatus of center-and-sides placement and precious metal plating.

The *chatzotzrot* as instruments of calling out to God have their own independent biblical basis — *ki tavo'u milchama be-artzechem al ha-tzar ha-tzorer etchem ve-hareiotem ba-chatzotzrot* — and the integration of *chatzotzrot* into the *tefillot* of *ta'anit* reflects this distinct scriptural mandate. The parallels between fast-day practice and the *shofar* of Rosh Hashanah are worth noting even if they are not identical in their sources.

### Rosh Hashanah vs Yovel

The *mishna* then states that *yovel* is equivalent to Rosh Hashanah with respect to the *tekiot* and the *berachot* — meaning the number of blasts and the structure of *malchuyot*, *zichronot*, and *shofrot*. This equation sets up the next section's debate about whether the horn types used on Rosh Hashanah and *yovel* must in fact be identical.

## Rabbi Yehuda View

### Yehuda Distinction

Rabbi Yehuda disputes the first part of the *mishna* on two fronts. He holds that Rosh Hashanah and *yovel* are not equivalent in the type of horn used, and that the horn for Rosh Hashanah itself differs from what the *tanna kamma* describes. *Be-Rosh Hashanah tokiyim be-shel zecharim* — on Rosh Hashanah one blows with rams' horns — *u-ve-yovel be-shel yeilim* — and on *yovel* with ibex horns. So Rabbi Yehuda actually reverses the *tanna kamma*'s assignment: ibex (*ya'el*, straight) for *yovel*, ram (*zachar*, bent) for Rosh Hashanah. He explicitly maintains a distinction between the two occasions rather than treating them symmetrically.

### Levi Classification

Rabbi Levi adds a further dimension: *mitzvah shel Rosh Hashanah ve-shel Yom Kippurim bi-kfufim* — the ideal for both Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur is bent horns — *ve-shel kol ha-shanah bi-fshutim* — while for all other occasions (meaning the fast-day *shofarot*) straight horns are used. Rabbi Levi thus agrees with Rabbi Yehuda that Rosh Hashanah calls for a bent horn, but disagrees with Rabbi Yehuda's distinction between Rosh Hashanah and *yovel*: for Rabbi Levi, both Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur (*yovel*) are alike in requiring a bent horn.

The *gemara* reconciles Rabbi Levi's view with the *mishna* by noting that the *mishna* already records Rabbi Yehuda's opinion that *zecharim* (rams) are used on Rosh Hashanah, and a *baraita* makes explicit what the *mishna* only implies: *Rabbi Yehuda omer be-Rosh Hashanah yotzim be-shel zecharim kfufim* — rams are bent. Rabbi Levi is therefore understood as following this *tanna*. The *gemara* explains why Rabbi Levi did not simply rule *halakha ke-Rabbi Yehuda*: had he done so, one might have thought he also accepts Rabbi Yehuda's distinction between Rosh Hashanah and *yovel*, with *yovel* using a straight horn. By specifying that both Rosh Hashanah and *yovel* take bent horns, Rabbi Levi makes clear he agrees with Rabbi Yehuda only on the Rosh Hashanah side, not on the differentiation.

The resulting picture is that all three positions — *tanna kamma*, Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Levi — take distinct stances along two axes: (1) whether Rosh Hashanah and *yovel* are the same or different, and (2) whether the horn for Rosh Hashanah is straight (*pashut*) or bent (*kafuf*). *Tanna kamma* holds them the same and straight; Rabbi Yehuda holds them different (Rosh Hashanah bent, *yovel* straight); Rabbi Levi holds them the same and both bent.

## Bent vs Straight

### Core Debate

The *gemara* now asks *be-mai kamifligi* — what exactly is the underlying disagreement between the *tanna kamma* and the others regarding the Rosh Hashanah *shofar*? Both sides agree that the *shofar* must work; the dispute is about the preferred shape. The *gemara* frames the answer in terms of the symbolic meaning each shape carries for the day of Rosh Hashanah.

### Straight Symbolism

One view holds that *be-Rosh Hashanah kama de-pashit inish dateih tfei ma'alei* — on Rosh Hashanah, the more one's *da'at* is directed straight, the better. The idea is that Rosh Hashanah is fundamentally about approaching God directly — not about self-abnegation or prostration, but about bringing one's prayers and one's very self straight to God. A straight horn symbolizes and embodies that direct, forward-directed orientation.

### Bent Symbolism

The opposing view holds *kama de-khayif inish dateih tfei ma'alei* — the more one is bent over, the better. This conception of Rosh Hashanah emphasizes supplication, self-negation, a sense of being weighed down by one's inadequacy, the posture of one who comes before the King not in confidence but in humility and contrition. A bent horn embodies that downward, inward orientation of *teshuvah* and shame.

By contrast, those who hold that Rosh Hashanah calls for a straight horn would agree that *be-ta'aniyot kama de-khayif inish dateih tfei ma'alei* — on fast days, the bent posture is appropriate, since fast days are precisely about confronting sin and communal guilt. On Yom Kippur, meanwhile, the *yovel* aspect — the proclamation of freedom — calls for a straight, outward, announcing posture: *ve-Yom Kippurim kama de-pashit inish dateih tfei ma'alei*.

### Tanna Kamma

The *tanna kamma* thus holds that Rosh Hashanah is best approached with a *pashut*, straight, direct orientation toward God. This is not the day of self-flagellation and guilt — that belongs to the fast days. Rosh Hashanah is the day of coronation, of drawing close, of presenting oneself to the King in a posture of dignity and directness. The *tanna kamma*'s preference for a straight horn on Rosh Hashanah reflects this theological orientation.

## Shofar Direction

### Animal Symbolism

The discussion of straight versus bent horns intersects with the question of which animal the horn comes from — and each animal carries its own symbolic weight. A ram's horn evokes the *akeidah*, the binding of Yitzchak, and all that entails: self-sacrifice, surrender, the willingness to give everything up. It evokes a posture of submission and the negation of self before God. An ibex (mountain goat) horn, with its proud, outward-reaching form, projects a very different imagery — directness, confidence, even pride. The *gemara* focuses on the *pashut-kafuf* axis, but the animal dimension reinforces the same symbolic contrast.

### Direction Question

There is a deeper question underlying this entire discussion: to whom or toward what is the *shofar* directed? Is the *shofar* directed *to you* — meaning it is a call addressed to the listener, meant to awaken the hearer to repentance and fear — or is it directed *to God* — meaning it is part of Israel's prayer, functioning as an instrument through which we send our supplications upward? If the *shofar* is directed to you, then the bent posture makes perfect sense for Rosh Hashanah: the *shofar* breaks through your self-satisfaction, bends you, induces fear and reflection. But if the *shofar* is directed to God — if it is the mechanism of *zikaron* before God — then the straight, direct, upward-directed shape is more fitting. Rashi's commentary implies this second understanding.

### Tosafot Symbolic

Tosafot engages at length with the question of how we *poskin* — how we rule in practice on the question of bent versus straight. Tosafot concludes that this is presumably a matter of *lechatchila* only — a symbolic preference about the ideal form of the *shofar* — and not a question of strict *halakha* that would render one's obligation unfulfilled if not observed. Tosafot does note, however, that one could read the discussion as *halakha le-ma'aseh*, such that using the wrong shape would mean one has not properly fulfilled the *mitzvah* — which is hard to accept in light of the opening *mishna* of this section, which declares that all *shofarot* are *kasher*. The normative conclusion is therefore that the preference for one shape or another is a matter of *lechatchila* and symbolic significance, not a *me'akeiv* disqualification. Nevertheless, the underlying question of what the *shofar* is fundamentally about — a summons to *teshuvah* directed inward at the listener, or an instrument of prayer directed outward toward God — is far from merely symbolic. It goes to the heart of how one understands the *mitzvah* of *shofar* on Rosh Hashanah.
