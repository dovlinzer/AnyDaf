# Zevachim 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Piggul Intro

### Piggul Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַשּׁוֹחֵט, וְהַמְקַבֵּל, וְהַמְהַלֵּךְ, וְהַזּוֹרֵק; לֶאֱכוֹל דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל, וּלְהַקְטִיר דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לְהַקְטִיר; כְּזַיִת; חוּץ לִמְקוּמוֹ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – פִּגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת; וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיַּקְרִיב הַמַּתִּיר כְּמִצְוָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **This is the principle:** With regard to **anyone who slaughters** the animal, **or who collects** the blood, **or who conveys** the blood, **or who sprinkles** the blood, with intent **to partake of an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one **partakes** of it, e.g., the meat of the offering, **or to burn an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one **burns** it on the altar, if his intent was to partake of or to burn the measure of **an olive-bulk outside its** designated **area,** the offering is **disqualified, but there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it.** If his intent was to do so **beyond its** designated **time,** the offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it, provided that the permitting factor,** the blood, **is sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva.**

We arrive at the heart of the *piggul* discussion, picking up from the *gemara* at the bottom of 29b, immediately following the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* established the basic framework: *piggul* requires an improper intention during one of the four blood *avodot* — *shechita*, *kabalah*, *holachah*, or *zerikah* — directed toward one of the two categories of *achilot*: either the *achilat ha-dam ha-ra'uy lo* or the *achilat mizbeach* of the blood or the *emurim*, with the intent to consume or offer them at the wrong time. A thought about the wrong place renders the sacrifice *pasul* but does not produce *piggul*.

### Avodot & Achilot

The *mishnah* thus requires both a specific type of *avodah* and a specific type of *achilah* to be implicated. The four *avodot* of the blood — *shechita*, *kabalah*, *holachah*, and *zerikah* — each serve as the locus during which an improper *machshavah* can operate, while the categories of *achilah* define what kind of disqualifying thought is involved.

### Tanna Kamma vs. R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁמַּחְשֶׁבֶת הַזְּמַן קָדְמָה לְמַחְשֶׁבֶת הַמָּקוֹם – פִּגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת. וְאִם מַחְשֶׁבֶת הַמָּקוֹם קָדְמָה לְמַחְשֶׁבֶת הַזְּמַן – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זֶה וָזֶה – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said** that **this is the principle:** In **any** case **where the intent** with regard to **the time preceded the intent** with regard to **the area,** the offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it. And if the intent** with regard to **the area preceded the intent** with regard to **the time,** the offering is **disqualified and there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it. And the Rabbis say:** In both **this** case **and that** case, the offering is **disqualified and there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it.**

The *mishnah* then presents the dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda. The *tanna kamma* holds that an invalidating thought of *chutz li-mkomo* or *shelo lishmo* by a *chatas* prevents the sacrifice from becoming *piggul* even if that bad thought arrives after an initial *piggul* thought. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: identity is established by the first thought. If the first thought was something other than *piggul* — such as *chutz li-mkomo* — the sacrifice is already *pasul* and can never become *piggul*. But if the *piggul* thought came first, a subsequent invalidating thought cannot strip it of its *piggul* status.

## Ilfa's Position

### Ilfa: Two Avodot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר אִילְפָא: מַחְלוֹקֶת בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת; אֲבָל בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל עֵירוּב מַחְשָׁבוֹת הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the disagreement between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis concerning a case where one has intent with regard to both time and area, **Ilfa says:** There is **a disagreement** only **with regard to two** sacrificial **rites,** i.e., where each intent was held during a different rite, **but** if both were held **in** the course of **one rite, all agree** that **it constitutes a combination of intentions,** and the offering is not rendered *piggul*.

Ilfa opens the *gemara*'s analysis: *machloket bi-shtei avodot* — the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *chachamim* is specifically about a case involving two separate *avodot*. For example, one had a *piggul* thought during *shechita* and a *chutz li-mkomo* thought during *zerikah*, or vice versa. In such a case, Rabbi Yehuda says the first thought establishes the sacrifice's identity and the second cannot override it, while the *chachamim* hold that any *pasul* thought undermines *piggul* status.

### One Avodah Not Piggul

But *aval be-avodah achat divrei ha-kol eiruv machshavot havei* — if both thoughts occurred during one and the same *avodah*, even Rabbi Yehuda concedes that this constitutes *eiruv machshavot*, a bundling of thoughts, which prevents *piggul*. Rabbi Yehuda's position is only operative when the *piggul* thought has clearly been established first, at which point a later bad thought cannot interfere. His claim is not that any sequence of thoughts during a single *avodah* can lock in *piggul* status; that more aggressive reading is rejected, according to Ilfa, by Rabbi Yehuda himself.

## R. Yochanan Position

### Sequential Within One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת מַחְלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even** if the intentions were both held **in** the course of **one rite,** there is still **a disagreement.**

Rabbi Yochanan disputes Ilfa: *af be-avodah achat machloket* — even when both thoughts occur during a single *avodah*, the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *chachamim* still applies. The reason is that even within a single *avodah* there can be a genuine sequential ordering of thoughts. As one is performing the *shechita*, one might first think *chutz li-zmano* and then, in the next moment, think *chutz li-mkomo* — both during the same act, yet one preceding the other.

### First Thought Locks

According to Rabbi Yochanan's reading, Rabbi Yehuda maintains that so long as the *piggul* thought came first — even if the next thought about *chutz li-mkomo* followed immediately and all of it occurred within the single act of *shechita* — the sacrifice is already established as *piggul* and cannot be dislodged from that status. The question of whether such sequential thoughts within one *avodah* can truly be treated as discrete thus becomes the crux of the Ilfa–Rabbi Yochanan dispute.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְאִילְפָא – מִדְּרֵישָׁא בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת, סֵיפָא נָמֵי בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – רֵישָׁא בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת, וְסֵיפָא בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the mishna is understandable **according to Ilfa, since the first clause** of the mishna: If one slaughtered the animal in silence with no specific intent and collected…beyond its designated time, etc., is referring to different intentions held **in** the course of **two** sacrificial **rites,** one can assume that **the latter clause** is referring **to two rites as well. But according to Rabbi Yoḥanan,** could it be that **the first clause** is referring **to two rites, and the latter clause** is referring **to** only **one rite?**

The *gemara* then raises a textual consideration: according to Ilfa, the latter section of the *mishnah* — which formulates the case of *le-echol kezayit ba-chutz kezayit li-machar* — parallels the opening section of the *mishnah* and therefore must likewise concern two separate *avodot*. This is coherent for Ilfa: the *mishnah* remains internally consistent, always discussing cases across distinct *avodot*. For Rabbi Yochanan, however, the opening section discusses two separate *avodot* while the later section can encompass thoughts expressed even within a single *avodah* — whether one or two *avodot*, Rabbi Yehuda's principle applies as long as the *piggul* thought came first.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין; רֵישָׁא – בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת, סֵיפָא – בֵּין בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת בֵּין בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Yes,** it can be explained that **the first clause** is referring **to two** sacrificial **rites,** while **the latter clause,** i.e., the disagreement between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis, applies **to both one rite and two rites.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, זֶה הַכְּלָל: אִם מַחְשֶׁבֶת הַזְּמַן קָדְמָה לְמַחְשֶׁבֶת הַמָּקוֹם – פִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי ״זֶה הַכְּלָל״. אֶלָּא לְאִילְפָא – מַאי ״זֶה הַכְּלָל״? קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a proof: **We learned** in the mishna: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **this is the principle: If the intent** with regard to **the time preceded the intent** with regard to **the area,** the offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it. Granted, according to Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who says that the disagreement applies even to one rite, **this** is the reason **that** the *tanna* **teaches: This is the principle,** indicating that the principle is broad. **But according to Ilfa, what** is added by the phrase: **This is the principle?** The Gemara responds: This indeed poses **a difficulty** to the opinion of Ilfa.

The *gemara* then cites the formulation of Rabbi Yehuda's own *klal* in the *mishnah*: *im machshevet ha-zman kadmah le-machshevet ha-makom, piggul ve-chayavin alav karet* — if the thought about time preceded the thought about place, it is *piggul* and one is liable for *karet*. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the phrase *zeh ha-klal* signals a broad generalization covering multiple scenarios, whether across two *avodot* or within one — all governed by the single principle that the first thought locks in the sacrifice's identity. According to Ilfa, however, the *zeh ha-klal* applies only to the case of two separate *avodot*, and there is nothing broader being stated. The *gemara* acknowledges this as a genuine difficulty for Ilfa's reading.

## Temurah Parallel

### Tmura Olah Shelamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: ״הֲרֵי זוֹ תְּמוּרַת עוֹלָה תְּמוּרַת שְׁלָמִים״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ תְּמוּרַת עוֹלָה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Temura* 25b): If one had two animals standing before him, one a burnt offering and one a peace offering, and he said with regard to another non-sacred animal of his: **This** animal **is hereby the substitute of** the **burnt offering, the substitute of** the **peace offering,** then **that** animal **is the substitute of** the **burnt offering;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *gemara* now draws a comparison to a case in *Temurah*: if one declared *harei zeh tmurat olah tmurat shelamim* — this animal is an exchange for an *olah* and an exchange for a *shelamim* — is it governed by the first word (*olah*), or is it a hybrid? This parallel is directly relevant to the question at hand: when one utters two distinct designations in sequence, do we treat the first as establishing identity or do we treat the combined utterance as a single packaged statement?

### Meir vs. Yossi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אִם לְכָךְ נִתְכַּוֵּון תְּחִילָּה, הוֹאִיל וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר לְהוֹצִיא שְׁתֵּי שֵׁמוֹת כְּאַחַת – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין; וְאִם מִשֶּׁאָמַר ״הֲרֵי זוֹ תְּמוּרַת עוֹלָה״, וְנִמְלַךְ וְאָמַר ״הֲרֵי זוֹ תְּמוּרַת שְׁלָמִים״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said: If that was his intent from the outset,** i.e., his intent was that this animal be the substitute of both a burnt offering and a peace offering, **since it is impossible to produce two designations simultaneously** and one designation must precede the other, **his statement is effective,** and the animal is the substitute of both a burnt offering and a peace offering. **But if** it was only **after he said: This** animal **is hereby the substitute of** the **burnt offering,** that **he reconsidered and said: This** animal **is hereby the substitute of** the **peace offering,** then **that** animal **is the substitute of a burnt offering** alone.

The *mishnah* in *Temurah* records the dispute: *harei zu tmurat olah — divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir holds that the first words out of one's mouth establish its identity as an *olah*, and the subsequent mention of *shelamim* cannot alter that. Rabbi Yossi takes a more contextual approach: *im le-kach nitkaven techilah, ho'il ve-ee efshar le-hotzi shtei shemot ke-achat, devarav kayamin* — if the speaker's original intent was to create a hybrid *olah-shelamim*, and since it is physically impossible to pronounce two words simultaneously, we follow the intent and recognize it as a hybrid. However, if one genuinely changed one's mind after saying *tmurat olah* and then said *tmurat shelamim*, Rabbi Yossi agrees: it is an *olah* and the second statement is disregarded. Tosafot adds a further refinement within Rabbi Yossi's position: the presence or absence of a pause between the two declarations is a meaningful contextual clue — a pause within *toch kdei dibbur* suggests unified intent, while a longer interval suggests genuine reconsideration.

### Abaye: Packaged Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״הֲרֵי זוֹ תְּמוּרַת עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים״, מַהוּ? ״לַחֲצוֹת״, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If one expressed the two intentions simultaneously and said: **This is hereby the substitute of a burnt offering and a peace offering, what is** the *halakha*? Additionally, if one specified his intention **to split** the animal between a burnt offering and a peace offering, but mentioned the burnt offering first, **what is** the *halakha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּהָא וַדַּאי מוֹדֶה רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: In this** case, **Rabbi Meir certainly concedes** that both types of sanctity apply to the animal.

The *gemara* poses a sharper case: *harei zu tmurat olah u-shelamim* — what if one said "an exchange for an *olah* and a *shelamim*," using the conjunctive *vav*? Or alternatively, *lachatzot* — declaring that both designations take effect simultaneously at a fixed moment, so that no sequential priority can be established. Abaye holds that in this case, Rabbi Meir would certainly agree that the designation is treated as a packaged hybrid: *be-ha vadai modeh Rabbi Meir*. When the speaker's words unambiguously signal that both are intended as a unified whole, even Rabbi Meir concedes.

### Rava: First Word

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אֲמַר: עֲדַיִן הִיא מַחְלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: It is still a disagreement;** Rabbi Meir still holds in this case that since he mentioned the burnt offering first, only that sanctity applies to the animal.

Rava disagrees: *adayin hi machloket*. Even when the formulation packages the two designations together, Rava maintains that since the word *olah* appears first and the two categories are mutually exclusive, we simply apply the *olah* designation. The first word takes precedence regardless of how closely the second was bundled with it.

### Shechita Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא לְאַבָּיֵי: לְדִידָךְ דְּאָמְרַתְּ בְּהָא וַדַּאי מוֹדֶה רַבִּי מֵאִיר; הֲרֵי שְׁחִיטָה – (דלכי) [דִּכִי] ״לַחֲצוֹת״ דָּמֵי, וּפְלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Abaye: According to your** opinion, **as you say** that **in this** case **Rabbi Meir certainly concedes,** one may raise an objection from the mishna, which describes a case where one performs **slaughter** with intent to eat one olive-bulk of the offering beyond its time and one olive-bulk outside its area, **which is comparable to** a case where one says: **To split,** since both intentions are held during the singular act of slaughter. **And** nevertheless, Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis **disagree,** and Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that one attends only to the first statement, renders the animal *piggul*.

The *gemara* now draws this dispute back into the *piggul* context. Rava challenges Abaye: according to you, if someone clearly packages two thoughts together at a single moment, Rabbi Meir agrees it is all one statement — so why does Rabbi Yehuda still maintain that a *piggul* thought takes priority during *shechita*? Is not *shechita* precisely such a case where everything is packaged together at the conclusion of the act?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ אֵינָהּ לִשְׁחִיטָה אֶלָּא לְבַסּוֹף?! יֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׁחִיטָה מִתְּחִילָּה וְעַד סוֹף; וּמִשְׁנָתֵינוּ – דְּאָמַר: סִימָן רִאשׁוֹן חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, סִימָן שֵׁנִי חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him: Do you hold** that **slaughter is** considered to have been performed **only at** its **end,** such that it takes effect in a single instant? Actually, **slaughter is** accomplished progressively, **from beginning to end, and our mishna** is referring to a case **where one says:** I slaughter the **first *siman*,** the gullet or the windpipe, with intent to consume the offering **beyond its** designated **time,** and the **second *siman*** with intent to consume it **outside its** designated **area.**

The *gemara* explores this via the principle of *yeshna la-shechita mi-techila ve-ad sof* versus *ein la-shechita ela le-basof*. If *shechita* is only legally consummated at its final moment, then all thoughts entertained during the cutting — first one, then another — converge at that final instant, like saying both things take effect *lachatzot*. In that case, they would be treated as packaged together, and according to Abaye, even Rabbi Meir would concede there is no sequential priority. But the *gemara* counters: no, the correct view is *yeshna la-shechita mi-techila ve-ad sof* — the act of *shechita* is a process, operative throughout. The *mishnah* itself reflects this, discussing a case where one *siman* was cut with a *chutz li-zmano* thought and the second *siman* with a *chutz li-mkomo* thought. Each stage of the cutting constitutes a distinct moment within the *avodah*, and so the two thoughts are genuinely sequential, not packaged.

### Discrete Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי קְמִיצָה – (דלכי) [דִּכִי] לַחֲצוֹת דָּמֵי, וּפְלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** what about the **removal of a handful** from a meal offering with intent to eat one olive-bulk beyond its time and one outside of its area, **which is comparable to** a case where one says: **To split,** since both intentions are held during the removal, **and** nevertheless, Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis **disagree** with regard to it in tractate *Menaḥot* (12a)?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי – שֶׁהִקְטִיר קוֹמֶץ מִנְחָה חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, קוֹמֶץ לְבוֹנָה חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Since the mishna does not state such a dispute explicitly, one can say that their disagreement is not with regard to the removal of the handful but with regard to the burning of the two permitting factors of the meal offering, the handful and the frankincense. Accordingly, **there too,** the case is such **that** the priest **burned the handful of** the **meal offering** with intent to eat it **beyond its** designated **time,** and he burned **the handful of frankincense** with intent to eat the meal offering **outside its** designated **area.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי קוֹמֶץ דְּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא – דְּלֵיכָּא לְבוֹנָה בַּהֲדֵיהּ, וּפְלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara persists: **But** what about **the handful from a meal offering of a sinner, which has no frankincense with it, and** still Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis **disagree?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא פְּלִיגִי. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר פְּלִיגִי, פְּלִיגִי בִּפְסִיעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: In that case **they do not disagree. Rav Ashi says:** Even **if you say** that **they disagree** in that case, one may say that **they disagree** specifically **with regard to** a case where the priest had different intentions during his **steps** toward the altar while holding the handful. Consequently, even this rite is divisible into separate parts.

This leads to the more precise formulation: it is only when the sub-acts within a single *avodah* are discrete and separately identifiable that we treat the associated thoughts as sequential. Tosafot makes this point explicitly: we do not say that moving the knife one millimeter and then another millimeter constitutes two distinct acts — the motion is continuous and undivided. By contrast, the two *simanim* of the *behemah* — the trachea and the esophagus — are each identifiable as a separate anatomical stage, and therefore the thoughts corresponding to each *siman* are treated as genuinely sequential.

The same logic applies to *holachah*: each individual step (*psi'ah*) is a discrete act. Therefore, one might hold a *piggul* thought at one step and a *chutz li-mkomo* thought at the next step, and Rabbi Yehuda would say the *piggul* is already established. By contrast, the act of *kmitza* — taking the handful — is treated as a unified act consummated at its endpoint, so any two thoughts held during the process of *kmitza* would be packaged together and would constitute *eiruv machshavot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי מַתְנֵי כִּדְאַבָּיֵי, רַב הוּנָא בַּר נָתָן מַתְנֵי כִּדְרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi** would **teach in accordance with** the opinion **of Abaye** that in a case where one explicitly mentions both intentions at once, Rabbi Meir concedes that both sanctities apply to the animal. **Rav Huna bar Natan** would **teach in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava** that even in such a case, Rabbi Meir considers only the first sanctity mentioned.

The *gemara* raises the case of a *minchat chotei*, which has no *levonah*. Without the *levonah*, there is only a single discrete element — the *komatz* of flour — and so one cannot distinguish two separate discrete stages within the offering-up. The *gemara* initially wonders whether Rabbi Yehuda's rule even applies in such a case, and concludes: *lo peligi* — the debate is not stated for *minchat chotei*. Rav Ashi adds: *im timtza lomar peligi, peligi bi-fsi'ot* — if one were to insist they do debate even there, the scenario of debate would have to involve the steps of *holachah*, since that is where discrete, identifiable sub-acts occur within a single *avodah*.

The connection to Rabbi Meir's position is then drawn explicitly. Rav Dimi transmitted: *Rabbi Meir b'shitat Rabbi Yehuda amrah, d'amar tefos lashon rishon* — Rabbi Meir's rule in *Temurah* that the first word establishes identity is stated in the tradition of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *tefos lashon rishon* as his operative principle in *piggul*. Rabbi Yochanan, however — as transmitted by Rabba bar bar Chanah — notes that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yossi can be brought quite close to one another: the debate between them is not a sweeping philosophical disagreement about whether to follow the first word versus intent, but a narrow dispute about how to interpret an ambiguous declaration of *tmurat olah tmurat shelamim*. When the intent is explicit — either through the formulation *techol zo ve-achar kach techol zo* (clearly sequential) or *lo techol zo ela im ken chalah zo* (clearly simultaneous) — there is no dispute. The debate arises only in the middle case, where the formulation is ambiguous and the parties disagree about what to infer from it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אֲמַר: רַבִּי מֵאִיר – בְּשִׁיטַת רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֲמָרָהּ, דְּאָמַר: תְּפוֹס לָשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said:** When **Rabbi Meir** said that if one says: This animal is hereby the substitute of a burnt offering, the substitute of a peace offering, then the animal is the substitute of a burnt offering, he **said this according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that in general, the *halakha* is to **attend** only to the **first statement.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, וְהָא רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּי מַגְּעַתְּ לְהוּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, לָא פְּלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Dimi: **But doesn't Rabba bar bar Ḥana** say that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: When you consider their** opinions, **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei do not disagree with one another,** i.e., neither holds that one attends only to the first statement?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי?! וְהָא מִיפְלָג פְּלִיגִי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּלִיגִי בְּמַאי דִּפְלִיגִי, וְלָא פְּלִיגִי בְּמַאי דְּלָא פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Dimi objected: **And** is it so that **they do not disagree? But don't they disagree** explicitly in the mishna cited earlier? Abaye **said to him: They disagree where they disagree,** i.e., that specific case, **but they do not disagree where they do not disagree,** i.e., the underlying principle. Accordingly, one must understand the basis of their dispute in another manner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר ״תָּחוּל זוֹ וְאַחַר כָּךְ (אָמַר) תָּחוּל זוֹ״ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לָא חָיְילָא; ״לֹא תָּחוּל זוֹ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן חָלָה זוֹ״ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל חָיְילָא;
>
> **Translation:** This is **as Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: All,** i.e., Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei, **concede that** in a case **where one says: Let this** sanctity of a burnt offering **take effect and afterward let that** sanctity of a peace offering **take effect, everyone agrees** that the sanctity of a peace offering **does not take effect,** because the sanctity of the burnt offering took effect first. Likewise, if one said: **This** sanctity **shall not take effect unless that** sanctity **applies, everyone agrees** that the sanctity of a peace offering **takes effect** as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי, דְּאָמַר: ״תְּמוּרַת עוֹלָה תְּמוּרַת שְׁלָמִים״. רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: מִדַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר ״תְּמוּרַת עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים״ וַאֲמַר ״תְּמוּרַת עוֹלָה תְּמוּרַת שְׁלָמִים״ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִיהְדָּר קָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree** with regard to a case **where one says:** This animal is hereby **a substitute for a burnt offering, a substitute for a peace offering. Rabbi Meir holds: Since** if he wanted both sanctities to take effect, **he should have said: A substitute for a burnt offering and a peace offering, but he said** instead: **A substitute for a burnt offering, a substitute for a peace offering, learn from** his language that he originally intended for it to be a burnt offering, and he then **retracted** his first intention.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – אִי אֲמַר: ״תְּמוּרַת עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים״ – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא פַּלְגָא תְּמוּרַת עוֹלָה וּפַלְגָא תְּמוּרַת שְׁלָמִים; לְהָכִי אֲמַר: ״תְּמוּרַת עוֹלָה תְּמוּרַת שְׁלָמִים״ – לְמֵימְרָא דְּכוּלַּהּ עוֹלָה וְכוּלַּהּ שְׁלָמִים הָוְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei** holds that **if he** had **said: A substitute for a burnt offering and a peace offering, I would say** that he means that **half** the animal should be **a substitute for a burnt offering and half a substitute for a peace offering. He therefore says: A substitute for a burnt offering, a substitute for a peace offering; that is to say that all of it is a burnt offering and all of it is a peace offering.** In any event, according to this explanation, Rabbi Meir does not necessarily hold that one attends only to the first statement.

## Levi's Question

### One Kezayit Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דִּבְעָא מִינֵּיהּ לֵוִי מֵרַבִּי: חִישֵּׁב לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת לְמָחָר בַּחוּץ, מַהוּ? אָמַר לוֹ: זוֹ שְׁאֵילָה! עֵירוּב מַחְשָׁבוֹת הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from that **dilemma which Levi raised before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: If **one had intent to consume an olive-bulk the next day outside** its designated area, **what is** the *halakha*? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: This** is an excellent **question.** The answer is that even Rabbi Yehuda concedes that this **constitutes a combination of intentions** and that the animal is not rendered *piggul*.

The *gemara* now presents a question posed by Levi to Rabbi: *chishav le-echol kezayit li-machar ba-chutz, mahu?* What if the *kohen* thought about a single *kezayit* with both a *chutz li-zmano* and a *chutz li-mkomo* intention simultaneously? According to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that the first thought establishes identity, the *piggul* thought (*li-machar*) came first — does this constitute *piggul*? Or, since it is a single *kezayit* with a single unified thought about both violations, does Rabbi Yehuda agree that it is *eiruv machshavot* and not *piggul*?

### R. Shimon Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי: לֹא מִשְׁנָתֵינוּ הִיא?! לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת בַּחוּץ כְּזַיִת לְמָחָר; כְּזַיִת לְמָחָר כְּזַיִת בַּחוּץ; כַּחֲצִי זַיִת בַּחוּץ כַּחֲצִי זַיִת לְמָחָר; כַּחֲצִי זַיִת לְמָחָר כַּחֲצִי זַיִת בַּחוּץ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. הָא אִידַּךְ – עֵירוּב מַחְשָׁבוֹת הָוֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **said before him:** How is this an excellent question; **is it not our mishna?** As we learned in the mishna: If one expressed intent **to eat an olive-bulk outside** its designated area and **an olive-bulk the next day,** or **an olive-bulk the next day** and **an olive-bulk outside** its designated area, or **half an olive-bulk outside** its designated area and **half an olive-bulk the next day,** or **half an olive-bulk the next day** and **half an olive-bulk outside** its designated area, the offering is **disqualified and there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it.** Rabbi Yehuda disagrees only with regard to these cases; **but** in **another** case, it could easily be inferred that he concedes that **it constitutes a combination of intentions.**

Rabbi Shimon b'Rabbi challenged Levi's question before his father: *lo mishnateinu hi?* Is this not already resolved by our *mishnah*? The *mishnah* lists cases of *le-echol kezayit ba-chutz kezayit li-machar* — with two separate *kezayitim* — and declares those cases *pasul ve-ein bo karet*, with Rabbi Yehuda disagreeing. If those cases, which are more discrete, are where Rabbi Yehuda says *piggul* is established, then a fortiori the case of one *kezayit* directed to both violations simultaneously would be *eiruv machshavot* by Rabbi Yehuda's own reasoning. Levi's question, Rabbi Shimon implied, is self-evident from the *mishnah* itself.

### Abbreviated Texts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לוֹ: הוּא שָׁאַל בִּי דְּבַר חָכְמָה, וְאַתְּ אָמַרְתָּ מִשְׁנָתֵינוּ?! לְדִידָךְ דְּאַתְנִיתָךְ תַּרְתֵּי – לָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ;
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him:** Levi **asked me a matter of wisdom, and you say our mishna. For you, whom I taught two** phrasings of the mishna, both: An olive-bulk, an olive-bulk, and: An olive-bulk and an olive-bulk, the question is **not difficult,** since my omission of this other case allows you to infer its *halakha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְדִידֵיהּ דְּלָא אַתְנִיתֵיהּ אֶלָּא חֲדָא, וְשַׁמְעִינְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּקָא גָרְסִי תַּרְתֵּי – וְסָבַר: דִּידִי דַּוְוקָא, וְדִידְהוּ עֵירוּב מַחְשָׁבוֹת הָוֵי? אוֹ דִלְמָא דִּידְהוּ דַּוְוקָא, וּלְדִידִי שַׁיּוֹרֵי שַׁיַּיר לִי; וּמִדְּשַׁיַּיר לִי לְדִידִי הָא – שַׁיַּיר לְהוּ לְדִידְהוּ נָמֵי בְּהָךְ?
>
> **Translation:** **For him, whom I taught only one** phrasing, **and** who **heard the Rabbis reciting two,** the question is excellent, since Levi **thought:** Perhaps **my** phrasing is **exact, and their** additional phrasing **constitutes a combination of intentions** according to all opinions. **Or perhaps their** phrasing is **exact, and my** phrasing **omitted** this case. **And** if **my** phrasing **omitted this** case, perhaps **their** phrasing **also omitted that** other case, even though it is subject to disagreement.

Rabbi responded to his son: *hu she'al bi devar chochmah* — Levi asked me a question rooted in pure logical reasoning, without relying on the text. And *ve-atah amarta mishnateinu* — you answered by pointing to the *mishnah*. The reason Levi could not resolve his question from the *mishnah* is that the text he had received from Rabbi was abbreviated: Rabbi had taught Levi only the case of *kezayit kezayit* (two separate *kezayitim*, without the conjunctive *vav*), while the students in the *beit midrash* had received both *kezayit kezayit* and *kezayit ve-kezayit* (with the *vav*). Levi did not know whether his text was the complete text — in which case a more bundled formulation would obviously be agreed-upon *eiruv machshavot* — or whether the full list included the *vav* case as well, in which case Rabbi Yehuda disputes even that, and perhaps even the single *kezayit* case remains in dispute.

---

## Final Synthesis

### Established Status

The picture that emerges from Rabbi Yehuda's position across all these cases is consistent: once the *piggul* thought has been registered — whether across two *avodot* or, according to Rabbi Yochanan, within discrete stages of a single *avodah* — that status is established and cannot be undone by a subsequent *pasul* thought. The question "when are thoughts sufficiently bundled together that the *piggul* thought never gets to stand on its own?" is what drives the remainder of the analysis.

## Text Girsa

### Inferring Girsa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵי אַתְנְיֵיהּ? אִילֵּימָא ״כְּזַיִת״ ״וּכְזַיִת״ אַתְנְיֵיהּ – הַאי לָאו שִׁיּוּרָא הוּא! אֶלָּא ״כְּזַיִת״ ״כְּזַיִת״ אַתְנְיֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And which** phrasing **did** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **teach** Levi? **If we say** that **he taught him: An olive-bulk and an olive-bulk,** i.e., that Rabbi Yehuda still holds the terms to be distinct despite the conjunction, **this is not an omission,** since one can infer *a fortiori* that the same applies to: An olive-bulk, an olive-bulk. **Rather,** it must be that **he taught him: An olive-bulk, an olive-bulk,** so that it was unclear to Levi what the *halakha* would be if one used the conjunction: And.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ ״כְּזַיִת״ ״וּכְזַיִת״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, why did Levi raise the dilemma with regard to the phrasing: The next day outside? **Let him raise the dilemma** with regard to the phrasing: **An olive-bulk and an olive-bulk.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבַר: אִיבְעֵי מִינֵּיהּ חֲדָא – דְּשָׁמַעְנָא תַּרְתֵּי. דְּאִי בָּעֵינָא ״כְּזַיִת״ ״וּכְזַיִת״ – הָא נִיחָא אִי אָמַר לִי כְּלָלָא, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן ״כְּזַיִת לְמָחָר בַּחוּץ״; אֶלָּא אִי אָמַר לִי פְּרָטָא – אַכַּתִּי ״כְּזַיִת לְמָחָר בַּחוּץ״ קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Levi **thought: I will raise one dilemma before him so as to learn two** *halakhot*. **As, if I ask** only with regard to: **An olive-bulk and an olive-bulk, it will work out well if he says to me** that Rabbi Yehuda concedes that they constitute one **general** term, as this would apply **all the more so** if one said: **An olive-bulk the next day outside,** which is even more unified. **But if he says to me** that each term constitutes **a separate** term according to Rabbi Yehuda, then **I will still** need to **raise the dilemma** with regard to the case of: **An olive-bulk the next day outside.** I will therefore inquire with regard to the latter case.

The *gemara* now attempts to resolve the question of the *mishnah*'s correct *girsa*: was the text *kezayit kezayit* (without *vav*) or *kezayit ve-kezayit* (with *vav*)? The inference proceeds as follows: if the text Levi received was *kezayit ve-kezayit* and the students' text was *kezayit kezayit*, then Levi's text already implicitly covers the more discrete case — and what the students had would not represent any additional information. There would be nothing "abbreviated" about Levi's version. It must therefore be that Levi's text was *kezayit kezayit* and the students' text added the *kezayit ve-kezayit* case, since only then does it make sense that Levi was working from an abbreviated list and could not infer what Rabbi Yehuda would say about a more bundled formulation.

### One Q, Two Answers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי – הָתִינַח אִי אָמַר לֵיהּ ״כְּזַיִת לְמָחָר בַּחוּץ״ פְּרָטָא, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן ״כְּזַיִת״ ״וּכְזַיִת״; אֶלָּא אִי אָמַר לֵיהּ כְּלָלָא, אַכַּתִּי ״כְּזַיִת״ ״וּכְזַיִת״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so, now too, it works out well if he says to him** that Rabbi Yehuda maintains that the wording: **An olive-bulk the next day outside,** is constituted of **separate** terms, as this would apply **all the more so** if one said: **An olive-bulk and an olive-bulk,** which is less unified. **But if he says to him** that it is one **general** term according to Rabbi Yehuda, then **he will still** need to **raise the dilemma** with regard to the case of: **An olive-bulk and an olive-bulk.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן מִרְתָּח רָתַח:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **If** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi answered in **this** manner, i.e., that Rabbi Yehuda concedes that: An olive-bulk the next day outside, is considered one term, he would unwittingly provide the answer to the other question as well. As, if Rabbi Yehuda had also conceded with regard to: An olive-bulk and an olive-bulk, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi would **become angry** with Levi for asking the wrong question and say:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא ״כְּזַיִת״ ״וּכְזַיִת״ כְּלָלָא, ״כְּזַיִת לְמָחָר בַּחוּץ״ מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Now** that Rabbi Yehuda concedes that even: **An olive-bulk and an olive-bulk,** is one **general** term, **is** it **necessary** to inquire with regard to: **An olive-bulk the next day outside?**

## Final Synthesis…

### Bundling Exception

The upshot is that *kezayit ve-kezayit* — two *kezayitim* connected by a *vav* — is indeed a case where Rabbi Yehuda maintains that the first thought establishes *piggul* status and the second thought does not override it. The *vav* is not sufficient to bundle them into a unified, packaged statement. The only configuration that would truly constitute *eiruv machshavot* and prevent *piggul* even according to Rabbi Yehuda is when the entire thought concerns a single *kezayit* — thinking about one portion of food with both a *chutz li-zmano* and a *chutz li-mkomo* intention at once.

### Separate Kezayitim

When two discrete *kezayitim* are involved — even with the conjunctive *vav* linking the two thoughts — Rabbi Yehuda treats them as separate, sequential thoughts. The first thought locks in the *piggul* designation, and the second thought, however closely attached in formulation, cannot dislodge it. Similarly, when two discrete stages within a single *avodah* are involved — such as the two *simanim* of *shechita* or consecutive steps in *holachah* — the same logic applies: the thoughts are sequential, not packaged, and the first thought governs.

The conclusion reflects the principle derived from all these cases: according to the *chachamim*, even once *piggul* has been established, a subsequent invalidating thought at a later *avodah* can prevent the sacrifice from retaining *piggul* status. According to Rabbi Yehuda, once *piggul* is established, it cannot be undone — and the only way to prevent *piggul* from ever being established in the first place is to ensure the thoughts are so thoroughly packaged together, referring to a single *kezayit* with unified intent, that the *piggul* thought never stood alone.
