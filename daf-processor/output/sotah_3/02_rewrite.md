# Sotah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kina'ut: Mitzvah/Reshut

### Intro: Kina'ut Framework

The *gemara* on 3a opens by returning to a topic introduced earlier in the *masechet*: the question of *kina'ut* — whether a husband's act of warning or expressing jealousy toward his wife is a *mitzvah*, a *reshut*, or perhaps even an *issur*. The underlying tension is real: on one hand, if there is genuine cause for suspicion, allowing a problematic situation to go entirely unchecked could itself be deeply corrosive to the marriage. On the other hand, introducing unfounded suspicion into the home risks sowing hatred and conflict. These competing considerations reflect not merely technical *halachic* categories but genuine value tensions, and they will animate the discussion throughout this section.

### R. Akiva vs. R. Yishmael

The *gemara* returns to this debate in the form of a *gufa* — going back to the original statements to examine them more closely. The *gufa* presents a series of three paired disputes between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva. First: *v'kina et ishto* — a husband who warns his wife — is a *reshut*, according to Rabbi Yishmael, and a *chova* (obligation), according to Rabbi Akiva. Second: *l'hitama*, a *kohen*'s becoming *tamei* for his deceased sister, is a *reshut* according to Rabbi Yishmael and a *chova* according to Rabbi Akiva. Third: *le'olam bahem ta'avodu* — the verse permitting the permanent enslavement of Canaanite slaves — is a *reshut* according to Rabbi Yishmael and a *chova* according to Rabbi Akiva. Rav Pappa said to Abaye — and some versions have Rav Mesharshiya saying this to Rava — a pointed question: *leima Rabbi Ishmael v'Rabbi Akiva b'chol haTorah kullah hachi paligi* — does this debate extend across the entire Torah? Is it a general methodological dispute between these two schools?

### Methodology: Universal v…

The *gemara*'s question — whether Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva disagree universally or only in these specific cases — can be read in two ways. Rashi reads it rhetorically, as if to say: surely Rabbi Yishmael does not read every commandment in the Torah as a mere *reshut*? *V'asu lahem tzitzit* — permission? *V'sukkot teshvu* — permission? Clearly not. An alternative reading, however, takes the question as genuinely open. There are some *psukim* that are clearly imperative in form or context, and others that are grammatically or contextually ambiguous. *V'kina et ishto*, for instance, is narrative in form, not imperative. *L'hitama* could be read as permissive. The question thus might be: wherever a *pasuk* is genuinely ambiguous as to whether it conveys permission or obligation, does Rabbi Yishmael tend systematically toward *reshut* and Rabbi Akiva toward *chova*? The *gemara*'s answer — *hacha d'krayi paligi*, that there is something specific in each *pasuk* driving them in their respective directions — supports the view that it is the particular features of these verses, rather than a blanket methodological rule, that generate the dispute. Still, as the analysis of each case will show, underlying interpretive commitments are very much at work.

## Rashi's Rhetorical Readi…

### Rashi: Rhetorical Reading

Rashi's position is that the question *leima Rabbi Ishmael v'Rabbi Akiva b'chol haTorah kullah hachi paligi* is posed *b'timyah* — rhetorically, with an element of disbelief. The force of the question is: does Rabbi Yishmael really interpret every commandment as a mere *reshut*? The obvious answer is no, and the rhetorical edge makes that point sharply. *V'asu lahem tzitzit* — is that a *reshut*? *V'sukkot teshvu* — optional? The absurdity of that reading shows that the question cannot possibly be asking whether Rabbi Yishmael has a global preference for *reshut*. According to Rashi, the *gemara* is using the rhetorical form to foreclose the possibility of reading these debates as reflecting a sweeping general principle.

### Alt Reading: Ambiguity

An alternative reading of the *gemara*'s question takes it as non-rhetorical and genuine. On this reading, the *gemara* is asking whether, in cases of genuine textual ambiguity — whether arising from the grammatical form of the verb, as with *v'kina et ishto* (a narrative rather than imperative construction), or from contextual uncertainty, as with *l'hitama* — Rabbi Yishmael consistently tends toward *reshut* while Rabbi Akiva tends toward *chova*. This is not a claim that Rabbi Yishmael reads all of Torah as optional; it is a claim that each *tanna* has an interpretive disposition that shapes how he resolves ambiguous cases. The *gemara*'s answer — *hacha d'krayi paligi*, here the specific features of the verses drive them — does not necessarily refute this reading. It may mean that something in each *pasuk* specifically pushes Rabbi Yishmael one way and Rabbi Akiva another, but that on a different *pasuk* the same dispositions might yield different results. In other words, it is not a universal rule but neither is it random: the underlying hermeneutical commitments of each school are consistently operative wherever ambiguity exists.

## Three Case Studies

### Case 1: Vekina et Ishto

The *gemara* now examines each of the three cases individually. Beginning with *v'kina et ishto*, what is Rabbi Yishmael's reasoning for reading this as a *reshut*? The *gemara* answers that Rabbi Yishmael holds in accordance with the position of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who reasons as follows: since the Torah elsewhere commands *lo tishnah et achicha bilvavecha* — do not harbor hatred toward your fellow in your heart — one might have thought that a husband who suspects his wife should suppress his concerns and remain silent rather than raise them. By speaking up, he risks introducing open hatred into the marriage. Given this concern, one might have thought it was actually *assur* to warn the wife. Since the *pasuk* says *v'avar alav ruach kinah v'kina et ishto*, teaching that he is allowed to act on his jealousy, the most natural first reading is that this constitutes a *reshut* — permission to do something that might otherwise have been forbidden. This context illuminates the genuine value tension embedded in *kina'ut*: the concern about addressing a real problem on one side, and the concern about generating hatred and conflict in the home on the other.

### R. Akiva Responds: Repet…

Rabbi Akiva has a compelling counter-argument. The verse *v'kina et ishto* appears twice in the relevant passage — once in the case of *v'hi nitma'ah* (she became defiled) and once in the case of *v'hi lo nitma'ah* (she did not become defiled). The Torah could have simply stated *v'hi nitma'ah v'hi lo nitma'ah* without repeating *v'kina et ishto* a second time. The repetition, Rabbi Akiva argues, is not incidental: it is there to teach that the act of warning is not merely permitted but obligatory — a *mitzvah*.

### R. Yishmael: Parasha Pri…

Rabbi Yishmael responds by invoking a fundamental hermeneutical principle attributed to his school: *kol parasha she-ne'emra ve-nishnita lo nishnita ela bishvil davar she-nitchadesh bah* — any passage that was stated and then repeated was repeated only in order to convey one new point. Once the Torah needed to address the two possible outcomes — *nitma'ah* and *lo nitma'ah* — it was natural and indeed necessary to repeat the framing context of *v'kina et ishto*. But that repetition was compelled by the structure of the passage, not introduced to add a new level of obligation. One cannot therefore *darshan* the repeated phrase to yield a *chova*.

### Philosophy: Omnisignific…

This exchange crystallizes the deep methodological divide between the two schools. Rabbi Yishmael operates on the principle of *dibra Torah ki-leshon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in the language of human beings. He will certainly interpret verses, but he will not insist that every small redundancy or apparent superfluity carries an independent *halachic* lesson. By contrast, Rabbi Akiva is associated with what scholars have termed "omni-significance": every detail, every repetition, every seemingly extra letter or phrase is there to teach something. He will *darshan* even *kotzo shel yud* — the tip of the letter *yud*. With this difference in mind, the pattern across all three cases becomes clear: it is not that Rabbi Yishmael is committed to *reshut* as a conclusion, and it is not that Rabbi Akiva is committed to *chova* as a conclusion. Rather, Rabbi Akiva's hermeneutic naturally drives him further — wherever there is something extra in a *pasuk*, he will extract an additional level of instruction from it, reaching *chova* where Rabbi Yishmael, declining to *darshan* the apparent surplus, remains at *reshut*. Both schools begin from the same starting point: without the *pasuk*, one might have thought the act was *assur*. The *pasuk* establishes permission. The question is only whether something within the *pasuk* then elevates that permission to an obligation.

### Case 2: Letamei

The second case concerns the *kohen* who becomes *tamei* for a deceased relative, specifically the case of a sister. The verse in *Emor* reads: *ki im l'she'ero hakrov eilav l'imo ul'aviv ul'vno ul'vito ul'achiv* — for his close relative, his mother, father, son, daughter, and brother. It then continues with the sister. The question of *reshut* versus *chova* here has concrete *halachic* stakes. If it is a *reshut*, then a *kohen* who, for example, is *osek b'mitzvah* — engaged in fulfilling another commandment — need not interrupt his activity to become *tamei* for his sister. Likewise, if he wishes to protect his *kehunah* status and there is another person available to attend to the burial, he is exempt. That is the *reshut* position of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, holds that a *kohen* has an obligation to become *tamei* for his close relatives, regardless of competing considerations: it is a *mitzvah*.

### R. Yishmael: Presumption…

Rabbi Yishmael's reasoning proceeds from the structure of the relevant passage. The Torah begins with the general prohibition: *l'nefesh lo yitama b'amav* — a *kohen* shall not defile himself for any of his people. This establishes a baseline presumption that *tum'ah* is forbidden. Given this presumption, when the Torah carves out exceptions for close relatives, the natural reading is that it is granting permission — overriding the prohibition for those specific cases. The *pasuk*, on this reading, lifts the *issur* and grants *reshut*, but does not go further to create an affirmative obligation.

### R. Akiva: Extra Phrase A…

Rabbi Akiva responds with a textual argument. Before it ever said *lo yitama*, the verse had already enumerated the relatives for whom the *kohen* may become *tamei*: *ki im l'she'ero hakrov eilav l'imo ul'aviv ul'vno ul'vito ul'achiv*. The list already established permission; it could then simply have said *ul'achoto* (and for his sister) without any additional verb. Why does it then say *lah yitama* — *to her* shall he become *tamei* — adding an explicit verbal form? That extra phrase, Rabbi Akiva argues, elevates permission to obligation: it is a *chova*.

### R. Yishmael: Body vs. Li…

Rabbi Yishmael's response is sharp: the word *lah* (to her) is not extra in the way Rabbi Akiva supposes — it is present to teach a specific and independent *halacha*: *lama tama ve-eino mitamei le-evareiha* — he may become *tamei* for her as a whole body, but not for her individual limbs. If a limb falls off during her lifetime, the *kohen* may not become *tamei* for the burial of that limb. Once the Torah needed to write *lah* to convey this distinction, it naturally wrote the associated verb *yitama* as well — not to teach an obligation, but simply because the phrase would read awkwardly without it. Rabbi Akiva presses further: if it was only *lah* that was needed, the Torah could have written *lah* alone. Why include the full verb *yitama*? Rabbi Yishmael replies by citing the principle of *parashat she-ne'emra ve-nishnita*: the phrase was already needed to convey the body-versus-limbs distinction, so the verb accompanying it carries no additional weight. The pattern of the debate is thus entirely consistent with the first case: Rabbi Akiva extracts obligation from what appears extra; Rabbi Yishmael explains that apparent surplus is structurally necessary for another purpose.

### Case 3: Le'olam Bahem

The third case is the most challenging from a values perspective. The verse *le'olam bahem ta'avodu* — "you shall work them forever" — refers to Canaanite slaves. Rabbi Yishmael reads it as a *reshut*: one is permitted to keep them enslaved permanently. Rabbi Akiva reads it as a *chova*: one is obligated to do so and is not permitted to free them. The *reshut*/*chova* distinction in the context of *sotah* already raises significant questions of competing values — the concern about addressing genuine wrongdoing on one hand, and the potential for injustice or strife on the other. But this third case is harder still. Rabbi Akiva's position of *chova* seems to say not only that slavery is permitted but that it is mandated — that to free a Canaanite slave is *assur*.

### Values: Slavery Context

It is worth dwelling on the values dimension here. A compelling and textually grounded reading of the Torah's treatment of slavery is not that the Torah endorses or celebrates the institution, but that it accepted the reality of a world in which slavery had existed for millennia, and sought to regulate and humanize it — to put Israel on a path toward something better. This reading gains support from the very structure of *Sefer Shemot*: immediately following the *Aseret ha-Dibrot*, with the declaration *anochi Hashem elokecha asher hotzeiticha me-eretz mitzrayim mi-beit avadim*, the first laws in *Mishpatim* concern the treatment of slaves. The Torah leads with those laws precisely because humanizing slavery was the immediate moral work of that moment. The *eved Kena'ani* was also, in a sense, someone in process — not yet a full member of the Jewish people, but not entirely outside either. One might argue that precisely this liminal status would make freeing him and converting him *more* desirable, not less. Yet Rabbi Akiva's position, as understood in the *gemara*, is that freeing a Canaanite slave is *assur*. Other *gemaras* invoke *le'olam bahem ta'avodu* as the source for the ruling that *assur leshachrer avdo* — it is forbidden to free one's slave — though whether those sources specifically invoke Rabbi Akiva's position warrants independent examination. The *gemara* does note that there are circumstances in which freeing a slave is permitted when there is a specific purpose — such as completing a *minyan* — suggesting that even the *chova* position is not absolute in practice. Nevertheless, reconciling the *chova* reading with the view that the Torah is fundamentally opposed to the institution of slavery remains genuinely difficult.

## Canaanite Slaves Details

### Canaanite vs. Non-Canaan…

The *gemara* now addresses a crucial question: what is the reasoning behind Rabbi Yishmael's *reshut* position in the case of *le'olam bahem ta'avodu*? Rabbi Yishmael's reasoning is as follows: since the Torah elsewhere commands *lo techayeh kol neshamah* — do not leave any of the seven Canaanite nations alive — there would be a reason to think that enslaving a Canaanite is *assur*. One would be obligated to kill him, not enslave him. The verse *le'olam bahem ta'avodu* is therefore necessary to teach that a certain category of person — not a full Canaanite — may be enslaved rather than killed. Specifically, the *gemara* identifies this as the case of *echad mi-kol ha-umot she-ba al canaanit ve-holid mimena ben* — a man from a non-Canaanite nation who fathered a child with a Canaanite woman. The child of such a union may be purchased as a slave; it is not subject to the obligation of *lo techayeh kol neshamah*. The *pasuk* establishes permission for this case — *reshut* — but does not mandate it.

### Patrilineal Identity

The determining factor in this analysis is national identity, which the *gemara* treats as patrilineal. In the general framework of Jewish law, tribal identity (*shevet*) is patrilineal — one's *shevet* follows the father. Jewishness itself follows the mother, which is an exception to an otherwise thoroughly patriarchal structure. But for purposes of national identity among the nations — and specifically for the category of the seven Canaanite peoples — identity follows the father. A father from a non-Canaanite nation, even if he lived among Canaanites and fathered children with a Canaanite woman, transmits his own national identity to his children. Those children are therefore not Canaanites and not subject to the mandate of *lo techayeh*.

A *beraita* is cited in support: *minyan le-echad min ha-umot she-ba al canaanit ve-holid mimena ben she-ata rashai liknoto be-eved* — how do we know that a child born of a non-Canaanite father and a Canaanite mother may be purchased as a slave? The verse says *vegam mivnei hatoshavim hagarim imachem mehem tiknu* — from those who dwell among you as temporary residents, you may purchase. The father, being of another nation, is a temporary resident; his children may be acquired as slaves.

### Mixed Parentage: Non-Can…

The *gemara* draws the *halacha* specifically from the scenario of a non-Canaanite father and a Canaanite mother. Because national identity follows the father, the child in this case is not Canaanite — the father, though residing among Canaanites, belongs to another nation and is a temporary resident (*toshav*). The children he fathered are thus included in the category of *mivnei hatoshavim hagarim imachem* — the children of those temporarily settled among you — and may be purchased as slaves. The verse *mehem tiknu* establishes this as *reshut*: permission, not obligation.

### Reverse: Canaanite Father

What of the reverse scenario? Perhaps if a Canaanite father fathered a child with a non-Canaanite woman, that child — having a non-Canaanite mother — would also not be Canaanite, and could similarly be enslaved rather than killed? The *gemara* rejects this. The verse *asher holidu be'artzechem* — who were born in your land — refers to those born in the land but who do not truly *belong* there, in the sense that their father is from elsewhere. The underlying logic is that a person belongs to where their father belongs. If the father is Canaanite, he truly belongs in the land; he is not a *toshav* or temporary resident. His children, born there of a father who belongs there, are themselves truly settled — *hagarim be'artzechem* — and are not in the category of those who may be purchased. Thus, for both the permission to enslave and the exemption from *lo techayeh*, the determining factor is the national identity of the father.

## Rav Chisda: Home Destruc…

### Zenuta & Tukpa Comparison

The *gemara* now introduces a statement of Rav Chisda that returns to the theme of *kina'ut* and its consequences, though from a different angle. Rav Chisda says: *zenuta be-beita ke-kira le-shumshamei* — adultery or sexual immorality in the home is like a worm in sesame seeds. Just as a worm silently destroys the seeds from within, so too does unchecked sexual sin rot away at the fabric of the household — not only through the gravity of the transgression itself, but through its corrosive effect on the *shalom bayit*. This statement thus provides a rationale for why a husband who has genuine cause for suspicion cannot simply ignore the situation in the name of domestic peace: the unaddressed wrong itself destroys that peace. Rav Chisda adds a second observation: *tukpa be-beita ke-kira le-shumshamei* — anger in the home is equally corrosive, like that same worm in the sesame. The implication is striking: both the sin itself and the jealousy or anger it generates are destructive to the home. One might think this creates an impossible bind — damned if you address it, damned if you do not.

### Gender Asymmetry Analysis

The *gemara* then introduces a qualification that is difficult to read without discomfort: *idei ve-idei be-itata* — both of these (the *zenuta* and the *tukpa*) apply when it is the *woman* committing the adultery or generating the anger, *aval be-gavra leit lan bah* — but if it is the man doing it, it does not carry the same consequence for the household. Rashi explains the distinction pragmatically: if the woman is engaged in an affair, she is not attending to the children, not managing the household; therefore, the practical fabric of the home disintegrates. If the man is unfaithful, he is, in Rashi's framing, rarely home anyway, and so the household functioning is not similarly disrupted. The *tukpa* parallel is presumably explained on the same basis: if the wife is angry, she will not cook, clean, or manage the home properly. If the husband is angry, the home's basic functioning is less immediately affected. Whatever one makes of this reasoning, it effectively places the analysis entirely within the framework of the woman's role and responsibility. The *gemara* does not address the moral equivalence of the sin; it focuses on the real-world impact on the household. The result, however, is that the analysis tends strongly toward the *mitzvah le-kanaot* position: since neither the man's anger nor his infidelity is treated as equally corrosive, the framing places the burden and the obligation squarely on the husband's act of addressing the wife's behavior.

### Shechina Presence & Depa…

Rav Chisda continues with a further statement: *batchilah kodem shechatu Yisroel* — before Israel sinned (here referring specifically to sexual sin rather than the incident of the *egel*) — *haysa Shechina shora im kol echad ve'echad*. The divine presence rested with each individual — or, as Rashi reads it, within each household. The verse cited is *ki Hashem elokecha misalech b'kerev machanecha* — for God walks in the midst of your camp, present in the very midst of your dwelling place. *Keivan shechatu* — once they sinned sexually — *nistalka Shechina mehem*: the *Shechina* departed from them. The verse supporting this is *v'lo yireh becha ervat davar v'shav me'acharecha* — should God see a matter of indecency among you, He will recoil and distance Himself. When sexual sin or discord enters the home, the sanctity of the marriage is undermined and the divine presence can no longer dwell within it.

## Aggadic: Mitzvah & Aveir…

### Mitzvah's Path Forward

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonasan: *kol oseh mitzvah achas ba'olam hazeh makdimato v'holeches lefanav le'olam haba* — whoever performs even a single *mitzvah* in this world, that *mitzvah* goes before him and accompanies him into the World to Come. The verse cited is *v'halach lefanecha tzidkecha* — your righteousness will walk before you. The *mitzvah* precedes the person into *olam haba* and proclaims his merit.

### Aveirah's Wrapping Nature

The counterpart is sobering: *v'kol ha'over aveirah achas ba'olam hazeh melafafto v'holeches lefanav le'yom hadin* — whoever commits even a single *aveirah* in this world, that sin wraps itself around him and walks before him to the *yom hadin*. Significantly, the *aveirah* is said to walk before him to the *day of judgment*, not to *olam haba* — because if it is there at the *yom hadin*, the person will not make it to *olam haba*. The sin clings to him at the moment of judgment and testifies against him. The verse cited is *yilafsu orchot darkam ya'alu batohu v'yovedu* — their paths will entangle themselves, they will rise into nothingness and be lost.

### R. Eliezer: Dog Metaphor

Rabbi Eliezer adds a vivid image: *keshura bo ke-kelev* — the *aveirah* ties itself to the person like a dog. The prooftext is taken from the encounter between Yosef and the wife of Potifar: *v'lo shama eleiha lishkav etzlah lihyot imah* — he did not listen to her to lie with her, to be with her. Rabbi Eliezer reads *lishkav etzlah* as referring to this world, and *lihyot imah* as referring to the future — had Yosef succumbed, that sin would have been with him always, clinging like a dog, following him into whatever came after. It is worth noting that while the earlier teaching of Rav Chisda focused predominantly on the woman's adultery and its effects on the household, this *aggadic* section shifts the focus: the language of *kol ha'over aveirah* addresses any person, and the story of Yosef is paradigmatically a story of a man who resisted sexual sin. The *gemara* thus broadens the consequences of sexual transgression beyond the gendered framing of the earlier teaching.

## Halakha Transition: One…

### One Witness Logic

The *gemara* now transitions back to *halacha* with a formal *mishnah* reference: *tnan hasam*, "we learned elsewhere." The topic is the evidentiary structure of the *sotah* procedure, specifically the question of how many witnesses are required at different stages. The *gemara* presents what initially appears to be a logical tension: if the first testimony — *kinuy* (the warning) and *stirah* (the seclusion) — which does not definitively forbid the wife, requires two witnesses, then by logic the final testimony, which does definitively forbid her forever, should certainly require two witnesses as well. Yet the Torah teaches that one witness suffices for the final testimony. How does one make sense of this?

### Two vs. One Witness

The *gemara* articulates the evidentiary framework precisely. Two witnesses are required for *kinuy* and *stirah* — the groundwork of the *sotah* procedure. These establish the conditions under which the wife must undergo the test of the *sotah* water. If she does not drink — or if something prevents it — she is divorced without her *ketubah* and loses the right to the *sotah* test. Now, one witness who testifies after *kinuy* and *stirah* that she actually committed adultery is sufficient to make her definitively forbidden to her husband, to eliminate the option of the *sotah* water, and to cause her to be divorced without a *ketubah* — this despite the testimony being final and irreversible. The *chiddush* is that one witness suffices for this most consequential determination, specifically because the groundwork of *kinuy* and *stirah* has already been laid by two witnesses.

The verse cited is *v'ed ein bah* — and there is no witness against her. The logic of the *gemara* is: *kol she-yesh bah* — whenever there *is* a witness — even one — that is sufficient. This has been read in multiple ways earlier in the *masechet*, but here it yields the rule that a single witness after *kinuy* and *stirah* finalizes the matter. Had there been two witnesses testifying to the actual act of adultery with prior warning (*hasra'ah*), she would have been subject to capital punishment. The *chiddush* of one witness is specifically operative in the *sotah* context following *kinuy* and *stirah*, not as a pathway to prosecution but as a pathway to final *halachic* prohibition.

### Gezerah Shavah: Ervat Da…

The *gemara* now asks: from where is this derived? The *beraita* had cited *ki matza bah ervat davar* — "for he finds in her a matter of indecency" — and connected it via a *gezerah shavah* of the word *davar* to *al pi shenei edim o al pi sheloshah edim yakum davar* — "by the testimony of two or three witnesses shall a matter be established." The *gezerah shavah* teaches that *ervat davar* — the "nakedness of a matter" relevant to *sotah* — is subject to the same requirement of two witnesses as the general rule of testimony. This is the source for requiring two witnesses for *kinuy* and *stirah*: the word *davar* in *ervat davar* is read through the *gezerah shavah* to import the standard of *shenei edim*.

### One Witness Absent Groun…

The *gemara* then poses a further question: we now understand why one witness suffices after *kinuy* and *stirah* — the groundwork was laid by two. But how do we know that one witness who testifies to adultery *without* any prior *kinuy* and *stirah* would not be believed at all? Why wouldn't such a witness suffice to establish something, even if not the full consequence of the *sotah* procedure? The answer comes from the same *gezerah shavah*: *ne'emar kan ki matza bah ervat davar v'ne'emar le-halan al pi shenei edim* — the word *davar* here and there links the *sotah* testimony to the general requirement of two witnesses. *Mah davar ha-amur le-halan edim shenayim af kan edim shenayim*: just as in the general case two witnesses are required to establish a matter, so too for testimony about adultery in general, two are required. It is only in the specific case where *kinuy* and *stirah* have already been established by two witnesses that the Torah makes an exception and allows one witness to finalize matters — and this is derived from the word *bah* in *v'ed ein bah*: *bah v'lo b'kinnuy bah v'lo b'stirah* — it is specifically for the act of adultery itself that one witness suffices; for the preliminary stages of *kinuy* and *stirah*, two are always required.

## Rishona & Achrona

### Definitions: Rishona/Ach…

A *beraita* provides formal definitions for the terms used in this evidentiary framework. *Edut rishona* — the "first testimony" — refers to *edut stirah*, testimony of seclusion, which encompasses both *kinuy* and *stirah*. *Edut achrona* — the "last testimony" — refers to *edut tumah*, testimony of actual adultery. These definitions align with the framework just analyzed: the "first" testimony requires two witnesses, the "last" requires only one when the "first" has already been properly established.

## Measure of Stirah

### Definitional Chain

Having established that *stirah* is significant because it creates the conditions for potential *tumah*, the *gemara* now asks: *ve-kama shiur stirah* — how long must the seclusion last to constitute legally significant *stirah*? The answer is given through a chain of definitional steps: the duration of *stirah* is *kedei tumah* — the time sufficient for *tumah* to occur. What is the time sufficient for *tumah*? *Kedei bi'ah* — the time sufficient for intercourse to take place. What is the minimal definition of intercourse? *Kedei ha'ara'ah* — the time sufficient for *ha'ara'ah*.

### Ha'arah Definition

*Ha'ara'ah* is defined as the insertion of the *atarah* — the crown or tip — of the male organ, and not the completion of the full act of intercourse (*gmar bi'ah*). Halachically, *ha'ara'ah* constitutes a legally significant sexual act even without full penetration. The *gemara* also notes — addressing what one might reasonably wonder — that this definition does not require an erection to have occurred beforehand. The assumption underlying all the time-measurements that follow is that the seduction has already taken place and the relevant clothing has already been removed (the *Yerushalmi* specifies that at minimum the belt has been undone). Given all that, the question of how much time the seclusion must last reduces to: how long does *ha'ara'ah* itself take? This also has practical relevance beyond *sotah* — for instance, in determining the minimal duration of *yichud* after *chuppah*.

## Competing Time Measures

### R. Yishmael: Palm Tree W…

Rabbi Yishmael's measure for the duration of *stirah* — that is, the time sufficient for *ha'ara'ah* — is *kedei hakafat dekel*: the time it takes to walk around a palm tree. The question of how long this is in absolute terms is not specified; it is evidently understood to be quite short, consistent with the minimal definition of *ha'ara'ah*.

### R. Eliezer: Mixing Wine

Rabbi Eliezer gives a different measure: *kedei mezigat hakos* — the time it takes to mix a cup of wine (diluting wine with water, as was the practice).

### R. Yehoshua: Drinking

Rabbi Yehoshua says: *kedei lishtoto* — the time it takes to drink the cup.

### Ben Azzai: Frying Egg

Ben Azzai says: *kedei litzlot beitzah* — the time it takes to fry an egg.

### R. Akiva: Swallowing

Rabbi Akiva says: *kedei legomah* — the time it takes to swallow the egg just fried.

### R. Yehuda ben Beteira: T…

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says: *kedei ligmoa shalosh beitzim zo achar zo* — the time it takes to swallow three eggs in sequence.

### R. Elazar: String Tying

Rabbi Elazar ben Yirmiyah says: *kedei likshor girdei nima* — the time it takes to tie the ends of a thread or string together.

### Ben Chanin: Splinter Rem…

Ben Chanin ben Pinchas says: *kedei she-toshit yadah le-toch piha litol kaisam* — the time it takes for her to insert her hand into her mouth and remove a splinter or sliver of wood.

### Pelimo: Bread from Basket

Pelimo says: *kedei she-toshit yadah la-sal litol kikar* — the time it takes for her to reach her hand into a basket and take out a loaf of bread. The *beraita* adds a scriptural allusion: *af al pi she-ein raya la-davar zecher la-davar*, *ki be'ad isha zona ad kikar lechem* — for the price of a harlot woman is even a loaf of bread. Though the full exegesis of this verse will be addressed later in the *masechet*, the association of a woman, sexual sin, and a loaf of bread makes the imagery of Pelimo's measure thematically apt.

## Measure of Stirah (II)

### Enumeration Purpose

One might ask: why does the *beraita* enumerate the full chain — *stirah*, *tumah*, *bi'ah*, *ha'ara'ah* — rather than simply stating *kedei ha'ara'ah* directly? The *gemara* answers: *tzrichei* — each step in the chain is necessary. Had the *beraita* said only *kedei tumah*, one might have thought it means the time for *tumah* including the preceding seduction — *kedei tumah ve-artzota*. By saying *kedei bi'ah*, it makes clear that the seduction is excluded, and we are measuring only the act itself. Had the *beraita* then said only *kedei bi'ah*, one might have thought it means the time for full intercourse — *kedei gmar bi'ah*. By saying *kedei ha'ara'ah*, it specifies that even the minimum of *ha'ara'ah* suffices. And had it said only *kedei ha'ara'ah* without the preceding steps, one might have thought it means *ha'ara'ah* including the preceding desire or seduction. The phrase *kedei tumah* — derived from the *pasuk* *v'hi nitma'ah* — tells us that we measure only the act, not what came before it. Each step of the definitional chain thus functions to narrow and clarify the measure, ruling out broader readings at each stage.

## Reconciliation of Measur…

### Hakafa vs. Chazara

The *gemara* now notes a difficulty: a second *beraita* presents what appears to be a parallel set of positions, but the attributions are shifted by one — each *tanna* seems to say in this second source what the *tanna* one position later says in the first. Rather than simply acknowledging a dispute about who said what, the *gemara* attempts to reconcile all the positions as consistent. The first point of reconciliation concerns the opening measures. In the first *beraita*, Rabbi Yishmael said *kedei hakafat dekel* — the time to walk around a palm tree. In the second *beraita*, Rabbi Eliezer says *kedei chazarat dekel* — the time for the palm tree to return or sway back. The *gemara* initially assumes these are the same: *haynu hakafat dekel haynu chazarat dekel*. But Abaye corrects this: *hakafa beragel chazara beruach* — *hakafat dekel* means walking around the tree on foot, while *chazarat dekel* means the swaying of the tree blown by the wind and then returning to its upright position. These are two genuinely different actions and potentially different durations.

### Rav Ashi: Palm Return Ti…

Rav Ashi raises a further ambiguity regarding *chazarat dekel*: when exactly does the palm tree finish its return? Is the relevant endpoint when the tree reaches its upright position again — *ki heichi de-azal ve-hadar asi* — or does it include the time the tree continues to sway gently after returning upright until it fully settles — *ki heichi de-azal ve-asi ve-hadar kai be-duchtei*? The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

### Combining Positions

The *gemara* then reconciles Rabbi Yishmael's position across the two *baraitot*. In the first, Rabbi Eliezer said *kedei mezigat hakos*. In the second, Rabbi Yishmael says *kedei mezigat hakos*. How can both be right? They are simply the same position stated by different *tannaim* in different traditions. Moving to Rabbi Yehoshua: in the first *beraita* he said *kedei lishtoto* (drinking the cup), while in the second he said *kedei mezigat hakos* (mixing the cup). The *gemara* reconciles these: *eima kedei limzog velishtot* — combine the two; Rabbi Yehoshua's full measure is the time to both mix and drink. One might ask why the *gemara* does not instead say that mixing and drinking are the same amount of time and thus the two *baraitot* are simply using different language for the same thing. The answer is that we have already assigned the single measure of *mezigat hakos* to Rabbi Eliezer's position (via the first *beraita*), so we cannot also make it equal to *lishtoto* and thereby conflate Rabbi Yehoshua's position with Rabbi Eliezer's.

### Frying & Swallowing

Ben Azzai said *kedei litzlot beitzah* (frying an egg) in the first *beraita*, and *kedei lishtoto* (drinking the cup) in the second. The *gemara* resolves this by saying *idi ve-idi chad shiura hu* — frying an egg and drinking a cup take the same amount of time. For Rabbi Akiva: in the first *beraita* he said *kedei legomah* (swallowing), and in the second he said *kedei litzlot beitzah* (frying). The *gemara* reconciles: *eima kedei litzlot beitzah ve-ligmoa* — Rabbi Akiva's measure is the time to both fry and swallow. Again, one might ask why not say frying and swallowing are the same — but that would make Rabbi Akiva's position identical to Ben Azzai's, which we have already assigned. For Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira: in the first *beraita* he said swallowing three eggs, in the second he said swallowing one egg. The *gemara* explains that in the second *beraita*, Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira is responding to Rabbi Akiva's combined measure of frying-and-swallowing, telling him: you don't need both frying and swallowing to describe the measure — just say *kedei ligmoa shalosh beitzim*, swallowing three eggs, and that captures the same duration as frying one and swallowing it.

### Unresolved Ambiguities

Even after all these reconciliations, the *gemara* leaves two positions with unresolved internal ambiguities. Regarding Rabbi Elazar ben Yirmiyah's measure of tying string ends: Rav Ashi asks whether the string ends are close together (*d'mikrava*) or far apart (*d'mirchaka*), since the time to tie them would differ significantly. *Teiku* — unresolved. Regarding Ben Chanin ben Pinchas's measure of removing a splinter from one's mouth: Rav Ashi asks whether the splinter is tightly lodged (*d'mahadka*) or loose (*d'la mahadka*). *Teiku*. And for Pelimo's measure of reaching into a basket for a loaf of bread, Rav Ashi raises a cascade of variables: Is the loaf tightly packed in the basket or loose? Is it a new basket (with rough, gripping edges) or an old one? Is the bread warm or cold? Is it made of wheat flour or barley flour? Is it soft bread or hard? All *teiku* — none of these ambiguities are resolved. The multiplication of unresolved questions here is striking, given how carefully the *gemara* reconciled the other positions. The *gemara* simply acknowledges that the precise duration of these measures cannot be determined without knowing the relevant physical parameters.

## Personal Measurement Bas…

### Personal Measurement Pri…

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Rabbi Yosef said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol echad ve'echad be'atzmo shi'er* — each of these *tannaim* measured the duration from his own experience. That is, each *tanna* reflected on how long a given activity took him personally, and offered that duration as his measure for *ha'ara'ah*. This is a remarkable observation: the entire array of differing positions is not a dispute about an abstract unit of time but a record of individual experience — each *rabbi* consulting, as it were, his own life.

### Ben Azzai Problem

This observation immediately raises a well-known difficulty. Ben Azzai famously never married — or at least famously did not maintain a marriage. He explained that his love of Torah was so consuming that he could not devote himself to a wife. How, then, could he have measured *kedei litzlot beitzah* as the time for *ha'ara'ah* from his own experience, as Rabbi Yochanan's principle would require?

### Ben Azzai Solutions

The *gemara* offers three possible resolutions. *Ibba'it eima nasiv u-ferash*: perhaps Ben Azzai did marry but subsequently separated from his wife — he left the marriage to devote himself to Torah study, and this tradition records that episode. *Ibba'it eima me-rabeih shemia leih*: alternatively, he heard this measure from his *rabbi* and reported it as authoritative tradition, without it being derived from personal experience. *Ve-ibba'it eima*: and if you prefer — *Torah hi ve-lilmod ani tzarich* — this is Torah and I must learn it; Ben Azzai was, in the well-known tradition, one of those who studied the most intimate dimensions of human life as pure Torah, possessing knowledge through sacred learning rather than personal encounter. Finally, *sod Hashem liyreiav* — the secret of God is with those who fear Him: perhaps Ben Azzai simply knew, in the manner of one whose fear of Heaven grants him a form of direct apprehension.
