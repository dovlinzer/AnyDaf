# Nedarim 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Petach Framework

### Intro & Petach Theme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ פּוֹתְחִין בְּיָמִים טוֹבִים וּבְשַׁבָּתוֹת. בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: אוֹתָן הַיָּמִים — מוּתָּרִין, וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַיָּמִים אֲסוּרִין. עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְלִימֵּד שֶׁהַנֶּדֶר שֶׁהוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ — הוּתַּר כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one vowed that certain food or drink or all food and drink be forbidden to him, the halakhic authorities **may broach** dissolution **by** raising the issue of **Festivals and *Shabbatot*.** They ask him whether he realized at the time he stated his vow that he would have to uphold it on these festive days as well. **At first they said** that on **those days** that he did not intend to include in his vow, that item **is permitted, but** on **all the rest of the days,** food and drink ar**e** still **forbidden** by his vow, **until Rabbi Akiva came and taught that a vow that is partially dissolved is dissolved entirely.**

Daf 66 continues the theme of *petach* — the opening or grounds through which a *chacham* releases a person from a *neder*. The preceding *mishnayos* have already established that the ideal *petach* is something highly personal, specific to the individual's circumstances. Nevertheless, the *mishna* here addresses circumstances that are more generic — framings broad enough to apply to many people — which could also afford a valid basis for saying that, had the person thought of those circumstances at the time, he would not have made his *neder*.

Beyond practical advice, there is a conceptual *chiddush* embedded in these *mishnayos*. One might think that a *petach* so universal becomes self-defeating in two ways: first, that it renders nedarim trivially escapable, thereby undermining their weight; and second, that it creates conditions under which people will give untruthful responses to secure their release. These were precisely the concerns raised regarding *bichvod aviv ve-imo*, honor of one's father and mother — that concern being both the ubiquity of the circumstance and the embarrassment that would prevent anyone from honestly admitting they had been indifferent to parental honor. The *chiddush* of the upcoming *mishnayos* is therefore that the circumstances discussed, while genuinely broad, do not trigger those concerns.

### R. Meir vs. Chachamim

The debate between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* over *bichvod aviv ve-imo* as a *petach* turned on precisely these two concerns: universality and the risk of dishonest responses. No one would brazenly admit to having been indifferent to parental honor, and so using it as a *petach* was feared to produce false attestations. The cases discussed in the present *mishna*, by contrast, pass that threshold — they are general enough to be genuinely useful but not so universal or weighted that they would produce dishonesty.

### Universal Petachim

The distinction between *petachim* that are too universal and those that are appropriately broad is thus a live one throughout this section of the *gemara*. A *petach* that applies to literally everyone in every situation risks making nedarim feel meaningless; a *petach* tailored to something genuinely relevant to this person's situation remains halakhically sound and does not invite false testimony.

## Yom Tov & Shabbat

### Yom Tov Petach

The *mishna* opens: *potchin beyomim tovim u-vshabbatos* — one may open with the circumstance of *yom tov* and *Shabbos*. Since the paradigmatic *neder* involves forbidden benefit from food, the *chacham* asks the person: had you realized that this *neder* would interfere with your full enjoyment of *Shabbos* and *yom tov*, would you have made it? While it is admittedly difficult to claim one was unaware that the calendar contains a weekly *Shabbos*, many people making a *neder* in a moment of anger or distress are not thinking about an upcoming *yom tov*. If the person responds that he would not have made the *neder* had he thought about it, that constitutes a valid basis for release.

### Ran's Addition

The Ran adds a further dimension: beyond simply pointing out that *Shabbos* and *yom tov* would be impacted, one also emphasizes to the person that refraining from the forbidden item on those days would constitute a failure to fully discharge his *obligation* of *oneg Shabbos*. As the Ran frames it: *de-ilu haya yode'a she-assur le-hitzta'er be-Shabbos* — had he known it is forbidden to afflict himself on *Shabbos*. The dual emphasis — practical inconvenience and religious obligation — increases the likelihood of an honest response while also reflecting the genuine weight of what the person failed to consider.

## Berishona Practice

### Original Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? אָמַר ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאֵינִי נֶהֱנֶה לְכוּלְּכֶם״, הוּתַּר אֶחָד מֵהֶן — הוּתְּרוּ כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** In the case of one who **said** to a group of people: **I will not benefit from all of you** as **it is *konam*** for me, if benefit from **one of them was permitted** for whatever reason, benefit from **all of them is permitted.**

The *mishna* now introduces a historically and halakhically significant point: *berishona hayu omrim* — originally, they used to say something different. In the earlier practice, if a *petach* was found through the circumstance of *Shabbos* and *yom tov*, the release applied only to those days: *otan hayamim mutarim, u-shear kol hayamim assurim* — those days were permitted and all other days remained forbidden. The underlying logic was that the person's *neder* was, in retrospect, made *be-ta'us* only insofar as it included *Shabbos* and *yom tov*; the remainder of the *neder* was fully intended and therefore binding. The practical result was a kind of virtual reconstruction: what the person would have said, had he been thinking, was "meat and wine are forbidden to me on all days *except* Shabbos and yom tov."

### R. Akiva's Principle

This earlier practice was revised by Rabbi Akiva, who taught: *neder she-hutar miktzato hutar kullo* — a *neder* from which part has been released, the whole of it is released.

### Miktzato vs. Miklalo

There are two textual variants of this principle. One reads *miktzato* — if part of it has been permitted, the whole is permitted, since the *neder* is treated as a single unit. The other version reads *miklalo* — if something is excluded from the collective, from the totality of what was included, then the entire *neder* is rendered permissible. Both versions express the same underlying idea: once the integrity of the *neder* as a whole has been compromised by the discovery of a flaw in part of it, the whole falls.

One might ask whether this applies specifically to a *neder* or extends to a *shevu'ah* as well. The answer is that the case here remains a *neder* in the technical sense — *konam ha-basar alai* — since the prohibition focuses on the object rather than on the person's own activity. The same way a *neder* can be temporally limited (*konam basar alai kol sheloshim yom*), it can also have its prohibited status shift between weekdays and *Shabbos*.

### Yerushalmi Source

The Ran cites the Yerushalmi's derivation of this principle from the verse *ke-chol ha-yotzei mi-piv ya'aseh* — "according to all that issues from his mouth, he shall do." The Yerushalmi formulates it: *shavya lah shakul kayama* — his obligation is to fulfill everything. Consequently, once a person is released from *part* of what he declared, he is no longer obligated to fulfill *everything* — and therefore he is not obligated to fulfill *anything*.

## Multiple People Neder

### Keitzad Intro

The *mishna* then asks *keitzad* — how does this work? It proceeds to illustrate the principle of *neder she-hutar miktzato hutar kullo* through the case of a *neder* involving multiple individuals collectively. If someone declares *konam she'eini nehene l'kulchem* — "it is *konam* that I derive benefit from all of you" — he has formulated a single, unified *neder* encompassing the entire group.

### One Released All

The consequence: *hutar echad meihen, hutar l'kulan* — if he is released from one of them through a *petach*, he is released from all of them. Because the *neder* was framed as a collective unit, any breach of its integrity releases the whole.

### Vav Ha'Hibbur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שֶׁאֵינִי נֶהֱנֶה לָזֶה וְלָזֶה״, הוּתַּר הָרִאשׁוֹן — הוּתְּרוּ כּוּלָּן, הוּתַּר הָאַחֲרוֹן — הָאַחֲרוֹן מוּתָּר וְכוּלָּן אֲסוּרִין. הוּתַּר הָאֶמְצָעִי, הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְמַטָּה — מוּתָּר, הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְמַעְלָה — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** However, if one said: **I will not benefit from this** one **and from that** one as it is *konam* for me, then if benefit from **the first** one **was permitted** for whatever reason, benefit from **all** of them **is permitted.** But if benefit from **the last** one **was permitted,** benefit from **the last** one alone **is permitted, but** benefit from **all** the others **is forbidden,** as the benefit from each is considered to have been prohibited by a separate vow. If benefit from **the middle** one **was permitted,** then **from him and below,** i.e., all those enumerated after him, benefit **is permitted; from him and above,** i.e., those listed before him, benefit **is forbidden.**

The *mishna* then presents a more nuanced case: *she'eini nehene la-zeh u'la-zeh* — "that I derive no benefit from this one *and* this one *and* this one," using the conjunctive *vav*. The *vav ha-hibbur* connecting the individuals creates a chain: the prohibition assigned to the first person is transferred to the second, the second's to the third, and so on.

### Status Transfer

The conceptual model here is one of status transfer and daisy-chaining. When I say *la-zeh u'la-zeh u'la-zeh*, I am not making five independent nedarim; rather, the forbidden status I have assigned to David is then also assigned to Dov, and Dov's to Jonathan, and so on. The status is derivative — each person's prohibition is built upon the previous one.

### First vs. Last

This model generates precise consequences. *Hutar ha-rishon, hutar kullan* — if the first person in the chain is released, everyone is released, because all subsequent prohibitions are built upon his. By contrast, *hutar ha-acharon, mutar v'kullan asurin* — if only the last person is released, everyone else remains forbidden, since the last person's prohibition is built upon theirs, but theirs is not built upon his. Releasing the last link does not undo the earlier ones.

### Middle Release

The intermediate case: *hutar ha-emtza'i* — if the middle person is released. The result is that those *after* him in the chain — *heimenu u'lemata mutarin* — are also released, since their prohibitions were built upon his. Those *before* him — *heimenu u'lemala asurin* — remain forbidden, since his prohibition was built on theirs, not the reverse.

This structure is analogous to an earlier *gemara* discussion on 25a, where the placement of a qualifier — at the beginning or at the end — determines the scope of what it governs.

### Conceptual Model

It is important to appreciate the conceptual structure underlying all of this. If the *la-zeh u'la-zeh* formulation were treated as one large unified *neder*, the principle of *hutar miktzato hutar kullo* would apply straightforwardly and release one would release all. But the *mishna* demonstrates that releasing the last person does not release the first — meaning these are not simply one *neder*. The better conceptual model is that these are technically separate nedarim, but each subsequent *neder* is built upon the prior one, creating the daisy-chain effect. It is precisely this inter-dependence that produces the asymmetric results.

## Independent Nedarim

### Linked vs. Independent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שֶׁאֲנִי נֶהֱנֶה לָזֶה קׇרְבָּן וְלָזֶה קׇרְבָּן״ — צְרִיכִין פֶּתַח לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna gives another example of interconnected vows: If one stated: **I will** not **benefit from this** one, as if he were **an offering, and from that** one, as if he were **an offering,** then **an extenuation** enabling the dissolution of a vow **is required for each and every one,** as they have the status of separate vows.

The *mishna* then addresses the case where nedarim are fully independent. If someone says *konam la-zeh, konam la-zeh, konam la-zeh* — using the technical word *konam* (or *korban*, or *neder*) anew for each individual — he has created separate, self-contained nedarim. In this case, *tzrichim petach l'chol echad v'echad* — a distinct *petach* is required for each one individually, and releasing one has no effect on the others.

The *gemara* notes that the *mishna*'s formulation, which uses the word *konam* to mark the case of independence, implies that *anything less* than repeating the operative word renders the nedarim linked. This raises the question: does the *vav ha-hibbur* create the link, or is even *la-zeh, la-zeh, la-zeh* without a *vav* sufficient to create the chain? The *gemara* attributes the view in the *mishna* to Rabbi Shimon, based on a parallel principle in the laws of *shevu'ah*.

## Wine & Onion Cases

### Old Wine Release

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קֻוֽנָּם יַיִן שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם, שֶׁהַיַּיִן רַע לַמֵּעַיִם״, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וַהֲלֹא הַמְיוּשָּׁן יָפֶה לַמֵּעַיִם! הוּתַּר בִּמְיוּשָּׁן. וְלֹא בִּמְיוּשָּׁן בִּלְבַד הוּתַּר, אֶלָּא בְּכׇל הַיַּיִן. ״קֻוֽנָּם בָּצָל שֶׁאֵינִי טוֹעֵם, שֶׁהַבָּצָל רַע לַלֵּב״, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הֲלֹא הַכּוּפְרִי יָפֶה לַלֵּב! הוּתַּר בַּכּוּפְרִי. וְלֹא בַּכּוּפְרִי בִּלְבַד הוּתַּר, אֶלָּא בְּכׇל הַבְּצָלִים. מַעֲשֶׂה הָיָה, וְהִתִּירוֹ רַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּכׇל הַבְּצָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna gives another example of a vow that was partially dissolved. If one stated in a vow: **Wine is *konam*** for me and **I** will not **taste it, as wine is bad for the intestines,** and **they said to him: But aged wine is good for the intestines,** then the vow **is dissolved with regard to aged wine. And not only with regard to aged wine is it dissolved, but with regard to all** types of **wine,** since a vow that has been partially dissolved is entirely dissolved. Likewise, if one stated in a vow: **Onions are *konam*** for me and **I will not taste them, as onions are bad for the heart,** and **they said to him: But the *kuferi*** onion **is good for the heart,** then, in this case too, **it is dissolved with regard to *kuferi*** onions, **and not only with regard to *kuferi*** onions is it dissolved, **but with regard to all** types of **onions.** The mishna relates that **an incident** of this kind **occurred, and Rabbi Meir dissolved** the vow **with regard to all** types of **onions.**

The *mishna* presents: *konam yayin she'ani to'em, she-ha-yayin ra le-me'ai* — "Konam that I will not taste wine, because wine is bad for my digestion." If they then told him: *ve-halo ha-meyushan yafeh le-me'ai* — "but aged wine is actually good for digestion" — and he did not know this, then *hutar ba-meyushan* — he is released from aged wine.

### All Wine Released

And furthermore: *velo ba-meyushan bilvad hutar ella be-chol ha-yayin* — not only aged wine, but all wine is released. This is the application of *neder she-hutar miktzato hutar kullo*: since the *neder* was predicated on the assumption that wine is harmful, and part of that assumption has been shown to be false, the entire *neder* is undone.

### Ta'ut vs. Heter

This raises a subtle but important distinction. What if, upon being told that aged wine is good for digestion, the person responds: "Of course I knew that — when I said 'wine' I only meant new wine"? In that case, it is not a situation of *hutar miktzato* at all. Rather, the scope of what he declared in the first place is being interpreted according to his own stated intent at the time: he meant only new wine, so the word "wine" is understood accordingly, and no formal release is needed for aged wine because it was never included. The case of *hutar miktzato* — and its consequence that all wine is now permitted — applies specifically where he genuinely intended to include all wine, did not realize that aged wine was beneficial, and only in retrospect would not have included it. The distinction between a fundamental *ta'ut* in the scope of the *neder* and a subsequent *heter* through *hutar miktzato* is a fine but real one, and the *chacham* must assess which situation applies.

### Onion Case

The same principle plays out in the parallel case: *konam batzal she'ani to'em, she-ha-batzal ra la-lev* — "Konam that I will not taste onion, because onion is bad for the heart." If told that the *kufari* onion is actually good for the heart, he is released from it — *hutar ba-kufari* — and consequently released from all onions: *velo ba-kufari bilvad hutar ella be-chol ha-betzalim*. Again, if he were to say he always knew the *kufari* was fine and he only meant regular onions, the scope of his *neder* would be interpreted accordingly and no *heter* would be needed for the *kufari*.

### R. Meir Permits All

The *mishna* records: *ma'aseh hayah ve-hitir Rabbi Meir be-chol ha-betzalim* — there was an actual case and Rabbi Meir permitted all onions. This provides practical *ma'aseh* support for the principle of *hutar miktzato hutar kullo* as applied to these circumstances.

## Independent Nedarim…

### R. Shimon's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הוּתַּר הָאַחֲרוֹן — הָאַחֲרוֹן מוּתָּר, וְכוּלָּן אֲסוּרִין. מַאן תַּנָּא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna taught: If benefit from **the last** one **was permitted,** benefit from **the last** one alone **is permitted but** benefit from **all** the others **is forbidden.** If one stated: I will not benefit from this one, as if he were an offering, and from that one, as if he were an offering, then an extenuation enabling the dissolution of a vow is required for each and every one. The Gemara poses a question: **Who** is the *tanna* that **taught** this mishna?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״שְׁבוּעָה״ לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: It is** taught in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said** that one who utters a false oath to several people at once is not liable to bring an offering for each false oath **unless he says: An oath,** in his oath **to each and every one** (*Shevuot* 38a). If he said: An oath, only once, even if he specified each person by saying: Not to you, not to you, it is still considered to be a single oath. Otherwise, it is viewed as one oath. The mishna, which requires a separate extenuation for each person only if he stated: As if he were an offering, with regard to each of them, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon stated with regard to oaths.

Returning to the question of when nedarim are treated as linked versus independent, the *gemara* identifies the author of the *mishna*'s ruling as Rabbi Shimon. The basis: *d'amar Rabbi Shimon, ad she-yomar shevu'ah l'chol echad ve-echad* — Rabbi Shimon holds, in the context of *shevu'ah*, that the oath-word must be explicitly repeated for each individual in order to create independent obligations. In the *shevu'ah* context: if six people each demand repayment and one takes a single false *shevu'ah* that he owes nothing to any of them, that is one *korban*. If he says "*shevu'ah* I owe nothing to you, *shevu'ah* I owe nothing to you" — six separate *korbanot*. If he says "*shevu'ah* I owe nothing to you or you or you" — Rabbi Shimon holds that is still one, since the operative word *shevu'ah* was not repeated.

### Chachamim Disagree

The *chachamim*, however, disagree. In their view, saying *la-zeh, la-zeh, la-zeh* without the *vav* — that is, listing the individuals separately without the conjunctive — is itself sufficient to establish independent obligations, even without repeating the operative term for each one.

## Wine & Onion Cases…

### Wine Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קֻוֽנָּם יַיִן שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם וְכוּ׳. וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּאֵין רַע! אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: וְעוֹד, ״יָפֶה״ קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that if one said: **Wine is *konam*** for me and **I** will not **taste it,** as wine is bad for the intestines, his vow is dissolved, for aged wine is good for the intestines. The Gemara asks: **And let him derive** that the vow is dissolved from the fact **that** aged wine is **not bad** for the intestines. Even if it is not actively good for the intestines, the vow should nevertheless be considered mistaken. **Rabbi Abba said:** The mishna **teaches: And furthermore,** it is **good.** In other words, even if aged wine is merely not harmful to the intestines the vow is dissolved, and they strengthened their claim by pointing out that it is in fact beneficial.

The *gemara* returns to the wine case. One might ask: why does the *mishna* state that aged wine is *good* for digestion — would it not have been sufficient to say merely that it is *not bad*? Rabbi Abba responds: *v'od yafeh katani* — the *mishna* teaches an even stronger formulation, but the minimum required for the *heter* is simply that it is not harmful. The person's stated reason for his *neder* was that wine damages digestion; it is enough to demonstrate that this assumption is untrue, without needing to show that wine is positively beneficial.

## Honor Petachim

### Personal Honor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ פּוֹתְחִין לָאָדָם בִּכְבוֹד עַצְמוֹ וּבִכְבוֹד בָּנָיו. אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: אִילּוּ הָיִיתָ יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁלְּמָחָר אוֹמְרִין עָלֶיךָ ״כָּךְ הִיא וִוסְתּוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי, מְגָרֵשׁ אֶת נָשָׁיו״, וְעַל בְּנוֹתֶיךָ יְהוּ אוֹמְרִין ״בְּנוֹת גְּרוּשׁוֹת הֵן״, ״מָה רָאֲתָה אִמָּן שֶׁל אֵלּוּ לְהִתְגָּרֵשׁ״. וְאָמַר: ״אִילּוּ הָיִיתִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁכֵּן, לֹא הָיִיתִי נוֹדֵר״ — הֲרֵי זֶה מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The halakhic authorities **may broach** dissolution **for a person by** raising the issue of **his own honor and the honor of his children.** For example, if he took a vow that resulted in his needing to divorce his wife, they may **say to him: Had you known that tomorrow people will say about you: This is the habit [*veset*] of so-and-so,** that **he divorces his wives** due to vows, **and they will say about your daughters: They are daughters of divorce,** or they will ask: **What did their mother see to divorce,** thereby giving them a bad reputation. **And** if the man who vowed **said: Had I known it was so, I would not have vowed, it is dissolved.**

The *mishna* turns to a further category of *petach*: *potchin l'adam bichvod atzmo u-vichvod banav* — one may open with the person's own honor and the honor of his children. In the classic scenario driving this *mishna*, a husband has made a *neder* that his wife may not benefit from him, which halakhically compels him to divorce her.

### Vesto Concept

The *chacham* may say to him: *ilu hayita yode'a she-lemachar omrim alecha, kach hi vesto shel ploni, megoreshet ishto* — had you known that tomorrow people would say of you, "that is the practice of so-and-so, his wife is divorced," would you have made your *neder*? The word *veset* is used here in its sense of habit or regular practice — noteworthy given how frequently the term appears in the *gemara* in the context of a woman's menstrual cycle, where it means the regularity of her cycle. Here it simply connotes: this is the kind of person he is, this is his pattern. The *chacham* may also invoke the person's financial interests — had you thought about the *ketubbah* payment? But if the husband responds that he is unconcerned about paying the *ketubbah*, the *chacham* shifts to a weightier consideration: had you thought about the social consequences — that no one will want to marry you again, given your reputation for making destructive nedarim?

### Daughters' Honor

The *mishna* adds the dimension of *kvod banav* — the honor of his children: *ve-al bnotecha yomru, bnos grushos hein* — and regarding your daughters, people will say they are daughters of a divorcée. Already in the period of the *mishna*, the *shidduch*-implications of a mother's divorce were a recognized social reality. People would wonder: *mah ra'ah ima shel elu le-hisgareish* — what did the mother of these girls do to cause the divorce? They might suspect improper conduct on her part, or simply that she was not a suitable wife. And the daughters, having grown up in her household, would be viewed with wariness. The *mishna* is not creating this social reality; it is reflecting it, and using it as a lever to make the husband consider whether he would have made his *neder* had he appreciated all its consequences.

If the husband says *ilu hayiti yode'a she-kein lo hayiti noder* — had I known this I would not have made the *neder* — then *harei zeh mutar*, he is released.

## Woman's Appearance

### Ugly Neder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאֲנִי נוֹשֵׂא אֶת פְּלוֹנִית כְּעוּרָה״ וַהֲרֵי הִיא נָאָה, ״שְׁחוֹרָה״ וַהֲרֵי הִיא לְבָנָה, ״קְצָרָה״ וַהֲרֵי הִיא אֲרוּכָּה — מוּתָּר בָּהּ. לֹא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא כְּעוּרָה וְנַעֲשֵׂת נָאָה, שְׁחוֹרָה וְנַעֲשֵׂת לְבָנָה, קְצָרָה וְנַעֲשֵׂת אֲרוּכָּה — אֶלָּא שֶׁהַנֶּדֶר טָעוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Mishna continues: If a man said: **Marrying ugly so-and-so is *konam* for me, and she is** in fact **beautiful,** or if, in vowing not to marry her, he called her **black, and she is** in fact **white,** or if, in vowing not to marry her, he called her **short, and she is** in fact **tall, he is permitted to her. Not because she was ugly and became beautiful, black and became white,** or **short and became tall,** but **rather, because the vow was mistaken** from the outset.

The *mishna* now turns to another case, already introduced in the prior *daf*: *konam she'ani nosei plonis ke'urah* — "Konam that I will not marry so-and-so, because she is ugly." The *neder* was made on the basis of an assumed characteristic of the woman.

### True Beauty Found

If it then emerges that she is actually beautiful — *ve-harei hi na'ah* — or that he mistakenly thought she was dark-skinned and she is actually fair, or that she was short but is actually tall, the *neder* is permitted. The *mishna* clarifies the reason: this is not a case of *nolad* — a circumstance that arose after the fact — because the woman did not *become* beautiful; she *was* beautiful and he simply did not realize it. This is therefore not a change in circumstances but a fundamental error of fact.

### Neder Ta'us

The *mishna* makes explicit: *lo mipnei she-ke'urah ve-na'aseis na'ah* — it is not because she was ugly and became beautiful, which would be a *nolad* situation. Rather, *she-ha-neder ta'us* — the *neder* was fundamentally erroneous. Because the *neder* was based on a false assumption of fact, it arguably does not even require a formal *heter*; the *ta'ut* is sufficient to undo it at its root.

### R. Yishmael Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁנָּדַר מִבַּת אֲחוֹתוֹ הֲנָיָיה, וְהִכְנִיסוּהָ לְבֵית רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְיִיפּוּהָ. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: בְּנִי, מִזּוֹ נָדַרְתָּ? אָמַר לוֹ: לָאו, וְהִתִּירָהּ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** The Mishna relates: **And an incident** occurred **with regard to one who vowed** against deriving **benefit from the daughter of his sister,** as he did not wish to marry her. **And they brought her into** the **house of Rabbi Yishmael and he beautified her.** When she was later brought before the one who took the vow, **Rabbi Yishmael said to him: My son, did you vow** that you would not derive benefit **from this** woman? **He said to him: No, and Rabbi Yishmael permitted her** to him, as he demonstrated that the vow had been made in error.

The *mishna* then recounts a *ma'aseh*: *uma'aseh be-echad she-nadar mi-bat achoto hana'ah* — a certain man took a *neder* against deriving benefit from his niece. It was customary in that era for men to be encouraged to marry their nieces, and this *neder* was evidently a mechanism to escape that social pressure. They brought the woman to the house of Rabbi Yishmael, and he and his household beautified her — *ve-ipe'uha*. Rabbi Yishmael then said to the man: *beni, mi-zu nadarta?* — "My son, was it about this woman that you made your *neder*?" The man replied: *lav* — no, he would never have made a *neder* about a woman like this. Rabbi Yishmael permitted her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה בָּכָה רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְאָמַר: בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל נָאוֹת הֵן, אֶלָּא שֶׁהָעֲנִיּוּת מְנַוַּולְתָּן. וּכְשֶׁמֵּת רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הָיוּ בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל נוֹשְׂאוֹת קִינָה, וְאוֹמְרוֹת: ״בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּכֶינָה״, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר בְּשָׁאוּל: ״בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל שָׁאוּל בְּכֶינָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **At that time Rabbi Yishmael wept and said: The daughters of Israel are beautiful, but poverty makes them ugly. And when Rabbi Yishmael died, the daughters of Israel raised a lamentation, saying: Daughters of Israel, weep for Rabbi Yishmael. And it likewise states about Saul,** who also concerned himself with the welfare of the daughters of Israel: **"Daughters of Israel, weep over Saul,** who clothed you in scarlet with other delights, who put ornaments of gold upon your apparel" (II Samuel 1:24).

At that moment, Rabbi Yishmael wept and said: *bnos Yisrael na'os hein, ella she-ha-aniyus menaveltan* — the daughters of Israel are beautiful, but it is poverty that makes them appear unattractive. They are underfed, unable to bathe properly, without fine clothing. And when Rabbi Yishmael died, *hayu bnos Yisrael mekonenos kinah* — all the women of Israel would recite a lamentation, a formal *kinah* of poetic wailing. They declared: *bnos Yisrael al Rabbi Yishmael bechainah* — daughters of Israel, weep for Rabbi Yishmael. This is compared to the lament over King Shaul recorded in Scripture: *bnos Yisrael al Shaul bechainah*.

## Nolad Analysis

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַעֲשֶׂה לִסְתּוֹר?! חַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ כְּעוּרָה וְנַעֲשֵׂת נָאָה, שְׁחוֹרָה וְנַעֲשֵׂת לְבָנָה, קְצָרָה וְנַעֲשֵׂת אֲרוּכָּה. מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁנָּדַר מִבַּת אֲחוֹתוֹ וְהִכְנִיסוּהָ לְבֵית רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְיִיפּוּהָ וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara poses a question: Was **an incident** cited **to contradict** what was just taught? It first taught that if she was ugly and was later beautified, the vow is not dissolved, and then the mishna quoted an incident involving Rabbi Yishmael where he did dissolve the vow. The Gemara answers: The mishna **is incomplete and this is what it is teaching: Rabbi Yishmael says: Even if she was ugly and became beautiful, black and became white, short and became tall,** the vow can be dissolved. **An incident** occurred **with regard to one who vowed** against having benefit **from the daughter of his sister,** as he did not wish to marry her. **And they brought her into Rabbi Yishmael's house and he beautified her.**

The *gemara* now presses on the story: *maiseh listor* — is the *ma'aseh* a contradiction? The *mishna* began by saying that the permissibility of the *neder* is not because the woman became beautiful — that would be *nolad*. Yet the *ma'aseh* describes the man being shown a woman who has been made beautiful through cosmetic intervention. It sounds precisely like a case of *nolad* — she changed after the *neder* was made.

### Missing Text

The *gemara* resolves this through *chisorei mechsara* — there are missing words in the text, and the *mishna* must be read with an implicit addition.

### R. Yishmael Nolad

The reconstruction is: Rabbi Yishmael holds that *afilu ke'urah ve-na'aseis na'ah* — even if she was ugly at the time of the *neder* and subsequently became beautiful, one may still open with this as a *petach*. Rabbi Yishmael disagrees with those who would restrict this form of release only to cases where the woman was always beautiful and the man was simply mistaken. For Rabbi Yishmael, *poschin be-nolad* — one may use a circumstance that arose later as a basis for release — even in a case like this where perhaps the change was not entirely foreseeable. This aligns him with the position associated with Rabbi Eliezer earlier in the *masechta*, who permitted *petach* through *nolad*.

### Gold Tooth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: שֵׁן תּוֹתֶבֶת הָיְתָה לָהּ, וְעָשָׂה לָהּ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל שֵׁן שֶׁל זָהָב מִשֶּׁלּוֹ. כִּי שְׁכֵיב רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, פְּתַח עֲלֵיהּ הַהוּא סַפְדָנָא הָכִי: ״בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּכֶינָה הַמַּלְבִּישְׁכֶן וְכוּ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** It was **taught: She had a false tooth [*shen totevet*],** which disfigured her, **and Rabbi Yishmael made her a gold tooth from his own** money, thereby beautifying her. **When Rabbi Yishmael died, a certain eulogizer began** his eulogy **about him like this: Daughters of Israel, weep for Rabbi Yishmael, who clothed you.**

The *gemara* then specifies what the beautification actually consisted of: *shen shel cheves hayta lah* — she had a replacement tooth of some kind, presumably an unattractive prosthetic material available at the time. *Aseh lah Rabbi Yishmael shen shel zahav* — Rabbi Yishmael had it replaced with a gold tooth, and that single change transformed her appearance sufficiently that the man no longer recognized her as the woman about whom he had made his *neder*. This clarifies why the *gemara* reads the story as a *nolad* case: the woman genuinely changed after the *neder* was made, rather than always having been beautiful.

### Rabbi Yishmael's Eulogy

When Rabbi Yishmael died, the *gemara* records that a male eulogizer opened with the following *hesped*: *bnos Yisrael al Rabbi Yishmael bechainah* — daughters of Israel, weep for Rabbi Yishmael — *ha-malbishechem ve-chulei* — he who clothed you, and so forth. Interestingly, the *gemara* does not record the remainder of the verse; it trails off with *ve-chulei*. The eulogy echoes the words found in the book of Shmuel regarding the lament over Shaul — *bnos Yisrael al Shaul bechainah* — and the parallel is intentional: as Shaul provided material abundance for the women of Israel, so Rabbi Yishmael took a personal interest in the welfare and dignity of Jewish women.

## Bar Bavel Story

### Dialect Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לִדְבֵיתְהוּ ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאִי אַתְּ נֶהֱנֵית לִי עַד שֶׁתַּטְעִימִי תַּבְשִׁילֵךְ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וּלְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן״. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה טְעֵים, אֲמַר, קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה לַעֲשׂוֹת שָׁלוֹם בֵּין אִישׁ לְאִשְׁתּוֹ אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה שְׁמִי שֶׁנִּכְתַּב בִּקְדוּשָּׁה יִמָּחֶה עַל הַמַּיִם הַמְאָרְרִים בְּסָפֵק, וַאֲנִי עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person **who said to his wife: Benefiting from me is *konam*** for **you until you have given Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon your cooked food to taste,** so they can see for themselves what a bad cook you are. She brought the food to them, and **Rabbi Yehuda tasted** it, without concern for his honor. **He said:** This is an ***a fortiori*** inference: **And what** can be seen, **that** in order **to make peace between a man and his wife, the Torah said: My name, that is written in sanctity, shall be blotted out in the waters that curse,** as the words written on a scroll, including the name of God, were blotted out during the ceremony of preparing the water that a *sota* would drink. And this is so even **in** a case of where it is **uncertain** if this will bring peace between them, as she may or not be guilty of adultery. **I, all the more so,** should waive my honor in order to bring peace to this couple.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא טְעֵים, אֲמַר: יָמוּתוּ כׇּל בְּנֵי אַלְמָנָה, וְאַל יָזוּז שִׁמְעוֹן מִמְּקוֹמוֹ. וְעוֹד: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִתְרַגְּלִי לְמִינְדָּר.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, **Rabbi Shimon did not taste. He said: Let all the children of the widow die, and Shimon will not budge from his place.** In other words, the husband can die and leave his wife a widow and his children orphans, and let them die too, rather than have people belittle the dignity of Torah scholars by taking such vows. **And furthermore,** there is another reason for my refusal: **So that they should not become used to taking vows.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דְּאָמַר לִדְבֵיתְהוּ ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאִי אַתְּ נֶהֱנֵית לִי עַד שֶׁתָּרוֹקִּי בּוֹ בְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל״. אֲתָת וּרְקַק אַלְּבוּשֵׁיהּ. אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: וְהָא הַאי לְזִילוּתָא קָא מִיכַּוֵּין! אָמַר לֵיהּ: מִירָק עַל מָנֵי דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל זִילוּתָא רַבְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person **who said to his wife: Benefiting from me is *konam*** for **you until you have spat on Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. She came** to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **and spat on his clothing. Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: But this** man **intended the humiliation** of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, which is not achieved by spitting on his clothing. Ravina **said to him: Spittle on the clothing of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is a great humiliation** for him, and she has thereby fulfilled the vow.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לִדְבֵיתְהוּ ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאִי אַתְּ נֶהֱנֵית לִי עַד שֶׁתַּרְאִי מוּם יָפֶה שֶׁבִּיךְ לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֶּן רַבִּי יוֹסֵי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person **who said to his wife: Benefiting from me is *konam*** for **you until you show some beautiful [*yafeh*] part of you to Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei.** Rabbi Yishmael attempted to find something beautiful about the woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: שֶׁמָּא רֹאשָׁהּ נָאֶה? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: סְגַלְגַּל. שֶׁמָּא שְׂעָרָהּ נָאֶה? דּוֹמֶה לַאֲנִיצֵי פִּשְׁתָּן. שֶׁמָּא עֵינֶיהָ נָאוֹת? טְרוּטוֹת הֵן. שֶׁמָּא אׇזְנֶיהָ נָאוֹת? כְּפוּלוֹת הֵן. שֶׁמָּא חוֹטְמָהּ נָאֶה? בָּלוּם הוּא. שֶׁמָּא שִׂפְתוֹתֶיהָ נָאוֹת? עָבוֹת הֵן. שֶׁמָּא צַוָּארָהּ נָאֶה? שָׁקוּט הוּא. שֶׁמָּא כְּרֵיסָהּ נָאֶה? צָבֶה הוּא. שֶׁמָּא רַגְלֶיהָ נָאוֹת? רְחָבוֹת כְּשֶׁל אֲווֹזָא. שֶׁמָּא שְׁמָהּ נָאֶה? ״לִכְלוּכִית״ שְׁמָהּ. אָמַר לָהֶן: יָפֶה קוֹרִין אוֹתָהּ לִכְלוּכִית, שֶׁהִיא מְלוּכְלֶכֶת בְּמוּמִין, וְשַׁרְיַיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to** his students: **Perhaps her head is beautiful? They said to him:** It is **round [*segalgal*]. Perhaps her hair is beautiful?** They replied: Her hair **resembles stalks of flax. Perhaps her eyes are beautiful? They are narrow [*terutot*]. Perhaps her ears are beautiful? They are double** in size. **Perhaps her nose is beautiful? It is stubby. Perhaps her lips are beautiful? They are thick. Perhaps her neck is beautiful? It is low** and short. **Perhaps her stomach is beautiful? It is swollen. Perhaps her feet are beautiful?** They are **as wide as a goose's. Perhaps her name is beautiful? Her name is Likhlukhit. He said to them: It is fitting [*yafeh*]** that **she is called** by the name **Likhlukhit, as she is dirty [*melukhlekhet*] with blemishes, and he permitted her** to benefit from her husband, because she did have one beautiful feature, her fitting name.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא בַּר בָּבֶל דִּסְלֵיק לְאַרְעָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל, נְסֵיב אִיתְּתָא. אֲמַר לַהּ: בַּשִּׁילִי לִי תְּרֵי טַלְפֵי. בַּשִּׁילָה לֵיהּ תְּרֵי טַלְפֵי. רְתַח עֲלַהּ. לִמְחַר אֲמַר לַהּ: בַּשִּׁילִי לִי גְּרִיוָא. בַּשִּׁילָה לֵיהּ גְּרִיוָא. אֲמַר לַהּ: זִילִי אַיְיתִי לִי תְּרֵי בוּצִינֵי. אֲזַלַת וְאַיְיתַי לֵיהּ תְּרֵי שְׁרָגֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another incident: There was **a certain Babylonian who went up to Eretz Yisrael** and **married a woman** there. **He said to her: Cook two lentils,** i.e., some lentils, **for me. She cooked** exactly **two lentils for him. He grew angry with her. On the following day,** so that she would not repeat what she had done, **he said to her: Cook a *se'a* [*geriva*] for me,** intending: A large amount. **She cooked an** actual ***se'a* for him,** far more than what one person could eat. **He said to her: Go and bring me two *butzinei*,** intending small gourds, as *butzinei* are small gourds in the Aramaic dialect spoken in Babylonia. **She went and brought him two lamps [*sheraggei*],** called *butzinei* in the Aramaic dialect spoken in Eretz Yisrael.

> **Translation:** In anger, **he said to her: Go and break them on the head of the *bava*,** intending the gate, as *bava* means a gate in the Aramaic dialect spoken in Babylonia. She did not recognize this word. At that time, the Sage **Bava ben Buta was sitting as a judge at the gate. She went and broke them on his head,** as his name was Bava. **He said to her: What is this you have done? The *gemara* concludes the *masechta* with a famous and charming *ma'aseh* that, while seemingly a departure from the halakhic discussion, connects naturally to the overarching theme of *nedarim* — the interpretation of language, the gap between what one says and what one means, and the relationship between husband and wife. A man from Babylonia — *hahu bar Bavel* — went up to *Eretz Yisrael*, where they spoke Palestinian Aramaic rather than Babylonian Aramaic. The two dialects shared vocabulary but assigned different meanings to many terms. As one might say, the two communities were separated by a common language.

### Two Hooves/Lentils

He married a local woman. He said to her: *basheli li trei talsi* — "cook me two." In Babylonian Aramaic, this meant two legs or hooves of an animal — a substantial meat dish. In Palestinian Aramaic, the word could also mean lentil beans. She cooked him two lentils. Some explain that his "two" was simply a colloquial way of saying "some," and she took him quite literally.

### Escalation

He became angry — *ratah alah*. The next day, *basheli li garriva* — "cook me a large measure of it." She understood and cooked him an enormous quantity — taking his words literally once more. His frustration mounted.

### Melon vs. Candle

He then said: *zili ayiti li trei butzini* — "go get me two." In one Aramaic dialect, *butzina* means a melon; in the other, it means a lamp or candle. She brought him two candles — *ayita lei trei shargi*.

### Breaking on Head

At his wit's end, he told her: *zili tavrinhu al reisha d'Baba* — "go break them on the head of Baba." He meant: go break them at the gate, the *bava*, take them there. At that moment, Baba ben Buta happened to be sitting by the gate — *havai yativ Baba ben Buta ad d'baba* — adjudicating a court case. She went and broke the candles on his head.

The comic structure of the passage is unmistakable: *Baba* was sitting at the *baba*, and so the husband's instruction was fulfilled in the most literal and disastrous way possible.

### Pastoral Judgment

Baba ben Buta asked her: *mah deita avat* — what did you do? She replied: *kach tzivani ba'ali* — "that is what my husband commanded me." Baba ben Buta did not react with indignation.

### Husband's Will

Instead, he said to her: *asit retzon ba'alech* — you did your husband's will. This is the great irony of the episode: she demonstrably did *not* do what her husband intended, yet she did precisely what she understood him to say. Baba ben Buta chose to reframe the entire situation as one of good intent on all sides. He then blessed her: *ha-Makom yotzi mimcha shenei vanim k'Baba ben Buta* — may God bring forth from you two sons like Baba ben Buta.

The *gemara* closes the *masechta* here with *Hadran alach ha-Ma'adir* — the concluding formula of the tractate. The placement of this story at the very end is deeply apt. The entire *masechta* of *Nedarim* has been occupied with the question of how language is interpreted — what a person means when he speaks, how we assess the scope and intent of his words, and how *beit din* navigates the gap between utterance and intention. This final *ma'aseh* distills that theme into a scene of domestic miscommunication, where no one is acting in bad faith, yet the gap between what is said and what is understood produces chaos. Baba ben Buta's response — affirming the wife's good intent, reframing the situation as one of genuine effort rather than failure, and blessing the household — models a pastoral wisdom that transcends strict textual interpretation. Everyone was trying to do the right thing; the work now is to communicate more carefully.
