# Gittin 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Direct Appointment

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״נִכְתּוֹב גֵּט לְאִשְׁתְּךָ?״ וְאָמַר לָהֶן: ״כְּתוֹבוּ״; אָמְרוּ לַסּוֹפֵר וְכָתַב, וְלָעֵדִים וְחָתְמוּ; אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכְּתָבוּהוּ וַחֲתָמוּהוּ וּנְתָנוּהוּ לוֹ, וְחָזַר וּנְתָנוֹ לָהּ – הֲרֵי הַגֵּט בָּטֵל, עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר לַסּוֹפֵר ״כְּתוֹב״ וְלָעֵדִים ״חֲתוֹמוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If people **said to** the husband: **Shall we write a bill of divorce for your wife? And he said to them: Write** the document, and those people **told the scribe** to write it, **and he wrote** it **and** instructed **the witnesses** to sign it, **and they signed** it; **even if they wrote it, and signed it, and gave it to him, and he then gave it to** his wife, **the bill of divorce is void unless he** himself **says to the scribe: Write** the document, **and** he himself says **to the witnesses: Sign** the document.

The *mishna* at the top of 72a states, with particular explicitness, that when a husband instructs witnesses to write a *get*, they must carry out that task themselves — they cannot pass it on to a *sofer* and to other witnesses. The *mishna* concludes by requiring that the *sofer* and the witnesses hear the instruction directly from the husband. He must appoint them personally. This is entirely consistent with the position articulated earlier in the *gemara* regarding Rabbi Yosi's view of *mili lo mimseran leshaliach*: verbal agency cannot be transferred. You cannot delegate to a *shaliach* the authority to appoint someone else in your place; the appointment must flow directly from the husband.

### Three Stages

It is worth distinguishing carefully among the three levels of agency under discussion. The first is the case of *mili lo mimseran leshaliach* proper: the husband gives verbal agency to an agent, and that agent takes it upon himself to pass the responsibility to someone else. Here the agent is arrogating authority he was never given. The second case — *omer imru* — is a middle case: the husband explicitly instructs someone to appoint another party, making that person his *shaliach* to perform the appointment itself. The third is direct appointment of the *shaliach* in his presence with no intermediary.

Many *rishonim* hold that by ordinary *shlichus*, one may appoint an agent who is not present — as, for example, when someone authorizes another to inform a third party of an appointment, without personally appearing before that third party. But by *gittin*, an additional and more stringent requirement applies: given that the husband's *da'as* must be expressed directly in the *get*, and given the demand for *lishma*, there is a heightened requirement that the appointment be made face-to-face. This applies particularly — or perhaps exclusively — to the *shlichus* of writing the *get*, not merely delivering it.

### Lo Mibaya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כּוּלַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִבַּעְיָא הֵיכָא דְּלָא אֲמַר ״תְּנוּ״, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ אֲמַר ״תְּנוּ״ – לָא. וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא הֵיכָא דְּלָא אֲמַר לְבֵי תְלָתָא, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ אֲמַר לְבֵי תְּלָתָא – לָא. וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא הֵיכָא דְּלָא אֲמַר ״אִמְרוּ״, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ אֲמַר ״אִמְרוּ״ – נָמֵי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** an alternative explanation of the attribution of the mishna: The mishna in **its entirety is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who said that verbal directives cannot be delegated to an agent, **and it is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary,** as follows: **It is not necessary** to state that the bill of divorce is not valid in a case **where he did not say** to the agents: **Give** the document to my wife; **rather, even** if **he said** to the agents: **Give** the bill of divorce to my wife, the document is **not** valid. **And it is not necessary** to state the *halakha* in a case **where** the husband **did not say** his instructions **to three** people; **rather, even** if **he said** his instructions **to three** people, **no,** the bill of divorce is not valid. **And it is not necessary** to state the *halakha* in a case **where** the husband **did not say** to the agents: **Say** my instructions to a scribe, **but even** if **he said: Say** my instructions to a scribe, the bill of divorce is **also not** valid.

The *gemara* addresses an apparent tension within the *mishna*: the opening clause seems to leave room for alternative forms of agency that might work (saying *tenu* rather than *kisvu*, or addressing a *beis din*), while the concluding clause states categorically that nothing is valid unless the *sofer* and witnesses hear directly from the husband. How can the beginning suggest possibilities while the end forecloses everything?

The *gemara* offers what it calls *ella machvarta* — the clearest reading — attributing the *reisha* to Rabbi Meir, who allows some flexibility in agency, and the *seifa* to Rabbi Yosi. But Rav Ashi goes further, proposing that the entire *mishna* reflects Rabbi Yosi's position throughout. The structure, he explains, is *lo mibaya ka'amar* — a rhetorical escalation from the obvious case to the extreme. The *mishna* begins with a straightforward instance where agency clearly fails: the husband said *kisvu* (write) but not *tenu* (give), said it to two people rather than three, and did not invoke *imru* (the explicit authorization to appoint). That is obviously ineffective. But the *mishna* is telling us, through this escalation, that even the less obvious cases also fail: even if he said *tenu*, even if he addressed a *beis din*, even if he explicitly authorized the agent to appoint another via *omer imru* — none of it suffices. The only valid course is direct appointment of the *sofer* and *edim* by the husband himself.

This reading by Rav Ashi thus presents a unified *mishna* entirely in the voice of Rabbi Yosi.

The *Rambam* grapples directly with the practical *halacha* of *omer imru*. He distinguishes between *get batel* — invalid on a *d'oraisa* level — and *get pasul* — defective on a *d'rabbonan* level. In a case of *mili lo mimseran leshaliach*, where two people were told "write the *get*" and they transferred the task to others, the *Rambam* rules it a *get batel*. But in the case of *omer imru* — where the husband explicitly instructed them to appoint a *sofer* to write and witnesses to sign, and they did so — the *Rambam* rules it a *get pasul*, adding that the matter requires much deliberation, as it borders on *get batel*. The *Ra'avad* takes a more decisive position: he reads our *gemara* in *perek Kordaikos* as concluding that the *stam mishna* follows Rabbi Yosi, and that *omer imru* therefore renders the *get batel* outright. The *Rambam*'s uncertainty is thus a meaningful point of contention.

### Yishme'u Kolo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי: כָּתַב סוֹפֵר לִשְׁמָהּ וְחָתְמוּ עֵדִים לִשְׁמָהּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכְּתָבוּהוּ וַחֲתָמוּהוּ וּנְתָנוּהוּ לוֹ וּנְתָנוֹ לָהּ – הֲרֵי הַגֵּט בָּטֵל, עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁמְעוּ קוֹלוֹ שֶׁיֹּאמַר לַסּוֹפֵר ״כְּתוֹב״ וְלָעֵדִים ״חֲתוֹמוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: It **is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:7–8) **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Ashi** that Rabbi Yosei invalidated the bill of divorce even if the husband said: Tell my instructions to a scribe, as it is taught: If **the scribe wrote** the bill of divorce **for her sake and the witnesses signed it for her sake** then **even though they wrote it, and they signed it, and they gave it to** the husband **and he gave it to** his wife, **the bill of divorce is void until they hear** the husband's **voice when he says to the scribe: Write** the document for the sake of my wife, **and to the witnesses: Sign** the document for the sake of my wife.

The *gemara* continues with a *baraita* reinforcing Rav Ashi's reading: even if the *sofer* wrote *lishma* and the witnesses signed *lishma*, and the document was transmitted to the husband who gave it to the wife — it remains a *get pasul* — until *yishme'u kolo*, until they hear his voice directly. The *baraita* specifies that he must say to the *sofer* "write" and to the witnesses "sign," in his own voice, addressed to them personally.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יִשְׁמְעוּ״ – לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִמַּאן דְּאָמַר, מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּאוֹמֵר: ״אִמְרוּ״. ״קוֹלוֹ״ – לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַב כָּהֲנָא אֲמַר רַב.
>
> **Translation:** The inference from the *baraita* is that it uses the term: Until **they hear,** serves **to exclude** the opinion of **the one who said: Rabbi Yosei concedes in** a case where the husband **says: Say** my instructions to the scribe; because the scribe and the witnesses must hear the husband themselves. Furthermore, the *baraita* uses the term: **His voice, to exclude that which Rav Kahana** says that **Rav says,** that a husband may issue written instructions to the scribe and witnesses. According to the *baraita* the instructions must be verbal.

The *gemara* explains that the inclusion of the word *kolo* — "his voice" — serves to exclude the view of Rav Kahana citing Rav, who held that a *cheireish* (deaf-mute) who is capable of writing can be considered to have sufficient communicative capacity to appoint a *shaliach* and is not legally classified as a *cheireish* for this purpose. The *gemara* rejects this: the requirement of *kolo* teaches that communicating only through writing does not reassign one's personal legal status. One who is classified as a *cheireish* remains so, and cannot appoint a *shaliach* for the *get*. This ruling carries significant practical weight and presents a challenge for contemporary approaches that seek to redefine the halakhic status of a *cheireish* in light of modern communication capabilities for the deaf.

### Non-Verbal Comm

*Tosafos* raises the obvious difficulty: if the *baraita* requires *kolo* — literally "voice" — does this not also exclude the case in an earlier *mishna* where a mute husband (who is not a *cheireish*, simply unable to speak) communicates by nodding his head? The *mishna* there validates such signaling. Does this *baraita* contradict that ruling?

*Tosafos* answers that it is logically evident — and supported by the authority of the *mishna* — that nodding in that context is equivalent to *kol*: it is direct, unambiguous communication. The *gemara* does not intend to assign special sanctity to acoustic voice as such. For *shlichus* by *get*, there is no verse or textual anchor demanding specifically verbal communication the way there is a *pasuk* requiring verbal *eidus*. Therefore, *Tosafos* says, as long as we know with certainty what the husband is communicating and it is being directly expressed rather than inferred or relayed through another party, direct non-verbal communication would suffice for the appointment of the *sofer* and witnesses.

The only function of the word *kolo* in this context, then, is the narrower exclusion of Rav Kahana's position: it forecloses the argument that writing by a *cheireish* serves to reclassify his personal status. But it does not elevate voice per se above other forms of direct, intentional communication by a *bar da'as*.

## Conditional Get

### Four Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ אִם מַתִּי״; ״זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ מֵחוֹלִי זֶה״; ״זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ לְאַחַר מִיתָה״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. ״מֵהַיּוֹם אִם מַתִּי״; ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו אִם מַתִּי״ – הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce if I die,** or: **This is your bill of divorce** if I die **from this illness,** or: **This is your bill of divorce after** my **death,** then it is as if **he said nothing,** since a bill of divorce is valid only if it takes effect before the husband's death. But if the husband said to his wife: This is your bill of divorce **from today if I die,** or: This is your bill of divorce **from now if I die,** then **this is** a valid **bill of divorce,** because once he dies, the bill of divorce retroactively applies from when he made this statement.

The *mishna* now turns to a different dimension of *gittin*: the *tenai*, or stipulation, in a *get* given on the deathbed. The husband, gravely ill, wishes to divorce his wife — not necessarily because he wishes to end the marriage, but because he fears leaving her in a position where *yibbum* becomes obligatory. He wants the *get* to take effect only if he dies from this illness. The *mishna* presents several formulations, each with distinct legal consequences.

The four cases are: (1) *ze gittech im mati* — "this is your *get* if I die"; (2) *ze gittech mecholi zeh* — "this is your *get* from this illness"; (3) *ze gittech le'achar mitah* — "this is your *get* after I die"; and (4) *mehayom im mati* or *me'achshav im mati* — "this is your *get* as of today if I die" or "as of now if I die." The first three, without the word *mehayom*, are declared *lo amar klum* — he said nothing of legal effect. The fourth works.

### Im/When

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַלְמָא ״אִם מַתִּי״ כִּ״לְאַחַר מִיתָה״ דָּמֵי, וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: ״מֵהַיּוֹם אִם מַתִּי״; ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו אִם מַתִּי״ – אַלְמָא ״אִם מַתִּי״ לָאו כִּ״לְאַחַר מִיתָה״ דָּמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: If one says to his wife: This is your bill of divorce if I die, then it is as if he said nothing. The Gemara deduces: **Apparently,** the formulation: **If I die,** is **considered** to be **like** the formulation: The bill of divorce will be valid only **after** my **death. And afterward** the mishna **teaches:** If he said: This is your bill of divorce **from today if I die,** or: This is your bill of divorce **from now if I die,** then this is a valid bill of divorce. **Apparently,** the formulation: **If I die,** is **not considered** to be **like** the formulation: The bill of divorce will be valid only **after** my **death.** As explained previously in the mishna, a bill of divorce which takes effect only after the husband's death is not a valid bill of divorce. There is an apparent contradiction as to the meaning of the term: If I die.

The pivot between valid and invalid turns on the word *im* ("if") and the effect of *mehayom* ("from today"). The word *im* is ambiguous: it can mean a genuine conditional ("if I die") or it can mean "when I die" — that is, a future certainty rather than a condition. Without *mehayom*, *im mati* is interpreted as "when I die," which renders the *get* effective only at the moment of death — by which point the husband is no longer alive to give it. That is legally incoherent. The addition of *mehayom* transforms *im* into a genuine conditional: "as of today, on the condition that I die." That is a valid *tenai*.

### Mecholi Zeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״אִם מַתִּי״ – שְׁתֵּי לְשׁוֹנוֹת מַשְׁמַע; מַשְׁמַע כְּמֵעַכְשָׁיו, וּמַשְׁמַע כִּלְאַחַר מִיתָה; אָמַר לָהּ ״מֵהַיּוֹם״ – כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לָהּ ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ דָּמֵי, לֹא אָמַר לָהּ ״מֵהַיּוֹם״ – כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לָהּ ״לְאַחַר מִיתָה״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The expression: **If I die, indicates two** different **meanings.** It **indicates** the same meaning **as** one who says: **From now,** i.e., that the bill of divorce will take effect after death retroactively from now. **And** it **indicates** the same meaning **as** one who says: **After** my **death,** i.e., that the bill of divorce will take effect only after his death. If the husband **said to** his wife: **From today** if I die, it is **considered** to be **like one who says to her: From now,** conditional upon my death. But if **he did not say to her: From today,** it is **considered** to be **like one who says to her: After** my **death,** and the bill of divorce is void because it takes effect only after his death.

The phrase *mecholi zeh* requires separate analysis. The full intended statement is *im mati mecholi zeh* — "if I die from this illness" — the husband qualifying that his death from this particular illness, not death generally at some future time, is the operative condition. The *mishna* presents cases where only half that phrase was used, using *mecholi zeh* as shorthand for the conditional. Without *mehayom*, even *mecholi zeh* is read as a temporal designation — "after this illness" — rather than a true condition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ מֵהַיּוֹם, אִם מַתִּי מֵחוֹלִי זֶה״, וְעָמַד וְהָלַךְ בַּשּׁוּק, וְחָלָה וָמֵת – אוֹמְדִין אוֹתוֹ, אִם מֵחֲמַת חוֹלִי הָרִאשׁוֹן מֵת – הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינוֹ גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** If he said: **This is your bill of divorce from today if I die from this illness,** and he recovered, **and he arose and walked in the market, but** then **became ill** again **and died,** the court **assesses him. If he died because of the first illness** then **this is** a valid **bill of divorce,** as his conditional statement was fulfilled, **but if not,** i.e., if he was cured from the first illness and died from another illness, then **it is not** a valid **bill of divorce.**

The *mishna* further addresses what happens when, after giving the *get*, the husband recovers sufficiently to go out in the marketplace, then falls ill again and dies. The *gemara* requires an assessment: *omdinoso im machmas choli harishon mes* — we evaluate whether he ultimately died from the original illness. If medical judgment concludes that the relapse was a continuation of the first illness, the *get* is valid. If not, it is not.

### Abaye's Answer

Abaye resolves the apparent contradiction between the opening of the *mishna* — which treats *im mati* and *le'achar mitah* as equally ineffective — and the later clause, which distinguishes between *mehayom im mati* (valid) and *mehayom ule'achar mitah* (invalid). If without *mehayom* the two phrases mean the same thing, why with *mehayom* do they diverge?

Abaye answers that *im mati* carries two possible meanings: *mashma k'me'achshav* (it can be read as "as of now, conditionally") and *mashma k'le'achar mita* (it can be read as "when I die"). Without *mehayom*, the ambiguity resolves against validity — we default to the meaning of "when I die." With *mehayom*, the phrase is disambiguated and reads as a genuine *tenai*. By contrast, *le'achar mitah* is unambiguous — it explicitly means "after I die" — and therefore even when *mehayom* is added, the two halves point in opposite directions, and the *get* remains a *safek*.

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ אִם מַתִּי״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: וְחוֹלֶצֶת.
>
> **Translation:** If one says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce if I die,** then it is as if **he said nothing. Rav Huna says: But** if her husband died without children this woman must **perform *ḥalitza*** and not enter into a levirate marriage because perhaps it is a valid bill of divorce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם״ קָתָנֵי! לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם – דַּאֲסִירָא לְעָלְמָא; וּלְיָבָם נָמֵי אֲסִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a challenge: **But isn't** the expression: It is as if **he said nothing, taught** in the mishna? The Gemara answers: The mishna means that it is as if **he said nothing** with regard to the fact that **she is** still **forbidden to everyone** after the death of her husband, and is bound by a levirate bond. **And she is also forbidden to the *yavam*** because perhaps the bill of divorce was valid, in which case she has no levirate bond.

Rav Huna rules that even in the case where the *mishna* says *lo amar klum* — where *im mati* without *mehayom* is declared entirely without effect — the wife still requires *chalitzah* and may not perform *yibbum*. The *gemara* challenges this: if the *mishna* says *lo amar klum*, it is completely invalid, and *yibbum* should be permitted. The *gemara* responds: *lo amar klum d'asira l'alma* — the statement "said nothing" means only that the *get* is not effective to permit her to remarry; she remains a *safek* with respect to *yibbum* as well.

The *gemara* presses further: the *mishna*'s later clause explicitly uses *choletzes velo mityabemet* to indicate a *safek* case. The fact that the *reisha* does not use that language implies it is definitively without effect and *yibbum* is permitted. Rav Huna therefore cannot be reading the *mishna* — he must be departing from it, ruling instead in accordance with Rabbi Yosi in a different sense, as the *gemara* will elaborate.

## Date in Document

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הוּנָא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: זְמַנּוֹ שֶׁל שְׁטָר מוֹכִיחַ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **But Rav Huna** was not explaining the words of the mishna; the statement **that he said is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who says** as a principle: **The date** written **in a document proves** when **it** takes effect. Therefore, since the bill of divorce bears that day's date, it takes effect immediately, even though it is not stated explicitly.

This brings the *gemara* to a deeper tension. If Rav Huna holds that even *im mati* alone generates a *safek* rather than being truly worthless, he must be departing from the *mishna* and relying on a different halakhic principle. The *gemara* identifies this principle as that of Rabbi Yosi — but a different Rabbi Yosi from the one discussed in the first section: *d'amar zmano shel shtar mochiach alav*, "the date of a document proves what it intends."

### Zmano Shel Shtar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ חֲלַשׁ, עוּל לְגַבֵּיהּ רַב הוּנָא וְרַב נַחְמָן; אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַב נַחְמָן, בְּעִי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, אוֹ אֵין הֲלָכָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי לָא יָדַעְנָא, הֲלָכָה אֶיבְעֵי מִינֵּיהּ?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ בְּעִי מִינֵּיהּ הֲלָכָה, וְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אֲנָא אָמֵינָא לָךְ. בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אֲמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. לְבָתַר דִּנְפַק אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – דְּקָסָבַר: זְמַנּוֹ שֶׁל שְׁטָר מוֹכִיחַ עָלָיו!
>
> **Translation:** **But when Rabba bar Avuh was ill Rav Huna and Rav Naḥman entered** to visit **him. Rav Huna said to Rav Naḥman: Ask of him, Rabba bar Avuh:** Is **the *halakha* in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei or** is **the *halakha* not** in accordance with his opinion? Rav Naḥman **said to** Rav Huna: **I do not know the reason** for the opinion held **by Rabbi Yosei,** and **you ask me** to ask Rabba bar Avuh the ***halakha*?** Rav Huna **said to** Rav Naḥman: **You ask him** the ***halakha*, and I will tell you the reason** for the opinion held **by Rabbi Yosei** afterward. Rav Naḥman **asked of** Rabba bar Avuh what the *halakha* is. Rabba bar Avuh **said to him: So said Rav:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. After he left** Rav Huna **said to** Rav Naḥman: **This is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei, as he holds: The date** written **in a document proves** when **it** takes effect. From this incident it is clear that Rav Huna holds that the *halakha* is in accordance with Rabbi Yosei, as Rav Huna certainly accepted the opinion that Rabba bar Avuh said in the name of Rav.

In *Bava Batra*, the *gemara* deals with a parallel case involving a *shachiv meira* who gives a gift of property with the formulation *le'achar mitah* — "after I die." Ordinarily this fails, since a post-mortem gift has no legal mechanism. The Rabbis say the document is void. But Rabbi Yosi says: no — the document bears a date, and that date is a legally operative statement that the gift takes effect as of today. The document is therefore to be read as *mehayom ule'achar mita* — the *peirot* (fruits) transfer immediately and the *guf* (title) after death — a valid legal structure for deathbed gifts of property. *Zmano shel shtar mochiach alav*: the date in the document proves the intent that it take effect as of the date written.

Rav Huna's position on the *get* is that the same logic applies: even if the husband said only *im mati* or *le'achar mita* verbally, the date in the *get* functions halakhically as a *mehayom* statement, generating at minimum a *safek* — perhaps the *get* is fully valid.

The *gemara* then recounts the episode of Rabbah bar Avuha's illness, where Rav Huna asked Rav Nachman to visit him and inquire about the *halacha* regarding Rabbi Yosi's position. Rav Nachman demurred, saying he did not even understand Rabbi Yosi's reasoning; how could he ask about the *halacha*? Rav Huna replied: you ask the *halacha*, I will tell you the reason. Rav Nachman went, and Rabbah bar Avuha reported: *hachi amar Rav*, Rav himself said — *halacha k'Rabbi Yosi*. Afterward, Rav Huna explained the reason: *d'kasavar zmano shel shtar mochiach alav* — the date in the document is itself a halakhic statement of when the document takes effect.

### Written vs. Verbal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, חֲלִיצָה נָמֵי לָא תִּיבְעֵי! וְכִי תֵּימָא מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ לְרַב הוּנָא אִי הִלְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹ אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי; וּמִי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If** Rav Huna's statement is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** then the woman **should also not require *ḥalitza*,** as in his opinion the bill of divorce is entirely valid and there is no levirate bond at all. **And if you would say** that **Rav Huna is uncertain if the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei or** if **the *halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** and on account of this he was stringent in accordance with both opinions, i.e., the one which requires *ḥalitza* and the one which forbids levirate marriage, this also presents a difficulty. **But is** Rav Huna in fact **uncertain?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ אִי הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּעַל פֶּה, אוֹ אֵין הֲלָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is necessary to say as follows: Rav Huna **is uncertain if** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** when the husband adds **a verbal** condition when giving the document, **or** if the ***halakha*** is **not** in accordance with Rabbi Yosei's opinion in such a case.

If Rav Huna rules like Rabbi Yosi, and Rabbi Yosi holds that the date combined with even a verbal *le'achar mita* is equivalent to *mehayom le'achar mita*, why is Rav Huna's position expressed as a *safek* rather than a definitive ruling?

The *gemara* identifies the precise point of uncertainty. In the *Bava Batra* case, both the date and the conditional clause (*im mati* or *le'achar mita*) appear within the written document. They are co-equal textual elements, and their combination yields the reading *mehayom ule'achar mita*. In the case of a *get*, the date is in the document but the husband's verbal statement is external to it. The question is whether Rabbi Yosi's principle applies even when the *tenai* is oral and the *mehayom* is derived only from the written date — or whether the verbal statement operates independently of the written date and is interpreted on its own terms as *le'achar mita*, without modification.

This same question would arise in any *shachiv meira* case where the dated document is handed over with a verbal condition: does the date in the written document modify the external verbal statement, or not? This is the doubt Rav Huna could not resolve.

### Halacha K'RY

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמִי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ?! וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ אִם מַתִּי״ וְ״שֶׁאֲנִי מֵת״ – הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט. ״כְּשֶׁאָמוּת״ וּ״לְאַחַר מִיתָה״ – אֵין זֶה גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is** Rav Huna **uncertain** with regard to this matter? **But didn't Rava say:** If one says to his wife: **This is hereby your bill of divorce if I die, or:** "This is your bill of divorce **when I am dead,** then **this is** a valid **bill of divorce?** If he said to her: **When I die,** or: **After** my **death,** then **this is not** a valid **bill of divorce.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵימָא דְּאָמַר לַהּ ״מֵהַיּוֹם״, וְרַבָּנַן; צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר?! וְהָתְנַן: ״מֵהַיּוֹם אִם מַתִּי״ – הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט! וְאֶלָּא לָאו דְּלָא אָמַר לַהּ ״מֵהַיּוֹם״, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances? If we say that he said to her:** This is your bill of divorce **from today** if I die, **and** Rava's statement is in accordance with opinion of **the Rabbis,** which is the unattributed opinion of the mishna, is it **necessary** that this *halakha* **be said by Rava,** an *amora*? **But didn't we learn** explicitly in the mishna that if he said: This is your bill of divorce **from today if I die,** then **it is** a valid **bill of divorce? But rather, is it not** speaking about a case **where he did not say** the term: **From today,** when he gave the bill of divorce, **and** Rava's statement is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei? Conclude from it** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרָבָא פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, לְרַב הוּנָא מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is obvious to Rava** that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei even when the husband added a verbal condition. **But** according **to Rav Huna,** it **is uncertain** whether or not this is the *halakha*.

The *gemara* challenges this, arguing that Rav Huna should not have a *safek* at all. Rava rules that if one says *harei ze gittech im mati* — "this is your *get* if I die" — it is a valid *get*; but if one says *kishe'amut* — "when I die" — or *le'achar mita*, it is not. The *gemara* asks: when Rava validates *im mati*, what scenario does he mean? If he means *mehayom im mati*, that is merely repeating what the *mishna* already established. It must therefore mean that Rava validates *im mati* even without *mehayom* — ruling like Rabbi Yosi that the written date combines with the verbal *im mati* to produce a valid *get*. This suggests we rule like Rabbi Yosi even in the case of a verbal condition combined with a written date, which would mean Rav Huna should have no *safek*.

The *gemara* deflects this: Rava's ruling may still be referring to the case where *mehayom* was said explicitly — the *mishna* already knew this worked, but Rava was introducing new vocabulary. The *mishna* used the phrase *im mati*; Rava tells us that *she'ani meis* ("because I am dying") is treated like *im mati* and works when combined with *mehayom*. But *kishe'amut* ("when I die") is categorically equivalent to *le'achar mita* and does not work even with *mehayom*. Rava's contribution was halakhic taxonomy — which phrases count as conditional (*im mati*-type) and which count as temporal (*le'achar mita*-type) — not a ruling on the question of verbal condition versus written date.

Accordingly, the *gemara* concludes: for Rava the matter is clear in one direction; for Rav Huna the question of whether we rule like Rabbi Yosi when the *tenai* is verbal and the date is only in the document remains genuinely open.

### Rava's Phrases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם דַּאֲמַר לַהּ ״מֵהַיּוֹם״ – וְרַבָּנַן, וְהָנֵי לִישָּׁנֵי אִיצְטְרִיכִי לֵיהּ: ״שֶׁאֲנִי מֵת״ – כְּ״אִם מַתִּי״ דָּמֵי; ״כְּשֶׁאָמוּת״ – כִּ״לְאַחַר מִיתָה״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **Actually,** Rava's statement is referring to a case **where he said to her:** This is your bill of divorce **from today, and** it is in accordance with opinion of **the Rabbis. And it was necessary for** Rava to state his ruling to teach the *halakha* of **these expressions** not stated in the mishna: First, the husband saying: **When I am dead,** is **considered** to be **like** the expression: **If I die.** Second, the husband saying: **When I die,** is **considered** to be **like** the expression: **After** my **death.**

Rava's analysis organizes the relevant formulations into two categories. Phrases that function as a genuine conditional — equivalent to *im mati* — include *she'ani meis* ("as I am dying," reflecting the present precipitous condition). When combined with *mehayom*, these produce a valid *get*. Phrases that function as a pure temporal designation — equivalent to *le'achar mita* — include *kishe'amut* ("when I die"), which even when combined with *mehayom* does not yield a valid *get*, because the two elements point in opposite directions without producing a true *tenai*. The *gemara* also considers an alternative version of Rav Huna's position: that his statement about *chalitzah* applies not to the *reisha* of the *mishna* (*im mati* alone) but to the *seifa* (*le'achar mita* alone). According to Rabbi Yosi, a dated *get* with the verbal *le'achar mita* would be equivalent to *mehayom ule'achar mita* — which is itself a *safek* — and therefore, even on Rabbi Yosi's view, requires *chalitzah*.

## Rabbi vs. RY

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״ – גֵּט וְאֵינוֹ גֵּט; וְאִם מֵת – חוֹלֶצֶת, וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** If the husband says to his wife: This is your bill of divorce **from today and after** my **death,** then it is uncertain whether his primary intention was for the bill of divorce to take effect that day, in which case it is a valid bill of divorce, or if his primary intention was that it should take effect after his death and is therefore not valid. The *halakha* is that there is uncertainty whether it is a valid **bill of divorce or not** a valid **bill of divorce. And if he dies** without children his wife must **perform *ḥalitza*,** since perhaps the bill of divorce is not valid and she is bound by the levirate bond and may not remarry without first performing *ḥalitza*. **But she may not enter into levirate marriage,** since perhaps the bill of divorce is valid, and it is prohibited for a divorcée to marry her brother-in-law.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: ״כִּתְבוּ וּתְנוּ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתִּי, אִם לֹא בָּאתִי מִיכָּן וְעַד שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ״, כְּתָבוּהוּ בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ וּנְתָנוּהוּ לְאַחַר שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ – אֵינוֹ גֵּט; רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כָּזֶה – גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (76b): If one says: **Write and give a bill of divorce to my wife if I have not come from now until** the conclusion of **twelve months,** and the court **wrote** the bill of divorce **within twelve months and gave** it to the wife **after twelve months, it is not** a valid **bill of divorce. Rabbi Yosei** disagrees and **says: A case like this** is a valid **bill of divorce.**

The *gemara* now surfaces what appears to be a further tension. We have established that Rabbi Yosi holds *zmano shel shtar mochiach alav* — the date in the document functions as *mehayom*. Rabbi, by contrast, holds that even the explicit formulation *mehayom ule'achar mita* is a valid *get*. One might have thought that Rabbi Yosi, given his generous reading of the date in a document, would also embrace Rabbi's reading of *mehayom ule'achar mita* as a valid *tenai*. Why doesn't he?

### Two Kulot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא רַבִּי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, וְלָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **does not hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Yosei does not hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. Each has a unique, albeit similar, opinion.

The *gemara* resolves this by establishing that these are two entirely independent *kulot* — two distinct leniencies — and each authority holds only his own, not the other's. Knowing that someone is lenient in one halakhic domain tells us nothing about whether he is lenient in another.

### Rabbi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי לָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – דְּקָתָנֵי: ״כָּזֶה – גֵּט״, לְמַעוֹטֵי דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי; וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי לָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי – דְּקָתָנֵי: ״כָּזֶה – גֵּט״, לְמַעוֹטֵי דְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **does not hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, as it teaches** in a *baraita*: If the husband said: This is your bill of divorce from today and after my death, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says: A case **like this** is a valid **bill of divorce** and there is no need for the wife to perform *ḥalitza*. By emphasizing the term: Like this, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi intends **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei,** who is uncertain with regard to the matter. **And** similarly, **Rabbi Yosei did not hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as it teaches** in a mishna: Rabbi Yosei says: A case **like this** is a valid **bill of divorce, to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

Rabbi's position is that the explicit formulation *mehayom ule'achar mita* — even though it points in two directions and the Rabbis declare it a *safek* — is to be read as a valid *tenai*, a fully effective *get*. The reasoning is that *mehayom* establishes the effective moment and *le'achar mita* specifies the condition. Rabbi chooses to interpret the phrase as a conditional rather than as a contradiction. This is reflected in a *baraita*: *mehayom ule'achar mita, get ve'eino get, divrei chachamim; Rabbi omer k'zeh get* — the Sages call it a doubtful *get*, but Rabbi says it is a *get*. That *k'zeh get* — "like this is a *get*" — is to the exclusion of Rabbi Yosi's position; Rabbi is articulating his own distinct *kula*.

### Kisvu Vetenu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״ – גֵּט וְאֵינוֹ גֵּט, דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים; רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: כָּזֶה – גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the *baraita* and mishna that demonstrate the difference of opinion between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Yosei. **What is** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a husband says to his wife: This is your bill of divorce **from today and after** my **death,** then there is uncertainty whether **it is a bill of divorce or** whether **it is not a bill of divorce,** and the wife must perform *ḥalitza*. This is **the statement of the Rabbis. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi disagrees and **says:** A case **like this** is a valid **bill of divorce** without any uncertainty, and there is no need for the wife to perform *ḥalitza*.

Rabbi Yosi's independent *kula* operates in a completely different case. The *mishna* discusses a husband who says *kisvu u'snu get le'ishti im lo basi mikan v'ad yod beis chodesh* — "write and give a *get* to my wife if I don't return within twelve months." The Rabbis hold that since *im* here means "when," and the condition is not met until twelve months have elapsed, the writing too must wait. A *get* written during the twelve-month period and given afterward is therefore invalid. Rabbi Yosi disagrees: *k'zeh get* — he reads the instruction as having an implicit comma. *Kisvu* — write, immediately; *u'snu get le'ishti im lo basi* — and give it to my wife when I haven't returned. The conditional *im* applies only to *u'snu* (the giving), not to *kisvu* (the writing). Therefore, writing may begin immediately.

The *gemara* notes that the appearance of *k'zeh get* in both cases — Rabbi in the *mehayom ule'achar mita* passage and Rabbi Yosi in the *kisvu vetenu* passage — is deliberate: each *k'zeh get* is also a *l'mi'utei*, an exclusion of the other's position. Rabbi Yosi says *k'zeh get* to indicate that his *kula* applies in the *kisvu vetenu* case — but *not* Rabbi's *kula* in the *mehayom ule'achar mita* case. And Rabbi says *k'zeh get* to indicate that his *kula* in the *mehayom ule'achar mita* case is valid — but *not* Rabbi Yosi's principle of *zmano shel shtar mochiach alav*.

The *gemara*'s conclusion is thus nuanced: two independent lenient positions have been developed, one by Rabbi and one by Rabbi Yosi, each operative in its own halakhic domain. Neither authority endorses the other's *kula*, and the two positions are not additive. What emerges from our *mishna*, when viewed through this analysis, is a careful map of where each leniency applies and where the standard ruling — that a *get* taking effect only after death is no *get* — continues to govern.
