# Chagigah 25b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Produce Processing Times

### Pressing Season Trust

The *amud* opens with a principle that begins to dissolve the rigid distinctions between *amei ha-aretz* and *chaverim* regarding purity: *u-vish'at ha-gitot ne'emanim al ha-terumah* — at the time of the pressing of the grapes and the olives, common people are believed even with respect to *terumah*. This leniency operates across all geographical regions, unlike the believability regarding *kodesh*, which is confined to Judea on account of its proximity to the *Beit Ha-mikdash*. Two reinforcing rationales underlie this rule. First, as Rashi notes, the pressing season is a time when everyone collectively immersed their vessels in the *mikvah* and exercised heightened care, creating a stronger presumption of *taharah* across the board. Second — and Rashi will make this explicit shortly — there is a social policy at work: without this leniency, *kohanim* who are *chaverim* would be effectively excluded from receiving the great bulk of *terumah* produced during harvest season.

### Priest Livelihood Policy

The *gemara* immediately raises a contradiction. A *baraita* teaches that one who finishes pressing his olives should set aside an unpressed basket and give it to a poor *kohen* who is a *chaver*. The implication is striking: the reason to give it in unpressed form is precisely that unpressed olives have not come into contact with liquid and are therefore not susceptible to *tumah*. If the *am ha-aretz* were believed at the time of pressing, there would be no need for this workaround — the *kohen* could simply accept the pressed oil. The *baraita* thus seems to indicate that even during the pressing season one is not believed regarding *tumah* and *taharah*.

Rav Nachman resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya ha be-charfi ha be-afli* — the two cases are not in conflict but simply address different circumstances. The *baraita* concerns a late harvest, one pressed outside the normal season, and thus outside the period of heightened communal care and policy-driven leniency. When Rav Adda bar Abba pressed for a concrete example, Rav Nachman replied: *kegon oson shel Beis Avicha* — like the olives from your own father's house, which were known to ripen late and were therefore always pressed after the standard season.

### Wine vs. Grain Vessels

Rav Yosef adds a geographic dimension, suggesting that the rule of pressing-season believability was stated specifically in the context of the Galilee — *be-Galil lo shanu* — and does not apply there. This requires some explanation. We already understood why *kodesh* believability was limited to Judea: proximity to the *Beit Ha-mikdash* created conditions that did not obtain in Galilee. But why should pressing-season *terumah* believability differ by region? Abaye challenges this position by citing a *baraita*: *Ever ha-Yarden ve-ha-Galil harei hen ke-Yehudah le-terumah* — Transjordan and Galilee are treated like Judea for purposes of *terumah*. The *baraita* explicitly states that in those regions people are believed about wine during the wine-pressing season, and about oil during the oil-pressing season — but not each during the other's season.

### Judea-Galilee Split

The geographical question thus resolves in favor of universality. The Galilean distinction Rav Yosef proposed does not hold. The pressing-season leniency for *terumah* applies regardless of location.

### Unified Baraita Rule

Having examined Rav Nachman's resolution and the *baraita*, the *gemara* concludes: *ella machvarta kedi-shani me-ikara* — the original answer was preferable. The clear principle is that at the pressing season, people are believed about *terumah* everywhere, and the *baraita*'s concern was only an exceptional case of out-of-season pressing.

## Post-Season Restrictions

### Outside Season Ban

Once the pressing season — *sha'at ha-gitot* — passes, the leniency lapses entirely. *Avru ha-gitot ve-ha-badidim, ve-heivi lo chavis shel yayin lo yekabelenu* — if an *am ha-aretz* brings a *chaver* a barrel of wine after that time has passed, the *chaver* may not accept it. This holds even if it is the exact same barrel that would have been accepted during the pressing season. One might wonder: hasn't the wine simply had more opportunity to become *tamei* with the passage of time? This is a fair observation, and it points to how the rule is somewhat abstracted into a formal principle: the pressing season creates an institutional window of believability, not a calculation of actual likelihood of *taharah*.

### Heir Division Case

Against this backdrop, Rav Sheshes is posed a question: *avar ve-kiblah mahu she-yanichennu le-gat ha-ba'ah?* — if a *kohen chaver* accepted the barrel outside the season, is he permitted to hold onto it until the next pressing season and then, ten months hence, retroactively rely on what the *am ha-aretz* told him at the time of delivery? Can the vessel sit in his possession and change its purity status when the calendar turns?

In response, Rav Sheshes says *tneina* — we have already been taught this in a *mishnah*. The case involves a *chaver* and an *am ha-aretz* who were brothers and inherited the estate of their father, who was himself an *am ha-aretz*. Because the father was an *am ha-aretz*, the *chaver* must presume the entire estate is *tamei*. Among the estate's assets are various types of produce and grain, some that has come into contact with liquid and some that has not.

One might think the simplest resolution is for the *chaver* to take the dry, unwashed grain — which has never become susceptible to *tumah* — and leave the washed produce to the *am ha-aretz* brother. But this creates a serious problem. Since the two brothers are equal partners in the entire estate, proposing that one takes the dry produce and the other takes the washed produce is not merely a division — it is effectively a swap. The *chaver* would be handing over to the *am ha-aretz* produce that is already *tamei*, knowing full well that the *am ha-aretz* will not observe the relevant restrictions. This runs afoul of the prohibition of *v'lifnei iver* — facilitating another's transgression.

### Bereira Principle

The *mishnah* therefore defines carefully which modes of division are permissible. The *chaver* may say to the *am ha-aretz*: *tol atta chitin shebimkom ploni va-ani chitin shebimkom ploni* — you take the wheat from this location and I will take the wheat from that location. Similarly, he may designate portions of wine by location: you take the wine in this place and I will take the wine in that place. What he may not say is: *tol atta lach va-ani yavesh* — you take the wet grain and I will take the dry. Nor may he say: you take the wheat and I will take the barley, for that is transparently an exchange of his stake in one type of produce for the other's stake in a different type.

The underlying principle is *bereira* — the doctrine of retroactive selection. Where the division is natural and relatively arbitrary, such that one could say the portions were always *de facto* destined for each heir, we apply *yesh bereira*: the *chaver*'s portion was always, in some sense, his own, and no actual transfer of *tamei* property to the *am ha-aretz* has taken place. But where the division is structured so as to give the *am ha-aretz* the washed produce and the *chaver* the dry — a division explicitly calibrated by purity status — it is no longer a natural split but an intentional exchange, and *bereira* cannot be invoked to dissolve the transaction into a mere partition.

### Burning Impure Terumah

The *mishnah* then rules: *oto chaver soref ha-lach u-maniach et ha-yavesh* — that *chaver*, if he is a *kohen*, must burn the washed or liquid produce he has inherited — for it is *terumah tmei'ah*, impure priestly portions, which may only be disposed of by burning — while he may retain the dry produce as *tahor*.

This ruling circles back directly to the question raised about holding the barrel until the next pressing season. The *gemara* asks: *amai yanichenu le-gat ha-ba'ah?* — why must he burn it? Why not simply keep the washed produce until the next pressing season and at that point assume, as a matter of communal policy, that everything is *tahor*? The father who knew the purity status of the produce is dead; and if the pressing-season leniency requires no active claim by anyone — if the calendar alone suffices to restore a presumption of *taharah* — then why can't the *chaver* simply wait?

The *gemara* answers: *bidvar she-ein lo gat* — we are speaking of produce that has no pressing season. Grain washing, for instance, does not correspond to the kind of focused, communally observed pressing season that generates the institutional leniency. Without that seasonal anchor, there is no mechanism for restoring *taharah* through the passage of time. The *gemara* then asks: *v'yanichenu le-regel?* — perhaps he could wait until a *regel* (pilgrimage festival), when everyone is also believed about *terumah*? The *gemara* answers: *bidvar she-eino mishtamer le-regel* — the produce in question would spoil before the *regel* arrives. Accordingly, the ruling to burn it stands, but it does not actually resolve the theoretical question of whether, in cases where waiting is possible, such waiting would change the purity status. That question remains open.

### Pressing Season Requirem…

A further limitation on the pressing-season leniency is established: it applies only to produce that actually has a pressing season — olive oil and wine, whose harvest and processing are concentrated into a defined communal period. It does not extend to produce whose handling is diffuse and unstructured. This is the formal constraint that anchors the entire regime of *sha'at ha-gitot* believability.

## Festival Field Travel

### Blowing Path Leniency

The *mishnah* in another tractate records: *modin Beit Shammai le-Veit Hillel she-bodkin le-osei Pesach ve-ein bodkin le-ochlei terumah* — Beit Shammai agrees with Beit Hillel that one examines one's purity status for the purpose of bringing the *korban Pesach*, but not for the purpose of eating *terumah*. The *gemara* explains what *bodkin* means in this context. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel says: *menapeach adam beit ha-peras ve-holech* — a person may blow ahead of himself as he walks through a *beit ha-peras* and proceed. A *beit ha-peras* is a field in which a grave was plowed over, scattering fragments of bone, which create rabbinic *tumah* for anyone who passes through. By blowing the ground immediately in front of each step, one dislodges surface fragments and may traverse the field in order to reach Jerusalem to bring the *korban Pesach*. The result is that one is considered *tahor* for *Pesach* purposes — but this leniency is extended only for *osei Pesach*, not for the purpose of eating *terumah*.

### Pre-Trodden Path

A related leniency: Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Ulla teaches that a *beit ha-peras* that has been trodden — *she-nidash* — is considered *tahor* for those bringing the *korban Pesach*. The assumption is that a path already worn down by foot traffic has had the surface bone fragments dislodged or displaced, though the precise mechanism of this assumption is not fully transparent. The leniency applies because people traveling to Jerusalem for the festival create a pre-trodden path that can then be relied upon by subsequent travelers.

### Karet vs. Death Rationale

The principle unifying both leniencies is stated explicitly: *lo he'emidu divreihem bimkom karet* — the Rabbis did not maintain their restrictions at the cost of one's incurring *karet* for failing to bring the *korban Pesach*. The severity of *karet* for neglecting the *Pesach* obligation overrides the rabbinic stringency. The parallel rule for *terumah* runs in the opposite direction: *he'emidu divreihem bimkom mitah* — they maintained their position where the concern is the prohibition against eating *terumah* in a state of *tumat meit*, which carries the penalty of *mitah bi-dei shamayim*. The structure is somewhat inverted: in the case of *Pesach*, the Rabbis relax their rules to prevent the person from incurring *karet* for not performing a commandment; in the case of *terumah*, they maintain their rules to protect the person from incurring *mitah bi-dei shamayim* for performing a prohibited act. The logic in both cases, however, is the same — the Rabbis calibrate their stringencies against the severity of the transgression at stake.

It bears noting that the *regel*-based leniency discussed elsewhere is rooted less in an empirical assessment of people's actual care during the festival and more in *Chazal*'s deliberate policy of creating unity among the Jewish people at the time of pilgrimage. The pressing-season leniency is similarly policy-driven, as Rashi makes clear: the Rabbis needed to ensure that *kohanim* who are *chaverim* would not be cut off from the *terumah* of the entire Land of Israel. From a strict *halachic* standpoint, all of these restrictions on the believability of *amei ha-aretz* are layers of stringency beyond the letter of the law, and the Rabbis were therefore prepared to waive them when sufficient countervailing considerations existed.

### Pesach-Terumah Status

A natural question follows from the *beit ha-peras* leniency: *badak le-pischo mahu she-yochal be-trumaso?* — if a person walked through the field by blowing and thereby established his *taharah* for the purpose of the *korban Pesach*, is he also permitted to eat his *terumah*? It would seem peculiar to say the same person is *tahor* for one purpose and *tamei* for another. And yet the leniency was explicitly granted only for *osei Pesach*.

### Unified Status Resolution

Ulla rules: *badak le-fischo mutar le-echol be-trumaso* — one who cleared his path for *Pesach* may also eat his *terumah*. Rabba bar Ulla disagrees and holds that the *Pesach* clearance does not extend to *terumah*. A certain elder intervened and told Rabba bar Ulla not to disagree with Ulla, for the *mishnah* supports Ulla's position. The *mishnah* states: if an *am ha-aretz* declares that he has separated a *revi'it* of *kodesh* from within his barrel of wine, he is believed — and that believability extends to the wine in the barrel as a whole. The elder reasons: *alma mi-di-heimen a-kodesh heimen nami a-terumah* — if he is trusted regarding *kodesh*, he is trusted regarding *terumah* as well; we do not bifurcate a single person's *taharah* status by category. By the same logic, *hacha nami mi-di-heimen a-Pesach heimen nami a-terumah* — since this man's path-clearing was sufficient for the purposes of *Pesach*, it is sufficient for *terumah* as well. One cannot be *tahor* in one register and *tamei* in another simultaneously. The elder's argument stands without rebuttal.

## Mixed Vessel Belief

### Seventy-Day Prior Belief

The *mishnah* introduces a further category: *kadei yayin ve-kadei shemen ha-meduma'ot* — barrels of wine and oil that are *meduma'ot*, a term that typically denotes a mixture of *terumah* and *chulin*. The *mishnah* states that one is believed regarding them at the time of the pressing and, strikingly, seventy days beforehand as well. The plain reading of the *mishnah* suggests that belief in the vessels themselves — not merely the wine or oil — begins seventy days before the actual pressing season, presumably because the preparation of vessels requires that much advance lead time. Abaye infers from this: *shema mina dina hu de-iluiei arisa le-mitrach agufna* — the normative practice is for the sharecropper to begin tending and preparing the vineyard seventy days before the harvest.

The *baraita* then clarifies what remains: *ein ne'emanim lo al ha-kankanim ve-lo al ha-terumah* — they are not believed either regarding the jugs themselves or regarding the *terumah*. The *gemara* sets out to understand the precise scenario in which each of these limitations applies.

### Kodesh vs. Terumah Mix

The *gemara* asks: what exactly are we discussing? If the jugs contain wine or oil designated as *kodesh* — for *Beit Ha-mikdash* purposes — then *migu de-heimen a-kodesh heimen nami a-kankanim*: just as the *am ha-aretz* is believed that the *kodesh* wine is *tahor*, so too is he believed that the jug containing it is *tahor*. The belief in the contents extends to belief in the vessel. But if the jugs are vessels used for *terumah* — at a time other than the pressing season — then of course one is not believed about the jugs, since one is not believed about the *terumah* itself. As the *gemara* puts it: *hashta terumah lo meiman, kankanim meiman?* — if you are not trusted regarding the *terumah*, how could you be trusted regarding the vessels? So what case does the ruling of disbelief — regarding vessels — actually address?

The *gemara* proposes: *ella be-reikanim de-kodesh u-vish'ar yemot ha-shanah* — empty jugs normally used for *kodesh* wine, at a time other than the pressing season. In ordinary circumstances, if an *am ha-aretz* tells you that the *kodesh* wine in a jug is *tahor*, you believe him — and the belief extends to the jug. But if the jug is currently empty, even though it is normally used for *kodesh* purposes, a *chaver* who wishes to borrow it must take it to the *mikvah*. The believability attached to the *kodesh* wine does not persist when the jug is empty and standing alone without its *kodesh* contents.

Then the *gemara* identifies an even more counterintuitive scenario: *u-ve-mlei'im de-terumah u-vish'at ha-gitot* — jugs full of *terumah* wine during the pressing season. Here, the *kohen chaver* is prepared to believe that the *terumah* is *tahor* — because it is the pressing season — but he does not believe the *am ha-aretz* that the jug itself is *tahor*. The practical resolution: the *chaver* says to the *am ha-aretz*, pour the wine into my vessel, and take yours back. The wine is received as *tahor*; the jug is treated as *tamei*.

### Libation Intention Status

The *gemara* now returns to the question of what *meduma'ot* means in the context of *kodesh*. The term ordinarily denotes a mixture of *terumah* and *chulin*, but what would a *kodesh* mixture look like? The school of Rabbi Ilai explains: *be-metaheret tivlo litol mimenu nesakhim* — the case involves produce from which the owner has declared his intention to take wine for *nesakhim* (libation offerings). By announcing that he will be drawing *kodesh* wine for *nesakhim* from the batch being pressed, the grapes take on an ontological pre-separated *kodesh* status. They are, in some sense, already designated *kodesh*. As a result, the heightened believability that attaches to *kodesh* — including believability about the vessels — extends to the entire batch and the containers holding it, even before the pressing begins.

This is admittedly a striking reading of the *mishnah*. The plain meaning of *kadei yayin ve-kadei shemen ne'emanim aleihem* is that one is believed about the vessels in addition to the wine, and the seventy-day advance period explains why the vessels are addressed separately. But because the *gemara* has established that one is *not* believed about vessels in the context of *terumah*, the *mishnah* must be read as referring to a special *kodesh* scenario, not a *terumah* scenario.

### Declared Kodesh Vessel B…

The practical consequence is as follows. An *am ha-aretz* who has declared his intention to draw wine for *nesakhim* from a batch of grapes achieves a status for his produce and vessels such that a *chaver* may believe him even about the vessels — outside the pressing season and many weeks in advance. This stands in contrast to the *terumah* scenario, where belief in the vessel is withheld even when belief in the wine itself is granted. Rashi makes the policy dimension explicit: *she-af al pi she-lo gazru al trumatan bi-sh'at ha-gitot mishum hefsed kohanim chaverim* — the Rabbis refrained from applying their restrictions to *terumah* during the pressing season because to do so would deprive *kohanim* who are *chaverim* of the majority of the *terumah* of the Land of Israel. The limitation to the wine and not the vessels in the *terumah* case is itself a product of this policy: the *kohen chaver* needs the wine, not the *am ha-aretz*'s jug, and so the leniency is calibrated accordingly — *al ha-kankanim lo heiminum d'ein kohen chaver mekabel mehen kankanim im ha-terumah aval me'areh hu le-cheleihem* — he pours it into his own vessel.

## Modi'in Pottery Vessels

### Jerusalem Pottery Policy

The *mishnah* introduces a further leniency regarding pottery vessels from a specific geographic range: *min ha-Modi'in u-lifnim ne'emanim al klei cheres* — from Modi'in inward toward Jerusalem, *amei ha-aretz* are believed that their pottery vessels are *tahor*, at all times of the year. The rationale, as Rashi explains, is rooted in an infrastructural reality: kilns for firing pottery were not established within Jerusalem itself, on account of the smoke and smell they would have produced. The pottery industry was situated outside the city, precisely in the areas where *amei ha-aretz* predominated. People living in Jerusalem depended on pottery vessels produced by those outside the city, and if one could not trust the potters or vendors regarding the *taharah* of their vessels, the entire population of Jerusalem would be unable to function. Rashi frames this in terms of a standing principle: *lo gazru aleihem she-gez'eirah d'leit tzibbur she-ein rov tzibbur yecholim laamod bah* — a rabbinic decree that the majority of the public cannot observe is not enacted. Since the Jerusalemite public could not manage without pottery from these outer areas, the Rabbis established a policy of believability.

### Modi'in Geography Rule

The geographic line is drawn at Modi'in, approximately fifteen *mil* from Jerusalem — a meaningful radius, roughly a day's journey. From within that radius, *amei ha-aretz* are believed about pottery vessels. From beyond Modi'in, *ein ne'emanim* — they are not believed.

### Direction-Based Modi'in…

The *mishnah* then provides a remarkable illustration of the rule's formalist application: *ha-kadar she-hu mocher kadeirot, nichnas lifnim min ha-Modi'in* — if the pottery merchant, with his wares, is encountered inside Modi'in on his way toward Jerusalem, *hu ha-kadar ve-hen ha-kadeirot ve-hen ha-lokechim — ne'eman*: the same merchant, the same jugs, the same buyers — he is believed. *Yatza* — if you encounter that same merchant, with the same jugs and buyers, outside of Modi'in — *eino ne'eman*. The *mishnah* makes no apology for the formalism. Location determines believability; the individual facts of any particular case are irrelevant.

What of Modi'in itself? A *tanna* teaches: *Modi'in pe'amim ke-lifnim u-fe'amim ke-lachutz* — Modi'in is sometimes treated as inside and sometimes as outside, depending on the direction of movement of the parties. If the pottery merchant is heading away from Jerusalem (*yotze*) while the *chaver* is heading toward it (*nichnas*), the status is *ke-lifnim*: since this is the last opportunity the *chaver* will have to purchase from this merchant before the merchant exits the trusted zone, the transaction is treated as though it takes place within the inner region, and the *chaver* may buy. If both are heading toward Jerusalem — *shneihem nichnasin* — there is no urgency, and the *chaver* should wait until they are actually inside the inner zone before purchasing; in Modi'in itself the transaction is *ke-lachutz*. Similarly, if both are heading outward — *shneihem yotz'im* — even though they are physically in Modi'in, their trajectory is away from Jerusalem, and the location is treated as *ke-lachutz*.

Abaye then notes that this directional rule can actually be inferred from the original *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah*'s language — the merchant is found *inside* Modi'in, he is believed; if he *exits*, he is not believed — leaves Modi'in's own status ambiguous. Rather than a contradiction, the *gemara* reads the ambiguity as encoding exactly the directional rule the *tanna* articulated: *kan be-kadar yotze ve-chaver nichnas, kan be-shneihem yotz'im o shneihem nichnasin* — the *mishnah*'s silence about Modi'in proper is resolved by direction of travel.

### Small Vessel Limitation

A *tanna* further qualifies: *ne'emanim al klei cheres ha-dakim le-kodesh* — they are believed specifically regarding the thin, small pottery vessels, and for *kodesh* purposes. The leniency does not apply to large, heavy vessels. The distinction reflects the relative degree of need: small, thin vessels are the everyday items that people require in quantity and cannot easily do without, and for which the policy of universal believability is therefore most pressing.

Within this qualification, Resh Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan disagree. Resh Lakish holds: *vehu she-nitlin be-yado achat* — the vessel must be small enough to carry in one hand. Rabbi Yochanan holds: *afilu she-ein nitlin be-yado achat* — even if both hands are needed, as long as it is thin. Resh Lakish adds a further condition: *lo shanu ela reikanim aval mlei'im lo* — the leniency applies only to empty vessels. If the vessel is full, it is not believed.

### Pure Vessel-Impure Liquid

This creates a paradox that the *gemara* addresses directly. Since the *chaver* cannot trust the *am ha-aretz* about *chulin* wine, or about *terumah* wine outside the pressing season, if the vessel is sold full, the *chaver* must assume the liquid is *tamei*. Should he then assume the vessel is also *tamei*? Resh Lakish says yes — the full vessel loses its presumption of *taharah*. Rabbi Yochanan says *afilu mlei'im* — even a full vessel retains its presumed *taharah*, regardless of the status of the liquid within.

This is, as the *gemara* acknowledges, precisely the inverse of what was established earlier. Previously it was said that at pressing time the *terumah* wine is *tahor* but the vessel is *tamei*. Here Rabbi Yochanan says the vessel is *tahor* even though the wine inside is *tamei*. Rava confirms that Rabbi Yochanan does not claim the liquid itself is *tahor* — *u-modeh Rabbi Yochanan be-mashkin atzman she-hen tmei'im* — he acknowledges that the liquid is *tamei*. It is simply that the vessel's *taharah* is assessed independently.

The *gemara* anticipates the obvious objection — that this seems contradictory — and pre-empts it: *ve-al titma* — do not be astonished. For we have already encountered a similar disjunction. The case of *lugin mlei'im mashkin, lugin tamei tumat shiva u-mashkin tehorin* — pitchers that are *tamei* for seven days due to *tumat meit*, yet the liquid inside is *tahor* — was discussed previously in the context of Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai's dispute about a house containing a corpse. In that case, the rabbinic regime assigned different purity statuses to the vessel and its contents based on policy considerations about what the *am ha-aretz* would and would not heed. The same logic, in reverse, applies here: the vessel's status is assessed on its own terms, and the status of the liquid within does not automatically drag the vessel down — or up — with it.

## Tax Collectors-Thieves T…

### Jerusalem-Festival Trust

The *mishnah* culminates its survey of believability with two sweeping rules. In Jerusalem, everyone is believed regarding *kodesh* — we already knew that *kodesh* belief extended throughout Judea, and Jerusalem, as the center, naturally carries it. And *u-vish'at ha-regel, af al ha-terumah* — at the time of the *regel*, the pilgrimage festival, everyone everywhere is believed even regarding *terumah*. This is the capstone of the entire discussion: the broadest erasure of the *am ha-aretz*/*chaver* distinction, grounded entirely in *Chazal*'s policy of creating unity among the people of Israel at the time of pilgrimage.

### Tax Collector Supervision

Before reaching that climax, the *mishnah* introduces two other categories of people normally distrusted who are nonetheless believed in specific circumstances. Tax collectors (*gabba'im*) who entered a house to seize property for unpaid taxes, and thieves who have returned stolen vessels — both are believed to say: *lo naganu* — we did not touch these particular vessels. The tax collector, despite being a classic exemplar of the *am ha-aretz* who would not ordinarily be trusted about *taharah*, is believed to identify which items he touched and which he did not.

The *gemara* immediately raises a contradiction from a *mishnah* that states the opposite — that when tax collectors enter a house, *kulo tamei*, everything is *tamei*. The resolution is straightforward: *lo kashya — ha de-nochri bahadei ve-ha de-la nochri bahadei* — the two *mishnayot* address different circumstances. The ruling of universal *tumah* applies when a non-Jewish supervisor accompanied the tax collectors; the ruling of selective believability applies when no such supervisor was present.

### Gentile Supervisor Effect

Why does the presence of a non-Jewish supervisor make such a difference? Two *amoraim* — Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Eleazar — offer slightly different formulations. One says: *eimat nochri aleihem* — the Jewish tax collectors are afraid of the non-Jew himself. The other says: *eimat malchut aleihem* — they are afraid of the governmental authority the non-Jew represents, fearing that if they fail to conduct a thorough search, he will report them to the authorities. The practical difference between the two positions concerns a non-Jewish supervisor who is personally insignificant. If the fear is of the person himself, then an unimportant non-Jew would not trigger the rule. If the fear is of the governmental authority he represents, even a minor official might suffice.

When there is no non-Jewish supervisor present, the Jewish tax collectors will be relatively easygoing with one another — they will not feel compelled to conduct an exhaustive inspection — and therefore their claim that they did not touch certain items is credible. In the presence of a non-Jew, even one of their own co-religionists, they feel compelled to search everything thoroughly, and their subsequent claim of selective touching cannot be believed.

### Repentant Thieves

The case of thieves who return vessels presents a similar *raminhu*: a *mishnah* suggests that when thieves enter a house, only the area of their actual footpath is *tamei* — implying they are not assumed to have handled vessels. The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction through Rav Pinchas in the name of Rav or Rava: *k'she-asu teshuvah* — the believability applies specifically when the thieves have done *teshuvah*, as evidenced by their returning the stolen property. Only a thief who has repented and is in the act of returning what he stole is in a spiritual and legal posture in which his claims regarding what he touched are credited. The *gemara* confirms: *deika nami de-katani she-hecheziru et ha-kelim* — indeed, the *mishnah*'s language specifies thieves who *returned* the vessels, which is precisely the *teshuvah* condition. *Shma mina*.

One final question is worth noting. The *mishnah*'s believability for tax collectors and repentant thieves is not explained in terms of *sha'at ha-gitot* or *regel* or any other institutional period of leniency. Rashi limits the believability here to matters of *kodesh*, consistent with the general rule that *amei ha-aretz* are believed about *kodesh* in Judea at all times. Tosafot, however, extends it to *terumah* as well, and suggests that a distinction may exist between an *am ha-aretz*'s claims about how he handled *his own* property and claims about how he handled someone else's — with the latter perhaps carrying greater credibility, since one is less likely to misrepresent one's treatment of property belonging to another.
