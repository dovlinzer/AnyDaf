# Berakhot 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Interruptions & Breaks

### Chapter Overview

The first chapter of *Berakhot* focused primarily on the structural aspects of *Shema*: the times of its recitation, the surrounding *brachot*, and certain postural questions. The second chapter turns its attention to the act of saying *Shema* itself — the internal quality of the recitation, the permissibility of interruptions, and the nature of the intention required.

### Torah Learning & Kavanah

The chapter opens with the following case: one is reading through the Torah — simply learning, practicing the text of *Parshat Vaetchanan*, for example — and the time of *kriyat Shema* arrives. *Im kiven libo yatza* — if he directs his heart, if he reads with the intention of fulfilling the obligation, he is *yotzei*. This seemingly straightforward ruling carries within it a significant implication, which the *Gemara* will immediately interrogate: it appears to demonstrate that *mitzvot tzerichot kavanah* — that the performance of commandments requires intention.

### Bein Prakim vs. Emtza

The *Mishnah* then addresses permitted interruptions. *Biprakim* — at the breaks between sections — one may greet a person of importance out of *kavod*, honor, and may respond to a greeting from anyone. *Uve'emtza* — in the middle of a section — the threshold is higher: one may only initiate a greeting out of *yira*, genuine fear of the person's reaction. Rashi goes as far as to say this means fear for one's life. One may, however, respond even within a section out of a lesser degree of concern.

### R. Meir vs. R. Yehuda

This is the position of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda offers a slightly more lenient framework specifically regarding *responses*. He agrees that in the middle of a section one may only initiate a greeting out of *yira*, but to respond to a greeting, even *uve'emtza*, the threshold is only *kavod*. At the breaks, Rabbi Yehuda holds that one may respond to absolutely anyone — even a person toward whom there is no special concern of *kavod* — since failing to respond to any greeting is itself a form of affront. The underlying principle shared by both opinions is that responding carries a lower threshold than initiating, an implicit *kal vachomer*.

### Section Divisions

The *Mishnah* then enumerates what constitutes *bein haprakim* — the permissible breaks. These are: between the first *bracha* and the second, between the second *bracha* and *Shema*, between *Shema* and *Vehaya im Shamoa*, and between *Vehaya im Shamoa* and *Vayomer*. The units are understood as the full first *bracha*, the full second *bracha*, and then the three *parshiyot* of *Shema* in sequence. These are the halachic units between which interruption is comparatively more permissible.

### Vayomer-Emet Veyatziv

Notably, Rabbi Yehuda adds that between *Vayomer* and *Emet Veyatziv* — the *bracha* that begins immediately following *Shema* — one should not interrupt at all. The phrase *Hashem Elokeichem emet* bridges the two, and Rabbi Yehuda insists that this transition must flow without pause. This is striking: despite Rabbi Yehuda's generally more lenient posture regarding responses, here he draws a sharp line. Interestingly, this raises a question about the common practice of saying *Amen* after *habocheir b'amo Yisrael b'ahava*. The broader concern of *semichat ge'ula l'tefilla* — the imperative to adjoin the *bracha* of redemption directly to the *Shemoneh Esrei* — is even more stringent, as the *Gemara* elsewhere expressed discomfort even with *Hashem sefatai tiftach* as an interruption. The *bracha* before *Shema* does not have the full character of a classic *birchat hamitzvah* — it lacks the *asher kidshanu bemitzvotav* formulation — and that may explain why somewhat greater latitude applies there.

## Order of Parshiyot

### R. Yehoshua b. Korcha

With the *Mishnah* now being the first place to enumerate all three *parshiyot* of *Shema* together, the *Gemara* introduces a teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: *Lama kadma parshat Shema leVehaya im Shamoa?* — Why does the first *parsha* precede the second? The answer is: *kedei sheyekabel alav ol malchus shamayim techila v'achar kach mekabel alav ol mitzvot* — so that one first accepts the yoke of heavenly sovereignty and only thereafter accepts the yoke of commandments.

### Ol Malchus First

The structure reflects a theological priority. The first *parsha* — *Shema Yisrael*, *Ve'ahavta* — is centrally about the relationship between the person and God: belief, love, and acceptance of God's sovereignty. The obligations of observance appear only secondarily. The second *parsha*, *Vehaya im Shamoa*, contains the framework of reward and consequence for *mitzvot*, but lacks the foundational declaration of *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad*. The order thus places *ol malchus shamayim* before *ol mitzvot* — love and acceptance of God before the concrete obligations that flow from that relationship.

### Day vs. Night

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha then explains the placement of *Vehaya im Shamoa* before *Vayomer*: *she'Vehaya im Shamoa noheig bein bayom uvein balaila* — *Vehaya im Shamoa* applies both by day and by night, whereas *Vayomer* — the *parsha* of *tzitzit* — applies only by day. Rashi emphasizes that the *mitzvot* of the second *parsha*, including *talmud Torah*, apply at all times, while *tzitzit* is a daytime obligation.

A deeper reading, which Rashi also indicates later, concerns not the *mitzvot* of the *parsha* but the recitation of the *parsha* itself within *Shema*. Recall that the previous *Mishnah* raised the question of whether *Parshat Tzitzit* is said at night at all, since *mazkirin yetziat Mitzrayim* — the remembrance of the Exodus — which is the purpose of that *parsha*, may not apply at night. On this view, *Vayomer* was perhaps understood as a daytime recitation only, meaning the obligation to include it in *Shema* applies only by day. The halachic ruling ultimately follows the position that there is a *mitzvah* to say it at night as well, and indeed to mention *yetziat Mitzrayim* at night. But the *baraita* here may be articulating an earlier, differing opinion.

## Kavanah Debate

### Mitzvot & Kavanah

The *Gemara* now directly addresses the opening ruling: *shema mina mitzvot tzerichot kavanah* — apparently the *Mishnah* proves that *mitzvot* require intention. However, the *Gemara* immediately questions this inference: the debate over *mitzvot tzerichot kavanah* is well established, so how can this *Mishnah* simply resolve it?

### Act vs. Fulfillment

The *Gemara* therefore reframes the *Mishnah*'s meaning. When it says *im kiven libo*, it does not mean *kavanah* to be *yotzei* the *mitzvah* — the intent to fulfill the obligation — but rather *kavanah* to read, that is, intention to perform the act itself. There is an important distinction here: even those who hold that *mitzvot* do not require intent to fulfill still agree that one must intend to do the act one is doing. The paradigmatic case used elsewhere is the *shofar*: if a person attempts to clean out the *shofar* by blowing and inadvertently produces the required sound, everyone agrees one is not *yotzei*, because there was no intent to blow at all. The debate concerns whether, once the act is intended, one additionally needs intent to fulfill the *mitzvah*. Here too, then, the *Mishnah* may only be requiring that the person intend to read — not that one must specifically intend to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *Shema*.

### Proofreading Example

But the *Gemara* immediately presses: *veha ka kari* — the person is clearly reading! Unlike the case of the *shofar*, where one could blow without meaning to make a sound, how could someone be reciting words without intending to read? The *Gemara* answers: *bekorei lehagiah* — the case is one who is proofreading. Rashi explains that when one is comparing texts or checking a *Sefer Torah*, one may be mumbling the words as a function of that act of checking, without the recitation rising to the level of a genuine act of reading. The words may even be said aloud, but they serve the purpose of comparison rather than reading as such. In this case, *kavanah* is required to transform the incidental recitation into an act of *keriah*.

*Tosafot*, however, cannot accept this answer. How, Tosafot asks, can one be reciting words without intending to read them? Tosafot therefore understands the *Mishnah*'s *kavanah* requirement differently: it refers to attentiveness to read accurately, not to a formal act-intention. But this reading does not appear to capture what the *Gemara* itself is after. The upshot in any case is that this *Mishnah* cannot serve as a proof for the position of *mitzvot tzerichot kavanah*, since the *kavanah* required may refer only to the intent to perform the act, which is a separate and less contested requirement. The *Yerushalmi*, notably, draws a further inference from this *Mishnah*: that one is *yotzei* the biblical obligation of *Shema* even without the surrounding *brachot*, since the *brachot* are *derabbanan*. Though the full rabbinic package of *kriyat Shema uvirchoteha* carries particular significance — the *brachot* amplify and extend the themes of *Shema* rather than simply framing a *mitzvah* — one nonetheless fulfills the biblical *mitzvah* without them.

## Hebrew vs. Translation

### R. (Judah HaNasi) Rule

A *baraita* records the following dispute: *Kriyat Shema kechatvah* — *Shema* must be recited as written, meaning in Hebrew. This is the position of *Rebbi* (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi).

### Chachamim Rule

The *Chachamim* disagree: *bechol lashon* — in any language. Translation is permissible.

### Understanding vs. Act

The *Gemara* probes the reasoning of each position. *Rebbi* derives from the word *vehayu* — "and these words shall be" — that they must remain as they are, i.e., in Hebrew. The *Chachamim* derive from the word *shema* — "hear" — that the requirement is comprehension: in any language by which one can hear and understand. This conceptual disagreement — is *Shema* primarily an act of recitation or primarily a communicative act of understanding? — maps directly onto the subsequent debate about hearing one's own recitation.

### Hearing Requirement

The *Gemara* then notes that *Rebbi*, who derives from *vehayu* that Hebrew is required, must interpret *shema* differently. He therefore uses *shema* to teach *lehashmia le'oznecha mah she'atah motzi mipichah* — one must cause one's ears to hear what one's mouth is saying. The act of *Shema* requires audibility; one cannot recite it below the threshold of one's own hearing.

The *Chachamim*, by contrast, hold that even if one did not hear one's own recitation — *lo hishmia le'ozno yatza* — one is nonetheless *yotzei*. This position coheres with their conceptual emphasis: since *Shema* is primarily about understanding and communicating content rather than performing a formal act of speech, the failure to produce audible sound does not disqualify the recitation, as long as one genuinely engaged with the words.

This fits a broader pattern: *Rebbi*, who insists on Hebrew and on audibility, grounds the *mitzvah* in the act of recitation. The *Chachamim*, who permit any language and do not require audibility, ground it in communication and comprehension. The debate is not merely technical but reflects a deeper disagreement about the nature of the obligation. A further question arises as to whether the standard is that the person themselves must hear, or that the recitation must be objectively audible to someone — a distinction the *Gemara* leaves for subsequent discussion.

The *Gemara* then addresses how each camp handles the other's prooftext. *Vehayu*, which *Rebbi* uses to require Hebrew, the *Chachamim* interpret as requiring that *Shema* be recited in order — *shelo yikra lemafrei'a*, one may not reverse the sequence. And *Shema*, which the *Chachamim* use to permit any language, *Rebbi* interprets as the hearing requirement. The *Gemara* then considers whether the need to cite these prooftexts reveals each side's default assumption about the language of *mitzvot*, but concludes that no clean inference can be drawn: each side needed its prooftext precisely to counteract the inference created by the other's, so neither default can be established from this exchange alone.

## Kavanah Scope

### Two Pesukim Rule

The *Gemara* returns to the question of *kavanah* and now sets out to define precisely how much of *Shema* requires it. A *baraita* interprets the *pasuk* of *Shema* closely: *vehayu* teaches that one must not read out of order; *al levavecha* — "on your heart" — that one must have *kavanah*. The question then becomes: is it possible that the entire *parsha* requires *kavanah*? The *baraita* responds: *talmud lomar ha'eileh* — the word "these" limits the requirement. *Ad kan tzricha kavanah, mikan va'eilach ein tzricha kavanah* — only up to this point is *kavanah* required; beyond it, it is not.

The question of exactly where this boundary falls generates a dispute between Rashi and *Tosafot*. Rashi reads *al levavecha* literally as the endpoint — the middle of the third *pasuk*, *Vehayu hadevarim ha'eileh asher anochi metzavcha hayom al levavecha*. *Tosafot* understands *al levavecha* as referring back to the first two *pesukim* — *Shema Yisrael* and *Ve'ahavta* — and that the third *pasuk* itself identifies those two as the things that must be *al levavecha*. On this reading, *kavanah* is required only for the first two *pesukim*, because, as *Tosafot* explains, those two *pesukim* speak of *yichud Hashem*, the love of God, and *yirat Hashem* — the foundational matters of faith that constitute *ol malchus shamayim*.

### R. Eliezer

This position — that *kavanah* is required through *bechol me'odecha*, encompassing the first two *pesukim* — is identified as the view of Rabbi Eliezer.

### R. Akiva

Rabbi Akiva disagrees and derives a broader requirement from the phrase *asher anochi metzavcha hayom* — "that I am commanding you today." The present-tense formulation *metzavcha*, Rabbi Akiva argues, refers not only to what has just been said in the first two *pesukim* but to everything God is commanding in this moment, including the remainder of the *parsha*. Therefore: *mikan atah lameid shechol haparsha kullah tzricha kavanah* — one must have *kavanah* for the entire first *parsha*.

### R. Yochanan Rules

Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *halacha keRabbi Akiva* — the ruling follows Rabbi Akiva. *Kavanah* is required for the entirety of the first *parsha*. This ruling is also transmitted in a second version: a *tosefta* states simply that when reciting *Shema* one must direct one's heart, and an *Amora*, Rabbi Acha in the name of Rabbi Yehuda, interprets this to mean that once one has had proper *kavanah* during the first chapter, nothing further is required. Rabbi Yochanan again rules in accordance with this position. Both versions of the ruling converge on the same halachic conclusion: *kavanah* is required for the full first *parsha*.

## Keriah & Kavanah

### Mitzvat Types

A further *baraita* refines the analysis by introducing a conceptual distinction not merely about how much *kavanah* is needed, but about the qualitative nature of the obligation in different portions of *Shema*. The framing is striking: the *baraita* frames the obligation as either *mitzvat kavanah* or *mitzvat keriah* — either a *mitzvah* of intention or a *mitzvah* of recitation — as if these are categorically distinct modes of religious obligation.

### R. Zutra Position

Rabbi Zutra holds: *ad kan mitzvat kavanah, mikan va'eilach mitzvat keriah* — the first portion of *Shema*, up to *al levavecha*, constitutes a *mitzvah* of *kavanah*; from that point onward, meaning the remainder of the first *parsha* and the subsequent *parshiyot*, the obligation is one of recitation only.

### R. Yoshiya Reverse

Rabbi Yoshiya reverses the positions: *ad kan mitzvat keriah, mikan va'eilach mitzvat kavanah* — the first portion is a *mitzvah* of recitation, and the second portion is one of *kavanah*. This is initially puzzling, since the phrase *al levavecha* appears in the first part, not the second.

The *Gemara* works through both positions carefully. For Rabbi Zutra's view, the *Gemara* asks: why is the second portion only a *mitzvah* of recitation? The *Gemara* answers: because the second *parsha* contains the phrase *ledaber bam* — to speak of them — which obligates the act of speech. But then the *Gemara* presses: the first *parsha* also says *vedibarta bam* — and you shall speak of them — so it too should require recitation! The *Gemara* responds: *hachi ka'amar* — the correct reading is that the first portion requires *both* recitation and *kavanah*, since it says both *vedibarta bam* and *al levavecha*; the second portion requires only recitation, without *kavanah*.

But then the *Gemara* asks why the second *parsha* — which also says *al levavchem ledaber bam* — should not require both as well. The resolution: *hahu miba'ei lei lekhe'derabbi Yitzchak* — that phrase in the second *parsha* is not teaching the *kavanah* requirement for *Shema* at all. Rather, Rabbi Yitzchak derives from *al levavchem* that *tefillin* must be placed *keneged halev*, opposite the heart. The verse is thus "occupied" with teaching something about *tefillin*, and cannot simultaneously be read as requiring *kavanah* for the recitation of *Shema*.

### First Parsha Both

The conclusion the *Gemara* arrives at — through both opinions — is that the first *parsha* unambiguously requires both *keriah* and *kavanah*. No opinion downgrades the first *parsha*; the real dispute concerns only what follows.

### Second Parsha Nature

For Rabbi Yoshiya's reverse position, the *Gemara* similarly reconstructs: when he says the first portion is *mitzvat keriah*, he means the first *parsha* requires both recitation and *kavanah* — this cannot be read as demoting the first *parsha*. The question is about the second *parsha*. According to Rabbi Yoshiya, *mikan va'eilach mitzvat kavanah* means the second *parsha* requires *kavanah* only, without recitation. This is the genuine disagreement: Rabbi Zutra says the second *parsha* is *keriah belo kavanah* — just read it, no particular *kavanah* required; Rabbi Yoshiya says it is *kavana belo kriya* — just think it, formal recitation is not required.

### Teaching vs. Reciting

This position of Rabbi Yoshiya — that thinking suffices for the second *parsha* — is supported by a striking reinterpretation of the *parsha*'s own language. The *Gemara* notes that the second *parsha* uses the formulation *ledaber bam*, with the additional prefix *le-*, which the *Gemara* now reads as referring to *teaching* Torah rather than *reciting Shema*. *Agmiru benaychu Torah ki heichi deleigresu behu* — teach your children Torah so that they may engage with it, speak of it. On this reading, the obligation embedded in the second *parsha* is not a formal *mitzvah* of recitation at all; it is the *mitzvah* of *talmud Torah* and teaching. The observation that *vedibarta bam* in the first *parsha* uses a simple command form more naturally read as an obligation to perform an act of speech, while *ledaber bam* in the second *parsha* describes a goal or result of teaching, lends some grammatical support to this distinction.

### Comprehension vs. Act

This leads to a broader conceptual clarification about the nature of the *mitzvah* of *Shema* itself. The *Rishonim* raise the question: how does this internal debate about *kavanah* in specific sections relate to the general question of *mitzvot tzerichot kavanah*? If *mitzvot* require *kavanah*, one would seem to need *kavanah* throughout; if they do not, one would seem to need none at all. Some *Rishonim* conclude that the debate about how much of *Shema* requires *kavanah* is really a debate about which portions of *Shema* constitute the biblical *mitzvah* — perhaps only the first two *pesukim*, perhaps the full first *parsha*, perhaps only the first *pasuk* — with the remainder being at most rabbinic.

However, the *Rashba* offers what appears to be the better answer: the *mitzvah* of *Shema* is not simply an act of recitation — that would be *mitzvat keriah*. It is fundamentally *kabbalat ol malchus shamayim* — the acceptance of the yoke of Heaven through the act of recitation. This means the *kavanah* at stake in *Shema* is not the general *kavanah* to fulfill a *mitzvah*, but a specific, content-oriented *kavanah*: one must understand what one is saying and be actively engaging in the affirmation expressed by the words. This is structurally similar to the requirement in *tefilla* that one know what one is praying — not simply that one intends to fulfill an obligation. Accordingly, even according to the view that *mitzvot ein tzerichot kavanah*, one might still require this substantive form of *kavanah* for *Shema*, since without it the *kabbalat ol malchus shamayim* itself is absent.

The *Gemara* frames the different sections precisely in these terms: the first *parsha* — and especially its opening *pesukim* — requires the full merger of recitation and intentional affirmation; the second *parsha*, whose content concerns the framework of *mitzvot* and reward, may require only one or the other. One opinion holds that merely thinking the second *parsha* suffices because accepting the yoke of *mitzvot* can be accomplished by intention alone; the other holds that since the *parsha* obligates speech — or alternatively that the verse there is about *talmud Torah*, which is an act of learning and teaching — formal recitation without *kavanah* is sufficient.

## First Pesuk Focus

### R. Meir Opinion

A final *tanna'itic* position now enters: *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad — ad kan tzricha kavanat halev, divrei Rabbi Meir.* According to Rabbi Meir, *kavanah* is required only for the first *pasuk* itself — the affirmation of God's unity and sovereignty. Nothing beyond it — not *Ve'ahavta*, not the rest of the first *parsha* — requires the heightened *kavanah* of *kabbalat ol malchus shamayim*. The core of the obligation is concentrated entirely in that declaration.

### Rava's Ruling

Rava rules: *halacha keRabbi Meir*. The *halacha* follows Rabbi Meir. This stands in apparent tension with the earlier ruling of Rabbi Yochanan that *halacha* follows Rabbi Akiva, requiring *kavanah* for the entire first *parsha*. These are simply two competing *amoraic* rulings, each aligning with a different *tanna'itic* position.

### Long Echad

*Sumchos* teaches: *kol hamarich be'echad, maarichin lo yamav veshanosav* — whoever extends the word *echad* has his days and years extended. The *Gemara* then specifies: *uv'dalet* — the extension is specifically on the *dalet*. Rashi explains that extending the *alef* or *chet* would distort the word, whereas the *dalet*, as the final letter, can be extended without corrupting the pronunciation. Rav Ashi adds a limiting condition: *uvilvad shelo yachtof baches* — one must not race through the *chet* in order to get to the *dalet*, as that would result in an unclear pronunciation of the *chet*.

A narrative follows: Rabbi Yirmiyah was sitting before Rabbi Chiya bar Abba and was observed extending *echad* at very great length. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said to him: once you have *amlichtei* — once you have enthroned God, recognized His kingship above and below and in all four directions — *tu lo tzrichat*, you do not need to continue further.

### Dalet Symbolism

The practice of extending the *dalet* functions as a ritualization of the *kavanah* demanded by the first *pasuk*. Since the entire weight of *kabbalat ol malchus shamayim* is concentrated in this *pasuk*, the deliberate, extended pronunciation of *echad* provides a mechanism for focusing the mind on what is being affirmed: God's singular sovereignty over heaven and earth and all directions. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba's correction makes this explicit — the extension is not an end in itself but a vehicle for achieving the required *kavanah*. Once that *kavanah* has been achieved, the extension should cease. There is additionally a practical dimension noted by some: the concern not to confuse the *dalet* with a *reish*, which would transform *echad* into *acher*, "another" — a catastrophic error in a declaration of monotheism.

## Standing Requirement

### Standing Rules

The *Gemara* reports in the name of Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Shmuel bar Shilat in the name of Rav: *al levavecha be'amida* — the recitation up to *al levavecha* must be said while standing. The *Gemara* initially wonders whether this means only the words *al levavecha* themselves, and immediately clarifies: *ela eima ad al levavecha* — rather, the rule is that one must stand until reaching *al levavecha*.

### R. Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan extends the requirement further: *kol haparsha kulla be'amida* — the entire first *parsha* must be said while standing. The *Gemara* observes that this follows Rabbi Yochanan's own consistent position — *ve'azda Rabbi Yochanan letaimeih* — since he ruled that *kavanah* is required for the entire first *parsha*, the corresponding ritual requirement of standing similarly extends throughout. The two positions track each other: the scope of required *kavanah* and the scope of required standing are aligned.

### Posture Details

*Tosafot* cites the *Yerushalmi* for a significant qualification: *be'amida* here does not mean that one who is sitting must stand up. Rather, it means that one who is walking must stop and stand still. The concern is distraction: walking during *Shema* is insufficiently focused for the *kavanah* demanded, and one must therefore come to a halt. If one was already sitting, one need not rise. This framing connects the standing requirement to the broader concern for *kavanah*: it is not a formal postural *din* but a practical measure to ensure the requisite focus.

## R. Yehuda Practice

### First Pesuk Only

A *baraita* records: *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad — zu kriyat shema shel Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi.* This single *pasuk* constituted the *Shema* of *Rebbi*. As the *Gemara* will explain, *Rebbi* was engaged in teaching Torah throughout the day, and by the time his obligations as *Nasi* and as teacher of the community had run their course, he had only the opportunity to recite the first *pasuk* within *zman kriyat Shema*. Implicitly, the *baraita* is making a stronger point than simply noting which part requires *kavanah*: it suggests that at a *bedi'eved* level, at least, the first *pasuk* alone may suffice for the *mitzvah* itself.

### Rav's Question

Rav said to Rabbi Chiya: *lo chazina lei leRebbi demikabel alei malchus shamayim* — I sit in *shiur* all day and I have never seen *Rebbi* perform *kabbalat ol malchus shamayim*. The formulation is telling: Rav does not say he never saw *Rebbi* recite *Shema*, but that he never saw him perform the act of *accepting* God's sovereignty — underscoring that the essence of the obligation is not the verbal recitation but the inner affirmation.

### Eyes Covered

Rabbi Chiya responds: *bar pachti* — and then explains — *be'sha'ah shemavir yadav al panav, mikabel alav ol malchus shamayim* — when *Rebbi* draws his hand over his eyes in the middle of *shiur*, that is when he accepts the yoke of Heaven. He was reciting the first *pasuk* of *Shema* in that moment, marked by the gesture of covering the eyes. He would do this without interrupting the *shiur* as a formal matter, but using that moment of self-collection to fulfill his obligation.

### Making Up Later

This raises the question: *chozer vegomer o eino chozer vegomer?* — does *Rebbi* go back after the time has passed and recite the full *Shema*, or does he rely entirely on what he said during *zman kriyat Shema*? Bar Kafra says: *eino chozer vegomer* — there is no point in going back. Whatever one accomplished during *zman*, one accomplished; after the time passes, one does not return to complete it. Rabbi Shimon bar Rebbi disagrees: *chozer vegomer* — one should return and complete the full *Shema*. Whether this refers to the *mitzvah* of *Shema* itself or to the integrity of the morning *davening* as a whole — the package of *kriyat Shema uvirchoteha* — is itself a question.

### Yetziat Mitzrayim

Bar Kafra draws a telling inference in support of his own position. He observes that *Rebbi*, in the course of his *shiur*, would always find a way to mention *yetziat Mitzrayim* — the Exodus from Egypt. Bar Kafra argues: this makes sense according to his own view that *Rebbi* does not go back to complete *Shema* later. Since the third *parsha* of *Shema* — *Vayomer*, which mentions *yetziat Mitzrayim* — would not be recited within *zman*, *Rebbi* sought to fulfill the separate *mitzvah* of *zecher yetziat Mitzrayim* through some mention of the Exodus during his *shiur*. But according to Rabbi Shimon bar Rebbi, who holds that *Rebbi* does go back and complete the full *Shema* later, why would *Rebbi* bother mentioning it in the *shiur*? He could simply rely on reciting *Vayomer* when he completes *Shema* afterward.

The *Gemara* answers: *kedei lehazkir yetziat Mitzrayim bizmanah* — in order to mention the Exodus *in its proper time*. Rashi explains that *bizmanah* refers here to the *zman* of *kriyat Shema* itself. There is a distinct value — not simply to mention *yetziat Mitzrayim* at some point in the day, but to do so during the period designated for *kriyat Shema*. The placement of this *mitzvah* within the liturgy of *Shema* has transformed it: the ideal fulfillment of *zecher yetziat Mitzrayim* is not merely reciting the Exodus narrative but doing so within the framework of *Shema* and during its designated time. Even if one cannot recite *Vayomer* as part of *Shema* itself, *Rebbi* ensured that the *zecher* would occur during that window, preserving the liturgical structure as best he could.

## Sleep During Shema

### Yotzei Asleep

Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Shmuel bar Shilat reported in the name of Rav: if a person says *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* and then falls asleep — *yatza*, one is *yotzei*. This ruling, placed in the context of *kriyat Shema al hamita* — the bedtime recitation — reinforces the position that the first *pasuk* may constitute the full extent of the biblical *mitzvah*. The question of how much one must repeat at night, and at what level of obligation the subsequent *parshiyot* stand, is a debate among *poskim*; the *Gemara* here appears to anchor a significant position: at least *bedi'eved*, the first *pasuk* alone suffices.

### Nudging for First

Rav Nachman said to Dar, his servant: *bipsuka kama tzaaran* — for the first *pasuk*, trouble us. That is, if we are dozing off and have not yet said *Shema*, nudge us awake and ensure we are sufficiently alert and focused to say the first *pasuk* with *kavanah*. *Tfei lo tzaaran* — beyond that, do not trouble us. If we wish to fall back asleep after the first *pasuk*, leave us be.

### Rava's Practice

A parallel account concerns Rava. Rav Yosef asked Rav Yosef the son of Rava: what would your father do in this regard? The answer: *bipsuka kama hava kamatzaer nafsheih, tfei lo hava matzaer nafsheih* — for the first *pasuk*, he would exert himself to be focused and present; beyond that, he would not exert himself. Whether this refers to his own self-discipline in staying alert, or to ensuring those around him — children, household — were awake for the first *pasuk*, the message is identical: the first *pasuk* is treated as the irreducible core, and the remainder, while certainly recited when possible, is treated with lesser urgency.

### Lying Down

The *Gemara* then transitions to a related question of posture. Rav Yosef rules: *pirkadan lo yikra kriyat Shema* — one who is lying flat on one's back should not recite *Shema*. The concern here is not distraction or lack of *kavanah* in the ordinary sense, but *kavod* — the recitation of *Shema*, as an act of *kabbalat ol malchus shamayim*, demands a posture of respect and dignity.

### Position Rules

The *Gemara* asks: does this ruling apply only to recitation? Surely lying flat on one's back while sleeping is also problematic, since Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi would curse one who slept in that position — *layeit aman degani aparkid* — on grounds of immodesty. The *Gemara* distinguishes: for sleeping, a slight incline is sufficient to remove the concern; but for reciting *Shema*, even a slight incline does not suffice. One must sit up. The two concerns — physical modesty in sleep and dignified posture for *kabbalat ol malchus shamayim* — are related but distinct, and the threshold for the latter is higher.

### R. Yochanan Exception

The *Gemara* notes an apparent exception: Rabbi Yochanan would recline — *metzli* — while reciting *Shema*. The resolution: *shani Rabbi Yochanan deba'al basar hava* — Rabbi Yochanan was a very heavy man, and sitting upright presented a genuine and significant physical burden. Given those circumstances, his reclining while reciting *Shema* was not disrespectful; indeed, given the physical effort that sitting upright would have required of him, reclining was not a posture of casualness but of necessity. Accordingly, the ruling against *pirkadan* does not apply in situations where the physical demands of sitting upright are genuinely beyond a person's ordinary capacity.
