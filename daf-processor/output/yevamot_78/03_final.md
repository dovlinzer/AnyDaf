# Yevamot 78 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Shimon vs. Rabbanan

### Shimon's Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַמּוֹנִי וּמוֹאָבִי, שֶׁאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם, וְאַף עַל פִּי כֵן נְקֵבוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת — מִצְרִי וַאֲדוֹמִי, שֶׁאִיסּוּרָן שָׁלֹשׁ דּוֹרוֹת, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁנְּקֵבוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** Just as in the case of **an Ammonite and a Moabite, whose prohibition is eternal, and yet the females are permitted—so too in the case of an Egyptian and an Edomite, whose prohibition is only three generations, is it not all the more obvious that the females are permitted?**

We pick up toward the very bottom of 77b, in the midst of the debate concerning Rabbi Shimon's position in the Mishnah. Rabbi Shimon maintains that *Mitzri* and *Adomi* women should be permitted immediately — that is, from the first generation — reasoning by way of a *kal vachomer*: just as *Ammoni* and *Moavi*, where the prohibition is far stricter (applying to all generations), is limited to males — *Ammoni v'lo Ammonit* — then certainly *Mitzri* and *Adomi*, whose prohibition extends only two generations, should similarly not apply to the women.

### Rabbanan's Challal Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״לָא כִּי״? הָכִי קָאָמַר לְהוּ: לְדִידִי, לָא סְבִירָא לִי דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב. לְדִידְכוּ, דִּסְבִירָא לְכוּ כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב — הֲלָכָה אֲנִי אוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what** did Rabbi Shimon mean when he responded: **Not so?** The Gemara explains that **this is what he said to them: According to my own** opinion, **I do not hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov,** and therefore your refutation is not valid for me. But even **according to you, who do maintain in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, I am** nevertheless **stating a *halakha*** handed down to me by my teachers that female Egyptians and Edomites are permitted.

The Rabbis respond by adducing the cases of *mamzer* and *arayot* as precedent: in those contexts, the prohibition falls upon both men and women, demonstrating that a prohibition need not be limited to males even where other asymmetries exist. Rabbi Shimon would rejoin that *arayot* and *mamzer* are different in a crucial respect: *mamzer* is conceived through an act carrying *karet*, and *arayot* themselves are *karet* offenses. By contrast, the prohibition against marrying *Mitzri* or *Adomi* does not even rise to the level of a *lav* — it is merely an *issur asei*. Given that lighter status, the *kal vachomer* in favor of permitting the women should hold.

### Shimon's Kareis Distinction

The Gemara then asks: what would the Rabbis answer to this? The Rabbis would cite the case of the *challal* who is produced through a violation of an *issur asei*, in accordance with Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov. For example, a *Kohen Gadol* who marries a non-*besulah* violates only an *issur asei*, yet according to Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov the child of that union is a *challal*, and *challal* status applies to both males and females immediately. This parallel, the Rabbis argue, shows that even an *issur asei* can yield a prohibition that applies equally to both sexes.

### Rabbanan's Asei Argument

Of course, the analogy is imperfect in one respect — the *challal* case is a *issur kehunah*, not an *issur kahal* — but the point stands nonetheless: there do exist cases where a person's status, derived from an *issur asei*, applies equally and immediately to both men and women.

### Halakha Ani Omer

Six lines from the bottom of 77b, the Gemara asks what Rabbi Shimon means when he concludes the Mishnah debate with the formulation *halacha ani omer* — "I have a tradition." The Gemara clarifies: *hachi ka'amar lehu* — here is what he is actually saying to them. *Ledidi lo svira li de'Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* — I personally do not hold like Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov; I maintain that an *issur asei* does not create *challal* status. Therefore, *ledidchu de'svira lechu ke'Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* — you, who do hold like Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov, think you can draw the parallel to *Mitzri* and *Adomi*. But even so, *halacha ani omer* — I still have a received tradition, and therefore even if my logical argument cannot be pressed to a definitive conclusion, the tradition itself is determinative. It is worth noting, as an aside, that the logical argument is in fact on Rabbi Shimon's side as well, even if the Rabbis have a technical basis for pushing back.

### Tosafot on Tradition

An interesting point emerges from *Tosafot* here. *Tosafot* notes: *lav halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai ka'amar* — Rabbi Shimon is not claiming a *halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai* in the technical sense. For if he were, why would the Rabbis continue to debate him? Rather, *ella hachi ka'amar halacha ani omer kach kibalti mei'rabosai* — he is saying, "This is my tradition: I received it from my teachers."

This point resonates with the broader position of the Rambam, who argued that genuine *halachot le'Moshe mi'Sinai* are never subject to rabbinic debate. This was the Rambam's response to the Karaite critique that the very existence of Talmudic disputes undermines the claim to an authentic *mesorah*. The Rambam's answer is that all rabbinic debates concern human minds engaging with the sacred text, reaching different conclusions through reasoning — not a broken chain of tradition. Where there was an actual transmitted tradition, there is no dispute. While the Rambam's position is itself subject to challenge — there are in fact disputes recorded about matters described as *halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai* — *Tosafot*'s point here is more specific: in this Mishnah, the Rabbis do not appear to be advancing a competing tradition of their own. They are simply arguing from logic. That being the case, why do they not simply concede to Rabbi Shimon's tradition? The answer, says *Tosafot*, is that he does not have a *halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai* — he has something more limited, a ruling or understanding received from his *Rebbi*, perhaps based on earlier *pesak* or on argument, and thus the Rabbis feel entitled to contest it.

## Baraita on Women

### Barim Pasuk Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר לָהֶן רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: הֲלָכָה אֲנִי אוֹמֵר, וְעוֹד: מִקְרָא מְסַיְּיעֵנִי, ״בָּנִים״ — וְלֹא בָּנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon said to them: I am stating a** traditional ***halakha*, and furthermore a verse supports me,** as the verse with regard to Edomites and Egyptians states: "The **sons** of the third generation that are born to them may enter to them, the congregation of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 23:9), teaching that the prohibition applies to their sons, **but not** to their **daughters.**

A *baraita* now addresses the same dispute. *Tanu rabbanan*: Rabbi Shimon said to them, *halacha ani omer* — I have a received tradition — *v'od mikra mesay'ani* — and moreover the verse itself supports me. And this support is arguably even stronger than the inference from *Ammoni v'lo Ammonit*. The verse reads: *banim asher yivaldu lahem dor shelishi yavo'u lahem b'kahal Hashem* — "the children that will be born to them, the third generation, shall enter the congregation of Hashem." Rabbi Shimon reads *banim* — "sons" — as excluding daughters: the sons will be permitted in the third generation, implying the daughters were never restricted in the first place.

### Yehuda's Yuladu Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״בָּנִים אֲשֶׁר יִוָּלְדוּ לָהֶם דּוֹר שְׁלִישִׁי״, הַכָּתוּב תְּלָאָן בְּלֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said:** The verse **states: "The sons of the third generation that are born to them** may enter to them, the congregation of the Lord," and the phrase "born to them" indicates that **the verse made their** prohibition **dependent on birth,** with regard to which there is no difference between males and females.

Rabbi Yehuda offers an alternative reading of the same verse. The verse says *banim asher yivaldu lahem dor shelishi* — "the children that will be born to them, the third generation." Rabbi Yehuda observes: *hakatuv telayan b'leida* — the verse has made the matter dependent upon birth, upon the act of giving birth. The one who gives birth is the mother, and the mother is a woman. This suggests that the mothers themselves — that is, women of the first and second generation *Mitzri* or *Adomi* — are also forbidden. The verse is concerned with when they will give birth, implying those women are themselves subject to the prohibition until the third generation.

### Yochanan's Evaluation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִי לָאו דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַכָּתוּב תְּלָאָן בְּלֵידָה — לֹא מָצָא יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר מָר: קְהַל גֵּרִים אִיקְּרִי קָהָל,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yochanan says: Had Rabbi Yehuda not said** that **the verse made their** prohibition **dependent on birth,** so that females are also included in the prohibition, **he would not have found his hands and feet in the study hall,** i.e., he would have been caught in a self-contradiction. Why? **Since the Master said** that according to Rabbi Yehuda **a congregation of converts is** also **called a congregation** of the Lord,

Rabbi Yochanan's assessment of this exchange is striking. He says: *ilmalei d'amar Rabbi Yehuda hakatuv telayan b'leida* — if Rabbi Yehuda had not made his point about the verse focusing on birthing and thereby demonstrating that women too are forbidden, *lo matza yadav v'raglav b'beit hamidrash* — he would have had no standing in the study hall, meaning his overall position would have been logically incoherent. The reason will now become clear.

## Marriage Possibility Issue

### Gerim Are Kahal

This creates a severe problem for Rabbi Yehuda's position that had not yet been articulated. The issue stems from another ruling associated with Rabbi Yehuda: *kahal gerim ikrei kahal* — converts are considered part of *kahal*, that is, the term *kahal Hashem* encompasses converts as well. This means that those who are forbidden from marrying into *kahal Hashem* may not marry *gerim* either. The standard assumption in this entire sugya had been that *Ammoni*, *Moavi*, *Mitzri*, and *Adomi* converts could at least marry other *gerim*. But Rabbi Yehuda's ruling on *kahal gerim* closes that door.

### Marital Impasse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִצְרִי שֵׁנִי בְּמַאי יִטְהַר? דִּלְמָא דְּאִי עֲבַר וּנְסֵיב. ״דְּאִי״ לָא כְּתִיב קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **how could a second-**generation **Egyptian** convert ever **achieve purity,** so that it is permitted for his offspring, the third generation, to enter into the congregation? He may marry neither a Jew nor a female Egyptian convert. The Gemara answers: **Perhaps** it means **that if he transgressed and married** a convert or a Jewish woman, his seed will be purified. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: A case of: **That if, is not written** in the **verse.** In other words, the Bible does not speak of situations that can arise only through the commission of a transgression.

This creates a severe problem: *Mitzri sheni bameh yit-har?* — how does a second-generation *Mitzri* ever come to have a third-generation child who is permitted? He cannot marry a *Yisraelit* — she is in the *kahal*. He cannot marry a *giyores* — she too is now *kahal* according to Rabbi Yehuda. Who remains for him to marry?

One might suggest he could marry a *mamzeret* — but then the children would themselves be *mamzerim* and could never enter *kahal Hashem* regardless. Another suggestion: perhaps he could marry a *Mitzrit* convert. But if, following Rabbi Shimon, *Mitzrit* women are permitted to all of Israel — that is, they are included in *kahal Hashem* like ordinary *gerim* — then they too would be forbidden to him. So if one holds both that *gerim* are *kahal* and that *Mitzrit* women are unrestricted, there is simply no permissible path by which a male *Mitzri* convert can ever produce a third generation that enters *kahal Hashem*.

### Mutar Pathway

The resolution must be, then, that *Mitzrit* women are themselves forbidden — that is, they are not included in *kahal Hashem*, their status is restricted just like the men, and therefore a male *Mitzri* can marry a female *Mitzrit*. That union provides the *mutar* pathway through which the third generation can ultimately come about.

### Torah Doesn't Speak B'di'avad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי מַמְזֵר ״דְּאִי״, וְכַתְבֵיהּ קְרָא! ״דְּאִי״ לְאִיסּוּרָא — כְּתַב, ״דְּאִי״ לְהֶיתֵּרָא — לָא כְּתַב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **isn't** there the *halakha* of **a *mamzer*,** which is a case of **that if,** as a *mamzer* is the child of a forbidden union, **and** yet **the verse writes it?** The Gemara answers: A case of **that if** that results in **a prohibition,** the Torah **writes,** but a case of **that if** that leads **to an allowance,** the Torah **does not write.** The Torah teaches the *halakha* of a *mamzer*, whose very existence is the result of his parents having engaged in forbidden relations, in order to render it prohibited for him to enter into the congregation. However, it would not teach the *halakha* of a second-generation Egyptian convert who transgressed and married a woman who was forbidden to him, in order to permit his offspring to enter into the congregation.

The Gemara suggests an alternative: perhaps we are talking about *b'di'avad* — a second-generation *Mitzri* who transgresses and marries someone he should not, and whose child similarly transgresses, thereby producing a third generation through prohibited unions. But the Gemara rejects this: *d'i lo katav kra* — the Torah does not write a verse addressing what happens when people have been sinning for two generations. The Torah would not frame a *hetter* — a permission to enter *kahal Hashem* — around the premise that transgressions were committed to get there.

### Mamzer Precedent

The Gemara challenges this principle with the case of *mamzer*: is not *mamzer* status itself created only through a transgression, and yet the Torah writes about it? So the Torah does speak of *b'di'avad* scenarios.

### Negative vs. Positive

The Gemara answers: *d'ei l'issura ktiv* — that verse addresses *b'di'avad* in order to impose negative consequences. If someone transgresses and a child is born from a forbidden union, the Torah tells you the negative outcome: the child is a *mamzer*. That is fundamentally different from a verse that would, *de'ei lehateira*, come to teach a *hetter* — a positive permission — that results from transgression. The Torah will tell you the negative consequences of sin; it will not frame a *hetter* as something achieved through sin.

### Mechzir Grushato Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי מַחֲזִיר גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ, ״דְּאִי״ לְהֶיתֵּרָא וְכַתְבֵיהּ? הָתָם מִשּׁוּם עִיקַּר אִיסּוּרָא הוּא דְּכַתְבֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: But **isn't** there the *halakha* governing **one who remarries his divorcée** after she had been married to another man? This is a case of **that if** that leads **to an allowance, and** yet the Torah **writes it.** The words "It is an abomination before the Lord" (Deuteronomy 24:4) stated with regard to this case teach that although the woman herself is forbidden to her first husband, if she nevertheless remarried him, their children are fit to enter into the congregation. The Gemara answers: **There,** the Torah **writes** that case **due to the basic prohibition,** i.e., that a man may not remarry his divorced wife after she has been married to another man, and the allowance with regard to their children is learned incidentally.

But is that really true? The Gemara raises the case of *machzir grushato* — the prohibition against a man remarrying a woman he divorced after she had married another man. From the verse dealing with that prohibition, a *drasha* teaches *hi toeivah ve'ein baneha toeivim* — she is forbidden but her children from the second marriage do not acquire a degraded status. That is a *hetter* derived from a context of prohibition. So the Torah does teach *heteirim* arising from problematic situations.

### Primary vs. Secondary

The Gemara resolves the difficulty: *hasam mishum ikar issura hu dekatveih* — the primary purpose of that verse is to teach the *issur*, to say that this man may not remarry her. The *hetter* regarding the children is incidental, secondary to the main point. A verse whose primary purpose is to establish a prohibition may include, in passing, a *hetter*. But a verse would not be written *primarily* to teach a *hetter* that is only accessible through transgression.

### Women Forbidden Necessary

The conclusion stands: for the Torah to have written that the third generation *Mitzri* may enter *kahal Hashem*, there must be a *mutar* path to reach that third generation. Given Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that *kahal gerim ikrei kahal*, the only available *mutar* path is for *Mitzri* men to marry *Mitzrit* women. And for that to be permissible, the *Mitzrit* women must themselves be in a restricted status — forbidden to ordinary Israel, and therefore available to marry *Mitzri* men. This is Rabbi Yochanan's point: without Rabbi Yehuda's observation that the verse focuses on birthing — thereby establishing that women too are subject to the prohibition — Rabbi Yehuda would have been unable to defend any coherent position. His ruling about *kahal gerim* would render the entire mechanism of the three-generation process impossible.

## Textual Parsing

### Banim vs. Dorot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִם נֶאֱמַר ״בָּנִים״, לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר ״דּוֹרוֹת״? וְאִם נֶאֱמַר ״דּוֹרוֹת״, לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר ״בָּנִים״? אִם נֶאֱמַר ״בָּנִים״ וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר ״דּוֹרוֹת״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: בֵּן רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי — אָסוּר, שְׁלִישִׁי — מוּתָּר, לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״דּוֹרוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with respect to Egyptian and Edomite converts that "the sons of the third generation that are born to them may enter to them, the congregation of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 23:9). **If it is stated "sons" why is it** also **stated "generation," and if it is stated "generation" why is it** also **stated "sons"?** One of these terms seems superfluous. The *baraita* explains: **If it** had **stated** only **"sons" and not stated "generation," I would say** that the prohibition depends on the number of the son, meaning that the **first and second sons** of an Egyptian convert are **forbidden,** whereas the **third** is **permitted. Therefore, it is stated "generation"** to indicate that the prohibition depends not on the number of the son but on his generation.

The Gemara now turns to close textual analysis of the verse *banim asher yivaldu lahem dor shelishi yavo'u lahem b'kahal Hashem*. The verse contains apparent redundancy: it uses both *banim* (children) and *dor* (generation). Why are both necessary? If the Torah wished to say "the third generation may enter," why not simply say *ben shelishi* or *dor shelishi*?

### Banim Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם נֶאֱמַר ״דּוֹרוֹת״ וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר ״בָּנִים״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר לְאוֹתָן הָעוֹמְדִים עַל הַר סִינַי, לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״בָּנִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **if** it had **stated** only **"generation" and not stated "sons," I would say** that the generations are counted from **those standing on Mount Sinai,** so that any Egyptian born after three generations have passed from the time of the giving of the Torah would be permitted. **Therefore, it is stated "sons"** to indicate that certain sons are prohibited in later generations as well.

*Im ne'emar banim lama ne'emar dorot* — if it had only said *banim* (sons or children), without specifying *dorot* (generations), one might have thought the count is based on children, not generations. That is, the third child born to the convert — even if that child is a sibling of the first and second child — would already be permitted. This would mean you need only a third child, not a third generation. Therefore *lekach ne'emar dorot* — the term "generations" is necessary to establish that it is the third generational descent that is required, not merely the third child.

### Dorot Meaning

Conversely, *im ne'emar dorot lama ne'emar banim* — had it said only *dorot* without *banim*, one might have read the three generations as counting from Har Sinai, or from the time the Torah was given, rather than from the moment of conversion. This would be a reasonable reading: these were the nations that wronged Israel, and so three generations from the revelation — from a fixed historical point — they will be permitted. Therefore *lekach ne'emar banim* — the word *banim* is needed to anchor the count to when they begin having children, that is, from the time of conversion and the onset of their progeny. Both terms are thus indispensable.

### First Lahem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לָהֶם״ — מֵהֶם מְנֵה. ״לָהֶם״ — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר פְּסוּלָן.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "The sons of the third generation that are born to them may enter to them, the congregation of the Lord." The Sages expounded each of the two instances of the words "to them": The first mention of **"to them"** teaches that **from them** you should **count.** The count of generations begins with the converts themselves, as they are considered the first generation, and therefore their grandchildren are permitted. The second mention of **"to them"** teaches that you should **follow their disqualification.** If a male Egyptian convert married a Jewish woman, or if a female Egyptian convert married a Jewish man, the *halakha* with regard to the offspring born to them is that although one of the parents is not disqualified from entering into marriage, the status of the child follows the unfit parent, who disqualifies his or her offspring until the third generation.

The verse also contains the word *lahem* twice: *banim asher yivaldu lahem dor shelishi yavo'u lahem b'kahal Hashem*. Each appearance teaches something distinct. The first *lahem* — *mehem meneh*, count from them — establishes that the generational count begins with the converts themselves, not from Har Sinai, and not from the children. The *lahem* fixes the starting point as the converts themselves. Without this, one might have read *asher yivaldu* (that will be born) as making the children generation one, so that the "third generation" would in fact be the great-grandchildren of the convert. *Lahem* corrects this: the convert is generation one, the children are generation two, and the grandchildren are the third generation permitted to enter *kahal Hashem*.

This is confirmed by the following consideration: one might have thought that a pregnant *Mitzrit* who converted, she and her fetus count as one — the fetus, having been converted as a fetus, remains generation one along with the mother. Therefore *katav rachamana asher yivaldu* — "that will be born" — to teach that once the child is actually born, that birth already advances the count to the next generation, even though the fetus was converted in utero.

### Second Lahem - Psul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״לָהֶם״, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״אֲשֶׁר יִוָּלְדוּ״. דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֲשֶׁר יִוָּלְדוּ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מִבְּנֵיהֶם מְנֵה, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״לָהֶם״. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״לָהֶם״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מִצְרִית מְעוּבֶּרֶת שֶׁנִּתְגַּיְּירָה — הִיא וּבְנָהּ חַד, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֲשֶׁר יִוָּלְדוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And it is necessary to write "to them," and it is** also **necessary to write "that are born." As, if the Merciful One had written** only **"that are born," I would say** that one should **count** the generations **from** the converts' **sons.** Therefore, **the Merciful One writes "to them"** to teach that the converts themselves are counted as the first generation. **And if the Merciful One had written** only **"to them," I would say** that in the case of **a pregnant Egyptian** woman **who converted, she and her child,** i.e., the fetus, are considered **one** generation, as the fetus is deemed to be a part of its mother. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes "that are born,"** to indicate that each birth marks a new generation, and accordingly the fetus is regarded as a second-generation Egyptian convert.

The second *lahem* teaches *halech achar psulan* — go after their disqualification, whichever parent bears the *Mitzri* or *Adomi* status, the child inherits that status, regardless of whether it is the father or the mother. One might have assumed that status follows only the father — given the general patrilineal principle in matters of tribal and national identity — or conversely only the mother. The second *lahem* establishes that whenever a *Mitzri* or *Adomi* is in the parentage, the *psul* follows.

A practical example: if a first-generation *Mitzri* marries a second-generation *Mitzrit*, or vice versa, the child is counted as second generation rather than third — because the child goes after the more severe of the two statuses. Similarly, if a *Mitzri* marries a *Yisraelit*, the child does not simply become a full *Yisrael* by following the father; the *lahem* ensures the *Mitzri psul* continues.

### Mamzer Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״לָהֶם״ הָכָא, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״לוֹ״ גַּבֵּי מַמְזֵר. דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא הָכָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּבָא מִטִּיפָּה פְּסוּלָה, אֲבָל מַמְזֵר דְּבָא מִטִּיפָּה כְּשֵׁרָה — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי מַמְזֵר, מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין רָאוּי לָבֹא בַּקָּהָל לְעוֹלָם, אֲבָל הָכָא — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The verse concerning a *mamzer* states: "A *mamzer* shall not enter into the congregation of the Lord; even to the tenth generation shall none of his [*lo*] enter into the congregation of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 23:3). **And it is necessary to write "to them" here,** with regard to an Egyptian convert, to teach that the child's status follows the disqualified parent in his case, **and it is** also **necessary to write "to him [*lo*]" with regard to a *mamzer*,** to teach that a similar *halakha* applies to a *mamzer*. In the translation of the verse, *lo* is translated as: Of his, i.e., of his ilk. However, *lo* can also be translated as: To him. *Lo* is written with regard to both the Egyptian and the *mamzer*, **as, if the Merciful One had written** it only **here,** with regard to an Egyptian, one might have said that only in this case does the child's status follow the disqualified parent, **because** the Egyptian **comes from an unfit drop** of semen, that of a gentile. **But** as for **a *mamzer*, who comes from a fit drop** of semen, as his parents were proper Jews despite their grave sin, one might say that there is **no** room for such stringency. Therefore, the Torah teaches that the same *halakha* applies to a *mamzer*. **And** conversely, **if the Merciful One had written** this stringent *halakha* only **with regard to a *mamzer*,** one might have said that this is because **he is forever unfit to enter into the congregation, but here,** with regard to an Egyptian convert, I might **say** that this is **not** the case. Therefore, both verses are **necessary.**

A parallel derivation exists by *mamzer*. The verse regarding *mamzer* says *lo yavo mamzer b'kehal Hashem*. From the word *lo* — corresponding to the *lahem* by *Mitzri* — the Gemara derives that wherever the *mamzer* is in the parentage, the *psul* follows that parent, whether the *mamzer* is the father or the mother. The same principle applies: the child of a *mamzer* father or a *mamzeret* mother is a *mamzer*.

One might ask: why state this principle separately for both *mamzer* and *Mitzri*? State it once and derive the other by analogy. The Gemara explains that each is necessary. Had the principle been written only by *Mitzri*, one might have said it is so because the *Mitzri* comes from a *tipah pesulah* — he was born outside the Jewish people, and therefore there is a profound invalidity to either parental line. But a *mamzer*, who was born Jewish albeit through a forbidden union, might be different — perhaps the *psul* would not follow from either parent. Therefore *lo* is written by *mamzer* as well.

And conversely, had it been written only by *mamzer* — whose prohibition is absolute and eternal — one might have concluded that this severe, permanent *psul* is what generates the rule that either parent transmits it. But by *Mitzri*, whose prohibition is only two generations and lighter in character, perhaps the *psul* follows only one parent. Therefore *lahem* is needed by *Mitzri* as well. Both formulations are indispensable: *tzericha*.

## Paternity vs. Maternity

### Yochanan - Follow Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִצְרִי שֵׁנִי שֶׁנָּשָׂא מִצְרִית רִאשׁוֹנָה — בְּנָהּ שְׁלִישִׁי הָאוֵי. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר בָּתַר דִּידֵיהּ שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If **a second-**generation male **Egyptian** convert **married a first-**generation female **Egyptian** convert, **her child is** considered **a third-**generation convert for whom it is permitted to enter the congregation. The Gemara comments: **Apparently** Rabbi Yoḥanan **maintains** that **we assign** the child **to him,** the father, and not to the mother.

The Gemara now addresses whether the *psul* in practice follows the father, the mother, or simply the weightier of the two statuses. Rabba bar bar Chana cites Rabbi Yochanan: *Mitzri sheni she'natal Mitzrit rishona bnah shelishi havei* — a second-generation *Mitzri* man who marries a first-generation *Mitzrit* woman, their child is a third generation and therefore permitted. This is a significant statement: it means that what matters is the father's status, not the mother's. Since the father is second generation, the child is third, regardless of the mother being first generation.

### Patrilineal Status

The implication, as the Gemara draws it: *alma kasavar basar didei shadina lei* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that we follow the father. This is, after all, the intuitively natural position in a patrilineal system. Yet the Gemara does not simply accept this inference and moves on to interrogate it.

### Rav Yosef Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף, רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: יְכוֹלִין מַמְזֵרִים לִיטָּהֵר. כֵּיצַד? מַמְזֵר נָשָׂא שִׁפְחָה — הַוָּלָד עֶבֶד. שִׁחְרְרוֹ — נִמְצָא בֶּן חוֹרִין. אַלְמָא בָּתַר דִּידַהּ שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״הָאִשָּׁה וִילָדֶיהָ תִּהְיֶה לַאדוֹנֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raised an objection** from the following mishna (*Kiddushin* 69a): **Rabbi Tarfon says: *Mamzerim* can purify themselves** over the course of the generations. **How so?** If **a *mamzer* married** his non-Jewish **maidservant, the child** born to them is **a slave.** If the slave's master, the *mamzer* who owns the maidservant, subsequently **freed** the child, **he becomes a free man** and is fit to enter into the congregation. **Apparently we assign** the child **to her,** the mother, and not to the father, as the child is deemed a slave rather than a *mamzer*. The Gemara answers: **It is different there,** in the case of the slave, **as the verse states: "The wife and her children shall be her master's"** (Exodus 21:4). The words "her children" indicate that the children born to a non-Jewish maidservant are assigned to her.

Rav Yosef challenges this with a *baraita*. Rabbi Tarfon says: *yecholim mamzerim litaher* — it is possible to purify *mamzerim* from their status. How? If a *mamzer* marries a *shifcha* (a Canaanite slave woman), the child is an *eved* — a slave — and the *mamzer* status is overridden or displaced by the slave status. If that child is subsequently freed, the person is a free Jew with no *mamzer* status. This practice was in fact suggested as a practical ruling by Rav Moshe Feinstein.

The Gemara then notes that from the *shifcha* case it might appear that the child follows the mother exclusively — the child of a slave woman is a slave, which seems to say you go by the mother.

### Minymin Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מִנְיָמִין גֵּר מִצְרִי הָיָה לִי חָבֵר מִתַּלְמִידֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וְאָמַר: אֲנִי מִצְרִי רִאשׁוֹן, וְנָשָׂאתִי מִצְרִית רִאשׁוֹנָה, אַשִּׂיא לִבְנֵי מִצְרִית שְׁנִיָּה, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא בֶּן בְּנִי רָאוּי לָבֹא בַּקָּהָל. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בָּתְרֵיהּ דִּידֵיהּ שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ — אֲפִילּוּ רִאשׁוֹנָה נָמֵי! הָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְתַנָּא: תְּנִי רִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection** from a previously mentioned *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda said: Minymin, an Egyptian convert, was a friend of mine from among the students of Rabbi Akiva, and he said:** Following my conversion **I** was **a first-**generation **Egyptian** convert, **and** so **I married** another **first-**generation **Egyptian** convert. **I will marry off my son,** who is a second-generation Egyptian convert, **to** another **second-**generation **Egyptian** convert, **so that my grandson will be fit to enter into the congregation. Now, if it enters your mind** to say that **we assign** the child **to** the father, **even** if he marries off his son to a **first-**generation Egyptian convert his grandson should be permitted. The Gemara answers: **Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan** already **say to the *tanna*** reciting the *baraita*: You should **teach** that Minymin sought to marry off his son to **a first-**generation Egyptian convert.

Rav Yosef's challenge is further developed by a *baraita* about Minymin, a first-generation *Mitzri* convert who was a student of Rabbi Akiva. Minymin said: "I am a first-generation *Mitzri*, and I married a first-generation *Mitzrit*. I intend to have my son marry a second-generation *Mitzrit*, so that my grandson will be fit to enter *kahal*." The critical element here is that Minymin planned for his son — who as the child of two first-generation *Mitzrim* is himself second generation — to marry a second-generation *Mitzrit*. The assumption is that the child of a second-generation father and a second-generation mother would be third generation, and therefore permitted. But this only works if both parents' status matters. If you simply followed the father, Minymin's son could marry even a first-generation *Mitzrit* — the child would still be third generation by the father's reckoning. The fact that Minymin insists on a second-generation mother demonstrates that you do not simply follow the father.

### Yochanan Emends

Rabbi Yochanan responds to this challenge by amending the text: *l'tana tani rishona* — emend the *baraita* to read that Minymin said his son could marry even a first-generation *Mitzrit*, consistent with the view that you always follow the father. Rabbi Yochanan is revising the wording of the *baraita* to conform to his own ruling, rather than taking the *baraita* as evidence against his position.

### Dimi - Follow Mother?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִצְרִי שֵׁנִי שֶׁנָּשָׂא מִצְרִית רִאשׁוֹנָה — בְּנָהּ שֵׁנִי הָוֵי. אַלְמָא בָּתַר אִימֵּיהּ שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** just the opposite: If **a second-**generation male **Egyptian** convert **married a first-**generation female **Egyptian** convert, **her son is** considered **a second-**generation convert who is prohibited from entering the congregation. **Apparently,** Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that **we assign** the child **to the mother** and not to the father.

When Rav Dimi arrived from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted a different version of Rabbi Yochanan's ruling: *Mitzri sheni shenassa Mitzrit rishona beno sheni* — a second-generation *Mitzri* man who married a first-generation *Mitzrit* woman, the son is second generation. This is the opposite conclusion from what Rabba bar bar Chana cited in Rabbi Yochanan's name. In this version, the child is second generation — apparently because the mother is first generation, which is the more severe status. The Gemara reads this as implying that you follow the mother: *alma basar imeh shadinan lei*.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הִפְרִישׁ חַטָּאת מְעוּבֶּרֶת וְיָלְדָה — רָצָה מִתְכַּפֵּר בָּהּ, רָצָה מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּוְלָדָהּ. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא עוּבָּר לָאו יֶרֶךְ אִמּוֹ הוּא — הָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּמַפְרִישׁ שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת לְאַחְרָיוּת, וְאָמַר רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: הִפְרִישׁ שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת לְאַחְרָיוּת — מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה תִּרְעֶה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ עוּבָּר יֶרֶךְ אִמּוֹ הוּא, הָוֵה לֵיהּ וְלַד חַטָּאת, וּוְלַד חַטָּאת לְמִיתָה אָזֵיל?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But** what, then, will you say about **that which Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If **one set aside a pregnant** animal as a **sin-offering, and** the animal later **gave birth** to a female, if **he wishes he may gain atonement with** the mother **itself,** in which case the young is left to graze until it develops a blemish that renders it unfit for sacrifice, whereupon it is sold and the proceeds are used for a gift offering; and if **he wishes he may gain atonement with** the animal's **young,** and the mother is left to graze until it develops a blemish. **Granted, if you say** that **a fetus is not** considered **the thigh,** i.e., a part, **of its mother** but rather a separate creature, despite the fact that it is still attached to her, then an individual in this situation **is like one who sets aside two sin-offerings as a guarantee,** i.e., one who, owing to his concern that his sin-offering might become lost, sets aside two animals from the outset with the intention of using whichever one he chooses. **And Rav Oshaya said** with regard to such a case: If **one set aside two sin-offerings as a guarantee,** so that if one is lost he may gain atonement with the other, **he gains atonement with one of them, and the second is left to graze** until it develops a blemish and can be redeemed. **But if you say** that **a fetus is** considered **the thigh of its mother** and is regarded as part of her, **it is the young of a sin-offering, and the young of a sin-offering goes to** its **death.** Such an animal is not left to graze. Rather, it is put into isolation and caused to die, as it has been sanctified as a sin-offering through its mother but cannot be sacrificed on the altar and used to gain atonement.

Abaye challenges Rav Dimi: if Rabbi Yochanan truly holds that you go by the mother, this contradicts another ruling of Rabbi Yochanan concerning a *chatas* that was pregnant when sanctified.

### Chatas Precedent

The ruling is this: if one set aside a female animal as a *chatas* while it was pregnant, and it subsequently gave birth, one may use either the mother or the offspring as the *chatas*. Rabbi Yochanan holds that this works because *ubar lav yerech imo hu* — the fetus is not considered a limb of the mother; it has an independent identity even while in the womb. Therefore when the pregnant animal was sanctified, there were effectively two animals sanctified: the mother and the fetus as an independent entity. This is like sanctifying two animals *le'achrayut* — as mutual backups — in which case one may use either one, and the other grazes until it acquires a blemish and is redeemed. Rabbi Oshaya rules that if two *chataot* are designated as backups for each other, one may atone with one of them and the other grazes until it is redeemed.

### Ubar Yerech Imo

The principle *ubar lav yerech imo hu* — the fetus is not a limb or extension of the mother but has its own independent identity — is what enables this ruling. If, by contrast, *ubar yerech imo hu* — the fetus were merely part of the mother's body — then the fetus would be in the status of *velad chatas*, a child born to a sanctified animal, and *velad chatas lemissa azil* — such an offspring must be left to die and may not be used as a *korban*. The fact that Rabbi Yochanan permits using the offspring shows he holds *ubar lav yerech imo*.

### Abaye's Logic

Abaye's argument is as follows: if the fetus has an independent identity — separate from the mother even while in the womb — then its personal status should not follow the mother. The independent entity of the fetus ought to follow the father, consistent with the general patrilineal principle. How, then, can Rabbi Yochanan simultaneously hold *ubar lav yerech imo* and also that the *psul* follows the mother?

It must be said that this is a striking association. The principle of *ubar yerech imo* concerns whether a fetus is viewed as a separate legal entity for purposes of sanctification, ritual status of *kodshim*, and similar matters. Why would the same concept govern whether a child's national or ethnic *psul* follows the father or the mother? The application seems genuinely difficult to understand. Yet the Gemara draws precisely this connection — and it is worth flagging that the Gemara itself, in debates about abortion and other *peshat* applications of *ubar yerech imo*, does not typically invoke this principle in the same framework. Here, the Gemara is forcing an association that, as the Gemara itself seems aware, requires explanation. Nevertheless, that is the argument Abaye presents.

### Dimi's Pasuk Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתִּיק. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּלְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר יִוָּלְדוּ״, הַכָּתוּב תְּלָאוֹ בְּלֵידָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קַרְקַפְנָא, חֲזֵיתֵיהּ לְרֵישָׁךְ בֵּינֵי עַמּוּדֵי כִּי אֲמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. טַעְמָא דִּכְתִיב ״אֲשֶׁר יִוָּלְדוּ״, הָא בְּעָלְמָא — בָּתַר אֲבוּהּ שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Dimi **was silent,** momentarily unable to find an answer. Abaye **said to him: Perhaps it is different there,** with regard to Egyptian converts, **as it is written** with regard to them: "The sons of the third generation **that are born to them** may enter to them, the congregation of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 23:9), indicating that **the verse made** their prohibition **dependent on birth,** and therefore the child of Egyptian converts is assigned to the mother. Rav Dimi **said to him:** Man of great **skull,** i.e., man of distinction, **I saw your head between the pillars** of the study hall **when Rabbi Yoḥanan taught this *halakha*.** In other words, you grasped the meaning as though you were actually present in the study hall and heard the statement from Rabbi Yoḥanan himself. **The reason** that the child is assigned to its Egyptian mother is **that it is written: "That are born** to them." **But generally,** with regard to others for whom it is prohibited to enter into the congregation, **we assign** the child **to the father.**

After the challenge, Abaye himself suggests the resolution: *dilma shani hasam dichtiv asher yivaldu* — perhaps by *Mitzri* it is different, because the verse says *asher yivaldu*, "that will be born," and *hakatuv talao b'leida* — the Torah has made the matter dependent on the act of birth. Since the verse focuses on the birthing, perhaps the Torah is specifically indicating that here, uniquely, the status follows the mother — she is the one who gives birth — even though in general *ubar lav yerech imo* would seem to point the other way.

### Normally Father

Rav Dimi responds with warm acknowledgment: *karkafna chaziti lreishach beini amudei* — I saw your head among the pillars of the study hall, meaning: you were there with Rabbi Yochanan when this was taught. And indeed, *ki amar Rav Yochanan lha shmata* — when Rabbi Yochanan taught this ruling, *taama dichtiv asher yivaldu* — the reason he gave was precisely the verse *asher yivaldu*, the focus on birthing. And he explicitly added: *ha be'alma basar avuh shadinan lei* — in all other cases, you follow the father. The rule here is a local exception derived from the specific formulation of the verse, not a general principle.

It is worth noting that the Gemara's entire discussion oscillates between three apparent positions: always following the father, always following the mother, and following the more severe of the two *psulim*. The resolution, as the Gemara will ultimately frame it, is that in general you do indeed follow the *psul* — the more severe of the two parental statuses — but by *Mitzri* and *Adomi* there is the additional local rule derived from *asher yivaldu* that anchors the status particularly in the birth, and thus the mother. The discussion of the *chatas* and *ubar yerech imo* in this connection, though logically puzzling, is the Gemara's vehicle for arriving at this conclusion.

## Non-Jews Follow Father

### Males Determine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִינָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּאוּמּוֹת, הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַזָּכָר. נִתְגַּיְּירוּ, הַלֵּךְ אַחַר פָּגוּם שֶׁבִּשְׁנֵיהֶם. בָּאוּמּוֹת הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַזָּכָר, כִּדְתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לְאֶחָד מִן הָאוּמּוֹת שֶׁבָּא עַל הַכְּנַעֲנִית וְהוֹלִיד בֵּן, שֶׁאַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לִקְנוֹתוֹ בְּעֶבֶד — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְגַם מִבְּנֵי הַתּוֹשָׁבִים הַגָּרִים עִמָּכֶם מֵהֶם תִּקְנוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravina came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** With respect to lineage, **among the** other **nations** of the world, i.e., while they are still gentiles, **follow the male,** but if they married after **they converted, follow the** more **flawed** in lineage **of the two.** The Gemara explains: **Among the nations, follow the male, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that** if **one from the** other **nations had relations with a Canaanite** woman **and had a son** from her, **you are permitted to purchase him as a slave,** and he is not considered a Canaanite who may not be allowed to remain in Eretz Yisrael? **As it is stated: "And also from the children of the strangers that dwell among you, of them may you buy,** and of their families that are with you, which they have begotten in your land; and they may be your possession" (Leviticus 25:45).

When Ravin arrived from Eretz Yisrael, he cited a third version of Rabbi Yochanan's teaching, one that synthesizes the various strands: *ba'umot halech achar hazachar* — among non-Jews, before conversion, you follow the male. When two individuals of different national backgrounds — say, a *Mitzri* and an *Ammonit*, or any two nationalities — have a child, and neither parent is Jewish, the child's national identity follows the father. This is the straightforward expression of the patrilineal principle in a context of non-Jewish nationality.

### Canaanite Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד מִן הַכְּנַעֲנִים שֶׁבָּא עַל אַחַת מִן הָאוּמּוֹת וְהוֹלִיד בֵּן, שֶׁאַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לִקְנוֹתוֹ בְּעֶבֶד — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר הוֹלִידוּ בְּאַרְצְכֶם״ — מִן הַנּוֹלָדִים בְּאַרְצְכֶם, וְלֹא מִן הַגָּרִים בְּאַרְצְכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **even** if **one from the Canaanite** nations **had relations with** a woman **from** one of **the** other **nations and had a son** from her, **you are permitted to purchase him as a slave.** Therefore, **the** same **verse states: "Which they have begotten in your land,"** which indicates that slaves may be bought only **from those begotten in your land,** i.e., from those whose father was a non-Canaanite and whose mother was a Canaanite. It is the way of women to remain in their own land, and so a child born in Eretz Yisrael was certainly born to a Canaanite mother. **But** slaves may **not** be bought **from those dwelling in your land.** If a child is born to a Canaanite man and a non-Canaanite woman outside of Eretz Yisrael, and that offspring later returns to dwell in Eretz Yisrael, the offspring may not be acquired as a slave, because his lineage follows his father. He is regarded as a Canaanite, who may not be allowed to remain in Eretz Yisrael.

This is derived from a *baraita*: *minayin le'achad min ha'umot sheba al hakena'anit veholid ben* — from where do we know that a man of another nation who had relations with a Canaanite woman and produced a child — that child is not Canaanite and you may purchase him as a slave? The verse states: *vegam mibnei hatoshavim hagerim imachem mehem tiknu* — from the settlers and sojourners who dwell among you, from them you may purchase. These are men of other nations settling among the Canaanites and having children with Canaanite women. Those children are not Canaanite, and may be acquired as slaves — whereas a Canaanite would have to be killed. This demonstrates that among the nations, identity follows the father: the child of a non-Canaanite father is not Canaanite even if born of a Canaanite mother.

Conversely, one might have thought the reverse — that a Canaanite man who had children with a woman of another nation would produce Canaanite children. The verse *asher holidu b'artzechem* — that were born in your land — implies that the children are born where the mothers are from, and those born in the land of another nation (through a Canaanite father with a foreign mother) are not of the same national status as Canaanites born locally. This reinforces: among non-Jews, identity follows the father.

### Worse Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְגַּיְּירוּ הַלֵּךְ אַחַר פָּגוּם שֶׁבִּשְׁנֵיהֶם. בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּמִצְרִי שֶׁנָּשָׂא עַמּוֹנִית — מַאי פָּגוּם שֶׁבִּשְׁנֵיהֶם אִית בַּהּ? ״עַמּוֹנִי״ וְלֹא עַמּוֹנִית! אֶלָּא בְּעַמּוֹנִי שֶׁנָּשָׂא מִצְרִית. אִי זָכָר הָוֵי — שִׁדְיֵיהּ בָּתַר עַמּוֹנִי, אִי נְקֵבָה הָוֵי — שִׁדְיַיהּ בָּתַר מִצְרִית.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught above in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan that if they married after **they converted, follow the more flawed** in lineage **of the two.** The Gemara asks: **To what** circumstances is this referring? **If we say** it is referring to **a male Egyptian** convert **who married a female Ammonite** convert, **what** is the meaning of: More **flawed** in lineage **of the two,** in **this** case? The *halakha* is that **an Ammonite** man is barred from entering into the congregation, **but not an Ammonite woman,** and so she is not flawed at all. **Rather,** it must be referring **to a male Ammonite** convert **who married a female Egyptian** convert. **If** the child **is male, assign him to** his **Ammonite** father, so that he is permanently barred from entering the congregation. **If** it is **a female, assign her to** her **Egyptian** mother, so that she is treated like a second-generation Egyptian convert.

Once they have converted, however, the rule changes: *nitgeiru halech achar pagum she'bishneihem* — after conversion, you follow the worse status of the two parents. Whatever *psul* is weightier between the two parents is the one that determines the child's status.

### Gender-Dependent

Suppose it is an *Amoni* man who married a *Mitzrit* woman. What is the worse status? The answer depends on the sex of the child, and this is where the analysis becomes nuanced. If the child is male, then being *Amoni* is the worse outcome — an *Amoni* male is forbidden to *kahal Hashem* for all generations. Being *Mitzri* is better since it is only two generations. So for a male child, the worse status is *Amoni*, and the child is classified as *Amoni* and forbidden entirely. If the child is female, however, then being *Amoni* is in a sense not a *psul* at all — *Ammonit* women are entirely permitted. Being *Mitzrit* is actually worse for a female, because she carries the two-generation restriction. So a female child of this union would be classified as a second-generation *Mitzrit*.

In other words, both parental identities are present in the child, and the one that generates the greater actual disability in that particular case — given the child's sex — is the one that governs. The conclusion, *halach achar ha'psul*, is thus not a simple mechanical rule but must be applied with attention to the specific configuration of both parents and the sex of the child.

## Mamzer Discussion

### Mishna Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַמְזֵרִין וּנְתִינִין אֲסוּרִין, וְאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם. אֶחָד זְכָרִים וְאֶחָד נְקֵבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: ***Mamzerim* and** the **Gibeonites** who converted to Judaism in the days of Joshua **are prohibited** from entering into the congregation and marrying a woman who was born Jewish. **Their prohibition is eternal,** for all generations, and it applies to **both males and females.**

We now turn to the final sugya of the daf. The Mishnah states: *mamzerim u-netinim asurim be-issur olam echad zecharim ve-echad nekevot* — *mamzerim* and *netinim* are forbidden forever, and this applies equally to males and females. The *netinim* are the Gibeonites who converted; their precise status — whether the prohibition against them is biblical or rabbinic — is a matter for subsequent discussion. The *mamzer* prohibition, of course, is well established.

### Resh Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַמְזֶרֶת לְאַחַר עֲשָׂרָה דּוֹרוֹת מוּתֶּרֶת. יָלֵיף ״עֲשִׂירִי״ ״עֲשִׂירִי״ מֵעַמּוֹנִי וּמוֹאָבִי. מָה לְהַלָּן נְקֵבוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת — אַף כָּאן נְקֵבוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Reish Lakish said: A *mamzeret*,** a female *mamzer*, **is permitted after ten generations.** Why? He **derived** this *halakha* by way of a verbal analogy between the word **"tenth"** stated in relation **to an Ammonite and a Moabite** in the verse "An Ammonite or a Moabite shall not enter into the congregation of the Lord; even to the tenth generation shall none of them enter into the congregation of the Lord forever" (Deuteronomy 23:4), and the word **"tenth"** stated in relation to a *mamzer* in the verse "A *mamzer* shall not enter into the congregation of the Lord; even to the tenth generation shall none of his enter into the congregation of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 23:3) He explained the analogy as follows: **Just as below,** with regard to an Ammonite and a Moabite, **females are permitted, so too here,** with regard to a *mamzer*, **females are permitted.**

Against this Mishnah, Resh Lakish advances a startling position: *mamzeret le'achar asarah dorot muteret* — a female *mamzer* becomes permitted after ten generations. This is undeniably a bold claim given the explicit language of the Mishnah, and it demands careful examination.

### Gezeira Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי: מָה לְהַלָּן מִיָּד, אַף כָּאן מִיָּד! כִּי אַהֲנִי גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, מֵעֲשִׂירִי וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Or** perhaps one should say that **just as below,** with regard to an Ammonite and a Moabite, their females are permitted **immediately, so too here,** a *mamzeret* is permitted **immediately.** The Gemara answers: **The verbal analogy is effective** only **from** the **tenth** generation **and onward.**

Resh Lakish learns this through a *gezeira shavah* connecting *mamzer* to *Ammoni* and *Moavi*. The verse concerning *Ammoni* and *Moavi* reads: *lo yavo Ammoni u-Moavi b'kahal Hashem gam dor asiri lo yavo lahem b'kahal Hashem ad olam* — they shall not enter the congregation of Hashem; even the tenth generation shall not enter the congregation of Hashem forever. Here, *dor asiri* (tenth generation) functions as a gloss on *ad olam* (forever): what "ten generations" means is "forever." By *mamzer*, the verse says: *lo yavo mamzer b'kahal Hashem gam dor asiri lo yavo lo b'kahal Hashem* — the same formulation of *dor asiri* appears, but without *ad olam*. Resh Lakish proposes to borrow the *ad olam* from *Ammoni* and *Moavi* and apply it to *mamzer* via this *gezeira shavah*. But — and this is the crux — the *ad olam* of *Ammoni* and *Moavi* applies only to the males of those nations: *Ammoni v'lo Ammonit*. Women of *Ammon* and *Moav* are permitted. Therefore, when we extend *mamzer* to *ad olam* via this *gezeira shavah*, we can only extend the male *mamzer*. Female *mamzerim*, after the tenth generation, are no longer covered by the *ad olam* and become permitted.

### Gezeira Shavah Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: מַמְזֵרִים וּנְתִינִין אֲסוּרִין, וְאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם, אֶחָד זְכָרִים וְאֶחָד נְקֵבוֹת! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר דּוּן מִינַּהּ וּמִינַּהּ. הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר דּוּן מִינַּהּ וְאוֹקֵי בְּאַתְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that ***mamzerim* and Gibeonites are prohibited, and their prohibition is eternal** for all generations, and it applies to **both males and females?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult** for Reish Lakish, as he understands that there is a dispute in this regard: **This** opinion, that of Reish Lakish, is **in accordance with** the *tanna* **who said** that the application of a verbal analogy is extended by way of the principle: **Infer from it, and** again **from it.** In other words, after deducing case B from case A, all of the characteristics of case A are applied to case B. In the case discussed here, although the verbal analogy comes primarily to render a *mamzer* permanently forbidden, it is extended and understood to mean that a *mamzeret* is permitted after ten generations. **That** other opinion, i.e., the mishna, is **in accordance with** the *tanna* **who said** that the application of a verbal analogy is limited, according to the principle: **Infer from it, and** then **leave it in its place.** That is to say, after the main provision of case A is applied to case B, case B is recognized as having its own character and specific rules that apply to it. Accordingly, in the case discussed here, the verbal analogy teaches one specific *halakha* that a *mamzer* is prohibited permanently, but nothing else.

The Gemara reconciles Resh Lakish's position with the Mishnah, which states that males and females are equally forbidden *ad olam*, by reference to a classical dispute about the methodology of *gezeira shavah*. There are two approaches: *don minah u-minah* — you take the learned principle together with its original context — and *don minah ve-okei be-atrah* — you derive the principle from the *gezeira shavah* but then anchor it in the new context. If you follow *don minah u-minah*, you take the *ad olam* of *Ammoni* and *Moavi* along with its original restriction to males, and apply that to *mamzer*: males are forbidden forever, females are not. If you follow *don minah ve-okei be-atrah*, you take the *ad olam* from *Ammoni* and *Moavi*, but plant it in the soil of *mamzer* — where the context is males and females equally — and then *ad olam* applies equally to both sexes.

### Compromise Resolution

The Mishnah, then, follows *don minah ve-okei be-atrah*, and Resh Lakish follows *don minah u-minah*. The *gezeira shavah* itself operates on the word *asiri*: both verses contain *dor asiri*, and from this shared term the *ad olam* is extended to *mamzer*. The practical upshot of Resh Lakish's position is that the first ten generations of both male and female *mamzerim* are explicitly forbidden by the verse; beyond ten generations, the *ad olam* extension applies only to males.

### Eliezer Cryptic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מַמְזֶרֶת לְאַחַר עַשְׂרָה דָּרֵי מַהוּ? אָמַר לָהֶם: מִי יִתֵּן לִי דּוֹר שְׁלִישִׁי וַאֲטַהֲרֶנּוּ. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר מַמְזֵרָא לָא חָיֵי. וְכֵן אֲמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַמְזֵרָא לָא חָיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that the students **asked Rabbi Eliezer:** With regard to **a *mamzeret* after ten generations, what is** the *halakha*? **He said to them: Who will give me a third-generation** *mamzer* so that **I will declare him pure?** The Gemara comments: **Apparently he maintains** that **a *mamzer* does not survive.** *Mamzerim* perish at the hand of Heaven, and therefore this question is not a practical one. **And similarly, Rav Huna said** that **a *mamzer* does not survive.**

The Gemara then cites an enigmatic exchange: *sha'alu et Rabbi Eliezer* — they asked Rabbi Eliezer, what is the status of a *mamzer* after ten generations? He answered: *mi yiten li dor shelishi va'ataharenu* — if I had a third generation in hand, I would purify them, make them permissible.

### Mamzerim Don't Live

The Gemara explains: *amar kasavar mamzera lo chaya* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that *mamzerim* simply do not live long enough to reach many generations. This is a metaphysical proposition: God, as it were, would never allow a situation in which unwitting Jews might transgress by marrying *mamzerim* whose status has been forgotten. Therefore divine providence ensures that *mamzerim* do not survive beyond a small number of generations. When Rabbi Eliezer says "if I had a third generation I would purify them," he means: you are asking me about ten generations — but they won't even reach three. And Rav Huna similarly holds: *mamzera lo chaya*.

But if so, why does the Mishnah speak of *mamzerim* being forbidden *ad olam*? Presumably the Mishnah implies they do in fact persist. The Mishnah's language of eternal prohibition must be addressing the normative rule, even if in practice the situation is mitigated by providence.

### Zeira's Nuance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: מַמְזֵרִין אֲסוּרִין, וְאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם! אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: דִּידִיעַ — חָיֵי, דְּלָא יְדִיעַ — לָא חָיֵי. דִּידִיעַ וְלָא יְדִיעַ — עַד תְּלָתָא דָּרֵי חָיֵי, טְפֵי — לָא חָיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that ***mamzerim* are prohibited** from entering into the congregation, **and their prohibition is eternal** for all generations? How is this possible if they do not even live long enough to produce three generations? **Rabbi Zeira said:** This matter **was explained to me by Rav Yehuda** himself: One **who is known** to be a *mamzer* will **survive,** as there is no concern that there will be any mingling of his seed. On the other hand, one **who is not known** as a *mamzer* will **not survive,** as he will die at the hand of Heaven so that there will be no mingling of his seed. As for one **who is known and not known,** i.e., one who is under suspicion, but it is unclear whether or not he is actually a *mamzer*, his descendants will **survive for three generations,** but **more** than this they will **not survive.**

Rabbi Zeira offers a more precise formulation of this principle, transmitted from Rav Yehuda. The rule is not absolute: *d'yedya chaya* — a *mamzer* who is publicly known does live, because there is no danger of unwitting transgression. The community is aware of his status and will not intermarry with him. *D'lo yedya lo chaya* — a *mamzer* who is unknown to the community dies, because the danger of inadvertent transgression is immediate. *D'yedya v'd'lo yedya* — one who is somewhat known, where there is uncertainty, *ad telata dara chaya* — lives up to three generations, because for one or two generations the memory of his status will keep people wary, but by the third generation the memory will have faded entirely, and at that point he will die.

### Rav Ami Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דְּהָוֵי בְּשִׁבָבוּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי. אַכְרֵיז עֲלֵיהּ דְּמַמְזֵרָא הֲוָה. בָּכֵי וְאָזֵיל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חַיִּים נָתַתִּי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **a certain person** lived **in Rabbi Ami's neighborhood,** and following an investigation Rabbi Ami **declared him to be a *mamzer*.** The man **went about weeping** until Rabbi Ami **said to him:** You should not be upset, as now **I have given you life.** As explained above, once one is publicly known as a *mamzer*, he and his descendants may survive.

The Gemara concludes this section with a striking story. There was a *mamzer* who lived in the neighborhood of Rav Ami. Rav Ami investigated and determined that this person was indeed a *mamzer*, and he made a public announcement to that effect. The man wept: what have you done to me by announcing my status to all? Rav Ami said to him: *chayim natati lach* — I have given you life. Had your status remained unknown, you would have died — God's providence would have ensured it, to prevent the community from inadvertently transgressing. Now that everyone knows you are a *mamzer*, you will live. The announcement of his *mamzer* status was itself an act of kindness, preserving his life by transforming him from an unknown *mamzer* into a known one.

We will pick up tomorrow with the discussion of *netinim*.
