# Avodah Zarah 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Context & Overview

### Mishnah Harshness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִתְרַפְּאִין מֵהֶן רִיפּוּי מָמוֹן, אֲבָל לֹא רִיפּוּי נְפָשׁוֹת. וְאֵין מִסְתַּפְּרִין מֵהֶן בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מוּתָּר, אֲבָל לֹא בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna discusses the issue of accepting certain professional services from a gentile. **One may be treated by** gentiles, provided that it is **monetary treatment, but not personal treatment. And one may not have his hair cut by them anywhere,** due to the danger that the gentile will kill him with the razor; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: In the public thoroughfare,** it is **permitted** to have one's hair cut by a gentile, **but not** when the Jew and gentile are **alone together.**

The *mishnayos* of this section of *Maseches Avodah Zarah* are, by any measure, striking in their severity. Having moved from earlier *mishnayos* that addressed concerns about bestiality, we now arrive at *mishnayos* that touch upon matters of life itself — who may care for you, who may heal you, and under what circumstances you may place yourself in the hands of a non-Jew. This reflects just how seriously *Chazal* regarded the dangers at stake. It is one thing to say: prefer a Jewish midwife over a non-Jewish one, or choose a Jewish nursemaid. *Tosafos* notes, interestingly, that the *Yerushalmi* is more permissive regarding non-Jews in such roles — a remarkable observation given that the *Mishnah* itself originates from *Eretz Yisrael*. But even granting that, we are about to encounter positions in the *Gemara* that go considerably further, extending these concerns even to the domain of medical treatment.

### Rabbi Meir v. Chachamim

The fundamental tension in these *mishnayos* runs between *Rebbi Meir* and the *Chachamim*. *Rebbi Meir* is concerned under all circumstances, a position consistent with his well-known tendency to be *chaish l'miuta* — to give weight even to unlikely possibilities. The *Chachamim*, by contrast, permit interaction with non-Jews when there is supervision or some form of public setting. *Tosafos* extends this leniency further, holding that even periodic, informal oversight — someone coming in and out of the house from time to time — is sufficient to remove the concern. Notably, a story is recounted of a non-Jewish nursemaid who boasted of having harmed Jewish infants, and the rabbis simply dismissed it as idle boasting. That dismissal itself illustrates how much the authority to interpret the law includes the authority to interpret the facts on the ground.

### Eivah Concept

A principle that threads through all of these discussions is *eivah* — the concern about inciting hatred. The *Gemara* permits certain forms of assistance to non-Jews, and certain forms of receiving assistance from them, when refusing would generate *eivah*. This functions as a kind of social pressure valve: where the refusal to help would breed dangerous resentment, *eivah* overrides the ordinary restriction. The *Gemara* even acknowledges that one can avoid *eivah* through artful excuses — "I'm sorry, my son is calling me" — as a legitimate strategy for extricating oneself without provoking hostility.

### Eivah & Shabbos

The concept of *eivah* takes on remarkable practical significance in the hands of later *poskim*. Rav Moshe Feinstein famously invokes this principle as the basis for permitting Jews — including *Hatzalah* volunteers and hospital workers — to treat non-Jewish patients on *Shabbos*. The reasoning is that if refusing to provide care would generate significant *eivah*, then that *eivah* is great enough to permit *chilul Shabbos*. *Tosafos* raises the objection: how can *eivah* alone justify desecrating *Shabbos*? Rav Moshe's response is that when the *eivah* rises to a sufficiently serious level, it does in fact carry that weight.

### Heretics vs Non-Jews

What emerged most starkly from the previous *daf* is that while the ordinary non-Jew is subject to restrictions that balance concern with pragmatism, the *moser* — one who hands Jews over to the authorities — and the heretic who has placed himself outside the community occupy an entirely different category. The *Gemara* treats these individuals with a severity that goes well beyond anything applied to an ordinary non-Jew. That harsh dimension of yesterday's learning sets the stage for what we will encounter at the end of today's *daf*.

### Brit Milah Intro

At the close of yesterday's *daf*, the *Gemara* pivoted to the topic of *bris milah* — specifically, who may perform a circumcision on a Jewish infant. The question arises in the context of healing and bodily intervention: if a non-Jew performs a *bris milah*, and that service is rendered for payment, is there an *eivah*-based permission? More fundamentally, is a non-Jew even a valid performer of *bris milah* at all? The discussion of *Rebbi Meir* established that his concern about placing an infant in the hands of a non-Jew stems from fear for the infant's safety. According to *Rebbi Yehuda*, however, such a concern does not apply in the same way. Yet conflicting *braitas* complicate the picture.

## Non-Jew vs Kuti

### Braita Conflict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרוֹפֵא מוּמְחֶה, דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִם הָיָה מוּמְחֶה לְרַבִּים — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** We are dealing **with an expert physician,** who will not risk his reputation by harming a child. This is similar to that which Rabbi Yoḥanan said, **as when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: If** the physician **was** considered **a recognized expert,** it is **permitted** for one to be healed by him. When Rabbi Meir said that an Aramean may circumcise a Jewish boy, he was referring specifically to a doctor who is known for his expertise.

The *Gemara* opens *Kaf Zayin Amud Aleph* by attempting to reconcile what appear to be conflicting *braitas* regarding who may perform *bris milah*. One *braita* seems to permit a non-Jew, another seems to permit a Samaritan (*Kuti*), and the positions of *Rebbi Meir* and *Rebbi Yehuda* seem to be inverted in different sources. The *Gemara* works through several reversals and un-reversals of these *braitas* before arriving at a coherent picture.

### Rofe Mumcheh

The resolution begins with the category of *rofeh mumcheh* — the expert physician. The principle, introduced by *Rav Dimi* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*, is that if a practitioner is an established expert recognized by the public, one may place oneself in his hands for treatment. The reasoning: a *mumcheh* will not risk his professional reputation by harming his patient. Applied to the question of *bris milah*, this means that *Rebbi Meir*'s safety concern can be satisfied by ensuring we are dealing with a recognized expert. In that case, the prohibition is not about safety but about *kavannah* — intention.

### Rabbi Meir's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה כּוּתִי שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל מָל אֶת הַכּוּתִי, וְכוּתִי לֹא יָמוּל יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמָּל לְשֵׁם הַר גְּרִזִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The latter clause of the *baraita* states that Rabbi Yehuda maintains that a Samaritan may circumcise a Jewish infant. The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Yehuda** actually **hold** that it is **permitted** for a Samaritan to perform circumcision? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **A Jew may circumcise a Samaritan but a Samaritan may not** be allowed to **circumcise a Jew, because he circumcises** him **for the sake of Mount Gerizim;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

According to this resolution, *Rebbi Meir* holds that a non-Jew is permitted to perform *bris milah* — we are speaking of an expert, so safety is not the concern — but a *Kuti* (Samaritan) is not permitted, because *Rebbi Meir* is concerned about the *Kuti*'s *kavannah*. The *Kuti* may be performing the circumcision with the wrong religious intention entirely, directed not toward God but toward *Har Gerizim*.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: וְכִי הֵיכָן מָצִינוּ מִילָה מִן הַתּוֹרָה לִשְׁמָהּ? אֶלָּא מָל וְהוֹלֵךְ עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא נִשְׁמָתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said to him: And where do we find that** the mitzva of **circumcision from the Torah** must be performed **for the sake of** fulfilling God's will? **Rather,** a Samaritan **may continue to circumcise** Jews **until his soul leaves** his body, i.e., until the Samaritan dies, and there is no room for concern. But Rabbi Yehuda explicitly states above that circumcision may not be performed by a Samaritan.

*Rebbi Yehuda* takes the opposite position: a non-Jew may perform *bris milah*, but a *Kuti* may not. *Rebbi Yehuda* is not raising a safety concern but rather a *lishmah* concern — the requirement that *bris milah* be performed with proper intention. Strikingly, then, while *Rebbi Yehuda* does not exclude the non-Jew on grounds of obligation, he does exclude the *Kuti* on grounds of corrupted intention.

### Har Gerizim Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְעוֹלָם אֵיפוֹךְ כִּדְאָפְכִינַן מֵעִיקָּרָא, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — הָהִיא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, actually** you should **reverse** the opinions in the *baraita* **as we reversed** them **initially. And** as for the **difficulty** raised with regard to one statement **of Rabbi Yehuda against** the other statement **of Rabbi Yehuda, that** opinion, that a gentile may not perform circumcision, **is** actually the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.** Conversely, the first *baraita*, which is reversed and therefore cites Rabbi Yehuda as maintaining that an Aramean may perform circumcision, is referring to Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai. Accordingly, the different opinions reflect a dispute between *tanna'im* rather than a contradiction.

A *braita* is then cited that makes *Rebbi Yehuda*'s position explicit: *Yisrael* may circumcise a *Kuti*, but a *Kuti* may not circumcise a *Yisrael*, because the *Kuti* performs circumcision *leshem Har Gerizim* — in the name of their holy mountain. This was the theological focal point of the Samaritan cult, and the *Gemara* notes that over time the *Kutim* evolved from a people with a somewhat divergent practice to one that was essentially worshipping *avodah zarah* on *Har Gerizim*. Whether that is historically accurate is another question, but that is the *Gemara*'s understanding.

### Lishmah Debate

*Rebbi Yosi* challenges the entire framework of a *lishmah* requirement for *bris milah*: where in the *Torah* do we find any requirement for *milah* to be *lishmah*? His position is essentially that *milah* does not carry a *lishmah* requirement at all, and rather than worrying about bad *kavannah*, we might simply allow whoever performs the circumcision to do so as a technical procedure. The phrase he uses — *ela ma v'holech ad sheteitzei nishmato* — carries a note of sharp irony: let the Samaritan drop dead, but don't tell me we need *lishmah* for *milah*.

### Yehuda vs Rebbi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְמִילָה בְּגוֹי שֶׁהִיא פְּסוּלָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאַתָּה אֶת בְּרִיתִי תִשְׁמוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi a gentile is not qualified to perform circumcision. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says: From where** is it derived with regard **to circumcision** performed **by a gentile that** it **is not valid? The verse states:** "And God said to Abraham: **And as for you, you shall keep My covenant,** you, and your seed after you throughout their generations" (Genesis 17:9).

One remaining difficulty is that a *Rebbi Yehuda* cited earlier seemed to say that a non-Jew is also excluded — not just the *Kuti*. The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between two figures: the *Rebbi Yehuda* who says a non-Jew may perform *bris milah* is the *Tanna Rebbi Yehuda* (son of *Ilai*), while the *Rebbi Yehuda* who excludes non-Jews entirely is *Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi* — *Rebbi* himself, a full generation later. A *braita* in the name of *Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi* derives this exclusion from the verse *v'atah et brisi tishmor* — "and you shall observe My covenant" — emphasizing that only one who is *commanded* in the *brit milah* may perform it.

## Lishmah Requirement

### Rav Chisda Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? דִּכְתִיב: ״לַה׳ הִמּוֹל״, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — ״הִמּוֹל יִמּוֹל״.
>
> **Translation:** § It was stated that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda circumcision must be performed for the sake of fulfilling a mitzva, whereas Rabbi Yosei holds that no particular intention is necessary. The Gemara analyzes these opinions. **Rav Ḥisda said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda? As it is written:** "And when a stranger shall sojourn with thee, and will keep the Passover **to the Lord let** all his males **be circumcised"** (Exodus 12:48). It can be inferred from the verse that the males must be circumcised "to the Lord," i.e., for the sake of fulfilling God's will. The Gemara asks: **And** what is the reasoning of **Rabbi Yosei?** It is written: **"He must be circumcised [*himmol yimmol*]"** (Genesis 17:13). The usage of the doubled verb teaches that circumcision may be performed by anyone.

The *Gemara* now turns to examine the underpinnings of the *lishmah* debate more carefully. *Rav Chisda* explains *Rebbi Yehuda*'s *lishmah* requirement by means of a creative reading of the verse concerning the *Korban Pesach*. The verse reads *v'asah Pesach Lahashem* — followed shortly by *himol lo kol zachar* (all males shall be circumcised). By parsing the phrase *Lahashem himol* — "for God shall the circumcision be" — *Rebbi Yehuda* derives that *milah* must be performed with the *kavannah* of *Shem Hashem*. The circumcision must be directed toward God, not toward some other purpose.

### Rabbi Yosi Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ, הָכְתִיב: ״לַה׳ הִמּוֹל״! הָהוּא בְּפֶסַח כְּתִיב. וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי הָכְתִיב: ״הִמּוֹל יִמּוֹל״! דִּבְּרָה תוֹרָה כִלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** Sage, i.e., Rabbi Yosei, **isn't it written: "To the Lord let** all his males **be circumcised,"** which indicates that circumcision must be performed for the sake of fulfilling God's will? The Gemara answers: **That is written with regard to Passover.** According to Rabbi Yosei, the phrase "to the Lord" is referring to the previous mention of the Paschal offering, rather than to circumcision. Accordingly, the verse should be read: "Will keep Passover to the Lord." The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** Sage, Rabbi Yehuda, **isn't it also written: "He must be circumcised [*himmol yimmol*],"** indicating that circumcision may be performed by anyone? The Gemara answers: **The Torah spoke in the language of people,** i.e., the doubled verb is the usual style of the Torah, which does not serve to teach a novel *halakha*.

*Rebbi Yosi* counters by pointing to the verse *himol yimol yelid beisecha* — used in connection with *Avraham*'s circumcision — which contains no *Lahashem* qualifier. This, he argues, demonstrates that *milah* can be valid without any specific *lishmah* requirement. As for the verse that *Rebbi Yehuda* cites, *Rebbi Yosi* contends that the *Lahashem* in that passage grammatically modifies *Pesach*, not *himol* — it is speaking about offering the *Korban Pesach* to God, not about performing circumcision for God's sake. *Rebbi Meir*, in response to the verse *himol yimol*, simply invokes the principle of *dibra Torah kilshon bnei adam* — the *Torah* sometimes uses doubled language as an ordinary linguistic convention, not to teach a broader rule.

### Lishmah Nature

This debate raises a more precise question: what does *lishmah* actually mean in the context of *bris milah*? The answer that emerges — developed by *Rashi* and elaborated by *Tosafos* — is that *lishmah* does not mean an active, explicit declaration of intent. There is no requirement to say *hineni muchan u'mezuman* before performing a *bris milah*, no need to announce that one is bringing this child into the *bris shel Avraham Avinu*. Rather, the concern is the inverse: *lishmah* means the absence of bad *kavannah*. If the performer has a wrong religious purpose — circumcising in the name of *Har Gerizim*, for example — that corrupts the act. But if the performer is simply performing the procedure without any particular religious orientation, that is sufficient.

### Tosafot Stam

*Tosafos* formalizes this with the concept of *stam lishmah*: where there is no explicit contrary intention, the act is automatically treated as *lishmah*. The default, so to speak, runs in the direction of validity. The criteria is not that the performer must affirmatively intend the act for God — it is that the performer must not actively intend it for something else. This is a significant narrowing of what might initially seem like a demanding requirement.

### Non-Jew Fitness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: מִנַּיִן לְמִילָה בְּגוֹי שֶׁהִיא פְּסוּלָה? דָּרוּ בַּר פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אָמַר: ״וְאַתָּה אֶת בְּרִיתִי תִשְׁמֹר״, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״הִמּוֹל יִמּוֹל״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues discussing the issue of circumcisions performed by gentiles. **It was stated: From where** is it derived with regard **to circumcision** performed **by a gentile that** it **is not valid? Daru bar Pappa says in the name of Rav:** This is derived from a verse, as it is stated: And God said to Abraham: **"And as for you, you shall keep My covenant,** you, and your seed after you throughout their generations." **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** says that it is derived from the verse: **"He must be circumcised [*himmol yimmol*]."** According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, this verse teaches that a Jew must be circumcised by one who is already circumcised.

What emerges from all of this is genuinely surprising. Both *Rebbi Meir* and *Rebbi Yosi*, despite holding different views on *lishmah*, share an underlying assumption: a non-Jew is fundamentally *kasher* to perform *bris milah*. *Rebbi Meir* excludes non-Jews only on grounds of safety, not because of any inherent disqualification. *Rebbi Yosi* excludes no one for lack of *lishmah*. This would be counterintuitive to almost anyone who thinks about the matter instinctively. *Bris milah*, of all things, is about entering the covenant, about the relationship between God and the descendants of *Avraham* — one would expect it to require a Jewish performer more than almost anything else.

### Brit vs Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? עַרְבִי מָהוּל וְגַבְנוּנִי מָהוּל אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. מַאן דְּאָמַר ״הִמּוֹל יִמּוֹל״ — אִיכָּא, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אֶת בְּרִיתִי תִּשְׁמֹר״ — לֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the practical difference **between** these two opinions? **There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to **a circumcised Arab or a circumcised hill person [*gavnuni*].** According to **the one who says** that the *halakha* that a Jewish infant may be circumcised only by one who has been circumcised himself is derived from the verse: **"He must be circumcised [*himmol yimmol*]," there is** reason to permit an Arab or *gavnuni* to perform the circumcision, as they are circumcised. **And** according to **the one who says** that circumcision may not be performed by a gentile is derived from the phrase: **"You shall keep my covenant," there is no** reason to permit an Arab or Gibeonite to perform circumcision.

The *Tosafos Rid* presses this point directly. If we require a Jew to perform *shechita* — not as a matter of *lishmah* but as a categorical requirement, such that a non-Jew's *shechita* is invalid regardless of his intentions — why would we not apply the same logic to *bris milah*? His answer is illuminating. In the case of *shechita*, the *Gemara* analyzes the act in great detail: the knife must be examined, pausing is forbidden, the process itself is regulated extensively. By contrast, the *Gemara* never discusses the *process* of performing *bris milah* — it discusses only the *outcome*. The *orlah* must be removed; the *priya* must be done. But how it is accomplished, what instrument is used, what physical motions are made — none of this is specified. If the act is defined entirely by its end result, then who performs it becomes secondary.

### Medical Origins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״הִמּוֹל יִמּוֹל״ אִיכָּא? וְהָתְנַן: קוּנָּם שֶׁאֲנִי נֶהֱנֶה מִן הָעֲרֵלִים — מוּתָּר בְּעַרְלֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאָסוּר בְּמוּלֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם; אַלְמָא, אַף עַל גַּב דִּמְהִילִי, כְּמַאן דְּלָא מְהִילִי דָּמוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **And is it so, according to the one who says** it is derived from the verse: **"He must be circumcised [*himmol yimmol*],"** that a Jew may not be circumcised by a gentile, that **there is** reason to permit a circumcised gentile to perform circumcision? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 31b): With regard to one who vows: **Deriving benefit from those who are uncircumcised is *konam* for me,** he **is permitted** to derive benefit **from uncircumcised Jews** because they are not regarded as uncircumcised, **but he is prohibited** from deriving benefit **from the uncircumcised of the nations of the world? Apparently, even though** some gentiles **are circumcised, they are** nevertheless **considered as those who are uncircumcised.**

This observation opens onto a broader historical picture. The *Tosafos Rid*'s analysis suggests that at an earlier stage, *bris milah* functioned essentially as a medical procedure — an operation whose validity was assessed by whether the *orlah* had been properly removed, not by the ritual identity of the operator. Consistent with this, the *Gemara* itself never uses the term *mohel*; it speaks only of a *rofeh*, a physician. There is no specialized ritual practitioner in these sources; there is a person who knows how to perform this operation. Equally notable is the fact that the earlier *Gemara* sources do not even discuss a *bracha* over *bris milah* — the *berakhos* surrounding the ceremony represent a later development.

### Ritualization Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ אִשָּׁה, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״וְאַתָּה אֶת בְּרִיתִי תִשְׁמוֹר״ — לֵיכָּא, דְּאִשָּׁה לָאו בַּת מִילָה הִיא, וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״הִמּוֹל יִמּוֹל״ — אִיכָּא, דְּאִשָּׁה כְּמַאן דִּמְהִילָא דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, there is** a difference **between** these two opinions with regard to **a woman. According to the one who says** that the *halakha* is derived from the verse: **"And as for you, you shall keep My covenant," there is no** reason to permit a woman to perform circumcision, **as a woman is not subject to** the mitzva of **circumcision,** and therefore she is not included in those who must keep God's covenant. **And according to the one who says** that the *halakha* is derived from the verse: **"He must be circumcised [*himmol yimmol*]," there is** reason to permit a woman to perform circumcision, **as a woman is considered as one who is** naturally **circumcised.**

What we can trace, then, is a gradual process of ritualization. At the earliest stage, *bris milah* is treated almost entirely as a technical procedure judged by its results. The first step toward ritualization comes with *Rebbi Yehuda*, who introduces the negative *lishmah* requirement — not that you must actively intend the right thing, but that actively intending the wrong thing is disqualifying. A generation later, *Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi* takes the further step of excluding non-Jews categorically, grounding this in the verse *v'atah et briti tishmor* — the performer must be someone commanded in the *brit*. Each step moves further from a purely outcome-based procedure toward a ritual act whose validity depends on the identity and orientation of the performer. It is also worth noting that *Rambam* understands *bris milah* as serving, among other purposes, the function of sexual continence — a reading that further de-emphasizes the covenantal-entry framing and may explain why some early authorities were less troubled by the idea of a non-Jewish performer.

## Circumcision Identity

### Source Basis

The *Gemara* now assumes as settled law that a non-Jew may not perform *bris milah* — the only remaining question is the textual source for this exclusion. Two *Amoraim* are cited, deriving the rule from different verses, and the difference between their derivations has significant practical implications.

### Two Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״הִמּוֹל יִמּוֹל״ אִיכָּא? וְהָתְנַן: קוּנָּם שֶׁאֲנִי נֶהֱנֶה מִמּוּלִים״ — אָסוּר בְּעַרְלֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וּמוּתָּר בְּמוּלֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, אַלְמָא אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מְהִילִי כְּמַאן דִּמְהִילִי דָּמוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion as well: **And is it so that according to the one who says** that the *halakha* is derived from the verse: **"He must be circumcised [*himmol yimmol*]," there is no** reason to permit an uncircumcised Jew to perform circumcision? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 31b): With regard to one who vows: **Deriving benefit from those who are circumcised** is *konam* **for me, he is prohibited** from deriving benefit even **from uncircumcised Jews and** he is **permitted** to derive benefit **from the circumcised of the nations of the world. Apparently, even though** some Jews **are not circumcised, they are** nevertheless **considered as those who are circumcised.**

*Daru bar Papa* in the name of *Rav* cites *v'atah et briti tishmor* — the same verse invoked by *Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi* — as the source for excluding non-Jews. *Rabbi Yochanan* derives it instead from *himol yimol*: only one who is circumcised (*hamal*) may circumcise (*yimol*). This is a play on the doubled form of the verb — the first iteration describes the performer's status, the second the action performed.

### Obligation vs Physical

These two sources differ not merely in their textual basis but in the criterion they impose. *V'atah et briti tishmor* speaks to *obligation* — the performer must be someone commanded in *bris milah*. *Himol yimol* speaks to *physical state* — the performer must himself be circumcised. At first glance these seem to produce the same result: Jews are obligated and circumcised; non-Jews are neither. But there are edge cases in which the criteria diverge.

### Categorical Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁמֵּתוּ אֶחָיו מֵחֲמַת מִילָה, וְלֹא מָלוּהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״וְאַתָּה אֶת בְּרִיתִי תִשְׁמֹר״ — אִיכָּא, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״הִמּוֹל יִמּוֹל״ — לֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, there is** a difference **between them** with regard to **a Jew whose brothers died due to circumcision, and** as a result, **they did not circumcise him. According to the one who says** that the *halakha* is derived from the verse: **"And as for you, you shall keep My covenant," there is** reason to permit such a person to perform circumcision, as he is a Jew. **According to the one who says** that the *halakha* is derived from the phrase: **"He must be circumcised [*himmol yimmol*]," there is no** reason to permit this Jew to perform circumcision, as he is not circumcised himself.

The *Gemara* raises what seems like an obvious difference: an Arab, who is circumcised (tracing this practice to *Yishmael*), or a *Givoni*, who may also be circumcised outside of any covenantal context. Under *Rabbi Yochanan*'s criterion of physical circumcision, such a person might seem eligible; under *Rav*'s criterion of obligation, he would not be. But before this distinction can be established, the *Gemara* introduces a teaching about *neder* that transforms the analysis entirely.

The *Gemara* cites a case of someone who declares *konam she'ani neheneh min ha'arelim* — a vow forbidding benefit from anyone who is *arel*, uncircumcised. One might expect this to track physical status precisely. Yet the ruling is: *mutar b'arlei Yisrael* — even uncircumcised Jews are permitted, because Jews as a category are designated "circumcised." And conversely, *asur bemulei ovdei kokhavim* — even a circumcised non-Jew is forbidden, because non-Jews as a category are designated "uncircumcised." The terminology is not anatomical but covenantal and national.

### Group Identity

This principle — that Jews are categorically circumcised and non-Jews are categorically uncircumcised, regardless of the physical reality of any individual — collapses the apparent distinction between the two sources. An Arab who is physically circumcised remains, by the *Gemara*'s logic, categorically uncircumcised. His physical state does not change his identity category. And a Jew who, for legitimate medical reasons — say, because his brothers died from *bris milah* and he is presumed to be in a family of hemophiliacs — was never circumcised, is still categorically circumcised as a Jew. Whether you apply the criterion of obligation or the criterion of physical circumcision, the practical result in these cases is the same: the Arab is out, the uncircumcised Jew is in.

This observation has a powerful implication for Jewish identity more broadly. The *Gemara* reflects a world in which *bris milah* is so central to Jewish male identity that it operates as a marker of the collective even when it does not apply to a given individual. There is a *midrash* according to which *Avraham Avinu* stands guard at the gates of *Gan Eden* and does not admit any male who is uncircumcised — which led to the practice, apparently observed in some communities, of circumcising a male infant who dies within his first eight days before burial, so that he would not be so excluded. Whether or not the *midrash* is meant literally, it reflects how profoundly *bris milah* functioned as a marker of Jewish male belonging.

### Women's Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אִשָּׁה לָא? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַתִּקַּח צִפֹּרָה צֹר״! קְרִי בֵּיהּ ״וַתַּקַּח״. וְהָכְתִיב ״וַתִּכְרֹת״! קְרִי בֵּיהּ ״וַתַּכְרֵת״, דְּאָמְרָה לְאִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא וַעֲבַד. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: (אתיא) [אֲתַיאי] אִיהִי וְאַתְחִלָה, וַאֲתָא מֹשֶׁה וְאַגְמְרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty against this explanation: **And is there anyone who says** that **a woman may not** perform circumcision? **But isn't it written: "Then Zipporah took [*vattikkaḥ*] a flint** and cut off the foreskin of her son" (Exodus 4:25). This verse explicitly states that a circumcision was performed by a woman. The Gemara answers that one should **read into** the verse: **And she caused to be taken [*vattakkaḥ*],** i.e., she did not take a flint herself. **But isn't it written: And she cut off [*vattikhrot*]? Read into** the verse: **And she caused to be cut off [*vattakhret*], as she told another person** to take a flint and cut off her son's foreskin, **and he did** so. The Gemara provides an alternative explanation: **And if you wish, say** instead: **She came and began** the act, **and Moses came and completed** the circumcision.

Since the *Gemara* has established that the categories "circumcised" and "uncircumcised" apply to Jews and non-Jews as classes — and that a Jew who is physically uncircumcised is nonetheless categorically circumcised — the question arises: what about a woman? Here the two sources genuinely diverge. Under *v'atah et briti tishmor*, a woman is excluded — she is not *obligated* in *bris milah*, as the *Gemara* states explicitly: *isha lav bat milah hi*, a woman is not one who performs this commandment. Under *himol yimol*, the opposite conclusion follows: since Jews as a class — male and female alike — are designated as circumcised, *d'isha keman d'mahili damya*, a woman is treated as though she is circumcised, and is therefore eligible.

The *Gemara* raises an objection to the possibility of excluding women entirely, citing the episode of *Tziporah*: *vatikach Tziporah tzor* — she took a flint and circumcised her son. The *Gemara* attempts to read this verse in ways that would not constitute a direct performance of *bris milah* by a woman — perhaps she had someone else do it, or perhaps she began it and *Moshe* completed it — but these are acknowledged to be forced readings. The episode clearly creates at least a *precedent* that cannot be easily dismissed.

### Women Status

*Tosafos* candidly acknowledges uncertainty about how to rule. On one hand, there is a *braita* that also cites *v'atah et briti tishmor* — which would align with *Rav*'s derivation and would exclude women. On the other hand, the principle of *himol yimol* seems more broadly supported in other sources. *Tosafos* notes that even though the general rule is that the *halacha* follows *Rabbi Yochanan* over *Rav*, in certain cases we rule like *Rav*, and this may be one of them — particularly because the *braita* associated with *Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi* also invokes *v'atah et briti tishmor*.

### Practical Ruling

Despite this uncertainty, the *Rambam*, the *Rif*, and the *Shulchan Aruch* all rule that a woman is *kosher* to perform *bris milah* — aligning with *Rabbi Yochanan*'s derivation from *himol yimol*. At the same time, all three add that *lechatchila* a man should perform the *bris*. What is striking is that none of them explain why *lechatchila* a man is preferred if a woman is fully *kosher*. The unstated reasoning appears to be: while a woman is *kasher* because Jews as a class are categorized as circumcised — and that extends to women — she nonetheless does not actually have a *bris milah*. We therefore prefer someone who genuinely and physically possesses what he is symbolically conferring, even if the woman's symbolic status is sufficient *b'diavad*.

### Priority Order

This leads to an interesting question of priority: if one must choose between an uncircumcised Jewish male and a woman to perform a *bris milah*, who takes precedence? Since the preference for a man seems to rest on the fact that he is actually circumcised, an uncircumcised Jewish male would appear to be in the same position as a woman — both are categorically circumcised, neither is physically so. A non-Jew, however, is entirely excluded and cannot perform *bris milah* under any circumstances. The *Rambam*'s statement that if a non-Jew performed a *bris milah* one does not perform another circumcision is not a ruling of *b'diavad* validity — it reflects the reality that once the foreskin is removed, there is nothing further to correct. The separate *Ashkenazic* practice of *hatafat dam bris* as a corrective measure is a later development of questionable effectiveness, but that is a discussion for another occasion.

## Healing Categories

### Mishnah Healing

The *Mishnah* now returns to the broader question of medical treatment from non-Jews. *Mitrappin mehen ripuy mamon* — one may receive what the *Mishnah* calls *ripuy mamon* from non-Jews, but not *ripuy nefashot*. The precise meaning of these terms is, as the *Gemara* acknowledges, not self-evident.

### Haircut Restriction

On a separate but related note, the *Mishnah* records *Rebbi Meir*'s view that one may not receive a haircut from a non-Jew *b'chol makom* — anywhere, even in public. The concern is that the barber uses a razor in close proximity to the neck, and *Rebbi Meir*'s general concern about even slim possibilities of danger overrides the normal leniency extended for public settings. The *Chachamim* permit it in public but not in private. *Tosafos*, having already encountered the principle of *rofeh mumcheh*, extends that principle here as well: where the barber is an established professional, the same logic that would permit consulting a recognized doctor would presumably apply.

### Payment Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״רִיפּוּי מָמוֹן״, וּמַאי ״רִיפּוּי נְפָשׁוֹת״? אִילֵּימָא ״רִיפּוּי מָמוֹן״ בְּשָׂכָר, ״רִיפּוּי נְפָשׁוֹת״ בְּחִנָּם — לִיתְנֵי: מִתְרַפְּאִין מֵהֶן בְּשָׂכָר אֲבָל לֹא בְּחִנָּם!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** is **monetary treatment, and what** is **personal treatment? If we say** that **monetary treatment** is medical attention provided in exchange **for payment,** whereas **personal treatment** is medical attention provided **for free,** then **let** the mishna **teach: One may be treated by** gentiles in exchange **for payment, but not for free.**

The *Gemara* first proposes that *ripuy mamon* means treatment for which payment is rendered — you are paying the non-Jewish physician — while *ripuy nefashot* refers to free treatment. The implicit reasoning would be that a paid practitioner is accountable in a way that one who offers his services gratis is not: he risks losing his fee if something goes wrong, and his professional reputation is on the line.

### Payment Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, ״רִיפּוּי מָמוֹן״ — דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ סַכָּנָה, ״רִיפּוּי נְפָשׁוֹת״ — דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ סַכָּנָה. וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אֲפִילּוּ רִיבְדָּא דְּכוּסִילְתָּא לָא מִתַּסֵּינַן מִינַּיְיהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another explanation: **Rather, monetary treatment** is referring to medical treatment for **a matter that poses no** life-threatening **danger,** whereas **personal treatment** is referring to treatment for **a matter that does pose** life-threatening **danger.** The Gemara rejects this suggestion as well. **But doesn't Rav Yehuda say: Even** with regard to the wound of **a bloodletting incision [*rivda dekhusilta*] we are not** permitted to be **treated by** gentiles. The wound left after bloodletting certainly does not pose life-threatening danger, and yet a Jew is prohibited from having it treated by a gentile.

The *Gemara* rejects this interpretation. If the concern is genuinely about risk to life, the payment arrangement is irrelevant. Someone who intends to harm you is not deterred by the prospect of forfeiting his fee. Moreover, the *Gemara* notes that if this were the operative distinction, the *Mishnah* should simply have said: one may receive treatment when paying, but not when it is free — rather than employing the language of *ripuy mamon* and *ripuy nefashot*.

### Severity Theory

The alternative reading: *ripuy mamon* refers to a condition that does not endanger life — one seeks treatment for a cough, a rash, something uncomfortable but not dangerous — while *ripuy nefashot* refers to a life-threatening condition. The term *mamon* is puzzling in this context; *Rashi* explains it as referring to the fear that the treatment may worsen the condition and result in greater medical expenses, rather than a direct threat to life.

### Rav Yehuda Strict

*Rav Yehuda* takes the position further: even for something as minor as an ache from a bloodletting wound, he would not seek treatment from a non-Jew. The concern, apparently, is that once you are under any form of medical care, there is always some risk of escalation — you never know where it leads once you place yourself under their hands. The *Gemara* thus effectively closes off two avenues that might have seemed to provide leniency: paying a fee does not help, and the minor nature of the condition does not help.

### Animal vs Human

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, ״רִיפּוּי מָמוֹן״ — בְּהֶמְתּוֹ, ״רִיפּוּי נְפָשׁוֹת״ — גּוּפֵיהּ, וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אֲפִילּוּ רִיבְדָּא דְּכוּסִילְתָּא לָא מִתַּסֵּינַן מִינַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, monetary treatment** is referring to medical treatment provided for **one's animal,** whereas **personal treatment** is referring to treatment provided for **his** own **body, and this is** in accordance with **that** which **Rav Yehuda says: Even** with regard to the wound of **a bloodletting incision, we are not** permitted to be **treated by them.**

*Rav Chisda* in the name of *Mar Ukva* offers the definitive resolution. *Ripuy mamon* means healing of one's *property* — one's animals. You may call a non-Jewish veterinarian. *Ripuy nefashot* means healing of one's own *body*. This accounts for *Rav Yehuda*'s strictness as well: even a minor wound on one's own body is subject to the prohibition, because the moment the treatment touches your person, the risk cannot be safely bounded.

### Advice Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא: אֲבָל אִם אָמַר לוֹ סַם פְּלוֹנִי יָפֶה לוֹ, סַם פְּלוֹנִי רַע לוֹ — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Mar Ukva says: But if** a gentile **said to him: Such and such a potion is beneficial for** this ailment, or **such and such a potion is harmful for** this ailment, it is **permitted** to adhere to the gentile's advice.

There is, however, an important exception. *Rav Chisda* in the name of *Mar Ukva* permits asking a non-Jewish physician for medical *advice* — what treatment is beneficial, what is harmful — without actually receiving direct treatment.

### Advice Safety

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר שַׁיּוֹלֵי מְשַׁאֵל לוֹ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּמַשְׁאִיל לוֹ מְשַׁאֵיל לְאִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא, וַאֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא לְאוֹרוֹעֵי נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the rationale for this leniency: The gentile **thinks** to himself that the Jew **is asking him** for his opinion, and **just as he is asking him, he** will also **ask other people. And** the gentile further reasons that if the Jew understands that the gentile provided him with bad advice, **that man,** i.e., the gentile, **will bring harm to himself** by damaging his own reputation. It is therefore assumed that the gentile will provide good advice in order to avoid sullying his reputation.

The rationale is instructive. When a non-Jewish physician is merely consulted, he knows that his recommendation will be weighed, cross-checked, and perhaps compared with other opinions. He is not actually administering the treatment himself. Should he give bad advice, it will become known — *v'ata hu gavra lerei nafshei* — and his reputation will be destroyed. He has no incentive to mislead. This reasoning closely parallels the logic of *rofeh mumcheh*: it is precisely the professional's concern for his standing that makes him trustworthy. The safeguard of reputation provides the same guarantee as does the presence of other witnesses.

## Terminal Exception

### Safeik Chai Safeik Meis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: סָפֵק חַי סָפֵק מֵת — אֵין מִתְרַפְּאִין מֵהֶן, וַדַּאי מֵת — מִתְרַפְּאִין מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes a situation in which one may receive medical attention from gentiles. **Rava says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says, and some say** that it was **Rav Ḥisda** who **says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If there is **uncertainty** as to whether a patient will **live** through his ailment **or die** from it, the patient **may not be treated by** gentile doctors, due to the concern that a gentile doctor may kill him. But if it is **certain** that he will **die** from his affliction if he does not receive medical attention, the patient **is treated by them,** as it is possible that a gentile physician will save him.

*Rava* — some say *Rav Chisda* — in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* now draws a crucial distinction based on the patient's prognosis. Where there is *safeik chai safeik meis* — a genuine uncertainty as to whether the patient will live or die if left untreated — one may not seek medical treatment from a non-Jew. The fear that the non-Jewish physician may actively cause harm is considered real enough that we prefer the fifty-fifty odds of nature over the risks of placing oneself in non-Jewish hands. This reflects a level of concern that is not mere rhetoric or social prejudice but a genuine halakhic assessment of risk.

### Vadai Meis

If, however, the patient is *vadai meis* — certainly going to die without treatment — then he may seek treatment from a non-Jew. At that point, the calculus shifts: nothing can make his situation worse in ultimate terms, and the possibility of cure outweighs the risk of harm.

### Speed Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת? הָאִיכָּא חַיֵּי שָׁעָה! לְחַיֵּי שָׁעָה לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: Even if it is certain that the patient will **die** if he is not treated, **nevertheless, there is** value in **temporal life,** i.e., it is preferable for the Jew to live as long as his ailment permits rather than risking a premature death at the hands of a gentile physician. The Gemara explains: **We are not concerned with** the value of **temporal life** when there is a possibility of permanent recovery, and therefore it is preferable to receive medical attention from a gentile despite the risk involved.

The *Gemara* challenges even this permission. Even if someone is certainly going to die, surely the non-Jewish physician might kill him *sooner* — depriving him of whatever days remain. Why, then, should he be permitted to seek that treatment?

### Chayei Sha'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דִּלְחַיֵּי שָׁעָה לָא חָיְישִׁינַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם אָמַרְנוּ נָבוֹא הָעִיר וְהָרָעָב בָּעִיר וָמַתְנוּ שָׁם״, וְהָאִיכָּא חַיֵּי שָׁעָה! אֶלָּא לָאו לְחַיֵּי שָׁעָה לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do you say that we are not concerned with** the value of **temporal life? As it is written** with regard to the discussion held by four lepers left outside a besieged city: **"If we say: We will enter into the city, then the famine is in the city, and we shall die there;** and if we sit still here, we also die. Now therefore come, and let us fall unto the host of the Arameans; if they save us alive, we shall live; and if they kill us, we shall but die" (II Kings 7:4). The starving lepers decided to risk premature death rather than waiting to die of starvation. The Gemara asks rhetorically: **But isn't there temporal life** to be lost, in which case it would be preferable for the lepers to remain in their current location? **Rather, is it not** apparent that **we are not concerned with** the value of **temporal life?**

The answer: *lechayei sha'ah lo chaishinan* — we do not treat a brief extension of remaining life as a consideration that forecloses all other choices. Once a person is certainly going to die from his condition, the prospect of losing a few additional days does not constitute a decisive objection to undertaking treatment that might lead to recovery.

### Metzora'im Source

The *Gemara* finds a source for this principle in the episode of the four *metzora'im* — *Geichazi* and his three companions — who sat outside the city during the Aramean siege, knowing they would die of famine if they remained and possibly be killed at the Aramean camp if they ventured there. They chose to go to the camp regardless. The *Gemara* reasons: they were certainly going to die in any case; the risk of dying somewhat sooner at the Aramean camp was not treated as a compelling reason to forego the chance of survival.

### Trade-off Principle

*Tosafos* is careful to clarify what this principle does and does not mean. It does not mean that *chayei sha'ah* — a brief extension of life — is of no value. To the contrary: the *halacha* is that one may violate *Shabbos* in order to extend a dying person's life even by a day or two. *Chayei sha'ah* is real and weighty. What the principle establishes is rather that a *trade-off* between *chayei sha'ah* and the possibility of full recovery is a legitimate trade-off to make. If the choice is between certain death in three days on the one hand, and a procedure that might cause death today but might also lead to complete recovery on the other — that is a trade-off one is permitted to accept.

### Patient Choice

Crucially, the *Gemara*'s formulation is that one *may* seek treatment in such circumstances — *mitrappin*, permitted. The most natural reading of the *halacha* as it has developed is that this is a permission, not an obligation. The patient is entitled to choose the risky procedure that might bring recovery; equally, the patient is entitled to decline it. One need not submit to every available medical intervention simply because it might extend life.

### End-of-Life

This has profound implications for end-of-life *halacha*. The question of whether a terminally ill patient is *obligated* to undergo a painful or risky procedure that might add months to his life — or whether he is *permitted* to decline — turns significantly on this *Gemara*. The *poskim* have wrestled extensively with where the boundary of *chayei sha'ah* lies: is a patient with two years to live in this category? Six months? A year? Different authorities draw the line differently, but all are working from the same slender textual basis. What the *Gemara* here establishes is the core principle: in the domain of *chayei sha'ah*, certain trade-offs are permitted, and the individual has a degree of choice about whether to accept them.

## Heresy Exception

### Heretic Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: לֹא יִשָּׂא וְיִתֵּן אָדָם עִם הַמִּינִין, וְאֵין מִתְרַפְּאִין מֵהֶן אֲפִילּוּ לְחַיֵּי שָׁעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **A person may not engage in dealings with heretics, and one may not be treated by them even in** a case where it is clear that without medical attention one will experience only **temporal life.**

A *meitivei* — a challenge — is now raised against the permission to seek non-Jewish medical treatment when certainly dying. A *braita* states: *lo yissa v'yitten adam im haminin* — one may not engage in dealings with heretics — *v'ein mitrappin mehen afilu lechayei sha'ah* — and one may not be healed by them even for *chayei sha'ah*, even if one has only a brief time left to live. This appears to flatly contradict what was just established.

### Ben Dama Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּבֶן דָּמָא בֶּן אֲחוֹתוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל שֶׁהִכִּישׁוֹ נָחָשׁ, וּבָא יַעֲקֹב אִישׁ כְּפַר סְכַנְיָא לְרַפּאוֹתוֹ, וְלֹא הִנִּיחוֹ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְאָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָחִי! הַנַּח לוֹ וְאֵרָפֵא מִמֶּנּוּ, וַאֲנִי אָבִיא מִקְרָא מִן הַתּוֹרָה שֶׁהוּא מוּתָּר, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לִגְמוֹר אֶת הַדָּבָר עַד שֶׁיָּצְתָה נִשְׁמָתוֹ וָמֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* relates an incident illustrating this point. There was **an incident involving ben Dama, son of Rabbi Yishmael's sister,** in **which a snake bit him. And** following the attack, **Ya'akov of the village of Sekhanya,** who was a heretic, a disciple of Jesus the Nazarene, **came to treat him, but Rabbi Yishmael did not let him** do so. **And** ben Dama **said to him: Rabbi Yishmael, my brother, let him** treat me, **and I will be healed by him. And I will cite a verse from the Torah** to prove **that** accepting medical treatment from a heretic **is permitted** in this situation. **But** ben Dama **did not manage to complete the statement before his soul departed** from his body **and he died.**

The *baraita* illustrates this principle with the famous story of *Ben Dama*, the nephew of *Rabbi Yishmael*. *Ben Dama* was bitten by a snake. *Ya'akov ish Kefar Sechania* — identified in some versions as *mitalmidav shel Yeshu HaNotzri*, a disciple of Jesus — came to heal him. *Rabbi Yishmael* refused to allow the healing to take place.

### Ben Dama Argument

*Ben Dama* pleaded with his uncle: *Rabbi Yishmael achi*, let him heal me, and I will prove from a verse in the *Torah* that it is permitted. He was prepared to invoke the principle of *vechai bahem* — the *Torah*'s commandments are to be lived by, not died by — as grounds for receiving the heretic's treatment even under these circumstances.

### Ben Dama Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרָא עָלָיו רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אַשְׁרֶיךָ בֶּן דָּמָא, שֶׁגּוּפְךָ טָהוֹר וְיָצְתָה נִשְׁמָתְךָ בְּטׇהֳרָה, וְלֹא עָבַרְתָּ עַל דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירֶיךָ, שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: ״וּפוֹרֵץ גָּדֵר יִשְּׁכֶנּוּ נָחָשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael recited with regard to him: Fortunate are you, ben Dama, as your body is pure and your soul departed in purity, and you did not transgress the statement of your colleagues, who would state** the verse: **"And who breaks through a fence, a snake shall bite him"** (Ecclesiastes 10:8), i.e., one is punished for ignoring an ordinance of the Sages. This incident indicates that it is not permitted for one to accept medical treatment from a heretic even if it is clear that without it he will live only a short while.

*Ben Dama* never completed his proof. He died in the middle of his appeal, his soul departing before he could cite the verse. *Rabbi Yishmael* eulogized him: *ashreicha Ben Dama* — praised are you, *Ben Dama* — *shegufecha tahor v'yatza'ah nishmatcha betaharah* — your body is pure and your soul departed in purity. The formulation echoes the famous *midrash* about *Rebbi Eliezer* on his deathbed, whose last word was *tahor*. Here, the last word *Ben Dama* was reaching toward was *muttar* — permitted — itself a kind of tragic irony.

### Rabbinic Edict

*Rabbi Yishmael*'s praise is specific: *velo avarta al divrei chaveirecha* — you did not transgress what your colleagues instituted. The rabbis enacted a prohibition against receiving healing from heretics, and *Ben Dama* died without violating it. The warning cited — *poretz gader yishchennu nachash*, one who breaks down the fence will be bitten by a snake — is applied with a note of dark symmetry: he was bitten by a snake, but he did not violate the rabbinic fence, and therefore died pure. Had he been healed by the heretic and thereby violated the rabbinic edict, he would have been bitten by a different snake — the "rabbinic snake," for which there is no cure.

### Minut Different

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי מִינוּת דְּמָשְׁכָא, דְּאָתֵי לְמִימְּשַׁךְ בָּתְרַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Heresy is different, as it is enticing.** In other words, it is prohibited to accept medical treatment from a heretic, **as one might come to be drawn after** his heresy. By contrast, receiving medical attention from a gentile is permitted if it is certain that one will die if he is not treated.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between this *braita* and the earlier permission for the terminally ill to seek non-Jewish medical treatment: *shani minut demashcha* — heresy is different, because it leads people astray. The concern about receiving treatment from an ordinary non-Jewish physician is a concern about physical safety. Once the patient is certainly going to die anyway, that concern is neutralized. But the concern about receiving treatment from a heretic is not about physical safety at all — it is about spiritual contagion. When a disciple of Jesus heals someone through an incantation invoking Jesus's name, onlookers may conclude that the power of this belief is real and compelling. That perception leads people astray. To prevent that spiritual harm to the community, even a dying person may not receive this form of treatment.

### Tosafot Limit

*Tosafos* introduces an important qualification. The prohibition is not triggered merely by the identity of the healer. If *Ya'akov ish Kefar Sechania* had come as a straightforward medical professional, offering a treatment based entirely on medical knowledge, there would be no prohibition — he could have treated *Ben Dama* freely. Similarly, if the cure required something that happened to be physically associated with an *avodah zarah* context — say, dates from a palm tree growing near a temple — but the treatment carried no invocation of that *avodah zarah*'s power, it would also be permissible, even if there were a separate *issur hana'ah* concern. What is prohibited is specifically treatment in which the healing is attributed to, or presented through, the power of the heretical belief. It is the communicative dimension — the suggestion that "this works because of Jesus" or "because of this particular theological claim" — that creates the *issur*. The concern is about what the healing *says*, not merely who performs it.

### Private vs Public

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר? ״וְחַי בָּהֶם״ — וְלֹא שֶׁיָּמוּת בָּהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what would** ben Dama **have said?** What verse did he intend to cite as proof that it was permitted for him to be healed by a heretic? The verse: "You shall therefore keep My statutes, and My ordinances, which if a man do, **he shall live by them"** (Leviticus 18:5). This teaches that one should live by God's mitzvot, **and not that he** should **die by them.** This verse serves as a source for the *halakha* that one may violate a prohibition in order to save a life.

*Ben Dama*'s intended argument — *vechai bahem velo sheyamut bahem* — was that one should live by the commandments rather than die by them. *Rabbi Yishmael* did not dispute this principle in general; indeed, *Rabbi Yishmael* is in other contexts notably lenient in its application. A *braita* records *Rabbi Yishmael*'s view: if someone is told to commit *avodah zarah* or be killed, he should transgress and live — *vechai bahem*. The verse mandates life. But this applies only *betzina* — in private. In public — *befarhesya* — the verse *velo techalelu et shem kodshi* applies: one may not desecrate God's name publicly, even to save one's life.

### Chillul Hashem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל? הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּצִינְעָא, אֲבָל בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא — לָא. דְּתַנְיָא: הָיָה רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם אוֹמְרִים לוֹ לָאָדָם ״עֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְאַל תֵּהָרֵג״, שֶׁיַּעֲבוֹד וְאַל יֵהָרֵג? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְחַי בָּהֶם״ — וְלֹא שֶׁיָּמוּת בָּהֶם. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלֹא תְחַלְּלוּ אֶת שֵׁם קׇדְשִׁי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** why does **Rabbi Yishmael** disagree with ben Dama? He maintains that **this matter** applies only **in private, but in public** one **may not** transgress a prohibition even to save a life. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael would say: From where** is it derived **that if** oppressors **say to a person: Worship an idol and you** will **not be killed, that one should worship** the idol **and not be killed? The verse states: "He shall live by them," and not that he should die by them.** One **might** have thought that this applies **even in public.** Therefore, **the verse states: "And you shall not profane My holy name"** (Leviticus 22:32).

*Tosafos* draws out the logic of *Rabbi Yishmael*'s position. For *Rabbi Yishmael*, the governing consideration is not which prohibition is being violated — whether it is one of the *yehareg v'al ya'avor* offenses or a lesser transgression — but rather whether the act constitutes *chillul Hashem* through its public character. In *Rabbi Yishmael*'s framework, a Jew publicly performing any transgression under coercion — even eating *chazir* — constitutes *chillul Hashem* and cannot be permitted even to save one's life. Conversely, even *avodah zarah* may be transgressed in private to preserve life. The entire analysis is structured around publicity and the desecration of God's name, not around the inherent severity of the act.

This framework applies directly to the case of *Ben Dama*. Receiving healing from a disciple of Jesus was not *avodah zarah* per se. But it was public, and its effect was to demonstrate the power of a heretical belief system before witnesses. That public demonstration constitutes *chillul Hashem* in *Rabbi Yishmael*'s sense — or, in the *Gemara*'s broader framing, a form of leading people astray that the rabbis treated as equivalent to one of the gravest prohibitions.

### Faith Priority

The *Gemara*'s conclusion is sobering. Protecting the faith community from heresy, and preventing public acts that would lend credibility to beliefs that undermine *emunah*, is a value for which the *halacha* — at least in this context — demands the ultimate sacrifice. A person who is certainly dying may choose to seek medical treatment from an ordinary non-Jewish physician, trading *chayei sha'ah* for the chance of recovery. That trade-off is permitted. But where the treatment comes enmeshed with the demonstration of heretical power, the trade-off is not permitted — and the rabbis said so even knowing it meant that *Ben Dama* would die rather than be healed.
