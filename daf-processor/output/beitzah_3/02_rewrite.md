# Beitzah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Beitzah Explanations

### Rav Nachman: Muktzah

The daf begins at the very bottom of 2b, where we have already encountered two explanations for the debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel in the *mishnah*. The first and simplest explanation was Rav Nachman's: Beit Shammai does not hold of *muktzah* or *nolad*, while Beit Hillel does. The *gemara* rejected this explanation for what seems a fairly minor textual reason — though perhaps a questionable one — which nonetheless allowed the discussion to advance to the next explanation.

### Rabbah: Hachana

The second explanation is Rabbah's, which introduces the concept of *hachana* — preparation — derived from a biblical verse. This is not a rabbinic *muktzah* concern but a verse-based principle. As Rashi points out, *hachana* is specifically bound up with preparing for the *seudah* of Shabbat and Yom Tov. Accordingly, something completed not on a weekday, but intended for the Shabbat or Yom Tov meal, poses a problem even when its completion occurs naturally. The egg, which finishes developing inside the chicken on a Shabbat that precedes Yom Tov, is problematic because it was not already prepared and ready on a weekday for the Yom Tov meal. Whether this principle is limited specifically to food-related items is perhaps not fully clear, but that is Rabbah's framework.

### Rav Yosef: Peiros Hanosh…

The third explanation appears two lines from the bottom of 2b, where Rav Yosef states: *gezeirah mishum peiros hanoshrin* — it is a rabbinic concern modeled on the case of fruit that drops from a tree. On Shabbat and Yom Tov, one may not eat fruit that has fallen from a tree, even if *muktzah* is not technically a problem, because permitting it might lead one to go and pluck fruit directly from the tree. Rav Yosef argues that an egg dropping from a chicken belongs to the same category: it is like food that has fallen from its originating source. Accordingly, even when the chicken was designated for eating — meaning there is no *muktzah* or *nolad* concern — the egg remains forbidden by analogy to *peiros hanoshrin*.

Before continuing, one might ask: if the chicken was designated for eating, why is there a *muktzah* problem to begin with? Tosafot raises this point, noting that if the fruit on the tree were about to drop naturally — say, because ravens in the area would normally eat it — perhaps the fruit would not be considered out of reach and therefore not *muktzah*. Yet it would still be forbidden due to this rabbinic concern. The question of why *muktzah* does not itself suffice to prohibit it is therefore a real one, and Tosafot suggests constructing a scenario where the imminent and natural detachment of the item removes the *muktzah* problem while the rabbinic decree still applies.

## Abaye: Gezeirah L'Gezeir…

### Abaye's Question

Abaye challenges Rav Yosef with a pointed question. He asks: *peiros hanoshrin* — fruit that falls from a tree — why is that forbidden? Because of a *gezeirah shema yaaleh veyitlosh*: we are concerned one will climb the tree and pluck the fruit directly. But that prohibition is itself already a rabbinic *gezeirah*. So Rav Yosef is proposing that the egg be forbidden lest it lead to the fruit-from-the-tree scenario — which is itself only a fence around a Torah prohibition. This amounts to a *gezeirah legezeirah* — a fence around a fence. The general principle is that one rabbinic safeguard is sufficient to protect a Torah prohibition; constructing a second layer of protection around the first exceeds the rabbinic mandate.

### Kula Chada Gezeirah

The *gemara* responds: *kula chada gezeirah hi* — it is all one single *gezeirah*. This answer requires careful unpacking. The claim is not simply that the rabbis chose to make an extra fence for additional protection — though Tosafot does note in other contexts, such as the rules of meat and milk, that rabbis sometimes deliberately construct multiple layers of protection. The poultry-meat prohibition, the prohibition against eating chicken and milk even cold, the six-hour waiting period — Tosafot acknowledges that in certain areas, a *gezeirah legezeirah* is indeed erected. But that is not what *kula chada gezeirah* means here.

### Rashi: Abstract Category

Rashi explains the principle differently and more precisely. The rabbis did not say: "Fruit fallen from a tree is forbidden — and therefore, by extension, let us also forbid the egg lest it lead to that case." Rather, when the rabbis formulated the decree about *peiros hanoshrin*, they framed it in an abstract and expansive category: any food that separates from its originating source on Shabbat is forbidden. The egg was therefore included within that original *gezeirah* from the outset — not as a derivative safeguard, but as part of the same conceptual decree. The egg is not prohibited because it might lead someone to pick fallen fruit; rather, the egg is itself a species of "food that fell from its originating source," and that is the very category the rabbis meant to address. Beit Hillel endorses this concern; Beit Shammai does not.

## Four Beitzah Explanati…

### Rav Yitzchak: Mashkin Sh…

Rav Yitzchak, in the third line from the top of the new daf, offers a fourth explanation: *gezeirah mishum mashkin shezavu* — a rabbinic concern modeled on the case of liquids that flow from fruit. One is forbidden to squeeze fruit on Shabbat and Yom Tov, and by extension, even liquid that drains spontaneously from fruit is forbidden — lest permitting the naturally-expressed juice lead one to actively squeeze the fruit. Rav Yitzchak argues that the egg, which flows out of the chicken on its own, belongs to this same category of something that emerged from its source without active intervention. The egg, in his view, is analogous to grape juice that seeps out on its own.

## Abaye: Mashkin Gezeirah

### Abaye vs. Rav Yitzchak

Abaye raises the same structural challenge against Rav Yitzchak that he raised against Rav Yosef. *Mashkin shezavu* — liquids that flow from fruit — why are they forbidden? Because of *gezeirah shema yisochot*: we fear one will come to actively squeeze the fruit. That is itself a rabbinic safeguard. Is Rav Yitzchak then proposing that the egg be forbidden because it resembles a case that is itself already a *gezeirah*? That would again constitute a *gezeirah legezeirah*.

### Kula Chada (Liquids)

The *gemara* gives the same answer: *kula chada gezeirah hi*. Just as with Rav Yosef's explanation, the decree concerning *mashkin shezavu* was formulated at an abstract level that already encompassed the egg. When the rabbis said that things which flow out of their originating source are forbidden, they were speaking broadly enough to include the egg within that category. The egg is thus not a fence built around the fence of flowing liquids — it is part of the same original fence.

## Fruit vs. Liquids Debate

### Assessment of Positions

The *gemara* now pauses to take stock of where matters stand. It notes that all the remaining *Amoraim* — those offering the third and fourth explanations — declined to adopt Rav Nachman's position. The objection to Rav Nachman, recall, was a textual one: if the debate were simply about *muktzah* and the chicken were designated for laying eggs, we would expect the *mishnah* to address the chicken as well, not only the egg. Rav Nachman's explanation is therefore set aside. Similarly, the remaining *Amoraim* did not adopt Rabbah's position — they do not hold of this biblical concept of *hachana*. *Muktzah* as a rabbinic category they accept; the independent biblical principle of *hachana*, tied to preparation for Shabbat and Yom Tov meals, is Rabbah's alone.

### Rav Yosef's Choice

The *gemara* then asks: Rav Yosef, who frames the egg as analogous to *peiros hanoshrin* — why does he not instead adopt Rav Yitzchak's reasoning and analogize it to flowing liquids? The answer: *beitzah ochla* — an egg is food, a solid foodstuff. *Peiros ochla* — fruit is likewise food. The egg and the fallen fruit share the same category of solid edibles, and the analogy is therefore more natural. Liquids, *mashkin*, are a different type of entity entirely, and the egg is less similar to juice than it is to fruit. The closer analogy is therefore to *peiros hanoshrin*.

### Rav Yitzchak's Choice

Conversely, the *gemara* asks: Rav Yitzchak, who uses the flowing-liquids framework — why does he not adopt Rav Yosef's fruit-from-a-tree analogy? He acknowledges the difference in physical form, but argues that a more fundamental distinction governs the comparison. *Beitzah blu'ah* — an egg is absorbed, contained, held within the chicken. What emerges is something that was enclosed inside its source and then came out. That dynamic is precisely what characterizes juice inside a grape: it is held within the fruit and then flows out. Fruit hanging on a tree, by contrast, is *miglo v'kaimu* — exposed and present in front of you, visible and exterior. The egg's enclosed origin makes it more like the contained-and-released liquid than like the externally hanging fruit.

This comparison points once more to the fact that neither of these explanations involves a *gezeirah legezeirah* in the strict sense. The prohibition is not: "Avoid the egg lest it lead you to the other case." Rather, the egg is conceptually similar enough to one of these categories that when the rabbis originally formulated the decree in abstract terms, the egg was already included. The debate between Rav Yosef and Rav Yitzchak is simply about which abstract category the egg more closely resembles.

## Fruit Picking on Yom Tov

### Tosafot's Challenge

An important question now emerges. Both of these final explanations presuppose that picking fruit from a tree or squeezing fruit is forbidden even on Yom Tov. But this requires examination, because on Yom Tov, acts performed for the purpose of food preparation are generally permitted. One can perform *shechitah*, one can cook — why, then, can one not pick fruit from a tree? Is that not equally a food-related act?

Tosafot raises this question in the passage beginning with the word *yivla'enu*, approximately in the middle of the relevant discussion. He writes: *ach teima* — it is a wonder. Why must we worry, *shema ya'aleh veyitlosh*, that one will climb and pluck fruit from a tree? *Ha afilu l'chatchila mutar litlosh* — should it not be permitted outright on Yom Tov? After all, *ochel nefesh mutar* — acts for the purpose of food are permitted, and the fact that one could have prepared in advance is no disqualification: cooking on Yom Tov is permitted even though one could have cooked beforehand. So why is plucking fruit from a tree forbidden?

### Yerushalmi: Milishah V'E…

Tosafot cites an explanation from Rabbeinu Nesanel in the name of the Yerushalmi. The verse states: *ach asher ye'achel l'chol nefesh* — whatever is needed for eating — and this is immediately juxtaposed with *ushmartem et hamatzos* — you shall guard the *matzos*. The Yerushalmi learns from this juxtaposition that the *melachot* permitted for food preparation on Yom Tov are those that correspond to the stage of production at which one must begin guarding *matzah* from becoming *chametz*. When does one begin guarding? From the moment water meets flour — that is, from the stage of kneading. The Yerushalmi therefore derives: *mishimur va'eilach, hem mutarot d'haynu milisha va'eilach* — from kneading onwards, these *melachot* are permitted. *Aval she'ar melachot d'mikodem lachen asurin* — but all earlier stages of production are forbidden.

### Home vs. Field Preparati…

This becomes a foundational conceptual principle about what *ochel nefesh* permits on Yom Tov. It does not extend to going out into the field with a scythe and cutting down wheat. The Yerushalmi establishes a limit — understood by the Yerushalmi as biblical — and ties it to the analogy of *matzah*: only from kneading onward is preparation permitted.

What underlies this distinction conceptually? Some *Acharonim* suggest the key is the locus of the preparation: acts of food preparation that are performed within the domestic sphere — in the home — are permitted, while harvesting, grinding, and sifting, which take place in the field or at industrial scale, are not. Interestingly, *shechitah* in our context might seem to belong in the field, but in the ancient household one would slaughter in one's own courtyard, so it still fell within the domestic domain.

It should be noted that this principle is not universally accepted in precisely this form. Not all authorities hold that the Yerushalmi's formulation is the basis, nor that the cutoff is *de'oraita*. And there are complicating cases later in the tractate: certain spices, for example, may be ground on Yom Tov — a stage of production that precedes *lishahby far — because freshly ground spices are superior in a way that cannot be replicated in advance. Grinding is much earlier in the sequence than kneading, yet the *halachah* may permit it under certain conditions. So *milisha va'eilach* is not necessarily an absolute cutoff in every case, but what it establishes unambiguously is that not every food-related act is permitted on Yom Tov — there are limits, and the paradigmatic phrase is *milisha va'eilach*.

## Rabbi Yochanan's Problem

### R. Yochanan: Mashkin

The *gemara* now states that Rabbi Yochanan likewise holds that the reason behind the *mishnah*'s prohibition of the egg is *gezeirah mishum mashkin shezavu* — it is in the category of liquids that flow from their source. This is demonstrated by a question Rabbi Yochanan raises: he notes what appears to be a contradiction between two positions attributed to Rabbi Yehuda and proceeds to resolve it — but the very fact that he perceives a tension between these two positions reveals his underlying assumption about the egg.

The first statement of Rabbi Yehuda comes from a *mishnah* in Shabbat: *ein sochtin et hapeiros lehotzi mehen mashkin* — one may not squeeze fruit to extract liquid from it, and even if liquids flow out on their own, *v'im yatzu me'atzman asurin* — they are forbidden. But Rabbi Yehuda qualifies this: *im l'ochlin, hayotzei mehen mutar* — if the fruit was designated for eating, the liquid that comes out naturally is permitted. *V'im l'mashkin, hayotzei mehen assur* — but if the fruit was designated to be turned into liquid, what flows out is forbidden.

The logic is straightforward: the rabbinic concern is that permitting naturally-expressed juice will lead one to actively squeeze the fruit. But that concern only applies when the fruit's purpose is to yield juice — as with grapes designated for wine. If the fruit is meant to be eaten — like a grapefruit set aside for consumption — there is no reason to fear that allowing a bit of runoff juice will lead to active squeezing. The rabbinic decree therefore does not apply in that case.

### R. Yochanan's Contradict…

Rabbi Yochanan now identifies what appears to be a contradiction. The principle stated above is that any food that comes from food set aside for eating is permissible — *ochla de'afreshei* — like the juice from a grapefruit. But Rabbi Yehuda, in another context, speaks of an egg laid on Yom Tov as problematic. Rabbi Yochanan asks: the egg comes from a chicken that is set aside for eating. Should that not make it analogous to the grapefruit case, where the derived food is permitted? Why does Rabbi Yehuda treat the egg as a problem?

The very fact that Rabbi Yochanan finds this contradiction significant — that he is troubled by the apparent inconsistency between the egg case and the grapefruit case — reveals his assumption. He assumes that the egg's prohibition is grounded in the same principle as the *mashkin shezavu* concern. If he held that the egg was forbidden for a different reason — *muktzah*, or *hachana*, or fruit from a tree — the grapefruit case would be wholly irrelevant, and no contradiction would arise. The *gemara* notes this explicitly: from the fact that Rabbi Yochanan connects these two positions and is troubled by their apparent inconsistency, *shema mina chad ta'ama hu* — we infer that he holds they share a common basis. Rabbi Yochanan therefore sides with the view that the egg falls into the category of *mashkin shezavu*.

### Rosh Hashanah Two-Day Is…

The second statement of Rabbi Yehuda, which Rabbi Yochanan finds in tension with the first, comes from a debate in Eiruvin concerning the status of the two days of Rosh Hashanah. The question there is whether the two days of Rosh Hashanah function as a single continuous *kedushah* — one indivisible unit — or whether each day is independently uncertain, like a standard two-day Yom Tov of *sefeka deyoma*: each day is treated as though one does not know whether it is Yom Tov or a weekday.

The practical difference is significant. In a standard two-day Yom Tov, one can stipulate regarding a basket of *tevel* fruit as follows: on the first day, one declares that if today is Yom Tov, this designation has no effect, but if today is a weekday, one is hereby separating *trumos u'ma'asros*. On the second day, one repeats the process. Since one of the two days must actually be a weekday, the *ma'asros* will have been validly separated on whichever day was the weekday, and the fruit becomes permissible on the second day. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the two days of Rosh Hashanah may be treated this way — each day is independently uncertain, and this mechanism functions there just as it does on a normal two-day Yom Tov.

Rabbi Yehuda further states: *beitzah shenolda berishon, tochel besheini* — an egg laid on the first day of Rosh Hashanah may be eaten on the second day. The reasoning: if the second day is Yom Tov, then the first day was a weekday, and the egg was laid on a weekday and is perfectly permissible. If the second day is a weekday, then even if the egg was laid on Yom Tov, today is *chol* and the *muktzah* or other concern no longer applies. The Rabbis disagree, holding that Rosh Hashanah is a single continuous *kedushah* — *kedushah achat hi* — and the egg therefore remains forbidden on the second day as well.

The point for our purposes is that Rabbi Yehuda's discussion of the egg in the context of Rosh Hashanah confirms that he accepts, at least at some level, that there is an issue with an egg laid on Yom Tov. This is what triggers Rabbi Yochanan's question: Rabbi Yehuda allows the juice from a grapefruit yet acknowledges a problem with the egg — how are these consistent?

## Solutions to Contradicti…

### Ravina: Reverse Position

Rabbi Yochanan's own resolution is *muchlefet hashita* — reverse the positions. Originally, the understanding was that it is Rabbi Yehuda who permits the juice from the grapefruit while the Rabbis forbid all flowing liquids indiscriminately. Rabbi Yochanan proposes switching this: it is the Rabbis who make the distinction between fruit designated for eating versus fruit designated for squeezing, while Rabbi Yehuda forbids all naturally-expressed liquids without distinction. If Rabbi Yehuda's position is that all flowing liquids are forbidden regardless of the fruit's designation, then the egg — which is like a liquid that emerged from its source — would be forbidden consistently with his other rulings. This resolves the contradiction, even if it is not the most elegant of solutions.

The *gemara* adds that the very act of Rabbi Yochanan connecting these two statements and resolving them together confirms, *shema mina*, that he views both as resting on the same underlying principle — the *mashkin shezavu* framework.

### Ravina: Chicken for Eggs

Two alternative answers are then offered that avoid the need to reverse Rabbi Yehuda's position entirely.

The first is from Ravina: *le'olam lo teipoch* — there is no need to reverse anything. Rabbi Yehuda can consistently hold both that the juice of a grapefruit is permitted and that the egg is a problem — because in his statement about Rosh Hashanah, Rabbi Yehuda is not actually asserting that he himself believes the egg is forbidden. Rather, *Rabbi Yehuda lidvreihem deRabbanan ka'amar* — Rabbi Yehuda is speaking in terms of the Rabbis' position, not his own. His own view is that the egg presents no problem, just as the grapefruit juice presents no problem: *de'uchla de'aparsei hu* — it is simply food that derived from food. But he addresses the case of the egg on Rosh Hashanah as a vehicle for making a different point: to the Rabbis, who hold that the egg is forbidden, he says at minimum you must concede that on the second day of Rosh Hashanah it should be permitted, since on a standard two-day Yom Tov the second day's egg would be allowed. The Rabbis respond that Rosh Hashanah is different — *kedushah achat hi* — and the egg remains forbidden. So the egg case in the Rosh Hashanah debate is really a proxy argument about the nature of the two days, not a concession by Rabbi Yehuda that the egg is inherently problematic.

The second answer comes from Ravina son of Rav Ulla, who sidesteps the entire difficulty by reframing the case in the Rosh Hashanah discussion altogether: *hacha betarnegolet hamedet legadel beitzim* — there, the discussion concerns a chicken that was designated for laying eggs, not for eating. In such a case, Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that the egg is problematic poses no difficulty whatsoever, because a chicken set aside for egg-laying presents a straightforward *muktzah* issue that everyone agrees on — even Beit Shammai. *Rabbi Yehuda litaimei d'it lei muktzah* — and Rabbi Yehuda, who holds strongly of *muktzah* against Rabbi Shimon's more lenient position, would naturally rule that such an egg is forbidden.

This answer also clarifies the overall landscape of the four explanations. The three later explanations — Rabbah's *hachana*, Rav Yosef's *peiros hanoshrin*, and Rav Yitzchak's *mashkin shezavu* — all presuppose that the debate in the *mishnah* involves a chicken designated for eating, where a *muktzah* problem does not straightforwardly apply. In that case one needs a separate reason to prohibit the egg — hence the three alternative frameworks. But if the chicken is there for laying eggs, there is no need for any of these additional reasons: *muktzah* is sufficient, and there is no debate. The debate in the *mishnah* must therefore be about the more difficult case, where the chicken is designated for eating.

## Baraita on Eggs

### Baraita: Egg Prohibition

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* to test how the various explanations hold up under scrutiny. The *baraita* states: whether an egg was laid on Shabbat or on Yom Tov, *ein metaletlin otah* — one may not move it; it is effectively *muktzah*. One may not use it to cover a vessel — as one might plug up the top of a bottle with an egg — nor may one use it to support the legs of a bed. However, one may invert a vessel over the egg to prevent it from being broken. This raises an interesting sub-question: may one move a vessel for the sake of a *muktzah* object, to protect it? The *baraita* seems to permit this, in contrast to the position that *ein kli nital ela litzorech davar hanital* — a vessel may only be moved for the sake of something that is itself permitted to be moved.

The *baraita* then adds two further rulings: *usfeika asura* — a doubtful egg is likewise forbidden; and *v'im nitarva be'elef kullan assurot* — if the egg became mixed into a thousand other eggs, all of them are forbidden. The *gemara* now examines how each of these rulings fares under the different explanations of the egg's prohibition.

### Rabbah & Safek D'Oraita

The ruling about *sfeika* — doubt — fits naturally with Rabbah's position. Rabbah grounds the egg's prohibition in the biblical concept of *hachana*, and therefore any doubt about whether the egg was laid today or yesterday is a doubt in a *de'oraita* matter. *Vekol sfeika d'oraita lechumra* — all biblical doubts are resolved stringently. It should be noted that Rabbah's concern with *hachana* applies primarily when Yom Tov and Shabbat are juxtaposed, whereas the *baraita* is discussing a regular Yom Tov. Nevertheless, the *gemara*'s point is that Rabbah's framework involves a genuine biblical dimension, and it is therefore coherent that a case of doubt would be treated strictly.

### Rav Yosef/Yitzchak: Safe…

The difficulty arises for Rav Yosef and Rav Yitzchak, whose explanations are purely rabbinic in origin. For them, the egg's prohibition is a rabbinic decree — either by analogy to *peiros hanoshrin* or to *mashkin shezavu* — with no biblical basis. A doubt in a purely rabbinic matter is *sfeika d'rabbanan*, and *vekol sfeika d'rabbanan lekula* — all rabbinic doubts are resolved leniently. Why, then, does the *baraita* rule that a doubtful egg is forbidden?

## Shift to Treifah Case

### Gemara's Reinterpretation

To resolve this difficulty, the *gemara* proposes a forced but necessary reinterpretation: *seifa atan lesafek treifah*. The ruling about doubt in the *baraita* does not continue to discuss an egg whose status is uncertain with respect to Yom Tov. Rather, the *baraita* shifts mid-passage — without announcing the transition — to a different type of doubtful egg: one where the uncertainty concerns whether the chicken that laid it was a *treifah*. If the chicken itself was *treifah*, the egg it lays is likewise *treifah* and forbidden. A doubt about whether the chicken was a *treifah* is a doubt in a *de'oraita* matter, and such doubts are resolved stringently. Hence the ruling that the doubtful egg is forbidden.

This reading is admittedly strained. Shifting from the case of an egg laid on Yom Tov to the case of an egg from a possibly *treifah* chicken without any textual marker is not a natural reading of the *baraita*. But the *gemara* accepts this reinterpretation as the price of reconciling the *baraita*'s stringent ruling on doubt with the positions of Rav Yosef and Rav Yitzchak.

## Shift to Treifah Case…

### Davar Sheyesh Lo Matirin

With the *sfeika* ruling accounted for, the *gemara* now turns to the final clause: the egg mixed into a thousand. Here the analysis branches depending on which case the *baraita* is addressing.

If the *baraita* is discussing an egg that is forbidden because of Yom Tov — a purely rabbinic prohibition — then the reason it is not *batel* even in a thousand is the principle of *davar sheyesh lo matirin*: something that will become permissible at a later time. The egg forbidden on Yom Tov will be perfectly permissible tomorrow, when Yom Tov ends. Since the egg has a natural path to becoming permitted — simply by waiting — we do not allow *bittul* to serve as a workaround. *Vekol davar sheyesh lo matirin, afilu be'elef lo batel* — anything that has a permitting factor does not become *batel* even in a thousand. Rashi explains the logic: rather than eating something that is presently forbidden — even if *bittul* would technically permit it — it is better to simply wait and eat it when it is fully permitted without any workaround. *Ad she'tochlenu be'issur, tochlenu beheter* — rather than eating it under a dispensation, wait to eat it when it is entirely permitted.

This principle has notable relevance to *Pesach*: the stringency by which even the tiniest amount of *chametz* is forbidden is understood by some as an application of *davar sheyesh lo matirin* — since *chametz* will become permissible after *Pesach*, *bittul* cannot work to permit it during *Pesach*, no matter the ratio.

## Bittul and Eggs

### No Bittul via Matirin

So if the final clause of the *baraita* — the egg mixed into a thousand — is addressing the Yom Tov case, the explanation is clean: *davar sheyesh lo matirin* accounts for the absence of *bittul*, and the stringency follows naturally.

### Egg as Davar Shebeminyan

But the *gemara* has just proposed that the *sfeika* clause refers to a *treifah* egg. It now asks whether the final clause — the mixing into a thousand — also refers to the *treifah* case. If so, the *davar sheyesh lo matirin* rationale does not apply: a *treifah* egg has no path to becoming permissible, so that principle is irrelevant. Why, then, would the egg not become *batel*?

The *gemara* answers: *vechi teima beitzah chashuva velo batla* — one might say that an egg is a significant, discrete item and therefore does not become *batel* through mixture. The underlying principle is *davar shebeminyan*: something that is always counted individually, that is always treated as a distinct unit rather than as part of an undifferentiated mass, does not lose its identity in a mixture and therefore cannot undergo *bittul*.

The conceptual point is that *bittul* operates by causing an item to lose its individual identity — to be subsumed into the larger mass around it, like a single drop of non-kosher liquid dissolved into a vast quantity of kosher liquid. But items that are always perceived as discrete, counted units never acquire this quality of being subsumed. Each one is always its own entity, always treated individually, and therefore *bittul* cannot conceptually apply.

One might wonder: does this not apply to any physically discrete object, such as a potato? The answer is that physical discreteness is necessary but not sufficient. What matters is whether the item is *socially* treated as always individually counted and significant — whether, in other words, people always relate to it as an independent unit, never as part of a conglomerate.

### Mishna: Six/Seven Items

This raises the question of what the rule is. The *gemara* cites a *mishnah*: someone had bundles of *tiltan* — fenugreek — that were *kilei hakerem*, grown together with grapevines and therefore forbidden, requiring burning. If these became mixed with other bundles of fenugreek, and something from that mixture fell into yet another mixture, Rabbi Meir says *kulan yidlaku* — everything must be burned; *bittul* does not apply. The Rabbis say *ya'alu b'echad u'matayim* — 200-to-1 suffices to create *bittul*, as is the normal rule for *kilayim*.

Rabbi Meir grounds his ruling in the principle that *et she'darko limnot mekadesh* — items that are counted by number sanctify their mixture, meaning they do not undergo *bittul*. The Rabbis, rejecting the abstract rule, hold instead that only specific enumerated items are immune to *bittul*: *eino mekadesh ela shisha devarim bilvad* — only six things sanctify their mixture — or seven, according to Rabbi Akiva. These are: nuts from Phrach, pomegranates from Badan, sealed barrels of wine, bundles of spinach, bundles of cabbage, a Greek gourd, and — according to Rabbi Akiva — home-baked bread. These items are *chashuvim*, significant discrete units, and *bittul* does not apply to them. Rambam understands this list not as fixed for all time, but as contextually dependent: each era and locale must identify which items carry that quality of individual significance. But the underlying framework remains: not all counted items are equal — only those that are always treated as individual units of particular significance are immune from *bittul*.

### Et vs. Kol She'Darko

Within Rabbi Meir's position, there is a crucial textual debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. Rabbi Yochanan reads Rabbi Meir as saying *et she'darko limnot shaninu* — we are speaking only of items that are *exclusively* counted, never sold by weight or volume. Reish Lakish reads Rabbi Meir as saying *kol she'darko limnot shaninu* — anything that is *ever* counted, even if it is sometimes sold in bulk, is immune from *bittul*.

This debate directly bears on the egg. Eggs are sometimes sold by number — by the dozen — and sometimes bundled together in baskets without individual counting. Under Reish Lakish's reading of Rabbi Meir, the fact that eggs are sometimes counted suffices to render them immune from *bittul*, and the *baraita*'s ruling that the egg mixed into a thousand remains forbidden is explained. Under Rabbi Yochanan's reading, however, the egg is not exclusively counted, and therefore Rabbi Meir's rule would not apply to it — leaving open the question of why *bittul* does not work when the egg's prohibition is one of *treifah* rather than Yom Tov.

The *gemara* thus concludes this daf with an unresolved tension: according to Reish Lakish's reading of Rabbi Meir, the egg as *davar shebeminyan* explains the *baraita*'s stringency even in the *treifah* scenario. But according to Rabbi Yochanan's own reading of Rabbi Meir, the egg does not qualify as exclusively counted, and the explanation fails. The *gemara* will continue with this question on the following daf.
