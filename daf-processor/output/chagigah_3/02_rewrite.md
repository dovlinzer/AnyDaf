# Chagigah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Re'iyah & Two Eyes

### Tosafot: Korban vs. Mitz…

The opening discussion of our *daf* concerns the *cheresh* who is exempt from *re'iyah* — from being *oleh laregel*. Before proceeding, it is worth noting a fundamental distinction raised by the first Tosafot. Throughout the entire *masekhet*, *re'iyah* refers to the *korban re'iyah* — the *olat re'iyah*, the *korban* that is brought. Rashi, however, raises the question of whether the *mitzvah* to appear is distinct from actually bringing the *olat re'iyah*: is there a separate *mitzvah* to simply present oneself *bire'iyah ba'azarah*, to physically appear in the *azarah*, apart from the obligation to bring the *olat re'iyah*?

### Yerushalmi: Universal Ob…

Tosafot points to a Yerushalmi that addresses this very question. While only males — and only those enumerated in the *mishnah* — are obligated in the *olat re'iyah*, and all the exemptions listed there apply, the Yerushalmi maintains that the *mitzvah* to appear in the *azarah* — to literally be *oleh laregel* — applies to everyone, even women and children, derived from *hakhel*. This is a remarkable position: what the Torah presents as a once-in-seven-years obligation, the Yerushalmi understands as applying universally to all. Tosafot then goes on to demonstrate that this is clearly not the position of the Bavli. Still, it is important to note the distinction between focusing narrowly on the *korban* and thinking more broadly about the concept of being *oleh laregel*.

### Drasha: Two Eyes

A brief point from the previous *daf* also deserves mention. The *drasha* establishing that one must have sight in both eyes is grounded in the formulation *keshem sheba'im lirot kach ba'im lera'ot* — just as they come to see, so they come to be seen, as at a gathering where one is both seeing and being seen. The *drasha* draws on the imagery of God seeing with two eyes, which carries its own striking anthropomorphic dimension. But the crucial point arising from that *drasha* is the dual meaning of the verb it expounds.

### Pshat: Yira'eh et Pnei

One might assume from the *drasha* that the *pshat* of the *pasuk* is that one comes to be seen, and that the *drasha* adds the parallel sense of coming to see. But in fact the *pshat* of the *pasuk* runs in precisely the opposite direction. The verse reads *shalosh pe'amim bashanah yera'eh kol zechurecha et pnei*. The word *yera'eh* can be read as either "will be seen" or "will see," but the word *et* is decisive: *et* marks the object of the verb. To be seen *by* God would require *yera'eh lifnei*, not *yera'eh et*. Since there are no *nekudot* in the Torah scroll, the grammatically natural reading of *yira'eh et pnei* is "he will see God's face" — which is, of course, deeply anthropomorphic. It is precisely because of that anthropomorphism that we read it as *yera'eh*, the passive form. One might ask whether *et* could here mean "with" rather than the direct object marker — you will be seen together with God — and while that reading cannot be entirely dismissed, it remains difficult to argue that it represents *pshat*. What matters is that the *drasha* of the two eyes holds both meanings simultaneously, and it is important to appreciate that encountering and seeing God is in fact the simpler reading of the *pasuk*.

## Cheresh & Senses

### Medaber/Shome'a Dispute

The *gemara* now takes up the question of hearing. Reading from the two dots on amud bet: *medaber ve'eino shome'a, shome'a ve'eino medaber — chayav*. If one can speak but not hear, or hear but not speak, one is obligated. The *gemara* then raises a contradiction: a *baraita* states that *medaber ve'eino shome'a, shome'a ve'eino medaber — patur*. Even missing one of these two faculties produces an exemption. After establishing that the classic *cheresh* who is fully exempt from *mitzvot* is the one who can neither speak nor hear, the *gemara* had logically inferred that one who is only deaf or only mute must be *chayav*. Now the *gemara* plays out that inference against the *baraita*. The resolution offered by Ravina — or, some say, Rava — is *chisura mechsara vehachi katani*: the text is missing words and must be read with the following implicit content. The full teaching is: *hakol chayavin bire'iyah uvesimcha chutz micheresh hamedaber ve'eino shome'a, shome'a ve'eino medaber, shepatur min hare'iyah* — everyone is obligated in *simcha* and in bringing the *korban re'iyah*, except for a *cheresh* who is missing one of these two faculties, who is exempt from *re'iyah*. And yet — *ve'af al pi shepatur min hare'iyah, chayav besimcha* — even though such a person is exempt from *re'iyah*, they remain obligated in the *mitzvah* of bringing *shalmei simcha*.

### Cheresh Definition

Before proceeding, it is worth pausing on the category of *cheresh* itself. For a long time one might assume that the *cheresh* who is not a *bar chiyuva* — who is fully exempt from *mitzvot* — is someone both deaf and mute, and that such a combination is rare. In fact, for the *gemara*, nearly anyone who was deaf was a deaf-mute. The reason is that the ability to articulate speech in a comprehensible way depends on auditory feedback: without hearing, one cannot modulate one's speech to be intelligible. This was certainly the case for those born deaf in the ancient world, where no techniques existed for developing speech without hearing. The person who was not a deaf-mute in the Talmudic framework would be someone who had learned to speak normally, and only later lost their hearing. This makes the category of *cheresh* far broader than one might initially assume, and it is the background for a great deal of important *halakhic* development in later centuries — particularly when schools for the deaf demonstrated that deaf individuals possessed full intellectual capacity, could communicate through sign language, and had been systematically underestimated. That *halakhic* development is a story in itself. But for our purposes it is essential to recognize that the standard deaf person born deaf was, in the Talmudic framework, treated as a deaf-mute.

### Baraita: Exemptions

The *mishnah*'s interest is primarily to identify people who have a special exemption from *olat re'iyah* even though they are not generally exempt from *mitzvot*. The partial *cheresh* — one missing only one faculty — is a *bar chiyuva* in general; the exemption from *re'iyah* is a targeted exception rather than a sweeping exclusion. A different category is the full *cheresh* — one who can neither speak nor hear — who appears in the grouping of *cheresh shoteh vekatan*. That group represents those who are entirely exempt from all *mitzvot*: *patur mikol mitzvot ha'amurot batorah*. So while even a partial *cheresh* is exempt from *olat re'iyah* (which, as we shall see, carries many criteria), the *cheresh shoteh vekatan* of the *mishnah* refers to the fully exempt *cheresh* who is excused not only from *olat re'iyah* but from *shalmei simcha* and from all *mitzvot* altogether.

### Partial Cheresh in Simcha

The critical distinction is this: the partial *cheresh* — one who is missing only one of the two faculties — remains a *bar chiyuva* and is obligated in *simcha*. Their exemption is specifically and only from *re'iyah*. Whatever the halakhic basis for that targeted exemption turns out to be, it does not cascade into a general release from *mitzvot*. Such a person is obligated in the *mitzvah* of bringing *shalmei simcha*, because they are fundamentally subject to Torah obligation.

### Full Exemption Group

By contrast, the full *cheresh* who cannot hear and cannot speak — grouped together with *shoteh* and *katan* — is *patur af min hasimcha* and *patur mikol mitzvot ha'amurot batorah*. This is the complete exemption. The *mishnah* therefore encodes two different categories under the word *cheresh*: the full *cheresh* of the *cheresh shoteh vekatan* grouping, who is entirely exempt, and the partial *cheresh* who has a special and limited exemption from *re'iyah* alone.

## Hakhel Derivation

### Mai Shena Exemption

Having established that the partial *cheresh* — whether deaf but speaking, or speaking but deaf — is exempt from *re'iyah* while remaining obligated in *simcha* and all other *mitzvot*, the *gemara* now asks the obvious question: *mai shena le'inyan re'iyah deptiri* — what is different about *re'iyah* that produces this specific exemption? And correspondingly, *umai shena le'inyan simcha demichayvi bah* — why is *simcha* treated differently, such that these same individuals remain obligated?

### Gezerah Shavah

The answer is: *le'inyan re'iyah gamar re'iyah re'iyah mehakhel* — for the laws of *re'iyah* we derive by *gezerah shavah* from *hakhel*. This is, on the surface, a surprising source. *Hakhel* is an extraordinarily inclusive *mitzvah*: *ha'anashim vehanashim vehataf* — men, women, and children. We do not learn from *hakhel* to obligate women in *olat re'iyah*; yet we do learn this exemption from *hakhel*. The *gezerah shavah* connects *yera'eh kol zechurecha* with *bevo kol Yisrael lera'ot*. We then ask: how do we know from *hakhel* itself that a deaf or mute person is exempt? The answer comes from the language of the *pasuk* there: *lema'an yishme'u ulema'an yilmedu* — "so that they will hear" and "so that they will learn."

### Hearing Exclusion

The *baraita* teaches: *lema'an yishme'u — perat lemedaber ve'eino shome'a*. The requirement "so that they will hear" the reading of the Torah excludes someone who is deaf, even if they can speak, and even if they are otherwise a complete *bar chiyuva* who became deaf later in life. By *hakhel*, the essential experience is hearing the Torah read aloud, and one who cannot hear is therefore exempt.

### Teaching Exclusion

The exemption for someone who is mute but can hear is derived from *ulema'an yilmedu* — "so that they will learn" — *perat leshome'a ve'eino medaber*. But this is puzzling. One can readily understand why deafness would exempt someone from an experience centered on hearing Torah. But why would muteness prevent learning? The assumption that one who cannot speak cannot learn seems questionable — particularly since the category of *cheresh* who is fully exempt was specifically defined as one who can neither speak nor hear, precisely because intellectual capacity was associated with those faculties together. Surely a person who can hear but simply cannot produce speech retains their full capacity to absorb and understand?

### Two Mute Scholars

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *lemeimra dechi lo mishta'ei lo gamar* — do you mean to say that one who cannot speak cannot learn? And it brings a striking empirical refutation. There were two mute individuals living in the neighborhood of Rabbi — specifically identified as the grandsons or nephews of Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada. Whenever Rabbi would enter the *beit midrash*, these two mute scholars would enter and sit before him. They would nod their heads — indicating comprehension — and their lips would move, clearly expressing a desire to speak. Rabbi prayed for them, and they were healed. And when they could speak, it emerged that they had mastered *hilchata, Sifra, Sifrei* — the *midrashei halacha* — and the entire *shisha sedarim*. *Gemara* as a distinct corpus did not yet exist in Rabbi's time, but they knew the entirety of *Torah She-be'al Peh*. This is not merely a *theoretical* objection to the *baraita*'s apparent assumption — it is a concrete historical demonstration that muteness does not impair the capacity to learn.

### Rav Ashi Resolution

Mar Zutra initially responds that the verse must be read differently — the *pshat* of *lema'an yilmedu* does not actually exclude mute people from learning; rather, someone who is mute cannot *teach*. In an oral culture, without the development of sign language, the *gemara* understood that the fullest sense of *limud* — of teaching Torah over to others — was not achievable without speech. The exemption, then, is not premised on the incapacity to absorb knowledge, but on the incapacity to transmit it.

Rav Ashi, however, offers an internal proof that this must be the correct reading, without needing to appeal to the story of the mute scholars at all. If *lema'an yilmedu* were understood to exclude mute people on the grounds that they cannot learn, then the same reasoning would equally apply to deaf people — one who cannot hear presumably also cannot receive information and therefore cannot learn. But if that were so, the *pasuk* of *lema'an yishme'u* would be superfluous: both exemptions could have been derived from *lema'an yilmedu* alone. Since the Torah needed *lema'an yishme'u* specifically to exclude the deaf, it is clear that the Torah is not assuming an incapacity to learn in either case. *Lema'an yilmedu* must therefore be read as referring to the capacity to *teach*, not merely to learn. *Ela vadai lema'an yilmedu hu* — it must certainly be read as "so that they will teach." One who is mute cannot teach in the oral-transmission sense that the *hakhel* ceremony represents, and is therefore exempt. That exemption, via *gezerah shavah*, applies equally to *re'iyah*.

It is worth pausing to acknowledge the troubling character of these exclusions. Person after person — those blind in one eye, those who cannot hear, those who cannot speak — is being exempted from *olat re'iyah* on the basis of a physical faculty that is not present. Nobody is barring these individuals from entering the *beit hamikdash* or bringing *korbanot* of their own volition; these are exemptions from obligation, not prohibitions from participation. Still, exemption can carry the force of exclusion, and reading through these categories is genuinely challenging. One might note that even *hakhel* itself — which includes the *taf*, infants who can neither learn nor hear nor teach — suggests that mere presence carries its own significance. The *gemara* will return to this point when discussing why infants are brought to *hakhel* at all. The *olat re'iyah*, by contrast, is apparently understood as a *mitzvah* that requires full engagement of the relevant faculties, even though precisely what those faculties have to do with the experiential dimension of appearing before God in the *azarah* is not entirely clear.

## One-Sense Defects

### Deaf One Ear

Rabbi Tanchum now extends the principle: *cheresh b'ozen achat patur min hare'iyah* — someone deaf in one ear is exempt from *re'iyah*. Just as we have already exempted those blind in one eye, and just as through the *hakhel* comparison we have exempted the partially deaf and the mute, we now extend the principle even further: one who is deaf in only one ear is likewise exempt.

### Ba'ozneihem Both Ears

The source is the word *b'ozneihem* in the *hakhel* passage: the Torah is to be read *into their ears*. One might object that *b'ozneihem* refers collectively to the ears of all Israel gathered together — the assembled nation has many ears among them — and does not necessarily require that each individual possess two functioning ears. The *gemara* dismisses this: the requirement that each person be in a position to hear already emerges from the phrase *lema'an yishme'u*. If the verse had only said to read the Torah *neged kol Yisrael* — in front of all the people — one might have thought that physical presence, not auditory reception, was what mattered: the experiential sense of being there. *B'ozneihem* therefore adds something beyond what *lema'an yishme'u* already says. It cannot be teaching that individuals must personally be within earshot, because that is already derived from *lema'an yishme'u*. *B'ozneihem* must therefore be teaching that each individual requires both ears. Consequently, someone deaf in one ear is exempt from *hakhel*, and by extension — through the *gezerah shavah* — from *re'iyah* as well.

### Lame One Leg

Rabbi Tanchum adds a parallel ruling: *chiger b'regel achat patur min hare'iyah* — one who is lame in one leg is exempt from *re'iyah*. The pattern is clear: just as sight in both eyes, hearing in both ears, and the faculty of speech are each required, so too is full use of both legs.

### Regalim Etymology

The proof text is the word *regalim* itself. The festivals are called *shalosh regalim bashanah* — and while *regel* can mean an occasion or a time (as when Bilam's donkey says *ki hikisani zeh shalosh regalim*), the word very powerfully also means legs. It is not merely a play on words: the *regalim* are named for the journey, the walking, the act of traveling up to the *beit hamikdash* on foot. Therefore, the word *regalim* in the festival formula implies that one requires the full use of one's legs.

### Prosthetics & Limping

Here the *gemara* distinguishes between two different exclusions, each derived from a different word. The exclusion of someone who wears a prosthesis — *ba'alei kabin* — someone who has lost a foot or leg and uses a wooden substitute, comes not from *regalim* but from the word *pe'amim*. The *pasuk* reads both *shalosh regalim* and *shalosh pe'amim bashanah*. A *baraita* clarifies: *ein pe'amim ela regalim* — *pe'amim* means feet, specifically the soles or the feet themselves. The verse is quoted: *tirmesena regel raglei ani pa'amei dalim* — it will be trodden by a foot, the feet of the poor, the soles of the impoverished. And more directly: *ma yafu pa'amayich bine'alim bat nadiv* — how beautiful are your feet in sandals, daughter of the generous one. The feet are what are in the shoes. *Pa'amim*, then, specifically denotes feet. Someone with a prosthesis does not have *regalim* in this sense — not a foot — and is therefore excluded from the word *pe'amim*. Interestingly, this would suggest that even someone who has their leg intact but has lost the foot would fall into this category. Given that *pe'amim* already excludes prosthetic wearers, the word *regalim* must be doing additional work: it excludes someone who is not physically missing a leg but is simply lame in one leg. The presence of both words thus produces two distinct exclusions. Again, it must be emphasized: these are exemptions from obligation, not prohibitions on entry. Nevertheless, reading this accumulation of physical criteria one after another is genuinely sobering. The *beit hamikdash* context has its own aesthetic dimension — the expectation of wholeness and perfection that pervades the Temple service — but one is also aware that these exemptions carry a certain force of exclusion, however unintentional.

## Aggadic Teachings

### Beauty of Pilgrimage

The *gemara* now turns to *aggadeta*, and fittingly, the *drashot* here emerge directly from the very verses we have been analyzing. Rava expounds: what is meant by the verse *ma yafu pa'amayich bine'alim bat nadiv* — how beautiful are your feet in sandals, daughter of the generous one? *Kama na'im ragleihem shel Yisrael beshaa sheolin laregel* — how beautiful are the legs of Israel in the moment when they go up to the *regel*. The verse, which we have just been parsing for its halakhic implications regarding feet and prosthetics, is now read as an expression of the beauty of the pilgrimage itself: the sight of all of Israel ascending to Jerusalem for the *regel*.

### Bat Nadiv Drasha

Who, then, is *bat nadiv* — the daughter of the generous one? *Bito shel Avraham Avinu shenikra nadiv* — the daughter of Abraham our father, who was called *nadiv*, generous. The proof text is *nedivei amim ne'esafu, am Elohei Avraham* — the generous ones of the nations gathered, the nation of the God of Abraham. One might ask: in the verse, it is the *amim* — the nations — who are the *nedivim*, not Abraham himself. The *drasha* reads them in juxtaposition: they gather as *am Elohei Avraham*, as the nation of Abraham's God, and it is Abraham who is the paradigmatic *nadiv*. But why *Elohei Avraham* only, and not *Elohei Yitzchak veYa'akov*? *Ella Elohei Avraham shehaya techila lagerim* — because Abraham was the first of converts, the first to step forward in response to the call of God. Rashi explains *nedivei amim* as referring to *gerim* — those who give of themselves from among the nations to accept the *ol mitzvot*. Abraham is the model for that movement: not merely in the technical sense that Tosafot points to — *techila lagerim shenitchayev al hamila*, the first to be obligated in *milah* — but in the deeper sense that Rashi suggests: Abraham traversed the world calling people to God, *vayikra b'shem Hashem*, and drew followers in every place he traveled. There is a beautiful parallel here between *nedivei amim ne'esafu* — the generous ones of the nations gathering — and the event of the *regel* itself, when people come from all around to gather at the mountain of God. The pilgrimage, with its vast convergence of people, resonates with the image of Abraham drawing humanity toward recognition of God.

### Pki'in Story

The *gemara* now presents a *baraita*: it happened that Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka and Rabbi Elazar Chasma — Rabbi Elazar ben Chasma — went to visit Rabbi Yehoshua in Pki'in. The occasion is presumably *yom tov*, when the practice of *hakbalat pnei rabbo* — visiting one's teacher — was observed. Rabbi Yehoshua asked them: *ma chiddush haya b'veit hamidrash hayom* — what new idea came up in the *beit midrash* today? They demurred: *talmidecha anu, mimeimecha anu shotim* — we are your students; we drink from your waters. We have nothing to tell you that you would not already know. He pressed them: *af al pi chen, iy efshar l'veit hamidrash belo chiddush* — even so, it is impossible that nothing new arose in the study hall. I too can learn; tell me what came up. Whose week was it? It had been the week of Rabbi Eleazar ben Azarya — his turn to lead the *beit midrash* in the rotation. And what was the *hagada* delivered? This phrasing is itself notable: it implies that each session would include both *halakhic* study and a teaching of *hagada*, an *aggadic* or conceptual component — a model that perhaps some later academies might have taken to heart. It was in *parshat hakhel* that they had been learning.

### Hakhel Teaching

What, then, had Rabbi Eleazar ben Azarya taught about *hakhel*? *Hakhel et ha'am ha'anashim vehanashim vehataf* — assemble the people: the men, the women, and the children. *Im anashim ba'im lilmod* — men come to learn; that is self-evident. *Nashim ba'ot lishmo'a* — women come to hear. The *gemara*'s assumption is not that women lacked intelligence, but that they lacked the depth of formal education that would allow them to fully comprehend the legal and analytical content being transmitted. The differentiation is one of educational background. But then: *taf lama ba'im?* — why are infants brought? They cannot learn; they cannot even understand what is being said; it is, from a content-absorption standpoint, entirely beyond them. *Kedei litein sachar limvi'eihem* — in order to give reward to those who bring them. This might seem to beg the question — if the infants serve no purpose, why is there reward for bringing them? — but the answer contains its own depth. Bringing one's children to a transformative communal religious experience has an impact on the parents themselves, reinforcing the importance of raising children within these moments. And one cannot entirely discount even the young child's exposure to a powerful communal event: even if no cognitive content is absorbed, the impressionistic experience may have some formative effect. Rabbi Yehoshua's reaction upon hearing this: *margalit tovah hayetah beyadchem uvik'ashtem l'abdem mimeni* — you had a beautiful pearl in your hands and you sought to lose it from me, to deprive me of it. Why did I have to pull it out of you?

A further point arises from Tosafot's comment on *nashim ba'ot lishmo'a*. Tosafot cites the Yerushalmi, which notes that this does not accord with the position of Ben Azzai, who holds that a person is obligated to teach his daughter Torah — *chayav adam lelamed livto Torah*. Under that view, women would be educated and would come not merely to hear but to learn. This is the second Yerushalmi on our *daf* that takes an explicitly more inclusive position: the first held that women must be *oleh laregel* even without the obligation of *korban re'iyah*, and now a further position holds that women are obligated in Torah study. Tosafot also notes, in connection with *kedei litein sachar limvi'eihem*, that it is on this basis that the practice developed of bringing young children to *beit knesset* — the synagogue. This speaks to a genuine tension: does the presence of young children disrupt the *davening* of others, or does the importance of bringing them — for the children, and for the parents, and for the community — outweigh that disruption?

One further observation on the story: a compelling reading suggests that Rabbi Yehoshua may have been too infirm to attend the *beit midrash* himself that day, which would give the narrative its full poignancy. The students feel unable to tell him what transpired precisely because they are embarrassed that the *beit midrash* went on without him; they say *talmidecha anu, mimeimecha anu shotim* as a way of acknowledging that he is their source. And his response — *iy efshar l'veit hamidrash belo chiddush* — carries an implicit message: the *beit midrash* generates new ideas even beyond what any individual teacher already knows, and that is beautiful, not threatening.

### Reciprocal Choosing

Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya had a further *drasha* that day: *et Hashem he'emarta hayom* — you have chosen, or elevated, God today — *v'Hashem he'emircha hayom* — and God has chosen you. Rashi reads *he'emarta* as elevation and selection. The *gemara* explains: God said to Israel, *atem asituni chativa achat ba'olam* — you have made me one unit, one singular object, in the world — *va'ani e'eseh etchem chativa achat ba'olam* — and I will make you one singular unit in the world. Israel makes God one: *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem echad*. God makes Israel one: *umi k'amcha Yisrael goy echad ba'aretz*. Each declares the uniqueness of the other; and implicit within that declaration of uniqueness is the declaration of relationship, of chosenness — *Hashem Elokeinu*, our God, and *umi k'amcha Yisrael*, who is like Your nation Israel.

Tosafot adds a further beautiful element: three things testify to one another — *Yisrael, Shabbat*, and *Hakadosh Baruch Hu*. Israel and God together testify to *Shabbat* as a day of rest; Israel and *Shabbat* together testify to God's oneness; and God and *Shabbat* together testify to Israel's singular status among the nations, *she'hem yechidim ba'umot*. It is on this basis, Tosafot notes, that we conclude *Ata Echad* in the *Mincha* of *Shabbat*. Even though *Mincha* of *Shabbat* does not open immediately with the theme of *Shabbat* as *Maariv* and *Shachrit* do, the theme of *echad* — of oneness — connects Israel, God, and *Shabbat* in precisely the way the *drasha* has articulated.

In closing his response to the two visiting students, Rabbi Yehoshua said: *ein dor yatom she-Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah sharui b'tocho* — no generation is an orphan that has Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya within it. A generation led by such a teacher cannot be called bereft.

## Torah Diversity

### Goads & Nails

Now Rabbi Yehoshua himself opens with a *drasha*: *divrei chachamim kadorbanot uchmasmerot netuim ba'alei asufot nitnu me-ro'eh echad* — the words of the sages are like prods and like planted nails, given from the masters of collections, all given from one shepherd. Why are words of Torah compared to a prodding stick, a *dorban*? *Mah dorban zeh mechavein et haparah litalmeha lehotzi chayim la'olam* — just as a prod directs the cow along its furrow to bring forth life from the earth, so the words of Torah direct those who study them from the paths of death toward the paths of life. The image is precise: the *dorban* keeps the cow on a straight line to make a clean furrow; plowing that furrow is what brings forth the produce, the growth, the life from the ground. Torah constrains and directs, keeps one on a narrow path — but that narrow path is precisely what brings forth true life.

But a prod is also movable, not fixed. Is Torah similarly portable, shifting? *Talmud lomar masmerot* — the verse also says nails, something fixed and planted. Perhaps Torah is like a nail: stable, immovable. Yet a nail also only diminishes over time — it rusts, corrodes, never grows. Surely Torah is not merely static?

### Parah V'Rabah

*Talmud lomar netuim* — planted. A planting is not fixed in the rigid sense of a nail; it is *parah ve-rabah*, fruitful and multiplying. *Af divrei Torah parim ve-rabim* — the words of Torah are also fruitful and multiply. Torah is planted, takes root, and from that rootedness spreads forth, producing fruit, drawing others in, multiplying through study and transmission. This also resonates with the image of staying fixed in one place: if one is rooted in a community of Torah, it bears fruit outward and brings others to engage with it.

### Conflicting Opinions

*Ba'alei asufot* — the masters of collections, or assemblies. These are Torah sages who gather together in groups and groups, *yoshvin asufot asufot*, and engage in Torah. And here the beautiful complexity emerges: *hallalu metamein ve-hallalu metaherin* — these declare *tamei*, those declare *tahor*. *Hallalu osrin ve-hallalu mattirim* — these prohibit, those permit. *Hallalu poslin ve-hallalu machshirin* — these invalidate, those validate. The *beit midrash* generates a profusion of competing voices, competing rulings. This is, in one sense, exactly what makes Torah alive and fructifying — the *parah ve-rabah*, the fruitfulness, expressed through the energy of *machloket*. But it also raises an anxiety: *shema yomar adam, heicha ani lomed Torah me'atah* — how is a person supposed to navigate? With all these different voices, how does one find the path?

### One Shepherd Unity

*Talmud lomar kullam nitnu me-ro'eh echad* — all of them were given from one shepherd. *El echad netanan, parnas echad amran, mi-pi adon kol ha-ma'asim baruch hu* — one God gave them, one sustainer spoke them, from the mouth of the Master of all Creation. The verse is: *va-yedabber Elohim et kol ha-devarim ha-eileh* — God spoke all these things. All the different voices, all the competing opinions, all the tensions and arguments and rulings — they all ultimately flow from the one source. The multiplicity does not undermine the unity; rather, the unity undergirds and holds the multiplicity.

### Discerning Heart

*Af attah aseh oznecha ke-afarkeset* — make your ear like a funnel. Take it all in. *Ukhnei lecha lev mevin lishmoa et divrei metameim ve-et divrei metaherin, et divrei osrim ve-et divrei mattirim, et divrei poslin ve-et divrei machshirin* — acquire for yourself a discerning heart, to hear the words of those who declare *tamei* and those who declare *tahor*, those who prohibit and those who permit, those who invalidate and those who validate. The goal is not merely to absorb passively but to develop genuine analytic judgment — to hear all the voices, hold them in tension, and discern one's way through them. This is at once the responsibility and the beauty of Torah: the narrow furrow of the prod and the fruitful multiplicity of the planting are not contradictions. One walks a defined path, but that path requires a living, discerning mind to navigate.

The *aggadeta* here sits in elegant tension with the opening of the story: Rabbi Yehoshua told his visitors that *iy efshar l'veit hamidrash belo chiddush* — the *beit midrash* is not possible without new ideas. He wanted to hear a voice that was not his own. His *drasha* now articulates why: because all the different voices together are what makes Torah fruitful and alive, and they all come from one shepherd. He embraces the multiplicity rather than demanding that all knowledge be channeled through a single transmitted tradition.

## Rabbi Eliezer & Mesora

### Acknowledgment Before Te…

The *gemara* now asks: given the exchange we just read — the visiting students reluctantly acknowledging to Rabbi Yehoshua that they had something to tell him only after he pressed them — why did they need to go through that dance at all? Why didn't they simply tell him directly, *be-hedya*, what had transpired in the *beit midrash*? The answer: *mishum ma'aseh shehaya* — because of a prior episode in which someone went directly, without any such deference, and the outcome was not good. The lesson that one should first acknowledge the rabbi as the source of one's Torah, before simply presenting a teaching, is derived from that episode.

### Durmaski's Error

A *baraita* teaches: it happened that Rabbi Yose ben Durmaski went to receive Rabbi Eliezer in Lod — and this visit, like the one to Rabbi Yehoshua in Pki'in, may well have been on *yom tov*, in the context of *hakbalat pnei rabbo*. Rabbi Eliezer asked him: *ma chiddush haya b'veit hamidrash hayom?* — what new idea came up in the *beit midrash* today? And Rabbi Yose ben Durmaski simply answered. He did not go through the dance. He did not say: we are your students, we drink from your waters. He told Rabbi Eliezer directly what the *beit midrash* had concluded.

### Eyes Fall Out

Rabbi Eliezer's reaction was explosive: *pashat yadav vekibel einav* — he stretched out his hands and his eyes fell out. The physical drama here is striking, and the *gemara* records that Rabbi Eliezer wept. He cited the verse: *sod Hashem liyereiav uvrito lehodiam* — God's secret belongs to those who fear Him, and His covenant, to inform them.

### Ammon & Moav

What had Rabbi Yose ben Durmaski reported? *Nimnu vegamru* — they took a vote and concluded — *Ammon uMoav ma'asrin ma'aser ani beshvi'it*: that Ammon and Moav, the Transjordanian lands settled by Jews, give *ma'aser ani* — the tithe for the poor — in the seventh year, rather than *ma'aser sheni*. These lands do not practice *shemita* — they continue to plant and cultivate during the sabbatical year — and in place of the second tithe, which is set aside and eaten in Jerusalem, they give the poor tithe. Rabbi Eliezer told Rabbi Yose ben Durmaski to go back and tell the *beit midrash*: *al techoshu laminyanechem* — do not be anxious about your vote. And Rashi explains this as "don't worry, you got it right" — but Rabbi Eliezer's deeper point seems to be far more radical: who needed your vote in the first place? *Kach mekublani me-Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai, sheshama me-rabbo ve-rabbo me-rabbo* — I received this as a tradition from Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai, who heard it from his teacher, and his teacher from his teacher, going all the way back — *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — to a teaching transmitted from Moshe at Sinai. Exactly what you concluded by vote, I already possessed as received tradition. The use of the phrase *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* here is loose — the relevant *halakhot* regarding Ammon and Moav are clearly rabbinic — and this is noted by the *Rosh*, who points out that the phrase is being used metaphorically, meaning *davar barur kehalacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, something as certain as a law from Sinai.

### Secret Knowledge

The verse *sod Hashem liyereiav* — God's secret belongs to those who fear Him — is the key to understanding Rabbi Eliezer's reaction. He was in possession of this tradition. He knew. And it was this knowledge, this *mesora* transmitted through a chain of teachers all the way back to Moshe, that he regarded as authoritative. Not a vote. Not a *chiddush*. Not a process of deliberation and majority ruling. The *mesora* simply was, and those who feared God and received it faithfully were its custodians. What infuriated him, it seems, was not that the conclusion was wrong — it was identical to his tradition — but that the *beit midrash* had treated this as something to be decided through debate and vote, as though it were an open question amenable to *chiddush*. It was not a *chiddush*. It was already known.

### Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

The contrast between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua is now fully visible. Rabbi Yehoshua says: *iy efshar l'veit hamidrash belo chiddush* — it is impossible for the *beit midrash* to exist without something new. He celebrates the multiplicity of voices, the energy of differing opinions, the beauty of Torah's *parah ve-rabah*. He tells us to make our ears like a funnel and acquire a discerning heart to navigate the competing rulings. For Rabbi Yehoshua, Torah lives in the *machloket*, in the vote, in the process. Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, holds that Torah lives in the *mesora*. What you have received from your teacher, your teacher from his teacher, and so on back to Sinai — that is Torah. *Chiddush* is not Torah's vitality; it is a potential distortion of it. He is the one who, famously, could not accept a majority vote that overrode his tradition, and this episode illuminates precisely why. The *beit midrash* reached the right answer, but it reached it by the wrong method. There is a certain poignancy in the contrast: Rabbi Yehoshua would have been perfectly happy for the students to simply tell him what came up, multiple voices and all; Rabbi Eliezer, the one who most needed to be acknowledged as the transmitter of *mesora*, was the one who received the teaching without that acknowledgment. It is always the way: the adjustment one makes after an error tends to be applied to cases where it was not needed, and missed in the cases where it was.
