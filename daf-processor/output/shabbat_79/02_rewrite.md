# Shabbat 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shiur Framework

### Opening Review

Today's daf is 79b. We pick up at the bottom of 79b, where the discussion concerns the *shiur* — the minimum size or significance threshold — required for various objects when carrying on *Shabbat*. The broader framework asks two related questions: when does an object possess sufficient practical value to render one liable for carrying it into the public domain, and when has an object so thoroughly exhausted its purpose that it no longer counts as significant? We had previously discussed a receipt for having paid taxes; now the Gemara turns to a *shtar chov* — a writ of debt — that has already been paid and therefore cannot be used to collect. What is its *shiur*?

### General Principle

Before examining the specific cases, it is worth noting the underlying *klal*: the Mishnah in the previous chapter established that for an object to trigger liability, it must first be a *davar shematznien* — something that people consider worth setting aside or preserving — and second, it must meet a minimum threshold of significance. In the cases that follow, the question is not always one of physical size, but sometimes whether the object retains any function at all.

## Shtar Chov

### Mishnah Text

The *braita* states: *hamotzi shtar chov ad shelo pira'o* — if one carries out a writ of debt before it has been paid, he is *chayav*, liable, because the document clearly has significance as an instrument of collection. *Mishe-pira'o patur* — after it has been paid, he is exempt, because it can no longer be used to collect. *Rabbi Yehuda omer af mishe-pira'o chayav mipnei she-tzarich lo* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that even after payment one remains liable, because the person still has a use for the document.

A preliminary observation: one might ask why a paid *shtar* could not serve as a bottle stopper, in which case even the Rabbanan might grant it some residual significance. The Gemara will address the underlying dispute rather than this point, so we leave it for now.

### Rav Yosef Explanation

*Amar Rav Yosef* — Rav Yosef proposes that the dispute hinges on whether there is a prohibition to retain a paid *shtar*: *assur lish-ho'os shtar parua ita beinaihu*. The concern driving this prohibition is straightforward: the *shtar* contains no indication on its face that the debt has been discharged. There is no notation saying *parua* — paid. Accordingly, the lender might produce it again and attempt a second collection, or the borrower might lose it and it could find its way back to the lender. For this reason, there may be a general obligation to destroy a paid *shtar*.

*Rabbanan savri assur lish-ho'os shtar parua* — the Rabbanan hold that retention is forbidden. If it must be destroyed, it has no permissible use — not even as a bottle stopper — because one is obligated to eliminate it. *Rabbi Yehuda savar mutar lish-ho'os shtar parua* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that one may retain it, and therefore it retains at least some incidental utility: it could serve as a piece of paper, a stopper, a handkerchief. When Rabbi Yehuda says *tzarich lo* — "he needs it" — he does not mean the document functions as a *shtar* in its legal capacity, but simply that one may keep it and put it to some minor use.

### Abaye Alternative

*Abaye amar* — Abaye offers a different framework. He holds that *kulei alma assur lish-ho'os shtar parua* — everyone agrees that retaining a paid *shtar* is forbidden. There is no permissible use for it as a bottle stopper or otherwise; it must be destroyed. So what could Rabbi Yehuda possibly mean when he says it retains value?

*V'hacha be-modeh bi-shtar she-k'tavo tzarich l'kaymo kamipalgi* — the dispute concerns a case in which the debtor admits that the document is genuine. Does the creditor still need to authenticate the signatures — *kiyum shtar* — in order to collect?

The scenario works as follows. The creditor presents his *shtar* against the debtor — let us say for a substantial sum — bearing the signatures of two witnesses. To use this *shtar* in *beis din*, the signatures ordinarily must be authenticated: either the witnesses themselves must attest to their signatures, or others who recognize the handwriting must do so. This process is called *kiyum shtar*. Now suppose the debtor does not deny the debt but instead says: "Yes, I borrowed the money — but I have already paid it back; I simply never retrieved the *shtar*." Does the creditor still need to authenticate the signatures?

One argument for requiring authentication even after such an admission is that without it the *shtar* remains technically unverified. The counterargument is that by admitting the debt, the debtor has in effect validated the document: *modeh bi-shtar she-k'tavo* — his admission makes authentication unnecessary. And once authentication is unnecessary, the creditor has a fully operative *shtar* — while the debtor's unsupported claim of repayment is not believed against a document. The debtor has, in a sense, undermined his own position.

Of course, the obvious rejoinder is: by the same logic that you believed his admission of the debt, you should also believe his claim of repayment. This is the principle of *ha-peh she-asar hu ha-peh she-hitir* — the same mouth that created the problem should be trusted to resolve it — or more technically, an argument from *migo*: since he could have denied everything and escaped liability, his admission on one point grants credibility to his claims on others.

### Unauth. Writ Scenario

The debate thus plays out as follows. The *tanna kamma* holds *modeh bi-shtar she-k'tavo tzarich l'kaymo* — even after the debtor admits the debt, authentication is still required. Accordingly, when he says the debt is paid, and I cannot authenticate the signatures — which is the crucial contextual assumption Tosafot makes explicit — the *shtar* is worthless. I cannot collect with it and I cannot authenticate it. It has no value; I am not *chayav* for carrying it.

Rabbi Yehuda holds *modeh bi-shtar she-k'tavo eino tzarich l'kaymo* — once the debtor admits the debt, no authentication is required. The *shtar* is thereby operative against him, and his claim of repayment is not believed without proof. So the *shtar* retains value.

What then do the phrases "before it was paid" and "after it was paid" mean in this reading? The Gemara clarifies: *ad sheyomar loveh parati v'lo parati* — it does not mean the debt was literally paid or unpaid, but rather whether the debtor has *claimed* repayment. Before any such claim, the *shtar* has value and one is liable. After the debtor claims repayment, that is where the dispute lies.

Tosafot raises an obvious difficulty: if in the end everything depends on whether the debtor admits or denies — since the *shtar* cannot be authenticated in the given scenario — what independent value does the document have before any claim is made? The answer Tosafot gives is that the *shtar* may serve as a reminder that jogs the debtor's memory: upon seeing the document he might recall the debt and acknowledge it. One could add — though Tosafot does not — that a debtor may be more embarrassed to lie when faced with a physical document than when there is none at all, and that social pressure could move him to admit.

Tosafot offers a second function: if the debt is eventually admitted, the existence of a *shtar* means the creditor can collect from *shibudim* — liens on property the debtor has already sold — whereas without a *shtar* he could only collect from assets currently in the debtor's possession. The *shtar* thus retains real legal utility even when it cannot be independently enforced.

### Rava Receipt Debate

*Rava amar* — Rava offers yet a third reading. He holds *d'chulei alma modeh bi-shtar she-k'tavo tzarich l'kaymo* — everyone agrees that even after the debtor admits the debt, authentication is required. But in Rava's scenario, unlike Abaye's, authentication is not at issue because the *shtar* has already been authenticated. The debt has been paid. So what is the dispute about?

*V'hacha b'kotvin shovar* — the question is whether the creditor writes a receipt, a *shovar*, for the payment. Here is the practical tension: the debtor paid a month ago. Now the creditor appears with the *shtar*. The creditor is willing to write a receipt. The debtor refuses; he wants the *shtar* returned and destroyed. His concern is that if he accepts a receipt, he might lose it, and the creditor will still possess the *shtar* against him. He would rather have no *shtar* and no receipt than have the creditor holding a document against him while he holds a receipt he may lose.

Is the creditor entitled to write a receipt and retain the *shtar* for his own documentation? Or must he return the *shtar* to the debtor?

*Tanna kamma savar kosvin shovar* — the first position holds that one may write a receipt. Under this view: once a receipt has been written, does the *shtar* retain any value? Rashi notes that the debtor has the receipt, and the creditor has no legitimate use for the *shtar* — and in fact should not be holding it, given the danger of misuse. So once a receipt exists, the *shtar* is effectively worthless; one is *patur* for carrying it.

*Rabbi Yehuda savar ein kosvin shovar* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that one does not write a receipt. Under this view, the creditor retains the *shtar* but has an obligation to return it to the debtor. The *shtar* accordingly has significance — it is an object with respect to which the creditor bears a legal responsibility — and one is *chayav* for carrying it out. That at least is how Rashi and Tosafot read the position, though there is some question of the precise textual reading.

### Value w/o Receipt

The analysis reveals that the value of a paid *shtar* when no receipt has been written is not its capacity to serve as a legal instrument, but the legal relationship it creates between the parties. The creditor has a duty to deliver it to the debtor; the debtor has an interest in recovering it. This responsibility and interest suffice to give the document significance.

### Rav Ashi Value

*Rav Ashi amar* — Rav Ashi proposes an additional rationale for why a paid *shtar* retains value: *mishum detzarich leharoto lebaal chov sheini de-amar lei chazi degavra depara ana* — because one needs to show it to a second creditor, to demonstrate: "Look, I am a person who pays his debts." Just as one might hold on to tax receipts as evidence of one's financial reliability, a debtor may wish to retain paid *shtaros* as proof of a clean credit history. This is an independent ground for the document's significance. Whether the opposing views would concede this point, or would still override it with concerns about the danger of the *shtar* being misused, is left open.

In summary, the dispute about a paid *shtar* is not primarily one of physical size, but of whether the object retains any function at all — which brings us back squarely to the foundational *klal* that an object must have genuine practical value to be considered significant for purposes of *Shabbat* liability.

## Leather Shiur

### Rava's Question

*Ba'ei minei Rava me-Rav Nachman* — Rava asked Rav Nachman: *hamotzi or bekama* — if one carries out leather, how much must he carry to be liable? This opens what will become a sustained discussion of how the *shiur* of a material in an early stage of processing is determined.

### Nachman's Answer

*Amar lei kedetnan* — Rav Nachman replied that the Mishnah already tells us: *or kedei la-asot kamia* — the *shiur* for leather is the amount needed to make a pouch for an amulet, a *kamia*. A small leather pouch, used to hold a written amulet or medicinal herbs, is the standard function by which leather's minimum significance is measured.

### Untanned Leather

*Hane'avdo bekama* — Rava then asked: what about tanned leather — since *me'abed or*, tanning leather, is itself a *melachah* on *Shabbat*, how much must one tan to be liable? *Amar lei lo shna* — same amount; the *shiur* is the same as for finished leather, namely the amount to make an amulet pouch.

Then: *le'avdo bekama* — how about leather that has not yet been tanned, where the person intends to tan it but it is not yet usable for making pouches in its current state? Again, *amar lei lo shna* — no difference. Since once tanned it will be usable for this purpose, the untanned leather of that size is already considered significant, even though it cannot yet be put to that use.

### No Tan Plans

*V'shelo le-avdo bekama* — what if the untanned leather is not even intended to be tanned? *Amar lei lo shna* — still the same. This is admittedly somewhat less explained: if there are no plans to tan it and it has no immediate use, why should it be measured by the eventual tanned use? The answer appears to be a kind of default presumption: absent any specific category into which to place it, we place it in the category of material that will presumably be tanned and used accordingly.

### Thread Processing Proof

*U'mina teiman* — where does one derive this principle? The Gemara cites a Mishnah: *hamalben ve-hamanapetz ve-hatzoveia ve-hatoveh* — one who whitens wool, combs it, dyes it, or spins it — the *shiur* for each of these is *k'malo rochav hasit kaful*, double the width of a *sit*, which is the span between the middle finger and the forefinger. The *shiur* for weaving — *ha-oreg* — is one such span, *k'malo rochav hasit*. (The weaving requires only one *sit* rather than double because one weaves with two threads, and two threads together constitute the equivalent of the double span.)

The significance of this Mishnah is as follows: the early stages of processing thread — whitening, combing, dyeing, spinning — are each measured by the amount that, once processed through all the stages, will be sufficient to weave. In other words, *keivan d'litviya kaei* — since eventually it will be spun, *shiyurei k'tviya* — its *shiur* is determined by the stage of spinning, i.e., by the amount that will be usable once the processing is complete. The early-stage material has the same minimum significance as the finished product, because what determines significance is the eventual use.

*Hachi nami keivan d'le-avdo kaei shiyura k'me-ubad* — the same applies to leather: since the plan is to tan it, its *shiur* is as if it were already tanned, namely the amount to make an amulet pouch.

### Dye Challenge

*Eitvei* — a challenge is raised. The Mishnah teaches regarding *samanim* — plants used for dye extraction — that if they have already been steeped in water and the dye has been extracted, the *shiur* is *k'dei litzboa bahen dugma l'imra*: enough dye to color a small sample such as one would compare to a loom shuttle. But *uv'samanim she-einam shruyin* — if the plants have not yet been steeped and no dye has been extracted, the Mishnah states that the *shiur* for things like shells of nuts, shells of pomegranates, *istis*, and *puah* — various plants used in dyeing — is *k'dei litzboa bahen beged katan l'fi shvacha*: enough raw material from which one could extract sufficient dye to color a small garment, specifically the opening of a hairnet. This is clearly a larger amount than the *shiur* for already-extracted dye.

So here we have a counterexample: the *shiur* for the material *before* processing is *larger* than the *shiur* for the material *after* processing. This appears to contradict the principle that early-stage materials are judged by their later-stage significance.

### Bothering Standard

*Itmar alah* — it was said about this: *amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha* — the explanation is that *l'fi she-ein adam tore-ach lishros samanim litzboa bahen dugma l'imra* — a person does not bother going through the entire process of steeping dye plants just to produce enough dye to color a tiny sample. Nobody makes dye in industrial quantities for trivial purposes.

This answer clarifies a crucial conceptual distinction. There are two separate reasons why an early-stage material might fail to be considered significant:

The first is that the material is not yet at the stage where it can be put to its eventual use — but this alone does not undermine its significance. As Rav Nachman demonstrated with leather and as the Mishnah demonstrated with thread, *keivan d'le-avdo kaei* — since the processing will eventually take place, the pre-processed material retains the same significance as the finished product.

The second reason is more fundamental: if the quantity is so small that a person would not even bother processing it at all, then the material never reaches significance in the first place. The issue is not that it has not yet been processed; it is that it *will not* be processed, because no one processes such a negligible quantity. In that case, even the eventual use does not confer current significance, because that eventual use will never materialize for this amount.

Applied to the dye case: a tiny quantity of dye plants — enough to produce only a tiny sample's worth of dye — would simply not be worth steeping. A person who wanted to produce that sample's worth of dye would not bother with the full process. Therefore, only a larger quantity of raw plants — enough to make it worth the effort — has significance at the pre-processing stage. Once the dye is already extracted, even a tiny amount is significant because it is immediately usable. So the two *shiurim* are measuring different things.

### Seeds Example

The same analysis applies to seeds for a garden. *Zironi gina d'mikamei d'zar'inhu* — before seeds are planted, the Mishnah states the *shiur* is *pachos mik'grogueres*, less than the size of a pressed fig; Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira specifies five seeds, such as cucumber seeds. *V'ilu basar d'zar'inhu* — after the seeds are planted in the ground, the Mishnah says that the *shiur* for manure or fine sand used as fertilizer is *k'dei l'zavel bo kelach shel kruv* — enough to fertilize one head of cabbage (Rabbi Akiva), or enough to fertilize a single leek (the Sages). So after planting, even the amount to benefit a single plant is significant; before planting, five seeds are required.

Why does one not count even a single unplanted seed as significant? *Amar Rav Papa l'fi she-ein adam tore-ach le-hotzi nima achat li-zria* — nobody bothers taking out just one seed to plant it. Planting is done at scale; one does not make a special trip to plant a single seed. Therefore a single unplanted seed is not considered significant. It is not that the seed loses significance by virtue of being unplanted — it is that the quantity is below the threshold anyone would bother with. Five seeds, by contrast, is a quantity one might plant.

### Plaster Example

*Amar Rav Papa ve-harei tit* — Rav Papa raises the same question regarding plaster. A *braita* teaches that *modim chachamim le-Rabbi Shimon be-motzi shofchin li-reshut ha-rabim she-shi-uran bi-revi-it* — even dirty water, when taken out into the public domain, has a *shiur* of a *revi-it* — a quarter of a *log*. The Gemara asks: what would one use dirty water for? The answer: *ye-armeh leh gabeh de-na de-tita* — one mixes it into plaster. So the *shiur* for dirty water is the *revi-it* that one would use to mix a batch of plaster.

Once plaster has been mixed, however, a *braita* states the *shiur* is *kedei la-asot bahen pi kura* — enough to seal the opening of a bellows used in smelting. This is clearly a much smaller amount of plaster than what a full *revi-it* of water would produce. So again: the raw material before processing has a larger *shiur* than the processed product.

The Gemara gives the same answer: *l'fi she-ein adam tore-ach le-gabel et ha-tit la-asot bo pi kura* — a person does not bother mixing plaster from scratch just to produce the tiny amount needed for sealing a bellows opening. Nobody goes through the labor of mixing plaster for such a trivial application. The tiny quantity of plaster, if already mixed, has significance because it is immediately applicable; but the water from which it would be mixed has significance only if there is enough to make it worth mixing — which is the *revi-it*.

## Hide Types

### Three Types

The Gemara now presents a tradition: *amar Rabbi Chiya bar Ami mishmeh de-Ulla — sheloshah orot hen, matza chipa ve-diftera* — there are three types of hide, with corresponding names and stages of processing. The three are *matza*, *chipa*, and *diftera*.

*Matza* — the name evokes the unleavened bread: *de-lo mliach ve-de-lo kmiach ve-de-lo afitz* — no salting, no flouring, and not rubbed with gallnuts. Nothing has been done to it at all. It is raw, entirely unprocessed hide. The standard method of processing leather in this period involved three stages: first salting, then treating with flour of some kind, and finally rubbing with gallnuts. *Matza* leather has undergone none of these.

### Matza Weight Use

*Vekamah shiuro* — how much of this *matza* leather is significant? *Tani Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yehuda: kedei lutzor bo mishkalot ktana* — enough to wrap small weights. A balance scale requires fixed weights, and those weights were individually wrapped in raw hide so they would not rub against one another and gradually lose mass. Raw, untreated hide was suitable for this humble purpose. *Vekamah shiuro* — and how much is that? *Amar Abaye: riva deriva de-Pumbedisa* — a quarter of a quarter of the standard measure used in Pumbedita, which Rashi understands as a quarter of a *liter*-equivalent. This corresponds to the smallest weight in use — perhaps a gram or so — and the amount of raw hide needed to wrap it.

### Chipa Standard

*Chipa* — *demaliach velo kamiach velo apitz*: it has been salted but not yet treated with flour or rubbed with gallnuts. This is partially processed hide. *Vekamah shiuro* — how much? *Kedei la-asot kamia* — the amount to make an amulet pouch. This is the standard form of leather referred to throughout the earlier discussion of the *shiur* for carrying leather. The *kamia* size is the baseline for *chipa*.

### Diftera Writing

*Diftera* — *demaliach ve-kamiach velo apitz*: salted and treated with flour, but the gallnut rubbing has not yet been done. This is the most advanced stage before full processing. *Vekamah shiuro* — how much? *Kedei lichtov alav es ha-get* — enough to write a *get* (a bill of divorce) on it. *Diftera* functions as a form of writing surface — something approaching *klaf*, parchment — and the smallest meaningful document one would write on it is a *get*.

Rashi appears to suggest that each successive stage of processing corresponds to a progressively smaller *shiur*: the more processed, the more valuable per unit, and therefore a smaller piece retains significance. Tosafot disagrees sharply, arguing that this cannot be right — Rashi himself acknowledged elsewhere that more valuable materials are not necessarily measured by smaller *shiurim*, since the *shiur* is determined by the smallest use to which one would put the material. Tosafot's point is that once leather has been brought to the *diftera* stage — nearly ready for writing — one would not demean it by using it as a weight wrapper or even as a *kamia* pouch. The appropriate use for this more refined material is writing a document, and the smallest document one would write on it is a *get*, which happens to require a larger piece than an amulet pouch. The irony is that greater refinement can produce a *larger* minimum *shiur*, because refined material is reserved for higher-value uses that require more of it.

### Moist Hide Challenge

A challenge is raised from a *braita* that specifies the *shiur* for *matza* leather — *kedei la-asot bo mishkalot ktana*, the weight-wrapping amount — which is different from Rav Nachman's ruling that untanned leather goes by the *kamia* amount. These two positions appear inconsistent: Rav Nachman said untanned leather, even without plans to tan it, is measured by the *kamia* size, but the *braita* gives a different, smaller *shiur* for *matza* leather measured by weight-wrapping.

### Process Stage Dist.

*Hasam bevishula* — there the leather is literally as Rashi explains: very moist. The *matza* described in the *braita* is freshly skinned hide that has not even begun to dry out. This places it in a fundamentally different category from the leather Rav Nachman was discussing.

The distinction can be stated as follows. After an animal is skinned, there is a period during which the hide is wet and fresh — before any drying occurs and before any processing begins. This stage is qualitatively different from the stage where the hide has dried out and stands ready to begin the tanning process. When Rav Nachman said untanned leather goes by the *kamia* *shiur*, he was referring to leather that has dried and is *ready to be processed* — it simply has not yet been processed. The *matzah* of the *braita* that goes by the weight-wrapping *shiur* is in a state *before processing can even begin*, still moist from the animal.

To summarize the full framework: fresh moist hide — before the process begins — has its own small *shiur* (weight-wrapping). Dried hide ready for processing — *chipa* processing — goes by the *kamia* *shiur*. Boiled and hardened hide destined to be used as a firm seat (*kortuvla*) goes by yet another *shiur*. Finished leather used for writing — *diftera* — goes by the *get* *shiur*.

### Cloth Comparisons

A Mishnah is cited: *habeged shalsha al shalsha, ha-sak arba-a al arba-a, ha-or hey al hey, mapatz vav al vav* — for *tumas midras* (the impurity transmitted when a *zav* or *zavah* sits on something), the minimum sizes are three by three *tefachim* for cloth, four by four for sackcloth, five by five for leather, and six by six for a mat. A *braita* then states that *habeged ve-hasak ve-ha-or k'shier l'tumah kach shier l'hotzaah* — the *shiur* for *tumah* and the *shiur* for carrying on *Shabbat* are the same for cloth, sackcloth, and leather.

For leather, this yields five by five *tefachim* — vastly larger than the *kamia* pouch amount. How can these be reconciled? The answer: *hahu b'kurtovla* — that Mishnah concerns *kortuvla*, leather that has been boiled in hot water and hardened, used as a firm seat. This is a finished product with its own category of use, and its *shiur* is determined by that use — sitting — which requires a larger piece. This is not pre-processed leather heading toward the *kamia* stage; it is fully processed leather that has been transformed into something categorically different: a rigid seat cover.

### Boiled Leather

The *kortuvla* is thus a special case: *mevashlo b'roschin u'miskashe l'yeishev alav* — boiled in boiling water until it hardens, for use as a seat. It has not been processed toward the soft supple leather of pouches and straps; it has been processed in a different direction entirely. Therefore it is measured by its actual use — as a seat — and that *shiur* is five by five *tefachim*, consistent with the *tumah* *shiur* for leather.

The general principle emerges clearly: if hide is in the pre-process moist stage, it has the weight-wrapping *shiur*; if it is dried and ready for tanning, it takes the *kamia* *shiur* of whatever its tanned form will produce; if it has been processed in an entirely different direction — boiling for a seat — it is measured by that final use. What matters is the realistic trajectory of the material.

## Klaf & Parchment

### Two Parchments

We now transition from leather to parchment. The Mishnah stated that *klaf* — the parchment surface used for *tefillin* — has a *shiur* equal to the amount needed to write the smallest *parshah* in the *tefillin*. *Duchsustus* — the other type of parchment — is associated with the *mezuzah*. These are two distinct materials with distinct sacred functions, and the *shiur* of each is determined accordingly.

### Klaf Definition

*Klaf kedei lichtov alav parshah ktanah she-ba-tefillin* — the *klaf* *shiur* is the amount sufficient to write the smallest of the four *parshiyot* in the *tefillin shel rosh*, which is *Shema Yisrael*. Even that modest amount of *klaf* is considered significant because it has a realized ritual function: it can contain one of the sacred compartments of the *tefillin*.

### Duchsustus Def.

*Duchsustus kedei lichtov alav mezuzah* — the *duchsustus* *shiur* is the amount sufficient to write a full *mezuzah*, which contains both *Shema* and *Ve-haya im shamoa*. Since the entire *mezuzah* must be written on a single piece of parchment, the minimum significant piece of *duchsustus* is one large enough to contain both *parshiyot*.

### Contradictory Braita

*U-reminhu* — a *braita* is cited that seems to contradict what we have established. The *braita* states: *klaf ve-duchsustus kedei lichtov alav mezuzah* — both *klaf* and *duchsustus* go by the *mezuzah* size. Then it continues: *klaf kedei lichtov alav parshah ktanah she-ba-tefillin she-Shema Yisrael* — *klaf* goes by the *Shema* *parshah*. This is internally contradictory. Does *klaf* go by the *mezuzah* size (two *parshiyot*) or by the *Shema* size (one *parshah*)?

The Gemara suggests: *mai mezuzah she-ba-tefillin* — perhaps "mezuzah" in the *braita* refers not to a *mezuzah* on the doorpost, but to one of the parchment scrolls inside the *tefillin*. The term *mezuzah* can be used loosely to mean a scroll, and a *braita* is cited as proof: *retzuos she-ba-tefillin im ha-tefillin metamos es ha-yadayim bifnei atzman ad sheyiga bimzuzah atzmah* — the straps of *tefillin* transmit the *tumah* of *kitvei ha-kodesh* only when attached to the *tefillin*, and Rabbi Zakkai says not even then until one touches the scroll (*mezuzah*) itself. The parchment scroll inside the *tefillin* is there called a *mezuzah*. So the *braita* could be saying: *klaf* to write one *mezuzah* of the *tefillin* — i.e., one *parshah*, which is the *Shema Yisrael* *parshah*.

But this only resolves the end of the *braita*. The beginning said *klaf ve-duchsustus kedei lichtov alav mezuzah*, which the Gemara now says must be referring to a real *mezuzah*, making it a statement about *duchsustus*. The *braita* as a whole is still contradictory.

### Textual Fix

*Hachi katani* — the Gemara proposes a textual reconstruction. The original *braita* most likely read: *klaf ve-duchsustus: duchsustus kedei lichtov alav parshah she-ba-mezuzah; klaf kedei lichtov parshah she-ba-...* — with *duchsustus* going by the full *mezuzah* *parshah* and *klaf* going by the *Shema* *parshah* of *tefillin*. The second occurrence of the word *duchsustus* dropped out of the text — a classic scribal error called *homoioteleuton*, skipping from one instance of a repeated word to the next. The reading *klaf ve-duchsustus duchsustus kedei* would have looked redundant to a copyist, and one *duchsustus* was omitted.

With this reconstruction, the *braita* is perfectly consistent and confirms that *duchsustus* is for *mezuzah* (full *Shema* and *Ve-haya im shamoa*) and *klaf* is for *tefillin* (the single *Shema Yisrael parshah*).

### Physical Structure

What exactly are *klaf* and *duchsustus*? The Gemara now addresses their physical nature, which can be illustrated as follows. The complete untreated hide of an animal — hair side and flesh side together — is called *gevil*. This whole hide can be split horizontally: the outer layer, closer to the hair, and the inner layer, closer to the flesh. The outer layer, once split, is the *klaf* — from *kaluf*, to peel, since it is the outer "peel." The inner layer, closer to the flesh, is the *duchsustus*.

Crucially, both *klaf* and *duchsustus* are written on the inner surface — the surface that faced the point of splitting. For the *klaf*, this means *bimkom basar* — the writing is done on the flesh-facing side. For the *duchsustus*, *bimkom sear* — the writing is done on the hair-facing side. The surfaces on which one writes are thus the two interior surfaces that were formerly joined together.

The *braita* states this as a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*: *tefillin al ha-klaf u-mezuzah al duchsustus; klaf bimkom basar ve-duchsustus bimkom sear* — *tefillin* on the *klaf*, written on the flesh-side surface; *mezuzah* on the *duchsustus*, written on the hair-side surface. This is not a rabbinically derived rule but a tradition from Sinai, though some suggest practical reasons as well: *klaf*, being thinner and more flexible, is easier to roll and fold into the tiny compartments of the *tefillin*.

### Rav's Claim

*Amar Rav: duchsustus harei hi ke-klaf* — Rav declares that *duchsustus* has the same halachic status as *klaf*. *Ma klaf kotvin alav tefillin af duchsustus kotvin alav tefillin* — just as *klaf* is used for *tefillin*, so too may *duchsustus* be used for *tefillin*.

If this is so, one might ask: why does the *shiur* of *duchsustus* still go by the *mezuzah* rather than by the single *Shema parshah*? The answer would be that *lechatchila* one uses *klaf* for *tefillin* and *duchsustus* for *mezuzah*, so the *shiur* of *duchsustus* is defined by its standard use. But *bedi-eved*, if one used *duchsustus* for *tefillin*, it would be valid.

### Switching Forbidden

*Tenan klaf kedei lichtov parshah ktanah she-ba-tefillin shehi Shema Yisrael* — the Mishnah specifies *klaf* alone, implying that *duchsustus* does not carry this *shiur*. One response: *le-mitzvah* — the Mishnah is stating the ideal case, where one uses *klaf* for *tefillin*.

But a *braita* is cited: *shina pasul* — if you switched, it is invalid. If one used *klaf* for *mezuzah* instead of *duchsustus*, it is *pasul*. By extension, if one used *duchsustus* for *tefillin*, should that also be *pasul*?

Another *braita* states more explicitly: *shina be-zeh u-vezeh pasul* — switching either way is invalid. This appears to directly contradict Rav's position.

### Reconcile Switch

The Gemara distinguishes: *aidi ve-aidi mezuzah* — both cases of "switching" in that *braita* refer to errors with the *mezuzah*. There are two ways to err with a *mezuzah*: using *klaf* instead of *duchsustus*, or using the *duchsustus* on the wrong writing surface (*bimkom basar* instead of *bimkom sear*). Either error invalidates the *mezuzah*. The *braita* is not speaking about *tefillin* at all. Therefore, Rav can still maintain that *bedi-eved* using *duchsustus* for *tefillin* is valid.

### Tanna Dispute

*Ve-i bayit eima shina be-zeh u-vezeh tannai hi* — alternatively, the Gemara offers a stronger resolution: this is simply a *tannaitic* dispute, and Rav follows one side of it. *Shina be-zeh u-vezeh pasul* — one *tanna* holds switching either way is invalid. *Rabbi Acha machshir mishum Rabbi Acha bar Rabbi Chanina* (or *Rabbi Yaakov bar Rabbi Chanina*) — Rabbi Acha rules leniently, permitting switching, and it is on the basis of this opinion that Rav rules. The majority of evidence and opinion seems to favor strict separation of the two parchment types, but Rav holds with the minority lenient position — at minimum for the case of *duchsustus* used for *tefillin bedi-eved*.

### Menashe Braita

*Rav Pappa amar: Rav de-amar ke-tanna de-vei Menashe* — Rav Pappa offers yet another account: Rav follows the school of Menashe. *De-tanna de-vei Menashe: kesavah al ha-niyar ve-al ha-matlis psulah; al ha-klaf ve-al ha-gevil ve-al ha-duchsustus kesherah* — if the text was written on paper or on a rag, it is invalid; if it was written on *klaf*, *gevil*, or *duchsustus*, it is valid.

### Subject ID

*Kesavah mai* — what is being written on these three parchments? The Gemara works through the possibilities. *I-leima mezuzah* — if it is a *mezuzah*: we have established that a *mezuzah* cannot be written on *klaf*. But the *braita* includes *klaf* as valid. So this cannot be referring to *mezuzah*. *Ela lav tefillin* — then it must be *tefillin*, and the *braita* is saying that *tefillin* may be written on all three: *klaf*, *gevil*, or *duchsustus*.

### Proof Attempt

But immediately: *u-le-tameich tefillin a-gevil mi kasvinan* — who has ever said that *tefillin* can be written on *gevil*? Nobody suggests *gevil* as a legitimate parchment for *tefillin*. So it cannot be talking about *tefillin* either. *Ela* — by elimination, it must be referring to a *Sefer Torah*, which the Gemara states can be written on any of the three: *klaf*, *gevil*, or *duchsustus*.

### Correction

*Leima mesayei lei* — the Gemara then attempts to use this *braita* to support Rav's position. A Mishnah states that *tefillin she-balu ve-Sefer Torah she-balu ein osin mei-hem mezuzah*, because *ein moridin mi-kedushah chamura l'kedushah kala* — *tefillin* and a *Sefer Torah* may not be repurposed to make a *mezuzah*, because one does not descend from a more stringent holiness to a lesser holiness. The reverse, however, in theory would be permitted — in principle one could use material from a *mezuzah* for *tefillin* or *Sefer Torah*.

The Gemara reasons: since in theory a *Sefer Torah* or worn *tefillin* could be used for *mezuzah* (if the rule of *ein moridin* did not exist), the parchment of *tefillin* and the parchment of a *Sefer Torah* must be compatible with *mezuzah*. Since *mezuzah* requires *duchsustus*, this would suggest that *tefillin* can also be written on *duchsustus* — a support for Rav.

The Gemara rejects this: *lo d'chtiva a'klaf* — the scenario involves *tefillin* or *Sefer Torah* written on *klaf*, and the question is whether that *klaf* could then be used for *mezuzah*. Since *klaf* is valid for *mezuzah* as well (as we will shortly establish), it would theoretically be permissible — but this tells us nothing about *duchsustus* being valid for *tefillin*.

### Rav Reinterp.

This brings us to a striking reversal. *Hashata d'asis l'hachi* — now that we have established that *klaf* can be used for *mezuzah*, we must reread Rav's original statement. Rav said *duchsustus harei hi ke-klaf* — we understood him to mean that *duchsustus* is equivalent to *klaf* and may be used for *tefillin*. But *l'Rav nami lo teima duchsustus harei ke-klaf; ella eima klaf harei ke-duchsustus* — the correct reading of Rav's statement is not that *duchsustus* rises to the level of *klaf*, but that *klaf* descends (if one can say so) to be usable wherever *duchsustus* is used. *U-mah duchsustus kotvin alav mezuzah af klaf kotvin alav mezuzah* — just as one writes a *mezuzah* on *duchsustus*, so too may one write a *mezuzah* on *klaf*.

Thus Rav's ruling is not: *duchsustus* may be used for *tefillin*. It is: *klaf* may be used for *mezuzah*. *Tefillin* remains exclusively on *klaf*; *mezuzah* may be on either. This is the complete reversal of the earlier reading.

The role of Rabbi Meir corroborates this: *Rabbi Meir haya kosev al ha-klaf mipnei she-misameress* — Rabbi Meir would write *mezuzot* on *klaf* because *klaf* is more durable. This is a recognized practice, and the *braita* that seemed to forbid writing a *mezuzah* on *klaf* reflects the view that overruled Rabbi Meir's practice, not a universal ruling.

### Tosafot Modern

Tosafot draws the practical conclusion for later generations in a brief but important note: *b'klafim shelanu yesh lahem din klaf ve-kosvin aleihem Sefer Torah, Tefillin, u-mezuzah* — the parchment we use today carries the classification of *klaf* and may legitimately be used for all three: a *Sefer Torah*, *tefillin*, and a *mezuzah*. This resolves the question of modern practice elegantly. Since *klaf* has been established as valid for *tefillin* (its primary use), for *mezuzah* (as Rav demonstrates and Rabbi Meir practiced), and for a *Sefer Torah* (from the *braita* of the school of Menashe), a single category of parchment — *klaf* — suffices for all sacred writing. What we use today falls within this category, even if the precise technical process described in the Gemara differs somewhat from modern parchment production; Tosafot works through the details of why our parchment is classified as *klaf* and concludes that it is, making our standard practice consistent with all three uses.
