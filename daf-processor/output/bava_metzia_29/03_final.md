# Bava Metzia 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Duration of Announcement

### Mishnah: Duration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עַד כָּמָה חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיֵּדְעוּ בוֹ שְׁכֵנָיו. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁלוֹשׁ רְגָלִים. וְאַחַר הָרֶגֶל הַשְּׁלִישִׁית שִׁבְעַת יָמִים, כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּלֵךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה וְיַחְזוֹר שְׁלֹשָׁה וְיַכְרִיז יוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Until when is one obligated to proclaim** that he has found a lost item? **Rabbi Meir says: Until its neighbors know about it.** The Gemara will clarify that this refers to the neighbors of the location where the item was found. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Three pilgrimage Festivals.** At these Festivals, when all of Israel gathered in Jerusalem, the finder would announce the find. **And** for **seven days after the third pilgrimage Festival,** to allow time **for** the owner to **go to his home,** a trip lasting up to **three** days, **and** to **return** to Jerusalem, a trip lasting up to **three** days, **and** to **proclaim** his loss for **one day.**

The daf begins on 29a with the *mishnah*'s question: *ad masai chayav l'hachriz* — until when is one obligated to announce that he has found a lost object? The underlying assumption is that after the requisite period of announcement, if no one has come forward to claim the *aveidah*, the finder may keep it. The *mishnah* thus sets the threshold of effort required before that point is reached.

### R. Meir: Neighbors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁכֵנֵי אֲבֵידָה? אִי שְׁכֵנֵי בַּעַל אֲבֵידָה – לֵיזִיל וְלִידַרְשׁ נִיהַלֵּי! אֶלָּא שְׁכֵנֵי מָקוֹם שֶׁנִּמְצָא בּוֹ הָאֲבֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** is the meaning of: the neighbors **must know about the lost item?** If it refers to **the neighbors of the owner of the lost item,** then the owner should **go** and **search** for it; why would he need the finder to announce it? **Rather,** it refers to **the neighbors of the location where the lost item was found.**

Rabbi Meir holds that one must announce *ad k'dei sheyeidu bo sh'cheinav* — until one's neighbors know about the found object. The idea is that once knowledge of the find has spread through the neighborhood, anyone who has been searching can be directed to the finder through word of mouth. At that point, further active announcement is unnecessary.

### R. Yehuda: Three Regalim

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and requires a far more extensive process: *shalosh regalim* — three *regalim*, meaning three pilgrimage festivals. The logic is geographic and practical. When all of Israel gathered in Jerusalem for each *regel*, a finder could announce his discovery to an audience drawn from the entire nation. Anyone who had lost something and made the pilgrimage would have the opportunity to hear and respond.

Beyond the three *regalim* themselves, Rabbi Yehuda adds a further seven days after the final *regel*: *k'dei sheyeilech l'veito shlosha v'yachzor shlosha v'yachriz yom echad* — enough time for a person who heard the announcement in Jerusalem to travel home (three days), verify that he indeed lost the object in question, and return (three days), with one final day of announcement to allow the reunion to take place. Rashi understands this final day as the returning owner circulating through Jerusalem asking for the finder, while the Rambam understands it as the finder making one last announcement on the seventh day so that any returnee can find him.

### Gemara: Whose Neighbors?

The *gemara* immediately takes up the ambiguity in Rabbi Meir's formulation. *Mai sh'cheinei aveidah* — whose neighbors must know? The obvious difficulty is that "the neighbors of the owner" seems to require knowing who the owner is, but if you already know the owner's identity, the *gemara* notes *leizei v'lihadrei nihalei* — simply return it to him directly.

### Neighbors of Location

The *gemara* resolves this: *sh'cheinei makom shenimtza bo ha'aveidah* — the neighbors of the location where the object was found. The assumption is that a person who lost something likely lost it in the vicinity of where they live or frequent, and so the people in that area are precisely the ones most likely to know whether their neighbor is missing something. This provides a focused and reasonable coverage without requiring knowledge of the owner's identity. That is Rabbi Meir's approach.

## Travel Time Debate

### Three-Day Journey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, כׇּל מִי שֶׁמָּצָא אֲבֵידָה הָיָה מַכְרִיז עָלֶיהָ שְׁלֹשָׁה רְגָלִים, וְאַחַר רֶגֶל אַחֲרוֹן שִׁבְעַת יָמִים, כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּלֵךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה וְיַחְזוֹר שְׁלֹשָׁה וְיַכְרִיז יוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Initially, anyone who found a lost item would proclaim** his find for **three pilgrimage Festivals and** for **seven days after the last** of the three **pilgrimage Festivals, so that** its owner **will go to his home,** a trip lasting up to **three** days, **and will return** to Jerusalem, a trip lasting up to **three** days, **and proclaim** his loss for **one day.**

The *gemara* raises a pointed difficulty against Rabbi Yehuda's framework. The seven-day window after the final *regel* is predicated on a three-day journey home and a three-day return. But a *baraita* elsewhere establishes that one begins reciting *v'sein tal u'matar livracha* on the seventh of Marcheshvan — fifteen days after Sukkot — in order to allow the most distant inhabitants of the Land of Israel to travel home from Jerusalem. If the entire land can be reached in fifteen days but not in three, how can the *mishnah* assume that three days suffice?

### R. Yosef: Population Den…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כָּאן בְּמִקְדַּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן, כָּאן בְּמִקְדַּשׁ שֵׁנִי. בְּמִקְדַּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן דִּנְפִישִׁי יִשְׂרָאֵל טוּבָּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״יְהוּדָה וְיִשְׂרָאֵל רַבִּים כַּחוֹל אֲשֶׁר עַל־הַיָּם לָרֹב״, הַיְינוּ דְּנִזְדַּרְזוּ לַדָּמִים וּמִזְּדַּרְזוּ לְתָמִיד, וְטָרְחוּ יִתִּירָא כׇּל־כָּךְ דִּשְׁחִַק לַדָּמִים וְנִשְׁתַּמּוּ שְׁנַיִם עָשָׂר חֳדָשִׁים. הָשְׁתָּא בְּמִקְדַּשׁ שֵׁנִי, כׇּל הַקָּהָל כְּאֶחָד: שְׁנַיִם־וּשְׁלוֹשִׁים אֶלֶף וּמְאָתַיִם וּשְׁשִׁים. הַיְינוּ דִּלְמַעַן יִשְׂרָאֵל טוּבָּא לָא קְּרִיבִין וְטָרְחָא לָא מִזְּדַּרְזִין, לְפִי־כָּךְ שַׁלְּמוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָלוֹךְ וּשְׁלוֹשָׁה יָמִים חָזוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says:** The seven-day window in the *baraita* and the three-day window in the mishna refer to different eras: **one refers to the First Temple period, one refers to the Second Temple period.** In **the First Temple period,** the **Jewish population was enormous,** as **it is written: "Judah and Israel were numerous as the sand on the seashore in multitude"** (1 Kings 4:20). Because of this large population, they were spread throughout the land and required fifteen days of travel to reach the outermost communities. In **the Second Temple period, the entire community** numbered **only forty-two thousand, three hundred and sixty.** Because the **Jewish population was not numerous,** they were concentrated closer to the center and required only **three days to go and three days to return.**

Rav Yosef answers that the two sources describe different eras: *kan be-Mikdash Rishon, kan be-Mikdash Sheni* — one refers to the First Temple period and the other to the Second. During the First Temple, *d'nifishi Yisrael tuva* — the Jewish population was enormous, as the verse describes: *Yehuda ve-Yisrael rabbim ka-chol asher al ha-yam la-rov*, as numerous as the sand of the sea. With so many people, they were spread to the far reaches of the land, requiring fifteen days to reach the outermost communities. But in the early Second Temple period, *kol ha-kahal ke-echad arba rivo alapim shlosh me'os va-shishim* — the entire community numbered only forty-two thousand, three hundred and sixty. With so small a population, they were presumably concentrated closer to the center, requiring only three days of travel.

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לוֹ אַבָּיֵי: וַהֲרֵי כְּתִיב: ״וַיֵּשְׁבוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים וְהַלְוִיִּם וְהַמְּשָׁרְתִים וְהַשּׁוֹעֲרִים וְכׇל־יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּעָרֵיהֶם״! אִיפְכָּא מִסְתַּבְּרָא: בְּמִקְדַּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן דִּנְפִישִׁי יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִצְוַת עוֹלָם הוּא, וּשַׁיָּרְסָא דְּנָפִישׁ, וּמַיְיתוּ קַלְנָא דַּיּוֹמָא וּלֵיל, הִלְכָּךְ סָגִי בִּתְלָתָא יוֹמֵי. הָשְׁתָּא בְּמִקְדַּשׁ שֵׁנִי דִּלְמַעַן יִשְׂרָאֵל לָא נְפִישׁ וּשַׁיָּרְסָא לָא נְפִישׁ, הִלְכָּךְ בַּעְיָא טְמִיסְתָּא יוֹמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But isn't it written: "And the priests, the Levites, the attendants, the gatekeepers, and all Israel dwelt in their cities"** (Nehemiah 11:1), indicating that even in the Second Temple period, the Jewish people were dispersed throughout the land. **The opposite conclusion would actually be more logical: In the First Temple period,** when **the Jewish population was numerous,** the *mitzva* was constant, and there were **many caravans,** which **brought news day and night. Therefore,** the three-day journey **sufficed.** But **in the Second Temple period, when the Jewish population was not numerous and caravans were not numerous,** therefore **the journey required thirty days.** The Gemara notes the contribution of Tosafot that the scarcity of travelers also meant fewer functioning inns and way-stations, further slowing journeys in the Second Temple period.

Abaye challenges this reasoning sharply. The verse itself states *va-yeshvu ha-kohanim ve-ha-leviyim ve-ha-meshor'rim ve-ha-sho'arim ve-chol Yisrael be-areihem* — all of Israel, even in the Second Temple period, dwelt in their respective cities throughout the land. They may have been fewer in number, but they were just as geographically dispersed. Given that, Abaye argues *ipcha mistabra* — the opposite conclusion would actually be more logical.

In the First Temple period, the vast population produced *mitzvas alma* — constant accompaniment and movement of travelers. Caravans (*shayarsa*) ran continuously, day and night, along well-traveled routes. Under those conditions, *sagi bi-tlata yomei* — three days were sufficient even to reach distant points. But in the Second Temple period, with fewer travelers on the roads and no reliable all-night caravans, the same distances took longer — hence fifteen days. So if anything, the *mishnah*'s three-day assumption fits the First Temple period, and the fifteen-day threshold of the *baraita* fits the Second. Rav Yosef's answer thus inverts.

### Rava: Limited Duration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָא שְׁנָא מִקְדַּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן וְלָא שְׁנָא מִקְדַּשׁ שֵׁנִי. לֹא הִתְרִיחוּ רַבָּנַן בַּאֲבֵידָה יוֹתֵר מִדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The *halakha* with regard to the duration of the announcement **is not different in the First Temple period and not different in the Second Temple period.** Rather, the rabbis simply **did not impose an excessive burden on the finder of a lost item.** He has already announced the *aveidah* across three *regalim*, and now he is being asked to remain in Jerusalem for a further seven days after the final *regel* — time away from his fields, his livelihood, his household. The rabbis declined to demand a full thirty-day window. Seven days captures those who live within practical reach; those who live at the farthest margins of the land had ample opportunity across the first two *regalim* to have acted already.

Rava cuts through the difficulty with a straightforward resolution: *lo shna Mikdash Rishon ve-lo shna Mikdash Sheni, lo hitrichu Rabbanan b'aveidah yoteir midai* — it does not matter whether we speak of the First or Second Temple period. The rabbis simply did not impose an excessive burden on the finder. He has already announced the *aveidah* across three *regalim*, and now he is being asked to remain in Jerusalem for a further seven days after the final *regel* — time away from his fields, his livelihood, his household. The rabbis declined to demand a full thirty-day window. Seven days captures those who live within practical reach; those who live at the farthest margins of the land had ample opportunity across the first two *regalim* to have acted already.

## Announcement Specificity

### Ravina: Specificity Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, כִּי מַכְרִיז – גְּלִימָא מַכְרִיז, דְּאִי אָמְרַתְּ אֲבֵידְתָּא מַכְרִיז – בִּ־עִנְיַן לְמִתְבֵּי חַד יוֹמָא וּלְעַיּוּנֵי בְּמָנִי, וּשַׁמַּעְנָן בְּמִידֵי דְּלָא אִתּוּכַּח קַצְצֵי דִּי תְלוּתֵי יוֹמָא וְתְלוּתֵי יוֹמָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: One can infer from the** mishna's structure **that when** announcing a found object, **one announces the specific item,** e.g., **he proclaims that he found a cloak.** **For if you say** that **one proclaims that he found a lost item** without specifying its nature, then **a person would need an additional day to remain** at home **and to examine among his possessions** to determine whether anything was missing. Yet **the** mishna **accounts** only **for** the three days of travel each way; no such extra allowance is mentioned. It follows that **the announcement must include the type of object, enabling the claimant to identify immediately whether it might be his.**

Ravina draws an inference from the *mishnah*'s structure regarding the content of the announcement itself. *Shema mina ki machriz, glima machriz* — one can deduce that when announcing a found object, one announces the specific item: "I found a coat," not merely "I found a lost object." If one were permitted to announce only generically, then a person who heard "I found an *aveidah*" would need to go home and conduct an exhaustive inventory of all his possessions to determine whether anything was missing. That would require an extra day — *be'inan lemitvei chad yoma u-le'ayunei be-manei* — yet the *mishnah* accounts only for three days of travel each way with no such extra allowance. It follows that the announcement must include the type of object, enabling the claimant to identify immediately whether it might be his.

### Rava: Generic Announceme…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָא אֵתְרִיחוּ רַבָּנַן בַּאֲבֵידָה יוֹתֵר מִדַּאי, וּלְרַמַּאי לָא חַשִּׁינַן, דְּאִם כֵּן אֵין לַדָּבָר סוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The rabbis simply **did not impose an additional waiting period on the finder,** even though a generic announcement would require the claimant more search time. **For** the possibility of fraud is **not** a major **concern,** because **if so, there is no end to the matter.** Even a generic "I found an *aveidah*" leaves room for guessing, and you cannot protect against every possible fabrication.

Rava pushes back. Even granting that the *mishnah* gives no extra day, one could explain this differently: *lo etrichu Rabbanan b'aveidah yoteir mida'i* — the rabbis did not impose an additional waiting period on the finder even though the generic announcement would require the claimant more search time. The reason one might prefer a generic announcement is to filter out *rama'im* — deceitful claimants. If the finder announces "I found a coat," he hands a would-be fraud the first piece of information they need for a false claim. By announcing only "I found an *aveidah*," the burden of identifying the object falls entirely on the claimant, making fraudulent guessing far more difficult. The tradeoff is that the claimant needs more time to search his belongings — but the rabbis chose not to extend the finder's waiting period to accommodate this. Both the specific and the generic formulations are thus defensible positions, and the *gemara* accepts Rava's counter as a legitimate alternative reading.

## Three Regalim Practice

### Original Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, כׇּל מִי שֶׁמָּצָא אֲבֵידָה הָיָה מַכְרִיז עָלֶיהָ שְׁלֹשָׁה רְגָלִים, וְאַחַר רֶגֶל אַחֲרוֹן שִׁבְעַת יָמִים, כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּלֵךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה וְיַחְזוֹר שְׁלֹשָׁה וְיַכְרִיז יוֹם אֶחָד. מִשֶּׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, שֶׁיִּבָּנֶה בִּמְהֵרָה בְּיָמֵינוּ, הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ מַכְרִיזִים בְּבָתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבְבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת. וּמִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הָאַנָּסִים הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ מוֹדִיעִין לִשְׁכֵנָיו וְלִמְיוּדָּעָיו וְדַיּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Initially, anyone who found a lost item would proclaim** his find for **three pilgrimage Festivals and** for **seven days after the last** of the three **pilgrimage Festivals, so that** its owner **will go to his home,** a trip lasting up to **three** days, **and will return** to Jerusalem, a trip lasting up to **three** days, **and proclaim** his loss for **one day. But from** the time **that the Temple was destroyed, may it be rebuilt speedily in our days,** the Sages **instituted that** those who find lost items **shall proclaim** their finds **in synagogues and study halls. And from** the time **that the oppressors proliferated,** the Sages **instituted** an ordinance **that** one who finds a lost item **shall inform his neighbors and acquaintances, and** that will **suffice for him.**

A *baraita* elaborates on the practice underlying the *mishnah*. In the original period of the Temple, the finder would announce the found object on each of the three *regalim* in Jerusalem, identifying which *regel* in the sequence it was. On the first *regel* he would say, "This is the first *regel* that I am announcing." On the second *regel*, "This is the second *regel*." By the third *regel*, however, he announced without specifying a number at all — *regel shelishi omer stam*.

The *gemara* asks why: let him say "this is the third *regel*." The answer is *delo atei lachlufei be-sheni* — lest people mishear and confuse "third" for "second," leaving a claimant who has one *regel* remaining under the false impression that he still has two. By saying nothing at all on the third occasion, the ambiguity is eliminated; people understand from the omission that this is the final round. The *gemara* notes that a similar confusion on the second *regel* — between "second" and "first" — is less consequential, since even if someone mistakenly thinks they have two remaining chances, they actually still have one more.

### Post-Destruction Adjustm…

Once the Temple was destroyed, the centralized gathering that made the Jerusalem announcement system possible was gone. The rabbis therefore instituted that announcements be made *be-vatei knesiyot u-ve-vatei midrashos* — in synagogues and study halls. These venues, while local rather than national, were the new centers of communal gathering. The *gemara* notes that this shift is emblematic of a broader historical transition: after the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash*, the center of religious life and *kedushah* relocated to the *batei midrashos* and *batei knesiyot*.

### Forceful People Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הָאַנָּסִין? דְּאָמְרִי אֲבֵידְתָּא לְמַלְכָּא. רַבִּי אַמֵּי אַשְׁכַּח אוּדְיָיא דְּדִינָרֵי, חַזְיֵיהּ הָהוּא רוֹמָאָה דְּקָא מִירְתַת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל שְׁקוֹל לְנַפְשָׁךְ, דְּלָאו פָּרְסָאֵי אֲנַן דְּאָמְרִי אֲבֵידְתָּא לְמַלְכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the meaning of: **From** the time **that the oppressors proliferated?** The Gemara answers: It is from the time **that they say: A lost item** belongs **to the king.** The Sages were concerned that any public proclamation would result in confiscation of the lost item. The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Ami found a vessel** full **of dinars. A certain Roman saw that he was wary** and hesitant to take it. The Roman **said to him: Go, take it for yourself; as we are not Persians, who say that a lost item** belongs **to the king.**

A further historical adaptation followed. When *ha-nasim* — forceful individuals — became prevalent, who would insist that any found object be turned over to the king (*aveidata le-malka*), the rabbis adjusted again: rather than making any public announcement at all, the finder was instructed simply to inform his neighbors and acquaintances quietly, and that would suffice. The concern was that a public announcement in a synagogue or study hall might reach these agents of the government who would then seize the object before it could be returned to its rightful owner. Whether these were Jewish collaborators or non-Jewish enforcers is not made explicit in the *gemara*, and this raises broader questions about the principle of *dina de-malchuta dina* — whether the law of the land applies, and whether it applies even to laws that are manifestly unjust, such as confiscating a person's property. The *gemara* clearly regards this law as one the Jewish community should work around, consistent with the *mitzvah* of *hashavat aveidah*.

### Rav Ashi Story

The *gemara* illustrates this situation with a brief narrative. Rav Ashi once found a vessel filled with gold coins (*daisa de-dinrei*) and noticed that a certain bystander — some versions of the text read *bar Roma'ah*, a Roman — observed him with apparent concern, presumably worried that Rav Ashi feared being reported and compelled to surrender the coins to the authorities. The bystander reassured him: *zil shekol le-nafshach, de-lav Parsaya ana de-amri aveidata le-malka* — take it for yourself; I am not a Persian who holds that found objects belong to the king. This anecdote concretizes the social reality behind the legal adjustment: the fear of government confiscation was a genuine concern, not merely theoretical.

### Stone of Claims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֶבֶן טוֹעִ[י]ן הָיְתָה בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. כׇּל מִי שֶׁאָבְדָה לוֹ אֲבֵידָה נִפְנֶה לְשָׁם, וְכׇל מִי שֶׁמּוֹצֵא אֲבֵידָה נִפְנֶה לְשָׁם. זֶה עוֹמֵד וּמַכְרִיז, וְזֶה עוֹמֵד וְנוֹתֵן סִימָנִין וְנוֹטְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **There was a Claimant's Stone in Jerusalem,** and **anyone who lost an item would be directed there and anyone who found a lost item would be directed there. This** finder would **stand and proclaim** his find **and that** owner **would stand and provide** its **distinguishing marks and take** the item.

A *baraita* records an additional institution from the Temple era: *Even To'en hayta bi-Yerushalayim* — there was a Stone of Claims in Jerusalem. Anyone who had lost an object would go there, and anyone who had found one would go there as well. The finder would make his announcement, and the claimant would provide *simanim* — identifying marks — to establish ownership and receive his property. The *gemara* notes that this institution is referenced in the story of Choni HaMa'agel, who asked whether the stone was submerged in the floodwaters he had inadvertently produced through prayer, the stone serving as a familiar Jerusalem landmark.

## Identifying Marks

### Mishnah: Marks & Trust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳: אָמַר אֶת הָאֲבֵידָה, וְלֹא אָמַר סִימָנֶיהָ – לֹא יִתֵּן לוֹ. וְהָרַמַּאי, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמַר סִימָנֶיהָ – לֹא יִתֵּן לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַד דְּרֹשׁ אָחִיךָ אֹתוֹ״, עַד שֶׁתִּדְרוֹשׁ אֶת אָחִיךָ אִם רַמַּאי הוּא אִם אֵינוֹ רַמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a claimant accurately **stated** what type of item **the lost item** that was found by another is, **but did not state,** i.e., describe, **its distinguishing marks,** the finder **shall not give** it **to him. And** in the case of **a swindler, even though he stated its distinguishing marks,** the finder **shall not give** the lost item **to him, as it is stated:** "And if your brother be not near you, and you know him not, then you shall bring it into your house, and it shall be with you **until your brother claims [*derosh*] it [*oto*],** and you shall return it to him" (Deuteronomy 22:2). **You shall not return the lost item until** you **scrutinize [*shetidrosh*] your brother** to determine **whether** he, the claimant, **is a swindler** or **whether he is not a swindler.**

The next *mishnah* addresses the verification process. If someone claims an *aveidah* but says only the category of the object (*amar aveidah*) without providing identifying marks, do not return it to him. And even one who is known as a *ramai* — a deceptive person — do not return the object even if he does provide *simanim*, until one has investigated sufficiently: *ad she-tidrosh et achicha im ramai hu im eino ramai*.

The *mishnah* thus operates on two tracks simultaneously. For ordinary claimants, *simanim* are required and sufficient. For those known to be deceptive, *simanim* alone do not suffice. Conversely, a *tzurva mei-rabbanan* — a Torah scholar — may not even need to provide *simanim*, since his word alone is trusted.

### Announcement Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר, רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: ״אֲבֵידְתָּא״ מַכְרֵיז, וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: ״גְּלִימָא״ מַכְרֵיז. רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: ״אֲבֵידְתָּא״ מַכְרֵיז, דְּאִי אָמְרַתְּ ״גְּלִימָא״ מַכְרֵיז – חָיְישִׁינַן לְרַמַּאי. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: ״גְּלִימָא״ מַכְרֵיז, לְרַמַּאי לָא חָיְישִׁינַן, דְּאִם כֵּן אֵין לַדָּבָר סוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that **Rav Yehuda said:** One who finds an item **proclaims** that he found **a lost item** without specifying its nature. **And Rav Naḥman said:** He specifies the nature of the item, e.g., **he proclaims** that he found **a cloak. Rav Yehuda said:** One who finds an item **proclaims** that he found **a lost item, as if you say** that **he proclaims** that he found **a cloak, we are concerned about** the possibility that **a swindler** may attempt to claim the item. **Rav Naḥman said:** The finder **proclaims** that he found **a cloak, and we are not concerned about** the possibility that **a swindler** may attempt to claim the item, **as if so, there is no end to the matter.**

Here the *gemara* revisits the question of what is announced. Rav Yehuda holds *aveidata machriz* — announce generically that you found a lost object. Rav Nachman holds *gelima machriz* — announce the specific item, e.g., "I found a coat."

Rav Yehuda's reasoning: if you announce "I found a coat," you supply the *ramai* with his opening gambit. He knows what to claim. Better to say nothing about the nature of the object so that a would-be fraudster has no information from which to fabricate. Rav Nachman's reasoning: *le-ramai lo chashinan* — we are not overly worried about deceptive claimants; *simanim* are adequate protection, and an overly generic announcement is practically useless. Moreover, *de-im ken ein la-davar sof* — if you push the concern about fraud too far, there is no end to it. Even a generic "I found an *aveidah*" leaves room for guessing, and you cannot protect against every possible fabrication.

The *mishnah*'s own language becomes a point of contention. *Amar aveidah ve-lo amar simaneha lo yitein lo* — if the claimant identified the object but gave no *simanim*, do not give it back. This phrasing fits naturally with the position that the announcement was generic (*aveidata machriz*): the claimant volunteered that it was a coat, but he still must provide *simanim*. If the finder had announced it was a coat, the *mishnah*'s statement would be trivially obvious — of course identifying it as a coat adds nothing when the finder already said it was a coat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אָמַר אֶת הָאֲבֵידָה וְלֹא אָמַר אֶת סִימָנֶיהָ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִתֵּן לוֹ. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא ״אֲבֵידְתָּא״ מַכְרֵיז, הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן אַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמַר ״גְּלִימָא״, כִּי לָא אָמַר סִימָנִין לָא מַהְדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ ״גְּלִימָא״ מַכְרֵיז, אָמַר אִיהוּ גְּלִימָא, וְאָמַר אִיהוּ גְּלִימָא, צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר כִּי לָא אָמַר סִימָנִין לָא מַהְדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites proof from that which **we learned** in the mishna: If a claimant accurately **states** what type of item **the lost item** that was found by another is, **but did not state its distinguishing marks,** the finder **shall not give** it **to him. Granted, if you say** the finder **proclaims** that he found an unspecified **lost item, this** mishna **teaches us** that **even though** the claimant indeed **stated** that the lost item is **a cloak, as** long as **he did not state** its **distinguishing marks, we do not return** it **to him. But if you say** that the finder **proclaims** that he found **a cloak,** if the finder **stated** that he found **a cloak and** the claimant **stated** that he lost **a cloak,** does it **need to be said** that **when he did not state** its **distinguishing marks, we do not return it to him?**

Rav Safra, however, defends the position that the finder announces *gelima*. The *mishnah*'s phrase *lo amar simaneha* does not mean the claimant failed to name the object; it means he failed to provide *simanim muvhakim* — sufficiently distinguishing identifying marks. He may have said, "Was it a London Fog coat?" but that is not specific enough. The *mishnah* requires genuine *simanim*, not generic category identification.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא, לְעוֹלָם ״גְּלִימָא״ מַכְרֵיז, אָמַר אִיהוּ גְּלִימָא, וְאָמַר אִיהוּ סִימָנִין. וּמַאי לֹא אָמַר אֶת סִימָנֶיהָ? לָא אֲמַר סִימָנִין מוּבְהָקִין דִּידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Safra said: Actually,** one could say that the finder **proclaims** that he found **a cloak,** and the mishna is referring to a case where the finder **stated** that he found **a cloak, and** the claimant **stated** its **distinguishing marks. And what** is the meaning of the phrase in the mishna: If he **did not state its distinguishing marks?** It means: If **he did not state its clear-cut distinguishing marks** but rather stated distinguishing marks that are not exclusive to the item. Therefore, he does not prove his ownership.

### Baraita: Marks to Witnes…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָרַמַּאי אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמַר אֶת סִימָנֶיהָ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִתֵּן לוֹ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, כׇּל מִי שֶׁאָבְדָה לוֹ אֲבֵידָה – הָיָה נוֹתֵן סִימָנִין וְנוֹטְלָהּ. מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הָרַמָּאִין, הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: צֵא וְהָבֵא עֵדִים דְּלָאו רַמַּאי אַתְּ, וְטוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** in the case of **a swindler, even though he stated its distinguishing marks,** the finder **shall not give** the lost item **to him. The Sages taught: Initially, anyone who lost an item would provide** its **distinguishing marks and take it.** But **when the swindlers proliferated,** the Sages **instituted** an ordinance **that** the finders will **say to him: Go and bring witnesses** who can testify **that you are not a swindler, and take** your item.

A *baraita* records a historical evolution in the verification standards. Originally, *simanim* were sufficient and any claimant who provided them received the object. Once *ha-ramaim rabu* — deceptive claimants became more prevalent — the rabbis instituted a more demanding requirement: before returning the object, the finder was to tell the claimant, *tze ve-hava eidim de-lav ramai at ve-tol* — go and bring character witnesses who will testify that you are an honest person, and then you may take the object. This represents a more systematic suspicion than the *mishnah*'s baseline, where a known *ramai* is disqualified but an ordinary claimant is trusted on *simanim* alone.

### Rav Papa Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דַּאֲבוּהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא אִירְכַס לֵיהּ חַמְרָא וְאַשְׁכְּחוּהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי סָהֲדִי דְּלָאו רַמַּאי אַתְּ, וְטוֹל. אֲזַל אַיְיתִי סָהֲדֵי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: יָדְעִיתוּן בֵּיהּ דְּרַמַּאי הוּא? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִין. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא רַמָּאָה אֲנָא?! אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֲנַן ״לָאו רַמַּאי אַתְּ״ קָאָמְרִינַן. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: מִסְתַּבְּרָא, לָא מַיְיתֵי אִינִישׁ חוֹבְתָּא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: This is **as** in **that** incident involving **the father of Rav Pappa,** who **lost a donkey and** others **found it. He came before Rabba bar Rav Huna** to reclaim his donkey. Rabba bar Rav Huna **said** to the father of Rav Pappa: **Go and bring witnesses** who can testify **that you are not a swindler, and** you may **take** your donkey. The father of Rav Pappa **went and brought witnesses.** Rabba bar Rav Huna **said to** the witnesses: **Do you know about him** that **he is a swindler?** The witnesses **said: Yes.** Rav Pappa's father **said,** incredulously, **to** the witnesses: **I am a swindler?** The witnesses **said to him: We were saying that you are not a swindler.** They had thought the question was if he was not a swindler, and therefore responded in the affirmative. **Rabba bar Rav Huna said:** It **is reasonable** to conclude that the witnesses actually intended to support Rav Pappa's father, because presumably, **a person does not bring condemnation upon himself;** Rav Pappa's father would not have volunteered to provide witnesses who would testify against him.

The *gemara* illustrates this practice with a story about the father of Rav Papa, who had lost a donkey. Someone found it, and the father came before Rabba bar Rav Huna, who instructed him to bring character witnesses attesting to his honesty before the donkey would be returned. He brought the witnesses, and the finder turned to them and asked: *yadeitu beih d'ramai hu?* — do you know him to be a *ramai*? The witnesses, having prepared themselves to affirm his honesty and expecting the question to be phrased accordingly, instinctively answered "yes" — confusing the question's polarity. Rav Papa's father was understandably indignant: *ana ramai ana?* The witnesses quickly clarified that they had misheard the question and intended to testify to his integrity.

The question then arose whether this retraction was acceptable, or whether the principle of *keivan she-higid shuv eino chozer u-magid* — once a witness has testified he cannot retract — should bar them from amending their testimony. Rabba bar Rav Huna ruled that the retraction was credible: *mistabra lo mayiti inish chuvsa le-nafshei* — a person does not ordinarily bring witnesses who will testify against himself. Since the father himself brought these witnesses, it is obvious they intended to vouch for him, and the initial "yes" was plainly a mishearing. The court accepted the clarification.

## Use of Found Animals

### Work Animals Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה וְאוֹכֵל – יַעֲשֶׂה וְיֹאכַל, וְדָבָר שֶׁאֵין עוֹשֶׂה וְאוֹכֵל – יִמָּכֵר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַהֲשֵׁבוֹתוֹ לוֹ״, רְאֵה הֵיאַךְ תְּשִׁיבֶנּוּ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one finds **any** living **being that works and** generates enough revenue to cover the costs of the food that it **eats,** it **shall work and eat** while in the finder's possession. **And any** living **being that does not work but** it does **eat shall be sold, as it is stated:** "Then you shall bring it into your house, and it shall be with you until your brother claims it, **and you shall return it to him"** (Deuteronomy 22:2), indicating that the finder must **see how** best **to return it to him.**

The *mishnah* turns to a practical question that arises once a *shomer aveidah* has taken custody of a found animal. During the period before the owner comes forward, the animal incurs costs. Who bears them? The *mishnah* establishes: *kol davar she-oseh ve-ochel ya'aseh ve-yochel* — anything that performs labor and also eats may be put to work, with the proceeds of that labor used to cover the cost of its feed. If the found animal is a cow, the finder may plow with it. If it is a sheep, he may shear its wool and sell the milk. The net effect is that the owner receives his animal back without incurring a large debt to the finder, which is what the Torah intends when it says *ve-hashevoto lo* — you must see to it that you return something of real value to the owner. Having the finder charge for all upkeep costs would effectively penalize the owner for the loss.

### Sell Non-Work Animals

However, *davar she-ein oseh ve-ochel* — an animal that eats but produces no labor or saleable product — presents a different problem. Keeping it alive generates costs with no offsetting revenue. In such a case, the *mishnah* says *yimmacher* — the finder should sell it and hold the proceeds for the owner. The verse *ve-hashevoto lo* is read as a mandate to find the method of return that preserves the most value: better to hold cash than to accumulate expenses against a living animal whose upkeep the finder cannot recoup.

The *mishnah* then raises the question of what the finder may do with the cash proceeds from that sale while waiting for the owner to claim them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה יְהֵא בַּדָּמִים? רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן, לְפִיכָךְ אִם אָבְדוּ – חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן, לְפִיכָךְ אִם אָבְדוּ – אֵין חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **What shall be** done **with the money** received from the sale of the animal? **Rabbi Tarfon says:** The finder **may use it; therefore, if** the money **is lost,** he is **liable** to pay **restitution** for **it. Rabbi Akiva says: He may not use** the money; **therefore, if it is lost,** he is **not liable** to pay **restitution** for **it.**

*Rabbi Tarfon omer yishtamesh bahen* — Rabbi Tarfon permits the finder to use the money, treating it as a fund available to him as long as he returns the equivalent amount when the owner appears. *V'lefikach im avdu chayav be-achrayutan* — and precisely because he has the right to use the money, he bears responsibility for it even if it is lost. *Rabbi Akiva omer lo yishtamesh bahen* — Rabbi Akiva forbids use of the money, and *lefikach im avdu ein chayav be-achrayutan* — therefore if it is lost the finder is not liable.

### 12-Month Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּלְעוֹלָם? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: עַד שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה וְאוֹכֵל כְּגוֹן פָּרָה וַחֲמוֹר – מִטַּפֵּל בָּהֶן עַד שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – שָׁם דְּמֵיהֶן וּמַנִּיחָן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that an animal that generates enough revenue to cover the costs of the food that it eats shall work and eat while in the finder's possession. The Gemara asks: **And** must he care for the animal **forever? Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says:** He cares for the animal **until twelve months** pass. **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: If one finds **any** living **being that works and** generates enough revenue to cover the costs of the food that it **eats, e.g., a cow or a donkey,** he **tends to them until twelve months** pass. **From that** point **forward, one assesses their value,** sells them, **and places** the money aside for the owner.

Even for the case of work animals, the *mishnah*'s permission to continue working them is not indefinite. Rav Nachman citing Shmuel rules: *ad yud-bet chodesh* — for up to twelve months. The owner has a strong interest in recovering the actual animal, and so the finder must make this effort for a full year. After twelve months, even this case transitions to the same solution as non-work animals: appraise the animal at fair market value, sell it, and hold the proceeds.

A *baraita* confirms this ruling and begins to differentiate by animal type.

### Variable Time Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲגָלִים וּסְיָיחִין – מִטַּפֵּל בָּהֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – שָׁם דְּמֵיהֶן וּמַנִּיחָן. אֲווֹזִין וְתַרְנְגוֹלִין – מִטַּפֵּל בָּהֶם שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – שָׁם דְּמֵיהֶן וּמַנִּיחָן. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת כִּבְהֵמָה גַּסָּה.
>
> **Translation:** If one finds **calves and foals,** which are young and unfit for labor, **he tends to them** for **three months,** as they do not earn their keep. **From that** point **forward, one assesses their value,** sells them, **and places** the money aside for the owner. If one finds **geese and roosters, he tends to them** for **thirty days. From that** point **forward, one assesses their value,** sells them, **and places** the money aside for the owner. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says:** The legal status of **a chicken is like** that of **a large domesticated animal** in that the eggs it lays suffice to cover the cost of its food.

For large animals such as cows and donkeys that perform labor, *metapel bahen ad yud-bet chodesh* — twelve months of care, after which they are sold. For calves and foals (*agalim ve-syachim*) — young animals not yet capable of labor and thus generating only expense — the period is three months. For geese and roosters (*avazim ve-tarnegolim*), where the only economic benefit is slaughter and the entire purpose of keeping them alive is to restore them to the owner, thirty days.

A second *baraita*, however, specifies different figures for the latter two categories: calves and foals merit only thirty days, and geese and roosters as few as three days for any animal *she-tipulo merubeh mi-scharo* — whose cost of upkeep exceeds any potential benefit. The *gemara* reconciles the apparent contradiction in the calves-and-foals cases by distinguishing between situations where grazing land is available (low expense, hence three months) and where all feed must be purchased (high expense, hence thirty days). The apparent contradiction in the roosters-and-geese cases is resolved by distinguishing between large birds (high expense, thirty days) and small ones (minimal expense, three days). In all cases, the underlying principle is the same: the finder bears upkeep costs only to the degree that it does not impose an unreasonable burden, always with the goal of returning maximum value to the owner.

## Use of Proceeds Debate

### Mishnah: Use Rights

The *mishnah*'s dispute between Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva regarding the use of proceeds is now examined in the *gemara* at greater depth.

### Guardian Status Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁנִּשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן. אֲבָל לֹא נִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן, אִם אָבְדוּ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **only** in a case **where** the finder **used** the money. **But** in a case where the finder **did not use** the money, everyone agrees that **if** the money **is lost,** the finder is **exempt** from paying restitution for its loss.

The *gemara* frames the dispute in terms of the categories of *shmirah* — guardianship. There are three main types: a *shomer chinam*, who guards without compensation and is liable only for gross negligence but not for loss or theft; a *shomer sachar*, who receives payment and is liable for loss and theft but not for accidents (*ones*); and a *shoel*, a borrower, who is liable even for accidents. The debate of Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva turns on where within this spectrum the *shomer aveidah* falls once money is involved.

### R. Yosef: Paid Guardian

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב יוֹסֵף? דְּאִתְּמַר: שׁוֹמֵר אֲבֵידָה, רַבָּה אָמַר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **this shall be a conclusive refutation of** the statement of **Rav Yosef, as it was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute with regard to the legal status of **a bailee** charged with safeguarding **a lost item. Rabba said:** His legal status **is like** that of **an unpaid bailee,** who is liable to compensate the owner of the deposited item only in cases of negligence. **Rav Yosef said:** His legal status **is like** that of **a paid bailee,** who is liable to compensate the owner of the deposited item even in cases of theft or loss.

Rav Yosef holds that any *shomer aveidah* — regardless of whether he uses the money or not — has the status of a *shomer sachar*. His reasoning: while watching a lost object, the guardian is engaged in a *mitzvah*, and *osek be-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah* — one who is actively fulfilling a commandment is exempt from other commandments simultaneously. Thus, for the duration of his stewardship of the *aveidah*, if a poor person comes to his door soliciting *tzedakah*, he need not stop to give it. This exemption constitutes a form of benefit, and receiving benefit for guardianship makes one a *shomer sachar*.

Rabba disagrees: this benefit is too tenuous and too incidental to constitute the kind of compensation that elevates a guardian's status. In his view, a *shomer aveidah* is a *shomer chinam*.

The *gemara* now considers whether our *mishnah* contradicts Rav Yosef.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב יוֹסֵף: בִּגְנֵיבָה וַאֲבֵידָה – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּחַיָּיב, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּאוּנְסִין דְּשׁוֹאֵל, רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן סָבַר: שָׁרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְאִשְׁתַּמּוֹשֵׁי בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ שׁוֹאֵל עֲלַיְיהוּ. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: לָא שָׁרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְאִשְׁתַּמּוֹשֵׁי בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ, הִלְכָּךְ לָא הָוֵי שׁוֹאֵל עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rav Yosef** could have **said to you: In** cases of **theft or loss, everyone agrees that** a bailee charged with safeguarding a lost item is **liable** to pay restitution for it. **When they disagree** is **in** a case of damage caused by **circumstances beyond his control,** for which it is the obligation **of a borrower** to pay compensation. The Gemara elaborates: **Rabbi Tarfon holds: The Sages permitted him to use** the money, **and he is** therefore **a borrower with regard to it,** and is liable to compensate the owner even in the event of circumstances beyond his control. **And Rabbi Akiva holds: The Sages did not permit him to use** the money, and he **is therefore not a borrower with regard to it.**

### Borrower Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״לְפִיכָךְ״ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, לְמָה לִי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בִּגְנֵיבָה וַאֲבֵידָה הוּא דִּפְּלִיגִי, הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי ״רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר לֹא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן, לְפִיכָךְ אִם אָבְדוּ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן״. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר הָוֵי כִּדְרַב יוֹסֵף, וּבִגְנֵיבָה וַאֲבֵידָה מְחַיֵּיב, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן ״לְפִיכָךְ״. הַשְׁתָּה דְּאָמְרַתְּ לֹא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן, שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר לָא הָוֵי וְלָא מְחַיֵּיב בִּגְנֵיבָה וַאֲבֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, why do I** need the statement **that Rabbi Akiva said:** He may not use the money; **therefore,** if it is lost, he is not liable to pay restitution for it? **Granted, if you say** that it is **in** cases of **theft or loss that they disagree,** I understand **that** is the reason **that the** *tanna* **teaches** in the mishna that **Rabbi Akiva says: He may not use** the money; **therefore, if** it is **lost, he is not liable** to pay **restitution** for **it.** The Gemara explains: Since **it enters your mind to say** that the legal status of the finder **is** like that of **a paid bailee, in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Yosef, and** that **in** cases of **theft and loss** the finder is **liable** to pay restitution, Rabbi Akiva **teaches us: Therefore,** if it is lost, he is not liable to pay restitution. **Now that you said** that **he may not use** the money, **he is not a paid bailee and** is **not liable** to pay restitution **in** cases of **theft and loss.**

Rav Yosef's reinterpretation is striking. He argues that everyone agrees a *shomer aveidah* is a *shomer sachar*, and so the *mishnah*'s dispute cannot be about the transition from *shomer chinam* to *shomer sachar*. Rather, the debate concerns *onsin d'shoel* — liability for pure accidents, which only a *shoel* bears. According to Rav Yosef: *Rabbi Tarfon savar sharu leh Rabbanan le-ishtamushi be-gavaihu ve-havei lei shoel* — since the rabbis permit the finder to use the money, that permission transforms him into a borrower. A borrower has the highest level of liability, even for accidents — a ship sinking at sea, armed robbery, an act of God. And since Rabbi Tarfon holds that the finder has the right to use the money, *keivan d'sharu Rabbanan le-ishtamush be-gavaihu ke-man d'ishtamesh be-gavaihu dami* — it is as if he has already used it, and he bears the liability of a *shoel*. Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, holds that the finder has no right to use the money. Since he is not a borrower, he retains the status of a *shomer sachar* — liable for ordinary loss and theft but not for accidents.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּגְנֵיבָה וַאֲבֵידָה דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּחַיָּיב, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּאוּנְסִין דְּשׁוֹאֵל, מַאי ״לְפִיכָךְ״ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? הָכִי מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמִתְנֵא: ״רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר לֹא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן״, וַאֲנָא יָדַעְנָא דְּכֵיוָן דְּלֹא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן – לָאו שׁוֹאֵל הָוֵי וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן, ״לְפִיכָךְ״ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say** that **in** cases of **theft or loss, everyone agrees that** a bailee charged with safeguarding a lost item is **liable** to pay restitution for it, and **when they disagree** it is **in** cases of damage caused by **circumstances beyond his control** for which it is the obligation **of a borrower** to pay compensation, **what** is the meaning of the statement of **Rabbi Akiva: Therefore,** if it is lost, he is not liable to pay restitution for it? Rather, **this is what** the mishna **should have taught: Rabbi Akiva says: He may not use** the money; **and I would know that since he may not use** the money, **he is not** considered **a borrower,** and consequently bears no financial responsibility. **Why do I** need the statement **that Rabbi Akiva said: Therefore,** if it is lost, he is not liable to pay restitution for it?

The word *lefikach* in each case signals a non-obvious conclusion that requires stating explicitly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם ״לְפִיכָךְ״ דְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The explanation appended to the statement of Rabbi Akiva is indeed extraneous. It was added in order to create a parallel between the formulation of the statement of Rabbi Akiva and the formulation of the statement of Rabbi Tarfon. The phrase: Therefore, if the money is lost, he is not liable to pay restitution for it, was appended to the statement of Rabbi Akiva **due to** the explanation: **Therefore,** if the money is lost, he is liable to pay restitution for it, stated **by Rabbi Tarfon.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְפִיכָךְ דְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן לְמָה לִי? הָכִי קָאָמַר: כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְאִשְׁתַּמּוֹשֵׁי בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ כְּמַאן דְּאִישְׁתַּמַּשׁ בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ דָּמֵי וְחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need the statement **that Rabbi Tarfon** said: **Therefore,** if the money is lost, he is liable to pay restitution for it? The Gemara answers: **This** is what the mishna **is saying: Since the Sages permitted him to use** the money, his legal status is **like** that of **one who** actually **used it and** therefore, he is **liable** to pay **restitution** for **it.**

The *mishnah*'s use of the language *im avdu* — "if they were lost" — fits this reading as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״אָבְדוּ״ קָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can Rav Yosef explain that the dispute in the mishna is with regard to damage caused by circumstances beyond his control? **But doesn't** the mishna **teach:** Therefore, if the money **is lost?** The disagreement between Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva is with regard to a case of damage due to loss, and not with regard to a case of damage caused by circumstances beyond one's control.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַבָּה. דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: ״נִגְנְבוּ״ בְּלִסְטִים מְזוּיָּין, ״אָבְדוּ״ שֶׁטָּבְעָה סְפִינָתוֹ בַּיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the statement in the mishna: Therefore, if the money is lost, he is liable to pay restitution for it, can be explained **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabba, as Rabba says** concerning another mishna (58a): When the *tanna* says that **they were stolen,** the reference is to a case where the item was stolen **by armed bandits;** when he says that **they were lost,** the reference is to a case where the agent's **ship sank at sea.**

### Halacha: R. Tarfon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon,** who said that it is permitted for the finder to use the money.

Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel rules: *halacha ke-Rabbi Tarfon* — the ruling follows Rabbi Tarfon. The finder of a lost object may use the proceeds from the sale of the *aveidah*.

The *gemara* applies this ruling immediately to a practical case: Rechava held money belonging to orphans in trust. He came before Rav Yosef to ask whether he was permitted to use those funds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּיַד רַחֲבָה (הֲוָה לֵיהּ) [הֲווֹ] הָנְהוּ זוּזֵי דְיַתְמֵי, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַהוּ לְאִשְׁתַּמּוֹשֵׁי בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** There **were these dinars** that belonged **to orphans** that were **in the possession of Raḥava.** Raḥava **came before Rav Yosef** and **said to him: What is** the *halakha*; is it permitted for me **to use** these dinars? Rav Yosef **said to him: This** is what **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon.**

### Abaye: Effort Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְלָאו אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּדְמֵי אֲבֵידָה הוֹאִיל וְטָרַח בַּהּ, אֲבָל מָעוֹת אֲבֵידָה דְּלָא טָרַח בְּהוּ – לָא. וְהָנֵי כְּמָעוֹת אֲבֵידָה דָּמוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל לָא שְׁבַקוּ לִי דְּאֶשְׁרֵי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **Wasn't it stated concerning this** *halakha* that **Rabbi Ḥelbo says** that **Rav Huna says:** The Sages **taught this** *halakha*, that it is permitted to use the money, **only in** a case of **money** received from the sale **of a lost item** that one found and that is no longer financially viable for one to tend to it. This is permitted, **since he exerted** himself and tended **to it. But** in the case of **lost coins, where he did not exert** himself in order to tend **to them,** it is **not** permitted for him to use them. **And** the case of **these** dinars in Raḥava's possession **is similar** to a case of **lost coins.** Rav Yosef accepted Abaye's objection and **said to** Raḥava: **Go;** as **they did not allow me to permit** the use of the dinars **for you.**

Abaye intervened with a critical qualification. He cited a tradition from Rav Kahana in the name of Rav Huna: *lo shanu ela bi-dmei aveidah* — Rabbi Tarfon's permission to use the money applies specifically to the *proceeds of a sold aveidah*, and not to money found directly or entrusted for safekeeping without effort. The reason: *ho'il ve-tarach bah* — the finder exerted effort. He found the object, cared for it for some period, sought an owner, and then undertook the effort of selling it and converting it into cash. In recognition of that effort, the rabbis granted him the right to put the money to use.

But money that comes with no such effort — money found on the ground, or money handed over directly for safekeeping, such as orphans' funds — does not carry that right. *Ve-hanhu ke-me'ot aveidah damu* — such funds are analogous to cash found directly without any transformative effort on the finder's part. There, Rabbi Tarfon's dispensation does not apply.

Rav Yosef accepted Abaye's argument and told Rechava accordingly: *lo shavku li de'ashri lach* — they have not left me the ability to permit you. The permission to use entrusted funds does not follow from the ruling like Rabbi Tarfon; that ruling is limited to proceeds earned through the effort of handling and selling a found object.
