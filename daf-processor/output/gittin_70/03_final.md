# Gittin 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Medical Remedies

### Hemorrhoids Remedy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרוּשְׁחָתָא – לַיְתֵי אַקִּיקָא, וְאִילְווֹא, וְאַסְפִּירְכָא, וּמַרְתְּכָא, וְחוּמַרְתָּא דְפִילוֹן, וּשְׁיָאפָא דַחֲמִימְתָּא, וְנִינְקֹט בְּשַׁחֲקֵי דְכִיתָּנָא – בְּקַיְיטָא, וְדַעֲמַר גּוּפְנָא – בְּסִיתְוָא. וְאִי לָא – לִישְׁתֵּי שִׁיכְרָא מַרְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for hemorrhoids, let him bring acacia [*akika*], and aloe [*ilava*], and mercury, and silver dross, and a bundle [*ḥumreta*] of fragrant herbs [*defilon*], and feces of pigeons [*ḥamimta*]. And let him take it in linen bags in the summer, or cotton** bags **in the winter,** and place them on the afflicted area. **And if** he is **not** able to do that, **let him drink diluted beer.**

The daf opens with a series of medical remedies, beginning with a treatment for *leruchshasa* — hemorrhoids or a related condition in that area. The prescribed compound involves acacia wood, aloe, mercury (or, as Rashi understands it, raw silver, perhaps quicksilver), a silver dross, a small perfume vial containing an aromatic liquid mixture, and dog excrement. All of these are to be wrapped together in worn-out flax cloths — in summer using those cloths directly, and in winter or fall using cotton. Presumably the resulting package is applied to the affected area, though the Gemara does not spell this out explicitly. If this treatment proves unavailable or ineffective, the Gemara offers the considerably simpler alternative of drinking diluted beer.

### Hip Weakness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְשִׁיגְרוֹנָא – לַיְתֵי פַּתְיָא דְמוֹנִינֵי, וְנִיגַנְדְּרֵיהּ שִׁיתִּין זִימְנֵי אַהָא מְטַחְתֵּיהּ, וְשִׁיתִּין זִימְנֵי אַהָא מְטַחְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **For rheumatism [*shigrona*], let him bring a vessel full of brine from small fish [*moninei*] and roll it sixty times on this thigh and sixty times on that thigh.**

For *shiggrona* — a weakness or illness of the thighs or hips — the Gemara prescribes a pot full of fish brine. Rashi defines the vessel as one filled with *tzir dagim*, fish juice. The treatment involves rubbing it sixty times on one side and sixty times on the other.

### Bladder Stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִצְמִירְתָּא – נֵיתֵי תְּלָת נִיטּוּפְיָיתָא מִישְׁחָא דְכוּפְרָא, וּתְלָת נִיטּוּפְיָיתָא אִיצְרָא דְכַרָּתֵי, וּתְלָת נִיטּוּפְיָיתָא דְחַמְרָא נְקִידָא, וְלִישְׁדֵּי לֵיהּ לְאִישׁ – בָּאַמָּה, וּלְאִשָּׁה – בְּאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם. וְאִי לָא – לַיְתֵי אוּנָּא דְזִיקָא, וְלִתְלֵי לֵיהּ לְאִישׁ בָּאַמָּה, וּלְאִשָּׁה בַּדַּדִּין. וְאִי לָא – לַיְתֵי חוֹטֵא דִּזְהוֹרִיתָא דִּשְׂדֹתֶיהָ דּוּמָה בַּת דּוּמָה, וְלִיתְלֵי לֵיהּ לְאִישׁ בָּאַמָּה וּלְאִשָּׁה בַּדַּדִּין. וְאִי לָא – לַיְתֵי כִּינָּה דְּזָכָר וּנְקֵבָה, וְלִיתְלֵי לֵיהּ לְאִישׁ בָּאַמָּה וּלְאִשָּׁה בְּאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם; וְכִי מַשְׁתִּין, נַשְׁתִּין אַסִּיסָנָא יַבִּישְׁתָּא בְּצִינּוֹרָא דְּדַשָּׁא, וּנְעַיֵּין בְּחוּמַרְתָּא דְּנָפְקָא מִינֵּיהּ, דִּמְעַלְּיָא לְכוּלְּהוּ צִימְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for a bladder stone [*litzmireta*], let him bring three drops of tar oil,** which is oil that emerges from burning wood, **three drops from the squeezing [*itzra*] of leeks, and three drops of clean wine, and place** this mixture, **for a man on the penis, and for a woman on that place,** i.e., her genital area. **And if** he is **not** able to do this, **let him bring the ear,** i.e., handle, **of a wine sac and suspend it, for a man from** his **penis, and for a woman from** her **breasts. And if** he is **not** able to do this, **let him bring a crimson string spun by** a woman **suspected** of prostitution who is also **the daughter of a suspected** woman, **and let him suspend it, for a man from** his **penis and for a woman from** her **breasts. And if not, let him bring a louse from a male and** a louse from **a female, and suspend it, for a man from his penis, and for a woman on that place,** i.e., her genital area. **And when he urinates let him urinate on a dry branch [*sisna*] by the door pivot. And let him examine the** bladder **stone that comes out of him** with the urine, **as it is effective** as a remedy **for any** illness accompanied by **a fever [*tzimra*]** if he grinds it and uses it.

For *tzamirta* — a stone lodged in the bladder — the remedy calls for three drops of liquefied tar, three drops of leek juice, and three drops of clear wine. For a man, this mixture is applied to his member; for a woman, to the corresponding area. If that does not resolve matters, the next step is to take the ear of a pitcher and suspend it in the appropriate place — on the man's member, and for a woman on her breasts. If that still fails, one procures a purple string that was spun by a woman of ill repute, the daughter of a woman of ill repute, and suspends it in the same manner.

If none of the above treatments work, the Gemara prescribes obtaining a louse — understood to mean one taken from a man and one from a woman, not a male and female louse per se — and applying it to the genital area. Additionally, when the afflicted person needs to urinate, they should do so onto a dried thorn bush near the doorpost frame, and then observe the stone that passes. The Gemara notes that such a stone, if retrieved, has health benefits for all manner of *tzimra*, fever.

### Genital Ailments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְצִימְרָא בָּרָא – לַיְתֵי תְּלָתָא גְּרִיבֵי סוּפְלֵי, וּתְלָתָא גְּרִיבֵי אַטְרַף אַדָּרָא, וְנִישְׁלוֹקִינְהוּ כֹּל חַד לְחוֹדֵיהּ; וְלִיתֵיב בֵּינַיְיהוּ; וְלֹתְבִינְהוּ בְּתַרְתֵּי מְשִׁיכְלֵי, וְנַיְתֵי פָּתוּרָא וְנַנַּח עִילָּוֵיהּ, וְנֵיקוּם מֵעִילָּוֵי הַאי וְלֵותֵיב עִילָּוֵי הַאי, וּמֵעִילָּוֵי הַאי וְלֵותֵיב עִילָּוֵי הַאי, עַד דְּנִיסַּק בֵּיהּ הַבְלָא; וְלִסְחֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ, וְכִי שָׁתֵי – מִמַּיָּא דְאַדָּרָא לִישְׁתֵּי, מִמַּיָּא דְסוּפְלֵי לָא לִישְׁתֵּי; מִשּׁוּם דִּמְיעַקְּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for an external fever, let him bring three *se'a*** of **date pits [*suflei*], and three *se'a* [*gerivei*]** of **leaves of an *eder*** tree. **Let him cook each one individually and sit between them. And let him place them in two basins, and bring a table and place it over him. And let him stand up from over this** basin **and sit over this** basin, **and** then **let him stand up from over this** basin **and sit over this** basin, **until the heat rises on him** and he becomes very hot. **And let him wash from the** water of both basins, **and when** he **drinks, let him drink from the** basin containing the ***eder* water.** But do **not let him drink from** the basin containing **the date water, because** it **causes infertility.**

For *tzimra barana* — an external fever, possibly a surface condition — the treatment involves three sacks of date pits and three sacks of alder leaves, each boiled separately. The person sits between the boiling vessels, then stands over one and bends toward the other, alternating, until sweating begins, at which point one bathes in the liquid. The Gemara adds an important warning: when one drinks from these preparations, drink from the alder-leaf water, not from the date-pit water — because the date-pit water causes sterility.

For *tzimra gavna*, an internal fever, one takes seven handfuls of beets from seven different garden rows, together with their soil, eats them, and then drinks alder leaves steeped in beer. Alternatively, one may drink or eat grapes from a vine that grew around a palm tree beside water.

### Skin Lesions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לַחֲזָזִיתָא – לַיְתֵי שַׁב חִיטֵּי אַרְזַנְיָיתָא, וְנִיקְלִינְהוּ אַמָּרָא חַדְתָּא, וְנָפֵיק מִשְׁחָא מִינַּיְיהוּ, וְנֵישׁוּף. רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי עֲבַד לֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גּוֹי לְדָבָר אַחֵר, וְאִיתַּסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for lichen planus [*ḥazazita*]** on one's skin, **let him bring seven large wheat kernels [*arzanayata*] and** let him **roast them over** a fire on the blade of **a new hoe. And** let him **extract oil from** the wheat **and rub** it into his skin. It is told: **Rav Shimi bar Ashi used this** remedy **for a certain gentile** who had **something else,** i.e., leprosy, **and he was healed.**

For *chazizta* — a skin lesion, something resembling anthrax — seven kernels of a particularly large type of wheat (*arzanita*, according to Rashi) are burned on a new iron hoe, allowing a liquid to emerge from them, which is then rubbed onto the affected area.

### Rav Shimi Case

The Gemara records that Rav Shimi bar Rav Ashi — the son of Rav Ashi — applied precisely this wheat-kernel ointment to a non-Jew (*nochri*) who suffered from a condition described obliquely as *davar acher*, leprosy, and the man was healed. This brief narrative closes out what is evidently a self-contained collection of anonymous remedies, likely originating in the circle of Ravina and Rav Ashi, as is indicated by the Gemara's own internal evidence: at one point Ravina himself clarifies his own position within the discussion. The style of this collection — formulaic, anonymous, and structured as "for this disease, do this" — stands in marked contrast to what follows.

## Fatal Conditions

### Persian Spear Poison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַאי מַאן דִּמְחוֹ לֵיהּ בַּאֲלוֹנְכֵּי דְפָרְסָאֵי, מִיחְיָיא לָא חָיֵי; אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי, נִיסְפּוֹ לֵיהּ בִּשְׂרָא שַׁמִּינָא אַגּוּמְרֵי, וְחַמְרָא חַיָּיא, אֶפְשָׁר דְּחָיֵי פּוּרְתָּא וּמְפַקֵּיד אַבֵּיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said: One who is struck with Persian spears [*alunkei*] will not live** long afterward, as he will certainly die from this wound. **In the meantime, they should force-feed him fatty meat** that was roasted **over coals, and undiluted wine.** If they do this, it is **possible that he will live** for **a little bit** longer **and** have time to **instruct his household** with regard to what they should do after his death.

With the transition to named *amoraim* and a more narrative register, the Gemara shifts to a different category: not cures for recoverable ailments, but palliative care for conditions that are definitively fatal. Shmuel states that a person pierced by a Persian spear — whose tip was coated with poison — will not survive. However, in the time remaining, one should give him fatty roasted meat and raw wine; this may extend his life somewhat. The benefit of this extension is explicitly stated: it gives him enough lucidity to instruct his household and put his affairs in order — to give his last will and testament.

### Wasp Ingestion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: הַאי מַאן דִּבְלַע זִיבּוּרָא – מִיחְיָיא לָא חָיֵי, אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי נַשְׁקְיֵהּ רְבִיעֵתָא דְּחַלָּא שַׁמְגָּז, אֶפְשָׁר דְּחָיֵי פּוּרְתָּא וּמְפַקֵּיד לְבֵיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Idi bar Avin said: One who swallowed a hornet will not live. In the meantime they should give him a quarter-***log* **of sharp [*shamzag*] vinegar to drink.** If they do this, **it is possible that he will live** for **a little bit** longer **and** have time to **instruct his household** with regard to what they should do after his death.

Similarly, Ravidi bar Avin teaches that if a person swallows a wasp, he will not live. In the interim, however, he should drink a *revi'it* of *shamzag* — a particularly potent type of vinegar — which may keep him alive a little longer, long enough to make arrangements for his household. The underlying assumption is that a wasp, which causes severe damage through its sting on the outside of the body, would be catastrophically destructive from within.

## Diet & Prevention

### Beef & Moonlight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אָכַל בְּשַׂר שׁוֹר בְּלֶפֶת, וְלָן בַּלְּבָנָה בְּלֵילֵי אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר אוֹ חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בִּתְקוּפַת תַּמּוּז – אֹחַזְתּוֹ אֲחִילוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** If one **ate ox meat with a turnip, and slept by** the light of **the moon on the night of the fourteenth or the fifteenth** of the month **in the season of Tammuz,** i.e., summer, he will be **afflicted with *aḥilu*,** a severe fever.

The Gemara now turns from emergency palliative measures to preventative medicine — identifying behaviors and foods that are dangerous and conditions to be avoided. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches: if a person eats beef (*basar shor*) together with a turnip (*lefet*), and then sleeps outdoors in the moonlight on the fourteenth or fifteenth of the month — the full moon — during the summer months (*tekufat Tammuz*), he will be seized by *achilu*. The Gemara will shortly explain what *achilu* is.

### Overeating Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: וְהַמְמַלֵּא כְּרֵיסוֹ מִכׇּל דָּבָר, אֹחַזְתּוֹ אֲחִילוּ. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֲפִילּוּ מִתַּמְרֵי. פְּשִׁיטָא! סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הוֹאִיל וְאָמַר מָר: תַּמְרֵי – מַשְׂבְּעָן, וּמְשַׁחֲנָן, וּמְשַׁלְשְׁלָן, וּמְאַשְּׁרָן וְלָא מְפַנְּקָן; אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught: And one who fills his stomach with anything,** meaning that he eats too much, will be **afflicted with *aḥilu*. Rav Pappa said: Even** if he fills his stomach **with dates.** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** this **obvious,** as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi explicitly mentioned if one fills his stomach with anything. The Gemara answers: **It might enter your mind to say** that **since the Master said** the following in praise of dates: **Dates satisfy** the body, **warm** it up, **act as a laxative, strengthen** the body, **and do not spoil** it, one might **say** that as dates are beneficial, he would not be harmed by eating too many. Nevertheless, it **teaches us** that they can also cause harm when eaten in excess.

The Gemara appends a *tanna*'s teaching: one who stuffs his stomach with any food whatsoever is likewise subject to *achilu*. Rav Pappa adds that this applies even if the overeating is with dates. The Gemara challenges this: is that not obvious, given that the teaching said "any food"? The Gemara explains that one might have thought dates are exempt, since the tradition praises them as a superfood — they cause satiety, they warm the body, they aid digestion, they strengthen, and they do not ruin the palate. One might therefore have supposed that dates, possessing all these remarkable properties, do not carry this risk. Rav Pappa therefore teaches us: even with dates, overstuffing oneself can bring on *achilu*.

### Achilu Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אֲחִילוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵשׁ שֶׁל עֲצָמוֹת. מַאי ״אֵשׁ שֶׁל עֲצָמוֹת״? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֵשׁ גַּרְמֵי. מַאי אָסוּתֵיהּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אֲמַרָה לִי אֵם: כּוּלְּהוּ שַׁקְיָינֵי – תְּלָתָא, וְשִׁבְעָא, וּתְרֵיסַר; וְהַאי – עַד דְּמִתַּסֵּי. כּוּלְּהוּ שַׁקְיָינֵי – אַלִּיבָּא רֵיקָנָא, וְהַאי – בָּתַר דְּאָכֵל, וְשָׁתֵי, וְעָיֵיל לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא, וְנָפֵיק וּמָשֵׁי יְדֵיהּ. וּמַיְיתוּ לֵיהּ בּוּנָא דִּשְׁתִיתָא דִּטְלָפְחֵי, וּבוּנָא דְּחַמְרָא עַתִּיקָא, וְנִיגְבְּלִינְהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי וְנֵיכוֹל; וְנִיכְרוֹךְ בִּסְדִינֵיהּ, וְנַיגְנְיֵהּ, וְלֵיכָּא דְּנוֹקְמֵיהּ עַד דְּקָאֵי מִנַּפְשֵׁיהּ; וְכִי קָאֵי, לִישְׁקְלֵיהּ לִסְדִינֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ, וְאִי לָא, הָדַר עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the affliction ***aḥilu*** mentioned here? **Rabbi Elazar says: A fire of the bones.** The Gemara asks: **What is a fire of the bones? Abaye said:** This is what is called ***esh garmei*** in Aramaic. **What is its remedy? Abaye said: My mother told me** that **any drink** consumed for medicinal purposes should be taken for either **three or seven or twelve** days, depending on what is necessary for that specific ailment. **And** if **this** is taken to treat *aḥilu* then he must drink it **until he is healed.** **Any drink** consumed for medicinal purposes should be consumed **with an empty heart,** i.e., without eating first. **And** for **this** disease of *aḥilu* he consumes the medicine **after he eats and drinks and enters the bathroom, and exits and washes his hands, and they bring him a fistful of *shetita*,** a type of food made **from lentils, and a fistful of aged wine. And let him mix them together and let him eat** this mixture. **And let him wrap himself with his sheet and sleep. And let there be no** one **who will awaken him until he awakens on his own. And when he awakens let him remove the sheet from himself. And if** he does **not** do this then the illness **will return to him.**

The Gemara asks: what is *achilu*? Rabbi Elazar explains it as *eshet shel atzamot* — a fire of the bones. Abaye then glosses that with *eshet garmi* — which is simply the Aramaic equivalent, a fire of the bones. The Soncino suggests this may be arthritis, and the logic is not implausible: the heavier one becomes from overeating, the harder the joints must work to support the additional weight, and the worse the arthritic condition becomes.

The Gemara asks: what is the remedy? Abaye reports in the name of his mother — understood to mean his nursemaid — a comprehensive principle: all medicinal drinks are typically administered for a set period of three, seven, or twelve days; but the treatment for this *achilu* must continue until it is fully healed. Moreover, while all other medicinal drinks are taken on an empty stomach, this one is different.

The treatment is as follows: after eating and drinking a meal, the patient goes to the bathroom, relieves himself, comes out, and washes his hands. He is then given a fistful of lentil powder (*shasisa*), some sort of lentil-salt porridge, together with a fistful of aged wine. These are kneaded together and eaten — on a full stomach, after relieving oneself. The patient then wraps himself in his blanket and lies down to sleep; nobody should wake him until he rises on his own. When he does get up, he must make sure to remove the blanket immediately. If the blanket stays on him even after he wakes, the illness will return.

### Two-Thirds Full

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אֵלִיָּהוּ לְרַבִּי נָתָן: אֱכוֹל שְׁלִישׁ וּשְׁתֵה שְׁלִישׁ וְהַנַּח שְׁלִישׁ – לִכְשֶׁתִּכְעוֹס, תַּעֲמוֹד עַל מִילּוּאֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Elijah** the prophet **said to Rabbi Natan: Eat a third** of your fill, **and drink a third** of your fill, **and leave a third** of your fill, so that **when you become angry you** will **become full.** If you do this, there will be room, as it were, for the anger. If you become angry when your stomach is full you will be harmed.

The Gemara records that Eliyahu advised Rav Nassan: eat a third of your capacity, drink another third, and leave the final third empty — do not fill yourself completely. The rationale given is that when you become angry, your anger fills your belly; if the stomach is already full, the resulting tension may cause internal damage. The broader principle is one of calorie restriction: it is not merely a question of managing weight, but a more fundamental teaching about longevity. Indeed, the Rambam likewise rules that one should not eat until full. Studies on calorie restriction in animal populations have consistently shown significant extensions of lifespan, and researchers believe the same principle, applied to humans, could dramatically extend the human lifespan.

### Stomach Health

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁלֹּא יָבֹא לִידֵי חוֹלִי מֵעַיִם, יְהֵא רָגִיל בְּטִיבּוּל – קַיִץ וָחוֹרֶף; סְעוּדָתְךָ שֶׁהֲנָאָתְךָ מִמֶּנָּה – מְשׁוֹךְ יָדְךָ הֵימֶנָּה; וְאַל תְּשַׁהֶה עַצְמְךָ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאַתָּה צָרִיךְ לִנְקָבֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches: One who does not want to come to** a situation whereby he contracts **intestinal disease** should **become accustomed to dipping** his food in wine or vinegar, both in the **summer** and in the **winter.** He also teaches: **You should remove your hand,** i.e., stop eating, **from a meal that you enjoy** so that you do not overeat. **And do not delay yourself at the time when it is necessary to relieve yourself.**

Rabbi Chiya taught in a *baraita*: one who wishes to avoid intestinal illness should make a habit of dipping foods in something — vinegar, oil, or the like — both in summer and in winter. At a meal that is particularly pleasurable, one should withdraw one's hand before eating one's fill. And one should never delay relieving oneself when the need arises. This last point is also recorded by the Rambam. These teachings from Rabbi Chiya belong to an earlier stratum than the extended anonymous remedies that opened the daf; their character is more that of general health advice than specific cures, and they are correspondingly more transparent and intuitive. The Rambam's general skepticism about the medical remedies in the Gemara — he regarded the rabbis as not being physicians, and therefore did not treat their remedies as normative — makes more sense when applied to the elaborate, often opaque prescriptions from earlier in the daf than to these straightforward principles. Notably, virtually none of this material appears in the *Shulchan Aruch*; whatever does survive in normative *halacha* was preserved selectively by the Rambam.

## Sexual Health

### White Wine Disease

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא: הַאי מַאן דְּשָׁתֵי טִילְיָא חִיוָּרָא – אֹחַזְתּוֹ וִיתֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Ukva said: This one who drinks inferior white wine [*tilya*]** will be **afflicted with weakness [*vitak*].**

Mar Ukva teaches that one who drinks inferior white wine (*tilaya chivra*) will be seized by a disease called *vitak*.

### Wine Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: שִׁיתִּין מִינֵי חַמְרָא הֲווֹ, מְעַלְּיָא דְּכוּלְּהוּ – סוּמָּקָא רֵיחָתָנָא, גְּרִיעָא דְּכוּלְּהוּ – טִילְיָא חִיוָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: There are sixty types of wine. The best of them all** is **red, fragrant** wine. **The worst of them all** is **inferior white wine.**

Rav Hisda elaborates: there are sixty types of wine in the world. The finest of all is red aromatic wine (*sumka richana*); the worst of all is precisely this inferior white wine.

### Morning Warmth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַאי מַאן דְּיָתֵיב בְּצַפְרָנֵי נִיסָן גַּבֵּי נוּרָא, וְשָׁיֵיף מִשְׁחָא, וְנָפֵיק וְיָתֵיב בְּשִׁמְשָׁא – אֹחַזְתּוֹ וִיתַּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: This one who sits near the fire during the mornings** in the month **of Nisan and rubs** himself **with oil and** then **goes out and sits in the sun** will be **afflicted with weakness.**

Rav Yehuda adds another cause of *vitak*: sitting in the mornings of Nisan beside a fire, rubbing oneself with oil, and then going outside to sit in the sun.

### Bloodletting Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִקִּיז דָּם וְשִׁימֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ – הוֹוִיין לוֹ בָּנִים וִיתְּקִין. הִקִּיזוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם וְשִׁימְּשׁוּ – הוֹוִיין לָהֶן בָּנִים בַּעֲלֵי רָאתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** One who **let blood and** afterward **engaged in sexual intercourse has weak children** conceived from those acts of intercourse. If **both of them,** husband and wife, **let blood and engaged in sexual intercourse they will have children afflicted** with a disease known as ***ra'atan*.

A *baraita* teaches: one who lets blood and then has intercourse will have children who are *vitakin* — weak, a general sapping of their constitution. The Gemara understands *vitak* to describe a condition of pronounced weakness, and sees it as reflecting the state of the parents at the time of conception: after bloodletting, the body's strength is depleted, and that depletion is somehow transmitted to the child. If both parents had let blood before intercourse, the children will be still weaker, afflicted with *ra'atan*.

### Eating Mitigation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא טָעֵים מִידֵּי, אֲבָל טָעֵים מִידֵּי – לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: We said** this **only** if **he did not taste anything** after letting blood. **But** if **he tasted something** then **we have no** problem **with it.**

Rav Pappa qualifies this: the concern applies only when the person did not eat anything after letting blood. If one ate something between the bloodletting and the intercourse, the problem does not arise — a sensible precaution, corresponding to the contemporary practice of providing food to blood donors immediately after donation.

### Travel Exhaustion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: בָּא מִן הַדֶּרֶךְ וְשִׁימֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ – הוֹוִיין לוֹ בָּנִים וִיתְּקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna says:** One who **came** back **from** traveling on **the road and engaged in sexual intercourse** immediately **has weak children** conceived from those acts of intercourse.

Rava bar Rav Huna adds that if one engages in intercourse immediately upon returning from a long journey — when the body has been worn down by the physical exertion of travel — the children will similarly be *vitakin*, weakened.

### Bathroom Waiting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַבָּא מִבֵּית הַכִּסֵּא, אַל יְשַׁמֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁהֶה שִׁיעוּר חֲצִי מִיל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשֵּׁד בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא מְלַוֶּה עִמּוֹ; וְאִם שִׁימֵּשׁ – הוֹוִיין לוֹ בָּנִים נִכְפִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who comes** in **from the bathroom,** he should **not engage in sexual intercourse until he waits the measure of** time it takes to walk **half a *mil* because the demon of the bathroom accompanies him. And if he engaged in sexual intercourse** without waiting this measure of time, **he has children** who are **epileptic.**

A *baraita* teaches that one who has just returned from the bathroom should not engage in intercourse until a time equivalent to walking half a *mil* has elapsed — roughly nine minutes or so. The reason given is that the *shed* of the bathhouse, the demon associated with that space, accompanies the person for a period of time after leaving. One should not engage in intimacy while that spirit is still attached.

One might ask how much of this translates into normative *halacha*. The principle *chamira sakanta me'isura* — that danger is treated more strictly than prohibition — would suggest these matters deserve serious consideration. However, a glance at the marginal notes confirms that virtually none of this is codified in the *Shulchan Aruch*, and the Rambam was highly selective.

### Standing Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְשַׁמֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ מְעוּמָּד – אוֹחַזְתּוֹ עֲוִית; מְיוּשָּׁב – אוֹחַזְתּוֹ דָּלָרְיָא; הִיא מִלְּמַעְלָה וְהוּא מִלְּמַטָּה – אוֹחַזְתּוֹ דָּלָרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One who engages in sexual intercourse** while **standing** will be **afflicted by spasms.** One who engages in sexual intercourse while **sitting** will be **afflicted with *dalarya*. **If **she,** the woman, is **above and he,** the husband, is **below** during sexual intercourse, then he will be **afflicted with *dalarya*.**

Another *baraita*: one who engages in intercourse while standing will be seized by *avit* — Rashi identifies this as *karnafa*, some form of spasms or cramps. If one does so while seated, one will be seized by *dulyaria* — delirium, or possibly the variant reading *uleria*. Similarly, if the woman is on top and the man below, the same *dulyaria* results. The Gemara notes that there are other discussions elsewhere concerning the effects of different positions on the nature of children conceived; here the concern is specifically for the health of the participants.

### Delirium Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי דָּלָרְיָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: סַם דָּלָרְיָא – דַּרְדָּרָא. מַאי דַּרְדָּרָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מוֹרִיקָא דְחוֹחֵי. רַב פָּפָּא אָלֵיס וּבָלַע לֵיהּ. רַב פַּפֵּי אָלֵיס וְשָׁדֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is ***dalarya*? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** The **remedy for *dalarya*** is ***dardara*.** The Gemara asks: **What** is ***dardara*? Abaye said: Saffron of thorns. Rav Pappa** would **chew [*aleis*] and swallow** this remedy. **Rav Pappi** would **chew and spit it** out.

The Gemara asks: what is *dulyaria*? Rather than defining it, the Gemara provides the remedy: Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says the cure for *dulyaria* is *dardura*. Abaye explains that *dardura* is *morika dechuchei* — a type of saffron that grows among thorns. Rav Pappa would chew it and swallow it; Rav Pappi would chew it and spit it out.

### Sexual Weakness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּקִי בְּדֶרֶךְ אֶרֶץ, לֵיתֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה קְפִיזֵי קוּרְטְמֵי דְחוֹחֵי, וְנֵידוּקִינְהוּ, וְנִישְׁלְקִינְהוּ בְּחַמְרָא וְנִישְׁתֵּי. אֲמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֵן הֵן הֶחְזִירוּנִי לְנַעֲרוּתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says:** As a remedy for **one who is not an expert,** i.e., does not have strength, **in** the **way of the world,** i.e., in sexual intercourse, **let him bring three vessels [*kefizei*],** each containing three-quarters of a *log* of **safflower thorns. And let him grind them, and boil them in wine, and drink** the mixture. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: These are** the remedies that **return me to my youth** with regard to sexual intercourse.

Abaye then teaches: one who is not capable (*baki bederech eretz*) — which Rashi understands to mean one who is too weak for intercourse — should take three *kafizim* of *kortamei dechuchei*, which Rashi again identifies as a saffron variety, growing from soil that has been fertilized with sheep dung. Tosafot explains that three *kafizim* equals three-quarters of a *log*. This is ground, boiled in wine, and drunk. Rabbi Yochanan testifies: this is what restored me to the strength of my youth — and Rashi specifies that this refers to his capacity for sexual intercourse.

## Health Principles

### Three Weakeners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים מַכְחִישִׁים כֹּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: פַּחַד, דֶּרֶךְ וְעָוֹן. פַּחַד – דִּכְתִיב: ״לִבִּי סְחַרְחַר עֲזָבַנִי כֹחִי״. דֶּרֶךְ – דִּכְתִיב: ״עִנָּה בַדֶּרֶךְ כֹּחִי״. עָוֹן – דִּכְתִיב: ״כָּשַׁל בַּעֲוֹנִי כֹחִי״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Three things diminish a person's strength, and they are: Fear,** traveling on the **road, and sin.** The Gemara explains: **Fear, as it is written: "My heart flutters, my strength fails me"** (Psalms 38:11). Traveling on the **road, as it is written: "He has weakened my strength on the road"** (Psalms 102:24). **Sin, as it is written: "My strength fails because of my sin"** (Psalms 31:11).

The Gemara now moves to a series of general health principles structured as numbered lists. Three things weaken a person's strength: *pachad* — anxiety or fear; *derech* — travel; and *avon* — sin, or, as Tosafot suggests, anxiety over sin. Each is supported by a *pasuk*: for anxiety, "my heart has fluttered, my strength has abandoned me"; for travel, "God has sapped my strength on the path"; for sin, "my strength has stumbled through my sin."

### Three Shattering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים מַתִּיזִין גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: אָכַל מְעוּמָּד, וְשָׁתָה מְעוּמָּד, וְשִׁימֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ מְעוּמָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Three things break a person's body, and they are:** If he **ate** while **standing,** if he **drank** while **standing, and** if he **engaged in sexual intercourse** while **standing.**

Three things shatter the body: eating while standing, drinking while standing, and engaging in intercourse while standing.

### Five Fatal Things

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמִשָּׁה קְרוֹבִין לְמִיתָה יוֹתֵר מִן הַחַיִּים, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: אָכַל וְעָמַד, שָׁתָה וְעָמַד, הִקִּיז דָּם וְעָמַד, יָשֵׁן וְעָמַד, שִׁימֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ וְעָמַד.
>
> **Translation:** There are **five** actions that bring one **closer to death than** to **life, and they are:** If he **ate and stood** up immediately, if he **drank and stood** up immediately, if he **let blood and stood** up immediately, if he **slept and stood** up immediately, and if he **engaged in sexual intercourse and stood** up immediately.

Five things bring a person closer to death than to life: eating and then immediately standing up, drinking and then immediately standing up, letting blood and then immediately standing up, sleeping and then suddenly getting up, and having intercourse and then immediately standing up. The implication is not necessarily literal death, but that one will feel devastatingly ill.

### Six Lethal Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁשָּׁה, הָעוֹשֶׂה אוֹתָן – מִיָּד מֵת, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: הַבָּא בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְנִתְיַיגַּע, הִקִּיז דָּם, וְנִכְנַס לְבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ, וְשָׁתָה וְנִשְׁתַּכֵּר, וְיָשַׁן עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע, וְשִׁימֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהוּא שֶׁעֲשָׂאָן כְּסִידְרָן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who performs** the following acts, if he performs the **six** of **them** consecutively **he dies immediately, and they are:** If **one came** back **from** a journey **on the road and was exhausted, let blood, and entered the bathhouse, and drank and became intoxicated, and slept on the ground, and engaged in sexual intercourse,** then he will die. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: But he** will die for certain only in the case **where he performs them in this order.**

Six acts, performed in sequence, bring about actual death: coming home exhausted from a journey, letting blood, entering a bathhouse, drinking, becoming intoxicated, sleeping on the ground, and then having intercourse. If one performs all six in precisely that order, the outcome is death.

### Order Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּסִידְרָן – מֵת, שֶׁלֹּא כְּסִידְרָן – חָלֵישׁ. אִינִי?! וְהָא מְעוּרַת עֲבַדָה לֵיהּ לְעַבְדַּהּ תְּלָת מִינַּיְיהוּ, וּמִית! הָהוּא כָּחוּשׁ הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** If he performs these actions **in this order he will die.** But if he performs them **out of order he will become weak.** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't** a woman named **Me'oret make her slave** perform **three of these** actions **and he died** as a result? The Gemara answers: **That** slave **was weak,** which is why he died. But an ordinary individual would die only upon performing all of these acts in the previously mentioned order.

Rabbi Yochanan qualifies: it must be in exactly that order. Abaye adds: in that order, one dies; in a different order, one merely becomes seriously weakened. The Gemara then asks whether this is really true and cites a case: a woman named Me'arat did three of these things to her servant and he died. The Gemara deflects: that servant was already of a weak constitution to begin with.

### Eight Moderation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוֹנָה – רוּבָּן קָשֶׁה וּמִיעוּטָן יָפֶה, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: דֶּרֶךְ, וְדֶרֶךְ אֶרֶץ, עוֹשֶׁר, וּמְלָאכָה, יַיִן, וְשֵׁינָה, חַמִּין, וְהַקָּזַת דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Eight** actions are **difficult** for the body and the soul to handle **in large** amounts **and** are **beneficial in small** amounts, **and they are:** Traveling on the **road,** engaging in the **way of the world,** i.e., engaging in sexual intercourse, having **wealth, work,** drinking **wine, sleep, hot water, and bloodletting.**

Eight things are harmful in large quantities but beneficial in small amounts: travel; *derech eretz* (sexual intercourse); wealth; work; wine; sleep; hot water (both for bathing and drinking, according to Rashi); and bloodletting. One might wonder whether the inclusion of wealth and work is a health statement or a moral-religious one. A little wealth is good — enough to meet one's needs and have modest comfort. Too much wealth, however, leads to indulgence and, critically, crowds out *Torah* study. Similarly, a certain amount of work is healthy; excessive work is harmful.

### Eight Semen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוֹנָה מְמַעֲטִים אֶת הַזֶּרַע, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: הַמֶּלַח, וְהָרָעָב, וְהַנֶּתֶק, בְּכִיָּיה, וְשֵׁינָה עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע, וְגֻדְגְּדָנִיּוֹת, וּכְשׁוּת שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנָּהּ, וְהַקָּזַת דָּם לְמַטָּה – כִּפְלַיִם. תָּנָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁקָּשָׁה לְמַטָּה כִּפְלַיִם, כָּךְ יָפָה לְמַעְלָה – כִּפְלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Eight** actions or illnesses **decrease the semen, and they are: Salt, hunger,** a skin disease called ***netek*, crying, sleeping on the ground,** the **melilot** plant, **and dodder** eaten **not in its time,** i.e., before it is ripe. **And bloodletting** performed **below,** on the lower portion of the body, causes **twice** as much harm as the other actions mentioned. The Sage **taught: Just as** bloodletting **below** causes **twice** as much **harm, so too,** bloodletting **above,** on the upper portion of the body, is **twice** as **effective.** 

A *baraita* confirms: just as lower-body bloodletting is doubly harmful, upper-body bloodletting is doubly beneficial.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַטָּה – לְמַטָּה מִן הַבֵּיצִים, לְמַעְלָה – לְמַעְלָה מִן הַבֵּיצִים. וּכְשׁוּת שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנָּהּ. תָּנָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁקָּשָׁה שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנָּהּ, כָּךְ יָפָה בִּזְמַנָּהּ. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: זְמַנָּהּ – תַּמּוּז, שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנָּהּ – טֵבֵת, יוֹמֵי נִיסָן וְיוֹמֵי תִּשְׁרֵי – לָא מְעַלּוּ וְלָא קָשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** When the Gemara mentioned letting blood from **below** it meant **below the testicles,** and when it mentioned letting blood from **above** it meant **above the testicles.** It was taught: **And dodder** eaten **not in its time,** before it is ripe, causes harm. The Gemara explains: A Sage **taught: Just as** eating dodder **not in its time** is **harmful** to the body, **so too,** eating it **in its time,** when it is ripe, is **good** for the body. **Rav Pappa said: Its time** is **Tammuz,** in the summer, and **not in its time** is **Tevet,** in the winter. And during **the days of Nisan,** spring, **and the days of Tishrei,** autumn, **they neither help nor harm.**

Rav Pappa defines the dividing line as the testicles. Regarding hops specifically: the Gemara teaches that just as hops out of season (*shelo bizmanah*) are harmful, hops in their proper season (*bizmanah*) are beneficial for the body. Rav Pappa defines the proper season as Tammuz — summer; the improper season as Teves — winter; and the months of Nisan and Tishrei — spring and fall — as neutral, neither beneficial nor harmful.

## Get & Seizure

### Mishna Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: ״כִּתְבוּ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתִּי״, וַאֲחָזוֹ קוּרְדְּיָיקוֹס, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר: ״אַל תִּכְתְּבוּ״ – אֵין דְּבָרָיו הָאַחֲרוֹנִים כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to its discussion of the *halakhot* mentioned in the mishna. The mishna teaches: If **he said: Write a bill of divorce for my wife,** when he was lucid, **and was** then **afflicted with** temporary **insanity and he retracted** his previous statement **and said: Do not write it, his latter statement** is considered to be **nothing,** i.e., it is not halakhically valid.

The Gemara now returns to the *mishna*. The case: a husband instructs that a *get* be written for his wife, then is seized by *kordaikos*, and in that state retracts his instruction, saying not to write it. The *mishna* rules: his last words mean nothing. This is a striking ruling. It is one thing to say that the retraction during the seizure carries no weight. It is quite another — and a significant *chiddush* — to say that we proceed without any concern whatsoever for that retraction, and that the wife will be *vadai megureshet*, definitively divorced. The implication is that statements made during a *kordaikos* episode are entirely disregarded; the person in that state is not considered a *bar daas* whose expressed will has legal significance.

### Write Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כּוֹתְבִין וְנוֹתְנִין גֵּט לְאַלְתַּר. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין אֶלָּא לִכְשֶׁיִּשְׁתַּפֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments on this that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** In that case the court **writes and gives the bill of divorce immediately,** because even though he is insane, the court does not wait for him to return to his senses. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** They **write** this bill of divorce based on his instructions **only once he is healed** and returns to a sound state of mind.

Reish Lakish holds: the *get* is written and delivered immediately — even while the husband is still in the midst of the *kordaikos* episode — on the basis of his original instruction before the fit. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: the *get* is written only once he recovers. Rabbi Yochanan does not mean that after recovery one must ask him again whether he still wishes to give a *get* — if that were so, the *mishna*'s ruling that the retraction counts for nothing would be superfluous. Rather, Rabbi Yochanan holds that the *get* can only be executed when the husband is a *bar daas*, a person of sound legal mind. His retraction during the episode is null, but the *get* itself cannot be written or given while he lacks that status.

The mechanism underlying Rabbi Yochanan's position is the principle of *shelichus*: an agent (*shaliach*) acts on behalf of the principal, and the rule is *kol milsa de'ihu lo matzi avid, shaliach lo matzi avid* — whatever the principal himself could not do, the agent cannot do on his behalf. This refers not to physical incapacity but to *halachic* incapacity. The husband, during his *kordaikos* episode, lacks the halachic standing to deliver a *get* himself; therefore his agent cannot deliver it for him either. Notably, this is different from physical paralysis: one who is physically unable to hand over the *get* can still appoint an agent, because physical disability does not affect one's *halachic* capacity. Only a deficit in *halachic* capacity — in *da'at* — prevents the use of an agent.

### Sleeping vs Shote

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מְדַמֵּי לֵיהּ לְיָשֵׁן, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מְדַמֵּי לֵיהּ לְשׁוֹטֶה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן נָמֵי לִידַמְּיֵהּ לְיָשֵׁן! יָשֵׁן – לָא מְחוּסָּר מַעֲשֶׂה, הַאי – מְחוּסָּר מַעֲשֶׂה. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ נָמֵי נִידַמְּיֵיהּ לְשׁוֹטֶה! שׁוֹטֶה לָא סַמֵּיהּ בִּידַן, הַאי, סַמֵּיהּ בִּידַן – בִּשְׂרָא סוּמָּקָא אַגּוּמְרֵי, וְחַמְרָא מַרְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do** Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yoḥanan **disagree? Reish Lakish compares** one afflicted with temporary insanity **to one who is sleeping.** If one said to write a bill of divorce and went to sleep then the court may write it without waiting for him to awaken. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan compares him to an imbecile:** When he is afflicted with temporary insanity he is not of sound mind and is therefore unfit to give a bill of divorce. The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan also could compare him to one who is sleeping,** so why does he not do so? The Gemara answers: **One who is sleeping is not lacking an action,** meaning that no action is needed in order to awaken him, and he can awaken on his own. **This** one, who is afflicted with temporary insanity, is **lacking an action** by not taking the remedy mentioned earlier. The Gemara asks: **And Reish Lakish could also compare him to an imbecile,** so why does he not do so? The Gemara answers: There is a difference, as **there is no remedy in our possession** that can cure **an imbecile.** And since there is no remedy, a bill of divorce may not be written on his behalf. By contrast, for **this** one, who is afflicted with temporary insanity, **there is a remedy in our possession.** As the Gemara explained (67b): The remedy for this disease is for the afflicted person to eat lean **red meat** roasted **over coals and** drink **wine** that has been **diluted** with a large amount of water.

What is the root of the dispute? Reish Lakish compares the *kordaikos* victim to a sleeping person. A person who is asleep is temporarily unconscious and not in control of his *da'at*, but *begavra* — as a legal entity — he remains a *bar daas*. Rabbi Yochanan compares him to a *shote*, one who is genuinely of unsound mind and who is not considered a *bar daas* as a personal status.

The Gemara probes: why does Rabbi Yochanan not simply compare the case to a sleeping person? Because a person who is sleeping is not lacking any further act (*lo mechusar ma'aseh*) — he will simply wake up naturally, and his current unconsciousness does not change his legal status. The *kordaikos* patient, by contrast, is *mechusar maysa* — he lacks a treatment; his condition will not resolve on its own. That dependency on external treatment is what defines his status.

Conversely, why does Reish Lakish not compare the case to a *shote*? Because a *shote*'s condition is not in our hands to cure (*lo sami b'yadan*). The *shote* has no available remedy; his diminished status is effectively permanent or at least beyond our intervention. The *kordaikos* patient, however, does have a remedy in our hands (*sami b'yadan*): and here the earlier material pays off. The Gemara has just spent considerable time establishing a treatment for *kordaikos* — fatty roasted meat and diluted wine. The point of all that earlier medical material now becomes clear: we know that this condition is treatable, and therefore it does not define the person's status as a permanent *gavra* who lacks *da'at*. This is a fascinating conceptual question: at what point does a person's current state of mind define their *halachic* status as a legal entity, rather than merely representing a temporary condition that does not affect who they are?

### Treatability Test

The Gemara then considers a practical implication of Rabbi Yochanan's position. If the husband gives the instruction to write the *get*, is then seized by *kordaikos*, and never recovers, the *get* can according to Rabbi Yochanan never be delivered — the wife remains in legal limbo indefinitely. That consequence is precisely correct on Rabbi Yochanan's view.

The Gemara also notes that there is no expiration on the original *tzivuy* — the husband's instruction. As long as he has not retracted it while he was a *bar daas*, the instruction stands. The difficulty arises only at the moment of delivery: if at that moment the husband is not a *bar daas* as a status, no delivery can take place on his behalf.

## Mortal Wounds

### Slit Throat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שָׁחַט בּוֹ שְׁנַיִם, אוֹ רוֹב שְׁנַיִם, וְרָמַז וְאָמַר: ״כִּתְבוּ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתִּי״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ יִכְתְּבוּ וְיִתְּנוּ; וְתַנְיָא: רָאוּהוּ מְגוּיָּד אוֹ צָלוּב עַל הַצְּלִיבָה, וְרָמַז וְאָמַר: ״כִּתְבוּ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתִּי״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ יִכְתְּבוּ וְיִתְּנוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say this,** that the court must wait for him to regain his mental capabilities? **But didn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Shmuel says:** If one was attacked by another who **slit** his throat and severed the **two** pipes, his trachea and esophagus, **or the majority of** the **two** pipes, **and** the dying man **signaled and** thereby **stated** through his gestures: **Write a bill of divorce for my wife,** then **those** present **should write and give** a bill of divorce to his wife? **And it was** similarly **taught** in a *baraita*: If **they saw** a man whose limbs had been **severed or crucified on a cross, and he signaled and** thereby **stated: Write a bill of divorce for my wife,** then **those** present **should write and give** the document to his wife. This teaches that it is permitted to write a bill of divorce even on behalf of one who cannot be cured and will certainly die. If so, why does Rabbi Yochanan claim that a bill of divorce may be written for someone afflicted with temporary insanity only once he has been cured?

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* cited by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: if a person has had both his trachea and esophagus slit — or the majority of each — and he indicates (by gesture, since he presumably cannot speak) that a *get* should be written for his wife, it may be written and delivered. The clear motivation is to make the wife a *grusha* rather than an *almanah*, so that she need not worry about *yibum*.

This prompts a question on Rabbi Yochanan's position. Just as the *kordaikos* patient is *mechusar maysa* — lacking a treatment — so too is the person with a slit throat: his condition is certainly fatal and will not resolve. Should we not therefore say he lacks the status of a *bar daas* and the *get* cannot be written? Moreover, a further *baraita* teaches that even a person who was witnessed in a dismembered state (*meguyod*) or crucified (*tzaluv*), and who indicates that a *get* be written, may have the *get* written and delivered. These appear to be cases where the person is unambiguously dying and — one might think — will imminently lose coherent *da'at*.

### Distinguishing Confusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם דַּעְתָּא צִילּוּתָא הִיא, וּכְחִישׁוּתָא הוּא דְּאַתְחִילָה בֵּיהּ; הָכָא דַּעְתָּא שְׁגִישְׁתָּא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** after his throat was slit or he was crucified, **his mind is lucid, but he has begun** to feel **weakness** and will die very soon. Consequently, he cannot speak, but his intellectual capabilities are assumed to be intact. But **here,** in the case of one afflicted with temporary insanity, **his mind is confused,** and he is not lucid enough to act with intent.

The Gemara's answer is that these cases are distinguishable from *kordaikos*. In the case of the dying person — whether from a slit throat or from crucifixion — *chaziman da'atei tziluta*: we observe that their mind remains clear. Even if the person is physically incapacitated, wracked by pain, or unable to speak clearly, this is attributed to *kechishuta* — bodily weakness — rather than to any confusion or loss of mental capacity. The dying person retains *da'at* even as their body fails. The *kordaikos* patient is different: *da'atei shagishta* — his mind is actively confused. He is awake, but his thinking is disordered. It is this confusion of the mind — not physical weakness — that removes his status as a *bar daas*. Hence the cases of the mortally wounded, the dismembered, and the crucified are all in a different category and do not challenge Rabbi Yochanan's ruling.

### Death Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שָׁחַט בּוֹ שְׁנַיִם אוֹ רוֹב שְׁנַיִם, וּבָרַח – מְעִידִין עָלָיו. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ חַי הוּא, אַמַּאי מְעִידִין עָלָיו? אָמְרִי: חַי הוּא – וְסוֹפוֹ לָמוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But did Shmuel** actually **say this,** that someone whose throat has been slit is treated as though he is alive, and he can give a bill of divorce for his wife? **But didn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Shmuel says:** If someone **slit** the **two** pipes **in** his throat **or the majority of** the **two** pipes **and** the victim **fled** without the witnesses seeing what ultimately happened to him, then **they may testify with regard to him** that he is dead? **And if it enters your mind** to say that one whose throat has been slit **is alive** and able to instruct others to write a bill of divorce for his wife, then **why can** witnesses **testify with regard to him** that he is dead? The Gemara **says** in response to this that **he is** currently **alive** and on account of this he can give a bill of divorce to his wife, **but ultimately** he will certainly **die** within a short period of time. Consequently, it is possible to testify with certainty that he died later on.

This leads to a question on Shmuel's own position. Rav Yehuda cites Shmuel elsewhere: if a person has had both trachea and esophagus slit and then fled — presumably on a wagon — testimony can be given that he is dead and his wife may remarry. Does that not imply that once the throat is slit, the person is *halachically* already dead? If so, how can a *get* be written on his behalf?

The Gemara answers: the person is in the state of *chai hu vesofu lamut* — alive, but certain to die. The testimony about his death does not mean he is dead at this instant; it means that given his condition, witnesses may reliably testify after the fact that he did indeed die, without needing to observe the actual moment of death. As long as he is still visibly alive, he is *halachically* alive, and a *get* can be written.

### No Exile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, יְהֵא גּוֹלֶה עַל יָדוֹ! אַלְּמָה תַּנְיָא: שָׁחַט בּוֹ שְׁנַיִם אוֹ רוֹב שְׁנַיִם – הֲרֵי זֶה אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that he is assumed to have ultimately died, then the one who slit his throat **should be exiled on his account** if he did so unintentionally. Exile is the punishment for unintentionally killing another. If so, **why is it taught** in a *baraita*: If **he** unintentionally **slit** the **two** pipes **in** his throat **or the majority of** the **two,** for example if one dropped a knife and accidentally cut another's trachea and esophagus, then **this** one **is not exiled?**

This raises a further question. If the person is definitively going to die, why is the one who accidentally slits his throat exempt from *galus* — exile, the penalty for unintentional killing? If the death is certain, the causal chain from the act to the death seems unbroken, and exile ought to apply.

### Contributing Factors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא הָרוּחַ בִּלְבְּלַתּוּ. אִי נָמֵי, הוּא קֵירַב אֶת מִיתָתוֹ. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּשַׁחְטֵיהּ בְּבֵיתָא דְשֵׁישָׁא, וּפַירְכֵּס. אִי נָמֵי, דְּשַׁחְטֵיהּ בְּבָרָא, וְלָא פַּירְכֵּס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Wasn't it stated with regard to that** *baraita* that **Rabbi Hoshaya says: We are concerned that perhaps the wind made him senseless,** and it was not only the one who dropped the knife who caused this man's death, but also the wind or some other factor. One who only partially causes the death of another is not exiled. **Or also** perhaps **he,** the one who had his throat slit, **brought his death closer** through his convulsions and his death throes. **What is** the difference **between** the first and second explanations with regard to why the killer is not exiled? The difference **between** them **is if he slit his** throat **in a marble house and** the victim **convulsed.** In such a case the death cannot be attributed to the wind, so according to the first explanation the killer would be exiled. Rather, his death can be attributed to his convulsions, so according to the latter explanation the killer would not be exiled. **Alternatively, he slit his** throat **outside and** the victim **did not convulse.** In such a case the death can be attributed to the wind, so according to the first explanation the killer would not be exiled. Since it cannot be attributed to his convulsions, according to the latter explanation the killer would be exiled.

The Gemara cites Rabbi Oshaya: even though death from a slit throat is certain, there may be contributing factors that complicate the causal attribution. Wind entering the wound may have accelerated the death (*ruach bilbelato*); or the victim himself, by trembling and convulsing, may have hastened his own end (*hu kirev et mitato*). The Gemara asks: what would be a case distinguishing these two possibilities? If the slitting took place in a sealed marble chamber — where no wind could enter — then the wind cannot be a contributing factor; only the shaking would be relevant. If it took place outdoors but the victim did not convulse, then the wind might be a factor, but the victim's own movements cannot.

Tosafot raises an important objection: if someone intentionally slits a person's throat, surely they are liable for murder and execution, regardless of whether the wound got infected or the wind sped up the death. Tosafot resolves this by noting that for *galus* — as opposed to execution — the halachic framework is significantly narrower. The categories eligible for *galus* require a specific configuration: not too close to *shogeg* that it borders on accident, not too close to *meizid* that it approaches intention. Beyond the specific requirements of proximity to a ladder and similar conditions, the principle can be understood through the concept of *misasek*: the classic case of unintentional killing eligible for exile is one where the person did not truly perform a directed *ma'aseh* of killing. He was chopping wood and the ax head flew; that is *misasek* — his act did not make contact with the victim in the directed sense.

In the case of unintentionally slitting a throat, if we work forward from the perpetrator's act, then the act itself constitutes a *ma'aseh retzichah* — a mortal wounding — and had it been intentional, execution would follow. But *galus* is established not from the forward direction but in reverse: we see that someone has died, and we trace responsibility back to this individual, who caused it without intent. Because we are working in reverse, any contributing factor — even one that logically would have occurred in any case, such as natural convulsing — suffices to break the direct line of attribution, and the person escapes *galus*. This framework elegantly accounts for why unintentional throat-slitting does not result in exile, even though the death is certain: the absence of a clean, unmediated causal chain, once we reason from outcome back to act, means that contributing factors remain relevant.
