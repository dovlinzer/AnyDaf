# Ketubot 56b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## R. Meir: Ketubah Stipula…

### Yehuda vs. Meir on Waiver

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion about the respective positions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir regarding whether the obligations of the *ketubah* can be modified. The prior context established that Rabbi Yehuda allows a woman to be *mochel* — to waive — part of the *ketubah*, so that effectively the operative amount falls below the standard 200 *zuz* for a *betulah* or 100 for an *almanah*. However, Rabbi Yehuda does not permit the amount to be stipulated as less than those figures from the outset. The *Gemara* had asked: why not? He could say that one is permitted to be *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah* — to modify the parameters of the monetary aspects of biblical obligations of *kiddushin*, such as *shear* and *kesut* (though not *onah*). So why can he not modify a rabbinic one? Rabbi Yehuda's answer is that the rabbis wanted to strengthen the rabbinic institution of the *ketubah* — *asu chizuk l'divreihem yoter mishel Torah* — and therefore the base obligation must remain in place, even if the monetary obligations of the Torah could otherwise be modified. The woman must start with 200 or 100; only afterwards may she be *mochel*.

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge from the case of donkey drivers (*chamarin*): we were taught in the *Mishnah* that if merchants come to a city and one says *sheli chadash v'shel chaveirai yashan* — mine is new grain (and therefore inferior) and my friend's is old — or that his goods are untithed while his companion's are *metukan* with *terumos u'maasros* separated, they are not believed (*einam ne'emanin*). But Rabbi Yehuda says they are believed (*ne'emanin*). The issue concerns *demai* — the rabbinic concern that an *am ha'aretz* may not have separated *terumos u'maasros*. Why does Rabbi Yehuda not reinforce this rabbinic institution as he reinforces the *ketubah*?

Abaye answers: the rabbis reinforced only what was a definite (*vadai*) institution. The *ketubah* obligations are definite, and therefore the rabbis said they cannot be directly modified. The concern about *terumos u'maasros* in the case of *demai*, however, is inherently based on doubt — it is only a *safek* — and something that is fundamentally weaker in that way was not reinforced in the same fashion. Rava adds a further point: it is not merely that *demai* is based on doubt in general, but specifically *demai* has many leniencies because in principle we actually believe that *terumos u'maasros* were separated. Given that, the rabbis did not bother reinforcing it.

The bottom line of this section is that Rabbi Yehuda is less strict than Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir does not allow the woman to be *mochel* any part of the *ketubah* at all — the effective obligation for Rabbi Meir must be the full 200 or 100. Rabbi Yehuda allows a woman to be *mochel* after the fact but does not allow the amount to be modified at the outset, because even though he permits modification of the biblical monetary obligations of marriage, this rabbinic one he wishes to reinforce, and therefore the base obligation must stand.

### Stipulation Nullity Theo…

Turning to Rabbi Meir's position as it appears in the *Mishnah* — *kol hapocheis l'betulah mi'masayim u'l'almanah mi'maneh harei zu biat zenut* — one might initially have understood Rabbi Meir as saying that you cannot effectively make the obligation less, and that continuing to live in such a marital state constitutes something closer to *zenut* than to marriage. The whole weight of marriage — the *ketubah*, the reciprocal obligations and protections — is what gives *nissu'in* its character. So the simple reading of Rabbi Meir is that you may have lowered the amount but you transgress.

The *Gemara*, however, takes this one step further. From the word *hapocheis* — one who is *attempting* to reduce the amount — the *Gemara* infers *afilu b'tnai*, even if you explicitly stipulate that it should be less. *Alma kasavar tnai batel* — it seems that Rabbi Meir holds the stipulation itself is void. This is not a simple inference from the *Mishnah*, as the *Gemara* acknowledges and *Tosafos* addresses at length, but the *Gemara* assumes it as Rabbi Meir's position: the attempt to make the *ketubah* less does not succeed. The woman retains the full amount; *v'is lei* — she still has her 200.

### Biat Zenut Analysis

But if the stipulation is void and the woman still has the full amount in principle, how can the situation constitute *biat zenut*? The *Gemara* answers: *ve'cheivan de'amar lah leis lich ela maneh* — since the husband told her that she only has 100, *lo samcha daatah* — her sense of security is not established. Even though in truth and in law she has 200, her subjective sense that she is unprotected — because she believes she only has 100 — is sufficient to create *biat zenut*. *V'hava lah biatah biat zenut* — and therefore the relations take on the character of *zenut*. This is a striking position: even a legally full *ketubah* does not prevent the designation of *biat zenut* when the woman subjectively believes she lacks protection.

### Ketubah d'Oraisa

The *Gemara* then asks why the stipulation is void at all. It notes that we know Rabbi Meir holds that if you try to stipulate against a Torah obligation, even a financial one, the condition is *batel* — but *habderabbanan tnai kayyam* — specifically against a rabbinic obligation, an explicit stipulation would be binding. So why can you not stipulate against the *ketubah*? Could one not simply say that Rabbi Meir is reinforcing the *ketubah* institution in a special way, given that the stakes are marriage and *biat zenut*?

The *Gemara* does not take that route. Rather than treating the *ketubah* as uniquely protected among rabbinic enactments, the *Gemara* says: *kasavar Rabbi Meir ketubah de'Oraisa* — Rabbi Meir may actually hold that the *ketubah* is a biblical obligation. That debate appeared earlier in the *masechet*. And since it is biblical, one cannot stipulate against it — *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah* — and therefore the stipulation is void. This is a significant *chiddush*. Rabbi Meir, who in the *Mishnah* seems merely to be saying that you transgress by effectively lowering the amount, is reinterpreted by the *Gemara* to mean that the attempt does not even succeed — because the *ketubah* is *d'Oraisa*, and it is impossible to stipulate against a biblical financial obligation. And even though the amount therefore remains in full, the situation is still *biat zenut* because the woman believes otherwise.

## Three Positions: Stipula…

### R. Yosi: Allowed

A *baraisa* now presents all three positions. Rabbi Meir says: *kol hapocheis l'besulah mi'masayim u'l'almanah mi'maneh harei zu biat zenut* — any attempt to lower the amount from the base is *biat zenut*. Rabbi Yosi says *rashai* — it is permitted. Rabbi Yosi goes one step further than Rabbi Yehuda: not only may the woman be *mochel* after starting with the base amount, but the parties may stipulate from the outset for a lesser amount.

### R. Yehuda: Waiver Method

Rabbi Yehuda occupies the middle position. *Rotzeh koseiv l'vesulah shtar shel masayim v'hi koseves lo hikkabalti mimcha maneh* — the husband writes a *ketubah* for 200 and she writes a receipt acknowledging she has received 100. For an *almanah*, he writes 100 and she writes that she received 50. So Rabbi Yehuda requires the full base amount to be the starting point, but then permits the woman to effectively reduce it through a receipt — an indirect *mechilah* rather than a direct stipulation.

### R. Meir: Stipulation Void

Rabbi Meir's position remains as analyzed above: the stipulation fails entirely, and even the attempt to lower the amount creates *biat zenut*. The *Gemara* now asks how Rabbi Yosi — who showed himself so protective of the woman's interests, as we are about to see — could permit a lesser amount from the outset.

## Chattel Designation Disp…

### Chattel Prohibition

The *Gemara* introduces a *braisa*: *ein osin kesubas ishah mitaltlin mipnei tikkun ha-olam* — you do not designate *metaltalim*, movable property — chattel — as the lien on which the *ketubah* will be collected, for the sake of the benefit of society (*tikkun ha-olam*). This is not a reduction of the woman's liens on real estate — the standard formulation *kol nichasai achrayin*, which is a *tenai beit din*, remains — but rather a prohibition on affirmatively designating specific movable property as the designated source of collection. The *tikkun ha-olam* reason: chattel can be destroyed, lose value, or be lost, whereas real estate cannot be stolen away. If you designate only the Wedgwood China and it breaks, the woman is left with nothing.

### R. Yosi: Liability Insuf…

Rabbi Yosi objects: *v'chi mah tikkun ha-olam yesh bazeh* — what societal benefit is served by this? *Vehalo ein kesuvos u'pochsin* — does not the value of such property diminish? At first glance this seems to simply restate what the *Tanna Kamma* already said. The *Gemara* observes: *Tanna Kamma nami ein osin ka'amar* — the *Tanna Kamma* also said you are not allowed to make such a designation. So what precisely is their dispute?

### Unilateral vs. Mutual

The *Gemara* resolves this: the *braisa* must be reread as follows. *Bamah devarim amurim* — when is the designation of chattel as *ketubah* lien prohibited? *Beshelo kibel alav achrayus* — when the husband does not accept liability for its value, meaning the woman can only collect from the chattel itself. In such a case, if it breaks she is left with nothing. But *aval kibel alav achrayus* — if the husband backs it up by accepting personal liability for its worth even if the chattel is destroyed — then *osin*, you are allowed to designate it.

Rabbi Yosi then comes along and says: that is not sufficient protection. *Ki kibel alav achrayus amai osin* — even if you accept liability for breakage, why should that solve the problem? *Vehalo ein kesuvos u'pochsin* — the chattel may depreciate in value without breaking. Even if the China is intact when she comes to collect, it may be worth far less than her *ketubah* amount. So Rabbi Yosi holds that accepting liability for breakage does not fully protect the woman's interests when the value of the chattel itself may have declined.

The *Gemara* then uses this to ask the obvious question: if Rabbi Yosi is so concerned to protect the woman's interests that he objects even to a scenario where the husband accepts liability for breakage, how can he permit an outright stipulation for a lesser amount? The *Gemara* answers: *hachi hashta* — what kind of comparison is that? In the chattel case, *hasam lo yade'ah detimchol* — the woman does not know she is conceding. She is demanding her full 200 and the husband is unilaterally setting up a scenario in which she might not be able to collect the full amount. That is one-sided and impermissible. But in our case, *yada'ah v'kamachla* — she knew about it and agreed to it. She was a party to the stipulation from the outset. Rabbi Yosi therefore holds that if she agrees, a lesser amount is permissible. His objection in the chattel case applies only to a unilateral arrangement that compromises her interests without her knowledge.

## Lost Ketubah Case

### Sister of Rami: Lost Doc

The *Gemara* now presents a practical case. The sister of Rami bar Chama was married to Rabbi Avya. *Irkas kesuvtasa* — her *ketubah* document was lost. Not a divorce situation — simply the physical document had been misplaced. This scenario, as a practical matter, arises not infrequently: a couple returns from a wedding and cannot locate the *ketubah*, or a couple married for twenty or thirty years moves and cannot find the document, or the signatures on a *ketubah* hanging on the wall have faded to illegibility. In such cases one must write a substitute *ketubah*, known as a *ketubah d'irkasa* — *irkas* being the Aramaic for "lost" — typically signed by the original witnesses, who testify that they originally signed a *ketubah*, that it was lost, and that this document reflects its contents.

### Rav Yosef's Citation

They came before Rav Yosef. He said to them: *hachi amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel — zu divrei Rebbi Meir* — the principle that it is *biat zenut* to live together without proper *ketubah* protection is the position of Rabbi Meir alone. *Aval Chachamim omrim* — but the sages say *masha adam im ishto shtayim v'shalosh shanim belo kesuvah* — one can live with his wife for two or three years without a written substitute *ketubah*. There is no great urgency.

One might ask: who ever said Rabbi Meir would prohibit remaining together when the document is merely lost? The full amount is still owed, since we have already taught that even without a physical document the obligation exists. But the *Gemara* reasonably understands that just as the woman's subjective belief that she has only 100 when in fact she has 200 is sufficient to create *biat zenut*, so too the absence of the physical document is enough to create in her a subjective sense of vulnerability — she feels unprotected, she lacks proof that the debt exists, she cannot prevent him from claiming it was already paid. That subjective unease is, for Rabbi Meir, enough to constitute *biat zenut*.

### Sages: No Rush

The sages, however, hold that the mere absence of a document does not rise to that level. The underlying obligation remains intact and the woman is protected in substance even without the paper, so there is no prohibition against continuing to live together. There is no great rush.

### Abaye: Rule Like R. Meir

Abaye then interjects: *v'ha'amar Rav Nachman amar Shmuel halacha k'Rebbi Meir bigzeirotav* — but we have a ruling that we follow Rabbi Meir in his *gezeirot*, his edicts. It is noteworthy that the *Gemara* describes this as a *gezeirah*. Normally *gezeirot* are safeguards against transgression of some other prohibition. Here the *Gemara* uses the term to describe Rabbi Meir's protective stance toward the institution of marriage — a *gezeirah* in the sense of safeguarding the integrity and proper protections of the marital relationship. Since we rule like Rabbi Meir in his *gezeirot*, Abaye concludes: *zil katuv la* — go write her a substitute *ketubah*. So it sounds as though we rule like Rabbi Meir in this case.

### Modern Practice

What is the practical *halacha*? In principle, the answer follows Rabbi Meir — one should write a substitute *ketubah* as quickly as possible. However, the Rema notes that in the present day, given *takanas Rabbeinu Gershom* — the enactment that a husband cannot divorce his wife against her will — the underlying concern that generates *biat zenut* in Rabbi Meir's framework is substantially mitigated. The *biat zenut* concern arises because the woman feels that she is not truly protected: she could be casually divorced at any moment, and without the document she cannot prevent that or prove her claim. But now that she cannot be divorced without her consent, she knows she is protected even without the physical *ketubah*. The Rema therefore holds it is not *biat zenut* in such a case today. Nevertheless, one should take care of writing the replacement *ketubah* as promptly as possible; we do not tell the couple to cease living together until it is produced.

A question arises regarding *Sefardim*, who in various ways have adopted the spirit of *takanas Rabbeinu Gershom*, though the precise application is a matter that requires further analysis.

A related question: if one is writing a substitute *ketubah* with the same original witnesses, why not simply write the identical document? The answer is that writing an identical document on the same date creates a concern that the woman may possess two equivalent documents and attempt to collect twice. The *ketubah d'irkasa* therefore uses a different formulation that explicitly records that the original was lost — thereby preventing double collection, since once the husband has paid and the document is surrendered, no second claim can be made.

## Techilla vs. Sof Dispute

### Rav Dimi: Debate Techilla

The *Gemara* now turns to a question about the scope of the three-way debate — in particular the debate between Rabbi Yosi, who allows a lesser amount, and Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir, who do not. When exactly does the debate apply?

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported in the name of Rav Shimon ben Pazi, in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, in the name of Bar Kappara: *machlokes b'techilla* — the debate applies only at the outset of the wedding. *Aval besof divrei hakol eina mocheles* — but at the end, everyone agrees that the amount cannot be changed. The question of what "the beginning" and "the end" refer to will require clarification, but the initial formulation is: there is a *machlokes* before a certain point, and universal agreement after another point.

### R. Yochanan: Both Cases

Rabbi Yochanan says: *bein b'zo u'vein b'zo machlokes* — they debate in both cases, whether at the beginning or at the end.

### R. Abbahu: Harmonization

Rabbi Abbahu then clarifies: it was explained to him directly from Rabbi Yochanan that Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi are not actually disagreeing. When Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says the *machlokes* is *b'techilla* — at the beginning — he means *techillas chuppah*, before the *chuppah*. And when he says that everyone agrees at the end, he means *sof biah*, after they have actually had relations. And when Rabbi Yochanan says the *machlokes* applies to both cases — beginning and end — he means *techillas chuppah* (before the *chuppah*) and *sof chuppah* (at the *chuppah* but before *biah*). So both authorities agree that the *machlokes* spans everything up through the *chuppah*, and that once *biah* has occurred, everyone agrees the amount cannot be changed. On this reading, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi and Rabbi Yochanan are saying the same thing, simply using different terminology for adjacent points on the timeline.

### Ravin: Debate Sof

When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he had the opposite version of the same tradition — transmitted in the same chain, Rav Shimon ben Pazi, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, Bar Kappara — but with inverted positions. According to Ravin's version, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said *machlokes l'vsof* — the debate is only at the end. *Aval b'techilla divrei hakol mocheles* — but at the outset, everyone agrees the amount can be changed. Rabbi Yochanan in this version still says *bein b'zo u'vein b'zo machlokes* — they debate in both cases.

Rabbi Abbahu again harmonizes the two: when Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says the *machlokes* is *l'vsof* he means *sof chuppah* — after the *chuppah* but before *biah*. And when he says everyone agrees at the beginning, he means *techillas chuppah*, before the *chuppah*. And when Rabbi Yochanan says they debate both, he means *techillas biah* — the beginning of *biah*, i.e., after the *chuppah* — and *sof biah*, after relations. So this version has everyone agreeing that before the *chuppah* the amount can be changed, and everyone agreeing that after *biah* it cannot — and the entire *machlokes* falling in the middle zone between *chuppah* and *biah*. This is the mirror image of the green version: there, the *machlokes* covered everything before *biah*; here, the *machlokes* is confined to the interval between *chuppah* and *biah*.

### Rav Pappa: Sevara Best

Rav Pappa concludes the sugya with a methodological observation. Had Rabbi Abbahu not told us that Rabbi Yochanan himself said that he and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi were not really disagreeing, Rav Pappa says he would have preferred to read it differently: he would have said that Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi genuinely disagree — each one staking out a different position about where the *machlokes* of the *Tannaim* logically belongs — while the two transmissions of Rav Dimi and Ravin do not actually conflict. In that alternative reading, *sof* in Ravin's version equals *sof chuppah* and *techilla* in Rav Dimi's version equals *techillas biah*, making the two transmissions equivalent.

The *Gemara* asks: what difference does it make which debate we collapse? Rav Pappa's preference rests on a principle: *d'pligi trei amorai ta'ama d'nafshaihu v'lo pligi trei amorai aliba d'chad amora* — it is preferable to say that two *Amoraim* are arguing based on their own reasoning and analysis rather than to say that two *Amoraim* are disputing what a third *Amora* actually said. When Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi debate where the *machlokes* logically occurs, they are disagreeing about *sevara* — about what makes sense, about in which domain the debate is most coherent. That is the natural stuff of the *Shas*. But if we instead say Rav Dimi and Ravin are reporting different versions of what Rabbi Yochanan said, we are implying that one of them got it wrong — that there is an error in transmission. It is always better, if we must reduce one dispute, to do so by harmonizing transmissions than by eliminating a genuine debate in *sevara*.

## Twelve-Month Waiting Per…

### Mishnah: 12 vs. 30 Days

A new *Mishnah* opens: *notnin l'betulah shneim asar chodesh mishetav'ah ha-ba'al l'farnos et atzmah* — we give a *betulah* twelve months from the time that the husband demands to begin living together to prepare herself for marriage. This is not twelve months from the time of *kiddushin*, but from the time the husband says he is ready to proceed to *nissu'in*. She is entitled to use that time to prepare her trousseau. Similarly, *u'keshem shenotnin l'ishah kach notnin l'ish* — just as she is given time to prepare, so too he is given time to prepare the home if he needs it. And *v'almanah shloshim yom* — for a second marriage, only thirty days are required, since a widow is more independent and the wedding would not involve the same elaborate preparation.

*Higi'a zman v'lo nisu* — once the period expires and they are not yet married, *ochelet mishelo* — he must now begin supporting her financially. Once *nissu'in* should have occurred and it has not, his obligation of *mezonot* begins.

### Support Begins

The onset of this financial obligation is tied directly to the expiration of the waiting period. From that point forward, she eats from his account, regardless of who bears responsibility for the delay — a question addressed at the beginning of the *masechet* but not the focus here.

### Terumah Eligibility

*V'ochelet b'terumah* — if he is a *kohen*, he may now feed her *terumah*, since the wife of a *kohen* is entitled to eat *terumah* even before *nissu'in*, even at the stage of *erusin*. This ruling conveys two things: first, from the laws of *terumah*, she is permitted to eat it even as a mere *arusah* to a *kohen*; and second, in satisfying his obligation of *mezonot*, he may discharge it by providing *terumah*, which is cheaper.

### Terumah Dispute

Rabbi Tarfon says he may give her the entire amount in *terumah*. Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *mechtzah chullin u'mechtzah terumah* — half must be ordinary *chullin*. The reason is that roughly half the month — and more, given *chumras Rabbi Zeira* — she is *tme'ah* as a *niddah* and therefore cannot eat *terumah*. It is therefore unfair to give her only *terumah* that she will have to sell at a loss to obtain *chullin* she can actually consume when she is *tme'ah*. He must provide at least half as *chullin*, food she can eat at all times.

### Yavam Exception

*Ha-yavam eino ma'achil b'terumah* — the case of the *yavam* is different. If a *kohen* was married and died, and now his brother the *kohen* stands as *yavam* and she is *shomeres yavam*, she cannot eat *terumah* even after twelve months of waiting. There is no twelve-month clock after which she gains *terumah* eligibility; as long as she is awaiting *yibbum*, she does not eat *terumah*.

### Split Service

The *Mishnah* then covers cases where the twelve-month period is split between time in front of the husband and time in front of the *yavam*. If she spent six months in front of the husband (after he said it was time to proceed) and six months awaiting the *yavam*, she does not eat *terumah*. Even if eleven months and twenty-nine days were in front of the husband and one day awaiting the *yavam*, or vice versa — in all such mixed cases, *einah ochelet b'terumah*. Rashi notes that the last scenario — almost all the time in front of the *yavam* — is in a sense obvious, since the *Mishnah* already indicated that even years of waiting for *yibbum* do not generate *terumah* eligibility. Tomorrow's daf will examine the *Tosafos* position on this.

## No Terumah Until Chuppah

### Original Teaching

*Zu mishnah rishonah* — that was the first, original teaching. The term *mishnah rishonah* is significant: it refers to earlier collections of *mishnayot* that, even when subsequently revised, were not discarded. The *Gemara*'s phrase *mishnah rishonah lo zazah mimkomah* — the original *Mishnah* has not moved from its place — reflects the principle that earlier texts are preserved, not silently replaced by later rulings.

### Later Restriction

*Beit din shel achareihem amru* — the later *beit din* then ruled: *ein ha'ishah ochelet b'terumah ad shetikanes l'chuppah* — a woman does not eat *terumah* until she has entered the *chuppah*, meaning until *nissu'in* has actually taken place. This is actually to the woman's detriment in terms of the husband's food bill — since he must now provide all *chullin* during the waiting period rather than satisfying his obligation with cheaper *terumah* — and it prevents the woman from giving *terumah* to her family. The source for the twelve-month right is derived from the verse in which the family of Rivkah says *teshev hana'arah itanu yamim o asor* — let the girl remain with us for "days" or ten. *Yamim* unqualified, as shown through a *gezeirah shavah* from *yamim tihyeh ge'ulato* (the year of redemption for a house in a walled city), means a full year. And "ten" — in context, ten months rather than ten days, paralleling the year reduced by two months. The *Gemara* thus derives that there is an established right to request a full year.

## Postponement Rights: Ket…

### Ketanah: Can Postpone

Rabbi Zeira cites a *baraisa*: *ketannah bein hi uvein aviha yecholim l'akeiv* — while the girl is still a *ketannah* — a minor — either she or her father can prevent the *nissu'in* from proceeding. This introduces a new principle: the twelve-month framework applies once the woman is past minority; as long as she is a *ketannah*, she and her father can in principle delay indefinitely.

### Father's Right Question

The *Gemara* asks: *bishlama hi matzi me'akeva* — it is understandable that she can postpone, since she is the one being married. *Ella aviha* — but the father: *ei hi nicha lah* — if she herself wants to proceed to *nissu'in* after *kiddushin* has already occurred, *mai nafka lei minnah* — what is the father's legitimate claim to stand in the way?

### Father's Concern Logic

The *Gemara* answers: *sevar* — the father reasons as follows. *Hashta lo yade'ah* — she is young, perhaps seven years old, and does not know what she is agreeing to. *Lemachar mimreda* — tomorrow, once married, she will be too young and will rebel against the marriage, reject her husband. *V'nafkah* — she will leave or be divorced. *V'asya v'nafala alavei* — and she will fall back on him, become his responsibility once again. This is a striking *Gemara*: the more obvious reason for the father's right to delay is that as long as she remains at home before *nissu'in*, he retains certain entitlements to her labor and earnings. The *Gemara*, however, deliberately grounds the father's right in a concern internal to the marriage itself — that he legitimately believes the *nissu'in* is premature and will ultimately fail, and that he will bear the consequences. The other reason — his financial interest in her remaining home — is not given standing here as a sufficient justification.

### R. Abba: No Nissuin

Rabbi Abba bar Levi states explicitly: *ein poskin al ha-ketannah lehasi'ah keshehi ketannah* — one does not make an arrangement to perform *nissu'in* with a *ketannah* while she is still a *ketannah*. This goes one step beyond the prior ruling. First, the *Gemara* established that she and her father have the right to prevent it — which already signals disapproval. Now it states affirmatively that one should not do it. *Aval poskin al ha-ketannah lehasi'ah keshehi gedolah* — one may, however, arrange while she is a *ketannah* that the *nissu'in* will take place once she reaches adulthood, once she is a *na'arah* after *Bat Mitzvah*.

This is worth pausing over. People are rightly troubled when confronted with *kiddushei ketannah* — the betrothal of a minor. It is worth noting that *kiddushin* alone does not involve cohabitation; the sexual dimension of marriage begins only with *nissu'in*. And the *Gemara* here is clearly discouraging *nissu'in* during minority. Once she reaches *Bat Mitzvah*, the situation is different — and while that too may strike modern sensibilities as troubling, the *Gemara* is at least insisting that *nissu'in* not precede that threshold.

### Psychological Concern

The *Gemara* then asks: is it not obvious that one may arrange while she is still a *ketannah* for *nissu'in* to take place after she grows up? What is the *chiddush*? The *Gemara* answers: one might have thought that even this should be prohibited — *dilma milai pachda mehashta v'chalsha* — that by telling an eleven-year-old that she will be married after her *Bat Mitzvah*, one fills her with fear now, and she grows despondent or psychologically burdened. The *Gemara* therefore had to teach that we are not concerned about this — *kamashma lan* — and such advance planning is permitted.

## Bogeret Timing: 12 Months

### Rav Huna: 30 Days

Rav Huna rules: *bogra yom echad veniskadsha notnin lah shloshim yom k'almanah* — if a woman hits *bagrus* — majority at twelve and a half — and then has *kiddushin*, she is given only thirty days, not twelve months, in which to prepare for *nissu'in*.

### Baraisa: 12 Months

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this from a *baraisa*: *bogeret harei hi k'tav'uha* — when a woman hits *bagrus*, it is as if one of the parties has made a *tvi'ah*, a demand to proceed to *nissu'in*. One might read this as: it is like the *tvi'ah* of a *betulah* — twelve months. *Mai lav k'tav'uha d'betulah* — does that not mean twelve months, as when a *betulah* is *tavu'ah*? The *Gemara* responds: *la, k'tav'uha d'almanah* — no, it is like the *tvi'ah* made of a widow, thirty days. But a further *baraisa* is cited: *bogeret sheshahata shneim asar chodesh* — a woman who has been a *bogeret* for twelve months. Rabbi Eliezer says that since the husband is now obligated in *mezonot*, he may annul her vows. Setting aside the question of vow annulment, the implication of this *baraisa* is clear: twelve months after *bagrus* he is obligated in *mezonot* — meaning the clock runs for twelve months, not thirty days. This directly contradicts Rav Huna.

The *Gemara* attempts to adjust the text: *eima bogeret v'sheshahata shneim asar chodesh* — read it as a case of a *bogeret* combined with a separate twelve-month period, not twelve months of *bagrus*. But this is acknowledged as far-fetched, *v'lo pshat*, and the *Gemara* moves on. A further *baraisa* settles the matter explicitly: *hama'ares et ha-betulah* — one who betroths a *betulah* — whether the husband demanded and she postponed, giving her twelve months, or whether she demanded and he postponed, giving him twelve months, *notnin lah shneim asar chodesh misha'as tvi'ah* — twelve months from the time of the demand. *Aval lo misha'as erusin* — not from the time of *erusin* itself, but from the moment one side says it is time to proceed. *U'vogeret harei hi k'tav'uha* — and a *bogeret* is treated as if there has been a *tvi'ah*. *Bogra yom echad veniskadsha notnin lah shneim asar chodesh* — she hit *bagrus* one day and then had *kiddushin*: she is given twelve months. This explicitly refutes Rav Huna. *Tyuvta d'Rav Huna tyuvta* — a full contradiction of Rav Huna's position.

### Rav Pappa Resolution

Rav Pappa then resolves the apparent tension: *bogeres she'avru aleha shneim asar chodesh b'vagrus v'niskadshah* — if a woman is already a year into *bagrus* before *kiddushin* — she is the ripe old age of thirteen and a half — and then has *kiddushin*, she is given only thirty days. At that point the clock has long been running and one cannot give her a full additional year. The scenario in which a full twelve months applies is when she hits *bagrus* and then promptly has *kiddushin*; but if *bagrus* predates *kiddushin* by a year, the thirty-day rule applies.

### Tosafot Synthesis

*Tosafos* observes a broader structural point about the timeline. If a *ketannah* can postpone indefinitely, and a *bogeret* triggers the clock at *bagrus* itself, then the twelve-month scenario of the *Mishnah* applies specifically to the period of *na'arus* — between twelve and twelve and a half. The *tvi'ah* and the twelve-month clock begin in that window. But this raises a complication: what if the twelve-month period begins while she is a *na'arah* and she becomes a *bogeret* six months into that period? *Tosafos* works through the stages — *ketannah*, *na'arah*, *bogeret* — and their interaction with the *tvi'ah* and waiting periods, a matter that requires careful analysis of when precisely she transitions between those stages relative to the demand to proceed with *nissu'in*.
