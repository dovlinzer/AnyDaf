# Megillah 15b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Esther's Appeal

### Shechinah Departure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתַּעֲמֹד בַּחֲצַר בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ הַפְּנִימִית״. אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעָה לְבֵית הַצְּלָמִים, נִסְתַּלְּקָה הֵימֶנָּה שְׁכִינָה. אָמְרָה: ״אֵלִי אֵלִי לָמָה עֲזַבְתָּנִי״?! שֶׁמָּא אַתָּה דָּן עַל שׁוֹגֵג כְּמֵזִיד וְעַל אוֹנֶס כְּרָצוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its explanation of the verses of the Megilla. The verse states with regard to Esther: **"And she stood in the inner court of the king's house"** (Esther 5:1). **Rabbi Levi said: Once she reached the chamber of the idols,** which was in the inner court, **the Divine Presence left her. She** immediately **said: "My God, my God, why have You forsaken me?"** (Psalms 22:2). **Perhaps** it is because **You judge an unintentional sin as one** performed **intentionally, and** an action **done due to** circumstances **beyond one's control as** one done **willingly.**

The daf begins with Esther entering *bechatzer beit hamelech hapnimiyit* — the innermost chamber of the king's house. Rabbi Levi teaches that upon reaching the chamber of idols (*beit hatzelamim*), the *Shechinah* departed from her — *nistalka heimena Shechinah* — for she had been cloaked in the *ruach hakodesh* of *malchut*, and now that presence withdrew.

She cried out: *Eli Eli lama azavtani* — my God, my God, why have You abandoned me? This verse comes from the *perek* in Tehillim identified as *Ayelet HaShachar*, which the *Gemara* interprets as referring to Esther. She then asks: *shema ata dan al shogeg kemezid ve'al oness keratzon* — perhaps You are judging the inadvertent as intentional, and the coerced as the willing?

### Onesh Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ שֶׁמָּא עַל שֶׁקְּרָאתִיו ״כֶּלֶב״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַצִּילָה מֵחֶרֶב נַפְשִׁי מִיַּד כֶּלֶב יְחִידָתִי״. חָזְרָה וּקְרָאַתּוּ ״אַרְיֵה״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹשִׁיעֵנִי מִפִּי אַרְיֵה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** You have left me **because** in my prayers **I called** Haman **a dog, as it is stated: "Deliver my soul from the sword; my only one from the hand of the dog"** (Psalms 22:21). **She** at once **retracted and called him** in her prayers **a lion, as it is stated** in the following verse: **"Save me from the lion's mouth"** (Psalms 22:22).

This plea connects to an earlier *Midrash* in which Esther declares *ka'asher avadti avadti*. The *Gemara* understands her as now willingly approaching Achashveirosh, including for intimate purposes, which would render her forbidden to Mordechai. Esther protests that even if she acts willingly on the surface, the larger context renders her an *oness* — coerced by circumstance — since she acts for the sake of *Klal Yisrael*.

Rashi explains this precisely: *af al pi she'ani ba'ah elav me'elai oness hu* — even though she is choosing to go to him, the necessity is external and overriding.

This passage raises two distinct questions. First, was Esther *permitted* to do this at all? The *Gemara* elsewhere addresses Esther's conduct with the phrase *befarhesya havei*, and the full range of questions surrounding *giluy arayot* — whether it is *yehareg ve'al ya'avor*, the principle of *karka olam*, and so on — all bear on this. Second, and more fascinatingly, even granting that she was not only permitted but *obligated* by *pikuach nefesh* — even if she was a true *oness* in the fullest sense — does that exemption from transgression necessarily mean that she remains permitted to Mordechai? The verse governing that prohibition reads *ki tisteh ishto umalah bo ma'al*: the Torah concerns itself with the violation of the marital bond itself — *umalah bo ma'al* — not solely with whether she transgressed against God. It is therefore conceivable that even an act she was *metzuva* to perform could still affect her status relative to her husband. A fascinating *teshuvah* addresses a parallel scenario involving a woman deceived into believing she was fulfilling a *mitzvah* and later found to have been misled, and the question of her return to her husband turns on precisely this distinction. The *Gemara* here does not resolve the question, but frames it pointedly: she herself calls it *oness*.

### Dog/Lion Language

She continues: *o shekarasi lo kelev* — or perhaps You abandoned me because I called Achashveirosh a dog, as the verse says *miyad kelev yechidati* — save me from the dog. Even though Achashveirosh is a *rasha*, he is a king, and one must speak of him with respect. So she corrects herself: *chazra vekaraso aryeh* — she then calls him a lion. The next verse says *hoshieini mipi aryeh* — save me from the mouth of the lion — and with that correction, she has addressed both concerns she raised before God.

### Angels & Staff

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי כִרְאוֹת הַמֶּלֶךְ אֶת אֶסְתֵּר הַמַּלְכָּה״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁלֹשָׁה מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת נִזְדַּמְּנוּ לָהּ בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה, אֶחָד שֶׁהִגְבִּיהַּ אֶת צַוָּארָהּ, וְאֶחָד שֶׁמָּשַׁךְ חוּט שֶׁל חֶסֶד עָלֶיהָ, וְאֶחָד שֶׁמָּתַח אֶת הַשַּׁרְבִיט.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And so it was, that when the king saw Esther the queen** standing in the court, that she obtained favor in his sight; and the king held out to Esther the golden scepter that was in his hand" (Esther 5:2). **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Three ministering angels happened to join her at that time: One that raised up her neck,** so that she could stand erect, free of shame; **one that strung a cord** of divine **grace around her,** endowing her with charm and beauty; **and one that stretched the** king's **scepter.**

The verse then reads *vayehi keros hamelech es Esther hamalka*. Rabbi Yochanan teaches that three heavenly angels appeared to her at that moment. The first lifted her neck so that the king could see her through the crowd. The second drew the *chut shel chesed* — the strand of divine charm — over her, so that she found favor in his eyes. The third caused the king to extend the golden staff toward her, allowing her to approach.

### Staff Measures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת הָיָה, וְהֶעֱמִידוֹ עַל שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ עַל שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ עַל עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: עַל שִׁשִּׁים, וְכֵן אַתָּה מוֹצֵא בְּאַמָּתָהּ שֶׁל בַּת פַּרְעֹה, וְכֵן אַתָּה מוֹצֵא בְּשִׁינֵּי רְשָׁעִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״שִׁינֵּי רְשָׁעִים שִׁבַּרְתָּ״, וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אַל תִּקְרֵי ״שִׁבַּרְתָּ״ אֶלָּא ״שִׁרְיבַּבְתָּ״. רַבָּה בַּר עוֹפְרָן אָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר שֶׁשָּׁמַע מֵרַבּוֹ וְרַבּוֹ מֵרַבּוֹ: מָאתַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **How much** was it stretched? **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** The scepter **was two cubits, and he made it twelve** cubits. **And some say** that he made it **sixteen** cubits, **and** yet **others say twenty-four** cubits. **It was taught in a *baraita*:** He made it **sixty** cubits. **And similarly you find with the arm of Pharaoh's daughter,** which she stretched out to take Moshe. **And so too, you find with the teeth of the wicked, as it is written: "You have broken the teeth of the wicked"** (Psalms 3:8), with regard to which **Reish Lakish said: Do not read** it as **"You have broken [*shibbarta*]," but** as: **You have enlarged [*sheribavta*]. Rabba bar Oferan said in the name of Rabbi Elazar, who heard it from his teacher, who** in turn heard it **from his teacher:** The scepter was stretched **two hundred** cubits.

The *Gemara* then asks: how far did the staff extend? Rabbi Yirmiyah teaches it was originally two *amos* and was extended to twelve. Some say sixteen, others twenty-four, and a *Braisah* teaches sixty. Rabba bar Efron cites in the name of Rabbi Eliezer, who received it through an unbroken chain of tradition (*sheshama meirabo v'rabo meirabo*), that it extended two hundred *amos*.

The *Gemara* notes parallels elsewhere: the arm of Pharaoh's daughter that extended to draw Moshe from the water, and the teeth of the wicked which Reish Lakish reads not as *shibarta* — you broke — but *sheribavta* — you extended — referring to the miraculous extension of Og's teeth. The different specific numbers throughout this discussion may reflect different ways of counting from a particular word or letter in the verse, as the Maharsha explains.

The theme of the *Beit HaMikdash* surfaces here as well. The king's offer of "half the kingdom" is interpreted to exclude anything that "divides" (*chotzeitz*) the kingdom — and the *Gemara* identifies that as the *binyan Beit HaMikdash*. Achashveirosh will not consent to its rebuilding. This is consistent with the *Gemara*'s broader understanding that the Purim story takes place during a period when the construction had stalled. The Ramban understood the events as occurring later in the Persian period — after the rebuilding had resumed in earnest under Daryavesh — but the *Gemara* consistently treats this as a moment when the *binyan* had not yet truly begun. The *Beit HaMikdash* as a theme, as we will see, continues to surface throughout the story.

### Half Kingdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר לָהּ הַמֶּלֶךְ לְאֶסְתֵּר הַמַּלְכָּה מַה בַּקָּשָׁתֵךְ עַד חֲצִי הַמַּלְכוּת וְתֵעָשׂ״. חֲצִי הַמַּלְכוּת וְלֹא כׇּל הַמַּלְכוּת, וְלֹא דָּבָר שֶׁחוֹצֵץ לַמַּלְכוּת, וּמַאי נִיהוּ — בִּנְיַן בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"Then the king said to her" (Esther 5:3), to Esther the queen, "What is your wish, even to half the kingdom, it shall be performed"** (Esther 5:6). The Gemara comments that Ahasuerus intended only a limited offer: Only **half the kingdom, but not the whole kingdom, and not something that would serve as a barrier to the kingdom,** as there is one thing to which the kingdom will never agree. **And what is that? The building of the Temple;** if that shall be your wish, realize that it will not be fulfilled.

*Ad chatzi ha-malchut* — up to half the kingdom — means the king will give generously, but not anything that "divides" the kingdom. The *Gemara* identifies what that limitation protects: *u'mai nihu binyan Beit HaMikdash* — it is the rebuilding of the Temple. This sets the stage for Esther's reply, in which she does not ask for the *binyan* but instead invites the king and Haman to a feast.

## Why Invite Haman

### Trap Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יָבֹא הַמֶּלֶךְ וְהָמָן אֶל הַמִּשְׁתֶּה״. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מָה רָאֲתָה אֶסְתֵּר שֶׁזִּימְּנָה אֶת הָמָן? רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: פַּחִים טָמְנָה לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יְהִי שֻׁלְחָנָם לִפְנֵיהֶם לְפָח״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states that Esther requested: "If it seem good unto the king, **let the king and Haman come this day to the banquet** that I have prepared for him" (Esther 5:4). **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **What did Esther see to invite Haman** to the banquet? **Rabbi Elazar says: She hid a snare for him, as it is stated: "Let their table become a snare before them"** (Psalms 69:23), as she assumed that she would be able to trip up Haman during the banquet.

*Tanu rabanan*: what did Esther see in herself that she invited Haman? The question is entirely reasonable — if her goal is to save the Jewish people from Haman's decree, why bring Haman along to the feast? The *Gemara* proceeds through a remarkable series of *tanna'ic* and *amora'ic* answers.

Rabbi Eliezer says: she laid a trap for him — *pach tamnah lo* — as the verse states, *yehi shulchanam lifneihem l'phach*, let their table be a trap before them. By including Haman, she drew him in close, hoping he would expose himself or be caught in some incriminating behavior.

### Keep Close

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: מִבֵּית אָבִיהָ לָמְדָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם רָעֵב שׂוֹנַאֲךָ הַאֲכִילֵהוּ לֶחֶם וְגוֹ׳״. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל עֵצָה וְיִמְרוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says: She learned** to do **this from** the Jewish teachings of **her father's house, as it is stated: "If your enemy be hungry, give him bread to eat"** (Proverbs 25:21). **Rabbi Meir says:** She invited him **in order** that he be near her at all times, **so that he would not take counsel and rebel** against Ahasuerus when he discovered that the king was angry with him.

Rabbi Yehoshua says she learned this from her father's house: *im ra'ev sona'acha hachileihu lachem* — if your enemy is hungry, feed him bread. Keep your enemies close and your eyes upon them.

Rabbi Meir says: she feared that if Haman were absent he might catch wind of what was happening and take action — perhaps rebel against the king or foil her plan entirely. Better to keep him where she could watch him.

### Multiple Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יַכִּירוּ בָּהּ שֶׁהִיא יְהוּדִית. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל: אָחוֹת יֵשׁ לָנוּ בְּבֵית הַמֶּלֶךְ, וְיַסִּיחוּ דַּעְתָּן מִן הָרַחֲמִים. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא מָצוּי לָהּ בְּכׇל עֵת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר: אוּלַי יַרְגִּישׁ הַמָּקוֹם וְיַעֲשֶׂה לָנוּ נֵס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** She invited Haman **so that it not be found out that she was a Jew,** as had she distanced him, he would have become suspicious. **Rabbi Neḥemya says:** She did this **so that the Jewish people would not say: We have a sister in the king's house, and** consequently **neglect their** prayers for divine **mercy. Rabbi Yosei says:** She acted in this manner, **so that** Haman would **always be on hand for her,** as that would enable her to find an opportunity to cause him to stumble before the king. **Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya said** that Esther said to herself: **Perhaps the Omnipresent will take notice** that all are supporting Haman and nobody is supporting the Jewish people, **and He will perform for us a miracle.**

Rabbi Yehudah says: she did not want people to realize she was Jewish. If she suddenly appeared before the king without Haman, suspicion would arise that she was interceding against his decree. By including Haman, she created misdirection.

Rabbi Nechemya adds a striking theological dimension: she did not want the Jewish people to relax their prayers. If the Jews thought, "our sister is in the palace, we have nothing to worry about," they would ease off their supplications to God — *veyasichu da'atam min ha-rachamim*. By keeping Haman present and seemingly close to Esther, the Jews would remain anxious and continue to cry out to Heaven. It is a remarkable point: she is engineering a religious outcome, making sure God remains in the picture even as God is conspicuously absent from the text of the *Megillah*.

Rabbi Yose says: so that Haman would always be available to her — *kedei shelo yehei matzui lah bechol eit* — and as Rashi explains, *ulai tuchal lehachshilo beshum davar lifnei hamelech*: if Haman is always nearby, perhaps she could find some way to get him into trouble before the king.

Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya says: perhaps God will notice the desperate situation and act on their behalf — *ulai yargish HaMakom veya'aseh lanu nes*. Rashi unpacks this in two ways: either that God should perceive how low Esther has had to stoop, currying favor with the enemy of Israel; or that God should respond by bringing forth those who would stand by the Jews just as Esther has had to stand alongside their enemies.

This raises a genuine theological question that this *Gemara* pointedly touches: does God need to be shown human suffering before acting? The entire logic of *ta'anit*, which was just studied in Masechet Ta'anit, involves precisely this — displaying one's misery before God so that divine compassion is aroused. The anthropomorphism is real and the tension is not resolved here, but the *Gemara* makes no effort to soften it.

### Jealousy Plan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: אַסְבִּיר לוֹ פָּנִים כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּהָרֵג הוּא וְהִיא. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מֶלֶךְ הֲפַכְפְּכָן הָיָה. אָמַר רַבִּי גַּמְלִיאֵל: עֲדַיִין צְרִיכִין אָנוּ לַמּוֹדָעִי, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הַמּוֹדָעִי אוֹמֵר: קִנְּאַתּוּ בַּמֶּלֶךְ קִנְּאַתּוּ בַּשָּׂרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says:** She said to herself: **I will act kindly toward him** and thereby bring the king to suspect that we are having an affair; she did so **in order that** both **he and she would be killed.** Essentially, Esther was willing to be killed with Haman in order that the decree would be annulled. **Rabban Gamliel says:** Ahasuerus **was a fickle king,** and Esther hoped that if he saw Haman on multiple occasions, eventually he would change his opinion of him. **Rabban Gamliel said: We still need** the words of Rabbi Eliezer **HaModa'i** to understand why Esther invited Haman to her banquet. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer HaModa'i says: She made the king jealous of him and she made the** other **ministers jealous of him,** and in this way she brought about his downfall.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha opens with *asbir lo fanim* — let me explain it, implying he has something to add beyond what has already been said. His answer: *kedei sheyekaneh hu vehi* — so that the king would become jealous, believing that Esther and Haman were somehow intimate. This is a striking explanation: Esther was willing to sacrifice her own reputation in order to trigger the king's jealousy and bring about Haman's downfall. Most readers assume this is the central explanation, partly because the end of the story bears it out when Haman falls upon Esther's bed — but whether it was indeed her primary motivation, as opposed to the other explanations offered, is precisely what this passage is debating.

Rabban Gamliel says: the king was a *hapachpachan* — a flip-flopper, constantly reversing himself. Esther understood that if she convinced the king privately and Haman were not present, the next time Haman got the king's ear he would reverse course. The only way to catch the king at the right moment and seal the deal was to have everyone in the same room at once.

Rabban Gamliel then adds: *adayin tzerichin anu laModai* — we still need the one from Modi'in, meaning Rabbi Elazar HaModai, who specialized in *aggadata*. Rabbi Elazar HaModai says: *kinato bamelech kinato basarim* — she wanted to make the king jealous of Haman and also to make the other princes and ministers jealous of Haman. The Maharsha explains what Rabbi Elazar HaModai uniquely adds: the question was not only why Haman was invited but why *only* Haman, and not others. By inviting only Haman she created a scenario in which both the king and the other courtiers would be suspicious of Haman's special relationship with the queen — whereas had she invited others, the intimacy implied would have been diluted.

### All True

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר: ״לִפְנֵי שֶׁבֶר גָּאוֹן״. אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: ״בְּחוּמָּם אָשִׁית אֶת מִשְׁתֵּיהֶם וְגוֹ׳״. אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ לְאֵלִיָּהוּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּמַאן חַזְיָא אֶסְתֵּר וַעֲבַדָא הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּכוּלְּהוּ תַּנָּאֵי וּכְכוּלְּהוּ אָמוֹרָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba says:** Esther invited Haman to her banquet in order to fulfill that which is stated: **"Pride goes before destruction"** (Proverbs 16:18), which indicates that in order to destroy the wicked, one must first bring them to pride. It can be understood according to **Abaye and Rava, who both say** that she invited Haman in order to fulfill the verse: **"When they are heated, I will make feasts for them,** and I will make them drunk, that they may rejoice, and sleep a perpetual sleep" (Jeremiah 51:39). The Gemara relates that **Rabba bar Avuh** once **happened upon Elijah** the Prophet and **said to him: In accordance with whose understanding did Esther see** fit to **act in this manner?** What was the true reason behind her invitation? **He,** Elijah, **said to him:** Esther was motivated by all the reasons previously mentioned and did so **for all** the reasons previously stated by **the *tanna'im* and all** the reasons stated by **the *amora'im*.**

Rava adds: *lifnei shever ga'on* — pride comes before the fall. Abaye and Rava together cite the verse *bechumam ashit mishtehem* — in their heat I will arrange their drinking feast — the idea being that the downfall of one's enemies often comes through the indulgence of feasting and drinking. 

Rabba bar Avuha encountered Eliyahu and asked him directly: according to whom did Esther act? Eliyahu answered: *kechulhu tana'ei vechechulhu amora'ei* — all of them are true. All of these motivations operated simultaneously, and since none of them contradict one another, there is no reason to choose. This is among the most grounded sections of the *Gemara*'s treatment of the *Megillah* — a genuine effort to understand the inner logic of a character's decision — and the answer that all motivations coexist is, on reflection, entirely plausible.

## Haman Boasts

### Sons' Numbers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְסַפֵּר לָהֶם הָמָן אֶת כְּבוֹד עׇשְׁרוֹ וְרוֹב בָּנָיו״, וְכַמָּה רוֹב בָּנָיו? אָמַר רַב, שְׁלֹשִׁים: עֲשָׂרָה מֵתוּ, וַעֲשָׂרָה נִתְלוּ, וַעֲשָׂרָה מְחַזְּרִין עַל הַפְּתָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And Haman recounted to them the glory of his riches, and the multitude of his sons"** (Esther 5:11). The Gemara asks: **And how many** sons did he in fact have that are referred to as **"the multitude of his sons"? Rav said:** There were **thirty** sons; **ten** of them **died** in childhood, **ten of them were hanged** as recorded in the book of Esther, **and ten** survived and were forced to **beg at** other people's **doors.**

The verse relates that Haman boasted to his friends and family of his great wealth *verov banav* — and the myriad of his sons. How many sons did he actually have? Rav says thirty: ten died in battle, ten were hanged, and ten afterwards wandered begging from door to door — ongoing suffering as part of his downfall. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: אוֹתָן שֶׁמְּחַזְּרִין עַל הַפְּתָחִים שִׁבְעִים הָיוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״שְׂבֵעִים בַּלֶּחֶם נִשְׂכָּרוּ״, אַל תִּקְרֵי ״שְׂבֵעִים״ אֶלָּא ״שִׁבְעִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: Those that begged at** other people's **doors** numbered **seventy, as it is written: "Those that were full, have hired themselves out for bread"** (I Samuel 2:5). **Do not read it as: "Those that were full" [*seve'im***]; **rather,** read it as **seventy [*shivim*],** indicating that there were seventy who "hired themselves out for bread."

The *Rabbanan* say there were seventy who ended up begging, reading *shiv'im balechem niskaru* with *al tikrei* as *shiv'im* — seventy. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: כּוּלָּן מָאתַיִם וּשְׁמוֹנָה הֲווֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְרוֹב בָּנָיו״. ״וְרוֹב״ בְּגִימַטְרִיָּא מָאתַן וְאַרְבֵּיסַר הָווּ! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: ״וְרֹב״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rami bar Abba said: All of** Haman's sons together numbered **two hundred and eight, as it is stated: "And the multitude [*verov*] of his sons."** The numerical value of the word *verov* equals two hundred and eight, alluding to the number of his sons. The Gemara comments: **But** in fact, **the numerical value [*gimatriyya*] of** the word ***verov* equals two hundred and fourteen,** not two hundred and eight. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** The word ***verov* is written** in the Bible without the second *vav*, and therefore its numerical value equals two hundred and eight.

Rami bar Abba says the total was 208, since *rov* in *gematria* yields 208 (reading the *ketiv* without the middle *vav*, as Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak points out, making it *resh vav bet* = 208 rather than 214).

### Sleep Disturbance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בַּלַּיְלָה הַהוּא נָדְדָה שְׁנַת הַמֶּלֶךְ״. אָמַר רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם: נָדְדָה שְׁנַת מַלְכּוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם. וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: נָדְדוּ עֶלְיוֹנִים, נָדְדוּ תַּחְתּוֹנִים. רָבָא אָמַר: שְׁנַת הַמֶּלֶךְ אֲחַשְׁוֵרוֹשׁ מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"On that night the sleep of the king was disturbed"** (Esther 6:1). **Rabbi Tanḥum said:** The verse alludes to another king who could not sleep; **the sleep of the King of the universe,** the Holy One, Blessed be He, **was disturbed. And the Sages say:** The sleep of **the higher ones,** the angels, **was disturbed, and** the sleep of **the lower ones,** the Jewish people, **was disturbed. Rava said:** This should be understood **literally: The sleep of King Ahasuerus** was disturbed.

*Balaylah hahu nadeda shenat hamelech* — on that night the king's sleep was disturbed. Rabbi Tanchum reads this as: *nadeda shenat malko shel olam* — God's sleep was disturbed, meaning God was now stirring to intervene. This reading is also reflected in the special *trop* with which the *Megillah* is chanted, which is understood to evoke this sense of divine awakening. The *Rabbanan* say: *nadedo elyonim nadedo tachtonim* — both the upper realms and the lower realms were stirred into motion in order to bring about the redemption.

### Conspiracy Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְפַלָה לֵיהּ מִילְּתָא בְּדַעְתֵּיהּ, אָמַר: מַאי דְּקַמַּן דְּזַמֵּינְתֵּיהּ אֶסְתֵּר לְהָמָן? דִּלְמָא עֵצָה קָא שָׁקְלִי עִילָּוֵיהּ דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא לְמִקְטְלֵיהּ. הֲדַר אָמַר: אִי הָכִי, לָא הֲוָה גַּבְרָא דְּרָחֵים לִי דַּהֲוָה מוֹדַע לִי? הֲדַר אָמַר: דִּלְמָא אִיכָּא אִינִישׁ דַּעֲבַד בִּי טֵיבוּתָא וְלָא פְּרַעְתֵּיהּ, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מִימַּנְעִי אִינָשֵׁי וְלָא מְגַלּוּ לִי, מִיָּד: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לְהָבִיא אֶת סֵפֶר הַזִּכְרוֹנוֹת דִּבְרֵי הַיָּמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** And this was the reason Ahasuerus could not sleep: **A thought occurred to him** and **he said** to himself: **What is this before us that Esther has invited Haman? Perhaps they are conspiring against that man,** i.e., against me, **to kill him. He** then **said again** to himself: **If this is so, is there no man who loves me and would inform me** of this conspiracy? **He** then **said again** to himself: **Perhaps there is some man who has done a favor for me and I have not** properly **rewarded him, and due to that** reason **people refrain from revealing to me** information regarding such plots, as they see no benefit for themselves. **Immediately** afterward, the verse states: **"And he commanded the book of remembrances of the chronicles to be brought"** (Esther 6:1).

Rava, characteristically, offers a psychologically acute reading: *shenat hamelech Achashveirosh mamash* — it means the actual king Achashveirosh, and his sleeplessness reflects genuine anxiety. Something fell in his mind — *nafla milta bedateih*: what was Esther doing inviting Haman to her feast? *Dilma etza ka shakli ilvay* — maybe they are plotting against him, and Haman is the man seeking to kill him.

Then the king's thought reversed itself: if there truly is such a plot, why has no one informed me of it? That would mean that no one who loves me has warned me. And why would that be? Perhaps, he concluded, because someone once did good for me and I never rewarded him — *dilma ika inish de-avad bi tivusa velo pariteih* — and therefore people have learned not to bother, since informing the king earns no gratitude. The absence of a conspiracy report becomes, in Rava's reading, the very evidence of a conspiracy. It is a memorable observation that cuts well beyond the *Megillah*: people are often motivated more by who they hate than by who they love.

### History Books

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּהְיוּ נִקְרָאִים״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּקְרָאִים מֵאֵילֵיהֶן. ״וַיִּמָּצֵא כָתוּב״ — ״כְּתָב״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ? מְלַמֵּד
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And they were read** before the king" (Esther 6:1). The Gemara explains that **this** passive form: "And they were read," **teaches that they were read** miraculously **by themselves.** It further says: **"And it was found written [*katuv*]"** (Esther 6:2). The Gemara asks: Why does the Megilla use the word *katuv*, which indicates that it was newly written? **It should have said: A writing [*ketav*]** was found, which would indicate that it had been written in the past. The Gemara explains: This **teaches**

The king ordered the history books brought and read aloud. The verse says *vayimatze katuv* — and it was found *written*, in the present tense, when one might have expected *katav*, the past.

### Divine Records

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁשִּׁמְשַׁי מוֹחֵק וְגַבְרִיאֵל כּוֹתֵב. אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי, דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי שֵׁילָא אִישׁ כְּפַר תְּמַרְתָּא: וּמָה כְּתָב שֶׁלְּמַטָּה שֶׁלִּזְכוּתָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל — אֵינוֹ נִמְחָק, כְּתָב שֶׁלְמַעְלָה — לָא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **that Shimshai,** the king's scribe who hated the Jews (see Ezra 4:17), **was erasing** the description of Mordecai's saving the king, **and** the angel **Gavriel was writing** it again. Therefore, it was indeed being written in the present. **Rabbi Asi said: Rabbi Sheila, a man of the village of Timarta, taught: If something written** down **below** in this world **that is for the benefit of the Jewish people cannot be erased, is it not all the more so** the case **that something written** up **above** in Heaven cannot be erased?

The *Gemara* explains (citing Tosafot on 86a): the attendant reading the chronicle disliked the Jews and was trying to erase the line recording Mordechai's deed, while the angel Gavriel was simultaneously rewriting it — hence *katuv*, being written, in the ongoing present.

Rav Shila of Kefar Tamarta expounded: if a record written below (*shel matah*), in the king's earthly chronicle, which benefits Israel cannot be erased, how much more so what is written above (*shel ma'alah*) — the divine record of Israel's merits — which can never be erased. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לֹא נַעֲשָׂה עִמּוֹ דָּבָר״. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאוֹהֲבִין אֶת מׇרְדֳּכַי, אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשּׂוֹנְאִים אֶת הָמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states that Ahasuerus was told with regard to Mordecai: **"Nothing has been done for him"** (Esther 6:3). **Rava said: It is not because they love Mordecai** that the king's servants said this, **but rather because they hate Haman.**

The king then asks what was done for Mordechai and is told: *lo na'asa imo davar* — nothing was done for him. Rava adds pointedly: they didn't say this because they loved Mordechai — they said it because they hated Haman.

## Honor Mordechai

### Haman's Suggestion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַעֲשֵׂה כֵן לְמָרְדֳּכַי״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַנּוּ מָרְדֳּכַי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הַיְּהוּדִי״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טוּבָא מָרְדְּכַי אִיכָּא בִּיהוּדָאֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הַיּוֹשֵׁב בְּשַׁעַר הַמֶּלֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse relates that Ahasuerus ordered Haman to fulfill his idea of the proper way to honor one who the king desires to glorify by parading him around on the king's horse while wearing the royal garments: "And do so to Mordecai the Jew who sits at the king's gate, let nothing fail of all that you have spoken" (Esther 6:10). The Gemara explains that when Ahasuerus said to Haman: **"And do so to Mordecai,"** Haman **said to him** in an attempt to evade the order: **Who is Mordecai?** Ahasuerus **said to him: "The Jew."** Haman then **said to him: There are several** men named **Mordecai among the Jews.** Ahasuerus then **said to him:** I refer to the one **"who sits at the king's gate."**

Haman had come early in the morning to ask the king's permission to hang Mordechai on the gallows he had built. Instead the king asks him: what should be done for the man whom the king wishes to honor? Haman, assuming the king means him, elaborates a grand proposal involving the royal garments and the royal horse. The king tells him: excellent — do all this for Mordechai the Jew, the one who sits in the king's gate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סַגִּי לֵיהּ בְּחַד דִּיסְקַרְתָּא, אִי נָמֵי בְּחַד נַהֲרָא. אָמַר לֵיהּ: הָא נָמֵי הַב לֵיהּ, ״אַל תַּפֵּל דָּבָר מִכֹּל אֲשֶׁר דִּבַּרְתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** Haman **said to him:** Why award him such a great honor? **It would** certainly **be enough for him** to receive **one village [*disekarta*]** as an estate, **or one river** for the levy of taxes. Ahasuerus **said to him: This too you must give him. "Let nothing fail of all that you have spoken,"** i.e., provide him with all that you proposed and spoke about in addition to what I had said.

Haman attempts to minimize the honor: perhaps a village would suffice, or a river — something less conspicuous than a royal procession. The king says: *hai nami hav lei* — give him that too, and everything else you mentioned besides. *Al tapel davar mikol asher dibarta* — let not a word of all you said go undone.

### Haman Attendant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּקַּח הָמָן אֶת הַלְּבוּשׁ וְאֶת הַסּוּס״. אֲזַל אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ דְּיָתְבִי רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ וּמַחְוֵי לְהוּ הִלְכוֹת קְמִיצָה לְרַבָּנַן, כֵּיוָן דְּחַזְיֵיהּ מָרְדְּכַי דְּאַפֵּיק לְקִבְלֵיהּ וְסוּסְיָא מֵיחַד בִּידֵיהּ, מִירְתַת. אֲמַר לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן: הַאי רַשִּׁיעָא לְמִיקְטַל נַפְשַׁי קָא אָתֵי זִילוּ מִקַּמֵּיהּ דִּי לָא תִּכָּווּ בְּגַחַלְתּוֹ. בְּהָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא נִתְעַטֵּף מָרְדְּכַי וְקָם לֵיהּ לִצְלוֹתָא, אֲתָא הָמָן וִיתֵיב לֵיהּ קַמַּיְיהוּ וְאוֹרֵיךְ עַד דְּסַלֵּיק מָרְדֳּכַי לִצְלוֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara describes what occurred as Haman went to follow the king's orders, as the verse states: **"Then Haman took the apparel and the horse"** (Esther 6:11). When **he went, he found** Mordecai **as the Sages were sitting before him, and he was demonstrating to them the *halakhot* of the handful,** i.e., the scooping out of a handful of flour from the meal-offering in order to burn it on the altar. **Once Mordecai saw him coming toward him with his horse's** reins **held in his hands, he became frightened,** and **he said to the Sages: This evil man has come to kill me. Go away from him so that you should not get burnt from his coals,** i.e., that you should not suffer harm as well. **At that moment Mordecai wrapped himself** in his prayer shawl **and stood up to pray. Haman came** over to where they were **and sat down before them and waited until Mordecai finished his prayer.**

Haman went to find Mordechai and found him sitting before his rabbinic students. Mordechai was teaching the laws of *kmitzah* — the handful offering of the *minchah* — which Rashi connects to the timing around Pesach, given that the fast had begun then. When Mordechai saw Haman approaching leading the king's horse, he was frightened — *mirtat* — uncertain whether Haman was coming to execute him. He told his students to flee so they would not be caught in the conflagration, then wrapped himself in prayer, standing in *Shemoneh Esrei*.

Haman sat and waited until Mordechai finished his *Shemoneh Esrei* — presumably because Mordechai felt that interrupting his prayer would make no difference to the outcome — and then addressed the assembled group.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: בְּמַאי עָסְקִיתוּ? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים, מַאן דִּמְנַדֵּב מִנְחָה מַיְיתֵי מְלֵי קוּמְצֵיהּ דְּסוּלְתָּא וּמִתְכַּפַּר לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲתָא מְלֵי קוּמְצֵי קִמְחָא דִּידְכוּ וְדָחֵי עַשְׂרָה אַלְפֵי כַּכְּרֵי כַסְפָּא דִּידִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רָשָׁע, עֶבֶד שֶׁקָּנָה נְכָסִים, עֶבֶד לְמִי וּנְכָסִים לְמִי?
>
> **Translation:** In the interim, as he waited, Haman **said to** the other Sages: **With what were you occupied? They said to him: When the Temple is standing, one who pledges a meal-offering would bring a handful of fine flour and achieve atonement with it. He said to them: Your handful of fine flour has come and cast aside my ten thousand pieces of silver,** which I had pledged toward the destruction of the Jewish people. When Mordecai finished praying, **he said to** Haman: **Wicked man,** when **a slave buys property, to whom belongs the slave and to whom belongs the property?** As I once bought you as a slave, what silver can be yours?

He asked: *be-mai askiseitu* — what have you been dealing with? The rabbis explained: in the time of the *Beit HaMikdash*, a person could bring the most humble offering — a single handful of flour — and be atoned for. Haman responded bitterly: *ata mallei kumtzei kimcha dichi vedachi asara alfei kikarei kaspa didi* — your handful of flour has overridden my ten thousand talents of silver. For at the very moment they had been studying these laws of the *kmitzah*, the king's mind had turned toward rewarding Mordechai, effectively overturning Haman's decree purchased with his silver.

Mordechai then rejoined: *eved shekanah nechasim eved lemi unechasim lemi* — a slave who acquires property: to whom does the slave belong, and to whom does the property belong? Going back to the earlier theme that Haman was once a slave of Mordechai — his wealth was never truly his own.

This moment carries a broader thematic weight. The story is set in the interregnum between the two *Batei Mikdash*, and the *Gemara* here layers several things simultaneously: Esther's fast and the *tzaakah* of the people, the merit of the *Beit HaMikdash* and its offerings, and Mordechai standing in *Shemoneh Esrei*. The *Gemara* could have presented only the study of *kmitzah*; the insertion of Mordechai at *Shemoneh Esrei* seems deliberately juxtaposed. There is a larger question here about whether *tefillah* in exile replaces and stands parallel to *korbanot*, or whether these are simply two streams both drawing on the same underlying merit, and the *Gemara* does not fully resolve it — but the juxtaposition seems intentional.

### Preparation Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קוּם לְבוֹשׁ הָנֵי מָאנֵי וּרְכוֹב הַאי סוּסְיָא, דְּבָעֵי לָךְ מַלְכָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא יָכֵילְנָא עַד דְּעָיֵילְנָא לְבֵי בָנֵי וְאֶשְׁקוֹל לְמַזְיָיא, דְּלָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְאִשְׁתַּמּוֹשֵׁי בְּמָאנֵי דְמַלְכָּא הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** Haman then **said to him: Mount** the horse **and ride.** Mordecai **said to him: I cannot** do so **until I enter the bathhouse** [***bei vanei***] **and trim my hair, for it is not proper conduct to use the king's garments in this state** that I am in now.

Mordechai said to Haman: I cannot do this now. *Ad de-aleina lebei banei ve'ashkol le'mazaya* — I must first go to the bathhouse and have my hair cut. One in my current state — having been fasting and wearing sackcloth — cannot properly make use of the king's garments and horse.

### Haman Attendant Continued

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁדַּרָה אֶסְתֵּר וַאֲסַרְתִּינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ בֵּי בָנֵי וּלְכוּלְּהוּ אוּמָּנֵי. עַיְּילֵיהּ אִיהוּ לְבֵי בָנֵי וְאַסְחְיֵהּ, וַאֲזַל וְאַיְיתִי זוּזָא מִבֵּיתֵיהּ וְקָא שָׁקֵיל בֵּיהּ מַזְיֵיהּ. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָא שָׁקֵיל לֵיהּ אִינְּגִיד וְאִיתְּנַח. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי קָא מִיתְּנַחַתְּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה חֲשִׁיב לֵיהּ לְמַלְכָּא מִכּוּלְּהוּ רַבְרְבָנוֹהִי, הַשְׁתָּא לִישַׁוְּיֵיהּ בַּלְנַאי וְסַפָּר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רָשָׁע, וְלָאו סַפָּר שֶׁל כְּפַר קַרְצוּם הָיִיתָ? תָּנָא: הָמָן, סַפָּר שֶׁל כְּפַר קַרְצוּם הָיָה עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁתַּיִם שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the meantime, **Esther sent** messengers and **closed all the bathhouses and all** the shops of **the craftsmen,** including the bloodletters and barbers. When Haman saw that there was nobody else to do the work, **he** himself **took** Mordecai **into the bathhouse and washed him, and** then **he went and brought scissors [*zuza*] from his house and trimmed his hair.** While **he was trimming** his hair **he injured himself and sighed.** Mordecai **said to him: Why do you sigh?** Haman **said to him: The man whom the king had** once **regarded above all his** other **ministers is now made a bathhouse attendant** [***balanei***] **and a barber.** Mordecai **said to him: Wicked man, were you not** once **the barber of the village of Kartzum?** If so, why do you sigh? You have merely returned to the occupation of your youth. It **was taught** in a *baraita*: **Haman was the barber of the village of Kartzum for twenty-two years.**

So Esther sent and locked up all the bathhouses and all the barbers (*umane*) in the city. The *uman* — the craftsman-attendant — appears here as a barber, though the same term is used for a bloodletter and a *mohel*, all sharing the common denominator of sharp implements. By closing off every alternative, Esther ensured that Haman himself would have to serve as Mordechai's attendant. It is also worth noting that Mordechai was now needing a haircut during *Chol HaMoed*, during the *ta'anit*, and during *Sefirah* — a confluence of circumstances that the *Gemara* notes without fully resolving.

### Hair Cutting

Haman brought Mordechai to the bathhouse, bathed and scrubbed him himself, then went home to retrieve a scissors — his own — and began cutting Mordechai's hair. As he cut, he let out a moan and a sigh — *ingad ve'istnach*. Mordechai asked: why are you sighing? Haman said: the man who was more important to the king than all his ministers — *hashata shavei balani ve'sapara* — now I've been reduced to being a bathhouse attendant and a barber?

Mordechai replied: *velav sapara de'kfar kartzum hayita* — weren't you already a barber in the village of Kartsum? You are simply returning to your origins. The *Braisah* confirms: Haman was the barber of the village of Kartsum for twenty-two years. His entire elevation was a departure from what he truly was, and now circumstances are restoring the natural order.

### Horse Mounting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתַר דְּשַׁקְלִינְהוּ לְמַזְיֵיהּ, לַבְשִׁינְהוּ לְמָאנֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סַק וּרְכֹב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא יָכֵילְנָא, דִּכְחִישָׁא חֵילַאי מִימֵי תַּעֲנִיתָא. גְּחֵין וּסְלֵיק. כִּי סָלֵיק, בְּעַט בֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא כְּתִיב לְכוּ: ״בִּנְפֹל אוֹיִבְךָ אַל תִּשְׂמָח״? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל בְּדִידְכוּ כְּתִיב: ״וְאַתָּה עַל בָּמוֹתֵימוֹ תִדְרוֹךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **After** Haman **trimmed his hair,** Haman **dressed** Mordecai in **the** royal **garments.** Haman then **said to him: Mount** the horse **and ride.** Mordecai **said to him: I am unable, as my strength has waned from the days of fasting** that I observed. Haman then **stooped down** before him **and** Mordecai **ascended** on him. **As he was ascending** the horse, Mordecai **gave** Haman **a kick.** Haman **said to him: Is it not written for you: "Do not rejoice when your enemy falls"** (Proverbs 24:17)? Mordecai **said to him: This** statement **applies** only to **Jews, but with regard to you it is written: "And you shall tread upon their high places"** (Deuteronomy 33:29).

After the haircut, Haman dressed Mordechai in the royal garments and told him to mount the horse. Mordechai said: I cannot climb up — *de'chashishachali mi'yemei ta'anita* — I am weakened from the days of fasting. So Haman bent down and Mordechai used him as a mounting-step to climb onto the horse.

As Mordechai mounted, he kicked Haman. Haman protested: is it not written *binfol oyvecha al tismach* — do not rejoice when your enemy falls? Mordechai replied: that verse applies between fellow Jews — *hani mili be'Yisrael* — but regarding you it is written *ve'ata al bamotaymo tidroch* — you shall trample upon their high places. Mordechai was literally walking on the neck and head of Haman, fulfilling the verse.

### Daughter's Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּקְרָא לְפָנָיו כָּכָה יֵעָשֶׂה לְאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר הַמֶּלֶךְ חָפֵץ בִּיקָרוֹ״. כִּי הֲוָה נָקֵיט וְאָזֵיל בִּשְׁבִילָא דְּבֵי הָמָן, חֲזִיתֵיהּ בְּרַתֵּיה דְּקָיְימָא אַאִיגָּרָא, סְבַרָה הַאי דִּרְכִיב — אֲבוּהּ, וְהַאי דִּמְסַגֵּי קַמֵּיהּ — מָרְדֳּכַי. שְׁקַלָה עֲצִיצָא דְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא וּשְׁדֵיתֵיהּ אַרֵישָׁא דַּאֲבוּהּ. דַּלִּי עֵינֵיהּ וַחֲזָת דַּאֲבוּהּ הוּא, נְפַלָה מֵאִיגָּרָא לְאַרְעָא וּמִתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And he proclaimed before him: Thus shall it be done to the man whom the king delights to honor"** (Esther 6:11). **As** Haman **was taking** Mordecai **along the street of Haman's house,** Haman's **daughter was standing on the roof and saw** the spectacle. **She thought** to herself that **the one who is riding** on the horse must be **her father, and the one walking before him** must be **Mordecai. She** then **took a chamber pot** full of feces **and cast its** contents **onto the head of her father,** whom she mistakenly took as Mordecai. When Haman **raised his eyes** in disgust afterward, and looked up at his daughter, **she saw that he was her father.** In her distress, **she fell from the roof to the ground and died.**

Haman called out before Mordechai: *kacha ye'aseh la'ish asher hamelech chafetz bikiro* — thus shall be done for the man the king wishes to honor. As they passed through the alleys near Haman's house, Haman's daughter was watching from the rooftop. She assumed — logically enough — that the man on the horse must be her father and the man walking in front must be Mordechai. She took the chamber pot of the bathroom and hurled it — aiming at the figure walking in front. But when the man on the horse lifted his eyes, she saw that it was her father. She fell from the roof to the ground and died.

### Return to Gate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּשׇׁב מׇרְדֳּכַי אֶל שַׁעַר הַמֶּלֶךְ״, אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: שֶׁשָּׁב לְשַׂקּוֹ וּלְתַעֲנִיתוֹ. ״וְהָמָן נִדְחַף אֶל בֵּיתוֹ אָבֵל וַחֲפוּי רֹאשׁ״, ״אָבֵל״ — עַל בִּתּוֹ, ״וַחֲפוּי רֹאשׁ״ — עַל שֶׁאֵירַע לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is as it is written: "And Mordecai returned to the king's gate"** (Esther 6:12). **Rav Sheshet said:** This means **that he returned to his sackcloth and his fasting** over the troubles of the Jewish people. Simultaneously, **"but Haman hastened to his house, mourning, and having his head covered"** (Esther 6:12). **"Mourning"; over** the death of **his daughter. "And having his head covered"; due to what had happened to him,** as his head was full of filth.

The verse then reads: *vayashav Mordechai el shaar hamelech* — and Mordechai returned to the king's gate — *ve'Haman nidchaf el beiso avel v'chafui rosh*. Rav Sheshes explains: *vayashav Mordechai* means he returned to his fasting and his sackcloth — *l'sako ul'ta'aniso* — he did not take his elevation for granted or assume redemption was now assured. Haman returned to his house mourning: *avel* for his daughter, *v'chafui rosh* in shame for what had been done to him.

## Wise Men's Warning

### Tells Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְסַפֵּר הָמָן לְזֶרֶשׁ אִשְׁתּוֹ וּלְכׇל אוֹהֲבָיו וְגוֹ׳״. קָרֵי לְהוּ ״אוֹהֲבָיו״ וְקָרֵי לְהוּ ״חֲכָמָיו״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר דְּבַר חָכְמָה, אֲפִילּוּ בְּאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, נִקְרָא חָכָם.
>
> **Translation:** The following verse states: **"And Haman recounted to Zeresh his wife and to all his friends** everything that had befallen him. Then his wise men and Zeresh his wife said to him: If Mordecai, before whom you have begun to fall, be of the seed of the Jews, then you will not prevail over him, but you shall fall before him" (Esther 6:13). The Gemara comments: At the beginning of the verse **it calls them "his friends," and** in the continuation of the verse **it calls them "his wise men." Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Whoever says something wise, even if** he is **from the nations of the world, is called** a **wise** man.

Haman then told his wife Zeresh and all his friends what had happened to him. The verse calls them *ohavav* — his friends — but elsewhere they are called *chachamav* — his wise men.

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the discrepancy: *kol ha'omer davar chochma afilu b'umos ha'olam nikra chacham* — whoever speaks a word of wisdom, even among the nations of the world, is called wise. When they were simply his companions they were his friends; when they spoke wisdom, they became wise men.

### Tribes Assessment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אִם מִזֶּרַע הַיְּהוּדִים מׇרְדֳּכַי וְגוֹ׳״. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִי מִשְּׁאָר שְׁבָטִים קָאָתֵי — יָכְלַתְּ לֵיהּ, וְאִי מִשֵּׁבֶט יְהוּדָה וּבִנְיָמִין וְאֶפְרַיִם וּמְנַשֶּׁה — לָא יָכְלַתְּ לֵיהּ. יְהוּדָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״יָדְךָ בְּעֹרֶף אוֹיְבֶיךָ״, אִינָךְ, דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ: ״לִפְנֵי אֶפְרַיִם וּבִנְיָמִין וּמְנַשֶּׁה עוֹרְרָה אֶת גְּבוּרָתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that their wise remark, which earned them their distinction, is contained in their advice: **"If Mordecai be of the seed of the Jews [*Yehudim*],** then you will not prevail over him" (Esther 6:13). The word *Yehudim* can also refer to people from the tribe of Judah. Haman's wise men thereby **said to him: If he descends from the other tribes, you can** still **prevail over him, but if he** descends **from the tribe of** either **Judah, Benjamin, Ephraim, or Manasseh, you cannot prevail over him.** With regard to **Judah,** the proof of this is **as it is written: "Your hand shall be on the neck of your enemies"** (Genesis 49:8), indicating that Judah will emerge victorious over his enemies. And the proof that Haman cannot prevail over **the others** that were mentioned is **as it is written with regard to them: "Before Ephraim and Benjamin and Manasseh, stir up Your might"** (Psalms 80:3).

What was the wisdom? They said: *im mizerah hayehudim Mordechai* — if he is from the seed of the Jews, *lo tuchal lo* — you will not prevail over him. The *Gemara* asks: they already knew he was Jewish — why does this only become meaningful now? The answer is the *chiddush*: beforehand, Haman was ascending and Mordechai descending, and in that configuration it seemed manageable. Now Mordechai is ascending and Haman is falling, and *that* dynamic — they now understand — cannot be reversed once it is in motion.

Specifically, they say: if Mordechai descends from one of the *other* tribes, you may yet prevail. But if he is from Yehuda, Binyamin, Ephraim, or Menashe — *lo tuchal lo*. The verses: Yehuda — *yadcha b'oref oyvecha*, your hand shall be at the throat of your enemies; the others — *lifnei Ephraim uVinyamin uMenashe orera es gevurascha*, bestir Your might before them. Of course Mordechai is identified as *Yehudi* and *Yemini*, tracing descent through both Yehuda and Binyamin, and Esther through the line of King Shaul — closing the circle with Shaul and Amalek that underlies the entire confrontation.

### Double Falling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי נָפוֹל תִּפּוֹל לְפָנָיו״. דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר אִלְעַאי: שְׁתֵּי נְפִילוֹת הַלָּלוּ, לָמָּה? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אוּמָּה זוֹ, מְשׁוּלָה לְעָפָר וּמְשׁוּלָה לְכוֹכָבִים. כְּשֶׁהֵן יוֹרְדִין — יוֹרְדִין עַד עָפָר, וּכְשֶׁהֵן עוֹלִין — עוֹלִין עַד לַכּוֹכָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The wise men continued: **"But you shall fall [*nafol tippol*] before him"** (Esther 6:13). **Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai interpreted** a verse **homiletically: Why are these two fallings,** *nafol* and *tippol*, mentioned here? The wise men **said to** Haman: **This** Jewish **nation is compared** in the Bible **to the dust** of the earth **and it is** also **compared to the stars** in heaven. This teaches you that **when they descend, they descend to the dust, and when they rise, they rise to the stars.** Accordingly, when Mordecai is on the rise, you will be utterly incapable of prevailing over him.

Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai expounds: what is the meaning of the double language *nafol tipol* — you shall certainly fall before him? The answer: *uma zu m'shula l'afar um'shula l'chochavim* — this nation is compared both to dust and to stars. When they descend, they descend to the dust; when they rise, they rise to the heavens. The doubling of *nafol tipol* captures this full arc — from the greatest of heights to the depths, or in reverse.

### Eunuchs Arrive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְסָרִיסֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ הִגִּיעוּ וַיַּבְהִילוּ״. מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהֱבִיאוּהוּ בְּבֶהָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"The king's chamberlains came, and they hastened [*vayavhilu*]** to bring Haman" (Esther 6:14). **This teaches that they brought him in disarray [*behala*],** not even giving him a chance to wash himself from the filth.

*Ve'sarisei hamelech higiu vayavhilu* — the king's eunuchs arrived and rushed Haman to the feast. The *Gemara* teaches: *melamed sheheviu'u b'vahala* — they brought him in haste. Note the contrast: Mordechai was given time to bathe and prepare; Haman comes in a rush, still in a state of disarray, with no such courtesy extended.

## Final Downfall

### Accusation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי נִמְכַּרְנוּ אֲנִי וְעַמִּי וְגוֹ׳ כִּי אֵין הַצָּר שֹׁוֶה בְּנֵזֶק הַמֶּלֶךְ״. אָמְרָה לוֹ: צַר זֶה, אֵינוֹ שֹׁוֶה בְּנֵזֶק שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ. אִיקְּנִי בַּהּ בְּוַשְׁתִּי וְקַטְלַהּ, הַשְׁתָּא אִיקְּנִי בְּדִידִי וּ(מ)בָעֵי לְמִקְטְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** During the banquet Esther said to Ahasuerus: **"For we are sold, I and my people,** to be destroyed, to be slain, and to be annihilated. But if we had been sold merely for bondmen and bondwomen, I would have held my tongue, **since the affliction [*tzar*] would not have been worth [*eino shoveh*] the damage to the king"** (Esther 7:4). The Gemara explains that **she said to him: This adversary [*tzar*] is not concerned [*eino shoveh*] about the damage** that he is constantly causing **to the king. First he was jealous of Vashti and killed her,** as it has been explained that Memucan, who suggesting killing Vashti, was Haman; **now he is jealous of me and desires to kill me.**

Esther says before the king: *ki nimcharnu ani v'ami* — for I and my people have been sold. *Ki ein hatzar shoveh b'nezek hamelech* — this enemy has no concern with the king's losses. She then tells Achashveirosh: Haman was jealous of Vashti and got her killed — echoing the identification of Memuchan with Haman — *iknei b'didi umiba'i lemiktlai* — he is jealous of me and seeks my death as well. She is deliberately inflaming Achashveirosh's jealousy over both his former and current queen.

### Angel Intervention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר הַמֶּלֶךְ אֲחַשְׁוֵרוֹשׁ וַיֹּאמֶר לְאֶסְתֵּר הַמַּלְכָּה״. ״וַיֹּאמֶר״ ״וַיֹּאמֶר״ לְמָה לִי? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בַּתְּחִלָּה עַל יְדֵי תּוּרְגְּמָן, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמְרָה לֵיהּ מִדְּבֵית שָׁאוּל קָאָתֵינָא, מִיָּד: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לְאֶסְתֵּר הַמַּלְכָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"Then said the king Ahasuerus and said to Esther the queen"** (Esther 7:5). The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need it to say **"said"** and again **"said"? Rabbi Abbahu said: At first** he spoke to her **through the translator,** who would interpret on his behalf, because he thought that she was a common woman of lowly ancestry. **Once she told him that she came from the house of Saul, immediately** it says: **"And said to Esther the queen."** Ahasuerus himself spoke to her, as if she had royal lineage, she was a woman befitting his status.

*Vayomer hamelech Achashveirosh vayomer l'Esther hamalka* — the verse uses *vayomer* twice, which Rabbi Avahu explains: initially the king was speaking through a translator. Once Esther declared *ki nimcharnu ani v'ami* and revealed her descent from Shaul, establishing her royal lineage and knowledge of languages, the king addressed her directly — *miyad vayomer l'Esther* — he dropped the *meturgeman*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֹּאמֶר אֶסְתֵּר אִישׁ צַר וְאוֹיֵב הָמָן הָרָע הַזֶּה״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיְתָה מַחְווֹה כְּלַפֵּי אֲחַשְׁוֵרוֹשׁ, וּבָא מַלְאָךְ וְסָטַר יָדָהּ כְּלַפֵּי הָמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"And Esther said: An adversary and enemy is this wicked Haman"** (Esther 7:6). **Rabbi Elazar said:** This **teaches that she was** in fact **pointing toward Ahasuerus,** indicating that in fact he was an adversary and enemy, **and an angel came and pushed her hand toward Haman.**

Esther then said: *ish tzar v'oyev Haman hara hazeh*. But why does she not simply say "Haman"? The *Gemara* explains — a famous *Midrash* — that Rabbi Elazar teaches: *melamed shehayesa mechava k'lapei Achashveirosh* — she was gesturing toward Achashveirosh himself, since he too bore responsibility. A *malach* came and redirected her hand toward Haman. The message is not lost: Achashveirosh's complicity was real and it was almost named aloud.

### Garden Angels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַמֶּלֶךְ קָם בַּחֲמָתוֹ וְגוֹ׳ וְהַמֶּלֶךְ שָׁב מִגִּנַּת הַבִּיתָן״. מַקֵּישׁ שִׁיבָה לְקִימָה: מָה קִימָה בְּחֵימָה, אַף שִׁיבָה בְּחֵימָה. דַּאֲזַל וְאַשְׁכַּח לְמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת דְּאִידְּמוֹ לֵיהּ כְּגַבְרֵי, וְקָא עָקְרִי לְאִילָנֵי דְּבוּסְתָּנֵי, וַאֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי עוֹבָדַיְיכוּ? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: דְּפַקְּדִינַן הָמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And the king arose** from the banquet of wine **in his wrath** and went into the palace garden" (Esther 7:7), and the next verse states: **"Then the king returned out of the palace garden** to the place of the wine drinking" (Esther 7:8). The Gemara comments: The verses here **compare his returning to his arising: Just as his arising** was **in wrath, so too, his returning** was **in wrath.** And why did he return in wrath? **For** when **he went** out **he found ministering angels who appeared to him as people and they were uprooting trees from the garden, and he said to them: What are you doing? They said to him: Haman commanded us** to do this.

*V'hamelech kam b'chamato* — the king rose in his anger. *V'hamelech shav miginat habitan* — and then he returned from the garden. The verse creates a parallel between his rising and his return: just as he rose in fiery anger, so too when he returned he had not calmed down at all — *mah kima b'chema af shiva b'chema*.

Why hadn't he calmed down during his walk in the garden? Because when he went outside, he found heavenly angels appearing in the guise of men, uprooting the beautiful trees of the orchard. When he demanded to know who had ordered this, they replied: *d'fakdinan Haman* — Haman commanded us. So his anger was compounded, not relieved.

### Falls on Bed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהָמָן נוֹפֵל עַל הַמִּטָּה״, ״נוֹפֵל״ — ״נָפַל״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁבָּא מַלְאָךְ וְהִפִּילוֹ עָלֶיהָ, אֲמַר: וַיי מִבֵּיתָא, וַיי מִבָּרָא. ״וַיֹּאמֶר הַמֶּלֶךְ הֲגַם לִכְבּוֹשׁ אֶת הַמַּלְכָּה עִמִּי בַּבָּיִת״.
>
> **Translation:** And when **he entered his house** he saw that **"Haman was falling upon the bed"** (Esther 7:8). The Gemara asks: Why does it say **"was falling" [*nofel*]** in the present tense, implying that he was currently falling? **It should have said "fell" [*nafal*]** in the past tense. **Rabbi Elazar said: This teaches that an angel came and pushed him down on it,** and every time he would try to stand up, the angel would push him down again. Ahasuerus **said: Woe unto** me **in the house and woe** unto me **outside,** as the verse continues: **"Then the king said: Will he even force the queen before me in the house?"** (Esther 7:8).

*V'Haman nofel al hamita* — Haman was falling upon the bed where Esther lay. The present-tense *nofel* is odd — it should say *nafal*, past tense. Rabbi Elazar teaches: *melamed sheba malach v'hipilo aleiha* — a *malach* came and was pushing him down onto the bed. Haman was not trying to be in that position; he was being maneuvered there. The king returned and said: *vai mibeisa vai mibara* — woe from inside, woe from outside — everywhere I look, Haman is doing terrible things. *Hagam lichbosh es hamalka imi babayit* — are you even trying to subdue the queen with me here in the house?

### Harvona's Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר חַרְבוֹנָה וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אַף חַרְבוֹנָה רָשָׁע — בְּאוֹתָהּ עֵצָה הָיָה, כֵּיוָן שֶׁרָאָה שֶׁלֹּא נִתְקַיְּימָה עֲצָתוֹ, מִיָּד בָּרַח. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וְיַשְׁלֵךְ עָלָיו וְלֹא יַחְמוֹל מִיָּדוֹ בָּרוֹחַ יִבְרָח״.
>
> **Translation:** **"And Harbonah,** one of the chamberlains, **said** before the king, Behold also, the gallows fifty cubits high, which Haman has made for Mordecai, who spoke good for the king, stands in the house of Haman" (Esther 7:9). **Rabbi Elazar said: Harbonah was also wicked and involved in that plot,** as he too wanted Mordecai executed. **Once he saw that his plot had not succeeded, he immediately fled** and joined Mordecai's side. **And this is** the meaning of **that which is written: "It hurls itself at him, and does not spare; he would fain flee out of its hand"** (Job 27:22), indicating that when God sends calamity upon a wicked person, his friends immediately flee from him.

Charvona then spoke up and identified the gallows that Haman had built for Mordechai. Rabbi Elazar teaches: Charvona was himself a *rasha* who had been part of the plot — *af Charvona rasha b'osah eitzah hayah*. Once he saw things turning, he immediately switched sides — *kivan she'ra'ah lo niskaymu, miyad barach* — running away from Haman the moment it became clear the plan had failed. He was an opportunist, not a hero. The verse is cited: *vayashlech alav v'lo yachmol miyado baroach yivrach* — he has no compassion, he will run away. And yet — as the *Gemara* implies — even someone with less-than-ideal motives can end up doing the right thing.

### Anger Subsides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַחֲמַת הַמֶּלֶךְ שָׁכָכָה״. שְׁתֵּי שְׁכִיכוֹת הַלָּלוּ לָמָּה? אַחַת שֶׁל מַלְכּוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם וְאַחַת שֶׁל אֲחַשְׁוֵרוֹשׁ. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: אַחַת שֶׁל אֶסְתֵּר וְאַחַת שֶׁל וַשְׁתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"Then the king's wrath was assuaged [*shakhakha*]"** (Esther 7:10). The Gemara asks: **Why are there two assuagings here?** The term *shakhakha* is used rather than *shaka* and indicates doubled wrath. There was **one** assuaging of the wrath **of the King of the universe, and one** of the wrath **of Ahasuerus. And some say:** Ahasuerus's wrath burned within him for two reasons; **one due to** Haman's involvement with **Esther, and one due to** his involvement with **Vashti,** and now they were both assuaged.

*V'chamasan hamelech shachacha* — the king's anger abated. The *Gemara* asks: why is *shachacha* written with what appears to be a doubled form? Two separate calming-downs are implied. One opinion: one for God, whose anger had also been stirred — *akhat shemalkho shel olam* — and one for Achashveirosh himself. Another opinion: *akhat shel Esther ve'akhat shel Vashti* — the king was appeased regarding both queens, having been angry over the loss of Vashti and having feared for Esther, and Esther's accusation against Haman — that he was jealous of Vashti and got her killed — addressed both grievances.

One should note that the *Megillah* itself never explicitly states that Vashti was killed. It says only that she should no longer appear before the king — *asher lo tavo Vashti*. The popular assumption of her execution is read in from the *Gemara*. As one approach noted, in Roman times banishment carried the legal consequence that anyone could kill the banished person and claim their property — which may bring the *peshat* closer to the *derash* than it first appears.

## Five Changes

### Joseph's Mistake

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְכֻלָּם נָתַן לָאִישׁ חֲלִיפוֹת שְׂמָלוֹת וּלְבִנְיָמִין נָתַן חָמֵשׁ חֲלִיפוֹת״. אֶפְשָׁר דָּבָר שֶׁנִּצְטַעֵר בּוֹ אוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק —
>
> **Translation:** Before continuing its midrashic interpretation of the rest of the book of Esther, the Gemara expounds a verse concerning Joseph that relates to the Megilla: **"To all of them he gave each man changes of clothing, but to Benjamin he gave** three hundred pieces of silver, and **five changes** of clothing" (Genesis 45:22). The Gemara asks: **Is it possible that in the** very **thing from which that righteous man** Joseph **had suffered,** as his father's show of favoritism toward him aroused the enmity of his brothers,

The *Gemara* now pivots to a passage from Bereishit: *ve'chulam natan le'ish chalifot semalot uleBinyamin natan chamesh chalifot semalot* — Yosef gave each of his brothers a change of garments but gave Binyamin five changes. One might ask: is it conceivable that the very thing that caused Yosef HaTzaddik such suffering — his father's favoritism in giving him a special garment — he himself would repeat, knowingly, causing pain to the other brothers again?

### Benjamin's Hint

Rava bar Mechasya cites Rav Chama bar Guria citing Rav: because of a mere two *selaim* of extra wool — *milat* — that Yaakov added to Yosef's garment beyond what he gave the others — it was not the elaborate coat of many colors we imagine, just a slightly longer hem — *nisgalgal hadavar veyardu avoteinu leMitzrayim*: the chain of events unfolded and our ancestors descended to Egypt. Would Yosef truly repeat this mistake?

Rabbi Binyamin bar Yafet answers: no — it was not favoritism but a *remez*, a hint. *Remez ramaz lo sheatid ben latzet mimenu* — Yosef was hinting to Binyamin that a descendant would one day emerge from him *sheyetzei milifnei hamelech bechamisha levushei malkhut* — who would go forth from before the king wearing five royal garments.

### Mordechai's Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמׇרְדֵּכַי יָצָא בִּלְבוּשׁ מַלְכוּת תְּכֵלֶת וָחוּר וְתַחְרִיךְ בּוּץ וְאַרְגָּמָן וַעֲטֶרֶת זָהָב גְּדוֹלָה
>
> **Translation:** The fulfillment: *uMordekhai yatza bilevush malkhut techelet vachur vetachrikh butz veargaman veateret zahav gedola* — and Mordechai went forth from before the king in royal garments of blue and white, with a wrapping of fine linen and purple, and a great golden crown.

The fulfillment: *uMordekhai yatza bilevush malkhut techelet vachur vetachrikh butz veargaman veateret zahav gedola* — and Mordechai went forth from before the king in royal garments of blue and white, with a wrapping of fine linen and purple, and a great golden crown. Counting these, there are precisely five royal garments. The five changes of clothing that Yosef gave Binyamin were not an act of favoritism at all — they were a prophetic gesture, pointing centuries forward to the elevation of Mordechai, Binyamin's descendant, in the court of Achashveirosh. We will continue from this *Yosef* digression when we return.
