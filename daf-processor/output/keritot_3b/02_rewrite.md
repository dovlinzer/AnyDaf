# Keritot 3b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Multiple Offerings

### Review & Context

Today's shiur opens on the bottom of 2b, where the previous discussion reviewed the various *mishnayot* throughout the *masechet* that give specific numbers, with particular attention to what the number in our *mishnah* is meant to convey. The central point established was that the number in our *mishnah* signals that one may bring multiple *chataot* even within a single state of forgetfulness — though not thirty-six, since two of the cases involve *mitzvot aseh*, and Rashi later specifies the number as thirty-four. The review also covered the other *mishnayot* in the *masechet* that address variations on the themes of *korban chatat*, *asham talui*, *mechusrei kaparah*, and related topics.

### One Forgetfulness

The *gemara* resumes with the phrase *amar mar*, directing attention to the ruling: *hevi'um kulam behe'elam achat chayav al kol achat ve'achat* — if one performed all of these transgressions within a single state of forgetfulness, one is liable for each and every one separately. This represents the clearest case: each act is distinct, each transgression is distinct, and therefore each warrants its own *korban*.

The discussion then turns to the underlying principle. The *gemara* begins by establishing that complete exemption is certainly not an option. The verse states *ki kol asher ya'aseh mikol hato'evot ha'eleh venichretu* — whoever performs any of these abominations shall be cut off. If one transgression incurs *karet*, and *karet* in turn implies a *chatat* when done *beshogeg*, then committing multiple such transgressions in one state of forgetfulness clearly does not render one *patur*. The *gemara* notes this as self-evident.

### Single Offering?

The real question, then, is whether multiple transgressions committed in one state of forgetfulness might nonetheless require only a single *chatat*. As a point of comparison: if one committed the same *aveira* multiple times within one uninterrupted state of forgetfulness — for example, forgetting that cooking on *Shabbat* was prohibited and cooking five separate times without any intervening awareness of the *issur* — one brings only a single *korban*. The question is whether the same logic might extend to different sins: perhaps even distinct transgressions, if performed within one state of forgetfulness, should require only one *chatat*. Where does one find the foundational principle that separate sins committed in a single state of forgetfulness each require their own *korban*?

## Sister Prohibition

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan addresses this with a well-known formulation: *lekach yatzat karet ba'achoto lechaleik* — this is why the *Torah* stated *karet* explicitly in connection with the prohibition of sleeping with one's sister, even though a general verse at the end of *Parshat Acharei Mot* or *Kedoshim* already assigns *karet* to all *arayot* collectively. The general verse states *ki kol asher ya'aseh mikol hato'evot ha'eleh venichretu*, which encompasses all prohibited relations. Why, then, did the *Torah* need to reiterate *karet* specifically for one's sister? Rabbi Yochanan's answer is that the *Torah* singled out *achoto* in order *lechaleik* — to treat each case individually. The explicit *karet* by *achoto* signals that each of the *arayot* is to be treated distinctly, and since *karet* is the basis for *chatat* liability when the act is *beshogeg*, each separate transgression will generate its own *chatat*.

### Bibi bar Abaye

Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye challenges this derivation. He argues: *eima achoto deparat kra nitchayev chatat alah* — perhaps the *Torah*'s explicit mention of *karet* by the sister case generates a separate *korban* only for the sister prohibition itself, while all other *arayot* might yield only a single *chatat* regardless of how many are committed in one state of forgetfulness. The *gemara* immediately presses back on this objection: does Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye not accept the hermeneutical principle of *kol davar shehayah bichlal veyatza min haklal lelamed, lo lelamed al atzmo yatza ela lelamed al haklal kulo yatza*? When something that was part of a broader category is singled out with a specific ruling, that ruling is not treated as an exception applying only to that case — rather, it serves as a paradigm for the entire category.

### General Rules

To illustrate this principle, the *gemara* invokes the case of *shelamim*: the verse specifies that eating from a *korban shelamim* while *tamei* incurs *karet*. Yet *shelamim* was already included within the broader category of all *korbanos*. Why was it singled out? *Lehakish aleihem* — to use it as a paradigm. Just as *shelamim* is a *kodshei mizbe'ach* and one incurs *karet* for eating it while *tamei*, so too all *kodshei mizbe'ach* are subject to the same rule. The singling-out of *shelamim* simultaneously excludes *kodshei bedek habayit* from the *karet* prohibition and establishes *korbanos* as the paradigm for the rule's application. In the same way, the singling-out of *achoto* should instruct us about the entire category of *arayot*.

## Refining Principle

### Specific Traits

Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye is not satisfied. He accepts the hermeneutical principle — he has no objection to deriving a rule from a paradigm — but he presses on what the specific characteristics of *achoto* actually teach. By the analogy to *shelamim*, what was learned is defined by the relevant features of the paradigm case. Applied here: *mah achoto meyuchedet she'hi ervah* — a sister is *ervah*. One might therefore conclude that only *arayot* generate separate *chataot*, but not other sins that carry *karet*. Furthermore, *ve'ein lah heter bechayei osrah* — a sister can never become permissible during the lifetime of the one who renders her forbidden. This characteristic would exclude a married woman, *eshes ish*, who *can* become permissible if her husband dies. Therefore, Rabbi Bibi argues, the *achoto* derivation might apply only to permanently forbidden *arayot*, not to all *karet* transgressions and perhaps not even to all *arayot*. This leaves open the question of how we know that every separate *karet* offense, regardless of category, generates a distinct *chatat*.

### Explicit Verse

Rabbi Yona — and some say Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua — resolves this by pointing to an explicit verse: *ki kol asher ya'aseh mikol hato'evot ha'eil venichretu*. This verse at the end of the *parasha* expressly groups all the *arayot* together under a single *karet* formulation. The implication is: *hukshu kol ha'arayot kulan l'achoto* — all the *arayot* are explicitly equated with *achoto* with respect to *karet*. Once the verse itself links them all together, one is not required to examine what characteristics of *achoto* define the paradigm. The verse states that they all share the same *karet*, and therefore: *mah achoto chayav aleiha bifnei atzmah af kol chayav aleiha bifnei atzmah* — just as one is liable separately for *achoto*, one is liable separately for each of the *arayot*.

The *gemara* notes, however, that this still leaves a question: how do we extend the principle beyond *arayot* to all other *karet* transgressions? The *gemara* appears to assume that once the derivation is no longer confined to *achoto* alone, generalization follows, though the question could still be raised.

The *gemara* then introduces a further point. There is also a verse concerning *niddah* — *ve'el ishah benidat tumatah lo tikrav* — and the question is raised: why does the verse say *ishah* (woman) when it could simply have said *niddah*? Rashi explains that through the lens of rabbinic usage — where *niddah* names the woman rather than describing the state — the extra word *ishah* comes to teach: *lechaleik al kol ishah ve'ishah*, each woman is treated separately, meaning each *ervah* is a distinct case.

## Woman & Sister

### Niddah Woman

The *gemara* draws out a further implication of this word *ishah*. If the Rabbis can derive the principle of *lechalek* — treating each *ervah* separately — from the extra word *ishah* in the *niddah* prohibition, then it would seem one does not need the derivation from *achoto* at all for this purpose. The *gemara* acknowledges as much: *rabbanan nami tipok leh me've'el ishah benidat tumatah* — the Rabbis could have derived the point of separating the cases from this verse. The question then becomes: if the principle can be learned from *ishah*, what additional work does the explicit *karet* by *achoto* perform?

The *gemara* answers: *mibaei leh lechaleik al achoto ve'al achot aviv ve'al achot immo* — it is needed to distinguish between the case of one's own sister, one's father's sister, and one's mother's sister, and to teach that even these closely related prohibitions are treated as distinct for purposes of *chatat*. One might have thought that since these are all variations of the "sister" category, a single act involving one person who happens to be all three simultaneously would incur only one *chatat*. The explicit statement about *achoto* teaches otherwise.

To this the *gemara* objects: *harei shemos mechulakim ve'gufot mechulakim* — these are already distinct prohibitions (*shemos mechulakim*) and, in the ordinary case, distinct persons (*gufot mechulakim*). Where there are separate *lavin* and separate people or objects, no verse is needed to establish separate *chataot*. The *gemara* therefore refines the answer: the verse is needed specifically for a case where *achoto* and *achot aviv* and *achot immo* are all the same individual. While such a scenario requires considerable creative genealogy — best left to a discussion in Yevamos — the case is theoretically possible: a single person who stands in all three of these relationships simultaneously. There one person is involved (*gufot lo mechulakim*), yet the *aveirot* are distinct. The extra word *achoto* teaches that even in that case, the separate *shemot* — the separate prohibitions — are sufficient to generate separate *chataot*.

This articulates a fundamental principle: the criterion for multiple *chataot* does not require multiple persons or multiple objects. A single act, a single person, even a single moment — if multiple distinct prohibitions were violated, each generates its own *chatat*.

### Full vs. Half

The *gemara* introduces yet another view: *leRabbi Yitzchak de'amar chayvei keritot bichlal hayu, velama yatzat karet be'achoto ladun bekaret velo bemalkut*. Rabbi Yitzchak holds that the separate mention of *karet* by *achoto* comes to teach something different entirely — not *lechalek* for *chataot*, but to establish that *karet* for incestuous relations is not displaced by *malkut*. The background question is whether one who received *malkut* for a *karet*-eligible transgression is thereby discharged from *karet*: *kol chayvei keritot shelaku nifteru miyedei keritatan*. Rabbi Yitzchak disputes this and uses the explicit *karet* of *achoto* to demonstrate that *malkut* does not exempt from *karet*. (The *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Yitzchak on this point, but his derivation reorients what *achoto* is teaching.)

Having used *achoto* for this purpose, Rabbi Yitzchak no longer has it available to derive *lechalek*. The *gemara* asks: *lechaleik mena lei?* — from where does Rabbi Yitzchak derive that each *ervah* generates a separate *chatat*? The answer is: *nafka lei me've'el ishah benidat tumatah* — he derives it from the word *ishah* in the *niddah* verse, precisely as discussed above.

In turn, the *gemara* asks: since the Rabbis can also use *ishah* for *lechalek*, what do they do with *karet* at *achoto*? Their answer is that it was needed to address the case of one's full sister — *achoto shehi bat aviv u'vat immo*. The *Torah* explicitly prohibits one's paternal half-sister and one's maternal half-sister. A full sister, being both, might be thought to be covered by either prohibition without requiring a separate verse. Yet conceptually, a full sister is a distinct category from each type of half-sister. One could derive her prohibition through a *kal v'chomer*, but a *kal v'chomer* cannot establish *onesh* — punishment. This is the principle of *ein onshim min hadin*.

### Ein Onshim

The principle of *ein onshim min hadin* — that punishment cannot be derived from logical inference alone — is significant here. The *Torah* assigns *karet* explicitly to the half-sister cases. One might reason by *kal v'chomer* that a full sister, who incorporates both prohibitions, must also incur *karet*. Yet the *gemara* holds that such reasoning is insufficient for imposing a punishment as severe as *karet*. Without a *pasuk*, a *karet* obligation cannot stand. The extra *achoto* in the verse serves therefore to provide that explicit textual basis for the full sister, ensuring that her *karet* rests on the verse rather than on inference alone.

The *gemara* notes, somewhat pointedly, that Rabbi Yitzchak — who believes *onshim min hadin* is valid — does not need *achoto* for this purpose. He holds that the *kal v'chomer* for a full sister's *karet* is legitimate. Alternatively, the *gemara* offers: *yalif ones me'azhara* — since a full sister's prohibition can be derived textually (even if not by an explicit separate verse), Rabbi Yitzchak can link her *karet* to her prohibition by analogy, which is considered more than pure logic and therefore permissible.

A sharp point emerges here: the *nafka minah* of whether a *karet* obligation is textually grounded extends beyond the realm of divine punishment. The practical halakhic consequence is the ability to obligate a *chatat*: without a legitimate basis for *karet*, there is no basis for the *chatat* when done *beshogeg*. The system requires that we can properly establish *karet* status — not merely that we believe God will act in a certain way — for all the downstream halachic implications to follow.

## Separate Sins

### Two Lavin

The *gemara* now advances to a more sweeping principle, stated by Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: *kol makom she'ata motzei shnei lavin ve'karet echad, chilek chataot beineihem* — wherever the *Torah* presents two separate prohibitions with a single *karet* covering both, each prohibition nevertheless generates a distinct *chatat*. The singularity of the *karet* formulation does not collapse the two prohibitions into a single *aveira* for purposes of *chatat* liability. As long as there are separate *lavin*, the transgressions are sufficiently distinct.

### Anointing Oil

The illustrative case is that of the *shemen hamishchah*, the anointing oil. The verse contains two separate prohibitions: *al besar adam lo yisach* — do not apply the anointing oil to a person's skin — and *uvmatkunto lo ta'asu kamohu* — do not compound a private batch of oil according to the same formula. These are two distinct *lavin*. Yet the verse then assigns a single *karet* to both: *ish asher yirkach kamohu v'asher yitein mimenu al zar venichreat me'amav*. One might have thought that this single *karet* covering both prohibitions means that violating both in one state of forgetfulness incurs only one *chatat*. Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya teaches that this is not so. The separation of the *lavin* is what counts; the unity of the *karet* is irrelevant to the question of how many *chataot* are owed.

### Reconciliation

This raises an immediate difficulty: *vachi me'achar delavin mechulakim, karet achoto dechasav Rachamana lama li?* If the principle is that separate *lavin* automatically entail separate *chataot*, why was all the earlier discussion necessary? The entire elaborate derivation from *achoto* — with all its qualifications, the challenge of Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye, the response from *ishah*, and so forth — now appears superfluous.

The *gemara* responds that the earlier verses were in fact needed for other purposes, and the principle of separate *lavin* generating separate *chataot* makes them redundant only with respect to that specific teaching. According to Rabbi Yitzchak, *karet* by *achoto* was needed to establish that *malkut* does not discharge *karet*. According to the Rabbis, it was needed to establish the *karet* obligation for a full sister on textual grounds (*ein onshim min hadin*). Rashi understands the *gemara* to be saying that those verses were genuinely needed for their other purposes; the implication for *lechalek* was incidental and, in hindsight, unnecessary given Rabbi Elazar's principle. This resolution is admittedly somewhat unusual: one is left wondering where Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya himself derived the principle, since it was not stated as a derivation but seemingly as an *a priori* rule. The *gemara* does not address this directly.

The upshot, in any case, is now fully established: separate *chataot* are owed whenever the *aveirot* involve separate *lavin* — regardless of whether the act is singular or multiple, regardless of whether there is one *karet* or separate *karet* formulations, and regardless of whether the same person or object is involved, so long as the prohibitions are distinct.

## Mishnah Support

### Non-Sequential

Rav Natan bar Yitzchak offers additional support from the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* lists: *hamfatem et hashemen* (one who compounds the anointing oil), *hamfatem et haktoret* (one who compounds the incense), and *vehasach beshemen hamishchah* (one who anoints oneself with the anointing oil). The ordering is notable: the two cases involving the *shemen hamishchah* — compounding it and applying it — are not listed consecutively. Instead, the case of *ktoret* is inserted between them. Why? *Lama li detana hamfatem et haktoret bemetzia* — why did the *tanna* place the *ktoret* case in the middle?

The *gemara* answers: *lav ha kamashma lan* — it must be to convey the following. Just as *ktoret* is a separate *lav* and therefore incurs a separate *chatat*, the same applies to the two *shemen* cases. Since *ktoret* carries its own *lav* and its own *karet*, it generates a distinct *chatat*. The *mishnah*'s insertion of *ktoret* between the two *shemen* cases is a structural signal: even the two *shemen* prohibitions, though they involve the same substance and share a single *karet* verse, are treated as separate *aveirot* generating separate *chataot* — precisely because they are separate *lavin*.

### Not Stylistic

One might object that the *tanna* placed the two *pitum* cases — compounding oil and compounding incense — together for stylistic reasons, grouping the acts of compounding, rather than to signal anything about the *shemen* cases. The *gemara* rejects this. Had the *tanna* intended to group the *pitum* cases, the order could easily have been: compounding incense, compounding oil, and then anointing with oil. That arrangement would have preserved the grouping of the two *pitum* cases while still juxtaposing the two *shemen* cases. Instead, the *tanna* deliberately separated the two *shemen* cases, and this separation carries a message: *deyesh chiluk chataot beineihem* — there is a distinction of *chataot* between them. *Shema mina*.

The *gemara* acknowledges that one might have thought this was already implicit from the *mishnah*'s overall purpose — which is, after all, to enumerate separate cases that each generate a distinct *chatat*. But as the discussion develops, it becomes clear that whether the *mishnah* is claiming a precise count of *chataot* will itself become a matter of inquiry. Rav Natan bar Yitzchak therefore draws on the specific ordering of the *mishnah* as independent structural evidence, beyond any inference from the total count.

## Thirty-Four

### One Person?

The question now arises: can a single person actually commit all thirty-six (or thirty-four) transgressions listed in the *mishnah* within a single state of forgetfulness? The entire purpose of listing the number was to establish that one person could potentially bring that many *chataot* in a single state of forgetting. But a closer look reveals a problem.

*Ei b'gavra* — if the *mishnah* is speaking from the perspective of a man, then the case of a woman who allows an animal to be brought upon her (*dal isha venirva*) is unavailable to a man, which reduces the count by one to thirty-three. *Ei b'nashi kachashiv* — if the *mishnah* is speaking from a woman's perspective, then the case of *haba al hazachar* and the case of *dal behema* (a man penetrating an animal) are unavailable, reducing the count by two.

### Homosexual Act

Rabbi Yochanan resolves this by saying *le'olam tana gavrei* — the *mishnah* speaks throughout from the perspective of a man. He then reinterprets the case that had appeared to refer to a woman with an animal: *vetani hava al hazachar vehevi zachar alav* — the *mishnah* is actually listing two separate cases, namely a man who performs the active role in a homosexual act, and a man who performs the passive role.

This raises the question of whether those constitute two distinct sins. The *gemara* identifies the view behind this interpretation as that of Rabbi Yishmael, *de'amar chayav shtayim* — who holds that the two positions in a homosexual act are considered two distinct *aveirot* and therefore incur two separate *chataot*. This follows from the fact that Rabbi Yishmael derives the prohibition from two separate verses.

The first verse is *ve'et zachar lo tishkav mishkevei isha* — with a male you shall not lie as one lies with a woman. This verse addresses the one who performs the act, the active partner, with the phrase *mishkevei isha* emphasizing that the actor is the one taking the role normally associated with lying with a woman. The second source is *velo yihyeh kadesh mibnei Yisrael* — there shall be no *kadesh* among the children of Israel, which Rabbi Yishmael understands as addressing the receptive partner. Since two separate verses generate the two aspects of the prohibition, they are treated as two distinct *aveirot*, each with its own *chatat*.

The *gemara* notes in passing an interesting historical dimension: in the ancient Roman world, and in various societies where such acts were prevalent, a sharp social distinction existed between the active and receptive roles — the receptive role being associated with lower social status, with slaves, servants, or those in a subordinate position. Whether or not this is the conceptual background of the *Torah*'s language in *kadesh*, it helps explain how the Talmud frames these as conceptually distinct acts rather than a single unified transgression.

Rabbi Yishmael's position also entails a consequence for the case of an animal: *haba al habehemah vehevi behemah alav* — even if one argued that a man could play both roles with an animal, Rabbi Yishmael would not separate those into two distinct *aveirot*, because the textual derivation from two verses applies only to the case involving a male human. For the animal case, there is only one verse, and therefore only one *aveira* and one *chatat*.

An additional complication surfaces: the final item in the *mishnah* is *megadef* — blasphemy, a verbal act. The *mishnah* ends with a dissenting voice suggesting that verbal acts without a physical component should not be included, and the *gemara* attributes the view that *megadef* does incur a *chatat* to Rabbi Akiva, based on the principle in Sanhedrin that *akimat sefatav havei ma'aseh* — the movement of one's lips constitutes a physical act.

### Rabbi Akiva

Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, holds *lo chayav ela achat* — one who performs both roles incurs only a single *chatat*, deriving the entire prohibition from a single verse: *et zachar lo tishkav*. Rabbi Akiva reads the same verse in two ways — *lo tishkav* (you shall not lie) and *lo tishachav* (you shall not allow yourself to be lain with) — but treats both readings as aspects of a single prohibition derived from a single textual source. A single source means a single *aveira* and a single *chatat*.

This creates a tension: the *mishnah*'s final line about *megadef* is associated with Rabbi Akiva's position, yet the *reisha* of the *mishnah* — which requires two separate acts for the homosexual case to reach the count of thirty-four — requires Rabbi Yishmael's position. The *gemara* resolves this by saying the *mishnah* is authored by Rabbi Yishmael, who holds the two roles constitute two separate *aveirot* and who, in the case of *megadef*, agrees with Rabbi Akiva that a verbal act incurs a *chatat*.

## Pesach & Milah

### 36 Keritot

Rabbi Eliezer in the name of Rava offers an alternative resolution to the question of the *mishnah*'s count: *tani sheloshim veshalosh chataot* — the *mishnah* is in fact enumerating thirty-three *chataot*, not thirty-six. The number thirty-six is the total of *keritot*, not the total of *chataot* a single person can incur. The *mishnah* gives the full tally of thirty-six *karet* offenses in order to complete its enumeration of *keritot*, while the practical *chatat* liability for a single person reaches only thirty-three.

### Exclude Positive

The *gemara* supports this by asking directly: why are *Pesach* and *milah* listed at all? *Pesach u'Milah amai tani lehu?* If the purpose of the list were to enumerate cases where a *korban* is brought, *Pesach* and *milah* should not appear, since one never brings a *chatat* for failing to perform a positive *mitzvah*. A *baraita* confirms the principle: *hukesh kol haTorah kullah le'avodat kochavim* — the entire *Torah*'s *chatat* obligation is modeled on the case of *avodah zarah beshogeg*, as derived from the verse *Torah achat yihyeh lachem la'oseh bishgagah*, juxtaposed with *vehanefesh asher ta'aseh beyad ramah et Hashem hu megadef*. Since *avodah zarah* is a prohibition — a *shev ve'al ta'aseh* — only transgressions of that type generate a *chatat*. Failure to perform a positive commandment never generates a *chatat*. *Pesach* and *milah* are therefore listed solely to complete the tally of *keritot*, not to imply any *chatat* obligation. By the same logic, the debate about the passive role in the homosexual act — whether it generates a *chatat* (Rabbi Yishmael) or not (Rabbi Akiva) — has direct practical implications for the final count.

### 33 Chataot

The *gemara* concludes: *shema mina tana sheloshim veshalosh chataot* — the *mishnah* is enumerating thirty-three *chataot* that a single person can potentially incur. *Ve'hadar tani sheloshim veshesh keritot vesiyumei bekeritot* — it then completes the enumeration of thirty-six *keritot* to provide the full picture of *karet* offenses in the *Torah*. The *masechet* is called *Keritot* because *karet* is the starting point — identifying who is subject to *karet* determines who is obligated in a *chatat*. But the *masechet*'s practical focus is on those *chatat* obligations. The three cases that appear in the list but do not generate a *chatat* — the passive role in the homosexual act (on some views) and the two positive commandments of *Pesach* and *milah* — are present to give the full accounting of *keritot*, not to extend the *chatat* count. One person can bring at most thirty-three *chataot*.

## Shabbat Case

### Forgot Shabbat

The *gemara* now turns to *Shabbat* as a case study in how a single state of forgetfulness can interact with multiple possible violations. The *mishnah* lists *hamechallel et haShabbat* as a single entry. Yet one who violates *Shabbat* could potentially incur thirty-nine separate *chataot* — one for each *melacha* — if one knew it was *Shabbat* but forgot that each individual act was forbidden. Rabbi Yochanan explains: *tana shigagat Shabbat vezadon melachot* — the *mishnah* is referring to the case where one forgot it was *Shabbat* entirely, while intentionally performing acts that are in fact *melachot*. Because one's state of forgetfulness was specifically that it was *Shabbat*, all acts of *chillul Shabbat* committed in that state are subsumed under a single *chatat*. They are not thirty-nine distinct *aveirot* because what was forgotten was *Shabbat* itself, not the individual prohibitions.

### Me'achas

The *gemara* grounds this in the verse *asher ya'aseh mei'achas meiheina* — which does not say simply *achas meiheina* (one of these) but *mei'achas meiheina* (from one from these). This phrasing is taken to indicate variability: sometimes multiple actions count as one, and sometimes one category encompasses many. The full *baraita* unpacks both scenarios. The phrase *achas shehi heina* — one that is many — refers to *shigagat Shabbat vezadon melachot*: one forgot it was *Shabbat* and knowingly performed multiple acts. Despite the multiplicity of acts, it is counted as one, because the error was singular. Conversely, *heina shehein achat* — many that are one — refers to *zedon Shabbat veshigagat melachot*: one knew it was *Shabbat* but forgot that specific acts were prohibited. There, each *melacha* constitutes a distinct *aveira*, yielding thirty-nine separate *chataot*.

The *gemara* then asks: given that both scenarios exist, why did the *mishnah* list only the *shigagat Shabbat* case, which yields a single *chatat*? The answer is *demichatas miya lo miftar* — the *mishnah* preferred to cite the case from which one can never be *patur* from at least one *chatat*. In the *shigagat Shabbat* case, a single *chatat* is always incurred regardless of circumstances. In the *zedon Shabbat veshigagat melachot* case, the number of *chataot* is variable — it could collapse to one if one also forgot it was *Shabbat*. The *mishnah* chose the case that guarantees a fixed, certain *chatat* count, even if that count is only one.

## Shabbat Case (II)

### Avodah Zarah

A parallel structure applies to *avodah zarah*. Just as *Shabbat* presents two possible scenarios of forgetfulness that yield different *chatat* counts, so too with *avodah zarah*: one may forget that *avodah zarah* is forbidden entirely, or one may remember the general prohibition but fail to recognize that specific acts — *shechitah*, *haktarah*, *hishtachavayah* — constitute *avodah zarah*. The first scenario yields a single *chatat*; the second yields separate *chataot* for each distinct act. The *gemara* proceeds to examine what the first scenario — *shigagat avodah zarah* — actually means in practice, since determining the precise nature of the error is essential to characterizing it as *shogeg*.

---

## Avodah Zarah

### Cases Setup

The *gemara* asks directly: *shigagat avodas kochavim heichi dami* — what exactly does it mean to err about *avodah zarah* itself, as distinct from erring about the specific acts? The question is how one could simultaneously forget that *avodah zarah* is forbidden while still being aware that those acts are in some sense significant or problematic.

### Temple Intent

One proposed scenario: a person was in a temple of idol worship but believed he was in a synagogue, and bowed down. The *gemara* rejects this as a case of *shigagat avodah zarah* with the observation: *harei libo lashamayim* — his heart was directed toward heaven. If he was bowing down intending to pray to God, the fact that he was physically in a pagan temple and facing an idol is irrelevant. His act was directed to God and does not constitute *avodah zarah* at all. There is no *shogeg* because there is no *aveira*.

### Statue Case

A second scenario: one saw a statue — *andrarta*, a royal or decorative statue, not necessarily an object of worship — and bowed down to it. The *gemara* finds this equally unsuitable. *Iy de'kablih le'eloah* — if one treated the statue as a deity, then the worship was fully intentional *avodah zarah* and there is no element of *shogeg*. *Ve'iy de'lo kablih le'eloah* — if one did not treat it as a deity but merely showed obeisance to the king's image, then *lav midy hu* — this is not *avodah zarah* at all. Again, there is no case of *shogeg*.

### Motives

A third and more complex scenario: one worshipped an idol *me'ahavah u'miyirah* — out of love or fear, not out of genuine religious conviction. Rashi offers examples: bowing before an idol to gain social advancement (*me'ahavah*), or performing acts of worship under coercion or threat (*miyirah*). In these cases, one is going through the physical motions of *avodah zarah* but without sincere theological commitment to the idol. One may have believed — incorrectly — that such worship under these circumstances was permissible. That mistaken belief would constitute the *shogeg*.

### Abaye vs Rava

This scenario is the subject of a dispute between Abaye and Rava. Abaye holds *chayav* — even worship performed out of love or fear, without genuine theological belief in the deity, constitutes *avodah zarah*, and one who mistakenly thought such worship was permitted has committed *avodah zarah beshogeg*. Rava holds *patur* — such worship is not *avodah zarah* at all if one does not genuinely accept the object as a deity. Rava's position raises the question of martyrdom: if worship under duress or for ulterior motives is not *avodah zarah*, on what basis is there an obligation to give one's life rather than comply? The *gemara* notes this tension without fully resolving it here. Tosafot addresses it, and the suggestion is that the *kiddush Hashem* dimension operates independently of the technical *aveira* status.

### Thinking Permitted

The *gemara* ultimately settles on a case that works as *shigagat avodah zarah*: *be'omer mutar* — one who knew that *avodah zarah* was an act of idol worship but believed it was permitted. This person was not confused about what the act was; the error was legal — a mistaken belief that the *Torah* permits *avodah zarah*. This constitutes *shogeg*, and in such a case one's single error encompasses all the specific acts performed. All the various acts of *avodah zarah* performed while laboring under this single legal mistake are subsumed under one *chatat*.

The *gemara* closes by noting: *de'ad kan lo ka'ba'ei mineih Rava me'Rav Nachman ela iy echada mechayvei tartei mechayvei* — Rava's question to Rav Nachman was specifically about whether, when both the general prohibition and the specific acts were forgotten simultaneously, one incurs one *chatat* or multiple. The scenario of *be'omer mutar* is the baseline: knowing the acts were *avodah zarah* but thinking them permitted constitutes one clear error, generating one *chatat* for all acts performed in that state. The more complex case — where one forgot both the general prohibition and the particulars — raises the question of whether the two layers of forgetfulness collapse into one or multiply the *chatat* obligation. Importantly, the *gemara* notes that the *patur legamrei* option is not on the table: one minimally brings one *chatat*. This also stands in some tension with a remark attributed to a pseudo-Rashi on 2a, where *be'omer mutar* was characterized as an *ones*. The *gemara* here treats it unambiguously as *shogeg*, at least in the context of *avodah zarah* — though it remains possible that context matters, and *be'omer mutar* for other transgressions may be treated differently.
