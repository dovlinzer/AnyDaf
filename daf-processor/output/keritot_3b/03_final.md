# Keritot 3b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Multiple Offerings

### Review & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְיָינָא לְמָה לִי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁאִם עֲשָׂאָן כּוּלָּן בְּהֶעְלֵם אַחַת – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** Why do I need the mishna to state this **tally** of cases where one who performs the action is liable to receive *karet*? Why does it state a number? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** By listing the tally, the mishna is teaching **that if one performed all of them in** the course of **one lapse of awareness,** he is **liable** to receive punishment **for each and every one,** i.e., to bring thirty-six sin offerings.

Today's shiur opens on the bottom of 2b, where the previous discussion reviewed the various *mishnayot* throughout the *masechet* that give specific numbers, with particular attention to what the number in our *mishnah* is meant to convey. The central point established was that the number in our *mishnah* signals that one may bring multiple *chataot* even within a single state of forgetfulness — though not thirty-six, since two of the cases involve *mitzvot aseh*, and Rashi later specifies the number as thirty-four. The review also covered the other *mishnayot* in the *masechet* that address variations on the themes of *korban chatat*, *asham talui*, *mechusrei kaparah*, and related topics.

### One Forgetfulness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא פָּטוּר לִגְמָרֵי לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ, דִּכְתִיב: "כִּי כׇּל אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשֶׂה מִכֹּל הַתּוֹעֵבֹת הָאֵלֶּה וְנִכְרְתוּ". אֶלָּא אֵימָא: עֲבַר חֲדָא – נִתְחַיֵּיב חֲדָא, עֲבַר כּוּלְּהוּ בְּהֶעְלֵם אַחַת – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, you cannot say** that one who unwittingly violates every prohibition against engaging in forbidden intercourse is **entirely exempt, as it is written: "For whoever shall do any of these abominations,** the souls that do them **shall be cut off** from among their people" (Leviticus 18:29), and as they all are punishable by *karet* he is liable to bring a sin offering for his unwitting transgression. **But** why not **say** that if he **transgressed one** prohibition **he becomes liable** to bring **one** sin offering, and likewise if he **transgressed all of them in one lapse of awareness** he **is liable** to bring **only one** sin offering.

The *gemara* resumes with the phrase *amar mar*, directing attention to the ruling: *hevi'um kulam behe'elam achat chayav al kol achat ve'achat* — if one performed all of these transgressions within a single state of forgetfulness, one is liable for each and every one separately. This represents the clearest case: each act is distinct, each transgression is distinct, and therefore each warrants its own *korban*.

The discussion then turns to the underlying principle. The *gemara* begins by establishing that complete exemption is certainly not an option. The verse states *ki kol asher ya'aseh mikol hato'evot ha'eleh venichretu* — whoever performs any of these abominations shall be cut off. If one transgression incurs *karet*, and *karet* in turn implies a *chatat* when done *beshogeg*, then committing multiple such transgressions in one state of forgetfulness clearly does not render one *patur*. The *gemara* notes this as self-evident.

### Single Offering?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְכָךְ יָצְאָה כָּרֵת בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ – לְחַלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is **for this** reason that ***karet*** which one incurs for engaging in intercourse **with one's sister** is **singled out** and mentioned independently of the other acts of forbidden intercourse (Leviticus 20:17). This serves **to separate** the prohibitions and teach that one is liable to receive a separate punishment of *karet* or to bring a sin offering for each individual prohibition against forbidden intercourse.

The real question, then, is whether multiple transgressions committed in one state of forgetfulness might nonetheless require only a single *chatat*. As a point of comparison: if one committed the same *aveira* multiple times within one uninterrupted state of forgetfulness — for example, forgetting that cooking on *Shabbat* was prohibited and cooking five separate times without any intervening awareness of the *issur* — one brings only a single *korban*. The question is whether the same logic might extend to different sins: perhaps even distinct transgressions, if performed within one state of forgetfulness, should require only one *chatat*. Where does one find the foundational principle that separate sins committed in a single state of forgetfulness each require their own *korban*?

## Sister Prohibition

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי, אֵימָא: אֲחוֹתוֹ דִּפְרַט קְרָא – נִיחַיַּיב חֲדָא עֲלַהּ, וְכוּלָּן כֵּיוָן דִּבְהֶעְלֵם אַחַת – לָא נִיחַיַּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai bar Abaye objects to this:** Why not **say:** With regard to one who engages in intercourse with **his sister, which the verse detailed** separately, **he becomes liable** to bring **one** sin offering **for it, but** with regard to **all** the other acts of forbidden intercourse, **once** he transgressed all of them **in one lapse of awareness he becomes liable** to bring **only one** sin offering for all of them.

Rabbi Yochanan addresses this with a well-known formulation: *lekach yatzat karet ba'achoto lechaleik* — this is why the *Torah* stated *karet* explicitly in connection with the prohibition of sleeping with one's sister, even though a general verse at the end of *Parshat Acharei Mot* or *Kedoshim* already assigns *karet* to all *arayot* collectively. The general verse states *ki kol asher ya'aseh mikol hato'evot ha'eleh venichretu*, which encompasses all prohibited relations. Why, then, did the *Torah* need to reiterate *karet* specifically for one's sister? Rabbi Yochanan's answer is that the *Torah* singled out *achoto* in order *lechaleik* — to treat each case individually. The explicit *karet* by *achoto* signals that each of the *arayot* is to be treated distinctly, and since *karet* is the basis for *chatat* liability when the act is *beshogeg*, each separate transgression will generate its own *chatat*.

### Bibi bar Abaye

Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye challenges this derivation. He argues: *eima achoto deparat kra nitchayev chatat alah* — perhaps the *Torah*'s explicit mention of *karet* by the sister case generates a separate *korban* only for the sister prohibition itself, while all other *arayot* might yield only a single *chatat* regardless of how many are committed in one state of forgetfulness. The *gemara* immediately presses back on this objection: does Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye not accept the hermeneutical principle of *kol davar shehayah bichlal veyatza min haklal lelamed, lo lelamed al atzmo yatza ela lelamed al haklal kulo yatza*? When something that was part of a broader category is singled out with a specific ruling, that ruling is not treated as an exception applying only to that case — rather, it serves as a paradigm for the entire category.

### General Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? "וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכַל בָּשָׂר וְגוֹ׳", וַהֲלֹא שְׁלָמִים בִּכְלָל הַקֳּדָשִׁים הָיוּ. לָמָּה יָצְאוּ? לְהַקִּישׁ אֲלֵיהֶן: מָה שְּׁלָמִים שֶׁהֵן קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ וְחַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶם – אַף כׇּל שֶׁהֵן קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶם, יָצְאוּ קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains: **How so?** The verse states: **"But the soul that eats of the flesh of the sacrifice of peace offerings** that belong to the Lord, with his ritual impurity upon him, that soul shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 7:20). **But weren't peace offerings included in the** general category of all **sacrificial** offerings; **why,** then, were **they** explicitly **singled out** in this verse? They were singled out **to juxtapose** the *halakha* of other offerings **to them,** teaching: **Just as peace offerings** are unique in **that they are consecrated for the altar and** one is **liable** to receive *karet* **for** partaking of **them** while impure, **so too,** with regard to **all** food **that is consecrated for the altar,** one is **liable** to receive *karet* **for** partaking of **them** while impure. This **excludes** from this *halakha* items that are **consecrated for Temple maintenance.** The same reasoning should apply to the singling out of the prohibition against engaging in intercourse with one's sister, as it teaches that one is liable separately for all the categories of forbidden relations.

To illustrate this principle, the *gemara* invokes the case of *shelamim*: the verse specifies that eating from a *korban shelamim* while *tamei* incurs *karet*. Yet *shelamim* was already included within the broader category of all *korbanos*. Why was it singled out? *Lehakish aleihem* — to use it as a paradigm. Just as *shelamim* is a *kodshei mizbe'ach* and one incurs *karet* for eating it while *tamei*, so too all *kodshei mizbe'ach* are subject to the same rule. The singling-out of *shelamim* simultaneously excludes *kodshei bedek habayit* from the *karet* prohibition and establishes *korbanos* as the paradigm for the rule's application. In the same way, the singling-out of *achoto* should instruct us about the entire category of *arayot*.

## Refining Principle

### Specific Traits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב בִּיבִי: בַּר מִינַּהּ. לָאו מִי אָמְרַתְּ הָתָם יָצְאוּ קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת? הָכָא נָמֵי: מָה אֲחוֹתוֹ מְיוּחֶדֶת שֶׁהִיא עֶרְוָה וְאֵין לָהּ הֶיתֵּר בְּחַיֵּי אוֹסְרָהּ, אַף כֹּל שֶׁאֵין לָהּ הֶיתֵּר בְּחַיֵּי אוֹסְרָהּ, יָצְאָה אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ הֶיתֵּר בְּחַיֵּי בַעֲלָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Beivai elaborates: **Here too, just as** the case of **one's sister is unique** in **that she is a forbidden relative who cannot be permitted during the** entire **life of** the one **who prohibits her,** i.e., her brother, as she will always be his sister, **so too,** the *halakha* that one is liable to bring a sin offering for the transgression of each prohibition applies to **any** forbidden relative **who cannot be permitted in the life of** the one **who prohibits her.** This **excludes a married woman, who can be permitted in the life of** the one who prohibits her, i.e., **her husband,** as their relationship can be terminated by means of divorce. If so, one would not be liable to bring a separate sin offering for each instance of engaging in intercourse with a married woman, whereas Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that one is liable to bring a sin offering for violating each of the prohibitions listed in the mishna, including the one concerning a married woman.

Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye is not satisfied. He accepts the hermeneutical principle — he has no objection to deriving a rule from a paradigm — but he presses on what the specific characteristics of *achoto* actually teach. By the analogy to *shelamim*, what was learned is defined by the relevant features of the paradigm case. Applied here: *mah achoto meyuchedet she'hi ervah* — a sister is *ervah*. One might therefore conclude that only *arayot* generate separate *chataot*, but not other sins that carry *karet*. Furthermore, *ve'ein lah heter bechayei osrah* — a sister can never become permissible during the lifetime of the one who renders her forbidden. This characteristic would exclude a married woman, *eshes ish*, who *can* become permissible if her husband dies. Therefore, Rabbi Bibi argues, the *achoto* derivation might apply only to permanently forbidden *arayot*, not to all *karet* transgressions and perhaps not even to all *arayot*. This leaves open the question of how we know that every separate *karet* offense, regardless of category, generates a distinct *chatat*.

### Explicit Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָה וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אָמַר קְרָא: "כִּי כׇּל אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשֶׂה מִכֹּל הַתּוֹעֵבוֹת וְגוֹ׳", הוֹאִיל וְכָךְ הִקִּישׁ הוּא, הוּקְשׁוּ כׇּל הָעֲרָיוֹת כּוּלָּן לַאֲחוֹתוֹ: מָה אֲחוֹתוֹ חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ, אַף כֹּל חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yona said** in response, **and some say** this was stated by **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua: The verse states: "For whoever shall do any of these abominations,** the souls that do them shall be cut off from among their people" (Leviticus 18:29). **Since this is a juxtaposition,** it means that in this verse **all of those with whom relations are forbidden are juxtaposed to** the case of **one's sister,** written in that passage, as follows: **Just as** with regard to **one's sister** he is **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for** engaging in intercourse with **her alone, so too,** with regard to any one of **all** the cases of forbidden relations, one is **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for** engaging in intercourse with that woman **alone.** When a *halakha* is derived by juxtaposition the cases do not have to be entirely similar.

Rabbi Yona — and some say Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua — resolves this by pointing to an explicit verse: *ki kol asher ya'aseh mikol hato'evot ha'eil venichretu*. This verse at the end of the *parasha* expressly groups all the *arayot* together under a single *karet* formulation. The implication is: *hukshu kol ha'arayot kulan l'achoto* — all the *arayot* are explicitly equated with *achoto* with respect to *karet*. Once the verse itself links them all together, one is not required to examine what characteristics of *achoto* define the paradigm. The verse states that they all share the same *karet*, and therefore: *mah achoto chayav aleiha bifnei atzmah af kol chayav aleiha bifnei atzmah* — just as one is liable separately for *achoto*, one is liable separately for each of the *arayot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יִצְחָק דְּאָמַר: חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת בִּכְלָל הָיוּ, וְלָמָּה יָצְאָה כָּרֵת בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ – לְדוּנָהּ בְּכָרֵת וְלֹא בְּמַלְקוֹת, לְחַלֵּק מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ"וְּאֶל אִשָּׁה בְּנִדַּת טוּמְאָתָהּ לֹא תִקְרַב" – לְחַלֵּק עַל כׇּל אִשָּׁה וְאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** this presents a difficulty **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yitzḥak, who says:** All **those liable** to receive ***karet*** in cases of incest **were included in** the principle: "For whoever shall commit any of these abominations, even the people who commit them shall be cut off from among their people" (Leviticus 18:29). **And why was *karet*** incurred by engaging in intercourse with **one's sister singled out** in Leviticus 20:17? It was singled out **to judge it,** so that the transgressors receive the punishment **of *karet* and not** the punishment **of lashes.** Although they have violated a prohibition, which generally carries the punishment of lashes, they are not flogged, as they are liable to receive the more severe punishment of *karet*. **From where does** Rabbi Yitzḥak learn **to separate** the prohibitions and teach that one is liable to receive a separate punishment of *karet* or to bring a sin offering for each violation of the prohibition against forbidden intercourse? Unlike Rabbi Yoḥanan, he cannot derive it from the above verse, as he learns this aforementioned *halakha* from that verse. The Gemara answers: **He derives** it **from** the verse: **"And you shall not approach a woman** to uncover her nakedness **as long as she is impure by her menstruation"** (Leviticus 18:19). Since the verse could have stated simply: And you shall not approach a menstruating woman, the lengthy expression of: "A woman…as long as she is impure by her menstruation," serves **to separate** the transgressions and render him liable to receive a punishment **for each and every woman** with whom he engaged in forbidden sexual intercourse.

The *gemara* notes, however, that this still leaves a question: how do we extend the principle beyond *arayot* to all other *karet* transgressions? The *gemara* appears to assume that once the derivation is no longer confined to *achoto* alone, generalization follows, though the question could still be raised.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי, תִּיפּוֹק לְהוּ מִ"וְּאֶל אִשָּׁה בְּנִדַּת טוּמְאָתָהּ"! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי. אֶלָּא כָּרֵת דַּאֲחוֹתוֹ לְמַאי אָתָא? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְחַלֵּק עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ וְעַל אֲחוֹת אָבִיו וְעַל אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ. הָנֵי לְמָה לְהוּ לְחַלֵּק? הֲרֵי שֵׁמוֹת מוּחְלָקִין וַהֲרֵי גּוּפִים מוּחְלָקִים! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: לְחַלֵּק עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אָבִיו וְשֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And** with regard to **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Yitzḥak, **let them also derive** this *halakha* that one is liable to receive a separate punishment of *karet* or to bring a sin offering for each violation of the prohibition against forbidden intercourse **from** the verse cited by Rabbi Yitzḥak: **"And** you shall not approach **a woman** to uncover her nakedness **as long as she is impure by her menstruation."** The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so.** The Gemara asks: **But** if they derive that *halakha* from the same source as Rabbi Yitzḥak, **for what** purpose does the specific instance of ***karet*** stated **with regard to one's sister come?** The Gemara answers: The Rabbis **require** it **to separate** the transgressions and render one liable to bring a separate sin offering for engaging in intercourse **with his sister, and** for engaging in intercourse **with his father's sister, and** for engaging in intercourse **with his mother's sister,** in a single lapse of awareness. The Gemara asks: **Why** is it necessary for the verse **to separate** the transgressions and render one liable to bring a separate sin offering with regard to **these** forbidden relatives? **Aren't they separate categories** of prohibitions, **and aren't they separate bodies,** i.e., three different people? It is obvious that each act of intercourse will require a separate sin offering. **Rather, say** as follows: The verse serves **to separate** the transgressions and render one liable to bring a separate sin offering for unwittingly engaging in intercourse **with his sister who is** also **his father's sister and who is** also **his mother's sister,** i.e., one is liable to bring three sin offerings despite the fact that all three prohibitions are violated through one act of intercourse with the same person.

The gemara then introduces a further point. There is also a verse concerning *niddah* — *ve'el ishah benidat tumatah lo tikrav* — and the question is raised: why does the verse say *ishah* (woman) when it could simply have said *niddah*? Rashi explains that through the lens of rabbinic usage — where *niddah* names the woman rather than describing the state — the extra word *ishah* comes to teach: *lechaleik al kol ishah ve'ishah*, each woman is treated separately, meaning each *ervah* is a distinct case.

## Woman & Sister

### Niddah Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק, הָא מִילְּתָא מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִן סֵיפָא דִּקְרָא, דִּכְתִיב: "עֶרְוַת אֲחֹתוֹ גִּלָּה". וְרַבָּנַן, "אֲחֹתוֹ" דְּסֵיפָא דִּקְרָא מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לַאֲחוֹתוֹ שֶׁהִיא בַּת אָבִיו וּבַת אִמּוֹ, לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵין עוֹנְשִׁין מִן הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara turns its attention to the other opinion: **And** with regard to **Rabbi Yitzḥak, from where does he** derive **this matter?** The Gemara answers: **He derives** it **from the latter clause of the verse, as it is written: "He has uncovered his sister's nakedness"** (Leviticus 20:17). This seemingly superfluous phrase teaches this *halakha*. The Gemara further asks: **And** with regard to **the Rabbis,** concerning this mention of **"his sister" in the latter clause of the verse, what do they do with it?** The Gemara answers: **They require** it **to** teach the *halakha* in the case of **his sister who is** both **his father's daughter and his mother's daughter,** and this serves **to say that one does not administer punishment** based **on** an *a fortiori* **inference.** Although the verses state a punishment both for one who engages in intercourse with his half sister who is his father's daughter and for his half sister who is his mother's daughter, one cannot learn via an *a fortiori* inference that he should also be punished for engaging in intercourse with his sister from both parents, as punishments are applied only when the Torah expressly imposes them.

The *gemara* draws out a further implication of this word *ishah*. If the Rabbis can derive the principle of *lechalek* — treating each *ervah* separately — from the extra word *ishah* in the *niddah* prohibition, then it would seem one does not need the derivation from *achoto* at all for this purpose. The *gemara* acknowledges as much: *rabbanan nami tipok leh me've'el ishah benidat tumatah* — the Rabbis could have derived the point of separating the cases from this verse. The question then becomes: if the principle can be learned from *ishah*, what additional work does the explicit *karet* by *achoto* perform?

The *gemara* answers: *mibaei leh lechaleik al achoto ve'al achot aviv ve'al achot immo* — it is needed to distinguish between the case of one's own sister, one's father's sister, and one's mother's sister, and to teach that even these closely related prohibitions are treated as distinct for purposes of *chatat*. One might have thought that since these are all variations of the "sister" category, a single act involving one person who happens to be all three simultaneously would incur only one *chatat*. The explicit statement about *achoto* teaches otherwise.

To this the *gemara* objects: *harei shemos mechulakim ve'gufot mechulakim* — these are already distinct prohibitions (*shemos mechulakim*) and, in the ordinary case, distinct persons (*gufot mechulakim*). Where there are separate *lavin* and separate people or objects, no verse is needed to establish separate *chataot*. The *gemara* therefore refines the answer: the verse is needed specifically for a case where *achoto* and *achot aviv* and *achot immo* are all the same individual. While such a scenario requires considerable creative genealogy — best left to a discussion in Yevamos — the case is theoretically possible: a single person who stands in all three of these relationships simultaneously. There one person is involved (*gufot lo mechulakim*), yet the *aveirot* are distinct. The extra word *achoto* teaches that even in that case, the separate *shemot* — the separate prohibitions — are sufficient to generate separate *chataot*.

This articulates a fundamental principle: the criterion for multiple *chataot* does not require multiple persons or multiple objects. A single act, a single person, even a single moment — if multiple distinct prohibitions were violated, each generates its own *chatat*.

### Full vs. Half

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק, סָבַר: עוֹנְשִׁין מִן הַדִּין. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: יָלֵיף עוֹנֶשׁ מֵאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And Rabbi Yitzḥak,** who does not interpret the verses in this manner, **maintains** that **one administers punishment** based **on** an *a fortiori* **inference. And if you wish, say** instead a different reason why Rabbi Yitzḥak does not require this exposition: He **derives** the **punishment** of *karet* for engaging in intercourse with a sister from both parents **from** the **warning,** i.e., the prohibition that is stated with regard to her. Just as the verse explicitly prohibited engaging in intercourse with a sister from both parents, so too, the same punishment of *karet* applies to engaging in intercourse with her as it does to engaging in intercourse with other forbidden relatives.

The *gemara* introduces yet another view: *leRabbi Yitzchak de'amar chayvei keritot bichlal hayu, velama yatzat karet be'achoto ladun bekaret velo bemalkut*. Rabbi Yitzchak holds that the separate mention of *karet* by *achoto* comes to teach something different entirely — not *lechalek* for *chataot*, but to establish that *karet* for incestuous relations is not displaced by *malkut*. The background question is whether one who received *malkut* for a *karet*-eligible transgression is thereby discharged from *karet*: *kol chayvei keritot shelaku nifteru miyedei keritatan*. Rabbi Yitzchak disputes this and uses the explicit *karet* of *achoto* to demonstrate that *malkut* does not exempt from *karet*. (The *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Yitzchak on this point, but his derivation reorients what *achoto* is teaching.)

Having used *achoto* for this purpose, Rabbi Yitzchak no longer has it available to derive *lechalek*. The *gemara* asks: *lechaleik mena lei?* — from where does Rabbi Yitzchak derive that each *ervah* generates a separate *chatat*? The answer is: *nafka lei me've'el ishah benidat tumatah* — he derives it from the word *ishah* in the *niddah* verse, precisely as discussed above.

In turn, the *gemara* asks: since the Rabbis can also use *ishah* for *lechalek*, what do they do with *karet* at *achoto*? Their answer is that it was needed to address the case of one's full sister — *achoto shehi bat aviv u'vat immo*. The *Torah* explicitly prohibits one's paternal half-sister and one's maternal half-sister. A full sister, being both, might be thought to be covered by either prohibition without requiring a separate verse. Yet conceptually, a full sister is a distinct category from each type of half-sister. One could derive her prohibition through a *kal v'chomer*, but a *kal v'chomer* cannot establish *onesh* — punishment. This is the principle of *ein onshim min hadin*.

### Ein Onshim

The principle of *ein onshim min hadin* — that punishment cannot be derived from logical inference alone — is significant here. The *Torah* assigns *karet* explicitly to the half-sister cases. One might reason by *kal v'chomer* that a full sister, who incorporates both prohibitions, must also incur *karet*. Yet the *gemara* holds that such reasoning is insufficient for imposing a punishment as severe as *karet*. Without a *pasuk*, a *karet* obligation cannot stand. The extra *achoto* in the verse serves therefore to provide that explicit textual basis for the full sister, ensuring that her *karet* rests on the verse rather than on inference alone.

The *gemara* notes, somewhat pointedly, that Rabbi Yitzchak — who believes *onshim min hadin* is valid — does not need *achoto* for this purpose. He holds that the *kal v'chomer* for a full sister's *karet* is legitimate. Alternatively, the *gemara* offers: *yalif ones me'azhara* — since a full sister's prohibition can be derived textually (even if not by an explicit separate verse), Rabbi Yitzchak can link her *karet* to her prohibition by analogy, which is considered more than pure logic and therefore permissible.

A sharp point emerges here: the *nafka minah* of whether a *karet* obligation is textually grounded extends beyond the realm of divine punishment. The practical halakhic consequence is the ability to obligate a *chatat*: without a legitimate basis for *karet*, there is no basis for the *chatat* when done *beshogeg*. The system requires that we can properly establish *karet* status — not merely that we believe God will act in a certain way — for all the downstream halachic implications to follow.

## Separate Sins

### Two Lavin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא שְׁנֵי לָאוִין וְכָרֵת אֶחָד – חַלֵּק חַטָּאת בֵּינֵיהֶן. וּמַאי הִיא? מְפַטֵּם וְסָךְ, דִּכְתִיב: "עַל בְּשַׂר אָדָם לֹא יִיסָךְ וּבְמַתְכֻּנְתּוֹ וְגוֹ׳", כָּרֵת אֶחָד, דִּכְתִיב: "אֲשֶׁר יִרְקַח כָּמֹהוּ וַאֲשֶׁר יִתֵּן מִמֶּנּוּ עַל זָר וְנִכְרַת מֵעַמָּיו".
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says** that **Rabbi Hoshaya says: Anywhere you find two** different **prohibitions, and** only **one** punishment of ***karet*** is stated for both of them, you should **divide** the **sin offering between them,** i.e., if one transgressed both prohibitions unwittingly he is liable to bring a sin offering for each. **And what is** this case? One who **blends the anointing oil** according to the specifications of the oil prepared by Moses in the wilderness, for purposes other than use in the Temple, **and** one who **applies** this anointing oil to the skin of a person, **as it is written: "Upon the flesh of man it shall not be applied,** neither shall you make any like it **according to its composition"** (Exodus 30:32). These are two prohibitions, one for applying the oil and the other for blending it, and **one *karet*** is stated with regard to both, **as it is written: "Whoever compounds any like it, or whoever puts any of it upon a stranger, he shall be cut off from his people"** (Exodus 30:33).

The *gemara* now advances to a more sweeping principle, stated by Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: *kol makom she'ata motzei shnei lavin ve'karet echad, chilek chataot beineihem* — wherever the *Torah* presents two separate prohibitions with a single *karet* covering both, each prohibition nevertheless generates a distinct *chatat*. The singularity of the *karet* formulation does not collapse the two prohibitions into a single *aveira* for purposes of *chatat* liability. As long as there are separate *lavin*, the transgressions are sufficiently distinct.

### Anointing Oil

The illustrative case is that of the *shemen hamishchah*, the anointing oil. The verse contains two separate prohibitions: *al besar adam lo yisach* — do not apply the anointing oil to a person's skin — and *uvmatkunto lo ta'asu kamohu* — do not compound a private batch of oil according to the same formula. These are two distinct *lavin*. Yet the verse then assigns a single *karet* to both: *ish asher yirkach kamohu v'asher yitein mimenu al zar venichreat me'amav*. One might have thought that this single *karet* covering both prohibitions means that violating both in one state of forgetfulness incurs only one *chatat*. Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya teaches that this is not so. The separation of the *lavin* is what counts; the unity of the *karet* is irrelevant to the question of how many *chataot* are owed.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּלָאוִין מוּחְלָקִים, כָּרֵת דַּאֲחוֹתוֹ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this statement: **But** accordingly, **once** there are **separate prohibitions** stated in the Torah with regard to engaging in intercourse with all forbidden relatives (Leviticus, chapter 18), and at the conclusion of that passage it is written: "For whoever shall do any of these abominations, the souls that do them shall be cut off from among their people" (Leviticus 18:29), it is indicated that the punishments of *karet* or a sin offering apply to all these prohibitions. If so, **why do I** need the punishment of ***karet* that the Merciful One writes with regard to one's sister,** in a separate passage: "And they shall be cut off in the sight of the children of their people; he has uncovered his sister's nakedness" (Leviticus 20:17)?

This raises an immediate difficulty: *vachi me'achar delavin mechulakim, karet achoto dechasav Rachamana lama li?* If the principle is that separate *lavin* automatically entail separate *chataot*, why was all the earlier discussion necessary? The entire elaborate derivation from *achoto* — with all its qualifications, the challenge of Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye, the response from *ishah*, and so forth — now appears superfluous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי יִצְחָק – כִּדְאִית לֵיהּ, לְרַבָּנַן מִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לוֹמַר, שֶׁאֵין עוֹנְשִׁין מִן הַדִּין! לְרַבִּי יִצְחָק, מַפֵּיק לֵיהּ לְחַיֵּיב עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אָבִיו וְשֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This verse is required to teach one *halakha* according to the opinion of the Rabbis, and one *halakha* **according to Rabbi Yitzḥak, per his** opinion. The Gemara elaborates: **According to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, they require** this verse **to say that one does not administer punishment** based **on** an *a fortiori* **inference. According to Rabbi Yitzḥak, he derives** that this verse comes **to deem** one **liable** to bring three separate sin offerings for unwittingly engaging in intercourse **with his** own **sister who is** also **his father's sister and who is** also **his mother's sister.**

The *gemara* responds that the earlier verses were in fact needed for other purposes, and the principle of separate *lavin* generating separate *chataot* makes them redundant only with respect to that specific teaching. According to Rabbi Yitzchak, *karet* by *achoto* was needed to establish that *malkut* does not discharge *karet*. According to the Rabbis, it was needed to establish the *karet* obligation for a full sister on textual grounds (*ein onshim min hadin*). Rashi understands the *gemara* to be saying that those verses were genuinely needed for their other purposes; the implication for *lechalek* was incidental and, in hindsight, unnecessary given Rabbi Elazar's principle. This resolution is admittedly somewhat unusual: one is left wondering where Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya himself derived the principle, since it was not stated as a derivation but seemingly as an *a priori* rule. The *gemara* does not address this directly.

The upshot, in any case, is now fully established: separate *chataot* are owed whenever the *aveirot* involve separate *lavin* — regardless of whether the act is singular or multiple, regardless of whether there is one *karet* or separate *karet* formulations, and regardless of whether the same person or object is involved, so long as the prohibitions are distinct.

## Mishnah Support

### Non-Sequential

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: הַמְפַטֵּם אֶת הַשֶּׁמֶן, הַמְפַטֵּם אֶת הַקְּטֹרֶת, וְהַסָּךְ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה. לְמָה לִי דְּתַנְיָא הַמְפַטֵּם אֶת הַקְּטֹרֶת בְּמִיצְעָא? לָאו הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: מָה קְטֹרֶת לָאו בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ וְחַיָּיבִים עָלֶיהָ כָּרֵת בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ – אַף מְפַטֵּם שֶׁמֶן וְסָךְ נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּלָאו בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ – חַיָּיבִים עָלָיו בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak explains his inference: **Why do I** need the *tanna* **to teach** the clause: **One who blends the incense, in the middle,** between the two cases concerning the anointing oil? Is it **not** correct that **this** is what the mishna **teaches us: Just as** blending the **incense** is **an independent prohibition and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for it by itself,** and to bring a sin offering for its unwitting transgression, **so too,** with regard to one **who blends** the anointing **oil and** one **who applies** the anointing oil to his skin, **since** they are each **an independent prohibition, one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** each action **by itself,** and to bring a sin offering for the unwitting transgression of each of them.

Rav Natan bar Yitzchak offers additional support from the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* lists: *hamfatem et hashemen* (one who compounds the anointing oil), *hamfatem et haktoret* (one who compounds the incense), and *vehasach beshemen hamishchah* (one who anoints oneself with the anointing oil). The ordering is notable: the two cases involving the *shemen hamishchah* — compounding it and applying it — are not listed consecutively. Instead, the case of *ktoret* is inserted between them. Why? *Lama li detana hamfatem et haktoret bemetzia* — why did the *tanna* place the *ktoret* case in the middle?

The *gemara* answers: *lav ha kamashma lan* — it must be to convey the following. Just as *ktoret* is a separate *lav* and therefore incurs a separate *chatat*, the same applies to the two *shemen* cases. Since *ktoret* carries its own *lav* and its own *karet*, it generates a distinct *chatat*. The *mishnah*'s insertion of *ktoret* between the two *shemen* cases is a structural signal: even the two *shemen* prohibitions, though they involve the same substance and share a single *karet* verse, are treated as separate *aveirot* generating separate *chataot* — precisely because they are separate *lavin*.

### Not Stylistic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנָא פִּיטּוּמִין בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי – נִיתְנִהוּ אֵיפוֹךְ: הַמְפַטֵּם אֶת הַקְּטֹרֶת, וְהַמְפַטֵּם אֶת הַשֶּׁמֶן, וְהַסָּךְ שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה, אַמַּאי פַּלְּגֵיהּ לְשֶׁמֶן? לָאו הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּיֵשׁ חִילּוּק חַטָּאוֹת בֵּינֵיהֶן? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** the mishna uses this order because **it wants to teach** the two cases involving **blending together, let** the *tanna* **teach** those two *halakhot* in the **reverse** order: **One who blends the incense, and one who blends the** anointing **oil, and one who applies the anointing oil.** In this manner the two clauses addressing blending would be adjacent, and likewise the two clauses addressing the anointing oil would be adjacent. **Why** did the *tanna* **divide** the cases involving **the** anointing **oil** by teaching the *halakha* of one who blends the incense in between them? Is it **not** correct that **this teaches us that there is a separation between** the two prohibitions, requiring one to bring two **sin offerings?** The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** the mishna that this is the case.

One might object that the *tanna* placed the two *pitum* cases — compounding oil and compounding incense — together for stylistic reasons, grouping the acts of compounding, rather than to signal anything about the *shemen* cases. The *gemara* rejects this. Had the *tanna* intended to group the *pitum* cases, the order could easily have been: compounding incense, compounding oil, and then anointing with oil. That arrangement would have preserved the grouping of the two *pitum* cases while still juxtaposing the two *shemen* cases. Instead, the *tanna* deliberately separated the two *shemen* cases, and this separation carries a message: *deyesh chiluk chataot beineihem* — there is a distinction of *chataot* between them. *Shema mina*.

The *gemara* acknowledges that one might have thought this was already implicit from the *mishnah*'s overall purpose — which is, after all, to enumerate separate cases that each generate a distinct *chatat*. But as the discussion develops, it becomes clear that whether the *mishnah* is claiming a precise count of *chataot* will itself become a matter of inquiry. Rav Natan bar Yitzchak therefore draws on the specific ordering of the *mishnah* as independent structural evidence, beyond any inference from the total count.

## Thirty-Four

### One Person?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַבָּא עַל הַזָּכוּר. תַּנָּא בְּמַאי קָא חָשֵׁיב? אִי בְּגַבְרֵי – דַּל הָאִשָּׁה הַנִּרְבַּעַת, וּבָצְרָא לַהּ חֲדָא! אִי בִּנְשֵׁי קָחָשֵׁיב – דַּל מִינְּהוֹן הַבָּא עַל הַזָּכוּר וְעַל הַבְּהֵמָה, וּבָצְרִי לְהוּ תַּרְתֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **If** he is referring to prohibitions transgressed **by men,** then one should **remove** the case of **a woman who brings an animal upon her** to engage in bestiality, **and** if so, **one** case of *karet* **is lacking from** the tally. **If** he is referring to prohibitions transgressed **by women,** one should **remove from them** the case of a male **who engages in intercourse with** another **male or with an animal, and** if so, **two** cases of *karet* **are lacking from** the tally.

The question now arises: can a single person actually commit all thirty-six (or thirty-four) transgressions listed in the *mishnah* within a single state of forgetfulness? The entire purpose of listing the number was to establish that one person could potentially bring that many *chataot* in a single state of forgetting. But a closer look reveals a problem.

*Ei b'gavra* — if the *mishnah* is speaking from the perspective of a man, then the case of a woman who allows an animal to be brought upon her (*dal isha venirva*) is unavailable to a man, which reduces the count by one to thirty-three. *Ei b'nashi kachashiv* — if the *mishnah* is speaking from a woman's perspective, then the case of *haba al hazachar* and the case of *dal behema* (a man penetrating an animal) are unavailable, reducing the count by two.

### Homosexual Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְעוֹלָם תַּנָּא גַּבְרֵי קָחָשֵׁיב, וְתָנֵי הָכִי: הַבָּא עַל הַזָּכוּר וְהֵבִיא זָכוּר עָלָיו, וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הִיא, דְּאָמַר: חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Actually,** the ***tanna*** is referring to prohibitions transgressed by **men.** One should remove the case of a woman who brings an animal upon her to engage in bestiality, **and** instead **teach** the mishna **like this:** A male **who engages in intercourse with** another **male, and** a male **who brings** another **male upon him** to engage in intercourse. **And** this mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who says** that one who transgresses both of these prohibitions in a single lapse of awareness is **liable** to bring **two** sin offerings.

Rabbi Yochanan resolves this by saying *le'olam tana gavrei* — the *mishnah* speaks throughout from the perspective of a man. He then reinterprets the case that had appeared to refer to a woman with an animal: *vetani hava al hazachar vehevi zachar alav* — the *mishnah* is actually listing two separate cases, namely a man who performs the active role in a homosexual act, and a man who performs the passive role.

This raises the question of whether those constitute two distinct sins. The *gemara* identifies the view behind this interpretation as that of Rabbi Yishmael, *de'amar chayav shtayim* — who holds that the two positions in a homosexual act are considered two distinct *aveirot* and therefore incur two separate *chataot*. This follows from the fact that Rabbi Yishmael derives the prohibition from two separate verses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי מְגַדֵּף בְּסֵיפֵיהּ, וּמוֹקְמִינַן לַהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But from** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches in its latter clause** the case of one who **blasphemes, and we interpret** this clause **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** it may be inferred that **the first clause** of the mishna is **also** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.**

The first verse is *ve'et zachar lo tishkav mishkevei isha* — with a male you shall not lie as one lies with a woman. This verse addresses the one who performs the act, the active partner, with the phrase *mishkevei isha* emphasizing that the actor is the one taking the role normally associated with lying with a woman. The second source is *velo yihyeh kadesh mibnei Yisrael* — there shall be no *kadesh* among the children of Israel, which Rabbi Yishmael understands as addressing the receptive partner. Since two separate verses generate the two aspects of the prohibition, they are treated as two distinct *aveirot*, each with its own *chatat*.

The *gemara* notes in passing an interesting historical dimension: in the ancient Roman world, and in various societies where such acts were prevalent, a sharp social distinction existed between the active and receptive roles — the receptive role being associated with lower social status, with slaves, servants, or those in a subordinate position. Whether or not this is the conceptual background of the *Torah*'s language in *kadesh*, it helps explain how the Talmud frames these as conceptually distinct acts rather than a single unified transgression.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, וּבְרֵישָׁא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: הַבָּא עַל הַזָּכוּר וְהֵבִיא זָכוּר עָלָיו, לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל דְּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ מִתַּרְתֵּי קְרָאֵי, מִ"וְּאֶת זָכָר לֹא תִשְׁכַּב״, וּמִ״וְּלֹא יִהְיֶה קָדֵשׁ מִבְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And if you would say** the entire mishna **is** in fact in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, and with regard to** the *halakha* listed in **the first clause he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** that is not tenable, as **doesn't Rabbi Abbahu say:** In the case of a male **who engages in intercourse with** another **male, and** a male **who brings** another **male upon him** to engage in intercourse, **according to** the statement of **Rabbi Yishmael, who derives** these prohibitions **from two** separate **verses, from: "And you shall not lie with a man** as with a woman" (Leviticus 18:22), **and from: "There shall not be a sodomite of the sons of Israel"** (Deuteronomy 23:18), which is referring to one who engages in homosexual intercourse passively, one who transgresses both of these prohibitions in a single lapse of awareness is **liable** to bring **two** sin offerings.

Rabbi Yishmael's position also entails a consequence for the case of an animal: *haba al habehemah vehevi behemah alav* — even if one argued that a man could play both roles with an animal, Rabbi Yishmael would not separate those into two distinct *aveirot*, because the textual derivation from two verses applies only to the case involving a male human. For the animal case, there is only one verse, and therefore only one *aveira* and one *chatat*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת, דְּמֵחַד קְרָא נָפֵיק לֵיהּ: "אֶת זָכָר לֹא תִשְׁכַּב״, קְרִי בֵּיהּ: לֹא תִשָּׁכֵיב!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu continues: By contrast, **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, he is liable** to bring **only one** sin offering, **as he derives** both prohibitions **from one verse: "You shall not lie [*tishkhav*] with a man,"** in the active form, as one can **read** the verb **in** this verse, written in the Torah without vowels, as though it stated: **Enable your being lain with [*tishakheiv*],** in the passive. Evidently, Rabbi Akiva disagrees with the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael with regard to a male who engages in intercourse with another male and a male who brings another male upon him to engage in intercourse.

An additional complication surfaces: the final item in the *mishnah* is *megadef* — blasphemy, a verbal act. The *mishnah* ends with a dissenting voice suggesting that verbal acts without a physical component should not be included, and the *gemara* attributes the view that *megadef* does incur a *chatat* to Rabbi Akiva, based on the principle in Sanhedrin that *akimat sefatav havei ma'aseh* — the movement of one's lips constitutes a physical act.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: רֵישָׁא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וּבִמְגַדֵּף סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אִי הָכִי, נִיתְנֵי נָמֵי: הַבָּא עַל הַבְּהֵמָה, וְהֵבִיא בְּהֵמָה עָלָיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Rather, the first clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, and with regard to** one who **blasphemes he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so, let** the *tanna* **also teach** two additional cases with regard to bestiality: **One who copulates with an animal,** derived from the verse: "And you shall not lie with any beast" (Leviticus 18:23); **and** a man **who brings an animal upon him** for bestiality, derived from: "There shall not be a sodomite of the sons of Israel" (Deuteronomy 23:18), which can be interpreted as applying to all forms of male anal intercourse, including with animals.

Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, holds *lo chayav ela achat* — one who performs both roles incurs only a single *chatat*, deriving the entire prohibition from a single verse: *et zachar lo tishkav*. Rabbi Akiva reads the same verse in two ways — *lo tishkav* (you shall not lie) and *lo tishachav* (you shall not allow yourself to be lain with) — but treats both readings as aspects of a single prohibition derived from a single textual source. A single source means a single *aveira* and a single *chatat*.

This creates a tension: the *mishnah*'s final line about *megadef* is associated with Rabbi Akiva's position, yet the *reisha* of the *mishnah* — which requires two separate acts for the homosexual case to reach the count of thirty-four — requires Rabbi Yishmael's position. The *gemara* resolves this by saying the *mishnah* is authored by Rabbi Yishmael, who holds the two roles constitute two separate *aveirot* and who, in the case of *megadef*, agrees with Rabbi Akiva that a verbal act incurs a *chatat*.

## Pesach & Milah

### 36 Keritot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אָמַר: תַּנָּא תְּנִי שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשָׁלֹשׁ חַטָּאוֹת, וּתְנִי שָׁלֹשׁ כָּרֵיתוֹת, לְסַיּוֹמִינְהוּ בְּכָרֵיתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another interpretation: **Rabbi Elazar said in the name of Rav:** The mishna is listing prohibitions transgressed by men and not women, but one should not remove from the tally the case of a woman who brings an animal upon her to engage in bestiality. The reason is that the ***tanna*** of the mishna **teaches thirty-three** prohibitions for which a man brings **sin offerings, and he teaches** another **three** instances of ***karet*** in order **to complete** the list of transgressions for which one is liable to be punished **with *karet*.** Those three do not meet the same criteria as the other thirty-three, and the case of a woman who brings an animal upon her to engage in bestiality is one of those three.

Rabbi Eliezer in the name of Rava offers an alternative resolution to the question of the *mishnah*'s count: *tani sheloshim veshalosh chataot* — the *mishnah* is in fact enumerating thirty-three *chataot*, not thirty-six. The number thirty-six is the total of *keritot*, not the total of *chataot* a single person can incur. The *mishnah* gives the full tally of thirty-six *karet* offenses in order to complete its enumeration of *keritot*, while the practical *chatat* liability for a single person reaches only thirty-three.

### Exclude Positive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: פֶּסַח וּמִילָה בְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה – פֶּסַח וּמִילָה אַמַּאי קָתָנֵי לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** What supports this interpretation? **As** the mishna **teaches** in **the latter clause:** And one is liable to receive *karet* for failure to fulfill the mitzva of bringing the **Paschal offering and** the mitzva of **circumcision,** which unlike the cases of prohibitions enumerated in the mishna, are **positive mitzvot. Why** does the *tanna* **teach** the cases of the **Paschal offering and circumcision?**

The *gemara* supports this by asking directly: why are *Pesach* and *milah* listed at all? *Pesach u'Milah amai tani lehu?* If the purpose of the list were to enumerate cases where a *korban* is brought, *Pesach* and *milah* should not appear, since one never brings a *chatat* for failing to perform a positive *mitzvah*. A *baraita* confirms the principle: *hukesh kol haTorah kullah le'avodat kochavim* — the entire *Torah*'s *chatat* obligation is modeled on the case of *avodah zarah beshogeg*, as derived from the verse *Torah achat yihyeh lachem la'oseh bishgagah*, juxtaposed with *vehanefesh asher ta'aseh beyad ramah et Hashem hu megadef*. Since *avodah zarah* is a prohibition — a *shev ve'al ta'aseh* — only transgressions of that type generate a *chatat*. Failure to perform a positive commandment never generates a *chatat*. *Pesach* and *milah* are therefore listed solely to complete the tally of *keritot*, not to imply any *chatat* obligation. By the same logic, the debate about the passive role in the homosexual act — whether it generates a *chatat* (Rabbi Yishmael) or not (Rabbi Akiva) — has direct practical implications for the final count.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיתֵּימָא לְאֵיתוֹיֵי קׇרְבָּן עֲלֵיהוֹן, מִי מַיְיתֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא: הוּקְּשָׁה כָּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דִּכְתִיב: "תּוֹרָה אַחַת יִהְיֶה לָכֶם לָעוֹשֶׂה בִּשְׁגָגָה. וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשֶׂה בְּיָד רָמָה״ – מָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֵׁב וְאַל תַּעֲשֶׂה, אַף כֹּל שֵׁב וְאַל תַּעֲשֶׂה!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** the mishna mentions these positive mitzvot **to** teach that **one brings an offering for** unwittingly failing to perform **them, does one** in fact **bring** an offering in such a case? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **The entire Torah is juxtaposed to idolatry, as it is written: "You shall have one law [*torah*] for him that does unwittingly. But the soul that does with a high hand,** whether he be home-born or a stranger, he blasphemes the Lord; and that soul shall be cut off from among his people" (Numbers 15:29–30). **Just as idol worship** is a situation in which the Torah says to a person: **Sit and refrain from action,** and one who transgresses this prohibition unwittingly must bring a sin offering, **so too,** with regard to **every** transgression in which the Torah says that one must **sit and refrain from action,** one is liable to bring a sin offering for performing the act. Accordingly, one is not liable to bring an offering for the unwitting failure to perform a positive mitzva, such as bringing the Paschal offering or circumcision.

### 33 Chataot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: תְּנָא שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשָׁלֹשׁ חַטָּאוֹת מַאן דְּעָבְדִי בְּשׁוֹגֵג, וַהֲדַר תְּנָא שָׁלֹשׁ כָּרֵיתוֹת לְסַיּוֹמִינְהוּ לְכָרֵיתוֹת, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes its statement: **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from** the mishna that the *tanna* of the mishna **taught thirty-three** prohibitions for which a man brings **sin offerings,** which apply to **one who acts unwittingly, and he taught** another **three** instances of ***karet*** in order **to complete** the list of transgressions for which one is liable to be punished with ***karet*?** These three instances are a woman who brings an animal upon her to engage in bestiality, one who fails to bring the Paschal offering, and one who fails to perform circumcision. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** the mishna that this is the case.

The *gemara* concludes: *shema mina tana sheloshim veshalosh chataot* — the *mishnah* is enumerating thirty-three *chataot* that a single person can potentially incur. *Ve'hadar tani sheloshim veshesh keritot vesiyumei bekeritot* — it then completes the enumeration of thirty-six *keritot* to provide the full picture of *karet* offenses in the *Torah*. The *masechet* is called *Keritot* because *karet* is the starting point — identifying who is subject to *karet* determines who is obligated in a *chatat*. But the *masechet*'s practical focus is on those *chatat* obligations. The three cases that appear in the list but do not generate a *chatat* — the passive role in the homosexual act (on some views) and the two positive commandments of *Pesach* and *milah* — are present to give the full accounting of *keritot*, not to extend the *chatat* count. One person can bring at most thirty-three *chataot*.

## Shabbat Case

### Forgot Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְחַלֵּל אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת. אָמְרִי: שַׁבָּת אַרְבָּעִים חָסֵר אַחַת הָוְויָין! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תַּנָּא שִׁגְגַת שַׁבָּת וּזְדוֹן מְלָאכוֹת, דְּלָא מִחַיַּיב אֶלָּא חֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna's list of prohibitions for whose unwitting violation one must bring a sin offering includes **one who desecrates Shabbat.** The Sages express their surprise at this ruling and **say:** The primary labors of **Shabbat are forty-less-one,** and one is liable to bring a sin offering for each primary labor he violates. That alone totals more than the thirty-six cases mentioned in the mishna. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The *tanna* **taught** a case where his action was **unwitting with regard to Shabbat,** i.e., he was unaware it was Shabbat, **and** his action was **intentional with regard to** the prohibited **labors,** in that he was aware that the acts he performed were labors that are prohibited on Shabbat. In that case, the *halakha* is **that he is liable** to bring **only one** sin offering, as he committed only a single error.

The *gemara* now turns to *Shabbat* as a case study in how a single state of forgetfulness can interact with multiple possible violations. The *mishnah* lists *hamechallel et haShabbat* as a single entry. Yet one who violates *Shabbat* could potentially incur thirty-nine separate *chataot* — one for each *melacha* — if one knew it was *Shabbat* but forgot that each individual act was forbidden. Rabbi Yochanan explains: *tana shigagat Shabbat vezadon melachot* — the *mishnah* is referring to the case where one forgot it was *Shabbat* entirely, while intentionally performing acts that are in fact *melachot*. Because one's state of forgetfulness was specifically that it was *Shabbat*, all acts of *chillul Shabbat* committed in that state are subsumed under a single *chatat*. They are not thirty-nine distinct *aveirot* because what was forgotten was *Shabbat* itself, not the individual prohibitions.

### Me'achas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: "הֵנָּה״ שֶׁהִיא "אַחַת״ – שִׁגְגַת שַׁבָּת וּזְדוֹן מְלָאכוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "Speak to the children of Israel, saying: A soul that sins in error, from all the commandments of God that may not be performed, and performs from one of them [*me'aḥat mehena*]" (Leviticus 4:2). The expression "*me'aḥat mehena*" is interpreted in several ways. One of them is: There is an instance of **"them [*hena*],"** i.e., many unwitting transgressions, **which is** like **"one [*aḥat*],"** a single unwitting transgression, in that one is liable to bring only one sin offering. How so? When one performed many labors where his action was **unwitting with regard to Shabbat but** his action was **intentional with regard to** the prohibited **labors,** he brings only one sin offering. That is the case referred to in the mishna.

The *gemara* grounds this in the verse *asher ya'aseh mei'achas meiheina* — which does not say simply *achas meiheina* (one of these) but *mei'achas meiheina* (from one from these). This phrasing is taken to indicate variability: sometimes multiple actions count as one, and sometimes one category encompasses many. The full *baraita* unpacks both scenarios. The phrase *achas shehi heina* — one that is many — refers to *shigagat Shabbat vezadon melachot*: one forgot it was *Shabbat* and knowingly performed multiple acts. Despite the multiplicity of acts, it is counted as one, because the error was singular. Conversely, *heina shehein achat* — many that are one — refers to *zedon Shabbat veshigagat melachot*: one knew it was *Shabbat* but forgot that specific acts were prohibited. There, each *melacha* constitutes a distinct *aveira*, yielding thirty-nine separate *chataot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנֵי זְדוֹן שַׁבָּת וְשִׁגְגַת מְלָאכוֹת, דְּמִחַיַּיב אַרְבָּעִים חָסֵר אַחַת! דְּתַנְיָא: "וְעָשָׂה מֵאַחַת מֵהֵנָּה״ – פְּעָמִים שֶׁחַיָּיב אַחַת עַל כּוּלָּן, וּפְעָמִים שֶׁחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. דְּתַנְיָא: "אַחַת״ שֶׁהִיא "הֵנָּה״ – זְדוֹן שַׁבָּת וְשִׁגְגַת מְלָאכוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But let** the *tanna* **teach** a case where his action was **intentional with regard to Shabbat,** i.e., he knew it was Shabbat, **but** his action was **unwitting with regard to** the prohibited **labors,** as he was unaware they were prohibited. In that case, the *halakha* is **that he is liable** to bring **forty-less-one** sin offerings, one for each primary category of labor. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And performs from one of them."** This teaches that there are **times when** one is **liable** to bring **one** sin offering **for all** of his transgressions, **and** there are **times when** one is **liable** to bring a sin offering **for each and every** transgression. And **it is** further **taught** in a *baraita*: There is **"one [*aḥat*],"** i.e., the transgression of one prohibition, **which is** like **"them [*hena*],"** i.e., many unwitting transgressions, in that one is liable to bring multiple sin offerings. How so? When one performs many labors where his action was **intentional with regard to Shabbat but** his action was **unwitting with regard to** the prohibited **labors,** he brings multiple sin offerings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנָא שִׁגְגַת שַׁבָּת וּזְדוֹן מְלָאכוֹת עֲדִיפָא לֵיהּ, דְּמֵחַטָּאת מִיהָא לָא מִיפְּטַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The ***tanna* prefers** to state the case of one who performed many labors where his action was **unwitting with regard to Shabbat but** it was **intentional with regard to** the prohibited **labors,** in order to emphasize **that he is not exempt from** bringing **at least** one **sin offering,** even though he performed the acts of labor intentionally.

## Shabbat Case (II)

### Avodah Zarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אַתָּה מוֹצֵא גַּבֵּי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דְּקָתָנֵי: שִׁגְגַת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וּזְדוֹן עֲבוֹדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And likewise, you find** that the *tanna* of the mishna taught in this manner **with regard to idol worship, as he teaches** liability to bring one sin offering, despite the fact that the sinner may have performed four prohibited rites: Slaughtering, burning incense, pouring libations, and bowing. How so? This was a case where, as with Shabbat, his actions were **unwitting with regard to idol worship,** as he did not know he was worshipping an idol, **but** they were **intentional with regard to** the prohibited **rites,** i.e., he was aware these rites are prohibited.

A parallel structure applies to *avodah zarah*. Just as *Shabbat* presents two possible scenarios of forgetfulness that yield different *chatat* counts, so too with *avodah zarah*: one may forget that *avodah zarah* is forbidden entirely, or one may remember the general prohibition but fail to recognize that specific acts — *shechitah*, *haktarah*, *hishtachavayah* — constitute *avodah zarah*. The first scenario yields a single *chatat*; the second yields separate *chataot* for each distinct act. The *gemara* proceeds to examine what the first scenario — *shigagat avodah zarah* — actually means in practice, since determining the precise nature of the error is essential to characterizing it as *shogeg*.

---

## Avodah Zarah

### Cases Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁגְגַת אֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּעָמַד בְּבֵית עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְסָבַר בֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת הִיא וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה – הֲרֵי לִבּוֹ לַשָּׁמַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of performing an act **unwittingly with regard to idol worship? If we say** it is referring to a case **where he was standing in a house of idol worship and thought** that **it was a synagogue and bowed** there, and it turned out that he bowed toward an idol, in this situation he commits no transgression: Since **his heart was** directed **toward Heaven** it is not even an unwitting transgression.

The *gemara* asks directly: *shigagat avodas kochavim heichi dami* — what exactly does it mean to err about *avodah zarah* itself, as distinct from erring about the specific acts? The question is how one could simultaneously forget that *avodah zarah* is forbidden while still being aware that those acts are in some sense significant or problematic.

### Temple Intent

One proposed scenario: a person was in a temple of idol worship but believed he was in a synagogue, and bowed down. The *gemara* rejects this as a case of *shigagat avodah zarah* with the observation: *harei libo lashamayim* — his heart was directed toward heaven. If he was bowing down intending to pray to God, the fact that he was physically in a pagan temple and facing an idol is irrelevant. His act was directed to God and does not constitute *avodah zarah* at all. There is no *shogeg* because there is no *aveira*.

### Statue Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא דַּחֲזָא אִנְדְּרָטָא וּסְגֵיד לֵהּ? אִי דְּקַבְּלֵיהּ עֲלֵיהּ בֶּאֱלוֹהַּ – בַּר סְקִילָה הוּא! וְאִי דְּלָא קַבְּלֵיהּ בֶּאֱלוֹהַּ – לָאו מִידֵּי הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one might say it is referring to a case **where he saw a statue [*andarta*]** in the image of the king **and bowed to it.** Again, what are the circumstances of this case? **If** the *baraita* is referring to a situation **where** he bowed because **he accepted** that image **upon himself as a god,** he engaged in idol worship intentionally and therefore **he is subject to** death by **stoning,** and he is not required to bring a sin offering. **And if he did not accept** that image **upon himself as a god** and bowed merely in deference to the monarch, **it is a meaningless** act, not idol worship at all.

A second scenario: one saw a statue — *andrarta*, a royal or decorative statue, not necessarily an object of worship — and bowed down to it. The *gemara* finds this equally unsuitable. *Iy de'kablih le'eloah* — if one treated the statue as a deity, then the worship was fully intentional *avodah zarah* and there is no element of *shogeg*. *Ve'iy de'lo kablih le'eloah* — if one did not treat it as a deity but merely showed obeisance to the king's image, then *lav midy hu* — this is not *avodah zarah* at all. Again, there is no case of *shogeg*.

### Motives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מֵאַהֲבָה וּמִיִּרְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is referring to a case where one bowed **out of love** for someone who requested that he bow before the statue, **or out of fear** of someone demanding of him to do so. He was under the impression that unless he wholeheartedly has the intention to worship the idol there is no prohibition involved. This is an unwitting transgression.

A third and more complex scenario: one worshipped an idol *me'ahavah u'miyirah* — out of love or fear, not out of genuine religious conviction. Rashi offers examples: bowing before an idol to gain social advancement (*me'ahavah*), or performing acts of worship under coercion or threat (*miyirah*). In these cases, one is going through the physical motions of *avodah zarah* but without sincere theological commitment to the idol. One may have believed — incorrectly — that such worship under these circumstances was permissible. That mistaken belief would constitute the *shogeg*.

### Abaye vs Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְאַבַּיֵּי דְּאָמַר חַיָּיב, אֶלָּא לְרָבָא דְּאָמַר פָּטוּר, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This works out well according to Abaye, who says:** One who bows to an idol out of love or fear is **liable** to bring a sin offering. **But according to Rava, who says:** One who bows to an idol out of love or fear is **exempt** from bringing a sin offering, **what can be said?** What are the circumstances of an unwitting transgression with regard to idol worship?

This scenario is the subject of a dispute between Abaye and Rava. Abaye holds *chayav* — even worship performed out of love or fear, without genuine theological belief in the deity, constitutes *avodah zarah*, and one who mistakenly thought such worship was permitted has committed *avodah zarah beshogeg*. Rava holds *patur* — such worship is not *avodah zarah* at all if one does not genuinely accept the object as a deity. Rava's position raises the question of martyrdom: if worship under duress or for ulterior motives is not *avodah zarah*, on what basis is there an obligation to give one's life rather than comply? The *gemara* notes this tension without fully resolving it here. Tosafot addresses it, and the suggestion is that the *kiddush Hashem* dimension operates independently of the technical *aveira* status.

### Thinking Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּאוֹמֵר מוּתָּר, דְּעַד כָּאן לָא קָבָעֵי מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן אֶלָּא אִי חֲדָא מִחַיַּיב אִי תַּרְתֵּי מִחַיַּיב, אָבֵל מִיפְּטַר לִגְמָרֵי לָא בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is referring to a case **where one says** to himself that this is **permitted.** He is under the impression that idol worship is permitted, and his unwitting act was the result of ignorance, not forgetfulness. **As, Rava raised a dilemma before Rav Naḥman** with regard to Shabbat **only** so it could be determined **whether to deem** one who is unaware of both the essence of Shabbat and the specific prohibited labors **liable** to bring **one** sin offering, **or whether to deem** him **liable** to bring **two** sin offerings. **But** as for the possibility to **exempt him completely,** he did **not raise** such **a dilemma before him.**

The *gemara* ultimately settles on a case that works as *shigagat avodah zarah*: *be'omer mutar* — one who knew that *avodah zarah* was an act of idol worship but believed it was permitted. This person was not confused about what the act was; the error was legal — a mistaken belief that the *Torah* permits *avodah zarah*. This constitutes *shogeg*, and in such a case one's single error encompasses all the specific acts performed. All the various acts of *avodah zarah* performed while laboring under this single legal mistake are subsumed under one *chatat*.

The *gemara* closes by noting: *de'ad kan lo ka'ba'ei mineih Rava me'Rav Nachman ela iy echada mechayvei tartei mechayvei* — Rava's question to Rav Nachman was specifically about whether, when both the general prohibition and the specific acts were forgotten simultaneously, one incurs one *chatat* or multiple. The scenario of *be'omer mutar* is the baseline: knowing the acts were *avodah zarah* but thinking them permitted constitutes one clear error, generating one *chatat* for all acts performed in that state. The more complex case — where one forgot both the general prohibition and the particulars — raises the question of whether the two layers of forgetfulness collapse into one or multiply the *chatat* obligation. Importantly, the *gemara* notes that the *patur legamrei* option is not on the table: one minimally brings one *chatat*. This also stands in some tension with a remark attributed to a pseudo-Rashi on 2a, where *be'omer mutar* was characterized as an *ones*. The *gemara* here treats it unambiguously as *shogeg*, at least in the context of *avodah zarah* — though it remains possible that context matters, and *be'omer mutar* for other transgressions may be treated differently.
