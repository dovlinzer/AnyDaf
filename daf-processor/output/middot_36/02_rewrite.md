# Middot 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Southwest Corner

### Face of Altar

The mishnah opens with a discussion of the southwest corner of the *mizbe'ach*. To orient ourselves: the ramp (*kevesh*) approached from the south, and the west side of the *mizbe'ach* faced the *Heichal*. This places the southwest corner at the junction of those two sides — and that corner carries a particular significance, for it is *pnei hamizbe'ach*, the face of the altar. It is simultaneously *lifnei Hashem*, before God, because it faces the *Heichal*. This dual quality — facing the direction of approach from the ramp and facing the *Heichal* — made the southwest corner the focal point for many of the *zrikos* of the blood, since certain acts of *zrikat hadam* require this corner specifically in order to achieve both conditions at once.

It bears repeating what was established at the end of the previous session: the *mizbe'ach* did not have a *yesod* (base) running along most of the east side or most of the south side, but it did have a *yesod* at the northeast and southwest corners. This meant that *zrikat hadam* could not be performed at the southeast corner, since *zrikat hadam* requires *keneged hayesod* — it must be performed above a base. For the standard *zrikos* of non-*chatas* offerings, two *zrikos* were performed, each at a corner and each spreading across two walls, so that four sides of the altar were effectively covered. The only diagonally opposite pair of corners that both had a *yesod* beneath them were the northeast and the southwest — and so those were the corners used.

### Blood Holes

The mishnah now tells us that in this southwest corner there were *shnei nekavim*, two holes, described as *k'min shnei chotamim dakim* — like two thin nostrils. Through these holes the bloods that were placed on the *yesod maaravi* and the *yesod dromi* would flow down.

Some bloods were poured on the western base. These were the bloods brought out of the *Heichal* after *zrikat hadam* had been performed inside — the special cases of the *parim hanisrafim*, such as the bull brought when the *Beis Din* issued an erroneous ruling and the community followed it, and similarly the offerings of *Yom Kippur*. When the *kohen* exited the *Heichal* carrying this blood, the first thing he encountered was the western base, and so the blood was poured there — specifically on the western portion of the southwest corner. Other bloods were poured on the southern base: when descending from the *mizbe'ach* after the normal *zrikat hadam* on the outer altar, the *kohen* found himself opposite the southern face of this corner and poured the blood on the southern portion.

The arrangement of the two holes thus reflected these two distinct flows: one hole oriented more toward the west for the blood brought out of the *Heichal*, and one oriented toward the south for the blood from the regular outer-altar *zrika*. Both holes were aligned along a single line rather than being placed at a diagonal; one was simply positioned somewhat more to the west, and the other somewhat less so. The bloods that passed through these holes descended, mixed together in a channel (*amah*), and from there flowed out to the Kidron Valley to the east.

### Underground Chamber

The mishnah continues: *lemata b'ritzpa b'ota keren makom haya sham amah al amah* — lower down on the floor of that corner there was a space of one *amah* by one *amah*, covered by a *tavla shel shayish*, a marble slab serving as a kind of manhole cover. Unlike the round manhole covers familiar from modern infrastructure — chosen precisely because a round cover cannot fall through a round hole at any angle, whereas a square cover could — this one was square. It had a ring affixed to it, *tabaat hayta kvua ba*, by which it could be lifted, and through it one could descend to the underground caverns: *shebo yordim lashit*.

This connects to a discussion in tractate *Sukkah* about the *shis* — the underground hollow, whether understood as deep underground caverns or as a hollow space within the structure of the *mizbe'ach* itself — into which wine and water would flow when poured at the top of the altar during the libations of *Sukkos*. The access point through this manhole cover in the southwest corner served as the entry by which the caverns could be cleaned out. The mishnah states explicitly: *yordim lashit u'menakin oto* — they would descend and clean it out. In context this refers to congealed blood that had somehow accumulated there on its way through to the *amah*, though the *shis* is more commonly discussed in connection with the congealed wine and water of the *Sukkos* libations.

## The Ramp

### Ramp Dimensions

The mishnah tells us that the *kevesh* — the ramp — was to the south of the *mizbe'ach*: *vehakevesh haya lidromo*. Its length was 32 *amot* and its width was 16 *amot*. To place this in context: the base of the *mizbe'ach* was 32 *amot* across, and by the level of the *karnaiot* it had narrowed to 28 *amot*. A ramp of 16 *amot* width thus left 6 *amot* of the altar face visible on each side of the ramp at the top. The full width of the base below allowed 8 *amot* of clearance on each side.

### Ramp Angle

With a length of 32 *amot* and a rise of 10 *amot*, the ramp forms a right triangle with legs of 10 and 32. The angle of incline works out to approximately 17.35 degrees — a very gentle, manageable slope, which makes sense given that *kohanim* ascended and descended it constantly throughout the day carrying offerings, vessels, and burning coals.

### Bird Offering Niche

On the western side of the ramp there was a *revuva* — a carved-out section or niche: *ushrevuva hayeta lo bama'aravo*. This is where *psulei chatat ha'of* were stored — disqualified bird *chatas* offerings. They were placed there and left until they became *pasul* through *linah* (overnight), at which point they would be burned.

It is not immediately clear why *psulei chatat ha'of* specifically would require such a storage niche. One possibility, however, is that the phrase here really refers to the *chatat ha'of al hasafek* — the bird *chatas* brought in cases of doubt. There is a well-known *halacha* that one may not ordinarily bring a *chatas* when uncertain of one's obligation, as this would constitute *chullin ba'azarah*. Nevertheless, a special tradition permits the *chatat ha'of al hasafek*, most notably in cases involving a *yoledet* — a woman who has given birth — where a miscarriage may leave genuine uncertainty as to whether she is obligated. In such cases the bird *chatas* is brought conditionally, and since it cannot be eaten and cannot simply be discarded, it must wait until it is rendered *pasul b'linah* and then burned. If the incidence of such offerings was significant, a dedicated storage niche would have been practically necessary. The ramp was constructed entirely of stone, and this niche was simply a hollowed-out section within it.

## Altar Stones

### Biblical Sources

The mishnah now addresses the construction materials of both the ramp and the *mizbe'ach* itself. Stones for both were quarried from Beit Kerem, digging below the *betulah* — the virgin earth, ground that had never previously been dug — so as to bring up *avanim shlemot*, whole, uncarved stones. This requirement derives from two distinct *pesukim*: one states *avanim shlemot tivneh et mizbe'ach Hashem Elokecha* — the altar shall be built of whole stones; the other states *lo tivneh atehen gazit ki charbecha hunafta aleha vatachalaleha* — do not build them of hewn stone, for your sword you have waved over it and thereby desecrated it.

### Quarrying Methods

One might ask: if the stones had never to have iron waved over them, how were they extracted from the earth at all? *Rashi* states that they would dig with wooden axes: *v'chofrin b'kardumot etz*. How effective a wood implement would be against virgin earth is not entirely clear. Another possibility is that the prohibition is specific to *barzel* — iron — as the text explicitly says, and so other metals might have been permissible, or alternatively that the metal tools were wrapped in cloth so that the metal never directly contacted the stone. Perhaps the initial breaking of the earth was done with metal and then wood implements were used once the stone was exposed. These remain genuinely open questions.

### Iron Invalidation

The mishnah makes explicit that the concern is not limited to chipping: *shehabarzel posel b'negi'ah* — iron invalidates even by mere contact, even without chipping. And *hapgima b'chol davar* — a chip caused by anything, not only iron, also invalidates. The mishnah thus articulates two distinct disqualifications: a chip from any cause, and mere contact with iron even if the stone remains perfectly intact.

The reason iron is singled out goes beyond the practical concern of chipping. The mishnah concludes with a striking observation: *shehabarzel nivra l'katzer yamav shel adam* — iron was created to shorten human life, being the material of weapons of war; *v'hamizbe'ach nivra l'haarich yamav shel adam* — and the altar was created to extend human life, through atonement; *eino badin sheyanif ham'katzer al ham'aarich* — it is not fitting that the shortener be waved over that which exists to lengthen. This reasoning accounts not only for the prohibition on chipping but for why even contact alone suffices to invalidate.

It should be noted that this strict rule applies specifically to the *mizbe'ach*. For the other stones of the *Beis Hamikdash*, the prohibition appears to derive from a different *pasuk*, specifically regarding Shlomo's Temple, where no *kli barzel* was heard on *Har Habayis*. Some understand that verse to permit carving the stones outside of *Har Habayis* and then bringing them in — an extension that applies only to those other stones. With respect to the *mizbe'ach* itself, no such leniency is stated, and the absolute requirement of *shleimos* and freedom from iron contact remains in force. This explains why the *mizbe'ach* was apparently constructed not from large carved blocks but essentially from an aggregate of many small stones held together by a kind of plaster or cement — the only way to achieve a precise geometric shape using only whole, uncarved stones.

### Maintenance

If one of the stones of the *mizbe'ach* became chipped, only that stone is invalidated; the rest of the *mizbe'ach* remains valid. The damaged stone is removed and replaced, but there is no disqualification of the whole structure. The mishnah then states that the altar was whitewashed — *u'malbinin otan pa'amayim bashana* — twice a year: once before *Pesach* and once before *Chag* (Sukkos), the two major *regalim* that drew the largest pilgrimages. The *Heichal* — or, according to some *mefarshim*, the entire *Beis Hamikdash* — was whitened once a year, before *erev Pesach*, the time of greatest public attendance.

The *mefarshim* understand "whitening" here to mean replastering, applying a fresh coat of plaster. One might wonder whether repeated coats of plaster applied year after year would gradually expand the dimensions of the *mizbe'ach* beyond its prescribed measurements. The likely answer is that the lime plaster used was extremely thin — essentially a whitewash — and moreover that any replastering would have been preceded by scraping off the old coat, much as one prepares a surface before repainting. Lime is also a disinfectant, which would have been practically valuable given the volume of blood involved.

### Weekly Cleaning

*Rebbi* adds a further practice: *kol erev Shabbos malbinoson b'mappa mipnei hadamim* — every *erev Shabbos* they would clean the stones with a rag on account of the blood. This is not the twice-yearly replastering but a more modest weekly wiping, presumably with water, to remove the blood that had accumulated throughout the week.

The mishnah then notes that the plaster was not applied with an iron applicator — *lo haya sado b'chafis shel barzel* — lest the applicator accidentally touch the stone itself and thereby invalidate it. Even though the plaster ordinarily forms a *chatzitza* between the tool and the stone, one cannot always be sufficiently careful, and there is a risk of direct contact.

## Slaughtering Station

### Slaughtering Rings

The *taba'os* — the rings — were to the north of the *mizbe'ach*. The *kodshei kodashim* must be slaughtered in the north of the *azarah*, so the entire apparatus for *shechita* and subsequent processing was set up there. The rings were arranged in either six rows of four each or four rows of six each — in any event a six-by-four arrangement, the only question being which dimension ran along which axis. It was upon these rings that the *shechita* of the *kodshim* was performed.

According to most opinions, the rings were half-rings into which the neck of the animal was placed, with the other half then fixed into the ground — somewhat like a handcuff. According to the *Rambam*, however, the rings were used for the *akeidah* of the animals, holding their legs.

### Slaughterhouse Building

Beyond the area of the rings, further to the north, was the *beis hamitbachayim* — the slaughterhouse building — where the flaying, dismemberment, and disemboweling of the animals took place after *shechita*. The *beis hamitbachayim* had eight short pillars, *chet amudim nannasim*, with cedar frames resting on top of them.

### Iron Hooks

Into these cedar frames were fixed iron hooks — *ankleyot shel barzel hayu kevu'im bahem* — arranged in three rows on each frame. It is suggested that the three rows corresponded to the three types of animals offered: cattle, goats, and sheep, each requiring a different hanging height so that the full body of the animal could be properly suspended and extended for flaying.

### Flaying Tables

On these hooks the animals were suspended upside down — *shebahem tolin umafshitin* — and then flayed. The actual work of flaying was performed on marble tables positioned between the pillars: *al shulchanot shel shayish shebein ha'amudim*. Marble was used precisely because it is cold and non-porous, keeping the meat from spoiling. The arrangement seems to have been that the animal was first set up on the frame, hung at the appropriate height, and then transferred to the marble table for the skilled work of flaying and butchering.

## The Laver

### Laver Position

Moving inward from the *mizbe'ach* toward the *Ulam*, the mishnah turns to the *kiyor* — the laver, the large vessel of water in which the *kohanim* would sanctify their hands and feet before performing the *avodah*. The *kiyor* stood between the *Ulam* and the *mizbe'ach*: *hakiyor haya bein ha'ulam lamizbe'ach*. It was not centered in that space but was offset toward the south — *umashuch klapei hadarom* — in the direction of the ramp.

## Twelve Steps

### Step Space

The space between the *Ulam* and the back of the *mizbe'ach* measured 22 *amot*. The progression of sacred spaces as one moves from east to west is thus: the *Ezrat Nashim*, then the *Ezrat Yisrael* (11 *amot*), then the *Ezrat Kohanim* (another 11 *amot*), then the *mizbe'ach* with its 32-*amah* base, and then this 22-*amah* space before the steps of the *Ulam*.

### Step Dimensions

In ascending from the *azarah* to the *Ulam*, one climbed twelve steps. According to the *Bartenura*'s reading, the steps were configured as follows. The first step was half an *amah* high and one *amah* deep. Steps two and three were each one *amah* high and one *amah* deep. Then there was a *rovad* — a platform — of three *amot*. This pattern then repeated: one *amah*, one *amah*, *rovad* of three *amot*; again one *amah*, one *amah*, *rovad* of three; and finally another *amah*, *amah* sequence leading to the uppermost *rovad*, which measured four *amot* deep (and according to *Rabbi Yehudah*, five *amot*).

Adding these together: the first group of four steps and their platform covers six *amot*; the subsequent groupings of steps and platforms bring the total to approximately nineteen *amot* by the *Bartenura*'s reckoning, or twenty-one by other readings, and twenty according to *Rabbi Yehudah*. In a total space of 22 *amot*, this means that the steps consumed nearly the entire span, leaving very little open ground between the foot of the steps and the back of the *mizbe'ach*.

### Platform Function

The configuration — with its large platform steps of three or four *amot* — is striking, and it finds an explanation in the use of these steps for *birkat kohanim*. The *kohanim* delivering the priestly blessing would stand on these steps. The staggered platforms of varying depths created something like stadium seating, allowing multiple rows of *kohanim* to stand at different heights, each visible and each with adequate standing space. This accounts for why the steps were not simply uniform risers but alternated between narrow climbing steps and broad platforms — the platforms served as stations for the priestly blessing arrangement.

## The Ulam

### Ulam Doorway

The opening of the *Ulam* was enormous: forty *amot* high and twenty *amot* wide — sixty feet in height and thirty feet in width. This is double the standard dimensions used for the other major openings of the *Beis Hamikdash*, and since a door of such proportions would have posed extraordinary practical challenges, the *Ulam* was hung with a curtain rather than fitted with doors.

### Decorative Poles

Above the frame of the *Ulam* opening were five poles of ashwood. The lowest of these poles extended one *amah* beyond the opening on each side, making it twenty-two *amot* across. Each successive pole extended one *amah* further on each side beyond the one below it, creating a pyramidal or triangular silhouette. The topmost pole therefore measured thirty *amot* across: starting at twenty-two, then twenty-four, twenty-six, twenty-eight, and finally thirty. Between each pair of poles ran a *nidbach shel avanim* — a row of bricks — so that the arrangement formed a layered decorative structure above the doorframe. It is possible, though speculative, that the poles also served a functional role in suspending the curtain, though they sat above the frame rather than within it, which makes this less certain.

It is also worth noting — as had been observed in an earlier *mishnah* — that all the frames and openings of the *Beis Hamikdash* were constructed with lintels rather than arches, the *Tadi* gate being the single exception.

### Support Beams

To prevent the walls of the *Heichal* and the *Ulam* from buckling under their own weight, *kelonsaot shel erez* — cedar beams — were fixed running from the wall of the *Heichal* to the wall of the *Ulam*: *mikotlo shel Heichal lekotlo shel Ulam keday shelo yiva'at*. These structural beams counteracted the lateral forces that might otherwise cause the massive walls to shift or collapse outward.

### Golden Chains

On the ceiling of the *Ulam* — sixty feet overhead — golden chains were fixed: *sharsherot shel zahav hayu kevu'im b'tikrat ha'ulam*. Young *kohanim*, *pirchei kehuna*, would climb these chains in order to see the crowns: *v'ro'im et ha'atarot*. The biblical source cited is the verse from Zechariah stating that the crowns shall be *l'Chelem ul'Tuviya ul'Yeda'aya ul'Chen ben Tzefanya l'zikaron b'Heichal Hashem* — as a remembrance in the *Heichal* of God. The precise nature of these crowns and what the young *kohanim* could see by ascending to the ceiling of the *Ulam* is not entirely clear from the *mishnah*. As for the chains themselves, given that gold is a relatively weak metal, these must have been some manner of gold alloy capable of bearing weight.

### Golden Grapevine

Over the opening of the *Heichal* there stood an ornamental golden grapevine, *gefen shel zahav*, suspended on a lattice framework: *u'mudla al gabei kelonsot*. Donors could contribute a golden leaf, a golden grape, or an entire cluster — *aleh o gargir o eshkol* — and bring it to be hung on the vine. *Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Tzadok* relates that there was once an occasion when three hundred *kohanim* were required to remove all the gold that had accumulated on the vine, an account that conveys how substantial the donations became over time, even if the number is understood as a vivid exaggeration. The *Ulam* rather than the *Heichal* was the space being entered for the hanging, since the *Heichal* interior was restricted to those performing the *avodah*.

## Heichal Doors

### Heichal Opening

The opening of the *Heichal* itself — the inner sanctuary containing the *shulchan*, the *menorah*, and the *mizbe'ach hazahav* — was twenty *amot* high and ten *amot* wide: *govho esrim ama v'rochvo eser ama*. This is the standard dimension used throughout the *Beis Hamikdash* for its principal openings, in contrast to the doubled dimensions of the *Ulam* entrance.

### Wall Thickness

The wall of the *Heichal* was six *amot* thick — approximately ten feet. This enormous thickness was itself a functional element of the door system. The opening had four doors in total: two on the outside and two on the inside, *shtayim bifnim v'shtayim bachutz*, as derived from the verse in Yechezkel: *ushtayim dlatos la-heichal vela-kodesh*.

The outer doors opened inward, into the thickness of the wall — *ha-chitzonot niftachos letoch hapetach* — essentially covering the width of the wall on the inside, *lechafos ovyo shel kotel*. The inner doors opened outward into the *Heichal* interior — *vehapenimiyot niftachos letoch habayis* — folding back flat against the inner wall of the *Heichal*, *lechafos achar hadlatos*.

### Gold Plating

The entire interior of the *Heichal* was covered in gold — *shekol habayis tuach bezahav* — with one exception: the sections of wall concealed behind the open doors were left unplated, *chutz me-achar hadlatos*. The reasoning is straightforward: once the doors were opened, those sections of wall were invisible, covered by the door panels themselves. The only person who would ever see the bare wall would be the *kohen* entering to open the doors before anyone else arrived, and once the doors were open they covered that section entirely. Since the door panels themselves were gold-plated, there was no practical reason to plate the wall behind them.

### Bi-Fold Doors

*Rabbi Yehudah* disagrees with the basic arrangement described above. In his view, both sets of doors opened to the inside of the wall thickness. One might object that this is impossible: each door was five *amot* wide, and the wall was only six *amot* thick, leaving only one *amah* to spare — not enough for two sets of five-*amah* doors both opening inward. *Rabbi Yehudah*'s answer is that the doors were constructed as *istermita* — bi-fold doors, what we might call Chinese folding doors, hinged at the center. Each five-*amah* door was actually two panels of two and a half *amot* each, connected by a central hinge, so that when opened the door folded back on itself: *venifalos leachoreihen*, *elu shtei amos u-mechtza v'elu shtei amos u-mechtza*. With each folded door occupying only two and a half *amot*, both the outer pair and the inner pair could open into the six-*amah* thickness of the wall, with half an *amah* remaining on each side for the *mezuza* — the doorpost frame and hinge hardware: *chatzi amah mezuza mikan v'chatzi amah mezuza mikan*. The scriptural support cited is the verse stating *ushtayim musabos dlatos* — the two doors shall be *musabos*, hinged, with two sections to each door.

### Side Chambers

Alongside the great gate of the *Heichal* there were two small chambers, *pishpishin* — one to the north and one to the south: *echad batzafon v'echad badarom*. These were not small doors set within the larger door panels but rather separate chambers or passageways cut into the thickness of the wall to the side of the main opening. Through the northern *pishpash* one could gain access to the interior of the *Heichal* without passing through the main doors — an entry that was essential for the *kohen* who needed to get inside in order to open those same doors from within.

### Sealed Door

The southern *pishpash* was never entered by anyone: *shebadarom lo nichnas adam bo mei'olam*. The verse from Yechezkel is cited: *vayomer Hashem elay hasha'ar hazeh sagur yihiyeh lo yipateiach v'ish lo yavo vo ki Hashem Elokei Yisrael ba vo* — this gate shall be sealed, it shall not be opened, and no man shall enter by it, for the Lord God of Israel has entered through it. The southern chamber was permanently sealed as a symbolic statement: this is where the divine presence enters, and it therefore requires no human key.

### Door Opening Method

The practical procedure for opening the *Heichal* each morning was as follows. The *kohen* would take the key (*natal es hamafteiach*), unlock the northern *pishpash* (*u'fasach es hapishpash*), and enter the chamber in the wall (*v'nichnas lahata*). From that chamber there was passage into the *Heichal* itself (*u'meihata l'Heichal*), and from inside the *Heichal* the *kohen* could then open the main doors.

### R. Yehudah's View

*Rabbi Yehudah* understands the procedure differently. He maintains that within the thickness of the wall there was a crawlway (*besoch ovyo shel kosel haya mehalech*) through which the *kohen* would make his way until he emerged standing between the two sets of doors — between the outer pair and the inner pair. From that position between the doors, he would open the outer doors from inside and the inner doors from outside: *u'fasach es hachitzonah mibifnim v'es hapnimis mibachutz*. According to *Rabbi Yehudah*, then, one stands in the cavity of the wall between the two door-sets and opens each set in the direction away from oneself — the outer ones by pushing inward and the inner ones by pushing outward into the *Heichal*.
