# Middot 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## SW Corner Blood Holes

### Face of Altar

The mishnah opens with a discussion of the southwest corner of the *mizbe'ach*. The ramp ascended from the south, and the west side of the *mizbe'ach* faced the *Heichal*. This made the southwest corner the *pnei hamizbe'ach* — the face of the altar — on two counts simultaneously: it faced the direction of approach from the ramp, and it stood *lifnei Hashem*, oriented toward the *Heichal*. This dual significance meant that a great many of the various *zrikos* of blood required to be performed at the southwest corner, as it achieved both of these qualities together.

It is worth recalling what was established at the end of the previous session: the *mizbe'ach* did not have a *yesod*, a base, running along most of the south side or most of the east side — only at the northeast and southwest corners did the base appear. This had a direct halachic consequence: *zrikat hadam* could not be performed at the southeast corner, since the rule is that the blood must be thrown *keneged hayesod*, opposite a base. Consequently, the two *zrikos* for standard offerings — each poured at a corner so as to spread across two walls, which together covered all four sides — were performed at the northeast and southwest corners, as those were the only diagonally opposite corners both situated over a base.

### Twin Drainage Holes

In this southwest corner, the mishnah states *hayu shnei nekavim k'min shnei chotamim dakim* — there were two holes in the base, like two thin nostrils. These served to drain the bloods that were poured on the western *yesod* and the southern *yesod*.

The distinction between the two holes corresponds to two different scenarios. When a *korban* required the *zrikat hadam* to be performed inside the *Heichal* — as was the case on *Yom Kippur* with the *parim hanisrafim*, the bulls brought when the *Beis Din* ruled incorrectly and the public followed them, and similarly the *korbanot* of *Yom Kippur* more broadly — the blood was applied on the interior. Upon exiting the *Heichal*, the *kohen* immediately encountered the western base, and that blood was therefore poured at the western portion of the southwest corner. The hole oriented more toward the west served this purpose.

For ordinary *zrikos* performed on the outer *mizbe'ach*, the *kohen* would stand at the corner and pour. Coming down from the *mizbe'ach*, the side most immediately before one is the south, and so the blood was poured at the southern portion of the southwest corner — corresponding to the second hole, oriented slightly less to the west and more toward the south. Both holes were aligned along a line rather than set at angles to one another, with one simply positioned more westward than the other. The mishnah states that these bloods, whether from the western *yesod* or the southern *yesod*, would flow down through these holes, *mit'arvin b'amah* — they would mix together in the channel — and from there, *yotzim l'nachal Kidron*, they would flow out to the Kidron Valley to the east.

### Underground System

The Gemara in Sukkah elaborates on the *shit* — the underground cavern system — into which wine, water, and other liquids poured atop the *mizbe'ach* would flow. The access to these caverns was located at the southwest corner, which makes structural sense given that this is precisely where the pouring took place. The *mefarshim* describe the *shit* as either a deep underground cavern or a hollow within the body of the *mizbe'ach* itself. It was periodically cleaned out — the congealed wine, and apparently blood that had gathered on its way through to the *amah*, would accumulate and require removal. Indeed, the present mishnah sounds as though blood too collected there, which is somewhat puzzling given that the mishnah had just described the blood flowing out through the holes to the *amah*. Some *mefarshim* suggest that blood passed through a section of this system en route to the *amah* and could congeal along the way, requiring that artery, as it were, to be periodically cleaned out.

### Manhole Access

The mishnah describes how access to the caverns was achieved: *lemata b'ritzpa b'ota keren makom haya sham amah al amah* — lower down on the floor, in that very corner, there was a space of one *amah* by one *amah*. Over this square opening lay a marble slab, *tavla shel shayish*, serving as a manhole cover. Like the rings used elsewhere in the *azarah*, it had a ring set into it — *tabaat hayta kevua bah* — by which it could be lifted. And *shebo yordim lashit*: through this opening they would descend into the underground caverns to clean them out.

One might note the geometric curiosity that this cover was square rather than round. It may be recalled that a classic engineering interview question asks why manhole covers are round: because a round cover cannot fall through a round opening regardless of its orientation, whereas a square cover, if tilted to the wrong angle, could drop in. The *mishna*'s cover here was square, which is its own curiosity, but the *halacha* did not require otherwise.

## The Ramp Structure

### Ramp Dimensions

The mishnah states that the *kevesh* — the ramp ascending to the *mizbe'ach* — was to its south, *hayu lidromo*. It was 32 *amot* long and 16 *amot* wide. Considering that the base of the *mizbe'ach* itself was 32 *amot* across, the ramp's width of 16 *amot* means that it occupied exactly half the base's width. Positioned symmetrically, there would be 8 *amot* on each side of the ramp beyond it at the base level, narrowing to 6 *amot* on each side by the time the structure reached the upper dimensions of roughly 28 *amot*.

### Ramp Angle

Since the ramp was 32 *amot* long along its base and ascended 10 *amot* in height — for the *mizbe'ach* was 10 *amot* tall — the angle of incline can be computed as the arctangent of 10 over 32, which works out to approximately 17.35 degrees. This is a gentle, practical slope, well-suited for constant use by *kohanim* carrying loads of wood, flesh, and coals up and down throughout the day.

### Invalid Offerings Box

The mishnah adds that there was a *revuva* — a hollowed-out section — on the western side of the ramp. *Shesham hayu notnim psulei chatat ha'of*: this is where they would place *psulei chatat ha'of*, the invalid bird *chatat* offerings. They would be kept there until they became *pasul belina* — disqualified by having remained overnight — and only then disposed of by burning.

It is not entirely clear why *psulei chatat ha'of* specifically warranted their own dedicated space. One possibility is that the mishnah here is referring to *chatat ha'of al hasafek* — a bird *chatat* brought under conditions of doubt. There is a well-known rule that one cannot bring a *chatat* when uncertain whether one is obligated, because bringing a voluntary *chatat* would be *chullin ba'azarah*. However, a special tradition permits a *chatat ha'of al hasafek*, particularly relevant in cases of uncertain miscarriage where a *yoledet* may or may not owe a *chatat*. If that is the referent here, then these offerings were numerous enough to require a dedicated storage location. They could neither be eaten nor discarded while still potentially valid, and so they were placed in this hollowed space in the ramp's western side until the night had passed and they had become *pasul belina*, at which point they were burned. The ramp itself was constructed of stone — a solid stone structure — and this hollow was cut into its western face.

## Altar Stones Rules

### Stone Quarrying

The mishnah turns to the materials from which both the ramp and the *mizbe'ach* itself were built. *Avnei hakevesh v'avnei hamizbe'ach mibik'at Beis Kerem* — they were brought from the valley of Beis Kerem. More precisely, *chofer lemata mehabetula* — one would dig below the virgin earth, sourcing stones from ground that had never previously been disturbed. *U'mevi misham avanim shleimos* — and from there one would bring whole, complete stones.

The obvious question is how the stones could be quarried from virgin earth using metal tools without violating the prohibition against iron contact. Rashi states explicitly that they dug with wooden implements — *chofrin b'kardumot etz*. How effective such tools would be against compacted virgin earth is admittedly unclear. Some suggest the metal tools were wrapped in cloth so as never to make direct contact with the stones themselves, and there is indeed a principle that contact through a *chatzitza* is not problematic. Others wonder whether, at minimum, the first cut to break the earth was made with metal, after which wood was used for the remainder.

### Iron Prohibition

The Torah contains two distinct requirements concerning the *mizbe'ach*: *avanim shleimos tivneh et mizbe'ach Hashem Elokecha* — that it must be built of whole stones — and *lo tivneh atehen gazit ki charbecha hunafta aleha vatachaleleha* — that hewn stone, upon which iron was wielded, is forbidden. One might have thought the second requirement is subsumed within the first: if iron is used to chip the stone, the stone is no longer whole. But the mishnah makes explicit that these are two independent requirements: *shehabarzel posel b'negi'ah* — iron invalidates merely by contact, even if the stone remains perfectly whole. And conversely, *hapgima b'chol davar* — a chip invalidates regardless of what caused it, even if no iron was involved.

This raises a broader question about whether the prohibition on iron extended beyond the *mizbe'ach* to the other stones of the *Beis Hamikdash*. There is indeed a *pasuk* in connection with Shlomo's construction stating that no iron implement was heard in the *Beis Hashem*, which sounds more global. This leads to the famous discussions about the *shamir* — a creature or substance that could cut stone without metal — and more straightforwardly, to the view that stones for the rest of the structure could be carved outside the *Har Habayis* and then brought in already finished, since the prohibition was only against using iron on the *Har Habayis* itself. That leniency applies to the non-*mizbe'ach* stones, where the requirement is an extension from the *pasuk*. But for the *mizbe'ach* itself, no such workaround is available: the prohibition is absolute — no iron whatsoever, no chips of any kind, complete *shleimos* — and the only coherent explanation of how a precisely dimensioned *mizbe'ach* could be constructed under these constraints is that it was built not from large carved blocks but from many small, untouched stones bound together with a form of lime plaster or cement, with the plaster providing the shape rather than any carving of the stone. This also explains the dimensions: rather than massive ashlar blocks like those of the Herodian structure elsewhere, the *mizbe'ach* was essentially a shaped mass of small stones held in a mortar matrix.

### Chipped Stone Rule

*Nifgema achat mehen* — if one of the stones became chipped — *hi pesulah v'chulan kesheirot*: that individual stone is disqualified, but the rest of the *mizbe'ach* remains valid. The chipped stone is removed and replaced, and there is no need to invalidate or rebuild the entire structure.

### Plastering Schedule

*U'malbinin otan pa'amayim bashana* — they would whiten, meaning replaster, the *mizbe'ach* twice per year. *Echad b'Pesach v'echad b'Chag*: once before Pesach and once before Sukkos, in anticipation of the great gatherings at each of these *regalim*. The entire *Heichal* — and some read this as referring to the entire *Beis Hamikdash* — was replastered once a year, *b'erev Pesach*, before Pesach, since that *regel* drew the largest turnout.

*Rebbi omer*: beyond the biannual replastering, on every *erev Shabbos* they would clean the *mizbe'ach* with a cloth — *b'mappa* — *mipnei hadamim*, because the altar accumulated blood throughout the week. Rebbi is not contradicting the earlier ruling about replastering; he is describing an additional, weekly practice of physical cleaning. Presumably water was used alongside the cloths, and lime, being a natural disinfectant, would have assisted in the process. Even with thorough wiping, some staining would remain, which is why the more comprehensive lime treatment was still applied twice yearly.

One might wonder about the cumulative thickness: if the *mizbe'ach* were replastered twice a year for the hundreds of years the *Beis Hamikdash* stood, would it not have grown substantially beyond its specified dimensions? It is likely that before each new application of plaster the old surface was smoothed or lightly scraped. Lime whitewash in particular is applied in very thin layers, so the accumulation over time may not have been dramatic.

### Plaster Application

*Lo haya sado b'chafis shel barzel* — they would not apply the plaster using an iron implement, *shema yigga v'yifsal*: lest the applicator accidentally make direct contact with the actual stone of the *mizbe'ach* and disqualify it. Although the wet plaster itself would serve as a *chatzitza* between the tool and the stone, one cannot always be sufficiently careful, and a direct touch would be a problem.

The mishnah concludes with a midrashic observation: *shehabarzel nivra l'katzer yamav shel adam* — iron was created to shorten human lives, being the material of weapons of war; *v'hamizbe'ach nivra l'haarich yamav shel adam* — and the *mizbe'ach* was created to extend human lives through the atonement it brings; *eino badin sheyanif ham'katzer al ham'aarich* — it is simply not fitting that the shortener should be waved over that which is there to lengthen. This serves not only as a homiletical elaboration but also as an explanation of why mere contact — even without chipping — is sufficient to disqualify: the very nature of iron is antithetical to the purpose of the *mizbe'ach*.

## Northern Slaughter Area

### Slaughter Rings

*Hata'ba'os hayu l'tzafona shel mizbe'ach* — the rings were to the north of the *mizbe'ach*. This placement was dictated by halacha: the *kodshei kodashim* must be slaughtered in the north, and so the entire apparatus for *shechita* was arranged there. The rings were arranged in *sheish sdarim shel arba arba* — six rows of four each — or according to another view, four rows of six, that is, four rows of six rings in a row; either way, the total was twenty-four arranged in a 6 by 4 grid. *She'aleihem shochtin es hakodashim* — upon these the *kodashim* were slaughtered.

According to most opinions, the rings were half-rings set into the ground, through which the neck of the animal was inserted and then secured with the other half, somewhat like a shackle. According to the Rambam, the rings held the animal's legs — which is what the Rambam identifies as the *akeida* performed with the sacrificial animals.

### Flaying Apparatus

*Beis hamitbachayim haya litzfono shel mizbe'ach* — the slaughterhouse proper was also to the north of the *mizbe'ach*, but was distinct from the area of the rings. After *shechita* at the rings, each *mishmar* would move further north to this dedicated space where the flaying, dismemberment, and disemboweling were performed.

This facility had *chet amudim nannasim* — eight short pillars — with cedar frames, *rivi'im shel erez*, set on top of them. Into these frames were fixed iron hooks, *ankleyot shel barzel*, anchored permanently in the wood.

### Hook Rows Setup

Each pillar had *gimmel sdarim* — three rows of hooks. The reason for the three rows is understood to correspond to the three main categories of sacrificial animals — cattle, goats, and sheep — each of which required a different hanging height in order to stretch the animal fully along the frame. The animal would be hung upside down by its feet, suspended from the hooks, which was the most practical position for flaying.

*Shebahem tolin umafshitin al shulchanot shel shayish* — upon these they would hang and flay, and the actual work of processing was done on marble tables positioned between the pillars. Marble was chosen because of its cold surface, which retarded spoilage. The procedure as reconstructed is that the animal was first arranged on the frame with its hooks, then transferred to the marble table where the *kohen* could flay and portion it. This was the layout described previously in the context of Tractate Tamid.

## Ulam-Altar Space

### Laver Position

Having completed the description of the *mizbe'ach* and its northern processing area, the mishnah moves inward toward the *Heichal*. The sequence of spaces traversed is: the *Ezrat Nashim*, then 11 *amot* of *Ezrat Yisrael*, then 11 *amot* of *Ezrat Kohanim* with the *duchan* for the *Levi'im* at its step, then the *mizbe'ach* itself, and now the space between the *mizbe'ach* and the *Ulam* — a zone of elevated *kedusha*.

*Hakiyor haya bein ha'ulam lamizbe'ach* — the *kiyor*, the great laver in which the *kohanim* would sanctify their hands and feet, stood in this space between the *Ulam* and the *mizbe'ach*. *Umashuch klapei hadarom* — it was not centered but positioned toward the south, aligned with the direction of the ramp.

### Space Dimensions

*Bein ulam lamizbe'ach chaf bet amot* — the total width of this space between the *Ulam* and the *mizbe'ach* was 22 *amot*, double the combined width of the *Ezrat Kohanim* and *Ezrat Yisrael* together. Working outward from the *Heichal*, one had 22 *amot* to the face of the *mizbe'ach*, then 32 *amot* of the *mizbe'ach*'s own footprint at the base, then another 22 *amot* beyond it.

## Steps to Ulam

### Step One

*Yod bet ma'alot hayu sham* — there were twelve steps ascending from the *azarah* up to the *Ulam*. Reading the mishnah as the Bartenura does: *rom ma'alah chatzi amah v'shilucha amah* — the first step was half an *amah* in height and one *amah* in tread depth.

### Steps Two-Four

Steps two and three each measured one *amah* in height and one *amah* in tread. After the third step came *rovad*, a platform: *rovad gimel amot*, a landing three *amot* deep. This platform constituted the fourth element in the sequence, so one step of half an *amah* height and one *amah* depth, two steps each of one *amah* by one *amah*, and then a three-*amah* platform — totaling six *amot* of horizontal extent in four steps.

### Steps Five-Twelve

The pattern continued: *amah amah v'rovad gimel*, repeating through steps five, six, and seven with another three-*amah* platform, adding five more *amot* to bring the running total to eleven *amot* after seven steps. Then *amah amah v'rovad gimel* again, and onward — *amah amah amah* continuing — until the final step, where the platform was four *amot* deep rather than three: *v'ha'elyonah amah amah v'rovad daled*. This yields a total horizontal length of nineteen *amot* for all twelve steps according to the Bartenura's reading. Rabbi Yehuda, as will be seen below, reads the final platform as five *amot*, bringing the total to twenty.

### Total Step Length

The full space between the *Ulam* and the *mizbe'ach* was only 22 *amot* wide, and the steps consumed nineteen *amot* of that on this reading. This left only three *amot* between the front edge of the steps and the *mizbe'ach* itself — a remarkably tight space. Other *mefarshim* read the mishnah such that the steps occupy twenty-one *amot*, leaving virtually no open space at all in front of them. The Rambam offers a reading that produces somewhat shorter steps. In any event, practically all of the 22-*amah* space was occupied by the ascending staircase, making passage directly in front of the steps extremely constricted.

### Platforms for Blessing

This architectural arrangement is illuminated by an important function of the steps: it was upon these graduated landings that the *kohanim* would stand to perform *birkat kohanim*. The broad platforms — three *amot*, three *amot*, four *amot* — provided standing room for rows of *kohanim* at staggered heights, something like stadium seating. Each row had sufficient depth to stand, and the height differential allowed each row to be seen and heard above those in front of it. This explains the otherwise puzzling generosity of the platform dimensions relative to the very narrow individual step risers and treads.

### Rabbi Yehudah Variant

*Rabbi Yehuda omer v'ha'elyonah amah amah v'rovad hei* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the topmost platform was five *amot* rather than four, making the total horizontal extent of the steps twenty *amot*. Other *mefarshim* arrive at twenty-one *amot*. On those readings, the space between the bottom of the steps and the *mizbe'ach* was so minimal that access to the front of the steps would have been only from the sides.

## Ulam Structure

### Ulam Opening

*Pitcho shel Ulam* — the opening of the *Ulam* was on a monumental scale quite different from the standard dimensions seen elsewhere in the *Beis Hamikdash*. All other openings in the complex were twenty *amot* high by ten *amot* wide. The *Ulam* opening, by contrast, was *arba'im v'rochvo esrim* — forty *amot* high by twenty *amot* wide. That is sixty feet in height and thirty feet in width — a genuinely imposing entrance. As will be noted, this opening had no door but rather a curtain, which helps explain the structural solution to the challenge of covering such an opening.

### Top Poles

*V'chamesh malteraot shel mila hayu al gabav* — atop the opening there were five poles of what is described as ashwood. These were arranged in a pyramidal progression: *hatachtona odefet al hapetach amah mizeh v'amah mizeh* — the lowest of the poles extended one *amah* beyond the opening on each side, making it twenty-two *amot* in span. *V'shelimala mimena odefet aleha amah mizeh v'amah mizeh* — each successive pole extended one *amah* further on each side than the one below it. Continuing: 22, 24, 26, 28, 30 — *nimtzet ha'elyona sheloshim amah*, the topmost pole reached thirty *amot* in span. This created a graceful pyramidal silhouette of five staggered ashwood poles above the frame of the entrance, widening as they rose.

### Brick Pattern

*V'nidbach shel avanim haya bein kol achat v'achat* — between each pair of poles there was a course of stonework, a row of bricks filling the space. This combined with the poles to create an intricate decorative pattern above the massive entrance. The poles may also have served a functional purpose in supporting the curtain that hung across the opening, though the curtain would more naturally be suspended within the frame rather than from above it.

It is worth noting that all the openings in the *Beis Hamikdash* were spanned by lintels rather than arches — with one exception previously noted in the mishnah, the *Tadi* gate — which is itself architecturally striking, particularly for an edifice associated with Herod's rebuilding, a period in which the arch was well understood.

### Support Beams

*V'kelonsaot shel avanim hayu kevuim mikotlo shel Heichal lekotlo shel Ulam* — there were supporting beams of stone — though some *mefarshim* read this as cedar, *erez*, which is more structurally plausible for a beam — running from the wall of the *Heichal* to the wall of the *Ulam*, *keday shelo yiva'at*: so that the structure would not buckle. The walls were exerting lateral forces against one another, and these transverse beams served to stabilize the structure against that outward thrust.

### Golden Chains

*V'sharsherot shel zahav hayu kevuim b'tikrat ha'ulam* — golden chains were fixed to the ceiling of the *Ulam*, sixty feet above the floor, *shebahen pirchei kehunah olim v'ro'im et ha'atarot* — upon which the young *kohanim* would climb in order to see the crowns. The verse cited is *v'ha'atarot tihye l'Chelem ul'Tuviya ul'Yeda'aya ul'Chen ben Tzefanya l'zikaron b'Heichal Hashem* — the crowns were to remain as a memorial in the *Heichal*. Precisely what these crowns were and how climbing the chains enabled viewing them is not entirely clear from the text; the Rambam addresses this in chapter 4, halacha 8. The chains were positioned within the *Ulam* — the antechamber — not within the *Heichal* proper where the *shulchan* and *menorah* stood. A note on materials: gold is a soft and weak metal, and wherever one reads of golden objects of structural significance in the *Beis Hamikdash* it is reasonable to assume that what is meant is a gold alloy of some kind. The *shulchan*, uniquely, is specified as *zahav tahor*, pure gold.

### Golden Vine

*V'gefen shel zahav hayta omedet al pitcho shel Heichal* — there was also a golden vine positioned over and around the opening of the *Heichal*. *U'mudla al gabei kelonsot* — it was suspended upon a lattice framework. *Kol mi she'hitnadev aleh o gargir o eshkol mevi v'toleh bah* — whoever wished to donate would bring a golden leaf, a golden grape, or a golden cluster and hang it upon the vine. The impression is that donors could personally go in to suspend their gift, though given the space involved, this would have meant entry into the *Ulam* — not the *Heichal* — which would at minimum require being fit for the *avoda*, as one cannot enter even the *Ulam* if one is a *ba'al mum* or *paru'a rosh*. *Amar Rabbi Eleazar beRabbi Tzadok*: once the vine had accumulated so much gold that three hundred *kohanim* were needed to clear it — though that number is understood as an exaggeration conveying the vine's extraordinary weight of accumulated donations.

## Heichal Opening

### Heichal Dimensions

The mishnah now describes the opening of the *Heichal* itself — the inner sanctum — at the top of folio 36a. *Govho esrim amah v'rochvo eser amah*: it was twenty *amot* high and ten *amot* wide, the standard dimensions repeated throughout the complex, in contrast to the extraordinary proportions of the *Ulam* opening just described.

### Four-Door System

This opening was furnished with four doors in total — *shtayim bifnim v'shtayim bachutz*, two on the inside and two on the outside. Each set of two doors served as a pair, each door being five *amot* wide to fill the ten-*amah* opening.

### Wall Thickness

The wall of the *Heichal* was six *amot* thick — roughly ten feet of solid masonry — and the door arrangement was organized within that thickness. The outer doors, when opened by someone approaching from outside, swung inward into the wall's thickness. Since each door was five *amot* wide and the thickness was six *amot*, the back of each open outer door nearly filled the interior space of the opening. On the other side, the inner doors opened outward into the *Heichal* itself, their backs lying flat against the interior wall of the *Heichal* when open.

The procedure for opening was not simply to push the doors from outside. Rather — as will be described in the next section — a *kohen* entered through a side passage, came out inside the *Heichal*, and opened the doors from within.

### Gold Plating

*Shekol habayis tuach bezahav* — the entire interior of the *Heichal* was covered with gold plating — *chutz me'achar hadlatos*, with the single exception of the wall space behind the open doors. The reasoning is practical: when the doors stood open, they covered those sections of wall entirely, and the doors themselves were gold. No one would ever see those wall sections once the doors were open, and the only *kohen* who saw the bare wall was the one entering first to open the doors. The gold plating was therefore applied everywhere the eye could reach except behind the doors.

### Bifold Doors

*Rabbi Yehuda omer betoch hapetach hayu omados* — Rabbi Yehuda takes a different view: both sets of doors opened into the interior of the wall thickness, not one inward and one outward. The difficulty is geometric: each door was five *amot* wide and the wall was only six *amot* thick. If both the outer and inner doors opened to the inside, they would collide with each other. Rabbi Yehuda's answer is *kemin istarmita hayu* — they were constructed as what we would call bifold doors. Each door, though five *amot* wide in total, was itself hinged in the middle into two panels. When opened, each door folded upon itself, occupying only two and a half *amot* of depth within the wall thickness. With two and a half on each side and a half *amah* on each side for the *mezuza* — the doorpost structure itself — the full six *amot* of wall thickness was accounted for: half an *amah*, two and a half *amot*, two and a half *amot*, half an *amah*. The mishnah cites the verse *ushtayim dlatos la'heichal vela'kodesh, shtayim musabos dlatos* — two hinged doors for each opening — as supporting this reading.

## Access Passages

### Pishpeshin Chambers

*Ushne pishpishin hayu lo lasha'ar hagadol* — the great gate had two *pishpishin*, side chambers or passages flanking it. Rather than being small openings cut within the door panels themselves, the better understanding — and what the illustrations suggest — is that these were narrow chambers or crawlways cut into the thickness of the wall on each side of the doorway, allowing access to the interior without going through the main doors. *Echad batzafon v'echad badarom* — one to the north and one to the south. *Shebadarom lo nichnas adam bo me'olam* — the southern one was never entered by anyone, ever.

### Southern Gate

*V'alav hu mforash al yedei Yechezkel* — and regarding the southern passage, it is explained through Ezekiel, as it is said: *Vayomer Hashem elai hasha'ar hazeh sagur yihiyeh lo yipateiach v'ish lo yavo vo ki Hashem Elokei Yisrael ba vo* — the Lord said to me: this gate shall be sealed, it shall not be opened, and no man shall enter through it, for the Lord, the God of Israel, has entered through it. The southern passage was the divine passage — permanently sealed as a sign that God passed through it and it is therefore reserved. What exactly this theological claim communicates is not entirely clear; perhaps the intent is that the sealed door stands as a visible acknowledgment that this entry belongs to the divine presence rather than to human use.

### Opening Procedure

The procedure for opening the *Heichal* began with the *kohen* taking the key — the key stored in the *Beis HaMoked*, as described in Tractate Tamid. *U'fasach es hapishpish* — he would unlock and open the northern side chamber. *V'nichnas lahata* — and he would enter through it into the chamber or crawlway within the wall. *U'meihata l'Heichal* — and from there there was an exit into the *Heichal* itself. Once inside the *Heichal*, he could now open the main doors from within — the inner doors pushing outward into the *Heichal*, the outer doors pulling inward into the wall thickness.

### Alternate Crawlway

*Rebbi Yehuda omer besoch ovyo shel kosel haya mehalech* — Rabbi Yehuda, consistent with his view about the bifold doors, describes a different route. Rather than exiting from the side chamber directly into the *Heichal*, he says one would walk through a crawlway running within the thickness of the wall itself, *ad shenimtza omeid bein shnei hasha'arim* — until one found oneself standing between the two sets of doors, that is, within the wall thickness between the outer and inner door frames. *U'fasach es hachitzonah mibifnim v'es hapnimis mibachutz* — from that position between the doors, one would open the outer doors from their inner side and the inner doors from their outer side. On the standard reading, one would first open the inner doors from inside the *Heichal* and then walk back through to open the outer ones; on Rabbi Yehuda's reading, the *kohen* stands between the two sets and opens each set toward him in turn.
