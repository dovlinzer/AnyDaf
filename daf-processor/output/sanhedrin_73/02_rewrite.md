# Sanhedrin 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Haba BeMachteret & Rodef

### Haba BeMachteret Review

Today's daf is 73, and we begin by picking up at the bottom of 72b, which flows naturally into the new sugya. The preceding material concerned the *haba bemachteret* — the burglar who breaks through a tunnel — and established that the homeowner may act in self-defense. The *gemara* there recognized that while self-defense is what permits the killing, there are aspects of the discussion that suggest something further: the act of killing the intruder may be conceived of not merely as self-defense but as a type of *beit din din*, a quasi-judicial proceeding.

### Hatra'ah & Din Model

This impression was reinforced by the *gemara*'s formulation *machtarto zo hi hatra'ato* — his tunnel-entry is his *hatra'ah*, his warning. One reading of this phrase, which Rashi adopts, is simply that the act of breaking in through the tunnel demonstrates the intruder's readiness to use lethal force, and that demonstrated readiness is what permits the homeowner to respond. But the very use of the word *hatra'ah* evokes the formal warning required in capital cases adjudicated by *beit din*, suggesting that the framework here mimics a court procedure. Rashi goes so far as to describe a scenario where warning is given in front of witnesses and the intruder acknowledges it, which sounds very much like the classical *din* model rather than pure self-defense.

### Katan Rodef

With that tension in the background, the *gemara* now introduces a ruling of Rav Huna: *katan harodef nitan lehatzilo benafsho* — if a minor is pursuing someone with intent to kill, the potential victim may be saved even at the cost of the minor's life. This ruling functions as a precise counterpoint to the *din* model. A minor cannot be brought before a court, cannot be held legally liable for his actions, and yet Rav Huna rules that lethal intervention is permitted. The implication is clear: this is not about judging the pursuer. It is entirely about saving the person being pursued. The *gemara* makes this explicit — *lo shena gadol lo shena katan*, it makes no difference whether the *rodef* is an adult or a minor, because the operative principle is the protection of the *nirdaf*, the victim.

## Din vs Victim Model

### Fetus & Mother Case

Rav Chisda challenges Rav Huna with a well-known *mishnah*: if a fetus is endangering the mother's life during labor, one may dismember the fetus in the womb to save the mother, since at that stage the fetus has not yet attained the full status of a life. However, once *yatza rosho* — once the head has emerged — one may no longer touch the infant, because *she'ein dochin nefesh mipnei nefesh*, one life is not pushed aside for another. The difficulty is apparent: if the *rodef* principle operates purely to protect the *nirdaf* without any requirement of blameworthiness, why can we not treat the emerging infant as a *rodef* threatening the mother and intervene accordingly?

### Mishmaya Ka Radfu

The *gemara* responds: *shani hasam mishmaya ka radfu* — there it is different, because she is being pursued by heaven. This phrase requires unpacking. The earlier analysis suggested that the *rodef* framework does not require legal responsibility — a ten-year-old who knows what he is doing but is not yet *bar onesh* may still be stopped. But one might argue that there must at least be some degree of blameworthiness or fault, even if not formal legal culpability, in order to label someone a *rodef* and permit lethal intervention against him. If that is the standard, then perhaps below a certain age no one could ever be considered a *rodef*.

Alternatively, one might say that fault is entirely irrelevant — what matters is only the physical reality that one party is endangering another, and if we can identify pursuer and pursued, we protect the pursued. Even an infant falling from a roof and about to destroy a raft full of people would, on this reading, qualify as a *rodef*, innocent though it may be.

But the case of difficult childbirth resists both frameworks. The Rambam characterizes it as a completely natural event. Childbirth encounters complications; it always has. There is no identifiable pursuer here in the relevant sense. *Mishmaya ka radfu* means, on this reading, that there is no *rodef* — the infant is not pursuing anyone in any meaningful sense, either legally or even physically in the way that something out of the ordinary and threatening qualifies. The situation is simply the natural process of birth, and one cannot apply the *rodef* category to it.

This connects to cases discussed in other contexts: the crying baby in a hiding place whose silence might require suppression to protect others is framed as endangering lives, and that framing is what makes the concept of *rodef* applicable. What the *gemara* is doing here is carefully delimiting when the *rodef* label can and cannot be applied.

### Rodef Warning Case

The *gemara* then considers whether a support can be found for Rav Huna's position. The case presented describes a *rodef* who is running after another person to kill him, and a bystander who says to the *rodef*: "Look, this man is a *Yisrael*, a *ben brit*, and the Torah has said *shopech dam ha'adam ba'adam damo yishapech* — one who sheds blood, his blood shall be shed." This looks very much like a formal *hatra'ah* — the pursuer is being warned of the specific prohibition and its consequence. The *gemara* then asks: does this warning help or hinder the case for Rav Huna? At first glance it seems to import a *din* model into the *rodef* scenario, which would work against applying the rule to a *katan*.

### Rashi & Katan

Rashi explains the *gemara*'s reasoning differently from what one might initially suppose. The support for Rav Huna lies in the fact that nowhere in the description of this warning does the pursuer respond with *al menat ken ani oseh* — "I am doing this accepting full knowledge and responsibility." *Kabbalat hatra'ah* — the formal acceptance of the warning — is absent. Rashi reads this absence as evidence that one does not need to apply the full *din* model: there is no requirement for the *rodef* to formally accept the warning in order to permit intervention. The *gemara* responds that this proves nothing about Rav Huna's position, because the case follows the view of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that a *chaver* — someone demonstrably knowledgeable — does not require formal warning at all. For such a person, it suffices that he has been informed; he need not explicitly confirm *yode'a ani ve'af al pi ken*. So the absence of *kabbalat hatra'ah* in the case reflects that leniency, not a wholesale departure from the *din* model.

Yet this response is puzzling. If the *rodef* framework is truly not a *din* model but purely about protecting the *nirdaf*, why is the warning being issued at all? The time spent delivering a formal warning could be used to intervene and save the potential victim. The very appearance of the *hatra'ah* in the scenario implies that the *din* model has not been entirely displaced. The contrast between the two models, with the case of the *katan* as the critical *nafka mina*, seems to be what the *gemara* is genuinely holding in tension — without fully resolving it.

### River Case

A further case is introduced: a *rodef* who is on the opposite bank of the river, where intervention to save the *nirdaf* is physically impossible. Here, the warning given to the pursuer serves only one possible purpose — to enable a subsequent prosecution before *beit din*. The *gemara* states explicitly: *bei dina ba'ei hatra'ah*, for *beit din* proceedings you need *hatra'ah*. This case, then, does not represent the *rodef* scenario in its pure form; it is a *beit din* scenario using *rodef* language. It does not undermine Rav Huna.

### Machteret Hatra'ah

Rav Huna's final position is stated clearly: he holds like the *tanna* of the *machteret* case, who said *machterto zo hi hatra'ato*. But crucially, Rav Huna reads that phrase not as saying "the tunnel counts as the required *hatra'ah*" — which would still presuppose that *hatra'ah* is in principle necessary — but rather as saying: *almah mishum d'machzikinan lei rodef lo ba'ei hatra'ah* — once we establish that the man is a *rodef*, threatening another person's life, there is no requirement of *hatra'ah* at all. The phrase does not introduce a surrogate for the *din* requirement; it abolishes the requirement in this context entirely. It is about protecting the victim, and that principle applies regardless of whether the *rodef* is an adult or a *katan*, regardless of blameworthiness, and regardless of formal warning. What matters is only whether one can identify a pursuer and a pursued — and if one can, the pursued is protected even at the cost of the pursuer's life.

This *sugya* thus distills two models with precision. The *din* model would require that the act in question be treated like a capital case, with formal warning and acceptance. The victim-protection model asks only whether someone's life is imminently endangered by an identifiable pursuer. The *nafka mina* — a *katan rodef* — perfectly illustrates the difference, and Rav Huna's ruling comes down squarely for the victim-protection model.

## Mishna: Who to Save

### Mishna Introduction

The *gemara* now transitions to the *mishnah* on 73a, which lists the cases in which one may save potential victims at the cost of the pursuer's life: *harodef achar chaveiro lehargo* — one pursuing another to kill him; *ve'achar hazachar* — one pursuing a male for sexual assault; and *ve'achar hana'arah hame'orasah* — one pursuing a betrothed maiden. The *mishnah* will be extended by the *gemara* to cover additional cases.

### Rape & Severity

An important observation emerges here: rape alone is not sufficient to authorize lethal intervention. The *gemara*'s treatment of rape is notable. While the *Torah*'s comparison of the violated *na'arah me'orasah* to a murder victim (*ki ka'asher yakum ish al re'ehu uretzacho nefesh ken hadavar hazeh*) could be read as equating rape with murder in severity, and the Chazon Ish does develop something along those lines, the *gemara* does not treat rape as independently warranting lethal force. Rather, rape is treated in a framework closer to that of physical assault — a serious crime with penalties, but not automatically one that permits taking the pursuer's life.

What is required, the *gemara* makes clear, is a combination: the victim must be genuinely being pursued against her will, *and* the act being committed must be one that carries a penalty of death or *karet*. So the case of the *na'arah me'orasah* qualifies, as does any case of *arayot* within the category of *chayvei mitot beit din* or *chayvei karet* — a married woman, a sister, a brother's wife. But rape of an unmarried woman, which does not carry that level of sanction, does not suffice to authorize lethal force. One may still intervene with non-lethal force to protect such a victim — there is a discussion in Bava Kamma that addresses the use of physical force to stop someone who initiates assault — but lethal force requires both elements.

This combination is worth pausing over. It is tempting to say that after establishing that the *rodef* framework is purely about victim protection, we have completely abandoned the *din* model. But the requirement that the act carry a capital or *karet* sanction introduces something that looks at least partially like a *din* consideration. Perhaps the severity of the offense functions not as a *din* criterion but simply as a threshold — the graver the crime, the greater the license to use extreme measures to prevent it. That reading would allow one to hold that the criterion is still about protecting the victim rather than adjudicating the perpetrator, since it is the magnitude of the harm to the victim that determines how far intervention may go. Either way, it is clear that when we are not dealing with murder, something beyond bare victim-status is required.

### Excluded Cases

The *mishnah* then states: *aval rodef achar behemah* — but one who is pursuing an animal for purposes of bestiality — and *umechallel es haShabbos*, and *ve'oveid avodah zarah*, these are cases where *ein matzilin oso b'nafsho* — one does not intervene with lethal force. The pattern is now explicit: terrible sin without a human victim does not qualify. Great sin plus human victim does. If there is a victim without a correspondingly weighty sin, equally no. The *mishnah* is beautifully clear on this structure.

### Matzilin Oso

The phrase *matzilin osan b'nafsho* — literally "they save him with his soul" — presents an interesting grammatical problem. In the case of a *mekallel Shabbos* or *oved avodah zarah*, where there is no victim, who exactly is being saved? The phrase seems to be borrowed from the victim-rescue context, but it sits oddly in a context where no victim exists. Rashi resolves this by reading *matzilin osan min ha'aveirah b'nafshan* — they save the would-be perpetrator *from sinning*, with his own life as the price. On this reading, the intervention is framed as rescuing the sinner from the gravity of his own sin — better that he should die than commit such a transgression. But this reading creates its own difficulty: if saving someone from sin justifies taking his life, why should it not apply equally to *mechallel Shabbos* or *oved avodah zarah* even in the *mishnah*'s excluded cases? Tosafot notes this tension, observing that in the cases clearly involving a victim, the grammar of *matzilin* plainly refers to saving the victim rather than the perpetrator. The very ambiguity of the phrase encapsulates the deeper tension in the *mishnah* between the offense-oriented and victim-oriented frameworks.

## Kal VaChomer Source

### Lo Ta'amod Source

The *gemara* now asks: from where is the *rodef achar chaveiro lehargo* derived — that one may save the potential murder victim even at the cost of the pursuer's life? The *gemara* initially proposes *lo ta'amod al dam rei'echa*. But this verse, as the *gemara* immediately notes, is needed for something else entirely: *minayin l'ro'eh et chaveiro shehu tovei'a banahar o chayah gorareto o listim ba'in alav shehu chayav lehatzilo* — how do we know that if one sees his companion drowning, or being dragged by an animal, or attacked by bandits, he is obligated to rescue him? The verse *lo ta'amod al dam rei'echa* teaches that basic obligation, including the requirement to spend money, hire people, and go to significant lengths — not merely the minimal personal effort implied by the *mitzvah* of returning lost objects (*vehasheivoto lo*, which is read by the *gemara* as *aveidat gufo*, restoring a person's own life to him). So *lo ta'amod* is the source for the obligation to save life in general; it cannot simultaneously be the source for the permission to use lethal force in the *rodef* scenario.

### Kal VaChomer

The *gemara* therefore derives the lethal-intervention rule from *kal vachomer*: by the *na'arah me'orasah* the *Torah* writes *ein moshia lah* — there was no one to save her — which implies that had someone been available, he would have saved her by whatever means necessary. Since she was being harmed but not killed, this teaches that one may intervene with lethal force even to prevent a non-lethal serious offense against a victim. If so, *rodef achar chaveiro lehargo al achat kama vekhamah* — how much more so is lethal intervention permitted when the very life of the *nirdaf* is at stake.

### Makish Analysis

The *gemara* immediately objects: *ve'chi onshin min hadin* — one cannot impose punishment on the basis of a *kal vachomer*. Again, the evocation of the *din* model is striking — the language of punishment infiltrates a discussion that, as we have argued, is about protecting the victim. The *gemara* responds by shifting to a *gezerah shavah*: the verse *ki ka'asher yakum ish al re'ehu uretzacho nefesh ken hadavar hazeh* explicitly compares the case of rape to murder. The *gemara* asks: what does murder teach us about rape? We already know the woman is blameless. And it answers: *harei zeh ba lilamed v'nimtza lamed* — it comes to teach but ends up learning. Murder is brought to illuminate rape, but it is the case of the *na'arah me'orasah* that winds up being the teacher. From *na'arah me'orasah* — where *ein moshia lah* implies lethal intervention would have been permitted — we derive that by murder (*rotzeach*) equally, *nitan lehatzilo benafsho*, one may save the potential victim with the life of the pursuer. And *na'arah me'orasah* itself is derived from *devet Rabbi Yishmael*'s reading of *ve'ein moshia lah*: if there *were* someone who could save her, he would save her by *chol davar sheyachol lehoshiya* — with any and every means available.

### Expanded Cases

The *gemara* then cites the *braita*: *echad harodef achar chaveiro lehorgo ve'achar hazachar ve'achar hana'arah me'orasah ve'achar chayvei mitot beit din ve'achar chayvei keritot* — any case of *arayot* of sufficient severity falls within the rule. Both capital offenses and those incurring *karet* are included.

## Sexual Offenses & Pegam

### Lesser Violations

The *braita* continues: *anesa l'kohen gadol grusha vechalutza l'kohen hedyot* — if the offense is a lesser prohibition, such as a *kohen* raping a *grushah* or a *kohen gadol* raping an *almana*, then *ein matzilin otah b'nafsho* — one does not intervene with lethal force. The distinction is not from the perspective of the victim's suffering, but from the perspective of the weight of the *aveirah* from the *Torah*'s standpoint.

### Nistafcha Status

The *braita* also states: *nistafcha ba'aveirah ein matzilin otah b'nafsho* — if the act has already been committed, one can no longer intervene. This is not a matter of coming too late to prevent the specific act, though that is an intuitive reading. The *gemara* will make clear that the principle runs deeper.

### Pegam Explained

The concept of *pegam* — defilement or spoliation — is central to understanding this entire section. The *gemara* frames the victim's status through a particular social and halakhic lens: every woman belongs, in some sense, to a man — either her current husband or her future husband. When a man rapes a woman, he is *pogeim* her — he spoils her in relation to that man. If she is married and has had intimate relations with her husband, she has not been *pogeim*; but when another man violates her, that violation constitutes the *pegam*. A single woman who has not yet been married stands in relation to her future husband; any man who violates her is *pogeim* her with respect to him.

This is why the concept of *nistafcha* operates the way it does. If a woman has already been violated by one man — even through no fault of her own — then when a second man pursues her, he is not *pogeim* her in the same sense. She has already been *nistafcha*, and therefore the lethal intervention designed to prevent that *pegam* no longer applies. The conceptual framework is uncomfortable from a contemporary perspective, but it is the *gemara*'s own framing, and the subsequent analysis only makes it more explicit.

### Na'arah Basadeh

The case of *na'arah basadeh* addresses a scenario where the woman could be saved without lethal force. If non-lethal intervention is available and sufficient, one may not take the pursuer's life. The permission to use lethal force exists only where there is no lesser means of rescue.

## Pesukim Analysis

### Four Terms Needed

The *gemara* now asks why all four textual terms — *na'ar*, *na'arah*, *chet*, and *mavet* — are required. Could not the derivation have been accomplished with fewer? The answer is that each term excludes a different potential error in scope.

If only *na'ar* (without the *heh*) had been written, I might have thought the rule applies only to a male victim being raped, *mishum delav orchei* — because for a man to be penetrated is not the natural order and therefore that scenario demands the most severe protective response. I would not have derived the same for a female victim.

If only *na'arah* had been written, I might have thought the rule applies only where there is *pegam* — the spoiling of a woman — and therefore not to a male victim, *dela ka pagim lei*. The *pegam* concept is gender-specific in this framework; a man being raped, however traumatic, does not carry the same dimension of *pogeim* him.

If only those two terms were given — *na'ar* and *na'arah* — I might have thought the rule is limited to those particular cases of unusual assault (*lav orchei*) or *pegam*, and would not extend to other *arayot* involving, say, a married woman where the act is not rare and the *pegam* is less acute.

So *chet* is needed to include other cases of weighty sexual sin beyond *na'arah me'orasah*. And *mavet* is needed to make clear that even the most severe category — *chayvei mitot beit din* — is included; otherwise I might have stopped at *karet*. And *chet* is also needed to prevent limiting the rule only to capital offenses — without it, I would read *mavet* exclusively and exclude *karet*. Each term does necessary work.

### Exclude Behemah

Given that *chet* and *mavet* together already cover the scope of weighty sexual offenses, what function do *na'ar* and *na'arah* serve? The *gemara* answers: one of them excludes the case of *behemah* — an animal victim. Without these terms, one might have thought that sexual assault on an animal also triggers lethal intervention, since the act of bestiality carries severe sanctions. The term *na'ar*/*na'arah* signals that the framework requires a human victim.

### Rebbi Shimon Position

Rebbi Shimon ben Yochai holds a distinct position: *oved avodah zarah nitan lehatzilo benafsho* — one who is about to worship idols may be stopped with lethal force, even though there is no human victim. For Rebbi Shimon ben Yochai, the weightiest of sins — *avodah zarah* — is sufficient on its own to authorize the use of lethal force, without requiring a victim. This represents the offense-oriented model in its fullest articulation. Given this view, what does he need *na'ar* and *na'arah* for? *Chad le-mi'utei behemah* — one still excludes the case of an animal victim from the sexual-offense track. And *chad le-mi'utei Shabbos* — one would have thought, given that he permits lethal force to stop *avodah zarah*, that the same should apply to *chillul Shabbos*, which the *Torah* equates in various ways to *avodah zarah*. The verse therefore teaches that *Shabbos* is excluded on the sexual-offense track even for Rebbi Shimon ben Yochai.

### Rebbi Elazar Position

Rebbi Elazar bar Rebbi Shimon goes a step further than his father. He holds *mechallel Shabbos nitan lehatzilo benafsho* — one desecrating the Sabbath may also be stopped with lethal force, since *Shabbos* is explicitly compared to *avodah zarah* and the two are treated as equivalent in severity. On this view, what remains for *na'ar* and *na'arah* to exclude? *Chad le-mi'utei behemah* — even the most severe-sin approach still requires either a human victim or one of the two uniquely grave offenses; bestiality therefore falls outside the authorization for lethal force. The other term simply becomes redundant since both *Shabbos* and *avodah zarah* are already included, and *na'ar*/*na'arah* need not work as hard.

The *gemara* thus clarifies that the consensus position of our *mishnah* is the hybrid: weighty sin plus human victim. Some *tanna'im* take a position that the most serious offenses — *avodah zarah* and by extension *Shabbos* — are sufficient without a victim. One might note that even those cases admit of a victim-oriented reading in a broader sense: *avodah zarah* committed within a community endangers the community's religious identity; *chillul Shabbos* poses a parallel threat. The community itself can be understood as the victim. But that framing extends beyond what the *gemara* explicitly states.

## Rebbi Yehuda Dispute

### Woman's Request

The *mishnah* includes a dispute involving Rebbi Yehuda: *af omeret hanichu lo shelo yehargenah* — even if the woman says "leave him alone, otherwise he will kill me," Rebbi Yehuda holds one may not kill the pursuer. This is initially puzzling: she is still clearly a victim of rape, she is not consenting, and her very statement reveals that she fears for her life. Why should her plea to stand down affect the halakha?

### Rabanan View

The *gemara* explains the dispute. Rava frames it as follows: *bemakpedet al pigmah* — she is still clearly concerned about her *pegam*; her statement that she does not want him killed is driven by fear for her life, not by any willingness to be violated. She is still very much a victim, objecting to the rape. The *Rabanan* hold: *apigma kapid Rachmana* — the *Torah*'s concern is protecting her from *pegam*, and since she is genuinely objecting to it — *makpedet al pigmah* — she remains a victim in the halakhic sense, and one may intervene. Her expression of fear for her life does not transform her into a willing participant.

### Rebbi Yehuda Theory

Rebbi Yehuda, however, offers a fundamentally different account of why the *Torah* authorized lethal intervention in the first place. He holds: *hai d'ka'amar Rachmana katlei mishum d'masra nafshah l'ktala* — the reason the *Torah* says to kill the pursuer is not primarily to protect the woman from *pegam*, but because there exist circumstances in which a woman would actually give up her life rather than be violated. There are women — Rashi describes them as *tznuot*, women of particular modesty and self-restraint — who would choose death over rape. In such cases, her life is genuinely at risk, and it is that risk to her life that authorizes lethal intervention. The *rodef* here is threatening not just her *pegam* but her actual existence.

### Application

Given this framework, the application follows directly. When the woman says *hanichu lo shelo yehargenah* — "leave him alone, he will kill me otherwise" — she is communicating clearly that she is not among those who would sacrifice her life to avoid violation. She is declaring that she will not *masar nafshah l'ktala*. For Rebbi Yehuda, that declaration removes the very rationale for lethal intervention: if her life is not at risk — because she herself confirms she will not choose death — then there is nothing that requires us to kill the pursuer. The intervention was always about the life risk, and she has eliminated that risk by her own declaration.

### Application

This is a striking position. Rebbi Yehuda is not saying the rape itself is unimportant. He is saying that the specific legal authorization for lethal intervention was always predicated on the possibility of mortal risk, and once that predicate is removed by the woman's own statement, the authorization lapses. The question of whether a woman is *obligated* in *yehareg ve'al ya'avor* — to die rather than transgress — connects naturally to this analysis and will be addressed in tomorrow's material.

### Pegam Severity

Rav Pappa raises a question to Abaye: if the *Rabanan* hold that the entire framework is about protecting a woman from *pegam*, why does the *mishnah* not authorize lethal intervention in the case of an *almana* being raped by a *kohen gadol*? She too is being *pegem*! The answer given is: *pgima raba kapid Rachmana, pgima zuta lo kapid Rachmana* — the *Torah* is concerned only with a major *pegam*, not a minor one. And the severity of the *pegam* tracks the severity of the *aveirah*: because intercourse of a *kohen gadol* with an *almana* is a less weighty prohibition than *chayvei mitah* or *chayvei karet*, the resulting *pegam* is correspondingly lesser, and it does not cross the threshold that triggers the authorization of lethal force. This is a significant observation: the size of the *pegam* is not an independent measure of harm to the woman, but rather a function of the gravity of the transgression. The more severe the *aveirah*, the more severe the *pegam*, and the more justified the lethal intervention.

This brings us full circle to the tension running through the entire daf. Is the framework about the *din* — the severity of the offense as adjudicated by the *Torah*? Is it purely about protecting the victim? Is it about *pegam* as a kind of victim-harm that tracks the offense? The *gemara* does not resolve these tensions into a single formula. What emerges instead is a sophisticated interplay: the authorization for lethal intervention requires both a genuine human victim being pursued against her will and an offense of sufficient gravity, where the gravity of the offense is itself bound up with the nature and severity of the harm done to the victim.
