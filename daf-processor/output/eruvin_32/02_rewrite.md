# Eruvin 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hazakah/Biblical vs Rabb…

### Shaliach Principle Intro

The Gemara on 31b concludes with a statement of Rav Yehiel affirming the principle of *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* — the presumption that an agent performs the task he has been designated to carry out. This principle arose in the context of the Mishnah's ruling that an *eruv* may be sent via a *cheresh shoteh ve-katan*, individuals who cannot themselves place an *eruv* but who may serve as couriers to someone who will. Two questions had presented themselves. First, how do we know that a *cheresh shoteh ve-katan* actually delivered the *eruv*? There the answer was that one cannot assume delivery — one must observe it, given the limitations of these individuals. Second, even once a competent adult receives the *eruv*, how do we know he followed through and placed it? To this latter question, the Gemara answered: *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* — one may presume that a person who has agreed to perform a task on another's behalf will carry it out.

### Rav Nachman: Rabbinic On…

At the top of 32a, the Gemara moves from the interpretive gloss on the Mishnah into a direct statement of *halachah*. Rav Nachman draws a fundamental distinction: *beshel Torah* — when the matter at hand is biblical in nature — *ein hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto*. One cannot rely on this presumption, since people may be negligent or circumstances may intervene even with the best of intentions. The case of *eruv techumin*, however, is rabbinic in character — it permits one to walk beyond the *techum* on Shabbat — and for *beshel sofrim*, for rabbinic matters, *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* does apply. One may presume that the *shaliach* took care of what he was asked to do.

### Rav Sheshet: Universal

Rav Sheshet takes a more expansive position: *echad zeh ve-echad zeh* — whether the matter is biblical or rabbinic — *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto*. The presumption holds in either domain. This does not mean the task is accomplished with perfect certainty every time, but for practical *halachic* purposes, one may rely on the presumption that an agent performs his *shelichus*.

## Omer/Chadash Case

### Omer Permissibility

Rav Sheshet now seeks to establish a proof that the presumption applies even to biblical matters. He cites a Mishnah directly relevant to this season of the year: *mishekarev ha-omer* — once the *omer* offering was brought on the second day of Pesach, the new grain (*chadash*) was immediately permitted. The permission took effect at the precise moment of the offering. But what of those who lived at a distance from the *Beit HaMikdash* and did not know exactly when the *omer* had been brought? The Mishnah rules: *mutarim mechatzot hayom va-eilach* — they may eat the new grain from midday onward, presuming the *omer* had been brought by noon.

The Gemara then draws the necessary inference: *chadash* is a biblical prohibition, yet the Mishnah permits those at a distance to rely on the presumption that the offering was brought in time. This appears to demonstrate that *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* applies even in biblical cases — and moreover, it operates within a defined timeframe, namely that one may assume the task was completed by midday.

### Beit Din vs Individual

The question, of course, is why midday specifically. If the rule is simply that one may assume the agent completed the task, why would anyone presume the *omer* was brought by noon rather than at any point during the day? Tosafot addresses this: the *mitzvah* of the *omer* alone does not require that it be brought by *chatzot*, so the midday presumption requires a separate explanation.

Rav Nachman responds with the reason cited in the Mishnah itself: *lefi sheyodim she-ein Beit Din mitatzlin bo* — because the people know that *Beit Din* will not be lax about this matter. The *Beit Din* took pains to ensure the offering was brought before noon. It is an interesting feature of *Chazal*'s understanding of the Temple era that there was an institutional *Beit Din* that exercised oversight over the *kohanim* — a kind of rabbinic-priestly supervisory body. The point Rav Nachman draws from this is that the *omer* case is exceptional: precisely because it was *Beit Din* ensuring the task was done by a particular hour, one could make that specific presumption. A private individual, however, is subject to no such institutional oversight, and therefore one cannot make the same assumption about him.

There is also the version cited as *ve-ika d'amri* — some say — according to which Rav Nachman actually uses the same passage as proof *for* his position: the very fact that the Mishnah provides a specific rationale — that *Beit Din* does not delay — suggests that without that reason, one could not rely on *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* for a biblical matter. *Beit din hu d'lo misatzlin bo* — it is uniquely because *Beit Din* superintends the matter that the presumption holds. *Ha shaliach* — a private messenger — *misatzel bo*: one must be concerned that he would be neglectful. It is noted that one might equally have grounded the ruling in the principle that *kohanim zerizim hein* — *kohanim* are diligent — yet the Mishnah specifically introduces the *Beit Din* dimension, which Rav Nachman finds significant.

### Timeframe Assumptions

Rav Sheshet answers that the *Beit Din* rationale in the Mishnah explains only the earlier timeframe: *beit din al palga d'yoma* — because of *Beit Din*, you may assume it was done by noon. But *shaliach* — had it been an ordinary messenger — *kulei yoma* — you could still assume he would carry out the task, simply at some point during the day rather than necessarily by midday. The distinction is always about the assumed timeframe within which a task may be presumed completed. The *omer* must be brought before the end of the day; *eruv techumin* must be put down before Shabbat. If a *get* requires a messenger to travel over several days, the timeframe is entirely different. Within the given context, Rav Sheshet holds that one may presume the task will be accomplished within the relevant day, even if the early-midday assumption was unique to *Beit Din*'s involvement.

## Women's Korban Case

### Collection Box Setup

Rav Sheshet attempts yet again to find support for his position, this time from a *Braisa*. If a woman who had given birth or was a *zavah* — and who therefore needed to bring a *korban* to complete her *taharah* and resume eating *kodashim* — *mavia maos* — brought her money and deposited it in the special collection box in the *Beit HaMikdash* set aside for bird offerings brought by women for *leida* and *ziva*, she was not required to wait and observe the *kohanim* actually purchasing and offering the birds.

### Kohanim Completion

Rather, *v'tovelet v'ochelet b'kodashim l'erev* — she immerses in a *mikvah* and by nightfall she may eat *kodashim*, relying on the presumption that the *kohanim* will use all the money deposited in the box to purchase and bring the required bird offerings that very day. The boxes were structured much like a *shofar* — wide at the base and narrow at the top, so that money could be placed in but not easily withdrawn, similar to a *tzedakah* box or a piggy bank. There were thirteen such boxes in the *Beit HaMikdash*, each designated for a different purpose.

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason she may presume this? *Lav mishum d'amrinan hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* — is it not because we invoke the presumption that an agent performs his task? And Rashi notes the connection here to the classical principle that *kohanim shluchei didan* — the *kohanim* are our agents, carrying out the *korbanot* on our behalf — so there is an implicit *shlichut* upon which one may rely. Since the *chadash* prohibition is biblical, and since this *korban* is biblically required, this would appear to confirm that *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* applies even in biblical contexts.

### Beit Din Kohanim

Rav Nachman responds again in a manner parallel to his earlier answer. He invokes the ruling of Rav Shmaya: *hazakah ein beit din shel kohanim omdim misham* — there is a presumption that the rabbinic court overseeing the *kohanim* does not conclude its proceedings *ad sheyichlu kol maos shebashofar* — until all the money in the *shofar* box has been used. Once again, it is not the general principle of *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* that operates here but rather the special institutional supervision of a *Beit Din* — in this case, the *Beit Din* of the *kohanim* — that ensures the task is completed. As a general rule applicable to ordinary individuals, Rav Nachman maintains that no such presumption may be made.

## Figs/Terumot Case

### Eating Figs Ad Hoc

Rav Sheshet turns to a third *Braisa* in his attempt to demonstrate the principle. If a man says to his friend, "go and take figs from my fig tree," this creates a *halachic* complication: the moment the figs are picked, they are *tevel* — produce from which *terumot u-ma'asrot* have not yet been separated. The question is whether one may assume the owner (*ba'al ha-bayit*) has taken care of the separation, or whether the person who collected the figs must do so himself.

The *Braisa* rules as follows. In a case where the instructions were simply open-ended — "go pick figs for yourself" — one may eat from them *arai*, on an informal, *ad hoc* basis, without first separating *terumot u-ma'asrot*, since the owner did not know how many figs would be picked and could not have pre-separated for that quantity. But if one wishes to eat *kavu'a* — to sit down to a fixed, established meal — *me'asran vadai*: one must certainly separate *ma'aser*, as the owner could not have done so.

In the second case, where the owner specified an exact quantity — *malei lecha kol keli zeh te'einim mite'einati*, fill this basket with figs — the owner theoretically *could* have pre-separated for that precise amount. Therefore, if one wishes to eat *kavu'a*, the obligation is *me'asran demai* — one separates as if it were *demai*, doubtful produce. The reasoning: perhaps the owner already separated; perhaps not. This distinction between *vadai* and *demai* has a practical consequence beyond mere procedure. After the *ma'aser* is separated from a doubtful obligation, the produce is no longer *tevel* — it has been remedied — but the ownership of the *ma'aser* itself becomes a matter of doubt. The *Levi* claims it as *ma'aser* that belongs to him, but the person who separated can counter: I was not certainly obligated in the first place. The result is that one separates the *ma'aser* but retains it for oneself rather than giving it to a *Levi*.

### Am Ha'aretz vs Chaver

The *Braisa* then introduces a critical qualification: *bameh devarim amurim be-am ha-aretz* — when does the foregoing apply? When the owner is an *am ha-aretz*, an ordinary person not known to be scrupulous in observance of *terumot u-ma'asrot*. The Gemara later clarifies who is understood to be the collector in this scenario. It cannot be that an *am ha-aretz* was directing a fellow *am ha-aretz* to collect, because an *am ha-aretz* would not suspect his friend of laxity and would not accept the requirement to treat the produce as *demai*. The *Braisa* must therefore be addressing a *chaver* — a scrupulous observer — as the collector: in the first case, the *am ha-aretz* directed the *chaver*, and the *chaver* must treat the produce as potentially untithed.

### Rabbi's Position

If, however, the owner is a *chaver* — one who belongs to the recognized group particularly punctilious about *terumot u-ma'asrot* — then *divrei Rabbi*: according to Rabbi, *ochel ve-eino tzarich le-aser*. The collector may eat without any concern, certainly without needing to separate *ma'aser* at all. A *chaver* who has directed someone to take a specific quantity from his property would not allow food under his responsibility to go out without ensuring that *terumot u-ma'asrot* had been properly taken. Rabbi's reasoning is that the *chaver* is so meticulous that he would never allow another person access to food that might cause that person to sin.

### R. Shimon's Position

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — who was Rabbi's father — takes the precisely opposite position: if the owner was a *chaver*, *eino ochel ad she-ye'aser* — one may not eat without definitely separating *ma'aser*. The reason: *lefi she-lo nechshedu chaverim litrom she-lo min ha-mukaf* — a *chaver* is meticulous about the rule that *terumah* should ideally be separated only when all the produce is together (*min ha-mukaf*), not from one batch to cover a separate batch. Since the figs in the collector's basket are not physically together with the remaining produce at home, the *chaver* owner would not separate on behalf of the collector's portion. His scrupulosity, in Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's view, is turned inward — toward his own observance — rather than outward toward protecting others from sin.

### Sin Priority Debate

Rabbi responds to his father's position directly: *nirim devarai midivrei Abba* — my opinion appears more compelling than my father's. One notes, in passing, the remarkable directness with which Rabbi states his preference over his father's ruling. While *halachic* debate between a son and his father is certainly permitted — the *Gemara* is full of such cases — the particular phrasing here, placing his own view explicitly above *Abba's*, raises an interesting question in the context of *hilchos kibbud av va-em*, though the *Gemara* moves forward without dwelling on it.

Rabbi's reasoning: *mutav sheyechasdu chaverim litrom she-lo min ha-mukaf, velo yachilu amei ha-aretz tevalim* — it is better that a *chaver* be suspected of the technical impropriety of separating *terumah* from produce that is not all together than that *amei ha-aretz* be caused to eat *tevel*. In Rabbi's view, the more genuinely *frum* course of action is to prevent a greater prohibition even at the cost of a minor technical deviation in one's own practice.

The *Gemara* then frames the root of this dispute: *bemai kamiflagi* — what is the fundamental point of disagreement? Rabbi holds: *nicha lei lechaver deli'avid hu issura kalila velo li'avid am ha-aretz issura rabba* — a *chaver* would prefer to commit a minor infraction himself rather than allow an *am ha-aretz* to commit a major one on his account. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds the opposite: a *chaver* would rather the *am ha-aretz* commit the major prohibition than himself violate even the smallest detail. As the *Gemara* notes, this is not merely a theoretical policy discussion — it is a practical question about how people actually behave, which then determines what the *halachah* can presume about what the *chaver* owner did or did not do.

This dispute touches on the broader question of *omrim le-adam chatei k'dei she-yizkeh chavercha* — whether one is ever told to commit a minor sin in order to save another from a greater one. As Tosafot discusses in the context of *Masechet Shabbat*, there are distinctions: when the greater sin arises directly from one's own action — as here, where the owner himself sent the collector out with produce that may be *tevel* — there is greater reason to say one must act to prevent it, even at some minor personal cost. Where the other person's sin stems from that person's own negligence, the calculus may be different.

## Synthesis Agreement

### Technical Details Only

At this point, the *Gemara* steps back and assesses what has actually been proven. *Lo peligi* — the dispute between Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is only about a very specific detail: *ela demar savar nechashdu u-mar savar lo nechashdu* — one holds that we attribute to the *chaver* the willingness to separate *she-lo min ha-mukaf*, and the other holds we do not. Setting that narrow dispute aside, *kulei alma chazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* — everyone agrees to the underlying presumption that the *ba'al ha-bayit* took care of *terumot u-ma'asrot*. The mechanism of assuming the *shaliach* performed his task is accepted; they only disagree about a technical detail concerning the manner of performance.

The *Gemara* thus treats this as evidence for *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto*. One might fairly observe that the analogy here is somewhat strained: the *ba'al ha-bayit* in this case was not formally designated as anyone's *shaliach* in the way that a person who agrees to place an *eruv* is. Rashi explains that since the owner bears a responsibility not to cause the other person to sin, this is analogous to an accepted *shelichus*. Whether one finds this persuasive or not, it is the *Gemara*'s own analogy, and it stands.

### Responsibility Principle

A thread running through all three cases — the *omer*, the women's *korbanot*, and the *terumot u-ma'asrot* — is the element of responsibility that flows from the *shaliach*'s potential failure. In each case, if the designated agent or responsible party does not carry out the task, another person will be caused to sin: if the *eruv* is not placed, one violates the laws of *techumin*; if the *korban* is not brought, the woman remains in a state where eating *kodashim* entails *karet*; if *terumot u-ma'asrot* are not taken, the collector eats *tevel*. Tosafot raises the point that in cases where the failure of the *shaliach* will cause another's sin, there may be stronger grounds to apply *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* even in biblical contexts, regardless of how one rules in the general dispute between Rav Nachman and Rav Sheshet.

As for the *pesak halachah*: this dispute between Rav Nachman and Rav Sheshet is also a dispute among the *Geonim* and *Rishonim*. Some *Geonim* ruled in accordance with Rav Nachman — that one does not invoke *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* for biblical matters — while Rabbeinu Tam and several *Rishonim* ruled in accordance with Rav Sheshet that the presumption applies universally. *Ba'alei HaTosafot* preserve both views, and the question remains a *machloket Geonim ve-Rishonim*.

## Practical Assumptions

### Trust vs Formal Rule

A final practical consideration bears mention. Beyond the formal *halachic* framework, there is a real-world dimension to *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* that does not reduce neatly to the biblical-rabbinic distinction. In practice, one's knowledge of a particular individual — whether this specific person is reliable or not — inevitably colors how the presumption operates. One can readily think of individuals of whom one would say *vadai ein shaliach oseh shelichuto*, and others of whom *hazakah shaliach oseh shelichuto* describes the situation with complete confidence.

### Rav Moshe: Knowledge

This tension between personal knowledge and formal *halachic* rules is addressed directly by Rav Moshe Feinstein in a *teshuvah* concerning Jews in the Soviet Union. He was asked whether elderly observant parents could eat in the homes of their non-observant children who promised to maintain *kashrus* for the visit. Technically, a person who does not keep *kashrus* and does not keep *Shabbat* lacks *ne'emanus* — formal halachic credibility — in these matters; the rule of *eid echad ne'eman be-issurin* does not extend to those who are themselves not observant. Rav Moshe, however, ruled that the category of formal *ne'emanus* presupposes a situation where one has no independent means of knowing what took place. When one has *actual* knowledge of a person — a deep familiarity from which one can genuinely and reliably conclude that this individual will do what he has promised — then one need not rely on the formal rules at all. That real knowledge supersedes the formal framework.

Rav Moshe did show more caution when asked a similar question in a less pressing context — not a *sha'at ha-dachak* involving elderly parents and their children — and the question of whether subjective personal knowledge can override formal *halachic* presumptions is a genuinely difficult one. The stakes are real: in *hashgachah* contexts, for example, there are documented cases where non-Jewish workers maintain standards more scrupulously than those who think they know better. How much weight one gives to ground-level knowledge versus formal *halachic* categories — such as the twenty-four-hour rule for *kelim* that cannot be applied in a restaurant setting where *kelim* are used constantly — remains an open and consequential question. The *hazakah* must serve some function, or it would be worthless; yet the relationship between formal presumption and actual knowledge is not always straightforward.

## Eruv in Trees

### Height Requirements

The Mishnah now introduces a new set of cases concerning where an *eruv techumin* was placed. *Natanu be-ilan lemala me-asarah tefachim* — if the *eruv* was placed in a tree higher than ten *tefachim*, *ein eruvo eruv* — it is invalid. *Lemata me-asarah tefachim* — below ten *tefachim*, *eruvo eruv* — it is valid. Separately, if the *eruv* was placed in a pit, *afilu amok me-ah amah* — even if the pit is one hundred *amot* deep — *eruvo eruv*: it is valid. These rulings require unpacking, which the *Gemara* proceeds to provide.

### Domain Location

Three *Amoraim* — Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, Rabbi Assi, and Rava bar Nasan — were sitting, with Rav Nachman present, when they raised the foundational question: *hai ilan de-ka heicha* — where is this tree located? What domain does it stand in?

### Public Domain Tree

If the tree stands in a *reshus ha-yachid* — a private domain — then the height of the *eruv* within the tree would be irrelevant, since *reshus ha-yachid olah ad la-rakia* — the private domain extends upward without limit. Why would it matter whether the *eruv* is high or low in the tree? The answer must be that the tree stands in a *reshus ha-rabim* — a public domain.

### Shabbat Base Access

The problem then comes into focus: *de-mistavein lishpos heicha* — where does one intend to *be* when Shabbat begins? If one intended to begin Shabbat up in the tree with the *eruv*, then *hu ve-eruvo bi-makom echad* — he and his *eruv* are in the same place, both in *reshus ha-yachid*, and there is no difficulty. But *ella nistavein lishpos lemata* — the Mishnah's case is where one intends to start Shabbat at the base of the tree, in the *reshus ha-rabim*. The rule governing *eruv techumin* is that the *eruv* must be accessible from the place one has designated as one's Shabbat base. If the *eruv* is in the tree above ten *tefachim* — that is, within a *reshus ha-yachid* — then one cannot access it while standing below in the *reshus ha-rabim*.

### Transfer Concern

Retrieving the *eruv* would require taking it from *reshus ha-yachid* to *reshus ha-rabim*, a biblical prohibition. Therefore the *eruv* is invalid when it is placed above ten *tefachim* in a tree standing in the *reshus ha-rabim*, with one intending to begin Shabbat at the base.

### Tree Usage Issue

But a further question arises: if the tree is in a *reshus ha-rabim*, why is the *eruv* valid even when placed below ten *tefachim*? Using a tree on Shabbat is itself a rabbinic prohibition — *kamishtamesh be-ilan* — so regardless of the height, how can one access the *eruv*?

### Rabbi: Twilight Rule

The answer is: *ve-Rabbi hi* — this Mishnah follows the opinion of Rabbi, who rules that *kol davar she-hu mishum shevus lo gazru alav bein ha-shmashos* — rabbinic prohibitions (*shevut*) were not enacted for the *bein ha-shmashos* period, the twilight zone at the onset of Shabbat. This principle is of broad importance for the laws of *eruv*: the validity of an *eruv techumin* is determined by whether one has access to it at *bein ha-shmashos*, the moment that defines the onset of Shabbat. If acquiring that access at *bein ha-shmashos* requires only a rabbinic violation — such as using a tree — Rabbi holds that the rabbinic prohibition does not apply at that moment, and the *eruv* is therefore effective. The biblical issue — the *reshus ha-yachid* above ten *tefachim* — cannot be resolved by this principle, since it is a *d'oraisa* prohibition that remains in force even at *bein ha-shmashos*.

### Standard Explanation

Rav Nachman affirmed the three Rabbis' explanation, adding *yasher koach*, and confirmed that this is also how Shmuel explained the Mishnah. When the three Rabbis pressed him — *pashritah l'kulei hai*, had he gone to such effort to arrive at this explanation? — Rav Nachman clarified: they had all arrived at the same explanation, and the question was really whether he had established it as a fixed teaching, *keva'ituha be-Gemara*. He confirmed: *in* — yes, this is the standard way the Mishnah is explained.

### Ten-Tefachim Height

This explanation is then preserved formally as a *memra* of Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel: *hacha be-ilan ha-omed be-reshus ha-rabim askinan* — the Mishnah concerns a tree standing in the *reshus ha-rabim*. And one further detail is added that had not been stated in the earlier analysis: *gavo'ah asarah ve-rachav arba'ah* — the tree must be ten *tefachim* tall and four *tefachim* wide at that height. This dimensional requirement is essential: a space that is four by four *tefachim* and ten *tefachim* high qualifies as a *reshus ha-yachid*. If the tree lacks this width, it does not constitute a *reshus ha-yachid* at any height and the problem would not arise in the same way.

Rashi adds that even where the *eruv* is below ten *tefachim*, the tree itself may technically qualify as a *karmelis*, yet since the prohibition involved is only rabbinic in nature, Rabbi's principle — that *shevut* does not apply at *bein ha-shmashos* — resolves the difficulty. The bottom line of the Mishnah is thus: when the tree is in a *reshus ha-rabim*, stands at least ten *tefachim* high and four *tefachim* wide, and one intends to begin Shabbat at its base, the *eruv* must be accessible from below. If it is placed above ten *tefachim*, it sits in a *reshus ha-yachid* from which one cannot retrieve it without a biblical violation, and the *eruv* is invalid. If it is below ten *tefachim*, the only barrier to access is rabbinic — the prohibition against using a tree — which is suspended at *bein ha-shmashos* according to Rabbi, and the *eruv* is valid.
