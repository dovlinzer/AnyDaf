# Sukkah 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Schach Distance & Gud As…

### Three Tefachim Air Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הִרְחִיק אֶת הַסִּיכּוּךְ מִן הַדְּפָנוֹת שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים — פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one distanced the roofing from the walls** of the *sukka* at **a distance of three handbreadths** the *sukka* is **unfit,** because three handbreadths of open space, even adjacent to the walls, render the *sukka* unfit.

The present *daf* opens with a *mishna* that serves as a natural companion to the preceding one, which treated the principle of *gud asik* — the conceptual extension of a valid wall upward through empty space. That earlier *mishna* addressed gaps on the *vertical* plane: a wall standing ten *tefachim* from the ground may be imagined as rising all the way to the *schach*, even if there is air between the top of the wall and the roof. The principle of *lavud* — which treats a gap of less than three *tefachim* as legally closed — was central there as well.

This *mishna* now turns to a different problem: a gap on the *horizontal* plane. The case is one in which the wall rises to the full height of the *sukkah*, but at the level of the roof there is a gap of three *tefachim* or more between the edge of the wall and where the *schach* begins. In such a case, the *mishna* rules *pesulah* — the *sukkah* is invalid. The wall, although it rises to the proper height, is not connected to the *schach*, and since the horizontal gap exceeds three *tefachim*, *lavud* cannot close it. Without that connection, the *sukkah* lacks a proper wall.

### Gud Asik Extension

It is worth distinguishing this scenario from the previous *mishna*'s case. When the wall does not reach the full height but stands a valid ten *tefachim* from the ground, *gud asik* — "extend the wall upward" — applies, and the wall is treated as though it rises to meet the *schach*. But *gud asik* operates on the vertical axis; it imagines the existing wall continuing upward into space above it. Here, by contrast, the gap is lateral — a horizontal space at the level of the *schach* between the wall's edge and the *schach* itself. *Gud asik* cannot close that gap.

### Vertical vs Horizontal

This raises an immediate question: why does *dofen akuma* — the principle of the "bent wall" — not apply here to bridge the gap? *Dofen akuma* permits treating up to four *amot* of invalid *schach* adjacent to a wall as though it were a bent extension of that wall. If so, could one not invoke *dofen akuma* even for an air gap, imagining the wall as bending sideways through the open space?

The answer is that *dofen akuma* is operative only when something physical occupies that lateral space — specifically, *pasul schach*, invalid *schach*. An invalid material such as metal bars can be reconceived as a portion of the wall that has bent over horizontally. Empty air, by contrast, has no physical substance that could be re-categorized as part of the wall. Air is simply absence, and one cannot bend a wall through nothing.

### Rashi on Dofen Akuma

*Rashi* on this *daf* explicitly reflects on why he adopts one conceptual model of *dofen akuma* over another — a remarkably self-aware methodological comment. Two lines from the top of the *amud*, *Rashi* writes: *de-avir poseil beshelosha afilu min hatzad* — air invalidates at three *tefachim* even from the side, on the horizontal plane. He then adds: *umikan ani omer umefaresh* — and it is from this *mishna* that I derive and explain the following point about *dofen akuma*.

*Rashi* proceeds to articulate two possible models of *dofen akuma* and then explains which one he rejects and why. The model he accepts: *dechashvinan leschach ke'ilu min hadofen* — we treat the invalid *schach* as though it is actually part of the wall, *venekaf venichpaf lemala* — and the wall has simply bent upward and over. On this reading, the metal bars or wooden planks of the *pasul schach* are reconceived as a physical extension of the wall that has folded across the top. Since *pasul schach* is perfectly valid material for a wall, this reconceptualization is coherent.

The model *Rashi* rejects is the one that would make *dofen akuma* analogous to *gud asik*: *roein et hadofen ke'ilu hu okem veholech tachat haschach hapasul umagia laschach hakesher umotzi et hapasul min hasukkah* — we imagine the wall as bending and traveling beneath the invalid *schach* until it reaches the valid *schach*, thereby pulling the invalid section outside the *sukkah*. On this reading, *dofen akuma* would be an invisible extension of the wall through space, much as *gud asik* is an invisible extension of the wall upward. But if that were the model, then even air could function the same way — one would simply imagine the wall extending horizontally through the empty space. Since the *mishna* demonstrates that air does *not* permit such an extension, *Rashi* concludes that the invisible-extension model is wrong. *Dofen akuma* does not extend the existing wall through empty space; rather, it reclassifies whatever *physical material* is present — the *pasul schach* — as part of the wall itself. Since air cannot be reclassified as a wall, air does not trigger *dofen akuma*.

This is a striking piece of *Rashi*: he is not merely offering a definition but explaining the interpretive reasoning that led him to choose one model over the other, with the *mishna* itself serving as the decisive proof.

## Three Dofen Akuma Cases

### Collapsed Roof Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּיִת שֶׁנִּפְחַת וְסִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבָּיו — אִם יֵשׁ מִן הַכּוֹתֶל לַסִּיכּוּךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a house that was breached,** creating a hole in the middle of the roof, **and one roofed over** the breach, **if from the wall to the roofing there are four** or more **cubits** of the remaining original roof, **it is an unfit** *sukka*. If the roofing is less than four cubits from the wall, the *sukka* is fit, based on the principle of curved wall; the remaining intact ceiling is considered an extension of the vertical wall.

The *mishna* now presents three distinct applications of the *dofen akuma* principle. The first is the case of *bayit shenifchat* — a house whose roof partially collapsed. The house retains its walls and portions of its original roof, but there is now an opening in the middle through which the owner wishes to place valid *schach* and observe *Sukkot*. The remaining portions of the original roof are invalid *schach*: although the material may be wood or other fundamentally kosher material, the roof was constructed as a permanent fixture and was not built *leshem tzel* — for the purpose of shade in the manner of a *sukkah* — and therefore does not qualify. It is the collapsed center that becomes the *sukkah*, with the original rooftop surrounding it as *pasul schach*.

The rule: *im yesh min hakosel lesichuch arba amot pesulah* — if there are four *amot* or more of the original roof between the wall and the valid *schach* in the center, the *sukkah* is invalid. If the distance is less than four *amot*, *dofen akuma* applies — the wall is imagined as bending over and encompassing that strip of invalid *schach* — and the *sukkah* is valid.

### Portico Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן חָצֵר שֶׁהִיא מוּקֶּפֶת אַכְסַדְרָה, סוּכָּה גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁהִקִּיפוּהָ בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין מְסַכְּכִין בּוֹ — אִם יֵשׁ תַּחְתָּיו אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise,** in the case of **a courtyard that is surrounded** on three sides **by a portico,** which has a roof but no walls, if one placed roofing over the courtyard between the different sides of the portico and the roof of the portico is four cubits wide, the *sukka* is unfit. Similarly, **a large *sukka* that was surrounded** at the edge of its roofing **with material with which one** may **not roof** a *sukka*, e.g., vessels susceptible to ritual impurity, **if there are four cubits beneath** the unfit roofing, the *sukka* is **unfit.** The principle of curved wall does not apply to unfit roofing that measures four cubits or more.

The second case is *chatzer mukkefet achsadra* — a courtyard surrounded by a portico. Imagine a central open courtyard enclosed on all sides by a colonnade: columns supporting a covered walkway or arcade running around the perimeter, with the buildings' walls forming the outer boundary. The owner wishes to place valid *schach* over the central courtyard and use it as a *sukkah*. The covered portico roof, being a permanent structure, constitutes *pasul schach* separating the valid *schach* of the center from the walls of the surrounding buildings.

*Rashi*, in a passage about eight lines before the wider-margined lines begin, describes this structure and then offers a contemporary analogy: *dugma ledavar*, comparable to this concept, is a *cloister* — *lashon Ashkenaz* — the kind of courtyard with grass in the center and a covered walkway surrounding it found in ecclesiastical institutions. He writes: *ken hagallakhim* — just as the priests have such a structure — *sheyesh lahem la-avir sheyesh bah asavim vehekef akhsadra saviv lo* — with an open interior containing greenery and an arcade surrounding it.

As in the first case, if the portico is less than four *amot* wide, *dofen akuma* permits treating the covered walkway as a bent extension of the wall, and the central courtyard may serve as a valid *sukkah*.

### Invalid Edge Case

The third case is straightforward: *sukkah gedolah shehikifuha bedavar she'ein mesakh'khin bo* — a large *sukkah* whose perimeter of *schach* adjacent to the walls consists entirely of material that is intrinsically *pasul* — such as metal bars. Here the invalidity is not a matter of intent or permanence but of the material itself, which is categorically unfit for *schach*. Again the rule is the same: *im yesh tachtav arba amot pesulah* — four *amot* of such *pasul schach* at the edge renders the *sukkah* invalid; less than four *amot* allows *dofen akuma* to apply.

### Why Three Cases?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כׇּל הָנֵי לְמָה לִי? צְרִיכָא: דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בַּיִת שֶׁנִּפְחַת — מִשּׁוּם דְּהָנֵי מְחִיצוֹת לְבַיִת עֲבִידָן. אֲבָל חָצֵר הַמּוּקֶּפֶת אַכְסַדְרָה, דִּמְחִיצוֹת לָאו לְאַכְסַדְרָה עֲבִידִי — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי, מִשּׁוּם דִּסְכָכָן סְכָךְ כָּשֵׁר הוּא, אֲבָל סוּכָּה גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁהִקִּיפוּהָ בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין מְסַכְּכִין בּוֹ, דִּסְכָכָהּ סְכָךְ פָּסוּל הוּא — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why do I need all these** cases based on the identical principle of curved wall? The Gemara explains: It is **necessary** to cite all the cases, **as, if** the mishna had **taught us** only the case of **the house that was breached,** I would have said that the principle of curved wall applies there **because those walls were established for the house.** Therefore, when the house is transformed into a *sukka*, the walls continue to serve their original function as walls of the *sukka*. **However,** with regard to **a courtyard surrounded** on each of the three sides **by a portico,** where its **walls were established not for the portico** but for the house that opens into the portico, and they happen to serve as the interior walls of the portico, I could **say no,** they are not considered as connected to the roofing at all. Consequently, it is **necessary** for the mishna to cite that case as well. **And if** the mishna **taught us** only **these two** cases, one would have said that the principle of curved wall can apply **because** all of **their roofing is fit roofing,** and the preexisting roof of the house and the portico is unfit only due to the principle: Prepare it, and not from that which has already been prepared. **However,** here, in the case of **a large *sukka* that was surrounded** at the edge of its roofing **with material with which one** may **not roof** a *sukka*, where some of its **roofing is unfit** and the fit roofing does not actually reach the wall, one could **say no,** the roofing is unfit. Therefore, it is **necessary** to state that case as well.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *kol hani lamma li* — why do we need all three cases? They all seem to be identical applications of the same rule.

The answer is *tzricha* — each case is independently necessary. Had the *mishna* taught only the *bayit shenifchat*, one might have thought *dofen akuma* applies exclusively because the walls there are genuine interior walls — walls built for and serving the house itself — and their strong halakhic status makes it easier to extend them conceptually. But in the portico case, the walls used for the *sukkah* are the outer walls of the surrounding buildings, which were not built to enclose the courtyard at all; one might have thought such walls are too weak to trigger *dofen akuma*. Hence the second case is needed.

Had the *mishna* taught only those two cases, one might have thought that *dofen akuma* works because the invalid material in both cases is at least made of fundamentally kosher material — wood or similar substances that would be valid *schach* if placed correctly. Perhaps it is specifically that quasi-kosher quality that allows the material to be reconceived as a bent wall. But in the third case the *pasul schach* is intrinsically invalid material — metal bars or the like — and one might have thought that so severe an invalidity cannot be overcome by *dofen akuma*. The third case therefore teaches that even material that is categorically unfit for *schach* can function as a bent wall up to four *amot*.

The *mishna* thus conveys: whatever purpose the walls serve, however severe the invalidity of the *schach*, *dofen akuma* operates up to four *amot* without exception.

## Air vs Pasul Schach

### Yeshiva Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: אַשְׁכַּחְתִּינְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּבֵי רַב דְּיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: אֲוִיר פּוֹסֵל בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, סְכָךְ פָּסוּל פּוֹסֵל בְּאַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba said: I found the Sages of the school of Rav, who were sitting and saying** in the name of Rav: **Space** without roofing **renders** the *sukka* **unfit with** a measure of **three** handbreadths of space. However, **unfit roofing renders** the *sukka* **unfit with** a measure of **four** handbreadths.

Following this analysis of the *mishna*, the *Gemara* turns to a closely related question not addressed in any *mishna*: how much *pasul schach* in the *middle* of the *sukkah* — not at the edge — is sufficient to invalidate it? The *mishna* establishes the rule for the edge; the center requires its own discussion.

*Rabba* reports that he came upon the students of Rav's *yeshiva* — *rabbanan d'vei Rav* — seated and teaching the following: *avir posel bishelosha* — air invalidates at three *tefachim*; *sechach pasul posel be'arba'ah* — invalid *schach* invalidates at four *tefachim*. These measures apply wherever the gap or the *pasul schach* occurs — at the edge or in the middle.

### Separation Mechanism

It is worth pausing to understand the mechanism by which air or *pasul schach* invalidates. The invalidity is not a matter of one section of *schach* "contaminating" the whole; rather, air or *pasul schach* creates a *separation* between two portions of the *sukkah*, preventing them from being halakhically combined.

Consider a *sukkah* with three walls and valid *schach* throughout, save for a strip of air or *pasul schach*. If that strip runs in one direction, it may sever only one half of the *sukkah* from a wall, leaving the other half with three walls and still viable. If it runs in the perpendicular direction, it divides the *sukkah* into two halves, each of which possesses only two walls — and neither half is a valid *sukkah*. The rule is therefore about structural continuity: once the gap or *pasul schach* is sufficient to constitute a meaningful separation, the two sides cannot be combined, and each side must independently qualify as a *sukkah*.

*Tosafot* has a careful discussion of this mechanism, including the edge case in which a patch of *pasul schach* or air does not run from wall to wall but sits as an isolated square in the center. In such a case, if the surrounding portion of the *sukkah* independently meets the minimum size of seven by seven *tefachim*, the *sukkah* remains valid — the isolated patch is treated as an extension of the larger valid space, and one should simply not sit directly beneath that patch.

What of a patch smaller than the invalidating measure? Less than four *tefachim* of *pasul schach*, or less than three *tefachim* of air, does not break the structural continuity of the *sukkah* at all. The *schach* is treated as continuous, and one may even sit directly beneath such a small segment of *pasul schach* — a somewhat surprising conclusion, but that is the *halakha*.

### Four Tefachim Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמֵינָא לְהוּ אֲנָא: אֲוִיר דְּפוֹסֵל בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְנָא לְכוּ — דִּתְנַן: הִרְחִיק אֶת הַסִּיכּוּךְ מִן הַדְּפָנוֹת שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים — פְּסוּלָה. סְכָךְ פָּסוּל נָמֵי לָא לִיפְסֹיל אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, דִּתְנַן: בַּיִת שֶׁנִּפְחַת וְסִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבָּיו, אִם יֵשׁ בֵּין הַסִּיכּוּךְ לַכּוֹתֶל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — פְּסוּלָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And I said to them: From where** do **you** derive that **space renders** the *sukka* **unfit** when it amounts to **three** handbreadths? It is **as we learned** in the mishna: If **one distanced the roofing from the walls** of the *sukka* **at a distance of three handbreadths,** the *sukka* is **unfit.** If, indeed, this mishna is the source of the *halakha*, **also** in the case of **unfit roofing, let it render** the *sukka* **unfit only** if the roofing measures **four cubits, as we learned** in the same mishna: With regard to **a house that was breached and one roofed over** the breach, **if from the wall to the roofing there is four** or more **cubits** of the remaining original roof, the *sukka* is **unfit.**

*Rabba* challenges the students' position. He says: *avir depasel bishelosha mena lachu* — where do you derive that air invalidates at three *tefachim*? From the *mishna* itself: *hirchik et hasichuch min hadafnos shelosha tefachim pesulah* — if you distance the *schach* from the walls three *tefachim* on the horizontal plane, it is invalid. But if the *mishna* is your source, *Rabba* argues, then the *mishna*'s other case should also be your guide: *sechach pasul nami lo lifsol ela bearba amot* — invalid *schach* should not invalidate until four *amot*, even in the middle. After all, the *mishna* teaches four *amot* regarding *pasul schach* at the edge (*bayit shenifchat*), and *Rabba* sees no principled distinction between the edge and the middle — the same measure should apply throughout.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַרוּ לִי: בַּר מִינַּהּ דְּהַהִיא, דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מִשּׁוּם דּוֹפֶן עֲקוּמָּה נָגְעוּ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And they said to me:** Cite proof from the mishna, **apart from this** case, **as both Rav and Shmuel said that** in this case, the Sages in the mishna **touched upon** the principle of **curved wall**. In other words, the fact that this house is a fit *sukka* is unrelated to the minimum measure of unfit roofing. It is fit due to the principle of curved wall.

The students respond: *bar minah dehi* — that case cannot serve as proof, because Rav and Shmuel both explain that case as involving *dofen akuma* — the special rule of the bent wall, which is inherently a principle about the *edge*, not the middle.

This response reveals that *Rabba* fundamentally disagrees with the *dofen akuma* framework as an independent principle. In his view, there is no special bent-wall rule. Rather, the single governing principle is that *pasul schach* up to four *amot* does not break the continuity of the *sukkah* — whether at the edge or in the middle. Under 3.9 *amot*, one should not sit beneath the *pasul schach*, but the *sukkah* as a whole remains intact. Once four *amot* are reached, the continuity breaks. The asymmetry between edge and middle that Rav and Shmuel posit — four *amot* at the edge due to *dofen akuma*, only four *tefachim* in the middle — does not exist for *Rabba*.

### Rava's Thought Exp

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמֵינָא לְהוּ אֲנָא: מָה אִילּוּ אִיכָּא סְכָךְ פָּסוּל פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבָּעָה וַאֲוִיר פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה מַאי? כְּשֵׁרָה. מַלְּיֵיהּ בְּשַׁפּוּדִין מַאי — פְּסוּלָה, וְלֹא יְהֵא אֲוִיר הַפּוֹסֵל בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה כִּסְכָךְ פָּסוּל הַפּוֹסֵל בְּאַרְבָּעָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And I said to them: What if** there **is** a *sukka* with **less than four** handbreadths of **unfit roofing and** an adjacent **space** of **less than three** handbreadths; **what** would be the status of the *sukka*? The *sukka* would be **fit,** since it lacks the minimum measure of both space and unfit roofing that renders a *sukka* unfit. If one then **filled** the space **with skewers, what** would be the status of the *sukka*? It would be **unfit,** as there would be more than four handbreadths of unfit roofing. **But shouldn't space, which** is more stringent, as it **renders** the *sukka* **unfit with** only **three** handbreadths, **be as** stringent as **unfit roofing, which renders** the *sukka* **unfit** only **with four** handbreadths of unfit roofing?

*Rabba* then poses a striking logical thought experiment against the students' position. He says: *mah ilu ika sechach pasul pachot me'arba'ah veavir pachot mishloshah* — suppose you had three *tefachim* of *pasul schach* together with two *tefachim* of air. Each element individually is below its threshold — three *tefachim* of *pasul schach* is less than four, and two *tefachim* of air is less than three — and so the students would say the *sukkah* is *kasher*. Now, *milao beshpudim* — fill those two *tefachim* of air with metal bars. Now you have five *tefachim* of *pasul schach*, which exceeds four, and the *sukkah* is *pasul*. *Rabba* asks: *velo yehei avir haposel beshelosha kesechach pasul haposel bearba'ah* — is this not internally inconsistent? Air is the *stronger* invalidator — it creates a break at only three *tefachim*, while *pasul schach* requires four *tefachim* to create the same break. How can it be that the combination is *kasher* when the stronger element (air) is present in its full two *tefachim*, but becomes *pasul* when those same two *tefachim* are replaced by the *weaker* invalidator (*pasul schach*)?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַרוּ לִי: אִי הָכִי, לְדִידָךְ נָמֵי דְּאָמְרַתְּ סְכָךְ פָּסוּל פּוֹסֵל בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, מָה אִילּוּ אִיכָּא סְכָךְ פָּסוּל פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וַאֲוִיר פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה, מַאי? כְּשֵׁרָה. מַלְּיֵיהּ בְּשַׁפּוּדִין מַאי — פְּסוּלָה. לֹא יְהֵא אֲוִיר הַפּוֹסֵל בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה כִּסְכָךְ פָּסוּל הַפּוֹסֵל בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **And they said to me: If so, according to you, who said** that **unfit roofing renders** a *sukka* **unfit** only **with four cubits** of unfit roofing, the same question arises. **Just as, if there were** a *sukka* with **less than four cubits of unfit roofing and** an adjacent **space** measuring **less than three** handbreadths, **what** would be its status? It would be **fit.** If one then **filled** the space **with skewers,** what would be its status? It would be **unfit.** Here too, the question arises: **Shouldn't space, which** is more stringent, as it **renders** the *sukka* **unfit with** only **three** handbreadths of space, **be as** stringent as **unfit roofing, which renders** the *sukka* **unfit with** only **four cubits** of unfit roofing?

## Rava vs Rav/Shmuel

### Rava's Shiur View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמֵינָא לְהוּ אֲנָא: הַאי מַאי? בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי דְּאָמֵינָא אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — מִשּׁוּם שִׁיעוּרָא וְלָאו שִׁיעוּרָא הוּא. הַאי לָאו שִׁיעוּרָא הוּא, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא שָׁווּ שִׁיעוּרַיְיהוּ לַהֲדָדֵי — לָא מִצְטָרְפִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And I said to them: What is this** comparison? **Granted, according to** my opinion, **that I say** that the measure of unfit roofing that renders a *sukka* unfit is **four cubits,** the status of the *sukka* is determined **on the basis** of whether it is **the** requisite **measure or** it is **not the** requisite **measure.** In other words, the difference between unfit roofing that is four cubits and unfit roofing that is less is a unique *halakha*, completely unrelated to the principle of curved wall. Similarly, it is a unique *halakha* that three handbreadths of space in a roof render a *sukka* unfit. In **this** case, there is **not the** requisite **measure** according to either *halakha*; and **since their measures are not equal** to each other, **they do not combine** to render the *sukka* unfit. The *sukka* is rendered unfit only when the measure of unfit roofing reaches four cubits.

*Rabba* accepts the symmetry of the challenge but argues that his position resolves it cleanly: *bishlama ledidi de'amina arba amot mishum shiura d'lav shiura hu* — for me, the four *amot* figure is simply an arbitrary quantitative *shiur*, nothing more. Just as on *Yom Kippur* one does not combine slightly less than a *k'kotevet* of food with slightly less than a *melo lugmav* of liquid on the grounds that one is a stronger prohibition than the other — because these are simply different categories with different independent measures — so too here. Air has its measure: three *tefachim*. *Pasul schach* has its measure: four *amot*. Since they are different categories with different measures, *keivan d'lo shavu shiureihu lahadei* — since their measures are not equal to each other — *lo mitztarfi* — they do not combine. This is not a logical paradox; it is simply the nature of distinct categories with distinct *shiurim*.

### Rav/Shmuel Haflaga

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְדִידְכוּ דְּאָמְרִיתוּ שִׁיעוּר מִשּׁוּם הַפְלָגָה — מָה לִי הִפְלַג בִּסְכָךְ פָּסוּל, מָה לִי אִתַּפְלַג בִּסְכָךְ פָּסוּל וַאֲוִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **However, according to you, who say** that the measure of four handbreadths for unfit roofing is **due to** the **distance** between the wall and the fit roofing, which renders the *sukka* unfit, **what** is the difference **to me if it was distanced due to unfit roofing,** and **what** is the difference **to me if it was distanced due to** a combination of **unfit roofing and space?** In either case, the distance between the roofing and the wall should prevent connecting the roofing to the wall. This concludes Rabba's account of his exchange with the Sages of the school of Rav.

The challenge to Rav and Shmuel's position, however, is of a fundamentally different character. *Rabba*'s question is not merely that the numbers seem inconsistent. It is that Rav and Shmuel did not derive the four-*tefachim* figure from any *mishna* or *braita*. They arrived at it through their own reasoning. What logic could have produced it?

The only available logic — and *Rabba* argues this is precisely what Rav and Shmuel must have been thinking — is that four *tefachim* defines a minimal independent *reshut*, a space unto itself. Once *pasul schach* occupies four *tefachim*, that strip constitutes its own separate space, and the two halves of the *sukkah* are thereby *separated*. The invalidating concept is not an arbitrary quantity of a particular substance; it is the reality of *haflaga* — separation, division — between the two sections of *schach*.

But if the concept is separation, then *Rabba*'s question returns with full force: *mah li hiflig besechach pasul mah li hiflig besechach pasul ve-avir* — why should I care whether the separating material is entirely *pasul schach* or a mix of *pasul schach* and air? In either case, the two halves of the *schach* are separated by four *tefachim* of space. The reality of the gap is the same. On Rav and Shmuel's own logic, combining *pasul schach* and air should produce the same result as four *tefachim* of *pasul schach* alone.

The underlying *machloket* is thus a fundamental difference in how one conceptualizes invalidation. For *Rabba*, the question is quantitative: how much of a given substance is present, and does it reach the specified *shiur* for that substance? For Rav and Shmuel, the question is qualitative and structural: is there a sufficient break in continuity, regardless of what fills the gap?

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּלְמָר נָמֵי, נְהִי דְּלָא שָׁווּ שִׁיעוּרַיְיהוּ בְּסוּכָּה גְּדוֹלָה, בְּסוּכָּה קְטַנָּה מִי לָא שָׁווּ שִׁיעוּרַיְיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabba: **And according to** the **Master, too, although their measures are not equal in a large *sukka*,** which is larger than four cubits, **in a small *sukka* aren't their measures equal?** In a minimally sized *sukka*, seven by seven handbreadths, three handbreadths of unfit roofing must render the *sukka* unfit. If the measure of fitness were to remain up to four handbreadths, that would mean that a *sukka* with a majority of unfit roofing is fit, which is unreasonable. Therefore, Rabba's contention that the measures of unfit roofing and space are totally different is not precise.

*Abaye* raises an objection to *Rabba*'s position from within its own framework. He says: *u-le-mar nami hai de-lo shavu shi'ureihu be-sukkah gedolah* — according to you it is true that in a large *sukkah*, air and *pasul schach* have different measures (three *tefachim* and four *amot* respectively) and therefore do not combine. But *be-sukkah ketanah mi lo shavu shi'ureihu* — in a minimum-size *sukkah* of seven by seven *tefachim*, don't their measures effectively become the same?

*Abaye*'s argument is as follows: in a minimum *sukkah*, four *amot* is simply not available — the entire *sukkah* is only seven *tefachim* across. What, then, is the operative measure for *pasul schach* in such a *sukkah*? *Rashi* explains in the relevant passage: *she-shi'urah metzumtzam mi lo shavu shi'ureihu* — since the *sukkah*'s measure is contracted, one must admit that three *tefachim* of *pasul schach* becomes problematic in a small *sukkah*. The reasoning: *keivan de-nafik mi-torat lavud, chashiv levatzurei le-shi'urei de-sukkah* — once the *pasul schach* exceeds the *lavud* threshold of three *tefachim*, it is significant enough to diminish the effective size of the *sukkah* below the minimum, since the entire *sukkah* is barely above minimum size to begin with. Therefore, in a small *sukkah*, both air and *pasul schach* become problematic at three *tefachim* — their measures coincide. And if there is a context in which they share a common measure, argues *Abaye*, this should provide a basis for combining them even in the large-*sukkah* case.

### Rava's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּשָׁווּ שִׁיעוּרַיְיהוּ לַהֲדָדֵי הוּא, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיתֵיהּ לְשִׁיעוּרָא דְסוּכָּה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to him: There,** in the case of the minimally-sized *sukka*, the fact that the *sukka* is unfit is **not due to** the fact **that their measures are equal. Rather,** it is **due to** the fact **that** in a case where the unfit roofing is three handbreadths, **the *sukka* lacks the** minimum required **measure** of fit roofing. In other words, it is not the amount of unfit roofing that creates the problem; rather, it is that the fit area of the *sukkah* is too small.

*Rabba* rejects the premise. *Amar lei hatam lav mishum de-shavu shi'ureihu le-hadadi hu — ella mishum de-leiteih* — the convergence at three *tefachim* in the small *sukkah* is not because three *tefachim* is the formal, principled *shiur* for *pasul schach* in that context. The formal *shiur* for *pasul schach* remains four *amot* even in a small *sukkah*. What happens in a small *sukkah* is simply a practical consequence: once you have three *tefachim* of *pasul schach*, that strip cannot be counted toward the *sukkah*'s required minimum area, and so the *sukkah* falls below *shi'ur* — but that is a side effect of size constraints, not an independent *din* of *pasul schach* being problematic at three *tefachim*. The measures of the two categories are therefore *never* formally equivalent; air is always three *tefachim* and *pasul schach* is always four *amot*, and they do not combine.

## Tumaot Analogy

### Material Minimums

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: הַבֶּגֶד שְׁלֹשָׁה עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה, הַשַּׂק אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה, הָעוֹר חֲמִשָּׁה עַל חֲמִשָּׁה, מַפָּץ שִׁשָּׁה עַל שִׁשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **And didn't we learn** in the mishna: **The garment** must be at least **three by three** handbreadths in order to become a primary source of ritual impurity by means of ritual impurity imparted by treading of a *zav*; **and the sackcloth** made from goats' hair must be at least **four by four** handbreadths; **and the** animal **hide** must be **five by five;** and **a mat** must be **six by six?**

Having established *Rabba*'s principle that different categories with different *shiurim* do not combine, the *Gemara* tests this principle against the laws of *tumah*. It cites a *mishna*: *ha-beged shelosha al shelosha* — a cloth garment becomes susceptible to *tumah* at a minimum size of three *tefachim* by three *tefachim*. *Ha-sak* — sackcloth — requires four *tefachim* by four *tefachim*. *Ha-or* — leather — requires five by five *tefachim*. *Mapatz* — a reed mat — requires six by six *tefachim*. These different materials, because of their varying quality and practical utility, each have their own minimum size below which they are considered insignificant and do not contract *tumah*.

### Stronger Fills Weaker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: הַבֶּגֶד וְהַשַּׂק, הַשַּׂק וְהָעוֹר, הָעוֹר וְהַמַּפָּץ — מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** a *baraita* **is taught concerning** the mishna: **The garment and the sackcloth, the sackcloth and the hide,** and **the hide and the mat join together with one another.** If one attaches a piece of material that has a smaller, more stringent measure for ritual impurity to a piece of material that has a larger, more lenient measure, the combined cloth is susceptible to contract ritual impurity if together they compose the larger measure. Apparently, two items whose measures are not equal combine to compose the more lenient measure.

A *braita* then teaches: if one combines contiguous materials from adjacent categories on this list — cloth with sackcloth, sackcloth with leather, or leather with a mat — *mitztarfin zeh im zeh* — they combine with each other. The combination is governed by the measure of the weaker (larger-*shiur*) material, but the stronger (smaller-*shiur*) material fills out whatever portion is needed. For example: three *tefachim* of sackcloth (just below its four-*tefachim* threshold) together with one *tefach* of cloth (cloth has a three-*tefachim* threshold, making it the "stronger" material in the sense that a smaller amount matters) — the combination meets the four-*tefachim* requirement for sackcloth *tumah*.

The parallel to the *sukkah* question is immediate. Air is the "stronger" invalidator, analogous to cloth (problematic at a smaller measure). *Pasul schach* is the "weaker" invalidator, analogous to sackcloth. Why, then, cannot air combine with *pasul schach* to meet the four-*amot* threshold — with the stronger air doing the work of the weaker *pasul schach*, just as the stronger cloth does the work of the weaker sackcloth?

### Common Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם — הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְטַמֵּא מוֹשָׁב. כְּדִתְנַן: הַמְקַצֵּעַ מִכּוּלָּן טֶפַח עַל טֶפַח — טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this. **There, it is as the reason is taught** that **Rabbi Shimon said: What is the reason** that these different fabrics combine? They combine **since** all the component materials **are fit to become ritually impure** through the ritual impurity imparted to **a seat** upon which a *zav* sits, as they can each be used to patch a saddle or saddlecloth. Since they are all suitable for the same use, they join together with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity.** As we learned** in a mishna: In the case of **one who trims** and processes a piece **of any of** the above-mentioned materials measuring one **handbreadth by** one **handbreadth,** that piece **is** capable of becoming **ritually impure.** There is a certain *halakha* for which each of the different materials has the same measure; they therefore join together in other areas as well.

The *Gemara* answers by citing the reason given by *Rabbi Shimon*: *hoil ve-raui le-tamei moshav* — these materials combine because they share a common application in one area of *tumah* law, namely the laws of a seat used by a *zav*. Specifically, a *Tanna* teaches: *ha-mekatzea mikullan tefach al tefach tamei* — if one cuts out a piece of any of these materials measuring one *tefach* by one *tefach*, it is *tamei*. As *Reish Lakish* explains in the name of *Rabbi Yannai*, this is because a one-*tefach* square from any of these materials is fit to serve as a patch on a saddle seat — *hoil ve-raui le-tala'i agav chamor*. In that specific application, all four materials share the same minimum size: one *tefach* by one *tefach*. This common measure in at least one halakhic context is what legitimizes treating them as sufficiently related to allow combination in other *tumah* contexts as well. The stronger material can fill out the measure of the weaker precisely because they share a common minimum somewhere in the legal landscape.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טֶפַח עַל טֶפַח לְמַאי חֲזֵי? וְאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַנַּאי: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי (לִיטְּלוֹ) עַל גַּבֵּי הַחֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **For what** use **is** a cloth that is one **handbreadth by** one **handbreadth fit?** After all, a rag that has no use does not contract ritual impurity. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said in the name of Rabbi Yannai: Since it is suitable for** use as **a patch on a donkey's** saddlecloth, it is capable of contracting ritual impurity. This ends the discussion of the exchange between Rabba and the Sages of the school of Rav.

### No Combination

Air and *pasul schach*, by contrast, share no common halakhic context in which they have the same measure. There is no application — not in the laws of *sukkah*, not elsewhere — where three *tefachim* of air and three *tefachim* of *pasul schach* are treated identically and both become significant at the same threshold. Without any common category or shared minimum, they remain fundamentally distinct, and the stronger one cannot fill out the measure of the weaker. Combination requires not just that both elements be capable of producing the same effect in principle, but that there exist some recognized halakhic context in which they share equivalent status.

*Abaye*'s attempt to create such a context — pointing to the small *sukkah* where both measures effectively converge at three *tefachim* — was rejected precisely on these grounds: that convergence is a practical side effect, not a formal equivalence in *din*. Since there is no context in which air and *pasul schach* share a formal common measure, they cannot combine to invalidate a *sukkah*.

## Version Differences

### Sura Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּסוּרָא אָמְרִי לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא בְּהַאי לִישָּׁנָא. בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא מַתְנוּ: אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: סְכָךְ פָּסוּל בָּאֶמְצַע — פּוֹסֵל בְּאַרְבָּעָה. מִן הַצַּד — פּוֹסֵל בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. וְרַב אָמַר: בֵּין מִן הַצַּד בֵּין בָּאֶמְצַע — בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **In Sura, they stated this *halakha* in that language** cited above. **In Neharde'a,** however, **they taught** it as follows: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Unfit roofing in the center** of the *sukka* **renders** the *sukka* **unfit with** a measure of **four** handbreadths of unfit roofing. **Along the side** of the *sukka*, **it renders** the *sukka* **unfit with** a measure of **four cubits** of unfit roofing. **And Rav said: Both along the side and in the center,** it renders the *sukka* unfit **with** a measure of **four cubits** of unfit roofing.

*Rav Chisda* notes that the entire foregoing discussion — *Rabba* finding the students of Rav's *yeshiva*, the back-and-forth about four *tefachim* and four *amot*, the challenge and counter-challenge — represents the version of this teaching as it circulated in *Sura*, the *yeshiva* of Rav himself. In this version, Rav and Shmuel together held the combined position: four *amot* from the edge by virtue of *dofen akuma*, four *tefachim* in the middle. That was the shared, unified position attributed to both Sages in Sura.

### Naharda'a Version

In *Naharda'a* — the *yeshiva* of Shmuel — the tradition was different. There, the teaching was transmitted as *amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: Shmuel holds that *sechach pasul be-emtza posel be-arba'ah* — *pasul schach* in the middle invalidates at four *tefachim* — while *min hatzad posel be-arba amot* — from the side it invalidates at four *amot* (the *dofen akuma* rule). And in this version, *Rav* takes a different position: *bein min hatzad bein be-emtza be-arba amot* — whether at the edge or in the middle, the threshold is four *amot*. This is precisely the position that in the Sura version was *Rabba*'s challenge to the yeshiva students. In the Naharda'a version, it is Rav's own stated position, and the *machloket* is between Rav and Shmuel directly.

### Chananel's Girsa

*Rabbeinu Chananel* preserves a *girsa* — a manuscript tradition — that differs from the standard text in one significant detail. His version reads: *be-Sura matnu hachi amrin* — in Sura they taught as we have above, followed by the joint formulation and the ruling *ve-chen hilchata*. But where our printed text says the alternative version was taught in *Naharda'a*, *Rabbeinu Chananel* writes *u-ve-Pumbedita matnu* — it was in *Pumbedita* that they taught the version attributed to Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel.

This alternative reading is illuminating. *Pumbedita* was the *yeshiva* of *Rav Yehuda* — himself a student of both Rav and Shmuel. The teaching *Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* would thus have been taught in Rav Yehuda's own *yeshiva* in Pumbedita, linking the transmission to Shmuel through Rav Yehuda. In our standard text, the same teaching is located in Naharda'a — Shmuel's city — which also makes sense, given that the ruling is transmitted in Shmuel's name. The tradition in *Rabbeinu Chananel*'s text bridges the two *yeshivot*: the teaching *Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* connects Pumbedita (Rav Yehuda) with Naharda'a (Shmuel), and either location could serve as its point of transmission.

In either version of the *girsa*, the substantive conclusion is the same. In Rav's *yeshiva* (Sura), the single combined position of Rav and Shmuel was: four *amot* on the side, four *tefachim* in the middle. In the Shmuel/Rav Yehuda tradition (Naharda'a or Pumbedita), this is a debate: Shmuel holds four *amot* on the side and four *tefachim* in the middle, while Rav holds four *amot* in both cases.
