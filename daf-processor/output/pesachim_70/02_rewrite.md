# Pesachim 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prep Acts Debate

### Tumah Recap

Today's *daf* is 70. We pick up on 69b, just before the *mishnah* of *klal amar Rabbi Akiva*. The previous discussion had centered on the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua concerning whether *de'oraita* acts — and even *derabbanan* acts — that could have been performed the day before, yet now stand as obstacles to the *Korban Pesach*, may be carried out on Shabbat. Rabbi Eliezer permits this, both for *Pesach* and for *milah*, while Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Akiva do not.

The *Gemara* had just concluded by noting that even according to Rabbi Eliezer's tradition — which he himself had forgotten — *haza'ah*, the sprinkling of the ashes of the *Parah Adumah*, was the one act he would not permit. Two reasons had been offered: the first, ultimately rejected, was that the *tamei* person has fallen so far outside the category of obligation that he cannot perform acts necessary to become obligated. The second, and more striking, was the radical possibility that a person who is *tamei* for only a single day — and who will therefore become *tahor* by evening — can have a *Korban Pesach* brought on his behalf even if he does not ultimately eat it. His obligation of bringing the *korban* is thereby fulfilled even in his *tamei* state, and there is consequently no warrant to perform any violation on his behalf. This is a quite fascinating and radical conclusion: that someone who theoretically could be *tahor* that evening can be *yotzei* through the *Pesach* being brought for him, even if he does not in the end become *tahor* and does not eat it.

### Akiva's Principle

We now turn to the opposing opinion and the question of *p'sak*. The *mishnah* states: *klal amar Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva articulated a general principle: anything that could have been done the day before cannot override Shabbat today. It should be emphasized that *haza'ah*, while it technically cannot be performed the day before, is nevertheless not mandated to be done specifically today. It is therefore not merely the question of whether something could have been done earlier; the operative principle is whether there is an affirmative mandate to perform it today. Since no such mandate exists for *haza'ah*, it does not override Shabbat even if it is an obstacle to bringing the *korban*.

The same debate is recorded in the context of *milah*. Rabbi Akiva states there as well: *kol melachah she'efshar la'asotah me'erev shabbat einah dochah et hashabbat* — any act that could have been performed before Shabbat does not override Shabbat. Even if, for example, no *mohel* was available and one became available only on Shabbat in a neighboring city, the fact that the *milah* does not carry a mandate to be performed specifically today means that traveling to fetch him — even though it prevents the *milah* — may not be done on Shabbat. On the other hand, *milah she-iy efshar la'asotah me'erev shabbat* — a circumcision that could not have been performed before Shabbat and that is mandated to be performed today — does override Shabbat.

### Rav's Ruling

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav ruled, on 69b, *halachah k'Rabbi Akiva* — we rule like Rabbi Akiva. One may perform the act of *milah* or the slaughter of the *Pesach* on Shabbat, but not the preparatory acts that serve as obstacles to those acts.

### Two Rulings Needed

*V'tzricha*: it was necessary to state this ruling in both contexts. Had the ruling been stated only for *Pesach*, one might have said that it was only there that Rabbi Akiva applied his principle, since preparatory acts do not override Shabbat — *mishum d'lo nichretu aleha shelosh-esrei britot* — because *Pesach*, for all its stringencies, is not the subject of a *brit*, a covenant, repeated thirteen times, as is the case with *brit milah*. Perhaps *milah*, with its thirteen covenants sealed upon it, carries greater weight and would permit a broader override. Conversely, had the ruling been stated only for *milah*, one might have inferred that it was only there that preparatory acts are restricted, since there is no *karet* for failing to perform *milah* specifically today — whereas *Pesach* carries *karet* for non-performance today, and one might therefore have thought it should override. Hence both rulings are necessary.

### 13 Covenants

The argument that thirteen covenants ought to carry halachic weight — to the point of determining whether one may override Shabbat — might seem more in the realm of *aggadata* than of technical *halachah*. Indeed, Tosafot explains that what is at stake is the *derabbanan* dimension of the override, where there is somewhat more discretion as to whether we permit it or not. On that level, the additional weight of thirteen covenants might have tipped the balance differently for *milah* than for *Pesach*. Tosafot therefore understands these considerations as bearing on the *derabbanan* question and not on overriding a *de'oraita* prohibition. The word *brit* appears thirteen times in the *parashah* of the covenant with Abraham — *ve'et brisi tishmor*, *zot brisi asher tishmeru*, *vehayesah le'os bris beini uveineichem* — and if one counts them, indeed thirteen instances of the word appear, underscoring the depth of that covenant.

## Chagigah Overview

### Chagigah Definition

Having concluded the discussion of preparatory acts, the *Gemara* turns to other aspects of bringing the *Korban Pesach*, and specifically to the *chagigah*. There is ordinarily a *chagigah* brought on *Yom Tov*. When the Torah states *vechagosem oto chag la'Hashem shivat yamim bashana*, the word *chag* according to the Sages does not simply mean to celebrate: it refers to a *korban* classified as a *Korban Chagigah*, which is a *shelamim* specifically designated as such. This is how one fulfills *vechagosem oto chag* — by bringing a *Korban Chagigah*. Ordinarily it should be brought on the first day of *Yom Tov*, but if not brought then, it may be brought on any of the remaining days of the festival.

### Chagigat 14th

By *Pesach*, however, there is a specific institution known as *chagigat arba'ah asar* — a *Korban Chagigah* brought on *Erev Pesach*, the fourteenth of Nisan. As we shall see in the *Gemara*, this is derived from the verse *zevach chag hapesach* — a *zevach* of the *chag* accompanies the *Pesach*. The word *chag* in the idiom of the Sages denotes a *Korban Chagigah* that comes together with the *Korban Pesach*.

### Al HaSova

There is a debate in the *Gemara* as to whether there is a genuine obligation to bring this *chagigah* at all. According to some, it is not strictly obligatory; it is brought in order to fulfill the concept that the *Pesach* must be eaten *al hasova* — while one is already satiated. This connects to the institution of the *afikoman*, which for some authorities is the eating that most directly represents the *Korban Pesach*: not the earlier eating of *matzah*, which fulfills *mitzvas matzah*, but the eating that comes at the end of the *seder*, after one is already full. How does one become full? There may not be sufficient meat from the *Pesach* itself, depending on the size of the group relative to the animal. One therefore eats a substantial meal first — the meat of the *Korban Chagigah* — and then eats the *Pesach* at the end, *al hasova*, when already sated. According to this view, there is no strict obligation to bring the *chagigah*; it is appropriate when and if necessary so that the *Korban Pesach* is eaten *al hasova*.

Other opinions hold that there is an independent *chiyuv* to bring the *chagigah* regardless of these considerations. We will see that debate unfold through the *pesukim* and their interpretation.

## Mishnah Parameters

### When Brought

The *mishnah* asks: *eimasai mevi'in chagigah imo* — when does one bring a *chagigah* together with the *Korban Pesach*? The *mishnah* specifies conditions, reflecting the view that it is not strictly obligatory:

*Bizman shehu ba bechol* — when the *Pesach* falls on a weekday; *betahara* — when the people are in a state of *taharah*; and *u'b'muat* — when the portion of meat from the *Pesach* is small relative to the number of people in the group. It is worth noting that "small" here means a small amount of meat per person — a large group sharing a single animal — and not a small group. In that case, the meat of the *chagigah* is needed to supplement what each person receives from the *Pesach*.

### When Not Brought

Correspondingly, the *mishnah* states that when the *Pesach* falls on Shabbat, or when the portions are large, or when the people are *tamei*, one does not bring the *chagigah*. Just as *Pesach* overrides Shabbat, and similarly overrides *tumah*, the *chagigah* overrides neither. The ordinary *halachah* is that a *korban* is not brought in a state of *tumah*, and so if the community is *tamei* and brings the *Pesach* in *tumah*, the *chagigah* does not follow. Any one of these conditions — Shabbat, *tumah*, or a large portion of meat — is sufficient to preclude bringing the *chagigah*.

### Chagigah Properties

The *mishnah* continues to distinguish the *chagigah* from the *Pesach* in its very nature. The *chagigah* may come *min hatzon* from sheep, or *min habakar* from cattle — cattle being a more practical option for feeding a large group. Furthermore, it may come *min hazacharim u'min hanekevot* — from both males and females — which is explicitly not the case for the *Korban Pesach*, which is restricted to males.

### Eating Rules

The *chagigah* is eaten *ne'echelet l'shnei yamim v'layla echad* — for two days and the intervening night, like a standard *shelamim*. This contrasts sharply with the *Korban Pesach*, which must be consumed entirely that night, as derived from the verse *v'lo yalin min habasar* — none of the flesh shall remain until morning. As we shall see, there is an opinion that departs sharply from all of this.

## Why Discuss

### Context

The *Gemara* asks: *man tanna, mai ta'ama d'katani chagigah* — what is the *tanna*'s context for discussing the *chagigah* here? The *mishnah* plunges directly into a discussion of when the *chagigah* is and is not brought, without first establishing that such a *chagigah* exists at all. The answer offered by the *Gemara* is that the preceding discussion established that certain preparatory acts do not override Shabbat. The *mishnah* therefore transitions naturally to another item that also does not override Shabbat — the *chagigah* — and that is how one discussion led to the other. The *mishnah* is, in effect, parenthetically noting: there is also a *chagigah* that does not override Shabbat, and here are the conditions under which it is brought.

### Not Obligatory

Rav Ashi draws a significant conclusion from the structure of the *mishnah*: *shma mina* — we learn from this that *chagigat arba'ah asar lav chovah hi* — the *chagigah* of the fourteenth is not a strict obligation. It is brought to serve the function of *ne'echal al hasova*, but there is no absolute requirement to bring it. The proof is decisive: *d'ee salka da'atach chovah hi* — if you thought it were a strict obligation, *teitei b'Shabbat* — it should be brought on Shabbat, as any *korban* with a fixed time overrides Shabbat. *V'teitei b'merubeh* — it should be brought even when the portions are large, since an obligation is an obligation. *V'teitei b'tumah* — it should be brought even in a state of *tumah*, as any *korban* with a fixed time and a strict obligation is brought even in *tumah*. Since all of these exceptions apply, the *chagigah* clearly is not a strict obligation.

The *Gemara* then asks: granted that it is optional and therefore does not come on Shabbat or in *tumah* — but what is the connection to the amount of meat? The answer comes from a *braisa*: *chagigas haba im hapesach ne'echeles techila kidei sheyehei hapesach ne'echeles al hasova* — the *chagigah* that accompanies the *Pesach* is eaten first, so that the *Pesach* is eaten while one is already full. The *Gemara* understands this *braisa* as revealing the entire rationale for bringing the *chagigah*: it is brought not because of an independent obligation but solely so that the *Korban Pesach* is eaten *al hasova*.

## Chagigah Overview…

### Full Eating

One might ask: if the goal is simply to be full when eating the *Pesach*, why not simply bring sandwiches? Why must this be done in the context of a *korban* at all? The answer is that there is indeed some concept of a *chagigah*, but not a strictly obligatory one — it occupies a middle ground where the form of a *korban* is appropriate, but no absolute mandate exists.

Two reasons are given for the requirement of eating the *Pesach al hasova*. The first is symbolic: one should not eat the *Pesach* in a state of ravenous hunger, as though eating simply to satisfy one's appetite. The *Pesach* is eaten for its symbolic and religious significance. There is an interesting balance here — one is not supposed to eat excessively before *Erev Pesach* and must approach the *seder* with some appetite, yet one must not arrive at the *Korban Pesach* famished. The *Pesach* is eaten with desire but not desperation.

The second reason is offered in the *Yerushalmi* and endorsed by Tosafot as a genuinely rabbinic concern: there is a prohibition against breaking the bones of the *Korban Pesach*. If a person is truly hungry while eating the *Pesach*, the temptation to crack the bones and suck out the marrow becomes very real. One must therefore arrive at the *Pesach* already sated, so that there is no *yetzer hara* to break the bones — a far more technical, practical concern. Both reasons reflect the importance of the *chagigah*'s role, even within the framework of a non-obligatory *korban*.

## Ben Teima View

### Identical to Pesach

Having established the position of our *mishnah* — which treats the *chagigah* as independent from and considerably more lenient than the *Korban Pesach* — the *Gemara* announces: *matnisin delo ke'Ben Teima* — our *mishnah* does not follow Ben Teima.

*Detanya*: Ben Teima says in a *braisa* that *chagigah haba'ah im hapesach harei hi ke'Pesach* — the *chagigah* that accompanies the *Pesach* is treated exactly like the *Pesach* itself. It may be eaten only *leyom velailah* — the day and the following night — and may not be eaten the next morning. There is a subtle question worth noting here: the *Korban Pesach* itself is brought on the fourteenth but not eaten until the night of the fifteenth. If so, when Ben Teima says *yom velailah*, does he mean the night of the fifteenth only, or might the *chagigah* actually be eaten beginning on the fourteenth? That question deserves attention and was not resolved here.

Ben Teima continues: *vachagigas chamisha asar ne'echeles lishnei yamim velayla echad* — the *chagigah* of the fifteenth, the standard *Yom Tov chagigah*, is eaten for two days and the intervening night, as normal. And regarding the *chagigah* of the fourteenth: *yotzei bah mishum simcha ve'ein yotzei bah mishum chagigah* — one fulfills one's obligation of *simcha* through it, but not one's obligation of the standard *chagigah* of the festival.

The meaning of this last clause requires clarification, and the *Gemara* understands it differently from what might seem to be the straightforward reading. One might have thought Ben Teima is saying that eating the *chagigah* meat on the *leil haseder* satisfies *mitzvas simcha* but cannot substitute for the *chagigah* of the fifteenth. The *Gemara*, however, understands the case differently: the animal was sanctified for *chagigat arba'ah asar* but was not brought on the fourteenth. The owner now wishes to bring it on the fifteenth. In that case, it may function as *shalmei simcha* — animals sanctified for one purpose may be brought on *Yom Tov* and used to fulfill *mitzvas simcha* — but it cannot fulfill the obligation of *chagigat hachag*, which requires an animal sanctified specifically for that purpose, not one previously designated for another *korban*.

### Baraita Proof

What is Ben Teima's reasoning? *Ki dematni Rav Chiyya bar Abba* — as Rav taught his son Chiyya: the verse states *lo yalin laboker zevach chag hapesach* — the offering of the *chag* of the *Pesach* shall not remain until morning. The verse contains both the word *chag* and the word *pesach*: as Dov correctly noted, *zevach chag* refers to the *chagigah*, and the Torah instructs that this too — like the *Pesach* itself — may not remain until morning. Ben Teima thus learns from this verse that the *chagigah haba'ah im hapesach* is in all respects like the *Pesach*: it is a strict obligation and may be eaten only that night.

### Questions

The *Gemara* then poses a series of questions: according to Ben Teima, can the *chagigah* come from cattle, or only from sheep? Can it come from female animals? Can it be older than one year, as the *Pesach* is limited to its first year of life?

### Roasted Only

The *Gemara* addresses the question of roasting. It asks: *le'Ben Teima ne'echelet tzali o ne'echelet tzali* — must the *chagigah* be eaten specifically roasted, as the *Pesach* must be? Perhaps the verse connects it to the *Pesach* only for the *halachah* of not remaining until morning — that being related to the act of eating — but not for the manner of preparation. Or perhaps *chag hapesach* means the *chag* and the *Pesach* are entirely assimilated, and all the *halachot* of the *Pesach* apply. *Ta shema*: the *mishnah* elsewhere records among the four questions the phrase *halailah hazeh kulo tzali* — on this night everything is roasted. Rav Chisda explains: *zu divrei Ben Teima* — this question is formulated according to Ben Teima's view. This serves as a clear proof that according to Ben Teima the *chagigah* must also be eaten roasted.

### Full Requirements

A *braisa* settles the broader questions decisively: *chagigah haba'ah im hapesach harei hi ke'Pesach* — the *chagigah* is entirely like the *Pesach*. *Ba'ah min hatzon ve'eina ba'ah min habakar* — it comes from sheep and not from cattle, just like the *Pesach*. *Ba'ah min hazacharim ve'eina ba'ah min hanekevot* — from males and not females. *Ba'ah bas shnatah ve'eina ba'ah bas shtei shanim* — it must be within its first year and cannot be in its second year. *Ve'eina ne'echelet ela leyom velailah* — it may be eaten only day and night. *Ve'eina ne'echelet ela tzli* — it may only be eaten roasted. *Ve'eina ne'echelet ela leminuyav* — it may only be eaten by those who have been formally enrolled in its group, exactly as one must be enrolled for the *Korban Pesach*.

The *Gemara* notes: *man shamsat leh d'is leh hai sevara* — who holds this entire position? Ben Teima. *Shma mina kulhu milamdinan* — and we see that Ben Teima takes the equation to its logical extreme: the *chagigah* must be identical to the *Pesach* in every particular.

This raises a striking question. If the *chagigah* must come from sheep, must be male, must be under a year old, must be eaten roasted, and must be eaten by enrolled members — how does one even distinguish experientially between the *chagigah* and the *Pesach*? Both animals look identical. One is brought before the other, but practically speaking, the only distinguishing features at the table would be the *matzah* and *maror* that accompany the *Pesach*. One might further ask: when is the *chagigah* slaughtered relative to the afternoon *Korban Tamid*? What is done with its blood? Does one group formation suffice for both, or must each have a separate enrollment? These questions, while not explicitly addressed in the *Gemara*, underscore just how far-reaching and in some ways challenging Ben Teima's position is in practice.

One could argue the opposite: rather than undermining the uniqueness of the *Pesach*, the identity of the *chagigah* with it serves to transform *Erev Pesach* into a kind of miniature *chag* — *chag haPesach* — emphasizing the festive dimension of the fourteenth itself. Already there are aspects of *Erev Pesach* that mark it as semi-sacred: restrictions on labor from midday, prohibition of *chametz* from midday, and according to Beit Shammai's view, even from the night before. The *chagigah* as an obligation identical to the *Pesach* fits naturally into this picture. But making the two animals practically indistinguishable pushes the equation further than one might expect.

## Bone-Breaking

### Bones Question

The *Gemara* now asks whether, according to Ben Teima, there is a prohibition of *sheviras etzem* — breaking bones — that applies to the *chagigah* as well. The verse states *kol etzem lo tishberu bo* — you shall not break any bone *bo*, in it, with the word *bo* being very specific. Perhaps *bo* limits the prohibition to the *Pesach* itself, not to the *chagigah*. Or perhaps *bo* means only that the prohibition applies to a valid *korban* and not to a *pasul* one — in which case it would apply equally to the *chagigah*.

### Knife/Cleaver

A *mishnah* is cited to explore this question. If one finds a *shechita* knife on the fourteenth of Nisan, one may use it immediately — one can assume it is *tahor*. The reasoning: in Jerusalem on *Erev Pesach*, everyone is preparing their *Korban Pesach* and has already immersed their utensils. Since a *kli* immersed in a *mikveh* requires nightfall to become fully *tahor*, anyone who needed a knife for *shechita* on the fourteenth would have immersed it on the thirteenth. Therefore a knife found on the fourteenth may be assumed to have been immersed the previous day and is now *tahor*.

If the knife is found on the thirteenth, however, one must immerse it again — or at least ensure it has been immersed — since the owner may not yet have done so, or may have dropped it on the way to the *mikveh*.

A *kofit* — a meat cleaver — is treated differently. Whether found on the thirteenth or the fourteenth, one must immerse it. Why? Because one does not need a cleaver for the *Korban Pesach* — one may not break bones, and the cleaver's function is to chop through meat and bone. At most, one would need a cleaver starting on the fifteenth of Nisan, when other *korbanot* are brought. A person planning to use the cleaver on *Yom Tov* would only need to immerse it by the fourteenth — not earlier — so finding it on the fourteenth provides no assurance that it has been immersed.

### Cleaver Use

The *Gemara* now asks: *mani* — whose position is this *mishnah* reflecting? If it reflects the view of the Rabbis — *rabanan* — then why do they not assume that the cleaver has been immersed because the owner would need it today for his *chagigah*? One can chop the meat of the *chagigah* with a cleaver even if one cannot use it on the *Pesach*! If so, by the reasoning that everyone prepares their slaughtering knife the day before, everyone should also have prepared their cleaver the day before for use on their *chagigah*. The only one who would have no need for a cleaver on the fourteenth would be Ben Teima — because according to Ben Teima, one cannot break the bones of the *chagigah* either, making the cleaver useless on the fourteenth just as it is useless for the *Pesach*. This appears to yield a compelling proof: *shma mina yesh bah mishum sheviras etzem* — even the *chagigah* is subject to the prohibition of breaking bones according to Ben Teima.

### Resolution

The *Gemara* resists this conclusion and attempts alternative explanations. *Le'olam rabanan* — perhaps the *mishnah* follows the Rabbis after all — and the case is one where the *Pesach* falls on Shabbat, when there is no *chagigah* brought at all. Since there is no *chagigah*, there is no reason anyone would have needed the cleaver on the fourteenth, and so one cannot assume it has been immersed.

The *Gemara* objects: this cannot be the case in the first half of the *mishnah*, because the end of the *mishnah* teaches explicitly: *chal arba'ah asar* — if the fourteenth falls on Shabbat, one may use both the knife and the cleaver immediately. Why? Because when *Erev Pesach* is Shabbat, *Erev Yom Tov* was Friday, and one cannot immerse a *kli* on Shabbat. To use the cleaver on *Yom Tov* one would have had to immerse it on Thursday. Therefore anything found on Shabbat the fourteenth has certainly already been immersed, and both knife and cleaver may be used immediately.

The *Gemara* then attempts: perhaps the case involves *sheba bimrubeh* — a group with sufficient meat, meaning the household has no need for a *chagigah* at all. But the *Gemara* challenges this too: one never knows in advance how large one's group will be. The membership of a *Pesach* group may be changed until the last moment. Therefore one could never be sure in advance whether a *chagigah* would be needed, and one would presumably always bring the cleaver to the *mikveh* just in case. Even people with small groups might find at the last minute that additional members join, and so everyone would have prepared their cleaver.

The *Gemara* then tries: *sheba betumah* — perhaps everyone became *tamei*, eliminating the need for a *chagigah*. But this raises the question: if you knew everyone was going to be *tamei*, why did you bother immersing the *shechita* knife? And if you immersed the knife because you did not yet know, why did you not also immerse the cleaver for the same reason?

The *Gemara* introduces a complex scenario: *demet nasi* — the *nasi* died, causing mass *tumah* as everyone was obligated to attend his burial. When did he die? If on the thirteenth, the knife question dissolves — you would have known you were going to become *tamei* and would not have immersed anything. If on the fourteenth, you were already immersing on the thirteenth, before his death, so again you should have treated knife and cleaver equally.

The *Gemara* resolves this with the scenario of *nasi goseis b'shlosha asar* — the *nasi* was dying on the thirteenth, near death but not yet deceased. In that case: the knife involves only one *safek* — perhaps he will die, perhaps not. Given this single doubt, you prudently immerse the knife in case he recovers and you bring the *Pesach* in *taharah*. The cleaver, however, involves *terei safeika* — a double doubt: maybe the *nasi* will die and there will be no *chagigah* due to *tumah*, and even if he does not die, perhaps you will have a large enough portion of meat that you will not need a *chagigah* anyway. Given this compound uncertainty, you do not bother immersing the cleaver.

This is an intricate resolution, and while it does explain the distinction between knife and cleaver without relying on Ben Teima's prohibition of bone-breaking, it must be acknowledged that the initial and simpler reading of the *mishnah* seemed to support the conclusion that even the *chagigah* is subject to *sheviras etzem* according to Ben Teima.

## Ben Dortai

### Complaint

The *Gemara* now introduces a new figure and a striking episode. *Yehuda ben Dortai* separated himself from the Rabbis — he and his son Dortai went to live in the south, away from Jerusalem. He did not want to be in Jerusalem when the *Korban Pesach* was being brought in a year when *Erev Pesach* fell on Shabbat. His reason: *amar — im yavo Eliyahu ve-yomar lahem l'Yisrael: mipnei mah lo chagagtem chagigah be-Shabbat* — he said: if Elijah the Prophet were to come and ask the Jewish people why they did not bring their *chagigah* on Shabbat, what would they answer him?

Ben Dortai's position was that the *chagigah* must be brought even on Shabbat, and he was scandalized that it was not. He declares: *tameihani al shnei gedolei ha-dor, Shemaya ve-Avtalyon* — I am astounded by the two great leaders of the generation, Shemaya and Avtalyon, who are great sages and great expositors of Scripture, *ve-lo amru lahem l'Yisrael: chagigah docha et ha-Shabbat* — and they did not tell the Jewish people that the *chagigah* overrides Shabbat. This is particularly striking in context: it was Shemaya and Avtalyon who were the source of the teaching that the *Pesach* overrides Shabbat — the very ruling that Hillel invoked. Ben Dortai is saying that in his view that principle should have been extended to the *chagigah* as well.

### Rav Defends

*Amar Rav: mai ta'ama d'Ben Dortai* — Rav explains his reasoning. The verse states *ve-zavachta Pesach la-Hashem Elokecha tzon u-vakar* — you shall slaughter the *Pesach* for God, sheep and cattle. But the *Pesach* does not come from cattle — it comes only from *kevasim* and *izim*, sheep and goats. Ben Dortai therefore reads the verse as follows: *tzon zeh Pesach, bakar zo chagigah* — the sheep refers to the *Pesach*, the cattle refers to the *chagigah*. The Torah thus presents both together under the same verb *ve-zavachta*, implying that the *chagigah* carries the same obligation as the *Pesach* — including the requirement to bring it on its fixed time, even on Shabbat.

### Ashi Objects

Rav Ashi objects sharply: *anan ta'ama d'perushim nekum u-nefaresh* — are we going to stand up and expound Scripture according to these separatists? There is no obligation on our part to defend or explain a reading advanced by those who broke from the rabbinic tradition. The word play here is pointed: the same root *p-r-sh* that means "to expound" also means "to separate," and Ben Dortai's group had separated themselves — *peireish* — from the community. Rav Ashi refuses to engage with the exegesis of *perushim* who are themselves *mafrish* themselves from the fold.

There is also a broader intertextual resonance that should not be missed. Ben Dortai went south of Jerusalem and separated himself. The word *perushim* — those who separate — recalls the Pharisees, but the act of going south to form a separate community recalls other known sectarian movements of the period. It would be very interesting to examine whether the Essene community — whose solar calendar was engineered so that the fifteenth of Nisan always fell on Wednesday — had any position on whether a *chagigah* accompanied the *Pesach* or whether it overrode Shabbat. The connection was not fully explored here, but it is a suggestive line of inquiry, particularly given the loaded use of *perushim* and *nefaresh* in Rav Ashi's formulation. Rashi himself notes the rhetorical weight of this phrase: *chazei heichi ka'amar — itztarich l'midrash mikra k'da'ateihu d'hanech muvdalin* — see how he phrases it: must we expound the verse according to the view of those who have separated themselves?

## Surplus Money

### Money Use

*Ella kra* — what then does the verse *ve-zavachta Pesach la-Hashem Elokecha tzon u-vakar* actually teach? The *Gemara* answers: *kra l'chi'd'Rav Nachman*. *D'amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha*: the verse teaches what happens to surplus *Pesach* money. If one designated money for a *Korban Pesach* and ultimately used only part of it, what is done with the remainder? From the verse — which mentions cattle alongside the *Pesach*, yet the *Pesach* cannot come from cattle — we derive: *motar ha-Pesach yehei l'davar ha-ba min ha-tzon u-min ha-bakar* — the surplus money may be used to purchase a *shelamim*, which may come from either sheep or cattle, without restriction as to sex or species. The mention of cattle in the verse is thus not a reference to the *chagigah* at all, but a source for the law of surplus *Pesach* funds.

## Why No Override

### Core Question

The *Gemara* now takes up the fundamental question: *l'rabanan mai ta'ama lo dachi Shabbat* — according to the Rabbis, why does the *chagigah* not override Shabbat? Even setting aside Ben Teima's extreme position — even if one does not require the *chagigah* to be sheep, or roasted, or restricted to enrolled members — if the *chagigah* is an obligation fixed to the fourteenth, any fixed-time obligation should override Shabbat. What is the Rabbis' reasoning?

### Corporate Answer

One answer offered is: *chovat korban tzibbur hu* — it is a corporate communal obligation. This framing requires some care. The *chagigah* is not brought by the community as a single entity in the way that, say, the daily *Tamid* is. Every individual brings his own. Yet the *Gemara* applies the terminology *korban tzibbur* when all Israel brings something simultaneously. More importantly, as Tosafot notes, this framing does not actually explain the exemption from overriding Shabbat. The real question is not whether it is a *korban tzibbur* or *korban yachid* — there are cases of individual *korbanot* that do override Shabbat because they have a fixed time. The operative criterion is whether the *korban* must be brought specifically today. If it is a strict obligation with a fixed time — *bemo'ado* — it overrides Shabbat regardless.

### Seven Days Proof

*Amar Rav Ulla mishum Rabbi Yehuda ben Safra*: a verse-based proof is offered. The Torah states *ve-chagosem oto chag la-Shem shivat yamim bashana* — you shall celebrate it as a *chag* for seven days. This verse, which pertains to the *chagigah* of *Sukkot*, specifies seven days. But the full festival has eight days — *shmona havuh*! Why then does the verse say seven? *Ela mikan la-chagigah she'einah dochah et ha-Shabbat* — from here we derive that the *chagigah* does not override Shabbat: one of the eight days will inevitably be Shabbat, during which the *chagigah* is not brought, leaving seven days available for it.

### Tosafot Caveat

Tosafot raises a critical qualification: this proof establishes only that the *chagigah* of *Yom Tov* — the *chagigah* of the fifteenth onward — does not override Shabbat, precisely because there are other days on which to bring it. The fact that the Rabbis gave you seven days instead of requiring you to bring it on a specific single day is itself the reason it does not override Shabbat. But this proof says nothing about *chagigat arba'ah asar*, which has no such flexibility — it must be brought specifically on the fourteenth. Tosafot therefore suggests that the separatist Ben Dortai was not even arguing about *chagigat arba'ah asar*; he was arguing that even the *chagigah* of *Yom Tov* overrides Shabbat — which is why the Rabbis were so indignant. Ben Dortai's position, on Tosafot's reading, was the obviously untenable one that the *Yom Tov chagigah* overrides Shabbat even when it can be brought on other days.

### Ravin Challenge

*Ki ata Ravin amar*: when Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael he reported having posed a challenge to his teachers. You say the *chagigah* may be brought over seven days and not eight because Shabbat is excluded — but sometimes there are only six available days: *kegon she-chal Yom Tov rishon shel chag be-Shabbat* — if the first day of *Sukkot* falls on Shabbat, then the last day of *Sukkot* also falls on Shabbat, leaving only six days on which the *chagigah* may be brought.

### Abaye Answer

*Amar Abaye* — and Abaye did not receive this objection kindly, addressing Ravin with the phrase *avin tichla*, a pointed expression — you will never find eight days on which to bring the *chagigah*. *Shivah ita be-rov shanim* — in most years you will have seven days. The Torah is not stating the absolute minimum; it is stating the standard. Seven days is the ordinary situation; occasionally there will only be six, and so be it. The verse reflects the norm.

### Conclusion

The conclusion of this *Gemara* is therefore as follows. The *chagigah* of *Yom Tov* does not override Shabbat because there are other available days on which to bring it — the seven-day proof. Whether this reasoning applies equally to *chagigat arba'ah asar*, which has no such alternative days, is left with Tosafot's caveat. But the *Gemara* at the outset, immediately after the *mishnah*, already established the most direct answer: the *chagigah* does not override Shabbat because it is not a strict obligation — *lav chovah hi*. It exists solely to ensure that the *Korban Pesach* is eaten *al hasova*, and a *korban* that is not strictly obligated does not override Shabbat or *tumah*. The discussion of *korban tzibbur* and the seven-day verse are additional frameworks, but the foundational answer is the one the *Gemara* introduced first: the *chagigah* is not strictly obligatory, and therefore it yields to Shabbat.
