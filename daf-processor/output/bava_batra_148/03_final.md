# Bava Batra 148 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Matnat Shchiv Mera Intro

### De-Oraisa Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן הָכִי?! וְהָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמּוֹכֵר שְׁטַר חוֹב לַחֲבֵירוֹ, וְחָזַר וּמְחָלוֹ – מָחוּל; וַאֲפִילּוּ יוֹרֵשׁ מוֹחֵל; מוֹדֶה שְׁמוּאֵל שֶׁאִם נְתָנוֹ בְּמַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע, דְּאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְמוֹחְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rav Naḥman** actually **say this? But doesn't Rav Naḥman say: Even though Shmuel says** that with regard to **one who sells a promissory note to another and then forgives** the debt, the debt **is forgiven** and the note is nullified, **and even** the **heir** of the creditor can **forgive** the debt, **Shmuel concedes that if** the creditor **gave** the promissory note **as the gift of a person on his deathbed,** his heir **cannot forgive** the debt?

The daf opens in the midst of a fundamental debate about the nature of *matnat shchiv mera*: is it *de-oraisa* or *de-rabbanan*? Tosafos clarifies that when the Gemara uses the term *de-oraisa* in this context, it may not mean strictly biblical in origin — and yet the Gemara's own framing seems to pull in that direction.

### Shmuel Debt Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִמְחוֹל. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּרַבָּנַן הִיא, אַמַּאי אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִמְחוֹל? אֵינָהּ שֶׁל תּוֹרָה, וַעֲשָׂאוּהָ כְּשֶׁל תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that the gift of a person on his deathbed is valid **by Torah law** without an act of acquisition, **due to that** reason the heir **cannot forgive** the debt, as the debt was acquired by another. **But if you say** that **it** is valid **by rabbinic law, why** is the heir **unable to forgive** the debt? The Gemara replies: Rav Naḥman maintains that although this gift **is not** effective **by Torah law,** nevertheless, the Sages **made it** a *halakha* **with** the force **of Torah law.**

The Gemara raises Shmuel's ruling on debt transfer as the crux of the difficulty. Shmuel holds that if the original creditor sells a *shtar chov*, he retains the ability to annul the debt through *mechila*. However, if he transferred the *shtar chov* via *matnat shchiv mera*, he loses that power of annulment. The Gemara presses: does this not indicate that *matnat shchiv mera* carries *de-oraisa* weight? If it were merely rabbinic, how could it operate with greater finality than a standard sale?

### Yerusha Model Foundation

The more satisfying explanation — and the one the discussion gravitates toward — is that the argument for *de-oraisa* is really an argument about the conceptual model underlying *matnat shchiv mera*. If *matnat shchiv mera* operates on the model of *kinyan*, the standard mechanisms of transaction apply: just as a creditor can annul a debt transferred by ordinary sale, so too by *matnat shchiv mera*. But if *matnat shchiv mera* is modeled after *yerusha* — inheritance — then the recipient stands in the position of an heir. And just as a son who inherits a debt owed to his father cannot have that debt annulled by the original creditor, so too the recipient of a *matnat shchiv mera* cannot have the transferred obligation undone. The force of the *de-oraisa* claim, then, is really a claim about which paradigm governs.

### Gemara Resolution

The Gemara resolves the tension by concluding that *matnat shchiv mera* is *de-rabbanan*, but the Rabbis consciously modeled it after *yerusha* rather than after standard *kinyan*. This distinction carries significant halakhic weight and will shape the entire subsequent discussion. Whether this means the Rabbis gave it the *force* of a *de-oraisa* institution, or that they modeled its internal logic after *yerusha*, the practical consequence is the same: for certain purposes, *matnat shchiv mera* behaves more like inheritance than like a commercial transaction.

## Abstract Transfers

### Rav Nachman Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁאָמַר ״יָדוּר פְּלוֹנִי בְּבַיִת זֶה״; ״יֹאכַל פְּלוֹנִי פֵּירוֹת דֶּקֶל זֶה״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם, עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר: ״תְּנוּ בַּיִת זֶה לִפְלוֹנִי וְיָדוּר בּוֹ״; ״תְּנוּ דֶּקֶל זֶה לִפְלוֹנִי וְיֹאכַל פֵּירוֹתָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** With regard to **a person on his deathbed who says: So-and-so shall reside in this house,** or who says: **So-and-so shall eat** the **fruit of this palm tree,** he **has not said anything,** as one cannot give that which is not tangible, such as the right to use property, or the right to consume fruit that has not yet grown. His statement is ineffective **until he says: Give this house to so-and-so and he shall reside there,** or: **Give this palm tree to so-and-so and he shall eat its fruit.**

Rav Nachman now rules on a case that tests the limits of *matnat shchiv mera*. A *shchiv mera* on his deathbed says: "Let Reuven live in this house" (*yeidor ploni b'vayis zeh*), or "Let Reuven eat the fruit of this palm tree" (*yochal ploni peiros de-ikal zeh*). These seem like entirely natural expressions of a dying man's wishes — the kind of instruction one might imagine in any ethical will or final directive. And yet Rav Nachman rules: *lo amar klum* — these declarations have no legal effect.

Why not? Because neither statement transfers any concrete property. Dwelling in a house is an abstraction; the right to future fruit is a *davar shelo ba le-olam*, something that does not yet exist in the world. The family may choose to honor the dying man's wishes out of respect for his memory, but no legally cognizable gift has been made. The *shchiv mera* has not transferred anything.

### Property Transfer Requirement

To achieve the desired result, Rav Nachman specifies the required formulation: *tenu bayis zeh le-ploni ve-yeidor bo* — "Give Reuven this house so that he may live in it"; *tenu de-ikal zeh le-ploni ve-yochal peirosav* — "Give Reuven this palm tree so that he may eat its fruit." Once actual ownership of the object is transferred, the incidental benefits — dwelling, fruit — follow automatically. The explicit statement of purpose (*ve-yeidor bo*, *ve-yochal peirosav*) is not strictly necessary once ownership is conveyed, but the ownership transfer itself is indispensable. An intermediate option also exists — conveying the tree *le-peirosav*, transferring ownership for the specific purpose of its fruit — but even here, some concrete property right must be the subject of the transfer.

### Conditional Language Debate

This raises a genuinely difficult question: can a *matnat shchiv mera* include conditional language — for instance, giving Reuven the house *letzorech dira*, for the purpose of dwelling, in a way that might limit what Reuven can do with it? The question cuts to the heart of the conceptual debate. A *kinyan* can certainly include conditions: one can transfer property *al tnai*, with a stipulation that reverts ownership if a condition is unmet. But a *yerusha* cannot be conditioned — one cannot inherit *b'tnai*. If *matnat shchiv mera* operates on the *yerusha* model, conditional transfers may be structurally unavailable. This remains an open and genuinely interesting question.

### Tanu Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּסָבַר רַב נַחְמָן מִילְּתָא דְּאִיתָא בְּבָרִיא – אִיתָא בִּשְׁכִיב מְרַע, דְלֵיתָא בְּבָרִיא – לֵיתָא בִּשְׁכִיב מְרַע? וְהָא אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that Rav Naḥman maintains** that only **a matter that can be** conferred as a gift **by a healthy person** with an act of acquisition **can be** conferred as a gift **by a person on his deathbed** by means of verbal instruction, but a matter **that cannot be** conferred as a gift **by a healthy person cannot be** conferred as a gift **by a person on his deathbed?** Consequently, a person on his deathbed cannot transfer ownership of an intangible right, just as a healthy person cannot do so. **But doesn't Rava say** that **Rav Naḥman says:**

The Gemara now introduces a *braita* that, in the view of the discussion, demonstrates Rav Nachman's broader principle: *milta de-ita b'vari ita b'shchiv mera* — something that can be accomplished by a healthy person (*bari*) can be accomplished by a *shchiv mera*; *milta de-leita b'vari leita b'shchiv mera* — something a healthy person cannot do, a *shchiv mera* cannot do either. Giving over future fruit that does not yet exist is something a *bari* cannot accomplish. It remains a *davar shelo ba le-olam*, and the special status of *matnat shchiv mera* — which eliminates the need for a formal act of *kinyan* — does not transform the institution into some superhuman *kinyan* that overcomes all other halakhic limitations. It simply means that the words themselves function as the act. All other restrictions of the standard *kinyan* framework remain in place.

## Debt Transfer Power

### Rava Debt Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁאָמַר ״תְּנוּ הַלְוָאָתִי לִפְלוֹנִי״ – הַלְוָאָתוֹ לִפְלוֹנִי, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בְּבָרִיא!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a person on his deathbed who says: Give** the **loan** owed **to me to so-and-so** as a gift, the **loan** owned to **him** is acquired **by so-and-so. And** this is the *halakha* **despite** the fact **that this does not** apply **in** the case of **a healthy person,** as one cannot transfer a loan to a third party without a promissory note.

Rava, citing Rav Nachman, now introduces what appears to be a direct challenge to this principle. A *shchiv mera* declares: "Give my debt — the money Reuven owes me — to Shimon." No *shtar chov* changes hands. There is no physical document, no formal act. And yet the ruling is that this transfer is effective. This is a striking Gemara. Even in the case of *shtar chov* transfer — where at least a physical document representing the debt is conveyed — the mechanism is already halakhically complex, requiring specific procedures to make the piece of paper constitute a genuine transfer of the underlying obligation. Here, there is not even a *shtar*. The debt is a purely abstract entity. A *bari* could not transfer a debt this way. And yet, by *matnat shchiv mera*, it works. This seems to contradict directly the principle just established: *milta de-leita b'vari leita b'shchiv mera*.

### Rav Pappa Yerusha Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: הוֹאִיל וְיוֹרֵשׁ יוֹרְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** In this case the *halakha* of transferring loans differs with regard to a person on his deathbed, **since an heir inherits** loans without a promissory note, and the Sages accorded the gift of a person on his deathbed the halakhic status of an inheritance.

Rav Pappa offers an elegant resolution. *Ho'il ve-yoresh yorsha* — since an heir inherits a debt. When a person dies, the money owed to the father passes to the sons, even if there is no *shtar*, even if the debt is a purely abstract obligation. The sons become the party to whom the debt is owed by operation of *yerusha*, not through any *kinyan*. So when a *shchiv mera* designates Shimon to receive the debt, he is in effect saying: Shimon is my heir with respect to this obligation. And since an heir inherits debts — without a *shtar*, without any formal act — *matnat shchiv mera*, modeled as it is on *yerusha*, can accomplish the same thing.

This answer is internally coherent and beautifully consistent with the earlier discussion. It also explains why, as Shmuel ruled, the original creditor cannot annul the debt after transferring it by *matnat shchiv mera*: the recipient stands in the position of the heir, and the debt is now owed directly to him. Crucially, however, this *yerusha* model does not extend infinitely. An heir does not inherit just the fruit of a tree without inheriting the tree itself. An heir does not inherit just the right to dwell in a house without inheriting the house. So the *yerusha* model explains the power of *matnat shchiv mera* to transfer debts while simultaneously preserving the earlier ruling that future fruit cannot be transferred in abstraction from the tree.

### Rav Acha Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא אָמַר: הַלְוָאָה אִיתָא בְּבָרִיא, וְכִדְרַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב – דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ, תְּנֵהוּ לִפְלוֹנִי״; בְּמַעֲמַד שְׁלָשְׁתָּן – קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said: A loan** without a promissory note **can be** conferred as a gift **by a healthy person. And** this is **in accordance with** that which **Rav Huna** says that **Rav says, as Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** With regard to one who said to another: **I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession, give it to so-and-so,** if this was stated **in the presence of** all **three of the** parties, the third party **acquires** the money without an act of acquisition and without the need for witnesses.

Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika offers an alternative approach that does not invoke the *yerusha* model at all. He maintains that even without *matnat shchiv mera*, a debt can in fact be transferred between healthy parties under certain circumstances — specifically through the rabbinic mechanism of *ma'amad sheloshtan*, the three-party transaction. Rav Huna in the name of Rav rules: if Reuven tells Shimon, "The hundred dinars you owe me — give them to Leah," and all three parties are present simultaneously, Leah acquires the debt and Reuven is no longer owed anything. This is already an established rabbinic *kinyan*. The *chiddush* of *matnat shchiv mera* is that it functions without requiring a formal *ma'aseh kinyan* — the words of the *shchiv mera* substitute for the act. By the same logic, just as *ma'amad sheloshtan* can transfer a debt among healthy parties, *matnat shchiv mera* — which is at least as powerful as any standard *kinyan* — can transfer a debt as well.

### Debt Transfer Conclusion

Both models thus remain viable. Whether *matnat shchiv mera* operates on the *yerusha* model or on the *kinyan* model, it can accomplish a debt transfer. The two approaches converge on this result while diverging in their reasoning — and the divergence matters: the *yerusha* model additionally explains why *mechila* is ineffective after such a transfer, whereas the *kinyan* model, standing alone, would not. What both models agree on is that *matnat shchiv mera* is not a blanket override of all halakhic limitations. It cannot transfer future fruit that does not yet exist, because neither *yerusha* nor any recognized *kinyan* can accomplish that. The institution has real but bounded power.

## Property Splitting

### Tree/Fruit Split Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: דֶּקֶל לְאֶחָד וּפֵירוֹתָיו לְאַחֵר, מַהוּ? מִי שַׁיַּיר מְקוֹם פֵּירֵי, אוֹ לָא שַׁיַּיר? אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר: לְאַחֵר לָא הָוֵי שִׁיּוּר; לְעַצְמוֹ – ״חוּץ מִפֵּירוֹתָיו״, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to one who gave **a palm tree to one** person **and its fruit to another, what is** the *halakha*? **Did** he **reserve the place of the fruit** on the tree for the second person in order to give him a tangible object, and therefore the second person acquires the fruit, **or** did **he not reserve** it, and the second person does not acquire the fruit? Furthermore, **if you say** that giving the palm tree to one person and giving the fruit **to another is not** considered **reserving** the place of the fruit unless this is explicitly stipulated, then if he reserves the fruit **for himself,** stating that the tree is given to another **except for its fruit, what is** the *halakha*?

The Gemara now poses a more refined question (*iba'ya lehu*): what is the *halacha* if a *shchiv mera* assigns a palm tree to one person and its fruit to another? This question is read here following the approach of Tosafos rather than the Rashbam, whose reading is considerably more complex. The setup is this: we just ruled that a *shchiv mera* cannot give someone just the fruit of a tree, since the fruit does not yet exist and is a *davar shelo ba le-olam*. And yet, in the present case, the *shchiv mera* is not simply saying "give Reuven the fruit" — he is distributing the tree and its fruit as two separate items to two separate recipients. Perhaps when he designates the fruit to a second person, he actually means to convey the *makom peiri* — the place on the branches from which the fruit will grow — a concrete piece of the tree itself. The question is: *shiyer mekom peirot o lo shiyer?* Has the *shchiv mera* actually retained and conveyed a partial ownership interest in the tree, or is he doing nothing more than designating future fruit — which is no gift at all?

### Makom Peiri Principle

The concept of *makom peiri* is the critical analytical tool here. The question is not merely about what the *shchiv mera* intended but about what his words can legally accomplish. If designating the fruit to a second recipient is understood as conveying a portion of the tree — the physical location from which the fruit will emerge — then the transfer is of a concrete object and is valid. If it is understood as nothing more than granting entitlement to future fruit as an abstraction, then it remains a *davar shelo ba le-olam* and confers nothing. The challenge is determining which reading applies, and the Gemara suggests the answer may depend on who the designated recipient is.

### Self-Interest Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר: דֶּקֶל לְאֶחָד וּפֵירוֹתָיו לְאַחֵר לָא הָוֵי שִׁיּוּר מְקוֹם פֵּירֵי; דֶּקֶל לְאֶחָד וְשִׁיֵּיר פֵּירוֹתָיו לְפָנָיו – שַׁיַּיר מְקוֹם פֵּירֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֹּל לְגַבֵּי נַפְשֵׁיהּ – בְּעַיִן יָפָה מְשַׁיֵּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that **Rav Naḥman said:** Even **if you say** that giving the **palm tree to one** person **and its fruit to another is not** considered **reserving the place of the fruit** for the second person, if he gave the **palm tree to one** person **and reserved the fruit for himself,** he also **reserved the place of the fruit** for himself. **What is the reason** for this distinction? The reason is that **anything** that one reserves **with regard to himself, he reserves generously,** and therefore he reserves for himself not only the fruit itself, but also the place of the fruit.

Rav Amram citing Rav Nachman resolves the ambiguity by drawing a distinction based on the identity of the recipient. *Im timtza lomar dekel le-echad u-feirotav le-acher lo hashayir makom peiri* — if the *shchiv mera* splits the tree to one person and the fruit to someone else, we do not interpret his words as retaining or conveying the *makom peiri*. He is simply attempting to give the other person the future fruit, which does not exist yet, and the transfer fails. Charlie, as it were, gets nothing.

But *dekel le-echad ve-shayir peirotav l'atzmo* — if the *shchiv mera* gives the tree away and retains the fruit for himself, we do interpret that retention as constituting a genuine retention of *makom peiri*. The *shchiv mera* is treated as having held back a partial ownership interest in the tree itself, sufficient to sustain his entitlement to the fruit. *Mai ta'ama? Kol l'gabei nafshei b'ayin yafa meshayer* — when it concerns one's own interests, a person tends to retain more generously for himself. There is a greater presumption that the words carry full legal weight when the speaker is preserving something for himself than when he is attempting to confer an abstract benefit on a third party. One might also frame this differently: since the *shchiv mera* began as owner of the entire tree, it takes a clearer act of transfer to fully divest him of it; when his words are ambiguous, the inertia of ownership operates in his favor.

### House/Upper Floor Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אֲנַן אַדְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מַתְנֵינַן לַהּ. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: הַמּוֹכֵר בַּיִת לַחֲבֵירוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁדְּיוֹטָא הָעֶלְיוֹנָה שֶׁלִּי״ – דְּיוֹטָא הָעֶלְיוֹנָה שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said to Rav Ashi: We learned** the aforementioned statement of Rav Naḥman **with regard to** the statement **of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** concerning a different matter, **as Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** In the case of **one who sells a house to another and says to him:** I am selling the house **on the condition that the upper story [*deyota*]** remains **mine, the upper story** remains **his.**

Rav Abba reports to Rav Ashi that this same distinction between conveying to another and retaining for oneself is actually taught in connection with a different case altogether — a ruling of Reish Lakish. Reish Lakish holds that if someone sells a house to another and says *al menat she-diyuta eliyona sheli* — "on condition that the upper story remains mine" — the seller retains the upper floor. The question then raised is whether selling the ground floor to one person and the upper story to a different person is equivalent, or whether there is a meaningful difference.

The Gemara rules: *im timtza lomar beita le-chad ve-dyuta le-chad lo havei shiyur* — if you divide the ground floor and upper story between two different recipients, you are not retaining any additional rights. But *chutz mi-dyuta havei shiyur* — if you are selling the house and explicitly reserving the upper story for yourself, that reservation carries additional air rights, including the right to extend protrusions (*zizin*) from the wall, following the view of Rav Zavid. The extra explicitness of the self-reservation language signals a broader retention. And the Gemara notes an analogy: *keivan de-amar chutz mi-peroseha makom peirot shiyer* — when a person declares "except for its fruit," perhaps we should similarly read that as a retention of *makom peirot*. That said, the analogy is imperfect — in the house case, the seller could have remained silent about the upper floor since the default rule (*mokher et ha-bayis lo mokher et ha-aliya*) would have protected it automatically, making the explicit reservation carry additional legal weight — whereas no such default operates in the tree case. The parenthetical comparison in the Gemara text is accordingly somewhat strained.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בַּיִת לְאֶחָד וּדְיוֹטָא לְאֶחָד, מַהוּ? מִי הָוֵי שִׁיּוּר, אוֹ לָא? אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר בַּיִת לְאֶחָד וּדְיוֹטָא לְאֶחָד לָא הָוֵי שִׁיּוּר, ״חוּץ מִדְּיוֹטָא״, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages with regard to the statement of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: If one sold **a house,** i.e., a lower story, **to one** person **and** stated that the upper **story** will be sold **to one** other person, **what is** the *halakha*? **Is this** considered **reserving** additional rights for the second person apart from the upper story, **or not? If you say** that in a case where one sells his **house to one and** the upper **story to one,** his stipulation **is not** considered **reserving** rights for the second person, there is another dilemma. If one sold a house **except for** the upper **story,** reserving the upper story for himself, **what is** the *halakha*? Is this considered reserving additional rights for himself?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר בַּיִת לְאֶחָד וּדְיוֹטָא לְאֶחָד לָא הָוֵי שִׁיּוּר, ״חוּץ מִדְּיוֹטָא״ הָוֵי שִׁיּוּר – וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַב זְבִיד, דְּאָמַר: שֶׁאִם רָצָה לְהוֹצִיא בָּהּ זִיזִין – מוֹצִיא;
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that **Rav Naḥman said:** Even **if you say** that where one sells the **house to one** person **and** the upper **story to one** other person, he **is not** considered to be **reserving** rights for the second person, if one sold a house **except for** the upper **story,** he **is** considered to be **reserving** additional rights for himself. **And** this is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Zevid, who says** that the reason the seller reserves additional rights is **that if** he **wants to extend projections from** the upper story into the space of the courtyard, **he may extend** them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּשַׁיַּיר דְּיוֹטָא – מְקוֹם זִיזִין נָמֵי שַׁיַּיר; הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״חוּץ מִפֵּירוֹתָיו״ – מְקוֹם פֵּירֵי שַׁיַּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Evidently, since he reserved** the upper **story, he also reserved the place of the projections. Here too,** with regard to the one who gave a palm tree to another and reserved the fruit for himself, **since he said** that the tree is given to another **except for its fruit, he reserved the place of the fruit** for himself as well.

### Concrete Transfer Rule

The upshot of the entire discussion is a clean governing principle: even a *shchiv mera*, who possesses the power to transfer debts that a *bari* cannot transfer through ordinary *kinyan*, cannot transfer the fruit of a tree without transferring the tree itself. The extra power of *matnat shchiv mera* is real but specific: it can accomplish what *yerusha* accomplishes, and it can accomplish what *kinyan* mechanisms like *ma'amad sheloshtan* accomplish. What it cannot do is conjure a valid transfer of something that is neither concrete nor inheritable. If, however, the *shchiv mera* is retaining fruit for himself rather than conveying it to another, his words will be interpreted generously to preserve a meaningful ownership interest in the underlying property.

## Kol/Miktzat Nechasav

### Kol Nechasav Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁכָּתַב כׇּל נְכָסָיו לַאֲחֵרִים – רוֹאִין; אִם בִּמְחַלֵּק, מֵת – קָנוּ כּוּלָּן, עָמַד – חוֹזֵר בְּכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna (146b) states that if a person on his deathbed granted all of his property to others but reserved for himself any amount of land, his gift is valid even if he recovers. If he did not reserve for himself any amount of land, his gift is not valid if he recovers. **Rav Yosef bar Minyumi says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** In the case of **a person on his deathbed who wrote** a deed granting **all of his property to others,** the manner in which he divided his property **is examined. If** it appears **that he was** intending **to divide** his entire estate among various recipients, then if **he dies, all** the recipients **acquire** their gifts. If he **recovers,** he can **retract all** of the gifts, in accordance with the ruling of the mishna.

Rav Yosef bar Minyumi citing Rav Nachman now introduces the next major parameter of *matnat shchiv mera*. The two distinctive features of *matnat shchiv mera* — that words function as *kinyan* without any formal act, and that *amad chozer*, the gift reverts if the *shchiv mera* recovers — apply only when the gift is *kol nechasav*, all of the *shchiv mera*'s property. When a *shchiv mera* gives away only *miktzat nechasav*, a portion of his property, the gift is governed by ordinary *kinyan* rules. A formal act of *kinyan* is required, and if the *shchiv mera* recovers, the gift stands: *amad eino chozer*. The rationale is that a person who gives away only part of his property is not acting from the desperation of one who believes he is dying and has no further use for his possessions — he is making a regular gift that happens to occur while he is ill. The unique rabbinic institution applies only when the *shchiv mera* has divested himself of everything.

### Mechaleik vs. Nimlach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם בְּנִמְלָךְ, מֵת – קָנוּ כּוּלָּן, עָמַד – אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר אֶלָּא בָּאַחֲרוֹן. וְדִלְמָא עַיּוֹנֵי קָא מְעַיֵּין, וַהֲדַר יָהֵיב! סְתָמֵיהּ דִּשְׁכִיב מְרַע מִידָּק דָּיֵיק וַהֲדַר יָהֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** But **if** it appears **that he was reconsidering** the matter, initially intending to give away only a portion of his property, and then reconsidering and distributing the rest, then if **he dies, all** of the recipients **acquire** their gifts. If **he recovers, he can retract only the last** gift, as the previous gifts were given with the intent to reserve part of the property for himself, and they are valid even if he recovers. The Gemara asks: **Perhaps** he was not reconsidering; rather, he initially intended to transfer ownership of all of his property, and **he was considering** to whom to give each portion **and then giving** it. The Gemara answers: **The usual** way **of a person on his deathbed is to consider** the matter **carefully** first **and** only **then** to **confer** his gifts. Therefore, if he pauses to consider in the interim, it appears that he did not initially intend to transfer ownership of his entire estate.

The practical application of this principle becomes complicated when a *shchiv mera* distributes his property not in a single statement but sequentially — giving one thing to Reuven, then the next day something to Shimon, and so on until all is distributed. The question is whether this sequential process is to be read as *k'mechaleik* — as a single unified act of distributing all one's property — or *b'nimlach* — as a series of separate reconsidered decisions, each of which constitutes only *miktzat* at the time it was made.

### Last Recipient Status

The *nafka mina* is significant. If the process is treated as *k'mechaleik* — one continuous act of comprehensive distribution — then every recipient's gift has *matnat shchiv mera* status: no *kinyan* is required and *amad chozer* applies to all. If, however, it is treated as *b'nimlach* — separate sequential decisions — then the early recipients received only *miktzat* at the time of their gifts, making those ordinary *kinyanim* that require a formal act and are not reversed by recovery. Only the final recipient, who received whatever was left when everything else had already been given away, received *kol nechasav* at the moment of his gift and thus holds a genuine *matnat shchiv mera* subject to *amad chozer*.

The Gemara attempts to provide a working standard: *stamei d'shchiv mera ma'ayan b'reisha ve-hadar yahiv* — typically, a *shchiv mera* thinks through his entire distribution in advance and only then begins allocating. If that is so, even a multi-day process of gifting reflects a single prior decision, and the *mechaleik* category applies. The Gemara acknowledges, however, that this standard is difficult to apply with confidence in practice — and the related question of whether a given *shchiv mera* even has the legal sophistication to frame his gifts correctly remains a genuine practical concern.

### Hidden Assets Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁכָּתַב כׇּל נְכָסָיו לַאֲחֵרִים, וְעָמַד – אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר, חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא יֵשׁ לוֹ נְכָסִים בִּמְדִינָה אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Aḥa bar Minyumi says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** With regard to **a person on his deathbed who wrote** a deed granting **all of his** generally known **property to others, and** he then **recovered, he cannot retract** it, as **we are concerned** that **perhaps he has property in another province** that he reserved for himself, in which case the gift is valid even if he recovered.

Rav Nachman raises what seems at first to be a devastating objection. Even when a *shchiv mera* appears to have distributed all his property, how can we ever be confident that we are truly in the category of *kol nechasav*? Perhaps he holds assets in another location — a forgotten account, an investment buried in paperwork, some property elsewhere. *Chashinan shema yesh lo nechasim b'medina acheret* — we must worry that he has property in another jurisdiction. If this concern is taken seriously, it would mean that virtually every case falls into *miktzat nechasav*, effectively eliminating the category of *kol nechasav* — and with it, the institution of *amad chozer* — almost entirely.

### Explicit Language Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי: לֹא שִׁיֵּיר קַרְקַע כָּל שֶׁהוּא – אֵין מַתְּנָתוֹ קַיֶּימֶת; הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? אָמַר רַב חָמָא: בְּאוֹמֵר ״כׇּל נְכָסַי״. מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: בְּמוּחְזָק לַן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** as for **the mishna, which teaches:** If **he did not reserve** for himself **any amount of land,** and he recovered, **his gift is not valid, how can you find these** circumstances? **Rav Ḥama said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where he says: All my property** is given to so-and-so, in which case he clearly reserved nothing for himself. **Mar bar Rav Ashi said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where our presumption is that he does not have** property elsewhere.

Rav Chama resolves the difficulty: the category of *kol nechasav* is established when the *shchiv mera* explicitly uses the language *kol nechasai* — "all my property." When he frames his gift with that phrase, the hidden-assets concern is set aside because the gift is self-defining: whatever he owns, all of it, is being transferred. The concern about unknown assets does not override an explicit declaration of totality.

The Rashbam also cites a view in the name of Rabbeinu Chananel that extends this further: even when the *shchiv mera* specifies particular items — this house to one person, this vehicle to another, this account to a third — he may still achieve *kol nechasav* status by adding the clause that this constitutes all his property. Such a formulation combines the specificity of concrete itemization with the triggering language of comprehensive transfer. This approach has considerable practical appeal, since it allows a *shchiv mera* to be maximally concrete while still invoking the full protection of the institution. The Rashbam himself does not endorse this reading, though his precise objection is not entirely clear.

## Chazara B'Miktzat

### Retraction Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: חֲזָרָה בְּמִקְצָת – הָוֵי חֲזָרָה בְּכוּלָּהּ, אוֹ לָא? תָּא שְׁמַע: כּוּלָּן לָרִאשׁוֹן וּמִקְצָתָן לַשֵּׁנִי – שֵׁנִי קָנָה, רִאשׁוֹן לֹא קָנָה. מַאי, לָאו בְּשֶׁמֵּת?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to the gift of a person on his deathbed, **is a partial retraction** of the gift considered **a retraction of the entire** gift, **or** is it **not** considered a retraction of the entire gift? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: If one gave **all** of his property **to the first** recipient **and** then he partially retracted his gift and gave **part** of his property **to the second** recipient, **the second acquired** his gift, but **the first did not acquire** anything. **What, is it not** referring to a case **where** the giver **died,** and the reason that the first recipient does not acquire anything is that a partial retraction is considered a retraction of the entire gift?

The final major question of the daf: *chazara b'miktzat havi chazara b'kula o lo?* A *shchiv mera* has declared all his property to Reuven — a textbook *matnat shchiv mera*. The next day, he says that Shimon should receive the car. He has not said "I am revoking my prior gift." He has simply redirected one item. Is this partial modification a retraction of everything — *chazara b'kula* — leaving Reuven with nothing and Shimon with only the car? Or is it a limited revision — *chazara b'miktzat* — leaving Reuven with everything except the car and giving Shimon the car?

The *nafka mina* is concrete: if it is *chazara b'kula*, Reuven receives nothing and the original gift is entirely voided. If it is only *chazara b'miktzat*, Reuven retains approximately ninety-five percent of the estate and only the car shifts to Shimon. The further relevance of the principle *daitiki mevatelet daitiki* — a later will annuls an earlier one — frames the analysis: whenever a *shchiv mera* issues a new directive, it supersedes prior directives. The question is how broadly a new directive sweeps when it addresses only one item.

### Braita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, בְּשֶׁעָמַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **No,** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the giver **recovered.** The first gift was invalidated, because he left nothing for himself, whereas the second gift remained valid, since he gave only part of his property. Had he died, the first recipient would have received the remainder, as the giver retracted only part of the first gift, which he gave to the second recipient.

The Gemara cites a *braita* that appears to resolve the question directly. *Kulam le-rishon u-v'miktzatam le-sheni — sheni kana, rishon lo kana.* When the *shchiv mera* first gave everything to the first recipient and subsequently redirected some portion to a second recipient, the second recipient acquires his portion and the first recipient acquires nothing. This seems to be a clear ruling of *chazara b'kula*: the partial modification to the second recipient completely voids the original gift to the first.

### Retention Hypothesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: מִקְצָתָן לָרִאשׁוֹן, וְכוּלָּן לַשֵּׁנִי – רִאשׁוֹן קָנָה, שֵׁנִי לֹא קָנָה. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּשֶׁעָמַד, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי שֵׁנִי לֹא קָנָה; אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּשֶׁמֵּת, תַּרְוַיְיהוּ לִיקְנוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **So too, it is reasonable** to understand the *baraita* in this manner. **From** the fact **that the last clause** of the *baraita* **teaches:** If he gave **part** of his property **to the first** recipient, **and** then he gave **all** of his property **to the second** recipient, **the first** recipient **acquired** his gift, but **the second did not acquire** anything. **Granted, if you say** that the *baraita* is referring **to** a case **where** the giver **recovered, due to that** reason **the second** recipient **did not acquire** the gift. **But if you say** that the *baraita* is referring **to** a case **where he died, both of them should acquire** their gifts.

The Gemara initially resists this reading. Perhaps, it suggests, the *braita* is not a case of *amad* followed by *chazara b'kula*, but rather a case where the *shchiv mera* recovered (*amad*) after the sequential gifts. Under that reading, Reuven still holds approximately ninety-five percent of the estate as a *matnat shchiv mera*, and Shimon holds his five percent — but if the *shchiv mera* recovered, the *amad* principle would reverse Reuven's gift (*amad chozer*) since it was *kol nechasav*, while Shimon's gift would survive as *miktzat nechasav* — a regular *kinyan* not undone by recovery. This reading avoids the conclusion that a partial modification produces *chazara b'kula*.

The Gemara supports this hypothesis by reference to the *seifa* of the *braita*: *miktzatan le-rishon ve-chulan le-sheni — rishon kana, sheni lo kana.* When a small gift was made first to Reuven, and subsequently everything remaining was given to Shimon, Reuven's gift stands and Shimon's does not. The Gemara reasons that this *seifa* must be a case of *amad*: if the *shchiv mera* died, both gifts would be valid and there would be no basis to deny Shimon his gift. Only if he recovered does it make sense that Shimon's gift — which was *kol nechasav* at the time it was given — reverts, while Reuven's earlier *miktzat* gift, a standard *kinyan*, remains valid.

### Amad Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יֵימַר לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְתֶהֱוֵי נָמֵי בְּשֶׁעָמַד; אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא חֲזָרָה בְּמִקְצָת הָוְיָא חֲזָרָה בְּכוּלָּהּ – הַיְינוּ דְּשֵׁנִי מִיהַת קָנָה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ חֲזָרָה בְּמִקְצָת לָא הָוְיָא חֲזָרָה בְּכוּלָּהּ – נִיהְוֵי כִּמְחַלֵּק, וּלְחַד מִינַּיְיהוּ לָא לִיקְנוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeimar said to Rav Ashi: And even** if the first clause of the *baraita* **is** referring **to** a case **where he recovered,** one can prove that a partial retraction is considered a retraction of the entire gift. **Granted, if you say** that **a partial retraction is** considered **a retraction of the entire** gift, **this is** the reason **that at least the second** recipient **acquired** the gift, as the giver reserved part of his property for himself. **But if you say** that **a partial retraction is not** considered **a retraction of the entire** gift, the giver **should be** considered **as one who was dividing** all of his property between the two recipients, **and neither of them should acquire** their gifts.

Rav Yimar presses Rav Ashi: if the *seifa* can be explained through *amad*, why not similarly explain the *reisha*? If *chazara b'miktzat* is not a *chazara b'kula*, then the *reisha* — where everything was given first and a little redirected to a second person — should also be resolvable through *amad*: the second person's *miktzat* gift remains (it was a standard *kinyan*), while the first person's *kol nechasav* gift reverts due to *amad chozer*. So neither the *sheni* losing nor the *rishon* losing requires *chazara b'kula*. But then the Gemara realizes the problem with this reasoning: if the original gift to Reuven (*kol nechasav*) and the subsequent gift to Shimon (*miktzat*) are both present and *amad* reverses the *kol nechasav* gift while preserving the *miktzat* gift, the entire estate should be divided accordingly — roughly ninety-five percent to Reuven reverting, five percent to Shimon standing. The *rishon* should not be left with zero. And yet the *braita* says *rishon lo kana* — the first recipient gets nothing. The *amad* explanation of the *reisha* cannot work unless one accepts that the partial modification to Shimon completely voided the prior gift to Reuven.

### Final Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: חֲזָרָה בְּמִקְצָת הָוְיָא חֲזָרָה בְּכוּלָּהּ; רֵישָׁא – מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בֵּין שֶׁמֵּת בֵּין שֶׁעָמַד, סֵיפָא – לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁעָמֵד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that **a partial retraction is** considered **a retraction of the entire** gift. **You find** the *halakha* that is stated in **the first clause** of the *baraita* **both** in a case **where** the giver **died and** in a case **where he recovered. You find** the *halakha* that is stated in **the last clause only** in a case **where he recovered.**

The Gemara concludes: *ve-hilcheta, chazara b'miktzat havi chazara b'kula.* Partial modification is total retraction. When a *shchiv mera* who has given all his property to one person subsequently redirects even a single item to someone else, the original comprehensive gift is entirely nullified. The first recipient receives nothing. The second recipient's gift, now a *miktzat nechasav*, is a standard *kinyan* — valid regardless of whether the *shchiv mera* lives or dies. And the *seifa* of the *braita* is accordingly confined to a case of *amad*: only when the *shchiv mera* recovered does the second person's *kol nechasav* gift revert, while the first person's earlier *miktzat* gift stands as a regular *kinyan*.

The practical implication of *chazara b'miktzat havi chazara b'kula* is striking and worth emphasizing: a *shchiv mera* who wishes to protect the integrity of a comprehensive gift cannot make any subsequent modification, however minor, without completely destroying the original gift and starting over. Conversely, if he wishes to revise, a single redirection of one item is sufficient to wipe the slate clean. The institution of *matnat shchiv mera* is as fragile as it is powerful — words both create it and dissolve it.
