# Bava Batra 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tree Roots Ownership

### Roots & Excavation

The *mishnah* addresses a case in which someone plants a tree near a boundary line and the roots extend underground into a neighbor's property. If the neighbor subsequently digs a pit, cistern, or cave, he is permitted to cut through any roots he encounters in order to continue digging straight down. The *mishnah* then states that the wood of those cut roots belongs to "him" — but the referent of "him" is ambiguous, and the *gemara* takes up the question directly.

### Ownership Dispute

Yaakov Hadava posed the question to Rav Chisda: to whom does the wood of the roots belong — to the owner of the tree from which they grew, or to the owner of the yard in which they are found? Rav Chisda replied that the matter has already been taught, and he cited a *mishnah* as evidence.

### Hekdesh Baraita

The *mishnah* invoked discusses roots of a tree belonging to a layperson (*hedyot*) that grow underground into property belonging to *hekdesh* (consecrated Temple property). The ruling is: *lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin* — one may not derive benefit from them (even rabbinically), yet there is no biblical prohibition of *me'ilah* (misappropriation of consecrated property). Rav Chisda's argument runs as follows: if ownership follows the tree — that is, if the roots still belong to the *hedyot* tree owner even after entering *hekdesh* land — then the absence of *me'ilah* is perfectly intelligible, since the roots originate from non-sacred property. This would prove that roots remain the property of the tree owner, not the yard owner.

### Me'ilah Problem

The *gemara* rejects this proof by pointing to the *seifa* of the very same *mishnah*. The reverse case is also taught: roots of a *hekdesh* tree that grow into the property of a layperson — and there too the ruling is *lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin*, no *me'ilah*. But this is equally problematic for Rav Chisda's reasoning: if ownership follows the tree, then roots originating from a *hekdesh* tree should be subject to *me'ilah* wherever they go. The fact that *me'ilah* is absent in both cases shows that the ownership rule — whether following the tree or following the land — cannot by itself explain the outcome. The *gemara* thus concludes that neither case proves the point, because the same anomaly appears in both directions.

### Growth Sanctified

The *gemara* resolves both cases together: *bigidulin haba'im le-achar mikan askinan* — we are dealing with growth that occurred after the act of sanctification. The roots in question continued to grow after the tree was already consecrated. Since the additional growth was never explicitly declared *hekdesh*, there is no *me'ilah* by those roots regardless of which property they are located in. Even if the roots of a *hekdesh* tree spread through *hekdesh* land, the subsequently grown portions were never verbally sanctified and therefore fall outside the biblical prohibition of *me'ilah*. The *mishnah* thus cannot serve as proof for either position on the underlying ownership question.

### Sixteen Amot

Rav Papa offers a different resolution of the *mishnah*: the distinction between the two cases is not ownership per se, but proximity. Within sixteen *amot* of the tree, the roots are still functionally connected to the tree and draw their sustenance from it — and within that range, ownership follows the tree. Beyond sixteen *amot*, the roots are no longer considered part of the tree's vital nutrient zone, and at that distance the ownership is governed by the property in which they lie. This is admittedly a difficult reading of the *baraita*, since no such qualification appears in its text, but it anticipates a major topic about to emerge — the significance of sixteen *amot* as the radius within which a tree draws nutrients from the surrounding soil.

## Ulla's 16 Amot

### Gazlan Concept

The *gemara* transitions to a teaching of *Ulla* that introduces the principle of sixteen *amot* on its own terms. *Ulla* states: *ilan hasamuch lemeitzar betoch shesh esreh amah gazlan hu* — a tree planted within sixteen *amot* of a neighbor's boundary line is a *gazlan*, a thief, because its roots will grow under the ground and draw nutrients from the neighbor's property within that radius.

### Gazlan Permitted

Strikingly, the language of *gazlan* does not mean that planting such a tree is forbidden. The *mishnah* itself makes clear that the rules of distancing a tree from a boundary are governed by other concerns — specifically, allowing room for plowing (four *amot* from an unenclosed border), or proximity to a well (twenty-five *amot*). Short of those restrictions, one may plant right up to the boundary line. *Tosafot* accordingly notes that the designation *gazlan hu* does not create a prohibition; rather, its practical consequence is the following ruling: *ve'ein meivi mimenu bikurim* — the owner may not bring *bikurim* (first fruits) from such a tree. When bringing *bikurim*, the owner recites the verse *ha'adamah asher natata li* — "the land which You gave to me." If the tree's fruit grew partly off nutrients drawn from a neighbor's soil, that declaration is not entirely accurate, and the obligation of *bikurim* therefore does not apply.

### Saplings Math

The *gemara* asks: from where did *Ulla* derive the sixteen *amot* figure? It initially suggests the *mishnah* discussing ten saplings (*netiot*) distributed across a *beit se'ah*. A *beit se'ah* is the area needed to sow a *se'ah* of grain — it equals twenty-five hundred square *amot*, corresponding to half the courtyard of the *mishkan* (fifty by one hundred *amot* = five thousand square *amot*). The *halacha* (a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*) is that when ten saplings are distributed across a *beit se'ah* in the period before *shemita*, one may plow the entire area up to *rosh hashana* on their behalf, since the whole space is considered necessary for the saplings' sustenance.

### Sapling Calculation

The *gemara* now works through the arithmetic. A *beit se'ah* is twenty-five hundred *amot*. Divide by ten saplings: each sapling claims two hundred and fifty *amot* of surrounding area. *Ulla*'s figure, by contrast, would require a tree to draw from a square of thirty-two by thirty-two *amot* (sixteen in each direction), which is one thousand and twenty-four square *amot*. The *mishnah*'s number — two hundred and fifty per sapling — is far smaller. The *gemara* therefore sets this source aside: that *mishnah* deals with saplings, which require less than mature trees.

### Rounding Question

The *gemara* then tries the *mishnah* about three mature trees: three trees belonging to three different owners in a shared *beit se'ah* may also have their entire area plowed up to *rosh hashana* of *shemita*. Dividing twenty-five hundred by three yields eight hundred and thirty-three *amot* per tree. *Ulla*'s square of thirty-two by thirty-two gives one thousand and twenty-four per tree — still significantly larger. At this point the *gemara* proposes that *Ulla* may simply have been imprecise (*lo dak*). The *gemara* immediately challenges this: one can say a figure is rounded only when rounding is more stringent (*lechumra*). Here, saying sixteen *amot* creates an exemption from *bikurim* over a wider area than is strictly warranted. If the true radius is smaller than sixteen *amot*, then *Ulla*'s figure is rounding in a lenient direction (*lekula*), and we do not say a *tanna* (or *amora*) is imprecise in the lenient direction.

### Square to Circle

At this point the *gemara* corrects its own geometry: *mi savrat b'rivua kamrinan? b'igul kamrinan* — did you think we meant a square? We mean a circle. If a tree draws nutrients sixteen *amot* in every direction, the relevant shape is a circle with a radius of sixteen, not a square with a side of thirty-two. The question then becomes: how much smaller is the inscribed circle than the surrounding square?

### Pi Equals 3

The *gemara* states that a square is larger than its inscribed circle by one quarter. This is the *gemara*'s standard working approximation using *pi* = 3. With a radius of one, the circumscribed square (side = 2) has area four; the circle has area *pi* × 1² = 3. The ratio is thus four to three, meaning the square exceeds the circle by exactly one quarter. Taking a quarter off the square's area of one thousand and twenty-four yields seven hundred and sixty-eight — identically the result of computing the circle directly: 16² × 3 = 256 × 3 = 768.

### 768 Amot Circle

So if *Ulla* is speaking of a circle of radius sixteen, the area in question is seven hundred and sixty-eight square *amot* per tree. But the *mishnah*'s figure is eight hundred and thirty-three. The circle is still smaller than what the *mishnah* requires, meaning the true radius must be somewhat more than sixteen *amot*.

### 16⅔ Amot Exact

The *gemara* resolves this as follows: *lo daku lechumra lo daku* — *Ulla* was indeed imprecise, but imprecise in the stringent direction. He rounded down from the true figure, which means he was more demanding, not more lenient, about the exemption from *bikurim*. The true radius is sixteen and two-thirds *amot*, as *Rashi* and *Tosafot* note that this is the correct *girsa*: (16⅔)² × 3 = approximately 833 square *amot* — matching the *mishnah* precisely. By stating "sixteen *amot*," *Ulla* ensured that anyone within sixteen *amot* of the boundary would certainly fall within the zone of nutrient-drawing, even though the exact cutoff is sixteen and two-thirds. The rounding was toward stringency, making the exemption from *bikurim* apply over the full correct range.

## Ulla Challenged

### Buy Tree & Land

Having established *Ulla*'s rule, the *gemara* subjects it to a series of challenges from other *mishnayot*. The first: *hakoneh ilan vekarkao mevi vekoreh* — one who buys a tree together with its land brings *bikurim* and recites the declaration. The *gemara* asks whether *karkao* (its land) implies even the smallest plot of land suffices, which would contradict *Ulla*'s requirement for a full sixteen *amot* radius. The *gemara* deflects: *lo, tes-zayin amos* — no, it means specifically sixteen *amot* of surrounding land. The reply is admittedly forced, since one would have expected the *mishnah* to specify this explicitly.

### Two or Three Trees

The second challenge: *koneh shenei ilanos besoch shel chaveiro mevi ve'eino koreh* — one who buys two trees in a neighbor's field brings *bikurim* but does not recite the declaration; *ha shelosha mevi vekoreh* — but three trees, and one does recite. The implication seems to be that buying three trees automatically includes some surrounding land sufficient for *bikurim* — and the *mishnah* elsewhere clarifies this land is the immediately adjacent area under and between the trees, not a sixteen-*amah* radius. The *gemara* again proposes: no, when three trees are purchased, what is included is the surrounding area up to sixteen *amot* in each direction. *Tosafot* notes that this strains the plain meaning of the *mishnah*, which elsewhere describes the acquired land as whatever lies directly beneath the foliage — but adds that if the foliage extends beyond sixteen *amot*, one would receive that; and if not, one would receive at least sixteen *amot* in each direction.

### Rabbi Akiva Tiny

The third and most direct challenge comes from a statement of Rabbi Akiva: *karka kol shehu chayav bepe'ah uvevikurim* — even the smallest amount of land obligates in *pe'ah*, *bikurim*, and *prosbol*. The *prosbol* connection: a loan was customarily given only when the borrower held land that served as collateral, allowing the creditor to place liens. *Shmita* cancels such loans; *chazal* enacted the *prosbol* by which a creditor hands the debt over to *beit din*, enabling collection after *shemita*. This institution applies only to standard land-secured loans; even a tiny plot of land places the debt within that category. A further principle: *niknin imo nechasim she'ein lahem achrayot* — when real property is transferred, movable property (*chattel*) can be transferred along with it, even chattel not physically located on the land, and this too requires only the smallest parcel of real estate. And critically for our discussion, Rabbi Akiva states that *bikurim* applies even on the smallest amount of land — which seems directly contrary to *Ulla*'s position requiring sixteen *amot*.

### Wheat Not Trees

The *gemara* resolves the challenge by distinguishing the subject matter: *be'chitei* — Rabbi Akiva is speaking about grain crops such as wheat and barley (*eretz chitah u'seorah*), not trees. The obligation of *bikurim* applies to all seven species, and for grain there is no requirement of sixteen *amot*, since grain does not generate the same nutrient-drawing issue as trees. Only for trees does *Ulla*'s requirement apply. The *gemara* reinforces this: the very language *kol shehu* (any amount) indicates we are speaking of grain, since you cannot plant a tree in an infinitesimally small plot of land — that language only makes sense for something like a single stalk of wheat. *Shema mina* — the distinction is textually supported.

## Boundary Tree

### Split Tree Fruit

The *gemara* introduces a *mishnah* that presses on the relationship between nutrient-drawing and *halachic* status: *ilan miktzaso ba'aretz u'miktzaso b'chutz la'aretz* — a tree whose trunk straddles the boundary between *Eretz Yisrael* and *chutz la'aretz*. Produce of such a tree raises the question of what is *tevel* (untithed, therefore forbidden) and what is *chulin* (ordinary, exempt from tithing obligations applicable only in *Eretz Yisrael*).

### Nutrient Flow Debate

*Rebbe* (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi) holds: *tevel v'chulin me'uravin zeh bazeh* — the produce of the entire tree is considered a mixture of *tevel* and *chulin*. One cannot identify which fruit grew from which side of the tree; the tree is a single organism, and the produce must be treated as a blend. One would therefore set aside half the normal amount of *terumot u'ma'asrot* from the total yield. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* disagrees: *hagadel bechiyuv chayav v'hagadel bip'tur patur* — produce on the *Eretz Yisrael* side of the vertical boundary is obligated; produce on the *chutz la'aretz* side is exempt. Each fruit is assigned to the half of the tree above which it grows.

### Breira Doctrine

The *gemara* initially frames this dispute as one of *breira* — the doctrine of retroactive determination. The question is whether we can retroactively say that the fruit growing on the *Eretz Yisrael* side of the tree was always "the fruit of the *Eretz Yisrael* portion" and vice versa. The *gemara* then clarifies a critical limiting condition: *hacha b'mai askinan d'mafsik tzunma* — the *mishnah* here is dealing with a special case where a rock or boulder runs along the boundary line underground, physically preventing the roots of each side from crossing into the other. Without this qualification, *Ulla*'s principle would require us to say that even the *chutz la'aretz* side of the tree is drawing nutrients from *Eretz Yisrael* soil and should therefore be subject to *terumot u'ma'asrot*. With the rock dividing the roots, this concern is removed, and the debate between *Rebbe* and *Rabban Shimon* can proceed on its own terms.

### Trunk Mixing

The question then shifts: given that the rock physically separates the roots on each side, why does *Rebbe* still treat the produce as mixed? If the nutrients are kept separate by the rock, why not simply say each side feeds its own fruit? The *gemara* answers: *mar savar avira mevalbel* — *Rebbe* holds that even if the roots are divided, the nutrients from both sides ultimately flow into the common trunk, mixing together there and feeding the entire tree indiscriminately. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, by contrast, holds *hai l'chudei ka'ei v'hai l'chudei ka'ei* — each side's nutrients remain in their own channel within the tree, feeding only the fruit on that corresponding side. Because the nutrients do not mix in the trunk according to *Rabban Shimon*, the boundary line can be extended vertically, and the fruit on each side is treated as having grown entirely from the nutrients of that side alone.

## 16 vs. 25 Amot

### 25 Amot Well

A further challenge to *Ulla*'s sixteen-*amah* rule arises from a different *mishnah*: *merchikin et ha'ilan min habor esrim v'chamesh amah* — a tree must be kept twenty-five *amot* away from a pit or well. If roots extend their damaging reach only up to sixteen *amot*, why does the *halacha* require a distance of twenty-five?

### Abaye: Weaken

*Abaye* resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing between two different effects of roots. Roots will indeed grow far — twenty-five *amot* and beyond — and at that distance they can invade and damage the walls of a well or pit. However, the significant *drawing* of soil nutrients, the weakening of the ground, occurs only up to sixteen *amot*. *Ulla*'s concern for *bikurim* is predicated on where the tree is actually drawing its sustenance from, which is within sixteen *amot*. The rule of twenty-five *amot* by a well addresses physical intrusion and structural damage, which roots can cause at greater distances. The two figures thus measure different phenomena and do not contradict each other.

## Reish Lakish & Yochanan

### Gazlan Bikurim

When Rav Dimi came (from *Eretz Yisrael* to Babylonia), he reported a discussion between *Reish Lakish* and *Rabbi Yochanan*. *Reish Lakish* asked: what is the status of a tree situated close to a boundary line — does the *gazlan* designation prevent the owner from bringing *bikurim*?

### Cannot Bring

*Rabbi Yochanan* answered, as reported by Rav Dimi: *gazlan hu ve'ein mevi'in mimenu bikurim* — it is indeed a *gazlan*, and one may not bring *bikurim* from it. This aligns with *Ulla*'s ruling as we have been analyzing it.

### Allows Bikurim

However, a subsequent report by Ravin tells a different story: *Rabbi Yochanan* reversed his position. Whether the tree is close to the boundary or whether its canopy overhangs the neighbor's property, *echad ilan hasamuch lemeitzar ve'echad ilan hanoteh mevi vekoreh* — in either case the owner may bring *bikurim* and recite the declaration. All the evidence brought through the *sugya* had, in a sense, been pushing in this direction, and here we finally have an explicit ruling against *Ulla*'s original formulation.

### Al Menat Ken

The rationale is striking: *al menat kein hinchil Yehoshua le-Yisrael et ha'aretz* — Joshua apportioned the land of *Eretz Yisrael* to the Jewish people with the understanding that this is how things work. Trees near borders draw nutrients from adjacent plots; branches overhang into neighboring fields. These realities are built into the fabric of land ownership in *Eretz Yisrael*, and Joshua's allocation assumed and accommodated them. Because you have a right to these nutrients as part of the normal conditions of land use, it cannot be said that the produce is growing from land that was "given to someone else." You may therefore recite *adamah asher natata li* without qualification. The concept of *al menat ken* effectively transforms what *Ulla* framed as *gezel* (even if permitted) into something entirely within one's rights, thereby removing the obstacle to *bikurim* entirely. The *sugya* thus ends on a note that somewhat undercuts *Ulla*'s framing, while preserving the underlying agronomic observation about root reach.

## Branch Cutting

### Plow Clearance

The *mishnah* now addresses a different problem arising from overhanging branches. If a tree's canopy extends over a neighbor's field — a field where grain is grown and plowed — the neighbor may cut back the branches to a height sufficient to allow plowing. The standard of measurement is *melo hamardea al gabei hamachreishah*: the full height of the *mardea* (the goad or prod that the plowman holds over his animals while walking behind the plow) above the *machreishah* (the plow itself). In practical terms, this means enough clearance for the plowman and his equipment to pass through without obstruction.

### Carob Sycamore

An exception applies to carob trees and sycamore trees. These trees produce such dense, heavy foliage that even cutting to plowing height would leave a substantial overhang causing significant shade damage to the field. In these cases, the neighbor may cut the entire overhang — *keneged hamishkolet*, opposite the plumb line. That is, one drops a plumb line straight down from the boundary marker, and any branch extending beyond that vertical plane may be removed entirely, without limit as to height.

### Irrigated Field

The rule is further expanded for *beit hashlachin* — an irrigated field. Irrigation requires maximum sunlight and is especially sensitive to shade. Therefore, in the case of an irrigated field, any tree — not just carob and sycamore — whose branches overhang may be cut back entirely opposite the plumb line.

### Abba Shaul Non-Fruit

*Abba Shaul* adds a further ruling: *kol ilan srak keneged hamishkolet* — any non-fruit-bearing tree may be cut back entirely opposite the plumb line. The significance of his position, and precisely which case of the *mishnah* he is responding to, is the subject of immediate *gemara* discussion.

---

## Abba Shaul Position

### Which Case?

The *gemara* opens with a question: is *Abba Shaul* addressing the *reisha* (the first case of the *mishnah*, concerning a non-irrigated field) or the *seifa* (the second case, concerning an irrigated field)? In the first case, the *Tanna Kamma* limited complete removal to carob and sycamore; in the second, complete removal applied to all trees. *Abba Shaul* is either expanding the first case (adding all non-fruit trees to the list of those that may be fully cut) or restricting the second (limiting the irrigated-field rule to non-fruit trees). Is he being more lenient toward the aggrieved neighbor, or more lenient toward the tree owner?

### Baraita Evidence

A *baraita* resolves part of the question: it explicitly states that for an irrigated field, *Abba Shaul* rules *kol ha'ilan keneged hamishkolet* — any tree may be fully cut back, because the shade is harmful to an irrigated field. Since *Abba Shaul* explicitly allows all trees in the irrigated-field case, he cannot be the one introducing a restriction there. His position on the irrigated field aligns with the *Tanna Kamma*'s ruling in the *mishnah*.

### Non-Irrigated

It follows that *Abba Shaul*'s distinction between fruit and non-fruit trees must address the *reisha*, the non-irrigated field. In that case, the *Tanna Kamma* permitted complete removal only for carob and sycamore. *Abba Shaul* expands this: any non-fruit-bearing tree may be fully cut back even in a non-irrigated field. *Shema mina areisha ka'i* — the *gemara* accepts this conclusion.

### Kol Expansion

Rav Ashi adds a confirmation from the text of the *mishnah* itself. *Abba Shaul*'s language is *kol ilan srak* — "all non-fruit trees." The word *kol* (all/every) signals expansion. If *Abba Shaul* were responding to the irrigated-field case and limiting it to non-fruit trees, the word *kol* would be oddly chosen; one would simply say "non-fruit tree," not "all non-fruit trees." The word *kol* makes sense only if he is broadening the prior category — taking the *Tanna Kamma*'s narrow list of carob and sycamore and expanding it to all non-fruit-bearing trees. This linguistic argument confirms that *Abba Shaul* is arguing on the *reisha*, not the *seifa*. *Shema mina.*

## Public Domain Tree

### Camel Clearance

The final *mishnah* of the chapter addresses a tree overhanging *reshut harabbim* — the public thoroughfare. Here the concern is not agricultural damage but physical clearance for those using the public way. The rule is: *kotzetz kedei sheyehei gamal over verokhvo* — the branches must be cut back sufficiently to allow a camel and its rider to pass through without obstruction.

### Flax Measure

*Rabbi Yehuda* refines the standard: *gamal ta'un pishtan o havilei zmorot* — the measure is not the height of a rider on a camel but of a camel laden with flax or bundles of branches. The load sits atop the camel and extends above the rider's head, requiring a higher clearance.

### Plumb Line Tumah

*Rabbi Shimon* takes a more sweeping position: *kol ha'ilan keneged hamishkolet mipnei hatumah* — the entire overhang must be removed, opposite the plumb line, on account of ritual impurity (*tumah*). His concern is for *kohanim* and others who could become *tamei* by passing under branches that form an *ohel* (tent or canopy) over a corpse or part of a corpse. Since corpses or pieces thereof might be found or carried in public areas, and since *tumah* spreads under any covering, any overhanging branch creates a potential corridor of impurity through *reshut harabbim*.

## Current vs. Future

### Regrowth Problem

The *gemara* raises a structural difficulty: the *mishnah*'s standard — cut to allow a camel and rider to pass — seems to address only the present situation. Branches grow back. Why does the *mishnah* not require a more permanent solution, such as removing the entire overhang? What principle justifies limiting the remedy to the present clearance need?

### Regular Maintenance

*Reish Lakish* answers: this reflects a deliberate approach, and it follows the position of *Rabbi Eliezer*. The *gemara* cites a *mishnah* in Bava Kamma: the *chachamim* forbid digging cavities beneath *reshut harabbim* — for wells, cisterns, or tunnels — because the roof might eventually collapse. *Rabbi Eliezer* permits it provided the ceiling can currently bear the weight of a wagon laden with stones, attending only to the present structural integrity. The *chachamim* reject this because the ceiling may weaken undetected over time, eventually collapsing without warning. Our *mishnah*, which likewise asks only about the current situation rather than anticipating future growth, accordingly follows *Rabbi Eliezer*.

### Pruning vs. Tunnels

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees and holds that even the *rabbanan* can accept the *mishnah*'s approach. The difference is fundamental: *hasam zimnin de-mifchat ve-lav adatei* — in the case of the underground tunnel, the ceiling deteriorates gradually and invisibly; no one knows when it will give way, making the danger essentially impossible to monitor. *Aval hacha kamma kamma ka-kayitz lei* — here, with branches, one can observe regrowth incrementally and prune regularly as needed. Because the hazard is visible and manageable through routine maintenance, there is no structural reason to demand more than the present clearance even according to the *rabbanan*. The *mishnah* can therefore be read as consistent with the majority position.

## Flax vs. Person

### Height Comparison

Having recorded the dispute between the *rabbanan* (measuring by camel and rider) and *Rabbi Yehuda* (measuring by a camel laden with flax), the *gemara* raises a threshold question: which is taller — a person seated atop a camel, or a load of flax on a camel?

### Flax Must Be Higher

The *gemara* first establishes that the flax load must be the taller of the two, for the following reason: *de'i salka daitach shiura de-Rabbi Yehuda nafish rabbanan be-shiura de-Rabbi Yehuda heichi avidi* — were the flax load the shorter measure, the *rabbanan* who require clearance for a camel and rider would obviously be requiring even more clearance than *Rabbi Yehuda*, and on the occasions when a camel carrying flax comes through, there would be no protection at all. Since both *rabbanan* and *Rabbi Yehuda* are addressing the realistic hazards of *reshut harabbim* — and camels carrying loads of flax are a foreseeable occurrence — the *rabbanan* could not have permitted a standard that fails to protect against loaded camels. The flax load must therefore be higher than the mounted rider, and *Rabbi Yehuda*'s standard is more demanding in this regard.

### Duck or Cannot

But this immediately raises a reverse question: if the flax load is higher, how does *Rabbi Yehuda* accommodate the *rabbanan*'s case — the person riding on a camel? *Rabbi Yehuda* demands clearance only for the flax height; what about the mounted rider who may be taller than the flax? The *gemara* answers: *efshar de-gachin ve-chalif* — it is possible for a rider simply to duck. A person can lower his head and lean forward when passing under an obstruction. A load of flax cannot duck. Therefore, *Rabbi Yehuda* requires clearance sufficient for the immovable load but does not require it for the rider, who can accommodate himself. The *rabbanan*, by contrast, require sufficient clearance for the rider at full height without ducking — but since the flax load is even taller, the *rabbanan*'s standard by itself does not address the flax case, which is the specific concern that *Rabbi Yehuda* makes explicit.

## Tumah Overhang

### Dead Body Tumah

*Rabbi Shimon*'s ruling that the entire overhang must be removed on account of *tumah* is recorded in the *mishnah* with the phrase *mipnei hatumah*. A *baraita* elaborates: *mipnei ohel hatumah* — specifically because of the *ohel* (tent-like covering) created by the overhanging branches. The *ohel* effect means that any *tumah* located beneath the canopy of branches — most critically, a corpse or part of a corpse — spreads its impurity to everyone and everything under that same canopy. Since *kohanim* and others traversing *reshut harabbim* may pass under such overhanging branches, and since there may be a corpse or fragment thereof in the public domain, every overhang creates a potential conduit of *tumah*.

### Ohel Not Dropped

One might ask: is the concern perhaps simpler — that a raven or other bird might drop a piece of *tamei* matter directly onto one of the branches, making those specific branches a source of *tumah* from above? If so, the problem would be contact with *tumah* landing on the branch, not the *ohel* effect of the overhang itself.

### Pruning Insufficient

The *gemara* refutes this interpretation by a process of elimination: *ve-sagi bi-dilulei be-alma* — if the concern were something dropped onto the branches, it would be sufficient to thin the branches (*dilulei*), ensuring no piece of *tamei* material could be supported or lodged there. There would be no need to remove the entire overhang. But *Rabbi Shimon* requires total removal, not merely thinning. This demonstrates that the concern is the *ohel* created by the canopy itself: even a full, intact overhang with nothing resting on it spreads *tumah* from below by forming a tent over a source of impurity in the public domain. Because no amount of pruning eliminates the *ohel* effect — only total removal of the overhang does — *Rabbi Shimon* demands that the branches be cut back entirely opposite the plumb line.

With this we conclude the second *perek* of Bava Batra. *Hadran alach lo yachfor.*
