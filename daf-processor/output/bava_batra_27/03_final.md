# Bava Batra 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tree Roots Ownership

### Roots & Excavation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה חוֹפֵר בּוֹר, שִׁיחַ וּמְעָרָה – קוֹצֵץ וְיוֹרֵד וְהָעֵצִים שֶׁלּוֹ (וְכוּ׳). בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ יַעֲקֹב הַדְיָיבָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: עֵצִים שֶׁל מִי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if **he was digging a cistern, a ditch, or a cave, he may cut downward and the wood is his.** The Sage **Ya'akov of Hadeyyav raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda: To whom does the wood belong?** The mishna says that the wood is his, without specifying to which of the two individuals this refers, the owner of the tree or the owner of the land.

The *mishnah* addresses a case in which someone plants a tree near a boundary line and the roots extend underground into a neighbor's property. If the neighbor subsequently digs a pit, cistern, or cave, he is permitted to cut through any roots he encounters in order to continue digging straight down. The *mishnah* then states that the wood of those cut roots belongs to "him" — but the referent of "him" is ambiguous, and the *gemara* takes up the question directly.

### Ownership Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: שׇׁרְשֵׁי אִילָן שֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט הַבָּאִים בְּשֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ, לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: You learned** the answer in a mishna in tractate *Me'ila* (13b). If **roots of a tree** belonging **to an ordinary person [*hedyot*] extend into** a field belonging **to the Temple** treasury, **one may not derive benefit** from them, but if one derived benefit from them he is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property.

Yaakov Hadava posed the question to Rav Chisda: to whom does the wood of the roots belong — to the owner of the tree from which they grew, or to the owner of the yard in which they are found? Rav Chisda replied that the matter has already been taught, and he cited a *mishnah* as evidence.

### Hekdesh Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא: בָּתַר אִילָן אָזְלִינַן – מִשּׁוּם הָכִי לֹא מוֹעֲלִין; אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: בָּתַר קַרְקַע אָזְלִינַן, אַמַּאי לֹא מוֹעֲלִין?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that **we follow the tree,** and the roots are considered part of it, it is **due to that** reason that **one is not** liable for **misuse,** as the tree is not consecrated. **But if you say we follow the land,** i.e., the roots belong to the land's owner, **why** is he **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property?

The *mishnah* invoked discusses roots of a tree belonging to a layperson (*hedyot*) that grow underground into property belonging to *hekdesh* (consecrated Temple property). The ruling is: *lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin* — one may not derive benefit from them (even rabbinically), yet there is no biblical prohibition of *me'ilah* (misappropriation of consecrated property). Rav Chisda's argument runs as follows: if ownership follows the tree — that is, if the roots still belong to the *hedyot* tree owner even after entering *hekdesh* land — then the absence of *me'ilah* is perfectly intelligible, since the roots originate from non-sacred property. This would prove that roots remain the property of the tree owner, not the yard owner.

### Me'ilah Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי? בָּתַר אִילָן אָזְלִינַן?! אֵימָא סֵיפָא: שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ הַבָּאִים בְּשֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט, לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין. וְאִי בָּתַר אִילָן אָזְלִינַן, אַמַּאי לֹא מוֹעֲלִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, what** will you say, that **we follow the tree?** If so, **say the last clause** of that mishna: If roots of a tree belonging **to the Temple** treasury **extend into** a field **of an ordinary person, one may not derive benefit** from them, but if one derived benefit from them he is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. **But if we follow the tree, why** is he **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property?

The *gemara* rejects this proof by pointing to the *seifa* of the very same *mishnah*. The reverse case is also taught: roots of a *hekdesh* tree that grow into the property of a layperson — and there too the ruling is *lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin*, no *me'ilah*. But this is equally problematic for Rav Chisda's reasoning: if ownership follows the tree, then roots originating from a *hekdesh* tree should be subject to *me'ilah* wherever they go. The fact that *me'ilah* is absent in both cases shows that the ownership rule — whether following the tree or following the land — cannot by itself explain the outcome. The *gemara* thus concludes that neither case proves the point, because the same anomaly appears in both directions.

### Growth Sanctified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי אִירְיָא?! בְּגִידּוּלִין הַבָּאִים לְאַחַר מִכָּאן עָסְקִינַן, וְקָא סָבַר: אֵין מְעִילָה בְּגִידּוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Are the cases comparable?** In both clauses of the mishna **we are dealing with growths that came thereafter,** i.e., after the tree was consecrated, **and** the *tanna* of that mishna **holds** that **with regard to growths** that grew from a consecrated plant or tree, they are **not** subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse** of consecrated property. Only the original plant is. Consequently, there is no connection between that mishna and the question of whether roots are considered part of the tree or part of the land.

The *gemara* resolves both cases together: *bigidulin haba'im le-achar mikan askinan* — we are dealing with growth that occurred after the act of sanctification. The roots in question continued to grow after the tree was already consecrated. Since the additional growth was never explicitly declared *hekdesh*, there is no *me'ilah* by those roots regardless of which property they are located in. Even if the roots of a *hekdesh* tree spread through *hekdesh* land, the subsequently grown portions were never verbally sanctified and therefore fall outside the biblical prohibition of *me'ilah*. The *mishnah* thus cannot serve as proof for either position on the underlying ownership question.

### Sixteen Amot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בְּתוֹךְ שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה, כָּאן חוּץ לְשֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said** that it is **not difficult: Here,** in the first clause of the mishna in *Me'ila*, it is referring to **within sixteen cubits** of the tree. In this case the roots are considered part of the tree. **There,** in the second clause, it is referring to roots **beyond sixteen cubits,** in which case the roots are considered part of the ground where they are found.

Rav Papa offers a different resolution of the *mishnah*: the distinction between the two cases is not ownership per se, but proximity. Within sixteen *amot* of the tree, the roots are still functionally connected to the tree and draw their sustenance from it — and within that range, ownership follows the tree. Beyond sixteen *amot*, the roots are no longer considered part of the tree's vital nutrient zone, and at that distance the ownership is governed by the property in which they lie. This is admittedly a difficult reading of the *baraita*, since no such qualification appears in its text, but it anticipates a major topic about to emerge — the significance of sixteen *amot* as the radius within which a tree draws nutrients from the surrounding soil.

## Ulla's 16 Amot

### Gazlan Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: אִילָן הַסָּמוּךְ לַמֶּצֶר – בְּתוֹךְ שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה, גַּזְלָן הוּא – וְאֵין מְבִיאִין מִמֶּנּוּ בִּכּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said:** An individual who maintains **a tree that is within sixteen cubits of a boundary is a robber,** as it draws nourishment from the neighbor's land, **and one does not bring first fruits from it,** since that would be a mitzva that is fulfilled by means of a transgression.

The *gemara* transitions to a teaching of *Ulla* that introduces the principle of sixteen *amot* on its own terms. *Ulla* states: *ilan hasamuch lemeitzar betoch shesh esreh amah gazlan hu* — a tree planted within sixteen *amot* of a neighbor's boundary line is a *gazlan*, a thief, because its roots will grow under the ground and draw nutrients from the neighbor's property within that radius.

### Gazlan Permitted

Strikingly, the language of *gazlan* does not mean that planting such a tree is forbidden. The *mishnah* itself makes clear that the rules of distancing a tree from a boundary are governed by other concerns — specifically, allowing room for plowing (four *amot* from an unenclosed border), or proximity to a well (twenty-five *amot*). Short of those restrictions, one may plant right up to the boundary line. *Tosafot* accordingly notes that the designation *gazlan hu* does not create a prohibition; rather, its practical consequence is the following ruling: *ve'ein meivi mimenu bikurim* — the owner may not bring *bikurim* (first fruits) from such a tree. When bringing *bikurim*, the owner recites the verse *ha'adamah asher natata li* — "the land which You gave to me." If the tree's fruit grew partly off nutrients drawn from a neighbor's soil, that declaration is not entirely accurate, and the obligation of *bikurim* therefore does not apply.

### Saplings Math

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא לֵיהּ לְעוּלָּא הָא? אִילֵּימָא מִדִּתְנַן: עֶשֶׂר נְטִיעוֹת הַמְפוּזָּרוֹת בְּתוֹךְ בֵּית סְאָה, חוֹרְשִׁין כׇּל בֵּית סְאָה בִּשְׁבִילָן, עַד רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where does Ulla** derive **that** measurement? **If we say** it is **from** that **which we learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 1:6), this is problematic. That mishna teaches: If there were **ten saplings scattered in a *beit se'a*, one may plow the entire *beit se'a* for their** sake **until Rosh HaShana** of the Sabbatical Year. Although it is prohibited to plow other land in the time leading into the Sabbatical Year, to avoid the appearance of preparing to work the ground in that year, it is permitted to do so for the purpose of sustaining these young trees.

The *gemara* asks: from where did *Ulla* derive the sixteen *amot* figure? It initially suggests the *mishnah* discussing ten saplings (*netiot*) distributed across a *beit se'ah*. A *beit se'ah* is the area needed to sow a *se'ah* of grain — it equals twenty-five hundred square *amot*, corresponding to half the courtyard of the *mishkan* (fifty by one hundred *amot* = five thousand square *amot*). The *halacha* (a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*) is that when ten saplings are distributed across a *beit se'ah* in the period before *shemita*, one may plow the entire area up to *rosh hashana* on their behalf, since the whole space is considered necessary for the saplings' sustenance.

### Sapling Calculation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה הָווּ לְהוּ – תְּרֵי אַלְפִים וַחֲמֵשׁ מְאָה גַּרְמִידֵי; לְכׇל חַד וְחַד כַּמָּה מָטֵי לֵיהּ – מָאתַן וְחַמְשִׁין; הָא לָא הָוֵי דְּעוּלָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara calculates: **How much is** the area of a *beit se'a*? It is **2,500** square **cubits. And how much** area is allocated **for each and every one** of the ten trees? It is **250** square cubits. **This is not** the distance **that Ulla** taught. An area of sixteen cubits to each side of the tree is a square of thirty-two by thirty-two cubits, or 1,024 square cubits, which is much larger than 250.

The *gemara* now works through the arithmetic. A *beit se'ah* is twenty-five hundred *amot*. Divide by ten saplings: each sapling claims two hundred and fifty *amot* of surrounding area. *Ulla*'s figure, by contrast, would require a tree to draw from a square of thirty-two by thirty-two *amot* (sixteen in each direction), which is one thousand and twenty-four square *amot*. The *mishnah*'s number — two hundred and fifty per sapling — is far smaller. The *gemara* therefore sets this source aside: that *mishnah* deals with saplings, which require less than mature trees.

### Rounding Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מִדִּתְנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה אִילָנוֹת שֶׁל שְׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מִצְטָרְפִין, וְחוֹרְשִׁין כׇּל בֵּית סְאָה בִּשְׁבִילָן. כַּמָּה הָווּ לְהוּ – תְּרֵי אַלְפֵי וַחֲמֵשׁ מְאָה גַּרְמִידֵי; לְכׇל חַד כַּמָּה מָטֵי לֵיהּ – תַּמְנֵי מְאָה וּתְלָתִין וּתְלָתָא וְתִילְתָּא; אַכַּתִּי נְפִישִׁי לֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא! לָא דָּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara tries the *mishnah* about three mature trees: If there were **three** large **trees** belonging **to three** different **people** in one *beit se'a*, **these** trees **combine, and one may plow the entire [beit se'a for their sake]. How much** is that area in cubits? It is **2,500** square **cubits. And how much** area is that **for each** of the trees? It is **833⅓.** Still, **Ulla's** amount **is greater** than this. The Gemara answers: Ulla **was not precise** in this matter.

The *gemara* then tries the *mishnah* about three mature trees: three trees belonging to three different owners in a shared *beit se'ah* may also have their entire area plowed up to *rosh hashana* of *shemita*. Dividing twenty-five hundred by three yields eight hundred and thirty-three *amot* per tree. *Ulla*'s square of thirty-two by thirty-two gives one thousand and twenty-four per tree — still significantly larger. At this point the *gemara* proposes that *Ulla* may simply have been imprecise (*lo dak*). The *gemara* immediately challenges this: one can say a figure is rounded only when rounding is more stringent (*lechumra*). Here, saying sixteen *amot* creates an exemption from *bikurim* over a wider area than is strictly warranted. If the true radius is smaller than sixteen *amot*, then *Ulla*'s figure is rounding in a lenient direction (*lekula*), and we do not say a *tanna* (or *amora*) is imprecise in the lenient direction.

### Square to Circle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּאָמְרִינַן לָא דָּק – לְחוּמְרָא; לְקוּלָּא – לָא דָּק מִי אָמְרִינַן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: One can **say that we say** that a Sage **was not precise** in his measurements when his ruling leads **to a stringency;** but **do we say** that he **was not precise** if his measurements lead **to a leniency?** According to the previous explanation, Ulla exempts the owner of a tree from first fruits even in a case where his tree does not in fact draw nourishment from his neighbor's field.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ בְּרִיבּוּעָא קָא אָמְרִינַן?! בְּעִיגּוּלָא קָא אָמְרִינַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Do you maintain** that **we say** the roots extend that far **in a square,** i.e., one measures sixteen cubits to each side of the tree? Not so; **we say** this **with regard to a circle,** that is, the roots extend in a circle surrounding the tree, as the area of a circle is smaller than that of the square circumscribing it.

At this point the *gemara* corrects its own geometry: *mi savrat b'rivua kamrinan? b'igul kamrinan* — did you think we meant a square? We mean a circle. If a tree draws nutrients sixteen *amot* in every direction, the relevant shape is a circle with a radius of sixteen, not a square with a side of thirty-two. The question then becomes: how much smaller is the inscribed circle than the surrounding square?

### Pi Equals 3

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי כַּמָּה מְרוּבָּע יוֹתֵר עַל הָעִיגּוּל – רְבִיעַ; פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ שְׁבַע מְאָה וְשִׁתִּים וּשִׁשִּׁים וּתְמָנְיָא; אַכַּתִּי פָּשׁ לֵיהּ פַּלְגָא דְאַמְּתָא! הַיְינוּ דְּלָא דָּק – וּלְחוּמְרָא לָא דָּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now,** by **how much is** the area of **a square greater than the** area of a **circle** with a diameter the length of the side of that square? It is greater by **one-quarter** of the area of the circle. If so, **768** square cubits, three-quarters of 1,024, **remain** for each tree, but **there still remains half a cubit** more based on the mishna's calculation. In other words, the measurement would be more accurate if a tree is considered to draw nourishment from a distance of sixteen and a half cubits on each side. The Gemara answers: **This is** why we said that Ulla **was not precise, and** he was **not precise** in a manner that leads **to a stringency,** as one brings first fruits even from a tree that stands just sixteen cubits from the boundary, rather than 16½.

The *gemara* states that a square is larger than its inscribed circle by one quarter. This is the *gemara*'s standard working approximation using *pi* = 3. With a radius of one, the circumscribed square (side = 2) has area four; the circle has area *pi* × 1² = 3. The ratio is thus four to three, meaning the square exceeds the circle by exactly one quarter. Taking a quarter off the square's area of one thousand and twenty-four yields seven hundred and sixty-eight — identically the result of computing the circle directly: 16² × 3 = 256 × 3 = 768.

### 768 Amot Circle

So if *Ulla* is speaking of a circle of radius sixteen, the area in question is seven hundred and sixty-eight square *amot* per tree. But the *mishnah*'s figure is eight hundred and thirty-three. The circle is still smaller than what the *mishnah* requires, meaning the true radius must be somewhat more than sixteen *amot*.

### 16⅔ Amot Exact

The *gemara* resolves this as follows: *lo daku lechumra lo daku* — *Ulla* was indeed imprecise, but imprecise in the stringent direction. He rounded down from the true figure, which means he was more demanding, not more lenient, about the exemption from *bikurim*. The true radius is sixteen and two-thirds *amot*, as *Rashi* and *Tosafot* note that this is the correct *girsa*: (16⅔)² × 3 = approximately 833 square *amot* — matching the *mishnah* precisely. By stating "sixteen *amot*," *Ulla* ensured that anyone within sixteen *amot* of the boundary would certainly fall within the zone of nutrient-drawing, even though the exact cutoff is sixteen and two-thirds. The rounding was toward stringency, making the exemption from *bikurim* apply over the full correct range.

## Ulla Challenged

### Buy Tree & Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַקּוֹנֶה אִילָן וְקַרְקָעוֹ – מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא. מַאי, לָאו כׇּל שֶׁהוּא? לֹא; שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof against the opinion of Ulla. **Come** and **hear** the following mishna (*Bikkurim* 1:11): **One who buys a tree and its land brings** first fruits **and recites** the requisite Torah verses (Deuteronomy 26:5–11) over them. **What, is it not** referring to a case where one buys **any amount** of land with the tree? The Gemara rejects this claim: **No;** it is referring to a case where one buys **sixteen cubits** of land around the tree.

Having established *Ulla*'s rule, the *gemara* subjects it to a series of challenges from other *mishnayot*. The first: *hakoneh ilan vekarkao mevi vekoreh* — one who buys a tree together with its land brings *bikurim* and recites the declaration. The *gemara* asks whether *karkao* (its land) implies even the smallest plot of land suffices, which would contradict *Ulla*'s requirement for a full sixteen *amot* radius. The *gemara* deflects: *lo, tes-zayin amos* — no, it means specifically sixteen *amot* of surrounding land. The reply is admittedly forced, since one would have expected the *mishnah* to specify this explicitly.

### Two or Three Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: קָנָה שְׁנֵי אִילָנוֹת בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא. הָא שְׁלֹשָׁה – מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא; מַאי, לָאו כׇּל שֶׁהוּא? לָא; הָכָא נָמֵי שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an additional proof from another mishna (*Bikkurim* 1:6): If one **bought two trees in** the field **of another, he brings** first fruits **and does not recite** the verses, because the land does not belong to him. It may be inferred from here that if he bought **three** trees **he does bring** first fruits **and recite** the verses. **What, is it not** referring to a case where one buys **any amount** of land with the trees? The Gemara rejects this claim as well: **No; here too** it is referring to a case where he acquires **sixteen cubits** of land around the trees.

The second challenge: *koneh shenei ilanos besoch shel chaveiro mevi ve'eino koreh* — one who buys two trees in a neighbor's field brings *bikurim* but does not recite the declaration; *ha shelosha mevi vekoreh* — but three trees, and one does recite. The implication seems to be that buying three trees automatically includes some surrounding land sufficient for *bikurim* — and the *mishnah* elsewhere clarifies this land is the immediately adjacent area under and between the trees, not a sixteen-*amah* radius. The *gemara* again proposes: no, when three trees are purchased, what is included is the surrounding area up to sixteen *amot* in each direction. *Tosafot* notes that this strains the plain meaning of the *mishnah*, which elsewhere describes the acquired land as whatever lies directly beneath the foliage — but adds that if the foliage extends beyond sixteen *amot*, one would receive that; and if not, one would receive at least sixteen *amot* in each direction.

### Rabbi Akiva Tiny

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: קַרְקַע כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – חַיָּיב בַּפֵּאָה וּבַבִּכּוּרִים, וְכוֹתְבִין עָלָיו פְּרוֹזְבּוּל, וְנִקְנִין עִמָּהּ נְכָסִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם אַחְרָיוּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (*Pe'a* 3:6). **Rabbi Akiva says:** The owner of **land of any** size **is obligated in *pe'a* and in first fruits, and** a lender **can write a document that prevents the Sabbatical Year from abrogating an outstanding debt [*prosbol*] for** this land, so that loans he provided will not be canceled at the close of the Sabbatical Year, **and he can acquire property that does not serve as a guarantee,** i.e., movable property, along **with it.**

The third and most direct challenge comes from a statement of Rabbi Akiva: *karka kol shehu chayav bepe'ah uvevikurim* — even the smallest amount of land obligates in *pe'ah*, *bikurim*, and *prosbol*. The *prosbol* connection: a loan was customarily given only when the borrower held land that served as collateral, allowing the creditor to place liens. *Shmita* cancels such loans; *chazal* enacted the *prosbol* by which a creditor hands the debt over to *beit din*, enabling collection after *shemita*. This institution applies only to standard land-secured loans; even a tiny plot of land places the debt within that category. A further principle: *niknin imo nechasim she'ein lahem achrayot* — when real property is transferred, movable property (*chattel*) can be transferred along with it, even chattel not physically located on the land, and this too requires only the smallest parcel of real estate. And critically for our discussion, Rabbi Akiva states that *bikurim* applies even on the smallest amount of land — which seems directly contrary to *Ulla*'s position requiring sixteen *amot*.

### Wheat Not Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּחִיטֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** We are dealing **with wheat,** which is subject to the *halakhot* of first fruits and requires very little land to nourish it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments that the language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches:** Land **of any** size, which indicates even a miniscule amount, and all normal trees certainly require more land than that. The Gemara affirms: **Learn from it** that it is so.

The *gemara* resolves the challenge by distinguishing the subject matter: *be'chitei* — Rabbi Akiva is speaking about grain crops such as wheat and barley (*eretz chitah u'seorah*), not trees. The obligation of *bikurim* applies to all seven species, and for grain there is no requirement of sixteen *amot*, since grain does not generate the same nutrient-drawing issue as trees. Only for trees does *Ulla*'s requirement apply. The *gemara* reinforces this: the very language *kol shehu* (any amount) indicates we are speaking of grain, since you cannot plant a tree in an infinitesimally small plot of land — that language only makes sense for something like a single stalk of wheat. *Shema mina* — the distinction is textually supported.

## Boundary Tree

### Split Tree Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אִילָן – מִקְצָתוֹ בָּאָרֶץ וּמִקְצָתוֹ בְּחוּץ לָאָרֶץ – טֶבֶל וְחוּלִּין מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Ma'asrot* 2:22): If there is **a tree, part of which is in Eretz** Yisrael **and part of which is outside of Eretz** Yisrael, it is considered as though **untithed produce,** i.e., produce that is subject to the *halakhot* of *terumot* and tithes, **and non-sacred produce,** i.e., produce that is exempt from the *halakhot* of *terumot* and tithes, **are mixed together** in each one of this tree's fruits. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

The *gemara* introduces a *mishnah* that presses on the relationship between nutrient-drawing and *halachic* status: *ilan miktzaso ba'aretz u'miktzaso b'chutz la'aretz* — a tree whose trunk straddles the boundary between *Eretz Yisrael* and *chutz la'aretz*. Produce of such a tree raises the question of what is *tevel* (untithed, therefore forbidden) and what is *chulin* (ordinary, exempt from tithing obligations applicable only in *Eretz Yisrael*).

### Nutrient Flow Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: הַגָּדֵל בְּחִיּוּב – חַיָּיב, הַגָּדֵל בִּפְטוּר – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** With regard to the fruits in the part of the tree that is **growing in** a place where there is **an obligation** to separate tithes, i.e., in Eretz Yisrael, the owner is **obligated** to separate tithes. With regard to the fruits that are **growing in** a place where there is **an exemption** from separating tithes, i.e., outside of Eretz Yisrael, the owner is **exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי, אֶלָּא דְּמָר סָבַר: יֵשׁ בְּרֵירָה, וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין בְּרֵירָה;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **They disagree only in that** one **Sage,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **holds** that **there is** retroactive **designation,** and therefore it is assumed that the nourishment drawn from Eretz Yisrael sustained the fruit that grew on that side of the tree, and the nourishment drawn from outside Eretz Yisrael sustained the fruit that grew there. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that **there is no** retroactive **designation,** and the fruit is considered mixed.

*Rebbe* (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi) holds: *tevel v'chulin me'uravin zeh bazeh* — the produce of the entire tree is considered a mixture of *tevel* and *chulin*. One cannot identify which fruit grew from which side of the tree; the tree is a single organism, and the produce must be treated as a blend. One would therefore set aside half the normal amount of *terumot u'ma'asrot* from the total yield. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* disagrees: *hagadel bechiyuv chayav v'hagadel bip'tur patur* — produce on the *Eretz Yisrael* side of the vertical boundary is obligated; produce on the *chutz la'aretz* side is exempt. Each fruit is assigned to the half of the tree above which it grows.

### Breira Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל גָּדֵל בִּפְטוּר – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** **But** if the tree **grew** entirely **in** a place where there is **an exemption** from separating tithes, i.e., outside Eretz Yisrael, **all agree** that the owner is **exempt,** even though the tree might have roots within sixteen cubits of Eretz Yisrael and draw nourishment from there. This presents a difficulty for the opinion of Ulla, as he claims that the place from where a tree draws its nourishment is decisive with regard to first fruits.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּמַפְסִיק צוּנְמָא. אִי הָכִי, מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי? דְּהָדְרִי עׇרְבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where a rock divides** the roots up to the trunk, and therefore it is possible to distinguish between the parts of the tree that draw nutrients from Eretz Yisrael and the parts that draw nutrients from outside of Eretz Yisrael. The Gemara asks: **If so, what is the reasoning of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? Why does he view the fruits as being a mixture? The Gemara answers: He holds that although there is a division between the roots, they cannot be distinguished from one another, **as they then become mixed** in the body of the tree.

The *gemara* initially frames this dispute as one of *breira* — the doctrine of retroactive determination. The question is whether we can retroactively say that the fruit growing on the *Eretz Yisrael* side of the tree was always "the fruit of the *Eretz Yisrael* portion" and vice versa. The *gemara* then clarifies a critical limiting condition: *hacha b'mai askinan d'mafsik tzunma* — the *mishnah* here is dealing with a special case where a rock or boulder runs along the boundary line underground, physically preventing the roots of each side from crossing into the other. Without this qualification, *Ulla*'s principle would require us to say that even the *chutz la'aretz* side of the tree is drawing nutrients from *Eretz Yisrael* soil and should therefore be subject to *terumot u'ma'asrot*. With the rock dividing the roots, this concern is removed, and the debate between *Rebbe* and *Rabban Shimon* can proceed on its own terms.

### Trunk Mixing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: אַוֵּירָא מְבַלְבֵּל, וּמָר סָבַר: הַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי וְהַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And with regard to what** principle **do they disagree?** The Gemara answers: One **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds: The air** above the ground **mixes** the nutrients, **and** one **Sage,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **holds: This** part of the tree **stands alone and this** part of the tree **stands alone.** From the roots up to the branches, it is as if the tree were cut along the line of the border.

The question then shifts: given that the rock physically separates the roots on each side, why does *Rebbe* still treat the produce as mixed? If the nutrients are kept separate by the rock, why not simply say each side feeds its own fruit? The *gemara* answers: *mar savar avira mevalbel* — *Rebbe* holds that even if the roots are divided, the nutrients from both sides ultimately flow into the common trunk, mixing together there and feeding the entire tree indiscriminately. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, by contrast, holds *hai l'chudei ka'ei v'hai l'chudei ka'ei* — each side's nutrients remain in their own channel within the tree, feeding only the fruit on that corresponding side. Because the nutrients do not mix in the trunk according to *Rabban Shimon*, the boundary line can be extended vertically, and the fruit on each side is treated as having grown entirely from the nutrients of that side alone.

## 16 vs. 25 Amot

### 25 Amot Well

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה – וְתוּ לָא? וְהָא תְּנַן: מַרְחִיקִין אֶת הָאִילָן מִן הַבּוֹר, עֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ אַמָּה! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מֵיזָל – טוּבָא אָזְלִי, אַכְחוֹשֵׁי לָא מַכְחֲשִׁי אֶלָּא עַד שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה, טְפֵי לָא מַכְחֲשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty against Ulla's opinion from a different perspective: **And** do roots extend **sixteen cubits and no more? Didn't we learn** in a mishna (25b): **One must distance a tree twenty-five cubits from a cistern?** This indicates that tree roots reach more than sixteen cubits. **Abaye said:** The roots **extend farther,** but **they weaken the ground only up to sixteen cubits;** with regard to an area any **more** distant than that, **they do not weaken** the ground.

A further challenge to *Ulla*'s sixteen-*amah* rule arises from a different *mishnah*: *merchikin et ha'ilan min habor esrim v'chamesh amah* — a tree must be kept twenty-five *amot* away from a pit or well. If roots extend their damaging reach only up to sixteen *amot*, why does the *halacha* require a distance of twenty-five? *Abaye* resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing between two different effects of roots. Roots will indeed grow far — twenty-five *amot* and beyond — and at that distance they can invade and damage the walls of a well or pit. However, the significant *drawing* of soil nutrients, the weakening of the ground, occurs only up to sixteen *amot*. *Ulla*'s concern for *bikurim* is predicated on where the tree is actually drawing its sustenance from, which is within sixteen *amot*. The rule of twenty-five *amot* by a well addresses physical intrusion and structural damage, which roots can cause at greater distances. The two figures thus measure different phenomena and do not contradict each other.

## Reish Lakish & Yochanan

### Gazlan Bikurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר, בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִילָן הַסָּמוּךְ לַמֶּיצֶר בְּתוֹךְ שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גַּזְלָן הוּא, וְאֵין מְבִיאִין מִמֶּנּוּ בִּכּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning this matter the Gemara relates that **when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael **he said: Reish Lakish raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** With regard to **a tree** that is **within sixteen cubits of a boundary, what is** the *halakha*? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** The owner **is a robber, and one does not bring first fruits from it.**

When Rav Dimi came (from *Eretz Yisrael* to Babylonia), he reported a discussion between *Reish Lakish* and *Rabbi Yochanan*. *Reish Lakish* asked: what is the status of a tree situated close to a boundary line — does the *gazlan* designation prevent the owner from bringing *bikurim*? *Rabbi Yochanan* answered, as reported by Rav Dimi: *gazlan hu ve'ein mevi'in mimenu bikurim* — it is indeed a *gazlan*, and one may not bring *bikurim* from it. This aligns with *Ulla*'s ruling as we have been analyzing it.

### Allows Bikurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֶחָד אִילָן הַסָּמוּךְ לַמֶּיצֶר, וְאֶחָד אִילָן הַנּוֹטֶה – מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא; שֶׁעַל מְנָת כֵּן הִנְחִיל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael, he related that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Both** in the case of **a tree that is close to a boundary and a tree that leans** into a neighbor's yard, **one brings** first fruits **and recites** the verses, **as it was on this condition that Joshua apportioned Eretz** Yisrael **to the Jewish people,** i.e., that they would not be particular about such matters.

However, a subsequent report by Ravin tells a different story: *Rabbi Yochanan* reversed his position. Whether the tree is close to the boundary or whether its canopy overhangs the neighbor's property, *echad ilan hasamuch lemeitzar ve'echad ilan hanoteh mevi vekoreh* — in either case the owner may bring *bikurim* and recite the declaration. All the evidence brought through the *sugya* had, in a sense, been pushing in this direction, and here we finally have an explicit ruling against *Ulla*'s original formulation. The rationale is striking: *al menat ken hinchil Yehoshua le-Yisrael et ha'aretz* — Joshua apportioned the land of *Eretz Yisrael* to the Jewish people with the understanding that this is how things work. Trees near borders draw nutrients from adjacent plots; branches overhang into neighboring fields. These realities are built into the fabric of land ownership in *Eretz Yisrael*, and Joshua's allocation assumed and accommodated them. Because you have a right to these nutrients as part of the normal conditions of land use, it cannot be said that the produce is growing from land that was "given to someone else." You may therefore recite *adamah asher natata li* without qualification. The concept of *al menat ken* effectively transforms what *Ulla* framed as *gezel* (even if permitted) into something entirely within one's rights, thereby removing the obstacle to *bikurim* entirely. The *sugya* thus ends on a note that somewhat undercuts *Ulla*'s framing, while preserving the underlying agronomic observation about root reach.

## Branch Cutting

### Plow Clearance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אִילָן שֶׁהוּא נוֹטֶה לִשְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ – קוֹצֵץ מְלֹא הַמַּרְדֵּעַ, עַל גַּבֵּי הַמַּחֲרֵישָׁה. וּבֶחָרוּב וּבַשִּׁקְמָה – כְּנֶגֶד הַמִּשְׁקוֹלֶת. בֵּית הַשְּׁלָחִין – כׇּל הָאִילָן כְּנֶגֶד הַמִּשְׁקוֹלֶת. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל אִילַן סְרָק כְּנֶגֶד הַמִּשְׁקוֹלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a tree that leans into the field of another,** the neighbor **may cut** the branches **to the height of an ox goad** raised **over the plow,** in places where the land is to be plowed, so that the branches do not impede the use of the plow. **And** in the case **of a carob tree and** the case **of a sycamore tree,** whose abundance of branches cast shade that is harmful to plants, all the branches overhanging one's property may be removed **along the plumb line,** i.e., along a line perpendicular to the boundary separating the fields. And if the neighbor's field is **an irrigated field, all** branches of **the tree** are removed **along the plumb line. Abba Shaul says: All barren trees** are cut **along the plumb line.**

The *mishnah* now addresses a different problem arising from overhanging branches. If a tree's canopy extends over a neighbor's field — a field where grain is grown and plowed — the neighbor may cut back the branches to a height sufficient to allow plowing. The standard of measurement is *melo hamardea al gabei hamachreishah*: the full height of the *mardea* (the goad or prod that the plowman holds over his animals while walking behind the plow) above the *machreishah* (the plow itself). In practical terms, this means enough clearance for the plowman and his equipment to pass through without obstruction. An exception applies to carob trees and sycamore trees. These trees produce such dense, heavy foliage that even cutting to plowing height would leave a substantial overhang causing significant shade damage to the field. In these cases, the neighbor may cut the entire overhang — *keneged hamishkolet*, opposite the plumb line. That is, one drops a plumb line straight down from the boundary marker, and any branch extending beyond that vertical plane may be removed entirely, without limit as to height. The rule is further expanded for *beit hashlachin* — an irrigated field. Irrigation requires maximum sunlight and is especially sensitive to shade. Therefore, in the case of an irrigated field, any tree — not just carob and sycamore — whose branches overhang may be cut back entirely opposite the plumb line. *Abba Shaul* adds a further ruling: *kol ilan srak keneged hamishkolet* — any non-fruit-bearing tree may be cut back entirely opposite the plumb line. The significance of his position, and precisely which case of the *mishnah* he is responding to, is the subject of immediate *gemara* discussion.

## Abba Shaul Position

### Which Case?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: אַבָּא שָׁאוּל – אַרֵישָׁא קָאֵי, אוֹ אַסֵּיפָא קָאֵי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before them:** Is **Abba Shaul referring to the first clause** of the mishna, which states that a tree extending into a neighbor's field is cut only to the height of an ox goad, and Abba Shaul maintains that barren trees have the same *halakha* as carob and sycamore trees in that they are cut along the plumb line? **Or** is he **referring to the latter clause,** which discusses an irrigated field, and he permits cutting only barren trees along the plumb line, but not fruit trees?

The *gemara* opens with a question: is *Abba Shaul* addressing the *reisha* (the first case of the *mishnah*, concerning a non-irrigated field) or the *seifa* (the second case, concerning an irrigated field)? In the first case, the *Tanna Kamma* limited complete removal to carob and sycamore; in the second, complete removal applied to all trees. *Abba Shaul* is either expanding the first case (adding all non-fruit trees to the list of those that may be fully cut) or restricting the second (limiting the irrigated-field rule to non-fruit trees). Is he being more lenient toward the aggrieved neighbor, or more lenient toward the tree owner?

### Baraita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: בֵּית הַשְּׁלָחִין – אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָאִילָן כְּנֶגֶד הַמִּשְׁקוֹלֶת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַצֵּל רַע לְבֵית הַשְּׁלָחִין. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אַרֵישָׁא קָאֵי! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **an irrigated field, Abba Shaul says: All** types of **trees** are cut **along the plumb line, because the shade is harmful to an irrigated field.** This shows that he does not dispute the *halakha* of the latter clause. **Learn from it** that **he is referring to the first clause** of the mishna. The Gemara affirms: **Learn from it** that it is so.

A *baraita* resolves part of the question: it explicitly states that for an irrigated field, *Abba Shaul* rules *kol ha'ilan keneged hamishkolet* — any tree may be fully cut back, because the shade is harmful to an irrigated field. Since *Abba Shaul* explicitly allows all trees in the irrigated-field case, he cannot be the one introducing a restriction there. His position on the irrigated field aligns with the *Tanna Kamma*'s ruling in the *mishnah*.

### Non-Irrigated

It follows that *Abba Shaul*'s distinction between fruit and non-fruit trees must address the *reisha*, the non-irrigated field. In that case, the *Tanna Kamma* permitted complete removal only for carob and sycamore. *Abba Shaul* expands this: any non-fruit-bearing tree may be fully cut back even in a non-irrigated field. *Shema mina areisha ka'i* — the *gemara* accepts this conclusion.

### Kol Expansion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: ״כׇּל אִילַן סְרָק״. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא אַרֵישָׁא קָאֵי – הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי כׇּל אִילָן; אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ אַסֵּיפָא קָאֵי, אִילַן סְרָק מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ?! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אַרֵישָׁא קָאֵי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that the wording of **the mishna is also precise, as it teaches: All barren trees. Granted, if you say** that Abba Shaul **is referring to the first clause** of the mishna, **this** is the reason **that** Abba Shaul **teaches: All** barren **trees. But if you say** that **he is referring to the latter clause, he should have** said simply: **Barren trees,** as the first *tanna* permits one to cut down any type of tree. **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from it** that he is **referring to the first clause?** The Gemara affirms: **Learn from it** that it is so.

Rav Ashi adds a confirmation from the text of the *mishnah* itself. *Abba Shaul*'s language is *kol ilan srak* — "all non-fruit trees." The word *kol* (all/every) signals expansion. If *Abba Shaul* were responding to the irrigated-field case and limiting it to non-fruit trees, the word *kol* would be oddly chosen; one would simply say "non-fruit tree," not "all non-fruit trees." The word *kol* makes sense only if he is broadening the prior category — taking the *Tanna Kamma*'s narrow list of carob and sycamore and expanding it to all non-fruit-bearing trees. This linguistic argument confirms that *Abba Shaul* is arguing on the *reisha*, not the *seifa*. *Shema mina.*

## Public Domain Tree

### Camel Clearance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אִילָן שֶׁהוּא נוֹטֶה לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, קוֹצֵץ כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא גָּמָל עוֹבֵר – וְרוֹכְבוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: גָּמָל טָעוּן פִּשְׁתָּן אוֹ חֲבִילֵי זְמוֹרוֹת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָאִילָן כְּנֶגֶד הַמִּשְׁקוֹלֶת, מִפְּנֵי הַטּוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a tree that extends into the public domain, one cuts** its branches **so that a camel can pass** beneath the tree **with its rider** sitting on it. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** One cuts enough branches that **a camel loaded with flax or bundles of branches** can pass beneath it. **Rabbi Shimon says:** One cuts **all** branches of **the tree that** extend into the public domain **along the plumb line,** so that they do not hang over the public area at all, **due to ritual impurity.**

The final *mishnah* of the chapter addresses a tree overhanging *reshut harabbim* — the public thoroughfare. Here the concern is not agricultural damage but physical clearance for those using the public way. The rule is: *kotzetz kedei sheyehei gamal over verokhvo* — the branches must be cut back sufficiently to allow a camel and its rider to pass through without obstruction. *Rabbi Yehuda* refines the standard: *gamal ta'un pishtan o havilei zmorot* — the measure is not the height of a rider on a camel but of a camel laden with flax or bundles of branches. The load sits atop the camel and extends above the rider's head, requiring a higher clearance. *Rabbi Shimon* takes a more sweeping position: *kol ha'ilan keneged hamishkolet mipnei hatumah* — the entire overhang must be removed, opposite the plumb line, on account of ritual impurity (*tumah*). His concern is for *kohanim* and others who could become *tamei* by passing under branches that form an *ohel* (tent or canopy) over a corpse or part of a corpse. Since corpses or pieces thereof might be found or carried in public areas, and since *tumah* spreads under any covering, any overhanging branch creates a potential corridor of impurity through *reshut harabbim*.

## Current vs. Future

### Regrowth Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן תְּנָא דְּבִנְזָקִין – בָּתַר אוּמְדָּנָא דְּהַשְׁתָּא אָזְלִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that **with regard to damage** one **follows the current assessment,** and future damage is not taken into account? The mishna states that the tree's branches are cut to the height of a camel and its rider or load but no more, despite the fact that they will certainly grow again.

The *gemara* raises a structural difficulty: the *mishnah*'s standard — cut to allow a camel and rider to pass — seems to address only the present situation. Branches grow back. Why does the *mishnah* not require a more permanent solution, such as removing the entire overhang? What principle justifies limiting the remedy to the present clearance need?

### Regular Maintenance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: בְּמַחֲלוֹקֶת שְׁנוּיָה, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא – דִּתְנַן: אֵין עוֹשִׂין חָלָל תַּחַת רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – בּוֹרוֹת, שִׁיחִין וּמְעָרוֹת. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מַתִּיר, בִּכְדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא עֲגָלָה מְהַלֶּכֶת – וּטְעוּנַהּ אֲבָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says:** This *halakha* **is taught as a dispute, and it is** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer. As we learned** in a mishna (60a): One **may not make** an empty **space beneath the public domain** by digging **pits, ditches, or caves. Rabbi Eliezer permits** one to dig a pit if it is subsequently covered with material strong **enough that a wagon loaded with stones can travel on it** without it collapsing. If the cover can withstand such weight when the pit is dug, it is permitted, despite the fact that the cover might eventually rot.

*Reish Lakish* answers: this reflects a deliberate approach, and it follows the position of *Rabbi Eliezer*. The *gemara* cites a *mishnah* in Bava Kamma: the *chachamim* forbid digging cavities beneath *reshut harabbim* — for wells, cisterns, or tunnels — because the roof might eventually collapse. *Rabbi Eliezer* permits it provided the ceiling can currently bear the weight of a wagon laden with stones, attending only to the present structural integrity. The *chachamim* reject this because the ceiling may weaken undetected over time, eventually collapsing without warning. Our *mishnah*, which likewise asks only about the current situation rather than anticipating future growth, accordingly follows *Rabbi Eliezer*.

### Pruning vs. Tunnels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, הָתָם – זִימְנִין דְּמַפְחִית וְלָאו אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ, אֲבָל הָכָא – קַמָּא קַמָּא קָא קְיִיץ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: You** may **even say** that the mishna here represents the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who prohibit one from digging beneath the public domain under any circumstances. The difference is that **there,** the cover **will occasionally deteriorate, and** as this matter is **not on his mind** it will cause damage. **But here,** as **each** branch grows **he cuts it off.** Since the potential cause of damage is visible, there is no concern that it might be neglected.

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees and holds that even the *rabbanan* can accept the *mishnah*'s approach. The difference is fundamental: *hasam zimnin de-mifchat ve-lav adatei* — in the case of the underground tunnel, the ceiling deteriorates gradually and invisibly; no one knows when it will give way, making the danger essentially impossible to monitor. *Aval hacha kamma kamma ka-kayitz lei* — here, with branches, one can observe regrowth incrementally and prune regularly as needed. Because the hazard is visible and manageable through routine maintenance, there is no structural reason to demand more than the present clearance even according to the *rabbanan*. The *mishnah* can therefore be read as consistent with the majority position.

## Flax vs. Person

### Height Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: גָּמָל טָעוּן פִּשְׁתָּן אוֹ חֲבִילֵי זְמוֹרוֹת. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְפִישׁ, אוֹ דִּלְמָא שִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבָּנַן נְפִישׁ?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if the branches of a tree extend into the public domain, one may cut them to allow a camel and its rider to pass underneath; **Rabbi Yehuda says:** One cuts enough branches that **a camel loaded with flax or bundles of branches** can pass beneath it. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is **Rabbi Yehuda's measure greater, or** is **perhaps the Rabbis' measure greater?** Which reaches a greater height, a camel and rider or a camel loaded with flax?

Having recorded the dispute between the *rabbanan* (measuring by camel and rider) and *Rabbi Yehuda* (measuring by a camel laden with flax), the *gemara* raises a threshold question: which is taller — a person seated atop a camel, or a load of flax on a camel?

### Flax Must Be Higher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא דְּשִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבָּנַן נְפִישׁ! דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ שִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְפִישׁ, רַבָּנַן – בְּשִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הֵיכִי עָבְדִי? וְאֶלָּא מַאי, שִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבָּנַן נְפִישׁ? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה – בְּשִׁיעוּרָא דְּרַבָּנַן מַאי עָבֵיד? אֶפְשָׁר דִּגְחִין וְחָלֵיף תּוּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **obvious that the Rabbis' measure** is **greater, as, if it enters your mind** that **Rabbi Yehuda's measure** is **greater, how would the Rabbis act in** the circumstance of **Rabbi Yehuda's measure?** It is clear that a camel will have to pass beneath the tree with a burden, and it would not be able to do so. The Gemara expresses surprise at this claim: **Rather, what** then would you say? That **the Rabbis' measure** is **greater?** If so, **how would Rabbi Yehuda act in** the circumstance of **the Rabbis' measure?** It is also clear that a camel will have to pass there with its rider. The Gemara answers: **It is possible for** the rider **to bend over and to pass underneath** the branches.

The *gemara* first establishes that the flax load must be the taller of the two, for the following reason: *de'i salka daitach shiura de-Rabbi Yehuda nafish rabbanan be-shiura de-Rabbi Yehuda heichi avidi* — were the flax load the shorter measure, the *rabbanan* who require clearance for a camel and rider would obviously be requiring even more clearance than *Rabbi Yehuda*, and on the occasions when a camel carrying flax comes through, there would be no protection at all. Since both *rabbanan* and *Rabbi Yehuda* are addressing the realistic hazards of *reshut harabbim* — and camels carrying loads of flax are a foreseeable occurrence — the *rabbanan* could not have permitted a standard that fails to protect against loaded camels. The flax load must therefore be higher than the mounted rider, and *Rabbi Yehuda*'s standard is more demanding in this regard. But this immediately raises a reverse question: if the flax load is higher, how does *Rabbi Yehuda* accommodate the *rabbanan*'s case — the person riding on a camel? *Rabbi Yehuda* demands clearance only for the flax height; what about the mounted rider who may be taller than the flax? The *gemara* answers: *efshar de-gachin ve-chalif* — it is possible for a rider simply to duck. A person can lower his head and lean forward when passing under an obstruction. A load of flax cannot duck. Therefore, *Rabbi Yehuda* requires clearance sufficient for the immovable load but does not require it for the rider, who can accommodate himself. The *rabbanan*, by contrast, require sufficient clearance for the rider at full height without ducking — but since the flax load is even taller, the *rabbanan*'s standard by itself does not address the flax case, which is the specific concern that *Rabbi Yehuda* makes explicit.

## Tumah Overhang

### Dead Body Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָאִילָן כְּנֶגֶד הַמִּשְׁקוֹלֶת, מִפְּנֵי הַטּוּמְאָה. תָּנָא: מִפְּנֵי אֹהֶל הַטּוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Shimon says:** One cuts **all** branches of **the tree** that extend into the public domain **along the plumb line, due to ritual impurity.** The Gemara explains: A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita* that this is **due to ritual impurity** imparted in **a tent.** Branches over a corpse might create a tent, thereby transferring impurity to whatever is beneath the branches, rendering impure those passing under the tree in the public domain.

*Rabbi Shimon*'s ruling that the entire overhang must be removed on account of *tumah* is recorded in the *mishnah* with the phrase *mipnei hatumah*. A *baraita* elaborates: *mipnei ohel hatumah* — specifically because of the *ohel* (tent-like covering) created by the overhanging branches. The *ohel* effect means that any *tumah* located beneath the canopy of branches — most critically, a corpse or part of a corpse — spreads its impurity to everyone and everything under that same canopy. Since *kohanim* and others traversing *reshut harabbim* may pass under such overhanging branches, and since there may be a corpse or fragment thereof in the public domain, every overhang creates a potential conduit of *tumah*.

### Ohel Not Dropped

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, ״מִפְּנֵי הַטּוּמְאָה״ תְּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise at this statement: It is **obvious** that this is the reason. **We** already **learned** that it is **due to ritual impurity.** How else could ritual impurity be transferred through branches other than by means of a tent?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִמַּתְנִיתִין, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: דִּלְמָא מַיְיתֵי עוֹרֵב טוּמְאָה וְשָׁדֵי הָתָם, וְסַגְיָא בְּדַחְלוּלִי בְּעָלְמָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If** this is learned **from the mishna** alone, **I would say** that the concern is that **perhaps a crow might bring** a source of **impurity** and perch on the tree's branches **and throw it there. And** if that were the only concern, **a mere scarecrow [*bedaḥlulei*] would be sufficient** to frighten the crows away and prevent that type of impurity. Therefore, the *tanna* of the *baraita* **teaches us** that the concern is due to impurity imparted in a tent.

One might ask: is the concern perhaps simpler — that a raven or other bird might drop a piece of *tamei* matter directly onto one of the branches, making those specific branches a source of *tumah* from above? If so, the problem would be contact with *tumah* landing on the branch, not the *ohel* effect of the overhang itself. The *gemara* refutes this interpretation by a process of elimination: *ve'sagi bi-dilulei be-alma* — if the concern were something dropped onto the branches, it would be sufficient to thin the branches (*dilulei*), ensuring no piece of *tamei* material could be supported or lodged there. There would be no need to remove the entire overhang. But *Rabbi Shimon* requires total removal, not merely thinning. This demonstrates that the concern is the *ohel* created by the canopy itself: even a full, intact overhang with nothing resting on it spreads *tumah* from below by forming a tent over a source of impurity in the public domain. Because no amount of pruning eliminates the *ohel* effect — only total removal of the overhang does — *Rabbi Shimon* demands that the branches be cut back entirely opposite the plumb line.

With this we conclude the second *perek* of Bava Batra. *Hadran alach lo yachfor.*
