# Sanhedrin 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Psulei Eidus Overview

### Mishna: Gambled & Lenders

The *daf* opens in the middle of a discussion on 26b, with the words *amar Rav Yehuda eimatai*, building on the *mishna* that lists categories of people disqualified from serving as witnesses — *psulei eidus* — on account of various transgressions. The common denominator running through the *mishna's* list is that all of these individuals are engaged in sins committed for the sake of financial gain.

The *mishna* enumerates: *misachek be-kubiya* (gamblers), *malveh be-ribbit* (one who lends money with interest, which is a clear *issur de-oraita*), *mafrichai yonim* (a type of gambling involving pigeons), and *sochrei shevi'it* (those who conduct commerce with *shemitah* produce). Some of these are *de-oraita* prohibitions, while others are *de-rabbanan*. Gambling, for example, is *de-rabbanan*, stemming from either the problem of *asmachta kanya lo kanya* — the view that a transaction based on a conditional wager is not legally binding — or, as *Tosafot* notes, the concern that such individuals are not *oskim be-yishuvo shel olam*, that is, they are not engaged in productive labor that sustains society. The underlying worry in all these cases is that a person willing to transgress — whether *de-oraita* or *de-rabbanan* — for monetary gain may be susceptible to accepting a bribe in exchange for false testimony. That is the essential common denominator.

### De-Oraita vs De-Rabbanan

When an individual knowingly and willingly violates a *de-oraita* prohibition for financial gain, his disqualification as a witness is itself *de-oraita*. The verse *al tashet yadcha im rasha lihyot eid chamas* — "do not put your hand with the wicked to be a false witness" — establishes the category of *eid chamas*, understood to refer to one who is motivated by financial gain. One who deliberately transgresses a *de-oraita* prohibition for such gain is *pasul de-oraita*.

However, the *mishna's* cases are all assumed to carry only *de-rabbanan* disqualification. Either the underlying problem is itself only rabbinic — such as *asmachta lo kanya* or the failure to be *oskim be-yishuvo shel olam* — or, as *Tosafot* explains for the case of *malveh be-ribbit*, either we are dealing with *ribbit de-rabbanan*, or the person has rationalized the behavior and does not truly view himself as willfully transgressing a *de-oraita* prohibition. After all, the borrower entered the arrangement consensually, with a contract — so it does not feel like genuine theft. Similarly, *shemitah* in the Babylonian context is *de-rabbanan*, rendering these cases all *de-rabbanan*. Nevertheless, an individual who transgresses a *de-oraita* prohibition knowingly, willingly, and for monetary gain would incur a *de-oraita* disqualification.

### Rav Yehuda's Condition

Rav Yehuda's qualifying statement — that these individuals are invalid only when they have no other gainful employment — appears to apply specifically to the gambling cases (*misachek be-kubiya* and *mafrichai yonim*) and does not straightforwardly extend to the other two categories.

## Hachraza Principle

### Rav Elazar Rule

The *gemara* then cites Rabbi Abahu in the name of Rav Elazar: *halacha ke-Rav Yehuda* — we follow Rav Yehuda at least in the gambling cases, where a person who has other gainful employment may not be disqualified. Rabbi Abahu then adds a second ruling in the name of Rav Elazar: *kullan tzerichin hachraza be-veit din* — all those listed in the *mishna* require a formal court announcement before their disqualification takes effect. This principle applies specifically to *psul de-rabbanan*.

The logic is as follows. When a *de-oraita* disqualification applies — say, someone committed an act of *gezeila* (robbery) in January and thereby became *pasul de-oraita* — his disqualification is effective from the moment of the transgression. If witnesses come in July and establish that he was a *gazlan* back in January, every testimony he gave from January onward is retroactively invalid. But when the disqualification is only *de-rabbanan*, the Rabbis have the authority to define when the *psul* takes effect, and they rule that it begins only once a formal announcement — *hachraza be-veit din* — has been made publicly. Until that point, all prior testimony stands. This is a critical and far-reaching principle in the laws of *psulei eidus de-rabbanan*.

### Shepherd Debate

The *gemara* turns to the case of a shepherd. It was previously established that shepherds are *pasul de-rabbanan* because they tend to graze their flocks on land that does not belong to them. The question is whether even a shepherd requires *hachraza be-veit din* before his disqualification takes effect. Rav Acha and Ravina dispute this. One holds that *hachraza* is required; the other holds it is not. *Rashi* explains why a shepherd might be different from the other cases: whereas a gambler's activity may be known only to a small circle, a shepherd's profession is public knowledge, observed daily. If everyone already knows he is a shepherd, one might argue that no formal announcement is needed — his status is self-evident.

### Stam Ro'eh Explained

The *gemara* raises an apparent difficulty: there is a teaching attributed to Rav in the name of Rav Yehuda that *stam ro'eh pasul* — a shepherd is presumed invalid without requiring any additional investigation. This fits well with the view that no *hachraza* is required, since the shepherd's status is publicly known. But according to the view that even a shepherd requires *hachraza be-veit din*, what meaning does *stam ro'eh pasul* carry? His disqualification does not arise automatically.

The *gemara* resolves this: *de-bistama machrizan alei* — the statement does not mean the general public may simply assume the shepherd is invalid. Rather, it means that when a *beit din* is required to make an announcement, it will issue one regarding any shepherd who has not affirmatively established his innocence. The presumption of guilt shifts the burden of proof onto the shepherd, but the actual *psul* still takes effect only through the announcement. The phrase *stam ro'eh pasul* thus describes the evidential posture of *beit din* — the shepherd bears the burden — but not an automatic, announcement-free disqualification.

## Gazlan Invalidity

### Rav Pappa Case

A practical case illustrates the stakes. A certain document had been signed by two witnesses who were *gazlanim*. Rav Pappa bar Shmuel was inclined to uphold the document's validity on the grounds that no *hachraza be-veit din* had been issued regarding these witnesses, and therefore they had not yet been formally disqualified.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava challenged this reasoning sharply: *nehi da-beinan hachraza be-gazlan de-rabbanan, be-gazlan de-oraita mi beinan hachraza?* — granted, we require *hachraza* for a *de-rabbanan* disqualification, but does a *de-oraita* disqualification require *hachraza* as well? Certainly not. A genuine *gazlan* — one whose robbery constitutes a *de-oraita* transgression done knowingly and for gain — is *pasul* from the moment he committed the act, regardless of whether any court announcement was ever made. The *hachraza* requirement is a rabbinic mechanism to protect previously accepted testimony, and it applies only where the *psul* itself is rabbinic. This distinction between *de-oraita* and *de-rabbanan* invalidity will resurface in the discussion of *eidim zomemim*.

## Davar Acher Case

### Rav Nachman Rule

The *gemara* records that Rav Nachman said: *ochlei davar acher psulin le-eidus* — those who eat *davar acher* are invalid as witnesses. The phrase *davar acher*, ordinarily a euphemism for pig meat, is explained by *Rashi* — and endorsed by *Tosafot* — as referring here to Jews who sustain themselves from charitable funds distributed by non-Jews. This was considered a *chillul Hashem*, an affront to communal dignity. *Rashi* frames the disqualification under the rubric of *rasha de-chamas*: such a person is willing to compromise their dignity and integrity when under financial pressure, and we therefore fear they may be susceptible to giving false testimony for money. This would be a *de-rabbanan* invalidity.

### Public vs. Private

The *gemara* immediately qualifies the ruling. *Hani mili be-farhesya aval be-tzina lo* — this applies only when the individual accepts these funds openly and publicly. If done quietly, the person demonstrates sensitivity to social norms and does not thereby compromise their credibility. Furthermore, even when it is done publicly, disqualification applies only if the person had a more discreet alternative: *de-efshar leih le-izonei be-tzina* — if they could have managed this privately and chose not to, their willingness to humiliate themselves publicly for money *— ve-kamvazyei nafshai be-farhesya —* reveals the kind of character that might compromise testimony for financial benefit. But if there was no alternative — *lo efshar leih* — and the only way to obtain subsistence was through public acceptance of non-Jewish charity, then *chiyyusei hu*: people do what they must to survive, and the fact of their need does not translate into a readiness to accept bribes. One does not leap from dignitary compromise born of desperation to the willingness to testify falsely.

## Sexual Transgression

### Chashud Arayos Valid

Rav Nachman then states: *ha-chashud al ha-arayos kasher le-eidus* — one who is suspected of forbidden sexual conduct remains valid as a witness. This is a striking ruling. Throughout the preceding discussion, the principle has been that one who compromises halakhic standards for monetary gain becomes suspect as a witness. But suspicion of *arayos* — even of serious sexual transgressions — does not invalidate. The key word here is *chashud*: the person is merely suspected; no concrete transgression has been established. Bare suspicion — even strong suspicion — is insufficient grounds for disqualification. We do not know that he actually violated anything.

### Rav Sheshet Query

Rav Sheshet objects: *ana marei achil arba'in be-katfei ve-kasher?* — can someone who deserves forty lashes still be valid as a witness? This question presupposes that the person in question actually did something deserving of punishment. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* explain that the reference is to *malkus de-rabbanan* administered to someone whose public conduct around women has been so egregious that rabbinic penalty is imposed — despite the absence of proven *de-oraita* transgression. Rav Sheshet's surprise: even such a person, publicly censured and lashed, remains *kasher*?

The *gemara* does not provide a formal counter-argument. The answer, understood in context, is that being the subject of suspicion and even rabbinic censure does not translate into suspicion of false testimony. A person may behave reprehensibly in one domain without that reflecting on their integrity as a witness in another.

### Money vs. Arayos

This leads to a conceptually important distinction. All the cases of *psul* examined thus far involve compromising halakhic integrity for *monetary* gain, and the concern is that such a person — a *rasha de-chamas* — can be bought. But *arayos* involves a different *yetzer*, a different category of desire. Even if someone has a strong drive toward forbidden sexual conduct, that does not mean they are susceptible to being bribed for false testimony. Money and sexual desire are distinct motivations, and a weakness in one domain does not establish vulnerability in the other. *Tosafot* raises this question and suggests that the *yetzer ha-ra* for *arayos* is a specific passion that does not transfer to monetary matters, and therefore we do not assume it will translate into the kind of compromised testimony we fear from a *rasha de-chamas*. The principle emerging from Chazal's analysis is subtle but important: transgression in one area of life does not automatically impugn one's reliability in another.

### Woman's Status

Rava adds a qualification: *u-modeh Rav Nachman le-inyan eidus isha she-hu pasul* — even Rav Nachman agrees that when it comes to testimony about a woman's marital status — for example, whether her husband died or divorced her — this individual who is suspected of *arayos* is disqualified. The concern there is not that he will be bribed, but that he may have a personal stake: perhaps he wishes to marry the woman and therefore testifies falsely that her husband died.

Ravina — some say it was Rav Pappa — refines this further: *lo amran ela le-afuka* — we disqualify his testimony only when he is testifying to free the woman (establish that she is available to remarry). But if he is testifying that she is married — *le-ayulei* — he would be believed. The *gemara* entertains the counter-thought: perhaps he would also lie to establish that she is married, since, as the verse says, *mayim genuvim yimtaku* — stolen waters are sweet; perhaps an illicit affair with a married woman is particularly attractive to such a person. The *gemara* rejects this: *de-kama de-kaimi hacha shechica lei* — as long as she is single, she is more available to him, and he has no incentive to falsely testify that she is married. The disqualification is thus limited and situational, not formal. There is no categorical *psul* — only a specific domain, tied to the precise area of his suspected misconduct, where his testimony cannot be credited.

## Harvest Theft

### Nissan/Tishrei Theft

Rav Nachman states: *ganav Nissan ve-ganav Tishrei lo shemei ganav* — one who steals during *Nissan* or *Tishrei* is not thereby called a thief. These are the great harvest seasons — the wheat harvest and the ingathering — and Rav Nachman's ruling addresses the case of a farmhand who helps himself to a bit of food during these busy agricultural periods. Even though the food belongs to the field's owner and taking it would technically constitute *gezeila*, the person's strong rationalization — the sense that harvesting and eating a little is natural and expected — means that this behavior does not mark him as the kind of deliberate, knowing violator for whose testimony we should fear. The transgression is real, but the psychology is that of rationalization rather than willful lawbreaking for gain.

### Sharecropper Logic

This principle extends with particular force to the *arisa* — the sharecropper — who already has a share in the harvest. When a sharecropper takes a small additional portion — *davar mu'at* — after the work is complete — *she-nigmera melachto* — the sense that he has earned more than his strict legal share makes the taking feel like anything but stealing.

### Arisa vs. Tenant

The *gemara* distinguishes the *arisa* (sharecropper) from a paid tenant farmer. The farmhands of Rav Zvid serve as an illustration: one stole a measure of barley, another stole a cluster of grapes, and Rav Zvid disqualified both. *Rashi* indicates that the basis for disqualification is precisely that these were paid laborers, not sharecroppers. Without the sharecropper's partial ownership interest, the rationalization logic applies with less force, and the taking is treated as real theft with real consequences for witness validity.

### Gravediggers

A further illustration: certain gravediggers buried a corpse on the first day of *Shavuot*, which is prohibited. Proper procedure requires waiting until the second day of *Yom Tov*. Rav Pappa placed them in *cherem* and disqualified them from testimony, reasoning that they had committed a transgression for the sake of monetary gain — they were paid to perform the burial — and we therefore fear they can be bought off for false testimony.

### Kapara Logic

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua disagreed and reinstated their validity. When Rav Pappa pressed the point — *ve-resha'im ninhu*, but are they not wicked for having done this for money? — Rav Huna replied: *savri mitzvah ka avdi* — they understood themselves to be performing a *mitzvah*. Burying the dead is a sacred obligation, and they rationalized that the *mitzvah* of burial superseded the *Yom Tov* restriction.

When Rav Pappa countered that they had already been placed in *cherem* and surely knew their conduct was prohibited, the response was equally telling: *savri kapara ka avdi lan rabbanan* — they said to themselves that the *cherem* itself served as atonement for having violated *Yom Tov*, but they still believed the burial was the right thing to do. People find ways to rationalize their actions, and when that rationalization is operative — when they are not acting with clear-eyed knowledge that they are committing a deliberate violation for gain — they do not incur *psul de-oraita*. The *psul de-oraita* of *rasha de-chamas* requires an *aveira de-oraita* done knowingly, willingly, and without rationalization. Similarly, a *de-rabbanan psul* requires an *aveira de-rabbanan* done in that same unracionalized manner.

## Eidim Zomemim

### Abaye: Retroactive

At the top of 27a, the *gemara* introduces a dispute between Abaye and Rava concerning *eidim zomemim* — witnesses who testified and were subsequently contradicted by a second pair of witnesses who established that the first pair could not have witnessed what they claimed, since they were elsewhere at the time. The Torah commands *ve-asitem lo ka'asher zamam* — you do to the false witnesses what they conspired to do to their victim. The question here is: from when does their *psul* as witnesses take effect?

Abaye says *le-mafrei'a hu nifsal* — they are disqualified retroactively, from the moment they gave false testimony. Their act of false witness in January made them *resha'im* from that moment, and since *al tashet rasha eid* — a *rasha* cannot be a witness — every testimony they gave thereafter is retroactively invalid.

### Rava: Prospective

Rava says *mikan u-le-haba hu nifsal* — they are disqualified only going forward, from the time the contradicting witnesses (*mazzimim*) come and establish their falsity. If they testified in January and the *mazzimim* appear in March, their *psul* begins in March.

### Chiddush Reasoning

Rava's first rationale: *eid zomem chiddush hu* — the entire institution of *eidim zomemim* is a *chiddush*, a legal innovation of the Torah. The problem is fundamentally one of epistemology: why do we credit the second set of witnesses over the first? It is two against two. *Mai chazit de-samchat a-hani — samach a-hani* — why do you rely on the second pair rather than the first? The Torah innovates that when witnesses say *imanu hayitem oto ha-yom* — you were with us on that day — we follow the second pair. But this is a purely legal determination; it does not establish as a matter of historical fact that the first witnesses were lying in January. What it establishes is that as of March, when the *chiddush* of *eid zomem* is activated, they receive the legal status of false witnesses. *Ve-ein lecha bo ela mi-sha'at chiddusho ve-eilach* — you may draw on this innovation only from the moment it is triggered, not retroactively. The Torah's declaration does not clarify that they were actually lying in January; it merely imposes legal consequences as of the moment the determination is made.

### Lakuchos Protection

The *gemara* cites an alternative formulation of Rava's position: perhaps Rava in principle agrees with Abaye that the *eidim zomemim* are retroactively *resha'im*, and his reason for limiting the *psul* to going forward is *mishum pseida de-lakuchos* — out of concern for the harm to innocent purchasers and other parties who relied on these witnesses' testimony between January and March. Documents executed and transactions completed based on their testimony would be retroactively invalidated if we apply the *psul* from January. To protect those who in good faith relied on the witnesses when they were still accepted by the court, we limit the *psul* to the point when their invalidity becomes publicly established. This rationale bears a family resemblance to the *hachraza be-veit din* principle: in both cases, the concern is to protect third parties who could not have known of the *psul*.

### Distinguishing Reasons

The difference between Rava's two rationales has practical consequences. One case where they diverge is when it is two witnesses against one — a *bei trei be-chad* scenario. If the *mazzimim* in March come and establish that only one of the January witnesses was elsewhere on that day, then the credibility determination is two against one, not two against two. There is no longer a *chiddush* involved — two witnesses against one are simply believed. Accordingly, if Rava's reason is the *chiddush* logic, then in this case the *psul* would be retroactive to January, like Abaye. But if Rava's reason is the protection of *lakuchos*, then even here the *psul* would only begin in March, since the same practical concerns apply.

A second divergence: what if in March the witnesses do not contradict the January testimony as *eidim zomemim* at all, but instead testify that one of the January witnesses was a *gazlan* — a thief known to have stolen back in January? This moves outside the *eidim zomemim* framework entirely. If Rava's reason is the *chiddush* of *eidim zomemim*, then since there is no *chiddush* at play here, the *psul* would begin in January when the *gezeila* occurred. But if the reason is the protection of *lakuchos*, then even in this case the *psul* would begin only in March. It is striking that this latter question is closely connected to the earlier ruling of Rava: when it came to a *de-oraita* case of a *gazlan*, Rava held that no *hachraza be-veit din* is required. Yet here, under one reading, Rava might hold that even a *gazlan's* *psul* does not take effect until it is established in court — a position that seems in tension with his earlier ruling, though the *gemara* leaves this nuance in place.

The *gemara* records that Rav Pappi acted in accordance with Rava's position. However, Mar bar Rav Ashi rules that the *halacha* follows Abaye, and this is one of the cases covered by the mnemonic *ya'al kagam* — the letter *ayin* standing for *eidim zomemim*: specifically the question of *le-mafrei'a* versus *mikan u-le-haba*. The *halacha* is thus *le-mafrei'a hu nifsal* — the disqualification is retroactive.

## Mumar Neveilot

### For Appetite

The *gemara* now turns to the case of *mumar ochel neveilos le-te'avon* — a habitual transgressor who eats *neveilah* (meat not slaughtered in accordance with halakha) out of appetite and desire, not as a theological statement. *Divrei ha-kol pasul* — all opinions agree he is invalid as a witness.

### Neveilah Economics

The reasoning is important and somewhat counterintuitive. One might think this disqualification is about pleasure — the person has a *yetzer ha-ra* for food and indulges it, so perhaps we fear he has similar weaknesses in other areas. But the *gemara* clarifies: *neveilah* is not a pleasure-based issue in the same sense. *Neveilah* is the same meat as kosher meat; one does not eat it because it tastes better. The only reason to eat *neveilah* rather than kosher meat is that it is cheaper. Choosing *neveilah* over kosher meat is therefore fundamentally a monetary calculation. This person is a *rasha de-chamas*: he is willing to transgress for financial benefit, and we fear he can be bought for false testimony.

### L'Hach'is Category

The *gemara* then turns to the case of *mumar ochel neveilos le-hach'is* — one who eats *neveilah* specifically in spite, as an act of theological defiance. This is a very different motivation. In general halakhic terms, *mumar le-hach'is* is regarded as far more severe than *mumar le-te'avon* — defiant transgression is worse than weakness of will. But when it comes to *eidus*, the calculus may flip: the person who eats *neveilah* out of spite is not doing so for money. He is not susceptible to bribery; he simply has no regard for the commandment. Does that compromise his reliability as a witness?

### Abaye/Rava Dispute

Abaye and Rava dispute this question.

## Mumar Neveilot…

### Abaye: Formal Rasha

Abaye says *pasul* — he is invalid. His reasoning: when a person commits a transgression for which he is *chayav malkus* (liable to lashes), he is formally designated a *rasha*. The verse *ve-hirshi'u et ha-rasha* — "and they shall declare the wicked one guilty" — establishes the category. A *rasha* is defined by legal obligation for lashes, and *al tashet rasha eid* prohibits such a person from serving as a witness. This is a formal, categorical disqualification — it does not depend on concerns about bribery or monetary integrity. Anyone liable to *malkus* is formally a *rasha*, and therefore *pasul* as a witness.

### Rava: Money Only

Rava says *kasher* — he is valid. Rava rejects Abaye's formal category. *Rasha de-chamas beinan* — only someone who compromises himself for monetary reasons is disqualified. A person who transgresses out of spite or for non-monetary reasons is not the kind of witness we fear will sell false testimony. Rava does not accept that the formal label of *rasha* arising from the *malkus* obligation is sufficient to disqualify from *eidus*.

---

## Two Psul Categories

### Two Psul Types

The discussion crystallizes two distinct grounds for witness disqualification. The first is *rasha de-chamas*: disqualification arising from monetary compromise, either because the underlying transgression is itself *de-oraita* (yielding *psul de-oraita*) or *de-rabbanan* (yielding *psul de-rabbanan*), provided the transgression is committed knowingly and willingly without rationalization. The second, according to Abaye, is formal *rasha* status arising from obligation in *malkus* — a categorical disqualification grounded in the legal definition of wickedness rather than in any specific concern about monetary corruptibility. As the *gemara* ultimately rules, the *halacha* follows Abaye (this is the *mem* of *ya'al kagam*: *mumar ochel neveilos le-hach'is*). These two categories together define the full scope of *psulei eidus* arising from personal conduct.

## Tannaim Resolution

### Rabbi Meir View

The *gemara* now attempts to ground this dispute between Abaye and Rava in a *tannaitic* debate, introducing the principle *nima ke-tanna'i*. Rabbi Meir holds that *eid zomem pasul le-chol ha-Torah kullah* — if witnesses are found to be *eidim zomemim*, they are disqualified for all testimony across the board, whether the original false testimony concerned capital cases or monetary cases.

### Rabbi Yossi View

Rabbi Yossi disagrees. In his view, the disqualification of *eidim zomemim* is not categorical. It is triggered only in cases similar in nature to the original offense. Specifically, *eidim zomemim* who were found lying in a capital case are disqualified broadly, including for monetary cases. But *eidim zomemim* found in a monetary case — *huzmmu be-dinei mamonos* — remain *kasher le-dinei nefashos*, valid to testify in capital cases. Rabbi Yossi reasons that just because someone was willing to falsely implicate someone for money, we cannot assume they would falsely testify to get someone killed.

### Alignment Theory

The *gemara* suggests aligning these *tannaitic* positions with the Amoraic dispute: *nima Abaye ke-Rabbi Meir ve-Rava ke-Rabbi Yossi*. Abaye, with his categorical formal *psul*, mirrors Rabbi Meir's approach: once you are disqualified in one domain, you are disqualified across the board. Rava, who requires a logical connection between the transgression and the feared false testimony, mirrors Rabbi Yossi's practical approach: disqualification tracks the specific concern it is meant to address.

Rabbi Meir's approach, as understood by Abaye, is that once someone proves themselves capable of false witness — even for monetary purposes — there is a categorical formal invalidity that extends to all testimony. It is not about predicting behavior; it is about formal legal status. Rabbi Yossi, by contrast, thinks entirely in practical terms: is there a reason to fear that because this person lied for X, they will also lie for Y? If going from a weighty case to a light case (*me-chumra le-kulla*) we are concerned — surely someone who would lie about a person's life would lie about money. But going from light to heavy (*me-kulla le-chumra*) we do not assume: just because someone lied about money does not mean they would risk testifying falsely in a capital matter.

### Fact vs. Form

The *gemara* presses further: can Rava truly claim Rabbi Yossi as his *tannaitic* support? The *gemara* observes that no one disputes that Rabbi Yossi's approach is incompatible with Abaye. The debate between Abaye and Rava, properly understood, is about *Rabbi Meir's* position: does Rabbi Meir take a purely formal-categorical approach (Abaye's reading), or does Rabbi Meir's ruling have a more limited logic that Rava can still accept?

Rava explains: *ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi Meir hasam ela le-gabbei eidim zomemim de-mamona de-mara le-shamaya u-mara le-briyos* — Rabbi Meir's extension from monetary false testimony to capital cases is still grounded in a logical similarity. When someone testifies falsely against another person in a monetary case, they are sinning against both God (*bein adam la-makom*) and their fellow (*bein adam le-chavero*). Given that two-dimensional wrong, Rabbi Meir concludes they are categorically invalid for all testimony. But the case of *mumar ochel neveilos le-hach'is* is different: *de-mara le-shamaya ve-eino mara le-briyos* — the person is sinning only against God, not against another human being. There is no interpersonal dimension to this transgression. Rabbi Meir, Rava contends, might well not extend categorical disqualification to such a purely *bein adam la-makom* offense. Abaye insists that Rabbi Meir's formal *rasha* category is truly categorical and sweeps in even *bein adam la-makom* transgressions bearing the liability of *malkus*.

The *gemara* establishes the *halacha*: *ve-hilchesa ke-votsei de-Abaye* — we rule like Abaye, and this is one of the *ya'al kagam* rulings.

## Bar Chama Case

### Case Setup

The *gemara* now brings the *sichiya* — the practical case — that prompted the claim of a *stam mishna* like Rabbi Meir. A man named Bar Chama had committed murder (*ktal nafsha*). The *Reish Galuta* — the Exilarch, head of the Jewish community in Babylonia — charged Rav Abba bar Yaakov: go, investigate the matter, and if it is established beyond doubt that he killed, blind his eyes. (*Rashi* notes that the Jewish leadership of the time exercised limited but real forms of physical authority over their community, even absent the power of capital execution.)

### Counter-Testimony

Two witnesses came and testified that Bar Chama had indeed committed the murder. Bar Chama then produced two witnesses of his own, who testified against one of the prosecution witnesses: one said, "In my presence, this man stole a *kav* of *chumsha*" (a quantity of millet), and the other said, "In my presence, he stole a cluster — *kata de-vortia*." Both defense witnesses were thus testifying that the prosecution witness was a *gazlan* and therefore *pasul*.

### Rav Abba Query

Rav Abba bar Yaakov turned to Bar Chama and asked: *mai da'atacha — ke-Rabbi Meir?* What is your thinking — are you relying on Rabbi Meir? The implicit argument was: Bar Chama's defense rests on the claim that a witness *pasul* in monetary matters (a *gazlan*) is also *pasul* for a capital case. That is precisely Rabbi Meir's position. But, Rav Abba continues, *Rabbi Meir ve-Rabbi Yossi halacha ke-Rabbi Yossi* — in a dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yossi, we rule like Rabbi Yossi. And Rabbi Yossi holds that someone found *pasul* for monetary cases is still *kasher* for capital cases. Therefore, even if the prosecution witness is a *gazlan*, he can still testify in a capital matter.

### Mishna Analysis

At this point, Rav Pappi intervened: *hani mili heicha de-la stam lan tanna ke-Rabbi Meir* — that rule, that we follow Rabbi Yossi, holds only where there is no *stam mishna* like Rabbi Meir. *Hacha stam lan tanna ke-Rabbi Meir* — here there is an anonymous *mishna* that reflects Rabbi Meir's position. The existence of a *stam mishna* aligned with Rabbi Meir overrides the general presumption in favor of Rabbi Yossi. The question becomes: which *mishna* constitutes this *stam*?

The first candidate is a *mishna* that states: *kol ha-ra'ui la-dun dinei nefashos ra'ui la-dun dinei mamonos* — whoever is qualified to adjudicate capital cases is qualified to adjudicate monetary cases. One might argue this implies that the two categories use the same standard of fitness, consistent with Rabbi Meir's categorical approach. But the *gemara* immediately tests this: if the *mishna* is Rabbi Yossi, is it contradicted? *Ha ika eid zomem de-mamona de-kasher le-dinei nefashos u-fasul le-dinei mamonos* — under Rabbi Yossi, someone who testified falsely in a monetary case is *kasher* for capital cases and *pasul* for monetary ones. That would contradict the *mishna's* apparent symmetry only if the *mishna* is using the same criteria for both, as Rabbi Meir would have it. So must the *mishna* be Rabbi Meir?

### Yuchas Clarification

The *gemara* resists this conclusion: *dilma be-psulei yuchasin kai* — perhaps the *mishna* is not discussing disqualification due to sin or false testimony at all, but rather disqualification due to *yuchasin* — lineage. Capital cases require judges of impeccable lineage (*yuchas*); monetary cases have a lower bar. The *mishna* may simply be saying that anyone with the *yuchas* for capital cases certainly has it for monetary cases, and some have sufficient *yuchas* for monetary but not for capital. That makes the *mishna* entirely coherent under Rabbi Yossi — no reference to the relative scope of disqualification based on false testimony is implied.

*De-ei lo teima hachi* — if you do not say the *mishna* is about *yuchas*, the *seifa* (latter clause) of that same *mishna* becomes difficult: *yesh ra'ui la-dun dinei mamonos ve-ein ra'ui la-dun dinei nefashos* — there are those fit for monetary cases but not for capital. If we are talking about the case of one found as a false witness in a capital matter, the *gemara* asks: *amai eino ra'ui?* — why would he then be disqualified from monetary cases? Under Rabbi Yossi, that scenario would still leave him *kasher* for monetary cases. The only way to make the *seifa* coherent under the *yuchas* framework is that the *mishna* throughout addresses lineage requirements, not testimony-based disqualification. *Ela pasul yuchasin kai — hacha nami pasul yuchasin kai* — the entire *mishna* is about *yuchas* from start to finish. It therefore provides no proof of a *stam mishna* like Rabbi Meir.

### Rosh Hashanah Source

The *gemara* then identifies the actual *stam mishna* that Rav Pappi had in mind. It is found in tractate *Rosh Hashanah* and reads: *eilu hen ha-psulin* — the following are disqualified as witnesses: *ha-misachek be-kubiya, u-malveh be-ribbit, u-mafrichei yonim, ve-sochrei shevi'it, va-avadim* — the same catalogue as our *mishna*, with the addition of slaves. The *mishna* then states the general principle: *ze ha-kelal — kol edut she-ein ha-isha keshera lah, af hen ein kesheran lah* — any testimony for which a woman is unfit, these individuals are also unfit.

The force of this general principle is the key: it applies their *psul* categorically, without distinction between the type of case. If a woman cannot testify in a particular matter, neither can any of these individuals. The *gemara* now asks whose view this *mishna* reflects. If it were Rabbi Yossi, there would be a contradiction: Rabbi Yossi holds that those found false in monetary matters are still *kasher* for capital cases — and in capital cases, a woman cannot testify. So under Rabbi Yossi, all the individuals in our *mishna*, having been disqualified on monetary grounds, should still be *kasher* for capital cases. Yet the *stam mishna* from *Rosh Hashanah* apparently disqualifies them from all categories of testimony for which a woman is disqualified — including capital cases. *Ela lav Rabbi Meir hi* — it must follow Rabbi Meir's categorical approach.

This is the *stam mishna* that Rav Pappi invoked. Since we have an anonymous *mishna* reflecting Rabbi Meir, the presumption in favor of Rabbi Yossi is overridden, and the *halacha* follows the categorical approach.

### Case Outcome

The result: Bar Chama was acquitted. The prosecution witness was established as a *gazlan*, and following the categorical approach of Rabbi Meir — as reflected in the *stam mishna* — a *gazlan* is *pasul* across the board, including for capital cases. Rav Pappi's analysis thus dismantled the testimony against Bar Chama, and the case for blinding him collapsed.

*Kam Bar Chama nashkei achreia de-Rav Pappa* — Bar Chama rose and kissed Rav Pappa on his feet in gratitude. *Ve-kibblei le-chargei de-chulei shenei* — he undertook to pay Rav Pappa's taxes for all of his years, so profound was his gratitude for the analysis that saved him.

The broader upshot is striking: even though the logical case for Rabbi Yossi — that someone who lies for money may not lie for a person's life — is compelling on its merits, and even though the *gemara* itself acknowledged that Abaye's further extension to *mumar ochel neveilos le-hach'is* is an even larger leap, the *halacha* follows the categorical approach. Once a person is formally *pasul* — whether through *rasha de-chamas* or through the formal *rasha* of *malkus* liability — the disqualification extends across all categories of testimony.

## Two Psul Categories…

### Psul Summary

In summation: witness disqualification arising from personal conduct operates along two tracks. The first is *psul de-oraita* arising from *rasha de-chamas* status — knowingly and willingly transgressing a *de-oraita* prohibition for monetary gain, without rationalization. The second is *psul de-rabbanan*, which arises when the underlying transgression is only rabbinic in nature, or when a *de-oraita* prohibition is violated but with significant rationalization. Crucially, *psul de-rabbanan* requires *hachraza be-veit din* before it takes effect, while *psul de-oraita* is effective from the moment of the transgression. In addition, following Abaye's ruling (as one of the *ya'al kagam* cases), a second formal category of *psul* exists: anyone liable to *malkus*, even for a purely *bein adam la-makom* transgression, is formally designated a *rasha* and disqualified as a witness on that basis alone, independent of any concern about monetary corruptibility. And once disqualified — by either route — the *psul* operates categorically across all testimony.

## Relatives Invalidity

### Relatives Overview

The *mishna* turns to a second major category of witness disqualification: relatives. *Ve-elu hein ha-krovim* — the following are the relatives who are *pasul* as witnesses. Before enumerating the specific cases, it is necessary to understand the nomenclature used in *halacha* to describe degrees of relation. The system is best visualized through a family tree. Individuals at the same generational level are referred to as *rishon be-rishon* — generation one to generation one. When the relationship crosses one generation, it is *rishon be-sheini* or *sheini be-rishon*. First cousins — whose fathers are brothers — are *sheini be-sheini*. The *halacha* disqualifies witnesses related up to and including the *sheini be-sheini* level.

### Rabbi Akiva Relatives

The *mishna* lists: *achiv* — his brother (*rishon be-rishon*); *ve-achi aviv* — his father's brother, i.e., his uncle (*sheini be-rishon*); *ve-achi imo* — his mother's brother (*sheini be-rishon*). The inclusion of the mother's brother is attributed to Rabbi Akiva's teaching, which extends the disqualification to maternal relatives as well as paternal ones.

### In-Laws Rule

The *mishna* continues with relatives by marriage, applying the principle of *ishto ke-gufo* — a man's wife is considered an extension of himself. Accordingly: *u-va'al achotov* — his sister's husband (brother-in-law), equivalent to a *rishon be-rishon*; *u-va'al achot aviv* — his father's sister's husband (uncle by marriage), treated as *sheini be-rishon* on account of *ba'al ke-ishto*; *u-va'al achot imo* — his mother's sister's husband, parallel on the maternal side; *u-va'al imo* — his mother's husband (stepfather), who through *ishto ke-gufo* functions as a father figure and is therefore disqualified; *va-chamiv* — his father-in-law (since he and his wife are one, the father-in-law is like his father); *ve-giso* — his wife's sister's husband. This last case involves a double application of *ishto ke-gufo*: he and his wife are one, and his wife's sister and her husband are one, making the wife's sister's husband effectively a brother-in-law's equivalent.

### Children & Stepsons

The *mishna* adds: *hein u-veneihem ve-chataneihem* — all of those enumerated, along with their children and sons-in-law. The inclusion of children and sons-in-law extends the coverage to the *sheini be-sheini* level: where the uncle (*rishon be-sheini*) is disqualified, the cousin (*sheini be-sheini*) is disqualified as well; where the brother-in-law is disqualified, his son-in-law — by application of *ba'al ke-ishto* — is disqualified too.

*Ve-churgo levado* — and one's stepson, but him alone. Unlike all the other relationships enumerated, the stepson's disqualification does not extend further — not to his children, not to his son-in-law. The reason is that all the other cases ultimately rest on a conceptual blood relationship once *ishto ke-gufo* is applied. The uncle by marriage, for instance, becomes through that principle effectively equivalent to a blood uncle. But the stepson lacks any such underlying blood connection, and therefore the disqualification stops with him alone.

### Earlier Mishna

Rabbi Yossi remarks: *zu Mishnat Rabbi Akiva — aval Mishnah Rishonah* — this entire catalogue is according to Rabbi Akiva. The earlier teaching was more limited: *dodo u-ven dodo* — one's uncle and cousin, and *ve-chol ha-ra'ui le-yorsho* — anyone who is eligible to inherit from him. This formulation is simultaneously broader in one sense and narrower in another. It appears broader — "anyone who may inherit" — but is in practice narrower because inheritance flows through the paternal line. Accordingly, one's maternal uncle would not qualify under the *Mishnah Rishonah*'s criterion, since one's property would not pass through one's mother to one's mother's brother. The underlying logic of the earlier teaching is not purely relational — it is about financial interest: *nogea ba-davar*, a stake in the outcome. If one can inherit, one has an interest in the verdict and therefore cannot be trusted.

This distinction also creates an asymmetry: a nephew cannot testify for his maternal uncle because he may stand to inherit from him, but the maternal uncle *can* testify for the nephew — because the maternal uncle would never inherit from the nephew. The directionality of the *psul* tracks the directionality of the potential financial interest.

### Former Relatives

*Ve-chol ha-karov lo be-otah sha'ah* — the disqualification applies to anyone who was a close relative at the time the relevant testimony was given or observed. *Hayah karov ve-nisrachek* — but if someone was a relative and has since become a non-relative — for example, a father-in-law whose daughter died or divorced — *harei zeh kasher*, he is once again valid, provided the key moments of witnessing and testifying were after the separation.

Rabbi Yehuda adds: *afilu mesah bito ve-yesh lo banim heimena — harei zeh karov* — even after the wife's death, if there are children from the marriage, the father-in-law and former son-in-law remain connected through the shared grandchildren. The relationship is not fully severed; they continue to have a nexus, and the *psul* persists.

### Love & Hate

The *mishna* then introduces two emotionally based categories: *ha-ohev ve-ha-soneh* — the beloved and the hated. *Ohev zeh shushbino* — the *ohev* is one's closest companion, traditionally the person who served as groomsman at the wedding. *Rashi* explains that during the week of the wedding celebrations, the bond of closeness and loyalty between them is so intense that objectivity cannot be assumed. *Soneh* — the *soneh* — is defined as one who has not spoken with the person for three consecutive days out of animosity. When a bitter quarrel has reached that threshold, the anger may distort the person's testimony.

### Not Suspected

The *mishna* concludes — and here the attribution is somewhat uncertain, as it follows what appears to be a statement of Rabbi Yossi — with the declaration: *lo nechshadu Yisrael al kach* — Israel is not suspected of distorting testimony on account of mere emotional closeness or animosity alone. This is a remarkable statement. *Chazal* recognized that a friend testifying on behalf of a friend, or an enemy testifying against an enemy, operates on a fundamentally different plane than a person who has been shown to compromise their integrity for money. The concern about the *rasha de-chamas* is concrete and empirical: we have evidence of a character trait that predicts corruptibility. But emotional bias — without evidence of actual compromise — does not rise to the level of formal suspicion. Jews, the *mishna* declares, are not assumed to allow friendship or hatred to cause them to testify falsely. These categories of *ohev ve-soneh* describe limited, contextual concerns — not categorical invalidity on the model of monetary transgression.
