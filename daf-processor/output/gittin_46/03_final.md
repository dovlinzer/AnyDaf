# Gittin 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sefer Torah & Tikkun Olam

### Pidyon Shvuyim Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **for the betterment of the world,** so as not to cause an increase in the theft of sacred Jewish ritual objects in order to sell them for large sums of money.

The present daf picks up at the Mishnah toward the bottom of 45a, following an extended discussion of the *tikkun olam* of *pidyon shvuyim* — a wide-ranging and conceptually rich treatment of what *tikkun olam* means in practice: balancing personal needs against community needs, and weighing present circumstances against future consequences. The Mishnah now introduces another application of *tikkun olam*, this one concerning the redemption of sacred objects from non-Jews.

### Seforim Pricing Rule

The Mishnah states: *ein lokhin sfarim tefillin u'mezuzot min ovdei kokhavim yoter k'dei dmeihem* — we do not purchase *sifrei torah*, *tefillin*, or *mezuzot* from non-Jews for more than their market value. The reasoning is precisely parallel to the rule against over-paying for captives: doing so would only encourage more theft. This is a real and serious concern; synagogues require substantial security measures to protect *sifrei torah*, given how valuable they are. One might feel instinctively that a *sefer torah* is not merely a matter of dollars and cents — it is a holy object, surely worth whatever price is demanded. Yet the Mishnah rules otherwise. One does not redeem it for more than its going rate, and this, the Mishnah declares, is *mipnei tikkun olam* — for the sake of *tikkun olam*.

### Sefer Torah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב בּוּדְיָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: יָתֵר עַל כְּדֵי דְמֵיהֶן הוּא דְּאֵין לוֹקְחִין, הָא בִּכְדֵי דְמֵיהֶן – לוֹקְחִין; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה שֶׁנִּמְצָא בְּיַד גּוֹי – קוֹרִין בּוֹ? דִּילְמָא לִגְנוֹז.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Budya said to Rav Ashi** that one could infer the following from the mishna: **It is** for **more than their** actual monetary **value** that **one may not purchase them; however, for their** precise **value, one may purchase them.** Can one **learn from** the mishna that with regard to **a Torah scroll that is found in the possession of a gentile, one can read** from **it** after obtaining it from the gentile, and there is no concern that perhaps the gentile wrote it and it is unfit? Rav Ashi answered: **Perhaps** this does not mean that the Torah scroll is purchased in order to read from it, but rather in order **to inter** it and not to use it; however, it is nevertheless purchased so that it will not be desecrated by the gentiles.

The Gemara now focuses on a different legal dimension of the same Mishnah. Rav Budya poses a question to Rav Ashi: the Mishnah specifies that one does not pay *more* than market value — implying that at market value one *does* purchase it. From this, may we infer that a *sefer torah* found in the possession of a non-Jew is presumed to be kosher? After all, who writes *sifrei torah* if not Jews? Does the Mishnah's implicit permission to buy it back indicate that it is fit for use?

### Lignoz Principle

The Gemara deflects the inference: *dilma lignoz* — perhaps one buys it back only in order to bury it properly. The Mishnah's permission to purchase at market value does not necessarily imply that the *sefer torah* is fit for use; one might purchase it *lignoz*, to give it proper disposal out of respect, while still suspecting that it may be *pasul*. This is not merely theoretical — it is not uncommon to encounter old *sifrei kodesh* in second-hand settings where no one will care for them properly, and purchasing them solely in order to place them in *sheimos* is a recognizable practice, whether or not there is a formal *chiyuv* to do so.

### Min & Yisaref

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, נָקְטִינַן: סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ מִין – יִשָּׂרֵף, כְּתָבוֹ גּוֹי – יִגָּנֵז. נִמְצָא בְּיַד מִין – יִגָּנֵז. נִמְצָא בְּיַד גּוֹי – אָמְרִי לַהּ יִגָּנֵז, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ קוֹרִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: We have a tradition** that **a Torah scroll that was written by a heretic should be burned;** a Torah scroll **written by a gentile should be interred;** a Torah scroll **found in the possession of a heretic,** and it is not clear who wrote it, **should be interred.** With regard to a Torah scroll **found in the possession of a gentile, some say** it **should be interred and some say** that **one may read** from **it.**

The Gemara proceeds to a related ruling. Rav Nachman declares: *naktinan* — our accepted position is that *sefer torah she-ktavo min yisaref*, a *sefer torah* written by a *min* must be burned. The *min* in this context is a Jewish sectarian — most relevantly, a Jewish Christian — someone who has broken fundamentally from the faith, and whose understanding of God is categorically different from that of normative Judaism. When such a person writes a *sefer torah*, the divine names are written with a conception of God that is, from the rabbinic perspective, idolatrous or at least profoundly distorted. Beyond the technical question of intent, such a text poses a particular danger precisely because it comes from within — it is the most insidious threat. Hence the severe ruling: not merely *yiganeiz*, burial, but *yisaref*, destruction by fire. The text does not merely lack *kedushat sefer torah*; it is treated as something actively dangerous, analogous to idolatrous literature, and must be destroyed.

### Three Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ גּוֹי – תָּנֵי חֲדָא: יִשָּׂרֵף, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: יִגָּנֵז, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: קוֹרִין בּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **a Torah scroll that was written by a gentile, it is taught** in **one** *baraita*: It **should be burned, and it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: It **should be interred, and it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **One may read** from **it.** There is a three-fold contradiction concerning the *halakha* of a Torah scroll written by a gentile.

What of a *sefer torah* written by an ordinary non-Jew (*oved kochavim*)? The Gemara records three positions, attributed to different *tannaim* in different *baraitot*: one says it must be burned (*yisaref*); one says it must be buried (*yiganeiz*); and one says it may actually be read (*korin bo*). The most radical of these — that a *sefer torah* written by a non-Jew is fit for use — raises a striking halakhic question. Does the writer need to be a believing monotheist? Is any *kavanah* required at all? The Gemara notes that *sifrei torah* written in Greek have, in some opinions, been considered valid; perhaps when a non-Jew simply copies letter by letter, functioning as a scribe without any particular religious intent in either direction, the text retains its validity.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּתַנְיָא ״יִשָּׂרֵף״ – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: סְתָם מַחְשֶׁבֶת גּוֹי לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: This is **not difficult: That which is taught** in a *baraita*, that it **should be burned, is** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who says: The unspecified intentions of a gentile are for idol worship,** and therefore everything he wrote is assumed to be written for the sake of idolatrous worship and must be burned.

The Gemara resolves the three-way *machloket* not as a genuine dispute but as representing distinct positions: *lo kashya* — there is no real contradiction. The opinion that the *sefer torah* must be burned reflects the view of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that *stam machshevet oved kochavim l'avodat kochavim* — one presumes by default that the thoughts of a non-Jew are directed toward idolatrous worship. If the *sefer torah* was written with idolatrous intent, it is not merely *pasul* but is transformed into an idolatrous work requiring destruction — a striking and severe position.

### Disqualified Writers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּתַנְיָא ״יִגָּנֵז״ – הַאי תַּנָּא הוּא, דְּתַנְיָא: סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, תְּפִלִּין וּמְזוּזוֹת שֶׁכְּתָבָן מִין, וּמָסוֹר, גּוֹי, וְעֶבֶד, אִשָּׁה, וְקָטָן, וְכוּתִי, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד – פְּסוּלִין; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּקְשַׁרְתָּם״–״וּכְתַבְתָּם״ – כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בִּקְשִׁירָה יֶשְׁנוֹ בִּכְתִיבָה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בִּקְשִׁירָה אֵינוֹ בִּכְתִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And that which is taught** in a *baraita*, which said that **it should be interred, is** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as Rav Hamnuna, son of Rava of Pashronya, taught: A Torah scroll, phylacteries, or *mezuzot* that were written by a heretic or an informer, a gentile or a slave, a woman or a minor, or a Samaritan or a Jewish apostate, are unfit, as it is stated: "And you shall bind them** as a sign on your hand…**and you shall write them** on the doorposts of your house" (Deuteronomy 6:8–9). From this juxtaposition, one can derive the following: **Anyone who is** included **in** the mitzva of **binding** the phylacteries, i.e., one who is both obligated and performs the mitzva, **is** included **in** the class of people who may **write** Torah scrolls, phylacteries, and *mezuzot*; **but anyone who is not** included **in** the mitzva of **binding is not** included **in** the class of people who may **write** sacred texts. This *baraita* equates the *halakha* of a Torah scroll written by a gentile to the *halakha* of Torah scrolls written by these other types of people, which are interred.

The intermediate position — that a *sefer torah* written by a non-Jew requires burial — reflects the view of a *tanna* transmitted in the name of Rav Hamnuna the son of Rav Ami of Pashrunya. The *braita* states that *sifrei torah*, *tefillin*, and *mezuzot* written by a *min*, an informer (*masur*), a non-Jew, a slave, a woman, a minor, a Samaritan (*kuti*), or a Jewish apostate (*yisrael mumar*) are all *pasul*. The disqualification is derived from a *drasha* on the verse *ukeshartem uchetavtem* — "you shall bind and you shall write": whoever is subject to the obligation of *tefillin* is qualified to write sacred texts; whoever is not subject to that obligation is not qualified.

This excludes women, minors, and slaves, who are not obligated in *tefillin*. The Samaritan is excluded presumably because, in rejecting the rabbinic understanding of *tefillin*, he places himself outside that *mitzvah*. The apostate Jew, though technically obligated, has by his rejection of his obligations effectively removed himself from the category. Most striking is the inclusion of the *masur*, the informer — a person who may be fully observant and believing but who, by betraying his community, is classified as outside it, and that classification is sufficient to disqualify him from writing sacred texts.

This Gemara sparks a broader question taken up by Tosafot: is the principle that one must be obligated in a *mitzvah* in order to create its object a general rule, or specific to *sefer torah*, *tefillin*, and *mezuzot* by virtue of this *drasha*? Tosafot cites the case of *tzitzit*, where the Talmud excludes only non-Jews (from the verse *bnei Yisrael v'asu*) while implying that women are qualified — and similarly the rule that a *sukkah* built by a woman or various other categories of people is nonetheless valid. The conclusion is that *kol sheyeshno bikshirah yeshno bichitivah* operates as a specific *drasha* limited to these three sacred texts, and is not a default rule for *mitzvah* objects generally.

### Rabban Gamliel Permit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּתַנְיָא ״קוֹרִין בּוֹ״ – הַאי תַּנָּא הוּא, דְּתַנְיָא: לוֹקְחִין סְפָרִים מִן הַגּוֹיִם בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיִּהְיוּ כְּתוּבִין כְּהִלְכָתָן. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּגוֹי אֶחָד בְּצַיְדָּן שֶׁהָיָה כּוֹתֵב סְפָרִים, וְהִתִּיר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לִיקַּח מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** concerning **that which is taught** in a *baraita*, i.e., that **one may read** from **it, that** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **this** following ***tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Avoda Zara* 3:6): **One may purchase** Torah **scrolls from gentiles in any location, provided that they are written in accordance with their *halakhot*. And** there was **an incident involving a gentile in Tzaidan who would write** Torah **scrolls, and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel permitted** the Jews **to purchase** the Torah scrolls **from him.**

The third and most permissive position — that a *sefer torah* written by a non-Jew is *korin bo*, fit for use — is supported by a *braita*: *lokchim sefarim me'ovdei kochavim bechol makom u'vilvad she'yihyu kesuvim kehilchasam* — one may purchase *sifrei torah* from non-Jews anywhere, provided they are written according to *halacha*. The *braita* goes further and records a *maaseh*: a certain non-Jew in Tzidan was actually employed as a *sofer*, writing *sifrei torah*, and Rabban Gamliel permitted purchasing from him — meaning, using them. So the radical position is not merely theoretical but has practical precedent: a non-Jew who writes a *sefer torah* correctly, without any demonstrable idolatrous *kavanah*, produces a valid text.

### Apostate Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל – עִיבּוּד לִשְׁמָן בָּעֵי, כְּתִיבָה לִשְׁמָן לָא בָּעֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel require** that the **preparation** of the parchment of Torah scrolls, phylacteries, and *mezuzot* be **for their sake,** i.e., for the sake of their use in a mitzva, but **he does not require** that the **writing** be **for their sake?**

The Gemara, however, raises an internal contradiction: Rabban Gamliel himself requires that the *klaf* — the parchment — be prepared *lishma*, with the intent to be used for a sacred text. As a *braita* records concerning *tefillin*: if one covered them with gold or attached a leather patch from a non-kosher animal they are *pasul*; a patch from a kosher animal is valid, *ve'af al pi shelo ibdan lishma* — even if the leather was not processed *lishma* — but Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says that even leather from a kosher animal is *pasul* unless it was processed *lishma*. If Rabban Gamliel demands *lishma* even for the raw materials, how could he not require *lishma* for the writing itself?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: צִיפָּן זָהָב אוֹ שֶׁטָּלָה עֲלֵיהֶן עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה – פְּסוּלוֹת. עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה – כְּשֵׁרוֹת, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא עִיבְּדָן לִשְׁמָן. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה – פְּסוּלוֹת, עַד שֶׁיְּעַבְּדֵן לִשְׁמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara quotes the source that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel requires that the parchment be prepared for their sake: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one took phylacteries and **coated them with gold or patched them** with the **skin of a non-kosher animal,** then **they are unfit.** However, if one patched them with the **skin of a kosher animal,** then **they are fit, and** this is so **even though he did not prepare them,** i.e., the skin, **for their sake,** i.e., for the sake of their use in a mitzva. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Even** if he patched them with the **skin of a kosher animal they are unfit, until he prepares them for their sake.** Being that he holds that the parchment needs to be prepared for their sake, their actual writing should certainly be done for their sake.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּגֵר שֶׁחָזַר לְסוֹרוֹ. לְסוֹרוֹ?! כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּהָוֵי לֵיהּ מִין! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: שֶׁחוֹזֵר לְסוֹרוֹ מִשּׁוּם יִרְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Shmuel says:** That incident in Tzaidan involved **a convert who returned to his** previous **corrupt ways [*suro*]** by reverting to living like a gentile. He nevertheless remains a Jew. The Gemara asks: If he returned **to his** previous **corrupt ways all the more so** should the Torah scroll be rendered unfit, **as he is a heretic.** Once he learns the Jewish faith and abandons it, he is considered a heretic. **Rav Ashi says:** This means **that he returned to his** previous **corrupt ways due to fear,** and not because he rejected the Jewish faith. He ceased acting like a Jew out of fear of reprisal. Since he is a Jew, it is permitted for him to read from the Torah scrolls that he writes.

The Gemara resolves this by reinterpreting the *maaseh*: *amar Rabbah bar Shmuel be-ger she-chazar le-surro* — the non-Jew in Tzidan was actually a convert who had reverted to his former ways. He had once been Jewish, knew how to write a *sefer torah*, and was now outwardly practicing as a non-Jew. Rav Ashi pushes this further: *she-chazar le-surro mishum yirah* — he reverted out of fear, like a *converso*, while inwardly remaining observant. But this interpretation essentially evacuates the *pshat* of the story entirely: the man in Tzidan, on this reading, is fundamentally an observant Jew practicing under duress, and the *chiddush* is minimal.

### Tosafot Alternative

Tosafot offers a different approach. Rather than accepting that the writing need not be *lishma*, Tosafot argues that in fact writing *does* require *lishma* — but that the *lishma* of the writing is supplied automatically when the *klaf* has been prepared *lishma*. Once the parchment has been designated and prepared for the purpose of a *sefer torah*, the context is established, and the writing upon it is understood as occurring within that sacred framework. The writing, in other words, draws its *lishma* from the *klaf*. Tosafot's further point is that *stam* writing — writing whose purpose is not otherwise specified — should by default be understood as writing *lishma*, since why else would one be writing a *sefer torah*? This contrasts with *stam ibud*, tanning leather without specification, which could be for any of countless purposes. The default for tanning is therefore not *leshem sefer torah*, whereas the default for writing words of *Torah* in the form of a *sefer torah* should naturally be understood as *lishma*. Tosafot acknowledges the difficulty without fully resolving it.

The upshot of this entire sugya is that there does appear to be a genuine *tannaic* position that a non-Jew may write a *sefer torah* without any requirement of *lishma* on his part. Because of the apparent contradiction with Rabban Gamliel's position on *ibud lishma*, the Gemara reinterprets the precedent. But the underlying possibility retains its interest. Notably, the Gemara elsewhere entertains the analogous *hava amina* that a non-Jew might perform *milah* — two acts one would naturally assume require the deepest Jewish connection and inner commitment turn out to be, at least as a matter of Talmudic discussion, potentially performable by a non-Jew.

## Divorce & Remarriage

### Mishnah Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שֵׁם רַע – לֹא יַחְזִיר. מִשּׁוּם נֶדֶר – לֹא יַחְזִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל נֶדֶר שֶׁיָּדְעוּ בּוֹ רַבִּים – לֹא יַחְזִיר, וְשֶׁלֹּא יָדְעוּ בּוֹ רַבִּים – יַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A man **who divorces his wife due to** her **bad reputation,** i.e., he heard that she had committed adultery, **may not remarry** her, even if it becomes clear that she did not in fact commit adultery. Similarly, if one divorces his wife **due to a vow** that she took, and he could not live with her under the conditions of her vow, **he may not remarry** her. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he divorces her due to **any vow that the public was aware of, he may not remarry** her, **but** if he divorces her due to a vow **that the public was not aware of, he may remarry** her.

The Mishnah states two prohibitions: *hamotzi et ishto mishum shem ra lo yachazir* — a man who divorces his wife on account of *shem ra*, an ill reputation regarding her conduct, may not subsequently remarry her; *mishum neder lo yachazir* — and similarly, if he divorces her on account of vows she has taken. The Mishnah thus creates a category of divorce that carries a permanent consequence for the husband: he forfeits the ability to remarry this woman.

### Two Rationales

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל נֶדֶר שֶׁצָּרִיךְ חֲקִירַת חָכָם – לֹא יַחְזִיר, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ חֲקִירַת חָכָם – יַחְזִיר. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא אָסְרוּ זֶה אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Rabbi Meir says:** If he divorces her due to **any vow that requires investigation** and dissolution **by a halakhic authority, he may not remarry** her, **but** if he divorces her due to a vow **that does not require investigation** and dissolution **by a halakhic authority,** and is dissolved even without that, **he may remarry** her. **Rabbi Elazar said: They prohibited** him from remarrying her in **this** case, where she stated a vow that requires dissolution by a halakhic authority, **only due to that** case, where she stated a vow that does not require dissolution by a halakhic authority.

Two explanations are given for this prohibition, and they correspond to two fundamentally different understandings of the *takkanah*.

The first and more widely cited rationale concerns protecting the wife's interests. The concern is that the husband, after divorcing his wife on the stated grounds, might subsequently want to reclaim her — particularly if the basis for the divorce is later undermined. If the *shem ra* turns out to have been false, or if the *neder* is annulled by a sage, the husband might assert: *ilu hayisi yodeia sheken hu afilu hayu nosnim lo meiah maneh lo hayisi megarshah* — had I known this, not for any amount of money would I have divorced her. By claiming that the *get* was issued under a fundamental misapprehension, he raises questions about the validity of the original divorce — and if the divorce was invalid, any subsequent marriage she entered into is adulterous, and children born from it are *mamzerim*.

According to Rashi, the *takkanah* addresses this by ensuring that the husband's retroactive claim will not be believed: since he was told explicitly at the time that divorcing her under these circumstances would permanently foreclose the option of remarrying her, and he chose to proceed anyway, he clearly had full *gemirut da'at* — complete and unconditional intention to divorce. He cannot now claim otherwise.

According to Tosafot, the mechanism is different: the husband has no incentive to raise the claim in the first place, since making it will not restore the marriage. If he cannot remarry her regardless of what he claims, there is no reason to spread rumors or fabricate the claim that the divorce was invalid.

The second rationale is of a different character entirely: the prohibition serves to control women's behavior. The fear is that women will become *perutzot ba'arayot u'bi'nedarim* — too loose in matters of sexual propriety and in taking vows. Knowing that any *shem ra* arising about her conduct could lead to an irreversible divorce, a woman will be more careful. Similarly, excessive vow-taking will carry a permanent personal cost. It should be noted, as Tosafot observes, that the *shem ra* rationale sits somewhat awkwardly with the behavioral-control explanation: if someone is simply the victim of rumors, there is little she could have done differently.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda draws a distinction within the *neder* case: *kol neder she'yadu vo rabbim lo yachazir v'shelo yadu vo rabbim yachazir* — if the vow was publicly known, the man may not remarry; if it was made privately, he may. This position is consistent with the behavioral-control rationale: the prohibition is aimed at discouraging reckless vow-taking, and it is only the public vow — which by its nature cannot be retracted, and which demonstrates a particular lack of care — that triggers the sanction. A private vow, which could be quietly annulled, reflects a lesser degree of irresponsibility and does not attract the prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלֹא הִכּוּם בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל כִּי נִשְׁבְּעוּ לָהֶם נְשִׂיאֵי הָעֵדָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: What is the reason of Rabbi Yehuda?** Based on what does he make the distinction of whether the public is aware of the vow or not? **As it is written** in the Torah portion describing the interaction of the Jews with the people of Gibeon: **"And the children of Israel did not strike them, for the leaders of the congregation swore to them"** (Joshua 9:18). This teaches that a vow taken in the presence of the public cannot be dissolved. In this case as well, if she took an oath that is known by the public, it cannot be dissolved.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi grounds Rabbi Yehuda's position in the episode of the Gibeonites: the Israelite leaders could not break their oath to spare the Gibeonites because it had been made publicly, as the verse states, *ki nishbeu lahem nesi'ei ha-edah*. The Rabbis, however, respond that the oath to the Gibeonites was not binding at all — since it was made under false pretenses (*be-ta'us*), it never took effect halachically. The reason the oath was nonetheless honored was *mishum kiddush Hashem*: repudiating a public oath, even a technically invalid one, would have constituted a *chillul Hashem* in the eyes of the nations. How many people constitute *rabbim* for the purposes of Rabbi Yehuda's ruling? Rav Nachman says three, deriving the number from the word *rabbim* in the context of a *zavah* (*yamim rabbim*: *yamim* = two, *rabbim* = one more, totaling three). Another opinion holds ten, derived from the word *edah*, which elsewhere connotes a quorum of ten representing the community.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן: הָתָם, מִי חָלָה שְׁבוּעָה עִילָּוַיְיהוּ כְּלָל?! כֵּיוָן דַּאֲמַרוּ לְהוּ: ״מֵאֶרֶץ רְחוֹקָה בָּאנוּ״ – וְלֹא בָּאוּ, לָא חָיְילָה שְׁבוּעָה עִילָּוַיְיהוּ כְּלָל; וְהַאי דְּלָא קַטְלִינְהוּ – מִשּׁוּם קְדוּשַּׁת הַשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis,** who hold that such a vow can also be dissolved, respond to this claim in the following manner: **There, did the oath take effect with regard to them at all? Since** the people of Gibeon **said to them: "We came from a far country"** (Joshua 9:6), **and they did not come** from a far country, **the oath did not take effect with regard to** the Jewish people **at all,** as it was taken under false pretenses. **And this, that** the Jewish people **did not kill** the Gibeonites, like they did to the other nations who resided in the land of Canaan, despite the fact that the vow did not take effect, was **due to the sanctity of God's name.** If they would have killed them, they would have desecrated God's name by not fulfilling their word, although this vow did not take effect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה רַבִּים? רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁלֹשָׁה, רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how many** people constitute the **public** with regard to this *halakha*? **Rav Naḥman says: Three** people. **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: Ten** people. The Gemara explains their opinions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁלֹשָׁה, ״יָמִים״ – שְׁנַיִם, ״רַבִּים״ – שְׁלֹשָׁה. רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר עֲשָׂרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״עֵדָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: Three,** and he derives this from a verse written with regard to a *zava*. The verse states: "And if a woman has an issue of her blood for many days [*yamim rabbim*]" (Leviticus 15:25). Rav Naḥman holds that **"days"** are a minimum of **two,** so the addition of the word **"many"** indicates that it means **three** days. Here too, where the mishna uses the term many [*rabbim*], it is a minimum of three people. **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** The *halakha* that a vow taken in the presence of the public cannot be dissolved is referring to a vow taken in the presence of at least **ten** people, **as it is written** in the Torah portion discussing the people of Gibeon, which is the source for this *halakha*: "For the leaders of the **congregation** swore to them," and a congregation consists of at least ten people, as is derived from the episode concerning the spies.

### Rabbi Meir

Rabbi Meir offers a different criterion: *kol neder she'tzarich hakiras chacham lo yachazir v'she'eino tzarich hakiras chacham yachazir* — if the vow required a sage to permit it, the husband may not remarry; if it did not require a sage — meaning the husband himself had the power to annul it — he may. Rabbi Meir's concern is specifically about the husband's retroactive claim. If the vow required a sage to annul, he might plausibly say: had I known you could have gone to a *beit din* and had it annulled, I never would have divorced you. But if he himself could have annulled the vow, that claim has no credibility: of course he knew. His decision to divorce her despite having the power to annul the vow himself indicates that his objection was not to this particular vow but to her pattern of vow-taking — and that is a fully deliberate divorce with complete *gemirut da'at*.

### Rabbi Elazar

Rabbi Elazar reverses Rabbi Meir's logic. The primary concern, Rabbi Elazar holds, is actually the case where the husband *could* have annulled the vow himself — because in that case his claim (*had I known I could have done it, I wouldn't have divorced her*) has maximum plausibility. In the case where a sage is required, the claim is weaker: a husband would not naturally expect his wife to go seeking out a *beit din* to annul her vow, and so he cannot convincingly say that he would have relied on that possibility. Having established the prohibition for the more credible case, Rabbi Elazar extends it to include the less credible case as well — not because the claim there is truly feared, but to maintain the coherence of the rule.

The Gemara articulates the underlying dispute: Rabbi Meir holds that *adam rotzeh she'tisbazeh ishto b'veit din* — a man does not mind his wife being subject to the process of appearing before a court for annulment. Rabbi Elazar holds the opposite: a man does *not* want his wife making the rounds of rabbinic courts, and therefore the claim that *had I known she could have gone to a sage* is simply not believable.

### Gemara Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: וְהוּא שֶׁאָמַר לָהּ: ״מִשּׁוּם שֵׁם רַע אֲנִי מוֹצִיאֵךְ״;
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yosef bar Minyumi says** that **Rav Naḥman says: And** the *halakha* stated in the mishna, which says that in certain cases a man who divorces his wife may not remarry her, **is** only applicable **when he said to her** explicitly: **I am removing you** from the house **due to your bad reputation,**

The Gemara frames a debate about the fundamental rationale of the Mishnah's prohibition. Is the prohibition rooted in *kilkul* — the concern that the husband will damage his ex-wife's status by claiming the divorce was invalid — or is it rooted in the desire to control women's behavior?

### Explicit Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* ״מִשּׁוּם נֶדֶר אֲנִי מוֹצִיאֵךְ״ – קָסָבַר: טַעְמָא מַאי – מִשּׁוּם קִלְקוּלָא; אִי אָמַר לַהּ הָכִי – מָצֵי מְקַלְקֵל לַהּ, וְאִי לָא – לָא מָצֵי מְקַלְקֵל לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** or: **I am removing you** from the house **due to a vow.** The Gemara explains that Rav Naḥman **holds: What is the reason** that he is prohibited from remarrying her? This is **due to** potential **harm** caused to her. If he would wish to remarry her after she had married someone else, he may cast aspersions on the validity of the bill of divorce by claiming that he gave it under a false impression. This may lead to her child from her second marriage to be considered a *mamzer*. Therefore, **if he said to her** at the time of the divorce that **this** is the reason he is divorcing her, then **he will be able to cause** this **harm to her, but if** he did **not** state this explicitly at the time of the divorce, **he will not be able to cause** this **harm to her,** as his claim will be disregarded.

Rav Nachman rules: *v'hu she'amar lah mishum shem ra ani motzi'ach* — the prohibition applies specifically when the husband explicitly states, at the time of the divorce, that this is the reason he is divorcing her. If he never articulates the reason, he cannot later make a credible claim that undermines the *get*: without an explicit statement at the time, any subsequent assertion that he would not have divorced her under different circumstances amounts to *devarim she'belev einam devarim* — an unexpressed intention with no legal standing.

### Two Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: צָרִיךְ שֶׁיֹּאמַר לָהּ: ״הֱוִי יוֹדַעַת שֶׁמִּשּׁוּם שֵׁם רַע אֲנִי מוֹצִיאֵךְ וּמִשּׁוּם נֶדֶר אֲנִי מוֹצִיאֵךְ״ – קָסָבַר: טַעְמָא מַאי – כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל פְּרוּצוֹת בַּעֲרָיוֹת וּבִנְדָרִים; הִלְכָּךְ צָרִיךְ לְמֵימַר לַהּ הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** a different version of Rav Yosef bar Minyumi's statement. **Rav Yosef bar Minyumi said** that **Rav Naḥman said: He must say to her: Be aware that I am removing you** from the house **due to** your **bad reputation, or:** Be aware that **I am removing you** from the house **due to a vow.** The Gemara explains that Rav Naḥman **holds: What is the reason** that he is prohibited from remarrying her? **So that Jewish women will not be promiscuous with regard to forbidden** sexual **relations or** lax with regard to **vows.** The Sages penalized these women who acted improperly by instituting that if they were divorced due to their actions, they cannot return to their husbands. **Therefore he must say this to her:** Be aware. It will therefore serve as a warning for other women.

The Gemara presents two versions of this ruling. The first version holds that the prohibition applies *only* when the husband explicitly states the reason at the time of the divorce; if he does not state it, the prohibition does not apply, and he may remarry, because any retroactive claim of his will carry no weight. The second version holds that the prohibition applies regardless of whether the reason was stated, but we nonetheless instruct him to make the declaration explicitly — not for halachic effect, but for the purpose of social publicity. By announcing before witnesses why he is divorcing her, the divorce becomes a public statement of consequence, serving the goal of controlling behavior: *kol Yisrael yishme'u ve'yira'u*.

### Supporting Baraitot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּלִישָּׁנָא קַמָּא, וְתַנְיָא כְּלִישָּׁנָא בָּתְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with the first version** of Rav Yosef bar Minyumi's statement citing Rav Naḥman, **and** it **is taught** in another *baraita* **in accordance with the latter version.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּלִישָּׁנָא קַמָּא – אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ הַמּוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שֵׁם רַע לֹא יַחְזִיר, וּמִשּׁוּם נֶדֶר לֹא יַחְזִיר? שֶׁמָּא תֵּלֵךְ וְתִנָּשֵׂא לְאַחֵר, וְנִמְצְאוּ דְּבָרִים בַּדָּאִין, וְיֹאמַר: ״אִילּוּ הָיִיתִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁכֵּן הוּא, אֲפִילּוּ אִם הָיוּ נוֹתְנִים לִי מֵאָה מָנֶה – לֹא הָיִיתִי מְגָרְשָׁהּ״; וְנִמְצָא גֵּט בָּטֵל וּבָנֶיהָ מַמְזֵרִין; לְפִיכָךְ אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: הֱוֵי יוֹדֵעַ, שֶׁהַמּוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שֵׁם רַע – לֹא יַחְזִיר, וּמִשּׁוּם נֶדֶר – לֹא יַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 3:4) **in accordance with the first version: Rabbi Meir said: For what** reason **did they say** that a man **who divorces his wife due to** her **bad reputation may not remarry** her, **and** one who divorces his wife due to **a vow may not remarry** her? **Perhaps she will go and marry another** man, **and** later **the matter will be found** to have been **fabricated,** i.e., he will find that the rumor was false or that it was possible to dissolve the vow, **and he will say: If I would have known that it is like this,** then **even if they would have given me one hundred** times **one hundred dinars** to divorce her **I would not have divorced her; and** the **bill of divorce** will be **found** to be **void, and her children** from her second husband will be considered ***mamzerim*. Therefore, one says to him** from the outset: **Be aware that** a man **who divorces his wife due to** her **bad reputation may not remarry** her, **and** one who divorces his wife **due to a vow may not remarry** her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּלִישָּׁנָא בָּתְרָא – אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ הַמּוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שֵׁם רַע לֹא יַחְזִיר, וּמִשּׁוּם נֶדֶר לֹא יַחְזִיר? שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל פְּרוּצוֹת בַּעֲרָיוֹת וּבִנְדָרִים. לְפִיכָךְ אוֹמְרִים לוֹ, אֱמוֹר לָהּ: ״הָוֵי יוֹדַעַת שֶׁמִּשּׁוּם שֵׁם רַע אֲנִי מוֹצִיאֵךְ וּמִשּׁוּם נֶדֶר אֲנִי מוֹצִיאֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 3:4) **in accordance with the latter version: Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: For what** reason **did they say that** a man **who divorces his wife due to** her **bad reputation may not remarry** her, **and** one who divorces his wife due to **a vow may not remarry** her? So that **Jewish women will not be licentious with regard to forbidden** sexual **relations or** lax **with regard to vows. Therefore,** in order to publicize this matter, **one says to him** upon divorcing his wife: **Say to her: Be aware that I am removing you** from the house **due to** your **bad reputation, or: I am removing you** from the house **due to a vow.**

### Tosafot vs Rashi

On the question of how the *takkanah* operates, Tosafot and Rashi diverge. According to Rashi, the husband's claim of *had I known* is simply not believed — the reasoning is that since he was informed he could never remarry her under these circumstances and yet proceeded with the divorce, he is presumed to have had complete *gemirut da'at*, unconditional intention. According to Tosafot, the mechanism is prophylactic: the man has no incentive to raise the retroactive claim at all, since raising it will gain him nothing — he cannot remarry her regardless. The practical difference emerges in a case where the husband makes the claim anyway, not to remarry her but for some other purpose. On Rashi's view the claim is categorically disbelieved; on Tosafot's view, the question is more complex.

### Public Announcement

According to the second version of Rav Nachman's ruling, the requirement that the husband make a declaration is about achieving public awareness — the logic of *kedei she'lo yiheyu bnot Yisrael perutzot ba'arayot u'vi'nedarim*. The husband is instructed to say, openly, to his wife: *mishum shem ra ani motziech* — it is because of your reputation that I am divorcing you; or *mishum neder ani motziech* — it is because of your vow. The purpose of this declaration is not to give halachic teeth to the retroactive-claim concern (that applies regardless), but to publicize the cause and thereby deter similar conduct among others. This is akin to the principle of *kol Yisrael yishme'u ve'yira'u*: public consequences function as a social deterrent. The question of how one could ever substantiate the husband's motivation if he does not announce it is implicit in the discussion — the answer is presumably that by the time of the divorce, he has already communicated his grievance to others, but the formal declaration at the time of the *get* is what gives it the required publicity and effect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּצַיְדָּן, בְּאֶחָד שֶׁאָמַר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ: ״קֻוֽנָּם אִם אֵינִי מְגָרְשִׁיךְ״, וְגֵרְשָׁהּ, וְהִתִּירוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים שֶׁיַּחְזִירֶנָּה, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, said:** There was **an incident in Tzaidan involving one** man **who said to his wife:** It is ***konam*,** i.e., it is forbidden like an offering, **if I do not divorce you, and he divorced her; and the Sages permitted him to remarry** her **for the betterment of the world.**

The *braita* recorded in support of the first position reflects Rabbi Meir's approach: *mipnei mah amru hamotzi et ishto mishum shem ra lo yachazir* — why did the sages say he cannot remarry her? Because he may otherwise go and claim the divorce was made in error, that the underlying premise was false, that *ilu hayisi yodeia* he never would have divorced her — thereby retroactively calling the *get* into question and rendering her subsequent children *mamzerim*. Therefore, the sages tell him in advance: know that regardless of what you later discover, you will never be able to remarry this woman. This removes the incentive or, on Rashi's account, ensures that any subsequent claim is disbelieved.

The *braita* supporting the second version reflects Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Yosi's approach: the goal is that women not be *perutzot ba'arayot u'vi'nedarim*, and therefore the husband is instructed to say *to his wife* — not merely to himself — why he is divorcing her. The declaration is not received by the husband as a warning but is made by him as a public statement of cause.

One might ask whether the *takkanah* should ever compel a man to divorce his wife against his will on these grounds. The answer is no — even from the perspective of *shelo yiheyu bnot Yisrael*, the community does not intervene to dissolve a marriage. The husband's private decision whether to tolerate his wife's conduct remains his. What the *takkanah* does is attach permanent consequences to the exercise of *that* decision: if he chooses to divorce her on these grounds, he surrenders the option of returning to her. The marriage is not broken by rabbinic authority; rather, the one who breaks it on these grounds is held to have done so definitively.

There follows a discussion touching on a concern raised about whether the husband's claim, if made, could ever actually succeed in retroactively voiding the *get*. The answer involves the principle of *devarim she'belev einam devarim*: unstated intentions carry no legal weight, and retroactive claims about what one would or would not have done — divorced purely in his own mind without any articulated condition — receive no halachic credence. The Gemara also addresses the question of whether, even if such a claim were theoretically valid, the wife who remarried in good faith on the strength of a *get* she had every reason to believe was valid would be considered to have acted *be-ones* — under compulsion — such that while the children might technically be *mamzerim* on his account, her return to him would in principle be possible, since the second marriage was entered into without any real *get* having divorced her from the first husband. These scenarios are treated as matters of serious concern warranting the structural protections the Mishnah establishes.

### The Case of the Man's Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּנָּא דְּקָתָנֵי מַעֲשֶׂה?
>
> **Translation:** **What** did the mishna **teach that** causes Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda to **teach** this **incident?** The mishna had just discussed a case where the woman took a vow, and it relates an incident where the man took a vow.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – כְּשֶׁנָּדְרָה הִיא, אֲבָל נָדַר אִיהוּ – יַחְזִיר; וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה נָמֵי בְּצַיְדָּן, בְּאֶחָד שֶׁאָמַר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ: ״קוּנָּם אִם אֵינִי מְגָרְשִׁיךְ״, וְגֵירְשָׁהּ, וְהִתִּירוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים שֶׁיַּחְזִירֶנָּה – מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna **is incomplete and this is what it is teaching: In what** case **is this statement said? When she took a vow,** since he may not remarry her in that case. **However,** if **he took a vow, he may remarry** her. **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, said:** There was **also an incident in Tzaidan involving one** man **that said to his wife:** It is ***konam* if I do not divorce you, and he divorced her; and the Sages permitted him to remarry her, for the betterment of the world.**

The Mishnah concludes this section with a case narrated by Rabbi Yosi ben Rabbi Yehuda: *maaseh be-Tzidan be'echad she'amar le'ishto konam im eini megareshech* — an incident in Tzidan involving a man who declared, "All fruit of the world be forbidden to me if I do not divorce you," and then divorced her to prevent the *konam* from taking effect. The sages permitted him to remarry her, *mipnei tikkun olam*. 

The Gemara asks what this case has to do with what preceded it, since the earlier *mishnah* concerned the wife's vow, not the husband's. The Gemara resolves this by explaining that the text is incomplete as transmitted: the full teaching would distinguish between the case where *she* makes the vow — in which the restrictions of the *mishnah* apply — and the case where *he* makes the vow — in which case he may remarry. The asymmetry is not surprising in formal terms: the concern about *bnot Yisrael perutzot bi'nedarim* is causally linked to the wife's conduct leading to divorce, not the husband's. When it is the husband who vows in a way that forces his own hand, the same dynamic does not obtain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״קֻוֽנָּם״? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, בְּאוֹמֵר: ״יֵאָסְרוּ כׇּל פֵּירוֹת שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם עָלַי אִם אֵינִי מְגָרְשָׁהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the formulation of the vow taken by the husband: **What** is meant by the expression: ***Konam*,** as stated by the husband? Generally this term is used when stating a vow of consecration; how does such a vow render her forbidden to him? **Rav Huna said** that the case is **when he says: All the produce that is in the world will become forbidden to me if I do not divorce you.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִתִּירוּ לוֹ שֶׁיַּחְזִירֶנָּה: פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא לִיגְזוֹר מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי נָתָן – דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: הַנּוֹדֵר – כְּאִילּוּ בָּנָה בָּמָה, וְהַמְקַיְּימוֹ – כְּאִילּוּ הִקְרִיב עָלֶיהָ קׇרְבָּן; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught: **And they permitted him to remarry her.** The Gemara asks: **It is obvious** that this is the case, as why shouldn't he be able to remarry her? The Gemara answers that **lest you say: Let us** institute a **decree due to** the statement **of Rabbi Natan. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan said: One who vows,** it is **as if he built** a personal **altar** during the time the Temple stood, when it became prohibited to build a personal altar to God outside the Temple. **And one who fulfills** a vow, **it is as if he sacrificed an offering on this** personal altar, thereby doubling his sin. Therefore, it would be possible to suggest that the husband is penalized and prohibited from remarrying her because he sinned by taking a vow and fulfilling it. Therefore, **it teaches us** that this is not the case.

The Gemara nonetheless wonders whether we should impose a *knas* even in the husband's case, under the principle that vow-making is itself problematic: Rabbi Natan teaches that *hanoder ke'ilu banah bamah* — one who makes a vow is as though he built an illicit altar, and one who fulfills it rather than seeking annulment is as though he sacrificed upon it. Perhaps, then, since his vow caused his wife to be divorced, he should bear a penalty and not be permitted to remarry. The Gemara rejects this: *ka mashma lan* — the ruling comes to teach us that we do not impose such a *knas*. The *tikkun olam* of allowing him to remarry is that there was no sufficient countervailing *tikkun olam* interest to prevent it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם: מַאי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם אִיכָּא? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אַרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The end of the mishna stated that the reason for this *halakha* was **for the betterment of the world.** The Gemara asks: **What betterment of the world is there** in allowing him to remarry her? **Rav Sheshet said:** This statement refers back **to the first clause** of the mishna. The expression: For the betterment of the world, is not an explanation of Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda's statement, but rather, it is meant to explain why there is a penalty that one may not remarry a woman whom he divorced due to her bad reputation or due to a vow she had taken.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם אַסֵּיפָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: אֵין בָּזוֹ מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Actually,** this expression applies **to the latter clause** of the mishna, **and this is what it taught: This is not for the betterment of the world.** In other words, when they instituted that he may not remarry her for the betterment of the world, it is only with regard to a case where the woman has taken a vow. However, when the husband takes a vow, there is no element of the betterment of the world and he is permitted to remarry her in that case.

The Gemara then debates whether the *tikkun olam* language of the Mishnah refers to the earlier cases — in which the prohibition on remarriage was itself the *tikkun olam* — or to this final case, in which the permission to remarry represents the *tikkun olam*. Rav Sheshet says the language refers to the opening of the Mishnah, the cases where remarriage is prohibited. Ravina says it refers to the final case: *ein bezo mipnei tikkun olam* — in the case of the husband's vow, there was no sufficient *tikkun olam* concern to prevent remarriage, and so the sages left the default in place.

## Aylonit Case

### Mishnah Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִשּׁוּם אַיְלוֹנִית, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא יַחְזִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who divorces his wife because** she is **a sexually underdeveloped woman** who is incapable of bearing children [***ailonit***], meaning that after their marriage it became clear that she was sexually underdeveloped, **Rabbi Yehuda says: He may not remarry** her, **and the Rabbis say: He may remarry** her.

The next Mishnah introduces a third scenario: *hamotzi et ishto mishum aylonit* — one who divorces his wife because she is an *aylonit*. An *aylonit* is a woman who does not develop female physical characteristics — she does not mature sexually in the expected way, presenting what might today be described as a failure to develop secondary sexual characteristics, and possibly some characteristics typically associated with males such as a deep voice. Rabbi Yehuda says: *lo yachazir* — he cannot remarry her. The Sages say: *yachazir* — he may.

An important preliminary observation from Tosafot: in many contexts in the Talmud it appears that when a woman turns out to be an *aylonit* unknown to her husband at the time of the marriage, the marriage itself is void — a *mekach taut*, a fundamental error — and no *get* is required. Rashi argues that after *nissu'in*, once the couple has consummated the marriage, the husband's reservations are presumed to be set aside, because he would not want the marriage to be retroactively void and his *bi'ah* to be *bi'at znut*. Tosafot rejects this: there are cases where even after consummation the error is so fundamental that the marriage is deemed void. The *aylonit* case is one of them. Therefore, Tosafot explains, the Mishnah's discussion of a *get* presupposes that she has not been definitively identified as an *aylonit* — it is a case of suspicion or reasonable doubt that she is one, not a case of certainty.

### Wife Remarries

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִישֵּׂאת לְאַחֵר וְהָיוּ לָהּ בָּנִים הֵימֶנּוּ, וְהִיא תּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ; אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אוֹמֵר לַהּ: שְׁתִיקוּתִיךְ יָפָה מִדִּיבּוּרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** If, after he divorced her, this *ailonit* **married another** man **and had children from him,** meaning that she was not actually an *ailonit*, **and she is demanding** payment of **her marriage contract** from her first husband, claiming that he unlawfully divorced her without paying her marriage contract as he claimed that she was an *ailonit* and their marriage was a mistaken transaction, **Rabbi Yehuda said** that **he** may **say to her: Your silence is preferable to your speech,** meaning that it is preferable for her to withdraw her claim. If she persists, he may say that he divorced her only because he believed her to be an *aylonit*, casting aspersions on the validity of the divorce and the status of her children. Therefore, it would be wise of her to withdraw her claim.

The Mishnah proceeds: *niset le'acher ve'hayu lo banim heimena* — she subsequently married another man and had children with him. This sequence of events retroactively demonstrates that she was *not* an *aylonit* after all — she bore children, which an *aylonit* cannot do. Now she returns to claim her *ketubah* from her first husband: since it is now apparent that she was not an *aylonit*, the premise of the divorce was false, and she is entitled to her *ketubah*, which presumably was not paid when he divorced her on account of a perceived defect on her part.

Rabbi Yehuda counsels: *ashtikusaich yafeh midiburaich* — your silence is better than your speech. He advises the woman not to press her claim too vigorously, because by loudly insisting that she was not an *aylonit*, she risks reopening the entire question of the validity of the original divorce. If the divorce is called into question, and she is found to have remarried while the *get* was in doubt, serious consequences follow. Better, Rabbi Yehuda advises, to quietly accept the situation and not pursue the *ketubah* in court.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה חָיֵישׁ לְקִלְקוּלָא, וְרַבָּנַן לָא חָיְישִׁי לְקִלְקוּלָא?! וְהָא אִיפְּכָא שָׁמְעִינַן לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara clarifies the dispute in the mishna: **Is this to say that Rabbi Yehuda is concerned about** potential **harm,** i.e., the harm that she may later incur if he casts aspersions on the validity of the divorce, and therefore rules that he may not remarry her, ensuring that he will divorce her only if he is absolutely certain that he wishes to do so, **and the Rabbis are not concerned about** potential **harm? But didn't we learn** that **they** each said **the opposite?**

The Gemara notes an apparent contradiction. Rabbi Yehuda in the present Mishnah seems to be concerned about *kilkul* — the danger that the husband will raise questions about the divorce — since that is precisely the concern underlying his advice to the woman to remain silent. But earlier, in the Mishnah about *shem ra* and *nedarim*, it was the *Rabbanan* who prohibited remarriage out of concern for *kilkul*, while Rabbi Yehuda was the one who permitted remarriage when the vow was not publicly known — suggesting that Rabbi Yehuda was *not* concerned about *kilkul*. Conversely, in the present Mishnah the *Chachamim* permit remarriage, suggesting they are *not* concerned about *kilkul*. The two Mishnahs seem to swap the positions of Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages entirely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: הַמּוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שֵׁם רַע – לֹא יַחְזִיר, וּמִשּׁוּם נֶדֶר – לֹא יַחְזִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל נֶדֶר שֶׁיָּדְעוּ בּוֹ רַבִּים – לֹא יַחְזִיר, וְשֶׁלֹּא יָדְעוּ בּוֹ רַבִּים – יַחְזִיר. אַלְמָא [רַבָּנַן חָיְישִׁי לְקִלְקוּלָא], וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא חָיֵישׁ לְקִלְקוּלָא!
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in the previous mishna (45b): A man **who divorces his wife due to** her **bad reputation may not remarry** her. **And** if one divorces his wife **due to a vow** that she stated, and he could not live with her under the conditions of the vow, **he may not remarry** her. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he divorced her due to **any vow that the public was aware of, he may not remarry** her, **but** if he divorced her due to a vow **that the public was not aware of, he may remarry** her. **Apparently, the Rabbis are concerned about** potential **harm,** and consequently, they do not make a distinction between one vow and another; **and Rabbi Yehuda is not concerned about** potential **harm,** and he makes a distinction between a vow that may lead to permissiveness and one that does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵיפוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said: Reverse** the opinions in this mishna. Rather, say that it is Rabbi Yehuda who says that he may remarry her, and he is not concerned about potential harm, while the Rabbis say he may not remarry her, and they are concerned about potential harm.

The Gemara initially proposes simply reversing the attributions (*ipoch*), but this creates further problems, since the end of the present Mishnah — where Rabbi Yehuda counsels silence — is clearly attributable to Rabbi Yehuda and clearly reflects a concern for *kilkul*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: נִישֵּׂאת לְאַחֵר וְהָיוּ לָהּ בָּנִים הֵימֶנּוּ, וְהִיא תּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ; אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אוֹמֵר לָהּ: ״שְׁתִיקוּתִיךְ יָפָה מִדִּיבּוּרִיךְ״ – מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה חָיֵישׁ לְקִלְקוּלָא! הָא נָמֵי אֵיפוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But from** the fact **that it teaches in the last clause** of the mishna: If, after he divorced her, this *ailonit* **married another** man **and had children from him, and she is demanding** payment of **her marriage contract** from her first husband, **Rabbi Yehuda said** that **he** may **say to her: Your silence is preferable to your speech,** and one can learn **by inference that Rabbi Yehuda is concerned about** potential **harm.** The Gemara answers: **Here too, reverse** the opinions, and it is the Rabbis who stated the last case of the mishna.

### Abyei Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּהָהִיא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – בְּצָרִיךְ סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Actually, do not reverse** the opinions, **and** say that **Rabbi Yehuda** is concerned about potential harm. The distinction is that **in that** mishna, which discussed a woman who took a vow, **he holds** in one matter **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and he holds** in another matter **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar. In** the case of a vow that **requires** dissolution by a halakhic authority **he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar** that there is no concern about potential harm in such a case, as a husband would not desire that his wife be disgraced before the court. **In** the case of a vow that **does not require** dissolution by a halakhic authority **he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who maintains that the husband would not be deemed credible if he were to state that he was unaware that he could nullify her vow.

Abaye resolves the contradiction without reversing any attribution. *L'olam lo tipoch* — leave the text as it stands. In the earlier Mishnah, Rabbi Yehuda's position on *nedarim* combined elements of two different Tannaic positions: in cases where the vow required a sage to annul (*tzarich chacham*), he holds like Rabbi Elazar — the husband would not be believed to claim *had I known you could go to a sage, I wouldn't have divorced you*, because a husband would not want his wife making the rounds of rabbinic courts. And in cases where the husband himself could have annulled the vow (*eino tzarich chacham*), he holds like Rabbi Meir — who says that of course you knew you could have annulled it, so your claim carries no weight. The practical result is that in the *neder* case, Rabbi Yehuda allows remarriage because whatever claim the husband might make will not be believed. But this is not because Rabbi Yehuda is categorically unconcerned about *kilkul*; it is because, in those specific cases, the *kilkul* concern lacks practical force. Where it does have practical force — as in the *aylonit* case — Rabbi Yehuda is indeed concerned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קַשְׁיָא, דְּרַבָּנַן אַדְּרַבָּנַן לָא קַשְׁיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Is it **difficult** to reconcile the statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** in this mishna **with** the statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** in the previous mishna, but it is **not difficult** to reconcile the statement **of the Rabbis** in this mishna **with** the statement **of the Rabbis** in the previous mishna? Abaye only addressed how to explain the contradiction between the statements of Rabbi Yehuda.

Rava adds a further dimension: even granting Abaye's reconciliation of Rabbi Yehuda's positions, what about the Rabbanan? In the earlier Mishnah they prohibited remarriage out of concern for *kilkul*, yet here they permit it by a husband who divorced an *aylonit*. Are they not equally concerned that he will later say *had I known she wasn't an aylonit, I wouldn't have divorced her*? Rava answers: *man Chachamim? Rabbi Meir* — the *Chachamim* who permit remarriage in the *aylonit* case are identified as Rabbi Meir, who holds that conditions which were not formally articulated carry no legal weight. Whatever was in the husband's mind at the time of the divorce, unless he articulated a proper *tnai*, it is irrelevant to the validity of the *get*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא קַשְׁיָא – כִּדְשַׁנִּינַן. דְּרַבָּנַן אַדְּרַבָּנַן לָא קַשְׁיָא, מַאן חֲכָמִים – רַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: בָּעֵינַן תְּנַאי כָּפוּל, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בִּדְלָא כַּפְלֵיהּ לִתְנָאֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** It is **not difficult** to reconcile the statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** in this mishna **with** the statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** in the previous mishna, **as we answered.** Additionally, it is **not difficult** to reconcile the statement **of the Rabbis** in this mishna **with** the statement **of the Rabbis** in the previous mishna, as **who** are **the Rabbis** in this mishna? It is **Rabbi Meir, who said: We require a compound condition** stipulating both positive and negative outcomes. **And with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with **a situation where he did not compound his condition,** as he stated only that he is divorcing her because she is an *ailonit*, but did not state that he would not divorce her if she were not an *ailonit*. Therefore, his condition is disregarded, and he may remarry her.

### Tnai Kafful

Rabbi Meir requires *tnai kafful* — a double condition — for any condition to be legally operative. A proper *tnai* must be stated in both its positive and negative forms: "if this is the case, the *get* takes effect; if this is not the case, the *get* does not take effect." Without this formulation, no mental reservation, however sincere, can undermine the legal act. Therefore, even if the husband genuinely would not have divorced her had he known she was not an *aylonit*, this is of no halachic consequence: he failed to articulate a *tnai kafful*, and the *get* is completely valid. Any retroactive claim he makes will be met with the response: whatever your unexpressed intentions were, you did not make the proper conditions, and therefore the *get* stands. *Devarim she'belev einam devarim* — what remains in the heart, unspoken and unformalized, has no legal standing.

This is a position of considerable stringency and clarity: the halachic act speaks for itself. Subjective intention that is not properly expressed and formalized in the act itself simply does not alter its legal character. Rabbi Meir's insistence on *tnai kafful* here serves a protective function — not for the husband who might want to reclaim his wife, but for the wife who acted in good faith on the basis of a *get* she received and whose subsequent life and children should not be placed in jeopardy by the husband's retroactive assertions.

### Final Resolution

The *Chachamim* thus permit remarriage in the *aylonit* case for a straightforward reason: since no properly formalized condition was attached to the *get* at the time it was given, the husband's claimed intentions are irrelevant. Even if he goes around asserting that he never would have divorced her had he known, that claim can get no traction. Nobody will question the validity of the *get* on that basis, because the recognized rule — that *tnai* without *tnai kafful* is ineffective — forecloses it. There is accordingly no danger of *kilkul*, and therefore no basis for the prohibition on remarriage. The *get* was valid; she was free to remarry; and the husband is free to take her back.
