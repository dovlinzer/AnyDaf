# Shabbat 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Previous Principles

### Patur & Domain

The daf begins at the very bottom of 3b with the word *iba'it eima*. Before proceeding, it is worth reviewing several principles that have emerged over the preceding days of learning. First, the word *patur* as it appears in the *Mishna* does not mean fully permitted — it still indicates a rabbinic problem. Second, regarding the location of an object: if an object is resting on your person and you are stationary, the object is considered to be in the same domain that you occupy. A useful way to picture this is that you become like a piece of furniture — the object on you is like an object on a table. Consequently, when you move, it is as though you are lifting the object off that table and removing it from its domain.

### Person as Furniture

This principle has a practical implication: if one finds oneself walking on *Shabbos* outside of an *eiruv* with something on one's person, one should not stop — because stopping is like setting the object down, and then moving again is like picking it up anew. That is the rule when the object rests on one's person and one is stationary.

### Hand in Two Domains

The situation changes, however, when one's body is in one domain and one's hand is extended into another. According to the way *Tosafot* mostly understands the *Gemara*, if one's person is inside, in *reshut hayachid*, while one's hand is extended outside into *reshut harabim* — or vice versa — and the object is held in that extended hand, the object is considered to be in neither domain. It rests in a kind of indeterminate middle zone. The central question the *Gemara* was grappling with is: given that the object is in this suspended state, may one retract one's hand?

The concrete scenario is as follows: one extends one's arm through a window on *Shabbos* without an *eiruv*, and the object in one's hand is now suspended over the airspace of *reshut harabim* — which, as was established, extends only up to *asara tefachim*. The object is neither in *reshut hayachid* (because the hand is outside) nor in *reshut harabim* (because the person is inside). May one pull the hand back in?

### Two Braitot

Two *braitot* were cited earlier, one permitting retraction and one prohibiting it. The *Gemara* offered various interpretations relating to when a *knas* — a rabbinic fine — is imposed and when it is not.

### Kaneis Resolution

The *Gemara* now resolves the tension. Two lines from the bottom of 3b, the *Gemara* states: *iba'it eima le'olam lo knasu* — in truth, no fine was ever imposed to prevent one who extended one's hand *beshogeg*, unintentionally, from pulling it back in. *Velo kashya* — there is no difficulty between the two *braitot*: *kan le'oto chatzer, kan lachatzer acheret* — one case refers to pulling the hand back into one's own yard, the other to dropping the object into a neighbor's yard.

The distinction is this: if one pulls one's hand back into one's own house, one has simply reversed one's action. The net result is as though nothing was done, and we permit that. However, if one's hand is suspended over *reshut harabim* and one then drops the object into a neighbor's yard, one has in effect accomplished something — the object is no longer in one's own domain. Even if the full intention was not realized (perhaps one wanted it in *reshut harabim* itself, not a neighbor's yard), part of the original intention has been satisfied.

This is the substance of the exchange between Rabba and Rav Nachman cited by the *Gemara*: *haita yado mele'ah peirot vehotzi'ah lachutz* — one extended a hand full of fruit outside — *mahu lehachzirah le'oto chatzer?* May one return it to one's own yard? *Amar lei mutar* — it is permitted. *Lachatzer acheret mai?* — what about a different yard? *Ve'amar lei assur* — that is forbidden.

Rabba then asked *u'mai shena* — what is the difference? In either case the object was in this middle zone, neither *reshut harabim* nor *reshut hayachid*, and one is now placing it somewhere. Rav Nachman responded with a somewhat idiomatic expression — *lechi tichol ala kura d'malcha*, weigh out for me a lump of salt — which *Rashi* explains either as a gentle way of saying "sit with it and you'll figure it out," or perhaps a mild expression of amusement at the question. The answer, once one thinks it through: *hatam lo it'avid machshavto, hacha it'avid machshavto* — in the case where one pulls the object back to one's own house, one's intention was not satisfied; we have essentially had one reverse one's action, leaving things as they began. But placing it in another yard means the object has ended up somewhere other than one's original house — part of one's purpose has been achieved.

The conclusion of all this is that while the *Gemara* had entertained the possibility of requiring one to stand with one's arm extended throughout *Shabbos* as a kind of fine — a position that seems extreme and risks the person simply dropping the object into *reshut harabim* and committing a biblical transgression — the resolution is that at least for *shogeg*, one is permitted to pull one's hand back. One may not necessarily drop the object into a neighbor's yard, but one may at least retract. Whether the same leniency applies to *mezid* is less clear; the *Gemara* seriously entertained the possibility that in a case of willful violation we might insist one keep one's arm extended even at the risk of it ultimately leading to a biblical transgression.

That same tension — how much concern do we have that a rabbinic restriction will lead to a biblical transgression — is precisely the issue in the next topic: the bread in the oven.

## Bread in Oven

### Remove Before Violation

*Ba'ei Rav Bibi bar Abaye* — Rav Bibi bar Abaye raised the famous question: *hidbik pas b'tanur* — someone stuck dough on the walls of an oven to bake on *Shabbos*. On *Shabbos*, one does not transgress the moment the dough is placed; the transgression requires the food to actually cook. This opens a deeper question about the nature of the transgressive act: is the act completed by the initial placement of the dough on the heat, on condition that it eventually finishes cooking? Or is the transgression constituted by the ongoing process of cooking, such that one is considered to be continuously involved in the act as it proceeds on *Shabbos*?

An instructive parallel is the case of planting a seed, where the *Rashash* notes that the seed must ultimately take root — a process that takes about two weeks according to *halacha*. Yet the act of placing the seed in the ground is the transgression, on condition that it is not a meaningless act, that it eventually takes root. One could ask the same question about cooking: is the transgression the initial placing on the fire, or is it the entire cooking process, of which the placement was merely the beginning?

With that conceptual backdrop, the question is: if one put dough in the oven on *Shabbos* and the natural course of events will result in the bread baking and a transgression being completed — may one remove the dough before it has finished baking in order to prevent the transgression? Ordinarily, removing bread from an oven on *Shabbos* is not a biblical prohibition — it is rabbinically forbidden as a form of skilled labor, *chochmah*, too reminiscent of weekday activity. But here, if one is not permitted to remove it, one will have committed a full violation of *Shabbos*. The question is: *hitiru lo lirdoto kodem sheyavo lidei issur chatas o lo hitiru lo?* Did they allow one to remove the bread before one became obligated in a *chatas*, before reaching the point of having violated *Shabbos* — or not?

### Shogeg vs. Mezid

Rav Acha bar Abaye posed the clarifying question to Ravina: *heichi dami?* — what exactly is the case? If we say the person placed the dough *beshogeg* — unintentionally, having forgotten it was *Shabbos* — and then continued in that state of unawareness, *velo yadei*, still unaware that he had done anything wrong — then who are we permitting to remove the bread? The person himself doesn't know there is a problem. He thinks it is an ordinary day. There is no one asking for permission.

So it must be, says the *Gemara*, *de-adkar ve-iftar* — that the person subsequently remembered it was *Shabbos*. This is a very common real-life situation: one walks into a room without thinking, performs some act, and then suddenly realizes it is *Shabbos*. Here, however, the realization is potentially helpful — if one is permitted to remove the bread at that point, one can actually reverse the problematic situation and prevent the full transgression from being completed.

### Chatas Awareness Rule

But the *Gemara* presses: if the person became aware in the middle — *mi mechayiv?* — even if he does nothing, he would not be obligated in a *chatas*. Why? *Ve-hasnan: kol chayavei chataos einan chayavin ad she-tehei techilasan shegaga ve-sofan shegaga* — all those obligated in a *chatas* are only obligated if they were unaware from the beginning of the act to its conclusion. If one became aware at any point during the commission of the transgression, one does not bring a *chatas*. One is not *chayav misa* because one did not begin the act knowingly, but one is also not *chayav chatas* because one did not complete it in a state of unawareness.

This rule also reveals something important about the nature of the transgression. If becoming aware midway through the process disqualifies one from *chatas* obligation — as though one became aware "during" the transgression — that implies the act of cooking is understood as an ongoing process, not merely the instantaneous moment of placing the dough on the fire. If the transgression were simply the act of placement, then becoming aware afterward would be irrelevant; one was fully unaware at the moment of placement. The fact that awareness in the middle matters strongly suggests that the ongoing cooking is itself considered part of the continuous act, and one is treated as ongoingly performing the *melacha* throughout the baking process.

### No Korban Question

This gives the *Gemara* pause. If becoming aware midway means one would not be *chayav chatas* regardless, why does one need permission to remove the bread? *Tosafot* questions the *Gemara*'s framing: one might think the goal is simply to prevent any violation of *Shabbos*, not merely to avoid the specific obligation of a *korban*. Even if one would not bring a sacrifice, the *melacha* would still constitute a transgression of *Shabbos*, and preventing that would seem a worthy enough reason to permit the removal. For some reason, the *Gemara* does not seem willing to frame it that way — it treats the rationale as being specifically about saving one from a *korban* obligation.

### Mezid Case

*Ella b'meizid* — so perhaps the case is one of willful violation. The person intentionally placed the dough in the oven on *Shabbos*. Now, despite having begun with full awareness and willfulness, a spirit of regret came over him — and the question is whether we allow him, even in this situation, to remove the bread. The *Gemara* notes: *kodem sheyavo lidei issur sekilah miba'ei lei* — the question should really be phrased as whether he can reverse the act before becoming liable to *sekilah* (stoning, the penalty for willful *Shabbos* violation), not before becoming liable in a *chatas*. This confirms that the case is indeed *mezid*.

### Rav Sheshet Challenge

*Maskif leh Rav Sheshet* — Rav Sheshet challenges this framing. He first raises the position *le'olam beshogeg* — that it really is *shogeg* — and asks: *ul'man hitiru? l'acherim* — for whom are we granting permission? Not for the person himself, who has walked away unaware, but for a bystander. Someone else witnessed the situation — passed by, noticed the bread baking, and wants to remove it in order to spare this oblivious fellow from the full transgression and the *korban* obligation. The bystander is contemplating violating a *d'rabbanan* in order to help someone who transgressed *beshogeg*.

*V'chi omrin lo le'adam cheit b'kdei sheyizkeh chavercha?* — Do we tell a person to sin in order to benefit his fellow? This raises profound questions. On one hand, there is clearly a real obligation toward one's fellow: one may not place a stumbling block before another (*lifnei iver, machshol*), one may not be a passive bystander when another is sinning, and there is a genuine *chiyuv* of *tochacha* — of remonstrance, of *l'hafrisho min issur* — separating one's fellow from transgression. These responsibilities are real. Yet it seems that this obligation does not extend to the point of actively transgressing oneself on behalf of another.

The case is actually *shogeg*, which makes the challenge more striking. The person was unintentional, would have been *chayav korban*, and yet even so, Rav Sheshet objects: we would not permit a bystander to violate a *derabbanan* to spare him. The analogy to *chayecha v'chayei chavercha chayecha kodmin* is instructive — one is not halachically required to sacrifice a limb to save another person's life, even though one must take significant steps to help someone in danger. There is a principle of proportionality and primacy of self that runs through *halacha*.

### Allow Removal

*Ella amar Rav Ashi le'olam bemezid* — Rav Ashi returns to the case of *mezid*. *Ve'eima* — and therefore shift the reading of the text: the question was not whether to permit removal before becoming *chayav chatas* (which would not apply to *mezid*), but before becoming *chayav sekilah*. Rav Acha the son of Rava (*Rav Acha brei deRava*) stated that the matter was actually transmitted not as a question but as a ruling: *amar Rav Bibi bar Abaye lo ba'ei* — Rav Bibi bar Abaye did not ask; he declared. *Hidbik pas b'tanur, hetiru lo lirdosah kodem sheyavo lidei issur sekilah* — if one placed the bread in the oven, they did permit one to remove it before becoming liable to *sekilah*. The conclusion is affirmative, and it is in the case of *mezid*.

This conclusion is consistent with a broader theme that recurs throughout Masechet *Shabbos*: the *Gemara* frequently permits one to violate a *derabbanan* prohibition in order to avoid a *de'oraissa* transgression. The concern for human psychology — that if no relief valve is provided, people will simply go ahead and commit the biblical transgression — is a recurring consideration. Whether there is a distinction between cases involving a new act versus reversing a previous act could perhaps be argued, but the conclusion here is the same: even beginning from willful violation, we allow one to violate the *derabbanan* and remove the bread in order to avoid *issur sekilah*.

### Tosafot: Interrupt Act

*Tosafot* raises a sharp question on the entire discussion. If a person willfully placed the bread in the oven, he is hardly the type to suddenly become punctilious about a *derabbanan* prohibition. If we were to tell him he is not allowed to remove the bread, who would listen? He would say: so now, all of a sudden, I'm going to be stringent about a *derabbanan* and thereby become *chayav sekilah*? The practical effect of prohibiting him from removing the bread seems negligible — he would simply remove it anyway.

One could respond that the question is simply whether he receives a demerit for violating the *derabbanan* or not, regardless of whether he would actually comply. But *Tosafot* does not find this satisfying. Instead, *Tosafot* proposes a remarkable *chiddush*: if we were to rule that he is not permitted to remove the bread, then his non-removal would be *ba'ones* — against his will, coerced by halacha. And if the conclusion of the baking process occurred *ba'ones* — because the halacha effectively tied his hands — then he would not be *chayav* for the completion of the act. Crucially, this reasoning again reflects the understanding that the act of baking is an ongoing continuous act: the initial placement was willful, but if the conclusion was *ba'ones* (he wanted to remove it and was prohibited), then the end of the act was effectively forced upon him.

The conclusion that emerges from *Tosafot* is therefore even more powerful: the *Gemara*'s preference is for him to actively violate the *derabbanan* and remove the bread — thereby genuinely interrupting the act — rather than to benefit from the exemption of *ba'ones* at the conclusion. It is better to interrupt the act genuinely and thereby not transgress at all, than to conclude the act under duress and be technically exempt. The commitment to avoiding *chillul Shabbos* is so strong that we prefer one incur a rabbinic violation in order to genuinely prevent the biblical one, even when the alternative would also technically exempt one from liability.

### Tosafot Distinctions

*Tosafot* then turns to address the challenge of Rav Sheshet — *omerin le'adam cheit kdei sheyizkeh chaveircha* — and notes that there appear to be numerous cases in the *Gemara* where not only is one permitted to violate a lesser prohibition in order to prevent another person from committing a greater one, but one may even be obligated to do so. How do we reconcile those cases with Rav Sheshet's position?

*Tosafot* offers several distinctions. The first involves a case where poor people had taken fruit from someone's field, and if the owner did not perform a certain minor *derabbanan* act, the poor people would end up eating *tevel*. In that case, the *Gemara* permits the owner to perform the minor violation. *Tosafot* explains: that case is different because the owner is implicated in the situation. The transgression that is about to occur is connected to the owner's role — the people took the fruit from his field, and he bears some degree of responsibility for what flows from that. He is not a pure third party; he is party to the chain of events. That is why we permit him to violate a *derabbanan* to prevent their sin.

A second distinction relates to the difference between *preventing* someone from sinning before it begins, as opposed to *reversing* a sin already set in motion. There is a meaningful difference between the two. When one can intervene before a transgression has started, the case for permitting a minor violation is much stronger. In the case of the bread in the oven, the initial act has already been set in motion — the transgression has begun. One's moral responsibility to help is considerably reduced when the other person has already dug their own hole, even if through *shogeg*.

A third consideration raised by *Tosafot* concerns the degree to which the person is to blame. If someone is about to transgress through no fault of their own — not willfully, not negligently, but simply by circumstances entirely beyond their control — the case for intervention is stronger. One might imagine a scenario where someone unknowingly purchased food from a store later found to be selling *treifos*, and one would have to violate a *derabbanan* — such as going *chutz l'tchum* — in order to get the information to the person in time. In such a case, the person is entirely blameless and there may be a genuine obligation to intervene.

### Self vs. Others

There is also the distinction the *Gemara* itself draws: the person in whose hands the situation lies — the one who originally placed the bread in the oven — clearly has more latitude to perform a minor violation to save himself from a major sin. That is why the *Gemara* ultimately shifted to talking about the person himself (*me*) rather than a bystander. It is one thing to say *ein omrin le'adam chatei k'dei she'yizkeh chaveircha* — we do not tell *you* to sin for *your fellow's* sake. It is another matter entirely when one is seeking to save oneself.

As for whether the *shogeg* case — where one became aware midway and would therefore not be *chayav korban* — also permits removal, the *Gemara* did not formally return to that question. *Tosafot* indicates that even so, the violation of *Shabbos* itself would remain something one would wish to avoid, and it would follow by analogy that removal would also be permitted in that scenario.

## Four-by-Four Requirement

### Poor Man Takes Bread

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Mishna*'s case of the *ani* and the *ba'al habayit*. *Pashat he'ani es yado* — the poor man extends his hand inside the house, takes bread from the hand of the homeowner, and carries it out. In so doing he has performed *akira*, *hotza'ah*, and *hanacha* — the complete act of carrying. The *Gemara* asks: *amai chayav?* — why is he *chayav*? *Ve'ba'inan akira v'hanachah mei'al gabei makom arba al arba v'leika* — we require the removal and placement to involve a space of four by four, and that condition does not appear to be met here. The object was taken from the homeowner's hand, which is not a *makom arba al arba*. Why is the *ani* liable?

### Four-by-Four Meaning

*Rashi* and *Tosafot* offer different explanations of what the *makom arba'ah* requirement means and why it exists.

*Rashi* explains — from about eight lines before the end of the passage — *d'havi chashuv l'meihavi hanachato hanachah v'akira-to akira*: the four-by-four space is what lends *significance* to the act. It is not about the size of the object being carried but about the significance of the act of removal from that place. *Ve'yado lav makom arba hu* — one's hand is not a *makom arba*, and therefore the act lacks the requisite significance.

This connects to a broader difficulty with understanding *hotza'ah* as a *melacha*. *Hotza'ah* is the most conceptually challenging of the *melachot* on *Shabbos*. It is called by the *Rishonim* *melacha geru'ah* — a deficient or weak form of creative labor — because unlike other *melachot*, it does not produce something; it merely moves something. The *Rishonim* therefore focus more heavily on the significance of the activity rather than its result. One way of giving the activity significance is to require that it constitute a meaningful change — not just the movement of an object, but the removal of an object from its space and the placement of it into another space. If something is removed from a significant space, one has changed the nature of that space; one has also changed the status of the object by relocating it. Without a space of four by four, there is no meaningful "space" from which the removal constitutes a genuine change.

*Tosafot* points to a *pasuk*: *al yetze ish mi-mekomo be-yom ha-Shabbat* — do not leave your place on the seventh day. One *drasha* in Eruvin reads this as *al yotzi* — do not take your vessels from their place, connecting it to *hotza'ah*. The phrase *mi-mekomo* — from its place — suggests that the transgression involves removing something from its *makom*, its established space. From this reading, both the concept of place and the requirement of a significant *makom* are anchored in the biblical source for *hotza'ah*.

*Tosafot* quotes *Rabbeinu Tam*: *ein regilut le-hani'ach chefetz pachot me-arba'ah* — it is not normal practice to set an object down in a space smaller than four. He then also cites *Ri*, who distinguishes between *mekomo shel adam* — the personal space of a person, which is *arba amot* — and *mekomo shel chefetz* — the space of an object, for which *Tosafot* ultimately seems to acknowledge that the relevant measure is *arba'ah al arba'ah tefachim*, not *amot*. This is a critical distinction: *arba'ah tefachim* is approximately one foot by one foot — roughly the size of a tray — as opposed to six feet by six feet. The *Gemara* will later entertain the possibility that the person's hand was spread out, or that an apron was involved — clearly indicating the measure is *tefachim*, not *amot*.

### Gemara's Question

The *Gemara* thus presses: *ve-leika* — the four-by-four requirement is not met here. Rava responds: *ha mani Rabbi Akiva hi* — this is the view of Rabbi Akiva. *De-amar lo ba'inan makom arba'ah al arba'ah* — Rabbi Akiva holds that we do not require a *makom arba'ah al arba'ah*. But what exactly is the *girsa* here — is the measurement in *tefachim* or *amot*? From the context, it is *tefachim*, as the subsequent discussion will make clear. The question then is: what is Rabbi Akiva's principle that dispenses with this requirement?

## Rabbi Akiva Position

### Kluta Position

Rava identifies the source: *d'tnan*, as we learned in the *Mishna* — *hazorek mereshut hayachid lereshut hayachid ureshut harabim be'emtza* — one who throws an object from a private domain to a private domain with a public domain in between. *Rabbi Akiva mechayev, vechachamim potrim* — Rabbi Akiva declares the person *chayav* and the *chachamim* exempt.

Now, why would Rabbi Akiva say one is *chayav* here? The object never stopped in *reshut harabim* — it flew straight through the air. Yet Rabbi Akiva says: *amrinan kluta k'mi she'huncha damya* — we say that something absorbed in the airspace is considered as though it was placed on the ground. *Verabanan savri lo amrinan kluta k'mi she'huncha damya* — and the Rabbis say we do not apply this principle. Rabbi Akiva's position is thus that once an object passes through the airspace of a domain, it is as though it came to rest there. And if so, it has effectively been placed in that domain — and one is *chayav* not only for bringing it into *reshut harabim* but perhaps even a second time for then taking it from *reshut harabim* into the second *reshut hayachid*.

If Rabbi Akiva can apply this to an object flying through the air — never even touching the ground — then certainly he would apply it to an object held stationary in one's hand: once it is in the airspace of a domain, it is considered fully present there and one is *chayav*.

### Rava's Question

But the *Gemara* raises a challenge: *lemeimra d'pshita leh l'Rava de'bi'kluta k'mi she'huncha damya?* — does Rava simply take it as clear that the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim* is about *kluta k'mi she'huncha*? *Uvetoch asara paligi* — and that they are arguing specifically about the case below ten *tefachim*, which is the airspace of *reshut harabim*? *V'ha mibaya ba'i lah Rava* — but Rava himself raised this as an open question! The *Gemara* quotes Rava's earlier inquiry: *lemata me'asara paligi?* — is their argument specifically about the case below ten *tefachim*?

### Two Interpretations

Two entirely different framings of the debate are possible, and Rava himself raised both.

The first interpretation: *Rabbi Akiva savar kluta k'mi she'huncha damya* — Rabbi Akiva holds that something caught in the airspace is like it was placed on the ground, and the Rabbis disagree. On this reading, their dispute is specifically about what happens when the object travels below ten *tefachim* — the airspace of *reshut harabim*. The object passed through the air within that zone, and the question is whether that counts as *hanacha*. *Aval lemala me'asara divrei hakol patur* — above ten *tefachim*, everyone agrees one is *patur*, since that is not the airspace of *reshut harabim*. According to this reading, the debate has nothing to do with the principle of extending beams (*moshe*), and *lo yalpinan zorek mimushit* — we do not derive the law of throwing from the law of handing.

The second interpretation inverts the logic entirely. *Od eima lemala me'asara paligi* — perhaps they are arguing specifically about what happens *above* ten *tefachim*, and the dispute has nothing to do with *kluta k'mi she'huncha* at all. The real issue is whether we learn the law of throwing from the special case of Moshe handing the beams: *d'Rebbi Akiva savar yalpinan zorek mi'Moshe* — Rabbi Akiva holds that throwing from one *reshut hayachid* to another over a *reshut harabim* is analogous to the case of Moshe's Leviyim handing beams from one wagon to another over public ground, which was the prototype for *hotza'ah* in this form. *Verabbanan savri lo yalpinan zorek mi'Moshe* — the Rabbis say we do not derive throwing from that case.

The case of Moshe's beams: the *Gemara* describes a specific scenario from the *midbar* in which Leviyim on wagons carrying the beams of the *Aron* would hand objects from one wagon to the next. Each wagon constituted a *reshut hayachid*, and the transfer occurred over *reshut harabim* in between. That act of handing — *mushit* — over a *reshut harabim* is itself considered a form of *hotza'ah*. The question is whether *zorek* — throwing — is comparable. On the second reading, the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis is not about whether the object rested in the airspace below ten *tefachim*, but about whether throwing is sufficiently analogous to the case of Moshe handing the beams. And if they argue above ten *tefachim* — in the zone that is definitely not *reshut harabim* proper — then according to this reading, *aval lemata me'asara divrei hakol chayav* — below ten *tefachim*, everyone would agree one is *chayav*, because below ten we would say *kluta k'mi she'huncha dami*.

After raising both possibilities, the *Gemara* concludes: *ha lo kashya. Batar d'ibaya hadar ipshita lei* — this is not difficult, because perhaps after Rava raised the question, he ultimately resolved it: *d'savar Rebbi Akiva kluta k'mi she'huncha damya* — Rabbi Akiva's principle is indeed about the airspace below ten *tefachim*, and it is the principle of *kluta k'mi she'huncha*. And if so, the *chiddush* of Rabbi Akiva is radical: not only does one not need the object to be stationary, not only does one not need it to be on a physical surface, but merely passing through the airspace is equivalent to having rested there.

### Akira vs. Hanacha

Even granting all of this, the *Gemara* presses further: *v'dilma hanacha hu d'la bei, akira bei* — perhaps it is only *hanacha* that does not require a four-by-four space according to Rabbi Akiva; perhaps *akira* — the uprooting, the initial removal — still requires that the object be removed from a *makom* of four by four.

Why would *akira* carry a greater requirement than *hanacha*? *Tosafot* points back to the *pasuk* cited earlier — *al yetze ish mi-mekomo* — which, when read as *al yotzi*, emphasizes the act of removal from a *makom*. The very language focuses on removing something *from* its place, giving the *akira* a particular dependence on the concept of *makom*. The act of *hanacha* might not be subject to the same textual emphasis.

Another possible explanation offered relates to the concept of *kluta* itself. *Kluta* derives from a root suggesting absorption or reception — as in *arei miklat*, cities that absorb and shelter. When an object enters a domain's airspace, the domain, so to speak, receives it. That act of entry — of being absorbed — is what *kluta k'mi she'huncha* describes. This dynamic of reception applies to *hanacha*, to placing: the receiving domain takes in the object. But departure — *akira*, leaving a space — is not an act of reception by the destination, and the principle of *kluta* may not apply there in the same way.

In our *Mishna*, it appears from the case itself that even the *akira* did not require four by four, since the *ani* took the bread from the *ba'al habayit*'s hand, which is plainly not four by four. This seems to push back against saying that *akira* requires a four-by-four space. But the *Gemara* cautions that one cannot prove this definitively from the case, since there may be other explanations. As *Rashi* might suggest, one cannot simply assume the *Gemara* has resolved this; perhaps Rabbi Akiva's position covers both *akira* and *hanacha*, but perhaps it extends only to one. The *Gemara* leaves this as an unresolved question.

## Tree Branch Case

### Small Protrusion

The *Gemara* now considers a *braita*: *zarak venach agabi ziz kol shehu* — one threw an object and it came to rest on a small protrusion. *Rabbi mechayev, vachachamim potrin* — Rabbi declares the person *chayav*, and the *chachamim* exempt. This appears to be a direct proof that one may be *chayav* even when the object lands on a space smaller than four by four *tefachim* — since *kol shehu* means any size at all.

### Abaye Reframing

The *Gemara* deflects: *hasham kidebayeina lemeimar lekaman kideAbaye* — there, it will be reframed as Abaye reframes it. *De'amar Abaye: hacha be'ilan ha'omed bereshus hayachid venofov noto lereshus harabim* — we are dealing with a tree whose trunk stands in *reshut hayachid* and whose branch extends into *reshut harabim*. *Ve'zarak venach al nofo* — one threw an object from *reshut harabim* and it came to rest on the branch. *Rabbi savar amrinan shadi nofo basar ikaro* — Rabbi holds that we treat the branch as following the trunk. *Verabbanan savri lo amrinan shadi nofo basar ikaro* — the Rabbis hold that we do not say the branch follows the trunk.

### Branch Follows Trunk

*Tosafot* understands this debate in a fairly *peshat* manner: the dispute is about which domain the branch is considered to belong to. If the object lands on the branch and the branch follows the trunk — which is rooted in *reshut hayachid* — then throwing the object onto the branch is considered as though one threw it into *reshut hayachid*, making one *chayav*. If the branch does not follow the trunk, it is considered part of *reshut harabim*, and the object never genuinely arrived in *reshut hayachid*.

But there is a further relevance to the question of four by four: if the branch follows the trunk, then the significance of the trunk — which presumably does have a base of four by four *tefachim* — extends to the branch. The object on the branch is, in effect, considered to be resting on the trunk, which is a *makom arba'ah*. That is why one would be *chayav* according to Rabbi — not because a small protrusion suffices as a *makom* without four by four, but because the trunk provides the four by four and the branch is regarded as an extension of it.

*Rashi*'s reading differs somewhat. According to *Rashi*, the object is actually considered to be in *reshut harabim*, not *reshut hayachid*, but the connection to the trunk gives the branch significance — as though it were a four-by-four space — by virtue of being attached to an object that is itself large and significant. Imagine a table that is four by four *tefachim* with a small extension: placing something on the extension is significant because it is connected to the whole. Both *Rashi* and *Tosafot* agree, however, on the essential point for our purposes: the reason Rabbi says one is *chayav* is because the trunk provides a four-by-four base, not because four by four is unnecessary.

### Cannot Prove Absence

Therefore, says the *Gemara*, one cannot derive from this *braita* that four by four is not required. *Ella* — rather, this case depends entirely on the status of the trunk. It does not demonstrate a general principle that the four-by-four requirement may be dispensed with. The question of whether *hotza'ah* can be *chayav* without any *makom arba'ah* remains unresolved from this source.

## Public-to-Public Case

### Window Throw

*Ella ha Rebbi, detanya: zarak mereshus harabim lereshut harabim ureshut hayachid be'emtza* — the following case attributed to Rabbi: one threw an object from a public domain to a public domain with a private domain in between. The object flew through someone's window, crossed through the private house, and landed in the street on the other side.

### Two Transgressions

*Rabbi mechayiv, vachachamim potrin*. Rabbi holds one is *chayav*, the *chachamim* exempt the person. Now, crucially, this case cannot be explained by the principle of Moshe's beams: that principle applies specifically to private-to-private with public in the middle, not public-to-public with private in the middle. So Rabbi's ruling here must reflect a genuine position that does not require the object to come to rest — it flew straight through without stopping — and also does not require a *makom arba'ah*. And *Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* adds: *mechayiv haya Rebbi shtayim* — Rabbi would hold one *chayav* for two separate transgressions: *achas mishum hotza'ah ve'achas mishum hachnasah* — one for the *hotza'ah* (carrying out, from *reshut harabim* into *reshut hayachid*) and one for the *hachnasah* (bringing in, from *reshut hayachid* back into *reshut harabim* on the other side). This demonstrates that Rabbi requires neither a *hanacha* on a *makom arba'ah* nor an *akira* from a *makom arba'ah* — the object was in motion the entire time.

### Roofed House Limit

However, *Rava* and *Shmuel* both qualify Rabbi's position: *lo mechayiv Rebbi ella bereshut hayachid mekura* — Rabbi only declares one *chayav* if the private domain through which the object passed was a roofed house. *De'amrinan beisa keman demalya dami* — because we say a house is considered as though it were full. The roof of the house is what makes the airspace of the house count as genuinely "occupied" — when the object passes through that enclosed, roofed space, it is as though it was truly present within the domain. *Aval she'eino mekura lo* — but if the private domain had no roof, Rabbi would not declare one *chayav*. This is the principle of *kluta k'mi she'huncha* at work: a closed, roofed house truly absorbs the object, making the passage through its airspace equivalent to placement; an unroofed space does not.

This limitation is directly relevant to our case. The scenario of the *ani* taking bread from the *ba'al habayit*'s hand does not involve a roofed house through which an object is thrown — it involves taking directly from another's hand in an open setting. Rabbi's position, restricted to *bayit mekurah*, cannot serve as a source for the principle that four by four is unnecessary in the hand-to-hand case.

### Unresolved Hand Case

The *Gemara* thus reaches the end of the *amud* without a resolution to the original question. The case of the *ani* and the *ba'al habayit* — where the object was transferred from hand to hand, with no *makom arba'ah* at either end of the act — remains without a satisfactory answer. The matter is to be continued.
