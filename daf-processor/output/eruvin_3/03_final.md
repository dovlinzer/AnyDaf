# Eruvin 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Heichal Basis & Dimensions

### Tanna Kamma & R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פִּתְחוֹ שֶׁל הֵיכַל עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה גָּבוֹהַּ וַעֲשָׂרָה אַמּוֹת רָחָב. וּמִתּוֹךְ זֶה הִיא הַדַּלְתוֹת שֵׁשׁ אַמּוֹת בָּעוּבְי שְׁלוֹשׁ אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The entrance of the Sanctuary was twenty cubits high and ten cubits wide.** The doors within that entrance were six cubits high and three cubits thick.

The *daf* opens at the bottom of 3b. The *mishna* had presented a debate between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda concerning the *korah* — the beam placed across the opening of a *mavoi* — and the dimensions that render it valid. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that the beam may be no higher than 20 *amot* and that the opening may be no wider than 10 *amot*. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees, at least with respect to the height limit; whether he also disputes the width limit remains unclear from the *mishna* itself.

### Rav's Heichal Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: מִדְּרַבָּנַן מִפִּתְחוֹ שֶׁל הֵיכָל גָּמְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said:** The opinion of **the Rabbis is derived from the entrance of the Sanctuary.**

Rav's approach to understanding this debate is that it is learned from the *heichal*. A *baraita* invokes the dimensions of the *heichal*'s entrance as the comparative standard: the doorway of the *heichal* was 20 *amot* tall by 10 *amot* wide, and these proportions define the maximum parameters for what may be called a *petach* — a valid doorway. The *gemara* initially considered the possibility that Rabbi Yehuda derived his more expansive view from the *ulam*, but ultimately concluded that Rabbi Yehuda does not base himself on any structure of the *Beit HaMikdash* at all; in his view, there are simply no such dimensional limits.

### Doorframe Limitations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, לָא תַּיהֲנֵי לֵיהּ צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח, דְּהָא הֵיכַל צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח הָוְיָא לוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת הוּא דִּרְוִיח. אַלְּמָה תְּנַן: אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁרָחָב מֵעֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת — אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְמַעֵט!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another question: **But if so,** that the Rabbis derive their opinion from the entrance to the Sanctuary, **the form of a doorway,** i.e., two vertical posts on the two sides, with a horizontal cross beam spanning the space between them, **should not be effective** if the alleyway is more than ten cubits wide, **as the Sanctuary had the form of a doorway, and even so, it was** no more than **ten cubits wide. Why** then **did we learn** in the mishna: **If** the entrance **has the form of a doorway,** then **even if it is wider than ten cubits, he need not diminish** its width?

The *gemara* then raises a difficulty. The *mishna* implies that if the opening is wider than 10 *amot*, one may remedy this by constructing a *tzurat ha-petach* — a full doorframe consisting of two posts and a crossbeam — and the opening would then be valid despite its width. But if the entire framework is derived from the *heichal*, this cannot be correct: the *heichal* itself had a doorframe and was still only 10 *amot* wide. The presence of a doorframe did not permit the *heichal* to exceed its dimensions. The *gemara* therefore accepts the conclusion that according to Rav's approach, even with a full doorframe, the maximum permissible width remains 10 *amot*.

### Amaltera Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא תַּיהֲנֵי לֵיהּ אֲמַלְתְּרָא, דְּהָא הֵיכָל אֲמַלְתְּרָא הַוְיָא לֵיהּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי — עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה הוּא דְּגָבוֹהַּ, דִּתְנַן: חָמֵשׁ אֲמַלְתְּרָאוֹת שֶׁל מֵילָה הָיוּ עַל גַּבָּיו, זוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִזּוֹ, וְזוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִזּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **a** molded or protruding **cornice [*amaltera*]** crowning the entrance **should not be effective** in rendering an alleyway fit to carry within it if it is higher than twenty cubits, **as the Sanctuary had a cornice, and even so** it was **twenty cubits high,** and no more. **As we learned** in a mishna: **Over it were five oak cornices, one** protruding **above the other.**

Having established Rav's position, the *gemara* proceeds — on the very first line of 3a — to press a further difficulty. It states: *lo tehanei lei amaltera* — if the *heichal* is the controlling paradigm, then an *amaltera* should similarly confer no benefit. The *amaltera* is a kind of ornamental cornice or molding at the top of a doorframe, and we will shortly discover that if such a cornice is present, the *korah* is valid even if it is positioned higher than 20 *amot*. The *gemara*'s challenge is that this makes no sense on Rav's theory: the *heichal* itself had an *amaltera* — indeed, a *baraita* records that there were five tiers of such cornice work, one atop the other — and yet the *heichal*'s opening was still only 20 *amot* high. If those ornamental features did not permit the *heichal* to exceed 20 *amot*, why should an *amaltera* on a *mavoi* permit it to exceed that limit?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי מַאי תְּיוּבְתָּא? דִילְמָא כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא דַּאֲמַלְתְּרָאוֹת, בָּאוּלָם תַּנְיָא? וְהַאי מַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִילְמָא תַּבְנִית הֵיכַל כְּתַבְנִית אוּלָם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to dismiss this difficulty: **And,** with regard to **that** mishna, **what is the refutation? Perhaps when that** mishna **with regard to cornices was taught, it was taught with regard to the Entrance Hall,** whose height was forty cubits, and not with regard to the Sanctuary. The Gemara responds: **And what is the difficulty** with **that? Perhaps the design of the Sanctuary was like the design of the Entrance Hall.** Just as there were cornices in one doorway, there were cornices in the other.

The *gemara* initially deflects this challenge by suggesting that perhaps the *baraita* describing the cornice work refers to the *ulam* rather than the *heichal*. But the *gemara* immediately dismisses this escape route: it is perfectly reasonable to assume that the structure of the *heichal*'s entrance resembled that of the *ulam*, and so if the *ulam* had such cornice work, we may presume the *heichal* did as well. The difficulty stands.

## Amaltera Resolution

### Amaltera Source Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְּמָה אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא אָמַר רַב: רְחָבָה אַרְבָּעָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָהּ בְּרִיאָה. וְאִם יֵשׁ לָהּ אֲמַלְתְּרָא אֲפִילּוּ גְּבוֹהָה יוֹתֵר מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְמַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its question with regard to a cornice: **Why** then **did Rabbi Ile'a say** that **Rav** himself **said:** If the **width** of a cross beam is **four** handbreadths, **even if it is not sturdy,** it renders the alleyway fit for carrying within it. **And if it has a cornice, even if it is higher than twenty cubits, one need not diminish** its height.

With the *heichal*-based theory under pressure, the *gemara* shifts its analysis. To contextualize the discussion, the *gemara* cites the ruling of Rabbi Ila'a in the name of Rav: a *korah* that is four *tefachim* wide is valid even if it is not structurally strong enough to support a brick (*rechava arba af al pi she-eina bri'ah*); and if the *mavoi*'s opening features an *amaltera*, it is valid even if it exceeds 20 *amot* in height (*afilu gevoha yoter me-esrim ama eino tzarich le-ma'et*). It is on this second ruling that the *gemara* focuses. Rav Yosef proposes that the *amaltera* ruling is simply an unattributed *baraita* whose author is unknown, and therefore may not carry normative weight.

### Amaltera Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אֲמַלְתְּרָא מַתְנִיתָא הִיא, מַאן קָתָנֵי לַהּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְהָא חָמָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ קָתָנֵי לַהּ, וְתִיהְוֵי אֲמַלְתְּרָא מַתְנִיתָא וְתִיקְשֵׁי לְרַב!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** This *halakha* with regard to a **cornice** was not actually stated by Rav, but rather it **is a *baraita*. Who,** in fact, **teaches** that *baraita*? Perhaps it is not an authoritative *baraita*, and Rav does not have to accept what it says. **Abaye said: Isn't it Ḥama, son of Rabba bar Avuh, who teaches it? And** therefore, even if the *halakha* with regard to a **cornice will be a *baraita*, it** nevertheless **poses a difficulty to Rav.**

Abaye immediately objects. He points out that the *amaltera* ruling was in fact taught by Chama the son of Rabba bar Avuha — an authoritative transmitter — and therefore cannot be dismissed. Moreover, the *gemara* had originally framed this as a contradiction within Rav himself: it was Rav who stated that the framework is derived from the *heichal*, and it was also in Rav's name that the *amaltera* exemption was transmitted. The problem is therefore not merely academic — it is a direct internal contradiction in Rav's own position.

### Conflicting Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב: דַּל אֲנָא מֵהָכָא, מַתְנְיָיתָא מִי לָא קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי? אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר — תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, לְדִידִי נָמֵי תַּנָּאֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav** could have **said to you:** Even if you **eliminate me** and my explanation **from** the discussion **here, don't the** two ***baraitot*** themselves, the *baraita* that states that the Rabbis derive the dimensions of an entrance from the doorway of the Sanctuary and the *baraita* that states that in the case of a cornice, even if it is higher than twenty cubits, it need not be lowered, **contradict each other? Rather, what have you to say** to reconcile the contradiction? The matter is the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*; so too, according to my** opinion, it **is** the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*.**

Rav's response is to step back from the framing entirely. He says: *dal anu me-hacha* — set me aside. Even without invoking his personal position, the contradiction exists independently, because the *baraitot* themselves conflict with one another. One *baraita* grounds the height limit in the *heichal* (*ke-pitcho shel Heichal*), while another *baraita* — the *amaltera* ruling — permits exceeding 20 *amot* when a cornice is present, despite the fact that the *heichal* had such a cornice and remained at 20 *amot*. The tension, Rav argues, is in the sources themselves, not in his interpretation.

### Heker Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: בְּלָא רַב — מַתְנְיָיתָא אַהֲדָדֵי לָא קַשְׁיָין: לְרַבָּנַן קוֹרָה טַעְמָא מַאי — מִשּׁוּם הֶיכֵּרָא, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי ״יָתֵר מִפִּתְחוֹ שֶׁל הֵיכָל״ — סִימָנָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Without Rav, the *baraitot* do not contradict each other,** as **according to the Rabbis, what is the reason** that **a** cross **beam** renders an alleyway fit for carrying? Because it serves **as a conspicuous marker** between the alleyway and the public domain. Ordinarily a cross beam more than twenty cubits high is not noticeable; however, a cornice attracts attention even at that height. **And that which is taught** in the other *baraita* with regard to the height of a beam at the entrance to an alleyway: **Greater than the entrance of the Sanctuary,** is **merely** a **mnemonic** device. No actual *halakhot* are derived from the entrance of the Sanctuary.

The *gemara* then offers a resolution that reframes the entire discussion. According to the *rabanan*, the *korah* does not function as a symbolic doorway modeled after the *heichal*. Rather, its function is *mishum heikeira* — it is there to be noticed. The concern is practical: when someone's ball rolls out of a courtyard into the adjacent domain, or when a person walks out of a *reshut ha-yachid* into a *karmelit* or *reshut ha-rabim*, it may not be visually obvious where one domain ends and another begins. The *korah* or the *lechi* catches the eye at the threshold and alerts the person that they are crossing a boundary.

### Korah & Heker

On this theory, the logic of the 20-*amah* limit is entirely different. The *korah* must be low enough that a person's eye will actually register it as they pass beneath it. If it is higher than 20 *amot*, one simply does not notice it, and it fails its function. But if there is an ornamental *amaltera* — a distinctive, elaborate cornice — people look up specifically to see it; it draws attention to itself in a way that plain *sechach* or a simple beam does not. Hence, the presence of an *amaltera* restores the *korah*'s capacity to be noticed even at greater heights, and the 20-*amah* limit no longer applies. This has nothing to do with the *heichal*; the *heichal* reference in the other *baraita*, on this reading, is merely a mnemonic device for remembering the standard parameters, not the conceptual basis for the law.

## Sukkah Parallel

### Rabbah's Sukkah Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אִשְׁמַע מִרַבָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לְמַעַן יֵדְעוּ דוֹרוֹתֵיכֶם כִּי בַסֻּכּוֹת הוֹשַׁבְתִּי״, עַד עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁדָּר בַּסּוּכָּה, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה אֵין אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא שָׁלְטָא בֵּיהּ עֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, hear from Rabba,** who explains the reason, **as it is written: "In order that your generations should know that I made** the children of Israel **dwell in booths** when I brought them out of the land of Egypt; I am the Lord your God" (Leviticus 23:43). When the roofing of a *sukka* is **up to twenty cubits** high, **a person is aware that he is dwelling in a *sukka*;** however, when the roofing of the *sukka* is **above twenty cubits, a person is not aware** that he is dwelling in a *sukka*, **because the eye does not discern** the *sukka* roofing. One does not usually raise his head to look that high, and consequently, he sees the walls and does not notice the defining feature of the *sukka*, its roofing.

The *gemara* now turns to the parallel case in *masechet Sukkah*, where the first *mishna* likewise rules that a *sukkah* higher than 20 *amot* is invalid. Rabbah explains this with a *pasuk*: *lema'an yedu doroteichem ki ba-sukkot hoshavti* — so that your generations shall know that I caused the Israelites to dwell in *sukkot*. Rabbah derives from here that the *sukkah* must be something one is actually aware of dwelling in. Up to 20 *amot*, a person knows he is sitting beneath the *sechach* and receiving its shade; above 20 *amot*, his eye does not register the roof above him (*delo shalta bei eina*), and the experiential awareness required by the *mitzvah* is absent.

### Redundancy Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא גַּבֵּי סוּכָּה נָמֵי בְּהֶיכֵּרָא פְּלִיגִי, אִיפְּלוֹגֵי בְּתַרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty: **Apparently, with regard to *sukka* as well,** the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda **disagree** whether or not an item more than twenty cubits high is **conspicuous.** According to Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak, **why do I** need them to **disagree** about the same point **in two cases,** that of *sukka* and that of an alleyway? That the dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda with regard to *sukka* revolves around this issue indicates that their dispute with regard to an alleyway revolves around a different point, as asserted by Rav.

This raises an obvious structural question. The debate between the *rabanan* and Rabbi Yehuda — whether one can see and notice something above 20 *amot* — appears identically in both *sukkah* and *mavoi*. If both disputes rest on exactly the same principle of *heker* and visual recognition, why does the *gemara* need to record the argument in two separate contexts? This is not the world's most pressing question, but it is a genuine one.

### Different Contexts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גַּבֵּי סוּכָּה — בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה כֵּיוָן דְּלִישִׁיבָה עֲבִידָא, שָׁלְטָא בֵּיהּ עֵינָא. אֲבָל מָבוֹי, דִּלְהִילּוּךְ עֲבִיד — אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָא — בְּהָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן, אֲבָל בְּהָךְ אֵימָא מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **necessary** to teach both disputes, **as had** the mishna **taught us** only **with regard to *sukka*,** one might have thought that only **in this** case does **Rabbi Yehuda say** that an object is conspicuous even above twenty cubits; **since** a *sukka* **is designed for** extended **dwelling, the eye** undoubtedly **discerns** the roofing at some point. **However,** in the case of **an alleyway,** which **is designed for walking, say** that **he concedes to** the **Rabbis** that a person walking in an alleyway does not notice objects at so significant a height. **And had** the mishna **taught us** only **with regard to that** case of an alleyway, one might have thought that only **in that** case do **the Rabbis say** that people do not notice objects at so significant a height; **however, in that** case of *sukka*, **say** that **they concede to Rabbi Yehuda,** for the above-stated reason. Therefore, it is **necessary** to teach both disputes.

The *gemara* answers: *tzericha* — both are necessary. Had the debate been recorded only regarding *sukkah*, one might have thought that Rabbi Yehuda's leniency applies exclusively there because *sukkah* is a place one sits and dwells — *leyeshiva avida* — and therefore the eye has ample opportunity to register the *sechach* above, even at great height. But in a *mavoi*, where the entire purpose of the *korah* is to be noticed in the brief moment of walking out, one might have conceded that Rabbi Yehuda would agree with the *rabanan* — that above 20 *amot*, walking traffic simply will not catch it. Conversely, had only the *mavoi* case been taught, one might have thought the *rabanan*'s stringency applies only there, since one is moving quickly, but that they would agree with Rabbi Yehuda that a sitting person in a *sukkah* can be expected to notice even a high roof. Both cases are therefore required.

### Amaltera Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אֲמַלְתְּרָא? רַב חָמָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ אָמַר: קִינֵּי. כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אֲמַר, אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא: פִּסְקֵי דְאַרְזָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to arrive at a precise definition of *amaltera*, translated above as cornice. **What** is **an *amaltera*? Rav Ḥama, son of Rabba bar Avuh, said:** It refers to decorative wood carvings in the shape of **birds' nests. When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that **they say in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, it is referring to **cedar poles.**

The *gemara* pauses to define the *amaltera* more precisely. Rav Chama, son of Rabba bar Avuha, says the *amaltera* refers to *kini* — a kind of corner molding, similar to the ornamental stonework sometimes found at the upper corners of grand buildings. When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported that in the West they use a different term: *piski d'arza* — horizontal cedar molding, a decorative band that runs lengthwise along the top of a structure rather than at the corners. The *gemara* notes that the two opinions carry different implications. According to the one who says *piski d'arza* — the long horizontal molding — is sufficient, the more distinctive corner molding (*kini*) would certainly qualify as well. But according to the one who requires *kini* specifically, the horizontal molding alone would not be sufficient, as it is deemed less visually striking.

### Amaltera Noticeable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּאָמַר פִּסְקֵי דְאַרְזָא מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם דִּנְפִישׁ מְשָׁכֵיהּ. וְהָא סוּכָּה דִּנְפִישׁ מְשָׁכֵיהּ, וְקָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן דְּלָא! אֶלָּא: כֵּיוָן דְּקָא חֲשִׁיב אִית לֵיהּ קָלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the opinion of **the one who said cedar poles. What is** the **reason** for his opinion? He holds that **since its length is great,** a cedar pole attracts attention. **But isn't the length of a *sukka* great** as well, **and** nevertheless, **the Rabbis say that** a *sukka* higher than twenty cubits is **not** fit? **Rather,** the reason is as follows: **Since** a cedar pole **is** of **significant** value, **it** generates **publicity.** People passing through an alleyway stop and stare at a cross beam of that kind, even when it is higher than twenty cubits, leading others to do so as well.

This leads directly to the question raised in the course of the discussion: if the entire rationale is noticeability, why does ordinary *sechach* not qualify in the same way as *piski d'arza*? After all, *sechach* too extends across a horizontal span and covers a considerable length. The *gemara*'s answer is instructive: *keivan d'chashiv it lei kala* — since the cornice is prestigious, people talk about it. The *amaltera* is not merely visually present in the way *sechach* is; it is the subject of active attention and discussion. People are told to look up and admire it. This social salience is what guarantees that people will actually notice it, even from a great height. Plain *sechach*, however attractive, does not carry that kind of cultural prominence, and one cannot rely on it being noticed at 20 *amot* or beyond.

## Partial Height Issue

### Rava's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקְצָת קוֹרָה בְּתוֹךְ עֶשְׂרִים, וּמִקְצָת קוֹרָה לְמַעְלָה מֵעֶשְׂרִים, מִקְצָת סְכָךְ בְּתוֹךְ עֶשְׂרִים, וּמִקְצָת סְכָךְ לְמַעְלָה מֵעֶשְׂרִים? אָמַר רַבָּה: בְּמָבוֹי כָּשֵׁר, בְּסוּכָּה פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question: If **part of the** cross **beam** of an alleyway is **within twenty** cubits of the ground, **and part of the** cross **beam** is **above twenty** cubits, and similarly, if **part of the roofing** of a *sukka* is **within twenty** cubits, **and part of the roofing** is **above twenty** cubits, what is its legal status? **Rabba said: In** the case of **an alleyway, it is fit; in** the case of **a *sukka*, it is unfit.**

The *gemara* now raises a closely related question involving a case where a *korah* or *sechach* straddles the 20-*amah* boundary — part of it lies within 20 *amot* and part extends above. Is such an arrangement valid? Rava rules: the *mavoi* is *kasher* and the *sukkah* is *pasul*.

### Mavoi Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא בְּמָבוֹי דְּכָשֵׁר, דְּאָמְרִינַן קְלוֹשׁ? סוּכָּה נָמֵי לֵימָא קְלוֹשׁ! אִי קָלְשַׁתְּ — הָוְיָא לַהּ חֲמָתָהּ מְרוּבָּה מִצִּילָּתָהּ. הָכָא נָמֵי, אִי קָלְשַׁתְּ הָוְיָא לַהּ קוֹרָה הַנִּיטֶּלֶת בָּרוּחַ. אֶלָּא עַל כׇּרְחָךְ, נַעֲשׂוּ כְּשַׁפּוּדִין שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different in** the case of **an alleyway** that the ruling is **that it is fit?** It is because **we say: Thin** the part of the cross beam that is beyond twenty cubits, i.e., consider it as if it were not there. If so, in the case of ***sukka* too, say: Thin** the roofing that is beyond twenty cubits. **If you thin** the roofing beyond twenty cubits, **it will result in** a *sukka* **whose sun is more than its shade.** Were the section of the roofing above twenty cubits removed, the roofing that remained would not provide sufficient shade for the *sukka*. Here too, in the case of a cross beam, **if you thin** the part beyond twenty cubits, **it would become a** weak and unstable cross **beam that moves in the wind.** The Gemara rejects this argument: **Rather, perforce,** the status of the remaining parts of those cross beams **becomes like** that of **metal skewers,** which, although they are thin, are not removed by the wind.

Why is the *mavoi* valid? The *gemara* explains with the principle of *kalush* — thin it out. Conceptually, disregard the portion of the beam that lies above the 20-*amah* line. Consider only the part that falls within 20 *amot*. Is that remaining portion adequate on its own to constitute a valid *korah*? The *gemara* affirms that it is, and moreover, we treat that lower portion *ke-shipudin shel mateses* — as though it were made of metal — meaning we grant it all the physical properties it currently has by virtue of being part of the full beam: it is thick enough, solid enough, and stable enough to serve as a valid *korah*. Since the lower portion, so treated, meets all the requirements, the *mavoi* is valid.

### Sukkah Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי — עַל כׇּרְחָךְ נַעֲשֵׂית צִילָּתָהּ מְרוּבָּה מֵחַמָּתָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Here too,** in the case of a *sukka*, **perforce,** even after the upper roofing is removed, the status of the *sukka* **becomes like** that of a *sukka* **whose shade is more than its sun.**

The same logic of *kalush* cannot be applied in the *sukkah* case, because even if one mentally isolates the *sechach* that lies beneath the 20-*amah* line, the *gemara* points out that *ai kalsht hava lei chamasa meruba mi-tzilatah* — that portion alone would not provide sufficient shade. A *sukkah* requires that the *sechach* produce more shade than sunlight (*tzilatah merubah me-chamatah*), and the lower stratum of *sechach*, taken by itself, fails this test. The *gemara* then considers applying the same *ke-shipudin shel mateses* reasoning to the *sukkah*: treat the *sechach* as an integrated whole, note that in its totality it provides sufficient shade, and attribute all those properties to the portion below 20 *amot*. The *gemara* entertains this possibility but finds that the analogy does not hold symmetrically between the two cases.

### Memory Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא מִפַּרְזַקְיָא: סוּכָּה דִּלְיָחִיד הִיא לָא מִדְּכַר, מָבוֹי דִּלְרַבִּים מַדְכְּרִי אַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains Rabba's distinction differently. **Rava from Parzakya said: A *sukka*, which is** generally erected **for an individual,** if the portion of the roofing below twenty were removed and only the portion above twenty remained, **he** would **not be reminded** to lower the remaining roofing and would dwell in a *sukka* that is unfit. **An alleyway,** in contrast, **which is** used by **many** people, if the section of the cross beam below twenty cubits were removed, **they** would **remind each other** to remedy the situation.

Rava then offers a second, more technically oriented explanation for the distinction. A *sukkah* is typically used by an individual (*yachid*) or a small household; there is no ongoing community of people constantly present to notice if the status of the *sechach* changes. Specifically — and this is the concern — if the *sechach* beneath 20 *amot* were to wither and fall down, one would be left with only the portion above 20 *amot*, and having started in a mixed condition, the occupant would likely not notice. By contrast, a *mavoi* is a space frequented by many people (*rabbim*), who will remind one another if something changes: *midkarei ahadadi*. The *gemara* even cites a folk saying in illustration: a pot watched by a committee (*kadeira de-vei shutfei*) never gets fully hot or fully cold — shared responsibility leads to imperfect care — but the inverse concern applies here, where many eyes at least provide some guarantee of ongoing oversight.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: סוּכָּה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — אַחְמִירוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן, מָבוֹי דְּרַבָּנַן — לָא אַחְמִירוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina** offered a different explanation and **said:** The mitzva of ***sukka*,** which **is by Torah law, the Sages were stringent.** However, since the entire requirement to place a cross beam across the entrance to **an alleyway is by rabbinic law, the Sages were not stringent.**

Ravina offers a parallel but structurally opposite formulation. Since *sukkah* is a biblical obligation, the rabbis applied additional stringency even in cases that might technically qualify. Since *mavoi* is of rabbinic origin, the rabbis were lenient and did not add stringency beyond the basic requirement. The two explanations — Rava's and Ravina's — thus reach the same practical conclusion by different paths, and the *gemara* notes that a reverse version of the entire discussion is also attributed to Rava, yielding the opposite ruling: *pasul* for *mavoi* and *kasher* for *sukkah*, with correspondingly inverted reasoning for each distinction.

## Space vs. Object

### Challal Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲלַל סוּכָּה תְּנַן, חֲלַל מָבוֹי תְּנַן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא, תַּנְיָא דִּמְסַיַּיע לָךְ: מָבוֹי שֶׁהוּא גָּבוֹהַּ מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה יוֹתֵר מִפִּתְחוֹ שֶׁל הֵיכָל יְמַעֵט. וְהֵיכָל גּוּפוֹ חֲלָלוֹ עֶשְׂרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **that which** we **learned** in the mishna, with regard to the unfitness of a *sukka* higher than twenty cubits, is referring to the interior **space of the *sukka*;** and **that which** we **learned** in the mishna, that a cross beam spanning an alleyway that is more than twenty cubits must be lowered, is referring to the **space** at the entrance **of the alleyway** beneath the cross beam. **Rav Pappa said to Rava:** A *baraita* **was taught that supports your** opinion: If the cross beam spanning the entrance of **an alleyway is higher than twenty cubits** off the ground, **greater than the entrance of the Sanctuary, one must diminish its** height. **And the space of the** entrance of the **Sanctuary itself was twenty** cubits high, and its roof was higher than twenty cubits. Apparently, the twenty cubits mentioned with regard to a *sukka* and an alleyway refers to the space beneath the roofing and the cross beam.

The *gemara* now resolves a deeper conceptual question underlying the whole discussion. Rabbah bar Rav Huna had ruled that both cases — partial beam and partial *sechach* — are *pasul*, apparently because he understands the law to be a disqualification of the beam or *sechach* as an object: if any part of the *korah* or *sechach* is above 20 *amot*, then the *korah* or *sechach* itself is *pasul*. Rava disagrees, ruling both *kasher*, and explains his position with a single principle: *challal sukkah tnan, challal mavoi tnan* — the *mishna* speaks of the empty space (*challal*) of the *sukkah* and the *challal* of the *mavoi*. The law is not about where the beam or the *sechach* is positioned; it is about the height of the open space below the roof. If the cavity — the living space or the passable zone — does not exceed 20 *amot*, the requirement is satisfied, regardless of whether the object defining that ceiling begins precisely at the 20-*amah* line or extends somewhat above it.

### Heichal Precedent

The *baraita* cites precedent from the *heichal* itself.

### Lemata Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב שִׁימִי בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב פָּפָּא: כֵּיצַד הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה — מַנִּיחַ קוֹרָה מִשְּׂפַת עֶשְׂרִים וּלְמַטָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Rav Ashi raised an objection to Rav Pappa** from the *Tosefta*: **How** precisely **would he do it? He places the** cross **beam from the edge of twenty** cubits **and below.** Apparently, the entire cross beam must be within twenty cubits of the ground, and if any part of it rises above twenty it is unfit.

Rav Shimi bar Ashi challenges this from another *baraita*: *maniach korah mi-sfat esrim u-lemata* — when placing a beam on a *mavoi*, one places it from the edge of 20 and *below*. The plain reading of *lemata* seems to require that the beam itself be positioned below the 20-*amah* line, which would contradict Rava's *challal*-based principle.

### Lemata Reread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דִּלְמַטָּה כִּלְמַעְלָה: מָה לְמַעְלָה חֲלָלָהּ עֶשְׂרִים, אַף לְמַטָּה חֲלָלָהּ עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava explained that there is no need to emend the language of the *baraita*, but merely to reinterpret it. The *baraita* **is teaching us the following:** The *halakha* **below is like** the *halakha* **above. Just as above,** with regard to the maximum height of the cross beam, it is the **space** beneath the cross beam that may not be more than **twenty** cubits, **so** too **below,** with regard to the minimum height of the cross beam, it is the **space** beneath the cross beam that may not be less than **ten** handbreadths. However, a cross beam placed within ten handbreadths of the ground is unfit and does not render it permitted to carry within the alleyway.

The *gemara* resolves this by reinterpreting *lemata*. The *baraita* is not saying that the beam must be placed physically below the line of 20; rather, *lemata* here teaches an additional rule about the lower limit. Just as the upper limit is defined by the *challal* — the space may not exceed 20 *amot* — so too *lemata*, the lower limit: the *challal* must be at least 10 *tefachim* in height (as we will see is required for a *mavoi* to be valid at all). The word *lemata* is thus redirected to convey that both upper and lower limits are measurements of the *challal*, the open cavity, and not of the position of the beam itself. This is admittedly a forced reading of the language, but the *gemara* accepts it as the necessary interpretation in light of Rava's ruling.

## Amah Measurement

### Abaye's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: אַמַּת סוּכָּה וְאַמַּת מָבוֹי בְּאַמָּה בַּת חֲמִשָּׁה, אַמַּת כִּלְאַיִם בְּאַמָּה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara considers the measure of the cubit mentioned in the mishna and elsewhere. **Abaye said in the name of Rav Naḥman: The cubit** mentioned with regard to the *halakhot* **of *sukka* and the cubit** mentioned in connection with the *halakhot* **of an alleyway** is **a** small **cubit** consisting **of five** handbreadths. In contrast, **the cubit of** a forbidden mixture of **diverse kinds** of seeds is **a** large **cubit** consisting **of six** handbreadths. Apparently, Rav Naḥman rules stringently in all cases.

The *gemara* records a further dispute concerning the precise standard of measurement to be applied in these laws. Abaye in the name of Rav Nachman states: *amat sukkah ve-amat mavoi be-ama bat chamisha; amat kilayim be-ama bat shisha* — the *amah* used for measuring *sukkah* and *mavoi* is a five-*tefach amah*, while the *amah* used for *kilayim* is a six-*tefach amah*. This requires explanation. The standard *amah* is universally understood to be six *tefachim* (approximately a foot and a half, though opinions range up to two feet depending on the size of the *tefach*). Abaye is invoking an alternative, shorter *amah* of five *tefachim* and applying it selectively, on the principle of *le-chumra* — always measuring in whichever way produces the stricter result. When a measurement serves to *disqualify* — i.e., once a dimension reaches a certain threshold, the object is *pasul* — one uses the shorter *amah*, because with a smaller unit of measure, the disqualifying threshold is reached sooner. When a measurement serves to *qualify* — i.e., the object must reach a minimum size in order to be valid — one uses the longer *amah*, because with a larger unit of measure, the qualifying threshold is harder to attain.

### Mavoi Measurements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּת מָבוֹי בְּאַמָּה בַּת חֲמִשָּׁה, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? לְגוֹבְהוֹ וּלְפִירְצַת מָבוֹי. וְהָא אִיכָּא מֶשֶׁךְ מָבוֹי בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, דִּלְקוּלָּא! כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר בְּאַרְבָּעָה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **The cubit of an alleyway is a cubit** consisting **of five** handbreadths. **With regard to what *halakha*** does this ruling apply? It applies **to** the issue of the height of the cross beam spanning an alleyway that may not be more than twenty cubits high, **and to the breach of an alleyway** that may not be more than ten cubits wide. The Gemara poses a question: **But isn't there** also the issue of **the** minimal **length of an alleyway?** For carrying in an alleyway to be rendered permissible by means of a side post or a cross beam, it must be at least **four cubits** long. In that case, measuring the alleyway with small cubits will lead **to a leniency.** The Gemara resolves this difficulty: Rav Naḥman holds **in accordance with the one who said** that the length of an alleyway need only be **four handbreadths.** However, a cubit mentioned in the context of an alleyway is always a small cubit, which is a stringency.

Applied to *mavoi*: measuring the height (too tall is *pasul*) and measuring a breach in the wall (too wide is *pasul*) are both disqualifying measurements, so the short five-*tefach amah* is used. The *gemara* raises a complication: a *mavoi* must also be at least four *amot* in length in order to qualify for the leniency of the *korah* — and there the *amah* functions to *validate*, which should require the longer measure. The *gemara* resolves this in two ways. First, one opinion holds that the length requirement is actually only four *tefachim*, not four *amot*, so no *amah*-based qualifying measurement exists for *mavoi*. Alternatively, even if one holds that four *amot* of length is required, Abaye's principle was stated about *rov amot* — most of the measurements of *mavoi*. Since the majority of *amah*-based measurements in the laws of *mavoi* serve to disqualify, Abaye's rule calibrates accordingly, with the understanding that the exceptional qualifying measurement (the length) would use the longer stick.

### Sukkah Measurements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּת סוּכָּה בְּאַמָּה בַּת חֲמִשָּׁה, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? לְגוֹבְהָהּ וּלְדוֹפֶן עֲקוּמָּה. וְהָא אִיכָּא מֶשֶׁךְ סוּכָּה בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, דִּלְקוּלָּא! דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אוֹמֵר אֲנִי, כׇּל סוּכָּה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת פְּסוּלָה! כְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ אֵינָהּ מַחְזֶקֶת אֶלָּא רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ וְשׁוּלְחָנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman said that **the cubit of a *sukka*** is **a** small **cubit** consisting **of five** handbreadths. The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** does this ruling apply? It applies **to** the *halakha* governing **its height,** i.e., that a *sukka* may not be more than twenty cubits high, **and to the** *halakha* of **a curved wall.** A *sukka* is considered valid if there are up to four cubits of invalid roofing, provided that this roofing is adjacent to one of the walls of the *sukka*. In that case, the invalid roofing is considered an extension of the wall, i.e., the wall is considered to be curved, and consequently, the entire *sukka* is valid. With regard to both *halakhot*, the ruling is stringent and distance is measured with small cubits. The Gemara asks: **But isn't there** also the *halakha* of **the** minimal **length of a *sukka*,** which must be at least **four cubits** long? If it is measured with small cubits, **that** will lead **to a leniency. As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: I say, any *sukka* that does not** have **in it** an area of at least **four cubits by four cubits is invalid.** The Gemara resolves this difficulty: Rav Naḥman made his statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say:** A *sukka* is valid **even if it holds only one's head, most of his** body, **and his table.**

The same framework applies to *sukkah*. Height exceeding 20 *amot* disqualifies, and *dofen akuma* — the principle by which up to four *amot* of invalid *sechach* adjacent to a wall is treated as a continuation of the wall — also disqualifies when the distance exceeds four *amot*: both use the short *amah*. The *gemara* notes that a *sukkah* must also be at least four *amot* by four *amot* in area to qualify (according to Rebbi), which would seem to require the longer *amah* for a qualifying measurement. The *gemara* resolves this by invoking the opinion of the *rabanan*, who require only that the *sukkah* accommodate one's head, most of one's body, and one's table — approximately seven *tefachim* — and therefore no *amah*-based qualifying measurement arises for *sukkah* at all. Alternatively, even following Rebbi, the principle of *rov amot* applies: since most measurements of *sukkah* serve to disqualify, the short *amah* is the default, and the exceptional qualifying case uses the longer one as needed.

### Kilayim Measurements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּת כִּלְאַיִם בְּאַמָּה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה, לְמַאי הִילְכְתָא? לְקָרַחַת הַכֶּרֶם וְלִמְחוֹל הַכֶּרֶם. דִּתְנַן: קָרַחַת הַכֶּרֶם, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמּוֹת. וּמְחוֹל הַכֶּרֶם, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמּוֹת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman said that **the cubit of a** forbidden mixture of **diverse kinds** of seeds **is a cubit** consisting **of six handbreadths.** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** does this ruling apply? It applies **to** the *halakha* of **a clearing in a vineyard and to** the *halakha* of **the perimeter of a vineyard.** **As we learned** in a mishna in tractate *Kilayim*: With regard to **a clearing in a vineyard, Beit Shammai say:** Its measure is **twenty-four cubits, and Beit Hillel say: Sixteen cubits.** With regard to **the perimeter of a vineyard, Beit Shammai say: Sixteen cubits, and Beit Hillel say: Twelve cubits.**

For *kilayim*, the situation is inverted. The primary measurements — the *karachat ha-kerem* (a bare patch in a vineyard) and the *machol ha-kerem* (the unplanted border around a vineyard) — are measured in order to determine whether there is enough space to permit planting another species. These are qualifying measurements: only if the space is sufficiently large is planting permitted. Therefore, the long six-*tefach amah* is used, making it harder to reach the required minimum and thus more difficult to be lenient.

### Vineyard Compression

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיכָּא רְצוּפִים בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, דִּלְקוּלָּא! דִּתְנַן: כֶּרֶם הַנָּטוּעַ עַל פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ כֶּרֶם. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כֶּרֶם, וְרוֹאִין אֶת הָאֶמְצָעִיִּים כְּאִילּוּ אֵינָם. כְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי הָוֵי כֶּרֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But isn't there** also the *halakha* of vines that planted **consecutively, within four cubits** of each other, with regard to **which** measuring the distance with large cubits would lead **to a leniency? As we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **a vineyard that was planted** in consecutive rows **with less than four cubits** between them, **Rabbi Shimon says:** Since the rows are planted so closely together, **it is not** considered **a vineyard,** and if one plants other crops there he is not liable. **And the Rabbis say:** It is **a vineyard, and one regards the middle** vines, those planted between two appropriately spaced rows, **as if they are not** there. One who plants other crops there is indeed liable. According to Rabbi Shimon's opinion, measuring the distance between the rows with large cubits leads to leniency. The Gemara resolves the difficulty: Rav Naḥman made his statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** that a densely planted vineyard **is a vineyard.**

The *gemara* raises a counterexample from within *kilayim* itself. Rabbi Shimon rules that a vineyard planted too densely — with less than four *amot* between the rows — loses its status as a vineyard (*kerem*) altogether, paradoxically permitting other seeds to be planted in its vicinity. In this case, using the longer *amah* would work as a leniency, because a larger unit of measure would more readily characterize the spacing as less than four *amot*, thereby triggering Rabbi Shimon's ruling that it is not a *kerem* and allowing planting. One might therefore expect the short *amah* to be used here (to be strict and call it a *kerem*). But the *chachamim* reject Rabbi Shimon's position entirely, holding that a too-densely planted vineyard retains its *kerem* status; therefore, no situation within *kilayim* (as the *halacha* is ruled) ever requires the short *amah* for leniency. Alternatively, even following Rabbi Shimon, the *rov amot* principle again applies: since the great majority of *kilayim* measurements are qualifying ones, the long *amah* is the operative standard, with the compressed-vineyard case as an outlier.

### Rava Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: כׇּל אַמּוֹת בְּאַמָּה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה, אֶלָּא: הַלָּלוּ שׂוֹחֲקוֹת, וְהַלָּלוּ עֲצֵבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The above was based on the ruling of Rav Naḥman according to Abaye. **But Rava said in the name of Rav Naḥman: All cubits** mentioned in measurements by the Sages consisted of a large **cubit of six** handbreadths. **However, these,** the cubits mentioned with regard to diverse kinds of seeds, **are** measured with **expansive** handbreadths, with the fingers spread apart, **whereas those,** the cubits mentioned with regard to an alleyway and a *sukka*, **are** measured with **compressed** handbreadths, with the fingers held together.

Rava, citing Rav Nachman, rejects Abaye's entire framework. Rava holds that all *amot* everywhere follow the standard six-*tefach amah* — there is no five-*tefach amah* to be selectively applied. What Rav Nachman actually meant when he spoke of measuring strictly in both directions is not that one switches between two different standard sizes, but rather that within the single standard of six *tefachim*, the actual physical measurement can vary slightly depending on how the *tefach* is measured: a *tefach* can be measured with relaxed fingers (*sochakot*), yielding a somewhat larger measure, or with tightly pressed fingers (*atzeivot*), yielding a somewhat smaller one. When measuring to disqualify, one uses the looser, larger measure so that the disqualifying threshold is reached sooner; when measuring to qualify, one uses the tighter, smaller measure, so that the qualifying threshold is harder to attain. The conceptual difference between Abaye and Rava is therefore not about which historical *amah* standard applies, but about the nature of the variation: Abaye posits two discrete historical standards (five and six *tefachim*), while Rava posits a single standard whose practical application admits of minor variation in either direction. The *gemara* notes that Rava's position will require qualification in certain *Beit HaMikdash*-related contexts — where a five-*tefach amah* does appear — but as a general rule, the operative standard throughout *halacha* is the six-*tefach amah*.
