# Berakhot 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Night Shema Deadline

### V'lo Zu Bilvad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא זוּ בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא כָּל מִי שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים עַד חֲצוֹת — מִצְוָתוֹ עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר.
>
> **Translation:** **And that is not only** with regard to the mitzva of reciting *Shema*. **Rather, wherever the Sages said** that a mitzva may be performed **until midnight,** the mitzva may actually be performed **until dawn.**

The Mishnah's phrase *v'lo zu bilvad amru* — "and they did not say only this" — signals that the principle under discussion is not an isolated ruling but part of a broader framework in which the Rabbanan established *chatzos* as the operative deadline for various nighttime obligations. The Gemara now turns to examine precisely what Rabban Gamliel is clarifying when he invokes this framework.

### Gamliel vs. Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מִי קָאָמַר ״עַד חֲצוֹת״ דְּקָתָנֵי ״וְלֹא זוֹ בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ״? הָכִי קָאָמַר לְהוּ רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לִבְנֵיהּ: אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּקָאָמְרִי ״עַד חֲצוֹת״, מִצְוָתָהּ עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר. וְהַאי דְּקָא אָמְרִי עַד חֲצוֹת — כְּדֵי לְהַרְחִיק אָדָם מִן הָעֲבֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Does Rabban Gamliel say until midnight, that he teaches: And not only did they say?** Rather, **this is what Rabban Gamliel said to his sons: Even according to the Rabbis, who say** that the mitzva may be performed only **until midnight,** the biblical obligation to perform **the mitzva** continues **until dawn, and that which they say** that it may only be recited **until midnight** is **in order to distance a person from transgression.**

The Gemara identifies an ambiguity in the Mishnah: *rabban gamliel mi ka'amar ad chatzos dekatani v'lo zu bilvad* — is Rabban Gamliel clarifying his own position, walking back an implicit earlier suggestion that *chatzos* is an absolute limit, or is he rather explaining what the Rabbanan meant when they set *chatzos* as the deadline? The Gemara initially entertains the possibility that Rabban Gamliel himself had originally implied *chatzos* and is now qualifying that statement.

### Chatzot as Safeguard


The Gemara resolves this: *hachi ka'amar lehu Rabban Gamliel l'vanav* — this is what Rabban Gamliel was saying to his sons. Even the Rabbanan, who say *ad chatzos*, agree in principle that *mitzvatah ad sheya'aleh amud hashachar* — the mitzvah extends until *amud hashachar*. The reason they declared *chatzos* as the deadline is *kedei l'harchik adam min ha'aveira* — as a safeguard, a rabbinic fence to prevent a person from becoming complacent and missing the obligation entirely. Rabban Gamliel himself does not appear to treat *chatzos* as a meaningful threshold at all — ideally one says *shema* at the first opportunity, but the entire night remains valid.

### Post-Chatzot Prohibition?


This raises a pointed question: if the Rabbanan's motivation is purely protective, is there any real practical difference between them and Rabban Gamliel? The *talmidei rabbeinu yonah* take a more stringent view, maintaining that the Rabbanan would in fact prohibit reciting *shema* — or at least its attendant *brachos* — after *chatzos*, even though the underlying biblical mitzvah goes the whole night. According to this reading, the Rabbanan erected *chatzos* as a genuine halachic barrier in order to reinforce the safeguard, even at the cost of preventing fulfillment of the biblical obligation in the later part of the night.

### Psak: Whole Night Valid


The practical *psak* follows Rabban Gamliel: if one missed *chatzos*, one may still recite *shema* for the remainder of the night. At the same time, everyone agrees — including Rabban Gamliel — with the teaching of the earlier *braita* that one should say *shema* as early as possible upon returning home, before becoming occupied with other matters and risking falling asleep. The whole night is valid, but the ideal is to fulfill the obligation without delay.

## Korban Pesach Omission

### Hekter Chalavim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים וְאָכִילַת קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, מִצְוָתָן עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר.
>
> **Translation:** **The burning of sacrificial fats and the eating of sacrifices of lesser sanctity,** their mitzva extends **until dawn.**

The Mishnah applies the same *chatzos*-as-safeguard principle to *hekter chalavim* — the burning of sacrificial fats on the altar — and to the eating of *korbanos*, both of which are permitted the entire night but for which the Rabbanan declared *chatzos* as the recommended deadline. The same logic governs all of these: the biblical time extends until *amud hashachar*, and *chatzos* functions solely as a precautionary measure.

### Pesach Not Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ אֲכִילַת פְּסָחִים לָא קָתָנֵי. וּרְמִינְהִי: קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע עַרְבִית, וְהַלֵּל בְּלֵילֵי פְּסָחִים, וַאֲכִילַת פֶּסַח, מִצְוָתָן עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: In our mishna, **the eating of the Paschal lamb was not taught** among those mitzvot that may be performed until dawn, indicating that the mitzva of eating the Paschal lamb does not extend until dawn. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** to this conclusion based on a *baraita*: **The mitzvot of the recitation of the evening *Shema,*** the recitation of ***hallel* on the nights of Passover** accompanying the sacrifice of the Paschal lamb, **as well as eating the Paschal lamb,** may all be performed **until dawn.**

The Gemara raises an obvious question: *v'ilu b'achilas psachim lo katani* — the Mishnah lists eating *korbanos* in general but conspicuously omits *korban Pesach* from this category. Since we know that there is debate about when eating the *pesach* ends, the omission is meaningful. The implication of its exclusion appears to be that the *chatzos* limit for *korban Pesach* is not merely a rabbinic safeguard but something more fundamental — a biblical limitation. This is what the Gemara now proceeds to explore. *tosfos* in *pesachim* adds that *korban Pesach* cannot be included in the general category of sacrifices eaten "a day and a night" since *pesach* is eaten only at night, not during the day at all, providing an additional reason for its separate treatment.

### Leil Pesach Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע עַרְבִית, וְהַלֵּל בְּלֵילֵי פְּסָחִים, וַאֲכִילַת פֶּסַח, מִצְוָתָן עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **The mitzvot of the recitation of the evening *Shema,*** the recitation of ***hallel* on the nights of Passover** accompanying the sacrifice of the Paschal lamb, **as well as eating the Paschal lamb,** may all be performed **until dawn.**

A *braita* teaches that *krias Shema arvis*, *hallel b'leilei psachim*, and *achilas Pesach* all share the same principle: *mitzvatan ad sheya'aleh amud hashachar* — they may be performed until *amud hashachar*. The recitation of *hallel* on *leil Pesach*, which we do not ordinarily say at night, is understood as accompanying the eating of the *korban Pesach*, and since the *pesach* extends the whole night, so too does the *hallel*. This *braita* thus places *achilas Pesach* squarely in the category of all-night obligations — which raises the question of why it was not included in the Mishnah's list.

### Elazar vs. Akiva Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, הָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: This is not difficult** as these two sources reflect two conflicting opinions. **This,** our mishna, **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. While this,** the *baraita*, **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.**

Rav Yosef resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. The *braita* that places *achilas Pesach* among all-night obligations and the Mishnah that excludes it reflect a *machloket tannaim*. The Mishnah follows the position of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, and the *braita* follows Rabbi Akiva.

### Gezeirah Shavah Chatzot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְאָכְלוּ אֶת הַבָּשָׂר בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן: ״וְעָבַרְתִּי בְאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״, מַה לְּהַלָּן עַד חֲצוֹת, אַף כָּאן עַד חֲצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse discussing the mitzva to eat the Paschal lamb: **"And they shall eat of the meat on that night;** roasted over fire and *matzot* with bitter herbs shall they eat it" (Exodus 12:8); **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says: Here it is stated: "On that night,"** from which we cannot determine when night ends. The same expression is encountered later in the same chapter: **"And I will pass through the land of Egypt on that night** and I will strike every firstborn in the land of Egypt, from person to animal" (Exodus 12:12). We know when the firstborns were struck down based on the verse "Thus said the Lord: At about midnight, I will go out into the midst of Egypt and every firstborn in Egypt shall die" (Exodus 11:4–5). Therefore, **just as** in the verse **below,** the striking of the firstborns took place **until midnight,** as stated explicitly in the verse, **so too** in the verse **here,** the mitzva to eat the Paschal lamb continues **until midnight.**

The *braita* records the dispute explicitly. The Torah says *v'achlu es habasar balaila hazeh* — you shall eat the meat of the *pesach* "on this night." Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah applies a *gezeirah shavah*: the phrase *balaila hazeh* appears here and also in *ve'avarti be'eretz Mitzrayim balaila hazeh* — God's declaration that He would pass through Egypt on that same night. Just as the event referenced in the latter verse — *makas bechoros* — occurred at *chatzos*, so too the eating of the *pesach* is limited to *chatzos*. According to Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, this is not a rabbinic safeguard but a biblical limit derived through the *gezeirah shavah*. This explains why the Mishnah does not classify *korban Pesach* alongside other things limited to *chatzos* as a mere precaution — for Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, the *chatzos* limit is the actual biblical *din*.

This debate has direct practical implications for the *seder* night: the *afikomen*, which represents the *korban Pesach*, becomes subject to the question of whether one must finish it by *chatzos* — and how that squares with the practice of *mesaper biyetzias Mitzrayim kol oso halailah*, recounting the Exodus throughout the night.

### Akiva: Whole Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״בְּחִפָּזוֹן״, עַד שְׁעַת חִפָּזוֹן. אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּלַּיְלָה״? יָכוֹל יְהֵא נֶאֱכָל כְּקָדָשִׁים בַּיּוֹם תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּלַּיְלָה״, בַּלַּיְלָה הוּא נֶאֱכָל, וְלֹא בַּיּוֹם. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה דְּאִית לֵיהּ גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, אִצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב לֵיהּ ״הַזֶּה״. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הַאי ״הַזֶּה״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? לְמַעוֹטֵי לַיְלָה אַחֵר הוּא דַּאֲתָא, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּפֶסַח קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, וּשְׁלָמִים קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, מָה שְׁלָמִים נֶאֱכָלִין לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד — אַף פֶּסַח נֶאֱכָל שְׁתֵּי לֵילוֹת בִּמְקוֹם שְׁנֵי יָמִים, וִיהֵא נֶאֱכָל לִשְׁנֵי לֵילוֹת וְיוֹם אֶחָד. קָמַשְׁמַע לָן ״בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״, בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה הוּא נֶאֱכָל וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל בְּלַיְלָה אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: Was it not already said,** "Thus you shall eat it, with your loins girded, your shoes on your feet, your staffs in your hands and you will eat it **in haste** for it is the Paschal offering for the Lord" (Exodus 12:11)? Therefore the Paschal lamb may be eaten **until the time of haste.** Since the time of haste is when Israel left Egypt, and it is said, "You will not leave, every man from his house, until the morning," then the Paschal lamb may be eaten until dawn. **If that is so, why does the verse state: On** that **night?** The Gemara explains that the phrase on that night is necessary because without it I **might** have thought that the Paschal lamb **is eaten during the day, like** all other **sacrifices,** which must all be slaughtered and eaten during the day. Therefore, **the verse states: On** that **night,** to underscore that this particular sacrifice **is eaten at night and not during the day.** Essentially, the difference between these two opinions revolves around which word they deemed significant. **Granted, according to Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, who has** the tradition of **a verbal analogy** between the phrase, on that night, with regard to the eating of the Paschal lamb and the phrase, on that night, with regard to the striking of the firstborn in Egypt, **it was necessary** for the verse **to write "that"** in order to indicate that these times are parallel. **However, according to Rabbi Akiva,** who has no such tradition, **what does he do with "that"?** Why is it necessary to emphasize on that night? On that night **comes to exclude another night,** as one might otherwise have concluded that the Paschal lamb may be eaten for two nights. **It would have entered your mind to say: Since the Paschal lamb** falls into the category of **sacrifices of lesser sanctity, and peace-offerings are** also **sacrifices of lesser sanctity, just as peace-offerings may be eaten for two days and one night,** i.e., the day that they are sacrificed through the following day, as we learned in the Torah, **so too the Paschal lamb may be eaten for two nights instead of two days.** In other words, one might otherwise mistakenly conclude from its parallel to peace-offerings, that the Paschal lamb **is to be eaten for two nights and the day** between them. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** specifically **on that night,** i.e., **on that night it is eaten, and it is not eaten on another night.**

Rabbi Akiva responds: *v'lo kvar ne'emar b'chipazon* — does the Torah not say you shall eat it *b'chipazon*, in haste? The *sha'as chipazon* — the moment of haste — refers to the morning, when Yisrael was being driven out. Therefore, one has the entire night to eat the *pesach*. If so, what is the purpose of the word *balaila hazeh*, "on this night"? The Gemara explains: *yachol ye'achlu kekodshim bayom* — one might have thought that like other *korbanos*, if the *pesach* was slaughtered during the day, it could begin to be eaten in the day. The Torah therefore specifies *balaila* — at night, and not by day. The *korban Pesach* is a uniquely structured *korban* in that it is offered in the afternoon but may only be eaten beginning at night.

### Two-Night Hava Amina


The Gemara asks why the additional word *hazeh* — "this" night — is needed even according to Rabbi Akiva, since it is already established that eating occurs at night. The Gemara proposes a *hava amina*: *ho'il u'Pesach ve'kodshim kalim v'Shelamim kodshim kalim* — since *pesach* and *shelamim* are both *kodshim kalim*, eaten outside the *beis hamikdash* by ordinary Israelites, one might reason by analogy: just as *shelamim* are eaten for two days and the intervening night, so too *pesach*, since it begins at night, should be the mirror image — eaten for two nights and the intervening day. Rashi notes, however, that even within this *hava amina*, the day itself would not be a time of eating *pesach*, given its deep association with the nighttime *ge'ulah*; the point is only that perhaps one would not be *pasul* in waiting and finishing on the second night. The Torah therefore writes *balaila hazeh* — "this night" — to exclude any second night: *balaila hazeh hu ne'echal v'eino ne'echal b'laila acher*.

### Boker Rishon Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה מִ״לֹּא תוֹתִירוּ עַד בֹּקֶר״ נָפְקָא. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא — אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא מַאי ״בֹּקֶר״ — בֹּקֶר שֵׁנִי. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר לָךְ: כָּל ״בֹּקֶר״ — בֹּקֶר רִאשׁוֹן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, from where does **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya** derive that the Paschal lamb cannot be eaten for two nights? Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya **derives** this conclusion **from** the verse: **"It should not remain until the morning"** (Exodus 12:10). If one is prohibited from leaving over any part of the sacrifice until the morning, he is certainly prohibited from leaving it over until the following night. Therefore, it is unnecessary to cite an additional source to teach that the Paschal lamb may only be eaten on the first night. **And** why does **Rabbi Akiva** require "that" to derive that the Paschal lamb may not be eaten on the second night? According to Rabbi Akiva, **if** it was derived **from** the verse: "It should not remain until the morning," **I would have said: What** is the meaning of **morning?** It means the **second morning,** as the Torah does not specify until which morning the Paschal lamb may not be left; until the first morning or the second morning. Therefore, the Torah needed to write on that night and no other. **And** what would **Rabbi Elazar** ben Azarya respond? He **could have said to you:** If it is not otherwise noted, **every** unmodified mention of the word **morning** in the Bible **refers to the first,** i.e., the next, **morning.** If that were not the case, no biblical text could have any definite meaning.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, for his part, derives the exclusion of a second night from the verse *lo sosiru ad boker* — do not leave it until morning — which clearly precludes waiting until the following night to finish it. Rabbi Akiva counters that this verse alone would be ambiguous: *hava amina mai boker* — one might have thought that *boker* means the second morning, the morning after the second night, and thus a second night of eating would still be permitted. Rabbi Elazar responds: *kol boker boker rishon hu* — whenever the Torah says "morning" it means the immediate next morning.

### Why Pesach Excluded


The Gemara's resolution is now clear. According to Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, *korban Pesach* is excluded from the Mishnah's list because its *chatzos* limit is biblical, not rabbinic — it cannot be placed in the category of things limited to *chatzos* merely as a safeguard. According to Rabbi Akiva, *pesach* is not in the Mishnah's list because, in his view, the *braita* he authored allows the whole night without any rabbinic safeguard cutoff — the framework of *chatzos* as a protective measure simply does not apply to it.

## Elazar/Yehoshua on Pesach

### Hanei Tannai Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי כְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי. דְּתַנְיָא, ״שָׁם תִּזְבַּח אֶת הַפֶּסַח בָּעָרֶב כְּבוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ מוֹעֵד צֵאתְךָ מִמִּצְרָיִם״. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״בָּעֶרֶב״ אַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ, וּ״כְבוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״ אַתָּה אוֹכֵל, וּ״מוֹעֵד צֵאתְךָ מִמִּצְרָיִם״ אַתָּה שׂוֹרֵף. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר ״בָּעֶרֶב״ אַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ ״כְּבוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״ אַתָּה אוֹכֵל וְעַד מָתַי אַתָּה אוֹכֵל וְהוֹלֵךְ — עַד ״מוֹעֵד צֵאתְךָ מִמִּצְרָיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning the tannaitic dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya regarding until when the Paschal lamb may be eaten, the Gemara remarks: The dispute between **these *tanna'im* is parallel to** the dispute between **those *tanna'im*,** who disagree over the same issue. **As it was taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"There you will offer the Paschal lamb, in the evening when the sun sets at the time when you left the land of Egypt"** (Deuteronomy 16:6). Upon closer examination, it seems that this verse mentions three distinct times: In the evening, refers to the afternoon until sunset; when the sun sets, refers to the time of sunset itself; and the time when you left the land of Egypt refers to, as explained in Exodus, the early hours of the morning. Therefore it seems that these times parallel the different stages of the mitzva of the Paschal lamb, and it is regarding these details that the *tanna'im* disagree. **Rabbi Eliezer says: In the evening,** the afternoon, **you slaughter** the sacrifice, from **when the sun sets** until midnight **you eat it, and at the time when you left the land of Egypt you burn** what remains from the sacrifice, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. **Rabbi Yehoshua says: In the evening,** the afternoon, **you slaughter** the sacrifice, from **when the sun sets, you eat it. And until when do you continue eating? Until the time when you left the land of Egypt,** meaning until morning, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva.

The Gemara notes that this dispute is paralleled by another *machloket tannaim*: *ve-hanei tannai ke-hanei tannai* — these Tannaim correspond to those Tannaim. The *braita* derives from the verse that mentions three distinct temporal references in connection with the *pesach*: *ba-erev* (evening, before sunset), *ke-vo ha-shemesh* (at the setting of the sun), and *mo'ed tzeitcha mi-Mitzrayim* (the appointed time of your departure from Egypt, i.e., daybreak). The question is how to distribute these three times across the activities of slaughtering, eating, and burning.

### Rav Elazar: Three Times


Rabbi Elazar reads the three temporal markers discretely: *ba-erev ata zoveach* — you slaughter in the late afternoon, before sunset; *ke-vo ha-shemesh ata ochel* — once the sun has set you may begin eating; and *mo'ed tzeitcha mi-Mitzrayim ata soref* — the time of the Exodus, meaning daybreak, is when the obligation to burn the remains arrives. The implication is that the period of eating is bounded — it ends before daybreak — which parallels the position of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah that the eating terminates at *chatzos*, with the period afterward leading to the morning burning.

### Yehoshua: Whole Night


Rabbi Yehoshua presents a harmonious reading in which the eating continues uninterrupted: *ba-erev ata zoveach*, *ke-vo ha-shemesh ata ochel*, and then *ve-holekh ad mo'ed tzeitcha mi-Mitzrayim* — you keep eating all the way until the time of your departure from Egypt, i.e., until daybreak. This explicitly places *achilas Pesach* as an all-night activity, corresponding to the position of Rabbi Akiva. The distinction between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yehoshua in this *braita* thus maps precisely onto the earlier dispute between Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah and Rabbi Akiva.

## Ge'ulah at Night

### Ge'ulah Began at Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים כְּשֶׁנִּגְאֲלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִמִּצְרַיִם, לֹא נִגְאֲלוּ אֶלָּא בָּעֶרֶב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹצִיאֲךָ ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ מִמִּצְרַיִם לָיְלָה״, וּכְשֶׁיָּצְאוּ — לֹא יָצְאוּ אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִמָּחֳרַת הַפֶּסַח יָצְאוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּיָד רָמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said: Everyone agrees that when** the children of **Israel were redeemed from Egypt** were given permission to leave, **they were redeemed only in the evening, as it is stated:** "In the spring **the Lord, your God, took you out from Egypt at night"** (Deuteronomy 16:1). **And when they** actually **left, they left only during the day, as it is stated:** "On the fifteenth of the first month, **on the day after the offering of the** Paschal lamb, the children of Israel went out with a high hand** before the eyes of Egypt" (Numbers 33:3), indicating that they actually went out during the day.

Rabbi Abba introduces a clarifying principle: *ha-kol modin keshe-nig'alu Yisrael mi-Mitzrayim lo nig'alu ela ba-erev* — everyone agrees that the redemption of Israel from Egypt was initiated in the evening. The verse itself states *hotzi'acha Hashem Elokecha mi-Mitzrayim lailah* — God took you out from Egypt at night. At the same time, the actual departure — the physical leaving — occurred during the day, as the verse says *mimachorat ha-Pesach yatze'u bnei Yisrael be-yad ramah* — the day after the *pesach*, Israel went out with an outstretched hand.

### Sha'at Chipazon Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ — עַל שְׁעַת חִפָּזוֹן, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה סָבַר: מַאי ״חִפָּזוֹן״ — חִפָּזוֹן דְּמִצְרַיִם. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: מַאי ״חִפָּזוֹן״ — חִפָּזוֹן דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** However, **with regard to what did they disagree?** They disagreed **with regard to the time of haste,** as it is written: "You will eat it in haste for it is the Paschal offering for the Lord" (Exodus 12:11). **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya held: What is** the meaning of **haste?** It is **the haste of the Egyptians** at midnight, as they hurried to the houses of the people of Israel to send them away, in fear of the plague of the firstborn. **And Rabbi Akiva held: What is** the meaning of **haste?** It is **the haste of Israel** in the morning, as they rushed to leave Egypt.

What then is the subject of the dispute between Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah and Rabbi Akiva? Rabbi Abba explains: *al sha'at chipazon* — they disagree about how to understand the *sha'at chipazon*, the moment of haste referenced in the Torah's command to eat the *pesach b'chipazon*. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah holds that *chipazon de-Mitzrayim* is the relevant reference — the haste of Egypt, when the Egyptians rose up at midnight to drive Israel out. By this reading, the time of the *pesach* extends until *chatzos*, the moment of Egypt's haste. Rabbi Akiva holds that *chipazon de-Yisrael* is the operative moment — the haste of Israel itself, when they were physically running out in the morning. By this reading, one may eat the *pesach* for the entire night.

### Lailah as Ge'ulah Start

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״הוֹצִיאֲךָ ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ מִמִּצְרַיִם לָיְלָה״: וְכִי בַּלַּיְלָה יָצְאוּ? וַהֲלֹא לֹא יָצְאוּ אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִמָּחֳרַת הַפֶּסַח יָצְאוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּיָד רָמָה״! אֶלָּא, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהִתְחִילָה לָהֶם גְּאוּלָּה מִבָּעֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** A *braita* confirms and elaborates: **Did they actually leave at night? We know they did not. Rather,** it **teaches that the redemption began for them in the evening.**

A *braita* confirms and elaborates: *hotzi'acha Hashem Lailah* — did they actually leave at night? We know they did not. Rather, *me-eit she-hitchila lahem ge'ulah me-erev* — from the time the redemption began for them in the evening. The verse attributes the leaving to the night because that is when the process of *ge'ulah* commenced.

### Tzitzit at Night


This principle is directly relevant to a question that will arise later in *maseches Brachos*: whether the third *parsha* of *shema* — parashat *tzitzit*, which includes the memory of *yetzias Mitzrayim* — must be recited at night. The Mishnah records the position *lo zachiti she-tei'amer *yetzias mitzrayim* ba-leilos* — it was not clear that one is obligated to recall *yetzias Mitzrayim* at night, since the actual *yetziah* occurred during the day. The *braita* here responds to that concern: the night is not incidental to the *ge'ulah*; it is when the *ge'ulah* began. It would be a peculiar irony if the entire *seder* and *achilas korban Pesach* were conducted at night and one were to conclude that *ge'ulah* was only a daytime event. The night was unquestionably the beginning of the *ge'ulah* — the only dispute concerns the technical question of *sha'at chipazon* and how late the *pesach* may be eaten.

### Aggadeta Opens Shas


It is worth pausing to note that the first *perek* of *brachos* contains a remarkable quantity of *aggadeta* — and this is not coincidental. The *yerushalmi* similarly opens with extensive *aggadic* material. It appears that from very early on, the beginning of *maseches Brachos* was understood as an occasion to range broadly through narrative, theology, and *midrash*. Many learners instinctively feel the pull to move through the *aggadeta* and arrive at the *halachic* analysis — yet the Gemara quite deliberately chooses to open *shas* with sustained *aggadic* investment. This may say something important about how *halachic* discussion is meant to be framed — always within larger religious and theological terms.

## Egypt Aggadeta

### Daber Na — Please

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דַּבֶּר נָא בְּאָזְנֵי הָעָם״ וְגוֹ׳. אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: אֵין ״נָא״ אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן בַּקָּשָׁה, אָמַר לֵיהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: בְּבַקָּשָׁה מִמְּךָ, לֵךְ וֶאֱמוֹר לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: בְּבַקָּשָׁה מִכֶּם, שַׁאֲלוּ מִמִּצְרַיִם כְּלֵי כֶסֶף וּכְלֵי זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that when God instructs Moshe *daber na be'oznei ha'am* — "please speak in the ears of the people" — the word *na* is a term of supplication. The school of **Rabbi Yannai** teaches: ***na* is nothing more than an expression of supplication.** God was, as it were, entreating Moshe: please go and ask Israel to **borrow** gold and silver vessels from the Egyptians.

The Gemara notes that when God instructs Moshe *daber na be'oznei ha'am* — "please speak in the ears of the people" — the word *na* is a term of supplication. The school of Rabbi Yannai teaches: *ein na ela lashon bakasha* — *na* means please. God was, as it were, entreating Moshe: please go and ask Israel to borrow gold and silver vessels from the Egyptians. The reason for this divine urgency was to fulfill the prophecy made at the *bris bein habesarim*: *va-avadum ve-inu osam kiyam bahem* — God had fulfilled the prediction of enslavement and affliction — but *ve-acharei chen yetzu birchush gadol* — they would afterward depart with great wealth. If Israel left without taking wealth from Egypt, Avraham's prophecy would remain partially unfulfilled.

### Prisoner Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וּלְוַאי שֶׁנֵּצֵא בְּעַצְמֵנוּ! מָשָׁל: לְאָדָם שֶׁהָיָה חָבוּשׁ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִים וְהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים לוֹ בְּנֵי אָדָם: מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתְךָ לְמָחָר מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין, וְנוֹתְנִין לְךָ מָמוֹן הַרְבֵּה, וְאוֹמֵר לָהֶם: בְּבַקָּשָׁה מִכֶּם, הוֹצִיאוּנִי הַיּוֹם, וְאֵינִי מְבַקֵּשׁ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** When Moshe conveyed this to Israel, the response was: **If only we could get ourselves out; do not worry about the gold and silver, just take us out.** The Gemara offers a parable: a man imprisoned in a dungeon is told that tomorrow he will be freed and given much money. He replies — please, just take me out today; I want nothing else.

When Moshe conveyed this to Israel, the response was: *u-levai she-neitzi be-atzmeinu* — if only we could get ourselves out; do not worry about the gold and silver, just take us out. The Gemara offers a parable: a man imprisoned in a dungeon is told that tomorrow he will be freed and given much money. He replies — please, just take me out today; I want nothing else. The parable captures the psychology of slaves whose entire being is oriented toward freedom alone, with no mental space for material concerns. There is, in a sense, a *shevach l'Yisrael* embedded in this — the Jewish people had to be urged and encouraged to take the wealth; their only desire was redemption.

### Al Korcham Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּשְׁאִלוּם״, אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהִשְׁאִילוּם בְּעַל כָּרְחָם. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי בְּעַל כָּרְחָם דְּמִצְרַיִם, וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי בְּעַל כָּרְחָם דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the spoils taken from Egypt described in the verse: "And the Lord gave the nation grace in the eyes of Egypt, **and they gave them what they requested** and they emptied Egypt" (Exodus 12:36), **Rabbi Ami said: This teaches that** the Egyptians **gave them what they requested against their will.** There is a dispute with regard to the question: Against whose will? **Some say** it was given **against the will of the Egyptians, and some say** it was given **against the will of Israel.**

The Torah uses the *hif'il* form *va-yash'ilum* — they caused them to lend — indicating that the borrowing was imposed against someone's will. Rabbi Ami teaches: *melamed she-hish'ilum al korcham* — it was against their will. Against whose will? Two opinions are offered. According to one view, it was against the will of Egypt — *al korcham de-Mitzrayim* — the Egyptians were compelled by force or circumstance. The verse *ve-navas bayis techalek shalal* supports this: those who had dwelt in the houses were now watching the booty be divided. According to the other view, it was against the will of Israel — *al korcham de-Yisrael* — because Israel did not want the burden of carrying it. They were focused entirely on leaving quickly and had no interest in being encumbered with silver and gold.

### Va-yenatzlu Mitzrayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְנַצְּלוּ אֶת מִצְרָיִם״. אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעֲשָׂאוּהָ כִּמְצוּדָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דָּגָן, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר, עֲשָׂאוּהָ כִּמְצוּלָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דָּגִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the continuation of the verse: **And they emptied Egypt, Rabbi Ami said:** This indicates that **they made** Egypt **like a trap in which there is no grain** that serves as bait to attract birds. **Reish Lakish said: They made** Egypt **like an abyss** in the sea **without fish.**

The verse says *va-yenatzlu es Mitzrayim* — they emptied Egypt. Rabbi Ami explains: *melamed she-asuha kimetzuda she-ein ba dagan* — they made Egypt like a trap that has no grain in it. Rashi explains the image: grain was thrown out to attract birds; once all the grain is removed, no birds will come. The point is that Egypt was so thoroughly stripped of everything valuable that nothing remained to draw anyone there. Reish Lakish offers a slightly different formulation: they made it *kimetzuda she-ein bah dagin* — like a pond that has no fish. The fish — the things of value — have all been removed. Both images convey total depletion, though the first emphasizes that nothing remains to attract others, while the second emphasizes that everything of worth was already taken out.

### Ehyeh Asher Ehyeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶהְיֶה״, אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה, לֵךְ אֱמוֹר לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: אֲנִי הָיִיתִי עִמָּכֶם בְּשִׁעְבּוּד זֶה, וַאֲנִי אֶהְיֶה עִמָּכֶם בְּשִׁעְבּוּד מַלְכֻיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** When Moses asked God what to say when Israel asks him God's name, God said: **"I will be that I will be,"** meaning **I was with you in this enslavement,** and in this redemption, **and I will be with you in the enslavement of the kingdoms** in the future.

The Gemara turns to the divine name *ehyeh asher ehyeh*, revealed to Moshe at the burning bush. The Gemara interprets it as God telling Moshe: *ani hayiti imachem b'shibud zeh v'ani eheyeh imachem b'shibud malchuyot* — I was with you in this exile, and I will be with you in the future exiles of the other kingdoms. The name encodes both past presence and future accompaniment in suffering.

### Dayah L'tzarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, דַּיָּה לַצָּרָה בִּשְׁעָתָהּ. אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: לֵךְ אֱמוֹר לָהֶם ״אֶהְיֶה שְׁלָחַנִי אֲלֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** Moshe **replied to God: Master of the Universe, the affliction is enough at its own time.** Do not burden Israel now with the knowledge of future exiles; the present suffering is sufficient without adding awareness of what is yet to come. God concedes the point and tells Moshe: go say to them simply **"I Am has sent me to you."**

Moshe responds to God: *ribbono shel olam, dayah l'tzarah b'sha'atah* — Master of the Universe, the affliction is enough at its own time. Do not burden Israel now with the knowledge of future exiles; the present suffering is sufficient without adding awareness of what is yet to come. God concedes the point and tells Moshe: go say to them simply *ehyeh shelachani aleichem* — "I Am" has sent me to you. The full double formulation of future exiles need not be communicated at this moment. *dayah l'tzarah b'sha'atah* stands as a memorable principle in its own right: when a person is in the midst of one difficulty, this is not the moment to enumerate the others.

### Eliyahu Aneini

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עֲנֵנִי ה׳ עֲנֵנִי״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: לָמָּה אָמַר אֵלִיָּהוּ עֲנֵנִי שְׁתֵּי פְּעָמִים? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאָמַר אֵלִיָּהוּ לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, ״עֲנֵנִי״ — שֶׁתֵּרֵד אֵשׁ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם וְתֹאכַל כָּל אֲשֶׁר עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וַ״עֲנֵנִי״ — שֶׁתַּסִּיחַ דַּעְתָּם, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ מַעֲשֵׂה כְשָׁפִים הֵם. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה הֲסִבֹּתָ אֶת לִבָּם אֲחוֹרַנִּית״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **"Answer me, God, answer me,** that this people will know that You are the Lord, God, and You have turned their hearts backward" (I Kings 18:37). This repetition **teaches that Elijah said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, answer me that fire will descend from heaven and consume everything that is on the altar, and answer me that You will divert their mind** from devising alternative explanations for what they witnessed so **that they will not say that they were acts of sorcery. As it is stated** that Elijah said: **"And You have turned their hearts backward,"** God can restore them to the proper path as well.

The Gemara transitions to the episode of Eliyahu on Mount Carmel, where he cries *aneini Hashem aneini* — "answer me God, answer me" — twice. Rabbi Abbahu explains: the two requests were distinct. The first *aneini* was that fire descend from heaven and consume everything on the altar. The second *aneini* was that God distract the hearts of the assembled people — *shetasiach da'atam* — so that they would not claim that what they witnessed was the work of witchcraft. This is a striking observation: proving a negative is impossible. Anyone determined to attribute a miracle to sorcery will find a way to do so. Eliyahu therefore asked not for evidence that would refute the accusation, but that the people be so overwhelmed by the experience that the thought would not even occur to them. The verse *va-atah hasibota et libam achoranit* — You caused their hearts to turn backward — is read as God's response to this second request.

## Morning Shema & Vatikin

### Misheyakir to Hanetz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מֵאֵימָתַי קוֹרִין אֶת שְׁמַע בְּשַׁחֲרִית? מִשֶּׁיַּכִּיר בֵּין תְּכֵלֶת לְלָבָן. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בֵּין תְּכֵלֶת לְכָרָתֵי. וְגוֹמְרָהּ, עַד הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר עַד שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁעוֹת, שֶׁכֵּן דֶּרֶךְ מְלָכִים לַעֲמוֹד בְּשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **From when does one recite *Shema* in the morning?** **From** when a person **can distinguish between sky-blue [*tekhelet*] and white.** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** From when one can distinguish **between sky-blue and leek-green.** **And** one must **finish** reciting *Shema* **until** the end of the period when you rise, i.e., **sunrise,** when the sun begins to shine. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** One may recite the morning *Shema* **until three hours** of the day, which this is still considered when you rise, **as that is the habit of kings to rise** from their sleep **at three hours** of the day.

The second Mishnah in the chapter asks: *me'imatai korin et Shema b'shacharit* — from when do we recite *shema* in the morning? The answer: *misheyakir bein techelet l'lavan* — from the time one can distinguish between the blue (*techelet*) and the white (*lavan*). Rabbi Eliezer extends this to distinguishing between *techelet* and *karti*, a greenish color. The Mishnah then gives the endpoint of the morning *shema*: *ad hanetz hachama* — until the rising of the sun. It is worth noting that the correct pronunciation is *hanetz*, not *netz* — the *hei* is part of the word itself, not a prefixed definite article. Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees with the cutoff, extending it until three hours into the day, since *kach derech melachim la'amod b'shalosh sha'os* — it is the custom of kings to rise at three hours. One who recites *shema* after either of these endpoints, according to the Mishnah, *lo hifsid k'adam hakorei batorah* — has not lost out entirely but is as one who reads passages of Torah, without fulfilling the specific mitzvah.

This temporal structure parallels the first opinion in the night Mishnah, which set a cutoff based on when people typically retire, here operating in reverse based on when people typically rise. According to the first opinion, the window of morning *shema* closes very early — by sunrise — reflecting the working person's day which begins at dawn. Rabbi Yehoshua's extension to three hours acknowledges that even among those who rise later, the *shema* period can be extended.

### Techelet vs. Lavan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי בֵּין תְּכֵלֶת לְלָבָן? אִילֵּימָא בֵּין גְּבָבָא דְעַמְרָא חִיוָּרָא לִגְבָבָא דְעַמְרָא דִתְכֵלְתָּא — הָא בְּלֵילְיָא נָמֵי מִידָּע יָדְעִי. אֶלָּא, בֵּין תְּכֵלֶת שֶׁבָּהּ לְלָבָן שֶׁבָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna stated that the time for the recitation of the morning *Shema* begins when one can distinguish between sky-blue and white. The Gemara asks: To **what** is **between sky-blue and white** referring? **If you say** that it means distinguishing between **a pile of white wool and a pile of sky-blue wool, wouldn't one know** the difference **at night, as well? Rather,** it must be a reference to ritual fringes made with sky-blue strings (see Numbers 15:38) along with white strings, and one must be able to distinguish **between the sky-blue** strings in the ritual fringes **and the white** strings **in** the ritual fringes.

The Gemara asks: *mai bein techelet l'lavan* — what exactly does distinguishing between *techelet* and *lavan* mean? If it means distinguishing between a pile of blue-dyed wool and a pile of white wool — *bein gvava de'amra chivara legvava de'amra tichleta* — this could be done even at night; there is enough visual contrast between clearly different colors even in dim light. Rather, the Gemara explains: *ela bein techelet shebo l'lavan shebo* — between the *techelet* threads in the *tzitzit* itself and the *lavan* threads of the same *tzitzit*. Because the blue and white strings are wound in close alternation around each other in the *krichot*, the sections between the knots, they are directly juxtaposed. In low light, the whole ensemble blurs together and appears uniformly colored. Only with sufficient daylight can one perceive that a blue string runs immediately alongside a white string. This is therefore a more demanding light threshold, requiring the light of morning to be meaningfully advanced.

### Misheyakir Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיַּכִּיר בֵּין זְאֵב לְכֶלֶב. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: בֵּין חֲמוֹר לְעָרוֹד. וַאֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: מִשֶּׁיִּרְאֶה אֶת חֲבֵרוֹ רָחוֹק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וְיַכִּירֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir says** that the day begins when **one can distinguish** a wolf from a dog; in those times, domesticated dogs were not so distinct in appearance from wolves, making this a demanding standard of visibility. **Rabbi Akiva** provides a different sign, and **says that the** day begins when there is sufficient light to distinguish **between a donkey and a wild donkey.** **And *Aḥerim* say: When one can see another** person, who is merely an acquaintance (Jerusalem Talmud) from **a distance of four cubits and recognize him.**

A *braita* records additional Tannaitic formulations of the *misheyakir* threshold. Rabbi Meir says: *misheyakir bein ze'ev l'kelev* — from when one can distinguish a wolf from a dog; in those times, domesticated dogs were not so distinct in appearance from wolves, making this a demanding standard of visibility. Rabbi Akiva says: *bein chamor l'arod* — from when one can distinguish a domesticated donkey from a wild donkey. Others (*acherim*) say: *misheyireh et chaveiro rachok arba amot v'yakirenu* — from when one can recognize a friend at a distance of four *amos*, approximately six feet. The *yerushalmi* wryly observes that a close friend can be recognized from much farther, while an acquaintance might not be recognized even face to face — and so the standard must refer to someone one knows in passing, neither intimately nor barely at all.

### Huna & Abaye Rulings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הֲלָכָה כַּאֲחֵרִים. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לִתְפִילִּין כַּאֲחֵרִים. לִקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע כְּוָתִיקִין. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וָתִיקִין הָיוּ גּוֹמְרִין אוֹתָהּ עִם הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: The *halakha* is in accordance with *Aḥerim*. Abaye said:** Regarding the time from which one may don **phylacteries,** a mitzva incumbent only by day, the *halakha* **is in accordance with *Aḥerim*.** But with regard to **the recitation of** *Shema*, one should conduct himself **in accordance with** the custom of **the *vatikin*,** pious individuals who were scrupulous in their performance of mitzvot. As **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The *vatikin* would conclude** the recitation of *Shema* **with sunrise,** and one should act accordingly.

Rav Huna rules: *halacha k'Acherim* — the *halacha* follows the opinion of *acherim*, the four-*amos* recognition standard. Abaye then draws a distinction between two applications of *misheyakir*: *l'tefillin k'Acherim* — for the obligation of *tefillin*, the threshold is *misheyakir* as Rav Huna rules. But *l'krias Shema k'Vatikin* — for *krias Shema*, the guiding standard is that of the *vatikin*, who would finish *shema* precisely at *hanetz hachama*. This is derived from the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan: *vatikin hayu gomrim osah im hanetz hachama* — the *vatikin* would finish *shema* with the rising of the sun.

### Zmanim Today

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: וָתִיקִין הָיוּ גּוֹמְרִין אוֹתָהּ עִם הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּסְמוֹךְ גְּאוּלָּה לִתְפִלָּה, וְנִמְצָא מִתְפַּלֵּל בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also taught** in a *baraita*: **The *vatikin* would conclude** the recitation of *Shema* **with sunrise in order to juxtapose** the blessing of **redemption,** which immediately follows the recitation of *Shema,* **with prayer, and pray during the day.**

To illustrate the concrete significance of these temporal categories: *alot hashachar* is conventionally reckoned as seventy-two minutes before sunrise. *misheyakir* falls approximately forty-five minutes before sunrise according to one opinion, or at eleven degrees before sunrise by another, yielding a range of roughly forty-five minutes to an hour before *hanetz*. As a practical example — sunrise at 6:52 AM would place *misheyakir* at approximately 6:07 or 5:58, with *alot* at 5:40.

The halachic significance of each threshold is distinct. *alot hashachar* marks the basic beginning of the halachic day — *mitzvos* can technically be performed from this point, but the ideal for most daytime obligations is to wait for *hanetz hachama*. *misheyakir* is the relevant threshold for *tefillin* and for the beginning of morning *shema* according to the Mishnah. *hanetz hachama* is the ideal time for *shema*, *shemoneh esrei*, and the practice of *vatikin*.

### Vatikin Practice


The *vatikin* — described as those especially scrupulous in the performance of *mitzvos*, or possibly as the ancient ones — would calibrate their morning prayer precisely so as to finish *shema* with the emerging sun. The reason: *k'dei sheyisomach ge'ulah l'tefillah v'nimtza mispalel ba-yom* — so that they would juxtapose *ge'ulah* to *tefillah* and find themselves praying during the day. The structure of the morning liturgy creates a tension: *shema* belongs to the period of rising, ideally before *hanetz*, while *shemoneh esrei* ideally begins in the full day, after *hanetz*. The only way to satisfy both requirements — *shema* within its proper time and *shemoneh esrei* in the day — while also maintaining *semichas ge'ulah l'tefillah*, is to finish *shema* at the precise last moment before sunrise and transition immediately into *shemoneh esrei* as the sun rises. This is the *vatikin* practice.

### Yira'ucha Im Shemesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: מַאי קְרָאָה — ״יִירָאוּךָ עִם שָׁמֶשׁ וְלִפְנֵי יָרֵחַ דּוֹר דּוֹרִים״.
>
> **Translation:** Regarding this custom of the *vatikin*, **Rabbi Zeira said: What verse** is the source for this tradition? **"They shall fear You with the sun, and before the moon for all generations"** (Psalms 72:5).

Rabbi Zeira cites the supporting verse: *yira'ucha im shemesh v'lifnei yarei'ach dor dorim* — they shall fear You with the sun and before the moon for all generations. Rashi reads "the fear of You" as referring to *krias Shema* — *kabbalas ol malchus shamayim* — and associates it specifically with the sun, meaning *shema* is said as the sun rises. Others read the verse as referring to *shemoneh esrei* with the sun — emphasizing not merely the technical logic that constrains us to daven at sunrise, but the inherent spiritual power of prayer at that moment. There is something in the experience of the sun emerging that corresponds to encountering God's majesty and the act of turning toward the Creator. The *yerushalmi* elsewhere connects the three daily prayer times to the three stages of the sun's movement — rising, noon, and setting — suggesting that responding to the sun's rhythms is not incidental but intrinsic to the meaning of *tefillah*. According to this view, the *vatikin* practice captures not only an optimal halachic timing but a deeper theological relationship between prayer and the natural order.

### Kahal Kadosh Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵעִיד רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן אֶלְיָקִים מִשּׁוּם קְהָלָא קַדִּישָׁא דְבִירוּשָׁלַיִם: כָּל הַסּוֹמֵךְ גְּאוּלָּה לִתְפִלָּה אֵינוֹ נִזּוֹק כָּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei ben Elyakim testified in the name of the holy community in Jerusalem,** a title accorded a particular group of Sages who lived there, that **one who juxtaposes redemption and prayer** at sunrise **will incur no harm for the entire day.**

The Gemara records in the name of the holy congregation of Jerusalem: *kol ha-somech ge'ulah l'tefillah eino nizak kol ha-yom kulo* — whoever juxtaposes *ge'ulah* to *tefillah* will not be harmed for the entire day. *tosfos* notes that this cannot refer to simply saying *shema* followed by *shemoneh esrei*, since everyone does that; it must refer specifically to performing this juxtaposition in the *vatikin* manner — *shema* ending immediately before sunrise and *shemoneh esrei* beginning precisely at sunrise. The protection afforded is tied to the precise and dedicated execution of this practice.

### Zeira's Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אִינִי?! וְהָא אֲנָא סְמַכִי וְאִיתְּזַקִי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּמַאי אִיתְּזַקְתְּ — דְּאַמְטֵיִית אָסָא לְבֵי מַלְכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: Is that so? Didn't I juxtapose** redemption and prayer **and** nevertheless **I was harmed?** Rabbi Yosei ben Elyakim **asked** Rabbi Zeira: **How were you harmed? That you brought a myrtle branch to the king's palace?**

Rav Zeira tested the principle personally: *va'ana samchi v'itziki* — I did that and yet I was harmed. When pressed as to what harm he experienced, he reported: *d'amtiyis asa l'vei malka* — they summoned him to bring myrtle to the king's house. But his interlocutor responds: *hatam nami d'ba'ei lach l'meiv agra l'mechezei apei malka* — that was not harm; under ordinary circumstances you would have had to pay for the privilege of appearing before the king. The appearance before the king was a reward, not a punishment. The Gemara adds the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan: *le'olam yishtadel adam larutz likrat malchei Yisrael v'lo likrat malchei Yisrael bilvad ella afilu likrat malchei akum* — a person should always make the effort to appear before kings, not only Jewish kings but even non-Jewish kings, *she'im yizkeh yavchin bein malchei Yisrael l'malchei akum* — so that if one merits to see the kings of Israel in the messianic era, the contrast with the nature of non-Jewish kingship will be fully appreciated.

### Rabbi Bruna's Joy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אִלָּעָא לְעוּלָּא: כִּי עָיְלַתְּ לְהָתָם שְׁאֵיל בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּרַב בְּרוֹנָא אֲחִי בְּמַעֲמַד כָּל הַחֲבוּרָה, דְּאָדָם גָּדוֹל הוּא וְשָׂמֵחַ בְּמִצְווֹת. זִימְנָא חֲדָא סְמַךְ גְּאוּלָּה לִתְפִלָּה וְלָא פְּסֵיק חוּכָא מִפּוּמֵּיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ יוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi El'a said to Ulla** before Ulla left for Babylonia: **When you go to** Babylonia, **ask after my brother, Rav Beruna, in the presence of the entire group, as he is a great man who rejoices in mitzvot.** The Gemara provides proof that he was indeed a great man who rejoiced in mitzvot: **Once,** Rav Beruna **juxtaposed redemption and prayer** at sunrise, as per the custom of the *vatikin*, **and laughter** and joy **did not cease from his mouth for the entire day.**

Rabbi Illa asked Ulla, upon the latter's journey to Eretz Yisrael, to inquire after the welfare of Rabbi Bruna in the presence of the full community — a sign of significant public honor — because *adam gadol hu v'sameach b'mitzvot* — he is a great person who rejoices in *mitzvos*. The occasion of that praise: *zimna chada samach ge'ulah l'tefillah v'lo pasik chucha mipumei kol hayoma* — one day Rabbi Bruna managed to properly juxtapose *ge'ulah* to *tefillah*, and the smile did not leave his face the entire day. The *vatikin* practice is thus not merely a technical scheduling preference but an occasion for deep rejoicing — the sense that one has seized the full opportunity of the mitzvah in its ideal form.

### Sefatai Tiftach Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי מָצֵי סָמֵיךְ? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּתְּחִלָּה הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״ה׳ שְׂפָתַי תִּפְתָּח״, וּלְבַסּוֹף הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״יִהְיוּ לְרָצוֹן אִמְרֵי פִי וְגוֹ׳״! אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: תְּהֵא בִּתְפִלָּה שֶׁל עַרְבִית.
>
> **Translation:** In practice, the Gemara asks: **How is one able to juxtapose redemption and prayer? Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: At the beginning** of prayer, **one says: "Lord, open my lips,** that my mouth may declare Your glory" (Psalms 51:17), **and at the end** of prayer one says: **"May the words of my mouth** and the meditation of my heart **be acceptable** before You, Lord, my Rock, and my Redeemer" (Psalms 19:15). If so, the first verse is an interruption between redemption and prayer. **Rabbi Elazar said: Let this** verse, "Lord, open my lips," be recited only **in the evening prayer** but not in the morning prayer.

The Gemara asks a fundamental difficulty: *heichi mati samach* — how can ge'ulah and *tefillah* truly be juxtaposed? Rabbi Yochanan teaches that at the beginning of *shemoneh esrei* one says *hashem sefatai tiftach* — "God, open my lips" — and at the end one says *yihyu l'ratzon imrei fi*. If *hashem sefatai tiftach* intervenes between *ga'al Yisrael* and the body of *shemoneh esrei*, is the juxtaposition not broken?

Rabbi Elazar responds that the problem of *hashem sefatai tiftach* applies to *tefillas arvit* — the evening prayer — but not to *shacharis*, where the imperative of *semichas ge'ulah l'tefillah* is most fully operative. Rabbi Yochanan himself adds that whoever juxtaposes *ge'ulah shel arvit l'tefillah shel arvit* merits the world to come — underscoring that even at night the concept applies, though with less force than at *shacharis*. Rav Ashi resolves the problem comprehensively: *de-keivan de-kavu'a rabbanan b'tefillah, ke-tefillah arichta damya* — since the Rabbanan established *hashem sefatai tiftach* as a fixed part of the prayer, it is treated as an extension of *shemoneh esrei* itself, not an interruption.

### Hashkivenu Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵיזֶהוּ בֶּן הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא — זֶהוּ הַסּוֹמֵךְ גְּאוּלָּה שֶׁל עַרְבִית לִתְפִלָּה שֶׁל עַרְבִית! רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא אַכּוּלְּהוּ, וְכֵיוָן דְּקַבְעוּהָ רַבָּנַן בִּתְפִלָּה — כִּתְפִלָּה אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמְיָא. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, עַרְבִית הֵיכִי מָצֵי סָמֵיךְ, וְהָא בָּעֵי לְמֵימַר ״הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ״! אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן דְּתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ — כִּגְאוּלָּה אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: Who is worthy of** a place in **the World-to-Come? He who juxtaposes redemption of the evening prayer to the evening prayer.** Therefore, this verse from Psalms should not be recited before the evening prayer either. **Rav Ashi said** another explanation: **Even if you say** that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that "Lord, open my lips" is recited before **all** prayers, including the morning and the evening prayers. **Since the Sages instituted** this verse, **it is considered as an extended prayer;** it is an inseparable part of the prayers, and neither constitutes a problematic interruption. Rabbi Ashi supports his claim: **As if you do not say so, how does one juxtapose** redemption of the evening prayer to **the evening prayer? Mustn't one recite: Help us lie down [*hashkivenu*]** after redemption? **Rather, since the Sages instituted** the recitation of: **Help us lie down, it is considered as an extended blessing of redemption.**

The same logic that resolves the *hashem sefatai tiftach* problem had already been applied earlier in the Gemara — in the opposite direction — to *hashkivenu*. There the question was: how can one say *hashkivenu* after *ga'al Yisrael* at night without violating *semichas ge'ulah l'tefillah*? The answer given was: *keivan de-takinu rabbanan Hashkivenu, ki-ge'ulah arichta damya* — since the Rabbanan established *hashkivenu* as fixed liturgy, it functions as an extended *ge'ulah*, not an interruption between *ge'ulah* and *tefillah*. The result is a striking structural symmetry: when resolving the *hashkivenu* question, the Gemara proved it from the model of *hashem sefatai tiftach*; here, when resolving the *hashem sefatai tiftach* question, the same principle is invoked independently. Both are established liturgy, both are extensions — one a *ge'ulah arichta*, the other a *tefillah arichta* — and neither constitutes a problematic interruption.

### Yihyu L'ratzon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי, הַאי ״יִהְיוּ לְרָצוֹן אִמְרֵי פִי״ מַשְׁמַע לִבְסוֹף, וּמַשְׁמַע מֵעִיקָּרָא דְּבָעֵינָא לְמֵימַר. מַאי טַעְמָא תַּקְּנוּהוּ רַבָּנַן לְאַחַר שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה בְּרָכוֹת? לֵימְרוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא! אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי: הוֹאִיל וְלֹא אֲמָרוֹ דָּוִד אֶלָּא לְאַחַר שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה פָּרָשִׁיּוֹת, לְפִיכָךְ תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן לְאַחַר שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה בְּרָכוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse with which the prayer concludes, the Gemara deliberates: **Now, since this** verse: **"May the words of my mouth** and the meditation of my heart **be acceptable** before You," can **connote the end** of prayer, petitioning God that He accept the prayer that was just recited, **and** it can **connote the beginning** of the prayer **that he wants to recite:** May the words of my mouth which I am about to recite be acceptable before You. If so, the question arises: **Why did the Sages institute** that it is to be recited **after the eighteen blessings** that constitute the *Amida*? **Let it be recited at the beginning** of the prayer. **Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi, said:** This verse is recited after the eighteen blessings comprising the *Amida* **because David only said** this verse **after eighteen chapters** of Psalms (end of ch. 19). **Therefore, the Sages instituted** to recite it **after the eighteen blessings** of the *Amida*.

The Gemara asks about *yihyu l'ratzon imrei fi*: since this phrase can be read as referring either to what one has just said or to what one is about to say, why did the Rabbanan establish it at the end of *shemoneh esrei* rather than at the beginning? Rabbi Yehuda the son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi answers: *ho'il v'amru David l'achar yod ches parshiyos* — since David recited this verse after eighteen *parshiyos*, placing it at the end of *tehillim* 19, the Rabbanan correspondingly established it after the eighteen *brachos* of *shemoneh esrei*. The Gemara immediately challenges this: *tehillim* 19 is the nineteenth chapter, not the eighteenth. The resolution: *ashrei ha-ish u-lama ragshu goyim — chada parsha hi* — the first and second chapters of *tehillim* are in reality a single *parsha*, which brings the count to eighteen. This is why *yihyu l'ratzon* appears after *shemoneh esrei* and not before.

One might add that there is also something intrinsically powerful about *hashem sefatai tiftach* as an opening — not only for its contextual connection, through the surrounding verses in *tehillim*, to the preference for verbal prayer over sacrifice (*ki lo sachpotz olah va-zevach*), but because it expresses a very personal, singular entreaty — *my* mouth, God open *my* lips — in contrast to the plural voice of *shemoneh esrei*, thereby framing the entire prayer in a deeply individual key.

### Tehillim Perek 1–2 Unity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה?! תְּשַׁע עֶשְׂרֵה הָוְיָין! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ ״אַשְׁרֵי הָאִישׁ״ וְ״לָמָּה רָגְשׁוּ גוֹיִם״ חֲדָא פָּרָשָׁה הִיא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כָּל פָּרָשָׁה שֶׁהָיְתָה חֲבִיבָה עַל דָּוִד, פָּתַח בָּהּ בְּ״אַשְׁרֵי״ וְסִייֵּם בָּהּ בְּ״אַשְׁרֵי״.
>
> **Translation:** Here too, the Gemara notes that the calculation appears inaccurate: Are **these one hundred and three** psalms? **They are one hundred and four. Rather, conclude from this** that **"Happy is the man" and "Why are the nations in uproar" constitute a single portion.** Additional proof that these two chapters comprise a single portion is cited from what **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Every chapter that was dear to David, he began with "happy is" and concluded with "happy is."**

The Gemara adduces further evidence that the first two chapters are in fact a single chapter. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani cites Rabbi Yochanan: *kol parsha she-haysah chaviva al David pasach bah b'ashrei v'siyem bah b'ashrei* — any passage that was beloved to David he opened with *ashrei* and concluded with *ashrei*. The combined unit of chapters 1 and 2 opens with *ashrei ha-ish* and closes with *ashrei kol chosei vo* — demonstrating that David himself regarded them as one *parsha* with a unified *ashrei* frame. Structurally, the evidence is compelling as well: every other chapter in *tehillim* opens with a heading or key word — *hallelujah*, *ashrei*, *lamnatze'ach*, and so on — but chapter 2 has no such opening. Additionally, chapter 2 ends with a word that echoes the beginning of chapter 1. *tosfos* notes that this is the only instance of a passage opening and closing with *ashrei*, suggesting a unique literary unit.

A further proof is brought from the counting of David's *parshiyos* in *tehillim*. The tradition holds that David did not say *hallelujah* until he saw the downfall of the wicked — *shene'emar yitamu chata'im min ha-aretz u-resha'im od einam, barchi nafshi es Hashem hallelujah* — as written at the end of *tehillim* 104. But this comes after 103 *parshiyos*, not 104 — only if chapters 1 and 2 are counted as one. The Gemara thus concludes: *shma mina — chada parsha hi* — it may be derived from here that they are one *parsha*. The numerical elegance is also notable: just as *shemoneh esrei* has eighteen *brachos* yet is sometimes counted as nineteen, the first nineteen *parshiyos* of *tehillim* are actually eighteen — a parallel that is deeply satisfying given the Gemara's connection between *yihyu l'ratzon* and the structure of both.

### Rif: Night/Day Shema Psak


Having traversed the various opinions, the Gemara's material can be organized into a coherent halachic framework as ruled by the *rif*. Night *shema* should *l'chatchila* be completed by *alot hashachar*; *b'diavad* and *b'sha'as had'chak*, one may rely on Rabbi Shimon and say it until *hanetz hachama*, as the Gemara acknowledged earlier. Day *shema* may begin as early as *alot* when circumstances demand, but the proper starting point is *misheyakir*. The ideal for day *shema* is the *vatikin* practice of finishing precisely at *hanetz hachama*, so that *shemoneh esrei* commences in the day and *semichas ge'ulah l'tefillah* is fully achieved. The period between *alot* and *misheyakir* is thus a kind of twilight zone: one cannot simultaneously say a valid night *shema* and a valid day *shema* within it, though *alot* itself marks the cutoff for night and the earliest possible beginning of day. The *ba'al hamaor* engages this material with particular depth, but the *rif*'s framework captures the essential structure.

### Beruriah on Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה לוֹ בְּרוּרְיָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ: מַאי דַּעְתָּךְ — מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב ״יִתַּמּוּ חַטָּאִים״, מִי כְּתִיב ״חוֹטְאִים״? ״חַטָּאִים״ כְּתִיב. וְעוֹד, שְׁפֵיל לְסֵיפֵיהּ דִּקְרָא ״וּרְשָׁעִים עוֹד אֵינָם״, כֵּיוָן דְּ״יִתַּמּוּ חַטָּאִים״ ״וּרְשָׁעִים עוֹד אֵינָם״? אֶלָּא בְּעִי רַחֲמֵי עִלָּוַיְהוּ דְּלַהְדְּרוּ בִּתְשׁוּבָה, ״וּרְשָׁעִים עוֹד אֵינָם״.
>
> **Translation:** Beruriah, Rabbi Meir's wife, challenged him: **What are you thinking?** On what basis do you pray for the death of these hooligans? Do you base yourself on the verse, **as it is written: "Let sins cease from the land"** (Psalms 104:35), which you interpret to mean that the world would be better if the wicked were destroyed? But **is it written,** let **sinners** cease?" Let **sins** cease, **is written.** One should pray for an end to their transgressions, not for the demise of the transgressors themselves. **Moreover, go to the end of the verse,** where it says: **"And the wicked will be no more."** If, as you suggest, **transgressions shall cease** refers to the demise of the evildoers, how is it possible that **the wicked will be no more,** i.e., that they will no longer be evil? **Rather, pray for** God to have **mercy on them, that they should repent,** as if they repent, then the wicked will be no more, as they will have repented.

Beruriah, Rabbi Meir's wife, challenged him: *mai da'atach* — what are you thinking? The verse *yitamu chata'im min ha-aretz u-resha'im od einam* — "let *chata'im* cease from the earth and the wicked will be no more" — does not use the word *chotim* (sinners) but *chata'im*. She reads it as referring to sins, not sinners. She then directs attention to the end of the verse: *u-resha'im od einam* — the wicked will be no more. How does this happen? *keivan de-yisamu chata'im u-resha'im od einam* — if the sins cease, those who commit them naturally cease to be wicked. The conclusion: pray for them to do *teshuvah*, not to die. *ba'ei rachmei alaihu de-lihadru bi-teshuvah v'resha'im od einam* — pray for their repentance, and there will no longer be wicked people. Rabbi Meir accepted her correction and prayed accordingly — and they repented.

This is a celebrated episode on multiple levels. Beruriah demonstrates superior textual sensitivity, a more refined *middat harachamim*, and a vision in which the goal is not the destruction of the wicked but their transformation. The characterization of Rabbi Meir's prayer as *ba'ei rachmei alaihu* — *rachmei* meaning compassion or supplication — is itself worth noting: one might read it as Rabbi Meir convinced himself that death was actually merciful for them, preventing further sin; Beruriah's response is that true *rachamim* means praying for their *teshuvah*, not their removal.

### Beruriah vs. Min

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לַהּ הַהוּא מִינָא לִבְרוּרְיָא: כְּתִיב ״רָנִּי עֲקָרָה לֹא יָלָדָה״, מִשּׁוּם דְּלֹא יָלָדָה — רָנִּי? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: שָׁטְיָא, שְׁפֵיל לְסֵיפֵיהּ דִּקְרָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי רַבִּים בְּנֵי שׁוֹמֵמָה מִבְּנֵי בְעוּלָה אָמַר ה׳״. אֶלָּא מַאי ״עֲקָרָה לֹא יָלָדָה״ — רָנִּי כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁדּוֹמָה לְאִשָּׁה עֲקָרָה שֶׁלֹּא יָלְדָה בָּנִים לְגֵיהִנָּם כְּוָתַיְיכוּ.
>
> **Translation:** A *min* engaged Beruriah directly, citing the verse: **"Sing, barren woman who has not given birth,** open forth in song and cry, you did not travail, for more are the children of the desolate one than the children of the married wife, said the Lord" (Isaiah 54:1). **Because she has not given birth,** she should **sing** and rejoice? Beruriah responded to this heretic's mockery and **said: Fool! Go to the end of the verse, where it is written: "For the children of the desolate shall be more numerous than the children of the married wife, said the Lord."** **Rather, what** is the meaning of: **"Sing, barren woman who has not given birth"?** It means: **Sing congregation of Israel, which is like a barren woman who did not give birth to children who are** destined **for Gehenna like you.**

A *min* challenged Beruriah on the verse *roni akara lo yalada* — "rejoice, O barren one who has not given birth." His challenge focused on the plain reading: what cause for rejoicing is there in barrenness? Beruriah replied: *shatya* — fool — *shpil l'seifa d'kra*, look at the end of the verse: *ki rabbim bnei shomema mi-bnei be'ula amar Hashem* — for the children of the desolate one are greater than the children of the inhabiting one, says God. The *peshat* is that Jerusalem, desolate in exile, will ultimately have more children than the conquering nation. But Beruriah reads it more pointedly: *roni Knesses Yisrael she-domah l'isha akara she-lo yalda banim l'Gehinnom k'vatyichu* — praised is the congregation of Israel that is like a barren woman who has not given birth to children destined for *gehinnom* — as opposed to you, whose children go straight there. It is possible that the *min*'s challenge carried an implicit theological implication — perhaps related to claims about miraculous birth — and Beruriah's response deflects it entirely by recontextualizing the verse within the framework of Israel's ultimate vindication.

### Abbahu: Smuchin Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הַהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, כְּתִיב: ״מִזְמוֹר לְדָוִד בְּבָרְחוֹ מִפְּנֵי אַבְשָׁלוֹם בְּנוֹ״, וּכְתִיב: ״לְדָוִד מִכְתָּם בְּבָרְחוֹ מִפְּנֵי שָׁאוּל בַּמְּעָרָה״. הֵי מַעֲשֶׂה הֲוָה בְּרֵישָׁא? מִכְּדִי מַעֲשֵׂה שָׁאוּל הֲוָה בְּרֵישָׁא, לִכְתּוֹב בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** In explaining passages from Psalms, the Gemara relates another instance of a response to the question of a heretic: **A certain heretic said to Rabbi Abbahu, it is written: "A Psalm of David, when he fled from his son, Absalom"** (Psalms 3:1), **and** similarly **it is said:** "To the chief musician, *al tashḥet*, **a *mikhtam* of David when fleeing from Saul into the cave"** (Psalms 57:1). **Which event was first? Since the event with Saul was first,** it would have been appropriate **to write it first.**

A *tzadoki* — in the original text, a *min* — said to Rabbi Abbahu: the verse says *mizmor l'David b'vorcho mipnei Avshalom bno*, a song of David when he fled from Absalom his son (*tehillim* 3), and separately there is the psalm *l'David michtam b'vorcho mipnei Shaul b'me'ara* (*tehillim* 57), when David hid from Saul in a cave. Chronologically, the Saul episode preceded Absalom by years. Why then is the Absalom psalm written first? The question implies an apparent disorder in *tehillim*.

### Avshalom & Gog u-Magog

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לַהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אַתּוּן דְּלָא דָּרְשִׁיתוּן סְמוּכִין — קַשְׁיָא לְכוּ, אֲנַן דְּדָרְשִׁינַן סְמוּכִים — לָא קַשְׁיָא לַן. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: סְמוּכִין מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִן — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״סְמוּכִים לָעַד לְעוֹלָם עֲשׂוּיִם בֶּאֱמֶת וְיָשָׁר״. לָמָּה נִסְמְכָה פָּרָשַׁת אַבְשָׁלוֹם לְפָרָשַׁת גּוֹג וּמָגוֹג — שֶׁאִם יֹאמַר לְךָ אָדָם: כְּלוּם יֵשׁ עֶבֶד שֶׁמּוֹרֵד בְּרַבּוֹ?! אַף אַתָּה אֱמוֹר לוֹ: כְּלוּם יֵשׁ בֵּן שֶׁמּוֹרֵד בְּאָבִיו? אֶלָּא הֲוָה, הָכָא נָמֵי הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu **said to him:** For **you, who do** not employ the **homiletic** method **of juxtaposition** of verses, **it is difficult.** But for **us, who** employ the **homiletic** method **of juxtaposition** of verses, **it is not difficult,** as the Sages commonly homiletically infer laws and moral lessons from the juxtaposition of two verses. Regarding the juxtaposition of verses, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: From where** in the Bible is it derived that one may draw homiletical inferences from the **juxtaposition** of verses? **As it is said:** "The works of His hands in truth and justice, all His commandments are sure. **Adjoined forever and ever, made in truth and uprightness"** (Psalms 111:7–8). Conclude from here that it is appropriate to draw inferences from the juxtaposition of God's commandments. Accordingly, David's fleeing from Absalom is situated where it is in order to juxtapose it to the next chapter, which mentions the war of Gog and Magog; the second chapter of Psalms opens: "Why are the nations in an uproar?" **Why was the chapter of Absalom juxtaposed with the chapter of Gog and Magog?** They are juxtaposed **so** that **if a person should say to you,** expressing doubt with regard to the prophecy of the war of Gog and Magog: **Is there a slave who rebels against his master?** Is there someone capable of rebelling against God? **You too say to him: Is there a son who rebels against his father** and severs the relationship with the one who brought him into the world and raised him? **Yet,** nevertheless, **there was** such a son, Absalom, and **so too there** can **be** a situation where people will seek to rebel against God.

Rabbi Abbahu responds: *atun d'lo darshitu smuchin kashya l'chu* — for you who do not expound *smuchin* — the meaning of juxtaposed passages — this is a difficulty. *anan d'darshinan smuchin lo kashya lan* — for us who do, it is not. The principle of *smuchin* — that the placement of passages adjacent to one another is intentional and meaningful — is grounded in the verse Rabbi Yochanan cites: *smuchim la'ad l'olam asuyim be'emet v'yashar* — the supports are forever, made in truth and righteousness. The question then becomes not why the order seems wrong but what the juxtaposition teaches.

The answer: *lama nismacha parshas Avshalom l'parshas Gog u-Magog* — why is the Absalom narrative placed adjacent to the *gog u-magog* narrative? The second chapter of *tehillim*, as we established, is a single unit with the first and alludes to the battles of *gog u-magog* and the ultimate establishment of God's kingdom. One might object: could such a thing as *gog u-magog* ever come to pass? Could a slave rebel against his master? The response: *klum yesh ben she-mored be-aviv* — has there ever been a son who rebelled against his father? Indeed there was — Absalom rebelled against David. If such a thing could happen, so too can the nations rebel against God, and so too will the drama of *gog u-magog* unfold. The juxtaposition is thus didactic: the Absalom narrative, positioned before the *gog u-magog* passage, furnishes the proof of possibility.

### Christian Subtext


If the interlocutor is indeed a *min* in the sense of a Hebrew Christian — as the original *girsa* suggests — the exchange takes on an additional resonance. The notion of a son who rebels against the father, offered here as a proof of a painful but historically verified possibility, may carry a polemical undercurrent: the claim of a son who displaces or supersedes the father, presented by some as fulfillment rather than rebellion, is here reframed through the lens of Absalom — an act of treacherous revolt. The proof that such rebellion can occur is simultaneously a characterization of what kind of act it is. Whether this reading is intended or merely suggested by the context cannot be stated with certainty, but it is difficult not to notice the resonance when the debate partner is assumed to be from within a tradition that understood the son-father relationship in theological terms.
