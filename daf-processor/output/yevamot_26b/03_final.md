# Yevamot 26b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Brothers Case

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַרְבָּעָה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶם נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וּמֵתוּ הַנְּשׂוּאִים אֶת הָאֲחָיוֹת — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת. וְאִם קָדְמוּ וְכָנְסוּ — יוֹצִיאוּ. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יְקַיֵּים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: יוֹצִיאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **four brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters, and the ones married to the sisters died, then those** sisters must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.** Since both sisters require levirate marriage with each of the surviving brothers, a levirate bond exists between each sister and the brothers. Each of them is considered the sister of a woman with whom each brother has a levirate bond and is therefore forbidden to him by rabbinic law. **And if they married** the sisters **before** consulting the court, **they should divorce** them, for the Sages decreed that in this situation they may not remain married. **Rabbi Eliezer says** that there is a dispute in this matter: **Beit Shammai say: He may maintain** her as his wife, **while Beit Hillel say: They** must **divorce** them.

The discussion opens at the very bottom of 26a, returning to a *mishnah* that has appeared several times: four brothers married to four women, two of whom are sisters. Because of the principle of *achos zekukaso* — the sister of one's *zekukah* — neither surviving brother may perform *yibum*, and both women require *chalitzah*. The *mishnah* thus far has been explained in two distinct ways. The first explanation focuses on *achos zekukaso* itself. The second explanation holds that the real concern is *assur levatel mitzvas yabmin*: if, for example, Levi performs *yibum* with Rachel, and Yehudah subsequently dies, Leah becomes *achos ishto* — forbidden to both Levi (as his wife's sister) and now unavailable for *chalitzah* or *yibum* from anyone. To prevent this, Levi is required to perform *chalitzah*, so that even in the event of Yehudah's death, Levi would still be able to perform *chalitzah* with Leah.

### Why Four Brothers?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: יֵשׁ זִיקָה. דְּאִי אֵין זִיקָה, מִכְּדִי הָנֵי מִתְּרֵי בָתֵּי קָאָתְיָין — הַאי לְיַיבֵּם חֲדָא וְהַאי לְיַיבֵּם חֲדָא? לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ אֵין זִיקָה, וּמִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר אָסוּר לְבַטֵּל מִצְוַת יְבָמִין. דִּלְמָא אַדִּמְיַיבֵּם חַד — מָיֵית אִידַּךְ, וְקָמְבַטֵּל מִצְוַת יְבָמִין. אִי הָכִי, תְּלָתָא נָמֵי? לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמְרִינַן: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא תְּלָתָא, דְּוַדַּאי בָּטְלָה מִצְוַת יְבָמִין. אֲבָל אַרְבְּעָה, לְמִיתָה לָא חָיְישִׁינַן — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara deduces from the *halakha* cited in the mishna: **Conclude from here** that the **levirate bond is substantial.** That is, the very obligation of levirate marriage creates a bond that is similar to marriage. **For if the levirate bond were not substantial,** why would these two women not enter into levirate marriage? **After all, these** two women **come from two households,** as each had a different husband, and they both require levirate marriage. **Let this** brother **consummate the levirate marriage with one** sister **and let that** brother **consummate the levirate marriage with** the **other** sister. The fact that the mishna requires *ḥalitza* in this situation indicates that the levirate bond is substantial and resembles marriage to the extent that each sister is forbidden to each brother due to the prohibition against marrying the sister of a woman to whom one has a levirate bond. The Gemara rejects this: **Actually, I** could **say to you** that according to this *tanna* **the levirate bond is not substantial,** and yet they are prohibited from entering levirate marriage for a different reason. It is **because** the *tanna* **holds that it is prohibited to negate the mitzva of levirate marriage.** It is prohibited to act in a way that would lead to a situation where the mitzva to perform levirate marriage is negated. How would this situation arise? **Perhaps before one** brother **consummates the levirate marriage, the other** brother **dies,** and only one brother remains. In that case, the second sister would also happen before him for levirate marriage, **and** by performing levirate marriage with one sister **he would** thereby **negate the mitzva of levirate marriage** with the other sister. When the remaining brother marries one of the sisters, the mitzva to enter into levirate marriage or perform *ḥalitza* is automatically negated from the second sister, as she is then forbidden to him as his wife's sister. The Gemara asks: **If so,** if this is the rationale behind the ruling in the mishna, then the same concern would exist if there were **three** brothers, **as well.** Why did the mishna specify four brothers? It could have cited the case of three brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters. In these circumstances, the concern for negating the mitzva of levirate marriage also exists. The Gemara answers: Indeed, the ruling would be the same in that case. However, the mishna **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary. It is not necessary** to specify that the women must perform *ḥalitza* in a case involving **three** brothers, **as certainly the mitzva of levirate marriage is negated** with one of the sisters when the *yavam* marries the other sister. **But** in the case of **four** brothers, where there is concern only over the possibility that one of the brothers might die, we might have said that **we are not concerned over** the possibility of **the death** of a brother and therefore allow the brothers to consummate the levirate marriage. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that even in the case where there is concern only for the negation of the mitzva, they must perform *ḥalitza* and not consummate the levirate marriage.

The *Gemara* now presses a sharp question against the second explanation: *i hachi, tlosa nami* — if the concern is *assur levatel mitzvas yabmin*, why does the *mishnah* require four brothers? Three would suffice. With only three brothers and two sisters, if Levi performs *yibum* with Rachel, Leah is immediately left without any option — she becomes *achos ishto* to Levi and has no other brother to turn to. The prohibition is obvious even in the three-brother case.

### Zika Strength vs Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי — חַמְשָׁה נָמֵי! לְמִיתָה דִּתְרֵי לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** then this should **also** be true in a case of **five** brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters and died, and their wives happened before the three remaining brothers for levirate marriage. The Gemara answers: **We are not concerned over** the possibility of **the death of two** brothers in such a short period of time.

The *Gemara* resolves this by invoking the principle of *lo mibaya ka'mar*: the three-brother case is the more obvious case and therefore need not be stated. The *chidush* of the four-brother case operates at a different level. When two brothers remain behind, there is reason to argue that the concern of *achos zekukaso* is attenuated — the *zika* is weaker when two brothers share it, since it is not exclusively Levi's *zika* nor exclusively Yehudah's. The *mishnah* therefore teaches that even with two remaining brothers, the *achos zekukaso* prohibition is operative. The alternative concern — *assur levatel mitzvas yabmin* — introduces a further *chidush* in the four-brother case: even though Yehudah is alive and presumably available, we nonetheless fear that he might die before performing *yibum* or *chalitzah*, leaving Leah stranded.

The *Gemara* then asks: if we fear Yehudah's death in the four-brother case, why not fear the deaths of both Yehudah and Yissachar in a five-brother case? The *Gemara* responds: *lemisa detrei lo chashinan* — we do not entertain the fear that two brothers will die simultaneously before any act of *yibum* or *chalitzah* can be performed. That possibility is too remote. We allow for the possibility of one death; two is beyond the scope of our concern. Thus in the four-brother scenario, where Leah depends on Yehudah alone, we do fear his death — but once two brothers stand between a woman and her potential *yibum*-less fate, we no longer worry.

## Three Sisters Case

### Three Sisters Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: שָׁלֹשׁ אֲחָיוֹת יְבָמוֹת שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לִפְנֵי שְׁנֵי אַחִין יְבָמִין — זֶה חוֹלֵץ לְאַחַת וְזֶה חוֹלֵץ לְאַחַת, וְאֶמְצָעִית צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִשְּׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava bar Rav Huna said** that **Rav said:** In a case where **three sisters** who were married to three brothers became ***yevamot*** and **happened before two** remaining **brothers** who were their ***yevamin*** for levirate marriage, then **this** one brother **performs *ḥalitza* with one** of the women, **and that** brother **performs *ḥalitza* with** another **one** of the women. **And the middle,** i.e., third, sister **requires *ḥalitza* with both of** the brothers.

The *Gemara* now presents a new case: *shalosh achayos yevamos shenaplu lifnei shenei achim yevamim* — three sisters, all *yevamos*, who have fallen before two surviving brothers. To cast this concretely: three brothers — Reuven, Shimon, and Levi — each married one of three sisters, and all three have died. The two surviving brothers, Yissachar and Zevulun, now face three sisters — Rachel, Leah, and a third (call her Sarah) — all of whom stand in a relationship of *zika* to both of them. No woman is a genuine *ervah* to either brother, since each was married to a different man. But under the principle of *yesh zika*, any act of *yibum* would implicate *achos zekukaso*, since each woman is a sister of the others to whom the brother is also *zakuk*. The case is therefore a larger variant of *achos zekukaso*, and the question is how to distribute *chalitzah* between the two brothers and three women.

One might ask: why not simply allow Yissachar to perform *chalitzah* with all three women? As the *Gemara* will develop, the answer turns on a new principle — that not all *chalitzah* acts are of equal weight — and the distribution matters precisely because of differential strength in the *zika*.

### Distribution Pattern

The ruling, stated in the name of Rav via Rava bar Rav Huna, is as follows: one brother performs *chalitzah* with the first sister, the other brother performs *chalitzah* with the second sister, and the third sister — called the *emtza'it*, the "middle" one — requires *chalitzah* from both brothers. The term *emtza'it* is somewhat misleading: she is called "middle" not because she is the second to fall to *yibum*, but because she stands between both brothers in terms of their obligations. She has a claim on each of them and, as will be explained, requires *chalitzah* from each.

### Sequential Deaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה: מִדְּקָאָמְרַתְּ אֶמְצָעִית צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִשְּׁנֵיהֶם, קָסָבְרַתְּ יֵשׁ זִיקָה, וְהָוְיָא לַהּ חֲלִיצָה פְּסוּלָה — וַחֲלִיצָה פְּסוּלָה צָרִיךְ לַחֲזוֹר עַל כׇּל הָאַחִין, אִי הָכִי, קַמַּיְיתָא נָמֵי! אִי דִּנְפוּל בְּבַת אַחַת — הָכִי נָמֵי. לָא צְרִיכָא: דִּנְפוּל בְּזוֹ אַחַר זוֹ. נְפַלָה חֲדָא — חָלֵץ לַהּ רְאוּבֵן, נְפַלָה אִידַּךְ — חָלֵץ לַהּ שִׁמְעוֹן, נְפַלָה אִידַּךְ, חָלֵץ לַהּ הַאי — מַפְקַע זִיקָתוֹ, חָלֵץ לַהּ הַאי — מַפְקַע זִיקָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said to them: From** the fact **that you say** that **the middle one requires *ḥalitza* with both of** the brothers, it can be deduced that **you hold** that **the levirate bond is substantial** and creates a familial relationship, **and** similarly **this** *ḥalitza* performed by each of the brothers with the third sister **is invalid *ḥalitza*.** Since the third woman is the sister of a woman with whom he performed *ḥalitza*, it is not possible to consummate the levirate marriage with her. Therefore, the *ḥalitza* performed with her is not entirely valid and does not release her from the levirate bond between her and the two brothers. **And** there is a principle that **invalid *ḥalitza* must be repeated by all of the brothers;** each of them must perform an act of *ḥalitza* with this woman. The Gemara asks: **If that is so, the first** two women should **also** require *ḥalitza* from each of the brothers because each act of *ḥalitza* was invalid, as each woman has the status of the sister of a woman with whom the man has a levirate bond, and it is therefore prohibited for the men to consummate the levirate marriage with them. The Gemara responds: **If** the case is **that they,** i.e., the sisters, **happened** before the brothers **simultaneously, it is indeed so** that the two brothers would each be obligated to perform *ḥalitza* with each of the three sisters. This *halakha* is **necessary only** in the case **where they happened** before the brothers, **one after the other.** How so? **One** sister **happened** before the brothers for levirate marriage and then **Reuven,** one of the brothers, **performed** valid ***ḥalitza* with her. Another** sister **happened** before the brothers for levirate marriage, and the brother **Shimon performed *ḥalitza* with her.** In this case, Shimon was allowed to consummate the levirate marriage with her if he so desired, as she had no family relationship to him at all. When Reuven performed *ḥalitza* with the first sister, he removed the levirate bond between her and the other brothers, such that Shimon has no relationship with the first sister at all and is free to marry the second sister. Therefore, when he performed *ḥalitza*, the act was valid. **Another** third sister then **happened** before the brothers for levirate marriage. When **this** first brother **performs *ḥalitza* with her, he terminates his levirate bond, and** when **this** second brother **performs *ḥalitza* with her, he terminates his levirate bond.** However, neither brother can, by performing *ḥalitza*, terminate the other brother's levirate bond because each brother's *ḥalitza* is invalid, as each brother is unable to consummate the levirate marriage in this case. This is due to the woman's status as the sister of a woman with whom he performed *ḥalitza*.

The *Gemara* refines the scenario. If all three brothers had died simultaneously, every sister would be *achos zekukaso* from the outset, and each woman would indeed require two *chalitzos*. But the case being discussed is one of sequential deaths — *denafelu zu achar zu*. When the first brother dies, one sister becomes subject to *yibum* with no complicating factor: there is no co-zekukah, the *zika* is complete, and the *chalitzah* performed by, say, Yissachar is fully valid. When the second brother dies, the next sister becomes available. At this point, from Yissachar's perspective, she is *achos chalutzaso* — the sister of the woman he just performed *chalitzah* with — making his *chalitzah* weaker. Zevulun, however, has no such problem: the first *chalitzah* was Yissachar's, not his. Zevulun's *chalitzah* with the second sister is therefore first-rate. By the time the third brother dies and Sarah becomes available, both brothers have each performed *chalitzah* with one of her sisters. She is *achos chalutzaso* to both Yissachar and to Zevulun. Any *chalitzah* she receives will be second-rate from either one of them. And this is precisely why she needs it from both.

### Chalitzah Pesulah

This brings us to a new and central principle that governs the remainder of the *daf*: the concept of *chalitzah pesulah*. The observation is this — when the *zika* between a brother and his *yevamah* is weakened, the *chalitzah* performed in that context is correspondingly weakened. A full, first-rate *chalitzah* by one brother frees a *yevamah* completely; all other brothers are discharged and she may go free. But a *chalitzah pesulah* — a second-rate *chalitzah* — does not accomplish this. It requires, instead, *chazor al kol ha'achim*: the woman must receive *chalitzah* from every surviving brother, each of whom must separately discharge his own *zika*.

## Chalitzah Pesulah

### Defining Pesulah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב: אֵין זִיקָה! לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר יֵשׁ זִיקָה קָאָמַר. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֶחָד חוֹלֵץ לְכוּלָּן. מִכְּדִי שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר: חֲלִיצָה מְעַלְּיָא בְּעֵינַן, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חָלַץ לַאֲחָיוֹת — לֹא נִפְטְרוּ צָרוֹת. הֵיכָא דְּקָיְימָא חֲלִיצָה דְּשִׁמְעוֹן חֲלִיצָה כְּשֵׁרָה, חָלֵיץ לַהּ רְאוּבֵן חֲלִיצָה פְּסוּלָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can this statement be cited in the name of Rav? **But didn't Rav say: The levirate bond is not substantial.** This *halakha*, however, was explained based on the assumption that the levirate bond is substantial. The Gemara answers: Indeed, according to Rav himself it is unnecessary to perform *ḥalitza* with multiple brothers. He, however, **states** this *halakha* **in accordance with the statement of the one who says that the levirate bond is substantial,** despite the fact that he does not hold this himself. **And** with regard to the above case **Shmuel said** a different halakhic ruling: It is sufficient if **one** brother **performs *ḥalitza* with each** of the sisters. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But since we have heard that Shmuel said: We require a full-fledged *ḥalitza*,** and invalid *ḥalitza* does not result in complete exemption, this ruling is difficult. It is not clear why the invalid *ḥalitza* in this case would be sufficient. In the case of three brothers, two of whom were married to several women, including two sisters, and the two married brothers later died, and their wives happened before the *yavam* for levirate marriage, if the *yavam* **performed *ḥalitza* with the sisters** who were among the wives, **the rival wives are not** thereby **exempt.** One can deduce from here that since the *yavam* cannot consummate the levirate marriage with the sisters, as each is the sister of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond, then the act of *ḥalitza* is invalid, and invalid *ḥalitza* is ineffective in exempting their rival wives. It is concluded from here that even Shmuel requires valid *ḥalitza*, i.e., *ḥalitza* that occurs when there is a possibility of consummating the levirate marriage. According to this rationale, however, Shmuel's ruling in the above case is difficult: With regard to the second sister, **when there exists** the possibility for **Shimon's *ḥalitza*,** i.e., the *ḥalitza* of the second brother who did not yet perform *ḥalitza*, to be **a valid *ḥalitza*,** would it be allowed for **Reuven,** the brother who already did *ḥalitza* with one sister, **to perform invalid *ḥalitza* with** her?

The principle of *chalitzah pesulah* thus emerges as the organizing concept for the cases that follow. When is a *chalitzah* considered *pesulah*? Rashi offers a general formulation: *chalitzah pesulah* is *chalitzah gerua*, a second-rate *chalitzah*, in any case where *yibum* was not possible — *she'im ratza leyabbem eino yachol*. If the brother could not have performed *yibum* with this woman, then the *chalitzah* he performs with her is by definition second-rate.

### Tosafos Refinement

Tosafos, however, sharpens this definition. Rashi's criterion is accurate as far as it goes, but it is somewhat overbroad. There are cases where *yibum* is impossible for reasons unrelated to any weakening of the *zika* — and Tosafos holds that such cases would not qualify as *chalitzah pesulah* in the operative sense. For *chalitzah pesulah* to apply, the specific reason *yibum* is impossible must be that the *zika* itself has been problematized or attenuated. It is not merely the impossibility of *yibum* that creates the category, but the particular mechanism: a *zika* that has been weakened, distorted, or displaced from its normative form.

### Examples of Weakening

Tosafos identifies three paradigmatic cases in which the *zika* is weakened in this operative sense, collected at the end of the relevant *dibur hamatchil*. The first is *achos zekukaso*: when among all the women to whom a brother is *zakuk*, one is the sister of another. She is not a genuine *ervah* — that would make her *achos ishto* and would generate full exemption — but she is a quasi-*ervah* through rabbinic extension. The *zika* to her is real, but it operates under a cloud, and any *chalitzah* performed with her is therefore *pesulah*. The second is *ba'alas haget*: if a brother gave the *yevamah* a *get* before performing *chalitzah*, the *get* has no Torah-level effect in this context — the woman is not his wife, and a *get* cannot dissolve the *zika* — but rabbinic law grants it enough weight to preclude *yibum*. Having now made *yibum* impossible, the subsequent *chalitzah* is second-rate. The third case is the inverse and is in some ways more striking: *ba'alas ma'amar*. Here the brother performed *ma'amar* — a rabbinic-level *kiddushin* with the *yevamah*, a formal act of partial acquisition that is the precursor to full *yibum* — thereby strengthening rather than weakening the apparent connection. Yet this too renders *chalitzah* second-rate. The dynamic is instructive: *chalitzah* is relevant to the middle zone of the *zika* relationship. Move the woman too far away — as with *achos zekukaso* or *ba'alas haget* — and *chalitzah* is less relevant because she is quasi-forbidden. Move her too close — as with *ma'amar* — and *chalitzah* is less relevant because she is quasi-his-wife. In either direction, the displacement from the normative *zika* weakens the *chalitzah*.

It should be noted that when *ma'amar* has been performed, the full procedure requires both a *get* (to undo the *ma'amar*) and *chalitzah* (to undo the underlying *zika*). But the *chalitzah* in that context is still regarded as second-rate because the woman's status has shifted from the classic *yevamah* who stands ready for *yibum* or *chalitzah* without prior complication.

### Multiple Chalitzos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אֶחָד חוֹלֵץ לְכוּלָּן״ נָמֵי דְּקָאָמַר — אַאֶמְצָעִית. וְהָא ״כּוּלָּן״ קָאָמַר! כֵּיוָן דְּרוּבָּה גַּבֵּיהּ, קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״כּוּלָּן״. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כִּי קָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל חֲלִיצָה מְעַלְּיָא בְּעֵינַן — הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְמִיפְטַר צָרָתָהּ, אֲבָל מִפְטַרא נַפְשַׁהּ — פָּטְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves this difficulty by reinterpreting Shmuel's statement. **What** does it mean **that it says: One performs *ḥalitza* with each of them, that** Shmuel **stated?** Shmuel **says that with regard to the middle one,** i.e., the third sister, one of the two brothers performs *ḥalitza* with her. The Gemara asks: **But didn't he say: Each of them,** indicating that one brother performs *ḥalitza* with all of the sisters? The Gemara answers: **Since** the brother who performed *ḥalitza* with one sister repeats the act with another, it turns out that **most** of the acts of *ḥalitza* **are** performed **with him, and this is called:** With **each of them. And if you wish, say** a different answer: **When Shmuel said we require a full-fledged *ḥalitza*, this applies only to exempt her rival wife** by means of that *ḥalitza*. **But to exempt** the woman **herself,** even invalid *ḥalitza* would render her **exempt.** In the case above, since no rival wives are involved, it would be sufficient for one brother to perform *ḥalitza* with each of the sisters.

The practical upshot of *chalitzah pesulah* is the rule of *chazor al kol ha'achim*: she must go around to all the brothers. Why? Because when a *chalitzah* is first-rate and complete, one brother's act fully extinguishes the entire nexus of *zika* — all brothers are discharged simultaneously, since the *zika* was a unified bond. But when the *chalitzah* is second-rate, it is only sufficient to remove the particular *zika* of the one brother who performed it. Each brother retains his own residual *zika*, and each must therefore act separately to discharge it. This explains the case of Sarah in the three-sisters scenario: since she is *achos chalutzaso* to both Yissachar and Zevulun, any *chalitzah* she receives is *pesulah*, and she must therefore obtain *chalitzah* from each of them individually.

## Rav vs Shmuel

### Rav Position

The *Gemara* raises an immediate difficulty: this entire analysis — *achos zekukaso*, *chalitzah pesulah*, *chazor al kol ha'achim* — is predicated on the position of *yesh zika*. But the ruling in question was stated by Rav, and Rav is on record elsewhere as holding *ein zika*. The *Gemara* resolves this: Rav was speaking *ledivrei ha'omer yesh zika* — he was articulating the *halacha* according to the position that holds *yesh zika*, not necessarily expressing his own view. Within that framework, the ruling stands: *chalitzah pesulah* requires *chalitzah* from all the brothers.

### Shmuel Position

Shmuel disagrees. His position is stated concisely: *echad choletz lekullan* — one brother may perform *chalitzah* for all of them. Shmuel apparently does not accept the *chazor al kol ha'achim* requirement. Even where a *chalitzah* would be second-rate — because the woman is *achos chalutzaso* — Shmuel maintains that one brother's *chalitzah* suffices, and the woman need not seek *chalitzah* from the remaining brothers.

## Rav vs Shmuel…

### Achos vs Tzaros

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חָלַץ לַאֲחָיוֹת — לֹא נִפְטְרוּ צָרוֹת, לְצָרוֹת — נִפְטְרוּ אֲחָיוֹת. חָלַץ לְבַעֲלַת הַגֵּט — לֹא נִפְטְרָה צָרָה, לְצָרָה — נִפְטְרָה בַּעֲלַת הַגֵּט. חָלַץ לְבַעֲלַת הַמַּאֲמָר — לֹא נִפְטְרָה צָרָה. לְצָרָה — נִפְטְרָה בַּעֲלַת מַאֲמָר.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos of Shmuel's statement, the Gemara examines **the** matter **itself. Shmuel said:** In the case of three brothers, two of whom were married to several women, including two sisters, and the two married brothers later died, and their wives happened before the *yavam* for levirate marriage, if the *yavam* **performed *ḥalitza* with the sisters, the rival wives are not** thereby **exempt.** But if he performed *ḥalitza* **with the rival wives, the sisters are exempt.** Similarly, if he gave a bill of divorce to one of these women, whereby he would no longer be permitted to consummate the levirate marriage with them due to a rabbinic decree, and he then **performed *ḥalitza* with the woman who received a bill of divorce, the rival wife is not** thereby **exempt.** Since he was unable to consummate the levirate marriage with her, the *ḥalitza* performed with her was invalid, and invalid *ḥalitza* does not exempt the rival wife. If he performed the act of *ḥalitza* **with the rival wife,** then **the woman who received a bill of divorce is exempt.** The ruling is similar with regard to the case where the *yavam* performed *ma'amar*, i.e., levirate betrothal, to one of the wives. If **he** then **performed *ḥalitza* with** the woman **who received** his **levirate betrothal** then **the rival wife is not exempt.** Indeed, this *ḥalitza* is invalid as well, for once the *yavam* performed levirate betrothal, this act can be rescinded only by means of a bill of divorce. Because the woman needs to receive a bill of divorce in addition to the *ḥalitza* in order to exempt her from her bond, the *ḥalitza* is considered invalid and is not sufficient to exempt the rival wife. But if the *yavam* performed *ḥalitza* **with the rival wife,** then **she who received** his **levirate betrothal is exempt** from *ḥalitza* and requires only a bill of divorce.

The *Gemara* now presses Shmuel's position from a different direction. We know from elsewhere on 26a that Shmuel himself holds *chalitzah ma'alya ba'inan* — that a first-rate *chalitzah* is required. Given this, there is a question about Shmuel's permitting one brother to perform *chalitzah* with all three sisters: even setting aside the question of *how many* brothers must participate, shouldn't we at least insist that the *better* *chalitzah* be used when available?

To see why this matters, consider the following case — stated by Shmuel on 26a — involving two sets of women. Yissachar is married to Rachel, and Rachel has a co-wife, Devorah. Zevulun is married to Leah, and Leah has a co-wife, Naomi. Both brothers die. Reuven is the sole surviving brother. All four women stand before him. He cannot perform *yibum* with Rachel, because Rachel is *achos zekukaso* — she is the sister of Leah, to whom he is also *zakuk*. He cannot perform *yibum* with Leah for the same reason. What about Devorah? Devorah is a co-wife of Rachel, who is herself a quasi-*ervah* by virtue of *achos zekukaso*. Devorah is therefore, in the language of the *Gemara*, *tzaras achos zekukaso* — a co-wife of a quasi-*ervah*. This is one degree removed from the problem, but Reuven still cannot perform *yibum* with her. Nevertheless, Reuven's relationship with Devorah is of a different character than his relationship with Rachel: his connection with Devorah is less problematized, and therefore a *chalitzah* with Devorah is stronger than a *chalitzah* with Rachel.

Shmuel's ruling: *chalatz l'achayos lo nifteru tzaros* — if Reuven performs *chalitzah* with the sisters (Rachel or Leah), the co-wives (Devorah, Naomi) are not freed. But *chalatz letzaros nipteru achayos* — if he performs *chalitzah* with the co-wives, the sisters are freed. This demonstrates that Shmuel does recognize a hierarchy of *chalitzah* quality, and that the stronger *chalitzah* — with the co-wife — is more powerful than the weaker one. The same principle applies to *ba'alas haget* and *ba'alas ma'amar*: in each case, *chalitz leba'alas haget* (or *ma'amar*) does not free her co-wife, but *chalatz letzarah* frees the *ba'alas haget* (or *ma'amar*).

### Shmuel's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא לַאֲחָיוֹת דְּלֹא נִפְטְרוּ צָרוֹת — דְּהָוְיָא (לוֹ) [לַהּ] אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה בְּזִיקָה? חָלַץ לְצָרוֹת נָמֵי, לָא לִיפַּטְרוּ אֲחָיוֹת — דְּהָוְיָא לְהוּ צָרוֹת אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה בְּזִיקָה! קָסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין זִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** in the two cases? Why, if he performed *ḥalitza* **with the sisters, are the rival wives not exempt?** This is because the sister **is** related **to him** as **the sister of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond.** Since, under these circumstances he would not be permitted to consummate the levirate marriage with her, her *ḥalitza* is then considered invalid *ḥalitza*. However, if that is so, when **he performed *ḥalitza* with the rival wives, the sisters should not be exempt either, as the** rival wives **are** related **to him as rival wives of the sister of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond.** If the woman is forbidden to him due to a relationship created by the levirate bond, then her rival wife is forbidden to him in the same way, and her *ḥalitza* would be invalid as well. The Gemara answers: **Shmuel holds** that **the levirate bond is not substantial,** and therefore the levirate bond does not create a relationship between the *yavam* and the sisters such that the prohibition would be extended to the rival wives as well.

Given this, the *Gemara* objects: if Shmuel holds that a first-rate *chalitzah* is always preferable — indeed, necessary to free a co-wife — why does he permit one brother to perform all three *chalitzos* in the three-sisters case, including the weaker ones with the sisters who are *achos chalutzaso*? When Shimon's *chalitzah* with the second sister would be fully first-rate, why allow Reuven to do a second-rate *chalitzah* instead?

## Co-wives Case

### Co-wives Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל יֵשׁ זִיקָה! לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר אֵין זִיקָה קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But didn't Shmuel say** explicitly that **the levirate bond is substantial?** The Gemara responds: **He stated** this *halakha* **in accordance with the statement of those who say that the levirate bond is not substantial,** although he himself maintains the opposite.

To clarify the stakes of this debate, the *Gemara* works through the case involving co-wives directly. Here the scenario is as described above: two brothers married to two pairs of women (each wife having a co-wife), with only one brother surviving. All four women are now before Reuven.

### Pesulah Doesn't Free

Shmuel's ruling establishes a clear principle: a second-rate *chalitzah* — one performed in a context where the *zika* is weakened — lacks the force to free a co-wife. If Reuven performs *chalitzah* with Rachel, who is *achos zekukaso*, Devorah remains bound. The *chalitzah pesulah* is effective for Rachel herself — she is freed from the obligation — but it cannot discharge the obligation of a co-wife, because the co-wife's release depends on the *chalitzah* being fully valid.

### First-Rate Required

The inverse, however, holds: a first-rate *chalitzah* with Devorah — whose relationship to Reuven, while still not permitting *yibum*, is less burdened — does free Rachel. The logic is that Reuven's *zika* to Devorah, although precluded from *yibum* by virtue of her being *tzaras achos zekukaso*, is not as deeply problematized as his *zika* to Rachel. It is therefore a stronger act of *chalitzah*, and a stronger *chalitzah* has the power to discharge co-wives as well. This establishes that when co-wives are present, a first-rate *chalitzah* is not merely preferable but necessary to achieve their release.

## Chalitzah Pesulah…

### Strength Weakens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי: ״חָלַץ לְצָרוֹת נִפְטְרוּ אֲחָיוֹת״, וְאַצָּרַת רָחֵל מִי מִיפַּטְרָא? וְהָא תְּנַן: אָסוּר אָדָם בְּצָרַת קְרוֹבַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: **If that is so,** that when Shmuel chose to speak in the plural he was referring only to the rival wife of Rachel, there arises a difficulty with the second half of the statement: If **he performed *ḥalitza* with the rival wives, the sisters are exempt. But would Rachel** become **exempt** by *ḥalitza* performed **with her rival wife? But didn't we learn** in a mishna: **A man is forbidden to** marry **the rival wife of a close relative of his *ḥalutza*?** Once the *yavam* performs *ḥalitza* with one sister, Leah, then her sister's rival wife, i.e., Rachel's rival wife, would be considered the rival wife of the sister of a woman with whom he performed *ḥalitza*. Being as she is forbidden to him, her *ḥalitza* is invalid and should not exempt Rachel.

There is a striking irony embedded in the case of *ba'alas ma'amar* that is worth pausing on. When a brother performs *ma'amar* with his *yevamah*, he is strengthening the *zika* — he is moving closer to full *yibum*, not away from it. Yet this very strengthening renders the subsequent *chalitzah* weaker. The explanation, as noted above, is that *chalitzah* is calibrated to the specific relational zone of the *zika*: the woman stands in a liminal status, available for *yibum* but not yet his wife, available for *chalitzah* but not an *ervah*. Any movement from that liminal zone — whether toward greater distance (as in *achos zekukaso* or *ba'alas haget*) or toward greater closeness (as in *ma'amar*) — displaces the act of *chalitzah* from its normative context and weakens it. The *chalitzah* in a *ma'amar* case is therefore *pesulah*, even though the *zika* was, in one sense, intensified. The result is that both weakening and strengthening the *zika* produce the same outcome: a *chalitzah pesulah* that requires *chazor al kol ha'achim*.

## Shmuel Reread

### Challenge to Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל נָמֵי, הִתְחִיל וְלֹא הִתְחִיל קָאָמַר: הִתְחִיל בַּאֲחָיוֹת — לֹא יִגְמוֹר בְּצָרוֹת, דִּתְנַן: אָסוּר אָדָם בְּצָרַת קְרוֹבַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ. הִתְחִיל בְּצָרוֹת — יִגְמוֹר אַף בַּאֲחָיוֹת. דִּתְנַן: מוּתָּר אָדָם בִּקְרוֹבַת צָרַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Shmuel also** meant to distinguish between a case where **he began and** the case where **he did not begin.** This is how his statement should be understood: If **he began** by performing *ḥalitza* **with** one of **the sisters, he may not finish by** performing a second act of *ḥalitza* **with** any one of **the rival wives, as we learned** in a mishna (40b): **A man is forbidden** to marry **the rival wife of a close relative of his *ḥalutza*.** Due to this prohibition, *ḥalitza* performed with the second rival wife is invalid *ḥalitza* and would not exempt the second sister. If, however, **he began with the rival wives** and performed the first *ḥalitza* with the rival wife of Leah, **he may finish with the sisters as well** and perform the second *ḥalitza* with Leah, **as we learned** in a mishna (40b): **A man is permitted to** marry **the close relative of the rival wife of his *ḥalutza*.** Therefore, if he performed *ḥalitza* with Leah's rival wife, then Rachel, who is the sister of the rival wife of his *ḥalutza*, is permitted to him. He can therefore perform a completely valid *ḥalitza* with her and thereby exempt her rival wife.

Returning now to the case of Reuven and the four women — Rachel, Leah, Devorah, and Naomi — the *Gemara* presses a deeper question. The *Gemara* had accepted that performing *chalitzah* with Devorah is preferable to doing so with Rachel, because Devorah is *tzaras achos zekukaso* (one degree removed) rather than *achos zekukaso* directly. But the *Gemara* now asks: if one holds *yesh zika*, doesn't this make Devorah *tzaras* a quasi-*ervah*? And if so, isn't she just as problematic in her own way as Rachel? Why is the *chalitzah* with Devorah considered stronger?

This challenge threatens the entire framework of Shmuel's ruling. If both Rachel and Devorah are problematic — one directly as *achos zekukaso* and the other as *tzaras achos zekukaso* — on what basis do we say that the *chalitzah* with Devorah is first-rate and gets Rachel off the hook, while the *chalitzah* with Rachel is second-rate and does not free Devorah?

The *Gemara* initially responds by suggesting that Shmuel holds *ein zika*. But this immediately generates a new difficulty: if there is no *zika*, then Rachel is not *achos zekukaso* at all, so why can't Reuven simply perform *yibum* with her? And if all four women stand on equal footing before him, what is the basis for distinguishing the *chalitzah* performed with Rachel from the *chalitzah* performed with Devorah?

### Beginning vs Ending

The *Gemara* therefore undertakes a radical re-reading of Shmuel's ruling. Rather than comparing Rachel and Devorah as two fixed alternatives and asking which is better, the *Gemara* proposes that Shmuel is addressing a sequential question: *hitchil velo hitchil* — the question of how one begins the process and how one ends it.

### Begin Sisters

If Reuven begins with the sisters — that is, he performs *chalitzah* first with Leah (one of the sisters) — then Leah becomes his *chalutzah*. Rachel is now *achos chalutzaso*. At this point, Naomi (Leah's co-wife) is *tzaras krovat chalutzaso* — the co-wife of a relative of his *chalutzah*. The *mishnah* teaches that a man is *assur* in *tzaras krovat chalutzaso*. Consequently, if Reuven started with Leah, he may not finish with Naomi, because doing so would implicate *tzaras krovat chalutzaso*. Doing *chalitzah* with Naomi would not free Rachel either, because she is independently problematic. He is therefore stuck and must still do *chalitzah* with Rachel — but that *chalitzah* will not get Naomi off the hook.

### Begin Co-wives

However, if Reuven begins with the co-wives — performing *chalitzah* first with Devorah — then the situation is entirely different. Devorah is now his *chalutzah*, and Rachel is not *achos chalutzaso* because no *chalitzah* has yet been performed with any sister. At this stage, the *mishnah* teaches *mutar adam bekrovat tzaras chalitzaso* — a man is permitted to the relative of his *chalutzah*'s co-wife. This is the mirror image of the previous case and is not considered problematic. With Devorah as his *chalutzah*, he may proceed with either Rachel or Naomi. If he does *chalitzah* with Rachel, it will free Naomi; if he does it with Naomi, it will free Rachel. By beginning with the co-wife, both options remain open, and first-rate *chalitzah* is available for each.

## Shmuel Reread…

### Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ, וּמִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אַלִּימָא זִיקָה לְשַׁוּוֹיֵי לְצָרָה כְּעֶרְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Actually,** Shmuel's statement should be interpreted **as you** originally **said,** that Shmuel's rationale for these *halakhot* accords with his opinion that the levirate bond is substantial. As for the objection that was raised as to why the sisters would be exempted by *ḥalitza* performed with the rival wives if these rival wives were considered the rival wife of the sister of a woman with whom the *yavam* had a levirate bond, this can be resolved as follows: This **is because the levirate bond is not** so **strong as to render the** status of **a rival wife like** an actual **forbidden relative.** The levirate bond is sufficient to prohibit levirate marriage with the sister of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond, but not sufficient to prohibit their rival wives to the *yavam*.

The *Gemara* ultimately abandons the radical re-reading and returns to the simpler original interpretation of Shmuel, through the intervention of Rav Ashi.

## Rav Ashi Resolution

### Zika Insufficient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי: חָלַץ לָאֲחָיוֹת — לֹא נִפְטְרוּ צָרוֹת. הָא לְצָרוֹת — נִפְטְרוּ אֲחָיוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא — לָאוּ מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר יֵשׁ זִיקָה, וְלָא אַלִּימָא זִיקָה לְשַׁוּוֹיַיהּ לְצָרָה כְּעֶרְוָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Ashi:** If **he performed *ḥalitza* with the sisters, the rival wives are not exempt** from levirate marriage. From here one can deduce: **Consequently,** if he performed *ḥalitza* **with the rival wives, the sisters are exempt. What is the reason** for this? **Is it not because** this *tanna* **held** that **the levirate bond is substantial,** and therefore the rival wives were not rendered exempt by the *ḥalitza* of the sisters, **but** nevertheless **the levirate bond is not** so **strong as to render the rival wife equivalent to a forbidden relative?** Therefore, the prohibition with regard to the rival wives in this case is less severe than the prohibition concerning the sisters themselves, and when they perform *ḥalitza*, the *ḥalitza* is valid and the sisters are exempt.

Rav Ashi states: *leolam kidekamris* — Shmuel means exactly what we originally thought he meant. The *chalitzah* with Devorah is better than the *chalitzah* with Rachel, even within the framework of *yesh zika*. The key principle is: *umishum delo alima zika lishveiye litzarah ke'erva* — the *zika* is not strong enough to render the co-wife fully equivalent to an *ervah*. The reasoning proceeds as follows: Rachel, through *achos zekukaso*, is a quasi-*ervah* — a rabbinic extension of the prohibition of *achos isha*. She is not a genuine Torah-level *ervah*, but she is treated as one rabbinically. Normally, in a case of full *ervah*, the principle of *tzaras ervah* would apply — the co-wife of an *ervah* is also exempt from *yibum* and *chalitzah*. But in this case, since Rachel herself is only a quasi-*ervah* through rabbinic extension, the derivative principle — that her co-wife Devorah is *tzaras ervah* — does not carry the same weight. The *zika* generates the *achos zekukaso* prohibition, but it is not strong enough to then activate, in full, the *tzaras ervah* mechanism for Devorah.

### Co-wife Better

The practical consequence is that Devorah, although she cannot be taken in *yibum*, stands in a better position than Rachel for purposes of *chalitzah*. Rachel's *zika* is directly burdened by *achos zekukaso*, making her *chalitzah* second-rate. Devorah's *zika*, while indirectly affected, is not burdened to the same degree — because the chain of derivation (from *achos zekukaso* to *tzaras achos zekukaso*) attenuates the problem. She is, in the terms introduced earlier, perhaps a grade B rather than grade A, while Rachel is grade C. The *chalitzah* with Devorah is therefore relatively first-rate — stronger than the *chalitzah* with Rachel — and suffices to free Rachel.

### Mishna Alignment

The *Gemara* confirms that this understanding is in line with a *baraita*: *tanya kevasei deRav Ashi* — it was taught in accordance with Rav Ashi. The *baraita* states: *chalatz l'achayos lo nifteru tzaros, chalatz letzaros nipteru achayos* — performing *chalitzah* with the sisters does not free the co-wives, but performing *chalitzah* with the co-wives does free the sisters. And the reason given is precisely: *mishum dekasavar yesh zika velo alumi zika lishveiye litzara ke'erva* — because the *zika* exists but is insufficient to make the co-wife a full *ervah*. This is exactly what Rav Ashi said. The *baraita* resolves the debate in Rav Ashi's favor.

## Beis Shammai View

### Beis Shammai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: הָא מַנִּי — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא. דִּתְנַן: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מַתִּירִין הַצָּרוֹת לָאַחִין. אִי הָכִי, יַבּוֹמֵי נָמֵי תִּתְיַיבֵּם?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba bar Memel** rejected this explanation and **said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita* taught? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Shammai, as we learned** in a mishna: **Beit Shammai permitted the rival wives to** marry **the brothers;** even if they are the rival wives of his actual relatives, they are permitted to enter into levirate marriage. In the case above, where they are merely rival wives of the sister with whom he has a levirate bond, all the more so they are permitted to enter into levirate marriage. The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** if this ruling is in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai, who say that the rival wives are permitted, then the rival wife **should enter into levirate marriage as well.** Why does it speak here only of *ḥalitza* but not of the possibility of entering levirate marriage?

Rabbi Abba bar Mamul offers an alternative account of why performing *chalitzah* with the co-wife is preferable. Rather than invoking the subtlety of *zika* strength, he proposes that Shmuel's ruling reflects the position of Beis Shammai. The *mishnah* elsewhere records: *Beis Shammai matirin hatzaros leachim* — Beis Shammai permit the co-wives to the brothers. According to Beis Shammai, the principle of *tzaras ervah* does not apply at all. Co-wives of an *ervah* are not exempt from *yibum*; they are fully obligated. On this view, Devorah is not problematic in any sense — she is simply another *yevamah* who may be taken in *yibum*. There is therefore no question of first-rate versus second-rate *chalitzah* with her; she stands in a fully normal *chalitzah* relationship with Reuven.

### Objection

The *Gemara* immediately objects: if Beis Shammai permits *yibum* with co-wives, and Shmuel is following Beis Shammai, then why is Shmuel requiring *chalitzah* with Devorah at all? Let Reuven perform *yibum* with her outright.

### Compromise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי, דְּאָמַר: בּוֹאוּ וּנְתַקֵּן לָהֶן לַצָּרוֹת שֶׁיְּהוּ חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת. וְהָאָמַר מָר: לָא הִסְפִּיקוּ לִגְמוֹר אֶת הַדָּבָר עַד שֶׁנִּטְרְפָה הַשָּׁעָה! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אַחֲרָיו חָזְרוּ וְתִקְּנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The ruling is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, who said: Come and let us establish** a ruling **that the rival wives must perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage,** thereby circumventing the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. Although Beit Shammai permitted the rival wives to perform levirate betrothal, they should perform *ḥalitza* instead in order to conform to Beit Hillel's opinion as well. The Gemara objects: **But didn't the Master say** that **they did not succeed in finalizing the matter** and establishing Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri's amendment **before the times of trouble came** in the form of the anti-Jewish decrees, and so this ruling was never actually established? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: After his** time, other Sages **returned** to this issue and **established** this amendment in accordance with his opinion.

The *Gemara* responds by invoking the position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, who proposed: *bo'u unetaken lahem latzaros sheyihyu choltzos velo mityabmos* — let us enact for the co-wives that they perform *chalitzah* and not *yibum*. This ruling is a compromise designed to honor both Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel. Beis Hillel holds that co-wives of an *ervah* are exempt even from *chalitzah*; Beis Shammai holds that they are fully obligated and may even perform *yibum*. The enactment of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri requires *chalitzah* but forbids *yibum* — satisfying the concern of Beis Hillel (who would object to *yibum*) while acknowledging the Beis Shammai position that some obligation exists. The *Gemara* notes that the full ratification of this ruling was not completed in its time — *lo hispiku ligmor et hadavar* — but Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak replies that it was subsequently enacted. The position of Rabbi Abba bar Mamul is thus that Shmuel's ruling reflects this compromise: operating within a Beis Shammai framework modified by the later enactment requiring *chalitzah* rather than *yibum*.
