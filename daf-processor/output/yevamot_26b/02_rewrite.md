# Yevamot 26b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Brothers Case

### Mishnah Setup

The discussion opens at the very bottom of 26a, returning to a *mishnah* that has appeared several times: four brothers married to four women, two of whom are sisters. Because of the principle of *achos zekukaso* — the sister of one's *zekukah* — neither surviving brother may perform *yibum*, and both women require *chalitzah*. The *mishnah* thus far has been explained in two distinct ways. The first explanation focuses on *achos zekukaso* itself. The second explanation holds that the real concern is *assur levatel mitzvas yabmin*: if, for example, Levi performs *yibum* with Rachel, and Yehudah subsequently dies, Leah becomes *achos ishto* — forbidden to both Levi (as his wife's sister) and now unavailable for *chalitzah* or *yibum* from anyone. To prevent this, Levi is required to perform *chalitzah*, so that even in the event of Yehudah's death, Levi would still be able to perform *chalitzah* with Leah.

### Why Four Brothers?

The *Gemara* now presses a sharp question against the second explanation: *i hachi, tlosa nami* — if the concern is *assur levatel mitzvas yabmin*, why does the *mishnah* require four brothers? Three would suffice. With only three brothers and two sisters, if Levi performs *yibum* with Rachel, Leah is immediately left without any option — she becomes *achos ishto* to Levi and has no other brother to turn to. The prohibition is obvious even in the three-brother case.

### Zika Strength vs Mitzvah

The *Gemara* resolves this by invoking the principle of *lo mibaya ka'mar*: the three-brother case is the more obvious case and therefore need not be stated. The *chidush* of the four-brother case operates at a different level. When two brothers remain behind, there is reason to argue that the concern of *achos zekukaso* is attenuated — the *zika* is weaker when two brothers share it, since it is not exclusively Levi's *zika* nor exclusively Yehudah's. The *mishnah* therefore teaches that even with two remaining brothers, the *achos zekukaso* prohibition is operative. The alternative concern — *assur levatel mitzvas yabmin* — introduces a further *chidush* in the four-brother case: even though Yehudah is alive and presumably available, we nonetheless fear that he might die before performing *yibum* or *chalitzah*, leaving Leah stranded.

### One Death Fear

The *Gemara* then asks: if we fear Yehudah's death in the four-brother case, why not fear the deaths of both Yehudah and Yissachar in a five-brother case? The *Gemara* responds: *lemisa detrei lo chashinan* — we do not entertain the fear that two brothers will die simultaneously before any act of *yibum* or *chalitzah* can be performed. That possibility is too remote. We allow for the possibility of one death; two is beyond the scope of our concern. Thus in the four-brother scenario, where Leah depends on Yehudah alone, we do fear his death — but once two brothers stand between a woman and her potential *yibum*-less fate, we no longer worry.

## Three Sisters Case

### Three Sisters Setup

The *Gemara* now presents a new case: *shalosh achayos yevamos shenaplu lifnei shenei achim yevamim* — three sisters, all *yevamos*, who have fallen before two surviving brothers. To cast this concretely: three brothers — Reuven, Shimon, and Levi — each married one of three sisters, and all three have died. The two surviving brothers, Yissachar and Zevulun, now face three sisters — Rachel, Leah, and a third (call her Sarah) — all of whom stand in a relationship of *zika* to both of them. No woman is a genuine *ervah* to either brother, since each was married to a different man. But under the principle of *yesh zika*, any act of *yibum* would implicate *achos zekukaso*, since each woman is a sister of the others to whom the brother is also *zakuk*. The case is therefore a larger variant of *achos zekukaso*, and the question is how to distribute *chalitzah* between the two brothers and three women.

One might ask: why not simply allow Yissachar to perform *chalitzah* with all three women? As the *Gemara* will develop, the answer turns on a new principle — that not all *chalitzah* acts are of equal weight — and the distribution matters precisely because of differential strength in the *zika*.

### Distribution Pattern

The ruling, stated in the name of Rav via Rava bar Rav Huna, is as follows: one brother performs *chalitzah* with the first sister, the other brother performs *chalitzah* with the second sister, and the third sister — called the *emtza'it*, the "middle" one — requires *chalitzah* from both brothers. The term *emtza'it* is somewhat misleading: she is called "middle" not because she is the second to fall to *yibum*, but because she stands between both brothers in terms of their obligations. She has a claim on each of them and, as will be explained, requires *chalitzah* from each.

### Sequential Deaths

The *Gemara* refines the scenario. If all three brothers had died simultaneously, every sister would be *achos zekukaso* from the outset, and each woman would indeed require two *chalitzos*. But the case being discussed is one of sequential deaths — *denafelu zu achar zu*. When the first brother dies, one sister becomes subject to *yibum* with no complicating factor: there is no co-zekukah, the *zika* is complete, and the *chalitzah* performed by, say, Yissachar is fully valid. When the second brother dies, the next sister becomes available. At this point, from Yissachar's perspective, she is *achos chalutzaso* — the sister of the woman he just performed *chalitzah* with — making his *chalitzah* weaker. Zevulun, however, has no such problem: the first *chalitzah* was Yissachar's, not his. Zevulun's *chalitzah* with the second sister is therefore first-rate. By the time the third brother dies and Sarah becomes available, both brothers have each performed *chalitzah* with one of her sisters. She is *achos chalutzaso* to both Yissachar and to Zevulun. Any *chalitzah* she receives will be second-rate from either one of them. And this is precisely why she needs it from both.

### Chalitzah Pesulah

This brings us to a new and central principle that governs the remainder of the *daf*: the concept of *chalitzah pesulah*. The observation is this — when the *zika* between a brother and his *yevamah* is weakened, the *chalitzah* performed in that context is correspondingly weakened. A full, first-rate *chalitzah* by one brother frees a *yevamah* completely; all other brothers are discharged and she may go free. But a *chalitzah pesulah* — a second-rate *chalitzah* — does not accomplish this. It requires, instead, *chazor al kol ha'achim*: the woman must receive *chalitzah* from every surviving brother, each of whom must separately discharge his own *zika*.

## Chalitzah Pesulah

### Defining Pesulah

The principle of *chalitzah pesulah* thus emerges as the organizing concept for the cases that follow. When is a *chalitzah* considered *pesulah*? Rashi offers a general formulation: *chalitzah pesulah* is *chalitzah gerua*, a second-rate *chalitzah*, in any case where *yibum* was not possible — *she'im ratza leyabbem eino yachol*. If the brother could not have performed *yibum* with this woman, then the *chalitzah* he performs with her is by definition second-rate.

### Tosafos Refinement

Tosafos, however, sharpens this definition. Rashi's criterion is accurate as far as it goes, but it is somewhat overbroad. There are cases where *yibum* is impossible for reasons unrelated to any weakening of the *zika* — and Tosafos holds that such cases would not qualify as *chalitzah pesulah* in the operative sense. For *chalitzah pesulah* to apply, the specific reason *yibum* is impossible must be that the *zika* itself has been problematized or attenuated. It is not merely the impossibility of *yibum* that creates the category, but the particular mechanism: a *zika* that has been weakened, distorted, or displaced from its normative form.

### Examples of Weakening

Tosafos identifies three paradigmatic cases in which the *zika* is weakened in this operative sense, collected at the end of the relevant *dibur hamatchil*. The first is *achos zekukaso*: when among all the women to whom a brother is *zakuk*, one is the sister of another. She is not a genuine *ervah* — that would make her *achos ishto* and would generate full exemption — but she is a quasi-*ervah* through rabbinic extension. The *zika* to her is real, but it operates under a cloud, and any *chalitzah* performed with her is therefore *pesulah*. The second is *ba'alas haget*: if a brother gave the *yevamah* a *get* before performing *chalitzah*, the *get* has no Torah-level effect in this context — the woman is not his wife, and a *get* cannot dissolve the *zika* — but rabbinic law grants it enough weight to preclude *yibum*. Having now made *yibum* impossible, the subsequent *chalitzah* is second-rate. The third case is the inverse and is in some ways more striking: *ba'alas ma'amar*. Here the brother performed *ma'amar* — a rabbinic-level *kiddushin* with the *yevamah*, a formal act of partial acquisition that is the precursor to full *yibum* — thereby strengthening rather than weakening the apparent connection. Yet this too renders *chalitzah* second-rate. The dynamic is instructive: *chalitzah* is relevant to the middle zone of the *zika* relationship. Move the woman too far away — as with *achos zekukaso* or *ba'alas haget* — and *chalitzah* is less relevant because she is quasi-forbidden. Move her too close — as with *ma'amar* — and *chalitzah* is less relevant because she is quasi-his-wife. In either direction, the displacement from the normative *zika* weakens the *chalitzah*.

It should be noted that when *ma'amar* has been performed, the full procedure requires both a *get* (to undo the *ma'amar*) and *chalitzah* (to undo the underlying *zika*). But the *chalitzah* in that context is still regarded as second-rate because the woman's status has shifted from the classic *yevamah* who stands ready for *yibum* or *chalitzah* without prior complication.

### Multiple Chalitzos

The practical upshot of *chalitzah pesulah* is the rule of *chazor al kol ha'achim*: she must go around to all the brothers. Why? Because when a *chalitzah* is first-rate and complete, one brother's act fully extinguishes the entire nexus of *zika* — all brothers are discharged simultaneously, since the *zika* was a unified bond. But when the *chalitzah* is second-rate, it is only sufficient to remove the particular *zika* of the one brother who performed it. Each brother retains his own residual *zika*, and each must therefore act separately to discharge it. This explains the case of Sarah in the three-sisters scenario: since she is *achos chalutzaso* to both Yissachar and Zevulun, any *chalitzah* she receives is *pesulah*, and she must therefore obtain *chalitzah* from each of them individually.

## Rav vs Shmuel

### Rav Position

The *Gemara* raises an immediate difficulty: this entire analysis — *achos zekukaso*, *chalitzah pesulah*, *chazor al kol ha'achim* — is predicated on the position of *yesh zika*. But the ruling in question was stated by Rav, and Rav is on record elsewhere as holding *ein zika*. The *Gemara* resolves this: Rav was speaking *ledivrei ha'omer yesh zika* — he was articulating the *halacha* according to the position that holds *yesh zika*, not necessarily expressing his own view. Within that framework, the ruling stands: *chalitzah pesulah* requires *chalitzah* from all the brothers.

### Shmuel Position

Shmuel disagrees. His position is stated concisely: *echad choletz lekullan* — one brother may perform *chalitzah* for all of them. Shmuel apparently does not accept the *chazor al kol ha'achim* requirement. Even where a *chalitzah* would be second-rate — because the woman is *achos chalutzaso* — Shmuel maintains that one brother's *chalitzah* suffices, and the woman need not seek *chalitzah* from the remaining brothers.

---

## Rav vs Shmuel (II)

### Achos vs Tzaros

The *Gemara* now presses Shmuel's position from a different direction. We know from elsewhere on 26a that Shmuel himself holds *chalitzah ma'alya ba'inan* — that a first-rate *chalitzah* is required. Given this, there is a question about Shmuel's permitting one brother to perform *chalitzah* with all three sisters: even setting aside the question of *how many* brothers must participate, shouldn't we at least insist that the *better* *chalitzah* be used when available?

To see why this matters, consider the following case — stated by Shmuel on 26a — involving two sets of women. Yissachar is married to Rachel, and Rachel has a co-wife, Devorah. Zevulun is married to Leah, and Leah has a co-wife, Naomi. Both brothers die. Reuven is the sole surviving brother. All four women stand before him. He cannot perform *yibum* with Rachel, because Rachel is *achos zekukaso* — she is the sister of Leah, to whom he is also *zakuk*. He cannot perform *yibum* with Leah for the same reason. What about Devorah? Devorah is a co-wife of Rachel, who is herself a quasi-*ervah* by virtue of *achos zekukaso*. Devorah is therefore, in the language of the *Gemara*, *tzaras achos zekukaso* — a co-wife of a quasi-*ervah*. This is one degree removed from the problem, but Reuven still cannot perform *yibum* with her. Nevertheless, Reuven's relationship with Devorah is of a different character than his relationship with Rachel: his connection with Devorah is less problematized, and therefore a *chalitzah* with Devorah is stronger than a *chalitzah* with Rachel.

Shmuel's ruling: *chalatz l'achayos lo nifteru tzaros* — if Reuven performs *chalitzah* with the sisters (Rachel or Leah), the co-wives (Devorah, Naomi) are not freed. But *chalatz letzaros nipteru achayos* — if he performs *chalitzah* with the co-wives, the sisters are freed. This demonstrates that Shmuel does recognize a hierarchy of *chalitzah* quality, and that the stronger *chalitzah* — with the co-wife — is more powerful than the weaker one. The same principle applies to *ba'alas haget* and *ba'alas ma'amar*: in each case, *chalitz leba'alas haget* (or *ma'amar*) does not free her co-wife, but *chalatz letzarah* frees the *ba'alas haget* (or *ma'amar*).

### Shmuel's Logic

Given this, the *Gemara* objects: if Shmuel holds that a first-rate *chalitzah* is always preferable — indeed, necessary to free a co-wife — why does he permit one brother to perform all three *chalitzos* in the three-sisters case, including the weaker ones with the sisters who are *achos chalutzaso*? When Shimon's *chalitzah* with the second sister would be fully first-rate, why allow Reuven to do a second-rate *chalitzah* instead?

## Co-wives Case

### Co-wives Setup

To clarify the stakes of this debate, the *Gemara* works through the case involving co-wives directly. Here the scenario is as described above: two brothers married to two pairs of women (each wife having a co-wife), with only one brother surviving. All four women are now before Reuven.

### Pesulah Doesn't Free

Shmuel's ruling establishes a clear principle: a second-rate *chalitzah* — one performed in a context where the *zika* is weakened — lacks the force to free a co-wife. If Reuven performs *chalitzah* with Rachel, who is *achos zekukaso*, Devorah remains bound. The *chalitzah pesulah* is effective for Rachel herself — she is freed from the obligation — but it cannot discharge the obligation of a co-wife, because the co-wife's release depends on the *chalitzah* being fully valid.

### First-Rate Required

The inverse, however, holds: a first-rate *chalitzah* with Devorah — whose relationship to Reuven, while still not permitting *yibum*, is less burdened — does free Rachel. The logic is that Reuven's *zika* to Devorah, although precluded from *yibum* by virtue of her being *tzaras achos zekukaso*, is not as deeply problematized as his *zika* to Rachel. It is therefore a stronger act of *chalitzah*, and a stronger *chalitzah* has the power to discharge co-wives as well. This establishes that when co-wives are present, a first-rate *chalitzah* is not merely preferable but necessary to achieve their release.

## Chalitzah Pesulah (II)

### Strength Weakens

There is a striking irony embedded in the case of *ba'alas ma'amar* that is worth pausing on. When a brother performs *ma'amar* with his *yevamah*, he is strengthening the *zika* — he is moving closer to full *yibum*, not away from it. Yet this very strengthening renders the subsequent *chalitzah* weaker. The explanation, as noted above, is that *chalitzah* is calibrated to the specific relational zone of the *zika*: the woman stands in a liminal status, available for *yibum* but not yet his wife, available for *chalitzah* but not an *ervah*. Any movement from that liminal zone — whether toward greater distance (as in *achos zekukaso* or *ba'alas haget*) or toward greater closeness (as in *ma'amar*) — displaces the act of *chalitzah* from its normative context and weakens it. The *chalitzah* in a *ma'amar* case is therefore *pesulah*, even though the *zika* was, in one sense, intensified. The result is that both weakening and strengthening the *zika* produce the same outcome: a *chalitzah pesulah* that requires *chazor al kol ha'achim*.

## Shmuel Reread

### Challenge to Order

Returning now to the case of Reuven and the four women — Rachel, Leah, Devorah, and Naomi — the *Gemara* presses a deeper question. The *Gemara* had accepted that performing *chalitzah* with Devorah is preferable to doing so with Rachel, because Devorah is *tzaras achos zekukaso* (one degree removed) rather than *achos zekukaso* directly. But the *Gemara* now asks: if one holds *yesh zika*, doesn't this make Devorah *tzaras* a quasi-*ervah*? And if so, isn't she just as problematic in her own way as Rachel? Why is the *chalitzah* with Devorah considered stronger?

This challenge threatens the entire framework of Shmuel's ruling. If both Rachel and Devorah are problematic — one directly as *achos zekukaso* and the other as *tzaras achos zekukaso* — on what basis do we say that the *chalitzah* with Devorah is first-rate and gets Rachel off the hook, while the *chalitzah* with Rachel is second-rate and does not free Devorah?

The *Gemara* initially responds by suggesting that Shmuel holds *ein zika*. But this immediately generates a new difficulty: if there is no *zika*, then Rachel is not *achos zekukaso* at all, so why can't Reuven simply perform *yibum* with her? And if all four women stand on equal footing before him, what is the basis for distinguishing the *chalitzah* performed with Rachel from the *chalitzah* performed with Devorah?

### Beginning vs Ending

The *Gemara* therefore undertakes a radical re-reading of Shmuel's ruling. Rather than comparing Rachel and Devorah as two fixed alternatives and asking which is better, the *Gemara* proposes that Shmuel is addressing a sequential question: *hitchil velo hitchil* — the question of how one begins the process and how one ends it.

### Begin Sisters

If Reuven begins with the sisters — that is, he performs *chalitzah* first with Leah (one of the sisters) — then Leah becomes his *chalutzah*. Rachel is now *achos chalutzaso*. At this point, Naomi (Leah's co-wife) is *tzaras krovat chalutzaso* — the co-wife of a relative of his *chalutzah*. The *mishnah* teaches that a man is *assur* in *tzaras krovat chalutzaso*. Consequently, if Reuven started with Leah, he may not finish with Naomi, because doing so would implicate *tzaras krovat chalutzaso*. Doing *chalitzah* with Naomi would not free Rachel either, because she is independently problematic. He is therefore stuck and must still do *chalitzah* with Rachel — but that *chalitzah* will not get Naomi off the hook.

### Begin Co-wives

However, if Reuven begins with the co-wives — performing *chalitzah* first with Devorah — then the situation is entirely different. Devorah is now his *chalutzah*, and Rachel is not *achos chalutzaso* because no *chalitzah* has yet been performed with any sister. At this stage, the *mishnah* teaches *mutar adam bekrovat tzaras chalitzaso* — a man is permitted to the relative of his *chalutzah*'s co-wife. This is the mirror image of the previous case and is not considered problematic. With Devorah as his *chalutzah*, he may proceed with either Rachel or Naomi. If he does *chalitzah* with Rachel, it will free Naomi; if he does it with Naomi, it will free Rachel. By beginning with the co-wife, both options remain open, and first-rate *chalitzah* is available for each.

## Shmuel Reread (II)

### Rav Ashi

The *Gemara* ultimately abandons the radical re-reading and returns to the simpler original interpretation of Shmuel, through the intervention of Rav Ashi.

---

## Rav Ashi Resolution

### Zika Insufficient

Rav Ashi states: *leolam kidekamris* — Shmuel means exactly what we originally thought he meant. The *chalitzah* with Devorah is better than the *chalitzah* with Rachel, even within the framework of *yesh zika*. The key principle is: *umishum delo alima zika lishveiye litzarah ke'erva* — the *zika* is not strong enough to render the co-wife fully equivalent to an *ervah*. The reasoning proceeds as follows: Rachel, through *achos zekukaso*, is a quasi-*ervah* — a rabbinic extension of the prohibition of *achos isha*. She is not a genuine Torah-level *ervah*, but she is treated as one rabbinically. Normally, in a case of full *ervah*, the principle of *tzaras ervah* would apply — the co-wife of an *ervah* is also exempt from *yibum* and *chalitzah*. But in this case, since Rachel herself is only a quasi-*ervah* through rabbinic extension, the derivative principle — that her co-wife Devorah is *tzaras ervah* — does not carry the same weight. The *zika* generates the *achos zekukaso* prohibition, but it is not strong enough to then activate, in full, the *tzaras ervah* mechanism for Devorah.

### Co-wife Better

The practical consequence is that Devorah, although she cannot be taken in *yibum*, stands in a better position than Rachel for purposes of *chalitzah*. Rachel's *zika* is directly burdened by *achos zekukaso*, making her *chalitzah* second-rate. Devorah's *zika*, while indirectly affected, is not burdened to the same degree — because the chain of derivation (from *achos zekukaso* to *tzaras achos zekukaso*) attenuates the problem. She is, in the terms introduced earlier, perhaps a grade B rather than grade A, while Rachel is grade C. The *chalitzah* with Devorah is therefore relatively first-rate — stronger than the *chalitzah* with Rachel — and suffices to free Rachel.

### Mishna Alignment

The *Gemara* confirms that this understanding is in line with a *baraita*: *tanya kevasei deRav Ashi* — it was taught in accordance with Rav Ashi. The *baraita* states: *chalatz l'achayos lo nifteru tzaros, chalatz letzaros nifteru achayos* — performing *chalitzah* with the sisters does not free the co-wives, but performing *chalitzah* with the co-wives does free the sisters. And the reason given is precisely: *mishum dekasavar yesh zika velo alumi zika lishveiye litzara ke'erva* — because the *zika* exists but is insufficient to make the co-wife a full *ervah*. This is exactly what Rav Ashi said. The *baraita* resolves the debate in Rav Ashi's favor.

## Beis Shammai View

### Beis Shammai

Rabbi Abba bar Mamul offers an alternative account of why performing *chalitzah* with the co-wife is preferable. Rather than invoking the subtlety of *zika* strength, he proposes that Shmuel's ruling reflects the position of Beis Shammai. The *mishnah* elsewhere records: *Beis Shammai matirin hatzaros leachim* — Beis Shammai permit the co-wives to the brothers. According to Beis Shammai, the principle of *tzaras ervah* does not apply at all. Co-wives of an *ervah* are not exempt from *yibum*; they are fully obligated. On this view, Devorah is not problematic in any sense — she is simply another *yevamah* who may be taken in *yibum*. There is therefore no question of first-rate versus second-rate *chalitzah* with her; she stands in a fully normal *chalitzah* relationship with Reuven.

### Objection

The *Gemara* immediately objects: if Beis Shammai permits *yibum* with co-wives, and Shmuel is following Beis Shammai, then why is Shmuel requiring *chalitzah* with Devorah at all? Let Reuven perform *yibum* with her outright.

### Compromise

The *Gemara* responds by invoking the position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, who proposed: *bo'u unetaken lahem latzaros sheyihyu choltzos velo mityabmos* — let us enact for the co-wives that they perform *chalitzah* and not *yibum*. This ruling is a compromise designed to honor both Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel. Beis Hillel holds that co-wives of an *ervah* are exempt even from *chalitzah*; Beis Shammai holds that they are fully obligated and may even perform *yibum*. The enactment of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri requires *chalitzah* but forbids *yibum* — satisfying the concern of Beis Hillel (who would object to *yibum*) while acknowledging the Beis Shammai position that some obligation exists. The *Gemara* notes that the full ratification of this ruling was not completed in its time — *lo hispiku ligmor et hadavar* — but Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak replies that it was subsequently enacted. The position of Rabbi Abba bar Mamul is thus that Shmuel's ruling reflects this compromise: operating within a Beis Shammai framework modified by the later enactment requiring *chalitzah* rather than *yibum*.
