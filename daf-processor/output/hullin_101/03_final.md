# Hullin 101 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gid Hanasheh & Non-Kosher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נוֹהֵג בִּטְהוֹרָה, וְאֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג בִּטְמֵאָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף בִּטְמֵאָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וַהֲלֹא מִבְּנֵי יַעֲקֹב נֶאֱסַר גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה, וַעֲדַיִין בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה מוּתֶּרֶת לָהֶן? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: בְּסִינַי נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּכְתַּב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The prohibition of eating the sciatic nerve **applies to a kosher** animal **and does not apply to a non-kosher** animal. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** It applies **even to a non-kosher** animal. **Rabbi Yehuda said** in explanation: **Wasn't the sciatic nerve forbidden for the children of Jacob,** as it is written: "Therefore the children of Israel eat not the sciatic nerve" (Genesis 32:33), **yet** the meat of **a non-kosher animal was still permitted to them?** Since the sciatic nerve of non-kosher animals became forbidden at that time, it remains forbidden now. The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Yehuda: The prohibition **was stated in Sinai, but** it **was written in its place,** in the battle of Jacob and the angel despite the fact that the prohibition did not take effect then.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Position

The daf opens with the position of Rabbi Yehuda in the Mishnah, who holds that *gid hanasheh* applies to non-kosher animals. His argument is partly historical: since the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* predates the giving of the Torah, it applied already to the children of Yaakov, which would include all animals they ate—kosher and non-kosher alike.

### Rambam's Approach

The Rabbis in the Mishnah dispute this, arguing that *bnei Yisrael* refers to the Israelites—a national designation that did not exist before Har Sinai—and not to the children of Yaakov. Consistent with this, the Rambam holds emphatically that nothing binding upon us today derives from pre-Torah practice. On this reading, the verse was given at Har Sinai but was inserted into the narrative of Yaakov's struggle with the angel in order to provide historical context—to tell us *why* the prohibition exists—rather than to indicate that it was operative at that earlier moment.

### Issur Chal Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר? וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל תְּהֵא נִבְלַת עוֹף טָמֵא מְטַמְּאָה בְּגָדִים בְּבֵית הַבְּלִיעָה?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Yehuda hold** that **a prohibition takes effect where** another **prohibition** already exists? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** One **might** have thought that **the carcass of a non-kosher bird renders** the **garments** of one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat,** similar to the carcass of a kosher bird.

The Gemara raises a separate concern: even setting aside the historical question, how does Rabbi Yehuda avoid the problem of *issur chal al issur*—one prohibition taking effect on top of a pre-existing one? If we eat the *gid hanasheh* of a camel, the prohibition of eating camel surely pre-exists; how can the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* then take effect upon it?

### Timing of Prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״נְבֵלָה וּטְרֵפָה לֹא יֹאכַל לְטׇמְאָה בָהּ״ – מִי שֶׁאִיסּוּרוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תֹּאכַל נְבֵלָה״, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵין אִיסּוּרוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תֹּאכַל נְבֵלָה״ אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תֹּאכַל טְמֵאָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore **the verse states** concerning the impurity of carcasses of birds: **"A carcass, or that which is torn of animals, he shall not eat to defile himself with it"** (Leviticus 22:8). The verse indicates that only **those** birds **that are forbidden** specifically **due to** the prohibition: **You shall not eat** of **a carcass,** i.e., kosher birds that died without ritual slaughter, cause impurity in this manner. This serves to **exclude** any bird **that is not forbidden due to** the prohibition: **You shall not eat** of **a carcass,** but **rather due to** the prohibition: **You shall not eat a non-kosher** bird. This indicates that according to Rabbi Yehuda, the prohibition of eating a carcass does not take effect with regard to a non-kosher bird, because it is already subject to a different prohibition.

The Gemara's answer exploits the biological fact that the *gid* only forms in the fetus some weeks into gestation. Thus it is already forbidden as part of a non-kosher animal before the *gid* as such even exists. The Gemara then repurposes Rabbi Yehuda's historical claim—originally a point about when the prohibition began—to resolve this halachic concern.

### Bnei Noach Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, קָסָבַר אֵין בְּגִידִין בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם, וּבִטְמֵאָה נָמֵי אִיסּוּר גִּיד אִיכָּא, אִיסּוּר טוּמְאָה לֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues with its question: **And if you would say** that Rabbi Yehuda **holds** that sciatic **nerves do not impart flavor,** i.e., they do not have flavor and therefore are not classified as food, **and even with regard to a non-kosher** animal it is prohibited to eat the sciatic nerve because the general **prohibition** of eating **a sciatic nerve applies,** whereas the **prohibition** of eating **non-kosher** animals **does not** apply because the sciatic nerve is not considered food, this is untenable.

The reframing works as follows: Rabbi Yehuda's line in the Mishnah, which speaks of the prohibition applying to *bnei Yaakov*, is now understood (or at least suggested) to refer to *bnei Noach*—non-Jews as well. If *gid hanasheh* has a broader scope, applying even to non-Jews, then it constitutes an *issur kolel*, a prohibition with wider reach, which is one of the recognized exceptions to the rule that one prohibition cannot take effect upon another. Because the prohibition applies to a larger group of people, it can take effect even on top of a pre-existing prohibition.

### Lashes Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֵין בְּגִידִים בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם? וְהָתַנְיָא: הָאוֹכֵל גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה שֶׁל בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב שְׁתַּיִם, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And does Rabbi Yehuda hold** that sciatic **nerves do not impart flavor? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who eats the sciatic nerve of a non-kosher animal, Rabbi Yehuda deems** him **liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes, one for eating the sciatic nerve and one for eating the meat of a non-kosher animal, **and Rabbi Shimon exempts** him entirely from lashes. This indicates that Rabbi Yehuda holds that the sciatic nerve does have flavor.

According to Rabbi Yehuda, one who eats the *gid hanasheh* of a camel therefore receives two sets of lashes—one for eating camel and one for eating *gid hanasheh*. Rabbi Shimon, however, exempts entirely. And that is puzzling: even if one accepts that *issur chal al issur* prevents the *gid hanasheh* prohibition from taking effect, at a minimum one should receive lashes for eating camel. How does Rabbi Shimon arrive at zero?

## Rabbi Shimon Zero

### Dilemma Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם, קָסָבַר יֵשׁ בְּגִידִין בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם, וְקָסָבַר נוֹהֵג בַּשְּׁלִיל, דְּאִיסּוּר גִּיד וְאִיסּוּר טוּמְאָה בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָאָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually** Rabbi Yehuda **holds** that sciatic **nerves do impart flavor, and he** also **holds** that the prohibition of eating the sciatic nerve **applies to** the sciatic nerve of an animal **fetus.** Consequently, although Rabbi Yehuda holds that a prohibition does not take effect where another already exists, one who eats the sciatic nerve of a non-kosher animal is flogged twice, **because the prohibition of** eating a sciatic **nerve and the prohibition of** eating meat from **a non-kosher** animal **come** into effect **at the same time.**

The Gemara poses the dilemma directly: *mah nafshach*—make up your mind. If Rabbi Shimon holds that one prohibition does take effect upon another (*issur chal al issur*), he should agree with Rabbi Yehuda and impose two sets of lashes. If he holds that one prohibition does not take effect upon another, then at least the pre-existing prohibition—the prohibition of eating a non-kosher animal, which preceded the *gid*—should generate lashes. How does he arrive at zero?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ נוֹהֵג בַּשְּׁלִיל? וְהָתְנַן: נוֹהֵג בַּשְּׁלִיל, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג בַּשְּׁלִיל, וְחֶלְבּוֹ מוּתָּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this answer: **And can you say** that Rabbi Yehuda holds that the prohibition of eating a sciatic nerve **applies to** the sciatic nerve of **a fetus? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (89b): The prohibition **applies to** a late-term **fetus** in the womb. **Rabbi Yehuda says: It does not apply to a fetus, and** similarly the **fat** of a fetus **is permitted?**

The Gemara entertains one possible resolution: perhaps Rabbi Shimon holds that *gidim* have no taste (*ein begidim noten ta'am*). If the *gid* has no taste, it would not be defined as meat, and one would not receive lashes for eating camel on account of it. But this only explains why there are no lashes for camel; it does not explain why there are no lashes for *gid hanasheh* itself. After all, the rule that *gid* has no taste is relevant to questions of mixture—whether cooking a *gid* in meat renders the meat forbidden—but it does not by itself exempt one from the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* on a kosher animal. If anything, one freed from liability for camel should now be liable for *gid hanasheh*. The question remains.

### No Taste Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי גַּבֵּי טְהוֹרָה, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״כֹּל בַּבְּהֵמָה תֹּאכֵלוּ״, אֲבָל בִּטְמֵאָה נוֹהֵג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This statement,** that the prohibition of eating the sciatic nerve does not apply to a fetus, is **with regard to a kosher** species of animal, **because the Merciful One stated** in the Torah: "And **every** animal that divides the hoof, and has the hoof wholly cloven in two, and chews the cud, **among the animals [*babehema*],** that **you may eat"** (Deuteronomy 14:6). The term *babehema* may also be translated as: Inside the animals, indicating that anything inside a kosher animal when it is slaughtered is permitted for consumption, including all parts of a fetus. **But with regard to a non-kosher** species of animal the prohibition **applies.**

The Gemara continues to probe whether the absence of taste in *gidim* can account for the zero-lash result. As noted, this principle explains why one does not transgress for eating camel—the *gid* is not considered meat in that context—but it does not automatically discharge the *gid hanasheh* prohibition itself. The two directions of the dilemma seem to cancel each other out, leaving Rabbi Shimon's zero-lash position unexplained on purely logical grounds.

### Rava's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָאָתוּ? וְהָתְנַן: עַל אֵלּוּ טוּמְאוֹת הַנָּזִיר מְגַלֵּחַ, עַל הַמֵּת וְעַל כְּזַיִת מִן הַמֵּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the assertion that the prohibitions of eating the sciatic nerve and of eating non-kosher meat take effect at the same time. **And can you say** that **they both come** into effect **at the same time? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Nazir* 49b): **A nazirite shaves for** having become impure from **these** following sources of **ritual impurity: For** having become impure with impurity imparted by **a corpse; and for** impurity imparted by **an olive-bulk of a corpse.**

Rava resolves the difficulty. He says: indeed, Rabbi Shimon holds that *gidim* have no taste, and that is why there are no lashes for eating camel. But the reason there are also no lashes for *gid hanasheh* has nothing to do with *issur chal al issur*. Rather, it follows from the verse itself.

### Exclusion Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיסּוּר טוּמְאָה קָדֵים, אָתֵי אִיסּוּר גִּיד חָיֵיל עֲלֵיהּ, שֶׁכֵּן אִיסּוּרוֹ נוֹהֵג בִּבְנֵי נֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Even though the prohibition** of eating **non-kosher** meat takes effect **prior** to the prohibition of eating the sciatic nerve, the **prohibition of** the sciatic **nerve** also **comes and takes effect upon** a non-kosher animal **because** this **prohibition applies to descendants of Noah,** i.e., to gentiles. Since the prohibition of eating the sciatic nerve adds an additional stringency that did not exist with regard to non-kosher meat, it takes effect even though there was an already existing prohibition.

The Torah states: *al ken lo yochlu bnei Yisrael et gid hanasheh*—"therefore the children of Israel shall not eat the *gid hanasheh*." Rava reads this verse as implicitly limiting its own scope. The prohibition of *gid hanasheh* applies to animals *whose meat you are otherwise permitted to eat*. The verse defines the category: an animal from which the *gid* is forbidden is one from which the meat is permitted. This creates a reciprocal exclusion: if the meat of an animal is forbidden, the *gid* of that animal is outside the scope of the *gid hanasheh* prohibition entirely.

### No Transgression

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וַהֲלֹא מִבְּנֵי יַעֲקֹב נֶאֱסַר גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה, וַעֲדַיִין בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה מוּתֶּרֶת לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** According to this answer, the language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches: Rabbi Yehuda said: Wasn't the sciatic nerve forbidden for the children of Jacob,** as it is written: "Therefore the children of Israel eat not the sciatic nerve" (Genesis 32:33), **yet** the meat of **a non-kosher animal was still permitted to them?** This indicates that the basis of Rabbi Yehuda's opinion is the fact that the sciatic nerve was forbidden to the children of Jacob, who had the status of descendants of Noah.

It follows that if one eats the *gid hanasheh* of a camel, one commits no transgression whatsoever—not for camel, because the *gid* has no taste and is thus not considered meat in the relevant sense, and not for *gid hanasheh*, because the Torah's prohibition of *gid hanasheh* simply does not apply to animals whose meat is forbidden. This is the source of Rabbi Shimon's position. It is worth noting the irony: the verse that establishes the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* simultaneously, by implication, limits its application.

## Taste & Food Status

### Gid as Meat?

This raises a conceptual question: if the *gid* has no taste, in what sense is it considered food at all? And conversely, if it is not considered food in the taste-based sense, why should it be forbidden under *gid hanasheh* even on kosher animals?

### Yotze Min HaTameh

The answer requires distinguishing between different bases for a prohibition. There is a principle—*yotze min hatameh tameh*—that something which comes from a non-kosher source is itself forbidden, even if it is not itself meat. Milk from a non-kosher animal, for example, is forbidden not because it is meat but because of this principle. So the absence of taste does not automatically exempt something from being prohibited.

### Hoof Example

However, this principle does not extend without limit. Consider the hoof of a camel. A hoof is hard, indigestible, and utterly devoid of taste. Eating it would not constitute a violation of *yotze min hatameh*, because it is not considered food at all. It is not a matter merely of having no taste; it is that the combination of physical properties—hardness, indigestibility, absence of taste—removes it entirely from the category of *ma'aseh achilah*, of eating as the Torah contemplates it. One who chews on a rock has not "eaten" in any legally cognizable sense.

### Taste & Properties

The *gid*, being a sinew, shares some of these properties. It is physically unlike meat; it does not dissolve or digest in the ordinary way. In this physical respect, it resembles a bone without marrow. Now, if a bone has no marrow but somehow retained some taste, one might consider whether it constitutes food. But if it has no taste and no ordinary food-like physical properties, it is simply not food—like eating a rock. The *gid*'s lack of taste, combined with its nature as a sinew, is what removes it from the category of non-kosher meat. Taste, in this analysis, functions as a decisive factor given the *gid*'s physical reality: it is the presence or absence of taste that determines whether a substance with unusual physical properties crosses the threshold into "food."

### Gelatin/Rennet

These principles bear directly on contemporary questions such as the status of gelatin derived from pig's bones and rennet derived from non-kosher animals. With respect to gelatin specifically, two distinct arguments arise. First, there is a question whether the industrial transformation of bones into gelatin is so complete that the product no longer retains its original identity at all. Second, even if it does retain some identity, the question becomes whether pig's bones—stripped of marrow and converted into an essentially tasteless, physically altered substance—ever qualified as food in the first instance. The analysis of the *gid*'s status, and of the camel's hoof, maps directly onto this debate.

## Meir Neveila Case

### Setup: Healthy→Dead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: הָאוֹכֵל גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה שֶׁל בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב שְׁתַּיִם,
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to **the** matter **itself** cited above. With regard to **one who eats the sciatic nerve of a non-kosher animal, Rabbi Yehuda deems** him **liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes: One for eating the sciatic nerve and one for eating the meat of a non-kosher animal;

The Gemara now presents a related scenario involving a kosher animal. A healthy, properly kosher animal has a *gid hanasheh*, which is forbidden. It then dies without proper *shechita* and becomes *neveilah*. The *gid hanasheh* prohibition pre-existed; the *neveilah* prohibition came later. Does the *neveilah* prohibition take effect on top of the *gid hanasheh* prohibition? This is again the question of *issur chal al issur*.

### Meir: Two Lashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **and Rabbi Shimon exempts** him entirely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר – לִיחַיַּיב נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם גִּיד, אִי אֵין אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר – לִיחַיַּיב מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה דִּקְדֵים, וְאִי אֵין בְּגִידִין בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם – לִיחַיַּיב מִשּׁוּם גִּיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** according to **Rabbi Shimon, whichever way you** look at it, it is difficult. **If a prohibition takes effect where** another **prohibition** already exists, **let** Rabbi Shimon **deem** one **liable** for eating non-kosher meat and **also due to** the prohibition of eating the sciatic **nerve.** Conversely, **if a prohibition does not take effect** where another **prohibition** already exists, **let** Rabbi Shimon **deem** one **liable due to the** prohibition of eating meat from **a non-kosher** species, **which preceded** the prohibition of the sciatic nerve. **And if** Rabbi Shimon holds that sciatic **nerves do not impart flavor,** and therefore the prohibition of eating non-kosher meat does not apply, **let** him **deem** one **liable due to** the prohibition of eating the sciatic **nerve.**

Rabbi Meir holds that one who eats the *gid hanasheh* of such an animal receives two sets of lashes—one for *gid hanasheh* and one for *neveilah*. In his view, the *neveilah* prohibition does take effect on top of the pre-existing *gid hanasheh* prohibition.

### Sages: One Lash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר אֵין בְּגִידִים בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם, וְשָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״עַל כֵּן לֹא יֹאכְלוּ בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה״, מִי שֶׁגִּידוֹ אָסוּר וּבְשָׂרוֹ מוּתָּר, יָצְתָה זוֹ שֶׁגִּידוֹ אָסוּר וּבְשָׂרוֹ אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in response: **Actually** Rabbi Shimon **holds** that sciatic **nerves do not impart flavor,** and therefore they are not subject to the prohibition of eating non-kosher meat. **And** the reason the prohibition of eating the sciatic nerves does not apply to non-kosher animals is that **it is different there, because the verse states: "Therefore the children of Israel eat not the sciatic nerve"** (Genesis 32:33). This teaches that the prohibition applies only to a species **whose** sciatic **nerve is forbidden but whose meat is permitted,** and **excludes this** case of a non-kosher animal, **whose** sciatic **nerve** would be **forbidden and whose meat** would also be **forbidden.**

The Sages disagree. They hold *ein issur chal al issur*—one prohibition does not take effect on another—and therefore only one set of lashes applies, presumably for *gid hanasheh*, which was the first prohibition. The *neveilah* prohibition did not take additional hold on the *gid*.

### Olah/Niskal Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: הָאוֹכֵל גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה שֶׁל נְבֵלָה – רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב שְׁתַּיִם, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** § Having discussed the status of the sciatic nerve of a non-kosher animal, the Gemara addresses the status of the sciatic nerve of a kosher animal that did not undergo a proper ritual slaughter. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **one who eats the sciatic nerve of an unslaughtered carcass, Rabbi Meir deems** him **liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes, **and the Rabbis say: He is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes.

However, the Sages concede to Rabbi Meir in a different case. If the animal was initially sanctified as an *olah* (a burnt offering) or if it gored and killed a person and thereby acquired the status of *shor haniskal* (an ox condemned to be stoned), they agree that two sets of lashes apply. In these cases, even the Sages say the second prohibition takes effect on top of the *gid hanasheh* prohibition.

### Asur BeHana'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּאוֹכֵל גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה שֶׁל עוֹלָה וְשֶׁל שׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל, שֶׁחַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Meir** in a case **where one eats the sciatic nerve of a burnt offering or of an ox that is stoned that he is liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes. The prohibitions concerning a burnt offering and an ox that is stoned are more severe than that of the sciatic nerve, in that it is forbidden to derive any benefit from them, whereas the sciatic nerve is merely forbidden for consumption. Consequently, these prohibitions take effect even with regard to the sciatic nerve, despite the fact that the sciatic nerve was already forbidden before the animal was consecrated or before it gored a person and became liable to be stoned.

What distinguishes the *olah* and *shor haniskal* from plain *neveilah*? Both *olah* and *shor haniskal* are *asur beHana'ah*—forbidden not merely to be eaten but forbidden in any form of benefit. The ordinary *neveilah* prohibition is a prohibition on eating; the prohibition of *olah* and *shor haniskal* extends to deriving any benefit whatsoever. This added dimension—a prohibition that is not merely broader in scope but weightier in character—is what, in the Sages' view, allows the second prohibition to take hold.

## Kolel & Mosif

### Two Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן הַאי תַּנָּא, דִּבְאִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל – אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר לֵית לֵיהּ, אִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל בְּאִיסּוּר חָמוּר – אִית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And who is this *tanna*** who **does not hold that in** the case of a more **inclusive prohibition,** the **prohibition takes effect where** another **prohibition** already exists, and consequently, according to his opinion the prohibition of eating an unslaughtered animal, which applies to the entire animal, does not take effect with regard to the sciatic nerve. Yet, **he** does **hold** that where the second prohibition is both a more **inclusive prohibition** and a more **stringent prohibition,** it does take effect, and therefore the prohibition of eating a burnt offering or an ox that is stoned does take effect with regard to the sciatic nerve.

The Gemara in Kerisot recognizes two categories of exceptions to the general rule that one prohibition does not take effect upon a pre-existing one: *issur kolel* and *issur mosif*.

### Kolel Defined

*Issur kolel* refers to a prohibition whose scope of affected objects or people is larger than that of the pre-existing prohibition. Because the new prohibition takes effect on other things or people not previously covered, it is considered to "bring along" the original item as well, and thus takes effect even on what was already forbidden.

### Neveila Example

*Neveilah* is a classic illustration of *issur kolel* in the dimension of objects. Before the animal died, only the *gid* was forbidden. When it became *neveilah*, every part of the animal—all the meat—became forbidden. Since the *neveilah* prohibition takes effect on the meat, it also takes effect on the *gid*. The *gid* is "brought along" by virtue of the prohibition's larger reach. This is Rabbi Meir's logic: because *neveilah* is an *issur kolel* that applies to the meat, it can also apply to the *gid*, and therefore double liability obtains.

An *issur kolel* in the dimension of persons would arise when the new prohibition applies to a wider group of people than were previously bound. For example, if a prohibition that previously applied only to Jews now also applied to *bnei Noach*, the wider personal scope would constitute an *issur kolel*.

### Mosif Defined

*Issur mosif* is more contested. The term suggests a prohibition that "adds" something—but adds what, exactly? The Gemara uses the related term *issur chamur* (a weightier prohibition), and Rashi throughout this sugya conflates the two, treating a more weighty prohibition as equivalent to an *issur mosif*. In this reading, if the new prohibition is more severe than the pre-existing one, it can take effect.

### Chamur Factor

The example at hand is again the *olah* and *shor haniskal*. These impose not merely a prohibition on eating (*issur achilah*) but a prohibition on benefit (*issur hana'ah*). The new prohibition is thus weightier—*chamur*—than what pre-existed.

### Rashi vs. Rishonim

Rashi's conflation of *issur mosif* with *issur chamur* is not accepted by most Rishonim. They maintain that *issur chamur* is a distinct and secondary consideration—an added factor—and that the primary mechanism permitting *issur chal al issur* is always a question of scope, whether *kolel* or *mosif*. The precise distinction between *issur kolel* (larger scope of objects or persons) and *issur mosif* (larger scope of the prohibition itself) is nuanced and debated among the Rishonim, and *issur mosif* as an independent category does not arise explicitly in our immediate sugya. For present purposes, the operative distinction is this: *issur kolel* is clear—a wider reach over objects or people—while *issur chamur*, a weightier prohibition, functions as an added factor that may augment the force of *issur kolel* but does not alone suffice.

## Neveila Analysis

### Meir's Kolel Logic

With that framework in place, we can now read Rabbi Meir's position precisely. He holds that *neveilah* is an *issur kolel*—it applies to the meat of the animal, which was not previously forbidden—and therefore it takes effect even on the *gid hanasheh*, which was already forbidden. The logic of *kolel* is that because the prohibition reaches new territory (the meat), it is not merely piling onto a pre-existing prohibition; it is a genuinely broader prohibition that sweeps in everything, including what was already forbidden. Two lashes follow.

### Sages' Position

The Sages reject this. *Issur kolel* alone is not sufficient for *issur chal al issur* to operate. However, when the second prohibition is both *kolel* (larger in scope, applying to the meat) and *chamur* (weightier, prohibiting benefit as well), they agree that it takes effect. Hence their concession in the *olah* and *shor haniskal* cases: there the prohibition is not merely broader but also more severe, and both factors together permit the second prohibition to take effect on the *gid*.

### Mystery Tanna

The Gemara now searches: who is the *tanna* that rejects *issur kolel* alone (refusing to say *issur chal al issur* when the second prohibition is merely broader in scope) but would accept it when the second prohibition is both broader and weightier (*kolel* plus *chamur*)? Rabbi Shimon is not a candidate: he is explicit that he rejects *issur chal al issur* in all cases, regardless of whether the second prohibition is *kolel* or *chamur*. We need someone more nuanced—someone who rejects *kolel* alone but might accept *kolel* combined with *chamur*. The Gemara will now propose a candidate.

## Rabbi Yosi HaGelili

### Tamei/Kodesh Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הִיא, דִּתְנַן: טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל קֹדֶשׁ, בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ טָמֵא בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ טָהוֹר – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: It is Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** who holds that a more inclusive prohibition does not take effect where there is an already existing prohibition. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 106a): One who is **ritually impure who ate sacrificial** food, **whether it was ritually impure sacrificial** food **or ritually pure sacrificial** food, is **liable** to receive *karet* if he did so intentionally and to bring a sliding-scale offering if he did so unwittingly.

Rava identifies Rabbi Yosi HaGelili as this figure. The relevant source is a Mishnah: a *tamei* person who eats *kodesh* (consecrated meat) is liable to *karet*. But what if the *kodesh* itself had already become *tamei*? Rabbi Yosi HaGelili says: if the person was *tamei* and ate *tahor kodesh*, he is liable (*chayav*). But if he ate *tamei kodesh*—consecrated meat that had already been defiled—he is exempt (*patur*), because the meat's sanctity had already been compromised.

### Contradiction Raised

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל אֶת הַטָּהוֹר – חַיָּיב, טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל אֶת הַטָּמֵא – פָּטוּר, שֶׁלֹּא אֲכַל אֶלָּא דָּבָר טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: An impure** individual **who ate pure** sacrificial food is **liable.** But **an impure** individual **who ate impure** sacrificial food is **exempt, as he merely ate an impure item,** and the prohibition of eating sacrificial food while one is impure does not apply to impure sacrificial food.

The Rabbis challenge Rabbi Yosi HaGelili. They point out that even in the case he says generates liability—a *tamei* person eating *tahor kodesh*—by the time the meat reaches the person's mouth, he will have touched it and made it *tamei*. Every case of a *tamei* person eating *tahor kodesh* inevitably passes through a moment where the person renders the meat *tamei* before swallowing it. If eating *tamei kodesh* is always exempt, then liability would never arise. This appears to collapse Rabbi Yosi HaGelili's distinction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַף טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל אֶת הַטָּהוֹר, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּגַע בּוֹ, טִמְּאָהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him:** According to your logic, this *halakha* would apply **even** in a case of **an impure** individual **who ate** what had been **pure** sacrificial food, because **once he has touched it, he has** thereby **rendered it impure.** Yet, in such a case, he is certainly liable for eating it. So too, an impure individual who ate impure sacrificial food is liable.

One could construct an artificial scenario in which the meat was never rendered susceptible to *tumah* (*huchshar lekabel tumah*) or in which a *tahor* party placed it in the person's mouth. Tosafot acknowledges this possibility, but notes that the Gemara is operating under the assumption of a normal case and that Rabbi Yosi HaGelili would need to explain how liability arises in the standard situation.

### Order Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁפִּיר קָא אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, וְאָמַר רָבָא: בְּנִטְמָא הַגּוּף וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִטְמָא בָּשָׂר – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּחַיָּיב, דְּאִיסּוּר כָּרֵת קָדֵים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **The Rabbis are saying well to Rabbi Yosei HaGelili;** why does Rabbi Yosei HaGelili disagree? **And Rava said** in elaboration of the dispute: **In** a case where the person's **body became impure and** then **afterward** the sacrificial **meat became impure, everyone agrees that** he is **liable** if he eats the meat, **as the prohibition** of eating sacrificial meat while impure, which carries the punishment of ***karet*, preceded** the prohibition of eating impure sacrificial meat.

Rava resolves the difficulty by attending to the order of events. The question is not simply whether the meat was *tamei* at the moment of consumption, but which status—the person's *tumah* or the meat's *tumah*—came first.

### Person First: Agree

If the person became *tamei* first, and then picked up *tahor* meat to eat it—thereby rendering it *tamei* in the act of eating—all opinions agree he is *chayav* and receives *karet*. The reason: the *issur karet* (the prohibition with *karet*-level severity) attached to him the moment he became *tamei*, in relation to all *tahor kodesh*. The fact that he subsequently defiled the meat in the act of eating does not dissolve the pre-existing *issur karet*. The meat's *tumah* came second, after the prohibition was already operative.

### Meat First: Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּנִטְמָא בָּשָׂר, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִטְמָא הַגּוּף. רַבָּנַן אִית לְהוּ אִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל, דְּמִגּוֹ דְּמִיחַיַּיב אַחֲתִיכוֹת טְהוֹרוֹת דְּעָלְמָא, מִיחַיַּיב נָמֵי אַחֲתִיכָה טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree when** the **meat became impure and** then **afterward the** person's **body became impure. The Rabbis hold** that a more **inclusive prohibition** takes effect even where there is an already existing prohibition. Consequently, **since** the prohibition for an impure person to eat sacrificial meat is more inclusive than the prohibition for a pure person to eat impure sacrificial meat, **as** an impure person **is liable** for eating even **pure pieces** of sacrificial meat that are permitted to the rest **of the world, he is also liable** for this prohibition when he eats **an impure piece** of sacrificial meat.

The debate concerns the reverse scenario: the meat became *tamei* first, and then the person became *tamei*. Now the meat is already forbidden with a *lav*-level prohibition (eating *tamei kodesh*)—a lesser prohibition than *karet*. The question is whether the person's becoming *tamei* adds an *issur karet* on top of that pre-existing *lav*.

### Sages: Kolel Works

The Rabbis hold that it does, on the basis of *issur kolel*. When a person becomes *tamei*, the scope of his prohibition is larger than just this one piece of *tamei* meat: he is now forbidden from eating all *korban* meat, including *tahor kodesh* throughout the world. Because his *tumah* prohibition applies to *tahor kodesh* generally, it also takes effect on this particular piece of *tamei kodesh*. The logic is: *migu demichayev achatechos tehoros* (since he would now be liable for eating other *tahor* pieces), *michayev nami achatecha temeiah* (he is also liable for eating this defiled piece). The wider scope pulls the pre-existing lesser prohibition up into the larger prohibition.

### Yosi: Kolel Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי לֵית לֵיהּ אִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל, דְּמִגּוֹ לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei HaGelili does not** accept the principle that a more **inclusive prohibition** takes effect even where there is an already existing prohibition, **as** he holds that **we do not say that since** it applies to cases that were not yet prohibited it applies to all cases.

Rabbi Yosi HaGelili rejects this. He does not accept the *migu* logic—that because the prohibition reaches *tahor* pieces, it also reaches this *tamei* piece. The meat was already forbidden before he became *tamei*; the fact that his *tumah* creates new prohibitions elsewhere does not give it the power to redefine the status of something already prohibited. *Leit lei issur kolel*: he does not hold of *issur kolel* as a valid mechanism.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, נְהִי דְּאִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל לֵית לֵיהּ, בְּאִיסּוּר קַל יָבֹא אִיסּוּר חָמוּר יָחוּל עַל אִיסּוּר קַל, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? טוּמְאַת הַגּוּף, שֶׁהֲרֵי טוּמְאַת הַגּוּף בְּכָרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** even according to **Rabbi Yosei** HaGelili, **granted that he does not** hold that a more **inclusive prohibition** always takes effect where there is an already existing prohibition. But **in** the case of an already existing **lenient prohibition,** a more **stringent prohibition should come** and **take effect on** the more **lenient prohibition. And what is** the more stringent prohibition? The prohibition due to the **impurity of** a person's **body, as** one who eats sacrificial food when he has **impurity of the body** is liable **to *karet*,** whereas a pure person who eats impure sacrificial food is merely liable to be flogged. Consequently, the prohibition of eating sacrificial food while impure should apply even though the meat became impure before the person became impure.

The Gemara then asks: why not say that Rabbi Yosi HaGelili would at least admit when the second prohibition is not only *kolel* but also *chamur*—as the Sages require? After all, the person's *tumah* does create liability with *karet*, which is weightier than the *lav* of eating *tamei kodesh*. The Gemara answers via Rav Ashi: it is not obvious that the person's *tumah* is the weightier prohibition. One could argue that the meat's *tumah* is in some sense more severe—*deleit lei taharah bemikvah*—because the meat cannot be purified by immersion in a *mikvah*. The meat will remain *tamei* permanently; the person's *tumah* is theoretically transitory, curable by *mikvah*. So even though the person's *tumah* carries the heavier formal punishment (*karet*), the meat's *tumah* is "weightier" in the sense of being irremediable. Because the weightier-prohibition determination is ambiguous, Rabbi Yosi HaGelili cannot be said to have admitted the principle even for *kolel* plus *chamur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַאן לֵימָא לַן דְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף חֲמוּרָה, דִּלְמָא טוּמְאַת בָּשָׂר חֲמוּרָה, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ טׇהֳרָה בְּמִקְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Who** can **say to us that** the prohibition due to the **impurity of** a person's **body is** more **stringent? Perhaps** the prohibition due to the **impurity of the meat is** more **stringent, as** impure meat **does not have** the possibility of restoring its state of **purity** via immersion **in a ritual bath,** whereas a ritually impure person can become pure in this manner.

## Shabbat & Yom Kippur

### Setup: Double Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי לֵית לֵיהּ אִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Yosei HaGelili not** hold that a more **inclusive prohibition** takes effect where there is an already existing prohibition?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: שַׁבָּת וְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, שָׁגַג וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכָה, מִנַּיִן שֶׁחַיָּיב עַל זֶה בְּעַצְמוֹ וְעַל זֶה בְּעַצְמוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שַׁבָּת הִיא״, ״יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים הוּא״, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: When **Shabbat and Yom Kippur** occur on the same day, if one acted **unwittingly and performed** prohibited **labor, from where** is it derived **that he is liable for this by itself and for that by itself,** i.e., he is liable to bring two sin offerings, for having transgressed both Shabbat and Yom Kippur? **The verse states:** "You shall do no manner of work; **it is a Shabbat** for the Lord in all your dwellings" (Leviticus 23:3), and another verse states: **"It is Yom Kippur"** (Leviticus 23:27). The term "it is" in each verse teaches that each of these days is considered independently even when it occurs together with another holy day. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Rabbi Akiva says:** He **is liable** to bring **only one** sin offering because a prohibition does not take effect where another prohibition already exists.

The Gemara now turns to a case directly relevant to the present moment: Shabbat and Yom Kippur falling on the same day. A *Mishnah* asks: if someone accidentally performs *melacha* on a day that is simultaneously Shabbat and Yom Kippur, how many *korbanot* does he bring? Each of these days, taken alone, would independently generate liability for a *korban* for inadvertent *melacha*. The question is whether both can apply simultaneously, or whether one prohibition is subsumed into the other.

### Yosi: Two Korbanos

One opinion holds that two *korbanot* are required—one for Shabbat and one for Yom Kippur—since each day retains its independent force. The verse says *Shabbat hi* (it is Shabbat) and *Yom HaKippurim hu* (it is Yom Kippur), implying that even when they coincide, each maintains its own distinct status.

### Akiva: One Korban

The opposing opinion holds that only one *korban* is required. On this view, the second prohibition does not independently take hold, and liability is singular.

### Yom Kippur Scope

The Gemara's framing suggests that if one accepts *issur kolel* as a valid mechanism, Yom Kippur would take effect on top of Shabbat, because Yom Kippur has a wider scope: it prohibits not only *melacha* (as Shabbat does) but also *achilah* (eating). Since Yom Kippur makes its impact felt in a broader domain—the eating prohibitions—it can also take effect in the *melacha* domain, and therefore a second *korban* would be required.

### Name Reversal Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח רָבִין מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כָּךְ הַצָּעָה שֶׁל מִשְׁנָה, וְאֵיפוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that on this topic **Ravin sent** a letter citing a statement **in the name of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina: This is the** correct **presentation of** this **teaching [*hatza'a shel mishna*],** i.e., the opinions in this *baraita* are accurate, **but** one must **reverse** the attributions of each opinion so that the first opinion is that of Rabbi Akiva and the second opinion is that of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Consequently, Rabbi Yosei HaGelili holds that two prohibitions do not take effect at the same time even if one is more inclusive or stringent than the other.

The Gemara initially identifies the opinion requiring two *korbanot* with Rabbi Yosi HaGelili and the opinion requiring only one with Rabbi Akiva. But this creates an immediate problem: given everything the Gemara has established, Rabbi Yosi HaGelili does *not* hold of *issur kolel*. He should be the one saying only one *korban* applies. If the logic for two *korbanot* rests on *issur kolel*—the wider scope of Yom Kippur—then Rabbi Yosi HaGelili should reject it, not endorse it. The attribution appears to be internally contradictory.

### Ravin's Correction

The resolution comes from a transmission sent by Ravin from Eretz Yisrael, in the name of Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina. The names in the Mishnah are simply reversed (*ipoch*). Rabbi Yosi HaGelili is the one who says only one *korban* is required—consistent with his rejection of *issur kolel*—and Rabbi Akiva is the one who says two are required, holding that Yom Kippur's wider scope (*kolel*) allows it to take effect on top of Shabbat. With this reversal, the positions are internally consistent.

## Mixed Intent Scenarios

### Forgot Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יַעֲקֹב בַּר גִּיּוֹרֵי מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, לְמַאי דַּאֲפַכַן, שָׁגַג בְּשַׁבָּת וְהֵזִיד בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים – חַיָּיב, הֵזִיד בְּשַׁבָּת וְשָׁגַג בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the opinion of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. **Rav Yitzḥak bar Ya'akov bar Giyorei sent** a letter citing a statement **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: According to the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, as** stated in the *baraita* once the attributions have been **reversed,** if one unwittingly performed a forbidden labor on a Yom Kippur that occurred on Shabbat, he is obligated to bring one sin offering. If he acted **unwittingly with regard to** the fact that it was **Shabbat,** i.e., he forgot that it was Shabbat, **but** acted **intentionally with regard to Yom Kippur,** he is **obligated** to bring a sin offering. But if he acted **intentionally with regard to Shabbat but unwittingly with regard to Yom Kippur,** he is **exempt** from bringing any offering.

The Gemara now examines a series of sub-cases arising from Shabbat and Yom Kippur coinciding. In the first scenario, a person forgot that it was Shabbat but remembered that it was Yom Kippur, and willfully performed *melacha*.

### One Korban Shabbat

He brings one *korban* for Shabbat—because his violation of Shabbat was inadvertent (*shogeg*), which is the condition for *korban* liability—but no *korban* for Yom Kippur, because his violation of Yom Kippur was willful (*mezid*), for which there is no *korban*. The result: one *korban* for Shabbat.

### Forgot Yom Kippur

In the reverse scenario: the person knew it was Shabbat and willfully violated it, but forgot that it was Yom Kippur.

### No Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שַׁבָּת קְבִיעָא וְקַיְימָא, יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים – בֵּי דִינָא דְּקָא קָבְעִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement? **Abaye said: Shabbat is established and permanent,** i.e., it always occurs on the seventh day of the week, whereas in the case of **Yom Kippur,** it is **the court that establishes it,** as the Sages determine the New Moon. Consequently, Shabbat is considered to have preceded Yom Kippur, and the prohibition to perform labor on Yom Kippur does not apply, due to the fact that labor is already prohibited because it is Shabbat. Since one brings a sin offering only due to unwitting transgression, he is obligated to bring a sin offering only if he performed labor without realizing it was Shabbat.

He brings no *korban* at all. For Shabbat, there is no *korban* because he was *mezid*. For Yom Kippur, even though he was *shogeg*, according to Rabbi Yosi HaGelili the Yom Kippur *melacha* prohibition did not take effect on top of the Shabbat prohibition. If the *melacha* prohibition of Yom Kippur does not independently exist as a distinct prohibition in the presence of Shabbat, then there is nothing to be *shogeg* about with respect to Yom Kippur.

### Abaye: Shabbat Prior

Abaye explains the underlying principle: *Shabbat kevia ve-kayama*—Shabbat is fixed and standing. It occurs every week with complete regularity. Yom Kippur, by contrast, is set by *Beit Din* each year based on the sanctification of Rosh Chodesh Tishrei. Until witnesses testified and *Beit Din* declared Rosh Chodesh, the precise day of Yom Kippur was not determined. When they sanctified Rosh Chodesh Tishrei, they effectively "placed" Yom Kippur on top of Shabbat after Shabbat's prohibition was already fixed. Therefore, Shabbat is treated as pre-existing, and Yom Kippur's *melacha* prohibition does not add to it.

### Rava Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: סוֹף סוֹף, תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָאָתוּ. אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁמָדָא הֲוָה, וּשְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם דְּיוֹמָא דְּכִפּוּרֵי דְּהָא שַׁתָּא שַׁבְּתָא הוּא. וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין וְכֹל נָחוֹתֵי אַמְרוּהָ כְּרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Abaye: **Ultimately both of them,** i.e., Shabbat and Yom Kippur, **come** into effect **at the same time.** Since both take effect at the beginning of the calendar day, it cannot be said that Shabbat precedes Yom Kippur. **Rather Rava said** a different explanation. It **was** a time of religious **persecution, and they sent from there,** i.e., from Eretz Yisrael, a directive stating **that Yom Kippur of this year** will not be observed on its proper day but rather on **Shabbat.** Rabbi Yoḥanan was merely stating that on that particular year one who would unwittingly transgress Yom Kippur would be exempt from bringing a sin offering. **And similarly, when Ravin and all those who descended** from Eretz Yisrael **came** to Babylonia, **they said** that the true explanation is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava.**

Rava objects to Abaye's reasoning. Whatever was true during Elul, at the actual moment when Friday night arrives—*sof sof tarveihu bahadi hadadi ka-asu*—both Shabbat and Yom Kippur take effect simultaneously. Since they take effect at exactly the same moment, there is no sense in which one pre-exists the other, and the problem of *issur chal al issur* should not arise. If they take effect simultaneously, each should operate independently without one subordinating the other.

### Rava: Shmad Case

Because Rava rejects Abaye's framework, he reinterprets the entire discussion. He says that the case under discussion involves a *shmad*—a year of persecution in which the non-Jewish authorities issued a decree forbidding Jews to observe Yom Kippur. In that year, Yom Kippur did not actually fall on Shabbat; it fell on another day. But the Rabbis instructed the community to observe it on Shabbat, since abstaining from food on Shabbat would not attract the attention of the authorities in the way that ceasing *melacha* on a weekday would. For this special, Rabinically-reassigned Yom Kippur on Shabbat, no *korban* liability would apply for violating what was not a real Yom Kippur in the ordinary halachic sense. This is an extraordinary scenario, not a standing ruling about the normal calendar.

### E.Y. Agreement

This reading is confirmed by a transmission from Eretz Yisrael: *ki ada de-yoma de-kippura de-hai shata shabta hava*—the Yom Kippur of that year was observed on Shabbat. When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael together with others who had made the journey to Bavel, they all confirmed Rava's understanding. The scenario is exceptional, not normative, and therefore does not constitute a general ruling about the interaction of Shabbat and Yom Kippur when they coincide in the regular calendar.

## Bnei Yisrael Debate

### Yehuda's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וַהֲלֹא מִבְּנֵי יַעֲקֹב [וְכוּ׳].
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Rabbi Yehuda said** in explanation: **Wasn't** the sciatic nerve forbidden for **the children of Jacob,** as it is written: "Therefore the children of Israel eat not the sciatic nerve" (Genesis 32:33), yet the meat of a non-kosher animal was still permitted to them? Since the sciatic nerve of non-kosher animals became forbidden at that time, it remains forbidden now.

The Gemara now returns to Rabbi Yehuda's position in the Mishnah: that *gid hanasheh* was prohibited to the *bnei Yaakov* already before the Torah was given. This historical claim underpins his view that the prohibition applied in some form to non-kosher animals from the outset.

### Sages Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וְכִי נֶאֱמַר ״עַל כֵּן לֹא יֹאכְלוּ בְּנֵי יַעֲקֹב״? וַהֲלֹא לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא ״בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וְלֹא נִקְרְאוּ ״בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ עַד סִינַי! אֶלָּא בְּסִינַי נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּכְתַּב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ לֵידַע מֵאֵיזֶה טַעַם נֶאֱסַר לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that the Rabbis **said to Rabbi Yehuda: But is it stated** in the verse: **Therefore the children of Jacob do not eat** the sciatic nerve? **Isn't** it true that **it is stated only** that: "Therefore **the children of Israel** do not eat the sciatic nerve?" **And** the Jewish people were **not called "the children of Israel" until** they received the Torah at Mount **Sinai. Rather,** this terminology indicates that the prohibition of eating the sciatic nerve **was stated** to the Jewish people at Sinai, **but was written in its place,** after the incident of Jacob wrestling with the angel, to allow the Jewish people **to know the reason** the sciatic nerve **was forbidden to them.** Since the prohibition came into effect only at Sinai, there is no proof that it ever applied with regard to non-kosher animals.

The Rabbis object: the verse does not say *bnei Yaakov*. It says *bnei Yisrael*. These are different things.

### Sinai Proof

*Bnei Yisrael* is a national term—the Israelites—a designation that did not exist until the people stood at Har Sinai. Therefore, the verse must have been given at Har Sinai. It cannot be read as referring to an earlier period in history when the children of Yaakov were not yet a nation called Israel.

### Narrative Placement

The Rabbis acknowledge that the verse appears in the narrative of Yaakov's struggle with the angel—seemingly in the wrong place for a law given at Sinai. They explain: the verse was indeed given at Sinai, but it was written here, in the narrative of the struggle, in order to communicate the reason for the prohibition. The Torah is providing historical context: *this* is why Bnei Yisrael were later commanded not to eat the *gid hanasheh*—because of what happened to Yaakov. The verse is inserted into the narrative as explanation, not as the legal moment of the prohibition's inception. In this, the Torah departs from its usual pattern (stating a law and then connecting it to its historical basis) and instead embeds the law in the story.

### Rava's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: ״וַיִּשְׂאוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת יַעֲקֹב אֲבִיהֶם״, לְאַחַר מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises an objection** to this *baraita*: The verse states: "And Jacob rose up from Beersheba; **and the children of Israel carried Jacob their father,** and their little ones, and their wives in the wagons that Pharaoh had sent to carry him" (Genesis 46:5). This occurred before the Torah was given at Sinai, and therefore proves that the title "the children of Israel" was in use before the Torah was given. The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, this occurred **after the incident,** i.e., after Jacob wrestled with the angel and after the prophetic vision in which God changed Jacob's name to Israel (Genesis 35:10).

Rava challenges the Rabbis' argument that *bnei Yisrael* is necessarily a post-Sinaitic term. He cites the verse *vayisu bnei Yisrael et Yaakov avihem*—"and the children of Israel carried Yaakov their father" (to Egypt). There, the phrase *bnei Yisrael* clearly refers to the sons of Yaakov, not to the nation of Israel. So the term can evidently be used to mean Yaakov's children even in an earlier context.

### Name Change Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מֵהָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא לִיתְּסַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** In that case, the sciatic nerve **should be** understood as having become **forbidden** to them **from that time** when they were first called the children of Israel. Since this was before the giving of the Torah, this would be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda and not that of the Rabbis.

The Gemara responds: that verse appears *after* Yaakov's name had already been officially changed to Yisrael by God. Once God ratified the new name, the children could appropriately be called *bnei Yisrael* in the sense of "sons of Yisrael/Yaakov." But in the passage about the struggle with the angel, Yaakov's name change had not yet been ratified by God—only the angel had given him the name, and that is insufficient for official use. Therefore, at that moment in the narrative, calling his children *bnei Yisrael* would be premature; the verse must be understood as referring to a later period.

### Rav Aha Query

Rav Aha the son of Rava presses the point to Rav Ashi: perhaps from the moment that God did officially rename Yaakov as Yisrael—in a later episode—the verse should be understood as taking effect from that point forward? Perhaps the prohibition applied from the moment of God's ratification of the name, not only from Har Sinai?

### Rav Ashi Reply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי תוֹרָה פְּעָמִים פְּעָמִים נִיתְּנָה? הָהוּא שַׁעְתָּא לָאו שְׁעַת מַעֲשֶׂה הֲוַאי, וְלָא שְׁעַת מַתַּן תּוֹרָה הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: Was the Torah given** piecemeal, on numerous different **occasions?** It was given at Sinai. Rather, **that time** when the title "children of Israel" was first used **was not the time** when **the incident** of Jacob wrestling with the angel occurred **and** also **was not the time of the giving of the Torah** at Mount Sinai. Therefore, there is no reason to assume that the prohibition took effect at that time.

Rav Ashi rejects this. The Torah is not given piecemeal. There is no halachic mechanism by which a heavenly communication at one point in the patriarchal narrative generates a binding commandment. A commandment binds when it is given as part of the formal reception of Torah at Har Sinai. Neither the moment of the struggle with the angel nor the moment of God's subsequent renaming of Yaakov was a *shat matan Torah*—a moment of Torah-giving. Therefore neither occasion could have been the binding moment for the *gid hanasheh* prohibition.

### Final Resolution

The conclusion is as the Rabbis maintained all along: *bnei Yisrael* means Israelites; the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* was given at Har Sinai; and the verse was inserted into the narrative of the struggle with the angel not because it was given then, but in order to embed its reason within the story. The prohibition belongs to the world of Sinai, not to the world of Yaakov and his children.
