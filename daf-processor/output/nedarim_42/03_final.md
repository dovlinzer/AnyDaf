# Nedarim 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah: Nedarim & Shemi…

### Opening Remarks

Today's daf is 42. We continue the discussion of what is or is not forbidden to someone who is *assur* — who cannot derive *hana'ah* from another person.

### Mishnah: Before Shemitah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לִפְנֵי שְׁבִיעִית — אֵינוֹ יוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ, וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל מִן הַנּוֹטוֹת. וּבַשְּׁבִיעִית — אֵינוֹ יוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ, אֲבָל אוֹכֵל הוּא מִן הַנְּטִיעוֹת הַנּוֹטוֹת. נָדַר הֵימֶנּוּ מַאֲכָל, לִפְנֵי שְׁבִיעִית — יוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ, וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל מִן הַפֵּירוֹת, וּבַשְּׁבִיעִית — יוֹרֵד וְאוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one for whom benefit from another is forbidden, before,** i.e., a year other than **the Sabbatical Year, he may neither enter the field** of that other person, **nor eat from** the **produce that leans out of the field**, even if he does not enter the field. **And during the Sabbatical Year,** when the produce of the trees is ownerless, **he may not enter his field; however, he may eat from the growths that lean** out of the field, as the produce does not belong to the other person. If **one vowed before the Sabbatical Year** that benefit **from** another's **food** is forbidden for him, **he may enter his field; however, he may not eat of the produce. And during the Sabbatical Year, he may enter** the field **and may eat** the produce.

The Mishnah opens: *hamudar hana'ah mechavero lifnei shevi'it* — one who is forbidden from deriving benefit from his fellow before *shevi'it*. This Mishnah turns out to be as much about *shevi'it* as it is about *issur hana'ah*. The ruling is: *eino yored letoch sadehu ve'eino ochel min haneti'ot* — he may neither enter the field nor eat from branches overhanging outside the field boundary.

As we have discussed previously, *hana'ah* is not limited to eating the fruit. Walking through someone's yard — the right of access to another's property — is itself a form of *hana'ah*. Trespassing on property one is forbidden to use constitutes a violation. Accordingly, someone forbidden from *hana'ah* may neither enter the field nor pluck fruit from overhanging branches outside it.

### Mishnah: During Shemitah

The Mishnah then states: *uveshevi'it* — and during *shevi'it*. This clause admits two readings, and the Gemara will explore them at length. On one reading, the *neder* was taken before *shemitah* and the question is what happens once *shemitah* arrives. On another reading, the *neder* itself was taken *on shevi'it* — meaning Reuven made the vow during the sabbatical year itself.

On *shevi'it*, the ruling is: *eino yored letoch sadehu* — he still cannot enter the field — *aval ochel hu min haneti'ot hanotos* — but he may eat from the overhanging fruit. Why? Because on *shevi'it*, the fruit is no longer Reuven's to forbid. The fruit belongs, in a meaningful sense, to everyone; it is not within the owner's power to make it forbidden. The land itself, however, remains Reuven's property — ownership of the land is not lost on *shemitah* — and so access to the field can still be rendered *assur* through a *neder*.

This raises an immediate question: on *shevi'it*, do the poor not have an inherent right of access to one's property for the purpose of gathering fruit? If so, how can Reuven make entry to his yard forbidden through a *neder*? After all, if that device were effective, every landowner could simply forbid access to his yard on *shemitah* and thereby prevent anyone from entering. The Gemara will address this at length; the short answer, for now, is that the fruit is not Reuven's to forbid, but the property and access to it remain his, and that access can still be made *assur*.

### Two Readings Explained

A second, clarifying section of the Mishnah addresses the case where only food — not general *hana'ah* — was forbidden: *nadar mimenu ma'achal lifnei shevi'it* — if the vow was only about food benefit, taken before *shevi'it*. Then *yored letoch sadehu ve'eino ochel min haperot* — he may enter the field (since entering the yard is not food), but he may not eat the fruit. And *uvashevi'it yored ve'ochel* — during *shevi'it*, he may both enter the field and eat the fruit, because on *shevi'it* the owner cannot make the fruit forbidden; it is no longer his.

The Mishnah is thus fairly straightforward in its basic contours. The central analytical question — which will dominate the Gemara's discussion — is whether the first part of the Mishnah, dealing with full *issur hana'ah*, means that *once shevi'it arrives* the previously forbidden fruit becomes permitted, or whether it means that *if the neder was taken on shevi'it* the fruit is not subject to prohibition. This question will turn into a deeper debate about the very nature of a person's power to forbid.

## Rav/Shmuel vs. R. Yochanan

### Rav & Shmuel Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: ״נְכָסִים אֵלּוּ עָלֶיךָ״ — לִפְנֵי שְׁבִיעִית אֵין יוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ, וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל מִן הַנּוֹטוֹת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהִגִּיעַ שְׁבִיעִית. וְאִם בַּשְּׁבִיעִית נָדַר — אֵין יוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ, אֲבָל אוֹכֵל מִן הַנּוֹטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It is **Rav and Shmuel who both say** that if one vowed **before the Sabbatical Year:** Benefit from **this property** is forbidden **to you,** the other **may neither enter his field, nor eat from** the produce **that leans** out of the field, **even though the Sabbatical Year arrived** in the interim, because the prohibition of the produce took effect before the Sabbatical Year and remained in effect after the Sabbatical Year began. **And if he vowed during the Sabbatical Year, he may not enter a field** that is included in that property; **however, he may eat from** the produce that **leans** out of the field, because the produce was ownerless when he vowed.

Rav and Shmuel both say: if someone declared *nechasim elu alecha* — "these properties are forbidden to you" — before *shevi'it*, then *eino yored letoch sadehu ve'eino ochel min haneti'ot* — he cannot enter the field and cannot eat from the overhanging fruit. And crucially: *af al pi shehigi'a shevi'it* — even once *shevi'it* arrives, this status does not change. Once the *neder* is in effect, it does not cease merely because the fruit becomes ownerless.

The reasoning is as follows. If I say "this book is forbidden to you" while I own it, and then I sell it to someone else, the forbidden status remains — because when I made the *neder* it was mine to make. The fruit that was in existence at the time of the *neder* and was rendered *assur* retains that status even as *shemitah* arrives, even though ownership of the fruit is effectively lost. New fruit that grows during *shemitah* would be a different matter entirely — the owner never had proprietary rights over that, and could not have forbidden it. But fruit that was already subject to the *neder* remains forbidden.

Accordingly, Rav and Shmuel read the Mishnah's statement *uveshevi'it* as referring to a *neder* taken *on shevi'it* — only then is the fruit permissible. But if the *neder* was taken before *shevi'it*, the prohibition persists even after *shemitah* arrives.

### R. Yochanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: ״נְכָסַי עָלֶיךָ״ — לִפְנֵי שְׁבִיעִית אֵין יוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ וְאֵין אוֹכֵל מִן הַנּוֹטוֹת, הִגִּיעַ שְׁבִיעִית — אֵינוֹ יוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ, אֲבָל אוֹכֵל הוּא אֶת הַנּוֹטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it is **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish who both say** that if one vowed **before the Sabbatical Year:** Benefit from **my property** is forbidden **to you,** the other **may neither enter his field nor eat from** the produce that **leans** out of the field. When **the Sabbatical Year arrives, he may not enter his field; however, he may eat from** the produce that **leans** out of the field, because the produce is ownerless.

Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish both say: if someone declared *nechasai alecha* — "my property is forbidden to you" — before *shemitah*, then *eino yored letoch sadehu ve'eino ochel min hanotos* — naturally, he cannot enter the yard or eat the overhanging fruit. But *hegia shvi'is* — once *shevi'it* arrives — *eino yored letoch sadehu aval ochel hu et hanotos* — he still cannot enter the yard, but he may now eat the overhanging fruit. The vow made before *shemitah* ceases to apply to the fruit once *shemitah* arrives, because the owner no longer owns that fruit.

### Property Access & Gezeir…

This position gives rise to a pointed observation from within the sugya. If forbidding access to one's field through a *neder* is effective on *shevi'it*, that would seem to be a highly convenient tool: one could effectively shut the poor out of one's land merely by declaring the property forbidden. But the Gemara does not regard this as an insuperable problem, because the person cannot forbid the new fruit that grows during *shemitah* — that was never his. And the stuff that already existed before *shemitah* could equally have been harvested and stored away before the sabbatical year began.

One might press further: what about fruit that exists on the tree but has not yet fully grown — fruit that will continue to develop once *shemitah* begins? Presumably the owner cannot forbid the additional growth, but practically speaking, how would anyone separate the permissible portion from the *assur* portion? This is a genuinely difficult question, and it points toward the broader tension in this Mishnah between formal legal categories and the practical realities of *shemitah* observance.

### Ownership & Power

The most conceptually compelling way to read the position of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is as follows. Their ruling is not merely a function of what the person said or intended; it is a function of the limits of his actual power. A person's capacity to render something forbidden to another derives from his ownership and control over that thing. That power does not extend beyond the scope of his ownership. Therefore, once the fruit passes out of his domain — as happens with *shemitah* — the forbidden status he instilled in it cannot persist, because it was sustained only by his continued ownership.

This is a striking conceptual claim: it is not about intent, not about language, not about the terms of the *neder*. It is about the ontological limits of a person's power to prohibit. The Gemara, as we will see, will ultimately not accept this framing as the fundamental principle at stake — but it is worth pausing to appreciate its force.

## This vs. My Property

### Initial Proposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל סָבְרִי: אָדָם אוֹסֵר דָּבָר שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּתוֹ אֲפִילּוּ לִכְשֶׁיֵּצֵא מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ סָבְרִי: אֵין אָדָם אוֹסֵר דָּבָר שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּתוֹ לִכְשֶׁיֵּצֵא מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that they disagree about this, that Rav and Shmuel hold: A person** can **render an item in his possession forbidden,** and the prohibition remains in effect **even when it leaves his possession.** Since he rendered the produce forbidden before the Sabbatical Year, the prohibition remains in effect after the produce becomes ownerless during the Sabbatical Year. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish hold: A person cannot render an item in his possession forbidden** and have the prohibition remain in effect **when it leaves his possession.** Therefore, it is permitted to eat the produce during the Sabbatical Year.

The Gemara proposes that this is indeed the crux of the dispute. Rav and Shmuel hold: *adam oser davar she'birshuso afilu l'khesheyeitzei mershuso* — a person can render something in his possession forbidden, and that prohibition persists even after the object leaves his possession. Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish hold: *ein adam oser davar she'birshuso l'khesheyeitzei mershuso* — a person's power to forbid is coextensive with his ownership; it does not extend beyond it. This would be a profound and fundamental disagreement about the nature of *nedarim*.

The Gemara immediately challenges this framing: *v'sisbara* — but does this really hold together? If the debate were truly so fundamental, they should have stated their positions with respect to *nechasim elu*, "this property," as well — for if even generic language without any possessive is insufficient to sustain a prohibition past the loss of ownership, one would expect Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish to say so. Yet their formulation specifically uses *nechasai* — "my property." This already hints that the debate is not about the fundamental limits of one's power to forbid, but about the linguistic interpretation of the possessive.

### Mishnah Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ, הָא תְּנַן דְּאָדָם אוֹסֵר דָּבָר שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּתוֹ לִכְשֶׁיֵּצֵא מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ, דִּתְנַן: הָאוֹמֵר לִבְנוֹ ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאַתָּה נֶהֱנֶה לִי״, מֵת — יִירָשֶׁנּוּ. ״בְּחַיָּיו וּבְמוֹתוֹ״, מֵת — לֹא יִירָשֶׁנּוּ! שָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּקָא אָמַר לֵיהּ ״בְּחַיָּיו וּבְמוֹתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, didn't we learn** in a mishna that **a person** can **render an item in his possession forbidden** and the prohibition remains in effect **when it leaves his possession?** This is **as we learned** in a mishna, that with regard to **one who says to his son: Benefit from my** property is ***konam* for you,** if the father **dies,** the son **will inherit him.** He is not deriving benefit from his father's property, as after death it is no longer his. If the father vowed to render benefit from his property forbidden to his son **during his lifetime and upon his death, then if** the father **dies,** his son **does not inherit** from **him.** Apparently, one can render his property forbidden and have it remain forbidden after it is no longer in his possession. The Gemara rejects that proof: It **is different here, as he said to him** explicitly: **During his lifetime and upon his death.** There is no proof that in a case where he did not explicitly extend the prohibition to the period after it leaves his possession, the prohibition would not remain in effect.

Furthermore, the Gemara raises a proof from a Mishnah that clearly establishes the ability to render something forbidden even beyond one's lifetime and ownership: *omer livno konam she'atah neheneh li* — a father says to his son, "Korban — all my property is forbidden to you as benefit from me." If the father dies and the son inherits him: *yirshenu* — he inherits and may benefit. Why? Because inheritance does not constitute receiving something *from* the father. At the moment of death, the property passes automatically to the son's domain; the son is not receiving it *from* the deceased. Hence, if the father said only "from me," the prohibition does not apply to the inherited property.

But if the father said *bechayav uvemoto* — both in his lifetime and after his death — then *met lo yirshenu*: the son may not benefit from the property. The Ran here points out that this does not mean the son literally fails to inherit; the laws of inheritance operate automatically through *choshen mishpat* and cannot be stopped by a *neder*. What it means is that the son owns the property but may not derive benefit from it — he cannot sell it, use it, or profit from it in any way. This raises its own fascinating questions: can mere ownership, without benefit, itself constitute *hana'ah*? And what would the son ultimately be able to do with property he owns but cannot benefit from?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא? אֶלָּא: בִּ״נְכָסִים אֵלּוּ״ — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בִּ״נְכָסַי״.
>
> **Translation:** The second question was answered, but **in any case** the first question remains **difficult:** Why didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish disagree in a case where he said: This property, as well? **Rather,** this is the explanation of their dispute: **In** the case of one who said: Benefit from **this property** is forbidden to you, **everyone agrees** that the prohibition remains in effect even after the item is no longer in his possession. **When they disagree,** it is **in** the case of one who said: Benefit from **my property** is forbidden to you.

The Mishnah just cited proves that one can render property forbidden *even after one's death* — even after one has entirely ceased to be the owner. This demonstrates conclusively that *adam oser davar she'birshuso l'khesheyeitzei mershuso* is not a contested position; it is universally accepted. But the Gemara notes: perhaps this requires explicit language — the father had to say *bechayav uvemoto*, explicitly extending the prohibition beyond his lifetime. Without such explicit language, one might still maintain that the default interpretation limits the prohibition to the period of actual ownership.

The Gemara then cites a further Mishnah that is decisive. The Mishnah teaches: if someone says *konam letoch beitecha she'ani nichnas* — "your house is forbidden to me, that I shall not enter it" — or *sadecha she'ani loke'ach* — "your field, that I shall not purchase it" — and then the owner dies or sells to another, the vow lapses: *mutar*, it is permitted. The prohibiting party said "your" house, "your" field — and once it is no longer *your* house, the prohibition dissolves. But if he said *le'bayit zeh she'ani nichnas*, *sadeh zo she'ani loke'ach* — "this house I shall not enter," "this field I shall not purchase" — and the owner dies or sells to another, the prohibition persists: *assur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לָא שְׁנָא ״נְכָסִים אֵלּוּ״ וְלָא שְׁנָא ״נְכָסַי״? וְהָא תְּנַן: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״קֻוֽנָּם לְתוֹךְ בֵּיתְךָ שֶׁאֲנִי נִכְנָס״, ״שָׂדְךָ שֶׁאֲנִי לוֹקֵחַ״, מֵת אוֹ שֶׁמְּכָרוֹ לְאַחֵר — מוּתָּר. ״לְבַיִת זֶה שֶׁאֲנִי נִכְנָס״, ״שָׂדֶה זוֹ שֶׁאֲנִי לוֹקֵחַ״, מֵת אוֹ שֶׁמְּכָרוֹ לְאַחֵר — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is there anyone who says** that it **is no different** if he said: **This property, and** it **is no different** if he said: **My property,** and that the prohibition remains in effect even after the item is no longer in his possession? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna: **If one says to another: Entering into your house is *konam* for me,** or: **Buying your field is *konam* for me,** then if the owner **died or sold** the property **to another,** it is **permitted** for the one who vowed to enter the house or buy the field, as the prohibition is in effect only as long as it belongs to that person. However, if he said: **Entering this house** is *konam* **for me,** or: **Buying this field** is *konam* **for me,** then if the owner **died or sold** the property **to another,** it remains **forbidden.** Apparently, there is a difference between a case where he simply renders an item forbidden and a case where he renders an item belonging to a particular individual forbidden.

This Mishnah is extremely clear. The use of the possessive — "your house," "your field" — limits the prohibition to the duration of that person's ownership. The use of the demonstrative "this house," "this field" — generic reference to the object itself — creates a prohibition that follows the object regardless of ownership. And note: in that Mishnah, the person forbidding is *not the owner at all* — he is the one seeking to enter or purchase. Yet even his prohibition is interpreted according to this possessive/demonstrative distinction. If the possessive limits *his* prohibition when the property is someone else's entirely, then certainly when I use the possessive about *my own* property, the limitation applies.

### Linguistic Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: כִּי אָמְרִי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, בִּ״נְכָסַי״. וְרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, בִּ״נְכָסִים אֵלּוּ״, וְלָא פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this is the explanation of the statements of the *amora'im*: **When Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish said** that the prohibition is no longer in effect after the item is no longer in his possession, it was **in** a case where he said: **My property. And** when **Rav and Shmuel** said that the prohibition remains in effect after the item is no longer in his possession, it was **in** a case where he said: **This property. And they do not disagree,** as each pair of *amora'im* addressed a different situation.

Given this evidence, the Gemara concludes that there is no fundamental dispute between the Amoraim at all. The debate between Rav and Shmuel on the one hand and Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish on the other is not about whether a person's power to forbid is ontologically bounded by ownership. Everyone agrees on the following: if one uses the demonstrative — *nechasim elu*, "these properties" — the prohibition persists even after one loses ownership. If one uses the possessive — *nechasai*, "my property" — the prohibition lapses once ownership ends.

When Rav and Shmuel said the *neder* remains in force even when *shemitah* arrives, they were speaking of a case where the declarant said *nechasim elu* — "these properties." When Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish said the fruit becomes permitted once *shemitah* arrives, they were speaking of a case where the declarant said *nechasai* — "my property." *Velo pligi* — they do not actually disagree. They are describing two distinct scenarios, and the Mishnah's ambiguity allowed each pair of Amoraim to read it in accordance with their respective case.

What remains is a striking *hava amina*: one might have thought that the power to forbid is inherently and always bounded by ownership, such that no *neder* could ever outlast one's control over the object — but at the end of the day we reject this and hold that the determinative factor is not metaphysical power but linguistic formulation. Generic, demonstrative language creates a prohibition that is object-bound; possessive language creates one that is relationship-bound and lapses with the relationship.

### Possessive & Demonstrati…

The Ran at the close of this section cites a fascinating *Yerushalmi* addressing a case where someone uses *both* a possessive and a demonstrative: *beitecha zeh* — "your house, this one." Which term governs the interpretation? Does "your house" limit the prohibition to the duration of the other person's ownership, or does "this" extend it to the object itself regardless of ownership? The *Yerushalmi* concludes: *mishum zeh* — you follow the final term. If he ended with the demonstrative "this," that is the operative word, and the prohibition follows the object. The principle is *holchin achar lashon acharon* — we follow the last formulation.

## Shemitah Rights & Access

### Rights vs. Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַשְּׁבִיעִית אֵין יוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ כּוּ׳. מַאי שְׁנָא דְּאוֹכֵל מִן הַנּוֹטוֹת — דְּפֵירֵי דְהֶפְקֵירָא אִינּוּן, אַרְעָא נָמֵי אַפְקְרַהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And during the Sabbatical Year he may not enter his field;** however, he eats from the produce that leans out of the field. The Gemara asks: **What is different** about the Sabbatical Year **that he** is permitted to **eat of** the produce that leans out of the field? It is **due to** the fact **that the produce is ownerless.** With regard to **land as well,** the Torah **rendered it ownerless,** as during the Sabbatical Year, it is permitted for everyone to enter the field and eat the produce.

Returning now to the Mishnah's ruling that even on *shevi'it* the *neder* prevents the other party from entering the field: why should this be? On *shevi'it*, the poor have an inherent right of access to private fields in order to gather the ownerless fruit. If the Torah grants them this right of access, how can a *neder* override it? Indeed, one might ask more pointedly: if someone could simply declare *nechasim elu* over his field and thereby bar the poor from entering, this would effectively allow any landowner to protect his produce from the poor on *shemitah* simply by making a *neder* — which would seem to undermine the entire intent of the sabbatical year.

### Ulla's Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: בְּעוֹמְדִין אִילָנוֹת עַל הַגְּבוּלִים. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְיָקִים אָמַר: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִשְׁהֶא בַּעֲמִידָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where** the fruit **trees are standing on the borders** of the field. Since it is possible to eat the produce without entering the field, it is not permitted for him to enter it. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elyakim said:** Even in a case where the fruit trees are standing in the middle of the field, it is also prohibited for him to enter the field, due to a rabbinic **decree lest he remain standing** there longer than necessary for purposes of eating, which is prohibited even during the Sabbatical Year.

Ulla answers: *be'omdim ilanot al ha-gvulin* — the Mishnah is speaking of a case where the trees are situated right at the boundary of the property. In such a configuration, the poor do not actually need to enter the field in order to gather the fruit. They can position themselves outside the border, place a ladder against the boundary, and reach the fruit without setting foot on the owner's land. The Torah's grant of access to the poor is a right of *entry for the purpose of gathering*, not an unlimited right of access. Where gathering is possible without entry, the right of entry does not exist. Therefore, the *neder* can effectively prevent entry — because the poor have no independent right to enter in the first place.

This is a genuinely striking limitation on the *shemitah* access rights we might have assumed the poor possess. The right is functional and proportionate: they may enter to the extent necessary to gather the fruit, and no more. If the fruit is accessible from outside, entry is not required and cannot be demanded.

### R. Shimon Gezeirah

Rabbi Shimon ben Elyakim offers a related but distinct answer. The poor do have a right to enter the field to gather fruit, but they do not have a right to *linger* there after gathering is complete. The owner retains the right to insist they leave once they have collected what is owed to them. Now, through his *neder*, the owner has made any extended presence in his yard *assur* — the poor person who stays longer than necessary to collect the fruit would transgress the *neder*. Since the *neder* creates a real prohibition against lingering, the rabbis enacted a *gezeirah*: the poor may not enter the field at all, lest they come to tarry after gathering and thereby transgress.

This is a remarkable rabbinic safeguard, and it invites reflection. The *gezeirah* has the practical effect of barring all access to the field — which, as noted, might seem to provide an effective loophole against the obligations of *shemitah*. The question of why the rabbis would institute such a *gezeirah*, knowing its potential consequences for the poor's access to *shemitah* produce, is a genuine difficulty. Presumably, the individual owner's ability to benefit from this arrangement is circumscribed by other aspects of *shemitah* law — including the prohibition on commercial harvesting — but exactly how these constraints interact is worth further study.

## Lending & Selling Rules

### Mishnah Prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ — לֹא יַשְׁאִילֶנּוּ וְלֹא יִשְׁאַל מִמֶּנּוּ, לֹא יַלְוֶנּוּ וְלֹא יִלְוֶה מִמֶּנּוּ, וְלֹא יִמְכּוֹ לוֹ וְלֹא יִקַּח מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one for whom benefit from another is forbidden by vow,** that other person **may neither lend** an item **to him nor borrow** an item **from him.** Similarly, **he may neither lend** money **to him nor borrow** money **from him. And he may neither sell** an item **to him nor purchase** an item **from him.**

The next Mishnah addresses a person who is *mudar hana'ah me'chaveiro* — forbidden from benefiting from his fellow. The Mishnah states: *lo yash'ileinu* — he may not lend him objects; *ve'lo yish'al mimenu* — and may not borrow objects from him; *lo yelvennu* — may not lend him money; *ve'lo yilveh mimenu* — and may not borrow money from him; *lo yimkor lo* — may not sell to him; *ve'lo yikach mimenu* — and may not purchase from him.

### Gemara Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא לֹא יַלְוֶנּוּ — דְּקָא מַהֲנֵי לֵיהּ. אֶלָּא לָא יִלְוֶה הֵימֶנּוּ, מַאי קָא מַהֲנֵי לֵיהּ? וּבִשְׁלָמָא לֹא יִלְוֶה הֵימֶנּוּ וְלֹא יִקַּח הֵימֶנּוּ — דְּקָמִיתְהֲנֵי מִינֵּיהּ, אֶלָּא לֹא יִשְׁאַל הֵימֶנּוּ, מַאי קָא מִיתְהֲנֵי מִינֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Granted** that the person from whom benefit is forbidden **may not lend** money **to** the person for whom benefit is forbidden, **as he** thereby **benefits him. However,** with regard to the fact that the former **may not borrow** money **from** the latter, in **what** way **does he benefit him** by borrowing his money? **And** it could even be said, **granted** that **he may neither borrow** money **from him nor purchase** an item **from him, as one benefits** in lending money by preserving the value of that money in case the coins deteriorate, and in selling by ridding oneself an item that is difficult to sell. **However,** with regard to the fact that the person from whom benefit is forbidden **may not borrow** an item **from** the person for whom benefit is forbidden, in **what** way **does** the lender **benefit from him?** The borrower returns the same item to the lender.

The Gemara raises an immediate difficulty. *Bishlama lo yelvennu* — we understand why he may not lend him money: lending is a benefit conferred on the borrower. But *lo yilveh himenu* — why may he not borrow *from* him? What benefit is the borrower giving to the lender? The same question applies across the categories: we understand why the person forbidden from *hana'ah* may not receive objects, money, or goods — but why may he not give objects, lend money, or sell?

The Ran's *girsa* makes the structure of the Gemara's question cleaner: we understand *lo yilveh mimenu* — he may not borrow money — *ve'lo yikach mimenu* — may not purchase — *ve'lo yish'al mimenu* — may not borrow objects, because in all these cases he is receiving benefit. But why *lo yash'ileinu* — may he not lend objects — *ve'lo yelvennu* — and lend money — *ve'lo yimkor lo* — and sell to him? What benefit is flowing in those directions?

### R. Yose View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּדְרוּ הֲנָאָה זֶה מִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where they** both **vowed** that **benefit from each other** is forbidden.

Rabbi Yose bar Chanina answers: the Mishnah is speaking of a case where both parties took mutual vows — *shenadaru hana'ah ze mize* — neither may benefit from the other. Under this reading, the Mishnah is not surprising: it is simply listing all the transactions that are prohibited when the *issur* runs in both directions. Each prohibition can be explained straightforwardly as preventing benefit to one or the other party. The difficulty with this reading is that it renders the Mishnah somewhat less interesting — it is merely applying the same principle twice, once in each direction.

### Abaye Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: גְּזֵירָה לִשְׁאוֹל מִשּׁוּם לְהַשְׁאִיל, וְכֵן בְּכוּלְּהוּ גְּזֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** It is possible to explain the mishna as it is written, as referring to a case where only one vowed that benefit from the other is forbidden. However, the Sages issued **a decree** that it is also prohibited for one **to borrow** from a person for whom benefit from him is forbidden, **due to** the concern that he might come **to lend** to him, as reciprocity is common in these matters. **And likewise,** that is the explanation **in all** the cases in the mishna; it is prohibited to borrow money, borrow items, and to purchase items from him due to a rabbinic **decree,** lest he come to benefit him.

Abaye offers a different answer: *gezera lish'ol mishum lehash'il vechein bekulhu* — it is a rabbinic *gezeirah*: even though in principle only one direction of each transaction is actually prohibited, the rabbis forbade the reverse direction as well, lest one come to do the forbidden direction. Don't borrow, lest you come to lend. Don't lend, lest you come to borrow. And so forth for all the categories. This reading is likely closer to the plain sense of the Mishnah.

### Sale Benefit & Avoda Zara

The discussion of sales raises a related point from earlier in the tractate: in a standard commercial transaction, who is the beneficiary? The Gemara's reasoning here seems to assume that primarily it is the buyer who benefits — which is why we understand why the *mudar hana'ah* cannot purchase, but need an explanation for why he cannot sell. The possibility that both buyer and seller mutually benefit from a transaction — an idea that surfaces elsewhere in the Gemara — would explain why the sale prohibition naturally runs in both directions without needing either Rabbi Yose's mutual-vow reading or Abaye's *gezeirah*.

This sugya has a striking parallel in the opening Mishnah of Avoda Zara, which lists a similar set of restrictions — no selling, no buying, no lending of money, no borrowing, no lending of objects, no borrowing — that apply to transactions with non-Jews in the three days before their holidays. The Gemara there similarly asks: we understand why one might not give benefit to a non-Jew before his holiday, but why may one not *receive* benefit from him? And Abaye there offers the same answer: it is a *gezeirah*, one direction is forbidden because of the other.

The parallel is illuminating. The collection of prohibited transactions here — lending, borrowing, buying, selling — does not operate purely as a technical consequence of *issur hana'ah*. As we saw throughout the previous chapter, many interactions remain entirely permissible even when *hana'ah* is forbidden: one may pay off another's debts, support his wife, visit him when ill. Yet this specific set of commercial and financial transactions is *assur* in both directions. It creates, in effect, a wall of separation. In the context of *nedarim*, this barrier is a legal consequence of the vow; in *Avoda Zara*, it expresses the social and religious distance between the Jewish community and idolatrous practice. That both contexts generate the same formal structure, and that Abaye's *gezeirah* appears in both, is worth noting as a reflection of how the Talmud thinks about the social meaning of prohibited transactions.
