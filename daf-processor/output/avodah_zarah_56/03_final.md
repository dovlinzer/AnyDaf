# Avodah Zarah 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## When Does Juice Become Wine?

### Wine Processing Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה יֵין נֶסֶךְ עַד שֶׁיֵּרֵד לְבוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the gentile's touch **does not render** the juice of the grapes into **wine** used for **a libation until it descends into the** collection **vat,** because until then it does not have the status of wine.

The central question animating the opening of daf 56a is: at what point does grape juice become *yayin* — wine — for purposes of *yayin nesech*? A clarification to the physical layout is worth noting at the outset. The *gat* is the stomping area itself, and the reference in the *mishna* to lifting the grapes and bringing them to the *tapuach* is not a transfer to a separate location but rather a reshifting of the material within the same press — redistributing the grape mass to extract more juice. The basic topography, then, consists of two stages: the *gat* (the press and stomping floor), and the *bor* (the underground cistern), with a *tzinor* — a pipe — connecting them. The practical stakes of determining when juice becomes *yayin* are twofold: once it achieves that status, deriving benefit from it becomes problematic, and a Jew may not be paid for work involving *issurei hana'ah*.

### Mishna Rishona: At Bor

The *mishna* establishes that grape juice does not become *yayin nesech* until it descends into the *bor*. Prior to that point — even when flowing through the pipe — it has not yet achieved the legal status of wine. This is the *mishna rishona*, the earlier formulation. An ancillary question lurks here: once the wine does reach the *bor*, does it become forbidden automatically on account of the non-Jew's *kocho* — the energy he exerted in causing it to flow — or only if a non-Jew actually touches it at that stage? That question will recur throughout the daf.

### Rav Huna: When Flowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: יַיִן, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִתְחִיל לְהִמָּשֵׁךְ, עוֹשֶׂה יֵין נֶסֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Huna says:** With regard to **wine, once it begins to flow** toward the exit from the winepress it can **become wine** used for **a libation** in idol worship if touched by a gentile.

Against the *mishna rishona*, the *gemara* introduced the position of Rav Huna: *keivan she'hitchil lehimashech* — from the moment the juice begins to flow, it acquires the status of *yayin* and becomes forbidden, even before it reaches the *bor*. The *gemara* sought initially to reconcile this with the *mishna* but ultimately concluded that Rav Huna's ruling represents a later revision — *mishna achrona* — that supersedes the earlier position. The tradition records an evolution: first the ruling was *b'dad*, then *b'dabbah*, reflecting successive tightenings of the standard.

### Tosafos: Pooling vs. Leaking

Tosafos engages a fine-grained question about the precise meaning of *hitchil lehimashech*. Does this threshold require that the liquid actually exit the barrel — that it literally "begin to flow" outward — or is it triggered even earlier, when juice merely pools at the bottom of the pressing barrel before leaving it? The plain sense of the word *lehimashech* — to be drawn out — suggests the former: actual egress from the barrel. But if a small barrel is being used and the juice might sometimes pool at the bottom without escaping, whether that would already constitute a problem is a live question. What is clear, at minimum, is that as long as the juice is entirely mixed in with the grape mass and has not pooled at all, it is certainly not yet *yayin*.

### Mesayeia Assisting Issue

A separate issue arises in connection with the prohibition of *mesayei'a lidey ovrei aveira* — assisting one who commits a transgression. A non-Jew performing the stomping is not himself violating a prohibition related to *tumah*, but a Jew who stomps *b'tumah* is, and one may not participate alongside him once the transgression is underway. The debate between Rashi and Tosafos concerns the outer boundary of *mesayei'a*: does it extend to preparatory acts — gathering the grapes or setting up the press — that lead toward but precede the *aveira* itself? Once the *aveira* is definitively complete, participation is no longer an issue, but the scope of the prohibition during the ongoing process is a significant question with implications well beyond this sugya.

## Yayin Nesech vs. Ma'aser

### Ma'aser: When Skins Float

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיתוֹ עוֹשֶׂה יֵין נֶסֶךְ עַד שֶׁיֵּרֵד לְבוֹר. וְהָתַנְיָא: יַיִן מִשֶּׁיְּקַפֶּה!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the gentile's touch **does not render** the juice of the grapes into **wine** used for **a libation until it descends into the** collection **vat,** because until then it does not have the status of wine. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a mishna (*Ma'asrot* 1:7) with regard to the stage when the work of producing the wine is considered complete and one is obligated to separate tithes from the wine: With regard to **wine,** one is obligated to separate tithes **from** the time **that** the solid residue **floats [*misheyikpe*]** on top of the wine.

The discussion now broadens by introducing a *mishna* from the tractate dealing with *ma'aser* — tithes. That *mishna* states: *yayin misheyikpeh* — wine becomes obligated in *terumos u'ma'asros* from the point at which the grape skins float to the surface. According to Rashi, this refers to a layer of grape skins or lees that rises to the top of the collecting wine in the *bor*. This evidently occurs not the instant the wine enters the *bor*, but at some later point during the collection process. The tension is immediate: if wine becomes subject to *terumos u'ma'asros* only at this later stage, why should it become forbidden as *yayin nesech* at the earlier stage of entering the *bor*?

### Rava's Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הָא רַבָּנַן, דִּתְנַן: יַיִן מִשֶּׁיֵּרֵד לַבּוֹר, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיְּקַפֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It is **not difficult. That** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** whereas **this** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** This is **as we learned** in a *baraita*: With regard to **wine,** one is obligated to separate tithes **from** the time **that** the wine **descends into the** collection **vat. Rabbi Akiva says:** One is obligated to separate tithes only **from** the time **that** the solid residue **floats** on top of the wine.

Rava's initial response is that the apparent contradiction is no contradiction at all: *lo kashya, ha Rabbi Akiva, ha rabbanan* — the two standards simply reflect a *tannaim* dispute in the domain of *terumos u'ma'asros* itself. A *baraisa* teaches: *yayin misheyared labor* — wine becomes obligated when it descends into the cistern. Rabbi Akiva, however, maintains: *misheyikpeh* — only when the skins float. The *mishna* in *Ma'asros* that says *misheyikpeh* is Rabbi Akiva's position; the standard of *yayin misheyared labor* is the *Tanna Kamma*, and that is the same position as our *mishna* on *yayin nesech*.

### Kipui: Cistern or Barrels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: קִיפּוּי דְּבוֹר, אוֹ קִיפּוּי דְּחָבִית?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is Rabbi Akiva referring to the **floating** of the seeds on top of the wine **in the** collection **vat or** to the **floating** of the yeast on top of the wine **in** the **barrel?**

The *gemara* then raises a further question: *kipui debor o kipui dechavis* — when Rabbi Akiva says the skins must float, does he mean floating in the *bor* itself, or floating after the wine has been transferred into storage barrels? The latter would obviously represent a considerably later stage. This is not a trivial question, since the practical workflows are quite different.

### Drawing from Gat Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: יַיִן — מִשֶּׁיְּקַפֶּה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁקִּפָּה, קוֹלֵט מִן הַגַּת הָעֶלְיוֹנָה וּמִן הַצִּינּוֹר וְשׁוֹתֶה. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קִיפּוּי דְּבוֹר קָאָמְרִינַן, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution of this issue, **as it is taught** in that mishna: With regard to **wine,** one is obligated to separate tithes **from** the time **that** the solid residue **floats** on top of the wine. Subsequently, **even though** the residue **has floated,** one **may collect** wine **from the upper winepress and from the pipe** that connects the upper winepress to the vat, **and** one **may drink** it without separating tithes. **Conclude from** the mishna that **we are speaking** about the **floating** of the seeds on top of the wine **in** the collection **vat.** The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from it** that it is so.

A proof is adduced from a *baraisa*: *yayin misheyikpeh* — and yet, *af al pi shekappah*, even after the skins have floated, one may still draw wine from the upper *gat* or from the *tzinor* and drink it without having separated *terumos u'ma'asros*. The inference is clear: since the *baraisa* contrasts the floating stage with the ability to still draw from the *gat* and *tzinor*, the *kipui* in question must be the floating *within the bor*. If *kipui* referred to floating in the barrels, there would be nothing remarkable about permitting the use of wine still sitting in the *gat* — that wine would be in an entirely different part of the process. The *gemara* concludes: *shema mina kipui debor ka-amran* — the floating in the cistern is what is meant.

### Three-Stage Tannaim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָנֵי רַב זְבִיד בִּדְבֵי רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: יַיִן — מִשֶּׁיֵּרֵד לַבּוֹר וִיקַפֶּה, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיְּשַׁלֶּה בֶּחָבִיּוֹת! תָּרְצַהּ נָמֵי לְהָךְ קַמַּיְיתָא הָכִי: יַיִן — מִשֶּׁיֵּרֵד לַבּוֹר וִיקַפֶּה, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיְּשַׁלֶּה בֶּחָבִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rav Zevid teach** a different version of the aforementioned dispute **in** a *baraita* **of the school of Rabbi Oshaya:** With regard to **wine,** one is obligated to separate tithes **from** the time **that** the wine **descends into the** collection **vat and** the seeds **float** on top. **Rabbi Akiva says:** One is obligated to separate tithes only **from** the time **that** one **siphons** the wine from the vat **into barrels.** The Gemara replies that one can resolve the contradiction: **Explain that first** *baraita* in **this** manner **as well:** With regard to **wine,** one is obligated to separate tithes **from** the time **that** the wine **descends into the** collection **vat and** the seeds **float** on top. **Rabbi Akiva says:** One is obligated to separate tithes only **from** the time **that** one **siphons** the wine from the vat **into barrels.**

At this point the analysis is complicated by a second *baraisa*, taught in the name of Rabbi Oshaya, which sets out a different version of the debate. According to this *baraisa*, the *Tanna Kamma* does not simply say *misheyared labor*; rather, his position is *misheyared labor veyikpeh* — both the descent into the cistern and the floating of the skins are required. Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, holds an even later standard: *misheyishleh bachaviyos* — only when the wine is scooped into the storage barrels. Under this reading, every position has shifted one stage later. The first *baraisa* can be harmonized with this by reading *misheyared labor* in the first *baraisa* as also implying *veyikpeh* — the two are conflated — and Rabbi Akiva's position there likewise corresponds to the barrel stage.

But this creates a new problem. Our *mishna* on *yayin nesech* holds that the wine becomes forbidden already at the simple *misheyared labor* stage, without any requirement of *kipui*. Under the revised reading of the *braitot*, no *tanna* holds that position for purposes of *terumos u'ma'asros*. The *gemara* therefore asks: *layma telata tannai hi* — should we conclude there is a three-way *tannaim* debate, with one position (the *bor* alone), a second (the *bor* plus floating), and a third (the barrels)? That would mean the position of our *mishna* on *yayin nesech* corresponds to a third *tanna* who also applies that earliest standard to *terumos u'ma'asros*.

### Rava: Yayin Nesech Stricter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי: אֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה יֵין נֶסֶךְ עַד שֶׁיֵּרֵד לַבּוֹר, לֵימָא תְּלָתָא תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? לָא, שָׁאנֵי יֵין נֶסֶךְ דְּאַחְמִירוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** whose opinion is expressed in **the mishna that teaches:** The gentile **does not render** the juice of the grapes into **wine** used for **a libation until it descends into the** collection **vat? Shall we say** that the stage when the work of producing the wine is considered complete **is** subject to a dispute between **three *tanna'im*?** The Gemara replies: **No,** the prohibition of **wine** used for **a libation is different, as the Sages were stringent concerning it** and render the wine forbidden even before the seeds float. By contrast, with regard to tithes, the *tanna* of this mishna holds in accordance with the opinion of either Rabbi Akiva or the Rabbis.

The *gemara* offers an alternative: *shani yayin nesech de'achmiru bei rabbanan* — perhaps *yayin nesech* is simply more stringent, and the rabbis applied an earlier threshold uniquely to this domain. This would mean that even if no *tanna* holds the bare *bor* standard for *terumos u'ma'asros*, the rabbis set that stricter threshold for *yayin nesech* — or in this context, for *stam yeinam* (the rabbinic prohibition on wine handled by non-Jews). One might note that *terumos u'ma'asros* are themselves *de'Oraisa*, whereas *stam yeinam* is *derabbanan*, which makes the stricter treatment of the rabbinic prohibition in *yayin nesech* somewhat surprising — though not inconceivable given the particular gravity with which the rabbis treated the *avoda zara* context.

### Three Positions Both Domains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבָא, דְּלָא שָׁאנֵי לֵיהּ, מוֹקֵים לֵיהּ כִּתְלָתָא תַּנָּאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And according to Rava, who** explains that the mishna with regard to wine used for libations is in accordance with one opinion whereas the mishna with regard to tithes is in accordance with another opinion, and **he does not** hold that the case of wine used for a libation **is different, he interprets it as** a dispute between **three *tanna'im*.**

This is precisely where Rava's approach comes into focus. The *gemara* notes: *u'leRava de'lo shani leh* — Rava, who originally posed the contradiction between the two domains and sought to resolve it by assigning the positions to different *tannaim*, evidently did not accept the distinction between *stam yeinam* and *terumos u'ma'asros*. Had he been willing to invoke that distinction, he could have answered immediately: the *bor* standard applies only to *yayin nesech* because the rabbis were stricter there. Instead, Rava tried to map the positions onto each other across both domains. This means Rava would maintain that there are indeed three *tannaim* positions, and those three positions apply equally to both *yayin nesech* and *terumos u'ma'asros*. Tosafos further notes that once one factors in the *mishna achrona*'s position — *misheyachol lehimashech*, from when it begins to flow — there is arguably a still earlier fourth stage. Whether Rava would extend the parallel all the way to that position or treat it differently is worth considering, though the *gemara* does not resolve this fully.

## Strainer Returned to Gat

### Rav Huna: Return Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַה שֶּׁבַּבּוֹר אָסוּר וְהַשְּׁאָר מוּתָּר. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא הֶחֱזִיר גַּרְגּוּתְנִי לַגַּת, אֲבָל הֶחְזִיר גַּרְגּוּתְנִי לַגַּת — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Once the wine descends into the collection vat, **that which is in the** collection **vat** is **prohibited, and the rest,** which did not yet descend into the collection vat, is **permitted. Rav Huna says:** The mishna **taught** that the rest of the wine is permitted **only** in a case **where he did not return** the **wicker basket [*gargutni*],** which is used to strain the wine on the way to the vat, **to the winepress. But** if **he returned** the **wicker basket to the winepress,** even the wine remaining in the winepress is **prohibited,** as the wine in the basket renders the wine in the winepress forbidden.

The *gemara* now turns to a practical ruling: *mah shebabor assur vehashear mutar* — what has entered the *bor* is forbidden and the rest is permissible. This is again operating within the framework of the *mishna rishona*, that only what is in the *bor* has become *yayin*. Rav Huna qualifies this: *lo shanu ela shelo hechzir gargusni legat* — this leniency applies only if the *gargusni* has not been returned to the *gat*. But *aval hechzir gargusni legat assur* — if the strainer has been returned to the press, everything becomes forbidden.

### Gargusni Description

The *gargusni* is a woven basket or strainer — a sieve — positioned at the mouth of the pipe leading into the *bor*. As the pressed juice flows from the *gat* toward the *bor*, it passes through this strainer, which separates the liquid from the bulk of the grape skins and seeds. The wine passes through and collects in the *bor*; the solids are left behind or caught by the strainer. The reason one might return the *gargusni* to the *gat* is presumably to extract more juice: the grape mass is brought back to the press for additional pressing, and it makes sense to reuse the strainer to run those additional pressings through the same filtration.

When the *gargusni* is returned to the *gat* after having been positioned near the *bor*, it carries with it residual drops of *yayin nesech* — drops that have already descended into the *bor* or at minimum were already at the threshold. Those drops, when returned to the *gat*, mix with the remaining grape must in the press and render it forbidden. The *gemara* will have to address why the strainer itself is considered forbidden — a question it handles below.

### Tosafos: Bitul Problem

Tosafos raises a sharp question. Even granting that the drops of wine on the *gargusni* are forbidden, the grape mass in the *gat* vastly exceeds sixty times the volume of those few drops. Why does the forbidden wine not become *batel* — nullified — in the much larger quantity of grapes? Tosafos frames the question precisely: *mistama yesh bagat yoter mishishim mitipot hayayin shebagargusni* — there is surely more than sixty times the quantity of the drops in the *gat*. Standard rules of *bitul* would seem to dictate that those drops should be nullified rather than prohibiting the entire contents of the press.

### Tosafos: Grapes vs. Wine

The answer to this question turns on a fundamental debate: are grapes and grape juice considered *min bemino* — the same category — or *eino mino* — different categories? The practical stakes are significant. If they are *min bemino*, then even a minute quantity of the forbidden substance (*b'mashehu*) prohibits the mixture, and no ratio of sixty-to-one provides relief. If they are *eino mino*, then the standard ratio of sixty applies, and the tiny amount of wine would be nullified in the much larger volume of grapes.

Tosafos traces this to the debate between Abaye and Rava recorded later in the tractate. The question is whether *min bemino* and *eino mino* are determined by *shma* — what the substance is called — or by *taam* — how it tastes. If taste determines the category, then grapes and grape juice taste essentially the same, making them *min bemino*, and *b'mashehu* applies with no possibility of *bitul*. If nomenclature determines the category, then "grapes" and "grape juice" are called by different names and would be *eino mino*, making the standard sixty-to-one ratio applicable.

Tosafos entertains the possibility that the ruling of Rav Huna follows Abaye — that grape juice in grape mass is considered *min bemino* based on taste, and therefore *b'mashehu* makes the entire contents forbidden. He also offers an alternative: perhaps Rav Huna follows the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda that *min bemino b'mashehu b'chol issur shebaTorah* — that for all Torah-prohibited substances, even a trace amount of *min bemino* is prohibited. That said, Tosafos notes, this still does not fully resolve the difficulty, since the grapes themselves are not the same thing as wine, and the *eino mino* problem persists.

For practical *halacha*, Tosafos notes the important ruling of Rabbeinu Tam (as explained later in *perek basra*) that *stam yeinam* — as opposed to actual *yayin nesech* — is subject to *bitul* in sixty, not *b'mashehu*. This distinction between *yayin nesech* and *stam yeinam* with respect to *bitul* is a major point in *Rishonim*, and the present case may not reflect the normative ruling.

### Gemara Q: Why Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּרְגּוּתְנִי גּוּפַהּ, בְּמַאי קָא מִיתַּסְרָא? בְּנִצּוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the wine in the **wicker basket itself, in what** manner **is it rendered forbidden?** Apparently, it is rendered forbidden **by** the **stream** of liquid that flows from the basket into the vat below, thereby connecting the wine in the basket to the forbidden wine in the vat.

Having addressed why the drops of wine forbid the *gat*, the *gemara* now turns to the prior question: *gargusni kfufa b'mai kamissari* — how does the strainer itself become forbidden? It has not yet descended into the *bor*. According to the *mishna rishona*, wine is only *yayin nesech* once it enters the cistern, and the strainer sits above that threshold.

## Nitzok Principle

### Nitzok Chibbur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: נִצּוֹק חִיבּוּר! כִּדְתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: שֶׁפְּחָסַתּוּ צְלוֹחִיתוֹ. הָכָא נָמֵי: שֶׁפְּחָסַתּוּ בּוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **conclude from** Rav Huna's statement that an uninterrupted **stream** of liquid is considered **a connection.** The Gemara rejects the inference: The reason for Rav Huna's statement could be different, **as Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** that if one fills a gentile's flask through a funnel, the wine in the funnel is rendered forbidden in a case **where** the level of the wine in **his flask** rose and **reached** the funnel. **Here too,** Rav Huna is referring to a case **where** the level of the wine in **his** collection **vat** rose and **reached** the basket.

The *gemara*'s answer is *b'nitzok* — because of the principle of *nitzok chibbur*. The wine that has already collected in the *bor* is forbidden. That wine is connected by a continuous stream to the wine still in the strainer above. The principle of *nitzok chibbur* holds that a flowing liquid connection constitutes a legal link, making the strainer's contents part of the same forbidden mass. The *gemara* then asks: *shema mina nitzok chibbur* — does this *gemara* constitute a proof that flowing liquid establishes such a connection?

### Nitzok vs. Water Level

The *gemara* is reluctant to commit to that conclusion, since *nitzok chibbur* is itself a contested principle. It therefore proposes an alternative explanation following a teaching of Rabbi Chiya: *shepachasta boro* — the cistern has been flattened against the basket, meaning the cistern is so full that the wine level has risen high enough to directly contact the bottom of the strainer. The strainer is not being rendered forbidden through the principle of a flowing connection; rather, the already-forbidden wine in the cistern has physically risen to touch the strainer from below. This avoids invoking *nitzok chibbur* and leaves that principle's status unresolved.

### Rashi: Nitzok Bar Nitzok

If one does accept *nitzok chibbur* as operative, a further question arises: why does the effect not travel further back — back through the *tzinor* and into the *gat* itself? The entire system, from *gat* to pipe to strainer to *bor*, is one continuous flow. Rashi's answer is that *nitzok bar nitzok* — a flow that derives from another flow — does not create a connection. The connection established by *nitzok* extends one degree, but not two. Tosafos explains that this second-degree quality arises from a change in the direction or character of the flow: the wine strikes the strainer, collects, and then exits from the side, so that the flow out of the strainer is not a direct continuation of the flow from above but rather a new, secondary stream emerging from the impact. In such a case, the second-degree connection does not propagate the prohibition further upstream.

### Tosafos Example: Pouring from Flask

Tosafos applies this principle to a practical case that came before Rashi himself. A non-Jew poured wine from a *kankan* (a flask or pitcher) into a cup. Since the non-Jew's *kocho* — the force of his arm — drove the wine out of the *kankan*, the wine in the cup is forbidden. The wine remaining in the *kankan* is then also forbidden because of *nitzok*: the continuous stream connecting the *kankan* to the cup renders the contents of the *kankan* connected to the forbidden wine in the cup. Subsequently, a Jew drew wine from a large barrel into that same (now-forbidden) *kankan*. The question is whether the *nitzok* from the *kankan* to the barrel now renders the wine in the barrel forbidden as well. Rashi ruled leniently: this is *nitzok bar nitzok*, a second-degree flow, and the prohibition does not travel that far. The wine in the *kankan* remains forbidden, but the wine in the barrel is unaffected.

### Nitzok Debate: Wine Status Matters

An alternative approach, distinct from Rashi's, rejects the framework entirely. This position argues that the question of why the prohibition does not travel back from the strainer all the way to the *gat* has nothing to do with *nitzok bar nitzok*. The answer is simpler: the juice in the *gat* is not yet *yayin*. Even direct contact with a non-Jew would not render it *yayin nesech*, because it lacks the legal status of wine. Naturally, then, *nitzok* — which presupposes that the connected substance is wine — cannot extend a prohibition to something that is not yet wine. Under this reading, the *chiddush* of the *gemara* is not about degrees of *nitzok* at all, but about the status of what is in the *gat*.

This reading, however, requires a reformulation of the basket case. If the juice in the *gat* is not wine, why is the juice in the strainer wine? The answer, under this approach, is that once the juice has reached the strainer — *gargusni* — it is already considered to have entered the category of *yarad labor*. The strainer is functionally equivalent to the *bor* for these purposes. On this reading, the *gargusni* case is not about *nitzok* connecting pre-wine to wine; rather, the contents of the strainer are already halachically wine, and when the non-Jew touches the wine in the *bor*, the *nitzok* connection renders his touch effective for the wine in the strainer as well, since they are all wine at that point. What *nitzok* cannot do is reach back further, into the *gat*, where the material is not yet wine.

This generates a sharp conceptual divergence. Rashi's approach turns on the architecture of the flow — degrees of remove. The alternative turns on the ontological status of the liquid — whether it has yet become *yayin*. Both positions agree that the strainer is forbidden and the *gat* is not; they disagree entirely about why.

## Six-Year-Old Prodigy Story

### Question: Stomp Together

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא יָנוֹקָא דִּתְנָא עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּשֵׁית שְׁנֵי, בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ: מַהוּ לִדְרוֹךְ עִם הַנׇּכְרִי בַּגַּת? אֲמַר לְהוּ: דּוֹרְכִין עִם הַנׇּכְרִי בַּגַּת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** outstanding **child who learned** the tractate of ***Avoda Zara* when** he was **six years** old. People **raised a dilemma to him: What is** the *halakha*? Is it permitted **to tread** on grapes **in the winepress** together **with the gentile?** The child **said to them:** One **may tread** on grapes **in the winepress** together **with the gentile,** in accordance with the ruling of the mishna.

The *gemara* introduces a remarkable figure: *hahu yanuka de-tana avodat kochavim be-shish shanim* — a young child who had mastered *masechet Avodah Zarah* at the age of six. Rashi and Rabbeinu Chananel both explain that the six years refers not to the duration of his study but to his age: at six years old he had memorized and thoroughly learned the entire tractate. They posed him the question: *mahu lidroch im oved kochavim begat* — is it permissible to stomp grapes together with a non-Jew? The child answered: *dorchin im oved kochavim begat* — yes, it is permitted. That answer reflects the position of the *mishna*, at least the *mishna rishona*, that the grape juice in the *gat* has not yet become *yayin*.

### Hand Touch Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָא מְנַסֵּךְ בִּידֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** They asked the child: **But doesn't** the gentile **render** the wine **a libation** by touching it **with his hands,** rendering it forbidden? If so, how may a Jew derive benefit from the wine by receiving payment for his work?

The *gemara* challenges this immediately: *veha kemenasach beyadeih* — but the non-Jew's hand might touch the wine, making it *yayin nesech*! Now, given the *mishna rishona*, one might have simply answered that the juice in the *gat* is not yet wine, so there is nothing to forbid. But Rashi and Tosafos both explain that the question operates under the assumption of *mishna achrona*, which treats the juice as *yayin* from the moment stomping begins. Under that assumption, the non-Jew's hand touching the grape-wine mixture would render it forbidden — and if it is *yayin nesech*, a Jew would be forbidden to receive wages for this work, since it involves *issurei hana'ah*.

### Hands Tied Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּצָיַירְנָא לְהוּ לִידֵיהּ. וְהָא קָא מְנַסֵּךְ בְּרֶגֶל? נִיסּוּךְ דְּרֶגֶל לָא שְׁמֵיהּ נִיסּוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The child replied: It is permitted in a case **where we tie his hands** so that he cannot pour the wine as a libation. The child was then asked: **But doesn't** the gentile **render** the wine **a libation** by touching it **with** his **foot?** The child replied: **Rendering** wine **a libation** by touching it **with** one's **foot is not considered rendering** it **a libation.**

The child's response is *ditzarinan lehu leyadeih* — we tie his hands behind his back. Ensuring that the non-Jew cannot use his hands eliminates the concern about hand contact. But the *gemara* presses further: *veha ka mansei lei beregel* — his feet are still touching the wine as he stomps! Shouldn't foot contact create the same problem?

The answer is *nisuch beregel lo shemei nisuch* — touching wine with one's foot does not constitute *nisuch*, and therefore does not render it *assur behana'ah*. The logic is that the prohibition of *yayin nesech* at its most severe level — *assur behana'ah* — is predicated on an act that could plausibly function as a libation to *avoda zara*. A libation performed with one's foot is simply not something anyone would do as an act of worship; it cannot reach the level of genuine *nisuch*. Therefore, foot contact — while it may render the wine *assur bishitiyah* — does not make it *assur behana'ah*, and a Jew may therefore continue working alongside a non-Jew who is stomping with his feet, as long as the non-Jew's hands are bound.

## Shmuel's Three Festivals

### Shmuel Waits for Festivals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא עוֹבָדָא דַּהֲוָה בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא, דְּדָשׁוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹי לְהָהוּא חַמְרָא, וְשַׁהֲיֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל תְּלָתָא רִיגְלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **There was a certain incident in Neharde'a** in **which a Jew and a gentile trod** on grapes and produced **a certain wine** together, **and Shmuel delayed** ruling on the matter for **three Festivals,** as the Sages gathered during the Festivals and presented Shmuel with the opportunity to clarify the matter with them.

A related incident is recorded: *hahu uvda dehavai benaharda* — a case that arose in Nehardea in which Jews and non-Jews stomped grapes together. Shmuel did not immediately rule on the matter. Instead, *ushiya Shmuel telasa riglei* — he held the question over for three *regalim*. The reason is that at each *yom tov* pilgrimage festival, a larger gathering of scholars convened, providing an opportunity for broader discussion. Shmuel wanted to crowd-source the analysis before rendering a decision.

### Hand vs. Foot Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר: דְּאִי מַשְׁכַּחְנָא תַּנָּא דְּאָסַר כְּרַבִּי נָתָן, אוֹסְרִינֵּיהּ אֲפִילּוּ בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that Shmuel delayed his ruling? **If we say** that Shmuel delayed ruling on the matter **because he thought** to himself: **as if I find a *tanna* who prohibits** the wine **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan, I will prohibit even** deriving **benefit from it;** this is difficult.

The *gemara* asks: what was Shmuel trying to determine? Two possible lines of inquiry are identified. One possibility is that Shmuel was looking for a *tanna* who rules like Rabbi Natan — whose stringent position would make the wine *assur behana'ah* even in the stomping context. The other possibility is precisely the opposite: that Shmuel was hoping to find a *tanna* who rules like Rabbi Shimon — whose lenient position would make the wine permissible even for drinking.

### Measuring Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מְדָדוֹ, בֵּין בַּיָּד בֵּין בָּרֶגֶל — יִמָּכֵר. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: בַּיָּד — אָסוּר, בָּרֶגֶל — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a gentile **measured** a Jew's wine, **whether** he measured it **with** his **hand or with** his **foot, it may be sold. Rabbi Natan says:** If he measured it **with** his **hand** it is **prohibited,** but if he measured it **with** his **foot** it is **permitted.**

A *baraisa* is introduced: *medado* — if someone measured the depth of wine in a barrel by inserting a limb. The *Tanna Kamma* rules: *bein beyad bein beregel, yimacher otah* — whether by hand or by foot, the wine may be sold, i.e., it is not rendered *yayin nesech* of the highest severity (*assur behana'ah*), because the clear intent of the measurer was measurement, not libation. This is fundamentally a *kavanah* — intent — analysis.

### Natan: Hand Forbidden

Rabbi Natan, however, draws a sharp distinction: *beyad assur, beregel mutar* — contact by hand renders the wine forbidden; contact by foot does not. Rabbi Natan's ruling is not tied to the question of *kavanah*; he holds that even incidental hand contact, without any intent toward *nisuch*, renders the wine *assur*. This is a notably stringent position.

### Gemara Challenge on Stomping

The *gemara* interrogates whether Rabbi Natan's distinction between hand and foot would also apply in the stomping context. In the measuring case, the non-Jew's foot is clearly incidental — he is not stomping, he is measuring. But in the *gat*, the non-Jew is actively stomping with his feet. Could stomping with the feet — which involves a back-and-forth pressing motion, perhaps akin to *shichshuch* — be treated differently by Rabbi Natan than mere incidental foot contact? After all, *shichshuch* — the mixing or agitating motion — more closely resembles a libation act than a simple measurement touch. The *gemara* leaves open whether Rabbi Natan's leniency for foot contact would extend to the more dynamic context of stomping.

### Shimon: Intent Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵלָּא דְּאִי מַשְׁכַּחְנָא תַּנָּא דְּשָׁרֵי כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, אֶישְׁרְיֵיהּ אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁתִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Shmuel delayed ruling on the matter **because** he thought to himself: **If I find** another ***tanna* who permits** the wine **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that if a gentile touches wine without intending to render it a libation, it is permitted, **I will permit** the wine **even for drinking.**

The other direction Shmuel might have been exploring was the position of Rabbi Shimon. As Rashi explains in the narrow lines: *deshari magash shel oved kochavim shelo niskavein lenasech* — Rabbi Shimon permits the wine for drinking if the non-Jew's contact was not performed with the intention of *nisuch*, of libation. Under Rabbi Shimon's approach, the entire architecture of *stam yeinam* collapses dramatically: unless there is positive evidence that the non-Jew intended his contact as a libation, the wine remains permissible even for drinking. In the ordinary context of a non-Jew stomping grapes for commercial wine production, there is clearly no such intent, and the wine would be fully permitted.

### Summary: Positions Range

The daf thus closes with a wide spectrum of opinions on what type of contact produces what level of prohibition. The variables are multiple: Does contact have to be with the hand or is foot contact also legally significant? Does intent — *kavanah* — to perform a libation matter? Does the nature of the contact matter — mere touch versus *shichshuch*, the agitating motion? And what is the resulting level of prohibition — *assur bishitiyah* (forbidden to drink), *assur behana'ah* (forbidden to derive any benefit), or no prohibition at all? The range of *tannaitic* positions maps these variables onto three possible outcomes, and the daf as a whole leaves the practical resolution for continued analysis.
