# Rosh Hashanah 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Megillat Ta'anit Status

### Rav vs. Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַב וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמְרִי: בָּטְלָה מְגִילַּת תַּעֲנִית. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמְרִי: לֹא בָּטְלָה מְגִילַּת תַּעֲנִית.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the Sages disagreed about the following matter: **Rav and Rabbi Ḥanina** both **say: *Megillat Ta'anit*,** a listing of days on which fasting and eulogizing are forbidden, **has been nullified,** as in the present period of exile there is no reason to celebrate the joyous events that these days commemorate. **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi say: *Megillat Ta'anit* has not been nullified.**

The daf opens with an *itmar* — a recorded dispute among Amoraim — presenting two diametrically opposed positions regarding *Megillat Ta'anit*. Rav and Rabbi Chanina (some versions read Rav Huna and Rabbi Chanina) hold *batla Megillat Ta'anit* — the scroll of fasts has been annulled. Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi disagree, maintaining *lo batla Megillat Ta'anit* — its halakhic status remains intact.

### What is Megillat Ta'anit

*Megillat Ta'anit* is an ancient document dating from the Second Temple period, listing all the dates on which fasting and *hesped* (eulogizing) are prohibited — minor festivals that were decreed for the Jewish people, many connected to events of the Temple era or to the Hasmonean period. Chanukah, as we will see, is among the days listed. The document is notable for its brevity: each entry consists of the date and a terse half-sentence explanation of the historical event it commemorates, making it a source of genuine historical significance for the Second Temple period. It is written in Aramaic — the vernacular of the time — and was actually committed to writing even during the era of oral tradition, which is why it is called a *megillah* in the first place.

We do possess manuscript copies of *Megillat Ta'anit*, though not from the original period, and there is an excellent modern critical edition by the Israeli scholar Vered Noam. In addition to the core text, there exists a *scholion* — later elaborations written in the Talmudic or post-Talmudic period — which at times reflects the Gemara's own discussions of the entries. This is relevant to the *sugyot* surrounding Chanukah as well: the *scholion* traditions about the miracle of the oil show evidence of having been shaped and hybridized by the Gemara's own treatment of the topic.

The central halakhic question, then, is whether the status of these minor festival days endures. Most of the events they commemorate were tied to the Second Temple and have little resonance for later generations. Did those days retain their character as minor *yamim tovim*, continuing to prohibit fasting and eulogizing? Or, with the absence of the Temple and the changed circumstances of exile, did they lose that status?

### Five Fast Days Reversed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמְרִי בָּטְלָה מְגִילַּת תַּעֲנִית, הָכִי קָאָמַר: בִּזְמַן שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁלוֹם — יִהְיוּ לְשָׂשׂוֹן וּלְשִׂמְחָה, אֵין שָׁלוֹם — צוֹם. וְהָנָךְ נָמֵי כִּי הָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rav and Rabbi Ḥanina say** that ***Megillat Ta'anit* has been nullified. This is what** the prophet **is saying: At a time when there is peace** in the world, the dates listed **will be** times of **joy and gladness,** on which eulogies and fasting are forbidden; but when **there is no peace,** they are days of **fasting. And those** days mentioned in *Megillat Ta'anit* **are also like these** days of fasting, that is to say, the days of joy listed in *Megillat Ta'anit* are also nullified when there is no peace.

The Gemara begins its analysis by connecting the question to the earlier discussion of the five fast days mentioned in Zechariah. The verse may be read as follows: when there is peace — *bizman sheyesh shalom* — those days that had been fast days will turn into days of rejoicing; when there is no peace — *ein shalom, tzom* — they revert to being fast days. Rav and Rabbi Chanina apply this paradigm to *Megillat Ta'anit*: just as the five fast days lose their character as days of rejoicing when the *Beit Hamikdash* is absent and revert to their original nature as days of mourning, so too the minor holidays of *Megillat Ta'anit* lose their festive status in the post-*churban* reality.

The analogy is admittedly strange. The five fast days began as days of tragedy — their natural state is mourning. During the era of the Second Temple they were transformed, paradoxically, into days of rejoicing, a kind of "last laugh" of history. With no *Beit Hamikdash*, they revert to that natural state. By contrast, the days in *Megillat Ta'anit* began as days of celebration — their natural state is festivity. One might argue they should retain that status even without the Temple. The very comparison thus seems to cut against Rav and Rabbi Chanina's position. Nevertheless, that is the analogy they offer.

### Fundamental vs. Contingent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמְרִי: לֹא בָּטְלָה מְגִילַּת תַּעֲנִית — הָנֵי הוּא דִּתְלִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא בְּבִנְיַן בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, אֲבָל הָנָךְ — כִּדְקָיְימִי קָיְימִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi say** that ***Megillat Ta'anit* has not been nullified,** and they reason as follows: **It was those** fast days mentioned in the Bible **that the Merciful One makes contingent on the building of the Temple, but these** festive days listed in *Megillat Ta'anit* **remain as they were** and have not been nullified.

Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi respond to this argument directly. It is specifically the five fast days whose festive status is *explicitly* tied by the verse to the existence of the *Beit Hamikdash* — *talia Rachmana bevinyan Beit Hamikdash*. Those days' transformation into days of joy was always conditional on the Temple's presence. But the days listed in *Megillat Ta'anit* carry no such condition. Their status as holidays is not contingent on the *Beit Hamikdash*, and therefore — *kidekai kai* — they stand on their own terms. Stated more simply: the five fast days are fundamentally fast days that were temporarily elevated; without the Temple they revert. The *Megillat Ta'anit* days are fundamentally holidays; without the Temple, they remain holidays.

## Lod Fast on Chanukah

### Lod Fast Declared

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב כָּהֲנָא: מַעֲשֶׂה וְגָזְרוּ תַּעֲנִית בַּחֲנוּכָּה בְּלוֹד, וְיָרַד רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרָחַץ, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְסִיפֵּר. וְאָמְרוּ לָהֶם: צְאוּ וְהִתְעַנּוּ עַל מַה שֶּׁהִתְעַנִּיתֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana raised an objection** against Rav and Rabbi Ḥanina from a *baraita*: **There was an incident and** the Sages **decreed a fast on Hanukkah in Lod, and Rabbi Eliezer went down** on that day **and bathed** in the bathhouse **and Rabbi Yehoshua went down and cut** his hair to show that they did not accept the fast. Furthermore, these two Sages **said to** the others: **Go out and fast** another fast as an act of penitence **for what you have** already **fasted,** as the days of Hanukkah are days of joy, on which fasting is forbidden. Hanukkah is one of the Festivals listed in *Megillat Ta'anit*. Even after the destruction of the Temple Hanukkah is celebrated, demonstrating that *Megillat Ta'anit* has not been nullified.

Rav Kahana raises a challenge from a recorded incident: *ma'aseh vegezeru ta'anit beChangukah beLod* — there was a case in Lod where a fast was declared on Chanukah. Whether the motivation was a need for rain or some other pressing circumstance, those who declared the fast were treating Chanukah as a day on which the prohibitions of *Megillat Ta'anit* no longer applied — that is, they were acting in accordance with the position of *batla Megillat Ta'anit*.

### Public Demonstration

The response of the Sages was swift and public. Rabbi Eliezer went and bathed, and Rabbi Yehoshua went and got a haircut — acts that explicitly violated the norms of a fast day, performed openly to demonstrate that they did not recognize the fast's validity. They then declared to those who had fasted: *tze'u vehit'anu al mah shehit'anitem* — go now and make a fast as *teshuvah* for having fasted. Their willingness to do so publicly shows that *lo batla Megillat Ta'anit* — the prohibition on fasting on Chanukah remained in force.

### Tosafot: Holiday vs. Fast

Before one concludes from this incident that it constitutes proof against *batla Megillat Ta'anit*, Tosafot makes an important observation. One might have thought that the Mishnah's continued treatment of Chanukah as a holiday — with candle-lighting and *Al Hanissim* — itself proves *lo batla Megillat Ta'anit*. Tosafot rejects this inference. It is entirely possible to hold *batla Megillat Ta'anit* and still light Chanukah candles. The candle-lighting and commemoration of the miracle stand independently of the question of *Megillat Ta'anit*'s annulment. *Batla Megillat Ta'anit* addresses only whether a day has the status of a minor *yom tov* such that fasting and *hesped* are forbidden on it — not whether other *mitzvot* associated with that day continue to be observed.

### Status Independence

This distinction is critical. What the people of Lod were doing was precisely this: they acknowledged Chanukah as a day of candle-lighting while treating the fasting prohibition as annulled — holding that *batla Megillat Ta'anit* removes only the *ta'anit* prohibition, not the observances connected to the miracle itself. Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua rejected that position, insisting that *lo batla Megillat Ta'anit* — Chanukah retains its full status including the prohibition on fasting. The halakhic status of Chanukah as a day of *hadlakat nerot* and its status as a day on which fasting is forbidden are conceptually separable, even if in practice both apply.

## Chanukah's Special Status

### Chanukah Has Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: שָׁאנֵי חֲנוּכָּה דְּאִיכָּא מִצְוָה. אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְתִיבְטַיל אִיהִי וְתִיבְטַל מִצְוָתָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Hanukkah is different, as there is the mitzva** of lighting candles, and so, unlike the other days listed in *Megillat Ta'anit*, the festival of Hanukkah was not nullified. **Abaye said to him:** What is this argument? **Let** Hanukkah **itself be nullified, and let its mitzva** of lighting candles **be nullified** with it.

Rav Yosef draws a conclusion from the Lod incident: the incident demonstrates *lo batla Megillat Ta'anit*, at least with respect to Chanukah. But this leads to an immediate qualification: *shani Chanukah d'ika mitzvah* — Chanukah is different because it has a specific *mitzvah*, namely candle-lighting. One cannot simply extrapolate from Chanukah's retained status to the retained status of all other days in *Megillat Ta'anit*.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye presses back: if we are in a position of *batla Megillat Ta'anit* for the other days, why not go further and eliminate Chanukah entirely — including its candle-lighting? Why should the existence of a *mitzvah* associated with a day preserve its status if we are annulling the framework within which that day's significance was established? In other words, if *batla Megillat Ta'anit* sweeps away the minor holidays, why does the associated *mitzvah* of Chanukah save it?

### Pirsumei Nisa Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, שָׁאנֵי חֲנוּכָּה דְּמִיפַּרְסַם נִיסָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef** retracted his previous explanation and **said: Hanukkah is different, as its miracle is well known,** and it has become so widely accepted by all the Jewish people that it would be inappropriate to nullify it.

Rav Yosef refines his answer: *shani Chanukah demefarsam nisa* — Chanukah is different because the miracle was very public. The principle of *pirsumei nisa* — publicizing the miracle — carries particular weight. Whether this means the miracle itself was objectively greater in its visibility, or that the value placed on publicizing it gives Chanukah an enduring significance that other *Megillat Ta'anit* days lack, the implication is that Chanukah occupies a distinct halakhic category that survives the general annulment.

### Rashi: Social Entrenchment

Rashi reads the situation somewhat differently. He writes: *kevar hu galuy lechol Yisrael al yedei shenahagu bo mitzvah vehiziku bo keshel Torah velo nachon levattla* — it is already manifest throughout all Israel, through the practice of its *mitzvah*, and they have treated it with the force of Torah law, and it would not be proper to annul it. For Rashi, Chanukah's retention is not primarily a function of objective factors like the nature of the miracle, but of social and communal entrenchment. It has become so deeply embedded in Jewish practice that reversing it is simply not a viable option — and that social reality is itself halakhically significant.

## 3rd Tishrei Holiday

### God's Name Removed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹתֵיב רַב אַחָא בַּר הוּנָא: בִּתְלָתָא בְּתִשְׁרִי בְּטֵילַת אַדְכָּרְתָּא מִן שְׁטָרַיָּיא. שֶׁגָּזְרָה מַלְכוּת יָוָן גְּזֵרָה שֶׁלֹּא לְהַזְכִּיר שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם עַל פִּיהֶם, וּכְשֶׁגָּבְרָה מַלְכוּת חַשְׁמוֹנַאי וְנִצְּחוּם הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ מַזְכִּירִין שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁטָרוֹת. וְכָךְ הָיוּ כּוֹתְבִים: בִּשְׁנַת כָּךְ וְכָךְ לְיוֹחָנָן כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל לְאֵל עֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Huna raised an objection:** It is stated in *Megillat Ta'anit*: **On the third of Tishrei the** ordinance requiring the **mention** of God's name **in** legal **documents was abolished,** and on that day fasting is forbidden. **For the kingdom of Greece had issued a decree** against the Jews **forbidding them to mention the name of Heaven on their lips. When the Hasmonean kingdom became strong and defeated** the Greeks, **they instituted that people should mention the name of Heaven even in their** legal **documents. And therefore they would write: In year such and such of Yoḥanan the High Priest of the God Most High.**

The Gemara now turns to another entry in *Megillat Ta'anit*, introduced as a further challenge: *be-telasa be-Tishrei beteilit adkarata min shtaraya* — on the third of Tishrei, the mention of God's name was removed from legal documents. This terse Aramaic formulation — characteristic of the original text — sounds at first like a negative development, but the explanation that follows reveals otherwise.

### Greek Decree & Reversal

The explanatory gloss reads: *she-gazra malchut Yavan gezerah shelo lehazkir Shem Shamayim al pihem* — the Greek kingdom decreed that the Jewish people could not mention God's name. When the Hasmoneans were victorious — *gavra malchut Chashmonai ve-nitzchum* — they instituted that God's name should be mentioned, even in legal documents. The formula they established for such documents was: in the year such-and-such of Yochanan the *Kohen Gadol*, *le-El Elyon* — to the Most High God. The presence of these Hasmonean entries in *Megillat Ta'anit* reflects the centrality of that period to the document as a whole.

### Desecration Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁשָּׁמְעוּ חֲכָמִים בַּדָּבָר, אָמְרוּ: לְמָחָר זֶה פּוֹרֵעַ אֶת חוֹבוֹ וְנִמְצָא שְׁטָר מוּטָּל בָּאַשְׁפָּה, וּבִיטְּלוּם, וְאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם עֲשָׂאוּהוּ יוֹם טוֹב. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתְּךָ בָּטְלָה מְגִילַּת תַּעֲנִית — קַמָּיָיתָא בְּטוּל, אַחְרָנְיָיתָא מוֹסִיפִין?!
>
> **Translation:** **And when the Sages heard about this they said: Tomorrow this one,** the borrower, **will repay his debt,** the lender will no longer need to save the loan document, **the document will be cast on a dunghill,** and the name of Heaven written there will come to disgrace. **And** so **they annulled** the ordinance to mention God's name in documents, **and they made that day into a Festival. And if it enters your mind** to say that ***Megillat Ta'anit* has been nullified,** can you say that **the first** prohibitions against fasting **they annulled, and** then **later** ones **were added?**

When the Sages heard of this practice of writing God's name in legal documents, they raised an objection: *limachara zeh poreh et hovo* — tomorrow a person will repay his debt, the IOU will be returned to him, and *ve-nimtza shtar mutal be-ashpah* — the document will wind up discarded in the trash, with God's name on it. The concern for the desecration of God's name in mundane discarded documents led the Sages to reverse the Hasmonean innovation and prohibit writing God's name in secular legal instruments.

### Made into Holiday

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** This is referring to a time **when the Temple was standing** and all the days listed in *Megillat Ta'anit* were in force. From time to time new days of commemoration were added. When the *amora'im* stated that *Megillat Ta'anit* was nullified they were referring to the time after the destruction of the Temple.

The success of this reversal — changing an entrenched practice in the business world — was itself considered a significant achievement, and the day was therefore made into a *yom tov*. It is worth noting that while coins and documents from this period do survive, including references to Yochanan the *Kohen Gadol*, no archaeological evidence of the formula *le-El Elyon* in documents has been found. Whether that means the practice never became widespread or that the Sages' intervention succeeded quickly is an open question.

## Tzom Gedaliah Problem

### Why Another Holiday?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ יוֹם שֶׁנֶּהֱרַג בּוֹ גְּדַלְיָה בֶּן אֲחִיקָם! אָמַר רַב: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לֶאֱסוֹר אֶת שֶׁלְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: But if this was at the time that the Temple was standing, **derive** the prohibition against fasting on the third of Tishrei from the fact that **it is the day that Gedaliah, son of Ahikam, was killed.** During the time of the Temple the biblical fast days were celebrated as days of joy. **Rav said: It was only necessary** to include the third of Tishrei in *Megillat Ta'anit* **in order to prohibit** fasting on **the preceding** day as well. Fasting was forbidden not only on the actual days listed in *Megillat Ta'anit*, but also on the preceding day and the following day.

Rav Acha bar Huna's question now generates one of the more striking dialectical moments in the *sugyia*. The Gemara assumes that the third of Tishrei entry was added to *Megillat Ta'anit* during the post-*churban* period — which would demonstrate *lo batla Megillat Ta'anit*, since they were still actively adding to it after the Temple's destruction. The Gemara initially tries to deflect this by saying the event occurred during the *Beit Hamikdash* era. But then a further question arises: if this happened during the time of the *Beit Hamikdash*, why was there any need to add the third of Tishrei as a minor holiday? During the Temple period, as we have established, the five classical fast days were transformed into days of rejoicing. Tzom Gedaliah — the fast of Gedaliah — was among them. The third of Tishrei is precisely the date of Tzom Gedaliah. So during the *Beit Hamikdash* period, it was already a holiday. Who needed to create a new one?

The irony is almost comic: Tzom Gedaliah, a fast commemorating a profound tragedy at the close of the First Temple period, had become a day of celebration — *lesason u'lesimcha* — during the Second Temple era. There was, so to speak, no fast to override. Why add it to *Megillat Ta'anit*?

### Strengthening via Days Before

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁלְּפָנָיו נָמֵי, תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ יוֹם שֶׁלְּאַחַר רֹאשׁ חֹדֶשׁ! רֹאשׁ חֹדֶשׁ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא לָא בָּעֵי חִיזּוּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: With regard to the prohibition against fasting on **the preceding day,** the second of Tishrei, **also derive it because it is** the **day after** the **New Moon,** and fasting is forbidden not only on festive days, but also on the preceding day and the following day. The Gemara rejects this argument: The **New Moon is by Torah law,** and festive days that are **by Torah law do not require reinforcement.** Therefore no decree was ever enacted prohibiting fasting on the days before and after.

The Gemara then raises a further objection: the day before the third of Tishrei is the second of Tishrei, which is the day after Rosh Hashanah — and since one does not fast on the day after a *yom tov*, why would any additional designation be necessary? The Gemara answers that Rosh Hashanah's status as a *yom tov* is biblical, and *d'Oraita lo baei chizuk* — biblical holidays do not require the halakhic "strengthening" that extends their prohibition to surrounding days. Only rabbinic designations carry that strengthening effect on adjacent days.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הַיָּמִים הָאֵלֶּה הַכְּתוּבִים בִּמְגִילַּת תַּעֲנִית אֲסוּרִין, בֵּין לִפְנֵיהֶם בֵּין לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם. שַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים — הֵם אֲסוּרִים, לִפְנֵיהֶן וּלְאַחֲרֵיהֶן מוּתָּרִין. מָה הֶפְרֵשׁ בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה? הַלָּלוּ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, וְאֵין דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה צְרִיכִין חִיזּוּק. הַלָּלוּ דִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים, וְדִבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים צְרִיכִין חִיזּוּק.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **These days that are written in *Megillat Ta'anit* are** days on which fasting is **prohibited,** as are **both** the day **before them and** the day **after them.** With regard to ***Shabbatot* and Festivals,** fasting on **them is forbidden,** but on the day **before them and** the day **after them** fasting **is permitted. What is the difference between this** class of days **and that** class of days? **These** days, *Shabbatot* and Festivals, **are** by **Torah law, and Torah laws do not need reinforcement,** and therefore even if a fast day were decreed on the day before or after them, the Festival itself would not be nullified; whereas **those** days mentioned in *Megillat Ta'anit* **are** by **rabbinic law, and rabbinic laws need reinforcement,** and therefore fasting is prohibited even on the day before and the day after.

A *Beraita* explicitly articulates this principle: the days written in *Megillat Ta'anit* — being *divrei sofrim*, rabbinic enactments — prohibit fasting on the days both before and after them; *Shabbat* and *yom tov* — being *divrei Torah*, biblical law — prohibit fasting only on the day itself, not on the surrounding days. *Divrei Torah lo tzrichim chizuk* — what rests on biblical authority does not need strengthening. *Divrei sofrim tzrichim chizuk* — rabbinic enactments do require that additional reinforcement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ יוֹם שֶׁלִּפְנֵי יוֹם שֶׁנֶּהֱרַג בּוֹ גְּדַלְיָה בֶּן אֲחִיקָם! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: גְּדַלְיָה בֶּן אֲחִיקָם דִּבְרֵי קַבָּלָה הוּא, וְדִבְרֵי קַבָּלָה כְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises yet another difficulty: The prohibition against fasting on the second of Tishrei, **derive it** from the fact that **it is the day before the day that Gedaliah, son of Ahikam, was killed,** and since in Temple times the fast of Gedaliah was celebrated as a festive day, fasting should also be prohibited on the preceding day. **Rav Ashi said:** The fast of **Gedaliah, son of Ahikam, is** derived from **the texts of the tradition,** i.e., Prophets and Writings, **and** as **the texts of the tradition are** treated **like Torah statements** for this purpose, they too do not need reinforcement.

The same logic applies to Tzom Gedaliah: though it had become a day of rejoicing during the Second Temple period, its halakhic basis rests on verses in Zechariah — *divrei kabbalah*, which the Gemara treats similarly to *divrei Torah*. Therefore, even as a holiday, Tzom Gedaliah would not extend its prohibition to the day before it. The Hasmonean-era addition of the third of Tishrei as a distinct *Megillat Ta'anit* day — a rabbinic designation — was thus purposeful: it extended the *ta'anit* prohibition to the second of Tishrei, something Tzom Gedaliah alone, as a quasi-biblical holiday, could not accomplish.

## 28 Adar: Roman Edict

### Roman Persecutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב טוֹבִי בַּר מַתְנָה: בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּתְמָנְיָא בֵּיהּ אֲתָת בְּשׂוֹרְתָּא טָבְתָּא לִיהוּדָאֵי דְּלָא יְעִידוֹן מֵאוֹרָיְיתָא. שֶׁגָּזְרָה מַלְכוּת הָרְשָׁעָה גְּזֵרָה שֶׁלֹּא יַעַסְקוּ בַּתּוֹרָה, וְשֶׁלֹּא יָמוּלוּ אֶת בְּנֵיהֶם, וְשֶׁיְּחַלְּלוּ שַׁבָּתוֹת. מָה עָשָׂה יְהוּדָה בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ וַחֲבֵירָיו? הָלְכוּ וְנָטְלוּ עֵצָה מִמַּטְרוֹנִיתָא אַחַת שֶׁכׇּל גְּדוֹלֵי רוֹמִי מְצוּיִין אֶצְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Tovi bar Mattana raised an objection** against the opinion that *Megillat Ta'anit* was nullified, from that which is written in it: **On the twenty-eighth of** Adar the **good tidings came to the Jews that they should not turn away from the Torah,** and on that day fasting is forbidden. And this is explained: **For the wicked kingdom issued a decree against Israel that they should not occupy themselves with Torah** study, **and that they should not circumcise their sons, and that they should desecrate Shabbat. What did Yehuda ben Shammua and his colleagues do? They went and took advice from** a certain **matron [*matronita*] whom all the prominent men of Rome would visit regularly,** thinking that she would know how to annul the decree.

Rav Tuvi bar Masna raises yet another entry from *Megillat Ta'anit*, this time from the Adar section: *be-esrim u-tumnia beih asas besorta tava le-Yehuda'ei, de-la i-idun m-Oraisa* — on the twenty-eighth of Adar, a good report reached the Jews, that they would not have to remove themselves from the Torah. Six words of explanation for six words of date. The Hebrew gloss then elaborates: the ruling government had decreed *shelo ya'asku ba-Torah* — that they could not engage in Torah study — *shelo yimolu es beneihem* — that they could not perform *brit milah* — and *she-yechalelu Shabbasos* — that they must violate *Shabbat*. This set of three decrees is recognizable as the pattern of Hadrianic persecution following the Bar Kokhba revolt, placing this event in the mid-second century.

### Seeking Influence

The Gemara then asks: *ma asa Yehuda ben Shamua ve-chaverav* — what did Yehuda ben Shamua and his colleagues do? Yehuda ben Shamua was a student of Rabbi Meir, himself a student of Rabbi Akiva, which firmly locates him in the post-*churban* period, in the middle to later second century. *Halchu ve-natlu eitza mi-matronisa achas* — they went and sought advice from a certain *matronisa*, a woman of high social standing in Rome, *she-kol gedolei Romi metzuyin etzlah* — with whom all the great Roman officials were to be found. Her influence over these officials gave her the access that Yehuda ben Shamua and his group needed.

### Night Protest & Appeal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה לָהֶם: בּוֹאוּ וְהַפְגִּינוּ בַּלַּיְלָה. הָלְכוּ וְהִפְגִּינוּ בַּלַּיְלָה, אָמְרוּ: אֵי שָׁמַיִם! לֹא אֲחֵיכֶם אֲנַחְנוּ, וְלֹא בְּנֵי אָב אֶחָד אֲנַחְנוּ, וְלֹא בְּנֵי אֵם אַחַת אֲנַחְנוּ? מָה נִשְׁתַּנֵּינוּ מִכׇּל אוּמָּה וְלָשׁוֹן שֶׁאַתֶּם גּוֹזְרִין עָלֵינוּ גְּזֵירוֹת קָשׁוֹת? וּבִיטְּלוּם. וְאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם עֲשָׂאוּהוּ יוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **She said to them** as follows: **Come and cry out [*hafgginu*] at night** in the streets and markets. **They went and cried out at night, saying: O Heavens! Are we** Jews **not your brothers; are we not children of one father; are we not children of one mother? How are we different from every** other **nation and tongue that you issue** such **harsh decrees against us? And** indeed the decrees **were annulled, and** the Sages **made that day a festive day. And if it enters your mind** to say that ***Megillat Ta'anit* has been nullified,** can you say that **the first** prohibitions against fasting **they annulled, and** then **later** ones **were added?**

Following her advice, they organized a nighttime protest — perhaps because it was safer, perhaps because the sounds of a crowd carry differently at night, or because the psychological weight of numbers feels greater in darkness. Their public declaration took the form of an appeal to shared ancestry: *lo acheichem anachnu* — are we not your brothers? *Velo benei av echad anachnu* — do we not share the same father, Yaakov? *Velo benei em achas anachnu* — do we not share the same mother, Rivka? *Mah nishtaninu mikol umma velashon* — why have we been treated differently than all other nations? The argument invoked the historical kinship between Esau and Jacob, the ancestors of Rome and Israel respectively, as a basis for appealing to Roman tolerance. The Romans generally extended a degree of religious tolerance to peoples who acknowledged their pantheon; those who did not — and Israel was uniquely in this category — strained that tolerance to its breaking point.

### Decree Reversed

Nevertheless, the appeal succeeded. *Ve-oto hayom asu yom tov* — and that day was made into a *yom tov*. Comparing this to the third of Tishrei, one is struck by the enormous disparity in significance: on one hand, a procedural religious achievement regarding God's name in commercial documents; on the other, the reversal of a decree that would have effectively suppressed Torah study, *brit milah*, and *Shabbat* observance. Both were made into *yamim tovim* — the same halakhic designation for very different magnitudes of historical event.

## Post-Churban Usage

### Addition Post-Churban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכָא נָמֵי בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים — וְהָא יְהוּדָה בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ תַּלְמִידוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בָּתַר הָכִי הֲוָה! דִּתְנַן: כְּלֵי זְכוּכִית שֶׁנִּיקְּבוּ וְהִטִּיף לְתוֹכָן אֲבָר, אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: יְהוּדָה בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ מְטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר,
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say that here too** it is referring to **the time when the Temple was standing,** there is a difficulty, **as Yehuda ben Shammua was a student of Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Meir was after** the destruction of the Temple. And proof that Rabbi Yehuda ben Shammua was a student of Rabbi Meir may be brought, **as we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **glass vessels that had holes** in them, which afterward **were filled in with lead,** the Sages dispute whether the utensil is considered a whole utensil, which can become ritually impure, or whether it is considered a broken utensil, which does not. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: Yehuda ben Shammua** declares that it **becomes impure, in the name of Rabbi Meir;**

The Gemara now draws out the implication of the twenty-eighth of Adar entry. If *Megillat Ta'anit* had been annulled at the *churban* — *batla Megillat Ta'anit* — how could new days have been added to it afterward? The Gemara assumes from the verses in Zechariah that if the annulment occurred, it occurred at the moment of the *churban*. Yehuda ben Shamua is unambiguously post-*churban*. Adding a *yom tov* to *Megillat Ta'anit* in his time suggests the framework was still operative — *lo batla Megillat Ta'anit*.

### Pre-Churban Dating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים מְטַהֲרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **whereas the Sages declare it pure.** According to them, it is still considered a broken utensil. Rabbi Meir himself lived after the destruction of the Second Temple. The festive day commemorating the annulling of the decree of Rome was instituted as a result of an incident involving his student, Rabbi Yehuda ben Shammua. From this, it is clear that *Megillat Ta'anit* had not yet been nullified.

The Gemara attempts to deflect this by suggesting that the events underlying both entries — the third of Tishrei and the twenty-eighth of Adar — took place during the *Beit Hamikdash* era. The Gemara then establishes, from the *Mishnah* in tractate *Kelim*, that Yehuda ben Shamua transmitted halakhot in the name of Rabbi Meir — and Rabbi Meir was definitively post-*churban*. This makes the claim that Yehuda ben Shamua's activities occurred during the *Beit Hamikdash* period impossible. The Gemara therefore concedes that the issue is unresolved and declares: *tannai* — this is itself a dispute among *Tannaim*.

### Tannaitic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: הַיָּמִים הָאֵלּוּ הַכְּתוּבִים בִּמְגִילַּת תַּעֲנִית, בֵּין בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים בֵּין בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים — אֲסוּרִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים — אֲסוּרִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשִּׂמְחָה הִיא לָהֶם. אֵין בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים — מוּתָּרִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵבֶל הוּא לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The question whether or not *Megillat Ta'anit* has been nullified is the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **These days, which are written in *Megillat Ta'anit*, both when the Temple is standing and when the Temple is not standing, are** days on which fasting is **prohibited;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yosei says: When the Temple is standing,** these days **are prohibited** for fasting **because** these days **are** a source of **joy for** Israel. But when **the Temple is not standing,** these days **are permitted** for fasting **because these** days **are** a source of **mourning for them.**

A *Beraita* is cited: Rabbi Meir holds that the days listed in *Megillat Ta'anit* — *bein bizman sheBeit Hamikdash kayom uvein bizman she'ein Beit Hamikdash kayom asurin* — whether or not the Temple stands, retain their prohibitive status. This fits perfectly: Yehuda ben Shamua, Rabbi Meir's student, was operating under the assumption that *Megillat Ta'anit* remained active even post-*churban*, consistent with his teacher's view. Rabbi Yose takes the opposite position: *bizman sheBeit Hamikdash kayom asurin mipnei shesimcha hi lahem* — when the Temple stands they are days of joy and are forbidden; *ein Beit Hamikdash kayom mutarin mipnei she'avel hu lahem* — when there is no Temple they become permitted, because they are now days of mourning. Rabbi Yose goes even further than simply annulling the days: he regards them not merely as neutral but as actually transformed into days of loss and grief, symmetrically inverting the transformation they underwent when the Temple stood.

### Halacha: Chanukah & Purim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא בָּטְלוּ, וְהִלְכְתָא לֹא בָּטְלוּ. קַשְׁיָא הִלְכְתָא אַהִלְכְתָא! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בַּחֲנוּכָּה וּפוּרִים, כָּאן בִּשְׁאָר יוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is** that these days **were nullified, and the *halakha* is** that **they were not nullified.** The Gemara asks: This **is difficult,** as one ***halakha*** contradicts **the** other ***halakha*.** The Gemara answers: **It** is **not difficult. Here,** it is referring to **Hanukkah and Purim.** These Festival days were never nullified, and Hanukkah is listed among the Festivals of *Megillat Ta'anit*. **There,** the *halakha* is referring to **the rest of the days** listed in *Megillat Ta'anit*, all of which were nullified.

The Gemara then presents what appears to be a tension between two halakhic rulings — one stating *batlu* and one stating *lo batlu* — and resolves it: *kan baChanukah u'Purim, kan b'sha'ar yomei* — the ruling of *lo batlu* refers to Chanukah and Purim, which retained lasting significance; the ruling of *batlu* refers to all other days in *Megillat Ta'anit*, which lost their status after the *churban*. The reasons for Chanukah's retention have already been explored above — the entrenchment of its *mitzvot*, the weight of *pirsumei nisa*, and in Rashi's reading, the social impossibility of reversing it. Purim, one might add, rests on a biblical book entire unto itself; whatever basis *Megillat Ta'anit* might have provided for it, its retention hardly required that framework. All other minor holidays of the Second Temple period — including the twenty-seventh of Iyar commemorating the cessation of tribute to the Greeks, and all the other Hasmonean-era commemorations — were annulled, and we no longer observe any halakhic restrictions on them.

## Sending Shlichim

### Elul Shlichim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל אֱלוּל מִפְּנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, וְעַל תִּשְׁרִי מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת הַמּוֹעֲדוֹת. כֵּיוָן דְּנָפְקִי לְהוּ אַאֱלוּל, אַתִּשְׁרִי לְמָה לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: Messengers go out to inform about the sanctification of the New Moon **in Elul, due to Rosh HaShana, and in Tishrei, due to the** need to establish the **correct** dates on which to celebrate **the Festivals** of Tishrei. The Gemara asks: **Once** the messengers **have gone out in** the month of **Elul** to inform the people when the New Moon was declared, **why do they** need to go out again **in Tishrei,** as the New Moon of Tishrei always falls on the thirtieth day after the New Moon of Elul?

The Gemara now returns to the *Mishnah*. *Al Elul mipnei Rosh Hashanah* — *shlichim* were dispatched in Elul so that distant communities could determine when Rosh Hashanah would fall, on the assumption that Elul would be only twenty-nine days. Since witnesses could not be dispatched on Rosh Hashanah itself — it would already be too late — they had to go in advance.

### Tishrei Shlichim

*Ve'al Tishrei mipnei takanat hamo'adot* — they would go out again in Tishrei to confirm when Tishrei had been established, reinforcing for those communities the precise date of the *mo'adot*. This second dispatch would serve as a backup, ensuring that even if the first message had not arrived clearly, communities would know with certainty when the month began.

### Always 29 Days?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא דִּלְמָא עַבְּרוּהּ לֶאֱלוּל, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חִינָּנָא בַּר כָּהֲנָא אָמַר רַב: מִימוֹת עֶזְרָא וְאֵילָךְ לֹא מָצִינוּ אֱלוּל מְעוּבָּר!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that messengers must go out for Tishrei as well, as **perhaps the** court **added** another day to the month of **Elul,** so that Rosh HaShana occurs on the thirty-first day after the New Moon of Elul, there is a difficulty. **Didn't Rabbi Ḥinnana bar Kahana say** that **Rav said: From the days of Ezra and onward, we have never found that** the month of **Elul** had **an additional** day. Consequently, it is simple to calculate the days on which the Festivals of Tishrei occur, and there should be no need to send out messengers in Tishrei.

The Gemara questions the need for a second dispatch. If *shlichim* had already gone out in Elul, they would have communicated the assumption that Elul was twenty-nine days and Rosh Hashanah fell accordingly. Why go out again for Tishrei? The Gemara considers whether the answer might be that Elul could theoretically be thirty days — but then cites the statement of Rabbi Chanina bar Kahana in the name of Rav: from the time of Ezra onward, *lo matzinu Elul me'ubar* — we have never found Elul to have been thirty days. This tradition is connected to a close reading of the *pesukim* in Ezra, which Tosafot suggests implies Elul was thirty days at some earlier moment.

### Theoretical Possibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא מָצִינוּ — דְּלָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ, הָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ — מְעַבְּרִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: When we say: **We have not** found that the month of Elul ever had an additional day, this does not mean that Elul cannot have an additional day, but only that it never happened **because it was not necessary** to add a day. **But** if it had been **necessary, they would** have added **an additional** day. Since it is possible that the month of Elul could have had another day added, there is reason to send out messengers for the month of Tishrei, so that all will know when to celebrate the Festivals.

The Gemara's response is crucial: *lo matzinu d'lo itztarich* — the fact that it has never happened is not because it is structurally impossible, but simply because the circumstances never required it. *Ha itztarich, m'abrinan leh* — if circumstances demanded, we would make Elul thirty days. What circumstances might warrant this? Rashi suggests it relates to the desire not to have Yom Kippur fall immediately adjacent to Shabbat. Tosafot disputes this, arguing that such a consideration does not qualify as *tikkun mo'adot* in the full sense the Mishnah intends, and that the relevant scenario is simply if witnesses came late and could not testify on the thirtieth day of Elul. In that case, Elul would be extended to thirty days, and *shlichim* for Tishrei would be essential.

## Tikkun HaMo'adot

### Better Wrong RH

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מִיקַּלְקַל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה! מוּטָב תִּיקַּלְקֵל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וְלֹא יִתְקַלְקְלוּ כּוּלְּהוּ מוֹעֲדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if Elul has an additional day **Rosh HaShana will be ruined,** because people will celebrate it thirty days after the New Moon of Elul, when its real date is on the thirty-first day. The Gemara answers: **Better that Rosh HaShana be ruined, and all the Festivals,** i.e., Yom Kippur, *Sukkot*, and the Eighth Day of Assembly, **not be ruined.**

The Gemara confronts a significant tension: if Elul could conceivably be thirty days, one could argue that the *Beit Din* should simply refuse to accept such testimony — protecting people from celebrating Rosh Hashanah on what they had assumed to be the right day, only to have it shifted. The Gemara rejects this. *Mutav titkalkal Rosh Hashanah v'lo yitkalkelu kulhu moadot* — it is better that Rosh Hashanah be "ruined" — meaning that some communities observed it on the wrong day due to the assumption of a twenty-nine day Elul — than that all the *mo'adot* be compromised.

### Moon Sighting Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: עַל תִּשְׁרִי מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת הַמּוֹעֲדוֹת, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches:** Messengers go out **in** the month of **Tishrei due to the** need to establish the **correct** dates on which to celebrate **the Festivals** of Tishrei. The Gemara summarizes: Indeed, **conclude from here** that this is the correct understanding.

The principle at stake is *tikkun hamo'adot*: the proper fixing of the festivals. The *mo'adot* must be established based on the actual appearance of the moon — testimony from witnesses who saw the new moon — and the *Beit Din* cannot manipulate that process to avoid inconvenience. Even if accepting testimony that Elul was thirty days causes communities who had assumed twenty-nine days to have observed Rosh Hashanah incorrectly, that is a lesser harm than undermining the integrity of the entire *mo'ed* system by refusing valid testimony. The *shlichim* for Tishrei must therefore always go out, because there always remains the theoretical possibility — however rarely realized — that Elul was a full thirty-day month.

### Lunar Basis

The Gemara infers from the language of the Mishnah itself — *tikkun hamo'adot* — that the *mo'adot* are indeed grounded in the actual appearance of the moon, not in a fixed arithmetic cycle. The months are lunar-based and tied to real astronomical events, and the entire system of witnesses and *Beit Din* declaration exists precisely because the calendar is responsive to that appearance, not predetermined independently of it.

## Chanukah & Purim

### Listed in Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל כִּסְלֵיו מִפְּנֵי חֲנוּכָּה, וְעַל אֲדָר מִפְּנֵי הַפּוּרִים. וְאִילּוּ נִתְעַבְּרָה הַשָּׁנָה יוֹצְאִין אַף עַל אֲדָר שֵׁנִי מִפְּנֵי הַפּוּרִים — לָא קָתָנֵי. מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: Messengers go out **in Kislev, due to Hanukkah, and in Adar, due to Purim. Whereas, it is not taught: If the year was a leap year,** with an additional month of Adar, the messengers **go out also in the second Adar due to Purim,** which is celebrated in the second Adar. This indicates that **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: If the year was a leap year,** the messengers **go out also in the second Adar, due to Purim.**

The Mishnah lists Kislev and Adar as months for which *shlichim* are dispatched — Kislev for Chanukah and Adar for Purim. This is entirely consistent with the Gemara's earlier conclusion that Chanukah and Purim are the two rabbinic holidays that survived the annulment of *Megillat Ta'anit* and retain lasting significance requiring communal coordination.

### Adar Sheni Omission

A closer look at the Mishnah reveals a notable omission: when a leap year is declared, the Mishnah does not mention sending *shlichim* for Adar Sheni, even though Purim is celebrated in Adar Sheni. The Gemara notes this gap: *ve-ilu nitabra hashanah yotzim af al Adar Sheni mipnei Purim lo katani* — the Mishnah does not include this as a required dispatch. This silence is significant and requires explanation.

### Majority: Rishon Suffices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר: כׇּל מִצְוֹת הַנּוֹהֲגוֹת בַּשֵּׁנִי — נוֹהֲגוֹת בָּרִאשׁוֹן, וּמָר סָבַר: כׇּל מִצְוֹת הַנּוֹהֲגוֹת בַּשֵּׁנִי — אֵין נוֹהֲגוֹת בָּרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that they disagree about this.** One **Sage,** the author of this mishna, **holds** that **all the mitzvot observed in the second** Adar, i.e., the special Torah readings and the mitzvot of Purim, **are** also **observed in the first** Adar. If they were observed in the first Adar and not in the second, the people have fulfilled their obligation. Therefore, there is no need to send messengers in the second Adar. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that **all the mitzvot observed in the second** Adar **are not observed in the first.** It is therefore necessary to send messengers in the second Adar, so that people will know when to keep the mitzvot of Adar.

The Gemara's explanation: *matnitin d'lo k'Rabbi* — the Mishnah does not follow the view of Rabbi. The majority position holds that all *mitzvot* applicable in Adar Sheni may be performed in Adar Rishon — that is, if one observed Purim in Adar Rishon, one has discharged the obligation. Since the *shlichim* dispatched in Adar Rishon already enabled communities to observe Purim at that point, there is no need for a second set of *shlichim* for Adar Sheni.

### Rabbi: Need Sheni

Rabbi disagrees: *mitzvot ha-noahgot be-sheni einan nohagot be-rishon* — the *mitzvot* specific to Adar Sheni cannot be performed in Adar Rishon. Observing Purim in Adar Rishon does not count. Purim must be observed in Adar Sheni. Therefore, *shlichim* must go out for Adar Sheni as well, so that communities know precisely when Purim falls. The Mishnah's silence on this point aligns it with the majority position and against Rabbi's view.

## Adar Rishon Length

### Fixed vs. Variable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִצְוֹת הַנּוֹהֲגוֹת בַּשֵּׁנִי — אֵין נוֹהֲגוֹת בָּרִאשׁוֹן, וְהָכָא בְּעִיבּוּר שָׁנָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **No, everyone agrees that the mitzvot observed in the second** Adar **are not observed on the first, and here they disagree about** the length of the additional month in the **leap year, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **How long is** the additional month in **a leap year? Thirty days. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: A month.**

The Gemara proceeds to analyze the underlying debate more carefully, asking whether the omission of *shlichim* for Adar Sheni in the Mishnah necessarily tracks the dispute between Rabbi and the majority about whether Adar Rishon discharges the Purim obligation — or whether it might reflect a separate question entirely.

### Need Depends on Length

The Gemara raises an alternative framing: even if everyone agrees that Purim must be observed in Adar Sheni, the question of whether *shlichim* are needed for Adar Sheni depends on whether communities can independently calculate when Adar Sheni begins. If Adar Rishon is always a fixed number of days, communities know when it ends and therefore when Adar Sheni begins — making *shlichim* unnecessary. But if the length of Adar Rishon is variable, communities cannot make that calculation on their own, and *shlichim* become necessary to communicate the start of Adar Sheni.

### All Combinations Possible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: כַּמָּה עִיבּוּר שָׁנָה — שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: חֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **No, everyone agrees that the mitzvot observed in the second** Adar **are not observed on the first, and here they disagree about** the length of the additional month in the **leap year, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **How long is** the additional month in **a leap year? Thirty days. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: A month.**

A remarkable tradition is cited: Rabbi Simai testified in the name of Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi — the last three prophets, present at the dawn of the Second Temple era — that when there are two Adars, *sheratzu la'asot sheneihem mele'im osim* — one may make both full (thirty days); *sheneihem chaseirim osim* — or both lacking (twenty-nine days); *echad malei ve'echad chaser osim* — or one full and one lacking. All combinations are permissible. The practice in the exile (*baGola*) before the return had followed this flexible approach, accommodating a range of arrangements.

### Rav: Always 30-29

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִשּׁוּם רַבֵּינוּ אָמְרוּ: לְעוֹלָם אֶחָד מָלֵא וְאֶחָד חָסֵר, עַד שֶׁיִּוּוֹדַע לָךְ שֶׁהוּקְבַּע רֹאשׁ חֹדֶשׁ בִּזְמַנּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And in the name of our teacher,** Rav, **they said:** The two months of Adar are **always** observed, **one full and one short, unless it is known to you that** the **New Moon was fixed in its** proper **time,** i.e., the first Adar is also short.

Against this, a ruling attributed to Rav (*Rabbeinu*) establishes a default: *le'olam echad malei ve'echad chaser* — always one full and one lacking, with Adar Rishon being the full thirty-day month and Adar Sheni being the twenty-nine-day month — *ad sheyivadei lach shekave'u Rosh Chodesh bizmano* — unless one has explicit testimony that Adar Sheni was sanctified in its proper time, meaning that the preceding month was twenty-nine days and the new moon appeared on day thirty. Without such testimony, the default assumption is thirty-day Adar Rishon and twenty-nine-day Adar Sheni.

## Adar Before Nissan

### 29 Day Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ לְמָר עוּקְבָא: אֲדָר הַסָּמוּךְ לְנִיסָן — לְעוֹלָם חָסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** A ruling **was sent** from Eretz Yisrael **to Mar Ukva,** the Exilarch in Babylonia: **The Adar that** immediately **precedes Nisan is always short,** both in a regular year and in a leap year. But the first Adar in a leap year, which does not immediately precede Nisan, is sometimes full.

A ruling was sent to Mar Ukva: *Adar hasamuch leNissan* — the Adar immediately preceding Nissan, whether in a regular year or as Adar Sheni in a leap year, is always twenty-nine days. This is consistent with Rav's position that Adar Sheni is the *chaser* month — and it provides a practical anchor: whatever uncertainty may exist about Adar Rishon, the month that connects directly to Nissan is always twenty-nine days.

## Shabbat Violation

### Two Months Only

The Mishnah teaches that witnesses were permitted — indeed required — to violate *Shabbat* in order to testify about the new moon of two months: Nissan and Tishrei. These are the months whose proper establishment has cascading consequences for the *yamim tovim* — Pesach on the one hand, Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, and Sukkot on the other.

### Nachman's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב נַחְמָן: עַל שְׁנֵי חֳדָשִׁים מְחַלְּלִין אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת — עַל נִיסָן וְעַל תִּשְׁרִי. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא זִמְנִין מָלֵא זִמְנִין חָסֵר — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מְחַלְּלִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman raised an objection** from what was taught in a mishna: Witnesses who saw the new moon **may desecrate Shabbat for** the fixing of the New Moon of **two months, for** the month of **Nisan and for** the month of **Tishrei,** due to the important Festivals that occur in them. **Granted, if you say** that the Adar immediately preceding Nisan is **sometimes full and sometimes short, due to that** reason the witnesses **may desecrate** Shabbat, as if the witnesses come on the thirtieth, the month will be made short and that day will be declared the New Moon; otherwise, the month will be made full and the next day will be declared the New Moon.

Rav Nachman challenges the rule regarding Nissan in light of the ruling just transmitted to Mar Ukva. If the Adar preceding Nissan is always twenty-nine days, then everyone already knows when Nissan will begin — on day thirty of Adar, without any need for witness testimony. Why then should witnesses violate *Shabbat* to testify? Their testimony changes nothing about when Nissan falls. The entire apparatus of *chilul Shabbat* for lunar testimony is premised on the calendar being dependent on that testimony — but if the answer is already known, what purpose does the *Shabbat* violation serve?

### Mitzvah of Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְעוֹלָם חָסֵר, אַמַּאי מְחַלְּלִינַן? מִשּׁוּם דְּמִצְוָה לְקַדֵּשׁ עַל הָרְאִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say** that the Adar immediately preceding Nisan is **always short, why should they desecrate** Shabbat? The court can calculate the time of the New Moon without witnesses. The Gemara answers: **Because it is a mitzva to sanctify** the New Moon **on the basis of** the testimony of witnesses who actually **saw** the new moon and not rely on calculations or established practices.

The Gemara responds with a fundamental principle: *mishum de-mitzvah lekadesh al ha-re'iyah* — there is an independent *mitzvah* to sanctify the month based on actual testimony about the moon's appearance. This *mitzvah* exists regardless of whether the calendar's outcome depends on the testimony. The obligation to witness and formally declare the new moon is not merely instrumental — it is not only a means to an end of establishing the correct date — but is itself a religious obligation. And since that *mitzvah* exists, the *Shabbat* violation is warranted to fulfill it, even when the calendar result could theoretically have been determined without the witnesses.

### Alternative: Adar Varies

The Gemara offers an alternative reading of the entire exchange. According to some (*ika de-amrei*), Rav Nachman's statement is inverted: if Adar is *always* twenty-nine days, then one understands *why* witnesses violate *Shabbat* for Nissan — because of the *mitzvah* of sanctification through testimony. But if Adar's length varies, then the witnesses' testimony is materially necessary to determine when Nissan begins, and in that case a different solution would be available: simply assume Adar is thirty days and sanctify Nissan on day thirty-one, without requiring *Shabbat* violation. The Gemara leaves this tension — between these two ways of reading the *Shabbat* violation for Nissan — for further analysis, picking up with the question on the following daf of whether the evidence cuts in favor of a fixed or variable Adar.
