# Bava Batra 162 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Blank Lines

### Blank Lines Rule

The *daf* opens on 162b with the established principle that a *shtar* is invalidated when two blank lines appear between the body of the document and the signatures of the witnesses. The concern is straightforward: people naturally leave some space when they sign, writing, say, *Yaakov ben Yitzchak*, *eid*, a bit below the last line of text. That gap creates an opportunity for a forger to insert an additional line of content. The first principle invoked is *ayn lamedin mishitah achronah* — we do not derive the terms of the document from the last line — meaning that even if a forged line were inserted just above the witnesses, it could be argued that we would simply disregard that last line. But if there are two blank lines, a forger could insert two lines of text, and we would then learn from the *second*-to-last line, which would not be covered by that principle. Hence the document becomes *pasul*.

### Ayn Lamedin

The principle of *ayn lamedin mishitah achronah* is thus the background assumption against which the entire discussion of blank lines must be understood. It protects against a single line of forgery, but it cannot protect against two, since we would then be forced to reckon with what appears above the last line.

### Line and Half

The *Gemara* raises the intermediate case: what about a space of a line and a half? The question is whether that much space is sufficient to enable a damaging forgery. If it is, the *shtar* should be *pasul*; if not, it should remain *kasher*.

### Tosafot Interpretation

*Tosafot* entertains a possible reading in which "a line and a half" refers to the height — that is, 1.5 times the normal line height. *Tosafot* initially considers whether this might mean that the *eidim* signed at a certain point, leaving a space of roughly one and a half lines in which something could be inserted. *Tosafot* then rejects this, noting that in such a case we would simply presume that neither the line above the *eidim* nor the line alongside them would be learned from. The more straightforward reading of "a line and a half" is that it refers to the vertical height of a line-and-a-half's worth of space — the question being whether a forger can squeeze two small lines into that height, or whether any attempt to do so would be obvious.

### Braita Evidence

The *Gemara* brings a *braitah* to resolve the matter: *hirchik et haeidim shtei shitim min haksav pasul* — if the witnesses are placed two lines away from the text, the *shtar* is *pasul*. Critically, the *braitah* does not introduce an intermediate figure; it simply states *pachot mikan*, anything less than two lines. This is far cleaner than the earlier formulation, which introduced the ambiguous case of a line and a half.

### Conclusion

The conclusion is therefore that anything less than two lines of distance is *kasher*, because any attempt to squeeze two lines into a smaller space would be detectable — it would be obvious that lines were inserted after the fact. Two full lines, by contrast, provide enough room for a natural-looking insertion, and the document is therefore *pasul*.

## Multiple Witnesses

### Multiple Witnesses Case

The *Gemara* next presents a distinct but related *halachah*: if four or five witnesses signed a *shtar* and one of them is found to be either a *karov* (a relative of one of the parties) or otherwise *pasul*, the testimony of the remaining valid witnesses stands — *titkayem eidut bashear*. This ruling is introduced here in close connection with the preceding discussion of blank lines, and *Tosafot* argues that it reads most naturally as a continuation of that discussion rather than a fully independent statement.

### Chizkiyah Ruling

The connection to the blank lines discussion becomes clear through the statement attributed to *Chizkiyah*: *miluo bikrovim kasher* — if one filled the white space in a *shtar* with *krovim*, the document is *kasher*. The principle is that when the document has been placed too far from the signatures, the problematic white space can be filled in with additional signatories, even if those signatories are *pasul*, and the *shtar* remains valid.

### Sukkah Analogy

The *Gemara* supports this with an analogy to *sukkah*: *avir sukkah pasul bisheloshah, schach pasul posel b'arba'ah* — a gap of air in the *sechach* of a *sukkah* invalidates it at three *tefachim*, whereas invalid *sechach* only creates a problem at four *tefachim*. The initial reaction to *Chizkiyah*'s ruling is naturally one of surprise: how can filling a *pasul* space with something itself *pasul* make the *shtar* more *kasher*? The *sukkah* analogy is invoked to show that such a result is not without precedent.

### Sukkah Details

To appreciate the analogy, consider a *sukkah* with only three walls, viewed from above. If there is a gap of three *tefachim* of air in the *sechach*, the *sechach* is no longer considered continuous. The *sukkah* effectively splits into two halves, each of which has only two walls — rendering both halves *pasul*. However, if instead of air there is *pasul sechach* — say, metal bars that cannot serve as valid *sechach* — the covering is still physically present and continuous. A gap of invalid material does not rupture the continuity the way air does. Therefore, up to four *tefachim* of *pasul sechach*, the *sukkah* remains *kasher*, because it is still treated as a single continuous entity. Only at four *tefachim* does the invalid material constitute its own independent section, effectively dividing the *sukkah* into three parts and rendering the whole *pasul*. Three *tefachim* is *kasher*; four *tefachim* is *pasul*.

### Logic Comparison

It must be said clearly: the logic in the two cases is entirely different, and the *Gemara* itself signals this with the words *v'al titamah* — do not be astounded. There is no genuine derivation from *sukkah* to *shtar*; it is purely an analogy meant to prevent surprise. In the *sukkah*, invalid material preserves continuity in a way that air does not — the physical presence of something, even something *pasul*, prevents a rupture. In the *shtar*, the operative concern is forgery. White space is dangerous because a forger can fill it with fabricated lines. Invalid signatories — *pasul eidim* — fill that white space physically and thereby remove the opportunity for forgery, even though what fills it is itself *pasul*. The logics are distinct: one is about structural continuity, the other is about eliminating the possibility of fraudulent insertion. *Tosafot* adds a further dimension by framing the *sukkah* comparison as a lesson against enacting a *gezeirah*: just as we do not prohibit three *tefachim* of invalid *sechach* out of concern that it will lead to permitting three *tefachim* of air, so too we do not prohibit *pasul eidim* filling the white space out of concern that it will lead to permitting blank space itself. But this *Tosafot* reading may not represent the core of *Chizkiyah*'s point.

### Ravad Tefillin

A fascinating *teshuvah* of the *Ravad* applies this same logic to *tefillin*. If the scrolls inside the *tefillin* contain a word that appears twice — for instance, a line ending with *l'vanecha* and the following line beginning again with *l'vanecha v'dibarta bam* — the *Ravad* rules that they are not *pasul*, and invokes precisely the same principle: *v'al titma*, do not be astounded. If blank space would have been *kasher*, then filling that space with a repeated word is also *kasher*. The logic is a striking extension of *Chizkiyah*'s ruling into the realm of *sifrei Torah* and *tefillin*.

### Tosafot Witnesses

Returning to the *shtar*, *Tosafot* raises the obvious difficulty: normally the rule is *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* — if one witness in a group is found to be a *karov* or *pasul*, all of the testimony is invalidated. Why does that rule not apply here to invalidate the remaining valid witnesses? *Tosafot* explains by focusing on the formal act that constitutes testimony. When witnesses appear before a *beit din* to testify, they undergo a process of formal acceptance — they declare their intent to testify, and the court receives them. At that moment they constitute a defined group, and if one member of that group is *pasul*, the entire group's testimony falls. The operative factor is that they all declared themselves witnesses together in that formal context.

### Invalid Permitted

With a *shtar*, however, there is no *beit din* formally accepting anyone's testimony at the moment of signing. There is only a person, a pen, and a document. In that context, it is possible to argue — and indeed the *halachah* does argue — that the *pasul* signer was not acting *lesheim eidus* at all.

### Intent Context

The question of intent is central here. The *Gemara* itself records, and the *Rema* confirms that this was actual practice, that additional people would sign a *shtar* or *ketubah* not as formal witnesses but as a form of *kavod* — honor. At a wedding, for example, people sign the *ketubah* to participate in and honor the *choson v'kallah*. If that is the context in which someone signs, they are not constituting themselves as *eidim* in the formal sense, and therefore the disqualifying logic of *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* never applies to them in the first place.

The argument becomes more subtle when one considers that signing a *shtar* is, on its face, an act of testimony — indeed, the *halachah* treats signatures on a *shtar* as equivalent to testimony received by a *beit din* (*k'mi shenichkra edutam b'veit din*). But there is a certain circularity here: a signature attains the status of formal testimony precisely because it is recognized as valid participation in the *shtar*. If the signer is *pasul*, the signature can never be so recognized, and therefore it never acquires the status of testimony in the first place. It is automatically excluded before the logic of group invalidation can even engage. One might test this in a hypothetical: what if the *pasul* witness signed fully intending to testify, unaware of his disqualifying status? Even then, it may be that the structure of *shtar* law is such that a *pasul* signature is by definition excluded from the document, and no subjective intent can change that.

### Witness Position

*Tosafot* raises a further question about the physical placement of the *pasul eidim* on the document. If we are to set aside the invalid signatures, which ones do we set aside? Consider a document in which the *kasher* witnesses signed above and the *pasul* witnesses signed below — or vice versa. Intuitively, one might think that the signatures closest to the body of the document are the more significant ones, as they directly address the question of whether the white space between the text and the signatures is impermissibly large. Those below, farther from the text, might seem more like afterthoughts. On the other hand, one could argue the opposite: the signatures below are closer to the *beit din*'s *kiyum hashtar*, which is appended at the bottom, and are thus more connected to the validation apparatus.

From the *sukkah* analogy, it would seem that the signatures filling the white space closer to the document are the ones doing the critical work of preventing forgery. Even if those are *pasul*, they are *kasher* by *Chizkiyah*'s ruling, since without them the *shtar* would have been *pasul* due to the white space. And if even those are acceptable, then certainly the *pasul* ones at the bottom, which are not filling the critical zone, would be acceptable.

### Karov Pasul

*Tosafot* then notes a further discrepancy in the sources: the *braitah* uses the phrase *nimtza karov o pasul* — a relative or an otherwise invalid witness — while *Chizkiyah*'s formulation mentions only *krovim*. Is there a meaningful distinction? A *karov* is someone who is disqualified only in relation to a specific case — in a different *shtar*, he would be a fully valid witness. A *pasul* in the broader sense — a *gazlan*, for instance — is disqualified in every context. *Tosafot* is uncertain whether *Chizkiyah* intends *krovim* precisely, or whether his ruling extends equally to any *pasul* signer. The majority position in *Tosafot* seems to be that the ruling extends to *pasul* witnesses as well, but the question is left without a definitive resolution.

## Women Ketubah

### Rama Practice

The *Rema* in *Hilchos Kesubot* records that the actual practice was for multiple relatives and others to sign the *ketubah* after the valid *eidim* had signed, specifically as a form of *kibbud* for the *choson v'kallah*. This practice was grounded in the principle that *miluhu bikrovim kasher* — filling the space with *krovim* is valid — and in the understanding that such additional signatories were signing for *kavod*, not *lesheim eidus*.

### Women Signatories

In our contemporary context, this question has acquired a new dimension. The question arises whether women may serve as additional signatories on a *ketubah* after the two valid male witnesses have signed. This is often asked by people who are unaware of the *Rema*'s ruling or the underlying *sugyah*, but who are, in effect, asking whether the traditional practice of adding *kavod* signatories can be extended to women.

### Woman Status

The question of a woman's status in this context is genuinely complex. One might argue that since a woman cannot serve as a valid *eid*, she is *pasul* in every context — even more so than a *gazlan*, who at least belongs to the category of potential witnesses and could theoretically become *kasher* through *teshuvah*. On this view, a woman is more disqualified than a *pasul*, and one might hesitate to include her even under *Chizkiyah*'s ruling.

However, the majority of *poskim* reason differently. A *pasul eid* is someone who is within the category of witnesses but currently disqualified. A woman, in traditional *halachic* analysis, is not a *pasul eid* — she is simply outside the category of *edim* entirely. She is not a disqualified witness; she is a non-witness. This distinction may actually make her signature *less* problematic for the *shtar*, not more: since she is not an *eid* at all, she cannot be *nimtza pasul*, and the rule of *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* does not engage with her signature in any way.

### Honor Line

In light of this analysis, a practical recommendation emerges for those who wish to have women sign the *ketubah* in addition to the valid witnesses. After the two *kasher eidim* have signed with the word *eid*, a separate line should be added in which the additional signatories — including women — sign beneath a phrase such as *v'gam anu chatamnu mata likvod hachoson v'hakallah*, "and we too have signed below in honor of the *choson v'kallah*." Critically, the word *eid* should not appear after their names. This achieves two things simultaneously: it replicates the historical *kavod* practice attested by the *Rema*, and it makes explicit that these signatures are not intended as *eidus* — thereby resolving *Tosafot*'s concern about whether the context of signing can be assumed to be *lesheim kavod* rather than *lesheim eidus*. In a society where this practice is not broadly familiar, it is harder to presume that context without explicit statement; making it explicit removes the ambiguity entirely.

## Two Lines Measurement

### With/Without Space

After the preceding discussions, the *Gemara* returns to the technical measurement of what constitutes "two lines" of space, and poses a careful question: *shnei shitin she'amru hen v'aviram o dilma hen v'lo aviram* — when the *halachah* says that two lines of distance renders the *shtar pasul*, does that measure include the normal intervening space between lines, or does it refer only to the height of the text itself, without that space?

To understand the stakes of this question, consider how a line of text actually occupies space on a page. If the text is written in a twelve-point font, say, each line has a certain height, but there is also a standard inter-line gap above and below it. When we say two lines of blank space invalidate the document, does that mean two text-heights plus the normal gaps — i.e., the full visual impression of two lines of writing — or only two text-heights with no additional space?

The practical difference is significant. If two full "natural" lines including their spacing constitute the danger zone, then anything that appears less than two naturally spaced lines is *kasher*. If the measure excludes the spacing, then the *pasul* threshold is reached more quickly. The concern in either case is that a forger will exploit the space to insert two new lines that appear to have always been there.

### Nachman Ruling

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* resolves the question: *nistavera d'hen v'aviram* — it stands to reason that the measure is the lines with their intervening space. His reasoning is elegant: if we were to say the measure excludes the spacing, then a single line of text without any following space would itself be a meaningful measure — but a line without any spacing after it is not a natural or functional unit of writing. The measure must therefore reflect actual writing practice, meaning the full height of a line as it would normally appear, including its spacing.

### Scribe Script

A further refinement is introduced by *Rav Chisda* in the name of *Chizkiyah*: *shnei shitin she'amru biksav yad eidim v'lo biksav yad sofer* — the two-line measure that renders a *shtar pasul* applies specifically to the handwriting size of non-professional witnesses, not to the smaller, more controlled handwriting of a professional *sofer*. The distinction is rooted in the different scales of writing. A trained *sofer* writes with precision and uniformity, producing smaller, tighter letters. An ordinary person writing in script produces larger, less uniform lettering.

### Forgery Risk

The entire concern about blank lines is that a forger will insert additional text after the witnesses have signed. For such an insertion to be dangerous, it must be capable of appearing natural — indistinguishable from the original. *Rav Chisda* reasons that the only person who could plausibly forge in a *sofer*'s handwriting is another *sofer*, and a *sofer* would not agree to participate in a forgery. Therefore, two lines of *sofer* script in a blank space do not create the same risk as two lines of ordinary script. The blank space is only *pasul* when it could plausibly accommodate the insertion of ordinary-sized lines.

One might object that even if the blank space contains ordinary script different from the *sofer*'s hand, that difference itself would signal a forgery. The response is that this is not necessarily so: it is perfectly plausible that an amendment or additional clause was written in by hand before the witnesses signed, without the *sofer* having written it. The discrepancy in handwriting styles does not, by itself, prove forgery.

### Lach Lach

*Rav Yitzchak ben Elazar* further refines the measurement with a concrete example: *k'gon lach lach zeh al gabi zeh* — imagine the word *lach* written one on top of the other. The word *lach* is written with a *lamed* and a *chof*. The *lamed* has an ascending stroke that rises above the normal line height, demanding an extra airspace above the line. The *chof* has a descending stroke that falls below the normal baseline, demanding an extra airspace below. When two such words are stacked, the visual footprint is therefore: the body of the first line, an airspace below for its *chof*, an airspace above for the *lamed* of the second line, and the body of the second line. This gives a total of two lines plus four airspaces — *shnei shitim v'arba'ah avirim* — as the full measure of what a forger would need to insert two lines of text that appear completely natural.

### Two Lines Four Air

To visualize this: the blank space above the witnesses, measured from the last line of the document's text, must span the full height of two naturally written lines — each with their ascenders and descenders accounted for. That amounts to two line-bodies and four airspaces. Anything less than this total height is considered *kasher*, because a forger could not squeeze in two full natural lines without it being apparent that they were added after the fact.

### Lamed Chof

*Rabbi Chiya bar Ammi* in the name of *Ulla* offers a slightly different account: one need only allow for the *lamed* above and the *chof* below, with a single normal space between the two lines — *shnei shitin v'shalosh avirim*, two lines and three airspaces. The point is that a skilled writer could avoid having both the descender of the upper line and the ascender of the lower line occupy space simultaneously; one can simply position the letters so they do not interfere with each other. The minimum natural arrangement requires only three airspaces rather than four.

### Single Line Two Air

*Abba* goes further still: *k'gon Baruch ben Levi b'shita achas* — take the name *Baruch ben Levi* written in a single line. *Baruch* contains a *chof* with a descender, and *Levi* contains a *lamed* with an ascender. Even within a single line, these letters demand airspace above and below. *Abba* therefore says: *shita achas u'shnei avirim* — only one line and two airspaces. This is a striking position, apparently arguing that even the threshold of two lines set earlier is not absolute — perhaps only a single line with the appropriate airspaces constitutes the danger zone. *Tosafot* notes that this seems to be a direct disagreement with the prior ruling.

### Before Hashrat

*Rav* adds a final qualification: *lo shanu ella bein eidim l'chsav* — the distance rule applies only to the space between the witnesses' signatures and the body of the *shtar*. However, *bein eidim l'ashrasa afilu tuvnami kasher* — between the witnesses and the *hashrat*, the court's validation of the document, even a large space is *kasher*. This distinction opens the discussion that occupies the final section of the *daf*.

## Witnesses Hashrat

### Space Permitted

The ruling of *Rav* — that a large space between the witnesses and the *kiyum hashtar* (the court's validation) is *kasher* — invites immediate scrutiny. The *kiyum hashtar* is appended by the *beit din* at the bottom of the *shtar* to certify that the witnesses' signatures are genuine. Why should white space in that location not create the same forgery concern as white space between the witnesses and the body of the document?

### Forgery Between

The *Gemara* itself voices this objection: *mai shena bein eidim l'chsav? dilma mezayef v'chasav mai d'ba'ei* — what is different about the space between the witnesses and the text? The concern is that a forger will write something in that space, and the witnesses' signatures below will appear to validate it. But, the *Gemara* continues, *bein eidim l'ashrasa nami* — should the same not be true between the witnesses and the *kiyum*? If there is a large blank space before the *beit din*'s certification, could a forger not insert text there and have the court's *kiyum* appear to validate it?

### Court Marks

The *Gemara* answers: *d'mitayeit lei* — what the *beit din* does is mark up or fill in the blank space between the witnesses' signatures and the *kiyum*. The court draws lines, marks, or other indications across that space, rendering it unusable. Any attempt to scrape those marks away and insert forged text would be visually apparent. With this practice in place, the blank space between the witnesses and the *kiyum* presents no forgery danger, and the *shtar* remains *kasher* even with a large gap in that location.

### Why Not Above

The *Gemara* then raises the obvious question: if such marking can solve the problem below, why not simply apply the same solution above? Mark the space between the witnesses and the body of the *shtar*, and the forgery concern is resolved there as well. If that worked, there would be no need for the strict two-line rule at all.

### Cannot Mark Above

The *Gemara* states that this solution cannot be applied above, between the witnesses' signatures and the text of the *shtar*.

### Signing Ambiguity

The reason is that if there are marks or designs in the space between the witnesses and the text, people will say — *amri, sa'adei atiyusa hu d'chasimi* — that the witnesses are signing on the design or marks, not on the *shtar* above. Once there is something other than blank space between the signatures and the document, the signatures no longer unambiguously attest to the document. They could be interpreted as attesting to whatever appears directly above them, which in this case would be the markings rather than the *shtar*'s content. This creates a fundamental ambiguity about what exactly the witnesses are validating.

### Court Clarity

Why does this problem not apply to the *kiyum hashtar* below? The *Gemara* answers: *bedina tiyuta lo chasimi* — no one would think that a *beit din* is signing its certification on a design or set of marks. It is universally understood that the *kiyum* of a *beit din* refers exclusively to the witnesses' signatures. Its referent is unambiguous: it certifies the identity and validity of the witnesses above it, nothing else. Therefore, even with marks between the witnesses and the *kiyum*, there is no confusion about what the court is validating.

The logic is admittedly not entirely symmetric, and a number of questions remain difficult to resolve. Why do we not simply mark the space between witnesses and text and then stipulate that the witnesses signed with reference to the document above? Why do we not say that the *kiyum* in any case only refers to the witnesses, and therefore even if forged content were inserted above the witnesses but below the body of the text, the *kiyum* would not be construed as validating it? The *Gemara* does not resolve these questions fully, and they remain points of difficulty in the *sugya*.

### No Blank Space

The conclusion is clear, however: no blank space is permitted between the witnesses and the body of the *shtar*. The space must contain either actual text — the continuation of the document's content — or, as *Chizkiyah* permitted, additional signatories even if they are *pasul*, since their physical presence removes the opportunity for forged insertion. What it may not contain is empty space, nor may it be solved by marks or designs, since those would undermine the unambiguous connection between the witnesses' signatures and the document above. The *kiyum* below may be separated from the witnesses by a marked space, since *beit din* practice ensures the area is filled and the court's certification unambiguously references the witnesses alone.
