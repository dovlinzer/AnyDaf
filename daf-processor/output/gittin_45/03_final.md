# Gittin 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sale to Chutz La'aretz

### Baraita: 2nd Master

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר מֵרַבּוֹ שֵׁנִי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: פְּעָמִים יָצָא, וּפְעָמִים לֹא יָצָא. כֵּיצַד? אָמַר: ״פְּלוֹנִי עַבְדִּי מְכַרְתִּיהוּ לִפְלוֹנִי אַנְטוֹכִי״ – לֹא יָצָא. ״לְאַנְטוֹכִי שֶׁבְּאַנְטוֹכְיָא״ – יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught: With regard to **one who sells his slave to a Jew outside of Eretz** Yisrael, the slave **is emancipated, but** he nevertheless **requires a bill of manumission from his second master. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Sometimes he is emancipated and sometimes he is not emancipated. How so?** If the master **said: So-and-so my slave, I sold him to so-and-so of Antioch,** then **he is not emancipated,** because it is possible that he describes the purchaser this way because he was born in Antioch, and now he lives in Eretz Yisrael. However, if he said: I sold him **to** so-and-so **of Antioch who is in Antioch,** then **he is emancipated,** as his statement clarifies that he is selling his slave to one who lives outside of Eretz Yisrael.

The *daf* opens with a *baraita*: *hamocher avdo l'chutz la'aretz yatza l'cherut v'tzarich get shichrur merabbo sheni* — one who sells his slave to *chutz la'aretz* to another Jew, that slave goes free and requires a *get shichrur* from the second master. The *chiddush* of the *baraita* is that the obligation to issue the writ of freedom falls on the second master, not the first. The second master is, after all, the Jew currently residing in *chutz la'aretz*, and the *baraita* does not suggest he receives his money back. The entire weight of the sanction, it appears, is placed on the buyer.

It is worth pausing to acknowledge how striking this concern is from the perspective of the Babylonian *Talmud*. For those of us in *chutz la'aretz*, and indeed from the vantage point of the *Bavli* itself, the tragedy of being outside the Land of Israel — deprived only of *mitzvot hatluyot ba'aretz*, of *terumot u'ma'asrot* — can produce a certain cognitive dissonance. Yet the *mishnah* was composed in *Eretz Yisrael*, and the *takkanot* of *Chazal* were designed to maintain a rooted Jewish presence in the Land, not merely a physical one but one expressed through land, cattle, and slaves. That context makes the severity of the concern more intelligible.

### Tosafot's Question

*Tosafot* raises a sharp question: when exactly does the slave go free? The slave cannot be forced to go to *chutz la'aretz* — if he refuses, the new master cannot compel him. But if he agrees to go willingly, he has exercised his own choice, and one might argue he has forfeited his right to freedom on those grounds. So what is the operative scenario in which we free him?

*Tosafot* resolves this by explaining that *beit din* forces the master to free the slave before he even departs, precisely to prevent the master from persuading him to go. The concern is that the master may pressure or convince the slave to agree, and we do not want such consent to vitiate the slave's right. The *get shichrur* is therefore issued preemptively, at the point where the slave still says he does not wish to leave.

### Rabban Shimon Defaults

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנְיָא: ״מְכַרְתִּיהוּ לְאַנְטוֹכִי״ – יָצָא, ״לְאַנְטוֹכִי הַשָּׁרוּי בְּלוֹד״ – לֹא יָצָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But isn't it taught** in that same *baraita*: If he said: **I sold him to** so-and-so **of Antioch,** then the slave **is emancipated,** but if he said: I sold him **to** so-and-so **of Antioch who dwells in Lod,** a city in Eretz Yisrael, then the slave **is not emancipated.** This indicates that if he states that he sold the slave to so-and-so of Antioch, without further comment, the slave is emancipated; this is not in accordance with the statement of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ בֵּיתָא בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, הָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ אוּשְׁפִּיזָא בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult. This** case, when he is not emancipated, is referring to when the purchaser from Antioch **has a house in Eretz Yisrael,** and it may be that he purchased the slave to serve in his house in Eretz Yisrael. **That** case, in which the slave is emancipated, is referring to **when he has** only **an inn [*ushpiza*]** where he is staying **in Eretz Yisrael,** and the only home belonging to the purchaser is outside of Eretz Yisrael.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* introduces a more nuanced position: *pe'amim yatza u-pe'amim lo yatza* — sometimes the slave goes free and sometimes he does not. The determinative factor is where the buyer actually lives, not merely where he originates. If you say *machartiv l'Ploni Antochi* — I sold him to a certain Antiochian — that is ambiguous, and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says the slave does not automatically go free; perhaps this Antiochian is living in Israel. But if it is specified that this is an Antiochian *she-b'Antochia* — who resides in Antioch — the slave goes free.

A counter-source complicates the picture: a separate *baraita* states that if you merely say *machartiv l'Antochi* without qualification, the slave does go free, on the presumption that he is returning to Antioch. And if you know the buyer has an apartment in *Lod*, he does not go free. The *gemara* reconciles: *lo kashya — ha d'is lei bayit b'Eretz Yisrael, ha d'is lei ushpiza b'Eretz Yisrael* — there is no difficulty; the cases differ based on whether the Antiochian has a *house* in Israel versus merely a lodging, a temporary rental.

### Buyer's Intent/Location

What if you know nothing about the buyer's plans — you met him in the marketplace without inquiring? The *gemara* does not directly address the case of total uncertainty, implying one would presumably investigate. The underlying principle, however, is clear: the question is entirely whether the slave is actually being transferred to *chutz la'aretz*, and that depends on where the buyer is domiciled. Interestingly, from the *gemara*'s formulation, it appears that if the buyer splits his time between *Eretz Yisrael* and *chutz la'aretz*, the slave would not go free — though the case is left somewhat open.

## Husband-Wife Slave Case

### Setup & Dowry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: בֶּן בָּבֶל שֶׁנָּשָׂא אִשָּׁה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְהִכְנִיסָה לוֹ עֲבָדִים וּשְׁפָחוֹת, וְדַעְתּוֹ לַחֲזוֹר, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** If there was **a resident of Babylonia who married a woman in Eretz Yisrael, and she brought in** to the marriage **slaves and maidservants for him, and he intends to return** to Babylonia, then **what is** the *halakha*? Is marrying him akin to selling the slaves to her husband? Since he plans to take them out of Eretz Yisrael, will they be emancipated?

The *gemara* now introduces a question posed by *Rabbi Yirmiyah* that intersects with the laws of marriage and divorce: *ben Bavel she-nasa ishah* — a man from *Bavel* who marries a woman in *Eretz Yisrael*, and she brings slaves into the marriage as part of her dowry. Crucially, this is not a case of *nichsei milog*, where the wife retains title and the husband enjoys only the usufruct. This is *nichsei tzon barzel*, where the husband assumes full title to the property, with the obligation to return its assessed value to the wife upon divorce. The husband intends to return to *Bavel*. Does his acquisition of these slaves constitute something akin to a sale to *chutz la'aretz*, such that the slaves should go free?

### Tzon Barzel Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַדִּין עִמָּהּ, תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַדִּין עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Let the dilemma be raised according to the one who said: The law is with her,** that in the event of a divorce the slaves remain in her possession, and her husband cannot pay her for them in order to maintain possession of them; and **let the dilemma be raised according to the one who said: The law is with him,** and he may pay her and retain possession of the slaves.

To assess this, one must first understand the nature of *nichsei tzon barzel* more precisely. At the time of marriage, the wife's property is appraised and its value is written into the *kesubah*. The husband assumes ownership. If the slaves die, he owes her their assessed value at divorce. If they are alive and she wants them back at the time of divorce, the question — a general *nichsei tzon barzel* question — is whether she can demand the slaves themselves or only their monetary value.

The resolution of that underlying question bears on the present case: the degree to which we treat the transfer of the slaves to the husband as a full sale, versus a contingent or partial transfer, will determine whether his taking them to *Bavel* constitutes the kind of transfer that triggers the slave's right to freedom.

### Ownership Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַדִּין עִמָּהּ – כֵּיוָן דְּהַדִּין עִמָּהּ, כְּדִידֵהּ דָּמוּ; אוֹ דִילְמָא, כֵּיוָן דִּמְשַׁעְבְּדִי לֵיהּ לְפֵירָא, כְּדִידֵיהּ דָּמוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Let the dilemma be raised according to the one who says** that **the law is with her,** and **since the law is with her,** the slaves **are** therefore **considered as hers.** It is not considered as if she sold them, and consequently they are not emancipated; **or perhaps since they are liened to** the husband **for** him to keep the **profits** of the slaves' labor, as the profits from their labor belong to the husband like the revenue from other property that a woman brings into the marriage, the slaves **are considered as his,** and it is as though he purchased her slaves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַדִּין עִמּוֹ – כֵּיוָן דְּהַדִּין עִמּוֹ, כְּדִידֵיהּ דָּמוּ; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא קָנֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ – כְּדִידֵהּ דָּמוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And let the dilemma be raised according to the one who says: The law is with him,** and **since the law is with him,** the slaves **are** therefore **considered as his,** and they should be emancipated; **or perhaps since** the husband **did not acquire the slave himself,** but only the rights to his labor, the slaves **are considered as hers.** No answer was found, and the Gemara concludes that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* presents the question in both directions. Even if you hold that she retains the right to reclaim the slaves themselves at divorce — *hadini mahu* that she gets them back — one could still argue: *keivan d'mishta'abdei leh l'peira k'dideh dami* — since the husband has the full right to use them and benefit from them for the entire duration of the marriage, that is sufficient ownership to treat this as a sale to *chutz la'aretz*, and the slaves should go free.

Conversely, even if you hold that the husband can keep the slaves and need only pay her their value at divorce — *hadini mahu* that he is not required to return them — one could still argue: *keivan d'lo kanei l'gufeh k'didah dami* — since he does not own them *for their body*, meaning his ownership is of a different character than ordinary ownership, they are treated as still belonging to her, and the transfer does not qualify as a sale that would free them.

The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

### Rashi's Explanation

*Rashi* addresses the puzzling phrase *lo kanei l'gufeh* — that the husband does not own the slaves "for their body." *Rashi* explains: *d'ei mipattar migaresh lah hadrei lah* — if he divorces her, they go back to her. But this is precisely the case that is in dispute! We are entertaining the possibility that *hadini mahu* — she is entitled to take them back. So the *pshat* in *Rashi* here is admittedly somewhat difficult to follow. The best reading may be that even within the scenario where she can theoretically demand the slaves themselves, the fact that his ownership is always shadowed by the possibility of having to return them renders it qualitatively different from ordinary ownership — something more akin to holding property in trust. It remains a challenging *Rashi*.

## Syria/Willingness Case

### Syria Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, שָׁנָה לִי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֶבֶד שֶׁיָּצָא אַחַר רַבּוֹ לְסוּרְיָא, וּמְכָרוֹ שָׁם רַבּוֹ – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת. וְהָתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אִיבֵּד אֶת זְכוּתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says: Rabbi Yoḥanan taught me:** If there was **a slave who** willingly **followed his master to Syria,** which is considered to be outside of Eretz Yisrael with regard to the sale of slaves, **and his master sold him there,** then the slave **is emancipated.** The Gemara challenges: **But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach:** If a slave left Eretz Yisrael willingly, he **lost his right** to be emancipated if he is then sold outside of Eretz Yisrael?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן שֶׁדַּעַת רַבּוֹ לַחְזוֹר, כָּאן שֶׁאֵין דַּעַת רַבּוֹ לַחְזוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult. Here,** where he is emancipated, it is referring to a case **where his master's intention** upon traveling to Syria was **to return,** and the slave followed him under that assumption. Therefore, when the master sold the slave in Syria, it is as though he sold him from Eretz Yisrael to outside of Eretz Yisrael. **There,** where he is not emancipated, is referring to a case **where his master does not intend to return,** and the slave followed him under that assumption. Since the slave willingly left Eretz Yisrael permanently, he lost his right to be emancipated if he is then sold outside of Eretz Yisrael.

*Rav Abba* teaches in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: an *eved* who followed his master to *Suriya* — the region of Syria, which has a quasi-*Eretz Yisrael* status having been conquered by *Dovid HaMelech* — and the master then sold him there, the slave goes free. This was already established in the first *perek*: selling a slave to *Suriya* is treated like selling to *chutz la'aretz*. The *chiddush* here is that even though the slave went along willingly, we do not say that he forfeited his right to freedom simply by choosing to accompany his master on the journey.

A counter-*baraita* from *Rabbi Chiya* states that the slave *lost* his right. The apparent contradiction is resolved without difficulty.

### Intent Distinction

*Lo kashya: kan she-da'at rabo lachzor, kan she-ein da'at rabo lachzor* — there is no contradiction. The two *baraitot* address different scenarios based on the master's intent. If the master was traveling to *Suriya* temporarily, intending to return to *Eretz Yisrael*, then the slave who accompanied him cannot be said to have voluntarily chosen to settle outside the Land. When the master subsequently sells him there, it is a sale to *chutz la'aretz* against the slave's wishes, and he goes free. But if the master was relocating permanently to *Suriya* and the slave willingly followed, the slave has chosen to settle outside the Land of his own accord. Having made that choice, he cannot now claim that being sold in *Suriya* entitles him to freedom.

### Proof Problem

This raises an evidentiary question: how does one determine the master's intent, particularly in ambiguous cases where the master never explicitly stated his plans? If the slave simply followed his master without asking, and the master never told him whether this was a permanent move or a trip, what is the default assumption? The intuition would be to favor the slave — we would not say he forfeited his rights unless we can establish that he affirmatively and knowingly chose to resettle outside *Eretz Yisrael*. But the *gemara* does not spell this out explicitly, and the question remains somewhat open.

One might also ask: if no master who sells his slave to *chutz la'aretz* will ever recover his investment, who would ever buy such a slave? The point may be precisely that — this *takana* is designed to make such purchases unattractive, and thereby to prevent the slave from ever being taken out of *Eretz Yisrael* in the first place.

## Sale Goes Through?

### Rav Anan Confused

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עָנָן, שְׁמַעִית מִינֵּיהּ דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל תַּרְתֵּי: חֲדָא – הָךְ; וְאִידָּךְ – דְּאִיתְּמַר: הַמּוֹכֵר שָׂדֵהוּ בִּשְׁנַת הַיּוֹבֵל עַצְמָהּ; רַב אָמַר: מְכוּרָה, וְיוֹצְאָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵינָהּ מְכוּרָה כׇּל עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Anan said: I learned two** *halakhot* **from Mar Shmuel. One** was **this** *halakha*, that a slave is emancipated after being sold to someone outside of Eretz Yisrael; **and the other** *halakha* I learned is **as it was stated** that there was a dispute with regard to the following issue: Certain types of fields that were sold before the Jubilee Year are returned to their original owners in the Jubilee Year. What is the *halakha* with regard to **one who sells his field in the Jubilee Year itself? Rav says: It is sold** in principle. However, **it leaves** the possession of the purchaser immediately, and the purchaser is not refunded his money. **And Shmuel says: It is not sold at all.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּחֲדָא – הָדְרִי זְבִינֵי, וּבַחֲדָא – לָא הָדְרִי זְבִינֵי; וְלָא יָדַעְנָא הֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Anan continues his statement with regard to the two *halakhot* he was taught by Mar Shmuel: **In one** of the two sales **the** money received from the **sale is returned, and in one** of the two sales **the** money received from the **sale is not returned,** and the purchaser loses his money, **but I do not know** in **which of** the cases the money is returned and in which of the cases it is not returned.

*Rav Anan* reports: *shma'na minei d'Mar Shmuel tarti* — I have heard from *Shmuel* two rulings, and I am uncertain how they relate. The first concerns the selling of a slave to *chutz la'aretz*; the second concerns the case of *hamocher sadeh bishnas hayovel* — one who sells a field during the *yovel* year.

In the *yovel* case, *Rav* and *Shmuel* dispute the status of such a sale. *Rav* holds that the sale *does* go through — it is a valid *kinyan* — and then a moment later it is reversed and the field returns to the original owner. One practical consequence is that the money is retained, since a valid sale occurred. Another *nafka mina* involves *metaltelin agav karka*: if movable property was acquired *agav* the field transaction, that acquisition stands, since the underlying *kinyan* was legitimate. *Shmuel* disagrees and holds that the sale does not go through at all — *eino mechura kol ikkar*.

Rav Anan's uncertainty is: in the slave case and in the *yovel* case, Shmuel holds one way in one and another way in the other. In which case does the sale go through, and in which does the money return to the purchaser?

### Abaye's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: נִיחְזֵי אֲנַן, מִדְּתָנֵי בְּבָרַיְיתָא: הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר מֵרַבּוֹ שֵׁנִי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קַנְיֵיהּ רַבּוֹ שֵׁנִי, וְלָא הָדְרִי זְבִינֵי; וְכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָתָם אֵינָהּ מְכוּרָה – וּמָעוֹת חוֹזְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Let us see** if it could be determined which case involves which *halakha*. **From what is taught in the *baraita*: One who sells his slave to** a Jew **outside** of **Eretz** Yisrael, then **he is emancipated, but** he nevertheless **requires a bill of manumission from his second master. Learn from** the *baraita* that **his second master acquired him, and the** money of the **sale is not returned.** In other words, from the fact that there is the need for the second master to emancipate him, it is clear that the sale took effect. Therefore, it stands to reason that the purchaser is not refunded the money of the sale. **And** if so, **when Shmuel said there** that the field **is not sold,** he meant that the sale does not take effect at all **and the money returns** to the purchaser.

*Rav Yosef* says: *v'lo yadana* — I don't know which is which. *Abaye* responds: *nechezei anan* — let us work it out ourselves. He cites the *baraita*: *hamocher avdo l'chutz la'aretz yatza l'cherut v'tzarich get shichrur merabbo sheni* — the second master must free him. *Shma minei*: the fact that it is the *second* master who must write the *get shichrur* proves that the sale *went through*. The buyer acquired the slave. *V'lo hadra zvini* — and the buyer does not get his money back; he loses it, and he must also free the slave. *De-chi amar Shmuel hatam eino mechura u-mos chozrin* — and in the *yovel* case, when *Shmuel* says the sale did not go through, the money returns to the purchaser.

### Why Rav Anan Uncertain

One might ask: is this not obvious from both cases? From the *baraita*, the second master is the one who frees the slave — clearly the sale went through. From *Shmuel*'s ruling on the *yovel* field — clearly the sale did not go through. Why was *Rav Anan* confused?

The answer is twofold. First, *Rav Anan* did not have access to this *baraita*. From the *mishnah* alone, one might have read the ruling as saying the sale did *not* go through — that the original seller must free the slave, similar to the *shemita* case. Second, even in the *yovel* case, even if *Shmuel* says the sale did not go through, does it necessarily follow that the money returns? Perhaps the money is treated as a *matanah* — a gift — on the theory that both parties knew the sale was invalid and the money was given anyway. This possibility is illustrated by the case of *ha-mekadesh et achoto* — one who gives money as *kiddushin* to his own sister. *Rav* holds the money is returned; *Shmuel* holds it is a *matanah*, since one cannot marry one's sister and the parties presumably knew this. So too, one might have argued the *yovel* money was a gift. The *gemara* concludes that in the *yovel* case, *Shmuel* means the money returns — it is not a gift — but *Rav Anan* could not have assumed this without the *baraita* to clarify the slave case.

### Shemitta Comparison

The parallel to *shemita* is instructive. In the *shemita*/yovel case where *Shmuel* says the sale did not go through, the money returns. In the slave case, the sale *did* go through, and the money does *not* return — the buyer loses. These are two distinct responses to halakhically problematic transactions: in one, the transaction is voided entirely; in the other, it is allowed to stand but the buyer bears the penalty.

## Buyer Loses: Why?

### Abaye Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי חָזֵית דְּקָנְסִינַן לְלוֹקֵחַ? נִקְנְסֵיהּ לְמוֹכֵר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו עַכְבְּרָא גַּנָּב אֶלָּא חוֹרָא גַּנָּב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי לָאו עַכְבְּרָא, חוֹרָא מְנָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the *halakha* that if one sells his slave to a Jew outside of Eretz Yisrael it is the purchaser who loses his money, **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: What did you see** to cause you to say **that we apply the penalty to the purchaser,** in that he is required to emancipate the slave and loses his money; **we should apply the penalty to the seller,** and he should be required to return the money. Rav Yosef answered Abaye with a parable and **said to him:** It is **not the mouse** that **steals, but the hole** that **steals.** In other words, a mouse cannot steal anything unless he has a hole for hiding the stolen items. Here too, the slave would not have been sold without the help of the purchaser. The Gemara questions this logic: But **if not** for **the mouse, from where** would **the hole** have the stolen item; since they both contribute to the prohibited act, each of them is deserving of being penalized.

This asymmetry — that the buyer is the one who loses his money and must free the slave — prompts *Abaye* to press *Rav Yosef*: *mai chazit de-kansinan le-loke'ach?* Why do we fine the purchaser? Why not say the sale did not go through and fine the seller — *nikhansinah le-mocher* — requiring *him* to free the slave while the buyer gets his money back, parallel to the *shemita* case?

### Weasel/Hole

*Rav Yosef* responds with a memorable formulation: *lav achbara ganav ela chura ganav* — it is not the weasel that steals, it is the hole. That is, the seller may be the proximate wrongdoer, but it is the buyer's domicile in *chutz la'aretz* that makes the whole transaction possible and problematic. Without the hole — without someone willing to receive the slave in *chutz la'aretz* — the weasel has nowhere to go.

*Abaye* pushes back: *ei lav achbara chura mai avid?* — if there were no weasel, what could the hole do? The hole does nothing on its own; the weasel is the initiator. This is in fact a genuine tension. In a market transaction, it is not always obvious who is the initiator. The buyer may well have approached the seller, sought him out, and pressured him to sell. The seller's being the *mocher* does not inherently make him the active wrongdoer. The analogy to a thief and his fence is imperfect, because there the wrongful initiative is clearly with the thief; here, either party could be the driving force.

### Locus of Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא, הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא אִיסּוּרָא הָתָם קָנְסִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef responded to him: **It stands to reason** that **anywhere that the forbidden** item, i.e., the slave, **is,** in this case, with the purchaser, **there we** should **penalize.**

The resolution offered is: *mistabra heicha de-ika issura hatam kansinan* — we fine where the *issur* is located. The buyer is the one living in *chutz la'aretz*; *chutz la'aretz* is the locus of the problem. It is the buyer's life situation — his residence outside the Land — that creates the problematic condition into which the slave is being introduced. Regardless of who initiated the transaction, the buyer is the one whose circumstances represent the ongoing *issur*. This is an interesting form of moral and legal reasoning: not simply who acted first, but where the sustained violation resides.

## Runaway Slave Case (II)

### Facts of Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא עַבְדָּא דַּעֲרַק מֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ לָאָרֶץ. אֲזַל מָרֵיהּ אַבָּתְרֵיהּ, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נִכְתּוֹב לָךְ שְׁטָרָא אַדְּמֵיהּ, וּכְתוֹב לֵיהּ גִּיטָּא דְחֵירוּתָא. וְאִי לָא, מַפְקַעְנָא לֵיהּ מִינָּךְ מִדְּרַבִּי אַחַי בְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain slave who fled** from his master **from outside of Eretz** Yisrael **to Eretz** Yisrael. **His master followed him** to Eretz Yisrael and **came before Rabbi Ami.** Rabbi Ami **said to** the master: **We will write a** promissory **note for his value for you, and you** should **write a bill of manumission for him. And if** you do **not** do this, **I will remove him from you** entirely, since he does not have to return to outside of Eretz Yisrael, **based on** the statement **of Rabbi Aḥai, son of Rabbi Yoshiya.**

The *gemara* presents a new case: *hahu avda de-ark mi-chutz la'aretz le-aretz* — a slave ran away from *chutz la'aretz* to *Eretz Yisrael*. His master, who had come from *chutz la'aretz*, pursued him and brought the matter before *Rabbi Ami*.

### Rabbi Ami Ruling

*Rabbi Ami* told the master: *nichtov lach shtra admei* — I will write you a document for his monetary value, so you are not left without compensation. *Ve-chtov lei gita de-cheiruta* — and you will write the slave a *get shichrur*. But if you refuse: *mafkeina lei minach* — I will free him from you against your will, and you will receive nothing. The incentive structure is clear: cooperate and be compensated; refuse, and lose everything. This is the inverse of the previous case: there, selling a slave to *chutz la'aretz* triggers a penalty on the buyer; here, a slave who flees *from chutz la'aretz to Eretz Yisrael* is freed, with the master offered compensation as an inducement to comply.

### Source Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״לֹא יֵשְׁבוּ בְּאַרְצְךָ פֶּן יַחֲטִיאוּ אוֹתְךָ לִי״ – יָכוֹל בְּגוֹי שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו שֶׁלֹּא לַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תַסְגִּיר עֶבֶד אֶל אֲדוֹנָיו אֲשֶׁר יִנָּצֵל אֵלֶיךָ מֵעִם אֲדוֹנָיו״, מַאי תַּקָּנָתוֹ? ״עִמְּךָ יֵשֵׁב בְּקִרְבְּךָ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the residents of the Land of Canaan: **"They shall not dwell in your land lest they make you to sin against Me,** for you will serve their gods; for they will be a snare to you" (Exodus 23:33). One **might** have thought that **the verse is** also **speaking of a gentile who accepted upon himself not to** engage in **idol worship,** and is teaching that such a gentile may not dwell in Eretz Yisrael as well; therefore **the verse states: "You shall not deliver to his master a slave who escaped to you from his master"** (Deuteronomy 23:16). The *baraita* understands that the verse is speaking in metaphoric terms about a gentile who has come to Eretz Yisrael, escaping his idolatrous past. The *baraita* continues: **What** is this gentile's **remedy? "He shall dwell with you in your midst"** (Deuteronomy 23:17). This teaches that as long as he accepts upon himself not to engage in idol worship, he may remain in Eretz Yisrael.

The basis for this ruling is *de-Rabbi Achi be-Rabbi Yoshiya*, from a *baraita*. The *baraita* opens by interpreting the verse *lo yeshvu be-artzecha pen yachti'u otcha li* — they shall not dwell in your land lest they cause you to sin against Me. A preliminary reading asks: might this apply even to a non-Jew who accepted upon himself not to worship idols — even to someone keeping the *sheva mitzvot benei Noach*? The *talmud lomar* responds by citing: *lo tasgir eved el adonav asher yinatzel eleicha me-im adonav* — do not hand over a slave who has fled to you from his master.

That verse is then read — in a striking and admittedly forced fashion — as referring not to a slave at all, but to a non-Jew seeking to leave a pagan community and find refuge among a monotheistic people. Even if he has not converted, the verse instructs us not to return him to his *avoda zara*. The continuation, *imcha yeshev be-kirbecha*, supports this reading.

### Rabbi Yoshiya Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: הַאי ״מֵעִם אֲדוֹנָיו״?! ״מֵעִם אָבִיו״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: בְּמוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the explanation of the verse in the *baraita* is **difficult for Rabbi Yoshiya: This** expression employed in the verse: **"From his master,"** is imprecise if it is speaking about a gentile who abandons idol worship, as **it should have** stated: **From his father,** as a father is a more apt metaphor for the religion in which one was raised. **Rather, Rabbi Yoshiya** explains differently and **says: The verse is speaking of one who sells his slave to** a Jew who lives **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, and the continuation of the verse: "He shall dwell with you," means that he does not go to his new master outside of Eretz Yisrael, but is emancipated and remains in Eretz Yisrael.

*Rabbi Yoshiya* was dissatisfied with that reading. The phrase *me-im adonav* — from his master — does not fit a man fleeing from his religious community; it would have said *me-im aviv* or *me-im elohav*. *Ella amar Rabbi Yoshiya*, here is the *pshat* of the verse: *bemocher avdo l'chutz la'aretz hakasuv medaber* — the verse is speaking of one who sells his slave to *chutz la'aretz*. On this reading, *lo tasgir* means do not hand the slave over to the second master who bought him in *chutz la'aretz*; *asher yinatzel eleicha* describes the slave seeking to remain in *Eretz Yisrael* rather than being transferred outside. This is the *pshat* of our *mishnah*, according to *Rabbi Yoshiya* — the verse is the direct source for the prohibition of selling a slave to *chutz la'aretz*.

### Rabbi Achi Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַחַי בְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: הַאי ״אֲשֶׁר יִנָּצֵל אֵלֶיךָ״?! ״אֲשֶׁר יִנָּצֵל מֵעִמְּךָ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אַחַי בְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: בְּעֶבֶד שֶׁבָּרַח מֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ לָאָרֶץ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the explanation of Rabbi Yoshiya is **difficult for Rabbi Aḥai, son of Rabbi Yoshiya:** If it is referring to a slave who is sold to one outside of Eretz Yisrael, then **this** expression: **"Who escaped to you,"** is not accurate, as he is leaving from Eretz Yisrael, not escaping to Eretz Yisrael. According to Rabbi Yoshiya's explanation, **it should have** stated: **Who escaped from you. Rather, Rabbi Aḥai, son of Rabbi Yoshiya, said: The verse is speaking of a slave who escaped from outside of Eretz** Yisrael **to Eretz** Yisrael, which indicates that in such a case he may dwell there and is not returned to his master. Based on this statement of Rabbi Aḥai, son of Rabbi Yoshiya, Rabbi Ami told the master that the slave will in any case be emancipated.

*Rabbi Achi b'rei d'Rabbi Yoshiya* — his son — challenged this reading. The phrase *asher yinatzel eleicha* — who seeks refuge with you — implies active movement *toward* the speaker. If the verse meant to describe a slave being taken away from someone, it should have said *asher yinatzel me-imcha*, that he is being wrested *from* you. The word *eleicha* — toward you — strongly implies the slave is running *to* someone. *Ella amar Rabbi Achi b'rei d'Rabbi Yoshiya*: *b'eved shebarach mi-chutz la'aretz la'aretz hakasuv medaber* — the verse speaks of a slave who fled from *chutz la'aretz* to *Eretz Yisrael*. This is the straightforward *pshat*: a runaway slave. Do not hand him back. *Imcha yeshev be-kirbecha b'echad she'arecha b'tov lo* — he shall dwell with you in the land, in one of your cities, wherever is good for him.

On this reading, if a slave runs from one place in *chutz la'aretz* to another in *chutz la'aretz*, or from one place in *Eretz Yisrael* to another in *Eretz Yisrael*, the verse does not apply and you would return him. It is only the specific case of flight from *chutz la'aretz* to *Eretz Yisrael* that triggers the protection.

### Written Writ Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״לֹא תַסְגִּיר עֶבֶד אֶל אֲדוֹנָיו״ – רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בְּלוֹקֵחַ עֶבֶד עַל מְנָת לְשַׁחְרְרוֹ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: דִּכְתַב לֵיהּ הָכִי: ״לִכְשֶׁאֶקָּחֲךָ, הֲרֵי עַצְמְךָ קָנוּי לְךָ מֵעַכְשָׁיו״.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: The verse states: **"You shall not deliver to his master a slave"** (Deuteronomy 23:16); **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: The verse is speaking of one who acquires a slave in order to emancipate him.** The court may not deliver him to this master, because he is not his slave and he may not treat him as such. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** For example, **when he wrote to** the slave **like this: When I will purchase you, you are hereby acquired to yourself from now.** The new master does not take possession of the slave, as he is emancipated immediately upon being purchased.

A separate *baraita* is then cited: *lo tasgir eved el adonav* — *Rebbi* interprets this as speaking of one who *buys a slave on condition that he will free him*: *b'lo'ke'ach eved al m'nas l'shachreru hakasuv medaber*. If the buyer reneges and tries to keep the slave, *beit din* will not hand the slave over to him. *Rav Dimi bar Yitzchak* specifies the precise scenario: *dechasiv lei hachi — l'cheshitkaneh harei atzmecha k'nuyeh lach me-achshav* — the buyer had already written a *shtar shichrur* to take effect upon purchase. Having issued such a document, he may not now reverse course and enslave the man.

## Runaway Slave Case (II)

### Samaritans Episode

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא עֲרַק לֵיהּ עַבְדָּא לְבֵי כוּתָאֵי, שְׁלַח לְהוּ: [אַ]הְדְּרוּהּ נִיהֲלִי. שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: ״לֹא תַסְגִּיר עֶבֶד אֶל אֲדוֹנָיו״. שְׁלַח לְהוּ: וְכֵן תַּעֲשֶׂה לַחֲמוֹרוֹ וְכֵן תַּעֲשֶׂה לְשִׂמְלָתוֹ וְכֵן תַּעֲשֶׂה לְכׇל אֲבֵידַת אָחִיךָ״. שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: וְהָכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַסְגִּיר עֶבֶד אֶל אֲדוֹנָיו״, שְׁלַח לְהוּ: הַהוּא, בְּעֶבֶד שֶׁבָּרַח מֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ לָאָרֶץ, וְכִדְרַבִּי אַחַי בְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Ḥisda's slave escaped to Bei Kutai,** a place where Samaritans lived. **He sent** a request **to** the residents of that place: **Return him to me. They sent** a response **to him:** The verse states: **"You shall not deliver to his master a slave,"** so we will not return this slave to you. **He sent** a response **to them:** The verse also states with regard to lost items: "And you shall restore it to him…**and so you shall do for his donkey and so you shall do for his garment and so you shall do for anything your brother has lost"** (Deuteronomy 22:2–3). **They sent** a response **to him** again: **But isn't it written: "You shall not deliver to his master a slave"?** Rav Ḥisda **sent** a response **to them: That** verse is referring **to a slave who escaped from outside of Eretz** Yisrael **to Eretz** Yisrael, **and in accordance with** the explanation **of Rabbi Aḥai, son of Rabbi Yoshiya,** and my slave escaped from one location outside of Eretz Yisrael to another location outside of Eretz Yisrael.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא דִּשְׁלַח לְהוּ כִּדְרַבִּי אַחַי בְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה? מִשּׁוּם דְּמַשְׁמַע לְהוּ קְרָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is different** about this case **that** led him to **send** a response **to them** specifically **in accordance with** the explanation **of Rabbi Aḥai, son of Rabbi Yoshiya,** and not in accordance with any other interpretation of the verse? The Gemara answers: **Because that is** how the Samaritans would **understand the verse.** Samaritans did not generally accept the explanations of the Sages, and this explanation accords with the straightforward reading of the verse, while the other explanations do not.

*Rav Chisda*'s slave ran to the *Kutim* — the Samaritans. *Rav Chisda* sent word: *haderoni nihalei* — return him to me. The *Kutim* responded by quoting the verse *lo tasgir eved el adonav* — we do not return runaway slaves. *Rashi* notes that the *Kutim* were in *chutz la'aretz*, though they read the verse as a flat prohibition without any geographical qualification.

*Rav Chisda* countered: *v'chen ta'aseh lachamoro v'chen ta'aseh l'simlato v'chen ta'aseh l'chol avedat achicha* — there is also a verse obligating the return of lost property, and a slave is property. The *Kutim* replied that the explicit verse *lo tasgir eved el adonav* overrides a general lost-property obligation. *Rav Chisda* then invoked the reading of *Rabbi Achi b'rei d'Rabbi Yoshiya*: *hahu b'eved shebarach mi-chutz la'aretz la'aretz* — that verse applies only when the slave fled *from chutz la'aretz to Eretz Yisrael*, not to an ordinary runaway.

The *gemara* notes that *Rav Chisda* chose to invoke *Rabbi Achi b'rei d'Rabbi Yoshiya*'s *drasha* rather than *Rebbi*'s. Why not simply cite *Rebbi*, who also limits the verse? Because *Rabbi Achi b'rei d'Rabbi Yoshiya*'s reading is closer to the *pshat* of the verse, and the *Kutim* were more likely to be persuaded by an argument grounded in the natural reading of the text than by rabbinic authority alone.

### Abaye's Mule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אִירְכַס לֵיהּ חֲמָרָא בֵּי כוּתָאֵי, שְׁלַח לְהוּ: שַׁדְּרוּהּ לִי. שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: שְׁלַח סִימָנָא. שְׁלַח לְהוּ: דְּחִיוּוֹרָא כְּרֵיסֵיהּ. שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: אִי לָאו דְּנַחְמָנִי אַתְּ, לָא הֲוָה מְשַׁדַּרְנָא לֵיהּ נִיהֲלָךְ; אַטּוּ כּוּלֵּי חֲמָרֵי לָאו כְּרֵיסַיְיהוּ חִיוָּורֵי נִינְהוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Abaye lost a donkey** among the Samaritans in **Bei Kutai. He sent** a request **to them: Send it to me. They sent** a response **to him: Send a distinguishing mark** and we will return it to you. **He sent** the following distinguishing mark **to them: That its belly is white. They sent** a response **to him: If not** for the fact **that you are Naḥmani,** meaning that we know that you are a trustworthy man, **we would not send it to you. Is that to say** that **bellies of all donkeys aren't white?** Therefore, it is not a true distinguishing mark.

*Abaye* lost a mule that strayed to the *Kutim*. He sent word requesting its return. They replied: *tzaruch simana* — give us an identifying mark. He sent back: *d'chivara kerisei* — it has a white belly. They replied: if you were not *Nachmani* — his given name — we would not return it to you. *Atu kulei chamra lav keriseihu chivranin ninhu?* What kind of *siman* is that? Every mule has a white belly. But given who you are, we will send it back. The episode illustrates the looseness of identifying marks when a claimed *siman* is common to many animals, while also reflecting the particular dynamics of interactions with the *Kutim*.

## Pidyon Shvuyim

### Value Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין פּוֹדִין אֶת הַשְּׁבוּיִין יָתֵר עַל כְּדֵי דְּמֵיהֶן, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם. וְאֵין מַבְרִיחִין אֶת הַשְּׁבוּיִין, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת הַשְּׁבוּיִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The captives are not redeemed** for **more than their** actual monetary **value, for the betterment of the world; and one may not aid the captives** in their attempt to **escape** from their captors **for the betterment of the world,** so that kidnappers will not be more restrictive with their captives to prevent them from escaping. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: For the betterment of the captives,** so that kidnappers will not avenge the escape of the captives by treating other captives with cruelty.

The *mishnah* states: *ein podin et hashvuyim yoter k'dei d'meihen* — one does not redeem captives for more than their monetary value. That value is assessed against the slave market: what would this person fetch in an open slave market? The captors, of course, know that a particular individual is worth far more to his family or community, and will demand accordingly. The *mishnah* instructs that one should not pay the inflated ransom. The stated reason: *mipnei tikkun ha'olam* — for the betterment of the world.

The *mishnah* also states: *v'ein mavrichin et hashvuyim* — one does not mount an operation to free captives by stealth or rescue. This too is *mipnei tikkun ha'olam*, or, according to *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, *mipnei tikkun hashvuyim*.

### Rabban Shimon Reason

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says the reason is *mipnei tikkun hashvuyim* — for the welfare of the captives themselves, not for the world at large. The distinction between *tikkun ha'olam* and *tikkun hashvuyim* proves significant, as the *gemara* will show.

### Two Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הַאי ״מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם״ – מִשּׁוּם דּוּחְקָא דְצִבּוּרָא הוּא, אוֹ דִילְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא לִגְרְבוּ וְלַיְיתוֹ טְפֵי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **this** expression: **For the betterment of the world,** is **it due to the** financial **pressure of the community?** Is the concern that the increase in price will lead to the community assuming financial pressures it will not be able to manage? **Or perhaps** it is **because** the result of this will be **that they will not seize and bring additional** captives, as they will see that it is not worthwhile for them to take Jews captive?

*Ibaya lehu*: the *gemara* asks what *tikkun ha'olam* means in this context. Is the concern *mishum duchka de-tzibbura* — because the financial burden on the community of paying inflated ransom is simply unsustainable? Or is it *mishum d'lo ligrebu v'layeisu tefei* — because paying high ransom encourages future kidnapping, as captors learn that Jews will pay whatever is demanded?

The *nafka mina* is substantial. If the reason is financial burden, then an individual with sufficient private means may pay whatever he wishes for his own family member, since he is not burdening the community. But if the reason is systemic — that paying inflated ransoms incentivizes future kidnappings — then even private individuals should be prohibited from doing so, because their actions affect the broader policy. Similarly for the rescue operation: if the concern is about reprisals against remaining captives (*tikkun hashvuyim*), then the prohibition only applies when others will suffer for it. If no one is left behind, a rescue operation would be permitted. If the concern is discouraging future kidnappings (*tikkun ha'olam*), one must weigh that even a successful rescue with no immediate victims perpetuates the broader problem.

### Levi Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּלֵוִי בַּר דַּרְגָּא פַּרְקַהּ לִבְרַתֵּיהּ בִּתְלֵיסַר אַלְפֵי דִּינְרֵי זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an answer based on the fact **that Levi bar Darga redeemed his daughter** who was taken captive **with thirteen thousand gold dinars.** This indicates that private citizens may pay excessive sums to redeem a captive if they so choose. Therefore, it must be that the reason for the ordinance was to avoid an excessive burden being placed upon the community. If the ordinance was instituted to remove the incentive for kidnappers to capture Jews, a private citizen would also not be permitted to pay an excessive sum.

The story of *Levi bar Darga* is brought as evidence: *parkei lbarteih btlisar alfei dinarei zahav* — he redeemed his daughter for 13,000 gold dinars, clearly far in excess of her market value. If the reason for the prohibition is communal financial burden, this presents no problem, since he used his own funds. If the reason is systemic discouragement of future kidnapping, it should have been prohibited even here.

### Abaye Uncertainty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וּמַאן לֵימָא לַן דְּבִרְצוֹן חֲכָמִים עֲבַד? דִּילְמָא שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְצוֹן חֲכָמִים עֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: And who told us that he acted in accordance with the wishes of the Sages? Perhaps he acted against the wishes of the Sages,** and this anecdote cannot serve as a proof.

*Abaye* responds: *u-man leima lan de-biratzon chachamim avad* — who says the sages approved of what he did? *Dilma shelo biratzon chachamim avad* — perhaps he acted without their approval, and the case cannot serve as a proof. The question thus remains unresolved by this story.

This issue resonated through the medieval period. The *Maharam miRothenburg* famously refused to allow his community to redeem him at the exorbitant price demanded by his captors, invoking precisely this principle, and died in captivity as a result. The irony was compounded when, after his death, an enormous sum was paid to retrieve his body for burial.

### Tosafot Distinctions

*Tosafot* addresses what seems to be an exception: a *baraita* in *perek Naarah* states that if a man's wife is taken captive and her captors demand ten times her value, *paam rishon podeh* — the husband is obligated to redeem her the first time, even at this inflated sum. How is this consistent with the prohibition?

*Tosafot* explains: *shani ishto de-havya k'gufo yoter mibito* — his wife is different because she is like his own body, even more so than his daughter. As for redeeming oneself: *deal atzmo lo tiknu shelo yitein kol asher lo bad nafsho* — the sages never enacted that a person may not give everything he has to save his own life. Whatever the broader societal concern, we would not extend it to the point of preventing a person from paying any price for his own life. And since one may redeem oneself, one may also redeem one's wife, who is treated as one's own body. The daughter, however, does not benefit from this exception; *Levi bar Darga*'s case, as *Abaye* noted, was of uncertain approval.

## Rav Nachman Story

### Pot Miracle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנָתֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן בָּחֲשָׁן קִדְרָא בִּידַיְיהוּ. קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַב עִילִישׁ, כְּתִיב: ״אָדָם אֶחָד מֵאֶלֶף מָצָאתִי וְאִשָּׁה בְכׇל אֵלֶּה לֹא מָצָאתִי״, הָא אִיכָּא בְּנָתֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן! גְּרַמָא לְהוּ מִילְּתָא וְאִשְׁתַּבְיָין, וְאִישְׁתְּבִאי אִיהוּ נָמֵי בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates that **Rav Naḥman's daughters would stir a** boiling **pot with their** bare **hands,** and people thought that the heat did not harm them due to their righteousness. **Rav Ilish had a difficulty** with a verse, as it **is written: "A man one of a thousand I have found, and a woman among all those have I not found"** (Ecclesiastes 7:28). **Aren't there Rav Naḥman's daughters,** who were exceptionally righteous? **These words caused them to be taken captive,** due to the evil eye, **and** Rav Ilish **was also taken captive with them.**

The *daf* concludes with a striking aggadic narrative. *Bantei de-Rav Nachman* — the daughters of *Rav Nachman* — possessed the ability to stir a boiling pot directly with their bare hands, apparently a miraculous sign of their righteousness.

### Rav Illish Puzzle

*Rav Illish* found this perplexing. The verse states *adam echad me-elef matzati v'ishah be-chol eleh lo matzati* — I found one righteous man among a thousand, but among women I found not one. How then could *Rav Nachman*'s daughters be recipients of such a miracle? They appeared to be exceptional *tzadikim*.

### Birds Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה יָתֵיב גַּבֵּיהּ הָהוּא גַּבְרָא, דַּהֲוָה יָדַע בְּלִישָּׁנָא דְצִיפּוֹרֵי. אֲתָא עוֹרְבָא וְקָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי קָאָמַר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עִילִישׁ בְּרַח, עִילִישׁ בְּרַח״. אָמַר: עוֹרְבָא שַׁקָּרָא הוּא וְלָא סָמֵיכְנָא עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **One day a certain man was sitting with him** in captivity **who knew the language of birds. A raven came and called to** Rav Ilish. Rav Ilish **said to** the man: **What is** the raven **saying? He said to him** that it is saying: **Ilish, escape; Ilish, escape.** Rav Ilish **said: It is a lying raven, and I do not rely on it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּהָכִי אֲתָא יוֹנָה וְקָא קָרְיָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי קָאָמְרָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עִילִישׁ בְּרַח, עִילִישׁ בְּרַח״. אָמַר: כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל כְּיוֹנָה מְתִילָא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִתְרְחִישׁ לִי נִיסָּא. אָמַר: אֵיזִיל אֶחְזֵי בְּנָתֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אִי קָיְימָן בְּהֵימָנוּתַיְיהוּ – אַהְדְּרִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **In the meantime, a dove came and was calling** out. Rav Ilish **said to** the man: **What is it saying? He said to him** that the dove said: **Ilish, escape; Ilish, escape.** Ilish **said: The Congregation of Israel is compared to a dove;** I **conclude from** the dove's words that **a miracle will happen for me,** and I can attempt to escape. Rav Ilish **said:** Before I leave, **I will go** and **I will see Rav Naḥman's daughters. If they remained** steadfast **in their faith** and are acting appropriately, then I will take them with me and **I will return them** to their home.

While *Rav Illish* and *Rav Nachman*'s daughters were all in captivity together, *Rav Illish* was sitting one day with a certain man who could understand the language of birds. A raven came and called out. *Rav Illish* asked: what is it saying? The man told him: *Illish berach Illish berach* — Illish, flee, Illish, flee. *Rav Illish* dismissed it: *urva shikra hu v'lo samichna aleh* — ravens are liars; I do not trust him. Then a dove came and called out the same message: *Illish berach Illish berach*. *Rav Illish* took this seriously: *Knesset Yisrael ke-yonah metila* — the Jewish people are compared to a dove, as in *Shir HaShirim*. *Shma mina mitrachish li nisa* — this tells me that a miracle will indeed happen for me if I flee.

This episode carries a *halachic* implication as well: the prohibition against mounting rescue operations for captives does not prevent *the captive himself* from attempting to escape. Just as one may redeem oneself, one may save oneself — paralleling *Tosafot*'s point about self-redemption in the *pidyon shvuyim* discussion.

### Daughters Prefer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר: נְשֵׁי, כׇּל מִילֵּי דְּאִית לְהוּ סָדְרָן לַהֲדָדֵי בְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא. שַׁמְעִינְהוּ דְּקָאָמְרָן: עָדֵי גּוּבְרִין וּנְהַרְדָּעֵי גּוּבְרִין, לֵימָא לְהוּ לְשָׁבוֹיַיְהוּ דְּלִירַחֲקִינְהוּ מֵהָכָא, דְּלָא לֵיתוֹ אִינָשִׁין וְלִישְׁמְעִי וְלִיפְרְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **He said: Women tell all of their** secret **matters to each other in the bathroom,** so he went there to eavesdrop on them. **He heard them saying: These** captors are now **our husbands, and the men of Neharde'a** to whom we are married are **our husbands. We should tell our captors to distance us from here so that our husbands should not come** to this area **and hear** that we are here, **and redeem us,** and take us home. They preferred to remain with their captors.

Before fleeing, *Rav Illish* resolved to check on *Rav Nachman*'s daughters: *i kaiman be-heimanutaihu* — if they had kept their faith and not surrendered themselves sexually to their captors, *adrinhu* — he would take them with him. Women, he knew, share their secrets with one another in private. He went to eavesdrop outside the bathroom. He heard them saying: *adei guvrin u-neharda-ei guvrin* — we used to have husbands back there, and now these are our husbands. *Leimru lehu le-shavuyaihu de-lerachkinu me-hacha de-la leitu inshi ve-lishme-u ve-lifre-kunu* — let us tell our captors to take us further away, so that our former husbands cannot find out and redeem us. They were happy with their situation and wanted to prevent being rescued.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָם, עֲרַק, אֲתָא אִיהוּ וְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא. לְדִידֵיהּ אִיתְרְחִישׁ לֵיהּ נִיסָּא עֲבַר בְּמַבָּרָא, וְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא אַשְׁכְּחוּהּ וְקַטְלוּהּ. כִּי הָדְרָן וְאָתָן, אֲמַר: הֲווֹ קָא בָחֲשָׁן קִידְרָא בִּכְשָׁפִים.
>
> **Translation:** Upon hearing this Rav Ilish **arose** and **escaped. He and that man** who knew the language of the birds **came** to a river crossing. **A miracle happened for him** and **he crossed** the river **on a ferry, and** the captors **found that man and killed him. When** Rav Naḥman's daughters **were returned and they came** back from their captivity, Rav Ilish **said: They would stir the pot with witchcraft,** and that is why they were not burned by the boiling pot, but it was not due to their righteousness.

Clearly, they had not kept faith. They were not *tzadikim*. The miracle of stirring the pot could not have been on their account. *Rav Illish* fled — *kam arak* — without them. He and the bird-interpreter escaped together. For *Rav Illish*, a miracle occurred: *avar be-mavra* — he was able to cross a body of water and escape the captors. The bird-interpreter, *dahu gavra*, was found by the captors and killed. The miracle was for *Rav Illish* alone.

### Witchcraft Theory

When *Rav Illish* returned, he concluded: *havei kavshi kaderah be-chashafim* — they had been stirring the pot with witchcraft, not righteousness. The miracle was never theirs; they had simply been practicing sorcery. The verse, after all, is vindicated: righteous women of that caliber are not to be found, and what appeared to be a miracle of piety turns out to have been an act of *kishuf*.
