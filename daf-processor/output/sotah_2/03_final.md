# Sotah 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Sotah

### Rashi & Commentary

We begin a new *masechet* today: *Sotah*, daf 2. A few preliminary remarks about the tractate are in order. After the extended journey through *Nazir*, we are grateful to have genuine *Rashi* restored to us — and one comes to appreciate *Rashi* all the more having gone without him for a time. His commentary is distilled, clear, and remarkably terse, and that terseness has cascading effects on the shape of the *daf* as a whole.

In *Nazir*, the pseudo-*Rashi* had to do double duty: both explain the flow of the *gemara* in the manner of *Rashi* and take on a broader expository role — and he did so at considerable length. *Tosafos*, in turn, was largely occupied with the work of basic clarification rather than the broader analytical and comparative questions that are *Tosafos*'s hallmark. Here in *Sotah*, by contrast, genuine *Rashi* handles the baseline explanation with characteristic economy, freeing *Tosafos* to focus on precisely those sharper, cross-tractate analytical questions that define it at its best. The result is that both commentaries are leaner, and there is correspondingly more *gemara* per *daf*. Add to this the substantial amount of *aggadeta* woven throughout *Sotah* — one can glance ahead and see it already taking over entire *dappin* — and the conclusion is that we will need to move at a somewhat brisker pace.

### Content Overview

As advertised by its placement in *Seder Nashim*, *Masechet Sotah* deals with *parshat Sotah* in the *Torah* and follows a remarkably sequential logical structure. The first six or so *perakim* work through the procedure systematically: under what circumstances is a woman declared a *sotah* — a woman suspected of having committed adultery — such that she must be brought to the *Beit Hamikdash* to undergo the ritual of the bitter waters? Once those circumstances are established, the *masechet* moves to the procedure itself: the bringing of the waters, the ritual of drinking, the offering of sacrifices, the possible consequences, and then the treatment of special cases. For instance, what is the *halakha* if the woman is a *shomeres yabam* — a woman awaiting *yibbum* — who is suspected of adultery? What if she is an *arusah* rather than a *nesuah*? The structure is almost entirely logical and sequential, proceeding from initial suspicion through to the ritual and its ramifications.

### Later Chapters

The final chapters of the *masechet* are particularly rich. The seventh *perek* opens with the question of *eilu ne'emarim bechol lashon* — which ritual recitations may be said in any language, rather than exclusively in Hebrew. Since the recitation of *parshat sotah* itself is among those discussed, this becomes an occasion for a wide-ranging survey of ritual speech: *Kriat Shema*, *tefillah*, *Birkat Kohanim*, and many others. The *masechet* uses the question of language as a lens through which to explore other dimensions of these *mitzvot* as well.

The final *perek* concerns *eglah arufah* — the breaking of the neck of the heifer in a case of unresolved murder — which enters through the same hook of whether its declaration is said *bechol lashon*. But the *masechet* then takes the opportunity to treat the full parameters of *eglah arufah*: when it applies, how one measures the distance to the nearest city, and all its other details. This is, in fact, the one place in *Shas* where *eglah arufah* receives a sustained treatment; *Sanhedrin* handles the prosecution of a murderer once he is found, but *Sotah* is the natural home for the case of not knowing. And that, one might argue, is not merely a coincidence of where the hook of language happened to land. Both *sotah* and *eglah arufah* are rituals performed under conditions of uncertainty — in one case, uncertainty about whether a grave offense was committed at all; in the other, uncertainty about who committed a grave offense that may itself be known. The pairing seems deliberate and conceptually coherent.

## From Jealousy to Law

### Makane Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְקַנֵּא לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מְקַנֵּא לָהּ עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם, וּמַשְׁקָהּ עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד, אוֹ עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: מְקַנֵּא לָהּ עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם, וּמַשְׁקָהּ עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who issues a warning to his wife** not to seclude herself with a particular man, so that if she does not heed his warning she will assume the status of a woman suspected by her husband of having been unfaithful [*sota*], **Rabbi Eliezer says:** He **issues a warning to her based on,** i.e., in the presence of, **two** witnesses for the warning to be effective. If two witnesses were not present for the warning, she is not a *sota* even if two witnesses saw her seclusion with another man. **And** the husband **gives** the bitter water to her **to drink based on** the testimony of **one witness** who saw the seclusion, **or** even **based on his own** testimony that he himself saw them secluded together, as Rabbi Eliezer holds that only the warning requires witnesses, not the seclusion. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** He both **issues a warning to her based on two** witnesses **and gives** the bitter water to her **to drink based on** the testimony of **two** witnesses.

The *mishna* opens: *hamekane le'ishto* — if a man warns his wife. Here we immediately encounter a striking transformation of a verb. In the *Torah*, the operative verb is *ve'kina et ishto* — and he was jealous of his wife, or he harbored jealousy toward his wife. The root is *kaneh*, connoting jealousy, a spirit of *kinah*, something entirely internal and subjective. The *Torah* itself presents the matter in terms of *ruach kinah* — a spirit of jealousy passed over him — and then the same act is described again whether or not his suspicion proved correct: *ve'kina et ishto ve'hi nitme'ah, o avar alav ruach kinah ve'kina et ishto ve'hi lo nitme'ah*. He is jealous; she may or may not have been *tmeiah*. The whole structure in the *pesukim* is personal, subjective, unverified.

What *Chazal* do with *hamekane le'ishto* is something remarkable. Notice even the preposition shifts: in the *Torah* it is *ve'kina et ishto* — jealousy directed *at* his wife — whereas the *mishna* has *mekane le'ishto*, warning or admonishing *his wife*. The entire act is transformed from an expression of private jealousy into a formal legal act: a specific warning, directed at a specific named man, delivered in the presence of witnesses. The subjective emotional state becomes an objective judicial foundation.

### Presumption Debate

This transformation is all the more striking when one reads the *pesukim* carefully, as they introduce the *parsha* of *sotah*. The *Torah* begins: *ish ish ki tisteh ishto u'ma'alah bo ma'al, ve'shachav ish otah shichvat zera, ve'ne'elam me'einei ishah, ve'nistarah ve'hi nitme'ah, ve'ed ein bah ve'hi lo nitpasah* — a man's wife has gone astray and was unfaithful; another man lay with her, it was hidden from her husband's eyes, she was secretive, she became *tmeiah*, and there is no witness, and she was not caught. Only afterward does the *Torah* describe the husband's jealousy and the possibility that perhaps she did not sin after all.

One might argue — and it is a compelling reading — that the *pshat* of the *pesukim* carries a built-in presumption of guilt. The opening verses do not present an uncertain situation from the outset; they describe what seems to have occurred and then note only the absence of witnesses. From this reading, the ritual of the bitter waters functions not so much to resolve genuine doubt as to clear a woman who is already presumed guilty but against whom the evidentiary threshold for prosecution has not been met. The *pasuk* at the conclusion of the *parsha* — *ve'nikah ha'ish me'avon ve'ha'isha ha'hi tisa et avonah* — reinforces this: the husband is explicitly exonerated, implying he did something right in pursuing this.

Another possibility worth considering is that the opening verses — *ve'shachav ish otah* and what follows — describe not objective events but the images filling the husband's imagination. They render his inner world of suspicion, not a factual account.

In any case, *halakha* reads the situation differently than the *pshat* of the *pesukim* might suggest. Once a woman achieves *sotah* status, she carries the status of *tmeiah*: she is forbidden to continue having relations with her husband, who is likewise forbidden from continuing to be with her. She is treated, in effect, as though she committed adultery — until the ritual in the *Beit Hamikdash* clarifies her status. This is the *gemara*'s framing: not that she is presumed guilty absent any foundation, but that the circumstantial evidence — the formal warning, the disregard of that warning, the subsequent *stirah* — creates a sufficient legal foundation. The *gemara* will articulate this as *raglayim la-davar*: circumstantial evidence, a legitimate basis for assuming.

It is worth noting that *Masechet Nazir* closed with a sequence of *mishnayot* about *raglayim la-davar* — when circumstantial evidence provides sufficient basis to proceed — and this may well have been an intentional segue from *Nazir* to *Sotah*. That phrase becomes central to understanding the entire structure of *sotah* as *Chazal* reframe it.

The reframing also calls to mind the *Torah*'s treatment of the *go'el ha-dam*, the blood avenger. There too, the *Torah* begins from an existing social reality — the avenger of blood, acting on tribal impulse — and then introduces a judicial structure around it: the *arei miklat*, the role of the *beit din*, the return of the killer after the death of the *kohen gadol*. The *Torah* does not eliminate the institution entirely but channels and constrains it. Something similar is happening with *sotah*: the raw jealousy of a husband that might, in a pre-legal context, have resulted in vigilante honor killing is brought under judicial auspices. This point deserves particular emphasis. Already within the *pesukim*, the *Torah* is controlling what could otherwise become unchecked violence against women, directing it instead into a structured process. The *Torah she'ba'al peh* goes further still, requiring objective circumstantial foundations before the procedure may even begin.

## From Jealousy to Law (II)

### Three Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד מְקַנֵּא לָהּ? אוֹמֵר לָהּ בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם: "אַל תְּדַבְּרִי עִם אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי", וְדִבְּרָה עִמּוֹ — עֲדַיִין הִיא מוּתֶּרֶת לְבֵיתָהּ וּמוּתֶּרֶת לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna asks: **How** does he **issue a warning to her** in an effective manner? If he **says to her in the presence of two** witnesses: **Do not speak with** the **man** called **so-and-so, and she** nevertheless **spoke with him, she is still permitted to her home,** i.e., she is permitted to engage in sexual intercourse with her husband, **and** if she is the wife of a priest **she is** still **permitted to partake of *teruma*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִכְנְסָה עִמּוֹ לְבֵית הַסֵּתֶר וְשָׁהֲתָה עִמּוֹ כְּדֵי טוּמְאָה — אֲסוּרָה לְבֵיתָהּ, וַאֲסוּרָה לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה. וְאִם מֵת — חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** However, if after he told her not to speak with so-and-so, **she entered into a secluded place and remained with** that man long **enough to** become **defiled,** i.e., sufficient time to engage in sexual intercourse, **she is forbidden to her home** from that moment until she undergoes the *sota* rite. **And** likewise, if she was the wife of a priest **she is prohibited from partaking of *teruma*,** as she was possibly disqualified by her infidelity, so long as her innocence is not proven by means of the bitter water. **And if** her husband **dies** childless before she drinks the bitter water, **she performs *ḥalitza*** with her late husband's brother **and may not enter into levirate marriage,** as, if she had been unfaithful, levirate marriage is forbidden.

The *halakha* as *Chazal* develop it thus requires three distinct elements before a woman can acquire *sotah* status. First, the husband must formally warn her — *kinuy* — in the presence of witnesses, specifying a particular man with whom she is not to seclude herself: *al tidberi im ish peloni*, do not speak with so-and-so. Second, she must subsequently seclude herself with that man — *stirah* — in a private location. Third, that seclusion must be of sufficient duration: *kedei tumah*, the amount of time it would take to engage in sexual intercourse.

It bears noting that the *kinuy* need not be about private seclusion specifically. A husband's jealousy might lead him to warn his wife even against speaking with a particular man, well beyond any concern about actual seclusion. That level of warning is sufficient. The *mishna* makes explicit, however, that merely violating the warning by speaking with the man does not confer *sotah* status and does not affect her permissibility to her husband or — if she is married to a *kohen* — her eligibility to eat *terumah*. What is required is the actual private seclusion of sufficient duration. The word *tidberi* in the warning is clearly not a euphemism for sexual relations; the *mishna* underscores this by noting that speech alone leaves her fully permitted.

## R. Eliezer vs R. Yeh (II)

### Seclusion Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מְקַנֵּא לָהּ עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם וְכוּ׳. עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא בְּקִינּוּי וּסְתִירָה, אֲבָל בְּטוּמְאָה — עֵד אֶחָד מְהֵימַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara now clarifies the dispute in the mishna. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The husband must **issue a warning to her based on,** i.e., in the presence of, **two** witnesses, and he gives the bitter water to her to drink based on the testimony of one witness. Rabbi Yehoshua says: He both issues a warning to her based on two witnesses and gives the bitter water to her to drink based on the testimony of two witnesses who saw them secluded together. The Gemara notes: Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua **disagree only with regard to** the requisite number of witnesses for the **warning and** the **seclusion,** whether one or two witnesses are required, **but with regard to** the testimony concerning **defilement** after the warning was issued and seclusion had occurred, they agree that even the testimony of **one witness is deemed credible** to establish that the woman actually engaged in sexual intercourse with the man while secluded.

The *mishna* presents the dispute between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* regarding the evidentiary requirements. On the question of *kinuy* — the warning — both agree: it must be delivered in the presence of two witnesses. The dispute is about *stirah*, the testimony of seclusion.

*Rabbi Eliezer* holds that *mekane lah al pi shenayim u'mashkah al pi ed echad o al pi atzmo* — the warning requires two witnesses, but to establish the seclusion and thereby compel her to drink, even one witness suffices, or even the husband's own testimony. This is a striking position: the husband himself — who cannot ordinarily serve as a witness in matters concerning his wife, let alone in a case where he is manifestly interested — is believed to testify to the *stirah*. He is not merely expressing suspicion; he is making a positive claim that he directly observed it. But it remains his testimony, which in ordinary jurisprudence would carry no weight whatsoever.

*Rabbi Yehoshua*, by contrast, maximizes objectivity: *mekane lah al pi shenayim u'mashkah al pi shenayim* — both the warning and the seclusion must be established by two witnesses, and the husband cannot be counted among them.

## Asur LeKanot

### First Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִכְּדֵי תַּנָּא מִנָּזִיר סָלֵיק, מַאי תְּנָא דְּקָא תָּנֵא סוֹטָה?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara questions the placement of this tractate within the mishnaic order of *Nashim*. **Now,** the ***tanna* arose from** tractate ***Nazir*,** which is the tractate preceding *Sota* in the order of the Mishna. **What** did he **teach** in *Nazir* **that** required that he **teach** tractate ***Sota*** immediately afterward, as at first glance there seems to be no connection between this tractate and *Nazir*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַבִּי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: לָמָּה נִסְמְכָה פָּרָשַׁת נָזִיר לְפָרָשַׁת סוֹטָה, לוֹמַר לָךְ: שֶׁכׇּל הָרוֹאֶה סוֹטָה בְּקִלְקוּלָהּ — יַזִּיר עַצְמוֹ מִן הַיַּיִן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This was done **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi with regard to the sequence of passages in the Torah, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Why is the portion of a nazirite** (Numbers, chapter 6) **placed adjacent to the portion of a *sota*** (Numbers, chapter 5)? This was done **to tell you that anyone who sees a *sota* in her disgrace** as she undergoes the rite of the bitter water **should renounce wine,** as wine is one of the causes of sexual transgression, as it loosens inhibitions. For the same reason that the Torah teaches these passages one after the other, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi arranged these tractates one after the other.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי סוֹטָה, וַהֲדַר לִיתְנֵי נָזִיר! אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא כְּתוּבּוֹת וּתְנָא "הַמַּדִּיר", תְּנָא נְדָרִים. וְאַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא נְדָרִים — תְּנָא נָזִיר, דְּדָמֵי לִנְדָרִים. וְקָתָנֵי סוֹטָה, כִּדְרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **let** him **teach** tractate ***Sota* first and then let** him **teach** tractate ***Nazir*,** which is the way these topics are ordered in the Torah, and also accords better with the statement of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. The Gemara answers: **Since** the *tanna* **taught** tractate ***Ketubot*, and** in that tractate he **taught** a chapter that begins: **One who vows,** in which there are several *mishnayot* concerning vows between husbands and wives, he then **taught** tractate ***Nedarim*,** whose subject is the *halakhot* of vows. **And since** he **taught** tractate ***Nedarim*,** he then **taught** tractate ***Nazir*, which is similar to** tractate ***Nedarim*** in that one becomes a nazirite by taking a vow. **And** he then **teaches** tractate ***Sota*, in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "הַמְקַנֵּא", דִּיעֲבַד — אִין, לְכַתְּחִילָּה — לָא. קָסָבַר תַּנָּא דִּידַן: אָסוּר לְקַנּאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara begins clarifying the mishna. The mishna states: **One who issues a warning** to his wife. By employing the descriptive phrase: One who issues a warning, and not the prescriptive phrase: One issues a warning, the *tanna* indicates that **after the fact, yes,** it is effective if he issues a warning in this manner, but ideally, **no,** one should not issue a warning to his wife at all ***ab initio*.** Apparently, **the *tanna* of our** mishna **holds** that it **is prohibited to issue a warning** to one's wife *ab initio* in a manner that can cause her to become a *sota*, and all the *halakhot* concerning a *sota* are for one who issued a warning when not obligated to do so.

Before proceeding to the *gemara*'s analysis of these positions, the *gemara* makes a striking opening move. The *mishna*'s use of *hamekane* rather than *mekane adam le'ishto* is read as a *di'eved* formulation — describing what happens when this procedure occurs, not prescribing that a man ought to undertake it. *Tosafos* raises the objection that the *ha-X* construction does not always signal *di'eved* — the phrase *haholech lishchot et hapesach*, for example, is not read as a *di'eved* — and there may well be a genuine grammatical or contextual argument embedded in the *gemara*'s reading here. But what follows makes the *gemara*'s inclination clear. *Kasavar tana didan asur lekanot* — the *tanna* of our *mishna* holds that it is *forbidden* to initiate this procedure. This is the first substantive claim one encounters upon opening *Masechet Sotah*: not a technical rule about witnesses, not a procedural requirement, but a moral-legal prohibition. The juxtaposition is powerful and deliberate.

This posture is entirely consistent with an ambivalence toward these institutions that runs through *Seder Nashim*. Just as *Masechet Nedarim* and *Masechet Nazir* presented *nedarim* and *nezirut* with persistent ambivalence — not simply as neutral legal categories but as practices to be approached with caution and even disapproval — so too *Sotah* opens by registering disapproval of the husband's act. The *Torah*'s own framing, recall, concluded with *ve'nikah ha'ish me'avon* — the husband is exonerated. The *gemara* will now say something quite different about that.

### Resh Lakish Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: כִּי הֲוָה פָּתַח רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ בְּסוֹטָה, אָמַר הָכִי: אֵין מְזַוְּוגִין לוֹ לְאָדָם אִשָּׁה אֶלָּא לְפִי מַעֲשָׂיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כִּי לֹא יָנוּחַ שֵׁבֶט הָרֶשַׁע עַל גּוֹרַל הַצַּדִּיקִים". אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְקָשִׁין לְזַוְּוגָן כִּקְרִיעַת יַם סוּף, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "אֱלֹהִים מוֹשִׁיב יְחִידִים בַּיְתָה מוֹצִיא אֲסִירִים בַּכּוֹשָׁרוֹת".
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak says: When Reish Lakish would introduce** his discussion of the Torah passage **of *sota*** he would **say this:** Heaven **matches a woman to a man only according to his actions, as it is stated: "For the rod of wickedness shall not rest upon the lot of the righteous"** (Psalms 125:3), indicating that if one has a wicked wife it is due to his own evil conduct. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And it is as difficult to match** a couple together **as** was **the splitting of the Red Sea, as it is stated** in a verse that speaks of the exodus from Egypt: **"God makes the solitary individuals dwell in a house; He brings out prisoners into prosperity [*bakosharot*]"** (Psalms 68:7). God takes single individuals and causes them to dwell in a house by properly matching a man to a woman. This is similar to the exodus from Egypt, which culminated in the splitting of the Red Sea, where He released prisoners into prosperity.

The *gemara* then cites a tradition about *Resh Lakish*: *amar Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak, ki hava patach Resh Lakish beSota* — when *Resh Lakish* would open his teaching on *Sotah*, he would begin with a *drasha*, the kind of *aggadic* opening one would give in the context of a public discourse. And indeed what he says is *aggadic* in register but pointed in implication.

### Matching by Deeds

*Resh Lakish* would say: *ein mezavgin lo le'adam isha ela lefi ma'asav* — a person is only matched with a wife in accordance with his deeds, as it says, *ki lo yanuach shevet ha'rasha al goral ha'tzaddikim* — the staff of the wicked shall not rest upon the lot of the righteous. The implication: if a righteous man would not be given a wicked portion, then a man who finds himself with an unfaithful wife must ask what it says about him. If your wife has reached the point of this procedure, what kind of person were you, what did you do, that you deserved a wife capable of this?

This is a direct counterweight to *ve'nikah ha'ish me'avon*. The *Torah* said the husband is clear of sin. *Resh Lakish* is saying: think very carefully before you conclude that. Your wife's behavior reflects on you. And if that is the theological context, then *asur lekanot* gains additional force: going ahead with this procedure is not only halachically disfavored — it is a public announcement about what kind of person you are. There is also a practical dimension: the waters only work against the wife if the husband himself has been faithful. So this is not merely a theological observation; it has direct ritual consequences.

## Bashert Debate

### Difficulty of Match

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם קוֹדֶם יְצִירַת הַוָּלָד בַּת קוֹל יוֹצֵאת וְאוֹמֶרֶת: בַּת פְּלוֹנִי לִפְלוֹנִי, בֵּית פְּלוֹנִי לִפְלוֹנִי, שְׂדֵה פְלוֹנִי לִפְלוֹנִי! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּזוּג רִאשׁוֹן, הָא — בְּזוּג שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so** that a man is matched to a woman according to his actions? **But Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Forty days before an embryo is formed a Divine Voice issues forth and says: The daughter of so-and-so is** destined **to** marry **so-and-so; such and such a house is** destined **to** be inhabited by **so-and-so; such and such a field is** destined **to** be farmed by **so-and-so.** This clearly states that these matters, including marriage, are decreed for a person even before he is formed. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** statement that Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav is **with regard to a first match [*zivug*],** while **this** statement of Rabba bar bar Ḥana in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan is **with regard to a second match.** A first match is decreed in heaven; a second match is according to one's actions.

The *gemara* then cites *Rabbah bar Bar Hana* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *kashah mezavgam ki'kriat Yam Suf* — matching two people [in marriage] is as difficult as the splitting of the sea, as it says, *Elokim moshiv yechidim baitah* — God causes isolated individuals to dwell in a home together. The *gemara* derives from the juxtaposition with *motzi asirim bakosharot* — bringing out those who are bound — that God's work in matching spouses is compared to the great miracle of *yetziat Mitzrayim*.

### Bat Kol Midrash

At this point the *gemara* challenges: *aini?* Is it really that difficult? But *Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Rav*: *arba'im yom kodem yetziras ha-vlad bat kol yotzei ve'omeret* — forty days before the formation of the fetus, a heavenly voice goes out and declares: the daughter of so-and-so is destined for so-and-so, the house of so-and-so for so-and-so, the field of so-and-so for so-and-so. The forty-day reference marks the moment of conception — the period before which the embryo is considered *maya be'alma*, mere fluid — meaning at the very instant of conception, the match is already declared. If that is so, then the match is not *kashah* at all; it is simply pre-determined.

The difficulty this creates is serious. If the match is declared at conception, how can it be *lefi ma'asav* — determined by one's deeds — when those deeds have not yet occurred, and indeed the person has not yet been born? The principle of *bechirah chofshit*, free will, is fundamental: *hakol bidei shamayim chutz miyirat shamayim* — everything is in heaven's hands except the fear of heaven. Indeed, a parallel *midrash* that enumerates what is declared at conception — will the child be wise or foolish, rich or poor, righteous or wicked — is itself challenged by the *gemara* elsewhere: *tzadik* or *rasha* must be removed from the list, since righteousness is precisely the domain of free will. How, then, can we say the match is fated?

It is worth pausing over what this *midrash* reflects about reality even beyond its theological dimensions. So much about a person's life is already set before birth: one's genetic constitution, family of origin, social position, the education one will receive, the community one will inhabit. All of this can be imagined as the content of that *bat kol* at conception. That the *midrash* includes one's spouse in this list reflects how deeply pre-determined marriage was in the ancient world — and raises profound questions about how to hold that together with the notion that *Resh Lakish* has asserted, that the match reflects the husband's moral worth.

### First vs Second

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya, ha be'zivug rishon, ha be'zivug sheni* — there is no difficulty. The *bat kol* at conception, the idea of *bashert*, applies to the first marriage. *Lefi ma'asav* — matching by deeds — applies to the second marriage.

This resolution is not without its difficulties. *Tosafos* — specifically *Tosafos Shantz*, from the school of the *Rash mi'Shantz* — raises the question of perspective: if the matching is from the man's perspective, then a second marriage for the man might still be a first marriage for the woman, and vice versa, which complicates the picture considerably.

More significantly: if the first marriage is *bashert* and not *lefi ma'asav*, then the entire force of *Resh Lakish*'s *drasha* about *sotah* is undermined for the vast majority of cases. Since most men in that era were in first marriages — divorce was less common, and while death in childbirth did occur, second marriages were far from universal — *Resh Lakish* could hardly have intended his statement to apply only to those on second marriages. Clearly *Resh Lakish* meant *lefi ma'asav* as a general principle applicable to all marriages. The *gemara*'s reconciliation of the two *midrashim* is doing a specific technical job — resolving a contradiction between two sources — but it should not be read as undermining the moral-theological thrust of *Resh Lakish*'s opening teaching. When we speak colloquially of *bashert*, we typically mean a fated match that is also the *right* match. The *gemara* here seems to leave the first marriage as fated, not necessarily on account of the husband's merit, and reserves *lefi ma'asav* for the second. That is a rather unsettling picture of what *bashert* means.

## R. Eliezer vs R. Yeh (II)

### R. Eliezer View

Returning now to the *mishna*'s dispute: *Rabbi Eliezer* holds that the *kinuy* — the warning — requires two witnesses, but the *stirah* — the seclusion — can be established by even one witness, or by the husband's own testimony. His own testimony: *al pi atzmo*. This is indeed exceptional. The husband cannot ordinarily testify in matters involving his wife; he is a party with the clearest possible interest. Yet *Rabbi Eliezer* permits it here. He is not merely expressing suspicion — he is making a factual claim of direct observation — but by any standard of normal jurisprudence, his testimony would be inadmissible.

### R. Yehoshua View

*Rabbi Yehoshua* maintains that both *kinuy* and *stirah* require two witnesses, and the husband cannot be counted among those two. This is the most objective standard possible: every step in establishing *sotah* status must rest on independently verified evidence, and the husband's personal interest entirely disqualifies him.

## One Witness Authority

### Direct Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן נָמֵי: עֵד אֶחָד אוֹמֵר "אֲנִי רָאִיתִי שֶׁנִּיטְמְאָת" — לֹא הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And we learned also** in another mishna (31a) that if **a single witness says: I saw that she was defiled,** then **she would not drink** the bitter water, as the testimony is accepted, and her husband must divorce her and she forfeits payment of her marriage contract. Therefore, there is no need to perform the *sota* rite.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא מְנָלַן דִּמְהֵימַן עֵד אֶחָד? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: "וְעֵד אֵין בָּהּ", בִּשְׁנַיִם הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶחָד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "לֹא יָקוּם עֵד אֶחָד בְּאִישׁ". מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר "לֹא יָקוּם עֵד בְּאִישׁ", אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהוּא אֶחָד? מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "אֶחָד"? זֶה בָּנָה אָב: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר "עֵד" — הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁנַיִם, עַד שֶׁיִּפְרוֹט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב "אֶחָד".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **By Torah law, from where do we** derive **that one witness is deemed credible** with regard to testifying that a *sota* engaged in sexual intercourse? The Gemara answers: **As the Sages taught** in reference to the verse describing the circumstances in which a woman defiled through an act of adultery becomes forbidden to her husband, which states: "And a man lie with her carnally and it be hid from the eyes of her husband, she being defiled secretly, **and there is no witness [*ed*] against her"** (Numbers 5:13), **the verse is speaking of** a lack of **two** witnesses. When the verse refers to the lack of an *ed*, written in the singular, it actually indicates that there are not two witnesses against her, but only one, as the *baraita* will now explain. The *baraita* continues and asks: **Or** perhaps the verse is referring **only** to a case where there was not **even one** witness to the act of sexual intercourse, as the singular usage of the word *ed* would seem to indicate? The *baraita* now proves that elsewhere the word *ed* is used to indicate two witnesses, as **the verse states: "One witness [*ed*] shall not rise up against a man** for any iniquity or any sin that he sins; by the mouth of two witnesses or by the mouth of three witnesses shall a matter be established" (Deuteronomy 19:15).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר "לֹא יָקוּם עֵד בְּאִישׁ", אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהוּא אֶחָד? מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "אֶחָד"? זֶה בָּנָה אָב: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר "עֵד" — הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁנַיִם, עַד שֶׁיִּפְרוֹט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב "אֶחָד".
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* infers a general principle from this verse by asking: **By inference from that** which **is stated** in the verse, even with the omission of the word "one": **"A witness shall not rise up against a man"** (Deuteronomy 19:15), **do I not know that it is** referring to **one** witness, as the term "rise up [*yakum*]" is written in the singular form? **What** is the meaning when **the verse states** explicitly: **"One** witness," since it is obviously referring to only one witness? The *baraita* answers: **This established a paradigm** that **every place** where the word **"witness [*ed*]" is stated** in the Torah without specifying a number, **there are two** witnesses **here, unless the verse specifies** that it is referring to only one witness by writing the word **"one."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: תְּרֵי לֵית בַּהּ אֶלָּא חַד, "וְהִיא לֹא נִתְפָּשָׂה" — אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* returns to discuss the verse concerning a *sota*. **And the Merciful One states:** "There is no witness [*ed*] against her" (Numbers 5:13), which therefore means that **there are not two** witnesses to the sexual intercourse that could testify **with regard to her; rather,** there is only **one** witness. The *baraita* completes its interpretation: Further in the verse it states: **"And she was not taken,"** indicating that the verse is referring to a case in which it is known that the woman had not been raped. This knowledge is based on the testimony of only one witness, as the verse had already stated that there were not two witnesses, and since one witness saw her willingly engage in sexual intercourse with another man, **she is forbidden.**

The *gemara* now moves to a related but distinct question. The dispute between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* concerns the *stirah* — the testimony of seclusion. But what about *tumah* directly — that is, what if a witness testifies not merely that the woman secluded herself with the man but that the witness directly observed adultery? The *gemara* states that in matters of *tumah* — direct testimony of the act itself — *ed echad meheman*, one witness is believed. This is stated as common ground, not as part of the *Rabbi Eliezer*-*Rabbi Yehoshua* dispute. Importantly, as *Rashi* makes explicit — *betuma shel stira achar kinuy* — this applies only when the background of *kinuy* and *stirah* has already been established. A single witness appearing out of nowhere to accuse a woman of adultery, without that foundation, is not taken seriously; indeed, the *gemara* elsewhere notes that such a claim risks the territory of *motzi shem ra*. The single witness's testimony is only credible when it builds upon the circumstantial foundation of warning and seclusion.

*Tosafos* raises a sharp question here: why assume that one witness is sufficient even in the case of direct testimony? Given the severity of the consequence — the woman would be divorced and lose her *ketubah* — perhaps the *gemara* should have demanded two witnesses. Indeed, the *mishna* itself does not make this explicit; it is the *gemara* that introduces the assumption. *Tosafos* concludes that we proceed on this assumption because a *baraita* is about to confirm it, and the *gemara* is using the *mishna* as a lead-in to that *baraita*. But logically, given the stakes, one could have argued for a higher standard.

### Linguistic Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דִּכְתִיב "לֹא יָקוּם עֵד אֶחָד בְּאִישׁ", הָא לָאו הָכִי הֲוָה אָמֵינָא "עֵד" דְּסוֹטָה חַד הוּא. וְאִי אֲפִילּוּ חַד לֵיכָּא, אֶלָּא בְּמַאי מִיתַּסְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this reasoning: **But** this would seem to indicate that **the** only **reason** to interpret the verse concerning a *sota* as referring to a case where there is only one witness is **that it is written** in the other verse: **"One witness shall not rise up against a man"** (Deuteronomy 19:15), indicating that any unspecified usage of the word *ed* in the Torah refers to two witnesses, **but** were it **not** for **this** inference, **I would say** that when the term **witness** is employed in the verse **concerning a *sota*** it **is** referring to **one** witness. However, this would mean that the woman is forbidden to her husband even if there is not even one witness who saw the alleged sexual intercourse, **and if there is not even one** witness to testify, **then with what** testimony **does she become forbidden** to her husband? Obviously, even without another verse, it must be understood that the verse is indicating that there are not two witnesses but there is one, or else there would be no testimony to her actions.

The *gemara* then asks *mid'oraita minalan de'meheman ed echad* — what is the *Torah*-level source for believing a single witness in this context? The derivation proceeds from two *pesukim*. The *Torah* says in *parshat sotah*, *ve'ed ein bah ve'hi lo nitpasah* — there is no witness against her and she was not taken by force. And the *Torah* elsewhere says, *lo yakum ed echad be'ish* — one witness shall not rise against a man. Now the *gemara* constructs the following: *mimashma shene'emar lo yakum ed be'ish*, one could have simply written *ed* — a witness — shall not rise against a man; we would have understood this to mean even a single witness. Why then does the *Torah* add the word *echad* — one? This superfluous word teaches a universal principle: *kol makom shene'emar ed harei kan shenayim* — wherever the *Torah* uses the word *ed* without qualification, it means two witnesses. The word *ed* on its own always implies two; *echad* is what explicitly signals that only one is meant. This is a foundational hermeneutical principle in the *gemara*'s reading of testimony passages throughout *Shas*.

### Single Witness Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: "עֵד אֵין בָּהּ" — אֵין נֶאֱמָן בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** to infer the interpretation of the verse concerning the *sota* from the other verse mentioning one witness. Otherwise it might **enter your mind to say** that the verse here that states: **"There is no witness against her,"** means that a single witness testifying about the sexual intercourse **is not deemed credible with regard to her** under any circumstances, and the testimony of one witness is not accepted in the case of a *sota*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נֶאֱמָן בָּהּ?! וְאֶלָּא מַאי בָּעֵי —
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this analysis: What would be the logic in interpreting the verse as indicating that a single witness **is not deemed credible with regard to her? But** if that is the interpretation, **what** does the verse **require** in order for a *sota* to be rendered forbidden?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד דְּאִיכָּא תְּרֵי? לִישְׁתּוֹק קְרָא מִינֵּיהּ. דְּאָתְיָא "דָּבָר" "דָּבָר" מִמָּמוֹן. וַאֲנָא יָדַעְנָא, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַכׇּל עֵדֻיוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains its question: If the verse is understood as indicating that a woman isn't forbidden **until there are two witnesses** to testify to her infidelity, then **let the verse be silent from** any mention of witnesses, **as** the requirement for two witnesses in matters of sexual impropriety **is derived** by means of a verbal analogy **from** the word **"matter"** written with regard to forbidden relations, and the word **"matter"** written with regard to **monetary matters.** The verbal analogy by which it is learned that two witnesses are required is as follows: A verse concerning forbidden relations states: "Because he has found some unseemly matter about her" (Deuteronomy 24:1), and a verse concerning monetary matters states: "By the mouth of two witnesses or by the mouth of three witnesses shall a matter be established" (Deuteronomy 19:15). **And I** would **know** that the requirement that two witnesses testify applies in the case of a *sota*, **just as it does in all** other matters of **testimony in the Torah.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: סוֹטָה שָׁאנֵי, דְּרַגְלַיִם לְדָבָר, שֶׁהֲרֵי קִינֵּא לָהּ וְנִסְתְּרָה — לִיתְהֵימַן בַּהּ עֵד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** that the verse be stated in the case of a *sota* as well, for it might **enter your mind to say** that testimony concerning **a *sota* is different** from other testimony, and even testimony of one witness would be sufficient **because** there is **a basis for** anticipating **the matter. Since** the husband **issued a warning to her** about this particular man **and she** then **secluded** herself with him, perhaps even **one witness should be deemed credible with regard to her.** Therefore, the verse informs us that one witness is not deemed credible to render her forbidden to her husband.

Armed with this principle, the *gemara* now returns to *ve'ed ein bah*. Since *ed* by itself means two witnesses, the phrase *ve'ed ein bah* — parsed as *ve'ed* alone, without *echad* — means: there are not *two* witnesses against her. There is, however, one. *Ve'hi lo nitpasah* — and she was not taken by force — adds that the act, if it occurred, was consensual. From this combination — one witness testifying to the act, no claim of coercion — the *gemara* derives that the woman is *asura*, forbidden to continue living with her husband. The single witness is believed to the degree that she is divorced without a *ketubah* and does not undergo the ritual of drinking the waters; that ritual is reserved for cases of genuine unresolved doubt, not for cases where a witness has established what occurred.

This is a striking read of the *pesukim*. The straightforward *pshat* reading of *ve'ed ein bah ve'hi lo nitpasah ve'hevi ha'ish et ishto* is: because there is no witness, because she was not caught, the husband must bring her to the *Beit Hamikdash* for the ritual. That is the simple sequence: absence of witnesses → proceed to ritual. The *gemara* reads it instead as: there are not two witnesses, but there is one, and the woman has not been raped, therefore she is forbidden and does not undergo the ritual at all. Not only is the *gemara* deriving the presence of a single witness from a phrase that literally says "there is no witness," but it is also reading the *pasuk* as describing a case where the ritual does *not* take place — the exact opposite of what the *pshat* of the sequence suggests.

### Raglayim L'Davar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ דְּאֵין נֶאֱמָן בָּהּ וְשַׁרְיָא? וְהָא מִדִּכְתִיב "וְהִיא לֹא נִתְפָּשָׂה" — מִכְּלָל דַּאֲסוּרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question concerning its earlier analysis: **But how can you** think to **say** that the verse would be stating that one witness **is not deemed credible with regard to her** having engaged in sexual intercourse **and she** would remain **permitted** to her husband? **But from** the continuation of the same verse, from the fact **that it is written: "And she was not taken,"** which indicates that the verse is referring to a case where she was not raped, one concludes **by inference** that the verse is referring to a woman **who** becomes **forbidden** to her husband for engaging in consensual adulterous sexual intercourse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: אֵין נֶאֱמָן בָּהּ — עַד דְּאִיכָּא תְּרֵי, וּבִתְרֵי נָמֵי "הִיא דְּלֹא נִתְפָּשָׂה", קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, it still **was necessary** to teach the principle derived from the other verse that the term "*ed*" is referring to two witnesses even in the context of a *sota*, as it might **enter your mind to say** that the verse should be understood to mean that one witness **is not deemed credible with regard to her,** and she remains permitted **until there are two** witnesses who testify to the sexual intercourse, **and with** the testimony of **two witnesses as well, it is** only **when she was not seized** and forced to cohabit with the man. Therefore, to refute this possible interpretation, the *baraita* **teaches us** that "*ed*" always refers to two witnesses unless stated otherwise. Therefore, the phrase in the verse concerning a *sota* that says: "There is no witness [*ed*] against her," means that there were not two witnesses, but if there was only one witness he is deemed credible.

The *gemara* then works through what might have been thought — *salka daitach amina* — had this derivation not existed. One might have thought that in the case of *sotah*, a single witness ought to be believed even without a specific *Torah* source, and therefore the *Torah* would have needed to tell us the *opposite*: that a single witness is *not* sufficient, that two are required. Why would one naturally have expected a single witness to suffice here? *Sotah shani* — *sotah* is different. *De'raglayim la-davar* — because there is circumstantial evidence. The combination of the formal *kinuy* — the husband's warning in the presence of witnesses — and the *stirah* — her subsequent entry into private seclusion with the very man against whom she was warned — creates a backdrop of substantial circumstantial grounds for suspicion. With that foundation already in place, one might naturally have thought that a single witness testifying to the act would be believed, precisely because his testimony is not standing alone but is corroborating what the circumstantial evidence already suggests. In that case, we would have needed the *Torah* to explicitly tell us that one witness is *not* enough and two are required.

### Hypothetical Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ דְּאֵין נֶאֱמָן בָּהּ וְשַׁרְיָא? וְהָא מִדִּכְתִיב "וְהִיא לֹא נִתְפָּשָׂה" — מִכְּלָל דַּאֲסוּרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question concerning its earlier analysis: **But how can you** think to **say** that the verse would be stating that one witness **is not deemed credible with regard to her** having engaged in sexual intercourse **and she** would remain **permitted** to her husband? **But from** the continuation of the same verse, from the fact **that it is written: "And she was not taken,"** which indicates that the verse is referring to a case where she was not raped, one concludes **by inference** that the verse is referring to a woman **who** becomes **forbidden** to her husband for engaging in consensual adulterous sexual intercourse.

The *gemara* dismisses this hypothetical reading. How could one have thought that reading the *pasuk* to mean "one witness is not believed, two are required" and she is then forbidden only if there are two witnesses — how could one have read *ve'hi lo nitpasah*, that she was not taken by force, as an *additional* condition for guilt only when there are two witnesses? The phrase *miklal de'asura* — the logical implication of the *pasuk* is that she is forbidden — shows that the *pasuk* cannot be read as describing a scenario of doubt or permissibility. *Ve'hi lo nitpasah* is an aggravating factor, establishing that what occurred was consensual. It does not introduce a new evidentiary threshold.

Therefore, *kamashma lan*: read the *pasuk* as the *gemara* reads it. *Ve'ed ein bah* means there are not two witnesses but one. *Ve'hi lo nitpasah* confirms that the act, if it occurred, was consensual. Together they establish that one witness of adultery — following *kinuy* and *stirah* — is sufficient to treat her as forbidden: she is divorced, she loses her *ketubah*, and she does not drink the waters.

The *gemara*'s treatment of this first *daf* thus establishes two fundamental orienting principles for the entire *masechet*. First, *asur lekanot*: the *tanna* views the husband's initiating this procedure with disfavor, and the opening *aggadeta* of *Resh Lakish* — *ein mezavgin lo le'adam isha ela lefi ma'asav* — gives that disfavor theological grounding. Second, the entire structure of *sotah* halakha, including the exceptional credibility granted to a single witness, is justified by *raglayim la-davar*: the accumulating circumstantial evidence that the formal structure of *kinuy* and *stirah* creates. Without that foundation, nothing proceeds. With it, the *halakha* can extend credibility in ways it would never do in ordinary jurisprudence.
