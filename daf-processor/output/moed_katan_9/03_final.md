# Moed Katan 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Marriage Prohibitions

### Forbidden Activities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין נוֹשְׂאִין נָשִׁים בַּמּוֹעֵד, לֹא בְּתוּלוֹת וְלֹא אַלְמָנוֹת, וְלֹא מְיַיבְּמִין — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשִּׂמְחָה הִיא לוֹ. אֲבָל מַחְזִיר הוּא אֶת גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not marry a woman on** the intermediate days of **a Festival, not virgins and not widows, and one may not perform levirate marriage** with his sister-in-law, if his brother died childless, **because it is** a **joyous** occasion **for him. However, one may remarry his divorced wife** on the intermediate days of a Festival, as this is not as great a joy for him.

The *mishnah* on daf 9 addresses a range of forbidden activities on *Chol HaMoed*, organized around several distinct categories. Beyond things that are labor-intensive — which the *gemara* labels *tircha* — and things related to *avelut*, such as the reburial of bones and becoming overwrought during *hespeidim*, the *mishnah* turns to activities that are prohibited because of their relationship to *simcha*. The paradigmatic case in this category is getting married.

### Four Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְכִי שִׂמְחָה הִיא לוֹ, מַאי הָוֵי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מְעָרְבִין שִׂמְחָה בְּשִׂמְחָה. רַבָּה בַּר [רַב] הוּנָא אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ שִׂמְחַת הָרֶגֶל וְעוֹסֵק בְּשִׂמְחַת אִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It was taught in the mishna that one may not marry a woman on the intermediate days of a Festival, because it is a source of joy for him. The Gemara asks: **And if it is a** source of **joy for him, what of it?** Isn't there is a mitzva to rejoice on a Festival? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said, and, so too, Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rabbi Oshaya said, and some say** that **Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rabbi Ḥanina said:** The reason that one may not get married on the intermediate days of a Festival is **because one may not mix** one **joy with** another **joy,** as each requires its own celebration. **Rabba bar Rav Huna said:** The reason is **because he forsakes the rejoicing of the pilgrimage Festival and occupies** himself **with rejoicing** with **his wife.**

The *gemara* offers three, or if one prefers four, distinct reasons for the prohibition on marriage during *Chol HaMoed*. The most widely known is *ein me'arvin simcha b'simcha* — one does not mix one *simcha* with another. A somewhat narrower formulation of this principle is *v'samachta b'chagecha v'lo b'ishtecha* — you shall rejoice on your festival, but not by means of your wife. This second formulation is actually narrower than the first: it may apply specifically to the conflict between marriage and *regel*, such that if either component were absent, the prohibition might not apply. Moreover, the *gemara* frames *v'samachta b'chagecha v'lo b'ishtecha* specifically as a concern about abandoning *simchas haregel* in favor of the *simcha* of marriage, whereas *ein me'arvin simcha b'simcha* captures a broader idea — not that one *simcha* is privileged over the other, but that one cannot give each *simcha* the full, undivided attention it deserves on its own terms. In addition to these two reasons, the *gemara* offers two others that seem less directly expressed in the plain meaning of the *mishnah*: the sheer effort a wedding requires, and the concern that people will postpone the wedding beyond *Chol HaMoed*.

### Tosafot's Exceptions

The practical significance of the distinction between these two formulations becomes apparent in Tosafot's discussion of a *seudat bris milah* or *seudat pidyon haben* on *Chol HaMoed*. Tosafot notes that such a feast does not pose the same problem, and offers several explanations. In the case of *bris milah*, since the *bris* itself cannot be postponed, one cannot reasonably be expected to postpone the *seudah* either. *Pidyon haben* is less clear-cut. One of Tosafot's answers is that perhaps the broader formulation of *ein me'arvin simcha b'simcha* is not the operative ruling, and only the narrower *v'samachta b'chagecha v'lo b'ishtecha* applies — which would not necessarily reach a *seudat pidyon haben*. Tosafot's other answer is that not every festive occasion rises to the level of *simcha* that triggers the concern; only *simcha* of a sufficiently high degree is implicated. In any case, it is worth noting that these two reasons are not identical, even though we tend to reach for the more generic formulation.

## Temple Dedication

### Shlomo's Dedication

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְאֵין מְעָרְבִין שִׂמְחָה בְּשִׂמְחָה מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעַשׂ שְׁלֹמֹה בָעֵת הַהִיא אֶת הֶחָג וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל עִמּוֹ קָהָל גָּדוֹל מִלְּבוֹא חֲמָת עַד נַחַל מִצְרַיִם [לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ] שִׁבְעַת יָמִים וְשִׁבְעַת יָמִים אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: With regard to the principle that **one may not mix** one **joy with** another **joy, from where do we** derive it? The Gemara explains that the source is **as it is written** with regard to the dedication of the Temple: **"So Solomon held the feast at that time, and all Israel with him, a great congregation, from the entrance of Hamath to the Brook of Egypt, before the Lord our God, seven days and seven days, fourteen days"** (I Kings 8:65).

The *gemara* now turns to the derivation of *ein me'arvin simcha b'simcha* from the event that everyone tends to associate with it. The verse states: *vaya'as Shlomo ba'eis ha'hee es hachag v'chol Yisrael imo kahal gadol mibo Chamas ad nachal Mitzrayim lifnei Hashem Elokeinu shivas yamim v'shivas yamim arba'ah asar yom* — Shlomo made at that time the festival, and all of Israel with him, a great congregation, from the entrance of Hamath to the river of Egypt, before Hashem our God, seven days and seven days, fourteen days. The *gemara* understands this to mean seven days for *chanukas habayis* and seven days for *Sukkot*.

### Gemara's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא דִּמְעָרְבִין שִׂמְחָה בְּשִׂמְחָה — אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמִינְטַר עַד הֶחָג, וּמִיעְבַּד שִׁבְעָה לְהָכָא וּלְהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is so** that **one may** in fact **mix** one **joy with** another **joy, he should have waited until the festival** of *Sukkot*, which was the second set of seven days, **and made** a feast of **seven** days **for this and for that,** i.e., for the dedication of the Temple and for the festival of *Sukkot* together.

From this the *gemara* raises the obvious question: if there were no objection to mixing *simcha b'simcha*, why was it necessary to make fourteen days of commitment rather than simply combining the two? Just schedule the *Sukkot* celebration to serve simultaneously as the seven days of *chanukas habayis*, and it would all be done at once. This question is what drives the derivation.

### Explanations Examined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא מִינְטָר לָא נָטְרִינַן, וְהֵיכָא דְּאִתְרְמִי — עָבְדִינַן! אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְשַׁיּוֹרֵי פּוּרְתָּא. שַׁיּוֹרֵי בִּנְיַן בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ לָא מְשַׁיְּירִינַן. אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְשַׁיּוֹרֵי בְּאַמָּה כָּלְיָא עוֹרֵב. אַמָּה כָּלְיָא עוֹרֵב צוֹרֶךְ בִּנְיַן הַבַּיִת הוּא. אֶלָּא מִדִּמְיַיתַּר קְרָא, מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב ״אַרְבְּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם״, ״שִׁבְעַת יָמִים וְשִׁבְעַת יָמִים״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הָנֵי לְחוֹד וְהָנֵי לְחוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question: **Perhaps,** however, it may be derived from here only that **we may not delay** a wedding to be on a Festival, just as King Solomon did not delay the Temple dedication to be on the Festival, but nevertheless, **where it happens** to occur that way, **we may** indeed **prepare** a feast to celebrate both occasions together. The Gemara answers: If this were permitted, Solomon **should have left a small** part of the Temple unfinished until the Festival, and thereby arranged for a joint celebration of the dedication of the Temple and the festival of *Sukkot*. The Gemara responds: **One may not leave** any part of **the building of the Temple** undone, as a mitzva should be completed as quickly as possible. The Gemara modifies its previous opinion: Solomon **should have left the cubit-**wide plates with spikes, which were designed to **eliminate the ravens,** unfinished. The roof of the Temple was fitted with sharp metal spikes to deter the ravens, who were attracted by the smell of the sacrificial meat, from perching there. Although this was not considered a part of the building itself, delaying its installation would have allowed Solomon to delay the celebration of the Temple dedication. The Gemara rejects this opinion as well: **The cubit**-wide plates with spikes to **eliminate the ravens was a necessary** element **in the building of the Temple,** and consequently Solomon could not delay its construction either. **Rather,** the proof is **from the redundancy** in **the verse. Since it is written "fourteen days," why do I** need the verse to specify **"seven days and seven days"? Learn from it** that **these** seven days of celebrating the Temple dedication must **be discrete, and** similarly, **these** seven days of celebrating the Festival must **be discrete,** due to the principle that one may not mix one joy with another.

The *gemara* immediately resists the inference. Perhaps, it suggests, *v'dilma minteira lo natrinan v'heicha d'isreimyan avdinan* — it just so happened that they finished the construction a week before *Sukkot*. One would not proactively try to make the dedication coincide with *Sukkot*, but if it naturally fell out that way, perhaps there would be no objection. So the most one can derive from what occurred is that one should not actively seek to combine the two — not that one is required to keep them apart.

To this the *gemara* responds: *iba'i lei l'shiruya pursa* — why not deliberately leave a small section unfinished, as one does for a *siyum*, and time the completion to coincide with *Sukkot*? If combining them were truly permissible, they could easily have arranged it. The *gemara* then blocks this line: *shiurei binyan Beit HaMikdash lo mash'arinan* — one may not deliberately leave over any portion of the construction of the *Beit HaMikdash* incomplete. When it is finished, it is finished, and then the *simcha* follows. Therefore, the fact that they did not contrive to have them coincide proves nothing, since they finished early and could not artificially delay the completion.

The *gemara* presses further: *iba'i lei l'shiurei d'ama kalya orev* — they could have left over the *ama kalya orev*. This was a row of spikes, one *ama* wide, installed along the roof of the *Beit HaMikdash* to prevent ravens from perching and swooping down to consume the *korbanot*. Since this element is supplementary rather than central to the structure itself, perhaps it would have been acceptable to delay its installation and thereby time the full completion to coincide with *Sukkot*. To this the *gemara* responds: *ama kalya orev tzarich binyan habayit* — the *ama kalya orev* is considered part of the *binyan* of the *Beit HaMikdash* and could not be deliberately deferred. The conclusion is that no proof can be drawn from the timing of events, since various constraints prevented any artificial arrangement.

### Proof from Language

The *gemara* therefore shifts its approach: *ella mideiyatar kra* — from the very language of the verse a proof can be drawn, regardless of the timing of events. The verse says fourteen days, yet it elaborates: *shivas yamim v'shivas yamim* — seven days and seven days. Why write it that way? Simply saying fourteen would have been sufficient. The doubling of the language — *shma mina hani l'chud v'hani l'chud* — demonstrates that each set of seven days was intentionally kept distinct from the other. The seven days of *chanukas habayis* were purposefully separated from the seven days of *Sukkot*. This establishes *ein me'arvin simcha b'simcha* as a broader principle, since this case has nothing to do with marriage — it involves only two distinct forms of communal *simcha*, and the principle applies equally to both.

## Yom Kippur Story

### Violation of Yom Kippur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי פַּרְנָךְ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹתָהּ שָׁנָה לֹא עָשׂוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים. וְהָיוּ דּוֹאֲגִים וְאוֹמְרִים: שֶׁמָּא נִתְחַיְּיבוּ שׂוֹנְאֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל כְּלָיָיה, יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לָהֶם: כּוּלְּכֶם מְזוּמָּנִים לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the discussion of the celebration at Solomon's dedication of the Temple, the Gemara relates that **Rabbi Parnakh said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: That year,** the Jewish people **did not observe Yom Kippur,** as the seven-day celebration of the dedication of the Temple coincided with Yom Kippur and all seven days were celebrated with feasting. **And** the people **were worried and said: Perhaps the enemies of the Jewish people,** a euphemism for the Jewish people themselves, **have become liable to be destroyed** for the transgression of eating on Yom Kippur, which is punishable by *karet*. **A Divine Voice issued forth and said to them: All of you are designated for life in the World-to-Come.**

With this broader principle established, the *gemara* introduces a striking passage. *Amar Parnach amar Rabbi Yochanan*: that year — the year of *chanukas habayis* — the Jewish people did not observe *Yom Kippurim*. Seven days before *Sukkot* overlaps with *Yom Kippur*, and since those were days of feasting in celebration of the dedication, the people ate on *Yom Kippur*.

The *gemara* then describes the people's reaction: *v'hayu do'agim v'omrim* — they were anxious and troubled, fearing that *shema nitchayvu soneihem shel Yisrael kilaya* — perhaps the enemies of Israel, a well-known euphemism for the Jewish people themselves, are deserving of total destruction for having violated *Yom Kippur*. This is a striking detail. They did not hesitate in the moment — the king was leading, everyone around them was doing it, there was a kind of collective momentum — yet this did not eliminate the profound sense of guilt and dread that followed.

### Kal V'Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי דְּרוּשׁ? אָמְרוּ, קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה מִשְׁכָּן, שֶׁאֵין קְדוּשָּׁתוֹ קְדוּשַּׁת עוֹלָם — וְקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד דּוֹחֶה שַׁבָּת דְּאִיסּוּר סְקִילָה, מִקְדָּשׁ, דִּקְדוּשָּׁתוֹ קְדוּשַּׁת עוֹלָם, וְקׇרְבַּן צִבּוּר, וְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים דְּעָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What derivation** led them to conclude that it was permitted for them to eat on Yom Kippur? The Gemara explains that they based their ruling on an ***a fortiori*** inference: **If** at the dedication of **the Tabernacle, whose sanctity is not a permanent sanctity, an individual's offering,** i.e., an offering of one of the tribal princes, **overrides** the prohibition of **Shabbat,** as the princes' offerings were brought every day including Shabbat despite the attendant transgression of **a prohibition** that is punishable by **stoning;** then with regard to the dedication of **the Temple, whose sanctity is a permanent sanctity** and the offerings brought there were **communal offerings,** is it **not all the more so** clear that the dedication of the Temple overrides the prohibition of **Yom Kippur,** a violation that is **punishable by** the less severe punishment of ***karet*?**

The *gemara* then asks: *mai darash* — what reasoning allowed *Shlomo*, or the people, to conclude that eating on *Yom Kippur* was permissible in the context of *chanukas habayis*? The answer is a *kal va-chomer*. When the *mishkan* was dedicated — even though its sanctity was not eternal, since it would eventually be disassembled and superseded by the *Beit HaMikdash* — a private *korban* brought by each *nasi* overrode *Shabbos*, which carries the penalty of *sekila*. If so, all the more so in the case of the *Mikdash*, whose sanctity is eternal and weightier, the *korbanot* being brought were *korbanot tzibbur* on behalf of the entire community rather than private offerings of individuals, and the prohibition being overridden — *Yom Kippur* — is punishable only by *kares* rather than *sekila*. All three components favor the *Mikdash* case: the cause is weightier, the *korban* is weightier, and the prohibition being displaced is easier to displace.

The *gemara* then asks: if the *kal va-chomer* is so compelling, why were they worried at all? The *gemara* answers: *hasam tzorech gavo'ah hacha tzorech hedyot* — there, the *korban* brought for the dedication of the *mishkan* was for God's benefit, whereas here, they were eating for themselves. That distinction undermined the force of the argument.

### Simcha Requires Food

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין שִׂמְחָה בְּלֹא אֲכִילָה וּשְׁתִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **There is no** complete **rejoicing without eating and drinking.**

The *gemara* then responds with a fundamental theological principle: *ein simcha belo achila u'shtiya* — there is no *simcha* without eating and drinking. This is not merely the incidental enjoyment of food on the side. The eating itself was constitutive of the *simcha* of the dedication. A *seudas mitzvah* is not simply a meal that happens to accompany a *mitzvah*; participation in the *seudah* is part of what creates the *simcha*, and that is precisely why it is a *mitzvah*. Without eating and drinking, there would be no proper *chanukas habayis* at all. The dedication required *simcha*, and *simcha* requires eating. Therefore, eating was not an indulgence alongside the ceremony — it was an integral component of what the ceremony had to be.

This connects to a broader principle familiar from *Yom Tov*: normally we say *ein simcha ela b'basar v'yayin*, and more specifically *ein simcha ela b'basar shelamim*, meaning that the eating of the *korban shelamim* is itself the *simcha*. Here too, it was not sufficient to bring the *korbanot* and then refrain from eating them; the eating was what transformed the bringing into a genuine act of rejoicing.

Nevertheless, even with this reasoning firmly in mind, the people remained deeply worried. When you are actually eating delicious food, one can say as much as one likes that it is all a *mitzvah* and entirely for the sake of *simcha*, and it truly is — yet there is an inevitable sense that some personal enjoyment is also present, and the question of whether this was truly permissible on *Yom Kippur* weighed heavily on them.

A further point the *gemara* addresses is how one establishes that they actually ate on *Yom Kippur* — that is, that the fourteen days were consecutive and without interruption. The verse in the *parasha* of the *nesi'im*' dedication of the *mishkan* records: *b'yom ashti asar yom* — the phrase uses the word "day" twice. Just as a single day is continuous and unbroken, so too the eleven days were entirely consecutive, leaving no room for skipping *Shabbos*. When the *gemara* raises the possibility that perhaps *yamim ha-re'uyim* — only halachically eligible days — were being counted, it notes that another verse says *b'yom shneim asar yom*, repeating the doubling formulation for the twelfth day as well. If both the eleventh and twelfth days require this emphasis on continuity, the implication is that there were no interruptions whatsoever, even for *Shabbos*. From there, by a *gezeira shava* of *yom yom*, the same inference is extended to the dedication of the *Mikdash*: the fourteen days were entirely unbroken, which means *Yom Kippur* was included.

### Heavenly Voice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לָהֶם: כּוּלְּכֶם מְזוּמָּנִים לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **A Divine Voice issued forth and said to them: All of you are designated for life in the World-to-Come.**

Following the establishment of all this, the *gemara* records the reassurance that came from Heaven. *Yatzta bas kol v'amrah lahem: kulchem mezumanim le-chayei ha-olam haba* — a heavenly voice came forth and declared: you are all designated for the world to come.

## David's Forgiveness

### Verse Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּאַחֵיל לְהוּ? דְּתָנֵי תַּחְלִיפָא: ״בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁמִינִי שִׁלַּח אֶת הָעָם וַיְבָרְכוּ אֶת הַמֶּלֶךְ וַיֵּלְכוּ לְאׇהֳלֵיהֶם שְׂמֵחִים וְטוֹבֵי לֵב עַל כׇּל הַטּוֹבָה אֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה ה׳ לְדָוִד עַבְדּוֹ וּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל עַמּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that** God **pardoned them** for this sin? The Sage **Taḥlifa taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states: **"On the eighth day he sent the people away, and they blessed the king, and went to their tents joyful and glad of heart for all the goodness that the Lord had done for David His servant and for Israel His people"** (I Kings 8:66).

The *gemara* proceeds to interpret the verse that records the conclusion of the dedication: *vayivarchu es ha-melech vayelchu le-oholeihem smeichim v'tuvei lev al kol ha-tova asher asa Hashem l'David avdo u-l'Yisrael ammo* — they blessed the king and went to their tents, joyful and of good heart, for all the good that God had done for David His servant and for Israel His people. The *gemara* unpacks each phrase in turn.

### Stuck Gates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְאׇהֳלֵיהֶם״ — שֶׁהָלְכוּ וּמָצְאוּ נָשֵׁיהֶם בְּטָהֳרָה, ״שְׂמֵחִים״ — שֶׁנֶּהֱנוּ מִזִּיו הַשְּׁכִינָה, ״וְטוֹבֵי לֵב״ — שֶׁכׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד נִתְעַבְּרָה אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּבֵן זָכָר, ״עַל כׇּל הַטּוֹבָה״ — שֶׁיָּצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לָהֶם: כּוּלְּכֶם מְזוּמָּנִים לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. ״לְדָוִד עַבְדּוֹ וּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל עַמּוֹ״. בִּשְׁלָמָא ״לְיִשְׂרָאֵל עַמּוֹ״ — דְּאַחֵיל לְהוּ עֲוֹן יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, אֶלָּא ״לְדָוִד עַבְדּוֹ״ מַאי הִיא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁבִּיקֵּשׁ שְׁלֹמֹה לְהַכְנִיס אָרוֹן לַמִּקְדָּשׁ דָּבְקוּ שְׁעָרִים זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** This verse may be expounded as follows: The words **"to their tents"** indicate **that they went and found their wives in** a state of **purity,** as the terms tent and house often denote one's wife. The term **"joyful"** is referring to the fact **they had enjoyed the splendor of the Divine Presence,** as there was a revelation of the Divine Presence when the offerings were sacrificed in the Temple. The phrase **"and glad of heart"** refers to the fact that **each of their wives conceived a male child.** The words **"for all the goodness"** indicate **that a Divine Voice issued forth and said to them: All of you are designated for life in the World-to-Come,** which is the ultimate good. The aforementioned verse stated: "For all the goodness that the Lord had done **for David His servant and for Israel His people.**" The Gemara asks: **Granted,** when the verse mentions the goodness that God did **for Israel His people,** this is referring to the fact **that He pardoned them the sin of** eating on **Yom Kippur** that year; **but what is** the goodness that God performed **for David His servant? Rav Yehuda said that Rav said: When Solomon sought to bring the Ark into the Temple the gates clung together** and could not be opened.

*Le-oholeihem* — to their tents — is read as referring to their homes and their wives, echoing the idiom of *shuvu lachem le-oholeichem* from *Har Sinai*: they found their wives *tehoros*, ritually permitted, after having been away for fourteen days. *Smeichim* — joyful — because they had basked in the *ziv shechinah*, the divine radiance. *V'tuvei lev* — of good heart — because every man's wife became pregnant with a male child. *Al kol ha-tova* — for all the goodness — this refers to the heavenly voice's declaration that they were all designated for *Olam HaBa*.

That accounts for the good done to *Yisrael ammo*. But the *gemara* then asks: *l'David avdo mai hi* — what was the good that God did for David His servant? *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: when *Shlomo* attempted to bring the *Aron* into the sanctuary, *davku she'arim zeh ba-zeh* — the gates became stuck to one another, blocking entry.

### Shlomo's Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁלֹמֹה עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע רְנָנוֹת וְלֹא נַעֲנָה, פָּתַח וְאָמַר ״שְׂאוּ שְׁעָרִים רָאשֵׁיכֶם וְגוֹ׳״ וְלֹא נַעֲנָה, כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָמַר ״ה׳ אֱלֹהִים אַל תָּשֵׁב פְּנֵי מְשִׁיחֶךָ זׇכְרָה לְחַסְדֵי דָּוִד עַבְדֶּךָ״ — מִיָּד נַעֲנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Solomon uttered twenty-four songs** of praise, **and** his prayer **was not answered. He began and said: "Lift up your heads, O you gates,** and be lifted up, you everlasting doors; and the King of glory shall come in" (Psalms 24:7), **but** once again his prayer **was not answered,** and the Temple gates remained closed. **Once he said:** "Now therefore arise, O Lord God, into your resting place, You, and the Ark of Your strength; Let your priests, O Lord God, be clothed with victory and let Your pious ones rejoice in goodness. **O Lord God, do not turn away the face of Your anointed; remember the faithful love of David Your servant"** (II Chronicles 6:41–42), **he was immediately answered.**

*Shlomo* responded by reciting twenty-four expressions of prayer — the various formulations of petition, supplication, and entreaty that appear throughout his dedicatory prayer in which he envisions people turning to God through the *Mikdash* with *rinah*, *tefillah*, *bakasha*, and so forth. Yet despite all of this, the gates remained closed and God did not respond.

*Patach v'amar* — he then opened with the verse from *Tehillim*: *se'u she'arim rasheichem v'hinas'u pischei olam v'yavo melech ha-kavod* — lift your heads, O gates, and be lifted, O eternal entrances, that the King of Glory may enter. Yet even this produced no result. Only when *Shlomo* turned to the prayer recorded in *Divrei HaYamim* — *Hashem Elokim al tashev penei meshichecha, zachra l'chasdei David avdecha* — remember the kindness of David Your servant — did everything change. The moment David was invoked, *miyad ne'ena* — the gates opened immediately.

### Enemies Shamed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה נֶהְפְּכוּ פְּנֵי שׂוֹנְאֵי דָוִד כְּשׁוּלֵי קְדֵירָה, וְיָדְעוּ הַכֹּל שֶׁמָּחַל לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עַל אוֹתוֹ עָוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **At that moment the faces of David's enemies turned** dark **like the** charred **bottom of a pot, and all knew that the Holy One, Blessed be He, forgave him for that sin** involving Bathsheba, as they saw that it was only in his merit that the gates of the Temple opened.

*Ba'otah sha'ah nehepchu penei sonei David k'shulei kederah* — at that moment, the faces of David's enemies turned as dark as the bottom of a cooking pot. They were shamed before all. *V'yadu hakol shemachal lo HaKadosh Baruch Hu al oto avon* — and everyone knew that God had forgiven David for that sin, the sin of *Bat Sheva* and *Uriah*, which had been hanging over him. Just as the people received atonement for eating on *Yom Kippur*, David received atonement for his own transgression — and both forms of forgiveness were publicly manifest in the same moment of divine favor.

This is a striking passage on multiple levels. First, *Chazal* envision that even in the era of *Shlomo*, long after David's death, David still had enemies who harbored resentment toward him. The fact that they were shamed — *davka* at this moment — underscores what was at stake. Second, the passage reflects a *Chazal*ic understanding that the sin of *Bat Sheva* and *Uriah* was not easily dissolved; it lingered, and full divine forgiveness was only publicly evident at this later juncture. This theme appears elsewhere in *Chazal* as well — there is a well-known *midrash* in which someone approaches David and asks what the punishment is for one who lies with a married woman, and David's answer resonates uncomfortably with his own history. One additional dimension worth noting: it was *Shlomo*, the son of *Bat Sheva*, who stood at the center of this moment of divine vindication for his father. That is a detail of considerable narrative power.

## Taking Leave Protocol

### Leave Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן בֶּן עַסְמַיי וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן גֵּרִים תָּנוּ פָּרָשַׁת נְדָרִים בֵּי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי, אִיפְּטוּר מִינֵּיהּ בְּאוּרְתָּא. לְצַפְרָא הֲדוּר וְקָא מִפַּטְרִי מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: וְלָאו אִיפַּטְרִיתוּ מִינַּי בְּאוּרְתָּא? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לִמַּדְתָּנוּ רַבֵּינוּ תַּלְמִיד שֶׁנִּפְטָר מֵרַבּוֹ וְלָן בְּאוֹתָהּ הָעִיר, צָרִיךְ לִיפָּטֵר מִמֶּנּוּ פַּעַם אַחֶרֶת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁמִינִי שִׁלַּח אֶת הָעָם וַיְבָרֲכוּ אֶת הַמֶּלֶךְ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וּבְיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה לַחֹדֶשׁ הַשְּׁבִיעִי שִׁלַּח אֶת הָעָם״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Yonatan ben Asmai and Rabbi Yehuda, son of converts, studied the portion** dealing with **oaths in the study hall of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai.** After completing their studies, the disciples **took leave of** their master **in the evening,** but did not yet leave the city. **In the morning they went back and took leave of him** a second time. **He said to them: Did you not** already **take leave of me** yesterday **in the evening? They said to him: You have taught us, our teacher,** that **a disciple who takes leave of his teacher and** then **stays overnight in the same city must take leave of him an additional time, as it is stated** at the conclusion of the dedication of the Temple: **"On the eighth day he sent the people away, and they blessed the king"** (I Kings 8:66), **and** elsewhere **it is written: "And on the twenty-third day of the seventh month he sent the people away"** (II Chronicles 7:10). The eighth day in the verse is referring to the Eighth Day of Assembly, the twenty-second of the month of Tishrei, yet it says that he sent the people away on the next day, the twenty-third of the month.

The *gemara* now introduces what may appear to be an aside, though its connection to the surrounding material will become clear. *Rabbi Yonatan ben Asmai v'Rabbi Yehuda ben Gerim* had been studying *parshas nedarim* — the tractate *Nedarim* and its *tannaitic* material — in the *yeshiva* of *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*. Having spent time with him, they took leave of him at night — *iftur minei be'orta* — intending to depart the next morning. The following morning, however, they returned to his house and took formal leave of him again.

*Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* expressed puzzlement: he had already said goodbye to them the night before. They replied: *limadtanu rabbeinu* — our master has taught us — that a student who takes leave of his teacher but then sleeps that night in the same city, rather than immediately beginning his journey, must take leave again the following morning when he actually sets out. *Tzarich lipatar mimenu pa'am acheret*.

### Shlomo Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִכָּאן לְתַלְמִיד הַנִּפְטָר מֵרַבּוֹ וְלָן בְּאוֹתָהּ הָעִיר, צָרִיךְ לִיפָּטֵר מִמֶּנּוּ פַּעַם אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it can be derived **from here** that **a disciple who takes leave of his teacher and** then **stays overnight in the same city must take leave of him an additional time,** just as the Jewish people took leave of Solomon an additional time on the day after the Festival, on the twenty-third of Tishrei.

The source for this rule is derived from *Shlomo*. The verse says that on the eighth day — which, counting from the fifteenth of *Tishrei*, is the twenty-second — he sent the people home and they blessed the king. Yet another verse records that on the twenty-third of *Tishrei* he sent the people away. How can both verses be correct? The resolution is that on the twenty-second he took formal leave of them, but they remained in the city that night, and on the twenty-third, when they actually began traveling, he sent them off again. This establishes the rule: *talmid sheniftar me-rabbo velan be-oto ha-ir tzarich lipatar mimenu pa'am acheret*.

### Blessing Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ: בְּנֵי אָדָם הַלָּלוּ אֲנָשִׁים שֶׁל צוּרָה הֵם, זִיל גַּבֵּיהוֹן דְּלִיבָרְכוּךְ. אֲזַל, אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ דְּקָא רָמוּ קְרָאֵי אַהֲדָדֵי. כְּתִיב: ״פַּלֵּס מַעְגַּל רַגְלֶךָ וְכׇל דְּרָכֶיךָ יִכּוֹנוּ״, וּכְתִיב: ״אוֹרַח חַיִּים פֶּן תְּפַלֵּס״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai **said to his son: These** two **people,** Rabbi Yonatan ben Asmai and Rabbi Yehuda, son of converts, **are men of** noble **form [*tzura*]** i.e., wise and learned individuals; **go to them so that they will bless you. He went** and **found them** deep in discussion, **raising** apparent **contradictions between verses** as follows: **It is written: "Make level the path of your feet, and let all your ways be established"** (Proverbs 4:26), indicating that when one has the opportunity to perform more than one mitzva, he must evaluate which of them is most important. **And** elsewhere **it is written: "Lest you level out the path of life,"** (Proverbs 5:6), indicating that one must perform each mitzva as the opportunity arises, without considering its relative importance.

*Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* then said to his son: *beni, benei adam hallalu anashim shel tzura hen* — these are people of stature, both learned and possessed of proper *derech eretz*. He instructed his son to go to them before they left town and receive a *beracha* from them.

The son went and found the two scholars engaged in resolving contradictions between verses. One pair of verses concerned weighing one's steps: one verse says *palles ma'agal raglecha v'chol derachecha yikonu* — carefully weigh your steps and all your paths will be established, while another says *orach chayyim pen tepalles* — the path of life, lest you over-deliberate. The resolution: when a *mitzvah* can be performed by others, one ought to weigh whether this is the most pressing obligation; when a *mitzvah* can only be performed by oneself, one does not deliberate — one seizes the first *mitzvah* that presents itself. The *gemara* here gestures toward broader questions of *osek bemitzvah patur min hamitzvah* and *ein ma'avirin al hamitzvot*, though it does not fully elaborate the halachic implications.

A second pair of verses concerned *talmud Torah* versus the performance of *mitzvot*: one verse implies *mitzvot* take precedence over *Torah* study, another implies the opposite. The resolution follows the same framework: if others can perform the *mitzvah*, one continues learning; if the *mitzvah* can only be performed by oneself, one interrupts. Tosafot notes in this connection that *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* is used elsewhere as the paradigm of constant engagement in *Torah* — and even he would interrupt for certain obligations such as making a *sukkah* or taking *lulav* — yet Tosafot also cites the *Yerushalmi* to the effect that *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* did not interrupt his learning for *tefillah* and *krias shema*, the reasoning being *zeh shinuno ve-zeh shinuno* — both are forms of *Torah* study. It is notable that this very discussion, so closely associated with *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*, is taking place with his son standing in the room.

The son introduced himself: *de'amar li abba zil legabeihu delivarchuch* — my father sent me to receive your blessing. The two scholars responded: *yehei ra'ava* — and then proceeded to deliver what sounded entirely like a series of curses.

## Riddle-Blessings

### First Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרוּ לֵיהּ: יְהֵא רַעֲוָא דְּתִזְרַע וְלָא תֶּחְצַד, תְּעַיֵּיל וְלָא תַּיפֵּוק, תַּיפֵּוק וְלָא תְּעַיֵּיל, לִיחְרוֹב בֵּיתָךְ וְלִיתּוֹב אוּשְׁפִּיזָךְ, לִבַּלְבַּל פָּתוּרָךְ, וְלָא תִּחְזֵי שַׁתָּא חַדְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him** as follows: **May it be** God's **will that you should sow and not reap, that you should bring in and not take out, that you should take out and not bring in, that your house should be destroyed and your lodging place should be inhabited, that your table should become confused, and that you should not see a new year.**

The scholars said: may it be God's will that *detizra v'lo tachtzod* — you plant and do not harvest; *te'ayeil v'lo tapik* — you bring in and do not take out; *tapik v'lo te'ayeil* — you take out and do not bring in; *lichariv beisecha* — let your house be destroyed; *velisiv ushpizach* — and let your lodging be settled; *levavle pasurach* — let your table be overturned; *v'lo techzei shata chadta* — and may you not see the new year. The son returned to his father shaken: not only did they not bless him, *aval tzu'arei tzu'arun* — they caused him anguish with what seemed like a string of curses.

### Blessings Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנָךְ כּוּלְּהוּ בִּרְכָתָא נִינְהוּ. תִּזְרַע וְלָא תֶּחְצַד: תּוֹלִיד בָּנִים וְלָא יְמוּתוּ. תְּעַיֵּיל וְלָא תַּיפֵּוק: תְּעַיֵּיל כַּלְּתָא וְלָא לֵימוּתוּ בְּנָךְ דְּלִיפְּקוּן. תַּיפֵּוק וְלָא תְּעַיֵּיל: תּוֹלִיד בְּנָתָא וְלָא יְמוּתוּ גּוּבְרַיְיהוּ וְלִיהְדְּרוּ לְוָתָיךְ. לִיחְרוֹב בֵּיתָךְ וְלִיתּוֹב אוּשְׁפִּיזָךְ — דְּהַאי עָלְמָא אוּשְׁפִּיזָךְ, וְהָהֻיא עָלְמָא בֵּיתָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״קִרְבָּם בָּתֵּימוֹ לְעוֹלָם״. אַל תִּקְרֵי ״קִרְבָּם״, אֶלָּא ״קִבְרָם״. לִבַּלְבַּל פָּתוּרָךְ: בִּבְנֵי וּבְנָתָא. וְלָא תִּיחְזֵי שַׁתָּא חַדְתָּא: דְּלָא תְּמוּת אִתְּתָךְ וְלָא תִּנְסַב אִינְתְּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai said to** his son: **These are all blessings,** uttered in a cryptic manner, and this is what they meant: When they said that **you should sow and not reap** they meant that **you should bear sons and they should not die.** Their statement that **you should bring in and not take out** means that **you should bring in brides** for your sons **and your sons should not die, which** would cause their wives **to leave.** When they said **you should take out and not bring in** they meant that **you should have daughters and their husbands should not die,** which would cause your daughters to **return to you. When they said that your house should be destroyed and your lodging place should be inhabited,** this should be interpreted allegorically. **As this world is** compared to **your lodging place, and the World-to-Come is** compared to **your house, as it is written: "Their inward thought [*kirbam*], is that their houses shall continue forever"** (Psalms 49:12), and the Sages said: **Do not read** it as **"their inward thought [*kirbam*]"; rather** read it as **their graves [*kivram*].** According to this reading, one's grave is considered his permanent house. The Sages gave Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai's son a blessing that he should dwell in his temporary home rather than his permanent home, i.e., he should live a long life. When they said that **your table should become confused,** they meant that you should be blessed **with** many **sons and daughters,** so that there will be noise and confusion at your table. When they said that **you should not see a new year,** they meant **your wife should not die and** as a result **you should not** have to **marry another woman,** about which it says: "When a man has taken a new wife, he shall not go out to war, neither shall he be charged with any business; he shall be free at home for one year" (Deuteronomy 24:5).

*Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* asked to hear what they had said, and upon hearing each phrase, he explained: *hanei kulhu birchat ninhu* — these are all blessings, each one concealing a hidden positive meaning.

*Tizra v'lo tachtzod* — plant and do not harvest: *tolid banin v'lo yamutu* — may you have children and may they not die; you will sow but never be forced to cut them down.

*Te'ayeil v'lo tapik* — bring in and do not take out: *teol kaltha* — may you bring a daughter-in-law into your house, *v'lo limutu banach delipku* — and may your sons not die such that she would have to leave.

*Tapik v'lo te'ayeil* — take out and not bring in: *tolid banata* — may you have daughters who go out of your house to marry, *v'lo yamutu gavrayhu velihaderu levatach* — and may their husbands not die such that they would have to return to you.

*Lichariv beisecha velisiv ushpizach* — let your house be destroyed and your lodging be settled: *dehai alma ushpiza* — because this world is merely a lodging, a temporary residence. What is your more permanent dwelling? In a physical sense, the grave. As the verse says *kirbam betemo leolam* — read not *kirbam* but *kivram*, their graves are their eternal house. So may your eternal house — your grave — be destroyed through *techiyat hameitim*, and may your temporary lodging — this world — be settled, because you will be resurrected to live in it again. The more fundamental concept of *Olam HaBa*, after all, is the resurrection of the dead and life in a renewed physical world.

*Levavle pasurach* — let your table be overturned: *libtav petorach bivnei uvivnata* — may your table be full of commotion with sons and daughters.

*V'lo techzei shata chadta* — may you not see the new year: *dela tamut intach v'lo tinsav intata achriti* — may your wife not die such that you would have to take another, for the Torah says *shana achat* — for one year a man rejoices with his wife. The new year in this blessing is a honeymoon year, and the blessing is that one should never need to begin again.

### Chalafta's Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן חֲלַפְתָּא אִפְּטַר מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַב, אֲמַר לֵיהּ אֲבוּהּ: זִיל לְגַבֵּיהּ דְּלִיבָרְכָךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יְהֵא רַעֲוָא דְּלָא תְּבַיֵּישׁ וְלָא תִּתְבַּיַּישׁ. אֲתָא גַּבֵּי אֲבוּהּ. אָמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי אֲמַר לָךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִילִּין בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּאָמַר לִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בָּרְכָךְ בִּרְכְּתָא דְּבֵרְכָן קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל וּתְנָא בַּהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲכַלְתֶּם אָכוֹל וְשָׂבוֹעַ וְהִלַּלְתֶּם וְגוֹ׳ וְלֹא יֵבוֹשׁוּ עַמִּי לְעוֹלָם. וִידַעְתֶּם כִּי בְקֶרֶב יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲנִי וְגוֹ׳ וְלֹא יֵבוֹשׁוּ עַמִּי לְעוֹלָם״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos a blessing with an obscure meaning, the Gemara relates: **Rabbi Shimon ben Ḥalafta took leave of Rav. His father said to him: Go to him so that he should bless you.** When **Rabbi Shimon ben Ḥalafta** returned to him for a blessing, Rav said to him: **May it be** God's **will that you should not shame** others **and that you should not feel ashamed. He came** home **to his father,** who **said to him: What did he say to you? He said to him: Mere words he said to me,** i.e., he did not say anything of significance. After Rabbi Shimon ben Ḥalafta repeated what Rav had said, his father **said to him: He blessed you with the blessing with which the Holy One, Blessed be He, blessed Israel and** then **repeated** the blessing, indicating that is a very great blessing, **as it is written: "And you shall eat in plenty, and be satisfied, and praise** the Name of the Lord your God, who has dealt wondrously with you; **and My people shall never be ashamed. And you shall know that I am in the midst of Israel,** and that I am the Lord your God, and there is none else; **and My people shall never be ashamed"** (Joel 2:26–27).

A parallel story follows. *Rav Shimon bar Chalafta* took leave of Rav, and Rav sent his son to receive a blessing. Rav Shimon bar Chalafta's blessing was brief and direct: *yehei ra'ava dela tevayesh v'lo tityayesh* — may it be God's will that you not embarrass others and that you not be embarrassed. The son returned to his father somewhat deflated — this seemed like ordinary advice, not a real blessing. His father responded: *birchach birchata d'mivarech Kudsha Brich Hu l'Yisrael* — he blessed you with the very blessing that God gives the Jewish people. As the verse says: *v'lo yevoshu ami l'olam* — My people shall not be shamed forever. The double formulation — do not embarrass and do not be embarrassed — captures both dimensions of that divine promise. One should not trivialize such a blessing merely because it does not sound cryptic or elaborate.

## Women's Cosmetics

### Permitted Makeup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹשָׂה אִשָּׁה תַּכְשִׁיטֶיהָ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, אֵלּוּ הֵן תַּכְשִׁיטֵי נָשִׁים: כּוֹחֶלֶת וּפוֹקֶסֶת וּמַעֲבִירָה סְרָק עַל פָּנֶיהָ. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי מַעֲבֶרֶת סְרָק עַל פָּנֶיהָ שֶׁל מַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna: **And a woman may engage in** all of **her** usual **cosmetic treatments** to enhance her physical appearance **on** the intermediate days of **a Festival.** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **These are the cosmetic treatments of women** that are permitted: **She may paint her eyelids, she may remove** unwanted **hair [*pokeset*], and she may put rouge on her face. And some say: She may pass a comb over her lower face,** i.e., she may remove the hair from her pubic area.

The *gemara* turns to another category of permitted activity on *Chol HaMoed*: *v'osa isha tachshiteha* — a woman may perform her cosmetic preparations. One might have thought this involves problematic effort. The *baraita* specifies the permitted practices: *kochelet* — applying *kachal*, a form of eye shadow; *pokeset* — which Rabbeinu Gershom explains as making a part in the hair or a form of combing; and *ma'avira srak al paneha* — applying a type of rouge to the face. Some say this last practice also includes a form of cosmetic application to parts of the body below the face. Comparable practices involving the hair and skin are discussed further below.

### Age Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא מִקַּשְּׁטָא בְּאַנְפֵּי כַּלְּתַהּ. יָתֵיב רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא יַלְדָּה, אֲבָל זְקֵנָה — לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Ḥisda's wife would adorn herself** on the intermediate days of a Festival **in the presence of her daughter-in-law,** i.e., when she already had a married son. **Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana sat before Rav Ḥisda, and he sat and said: They taught only** that a woman is permitted to engage in cosmetic treatments on the intermediate days of a Festival **only** with regard to **a young woman,** as such treatments bring her joy, **but** in the case of **an old woman, no,** the treatments are not permitted, as she does not need them.

The *gemara* then records a dispute. The wife of Rav Chisda would apply makeup *b'anpei kaltah* — in the presence of her daughter-in-law — indicating, as Rabbeinu Gershom notes with some wry precision, that she was old enough to have a daughter-in-law. *Rav Huna bar Chinana* sat before Rav Chisda and stated: *lo shanu ela yalda* — the permission for makeup on *Chol HaMoed* applies only to young women; an elderly woman, *aval zkeina lo* — it does not apply.

### Chisda's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָאֱלֹהִים! אֲפִילּוּ אִמָּךָ וַאֲפִילּוּ אִימָּא דְאִימָּךְ, וַאֲפִילּוּ עוֹמֶדֶת עַל קִבְרָהּ. דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: בַּת שִׁיתִּין כְּבַת שֵׁית, לְקָל טַבְלָא רָהֲטָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: By God! Even your mother, and even your mother's mother, and even a woman** so old **that** she **is standing at** the edge of **her grave** are all permitted to adorn themselves. **As people say** in the popular adage: **A woman of sixty years, like one of six, runs at the sound of the timbrel [*tavla*],** implying that women of all ages are young in spirit; since they all take pleasure in their adornments, they are allowed to adorn themselves, regardless of age.

Rav Chisda responded with some force: *ha'Elokim*, by God — *afilu imcha v'afilu ima d'imcha* — even your mother and even your grandmother may apply makeup on *Chol HaMoed*. *V'afilu omedet al kivra* — even if she were standing at the very edge of the grave.

### Aging Principle

The basis for this ruling is the folk saying: *bat shitin k'bat sheis* — a sixty-year-old is like a six-year-old, *u'l'kal tavla rahata* — she will run at the sound of a festive bell. The point is that people do not simply relinquish their zest for life, their care for appearance, or their desire to participate in joy, simply because they have grown old. It is not for others to determine at what age a person's sense of engagement with the world ceases to matter.

One further detail worth noting: the fact that this practice required explicit permission reveals something about the nature of prohibited labor on *Chol HaMoed*. The obvious response to Rav Huna bar Chinana's objection would simply have been: what is the problem with makeup at all? The fact that instead a *heter* is invoked — it is important to the person, therefore we permit it — implies that there is something initially problematic about it. This points toward the ongoing question of what exactly constitutes *melechet Chol HaMoed*: whether it resembles the thirty-nine *melachot* of *Shabbos* (in which case makeup, understood as *tzoveia*, might be problematic) or whether it is purely about backbreaking labor. That question will continue to animate the *gemara*'s discussion.

## Plaster Debate

### Yehuda's Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא תָּסוּד. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִשָּׁה לֹא תָּסוּד מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּיוּוּל הוּא לָהּ. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּסִיד שֶׁיְּכוֹלָה לְקַפְּלוֹ בַּמּוֹעֵד, שֶׁטּוֹפַלְתּוֹ בַּמּוֹעֵד. שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמְּצֵירָה הִיא עַכְשָׁיו — שְׂמֵחָה הִיא לְאַחַר זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says: She may not apply lime** to her skin **because it renders her** temporarily **unattractive** and she will be distressed during the Festival. **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says: A woman may not apply lime** to her skin on the intermediate days of a Festival **because it** is temporarily **a disgrace to her,** as she appears unattractive until it is peeled off. **But Rabbi Yehuda concedes that with regard to lime that she can peel off during the Festival, she may apply it on** the intermediate days of **the Festival, as even though she is distressed now,** as the lime renders her appearance unattractive, **she will be happy afterward,** when the lime is removed and she sees the results.

*Rabbi Yehuda* prohibits a woman from applying plaster to her skin on *Chol HaMoed* — the kind of treatment in which a heavy paste is packed onto the face or skin, as one would with mud from the Dead Sea — on the grounds that *nivul hu lah* — it is disfiguring. During *Chol HaMoed* it is inappropriate to appear in this condition, even if the ultimate result is beneficial.

### Plaster Exception

However, *Rabbi Yehuda* concedes — and *Rabbi Yosi* agrees — that if the plaster is of a type that can be removed during *Chol HaMoed* itself, it may be applied. *She-af al pi she-meitzar hi achshav* — even though it causes discomfort or disfigurement in the immediate term, *simcha hi le'achar zman* — it produces joy thereafter. Since the benefit is realized within *Chol HaMoed*, the present discomfort is acceptable. If, however, the benefit would only be enjoyed after *Yom Tov*, the activity is prohibited.

### Micro vs. Macro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַאי סְבָרָא? וְהָתְנַן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נִפְרָעִין מֵהֶן מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמֵּצֵר. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמֵּצֵר עַכְשָׁיו — שָׂמֵחַ הוּא לְאַחַר זְמַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But does Rabbi Yehuda accept this reasoning** that it is permitted, despite the fact that it causes distress, because it will cause joy later? **Didn't we learn** elsewhere in a mishna (*Avoda Zara* 2a): On the three days before the holidays of idolaters, it is prohibited to do business with them, so as not to cause them to thank their idols for their success. Similarly, it is prohibited on these days to lend to them, to borrow from them, to repay a debt to them, or even to collect a debt from them. **Rabbi Yehuda says: One may collect** a debt **from them** during this period, **due to** the fact **that** the gentile **is distressed** when he makes the payment. The Rabbis **said to him: Even though he is distressed now, he will be happy afterward,** as he is relieved of the debt, and therefore, it is prohibited to collect a debt from him during the three days before his holiday. In this mishna, Rabbi Yehuda considers only the immediate distress and does not consider the joy that will be experienced after some time.

This ruling appears to create a tension. Elsewhere, when discussing whether one may collect a debt from a non-Jew during the non-Jew's festival — where the concern is causing them distress on their holiday — *Rabbi Yehuda* rules that one may indeed collect, reasoning that *meitzar hu lo*, the debtor is distressed at the moment of payment. The *Chachamim* respond that *af al pi she-meitzar achshav aval same'ach hu le'achar zman* — even though he is distressed now, he is glad later. This exchange reveals a clear structural disagreement: *Rabbi Yehuda* evaluates the micro-moment, while the *Chachamim* evaluate the macro-picture. How, then, can *Rabbi Yehuda* permit plaster on *Chol HaMoed* on the grounds that it produces joy later? That reasoning is precisely the macro-perspective he normally rejects.

### Nachman's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הַנַּח לְהִלְכוֹת מוֹעֵד, דְּכוּלְּהוּ מֵצֵר עַכְשָׁיו וְשָׂמֵחַ לְאַחַר זְמַן נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Leave** aside **the *halakhot* governing a Festival,** which cannot be compared to other areas, as **all** of the labors that are permitted on a Festival are permitted because even though **one is distressed** by them **now, he will be happy** about them **afterward.** For example, cooking and baking are permitted even though preparing food involves effort and trouble, because eating the food after it is prepared provides pleasure and enjoyment.

*Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak*: do not apply the logic of other halachic disputes to the laws of *Chol HaMoed*, because *d'kulo meitzar achshav v'same'ach le'achar zman ninhu* — all permitted activities on *Chol HaMoed* involve present effort or discomfort in exchange for delayed joy. Cooking and baking involve effort now; the pleasure comes later. Even the basic activities of *Yom Tov* preparation demand exertion in the moment, and the *simcha* follows. *Chol HaMoed* is structurally built on the model of present investment for future rejoicing. Therefore, *Rabbi Yehuda* is not contradicting his usual perspective — in the context of *Chol HaMoed*, the macro-view is the only coherent framework available, and even *Rabbi Yehuda* operates within it.

Ravina offers an alternative resolution: the debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Chachamim* concerning non-Jews' debt repayment is not really about micro versus macro. *Rabbi Yehuda* also looks at the larger picture; he simply disagrees about what the larger picture reveals. In the case of collecting a debt, *Rabbi Yehuda* maintains that even from a macro-perspective, the debtor is likely always resentful — perhaps he hoped the debt would be forgotten or forgiven — and therefore the larger picture does not produce *simcha* either. The disagreement is about factual assessment, not about interpretive methodology.

## Hair Removal Practices

### Methods by Class

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהִגִּיעוּ לְפִירְקָן וְלֹא הִגִּיעוּ לְשָׁנִים, עֲנִיּוֹת טוֹפְלוֹת אוֹתָן בְּסִיד, עֲשִׁירוֹת טוֹפְלוֹת אוֹתָן בְּסוֹלֶת, בְּנוֹת מְלָכִים בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמּוֹר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמּוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to applying lime to one's body, **Rav Yehuda said: The daughters of Israel who reached** physical **maturity, but had not** yet **reached the age** of majority, i.e., girls that were less than twelve years of age, and therefore embarrassed by their precocious physical development, would seek to remove the excess hair from their bodies. **The poor** among them would **smear their** skin **with lime** in order to remove the hair. **The wealthy ones** would **smear themselves with fine flour** for this purpose, **and the daughters of kings would smear themselves with *shemen hamor*. As it is stated** with regard to the women who were brought to King Ahasuerus: **"Six months with *shemen hamor*"** (Esther 2:12).

*Rav Yehuda* now explains the practical context for these plaster applications. Young girls who have reached physical maturity — *higiu l'pirkan* — but are not yet of age find the appearance of pubic hair distressing and wish to remove it. The method used varied by economic means. *Aniyot sachot otan b'sid* — the poor used plaster. *Ashirot sachot otan b'solet* — the wealthy used a paste of fine flour. *Benot melachim b'shemen ha-mor* — and daughters of royalty used oil of myrrh, as we see from the verse describing the preparations in the time of *Achashverosh*: *shisha chodashim b'shemen ha-mor*.

### Olive Oil Effects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״שֶׁמֶן הַמּוֹר״? רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא אָמַר: סְטָכַת, רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַמֵּי אָמַר: שֶׁמֶן זַיִת שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא שְׁלִישׁ. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַנְפִּיקִינוּן — שֶׁמֶן זַיִת שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא שְׁלִישׁ. וְלָמָּה סָכִין אוֹתוֹ? שֶׁמַּשִּׁיר אֶת הַשֵּׂעָר וּמְעַדֵּן אֶת הַבָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is *shemen hamor*? Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya said:** This is the perfume called ***setaket*. Rav Yirmeya bar Ami said:** It is **olive oil** extracted **from** an olive **that** has **not** yet **reached one-third** of its growth; the acidic oil is effective as a depilatory. **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Wherever the Mishna mentions ***anpikinon*,** it is referring to **olive oil** from an olive **that has not yet reached one-third** of its growth. **And why do** women **spread it** on the body? It is due to the fact **that it removes the hair and pampers the skin.**

The *gemara* asks: *mai shemen ha-mor* — what exactly is this *shemen ha-mor*? *Rav Huna bar Chiya* says it is *stachet*, some form of aromatic or myrrh-based oil. *Rav Yirmiya bar Ami* says it is olive oil from olives that had not yet ripened a third of the way — *shemen zayit she-lo hevi shalish* — which possessed a particular pungency or quality effective for this purpose. A *tanna* taught before *Rav Yehuda*: this *anpikinon* is indeed olive oil from olives that have not ripened a third. Its effects are twofold: *she-mashir es ha-se'ar* — it causes unwanted hair to fall out — *u'madenes es ha-basar* — and it makes the skin supple and smooth.

### Rav Bibi Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב בִּיבִי הֲוָה לֵיהּ בְּרַתָּא, טַפְלַהּ אֵבֶר אֵבֶר. שְׁקַל בַּהּ אַרְבַּע מֵאָה זוּזֵי. הֲוָה הָהוּא גּוֹי בְּשִׁבָבוּתֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ בְּרַתָּא. טַפְלַהּ בְּחַד זִמְנָא וּמִתָה, אֲמַר: קַטְלַהּ בִּיבִי לִבְרַתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** It was related that **Rav Beivai had a daughter,** and **he spread** lime **on her limb** by **limb,** which caused her hair to fall out and her skin to whiten. She became so beautiful that when marrying her off, **he took four hundred dinars for her. There was a certain gentile in his neighborhood who** also **had a daughter** and wanted to do this as well. **He spread her** entire body with lime all **at once, and she died.** That gentile **said: Beivai killed my daughter.**

Rav Bibi had a daughter, and he applied this olive oil to her *eivar eivar* — one limb at a time — carefully treating her skin in this fashion. The result was that when she came to be married, someone was willing to pay four hundred *zuz* for her hand. The *gemara* records this matter-of-factly, though the treatment of a daughter as a commodity whose market value is enhanced through cosmetic preparation is jarring.

A Samaritan neighbor heard of this and decided to attempt the same treatment with his own daughter. Unlike Rav Bibi, who applied the oil carefully, one limb at a time, the neighbor applied it to her entire body at once — *tafla b'chad zimna*. The concentrated application was too powerful; *umeisas* — she died. The neighbor blamed Rav Bibi: *katla Bibi l'varti* — Bibi killed my daughter. Of course, the neighbor had failed to observe that Rav Bibi's careful, gradual approach was precisely what made the treatment safe.

### Mead-Drinkers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: רַב בִּיבִי דְּשָׁתֵי שִׁיכְרָא — בָּעֲיָין בְּנָתֵיהּ טִפְלָא, אֲנַן דְּלָא שָׁתֵינַן שִׁיכְרָא — לָא בָּעֲיָין בְּנָתִין טִפְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: Rav Beivai, who** regularly **drinks beer, his daughters require** that they be **smeared** with lime, as beer causes hair growth, **but we who do not drink beer, our daughters do not require** that they be **smeared** with lime, as they have little hair even without this treatment.

*Amar Rav Nachman bar Rav Bibi* — Rav Bibi's own son explains the background: *d'shasi shichra ba'in bnosei tefela* — the daughters of those who drink mead require this hair-removal treatment, because, as Rabbeinu Gershom explains, drinking mead has the effect of making the skin coarser and producing excess hair growth. *Anan d'lo shasina shichra lo ba'in bnasin tefela* — we who do not drink mead do not need to subject our daughters to this procedure. The comment functions as a general cautionary note: do not attempt to replicate what Rav Bibi did, given what happened to the Samaritan's daughter when someone tried to imitate it without understanding the conditions that made it safe.
