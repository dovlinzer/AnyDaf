# Bava Kamma 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nafal Lifanav

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפַל לְפָנָיו כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב: ״לְפָנָיו״ – לְפָנָיו מַמָּשׁ, ״לְאַחֲרָיו״ – אַחֲרָיו מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: If a man was digging or widening a pit, and an ox passing by **fell forward** into it in fright due to the sound of the digging, he is liable. If it fell backward into the pit due to the sound of the digging, he is exempt. **Rav says:** The term **forward** means **literally forward,** and the term **backward** means **literally backward,**

The daf opens at the very last line of 52b with the term *nafal lifanav* — the animal fell forward. The *mishna* had established that if someone is digging in a pit and the noise startles a passing animal, causing it to fall forward into the pit, the owner of the pit is liable; if the animal fell backward, the owner is exempt. The question the *gemara* immediately confronts is: what precisely does "fell backward" mean? After all, we understand well enough what it means for an animal to fall face-first into the pit. The other direction requires elaboration.

### Rav's Hevel Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזֶה וָזֶה בְּבוֹר. רַב לְטַעְמֵיהּ – דְּאָמַר רַב: בּוֹר שֶׁחִיְּיבָה עָלָיו תּוֹרָה – לְהֶבְלוֹ, וְלֹא לַחֲבָטוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **and** both **this and that** refer **to** where it fell into **the pit** itself, but nevertheless if it fell backward, he is exempt. The Gemara notes: **Rav** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as Rav says:** Damage classified as **Pit for which the Torah obligates him** to pay is referring specifically **to** damage caused by the pit's lethal **fumes,** such as if an animal suffocates inside it, **but not to** damage caused by the **impact** of hitting the ground, for which he is exempt from paying compensation. Since the ox in this case fell backward on its back, the owner of the pit is exempt from paying compensation, as the ox wasn't killed by the lethal fumes of the pit, but by the impact of the fall.

Rav explains the *mishna* in the most literal sense: *lifanav lifanav mamash, l'acharav l'acharav mamash, v'zeh v'zeh b'bor* — "forward" means literally face-first into the pit, and "backward" means that the animal fell into the pit with its back facing downward, its mouth and face pointing upward, and it descended in that orientation into the well. At first glance this seems puzzling: either way the animal is in the pit — why would the direction it was facing matter for the owner's liability?

Rav's answer flows from his foundational position established a few days earlier: *bor shechiyva alav torah l'havalo v'lo l'chavato* — the category of *bor* in which the Torah imposes liability is based on the *hevel*, the stagnant air within the pit, and not on the impact with the ground. The reasoning, though initially counterintuitive, is that the ground itself is *karka olam* — the earth has always been there and the pit owner did not create it. What the digger did create is the cavity, the absence of ground, and therefore the air within it. Accordingly, the pit owner bears responsibility specifically for the danger posed by that air. If the animal falls in with its face pointing upward, the argument is that it is breathing the air outside the pit rather than the hazardous *hevel* within it, and therefore the pit owner is absolved. One might push back that even a face-up animal receives some of the pit's *hevel*, but that is how Rav accounts for the *mishna*'s distinction.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בְּבוֹר – בֵּין מִלְּפָנָיו בֵּין מִלְּאַחֲרָיו, חַיָּיב. שְׁמוּאֵל לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: לְהֶבְלוֹ – וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן לַחֲבָטוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says:** If the ox fell **into the pit, whether** it fell **forward** or **whether** it fell **backward,** he is **liable.** The Gemara notes: **Shmuel** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as** Shmuel **says:** With regard to damage classified as Pit, the Torah holds one liable for damage caused **by its** lethal **fumes, and all the more so for** damage resulting from the **impact** of the fall.

Shmuel disagrees: *b'bor bein milifanav bein mil'acharav chayav* — if the animal fell into the well, it makes no difference whether it fell face-first or face-up; the owner is liable in either case. Shmuel holds *l'havalo v'chol shekein l'chavato* — while the Torah did indeed obligate the pit owner on account of the *hevel*, it certainly also obligated him for the impact against the ground. The pit was not a hole before he dug it. Even though the ground was always there, the walls of the pit and the opportunity to strike the ground at depth are all the pit owner's creation. Therefore, even a face-up fall still results in liability.

### Resolving Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הֵיכִי דָּמֵי לְאַחֲרָיו מִקּוֹל הַכְּרִיָּיה – דְּפָטוּר? כְּגוֹן דְּנִתְקַל בַּבּוֹר, וְנָפַל לַאֲחוֹרֵי הַבּוֹר חוּץ לַבּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to Shmuel, **what are the circumstances** concerning which the mishna stated that if the ox fell **backward** into the pit **due to the sound of the digging that** one is **exempt?** The Gemara answers: This applies, **for example, where** the ox **stumbled on the pit, and** then **fell behind the pit** and was injured **outside the pit.**

For Shmuel, then, how does the *mishna*'s exemption for *l'acharav* make sense? The *gemara* explains: *kigon shenitkal b'bor v'nafal l'achorei habor chutz labor* — the case of the *mishna* where the owner is exempt is not one where the animal fell backward into the pit, but rather one where the animal tripped over the edge of the pit and then fell *outside* of it — behind the pit, not into it. *Lifanav* means it fell into the pit; *l'acharav* means it fell to the ground outside the pit.

One might ask: even if it fell outside, shouldn't the pit owner still be liable? After all, the pit was the hazard that caused the trip, and this is analogous to the *gemara*'s case of *avno sakino umaso* — if one places a stone in the *reshus harabbim* and someone trips and hits the ground, liability attaches even though the ground itself caused the injury, not the stone. Why should this be different?

The answer is that in the *mishna*'s case, the tripping was caused not by the pit itself but by the person digging — the noise of the digging startled the animal. The digger, not the pit, is the proximate cause of the stumble. Unless the owner of the *bor* can be identified as the party who caused the damage, he is exempt. The ground outside is public ground, and the tripping was the digger's doing. In summary: according to Rav, the pit owner is exempt when the animal falls in face-up because *hevel* never reached it; according to Shmuel, it never matters which direction the animal fell *into* the pit, but if it fell *outside* the pit due to the digger's noise, the pit owner is fully exempt because a third party caused the stumble.

## Defending Rav

### Baraisa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: בְּבוֹר – בֵּין לְפָנָיו בֵּין לְאַחֲרָיו, חַיָּיב. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **raised an objection to** the opinion of Rav from a *baraita*: With regard **to** an animal that fell into **a pit, whether** it fell in **forward** or **whether** it fell in **backward,** he is **liable.** This constitutes **a conclusive refutation of Rav.**

The *gemara* now challenges Rav directly. A *baraisa* states: *b'vor bein milifanav bein mil'acharav chayav* — if the animal fell into the pit, the owner is liable whether face-first or backward. This appears to be an explicit contradiction of Rav's position. The *gemara* initially declares *tiyuvta d'Rav* — a refutation of Rav.

### Bor BiReshuto

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מוֹדֶה רַב בְּבוֹר בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ – דְּחַיָּיב, מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי בְּהַבְלָא מִית – הַבְלָא דִּידָךְ הוּא, אִי בְּחַבְטָא מִית – חַבְטָא דִּידָךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said** in explanation of how Rav's opinion could accord with the *baraita*: **Rav concedes** in the case **of a pit on his** own **property that** a person is **liable** even if the animal fell backward, **because** the owner of the animal can **say to** the owner of the pit: **Whichever way you** look at it, you are liable. **If** my animal **died** due **to the** lethal **fumes,** these **are your** lethal **fumes. If** it **died** on account **of the impact,** this **is the impact** of **your** land, and not the impact of land in the public domain, which is the typical case of Pit.

Rav Chisda resolves this: *modeh Rav b'bor bireshuso d'chayav* — Rav concedes liability in the case of a pit dug within one's own property. The standard *bor* liability in the public domain exempts the owner from *chavata*, the impact, because the ground belongs to *reshus harabbim* and is *karka olam*. But if the pit is on the owner's own property — even if the public has a right of passage through it — then the ground at the bottom belongs to the pit owner as well. In that case, *mima nafshach*: whether the animal died from the *hevel*, that air is his; and whether it died from the impact, that impact is his too. The *baraisa*, then, is speaking of a *bor bireshuso*, and Rav's position for the standard public-domain pit remains intact.

### Rava's Flipover Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּמִתְהַפֵּךְ, דִּנְפַל אַאַפֵּיהּ וְאִתְהֲפִיךְ וּנְפַל אַגַּבֵּיהּ; דְּהַבְלָא דְּאַהֲנִי בֵּיהּ, אַהֲנִי בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said** that this is how the *baraita* should be explained according to Rav: **Here we are dealing with where** the animal **tumbled,** i.e., **where** it began to **fall on its face, and** afterward **tumbled and** continued to **fall on its back.** In this case, he is liable **because the** lethal **fumes, which were effective** in injuring the animal, **were** also **effective** in killing **it.**

Rava offers a second resolution. The *baraisa* is speaking of a case where the animal initially fell face-first — *nafal al apei* — and then flipped over mid-fall — *ve'ishapech* — and ultimately struck the ground back-first — *venafal al gabei*. In this scenario, the animal did inhale the *hevel* in the initial descent, and since that *hevel* contributed to the outcome, the pit owner bears full liability despite the fact that in the end the back struck the ground. It is a strained reading of the *baraisa*, which seemed to be making a categorical statement about the direction of the fall, but it does resolve the apparent contradiction on Rav's terms.

### Water Damage Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: הָכָא בְּנִזְקֵי בוֹר בְּשׁוֹר עָסְקִינַן – מַאי נִיהוּ? שֶׁהִבְאִישׁ אֶת מֵימָיו; דְּלָא שְׁנָא לְפָנָיו וְלָא שְׁנָא לְאַחֲרָיו – מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Here,** in this *baraita*, **we are dealing with damage** to **the pit** that is caused **by the ox.** And **what is** the damage? The damage is **that it contaminated the water** in the pit, in which case the *baraita* states **that** it makes **no difference** if the animal fell **forward and** it makes **no difference** if it fell **backward,** since he **is liable** either way.

Rav Yosef proposes an entirely different reading of the *baraisa*. The *baraisa* is not discussing the pit owner's liability for damage to the ox at all. Rather, *hacha b'nizkei bor b'shor askinan* — we are speaking of the damage caused by the ox to the pit. Specifically, *she'hiv'ish meimav* — the animal fell in and fouled the water in the well. In that case, the direction in which the animal fell is entirely irrelevant, because what matters is that the owner's ox trespassed onto someone else's property — *reshus hanizak* — and spoiled the water there, which is a form of *shen varegel*. This is why the *baraisa* makes no distinction between *lifanav* and *l'acharav*: neither direction bears on the question of whether the ox fouled the water. The *baraisa* thus poses no challenge to Rav's ruling on pit liability for the animal's injury.

### Derech Nefilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב חֲנַנְיָה לְסַיּוֹעֵי לְרַב: ״וְנָפַל״ – עַד שֶׁיִּפּוֹל דֶּרֶךְ נְפִילָה. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: נָפַל לְפָנָיו מִקּוֹל הַכְּרִיָּיה – חַיָּיב, לְאַחֲרָיו מִקּוֹל הַכְּרִיָּיה – פָּטוּר. וְזֶה וָזֶה בְּבוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥananya taught** a *baraita* **in support of** the opinion of **Rav:** The verse states with regard to damage caused by a pit to an animal: **"And** an ox or a donkey **fall** therein" (Exodus 21:33), which is interpreted to mean that one is not liable **unless** the animal **falls** in the usual **manner of falling. From here** the Sages **stated** that if it **fell forward due to the sound of the digging,** he is **liable,** but if it fell **backward due to the sound of the digging,** he is **exempt, and** both **this** case **and that** case refer to damage **by a pit.**

Rabbi Chanina teaches a *drasha* that affirmatively supports Rav. The verse states *v'nafal shama shor o chamor* — an ox or donkey "falls" into the pit. The *drasha* derives: *ad sheyipol derech nefilah* — the verse requires that the animal fall in the natural manner of falling, which is face-first. From this, the *halacha* is stated explicitly: *nafal lefanav mikol hakriyah chayav, l'acharav mikol hakriyah patur* — if the animal fell forward because of the sound of the digging, the owner is liable; if backward, exempt. Furthermore, the source adds *v'zeh v'zeh b'bor* — both cases are speaking of the animal falling into the pit itself. Rabbi Chanina's source thus directly confirms Rav's reading: the direction of the fall into the pit is determinative, and falling backward, even into the pit, exempts the owner.

## Stumbling Debate

### Digger's Causation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: נָפַל לְפָנָיו מִקּוֹל הַכְּרִיָּיה – חַיָּיב. וְאַמַּאי? נֵימָא כּוֹרֶה גְּרַם לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** in the mishna: If it **fell forward due to the sound of the digging,** he is **liable.** The Gemara asks: **But why** is this so? **Let us say** that it is the sound of **the digger** that **caused** the animal to fall, and not the pit itself.

The *gemara* now turns to the deeper structural question embedded in the *mishna*. We have established that when an animal falls forward into the pit due to the sound of the digging, the pit owner is liable. But the *gemara* asks: *ve'amai? nema kol garam leh* — why? Cannot the owner of the pit argue that it was the sound of the digger that caused the animal to stumble, and that this should absolve him of liability?

This question highlights the dual function of a *bor* as a hazard: it both creates the stumbling condition — analogous to placing a stone in *reshus harabbim*, violating *lifnei iver lo titein michshol* — and it then inflicts the actual damage through impact. When these two functions are separated — when the stumble is caused by one party and the damage by another — what is the rule? Must the pit itself cause the stumble for the pit owner to be liable?

### Rabbi Noson Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי נָתָן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: בַּעַל הַבּוֹר הֶזֵּיקָא קָא עָבֵיד, וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר לְאִשְׁתַּלּוֹמֵי מֵהַאי – מִשְׁתַּלֵּם מֵהַאי;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rav Shimi bar Ashi said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this?** It **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Natan, who says: The owner** of **the pit causes the damage** by his digging, **and anywhere that** it is **not possible to collect** payment **from this** one who caused the fall, i.e., the digger, payment is **collected from that** one, i.e., the owner of the pit. This is because in any event, he bears responsibility for the hazard he created.

Rav Shimi bar Ashi resolves this: *ha meni Rabbi Natan hi* — this *mishna* follows the position of Rabbi Natan. Two principles characterize Rabbi Natan's view. First, *ba'al habor heizeika avid* — the owner of the pit causes the damage, and that alone is sufficient for liability, even without having caused the stumble. Second — and this addresses the concern about the digger — *kol heicha d'lo efshar le'ishtalumi mei'hai mishtalem mei'hai*: wherever one cannot collect from the party that caused the stumble — because, for example, causing a stumble through sound is a *grama*, indirect causation, for which one is exempt — the full liability can be collected from the owner of the pit. These two principles together explain why the *mishna* holds the pit owner liable even though the digger caused the trip.

### Ox Pushes Ox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: שׁוֹר שֶׁדָּחַף אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ לְבוֹר – בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר חַיָּיב, בַּעַל הַבּוֹר פָּטוּר. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר מְשַׁלֵּם מֶחֱצָה, וּבַעַל הַבּוֹר מְשַׁלֵּם מֶחֱצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **an ox that pushed another** ox **into a pit, the owner of the** first **ox** is **liable,** and **the owner of the pit** is **exempt. Rabbi Natan says: The owner of the** first **ox pays half** the amount, **and the owner of the pit pays half** the amount.

The *gemara* now identifies the *tannaic* source for Rabbi Natan's position. The *baraisa* states: *shor sheda'chaf et chaveiro labor* — an ox pushed a fellow ox into a pit. The *tanna kamma* rules *ba'al hashor chayav, ba'al habor patur* — the owner of the pushing ox is fully liable, and the owner of the pit is exempt. The reasoning of the *chachamim* is clear: liability for a *bor* requires that the *bor* itself create the hazard that caused the stumble. Since here an ox — not the pit — caused the other animal to fall in, the pit owner bears no responsibility. The pushing ox constitutes an act of *keren*, and the ox owner pays full damages. As a consequence of this view, in the case of our *mishna* — where the digger's sound caused the stumble — the pit owner would also be exempt according to the *chachamim*, and since the digger acted only as a *grama*, no one would be liable at all.

### Chachamim vs. Noson

The owner of the pushing ox is fully liable, and the owner of the pit is exempt according to the *chachamim*. Rabbi Natan disagrees: *ba'al hashor meshalem mechtza u'va'al habor meshalem mechtza* — the owner of the pushing ox and the owner of the pit split the payment equally. The pit inflicted the damage and is therefore jointly liable regardless of whether it caused the stumble. The fundamental dispute, then, is whether *bor* liability requires the pit to have generated the hazardous condition that caused the animal to fall in, or whether it is sufficient that the pit was the instrument of the actual damage. The *chachamim* focus on the stumble; Rabbi Natan focuses on the damage. It follows that in the case of the digger's sound, where the *grama* of the digging cannot be made to pay, Rabbi Natan imposes full liability on the pit owner.

## Liability Division

### 3/4 vs 1/2 Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: בַּעַל הַבּוֹר מְשַׁלֵּם שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲלָקִים, וּבַעַל הַשּׁוֹר רְבִיעַ! לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא בְּתָם, הָא בְּמוּעָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies Rabbi Natan's opinion: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says:** If an ox pushed another ox into a pit, **the owner of the pit pays three-quarters, and the owner of the** first **ox** pays **one-quarter?** The Gemara resolves the contradiction: This is **not difficult,** as **this** second *baraita* is referring **to an innocuous** ox that pushed the second ox. Therefore, the owner of the ox that caused damage pays only one-quarter of the cost of the damage, which is half of the half for which he is responsible. By contrast, **that** other *baraita* is referring **to a forewarned** ox that pushed a second ox. Consequently, the owner of the forewarned ox pays the full amount for which he is responsible, i.e., half the value of the total damage.

The *gemara* now notes an apparent internal contradiction in Rabbi Natan's rulings. In one *baraisa* he divides liability equally — the ox owner pays half and the pit owner pays half. But a second *baraisa* attributes three-quarters of the liability to the pit owner and only one quarter to the ox owner. How can Rabbi Natan maintain both positions?

The *gemara* resolves this simply: *lo kashya, ha b'tam, ha b'mu'ad* — there is no contradiction, because the two *baraisos* address different cases. Since we are dealing with *keren*, the distinction between a *tam* — a non-forewarned ox, which pays only half-damages — and a *mu'ad* — a forewarned ox, which pays full damages — changes the arithmetic of the split. In the case of a *mu'ad*, where both parties bear full individual liability, the split is 50/50. In the case of a *tam*, the numbers work out differently, producing the 3/4 and 1/4 division. The *gemara* then unpacks the conceptual machinery that generates these figures.

### Conceptual Models

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: רַבִּי נָתָן דַּיָּינָא הוּא, וְנָחֵית לְעוּמְקָא דְּדִינָא; לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר: הַאי כּוּלֵּיהּ הֶזֵּיקָא עֲבַד, וְהַאי כּוּלֵּיהּ הֶזֵּיקָא עֲבַד; וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ לְשַׁלֵּם הַאי פַּלְגָא וְהַאי פַּלְגָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר לְבַעַל הַבּוֹר: שׁוּתָּפוּתַאי מַאי אַהַנְיָא לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Rabbi Natan is a judge and** has **plumbed the full depths of the *halakha*. Actually, he holds** that **this** one **performed all of the damage and that** one **performed all of the damage. And as** for **your difficulty,** that **this** one **should pay half and that** one should pay **half,** that is not difficult. The owner of the ox pays only one-quarter, **because the owner of the ox** can **say to the owner of the pit: How** did **my partnership** with you in this situation **help me?** Even if my ox caused all the damage and none of the damage was caused by the pit, I would be required to pay only half the cost of the damage. Therefore, as we are partners in this situation, I should pay half of what I should have paid, which is one-quarter. You should pay the other half of what I should have paid, in addition to your share, since you would have had to pay full damages.

Rava explains that Rabbi Natan was a *dayan* who penetrated to *umka de'dina* — the very depth of the law. Rabbi Natan's view is *le'olam kasavar hai kulei heizeika avid v'hai kulei heizeika avid* — each party is considered to have caused 100% of the damage. Since the victim cannot collect double the value of his animal, the parties divide the payment — but they begin from full individual liability for each.

The alternative conceptual model would hold that each party caused only 50% of the damage. Under this model, in the case of a *tam*, the ox owner caused 50% and pays half of that — a quarter — and the pit owner pays his 50%, leaving the victim out one quarter. This yields a 50/25 split with a 25% loss to the victim, which does not match either *baraisa*.

### Ox's Complaint

Under the 100/100% model, in the case of a *mu'ad*, each party starts at full liability; the payment is divided 50/50. But in the case of a *tam*, the *gemara* explains the resulting 75/25 division through a compelling argument: *mishum d'amar lei ba'al hashor l'ba'al habor, shutafutai mai ahanei li* — the owner of the ox says to the owner of the pit: what benefit did this partnership bring me? Had my ox gored alone, as a *tam* I would owe only 50. Now that my ox and your pit both caused the full damage, why should I still pay 50? You are also in the equation. The argument is arithmetically persuasive. The pit owner, had he acted alone, would owe 100. The ox owner, had he acted alone, would owe 50. Their combined "debt" is 150 against a single payment of 100. A fair allocation proportionate to their individual liability yields 100/150 to the pit owner — that is, two-thirds of 100, or approximately 67 — but the *gemara* arrives at 75 and 25. The core logic is that the ox owner's 50% share gets divided in half once the pit owner enters the equation, so that the ox owner pays 25 and the pit owner pays 75.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר: הַאי פַּלְגָא הֶזֵּיקָא עֲבַד, וְהַאי פַּלְגָא הֶזֵּיקָא עֲבַד; וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ: בַּעַל הַבּוֹר מְשַׁלֵּם פַּלְגָא וּבַעַל הַשּׁוֹר מְשַׁלֵּם רְבִיעַ, וְאִידָּךְ רִיבְעָא נַפְסֵיד – מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר לְבַעַל הַבּוֹר: אֲנָא תּוֹרַאי בְּבֵירָךְ אַשְׁכְּחִיתֵיהּ – אַתְּ קְטַלְתֵּיהּ; מַאי דְּאִית לִי לְאִשְׁתַּלּוֹמֵי מֵהַיְאךְ – מִשְׁתַּלַּמְנָא, מַאי דְּלֵית לִי לְאִשְׁתַּלּוֹמֵי מֵהַיְאךְ – מִשְׁתַּלַּמְנָא מִמָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **If you wish, say** instead a different explanation: **Actually,** Rabbi Natan **maintains** that **this** one **performed half the damage and that** one **performed half the damage. As** for **your difficulty** with the fact that the **owner of the pit** should **pay half and the owner of the ox** should **pay one-quarter, and** the injured party **loses the other** remaining **quarter,** that is not difficult. This is **because the owner of the** killed **ox** can **say to the owner of the pit: I found my ox in your pit.** Consequently, I am assuming that **you killed it.** Therefore, concerning **that** portion of the payment **that I can receive from the other,** i.e., the owner of the damaging ox, **I will receive** it. Concerning **that** portion **that I am unable to receive from the other** individual, **I will receive it from you.**

This framework also clarifies the case of the digger: since each party holds 100% liability, and the digger cannot pay because his contribution was *grama*, the full obligation rests on the pit owner. You collect from the party from whom you *can* collect.

### Deductible Liability

The *gemara* then presents an alternative formulation: the pit owner bears full liability — 100% — and the ox owner's liability is calculated separately and is deductible from what the victim recovers from the pit owner. *Mai d'leisa lishalumi mei'hai mishtalamna mimach* — whatever I cannot collect from the ox owner, I collect from you. As *Tosafos* notes, this language suggests not a 50/50 conceptual split but rather that the pit bears 100% liability and the ox's share — half of 50% for a *tam*, i.e., 25% — is deducted, leaving 75% owed by the pit owner. Anything that cannot be collected from the ox owner falls entirely on the pit. This is the meaning of *kol heicha d'leika le'shalumi mei'hai mishtalama mimach*, and it fully explains why the *mishna*'s pit owner pays even when the stumble was caused by the digger.

## Stone at Pit Edge

### Rock Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִנִּיחַ אֶבֶן עַל פִּי הַבּוֹר, וּבָא שׁוֹר וְנִתְקַל בָּהּ, וְנָפַל בַּבּוֹר – בָּאנוּ לְמַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי נָתָן וְרַבָּנַן. פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** On the same subject, **Rava says:** If one **left a stone at the opening of a pit** belonging to another person, **and an ox came and stumbled on it and fell into the pit, we have arrived at the dispute** between **Rabbi Natan and the Rabbis** concerning the division of responsibility between them. The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious,** since what is the difference whether the ox fell into the pit due to another ox pushing it or due to the stone?

Rava introduces a new scenario five lines from the bottom of the page: *hiniach even al pi habor, ba shor v'niska bo v'nafal b'abor* — someone placed a stone at the entrance of a pit, an ox came, tripped on the stone, and fell into the pit. The question is how liability is allocated between the owner of the stone and the owner of the pit. Rava states that this case falls within the debate of Rabbi Natan and the *rabbanim*: the *rabbanim* would hold the stone owner fully liable as the one who caused the stumble, and Rabbi Natan would split it between stone and pit.

### Stone's Dependency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, הָתָם הוּא דְּאָמַר בַּעַל הַבּוֹר לְבַעַל הַשּׁוֹר: ״אִי לָאו בֵּירָא דִּידִי – תּוֹרָא דִּידָךְ הֲוָה קָטֵיל לֵיהּ״; אֲבָל הָכָא, מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ בַּעַל אֶבֶן לְבַעַל הַבּוֹר: ״אִי לָאו בֵּירָא דִּידָךְ, אַבְנָא דִּידִי מַאי הֲוָה עָבְדָא? אִי הֲוָה מִיתְּקֵל בָּהּ – הֲוָה נָפֵל וְקָאֵי!״
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This ruling needed to be stated explicitly, **lest you say** that only **there the owner of the pit** can **say to the owner of the ox** who was responsible for the damage: **If not** for **my pit, your ox would have killed** the second ox anyway, in which case my pit was not the cause of the damage, **but here, the owner of the stone can say to the owner of the pit: If not** for **your pit, what would my stone have done? If** the ox **would have stumbled on it,** it **would have** simply **fallen and gotten up,** and I should be exempt. Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that this claim is not a valid one, **because** the owner of the pit can **say** in response **to** the owner of the stone:

The *gemara* objects: *peshita* — isn't this obvious? Isn't the stone analogous to the ox that pushed the other ox into the pit? The *gemara* responds: no, one might have thought that this case is categorically different. In the ox-pushes-ox scenario, the pit owner can say to the ox owner: *ilav bira didi, tora didach hava katil lei* — even without my pit, your ox would have killed; the ox was goring on its own and the pit was incidental. That is why both the *rabbanim* and Rabbi Natan apply liability to the ox owner. But in the stone case, the stone owner might argue the reverse.

### Pit Owner Defense

The owner of the stone can say to the owner of the pit: *ilav bura didach, avna didi mai havai avda* — had it not been for your pit, my stone would have done nothing at all. *I havai miskel bah, havai nafal v'ka'i* — even if the animal tripped on my stone, had there been no pit, it would simply have fallen and gotten back up. My stone, standing alone, caused no lasting damage. On this reasoning, the stone owner might be completely exempt, and perhaps the *rabbanim* would hold both parties exempt while Rabbi Natan would — as always — impose liability on the pit for the damage it caused.

### Both Necessary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אִי לָאו אֶבֶן – לָא הֲוָה נָפֵיל לְבֵירָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **teaches us** that this claim is not a valid one, **because** the owner of the pit can **say** in response **to** the owner of the stone: **If not for** your **stone,** the ox **would not have fallen into the pit** in the first place.

The *gemara* rejects this escape: *ka mashma lan* — the case teaches us that this reasoning does not succeed. The counter-argument is symmetrical: *ilav even, lo havai nafal l'bira* — had it not been for the stone, the animal would never have fallen into the pit. Yes, the stone needed the pit to cause injury, but equally, the pit needed the stone to cause the stumble. Both parties were necessary causes, and therefore the debate of Rabbi Natan and the *rabbanim* applies here just as it does in the ox case. For Rabbi Natan, the two split liability; for the *rabbanim*, the stone owner bears full liability as the cause of the stumble, even though without the pit no damage would have occurred.

## Multiple Actors

### Sanctified Ox Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹר וְשׁוֹר פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין שֶׁנָּגְחוּ – מַאי נִיהוּ? שׁוֹר בְּכוֹר, דְּלָא פָּרֵיק לֵיהּ; אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק, רָבִינָא אָמַר: מְשַׁלֵּם רְבִיעַ נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** There is a dispute with regard to a case of an ordinary innocuous **ox and a disqualified consecrated ox that gored** another ox together. **What is** this case of an ox that was consecrated and disqualified? It is **a firstborn ox that** became blemished and was consequently disqualified as an offering. Nevertheless, its initial consecration remains, and therefore it may **not** be **redeemed.** Consequently, the priest in possession of it is not liable to pay damages, since it is not classified as: The ox of another, as its status is not that of a non-sacred ox belonging to a person. Given this background, what is the liability of the owner of the non-sacred ox that gored together with it? **Abaye says: He pays half** the cost of **the damage,** whereas **Ravina says: He pays one-quarter of** the cost of **the damage.**

At the top of 53b the *gemara* introduces a new scenario involving *pesulei hamukdashin* — animals consecrated for sacrifice that were later disqualified and thus retain a degree of sanctity while being permitted for ordinary use. A gloss in the text, attributed to Rav Hai Gaon in *Halachos Gedolos*, clarifies that the specific case here involves a firstborn ox with a blemish that was never redeemed. Such an animal retains sufficient sanctity that its owner falls outside the category of *shor rei'eihu* — "the ox of his fellow" — the phrase the Torah uses to define the subject of *keren* liability. Accordingly, the owner of such an ox is exempt from payment when it causes damage.

The case: this sanctified-but-disqualified ox and a normal ox jointly gore another animal. Abaye rules that the owner of the normal ox pays *chatzi nezek* — half damages. Ravina rules *revia nezek* — a quarter.

### Tam/Mu'ad Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא וְהָא בְּתָם; הָא כְּרַבָּנַן, וְהָא כְּרַבִּי נָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dispute: Both **this** one, Abaye, **and that** one, Ravina, refer **to** a case where the ordinary ox was **innocuous. This** opinion stated by Ravina is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** that each party is responsible for only half the damage. Since the non-sacred ox was innocuous, its owner pays half the cost of the damage for which he is responsible, i.e., one-quarter of the total. **And that** opinion stated by Abaye is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan** that each party in turn is responsible for all the damage. Consequently, since the non-sacred ox is innocuous, its owner pays half the total amount.

One way to understand this dispute is that both Abaye and Ravina are discussing a *tam* and arguing about whether the case follows the *rabbanim* or Rabbi Natan. According to Rabbi Natan's 100/100% model: the exempt ox did 100% and cannot be made to pay; the normal ox did 100% and, being a *tam*, pays half — that is, 50% of the full value. According to the *rabbanim*'s 50/50% model: each ox caused half the damage, and the normal *tam* ox pays half of its half — a quarter — with the remaining quarter unrecoverable. Thus Abaye's ruling of half reflects Rabbi Natan's model and Ravina's ruling of a quarter reflects the *rabbanim*'s model.

### Rabbanim Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא כְּרַבָּנַן; הָא בְּתָם, הָא בְּמוּעָד.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead that there is no dispute, and that both **this** one, Abaye, **and that** one, Ravina, state their opinions **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis. This** one, Ravina, is referring **to** where the non-sacred ox is **innocuous,** and so its owner pays half of a half, and **that** one, Abaye, is referring **to** where the non-sacred ox is **forewarned,** so its owner pays his entire share, i.e., half the total damage.

Alternatively, both Abaye and Ravina follow the *rabbanim*, and the difference between them is whether the normal ox is a *mu'ad* or a *tam*. Under the *rabbanim*'s 50/50 model: if it is a *mu'ad*, the normal ox caused 50% of the damage and pays its full 50% — hence Abaye's *chatzi nezek*. If it is a *tam*, the normal ox caused 50% but pays only half of that — hence Ravina's *revia nezek*. A third formulation in the *gemara* holds that both follow Rabbi Natan's 100/100% model and the difference is simply *mu'ad* vs. *tam*: for a *mu'ad* the normal ox pays 100%; for a *tam*, 50%.

### Rava's Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שׁוֹר וְאָדָם שֶׁדָּחֲפוּ לְבוֹר, לְעִנְיַן נְזָקִין – כּוּלָּן חַיָּיבִין. לְעִנְיַן אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים וּדְמֵי וְלָדוֹת – אָדָם חַיָּיב, וְשׁוֹר וּבוֹר פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** With regard to **an ox and a person that push** a person or animal **into a pit,** then **concerning** the **damage** that they caused, **they,** i.e., the man who pushed the person or animal, the owner of the ox, and the owner of the pit, **are all liable** to pay the indemnity of damage. **Concerning the four** additional **types of indemnity,** which are loss of livelihood, pain, medical costs, and humiliation, **and** also with regard to the **compensation for** miscarried **offspring,** the **person** who pushed is **liable, and** the owner of **the ox and** the owner of **the pit** are **exempt,** since the Torah did not impose any obligation on them to pay these payments.

Rava then presents a comprehensive illustration — an ox and a person who together push a victim into a pit — that draws all these threads together. *Tosafos* notes that the human must have acted *shelo b'kavana*, without intent, because a person acting intentionally would be the dominant cause and the other parties would be treated as instruments of his action, as in the case of someone who throws another's *tallis* into a fire and uses the fire as his instrument. Assuming unintentional action and following Rabbi Natan's 100/100% model, all three parties — ox, person, and pit — share liability for the full damage.

### Four Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעִנְיַן כּוֹפֶר וּשְׁלֹשִׁים שֶׁל עֶבֶד – שׁוֹר חַיָּיב, אָדָם וּבוֹר פְּטוּרִים. לְעִנְיַן כֵּלִים וְשׁוֹר פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין – אָדָם וְשׁוֹר חַיָּיבִין, וּבוֹר פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to** the payment of **ransom** if it was a person who was pushed and killed, **and** also concerning the **thirty** shekels paid **for** a Canaanite **slave** who was killed, the owner of **the ox** is **liable,** and **the man** who did the pushing **and** the owner of **the pit** are **exempt. With regard to vessels** and **a disqualified consecrated ox** that fell into the pit, **the man** who did the pushing **and** the owner of the **ox** are **liable, and** the owner of the **pit** is **exempt.**

Rava maps liability across four payment categories. For *nezek* — the straightforward damage payment — all three are liable, and it divides among them equally under Rabbi Natan's framework. For the four additional payments imposed when a human is injured — *tza'ar, rippui, shevet, boshet* — and similarly for *demei veladot* in cases of miscarriage, only the human is liable; the ox and the pit owner are exempt, as the Torah assigns these categories exclusively to human actors. Since each party holds 100% liability, the human pays full for these categories. For *kofer* — the ransom payment when an ox kills a person — and for the *shloshim* of an *eved* when a slave is killed, only the ox is liable, as the Torah imposes *kofer* solely on the *ba'al hashor* and never on a person who causes a death or on a pit. Accordingly, the ox's owner pays full for these. For *kelim* — vessels broken in the incident — the human and the ox are liable but the pit owner is exempt, as the Torah explicitly exempts a pit from liability for vessels. Therefore, the human and ox divide this payment between them.

One of the entries in Rava's table specifies an ox of *pesulei hamukdashin* in connection with *kelim*, noting that the pit owner is exempt there as well. This prompts the *gemara* to examine the source of the pit's exemption regarding *pesulei hamukdashin* — which leads to the final section.

## Invalid Sacrifices

### Exemption Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ – בְּמִי שֶׁהַמֵּת שֶׁלּוֹ, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵין הַמֵּת שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains: **What is the reason** that the owner of the pit is exempt from liability for damage caused to a disqualified consecrated ox? Since **the verse states** with regard to a pit: **"And the carcass shall be for him"** (Exodus 21:34), it teaches that the obligation to pay damages is imposed only **on the one to whom the carcass** subsequently **belongs,** thereby **excluding this** case of a disqualified consecrated ox, **where the carcass does not belong to him.** In this case, it is prohibited to sell the carcass. The owner of the pit cannot derive benefit from it, and it no longer belongs to anyone, so the conditions written in the verse are not in effect.

Rava's neat table assumes that the pit owner is exempt when an ox of *pesulei hamukdashin* falls into the pit. But the *gemara* notes that Rava himself had earlier raised this as an open question — *iba'i Rava*, Rava asked — *shor pesulei hamukdashin shenafal l'bor mahu*: if a disqualified consecrated animal falls into a pit, what is the law? If this was Rava's own unresolved question, how could he take the exemption for granted in his illustrative table?

### V'Hameit Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לְמֵימְרָא דִּפְשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ לְרָבָא? וְהָא מִיבַּעְיָא בָּעֵי לֵיהּ לְרָבָא – דְּבָעֵי רָבָא: שׁוֹר פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין שֶׁנָּפַל לַבּוֹר, מַהוּ? הַאי ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ – בְּמִי שֶׁהַמֵּת שֶׁלּו, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵין הַמֵּת שֶׁלּוֹ; אוֹ דִילְמָא, ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ – לִבְעָלִים מִטַּפְּלִין בַּנְּבֵילָה הוּא דַּאֲתָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that** this matter was **obvious to Rava? Is it not a dilemma** that was already raised **by Rava, as Rava asked: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **a disqualified consecrated ox that fell into a pit?** Is **this** verse: **"And the carcass shall be for him,"** referring **to the one** to whom **the carcass belongs,** i.e., the owner of the pit, **excluding this** case **where the carcass does not belong to him,** and therefore the owner is exempt from paying for it? **Or perhaps** the verse **"And the carcass shall be for him" comes to teach that** the **owner** of the animal **attends to,** i.e., retains ownership of, **the carcass,** and the perpetrator of the damage pays him the difference?

The substantive question Rava had raised was the proper interpretation of the verse *v'hameit yihyeh lo* — "and the dead animal shall be his" — which appears in the Torah's passage about pit liability. Two readings are possible. The first: *b'mi shehameishehameit shelo*, the verse speaks of a case where the dead body has some usable value; *yatza zu she'ein hameit shelo* — excluded is a case where the dead body belongs to no one or cannot be used, as with a consecrated animal that cannot be redeemed after death and whose carcass has no permitted utility. On this reading, where there is no usable carcass, the pit owner is categorically exempt. The second reading: *b'alim metaplin b'neveilah hu d'ata* — the verse is teaching the rule discussed earlier in the tractate, that it is the original owner of the dead animal who retains the carcass and the pit owner pays only the depreciation. On this reading, the verse says nothing about an exemption for *pesulei hamukdashin*.

### Categorical Exemptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתַר דְּבַעְיַהּ, הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Initially, Rava was in doubt about the matter, but **after** he **raised the dilemma,** he **then resolved it** and concluded that the verse imposes liability for the cost of damage only on a person to whom the carcass belongs, excluding the case of a disqualified consecrated ox.

Having resolved his question in favor of the first reading — an exemption for *pesulei hamukdashin* by the *bor* — the *gemara* now asks: where does the *b'alim metaplin b'neveilah* principle come from, if not from this verse? The answer: *nafkah lei mi'v'hameit yihyeh lo d'shor* — the same phrase appears in the context of one ox goring another, and *there* it teaches the principle of *b'alim metaplin b'neveilah*. The verse appears twice, and each instance teaches a different rule.

### Why Not Ox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּעָלִים מִטַּפְּלִים בִּנְבֵילָה – מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִן ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ דְּשׁוֹר. מַאי חָזֵית דִּ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ דְּשׁוֹר – מַפְּקַתְּ לֵיהּ לִבְעָלִים מִטַּפְּלִים בִּנְבֵילָה, ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ דְּבוֹר – מַפְּקַתְּ לֵיהּ לְמִי שֶׁהַמֵּת שֶׁלּוֹ? אֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, from where does he** derive the *halakha* that the **owner attends to the animal carcass?** The Gemara answers: He **derives it from** the verse: **"And the carcass shall be for him"** (Exodus 21:36), stated **with regard to Ox.** The Gemara asks further: **What did you see** to determine **that from** the verse **"And the carcass shall be for him,"** stated **with regard to Ox, you derive** from it the *halakha* that **the owner** of the dead ox **attends to the carcass,** and from the verse **"And the carcass belongs to him,"** stated **with regard to Pit, you derive from it** the *halakha* that only **the one to whom the carcass belongs** pays damages? Why can't **I reverse** the derivations from each verse and say the opposite?

The *gemara* then asks why we not reverse the assignments — let the verse by the *bor* teach *b'alim metaplin b'neveilah* and the verse by the *shor* teach the exemption for *pesulei hamukdashin*. The *gemara* responds: *mistabra p'tor gabei bor, ho'il u'patar bo et hakelim* — it is more logical to locate the exemption for *pesulei hamukdashin* with the *bor*, because the *bor* already has other categorical exemptions built into it. The pit is exempt from liability for *kelim* and in some contexts for human death as well. It is therefore fitting to add the exemption for *pesulei hamukdashin* to the *bor*'s list of categorical exemptions.

### Framework Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא פְּטוּר גַּבֵּי בוֹר – הוֹאִיל וּפָטַר בּוֹ אֶת הַכֵּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **is reasonable** that the **exemption** applies to the verse stated **with regard to Pit, since** the Torah also **exempts** one from paying for damage classified as Pit caused to **vessels** that fall into it and break.

With this resolved, Rava's illustrative table is fully vindicated. The framework in which ox, person, and pit each carry 100% liability under Rabbi Natan's model allows the *gemara* to map which party pays for which category: *nezek* is divided three ways, the four human payments fall entirely on the human, *kofer* falls entirely on the ox, and *kelim* — and the analogous case of *pesulei hamukdashin* — fall entirely on the human and the ox, with the pit categorically exempt. Whatever cannot be collected from one party due to categorical exemption or inability to pay is recovered in full from those who remain liable.
