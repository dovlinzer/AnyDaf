# Pesachim 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Galus to Bavel

### Ulla: Dates & Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיֹּאכְלוּ תְּמָרִים וְיַעַסְקוּ בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said** that Israel was exiled to Babylonia **in order** to enable them **to eat** the **dates** that grow there plentifully, which gave them strength **and** allowed them to **engage in Torah** study.

The daf opens at the bottom of 87b, picking up a remarkable stretch of *aggadata* concerning the reasons Israel was exiled specifically to Bavel. Several explanations had already been offered: to gather *gerim*, to return to a place of ancestral origin, and because Bavel was experienced as something like *sheol* — a descent into the depths. There was also the consideration that Bavel was simply more hospitable to Jews than remaining in Roman-controlled Eretz Yisrael. The Gemara now adds a final teaching on the matter: *Ulla amar kedei she-yochlu temarim ve-ya'asku ba-Torah* — Ulla said that Israel went to Bavel so that they could eat dates and engage in Torah. The point is that dates were extraordinarily plentiful and cheap in Bavel, making it remarkably easy to earn a livelihood. Rather than framing the exile solely in terms of fleeing persecution, Ulla reframes it positively: the material ease of Bavel freed people to devote themselves to Torah study.

### Ulla's Dates Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אִיקְּלַע לְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, קָרִיבוּ לֵיהּ טִירְיָנָא דְתַמְרֵי. אֲמַר לְהוּ: כַּמָּה כִּי הָנֵי בְּזוּזָא? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: תְּלָת בְּזוּזָא. אֲמַר: מְלָא צַנָּא דְּדוּבְשָׁא בְּזוּזָא, וּבַבְלָאֵי לָא עָסְקִי בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. בְּלֵילְיָא צַעֲרוּהוּ. אָמַר: מְלָא צַנָּא סַמָּא דְמוֹתָא בְּזוּזָא בְּבָבֶל, וּבַבְלָאֵי עָסְקִי בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara records a related incident: **Ulla visited Pumbedita,** and his hosts **brought him a basket [*tirina*] of dates. He said to them: How many** baskets of dates **like these** can one purchase **for a *zuz*? They said to him:** One can purchase **three for a *zuz*. He said:** How can it be that it is possible to purchase **a basketful of** date **honey for** just a single ***zuz*, and** yet **the Babylonians do not engage in Torah** study more extensively? Since the cost of food is so low and they do not need to work hard to support themselves, the Babylonians should be more extensively engaged in Torah study. **That night,** the dates he ate **afflicted him** and he suffered from indigestion. In light of this, Ulla retracted his original assessment of the Babylonians and instead praised them and **said: A basketful of lethal poison,** i.e., the dates that cause indigestion, sells **for a *zuz* in Babylonia, and** despite the fact that they suffer its effects **the Babylonians** still **engage in Torah** study.

The Gemara immediately follows with an anecdote that enriches and complicates this teaching. Ulla, who was himself from Eretz Yisrael, once visited Pumbedita. The locals brought him a large basket of dates. He asked the price and was told: three baskets for a *zuza* — extraordinarily cheap. His reaction was sharp: *mala tana de-duvsha be-zuza u-Vavlayei lo aski be-Oraisa* — a basket of honey for a *zuza*, and the Babylonians still can't manage to learn Torah? That night, having overindulged, he suffered a severe stomachache from all the dates. His tone shifted completely: *mala tana samma de-mosa be-zuza be-Bavel, Vavlayei aski be-Oraisa* — a basket of poison for a *zuza* here in Bavel, and yet they still manage to learn Torah! The reversal is not merely comic. What began as a critique of Babylonian scholars for failing to capitalize on their material ease becomes an admiring acknowledgment that they pursue Torah even amid conditions that are, in their own way, challenging.

### Comfort & Growth

The lesson here is one worth taking seriously in any era of material comfort. The assumption — Ulla's initial assumption — is that easy living ought to translate straightforwardly into greater Torah study. The reality is more complicated. Ease can breed complacency just as readily as it enables devotion. This is the same irony captured in the famous Chassidic story, attributed to the Berditchever Rebbe, in which a *Litvak* and a *Chassid* encounter a wagon-driver (*ba'al agala*) repairing his wheel, hands filthy with mud, all the while *davening*. The *Litvak* sees a man too distracted and dirty to be praying properly. The *Chassid* looks up and says: *Ribono Shel Olam*, your people — even in the mud, even in their difficulties — are still turning to You. The Babylonians, stomachache and all, are a version of that wagon-driver. The circumstances aren't ideal, but the commitment is genuine and perhaps all the more impressive for it.

## Beis HaMikdash

### Yaakov's House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָלְכוּ עַמִּים רַבִּים וְאָמְרוּ לְכוּ וְנַעֲלֶה אֶל הַר ה׳ אֶל בֵּית אֱלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב וְגוֹ׳״. אֱלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב, וְלֹא אֱלֹהֵי אַבְרָהָם וְיִצְחָק? אֶלָּא, לֹא כְּאַבְרָהָם שֶׁכָּתוּב בּוֹ ״הַר״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר יֵאָמֵר הַיּוֹם בְּהַר ה׳ יֵרָאֶה״, וְלֹא כְּיִצְחָק שֶׁכָּתוּב בּוֹ ״שָׂדֶה״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֵּצֵא יִצְחָק לָשׂוּחַ בַּשָּׂדֶה״, אֶלָּא כְּיַעֲקֹב שֶׁקְּרָאוֹ ״בַּיִת״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקְרָא אֶת שֵׁם הַמָּקוֹם הַהוּא בֵּית אֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And many peoples shall go and say: Go and let us go up to the mountain of the Lord, to the house of the God of Jacob;** and He will teach us of His ways, and we will walk in His paths" (Isaiah 2:3)? The Gemara notes that Jacob is the only Patriarch mentioned and asks: Is **He the God of Jacob and not the God of Abraham and Isaac?** **Rather,** the verse specifically mentions Jacob to allude to the fact that the Temple will ultimately be described in the same way that Jacob referred to it. It will **not** be referred to **as** it was referred to by **Abraham. It is written of him** that when he prayed at the location of the Temple mountain, he called it **mount, as it is stated: "As it is said on this day: On the mount where the Lord is seen"** (Genesis 22:14). **And** it will **not** be referred to **as** it was referred to by **Isaac. It is written of him** that he called the location of the Temple **field** when he prayed there, **as it is stated: "And Isaac went out to meditate in the field"** (Genesis 24:63). **Rather,** it will be described **as** it was referred to by **Jacob, who called it house, as it is stated: "And he called the name of that place Beth-El"** (Genesis 28:19), which means house of God.

The Gemara continues with a teaching of Rabbi Elazar, who asks about the verse in which the nations of the world declare: *ve-halchu amim rabim ve-amru lechu ve-na'aleh el har Hashem el beis Elokei Ya'akov* — many peoples will come and say, let us go up to the mountain of God, to the house of the God of Yaakov. The question is pointed: why specifically the God of Yaakov, and not Avraham or Yitzchak? Rabbi Elazar explains that the distinction tracks how each of the Patriarchs referred to the site of the *Beis HaMikdash*. By Avraham, the verse says *asher ye'amer hayom be-har Hashem yeira'eh* — he called it a *har*, a mountain. By Yitzchak, the verse says *vayeitzei Yitzchak lasu'ach ba-sadeh* — his encounter with the Divine was in a *sadeh*, a field. But Yaakov called it a *bayis*, a house: *vayikra shem ha-makom ha-hu Beis El*. It is Yaakov's terminology — *bayis* — that the nations invoke when they speak of the destination of their future pilgrimage.

### Kibbutz Galuyos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גָּדוֹל קִבּוּץ גָּלִיּוֹת כַּיּוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרְאוּ בּוֹ שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִקְבְּצוּ בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה וּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל יַחְדָּו וְשָׂמוּ לָהֶם רֹאשׁ אֶחָד וְעָלוּ מִן הָאָרֶץ כִּי גָדוֹל יוֹם יִזְרְעֶאל״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי עֶרֶב וַיְהִי בֹקֶר יוֹם אֶחָד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The day of the **ingathering of exiles is as great as the day on which heaven and earth were created.** This is derived by means of a verbal analogy between the word day in these two contexts, **as it is stated** concerning the ingathering of exiles: **"And the children of Judea and the children of Israel shall be gathered together, and they shall appoint themselves one head, and shall go up out of the land; for great shall be the day of Jezreel"** (Hosea 2:2), **and it is written** in the narrative of Creation: **"And there was evening and there was morning, one day"** (Genesis 1:5).

The Gemara adds a related teaching from Rabbi Yochanan: *gadol kibbutz galuyos ke-yom she-nivreu vo shamayim va-aretz* — the day of the ingathering of the exiles will be as great as the day on which heaven and earth were created; it is nothing less than a new creation. The source is the verse in Hoshea: *ve-nikbetzu bnei Yehuda u-vnei Yisrael yachdav... ki gadol yom Yizre'el*, and this is connected to *ve-yehi erev va-yehi voker yom echad* — the emphasis on *yom*, day, evoking the language of creation itself. This teaching brings to a satisfying close the discussion that had begun earlier with Hoshea, anchoring it in the imagery of a renewed world.

### Bayis vs. Mountain

The distinction between *har*, *sadeh*, and *bayis* is worth dwelling on, because it goes to the heart of what the *Beis HaMikdash* represents as a theological idea. Rashi explains *bayis* simply as *makom meyushav* — a settled, inhabited place — though this leaves the deeper significance somewhat underarticulated.

The theological paradox at stake is one that Shlomo HaMelech himself named explicitly at the Temple's dedication: *hinei ha-shamayim u-shmei ha-shamayim lo yechalchelucha v'af ki ha-bayis ha-zeh asher baniti* — even the heavens and the highest heavens cannot contain You, and yet here is this house I have built. How does one speak of a particular location for an infinite God? If God is everywhere, what is the meaning of a specific place of presence? And yet if God is everywhere in an undifferentiated way, it can become experientially equivalent to God being nowhere — too abstract, too vast to relate to. The proliferation of altars *al he-harim ha-gevohim v'al ha-gevaot v'tachat kol etz ra'anan* — on the high mountains and the hills and under every leafy tree — reflects one answer to that problem: you encounter the divine in nature, at particular striking locations. Avraham at the *har* and Yitzchak in the *sadeh* represent encounters of that kind, real and meaningful, but diffuse.

The *chiddush* of the *bayis* is different. The verse says *ve-asu li mikdash ve-shachanti b'tocham* — make Me a sanctuary and I will dwell among them. The *bayis* is not a natural landmark but a human construction, a space that people actively build. And there is something remarkable about that. Har Sinai, where the Divine presence was arguably felt most intensely in all of history, has no lasting *kedusha*. The site of the *Mikdash* does. The things human beings build and consecrate, the spaces they deliberately create and dedicate, those carry a *kedusha* that endures in a way that natural encounters do not. This is *Hashem amar lishkon ba-arafel* — God chose to concentrate His presence in a space that is in some sense opaque, bounded, constructed. That concentration, that focus, is not a limitation of God but a gift to human beings: one place toward which all prayers and energies and intentions converge, one location that makes the unity of God tangible and relatable. The very specificity of the *bayis*, which might at first seem to work against a universal theology, turns out to be what enables it.

## Pesach Membership

### Orphan & Guardians

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתוֹם שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ עָלָיו אַפּוֹטְרוֹפְּסִין וְכוּ׳. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ יֵשׁ בְּרֵירָה. אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: ״שֶׂה לַבָּיִת״ — מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: In the case of **an orphan** with multiple guardians, if each of his **guardians slaughtered** a Paschal lamb **on his behalf,** he may **eat in** whichever **place he wishes.** The Gemara suggests: **You can learn from it** that **there is retroactive clarification,** and one's ultimate decision as to which group he wishes to be part of retroactively indicates that from the outset he was registered in that group. This is problematic, as no halakhic conclusion has been reached in the matter of retroactive clarification. The Gemara therefore rejects this suggestion: **Rabbi Zeira said:** The *halakha* in the mishna is not based on retroactive clarification, but rather on the following principle: The verse states: "They shall take to them every man a lamb, according to their fathers' houses, **a lamb for a household"** (Exodus 12:3), indicating that a minor's membership in the household is sufficient for him to be registered in the household's Paschal lamb **in any case,** even without his agreement.

The Gemara now pivots to more strictly *halachic* terrain, returning to the *Mishnah*. The case is that of an orphan whose two guardians (*apitropsin*) each slaughtered a *korban Pesach* on his behalf. The orphan may eat from whichever of the two he chooses. The Gemara initially raises the question of *yesh breirah* — whether his retroactive choice can determine which *korban* was slaughtered on his behalf. Rabbi Zeira resolves this by invoking the principle of *seh l'vayit* — a lamb per household. The Torah frames the *Pesach* obligation in terms of the household unit, *seh l'veit avot seh l'vayit*, and that objective membership in a household is what determines inclusion, not individual consent or advance designation. The orphan is objectively part of both households and therefore was legitimately included in each *Pesach*. His choice between them is not an act of retroactive *breirah* but a selection from among memberships that were always genuinely his.

### Consent Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״שֶׂה לַבָּיִת״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאָדָם מֵבִיא וְשׁוֹחֵט עַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים וְעַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הַכְּנַעֲנִים, בֵּין מִדַּעְתָּן בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתָּן. אֲבָל אֵינוֹ שׁוֹחֵט עַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים וְעַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הָעִבְרִים וְעַל יַד אִשְׁתּוֹ, אֶלָּא מִדַּעְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A lamb for a household teaches that a person brings and slaughters** a Paschal lamb **on behalf of his minor son and daughter and on behalf of his Canaanite slave and maidservant, whether with their consent or without their consent.** Since they do not have a legal identity independent of their household membership, their membership is sufficient to include them, even without their consent. **However, one may not slaughter** the Paschal lamb **on behalf of his adult son or daughter, or on behalf of his Hebrew slave and maidservant, or on behalf of his wife unless** he has **their consent.** Since they have legal identities independent of their household membership, their inclusion can be achieved only through their consent.

A *baraita* spells out the framework in detail. A man may slaughter the *Pesach* on behalf of his minor sons and daughters, and on behalf of his male and female Canaanite slaves (*avdo ve-shifchato ha-kena'anim*), *bein mida'atam bein shelo mida'atam* — whether with their knowledge or without. He may not, however, slaughter on behalf of his adult children, his Jewish slaves (*avdo ve-shifchato ha-ivrim*), or his wife, without their explicit buy-in. A second *baraita* makes the same point from the reverse direction, establishing the two categories clearly: those who have no independent voice in the matter — minor children and Canaanite slaves — and those who must actively consent.

### Household vs. Consent

The Gemara in *Nedarim* makes explicit what is implicit here: the principle of *zachin* — doing something for a person's benefit without their knowledge — does not apply to *korban Pesach* membership. One cannot simply enroll someone in a *chaburah* as a favor. Membership in the *Pesach* group requires conscious, active participation, with the one exception being the case of minor children and slaves, whose inclusion is governed not by the principle of *zachin* but by the Torah's own category of *seh l'vayit*. They belong to the household as an objective fact, not as a matter of consent.

The interplay here between the model of *bayis* and the model of *chaburah* is fundamental. The Torah frames the *Pesach* in terms of the household; the Sages developed the concept of *chaburah* — a fellowship group whose membership need not track biological or domestic relationships. But how much does the original idea of *bayis* continue to operate even within the framework of *chaburah*? For minor children and slaves, it operates entirely, overriding even explicit objection. *Tosafot* notes, with reference to a discussion in Bava Metzia concerning *metzia*, that even an adult child who is financially dependent on his father (*samuch al shulchan aviv*) retains full independence for purposes of *Pesach*: *gedolim peshita d'yesh beyadam limchot afilu semuchin al shulchan avihen*. Once a child has reached *Bar Mitzvah*, no degree of financial dependence reduces that person to the status of a minor here. The criterion of *shelo mida'ato* — without their knowledge — must therefore refer to actual minors, whose inability to object effectively is taken for granted.

### Knowledge Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אֶלָּא מִדַּעְתָּן״ — לָאו דְּאָמְרִי אִין, אֶלָּא בִּסְתָמָא, לְאַפּוֹקֵי הֵיכָא דַּאֲמוּר לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves this difficulty: **What** does the first clause mean when it teaches that one may slaughter the Paschal lamb **only with their consent? It is not** referring to a case **where they** explicitly **said yes,** thereby clarifying their intent; **rather,** it is referring to **an indeterminate case** where they did not explicitly agree, but their implicit consent is presumed. The ruling of the *baraita* comes **to exclude** only the case **where they** explicitly **said no,** clearly excluding themselves from their master's Paschal lamb.

The apparent contradiction within the *baraita* is carefully resolved by the Gemara. The *baraita* says that for the independent categories — the wife, adult children, and Jewish slaves — the husband or father may only slaughter *mida'atan*, with their knowledge. But the *baraita* also suggests that in a case where no objection is raised, the wife is assumed to be included with her husband. These two statements seem to be in tension: if *mida'atan* is required, how can the silent wife be presumed included?

The Gemara resolves this by explaining that *mida'atan* does not mean active verbal consent. It means the absence of explicit protest. In a *stam* case — where nothing has been said either way — the wife is assumed to consent, just as the opening *Mishnah* assumed that a wife would ordinarily want to celebrate *Yom Tov* with her husband rather than her parents. The requirement of *da'at* is real, but the *da'at* can be presumed from silence. What the requirement excludes is the case where she has actively indicated otherwise.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כֵּיוָן שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ אֵין לְךָ מִיחוּי גָּדוֹל מִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** resolved this difficulty and **said: Since they slaughtered** their own Paschal lambs, **you do not have a protest greater than this.** The act of slaughtering their own Paschal lambs clearly demonstrates they intend to partake of their own lambs and do not intend to be included in the master's group.

Rava then addresses the case where the wife herself has slaughtered her own *korban Pesach*: *kivan she-shachtu ein lecha michuy gadol mizeh* — the act of *shechitah* itself constitutes the clearest possible statement of independent intention. She need not verbally protest; her action speaks for itself. This resolves the apparent contradiction at the end of the *baraita*, which says that while minor children and slaves who slaughter their own *korban* are nonetheless subsumed under the master's or father's *Pesach*, the wife is not — because her *shechitah* is itself an act of *michuy*, of opting out.

The underlying picture that emerges is one with two fully distinct categories and perhaps a third. Minor children and Canaanite slaves have no independent standing; the household defines them completely, and even active protest cannot alter that. Adults — wives, adult children, Jewish slaves — do have independent standing, and their buy-in is genuinely required; but that buy-in can be presumed from silence, and only an affirmative act of opting out removes them from the household's *Pesach*. Whether this presumption for the wife and adult children means they occupy a conceptually intermediate category — more firmly attached to the *bayis* than a complete stranger, requiring less explicit buy-in than a friend or neighbor — remains an open question that the Gemara does not fully resolve.

## Two Partners' Slave

### Rami's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶבֶד שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין וְכוּ׳. רָמֵי לֵיהּ רַב עֵינָא סָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: תְּנַן, עֶבֶד שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין לֹא יֹאכַל מִשֶּׁל שְׁנֵיהֶן. וְהָתַנְיָא: רָצָה — מִזֶּה אוֹכֵל, רָצָה — מִזֶּה אוֹכֵל!
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **A slave** jointly owned **by two partners** may not eat from the lamb of either of them unless it was stipulated beforehand from whose lamb he will partake. **Rav Eina the Elder raised a contradiction before Rav Naḥman. We learned** in the mishna: **A slave** jointly owned **by two partners** may not eat from the lamb of either of them. **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If **he wanted** to, **he** may **eat from this** one, and if **he wanted** to, **he** may **eat from that** one?

The Gemara presents a challenge from Rav Eina Saba to Rav Nachman. The *Mishnah* states that a slave owned jointly by two partners (*eved shel shnei shutfin*) cannot eat from either partner's *Pesach*. Yet a *baraita* teaches the opposite: *ratzah mizeh ochel ratzah mizeh ochel* — he may eat from whichever of the two he wishes. The contradiction seems direct.

### Kapdan Partners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עֵינָא סָבָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: פַּתְיָא אוּכָּמָא, מִינִּי וּמִינָּךְ תִּסְתַּיֵּים שְׁמַעְתְּתָא. מַתְנִיתִין — בִּדְקָפְדִי אַהֲדָדֵי, בָּרַיְיתָא — דְּלָא קָפְדִי אַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Eina the Elder, and some say** that he called him **black pot [*patya*],** a term of endearment for a scholar who works hard studying Torah: **From me and from you,** clarification of this ***halakha* will be concluded. The mishna** is referring to a case where the partners **are exacting with each other.** Therefore, presumably, neither partner will allow his half of the slave to partake from his partner's Paschal lamb. **The *baraita*** is referring to a case where **they are not exacting with each other.** In that case, the slave may eat from the Paschal lamb of whichever partner he chooses.

Rav Nachman's resolution — introduced with the playful exchange *mini u-minach tistayem shmai'ta* — is that the two sources are addressing different circumstances. The *Mishnah* speaks of partners who are hostile to one another (*bid'kapdi ahadadei*): each partner refuses to allow the slave to derive benefit from the other, and since each can invoke his ownership to exclude the slave from the other's domain, the slave is caught with no valid option. The *baraita*, by contrast, speaks of partners who are on good terms (*d'lo kapdi ahadadei*): neither objects to the slave participating in the other's *Pesach*, so his membership in both households functions as it does for the orphan with two guardians, and he may choose freely between them. The *chiddush* when they are hostile is that the power of each partner to deny benefits flowing from his ownership effectively removes the slave from the other's household for these purposes.

## Half-Slave Status

### Shammai-Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין לֹא יֹאכַל מִשֶּׁל רַבּוֹ וְכוּ׳. מִשֶּׁל רַבּוֹ הוּא דְּלֹא יֹאכַל, אֲבָל מִשֶּׁל עַצְמוֹ — יֹאכַל. וְהָא תַּנְיָא: לֹא יֹאכַל לֹא מִשֶּׁלּוֹ וְלֹא מִשֶּׁל רַבּוֹ. לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — כְּמִשְׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, כָּאן — כְּמִשְׁנָה אַחֲרוֹנָה. דִּתְנַן: מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין — עוֹבֵד אֶת רַבּוֹ יוֹם אֶחָד וְאֶת עַצְמוֹ יוֹם אֶחָד, דִּבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: תִּקַּנְתֶּם אֶת רַבּוֹ, וְאֶת עַצְמוֹ לֹא תִּקַּנְתֶּם.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **One who is half slave and half free man may not eat from his master's** Paschal lamb. It is specifically **from his master's** lamb that **he may not eat; however, from his own** lamb **he may eat. But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: He **may eat neither from his own nor from his master's** Paschal lamb? The Gemara answers that this is **not difficult: Here,** the *baraita* that rules that the half slave may partake neither of his own nor of his master's lamb, **is in accordance with the original** version of the **mishna,** which cites Beit Hillel's opinion that the master retains his rights to the half slave. **There,** the mishna that allows the half slave to partake of his own lamb, **is in accordance with the ultimate** version of the **mishna,** which cites Beit Hillel's revised opinion, according to which the status of the half slave is altered such that he is considered like a free man as pertains to his inclusion in a group for the Paschal lamb. **As we learned** in a mishna: **One who is half slave and half free man serves his master one day and himself one day;** this is **the statement of Beit Hillel. Beit Shammai say:** **You have remedied** the situation of **his master,** who benefits fully from all his rights to the slave, but **his own** situation **you have not remedied.**

The *Mishnah* presents the case of the *mi she-chetzyo eved ve-chetzyo ben chorin* — the person who is half slave and half free. Such a person cannot eat from his master's *Pesach*, since the master may reasonably object to including the free half of this individual in his *chaburah*; and he cannot bring his own *Pesach*, since his master has not authorized the slave half of him to participate in an independent group. Beit Hillel's original position — *mishna rishona* — was a practical accommodation: the person works one day for his master and one day for himself, dividing his labor accordingly. Beit Shammai objected.

### Forced Freedom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׂא שִׁפְחָה אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל — שֶׁכְּבָר חֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין, לִישָּׂא בַּת חוֹרִין אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל — שֶׁעֲדַיִין חֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד. יִבְטַל, וַהֲלֹא לֹא נִבְרָא הָעוֹלָם אֶלָּא לִפְרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תֹהוּ בְרָאָהּ (אֶלָּא) לָשֶׁבֶת יְצָרָהּ״. אֶלָּא, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם כּוֹפִין אֶת רַבּוֹ וְעוֹשֶׂה אוֹתוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין, וְכוֹתֵב שְׁטָר עַל חֲצִי דָמָיו. וְחָזְרוּ בֵּית הִילֵּל לְהוֹרוֹת כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **He is not able to marry a maidservant, since half of him is already free,** and a free Jew may not marry a Canaanite maidservant. **He** is also **not able to marry a free woman, since half of him is still a slave,** and a Jewish woman may not marry a Canaanite slave. And if you say **he should be idle** and not marry, **but is it not** true that **the world was created only for procreation, as it is stated: "He did not create it to be a waste; He formed it to be inhabited"** (Isaiah 45:18)? **Rather, for the improvement of the world we force his master to make him a free man, and** the slave **writes a bill** accepting his responsibility to pay **half his value** to his master. This was the original version of the mishna. The ultimate version of the mishna records the retraction of Beit Hillel: **And Beit Hillel retracted** its position **and ruled like Beit Shammai.**

Beit Shammai's argument is striking in its moral directness. *Tikantem et rabo ve-et atzmo lo tikantem* — you have provided a solution for the master but not for the person himself. The difficulty is fundamental: *lisha shifcha eino yachol she-kevar chetzyo ben chorin* — he cannot marry a maidservant because he is already half free; *lisha bat chorin eino yachol she-kevar chetzyo eved* — he cannot marry a free woman because he is still half a slave. He is caught in a liminal status that makes it impossible for him to fulfill *pirya ve-rivya*. And the world, the Torah insists, was not created for desolation: *lo tohu vera'ah lashevet yetzarah*. Therefore, Beit Shammai rules, *kofin et rabo ve-oseh oto ben chorin* — we compel the master who still holds half of this person in bondage to emancipate him, and the former slave assumes a financial debt for half his assessed value. His legal status remains, in some technical sense, transitional, but the master's practical control over him has ended. He is for all operative purposes a free man.

### Agunah Parallel

*Ve-chazru Beit Hillel le-horot ke-Beit Shammai* — Beit Hillel ultimately reversed course and accepted Beit Shammai's ruling. The case of the half-slave carries a direct and powerful resonance for the situation of the *agunah* — the woman trapped in a marriage she cannot exit, unable to remarry, suspended in a comparable limbo. If the Torah and *Chazal* were willing to override the master's property rights and compel manumission to ensure that a slave could fulfill his capacity for life and family, the argument for doing everything halachically possible to free a Jewish woman from her marital limbo is at least as compelling.

The Gemara also notes an interesting debate among the *Rishonim* arising from the specific language invoked here. Beit Shammai grounds the ruling not in the narrow *mitzvah* of *peru u-revu* but in the broader value of *lo tohu bera'ah lashevet yetzarah*. This raises the question of whether a *eved* is obligated in *peru u-revu* at all — the Gemara elsewhere suggests he has the *mitzvot* of a woman, and women are generally not obligated in *peru u-revu*. Some *Rishonim* argue that the resort to *lo tohu bera'ah* proves the slave lacks the *mitzvah* as such but is still subject to this overarching value of settling the world. Others argue the *mitzvah* is present but that invoking this larger value makes the case for compulsion more forceful than the *mitzvah* alone would. *Tosafot*, who align slaves and women in this regard, develop the further question of whether *lo tohu bera'ah* generates some form of obligation for women as well — a discussion that opens into substantial terrain and that the shiur sets aside for another time.

## Master's Pesach

### Unspecified Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לְעַבְדּוֹ צֵא וּשְׁחוֹט עָלַי אֶת הַפֶּסַח, שָׁחַט גְּדִי — יֹאכַל, שָׁחַט טָלֶה — יֹאכַל, שָׁחַט גְּדִי וְטָלֶה — יֹאכַל מִן הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who says to his slave: Go and slaughter the Paschal offering on my behalf,** but does not specify which type of animal to slaughter, the *halakha* is as follows: If the slave **slaughtered a kid,** his master **may eat** it; if **he slaughtered a lamb,** his master **may eat** it. If the slave **slaughtered** both **a kid and a lamb,** his master **should eat from the first one** that was slaughtered; the second is invalid and should be burned.

The *Mishnah* turns to a master who instructs his slave to slaughter the *Pesach* for him without specifying which animal. If the slave slaughters a goat (*gedi*), the master eats it — even if he is known to prefer lamb. If the slave slaughters a lamb (*taleh*), the master eats it — even if he is known to prefer goat. The *chiddush*, made explicit by the Gemara, is that since the master gave no specification, his personal preferences are simply irrelevant: *af al gav deragil betleh* and *af al gav deragil begdi* — regardless of habitual preference, the unspecified instruction gives the slave full discretion.

### Two Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט גְּדִי וְטָלֶה — יֹאכַל מִן הָרִאשׁוֹן. וְהָא תַנְיָא: אֵין נִמְנִין עַל שְׁנֵי פְסָחִים כְּאֶחָד!
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: If the slave **slaughtered a kid and a lamb,** his master **should eat from the first one** the slave slaughtered. To allow for the possibility of eating from whichever one is slaughtered first, the master must have been registered to eat from either animal. The Gemara cites a *baraita* that appears to contradict this: **Wasn't it taught: One may not be registered for two Paschal offerings at once?**

If the slave, uncertain which animal the master wanted, slaughtered both, the master eats from the first one slaughtered (*yochal min ha-rishon*). The Gemara initially questions this: given the rule that one cannot be registered as a member of two *Pesachs* simultaneously (*ein nimnin al shnei pesachim k'echad*), how does it follow that one simply eats the first? *Tosafot* clarifies the issue: it is not merely that two *Pesachs* were slaughtered, but that there is a question about the relationship between the order of designation and the order of *shechita*. The resolution — as the Gemara indicates — is that the *Mishnah* refers to a king and queen, or people in a comparable position, and the ruling flows from their particular circumstances, as will become clear below.

### Stipulation Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁכַח מָה אָמַר לוֹ רַבּוֹ, כֵּיצַד יַעֲשֶׂה? יִשְׁחַט טָלֶה וּגְדִי, וְיֹאמַר: אִם גְּדִי אֲמַר לִי רַבִּי — גְּדִי שֶׁלּוֹ וְטָלֶה שֶׁלִּי, וְאִם טָלֶה אֲמַר לִי רַבִּי — הַטָּלֶה שֶׁלּוֹ וּגְדִי שֶׁלִּי.
>
> **Translation:** If the master had stated explicitly which type of animal to slaughter, but the slave **forgot what his master said to him, what should he do? He should slaughter** both **a lamb and a kid and say** the following stipulation: **If my master said to me** that I should slaughter **a kid, the kid is** for **his** Paschal offering **and the lamb is** for **mine; and if my master said to me** that I should slaughter **a lamb, the lamb is** for **his** Paschal offering **and the kid is** for **mine.** In this way, once the master ultimately clarifies what he had originally said, both animals may be used accordingly.

If the slave forgot entirely what his master had specified, the Gemara teaches that he should slaughter both animals but make a careful stipulation: *im gedi amar li rabbi, gedi shelo ve-taleh sheli; ve-im taleh amar li rabbi, ha-taleh shelo ve-gedi sheli* — if my master told me a goat, then the goat is his and the lamb is mine; if he told me a lamb, then the lamb is his and the goat is mine. With this stipulation, both animals are valid *korbanot*; the only remaining uncertainty is which belongs to whom. The master is then consulted, and the question is resolved.

The Gemara raises an obvious difficulty: *mah she-kanah eved kanah rabbo* — whatever a slave acquires belongs to his master. How can the slave claim that one of the animals is *his* for these purposes? 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הוֹלֵךְ אֵצֶל רוֹעֶה הָרָגִיל רַבּוֹ אֶצְלוֹ, דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּתַקַּנְתָּא דְרַבֵּיהּ, וּמַקְנֵי לֵיהּ חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לְרַבּוֹ רְשׁוּת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** the slave can retain ownership of the lamb in the following manner: The slave **goes to a shepherd that his master regularly patronizes,** since it can be assumed such a person **is pleased** to find **a solution for his master.** The shepherd **grants** the slave **ownership of one of** the animals **on condition that his master has no rights to it.** With this condition, the slave is able to retain ownership of the animal, thus allowing him to effectively make the stipulation described in the mishna.

Abaye's resolution is ingenious: the slave should go to a shepherd who regularly does business with the master, one who has an interest in everything working out well (*ve-neicha lei be-takkanta de-rabbeh*). That shepherd gives the slave one of the animals *al menat she-ein le-rabbo reshut bo* — on the explicit stipulation that the master has no ownership claim over it. And this, the Gemara allows, is halachically effective: under ordinary circumstances, property given to a slave passes automatically to the master; but if the giver explicitly stipulates that the master shall have no claim, the property can remain with the slave. The independent ownership is thus created through the explicit *tenai* of the shepherd.

### Both Forgot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁכַח רַבּוֹ מָה אָמַר לוֹ, שְׁנֵיהֶן יֵצְאוּ לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה, וּפְטוּרִין מִלַּעֲשׂוֹת פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** If **his master** also **forgot what he said to him,** neither animal may be used, since it has not been clarified which of the animals the slave and master are registered for. Therefore, **both of them,** the lamb and the kid, **go out to the place** designated **for burning,** in accordance with the *halakha* pertaining to offerings that may not be eaten. However, despite this, both the master and slave are **exempt from observing the second *Pesaḥ*** if the blood of the animals has already been applied to the altar before the master forgot.

Finally, if both master and slave have forgotten what was specified, both *korbanot* are nonetheless valid, and both master and slave are exempt from *Pesach Sheni* — because when each animal was slaughtered and its blood thrown, it was fit to be eaten. The failure to actually eat the meat does not retroactively invalidate the *korban*. What matters is the fitness for eating at the time the *korban* was brought.

## King & Queen

### Royal Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין נִמְנִין עַל שְׁנֵי פְסָחִים כְּאֶחָד, וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּמֶלֶךְ וּמַלְכָּה שֶׁאָמְרוּ לְעַבְדֵיהֶם: צְאוּ וְשַׁחֲטוּ עָלֵינוּ אֶת הַפֶּסַח, וְיָצְאוּ וְשָׁחֲטוּ עֲלֵיהֶן שְׁנֵי פְסָחִים. בָּאוּ וְשָׁאֲלוּ אֶת הַמֶּלֶךְ, אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ וְשַׁאֲלוּ אֶת הַמַּלְכָּה. בָּאוּ וְשָׁאֲלוּ מִן הַמַּלְכָּה, אָמְרָה לָהֶם: לְכוּ וְשַׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, **it was taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not register for two Paschal offerings at once. And there was an incident involving a king and queen who said to their slaves: Go and slaughter the Paschal offering on our behalf. And they went out and slaughtered two Paschal offerings on their behalf. They came and asked the king** which one he wished to eat. **He said to them: Go and ask the queen. They came and asked the queen. She said to them: Go and ask Rabban Gamliel** to rule which one should be used.

The Gemara now tells the story underlying the *Mishnah*'s ruling about two animals slaughtered without specification. A king and queen instructed their servants to slaughter the *Pesach* for them. The servants slaughtered two — one a lamb, one a goat — and came to ask which was which and how to proceed. The king deferred to the queen, whose knowledge of *halacha* was clearly superior. The queen in turn directed them to Rabban Gamliel.

### Don't Care Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ וְשָׁאֲלוּ אוֹתוֹ, אָמַר לָהֶם: מַלְכָּה וּמֶלֶךְ דְּדַעְתָּן קַלָּה עֲלֵיהֶן — יֹאכְלוּ מִן הָרִאשׁוֹן. אֲנַן לֹא נֹאכַל לֹא מִן הָרִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא מִן הַשֵּׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **They came and asked him,** and **he said to them: A king and queen, who are easily accepting** of whichever foods their slaves choose to present to them, **should eat from the first one** that was slaughtered, since it is presumed they wished to be registered for any animal the slaves selected. But **we,** the general populace, who have limited supplies of food and so are particular about what food is served to us, **would not eat from the first or from the second,** since it is not permitted to be registered for two Paschal lambs at once.

Rabban Gamliel's ruling turned on a single observation about the king and queen: *kalla aleihem* — they simply do not care. People of their station have access to every type of delicacy; whether tonight's *Pesach* is lamb or goat is a matter of complete indifference to them. Since they have no preference, there is no unresolved question of designation to worry about. *Yokhlu min ha-rishon* — they eat from whichever was slaughtered first. For ordinary people, by contrast, who do have preferences and who would have had something specific in mind when they issued the instruction, neither animal can be definitively identified as the intended one, and so they would be unable to eat from either. The *Mishnah*'s ruling that one eats *min ha-rishon* thus applies specifically to cases — like the king and queen — where the owner is genuinely indifferent to which animal is used.

### Lizard Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוּב פַּעַם אַחַת נִמְצֵאת הַלְטָאָה בְּבֵית הַמִּטְבָּחַיִם וּבִקְּשׁוּ לְטַמֵּא כָּל הַסְּעוּדָה כּוּלָּהּ. בָּאוּ וְשָׁאֲלוּ אֶת הַמֶּלֶךְ, אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ וְשַׁאֲלוּ אֶת הַמַּלְכָּה. בָּאוּ וְשָׁאֲלוּ אֶת הַמַּלְכָּה, אָמְרָה לָהֶם: לְכוּ וְשַׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. בָּאוּ וְשָׁאֲלוּ אוֹתוֹ, אָמַר לָהֶם: בֵּית הַמִּטְבָּחַיִם רוֹתֵחַ אוֹ צוֹנֵן? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: רוֹתֵחַ. אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ וְהָטִילוּ עָלֶיהָ כּוֹס שֶׁל צוֹנֵן. הָלְכוּ וְהֵטִילוּ עָלֶיהָ כּוֹס שֶׁל צוֹנֵן וְרִיחֲשָׁה, וְטִהֵר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל כׇּל הַסְּעוּדָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And another time** there was a similar incident involving the king, queen, and Rabban Gamliel. It happened that a dead **lizard was found in the kitchen** of the royal house. Since a lizard is one of the creeping animals whose carcasses impart ritual impurity upon contact, **they wanted to pronounce the entire meal ritually impure. They came and asked the king. He said to them: Go and ask the queen. They came and asked the queen. She said to them: Go and ask Rabban Gamliel** to rule on the matter. **They came and asked him,** and **he said to them: Is the kitchen boiling or cold? They said to him:** It is **boiling. He said to them: Go and pour a cup of cold** liquid **upon** the lizard. **They went and poured a cup of cold** liquid **on it and it quivered,** demonstrating that it was still alive. That being the case, **Rabban Gamliel pronounced the entire meal to be ritually pure,** as a live creeping animal does not impart ritual impurity.

A second anecdote follows. On another occasion, a lizard (*lita'ah*) was found in the royal kitchen. The king and queen apparently ate their non-sacred food in a state of *tahara* (*chulin be-tahara*), and so there was concern that the discovery of a potentially dead reptile — a source of *tumah* — would render the entire meal impure. Again the king deferred to the queen, and the queen to Rabban Gamliel. Rabban Gamliel asked whether the kitchen was hot or cold. The servants said hot. He instructed them to throw a cup of cold water on the lizard. They did, and it began to thrash — it was not dead, merely prostrated by the heat. Rabban Gamliel declared the entire meal pure.

### Dependency Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא מֶלֶךְ תָּלוּי בַּמַּלְכָּה, וְנִמְצֵאתָ מַלְכָּה תְּלוּיָה בְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, נִמְצֵאת כׇּל הַסְּעוּדָה תְּלוּיָה בְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It turns out that the king is dependent on the queen, and it turns out that the queen is dependent on Rabban Gamliel. And** so **it turns out that the entire** royal **meal is dependent upon Rabban Gamliel.**

The Gemara closes the episode with a wry observation: *nimtza melech talui be-malka, ve-nimtza malka teluyah be-Rabban Gamliel, nimtza kol ha-se'udah teluyah be-Rabban Gamliel* — the king turned out to depend on the queen, and the queen on Rabban Gamliel, so the whole meal depended on Rabban Gamliel. The surface meaning is literal — the *halakhic* validity of the meal was determined by Rabban Gamliel's ruling at each turn — but the deeper point is clear: the entire world, kings and queens included, depends ultimately on the *talmidei chakhamim* and their mastery of Torah.

The episode also touches, in passing, on the phrase *nashim da'atan kallah*. Here the term is clearly not pejorative. The king and queen are described as *kalla aleihem* in the sense that food preferences are simply not weighty matters to them — they have everything they could want. If the expression *nashim da'atan kallah* carries that same valence in other contexts, it would mean not that women reason poorly but that they do not treat certain things as weighty decisions requiring careful deliberation — a reading that is quite different from the more negative sense the phrase sometimes bears.

## Before Zerika

### Abaye's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁכַח רַבּוֹ מָה שֶּׁאָמַר לוֹ וְכוּ׳. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁשָּׁכַח אַחַר זְרִיקָה, דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּאִיזְּרִיק דָּם — הֲוָה חֲזֵי לַאֲכִילָה. אֲבָל שָׁכַח לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, דְּכִי אִיזְּרִיק דָּם לָא הֲוָה חֲזֵי לַאֲכִילָה — חַיָּיבִין לַעֲשׂוֹת פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: If a slave forgot which animal his master had specified and therefore offered a lamb and a kid with a stipulation, and **his master** also **forgot what he said to him,** neither animal may be used. Instead, both are burned. However, despite this, both the master and the slave are exempt from observing the second *Pesaḥ*. **Abaye said: They taught** that they are exempt from the second *Pesaḥ* in a case where the master **forgot only after** the **sprinkling** of the blood on the altar, **for at the time the blood** of each animal **was sprinkled, it was** still **fit to be eaten,** since the master still knew which animal he desired. Both animals are therefore considered to have been offered properly, and so both master and slave are exempt from the second *Pesaḥ*. **But** if the slave **had** already **forgotten** which animal he specified **before the sprinkling,** so **that when the blood was sprinkled** the offering **was not fit to be eaten,** the animals are not considered to have been properly offered. Consequently, the master and slave **are** both **obligated to observe** the **second *Pesaḥ*.**

The Gemara now presents a teaching of Abaye that sharply qualifies the ruling about the slave who forgot what his master said. The *Mishnah* states that in a case where both master and slave have forgotten, they are both *yotzei* and exempt from *Pesach Sheni*. Abaye specifies: *lo shanu ela she-shachach achar zerika* — this applies only if the forgetting occurred after the *zrikas ha-dam*, the throwing of the blood on the altar. If neither master nor slave knows whose animal is whose at that point, the *korban* is nonetheless valid because at the decisive moment of *zerika* each animal was fit to be eaten.

### Fitness at Zerika

But *aval shachach lifnei zerika* — if the forgetting occurred before the blood-throwing — neither party knows at the critical moment of *zerika* whose animal is designated for whom. *De-chi izreik dam lo havi chazi la-achila* — when the blood was thrown, the animal was not genuinely fit to be eaten, because no one could identify it as their own. In that case, *chayavin la-asot Pesach Sheni* — they are obligated to bring a *Pesach Sheni*, since the first *Pesach* was never fully valid as a *korban* that was *rauy la-achila* at the time of *zerika*.

## Mixed Skins

### Blemish Found

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַבָּרַיְיתָא: חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ עוֹרוֹת פִּסְחֵיהֶן זֶה בָּזֶה, וְנִמְצֵאת יַבֶּלֶת בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן — כּוּלָּן יוֹצְאִין לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה, וּפְטוּרִין מִלַּעֲשׂוֹת פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Some teach this** statement of Abaye as referring **to** the following ***baraita*: Five** people had the **hides of their Paschal lambs mixed up together, and a wart was found on one of them.** Since a wart is one of the blemishes that disqualify an animal from being used as an offering, the Paschal lamb from which the hide came is invalid. Since it is not possible to identify which lamb the hide came from, the meat of **all** of the lambs must **go out to the place** designated **for burning.** Nevertheless, all five people **are exempt from observing** the **second *Pesaḥ*.**

The *baraita* presents a parallel case that draws on the same logic. Five people's Pesach skins (*oros*) became intermingled. Upon examination, a blemish (*mum*, specifically a *yabelet*, a wart) was found on one of the skins. Since a *Pesach* with a *mum* is invalid, one of the five *korbanot* was invalid — but no one can determine which one. All five skins are sent out to be burned, and the Gemara rules: *kulan yetzu le-veit ha-sereifa u-pturim mi-la'asot Pesach Sheni* — all are burned, and all five are exempt from *Pesach Sheni*.

### Impossible Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה, דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּאִיזְּרִיק דָּם מִיהָא הֲוָה חֲזֵי לַאֲכִילָה. אֲבָל נִתְעָרְבוּ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה — חַיָּיבִין לַעֲשׂוֹת פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** It is with regard to this *baraita* that **Abaye said: They taught** that they are all exempt from the second *Pesaḥ* in a case **where** the hides **were mixed up together only after the sprinkling** of the blood on the altar, **for at the time the blood** of each of the other four animals **was sprinkled,** each one of the four unblemished lambs **was, at any rate, fit to be eaten,** and therefore the owners are considered to have fulfilled their obligation to slaughter a fit Paschal lamb. As such, they are exempt from the second *Pesaḥ*. **But if they were mixed up together before the sprinkling,** each of the five lambs could possibly be the blemished one. Because of the doubt that exists with regard to all of them, they are all disqualified from being offered. Therefore, none of the five people fulfill their obligation, and **they are** all **obligated to observe** the **second *Pesaḥ*.**

The claim that all five are exempt is immediately counterintuitive: four were surely valid, and four are therefore certainly exempt. But one person brought an invalid *korban Pesach* and should, in principle, be obligated in *Pesach Sheni*. Abaye's parallel teaching applies here: *lo shanu ela she-nisarvu achar zerika* — the exemption holds only if the skins became mixed up after *zerika*. At the moment of *zerika*, each animal's identity was known, and each of the four valid animals was *chazi la-achila*. The subsequent confusion does not retroactively undermine the validity of the *zerika*. But *aval nisarvu lifnei zerika* — if the mixing up occurred before the blood-throwing — then at the moment of *zerika* it was impossible to know which animal was the valid one, and therefore none of them was genuinely *chazi la-achila* in a meaningful sense. In that case, *chayav la-asot Pesach Sheni*.

### Stipulation Attempts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא אִשָּׁה? אָמַר רָבָא: אִשָּׁה וְכׇל דְּדָמֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The fact that the conclusion of the *baraita* specifically mentions a wife implies she is the only exception, but adult children or Hebrew slaves would perforce be included in their father's and master's Paschal lamb, even if they slaughtered one for themselves. The Gemara challenges this: **What is different** about **a wife;** how is her status any different from that of adult children or Hebrew slaves? **Rava said:** The conclusion of the *baraita* is not limited to a wife, rather, it is referring to **a wife and all who are similar to her,** including adult children and Hebrew slaves.

The Gemara then acknowledges the obvious problem: the ruling that all five are *patur* from *Pesach Sheni* is practically convenient but conceptually uncomfortable — one person among the five is in fact obligated. *Mishum de-lo efshar* — but there is simply no way to remedy the situation. The Gemara explores and rejects several potential solutions.

If all five were to bring a *Pesach Sheni*, four of them would be bringing *chulin la-azara* — non-sacred animals into the Temple courtyard — since four of the five are not actually obligated. If all five were to join together as a single *chaburah* for one *Pesach Sheni*, the *Pesach* would be eaten by four people who are not registered members of that sacrifice — *pesach ne'echal shelo li-menuav*.

A more creative solution is proposed: let each person bring his own animal on *Pesach Sheni* and make a stipulation — *i didi hava ba'al mum, hai de-aiti hashta lihavei pesach; i didi tam hava, hai de-aiti hashta lihavei shelamim* — if mine was the blemished one, let this animal I am now bringing serve as my *Pesach*; if mine was unblemished, let it be a *shelamim*. This elegant stipulation would allow each person to cover both possibilities without definitively committing to either. However, the Gemara rejects this as well: *mishum de-ika chaze ve-shok de-kohanim de-achli lei* — if the animal turns out to be a *shelamim*, the *kohen* must receive the *chaze* and *shok*, the breast and the thigh, which are not part of the *Pesach* protocol. And if all five eat it as a *Pesach Sheni*, then for four of them it is *Pesach shelo li-menuav*, being eaten outside one's registered group. The Gemara closes with the difficulty unresolved, noting that the discussion will continue.
