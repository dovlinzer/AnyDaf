# Pesachim 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Maaser Sheni & Challah

### Intro: Matzah/Challah

The daf opens at the bottom of 37b, continuing the sustained comparison between *matzah* and *challah* that occupied much of the preceding discussion. Both the *mitzvah* of taking *challah* and the *mitzvah* of eating *matzah* turn on the Torah's use of the word *lechem* — bread — and a great deal of the previous analysis explored what qualifies as *lechem* in each context. The discussion encompassed the requirement that *matzah* have the capacity to become *chametz*, that it be *lechem oni* (poor person's bread), and that it remain plain in certain respects, even while fine flour is permitted. The word *lechem* functions similarly across *challah* and *matzah*, and yesterday's discussion examined that comparison in depth — touching on bagels, *sufganiyot*, and the *beracha* of *Hamotzi Lechem Min Ha'aretz*.

Now the Gemara turns to a more arcane dimension of the same question: what happens when *matzah* carries an additional halachic status — such as *maaser sheni*, *bikkurim*, or other sanctified designations — and how does that affect its fitness for the *mitzvah*? We re-enter a discussion that began two days ago and continues here in a different register.

### Rav Assi's Ruling

Rav Assi's statement appears at the bottom of 37b: *issa shel maaser sheni le-divrei Rabbi Meir patur min ha-challah* — dough of *maaser sheni*, according to Rabbi Meir, is exempt from the obligation of *challah*. According to the *Chachamim*, however, such dough is obligated in *challah*. The *challah* context is distinct from the earlier *matzah* discussion; here the question is not the criterion of *bechol moshvoteichem* (all your dwelling places) but rather a different issue entirely regarding the ownership status of *maaser sheni* dough.

The Gemara then extends the ruling: *matzos shel maaser sheni* — according to Rabbi Meir — do not fulfill one's obligation on Pesach, because the *matzah* is not considered the person's own. According to the *Chachamim*, one does fulfill the obligation. Similarly, an *esrog shel maaser sheni*: according to Rabbi Meir one may fulfill the obligation on *Yom Tov*, while according to the *Chachamim* one may not. The reversal between *matzah* and *esrog* is deliberate — in each case the direction of the ruling follows from who holds the *maaser sheni* to be *mammon gavoah* or *mammon baalim*.

### Ownership Status

The conceptual foundation of this debate is the famous dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* over the ownership of *maaser sheni*. When *maaser sheni* is brought to Jerusalem and eaten there, who is the owner? The parallel case is *kodshim kalim* — *shelamim* and the like — which must also be eaten in Jerusalem. One who brings a *korban* has certain rights: the *korban* is credited to him, he is entitled to eat from it, and yet he cannot sell it or dispose of it in other ways. Is it then *mammon baalim* — truly his — or *mammon gavoah* — ultimately belonging to God?

The same question applies to *maaser sheni*. One separates it and brings it to Jerusalem, but the conceptual framing is that it has been given over to God — yet the owner still eats it. Do the restrictions (it may only be eaten in Jerusalem, it may only be used in specified ways) mark it as *mammon gavoah*, or do the practical rights of ownership — control, consumption, designation — mark it as *mammon hedyot*, belonging to the owner?

Rabbi Meir holds *maaser sheni* is *mammon gavoah* — God's produce, not the owner's. The *Chachamim* hold it is *mammon baalim* — it remains the owner's. This dispute, the Gemara explains, directly determines both the *challah* obligation and the *matzah* obligation, under the assumption that both require the item to belong to the person performing the *mitzvah*.

### Rav Pappa's Question

Rav Pappa raises a sharp question. He grants the *challah* case: the verse says *arisoteichem* — "your kneading troughs" — a possessive form that clearly demands the dough be yours. He grants the *esrog* case: the verse says *u-lekachtem lachem* — "you shall take for yourselves" — explicitly requiring ownership. But regarding *matzah*, he asks: *mi ketiv matzatechem*? Is there anywhere a verse that says "your *matzah*"? There is no such verse. So why should the ownership question matter for *matzah* at all? Even if the *matzah* is theoretically God's under Rabbi Meir's view, as long as one is permitted to eat it and is not stealing it, why should one not fulfill the obligation?

This question implicitly challenges the symmetry Rav Assi assumed across all three cases. The *challah* and *esrog* cases rest on explicit textual pegs; *matzah* has no comparable peg. What entitles us to import the ownership requirement there?

### Rava's Gezeirah Shavah

Rava — and some attribute this to Rav Yeimar bar Shalamya — answers with a *gezeirah shavah*: *lechem lechem*. The verse regarding *matzah* uses the phrase *lechem oni* — poor person's bread. The verse regarding *challah* uses the phrase *ve-hayah be-acholchem mi-lechem ha-aretz* — when you eat from the bread of the land, you shall separate *challah*. Since both use the word *lechem*, we draw an analogy: just as *challah* must come from *your* dough — *mishalchem* — so too *matzah* must come from *yours* — *mishalchem*.

This *gezeirah shavah* does more than link a definitional quality of bread (as was discussed the day before); it imports the ownership requirement from *challah* into *matzah*. The conceptual comparison made throughout the chapter now acquires a technical halakhic edge: whatever "yours" demands in the *challah* context, it demands equally in the *matzah* context.

One might note a related unaddressed question lurking in the background: the issue of *matzah ha-gezulah* — stolen *matzah* — and *mitzvah ha-ba'ah ba-aveirah* — a *mitzvah* performed through or intertwined with a transgression. These are prominent concerns in the third *perek* of Masechet Succah when discussing a stolen *lulav*, yet they are not raised directly in this *perek* in connection with either *gezel* or *matzah*. The focus here remains squarely on the *lechem* linkage and the ownership question it generates.

### Double 'Ariso'teichem'

The Gemara then considers whether the *baraita* that records the Rabbi Meir/*Chachamim* debate about *challah* of *maaser sheni* serves as support for Rav Assi's broader position. The Gemara deflects: that *baraita* only establishes the *challah* case directly — it does not automatically extend to *esrog* and *matzah*. The question is whether we can infer from the *challah* debate that the same dispute plays out across all three cases.

Here the Gemara introduces an important distinction. The Torah uses the word *arisoteichem* — "your kneading troughs" — twice in connection with *challah*. This double expression reinforces the ownership demand: not merely "yours" in the practical sense that you possess and control it, but "yours" in a more exacting technical sense, such that even *maaser sheni*, which you do practically control and are entitled to eat, is still excluded under Rabbi Meir because theoretically it is God's. Perhaps this doubled demand is unique to *challah*. If so, *esrog*, where *lachem* appears only once, and certainly *matzah*, where there is no *lachem* at all (before the *gezeirah shavah* is applied), might have a more lenient standard — and even Rabbi Meir might concede that *maaser sheni* suffices there.

Rav Assi's position, then, is that the ownership requirement does apply across all three cases. The *chiddush* is specifically in extending it to *matzah* by way of the *gezeirah shavah*.

## Matzah of Maaser Sheni

### R. Shimon ben Lakish

The Gemara now shifts to a creative question posed by Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: *mahu she-yeitzei adam yedei chovaso be-challah shel maaser sheni bi-Yerushalayim*? Can a person fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* with *challah* that is also *maaser sheni*, eaten in Jerusalem?

The scenario: one brings *maaser sheni* grain to Jerusalem, makes it into dough, and separates *challah* from it before baking. The *challah* is given to a *kohen* (or the questioner is himself a *kohen*). This piece of dough now carries two halachic identities simultaneously — it is *challah* and it is *maaser sheni*. We established two days ago (based on the view of Rabbi Akiva) that *matzah* of plain *maaser sheni* can in principle fulfill the obligation, because were the *maaser sheni* to become *tamei*, it could be redeemed and eaten outside Jerusalem — meaning it has latent capacity to be eaten anywhere. We established elsewhere in the *Mishnah* that a *kohen* can fulfill the *matzah* obligation with *matzah* made from *challah*. If *challah* alone is fine and *maaser sheni* alone is fine, why should their combination be problematic?

### Tamei Status Problem

The Gemara first dispenses with the simpler case. According to Rabbi Yose HaGalili, who disputes Rabbi Akiva and holds that *maaser sheni matzah* can never fulfill the obligation, there is no question: if plain *maaser sheni* does not work, *challah* of *maaser sheni* certainly does not. The question only arises according to Rabbi Akiva.

According to Rabbi Akiva, plain *maaser sheni matzah* is permissible because, were it to become *tamei*, it could be redeemed and eaten outside Jerusalem — granting it theoretical accessibility to all dwelling places, as the verse requires. But the problem surfaces specifically when the *maaser sheni* is also *challah*. What happens when *challah* becomes *tamei*? It cannot be redeemed — it must be burned, like *terumah tme'ah*. The very scenario that makes *maaser sheni* workable (the possibility of *tumah* leading to redemption and broader availability) is blocked by the *challah* status. *Challah* that becomes *tamei* is consumed in fire, not consumed by a person. So perhaps this combination disqualifies the *matzah* after all.

### Hypothetical Arguments

Yet the Gemara entertains the opposing argument: *hoil ve-ilu lo kara aleha shem* — had one never designated it as *challah*, and had it become *tamei*, there would have been *heter be-moshavos*, permission to eat it anywhere. Since that hypothetical obtains, perhaps even now one fulfills the obligation.

This is a striking move: the argument essentially says, ignore the *challah* status, because in the abstract, without that designation, the *matzah* would be valid. Rashi explains the logic as follows: the *challah* status by itself does not disqualify something from the *matzah* obligation — a *kohen* may use *challah* for *matzah*. The *maaser sheni* status by itself also does not disqualify — Rabbi Akiva permits it. Neither status independently creates a problem. Therefore, the fact that they happen to converge should not create a disqualification. It is as though each status can be "bracketed" because on its own it causes no harm to the *matzah* obligation. Whether this line of reasoning is persuasive is precisely the Gemara's question.

The Gemara adds a complicating variant: *ikka de-amri* — some say the above case (original *maaser sheni* that is also *challah*) is in fact obviously permissible, and the real question is the next case.

### Purchased w/ Maaser Money

The more complex scenario involves *challah halakuach bekesef maaser sheni* — *challah* that was purchased with *maaser sheni* money rather than being originally *maaser sheni* produce. The mechanism: one redeems *maaser sheni* produce onto coins and brings the money to Jerusalem. There, one purchases *challah* from a *kohen* using those coins. The *challah* now carries *maaser sheni* status not because it originated as *maaser sheni* grain, but because it was acquired with *maaser sheni* money.

The question of what one may do with such food if it becomes *tamei* is disputed. The *Rabbanan* hold that food purchased with *maaser sheni* money may be redeemed if it becomes *tamei*, just like original *maaser sheni*. According to them, this case reduces to the earlier question and there is no new difficulty. But Rabbi Yehuda holds — as recorded in a *Mishnah* in Masechet Maaser Sheni — that food purchased with *maaser sheni* money that became *tamei* must be buried; it cannot be redeemed. His reasoning appears to be that derivative *kedushah* — the sanctity that attaches to something purchased with *maaser sheni* money — is weaker than the original *kedushah* of *maaser sheni* produce itself, and cannot transfer out through redemption the way the original can.

According to Rabbi Yehuda, then, even setting aside *challah*, one might simply ask: can *matzah* purchased with *maaser sheni* money fulfill the obligation at all? The problem of *maaser sheni* — its restriction to Jerusalem — is alleviated for Rabbi Akiva by the possibility of redemption following *tumah*. But if Rabbi Yehuda denies that possibility for purchased items, the *matzah* would be permanently restricted to Jerusalem and could never gain the theoretical universal accessibility the verse demands. And now add the *challah* status on top: if it becomes *tamei*, both its *challah* status and its derivative *maaser sheni* status independently block any resolution.

The Gemara frames this as: do we say *hoil ve-ilu lo lakuach hava* (had it not been purchased with *maaser sheni* money) *ve-hoil ve-lo kara lah shem* (and had it never been designated *challah*) — two hypothetical disregards — then it would be fine, and therefore even now it is fine? Or do we say *chad hoil amrinan u-trei hoil lo amrinan* — we can posit one counterfactual, but not two? Rashi's explanation of each *hoil*: the *challah* designation by itself is no problem for *matzah*; and the *maaser sheni* money origin, while problematic in Rabbi Yehuda's view, is only because it is derivative — had it been original *maaser sheni*, Rabbi Akiva would permit it. Bracketing each status individually, neither independently causes disqualification; only their intersection does. Whether both may simultaneously be bracketed is the open question.

### Rava's Resolution

Rava resolves: *shem maaser chad hu* — the identity of *maaser sheni* is a single unified category. Whether the *maaser sheni* status attaches because the item is original *maaser sheni* produce or because it was purchased with *maaser sheni* money, it is all one *maaser sheni* designation. Since *maaser sheni* as a category is permissible for the *matzah* obligation under Rabbi Akiva, the derivative form is no different, and the combination does not create a disqualification.

In summary, the Gemara explored three conceptual layers: (1) whether *maaser sheni matzah* can fulfill the obligation due to the restriction to Jerusalem; (2) whether the ownership question (*mammon gavoah* vs. *mammon baalim*) creates an additional problem; and (3) whether combining *maaser sheni* with *challah* — or with derivative *maaser sheni* — creates a compound problem greater than either part alone. Rava's inclination is that since each status independently does not obstruct the *matzah* obligation, their combination likewise does not.

## Chalos Todah & Nazir

### Mishnah Introduction

The final case in the *Mishnah* regarding unusual *matzah* statuses involves *chalos todah* and *rekikei nazir* — the loaves brought with a *korban todah* (thanksgiving offering) and the wafers brought with a *nazir*'s completion offering. A *korban todah* is accompanied by forty loaves, ten of which are *chametz* and thirty of which are *matzah*. A *nazir* who completes his term brings wafers that are also *matzah*. Both types are therefore *matzah* in form.

By way of context: on account of the *korban todah* requiring *chametz* loaves, the *Mizmor L'Todah* prayer is omitted from *Pesukei D'Zimra* throughout Pesach — including *erev Pesach* — since one cannot bring the *todah* bread then. The *matzah* portion of the *todah* bread, however, is what concerns us here.

The *Mishnah*'s ruling: if one made these *matzah* loaves for oneself (intending to use them as one's own *korban*), one cannot fulfill the *matzah* obligation with them. If one made them to sell in the marketplace (to potential buyers who would later bring the *korban*), one may fulfill the obligation with them. Crucially, the *Mishnah* is speaking of bread that has not yet been sanctified — it was prepared for the purpose of a *korban* but has not been formally consecrated. Once the buyer sanctifies it, the seller can no longer use it. But at the moment of making, the status is defined by intent, not yet by actual consecration.

### Rava's Derasha

The Gemara asks: *mena hanei milei* — what is the source for this disqualification? Rava derives it from the verse *u-shmartem et ha-matzos* — "you shall guard the *matzot*." This verse, he argues, requires that the guarding be *leshem matzah* — for the purpose of [the *mitzvah* of] *matzah*. *Matzah hamishtameres leshem matzah* — only *matzah* watched for the purpose of the *matzah* obligation qualifies. *Yatza* this case — which is watched to ensure it does not become *chametz* for the sake of the *korban*, not for the sake of the *matzah mitzvah*. Even though the bread is technically being protected from fermentation — because the *korban* requires *matzah* and not *chametz* — the intent of the guarding is directed toward the *korban*, not toward the *mitzvah* of *matzah* on the *seder* night.

This requires attention. Rashi, at the top of 38b, explains: *kol shimur she-ata mishamrah shelo tachmitz* — any guarding you perform to prevent fermentation — *hitkaven leshem matzah shel mitzvah* — must be directed toward its being *matzas mitzvah*. Preventing fermentation is necessary but not sufficient; the intent must be explicitly for the *mitzvah*.

This is a striking principle, and one might wonder why it is unique to *matzah*. Many *mitzvah* objects do not carry a *lishmah* requirement. A *sukkah* need only be built for shade; it need not be built for the purpose of the *mitzvah*, and it need not even be built by a Jew. A *lulav* and *esrog* have no such intent requirement. The answer is that this is not a general rule of *mitzvah* objects but a specific requirement derived from the particular verse *u-shmartem et ha-matzos*, which the Gemara reads as embedding a purposive demand into the guarding itself.

### Rav Yosef's Derasha

Rav Yosef offers a different derivation, from a different verse: *shivat yamim matzot tochelu* — "seven days you shall eat *matzah*." From the fact that the verse specifies seven days, Rav Yosef derives that the *matzah* must be of a type that *can* be eaten for seven days. *Matzah nechelet le-shiva yamim* qualifies; *yatza* this bread — which, once consecrated as part of a *korban todah* or *nazir* offering, can only be eaten for one day and the following night before becoming *notar* (leftover that may not be consumed) — does not qualify.

Both Rashi and Tosafot are compelled to clarify, in light of the later Gemara's treatment of the marketplace case, that we are speaking of bread not yet sanctified. The dough, at the time of its preparation, has not yet been formally consecrated. So how can one say it "can only be eaten for one day"? Rashi and Tosafot explain that Rav Yosef's criterion applies to the *intent* at the time of preparation: if the bread is made with the intent that it will ultimately only be usable for one day (because the maker intends it to become a *korban*), that intent-governed limitation disqualifies it — even before sanctification. If one makes the bread without that limiting intent (i.e., to sell in the marketplace, knowing it might not sell and might be kept for one's own use over the seven days of Pesach), then the bread is not disqualified on this ground.

This second approach is significant: it does not require that the *matzah* be prepared *leshem matzas mitzvah* — for the explicit sake of the *matzah* obligation. The problem is rather the negative intent of one-day limitation. Absent that negative intent, the *matzah* is permissible, without any affirmative requirement to have directed the guarding specifically toward the *mitzvah*.

The Gemara notes that a *baraita* can be adduced in support of each position: one *baraita* employs the *u-shmartem* verse in the manner of Rava, and another *baraita* employs the *shivat yamim* verse in the manner of Rav Yosef. Both derivations are thus supported by *tannaitic* sources.

### Kol Moshavos Query

Having established these reasons, the Gemara raises a challenge: *ve-teipok lei* — why not simply invoke the *bechol moshvoteichem* principle? The same criterion invoked earlier in the chapter — that *matzah* must be capable of being eaten in all dwelling places — should exclude *chalos todah* and *rekikei nazir* directly. Once consecrated as part of a *korban*, these breads can only be eaten in Jerusalem. Why do we need the novel derivations about *shimur* or *shivat yamim*?

### Nov & Givon History

Reish Lakish answers: from the very fact that the Gemara sought other reasons and did not simply invoke the *bechol moshvoteichem* criterion, we can derive that *chalos todah* and *rekikei nazir* are in fact *ne'echalim be-Nov u-ve-Givon* — they may be eaten in Nov and Givon. This requires explanation.

When the *Bnei Yisrael* entered *Eretz Yisrael*, there was an extended period before a permanent central sanctuary was established. During the fourteen years of conquest and division of the land, there were private altars (*bamot*). Then the *Mishkan* was established at Shilo, which functioned analogously to what Jerusalem would later become: a central location to which *korbanot* were brought, with the surrounding area serving as the designated eating zone. Then Shilo was destroyed, and there was a transitional period. The quasi-central places of worship during this period were Nov — the city of priests, familiar from the narratives of Shaul and David — and later Givon.

During the periods of Nov and Givon, the status was less formalized than at Shilo. Unlike Shilo, where eating of *korbanot* was geographically delimited, at Nov and Givon one could bring a *korban* at the central altar but eat the resulting meat and bread anywhere in Eretz Yisrael — *bechol arei Yisrael*. Therefore, the bread of a *korban todah* and the wafers of a *nazir* brought during the Nov and Givon periods could be eaten anywhere. Since there existed a period in Jewish history when these breads could be eaten in any location, they retain a theoretical classification as potentially universally edible — and therefore the *bechol moshvoteichem* criterion does not exclude them.

Tosafot acknowledges the tension: if we can reach back a millennium to the time of Nov and Givon to neutralize the *bechol moshvoteichem* concern, why could we not do the same for the earlier discussions about *maaser sheni* and its restriction to Jerusalem? Reish Lakish's answer is specific to the *todah* and *nazir* breads on account of their particular historical status during those transitional periods. The answer is admittedly somewhat far-reaching, but the Gemara requires it in order to preserve the marketplace/personal-use distinction — for if the *bechol moshvoteichem* criterion were operative, it would disqualify the bread regardless of who it was made for, and the distinction could not be maintained.

## Eliezer & Yehoshua

### Baraita: R. Ilai Query

A *baraita* records the following exchange. Rabbi Ilai related: I asked Rabbi Eliezer whether one may fulfill the obligation of *matzah* with *chalos todah* and *rekikei nazir*. Rabbi Eliezer replied: *lo shamati* — I have not received a tradition on this matter. This response is characteristically Rabbi Eliezer: he would not say anything he had not received by tradition, and absent a received tradition, the discussion was closed.

### R. Yehoshua's Tradition

Rabbi Ilai then went and asked before Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Yehoshua did have a received tradition: *harey amru* — they have said — that *chalos todah* and *rekikei nazir she-asa l'atzmo* — which one made for oneself — *ein adam yotzei bahem*, one does not fulfill the obligation. But *limkor ba-shuk* — if one made them to sell in the marketplace — *yotzei bahem*, one does fulfill the obligation.

### R. Eliezer's Acceptance

Rabbi Ilai returned and reported this tradition to Rabbi Eliezer. Upon hearing it, Rabbi Eliezer said: *Brit* — by covenant — *hen hen ha-devarim she-ne'emru le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — these are the very words transmitted to Moses at Sinai. He embraced the tradition as a genuine and authoritative *mesorah*, even though he himself had not possessed it. His response is notable: he did not independently hold the tradition, but once it was brought to him from Rabbi Yehoshua, he affirmed it as authentically *Halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*.

*Ikka de-amri* — some say — his response was in fact not a positive affirmation but a sharp critique. According to this reading, his statement was: by oath, these may be words *ne'emru le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — but *ve-lo ta'ama bah* — without any reason to them. He was questioning whether this could be a genuine transmitted tradition given that it makes no apparent sense. Why should it matter whether the bread was made for oneself or made for sale? What rational principle could account for such a distinction? If it were Rabbi Eliezer's own tradition, the absence of an apparent rationale would not matter — a tradition is a tradition. But receiving it from elsewhere, without a reason to validate it, gave him pause.

### Rava's Intent Explanation

The Gemara presses: *ve-ta'ama mai* — what is the reason? Rava explains. When one makes the bread *le-shuk* — for the marketplace — *imluchi memlach* — one has second thoughts. One says to oneself: if it sells, it sells; and if it does not sell — *eipuk bei ana* — I will use it for myself for the *matzah* obligation on Pesach. This inner reservation, this hedging of intent, means that even when the bread is made, the maker has in the back of his mind the possibility that it will ultimately be used for the *mitzvah* of *matzah*. Some level of intent toward *matzat mitzvah* is present from the outset.

This resolution also explains why the Gemara had to rule out other reasons — the oil, the restriction to Jerusalem — as the primary grounds for disqualification. If the oil content made it intrinsically *matzah ashirah*, or if the geographic restriction were the operative concern, then intent would be irrelevant — the bread would be disqualified regardless of whether it was made for personal use or for sale. Only if the problem is purely one of intent — specifically, the intent behind the guarding (Rava's derivation) or the intent behind the one-day limitation (Rav Yosef's derivation) — can the marketplace scenario serve as a corrective. When selling in the marketplace, one's intent is not unidimensionally directed toward a *korban*; the possibility of personal use for the *mitzvah* is held in mind, and that latent intention is sufficient.

The converse: if one made the bread explicitly for oneself — intending to use it personally as a *korban* — that unambiguous intent directed toward the *zevach*, without any reservation about possibly eating it as *matzas mitzvah*, disqualifies it. The relevant point for practical purposes is the question of whether protecting *matzah* from becoming *chametz* is sufficient on its own, or whether explicit intention directed toward the *mitzvah* of *matzah* is additionally required. That question does not receive a final definitive ruling here, but at minimum one of the positions in the Gemara — Rava's derivation from *u-shmartem* — clearly establishes that intent toward *matzas mitzvah* is indispensable, and that intent directed only toward avoiding *chametz* for the sake of a *korban* does not suffice.
