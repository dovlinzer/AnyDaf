# Nazir 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Masna vs Bar Pada

### 30 vs 29 Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: סְתַם נְזִירוּת שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב מַתְנָא — נִיחָא, אֶלָּא לְבַר פְּדָא — קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the opinions of Rav Mattana and bar Padda in light of the mishna and relevant *baraitot*. **We learned** in the mishna: An **unspecified naziriteship** lasts for **thirty days. Granted,** according **to Rav Mattana** it **works out well. However, according to bar Padda** it is **difficult,** since in his opinion the mishna should have stated that an unspecified naziriteship lasts for twenty-nine days.

The daf opens in the middle of 6a, picking up a dispute introduced in prior sessions. *Rav Masna* and *Bar Pada* disagree about the fundamental structure of *stam nezirus* — standard, unspecified *nezirut*.

*Rav Masna* holds that *nezirut* is, in principle, thirty days. Since a series of *mishnayot* makes clear that a *nazir* may bring his *korban* on day thirty — thereby completing his *nezirut* on that day — *Rav Masna* must account for this by invoking the principle of *miktzas hayom kekulo*: a partial day counts as the whole. Just as a mourner rises from *aveilus* on the morning of the seventh day without completing a full seventh day, so too the *nazir* who enters day thirty has, in principle, satisfied his thirty-day obligation. This also explains why the *mishna* specifies that *lechatchila* — as the preferred initial practice — one should wait until day thirty-one to bring the *korban*. If *miktzas hayom kekulo* applies, there is still some practical reason to wait, which will become clearer as the discussion unfolds.

*Bar Pada*, by contrast, rejects *miktzas hayom kekulo* entirely. In his view, *stam nezirus* means twenty-nine days — not thirty — but twenty-nine *complete* days, with no partial-day principle at play. The *nazir* brings his *korban* on day thirty as a matter of course, because by that point twenty-nine full days have already elapsed. Why *lechatchila* one should still wait until day thirty-one on his account is less clear — perhaps to accommodate cases where the *nazir* explicitly stipulated thirty days — but his fundamental position is twenty-nine full days, no *miktzas hayom*.

### Miktzas Hayom Kekulo

One might ask: what precisely is the conceptual basis for *miktzas hayom kekulo*, and when does it apply? There is a related but distinct principle, *rubo kekulo* — the majority counts as the whole — but these are not equivalent. The principle of *rubo kekulo* operates when the goal is to achieve a particular state: for instance, in *shechita*, one need not sever the trachea and esophagus entirely; cutting the majority of each is sufficient, because the required state — severance — is considered achieved. Similarly, in *eiruv*, a wall need not be continuous along the entire perimeter; as long as the majority is enclosed, the area is considered walled off. In each case, what matters is achieving a recognized condition, not completing a precise physical act.

*Miktzas hayom kekulo* is better understood as a principle specific to durations — periods of time framed as units rather than as counts. When one asks whether a person "completed a week" or "completed a month," the focus is on the conceptual unit, not on the literal number of hours. Once a person has entered the final day of that unit, one can say that the unit has, in a meaningful sense, been achieved — because the unit, not its constituent days, is what matters. This is why the principle is most naturally applied to things like mourning periods, *nezirut*, or any framework structured around temporal units rather than discrete counts.

### Rubo Kekulo

This distinction has real *halachic* consequences. *Rubo kekulo* and *miktzas hayom kekulo* each have their domain. When a goal is to achieve a state, the majority suffices. When a goal is to complete a duration understood as a unit, entering the final day may suffice. But where one is literally counting discrete actions — say, eating a *kezayis* or completing all eighteen blessings of *shemoneh esrei* — neither principle straightforwardly applies. The appropriate application of each principle depends on how one analyzes the underlying obligation.

## Consecutive Nezirut

### Day 30 Double Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מִי שֶׁנָּזַר שְׁתֵּי נְזִירוּת — מְגַלֵּחַ אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה יוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים וְאֶחָד, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה לְיוֹם שִׁשִּׁים וְאֶחָד. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב מַתְנָא — נִיחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **We learned** in that mishna: **One who accepted two** terms of **naziriteship shaves** his hair for **the first** naziriteship on the **thirty-first day, and** he shaves his hair for **the second** term **on the sixty-first day. Granted,** according **to Rav Mattana** this **works out well,** since he shaves his head for the first naziriteship after a full thirty days have passed. The second term of naziriteship then commences on that day, which is the thirty-first day since he took his vows. He then shaves for the second time on the thirty-first day of his second naziriteship, which is the sixty-first day since he took his vows.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְבַר פְּדָא קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **However, according to bar Padda** this is **difficult.** Why doesn't he shave his hair on the thirtieth day of each term of naziriteship?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ בַּר פְּדָא, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וְאִם גִּילַּח אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה לְיוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים — מְגַלֵּחַ אֶת הַשְּׁנִיָּה לְיוֹם שִׁשִּׁים. אֶלָּא, סֵיפָא מְסַיְּיעָא לֵיהּ, רֵישָׁא — בְּאוֹמֵר שְׁלֵימִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Bar Padda** could have **said to you: Say the latter clause** of the mishna: **And if he shaved** his hair for **the first** term **on the thirtieth day, he shaves** his hair for **the second** term **on the sixtieth day.** This demonstrates that the periods of naziriteship really last only twenty-nine days. **Rather, the latter clause** of the mishna **supports his** opinion, whereas the **earlier clause** of the mishna, which teaches that he shaves his hair on the thirty-first and sixty-first days, is referring **to one who says** that his naziriteship will be for thirty **complete** days.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב מַתְנָא קַשְׁיָא סֵיפָא! אָמַר לְךָ רַב מַתְנָא, כִּדְקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: יוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים עוֹלֶה לְכָאן וּלְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now asks: **And according to Rav Mattana, the latter clause** of the mishna is **difficult.** The Gemara answers that **Rav Mattana** could have **said to you that** it is **as the latter clause teaches: The thirtieth day counts for** both **this and that,** i.e., it is considered both the last day of the first term of naziriteship, as well as the first day of the second term.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא — מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ? הָא אַמְרַהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן חֲדָא נְזִירוּת, אֲבָל לִשְׁתֵּי נְזִירוּת — לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the novelty of the latter clause of the mishna? Is it that the legal status of **part of the day is like** that **of an entire** day? **But didn't he** already **say this once** in an earlier clause in that mishna, as explained above? The Gemara answers: The latter clause was necessary; **lest you say** that **this** principle **applies** only **with regard to one** term of **naziriteship, but** with regard **to two** terms of **naziriteship, no,** this principle does not apply, the *tanna* therefore **teaches us** that part of the day is like that of an entire day, even to allow counting the beginning of the day as the last day of one term of naziriteship and the end of the day as the first day of another term of naziriteship.

The *gemara* now cites a *mishna* with a striking implication. The case involves a person who has accepted two consecutive *neziruyot*, one immediately following the other. The rule is that *lechatchila* one should bring the *korban* for the first *nezirut* on day thirty-one. If, however, one did so *bedieved* — after the fact — on day thirty, that too is valid. Day thirty then becomes day one of the second *nezirut*, so the second *korban* should *lechatchila* be brought on day sixty — which is day thirty-one counting from that new starting point. And if *bedieved* one brought the second *korban* on day fifty-nine, that is also valid, for the same reason: day fifty-nine of the combined count is day thirty of the second *nezirut*.

The critical principle the *mishna* articulates is *oleh lakan ulakan* — that day thirty counts toward both the ending period and the beginning period. The arithmetic makes the point starkly: two units of thirty days should require sixty days, yet the *mishna* validates completion in fifty-nine. The only way to reconcile this is if the middle day — the day of the *korban* — is counted in both directions simultaneously. This is the *chidush* implicit in *oleh lakan ulakan*.

### Bar Pada's Challenge

The *gemara* asks: *bishlama* according to *Rav Masna*, this makes sense. His position is that *nezirut* is fundamentally thirty days, and *miktzas hayom kekulo* allows day thirty to function as the completion of the first unit and — simultaneously — the beginning of the next. One can say the partial day counted as a full thirtieth day for the first *nezirut*, and also as day one for the second. But according to *Bar Pada*, what is the *chidush*? If *nezirut* is twenty-nine full days, then by the end of day twenty-nine the obligation is already complete. Of course day thirty will work — there is nothing surprising about it counting toward the next *nezirut*, because it was never required for the first.

### Oleh Lakan Ulakan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: וְאִם גִּילַּח יוֹם שִׁשִּׁים חָסֵר אֶחָד — יָצָא, שֶׁיּוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים עוֹלֶה לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב מַתְנָא — נִיחָא. אֶלָּא לְבַר פְּדָא לְמָה לִי? הָא אָמַר שְׁלֹשִׁים חָסֵר אֶחָד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another question. **We learned** in the mishna (16a): **If he shaved** his hair on **day sixty less one,** i.e., on the fifty-ninth day after he vowed to observe two consecutive terms of naziriteship, **he has fulfilled** his obligation, **as the thirtieth day counts** as part **of his tally. Granted,** according **to Rav Mattana** this **works out well,** since the mishna is stating that the thirtieth day can be counted for both terms of naziriteship due to the principle that part of a day is like an entire day. **However, according to bar Padda, why do I** need the mishna to state that the thirtieth day counts in his tally? **Didn't he say** that an unspecified term of naziriteship is for **thirty** days **less one?** It is obvious that the thirtieth day counts for the second term of naziriteship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ: אֲנָא נָמֵי אַהָא סְמַכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Bar Padda could have **said to you: I too rely on this.** This is my proof for the principle that an unspecified naziriteship lasts for twenty-nine days.

*Bar Pada* responds: the *mishna*'s language of *oleh lakan ulakan* is actually where he derives his position. The reason day thirty can count toward the beginning of the next period is precisely because it was not needed for the previous one. This is, admittedly, a somewhat counterintuitive reading of the phrase — *oleh lakan ulakan* sounds like double-counting in both directions, yet *Bar Pada* reads it as conveying the opposite: that the day is available for the next period because it was never part of the prior one.

From *Rav Masna*'s perspective, the *chidush* of *miktzas hayom kekulo* is that bringing the *korban* midway through day thirty counts as completing the first *nezirut* and simultaneously initiates the second — a double application of the principle in a single day. *Bar Pada* finds this conceptually untenable. How can one day be counted twice? His answer is that the *mishna* is teaching something different: the principle is that *nezirut* is truly only twenty-nine days, and therefore there is no double-counting. Day thirty was never in play for the first unit, and it simply initiates the next.

It is worth noting that *Tosafot* here — whose commentary on this *daf* is unusually extensive and reads more like sustained running exposition than the familiar dialectical give-and-take — takes up much of the analytical burden that the *Ran* typically bears in *Nedarim*, precisely because the standard *Rashi* on *Nedarim* is not authentic *Rashi*.

The *gemara* at this point does not resolve the dispute by ruling definitively. The *gemara*'s primary goal is to explore and articulate each position rather than always to reach a *halachic* conclusion, and both positions remain alive.

## Tumah on Day 30

### Tamei Soter Hakol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מִי שֶׁאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר״, נִטְמָא יוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים — סוֹתֵר אֶת הַכֹּל. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב מַתְנָא — נִיחָא, אֶלָּא לְבַר פְּדָא קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to ask: **We learned** in the mishna (16a): In the case of **one who said: I am hereby a nazirite,** if he **became ritually impure** on the **thirtieth day, it negates the entire** tally, and he must start his naziriteship afresh. **Granted,** according **to Rav Mattana** it **works out well,** since he became impure before the conclusion of his naziriteship. **However, according to bar Padda** it is **difficult,** as the term of naziriteship ended at the conclusion of the twenty-ninth day, and one who becomes impure after completing his term does not have to observe a new term of naziriteship.

The *gemara* now cites another *mishna*: if someone said *hareini nazir* and became *tamei* on day thirty, *soter et hakol* — the entire *nezirut* is undermined and must be restarted.

According to *Rav Masna* this is immediately intelligible. The *nazir* may bring his *korban* on day thirty by virtue of *miktzas hayom kekulo*, but that same principle carries a vulnerability: if something happens later in day thirty that is fundamentally incompatible with *nezirut*, we cannot retroactively affirm that the partial day counted as a complete and valid thirtieth day. Drinking wine or getting a haircut after the *korban* is brought does not undo anything — those are acts made permissible by the *korban* itself. But *tumah* is categorically different: it does not merely violate the *nazir*'s restrictions; it retroactively impugns the integrity of the entire period, requiring him to start over. Since we relied on *miktzas hayom kekulo* to say the beginning of day thirty counted as a complete thirtieth day, we can only maintain that position if the remainder of the day is consistent with it. If *tumah* occurred later that day, we must step back and say: we cannot call that a *yom tahara* — a day of purity — and therefore cannot count it as the thirtieth day at all.

### Retroactive Negation

This is a striking idea: the *halachic* status of the beginning of the day is, in a sense, conditional on what unfolds in the remainder. When we say *miktzas hayom kekulo*, we are making a legal determination that the partial day counts as the whole — but that determination is open to reassessment if subsequent events within that day fundamentally contradict the status we assigned it. The *korban* does not lock in the result irrevocably; what happens for the rest of day thirty can redefine what the beginning of that day meant.

A useful analogy: in *shechita*, if one cuts the majority of the trachea and esophagus and then, in attempting to complete the cut, performs the remaining portion in a disqualifying manner, there is a serious opinion that the entire *shechita* is invalidated. One could have stopped while ahead — the majority was sufficient — but once the problematic cut was made in the remaining portion, it retroactively undermined the whole. The same logic applies here: once the day has run its full course and *tumah* occurred within it, we cannot sustain the fiction that the earlier portion of the day was equivalent to a complete, untainted thirtieth day.

### Zava Analogy

An illuminating parallel comes from the laws of *zava*. A woman who experiences three consecutive days of non-menstrual bleeding must count seven consecutive clean days. On the seventh day, she need not wait until nightfall — she may immerse in the *mikvah* during the day itself, invoking a form of *miktzas hayom kekulo*. However, if she immerses during day seven, then has relations with her husband, and then bleeds again before nightfall, the entire count is retroactively undermined — she was always in the status of *zava*, the immersion was void, and she must begin a new seven-day count. This is precisely why *halacha* prescribes that even after immersing on day seven, she should wait until nightfall before resuming marital relations — or better yet, wait to immerse at all until nightfall — so as not to create the conditions for this retroactive collapse.

### Lechatchila Day 31

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ בַּר פְּדָא: אֵימָא סֵיפָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ סוֹתֵר אֶלָּא שִׁבְעָה. אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים בָּעִינַן, נִסְתּוֹר כּוּלְּהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Bar Padda** could have **said to you: Say the latter clause** of that mishna: **Rabbi Eliezer says: It negates only seven** days. **If it enters your mind** that **we require thirty** days for a term of naziriteship, the nazirite **should negate all of them,** since he is still in the middle of his naziriteship. This proves that an unspecified naziriteship is only twenty-nine days long.

This is exactly the reason why *lechatchila* one should wait until day thirty-one to bring the *korban*. If the *korban* is brought on day thirty and the *nazir* then becomes *tamei* before nightfall, the entire *nezirut* is invalidated — every day of it — because day thirty cannot be counted as a valid conclusion. By waiting until day thirty-one, the *nazir* ensures that even if *tumah* occurs on that day, it falls entirely outside the completed thirty-day unit, which by then is unambiguously in the past. The *lechatchila* is thus a safeguard: it protects against a scenario in which *tumah* on the final day would force a complete restart.

## Rabbi Eliezer's 30 Days

### 7 vs 30 Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds that Rav Mattana can explain Rabbi Eliezer's opinion as follows: This does not prove that an unspecified term of naziriteship is less than thirty days, as **Rabbi Eliezer holds** that the legal status of **part of the day is like** that **of an entire** day, and it is viewed as though the nazirite has completed his term of naziriteship at the start of the thirtieth day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מֵאָה יוֹם״, נִטְמָא יוֹם מֵאָה — סוֹתֵר אֶת הַכֹּל. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ סוֹתֵר אֶלָּא שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ סָבַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ — נִסְתּוֹר שִׁבְעָה, וְאִי לָא סָבַר מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ — לִיסְתּוֹר כּוּלְּהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with Rabbi Eliezer's opinion. **We learned** in the mishna (16a): If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite** for **one hundred days,** if **he became impure** on the **one hundredth day, it negates the entire** tally. He must bring offerings for having become impure, and he then begins his term of naziriteship anew. **Rabbi Eliezer says: It negates only thirty days.** The Gemara comments: **And if it enters your mind** that **Rabbi Eliezer holds** that the legal status of **part of the day is like** that **of an entire** day, the nazirite **should negate seven** days because it is considered as though he became impure after completing his term of naziriteship. **And if he does not hold** that **part of the day is like an entire** day, **he should negate all of** the days, since he became impure during his naziriteship.

The *gemara* continues by examining the end of the *mishna*, where *Rabbi Eliezer* disagrees with the ruling that *tumah* on day thirty undermines everything: *Rabbi Eliezer* says it undermines only seven days. This makes sense if *Rabbi Eliezer* holds that day thirty is already beyond the completed *nezirut* — whatever happens there cannot retroactively erase what preceded. But now the *gemara* presents a second case in the same *mishna*: a person explicitly vowed a hundred days of *nezirut* and became *tamei* on day 100. Here *Rabbi Eliezer* says the *tumah* undermines thirty days, not seven and not a hundred.

This is deeply puzzling. According to *Rav Masna*'s framework, *Rabbi Eliezer* holds *miktzas hayom kekulo* in its most absolute form — an iron wall descends at the moment the partial day begins, making everything that follows completely irrelevant. On that account, *tumah* on day 100 should undermine only seven days, not thirty. And according to *Bar Pada*'s framework, *Rabbi Eliezer* holds there is no *miktzas hayom kekulo* at all — which would mean that on day 100 of an explicitly stated hundred-day *nezirut*, the *nazir* has not yet completed his obligation, so *tumah* would require him to do the entire hundred days over again.

Neither framework produces thirty days. *Rabbi Eliezer* seems to contradict himself.

### Bar Pada Sides Eliezer

*Bar Pada* now clarifies his position. He states that regarding the earlier *mishna* — the standard thirty-day case where *Rabbi Eliezer* said only seven days need be redone — he concedes the *chachamim*'s theoretical framework. The *chachamim* hold that thirty days are required and debate with *Rabbi Eliezer* only over how to conceptualize *miktzas hayom kekulo*: whether the partial day leaves the remainder of the day still consequential (the *chachamim*'s view) or closes everything off completely (what *Rav Masna* attributes to *Rabbi Eliezer*). *Bar Pada* concedes that the *chachamim*'s position on thirty days is internally coherent, but aligns himself with *Rabbi Eliezer* — whom he understands as holding twenty-nine full days and no *miktzas hayom kekulo*. On that account, when someone becomes *tamei* on day thirty of a standard *nezirut*, it is already past the completion point, so *tumah* there cannot impinge on the main unit — hence only seven days.

But this still leaves *Rabbi Eliezer*'s ruling of thirty days in the hundred-day case unexplained, on any account.

### Last Day Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם לָא אָמְרִינַן מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ. אִי הָכִי לִיסְתּוֹר כּוּלְּהוּ! אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַנָּזִיר בְּיוֹם מְלֹאת יְמֵי נִזְרוֹ״, הַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: נִטְמָא בְּיוֹם מְלֹאת — תֵּן לוֹ תּוֹרַת נָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually, we do not say** that the legal status of **part of the day is like** that **of an entire** day. The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** then **let him negate all of** the days. **Reish Lakish said** that **this is the reason of Rabbi Eliezer: The verse states: "This is the law of the nazirite, on the day when the days of his consecration are complete"** (Numbers 6:13). **The Torah stated:** If **he became impure on the day of the completion** of his term of naziriteship, **give him the *halakha* of** an unspecified **nazirite,** and he must recount an unspecified term of naziriteship, which is thirty days.

*Reish Lakish* provides the answer: there is a special *halacha* — not derived from logical extension of either *miktzas hayom* or the twenty-nine-day principle, but from a specific *drashah* on a *pasuk* — that applies uniquely when one becomes *tamei* on the very last day of a *nezirut*.

### Torah Verse Proof

*Reish Lakish* cites the verse: *zot tihiyeh torat ha-Nazir b'yom melot yemei nizro* — "this shall be the law of the *nazir* on the day that the days of his *nezirut* are completed." The *Torah* is teaching: if one becomes *tamei* specifically on the *yom melot* — the final day of the *nezirut* — then *ten lo torat nazir*, give him the law of the *nazir*, meaning a standard *nezirut* of thirty days. This is not derived from any general principle about *miktzas hayom*; it is a discrete *halacha* extracted from the verse. If you become *tamei* on your final day, whatever the duration of your *nezirut*, you do not redo the entire *nezirut* — you do a standard one.

This explains *Rabbi Eliezer*'s ruling of thirty days in the hundred-day case. A hundred days were explicitly stipulated; *miktzas hayom* is irrelevant for *Bar Pada* who rejects it. The *nazir* became *tamei* on day 100 — the final day. The verse's special *halacha* kicks in: he does a standard *nezirut* of thirty days, not a hundred. And in the earlier case — *tumah* on day thirty of a standard *nezirut* — according to *Bar Pada*, day thirty is already past the twenty-nine-day completion point, so day thirty is not the "last day" in the relevant sense; day twenty-nine was. Therefore the special *halacha* of *tumah* on the last day does not apply, and *tumah* on day thirty is simply *tumah* that occurs after the *nezirut* concluded, requiring only the standard seven-day purification process. *Reish Lakish* concedes there is no logical derivation for the thirty-day ruling; it is a *halacha* extracted from the *Torah*'s formulation.

## Melot Hayamim Debate

### Yoshiya vs Yonatan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״עַד מְלֹאת הַיָּמִים״, שׁוֹמְעַנִי מִיעוּט ״יָמִים״ שְׁנַיִם — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״קָדוֹשׁ יִהְיֶה גַּדֵּל פֶּרַע״, אֵין גִּידּוּל שֵׂעָר פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § Until this point the Gemara has been attempting to prove whether the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rav Mattana or bar Padda. The Gemara now suggests: **Let us say** that this is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"Until the days are complete,** in which he consecrated himself to the Lord, he shall be holy, he shall let the locks of the hair of his head grow long"** (Numbers 6:5). **I hear** from this: The **least** amount of days that the plural **"days"** can indicate is **two,** implying that one can become a nazirite for a term of two days. Therefore, **the verse states: "He shall be holy, he shall let** the locks of the hair of his head **grow long,"** and **there is no** significant **growth of hair** in **less than thirty** days. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״עַד מְלֹאת הַיָּמִים״, אִי אֵלּוּ הֵן יָמִים שֶׁצְּרִיכִין לְמַלּאוֹת? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר שְׁלֹשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yonatan says:** This proof is **not needed, as** the verse **says: "Until the days are complete." What are the days that require completion? You must say** this is referring to the days of a month. Since a lunar cycle lasts for approximately twenty-nine and a half days, some months are twenty-nine days, whereas full months are **thirty** days. This demonstrates that a term of naziriteship must be at least one month.

The *gemara* continues by examining the end of the *mishna*, where *Rabbi Eliezer* disagrees with the ruling that *tumah* on day thirty undermines everything: *Rabbi Eliezer* says it undermines only seven days. This makes sense if *Rabbi Eliezer* holds that day thirty is already beyond the completed *nezirut* — whatever happens there cannot retroactively erase what preceded. But now the *gemara* presents a second case in the same *mishna*: a person explicitly vowed a hundred days of *nezirut* and became *tamei* on day 100. Here *Rabbi Eliezer* says the *tumah* undermines thirty days, not seven and not a hundred.

*Rabbi Yoshiya* derives from this that there is no *gidul pera* — the required hair growth that is the hallmark of *nezirut* — in fewer than thirty days: *ein gidul pera sear pachos mishloshim*. His proof-text is the phrase *kadosh yihye gadel pera* — specifically the word *yihye*, whose *gematria* is thirty.

*Rabbi Yonatan* says the thirty-day requirement needs no derivation from *yihye*; it follows from the phrase *ad melot hayamim* itself. The concept of "days that need to be filled" implies a unit that can be either full or less than full — and the only temporal unit that admits of such variability is a month. A week is always seven days; it admits no partial version. A year is counted by months, not by days (as the *Rabbanan of Kisari* will clarify below), so a year does not yield a number of days in the relevant sense. A month, however, can be twenty-nine or thirty days — it can be full or deficient — and the notion of "filling" days points precisely to that structure.

### Gematria Yihye

The *gematria* of *yihye* — *yud* (10), *heh* (5), *yud* (10), *heh* (5) — equals thirty. *Rabbi Yoshiya* uses this *gematria* to establish the thirty-day minimum for *nezirut* from the word *yihye* in the verse. This is the hermeneutic *Rav Masna* is associated with in the *gemara*'s analysis.

### Rav Masna's Unity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו: רַב מַתְנָא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה, וּבַר פְּדָא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **What, is it not** the case that **Rav Mattana said in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoshiya,** who derives the minimum length of a term of naziriteship from the phrase "he shall be holy, he shall let his hair grow long," and therefore a term of naziriteship lasts thirty days, **and bar Padda said in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yonatan,** who derives this *halakha* from the length of a month, and since a month can be twenty-nine days, the minimum length of a term of naziriteship is also twenty-nine days?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב מַתְנָא: כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שְׁלֹשִׁים בָּעִינַן, וְהָכָא בְּ״עַד״ וְ״עַד״ בַּכְּלָל פְּלִיגִי. רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה סָבַר: ״עַד״ וְלֹא ״עַד״ בַּכְּלָל. וְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן סָבַר: ״עַד״ וְ״עַד״ בַּכְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rav Mattana** could have **said to you: Everyone** agrees that **we require** a term of naziriteship to last at least **thirty** days, **and here they disagree with regard to** whether **"until"** means **until and including. Rabbi Yoshiya holds** that **"until** the days are complete" indicates that a term of naziriteship must last **until, but not** necessarily **including,** the thirtieth day of a full month. Consequently, he derives the minimum length of a term of naziriteship from the phrase "he shall let the locks of the hair of his head grow long." **And Rabbi Yonatan holds** that the term **"until"** means **until and including** the thirtieth day of a full month, and he derives the minimum length of a term of naziriteship from the phrase "until the days are complete."

*Rav Masna* argues that the two *tannaitic* positions — *Rabbi Yoshiya* and *Rabbi Yonatan* — are not actually in dispute about the number of days. Both agree that thirty days are required. Their disagreement concerns the interpretive question of *ad v'ad bichlal*: does the word *ad* (until) mean "up to and including" or "up to but not including"? *Rabbi Yoshiya* reads *ad* as exclusive — "until but not including" — and therefore cannot derive thirty days from *ad melot hayamim* alone; he supplements with the *gematria* of *yihye* to reach thirty. *Rabbi Yonatan* reads *ad* as inclusive — "until and including" — and therefore *ad melot hayamim* by itself implies thirty full days, the full month inclusive.

On this reading, *Rav Masna* can claim that both *tannaim* support his view: everyone agrees that thirty days are required. The interpretive dispute about *ad* is purely internal to the verse and does not affect the substantive ruling. This is the stronger posture for an *amora*: demonstrating that one's position is consistent with all *tannaitic* opinions.

### Ad Inclusivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אִי אֵלּוּ הֵן יָמִים שֶׁצְּרִיכִין לְמַלּאוֹת — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר שְׁלֹשִׁים. וְאֵימָא שַׁבָּת! שַׁבָּת מִי אִיכָּא חַסִּירוּתָא? וְאֵימָא שָׁנָה? מִי מָנֵינַן לְיוֹמֵי?! וְהָא רַבָּנַן דְּקֵיסָרִי אָמְרִי: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין מוֹנִין יָמִים לְשָׁנִים — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְחׇדְשֵׁי הַשָּׁנָה״, חֳדָשִׁים מְחַשְּׁבִין לְשָׁנִים, וְלֹא יָמִים לְשָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now analyzes the *baraita* that cites the opinions of Rabbi Yoshiya and Rabbi Yonatan. **The Master,** Rabbi Yonatan, **said: What are the days that require completion? You must say** this is referring to the **thirty** days of a full month. The Gemara asks: **But say** it is referring to the seven days of **a week.** The Gemara responds: In the case of **a week, is there** ever a week that is **lacking?** All weeks are seven days. Conversely, since some months are twenty-nine days and others are thirty days, it can be said that the thirtieth day completes that which was lacking. The Gemara asks further: **But say** it is referring to **a year,** which can also be full or lacking. The Gemara responds: **Do we count** years **by days? Didn't the Sages of Caesarea say: From where** is it derived **that one does not count days toward years,** but that years are calculated according to months? **As it is stated: "Of the months of the year"** (Exodus 12:2). This teaches that **months are calculated to** comprise **years, but days are not** counted **toward years.** Therefore, only a month can be described as being completed by a particular day.

*Bar Pada*'s position, by contrast, requires conceding that *Rabbi Yoshiya*'s view does not support him. *Bar Pada* can align himself only with *Rabbi Yonatan*, and specifically with a particular reading of *Rabbi Yonatan*: that *ad melot hayamim* means "until the fullness of the days," with *ad* understood as exclusive — up to but *not* including the thirtieth day. On that reading, the verse points to twenty-nine days. This is, admittedly, a somewhat strained reading of *Rabbi Yonatan*, since *Rabbi Yonatan*'s own stated logic seems to lead to thirty days, but *Bar Pada* exploits the ambiguity in the word *ad* to extract a twenty-nine-day reading from it.

### Rabbanan Kisari

The *gemara* asks: why not interpret *melot hayamim* as referring to a year rather than a month? A year also admits of variability — Hebrew years can be shorter or longer depending on whether months are full or deficient and whether an extra month is intercalated. The *gemara* answers by citing the *Rabbanan of Kisari*, who teach: *minayin she'ein monin yamin leshanim?* — how do we know that years are not counted by days? From the verse *lechodeshei hashana* — "for the months of the year." Years are reckoned by months, not by days. Since *melot hayamim* invokes the counting of *yamim* — days — it cannot refer to a unit measured by months. Only a month, whose duration is itself measured in days and which can genuinely be full (thirty days) or deficient (twenty-nine), fits the category of a unit whose "days need to be filled."

## Indefinite Expressions

### Big/Small Nezirut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר אַחַת גְּדוֹלָה״, ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר אַחַת קְטַנָּה״, אֲפִילּוּ: ״מִכָּאן וְעַד סוֹף הָעוֹלָם״ — נָזִיר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one said: I am hereby a nazirite** for **one long** term, or: **I am hereby a nazirite** for **one short** term, or **even** if one said: I am hereby a nazirite **from now until the end of the world,** in all these cases he is **a nazirite** for **thirty days.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַמַּאי? וְהָא ״מִכָּאן וְעַד סוֹף הָעוֹלָם״ קָאָמַר! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֲרִיכָא לִי הָדָא מִילְּתָא כְּמִכָּאן וְעַד סוֹף הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna taught that even one who said: I am hereby a nazirite from now until the end of the world, becomes a nazirite for thirty days. The Gemara asks: **Why** does he become a nazirite for only thirty days? **Didn't he say: From now until the end of the world?** The Gemara answers: **This is what he is saying:** Because of the difficulties it entails, it is as though **this matter** of naziriteship **were as lengthy for me as** the time **from now until the end of the world.**

A *mishna* now establishes the practical scope of *stam nezirut*. If someone says *hareini nazir achat gedola* — "behold I am a *nazir*, a single big *nezirut*" — or *hareini nazir achat ketana* — "a single small *nezirut*" — he is a *nazir* for thirty days. The qualifiers "big" and "small" are meaningless without a specified duration. A person who says "big" or "small" has still said only that he is a *nazir*; and *stam nezirut* is thirty days. Since no number was given, no number can be derived. "Big" might mean it will feel big, or require great effort — but it does not communicate a time.

### Distance Limits

More striking is the *mishna*'s ruling: even if someone says *hareini nazir mikan ad sof ha'olam* — "behold I am a *nazir* from here to the end of the world" — he is nonetheless a *nazir* for thirty days. The expression *ad sof ha'olam* here is locative, not temporal — it describes a physical distance ("from here to China," as it were), not a duration of time. Since *nezirut* is measured in days, not in distance, distance language does not translate into an extended *nezirut*. The *gemara* explains: when someone says this, the intent is hyperbolic — this *nezirut* is going to feel as long as the journey from here to the end of the world. But since no time duration was specified, only the default *stam nezirut* of thirty days applies.

## Mishna-Braita Reconciliation

### Braita Distance-Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מִכָּאן עַד מָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״ — אוֹמְדִים כַּמָּה יָמִים מִכָּאן וְעַד מָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי, פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם — נָזִיר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. וְאִם לָאו — נָזִיר כְּמִנְיַן הַיָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **We learned** in the mishna (8a): If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite from here until such and such a place, one estimates how many days** it takes to walk **from here until such and such a place.** If it is a distance of **less than thirty days,** he is **a nazirite** for **thirty days,** since this is the minimum term of naziriteship. **And if not,** i.e., if it takes more than thirty days to walk that distance, he is **a nazirite in accordance with the number of days** it takes to walk to that place.

A *braita*, however, seems to rule differently. If someone says *hareini nazir mikan ve'ad makom ploni* — "behold I am a *nazir* from here until such-and-such place" — we calculate how many days it takes to travel from here to that place. If the travel time is less than thirty days, he is a *nazir* for thirty days (since *nezirut* cannot be less than thirty). If the travel time is more than thirty days, he is a *nazir* for the actual number of travel days. Here, distance is explicitly being translated into time duration, in apparent contradiction to the *mishna*.

### Rava on Journey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שֶׁהֶחְזִיק בַּדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** That mishna is referring to one **who had** already **set out on the way,** so that it is apparent that his intention is to be a nazirite until he reaches his destination.

*Rava* resolves the contradiction: the *braita* is discussing a case where the person *hechzik baderech* — has already set out on the journey. Once he is already traveling, saying "from here until that place" naturally expresses duration rather than mere spatial distance. When you are on the road and say "until we get there," you are measuring in time — thinking about how long until we arrive, not how many miles remain. In that context, translating distance into travel time is perfectly natural. But if a person is sitting at home and says he will be a *nazir* from here to the end of the world, there is no journey underway, no natural conversion of distance to duration, and the expression is best understood as rhetorical emphasis, not as a specification of time.

*Tosafot* adds a further nuance: when one is on a journey, the vow may carry an element of seeking divine protection for the duration of the travel, which further explains why distance would be understood as defining the time frame of the *nezirut*.

### Miles Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיהְוֵי כׇּל פַּרְסָה וּפַרְסָה! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּאַתְרָא דְּלָא מָנֵי פַּרְסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why is it assumed that he means to accept a single term of naziriteship that is the length of time it takes to travel to a certain destination? **And let each and every parasang [*parsa*]** be understood to refer to a separate term of naziriteship, such that the individual is understood to have accepted a separate term of naziriteship for each parasang that he must travel. **Rav Pappa said:** The mishna deals **with a place where** people **do not measure** distances in **parasangs.**

One might ask: even in the *mishna*'s case, where the person is not on a journey, could the distance at least yield a number — and could that number then indicate *how many* sequential *neziruyot* the person has accepted? After all, if the world is a certain number of *parsa'ot* across, then "from here to the end of the world" might mean that many sequential units of thirty-day *nezirut*. The same question applies to *Rava*'s case: why not say that each *parsa* of the journey represents one separate *nezirut*?

### Rav Pappa No Units

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל מִילְּתָא דְּאִית בַּיהּ קִיצּוּתָא לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Everything that has a fixed amount is not taught** in this clause, which states that one is a nazirite forever. When one declares that he will be a nazirite for a length of time corresponding to an item that people consider infinite, e.g., the dust of the earth, his intention is not to define the length of a single term of naziriteship, as it is unknown how many grains of dust there are. However, when one mentions something that does have a fixed number, e.g., the number of days it takes to walk to a certain place, his intention is to define the length of a single term of naziriteship.

*Rav Pappa* answers: *be-atra de-lo manu parsei* — in a locale where distances are not measured in *parsa'ot*. Without a fixed, conventional unit of measurement, no determinate number emerges from the distance expression. "From here to that place" does not yield a number if there is no standard way of measuring the intervening distance. And since no number can be extracted, no count of sequential *neziruyot* can be established. The same logic disposes of the question of counting by overnight lodgings along the route: even if one tried to count nightly stops, that still runs into the underlying problem that the vow was framed in spatial terms and there is no fixed unit that converts cleanly into a number of *neziruyot*.

## Countable vs Uncountable

### Nazir Olam Forms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיהְוֵי כׇּל אַוּוֹנָא וְאַוּוֹנָא! מִי לָא תְּנַן: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר כַּעֲפַר הָאָרֶץ״, וְ״כִשְׂעַר רֹאשִׁי״, וּ״כְחוֹל הַיָּם״ — הֲרֵי זֶה נְזִיר עוֹלָם, וּמְגַלֵּחַ אֶחָד לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **And let each and every way station [*avvana*],** where travelers lodge at night, be understood to refer to a separate term of naziriteship, such that the individual is understood to have accepted a separate term of naziriteship for each station that he passes along the way. **Didn't we learn** in a mishna (8a): If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite like the dust of the earth, or: Like the hair of my head, or: Like the sand of the sea, he is a nazirite forever,** as it is understood that he accepted upon himself terms of naziriteship in accordance with the number of his hairs, or grains of dust, or sand, **and he shaves once** every **thirty days?**

A final *mishna* presents cases that at first appear similar but yield dramatically different results. If someone says *hareini nazir ke-afar ha-aretz* — "like the dust of the earth" — or *ke-sear roshi* — "like the hairs of my head" — or *ke-chol ha-yam* — "like the sand of the sea" — he is a *nazir olam*, a *nazir* forever, and cuts his hair every thirty days. The mechanism here is conceptually distinct from a single extended *nezirut*: the person has accepted upon himself an effectively infinite sequence of sequential thirty-day *neziruyot*, each terminating with a *korban* and immediately followed by the next. In practice he will be a *nazir* for his entire life, bringing a *korban* at the end of each thirty-day unit and immediately beginning the next.

This raises the question: why does "like the dust of the earth" — clearly a spatial or quantity reference — translate into an infinite number of sequential *neziruyot*, while "from here to the end of the world" — also a spatial reference — translates into only one thirty-day *nezirut*?

### Ketzisusa Principle

The *gemara* articulates the principle: *kol milta d'is bei ketzisusa lo k'tani* — anything that has a fixed, determinate number is not what was taught. That is: expressions like "the hairs of my head" or "the sand of the sea" are understood as conveying an uncountable, practically infinite quantity. No one seriously believes they can enumerate every grain of sand on the sea or every hair on a head. The expression is understood as meaning an unlimited, indeterminate number — and therefore it is taken as expressing an intention to accept an unlimited series of *neziruyot*.

By contrast, "from here to the end of the world" — despite sounding equally hyperbolic — could in principle yield a concrete number depending on what unit of measurement is applied. The circumference of the world, as the *gemara* elsewhere records, is a specific figure (five hundred *parsa'ot*). Because a determinate number *could* be extracted, even if we do not know which unit the speaker has in mind, the expression does not clearly convey an infinite sequence. Since no definite time duration is specified and no definite number of sequential *neziruyot* is clearly implied, the vow defaults to a single standard *nezirut* of thirty days.

The principle, in short: where a spatial or quantity expression is so vast that no number can concretely emerge from it — sand of the sea, hairs of the head — the vow is taken as infinite sequential *neziruyot*. Where the expression, however expansive it sounds, could theoretically be resolved into a concrete number, it is treated as mere emphasis and yields only the default single *nezirut*.
