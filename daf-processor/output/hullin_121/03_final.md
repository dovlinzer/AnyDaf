# Hullin 121 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Liquids: Wine & Oil

### Liquids Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן: אֵין מְבִיאִין בִּכּוּרִים מַשְׁקֶה אֶלָּא הַיּוֹצֵא מִן הַזֵּיתִים וּמִן הָעֲנָבִים – מַנִּי?
>
> **Translation:** **And** it explains **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 11:3): **One may bring beverages** made from **first fruits** to the Temple **only** in the case of **that which emerges from the olives,** i.e., oil, **or from the grapes,** i.e., wine. The Gemara asks: In accordance with **whose** opinion is that mishna?

The discussion opens at the very bottom of 120a and carries into 121a, examining whether liquids that emerge from solids—or that liquefy solids—possess recognized halakhic status. Much of the preceding daf had been occupied with verses suggesting that they do: *chelev* in liquefied form, *chametz* in liquid form, and similar cases. But the sugya now closes on a more qualified, even negative note, establishing that when it comes to *terumah*, at least one position holds that only wine and oil are recognized liquids—no other type of liquid, even one derived from grain that would be *terumah* on a biblical level, enjoys halakhic standing.

### Bikkurim Liquids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא, דְּאָמַר: דּוּן מִינַּהּ וְאוֹקֵי בְאַתְרַהּ, וְגָמַר לְהוּ לְבִכּוּרִים מִתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, who says: Infer from it but interpret** the *halakha* **according to its** own **place.** He derives the *halakha* of liquid that emerges from *teruma* from first fruits only with regard to wine and oil, **and** in the opposite direction, **he derives** the *halakha* **of** liquid that emerges from **first fruits from *teruma*.** Therefore, even with regard to first fruits the status of liquid that emerges from the produce is like that of the produce itself only with regard to wine and oil. But according to the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that the *halakha* of liquid that emerges from *teruma* is derived from first fruits even with regard to other types of produce besides grapes and olives, contrary to the statement of that mishna, one may bring beverages extracted from other first fruits besides wine and oil to the Temple.

The Gemara introduces the point with a *mishnah*: *ein mevi'in bikkurim mashkeh ela hayotzei min ha-zeisim u-min ha-anavim*—one may only bring *bikkurim* in liquid form if that liquid is what comes from olives or grapes, meaning olive oil or wine. Earlier in the discussion, the sugya had noted that if fruit designated as *bikkurim* was subsequently pressed into liquid, that liquid is acceptable. But the *mishnah* here draws a sharp limit: the only liquid form that counts is oil or wine. The Gemara then asks: whose view does this reflect?

### R. Yehoshua Rules

The answer is: *Rabbi Yehoshua hi*—this follows Rabbi Yehoshua, who took the same position regarding *terumah*: only wine and olive oil possess recognized halakhic status as liquids; any other liquid, even one that derives from produce that is subject to *terumah* obligations, does not. The Gemara explains his derivation: *amar dami leh ve-kava lah*—he identifies an analogy between *terumah* and *bikkurim*, situating the rule within the context of *terumah*, where the Torah explicitly mentions wine and oil. Having established the rule for *terumah*, one then applies it back to *bikkurim* as well.

### Three Linked Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן: אֵין סוֹפְגִין אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים מִשּׁוּם עׇרְלָה, אֶלָּא עַל הַיּוֹצֵא מִן הַזֵּיתִים וּמִן הָעֲנָבִים – מַנִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites **that which we learned** in the same mishna (*Terumot* 11:3): **One incurs the forty** lashes **due to** drinking the juice squeezed from ***orla*,** i.e., the fruit of a tree during the first three years after its planting, **only for that which emerges from the olives or from the grapes.** The Gemara asks: In accordance with **whose** opinion is that mishna?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא, דְּאָמַר: דּוּן מִינַּהּ וְאוֹקֵי בְּאַתְרַהּ, וְגָמַר לְהוּ לְבִכּוּרִים מִתְּרוּמָה, וַהֲדַר מַיְיתֵי לַהּ לְעׇרְלָה ״פְּרִי״ ״פְּרִי״ מִבִּכּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, who says: Infer from it but interpret** the *halakha* **according to its** own **place, and he derives** the *halakha* **of** liquid that emerges from **first fruits from *teruma*.** Therefore, even with regard to first fruits, the status of liquid that emerges from the produce is like that of the produce itself only with regard to wine and oil. **And then he derives** the *halakha* **of** liquid that emerges from ***orla* from first fruits** via a verbal analogy between one instance of the word **fruit** and another instance of the word **fruit.** With regard to *orla* the verse states: "And you shall count the fruit thereof as forbidden" (Leviticus 19:23), and with regard to first fruits the verse states: "And you shall take of the first of all the fruit of the ground" (Deuteronomy 26:2). Therefore, just as with regard to first fruits the status of liquid that emerges from the produce is like that of the produce only with regard to grapes and olives, so too with regard to *orla* one receives lashes only for drinking the liquid of grapes and olives, but not for drinking the liquid of other types of produce.

The Gemara then adds a third case—*ain sofgin et ha-arba'im mishum orlah ela ha-yotzei min ha-zeisim u-min ha-anavim*: one only receives lashes for violating *orlah* (the prohibition on fruit of the first three years) in liquid form if the liquid consumed is olive oil, wine, or grape juice. Any other liquid form does not incur lashes. Here too the Gemara attributes the view to Rabbi Yehoshua, using the same mechanism. First, *bikkurim* is linked to *terumah*: just as *terumah* is only wine and oil, so too *bikkurim* is only wine and oil. Then, the Gemara reports a *gezeirah shavah* from the shared term *pri*—the word *pri ha-adamah* appears in the context of *bikkurim*, and the same root appears in the laws of *orlah*—and from this verbal analogy, the limitation on *bikkurim* is extended to *orlah* as well.

So after an entire daf exploring how many obligations attach to liquefied or liquidified forms of produce, the conclusion reached in these three domains—*terumah*, *bikkurim*, and *orlah*—is that at minimum one significant position restricts recognized liquids to wine and oil alone.

### Zei'ah Be'alma

What gives this position even broader weight is a statement in *Pesachim*. The Gemara there cites the same rule—that lashes for *orlah* apply only to wine and oil—and uses it to ground the sweeping conclusion that the juice of fruits other than wine and oil is *zei'ah be'alma*, mere sweat, possessing no halakhic status whatsoever. This is not a limited exception carved out for one domain; it is a global pronouncement about the standing of fruit juice. And in practice, this is largely how *halakhah* treats the matter: for instance, the only act of squeezing for which one violates *Shabbos* on a biblical level is squeezing olives or grapes, because only those products are considered to produce something genuinely significant.

### Shabbat Squeezing

This principle has direct practical application on *Shabbos*. While squeezing many types of fruit is prohibited on *Shabbos*, the biblical-level prohibition—*de-oraita*—applies only to squeezing olives (for oil) and grapes (for wine or grape juice), because only those liquids are regarded as producing something with real halakhic significance. Other fruit liquids, by contrast, fall into the category of *zei'ah be'alma*. The same logic underlies the uncertainty about whether *terumos u-ma'asros* can be taken from produce after it has been pressed: since the resulting liquid may not count as the fruit itself, the obligations may not follow. In any event, wherever the category is fruit juice as distinct from, say, liquefied meat, the general rule appears to be that only wine and olive oil matter.

### Central Question

This raises the fundamental question of what is rule and what is exception. Is the general principle that a liquid form of something does not have the halakhic status of the thing itself—with a handful of specific exceptions such as *chelev* and *chametz*? Or is the general principle that it does, with fruit juice carving out its own specialized rule that only wine and oil count? Or perhaps the rule is domain-specific: by fruit juice, only wine and oil; by liquefied meat, a different analysis would apply. It is worth noting that the entire halakhic discussion took shape in a cultural context in which drinking orange juice was entirely foreign—nobody even drank milk routinely, which is why the Gemara discussing *basar b'chalav* always focuses on cheese rather than milk. Against that backdrop, the notion of fruit juice as a significant category was simply not intuitive.

### Ta'am K'ikar Problem

*Tosafot* sharpens the difficulty by raising a third problem that the discussion has not yet addressed: what about *ta'am k'ikar*—the principle that the taste of something counts like the substance itself? If one boils water together with *orlah* fruit, the taste of the fruit pervades the water, and that water should be forbidden on account of *ta'am k'ikar*. Why, then, would squeezing *orlah* fruit not produce juice that is forbidden for the same reason? Even if the juice is not technically regarded as the fruit itself, it carries the taste of the fruit, and *ta'am k'ikar* should render it prohibited. A possible answer is that the juice is indeed forbidden but at a lesser level of severity—one would not receive lashes for it because it is not the substance itself, but it could still be prohibited through *ta'am k'ikar*. The question, however, remains genuinely open.

### Unresolved Issues

Several issues thus remain unresolved. First, there is a logical distinction between liquefying something and liquids that merely run off of something—the Gemara largely conflated these two scenarios, but one could argue they deserve separate treatment. Second, even if the halakhic standing of the liquid is unclear, the question of *ta'am k'ikar* remains operative and complicates any clean resolution.

The most significant practical application of this cluster of unresolved questions involves the status of liquor on *Pesach*. Liquor is a liquid derived from grain, and three distinct positions emerge from the analysis. Some hold it is *chametz gamur*—fully prohibited grain—on the basis of the principle that a liquidified form retains the status of the original substance. Others invoke the Gemara in *Pesachim* and argue that since grain-derived liquor is *zei'ah be'alma*, it may in fact be permissible to drink on *Pesach*, having no halakhic status as *chametz*. A third position, following *Tosafot*, holds that even if it does not have the full status of *chametz*, it must at minimum be forbidden through *ta'am k'ikar*, since the taste of grain pervades it. The final answer remains genuinely unclear—multiple possibilities exist without a clean systematic resolution.

## Meat Combinations

### Alaw Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָלָל. מַאי אָלָל? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מַרְטְקָא, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: בָּשָׂר שֶׁפְּלָטַתּוּ סַכִּין.
>
> **Translation:** §The mishna teaches that **the *alal*** joins together with the flesh to constitute the requisite egg-bulk to impart the impurity of food, despite not being considered food itself. The Gemara asks: To **what** is the term ***alal*** referring? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is referring to **the nuchal ligament [*marteka*]. And Reish Lakish says:** It is referring to the **meat** residue **that** is attached to the hide after **the knife has flayed** the flesh.

With the discussion of liquids concluded, the *mishnah* turns to a further category: things that combine with meat to count toward the minimum halakhic measure but are not themselves considered food when standing alone. The *mishnah* uses the term *alaw*. What exactly is this *alaw*? Rabbi Yochanan says it is *martka*. Rashi explains *martka* as a large structure—he calls it a *gid*, which literally means a ligament—running down the back and along the side of the neck. It turns out anatomically this is indeed a ligament (*ligamentum nuchae* in cattle), which holds up the head. It is something extremely difficult to eat on its own. *Tosafot* accepts the identification but adds a functional explanation: while one would not eat this ligament by itself, it might be cooked together with the surrounding meat, or one would encounter it as part of a piece and eat it along with the meat. *Tosafot* also cites Rabbeinu Chananel, who identifies the *alaw* differently—as a piece of rotting or decomposing flesh, which no one would eat on its own, but which, if it happened to be part of one's portion of meat, one would consume along with the rest rather than remove.

### Meat on Skin

Reish Lakish offers a competing interpretation: *basar she-paltoso sakin*, flesh that is "spit out" by the knife during the flaying process. When flaying an animal, the skin is removed with a blade, and in the same way that peeling a cucumber with a knife leaves some of the flesh adhering to the peel, the flaying knife leaves scraps of meat stuck to the hide. Normally this meat would be discarded along with the skin—it is meat, but nobody bothers to eat it. However, if someone does intend to eat it, they could scrape it off the skin and consume it with the rest. So this too is something that is not by itself treated as food, but might be eaten together with meat.

### Job Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״וְאוּלָם אַתֶּם טֹפְלֵי שָׁקֶר רֹפְאֵי אֱלִל כֻּלְּכֶם״. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מַרְטְקָא – הַיְינוּ דְּלָאו בַּר רְפוּאָה הוּא, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בָּשָׂר שֶׁפְּלָטַתּוּ סַכִּין – בַּר רְפוּאָה הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the explanation of Reish Lakish from that which is written: **"But you are plasterers of lies, you are all physicians of no value [*elil*]"** (Job 13:4). The term "no value [*elil*]" stems from the same linguistic root as the word *alal*. **Granted, according to the one who says** that the word *alal* is referring to **the nuchal ligament,** i.e., Rabbi Yoḥanan, **that is** why Job accused his companions of giving advice without merit by making an analogy to a physician who attempts to heal the nuchal ligament, **which cannot be healed. But according to the one who says** that the word *alal* is referring to the **meat** residue **that** is attached to the hide after **the knife has flayed** the flesh, i.e., Reish Lakish, flesh that is hanging from the hide **is able to be healed.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאָלָל דִּקְרָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְלִיגִי; כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בָּאָלָל דְּמַתְנִיתִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With regard to** the term ***elil* in the verse, everyone agrees** that it is referring to the nuchal ligament. **When** Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish **disagree,** it is **with regard to** the definition of the term ***alal*** employed by the Sages **in the mishna.**

The Gemara seeks to determine which definition applies by citing a verse. In the book of *Iyov*, among the speeches of Job's would-be comforters, it says: *ve-ulam atem toflei sheker rof'ei elil kulchem*—you are plasterers of lies, healers of an *elil* are you all. The *elil* is something that cannot be healed. The Gemara then tests each interpretation: *bishlama le-man de-amar martka*, if the *alaw* is indeed the ligament or the rotting flesh, it fits perfectly—a severed ligament cannot heal, and rotting flesh certainly cannot. But *ela le-man de-amar basar she-paltoso sakin*, if it is merely flesh scraped off during flaying, that is something that could conceivably heal if it were cut from a person's body, so why does the verse describe it as incurable?

The Gemara resolves this by distinguishing between two usages of the term. The verse in *Iyov* uses one form of the word, and everyone agrees that in the verse it refers either to the rotting flesh or to the inedible ligament—there is no debate about the verse. The debate is about what the word means in the *mishnah*, because a *mishnah* need not use language identically to a biblical verse.

### R. Yehuda Tumah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הָאָלָל הַמְכוּנָּס, אִם יֵשׁ כְּזַיִת בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו, וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: וְהוּא שֶׁכִּנְּסוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution from that which is taught in the mishna: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to **the *alal* that** was **collected, if there is an olive-bulk** of it **in one place** it imparts the impurity of animal carcasses. Therefore, one who eats it or touches it and then eats consecrated food or enters the Temple **is liable** to receive *karet* **for it. And Rav Huna said:** This *halakha* **is** applicable only **when** a halakhically competent person **collected** the *alal* in one place, but not if the *alal* was collected by a child or without human intervention. By collecting it in one place, the person indicates that he considers it to be food.

The *mishnah* then records Rabbi Yehuda's position: *ha-alu ha-mechunas—im yesh bo kezayit be-makom echad, chayav alav*. If one gathers together this *alu* into one place and there is a *kezayit* of it in that place, one incurs the consequences—meaning it carries the *tumah* of a *neveilah*. This is a significant statement: not only can the *alaw* combine with meat to constitute food susceptible to *tumah*, but if gathered together in sufficient quantity, it can itself function as a *neveilah* and be a source of *tumah*.

### Intentional Gathering

To this Rav Huna adds a critical qualification: *vehu she-kinso*—the *kezayit* must have been gathered intentionally. It is not merely that the pieces happened to accumulate in one spot; rather, a person deliberately collected them. That deliberate act of collection gives the scraps significance: it signals that the person regards them as worthwhile, that they intend to do something with them, presumably eat them. The intention, therefore, is what transforms these otherwise insignificant pieces into halakhically meaningful food.

### Inedible Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בָּשָׂר שֶׁפְּלָטַתּוּ סַכִּין, הַיְינוּ דְּכִי אִיכָּא כְּזַיִת מִיחַיַּיב, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מַרְטְקָא, כִּי אִיכָּא כְּזַיִת מַאי הָוֵי? עֵץ בְּעָלְמָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to the one who says** that the word *alal* is referring to the **meat** residue **that** is attached to the hide after **the knife has flayed** the flesh, i.e., Reish Lakish, **that is** why Rabbi Yehuda says **that one is rendered liable when there is an olive-bulk** of *alal* collected in one place, because the person who collected it considers it to be food. **But according to the one who says** that the word *alal* is referring to **the nuchal ligament,** i.e., Rabbi Yoḥanan, even in a case **when there is an olive-bulk** of *alal* collected in one place, **what of it? It is merely wood,** i.e., it is unfit for consumption.

This leads to a pointed difficulty. If the *alaw* is *martka*—the inedible ligament or rotting flesh—then even if a person gathers a *kezayit* of it together, what difference does that make? *Etz be'alma hu*—it is like a piece of wood. You cannot confer halakhic status on something that is objectively inedible simply by collecting it. So how can Rabbi Yehuda say that gathering it makes it a *neveilah*?

### Meat on Skin Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא פְּלִיגִי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מַרְטְקָא נָמֵי מִצְטָרֵף, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: דַּוְקָא בָּשָׂר שֶׁפְּלָטַתּוּ סַכִּין, אֲבָל מַרְטְקָא לָא מִצְטָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **According to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda,** Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish **do not disagree;** they agree that the term *alal* is referring to the meat residue attached to the hide after the knife has flayed the flesh. **When they disagree,** it is with regard to the definition of the word alal according to the opinion of the Rabbis. Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The Rabbis maintain that the nuchal ligament also joins together with the meat to constitute the requisite measure of an egg-bulk to impart the impurity of food. And Reish Lakish says: The Rabbis maintain that **specifically** the **meat** residue **that** is attached to the hide after **the knife has flayed** the meat joins together with the flesh, **but the nuchal ligament does not join together.**

The Gemara therefore concludes: *aliba de-Rabbi Yehuda lo peligi*—when it comes to Rabbi Yehuda's position, there is no debate. For Rabbi Yehuda to make sense, the *alaw* in the *mishnah* must refer to the flesh left on the skin during flaying. That interpretation is internally coherent: the meat is objectively edible, it is simply attached to the hide and would normally be discarded. When a person deliberately scrapes it off and collects it, they demonstrate that they regard it as significant and intend to eat it. That intention is what gives it halakhic status and makes it susceptible to the *tumah* of a *neveilah*. Whether the *mishnah*'s *alaw* also extends to the *martka*—the ligament or rotting flesh—is the subject of the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish among the *rabanan*, but for Rabbi Yehuda's ruling at least, the reference must be to meat on skin.

### Tosafot on Intention

*Tosafot* is careful to delineate precisely what role intention plays here. One might think that if intention can grant food status, it should also work in reverse—that a person's intention to discard meat would prevent it from having the status of *neveilah* or remove it from the category of forbidden food. *Tosafot* explicitly rejects this. Forbidden food status—*neveilah* and its prohibitions—does not depend on intention. Meat that is *neveilah* remains *neveilah* regardless of what the owner plans to do with it. The domain in which intention operates is *tumah*. The laws of *tumah* that apply to food are predicated on the item being treated as food. If a person intends to discard something, it does not qualify as food for purposes of *tumah*, and therefore would not ordinarily bear the *tumah* of a *neveilah*. But Rabbi Yehuda says: if you gather it together deliberately, that act of intentional collection demonstrates that you are treating it as food, and therefore it assumes the *tumah* of *neveilah* in the domain of *tumah* law. The distinction *Tosafot* draws is important: intention cannot remove the *issur* of eating *neveilah*, but it can determine whether an item is categorized as "food" for *tumah* purposes.

There is an interesting anatomical aside worth noting. The observation was raised that the "meat" people eat—a steak, a chicken drumstick—is in fact muscle tissue. Everything one eats from an animal's body is muscle; flesh is simply an imprecise lay term for that muscle. What distinguishes the *martka*, then, is not that it is "different from meat" in some obvious way, but that it is a ligament (or, in Rabbeinu Chananel's reading, decomposing tissue) rather than functional muscle—and thus not the sort of thing one would normally consume even as part of a meal.

### Constructed Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי בָּשָׂר שֶׁפְּלָטַתּוּ סַכִּין, הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּחַשֵּׁיב עֲלֵיהּ – אֲפִילּוּ בְּאַנְפֵּי נַפְשֵׁיהּ מִיטַּמֵּא, וְאִי דְּלָא חַשֵּׁיב עֲלֵיהּ – בַּטּוֹלֵיה בַּטְּלֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **What are the circumstances** of **that** which is taught in the mishna, that the **meat** residue **that** is attached to the hide after **the knife flayed** the flesh joins together with the meat to constitute the measure of an egg-bulk required to impart the impurity of food? **If** it is a case **where one intends** to eat this meat residue, then it **can become impure** not only by joining together with the meat, but **even by itself,** like any other food. **And if** it is a case **where one does not intend** to eat this meat residue, why should it be susceptible to impurity at all? One has completely **nullified** its status as food.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אָבִין וְרַבִּי מְיָישָׁא, חַד אָמַר: מִקְצָתוֹ חִישֵּׁב עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Avin and Rabbi Meyasha** answered this dilemma. **One said:** It is a case where **one intends** to eat **part of** the meat residue, but it is uncertain which part. Therefore, the meat residue is not susceptible to impurity by itself because it is not entirely considered to be food, but the part that he intends to eat joins together with the meat to constitute the measure of an egg-bulk.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַד אָמַר: מִקְצָתוֹ פְּלָטַתּוּ חַיָּה, וּמִקְצָתוֹ פְּלָטַתּוּ סַכִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And one said:** It is a case where one does not intend to eat any part of the meat residue. Rather, **an animal severed part of** the meat residue attached to the hide, and therefore that part of the meat residue retains its status as food. **And** the **knife severed part of** the meat residue, and one therefore nullified its status as food with regard to that part. Since it is uncertain which part was severed by a knife and which part by an animal, the meat residue itself is not susceptible to impurity, but the part that was severed by an animal joins together with the meat to constitute the measure of an egg-bulk.

The deeper problem, however, is not which interpretation of *alaw* is correct, but how the *mishnah*'s case can be coherently constructed even according to Reish Lakish, who says the reference is specifically to flesh left on the skin. The Gemara poses the dilemma sharply: *iy de-chashiv leh*—if the person intends to eat the flesh on the skin, then it counts as food on its own and should be able to convey *tumah* even without being combined with other meat; *ve-iy de-lo chashiv leh*—if the person intends to discard it, then it has been negated entirely, and why would it combine with the meat one does intend to eat? Either it is food and counts on its own, or it is not food and cannot combine at all. How does the *mishnah* construct a middle scenario?

Two *amoraim*, Rav Avin and Rav Maisha, each offer a *heichi timtza*—a constructed scenario designed to make the case coherent. A *heichi timtza* is not a general principle extracted from the case but rather a specific factual construction that brackets the case and limits its scope.

The first answer: *miktzato chishav alav*—the person intends to eat only part of the flesh left on the skin. Suppose there is a *kezayit* worth of such flesh scattered in various places on the hide, and the person intends to scrape off only some of it, not all. In that case, the portion one intends to eat has food status, but it is not enough by itself to reach the minimum measure. However, when combined with the meat one is definitely eating, that small portion tips the scale and completes the minimum. The bottom line of the principle is the same: what you plan to eat is food, what you plan to discard is not. The *mishnah*'s case works only when some but not all of the flesh on the skin is intended for consumption, and the some-but-not-all is sufficient to combine with actual meat to reach the required measure even though insufficient on its own.

The second answer: *miktzato paltoso chayah miktzato paltoso sakin*—some of the flesh hanging off the hide was torn by an animal that attacked the cow (but did not render it a *tereifah*), and some was scraped off during flaying by the knife. The person would be more inclined to eat the flesh torn by the attack, and less inclined to eat the flesh left by the knife—or perhaps the opposite reasoning applies—and because the two pieces are indistinguishable, the person is uncertain which is which. Therefore neither piece on its own has fully established food status, but combined with other meat, even one piece is enough to complete the minimum measure. Again, the underlying principle remains identical: intention to eat confers food status, absence of such intention negates it, and the *mishnah*'s scenario depends on a constructed ambiguity.

According to Reish Lakish, then, the entire discussion of the *mishnah*'s case—that the *alaw* combines with meat but is not food on its own—is essentially a *heichi timtza*, a carefully engineered factual situation rather than a broadly applicable rule.

## Bird Parts & Horns

### Taharot Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הַחַרְטוֹם וְהַצִּפׇּרְנַיִם מִיטַּמְּאִין וּמְטַמְּאִין וּמִצְטָרְפִין. חַרְטוֹם – עֵץ בְּעָלְמָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** §The mishna stated that the horns join together with the flesh to constitute the requisite egg-bulk to impart the impurity of food. The Gemara comments that **we learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Teharot* 1:2): **The beak and the talons** of a bird that come into contact with a creeping animal can **become impure, and transmit impurity** to food, **and join together** with the attached flesh to constitute the requisite measure to impart impurity. The Gemara asks: Why does **a beak** join together with the flesh to impart impurity? **It is merely wood,** i.e., it is unfit for consumption.

The Gemara now cites a related passage from the tractate *Taharos*: *tnan hatam*—we taught over there. The case involves a bird: *ha-chartom ve-ha-tzipornayim*—the beak and the claws. The *mishnah* teaches that they *metamme'in u-mitztarfin*—they transmit *tumah* and they combine to the minimum measure. The question this raises is obvious: who eats a beak or claws? The prior analysis established two mechanisms by which something can combine with food to complete the minimum halakhic measure: either by being *ochel*—food, even food that would not be eaten on its own but would be consumed together with other food—or by being a *shomer*, a protective covering that guards the food. Both a *shomer* and an *ochel* can contribute to the minimum size for *tumah* purposes.

### Beak/Palate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בְּחַרְטוֹם תַּחְתּוֹן. תַּחְתּוֹן נָמֵי עֵץ בְּעָלְמָא הוּא! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: תַּחְתּוֹן שֶׁל עֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** The mishna is stated **with regard to the lower** half of the **beak,** i.e., the lower mandible. The Gemara objects: **The lower** mandible **is also merely wood. Rav Pappa says:** The mishna is discussing **the lower** section **of the upper** mandible and is referring to the membrane inside the mouth that is attached to the beak.

The Gemara immediately challenges the inclusion of the beak: *etz be'alma hu*—it is like wood! Nobody eats a beak, and it does not obviously serve as a *shomer* for any food. Rabbi Elazar resolves this by specifying: *be-chartom tachton*—the lower beak. But the Gemara presses: the lower beak is also like wood. Rabbi Elazar then clarifies further: *tachton shel elyon*—not the lower beak as such, but the underside of the upper beak, meaning the palate. The palate of a bird's upper jaw contains soft tissue that is edible. While one would not eat the palate on its own, if one is in any case consuming the bird's head, the palate is scraped off and eaten along with it. As something eaten together with food, it constitutes *ochel* and therefore combines with the minimum measure.

### Claws Embedded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צִפׇּרְנַיִם, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מָקוֹם (המובלעים) [הַמּוּבְלָע] בַּבָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to the **talons** mentioned in that mishna, **Rabbi Elazar says:** That mishna is not discussing the talons themselves, but rather **the place** at the base of the talon **that is subsumed within the flesh.**

The claws present a similar challenge. Rabbi Elazar specifies: *mekom ha-muvla'in ba-basar*—where the claws are embedded within the flesh, submerged beneath the skin at their base. When the claw is embedded in that way, it may be soft enough to be edible, and in any case it would be consumed along with the surrounding tissue when one eats the foot. Whether this is better understood as the claw functioning as *ochel*—food eaten together with other food—or as a *shomer* protecting the tissue beneath it is not entirely clear from the Gemara's formulation, but the practical result is that such embedded claws combine toward the minimum measure.

### Horns Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַרְנַיִם, אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁחוֹתְכִין וְיוֹצֵא מֵהֶן דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to the **horns** mentioned in the mishna, **Rav Pappa says:** The mishna is not discussing the hard substance of the horn, but rather is referring **to the place** at the base of the horns **where one severs** the horns **and blood flows from them.**

The Gemara returns to the case from the earlier *mishnah*: *karnayim*, horns. What is the basis for horns combining with meat toward the minimum measure? Rav Pappa answers: *be-makom she-chotchin ve-yotzei mehem dam*—at the point where the horn is attached to the head, where blood vessels still run through it, such that if cut there, blood would flow out. Again, two possible explanations apply. One is that at this base of the horn, where blood vessels and possibly marrow are present, the tissue is edible and would be consumed together with the surrounding meat of the head, functioning as *ochel*. The other is that the horn serves as a *shomer*—a protective casing for the marrow within—and it is in that capacity as a guardian that it combines with the minimum measure. Either way, the horn at the point of attachment qualifies.

### Ochel & Shomer

With this the Gemara's treatment of these auxiliary body parts comes to a close. The organizing framework consists of two principles: *ochel*—something that even if not eaten on its own is nonetheless consumed together with food—and *shomer*—something that serves as a protective shell or casing for food. Both categories contribute to the minimum halakhic measure and participate in the *tumah* of *neveilah*. What *yad*—handle—and other structural categories contribute is left for the broader tractate of *Taharos*; for present purposes, the discussion has illustrated both principles through the cases of the beak/palate, the embedded claws, and the horns.

## Jew/Non-Kosher

### Mixed Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּיוֹצֵא בּוֹ, הַשּׁוֹחֵט בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** §The mishna teaches: **Similarly,** in the case of **one who slaughters** a non-kosher **animal** for a gentile and the animal is still twitching and comes into contact with a source of impurity, it imparts impurity of food, but does not impart impurity of an animal carcass.

The second part of the *mishnah* addresses a set of more intricate cases involving *shechita* that is halakhically significant but not fully so. The framework rests on the observation that for different actors and different animals, *shechita* plays entirely different roles. For a Jew slaughtering a *kosher* animal, *shechita* is everything: from the moment the act of *shechita* is complete, the animal is permitted for consumption and is definitively not a *neveilah*. For a non-Jew, *shechita* is halakhically irrelevant: the animal does not become permissible until it actually dies, and the formal act of *shechita* changes nothing in his framework. Ordinarily, halakhic status is all-or-nothing. The *mishnah* is searching for middle scenarios—cases in which *shechita* is meaningfully operative but not fully determinative—by mixing the two categories.

### Case 1 Setup

The first case: a Jew performs *shechita* on a non-*kosher* animal—a pig—for a non-Jew. Because a Jew is performing the act of *shechita*, that act carries some halakhic significance. But because the animal is a pig, being slaughtered for a non-Jew, the *shechita* is not fully operative: from the non-Jew's perspective, the animal remains prohibited as *eiver min ha-chai* until it dies, because a non-Jew's framework requires actual death, not *shechita*, for permissibility. And from a Jewish perspective, it was never going to be permissible regardless—it is a pig.

So the result is a middle state: the act of *shechita* by a Jew confers food status on the animal in the halakhic sense—the same act of *shechita* that would make a *kosher* animal permissible and would render it susceptible to *tumah* as food—even though the animal is not yet dead and will eventually become a *neveilah* when it does die. At this intermediate stage, the animal is halakhically food but not yet a *neveilah*. This parallels the earlier case in the *mishnah* where an animal was slaughtered and is still *mefarkheset*, still thrashing, not yet fully expired.

There is a more fundamental issue underlying the whole discussion: why does *shechita* require a Jew at all? The Gemara never cites an explicit verse for this. The *Rosh* cites *ve-zavachta ve-achalta*, but the derivation is not fully worked out. The general principle is that when a formal halakhic act is required—where procedure matters rather than just outcome—the person performing it must belong to the relevant halakhic category. A *Sefer Torah* written by a non-Jew, a *get* delivered by a non-Jew—there are several such cases where membership in the relevant category is a prerequisite. Interestingly, the question of whether a non-Jew can perform *milah* is more open than the question of *shechita*; the analogy is asymmetric.

### R. Assi Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: שׁוֹנִים יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּטְמֵאָה, וְגוֹי בִּטְהוֹרָה – צְרִיכִין מַחְשָׁבָה וְהֶכְשֵׁר מַיִם מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi says:** Some Sages **teach** that when **a Jew** slaughters **a non-kosher** animal **or a gentile** slaughters **a kosher** animal, in order for it to be susceptible to impurity of food, it is **necessary** that the **intention** of the one performing the slaughter be that the flesh be designated as food while it is still twitching. **And** furthermore, in order for the animal to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity, it requires contact with **water** or another liquid that renders food susceptible to impurity that comes **from another place.** The blood of this slaughter is not considered a liquid that renders food susceptible to impurity because it flowed from a valid slaughter.

Rabbi Assi formulates the rule: *Yisrael be-tum'ah ve-oved kochavim be-taharah tzrichin machshavah*—a Jew who slaughters an impure animal and a non-Jew who slaughters a pure animal—these are exactly the two mixed scenarios—require *machshavah*, intention. Because the result of the *shechita* is an animal in this middle state—considered food, but not fully *kosher*—the animal does not automatically become susceptible to *tumah*; the person handling it must intend to eat it in order for it to have the status of food. Additionally, says Rabbi Assi, *ve-hekhsher mayim mi-makom acher*—it requires the independent act of *hekhsher*, contact with liquid from somewhere other than the *shechita* site, to render it susceptible.

### Hekhsher Needed

The requirement of independent *hekhsher* might seem surprising. In the case of a fully valid *shechita*, the act itself substitutes for *hekhsher*: the blood of *shechita* flowing over the meat is itself the liquid contact that renders the meat susceptible. But that rule applies only to a fully valid act of *shechita*. Here—either because the *shechita* was done on a non-*kosher* animal or because it was done by a non-Jew—it is not a fully valid *shechita*, and therefore it cannot substitute for *hekhsher*. Nevertheless, the partial validity of the *shechita* is enough to confer food status, and once food status is established, the animal can become susceptible if it comes in contact with liquid from another source.

### Hekhsher Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶכְשֵׁר לְמָה לִי? סוֹפוֹ לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, וְכֹל שֶׁסּוֹפוֹ לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה – לָא בָּעֵי הֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the animal to come in contact with liquid in order for it to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity of food? The flesh of the animal **will eventually become impure** with a more **severe** level of **impurity** when it dies, i.e., impurity of an animal carcass. **And any** food **that will eventually become impure** with a more **severe** level of **impurity does not require** contact with liquid to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity of food.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: *hekhsher lama li?* Why does *hekhsher* matter at all? *Sofo letamei tumah chamurah*—in the end this animal is going to cause a serious form of *tumah*, because when it dies it will be a *neveilah*. And a general principle governs this area: *ve-khol she-sofo letamei tumah chamurah lo ba'i hekhsher*—anything that is ultimately destined to become a source of serious *tumah* does not require *hekhsher* to become susceptible. Its eventual status as a source of *tumah* already places it within the ambit of *tumah*-related law, so that it is susceptible even without the intermediary step of contact with liquid.

### Tumah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְכִי יֻתַּן מַיִם עַל זֶרַע״, מָה זְרָעִים שֶׁאֵין סוֹפָן לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה צְרִיכִין הֶכְשֵׁר, אַף כֹּל שֶׁאֵין סוֹפוֹ לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה צָרִךְ הֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now explains the source of this principle. **As the school of Rabbi Yishmael teaches:** With regard to rendering food susceptible to impurity through contact with liquid, the verse states: **"But if water is put upon the seed,** and any of the carcass falls on it, it is impure for you" (Leviticus 11:38). **Just as seeds, which will never contract** a more **severe** level of **impurity,** because no form of severe impurity applies to foods other than meat, **require** contact with liquid to **render** them **susceptible** to their less severe level of impurity, **so too any** food **that will never contract** a more **severe** level of **impurity requires** contact with liquid to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity of food. By contrast, any food that will become impure with a more severe level of impurity does not require contact with liquid to be rendered susceptible to impurity of food.

This principle emerges from the verse *ve-khi yutan mayim al zera*, which is the source for *hekhsher*: water coming upon seeds renders produce susceptible to *tumah*. The derivation by *tannai de-vei Rabbi Yishmael* is: just as seeds, which can never themselves become a source of serious *tumah*, require *hekhsher* before they can be rendered susceptible—so too anything that will never be a source of serious *tumah* requires *hekhsher*. But the converse applies equally: anything that will ultimately become a source of serious *tumah* does not require *hekhsher*.

### Zera Proof

The proof-text is explicit in its formulation. Seeds are the paradigm case of *hekhsher* because seeds can never themselves generate *tumah*; they can only receive it. The principle is therefore: only things that are in the same structural category as seeds—items that will never themselves be sources of *tumah*—require the triggering mechanism of *hekhsher*. Items destined to be sources of *tumah* are already effectively within the system.

### Dead Bird Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ נִבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר צְרִיכָה מַחְשָׁבָה וְאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֶכְשֵׁר? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסּוֹפָהּ לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, **it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says: For what** reason **did** the Sages **say** that in order for **the carcass of a kosher bird** to become susceptible to impurity it **requires** that the **intention** of the one performing the slaughter be to designate the animal as food, **but it is not required** for the bird to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity through contact with liquid? The reason is **because eventually** the carcass of the bird **will impart** a more **severe impurity** when it is in the throat of the person who consumes it. Therefore, it is not necessary for the carcass of a kosher bird to come in contact with liquid in order for it to be susceptible to impurity.

The Gemara corroborates this with the case of *neveilat of tahor*—the carcass of a ritually pure bird. A dead *kosher* bird is unique in that it causes *tumah* specifically through the act of swallowing: one who eats it while it is in the throat becomes *tamei*. The rule is: *tzricha machshavah ve-einah tzricha hekhsher*—it requires *machshavah*, intention to eat it, to have food status, but it does not require *hekhsher*. Why not? *Mipnei she-sofo letamei tumah chamurah*—because this bird can ultimately be a source of *tumah*, namely when someone swallows it. Since it has the potential to function as a source of serious *tumah*, it does not require *hekhsher*. But it does require *machshavah* because, unlike a standard *neveilah*, the dead bird's *tumah* mechanism is so specific—swallowing—that without someone's intention to eat it, it does not have an operative food status.

Returning to the question: why does the pig being slaughtered by a Jew in this middle state require *hekhsher*? If it is destined to become a *neveilah* when it dies, the principle should exempt it from needing *hekhsher*.

### Chizkiyah Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לְגוֹרְרָהּ, וּלְהַעֲמִידָהּ עַל פָּחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiyya says** in response: The reason for the opinion of the Sages stated by Rabbi Asi is **since** the slaughterer **is able to chop** the animal into small pieces **and** thereby **establish** the volume of every piece of the animal **as less than an olive-bulk.** In such a scenario, the animal would not be susceptible to impurity. Therefore, it is not certain that the animal will eventually become impure with a more severe impurity.

Chizkiyah answers: *ho'il ve-yakhol le-gorareha u-le-ha'amidah al pachot mi-kezayit*—because it is possible to bring it about that the animal will never become a *neveilah*, or at least that the specific meat being touched will never become a *neveilah*. Here is the mechanism: while the animal is still *mefarkheset*—still thrashing around after *shechita*—one could begin dismembering it. If one removes flesh (without severing a complete limb—a complete limb removed while the animal is still alive would be *eiver min ha-chai* and would itself cause *tumah*, but partial flesh, *basar min ha-chai*, does not cause *tumah* though it is forbidden to eat) then that flesh, having been removed while the animal was still alive, will never attain the status of *neveilah* even after the animal dies. Only flesh still attached to the animal at the moment of death becomes a *neveilah*. So in theory, one could so thoroughly dismember the animal while it is still thrashing that the remaining flesh attached to the carcass is less than a *kezayit*—and that specific piece of meat one is touching might be removed while the animal is alive and will therefore never be a *neveilah*.

Because the meat in question is not necessarily destined to become a *neveilah*—since it could be removed before death—the exemption from *hekhsher* does not apply, and the meat therefore requires *hekhsher* to become susceptible to *tumah*.

### Yirmeyah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי זֵירָא: וּמִי אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה הָכִי? וְהָא אִיתְּמַר: שָׁחַט בָּהּ שְׁנַיִם אוֹ רוֹב שְׁנַיִם, וַעֲדַיִין הִיא מְפַרְכֶּסֶת – חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: אֵינָהּ לְאֵבָרִים, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: יֶשְׁנָהּ לְאֵבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said to Rabbi Zeira: And did Ḥizkiyya** actually **say such** a statement? **But wasn't it stated:** If one **slaughtered** a non-kosher animal in a valid manner by cutting the **two** *simanim*, i.e., the windpipe and the gullet, **or the majority of** the **two** *simanim*, **and** the animal **is still twitching, Ḥizkiyya says: There is no** prohibition against eating the **limbs** from such a twitching animal. Therefore, a gentile, who is prohibited from consuming a limb from a living animal, may consume this animal. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: There is** a prohibition against eating the **limbs** of such an animal.

Rabbi Yirmeyah then asks Rabbi Zeira: *u-mi amar Chizkiyah hachi?* Did Chizkiyah truly reason this way? Is this Chizkiyah's view? Because there is another explicit statement attributed to Chizkiyah: in the case of *shachat bah shnayim o rov shnayim ve-adayin hi mefarkheset*—one performed a valid *shechita* of two *simanim* or the majority of them on a non-*kosher* animal, and the animal is still thrashing—what is the status of a limb removed from the animal at this stage for a non-Jew? Does *eiver min ha-chai* apply?

Chizkiyah said *einah la-eivarim*—there is no problem of *eiver min ha-chai* for a non-Jew from this animal. Rabbi Yochanan said *yeshnah la-eivarim*—there is indeed a problem of *eiver min ha-chai* for a non-Jew. Rabbi Yochanan's position seems to align with the *mishnah*: the *shechita* gives the meat food status for *tumah* purposes, but from the non-Jew's perspective the animal is not yet dead, so removing a limb at this stage is still *eiver min ha-chai*.

But if Chizkiyah holds *einah la-eivarim*, meaning that from a non-Jew's perspective the animal is already considered dead after *shechita*, then why is the animal not yet a *neveilah* at this stage? If the animal is "dead" for the non-Jew's purposes—permissible to eat, no longer *eiver min ha-chai*—then it should simultaneously be a *neveilah* for *tumah* purposes. And that would flatly contradict the *mishnah*, which says it is not yet a *neveilah* at this stage. How does Chizkiyah accept the *mishnah* at all?

### Middle State

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יָצְתָה מִכְּלַל חַיָּה, וְלִכְלַל מֵתָה לֹא בָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira **said to** Rabbi Yirmeya in response: Ḥizkiyya maintains that such an animal has **left the category of a living** animal, **but has not entered the category of a dead** animal. Therefore, it is not prohibited for a gentile to consume such an animal, but the animal does not have the impurity of a carcass.

The Gemara resolves this with the formulation: *yatzta mi-klal chayah u-le-khlal meisah lo basa*—it has exited the category of the living, but it has not yet entered the category of the dead. This is a striking Gemara. The concept of a genuine middle state—neither alive nor dead—is invoked to allow Chizkiyah's position to cohere. The animal is no longer living in the full sense: a non-Jew may eat from it, and its limbs are not *eiver min ha-chai*. But it is not yet dead in the full sense: it does not yet cause the *tumah* of *neveilah*. *Shechita* has moved it out of the living category without yet placing it in the dead category.

This is an extraordinary halakhic concept. One way to understand what Chizkiyah is saying is that *shechita* functions as a *matir*—a halakhic permissive act—even for the benefit of a non-Jew, releasing the animal from the restrictions applicable to a living creature without yet making it dead. Under this framework, it is not that *shechita* counts as death even for a non-Jew; rather, *shechita* creates a third state in which the animal is neither fully alive nor fully dead.

Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, takes the position that for a non-Jew the framework remains binary: the animal is either alive or dead, and it is not dead until it actually expires. *Shechita* by a Jew on a non-*kosher* animal can confer food status for *tumah* purposes—that the *mishnah* establishes—but it does not make the animal "dead" from the non-Jew's perspective. Therefore removing a limb at this stage is still *eiver min ha-chai* for the non-Jew. The Gemara records that Rabbi Elazar rules *nakot le-ha de-Rabbi Yochanan be-yadcha*—hold onto Rabbi Yochanan's position, and a *baraita* from Rabbi Oshaya confirms it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁשָּׁחַט בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה לְגוֹי, שָׁחַט בָּהּ שְׁנַיִם אוֹ רוֹב שְׁנַיִם, וּמְפַרְכֶּסֶת – מְטַמְּאָה טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, אֲבָל לֹא טוּמְאַת נְבֵלוֹת. אֵבֶר הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִמֶּנָּה, כְּפוֹרֵשׁ מִן הַחַי, וּבָשָׂר הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִמֶּנָּה, כְּבָשָׂר הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִן הַחַי, וְאָסוּר לִבְנֵי נֹחַ, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְאַחַר שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rav Oshaya teaches** (*Tosefta*, *Oholot* 2:1): In the case of **a Jew who slaughtered a non-kosher animal for** the consumption of **a gentile,** if **he slaughtered** it by cutting **two** *simanim* **or the majority of two** *simanim*, **and** the animal is still **twitching,** the animal **imparts impurity of food; but** so long as it is twitching it does **not** impart **the impurity of** animal **carcasses. A limb that separates from** this twitching animal is considered **like** a limb that **separates from a living** animal, and as such it imparts the impurity of a carcass. **And flesh that separates from** this twitching animal is considered **like flesh that separates from a living** animal, and as such it does not impart impurity. **And it is prohibited for the descendants of Noah** to consume the flesh that separates from this twitching animal, **and** this prohibition applies **even after its soul departs.**

The *baraita* thus confirms Rabbi Yochanan's position and rules against Chizkiyah on the practical question. Nevertheless, the conceptual framework of *yatzta mi-klal chayah u-le-khlal meisah lo basa* remains a remarkable formulation, and the Gemara does not simply dismiss it—it uses it to explain Chizkiyah's internal consistency. The concept has resonance beyond its immediate *halakhic* context, touching on the question of what constitutes death and whether the transition from life to death admits of intermediate stages. The Gemara elsewhere speaks of individual moments—*ad shetamut*, until it dies—but never definitively specifies what that moment is, and these *sugyot* about animals have long formed part of the background for broader discussions about the definition of death.

## Non-Jew/Kosher

### Case 2 Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגוֹי שֶׁשָּׁחַט בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וּמְפַרְכֶּסֶת – מְטַמְּאָה טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, אֲבָל לֹא טוּמְאַת נְבֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, in the case of **a gentile who slaughtered a kosher animal for** the consumption of **a Jew, and** the animal **is** still **twitching,** the animal **imparts the impurity of food** because it is considered to be food, **but** so long as it is twitching it does **not** impart **the impurity of an animal carcass.**

The *baraita* continues with the reverse scenario: *ve-oved kochavim she-shachat behemah tehorah le-Yisrael*—a non-Jew who performs *shechita* on a *kosher* animal for a Jew. This is the mirror image of the first case. Here, everything about the animal and the intended beneficiary is *kosher*—it is a ritually pure animal being slaughtered for a Jew—but the person performing the act is a non-Jew, which means the *shechita* is not halakhically valid as a Jewish act of *shechita*. The result is the same middle state: the act has enough features of a valid *shechita*—right animal, right beneficiary—that it confers food status, but because it is a non-Jew performing it, it does not have the full force of *shechita*, and the animal will ultimately become a *neveilah* when it dies.

### Same Halakha

The halakhic result is identical: *metamei tumat ochlin, aval lo tumat neveilah, umefarkheset*—while the animal is still thrashing, it has food-level *tumah*, not *neveilah*-level *tumah*. The same logic applies: the act of *shechita* here is partially operative and confers food status, but the animal has not yet died and is therefore not yet a *neveilah*.

### Eiver Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵבֶר הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִמֶּנָּה, כְּפוֹרֵשׁ מִן הַחַי, וּבָשָׂר הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִמֶּנָּה, כְּפוֹרֵשׁ מִן הַחַי, וְאָסוּר לִבְנֵי נֹחַ, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְאַחַר שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A limb that separates from** this twitching animal is considered **like** a limb that **separates from a living** animal, and as such it imparts the impurity of a carcass. **And flesh that separates from** this twitching animal is considered **like** flesh that **separates from a living** animal, and as such it does not impart the impurity of food. **And it is prohibited for the descendants of Noah** to consume the flesh that separates from this twitching animal, **and** this prohibition applies **even after its soul departs.**

Following from this, *eiver ha-porish mimmenah ke-porish min ha-chai*—a limb removed from the animal at this stage has the status of *eiver min ha-chai*. The *shechita* was done by a non-Jew; from a halakhic standpoint it does not count as a valid act of *shechita* for purposes of rendering the animal dead. So the animal is still considered alive while it is thrashing, and taking a limb from a living animal is *eiver min ha-chai*. The rule *ve-asur li-vnei Noach ve-afilu achar she-teitzei nafshah*—the status of *eiver min ha-chai* is determined at the moment of removal, not by subsequent events—applies here as well. Even after the animal dies, a limb removed while it was still thrashing retains its *eiver min ha-chai* status.

### Invalid Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט בָּהּ אֶחָד אוֹ רוֹב אֶחָד – אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין; נְחָרָהּ – אֵין בָּהּ טוּמְאָה שֶׁל כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** If the gentile **slaughtered** the kosher animal by cutting only **one** *siman* **or the majority of one** *siman*, and the animal is still twitching, the animal **does not impart impurity of food** because the slaughter was not valid. Similarly, if he **stabbed** the animal rather than slaughtering it properly, **it has no impurity whatsoever.**

If the *shechita* was not even a full *shechita*—*shachat bah echad o rov echad*, only one *siman* or the majority of one *siman*—then the act is not an act of *shechita* at all, and *einah metamei tumat ochlin*: there is no food status, there is no *tumah* at the intermediate stage. Similarly, if the animal was killed through some other means entirely—*nachrah*—it simply has no *tumah* until it dies and becomes a *neveilah* in the ordinary sense. The capacity to be treated as food before death, and to receive *tumah* as food before dying, exists only when there is at least the semblance of a valid *shechita*. An act that is not *shechita* at all—whether because only one *siman* was cut or because the animal was killed by another means—confers nothing.

Interestingly, the Gemara's presentation implies that failing to cut both *simanim* (cutting only one) is treated as a more fundamental failure than having the wrong person perform the *shechita*. A non-Jew who cuts both *simanim* properly has performed an act that resembles *shechita* in form; a Jew who cuts only one *siman* has not performed a full act of *shechita* regardless of personal status. This ranking is perhaps counterintuitive but follows from the structure of the *halakhah*: the technical requirements of the act are definitional, whereas the identity of the performer is a separate layer of validity.

### Started/Completed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט גּוֹי בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין עוֹשֶׂה אוֹתָהּ טְרֵפָה, וּבָא יִשְׂרָאֵל וּגְמָרָהּ – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **a gentile** partially **slaughtered** a kosher animal **in a place that does not render** the animal **a *tereifa*,** i.e., unfit for consumption due to a mortal wound, e.g., he cut half of the windpipe, **and** then **a Jew came and completed** the slaughter, the animal is **fit** for consumption.

The *baraita* continues with composite cases. *Hekhel ha-nochri u-gamar ha-Yisrael*—a non-Jew began the *shechita* and a Jew completed it. The *baraita* specifies: if the non-Jew cut part of the *siman* but had not yet cut enough to constitute a meaningful act and had not rendered the animal a *tereifah* by that partial cut, then the Jew's completion of the *shechita* without pause renders it *ksherah*. The non-Jew's partial cut, which did not yet count as an act of *shechita* and did not disqualify the animal, can be subsumed into the Jew's completed act.

### Jew/Non-Jew Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט יִשְׂרָאֵל, בֵּין בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה אוֹתָהּ טְרֵפָה, וּבֵין בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין עוֹשֶׂה אוֹתָהּ טְרֵפָה, וּבָא גּוֹי וְגָמַר שְׁחִיטָתוֹ – פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** But if **a Jew slaughtered** the animal, **either in a place that renders** the animal **a *tereifa*,** e.g., he cut the majority of the windpipe, **or in a place that does not render** the animal **a *tereifa*,** e.g., he only partially cut the windpipe, **and** then **a gentile came and completed** the slaughter, **his slaughter is not valid.**

The reverse: *shachat ha-Yisrael—bein be-makom she-oseh tereifah bein be-makom she-eino oseh tereifah—u-va oved kochavim ve-gamar*—whether or not the Jew's partial *shechita* rendered the animal a *tereifah*, if a non-Jew then completed the act, *shechitato pesulah*—the *shechita* is invalid. Because the final operative act of cutting—the act that completes the *shechita* and brings it to its conclusion—was performed by a non-Jew, the entire *shechita* is invalid. The critical point is who performed the decisive concluding act.

## Meat Before Death

### Health Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיֹּאכַל מִבְּהֵמָה קוֹדֶם שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשָׁהּ, חוֹתֵךְ כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר מִבֵּית שְׁחִיטָתָהּ, וּמוֹלְחוֹ יָפֶה יָפֶה, וּמְדִיחוֹ יָפֶה יָפֶה, וּמַמְתִּין לַהּ עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשָׁהּ, וְאוֹכְלוֹ. אֶחָד גּוֹי וְאֶחָד יִשְׂרָאֵל מוּתָּרִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **One who wishes to eat from** the meat of a slaughtered **animal before its soul departs may cut an olive-bulk of meat from the area of its slaughter,** the neck, **and salt it very well,** i.e., more than is normally required, **and rinse it very well** in water to remove the salt and blood, **and** then **wait until** the animal's **soul departs, and** then **eat it. Both a gentile and a Jew are permitted to** eat **it** because the prohibition against eating a limb from a living animal is not applicable in such a case.

A practical application follows directly from the established middle state. The *baraita* records: *ha-rotzeh she-yochal mi-behemah kodem she-teitzei nafshah*—one who wishes to eat meat from the animal before it has fully expired. This was evidently considered medically beneficial: the meat taken from the animal in the immediate aftermath of *shechita*, while the animal is still thrashing, is saturated with blood that has not yet drained, and for some this was regarded as particularly healthful. The practical question is whether and how this is permissible.

### Salting/Rinsing

The procedure is: *choseich kezayit basar mi-beit shechitato*—one cuts a *kezayit* of meat from the site of the *shechita*, because that is the most accessible place immediately after the cut is made. Then *umolcho yafeh yafeh u-madicho yafeh yafeh*—one must salt it thoroughly and rinse it thoroughly, because the meat at that site is suffused with blood. The animal has not yet expelled all of its blood, and the blood concentration at the *shechita* site is especially high. A thorough job of salting and rinsing is therefore essential to remove the blood before the meat can be consumed.

### Waiting Requirement

Although technically the meat is permissible from the moment of valid *shechita*, the Gemara in *Sanhedrin* derives—at the level of *asmachta*—that one should not eat from an animal while it is still exhibiting signs of life. Therefore: *u-mamtin lah ad she-teitzei nafshah*—one waits until the animal has fully died. Only then does one eat the meat that was cut and prepared. The *halakhah* is technically satisfied from the moment of *shechita*, but propriety—and the rabbinic derivation—requires waiting for the animal to expire.

### Both Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין, דְּאָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַשְׁיָין: הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיַּבְרִיא, חוֹתֵךְ כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר מִבֵּית שְׁחִיטָה, וּמוֹלְחוֹ יָפֶה יָפֶה, וּמְדִיחוֹ יָפֶה יָפֶה, וּמַמְתִּין לָהּ עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשָׁהּ, אֶחָד גּוֹי וְאֶחָד יִשְׂרָאֵל מוּתָּרִים בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: This *baraita* **supports** the opinion of **Rav Idi bar Avin, as Rav Idi bar Avin said** that **Rav Yitzḥak bar Ashyan said: One who wants to be healthy should cut an olive-bulk of meat from the area of the slaughter, and salt it very well and rinse it very well, and** then **wait until** the animal's **soul departs,** and then **both a gentile and a Jew are permitted to** eat **it.**

*Echad oved kochavim ve-echad Yisrael mutarin bahem*—this piece of meat, cut from the site of a valid *shechita* of a *kosher* animal, is permissible both for a Jew and for a non-Jew. This is the mirror image of the earlier case. When a non-Jew performs *shechita* on a non-*kosher* animal, or when a Jew performs *shechita* on a non-*kosher* animal, the flesh at the intermediate stage is *eiver min ha-chai* for a non-Jew—it cannot be consumed until the animal dies. But when a Jew performs *shechita* on a *kosher* animal, the *shechita* is fully valid, and the same way a Jew can technically eat from it before the animal expires, a non-Jew can eat from it as well. It is not *eiver min ha-chai* for a *ben Noach*, because the *shechita* has effectively resolved the status of the animal even from the framework of the *ben Noach*'s obligations. This point is corroborated by a teaching of Rav Idi bar Avin in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Ashin, who formulates the same procedure—cut a *kezayit* from the *shechita* site, salt and rinse thoroughly, wait for the animal to die—as general guidance for someone who wants to be healthy.

## Technical Problems

### Shehah/Daras Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שָׁהָה בָּהּ, דָּרַס בָּהּ – מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** §It was previously taught in the *baraita* that if a Jew slaughtered a non-kosher animal for a gentile's consumption, or a gentile slaughtered a kosher animal for a Jew's consumption, that animal imparts impurity of food when it is twitching after the slaughter. With regard to that *halakha*, **Rabbi Elazar raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* in such a case if **one interrupted** the slaughter or **pressed** on the knife during the slaughter? Do these acts, which normally invalidate slaughter, also invalidate this slaughter, or does the slaughter in this case not have to fulfill all of the halakhic requirements of valid slaughter in order to render the slaughtered animal as food with regard to imparting the impurity of food?

The final question on the *daf*: Rabbi Elazar asks about cases where the *shechita* was technically defective. We established that what gives meat the status of food before the animal dies, in the mixed cases, is the act of *shechita*. A non-Jew's *shechita* of a *kosher* animal for a Jew is meaningfully operative even though it does not produce *kosher* meat; it is enough to confer food status. But suppose the *shechita* had a technical *pesul*—specifically *shehah* (pausing in the middle of the cut) or *daras* (pressing down with the knife rather than drawing it across). Does such a technically defective *shechita* still confer food status? Or does the technical flaw reduce it to the same level as not having performed *shechita* at all—meaning no food status until death?

### R. Yochanan Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: צְרִיכָה הֶכְשֵׁר שְׁחִיטָה כִּבְהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה. הֶכְשֵׁר לְמַאי? אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יִצְחָק: בְּדִיקַת סַכִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain elder** resolved this dilemma and **said to him: Rabbi Yoḥanan said as follows:** This case **requires a valid slaughter** in every detail, **just like** a Jew slaughtering **a kosher animal.** The Gemara clarifies: **To what** requirement does **valid** slaughter just like a kosher animal refer? **Rav Shmuel bar Yitzḥak said:** It is referring to the requirement of **examining the knife** before the slaughter.

An elder transmits Rabbi Yochanan's answer: *tzrichah hekhsher shechita ke-vehemah tehorah*—what is required for food status to be conferred is *hekhsher shechita*, a *shechita* that would be valid on a *kosher* animal. The act must be technically proper: it must meet all the requirements of a valid *shechita* as applied to a pure animal, even though the circumstances here—non-Jew performing it, or non-*kosher* animal—mean it will not produce *kosher* meat. Only if the act itself would have been valid *shechita* on a *kosher* animal does it confer the middle-state food status.

### Precision Required

Rav Shmuel Yitzchak sharpens this further: *ve-dayka salka*—the precision matters throughout. No pausing, no pressing, and even the knife must be checked beforehand. Every technical requirement of *shechita* must be met. The reason the mixed-genre cases (non-Jew performing *shechita*, or *shechita* on a non-*kosher* animal) still confer food status is not that they are some lesser form of *shechita*—they must be, in technical execution, perfect *shechita*. The only element that is excused is the one that cannot be supplied: in the case of the non-Jew, it is his personal status; in the case of the pig, it is the animal's nature.

### Only Kavanah Exempted

The conclusion: *kavanah* alone is waived. A Jew performing *shechita* on a *kosher* animal must have the proper intention—*kavanah*—for the *shechita* to be valid; one who performs *shechita* thoughtlessly, without intent, has performed a technically valid physical act that is nonetheless halakhically questionable. In the mixed cases, the person performing *shechita* (the non-Jew) cannot have the relevant Jewish *kavanah*, or the animal being slaughtered (the pig) is not the kind of animal for which *kavanah* is meaningful in the normal sense. That exemption—the non-Jew's lack of the relevant intention—is what the halakhah waives. Everything else—the knife's condition, the absence of *shehah*, the absence of *daras*, the proper drawing of the blade—must be present in full. Only once all those technical conditions are satisfied does the act constitute the kind of *shechita* that can confer food status in the middle-state cases.
