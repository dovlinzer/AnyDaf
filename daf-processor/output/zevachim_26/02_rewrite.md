# Zevachim 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Unblemished at Kabbalah

### Tzorem Ozen Parah

The daf begins with the final line of the previous *sugya*, which concerned the principle of *avir k'kli dami* — the halakhic status of the airspace directly above an open vessel as equivalent to the vessel itself. This principle was applied to the blood of sacrifices, which must flow directly from the neck of the animal into the receiving vessel, and was extended as well to the waters of the *parah adumah*. The concluding point of that *sugya* is a teaching of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the ruling regarding how the waters of the *parah adumah* may be directed into a vessel was established from the testimony of Rabbi Tzadok, as recorded in tractate *Eduyot*. Rabbi Tzadok testified that if the flowing waters were directed using the leaves of nut trees — which, being inedible, are not *mekabel tumah* — the gathering is valid. This case came before the sages in the *Lishkat HaGazit* and was ruled *kasher*. With that, the prior *sugya* is closed.

The Gemara now introduces a new teaching. Rabbi Zeira said in the name of Rebbe: if one pierced the ear of the animal (*tzorem ozen parah*) after the *shechitah* but before the blood was received (*kibel damo*), the *korban* is *pasul*. The reasoning derives from the verse *v'lakach midam hapar* — "he shall take from the blood of the ox" — where the definite article (*heh ha-yedi'ah*) indicates that the animal must be in the same state it was in before: unblemished, without a *mum*. Therefore, at the moment the blood is received, the animal must still be *tamim*.

One might find this ruling striking, even somewhat paradoxical. The animal has already had its neck severed — surely that is a far greater *baal mum* than a hole in the ear. But the Gemara's *drasha* operates within precisely defined parameters: bracketing the necessary wound of *shechitah* itself, the animal must otherwise remain unblemished at the time of *kabbalah*. If someone pierced the ear in that instant between the cut and the catching of the blood, that additional blemish invalidates the *korban*.

### Kodshim Kalim Extension

With Rabbi Zeira's ruling established for *kodshei kodashim*, the Gemara asks: *ashkachan kodshei kodashim* — we have found the source for the most sacred offerings, but from where do we derive the same requirement for *kodshim kalim*? Would one not readily assume the rule carries over? Tosafot raises this question explicitly: ordinarily, a ruling established for one category of *korban* would be assumed to apply to others without requiring a separate derivation. Tosafot suggests that perhaps because this particular ruling is so counterintuitive — requiring the animal to remain unblemished even after its head is severed — there was reason to think it might be a special stringency limited to *kodshei kodashim*, and therefore a separate source is necessary.

Rava answers from the verse concerning the *korban pesach* — a *kodshim kalim*: *seh tamim zachar ben shanah yihiyeh lachem*, "it shall be a perfect male lamb, one year old" (Shemot 12:5). The immediately preceding verse, *tachossu al haseh*, speaks of the *shechitah*. The subsequent verse then specifies that the animal must be *tamim* and *ben shanah* — indicating that even after the act of *shechitah*, the animal must continue to satisfy both conditions. So the requirement of *tamim* at the time of *kabbalah* is established even for *kodshim kalim*.

### Meat/Fat Remain

The Gemara then extends the discussion further. Rava reads the word *yihiyeh* — "it shall be" — as encompassing all stages of the sacrificial service: the *shechitah*, the *kabbalah*, the *holachah* (carrying the blood), and the *zerikah* (throwing the blood onto the altar). The *yihiyeh* means that throughout all these acts, the animal must remain *tamim* and *ben shanah*.

Abaye challenges this broad reading from the teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua, who holds that for all sacrifices in the Torah, the throwing of the blood is only valid if at least a *kezayit* of meat or a *kezayit* of fat remains from the animal at the time of *zerikah*. Rabbi Yehoshua's view establishes that even if the majority of the meat or fat has been destroyed or rendered *tamei*, so long as a *kezayit* remains, the *zerikah* is valid. Now, if Rava's principle requires the animal to remain *tamim* — unblemished — all the way through *zerikah*, then a case in which most of the meat has been destroyed would present a contradiction: the animal is clearly no longer in its original state.

### Psul Limited to Kabbalah

The Gemara resolves this tension by drawing back the scope of Rava's ruling. The *yihiyeh*, it concludes, extends the ongoing requirement only with respect to the condition of *ben shanah* — one year old — not with respect to *tamim*. The requirement of *tamim* is limited to the moment of *kabbalah* alone, derived from *v'lakach midam hapar*. After the blood has been received, if the meat becomes damaged or destroyed, this does not retroactively invalidate the service with respect to the *tamim* requirement. What must remain valid at all stages is only the age qualification.

### Sha'ot Poslot

This resolution prompts a question from the Gemara itself: how is it even possible that an animal could be *ben shanah* at the time of *shechitah* and *ben shtayim* — two years old — by the time of *holachah* or *zerikah*? After all, the blood-throwing follows the *shechitah* by only moments. The Gemara answers with a ruling of Rava: *sha'ot poslot bekodshim* — in matters of sacrificial law, age is measured by the hour, not by the calendar day. This contrasts with, for example, *bar mitzvah* and *bat mitzvah*, where we measure by days: once the date of one's birth has come around again, one is considered to have entered the next year, regardless of the hour of birth. By *korbanot*, however, the calculation is precise down to the hour. It is therefore possible that an animal was *shechted* while still in its first year — say, an hour before the exact anniversary of its birth — and by the time of *zerikah*, it has entered its second year. Hence the requirement that it remain *ben shanah* through all stages is practically significant, and the ruling of *sha'ot poslot* is directly derived from this discussion.

The ultimate conclusion: the requirement of *tamim* applies specifically and only at the time of *kabbalat hadam*. If thereafter the meat is destroyed or blemished, the *korban* is not invalidated on that account. What must hold through all stages — *shechitah*, *kabbalah*, *holachah*, and *zerikah* — is the condition of *ben shanah*, measured by the hour.

## Animal Inside/Outside

### Raglah Bachutz

A small but essential ruling of Tosafot at the top of 26a sets the foundation for this entire section: a single drop of *pasul* blood that becomes mixed with valid blood is not subject to *bittul berov* — it is not nullified by a majority of valid blood. Whether by rabbinic enactment or by Torah law, any admixture of *pasul* blood invalidates the entire contents of the vessel. This principle, that invalid blood cannot be nullified, governs everything that follows.

With this in mind, the Gemara introduces the ruling of Rabbi Ami citing Rabbi Elazar: if the animal's body was inside the *Azarah* but one leg extended outside (*hi bifnim v'raglah bachutz*), and one cut off that leg and then performed the *shechitah*, the *korban* is *kasher*. This requires explanation.

When the *shechitah* is performed, the blood in every part of the animal's body begins to flow toward the wound in the neck and out into the receiving vessel. Blood flowing from a limb that is positioned outside the *Azarah* is *pasul* — it is analogous to blood that has been taken out of its proper domain. Just as blood that is carried out of the *Azarah* after *kabbalah* becomes invalid (*psul yotzei*), so too blood pumped in from outside the *Azarah* at the moment of *shechitah* is invalid. Since even a single drop of such blood mixed into the vessel invalidates the entire reception, the presence of a limb outside the *Azarah* poses a serious problem.

If one severs that limb *before* the *shechitah*, no blood will flow from outside — the problem is solved. But there is an immediate difficulty: cutting off a limb creates a *baal mum*, and we have already established that the animal must be *tamim* at the time of *kabbalah*. Performing the *shechitah* on a blemished animal would be invalid.

The Gemara works through the possible solutions. Cutting the limb after *shechitah* but before *kabbalah* does not work either, because Rabbi Zeira's teaching states that the animal must remain unblemished at the time of *kabbalah*, and cutting off a limb between *shechitah* and *kabbalah* would introduce a *mum* before the blood is received. This is derived from *v'lakach midam hapar* — the animal must remain as it was.

Rav Chisda citing Rav Avimi provides the resolution: one may cut into the flesh of the limb down to the bone without severing the bone itself (*chotech ba-eivar ad she-magia la-etzem*). Cutting flesh without cutting bone does not constitute a *baal mum*, since the bone structure remains intact. Yet because all the soft tissue has been cut through, no blood will travel up through that limb. This procedure can therefore be performed before the *shechitah*, precluding the flow of outside blood without creating a disqualifying blemish.

The Gemara thus recasts the ruling: if one cut the limb after *shechitah* but before *kabbalah*, the *korban* is invalid on account of the *mum* created before *kabbalah*. If the blood was received and only thereafter the limb was severed, that too is invalid — though for a different reason, which the Gemara now addresses.

### Cutting Limbs

The Gemara reformulates the ruling: *chatach v'achar kach kibel* — if one cut (using the technique of cutting flesh to the bone, without severing it) and then received the blood, it is *kasher*. *Kibel v'achar kach chatach* — if one first received the blood and then cut — it is *pasul*. Why should the *kabbalah* be invalidated if the severing occurs only after the blood has already been caught? The Gemara says we must infer from this that the blood embedded in the limbs, *dam hamuvla ba-eivarim*, is halachically considered blood — blood that carries the capacity to invalidate. That capillary blood, present throughout the flesh, flows during *shechitah* and can enter the vessel, and if it comes from a limb positioned outside the *Azarah*, it renders the *kabbalah* invalid.

### Dam Hamuvla

The Gemara pauses to examine this assumption. Why should *dam hamuvla ba-eivarim* — the blood embedded within the meat, blood found in capillaries and soft tissue, not the primary life-blood used for the altar service — be treated as halachically significant blood? It is more akin to an irrelevant fluid. If water from outside the *Azarah* were somehow mixed in, it would not invalidate; why then should this embedded blood, which has no function on the *mizbe'ach*, invalidate?

The Gemara responds: *dilma mishum shamnunit* — perhaps the problem is not the blood itself but the *shamnunit*, the fattiness present within the meat. According to Rashi, what invalidates is not the embedded blood per se, which could indeed be treated as irrelevant liquid, but rather that this blood carries with it the fat content of the meat. That fattiness — unlike the *chelev* (the suet-like free-floating fat that is placed on the altar) — is the normal fat embedded within muscle tissue. When a portion of the animal is outside the *Azarah*, that meat and its embedded fat become *pasul* as a component of the sacrifice. When the blood from that limb enters the vessel, it carries with it that invalid fattiness, which mingles with and invalidates the blood in the vessel.

One might ask: why should invalid fat mixed into the blood prevent the blood from being thrown on the altar? The fat itself is not the blood. The prohibition regarding invalid fat is that it may not be burned on the altar — but why does its admixture with the blood prevent the blood from being dashed on the wall of the altar? The Gemara does not resolve this question explicitly, but proceeds on the assumption that such commingling does render the blood unusable.

### Basar Outside Azarah

The Gemara then draws a broader inference. Since the case of the animal with a limb outside was not limited by the Gemara to *kodshei kodashim*, it would seem to apply to *kodshim kalim* as well. This raises a question: *basar kodshei kalim she-yatza lifnei zrikat hadam, pasul?* — does meat of a *kodshim kalim* offering become invalid if it exits the *Azarah* before the blood has been thrown?

This question has practical significance. The relevant zones differ by category of sacrifice. *Kodshei kodashim* must remain entirely within the *Azarah* — meat, blood, and fat alike. *Kodshim kalim*, such as a *shelamim* or *korban pesach*, have a broader zone: once the blood has been thrown and the *korban* is accepted, the meat may be eaten throughout Jerusalem. But what is the status of the meat *before* the blood is thrown? Does the meat's expanded zone (*kol Yerushalayim*) apply from the moment of *shechitah*, or only retroactively once the blood is thrown? In other words, does the meat of a *shelamim* become invalid if removed from the *Azarah* in the period between *shechitah* and *zerikah*?

The Gemara attempts to derive an answer from the case of the animal with a limb outside the *Azarah*, but rejects the inference. *Dilma bekodshei kodashim* — perhaps the ruling in that case was specifically about *kodshei kodashim*, for which there is never any allowance to leave the *Azarah*, not even for meat. If the concern arose from the *shamnunit* — the fattiness — then for *kodshei kodashim* the fattiness of the meat is like the meat, and the meat of *kodshei kodashim* can never exit the *Azarah*. But for *kodshim kalim*, the fattiness of the meat might not be problematic even before *zerikah*, since the meat's ultimate zone is all of Jerusalem. 

Similarly, when the Gemara discusses a case where the animal after *shechitah* thrashed out of the *Azarah* entirely (*pirchasa v'yatzah*), the question arises: what is invalid? By *kodshim kalim*, the meat going outside the *Azarah* might not be the problem. The Gemara answers: *dilma b'alyah v'yoteret hakaved u-shtei haklayot* — perhaps what becomes invalid is not the meat at all, but the *chelev* and the other sacrificial portions — the fat-tail, the diaphragm, the kidneys — which must always remain in the *Azarah* for burning on the altar, and which are invalid if removed even from a *kodshim kalim* sacrifice. The meat, on the other hand, may still be eaten, since ultimately its zone is all of Jerusalem and it is not necessarily invalidated by exiting the *Azarah* before *zerikah*. The question of whether meat of a *kodshim kalim* becomes invalid if it exits the *Azarah* before *zerikah hadam* thus remains unresolved from this passage.

## Kodshei Kodashim Location

### Shechitah Batzafon

A *baraita* is introduced: *kodshei kodashim* — the *olah*, *chatat*, and *asham* — require *shechitah* in the north (*batzafon*) and the reception of blood with a *klei sharet* also in the north. To fix the relevant geography: the *Azarah* is divided along its northern boundary, and the area along the north of the altar — *al yerech hamizbe'ach tzafonah* — is where *kodshei kodashim* must be slaughtered. The *baraita* then poses a case: *amad badarom v'hoshit yado latzafon v'shachat* — the *shochet* was standing in the southern half of the *Azarah* but reached his hand into the northern zone and performed the *shechitah*. What is the ruling? *Shechitah zo kesherah* — that *shechitah* is valid. The determinative factor is where the animal is located, not where the *shochet* is standing. The person performing the *shechitah* need not be a *kohen* — *shechitah* may be performed by any Israelite — and therefore there are no requirements governing where the *shochet* must stand. As long as the animal is in the north, the *shechitah* is valid regardless of the *shochet*'s position.

### Kibel Batzafon

With respect to *kabbalah*, however, the rule is different. *Kibel kabbalato pesulah* — if the *kohen* caught the blood while standing in the south, the *kabbalah* is invalid. The *kabbalah* must be performed by a *kohen*, and from the stage of *kabbalah* onward, the *kohen* must himself be in the appropriate zone. By *kodshei kodashim*, not only must the vessel and blood be in the north; the *kohen* performing the *kabbalah* must also be situated in the north.

The *baraita* then addresses partial presence. If the *kohen* was standing in the south but leaned his head and the majority of his body across the line into the north (*hichnis rosho v'rubo*), this counts as though he were fully inside (*k'ilu nichnas kulo*), and the *kabbalah* is valid. One might have assumed that a person is defined by where his feet are planted, but the Gemara rules otherwise for the north-south threshold: the majority of the body, including the head, determines location.

After *shechitah*, if the animal thrashed and moved into the southern zone (*pirchasa v'yatza ladarom*), this does not invalidate — once the *shechitah* and *kabbalah* have been performed validly, the subsequent movement of the carcass within the *Azarah* is inconsequential.

### Kodshim Kalim Location

For *kodshim kalim*, the requirements are calibrated differently. *Shechitatan bifnim* — the *shechitah* need only be inside the *Azarah*, not specifically in the north. *Kibel damam bichlei sharet bifnim* — the blood must also be caught with a *klei sharet* inside the *Azarah*. If the person performing the *shechitah* was standing outside the *Azarah* but reached his hand inside and performed the *shechitah*, it is valid — *shechitato kesherah*. Since *shechitah* requires no *kohen* and imposes no requirement of sacred standing, the *shochet* need not be inside the *Azarah* at all. One could theoretically perform the *shechitah* from any distance, as long as the animal itself is inside the *Azarah*.

For *kabbalah*, however, the *kohen* must be inside the *Azarah*. Here a crucial distinction emerges. For the north-versus-south question within the *Azarah*, we said that if the *kohen* leans his head and majority into the north, he counts as being in the north even if his feet are planted in the south. But for the *Azarah*-versus-outside question, the rule is reversed: *hichnis rosho v'rubo k'ilu lo nichnas* — if his head and the majority of his body lean into the *Azarah* while his feet remain outside, it is as though he has not entered at all.

The reason for this distinction is conceptually significant. The *kohen*'s sanctified status as a servant of the Temple derives from his direct physical connection to its floor — he is barefoot and his feet touch the ground of the *Azarah*, making him part of the apparatus of the *Beit HaMikdash*. When we measure north versus south within the *Azarah*, both zones are already within the sacred precincts, and we may use the majority of the body to determine location. But when determining whether a *kohen* is "inside" the *Azarah* at all, what matters is whether his feet are planted on the sacred ground. A *kohen* whose feet are outside the *Azarah* — even if most of his body leans in — has not established the foundational connection required for the *avodah*.

### Pirchasa V'Yatzah

For *kodshei kodashim*, if the animal thrashed after *shechitah* and exited the *Azarah* entirely, then returned, it is *pasul*. The act of exiting invalidated it. The question raised is: if this is a *shelamim*, why does it matter that the meat exited the *Azarah*? The meat of a *shelamim* is ultimately eaten throughout Jerusalem. The answer, as discussed above, is that the problem lies not with the meat but with the portions that must always remain in the *Azarah* — the *chelev*, the *alyah*, the kidneys. These components of even a *kodshim kalim* sacrifice cannot exit the *Azarah*, and their doing so before *zerikah* renders them invalid for the altar.

## Animal/Priest Positioning

### Kula Bifnim

The father of Shmuel posed a question to Shmuel: if the animal was inside the *Azarah* but its legs were outside (*hayu bifnim v'ragleihen bachutz*), what is the ruling? One might have thought this question was already resolved by the earlier discussion — the blood from outside would flow in and render the *kabbalah* invalid. But the question here is framed differently. The father of Shmuel is asking whether the animal is considered halachically "presented before God" when not all of its body is inside, and whether there is an independent requirement that the entire animal stand within the *Azarah*, beyond the concern about blood or fat traveling from outside.

The answer: *v'yuvam laHashem, ad she-tihyeh kula bifnim* — "it shall be brought before God," meaning the entire animal must be inside. This establishes a distinct requirement from the earlier *shamnunit* discussion: not only is there a practical concern about blood or fat from outside invalidating the *kabbalah*, but there is a formal requirement that the entire animal be present within the *Azarah* — all four legs included. Even if one leg is outside, the animal is not considered to be "before God."

### Taluy V'Shachat

Shmuel's father then asked about a suspended animal: *tala v'shachat mahu* — if the animal was hoisted in the air within the *Azarah* and the *shechitah* was performed while it was suspended, what is the ruling? Shmuel answered: *kesherah* — it should be valid. His father replied: *ishtabashta* — you have erred. The verse says *al yerech hamizbe'ach tzafonah* — "by the side of the altar, northward" — and this implies that just as the altar is planted on the ground, so too the animal, at the time of *shechitah* of *kodshei kodashim*, must be standing on the ground. A suspended animal, even if positioned in the correct northern zone, does not satisfy the requirement of *al yerech*.

### Shochet Position

Shmuel then guesses that the next case — the *shochet* himself suspended in the air while performing the *shechitah* — is likewise invalid. His father again corrects him: *shechitah al yerech v'lo shochet al yerech* — it is the *shechitah*, meaning the animal's position, that must satisfy *al yerech*, not the *shochet's* position. The *shochet* need not be in the north, need not be in the *Azarah*, and need not stand on the ground — because *shechitah* does not require a *kohen*, and therefore imposes none of the positioning requirements that attach to a *kohen* performing *avodah*. The *shochet* could, in principle, be suspended in midair.

### Suspension Validity

The series of questions continues. Shmuel's father then asks: *nitla v'kibel mahu* — if the *kohen* himself was suspended in midair while performing the *kabbalah*, what is the ruling? Shmuel guesses invalid, reasoning from the last answer. His father corrects him again: *ishtabashta* — you erred. The problem is not that the *kohen* was physically off the ground in any geometric sense, but that this is not the normal manner of Temple service — *ein derech sheirut bekach*. From the stage of *kabbalah* onward, the *avodah* must be performed in the manner of dignified service to God. A *kohen* suspended in midair does not constitute *derech sheirut*, and the *kabbalah* is therefore invalid. This is distinct from the *shechitah* case, where *derech sheirut* is not required because *shechitah* is not confined to the *kohen*'s act.

The next case: *tala v'kibel mahu* — the animal was suspended and the *kohen* stood on the ground and received the blood. Shmuel guesses invalid, still working from the previous answer. His father again corrects him: *shechitah al yerech v'lo kabbalah al yerech* — the requirement that the animal be situated on the ground (*al yerech*) is derived from the verse about *shechitah*, and applies only to *shechitah*. For *kabbalah*, there is no such verse requiring the animal to be ground-level. The feet of the animal do not need to touch the ground at the time of *kabbalah*.

### Abaye's Stringency

Abaye formulates his own summary. For *kodshei kodashim*, all suspension scenarios are invalid except for the case where the *shochet* himself is suspended — because *shechitah* imposes no *kehunah* requirements on the person performing it. By contrast, Abaye is more stringent than Shmuel's father on one point: he holds that even at the time of *kabbalah*, the feet of the *animal* must be on the ground. He reads *al yerech* as applying both to *shechitah* and to *kabbalah*, not just to *shechitah* alone.

For *kodshim kalim*, Abaye holds that all cases are valid except the case where the *kohen* performing the *kabbalah* is suspended. There is no *al yerech* requirement for *kodshim kalim* — neither for the *shochet* nor for the animal. The only suspension that is invalid is that of the *kohen* during *kabbalah*, which violates *derech sheirut* and the requirements of *kehunah*.

### Rava's Lenient View

Rava challenges Abaye's stringency regarding the animal at the time of *kabbalah* for *kodshei kodashim*. If, for *kodshim kalim*, a suspended animal at the time of *kabbalah* is valid because *avir pnim k'pnim dami* — the airspace within the *Azarah* is halachically equivalent to the *Azarah* itself — then the same principle should apply for *kodshei kodashim*: *avir tzafon k'tzafon dami* — the airspace over the northern zone is halachically equivalent to being in the north. There is no verse requiring the animal's feet to touch the ground at the time of *kabbalah*; the verse *al yerech* is uniquely bound to *shechitah*. Therefore, Rava argues that suspension of the animal at *kabbalah* is not independently derived and should not invalidate.

Rava's conclusion: for both *kodshei kodashim* and *kodshim kalim*, all cases involving a suspended animal are valid — except for one: the *shechitah* of *kodshei kodashim* where the animal is suspended, which is excluded by *al yerech hamizbe'ach tzafonah*. And in every category of sacrifice, if the *kohen* performing the *kabbalah* is himself suspended, that is invalid on account of *ein derech sheirut bekach* — it does not constitute proper priestly service. This applies equally to *kodshei kodashim* and *kodshim kalim*.

### Tzitzit Outside

The daf concludes with a question posed by Rav Yirmeyah to Rabbi Zeira. If the *kohen* is standing fully inside the *Azarah* — his feet planted on the sacred ground — but his *tzitzit*, a strand of hair (literally, his forelock or ponytail), extends outside the *Azarah*, what is the ruling at the time of *kabbalah*?

Rabbi Zeira answers by referencing the principle already established regarding the animal: *v'yuvam laHashem, ad she-tihyeh kula bifnim* — "it shall come before God," meaning the entire entity must be inside. The same applies to the *kohen*: *bevo'am el ohel mo'ed* — "when they enter the Tent of Meeting" — implies that the *kohen* must be fully present, *kulo l'ohel mo'ed*, not merely his feet and the majority of his body. Even a single strand of hair outside the *Azarah* at the time of *kabbalah* would constitute a deficiency in that complete presence.

This stands in sharp contrast to *shechitah*, where the *shochet* can stand outside the *Azarah*, can be suspended in midair, and faces virtually no locational requirements. From the moment of *kabbalah* onward, however — where *kehunah* is required — the *kohen* must be entirely and completely present within the sacred precincts. There is, embedded here, a teaching that resonates beyond the narrow *halakha*: when one is engaged in the *avodah* that requires the full weight of *kehunah*, one must be wholly present — not physically divided between the sacred and the outside.
