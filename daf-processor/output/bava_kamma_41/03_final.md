# Bava Kamma 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Kofer

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן דְּקַטְלַהּ דָּמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** that the ox should be considered **like one that killed her,** as it was the reason for her execution, and therefore its owner should be liable to pay a ransom, the *baraita* **teaches us** that this is not the case.

We pick up on daf 41b, four lines from the bottom. The *mishnah* had been treating the case of *nezek* caused by an ox, but with an appendix concerning the *apotropus* — whether an ox that becomes a *mu'ad* under a legal guardian acquires that status in the usual way. What the publishers of the Vilna Shas appended to the close of that *mishnah* is really the beginning of a new *mishnah*, one that shifts the focus to *kofer*. The transition is clean: the previous *mishnah* had discussed a stadium ox, one provoked into goring, and established that such an animal is not stoned. Before moving to the new *mishnah* proper, the *gemara* had asked whether the ox could still be used on the *mizbe'ach* — whether the fact that it committed an *aveira*, however forced upon it, creates the stigma of *ne'evad ba'aveira* sufficient to disqualify it. That question was left unresolved. We now return to the more central discussion of stoning and *kofer*.

### Stoning vs Kofer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם דְּרַבְעַהּ וְקַטְלַהּ; וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ: מָה לִי קַטְלַהּ בְּקַרְנַיִם, מָה לִי קַטְלַהּ בִּרְבִיעָה – קֶרֶן כַּוּוֹנָתוֹ לְהַזִּיק, הַאי כַּוּוֹנָתוֹ לַהֲנָאַת עַצְמוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Actually,** it is a case **where** the animal **engaged in bestiality with her and killed her** in the process. **And as for the difficulty you** pose: **What** is the difference **to me** whether **it killed her with** its **horns,** and **what** is the difference **to me** whether **it killed her through bestiality,** the answer is that in that case of an animal killing with its **horn, its intention is to cause injury,** whereas in **this** case of killing through bestiality **its intention is to** achieve **its own pleasure.**

The *pasuk* is explicit: *shor nogeach hu mi-tmol shilshom ve-hu'ad bi-ve'alav ve-lo yishmerennu ve-hemit ish o ishah ha-shor yisakkel ve-gam be'alav yumat im kofer yushas alav* — a *mu'ad* ox that kills is stoned, and its owner is liable to death, redeemed by payment of *kofer*. The first instance of goring carries the *sekilah* of the ox without *kofer*. The interplay between these two consequences — *sekilah* and *kofer* — is what the *gemara* now undertakes to analyze.

## Rovia vs Nogeiach

### Basic Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב: שׁוֹר הָאִיצְטָדִין – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב מִיתָה, וְכָשֵׁר לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ; מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כִּמְעוּשֶּׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav: A stadium ox** that killed a person **is not liable** to be put to **death and** is **fit to** be sacrificed as an offering on **the altar, because it is as though it was compelled** to behave in this manner.

The *gemara* cites a prior discussion in which animals are disqualified for the *mizbe'ach*: among them, an ox that gored (*nogei'ach*) and one that participated in an act of bestiality (*rovei'a*). Both, with sufficient witnesses, would be stoned; even without sufficient testimony they remain *pasul* for the *mizbe'ach*. The *gemara* notes a distinction in their relative gravity: *nogei'ach meshalelem et hakofer, rovei'a eino meshalelem et hakofer* — a goring ox obligates *kofer*; a *rovei'a* does not.

### What Case?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם דְּרַבְעַהּ וְלָא קַטְלַהּ, דְּאַתְיוּהָ לְבֵי דִינָא וְקַטְלוּהָ; מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Actually,** it is referring to a case **where** the animal **engaged in bestiality with her but did not kill her,** and it is still noteworthy that its owner is not liable to pay ransom, **as** the woman **was brought to court, and they executed her** for her transgression. **Lest you say**

The *gemara* immediately interrogates this distinction: *heichi dami* — what is the case? If the *rovei'a* killed the woman through the act of bestiality — *ilima de-rava ve-katla* — then *ma li katla bekarna ma li katla birvi'ah*: what difference does it make whether it killed with its horns or through the act itself? Death is death; why would *kofer* not be owed? Conversely, if the *rovei'a* did not kill — *de-rava velo katla* — then the exemption from *kofer* is simply because no death occurred, and that would be a perfectly natural reading: *kofer* is owed when a life is taken, so of course there is none here. Indeed, that seems to be the plain sense of the *baraisa*: in one case the animal killed, in the other it committed a grave *averia* but took no life, and *kofer* attaches to life-taking.

The *gemara*, however, resists this simple reading, insisting that there must be a legal distinction between the categories, not merely a factual one about whether death occurred.

### Abaye's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן דְּקַטְלַהּ דָּמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** that the ox should be considered **like one that killed her,** as it was the reason for her execution, and therefore its owner should be liable to pay a ransom, the *baraita* **teaches us** that this is not the case.

*Amar Abaye l'olam de-rava velo katla* — Abaye maintains that the *rovei'a* did not kill the woman directly. Yet because the act of bestiality subjects her to capital punishment by *beis din*, and because she was in fact executed for her participation, one might have thought: *keman dkatla dami* — perhaps the act of *revia* is treated as if it took a life, since it caused the execution of the woman through the machinery of *beis din*. The *gemara* therefore teaches: no, we do not say that.

*Tosafos* presses a sharp question here: if the woman is being stoned, that implies she was a willing and active participant in the transgression. In that case, the ox did not bring about her death — she brought it upon herself. How then can we even entertain the *havamina* that the ox bore responsibility? The question is acknowledged as stronger than the answer given.

### Rava's View

The passage you quoted above — where Rava says *le'olam de-reva ve-katla* — Rava holds that the *rovei'a* did in fact kill the woman through the violent act itself. So why no *kofer*? Because *keren kavanato lehazzik* — *keren* acts with intent to damage, while *hai kavanato la-hana'at atzmo* — this act was performed for the animal's own gratification, not with intent to harm.

### Shen ve-Regel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי? בְּרֶגֶל שֶׁדָּרְסָה עַל גַּבֵּי תִּינוֹק בַּחֲצַר הַנִּיזָּק; לְאַבָּיֵי – מְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר, לְרָבָא – לָא מְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** other case **would** Abaye and Rava **disagree?** They disagree **with regard to** a case where an ox **trampled a child with its foot,** killing it, **in the courtyard of the injured** party. **According to Abaye,** the owner **pays** the **ransom,** as he holds that ransom is paid even if the animal's objective was not to cause injury or death. **According to Rava, he does not pay** the **ransom.**

This leads directly to a striking question: does *kofer* apply in the category of *shen ve-regel* when a person is killed? Is *kofer* limited exclusively to *keren* — to *kavanato lehazzik* — or does it also apply to categories where no such intent is present?

The parallel case the *gemara* raises is *regel she-darsa gabei tinok be-chatzer hanizzak* — a foot that trampled an infant in the domain of the injured party. Since we are dealing with *shen ve-regel*, liability follows the parameters of those categories, including the territorial restrictions — there would be no liability in *reshut harabbim*, though one might question whether an act of bestiality against a person could ever be analogized to normal *shen ve-regel* activity that people are expected to guard against in a public domain.

The upshot: *ve-Abaye meshallem kofer, le-Rava lo meshallem kofer* — according to Abaye, *kofer* is owed even in the *shen ve-regel* case; according to Rava, it is not. Remarkably, this significant question does not emerge from a dedicated *mishnah* or *baraisa* on the topic. It arises as a byproduct of interpreting the contrast between *nogei'ach* and *rovei'a* in the context of *mizbe'ach* disqualifications.

The *gemara* then cites a supporting *baraisa*: *tanya kevattei de-Rav* — a *sitia* ox (one to which something was done) is not *chayav mita* and may even be used on the *mizbe'ach* because it is *ke-anusah*, treated as though coerced; it did not choose the act. This further clarifies the issue of *shen ve-regel* and *kofer* independently of the *mizbe'ach* question.

The lecturer flags a further pair of questions that hover over this entire analysis and remain, at this stage, unresolved: In the case of the stadium ox, where the animal is not stoned, would its owner pay *kofer*? And in the *shen ve-regel* case where Rava says no *kofer* — would the animal even be stoned? The logic seems clear enough in the latter case: if an animal acted without *kavanato lehazzik*, there is less grounds for treating the death as a culpable killing and therefore less basis for *sekilah*. But the question of whether *kofer* and *sekilah* can come apart — one present without the other — is precisely the tension the *gemara* is mapping, and it remains open whenever only one side of the pair is addressed.

## Tam to Mu'ad

### The Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר שֶׁנָּגַח אֶת הָאָדָם, וּמֵת – מוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר, וְתָם פָּטוּר מִן הַכּוֹפֶר. וְזֶה וְזֶה חַיָּיבִין מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **an ox that gored a person and** the person **died,** if the ox was **forewarned** its owner **pays ransom, but** if it was **innocuous he is exempt from** paying **the ransom. And** both **this** forewarned ox **and that** innocuous ox **are liable** to be put to **death** for killing a person.

The *mishnah* states: *mu'ad meshalem kofer ve-tam patur min hakofer* — a *mu'ad* pays *kofer* and a *tam* is exempt. Yet both are *chayav mita* — *zeh va-zeh chayavim misa*. This last point the *mishnah* derives from the *pesukim*, which speak of stoning from the very first goring: *sakol yisakel hashor velo yeachel et besaro uva'al hashor naki* — the ox shall be stoned and its flesh shall not be eaten and its owner shall be clean.

This raises an acute logical problem: *vechi me'achar demitam katlinan lei mu'ad heichi mashkachat lei* — if we stone the *tam* after the first killing, how does an ox ever live long enough to become a *mu'ad*? If the *Torah* is followed literally, the ox is executed the first time it kills. How does it accumulate the three prior gorings necessary to reach *mu'ad* status?

### Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אוּמְדָּנָא לָאו כְּלוּם הוּא. אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁסִּיכֵּן לִשְׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** Such **an assessment is not** worth **anything.** Since the ox did not actually kill them, it is not rendered forewarned even if it intended to kill. **Rather, here we are dealing with** a case **where it endangered** the lives of **three people** by goring them, and they all died only after the third goring. Therefore, the ox had not been put to death.

The *gemara* first considers an answer attributed to *Rabba*: *she'amdu lishelosha bnei adam* — three people were seriously threatened; based on assessment (*umdna*) they would have been killed had they not escaped at the last moment. But Rav Ashi rejects this: *umdna lav klum hu* — an assessment does not count for anything. You cannot establish *mu'ad* status on the basis of a near-miss.

Rav Ashi's own answer is: *kegon shesiken lishelosha bnei adam* — the ox critically injured three people. Because the victims had not yet died, the ox could not yet be executed — *sekilah* requires an actual death, and the court case must await that outcome. But the owner can be warned based on the critical injuries: the ox has demonstrated both the capacity and the propensity to kill fatally. That is sufficient to establish a warning and begin the process of rendering it a *mu'ad*, even if the full status with attendant *kofer* liability awaits the actual deaths.

Rashi's formulation — *l'kach lo niscal b'shem harishon u'kshenagach shlishi metu kulam hilchach meshalem alav et hakofer* — requires that all three victims die. It is not entirely clear from the *gemara* why that is necessary if even one death would suffice to trigger *kofer* liability on a *mu'ad*. It is worth noting that Rashi consistently assumes that the full *mu'ad* payment, including *kofer*, is triggered at the third goring. *Tosafos* points out that the plain sense of the other *sugyot* is that the first three gorings establish *mu'ad* status, so that it is the fourth goring where *mu'ad* liability and *kofer* apply. Rashi's reading runs against this in a consistent and notable way.

### Rav Zevid Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָרַג שָׁלֹשׁה בְּהֵמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said:** The mishna is discussing a case **where it killed three animals,** which is sufficient to render the ox forewarned but for which it is not put to death.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוּעָד לִבְהֵמָה הָוֵי מוּעָד לְאָדָם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is** an animal that is **forewarned with regard to animals** considered **forewarned with regard to people** as well?

Rav Zevid proposes: *kegon sheharag shelosha behemot* — the ox killed three animals. Michael raises this question, and the *gemara* immediately rejects it: *u-mu'ad livehema havai mu'ad l'adam*? Being a habitual killer of animals makes it a *mu'ad* with respect to people? The *gemara* treats this as unacceptable.

This rejection is significant. In an earlier *sugya*, there was a debate between Rav Zevid and Rav Ashi about whether *mu'ad* status extends across categories — whether an ox habituated to goring one type of victim is considered *mu'ad* for another. Rav Zevid had read the relevant text as *v'imu'ad*, suggesting that once *mu'ad* status is established, we presume it applies broadly unless the evidence clearly limits it to a specific type. The *stama d'gemara* here clearly finds the extension from *behema* to *adam* absurd, treating it as self-evidently wrong. This in turn supports the position that we read *mu'ad* status as narrowly as the evidence of the three prior acts warrants. The formalist approach dominates: the *mu'ad* character is defined by what the ox was actually seen to do, not abstracted into a general propensity for violence.

### Victims & Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָרַג שְׁלֹשָׁה גּוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Shimi said:** The mishna is discussing a case **where it killed three gentiles,** for which the animal is not put to death.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָרַג שְׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said:** The mishna is discussing a case **where it killed three people who had wounds that would have caused them to die within twelve months [*tereifa*].** Since they were on the verge of dying anyway, the ox is not put to death for killing them. Nevertheless, it is rendered forewarned with regard to its future goring and killing of people.

*Rav Shimi* then suggests: *kegon she'harag shelosha ovdei kochavim* — the ox killed three non-Jews. The original text used *goyim*, though that has been censored in standard printings. The logic is clear: a *shor* is not stoned for killing a non-Jew; therefore the ox could not be executed after those three deaths, yet it has demonstrated a propensity for fatal violence that suffices to establish a warning and *mu'ad* status. The *gemara* rejects this too: *u-mu'ad le'ovdei kochavim havei mu'ad le'Yisrael*? — just because it kills non-Jews makes it *mu'ad* for Jews?

The question of whether there is a principled reason for the distinction, or whether this is purely formalist — the ox has only done what it has done — runs through all these cases. The *gemara* may be assuming some substantive difference, perhaps that Jews and non-Jews present different circumstances to a goring animal. But the formalist reading is equally available: if the three prior acts involved only non-Jewish victims, that is the only identity the ox has established as a *mu'ad*.

*Resh Lakish* then proposes: *kegon she'harag shelosha benei adam tereifa* — it killed three people who were *tereifa*, i.e., who had internal injuries rendering them halachically in a category such that killing them does not constitute capital murder. For a human murderer who kills a *tereifa*, there is no capital punishment — the victim is not *shalem* in the relevant sense, though this is not merely a question of life expectancy. A *goses* — someone actively dying — does not carry this exemption; it is specifically the halachic category of *tereifa*, defined by a particular type of internal injury, that changes the status of the killing. By the same logic applied to the ox: it is not stoned for killing *tereifos*, so it survives. But the *gemara* again rejects the extension: *u-mu'ad le-tereifa havei mu'ad le-shalem*? — perhaps a *tereifa*, for whatever reason, attracts violence from animals in a way a healthy person would not.

### Rav Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דִּקְטַל וַעֲרַק לְאַגְמָא, דִּקְטַל וַעֲרַק לְאַגְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Pappa said:** The mishna is discussing a case **where it killed** a person **and fled to the marsh,** then **killed** again **and fled to the marsh,** and then killed again and fled to the marsh, so the court was not able kill it before it had killed three times, rendering it forewarned.

*Rav Pappa* cuts through all of this: *de'katal ve'arak le'agma de'katal ve'arak le'agma* — each time it killed, the ox fled to the swamp. The court was unable to capture and execute it, but the owner was warned three times. There is no need to construct exotic categories of victims. The ox did kill, the owner was warned, but the physical impossibility of apprehending the animal prevented the court from carrying out *sekilah*. On the fourth goring the animal is finally caught, it can be stoned, and the owner — having been warned three times — owes *kofer*.

A subtle question arises: if *sekilah* requires the court case to be conducted in the presence of the ox, can the testimony of witnesses even be received — for purposes of warning the owner — without the ox present? It may be that the warning can proceed on the basis of witness testimony even when the ox is not before the court, while the formal judgment of *sekilah* remains suspended until the animal is apprehended.

### Zommin Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוּזְמוּ זוֹמְמֵי זוֹמְמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said:** The mishna is discussing a case **where** the witnesses **who had rendered** the witnesses who testified to the three incidents of goring as **conspiring witnesses,** resulting in the animal not being put to death, **were** themselves subsequently **proven** to be **conspiring witnesses** by other witnesses. Consequently, the testimonies of the witnesses who testified about the incidents of goring were reinstated, rendering the ox forewarned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא אִי לְיַיעוֹדֵי תּוֹרָא בָּעֵינַן – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא אִי לְיַיעוֹדֵי גַּבְרָא בָּעֵינַן, מֵימַר אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא הֲוָה יָדַעְנָא! כְּגוֹן דְּאָמְרִי: כֹּל אֵימַת דְּקָטֵיל תּוֹרֵיהּ, גַּבֵּיהּ הֲוָה קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** explanation **works out well if** it is assumed that the purpose of testifying to the ox's goring is because **we wish to establish the ox as forewarned;** once the testimony concerning the incidents of goring is reinstated, it is established that the ox gored three times. **But if we wish to warn the man** who owns the ox by testifying that the animal gored, **he could say to** the judge, after it is established that his ox was forewarned: **I did not know** that my ox was forewarned, since the witnesses had previously been rendered conspiring witnesses. The Gemara answers: This is a case **where** the witnesses **say: Each time his ox killed** a person **he was standing by it,** so that he cannot claim ignorance.

*Rav Acha brei de-Rav Ika* offers a further scenario involving *zommin*: *kegon she-huzmu zommei zomemin* — those who invalidated the witnesses were themselves invalidated. Here is the sequence: a first pair of witnesses testifies that the ox gored and killed; their testimony is accepted in court and *sekilah* is ordered. A second group then appears and says: *immanu hayisa b'oso hayom* — you were with us that day, you could not have witnessed the goring. The first testimony is thrown out. This happens a second and third time. Then a third group of witnesses comes and says the same thing to the second group — that *they* could not have been where they claimed to be. The second group is thereby invalidated, and the original testimony of the first group is retroactively resurrected.

The *gemara* asks: does this work? *Haneicha i-leyudei tora ba-inan* — if what we need is simply to establish that the ox has a history of goring (*yedi'at tora*), then yes: we now know that the first group's testimony was always true, and the ox did in fact gore three times. *Ella i-leyudei gavra ba-inan* — but if we require that the *owner* be meaningfully warned and personally violate that warning, then the owner can say: I relied on the second group's invalidation of the first witnesses; I genuinely believed my ox had not gored.

The *gemara* resolves this by stipulating that the first group's testimony included that the owner himself was present at each goring: *kol eimas de-katil tora gabei havai kai* — every time your ox gored, you were standing there. Given that the owner was present, his claim of ignorance fails: he cannot say he did not know. He knew, or should have known, that the first group was telling the truth because they were testifying to something he personally witnessed.

The distinction between two formulations of warning is important: *yedia tori* — knowing the ox's history — versus *yedia gavra* — knowing the owner knew and failed to act. In the simplest case, a single pair of witnesses before *beis din* testifying that the ox gored three times would establish *mu'ad* status without requiring the owner to be warned after each individual incident. But the present scenario is specifically designed to show how the owner's personal presence allows the warning to function even when intermediary testimony had temporarily invalidated it.

### Ravina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: בְּמַכִּירִין אֶת בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר, וְאֵין מַכִּירִין אֶת הַשּׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** The mishna is discussing a case **where** the witnesses **recognized the owner of the ox but did not recognize the ox** itself. Therefore, with regard to the first incidents of goring, they testified that it was his ox that gored, but they did not testify with regard to the ox itself. That is why the ox was not put to death. Only afterward did they realize that this was the ox that had gored three times previously.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֶעְבַּד? מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: תּוֹרָא נַגְחָנָא אִית לָךְ בְּבַקְרָךְ, אִיבְּעִי לָךְ נַטּוֹרֵי כּוּלֵּי בַּקְרָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, why is the ox rendered forewarned? **What could** the owner **have done** to prevent it from goring again, as he did not know which of his oxen had gored? The Gemara answers that it is rendered forewarned **because** the court effectively **said to him: You have** a habitually **goring ox in your herd,** so **you must safeguard your entire herd.**

*Ravina* proposes yet another scenario: *bemakirin es baal hashor ve'ein makirin es hashor* — the witnesses know one of the owner's oxen gored, but they cannot identify which ox it was. The ox cannot be stoned because the court cannot identify it. But the owner can be warned: you have a goring ox among your cattle. *Ibayi lach linterei kulei bakra* — you are obligated to watch all of your cattle. The owner cannot excuse himself by saying he did not know which ox to restrain. Eventually, on the last occasion, the witnesses recognize the ox; it can now be stoned and the owner is liable for *kofer*.

The *gemara* asks — *mai hava lei lemevad*, what was the owner supposed to do if he did not know which ox it was? — and gives the answer just stated: his obligation, once warned that a killer is among his herd, is to restrain the entire herd.

## Mishnah Analysis

### Lo Yochel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב: שׁוֹר הָאִיצְטָדִין – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב מִיתָה, וְכָשֵׁר לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ; מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כִּמְעוּשֶּׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav: A stadium ox** that killed a person **is not liable** to be put to **death and** is **fit to** be sacrificed as an offering on **the altar, because it is as though it was compelled** to behave in this manner.

Before turning to the *mishnah* itself, the *gemara* derives from the *Torah* when the prohibition on the ox's meat and benefit begins. The *baraisa* says: from the verse *sakol yisakel hashor*, we would know it becomes *neveila* — but *neveila* is obviously forbidden to eat. Why then does the *Torah* say *lo yeachel et besaro*? This teaches that once judgment has been passed on the ox — even before *sekilah* has been carried out — if the owner were to shecht the animal, its meat remains forbidden. *Leachar shenigmar dino asur ba'achilah* — the prohibition attaches at the conclusion of the legal judgment, not at the moment of execution.

### Mishnah Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר שֶׁנָּגַח אֶת הָאָדָם, וּמֵת – מוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר, וְתָם פָּטוּר מִן הַכּוֹפֶר. וְזֶה וְזֶה חַיָּיבִין מִיתָה. וְכֵן בַּבֵּן, וְכֵן בַּבַּת. נָגַח עֶבֶד אוֹ אָמָה – נוֹתֵן שְׁלֹשִׁים סְלָעִים; בֵּין שֶׁהוּא יָפֶה מֵאָה מָנֶה, וּבֵין שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָפֶה אֶלָּא דִּינָר אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **an ox that gored a person and** the person **died,** if the ox was **forewarned** its owner **pays ransom, but** if it was **innocuous he is exempt from** paying **the ransom. And** both **this** forewarned ox **and that** innocuous ox **are liable** to be put to **death** for killing a person. **And the same** *halakha* applies **in** a case where the animal killed **a boy and the same** applies **in** a case where it killed **a girl.** If the ox **gored** and killed a Canaanite **slave or** a Canaanite **maidservant,** its owner **gives** the victim's master **thirty *sela*, whether he was** a slave **worth one hundred *maneh*,** i.e., one hundred silver dinars, **or worth only one dinar.**

The *mishnah* itself: *shor shenagach es adam vames — mu'ad meshalem kofer ve-tam patur min hakofer, zeh va-zeh chayavim misa* — a *mu'ad* pays *kofer*, a *tam* is exempt, but both are liable to *mita*. *Ve-chein be-ven ve-chein be-vat* — the same applies if the ox kills a child. This is obvious as a matter of law but the *mishnah* is working through the *pesukim* systematically: the *Torah* says *o ven yigach o vat yigach kamishpat hazeh yei'aseh lo* — if it gores a son or a daughter, the same law applies. The next *pasuk* addresses the case of a slave: *im eved yigach hashor*, and the *mishnah* follows accordingly.

*Nagach eved o amah noten shloshim sla'im* — if it gores a slave or maidservant, the payment is thirty *shekel, bein shehu yafeh me'ah maneh bein she'eino yafeh ela dinar echad* — whether the slave is worth a hundred *maneh* or only one *dinar*, the payment is always the fixed amount of thirty *shekel*. This is the irony of the slave *pasuk*: whereas *kofer* varies with the value of the victim — assessed by the slave market value of the person killed — the payment for a slave is counterintuitively fixed regardless of that slave's market value.

### Ba'al Naki

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזֶה וָזֶה חַיָּיבִין מִיתָה כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״סָקֹל יִסָּקֵל הַשּׁוֹר״ – אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁנְּבֵילָה הִיא, וּנְבֵילָה אֲסוּרָה בַּאֲכִילָה? מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא יֵאָכֵל אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ״? מַגִּיד לְךָ הַכָּתוּב, שֶׁאִם שְׁחָטוֹ לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ – אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** both **this** forewarned ox **and that** innocuous ox **are liable** to be put to **death. The Sages taught:** We learn **by inference from** that **which is stated** with regard to an ox that killed a person: "And if an ox gores a man or a woman, that they die, **the ox shall be stoned,** and its flesh shall not be eaten; but the owner of the ox shall be clear" (Exodus 21:28). **Don't I know** from this verse **that** the stoning makes it an unslaughtered **animal carcass, and it is prohibited to eat** an unslaughtered **animal carcass? What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "Its flesh shall not be eaten"? The verse is telling you that** even **if one slaughtered** the ox **after its verdict had been reached** but before it was stoned, it is still **prohibited to eat** it.

The *gemara* then derives from the phrase *uba'al hashor naki* — and the owner of the ox is clean — that the owner may not derive any benefit from the ox, not merely that he cannot eat it.

The *baraisa* begins the derivation: once we learn from *lo yeachel et besaro* that the meat may not be eaten, we know this applies even when the ox was shechted after judgment. But how do we know there is also an *issur hanaah* — a prohibition on benefit? *Talmud lomar uba'al hashor naki.* The owner is "cleaned out" — *shimon ben zoma omer keadam sheomer lechavero yatza ish ploni naki mikhasav ve'ein lo vo hana'ah klum* — like one who says of another that he has been completely cleaned out of his property and has nothing left. The owner of the ox is similarly stripped of any benefit from the animal.

But a question arises: if *lo yeachel et besaro* tells us that you cannot eat it even after shechting, and if — following the principle of *Rabbi Abbahu* in the name of *Rabbi Elazar* — *lo yeachel* always includes *issur hanaah* unless the *Torah* explicitly permits benefit (as it does by *neveila*, where it says to give to the stranger or sell to the foreigner), then *lo yeachel et besaro* already tells us both *issur achilah* and *issur hanaah*. What then remains for *uba'al hashor naki* to add?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּאֲכִילָה, בַּהֲנָאָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּבַעַל הַשּׁוֹר נָקִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **I have** derived **only** that one is prohibited **to eat** it; **from where** is it derived that one is prohibited **from** deriving **benefit** from the ox as well? **The verse states: "But the owner of the ox shall be clear."**

The *gemara* answers: *lehana'at oro* — to prohibit benefit even from the hide. One might have thought *besaro* — the flesh — is what is restricted, but the leather, being commercially valuable, might remain permitted. *Uba'al hashor naki* teaches that the owner is stripped entirely, including of the hide.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַתָּה דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ אִיסּוּר אֲכִילָה וְאִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה מִ״לֹּא יֵאָכֵל אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ״, ״בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר נָקִי״ לְמָה לִי? לַהֲנָאַת עוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to Rabbi Abbahu, **now that he derives the prohibition of eating and the prohibition of deriving benefit from** the verse **"Its flesh shall not be eaten," why do I** require the statement: **"The owner of the ox shall be clear"?** The Gemara answers: This is stated **to** prohibit **deriving benefit from its hide** after it has been killed.

A further question: why not derive the prohibition on the hide from the word *et* in *et besaro*? Following the hermeneutic principle of *Shimon HaAmsuni* — who interpreted every instance of *et* in the *Torah* as including something adjacent to the named object — the word *et* would teach that *hatafeil lebesaro*, what is secondary and attached to the meat, namely the hide, is also included. The *gemara* answers that this *tanna* does not derive legal content from the word *et* at all, following a different hermeneutic approach.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי תַּנָּא – ״אֶת״ לָא דָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And this *tanna*,** who derives it from the statement: "The owner of the ox shall be clear," **does not interpret** the word **"*et*"** as a means to derive new *halakhot*. He considers the word "*et*" to be an ordinary part of the sentence structure and not a source for exegetical exposition.

This occasions the famous *aggadic* passage:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: שִׁמְעוֹן הָעַמְסוֹנִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ נְחֶמְיָה הָעַמְסוֹנִי, הָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ כׇּל ״אֶתִּין״ שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְ״אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּירָא״, פֵּירַשׁ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ תַּלְמִידָיו: רַבִּי, כׇּל ״אֶתִּין״ שֶׁדָּרַשְׁתָּ – מָה תְּהֵא עֲלֵיהֶן? אָמַר לָהֶם: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁקִּבַּלְתִּי שָׂכָר עַל הַדְּרִישָׁה, כָּךְ קִבַּלְתִּי שָׂכָר עַל הַפְּרִישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Shimon HaAmasoni, and some say** that it was **Neḥemya HaAmasoni, would interpret all** occurrences of the word **"*et*" in the Torah,** deriving additional *halakhot* with regard to the particular subject matter. **Once he reached** the verse: **"You shall fear the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 6:13), which is written with the added word "*et*," **he withdrew** from this method of exposition, as whose fear could be an extension of the fear of God? **His students said to him: Our teacher, what will be with all** the occurrences of the word **"*et*" that you interpreted** until now? **He said to them: Just as I received reward for the exposition, so I received reward for** my **withdrawal** from using this method of exposition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וְלִימֵּד: ״אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּירָא״ – לְרַבּוֹת תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The word "*et*" in this verse was not explained **until Rabbi Akiva came and expounded: "You shall fear the Lord your God":** The word "*et*" serves **to include Torah scholars,** i.e., that one is commanded to fear them just as one fears God.

*Tosafos* observes that elsewhere the *gemara* does draw a comparison between fear of parents and fear of Heaven — *ish immo ve'aviv tira* is paralleled to *et Hashem Elokecha tira* — without discomfort. *Tosafos* distinguishes: there the *Torah* draws an analogy between two separate *mitzvos*. Here, however, to say that the *mitzvah* of fearing God intrinsically *includes* another category of beings would be to place them far too close to the divine — a qualitatively different and far more fraught claim, which is precisely why *Shimon HaAmsuni* was unwilling to say it.

On the broader question of whether *drashot* carry the weight of *Sinai* transmission: the *gemara* does not assume that the content of exegetical derivations was transmitted from Sinai. The most that can be said of *masora* as obligating content applies primarily — and even then not absolutely — to *gzera shava*, where the Ramban already shows that the *gemara* itself debates which terms to pair. The Rambam's position, developed against the Karaite critique, is that very little content was handed down intact; rather, the hermeneutic capacity — the empowerment of rabbinic courts to derive and rule — is what was transmitted, and those derivations, properly made, carry *d'Oraisa* force.

## Half-Kofer Debate

### Eliezer vs Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּבַעַל הַשּׁוֹר נָקִי״ – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: נָקִי מֵחֲצִי כוֹפֶר.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** with regard to the verse: **"But the owner of the ox shall be clear,"** that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** It means that **he shall be clear from paying half a ransom.** Although the owner of an innocuous ox that causes damage is liable to pay half the cost of the damage, if an ox kills a person its owner is not liable to pay any ransom.

The *gemara* then presents a tannaitic dispute over the *drasha* on *uba'al hashor naki*. *Rabbi Eliezer* reads: *naki michatzi kofer* — the owner of a *tam* is clean from having to pay half *kofer*. The argument is by analogy: if a *mu'ad* pays full *kofer*, one might reason that a *tam* — by the same logic that it pays half-damages for ordinary *nezek* — should pay half *kofer*. *Rabbi Eliezer* reads *uba'al hashor naki* as foreclosing that analogy.

### Akiva's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וַהֲלֹא הוּא עַצְמוֹ אֵין מִשְׁתַּלֵּם אֶלָּא מִגּוּפוֹ, ״הֲבִיאֵהוּ לְבֵית דִּין, וִישַׁלֶּם לָךְ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him:** Why is it necessary for the verse to teach this? **But isn't** compensation for damage caused by an innocuous ox **itself** anyway **paid only from** the value of **its body?** Here too, its owner can say to the claimant: **Bring it to court and it will pay you,** i.e., you will be paid from its value. Since the ox is stoned, there is nothing from which he can receive payment. Therefore, according to your interpretation, the verse does not introduce any *halakha* that could not have been inferred by logic, and is therefore superfluous.

*Rabbi Akiva* objects: who needs a special *drasha* for this? *V'halo hu atzmo ein mishtalem ela migufo* — the *tam*'s payment of half-damages is collected from the body of the ox itself. So if you were to reason by analogy that the *tam* owes half *kofer*, the payment would similarly come from the animal. But the animal is stoned and its body is forbidden for any benefit. *Yavou l'veit din v'yisalku lach* — let the owner tell the claimant: come to court and take your half *kofer* from the body of the ox. The body is worthless. There is nothing to pay. No *drasha* is needed; the halacha enforces itself automatically.

### Eliezer Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: כָּךְ אֲנִי בְּעֵינֶיךָ, שֶׁדִּינִי בָּזֶה שֶׁחַיָּיב מִיתָה? אֵין דִּינִי אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁהֵמִית אֶת הָאָדָם עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד, אוֹ עַל פִּי בְעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer said to him: Is this how I** appear **in your eyes, that my derivation** from the verse is **in** reference to **this** case, that of an ox **that is liable** to be put to **death?** Obviously no verse would be required to teach this *halakha* in such a case. **My derivation is only** with regard to a case **where** the assumption that the ox **killed a person** is **based on** the testimony of **one witness, or** is **based on** the admission of **the owner.** Such proof is not sufficient for the ox to be put to death by stoning, but one might have thought that it is sufficient to require its owner to pay ransom.

*Rabbi Eliezer* responds: *kach ani b'einecha shedanti b'zeh?* — is that what you think of me? That I was discussing a case where the ox is stoned? Of course in that case there is nothing to pay. *Ein dini ela k'shehe'emidu et ha'adam al pi ed echad o al pi be'alim* — my case is where the testimony establishing that the ox killed came from a single witness or from the owner's own admission, which suffices to obligate *kofer* as a *kapara* payment but is insufficient to bring about *sekilah*, which requires two witnesses as in a capital case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל פִּי בְעָלִים?! מוֹדֶה בִּקְנָס הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If it is **based on** the admission of **the owner,** he clearly would not be liable to pay ransom, since **he** would be **admitting to** an act that results in **a fine,** in which case a person is exempt from paying the fine; so why would it be necessary for the verse to state this exemption?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר כּוּפְרָא – כַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Eliezer **holds** that **ransom** is for the purpose of **atonement,** and is not considered a fine.

*Rabbi Akiva* raises a further objection: if the owner is admitting, that is an admission to a *knas* — a fine — and one who voluntarily admits to a fine is exempt from it. *Rabbi Eliezer* replies: *kasvar kofer kapara* — he holds that *kofer* is not a *knas* but a *kapara*, an atonement payment. An admission to a *kapara* obligation — analogous to admitting one owes a *korban* — is fully effective. The owner admits liability, the liability stands, and *sekilah* is not triggered because two witnesses are lacking.

## Kofer No Stone

### Intended Beast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ, אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: עֲקִיבָא, כָּךְ אֲנִי בְּעֵינֶיךָ, שֶׁדִּינִי בָּזֶה שֶׁחַיָּיב מִיתָה? אֵין דִּינִי אֶלָּא בְּמִתְכַּוֵּון לַהֲרוֹג אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה וְהָרַג אֶת הָאָדָם; לְגוֹי וְהָרַג יִשְׂרָאֵל; לִנְפָלִים וְהָרַג בֶּן קַיָּימָא.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer said to him: Akiva, is this how I** appear **in your eyes, that my derivation** from the verse is **in** reference to **this** case, that of an ox **that is liable** to be put to **death? My derivation is only** with regard to a case **where** the ox **intended to kill an animal but killed a person** instead; or where it intended **to** kill **a gentile but killed a Jew;** or where it intended **to** kill **a non-viable baby but killed a viable person.** An ox is not put to death if it intended to gore in a manner which would not render it liable to be put to death, even if it did gore in such a manner. In such a case, the owner of the ox must pay ransom. Therefore, the verse states that if it is an innocuous ox, the owner is exempt from paying half a ransom.

A second *baraisa* clarifies *Rabbi Eliezer*'s position still further. When *Rabbi Akiva* pressed him — how can there be *kofer* from a stoned ox? — *Rabbi Eliezer* answered: *kach ani b'einecha shedanti b'zeh shechayav mita? Ein dini ela b'mitkavein laharog et habeheima v'harag et ha'adam* — I am not dealing with a case where the ox is deserving of *sekilah* at all. My case is where the ox intended to kill an animal and instead killed a person. In such a case, since the intent was not to kill a human being, the ox is not executed: this was not murder in the full sense. Yet a human life was lost, so *kofer* is owed. The animal is not subject to *sekilah* — it is not wicked enough to be stoned — but the death of a human being generates a *kofer* obligation.

### Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְגוֹי וְהָרַג יִשְׂרָאֵל; לִנְפָלִים וְהָרַג בֶּן קַיָּימָא.
>
> **Translation:** or where it intended **to** kill **a gentile but killed a Jew;** or where it intended **to** kill **a non-viable baby but killed a viable person.**

*Rabbi Eliezer* elaborates: *sheniskaven laharog et habeheima veharag et haadam* — it intended to kill an animal and killed a human. Or where it intended to kill a non-Jew (*le-goy*) and killed a Jew — no *sekilah*, but *kofer*. Or where it intended to kill a *nefil* — a fetus — *veharag ben kayama* — and killed a viable person. In each of these cases the ox lacks the criminal intent that would warrant *sekilah*, but a human life was taken and *kofer* applies.

These cases also bear on the question raised earlier about *shen ve-regel* and *kofer*. There, the animal acts without *kavanah le-hazik* at all — it is not intending to harm. Here, the animal does have *kavanah le-hazik*, but that intent is directed at a category of victim whose death would not trigger *sekilah*. The parallel structure strengthens the case that *kofer* and *sekilah* can come apart, and that *kofer* is triggered by the fact that a human life was lost, while *sekilah* is reserved for acts of killing that satisfy the full criteria of culpable homicide.

### Big Fish Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ בְּרֵישָׁא? רַב כָּהֲנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: ״מִתְכַּוֵּון״ אֲמַר לֵיהּ בְּרֵישָׁא. רַב טָבְיוֹמֵי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: ״הֵמִית״ אֲמַר לֵיהּ בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** These two *baraitot* cite different responses that Rabbi Eliezer gave Rabbi Akiva. **Which** one of these explanations **did he say to him first? Rav Kahana said in the name of Rava: He first said to him** the explanation referring to the ox's **intention** to kill someone for which it would not be liable to be put to death, and subsequently gave him the second explanation with regard to the testimony of a single witness, or the owner's admission. **Rav Tavyumei said in the name of Rava: He first said to him** the explanation that it is a case where the assumption that the ox **killed** was based on one witness or its owner's admission.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר ״מִתְכַּוֵּון״ אֲמַר לֵיהּ בְּרֵישָׁא – מָשָׁל לְצַיָּד שֶׁשּׁוֹלֶה דָּגִים מִן הַיָּם,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the reasoning behind the two opinions: **Rav Kahana said in the name of Rava** that **first he said to him** the explanation involving the ox's **intention,** since this opinion is preferable to the other one, against which a difficulty was raised earlier. This is **analogous to a fisherman pulling fish from the sea.**

The *gemara* closes with a dispute about the order in which *Rabbi Eliezer* offered his answers. *Rav Kahana* in the name of *Rava* says *niskaven amar lehu bereisha* — he gave the "intended to kill the animal" answer first. *Rav Tavyumi* in the name of *Rava* says *hen meisu amar lehu bereisha* — no, he gave the single-witness / owner-admission answer first.

Each position is illustrated by the parable of the fisherman: *mashal letzayad sheshola dagim min hayam* — like one pulling fish from the sea. According to *Rav Kahana*: first he pulls up big fish, takes them, then pulls up small fish, and takes those too. That is: *Rabbi Eliezer* led with the stronger answer — the ox intended to kill the animal and killed the person, a case where *sekilah* is simply inapplicable — and then added the weaker, supporting case of single-witness testimony. The better answer is the "big fish," the case where the animal is fundamentally not *chayav mita*; the single-witness case is the small fish, added as supplementary support.

According to *Rav Tavyumi*: first the fisherman pulls up small fish and keeps them not knowing what else he might find; then he pulls up big fish and throws the small ones back. That is: *Rabbi Eliezer* first offered the single-witness answer, then realized he had a far stronger response — the case of intended animal homicide — and discarded the first answer in favor of the better one.
