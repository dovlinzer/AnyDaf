# Bekhorot 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Purchased/Gifted Animals

### Mishnah Rule

The daf opens at the bottom of 55b, at a *mishnah* that addresses exemptions from *ma'aser behemah*. After the previous discussion of how one combines a flock to determine whether it constitutes a single unit for tithing purposes, the *mishnah* turns to a different class of animal altogether: *haloke'ach o shenitan lo b'matanah patur mi-ma'aser behemah* — if one purchases animals or receives them as a gift, they are exempt from the animal tithe. The obligation of *ma'aser behemah* applies only to animals born directly into one's own flock, not to those acquired from elsewhere.

### Verse Comparison

The Gemara asks: *mina hani milei* — from where is this derived? Rav Kahana answers by citing the verse in Shemot: *bechor banecha titein li, kein ta'aseh l'shorcha l'tzonacha, shivat yamim yihyeh im imo bayom hashmini titno li* — give Me the firstborn of your sons; so shall you do with your ox and your sheep; seven days it shall remain with its mother, and on the eighth day you shall give it to Me. The verse draws an explicit parallel between the *bechor* of one's sons and one's flock. From this juxtaposition, Rav Kahana derives: just as your sons are not acquired through purchase or gift — they are born to you — so too your flock and cattle, for purposes of this obligation, must be born to you. Purchased or gifted animals fall outside the scope of *ma'aser behemah*.

### Not for Bechor

An immediate difficulty presents itself: *v'hai b'bechorah ktiv* — that verse is written in the context of *bechorah*, not *ma'aser*. Furthermore, the exclusion of purchased animals is not true of *bechor* in the first place, since a *bechor* is *kadosh* from the moment it emerges from the womb. If someone sells a *bechor*, it retains its sanctity; the new owner cannot simply declare it non-*kadosh* by virtue of having purchased it. By *ma'aser behemah*, the rules are different — one can argue that under certain circumstances the obligation does not attach. But by *bechor*, sanctity is automatic and unconditional. So the verse is about *bechor*, and the rule it seems to be teaching does not even hold true for *bechor*. How, then, can we read it as a source for *ma'aser behemah*?

The Gemara answers: *amar kra kein ta'aseh* — the verse says "so shall you do," implying an act of sanctification. This language of *ta'aseh* is superfluous if we are speaking of *bechor*, because *bechor* does not require any act to render it sacred — *demeracham kadosh*, it is sanctified from the womb. *Im eino inyan l'bechor, teneihu inyan l'ma'aser behemah* — since this language of active doing is inapplicable to *bechor*, apply it instead to *ma'aser behemah*, which does require an act of sanctification. The extra phrase *kein ta'aseh* is thus read as referring to a different obligation altogether, one that requires a deliberate act of consecration.

Tosafot notes that this does not render the verse irrelevant to *bechor*; rather, the superfluous phrase *kein ta'aseh* carries an additional teaching about *ma'aser behemah*, while the rest of the passage continues to speak of *bechor* in its primary sense.

### Dumya Deduction

The Gemara is not satisfied with simply redirecting the verse to *ma'aser behemah*, because *ma'aser behemah* is not the only *korban* requiring a sanctifying act. Perhaps, the Gemara suggests, the verse is teaching that purchased animals cannot be used for a *chatas* or an *asham*? After all, *im eino inyan l'bechor* merely establishes that the verse must apply somewhere else — why specifically to *ma'aser behemah*?

The Gemara employs a *dumya deduction*: *ma bincha she'eino ba'al cheit* — the same way the *bechor* of one's sons in *pidyon haben* has nothing to do with sin, so too the obligation being compared to your son must be a *korban* that is not sin-based. This eliminates *chatas* and *asham* from consideration. But free-will offerings — an *olah* or *shelamim* — are also not sin-based, so the Gemara continues to narrow the field. *Ma bincha she'eino bineder unedava* — the same way *pidyon haben* is not an act of free will but an obligation, so too the relevant *korban* must be obligatory, not a *neder* or *nedava*. This eliminates free-will offerings. Even obligatory festival offerings such as *olas re'iya* can be ruled out: *ma bincha she'eino kavua lo zman* — the same way *pidyon haben* has no fixed calendar time, the *korban* in question must similarly have no fixed time.

What remains after all these eliminations? A *korban* that is not sin-based, is obligatory rather than voluntary, and carries no fixed time — this describes *ma'aser behemah*. The three-part *dumya* thus converges on *ma'aser behemah* as the target of the verse's teaching, establishing that the parallel to *bechor banecha* applies specifically to *ma'aser*, and that the exemption for purchased or gifted animals derives from this comparison.

## Fetuses In Utero

### Rabbi Yochanan

With the principle established that purchased animals are exempt from *ma'aser behemah*, the Gemara introduces what seems at first to be a straightforward corollary: *ei ma bincha eino belakuach klal* — the same way your children are never in any category of purchase, so too your animals; any connection to purchase should disqualify them entirely. Yet Rav Assi cites in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *lakach asara ubarim beme'ei iman, kulam nichnasim ledir le'isaser* — if one purchased ten fetuses while they were still in their mothers' wombs, all of them enter the pen to be tithed. Here is a case that has an undeniable element of purchase — the animals were explicitly bought and paid for — and yet Rabbi Yochanan rules that *ma'aser behemah* applies.

### Rava's Resolution

Rava resolves the apparent tension by pointing to the word *ta'aseh* in the verse — *bishat asiyah miat hakatuv* — what is determinative is the moment when the act of *ma'aser* becomes applicable. A fetus in the womb cannot be subjected to *ma'aser behemah*; the earliest one can perform the tithe is from the moment of birth. Therefore, the relevant question is not whether a purchase ever occurred at any point in the animal's history, but whether it was in the status of a purchased animal at the moment *ma'aser behemah* first becomes possible. If the purchase took place entirely in utero — before the animal ever entered the category of *ma'aser behemah* — then at the moment of birth, when *ta'aseh* first applies, the animals are simply yours. The purchase occurred before they were in any sense subject to the obligation, and so the exemption of *lakuach* never attaches to them.

This yields a conceptually striking result: an animal that was purchased — paid for with money, transferred via a transaction — can nonetheless be obligated in *ma'aser behemah*, provided the purchase was completed before birth. The animal was not born into your flock in the sense that you owned the mother, yet at the moment it was born, it was born into your ownership, and that suffices.

### Fetus Defense

The Gemara now revisits the statement of Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan in its original context. The *mishnah* had stated without qualification that a purchased animal is exempt — *patur min hama'aser behemah* — with no limiting conditions. Rabbi Elazar, noting the apparent tension between this unqualified ruling and Rabbi Yochanan's teaching about fetuses, reports that he saw Rabbi Yochanan in a dream, and that this inspired him to come to Rabbi Yochanan's defense. He invokes the same principle: *amar kra ta'aseh — bishat asiyah miat hakatuv* — the exemption for purchased animals is limited to the moment when the act of tithing becomes operative. Since in utero the animal is not yet subject to *ma'aser behemah*, the purchase that occurred at that stage does not disqualify it. Rashi explains that the dream gave Rabbi Elazar the confidence that his resolution in defense of Rabbi Yochanan was correct.

## Mechussar Zman

### Challenge

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish pressed Rabbi Elazar with a *braita* that complicates the *bishat asiyah* principle. The *braita* states that even if one purchased an animal that is *mechussar zman* — less than eight days old, and therefore not yet eligible to be brought as a *korban* — the exemption of *lakuach* nonetheless applies and the animal is excluded from *ma'aser behemah*. This is a serious challenge. If the exemption only applies from the moment the animal enters the category of *ma'aser behemah*, then an animal under eight days old should be in precisely the same position as an in-utero fetus: not yet subject to the *ta'aseh* of *ma'aser behemah*. Yet the *braita* rules that *lakuach* applies even then, which would seem to undercut the *bishat asiyah* principle entirely.

It is worth noting that *ma'aser behemah* does apply to a *ba'al mum*, a blemished animal — one can in fact render all one's animals blemished prior to the tithing and the *ma'aser* still takes effect. But certain categorical exclusions, such as *yotzei dofen* (a C-section birth) or *kilayim* (a hybrid), would not become *kadosh* through *ma'aser*. The question is whether *mechussar zman* is in this stronger category of exclusion, such that tithing cannot take effect at all before day eight.

### Braita Rejection

Rabbi Elazar's response is blunt: *zu einah mishnah* — we do not accept that *braita* as authoritative. And if one insists on treating it as a valid ruling, *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda hi mishum Rabbi Shimon* — it reflects the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, who holds that *mechussar zman nichnas ladir le'isaser* — an animal under eight days may be brought into the pen and counted for *ma'aser behemah*. His reasoning is *de'harei hu ke'bechor*: just as a *bechor* is sanctified from the moment of birth even though it cannot be offered until day eight — *kadosh lifnei zmano ve'karev le'achar zmano* — so too *mechussar zman* can acquire *kedushat ma'aser* even before day eight, with the offering deferred until the time arrives. Since *mechussar zman* is a temporary disqualification that resolves itself with time, Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai maintains that sanctification can take effect even before the period of eligibility. Under this view, *mechussar zman* does enter the category of *ma'aser behemah* and the *braita*'s ruling follows coherently. But it is a minority opinion, and the mainstream view does not accept it — which is why Rabbi Elazar dismisses the *braita* altogether.

## Esnan Zonah

### Esnan Problem

The Gemara presents a puzzle. A *tana* taught before Rav a *braita* asking: *eizu esnan shenichnas ladir le'isaser* — what would be an example of an animal that is *esnan zonah* — payment given to a prostitute, which is forbidden for use as a *korban* — yet still enters the pen to be counted for *ma'aser behemah*? The puzzle arises because *esnan zonah* and *lakuach* appear to be mutually exclusive in practice. For an animal to have the status of *esnan*, it must have been transferred as payment to the prostitute. But once transferred, it is in her hands; and once she, or anyone, sells or purchases it onward, it becomes *lakuach*, which exempts it from *ma'aser behemah* entirely. Conversely, if it reverts to the original owner's flock in some way, it seems difficult to see how the *esnan* status would survive. The *braita* thus poses what amounts to a legal puzzle: can one ever construct a valid scenario in which *esnan* is relevant to *ma'aser behemah*?

If the answer is yes — if the animal does enter the pen and is counted as *ma'aser* — there are fascinating downstream consequences. If another animal happens to be the tenth, there is no problem. But if this particular animal, the former *esnan*, happens to be the tenth to pass under the rod and thereby becomes *ma'aser behemah*, it cannot be brought as a *korban* because it is an *esnan*, yet it has no *mum* so it cannot be redeemed. One would be forced to wait for a blemish to develop naturally and then consume it outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. It would not be excluded from the *ma'aser* count simply by virtue of being *esnan* — unlike, say, *yotzei dofen* or *kilayim*, it is not categorically disqualified from entering the pen.

### Sell & Rebuy

The *braita* offers the following scenario to resolve the puzzle: *kol shenasnu lah ve'chazar ve'lakachah heimena* — any animal given to her as payment that the original owner then purchased back from her. The simple reading of the *braita* is that by buying it back, the animal reverts to the status of having been born into one's flock — since it originally belonged to the owner and was born to his animals — and the *lakuach* status does not permanently cling to it.

The Gemara, however, rejects this reading: *v'apsle b'lakuach*. The fact that the animal was once in the category of *lakuach* — once transferred through a transaction — remains a permanent disqualification for *ma'aser behemah*, even after repurchase. This is a conceptually significant ruling. One might have framed the rule in either of two ways: either the act of purchase itself disqualifies the animal going forward, or the requirement for *ma'aser behemah* is simply that the animal be born into your flock, and a subsequent purchase is irrelevant to that underlying fact. The Gemara here adopts the former framing — purchase at any stage in the animal's history disqualifies it from *ma'aser behemah* — which has striking implications. Were someone to sell his entire flock and buy it back, the flock would, according to this Gemara, remain permanently exempt from *ma'aser behemah* as *lakuach*.

The puzzle is thus thrown back open: how does one ever construct a valid *esnan* case for *ma'aser behemah*?

### Jewish vs Non-Jewish

At this point the Gemara notes that the person who raised the *lakuach* objection to the *braita* overlooked the answer already available: *hadar amar Rav Assi amar Rabbi Yochanan, lakach uvar beme'i behemah kulam nichnasim ladir le'hitaser* — purchasing fetuses in utero obligates them in *ma'aser behemah*. The entire transaction, the giving of the animal as *esnan* and the subsequent purchase, could be done in utero. While in utero, the animal already has the potential to acquire the status of *esnan* — the Gemara accepts that *esnan* can apply to a fetus, though this itself is a *chiddush*. And since the purchase likewise occurred in utero, before the animal entered the category of *ma'aser behemah*, the *lakuach* exemption never attaches.

But the Gemara immediately asks: if all we need is a case of *esnan* in the context of *ma'aser behemah*, why not simply have the prostitute herself perform *ma'aser behemah* on the animal — *veta'asrei ihi*? The answer is: *bezonah kutit* — in the standard case, it is a non-Jewish prostitute. A non-Jew is not obligated in *ma'aser behemah* and would not perform it. Hence the *braita* introduces the scenario of buying it back. But this naturally raises the further question: why not simply posit a Jewish prostitute who would herself be obligated in *ma'aser behemah* and would perform the tithe without any need for repurchase?

### Abaye's Rule

The Gemara answers: *kamashma lan dezona yisraelit lo havei esnan* — the reason we do not posit a Jewish prostitute is because payment given to a Jewish prostitute does not have the halachic status of *esnan* at all. This is the teaching of Abaye: *zona kutit esnanah assur* — the payment given to a non-Jewish prostitute is *esnan* and forbidden as a *korban*, but a Jewish prostitute's payment carries no such prohibition.

### Reversal Logic

This ruling appears to invert intuitions, and the Gemara explains the source. The prohibition of *esnan zonah* is derived from a *gezera shava*: the word *to'evah* appears both in the verse prohibiting *esnan* as a *korban* offering and in the *parasha* of forbidden sexual relations, *arayot*. Just as *arayot* are defined as relationships in which *kiddushin* cannot take effect — a man cannot marry his sister or a married woman — so too *esnan* applies only to a *zona* with whom *kiddushin* is impossible. A non-Jewish woman falls into this category; *kiddushin* cannot take effect with a non-Jewish woman. A Jewish woman, even a prostitute, is someone with whom *kiddushin* can in principle take effect, and therefore payment to her does not constitute *esnan* in the technical sense.

The reverse holds for the prohibition incumbent on a *kohen*. The verse states that a *kohen* may not take a *zonah*, and the rationale given is *lo yechalel zaro* — he must not desacralize his offspring. This consideration is meaningful only when the woman is Jewish, because a child born from a Jewish woman and a *kohen* is Jewish and inherits his father's status; such a child born of an act with a *zonah* would be a *chalal*. By contrast, if a *kohen* has relations with a non-Jewish woman, the child — *v'hai lav zar'ei hu* — is not halachically his seed; the child follows the mother's status. Therefore, the lashes for transgressing *zonah* apply to a *kohen* only when the woman is Jewish, not when she is non-Jewish. The practical result is a striking reversal: *esnan* is prohibited specifically when the woman is non-Jewish, while the *kohen*'s prohibition of *zonah* applies specifically when she is Jewish.

The resolution for the *esnan* puzzle is thus: the transaction was conducted entirely in utero — both the giving of the animal as *esnan* and, in the standard case involving a non-Jewish prostitute, the repurchase by the original owner — so that the animal is never in the category of *lakuach* at the moment *ma'aser behemah* applies. The *esnan* status nevertheless attaches even in utero, obligating it in *ma'aser behemah* while simultaneously disqualifying it from being brought as a *korban*.

## Brothers/Partnership

### Mishnah Rule

The Gemara now turns to a new *mishnah* addressing *achim veshutfin* — brothers and partners — and their obligations with respect to *ma'aser behemah* and the *kalbon*, the small surcharge added when giving the half-*shekel* to the *Beis Hamikdash*. The *kalbon* served either as a rounding adjustment, since the large number of half-*shekel* coins did not always total exact full *shekalim*, or as a payment to the money-changer who converted the coins. If two individuals jointly contributed a single whole *shekel*, they would still each be liable for their own *kalbon* because they gave as individuals.

The *mishnah* identifies what is, in a sense, the one institution in *halacha* that approximates the concept of a corporation: an undivided estate. An estate before division is not simply a partnership of co-owners who each hold a percentage share; rather, the estate itself functions as a single legal entity. This has two parallel but opposing consequences. For *ma'aser behemah*, which applies to individually owned animals, an undivided estate is treated as a single owner and is *chayav*. For the *kalbon*, where the liability arises from being a distinct individual, an undivided estate is treated as one entity and is exempt.

The *mishnah* spells this out: brothers and partners who are *chayav bekhalbon* — those who have the status of individuals, having already divided the estate — are *patur mi-ma'aser behemah* as partners. And those who are *chayavin be-ma'aser behemah* — those whose animals are held in an undivided estate — are *paturin min hakhalbon* as a single entity. The case of *kanu mitfisas habayis* — assets acquired through the estate — is *chayav* in *ma'aser behemah*. If they have divided and re-entered a partnership (*chalku ve'chazru venishtatfu*), they are *chayav be-kalbon* as individuals but *patur mi-ma'aser behemah* as partners.

### Verse Source

The Gemara establishes the scriptural basis for the rule that *shutfut* is exempt from *ma'aser behemah*. A *braita* derives it from the phrase *yiheyeh lecha* — it shall be yours — reading *lecha* in the singular, excluding jointly owned animals: *velo shel shutfut*. One might have thought that even animals owned by an estate — *kanu mitfisas habayis* — are exempt. The word *yiheyeh*, however, is more expansive and is read as including estate-held animals. Just as was done earlier with *lakuach*, the Gemara observes that this exclusion of *shutfut* appears in a verse contextually written about *bechor*: *v'hai bevachor kesiv*. But it does not apply to *bechor* — *im einu inyan levachor, deha issa beshutfut*, since *bechor* applies even to partnership animals, as the verse elsewhere uses the plural *uvachoros bekarkem vetzonchem*, indicating *bechor* applies regardless of how many owners share the animal. Therefore the *shutfut* exclusion cannot be about *bechor*; *teneihu inyan le-ma'aser behemah* — apply it to *ma'aser behemah*.

### Four Scenarios

Rabbi Yirmiyah now maps out all four possible combinations of obligation and exemption across the two *mitzvos*. There are times when one is obligated in both, times when one is exempt from both, times when one gives the *kalbon* but not *ma'aser behemah*, and times when one gives *ma'aser behemah* but not the *kalbon*. The latter two cases correspond to the *mishnah*'s own scenarios. The more analytically interesting cases are the first two, which require a degree of disaggregation.

### Money Divided

*Chayavin bezeh uvezeh* — obligated in both — arises when the brothers have divided the money and other assets of the estate but have not yet divided the animals. With respect to the animals, they remain an undivided estate and are therefore *chayav be-ma'aser behemah*. With respect to the *kalbon*, having already individually acquired portions of the estate's other assets, they are regarded as individuals rather than a single entity, and are *chayav be-kalbon*. The critical insight is that the two obligations are assessed against different subsets of the estate's assets, and the same brothers can be simultaneously an estate for one purpose and individuals for another.

### Animals Divided

The reverse case is *pturin mizeh umizeh* — exempt from both — which arises when the brothers have divided the animals but left the rest of the estate's money and property undivided. With respect to *ma'aser behemah*, the animals have been divided and the brothers are now either individual owners or partners, in either case exempt from *ma'aser behemah*. With respect to the *kalbon*, the bulk of the estate remains undivided; they are treated as a single entity and are exempt from the surcharge.

### Chiddush

The Gemara declares *pshita* — it finds all of this obvious — and asks what the genuine *chiddush* of Rabbi Yirmiyah's teaching is. The answer: *chilku bivehemah velo chilku bechsafim itztricha lei* — the case where they divided the animals but not the money. One might have argued that by dividing the animals, they have demonstrated intent to dissolve the estate — *giluy daata hu dilemiflag kaimei* — and this manifest intention to divide should render them individuals across all matters, including *shekalim*, thereby obligating them in the *kalbon*. The *chiddush* is that this does not follow. Even where division of the animals has occurred, the obligation in *kalbon* is assessed by looking at the rest of the estate's assets, and if those remain undivided, the brothers are still treated as a single entity for purposes of the *shekel* surcharge. One can be exempt from *ma'aser behemah* — because the animals are now divided — while simultaneously remaining exempt from the *kalbon* — because the estate is otherwise intact. Division does not cascade across categories; each obligation is evaluated according to its own relevant assets.

## Division Classification

### Rav Anani

Rav Anani introduces an important qualification to all of the above analysis. He holds that the rules discussed — treating divided brothers as *shutfim* exempt from *ma'aser behemah* — apply specifically when the division was non-uniform: *ela shechilku gedayim keneged teyashim uteyashim keneged gedayim* — where one brother took goats and the other took a different type of animal, say adults in exchange for young. Even if the two sets were equivalent in value, the fact that they exchanged different categories of asset means each brother effectively bought out the other's share. Such a division has the character of a purchase, not of a straight inheritance.

### Equal Division

However, Rav Anani draws a distinction for the case of *aval chilku gedayim keneged gedayim uteyashim keneged uteyashim* — where the division was perfectly homogeneous: each brother took his portion in kind, species for species, the same type of animal in equal shares. In this case, Rav Anani rules: *omer zehu chelko hamagio misha'ah rishonah lekach* — one says that this is the portion that was coming to him from the very beginning. There was no exchange, no buyout; each brother simply took what was always going to be his. The division is therefore classified as inheritance rather than purchase. The practical consequences are significant in both directions. As Rashi notes, if they were to subsequently pool the animals again, they could reconstitute the estate, since there was never any transaction between them. More immediately, as individuals holding their inherited portions, they are *chayav* in *ma'aser behemah* right now — their animals are not *lakuach*.

### Rav Nachman

Rav Nachman disagrees. Even a perfectly equal division — *afilu chilku gedayim keneged gedayim uteyashim keneged uteyashim* — is not characterized as *zehu chelko hamagio misha'ah rishonah lekach*. Every division of an estate, no matter how symmetric, constitutes each brother buying out the other's claim. The result is that after any division, the animals in each brother's portion are *lakuach* — purchased — and therefore exempt from *ma'aser behemah* immediately. And should they reconstitute their holdings, they would be *shutfim*, partners, not an estate.

### Rabbi Elazar

Rabbi Elazar offers a position structurally parallel to Rav Anani's, but drawing the line differently. Rather than distinguishing between types of animals exchanged, he focuses on numbers: *lo shanu ela shechilku tishah keneged asarah va'asarah keneged tishah* — the rule that division is treated as purchase applies when one brother took nine animals and the other took ten of the same type, compensating with a different animal or value adjustment. When the numerical quantities differ even if the species match, there is an element of exchange, and the division has the character of purchase. But *aval chilku tishah keneged tishah asarah keneged asarah* — if each brother took exactly the same number of each type of animal — then *omer zehu chelko hamagio misha'ah rishonah lekach*, and each received his direct inheritance. The *nafka mina* is identical to Rav Anani's: such brothers are *chayav* in *ma'aser behemah* right now, since their animals are inheritance rather than *lakuach*.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan takes the most sweeping position: *afilu chilku tishah keneged tishah va'asarah keneged asarah* — even a perfectly equal division in exact numbers and identical species — *ein omrin zeh chelko hamagio, sheharei zeh kelokeach*. We do not say that it was a straight inheritance; every division of brothers, however precisely equitable, is essentially buying the other out. The result is that each brother's portion is *lakuach* immediately upon division, and they are exempt from *ma'aser behemah* now. Should they pool the assets, they would be *shutfim*. Rashi notes, as the shiur concludes, that the dispute between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yochanan is structurally identical to the dispute between Rav Anani and Rav Nachman, with the *nafka mina* being in each case the same: when brothers divide an estate, is what they receive classified as *yerusha* — direct inheritance obligating them in *ma'aser behemah* — or as *lakuach* — effectively purchased, exempting them?
