# Avodah Zarah 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tikroves: Act Model

### Intro & Markulis

The daf begins at the very bottom of 50b and opens in the middle of a rich discussion that operates on two simultaneous levels: what type of act renders one liable (*chayav*) for *avodah zarah* — a question that belongs primarily to the tractate of Sanhedrin — and, relatedly, what type of act causes an object to become *tikroves avodah zarah*, forbidden in benefit (*hana'ah*). These two questions are connected but distinct, and the daf weaves between them throughout.

The immediate context is the earlier discussion about using the stones of *Markulis* to pave streets. If those stones constituted *tikroves avodah zarah*, no *bitul* would be possible and the stones could never be used. The *Gemara* had resolved this by explaining that the thrown stones are not *tikroves*; they may themselves be objects of worship (*davar hane'evad*) and thus subject to *bitul*, but they are not *tikroves*. The question then is: why not? Throwing is, after all, precisely the manner in which one serves *Markulis*. The *Gemara*'s answer, which is the organizing principle of this entire section, is that for something to constitute *tikroves* and be forbidden in *hana'ah*, the act must conform to the model of the *Beis HaMikdash* — what the *Gemara* calls *ke'ein panim*, like the service performed inside.

Rashi and Tosafot part ways on where to locate this criterion. Rashi focuses on the object: perhaps a stone is not the kind of thing that would be brought as an offering — not edible, not the sort of item one would place on the altar. Tosafot, drawing on a *Gemara* to be seen later on this very *daf*, focuses on the act: even granting that one throws objects in the context of Temple service, what matters is whether the act involves *mishtaberes* — that is, whether the material shatters or spatters upon impact. Simply hurling a whole object that lands intact does not resemble the act of *zrikat hadam*, the sprinkling of blood on the altar. This distinction between breaking and throwing will drive the analysis for the bulk of this section.

The relevance of these categories extends well beyond the *Gemara*'s world. A similar cluster of questions arose in connection with the controversy over Indian hair used for wigs — whether hair shaved in a religious ritual before a statue would constitute *tikroves* or not. The facts of that case were disputed, but the underlying legal framework is precisely what is being analyzed here: even if an act is performed in the context of *avodah zarah*, it does not automatically render the item *tikroves assur behana'ah*.

### Rav Yehuda's Chiddush

The *Gemara* presents a teaching of *Rav Yehuda amar Rav* (approximately eight lines from the bottom of 50b): if an *avodah zarah* is worshipped by means of a stick — say, some kind of ceremonial baton twirling — then the stick becomes the operative object that can be substituted into the framework of the four classic *avodot* of Temple service. The *chiddush* is that even though swinging a baton is not the normal form of *shechita*, if you break the stick before this idol (*shivar makel lefaneha*), you are *chayav* — because breaking a stick is analogous to slaughtering an animal. By contrast, if you merely throw the stick (*zarak makel lefaneha*), you are exempt (*patur*).

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye raises a sharp challenge to this distinction. If breaking the stick is deemed analogous to *shechita* — because we allow the object of the *avodah* (the stick) to substitute for the animal — why should throwing the stick not be analogous to *zrikat hadam*, the sprinkling of blood? Just as we permitted the substitution of the stick for the animal in one case, we should permit the substitution in the other, with throwing standing in for sprinkling.

### Rava's Criteria

Rava responds: the *zrika* that constitutes a Temple service is not mere throwing. *Zrika* in the *Beis HaMikdash* involves *mishtaberes* — the blood spatters, it disperses against the altar. When you throw a whole object and it simply lands undisturbed, that is not sufficiently similar to *zrikat hadam* to count. Breaking a stick, by contrast, at least involves the destruction of the object, which bears some resemblance to *shechita* — you sever something, something flows or breaks apart. The lines of comparison are not perfectly clean (after all, *shechita* is not merely breaking something; it requires the outflow of blood that enables subsequent acts of *avodah*), but the fundamental distinction stands: shattering is close enough; intact throwing is not.

### Dung & Urine Case

The *Gemara* raises a challenge (*eisivei*) from a case involving *tzoa* — excrement — and urine. If the mode of worship of a given *avodah zarah* involves feeding dung to the idol (Rashi understands this as feeding; Tosafot understands it as smearing), or pouring a bedpan of urine (*avit shel mei raglayim*) before it, one is *chayav*. Since these are presumably not the normal forms of service for this idol — otherwise there would be no novelty — the question is why.

The *Gemara* resolves the urine case straightforwardly: *bishlama avit shel mei raglayim ika zrika mishtaberes* — pouring urine splatters, it behaves like blood thrown against the altar, and so that case fits Rava's criterion. The dung case is trickier: how does solid excrement splatter? The *Gemara* answers: we are speaking of liquid excrement (*tzoa lacha*), which does in fact splash. A vivid image, but a clean legal answer.

### Grasshopper Dispute

The *Gemara* now asks whether the distinction between Rava's position and some alternative can be tied to a dispute among *Tannaim*. The case is *shachat lah chagav* — one slaughtered a grasshopper before this idol, cutting its throat with a small knife. Rabbi Yehuda holds one liable; the *Chachamim* exempt.

One might read this dispute as follows: Rabbi Yehuda holds that it is sufficient that the act *resemble* slaughter (*ke'ein zvicha*), while the *Chachamim* demand that it be genuinely *ke'ein panim* — truly like Temple service. The *Gemara* however rejects this framing. In fact, everyone agrees one cannot simply approximate *zvicha* with an arbitrary object; it must be genuinely *ke'ein panim*. The dispute is instead about the parameters of *ke'ein panim* itself: a grasshopper, unlike a stick, actually has a neck — or at least something resembling one. (Some species of locusts and grasshoppers have an elongated prothorax that gives the appearance of a neck.) So the question is whether the neck-like anatomy of the grasshopper brings it within the category of the animals slaughtered in the Temple. That is a debate about how broadly or narrowly to define the permissible objects, not about whether the act needs to look like Temple service.

### Tosafot's Proviso

Tosafot insists — throughout all of these cases — on a background assumption that the *Gemara* itself does not explicitly revisit: all of these substituted objects (the stick, the excrement, the urine, the grasshopper) are only legally relevant because they were already used in *some* capacity in the service of that particular idol. Only because the object participates in the *derech avodah* of that idol can it step into the role otherwise played by the animal. Without that prior connection to the idol's service, the substitution would not be permissible at all.

Rashi's simpler reading — that the *Gemara* is purely debating the scope of *ke'ein panim* without reference to whether the object was previously used — leads Tosafot to object. The grasshopper case illustrates why Tosafot presses this point: the grasshopper is neither a stick nor a cow, and without the assumption that it was already involved in the *avodah*, one might think it has no claim to substitutability at all. Tosafot maintains that this underlying assumption is always operative, even when the *Gemara* does not restate it.

## Tikroves vs. Chiyuv

### Rav Nachman's Rule

The *Gemara* now transitions explicitly to the question of *tikroves* — not whether one is *chayav* for *avodah zarah*, but whether the object becomes forbidden in *hana'ah*. Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha citing Rav teaches: if an *avodah zarah* is served by means of a stick, and one broke the stick before it (*shivar makel lefaneha*), one is *chayav* and the stick is forbidden (*ne'eseris*). If one threw the stick (*zarak makel lefaneha*), one is *chayav* — but the stick is not forbidden.

The first part of this teaching mirrors what we saw with Rava earlier: breaking is like *shechita* and constitutes a valid act. But the second part is striking. If throwing the stick makes one *chayav* — which Rav Nachman appears to affirm — then we are dealing with a case where an act is fully valid as *avodah* and yet does not render the object *tikroves*. The reason is clear from what we established: for *tikroves*, the act must be *ke'ein tikroves* in the *Beis HaMikdash* sense — it must *mishtaber*, it must splatter or break. A whole intact object that was simply thrown does not meet that threshold.

This is precisely what the *Gemara* had established earlier regarding *Markulis*: throwing a stone is the classic *derech avodah* of *Markulis*, one is *chayav* for it, and yet the stone is not *tikroves*. The parallel is exact. For the thrown stick: *chayav* for the act of *avodah*, but not *tikroves* because the act does not resemble an offering in the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Rashi's Brackets

Rashi takes a different approach to the *chiyuv* question. He says: let us bracket the entire debate about what constitutes sufficient *avodah* to be *chayav*, and simply assume that this is a case of *derech avodatah bekach* — the classic mode of service for this idol. Under that assumption, one is *chayav* regardless of whether one breaks or throws. The entire focus of Rav Nachman's teaching, on Rashi's reading, is not about the conditions for liability but exclusively about what creates *tikroves*. Something can be the paradigmatic act of service for an idol, one can be fully *chayav* for performing it, and still the object involved does not become *tikroves* — because *tikroves* requires not merely valid *avodah* but an act that mirrors the Temple offering.

This cuts interestingly against an intuitive expectation. One might have thought that if anything, *tikroves* would simply track *chiyuv* — if you're *chayav*, surely the object is forbidden. But the *Gemara* is insisting on a clean separation: *chiyuv* is governed by *ke'ein panim* in the sense of performing one of the classic *avodot* in a genuine manner; *tikroves* is governed by a further criterion that the act resembles the sacrificial act in the *Beis HaMikdash* — specifically, that it involves the breaking or splattering that characterizes *zrikat hadam* or *shechita*.

### Markulis Resolution

With this framework in place, the *Gemara* revisits the *Markulis* stones. The question is pressing: if throwing a stick is not *tikroves*, why are the stones of *Markulis* forbidden? The *Gemara* had already answered this on the previous *amud*, and now it restates the answer: *na'aseh kemigdal avodas kochavim* — when you throw a stone and it joins the pile, it becomes part of the pile itself, which is a *davar hane'evad*, an object of worship. The stone is not forbidden as *tikroves* but as *avodah zarah* itself. And since it is *avodah zarah* rather than *tikroves*, it is subject to *bitul* — hence the ruling in our *Mishnah* that if a non-Jew dismantled the pile and paved a street with the stones, one may walk on them. *Bitul* can operate on *avodah zarah* proper; it cannot operate on *tikroves*.

### Chain Analysis

But this resolution raises a further difficulty. If the stones only become *avodah zarah* once they are *worshipped*, and if the act of throwing is not valid *tikroves*, then at the moment of throwing, what exactly is the stone? It is not yet *avodah zarah* (it has not been worshipped), and it is not *tikroves* (the act did not *mishtaber*). According to the view that a non-Jew's idol becomes forbidden only upon worship (*ad she'te'aveid*), this seems to leave the stone in a permitted state.

The *Gemara*'s answer is elegant: *kol achas ve'achas na'aseis avodas kochavim ve'tikroves la'chaverta* — each stone becomes *avodah zarah* and *tikroves* for the next one. When the first person throws stone number one, it joins the pile. When the second person arrives and throws stone number two, that act of throwing constitutes *avodah* directed at all the stones already present, including stone number one. At the moment stone number two is thrown, stone number one becomes a *davar hane'evad* — it has now been worshipped. Stone number two will become *avodah zarah* when stone number three is thrown, and so on through the chain.

Under this analysis, every stone in the pile is forbidden — except, technically, the last one. The last stone has not yet been worshipped by a subsequent act, and it does not constitute *tikroves* (since it was thrown without *mishtaberes*). The *Gemara* acknowledges this openly: *ei yadas lah zil shakol* — if you can identify the last stone, you are entitled to take it. Practically speaking, of course, this is impossible to determine.

### Rav Ashi's View

Rav Ashi offers an alternative that eliminates even this loophole: *kol achas ve'achas na'aseis tikroves le'atzmah ve'tikroves la'chaverta* — each stone becomes *tikroves* to itself as well as to the next stone. The reasoning is that the very act of throwing the stone toward the pile, while it is in mid-air on its way to joining the *avodah zarah*, constitutes an act of worshipping that stone itself. Since the stone is destined to become part of the idol, the trajectory of throwing is itself an act of *avodah* directed at the stone. Under this view, even the last stone is forbidden. The reasoning is admittedly difficult to follow at full depth, but the practical outcome is clear: no stone is ever available.

## Object-Based Model

### Mishnah Rules

The *Gemara* now introduces the next *Mishnah*, which presents what appears to be a fundamentally different model of *tikroves*. The *Mishnah* states: if one found at the head of a *Markulis* garments (*ksus*), money (*ma'os*), or vessels (*keilim*) — classic types of offerings in the Greco-Roman world — those are permitted (*harei elu mutarim*). They are not considered *tikroves*. But if one found clusters of grapes (*parchilei anavim*), crowns of grain stalks (*ataros shel shibolim*), wine, oil, flour, or anything whose like is offered on the altar (*karev legabei ha-mizbe'ach*) — those are forbidden (*asur*).

The criterion the *Mishnah* employs is entirely object-based: does this type of item get brought on the altar? There is no reference to the act, no reference to *mishtaberes*, no reference to whether the item was broken or splattered. The garments and money are permitted not because of how they were placed there, but because garments and money are not the kind of thing one offers on the *mizbe'ach*. The grapes and grain are forbidden because these things *are* the kind of thing offered on the *mizbe'ach* — even though, as placed before the idol, they were presumably not processed, not thrown, not broken.

This stands in striking contrast to the act-based model developed on the previous *amud*, and the *Gemara* is forced to work hard to reconcile the two.

### Grapes & Wheat

The *Gemara* notes that for the liquid items — wine, oil, flour — one can still find a way to apply Rava's criterion: *ika ke'ein panim ve'ika ke'ein zrika mishtaberes*. If actually poured, these liquids would splatter, and so the act of offering them involves something resembling *zrika mishtaberes*, in addition to these being items normally brought on the altar. The double criterion is met.

But the grapes and grain stalks present a genuine problem: *lo ke'ein panim ika ve'lo ke'ein zrika mishtaberes ika*. They are not offered in the *Beis HaMikdash* in this form, and they certainly do not splatter. Yet the *Mishnah* forbids them because they are the type of thing that *would* be offered on the altar. The act-based model simply does not explain these cases.

### Rava's Intent

Rava (citing Ulla) proposes a solution: the *Mishnah*'s case is one where the grapes were cut from the vine specifically for the purpose of being placed before the idol (*shebatzran mitchila lekach*). The act of cutting them off the vine — severing them from their source — constitutes a form of *mishtaberes*. It is, in its own way, analogous to *shechita*: you sever something from its living connection, and that act of separation, done with intention for the idol, constitutes a *tikroves* act.

This is admittedly a stretch. The grapes are not being cut in front of the idol; the cutter may be in his vineyard, thinking about what he will do with them tomorrow. It lacks the immediacy of a *ma'aseh avodah* performed before the idol. The *Gemara* seems aware of this difficulty — the parallel to the discussion about *shem matzas mitzvah* (where intention at the moment of an act confers its legal character) is implicit here. One might compare it to cutting something off a vine while intending it for sacred purpose. But the *Gemara* is clearly reaching, and the *Mishnah*'s own language — *karev legabei ha-mizbe'ach* — speaks of the type of object, not the manner of cutting.

### Blemished Animals

The *Gemara* at this point introduces a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan: how do we know that one who slaughters a blemished animal (*ba'alas mum*) for *avodah zarah* is exempt (*patur*)? The source is the verse *zove'ach l'elohim yacharam bilti la-Hashem levado* — the Torah forbids only what is *ke'ein panim*, mirroring the Temple service. And in the Temple, only unblemished animals are valid. Therefore, slaughtering a blemished animal — even though it is a genuine act of *shechita* — does not meet the *ke'ein panim* threshold for liability.

Rava raises a question about the precise scope of this ruling. If we are speaking of a *blemish of the eye* (*dukin sheba'ayin*) — a type of blemish that disqualifies in the Temple but is still valid on a *bama* (an altar used when altars were permitted) and even for *Bnei Noach* — then why would one be exempt? Even by the most minimal standard, this animal is relevant to service of God. The *Gemara* resolves that the case must be *mechusar evar* — an animal lacking a limb — which even for *Bnei Noach*, per Rabbi Eliezer, is invalid.

Rabbi Eliezer's position is grounded in the verse *umikol ha-chai mikol basar shnayim mikol* (from all living things, two of each), which is connected to the *korban* brought by Noach. The word *ha-chai* (the living) is read as requiring that all the animal's limbs be intact — *chaiyin rashei eivarim shelah*. The *Gemara* challenges this: surely *ha-chai* is needed to exclude a *treifah* (a fatally injured animal), not *mechusar evar*. But the *Gemara* replies: we learn the exclusion of *treifah* from *itcha* — "with you" — which implies animals that resemble you (and since Noach was not a *treifah*, the animals cannot be *treifot*). The subsequent deduction is confirmed by the verse *tamim* (*Noach was wholesome*), which, read alongside *tzaddik* (*righteous*), establishes physical perfection. Since *itcha* covers *treifah*, *ha-chai* is freed up to exclude *mechusar evar*, and *lehachayot zera* (to preserve seed) establishes that the entire purpose of bringing animals was for them to repopulate the earth — *mechusar evar* is excluded on multiple grounds.

The *baal mum* ruling then raises a pointed question: if one is exempt for slaughtering a *baal mum* to *avodah zarah*, but liable for slaughtering a grasshopper (*chagav*), doesn't that suggest that the grasshopper's case was specifically predicated on the grasshopper having been used in some prior *avodah* context — precisely as Tosafot insisted? One might alternatively argue that *mum* is a qualitative disqualification in a way that species-narrowness is not: perhaps a deer or a chicken can stand in for a Temple animal because *ke'ein panim* covers the broad class of animals, whereas *mum* is a positive legal category of invalidity that takes the animal entirely outside the operative framework. This remains an open question worth considering, but Tosafot's position draws support from the juxtaposition.

## Ke'ein Panim Standard

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer asks: from where do we derive that slaughtering an animal for *Markulis* — even though slaughter is not the *derech avodah* of *Markulis* — renders one *chayav*? The source is the verse *velo yizbechu od et zivcheihem la-se'irim asher hem zovchim al pnei ha-sadeh* (Leviticus 17:7). Since the general prohibition of worshipping idols in their characteristic manner is derived from *eich ya'avdu ha-goyim ha-eileh et eloheihem va'e'eseh ken gam ani* (Deuteronomy 12:30) — how do those nations serve their gods, and I will do the same — we do not need the verse about *se'irim* for that purpose. Therefore it must be teaching something new: *te-neihu inyan lishe-lo ke-darkah* — apply it to non-characteristic worship. Even if slaughter is not how *Markulis* is served, performing a classic *avodah* like *shechita* renders one *chayav*.

### Pasuk Context

The *Gemara* immediately objects: that verse is needed for something else — specifically, to establish the *halachot* of *shchutei chutz* (slaughter of consecrated animals outside the Temple). The *Gemara* quotes the *baraita*: the beginning of that section (Leviticus 17) discusses animals sanctified during a period when the *Beis HaMikdash* already stood and *bamot* (private altars) were forbidden — *hikdisham be-sha'as issur bamot ve-hikrivum be-sha'as issur bamot*. If one slaughtered such an animal outside the Temple, one receives *karet* (spiritual excision). The verses establish: the prohibition (*lo sa'asei*) from *pen ta'aleh olotecha b'chol makom*, and the punishment of *karet* from *venichras ha'ish hahu*.

The second section of that passage then shifts to animals sanctified when *bamot* were *permitted* but which are now being offered when *bamot* are forbidden — *hikdisham be-sha'as heter bamot ve-hikrivum be-sha'as issur bamot*. The original sanctification was legitimate, but now circumstances have changed. The prohibition for this class is derived from *velo yizbechu od et zivcheihem*, and there is no *karet* — only a transgression of a *lo ta'asei* — as stated by *chukat olam tihyeh zot lahem: zot lahem ve-lo acheret lahem* — just this *asei* and *lo ta'asei*, nothing more.

The verse *velo yizbechu od et zivcheihem* is thus fully occupied with establishing the *lav* for *kodshim* sanctified when *bamot* were permitted. How then can Rabbi Eliezer use it to source the liability for non-characteristic *avodah zarah* service?

### Velo Od

Rava resolves this through a close reading of the verse: *krei beih velo yizbechu u'krei beih velo od* — the verse contains two separable prohibitions. *Velo yizbechu* addresses the prohibition of *shchutei chutz* for the second category of animals (sanctified during *heter bamot*). *Velo od* is a separate word that carries an independent prohibition: it forbids performing a classic *avodah* act, like *shechita*, for *avodah zarah* even when that is not the idol's characteristic mode of service. This *velo od* is the source for Rabbi Eliezer's ruling.

This is the *pasuk* underlying the entire discussion that has occupied this *daf*: the liability for performing a classic *ke'ein panim* act before an *avodah zarah* even when that is not the idol's *derech avodah*. And from that same principle, the *Gemara* has been deriving the parameters of *tikroves* — what type of act, performed before an idol in a manner that mirrors Temple service, causes the object to become permanently forbidden.

## Beauty & Sanctum

### Noy Definition

Having analyzed the act-based model, the *Gemara* now turns to what might be called the *noy*-based model. The *Gemara* cites a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan (as reported by Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef): two verses appear to be in tension. One verse (Deuteronomy 7:25) reads *ve-psileihem ve-khisuyeihem etz va-even kesef ve-zahav asher imahem* — it speaks of things that are *with* the idols, and lists a broad range of materials including wood, stone, silver, and gold. A second verse (*lo tachmod kesef ve-zahav aleihem*) mentions only silver and gold *upon* the idols, which are clearly items of *noy* — adornment and beauty.

How are these reconciled? *Imahem dumya de-aleihem*: things that are *with* the idols are governed by the same criterion as things that are *upon* them. Just as things placed *upon* the idol must be items of *noy* to be forbidden, so too things placed *with* the idol (the broader category of *imahem*, which might refer to *tikroves*) must be things of *noy* to be forbidden. *Ma aleihem davar shel noy assur she-eino shel noy mutar — af imahem davar shel noy assur she-eino shel noy mutar*.

This introduces, at minimum, the framework of three categories of *avodah zarah*-related prohibitions: *noy* (adornment of the idol), *tikroves* (objects offered to the idol), and *tashmish* (instrumental objects used in the service). All three render an object forbidden in *hana'ah*; only *tikroves* is immune to *bitul*. The *noy*-criterion now being discussed appears to apply, somewhat surprisingly, across at least the *noy* and *imahem* categories.

### Non-Beautiful Items

If the criterion is *noy*, the *Mishnah*'s ruling that money, garments, and vessels are permitted requires explanation — why aren't those beautiful? The *Gemara* provides circumstantial explanations for each. Money (*ma'os*) is permitted because the *Gemara* (citing the school of Rabbi Yannai) envisions the coins tied inside a pouch hung around the idol's neck — not an aesthetically pleasing arrangement. Garments (*ksus*) would ordinarily count as *noy*, but the *Mishnah*'s case involves garments folded up and placed on the idol's head (*ksut mekupelet u-munachat lo al rosho*) — like laundry left sitting on a shelf rather than beautifully draped. Vessels (*keilim*) would similarly seem to be *noy*, but Rav Papa clarifies that the case involves a broken bedpan overturned on the idol's head (*tzachifa mashchita a-reishei*) — hardly ornamental.

This exegetical strategy is, to put it plainly, strange. The *Mishnah* itself does not describe these circumstances; it states the rule as *matzah berosho ksus u-ma'os* — simply, he found garments and money at its head — and rules them permitted on the basis that they are not the type of thing *karev legabei ha-mizbe'ach*. The *Gemara* is importing the *noy* framework and then retrofitting all the *Mishnah*'s cases into it by imagining circumstances that would eliminate the *noy* quality. One is left wondering why the *Gemara* does not simply read the *Mishnah* on its own terms — that *tikroves* is defined by *karev legabei ha-mizbe'ach* — and leave it at that.

One possible resolution: the *Gemara* on the current *amud* (50b) may be reading this *Mishnah* through a different lens than its counterpart on 50a. Perhaps the context here is specifically *Markulis*, whose mode of worship is throwing stones. None of these items — the garments, the money, the vessels, the grapes — were brought as *tikroves* (in the sense of an actual act of worship involving *mishtaberes*) to *Markulis*. They were placed there purely for aesthetic purposes. If so, the entire discussion is about *noy avodah zarah*, not *tikroves avodah zarah*, and the *noy* framework makes perfect sense. The *Mishnah*'s phrase *karev legabei ha-mizbe'ach* then functions as a criterion for whether something serves a beautifying purpose — things that would be brought to an altar are beautiful and purposeful as offerings; things that would not be brought to an altar are neither. But this reading sits uncomfortably with the *Mishnah*'s plain language and with the earlier analysis on 50a.

### Inner Sanctum

Rav Assi bar Chiya introduces a third criterion, distinct from both *mishtaberes* and *noy*: *kol she-bifnim min ha-kikalin afilu mayim u-melach assur* — anything that is within the curtains (*kikalim*) of the idol's sanctuary is forbidden, even water and salt. These are items that would be neither *noy* nor anything resembling a Temple offering. Yet their location inside the inner sanctum renders them *tikroves*.

Outside the curtains, however, the *noy* criterion applies: *chutz la-kikalin davar shel noy assur she-eino shel noy mutar*. This produces a geography of *tikroves*: inside the inner sanctum, there are no criteria — everything is forbidden; outside, only items of *noy* are forbidden.

This is a radically different framework from anything developed on the previous *amud*, where the criteria were either the act-based *mishtaberes* model or the object-based *karev legabei ha-mizbe'ach* model. How these models fit together remains deeply unclear, and the *Gemara* does not synthesize them.

### No Curtains

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina adds a critical qualification: *ein kikalin lo le-pe'or ve-lo le-Markulis* — neither *Pe'or* nor *Markulis* had curtains or inner sanctums. These were outdoor, public forms of worship, conducted by the roadside. The question is how to read this ruling: does the absence of *kikalim* mean that items near *Pe'or* or *Markulis* are treated as though they are *outside* the curtains (requiring *noy* to be forbidden), or as though they are *inside* (with no criteria at all)?

The *Gemara* rejects the lenient reading: surely *Pe'or* is worshipped by defecation before it — *mefarin kamai* — and if that is so, water and salt before it would obviously be a problem without any need for *noy*. The ruling must therefore go in the stringent direction: *afilu chutz ke-fnim dami ve-assur* — since there are no curtains, everything outside is treated as though it is inside the inner sanctum, and all items in the vicinity are forbidden without any *noy* criterion.

## Garden & Bath

### Ancillary Structures

The *Gemara* now turns to a different category of objects: not things placed on or near the idol, but structures that beautify the surrounding area — a garden (*ginnah*) or a bathhouse (*merchatz*) that an *avodah zarah* maintained. These are not *tikroves*, not *tashmish*, and arguably not *noy* either, since they adorn the premises rather than the idol itself. The *Mishnah* rules: *nehenin me-hen shelo be-tovah* — one may derive benefit from them, but not with a sense of gratitude (*tovah*).

### Gratitude Issue

The prohibition is not on using the garden or bathhouse — that benefit is permitted. The prohibition is on allowing that use to generate a feeling of gratitude directed toward the institution of *avodah zarah*. Rashi offers two readings of *shelo be-tovah*. One reading: do not pay the fee, so as not to contribute financially to the idol's treasury. The second reading (which Tosafot identifies as Rashi's earlier position, and which Tosafot regards as the correct understanding of the phrase) is the opposite: *shelo be-tovah* means without generating emotional gratitude. On this reading, paying a fee actually helps — it transforms the interaction into a business transaction, less likely to produce feelings of indebtedness and positive connection to the institution. The concern is that benefiting freely creates a sense of *tovah* — a warm feeling of obligation and appreciation — that connects one emotionally to the *avodah zarah*.

### Joint Ownership

The *Mishnah* then addresses a case where the bathhouse was jointly owned by the *avodah zarah* and a private individual (*haya shelo ve-shel achereim*). In this case, the *Mishnah* rules: *neheneh me-hen bein be-tovah bein shelo be-tovah* — one may use it with or without a sense of gratitude. The logic is that any gratitude felt can be directed toward the non-idolatrous co-owner.

## Idol Status

### Mishnah Rules

The *Mishnah* then states the foundational rule about the idol itself — a rule that has been invoked repeatedly throughout this tractate but which now appears explicitly: an idol belonging to a non-Jew (*shel oved kochavim*) is forbidden from the moment it is created (*asur miyad*). An idol made by a Jew is not forbidden until it is actually worshipped (*ad she-te'aveid*). This ruling will be the subject of analysis in the next *Gemara*, where a view opposing the *Mishnah*'s distinction will be presented. The broader question — at what point in the creation or use of an object does its forbidden status attach — will occupy the discussion going forward.

## Garden & Bath (II)

### Abaye's Readings

Returning to the bathhouse ruling, the *Gemara* notes that Abaye's comment can be taught in two ways, yielding opposite outcomes. In the first reading (*man d'masni a-reisha*), Abaye's statement applies to the opening clause — the case where the institution is fully owned by the *avodah zarah*. There, *be-tovah* means gratitude toward the priests (*komarim*), while *shelo be-tovah* means without such gratitude. Critically, this reading permits gratitude toward the workers (*ovdeiha*) — the ordinary employees who run the bath. Under this lenient reading, if in the *reisha* one need only avoid gratitude to the priests (while gratitude to workers is fine), then in the *seifa* — the jointly owned case — one would certainly be permitted, and could even feel gratitude toward the institution, since there is a non-idolatrous co-owner to whom that gratitude can attach.

In the second reading (*man d'masni a-seifa*), Abaye's comment applies to the jointly owned case: even there, *be-tovah* requires directing gratitude specifically toward the non-idolatrous partner, and *shelo be-tovah* means avoiding gratitude toward the priests. This is the stricter position: even joint ownership does not eliminate the concern; one must consciously redirect one's sense of gratitude toward the legitimate co-owner. Under this reading, the *reisha* — where there is no non-idolatrous co-owner — would *a fortiori* be subject to this stringency, and gratitude to the workers alone would be the only permissible form of positive engagement.

The difference between the two readings is more than technical. The lenient reading treats the prohibition as institutional — directed at the *komarim* as representative of the religious institution — and therefore ordinary service workers are entirely outside its scope. The stricter reading makes the prohibition more psychologically demanding: even in a case of joint ownership, one must actively manage one's sense of gratitude, ensuring that it flows only toward the permissible party and not toward the *avodah zarah* dimension of the institution.

The discussion of the idol itself — when precisely its forbidden status attaches, and what distinguishes idols made by Jews from those made by non-Jews — will be taken up in the next *shiur*.
