# Ketubot 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Third-Party Solutions

### Vow & Interim Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: הַמּוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְאֵין לוֹ מַה יֹּאכַל, הוֹלֵךְ אֵצֶל חֶנְוָנִי הָרָגִיל אֶצְלוֹ, וְאוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי מוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מִמֶּנִּי, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה לוֹ״. הוּא נוֹתֵן לוֹ, וּבָא וְנוֹטֵל מִזֶּה. בֵּיתוֹ לִבְנוֹת, וּגְדֵירוֹ לִגְדּוֹר, וְשָׂדֵהוּ לִקְצוֹר — הוֹלֵךְ אֵצֶל פּוֹעֲלִין הָרְגִילִין אֶצְלוֹ, וְאוֹמֵר לָהֶן: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי מוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מִמֶּנִּי וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה לוֹ״. הֵן עוֹשִׂין עִמּוֹ, וּבָאִין וְנוֹטְלִים שְׂכָרָן מִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** § Since it mentioned the above case, the Gemara returns to discuss **the** matter **itself:** In the case of **one prohibited by a vow from deriving benefit from another** because of a vow the other took, **and he does not have** anything **to eat,** the one who took the vow **may go to a storekeeper with whom he is familiar and say to him: So-and-so is prohibited by a vow from deriving benefit from me, and I do not know what I can do for him.** The storekeeper **gives** food **to him, and** later **comes and takes** payment **from this** one who approached him. Similarly, if the subject of the vow needed someone **to build his house, or to erect his fence, or to reap his field,** and the one who took the vow wants to help him, he should **go to workers with whom he is familiar and say to them: So-and-so is prohibited by a vow from deriving benefit from me, and I do not know what I can do for him. They** subsequently **perform** work **for** the subject of the vow, **and they come and take their wages from this** person who spoke to them.

The *daf* begins by returning to a topic introduced earlier in the sugya: a man who has made a vow forbidding his wife from deriving benefit from him, and the practical question of how she is to be supported in the interim before the court demands a divorce. Since he is the one who made the vow, the halakha requires that he pay the *ketubah* upon divorce. But the more immediate question is: how does he provide for her in the meantime without violating his own vow? The Gemara's solution involves enlisting a third party who provides for the wife in a way that does not constitute a direct, explicit instruction from the husband — so that the support is not legally attributable to him.

### Nedarim Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ מְהַלְּכִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וְאֵין עִמּוֹ מַה יֹּאכַל — נוֹתֵן לְאַחֵר לְשׁוּם מַתָּנָה, וְהַלָּה נוֹטֵל וְאוֹכֵל, וּמוּתָּר. וְאִם אֵין שָׁם אַחֵר — מַנִּיחַ עַל גַּבֵּי הַסֶּלַע אוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הַגָּדֵר, וְאוֹמֵר: ״הֲרֵי הֵן מוּפְקָרִין לְכׇל מִי שֶׁיַּחְפּוֹץ״, וְהַלָּה נוֹטֵל וְאוֹכֵל, וּמוּתָּר. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹסֵר. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֶׂה דְּבֵית חוֹרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** If the two **were walking along the way, and** the one prohibited from benefiting from the other **does not have** anything **with him to eat,** the one who took the vow may **give** food **to a different** person **as a gift, and this** one **takes** it **and eats, and** this arrangement is **permitted,** as he did not give the food directly to him. **And if there is no other** person **there** apart from the two of them, he should **place** the items **on a rock or on a fence and say: They are** hereby declared **ownerless for anyone who wants** them, **and this** one **takes** the food items **and eats** them, **and** this too is **permitted. But Rabbi Yosei prohibits** this practice. **Rava said: What is the reason** for this ruling **of Rabbi Yosei?** It is a rabbinic **decree due to the incident of Beit Ḥoron.**

The Gemara signals a return to an earlier source with the word *gufa* — "the body of the matter itself" — and cites the relevant Mishnah from Nedarim. The case is: *ha-mudar hana'ah me-chavero* — someone is forbidden by vow from deriving benefit from his friend — and yet the friend has nothing to eat today. The solution the Mishnah describes is indirect: the one who wishes to help goes to the local shopkeeper, with whom he is accustomed to doing business, and says to him, "So-and-so has taken a vow and cannot receive benefit from me, and he has nothing to eat — I don't know what I can do for him." The shopkeeper, picking up on the hint, goes ahead and provides food. When he later returns and reports what he spent, the one who wanted to help gives him the money. The arrangement is deliberately indirect: no explicit instruction was ever given.

### Provision Scenarios

The Mishnah continues with additional scenarios. Where the person's house needs to be built, his fence repaired, or his field harvested, the same method is employed: the helper goes to workers he regularly employs and says the same thing — "I don't know what I can do for him." The workers go and do the labor, and afterward come back to receive their payment. It is worth noting that the language of the Mishnah — that they "take their wages" — implies they can actually demand it. In practice, if this arrangement were brought before a court, they likely could not press a formal legal claim, or it would look as though he had simply hired them directly. But within the understood norms of the arrangement, the transaction functions as a voluntary gift.

A further scenario: the two parties are traveling together and the person under the vow has no food. The solution is to find a third party, give him the food as a gift, and then the person under the vow approaches the third party and asks to share. The Mishnah says this is *mutar* — permitted. And if there is no third party at all? Then one places the food on a rock or wall and declares it *hefker* — ownerless — "available to anyone who wishes to take it." The only person present is the one under the vow, who comes and takes it. The Mishnah rules this too is permitted.

### Rabbi Yose Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה דְּבֵית חוֹרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** the **incident of Beit Ḥoron,** where an individual had vowed to prohibit his father from deriving benefit from him, and then in order to allow his father to come to the celebration of his son's wedding, he gave all of his property to someone else as a gift. The recipient of the property was concerned that the vow would be transgressed by the father, so he consecrated the son's property and declared that if he was not empowered to do so, then the original transfer of property as a gift would not be valid. Consequently, in the present case, the Sages are unconcerned by the artifice performed, while Rabbi Yosei is concerned with such artifice and therefore prohibits it.

The Mishnah concludes: *Rabbi Yose oser* — Rabbi Yose forbids this last case. The Gemara explains his reasoning: *gzeirah mishum ma'aseh d'Beit Choron* — a rabbinic decree on account of an incident involving a place called Beit Choron, which is itself a Mishnah in Nedarim. In that case, a man's father was forbidden from deriving benefit from him. Wishing his father to attend his son's wedding feast, he transferred ownership of all the food to a third party — so that the food legally belonged to someone else, which would permit the father to eat from it. The third party, recognizing what was being done, promptly declared the food *hekdesh* — consecrated to the Temple — in effect saying: if you are truly giving this to me so that you can get around your *neder*, I will treat it as mine in the fullest sense. The stratagem collapsed.

### Gzerah Analysis

The question arises: what exactly is the nature of Rabbi Yose's concern? It seems clear that Rabbi Yose forbids only this last case — the *hefker* scenario — and not all of the earlier cases involving third parties. After all, the incident of Beit Choron itself involved a third party. The lesson drawn from Beit Choron is not that genuine third-party arrangements are invalid, but rather that when the maneuver becomes so transparent and so obviously a legal fiction, it cannot be sanctioned. This is precisely what makes the *hefker* case different: when there is no one else in a radius of ten miles and you declare the food ownerless with one person standing right there, the entire arrangement crosses the line from a legitimate workaround into what can only be described as a farce. That, it seems, is the core of Rabbi Yose's *gzeirah*.

It is worth pausing here to consider the broader context. Vows in their original conception served a serious religious function — a way for individuals to structure a more personal religious life, to create self-imposed restrictions analogous to the *chumrot* of later generations, except that these were formal legal acts rather than mere pious customs. The Gemara elsewhere compares taking vows to building a *bamah* — a private altar — a kind of tailored personal religious experience. But as these cases illustrate, once the power to make things forbidden is in one's hands, it can be weaponized — used out of spite, used to create barriers between people, used in ways that distort and abuse the original purpose. The cases of the Mishnah show not so much a failure of vow law as an illustration of how any legal mechanism can be put to uses for which it was never intended.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Yehudah Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל חֹדֶשׁ אֶחָד וְכוּ׳. הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כֹּהֶנֶת אֲתָא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן. רָבָא אָמַר: חֹדֶשׁ מָלֵא וְחֹדֶשׁ חָסֵר אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna: **Rabbi Yehuda says: If** the husband is an **Israelite,** then if his vow will remain in effect for up to **one month,** he may maintain her as his wife; and if it will be two months, he must divorce her and give her the payment of her marriage contract. But if he is a priest, then he is given extra time: If the vow will remain in effect for up to two months, he may maintain her, and if it will be three months, he must divorce her and give her the payment of her marriage contract. The Gemara is puzzled by Rabbi Yehuda's statement with regard to an Israelite: **This is** the same as the opinion of **the first *tanna*. Abaye said:** Concerning an Israelite, Rabbi Yehuda in fact does not disagree with the first *tanna*, but **he comes to teach us** that the *halakha* is different for the **wife of a priest. Rava said:** The practical difference **between them is a full** thirty-day **month and a deficient month** with twenty-nine days: The first *tanna* requires exactly thirty days, while Rabbi Yehuda requires one month, whether it is a full month or a deficient one.

The *daf* begins by returning to a topic introduced earlier in the sugya: a man who has made a vow forbidding his wife from deriving benefit from him, and the practical question of how she is to be supported in the interim before the court demands a divorce. The Mishnah distinguishes between a man who is an ordinary Israelite and a *kohen*. For a non-*kohen*, the tolerable period is one month; for a *kohen*, two months, since divorce for a *kohen* is irreversible — he may not remarry a divorcée. The Gemara notes that Rabbi Yehudah's position regarding the non-*kohen* appears identical to the *tanna kamma*'s ruling of thirty days. Abaye resolves this: the *chiddush* of Rabbi Yehudah's formulation is the rule regarding the wife of a *kohen*. Rava adds a further distinction even with respect to the one-month period: the *tanna kamma* specifies thirty days as a fixed number, while Rabbi Yehudah says "a month," which may sometimes be twenty-nine days depending on the calendar. Whether this means the month is always twenty-nine days or that it follows the actual calendar month is a question raised by Tosafot.

### Rav Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּמְפָרֵשׁ, אֲבָל בִּסְתָם — יוֹצִיא לְאַלְתַּר וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בִּסְתָם לֹא יוֹצִיא, שֶׁמָּא יִמְצָא פֶּתַח לְנִדְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said:** With regard to the thirty-day time frame, where the Sages established that a husband supports his wife through a trustee, **they taught** this **only** with regard to a case **where he specifies** a limited time during which the vow will be in effect. **But** if he vows **without specification** of an end point, **he must divorce** her **immediately and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract. And Shmuel said: Even** if he vowed **without specification, he should not divorce** her immediately, as **perhaps he will discover an opening** enabling the dissolution **of his vow.**

The Gemara now turns to the core dispute between Rav and Shmuel, which will animate the remainder of the discussion. *Amar Rav lo shanu ella bi-mefaresh* — Rav says: this one-month tolerance applies only when the husband has explicitly specified a duration. If he says the vow lasts fifteen days, or twenty days, or up to a month, we tolerate it. If he specifies more than a month, we demand divorce immediately — because the *neder* was explicitly made for a period exceeding what the marriage can sustain. And if he specifies no duration at all, then it is as though he said forever, and we demand divorce immediately as well. According to Rav, then, the question of whether to wait or to act is entirely determined by what the husband explicitly declared at the time of the vow.

### Shmuel Position

Shmuel takes a fundamentally different position. *Afilu bi'stam lo yotzi* — even when the vow is *stam*, made without any specified duration and therefore effectively forever, we do not demand divorce immediately. The one-month period mentioned in the Mishnah is not a description of how long the vow was made for, but rather a description of how long we will wait before demanding a divorce — regardless of the vow's stated duration. During that month, we give the husband an opportunity: *shema yimtza pesach l'nidro* — perhaps he will find a *pesach*, an opening, through which a *chacham* can release him from the vow. The mechanism of *hataras nedarim* was not a formality; it required a genuine substantive basis — a realization that had one foreseen certain circumstances, the vow would never have been made. But it was a real possibility. Shmuel thus frames the Mishnah's time periods not as assessments of the vow's duration but as a window for resolution.

### Articulation Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיפְּלִגוּ בַּיהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא, דִּתְנַן: הַמַּדִּיר אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִתַּשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת. — וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: שַׁבָּת אַחַת. וְאָמַר רַב: מַחְלוֹקֶת בִּמְפָרֵשׁ, אֲבָל בִּסְתָם — יוֹצִיא לְאַלְתַּר וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בִּסְתָם נָמֵי לֹא יוֹצִיא, שֶׁמָּא יִמְצָא פֶּתַח לְנִדְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But they have already disagreed about this** issue **one time** with regard to a similar situation, **as we learned** in a mishna (61b): With regard to **one who vows** and obligates **his wife,** prohibiting her **from** engaging in **marital relations** with him, **Beit Shammai say:** If the vow will remain in effect for up to **two weeks,** he may maintain her as a wife, **and Beit Hillel say** the limit is **one week. And** with regard to this dispute, **Rav said: The dispute is** in a case **where he specifies, but in** the case of **an unspecified** vow, **he must divorce** her **immediately and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract. And Shmuel said: Even in** the case of **an unspecified** vow **he also should not divorce** her immediately, as **perhaps he will discover an opening** enabling the dissolution **of his vow.** If so, why does this dispute need to be repeated?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּהָהִיא: בְּהָהִיא קָאָמַר רַב — מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר בְּפַרְנָס, אֲבָל בְּהָא, דְּאֶפְשָׁר בְּפַרְנָס — אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל. וְאִי אִתְּמַר בְּהָא: בְּהָא קָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל — מִשּׁוּם דְּאֶפְשָׁר בְּפַרְנָס, אֲבָל בְּהַהִיא, אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ לְרַב — צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **necessary** to state it twice, **as, if it was stated** only **in that** case, i.e., that of a vow rendering it prohibited for her to engage in sexual intercourse with him, then one may have said that **in that** case **Rav says** he must divorce her immediately, **because it is impossible** to compensate for the vow **through a trustee. But in this** case, where the vow rendered it prohibited for her to benefit from his property, for **which** it **is possible** to compensate **through a trustee,** we would **say** that **he concedes to Shmuel** that he should not divorce her immediately. **And** conversely, **if it were stated in this** case, i.e., that of a vow concerning sustenance, then one may have said **in this** case that **Shmuel says** he should not divorce her **because it is possible** to compensate for the vow **through a trustee, but in that** case, where he vows to prohibit her from engaging in marital relations with him, one would **say he concedes to Rav** that he must divorce her immediately. Therefore, it is **necessary** to record the dispute twice.

The Gemara raises an objection: *ve-ha-ifligu bei chada zimna* — haven't Rav and Shmuel already debated this same question in the context of an earlier Mishnah? That Mishnah discussed a man who forbids his wife from marital relations — *tashmish ha-mitah* — by a vow. There too the same dispute appears: Rav holds that if the vow was indefinite, he must divorce immediately; Shmuel holds that we wait even so, up to a week or two weeks, to see if he can get out of it. Why must the debate be stated in both places?

The Gemara answers: *tzricha* — both are necessary. Had we only known their dispute regarding the *tashmish* case, one might have thought that Rav's stringency — demanding immediate divorce when the vow is indefinite — is specific to that case, because there is no third party who can substitute for the prohibited intimacy. Every day of waiting is a day in which she is denied something that cannot be provided another way. But perhaps Shmuel would agree with Rav in this case and reserve his lenient position only for cases of *mezonot*, where a third party can provide for her in the interim. Conversely, had we only heard the *mezonot* debate, one might have thought that Shmuel's leniency — waiting to see if a *pesach* can be found — applies only there, since she can be provided for otherwise, but that he would agree with Rav in the *tashmish* case where no substitute exists. Therefore the Gemara must state the dispute in both contexts.

### Sex vs. Sustenance

This leads to a precise articulation of the underlying logic. According to Rav, the distinction between cases is irrelevant — what matters is only the stated duration of the vow. According to Shmuel, even in the *tashmish* case, we give some time: one week according to Beit Shammai, two weeks according to Beit Hillel. The temporal thresholds differ between the sex case and the *mezonot* case — a week or two in one, a month or two months in the other — but the structural logic is the same in both.

## Mishnah Reading

### Conditional Vows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַמַּדִּיר אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא תִּטְעוֹם אֶחָד מִכׇּל הַפֵּירוֹת — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב: כָּאן בִּסְתָם, כָּאן בִּמְפָרֵשׁ. אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the continuation of the mishna: **One who vows** and obligates **his wife,** requiring her **not to taste a particular type of produce, must divorce** her **and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract. Granted** that according **to Rav,** there is no contradiction between the two clauses of the mishna. It can be said that **here** it is referring to an **unspecified** vow, so he must divorce her immediately, and **there,** in the first clause, it is referring to a case **where he specifies** a time limit. **But** according **to Shmuel,** it is **difficult.**

We turn now to the second part of the Mishnah, which at first glance presents the husband as making a vow that his wife cannot benefit from a certain fruit or use a certain perfume. This raises an immediate question: how can a husband make a vow forbidding his wife from eating kumquats, for instance? He has no direct power to make her food forbidden. The mechanism must be indirect. What he can say is something like: "If you eat a kumquat, then the pleasure of sex with me will be forbidden to you" — or the pleasure of the *mezonot* he provides. He cannot directly forbid the kumquat by vow, but he can construct a conditional structure in which a vow he *can* make — about his own benefit — is triggered by her performing a certain action. Effectively, by making her access to marital intimacy or sustenance contingent on her avoiding that fruit, he has made the fruit practically off-limits for her.

### Simple Reading Problem

If this is the simple reading of the Mishnah — that the husband made this conditional vow — then one understands why they must divorce. But a difficulty arises when the Gemara's debate between Rav and Shmuel is brought to bear. The Mishnah here says divorce immediately, not after a month. According to Rav, this is easily explained: the vow was *stam* — no duration was specified — so there is no waiting period. But according to Shmuel, who holds that even a *stam* vow gets a month's waiting period, why here is there no waiting? That is the Gemara's question.

To resolve this difficulty, Shmuel is forced to reread the Mishnah in a way that departs from its literal surface. Instead of the husband having made the vow, the Gemara will say: she made the vow, and his role was to affirm it — either actively or silently, by not annulling it during the window in which he had the power to do so. Since he had the power to annul her vow and chose not to, he effectively made the vow binding. On this reading, the question becomes: who is to blame?

### Rishonim Approaches

This reframing creates its own difficulties. If she made the vow and he affirmed it — and we are no longer talking about a vow that directly forbids him from providing her with anything — why must they divorce at all? And if they do divorce, why does he pay the *ketubah*? Tosafot adds yet another layer to the Mishnah, suggesting that her vow was formulated specifically as conditional on their marriage: "Such-and-such fruit is forbidden to me as long as we are married." Divorce thus releases her from the vow. But this still seems strange — is an inability to eat a certain fruit really grounds for divorce? The Ran takes a different approach: the divorce is not really about the practical deprivation at all. When the husband silently affirmed — or actively confirmed — this restriction on his wife, he communicated something about the emotional state of the marriage. His willingness to let the vow stand, to not protect her from it, functions as a symbol of indifference or distance. It is not the kumquat; it is what the affirmation of the vow says about their relationship.

### Defense of Simple

Despite these Rishonic efforts, the cleanest and most straightforward reading of the Mishnah remains the one that takes its language at face value: the husband made the vow — indirectly, through the conditional structure described above. This explains why they must divorce — because the vow effectively makes marital life untenable — and it explains why he pays the *ketubah* — because it was his vow. It does not require the linguistic gymnastics of positing that she made the vow, and it does not require the interpretive layering of Tosafot or the symbolic reading of the Ran. The simpler reading is clearly the better reading. The Gemara's move to the alternative reading is driven entirely by the need to accommodate Shmuel's position — not by any inherent strength of that reading.

## Who Made Vow

### Immediate Divorce Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּדְרָה הִיא, וְקִיֵּים לָהּ אִיהוּ. וְקָסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר, הוּא נוֹתֵן אֶצְבַּע בֵּין שִׁינֶּיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where she vowed** to prohibit herself from tasting the produce, **and he ratified it for her** and did not dissolve the vow. Since she made the vow, she certainly will not search for a way to dissolve it. Therefore, he must divorce her immediately. **And Rabbi Meir,** who is presumed to be the author of an unattributed opinion in a mishna, **holds** that when he ratifies her vow, **he is putting his finger between her teeth,** causing her to bite him, i.e., he is causing the vow to be in effect. If so, it is his responsibility, and he therefore must give her the payment of her marriage contract when he divorces her.

With the framework now set, the Gemara proceeds to work through the specific cases in the Mishnah. The Mishnah states that if a man takes a vow forbidding his wife from benefiting from any fruit, or forbidding her the use of a certain perfume, he must divorce her immediately and pay the *ketubah*. The Gemara asks: why immediately? The earlier part of the Mishnah established that we wait a month before demanding divorce. Why here is it immediate?

### Rav Answer

Rav's answer is clean and direct: *kan bistama kan bimfarash* — there it speaks of a case where a duration was explicitly specified, here it speaks of a *stam* vow, one made without any stated duration. When the vow has no time limit, it is effectively forever, and therefore the Mishnah demands immediate divorce. There is no tension in Rav's reading: the one-month tolerance applies only to vows with a defined, acceptable duration; a vow of indefinite scope admits no waiting period.

### Shmuel Problem

For Shmuel, however, there is a serious problem. According to Shmuel, the time period in the Mishnah is always about how long we wait, not about how long the vow was made for. Both cases are *stam* — both involve indefinite vows — yet the Mishnah treats them differently. The first case gets a month; this case gets immediate divorce. Shmuel must explain that difference.

### Wife Made Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר הוּא נוֹתֵן אֶצְבַּע בֵּין שִׁינֶּיהָ? וְהָתַנְיָא: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁנָּדְרָה בְּנָזִיר, וְשָׁמַע בַּעְלָהּ וְלֹא הֵפֵר לָהּ, רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמְרִים: הִיא נָתְנָה אֶצְבַּע בֵּין שִׁינֶּיהָ, לְפִיכָךְ אִם רָצָה הַבַּעַל לְהָפֵר — יָפֵר. וְאִם אָמַר אִי אֶפְשִׁי בְּאִשָּׁה נַדְרָנִית — תֵּצֵא שֶׁלֹּא בִּכְתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **Rabbi Meir hold** that in this case **he is putting his finger between her teeth,** i.e., he is causing the vow to be in effect? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **A woman who vowed,** prohibiting herself from benefiting from items **that** are prohibited to **a nazirite, and her husband heard and did not nullify it, Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda say: She** already **put her finger between her** own **teeth,** i.e., she caused the vow to remain in effect. **Therefore, if the husband wishes to nullify** this vow, **he may nullify** it. **And if he said: I do not want a vowing wife, she** can **be divorced without** the payment **of** the **marriage contract.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמְרִים: הוּא נוֹתֵן אֶצְבַּע בֵּין שִׁינֶּיהָ, לְפִיכָךְ אִם רָצָה הַבַּעַל לְהָפֵר — יָפֵר. וְאִם אָמַר: אִי אֶפְשִׁי בְּאִשָּׁה נַדְרָנִית — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה! אֵיפוֹךְ, רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמְרִים: הוּא נוֹתֵן, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמְרִים: הִיא נָתְנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Elazar say:** By deciding not to nullify the vow, **he is putting his finger between her teeth,** i.e., he is causing the vow to be in effect, and **therefore if the husband wishes to nullify** the vow, **he may nullify** it. **And if he said: I do not want a vowing wife, he must divorce** her **and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract.** The Gemara answers: **Reverse** the opinions. **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda say: He is putting** his finger between her teeth. **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Elazar say: She put** her finger between her own teeth.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא נָתְנָה? וְהָתְנַן: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בַּעֲנִיּוּת שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן קִצְבָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **Rabbi Yosei hold** that **she put** her finger between her own teeth? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: One who vows and obligates his wife, requiring her not to adorn herself with a particular type of perfume, and **Rabbi Yosei says: For poor women, when he did not establish a set amount of time** for the vow, he must divorce her and give her the payment of her marriage contract? According to the final explanation given by Shmuel, the mishna is referring to a case where the wife vowed and the husband ratified it, indicating that Rabbi Yosei also agrees that it is the husband's responsibility, and therefore he must give her the payment of her marriage contract.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמְרִים: הוּא נוֹתֵן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמְרִים: הִיא נָתְנָה. וְסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא נָתְנָה? וְהָתְנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל יוֹם אֶחָד — יְקַיֵּים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Say** that the text of the *baraita* should read as follows: **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei say: He is putting** his finger between her teeth; **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar say: She put** her finger between her own teeth. This way, there is no contradiction between statements attributed to either Rabbi Meir or Rabbi Yosei. The Gemara asks further: **And** does **Rabbi Yehuda hold** that **she put** her finger between her own teeth? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says: For an Israelite,** if the vow is in effect for **one day he may maintain** her as his wife, but if the vow is in effect for two days he must divorce her and give her the payment of the marriage contract? According to the explanation that the mishna is referring to a case where she vowed and he ratified it, it would appear that Rabbi Yehuda also agrees that he is putting his finger between her teeth.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמְרִים: הוּא נוֹתֵן, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הִיא נָתְנָה. וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר זוּגֵי זוּגֵי קָתָנֵי, אֵימָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמְרִים: הִיא נָתְנָה, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמְרִים: הוּא נוֹתֵן. וְהָא סְתָמָא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Say** the text of the dispute should read as follows: **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei say he is putting** his finger between her teeth, **and Rabbi Elazar says she put** her finger between her own teeth. **And if you say** the opinions of the *tanna'im* listed in the *baraita* **are taught in pairs,** and therefore it cannot be that three of them share the same opinion, **say: Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Elazar say she put** her finger between her own teeth, while **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei say he is putting** his finger between her teeth. **And this** particular **unattributed** opinion **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.**

The Gemara's solution, as noted above, is to reread the Mishnah: *hacha b'mai askinan — k'she-nadrah hi ve-kiyem la ihu* — here we are dealing with a case where she made the vow and he affirmed it. He had the opportunity to annul it within the first day and did not. The Gemara notes that this reading attributes the *stam* Mishnah to Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Meir holds: *hu nosein etzba bein shineha* — he put the finger between her teeth. The colorful idiom asks: who poked whom? Did he poke her in the teeth, or did she poke herself? In other words, who bears the blame?

The question of blame has real *halakhic* consequences: if she made the vow and he affirmed it, and then they divorce, does he pay the *ketubah* — implying it is his fault — or does she forfeit the *ketubah* — implying it is her fault? The Gemara indicates that even if she made the vow, since he had the power to annul it and did not, he bears sufficient responsibility that he must pay the *ketubah*.

As for why there is no waiting period in this case, Rashi explains: when he makes the vow, we assume he acted in anger and we give him time to cool off and find a *pesach*. But when she made the vow and he was calmly *mekayem* it — silently affirming it — there is no assumption of hot-headed anger to be resolved. That dynamic, as the Gemara will elaborate later, is more troubling and more urgent.

### Nazirite Baraita

The Gemara now challenges the attribution of the Mishnah's principle to Rabbi Meir by bringing a *baraita*. This *baraita*, then, directly contradicts the Gemara's assumption. If Rabbi Meir holds that it is her fault — *hi nasnah* — then how can our Mishnah, which is presumed to follow Rabbi Meir, hold that when she made the vow and he affirmed it, he pays the *ketubah*?

### Name Reconciliation

The Gemara attempts several solutions, each involving a rearrangement of the names in the *baraita* to reconcile it with the Mishnah. First, it suggests simply reversing the attributions: let Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah be the ones who say *hu nasan* — he is to blame — and Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Elazar be the ones who say *hi nasnah* — she is to blame. But this too creates a problem, because Rabbi Yosei appears explicitly in our Mishnah accepting the premise that the husband pays the *ketubah* — which implies he holds *hu nasan*. If we reassign him to the *hi nasnah* camp, he contradicts himself.

The Gemara then tries mixing the pairs: let Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei say *hu nasan*, and Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Elazar say *hi nasnah*. But Rabbi Yehudah also appears explicitly in the Mishnah on the side of *hu nasan*, and so this too fails. At this point the Gemara considers a three-against-one formulation — Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yehudah, and Rabbi Yosei all say *hu nasan*, and only Rabbi Elazar says *hi nasnah* — but the Gemara recognizes the absurdity of rewriting a *baraita* that was clearly structured as two against two into a three-against-one debate.

The final resolution the Gemara offers is to maintain the two-against-two structure but reassign the pairs: Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Elazar say *hi nasnah* — she is to blame — and Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Yosei say *hu nasan* — he is to blame. Since Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Yosei are explicitly named in the Mishnah as holding the *hu nasan* position, they can stay there. And as for the *stam* Mishnah being attributed to Rabbi Meir — the Gemara concludes: *ve-stama de-lo ke-Rabbi Meir* — the *stam* Mishnah here does not follow Rabbi Meir. This is an unusual concession, but the Gemara accepts it to preserve internal consistency.

## Vow Categories

### Innuy Nefesh Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁהַבַּעַל מֵיפֵר: דְּבָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן עִינּוּי נֶפֶשׁ, ״אִם אֶרְחַץ״, ״אִם לֹא אֶרְחַץ״. ״אִם אֶתְקַשֵּׁט״, ״אִם לֹא אֶתְקַשֵּׁט״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֵין אֵלּוּ נִדְרֵי עִינּוּי נֶפֶשׁ, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן נִדְרֵי עִינּוּי נֶפֶשׁ: ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל בָּשָׂר״, וְ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה יַיִן״, וְ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶתְקַשֵּׁט בְּבִגְדֵי צִבְעוֹנִין״!
>
> **Translation:** And the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a different mishna (*Nedarim* 79a): **These are the cases** of a wife's vow **that the husband may nullify: Cases** of vows **that involve affliction,** such as when the woman says: **If I bathe,** I forbid myself to benefit from it; or if she says: **If I do not bathe,** i.e., she vows not to bathe at all; or she vows: **If I adorn** myself; or vows: **If I do not adorn** myself, all of which cause her to suffer. **Rabbi Yosei said: These are not vows of affliction,** which the husband may nullify, **but** rather, **these,** i.e., the following, **are vows of affliction:** Such as when she vows **that I will not eat meat, or that I will not drink wine, or** even **that I will not adorn myself with colored garments!**

Having established that Shmuel reads the Mishnah as a case where she made the vow and he affirmed it, the Gemara now faces a further challenge regarding Rabbi Yossi's position. The Gemara asks: if that is how we read the Mishnah, then we are implicitly saying the husband had the power to annul her vow. But what category of vow is this? The Torah specifies two categories that the husband can annul. The first is *nidrei innuy nefesh* — vows of self-affliction. The second, derived from the phrase *bein ish l'ishto*, encompasses vows that come between husband and wife.

The Gemara cites a Mishnah in Nedarim that defines which vows fall into the *innuy nefesh* category. The *tanna kamma* lists vows about bathing or not bathing, about wearing jewelry or not wearing jewelry. Rabbi Yosei, however, is stringent about the threshold: *ein eilu nidrei innuy nefesh* — these do not meet the standard of *innuy nefesh*. What does? Not eating meat, not drinking wine, not adorning oneself in *bigdei tzevonim* — colored garments. In other words, according to Rabbi Yosei, relatively minor deprivations — like not wearing jewelry or not using perfume — do not rise to the level of *innuy nefesh*.

### Annulment Challenge

This creates a sharp difficulty for Shmuel's reading of the Mishnah. Our Mishnah involves a vow about perfume and jewelry-type adornment. According to Rabbi Yosei, these are not *innuy nefesh* vows. But Shmuel's reading of the Mishnah presupposes that the husband was in a position to annul the vow — that is why his failure to do so makes him responsible. If the vow is not *innuy nefesh*, can he still annul it?

The answer turns on the second category: *bein ish l'ishto* — vows that interfere with the marital relationship. The distinction between the two categories is practically significant. If a vow falls under *innuy nefesh*, the husband's annulment works universally — it removes the prohibition for everyone. If a vow falls only under *bein ish l'ishto*, the husband's annulment works only to the extent necessary to remove the barrier between them specifically; it does not release her from the prohibition vis-à-vis the rest of the world.

### Beino L'Veinah Annul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בִּדְבָרִים שֶׁבֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case where she vowed not to adorn herself **with regard to matters that are between him and her,** meaning that she vowed not to use a substance that removes her pubic hair. This is considered a matter between him and her, since the hair could interfere with sexual intercourse.

The Gemara resolves the difficulty: *hacha b'mai askinan — bidvarim she-beino l'veinah* — here we are talking about vows that fall in the *beino l'veinah* category. Even though Rabbi Yosei holds that these perfume and adornment vows are not *innuy nefesh*, they are still in the *beino l'veinah* category, which the husband can still annul. Rabbi Yosei is not saying the husband is powerless to annul these vows; he is merely downgrading them from one category to another.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר דְּבָרִים שֶׁבֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ הַבַּעַל מֵיפֵר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין הַבַּעַל מֵיפֵר, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דְּאִתְּמַר: דְּבָרִים שֶׁבֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: הַבַּעַל מֵיפֵר, רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר: אֵין הַבַּעַל מֵיפֵר, שֶׁלֹּא מָצִינוּ שׁוּעָל שֶׁמֵּת בַּעֲפַר פִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** works out **well according to the one who said the husband can nullify** his wife's vow if it relates to **matters that are between him and her,** i.e., that disrupt normal, intimate relations between them. **But according to the one who said the husband cannot nullify** such vows, **what can be said?** The *amora'im* had a dispute concerning this question, **as it is stated:** With regard to vows related to **matters that are between him and her,** such as the example above, **Rav Huna said** that **the husband** can **nullify** his wife's vow, while **Rav Adda bar Ahava said the husband cannot nullify** his wife's vow, since it does not interfere with sexual intercourse between them. Rav Adda bar Ahava explains his opinion with an analogy: **Since we have not found a fox that died in the dirt of a hole** where it lives, so too here, although she grows her pubic hair, he will not be harmed by it, since he is familiar with her body.

The Gemara then raises the position of Rav Adda bar Abba, who holds that *beino l'veinah* vows cannot be annulled at all — *ein ha-ba'al meifer*. He supports this with the adage: *shelo matzinu shual she-mes b'afar pir* — we have never seen a fox die in the dust of its own burrow. The implication is that domestic matters of this nature, which fall short of true *innuy nefesh*, are things people can manage within a marriage; they won't be destroyed by them. Tosafot clarifies that this is not a debate about whether the husband can annul *beino l'veinah* vows — that principle is taken as axiomatic — but rather a debate internal to Rabbi Yoshi's position: when he said these things are not *innuy nefesh*, did he mean they are entirely beneath the threshold of any annulment, or merely that they fall into the lesser *beino l'veinah* category?

If one takes the stricter view — that according to Rabbi Yosei these vows cannot be annulled at all — then Shmuel's reading of the Mishnah collapses, because it assumes the husband had the power to annul and chose not to.

### Rav Kahana Sex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן דִּתְלָ[תַ]נְהוּ לְקִישּׁוּטֶיהָ בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה, דְּאָמְרָה: ״יֵאָסֵר הֲנָאַת תַּשְׁמִישְׁךָ עָלַי אִם אֶתְקַשֵּׁט״, כִּדְאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא. דְּאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: ״הֲנָאַת תַּשְׁמִישִׁי עָלֶיךָ״ — כּוֹפָהּ וּמְשַׁמַּשְׁתּוֹ. ״הֲנָאַת תַּשְׁמִישְׁךָ עָלַי״ — יָפֵר, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מַאֲכִילִין לָאָדָם דָּבָר הָאָסוּר לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, with what are we dealing here?** With a case **where** through her vow **she made sexual intercourse contingent upon her adornment, as she said: The pleasure of intercourse with you is forbidden to me if I adorn myself, as Rav Kahana said** that such language qualifies as matters between him and her, and a husband can nullify such a vow. **As Rav Kahana said:** If the woman says to her husband: **The pleasure of intercourse with me** is forbidden **to you,** he may nevertheless **compel her** through legal and financial measures to fulfill her marital obligations **and have sexual intercourse with him,** since she does not have the power to render herself forbidden to him by a vow, due to her prior marital obligations. But if she says: **The pleasure of intercourse with you** is forbidden **to me,** this vow is valid but **he may nullify** it. Although she is obligated by the terms of the marriage to cohabit with him, she does not directly contravene her obligation but rather prohibits herself from deriving pleasure from sexual intercourse. Therefore, her husband may not compel her to engage in intercourse in violation of her vow, since one cannot feed a person an object which is forbidden to him. Instead, he may nullify it if he wishes.

The Gemara therefore pivots to yet another reading. The Gemara cites the statement of Rav Kahana: *hana'at tashmushi alecha* — if the wife says "the pleasure of my marital relations is forbidden to you," *kopeh u'meshamasht'o* — we compel her to be with him, or at least the vow is unenforceable, because she cannot forbid him what he is entitled to. But if she says *hana'at tashmishcha alai* — "the pleasure of your marital relations is forbidden to me" — that is an indirect formulation, and the vow does bind. In that case, *yeifer* — he must annul it — because the alternative is either they transgress by having relations while forbidden, or they refrain from relations, neither of which is acceptable. This vow clearly falls under both *innuy nefesh* and *beino l'veinah*.

## Reframe Case

### Vow Structure

With Rav Kahana's principle established, the Gemara introduces a new and final reading of the Mishnah that resolves the remaining difficulties. The case is not really about perfume or fruit at all — not directly. What the Mishnah is describing is a vow structured as follows: *ye'aser hana'at tashmishcha alai im etkashat* — "the benefit of having sex with you will be forbidden to me if I adorn myself with jewelry." The adornment is merely the condition; the substance of the vow is about marital relations. This is precisely the type of vow Rav Kahana discusses: an indirect but effective prohibition on *tashmish*.

This reading resolves all the difficulties simultaneously. It explains why they must divorce — because the vow puts marital relations at risk, not merely a luxury food item. It explains why the husband can annul the vow — because it is unambiguously *innuy nefesh* and *beino l'veinah* when the ultimate subject is *tashmish*. And it explains why getting divorced actually helps — because the vow is structured to be triggered by something that is inevitable in normal life. The Gemara notes: if she does not adorn herself, she will be publicly embarrassed — *karu lah menuveles* — and the social pressure makes it inevitable that eventually she will put on the jewelry and the prohibition will take effect.

### Calm Affirmation

But one difficulty remains: why is there no waiting period? Even once the vow takes effect on *tashmish*, why not give them a week or two, as the Mishnah's earlier section suggests?

### Emotional Dynamics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּאַדְּרַהּ אִיהוּ, דְּסָבְרָה: מִירְתָּח רָתַח עִילָּוַאי וְהַשְׁתָּא מוֹתֵיב דַּעְתֵּיהּ. אֲבָל הָכָא, דִּנְדַרָה אִיהִי וְשָׁתֵיק לַהּ, סָבְרָה מִדְּאִישְׁתִּיק — מִיסְנָא הוּא דְּסָנֵי לִי.
>
> **Translation:** This applies only **where he took a vow** to render intercourse with her prohibited, **as she thinks: He** vowed because he **is angry with me, but now he will calm down** and dissolve the vow. **But here,** as the mishna is explained as a case **where she vows and he is silent** and does not nullify it, **she thinks: Since he is silent,** this means **he despises me,** and consequently she desires a divorce.

The Gemara answers: *hani milei heicha d'adrah ihu* — the waiting period applies when he made the vow. In that case, she reasons: *mirtach rasach alai* — he was furious at me; he'll calm down. She can tolerate a week or two because she understands it as an episode of anger that will pass. But in our case: *aval hacha d'nadrah ihi, ve-shtik lah* — here she made the vow and he was silent. He calmly affirmed it. The subject of the vow is marital relations, and he — silently, without anger — let it stand. Her conclusion: *savrah mid'ishtik, misna hu d'sani li* — from the fact that he was quiet, she infers he must hate her. The emotional distance created by his calm acquiescence is far more damaging than the heat of an angry vow. It creates an immediate and unbridgeable rupture — and therefore the Mishnah demands immediate action rather than a waiting period.

### Rav Shmuel Reconcile

At this stage, the approaches of Rav and Shmuel can be fully reconciled within the Mishnah's framework. According to Rav, the husband made the conditional vow — indirectly, about sex — and since it was made without a specified duration, divorce is required immediately. According to Shmuel, she made the vow about sex, he affirmed it calmly and without anger, and the emotional dynamics of that scenario make waiting impossible. Both positions, through different paths, arrive at the same outcome: immediate divorce with full payment of the *ketubah*.

## Duration Thresholds

### Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בַּעֲנִיּוּת שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן קִצְבָה. וְכַמָּה קִצְבָה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים. רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר אֲבִימִי: רֶגֶל, שֶׁכֵּן בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל מִתְקַשְּׁטוֹת בָּרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that **Rabbi Yosei says: For poor women,** he must divorce her **when he did not establish a set amount of time** for the vow to remain in effect. The Gemara asks: **And how long is** this **set amount of time?** He is allowed to maintain her as a wife if he did set a time, but certainly there is a limit. This *halakha* would not apply in a case of a long period of time. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Twelve months. Rabba bar bar Ḥanna** said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Ten years. Rav Ḥisda** said that **Avimi said: A pilgrim Festival,** meaning until the next one of the three Festivals, **since Jewish women adorn themselves on the pilgrim Festival.** If his vow remains in effect beyond the Festival, it is considered as if he did not set a time limit, and he must divorce her.

The Mishnah concludes with Rabbi Yossi's distinction between poor women and wealthy women with respect to the perfume case. For poor women, the vow is intolerable only if it has no stated limit at all; if a duration was specified, even a long one, we tolerate it. For wealthy women — *isha chashuvah* — even a month is intolerable.

### Limit Debate

The Gemara immediately asks: what exactly does "no stated limit" mean? Surely a vow made for twenty years has a stated limit, yet no one would say it is tolerable. At what point does a stated but very long duration become the equivalent of having no limit?

### Time Standards

Three *amoraim* offer their standards. Rav Yehudah in the name of Shmuel: twelve months. More than a year is considered tantamount to having no limit. Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: ten years — a dramatically more lenient threshold. Rav Chisda in the name of Avimi: *regel* — until the next festival. His reasoning: *sheken bnot Yisrael miskashetos ba-regel* — Jewish women adorn themselves for *Yom Tov*. To deprive a woman of the ability to adorn herself even for the festival is intolerable even for the poor, and therefore if the vow extends beyond the nearest *regel*, it is effectively without limit.

### 30-Day Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַעֲשִׁירוּת שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. מַאי שְׁנָא שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שֶׁכֵּן אִשָּׁה חֲשׁוּבָה נֶהֱנֵית מֵרֵיחַ קִשּׁוּטֶיהָ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **And for wealthy women,** Rabbi Yosei said the limit is **thirty days.** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **thirty days** specifically? **Abaye said: Because an important** and wealthy **woman enjoys the scent of her adornments** that she put on previously for up to **thirty days,** and after that time she feels that she is repulsive.

The Gemara then turns to the wealthy woman's one-month threshold. Why specifically thirty days? The Gemara asks: *peshita* — is this not obvious? And responds: *mai shana l'shloshim yom* — what is it about thirty days? Abaye explains: *sheken isha chashuvah nehenes me-rei'ach kishuteiha l'shloshim yom* — a woman of means still enjoys the residual fragrance of her perfume for up to thirty days. Beyond thirty days, even the lingering scent has dissipated. The Gemara thus makes explicit that the subject here is indeed perfume, and the thirty-day threshold corresponds precisely to the point at which even the most generous residual benefit of the perfume has been fully exhausted.
