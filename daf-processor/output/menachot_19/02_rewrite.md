# Menachot 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yetzika & Kohen

### Yetzika Dispute

The daf begins on the last three lines of 18b, with the Gemara asking *mai ta'ama d'rabbanan* — what is the reasoning of the Sages? Recall the background: a *machlokes* was introduced between the Sages and Rabbi Shimon concerning whether *yetzika*, the pouring of oil onto the *mincha*, must be performed by a *kohen*. The *mishna* itself had stated that *yetzika* is not *me'akev*, but the Gemara immediately reinterpreted this: *yetzika* is indeed required — what the *mishna* means is that it is not *me'akev* with respect to requiring a *kohen*. Yet Rabbi Shimon's position, as cited previously, is that a *kohen* who does not accept all of the *mitzvot* of the *kehuna* has no portion in it — and among the acts he enumerated were *yetzika* and *belila*, acts that precede the *kemitza*. The working understanding, then, is that Rabbi Shimon requires a *kohen* for these pre-*kemitza* acts, while the *chachamim* do not.

A parenthetical note: the relevant *pasuk* quoted there, *lo yihye shok hayamin limana*, uses the language of *chelek* — a portion — and the Rambam reads this as relating to a *kohen*'s entitlement to a share in the *korbanot*, not as a restriction on his performing the *avoda* itself. The *Tosefta*, however, implies an actual restriction on performing the *avoda*, and extends this to a *levi* as well. The Rambam *paskens* this restriction for a *levi* but not for a *kohen* — a tension that Rav Soloveitchik addresses and that merits separate treatment.

### Yetzika Not Me'akev Kohen

The Gemara explains the reasoning of the *rabbanan* from the *pasuk*: *veyatzak aleha shemen u'natan aleha levona*, and then, *vehevia el bnei Aharon hakohanim*, and then *vekamatz misham*. The structure is telling. The owner pours on the oil, places the *levona*, and brings it to the *bnei Aharon hakohanim* — and then they take the *kemitza*. Since the *kohanim* are introduced into the *pasuk* only at the moment of *kemitza*, the straightforward reading is that *kemitza va'eilach mitzvat kehuna* — from the *kemitza* onward is the *mitzvat kehuna*. What precedes it — the *yetzika* and *belila* — may be performed by a non-*kohen* and is *kasher bezar*.

### Rabbi Shimon's View

How does Rabbi Shimon dispute this? The Gemara states that Rabbi Shimon employs the hermeneutic principle of *mikra nidrash lefanav ul'acharav* — a phrase in a *pasuk* can be read not only forward to what follows it, but also backward to what precedes it. The phrase *bnei Aharon hakohanim*, though it appears in the middle of the *pasuk*, extends, on his reading, back to the *yetzika* and *belila* that came before. The *pasuk* thus reads as though the *bnei Aharon hakohanim* performed the pouring and mixing as well. Admittedly, this is a difficult *pshat* — the text says *vehevia*, "and he brought it," which sounds as though the object was not yet in the *kohanim*'s possession — but Rabbi Shimon maintains that the entire passage centers on the *bnei Aharon hakohanim*.

### Rabbi Shimon's Reading

The Gemara, however, will ultimately reject this reading — not merely on grounds of *pshat*, but on account of another position that Rabbi Shimon himself holds.

### Challenge to Hermeneutic

The challenge begins at the last word of the first line of 19a: *vesavar Rabbi Shimon mikra nidrash lefanav ul'acharav* — does Rabbi Shimon truly subscribe to this hermeneutic of reading a phrase both forward and backward? The Gemara is asking whether Rabbi Shimon is genuinely prepared to interpret a *pasuk* based on a middle phrase even when that phrase clearly refers only to the latter portion of the verse, not the opening. The Gemara will now demonstrate from a separate case that Rabbi Shimon does not, in fact, accept this principle.

## Chattat Blood Case

### Blood Placement Debate

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* concerning the *chatat* blood placement: *velakach hakohen midam hachatat be'etzba'o, venatan al kornot hamizbeach* — the *kohen* shall take from the blood of the *chatat* with his finger and place it on the corners of the altar. The *baraita* derives from *etzba'o* that the right hand is required — since *etzba* was previously learned from the *metzora* to signify the right hand — and reads the *pasuk* in both directions. Reading *be'etzba'o* back onto *velakach*, the *baraita* derives that the *kabala*, the catching of the blood, must also be performed with the right hand: *melamed shelo tehe kabala ela biyamin*. Reading *be'etzba'o* forward onto *venatan*, it likewise derives that the placing must be with the right hand: *melamed shelo tehe netina ela biyamin*.

It should be noted that the plain sense of the *pasuk* is that *etzba* modifies both verbs quite naturally — one dips one's finger and then places it on the corners. What the *baraita* is doing is reading *velakach* not as "dip" but as the catching of the blood itself, and treating *etzba* not as literally "finger" but as a signal for "right hand." This is an interpretive move, not a *pshat* reading.

### Abaye's Framework

Rabbi Shimon disagrees with this *baraita*. He asks: *v'chi ne'emar yad b'kabala* — does the *pasuk* actually say "hand" by the act of catching the blood? Since it does not, *kibel bismol kasher* — the *kabala* may even be performed with the left hand. One might read this as Rabbi Shimon simply questioning whether the verb *velakach* can plausibly be taken to refer to catching blood. But Abaye explains that the fundamental dispute is precisely about *mikra nidrash lefanav ul'acharav*. The *chachamim* hold that *be'etzba'o* can be read backward to apply to *velakach* — the catching. Rabbi Shimon holds that *be'etzba'o* refers only forward, to *venatan* — the placing. He does not apply the word backward to the earlier verb.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

This is quite striking. In the case of the blood, the *pshat* of the *pasuk* actually supports reading *etzba* as applying to both verbs — one dips one's finger and places it. Yet even there, Rabbi Shimon refuses to apply the middle word backward. The Gemara concludes from this that Rabbi Shimon simply does not employ the principle of *mikra nidrash lefanav ul'acharav*. If that is so, then his position regarding the *mincha* — that *bnei Aharon hakohanim* applies backward to the *yetzika* and *belila* — cannot rest on this hermeneutic.

The Gemara then resolves the apparent contradiction: *ela hacha haynu ta'ama d'Rabbi Shimon* — here, in the *mincha* case, Rabbi Shimon's reasoning is different. The *pasuk* says *vehevia*, "and he brought it," with a *vav*. The *vav mosif al inyan rishon* — the conjunctive *vav* links the bringing to the *bnei Aharon hakohanim* with what came before it, drawing the *kohanim* into the earlier acts of pouring and mixing. It is not a case of reading the middle word backward; it is the *vav* itself that binds the earlier actions to the *bnei Aharon hakohanim*. Normally Rabbi Shimon would not apply the reference to *kohanim* backward — but here the linking *vav* provides the textual basis for doing so.

The Gemara, however, immediately challenges this: *v'savar Rabbi Shimon vav mosif al inyan rishon* — does Rabbi Shimon truly hold that a *vav* pulls the *kohanim* into earlier acts? If so, consider the *pasuk* concerning animal sacrifice: *veshachat et ben habakar vehikrivu bnei Aharon hakohanim et hadam vezarku et hadam al hamizbe'ach saviv* — one slaughters the animal, and then the *bnei Aharon* take the blood and sprinkle it. Since *bnei Aharon* are introduced only after the *shechita*, we learn *mi'kabala ve'eilach mitzvat kehuna* — from the receiving of the blood onward is the *kohen*'s domain — and accordingly the *shechita* is *keshera bezar*, valid when performed by a non-*kohen*. But there too the *pasuk* has a *vav* — *vehikrivu* — introducing the *bnei Aharon*. If Rabbi Shimon's principle is that the *vav* draws the *kohanim* backward, should he not then say that the *shechita* also requires a *kohen*?

## Animal vs Mincha

### Two Verses

This challenge forms the transition into a broader comparison of the two *psukim* — the one governing animal sacrifice and the one governing the *mincha*. The structure of both is parallel: an action precedes the introduction of *bnei Aharon hakohanim*, and in both cases there is a *vav* connecting the *kohanim* to what follows. The Gemara's question is pointed: if the *vav* is enough to draw the *kohanim* backward in the *mincha* case, why would it not do so in the animal sacrifice case as well?

### Gemara Objection

The Gemara makes the parallel explicit. By the animal sacrifice: *veshachat et ben habakar vehikrivu bnei Aharon*. By the *mincha*: *vayatzak aleha shemen venatan aleha levonah ve'hevi bnei Aharon hakohanim*. In both *psukim*, the *bnei Aharon* are introduced only after a prior act. In both, there is a *vav*. If everyone agrees that *shechita* can be done by a non-*kohen* because the *kohanim* only appear in the *pasuk* after the *shechita*, why should anyone dispute this by the *mincha*? The *vav* is present in both.

### Semicha Distinction

The Gemara answers: *shani hatam de'amar kra vesamach veshachat* — there it is different, because the *pasuk* on animal sacrifice begins with *vesamach yado al rosh korbano* — the owner leans his hand upon his *korban* — and then says *veshachat*. The beginning of the *pasuk* is unambiguously about the owner. When it says *veshachat*, we read it as the owner slaughtering. The *pasuk* thus makes explicit, through the juxtaposition with *semicha*, that the subject performing the *shechita* is the owner, not the *kohen*. This clarity means we do not need to extend the *bnei Aharon* backward.

### Ambiguity Argument

By contrast, in the *mincha pasuk*, the subject of the earlier actions is not so explicitly defined. It begins: *nefesh ki takriv korban mincha l'Hashem solet yihiyeh korbano* — a person who brings a *mincha*, it shall be his offering — *vayatzak aleha shemen venatan aleha levonah*. The *pshat* is undeniably that the person is performing these acts. But Rabbi Shimon's point is that at the moment of the *yetzika* and *belila*, the text does not juxtapose an act uniquely tied to the owner — as *semicha* is — that would anchor the subject as the owner. The *pasuk* could, at least implicitly, be read as shifting to a *kohen* performing those acts. The *mincha pasuk* is thus sufficiently ambiguous that Rabbi Shimon reads the *kohanim* into the earlier acts via the *vav*; the animal sacrifice *pasuk* is not ambiguous in the same way, because *semicha* pins the subject as the owner.

One might press further: could we then say that not only does the *shechita* not require a *kohen*, but it must be done specifically by the owner — since *semicha* is by the owner, and the juxtaposition with *veshachat* would imply the owner must slaughter too? The Gemara addresses this: *im smichah b'be'alim af shechitah b'be'alim* — if you push the argument from the connection of *shechita* to *semicha*, you would reach the conclusion that the owner himself must perform the *shechita*, which no one has ever said. This *reductio ad absurdum* undermines the argument.

### Ambiguity Argument (continued)

The Gemara, however, responds that this conclusion could not stand regardless, because of a *kal vachomer*: if *zerika*, which is the core act of atonement (*ikar kaparah*), does not require the owners to perform it — it is done by the *kohen* — then *shechita*, which is not the primary act of atonement, certainly does not require the owners. Therefore, we would never arrive at the conclusion that the owners must perform the *shechita*, and the argument from *semicha* cannot be weaponized to that extent.

The Gemara further considers the counter-argument — *ein danin efshar mi'she'ee efshar*, one cannot draw a proof from a case that has no alternative to a case that does have an alternative. By *zerika*, only a *kohen* may perform it, so the owners have no choice; perhaps when an act can be done by the owner, we require the owner. But this reasoning is also dismissed.

### Yom Kippur Exception

The Gemara's final resolution comes from a *gilui* of the *Torah* on Yom Kippur: *v'shachat et par hachatat asher lo* — Aaron slaughters the bull of the *chatat* which is his own. The Torah specifically emphasizes that he slaughters his own animal. *Michlal d'shechitah d'alma lo ba'inan be'alim* — from the fact that Yom Kippur requires a special emphasis on the owner performing the *shechita*, we learn that ordinarily *shechita* does not require the owners. Yom Kippur is the exception that proves the rule.

This is the foundation of Rabbi Shimon's complete position: the *mincha pasuk* is sufficiently ambiguous at the stage of *yetzika* and *belila* that the *vav* draws the *kohanim* into those acts; the animal sacrifice *pasuk* is not ambiguous, because *semicha* clearly identifies the owner as the subject up through and including *shechita*, and we independently know that *shechita* does not require the owner.

## Me'akev Principle

### Torah & Chok

Having concluded the analysis of Rabbi Shimon and the *chachamim*, the Gemara now turns to a related but distinct theme: the general principle by which any act in a *korban* is deemed *me'akev*, such that its omission invalidates the offering. This is the deeper question underlying the *mishna*, which lists a series of acts in the *mincha* that are not *me'akev*. Why not? If the Torah prescribes them, one might assume they are all indispensable.

The Gemara establishes that the principle of *shana alav hakatuv l'akeiv* — the Torah must repeat an act to render it *me'akev* — governs *korbanot*. This is counterintuitive: in no other area of *halacha* do we assume that an act is dispensable simply because it is mentioned only once. But the *rishonim* understood that the Torah's extraordinary level of detail regarding *korbanot*, and the fact that so much of that detail appears to be repeated, led the *chachamim* to the conclusion that not everything mentioned is indispensable — rather, indispensability is signaled by repetition.

Rav states the principle more precisely: *kol makom shene'emar Torah vehukah, eino ela l'akeiv* — wherever the Torah uses both the language of *torah* and *chok* (or *chukah*), that signals that what follows is *me'akev*. The paradigmatic case is *Parshat Parah*, which says *zot chukat hatorah* — employing both words together.

### Both Required?

The Gemara initially assumes that Rav requires both *torah* and *chukah* to appear together. It raises a series of challenges to test whether this dual requirement is truly necessary.

### Nazir Tenufah

The first case: by the *nazir*, the concluding *pasuk* says only *zot torat hanazir* — only *torah*, not *chukah*. Yet Rav himself says that *tenufah* by the *nazir* is *me'akev*, even though *tenufah* is ordinarily a prime example of an act that is not *me'akev*. The *pasuk* describes the ceremony: *ve'asah es zevach shelamim vegillach hanazir pesach ohel mo'ed velakach es se'ar rosh nizro venasan al ha'eish, velakach hakohen es hazroa beshela min ha'ayil, vechallat matzah achat min hasal urekik matzah echad, nasan al kapei hanazir vehinif osam hakohen* — one takes the cooked foreleg, breads from the basket, places them on the hands of the *nazir*, and the *kohen* waves them. The Gemara asks: how can the *tenufah* be *me'akev* if only *torah* appears, not *chukah*?

The Gemara answers: *shani hatam, keivan dichtsiv kein ye'aseh al torat nizro* — the *pasuk* adds the phrase "so shall he do," underscoring the obligation in a way that is *keman dichtsiva bei chukah dami* — equivalent to the text having written *chukah* as well. The double emphasis provides the necessary reinforcement.

### Todah Connection

The second case: the *todah*. By the *todah*, only *torah* appears — *vezot torat zevach hashelamim asher yakriv l'Hashem im al todah yakrivenu*. Yet the *mishna* teaches that the four types of loaves brought with the *todah* are *me'akvin zeh es zeh* — each type is indispensable, and the absence of any one invalidates the others. How is this *me'akev* without *chukah*?

The Gemara answers: *shani todah de'itkaish lenazir*. The *todah* is connected to the *nazir* because it is a type of *shelamim*, and the *pasuk* by the *todah* says *al zevach todat shelamav*, which a master teaching elsewhere derives to include the *shalmei nazir* for a particular *halakha*. Once the *todah* and the *shalmei nazir* are linked through that verse, the *me'akev* status that applies to the *nazir* extends to the *todah* as well.

### Metzora Case

The third case: the *metzora*. There too only *torah* appears — *zot tihye torat hametzora beyom taharato vehuva el hakohen* — and we have no *chukah*. Yet the *mishna* teaches that the four elements of the *metzora* ritual — the cedar wood, the hyssop, the red string, and the bird's blood — are all *me'akev*. The Gemara answers: *keivan dechtiv bei zot tihye* — the word *tihye* ("it shall be") re-emphasizes the obligation in a manner that is *keman dechtiv bei chok dami* — equivalent to *chok* having been written. Once again, a form of double emphasis substitutes for the explicit use of *chukah*.

### Yom Kippur

The fourth and most striking case: Yom Kippur. By Yom Kippur, the *pasuk* says only *chok* — *chukat olam ledoroteichem* — and does not say *torah*. The *mishna* teaches that the two goats of Yom Kippur are *me'akvin zeh es zeh* — they are mutually indispensable. The difficulty is that until now the Gemara had been assuming you need *torah* (or an equivalent) alongside *chok*. Here there is only *chok*. Rashi quotes: *vehayeta zot lachem lechukat olam lachaper al bnei yisrael mikol chatatam achat bashana* — and earlier, *vehayeta lachem lechukat olam bachodesh hashevi'i be'asor lachodesh ta'anu et nafshoteichem* — though that second instance concerns *inuy*, fasting, rather than the *avoda* itself, raising a question about which verse precisely the Gemara has in mind. Regardless, the *pasuk* concerning the Yom Kippur *avoda* speaks only of *chok*, not *torah*.

This presents a genuine difficulty for the position that both are required.

### Either Suffices

The Gemara's resolution is elegant: *o torah o chukah* — either is sufficient. One does not need both. The word *torah* alone — when properly emphasized or underscored — is enough to render something *me'akev*, and *chukah* alone is likewise sufficient. This explains the Yom Kippur case, where only *chok* appears.

But the Gemara immediately presses a counterpoint: if *torah* alone is enough, why is it not *me'akev* by all *korbanot*? The Torah states *zot hatorah la'olah lamincha velachatat vela'asham velamilu'im* — it says *torah* for all the major offerings — yet we do not say that every detail of every *korban* is *me'akev*. The Gemara therefore refines the conclusion: *torah* by itself, standing alone without additional emphasis, is not sufficient. What is required is either *chukah*, or *torah* that is reinforced by some form of re-emphasis — repetition, the word *tihye*, the phrase *kein ye'aseh*, or the like. *Chukah* on its own, by contrast, does carry sufficient weight without further reinforcement — as in the Yom Kippur case.

## Mincha & Repetition

### Rav's Rule

With the general principle now established, the Gemara turns to its application to the *mincha* specifically. Rav states: *kol makom shehechezir lecha hakatuv betorat minchah vetzav, eino ela l'akeiv* — wherever the Torah repeats something about the *mincha* in *Parashat Tzav* (having already mentioned it in *Vayikra*), that repetition signals that the act is *me'akev*. What is not repeated in *Tzav* is not *me'akev*, and omitting it is therefore not a problem *bedi'avad*.

### Problem Stated

The Gemara, however, immediately raises a difficulty. By the *mincha*, the Torah does use the language of *chok*: *kol zachar bivnei Aharon yochalena chok olam* — every male among the sons of Aaron shall eat it, it is an eternal *chok*. If *chukah* is sufficient on its own to render everything *me'akev*, why does Rav need the additional criterion of repetition in *Tzav*? The *chok* alone should make all aspects of the *mincha* *me'akev*.

### Shmuel's View

Shmuel offers a strikingly different position: *geres veshemen me'akvei, ve'ein davar acher me'akev* — only the flour (*geres*) and the oil (*shemen*) are *me'akev*; nothing else about the *mincha* is *me'akev*. This is a sweeping claim that cuts against the apparent logic of the *me'akev* principle. The Gemara immediately registers surprise at Shmuel's methodology: would Shmuel say that even when the Torah explicitly repeats an act in *Tzav*, it is still not *me'akev*? That would be a rejection of a fundamental *halachic* principle as it applies to *korbanot*.

The Gemara resolves this by reconstructing Shmuel's position more carefully. He does not actually reject the rule that repetition implies *me'akev*; rather, the dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerns one specific act: the *kemitza* itself, and whether it must be performed with the hand or may be done with a vessel.

### Challenge to Shmuel

The *pasuk* in one place says *melo kumtzo*, "the fullness of his hand," and in another says *bekumtzo*, "with his hand." The tension between these formulations raises a question: does *melo kumtzo* merely define a quantity — a volume equivalent to a full handful — such that one could use a vessel of appropriate size? Or does *bekumtzo* require literally using the hand itself? The Gemara explains that this is what Rav and Shmuel actually disagree about, not the general principle.

### Hand vs Vessel

A *baraita* rules explicitly: *melo kumtzo bekumtzo — shelo yaaseh middah lakometz* — one may not fashion a measuring vessel for the *kemitza*; the hand itself must be used. Rav accepts this and treats the matter as *me'akev*, while Shmuel disagrees.

### Rav on Aaron

Rav maintains that the *pasuk* in *Shemini* constitutes a repetition that renders using the hand *me'akev*: *va'yikriv et hamincha va'yimale chapo mimena* — Aaron brought the *mincha* and filled his palm from it. Since the narrative describes Aaron using his literal hand (*chapo*), and this narrative appears alongside the regular description of *kemitza*, Rav counts it as a textual repetition sufficient to establish *me'akev* status.

### Shmuel: One-Time

Shmuel rejects this: *dorosh mi'sha'ah lo yafinan* — one does not derive permanent *halachic* principles from a one-time event. The offering in *Shemini* was part of the *miluim*, the inauguration — a singular, unrepeated ceremony. The fact that Aaron used his hand on that occasion cannot be the basis for a permanent rule. Shmuel is not rejecting the principle that a narrative can serve as a textual repetition; he is rejecting this particular narrative because the underlying event was a *sha'ah*, a one-time occurrence, and therefore not instructive for ongoing practice.

This explains Shmuel's statement about *geres veshemen* more precisely: where the Torah has genuinely repeated something in the context of the regular *mincha* practice, Shmuel agrees it is *me'akev*. His dispute with Rav is limited to whether the *Shemini* narrative counts as such a repetition, given that it records a one-time event.

### Nesi'im Offering

The Gemara challenges Shmuel: does he truly hold that one may never learn from a one-time event? The Gemara cites Shmuel's own ruling elsewhere: *lei lach mekadesh es halach, umidas yavesh mekadesh es hayavesh* — liquid measuring vessels sanctify liquids and dry measuring vessels sanctify dry goods, but they do not work cross-purposes. Shmuel himself teaches an exception: *aval mizrakos*, the bowls used for blood, can sanctify even dry contents — *shneihem mele'im soles*, as the *pasuk* records that the *nesi'im* brought their *mizrakot* filled with flour. Yet that too was a one-time event. How can Shmuel learn from it?

### Repeated Events

The Gemara answers: *shani hatam detana bei kra treisar zimnei* — there it is different, because although it was a one-time event, it was repeated twelve times, once for each of the twelve *nesi'im* who each brought the identical offering. By repeating the same act twelve times in succession, the Torah has transformed what would otherwise be a singular *sha'ah* into something that carries the force of a general principle. *Tosafot* notes briefly that one might ask whether twelve repetitions of the same one-time event should instead be treated as *shnei ketuvim haba'im k'echad* — two (or more) texts coming to teach the same thing, which ordinarily signals that the lesson should *not* be generalized — but presumably twelve times constitutes too clear an affirmation of the principle to be dismissed.

The upshot is that Shmuel can indeed learn from a *sha'ah* when it is sufficiently repeated; he cannot learn from Aaron's single act of *kemitza* in *Shemini* precisely because it occurred once and only once.

## Acts Not Me'akev

### Hagasha

With the general framework in place, the Gemara turns to examine specific acts from the *mishna*'s list of things that are not *me'akev* by a *mincha*. Rav Kahana and Rav Assi raise the first challenge to Rav's rule: *veharei hagasha* — what about the act of *hagasha*, bringing the *mincha* vessel into contact with the *mizbe'ach* before the *kemitza*? The *pasuk* says *el pnei hamizbe'ach*, and this act is indeed repeated in *Tzav*. According to Rav's principle, repetition in *Tzav* implies *me'akev*. Yet the *mishna* teaches that *hagasha* is not *me'akev*.

### Location Purpose

The Gemara responds: *hahu likvo'a lo makom hu de'ata* — that repetition in *Tzav* does not serve to establish *me'akev* status. It serves an entirely different purpose: to specify the precise location where the *hagasha* must be performed. The act was already known and required; the *Tzav* repetition adds topographical information.

### Location Details

The relevant *pasuk* in *Tzav* reads: *zot torat hamincha, hakreiv otah bnei Aharon lifnei Hashem el pnei hamizbe'ach* — bring it before God, to the face of the altar. But these two phrases do not obviously point to the same spot. *Lifnei Hashem* — before God — means facing the *heichal*, which lies to the west; this would place the *hagasha* at the western wall of the *mizbe'ach*. *El pnei hamizbe'ach* — the face of the altar — means its front, the side facing the ramp, which is the south. One phrase implies west; the other implies south. A *baraita* resolves the tension: *hagisha bekheren dromit ma'aravit* — one performs the *hagasha* at the southwest corner, thereby satisfying both *lifnei Hashem* and *el pnei hamizbe'ach* simultaneously, *keneged chudah shel keren*, opposite the tip of the horn at that corner.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees with the corner solution. He holds that one need not synthesize the two phrases by choosing a corner; rather, one may read them as offering a choice — either the west or the south. He then applies a hermeneutic rule: *kol makom she'ata motze shnei mikra'ot, echad mekayem atzmo umekayem chaveiro, ve'echad mekayem atzmo umevatel chaveiro* — when two *psukim* each imply a different option, and one option satisfies both *psukim* while the other satisfies only itself at the expense of the other, one chooses the option that satisfies both. The question is: which of the two options — west or south — can satisfy both? Rabbi Eliezer reasons that choosing the west (*lifnei Hashem*) would negate *el pnei hamizbe'ach*, since the west side is not the face of the altar at all. But choosing the south (*el pnei hamizbe'ach*) — *kiyamta lifnei Hashem* — also satisfies being before God. Therefore, the south is the correct location.

### Rav Ashi

Rav Ashi offers a different explanation of how the south satisfies *lifnei Hashem*: *kasvar hai tana kulo mizbe'ach batzafon ka'i* — this *tanna* holds that the entire *mizbe'ach* stands in the northern portion of the *azara*. If the *mizbe'ach* is situated entirely to the north, then its southern face — *pnei hamizbe'ach* — stands precisely at the opening of the *heichal*, directly facing it. In that case, performing *hagasha* at the south of the *mizbe'ach* simultaneously fulfills *el pnei hamizbe'ach* and *lifnei Hashem*, since that position is directly in front of the entrance to the sanctuary.

### Conclusion

The Gemara's answer to Rav Kahana and Rav Assi's challenge is thus complete: *hagasha* is indeed repeated in *Tzav*, but the repetition is not there to signal *me'akev* — it is there to specify location. Whether that location is the southwest corner (per the *baraita*) or the south alone (per Rabbi Eliezer and Rav Ashi), the repetition has a clear explanatory purpose that is independent of *me'akev* status. This establishes the critical methodological point: not every repetition in *Tzav* signals *me'akev*. When the repetition can be explained as serving a different purpose — here, specifying where an act must be done — it does not invoke Rav's principle. The *daf* concludes at this point, with the Gemara set to continue examining the *mishna*'s remaining acts one by one to determine whether each repetition is *me'akev* or serves some other function.
