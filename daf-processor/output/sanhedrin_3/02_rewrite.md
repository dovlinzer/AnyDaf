# Sanhedrin 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dinei Mamonot Reading

### Rabbi Abbahu Exclusion

The *gemara* on Sanhedrin 3b opens by grappling with how to read the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* states that *dinei mamonot* require a *beit din* of three, and then appears to enumerate specifics: *gezelot ve-chavalot* and so on. The difficulty is that this enumeration seems to pass over the classic cases of *dinei mamonot* — the ordinary civil disputes that the Bavli calls *hoda'ot ve-halva'ot*, admissions and loans. The simplest reading would be that the generic phrase *dinei mamonot* refers to precisely those ordinary disputes, and that *gezelot ve-chavalot* then covers the more non-standard cases of civil liability. Rabbi Abbahu, however, rejects this reading. He narrows the scope of *dinei mamonot* in the *mishnah* to refer only to the specific cases enumerated there, thereby deliberately excluding the classic civil disputes of *hoda'ot ve-halva'ot*. Why exclude them? Do they not also require a *beit din* of three? Rabbi Abbahu agrees that they do. The point of the exclusion, as the Bavli explains, is that while *hoda'ot ve-halva'ot* require three judges, they do not require *mumchin*.

### Mumchin Concept

What does *mumchin* mean? Rashi explains it as *semikhah* — the tradition of judicial authority passed down in an unbroken chain from Moshe Rabbeinu, combined with a form of political appointment by the *nasi*. This matters practically: a *beit din* today outside of Eretz Yisrael can issue rulings that carry religious force, but what political authority actually backs up the court's decisions? Rashi notes that *mumchin* possessed authority conferred by the *nasi*. The concept of *mumchin* is intimately linked to the word *elohim*. *Elohim* conveys the idea of representing God, of holding real power and authority — and it is from *elohim* that the requirement of *mumchin* is derived. What Rabbi Abbahu is saying is that this requirement of *mumchin*, rooted in *elohim*, applies to torts — but not to basic civil cases of loans and admissions. Those cases need three judges, but those three do not have to be *mumchin*.

### Eruv Parshiyot

The *gemara* now asks: what is the internal logic of this position? It frames the question sharply: *mai kasavar* — what exactly does the *tanna* of the *mishnah* hold? The *gemara* presents a dilemma grounded in the concept of *eruv parshiyot*. The classic source in the *Torah* for the requirement of a *beit din* of three is the *parshah* dealing with the case of a *shomer* — a bailee who claims that the object entrusted to him was stolen: *ki yiten ish el re'ehu kesef o kelim lishmor vegunav mibet ha'ish im yimatze haganav yeshalem shenayim im lo yimatze haganav venikrav ba'al habayit el ha'elohim im lo shalach yado bimlechet re'ehu* — and the passage continues with *al kol devar pesha al shor al chamor al seh al salma al kol aveda asher yomar ki hu zeh*, followed by *ad ha'elohim yavo devar sheneihem* and *asher yarshi'un elohim yeshalem shenayim lere'ehu*. Three occurrences of *elohim* — three judges. The question is what this *parshah* is actually about.

### Three Elohim

The phrase *ki hu zeh* — "this is it" — requires unpacking. The *gemara* understands this phrase as referring to *modeh b'miktzat*, a partial admission: I claim you owe me three objects, and you acknowledge owing only one of them. The question is whether *ki hu zeh* is addressing the *shomer* case or a loan case. In a standard *shomer* case, the *shomer* swears that something happened — that the item was taken, for instance — and the whole claim may be denied outright; partial admission is not structurally required. Partial admission — *ki hu zeh* — is precisely the mechanism that triggers an oath in the case of a loan: I lent you a hundred, you admit to fifty but deny the rest. This suggests that embedded within the *parshah* of *shomer* is actually a discussion of loan cases. That is *eruv parshiyot* — two distinct *parshiyot* interleaved: one about *shomrim* and one about loans, sewn together in the same passage.

### Gezelah Definition

Why is the *shomer* case a case of *gezelah*? Because the *shomer* is holding onto an object while falsely claiming it was stolen. As Rashi explains in the *mishnah*, if I simply fail to repay a debt, I am wronging the lender, but halachically that is not *gezelah*. The active retention of a specific, concrete piece of property that belongs to someone else — that physical hold — constitutes *gezelah*. The non-payment of a monetary debt, while wrongful, falls into a different category.

### Modeh B'Miktzat

Now the *gemara* returns to the dilemma it raised. If the *tanna* holds that there is *eruv parshiyot* — that loan cases are embedded within this *parshah* — then the word *elohim*, with its connotations of authority, applies to loans as well, and loans should require not only three judges but *mumchin*. If, on the other hand, the *tanna* holds there is no *eruv parshiyot* — that the entire passage is only about the *shomer* — then there is no *Torah* source teaching that loans require three judges at all. The *gemara* poses the challenge directly: *im kasavar eruv parshiyot katuv kan libei nami mumchin; ve'im kasavar ein eruv parshiyot katuv kan shlosha lamah li?* Make up your mind. The *gemara* resolves this by saying: *le'olam kasavar eruv parshiyot katuv kan* — yes, there is *eruv parshiyot*, and in principle loans do require *mumchin*. But the reason the *mishnah* excludes loans from the *mumchin* requirement is *mishum de-Rabbi Chanina* — by analogy to the teaching of Rabbi Chanina.

## Rabbi Chanina Analogy

### Derishah Chakira

Rabbi Chanina teaches: *dvar Torah, echad dinei mamonot ve'echad dinei nefashot b'drisha ve-chakira* — from a *Torah* perspective, both monetary cases and capital cases require rigorous cross-examination of witnesses — *drisha ve-chakira* — pinning down the precise time, place, and circumstances of the witnessed event. The source is *shene'emar mishpat echad yihyeh lachem* — one law for you — a verse that links *dinei mamonot* and *dinei nefashot* in a single standard of judicial procedure.

### Removing Obstacles

Why then did the rabbis remove the requirement of *drisha ve-chakira* from monetary cases? *Kedei shelo tin'ol delet bifnei lovin* — so as not to bolt the door before borrowers. The lender receives no interest and has no obvious benefit from lending. Any additional obstacle — an onerous judicial procedure, witnesses subject to rigorous cross-examination — becomes a plausible reason not to lend. So the rabbis paved the path by removing *drisha ve-chakira* from *dinei mamonot* entirely.

### Tosafot Extension

A natural question arises: does this dispensation apply only to loans, or does it extend across all *dinei mamonot* — including *gezelah*, *chavalot*, and other torts? From the language of Rabbi Chanina's statement, it sounds as though *drisha ve-chakira* is removed from all *dinei mamonot*. Tosafot explains that there is indeed good reason to extend the removal broadly. Just as the smallest obstacle can deter lending, so too with other forms of civil liability: if it becomes too difficult to prosecute someone who damaged your property, people will become careless. My ox will range freely if I know you cannot easily prove damages against me; I will not guard against incidental trespass on your property if I know prosecution is burdensome. *Gezel* will be rampant. So Tosafot reformulates: we remove *drisha ve-chakira* from *dinei mamonot* — including tort cases — *kedei lin'ol delet bifnei gazlanin*, to bolt the door before thieves, making it harder for wrongdoers to escape accountability. The analogy to the American legal system is instructive: criminal conviction requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt, while civil liability requires only a preponderance of the evidence. Different thresholds apply to different proceedings.

By the same token, *mishum de-Rabbi Chanina*: just as *drisha ve-chakira* was removed from all *dinei mamonot* to facilitate civil society, the *mumchin* requirement is removed specifically from loan cases — not from all *dinei mamonot*, since *gezelah* and *chavalot* in the *mishnah* still require *mumchin*, but specifically from loans. Why only loans? Tosafot explains: with loans, I gain nothing from the transaction and the slightest obstacle deters me from lending. The removal of *mumchin* for loans is by rabbinic enactment, motivated precisely by the same concern for access to credit.

### Judicial Liability

The *gemara* then raises a sharp objection. *Ela me'ata ta'u lo yishalmu* — if you are trying to make it as easy as possible for a borrower to find a *beit din*, and you are not requiring *mumchin*, then you should also say that even if these non-*mumchin* judges rule incorrectly and cause a monetary loss, they should not be required to compensate for it. After all, if the judges face the threat of malpractice liability, no responsible person will want to serve. The *gemara* responds: *kol she-kein atana de-itta lifnei loveh* — on the contrary, this is an additional reason in favor of maintaining malpractice liability. If I know that the court handling my loan dispute is irresponsible and unreviewable, I will be that much more reluctant to lend in the first place. To encourage lending, the lender must have confidence that a court which mishandles his case can be held accountable. So even though malpractice liability may reduce the pool of willing judges, removing it would undermine lending even more decisively. This *sugya* thereby introduces, somewhat incidentally, the important principle that a *beit din* composed of *hedyotot* — non-expert judges — is subject to liability for erroneous rulings.

One of the functions of an opening *sugya* in the *gemara* is precisely this: it uses the apparently narrow question of how to read the *mishnah*'s first line to introduce the reader to a panorama of the major themes of the *perek*. In this short passage alone, we have encountered the word *elohim* as the basis for the *beit din*; the concept of *mumchin* derived from *elohim*; the exclusion of *hoda'ot ve-halva'ot* from the *mumchin* requirement; the distinction between *dinei mamonot* and *dinei nefashot* with respect to *drisha ve-chakira*; and the liability of a court composed of *hedyotot*. All of these themes will recur throughout the *perek*.

## Rava's Two Categories

### Challenging Exclusion

Having laid out Rabbi Abbahu's reading, the *gemara* now challenges it directly. *Iy hachi tarti katanei* — if you are going to say there are two different categories here, then why not read the *mishnah* as simply stating both? Read *dinei mamonot bishlosha hedyotot* — monetary cases, meaning loans, need three non-expert judges — and *gezelot ve-chavalot bishlosha mumchin* — torts need three *mumchin*. That is, why force a reading that excludes *hoda'ot ve-halva'ot* from the *mishnah*? Simply read *dinei mamonot* as referring to *hoda'ot ve-halva'ot* and treat the two phrases as two distinct categories, one not requiring *mumchin* and the other requiring *mumchin*.

The *gemara* adds a further challenge: *ve-od shlosha shlosha lamah li?* If *dinei mamonot* and *gezelot ve-chavalot* were meant to refer to the same category, why does the *mishnah* say *bishlosha* twice? The repetition of *bishlosha* strongly implies these are two separate rulings about two separate categories — not a single category with its examples. The plainest reading of the *mishnah*, the *gemara* presses, is that *dinei mamonot* means loans and *gezelot ve-chavalot* means torts, and both independently require three.

### Rava Resolution

*Ella amar Rava: tarti katanei* — Rava agrees: the *mishnah* does state both categories. *Dinei mamonot* refers to loans — three judges, without the *mumchin* requirement. *Gezelot ve-chavalot* refers to torts — three judges, with *mumchin*. The *mishnah* is not excluding loans; it is using *dinei mamonot* as its term for loans. Whether or not loans technically require *mumchin* is a separate debate — Rabbi Abbahu says they do not, and we have seen why — but the *mishnah* is not suppressing the category; it is naming it first. This is clearly the better reading of the *mishnah*, and the *gemara* adopts it through Rava.

### Minimum Size

Having clarified that loans require three judges who need not be *mumchin*, the *gemara* now opens up discussion of whether the requirement of three judges in loan cases is *de-oraita* or rabbinic. Rav Acha b'rei de-Rav Ika says: *mi-de'oraita chad nami kashar* — from the *Torah*'s perspective, even one judge suffices, at least for loan-type cases. The *Torah* source he cites is *be-tzedek tishpot amitecha* — "with justice you shall judge your fellow" — where *tishpot* is in the singular. As Rashi notes, this possibility flows naturally from the earlier discussion: if we concluded that when there is no *eruv parshiyot* loans have no explicit *Torah* source requiring three, then certainly one should be sufficient *de-oraita*.

### Yoshei Kranot

If one judge suffices *de-oraita*, why did the rabbis require three even in loan cases? *Ela mishum yoshei kranot* — because of the *yoshei kranot*, the idlers loitering on street corners. Rashi explains: *tagrin she-ein be-ki'in be-tiv dinin ve-yestu et ha-chayav ve-yachayavu et ha-zakai* — traders who are not expert in the proper conduct of cases, who will acquit the liable and convict the innocent. Once it was established that *mumchin* are not required for loan cases, anyone could volunteer to serve as a judge. Combining the absence of a *mumchin* requirement with the sufficiency of a single judge produces a court that could be, in practice, a single ignoramus. To counteract this, the rabbis raised the threshold to three: with three people convening as a court, at least one of them, presumably, has some idea of what he is doing. The *gemara* immediately asks: *atu be-telata mi lo havei yoshei kranot?* — can you not assemble three good-for-nothings just as easily as one? The *gemara* responds: *iy efshar de-la shechiach chad de-gamir* — it is unlikely that among three people willing to convene as a court, not a single one has any knowledge. One of the three, presumably, knows something. Whether that optimism is always warranted is another matter, but that is the rabbinic rationale.

The same *gemara* that raised the malpractice question earlier now raises it again in this context. If the goal is to encourage responsible people to serve on courts made up of *hedyotot*, perhaps malpractice liability should be removed — otherwise, only the irresponsible will agree to serve. The *gemara* inverts this: *kol she-kein ittefi yoshei kranot* — liability for error will actually deter the *yoshei kranot* from sitting in judgment. A person who recognizes he knows nothing will think twice before exposing himself to financial liability for a mistaken ruling. Far from chasing away the competent, malpractice liability weeds out the incompetent.

### Rava vs Rav Acha

What practical difference then exists between Rava and Rav Acha b'rei de-Rav Ika, if both ultimately require three non-*mumchin* judges for loan cases? The *gemara* answers: *ika beina'ihu de'amar Shmuel* — the difference lies in the ruling of Shmuel. Shmuel teaches: *shenayim she-danu dineihem din, ela she-nikra beit din chatzuf* — if two judges ruled, their ruling is valid after the fact, but they are called an impudent court. The question is whether *bidi'avad* — post facto — a court of fewer than three is valid.

According to Rava, the *Torah* itself requires three judges. Therefore, anything fewer than three — even two — is categorically invalid, and Shmuel's ruling does not apply. According to Rav Acha b'rei de-Rav Ika, who says that *de-oraita* one suffices and the requirement of three is rabbinic, Shmuel's ruling does apply: the rabbis required three, but if two ruled, the ruling stands *bidi'avad*, since the failure is merely rabbinic non-compliance. The court is *chatzuf*, but its ruling is not void. The question naturally arises whether, by the same logic, a single judge ruling *bidi'avad* would also be valid. The *gemara* suggests that even Rav Acha b'rei de-Rav Ika might not extend this *bidi'avad* leniency all the way to one. The principle that *al tehi dan yechidi she'ein dayan yechidi ela echad* — do not judge alone, for the only true sole judge is God — reflects a deep concern about the arrogance of a single person claiming total judicial authority. Moreover, a court of two that reaches a unanimous decision is functionally equivalent to two out of three: had a third been present, those same two would have prevailed. A ruling by two, while improper, mirrors the outcome of a proper court. A single judge has no analogous argument to make.

## Nezek Categories

### Nezek Mention

The *gemara* moves now to later phrases in the *mishnah*: *nezek va-chatzi nezek* — damages and half damages — need three. The *gemara* immediately asks: *nezek hainu chavalot* — isn't *nezek* already implicit in *chavalot*, personal injury? One of the payments in a case of personal injury is *nezek*, damage. And even if *nezek* is understood as property damage rather than bodily harm, if bodily harm requires three, obviously property damage does as well. Why state it separately?

The *gemara* answers: *mishum de-kava'ei le-misni chatzi nezek* — because the *mishnah* needed to teach the case of half damages. Half damages is a non-standard case that may be classified as a fine rather than as compensation. Because it had to introduce that exceptional case, the *mishnah* paired it with full damages in order to provide context, and taught *nezek* first as a transition.

### Chatzi Nezek Nature

The *gemara* then asks: even so, isn't *chatzi nezek* — half damages — still just a form of *chavalot*? It is still paying for damage, even if only half the amount. So why teach it separately? The *gemara* answers: *tani mamona ve-tani knasa* — no, the *mishnah* is distinguishing between monetary compensation and fines. The question of whether *chatzi nezek* is compensation or a fine is a classic debate in Bava Kamma. The case in question is an unhabituated ox that gores for the first time — or the first three times — before it is established as dangerous. The owner pays only half.

One view — *palga nizka mamona* — holds that the owner is genuinely negligent even in this case, and the payment is compensation. He is just over the threshold of liability, but fully liable; we simply went easy on him and required only half. On this view, half damages is still *mamona*. The other view — *palga nizka kansa* — holds that the owner was not quite negligent enough to be fully liable, but because he was so close to the line, the rabbis imposed a fine of half so that he would not escape entirely and become reckless. On this view, half damages is a *knas*, a fine that serves a deterrent function rather than a compensatory one. The *gemara* notes: if *palga nizka kansa*, then the separate mention of *chatzi nezek* makes perfect sense — it is a different legal category from standard compensation. But if *palga nizka mamona*, why mention it separately?

### Graduated Fines

The *gemara* resolves this: *ela aidi de-kava'ei le-misni tashlumei kefel ve-tashlumei arba'a va-chamisha* — because the *mishnah* needed to state the graduated fines of double payment (*kefel*), fourfold (*arba'ah*), and fivefold (*chamisha*). These are the fines for theft and for the theft-and-slaughter or theft-and-sale of an ox or sheep respectively, and everyone agrees these are *knasot* — pure fines, well in excess of compensation, of the category *de-mamona she'eino mishtalem be-rosh*, money that is not simply the base value of what was taken. Since those cases had to be taught, the *mishnah* drew *chatzi nezek* alongside them — even if technically it is *mamona* on one view, it shares the feature of being a non-standard, non-full payment. And once *chatzi nezek* was taught, *nezek* was taught alongside it as the natural transition from the territory of standard torts to the territory of fines. In this way, even under the view that *palga nizka mamona*, the literary structure of the *mishnah* is coherent: *nezek* and *chatzi nezek* serve as the bridge from *gezelot ve-chavalot* to *kefel ve-arba'ah va-chamisha*.

## Source: Three Elohim

### Pesukim Citation

Having worked through the structure of the *mishnah*, the *gemara* now turns to a question that has been implicit throughout: *shlosha minaalan* — where do we know you need three to constitute a *beit din*? The *gemara* cites a *baraita*. The source is the same *parshah* of the *shomer* we encountered earlier — the passage beginning *venikrav ba'al habayit el ha'elohim im lo shalach yado bimlechet re'ehu*, continuing with *al kol devar pesha asher yomar ki hu zeh*, then *ad ha'elohim yavo devar sheneihem*, and finally *asher yarshi'un elohim yeshalem shenayim lere'ehu*. The word *elohim* appears three times. Three *elohim*, three judges.

### Rabbi Yoshiya Count

This, says the *baraita*, is the position of Rabbi Yoshiya. He counts all three occurrences: *venikrav ba'al habayit el ha'elohim* — *harei kan echad*, that is one. *Ad ha'elohim yavo devar sheneihem* — *harei kan shenayim*, that is two. *Asher yarshi'un elohim* — *harei kan shlosha*, that is three. Three *elohim*, three judges.

### Rabbi Yonatan Object

Rabbi Yonatan disagrees: *rishon techilah ne'emar ve'ein dorshin techilot* — the first occurrence is simply the grammatical subject needed to introduce the passage; you do not count first occurrences for halachic derivation. Begin counting only from the second: *ad ha'elohim yavo devar sheneihem* — that is one. *Asher yarshi'un elohim* — that is two. And since a court cannot have an even number of members, *mosifin aleihem od echad harei kan shlosha* — you add one more and arrive at three. Both Tannaim reach the number three; they differ only on how they get there.

The *gemara* asks whether this reflects a general dispute about the hermeneutic principle of *dorshin techilot* — counting the first occurrence of a word. *Mar savar dorshin techilah*, one holds you do count it; *u-mar savar ein dorshin techilah*, the other holds you do not. That seems to be the debate. The *gemara* then says: perhaps not. *Dekulei alma ein dorshin techilah* — perhaps everyone agrees you do not count the first occurrence. How then does Rabbi Yoshiya justify counting it here? *Amar lach Rabbi Yoshiya*: the *Torah* could have written *ve-nikrav ba'al habayit el ha-shofet* — "the householder shall approach the judge" — and subsequently used *elohim*. Instead it used *elohim* all three times. That deliberate repetition of the same word signals that every occurrence is to be counted. Had the *Torah* not wanted us to count the first, it would have used a different word to introduce the subject. Rabbi Yonatan responds: *lishna de'alma nakat* — the *Torah* was simply speaking in a natural, echoing idiom. As people say: *man de'it lei dina likrav legabei dayana* — one who has a legal matter should approach a judge. The phrase uses *dina* and *dayana* — din and its cognate — for stylistic resonance, not because every instance of the word carries halachic weight. Similarly, the *Torah*'s repeated use of *elohim* is a matter of style, not instruction to count. Rashi offers a related explanation, and the *gemara* notes that the simpler reading remains that they are debating the general hermeneutic principle of *dorshin techilot*.

### Even Beit Din

The *gemara* now raises a striking question. If Rabbi Yoshiya derived three judges from the three occurrences of *elohim*, why did he not instead derive two from the second and third occurrences — and then, since a *beit din* cannot be of even number, add one to reach three? That is exactly what Rabbi Yonatan did. Does the fact that Rabbi Yoshiya needed to count three occurrences to reach three suggest that he holds it is in fact permissible to have a *beit din* of even numbers — and therefore two would not automatically yield three? The *gemara* cites a *baraita* in which Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Yosi ha-Gelili derives from the verse *lintos acharei rabim le-hatot* — "incline after the majority" — that a *beit din* must be structured so that it can *noteh*, tilt, that is, it must have an odd number of members in order for a majority to emerge. If this is a universal principle, then why did Rabbi Yoshiya not rely on it to move from two to three?

### Rabbi Yehuda Exception

The *gemara* proposes: *savar lah ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — perhaps Rabbi Yoshiya holds like Rabbi Yehuda. In our *mishnah* in Sanhedrin, Rabbi Yehuda says that the *Sanhedrin Gedolah* consisted of seventy members — *shivim*, not *shivim v'echad*. The standard *tanna kamma* says seventy-one. Rabbi Yehuda, who accepts an even number for the supreme court, might serve as the model for Rabbi Yoshiya's willingness to entertain an even-numbered *beit din*. The *gemara* is skeptical: is Rabbi Yehuda's allowance for an even *Sanhedrin Gedolah* a general principle, or a special ruling limited to that institution? The *gemara* demonstrates that it is strictly limited. A separate *mishnah* discusses *semikhah* — the laying of hands in the cases of *par helam davar* and *egla arufa*. Rabbi Shimon says three; Rabbi Yehuda says five. Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning: the verse says *ve-samkhu*, which implies two, plus *ziknei*, the elders, which implies another two — four in total. But since a *beit din* cannot be of even number, *ein beit din shakol*, we add one, arriving at five. Rabbi Yehuda, then, explicitly requires an odd-numbered court in this context. He never says that a standard *beit din* may be even-numbered. His ruling of *shivim* for the *Sanhedrin Gedolah* is an exceptional case driven by a specific *pasuk* — *shivim zekenim shivim ish* — not a general principle.

### Rabbi Yoshiya Even

How then can Rabbi Yoshiya be aligned with Rabbi Yehuda? The *gemara* resolves this: *Rabbi Yoshiya adifa mi-d'Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yoshiya's position is actually more radical than Rabbi Yehuda's. Rabbi Yehuda holds that even-numbered courts are permitted only for the *Sanhedrin Gedolah*, while all other *batei din* must have odd numbers — as proven by his ruling of five rather than four. Rabbi Yoshiya, by contrast, may hold that even-numbered courts are acceptable across all *batei din*. This is a striking inference. Since Rabbi Yoshiya derived three from three explicit occurrences of *elohim* rather than deriving two and adding one, we must conclude that he could not have relied on the *ein beit din shakol* principle to get from two to three — because for him, two would have been perfectly acceptable. He therefore needed the *Torah* to tell him three explicitly.

### Majority Rule

This leaves Rabbi Yoshiya with a textual problem. The verse *lintos acharei rabim le-hatot* — which Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Yosi ha-Gelili reads as requiring a *noteh*, tilting court, i.e., an odd-numbered court — how does Rabbi Yoshiya understand it? The *gemara* answers: *mookim lah b'dinei nefashot* — Rabbi Yoshiya applies that verse only to capital cases. Capital cases require an odd-numbered court. The *gemara* asks: does Rabbi Yoshiya then say that monetary cases do not follow a majority rule at all? But our *mishnah* explicitly states that if two say acquit and one says convict, he is acquitted — and vice versa. Majority rule clearly operates in *dinei mamonot*. The *gemara* resolves this: *afilu teima Rabbi Yoshiya — maisi lah b'kal va-chomer mi-dinei nefashot*. Rabbi Yoshiya derives the majority rule for *dinei mamonot* by *kal va-chomer* from *dinei nefashot*: *u-mah dinei nefashot de-chamiri amar Rachmana zil basar ruba, dinei mamonot lo kol she-ken* — if the *Torah* says that even a person's life is decided by majority rule, certainly monetary matters are decided by majority rule.

The crucial distinction the *gemara* is drawing is between the procedural principle that courts decide by majority vote — which applies universally — and the structural requirement that a court be constituted of an odd number of members so that a majority can always emerge. The verse *lintos acharei rabim* teaches the former, not the latter. A court of even numbers is structurally problematic — it can result in a deadlock — but Rabbi Yoshiya, on this reading, accepts that risk, at least in *dinei mamonot*, and relies on the derivation from the three occurrences of *elohim* to establish the minimum of three rather than on the structural necessity of an odd-numbered court. This is admittedly a position resting on thin evidence, and the *gemara* itself treats it with some tentativeness, but the logical structure is coherent: majority rule as a decisional principle is one thing, and the compositional requirement of an odd-numbered court is another.
