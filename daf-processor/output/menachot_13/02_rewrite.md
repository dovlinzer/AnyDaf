# Menachot 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pigul & Half-Measures

### Intro & Half-Measures

Daf 13 opens not with entirely new material but rather as a continuation of the ongoing treatment of *pigul*, the disqualification arising from an improper intent during a sacrificial rite. We had just completed a digression concerning the effect of a *pigul* thought when the full measure of *shirayim* is no longer present — a digression that resolved nothing conclusively. We now return to the central mechanics of *pigul* as articulated in the *mishna* on 12b.

The *mishna* presents the following case: if a *kohen* performing the *kemitzah* — the scooping of the *kometz* from the *mincha* — intends to eat half a *kezayit* of the *shirayim* at the wrong time and to burn half a *kezayit* of the *kometz* at the wrong time, the offering is *kasher*. Not only is it not *pigul*, it is entirely valid, because neither intention, taken alone, constitutes a meaningful act — a half-*kezayit* of eating does not reach the requisite threshold, and a half-*kezayit* of burning similarly does not. Moreover, the *mishna* adds, *she'ein achilah ve-haktarah mitztarpin* — eating and burning do not combine with one another.

### Eating vs. Burning

The reasoning behind this ruling deserves careful unpacking. If instead of combining eating with burning, one were to combine two eating-thoughts — intending to eat half a *kezayit* of the *shirayim* on Tuesday and another half on Wednesday — that might well combine, though questions about *k'dei achilat pras* and how thought combines actions remain. The critical point is that we are dealing there with two acts of the same type, yielding a combined eating of a *kezayit*. But here we have an eating and a burning — two different categories of activity, each insufficient on its own, and the *mishna* rules they do not combine because they are separate and distinct acts.

### Tanna Identification

The *Gemara* proceeds to analyze the *mishna*'s implication. The reason eating and burning do not combine is, as the *mishna* says, that they are fundamentally different activities. But the *Gemara* notes that this framing carries an implicit inference: *ha le'echol u'le'echol davar she'ein darko le'echol* — if instead one combined two eating-thoughts, even eating something not normally eaten, namely the *kometz* itself, it sounds as though that would combine and create *pigul*. Yet this inference conflicts with an earlier *mishna*, which specified that one is only liable for *pigul* if one intends to eat *davar she'darko le'echol* — something normally consumed, i.e., the *shirayim* — not the *kometz*, which is destined for the altar. Why, then, does the present *mishna* appear to imply otherwise?

The *Gemara* answers: *man tanna* — who is the *tanna* of this *mishna* that would suggest eating of the *kometz* might combine with eating of the *shirayim*? Rabbi Yirmiyah identifies the *tanna* as Rabbi Eliezer, citing a *mishna* appearing several folios later: *hakometz et haminchah le'echol davar she'ein darko le'echol lehaktir davar she'ein darko lehaktir kasher* — if the *kometz* is taken with intent to eat the *kometz* (not normally eaten) or to burn the *shirayim* (not normally burned), it is *kasher* according to the first *tanna*, but Rabbi Eliezer rules it invalid. Rabbi Eliezer holds that even a thought directed at an unusual act with the object — eating the *kometz* or burning the *shirayim* — constitutes at minimum some form of *achilah* and thus renders the offering invalid, even if it does not rise to classic *pigul*. It is Rabbi Eliezer's view, then, that underlies the inference that eating and eating, even where one involves an improper object, would combine.

## Abaye's Reinterpretation

### Abaye Rejects Inference

Abaye rejects this inference entirely. *Afilu teima Rabbanan* — it is possible to attribute the *mishna* even to the Rabbis, without resorting to Rabbi Eliezer. Abaye argues that the *diyuk* — the inference drawn from the *mishna*'s phrasing — is unjustified. One should not read the *mishna* as implying that combining two eating-thoughts, even where one involves the *kometz*, would suffice. Rather, Abaye says, the correct implicit comparison would be: *achilah ve-achilah davar she'darko le'achilah* — eating and eating where both involve something normally eaten.

### Previous Mishna Cited

But if that is the *mishna*'s implication, what novel ruling does it teach? That two genuine eating-thoughts combine even when they represent different categories of invalidity — one a thought of wrong time and one a thought of wrong place — is already made explicit in the previous *mishna*: *kezayit bachutz u'kezayit lemachar, kezayit lemachar u'kezayit bachutz, kechatzi zayit bachutz ve'chatzi zayit lemachar, kechatzi zayit lemachar ve'chatzi zayit bachutz — pasul ve'ein bo karet*. Even half a *kezayit* of wrong-place intent combined with half a *kezayit* of wrong-time intent invalidates the offering, though without *karet*, because it does not constitute a full *kezayit* of a single type of *pigul*. This demonstrates that two eating-thoughts of different types combine to invalidate — and that is already stated explicitly. There is no need to infer it from our *mishna*.

### Simple Meaning Prevails

The *Gemara* on 13a then asks: *ha tu lama li* — why, then, do I need the present *mishna* at all? If the implication about combining eating-and-eating of the *shirayim* is already explicit, and the implication about combining eating of the *shirayim* with eating of the *kometz* is negated by the *reisha* of the earlier *mishna*, what is the present *mishna* teaching?

The *Gemara* resolves: *le'echol u'lehaktir itztricha* — it is necessary to state explicitly that eating and burning do not combine. One might have thought otherwise. It is true that eating the *kometz* does not combine with eating the *shirayim*, because eating the *kometz* is a wholly meaningless act — so the second eating contributes nothing. But the burning of the *kometz*? That is a perfectly meaningful and appropriate act. And eating the *shirayim* is equally meaningful. Perhaps when both acts are individually significant — each *ki orchiah*, each performed in its proper manner — they combine to form a composite act totaling a *kezayit*. Therefore the *mishna* must explicitly tell us: *le'echol u'lehaktir*, eating-intent and burning-intent do not combine, even when both are directed at the correct object.

The *Gemara*'s conclusion, then, is that there are no valid *diyukim* to be drawn from this *mishna* beyond its plain ruling. One is only liable for *pigul* if one intends to eat the *shirayim* or to burn the *kometz* at the wrong time. Directing one's intent at the wrong activity with the object — burning the *shirayim* or eating the *kometz* — counts for nothing. And even when the activity is correct, two separate categories of act — eating and burning — cannot be combined even when each is individually meaningful. That is the *pshat*, and the inferential readings do not stand.

## Rashbam's Note

### Rashbam Commentary

At this point — following the *Hadran alach kol haminachot* that concludes the *perek* — Daf 13 is strikingly short. The reason becomes apparent when one looks at what appears in the position of Rashi: a substantial explanatory note bearing the heading *dikduk sheperishu Harav Meir ve-Harav Shmuel beno*, the analytical commentary of Rav Meir and his son Rav Shmuel — that is, the Rashbam, Rabbi Shmuel ben Meir, grandson of Rashi, and the elder brother of Rabbeinu Tam. The colophon at the end confirms: *ad kan hadikduk sheperish Rav Shmuel ve'aviv Harav Meir*.

This piece is not Rashi's commentary; it is a standalone explanatory note by the Rashbam (with his father) on the *sugya* treated in the previous daf. The editors of the page included it alongside the Rashi text because it functions as running commentary on the *Gemara*, much as the Rashbam's commentary on Bava Batra does. In truth, the Rashbam does not introduce the kinds of probing cross-tractate questions characteristic of Tosafot; this is straight *perush*. What the Rashbam does here is re-explain the *sugya* analyzed in yesterday's shiur — the difficult passage where Rava derived from Rabbi Chiyya's omission of the word *kezayit* that he was speaking of the *shirayim*. The Rashbam's explanation adjusts one nuance in how that proof operates and thereby renders it somewhat more intelligible, though the difficulty was not entirely resolved even with this correction.

It is a fascinating editorial decision to include an independent piece by later *Rishonim* within the Rashi column on the page. In one sense it illustrates the broader phenomenon of textual difficulty in *Kodashim* — a tractate that was not widely studied and whose manuscripts were correspondingly less reliable. As Tosafot itself noted with respect to the text of this very *sugya*, whole stretches were corrupted beyond use, requiring scholars to copy in the parallel text from Zevachim where the same *sugya* appears. The Rashbam's inclusion here reflects a similar editorial impulse: to restore and clarify. In any case, it is the presence of this Rashbam note that accounts for the brevity of the actual *Gemara* on this daf.

## Levona & Matir Principle

### Levona Case

With the new *perek*, we remain firmly in the world of *pigul* — as we learned from Zevachim, once the *Gemara* enters the topic of *pigul*, it stays with it at length. The new *mishna* opens with a case that at first glance seems straightforward: *hakometz et haminchah le'echol shireha o lehaktir kumtza lemachar* — if the *kohen* performs the *kemitzah* with intent to eat the remainder tomorrow or to burn the *kometz* tomorrow, these are classic *pigul* thoughts, and *modeh Rabbi Yose b'zeh she'hu pigul* — Rabbi Yose agrees that this constitutes *pigul* and one is liable to *karet*. The *mishna* finds it necessary to state this agreement explicitly because Rabbi Yose is about to dispute a related case, and the editor wants to make clear from the outset that his dispute is limited and that he accepts the foundational principle of *pigul*.

The disputed case follows: *lehaktir levonah lemachar* — if one performs the *kemitzah* with intent to burn the *levona* tomorrow. Here the improper intent is directed not at the *kometz* or the *shirayim*, but at the *levona* that accompanies the *mincha*. Rabbi Yose rules: *pasul ve'ein bo karet* — the offering is invalidated, but there is no *karet*, because this does not constitute *pigul*. The Rabbis disagree: *pigul vechayavin alav karet* — it is full *pigul*, and one is liable to *karet*.

The Rabbis challenge Rabbi Yose: *mah shina zeh min hazevach* — how is this different from an animal sacrifice? In a *korban*, one can have a *pigul* thought not only about eating the meat but also about burning the *imurim* — the fats. Just as the *pigul* thought encompasses all elements whose improper disposition would be problematic, so too here, why should a thought about the *levona* not constitute *pigul*? Rabbi Yose replies: *hazevach damo u'vesaro ve'imurav echad* — in an animal sacrifice, the blood, the meat, and the entrails are all one unified entity. *Halevona eina min hamincha* — the *levona* is not part of the *mincha*. From his response, the plain reading of the *mishna* appears to be that Rabbi Yose's objection is grounded in the *levona* being conceptually a different object from the *mincha* itself. Though the *levona* is physically placed upon the *mincha* in the same vessel and must be brought along with it, the Torah defines it as something riding on top — a distinct object — and therefore a *pigul* thought cannot be generated by directing one's intent toward it.

This would have broader implications. Consider a *shlamim* offering brought together with loaves of bread: if one slaughters the *shlamim* with intent to eat the bread at the wrong time, would that constitute *pigul*? Or the *nesachim* — if one had a *pigul* thought about the libation? These cases would follow from Rabbi Yose's principle, and the *Gemara* will press on exactly what that principle is.

### Reish Lakish Explains

The *Gemara* turns to explain Rabbi Yose's position. *Amar Reish Lakish: omer haya Rabbi Yose* — Reish Lakish formulates what Rabbi Yose's reasoning was. The phrasing *omer haya*, as Rashi notes several lines later (*klomar hainu ta'ama de-Rabbi Yose*), does not indicate a direct tradition but rather Reish Lakish's interpretive reconstruction of the *ta'am*, the underlying rationale.

Reish Lakish proposes: *ein matir mefagel et hamatir* — one *matir* cannot create *pigul* in another *matir*. The principle of *pigul* operates when one performs an act with the *matir* — the element that renders the offering permissible — and directs improper intent toward what is permitted or toward the *matir* itself. The *kometz*, for example, is a *matir*: its burning renders the *shirayim* permissible for consumption. A *pigul* thought during the *kemitzah* can therefore be directed at eating the *shirayim* (the thing being permitted), or at the *kometz* itself (the *matir* performing the act). What it cannot do is extend to another *matir* that operates independently. The *levona* is also a *matir* for the *mincha* — it too must be burned as part of the process that validates the *mincha*. But because the *levona* and the *kometz* are two independent *matirim*, neither dependent on the other, one's thought during the *kemitzah* cannot generate *pigul* with respect to the *levona*.

The logic is elegant: the *pigul* thought during an act is relevant to what that act affects. The burning of the *kometz* affects the *shirayim* — the *shirayim* are dependent on the *kometz*, *dechama dela mitkatar kometz la mitchalu shirayim*, until the *kometz* is burned the *shirayim* cannot be eaten. My intent during the *kemitzah* is thus relevant to the *shirayim* and to the *kometz* itself. But it is not relevant to the *levona*, because the *levona* does not stand in a dependency relationship with the *kometz*. There is no chain of enabling: the *kometz* does not render the *levona* permissible or valid, and the *levona* does not render the *kometz* permissible or valid. They are two parallel paths, each contributing independently to the validity of the *mincha*.

### Two Bowls Case

Reish Lakish then applies this principle to a parallel case: *bi-shnei bezichei levona shel lechem hapanim* — the two bowls of *levona* accompanying the *lechem hapanim*, the showbread. There, two separate spoons of frankincense are burned, and their burning renders the bread permissible. Reish Lakish says that here too, *she'ein matir mefagel et hamatir* — if while burning one bowl one intends to burn the other bowl tomorrow, that does not create *pigul*. Even though the two bowls are the same physical substance — both *levona* — they function as two distinct *matirim*, neither dependent on the other. Burning one bowl does not affect the status of the other bowl; combining them makes the bread valid, but neither enables the other.

The *Gemara* notes, however, that one might have thought differently. Perhaps Rabbi Yose's ruling in the *mishna* is specific to the *levona* of the *mincha* because it is a conceptually distinct object — not the same *min* as the *mincha* flour — and *mishum delav mina demincha hi*, for that reason specifically it cannot generate *pigul*. In that case, the two bowls of *levona* for the *lechem hapanim*, being homogeneous with one another, might combine, and *pigul* thought from one might affect the other. The *Gemara* presses: *mahu deteima*... and then challenges the very premise of this reading.

## Dependency Analysis

### Rabbi Yose's Real Reason

The *Gemara* asks: *umi matzit amrat deta'ama de-Rabbi Yose belevonah lav mishum delav mina demincha hi* — how can you say Rabbi Yose's reason is simply that the *levona* is not the same type of object as the *mincha*? The *mishna* itself seems to say exactly that — Rabbi Yose's own reply to the Rabbis was *halevona eina min hamincha*, and the contrast he drew was between the unified blood-flesh-entrails complex of an animal sacrifice and the *levona* as an external element. Does the *mishna* not support reading his rationale as purely categorical — it's a different object?

The *Gemara* deflects: *mai einah mina demincha* — what does it mean that the *levona* is "not of the *mincha*"? It means *einah be'ikuv mincha* — it does not stand in the dependency relationship that characterizes the *mincha* itself.

### No Dependency Relation

This is the crucial reinterpretation. When Rabbi Yose says the *levona* is not of the *mincha*, he is not making a purely taxonomic observation about physical distinctness. He is saying it does not operate the way the *mincha* operates internally. The *mincha* is dependent on its own *kometz*: *dechama dela mitkatar kometz la mitchalu shirayim*, until the *kometz* is burned the *shirayim* cannot be eaten. The *kometz* holds back the *shirayim* — *ma'akev lahem*. The *levona*, by contrast, does not hold back the *kometz* and is not held back by it. *Halevona* is not *be'ikuv* the *mincha* in that sense. The dependency chain that runs from *kometz* to *shirayim* does not extend to the *levona*.

### Order Independence

The *Gemara* makes this concrete: *ela i bei hai maktar bereisha* — if one wishes, one may burn the *kometz* first; if one wishes, one may burn the *levona* first. The order is entirely interchangeable. Neither one is a *matir* for the other. Each stands independently in relation to the validity of the *mincha*, but they are not ordered or dependent with respect to each other. The *levona* is thus *lav mina demincha* not because it is a foreign object, but because it lacks the internal dependency relationship that characterizes the *mincha's* own components.

This understanding resolves what would otherwise be a tension in Rabbi Yose's position. If his rationale were purely about physical or categorical distinctness, one might challenge him with cases where objects of different types are nonetheless conceptually unified. But if his rationale is dependency — whether one *matir* enables or holds back another — then the principle becomes *ein matir mefagel et hamatir* in its precise form: because the *levona* and the *kometz* do not stand in a dependency relationship, a *pigul* thought during one act cannot generate *pigul* with respect to the other.

---

## Rabbis' Position

### Same Vessel Logic

The Rabbis agree with Rabbi Yose's underlying principle. *Ki amrinan ein matir mefagel et hamatir* — the *Gemara* concedes that the Rabbis accept the rule that one *matir* does not create *pigul* in another *matir*. They apply this rule themselves when analyzing the case of the two lambs of *Shavuot* that accompany the *shtei halechem*: those two lambs are equivalent *matirim* for the bread, neither dependent on the other, and therefore a *pigul* thought from one lamb cannot affect the other. On that case, the Rabbis and Rabbi Yose would agree.

But the Rabbis part ways with Rabbi Yose on the *levona* of the *mincha*. *Hani mili heicha dela ikvu behad mana* — the principle that one *matir* cannot generate *pigul* in another applies only when the two *matirim* are not placed together in one vessel. *Aval hacha de'ikvu behad mana, kechad dami* — here, where the *levona* is physically laid upon the *mincha* in the same vessel and sanctified together with it, they are treated as one. The Rabbis are saying: it is not that we disagree with the principle of dependency; we disagree about whether the *levona* qualifies as sufficiently integrated with the *mincha* to be treated as part of it. Because the *levona* is placed directly on the *mincha* in the same *kli*, it is conceptually unified with the *mincha* — and therefore a *pigul* thought during the *kemitzah* is relevant to the *levona* as well, yielding full *pigul* and *karet*. This is precisely what the *mishna* records: *vachachamim omrim pigul vechayavin alav karet*.

## Likut Levona Digression

### Rabbi Yanai's View

At this point the *Gemara* introduces a related digression that connects directly to the preceding analysis of the *levona*'s status. *Amar Rabbi Yanai: likut levona bezar pasul* — if a non-*kohen* gathers the *levona* off the surface of the *mincha* before handing it to the *kohen* for burning, this is invalid. The underlying question is whether *likut levona* — the physical act of gathering the *levona* from the *mincha* — constitutes an *avodah*, a formal sacrificial act, at all. The Torah does not describe this act explicitly; it simply states that the *levona* shall be burned. Yet before it can be burned it must be lifted from the *mincha*, and Rabbi Yanai rules that this act is invalid when performed by a *zar*.

### Rav Yirmiyah on Havicha

Rav Yirmiyah provides the conceptual basis for this ruling: *mishum holacha nag'u bah* — the gathering of the *levona* is governed by the category of *holacha*, conveying the offering toward the *mizbe'ach*. The act of picking up the *levona* from the *mincha* constitutes moving it — even a small displacement — in the direction of its ultimate destination on the *mizbe'ach*, and such conveyance is an *avodah*. *Kasavar holacha shelo beregel shemah holacha* — he holds that conveyance even without walking, a mere repositioning, qualifies as *holacha*. And since *holacha* is an *avodah* that requires a *kohen*, performing it as a *zar* renders the offering *pasul*.

## Four Avodot Analysis

### Four Avodot Listed

The *Gemara* now cites the *klal* from an earlier *mishna* as a springboard for deeper analysis. The *mishna* had stated: *kol hakometz ve-noten bikli ve-hamolich ve-hamaktir* — any of the four acts of the *mincha* — the *kemitzah*, placing in a vessel, conveying to the *mizbe'ach*, and burning — can serve as the moment at which a *pigul* thought takes effect. These four acts are the primary *avodot* of the *mincha*. The *Gemara* now examines why each one qualifies.

*Bishlama kometz* — it is clear why *kemitzah* is an *avodah*: *heinu shochet*, it parallels *shechitah* in a *korban behemah*, the act of separation that gains access to the element being offered. *Molich nami heinu molich* — conveying the *kometz* to the *mizbe'ach* parallels the *holacha* of the blood in an animal sacrifice. *Maktir — heinu zorek* — burning the *kometz* on the *mizbe'ach* parallels *zerikah*, the sprinkling of the blood, in that both are the culminating act of getting the offering onto the *mizbe'ach*, whether by throwing or by burning.

### Noten Bikli Debate

The difficulty is with *noten bikli* — placing the *kometz* into the vessel. *Ella noten bikli mai ka'avid* — why is this an *avodah*? The natural answer would be: *ilima mishum de'dami lekabbalah* — it parallels *kabbalah*, the receiving of the blood in a vessel. But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *mi dami* — are they really comparable? *Hatam mimmeila, hacha kashakul ve'ramei* — in *kabbalah*, the blood flows into the vessel on its own; here the *kohen* picks up the *kometz* and actively deposits it. The underlying activity is fundamentally different. *Kabbalah* is receiving; *noten bikli* is placing. These are not the same act.

### Necessity Principle

The *Gemara* then proposes an alternative rationale: *ella mishum de'keivan de'lo sagi lei de'lo avid lei* — since *noten bikli* is necessary, since the *kometz* requires *kedushat kli* (sanctification through placement in a vessel), the act is thereby significant. *Avodah chashuvah hi* — a necessary act is by definition an important act. *Al korchach meshavei lei ke'kabbalah* — its very necessity assimilates it to *kabbalah*, making it a pseudo-parallel even without a formal structural equivalence.

This raises a fundamental question in *lomdus*: what makes something an *avodah* in this context? Is an *avodah* defined by membership in the four formally established categories — the classical structure of *korban* procedure mapped onto the *mincha*? Or can any act that is necessary to accomplish the final result achieve the status of *avodah* by virtue of its necessity alone? The implications are significant. If necessity alone is sufficient, then *likut levona* — which is undeniably necessary — would also be an *avodah*, requiring a *kohen* and susceptible to *pigul* thought. If, on the other hand, an act must fit a defined category, then *noten bikli*'s status depends on whether it genuinely parallels *kabbalah*, and *likut levona* would require a separate analysis.

---

## Noten Bikli Resolution

### Noten Bikli = Kabbala

The *Gemara* now reverses course. *Le'olam de'dami lekabbalah* — in truth, *noten bikli* is like *kabbalah*, and that is the correct reason for its status as an *avodah*. Necessity alone does not elevate an act into the category of *avodah*. The *Gemara* therefore rejects the necessity-based rationale and returns to the structural parallel.

### Same Goal, Different Method

But what of the original objection — that *kabbalah* and *noten bikli* are mechanically different, one passive and one active? The *Gemara* dismisses it: *ravaihu kedushat kli hu* — both acts accomplish the same thing, namely getting the offering into the *kli* and thereby conferring *kedushat kli*. *Mah li mimila mah li kashakul ve'ramei* — what difference does it make whether it happens by itself or whether the *kohen* actively places it in? The end result — the *kometz* in a sanctified vessel — is identical. The means by which it gets there, whether the blood flows in or the *kometz* is placed, is not the defining criterion. What defines *kabbalah* as an *avodah* is that it is the act by which the relevant substance enters the vessel; *noten bikli* achieves precisely the same thing, and the difference in execution does not disqualify the parallel.

This resolution carries an important implication for *likut levona*. If an *avodah* requires a genuine structural parallel to the defined categories — not mere necessity — then the question is whether *likut levona* fits any such category. Rav Yirmiyah's answer was that it is a form of *holacha*. But a distinct possibility is that it parallels *kemitzah* more than *holacha*, since like *kemitzah* it is the act of separating the relevant element for its ultimate disposition on the *mizbe'ach*. The *Gemara*'s framing — *al korchach meshavei lei ke'holacha* — ultimately categorizes it under *holacha*, but the logical basis, as noted, seems more akin to a parallel with *kemitzah*. In any case, what emerges clearly is that the *Gemara* refuses to ground *avodah* status in necessity alone; what matters is whether the act fits within a recognized and structurally parallel category.

## Shtei Halechem Case

### Mishna Setup

The *Gemara* turns to a new *mishna* concerning the *shtei halechem* — the two loaves brought on *Shavuot* — and the two lambs that accompany them. *Shachat sheni kevasim le'echol achas min hachalos lemachar* — one slaughtered the two lambs with the intent to eat one of the loaves of the *shtei halechem* the next day. Alternatively, the same case arises with the *lechem hapanim*: one offered the two *bezichin* of *levona* intending to eat one column of the *lechem hapanim* tomorrow. In each scenario, the act was performed entirely — both lambs were slaughtered, or both bowls were offered — but the improper intent was directed only at one of the two loaves (or one column of the *lechem hapanim*), not both.

### Rabbi Yose's Position

*Rabbi Yose omer: oto hachalah ve'oto haseder shechishav alav — pigul, ve'chayavin alav karet; veha'sheni pasul ve'ein bo karet.* Rabbi Yose rules that the specific loaf or column about which one harbored the improper intent becomes *pigul*, and one who eats from it is liable to *karet*. But the second loaf, toward which no improper thought was directed, is merely *pasul* — invalid — without *karet*. Rabbi Yose's position is consistent with his general approach: *pigul* attaches specifically to the object of one's intent, not to the collective.

### Rabbis' Position

*Ve-chachamim omrim: zeh va-zeh pigul ve-chayavin alav karet* — the Rabbis rule that both loaves become *pigul* and carry *karet*. When one performs the act with an improper *pigul* intent regarding any part of the offering, the entire offering is infected. The dispute thus turns on whether *pigul* can be atomized to the specific object of one's thought, or whether it attaches to the whole when the foundational act — the *shechitah* of the lambs — is performed with improper intent.

## Specific Intent Principle

### Rav Huna Explains

*Amar Rav Huna: omer haya Rabbi Yose — pigul beyerech shel yamin lo nitpagel hayerech shel smol.* Rav Huna's formulation of Rabbi Yose's position is striking and goes well beyond what the *mishna* alone might suggest. Rabbi Yose holds that *pigul* applies only to the precise object of one's thought. It is not merely that distinct physical objects — two separate loaves — are unaffected by a *pigul* thought directed at only one. Even within a single animal, if one intends to eat the right thigh at the wrong time, the left thigh does not become *pigul* — it is merely *pasul*. The *pigul* is radically localized to the very thing one thought about.

### Thought = Action Logic

The *Gemara* provides a logical justification for this extreme position: *lo adifa machshava mimaaseh* — thought is no better than action. The principle underlying *pigul* is that the improper mental act is treated like the corresponding physical act. So one asks: what would happen if the corresponding physical act were performed only on the right thigh? If, for example, one left the right thigh beyond the allotted time, would it make the left thigh into *notar*? It would not. The *notar* status would attach to the right thigh, not the left. If the physical act is limited in its effect to the object on which it was performed, the thought should likewise be limited to the object toward which it was directed. Thought cannot be more powerful in its reach than action.

### Textual Source

The *Gemara* also cites a textual basis: *vehanefesh ha'ochelet mimenu avona tisa* — the soul that eats *from it* bears its sin. The word *mimenu* — from it, from that specific object — implies that liability attaches only when one eats from the very thing one thought about, not from the associated items that were not the object of the improper intent.

## Baraita Challenge

### Baraita Cited

Rav Nachman challenges Rav Huna from a *baraita*: *le'olam ein bo karet ad she'yefagel bi-shteihen bi-kezayit* — one is never liable to *karet* unless one had a *pigul* thought regarding both of the loaves together, with a combined *kezayit*. This appears to say that one must have harbored the improper intent concerning both loaves — and furthermore that a half-*kezayit* from this loaf and a half-*kezayit* from that loaf can combine to constitute the *kezayit* of *pigul*. If so, the two loaves are being treated as a single unit for this purpose, and thought directed at one does extend to the other.

### Tanna Identified

The *Gemara* identifies whose view this *baraita* reflects. *Bishteihen in — be'achat mehen lo*: one is liable only when both loaves were the object of the improper thought; if one thought only about one of them, no *karet* applies when eating from the other. This cannot be the Rabbis' position — the Rabbis hold that both loaves become *pigul* even if one thought only about one, and one would certainly be liable for eating from either. The *baraita* must therefore reflect Rabbi Yose's position.

## One Unit vs. Distinct

### One Unit Theory

The *Gemara* now offers an elegant resolution to the apparent tension. *I amarta bi-shlama chad gufa hu, mishum hachi mitztaref* — if you say Rabbi Yose's position is that *pigul* thought directed at one part of a unified entity affects the whole, and that his dispute with the Rabbis in the *mishna* concerns whether the two loaves of the *shtei halechem* constitute one unit or two distinct units — then the following follows naturally. When one intends to eat half a *kezayit* from this loaf and half a *kezayit* from that loaf, one is in one's own mind treating them as a single collection. One is thinking about a combined consumption of a *kezayit* that spans both. On Rabbi Yose's theory, this mental unification can indeed work: because one conceived of them as one unit in one's improper intent, the two half-*kezayit* measures combine, and that combined *kezayit* generates *pigul* across both loaves. This is how the *baraita* makes sense — it is Rabbi Yose's view, and it is consistent with his underlying principle.

### Distinct Objects Theory

But if instead one adopts the more extreme reading of Rabbi Yose's position — *ela i amarta trei gufei ninhu* — that Rabbi Yose holds everything to be fundamentally and irreducibly distinct, such that even physically connected parts of one animal are treated as separate objects, then it becomes very difficult to explain how the two half-*kezayit* measures could combine at all. *Mi mitztarfei* — how would they join? If the right thigh and the left thigh of a single animal are distinct objects for *pigul* purposes, then certainly a half-*kezayit* from one loaf and a half-*kezayit* from another loaf — which are unquestionably separate physical entities — cannot combine into a single *kezayit* of improper intent. On this reading, the *baraita*'s allowance for combination becomes difficult to account for. This tension is left for continuation in the following session.
