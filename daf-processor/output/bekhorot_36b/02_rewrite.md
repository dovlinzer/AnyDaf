# Bekhorot 36b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen's Testimony: Gidul

### Trust & Testimony

The daf begins on 36b in the midst of a broader discussion concerning the trustworthiness of a *kohen* who makes claims about how a *bechor* acquired its *mum*. The *mishnah* already established that a *kohen* may be believed when he says that a *chacham* confirmed the *mum* to be genuine. The reason this trust is extended is straightforward: while a *kohen* is certainly suspect of having deliberately inflicted a *mum* on a *bechor* — since doing so enables him to slaughter and eat it outside the *Beis Hamikdash* — he is not suspected of lying about whether the *mum* is halakhically valid. His entire motivation for creating the *mum* is to be able to eat it lawfully, and eating *kodshim bachutz* carries the penalty of *kares*; he would not risk that by relying on a false *mum*. His suspicion runs in the opposite direction: toward how the *mum* came to be, not whether it is real.

The question the *gemara* has been probing is therefore more pointed: do we believe a *kohen* when he claims that a *yisrael* gave him the *bechor* already bearing the *mum*? The force of the claim turns on the principle of *avida legiluyei* — that the matter can be checked. If a claim can be verified, the *gemara* reasons, it lends credibility to the person making it. This principle, as we shall see, carries implications far beyond the laws of *bechor*.

It is worth situating this discussion in its broader context. The general rule in *issur ve-heter* is that people are believed about the status of things under their supervision. Trusting a restaurant owner about the *kashrus* of his establishment, even though he has a financial interest, is the norm, not the exception. The various qualifications and suspicions discussed in this chapter are the exceptions. Rabbi Meir goes so far as to suspect the entire class of *kohanim* regarding *bechorot*, but the *halakhic* consensus narrows the suspicion considerably: we distrust a *kohen* only about his own *bechor* when he has direct personal interest. We do not distrust family members, and we do not distrust him if he was the one who first informed us that the animal was a *bechor* at all.

There is a separate but related question about whether a *chacham* may rule on his own *bechor*. The *gemara* earlier addressed this and raised the general issue: is there a rule that a sage may not render decisions that affect himself? Some hold that whenever something begins with a *chazakas issur* — a presumptive status of prohibition requiring a positive act to remove it, as in the case of *bechor* — a sage cannot rule for himself and must seek an outside opinion. Others hold this is the exception rather than the rule. In either reading, however, the *gemara* in our chapter functions as a set of carefully defined exceptions to generally permissive defaults.

### Gidul Transmission

The *gemara* resumes on 36b, three lines from the top. Rabbi Yirmiyahu bar Abba asks: *manai lei le-Rabbi Yehuda ha* — where did Rabbi Yehuda derive the teaching that a *kohen* is believed when he says a *yisrael* gave him the *bechor* with the *mum* already in place, on the grounds that the matter can be revealed? He answers: *ha'ana be-Gidul kaveiti* — I taught this to someone named Gidul, or perhaps "to a great one," and *ve-Gidul kava bedidi* — Gidul went ahead and transmitted it further, eventually reaching Rabbi Yehuda, but something was lost in transmission. The original teaching, as Rabbi Yirmiyahu bar Abba stated it, was not about a *kohen* at all.

What he actually said was: *ne'eman Yisrael lomar: bechor zeh natati le-Kohen be-mumo* — a *yisrael* is believed to say, pointing to an animal in a *kohen*'s possession, "that is the *bechor* I gave him, and I gave it to him with that *mum*." This is entirely different. The *yisrael* has no vested interest whatsoever. We have already established that a *yisrael* is trusted regarding his own *bechor*, and certainly he is trusted about a *bechor* now belonging to a *kohen*.

### Katan ve-Higdil

The *gemara* presses: if the teaching is simply that a *yisrael* is believed about an animal he gave to a *kohen*, why state the obvious? The answer is that the case involves *katan ve-higdil* — the *yisrael* gave the *kohen* a lamb, and it has since grown into a ram. One might have thought that the *yisrael* is simply misidentifying the animal — he gave a small creature and is now pointing at a large one. The teaching comes to tell us that we are not concerned about this kind of misidentification. The *gemara* notes that the original teaching had nothing to do with *avida legiluyei* at all; it was about the reliability of identification across the animal's growth.

One might observe that the *gemara*'s use of the term *be-gidul* — "through growing" — in describing how the teaching was transmitted to Gidul possibly plays on the word *gidul* itself, hinting that the subject matter concerned something that grew. That, however, is not how Rashi reads it.

### Sura vs Pumbedita

The *gemara* reports a geographic divergence in how this teaching was transmitted. *Besura matnu le-ha shemata* — in Sura they taught the tradition according to the second explanation, that it concerns a *yisrael* identifying his own animal. *Bepumbedita kelishna kama* — in Pumbedita they taught it the first way, that a *kohen* is believed to say that a *yisrael* gave him the *bechor* with the *mum*, because the claim can be checked. This reflects a well-attested phenomenon in the *gemara*: the same teaching was preserved differently in neighboring academies. The *gemara* itself acknowledges this with the phrase *nahara nahara u-pashtei* — each river has its own flow.

### Avida Legilui

*Ve-hilkheta afilu kelishna kama* — the *halakha* is ruled even in accordance with the first version, that a *kohen* is believed when he makes such a claim on the grounds of *avida legiluyei*. This is not self-evident. People lie all the time about things that can theoretically be checked, and very often no one bothers to check. A job applicant lists credentials from prestigious institutions and references who are never called. Nevertheless, the *gemara* establishes that in this area of *halakha*, the potential for verification is sufficient to confer believability.

This principle extends to other areas of *halakha* as well, wherever the question arises whether something that can naturally come to light grants greater credibility. And the overall picture of *when* we distrust a *kohen* has been substantially narrowed throughout the discussion: we distrust him only if he is the *kohen* himself in direct possession of the *bechor*; we trust family members; we trust him if he was the one who identified it as a *bechor* in the first place; and we now trust him about receiving it with a *mum* from a *yisrael*.

## Rafram's Story

### Initial Setup

The *gemara* now introduces a vivid narrative. Rafram was in Pumbedita — recall that Pumbedita held the position that a *kohen* is believed when the matter can be revealed — *ve-hava lei bechora*, and he had a *bechor* among his animals. *Ve-yahveih le-Kohen belo muma* — he gave it to a *kohen* without a blemish, as required, leaving it to the *kohen* to wait for a *mum* to develop naturally.

### The Deception

*Azal shada beih muma* — the *kohen* went and inflicted a *mum* on it himself, as *kohanim* are known to do, according to the *gemara*'s frank assessment. Then, one day, Rafram's eyes were bothering him. Seizing the opportunity — reasoning that Rafram's impaired vision meant he would not recognize the animal — the *kohen* brought the *bechor* before Rafram and said: *bechor zeh natan li Yisrael be-mumo* — this *bechor* was given to me by the *yisrael* already with its blemish. Since Pumbedita rules that a *kohen* is believed when the claim can be verified, he was counting on Rafram to issue a ruling on that basis.

The audacity here is striking: the *kohen* did not go to some other sage but approached Rafram himself — the very *yisrael* who had given him the animal. And whatever was wrong with Rafram's eyes, they were still functioning well enough to have inspected and ruled on the *mum*, as the subsequent events demonstrate.

### Recognition & Exposure

*Afrasinhu le-eineih* — Rafram opened or lifted his eyelids. *Chazyeih be-shikra* — and he saw the lie. Rashi reads *be-shikra* as meaning "in the lie," that he perceived the deception. Tosafot, however, reads the word differently, suggesting that *be-ashkara* or a similar root means that he recognized the animal — he knew it from before. In either reading, Rafram looked at the animal and immediately said: *lav ana hu de-yahavti lach* — was I not the one who gave this to you? He recognized his own animal and understood that the *kohen* had inflicted the *mum* himself.

### Exceptional Case

One might have thought that this incident would cause Rafram to reconsider the entire principle of *avida legiluyei*. If a *kohen* is brazen enough to try to deceive the very person who gave him the animal, does this not undermine the assumption that claims which can be checked are reliable? The *gemara* disposes of this concern: *ve-afilu hachi lo chash lah le-milta* — even so, Rafram was not troubled. *Hai de-chatzif* — this *kohen* was extraordinarily brazen. *Kulei alma lo chatzifi* — most people are not so bold as to come before the same sage who gave them the animal and lie to his face. The aberrant behavior of one *chatzifnik* does not invalidate the general rule. As a matter of principle, when a *kohen* tells a different *sage* that a *yisrael* gave him the *bechor* with its *mum*, that claim can be checked, and we believe him.

The underlying reason the *kohen* inflicted the *mum* in the first place should be recalled here. As long as the *bechor* has no *mum*, the *kohen* cannot slaughter it; eating a *bechor* without a *mum* outside the *Beis Hamikdash* is *chayav kares* as *kodshim bachutz*. Once a *mum* exists and a ruling is obtained, he may eat it. He is therefore suspect of making the *mum* himself — that is the recognized suspicion throughout this chapter — but not of lying about the validity of the *mum* once made, since his entire purpose is to eat it lawfully.

## Obvious Mum & Expertise

### Sarua Case

The *gemara* next presents a case that came before Rav Ashi. *Hahu sarua de-asa lekamei de-Rav Ashi* — an animal with visibly mismatched features, Rashi explaining it involved differently sized eyes, was brought before him. Rav Ashi observed: *le-mai de-cheish lei ei Kohen hu Yisrael hu, harei bechor mumo imo* — what is there to be concerned about, whether it belongs to a *kohen* or a *yisrael*? The *bechor* comes with its *mum* already present. A condition like this cannot be artificially induced by human intervention, and therefore there is simply nothing to worry about regarding its origins.

### Yisrael Owner

Ravina challenged Rav Ashi: *vedilma Yisrael hu* — perhaps the owner is a *yisrael*, not a *kohen*? And Rav Yehuda ruled: *ein ro'in bechor le-Yisrael ela im ken Kohen imo* — one may not give a ruling on a *bechor* for a *yisrael* unless a *kohen* is present. The concern is that while *yisraelim* are not suspected of inflicting a *mum* on a *bechor*, they are suspected of something else: once they receive a ruling that the animal has a qualifying *mum*, if no *kohen* is present, they may simply keep it for themselves. *Mamona de-Kohen chatafei* — they are suspected of misappropriating the *kohen*'s property.

### Public Show & Respect

Rav Ashi replied: *hati hashta* — wait a moment. The concern you are raising — that a *yisrael* might steal the *kohen*'s animal — is the ordinary concern: *hi de-kodshim bachutz lo achal* — a *yisrael*, like a *kohen*, will not eat *kodshim bachutz* without a *mum*, and is therefore suspected of keeping the animal that legitimately belongs to the *kohen*. But look at the specifics of *this* case. *Yada de-hai mumu paku* — the *yisrael* knew perfectly well, without anyone's ruling, that this animal had a manifestly obvious *mum*. He did not *need* to come before a sage. So why did he bother? *Mishum kevoda de-chacham* — out of deference and respect for the sage's authority, even when the ruling was practically superfluous.

### Respect = Honesty

The logic is elegant. If this *yisrael*'s only concern were that he could not eat the animal without a *mum* — and the *mum* was already patently obvious — and if he were the type who would steal from a *kohen*, then keeping it at home and eating it quietly would have been the natural course. He had no need to bring it before anyone. The fact that he voluntarily exposed himself to public scrutiny, brought the animal to a *chacham*, and subjected himself to the *halakhic* process demonstrates that he is committed to following the law. *Kevoda de-chacham lo avid issura lo avid* — if he would not disregard the obligation to show it to a sage, we are not concerned that he would disregard the prohibition against stealing. A person who is careful about the one would not transgress the other. Therefore, in this case, the absence of a *kohen* is not disqualifying.

The corollary follows naturally: the requirement that a *kohen* be present — *ein ro'in bechor le-Yisrael ela im ken Kohen imo* — applies specifically to non-obvious *mumim*, where the *yisrael* genuinely needed the ruling. There, if no *kohen* is present, we suspect he will keep the animal once told it is permitted. But by an obvious *mum*, where he already knew it was permitted and came to the sage purely as an act of respect, we have no such suspicion.

## Ma'aser Mummim

### General Trust

The *mishnah* states: *hakol ne'emanim al mumei ma'aser* — everyone is trusted regarding blemishes in *ma'aser behemah*. The same underlying prohibition applies: *ma'aser behemah* cannot be eaten outside the *Beis Hamikdash* without a *mum*, just like a *bechor*. So why should we extend broader trust for *ma'aser* than for *bechor*?

### Cheating Without Violati…

The *gemara* asks: *mai taama* — what is the reason? And answers: *de-i ba'ei shadi bei muma me'ikara* — because if the owner had wished to cheat, he could have inflicted a *mum* on the animal before it was designated as *ma'aser*. At the moment before the tenth animal passes under the rod, the owner could have blemished any animal in his flock, and it would not have been a violation of any prohibition — the animal was not yet sanctified. Since there was a technically permissible route to achieve the desired outcome, we do not suspect the person of having taken the impermissible route.

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: how could he have planned which animal would emerge as the tenth? *Mi yada hai nafak* — how does one know which one will come out as the tenth? One might answer that he could manipulate the order — but the *gemara* counters that *lo yevaker bein tov le-ra amar Rachmana* — the Torah explicitly prohibits selecting or manipulating the *ma'aser* process. Therefore the answer is different: *ela de-i ba'ei shadi bei muma be-chol edrei* — he could have put a *mum* in his entire flock, and whichever animal came out as the tenth would already have borne the *mum*. Since the legally compliant path to cheating existed, we do not suspect him of having taken the unlawful one. Even if this seems a somewhat far-fetched avenue of circumvention, the *halakha* determines that the mere theoretical availability of a non-*assur* path is sufficient to deflect suspicion.

## Expert vs Beit Din

### Mishnah's Position

The *mishnah* states: if a *bechor* had its eye blinded, its front limb severed, or its leg broken — *harei ze yishachet al pi gimel benei kenesset* — it may be slaughtered on the basis of a ruling from three members of the community. These are patently obvious blemishes; any person could identify them as such. Despite this, a separate *halakhic* requirement applies: one must obtain a ruling before slaughtering. In ordinary circumstances, that ruling must come from a *mumcheh*, an expert. But because the *mum* here is self-evident, the requirement for expertise is waived, and three ordinary men — *she-lasha mi-benei kenesset*, drawn from the *shul*, as it were — suffice.

Rabbi Yosi disagrees: *afilu yesh sham [esrim ve-]gimel eino nishchat ela al pi mumcheh* — even if twenty-three people are present, the animal may not be slaughtered except on the ruling of an expert. His concern is that allowing a lay *beis din* for obvious cases might lead people to assume that the same leniency applies to complicated ones. Rabbi Yosi therefore insists on an expert in all circumstances. The number twenty-three, conspicuously borrowed from the *beis din* structure of *sanhedrin*, is not incidental. The *gemara* is consciously overlaying onto the laws of *bechor* an entire framework borrowed from judicial proceedings. The concepts of *mumcheh*, *beis din*, and qualified experts are all terms that come from *sanhedrin* discourse. 

A famous mistranslation in the Soncino edition is illuminating here: the Soncino rendered *kaf-gimel* not as twenty-three but as *Kohen Gadol*. The error is understandable — given that we are discussing *kohanim* and have been laying a *beis din* structure over this domain, a copyist or translator might have conflated the two — but it is plainly incorrect.

### Gemara's Logic

The *gemara* asks the obvious question: what is the ruling of Rav Simlai and Rav Yehuda Nesi'ah adding? Is it not just our *mishnah* restated?

### Chutz La'aretz

*Traveihu mishmeih de-Rav Yehoshua ben Levi amri* — both said in the name of Rav Yehoshua ben Levi: *hataras bechor be-chutz la'aretz al pi shlosha bnei kenesset* — in *chutz la'aretz*, releasing a *bechor* requires a ruling by three members of the community. Rava limits this: *u-ve-mumim mufhakin* — only for obvious blemishes. This sounds again like our *mishnah*.

The *gemara* defends the novelty of the teaching: *ee mi-matnisin hava amina, be-chutz la'aretz afilu mumin she'ein mufhakin* — from the *mishnah* alone I might have thought that in *chutz la'aretz* even non-obvious *mumim* could be ruled upon by three laypeople. *Hai dikhatani mufhakin, le-odiya kocho d'Rabbi Yosi* — the *mishnah* discusses obvious cases only to show how far Rabbi Yosi goes in requiring an expert. Perhaps the *chakhamin* permit a *beis din* even for non-obvious *mumim*, and perhaps especially in *chutz la'aretz*. The ruling of Rav Yehoshua ben Levi therefore comes to teach that even in *chutz la'aretz*, a lay *beis din* suffices only for obvious *mumim*.

Why we might have expected greater leniency in *chutz la'aretz* is not entirely clear. If anything, Tosafot suggests the opposite concern: allowing the slaughter of a *bechor* in *chutz la'aretz* during a time when the *Beis Hamikdash* stands might lead people to extend the leniency improperly to *Eretz Yisrael* as well, creating a slippery slope. The *gemara* does not fully explore this question, but it does insist that evident *mumim* and a *beis din* — in *Eretz Yisrael* or *chutz la'aretz* — are the parameters, not expertise alone.

### Rav's Clarification

The essential clarification is twofold. First, one always needs a ruling, whether the *mum* is obvious or not. Second, when the *mum* is obvious, a *beis din* of three suffices and expertise is not required — but the *beis din* is the instrument, not just any informal attestation. Even the obviously blemished animal must be brought before someone.

### Expert Priority

The *gemara* continues: *amar Rav Yehuda amar Rabbi Yirmiyahu bar Abba* — and there is doubt whether this statement originated with Rav or with Shmuel — *gimel matirin es habechor bemakom she'ein mumcheh* — three people may release a *bechor* where no *mumcheh* is available. The *gemara* challenges: this too is in our *mishnah*. The defense: *ee mi-matnisin hava amina, afilu bemakom mumcheh* — from the *mishnah* alone I might have thought that even when an expert is available, a *beis din* of three will suffice. The ruling comes to teach: *bemakom she'yesh mumcheh lo* — where a *mumcheh* is present, you must go to the *mumcheh*. A *beis din* is not a substitute when an expert is accessible.

This is a significant point. It represents a halfway concession to Rabbi Yosi. Rabbi Yosi requires an expert in all circumstances; the *tanna kamma* allows a *beis din* for obvious cases. The ruling here mediates: a *beis din* is permitted only where no *mumcheh* is available. Where an expert can be consulted, the *beis din* track is closed. This prevents the institution of lay adjudication from eroding the role of the expert.

Tosafot raises the further question of what exactly constitutes a *mumcheh* in this context. The *gemara* in *Sanhedrin* discusses the concept of a *yachid mumcheh* — an individual expert — who can rule even without three. The *semicha* that goes back to Moshe Rabbeinu enabling one to sit on capital cases is not the *semicha* in view here. Rather, as Tosafot explains, the *mumcheh* for *bechor* is simply someone with genuine expertise in the subject. The *rambam* similarly emphasizes expertise. Supporting this reading, the historical evidence shows that those who actually ruled on *bechorot* did not possess the chain of *semicha* going back to Moshe Rabbeinu. The concept of *yoreh yoreh, yadin yadin, yatir bechoros* — the traditional three levels of authorization — is itself fascinating here: the ability to rule on *bechoros* sits at the top of that hierarchy precisely because it is the most technically demanding. The word *bechoros* was integrated into the very vocabulary of rabbinic ordination, once again illustrating how deeply the *beis din* model came to pervade this corner of *halacha*.

## Bechor & Neder Rules

### Three Can Release

Rav Chiya bar Amram states: *gimel matirin es habechor bemakom she'ein mumcheh* — three can release a *bechor* when there is no expert available. This is the *halakha* as established, and its purpose here is explicit: *le-afukei midivrei Rabbi Yosi* — to exclude the position of Rabbi Yosi, who requires an expert in all cases. The ruling permits three laypeople to proceed when a *mumcheh* is genuinely unavailable.

### Neder Release

Similarly, *gimel matirin es haneder bemakom she'ein chacham* — three can release a *neder* when no *chacham* is available. The parallel to the Rosh Hashanah practice of *hataras nedarim* is apparent: ideally one goes before a qualified *chacham*, but if none is accessible, a *beis din* of three suffices. The statement comes: *le-afukei midivrei Rabbi Yehuda* — to exclude Rabbi Yehuda's more stringent requirement.

### Yehuda's Requirement

A *baraisa* is cited: *hataras nedarim be-gimel* — releasing vows requires three. *Rabbi Yehuda omer echad mehem chacham* — Rabbi Yehuda says one of the three must be a *chacham*. The plain reading of this is that even if you have a *beis din* of three, Rabbi Yehuda insists that one of the three be a qualified sage.

### Sage Type Question

The *gemara* probes: does this make sense? If you have a genuine *chacham*, one who is qualified to be *matir* a *neder* on his own, why would he need two others alongside him? The entire premise of the three-person panel is that it substitutes for the unavailable *chacham*. What kind of hybrid is Rabbi Yehuda describing?

### Mid-Level Sage

Tosafot resolves the difficulty by positing that Rabbi Yehuda is speaking of a mid-level *chacham* — someone with enough knowledge and intelligence to participate meaningfully in the process, who can understand the grounds for release and explain them to the others, but who does not possess the independent qualification to be *matir* a *neder* on his own. When the *gemara* asks what sort of *chacham* is meant — and Rav Nachman says, somewhat disarmingly, "one like me" — this is understood as: one who can bring substantive engagement to the *beis din* and through whom the others are drawn into the process meaningfully. This leads Ravina to clarify that the other two members of the *beis din* must at minimum be capable of being made to understand — *dimstavra lei ve-savar*, they can be explained to and grasp what is being decided.

### Final Halakha

Rav Chananel states in the name of Rav: *ein halakha ke-Rabbi Yosi* — the *halakha* does not follow Rabbi Yosi. The *gemara* initially reacts: *peshita* — is this not obvious? When a single authority disputes the majority, *halakha kerabbim* — we follow the majority. The *gemara* defends the necessity of the ruling: one might have said that Rabbi Yosi's position warrants special weight because *nimuko imo* — his reasoning is consistently sound and accompanies his rulings. The ruling comes to tell us that even that consideration does not override the general principle in this instance.

### Redundancy Question

This in turn raises a question about the earlier statement attributable to either Rav or Shmuel regarding using a *beis din* in the absence of an expert. Now that Rav has explicitly ruled against Rabbi Yosi, if the earlier statement that "three may release a *bechor* where no *mumcheh* is available" also originated with Rav, he would appear to be saying the same thing twice. The *gemara* therefore concludes that the earlier statement must have originated with Shmuel — *tarti lama li*, why would Rav repeat himself? The *gemara* responds: *chada mikhlal de-chaverteih itmar* — it is possible that he said one statement and we inferred the other, so it is not truly a repetition but rather a restatement derived from a single source.

## Sale & Remedies

### Bechor Sold

The *mishnah* now turns to the practical consequences of failure to obtain a ruling. *Ha-shocheit et ha-bechor ve-noda she-lo her'ahu* — if one slaughtered a *bechor* and it then became known that he had not shown it to a *chacham*. The meat is therefore treated as forbidden, and those who purchased it must be addressed. *Ma she-achlu achlu* — what they have already eaten is done. *Ve-yachzir lahem et ha-damim* — he must return their money. If the *kohen* sold the meat and the buyers consumed it without knowing its status, they ate forbidden food and did so in good faith, but their money must be returned because the seller had no right to take it. The meat that has not yet been eaten must be buried — *ha-basar yikaver* — and again their money returned.

There is a question worth flagging but not resolved here: would this *post-facto* forbidding of the meat apply even to an obviously blemished animal where the failure was merely that no *chacham* gave a formal ruling? Or does this stringency apply only to non-obvious *mumim* where the need for expert adjudication was genuine? The *mishnah* does not specify, and it is likely that the *rishonim* address this. For present purposes, the *mishnah*'s clear point is that the ruling of a *chacham* (or *beis din*) is not a formality but a genuine halakhic prerequisite, and failure to obtain it has real consequences both for the meat and for the financial transaction.

### Treifah Sale

A parallel case: *ve-chen ha-shocheit et ha-parah u-machra ve-noda she-hi treifah* — a butcher slaughtered an animal, sold the meat, and it was subsequently discovered to have been a *treifah*. Perhaps he did nothing wrong; perhaps the defect — a diseased lung discovered after the fact, or a problem found upon post-slaughter examination — was not foreseeable. *Ma she-achlu achlu* — what the buyers ate they ate. But what they have not yet eaten — *hem yachziru lo et ha-basar* — they return to the butcher. Unlike the *bechor* case, where remaining meat must be buried, *treifah* meat need not be buried; it has permitted uses, such as feeding dogs or selling to non-Jews. So they return the *treifah* meat to the butcher. *Ve-hu yachzir lahem et ha-damim* — and he returns their money. They paid for kosher meat; they receive reimbursement.

The question arises whether the seller must reimburse even for what the buyers already ate. They received a *steak dinner*, as it were — the food had value, they benefited, and they are not revolted by the experience. Perhaps the seller owes nothing for consumed portions? The *mishnah*'s phrasing suggests a distinction between the *bechor* case (where both eaten and uneaten portions require reimbursement) and the *treifah* case (where the emphasis falls on the uneaten portion). Nevertheless, the *rambam* and the broader *halakhic* tradition appear to read it as full reimbursement in both cases: the transaction was made under false pretenses — *mekach ta'ut* — and the seller must make the buyer whole.

### Non-Jew Transfer

If the buyers, without knowing the meat was *treifah*, gave it to non-Jews or fed it to dogs — effectively using it as *treifah* meat — *yeshalmu demei treifah* — the seller reimburses them only the difference between the value of kosher meat and *treifah* meat. In this scenario, the buyers themselves realized the value of the *treifah* designation; they derived exactly what *treifah* meat is worth. They are therefore entitled to recover the premium they overpaid for kosher meat, not a full refund.

## Disgust & Refunds

### Disgust Standard

The *baraisa* introduces a further principle. *Tanu Rabbanan* — our sages taught: if one sold meat that turned out to be *bechor*, or fruit that turned out to be *tevel*, or wine that turned out to be *yayin nesech* — all of which are *assur be-hana'ah* — *ma she-achlu achlu, yachzir lahem et ha-damim*: what they ate they ate, and he returns their money. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar adds a distinction: *devarim she-nefesh katza bahen* — things that cause psychological revulsion upon discovery — warrant full reimbursement. *Ve-she-einah nefesh katza bahen* — things that do not cause revulsion — *yenakeh lahem et dmeihen*: only a discount is appropriate, since the buyers derived some benefit.

### Disgust Examples

*Ve-elu hen devarim she-nefesh katza bahen* — these are the things that cause disgust: *neveilot u-treifot, shekatzim u-remashim* — an animal that died on its own, a *treifah*, insects, and crawling creatures. If upon finishing one's meal one discovers one was eating a *neveila* or a roach had fallen in, the revulsion is such that one would never have wanted any part of the transaction. Full reimbursement is therefore due, as it is a complete *mekach ta'ut*. *Ve-elu hen devarim she-ein nefesh katza bahen* — the things that do not cause such revulsion: *bechorot*, *tevalim*, and *yayin nesech*. These are *halakhically* forbidden, but there is nothing physically or aesthetically repulsive about them. Perfectly normal meat, fruit, and wine — the buyer simply did not know of their technical *halakhic* status.

### Assur Paradox

The obvious difficulty must now be confronted directly. All three items — *bechor*, *tevel*, and *yayin nesech* — are *assur be-hana'ah*, forbidden even for benefit. If that is so, the seller had no right to sell them in the first place and certainly no right to receive any payment. The buyers derived benefit, but since the seller could never have legitimately taken money for these items, why should he be entitled to any partial payment even when the buyers are not disgusted?

### Bechor Exception

*Bechor leima lei mai afsadetich* — why can't the seller say to the buyer: what did I actually cause you to lose? The buyers were not permitted to benefit from the meat anyway, and they did benefit, so what claim do they have? The *gemara* answers: *lo tzricha de-zavin lei mi-makom mum* — the case is one where the seller sold the meat at the location of the *mum*, meaning the *mum* was visible in the cut. In such a case, theoretically, the meat could have been brought back to a *chacham* and a ruling obtained retroactively — *hava machzeina lei ve-sharu lei alai* — I could have shown it to a *chacham* and it could have been declared permissible to me. This follows the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who allows a *chacham* to rule on a *bechor* even after slaughter. By eating the meat before this could happen, the buyer deprived the seller of the opportunity to potentially render it permitted. That lost opportunity has monetary value, and it is on account of this that the seller retains some entitlement to partial payment.

### Tevel Fix

For *tevel*: *hava metakneina lei ve-achilna lei* — the seller could say that he would have separated *terumot u-ma'asrot* and then the fruit would have been entirely permitted. It was not permanently and irremediably forbidden; a simple act of tithing would have transformed it into ordinary permitted produce. By consuming it as *tevel*, the buyer robbed the seller of the opportunity to fix the situation. The seller's partial claim derives from this lost opportunity cost.

### Wine Mixing

For *yayin nesech*: the case is one involving a mixture — *al yedei ta'arovet* — where the *yayin nesech* had already fallen into a vat of kosher wine. *Kulo assur be-hana'ah* — the mixture as a whole is forbidden for benefit. But *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer: yimacher kulo le-oved kochavim chutz midmei yayin nesech shebo* — the entire mixture may be sold to a non-Jew, less the value attributable to the *yayin nesech* itself. The bulk of the wine — representing the permitted wine in the mixture — can be sold. The seller therefore suffered a real financial loss: he had wine worth ninety percent of its face value (or whatever the proportion), and the buyer's consumption prevented him from realizing that sale. It is for this opportunity cost that partial payment is justified, not for the *yayin nesech* portion itself. This reading is attributed to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position.

In sum, the paradox is resolved in each case by identifying a specific legal pathway through which the forbidden item could have been made permissible or its value recovered. The seller's partial claim is not a claim on the forbidden item itself but a claim for the lost opportunity to have remediated the situation.

## Blemish Definitions

### Listed Blemishes

With *Hadran Alach P'sulei Hamukdashin* — the conclusion of the perek — the *gemara* turns to the next chapter, which catalogs the specific *mumim* that permit slaughtering a *bechor*. *Elu mumim shochtin es ha-bechor* — the following blemishes permit slaughtering the *bechor*, and the *mishnah* begins with damage to the ear.

### Ear Blemish

*Nifgema ozno min ha-sachsuch* — the ear is blemished from the *sachsuch*, which is the cartilage. The key limitation is *aval lo ha-or* — but not merely the fleshy flap of the ear. Damage only to the external soft flap does not constitute a qualifying *mum*; it must affect the cartilage. Exactly where the skin flap ends and the cartilage begins is not perfectly clear from the text, and Rashi suggests the line falls somewhat deeper into the structure of the ear.

### Cracks & Holes

*Nisdeka af al pi shelo chasra* — if the ear is cracked even without any portion actually missing, this constitutes a *mum*. *Nikva melo karshina* — if it has a hole the size of a *karshina*, a type of legume, that too qualifies. The critical feature is not that material has been removed but that the integrity of the structure has been compromised.

### Dried Ears

*O she-yavesha* — or if it has dried out. The *mishnah* defines this: *kol she-tinakev ve-eina motzia dam* — any ear that, if pierced, produces no blood. Some dried condition has cut off the blood supply. Rabbi Yosi ben Meshullam is more stringent: *yevesha she-tehei nifrechet* — it must be so desiccated that it would crumble in one's hands if rubbed. Presumably this involves some condition that causes severe loss of blood circulation to the ear.

### Lulav Parallel

It is worth noting that several of these categories — *yavesh*, *nisdak*, *chaser*, *nekev* — appear also in the laws of *lulav* and *esrog*. Whether these laws served as a model for one another, or whether both draw from a shared vocabulary of organic integrity, is an interesting question. The parallel has practical consequences: some *rishonim* apply the standard of crumbling (*nifrechet*) found in our *mishnah* when determining how dry a *lulav* must be before it becomes invalid as *yavesh*.

## Exegesis & Generalization

### Text Specificity

The *gemara* now asks the fundamental hermeneutical question: why do we extend the *mum* that permits slaughtering a *bechor* to all of these cases? The *pasuk* says: *ve-chi yiheyeh bo mum pise'ach o iver* — if it has a blemish, whether lame or blind. Those are specific examples. How do we extrapolate?

### Gen-Spec-Gen

The *gemara* applies the rule of *klal u-prat u-khlal* — general, specific, general. The *pasuk* opens with a general statement (*ki yiheyeh bo mum*), narrows to specific examples (*pise'ach o iver*), and then broadens again (*kol mum ra*). When the Torah moves from general to specific and back to general, we generalize — but only in accordance with the character of the specific examples given. We do not include everything conceivable; we include things that share the defining features of *pise'ach* and *iver*.

### Pattern Criteria

What are those defining features? *Mah prat meforash mum she-bagaluy ve-eino chozer* — the specific examples, blindness and lameness, share two properties: they are external and visible (*she-bagaluy*), and they are permanent and non-healing (*ve-eino chozer*). *Af kol mum she-bagaluy ve-eino chozer* — so too, any *mum* that is visible and permanent qualifies.

### Functionality Test

The *gemara* raises an alternative formulation: perhaps the pattern requires not just visibility and permanence, but also functional impairment. *Mah prat meforash mum she-bagaluy u-vatel mi-melachto ve-eino chozer* — blindness and lameness both render the animal unable to perform work. Perhaps only a *mum* that likewise impairs the animal's utility qualifies.

### Aesthetic Objection

If the functionality criterion were required, then ear damage — a purely aesthetic defect that in no way impairs the animal's ability to work — could not constitute a qualifying *mum*. Yet the *mishnah* explicitly includes ear damage. The *gemara* therefore challenges this reading: *alma tenan nifgema ozno min ha-sachsuch* — why does the *mishnah* list ear damage if functional impairment were required?

### Ribui Resolution

The resolution is that *kol mum ra ribuyya hu* — the phrase "any bad *mum*" functions not as part of the *klal u-prat u-khlal* analysis but as a *ribuy*, an expansion. Even if the core of *klal u-prat u-khlal* would pattern-match on visible and functionally impairing defects, the *ribuy* of *kol mum ra* extends the category to include purely aesthetic defects as well. Ear damage is included not through the narrow channel of the specific examples but through this broader expansion.

### Hidden Exclusion

However, the *ribuy* is not unlimited. The *gemara* presses: if *kol mum ra* is so expansive, why not include hidden *mumim* as well? The *mishnah* itself provides the answer: regarding the teeth, front incisors that are chipped or cracked constitute a *mum*, but for molars, only complete removal — *ne'ekru* — qualifies, whereas chipping or cracking does not. Why the asymmetry? Because molars are not readily visible. A chip in a molar cannot be easily seen. The standard therefore escalates: the entire molar must be gone before the *mum* is recognizable. This demonstrates that visibility remains a criterion even within the expansive reading — hidden defects do not qualify.

### Final Criteria

*Ba'inan mum ra ve-leika* — the Torah requires a *mum ra*, a genuinely bad *mum*. Badness, in this context, is assessed from the human perspective: if it is not visible, it does not register aesthetically or practically as bad. A defect that no one can see is not a *mum ra*. The two criteria that emerge from the full analysis are therefore: the *mum* must be visible, and it must be permanent.

### Transitory Exclusion

The question remains: why are transitory *mumim* excluded? *Mum oveir sevara hu* — this exclusion is a matter of logic, not derivation. The *gemara* argues: *hashta mifrak lo farkinan aluyeh, mishchat shachtinan aluyeh* — if a transitory *mum* is insufficient even to redeem a *korban* (to transfer its *kedusha* and release it for ordinary use), how could it be powerful enough to grant license to eat a *bechor* outside the *Beis Hamikdash*? Redemption of a *korban* requires a permanent *mum*; without it, the animal remains *kadosh* and cannot be redeemed. If the standard for even so modest a consequence as redemption requires a permanent defect, then certainly the far more striking result — that one may take an animal which is inherently *kodesh* as a *bechor* and slaughter and eat it outside the Temple without any redemption at all — cannot be triggered by a fleeting condition.

The *gemara* proves this from a *baraisa* expounding the verse *ve-im kol behemah teme'ah asher lo yakrivu mimenah korban la-Hashem* — if it is an animal from which no *korban* can be brought, it may be redeemed. The *baraisa* explains that this refers not to literally non-kosher animals but to *ba'alei mumim*, animals with disqualifying blemishes. Could a transitory *mum* trigger this redemption? *Yachol yifdu al mum oveir* — perhaps. *Talmud lomar asher lo yakrivu mimenu korban la-Hashem* — the verse says "from which no *korban* can be brought," implying: that which *never* can be brought. *Mi she-eino karev kol ikar* — something that can never serve as a *korban*, excluding *ze she-eino karev hayom aval le-machar* — something that cannot be brought today but could be brought tomorrow once the *mum* heals. A transitory *mum* is therefore categorically insufficient to redeem a *korban*, and by the same logic — a fortiori — it cannot release a *bechor*.

An alternative proof for the exclusion of transitory *mumim* is offered: *im ken pise'ach ve-iver lama li* — if we were to include everything, why would the Torah have bothered specifying *pise'ach* and *iver* as examples at all? The very fact that the Torah provides examples implies a limiting principle. And the limit that best fits those examples — permanent, visible defects — necessarily excludes transitory conditions.

These, then, are the twin criteria on which the entire subsequent catalog of *mumim* rests: visible and permanent. The remaining *mishnayot* of the chapter will work through specific blemishes applying these principles case by case.
