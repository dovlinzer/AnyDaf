# Bava Batra 119 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzelofchad's Portions

### Three Portions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וְהַבָּנִים נָטְלוּ בִּזְכוּת אֲבִי אֲבִיהֶם, וּבִזְכוּת אֲבִי אִמּוֹתֵיהֶם. וְהָתַנְיָא: בִּזְכוּת עַצְמָן! לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לְיוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם, הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לְבָאֵי הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of the *baraita*. **The Master says: And the sons** of the spies and of the protesters **took** portions of the land **in the merit of their paternal grandfathers and in the merit of their maternal grandfathers** if those grandfathers were among those who left Egypt. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 7:10): The sons of the spies and of the protesters took portions **in their own merit?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult: This** *baraita*, which states they took portions in the merit of their grandfathers, is written **in accordance with** the opinion of the **one who says** that Eretz Yisrael was divided **among those who left Egypt;** and **that** *baraita*, which states that they took portions in their own merit, is written **in accordance with** the opinion of the **one who says** that Eretz Yisrael was divided **among those who entered Eretz** Yisrael.

The *mishna* on 119a establishes that *benot Tzelofchad* received three portions of the Land. The reasoning runs as follows: Tzelofchad himself was entitled to one portion as one of the *yotzei Mitzrayim*, the generation that left Egypt, and he passed that portion down to his daughters once they prevailed in their case before Moshe. In addition, Tzelofchad's father Chefer was also among the *yotzei Mitzrayim*, and since Chefer had predeceased him, Tzelofchad — as a *bechor* — received a double share of Chefer's portion. Chefer's portion was itself subdivided among all the brothers of that family, so the two shares Tzelofchad received from that subdivision were smaller than his own personal portion; nevertheless, he held two of them by virtue of being the firstborn. All three portions — his own and the two from his father's share — passed to his daughters, and this is what the *mishna* records.

### Ra'ui vs. Muchzak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁהָיָה בְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל שְׁנֵי חֲלָקִים. וְאַמַּאי? רָאוּי הוּא, וְאֵין הַבְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל בָּרָאוּי כִּבְמוּחְזָק! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּיִתְדוֹת אֹהָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** Zelophehad took an additional portion that he received from Hepher, **because he was a firstborn,** and a firstborn **takes two portions** of inheritance from his father. The Gemara asks: **But why** was he entitled to the double portion of the firstborn? His portion of land **is** property merely **due** to Hepher, as Hepher never actually possessed the land, **and** the *halakha* is that **a firstborn does not take** a double portion **of** property **due** to the deceased **as** he does **of** property the deceased **possessed. Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** This clause of the mishna is stated **with regard to tent pegs** and other assorted movable property that Hepher possessed.

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: why was Tzelofchad entitled to a double portion at all? The principle is that *ein ha-bechor notel be-ra'ui ke-vi-muchzak* — a firstborn does not receive a double portion in property that was merely *ra'ui*, coming to the father, rather than *muchzak*, already in the father's actual possession at the time of death. If a business owned by the father generated profits only after the father's death, those profits are *ra'ui* and the *bechor* receives no double share in them. Since Chefer never actually took possession of his portion of *Eretz Yisrael* — it was something he stood to receive, not something he held — why should Tzelofchad receive a double portion of it?

### Movables, Not Land

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel offers one resolution: the double portion was not taken from the land at all, but rather from other movable property — *be-yishuv olam* — whatever Chefer actually owned in his possession. The *mishna* speaks of the double portion in that context. This answer is acknowledged to be far from the plain meaning (*pshat*) of the text, since the entire discussion in both the *mishna* and the surrounding *pesukim* is self-evidently about portions of land.

## Eretz Yisrael Muchzaket

### Rabba's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבָּה, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּנוֹת צְלָפְחָד נָטְלוּ אַרְבָּעָה חֲלָקִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּפְּלוּ חַבְלֵי מְנַשֶּׁה עֲשָׂרָה״! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה: אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל מוּחְזֶקֶת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The **daughters of Zelophehad took four parts, as it is stated: "And ten parts fell to Manasseh"** (Joshua 17:5). Clearly, this is referring to portions of land, and not simply movable property. **Rather, Rabba says: Eretz Yisrael is** considered already **in possession** of one who was entitled to his portion of inheritance land, even before the land was assigned.

Rabba challenges Rav Yehuda's answer directly. He cites a *braita* in which Rabbi Yehuda holds that *benot Tzelofchad* actually received four portions, not three, as the verse states: *vayipplu chavlei Menashe asara* — the portions of Menashe were ten. Six of those went to the main families of Menashe, and four, of varying sizes, went to Tzelofchad's daughters. The point is that the discussion is unmistakably about portions of land. Telling us that the double portion was in "ten pegs" misses the entire point of the *mishna*.

### Already Possessed

Rabba therefore offers a different resolution: *Eretz Yisrael muchzeket hi* — the Land of Israel is already considered to be in the possession of those who left Egypt. This is a principle introduced earlier in the *masechta*: because the *yotzei Mitzrayim* already hold the land in a legal sense, it is not merely *ra'ui* but *muchzak*, and the laws of a *bechor*'s double portion therefore apply.

### Ve-natati Interpretation

The *Rashbam* explains this principle by citing the verse *ve-natati otah lachem morasha* — "I have given it to you as an inheritance." The verb is in the past tense: not "I will give," but "I have given." The land is already treated as received, already in the possession of those who left Egypt. What is notable is that this possession is attributed not to the *avot*, the patriarchs, nor to the generation that actually entered the land, but specifically to the *yotzei Mitzrayim* — the generation of the desert. They are considered to hold the land even before they set foot in it.

## Moshe's Knowledge

### What Moshe Knew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, אָמַר רַבִּי חִידְקָא: שִׁמְעוֹן הַשִּׁקְמוֹנִי הָיָה לִי חָבֵר מִתַּלְמִידֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וְכָךְ הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הַשִּׁקְמוֹנִי אוֹמֵר: יוֹדֵעַ הָיָה מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ שֶׁבְּנוֹת צְלָפְחָד יוֹרְשׁוֹת הֵן, אֲבָל לֹא הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ אִם נוֹטְלוֹת חֵלֶק בְּכוֹרָה אִם לָאו;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*. **Rabbi Ḥideka said: Shimon HaShikmoni was my colleague among the students of Rabbi Akiva, and so would Rabbi Shimon HaShikmoni say:** Even before turning to God for guidance, **Moses our teacher knew that the daughters of Zelophehad were inheritors** and that they were entitled to their father's portion, as well as his share in Hepher's portion. **But he did not know if they** were entitled to **take a portion of the firstborn** of Hepher's portion **or not.**

The *gemara* now cites a *braita* in which Rabbi Hidka relates a teaching in the name of Shimon HaShikmoni, a colleague who was among the students of Rabbi Akiva. The teaching addresses a potentially troubling implication of the story: did Moshe not know the law of daughters' inheritance? Shimon HaShikmoni insists: *yodea hayah Moshe Rabbeinu she-benot Tzelofchad yorshos hen* — Moshe certainly knew that daughters inherit in the absence of sons. What he did not know was whether they were entitled to the *chelek bechora*, the firstborn's double portion — specifically, whether they could stand in for Tzelofchad vis-à-vis his brothers and receive two shares of Chefer's portion. That narrow question — touching on whether *Eretz Yisrael* is considered *muchzak* for the *yotzei Mitzrayim* — was what Moshe lacked. All the broader *halachot* he already possessed; it was one precise point of clarification that he needed.

### Merit and Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְאוּיָה הָיְתָה פָּרָשַׁת נַחֲלוֹת לִיכָּתֵב עַל יְדֵי מֹשֶׁה, אֶלָּא שֶׁזָּכוּ בְּנוֹת צְלָפְחָד וְנִכְתְּבָה עַל יָדָן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥideka continues citing Rabbi Shimon HaShikmoni: **And** the Torah **portion** concerning the laws **of inheritances was fit to have been written through** God initiating a commandment to **Moses,** without mentioning Zelophehad's daughters. **But** by demonstrating their desire for land in Eretz Yisrael the **daughters of Zelophehad merited** that the portion was **written through** a response to **them.**

Because Moshe already knew essentially all of this, Shimon HaShikmoni continues, the *parsha* of inheritance *ra'uya hayta lehikashev al yedei Moshe* — it was fitting to have been communicated through Moshe, as all other Torah law was. It need not have been prompted by the daughters' petition; God could have simply transmitted it through Moshe in the ordinary fashion. Yet: *ela shezaku benot Tzelofchad venichteva al yadan* — the daughters merited, through their initiative and their love of *Eretz Yisrael* and their righteousness as *tzidkaniyot*, that the *parsha* was written attributed to their prompting. The purpose of this framing is to prevent any impression that Moshe had a serious blind spot, or that without the daughters this *halacha* would never have been taught.

### Gatherer of Wood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיוֹדֵעַ הָיָה מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ שֶׁהַמְקוֹשֵׁשׁ בְּמִיתָה – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מְחַלְּלֶיהָ מוֹת יוּמָת״, אֲבָל לֹא הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵי זוֹ מִיתָה הוּא יָמוּת; וּרְאוּיָהּ הָיְתָה פָּרָשַׁת מְקוֹשֵׁשׁ שֶׁתִּכָּתֵב עַל יְדֵי מֹשֶׁה, אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב מְקוֹשֵׁשׁ וְנִכְתְּבָה עַל יָדוֹ. לְלַמֶּדְךָ, שֶׁמְּגַלְגְּלִים זְכוּת עַל יְדֵי זַכַּאי, וְחוֹבָה עַל יְדֵי חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥideka continues citing Rabbi Shimon HaShikmoni: **And Moses our teacher** similarly **knew that the** wood **gatherer,** who gathered wood on Shabbat (see Numbers 15:32–36), was to be punished **by death, as it is stated: "Every one that profanes it shall be put to death"** (Exodus 31:14), **but he did not know by which death** penalty the wood gatherer **must die. And the** Torah **portion** concerning the punishment **of** the wood **gatherer was fit to have been written through** God initiating a commandment to **Moses,** to teach which form of court-imposed capital punishment is administered to one who violates Shabbat. **But** the wood **gatherer was** found **guilty, and** the portion was **written through** the incident involving **him. This is to teach you that merit is brought about by means of** one who **is meritorious and liability by means of** one who **is liable.**

A parallel case is offered: Moshe also knew that the man who gathered wood on Shabbat was deserving of death — *mechaleleha mot yumat*, the Torah had already stated this — but he did not know the specific mode of execution. Yet the *parsha* of the wood-gatherer (who, according to some opinions, was Tzelofchad himself) need not have waited for that event to be taught. It would have been written regardless. *Ela shenitchayav mikoshesh venichteva al yado* — it happened that the event provided the occasion, but the law was not contingent on it.

### Megalgelin Zechut

The principle that emerges is: *megalgelin zechut al yedei zakai ve-chov al yedei chayav* — merit is brought about through the meritorious, and liability through the guilty. Because *benot Tzelofchad* were righteous, the *parsha* of inheritance was written in connection with their case even though it would have been written anyway. Because the wood-gatherer was deserving of punishment, the *parsha* of that execution was connected to his act. In each case, the underlying law existed independently; the virtuous or the culpable person simply became the vehicle through which it was expressed.

## Moshe's Doubt

### Exact Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל מוּחְזֶקֶת, מַאי קָא מִסְתַּפְּקָא לֵיהּ? הִיא גּוּפַהּ קָא מִסְתַּפְּקָא לֵיהּ – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנָתַתִּי אֹתָהּ לָכֶם מוֹרָשָׁה אֲנִי ה׳״ – יְרוּשָּׁה הִיא לָכֶם מֵאֲבוֹתֵיכֶם; אוֹ דִלְמָא, שֶׁמּוֹרִישִׁין וְאֵינָן יוֹרְשִׁין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states its objection: **And if it enters your mind** to say that **Eretz Yisrael** was already **in possession** of the Jewish people even before the land was assigned, **what was** Moses **uncertain** about with regard to the right of Zelophehad's daughters to collect a double portion; after all, Hepher's portion in the land was in his possession, and Zelophehad was the firstborn? The Gemara answers: **This** matter **itself** is what Moses **was uncertain** about, **as it is written: "And I will give it to you for a heritage [*morasha*]: I am the Lord"** (Exodus 6:8). Moses was unsure if the verse should be understood: **It** is **an inheritance [*yerusha*] for you from your fathers,** such that it is considered in the possession of those who left Egypt; **or perhaps** the verse indicates another matter, **that** the generation of those who left Egypt **bequeath [*morishin*]** the portions to others **but they do not inherit [*yoreshin*]** the portions themselves, because they are destined to die in the wilderness.

Having established what Moshe did and did not know, the *gemara* sharpens the analysis. One might ask: if *Eretz Yisrael* is considered *muchzeket* in the possession of the *yotzei Mitzrayim*, then there is no *halachic* question — the *bechor* plainly gets his double portion. And if it is *not* considered *muchzeket*, then equally there is no question — the *bechor* plainly does not. So what exactly was Moshe's doubt? The *gemara* answers: *hi gufa kamistapka lei* — that very question, whether *Eretz Yisrael* is *muchzeket* in the hands of the *yotzei Mitzrayim*, was what Moshe was uncertain about. He knew all the surrounding *halachot* perfectly; the one open question was the status of the land itself.

### Morasha Ambiguity

The uncertainty traces back to the verse: *ve-natati otah lachem morasha ani Hashem* — "I have given it to you as an inheritance, I am God." Moshe did not know how to read the word *morasha*. Does it mean *yerushah lachem me-avoteichem* — a patrimony, an inheritance already received from your ancestors, meaning you are already in possession? Or does it mean *morishin* — something you will pass on, a bequest to your children, meaning you yourselves have not yet inherited it?

### Dual Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְשַׁטוּ לֵיהּ תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: יְרוּשָּׁה לָכֶם מֵאֲבוֹתֵיכֶם, וּמוֹרִישִׁין וְאֵינָן יוֹרְשִׁין. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״תְּבִיאֵמוֹ וְתִטָּעֵמוֹ בְּהַר נַחֲלָתְךָ״ – ״תְּבִיאֵנוּ״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״תְּבִיאֵמוֹ״; מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמִּתְנַבְּאִין, וְאֵינָן יוֹדְעִין מָה מִתְנַבְּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And** God **resolved** the question **for him:** The verse teaches **both of them. It** is **an inheritance for you from your fathers** and is considered in your possession; **and** also the generation that left Egypt **bequeath but they do not inherit. And this is** the meaning of that **which is written** in the song that the Jewish people sang after the splitting of the Red Sea: **"You will bring them in, and plant them in the mountain of Your inheritance"** (Exodus 15:17). It **is not stated: You will bring us in, rather: "You will bring them in,"** which **teaches that** in their song, the Jewish people **were prophesying** that their generation would never enter Eretz Yisrael, **but they did not know what they were prophesying.**

The resolution, *u-fashtu lei tarvaihu*, is that both readings are correct simultaneously. The land is both a *yerushah* already received — meaning the *yotzei Mitzrayim* are already considered to hold it — and something that they will transmit as *morishin* to the next generation.

### The Irony

This creates a striking irony: *einam yorshin* does not mean they will not be considered to possess it for legal purposes. Rather, it means they will not physically enter and inherit it. They are considered *muchzak* in the land for the purposes of the *bechor*'s double portion, enabling Tzelofchad's firstborn status to generate two shares. Yet at the same time, they themselves will pass through the desert and die without entering *Eretz Yisrael*. They hold it in a legal sense sufficient to transmit it, yet they will never actually come into physical possession of it.

### Prophecy Unknowing

The *gemara* finds this irony embedded in *Shirat HaYam* itself. At the sea, the verse reads *tavi'eimo ve-tita'eimo be-har nachalatecha* — "You will bring *them* and plant *them* in Your holy mountain." Crucially: *tavi'einu lo ne'emar, ela tavi'eimo* — it does not say "You will bring *us*," but "You will bring *them*." Moshe and *Bnei Yisrael* sang a prophecy they did not fully understand: *melamed she-mitnab'im ve-einam yod'im mah mitnab'im* — they prophesied without knowing what they were prophesying. In their moment of triumph at the sea they were already foretelling that it would be a future generation, not themselves, who would enter the land. And yet — by the same logic — they were already considered to hold it.

## Daughters' Approach

### Before All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתַּעֲמֹדְנָה לִפְנֵי מֹשֶׁה וְלִפְנֵי אֶלְעָזָר הַכֹּהֵן וְלִפְנֵי הַנְּשִׂיאִים וְכׇל הָעֵדָה״ – אֶפְשָׁר עָמְדוּ לִפְנֵי מֹשֶׁה כּוּ׳ וְלֹא אָמְרוּ לָהֶן דָּבָר, וְעָמְדוּ לִפְנֵי הַנְּשִׂיאִים וְכׇל הָעֵדָה?!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of the incident involving Zelophehad's daughters. The verse states: **"And they stood before Moses, and before Eleazar the priest, and before the princes and all the congregation"** (Numbers 27:2). The Gemara asks: Is it **possible** that Zelophehad's daughters **stood before Moses** and then Eleazar to ask their question, **and they said nothing to them; and** then the daughters **stood before the princes and all the congregation** to ask them? How would the princes or the congregation know an answer if Moses and Eleazar did not?

The Torah recounts that the daughters stood before Moshe, before Elazar the *kohen*, before the *nesi'im*, and before the entire congregation. The *gemara* examines this progression closely.

### Sequential or Simultaneous

Is this a sequential account — did the daughters approach each authority one after another — or did they approach all of them at once? If it was sequential, then the verse lists Moshe first but he was actually approached last. If it was simultaneous, the verse accurately reflects that all these figures were present together.

### Yoshiyah's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא סָרֵס הַמִּקְרָא וְדׇרְשֵׁהוּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. אַבָּא חָנָן אָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ הָיוּ יוֹשְׁבִין, וְהָלְכוּ וְעָמְדוּ לָהֶן לִפְנֵי כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather, transpose the verse and interpret it:** First, the daughters went to the congregation and ultimately came to Moses, this is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya. Abba Ḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer:** Those enumerated in the verse **were** all **sitting in the house of study, and** Zelophehad's daughters **went and stood before all of them** at once. They were not asked separately; rather, the order of the verse reflects their stature.

Rabbi Yoshiyah holds that they went to each authority in turn, working their way upward. They approached the entire congregation, then the *nesi'im*, then Elazar, and only finally Moshe — yet the verse, out of deference to Moshe's honor, mentions him first while implying through context that the others preceded him. The reason the other figures appear in the verse at all is precisely because the approach was sequential; each received the petition and evidently lacked the authority or knowledge to resolve it.

### Abba Chanan's View

Abba Chanan in the name of Rabbi Eliezer disagrees. He holds that Moshe was sitting in the *beit midrash* together with everyone — Elazar, the *nesi'im*, and the whole *eidah* — and the daughters approached the entire assembled group at once. The approach was not sequential but a single presentation before all authorities gathered together.

### Student vs. Master

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: חוֹלְקִין כָּבוֹד לְתַלְמִיד בִּמְקוֹם הָרַב, וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין חוֹלְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **With regard to what do they disagree?** One **Sage,** Abba Ḥanan, **holds** that one may **show honor to a student in the presence of the teacher,** such that the verse would mention all the others even though they were in the presence of Moses; **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoshiya, **holds** one may **not show** honor to a student in the presence of the teacher, such that only Moses would have been mentioned if they were all in the same place.

The underlying dispute, the *gemara* explains, is whether *cholkin kavod le-talmid bi-mekom ha-rav* — whether one shows honor to a student in the presence of the master. If one does, then when all were gathered together, there was no impropriety in the verse's acknowledging the *nesi'im* and Elazar alongside Moshe; their mention alongside the supreme authority does not diminish him. This is Abba Chanan's position. Rabbi Yoshiyah holds the opposite: *ein cholkin* — one does not acknowledge students or lesser figures in the master's presence. Therefore, had they all been together, the verse would have mentioned only Moshe. Since the verse does mention the others, it must be because they were approached separately, before Moshe.

### Master's Signal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: חוֹלְקִין, וְהִלְכְתָא: אֵין חוֹלְקִין. קַשְׁיָא הִלְכְתָא אַהִלְכְתָא! הִלְכְתָא אַהִלְכְתָא לָא קַשְׁיָא – הָא דִּפְלִיג לֵיהּ רַבֵּיהּ יְקָרָא, הָא דְּלָא פְּלִיג לֵיהּ רַבֵּיהּ יְקָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that one may **show** honor to a student, **and the *halakha*** is that one may **not show** honor. The Gemara asks: This is **difficult,** as there is a contradiction between **the** one ***halakha* and the** other ***halakha*.** The Gemara answers: The contradiction between **the** one ***halakha* and the** other ***halakha*** is **not difficult,** as **this** ruling, that one may show honor, was stated **where his teacher** himself **accords** the student **honor.** In such a case, others also may show the student honor. And **that** ruling, that one may not show honor, was stated **where his teacher does not accord him honor.**

The *gemara* rules that both positions contain truth: *hilchasa cholkin ve-hilchasa ein cholkin*. The resolution is that the determining factor is whether the master himself has signaled recognition of his student — *ha d'palig lei rabeih yikara*. If the *rav* has indicated, through word or gesture, that he wishes his *talmid* to be acknowledged in his presence, then one does acknowledge the *talmid*, mentioning both master and student. If the *rav* has given no such signal, one does not. The protocol follows the cues the master provides.

## Their Virtues

### Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: בְּנוֹת צְלָפְחָד חַכְמָנִיּוֹת הֵן, דַּרְשָׁנִיּוֹת הֵן, צִדְקָנִיּוֹת הֵן. חַכְמָנִיּוֹת הֵן – שֶׁלְּפִי שָׁעָה דִּבְּרוּ. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיָה מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ יוֹשֵׁב וְדוֹרֵשׁ בְּפָרָשַׁת יְבָמִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יֵשְׁבוּ אַחִים יַחְדָּו״. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אִם כְּבֵן אָנוּ חֲשׁוּבִין – תְּנָה לָנוּ נַחֲלָה כְּבֵן; אִם לָאו – תִּתְיַבֵּם אִמֵּנוּ! מִיָּד – ״וַיַּקְרֵב מֹשֶׁה אֶת מִשְׁפָּטָן לִפְנֵי ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages **taught: The daughters of Zelophehad are wise, they are interpreters** of verses, and **they are righteous.** That **they are wise** can be seen from the fact **that they spoke in accordance with the moment,** i.e., they presented their case at an auspicious time. **As Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak says:** Tradition **teaches that Moses our teacher was sitting and interpreting in** the Torah **portion about men whose married brothers had died childless, as it is stated: "If brothers dwell together,** and one of them dies, and has no child, the wife of the dead shall not be married abroad to one not of his kin; her husband's brother shall come to her, and take her for him as a wife" (Deuteronomy 25:5). The daughters of Zelophehad **said to** Moses: **If we are** each **considered like a son, give us** each **an inheritance like a son;** and **if not, our mother should enter into levirate marriage. Immediately** upon hearing their claim, the verse records: **"And Moses brought their cause before the Lord"** (Numbers 27:5).

A *braita* praises *benot Tzelofchad* with three distinct qualities: they were *chachmaniyot* — wise; *darshaniyot* — skilled explicators of Torah; and *tzidkaniyot* — righteous. Their wisdom, *she-le-fi sha'ah dibru*, consisted of keen awareness of timing — of reading circumstances and waiting for precisely the right moment to present their case.

### Yibum Argument

Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak teaches that the daughters did not simply walk up to Moshe with their petition. Rather, they waited until Moshe was expounding the *parsha* of *yibbum* — levirate marriage — before they approached. The verse in that context reads: *ki yeshvu achim yachdav*, when brothers dwell together and one dies childless, the brother-in-law is obligated in *yibbum*.

### If Sons Then Inherit

The daughters seized on this moment. They asked Moshe: if we are considered like a son — such that we exempt our mother from *yibbum* (which is in fact the operative *halacha*: a daughter exempts the widow just as a son would) — then grant us an inheritance as a son would receive. *Im ke-ven anu chashuvot, t'nah lanu nachalah ke-ven.* And if not — if we are not considered equivalent to a son — then let our mother be subject to *yibbum*. Since it was manifestly clear that their mother was not obligated in *yibbum*, they were essentially demonstrating through reductio: since we are already treated as equivalent to sons in the law of *yibbum*, we should be treated as equivalent to sons in the law of inheritance. Furthermore, *yibbum* and inheritance are conceptually intertwined: the institution of *yibbum* operates on the principle of *yakum al shem achiv ha-met*, that the brother-in-law continues the line and acquires the dead brother's estate. If the daughters are considered a continuation of their father sufficient to obviate the need for *yibbum*, the inheritance should naturally flow through them as well. *Miyad vayakrev Moshe et mishpatan lifnei Hashem* — Moshe recognized immediately that they had raised a compelling argument and brought it before God.

### Darshaniyot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּרְשָׁנִיּוֹת הֵן – שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרוֹת: אִילּוּ הָיָה לוֹ בֵּן, לֹא דִּבַּרְנוּ. וְהָתַנְיָא: ״בַּת״! אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: סְמִי מִכָּאן ״בַּת״. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ הָיָה בַּת לַבֵּן – לֹא דִּבַּרְנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** That **they are interpreters** of verses can be seen from the fact **that they were saying: If** our father **had had a son, we would not have spoken;** but because he had no son, we are filling the role of the heir. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: They would say, if he had had **a daughter,** we would not have spoken? **Rabbi Yirmeya said: Delete from** the *baraita* **here** the word: **Daughter.** As they were themselves daughters, this cannot have been their claim. **Abaye said** that the *baraita* need not be emended, and should be understood as follows: **Even if** there **was a daughter of a son** of Zelophehad, **we would not have spoken,** for she would have been the heir.

Their quality as *darshaniyot* — skilled explicators — is demonstrated by the precision of their legal reasoning. They stated: *ilu haya la ben lo dibarnu* — had our father had a son, we would not have spoken. This formulation reveals sophisticated legal literacy: they were not asserting that daughters are in all circumstances equal to sons, but rather that in the specific absence of sons, daughters step into that role.

### Legal Acumen

One might ask: what text precisely were they expounding, and how did they know the rules of inheritance clearly enough to formulate this argument? The *Rashbam* suggests they were expounding the *pesukim* of *nachala* — specifically *im ben ein lo*. *Tosafot* raises a difficulty: that *parsha* had not yet been transmitted; indeed it was transmitted only in response to their very petition. *Tosafot* therefore suggests they derived their position either from the verse *ve-hitnachaltem otam li-vneichem* — "you shall give it as inheritance to your children" — which could have been read as excluding daughters (*li-vneichem ve-lo li-vnoteichem*), or from the broader understood principle that daughters' inheritance could cause property to pass out of the tribe. They understood the legal landscape sufficiently to know that in the presence of sons they had no claim, and said so directly. It is this combination of textual fluency and principled restraint that earns them the designation *darshaniyot*.

## Righteousness

### Proper Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צִדְקָנִיּוֹת הֵן – שֶׁלֹּא נִישְּׂאוּ אֶלָּא לְהָגוּן לָהֶן. תָּנֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: אֲפִילּוּ קְטַנָּה שֶׁבָּהֶן לֹא נִשֵּׂאת פְּחוּתָה מֵאַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** That **they are righteous** can be seen from the fact **that they** did **not** rush to **marry,** but **rather** waited **to** marry those **fit for them. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov teaches: Even the youngest** to be married **among them was not married** at **less than forty years** of age.

The third quality — *tzidkaniyot* — is illustrated by their conduct in marriage. They did not marry hastily or indiscriminately; they married only those who were genuinely *hagun la-hen*, suitable for them. This is derived from the verse *la-tov be-eineihen tihyena le-nashim* — "they shall be wives to whoever is good in their eyes" — which *chazal* understand not as a license to marry freely, but as a directive and indeed a commendation to exercise patience and discernment in finding an appropriate husband.

### Waiting Till 40

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov teaches in a *braita* that even the youngest of the daughters did not marry until she was forty years old. She waited to find someone truly worthy. *Tosafot* notes that this may also reflect the chronology of events: Tzelofchad died near the beginning of the forty years in the desert, and the story of the daughters takes place near the end, which accounts for some of the elapsed time.

### Fertility Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: נִיסַּת פְּחוּתָה מִבַּת עֶשְׂרִים – יוֹלֶדֶת עַד שִׁשִּׁים, בַּת עֶשְׂרִים – יוֹלֶדֶת עַד אַרְבָּעִים, בַּת אַרְבָּעִים – שׁוּב אֵינָהּ יוֹלֶדֶת! אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁצִּדְקָנִיּוֹת הֵן, נַעֲשָׂה לָהֶן נֵס – כְּיוֹכֶבֶד, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּלֶךְ אִישׁ מִבֵּית לֵוִי, וַיִּקַּח אֶת בַּת לֵוִי״ –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But doesn't Rav Ḥisda say:** If a woman **marries** when she is **less than twenty** years **old,** she is able to **give birth until** she reaches the age of **sixty;** if she marries when she is **twenty** years **old** or older, she is able to **give birth until** she reaches the age of **forty;** if she marries when she is **forty** years **old** or older, she is **no longer** able to **give birth** at all. If so, how could Zelophehad's daughters have waited until the age of forty to marry? **Rather, since they are righteous women, a miracle was performed for them, like** the one done for **Jochebed. As it is written: "And a man of the house of Levi went, and took as a wife a daughter of Levi"** (Exodus 2:1).

This raises an obvious question. Rav Chisda states: a woman who marries before twenty can give birth until sixty; one who waits until her twenties can give birth until her forties; one who waits until her forties — *shuv einah yoledet* — can no longer give birth. If the daughters waited until forty to marry, would they not forfeit the ability to have children? Was this truly wise?

### Miraculous Fertility

The *gemara* answers: *mitoch she-tzidkaniyot hen, na'asu la-hen nes* — because they were righteous, a miracle was performed for them, as it was for Yocheved. Their righteousness in waiting for the right partner was itself the source of their merit, and that merit enabled them to conceive beyond the ordinary boundaries of fertility.

### Yocheved's Age

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶפְשָׁר בַּת מֵאָה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים שָׁנָה, וְקֹרֵא לָהּ: ״בַּת״?! דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: זוֹ יוֹכֶבֶד; שֶׁהוֹרָתָהּ בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וְנוֹלְדָה בֵּין הַחוֹמוֹת – דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר יָלְדָה אֹתָהּ לְלֵוִי בְּמִצְרָיִם״ –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is it **possible** that this is Jochebed? Jochebed was then **130 years old and** the verse **called her a daughter,** indicating one who is very young. Jochebed's age is established based on a tradition concerning the number of Jacob's descendants who came to Egypt, as follows: While the verse states that Leah had thirty-three descendants (Genesis 46:15), only thirty-two were enumerated. This was explained **as Rabbi Ḥama bar Ḥanina says: This** "daughter of Levi" is **Jochebed, whose conception** occurred **on the journey** as the family of Jacob descended to Egypt, **and she was born within the walls,** i.e., having entered Egypt, **as it is written:** "And the name of Amram's wife was Jochebed, the daughter of Levi, **who was born to Levi in Egypt"** (Numbers 26:59).

The parallel to Yocheved is developed at length. The verse in *Shemot* reads: *vayeilech ish mi-beit Levi vayikach et bat Levi* — a man from the house of Levi went and took the daughter of Levi. The *gemara* notes the strange usage: she is called *bat Levi* as though she were young, yet she was 130 years old when Moshe was conceived. How do we know? Rabbi Chama bar Chanina explains: Yocheved was *shehorasah ba-derech ve-nolda bein ha-chomot* — conceived on the journey to Egypt and born between the walls, at the very moment of entry. This resolves the famous numerical discrepancy: when Yaakov's family descended to Egypt, 69 descendants are enumerated; the Torah states that 70 came to Egypt. The missing seventieth is Yocheved, who was born precisely as they arrived. Since *Bnei Yisrael* were in Egypt 210 years, and Moshe was 80 at the time of the Exodus, Yocheved was 130 when she conceived him.

### Youth Restored

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵידָתָהּ בְּמִצְרַיִם, וְהוֹרָתָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּמִצְרַיִם. וְאַמַּאי קָרוּ לַהּ ״בַּת״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר זְבִידָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנּוֹלְדוּ בָּהּ סִימָנֵי נַעֲרוּת, נִתְעַדֵּן הַבָּשָׂר, נִתְפַּשְּׁטוּ הַקְּמָטִין, וְחָזַר הַיּוֹפִי לִמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** One can infer from the verse: **Her birth** was **in Egypt, but her conception was not in Egypt.** Since the Jewish people were in Egypt for 210 years and Moses was eighty years old at the time of the Exodus, Jochebed must have been 130 years old when Moses was born. The Gemara therefore asks: **And why** does the verse **call her "a daughter"? Rav Yehuda bar Zevida says:** This **teaches that** her **signs of youth** miraculously **came into being** again. **The flesh became smooth, the wrinkles were straightened out, and the** youthful **beauty returned to its place.**

Why then is she called *bat* — a daughter, a young woman? Rav Yehuda bar Zvida explains: *melamed she-noldu lah simanei na'arut* — when Amram retook Yocheved as his wife (as we shall see, this was a renewed marriage ceremony), the signs of youth returned to her. *Nisaden ha-basar* — the flesh became supple again. *Nispashtu ha-k'matim* — the wrinkles smoothed out. *Ve-chazar ha-yofi li-mkomo* — her beauty was restored to what it had been. This is the sense in which she is called *bat*: not that she was chronologically young, but that her youth was miraculously restored to her.

### Remarriage Ceremony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּקַּח״?! ״וַיַּחְזוֹר״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר זְבִידָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעָשָׂה לָהּ מַעֲשֵׂה לִקּוּחִין – הוֹשִׁיבָה בְּאַפִּרְיוֹן, וְאַהֲרֹן וּמִרְיָם מְשׁוֹרְרִים לְפָנֶיהָ, וּמַלְאֲכֵי שָׁרֵת אוֹמְרִים: ״אֵם הַבָּנִים שִׂמְחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse concerning Amram's marriage to Jochebed states: "And a man of the house of Levi went, **and took** as a wife a daughter of Levi" (Exodus 2:1). The Gemara asks: Since Jochebed had already been married to Amram for some years, as Miriam and Aaron were already born, the verse **should have** stated: **And** he **took back** as a wife. **Rav Yehuda bar Zevida says:** The wording of the verse **teaches that** Amram **performed for her** a formal **act of marriage** as though he were marrying her for the first time. He **seated her in** a bridal **palanquin [*be'appiryon*], and Aaron and Miriam** were **singing before her, and** the **ministering angels** were **saying: "A joyful mother of children"** (Psalms 113:9).

The verse says *vayikach* — he took her — as though for the first time. But the *gemara* asks: should it not have said *vayachzir* — he took her back — since she had already borne him Miriam and Aharon? Rav Yehuda bar Zvida explains: *melamed she-asah bah ma'aseh likuchin* — he performed a full act of betrothal and marriage with her anew. He seated her in a bridal carriage, *hoshivah be-apiryon*, and made a proper wedding ceremony. *Ve-Aharon u-Miriam meshorerin le-faneha* — Aharon and Miriam rejoiced and sang before her. And the *mal'achei ha-shareit* — the ministering angels — proclaimed: *eim ha-banim semeichah* — "the mother of children rejoices," she who was already mother to two and would soon give birth to Moshe.

### Miriam's Prophecy

This *midrash* is embedded in the broader narrative of Amram's separation from Yocheved. When Pharaoh decreed *kol ha-ben ha-yilod ha-ye'ora tashlichuhu* — that every newborn son be cast into the Nile — Amram separated from Yocheved, and the entire people followed his example. Miriam challenged her father: Pharaoh's decree applies only to sons, but your separation means no children will be born at all — your decree is harsher than Pharaoh's. Moreover, Miriam had already prophesied that the redeemer would be born through her mother — this is the force of *Miriam ha-nevi'ah achos Aharon*, that she is called the sister of Aharon (not yet the sister of Moshe), because her prophecy preceded Moshe's birth. It was Amram who listened to his daughter's wisdom and prophecy, took back Yocheved, and through that reunion Moshe was conceived. The story thus closes with an echo of the larger themes of the *daf*: the wisdom and righteousness of women — Miriam, Yocheved, *benot Tzelofchad* — operating alongside and at times correcting the hesitations of the men around them, including Moshe himself.
