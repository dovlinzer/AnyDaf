# Pesachim 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chagigah Status

### Ben Tema

The daf begins at the bottom of 71b, picking up the ongoing discussion of the status of the *korban chagigah*. We have already encountered the position of Ben Tema, who holds that the *chagigah* is nearly identical to the *korban Pesach* in its essential features: it must be a sheep in its first year, roasted, and eaten during the day and night — so similar to the *korban Pesach* that the two are difficult to distinguish. For Ben Tema, the *chagigah* carries the force of a strict obligation.

### Chachamim Dispute

The *chachamim* disagree. In their view, the *chagigah* follows the normal rules of a *shlamim*, retaining its independent identity — cattle, two years old, and so forth — while remaining unique in that it is brought on the fourteenth together with the *korban Pesach*, rather than at the onset of *yom tov* when a *chagigah* would ordinarily be brought. Within this disagreement lies a further question: even if one rejects Ben Tema's view on the essential character of the *chagigah*, one might still hold that it constitutes a strict obligation. The practical consequence of that question is significant.

### Kvua Lo Zman

Anything that is a strict obligation tied to a specific time is brought even on *Shabbat* and even in a state of *tumah*. The *gemara* uses the phrase *korban tzibur*, but as noted in earlier sessions, the truly operative criterion is that it is a *korban she-kavua lo zman* — an offering whose time is fixed. It is therefore possible, on the view that the *chagigah* is a real *chiyuv*, that it could override *Shabbat*. As for the *chagigah* brought on the *chag* itself — although the ideal occasion is the first day of *yom tov*, it may be made up on any subsequent day of the *chag*, and for that reason it does not override *Shabbat*.

### Ben Dortai

The *chachamim* dispute the stringent position on this point as well. This was also the subject of a heated controversy involving Yehuda ben Dortai, who separated himself from the *chachamim* over the question of whether the *chagigah* could be brought on *Shabbat* or in a state of *tumah*. Two questions thus run through the entire discussion: first, whether the *chagigah* is a strict obligation or merely a means of ensuring that the *korban Pesach* is eaten in a state of satiety; and second, how closely its identity tracks that of the *korban Pesach* itself.

## Holiday Korbanot

### Three Korbanot

Before proceeding further, it is worth pausing to survey the *korbanot* that *chazal* require on every *yom tov*. On *Pesach*, of course, one brings the *Pesach* on the fourteenth — it bears noting that *Pesach* is not the name of the *yom tov*, which is properly called *Chag HaMatzot*; *Pesach* refers specifically to the *korban Pesach* and the day of its offering. Alongside the *korban Pesach* comes the special *korban chagigah* called *chagigat arba'ah asar* — a *chagigah* brought even before the *chag* itself begins. As already discussed, there is debate as to whether this serves as a strict obligation or merely a practical measure to ensure the *korban Pesach* is eaten in satiety. To the extent the *chagigah* connotes the status of the day as a *chag*, one might infer that even the fourteenth carries something of that character — a point reflected in the discussions about refraining from *melachah* on *erev Pesach*. The *beitzah* on the Seder plate also recalls the *chagigah*; we do not rule like Ben Tema, and so we use something visually distinct from the *zroa* to make that difference clear.

### Olat Re'iyah

The first of the three standard obligations on every *yom tov* is the *mitzvah* of *olat re'iyah*. The Torah commands that all males appear before God — *yera'eh kol zchurecha* — and adds: *ve-lo yer'u panai reikam*, "do not appear before Me empty-handed." From this the *poskim* derive a positive obligation to bring an *olah* when appearing at the *Beit HaMikdash* on *yom tov*. If one does not appear at all, one has merely failed to fulfill the positive obligation of *re'iyah*. But if one does appear without a *korban* in hand, one additionally transgresses the negative prohibition of *ve-lo yer'u panai reikam*. This *olat re'iyah* is discussed in the first *mishnah* of Masechet Chagigah.

### Chagigah Offering

The second *korban* is the *chagigah* itself. When the Torah uses the word *chag* — as in *chag la-Shem* or *chag ha-Sukkot* — *chazal* understand the term *tachogu* to mean that one marks the day as a *chag* through the bringing of a *korban chagigah*, which takes the form of a *shlamim*. The owners eat the meat in Jerusalem over two days and the intervening night. The primary obligation falls on the first day of *yom tov*; if not fulfilled then, one may make it up on any other day of the *chag*. On Sukkot, one may even make it up on *Shemini Atzeret*. On Shavuot, although it is a single-day *chag*, the principle of equating the *chagim* allows for six days of makeup, parallel to Pesach.

### Shlamei Simcha

The third *korban* is the *shlamei simcha*. The Torah's command *ve-samachta lifnei Hashem Elokecha* is understood by *chazal* as mandating *simcha* through the bringing of *korbanot*. This is a separate obligation from the *chagigah*, yielding a third *korban* of *shlamim* brought for the purpose of *simcha*, whose meat is eaten in the context of the *Mikdash* and Jerusalem. When *chazal* say *ein simcha ella be-vasar shlamim*, the original formulation referred specifically to these *shlamim*; the joy achieved through bringing and eating a *korban* in connection with God is itself the *simcha*. The derived practice of eating meat on *yom tov* in our day reflects this principle in its post-*mikdash* form.

### Korban Shutafin

One might consider whether one could fulfill the obligations of *olat re'iyah*, *chagigah*, or *shlamei simcha* collectively, pooling resources across a group. These are not *korbanot tzibur* — they remain in the category of *korban yachid*. The broader question of *korban shutafin* — a partnership *korban* — remains somewhat unsettled as a category, and when and how it applies is not straightforwardly resolved.

## Slaughter Timing

### Ulla/R. Elazar

Four lines from the bottom of the daf, the *gemara* records: *amar Ulla amar Rabbi Elazar* — Ulla says in the name of Rabbi Elazar the *Amora* — that *shlamim she-shachtan me-erev Yom Tov*, if one slaughtered a *shlamim* on *erev Yom Tov* and then ate it that night, one does not fulfill one's obligation either of *simcha* or of *chagigah*. This applies whether it is *erev Pesach*, *erev Sukkot*, or *erev Shavuot*: if the *korban* was brought the day before and the meat was consumed that night — or even over the following day, since *shlamim* may be eaten for two days and the intervening night — neither obligation is discharged.

### Zvicha B'Sha'at Simcha

Why does slaughtering *erev Yom Tov* fail to fulfill the *simcha* obligation? The reason given is *mishum simcha de-chtiv ve-zavachta ve-samachta* — because the verse says "you shall slaughter and you shall rejoice." Although that verse concerns the offerings brought at Har Eival and is not directly about *simchat yom tov*, *chazal* derive from it a relationship: *be'inan zvicha be-sha'at simcha ve-leka* — the *shechita* must be connected to the joy. The fulfillment of *simcha* is achieved not merely through eating the meat of a *korban*, but through the act of bringing it. Joy in this sense is not mere physical pleasure; it is the specific *simcha* that arises from the connection with God through the act of offering. The *shechita* must therefore occur on *yom tov* itself, not the day before.

### Davar Shebechova

The reason slaughtering *erev Yom Tov* fails for *chagigah* is more fundamental. The *gemara* states: *havei davar she-be-chovah* — the *chagigah* is an obligatory *korban*, and *kol davar she-be-chovah eino ba ela min ha-chulin* — any obligatory offering may only be fulfilled from *chulin*, from an animal not previously sanctified and not already designated for another purpose. One may not bring an animal already sanctified as a *shlamim* — even as a *neder* one intended to fulfill — and have it do double duty as one's *chagigah*. The *chagigah* demands a fresh designation. As Tosafot points out, this problem is independent of timing: even had the animal been brought on the fifteenth rather than the fourteenth, the pre-existing sanctification would still disqualify it from serving as one's *chagigah*.

### Simcha Exception

One might ask: if *shlamei simcha* is itself an obligation, why does the same rule not apply — why can a previously sanctified animal be used for *shlamei simcha*? Rashi quotes the *midreshei halacha* that establish *shlamei simcha* as the single exception to this rule. Although it is an obligation, one may use an animal already sanctified for a different purpose — say, a *shlamim* set aside to fulfill an old *neder* — and have it count for *shlamei simcha* as well. This exception makes conceptual sense if the emphasis of *shlamei simcha* lies in the eating of the meat rather than the act of bringing. The bringing of that particular *korban* serves its original purpose; the eating of the meat in the context of *yom tov* constitutes the *shlamei simcha* — the two functions are not truly in conflict.

### Tosafot

Tosafot clarifies that the *gemara*'s emphasis on the animal having been slaughtered on the fourteenth is somewhat curious. The disqualification of the *chagigah* arises from the pre-existing sanctification, not from the timing — and therefore even had the animal been brought on the fifteenth, it still could not have counted toward the *chagigah* obligation. Tosafot's point is simply to ensure this is understood clearly. The case of *shlamei simcha*, by contrast, carries a separate timing problem: according to the opinion under discussion, the *shlamim* must be slaughtered on *yom tov* itself; bringing it before *yom tov* disqualifies it for *simcha* regardless of when the meat is eaten.

## Timing Dispute

### V'Hayita Ach Samei'ach

The *gemara* now attempts to find support for the claim that *shechita* must occur on *yom tov* itself. The verse regarding Sukkot — *ve-hayita ach same'ach* — concludes a passage that speaks of seven days of *simcha* (*v'samachta be-chagecha shivat yamim tachog*). The addition of *ve-hayita ach same'ach* is taken to include something beyond the seven days: *lerabot leilo Yom Tov ha'acharon le-simcha* — it extends the *simcha* even to the night of the last *yom tov*, the night of *Shemini Atzeret*, allowing one to eat the meat of a *chagigah* then and thereby fulfill the obligation.

### Ach Division

The *gemara* challenges: you say this verse includes the night of *Shemini Atzeret* — but perhaps it should include the first night of Sukkot, the night of the fifteenth, so that one who slaughtered on the fourteenth could eat that night and be *yotzei*? The verse answers with the word *ach*: *chilek* — it divides, it limits. The extension adds one night only, not both.

### Last Night Reason

The *gemara* asks: given that we may include only one of the two nights, why do we choose the last night of the *chag* rather than the first? The answer offered is: *lav mishum de'ein lo be-ma yismach* — because on the first night of Sukkot, there is nothing with which to perform *simcha*. Since *shechita* must occur on *yom tov*, one cannot have slaughtered the day before; therefore the first night is excluded. The *simcha* obligation has nothing available to activate it the first night, whereas the last night is preceded by a full period of *yom tov* during which *korbanot* may have been slaughtered.

### Daytime Focus

The *gemara* immediately deflects this reasoning. Perhaps, it suggests, one could slaughter before *yom tov* and still fulfill the obligation — and yet the last night would still be preferred over the first. Why? Because the *baraita* itself gives its own reason: *mah ra'ita lerabot leilo Yom Tov ha'acharon u-lehotzi leilo Yom Tov ha'rishon* — why include the last night and exclude the first? *Marbeh ani leilo Yom Tov ha'acharon she-yesh simcha lefanav* — I include the last night because *simcha* precedes it; *u-motzi ani leilo Yom Tov ha'rishon she-ein simcha lefanav* — I exclude the first night because it is not preceded by *simcha*. This reasoning does not require that *shechita* be on *yom tov* — it only explains the preference for the last night on independent grounds. The logic appears to rest on a *chazal*ic tendency to read *yom* as literally daytime. Beginning from the assumption that *shivat yamim* means seven days rather than seven nights, the nights must be included by extension — and the natural night to include is the night that follows the day, not the night that precedes it. That explains why the night of *Shemini Atzeret* is preferred: it follows the seventh day, whereas the night of the fifteenth precedes the first day rather than following it.

### Rashi/Tosafot

Rashi holds that the verse includes only the night of *Shemini Atzeret*, not its daytime. Given the framework just described — starting from seven days, then extending to include the following nights — the night of *Shemini Atzeret* is included, but the day of *Shemini Atzeret* is not drawn in by this logic. Tosafot disagrees. Based on other considerations, Tosafot holds that once the night of *Shemini Atzeret* is included, the day of *Shemini Atzeret* follows as well — since a *shlamim* is canonically eaten for a day and the following night, if the seventh day is the basis and its night is included, the day following that night should also be included. The practical conclusion remains that one could, strictly speaking, slaughter on the fourteenth and eat the meat on the day of the fifteenth and be *yotzei* — the attempt to prove that *shechita* must be on *yom tov* from this verse has not succeeded. The *gemara* treats it only as a *mesayea*, a supporting indication for a claim that requires further examination.

## Rav Yosef Challenge

### 14th Chagigah

The *gemara* records: *mativ Rav Yosef* — Rav Yosef challenges the claim that *shechita* must occur on *yom tov*. The *baraita* quoted earlier in connection with Ben Tema states that the *chagigah* brought on the fourteenth fulfills the obligation of *simcha* but does not fulfill the obligation of *chagigah* of *yom tov*. According to Rav Yosef's reading, one slaughters this *chagigah* on the fourteenth alongside the *korban Pesach* and eats it that night — the night of the fifteenth — thereby satisfying the *simcha* obligation. This appears to be a direct contradiction: here is a case where the *shechita* occurred on the fourteenth and the *simcha* is nonetheless fulfilled.

### Zvicha Timing

The difficulty is sharp: according to the requirement of *zvicha be-sha'at simcha*, the *shechita* must occur at the time of *simcha* — on *yom tov* itself. Here the *shechita* was on the fourteenth, yet the *baraita* says the *simcha* obligation is discharged. This seems to refute the rule entirely.

### Rav Idi Bar Avin

Rav Idi bar Avin resolves the difficulty: *she-ikvei ve-shachta* — the animal was held over. The *baraita* does not mean the animal was slaughtered on the fourteenth and the *simcha* was fulfilled by eating it that night. Rather, the animal was designated for the *chagigah* of the fourteenth but was not actually slaughtered then; it was held over and slaughtered on the fifteenth. On that reading, the *shechita* did occur on *yom tov*, and the *simcha* is fulfilled on *yom tov*. The animal's original sanctification for the *chagigah* of the fourteenth does not disqualify it for *shlamei simcha*, since *shlamei simcha* is the one obligatory category that permits this form of double duty.

Rav Ashi adds corroboration: *hachi nami mistabra* — this reading is indeed more reasonable. For if you assume the animal was slaughtered on the fourteenth, you immediately run into an insurmountable problem. When exactly would one eat this *chagigah* in fulfillment of *simcha*? Not at night — the *mitzvah* of *simcha* through *korbanot* begins with the day, not the preceding night, as established earlier. And by the daytime of the fifteenth, according to Ben Tema — who holds this *chagigah* is like the *korban Pesach* — the meat is already *pasul*, since Ben Tema holds it expires at daybreak. There is thus no window in which the meat is both fit for consumption and subject to the *simcha* obligation. This confirms that the *baraita* must be speaking of an animal slaughtered on the fifteenth, not the fourteenth.

## 8-Day Challenge

### Rava Objection

*Mativ Rava* — Rava now challenges the requirement that *shechita* occur on *yom tov*. The *mishnah* in Sukkah establishes that *hallel* and *simcha* over Sukkot span eight days — including *Shemini Atzeret*. If *shechita* must occur on *yom tov* itself, then *ve-im amarta ve-i'nan zvicha be-sha'at simcha*, one will frequently find oneself with only seven days of *shlamei simcha* rather than eight.

### Shabbat Problem

The problem arises when the first day of Sukkot falls on *Shabbat*. *Shlamei simcha* are not brought on *Shabbat*. If one could slaughter on *erev Yom Tov* and eat the meat the following day, there would always be a remedy: even when the day itself is *Shabbat*, one could have slaughtered the day before and eaten the meat that *Shabbat*. But if the *shechita* must be on *yom tov* — and that *yom tov* is *Shabbat* — there is no permissible day to slaughter, and the first day of *simcha* is lost. One would then have only seven days available, contradicting the *mishnah*'s statement of eight.

### Se'ir Chatat

The *gemara* proposes a resolution in the name of Rav Huna son of Rav Yehuda: *misamcha be-sei'rei ha-regalim* — one fulfills *simcha* by eating the meat of the *se'ir ha-chatat*, the goat sin-offering that accompanies each *musaf*. Since the *musaf* includes a *chatat* whose meat is available, perhaps eating that meat constitutes *simcha* even when no *shlamim* can be brought.

### Two Objections

Rava raises two objections. First: when the *se'ir ha-chatat* is brought on *Shabbat*, cooking it for consumption would require performing *melachah* — and one does not perform *melachah* on *Shabbat* to facilitate eating the *musaf korbanot*. The meat would therefore have to be eaten raw. *Ve-simcha be-chai leka* — there is no joy in eating raw meat. Second: *kohanim ochlin, Yisrael lo masmchan* — only *kohanim* eat the *chatat*; the rest of the people have no access to it. It cannot therefore provide a general basis for *simcha*. Both objections together dispose of the proposed solution.

### Rav Papa

With Rav Huna's solution eliminated and the difficulty left standing, Rav Papa offers an alternative: *ella amar Rav Papa misamcho bi-ksut nekiya ve-yayin yashan* — one achieves *simcha* through fine clothes and aged wine. This is precisely the alternative we rely upon today in the absence of *korbanot*: without *shlamei simcha*, one uses wine and new clothing to mark the joy of *yom tov*. The answer is admittedly weak — if the fundamental obligation is a *chiyuv korban*, a substitution of wine and clothing is a thin replacement — but it is the *gemara*'s conclusion in this context.

## Simcha Nature

### Korban Joy

Two broader points emerge from this sugya regarding the nature of *simcha* and how it is experienced through *korbanot*. First, within the framework of the *mikdash*, the emphasis of *simcha* lies in the bringing of the *korban*, not merely in the eating — the joy of connection with God achieved through the act of offering.

### Modern Practice

In our time, without *korbanot*, the obligation of *simcha* operates differently. Some are careful to eat meat on *yom tov* in honor of the principle of *ein simcha ella be-vasar* — and some observe this by eating *milchigs* on Shavuot and then serving a meat meal afterward. However, a closer look at the sources reveals that when the *gemara* considers the absence of *korbanot*, it does not even mention meat — it mentions wine and fine clothing. Other *gemarot* list *basar va-yayin* among the alternatives, but the strict requirement of meat is not as clearly mandated as is sometimes assumed. The meat referred to by *ein simcha ella be-vasar* was specifically the meat of *shlamim*; in the absence of that context, the obligation takes different forms.

### Context Matters

A second point concerns the starting point of *simcha* during the *chag*. Within the *korban* framework, the *simcha* obligation begins on the day of the fifteenth, not the preceding night — a conclusion emerging from all the foregoing analysis. It would be absurd, however, to say that a guest arriving on the first night of the *chag* need not be welcomed with a proper meal because *simcha* does not technically begin until the following morning. The timing rules developed in the *gemara* are specific to the *korban* context, with its halakhic emphasis on the *yom* as the primary unit and the night as derivative. In the non-*korban* context, *simcha* plainly begins at the onset of the *chag*.

### Rambam One

A final question concerns the extent of the obligation: must one bring a *korban* every single day to satisfy *simcha*, or every other day since the meat can be eaten over two days, or is a single *korban* for the entire *chag* sufficient? The Rambam appears to hold that the *mitzvah* of *simcha* in the context of a *korban* requires bringing at least one additional *korban* over the course of the *chag* — not necessarily one per day or one per two days. When the *gemara* extends *simcha* to the last night, the Rambam understands this to mean that if one has not yet had opportunity to fulfill the *mitzvah*, even the final night remains available — not that there is a recurring daily or two-daily obligation.

## Rava Response

### Rabin Report

The *gemara* now turns to a contrasting tradition: *ki ata Rabin amar Rabbi Elazar* — when Rabin came from Eretz Yisrael, he brought a different version of what Rabbi Elazar had said. This stands in direct opposition to what Ulla had reported in Rabbi Elazar's name.

### Eating as Key

According to Rabin's version: *shlamim she-shachtan me-erev Yom Tov yotzei ban mishum simcha* — if one slaughtered a *shlamim* on *erev Yom Tov* and eats the meat on *yom tov*, one does fulfill the obligation of *simcha*. The *simcha* is not contingent on the *shechita* occurring on *yom tov*; what matters is when the meat is eaten. *Lo be'inan zvicha be-sha'at simcha* — there is no requirement that the *shechita* occur at the time of *simcha*. Furthermore, the exception for *shalmei simcha* applies: even an animal sanctified for a prior *korban* and slaughtered before *yom tov* can count for *simcha*, as long as its meat is eaten on *yom tov*. This view strongly emphasizes the eating rather than the bringing — in a manner somewhat analogous to the *korban Pesach*, which is brought entirely for the purpose of *achila*.

### Chagigah Separate

This more lenient view regarding *simcha* does not extend to *chagigah*. *V'lo mishum chagigah — havei davar she-be-chovah, v'chol davar she-be-chovah eino ba ella min ha-chulin*: the *chagigah* remains an obligatory *korban*, and the rule that obligatory *korbanot* must come from *chulin* — from animals not previously sanctified — is not waived. Tosafot further notes that even had the animal been brought on the fifteenth itself, the pre-existing sanctification would disqualify it for *chagigah*. The two obligations — *simcha* and *chagigah* — thus diverge sharply: one is governed primarily by when the meat is eaten, the other by the status of the animal at the time of designation.

### Rambam Ruling

The Rambam rules in accordance with this opinion of Rabin. The *halacha* follows the view that *shlamim* slaughtered before *yom tov* fulfill the *simcha* obligation as long as the meat is eaten on *yom tov* — and that *zvicha be-sha'at simcha* is not required.

## Imurim Expiry

### Rav Kahana Q

The *gemara* transitions to a new question raised by Rav Kahana: *minayin le-imurei chagigas chamisha asar she-nifsalim be-lina* — from where do we know that the *imurim*, the fats and internal portions of the *chagigah* of the fifteenth, become invalid by lingering overnight? In a normal *shlamim*, the meat may be eaten over two days and the intervening night. But the *imurim* — the portions that must be burned on the *mizbe'ach* — have an earlier expiration: they become *pasul* by the following night, before the meat itself expires. The *hakrava* portion of the *korban* closes sooner than the *achila* portion. This is true not only of the *chagigah* but of all *shlamim*, as Tosafot notes — the *chagigah* is merely the vehicle for the discussion.

### Lo Yalin

The source is the verse: *ve-lo yalin chelev chagi ad boker* — do not let the fat of My *chag* remain until the morning. This verse prohibits leaving the *imurim* overnight. But which morning? The question is whether the verse means the morning immediately following the offering or some subsequent morning.

### Boker Rishon

Rav Kahana's derivation proceeds as follows: the verse immediately following is *reshit bikurei admatecha* — the firstfruits of your soil. Reading *boker* and *reshit* together yields *boker reshit* — the first morning. The *imurim* thus become *pasul* at the first morning following the offering, not some later point. This establishes that the *hakrava* expiration precedes the *achila* expiration by an entire day.

### Yosef Challeng

Rav Yosef challenges this derivation: *ta'ama de-chtiv reshit* — the only reason we know it means the first morning is because the verse contains the word *reshit*. *Ha lo katav reshit, hava amina mai boker, boker sheni* — had the Torah not written *reshit*, one might have thought that *boker* refers to the second morning rather than the first. But how could that possibly have been entertained as a reading? *Mi ika midi de-vasar ipsalei me-orta ve-imurin ad tzafra* — is there any case in which the meat becomes *pasul* that very night, and yet the fats remain valid until the following morning? That would mean the fats receive *more* time than the meat — a seemingly impossible inversion, given that the *imurim* are the *hakrava* component, which must logically expire before, not after, the *achila* component.

### Abaye Response

Abaye responds: *almah la* — why is that impossible? *Harei Pesach le-Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah* — consider the *korban Pesach* according to Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, who holds that the *Pesach* may only be eaten until midnight. According to this view, *de-vasar ipsalei me-chatzot* — the meat's time expires at midnight — *ve-imurin ad tzafra* — yet the fats may still be burned until morning. So there is a genuine precedent for the fats having more time than the meat. Rav Yosef's challenge was therefore not unreasonable: it was at least conceivable that *boker* might mean the second morning, since fats can in principle outlast the meat.

Rava then clarifies what was really troubling Rav Yosef. Although the precise formulation does not match his stated words, Rashi notes that the *gemara* here reconstructs what was actually bothering him. The deeper concern is this: *mi ika midi de-ilu tanna le-vasar lo ba'ei reshit, Rav Kahana le-imurin ba'ei reshit* — is there something for which, when the Torah restricts the *meat*, it does not use the word *reshit*, yet when restricting the *imurim*, Rav Kahana requires *reshit* to determine the morning? The word *boker* on its own, as we will see, already means the immediate next morning — and a *baraita* about the meat itself confirms this, assuming *boker* to mean the immediate morning without needing *reshit*. If *boker* automatically defaults to the first morning, why does Rav Kahana need *reshit* at all to establish this for the *imurim*?

## Baraita Unpack

### Repetition

The *gemara* turns to a *baraita* that appears to repeat itself in puzzling fashion. The verse states: *lo yalin min ha-basar asher tizbach ba-erev ba-yom ha-rishon la-boker* — do not let the meat that you slaughter that evening remain until morning, on the first day, until morning. The doubling is immediately striking. The *baraita* sets out to interpret this and in doing so appears to loop back on itself: it poses a question, offers a reading, then reintroduces what seems like the same question.

### Two Chagigot

The *baraita* teaches: *limed al chagigat arba'ah asar she-ne'echelet li-shnei yamim ve-laila echad* — this verse teaches that the *chagigah* of the fourteenth is eaten for two days and the intervening night. But that reading requires unpacking. The phrase *tizbach ba-erev* identifies the *korban* as one slaughtered in the afternoon — and the only *korban* other than the *Pesach* slaughtered in the afternoon is *chagigat arba'ah asar*. The phrase *ba-yom ha-rishon*, "on the first day," indicates that the eating continues through the fifteenth; and *la-boker*, "until morning," tells us it does not extend past the morning following the fifteenth, i.e., the morning of the sixteenth.

But initially, one might have read the verse differently: perhaps *la-boker* refers to the morning of the fifteenth — meaning the *chagigah* of the fourteenth may only be eaten for one day and one night. The *baraita* then re-poses this possibility: perhaps the verse means the first morning, and the *chagigah* of the fourteenth expires at the morning of the fifteenth. The *baraita* also raises the possibility of reading the verse as referring to two *chagigot* — one slaughtered *ba-erev* (the fourteenth) and one slaughtered *ba-yom ha-rishon* (the fifteenth) — with each expiring at its own respective next morning.

### Two-Day Problem

But this reading generates an impossible result. We have a firm tradition that some *chagigah* in the world is eaten for two days and the intervening night — *chagigah ne'echelet li-shnei yamim ve-laila echad*. If both the *chagigah* of the fourteenth and the *chagigah* of the fifteenth each expire at their own next morning, then neither is eaten for two full days. Every *chagigah* that exists would be limited to one day and a night. That contradicts the established principle.

### Neder Solution

The resolution is found in a separate verse: *im neder o nedava zevach korbano ba-yom zivcho ye'achel u-mi-macharat ve-ha-notar mimenu ba-esh yisaref* — if it is a *neder* or *nedava*, one eats the *korban* on the day it is slaughtered and the day after, and what remains is burned. From this verse, *chazal* derive — through an extra textual element, which Rashi identifies as the word *im* — that the *chagigah* of the fifteenth is eaten for two days and the intervening night. Once the *chagigah* of the fifteenth is relocated to this verse for its two-day span, the original verse — *lo yalin min ha-basar asher tizbach ba-erev ba-yom ha-rishon la-boker* — is now read as referring exclusively to the *chagigah* of the fourteenth.

### Fourteenth Proof

With the entire verse now speaking of the fourteenth's *chagigah*, the phrase *ba-yom ha-rishon* tells us that the eating extends through the fifteenth, and *la-boker* means the morning following the fifteenth — the morning of the sixteenth. So the *chagigah* of the fourteenth is also eaten for two days: the fourteenth and the fifteenth. And the word *boker* in this context refers to the *boker* that follows *ba-yom ha-rishon* — the second morning from when the *korban* was slaughtered. This is only because the verse itself establishes that eating continues through the fifteenth; *boker* is then naturally read as the morning that ends that extended period.

## Boker Meaning

### Bayom Context

This analysis yields a crucial methodological conclusion. The only reason *boker* in this verse refers to a morning two days removed from the slaughter is because the verse itself — through the phrase *ba-yom ha-rishon* — establishes that the eating period extends to the fifteenth. Given that context, *boker* means the morning that follows *yom ha-rishon*. Remove that context, and *boker* would revert to its default meaning.

### Default Meaning

The default meaning of *boker* is always the immediate next morning. *Ha kol heicha de-chtiv boker stam, boker rishon* — wherever the Torah writes *boker* without further context, it means the first morning, the morning immediately following. This holds *af al gav de-lo ktiv beih rishon* — even without the word *reshit* or *rishon* appearing explicitly. The word *boker* on its own already implies immediacy. The lesson of *reshit* in the verse *ve-lo yalin chelev chagi ad boker* is thus not to restrict *boker* to the first morning — that would be its meaning regardless — but rather to confirm it in a context where one might otherwise have been confused by the parallel to the meat's longer window.

### Imurim Rule

Applying this conclusion to the *imurim*: the fats of the *chagigah* — and of any *shlamim* — expire at the end of the night immediately following the *hakrava*. The *lo yalin* prohibition means that the *imurim* must be entirely burned on the *mizbe'ach* by the morning following the day of offering. This morning comes before the meat itself expires — the meat may be eaten through the following day — establishing a clear asymmetry: the *imurim* deadline is the night, the meat deadline extends further. The *hakrava* closes with the first morning; the *achila* continues beyond it.
