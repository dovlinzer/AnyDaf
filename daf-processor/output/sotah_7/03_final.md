# Sotah 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Private to Judicial

### Previous Context

The previous *mishna* dealt with circumstances that would prevent the *sotah* procedure from going forward — cases where the woman remained forbidden to eat *terumah*, including situations involving *edim* in *medinat hayam* and cases of admission. That *mishna* serves as a necessary background, because the present *mishna* now addresses what happens once none of those impediments apply: the procedure is cleared to proceed, and we turn to a description of how it would actually unfold.

### Woman to Beis Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד עוֹשֶׂה לָהּ? מוֹלִיכָהּ לְבֵית דִּין שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna details the procedure for administering the drinking of the bitter water of a *sota*. **What does** her husband **do with her** after she secluded herself with the man about whom she had been warned? **He brings her to the court that is** found **in that location**

The *mishna* opens with the question *keitzad oso lah* — what would you do with her once she has acquired the status of a *sotah*, having been warned and having gone into seclusion? The answer: *molicho l'veit din she'be'oto makom* — you bring her to the *beit din* of that locale.

This is already a striking departure from the *Torah*'s presentation of the *sotah* procedure. In the *Torah*, the husband brings his wife directly to the *Beis HaMikdash* for the ritual. Here, however, the first stop is the *beit din*. That choice is not incidental — it is a statement about what this process fundamentally is.

### Chazal's Authority

The *mishna*'s framing makes a clear declaration: this is a matter of *din*. What had begun as a husband's jealousy, his private suspicion of his wife, has been converted into a judicial proceeding. The witnesses, the circumstantial evidence, the formal warning — all of it transforms the dynamic from one of passion and individual initiative into one of institutional supervision and objective adjudication.

This move is characteristic of *Chazal* more broadly. The authority of *Chazal* and *beit din* is grounded in the *pasuk* of *al pi hatorah asher yorucha v'al hamishpat asher yomru lecha ta'aseh lo tasur yamin u'smol* — but that *pasuk* in the *Torah* is addressed *el hakohanim v'el hashofet asher yihyeh bayamim hahem*, with the *kohanim* far more prominent than the singular *shofet*. At the time of the *Beis HaMikdash*, institutional authority resided far more with the *kohanim* than with the rabbis. Yet *Chazal*, in describing the reality of how these procedures were conducted, consistently assert their own authority. We find this, for example, in the way the *beit din* would determine whether a *kohen* was fit for *avodah* when questions arose about his *yichus*.

The *sotah* procedure illustrates both dimensions of this assertion simultaneously. First, *Chazal* are taking something rooted in the husband's passion and making it objective — grounded in testimony and evidence rather than in one man's unilateral discretion. Second, they are taking something that belonged to the *kohanim* and the *Beis HaMikdash* and placing it under the authority of *beit din*. Both moves are reflected in the single word *keitzad*: what do you do? You bring her to a *beit din*.

## Two Sages

### Shema Yavo Aleha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹסְרִין לוֹ שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁמָּא יָבֹא עָלֶיהָ בַּדֶּרֶךְ
>
> **Translation:** and the court **provides him** with **two Torah scholars** to accompany him, **lest he engage in sexual intercourse with her on the way** to the Temple

Once the local *beit din* has received the case, the *mishna* continues: *u'mosru lo shnei talmidei chachamim* — they hand over two Torah sages to the husband, who will serve as escorts, accompanying the couple on their journey to *Yerushalayim*. The stated reason is *shema yavo aleha baderech* — lest he have relations with her along the way.

This detail is layered with significance. Once a woman has acquired the status of a *sotah*, husband and wife are forbidden to have relations with each other until the matter is resolved. The concern is not merely one of general propriety but of practical efficacy: we learn that the *sotah* waters function only when the husband himself is free of sin. If he were to violate the prohibition en route, the entire proceeding would be undermined. The waters would no longer be effective.

The presence of the two escorts therefore serves a concrete halachic purpose. But notice what it also does to the character of the whole process. At no point from here on is this the husband dragging his wife to judgment. The *beit din* has dispatched its agents; they are the ones accompanying this woman to the next stage. The husband is no longer the driver of events — the *beit din* is. This interestingly mirrors the description in *Yoma* of the *beit din*'s oversight of the *Kohen Gadol* before *Yom Kippur*, where the *mishna* uses the very same language: *mosru lo shnei talmidei chachamim*, the *beit din* hands over two sages to accompany the *Kohen Gadol* as he prepares for the *avodat hayom*. In that context too, a *Beis HaMikdash* ritual of the highest order is being overseen and structured by the *beit din*.

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תְּרֵי וְאִיהוּ — הָא תְּלָתָא. לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בָּעִיר, אֲבָל בַּדֶּרֶךְ — עַד דְּאִיכָּא שְׁלֹשָׁה, שֶׁמָּא יִצְטָרֵךְ אֶחָד מֵהֶן לִנְקָבָיו, וְנִמְצָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן מִתְיַיחֵד עִם הָעֶרְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara assumes that the requirement for there to be two Torah scholars is to avoid the prohibition against a woman being alone with a man. The Gemara notes: **Two** additional men **and he,** the husband, **are three** people altogether. **Let us say** that **this** mishna **supports** the opinion of **Rav, as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** When they taught that it is permitted for a woman to be secluded with two men, **they taught** that this is permitted **only in the town** (see *Kiddushin* 80b). **But on the way,** when traveling, this is not permitted **unless there are three** men with the woman. The reason for this stringency is that if there are only two men with her, **perhaps one will need to relieve himself** and will seek privacy, **and it will be found that one of them is in seclusion with a woman forbidden to him.**

The *Gemara* asks why two representatives were needed rather than one. The fact that there are two escorts plus the husband makes three men in total, and the *Gemara* suggests this might support a teaching of Rav. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *lo shanu ela be'ir* — the rule that two men and a woman do not constitute *yichud* applies only when they are in a city, but *aval be'derech*, on a journey, *ad de'ika tlasa*, you need three men to avoid concern of *yichud*. The reason: *shema yitztarech echad me'hem le'nekavav* — one of the men may need to attend to his bodily needs. In a city, stepping away for that purpose does not create meaningful seclusion; but on a journey in open terrain, the man who steps away from the group to find some privacy in the brush or forest will leave the remaining man and the woman alone in what would constitute *yichud*. Hence the need for a third person to ensure someone is always present.

On this reading, the requirement for two escorts rather than one follows directly from Rav's principle: because they are traveling, three people are required to prevent any situation of *yichud* arising along the route.

### Witnesses Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא: הָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִיהְווֹ עֲלֵיהּ סָהֲדִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this assumption: **No, here,** in the case of a *sota*, **this is the reason** why there is a requirement for two scholars, **so that there are two witnesses with regard to her,** i.e., there will be two witnesses to testify in the event that the husband engages in sexual intercourse with her on the way to the Temple.

The *Gemara* does not ultimately accept that as the explanation, however. *Lo*, it says — *hacha hainu ta'ama ki heichi de'lehavu alah sahadei*: here the reason is so that they should serve as witnesses. What would they be witnessing? As Rashi explains, if the husband were to go ahead and have relations with his wife during the journey, these two escorts would be positioned to testify to that fact, which would then disqualify the proceeding from going forward. A single witness would not suffice to establish that the husband and wife had relations on the journey — that requires the standard two-witness evidentiary threshold. Hence two escorts are needed to function as a proper *beit din*-style testimony mechanism.

### Talmidei Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים — אִין, כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא — לָא. לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְאִידַּךְ דְּרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא כְּשֵׁרִין, אֲבָל פְּרוּצִין, אֲפִילּוּ עֲשָׂרָה נָמֵי לָא. מַעֲשֶׂה הָיָה וְהוֹצִיאוּהָ עֲשָׂרָה בְּמִטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the husband is provided with Torah scholars. The Gemara further comments: **Torah scholars, yes; anyone** else, **no.** It is specifically Torah scholars who are provided to accompany the husband and wife. **Let us say** that this mishna **supports another** statement **of Rav, as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** When they taught that it is permitted for a woman to be secluded with two men, **they taught** that this is permitted **only** with regard **to** men of **fit** morals. **But** with regard to **those of loose morals,** she may **not** be secluded **even with ten** men. The Gemara adds: **There was an incident and ten** men **carried out** a woman **on a bier,** as if she were dead, and engaged in sexual intercourse with her.

This still leaves a question open: why must the escorts specifically be *talmidei chachamim*? If the purpose is simply to provide two witnesses, any two competent witnesses would do.

The *Gemara* initially considers whether this supports another teaching of Rav — *lo shanu ela ke'sherim aval prutzim afilu asara nami lo* — that the principle allowing two men to be alone with a woman without concern for *yichud* applies only to upstanding individuals, but if the men are *prutzim*, licentious in sexual matters, even ten such men with a woman cannot be trusted. The concern is not that those other men will exercise moral restraint; it is that a man known to be *parutz* will not be inhibited by their presence. The *Gemara* cites a case in which ten men actually smuggled a woman out of a city concealed in a bed for illicit purposes.

But the *Gemara* again sets this aside:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, הָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא — דְּיָדְעִי לְאַתְרוֹיֵי בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this assumption: **No, here,** in the case of a *sota*, **this is the reason** why there is a requirement for two scholars, **that they know** how **to** properly **warn him** not to engage in sexual intercourse with her.

The reason we need *talmidei chachamim* is that they will know to give him *hatra'ah*, a formal halachic warning. Rashi explains: if the husband is on the verge of violating the prohibition of having relations with his *sotah*, they will recognize what is happening and be positioned to issue a valid *hatra'ah* before the act, which is necessary for him to incur *malkos*.

This is, admittedly, a somewhat surprising rationale. It is one thing to want the proceeding to go forward without being compromised — but why does it matter to us that this man specifically receives a warning so that he can then be liable for *malkos*? In isolation, the concern for ensuring a particular transgressor receives his due punishment is not typically something we go out of our way to engineer proactively.

But the logic becomes clear when you recognize what this does to the character of the procedure. To send *talmidei chachamim* who are capable of giving *hatra'ah*, who function in effect as a small traveling *beit din* throughout the journey, is to make the most emphatic possible statement that this entire proceeding is framed within the world of *din*. Witnesses, warning, liability for lashes — these are the very structural elements of rabbinic judicial process. By foregrounding all of them, *Chazal* are foregrounding *din* itself. The *talmidei chachamim* are not incidental chaperones; they are representatives of the *beit din* whose presence at every stage of the journey makes clear that this is, above all, a judicial matter.

### Arei Miklat Parallel

*Tosafos* notes that this same language and structure appear in a different context — the case of one who kills accidentally and is sent to an *ir miklat*. There too, the *Gemara* records that two *talmidei chachamim* accompany the exile, *shema ye'hargenu baderech* — lest the *go'el hadam* kill him on the way. The *Gemara* there similarly explains that the two are needed so that if the *go'el hadam* is about to kill this man, they can issue a proper warning and, if necessary, intervene to stop him.

*Tosafos* observes that in both cases — the *sotah* and the accidental murderer — the *Gemara* grounds the requirement for *talmidei chachamim* in the need for *hatra'ah*. The parallel is striking and deliberate. At the beginning of the study of *sotah*, it was observed that several scenarios in the *Torah* involve something that begins as vigilante justice, as taking the law into one's own hands, and the process by which *Chazal* and sometimes the *Torah* itself bring these situations under institutional control. The *go'el hadam* is precisely the paradigm case: the *Torah* already legislates *arei miklat* as a structured response to the otherwise unbounded right of the blood-avenger. *Chazal*'s treatment of the *sotah* follows the same trajectory — taking what was a private act of jealousy and passion, placing the *beit din* at the center of the proceedings, and asserting its supervisory presence at every stage, from departure to arrival. The presence of two *talmidei chachamim* accompanying the accused, just as two *talmidei chachamim* accompany the accidental murderer, makes the parallel explicit and structural.

## Rabbi Yehuda

### Ba'ala Ne'eman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּעְלָהּ נֶאֱמָן עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: Her husband is trusted with regard to her,** so there is no need to provide scholars to accompany him.

Having established the position of the *Tanna Kamma* — that the *beit din* dispatches two *talmidei chachamim* to accompany the couple — the *mishna* records a dissenting view: *Rabbi Yehuda omer ba'alah ne'eman aleha*, Rabbi Yehuda holds that the husband is trusted, and no escorts are needed. If he is trusted not to have relations with her en route, there is no reason to send two *talmidei chachamim* along. And this difference is not merely logistical — it fundamentally changes the character of the journey. Is the experience one of the *beit din*'s agents bringing this woman toward judgment, or is it the jealous husband bringing his wife? Rabbi Yehuda's position restores something of the *Torah*'s original dynamic.

### Kal V'Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, בַּעְלָהּ נֶאֱמָן עָלֶיהָ מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה נִדָּה שֶׁהִיא בְּכָרֵת — בַּעְלָהּ נֶאֱמָן עָלֶיהָ, סוֹטָה שֶׁהִיא בְּלָאו — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* in the *Tosefta* (1:2): **Rabbi Yehuda says: Her husband is trusted due to an *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as** in the case of **a menstruating woman, who is** prohibited from engaging in sexual intercourse with her husband **by** penalty of ***karet*, her husband is** nevertheless **trusted with regard to her,** as he is permitted to seclude himself with her, so too, with regard to **a *sota*, who is** prohibited from engaging in sexual intercourse with her husband only **by** penalty of **a prohibition,** is it **not all the more so** that he should be trusted?

A *beraita* records Rabbi Yehuda's argument in the form of a *kal v'chomer*: *u'mah niddah she'hi b'kares ba'ala ne'eman aleha* — in the case of a *niddah*, whose prohibition carries the penalty of *kares*, a very serious transgression, we trust a husband and wife to sleep in the same room without having relations. *Sotah she'hi b'lav* — a *sotah*, whose prohibition is merely a negative commandment, *lo kol sheken* — how much more so can we trust him not to transgress?

### Rabbis Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הִיא הַנּוֹתֶנֶת: נִדָּה דְּכָרֵת — חֲמִירָא לֵיהּ וּמְהֵימַן. סוֹטָה דְּלָאו — לָא חֲמִירָא לֵיהּ, וְלָא מְהֵימַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** say: **That provides** support for the contrary opinion, as these considerations lead to the opposite conclusion. **A menstruating woman** is forbidden **by** penalty of ***karet*.** This is **a stringent** prohibition **for him, and** this is why **he is trusted** not to engage in sexual intercourse with her. By contrast, **a *sota*** is forbidden to him only **by a prohibition.** This is **not a stringent** prohibition **to him, and he is** therefore **not trusted** with her.

The Rabbis respond with the phrase *hi hanosenes* — that very argument is the reason we *don't* trust him. *Niddah d'kares chamura lei u'meheiman* — a *niddah* prohibition, being so weighty, will restrain him; we can rely on him. *Sotah b'lav lo chamura lei v'lo meheiman* — a *sotah* prohibition, being only a simple negative commandment, is not weighty enough to serve as a reliable deterrent, and we cannot trust him to observe it.

### Biblical Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִן הַתּוֹרָה הָאִישׁ מֵבִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ אֶל הַכֹּהֵן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהֵבִיא הָאִישׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* quotes a third opinion. **Rabbi Yehuda says: By Torah** law, **the man** alone **brings his wife** to the Temple, **as is stated: "Then shall the man bring his wife to the priest."**

A second *beraita* records the same dispute, but with different attribution. There, Rabbi Yosei presents the *kal v'chomer* argument, and the Rabbis push back with two objections. The first: *im amarta b'niddah she'yesh lah heter* — with a *niddah*, there is an eventual release; after she immerses in the *mikveh*, she becomes permissible again, and the prohibition has a cyclical, expected quality. *Tomar b'sotah she'ein lah heter* — with a *sotah*, if she is found guilty, she will never be permissible to him again. This finality might make him feel that relations during the journey are his last opportunity, creating a far more powerful temptation. The second objection adds: *mayim genuvim yimtaku* — stolen waters are sweet. Now that she is forbidden to him, the very fact of her prohibition may itself heighten the temptation. *Tosafos* explains why this second argument was necessary: one might have argued that since he has been with her before, there is no special urgency — but the very novelty of her forbidden status can make her more tempting, not less.

In that second *beraita*, Rabbi Yehuda's position is stated not as a *kal v'chomer* but as a direct appeal to the *pasuk*: *v'heivi ha'ish es ishto el hakohen* — the man brings his wife. This is the *Torah*'s own description of who initiates and executes this procedure.

### Chazal's Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהוּ קַל וָחוֹמֶר בְּרֵישָׁא וּפַרְכוּהּ, וַהֲדַר אָמַר לְהוּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda **first said to them** the ***a fortiori*** inference, **and they refuted it** as mentioned above, **and** he **then said to them** the derivation from the **verse.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא! אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ ״אֲבָל אָמְרוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: Apparently, the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda is** the same as that of **the first *tanna*** in the *baraita*, who also cites the verse as proof that by Torah law the husband alone brings his wife to the priest. The Gemara explains: The difference **between them** concerns the following clause: **But the Sages said** that the court provides him with two Torah scholars to accompany him. The first *tanna* holds that the Sages require two scholars to accompany the husband and wife, while Rabbi Yehuda holds that they do not.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between the two *beraisos* by explaining that Rabbi Yehuda first presented the *kal v'chomer* argument, which the Rabbis refuted. He then fell back on the *pasuk* itself: regardless of whether the *kal v'chomer* holds, the *Torah* says the man brings his wife, and that should be sufficient.

The *Gemara* then asks: *Rabbi Yehuda haynu Tanna Kamma* — isn't Rabbi Yehuda's position the same as the first *tanna*'s? Both concede that from the *Torah*'s perspective, it is the man who brings his wife. The *Gemara* answers: *ika beinaihu aval amru* — the difference is entirely in what *Chazal* added. Everyone agrees that on the *Torah*'s own terms, the husband brings his wife. The dispute is whether the Rabbis are authorized to change that procedure by adding their own supervisory layer. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that they did so and appropriately so. Rabbi Yehuda holds that we should not alter what the *Torah* prescribed. He is not necessarily denying that there is some basis for concern — but he does not want to transform the fundamental nature of the process to the degree that the *Tanna Kamma*'s position implies. He wants to keep it closer to its biblical form, where the husband is the driving force, rather than converting it into an exercise in institutional supervision.

## Beis Din Hagadol

### To Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיוּ מַעֲלִין אוֹתָהּ לְבֵית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna details the next stage of the process. **They would bring her up to the Sanhedrin that was in Jerusalem**

The next *mishna* describes the subsequent stages. *Hayu maalin osah le-Beis Din Hagadol she-bi-Yerushalayim* — they bring her to the Supreme Court in *Yerushalayim*. Again, notice the framing: not directly to the *Beis HaMikdash* for the ritual, but to the *Beis Din HaGadol*. It is true that the *Beis Din HaGadol* was situated within the *Beis HaMikdash* complex, in the *Lishkat HaGazit*, but that is not the point. The *mishna* is presenting the journey as proceeding from one *beit din* to the next — from the local *beit din* to the Supreme Court — and from there only then to the *kohanim* for the ritual itself. At no stage does the *mishna* describe this as simply a husband bringing his wife to the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Intimidation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְאַיְּימִין עָלֶיהָ כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁמְּאַיְּימִין עַל עֵדֵי נְפָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** and the judges would **threaten her** in order that she admit her sin. **And** this was done **in the manner that they** would **threaten witnesses** testifying **in** cases of **capital** law.

*U'me'ayemin aleha ke'derech she'me'ayemin al edei nefashot* — they frighten her in the manner in which witnesses in capital cases are frightened. In capital cases, the concern is that a witness might fill in gaps in his knowledge, might testify to something he is not fully certain of, and so the *beit din* would impress upon such witnesses the enormous weight of their testimony — that if an innocent man dies based on their words, his blood and the blood of his descendants would be on their hands. This cuts in both directions: if the witness truly knows, he must testify; but if there is any uncertainty, he must stand down. The purpose is to ensure that only reliable testimony enters the proceedings.

Here, the *beit din* applies analogous pressure to the *sotah* herself. The explicit parallel to capital cases is drawn — and the *mishna* makes no apology for it. The *sotah* proceeding is being framed within the same institutional framework as the gravest matters of *din*.

### Appeals to Admit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר לָהּ: בִּתִּי, הַרְבֵּה יַיִן עוֹשֶׂה, הַרְבֵּה שְׂחוֹק עוֹשֶׂה, הַרְבֵּה יַלְדוּת עוֹשָׂה, הַרְבֵּה שְׁכֵנִים הָרָעִים עוֹשִׂין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** additionally, the judge would **say to her: My daughter, wine causes a great deal** of immoral behavior, **levity causes a great deal** of immoral behavior, **immaturity causes a great deal** of immoral behavior, and **bad neighbors cause a great deal** of immoral behavior.

The *mishna* then describes what the *beit din* says to her: *biti, harbeh yayin osah* — my daughter, wine often causes people to act in ways they would not otherwise act. They invite her to confess by offering her mitigating circumstances: *harbeh se'chok osah*, excessive frivolity, *harbeh yaldus osah*, the passions and poor judgment of youth, *harbeh shechenim ra'im osim*, the corrupting influence of bad company and bad neighbors. They are, in effect, making it possible for her to admit by reducing the apparent weight of moral responsibility. They do not require her to present herself as a fully deliberate sinner — they offer her a range of explanations that would allow her to acknowledge what happened without bearing its full shame.

### Shelo Yimacheh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשִׂי לִשְׁמוֹ הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁנִּכְתַּב בִּקְדוּשָּׁה שֶׁלֹּא יִמָּחֶה עַל הַמַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The judge then continues: **Act for the sake of His great name, so that** God's name, **which is written in sanctity, shall not be erased on the water.**

The culminating appeal is: *asi lishmo hagadol she'nichtav bi'kedushah shelo yimacheh al hamayim* — act for the sake of God's great Name, which is written in holiness, so that it should not be dissolved into the water. This is a striking inversion. The purpose of the *sotah* procedure is precisely to dissolve the Divine Name into the water so that it can be administered to the woman. But here, the *beit din* presents that prospect as something to be avoided. The purpose, they are saying, is *shelo yimacheh* — not to erase it.

This raises a genuine question: if we trust that the ritual works, why are we working so hard to prevent it from being used? One might argue it is better for the sinner to confess of her own accord; or one might invoke the various reasons explored in earlier discussion about why the ritual's efficacy might be unreliable in practice. But the *mishna*'s focus here is revealing: it does not frame the goal as arriving at the truth, or even as vindicating the innocent. The goal, as stated, is to avoid erasing God's Name. Everything is oriented toward obtaining an admission and thereby bypassing the ritual.

### Admission Consequence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אָמְרָה ״טְמֵאָה אֲנִי״ — שׁוֹבֶרֶת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ וְיוֹצֵאת.
>
> **Translation:** **If** after the judge's warning **she says: I am defiled,** she writes **a receipt** for **her marriage contract.** That is, she writes a receipt indicating that she has no claims on her husband with regard to the sum written in her marriage contract, as a woman who admits to adultery forfeits her right to this payment. **And she is** then **divorced** from her husband.

If she responds *tme'ah ani* — I am impure, I am guilty — the consequences are clear: *shoveret ktuvatah*, she loses her *ketubah*, *v'yotzah*, and she is divorced. Admission ends the proceeding. She leaves without the financial protection of her *ketubah* and without her marriage.

### Maintains Innocence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמְרָה ״טְהוֹרָה אֲנִי״ — מַעֲלִין אוֹתָהּ לְשַׁעַר הַמִּזְרָח שֶׁעַל פֶּתַח שַׁעַר נִקָּנוֹר, שֶׁשָּׁם מַשְׁקִין אֶת הַסּוֹטוֹת, וּמְטַהֲרִין אֶת הַיּוֹלְדוֹת, וּמְטַהֲרִין אֶת הַמְצוֹרָעִין.
>
> **Translation:** **But if** after the warning **she** maintains her innocence and **says: I am pure, they bring her up to the Eastern Gate, which is at the opening of** the **Gate of Nicanor, because** three rites were performed **there: They give the *sota* women** the bitter water **to drink, and they purify women who have given birth** (see Leviticus 12:6–8), **and they purify the lepers** (see Leviticus 14:10–20).

If she says *tehorah ani* — I am pure, I am innocent — the proceedings advance. *Maalin otah leshaar hamizrach* — they bring her to the eastern gate, which is directly opposite the *shaar Nikanor*.

## Eastern Gate Ritual

### Eastern Gate

The eastern gate of the *Beis HaMikdash* is the entry point into the *ezrat nashim*. Proceeding westward through the *ezrat nashim*, one reaches the top of a series of steps leading into the *azarah* proper, where the *mizbe'ach* stands. At the top of those steps is the *shaar Nikanor*, the gate that transitions from the *ezrat nashim* into the *azarah*. The *mishna* tells us that the *sotah* is brought to the eastern gate, facing this *shaar Nikanor*, because that is where the *sotot* drink, where newly delivered mothers come for their purification, and where *metzora'im* undergo parts of their purification ritual.

### Gate Theology

The *shaar Nikanor* is a gate in a wall, and like ancient city gates it has physical thickness — a substantial passage of stone. That liminal space within the gate's thickness did not carry the *kedushah* of the *azarah*; it had only the *kedushah* of the *ezrat nashim*. This made it a legal threshold available to those who could not yet enter the *azarah* but whose ritual required some form of proximity to it.

A *yoledet*, a woman who had given birth, needed to bring a *korban* as part of her purification, but was not yet *tahor* in a way that allowed her into the *azarah*. By standing in the *shaar Nikanor*, she could be present for the bringing of her *korban* without entering the prohibited space. A *metzora* needed the blood of his *korban* applied to his thumbs and toes, but similarly could not yet enter the *azarah*. He would stand in the thickness of the gate and extend his thumb and toe into the *azarah*, allowing the *kohen* to apply the blood — though this raises the separate halachic question of *bi'ah b'miktzas shemah bi'ah*, whether partial entry into the *Beis HaMikdash* while *tamei* constitutes a prohibited entry.

The *sotah*, too, would stand in this liminal space. And what is striking is the company she is placed in. The *mishna* describes her alongside the *yoledet* and the *metzora* — people undergoing processes of *tum'ah* and *taharah*. This is no accident. The *Torah*'s own language for the outcome of the *sotah* procedure is *v'nitme'ah* and *hi lo nitme'ah* — she is *tamei* or she is not *tamei*. The procedure is being reframed not only as a matter of *din* but also as a process of *taharah*, of determining and rectifying a state of spiritual impurity. This is a different kind of framing than the trial-by-ordeal model, and perhaps a more accessible one — we know there are *taharah* processes that involve unusual rituals, birds, blood on fingers, strange mixtures. A *minchah* offering, words dissolved in water, a woman drinking — situated within that framework, the ritual becomes less anomalous.

### Garment Tearing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַכֹּהֵן אוֹחֵז בִּבְגָדֶיהָ. אִם נִקְרְעוּ — נִקְרְעוּ. וְאִם נִפְרְמוּ — נִפְרְמוּ. עַד שֶׁהוּא מְגַלֶּה אֶת לִבָּהּ. וְסוֹתֵר אֶת שְׂעָרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues describing the *sota* rite. **And the priest grabs hold of her clothing** and pulls them, unconcerned about what happens to the clothing. **If** the clothes **are torn,** so **they are torn; if the stitches come apart,** so **they come apart.** And he pulls her clothing **until he reveals her heart,** i.e., her chest. **And** then **he unbraids her hair.**

*Hakohen ochez bi'vgadeha* — the *kohen* takes hold of her garments. *Im nikre'u nikre'u v'im nifremu nifremu* — if they tear, they tear; if they split entirely, they split. The *kohen* holds her garments and does not release them regardless of how extensively they rend, continuing until *ad she'hu megaleh libah* — he has exposed her chest. He also undoes her hair, *v'soter et se'arah*, which echoes the *Torah*'s own language for this stage of the procedure.

### Modesty Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיָה לִבָּהּ נָאֶה — לֹא הָיָה מְגַלֵּהוּ, וְאִם הָיָה שְׂעָרָהּ נָאֶה — לֹא הָיָה סוֹתֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: If her heart was attractive he would not reveal it, and if her hair was attractive he would not unbraid** it.

Rabbi Yehuda dissents in a specific case: *im haya libah na'eh lo haya megalehu* — if her chest was particularly attractive, he would not expose it, and *im haya se'arah na'eh lo haya sotro* — if her hair was beautiful, he would not undo it. The underlying tension is obvious: this degradation ritual involves uncovering parts of the body that are normally covered, and in doing so risks transforming what is meant to be a shameful and sobering spectacle into something erotically charged for the onlookers. Rabbi Yehuda attempts to set limits precisely at the point where the degradation threatens to tip into something else.

### Degradation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה מִתְכַּסָּה בִּלְבָנִים — מְכַסֶּהָ בִּשְׁחוֹרִים. הָיָה עָלֶיהָ כְּלֵי זָהָב וְקַטְלֵיאוֹת, נְזָמִים וְטַבָּעוֹת — מַעֲבִירִין מִמֶּנָּה, כְּדֵי לְנַוְּולָהּ. וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵבִיא חֶבֶל מִצְרִי וְקוֹשְׁרוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִדַּדֶּיהָ,
>
> **Translation:** If **she was dressed in white** garments, **he** would now **cover her with black** garments. If **she was wearing gold adornments, or chokers [*katliyot*],** or **nose rings, or** finger **rings, they removed them from her in order to render her unattractive. And afterward** the priest **would bring an Egyptian rope** fashioned from palm fibers, **and he would tie it above her breasts.**

If she had been wearing white garments, she is dressed in black. If she wore golden jewelry — *k'lei zahav, ve'katliyot, ve'nezamim ve'tabba'ot* — various chokers, earrings, and rings — *ma'avirin mimena*, they are removed from her, *kedei lena'velah*, in order to dishevel her and render her repulsive. This is a systematic process of degradation, stripping away the markers of status and beauty and replacing them with their opposites. Then an Egyptian rope is taken and bound above her breasts to hold up the garment after it has been torn, so that it does not fall entirely. The sequence likely proceeds as follows: first the black garments are substituted and the jewelry removed; then the *kohen* tears the garments; then the rope is tied to hold them.

### Public Spectacle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הָרוֹצֶה לִרְאוֹת בָּא לִרְאוֹת, חוּץ מֵעֲבָדֶיהָ וְשִׁפְחוֹתֶיהָ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלִּבָּהּ גַּס בָּהֶן. וְכׇל הַנָּשִׁים מוּתָּרוֹת לִרְאוֹתָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִוַּסְּרוּ כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂינָה כְּזִמַּתְכֶנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And anyone who desires to watch her may come to watch, except for her slaves and maidservants,** who are not permitted to watch **because her heart is emboldened by them,** as seeing one's slaves reinforces one's feeling of pride, and their presence may cause her to maintain her innocence. **And all of the women are permitted to watch her, as it is stated:** "Thus will I cause lewdness to cease out of the land, **that all women may be taught not to do after your lewdness"** (Ezekiel 23:48).

*V'chol ha'rotze lir'ot ba v'ro'eh* — and whoever wishes to come and see may come and see. This is a public spectacle, and notably it is one that has no direct source in the *Torah*. The *Torah*'s *sotah* procedure is not a public event. *Chazal* have made it one.

This transformation is worth pausing over. The movement from a private matter between a jealous husband and his wife to a public spectacle before all of *Yerushalayim* mirrors a broader pattern in *Chazal*'s treatment of *Beis HaMikdash* rituals generally. The *Yom Kippur* service, for instance, is explicitly marked in the *Torah* as a moment of singular divine intimacy: *v'chol adam lo yihyeh b'ohel mo'ed b'vo'o l'chaper bakodesh*. Yet in the *mishna*'s description of *Yom Kippur*, the service becomes a public ritual — the people gather to see the lot selected, to watch the red string, to hear the reading of the *Torah*. *Chazal* consistently push these rituals into the public sphere.

And there is a further dimension here, drawn from the work of Yishai Rosen-Zvi, who wrote on *Masechet Sotah* in his book *Ha-tekes she-lo hayah* — the ritual that never was. Rosen-Zvi argues that the *mishna* proceeds throughout with an assumption of the woman's guilt. The entire procedure is oriented not toward clarifying her status but toward punishing her. One way to see this is in the scaring: when the *beit din* frightened the *sotah*, they appealed only in one direction — please confess, spare us the need to erase God's Name. When *beit din* frightens witnesses in capital cases, by contrast, they press in both directions: testify if you truly know, but do not testify if you are uncertain. Here, the *beit din* does not say: if you are truly innocent, go forward so that the waters can vindicate you and restore you to your husband. The assumption is that she did it, and the *beit din*'s appeals are structured accordingly.

This presumption of guilt also helps explain the public spectacle. A neutral judicial proceeding aimed at determining guilt or innocence does not need an audience. But if the outcome is already understood — if this is, in effect, a public punishment — then gathering the people makes perfect sense, both as a form of communal shaming and as a deterrent. The *Torah* itself provides the underlying principle: *v'chol ha'am yishme'u v'yira'u*.

### Who May Watch

*Chutz me'avadeha u'shifchoteha* — with one exception: her servants may not be present. The reason given is *mipnei she'libah gas bahen* — their presence would bolster her sense of confidence and importance. She might be less willing to confess with her servants watching; she might feel less the weight of the proceedings if surrounded by people over whom she has authority. The goal of obtaining a confession or at least ensuring the degradation achieves its full psychological effect requires that she be stripped of those familiar sources of comfort and status.

*V'chol ha'nashim mutarot lir'otah* — but all women are permitted and indeed encouraged to see her. The *mishna* quotes a *pasuk* from *Yechezkel*: *v'niv'ssru kol ha'nashim v'lo ta'asena k'zimatchena* — all the women shall take heed and not do as she did in her lewdness. The *sotah* proceeding, in its public form, serves the same function as *kol ha'am yishme'u v'yira'u* in capital cases: punishment as public instruction and deterrent, the logic of *din* made visible.

## Beis Din Authority

### Deriving Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר גַּמָּדָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אָתְיָא ״תּוֹרָה״ ״תּוֹרָה״. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְעָשָׂה לָהּ הַכֹּהֵן אֵת כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״עַל פִּי הַתּוֹרָה אֲשֶׁר יוֹרוּךָ״, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּשִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד — אַף כָּאן בְּשִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks concerning the *halakha* that the *sota* is brought before the Sanhedrin: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Gamda says** that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** This **is derived** by means of a verbal analogy between the words **"*tora*"** and **"*tora*."** It **is written here,** with regard to a *sota*: **"And the priest shall execute upon her all this law [*tora*]"** (Numbers 5:30), **and** it **is written there,** with regard to a rebellious Elder, who must go to the place chosen by God and follow the ruling of the Sanhedrin: **"According to the law [*tora*] that they shall teach you"** (Deuteronomy 17:11). **Just as there** the verse is referring to what occurs **in** the presence of the Sanhedrin of **seventy-one** judges, **so too here,** with regard to a *sota*, the verse is referring to what occurs **in** the presence of the Sanhedrin of **seventy-one** judges.

The *Gemara* asks: *mena hani millei* — from where is this derived? How do we know that the *sotah* must be brought before the *Beis Din HaGadol* in *Yerushalayim*? Rabbi Chiyya bar Gamda said in the name of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina: the derivation comes from a *gezeirah shavah* of *Torah-Torah*. It is written here, by the *sotah*: *v'asah lah ha'kohen et kol ha'Torah ha'zat* — the *kohen* shall do all of this *Torah*, this ritual. And it is written there, by the *zaken mamre*: *al pi ha'Torah asher yorucha* — in accordance with the *Torah* they teach you. *Mah le'halan b'shivim v'echad* — just as a *zaken mamre* who rebels against the ruling of the Sanhedrin requires a court of seventy-one, *af kan b'shivim v'echad* — so too here, the *sotah* procedure requires the oversight of a *beit din* of seventy-one.

### Zaken Mamre

The *kal v'chomer* from *zaken mamre* is an instructive choice of parallel. The *zaken mamre* is a Torah sage who rules contrary to the *Sanhedrin HaGadol*, the supreme authority, and whose defiance carries the penalty of death. The *Sanhedrin*'s authority in that case is total and essential — without their involvement the case cannot proceed. The *Gemara* is drawing on that model to establish the *Beis Din HaGadol*'s centrality to the *sotah* process as well.

### Chronology Problem

*Tosafos* raises a powerful difficulty with this derivation. The *halacha* of *zaken mamre* applies only when the *Sanhedrin* is sitting in its designated place within the *Beis HaMikdash*. Historically, we know that the *Sanhedrin* left the *Beis HaMikdash* — they moved out of the *Lishkat HaGazit* — before the *churban*, because they did not wish to bear responsibility for adjudicating capital cases. Yet the evidence indicates that *sotah* rituals continued even after the *Sanhedrin* had left the *Beis HaMikdash*. If the *sotah* procedure truly required a *Beis Din HaGadol* sitting in the *Beis HaMikdash*, on the model of *zaken mamre*, then *sotah* should have ceased the moment the *Sanhedrin* relocated. But that does not appear to be what happened.

### Warning Not Required

*Tosafos* resolves the difficulty as follows: *d'iyum lo haya ma'akev* — the intimidation, the warning administered by the *Beis Din HaGadol*, was not *me'akev*. It was not a blocking condition. *D'im lo hayu me'ayimin klal, af al pi chen haysa shosah* — even if the *beit din* had not issued the warning at all, she could still have gone through with the drinking of the waters. The *sotah* ritual would still function. *Hilchach haysa nohages afilu achar hagalus* — therefore the practice continued even after the *Sanhedrin*'s departure from the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Chazal Paradox

This resolution is, on reflection, remarkable. *Tosafos* is telling us that for all the elaborate *beit din* scaffolding that has been constructed around the *sotah* procedure — the local *beit din* receiving the case, the two *talmidei chachamim* accompanying the couple, the *Beis Din HaGadol* in *Yerushalayim* issuing its solemn warning — none of it is ultimately *me'akev*. None of it is strictly necessary. At its core, the *sotah* procedure is a *Beis HaMikdash* ritual: the man brings his wife to the *kohen*, and the *kohen* administers the waters. The entire *beit din* superstructure could theoretically be dispensed with, and the procedure would still be valid.

This paradox runs in both directions. *Chazal* foreground *beit din* authority at every stage, insist on its presence, draw explicit parallels to *din*, and embed witnesses and *hatra'ah* into the very journey to *Yerushalayim* — and then the sources reveal that it was all optional. This also provides an answer to the earlier question that the first *Tosafos* implicitly raised: why, if *sotah* requires a *beit din* of seventy-one on the model of *zaken mamre*, is it not listed in *Perek Kamma* of *Sanhedrin* among the cases requiring a *beit din* of seventy-one? The answer is precisely because the *sotah* proceeding is not, at its base, something that requires a *beit din* of seventy-one. It is a *Beis HaMikdash* ritual. The *beit din*'s role, however elaborate and however prominently *Chazal* present it, is ultimately not constitutive of the ritual's validity.

What we are left with is a picture of extraordinary complexity: a ritual that the *Torah* conceived as a husband's private action in the *Beis HaMikdash*, which *Chazal* systematically transformed into a judicial proceeding saturated with the language and structure of *din* — while simultaneously acknowledging, when pressed, that the transformation was always optional and the underlying reality remained a pure *Beis HaMikdash* ritual.
