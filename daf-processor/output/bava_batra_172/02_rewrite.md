# Bava Batra 172 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dating Sale Documents

### Known vs. Unknown Date

The daf opens with a ruling of Rava bar Rav Shila addressed to those who write *shtarei aknyata* — documents of acquisition. The Rashbam understands this term to refer not to a *shtar halva'ah* (loan document) but to a *shtar mekhar*, a deed recording the sale of land. The practical scenario is this: a *kinyan* took place on one date — say, November 1st — but the witnesses did not get around to writing the *shtar* until January 1st. How should they date the document?

Rava bar Rav Shila's instruction is clear: *im yiditu yoma dekannu bei, kisvu* — if you know the day that the *kinyan* was performed, write that date. *Ve'ei lo, kisvu yoma dematu bei* — and if you do not know that date, write today's date, *ki heichi dela mitchazi keshikra* — so that the document does not appear to be a lie. One might naturally ask: why not write both dates — noting both when the transaction occurred and when the *shtar* is being drafted? For all practical purposes, perhaps the standard practice does include both lines. But the critical *chiddush* here concerns the fill-in-the-blank for the original date. If one genuinely does not remember it, one might be tempted to reason: I know the transaction occurred before Thanksgiving, so let me simply write Thanksgiving as the date — at least that is a point before which I am certain the event took place. To this Rava bar Rav Shila says no: such an approximation is itself *mitchazi keshikra*, it has the appearance of a falsehood. If you do not know the precise date, write today's date — the one date of which you are certain — and do not guess.

### Land Sales & Liens

Beyond the question of which date to inscribe, the Rashbam identifies a second *chiddush* in this passage, one that is conceptually more significant. One might ask: in a case where the seller conveyed land to the buyer *with achrayus* — with a guarantee of security that creates liens on the seller's assets — is it not problematic to date the *shtar* two months retroactively to November 1st? Writing the *shtar* now but dating it to November 1st means that the buyer would hold liens stretching back two months. In that intervening period, however, people may have purchased land from the seller — the *lakuchot* — without any knowledge of those liens. Should we not be concerned that such a retroactively dated *shtar* would unfairly prejudice those subsequent buyers?

The Rashbam, reinforced by Tosafot, answers that in the case of land sales this concern does not arise. Sales of land are public and well-known events, especially when conducted before witnesses. We therefore assume that the *lakuchot* are already aware that the buyer acquired the land, even before any *shtar* is written, and they are expected to take that prior sale into account when purchasing land from the seller. The liens, in other words, are understood to exist from the moment of the sale — not from the moment of documentation — and buyers of land are presumed to know this.

### Loans Cannot Be Post-Dat…

The situation is starkly different when it comes to a *shtar halva'ah*. With a loan, liens on the borrower's property do not come into existence through the act of borrowing alone; they come into existence only when the *shtar* is written. This is a crucial legal distinction. If witnesses observed a loan on November 1st but did not write the *shtar* until December, they must write December — they are absolutely forbidden to write November, even though November is the accurate date of the transaction. To write November would be to create liens retroactively to a point when none legally existed, and such a document is a *shtar mukdam* — an antedated instrument — which is *pasul*.

This prohibition stands in deliberate contrast to the *shtar mekhar*. By land sales, the retroactive dating is permissible because the liens pre-exist the writing of the document. By a loan document, the liens are born with the *shtar* itself, and antedating it would therefore conjure liens out of thin air — a fraud against any *lakuchot* who acquired property from the borrower in the interval.

### Place of Writing & Event

Rav and Rav Huna each instructed their respective scribes on a related matter: *ki katbitu bi-Shili ve-af al gav de-mesiru lakhun mili be-Hini* — if you are writing the document in Shili, even though the testimony was given to you in Hini, write where you are at the time of writing. The Rashbam explains this to mean that one writes both: the place where you are sitting when drafting the document, and the place where the event itself occurred — *katavnu hakha mah she-rainu be-makom ploni*, "we have written here what we witnessed in such-and-such a place."

It may be asked: if one writes both lines anyway, what is the *chiddush*? Tosafot explains that Shili and Hini are like two villages immediately adjacent to one another — the Teaneck and Englewood of Babylonia, as it were — where the population moves freely between them. One might have supposed that since the two locales are functionally indistinguishable, it is acceptable to simply record the location of the event rather than the location where the *shtar* is being written. Rav and Rav Huna rule otherwise: no matter how close together these places are, you must write precisely where you are at the moment of drafting, distinct from where the underlying event took place.

## Receipts & Defective Doc…

### No Conversion: 100 to 50…

Having addressed the concerns that animate the *shover* (receipt), the Gemara now turns to a series of rulings by Rava concerning document conversion. Suppose a *malveh* holds a *shtar* for a hundred *zuz* and approaches the *beit din* with a seemingly reasonable request: since Michael is likely to pay in installments, could the court convert the single hundred-*zuz shtar* into two *shtarot* of fifty *zuz* each? The *malveh* would surrender the hundred-*zuz shtar* and receive two fifties in exchange, both bearing the same date. What could be the objection?

Ravina explains: *lo mashvinan lehu* — we do not make that conversion — because the current state of affairs, a single hundred-*zuz shtar*, carries distinct advantages for both parties, and we are not in the business of unilaterally dismantling those advantages.

The advantage for the *malveh* is one of leverage: *nicha lei le-malveh kedei she-yikof le-pora'o* — it is beneficial for the creditor in that it compels the debtor to pay. If the debtor pays fifty and receives a receipt, the *malveh* is still holding a hundred-*zuz shtar* against which the debtor must guard. That residual pressure creates an incentive for the *loveh* to pay off the remainder so that he does not remain exposed to a second claim.

The advantage for the *loveh* flows from a *halacha* about *shtar pagum* — a document that has been partially impaired. If the *malveh* comes to *beit din* with his hundred-*zuz shtar* and the *loveh* produces a receipt showing that fifty have already been paid, that partial payment creates a suspicion that perhaps the full debt has been discharged. The *shtar* is now *pagum* — like a *kiddush* cup from which some has been drunk — and the *malveh* cannot collect the remaining fifty without first taking a *shevuah* that the balance remains unpaid. This is a powerful position for the *loveh*: he compels the creditor to swear before collecting. If the *shtar* were converted into two fifties and the hundred-*zuz shtar* torn up, there would be no *shtar pagum*, no *chashash*, and no *shevuah* requirement. The *loveh* thus has a genuine interest in preserving the single hundred-*zuz shtar* even as it exposes him to continued liability. Since each scenario serves each party in different ways, we decline to alter it.

### No Conversion: 50+50 to…

Rava applies the identical reasoning in reverse. Suppose a *malveh* holds two *shtarot* of fifty *zuz* each, both dated on the same day, and asks the *beit din* to consolidate them into a single hundred-*zuz shtar*. Rav Adda bar Minyumi explains: *lo mashvinan lei* — we do not do it — because holding two fifties also carries distinct advantages for both parties.

The benefit to the *malveh* is that he can hand over one fifty-*zuz shtar* upon receiving partial repayment and retain the other fifty entirely intact — it is not *pagum* at all. And the benefit to the *loveh* is that *lo nikfayei le-pora'ei* — having paid off one fifty and received that *shtar* back, he is no longer under any coercive pressure with respect to that first fifty; only the second remains outstanding. Were the two fifties consolidated into a hundred and one fifty repaid, the *malveh* would still hold a hundred-*zuz shtar* with a receipt — a *shtar pagum* — and the *loveh* would face the leverage of that remaining instrument. Two fifties avoid that dynamic. The Rashbam notes that if both parties agree to the conversion, we would not stand in their way; it is only absent mutual agreement that the *beit din* refuses to act unilaterally.

### Fraud Prevention

Rav Ashi introduces a related prohibition that illuminates just how seriously the Gemara takes the potential for document manipulation. Suppose a *malveh* approaches the *beit din* and says: I have a hundred-*zuz shtar* against Michael, but poor Michael will never be able to pay the full amount, so please convert it to a fifty-*zuz shtar* — after all, *bichlal masayim maneh*, one who borrowed a hundred certainly borrowed fifty, so writing a fifty is not false. *Lo mashvinan lei* — we do not do it.

The reason is the following fraud scenario. Michael may have already paid off the full hundred-*zuz* debt, received a receipt from the *malveh*, and demanded his *shtar* back. The *malveh* told him: *irkas li* — sorry, I lost your *shtar* — and wrote him a *shover* instead. But the *malveh* still possesses the hundred-*zuz shtar*. Were he now to have it converted to fifty, he could produce the fifty-*zuz shtar* and claim it is a separate loan — *hai achrina hu* — distinct from the hundred-*zuz shtar* referenced in Michael's receipt. Michael's receipt would be useless against this new claim. This is why we refuse to perform such a conversion even when framed as an act of charity toward the debtor.

## Same-Name Identification

### Mutual Inability

The Mishnah now turns to a problem of a different character. If two people in the same city share the identical name — the classic case being *shenayim shehayu be'ir achat, shem echad Yosef ben Shimon veshem acher Yosef ben Shimon* — the result is a tangle of documentary uncertainty. Neither can bring a *shtar chov* against the other: I am Yosef ben Shimon, you are Yosef ben Shimon, and if I produce a *shtar* saying Yosef ben Shimon owes Yosef ben Shimon a hundred *zuz*, you can always claim it is you who are the creditor and I who have somehow come into possession of the document. Equally, a third party cannot bring a *shtar chov* against either of them, since the named debtor can always point to the other person of the same name and say the debt belongs to him.

The Mishnah further notes: *nimtza le'echad bein shtarotav shtar sheYosef ben Shimon parua* — if one finds a note among his own papers recording that the *shtarot* of Yosef ben Shimon are paid up, both individuals' *shtarot* are presumed discharged, since there is no way to determine which Yosef ben Shimon the memorandum referenced.

### Solutions: 3 Names & Sim…

*Keitzad ya'asu* — what, then, is to be done? *Yeshaleshu* — go three generations. Rather than identifying a party by father alone, include the grandfather's name as well. If the grandfathers also share the same name — *v'im hayu meshulashim* — then *yichtevu siman*, write an identifying characteristic: a physical description, some distinguishing feature, or the like. It is worth noting that the Mishnah makes no mention of a mother's name as a *siman*, even at this stage, before resorting to physical characteristics — a reflection of the patrilineal basis of formal identification in the legal tradition, though certainly a mother's name would qualify as a valid *siman* once that category is reached.

If even suitable *simanim* are lacking, the Mishnah suggests noting priestly or levitical status: *yichtevu kohen* — write that one is a *kohen* or *levi*. This does raise an eyebrow: one might have expected tribal status to appear earlier in the hierarchy, even before the three-generation name, since it is both stable and immediately known. And of course the solution fails if both men happen to be *kohanim* or both *leviyim*. The Mishnah's point, in the end, is simply that one must find *some* means of distinguishing between them; the specific tools it lists are illustrative of the principle rather than an exhaustive or rigidly ordered hierarchy.

## Bearer Bond Question

### Rav Huna's Challenge

The Gemara now presents a striking case that was brought before the *beit din* of Rav Huna. A *shtar* was produced that read: *ani ploni bar ploni liviti mimcha manah* — "I, so-and-so son of so-and-so, borrowed a hundred *zuz* from you." The *shtar* identified the debtor by name but left the creditor entirely open-ended: not "from Charlie" but simply "from you." Charlie arrives at *beit din* and says that "you" refers to him.

Rav Huna asks with evident skepticism: *mimcha afilu resh galuta?* — "from you" — would that mean even if the *resh galuta* were holding it? Even the *nishvor malka*, the Persian king? In other words, is it really sufficient that whoever is physically presenting the *shtar* at *beit din* gets to claim to be the "you" the document refers to? The question touches on a fundamental tension: either we read "from you" as identifying a specific but unnamed concrete person, in which case the challenge is how we determine who that person actually is, or we entertain the possibility — raised pointedly by Tosafot — that the document functions as what we would today call a bearer bond: the obligation runs not to a named individual but to whoever happens to be holding the instrument.

### Get Without Date

Rav Chisda instructs Rabbah to investigate the question, telling him that Rav Huna will be raising it that evening. Rabbah finds a relevant *beraita*: *get she-yesh alav eidim v'ein bo zman* — a *get* that bears witnesses but carries no date — is normally invalid. However, *Abba Shaul omer*: if the *get* contains the phrase *gerashtich hayom* — "I have divorced you today" — it is *kasher*.

The immediate implication seems strong: *alma hayom hahu yoma d'nafik bei mashma* — evidently, "today" is understood to mean the day on which the document is presented. And by analogy, *mimcha* — "from you" — should mean *me'hahu gavra d'nafik mitosei yadei mashma*, from the person from whose hand the document emerges when presented. Just as the *get* is treated as dated to the day it arrives at *beit din*, the *shtar chov* is treated as borrowing from the person who presents it. We do not ask what actually happened in the past; we interpret the document by the circumstances of its presentation.

### Tosafot's Bearer Bond Re…

Tosafot brings a deeper dimension to the comparison. In the case of the *get*, "today" does not merely identify a date after the fact; it is an operative legal term. When a husband on his deathbed says "here is your *get* if I die," there are complex questions about when the *get* takes effect and whether a conditional *get* that takes effect only upon death is valid. By writing *min yoma denan u'le'olam* — "from this day and forever" — the *get* takes legal effect as of today, neutralizing any concern that it activates only upon some future event. The Rashbam and Tosafot both note that standard *gittin* include this phrase for precisely that reason.

The parallel Tosafot draws to the *shtar* of "from you" is illuminating. Just as "today" in a *get* does not merely record a date but rather constitutes the operative moment of effectiveness — meaning the *get* takes effect on the day it is presented to *beit din*, whenever that may be — so too "from you" in a *shtar chov* might be read as constituting the operative identification of the obligee: the document creates an obligation running to whoever bears it at the time of presentation. On this reading, it is not a failure of identification but a deliberate legal structure, a *shtar* whose creditor is determined at the point of enforcement rather than at the point of creation. The debate, then, is whether such an open-ended formulation has legal force, and whether "from you" is properly read as invoking it.

### No Fear of Droppage

Abaye pushes back on the proof from *Abba Shaul*'s ruling about "today." He suggests that perhaps *Abba Shaul* holds like *Rabbi Eliezer*, who maintains *edei mesirah karti* — that the witnesses to the actual delivery of the *get* are the primary operative witnesses, and their presence at the moment of delivery supplies the date. On this view, "today" in the *get* does not function as a date at all; the *get* is valid not because the word "today" dates it but because the *edei mesirah* know when the delivery occurred. Abaye then adds the operative objection: *aval hacha leichush linfilah* — but here, in the case of the *shtar* that says "from you," we should be concerned that the original creditor dropped the document and Charlie found it, and "from you" therefore does not establish that Charlie is owed anything.

Rava responds directly: *linfilah lo chaishinan* — we do not entertain the fear of the document having been dropped and found. His proof is from the very Mishnah already learned: the case of the two men named Yosef ben Shimon establishes that *hahem al acheirim yecholim* — each of them *can* bring a *shtar chov* against a third party even though another person shares their name. If we were genuinely afraid of the *shtar* having been dropped and picked up, even a clearly named *shtar* would be suspect. The fact that the Mishnah permits such collection demonstrates that we do not operate with a fear of *nefilah*.

### Single vs. Multiple Drop…

Abaye distinguishes. *Linfilah dechad lo chaishinan* — we are not afraid that one specific person dropped the *shtar* and one specific other person found it. That scenario — the other Dov Linzer dropped his *shtar* and you happened to pick it up — is too implausible to take seriously. But *linfilah derabim chaishinan* — when the document uses an open-ended term like "from you," the universe of people who might theoretically have dropped it and from whom it might have passed to the current holder is unbounded. The *shtar* does not identify a specific person as the one who dropped it; anyone for whom such a *shtar* could have been written might be the source. That diffuse, open-ended possibility of loss among many is qualitatively different and cannot be dismissed.

The distinction is therefore not about probability but about structure: a named *shtar* implicates at most one other known individual, and that one-to-one coincidence we dismiss. An unnamed "from you" *shtar* potentially implicates any number of unknown creditors, and that open-ended quality sustains the concern.

### Same Name Affects Collec…

The Gemara then introduces a *beraita* that complicates the picture: *keshem she'ein motzi'in shtar chov zeh al zeh, kach ein motzi'in al acheirim* — just as neither Yosef ben Shimon can bring a *shtar* against the other, so too neither can bring a *shtar* against a third party. This seems to contradict the Mishnah's ruling that *hahem al acheirim yecholim*. If we genuinely are not afraid of *nefilah dechad*, why does the *beraita* prevent the identically named parties from collecting even against outsiders? The outsider could simply claim: I don't know which Yosef ben Shimon this *shtar* belongs to; perhaps the one I borrowed from dropped it and the other picked it up.

### Acquisition by Delivery

The Gemara resolves the apparent conflict by locating it within a broader dispute: *be'osiyot niknot be-mesirah kamipalgei* — the debate turns on whether documents are acquired through delivery. According to the *tanna* of our Mishnah, *osiyot niknot be-mesirah* — the transfer of a *shtar* is legally effectuated by handing it over. If this is so, then even if the *shtar* was originally written in the name of one Dov Linzer and then physically delivered to the other, that delivery itself transfers the underlying *chov*. The holder can therefore claim: this *shtar* belongs to me because it was handed to me, regardless of in whose name it was originally written. The concern about *nefilah* is thus displaced: even if it passed through someone else's hands, delivery is the operative legal act, and if I am holding it, I am holding it rightfully.

The *tanna* of the *beraita*, by contrast, holds *ein osiyot niknot be-mesirah* — delivery of a *shtar* does not transfer ownership of the underlying debt. On that view, holding the document is not itself proof of entitlement. The *shtar* might have been dropped by the person to whom it was written, or that person may have asked someone to hold it for him — a *pikadon* — without transferring the debt. Since mere possession does not establish ownership, there remains genuine uncertainty about whether the person presenting the *shtar* is the rightful creditor. The discussion continues into the following daf.
