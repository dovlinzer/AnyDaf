# Nazir 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Implicit vs Explicit

### Day 31 vs Day 30

The third *perek* of Masechet Nazir opens with a *mishnah* that in large part recapitulates ground covered on 5b, so the discussion here moves quickly, drawing on analysis already developed there. The *mishnah* states: *mi she-amar hareini nazir megaleiach leyom shloshim ve-echad* — a person who declares *stam* that he will be a *nazir* brings his *korbanos* and shaves on the 31st day. The reasoning is straightforward: the *nezirut* must encompass 30 days, and the morning of the 31st day is only a partial day, so in order to have a full 30 days for the *nezirut* one waits until the 31st. *Ve-im gileach leyom shloshim yatza* — but if such a person shaved and brought *korbanos* on the 30th day, he has nonetheless fulfilled his obligation *bedi'eved*.

By contrast, if a person was explicit — *hareini nazir shloshim yom*, I will be a *nazir* for 30 days — then *im gileach leyom shloshim lo yatza*: shaving on the 30th day does not suffice even *bedi'eved*, because once the person explicitly stipulated 30 days, we cannot rely on *miktzat hayom kekulo* to substitute a partial day for a full one.

### Tosafot on De'oraita

Tosafot on 5b provides a crucial framing for why the *stam* case works *bedi'eved*. The *machlokes* between Bar Pada and Rav Matna — which concerned how extensively to apply *miktzat hayom kekulo* — is, according to Tosafot, a dispute only at the rabbinic level. At the *de'oraita* level, all agree that 30 days is sufficient for a *stam nazir*, and the 30th day itself is acceptable. The rabbinic extension then requires waiting until the 31st day *lechatchila*. This explains why *bedi'eved*, when a person has already shaved on the 30th day, he is *yotzei*: *dino de'oraita de-stam nazir yegaleiach beyom shloshim* — the Torah-level obligation has been met.

### Miktzat Hayom

The underlying question threading through this section is how *miktzat hayom kekulo* operates when applied at both ends of a time period simultaneously. When a person accepts *nezirut*, the very day of acceptance counts as a full day even if only a small portion of the day remains before *shki'ah*. The question is whether we can then also count a partial day at the conclusion of the period. This same issue arises in *aveilus*: the first day and the final day of mourning each count through *miktzat hayom kekulo*. One notable exception is the 12-month period of mourning for a parent, where the year cannot be shortened through *miktzat hayom kekulo* at its close — because years are composed of months, not days, and one cannot invoke *miktzat hachodesh kekulo* to treat a partial month as though it were complete. The practical implication is that the 12-month period of *aveilus* extends until *shki'ah* of its final day. The *mishnah* here reflects this same structural concern about whether a partial day at both endpoints can legitimately stand in for full days.

## Two Naziriyos

### L'chatchila Bedieved

The *mishnah* then addresses the case of *mi she-nazar shtei neziriyos* — a person who took two consecutive *neziriyos*. *Lechatchila*, the person should shave and bring *korbanos* for the first *nezirut* on the 31st day, and for the second on the 61st day. The 31st day is split: its beginning counts toward the conclusion of the first *nezirut*, and its end counts as the opening of the second. *Bedi'eved*, if the person shaved on the 30th day, the second *nezirut* would then end on the 60th day — *im gilach yom shishim chasar echad yatza*, and if the person shaved on the 59th day that would be *yotzei* as well, returning to the calculations established in the first *perek*.

### One Day Two Periods

The ability to count a single day simultaneously toward the close of one *nezirut* and the opening of another raises the deeper question of how *miktzat hayom kekulo* operates. One might compare this to *rubo kekulo* — the principle that a majority functions as the whole. But the two principles may work differently. When we say *rubo kekulo*, one understands the majority as expanding to fill the entirety; the minority is not ignored but treated as subsumed. *Miktzat hayom kekulo*, however, seems capable of operating in both directions at once: a partial day at the start of one period and a partial day at the close of another can each be counted, and thus the same calendar day serves double duty for two distinct *neziriyos*. This is a striking application of the principle and points to its distinct logical character.

## 40 Minus 1

### 40 Structural

The *mishnah*'s phrasing — *yom shishim chasar echad yatza*, "60 minus one" — reflects a recurring rhetorical pattern in *mishnayos* of expressing a number as a round figure minus one. The most prominent instances of *arba'im chasar achat* appear in two contexts: the *melachos* of *Shabbos*, which are enumerated as 40 minus one, and the *makkos* administered for various *aveiros*, which are similarly given as 40 minus one. The Torah states explicitly *arba'im yakenu lo yosif*, and *Torah she-be'al peh* derives from this that 40 itself represents an extreme maximum never actually to be reached, so that in practice the count is always 39.

### 40 Examples

The number 40 throughout *halacha* functions as a marker of completeness or transition. A *mikvah* requires 40 *se'ah* of water; Moshe Rabbeinu ascended Har Sinai for 40 days on at least two, and possibly three, occasions; Israel wandered 40 years in the desert. To say 40 minus one is therefore to gesture at something that falls just short of a complete unit — as though something is still missing from the full reckoning.

### Melachot Count

With respect to *hilchos Shabbos*, the *Tannaim* and *Amoraim* themselves debated whether the exact enumeration of *melachos* is precisely 39 or whether additional *melachos* were inadvertently excluded from the canonical list. The *machlokes* on this point further underscores that "40 minus one" is not a mechanical count but a structural statement about the relationship between the number and its parent category.

## Two Naziriyos…

### Rabbi Papyas

The *mishnah* records a specific testimony: *v'zu eidus he'id Rabbi Papyas al mi she-nazar shtei neziriyos* — Rabbi Papyas testified regarding one who took two consecutive *neziriyos*: *she-im gilach es harishona yom shloshim megaleiach es hashniya l'yom shishim* — if he shaved for the first *nezirut* on the 30th day, he shaves for the second on the 60th day, still counting a full 30 days *lechatchila* for the second even though the first was completed *bedi'eved*. *V'im gilach yom shishim chasar echad yatza* — and if he shaved on the 59th day for the second, he is nonetheless *yotzei*. The formulation recalls *Masechet Eduyos*, where *Tannaim* came before the *beit din* to transmit recollections of earlier rulings; here too Rabbi Papyas brings an older tradition, the substance of which confirms *sheyom shel shloshim oleh lo min haminyan* — that the 30th day counts toward the reckoning, in this case serving both *neziriyos* simultaneously.

## Tumah Cancels

### Stam Day 30

The *mishnah* now turns to the case of a *nazir* who becomes *tamei*. *Mi she-amar hareini nazir ve-nitma yom shloshim soter es hakol* — a person who declared *stam nezirut* and became *tamei meis* on the 30th day: the *Tanna Kamma* holds that this cancels everything. Not only must the person undergo the seven-day *taharah* process for *tumas meis*, but the entire *nezirut* is nullified and must be restarted with a full 30-day count.

*Rabbi Eliezer omer eino soter ela shiva* — Rabbi Eliezer disagrees. Importantly, his use of the word *soter* is somewhat misleading: he is not saying that seven days of the *nezirut* are cancelled. Rather, he holds that only seven days of *taharah* are required, after which the person brings his *korbanos* and the *nezirut* stands. Nothing of the *nezirut* itself is undone; the person simply must complete the *taharah* process and then proceed. During those seven days the person remains bound by the prohibitions of *nezirut* — he may not drink wine or shave — since those prohibitions remain until the *korbanos* are brought. Whether a *nazir* who is *tamei meis* is prohibited from becoming further *tamei meis* is an interesting separate question that touches on a well-known dispute between the Rambam and the Ra'avad regarding *kohanim* today, but that discussion lies beyond the scope of the present *mishnah*.

### Explicit Day 30

The *mishnah* then takes up the case of *hareini nazir shloshim yom* — an explicit 30-day *nezirut*. *Nitma yom shloshim soter es hakol* — both the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer agree that becoming *tamei* on the 30th day cancels the *nezirut* entirely and requires restarting. The *Gemara* will explain that Rabbi Eliezer's leniency does not extend to this case because when a person specifies 30 days explicitly, the implication is *shloshim shlemim* — 30 complete days — which changes the halakhic calculus even for Rabbi Eliezer.

### 100-day Tumah

When a person takes an explicitly longer *nezirut* — *hareini nazir me'ah yom*, 100 days — and becomes *tamei* on the 100th day, *nitma yom me'ah soter es hakol* according to the *Tanna Kamma*. Rabbi Eliezer, however, says *eino soter ela shloshim* — only 30 days are cancelled. This initially appears to contradict Rabbi Eliezer's own position in the earlier case. In the *stam* 30-day case, Rabbi Eliezer said the *nezirut* is not cancelled at all on the 30th day; here, with 100 days, he says 30 days are cancelled. The resolution, which the *Gemara* will develop, turns on the *drasha* of *zot toras ha-nazir beyom melos* — that even on the *yom melos*, the day of completion, one may be required to treat the *nazir* as though he needs a full restart. Rabbi Eliezer holds that even when the person has kept 99 consecutive days of pure *nezirut*, the minimum *nezirut* required to fulfill the *neder* is 30 days, and therefore a new 30-day *nezirut* suffices. The person need not repeat the entire 100-day period, because Rabbi Eliezer understands the *neder* as fundamentally satisfied by a complete 30-day *nezirut* undertaken in fulfillment of it.

### Day 101

If the person becomes *tamei* on the 101st day — the day planned for bringing *korbanos* *lechatchila*, after the 100-day *nezirut* is already complete — both the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer agree that only 30 days are cancelled, corresponding to the 30 days needed for *gidul se'ar*, the required hair growth. The logic is consistent: once the *nezirut* period itself has fully elapsed, whatever happens afterward cannot undo the substance of the *neder* already kept; what remains is only the practical requirement of re-establishing sufficient hair growth before shaving and bringing *korbanos*.

### Yom Milat

Rabbi Eliezer's governing principle is stated explicitly in the *Gemara*: *kol she-achar milot shiva soter* — anything that occurs after *yom milot*, the day of completion of the *nezirut*, cancels only seven days, the seven days needed to become *tahor*, but does not cancel the *nezirut* itself. The conceptual distinction underlying this principle is important. Taking a *nezirut* is a *neder* for a specific block of time; if a person becomes *tamei* during that period, the *tumah* destroys the ability to have kept the *neder* for that time and requires restarting. But once the period has concluded, the person has already fulfilled the *neder*. Whatever happens afterward — even while the person is technically still a *nazir* awaiting the bringing of *korbanos* — falls outside the scope of the original *neder*. Becoming *tamei* at that stage triggers an obligation to become *tahor* but does not reach back to nullify what has already been kept.

## Reconcile R. Eliezer

### 30-day Puzzle

The *Gemara* presses on the apparent inconsistency within Rabbi Eliezer's positions. For the *stam* 30-day *nazir*, Rabbi Eliezer holds that becoming *tamei* on the 30th day does not cancel the *nezirut* — only seven days of *taharah* are required. For the explicit 30-day *nazir*, Rabbi Eliezer agrees with the *Tanna Kamma* that it cancels everything. If Rabbi Eliezer's principle is *kol she-achar milot shiva soter* — that anything after the day of completion cancels only seven days — why should an explicit 30-day *nazir* be treated differently from a *stam* 30-day *nazir*? In both cases the 30th day is the final day of the *nezirut*.

### Shelemim

The *Gemara*'s resolution is that the explicit declaration of 30 days necessarily implies *shloshim shlemim* — 30 complete, full days. When a person says *hareini nazir shloshim yom* explicitly, the additional specification transforms the *neder* into one requiring whole days, not days counted through *miktzat hayom kekulo*. Under that reading, the 30th day is not yet *yom milot* — the *nezirut* is not yet complete — because complete days are required, and the 30th day has not yet finished. Rabbi Eliezer's leniency therefore does not apply: it is not "after *milot*" because *milot* has not yet arrived. The *Tanna Kamma*'s rule — that *tumah* on the 30th day cancels everything — follows as a matter of course.

### Bar Pada Matna

This resolution directly connects back to the *machlokes* between Bar Pada and Rav Matna on 5b. The *Gemara* states explicitly: *kulah ke-dahavinan bah aliba de-Bar Pada ve-Rav Matna* — the entire set of disputes in this *mishnah* flows from that earlier *machlokes* about how to count *miktzat hayom kekulo* for *nezirut*. Bar Pada, who held that 29 days suffice at the *de'oraita* level, can read the *reisha* of the *mishnah* as dealing with a case of *shlemim*; Rav Matna, who requires 30 days, has his own *okimta* to resolve the apparent contradictions. The *Gemara* on the current *daf* returns to 5b's language — *reisha na'asit ke-omer shlemim* — to show that both *Amoraim* can accommodate the *mishnah* by reading the cases as instances where *shlemim* was intended, whether explicitly stated or implied. Rabbi Eliezer's position is then aligned with Rav Matna: since the 30th day is not yet *achar milot* if *shlemim* is required, Rabbi Eliezer agrees that *tumah* on that day cancels the *nezirut*.

The *Gemara*'s discussion of what Rabbi Eliezer requires for the 100-day *nezirut* follows the same logic. For a *nezirut meruba*, Rabbi Eliezer says *eino soter ela shloshim* — even when the person must restart, it is sufficient to do a 30-day *nezirut* rather than repeat the entire 100 days. This is because *zot toras ha-nazir beyom melos* teaches that a *nazir* who becomes *tamei* on *yom melos* is still treated as a *nazir* who must fulfill a complete period — but that complete period, according to Rabbi Eliezer, need only be 30 days. The person has kept 99 consecutive days of valid *nezirut*; it would be impossible at that point to repeat 100 days and maintain the integrity of the *neder* as originally conceived. Rabbi Eliezer therefore reads the requirement of a new *nezirut* as the minimum: 30 days of *gidul se'ar* and clean *nezirut* to bring the *neder* to its proper conclusion.

## Nazir Cemtery

### Cemetery Case

The *mishnah* opens with a striking case: *mi she-nazar ve-hu be-veit ha-kevaros* — a person who takes a *neder* to become a *nazir* while standing in a cemetery. The person is presumably already *tamei meis*, either because the *meis* is present and providing *tumah* or because being within the *ohel* of a grave imparts *tumas meis* in any event. *Afilu haya sham shloshim yom ein olin lo min haminyan* — even if the person remained in the cemetery for 30 days straight, those days do not count toward the 30-day *nezirut*. *Ve-eino meivi korban tumah* — and such a person does not bring a *korban tumah*. The *korban tumah* is required only of a *nazir* who was *tahor* and then became *tamei*; a person who was already *tamei* at the moment *nezirut* was accepted cannot satisfy that prerequisite.

There is a *machlokes* among the *Rishonim* as to whether the case of the cemetery is the only configuration that triggers this *halacha*, or whether it applies equally to any person who is *tamei meis* when accepting *nezirut* — including someone who is simply walking in the street while *tamei*. Tosafot and the Rosh suggest the *mishnah*'s specification of the cemetery is illustrative rather than exclusive, and the same *halacha* would apply to any *tamei meis* who accepted *nezirut*. Other *Rishonim* may have required specifically the *beit ha-kevaros*, perhaps because there the *avi avot ha-tumah* is actively and continuously present, creating a distinct relational dynamic, or perhaps because being in a cemetery at the moment of the vow reveals that the person cannot genuinely have intended a *nezirut* to take effect immediately, since a *nazir* patently may not be *tamei*.

### Left Returned

The *mishnah* continues: *yatza ve-nichnas* — what if the person left the cemetery and re-entered? *Olin lo min haminyan u-meivi korban tumah* — in that case the days do count and the *korban tumah* applies. The *Mefareish* explains that *yatza ve-nichnas* must involve a meaningful interruption: *kegon she-hiza ve-shana ve-taval* — the person received both *haza'os* of *mei chatas* from the *parah adumah* on the seventh day, immersed in a *mikveh*, and thus became *tahor*, and only afterward re-entered the cemetery. In other words, the person became *tahor* and then became *tamei* again — the precondition for the *korban tumah* obligation. The phrasing *yatza ve-nichnas* in the *mishnah* does not seem to require this on its face, but it is the only logically coherent reading: one cannot owe a *korban tumah* without having first been *tahor* as a *nazir*.

### R. Eliezer 7th Day

Rabbi Eliezer adds a caveat: *lo bo ba-yom* — not on that very day. If the person re-entered the cemetery on the seventh day of his *taharah* process — the day on which he receives the *haza'ah*, immerses, and becomes *tahor* — the days do not yet count. The source is *ve-hayamim ha-rishonim yippolu* — "the prior days will fall" — with the plural *rishonim* understood as requiring at least two prior days of established *nezirut* before *tumah* cancels them. *Ad she-yihyu lo yamim rishonim* — there must be multiple days. A person who becomes *tahor* and immediately re-enters the cemetery the same day has never established even a single full day of *tahor nezirut* before the interruption, and therefore the condition of *yamim rishonim* — plural prior days — is not met. Absent that, there is no *korban tumah* obligation.

A related question arises in the *machlokes* between Tosafot and the Rambam regarding how those two days are counted. The seventh day of the *taharah* process itself begins already as a partial day — the *haza'ah* and *tevilah* occur at some point during the day, and the person then waits until nightfall. That first day of *taharah* therefore already invokes *miktzat hayom kekulo*. Can the second day also be counted through *miktzat hayom kekulo*? If so, the person might establish *yamim rishonim* with only a brief span of actual time — perhaps 12 to 14 hours total. Tosafot appear to accept this, while the Rambam may require that the second day be a more complete day, reflecting a stricter view of when *miktzat hayom kekulo* applies.

## R. Yochanan Reish L.

### Matla Talya

The *Gemara* records a direct dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish: *mi she-nazar ve-hu be-veit ha-kevaros* — what is the status of a person who accepts *nezirut* while in a cemetery? *Rabbi Yochanan amar nezirus chalah alav* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that the *nezirut* takes effect. His reasoning: *savar matla talya ve-kayma* — he holds that the *nezirut* is a *matla talya*, a hanging or suspended thing. The *neder* attaches itself to the person and hovers, waiting to take hold once *taharah* is achieved. *Keivan de-mishkachat taharah chaila* — as soon as it finds the person *tahor*, it takes effect. Under this view, the person does not need to re-declare *nezirut* after becoming *tahor*; the original acceptance already binds him, suspended in place until the moment of *taharah*.

### No Effect

Reish Lakish disagrees: *ein nezirus chalah alav* — the *nezirut* does not take effect at all. *Ei hadar ve-amar chaila aleih* — only if the person goes back and re-accepts *nezirut* after becoming *tahor* does it take effect; *ve-ei lo lo* — otherwise it means nothing. The logic underlying Reish Lakish's position is that *nezirut*, as a *neder*, cannot take hold under conditions where it is impossible to fulfill. A person who is *tamei meis* cannot be a *nazir* who observes the prohibitions of *nezirut* in their fullest sense. Accepting *nezirut* while *tamei* is thus analogous to *matneh al mah she-kasuv ba-Torah* — conditioning a *neder* on circumstances that the Torah itself precludes. The *mitzvos ha-Torah* prevent the *neder* from taking root, and it simply fails.

### Challenge 1

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Reish Lakish from the *mishnah* itself: the *mishnah* states that a person in a cemetery for 30 days does not bring a *korban tumah* — *eino meivi korban tumah*. Rabbi Yochanan reads this as a pointed specification: the *mishnah* is telling us that this person is indeed a *nazir* — one who has certain exemptions — but that the exemptions apply. The very need to inform us *eino meivi korban tumah* implies that there is a *nazir* present who might otherwise be subject to that obligation. The clause would be superfluous if the person were not a *nazir* at all.

### Defense 1

Reish Lakish deflects: *eino be-toras tumah* — the person is not in the category of *tumah* at all, meaning he is not a *nazir* with exemptions but simply not a *nazir*. The *mishnah*'s statement that he does not bring a *korban tumah* and that the days do not count is, according to Reish Lakish, simply a comprehensive description of someone who has no *nezirut* — *eino be-toras tumah ve-eino be-toras korban*. One can grant that this reading is somewhat strained — it is an odd way to describe a non-*nazir* — and perhaps that awkwardness itself provides some support for Rabbi Yochanan's position.

### Challenge 2

Rabbi Yochanan presses further with a *Tosefta*: *mi she-hayah tamei ve-nazar asur le-galei'ach ve-lishtot yayin ve-tamei le-meisim* — a person who was *tamei* and accepted *nezirut* is prohibited from shaving, drinking wine, and becoming *tamei le-meisim*. Moreover, *ve-im gilach ve-shasah yayin ve-nitma le-meisim harei zeh sofeg et ha-arba'im* — if he shaved, drank wine, or became *tamei le-meisim*, he receives *malkos*. *Ei amrat bishlama chaila* — if the *nezirut* takes effect, that explains why violations carry *malkos*. But if Reish Lakish holds the *nezirut* does not take effect at all, why should there be any punishment?

### Defense 2

Reish Lakish responds: *hacha be-mai askinan be-yotzei ve-nichnas* — the *Tosefta* must be dealing with the case of *yatza ve-nichnas*, the person who left the cemetery, became *tahor*, and thereby re-affirmed the *nezirut*. There is a dispute between the *Mefareish* and the Meiri about how much of an affirmation is needed at that stage. According to the *Mefareish*, the person must verbalize a re-acceptance of the *nezirut* — stating explicitly that he upholds what he declared in the cemetery. According to the Meiri, even a mental re-acceptance upon becoming *tahor* — a recognition that "now that I am *tahor* I am a *nazir*" — may suffice. Either way, Reish Lakish maintains that the original declaration in the cemetery was inoperative; it is only the post-*taharah* affirmation, explicit or implicit, that creates the binding *nezirut*.

### Challenge 3

The third challenge, which arrives at the top of 17a, comes from a *baraita*: *ein bein tamei she-nazar le-nazir tahor she-nitma ela tamei she-nazar shevi'i shelo oleh lo min ha-minyan ve-nazir tahor she-nitma ein shevi'i shelo oleh lo min ha-minyan* — there is no difference between a *tamei* person who became a *nazir* and a *tahor nazir* who became *tamei*, except in one respect: for the *tamei* person who became a *nazir*, the seventh day of his *taharah* process counts toward the 30-day *nezirut*; but for the *tahor nazir* who became *tamei*, the seventh day of his *taharah* does not count toward the *nezirut*.

This *baraita* presents a strong challenge to Reish Lakish: *ve-ei salka da'atach lo chaila, amai oleh lo min ha-minyan?* — if the *nezirut* of the *tamei* person who accepted it while *tamei* does not take effect at all, why should his seventh day of *taharah* count toward his 30-day *nezirut*? If nothing attached at the time of the vow, the commencement of the 30-day count should only begin after full *taharah*, not on the seventh day itself. Yet the *baraita* explicitly grants him credit for that seventh day. Rabbi Yochanan's view explains this naturally: the *nezirut* was already hovering — *matla talya ve-kayma* — and *keivan de-mishkachat taharah chaila*, as soon as it finds *taharah*, even a partial day of *taharah* kicks the *nezirut* into counting through *miktzat hayom kekulo*. The continuing discussion of this exchange, and Mar bar Rav Ashi's contribution, carries over to 17a.

## Rambam

### Intentional Tumah

The Rambam in *perek* 5 of *Hilchos Nezirus* addresses several related cases. A *nazir tahor* who intentionally made himself *tamei* — *nazir tahor she-timei atzmo* — receives *malkos*. The Rambam specifies that such a person in fact incurs four separate sets of lashes: *mishum lo yitma* — for violating the prohibition on a *nazir* becoming *tamei*; *mishum lo yachel devaro* — for profaning his word; *mishum lo te'acher leshalmo* — for violating *bal tacher*, the prohibition on delaying fulfillment of a *neder*; and a fourth prohibition related to certain conditions of simultaneous violation. The *bal tacher* analysis is significant: since *nezirut* is a *neder*, intentionally becoming *tamei* delays the ability to fulfill the *neder* on schedule — the person now must wait seven days to become *tahor*, bring a *korban tumah*, and only then complete a fresh period. This postponement constitutes a violation of *bal tacher le-shalmo*, and the Rambam holds it independently actionable.

### Bal Tacher

The Rambam then addresses our case — a person who was in a *beit ha-kevaros* and accepted *nezirut* there. Such a person, the Rambam rules, still violates *bal yachel devaro* and *bal tacher*. This ruling presupposes that the *nezirut* takes effect — consistent with the *halacha* as we rule, following Rabbi Yochanan — and that the person is therefore bound by the *neder* from the moment of acceptance. Remaining in the cemetery, unable to begin counting the 30-day period, constitutes a delay in fulfilling the *neder*, and that delay triggers *bal tacher*.

### Lav She'ein Bo

The Lechem Mishnah raises a challenging question about the Rambam's ruling: awarding *malkos* to the person who stood in the cemetery and took *nezirut* there seems to run afoul of the principle of *lav she'ein bo maise* — a prohibition that does not involve a physical act does not ordinarily carry *malkos*. Standing in a cemetery while accepting *nezirut* does not appear to involve any active *maise*. The Lechem Mishnah's lengthy discussion explores how the act of remaining stationary in the cemetery — deliberately staying put — might constitute a sufficient *maise* to sustain a *malkos* penalty. The analysis is admittedly a stretch: remaining in place is at the outer boundary of what we ordinarily call an *action*. Nevertheless, the Lechem Mishnah entertains the possibility that intentional stationary presence, as a deliberate choice, qualifies as a *maise* for this purpose. Other prominent examples of *lav she'ein bo maise* include believing in a false god without any act of worship, and emotional prohibitions such as *lo sisna et achicha bilvavecha* — these carry no *malkos* precisely because they involve no outward action whatsoever.

## Days vs Prohibitions

### Distinction

A fundamental conceptual distinction emerges from the foregoing analysis. A person who accepts *nezirut* in a *beit ha-kevaros* is, according to Rabbi Yochanan and the ruling *halacha*, a *nazir* from the moment of acceptance — but the 30-day count cannot begin until after *taharah*. These are two separable dimensions of *nezirut*: the status of being a *nazir*, with its attendant prohibitions, and the counting of the prescribed time period, which demands consecutive *tahor* days.

### Neder-like

The prohibitions of *nezirut* — abstaining from wine, refraining from shaving, and avoiding *tumah le-meisim* — function much like any other *neder* and extend throughout the person's *nezirut*, regardless of *taharah* status. A *neder* binds from the moment of its utterance. The counting of the 30 days, by contrast, is a requirement specifically of the *nezirut* and can only be satisfied by days lived as a *tahor nazir* in sequence. The two aspects thus operate on different tracks: the prohibitions engage immediately, the count awaits *taharah*.

### Application

The practical consequence is illustrated by the following scenario. A person accepts *nezirut* in a cemetery, remains there for 20 days, leaves, and then undergoes the seven-day *taharah* process. On day 27 from the original acceptance, the person is fully *tahor*. At that point — day 27 — the 30-day *nezirut* count begins. The count will conclude on day 56, at which point the person shaves and brings *korbanos*. Throughout the entire period from day one, the prohibitions of *nezirut* have applied; but the days in the cemetery, and the seven days of *taharah*, do not count toward the 30. Only the 30 consecutive *tahor* days beginning on day 27 constitute the *nezirut* period proper.
