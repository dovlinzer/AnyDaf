# Bava Metzia 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Elu Metzios

### Perek Structure

The second *perek* of Bava Metzia, *Elu Metzios*, turns to the foundational questions of *avedah*: when may a finder keep a lost object, and when is he obligated to announce his find so that the owner may come forward and claim it? The first *perek* dealt with interesting disputes over objects already in contention — two people grasping a *tallis*, or questions about returning a *shtar* — but now the *Gemara* arrives at the basic framework governing found objects. Characteristically, the *Gemara* takes its time before laying out the central conceptual issues, beginning instead with secondary matters and working its way toward the heart of the *sugya*.

### Mishna: Keepers

The *mishna* opens: *elu metzios shelo ve'elu chayav l'hachriz* — here are the found objects you may keep, and here are those over which you bear an obligation to announce. The list of objects that may be kept begins with *matza peiros mefuzarin*, scattered fruit — presumably fallen from a cart — and *maos mefuzarim*, scattered money that has fallen from someone's pocket. Two conditions underlie every case on this list. First, the object must be genuinely lost, not merely set down by someone who intends to return for it. Second, there must be no *siman*, no identifying mark; if there is a *siman*, we cannot presume *yeiush* — the owner's giving up of hope — because the owner could come forward, provide the identifying mark, and reclaim the object.

*Yeiush* is the pivotal concept here. It denotes the owner's despair of ever recovering the lost item. The combination of physical detachment — the object is lost and the owner does not know where it is — and psychological detachment — the owner no longer regards the item as his and holds no expectation of its return — together create a state in which anyone who acquires the object bears no obligation to restore it. The first *Rashi* on the *perek* remarks that after *yeiush* the object is *hefker*. Whether this means the object is literally ownerless, such that no one owns it, or merely that anyone may take it while it technically remains the original owner's, is an interesting conceptual question — but the operative point is clear: lost without a *siman* and with *yeiush*, the object is free to be kept.

The *mishna* continues with further cases: *krichot b'reshut harabim*, bundles of wheat left in the public domain; *igulei devela*, rounds of pressed figs with no distinguishing characteristics; *kikarot shel nachtom*, a baker's loaves — generic, unremarkable; *machrozot shel dagim*, a line of fish; *chatichot shel basar*, cuts of meat purchased in the ordinary way. These are all grocery items, so to speak, that have been dropped or forgotten, not intentionally placed with an expectation of return. To these the *mishna* adds *gizei tzemer halekuchim mimdinatan*, raw freshly-sheared wool taken from its source — undyed, uncombed, without particular identifying features — and *anitzei pishtan*, stalks of flax, and *leshonot shel argaman*, strips of purple cloth. All are generic and undistinguished. Rabbi Meir rules: *harei elu shelo* — these are yours to keep.

### R. Meir vs. R. Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda qualifies this list: *kol sheyesh bo shinui chayav lehachriz* — anything on this list that nevertheless has something distinguishing about it must be announced. His examples: if you find one of these rounds of figs and there is a shard of pottery embedded inside — *matza igul uv'tocho cheres* — that is an identifying mark, as is finding a loaf with coins baked into it. The question is whether Rabbi Meir would actually disagree; looking at the cases, it seems Rabbi Yehuda is simply providing a qualification that Rabbi Meir would readily accept. This tension will be addressed in the *Gemara*.

### R. Shimon: Anporya

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar adds: *klei anporya ein chayav lehachriz* — garments of *anporya* do not require announcement. The *Gemara* will clarify what *anporya* denotes and why such garments, despite appearing to fall into a category that might require announcement, are in fact treated like objects requiring no proclamation.

## Kav be'Arba Amot

### Quantity: Kav

The *Gemara* opens on 21a by immediately seeking to quantify the *mishna*'s case of scattered fruit: *v'chama* — how much? Rabbi Yitzchak answers: *kav b'arba amot* — a *kav* (roughly the volume of a large container, somewhere between forty-six and sixty-four ounces) scattered across four *amot*, approximately six feet. Whether this denotes a square area of six by six feet is not specified, but the measure gives a concrete benchmark for what counts as "scattered."

### Quantity vs. Quality

The *Gemara* immediately presses back: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If you can tell simply by looking that the fruit was dropped haphazardly — *derech nefila* — then even a greater density of fruit across those four *amot* would obviously qualify; the visual evidence of an accidental drop renders the quantity irrelevant. And conversely, if the arrangement appears deliberate — *derech hinuach* — then even a sparser distribution still looks like something placed there intentionally. In either case, the quantitative ratio seems beside the point: you make a qualitative visual assessment and the quantity should not change the ruling. The *Gemara* has thus moved from demanding a number to questioning whether any number can do the work. This is a striking *Gemara*: the demand for quantification is introduced and almost immediately undermined.

### Rav Ukva's Explanation

Rav Ukva bar Chama resolves the tension by identifying a third scenario that the quantitative measure is meant to address: *k'vmachnashta d'vei darei askinan* — we are dealing with the situation of clearing out a grain house. A person has been gathering his grain and transporting it away, and when he is done there is a small amount left behind — not accidentally dropped and not deliberately placed for later retrieval, but simply left over from the clearing process. The question is whether he will bother to come back for it.

Here the ratio of *kav b'arba amot* becomes meaningful. A *kav* of grain spread across four *amot* — *nafish tarchaihu* — represents significant effort relative to the modest value of the grain. *Lo tarach inish ve-lo atei ve-shakel lehu* — a person will not exert himself to return and collect it. *Afkurei mafkeir lehu* — he effectively declares it *hefker*, in the manner of *aveida mida'at*: he knows he is leaving it and simply does not care. This is not the case of someone unaware that he has lost something — it is the case of someone who consciously decides the return trip is not worth it.

If, however, the grain is concentrated in a smaller area — *batzir meihachi* — so that a return trip would quickly yield a full *kav*, then *tarach ve-hadar atei ve-shakel lehu* — he would make the effort to come back and gather it, and we therefore do not say he has made it *hefker*. The quantitative measure, then, governs this specific middle scenario of intentionally-left grain, not the simple case of accidentally dropped fruit.

### Variations & Effort

Rabbi Yirmeyah builds on this framework with a series of questions, each designed to probe the relationship between *tarchia* — effort — and *chashivut* — value. The *Gemara* is essentially asking: which factor actually drives the determination of whether the owner will return?

The first question: *chatzi kav bi-shtei amot mahu* — half a *kav* in two *amot*? This is precisely half of the original case in both dimensions. On one hand, *lo nafish tarchaihu* — the effort is less, it is a quicker job, perhaps he will come back. On the other hand, *de-lo chashivi* — but the gain is also less, only half a *kav*, and perhaps even less value makes him less likely to bother. We do not know whether the reduced effort makes return more likely or the reduced gain makes it less so.

Second: *kabaim bi-shmoneh amot* — two *kavim* in eight *amot*? This doubles both the effort and the gain. The effort is now greater than the original case, which is already on the threshold of being too much — *kol sheken denafish tarchia* — so perhaps he definitely will not return. But two *kavim* is worth more — *keivan dechashivi* — and perhaps for a larger sum he would find the effort worthwhile. Again, unresolved.

Third: *kav shumshmin b'arba amot* — a *kav* of sesame seeds in four *amot*. Sesame seeds are far more valuable than grain — *keivan dechashivi lo mafkir lei* — so the monetary gain would seem to justify a return. But they are also far more laborious to gather — *nafish tarchia* — and one might argue that gathering tiny sesame seeds takes far longer than gathering grain of the same volume, making the effort prohibitive. Which dominates?

Fourth: *kav tamrei b'arba amot* and *kav rimonei b'arba amot* — a *kav* of dates or pomegranates in four *amot*. These items are roughly the same monetary value as a *kav* of grain, so by a value calculus one might not return for them, just as for grain. But they are much larger and easier to gather — *lo nafish tarchia* — so perhaps the ease of collection makes return more likely. Or perhaps what matters is only the value, which is the same as grain, and therefore he will not return.

In every case, the *Gemara* leaves the answer as *teiku* — unresolved. Rabbi Yirmeyah is characteristic in his fondness for these edge-case questions. What is illuminating is the framework the *Gemara* has built: the simple *mishna* case of scattered fruit is a matter of visual assessment, but the *Gemara* has introduced a second category — intentionally-left grain — where the quantitative ratio captures a genuine economic calculation, and then probed the limits of that calculation systematically.

## Yeiush Shelo Midaat

### Definition

At the top of 21b, the *Gemara* introduces the central conceptual debate of the *perek*: *yeiush shelo mida'at* — *yeiush* without awareness. The scenario is this: a person has lost something but does not yet know it. Someone else comes along and finds the lost object. At the moment of finding, the owner has not consciously despaired of recovering the item — he is entirely unaware of the loss. Yet once he learns of the loss, he will certainly despair, because there is no *siman* by which he could identify the object. May the finder keep it?

### Abaye: Conscious

Abaye rules: *lo havei yeiush* — it is not *yeiush*. *Yeiush* requires active, conscious despair. The owner has not given up hope because he does not even know there is anything to despair of. Therefore when the finder picks up the object, he does so at a time when *yeiush* has not occurred, and he may not keep it.

### Rava: Inevitable

Rava rules: *havei yeiush* — it is *yeiush*. Since we know with certainty that as soon as the owner learns of the loss he will despair — *dechi yada denafal minei meya'esh* — the *yeiush* is effectively operative from now. The object is already so detached from the owner that it may be treated as if *yeiush* has already occurred.

### Agreement Cases

The *Gemara* carefully delineates the cases where Abaye and Rava agree before isolating the case of their dispute. Where there is a *siman* — an identifying mark — everyone agrees that there is no *yeiush*, even if the owner later explicitly despairs. The reasoning is important: *dechi ata liyadei b'issura ata liyadei* — when the finder picked up the object, he did so at a time when it was not permitted for him to keep it. The subsequent *yeiush* of the owner does not retroactively change the status. Furthermore, we should not *impose* *yeiush* on an owner who has not yet declared it in circumstances that do not call for it; a person who loses a wallet with identification inside will naturally say, *yehavna simanin v'shakilna leih* — I will provide the identifying mark and get it back — and we assume he will not despair. If and when he does, that is his *yeiush*, but it cannot be backdated.

The second case of universal agreement is *bezuto shel yam uvshululito shel nahar* — something swept away by the sea at high tide, or carried off by an overflowing river. This represents a case designated by the *Gemara* as *avuda mimenu umikol adam* — lost from the owner and from all mankind. Here, even if the owner insists he has not despaired, his subjective stance is overridden: objectively, the item is so completely beyond human reach that no one has a reasonable claim to it. Even with a *siman*, *Rachmana shar'yei* — the Torah permitted it. So this is the inverse: rather than *yeiush* being blocked by objective circumstances (the siman case), here *yeiush* is imposed by objective circumstances regardless of the owner's stated intention.

### Dispute: No Siman

The debate between Abaye and Rava is thus confined to the intermediate case: an object without an identifying mark, where the owner is not yet aware of the loss. Abaye insists that *yeiush* requires active, conscious giving-up of hope — *deha lo yada denafal minei* — and since the owner does not yet know, there is no *yeiush*. Rava argues that since we know with certainty that upon discovering the loss the owner will have no recourse — *simana leis li bagevei*, he has no identifying mark — his *yeiush* is effective from this moment. *Mehashtah hu demeya'esh*: effectively, he gives up hope now, even if he does not yet know it.

## Proofs from Mishna

### Scattered Fruit

The *Gemara* now attempts to bring evidence from the cases of the *mishna* for one side of this debate or the other, working through each item on the list. The *mishna*'s first case — *peiros mefuzarin*, scattered fruit — seems at first glance to support Rava: the owner presumably does not know his fruit has scattered and yet the *mishna* permits keeping it, suggesting that *yeiush shelo mida'at* is operative. But the *Gemara* deflects the proof with the explanation of Rav Ukva bar Chama already developed above: the scattered-fruit case is *aveida mida'at* — the owner knew he was leaving behind the grain that remained after clearing his storehouse, and consciously decided not to bother returning for it. There is no unaware *yeiush* here; it is a deliberate abandonment.

### Scattered Money

The case of *maos mefuzarim* — scattered money — similarly appears to support Rava. But the *Gemara* invokes the principle of Rabbi Yitzchak: *adam asuy l'mashmesh b'kiso b'chol sha'ah* — a person habitually checks his pockets regularly. We therefore assume that within the time between when the owner dropped the money and when the finder came upon it, the owner had already realized the loss, consciously despaired (there being no *siman*), and thereby the *yeiush* that allows the finder to keep the money was in fact active and aware. The case is thus again removed from the domain of *yeiush shelo mida'at*.

### Weighty Items

The cases of *igulei dveilah* — rounds of figs — and *kikarot shel nachtom* — a baker's loaves — present the same apparent challenge. The *Gemara* answers: *agav d'yakiri meida yadei behu* — precisely because these items are physically weighty, the owner will quickly sense that his bag or load is lighter than it should be and will realize something has been lost. Again, rapid awareness means active *yeiush*, not *yeiush shelo mida'at*.

### Valuable Strips

The strips of purple cloth, *leshonot shel argaman*, are neither weighty nor particularly bulky, so the previous answer does not apply. Here the *Gemara* invokes Rabbi Yitzchak's rule directly: *agav d'chashivi mashmesh b'hu* — because these strips are valuable, the owner is regularly checking to make sure they are secure, just as one who carries expensive concert tickets continually confirms they remain in his pocket. Valuable items attract the owner's vigilant attention, producing rapid awareness and thus active *yeiush*.

### Money in Shul

A *braita* is then cited: *hamotzei ma'ot b'vatei k'nesiyot u'v'vatei midrashot u'v'chol makom sheharabbim metzuyin sham* — one who finds money in a synagogue, a study hall, or any place where crowds gather may keep it, because the owners despair. The challenge is the same: perhaps the owner does not yet know he has lost it. Here Rabbi Yitzchak's original statement is positioned: he made this declaration specifically in the context of money found in a *beit midrash* or synagogue, and the principle — that a person regularly checks his pockets — was subsequently extended to the more general *mishna* case of scattered money. In a public space with constant movement, the owner would rapidly discover the loss.

## Leket Case

### Leket Permission

A new proof is introduced: *me'ematai kol adam mutarin b'leket* — from when may all people, not only the poor, take *leket*, the gleanings left after the grain harvest? The *mishna* answers: *misheyechlu bah hanimoshot* — once the *nimoshot* have passed through. Rabbi Yochanan explains that *nimoshot* are elderly people who walk with a cane; once even the slowest, most thorough elderly gleaners have gone through the field and gathered every last grain, whatever remains is no longer considered *leket* set aside for the poor and may be taken by anyone. Reish Lakish understands it as a second round of gleaning — gatherers who follow behind the first wave of gleaners.

### Multi-Town Problem

This case is relevant to the debate over *yeiush shelo mida'at* in the following way. Normally one might say that the poor people of this town have consciously despaired — they have gone through the field and gathered what they could — so it is clear *yeiush*. But the *Gemara* presses: the poor people of other towns have not despaired. They are not aware that the local gleaners have already exhausted the field. In principle, *leket* belongs to all the poor everywhere — why should the unawareness of the poor people in surrounding towns not prevent the field from becoming permissible to all? If their lack of awareness forestalls *yeiush*, then the field should remain restricted.

### Preemptive Yeiush

The *Gemara* answers with a striking observation: *keivan deika aniim hacha, hanach meikara yaushi miyashi* — since there are local poor people who will get to the *leket* first, the poor of other towns have effectively despaired from the very beginning of the harvest season. They know they will not be able to compete with the local poor who are present on the scene. Their *yeiush* is preemptive and permanent — they have already conceded that the *leket* in other towns is not for them. Accordingly, when the local poor have finished gleaning, no external claim of unawareness remains to obstruct general permission. There is no *yeiush shelo mida'at* operating here; the non-local poor's despair was conscious from the outset.

## Figs vs. Olives

### Braita Rule

The *Gemara* brings a final case from a *braita*: *ketziot baderech* — drying figs on the road, and *teenah hanotah laderech* with figs found fallen under an overhanging fig tree — *mutarot mishum gezel*, there is no prohibition of theft in taking them, and they are also exempt from *terumot umaasrot* because they are ownerless. Conversely, *bezaitim uvecharuvin* — olives under an olive tree or carobs under a carob tree — *assur*, forbidden. The asymmetry between figs and olives or carobs requires explanation.

### Abaye: Known Fall

From Abaye's position — that *yeiush shelo mida'at* is not *yeiush* — the *braita* presents no difficulty. The figs are permissible because either the owner is aware that figs regularly fall and is therefore consciously *mityayesh* when they do, or because fallen figs become disgusting and the owner knows they will be stepped on. Either way, the owner's *yeiush* regarding the figs is a *yeiush mida'at*. Olives and carobs, by contrast, do not regularly fall and remain clean when they do, so the owner has not consciously despaired — it is *yeiush shelo mida'at*, which Abaye holds ineffective.

### Rava's Difficulty

For Rava, however, the *braita* creates a problem. Rava holds that *yeiush shelo mida'at* is operative *yeiush*. If so, why are olives and carobs forbidden? Once the owner learns they have fallen, he will surely despair — there is no *siman* — and by Rava's logic that should already count as *yeiush* from the moment they were found. The *seifa* of the *braita*, prohibiting olives and carobs, seems to contradict Rava's position.

### R. Abahu: Identifiable

Rabbi Abahu resolves Rava's difficulty: *shani zayit hu v'chazuso mochiach alav* — olives are different because their appearance testifies to their origin. Olives found beneath an olive tree are evidently *from that tree*, in that location. People recognize whose olives they are. This means that olives do in fact have a *siman* — their context and appearance identify them — and therefore we cannot presume *yeiush* at all, since an owner with a *siman* would say *yehavna simanin v'shakilna leih*. The case of olives and carobs is thus removed from the domain of *yeiush shelo mida'at* altogether; it belongs to the category of objects with an identifying mark where everyone agrees *yeiush* cannot be assumed.

### R. Papa: Disgust

Rav Papa offers a different and complementary resolution. The reason figs fallen from a tree are permissible is not primarily about awareness or the mechanics of *yeiush*, but about the nature of the object itself: *te'eina im nefilatah nim'eset* — a fig, when it falls, becomes disgusting. It will be trampled, it will rot, nobody wants it. The owner's *yeiush* is therefore essentially guaranteed regardless of awareness, because no owner retains any desire to recover something that has already been rendered revolting by the fall itself. Olives and carobs, by contrast, are hard and durable. An olive before it has been cured is quite hard, and a carob similarly. They do not become disgusting when they fall, and therefore the owner retains an interest in recovering them and will not be *mityayesh* even after becoming aware of the loss. Thus Rava can explain the difference between figs and olives not by invoking *siman* but by noting that the object's post-fall state determines whether *yeiush* — aware or unaware — will ultimately occur at all.
