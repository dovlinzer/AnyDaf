# Bava Metzia 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Elu Metzios

### Perek Structure

The second *perek* of Bava Metzia, *Elu Metzios*, turns to the foundational questions of *avedah*: when may a finder keep a lost object, and when is he obligated to announce his find so that the owner may come forward and claim it? The first *perek* dealt with interesting disputes over objects already in contention — two people grasping a *tallis*, or questions about returning a *shtar* — but now the *Gemara* arrives at the basic framework governing found objects. Characteristically, the *Gemara* takes its time before laying out the central conceptual issues, beginning instead with secondary matters and working its way toward the heart of the *sugya*.

### Mishna: Keepers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵלּוּ מְצִיאוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ, וְאֵלּוּ חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז. אֵלּוּ מְצִיאוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ: מָצָא פֵּירוֹת מְפוּזָּרִין, מָעוֹת מְפוּזָּרוֹת, כְּרִיכוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְעִגּוּלֵי דְבֵילָה, כִּכָּרוֹת שֶׁל נַחְתּוֹם, מַחְרוֹזוֹת שֶׁל דָּגִים, וַחֲתִיכוֹת שֶׁל בָּשָׂר, וְגִיזֵּי צֶמֶר הַלְּקוּחִין מִמְּדִינָתָן, וַאֲנִיצֵי פִשְׁתָּן, וּלְשׁוֹנוֹת שֶׁל אַרְגָּמָן – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case where one discovers lost items, **which found items** belong **to him, and** for **which** items is **one obligated to proclaim** his find so that the owner of the lost items can come and reclaim them? **These found items** belong **to him:** If one **found scattered produce, scattered coins, bundles** of grain **in a public area, round cakes** of pressed **figs, baker's loaves, strings of fish, cuts of meat,** unprocessed **wool fleeces that are taken from their state** of origin directly after shearing, bound **flax stalks, or** bound **strips of** combed **purple wool, these** belong **to him,** as they have no distinguishing marks that would enable their owners to claim them. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *mishna* opens: *elu metzios shelo ve'elu chayav l'hachriz* — here are the found objects you may keep, and here are those over which you bear an obligation to announce. The list of objects that may be kept begins with *matza peiros mefuzarin*, scattered fruit — presumably fallen from a cart — and *maos mefuzarim*, scattered money that has fallen from someone's pocket. Two conditions underlie every case on this list. First, the object must be genuinely lost, not merely set down by someone who intends to return for it. Second, there must be no *siman*, no identifying mark; if there is a *siman*, we cannot presume *yeiush* — the owner's giving up of hope — because the owner could come forward, provide the identifying mark, and reclaim the object.

*Yeiush* is the pivotal concept here. It denotes the owner's despair of ever recovering the lost item. The combination of physical detachment — the object is lost and the owner does not know where it is — and psychological detachment — the owner no longer regards the item as his and holds no expectation of its return — together create a state in which anyone who acquires the object bears no obligation to restore it. The first *Rashi* on the *perek* remarks that after *yeiush* the object is *hefker*. Whether this means the object is literally ownerless, such that no one owns it, or merely that anyone may take it while it technically remains the original owner's, is an interesting conceptual question — but the operative point is clear: lost without a *siman* and with *yeiush*, the object is free to be kept.

The *mishna* continues with further cases: *krichot b'reshut harabim*, bundles of wheat left in the public domain; *igulei devela*, rounds of pressed figs with no distinguishing characteristics; *kikarot shel nachtom*, a baker's loaves — generic, unremarkable; *machrozot shel dagim*, a line of fish; *chatichot shel basar*, cuts of meat purchased in the ordinary way. These are all grocery items, so to speak, that have been dropped or forgotten, not intentionally placed with an expectation of return. To these the *mishna* adds *gizei tzemer halekuchim mimdinatan*, raw freshly-sheared wool taken from its source — undyed, uncombed, without particular identifying features — and *anitzei pishtan*, stalks of flax, and *leshonot shel argaman*, strips of purple cloth. All are generic and undistinguished. Rabbi Meir rules: *harei elu shelo* — these are yours to keep.

### R. Meir vs. R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ שִׁינּוּי – חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז. כֵּיצַד? מָצָא עִגּוּל וּבְתוֹכוֹ חֶרֶס, כִּכָּר וּבְתוֹכוֹ מָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If one finds **any** lost item **in which there is an alteration,** he is **obligated to proclaim** his find. **How so?** If he **found a round** cake of pressed figs with an **earthenware** shard **inside it** or **a loaf** of bread **with coins inside it,** he is obligated to proclaim his find, as perhaps the owner of the item inserted them as a distinguishing mark by means of which he could reclaim his property in case it became lost.

Rabbi Yehuda qualifies this list: *kol sheyesh bo shinui chayav lehachriz* — anything on this list that nevertheless has something distinguishing about it must be announced. His examples: if you find one of these rounds of figs and there is a shard of pottery embedded inside — *matza igul uv'tocho cheres* — that is an identifying mark, as is finding a loaf with coins baked into it. The question is whether Rabbi Meir would actually disagree; looking at the cases, it seems Rabbi Yehuda is simply providing a qualification that Rabbi Meir would readily accept. This tension will be addressed in the *Gemara*.

### R. Shimon: Anporya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל כְּלֵי אַנְפּוּרְיָא אֵין חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** If one finds **any *anpurya* vessels,** since their shape is uniform and they are indistinguishable, he is **not obligated to proclaim** his find.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar adds: *klei anporya ein chayav lehachriz* — garments of *anporya* do not require announcement. The *Gemara* will clarify what *anporya* denotes and why such garments, despite appearing to fall into a category that might require announcement, are in fact treated like objects requiring no proclamation.

## Kav be'Arba Amot

### Quantity: Kav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מָצָא פֵּירוֹת מְפוּזָּרִין. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: קַב בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches as an example of items that one finds without any distinguishing mark: If one **found scattered produce.** The Gemara asks: **And how much** produce in how large an area constitutes scattered produce? **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** It is considered scattered produce when it has a dispersal ratio of one ***kav* in** an area of **four** by four **cubits.**

The *Gemara* opens on 21a by immediately seeking to quantify the *mishna*'s case of scattered fruit: *v'chama* — how much? Rabbi Yitzchak answers: *kav b'arba amot* — a *kav* (roughly the volume of a large container, somewhere between forty-six and sixty-four ounces) scattered across four *amot*, approximately six feet. Whether this denotes a square area of six by six feet is not specified, but the measure gives a concrete benchmark for what counts as "scattered."

### Quantity vs. Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דֶּרֶךְ נְפִילָה – אֲפִילּוּ טוּבָא נָמֵי! וְאִי דֶּרֶךְ הִינּוּחַ – אֲפִילּוּ בְּצִיר מֵהָכִי נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** he found the produce scattered in **a manner** indicating that it came there by **falling** and was not deliberately placed there, then **even** if the volume of produce in that area was **greater** than this limit, it should **also** belong to him, because there is no distinguishing mark that would enable the owner to reclaim it. **And if** he found produce scattered in **a manner** indicating intentional **placement,** then **even** if the volume of produce in an area that size was **less** than this limit, he should **also not** be allowed to keep the produce, as clearly the owner plans on returning to reclaim his produce.

The *Gemara* immediately presses back: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If you can tell simply by looking that the fruit was dropped haphazardly — *derech nefila* — then even a greater density of fruit across those four *amot* would obviously qualify; the visual evidence of an accidental drop renders the quantity irrelevant. And conversely, if the arrangement appears deliberate — *derech hinuach* — then even a sparser distribution still looks like something placed there intentionally. In either case, the quantitative ratio seems beside the point: you make a qualitative visual assessment and the quantity should not change the ruling. The *Gemara* has thus moved from demanding a number to questioning whether any number can do the work. This is a striking *Gemara*: the demand for quantification is introduced and almost immediately undermined.

### Rav Ukva's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: בְּמַכְנַשְׁתָּא דְּבֵי דָרֵי עָסְקִינַן. קַב בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת דִּנְפִישׁ טִרְחַיְיהוּ – לָא טָרַח אִינִישׁ וְלָא הָדַר אָתֵי וְשָׁקֵיל לְהוּ, אַפְקוֹרֵי מַפְקַר לְהוּ. בְּצִיר מֵהָכִי – טָרַח וְהָדַר אָתֵי וְשָׁקֵיל לְהוּ, וְלָא מַפְקַר לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ukva bar Ḥama said: We are dealing with** kernels of wheat that remained during **the gathering** of grain **on the threshing floor.** For kernels scattered with a dispersal ratio of one ***kav* in** an area of **four** by four **cubits, whose** gathering requires **great exertion, a person does not exert** himself **and does not return** and **take them.** Therefore, **he renounces** his **ownership** of **them** and one who finds the kernels may keep them. For kernels scattered in an area **smaller than that,** the owner **exerts** himself **and returns** and **takes them. And** therefore, **he does not renounce** his **ownership** of **them.**

Rav Ukva bar Chama resolves the tension by identifying a third scenario that the quantitative measure is meant to address: *k'vmachnashta d'vei darei askinan* — we are dealing with the situation of clearing out a grain house. A person has been gathering his grain and transporting it away, and when he is done there is a small amount left behind — not accidentally dropped and not deliberately placed for later retrieval, but simply left over from the clearing process. The question is whether he will bother to come back for it.

Here the ratio of *kav b'arba amot* becomes meaningful. A *kav* of grain spread across four *amot* — *nafish tarchaihu* — represents significant effort relative to the modest value of the grain. *Lo tarach inish ve-lo atei ve-shakel lehu* — a person will not exert himself to return and collect it. *Afkurei mafkeir lehu* — he effectively declares it *hefker*, in the manner of *aveida mida'at*: he knows he is leaving it and simply does not care. This is not the case of someone unaware that he has lost something — it is the case of someone who consciously decides the return trip is not worth it.

If, however, the grain is concentrated in a smaller area — *batzir meihachi* — so that a return trip would quickly yield a full *kav*, then *tarach ve-hadar atei ve-shakel lehu* — he would make the effort to come back and gather it, and we therefore do not say he has made it *hefker*. The quantitative measure, then, governs this specific middle scenario of intentionally-left grain, not the simple case of accidentally dropped fruit.

### Variations & Effort

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: חֲצִי קַב בִּשְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת, מַהוּ? קַב בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, טַעְמָא מַאי – מִשּׁוּם דִּנְפִישׁ טִרְחַיְיהוּ. חֲצִי קַב בִּשְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא נְפִישׁ טִרְחַיְיהוּ – לָא מַפְקַר לְהוּ. אוֹ דִּלְמָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא חֲשִׁיבִי, וַחֲצִי קַב בִּשְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא חֲשִׁיבִי – מַפְקַר לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** If **a half-*kav*** of kernels were scattered **in** an area of **two** by four **cubits, what is** the *halakha*? The aspects of the dilemma are: In the case of one ***kav*** of kernels **scattered in an area of four** by four **cubits, what is the reason** that the owner renounces his ownership of the kernels? It is **due to** the fact **that** gathering the kernels requires **great exertion.** In the case of **a half-*kav*** of kernels scattered **in** an area of **two** by four **cubits, since** gathering **them** does **not** require **great exertion,** he does **not renounce** his **ownership** of **them. Or perhaps,** the owner renounces ownership in the case of one ***kav*** of kernels **scattered in an area of four** by four **cubits due to** the fact **that they are not** of **significant** value. In the case of **a half-*kav*** of kernels scattered **in** an area of **two** by four **cubits, since** they are certainly **not** of **significant** value, **he renounces** his **ownership** of the kernels.

Rabbi Yirmeyah builds on this framework with a series of questions, each designed to probe the relationship between *tarchia* — effort — and *chashivut* — value. The *Gemara* is essentially asking: which factor actually drives the determination of whether the owner will return?

The first question: *chatzi kav bi-shtei amot mahu* — half a *kav* in two *amot*? This is precisely half of the original case in both dimensions. On one hand, *lo nafish tarchaihu* — the effort is less, it is a quicker job, perhaps he will come back. On the other hand, *de-lo chashivi* — but the gain is also less, only half a *kav*, and perhaps even less value makes him less likely to bother. We do not know whether the reduced effort makes return more likely or the reduced gain makes it less so.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַבַּיִם בִּשְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת, מַהוּ? קַב בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, טַעְמָא מַאי – מִשּׁוּם דִּנְפִישׁ טִרְחַיְיהוּ, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן קַבַּיִם בִּשְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת, כֵּיוָן דִּנְפִישׁא טִרְחַיְיהוּ טְפֵי – מַפְקַר לְהוּ. אוֹ דִלְמָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא חֲשִׁיבִי, וְקַבַּיִם בִּשְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲשִׁיבִי – לָא מַפְקַר לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya raises a related dilemma: If **two *kav*** of kernels were scattered **in** an area of **eight** by four **cubits, what is** the *halakha*? The aspects of the dilemma are: If **one *kav*** of kernels is scattered **in an area of four** by four **cubits, what is the reason** that the owner renounces ownership? It is **due to** the fact **that** gathering **them** requires **great exertion.** This is true **all the more so** in the case of **two *kav*** of kernels scattered **in** an area of **eight** by four **cubits,** and **since** gathering **them** requires **even greater exertion,** the owner **renounces** his **ownership** of **them. Or perhaps,** the owner renounces his ownership in the case of one *kav* of kernels **scattered in an area of four** by four **cubits due to** the fact **that they are not** of **significant** value. **But** in the case of **two *kav*** of kernels scattered **in** an area of **eight** by four **cubits, since they are** of **significant** value, he **does not renounce** his **ownership** of **them.**

Second: *kabaim bi-shmoneh amot* — two *kavim* in eight *amot*? This doubles both the effort and the gain. The effort is now greater than the original case, which is already on the threshold of being too much — *kol sheken denafish tarchia* — so perhaps he definitely will not return. But two *kavim* is worth more — *keivan dechashivi* — and perhaps for a larger sum he would find the effort worthwhile. Again, unresolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַב שׁוּמְשְׁמִין בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, מַהוּ? קַב בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת טַעְמָא מַאי – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא חֲשִׁיבִי, וְשׁוּמְשְׁמִין כֵּיוָן דַּחֲשִׁיבִי – לָא מַפְקַר לְהוּ. אוֹ דִלְמָא: מִשּׁוּם דִּנְפִישׁ טִרְחַיְיהוּ, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן שׁוּמְשְׁמִין, כֵּיוָן דִּנְפִישׁ טִרְחַיְיהוּ טְפֵי – מַפְקַר לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If one ***kav* of sesame** seeds was scattered **in** an area of **four** by four **cubits, what is** the *halakha*? The aspects of the dilemma are: In the case of one ***kav*** of kernels scattered **in** an area of **four** by four **cubits, what is the reason** that the owner renounces ownership? It is **due to** the fact **that they are not** of **significant** value. **And** in the case of **sesame** seeds, **since they are** of **significant** value he **does not renounce** his **ownership** of **them. Or perhaps,** the owner renounces ownership in the case of one *kav* of kernels scattered **in** an area of **four** by four **cubits due to** the fact **that** gathering **them** requires **great exertion.** That is true **all the more so** in the case of **sesame** seeds. **Since** gathering **them** requires **even greater exertion, he renounces** his **ownership** of **them.**

Third: *kav shumshmin b'arba amot* — a *kav* of sesame seeds in four *amot*. Sesame seeds are far more valuable than grain — *keivan dechashivi lo mafkir lei* — so the monetary gain would seem to justify a return. But they are also far more laborious to gather — *nafish tarchia* — and one might argue that gathering tiny sesame seeds takes far longer than gathering grain of the same volume, making the effort prohibitive. Which dominates?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַב תַּמְרֵי בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, קַב רִמּוֹנֵי בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, מַהוּ? קַב בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, טַעְמָא מַאי? מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא חֲשִׁיבִי. קַב תַּמְרֵי בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, קַב רִמּוֹנֵי בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא חֲשִׁיבִי – מַפְקַר לְהוּ. אוֹ דִלְמָא: מִשּׁוּם דִּנְפִישׁא טִרְחַיְיהוּ, וְקַב תַּמְרֵי בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וְקַב רִמּוֹנֵי בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא נְפִישׁ טִרְחַיְיהוּ – לָא מַפְקַר לְהוּ. מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If one ***kav* of dates** was scattered with a dispersal ratio of one *kav* **in** an area of **four** by four **cubits,** or if one ***kav* of pomegranates** was scattered with a dispersal ratio of one *kav* **in** an area of **four** by four **cubits, what is** the *halakha*? The aspects of the dilemma are: In the case of one ***kav*** of kernels scattered **in** an area of **four** by four **cubits, what is the reason** that the owner renounces ownership? It is **due to** the fact **that they are not** of **significant** value; **and also** in the case of one ***kav* of dates in** an area of **four** by four **cubits or** one ***kav* of pomegranates in** an area of **four** by four **cubits, since they are not** of **significant** value **he renounces ownership** of the fruit. **Or perhaps,** the owner renounces ownership in the case of one ***kav*** of **kernels** scattered **in** an area of **four** by four **cubits due to** the fact **that** gathering **them** requires **great exertion. And** in the case of one ***kav* of dates in** an area of **four** by four **cubits or** one ***kav* of pomegranates in** an area of **four** by four **cubits, since** gathering **them does not** require **great exertion** he does **not renounce** his **ownership** of **them.** In all these cases, **what** is the *halakha*? The Gemara concludes: All these dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

Fourth: *kav tamrei b'arba amot* and *kav rimonei b'arba amot* — a *kav* of dates or pomegranates in four *amot*. These items are roughly the same monetary value as a *kav* of grain, so by a value calculus one might not return for them, just as for grain. But they are much larger and easier to gather — *lo nafish tarchia* — so perhaps the ease of collection makes return more likely. Or perhaps what matters is only the value, which is the same as grain, and therefore he will not return.

In every case, the *Gemara* leaves the answer as *teiku* — unresolved. Rabbi Yirmeyah is characteristic in his fondness for these edge-case questions. What is illuminating is the framework the *Gemara* has built: the simple *mishna* case of scattered fruit is a matter of visual assessment, but the *Gemara* has introduced a second category — intentionally-left grain — where the quantitative ratio captures a genuine economic calculation, and then probed the limits of that calculation systematically.

## Yeiush Shelo Midaat

### Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵאוּשׁ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לָא הָוֵי יֵאוּשׁ. וְרָבָא אָמַר: הָוֵי יֵאוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one's **despair** of recovering his lost item **that is not a conscious** feeling, i.e., were he aware of the loss of his property, he would have despaired of its recovery, but he was unaware of his loss when the finder discovered the item, **Abaye said: It is not** considered **despair;** the owner maintains ownership of the item, and the finder may not keep it. **And Rava said: It is** considered **despair** and the finder may keep it.

At the top of 21b, the *Gemara* introduces the central conceptual debate of the *perek*: *yeiush shelo mida'at* — *yeiush* without awareness. The scenario is this: a person has lost something but does not yet know it. Someone else comes along and finds the lost object. At the moment of finding, the owner has not consciously despaired of recovering the item — he is entirely unaware of the loss. Yet once he learns of the loss, he will certainly despair, because there is no *siman* by which he could identify the object. May the finder keep it?

### Abaye: Conscious

Abaye rules: *lo havei yeiush* — it is not *yeiush*. *Yeiush* requires active, conscious despair. The owner has not given up hope because he does not even know there is anything to despair of. Therefore when the finder picks up the object, he does so at a time when *yeiush* has not occurred, and he may not keep it.

### Rava: Inevitable

Rava rules: *havei yeiush* — it is *yeiush*. Since we know with certainty that as soon as the owner learns of the loss he will despair — *dechi yada denafal minei meya'esh* — the *yeiush* is effectively operative from now. The object is already so detached from the owner that it may be treated as if *yeiush* has already occurred.

### Agreement Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּדָבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ סִימָן – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּלָא הָוֵי יֵאוּשׁ. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּשַׁמְעִינֵיהּ דְּמִיָּאַשׁ לְסוֹף, לָא הָוֵי יֵאוּשׁ, דְּכִי אֲתָא לִידֵיהּ – בְּאִיסּוּרָא הוּא דַּאֲתָא לִידֵיהּ, דִּלְכִי יָדַע דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ לָא מִיָּאַשׁ, מֵימָר אָמַר: סִימָנָא אִית לִי בְּגַוֵּיהּ, יָהֵבְנָא סִימָנָא וְשָׁקָילְנָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara limits the scope of the dispute. **In** the case of **an item on which there is a distinguishing mark, everyone agrees** that despair that is not conscious **is not** considered **despair. And even though we hear that he ultimately despairs** of recovering the item, it is **not** considered **despair, as when** the item **came into** the **possession** of the finder, it was **in a prohibited** manner **that it came into his possession.** It is prohibited **because when** the owner **learns that it fell from his** possession, **he does not despair** of its recovery immediately. Instead, **he says: I have a distinguishing mark on** the item; **I will provide the distinguishing mark** to the finder, **and I will take** it.

The *Gemara* carefully delineates the cases where Abaye and Rava agree before isolating the case of their dispute. Where there is a *siman* — an identifying mark — everyone agrees that there is no *yeiush*, even if the owner later explicitly despairs. The reasoning is important: *dechi ata liyadei b'issura ata liyadei* — when the finder picked up the object, he did so at a time when it was not permitted for him to keep it. The subsequent *yeiush* of the owner does not retroactively change the status. Furthermore, we should not *impose* *yeiush* on an owner who has not yet declared it in circumstances that do not call for it; a person who loses a wallet with identification inside will naturally say, *yehavna simanin v'shakilna leih* — I will provide the identifying mark and get it back — and we assume he will not despair. If and when he does, that is his *yeiush*, but it cannot be backdated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּזוּטוֹ שֶׁל יָם וּבִשְׁלוּלִיתוֹ שֶׁל נָהָר, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִית בֵּיהּ סִימָן, רַחֲמָנָא שַׁרְיֵיהּ, כִּדְבָעֵינַן לְמֵימַר לְקַמַּן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to an item swept away **by the tide of the sea or by the flooding of a river, even though** the item **has a distinguishing mark, the Merciful One permits** the finder to keep **it as we seek to state below,** later in the discussion.

The second case of universal agreement is *bezuto shel yam uvshululito shel nahar* — something swept away by the sea at high tide, or carried off by an overflowing river. This represents a case designated by the *Gemara* as *avuda mimenu umikol adam* — lost from the owner and from all mankind. Here, even if the owner insists he has not despaired, his subjective stance is overridden: objectively, the item is so completely beyond human reach that no one has a reasonable claim to it. Even with a *siman*, *Rachmana shar'yei* — the Torah permitted it. So this is the inverse: rather than *yeiush* being blocked by objective circumstances (the siman case), here *yeiush* is imposed by objective circumstances regardless of the owner's stated intention.

### Dispute: No Siman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ סִימָן. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לָא הָוֵי יֵאוּשׁ, דְּהָא לָא יָדַע דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ. רָבָא אָמַר: הָוֵי יֵאוּשׁ, דִּלְכִי יָדַע דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ – מִיָּאַשׁ. מֵימָר אָמַר: סִימָנָא לֵית לִי בְּגַוֵּיהּ, מֵהַשְׁתָּא הוּא דְּמִיָּאַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **When they disagree,** it is **with regard to an item in which there is no distinguishing mark. Abaye said:** Despair that is not conscious **is not** considered **despair, as he did not know that** the item **fell from him;** therefore, he cannot despair of recovering it. **Rava said:** Despair that is not conscious **is** considered **despair, as when he discovers that it fell from him, he will despair** of its recovery; as **he says** upon this discovery: **I have no distinguishing mark on** the item. Therefore, it is considered **from now,** when the item fell, **that he despairs.**

The debate between Abaye and Rava is thus confined to the intermediate case: an object without an identifying mark, where the owner is not yet aware of the loss. Abaye insists that *yeiush* requires active, conscious giving-up of hope — *deha lo yada denafal minei* — and since the owner does not yet know, there is no *yeiush*. Rava argues that since we know with certainty that upon discovering the loss the owner will have no recourse — *simana leis li bagevei*, he has no identifying mark — his *yeiush* is effective from this moment. *Mehashtah hu demeya'esh*: effectively, he gives up hope now, even if he does not yet know it.

## Proofs from Mishna

### Scattered Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: פֵּירוֹת מְפוּזָּרִין, הָא לָא יָדַע דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ! הָא אָמַר רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: הָכָא בְּמַכְנַשְׁתָּא (דְּבִיזְרֵי) [דְּבֵי דָרֵי] עָסְקִינַן, דַּאֲבֵידָה מִדַּעַת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: If one found **scattered produce,** it belongs to him. The Gemara asks: Why does it belong to him; **isn't** the owner **unaware that they fell from him?** Apparently, despair that is not conscious is considered despair. The Gemara rejects that proof: **Didn't Rav Ukva bar Ḥama say: We are dealing with kernels of wheat that remained during** the **gathering** of grain **on the threshing floor?** The owner knowingly left the kernels on the threshing floor because it was not worth his while to gather them. **That is a deliberate loss,** and therefore the despair is conscious. Therefore, this clause in the mishna is not relevant to the dispute in question.

The *Gemara* now attempts to bring evidence from the cases of the *mishna* for one side of this debate or the other, working through each item on the list. The *mishna*'s first case — *peiros mefuzarin*, scattered fruit — seems at first glance to support Rava: the owner presumably does not know his fruit has scattered and yet the *mishna* permits keeping it, suggesting that *yeiush shelo mida'at* is operative. But the *Gemara* deflects the proof with the explanation of Rav Ukva bar Chama already developed above: the scattered-fruit case is *aveida mida'at* — the owner knew he was leaving behind the grain that remained after clearing his storehouse, and consciously decided not to bother returning for it. There is no unaware *yeiush* here; it is a deliberate abandonment.

### Scattered Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מָעוֹת מְפוּזָּרוֹת – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, אַמַּאי? הָא לָא יָדַע דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ! הָתָם נָמֵי כִּדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק דְּאָמַר: אָדָם עָשׂוּי לְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בְּכִיסוֹ בְּכׇל שָׁעָה וְשָׁעָה. הָכָא נָמֵי אָדָם עָשׂוּי לְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בְּכִיסוֹ בְּכׇל שָׁעָה וְשָׁעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: If one found **scattered coins, these** belong **to him.** The Gemara asks: **Why** do they belong to the one who finds them; **isn't** the owner **unaware that they fell from him?** Apparently, despair that is not conscious is considered despair. The Gemara rejects that proof: **There too,** it is not a case of unconscious despair, **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak, who says: A person is prone to feel his** money **pouch constantly. Here too, a person is prone to feel his** money **pouch constantly;** therefore, it is reasonable to assume that shortly after the coins fell, the owner became aware of his loss.

The case of *maos mefuzarim* — scattered money — similarly appears to support Rava. But the *Gemara* invokes the principle of Rabbi Yitzchak: *adam asuy l'mashmesh b'kiso b'chol sha'ah* — a person habitually checks his pockets regularly. We therefore assume that within the time between when the owner dropped the money and when the finder came upon it, the owner had already realized the loss, consciously despaired (there being no *siman*), and thereby the *yeiush* that allows the finder to keep the money was in fact active and aware. The case is thus again removed from the domain of *yeiush shelo mida'at*.

### Weighty Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: עִיגּוּלֵי דְבֵילָה וְכִכָּרוֹת שֶׁל נַחְתּוֹם – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. אַמַּאי? וְהָא לָא יָדַע דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ? הָתָם נָמֵי, אַגַּב דְּיַקִּירֵי מִידָּע יָדַע בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: If one found **round cakes** of pressed **figs or baker's loaves, these** belong **to him.** The Gemara asks: **Why** do they belong to the one who finds them; **isn't** the owner **unaware that they fell from him?** Apparently, despair that is not conscious is considered despair. The Gemara rejects that proof: **There too,** it is not a case of unconscious despair. **Since** these items **are heavy he knows** that **they** fell, and it is reasonable to assume that shortly after they fell the owner became aware of his loss.

The cases of *igulei dveilah* — rounds of figs — and *kikarot shel nachtom* — a baker's loaves — present the same apparent challenge. The *Gemara* answers: *agav d'yakiri meida yadei behu* — precisely because these items are physically weighty, the owner will quickly sense that his bag or load is lighter than it should be and will realize something has been lost. Again, rapid awareness means active *yeiush*, not *yeiush shelo mida'at*.

### Valuable Strips

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וּלְשׁוֹנוֹת שֶׁל אַרְגָּמָן – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. וְאַמַּאי? הָא לָא יָדַע דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ! הָתָם נָמֵי, אַגַּב דַּחֲשִׁיבִי מַשְׁמוּשֵׁי מְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בְּהוּ, וְכִדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: If one found **strips of purple wool, these** belong **to him.** The Gemara asks: **And why** do they belong to the one who finds them; **isn't** the owner **unaware that they fell from him?** Apparently, despair that is not conscious is considered despair. The Gemara rejects that proof: **There too,** it is not a case of unconscious despair. **Since they are significant** and valuable, the owner **feels** around **for them** to ensure that they are not lost, **and** therefore, it is reasonable to assume that shortly after the strips fell, the owner became aware of his loss. This reasoning is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak** with regard to coins.

The strips of purple cloth, *leshonot shel argaman*, are neither weighty nor particularly bulky, so the previous answer does not apply. Here the *Gemara* invokes Rabbi Yitzchak's rule directly: *agav d'chashivi mashmesh b'hu* — because these strips are valuable, the owner is regularly checking to make sure they are secure, just as one who carries expensive concert tickets continually confirms they remain in his pocket. Valuable items attract the owner's vigilant attention, producing rapid awareness and thus active *yeiush*.

### Money in Shul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמּוֹצֵא מָעוֹת בְּבָתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבְבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת, וּבְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁהָרַבִּים מְצוּיִין שָׁם – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַבְּעָלִים מִתְיָאֲשִׁין מֵהֶן. וְהָא לָא יָדַע דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אָדָם עָשׂוּי לְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בְּכִיסוֹ בְּכׇל שָׁעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: In the case of **one who finds coins in synagogues, and in study halls, and in any place where the multitudes are found, these** coins belong **to him due to** the fact **that the owners despair of their** recovery. Why do they belong to him; **isn't** the owner **unaware that** the coins **fell from him? Rabbi Yitzḥak says: A person is prone to feel his** money **pouch constantly;** therefore, it is reasonable to assume that shortly after the coins fell, the owner became aware of his loss.

A *braita* is then cited: *hamotzei ma'ot b'vatei k'nesiyot u'v'vatei midrashot u'v'chol makom sheharabbim metzuyin sham* — one who finds money in a synagogue, a study hall, or any place where crowds gather may keep it, because the owners despair. The challenge is the same: perhaps the owner does not yet know he has lost it. Here Rabbi Yitzchak's original statement is positioned: he made this declaration specifically in the context of money found in a *beit midrash* or synagogue, and the principle — that a person regularly checks his pockets — was subsequently extended to the more general *mishna* case of scattered money. In a public space with constant movement, the owner would rapidly discover the loss.

## Leket Case

### Leket Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מֵאֵימָתַי כׇּל אָדָם מוּתָּרִים בַּלֶּקֶט? מִשֶּׁיֵּלְכוּ בָּהּ הַנָּמוֹשׁוֹת. וְאָמְרִינַן: מַאי נָמוֹשׁוֹת? וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: סָבֵי דְּאָזְלִי אַתִּיגְרָא. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לָקוֹטֵי בָּתַר לָקוֹטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (*Pe'a* 8:1): **From when is it permitted** for **any person to** collect **gleanings,** which the Torah designates as exclusively for the poor (see Leviticus 19:9–10)? It is permitted **once the *nemushot* have walked in** the field. **And we say** in interpreting the mishna: **What are *nemushot*? And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** They are the **elderly people who walk** leaning **on a cane.** Since they walk slowly, they will see any stalks that remain and take them. **Reish Lakish said:** They are the second wave of **gleaners** who pass through the field **after** the initial **gleaners,** collecting any stalks that remain.

This case is relevant to the debate over *yeiush shelo mida'at* in the following way. Normally one might say that the poor people of this town have consciously despaired — they have gone through the field and gathered what they could — so it is clear *yeiush*. But the *Gemara* presses: the poor people of other towns have not despaired. They are not aware that the local gleaners have already exhausted the field. In principle, *leket* belongs to all the poor everywhere — why should the unawareness of the poor people in surrounding towns not prevent the field from becoming permissible to all? If their lack of awareness forestalls *yeiush*, then the field should remain restricted.

### Preemptive Yeiush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? נְהִי דַּעֲנִיִּים דְּהָכָא מִיָּאֲשִׁי – אִיכָּא עֲנִיִּים בְּדוּכְתָּא אַחְרִיתָא דְּלָא מִיָּאֲשִׁי! אָמְרִי: כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא עֲנִיִּים הָכָא, הָנָךְ מֵעִיקָּרָא אִיָּאוֹשֵׁי מִיָּאַשׁ, וְאָמְרִי: עֲנִיִּים דְּהָתָם מְלַקְּטִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why** is it permitted for any person to take the stalks, given that **although the poor who** are **here renounce ownership** of the stalks after seeing the *nemushot* pass through the field, **there are poor** people **in another place who** are unaware of the passing of the *nemushot* and **do not renounce ownership?** Apparently, despair that is not conscious is considered despair. The Sages **say** in rejecting that proof: **Since there are poor** people **here, those** poor people in the other places **despair** of the gleanings **from the outset, and they say:** The **poor** people **who** are **there gather** the gleanings.

The *Gemara* answers with a striking observation: *keivan deika aniim hacha, hanach meikara yaushi miyashi* — since there are local poor people who will get to the *leket* first, the poor of other towns have effectively despaired from the very beginning of the harvest season. They know they will not be able to compete with the local poor who are present on the scene. Their *yeiush* is preemptive and permanent — they have already conceded that the *leket* in other towns is not for them. Accordingly, when the local poor have finished gleaning, no external claim of unawareness remains to obstruct general permission. There is no *yeiush shelo mida'at* operating here; the non-local poor's despair was conscious from the outset.

## Figs vs. Olives

### Braita Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: קְצִיעוֹת בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּצַד שְׂדֵה קְצִיעוֹת, וְכֵן תְּאֵנָה הַנּוֹטָה לַדֶּרֶךְ, וּמָצָא תְּאֵנִים תַּחְתֶּיהָ – מוּתָּרוֹת מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל, וּפְטוּרוֹת מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. בְּזֵיתִים וּבְחָרוּבִים – אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (*Ma'asrot* 3:4): If **dried figs** are found **on the path, and even** if they were found **at the side of a field** where **dried figs** are spread to dry, **and likewise,** if there is **a fig** tree whose branches **extend over a path and one found figs beneath it,** those figs **are permitted** and taking them is not prohibited **due to** the prohibition of **robbery. And** as these are ownerless property, one who finds them is **exempt from** the obligation to separate **tithes.** In the case **of olives or of carobs,** it is **prohibited** to take the fruit.

The *Gemara* brings a final case from a *braita*: *ketziot baderech* — drying figs on the road, and *teenah hanotah laderech* with figs found fallen under an overhanging fig tree — *mutarot mishum gezel*, there is no prohibition of theft in taking them, and they are also exempt from *terumot umaasrot* because they are ownerless. Conversely, *bezaitim uvecharuvin* — olives under an olive tree or carobs under a carob tree — *assur*, forbidden. The asymmetry between figs and olives or carobs requires explanation.

### Abaye: Known Fall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא רֵישָׁא לְאַבָּיֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא: אַגַּב דַּחֲשִׁיבִי – מְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בְּהוּ. תְּאֵנָה נָמֵי מִידָּע יְדִיעַ דְּנָתְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, the first clause** of the mishna is **not difficult according to** the opinion of **Abaye,** as he can explain that one consciously despairs of recovering the dried figs. **Since** dried figs **are significant** and valuable, **one feels** around **for them** to ensure that they have not become lost. It is reasonable to assume that shortly after the fruits fell, the owner became aware of his loss and despaired of recovering them. In the case of the **fig** tree, **too, one knows that** it is a common occurrence for the fruit of the fig tree to **fall** from the tree and he renounces ownership from the outset.

From Abaye's position — that *yeiush shelo mida'at* is not *yeiush* — the *braita* presents no difficulty. The figs are permissible because either the owner is aware that figs regularly fall and is therefore consciously *mityayesh* when they do, or because fallen figs become disgusting and the owner knows they will be stepped on. Either way, the owner's *yeiush* regarding the figs is a *yeiush mida'at*. Olives and carobs, by contrast, do not regularly fall and remain clean when they do, so the owner has not consciously despaired — it is *yeiush shelo mida'at*, which Abaye holds ineffective.

### Rava's Difficulty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא סֵיפָא לְרָבָא קַשְׁיָא, דְּקָתָנֵי ״בְּזֵיתִים וּבְחָרוּבִים אָסוּר״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: שָׁאנֵי זַיִת, הוֹאִיל וְחָזוּתוֹ מוֹכִיחַ עָלָיו, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּנָתְרִין זֵיתֵי מִידָּע יְדִיעַ, דּוּכְתָּא דְּאִינִישׁ אִינִישׁ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **But the latter clause** of the mishna is **difficult according to** the opinion of **Rava, as it teaches:** In the case **of olives or of carobs,** it is **prohibited** to take the fruit. Apparently, despair that is not conscious is not considered despair. **Rabbi Abbahu said:** The *halakha* of **an olive is different, since its appearance proves** the identity **of** the owner, as the fruit fallen from the tree appears similar to the fruit on that tree, **and even though the olives fall** off the tree, the one who finds the olives **knows** that an olive tree that is located in **a place that** is owned by a specific **person** belongs to that **person** and the owner will not renounce ownership of his fruit.

For Rava, however, the *braita* creates a problem. Rava holds that *yeiush shelo mida'at* is operative *yeiush*. If so, why are olives and carobs forbidden? Once the owner learns they have fallen, he will surely despair — there is no *siman* — and by Rava's logic that should already count as *yeiush* from the moment they were found. The *seifa* of the *braita*, prohibiting olives and carobs, seems to contradict Rava's position.

### R. Abahu: Identifiable

Rabbi Abahu resolves Rava's difficulty: *shani zayit hu v'chazuso mochiach alav* — olives are different because their appearance testifies to their origin. Olives found beneath an olive tree are evidently *from that tree*, in that location. People recognize whose olives they are. This means that olives do in fact have a *siman* — their context and appearance identify them — and therefore we cannot presume *yeiush* at all, since an owner with a *siman* would say *yehavna simanin v'shakilna leih*. The case of olives and carobs is thus removed from the domain of *yeiush shelo mida'at* altogether; it belongs to the category of objects with an identifying mark where everyone agrees *yeiush* cannot be assumed.

### R. Papa: Disgust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ רֵישָׁא נָמֵי! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: תְּאֵנָה עִם נְפִילָתָהּ נִמְאֶסֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, then** in the **first** clause **as well,** it should be prohibited to take the fruit that fell from the fig tree. **Rav Pappa said: A fig becomes disgusting with its fall** from the tree. Even if the fruit can be attributed to the tree of origin, since it is no longer fit for consumption, the owner would not want the fruit and consequently renounces his ownership of it.

Rav Papa offers a different and complementary resolution. The reason figs fallen from a tree are permissible is not primarily about awareness or the mechanics of *yeiush*, but about the nature of the object itself: *te'eina im nefilatah nim'eset* — a fig, when it falls, becomes disgusting. It will be trampled, it will rot, nobody wants it. The owner's *yeiush* is therefore essentially guaranteed regardless of awareness, because no owner retains any desire to recover something that has already been rendered revolting by the fall itself. Olives and carobs, by contrast, are hard and durable. An olive before it has been cured is quite hard, and a carob similarly. They do not become disgusting when they fall, and therefore the owner retains an interest in recovering them and will not be *mityayesh* even after becoming aware of the loss. Thus Rava can explain the difference between figs and olives not by invoking *siman* but by noting that the object's post-fall state determines whether *yeiush* — aware or unaware — will ultimately occur at all.
