# Ketubot 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Achas B'chayav

### Case Setup

The sugya before us involves a case of competing claims from two sets of children whose mothers were married to the same husband, Reuven. Reuven first married Rachel, whose *ketubah* was worth twenty thousand dollars. Rachel died during Reuven's lifetime, meaning he retained the funds he would otherwise have paid her. Under the rabbinic enactment known as *ketubat benin dichrin*, however, Rachel's sons are entitled, upon Reuven's death, to extract the value of their mother's *ketubah* from his estate before the remainder is divided equally among all his heirs. Reuven subsequently married Leah, who survived him. When Reuven dies, Leah's children come to collect their mother's *ketubah* as a debt owed by the estate.

The structural asymmetry is significant. Rachel's children come as heirs — they are invoking a special inheritance right to their mother's *ketubah* amount. Leah's children come as creditors — they are collecting a monetary obligation the estate owes their still-living mother. The Mishnah's basic ruling for this scenario, called *achas b'chayav v'achas b'moso* — one wife died while the husband was alive, one died after him — is straightforward: debt takes priority over inheritance. Leah's children first collect their mother's *ketubah*, and then Rachel's children take their special portion, with whatever remains distributed equally.

### Mishnah Ruling

The Mishnah's resolution follows the general principle that a creditor's claim precedes an heir's claim. Once Leah's children, acting in the capacity of creditors, have collected their mother's *ketubah*, the remaining estate is available for the application of *ketubat benin dichrin*. Rachel's sons then extract the value of their mother's *ketubah* from that remainder, and only what is left after that is divided equally among all heirs. This ordering — debt first, then the special inheritance, then equal division — reflects the hierarchy that governs estates in *halacha*.

### Initial Question

The Gemara, however, is not satisfied with simply establishing the order of collection. It probes a deeper question: in a scenario where Leah's children are coming as creditors rather than as co-beneficiaries of *ketubat benin dichrin*, are Rachel's children still entitled to their special status at all? When both wives predeceased the husband, each set of children holds equivalent standing — both are special heirs under *ketubat benin dichrin*, each taking their mother's *ketubah* amount before the equal division. But here, Leah's children are not invoking *ketubat benin dichrin*; they are simply collecting a debt. Why, Leah's children might argue, should Rachel's children enjoy a privileged position that we are not receiving ourselves?

### Ben Nannas/R. Akiva

The Gemara establishes that this very question is the subject of a *tannaitic* dispute, introduced by the phrase *detanya* — as taught in a *baraita*. The case in the *baraita* is precisely *achas b'chayav v'achas b'moso*: Rachel died during Reuven's lifetime, Leah died after him. Ben Nannas rules that Rachel's children may say to Leah's children: *b'nei ba'alat chov attem* — you are the children of a creditor. Take your mother's *ketubah* and leave. The implication is that Rachel's children retain their full entitlement to *ketubat benin dichrin*, undisturbed by the asymmetry of positions.

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He uses the vivid phrase *kvar kaftza nachalah lifnei bnei rishona v'nafla lifnei bnei shniya* — the inheritance has already leapt away from Rachel's children and fallen before Leah's children. The colorful language conveys that Rachel's sons, who seemed poised to take their special portion, have lost their grip on it. The Gemara initially reads this as a debate over a fundamental principle: does *ketubat benin dichrin* apply when the two sets of heirs come from different legal positions — one as special heirs, one as creditors? Ben Nannas says yes; Rabbi Akiva says no.

### Rava's Resolution

Rava, however, intervenes with a startling interpretive move. He reports that he encountered the Rabbis of the academy of Rav debating this very passage, and he challenged their reading. Their assumption had been that Rabbi Akiva and Ben Nannas dispute the fundamental question of whether *ketubat benin dichrin* applies in *achas b'chayav v'achas b'moso*. Rava argues otherwise: *d'kulei alma achas b'chayav v'achas b'moso yesh lahen ketubat benin dichrin* — everyone agrees that in principle Rachel's children are entitled to their special inheritance. The dispute, he insists, is not about whether the right exists but about a separate technical requirement: whether the conditions for activating that right have been met.

### Motar Dina Question

That separate technical requirement is the rule of *motar dina* — there must be leftover money in the estate beyond the amounts extracted by the special heirs. *Ketubat benin dichrin* cannot consume the entire estate; there must remain some residual amount subject to the ordinary rules of inheritance. The debate between Ben Nannas and Rabbi Akiva, on Rava's reading, is about whether paying out a *ketubah* debt counts as satisfying that requirement.

Consider the arithmetic: Reuven dies leaving an estate of thirty thousand dollars. Leah's children come and collect their ten thousand dollar *ketubah* debt. Twenty thousand dollars remains — precisely the amount of Rachel's *ketubah*. If Rachel's children take their twenty thousand dollars, nothing is left for equal division. Has the requirement of leftover money been met? Ben Nannas says yes: the money used to pay Leah's children counts as the portion of the estate that was subject to ordinary inheritance rules. All the heirs equally bore the obligation to pay that debt, and in discharging it, the estate effectively underwent a normal process of inheritance — then Rachel's children may step forward and take their special portion. Rabbi Akiva says no: that arrangement does not constitute genuine leftover money in the estate.

Rava sharpens the argument further. In the case of an ordinary third-party creditor — some stranger who had a claim against the estate — everyone would agree that paying that claim constitutes a *motar*, a leftover, sufficient to enable *ketubat benin dichrin*. The heirs collectively bore the debt, discharged it from the estate as a normal inheritance transaction, and Rachel's children may then take their portion. The dispute arises specifically when the creditor is Leah's children themselves. There, Leah's children might object: we are not first inheriting equally and then paying ourselves — we are simply being paid first, and the inheritance discussion begins only afterward. The payment to us is not a *motar*; it is merely the discharge of a prior claim. That is the crux of the debate.

Two related but distinct concerns thus animate the entire discussion. First, is it objectionable in principle that Rachel's children receive a special inheritance status that Leah's children do not? Second — and this is the concern Rava centers — even granting that Rachel's children are entitled in principle, has the *motar dina* requirement been satisfied when the only available funds were consumed in paying the debt to Leah's children?

### Rav Yosef Challenge

Rav Yosef challenges Rava's entire interpretive framework. If, as Rava claims, Rabbi Akiva agrees in principle that Rachel's children are entitled and disputes only the circumstantial question of whether there was sufficient *motar* in this case, then Rabbi Akiva should have expressed himself very differently. Instead of the dramatic phrase about the inheritance "leaping away" from Rachel's children, he should have said simply: *im yesh mosar dinar mibaei lei* — if there is leftover money, Rachel's children may collect; if not, they may not. The language Rabbi Akiva actually uses — the inheritance leapt away and they lost it — sounds like an absolute disqualification, not a circumstantial one. It sounds like Rabbi Akiva is saying that in *achas b'chayav v'achas b'moso*, Rachel's children categorically lose their special status.

Rav Yosef therefore concludes: *ela amar Rav Yosef, be'achas b'chayav ube'achas b'moso kamiflagi* — the dispute between Ben Nannas and Rabbi Akiva is genuinely about the fundamental principle. Rabbi Akiva holds that *ketubat benin dichrin* can only operate when both sets of children stand in the same position — both as special heirs simultaneously. When one set comes as creditors and the other as heirs, the mechanism breaks down entirely for the heirs. Ben Nannas holds that Rachel's children retain their special status regardless of the position from which Leah's children collect.

The Gemara then extends this analysis to a parallel *baraita* involving Rabbi Shimon and the *Tanna Kamma*, whose positions map onto the same underlying debate. In that *baraita*, the *Tanna Kamma* states only that Leah's children come and collect their mother's *ketubah*, conspicuously omitting any special treatment for Rachel's children — which appears to parallel Rabbi Akiva's categorical disqualification. Rabbi Shimon then states: if there is an extra *dinar* beyond the *ketubah* amounts, each set of children collects their mother's *ketubah* through *ketubat benin dichrin*; if not, they divide equally — which appears to parallel Ben Nannas's position that Rachel's children remain entitled in principle, subject to the *motar dinar* requirement.

The Gemara, however, resists accepting this straightforward alignment. It proposes alternative readings in which both the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Shimon agree that Rachel's children are entitled in principle, while debating only the technical question of what constitutes a sufficient *motar*. The Gemara explores whether the dispute concerns the type of property that counts as leftover — specifically, whether *karka* (land) alone qualifies, or whether *metaltelin* (movable property, chattel) suffices — and whether encumbered property (*meshubadei*) can count, or only unencumbered property (*bnei chorin*). Each of these readings, however, runs into difficulties with the precise language of Rabbi Shimon's formulation, which, on close inspection, appears to be imposing a stricter standard rather than a more lenient one. The Gemara ultimately concludes — *ela ki hanech terei lishnei kamma'ei v'ipoch* — that we should revert to the first two structural explanations but reverse who is being lenient and who is being strict. On this reading, the *Tanna Kamma* is actually lenient: whenever any *motar dinar* exists, Rachel's children collect. Rabbi Shimon is being strict: he requires that the *motar dinar* consist specifically of land or of unencumbered property. The *Tanna Kamma*'s silence about Rachel's children in the opening clause reflects only the circumstance that in the case he described there was no *motar dinar* at all — not that Rachel's children lack entitlement in principle.

## Three Wives Case

### Hilchata Conclusion

After this extended analytical journey, the Gemara delivers its *halachic* conclusion through Mar Zutra in the name of Rav Pappa: *hilchata, achas b'chayav v'achas b'moso yesh lahen ketuvat bnei dichrin* — the law is that even in *achas b'chayav v'achas b'moso*, Rachel's children are entitled to *ketubat benin dichrin*. This ruling vindicates the persistent interpretive instinct of the discussion, which kept trying to read every tannaitic position as agreeing in principle that Rachel's children are entitled. The social rationale is compelling: a father will give a generous dowry to his daughter only if he can be confident that those funds will remain within his grandchildren's inheritance, regardless of the circumstances surrounding the other wife's death or her children's legal posture at the time of collection.

The *hilchata* further establishes: *v'ketuvah na'asit motir l'chavertah* — a *ketubah* payment made to Leah's children counts as the *motar*, the leftover amount required to activate Rachel's entitlement. The logic is that all heirs equally bore the obligation to discharge Leah's *ketubah* debt, and the portion of the estate applied to that debt represents a legitimate predicate for the subsequent inheritance process. Concretely: if Reuven died leaving twenty thousand dollars and one additional dollar, and Leah's children collected their ten thousand dollar *ketubah* from that estate, that payment counts as establishing a *motar*. The remaining twenty thousand and one dollars is not fully consumed by Rachel's *ketubah*, because a real portion of the estate — even a single dollar — was independently inherited and paid out. Rachel's children may now take their special portion.

One might ask why the *hilchata* needs to state both principles explicitly — first that Rachel's children are always entitled, and second that paying Leah's *ketubah* counts as *motar*. If you only knew the first, you might have thought that Rachel's children are entitled only when there is genuine leftover money remaining *after* both *ketubah* payments, not money used to pay Leah's claim. If you only knew the second, you might have thought the rule about payment counting as *motar* applies only when both sets of children share equal standing as *ketubat benin dichrin* heirs — not when one set comes as creditors. Each principle is independently necessary.

### Three Wives Setup

The Gemara demonstrates why both principles require explicit statement through the case of three wives. Reuven married three women. Rachel and Leah both died during his lifetime, so their children are both entitled to *ketubat benin dichrin* on equal footing — this is the standard case with no asymmetry of standing. The third wife survived Reuven and has only daughters. When Rachel's and Leah's sons come to divide the estate, they discover that after their two *ketubah* extractions, the only remaining money in the estate is needed to pay the third wife's *ketubah* debt. Her daughters, as females, are not heirs under biblical law and will not claim *ketubat benin dichrin*.

### Why Both Rules

In this three-wife scenario, the second principle — that a *ketubah* payment counts as *motar* — is essential and would need to be stated even if one had never heard of *achas b'chayav v'achas b'moso*. The two boys (from Rachel and Leah) are on an equal playing field; their entitlement is not in question. What is in question is whether the money used to pay the third wife's *ketubah* qualifies as the *motar dinar*. Without the second ruling, one might think that only genuinely surplus funds — money left after all obligations are met — qualify, not money deployed to pay a creditor. The second principle answers this.

Conversely, in the *achas b'chayav v'achas b'moso* scenario, one might have thought that even if a payment to a third-party creditor counts as *motar*, the payment specifically to Leah's own children does not — because Leah's children might say we were never part of the inheritance, we were just getting paid first. Or one might have thought that since Leah's children are coming as creditors and Rachel's children as heirs, the asymmetry in standing disqualifies Rachel's children from *ketubat benin dichrin* entirely. The first principle — *achas b'chayav v'achas b'moso yesh lahen ketubat benin dichrin* — directly rules out that concern. Both rulings together ensure that the system functions even in the most complex configurations of wives, deaths, and creditor relationships.

## Motar Dinar Rules

### Two Wives Equal

The Mishnah now articulates the foundational mechanics of *ketubat benin dichrin* in the standard two-wife case. A man was married to two wives, both of whom predeceased him. He then died. The orphans — Rachel's sons and Leah's sons — each seek to extract their mother's *ketubah* from the estate as special heirs. If the estate contains exactly the combined total of the two *ketubah* amounts and nothing more, the rule of *ketubat benin dichrin* cannot operate. There is no *motar dinar* — no leftover amount for the ordinary laws of inheritance to govern. In such a case, *cholkin b'shaveh* — they divide the estate equally, without any special priority. The desire to activate *ketubat benin dichrin* is understandable: the children with the larger *ketubah*, or even those with fewer siblings (since a single son with a ten-thousand-dollar *ketubah* walks away with the full amount while ten sons sharing the same *ketubah* receive a thousand each), have a strong incentive to establish the *motar*. But absent that extra amount, no special extraction is permitted.

### With Extra Money

If, however, there is a *motar dinar* — a single *dinar*'s worth of value beyond the combined *ketubah* totals — then *elu notlin kesubas iman v'elu notlin kesubas iman*: each set of children extracts their mother's *ketubah* amount, and the residual estate, however modest, is then divided equally by the ordinary rules of inheritance. The extra *dinar* is what makes the entire mechanism operable.

### No Self-Assessment

A natural temptation arises: if the estate falls slightly short of having a *motar dinar*, can the heirs who want *ketubat benin dichrin* to apply simply declare that they will add money to the estate to create the needed surplus? The Mishnah answers emphatically: *ein shomin lahem* — we do not listen to them. Nor may they inflate the assessed value of estate property, claiming that a piece of land worth eight thousand dollars should be counted as ten thousand, thereby appearing to leave a surplus. The assessment of what the estate is actually worth is not in the hands of the interested heirs. *Ella shamin es hanechasim beit din* — rather, the court assesses the value of the assets. The *beit din* conducts an objective appraisal, and the legal outcome follows from that appraisal alone.

### What Counts

The Mishnah also addresses the question of anticipated but not yet received funds. If there is money that should be coming to the estate — whether because the estate expects to receive an inheritance from someone who has not yet died, or because there are outstanding receivables not yet collected — this does not count toward the *motar dinar*. *Einan kemuchzak* — such funds are not considered within the estate's current control. Only assets actually in hand at the time of assessment establish whether the *motar dinar* requirement has been met.

### R. Shimon Land Only

Rabbi Shimon adds a further stringency. Even if there is leftover money beyond the *ketubah* amounts, that money counts toward the *motar dinar* only if it consists of land — *karka*. Movable property, *metaltelin* — even valuable cattle or other chattel — does not satisfy the requirement. As Rabbi Shimon formulates it: *afilu yesh sham nechasim she-ein lahem achrayut einam klum* — even if there is property that carries no lien obligation (meaning it is technically free and clear but happens to be chattel), it counts for nothing. The extra *dinar* must be in real property, *nechasim she-yesh lahen achrayut*, encumberable land, in an amount exceeding the two *ketubah* obligations. The symbolic significance of land as the carrier of genuine ancestral inheritance — rooted in the biblical conception of the estate as a portion in the land — underpins Rabbi Shimon's position. The *Tanna Kamma*, by contrast, accepts chattel as a qualifying *motar*. The Gemara earlier sought to read the dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Shimon as bearing on exactly this question — whether *karka* alone qualifies or whether *metaltelin* suffices — and the Mishnah now confirms that Rabbi Shimon indeed holds the more stringent position.

### Unequal Amounts

A *baraita* supplements the Mishnah with the case of unequal *ketubah* amounts: one wife's *ketubah* is a thousand *zuz*, the other's is five hundred. The children of the wife with the larger *ketubah* have a particularly strong incentive to activate *ketubat benin dichrin*. The rule, however, is identical: if there is a *motar dinar* — in this case, any amount beyond fifteen hundred and one *zuz* — each set of children extracts their mother's *ketubah* amount and the remainder is divided equally. If not, they divide the total estate equally. The *baraita* adds no new principle; it simply confirms that the mechanics apply regardless of whether the two *ketubah* amounts are equal or disparate.

## Property Appreciation

### Depreciation Case

The Gemara turns to cases in which the value of the estate changes between the time of the husband's death and the time of collection. The straightforward case is depreciation: the estate was worth fifteen hundred and one *zuz* at the moment of death — sufficient to satisfy both *ketubah* amounts with a *motar dinar* to spare — but by the time the heirs come to collect, the value has fallen to fourteen hundred. The Gemara treats this as self-evident: *kevar zachu bahen yorshin* — the heirs already acquired their rights at the moment of death. The status of the estate is determined as of the date of death, and a subsequent decline in value does not retroactively strip the children of their entitlement. The *motar dinar* requirement was satisfied when it needed to be satisfied.

### Bar Tsartsur Case

More complex is the reverse: the property of the house of Bar Tsartsur presented the mirror image — at the time of death the estate was insufficient (*mu'atin*), but afterward it increased in value (*v'nisrabu*). The heirs came before Rav Ami seeking a ruling on whether the appreciation should trigger the application of *ketubat benin dichrin*.

### Rav Ami Opinion

Rav Ami did not issue a clear ruling. Instead, he instructed the parties to negotiate among themselves — specifically suggesting to those with the smaller *ketubah* that they try to reach a settlement (*zilu paysuhu*) with those who held the larger claim. The implication of his direction was that the children with the larger *ketubah* might have the stronger argument in principle, since the property now had sufficient value. When the parties refused to negotiate, Rav Ami threatened rabbinic sanctions — the painful but non-physical sanction of excommunication — to compel a settlement. The inability to reach resolution led the matter to be referred to Rav Nachman.

### Rav Nachman Ruling

Rav Nachman ruled with apparent symmetry: just as when the estate begins large and depreciates — *zachu bahen yorshin*, the special heirs already acquired their rights — so too when it begins small and appreciates — *zachu bahen yorshin*, the heirs acquire their rights. But this formulation raises an immediate question: *which* heirs? In the depreciation case, the answer is clear — the heirs who needed the *motar dinar* already had it, and they keep their entitlement despite the later decrease. But in the appreciation case, if you say *zachu bahen yorshin*, it is ambiguous whether you mean the heirs who were being excluded (because at death there was no *motar*) now gain the right due to the appreciation, or whether you mean something else entirely.

### Rashi/Tosafot

Rashi and Tosafot dispute the import of Rav Nachman's ruling. Rashi understands it to mean that one always assesses as of the date of death. In the appreciation case, because there was no *motar dinar* at death, the heirs of the smaller *ketubah* wind up benefiting — the estate is divided equally, which favors them, and the later appreciation simply increases what each party receives from the equal division. The determining moment is fixed at death. Tosafot reads Rav Nachman's symmetrical language differently: just as in the depreciation case the rule of *ketubat benin dichrin* is applied because the *motar* existed at death, so in the appreciation case *ketubat benin dichrin* is also applied because the *motar* now exists at the time of collection. On Tosafot's reading, the principle is that one always resolves ambiguity in favor of applying *ketubat benin dichrin* — whether the qualifying *motar* appeared at death or at distribution. This disagreement reflects a fundamental question: is the determining moment fixed (Rashi) or does the system prefer, at any available snapshot in time, to activate the special inheritance mechanism (Tosafot)?

## Two Houses Case

### Setup

The final section of the *daf* introduces a case that initially appears to involve the rules of creditor collection but turns out to implicate broader principles about negotiation rights and asset valuation. A man owed a thousand *zuz* to a creditor. He had two houses — each worth five hundred — and sold them both to Michael for five hundred each. The seller then, presumably, dissipated the proceeds without paying his debt. The creditor now pursues Michael as the buyer of the encumbered properties. Michael acquired the houses subject to the seller's lien, so the creditor may collect from Michael's property.

### Michael's Offer

The creditor moved to seize the first house, and Michael saw that he was about to lose the second house as well. Michael approached the creditor with a thousand *zuz* and made the following proposal: I am prepared to tell you that the first house you already seized is worth a thousand *zuz* — that is, your full debt is satisfied. If you accept that valuation, consider your debt paid and leave me the second house. If you reject that valuation, then here — take the thousand *zuz* in cash and return the first house to me. Michael's gambit is elegant: he offers the creditor either a favorable valuation of one house covering the entire debt, or a cash payment of the full thousand *zuz*. Either way, Michael is trying to avoid losing both houses. What he is doing is effectively asserting his own assessment of the first house's value — claiming it is worth the full thousand — and daring the creditor to disagree.

### Mishnah Parallel

Rami bar Chama raised the possibility that this is analogous to the Mishnah's case of orphans who sought to inflate the value of estate property in order to create a *motar dinar*: *im amru yetomim harenu ma'alim al nichsei avinu yafeh dinar* — if the orphans say we will assess our father's property at a higher value to generate the required surplus — the Mishnah says we do not listen to them. Is Michael's self-serving valuation similarly inadmissible?

### Rava Distinction

Rava rejected the analogy. *Mi dami?* — are the cases really comparable? *Hatam ika pseida l'yatmi* — in the Mishnah's case, when one set of orphans inflates the property value, the other set of orphans suffers a real loss, because the inflated valuation distorts the equal division they are entitled to. *Hacha mai pseida?* — but here, what loss does the creditor actually suffer? *Alfa yahiv v'alfa shakil* — he put in a claim for a thousand and he is going to collect a thousand. His debt is fully discharged either way. If the creditor suffers no loss from Michael's proposed valuation, the Mishnah's prohibition — designed to protect parties who would be harmed by self-serving assessments — may not apply. Michael may well have the right to negotiate on precisely these terms.

### Mishnah Parallel

The Gemara leaves open the broader question of whether a purchaser of encumbered property generally has the right to pay the creditor in cash rather than surrendering the property — Tosafot notes and analyzes this as a potentially independent principle — but the immediate ruling flows from Rava's distinction: because the creditor is made whole either way, Michael's negotiating posture is not automatically disqualified by the Mishnah's rule against self-interested valuation.

### Rava Distinction

The Gemara thus proceeds on the assumption that Michael's offer is at least potentially legitimate, and the case is resolved in the creditor's favor in the following sense: the creditor, having now accepted one house in satisfaction of his full debt (whether because he agreed to the thousand-*zuz* valuation or because Michael paid cash and reclaimed the first house, retaining the second), has collected his thousand *zuz*.

### Ravina/Rav Ashi

A final question arises regarding Michael's claim against the original seller. Michael purchased two houses for five hundred each. One was seized by the creditor. Michael is now out one house and is entitled to compensation from the seller who sold him encumbered property. But how much? Ravina says Michael is entitled to a thousand *zuz* — because the house effectively discharged a thousand-*zuz* debt, and that is the measure of what it was worth in the transaction. Rav Ashi says five hundred *zuz* — because the house's actual purchase price was five hundred, the underlying value is five hundred, and Michael's loss is five hundred regardless of the debt it happened to satisfy. The *halacha* follows Rav Ashi: *hilchata b'chamesh me'ah* — Michael recovers only five hundred from the seller. The creditor received his full thousand, Michael retained one house and was compensated five hundred for the one he lost, and the seller — who generated this entire predicament by selling encumbered property and failing to pay his debt — bears the consequences of the shortfall.
