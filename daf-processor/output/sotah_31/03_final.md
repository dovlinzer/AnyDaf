# Sotah 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening Mishnah (II)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁקִּינֵּא לְאִשְׁתּוֹ וְנִסְתְּרָה, אֲפִילּוּ שָׁמַע מֵעוֹף הַפּוֹרֵחַ — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who warned his wife** not to seclude herself with a particular man **and she** subsequently **secluded herself** with the man she was warned about, **even if he heard** about it **from a flying bird,** or any other source whatsoever, **he must divorce** his wife. However, he must still **grant her** the money accorded to her by her **marriage contract** because there is no actual proof of her seclusion with the man in question. This is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer,** who, as quoted in the first mishna of the tractate (2a), holds that there is no necessity for witnesses to testify with regard to the seclusion, and the woman becomes forbidden to her husband even in the absence of witnesses, by the husband's word alone.

### R. Eliezer: Bird Claim

The daf begins two-thirds of the way down the page, at the opening of a new *perek*. The *mishnah* returns to the very first *mishnah* of the *masechta*: the case of *mi she-kina le-ishto ve-nistara* — a man who warned his wife not to go into seclusion and she did so anyway. The question before us is: what quality of proof is required to establish that she went into seclusion, so as to permit or compel the continuation of the *sotah* process?

Rabbi Eliezer's position, already stated in the opening *mishnah*, is stated here in its most radical form: *afilu shama mi-of pore'ach* — even if one merely heard the claim of a passing bird. No matter how tenuous the assertion, any claim that she went into seclusion suffices as the basis for the *sotah* process. This is, by any measure, a strikingly unfair standard for the woman.

The consequence the *mishnah* states is *yotzi ve-yiten ketubah* — he divorces her and pays the *ketubah*. This phrasing is initially puzzling: one might have expected it to say that he must make her drink the *sotah* water, not that he must pay the *ketubah*. Rashi explains the logic: once the *sotah* process is triggered, she is forbidden to him. He has two options — either she undergoes the drinking, or, if neither party wishes to pursue that path, he divorces her and pays the *ketubah*. If she refuses to drink and the process is abandoned, she leaves without the *ketubah*. The *mishnah* states the case from the perspective of the husband who chooses not to proceed with the drinking.

### R. Yehoshua: Rumors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיִּשְׂאוּ וְיִתְּנוּ בָּהּ מוֹזְרוֹת בַּלְּבָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua** disagrees, as he did in the mishna (2a), and **says:** He does not divorce his wife in the absence of witnesses **until** the gossiping women who sit and **spin** thread **by** the **light of the moon** begin to **discuss her** behavior, as they share the gossip of the town. The Gemara earlier (6b) taught that a woman whose infidelity became subject to this public discussion can no longer be tested by the bitter water of a *sota*. Consequently, she must get divorced.

Rabbi Yehoshua requires something more substantial. His formulation is *ad she-ye-hayu nos'ot ve-notnot bah nashim ba-matyah ba-levanah* — until the women who sit and spin by moonlight begin to gossip about her. This somewhat colorful idiom conveys the requirement that there be actual social rumor circulating about her conduct.

Rabbi Yehoshua's standard, understood correctly, is that one needs two proper witnesses that she went into seclusion. The case of gossip here is not a *halacha* about rendering her forbidden to her husband as a *sotah*; there are no witnesses to seclusion at all. Rather, the issue is with the phrase *tehorah hi* — she is pure — which appears in connection with the *sotah* water. Once her reputation has been publicly tarnished, the waters will not be effective in any case. So the option of drinking is no longer on the table.

At the same time, since there are no formal witnesses to seclusion, she has not been rendered *teme'ah* as a *sotah*. The husband could theoretically remain married to her — there is merely this cloud of rumor. But if he chooses to divorce her because of this reputation, he must pay the *ketubah*. The result is an asymmetry in how *yotzi ve-yiten ketubah* functions in each case: for Rabbi Eliezer, it implies she *is* a *sotah* and may be compelled to drink; for Rabbi Yehoshua, she is not a *sotah*, but the husband who wishes to leave must nonetheless pay. This is, as Rashi reads the *mishnah*, a difficult but defensible reading.

## One Witness After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עֵד אֶחָד ״אֲנִי רְאִיתִיהָ שֶׁנִּטְמֵאת״ — לֹא הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ עֶבֶד אֲפִילּוּ שִׁפְחָה — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ נֶאֱמָנִין אַף לְפוֹסְלָהּ מִכְּתוּבָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues to list various possible testimonies concerning such acts of seclusion. If **one witness said: I saw that she became defiled** during her seclusion by engaging in sexual intercourse with that other man, **she does not drink** the bitter water, but rather, he divorces her immediately. **And furthermore, even** if the one who testified was **a slave or a maidservant,** neither of whom is generally regarded as a valid witness, **they are deemed credible** to testify to the wife's adultery **even to** the extent that their testimony **disqualifies her from** receiving **her marriage contract** and prevents her from drinking the bitter water.

### Slave/Maidservant Testimony

Assuming the standard of Rabbi Yehoshua — two witnesses to seclusion — the *mishnah* now addresses the consequences of testimony regarding the act of adultery itself. The *halacha* is that once the predicate of warning and seclusion has been established, a single witness who comes and says *ani ra'iti she-nitme'ah* — "I saw that she became *teme'ah*," that is, that she committed adultery within that seclusion — is believed. The consequence is not execution, for which two *kosher* witnesses would be required, but rather that she may not drink the *sotah* water; she is considered a proved adulteress, and upon divorce she forfeits her *ketubah*.

The *mishnah* extends this further: *afilu eved afilu shifchah harei elu ne'emanim* — even a male or female slave would be believed for this purpose. The one witness who testifies to the act of adultery need not be a *kosher* witness in the formal sense. The consequence of such testimony is that she loses her *ketubah*.

### Jealous Relatives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמוֹתָהּ וּבַת חֲמוֹתָהּ וְצָרָתָהּ וִיבִמְתָּהּ וּבַת בַּעְלָהּ — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ נֶאֱמָנוֹת. וְלֹא לְפוֹסְלָהּ מִכְּתוּבָּתָהּ, אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁתֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues by listing women whose testimony is only partially accepted concerning this matter: **Her mother-in-law, and her mother in-law's daughter, and her rival wife,** i.e., a second wife of the husband, **and her *yevama*,** i.e., her husband's brother's wife, **and her husband's daughter,** all of whom are generally not deemed credible if they say anything incriminating pertaining to this woman due to the tumultuous relationships these women often have. **They are** all **deemed credible** to testify concerning the woman's defilement while in seclusion, **but** are **not** deemed credible **to** the extent that their testimony will **disqualify her from** receiving **her marriage contract; rather,** it is deemed credible to the extent that she will **not drink** of the bitter water of a *sota*.

The *mishnah* then lists five categories of women who are presumed to harbor enmity toward the wife: the mother-in-law (*chamot*), the mother-in-law's daughter, the co-wife (*tzarat*), the widowed sister-in-law who stands to be subject to *yibum* with the husband, and the husband's daughter from another wife. These women all have identifiable personal interest in the wife's downfall and their credibility is therefore diminished.

Nevertheless, the *mishnah* rules *harei elu ne'emanos* — they are believed — but with a qualification: *ve-lo le-fosla mi-ketubatah ella shelo tishteh*. Their testimony is sufficient to stop the *sotah* drinking process — we treat their claim as sufficiently credible that we will not proceed with the water — but it is *not* sufficient to strip the wife of her *ketubah*. The husband must divorce her and pay the *ketubah*. So she loses the option of drinking and clearing her name, but she retains her financial protection.

## Opening Mishnah (II)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהָיָה בַּדִּין: וּמָה אִם עֵדוּת רִאשׁוֹנָה, שֶׁאֵין אוֹסַרְתָּהּ אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם — אֵינָהּ מִתְקַיֶּימֶת בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁנַיִם, עֵדוּת אַחֲרוֹנָה, שֶׁאוֹסַרְתָּהּ אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא תִּתְקַיֵּים בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁנַיִם?
>
> **Translation:** This ruling allowing one witness's testimony with regard to defilement needs to be stated, **as, by right,** it **should** not **have been** deemed credible based on the following *a fortiori* inference: **And just as if** with regard to **the first testimony** concerning seclusion, **which does not forbid her** with **an irrevocable prohibition,** as the woman can be found innocent permitting her again to her husband by drinking the bitter water, **is not established with fewer than two** witnesses, since according to the mishna the testimony of seclusion requires two witnesses, then with regard to **the final testimony** concerning defilement, **which forbids her** to her husband with **an irrevocable prohibition, is it not logical that it** should also **not be established with fewer than two** witnesses?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעֵד אֵין בָּהּ״, כׇּל עֵדוּת שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, to counter this derivation, **the verse states: "And there be no witness against her"** (Numbers 5:13), teaching that **any** testimony with regard to defilement **that there is against her** is sufficient, and two witnesses are not required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר לְעֵדוּת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה מֵעַתָּה: וּמָה אִם עֵדוּת אַחֲרוֹנָה שֶׁאוֹסַרְתָּהּ אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם — הֲרֵי הִיא מִתְקַיֶּימֶת בְּעֵד אֶחָד, עֵדוּת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁאֵין אוֹסַרְתָּהּ אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתִּתְקַיֵּים בְּעֵד אֶחָד?
>
> **Translation:** The Mishnah asks: **And from now** that it is established that one witness suffices to testify with regard to defilement, **an *a fortiori*** inference can be made **with regard to the first testimony** of seclusion: **And just as if** concerning **the final testimony** of defilement, **which forbids her** with **an irrevocable prohibition,** yet it **is established by one witness,** then with regard to **the first testimony, which does not forbid her** with **an irrevocable prohibition, is it not logical that it should be established with** only **one witness?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי מָצָא בָהּ עֶרְוַת דָּבָר״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״עַל פִּי שְׁנֵי עֵדִים ... יָקוּם דָּבָר״, מָה לְהַלָּן עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם — אַף כָּאן פִּי שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, to counter this derivation, **the verse states:** "When a man takes a wife, and marries her, and it comes to pass, if she finds no favor in his eyes, **because he has found some unseemly matter [*davar*] in her"** (Deuteronomy 24:1), **and there,** in the laws concerning monetary matters, **it states: "At the mouth of two witnesses,** or at the mouth of three witnesses, **shall a matter [*davar*] be established"** (Deuteronomy 19:15), teaching that **just as** the "matter" stated **there** is established **"at the mouth of two witnesses," so too, here** the "matter" of her seclusion must be established **"at the mouth of two** witnesses."

### Kal vaChomer Logic

The *mishnah* then raises the question of how we know that the *eid echad* is believed for the testimony of adultery at all. One might have constructed a *kal va-chomer* that would have led to the opposite conclusion. The reasoning would proceed: the first testimony — that she went into seclusion — is a less severe form of evidence, since even after seclusion is established she still has the option of the *sotah* water to clear her name. Yet even for this less consequential testimony, the Torah requires two witnesses. All the more so, then, for the second testimony — the one that actually seals her fate and makes her permanently forbidden — should we not require two witnesses?

This is a compelling argument. The *mishnah* answers by citing the verse *ve-eid ein bah* — there is no witness against her, implying that even one witness suffices. The *mishnah* reads this as: if there *is* even one witness against her, there is a problem; she may not drink.

The *mishnah* then runs the *kal va-chomer* in the opposite direction. Now that we have established from this verse that one witness suffices to seal her fate in the more consequential second testimony, we might reverse the logic: *kal va-chomer*, the first testimony about seclusion, which is less consequential because the *sotah* water remains available, should also be achievable with one witness. That would unravel the entire structure, eliminating the requirement for two witnesses to seclusion.

To block this reversal, the *mishnah* cites the verse *ki matza bah ervat davar* — when the husband finds "a matter of nakedness" regarding her, with the word *davar* (*matter*) linked by *gezeirah shavah* to the verse *al pi shenayim eidim yakum davar* — a matter is established by two witnesses. From this parallel, the *mishnah* derives that the seclusion testimony requires two witnesses: *mah lehavalahn al pi shenayim af kan al pi shenayim*.

This *gezeirah shavah* is somewhat surprising in context, since the verse *ki matza bah ervat davar* appears in the laws of *get* and not in the *sotah* passage itself. The *mishnah* reads it as referring to the husband's suspicion, which is precisely the situation of seclusion. The *Gemara*, however, will read both the *derashot* and this *pasuk* somewhat differently.

The *Gemara* interjects: why do we need this *gezeirah shavah* from the word *davar*? There is a simpler derivation. The verse *ve-eid ein bah* that grants believability to the single witness uses the word *bah* — "regarding her." This word *bah* is limiting: it is specifically in regard to the act of adultery itself that a single witness is believed. For *kinuy* and *stirah* — the warning and seclusion stages — the verse offers no such license, and those require two. The *Gemara* concludes *hachi nami ka'amar* — yes, that is indeed what the *mishnah* means to convey: *bah* — regarding the act itself one witness is believed, *ve-lo be-kinuy ve-lo be-stirah*.

A further question arises: once we know that in the *sotah* context one witness is believed for the act of adultery when building on the seclusion, might one extend this to suggest that one witness would always be believed to accuse a woman of adultery even without the prior structure of warning and seclusion? The answer comes from the same *gezeirah shavah*: *ne'emar kan davar ve-ne'emar lehavalahn davar* — the word *davar* appears here and in the verse about two witnesses, establishing that outside the specific *sotah* framework, testimony of adultery requires two. The *sotah* context is the exception; the general rule remains two *kosher* witnesses.

## Default Two Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַאי תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כִּי מָצָא בָהּ עֶרְוַת דָּבָר״,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara questions why the mishna proves the need for two witnesses to testify about the seclusion based upon the verbal analogy of "matter" and "matter," if there is an explicit source in the Torah stating that two witnesses are required. **This** reason is given by the mishna: **The verse states:** "When a man takes a wife, and marries her, and it comes to pass, if she finds no favor in his eyes, **because he has found some unseemly matter [*davar*] in her"** (Deuteronomy 24:1), which through a verbal analogy based on the word "*davar*" teaches the need for two witnesses, seems to be superfluous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בָּהּ״ — בָּהּ וְלֹא בְּקִינּוּי, בָּהּ וְלֹא בִּסְתִירָה מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** As, the mishna **should have** said in its place: The **verse states:** "She was defiled secretly and there was no witness [*ed*] **against her [*bah*]"** (Numbers 5:13), which is explained to mean there were not two witnesses, but only one, who testified concerning her defilement. One can infer from the term "*bah*," which could also be understood as: **With regard to it,** that in this matter of defilement one witness suffices, **but not with regard to** the **warning.** Additionally, one can infer: **With regard to it, but not with regard to** the **seclusion.** Therefore, there must be two witnesses to testify about both the warning and seclusion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי קָאָמַר: תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בָּהּ״ — בָּהּ וְלֹא בְּקִינּוּי, בָּהּ וְלֹא בִּסְתִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That is also what** the *tanna* of the mishna **is saying.** The source for the *halakha* that there is a requirement for two witnesses to testify about the seclusion is that **the verse states: With regard to it,** that in this matter of defilement one witness suffices, **but not with regard to** the **warning. With regard to it, but not with regard to** the **seclusion.**

### No Two Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטוּמְאָה בְּעָלְמָא בְּלֹא קִינּוּי וּבְלֹא סְתִירָה, דְּלָא מְהֵימַן עֵד אֶחָד מְנָלַן? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״דָּבָר״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״דָּבָר״. מָה לְהַלָּן בִּשְׁנֵי עֵדִים — אַף כָּאן בִּשְׁנֵי עֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to explain the mishna: **And** concerning **an ordinary** accusation of **defilement without a** previous **warning and without** an act of **seclusion** with another man, **from where do we** derive that **a single witness is not deemed credible?** It is for this *halakha* that the mishna cites the verbal analogy: **It is stated here:** "Because he has found some unseemly **matter [*davar*]** in her" (Deuteronomy 24:1), **and it is stated there** concerning monetary matters: "At the mouth of two witnesses, or at the mouth of three witnesses, shall **a matter [*davar*]** be established" (Deuteronomy 19:15). **Just as** the "matter" stated **there** with regard to monetary matters is clarified specifically **by** the testimony of **two witnesses, so too, here** with regard to adultery the "matter" of her defilement must be established **by** the testimony of at least **two witnesses.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* *minani mili* — from where do we know that one witness is believed for the act of adultery in the *sotah* case?
>
> **Translation:**

The *Gemara* now turns to the section of the *mishnah* dealing with one witness against one witness, where the *mishnah* appears to say they neutralize one another and the drinking proceeds. Before engaging with that debate, the *Gemara* establishes the foundational principle: *minani mili* — from where do we know that one witness is believed for the act of adultery in the *sotah* case?

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *ve-eid ein bah bi-shnayim ha-katuv medaber* — when the verse says "there is no witness against her," the Torah is speaking about two witnesses. That is, "no witness" means no *two* witnesses. The verse is understood as: there are no two witnesses against her, only one — and she was not raped — in which case she is forbidden and the drinking is halted.

This reading may seem counterintuitive. The plain reading of the *mishnah* seemed to say that "no witness" permits drinking, and "one witness" prohibits it. The *Gemara* instead understands the verse as: no two witnesses — only one — and that one witness suffices to stop the process.

### Eid = Two Default

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בִּשְׁנַיִם, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאֶחָד — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא יָקוּם עֵד אֶחָד בְּאִישׁ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues and asks: Do **you say** that the verse refers **only** to a case where there was not **even one** witness to the act of sexual intercourse, as the singular usage of the word *ed* would seem to indicate? The *baraita* now proves that the singular usage notwithstanding, elsewhere the word *ed* is used to indicate two witnesses, as **the verse states: "One witness [*ed*] shall not rise up against a man** for any iniquity or any sin that he sins; at the mouth of two witnesses, or at the mouth of three witnesses, shall a matter be established" (Deuteronomy 19:15).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לֹא יָקוּם עֵד״, אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהוּא אֶחָד, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֶחָד״ — זֶה בָּנָה אָב: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״עֵד״ — הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁנַיִם, עַד שֶׁיִּפְרוֹט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב ״אֶחָד״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* infers a general principle from this verse by asking: **By inference from that** which **is stated** in the verse, even with the omission of the word "one": **"A witness shall not rise up** against a man" (Deuteronomy 19:15), **do I not know that it is** referring to **one** witness, as the verse is written in the singular form? **What** is the meaning when **the verse states** explicitly: **"One** witness," being that it is obviously referring to only one witness? The *baraita* answers: **This established a paradigm** for the principle that **every place** where the word **"witness [*ed*]" is stated** in the Torah without specifying a number, **there are two** witnesses **here, until the verse specifies** that it is referring to only one witness, by writing the word **"one."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: תְּרֵי לֵית בַּהּ אֶלָּא חַד, ״וְהִיא לֹא נִתְפָּשָׂה״ — אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* returns to discuss the verse concerning a *sota*. **And the Merciful One states:** "There was no witness [*ed*] against her," which therefore means that: **There are not two** witnesses to the sexual intercourse that could testify **with regard to her, rather** there is only **one** witness. The *baraita* completes its interpretation: Further in the verse it states: **"And she was not taken in the act"** (Numbers 5:13), indicating that the verse is referring to a case in which it is known that the woman had not been raped. This knowledge is based on the testimony of only one witness, as the verse had already stated that there were not two witnesses, and since one witness saw her willingly engage in sexual intercourse with another man, **she is forbidden** to her husband.

How do we know that the unqualified word *eid* means two? The *Gemara* derives this from the verse *lo yakum eid echad be-ish le-chol avon* — one witness may not rise up against a person for any offense. The *Gemara* asks: since *eid* is already singular in form, why does the verse need to specify *echad* — one? It must be that the word *eid* by itself ordinarily denotes two witnesses, and the qualifier *echad* is needed to indicate that only one is meant.

This yields a general *klal*: *kol makom she-ne'emar eid harei kan shnayim ad she-yafrit lecha ha-katuv echad* — wherever the word *eid* appears, it means two witnesses, unless the text explicitly specifies *echad* to indicate a single one. This is a far-reaching principle that recurs throughout the *Gemara* in matters of testimony.

With this principle established, the *Gemara* can read *ve-eid ein bah* as referring to the absence of two witnesses: the Torah is saying there are no two witnesses against her, there is only one — and it is that one witness, acting in the context of established seclusion, who is believed to render her forbidden.

## Competing Testimonies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵד אוֹמֵר ״נִטְמֵאת״ וְעֵד אוֹמֵר ״לֹא נִטְמֵאת״, אִשָּׁה אוֹמֶרֶת ״נִטְמֵאת״ וְאִשָּׁה אוֹמֶרֶת ״לֹא נִטְמֵאת״ — הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna discusses the *halakha* in a case where two single witnesses contradict each other concerning her defilement. If one **witness says: She was defiled, and** another **witness says: She was not defiled,** or similarly in the case of those normally disqualified from bearing witness, if one **woman says: She was defiled, and** another **woman says: She was not defiled, she would drink** the bitter water of a *sota*, due to the uncertainty engendered by the contradictory testimonies.

### Ulla: Cannot Neutralize

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא עֵד אֶחָד מְהֵימַן, אִידַּךְ הֵיכִי מָצֵי מַכְחֵישׁ לֵיהּ? וְהָא אָמַר עוּלָּא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶאֱמִינָה תּוֹרָה עֵד אֶחָד, הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל אֶחָד בִּמְקוֹם שְׁנַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But since** the *baraita* taught **that by Torah law one witness is deemed credible** to state that the woman is defiled, **how can the other** witness who denies the wife's infidelity **refute him** with his conflicting testimony and enable the woman to drink the bitter water instead of becoming forbidden outright to her husband? **But doesn't Ulla say: Wherever the Torah relies on one witness, there is** the equivalent of the testimony of **two** witnesses **here.** Consequently, the testimony of the contradicting witness should be insignificant, as **the statement of one** witness **has no** standing **in a place where** it is contradicted by **two** witnesses, for the witness testifying about her infidelity is deemed credible as if two witnesses had testified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר עוּלָּא, תְּנִי: לֹא הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: לֹא הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה. וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא אָמַר: הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Ulla said: Teach** the mishna in the following way: If one witness says: She was defiled, and another witness says: She was not defiled, **she would not drink** the bitter water. **And likewise, Rabbi Yitzḥak said: She would not drink. But Rabbi Ḥiyya says** in line with the standard text of the mishna: **She would drink** the bitter water even in the case of a contradiction between two single witnesses with regard to her infidelity.

With the foundational principle established, the *Gemara* turns to the *mishnah*'s statement that one witness saying she became *teme'ah* against one witness saying she did not would neutralize one another, allowing the drinking to proceed. Rashi notes that the witness who says *lo nitme'ah* is not claiming to have observed the room and seen that nothing happened; rather, he is saying something more modest — "your observation is not reliable" or "I was with you and I know you could not have seen what you claim." It is a contradiction of credibility, not an affirmative counterclaim.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges the premise that such a contradiction could have any force at all. The first witness — the one who says she became *teme'ah* — is not an ordinary witness. He comes equipped with the prior testimony of warning and seclusion, which form a *raglayim la-davar*, a factual foundation for the suspicion. The Torah, given this foundation, has granted this witness extraordinary weight, treating his single testimony as authoritative. How, then, can a single witness offering a mere contradiction — without any comparable foundation — neutralize this specially-empowered testimony?

This is Ulla's principle: *kol makom she-min ha-Torah eid echad harei kan shnayim* — wherever the Torah accords special believability to a single witness, that witness effectively counts as two. And *ein dvaro shel echad bi-makom shnayim* — the word of an ordinary single witness has no weight against two. If the specially-empowered witness counts as two, the ordinary contradiction counts as one, and one cannot override two. It follows that the contradiction is simply ineffective, and we would consider her *teme'ah*.

Ulla therefore proposes a different *girsa* of the *mishnah*: where the *mishnah* reads *hayetah shotah* — she would drink — in the case of one against one, Ulla reads *lo hayetah shotah* — she would *not* drink. The one who says she became *teme'ah* wins, and the process is stopped. Rabbi Yitzchak adopts the same reading.

### R. Chiya: Simultaneous

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא קַשְׁיָא דְּעוּלָּא. לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּבַת אַחַת. כָּאן בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **for Rabbi Ḥiyya,** how will he respond to the same **difficulty** due to the teaching **of Ulla,** who explained that when a single witness is deemed credible he is considered as two and cannot be contradicted by a single witness to the contrary? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult: Here,** the mishna is discussing a case where both witnesses arrived in court and testified **simultaneously,** thereby canceling each other out. But **there,** Ulla's principle, that whenever a single witness's testimony is accepted it is considered as two and cannot be contradicted by a single witness testifying to the contrary, is referring to a case **in which** the two conflicting witnesses came to court and testified **one after the other.**

Rabbi Chiya, however, retains the original text of the *mishnah*: in the case of one against one, *hayetah shotah* — she would drink. The two testimonies neutralize one another, and the process continues. The *Gemara* notes that Ulla's argument — based on the specially-granted weight of the first witness — poses a genuine difficulty for Rabbi Chiya. It is not merely that Rabbi Chiya disagrees with Ulla's opinion; Ulla's position has compelling logical force, grounded in the structure of the Torah's grant of special believability.

### Timing: Simultaneous vs. Sequential

The *Gemara* resolves this tension by distinguishing between the timing of the testimonies. *Kan be-vat achas, kan be-zeh achar zeh* — in one case the witnesses arrive simultaneously, in the other they arrive sequentially. Rashi specifies that "simultaneously" means within *toch kedei dibbur* — the brief interval of a normal utterance.

The logic of this distinction is as follows. If both witnesses arrive at the same time, the court has not yet accorded full weight to the first witness's claim. Two people step forward together; one says she became *teme'ah* and the other immediately disputes this. Since the challenge is present from the outset, the testimony of the first has never been fully established, and the situation remains unresolved — she drinks. This is the case Rabbi Chiya is addressing, and it does not contradict Ulla's principle, because Ulla's principle applies once the testimony has been accepted.

If, however, the first witness testified and the court recognized his testimony — if that testimony has been received and accorded its special weight — then a witness who arrives afterward cannot dislodge it. The first testimony, once accepted, is treated as two witnesses, and nothing that comes subsequently can override it.

## Two-Against-One Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״נִטְמֵאת״ וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״לֹא נִטְמֵאת״ — הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה. שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״נִטְמֵאת״ וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״לֹא נִטְמֵאת״ — לֹא הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if **one** witness **says: She was defiled, and two** witnesses **say: She was not defiled, she would drink** the bitter water. However, if **two** would **say: She was defiled, and one says: She was not defiled,** the testimony of the two witnesses is accepted and **she would not drink** the bitter water, and the husband must divorce her.

### 1 vs 2: Neutralizes

The *Gemara* now turns to the *mishnah*'s next case: one says she became *teme'ah* and two say she did not — *hayetah shotah*, she would drink. The two neutralize the one, and the process continues. This makes immediate sense in light of everything established: if even a single opposing witness can neutralize the one who says *teme'ah* (when they arrive simultaneously), two opposing witnesses can certainly do so.

This is also why Rashi insists that *lo nitme'ah* does not mean the witnesses actively observed innocence; if two witnesses had positively testified that she did not commit adultery, the question would be entirely settled and she obviously would not drink. Their testimony is a contradiction of the first witness's claim, not an affirmative attestation of innocence.

### 2 vs 1: Two Win

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: עֵד אוֹמֵר ״נִטְמֵאת״ וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״לֹא נִטְמֵאת״ — הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה. הָא חַד וְחַד — לֹא הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא!
>
> **Translation:** But **we learned** in the mishna above that if **one witness says: She was defiled, and two say: She was not defiled, she would drink** the bitter water. The Gemara deduces from here: The implications of this statement are that the woman drinks only because the testimony of the incriminating witness was contradicted by two witnesses, **but** if there was **one** witness saying she was defiled **and** only **one** witness saying the opposite, **she would not drink;** this is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥiyya.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי חִיָּיא: וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״נִטְמֵאת״ וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״לֹא נִטְמֵאת״ — לֹא הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה. הָא חַד וְחַד — הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Ḥiyya** could have **said to you: And according to your reasoning** that she would not drink, **say the latter clause** of the mishna, which states: If **two** witnesses **say: She was defiled, and one says: She was not defiled,** the testimony of the two witnesses is accepted and **she would not drink** the bitter water. **But** you should deduce from here that if there was **one** witness testifying to her defilement **and one** witness stating the opposite, **she would drink** the bitter water. This inference is in line with Rabbi Ḥiyya's explanation, and contradicts the inference from the previous clause.

The final case in the *mishnah*: two say she became *teme'ah* and one says she did not — *lo hayetah shotah*, she would not drink. Two prevail over one and the process stops. This seems almost self-evident. One might wonder: if two witnesses testified that she committed adultery, why is she not executed? The *Gemara* implies they may not be *kosher* witnesses.

The *Gemara* notes an interesting structural point. The *mishnah* had just stated that one against one neutralizes (at least simultaneously). If so, the case of two against one — where the two win — does not seem to add much; by the same logic, one against one stopping the process ought to follow even more readily. And conversely, if the point of two against one stopping the process is to suggest that one against one would *not* stop it — that one against one would neutralize — then this aligns with Rabbi Chiya's reading.

The *Gemara* then presses: doesn't the case of two beating one (*lo hayetah shotah*) prove that one against one *would* let her drink — a problem for Ulla, who rules that even one against one stops the process? Rabbi Chiya responds: *al tameich, eima seifa* — look at the final clause of the *mishnah*. Two say *teme'ah*, one says not — she would not drink. Would you then say that one and one she *would* drink? That would also follow from the same logic. The *mishnah*'s final clause cannot be read as implying that two and one is different from one and one in terms of which direction they cut. Something else must be the purpose of these final clauses, and the *Gemara* now explains what that is.

## R. Nechemya: Majority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כּוּלַּהּ בִּפְסוּלֵי עֵדוּת, וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶאֱמִינָה תּוֹרָה עֵד אֶחָד — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב דֵּעוֹת, וְעָשׂוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד כִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rather,** the correct understanding is that **the entire** mishna is not dealing with valid witnesses, and stating an obvious *halakha* in order to enable an inference, but **with** people who are **disqualified** from giving **testimony,** and is teaching us a novel ruling. **And** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Neḥemya. As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta Yevamot* 14:1) that **Rabbi Neḥemya says: Wherever the Torah relies on one witness, follow the majority of opinions.** In other words, if the testimonies of two disqualified witnesses conflict, the court rules in accordance with the version supported by more witnesses, whether or not they are qualified to testify. **And** the Sages **established** that the testimony of **two women,** who are usually disqualified from testifying, when they testify in opposition to **one man,** should be **like** that of **two men against one man,** and the court will rule in accordance with the testimony of the two women.

### Majority When One Witness

The *Gemara* concludes that the closing section of the *mishnah* — the two-against-one cases in both directions — is not addressing the debate between Ulla and Rabbi Chiya at all. Rather, *kula be-pasulei edus ve-Rabbi Nechemya hi* — the entire passage concerns witnesses who are not *kosher* in the formal sense, and it follows the opinion of Rabbi Nechemya.

A *baraita* records Rabbi Nechemya's principle: *kol makom she-he'emina Torah eid echad halach achar rov de'os* — wherever the Torah has granted believability to a single witness, you follow the majority of opinions. The reasoning is fundamental. The Torah's ordinary framework of testimony requires two *kosher* witnesses. The *sotah* case has already broken that framework by accepting a single witness, and by accepting witnesses who are not formally *kosher*. Once the normal rules are set aside, the remaining organizing principle is simply numerical: whichever side has more voices prevails.

In the normal law of testimony, two witnesses are formally equivalent to one hundred — quantity above the threshold of two is irrelevant. But when we have already departed from the formal framework, we revert to a more intuitive standard: more voices on one side carry greater weight.

### Gender Parity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעָשׂוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד כִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Sages **established** that the testimony of **two women,** who are usually disqualified from testifying, when they testify in opposition to **one man,** should be **like** that of **two men against one man.**

Rabbi Nechemya extends this logic explicitly: *afilu shtei nashim be-ish echad ki-shnei anashim be-ish echad* — even two women against one man are equivalent to two men against one man. One might have thought that a single *kosher* male witness carries inherently greater weight than women, who are ordinarily *pasul* for testimony. Rabbi Nechemya rules otherwise. Since the entire framework of formal testimony has been suspended, there is no basis for preferring a *kosher* witness's testimony over that of a *pasul* witness. Two is simply more than one, and two women beat one man.

This is a remarkable *chiddush*. The *mishnah* in its closing cases is teaching precisely this: two women can override one man, and the majority — in either direction — determines the outcome.

### Male Witness Lock-In

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: כׇּל הֵיכָא דַּאֲתָא עֵד אֶחָד כָּשֵׁר מֵעִיקָּרָא אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה נָשִׁים נָמֵי כְּעֵד אֶחָד דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** that Rabbi Neḥemya actually stated something different: **And there are** those **who say** a different version of Rabbi Neḥemya's approach: **Anywhere that one valid witness came initially, even one hundred women** who later contradict him **are considered like one** witness, and do not override his testimony.

At this point the *Gemara* introduces a qualification. The full reach of Rabbi Nechemya's principle — that women can override a man even when they arrive later — may be too broad. A secondary opinion holds: *kol heicha de-ata eid echad kosher me-ikara afilu me'ah nashim nami ke-eid echad damyan* — if a single *kosher* male witness testified first, even one hundred women who came afterward count as only a single witness. His testimony locks in.

The question concerns sequential testimony, and here the *Gemara* takes the discussion in a direction that goes beyond the earlier Ulla–Rabbi Chiya debate. Ulla and Rabbi Chiya agreed: once a witness testifies and is accepted, later testimony cannot undo it. Rabbi Nechemya's *rov de'os* principle seemed to challenge this: perhaps if enough people testify afterward — even women against a man — the later majority prevails.

One position within the discussion of Rabbi Nechemya's view accepts this: even if the first witness was a man, the later majority can override. The second position, which the *Gemara* ultimately elaborates, resists this conclusion where a *kosher* witness testified first. In that case, his testimony acquires the status of formal *kosher edut*, equivalent to two proper witnesses, and that cannot be overridden by any subsequent accumulation of *pasul* witnesses. The *Gemara* therefore explains the *mishnah*'s case: *ve-hacha be-mai askinan ke-gon de-asi ishah me-ikara* — the case in the *mishnah* where later testimony does override must be one where the first witness was herself a woman. When the first to testify is a woman, her testimony does not attain the status of formal *edut*, and a later majority of women can override it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן דַּאֲתַאי אִשָּׁה מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And with what are we dealing here** in the mishna? A case **where a woman,** who is generally disqualified from bearing witness, **came initially,** and testified that the woman committed adultery, and two witnesses say that she did not.

### Strict vs Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָרְצַהּ לִדְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה הָכִי, רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶאֱמִינָה תּוֹרָה עֵד אֶחָד — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב דֵּיעוֹת, וְעָשׂוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת כִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד. אֲבָל שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד — כִּי פַּלְגָא וּפַלְגָא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according to this interpretation you must **amend the** statement **of Rabbi Neḥemya** so that it reads **like this: Rabbi Neḥemya says: Wherever the Torah relies on one witness, follow the majority of opinions. And** the Sages **established** that **two women against one woman** are **like two men against one man. But two women** in opposition **to one man** that is a valid witness, **is like half** of a pair of witnesses **and a half** of a pair of witnesses, and the mishna did not address that case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַרְתֵּי בִּפְסוּלֵי עֵדוּת לְמָה לִי? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כִּי אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר רוֹב דֵּיעוֹת לְחוּמְרָא, אֲבָל לְקוּלָּא לָא אָזְלִינַן, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question on these two interpretations of the mishna: **And why do I** need **two** cases in the mishna to teach the *halakha* that the majority opinion of **those disqualified from** bearing **witness** is followed? The Gemara explains: It is necessary, **lest you say** that **when we follow the majority opinion** in the case of invalid witnesses, this is **to be stringent** to force the woman to drink the bitter water, e.g., if one witness said that she committed adultery and two said that she did not, **but to be lenient** and absolve her from having to drink the water we do **not** follow the majority opinion, and she would still drink the water even if there is one witness saying that she did not commit adultery, therefore the mishna **teaches us** that there is no difference in this regard, and the majority opinion is followed in any case.

One further question: why does the *mishnah* need to give *both* two-against-one cases — two saying *teme'ah* against one saying not, *and* one saying *teme'ah* against two saying not? If the principle is simply that the majority prevails, both cases follow automatically.

The *Gemara* answers: *ma d'teima ki azlinan basar rov de'os le-chumra aval le-kula lo azlinan* — one might have thought that we follow the majority only when it produces the stringent outcome, but not the lenient one. That is, if the majority says *teme'ah*, we follow them because it is *le-chumra* to stop the process; but if the majority says she did not become *teme'ah*, perhaps we would not follow them *le-kula* to permit her to drink. The *mishnah* therefore needed both cases to teach that the majority principle operates symmetrically: we follow the greater number regardless of which direction the leniency or stringency cuts.

Rashi's view of what constitutes *chumra* and *kula* here is interesting. He reads it as lenient to believe the witness who says she committed adultery, since the consequence — that she does not drink and goes out without her *ketubah* — spares her from the risk of the waters. Others might read the *kula* as permitting her to drink and clear her name. Either way, the *mishnah*'s teaching is that the majority principle is not asymmetric: it applies in both directions.

To summarize the overall architecture of this *Gemara*: the one witness who testifies to adultery in the *sotah* context carries extraordinary weight, grounded in the prior testimony of warning and seclusion. Ulla holds that once accepted, this testimony is unassailable by a single contradicting voice — it is treated as two witnesses. Rabbi Chiya limits this to sequential testimony; simultaneous contradiction still neutralizes. Rabbi Nechemya introduces the further principle that when the Torah has suspended the formal rules of testimony by accepting a single witness, the remaining criterion is the majority of voices — and this majority operates across gender lines and, in some views, even overrides testimony already accepted, provided the first witness was not a *kosher* male witness whose testimony locks in as formal *edut*.
