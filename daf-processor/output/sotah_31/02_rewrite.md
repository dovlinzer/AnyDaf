# Sotah 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening Mishnah

### R. Eliezer: Bird Claim

The daf begins two-thirds of the way down the page, at the opening of a new *perek*. The *mishnah* returns to the very first *mishnah* of the *masechta*: the case of *mi she-kina le-ishto ve-nistara* — a man who warned his wife not to go into seclusion and she did so anyway. The question before us is: what quality of proof is required to establish that she went into seclusion, so as to permit or compel the continuation of the *sotah* process?

Rabbi Eliezer's position, already stated in the opening *mishnah*, is stated here in its most radical form: *afilu shama mi-of pore'ach* — even if one merely heard the claim of a passing bird. No matter how tenuous the assertion, any claim that she went into seclusion suffices as the basis for the *sotah* process. This is, by any measure, a strikingly unfair standard for the woman.

The consequence the *mishnah* states is *yotzi ve-yiten ketubah* — he divorces her and pays the *ketubah*. This phrasing is initially puzzling: one might have expected it to say that he must make her drink the *sotah* water, not that he must pay the *ketubah*. Rashi explains the logic: once the *sotah* process is triggered, she is forbidden to him. He has two options — either she undergoes the drinking, or, if neither party wishes to pursue that path, he divorces her and pays the *ketubah*. If she refuses to drink and the process is abandoned, she leaves without the *ketubah*. The *mishnah* states the case from the perspective of the husband who chooses not to proceed with the drinking.

### R. Yehoshua: Rumors

Rabbi Yehoshua requires something more substantial. His formulation is *ad she-ye-hayu nos'ot ve-notnot bah nashim ba-matyah ba-levanah* — until the women who sit and spin by moonlight begin to gossip about her. This somewhat colorful idiom conveys the requirement that there be actual social rumor circulating about her conduct.

Rabbi Yehoshua's standard, understood correctly, is that one needs two proper witnesses that she went into seclusion. The case of gossip here is not a *halacha* about rendering her forbidden to her husband as a *sotah*; there are no witnesses to seclusion at all. Rather, the issue is with the phrase *tehorah hi* — she is pure — which appears in connection with the *sotah* water. Once her reputation has been publicly tarnished, the waters will not be effective in any case. So the option of drinking is no longer on the table.

At the same time, since there are no formal witnesses to seclusion, she has not been rendered *teme'ah* as a *sotah*. The husband could theoretically remain married to her — there is merely this cloud of rumor. But if he chooses to divorce her because of this reputation, he must pay the *ketubah*. The result is an asymmetry in how *yotzi ve-yiten ketubah* functions in each case: for Rabbi Eliezer, it implies she *is* a *sotah* and may be compelled to drink; for Rabbi Yehoshua, she is not a *sotah*, but the husband who wishes to leave must nonetheless pay. This is, as Rashi reads the *mishnah*, a difficult but defensible reading.

## One Witness After

### Slave/Maidservant Testim…

Assuming the standard of Rabbi Yehoshua — two witnesses to seclusion — the *mishnah* now addresses the consequences of testimony regarding the act of adultery itself. The *halacha* is that once the predicate of warning and seclusion has been established, a single witness who comes and says *ani ra'iti she-nitme'ah* — "I saw that she became *teme'ah*," that is, that she committed adultery within that seclusion — is believed. The consequence is not execution, for which two *kosher* witnesses would be required, but rather that she may not drink the *sotah* water; she is considered a proven adulteress, and upon divorce she forfeits her *ketubah*.

The *mishnah* extends this further: *afilu eved afilu shifchah harei elu ne'emanim* — even a male or female slave would be believed for this purpose. The one witness who testifies to the act of adultery need not be a *kosher* witness in the formal sense. The consequence of such testimony is that she loses her *ketubah*.

### Jealous Relatives

The *mishnah* then lists five categories of women who are presumed to harbor enmity toward the wife: the mother-in-law (*chamot*), the mother-in-law's daughter, the co-wife (*tzarat*), the widowed sister-in-law who stands to be subject to *yibum* with the husband, and the husband's daughter from another wife. These women all have identifiable personal interest in the wife's downfall and their credibility is therefore diminished.

Nevertheless, the *mishnah* rules *harei elu ne'emanos* — they are believed — but with a qualification: *ve-lo le-fosla mi-ketubatah ella shelo tishteh*. Their testimony is sufficient to stop the *sotah* drinking process — we treat their claim as sufficiently credible that we will not proceed with the water — but it is *not* sufficient to strip the wife of her *ketubah*. The husband must divorce her and pay the *ketubah*. So she loses the option of drinking and clearing her name, but she retains her financial protection.

## Opening Mishnah (II)

### Kal vaComer Logic

The *mishnah* then raises the question of how we know that the *eid echad* is believed for the testimony of adultery at all. One might have constructed a *kal va-chomer* that would have led to the opposite conclusion. The reasoning would proceed: the first testimony — that she went into seclusion — is a less severe form of evidence, since even after seclusion is established she still has the option of the *sotah* water to clear her name. Yet even for this less consequential testimony, the Torah requires two witnesses. All the more so, then, for the second testimony — the one that actually seals her fate and makes her permanently forbidden — should we not require two witnesses?

This is a compelling argument. The *mishnah* answers by citing the verse *ve-eid ein bah* — there is no witness against her, implying that even one witness suffices. The *mishnah* reads this as: if there *is* even one witness against her, there is a problem; she may not drink.

The *mishnah* then runs the *kal va-chomer* in the opposite direction. Now that we have established from this verse that one witness suffices to seal her fate in the more consequential second testimony, we might reverse the logic: *kal va-chomer*, the first testimony about seclusion, which is less consequential because the *sotah* water remains available, should also be achievable with one witness. That would unravel the entire structure, eliminating the requirement for two witnesses to seclusion.

To block this reversal, the *mishnah* cites the verse *ki matza bah ervat davar* — when the husband finds "a matter of nakedness" regarding her, with the word *davar* (*matter*) linked by *gezeirah shavah* to the verse *al pi shenayim eidim yakum davar* — a matter is established by two witnesses. From this parallel, the *mishnah* derives that the seclusion testimony requires two witnesses: *mah lehavalahn al pi shenayim af kan al pi shenayim*.

This *gezeirah shavah* is somewhat surprising in context, since the verse *ki matza bah ervat davar* appears in the laws of *get* and not in the *sotah* passage itself. The *mishnah* reads it as referring to the husband's suspicion, which is precisely the situation of seclusion. The *Gemara*, however, will read both the *derashot* and this *pasuk* somewhat differently.

The *Gemara* interjects: why do we need this *gezeirah shavah* from the word *davar*? There is a simpler derivation. The verse *ve-eid ein bah* that grants believability to the single witness uses the word *bah* — "regarding her." This word *bah* is limiting: it is specifically in regard to the act of adultery itself that a single witness is believed. For *kinuy* and *stirah* — the warning and seclusion stages — the verse offers no such license, and those require two. The *Gemara* concludes *hachi nami ka'amar* — yes, that is indeed what the *mishnah* means to convey: *bah* — regarding the act itself one witness is believed, *ve-lo be-kinuy ve-lo be-stirah*.

A further question arises: once we know that in the *sotah* context one witness is believed for the act of adultery when building on the seclusion, might one extend this to suggest that one witness would always be believed to accuse a woman of adultery even without the prior structure of warning and seclusion? The answer comes from the same *gezeirah shavah*: *ne'emar kan davar ve-ne'emar lehavalahn davar* — the word *davar* appears here and in the verse about two witnesses, establishing that outside the specific *sotah* framework, testimony of adultery requires two. The *sotah* context is the exception; the general rule remains two *kosher* witnesses.

## Default Two Witnesses

### No Two Witnesses

The *Gemara* now turns to the section of the *mishnah* dealing with one witness against one witness, where the *mishnah* appears to say they neutralize one another and the drinking proceeds. Before engaging with that debate, the *Gemara* establishes the foundational principle: *minani mili* — from where do we know that one witness is believed for the act of adultery in the *sotah* case?

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *ve-eid ein bah bi-shnayim ha-katuv medaber* — when the verse says "there is no witness against her," the Torah is speaking about two witnesses. That is, "no witness" means no *two* witnesses. The verse is understood as: there are no two witnesses against her, only one — and she was not raped — in which case she is forbidden and the drinking is halted.

This reading may seem counterintuitive. The plain reading of the *mishnah* seemed to say that "no witness" permits drinking, and "one witness" prohibits it. The *Gemara* instead understands the verse as: no two witnesses — only one — and that one witness suffices to stop the process.

### Eid = Two Default

How do we know that the unqualified word *eid* means two? The *Gemara* derives this from the verse *lo yakum eid echad be-ish le-chol avon* — one witness may not rise up against a person for any offense. The *Gemara* asks: since *eid* is already singular in form, why does the verse need to specify *echad* — one? It must be that the word *eid* by itself ordinarily denotes two witnesses, and the qualifier *echad* is needed to indicate that only one is meant.

This yields a general *klal*: *kol makom she-ne'emar eid harei kan shnayim ad she-yafrit lecha ha-katuv echad* — wherever the word *eid* appears, it means two witnesses, unless the text explicitly specifies *echad* to indicate a single one. This is a far-reaching principle that recurs throughout the *Gemara* in matters of testimony.

With this principle established, the *Gemara* can read *ve-eid ein bah* as referring to the absence of two witnesses: the Torah is saying there are no two witnesses against her, there is only one — and it is that one witness, acting in the context of established seclusion, who is believed to render her forbidden.

## Competing Testimonies

### Ulla: Cannot Neutralize

With the foundational principle established, the *Gemara* turns to the *mishnah*'s statement that one witness saying she became *teme'ah* against one witness saying she did not would neutralize one another, allowing the drinking to proceed. Rashi notes that the witness who says *lo nitme'ah* is not claiming to have observed the room and seen that nothing happened; rather, he is saying something more modest — "your observation is not reliable" or "I was with you and I know you could not have seen what you claim." It is a contradiction of credibility, not an affirmative counterclaim.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges the premise that such a contradiction could have any force at all. The first witness — the one who says she became *teme'ah* — is not an ordinary witness. He comes equipped with the prior testimony of warning and seclusion, which form a *raglayim la-davar*, a factual foundation for the suspicion. The Torah, given this foundation, has granted this witness extraordinary weight, treating his single testimony as authoritative. How, then, can a single witness offering a mere contradiction — without any comparable foundation — neutralize this specially-empowered testimony?

This is Ulla's principle: *kol makom she-min ha-Torah eid echad harei kan shnayim* — wherever the Torah accords special believability to a single witness, that witness effectively counts as two. And *ein dvaro shel echad bi-makom shnayim* — the word of an ordinary single witness has no weight against two. If the specially-empowered witness counts as two, the ordinary contradiction counts as one, and one cannot override two. It follows that the contradiction is simply ineffective, and we would consider her *teme'ah*.

Ulla therefore proposes a different *girsa* of the *mishnah*: where the *mishnah* reads *hayetah shotah* — she would drink — in the case of one against one, Ulla reads *lo hayetah shotah* — she would *not* drink. The one who says she became *teme'ah* wins, and the process is stopped. Rabbi Yitzchak adopts the same reading.

### R. Chiya: Simultaneous

Rabbi Chiya, however, retains the original text of the *mishnah*: in the case of one against one, *hayetah shotah* — she would drink. The two testimonies neutralize one another, and the process continues. The *Gemara* notes that Ulla's argument — based on the specially-granted weight of the first witness — poses a genuine difficulty for Rabbi Chiya. It is not merely that Rabbi Chiya disagrees with Ulla's opinion; Ulla's position has compelling logical force, grounded in the structure of the Torah's grant of special believability.

### Timing: Simultaneous vs…

The *Gemara* resolves this tension by distinguishing between the timing of the testimonies. *Kan be-vat achas, kan be-zeh achar zeh* — in one case the witnesses arrive simultaneously, in the other they arrive sequentially. Rashi specifies that "simultaneously" means within *toch kedei dibbur* — the brief interval of a normal utterance.

The logic of this distinction is as follows. If both witnesses arrive at the same time, the court has not yet accorded full weight to the first witness's claim. Two people step forward together; one says she became *teme'ah* and the other immediately disputes this. Since the challenge is present from the outset, the testimony of the first has never been fully established, and the situation remains unresolved — she drinks. This is the case Rabbi Chiya is addressing, and it does not contradict Ulla's principle, because Ulla's principle applies once the testimony has been accepted.

If, however, the first witness testified and the court recognized his testimony — if that testimony has been received and accorded its special weight — then a witness who arrives afterward cannot dislodge it. The first testimony, once accepted, is treated as two witnesses, and nothing that comes subsequently can override it.

## Two-Against-One Cases

### 1 vs 2: Neutralizes

The *Gemara* now turns to the *mishnah*'s next case: one says she became *teme'ah* and two say she did not — *hayetah shotah*, she would drink. The two neutralize the one, and the process continues. This makes immediate sense in light of everything established: if even a single opposing witness can neutralize the one who says *teme'ah* (when they arrive simultaneously), two opposing witnesses can certainly do so.

This is also why Rashi insists that *lo nitme'ah* does not mean the witnesses actively observed innocence; if two witnesses had positively testified that she did not commit adultery, the question would be entirely settled and she obviously would not drink. Their testimony is a contradiction of the first witness's claim, not an affirmative attestation of innocence.

### 2 vs 1: Two Win

The final case in the *mishnah*: two say she became *teme'ah* and one says she did not — *lo hayetah shotah*, she would not drink. Two prevail over one and the process stops. This seems almost self-evident. One might wonder: if two witnesses testified that she committed adultery, why is she not executed? The *Gemara* implies they may not be *kosher* witnesses.

The *Gemara* notes an interesting structural point. The *mishnah* had just stated that one against one neutralizes (at least simultaneously). If so, the case of two against one — where the two win — does not seem to add much; by the same logic, one against one stopping the process ought to follow even more readily. And conversely, if the point of two against one stopping the process is to suggest that one against one would *not* stop it — that one against one would neutralize — then this aligns with Rabbi Chiya's reading.

The *Gemara* then presses: doesn't the case of two beating one (*lo hayetah shotah*) prove that one against one *would* let her drink — a problem for Ulla, who rules that even one against one stops the process? Rabbi Chiya responds: *al tameich, eima seifa* — look at the final clause of the *mishnah*. Two say *teme'ah*, one says not — she would not drink. Would you then say that one and one she *would* drink? That would also follow from the same logic. The *mishnah*'s final clause cannot be read as implying that two and one is different from one and one in terms of which direction they cut. Something else must be the purpose of these final clauses, and the *Gemara* now explains what that is.

## R. Nechemya: Majority

### Majority When One Witness

The *Gemara* concludes that the closing section of the *mishnah* — the two-against-one cases in both directions — is not addressing the debate between Ulla and Rabbi Chiya at all. Rather, *kula be-pasulei edus ve-Rabbi Nechemya hi* — the entire passage concerns witnesses who are not *kosher* in the formal sense, and it follows the opinion of Rabbi Nechemya.

A *baraita* records Rabbi Nechemya's principle: *kol makom she-he'emina Torah eid echad halach achar rov de'os* — wherever the Torah has granted believability to a single witness, you follow the majority of opinions. The reasoning is fundamental. The Torah's ordinary framework of testimony requires two *kosher* witnesses. The *sotah* case has already broken that framework by accepting a single witness, and by accepting witnesses who are not formally *kosher*. Once the normal rules are set aside, the remaining organizing principle is simply numerical: whichever side has more voices prevails.

In the normal law of testimony, two witnesses are formally equivalent to one hundred — quantity above the threshold of two is irrelevant. But when we have already departed from the formal framework, we revert to a more intuitive standard: more voices on one side carry greater weight.

### Gender Parity

Rabbi Nechemya extends this logic explicitly: *afilu shtei nashim be-ish echad ki-shnei anashim be-ish echad* — even two women against one man are equivalent to two men against one man. One might have thought that a single *kosher* male witness carries inherently greater weight than women, who are ordinarily *pasul* for testimony. Rabbi Nechemya rules otherwise. Since the entire framework of formal testimony has been suspended, there is no basis for preferring a *kosher* witness's testimony over that of a *pasul* witness. Two is simply more than one, and two women beat one man.

This is a remarkable *chiddush*. The *mishnah* in its closing cases is teaching precisely this: two women can override one man, and the majority — in either direction — determines the outcome.

### Male Witness Lock-In

At this point the *Gemara* introduces a qualification. The full reach of Rabbi Nechemya's principle — that women can override a man even when they arrive later — may be too broad. A secondary opinion holds: *kol heicha de-ata eid echad kosher me-ikara afilu me'ah nashim nami ke-eid echad damyan* — if a single *kosher* male witness testified first, even one hundred women who came afterward count as only a single witness. His testimony locks in.

The question concerns sequential testimony, and here the *Gemara* takes the discussion in a direction that goes beyond the earlier Ulla–Rabbi Chiya debate. Ulla and Rabbi Chiya agreed: once a witness testifies and is accepted, later testimony cannot undo it. Rabbi Nechemya's *rov de'os* principle seemed to challenge this: perhaps if enough people testify afterward — even women against a man — the later majority prevails.

One position within the discussion of Rabbi Nechemya's view accepts this: even if the first witness was a man, the later majority can override. The second position, which the *Gemara* ultimately elaborates, resists this conclusion where a *kosher* witness testified first. In that case, his testimony acquires the status of formal *kosher edut*, equivalent to two proper witnesses, and that cannot be overridden by any subsequent accumulation of *pasul* witnesses. The *Gemara* therefore explains the *mishnah*'s case: *ve-hacha be-mai askinan ke-gon de-asi ishah me-ikara* — the case in the *mishnah* where later testimony does override must be one where the first witness was herself a woman. When the first to testify is a woman, her testimony does not attain the status of formal *edut*, and a later majority of women can override it.

### Strict vs Lenient

One further question: why does the *mishnah* need to give *both* two-against-one cases — two saying *teme'ah* against one saying not, *and* one saying *teme'ah* against two saying not? If the principle is simply that the majority prevails, both cases follow automatically.

The *Gemara* answers: *ma d'teima ki azlinan basar rov de'os le-chumra aval le-kula lo azlinan* — one might have thought that we follow the majority only when it produces the stringent outcome, but not the lenient one. That is, if the majority says *teme'ah*, we follow them because it is *le-chumra* to stop the process; but if the majority says she did not become *teme'ah*, perhaps we would not follow them *le-kula* to permit her to drink. The *mishnah* therefore needed both cases to teach that the majority principle operates symmetrically: we follow the greater number regardless of which direction the leniency or stringency cuts.

Rashi's view of what constitutes *chumra* and *kula* here is interesting. He reads it as lenient to believe the witness who says she committed adultery, since the consequence — that she does not drink and goes out without her *ketubah* — spares her from the risk of the waters. Others might read the *kula* as permitting her to drink and clear her name. Either way, the *mishnah*'s teaching is that the majority principle is not asymmetric: it applies in both directions.

To summarize the overall architecture of this *Gemara*: the one witness who testifies to adultery in the *sotah* context carries extraordinary weight, grounded in the prior testimony of warning and seclusion. Ulla holds that once accepted, this testimony is unassailable by a single contradicting voice — it is treated as two witnesses. Rabbi Chiya limits this to sequential testimony; simultaneous contradiction still neutralizes. Rabbi Nechemya introduces the further principle that when the Torah has suspended the formal rules of testimony by accepting a single witness, the remaining criterion is the majority of voices — and this majority operates across gender lines and, in some views, even overrides testimony already accepted, provided the first witness was not a *kosher* male witness whose testimony locks in as formal *edut*.
