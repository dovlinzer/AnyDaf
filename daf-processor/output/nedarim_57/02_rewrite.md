# Nedarim 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Perek Transition

### Opening & Scope Shift

Daf 57 opens with a significant shift in character from the preceding material. Much of the previous perek and a half was dedicated to examining what objects are included within the scope of particular vow-words: what falls under *bayis*, what is encompassed by *ir*, what is meant by *mevushal* or *tavshil*, or when something is considered to have been poured into a pot. All of that analysis revolved around specific objects and the linguistic reach of particular terms. Now we encounter a considerably more conceptual *mishna* — one whose key *chiddushim* were largely anticipated in earlier *gemara* discussions and will therefore not be rehearsed at length here.

---

## Konam & Korban Model

### Konam Formula

The *mishna* at the top of 57a opens with several formulations: *konam peiros ha'eileh alai* — these fruits are forbidden to me via *konam*; *konam hein al pi* — they are forbidden to my mouth; *konam hein lifi* — to my mouth. In all such cases, the person is not merely restricted from the specific fruits themselves. The *mishna* rules *assur b'chilufeihen u'v'giduleihen* — he is also forbidden from things those fruits have been exchanged for (*chilufim*), and from what grows from them (*gidulim*). If the fruit is still on the tree and continues to grow, that growth is included. And even if we are speaking of a second generation — say, a peach pit that was planted and produced a new tree — those fruits are likewise forbidden.

### Hekdesh Parallel

The conceptual foundation for this rule lies in the model upon which *nedarim* are built. The keyword *konam* is drawn from the world of *korbanot*: to declare something *konam* is to treat it as though it were a *korban*. In the domain of *hekdesh*, if one sells a *korban* or attempts to exchange it, the money received also becomes *kadosh* — the principle of *tofes es dam'ha*, that the sanctity adheres to what replaces it, is operative. This is most pronounced in *avodah zarah* and *shvi'it*, and applies to some degree in *hekdesh* as well — most clearly in the laws of *temura*, where an attempt to transfer *kedusha* from one animal to another results in both animals becoming *kadosh*. The concept of *gidulim* is similarly rooted in the *hekdesh* paradigm: if one consecrates a cow and it gives birth, the offspring — *vlad hekdesh* — is itself *kadosh*. This is precisely the concept of *giduleihem* in miniature.

### Issurei Hana'ah

The *gemara* in an earlier section raised the question of how far these principles extend to *issurei hana'ah* generally. If one possesses *orlah* — fruit forbidden during the first three years of a tree's growth — and sells it, does the money received also become forbidden? As a general rule, the answer is no: the principle that exchanges become forbidden applies specifically to *avodah zarah* and *shvi'it*, and in certain respects to *hekdesh*, but not to other categories of *issurei hana'ah*. The *gemara* there, as the Ran develops it, explored whether at minimum the seller himself might remain forbidden from the proceeds even if others could benefit — a notion arising from the intimacy between the transgressor and his own exchange.

The *mishna*'s ruling that *chilufim* and *gidulim* are forbidden in the context of *konamot* draws heavily, then, on the specific character of *konam* language. Because *konam* invokes the world of *hekdesh*, both exchanges and growths are treated as carrying the original forbidden status forward. One earlier *gemara* also raised the question of *yesh me'ilah b'konamot* — whether transgressing a *konam* vow carries with it a violation of *me'ilah*, the prohibition of trespass against *hekdesh*. This too derives from the *konam*-as-*korban* framework, though the *gemara* does not restrict this principle exclusively to cases where the word *konam* was used.

It is worth noting that while the *konam* formulation seems to be the primary conceptual driver, the *Rishonim* — including the Ran — also pointed, in discussing the earlier *gemara*, to the *intent* of the person making the vow. One might argue that when a person says "this is forbidden to me," they implicitly mean to forbid not just this object but everything that flows from it. This, however, is less clearly the *pshat* of the *mishna* than the *konam*-based explanation.

---

## Konam vs. Action Words

### Ochel/To'em Language

The *mishna* then introduces a critical distinction. If instead of declaring the fruit *konam*, the person said *she'ani ochel* — I will not eat these fruits — or *she'ani to'em* — I will not taste them — then the rule reverses: *mutar b'chilufeihen u'v'giduleihen*. In these formulations, the exchanges and growths are permissible.

### State vs. Action

Two explanations converge here. The first concerns the absence of the word *konam*: without it, the vow does not invoke the *hekdesh* model and therefore does not carry forward the stringencies associated with that domain, including the extension of the prohibition to *chilufim* and *gidulim*. The second — and this is what Tosafot, the Ran, and the *Rishonim* generally emphasize — concerns not what was omitted but what was said. When one declares an object *assur*, that is a statement about the *state* of the object: it is forbidden. A state, by its nature, can transfer: the state of being forbidden inheres in this fruit, and that state can carry over to what replaces it or grows from it. But when one says *she'ani ochel* or *she'ani to'em*, the prohibition is not about the object's state — it is about a specific *action* with respect to that object. The prohibition is on the act of eating or tasting *this* fruit. If one then eats or tastes what grew from the pit of that fruit, or benefits from the proceeds of an exchange, one is not performing that specific action on that specific object. The vow simply does not reach there. Even phrasing like *konam hein al pi* — forbidden to my mouth — though it might seem to approximate action-language, is still essentially a statement of the object's state vis-à-vis oneself, not a specification of a concrete action.

This distinction also touches on the longstanding question about whether *she'ani ochel* implies an inadvertent blurring of *neder* and *shevuah*. A *shevuah* is an oath about one's own behavior, and only the person who swore can violate it. A *neder* is classically about the status of an object. The use of *she'ani ochel*, however, creates a *neder* about a specific action, a *neder* whose reach is therefore bounded by that action rather than by the object's general status.

## Zaro Kalah Categories

### Zaro Kalah Case

The *mishna* then distinguishes between two categories of *gidulim* based on the physical character of the original object. *B'davar she'zaro kalah* — when the original thing goes out of existence — the growth that emerges is permissible. This applies even when the formulation used was *konam* rather than *she'ani ochel*. The classic example is planting wheat seeds: the seeds themselves cease to exist, and what grows is an entirely new generation. In such a case, even if one used *konam* language, the *gidulim* are permitted.

### Ein Zaro Kalah Case

*Aval b'davar she'ein zaro kalah* — but when the original thing does not cease to exist, when it continues to grow and expand — the rule is reversed: *afilu gidulei gidulim assurim*, even what grows from the growth is forbidden. The *gemara* will discuss the case of an onion as the paradigmatic example, since replanting an onion results in the original onion continuing to grow rather than ceasing to exist and being replaced by a new generation. Similarly, fruit continuing to grow on a tree represents *ein zaro kalah*: the original fruit is still there, merely expanding. In such cases, even if the person used *she'ani ochel* language, the prohibition remains in force. One reason this might be so is that when the original forbidden thing is still present and the new growth is still physically attached to and an extension of it, the new growth partakes of the same status as the original — there is no clear boundary between old and new, and the growth may itself be *assur* as a continuation of the original forbidden matter.

### Gidulei Gidulim

The question of *gidulei gidulim* — growth of growth, i.e., the third generation — requires a moment's attention. When we say that *afilu gidulei gidulim assurim*, this applies in the *ein zaro kalah* scenario: the original thing is still there and continues to grow, and even what grows from that growth carries the same status. But what scenario would produce a meaningful *gidulei gidulim* in the *ein zaro kalah* case, given that the original is always present? The *Rishonim* note that a true third-generation scenario really only makes structural sense in the *zaro kalah* case — take the wheat seed, plant it, harvest the new generation, take seeds from *that*, and plant again. In the *ein zaro kalah* case, the original is always there, making it difficult to identify a distinct third generation. Nevertheless, the *mishna*'s language covers the case, even if the practical application in the *ein zaro kalah* context is less clear.

## Wife's Labor & Time

### Duration Restriction

The *mishna* now turns to a distinct and important set of cases involving the husband's forbidding of benefit from his wife's labor. The first formulation: *she'at osah eini ochel ad haPesach* — what you produce I will not eat until Pesach; or *she'at osah eini mitkaseh ad haPesach* — what you produce I will not clothe myself with until Pesach. In this formulation, *ad haPesach* — until Pesach — modifies the *duration* of the prohibition: he is forbidding himself from deriving benefit until a certain point in time. Accordingly, *asta lifnei haPesach mutar leechol ulehitkasot achar haPesach*: anything she produced before Pesach, if it is still available after Pesach, he may then eat it or wear it — because his restriction was temporal, not object-based.

### Object Restriction

The *mishna* then presents the contrasting case: *she'at osah ad haPesach eini ochel*, *she'at osah ad haPesach eini mitkaseh* — what you make until Pesach I will not eat; what you make until Pesach I will not wear. Here, *ad haPesach* shifts position and now modifies the *object*, not the duration: it specifies *which* things are forbidden — namely, everything produced between now and Pesach — with no temporal limit on the prohibition itself. Accordingly, *astah lifnei haPesach assur le'echol u'lehitkasot achar haPesach*: whatever she made before Pesach remains forbidden even after Pesach, because the restriction falls on those specific objects permanently. The *chiddush* of the *mishna* is precisely that the placement of the temporal modifier determines the entire structure of the prohibition. In the second formulation, the phrasing is somewhat ambiguous — one could theoretically read *she'at osah, ad haPesach eini ochel*, placing the comma differently and treating the time-limit as the modifier of the prohibition's duration rather than its object. The *mishna* reads it in the more stringent direction, which may itself be part of the *chiddush*.

### Mishna Reread

At this point it is worth noting that the *mishna* contains a preliminary clause that was inadvertently passed over — a case of what scribes call *hashmata al yedei hadmiyot*, omission due to similarity of adjacent phrases. Returning to the proper beginning: *ha'omer le'ishto* — a man says to his wife — *konam ma'aseh yadayich alai*, *konam hein al pi*, *konam hein lefi*: what you produce with your hands is *konam* to me, forbidden to my mouth. The *mishna* rules *assur b'chilufeihen u'v'giduleihen* — he is forbidden even from the exchanges and the growths. If he instead says *she'ani ochel* or *she'ani to'em* — I will not eat or taste — then *mutar b'chilufeihen u'v'giduleihen*: permissible. And as before, this last leniency applies only *b'davar she'zaro kalah*; if the original thing persists, *afilu gidulei gidulim assurim*.

### Konam Wife Labor

The *mishna* accordingly establishes that when *konam* language is used regarding the wife's labor, the husband is forbidden from *chilufim* and *gidulim* as well. The Ran, citing Rabbeinu Yona, addresses a practical consequence: if the wife bakes bread and sells it in the marketplace, the husband may not benefit from the proceeds — except that he is entitled to recoup the cost of the raw materials. The reasoning is that the household runs on the husband's income, and the raw materials were purchased with his money. What he is forbidden from is the value of *her* labor, not the cost of inputs that were his to begin with. He may therefore deduct the cost of the raw materials from whatever she received and retain that portion.

## Conditional Vow

### Scenario Setup

A further *mishna* introduces a considerably more complex structure: a conditional vow with two distinct time-frames. *She'at nehenes li ad haPesach* — you may not derive benefit from me until Pesach — *im holeches at l'veit avich ad hachag*: if you go to visit your father's house between now and *Succos*. The setting is assumed to be before Pesach — perhaps *Adar* — and the husband has set up a vow with a prohibition window of roughly one month (until Pesach) and a condition window of six months (until *Succos*). The condition can thus be triggered *after* the prohibition period has already ended.

### Triggered Before

The *mishna* first presents the straightforward case: *halcha lifnei haPesach asura b'hana'ah ad haPesach* — if she actually goes to her father's house before Pesach, during the period when both the condition window and the prohibition window are active, she is clearly forbidden from deriving benefit until Pesach. The condition has been triggered while the prohibition is in force; the vow operates without complication.

### Retroactive Transgress

The more striking case follows. What if she derives benefit before Pesach — relying on the fact that the condition has not yet been triggered — and then, after Pesach, she goes to her father's house and triggers the condition? The *mishna* rules that she transgresses *lo yachel devaro* retroactively. At the moment she benefited, it might have seemed permissible, since the condition was not yet operative. But once she later triggered the condition, her earlier benefit is retroactively found to have violated the vow.

### Lechatchila Question

The *mishna* does not, however, address a question that the *gemara* discussed at length in an earlier context: was she permitted *lechatchila* to derive benefit before Pesach, relying on her intention not to trigger the condition? Or do we say she may not, because we are concerned that people do not take the triggering condition as seriously as the vow itself, and three months hence she may well visit her father without the weight of the original vow bearing upon her decision? This debate among the *Amoraim* was recorded earlier in the *gemara* and remains unresolved by the *mishna* itself; the *mishna* tells us only about the retroactive case.

### Rambam vs. Ran

The *mishna*'s statement that she transgresses *lo yachel devaro* — he shall not void his word — raises another question discussed by the *Rishonim* in the earlier *gemara*: specifically, can she receive *lashes* for this transgression? The Rambam held that lashes for a vow-violation can only be administered to the person who actually took the vow and violated it. If the husband declares something forbidden to the wife, she may have transgressed a prohibition — but it was not *her* words she violated; it was his. Accordingly, the Rambam maintains that lashes for *lo yachel devaro* can only be given either when a person violates their own vow, or when the one who made the vow is the one who actively causes the transgression — e.g., if the husband made the vow and he is the one who fed her the forbidden item. If she herself benefited, she may have done something wrong, but she cannot be flogged, because it was not her *lo yachel devaro*.

The Ran disagrees fundamentally. For the Ran, a *neder* is not primarily about whose words were spoken; it is about the real *halachic* status that was created in the object. Once the husband declared something forbidden to his wife, that object or that benefit acquired a genuine prohibition-status. If she then violated that status, she has violated something real and tangible, not merely someone else's speech-act. The Ran's position draws directly on the distinction between *neder* and *shevuah*: a *shevuah* is categorically about the person's own words and can only be violated by the one who swore. A *neder*, by contrast — modeled as it is on *hekdesh* — creates an objective status in the world. If someone makes an object *hekdesh* and another person misappropriates it, that other person has committed *me'ilah* regardless of whose words consecrated the object. So too with a *neder*: the wife, by benefiting from what her husband forbade, violated the prohibition inherent in the object itself.

This also illuminates the husband's leverage within this structure: because the object's status is what matters, and because he owns the things he produces and can declare them forbidden to her, he has created a genuine constraint on her behavior rooted in the *hekdesh* paradigm. She, for her part, can make her own vows and create obligations on her own productions — though he retains the power of *hafara*, annulment, in areas that affect him, which significantly limits her ability to create binding constraints that he cannot dissolve. He, by contrast, has no such vulnerability.

## Condition/Prohibition Va…

### Condition Expires First

The final clause of the *mishna* reverses the time-frames: *ad manat li ad hachag im halachet l'veit avich ad haPesach* — you may not derive benefit from me until *Succos* if you go to your father's house between now and Pesach. Here the condition window is shorter than the prohibition window: the condition can only be triggered for approximately one month (until Pesach), while the prohibition lasts for six months (until *Succos*). Once Pesach arrives without the condition having been triggered, the condition has lapsed entirely and can no longer be activated.

### Visit Timing Effect

*Halcha lifnei haPesach asura b'hana'ah ad hachag* — if she went before Pesach, the condition was triggered during its active window, and she is now forbidden from benefit until *Succos*. *U'muteret leilech achar haPesach* — but if she did not go before Pesach, once Pesach passes, she may go freely; the condition has expired and the vow never took effect. The vow itself runs until *Succos*, but the triggering condition was limited to the period before Pesach, and that window has now closed.

## Gidulim & Bittul

### Onion Question

The *gemara* now turns to the specific phenomenon of *gidulim* and introduces a striking question. Yishmael of Kfar Yama — or, some say, Kfar Dima — raised the following case: an onion whose root was uprooted during *shvi'it*, the *shemita* year, and which therefore carried *kedushat shvi'it* — or perhaps was even subject to the laws of *biyur* and fully forbidden — was then planted in the eighth year, the year following *shemita*. The new growth in the eighth year eventually exceeded the original quantity — the amount that grew was a majority relative to, or at least enough to create *bittul* of, the original core. What is the status of the whole?

The physical intuition behind the question is clear: the original core is *assur* by virtue of its *shvi'it* status; the stuff that grew in the eighth year, however, grew without any such status and is *heter*. If the *heter* growth sufficiently exceeds the original *assur* core, might we apply the laws of *bittul* — nullification — and declare the whole permissible?

### Ran's Interpretation

Two preliminary points before the *gemara* proceeds. First, regarding the phrase *he'elah b'yado* — "he brought up in his hand" — the plain reading, followed by Tosafot, is that Yishmael literally took this onion and replanted it. This raises its own questions: does taking *shvi'it* produce from *Eretz Yisrael* to outside the Land raise issues? And if *shemita* does not apply in *Chutz La'aretz*, why would he need to plant it specifically in the eighth year? The Ran, however, reads *he'elah b'yado* idiomatically, as "he raised in hand" — meaning he brought forward this as a theoretical question, not that he literally performed the act.

Second, regarding the word *rov* — "majority": normally *rov* is far from sufficient to create *bittul*; depending on the *issur*, one needs sixty times, one hundred times, or even two hundred times the original amount. The Ran therefore reads *rov* here as *lav davka* — imprecise — meaning simply that the growth was sufficient, in principle, to create *bittul*, whatever amount that might require in the specific case.

### Bittul Question

With those clarifications, the *gemara* formulates the question precisely: *gidulav heter v'ikaro assur* — what grew is *heter*, the core is *assur*. *Keivan d'rabu gidulav me'ikaro, otan gidulei heter ma'alin et ha'issur* — since what grew constitutes enough to ordinarily create *bittul* of the original amount, does that *heter* growth nullify the *issur*? *O lo* — or does it not?

Why might *bittul* fail to operate here? One consideration is that *bittul* ordinarily requires that the forbidden item be truly lost within a larger, independently existing body of *heter*. Here, the *heter* is not independently existing — it grew *from* the *assur* core. It might therefore be argued that growth derived from a forbidden object cannot turn around and negate that very object; the derived cannot negate the source from which it derives.

The Ran presses even further. The plain reading of the *gemara*'s question is that the growth is genuinely *heter* but perhaps cannot perform *bittul*. The Ran, however, reads the *gemara* as leaving open the possibility that the *gidulim* are not really *heter* at all — that because they grew from an *assur* core, they themselves are *assur*. If that is so, there would be no *heter* growth to create *bittul* in the first place. These are two distinct conceptual positions: either the growth is *heter* but lacks the independent weight to negate what spawned it, or the growth is itself *assur* as an extension of the original forbidden matter.

### Terumah Onion

*Lo ha'biyadei* — Rabbi Ami had no answer. The questioner then came before Rabbi Yitzchak Naphcha, who attempted to resolve the question. He cited a statement of Rabbi Chanina Treita in the name of Rabbi Yannai: *batzal shel terumah sheneta'o v'rabu gidulav al ikaro, mutar* — a *terumah* onion that was planted, and whose growth exceeded the core, is *mutar*. This is precisely the same physical scenario transposed from *shvi'it* to *terumah*, and the ruling is permissibility. This seems to provide a clear *psak*: when growth sufficiently exceeds the original amount, *bittul* operates and the whole becomes permissible.

### Orlah Grafting

Rabbi Yirmiyah — and some say Rabbi Zereika — challenged Rabbi Yitzchak Naphcha's citation. His objection was pointed: *shavik mar trein v'avid ke'chad* — why are you setting aside the opinion of *two* authorities and ruling in accordance with *one*? He argued that there exist two rulings going the other way, establishing that in this type of scenario, *bittul* does not operate and the whole remains *assur*. The first of these rulings is attributed to Rabbi Abahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan.

### Orlah Specifics

Rabbi Abahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan taught: *yaldah she'sibkah b'zekeinah* — a young branch, grafted into an older tree. The governing *halacha* of grafting is that if a branch bearing no fruit is grafted onto an existing tree, it is absorbed into the tree and any subsequent fruit it produces takes on the status of the host tree, without *orlah* concerns. But if the young branch already has fruit growing on it when it is grafted — and that fruit is *orlah*, since the branch is from a tree within its first three years — then after grafting, the fruit continues to grow. The fruit that grew after grafting is itself not *orlah*, since it grew while incorporated into the older tree; but the portion of the fruit that existed at the moment of grafting carries *orlah* status. So we have a core of *orlah* and a growth of *heter*. The *halacha* requires two hundred times the original amount to create *bittul* for *orlah*. *Af al pi she'hosifu matayim, assur* — even if the *heter* growth reached two hundred times the amount of the original *orlah*, the fruit remains forbidden. The *gidulim* do not perform *bittul* on the original *issur*.

It is worth noting in passing that some *poskim* have invoked this principle — a grafted branch with existing growth that becomes incorporated into a host — as an analogy for certain questions in the domain of surrogate motherhood, where the original and the subsequent growth are similarly intertwined. But that is a discussion unto itself.

### Kilayim Case

The second ruling comes from Rabbi Shmuel bar Rabbi Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *batzal sheneta'o b'kerem* — an onion planted in a vineyard, which constitutes *kilayim* and renders the onion forbidden. The vineyard is subsequently uprooted — *ne'ekar hakerem* — so that the onion continues to grow without any ongoing *kilayim* violation, since the vineyard no longer exists. The portion that grew before the vineyard was uprooted is *assur* because of *kilayim*; the portion that grew afterward is *heter*. The ruling: *assur*. Here, then, is a fourth instance of the same physical scenario — we have now traversed *shemita*, *terumah*, *orlah*, and *kilayim*, four separate *halachic* domains — and in two of them, the ruling is *assur*. Rabbi Yirmiyah therefore pressed: why adopt the lone opinion of Rabbi Chanina Treita that the growth creates *bittul* when two authorities — Rabbi Yochanan in the *orlah* case and Rabbi Yonatan in the *kilayim* case — rule otherwise?

## Ma'asrot Bittul

### Ma'asrot Scenario

The questioner returned, now thoroughly confused, to Rabbi Ami, who attempted to resolve the matter from a different direction. Rabbi Ami cited a ruling of Rabbi Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *litra betzalim she'tiknah ve'zar'ah, mitaseret lefi kullah* — if one had a *liter* of onions from which *terumot u'ma'asrot* had already been properly separated, planted them, and harvested them after they had grown substantially, those onions are subsequently tithed based on the *total* quantity — not merely on the newly grown portion.

### Ma'asrot Halacha

The logic behind Rabbi Ami's citation is as follows: since *ma'asrot* had already been taken from the original amount before planting, the original portion technically has no further tithing obligation. If we were to tithe based on the total harvest, that implies that the new growth has, in some sense, nullified the original — rendering the whole as if it were all new, untithed produce. This would suggest that *bittul* does operate: the *heter* growth absorbs the original, and we treat everything as subject to the new status.

### Stringency Objection

The objection, however, is immediate: *dilma l'chumra shani* — perhaps the application of *bittul* in the *ma'asrot* case is categorically different because it operates in a *stringent* direction. In the *ma'asrot* scenario, saying that the new growth nullifies the original *results in greater obligation* — we must tithe the entire quantity, including the portion that was already tithed, rather than only the increment. This is a *chumra*. But the question before us — whether the *heter* growth can negate the *issur* core and render everything permissible — would require *bittul* to operate in a *lenient* direction. It is entirely coherent to say that *bittul* by growth may apply when it increases stringency, while declining to apply it when doing so would produce leniency.

### Unresolved Tension

The matter therefore remains unresolved. On one side stand the *terumah* ruling of Rabbi Chanina Treita and the *ma'asrot* ruling of Rabbi Yitzchak in Rabbi Yochanan's name — both of which, at least initially, seem to support the operation of *bittul* by growth. On the other stand the *orlah* ruling of Rabbi Yochanan himself and the *kilayim* ruling of Rabbi Yonatan, both of which deny it. And the *ma'asrot* case, which might have served as a resolution, is undermined by the asymmetry between applying *bittul* for stringency versus for leniency. The question of whether *heter* growth can negate an original *issur* through the mechanism of *bittul* — and whether such growth is truly *heter* at all or itself derives the *assur* status of its source — remains open, to be continued.
