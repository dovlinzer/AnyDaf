# Sotah 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Review & Opening

### Mincha & Megilla Review

Daf 20 continues the tractate's systematic walk through the *sotah* ceremony. The previous daf addressed the *mincha* — its composition and preparation — as well as the writing of the *megilla*: the ink, the parchment, and the requirement that it be written only after she took the *shvua*. Notably, the *mishna* itself omits any mention of the *shvua*, but the requirement emerges from other sources. The daf also treated the scraping of the *megilla* into the water.

### Drinking Order

A major debate occupied the previous daf as well: which comes first, the drinking of the water or the bringing of the *mincha*? The ambiguity arises directly from the *psukim*, which first describe the drinking and then the *mincha*, yet conclude with the phrase *v'achar yashke et ha'isha et hamayim* — "and afterward he shall make the woman drink the waters." Whatever the precise *halachic* order, it is clear that the *mishna* deliberately reserves the drinking for last, since it is the climax of the entire procedure. The dominant position, in any event, appears to be that the drinking follows the *mincha*, consistent with *v'achar yashke et ha'isha et hamayim*. With that backdrop established, the *mishna* of daf 20 now turns to the drinking itself.

## Refusal to Drink

### Before Erasure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִמְחֲקָה הַמְּגִילָּה אָמְרָה ״אֵינִי שׁוֹתָה״ — מְגִילָּתָהּ נִגְנֶזֶת, וּמִנְחָתָהּ מִתְפַּזֶּרֶת עַל הַדֶּשֶׁן, וְאֵין מְגִילָּתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה לְהַשְׁקוֹת בָּהּ סוֹטָה אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **before the scroll was erased she said: I will not drink, the scroll** that was written for **her is sequestered, and her meal-offering is** burned and **scattered over** the place of **the ashes, and her scroll is not fit to give** to **another *sota* to drink.**

The *mishna* opens with the case in which the woman refuses to drink before the *megilla* has been erased. If she says *eini shota* — "I will not drink" — she does not thereby admit guilt, but the proceedings cannot be completed through her refusal. In that case, *megillata nignenzet* — the *megilla* is placed in *gniza*. Since it bears God's name, it cannot simply be destroyed, yet it cannot be reused, as the *megilla* must be written specifically for that particular *sotah*. It is therefore stored away.

### Tzir & Wearing

Rashi explains that there was a small designated space near the walls of the *heichal* where it would be buried. Tosafot, however, cites a striking *Yerushalmi*: *megillata nignenzet tachat tziro shel heichal* — it would be placed beneath the *tzir* of the *heichal*, the hinge-socket or pivot of the door. The *Yerushalmi* adds that this was done *bishvil leshachaka* — in order to wear it down gradually. Small amounts of water would flow there and continue to dissolve the writing. This is a remarkable practice: rather than direct erasure, they arranged for the *megilla* to be worn away through *grama*, an indirect process. Why they would seek to cause it to be worn away rather than simply keep it buried is not entirely clear, but the *Yerushalmi*'s account is what it is.

### Mincha & Single Use

Similarly, the *mincha* — once it had entered a *kli sharet* — could not be redeemed or removed from the *azara*. The *mishna* states *u'minchata mitpazeret al hadeshen*: it would be scattered on the ashes, meaning burned in the *Beit hadeshen*, the section of the *azara* designated for *korbanot* that had become *pasul*. The *mishna* also states explicitly: *v'ein megillata kesheira lehashkot ba sotah acheret* — a *megilla* written for one woman cannot be used for another. This is precisely why it must be placed in *gniza* rather than reused. If she refuses to drink, she would be divorced without her *ksuva* — not because she admitted guilt, but because she is the cause of the proceedings' failure to complete.

## After Erasure

### Forced to Drink

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְחֲקָה הַמְּגִילָּה וְאָמְרָה ״אֵינִי שׁוֹתָה״ — מְעַרְעֲרִין אוֹתָהּ, וּמַשְׁקִין אוֹתָהּ בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If **the scroll was** already **erased and she said: I will not drink, she is forced to drink against her will.**

The *mishna* then turns to the case in which the *megilla* has already been erased and she then says *eini shota*. If she were to admit guilt at this point, the procedure would still be halted; but without an admission, the situation is entirely different. Now that God's name has been erased into the water, the proceedings cannot simply be abandoned at her whim. The *mishna* rules: *m'ar'arin otah u'mashkin otah al karchah* — they confuse her, agitate her, and force her to drink against her will. Some form of physical compulsion, such as a device to keep her mouth open, is implied.

### How Much Erased?

Tosafot cites an important *Yerushalmi* on this point: *kama yimachak?* — how much of the *megilla* must have been erased before we reach the threshold of compulsion? *Tani Rabbi Chanina: Beit Shammai omrim ot achat, Beit Hillel omrim shtei otiyot* — Beit Shammai holds that even one letter of erasure triggers the obligation, while Beit Hillel requires two letters. Rabbi Ila explains Beit Hillel's reasoning: the threshold is *kedei lichtov Yud Hei* — enough to have written two letters of God's name. This implies that the concern is specifically reaching God's name itself. It is worth noting that one could technically transgress the *issur mechikat hashem* even by erasing a single letter of the divine name — perhaps that is Beit Shammai's position. In any case, the *Yerushalmi* makes clear that they had to reach the point of erasing *shem Hashem* for the compulsion to apply.

## Physical Effects

### Manifestation Signs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵינָהּ מַסְפֶּקֶת לִשְׁתּוֹת עַד שֶׁפָּנֶיהָ מוֹרִיקוֹת, וְעֵינֶיהָ בּוֹלְטוֹת, וְהִיא מִתְמַלֵּאת גִּידִין. וְהֵם אוֹמְרִים: הוֹצִיאוּהָ שֶׁלֹּא תְּטַמֵּא הָעֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** When a guilty woman drinks **she does not manage to** finish **drinking before her face turns green and her eyes bulge, and her** skin becomes **full of** protruding **veins, and** the people standing in the Temple **say: Remove her,** so **that she does not render the Temple courtyard impure** by dying there.

Once she drinks — whether willingly or by compulsion — the *mishna* describes the consequences in vivid terms: *eina maspeket lishtot ad shepaneiha morikot* — almost instantaneously, her face turns green or jaundiced; *v'eineiha boltot* — her eyes bulge out; *v'hi mitmaleit gidim* — and she is filled with protrusions. Rashi explains *hagidim shebilchayeiha* — the sinews or veins of her jaw — *noffin venirin kemalei gidin*: it looks as though her entire face is swollen with distended veins. *Vegam besarah naaseit shitah shitah shel nefichah* — her flesh becomes puffed up in rows. The *Sifri* describes it as though poles are thrust into her, so she has ridges protruding from her flesh.

### Removal & Impurity

The *mishna* concludes this passage with: *v'heim omrim hotziuha, shelo tetamei ha'azara* — the attendants say, take her out, so that she does not render the *azara* impure. Notably, the *mishna* does not state that she dies. Yet the urgency of removing her strongly implies she is about to die, and the *gemara* will qualify this point further.

### Presumption Guilt

One striking feature of this *mishna* is that it does not say *im hayeta temei'ah* — "if she was impure" — or *keshehayetah temei'ah*. It simply describes these physical effects as the natural, expected outcome. The *mishna* thus speaks from a presumption of guilt, without conditional language. This is a significant editorial choice.

## Zechus Toleh

### One-Three Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם יֵשׁ לָהּ זְכוּת — הָיְתָה תּוֹלָה לָהּ. יֵשׁ זְכוּת תּוֹלָה שָׁנָה אַחַת, יֵשׁ זְכוּת תּוֹלָה שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים, יֵשׁ זְכוּת תּוֹלָה שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna limits the scope of the previous statement: **If she has merit, it delays** punishment **for her** and she does not die immediately. **There is a merit** that **delays** punishment for **one year, there is** a larger **merit** that **delays** punishment for **two years,** and **there is a merit** that **delays** punishment for **three years.**

The *mishna* continues: *im yesh lah zechut hayetah tolah lah* — if she had merit, it would hold the punishment in abeyance. The *mishna* specifies that merit can suspend the punishment for one year, two years, or three years, depending on the degree of merit. The *gemara* understands this as *bedavka* — precise — and will discuss it accordingly.

This concept of *zechut tolah* raises immediate difficulties. If women are seen going through the *sotah* procedure without apparent consequences, it undermines public confidence in the efficacy of the waters. This very concern, as we shall see, prompted one of the *tannaim* to object to the concept altogether. Nevertheless, this is the *mishna*'s position.

One might ask whether *teshuva* could forestall the punishment entirely. This depends on how one understands the nature of the procedure. If the *sotah* process is viewed as a purely divine punishment — a matter between the woman and God — then *teshuva* might logically be effective. There is a well-known responsum, likely from the *Noda Biyehudah*, addressing a parallel case: a person who is *chayav misa* and performs sincere *teshuva* before execution. The ruling is that the court carries out the sentence nonetheless. Divine forgiveness is between the person and God; the court operates by different parameters. The same principle may apply here. Yet this very distinction underscores a tension in the *sotah* procedure: to whatever degree it functions as a court-supervised process — the couple appeared before the court, the court sent her to the *Beit HaMikdash*, there are witnesses — it is not simply a private matter between the woman and God. As we will discuss at the end of the *daf*, this tension becomes increasingly significant as the *gemara* develops the concept of *zechut tolah*.

Relatedly, the concept of *zechut tolah* is very disruptive to the practical reality of the procedure. If the proceedings appear to work — husband and wife resume marital life — and then two or three years later it becomes apparent she was guilty, what does that do for the husband's understanding of what transpired? How can they resume normal life if the water has not definitively proven anything in the immediate aftermath? The *gemara* offers various explanations for when the water fails to take effect — if the husband is not *menukeh me'avon*, if she has *zechus*, and so on — but one wonders whether these explanations arose precisely in response to periods when the procedure did not appear to be functioning as intended.

### Teaching Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אוֹמֵר בֶּן עַזַּאי: חַיָּיב אָדָם לְלַמֵּד אֶת בִּתּוֹ תּוֹרָה, שֶׁאִם תִּשְׁתֶּה תֵּדַע שֶׁהַזְּכוּת תּוֹלָה לָהּ. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַמְלַמֵּד בִּתּוֹ תּוֹרָה — (כְּאִילּוּ) לוֹמְּדָהּ תִּפְלוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **From here Ben Azzai states: A person is obligated to teach his daughter Torah,** so **that if she drinks** and does not die immediately, **she will know that** some **merit** she has **delayed** punishment **for her. Rabbi Eliezer says: Anyone who teaches his daughter Torah is teaching her promiscuity [*tiflut*].**

The *mishna* draws an explicit conclusion from the concept of *zechut tolah*: *mikan omer Ben Azzai: chayav adam lelamed et bito Torah*. From here, Ben Azzai derived that a person is obligated to teach his daughter *Torah*. The reasoning is direct: if a woman drinks the water and experiences no immediate effect, she might conclude that the entire system is a fraud. However, if she has been taught *Torah* and specifically this *halacha* — that *zechut* sometimes suspends the punishment — she will understand that the absence of immediate effect does not invalidate the system. Knowledge of the system preserves faith in it.

### Ben Azzai & R. Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer disputes this directly: *kol hamelamed et bito Torah ke'ilu melamdah tiflut* — whoever teaches his daughter *Torah* is as though he taught her *tiflus*, a term with clear sexual connotations, which Birnbaum translates as promiscuity. The context makes this meaning unmistakable. Rabbi Eliezer's concern is precisely the inverse of Ben Azzai's optimism: yes, knowing how the system works produces trust in it — but it also reveals how to work around it. If she knows that *zechut* can suspend punishment, she might reason that by accumulating *zechus* in other areas of life, she can commit adultery and defer consequences indefinitely. A little knowledge, on this view, is a dangerous thing.

This raises the broader question of why the debate is framed specifically around women. The same concern could theoretically apply to men: someone who has learned *Nezikin* thoroughly can find technically permissible ways to act improperly in business. There is even a tradition recorded in the *gemara* that one who learns *Torah shelo lishma* in the sense of manipulating the system would have been better off not being created. The reason the debate focuses on women is likely twofold. First, men are already obligated in *talmud Torah*, so whatever potential negatives exist, the *mitzva* is not up for debate. What is open for debate is whether women, who are not so obligated, should be actively educated — and there one can weigh costs and benefits. Second, there may be a dimension here of how Rabbi Eliezer views women specifically: the final line of the *mishna* will make this clearer.

It is also worth noting that Ben Azzai's formulation is about preserving the woman's confidence in the system, not about enabling *zechut* to shield her. Rabbi Eliezer's concern is not that *talmud Torah* itself will give her the *zechut* to evade punishment — *zechut* only suspends, it does not fully exempt — but rather that knowing the mechanics of the system will teach her how to acquire *zechus* strategically in other ways. The distinction matters. One might also note an argument entirely absent from the *mishna*'s debate: that teaching a woman *Torah* might elevate her character and make her less inclined toward wrongdoing in the first place. The *mishna* does not entertain this possibility.

A further point: the *Rambam*, when codifying this material, uses the language *aval amru chachamim: kol hamelamed et bito Torah ke'ilu melamdah tiflut* — he frames it as rabbinic guidance rather than an outright prohibition, with the formulation *amru chachamim* suggesting policy direction rather than strict *isur*. To what degree this entire debate is a *halachic* debate, as opposed to a matter of *da'at Torah*-style communal guidance, is worth considering.

### R. Yehoshua View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: רוֹצָה אִשָּׁה בְּקַב וְתִפְלוּת, מִתִּשְׁעָה קַבִּין וּפְרִישׁוּת. הוּא הָיָה אוֹמֵר: חָסִיד שׁוֹטֶה, וְרָשָׁע עָרוּם, וְאִשָּׁה פְּרוּשָׁה, וּמַכּוֹת פְּרוּשִׁין — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מְבַלֵּי עוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says: A woman desires to** receive the amount of **a *kav*** of food **and a sexual relationship [*tiflut*]** rather **than** to receive **nine *kav*** of food **and abstinence. He would say: A foolish man of piety, and a conniving wicked person, and an abstinent woman [*perusha*], and those who injure** themselves out of false **abstinence;** all **these are** people **who erode the world.**

The *mishna* concludes this section with Rabbi Yehoshua's famous statement: *rotzah isha b'kav v'tiflut mitishah kabim u'prishut* — a woman would rather have one measure of sustenance and be able to have her sexual pleasure than nine measures with the requirement of abstinence. On this view, women are more prepared to sacrifice material wellbeing in order to maintain access to physical intimacy, and therefore cannot be trusted with knowledge that might give them theological cover for transgression.

The *mishna* then lists, in what appears to be Rabbi Yehoshua's name, figures who destroy the world: *chasid shoteh* — a foolish pietist; *rasha arum* — a crafty wicked person; *isha prushah* — a woman who is abstinent; and *makkot perushim* — the lashes of the ascetics. Rashi explains *makkot perushim* in the *gemara* as someone who strikes himself against walls to appear humble and modest — a performative false righteousness that has lost sight of genuine *frumkeit*.

The inclusion of *isha prushah* is striking. Rabbi Yehoshua has just argued that women inherently prefer physical pleasure over abstinence, and yet here he lists an abstinent woman as one who destroys the world. The implication appears to be that a woman who presents herself as *prushah* is dissembling — all women, on this view, are ultimately seeking physical intimacy, and the woman who performs abstinence is the most dangerous of all. This connects directly to the broader question that runs through the entire *masechet*: how one understands the cheating woman and where blame is located. Some threads in the *gemara* have refocused or at least included the husband in the picture; here, the lens is firmly on the woman as the locus of sexual transgression. The debate about teaching women *Torah* is not purely an educational policy discussion — it reflects fundamentally different views of what the *sotah* represents and who bears moral responsibility.

## Ink Debate

### Two Accounts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: כְּשֶׁהָיִיתִי לָמֵד תּוֹרָה אֵצֶל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הָיִיתִי מֵטִיל קַנְקַנְתּוֹם לְתוֹךְ הַדְּיוֹ, וְלֹא אָמַר לִי דָּבָר. כְּשֶׁבָּאתִי אֵצֶל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אָמַר לִי: בְּנִי מָה מְלַאכְתֶּךָ? אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: לַבְלָר אֲנִי. אָמַר לִי: בְּנִי, הֱוֵי זָהִיר, שֶׁמְּלַאכְתְּךָ מְלֶאכֶת שָׁמַיִם הִיא, שֶׁמָּא תַּחְסִיר אוֹת אַחַת אוֹ תֹּתִיר אוֹת אַחַת, נִמְצֵאתָ אַתָּה מַחְרִיב אֶת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כֻּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says in the name of Rabbi Meir: When I was studying Torah before Rabbi Akiva,** as his disciple, **I used to put iron sulfate into the ink** with which I wrote Torah scrolls, **and he did not say anything to me** in protest. Afterward, **when I came** to learn Torah **before Rabbi Yishmael, he said to me: My son, what is your vocation? I said to him: I am a scribe [*lavlar*]** who writes Torah scrolls. **He said to me: My son, be careful** in your work, **as your work is the work of Heaven, lest you omit a single letter** from the Torah scroll **or add a single letter,** and in this **you are found to be destroying the entire world** if the mistake alters the meaning of the verse and results in blasphemy.

The *gemara* now records a tradition transmitted by Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Shmuel, in the name of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir was a scribe (*lavlar*), and he reported that when he was learning *Torah* with Rabbi Akiva — *keshehaiti lomed Torah etzel Rabbi Akiva* — he would add *kankantom* to his ink, *v'lo amar li davar*: Rabbi Akiva said nothing to him. When he subsequently came to Rabbi Yishmael — *keshebati etzel Rabbi Yishmael* — Rabbi Yishmael asked him about his trade, and upon learning he was a scribe, told him: *hevei zahir shemelachtacha melachat shamayim hi* — be very careful, for your work is the work of heaven. If you add or omit even one letter — *shema tachsir ot achat o tatir ot achat* — you could destroy the entire world: *nimtzata ata machriv et ha'olam kulo*.

### Divine Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: דָּבָר אֶחָד יֵשׁ לִי שֶׁאֲנִי מֵטִיל לְתוֹךְ הַדְּיוֹ, וְקַנְקַנְתּוֹם שְׁמוֹ. אָמַר לִי: וְכִי מְטִילִין קַנְקַנְתּוֹם לְתוֹךְ הַדְּיוֹ? הַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה ״וּמָחָה״, כְּתָב שֶׁיּוּכַל לִמְחוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir continues: **I said to** Rabbi Yishmael: **I have one substance that I put into the ink, and it is called iron sulfate,** which prevents the writing from being erased. **He said to me: And may iron sulfate be put into the ink? The Torah** clearly **said** with regard to the scroll of the *sota*: "And the priest shall write these curses in a scroll, **and he shall blot** them out into the water of bitterness" (Numbers 5:23). This indicates that the Torah requires **writing that can be blotted out.**

Rashi illustrates why this is no hyperbole in terms of theological consequence: if one erases the *aleph* from *emet*, or the *heh* from *Elohim*, or adds a letter to *Bereishit* such that it reads as though something created God — one letter transforms the meaning entirely. The analogy to "let's eat grandma" versus "let's eat, grandma" captures the principle: punctuation, or in this case a single letter, saves worlds.

### Kankantom Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא מִבַּעְיָא בַּחֲסֵירוֹת וִיתֵירוֹת דְּבָקִי אֲנָא, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לְמֵיחַשׁ לִזְבוּב דְּדִילְמָא אָתֵי וְיָתֵיב אַתָּגֵיהּ דְּדָלֶית וּמָחֵיק לֵיהּ וּמְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ רֵישׁ — דָּבָר אֶחָד יֵשׁ לִי שֶׁאֲנִי מֵטִיל לְתוֹךְ הַדְּיוֹ וְקַנְקַנְתּוֹם שְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **This is what** Rabbi Meir **is saying to him: It is not necessary** to say that I do not err **in omissions and additions, as I am an expert. Rather,** there is not **even** any reason **for concern with regard to a fly lest** it **come and sit on the protrusion of** the letter ***dalet* and erase it,** thereby **rendering it** the letter ***reish*,** which could be a critical error. There is no concern of this erasure occurring, since **I have a certain substance that I put into the ink** and that prevents the writing from being erased, **and it is called iron sulfate.**

Rabbi Meir responded to Rabbi Yishmael's warning by saying: I already know the precise spellings — *debaki ana b'chasirut veyitirut* — but there is an additional concern beyond correctness at the moment of writing. Even after writing, *dilma ati veyativ akarna de'dalet* — perhaps a fly will land on the protruding corner of a *dalet* and smudge it, *u'mashvei lei reish* — turning it into a *reish*. Therefore, *davar acher yesh li she'ani matil l'toch hadyo v'kankantom shemo* — I add *kankantom* to the ink to ensure indelibility, so that no smudging can corrupt the letters after the fact.

Rabbi Yishmael's response: *vechi matilim kankantom l'toch hadyo? Ha-Torah amrah u'macha* — the *Torah* requires that the *megillat sotah* be erasable, and therefore the writing must be of a kind that can be erased. Indelible ink violates this requirement, at least for the *megillat sotah*.

## Source Contradiction

### Order Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִינִי? וְהָא תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: כְּשֶׁהָיִיתִי לָמֵד תּוֹרָה אֵצֶל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הָיִיתִי מֵטִיל קַנְקַנְתּוֹם לְתוֹךְ הַדְּיוֹ, וְלֹא אָמַר לִי דָּבָר. כְּשֶׁבָּאתִי אֵצֶל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֲסָרָהּ עָלַי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the initial part of Rabbi Meir's statement: **Is that so? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir said: When I was studying Torah before Rabbi Yishmael, I used to put iron sulfate into the ink** with which I wrote Torah scrolls, **and he did not say anything to me.** Afterward, **when I came** to learn Torah **with Rabbi Akiva, he prohibited me** from doing **so.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges the account: *v'hatanya* — we have a *braisa* that tells a different version. In that *braisa*, Rabbi Meir says it was when he was learning with Rabbi Yishmael that he used *kankantom* and Rabbi Yishmael said nothing — and it was when he came to Rabbi Akiva that Rabbi Akiva forbade it. So we have two contradictions: first, the order of apprenticeship (which teacher came first?), and second, who actually objected to the *kankantom* (Rabbi Yishmael or Rabbi Akiva?).

### Learning Sequence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* בִּשְׁלָמָא שִׁמּוּשׁ אַשִּׁמּוּשׁ לָא קַשְׁיָא: מֵעִיקָּרָא אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מָצֵי (קָם) [לְמֵיקַם] אַלִּיבֵּיהּ, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וּגְמַר גְּמָרָא. הֲדַר אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר סְבָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Granted,** the apparent contradiction between Rav Yehuda's statement with regard to Rabbi Meir's **serving** Rabbi Akiva first, and the statement of the *baraita* with regard to **serving** Rabbi Yishmael first, poses **no difficulty. Initially, he came before Rabbi Akiva** to study, but **since he could not comprehend his** extremely complicated **method** of learning, **he came before Rabbi Yishmael and learned** the oral **tradition** from him. Afterward, **he returned** and **came before Rabbi Akiva** and **studied** his method of logical **reasoning** in order to understand the reasons behind the *halakhot* he had already learned.

The *gemara* says: *bishlama shimush a-shimush lo kashya* — the apparent contradiction in order of apprenticeship can be resolved, because both sequences are true. *Me'ikara asa l'kamei d'Rabbi Akiva* — he first came to Rabbi Akiva. *Keivan d'lo matzi ka'ei alibeih* — but he could not follow what Rabbi Akiva was getting at. This is, incidentally, a remarkable statement about the future Rabbi Meir — the very sage whom no one else could fully understand — who in his youth could not keep up with Rabbi Akiva. He then went to Rabbi Yishmael and *gamar gemara* — he acquired the tradition, the foundational knowledge. Rabbi Yishmael's approach was more about transmitting tradition, while Rabbi Akiva's was more about analysis and creative reasoning. *Hadar asa l'kamei d'Rabbi Akiva savar svara* — he then returned to Rabbi Akiva and learned how to analyze and break down that foundation. So both sequences are accurate: it was Rabbi Akiva first and then Rabbi Yishmael, and also Rabbi Yishmael and then Rabbi Akiva.

### Who Forbade?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא אֲסָרָהּ אַאֲסָרָהּ קַשְׁיָא! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** the contradiction between Rav Yehuda's statement that it was Rabbi Yishmael **who prohibited** the addition of iron sulfate and the statement of the *baraita* that it was Rabbi Akiva **who prohibited it** still poses a **difficulty.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, the matter is **difficult.**

However, the contradiction about who forbade the *kankantom* cannot be resolved similarly. The *gemara* concludes: *ela asrah a-asrah kashya* — this remains a genuine difficulty, meaning the two *braisot* reflect a disagreement about what actually happened. The *gemara* leaves this as an unresolved contradiction between the sources.

## Torah Ink Rules

### Parshat Sotah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר: לַכֹּל מְטִילִין קַנְקַנְתּוֹם לְתוֹךְ הַדְּיוֹ, חוּץ מִפָּרָשַׁת סוֹטָה בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **Rabbi Meir would say: Iron sulfate may be put into the ink** that is used **for all** sacred writings, i.e., Torah scrolls, phylacteries, and *mezuzot*, **except for** the ink used to write the Torah **passage about the *sota*.**

A *braisa* then records Rabbi Yehuda's account of Rabbi Meir's ultimate position: *l'chol matilin kankantom l'toch hadyo* — for all purposes one may add *kankantom* to the ink, *chutz mi-parshat sotah bilvad* — except for *parshat sotah* alone. Rabbi Meir was unwilling to abandon his practice of using *kankantom* for scribal work generally; he accepted the restriction only for the *sotah* passage.

### Sefer vs. Mikdash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר מִשְּׁמוֹ: חוּץ מִפָּרָשַׁת סוֹטָה שֶׁל מִקְדָּשׁ. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לִמְחוֹק לָהּ מִן הַתּוֹרָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov says in the name of** Rabbi Meir: It is **except for** the ink used to write the scroll with the ***sota* passage** used **in the Temple.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between these** two opinions? The Gemara replies: **Rabbi Yirmeya says** that there is a difference **between them** with regard to whether it is permitted **to erase** the passage **for** the *sota* **from a Torah** scroll. According to Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Meir holds that this is permitted, and therefore the passage in the Torah scroll must be written with ink that does not contain iron sulfate, so that it can be erased. By contrast, according to Rabbi Ya'akov, Rabbi Meir holds that it is prohibited to erase the passage from a Torah scroll, and therefore the passage may be written with ink containing iron sulfate.

Rabbi Yaakov then refines this further in Rabbi Meir's name: *chutz mi-parshat sotah she-ba-mikdash* — the restriction applies only to the *parshat sotah* that is actually written in the *Mikdash* for the procedure itself; the section as it appears in a *Sefer Torah* does not require erasable ink.

The *gemara* asks: *mai beinaihu* — what is the practical difference between these two formulations? Rabbi Yirmiyah answers: *limchok limdinata ika beinaihu* — the debate concerns whether one may use the *parshat sotah* as written in a *Sefer Torah* for the actual *sotah* procedure. The question is thus not merely academic: can the *kohen* open a *Sefer Torah* to the relevant passage and erase it into the water, or must a dedicated *megilla* be written separately for this purpose?

Tosafot explains the underlying logic: since the *Torah* states *u'macha* — "and he shall erase" — there is an understanding that any text functioning as the *parshat sotah* must be written in a form that is capable of being erased. If the passage in a *Sefer Torah* could in theory be used for the procedure, then the *Torah* itself would demand that this passage be written in erasable ink. Conversely, if one holds that the *Sefer Torah* passage can never serve as the *megillat sotah*, there is simply no requirement that it be written with erasable ink.

This debate connects to a broader parallel about ink and *Sifrei Torah* generally. The ability to correct a *Sefer Torah* depends on the ink being erasable — it rides on the surface of the *klaf* and can be scraped away. Were the ink to be absorbed into the *klaf*, correction would be impossible. This capacity for erasure is almost a practical necessity, given that scribes do make mistakes. Similarly, a *Sefer Torah* need not be written *ksidran* — in order — which means corrections can be made without invalidating the entire scroll. The *Rambam* rules that writing with erasable ink is *mitzva min hamuvchar*, the preferred manner, but that *bedieved*, if one wrote with *kankantom* or similar indelible materials — *she-hu omed v'eino nimchak* — the scroll remains *kasher*. The practical *halacha* thus aligns with Rabbi Akiva's general principle, at least *lechatchila*.

### Megilla Reuse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי כִי הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי. דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין מְגִילָּתָהּ כְּשֵׁירָה לְהַשְׁקוֹת בָּהּ סוֹטָה אַחֶרֶת. רַבִּי אַחַי בַּר יֹאשִׁיָּה אוֹמֵר: מְגִילָּתָהּ כְּשֵׁירָה לְהַשְׁקוֹת בָּהּ סוֹטָה אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara assumes that according to Rabbi Ya'akov it is prohibited to erase the passage from a Torah scroll, since he holds that the scroll must be written for the sake of the *sota*, whereas Rabbi Yehuda, who permits this, holds that the scroll need not be written for the sake of the *sota*. **And** therefore, the opinions of **these *tanna'im* are parallel** to the opinions of **those *tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The scroll** of one *sota* **is not fit** to be used in the preparation of the water **to give** to **another *sota* to drink,** as it was not written for the sake of the other *sota*. **Rabbi Aḥai bar Yoshiya says: Her scroll is fit** to be used in the preparation of the water **to give** to **another *sota* to drink,** since it does not need to be written for the sake of the *sota*.

The *gemara* then introduces an independent debate: *ein megilla kesheira lehashkot ba sotah acheret* — a *megilla* written for one woman cannot be used to administer the waters to another woman. This is the standard position. But Rabbi Akha berabbi Yoshiya holds the contrary: *megilla kesheira lehashkot ba sotah acheret* — the same *megilla* can indeed be reused for another woman. This is a position we have not encountered until now. (One must also contend with the earlier ruling of Rava that the *megilla* cannot be written before the woman took the oath — Tosafot notes that this *gemara* appears to ignore that requirement, and raises the question of how to harmonize the two. One possibility is that both women in this scenario had already taken their oaths before the writing occurred.)

The *gemara* proposes that this debate about reuse is structurally parallel to the debate about whether the passage in a *Sefer Torah* can be used: if you cannot reuse a *megilla* written for one woman for another, perhaps the same logic prevents using the generic *Sefer Torah* text for any woman; and if you can reuse it, perhaps the generic text suffices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, דִּילְמָא לָא הִיא: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא הָתָם, אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן דְּאִינְּתִיק לְשׁוּם רָחֵל — לָא הָדְרָא מִינַּתְקָא לְשׁוּם לֵאָה, אֲבָל תּוֹרָה, דִּסְתָמָא כְּתִיבָה — הָכִי נָמֵי דְּמָחֲקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Perhaps** that **is not so,** and the two disputes are not comparable. It is possible that **the first *tanna*** of the *baraita* **states** that the scroll may not be used for another *sota* **only there,** in the case if a scroll written for a specific woman; **since it was** originally **designated in the name of** one woman, e.g., **Rachel, it cannot again be designated in the name of** another woman, e.g., **Leah. However,** in the case of **a Torah** scroll, **which is written without specifying** anyone, **indeed we may erase** the passage to prepare the water for a *sota* even though it was not written for her sake.

However, Papa then demonstrates — *dilma la hi* — that these debates need not align. The *tanna kamma*'s reason for prohibiting reuse is not about *lishmah* per se but about *keivan de'itkazai leshum Rachel*: once the *megilla* has been assigned to Rachel, it cannot be reassigned to Leah. But a *Sefer Torah* is *stama kesiva* — written generically — and was never assigned to any particular woman, so perhaps it *can* be used. Conversely, Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak argues the reverse: Rabbi Akha berabbi Yoshiya permits reuse *keivan de'ichtiv leshum alos v'alah* — because the *megilla* was written for the purpose of functioning as a curse, regardless of which woman it serves. But a *Sefer Torah* written *lilmod kesiva* — to be a *Sefer Torah* for learning — was never written for the purpose of being a curse at all, and *hacha nami delo machakinan* — one cannot use it for the *sotah* procedure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: דִּילְמָא לָא הִיא, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אַחַי בַּר יֹאשִׁיָּה הָתָם אֶלָּא בִּמְגִילָּה, דְּאִיכְּתוּב לְשׁוּם אָלוֹת בָּעוֹלָם. אֲבָל תּוֹרָה, דִּלְהִתְלַמֵּד כְּתִיבָה, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא מָחֲקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that the comparison between the two disputes can be refuted for a different reason: **Perhaps** that **is not so.** It is possible that **Rabbi Aḥai bar Yoshiya states** that the scroll may be used for another *sota* **only there, with regard to** the **scroll** written for a specific *sota*, **as it was written for the purpose of** the **curses** of a *sota* **in general. However,** in the case of **a Torah** scroll, **which is written to be learned** from, **indeed we may not erase** it for a *sota*, as it was not written for the sake of a *sota* at all.

The *gemara* thus elegantly demonstrates that there are independent considerations in each case pointing in opposite directions, and one cannot simply map one debate onto the other.

A challenge is then raised from the laws of *gittin*: if a man wrote a *get* for his wife and changed his mind, and a townsman found him and said *shmi keshimcha v'shem ishti k'shem ishtecha* — my name is the same as yours and my wife's name is the same as your wife's — may he use that *get*? The answer is clearly no: *ha-hu pasul legarsheto bo*. A *get* written for one woman is invalid for another. This would seem to support the view that a *megilla* likewise cannot be reused. The *gemara* responds that *gittin* and *sotah* are not governed by the same principles: by a *get*, the *Torah* states *v'chatav lah* — "and he shall write for her" — making *lishmah* the essential act of writing. But for *sotah*, the operative term is *v'asah lah* — and what does "doing" mean in this context? *Mechikah* — the erasing. It is the act of erasing into the water that must be done for her; the writing itself may not require the same standard of *lishmah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמְרִי: הָתָם ״וְכָתַב לָהּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, בָּעִינַן כְּתִיבָה לִשְׁמָהּ: הָכָא נָמֵי ״וְעָשָׂה לָהּ״! מַאי עֲשִׂיָּיה — מְחִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **There,** with regard to a bill of divorce, **the Merciful One states: "And he shall write for her** a bill of divorce" (Deuteronomy 24:1). This teaches that **we require the writing** to be performed **for the sake of** the specific woman. However, no similar requirement is mentioned with regard to a *sota*. The Gemara asks: **Here, too,** with regard to the *sota*, the verse states: **"And** the priest shall **perform with her** all of this law" (Numbers 5:30), indicating that the ritual must be performed for the sake of the specific woman. The Gemara answers: **What** is the **performance** referred to in the verse? It is referring to the **erasure,** whereas the writing need not be done for the sake of a specific woman.

## Fear & Niddah

### Sudden Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהֵם אוֹמְרִים הוֹצִיאוּהָ וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּדִילְמָא מֵתָה. לְמֵימְרָא דְּמֵת אָסוּר בְּמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **And** the people standing in the Temple **say: Remove her,** so that she does not render the Temple courtyard impure. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? It is **lest she die** there immediately and render the women's courtyard, where she drinks the water, impure. The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that a corpse is prohibited** from being **in** the women's courtyard, which has the same status as the **Levite camp** in the desert?

The *gemara* now returns to the question raised by the *mishna*'s statement that they say *hotziuha* — take her out of the *azara*. The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — why must she be removed? Is it because she will die, and they want to prevent a corpse from rendering the *azara* impure?

The *gemara* challenges this reasoning: a corpse is in fact permitted in *machaneh Leviyah*, as derived from the verse that Moshe took the bones of Yosef *imo b'mchitzato* — with him in his camp, and Moshe was in *machaneh Leviyah*. Furthermore, the area where the *sotah* stands — *Sha'ar Nikanor*, at the boundary between *Ezrat Nashim* and *Ezrat Yisrael* — has only the sanctity of *Ezrat Nashim*, which is *machaneh Leviyah*, not *machaneh Shechinah*. Even if she were to die there, a corpse would technically be permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָתַנְיָא: טְמֵא מֵת מוּתָּר לִיכָּנֵס לְמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה. וְלֹא טְמֵא מֵת בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ מֵת עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקַּח מֹשֶׁה אֶת עַצְמוֹת יוֹסֵף עִמּוֹ״, עִמּוֹ בִּמְחִיצָתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One who is ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse is permitted to enter the Levite camp. And** the Sages **said** this **not only** with regard to **one who is ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse; rather, even a corpse itself** may be brought into the Levite camp, **as it is stated: "And Moses took the bones of Joseph with him"** (Exodus 13:19), which is interpreted to mean: **With him, in his vicinity,** even though Moses was in the Levite camp.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שֶׁמָּא תִּפְרוֹס נִדָּה. לְמֵימְרָא דְּבִיעֲתוּתָא מְרַפְּיָא? אִין, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתִּתְחַלְחַל הַמַּלְכָּה מְאֹד״, וְאָמַר רַב: שֶׁפֵּירְסָה נִדָּה. וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: חֲרָדָה מְסַלֶּקֶת דָּמִים! פַּחְדָּא צָמֵית, בִּיעֲתוּתָא מְרַפְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The woman is removed not due to a concern that she will die there but **lest** the fear of the water cause her to **begin to menstruate,** and it is prohibited for a menstruating woman to enter the Levite camp. The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that fear causes** muscular **relaxation** and menstrual bleeding? The Gemara responds: **Yes, as it is written: "And the Queen was exceedingly pained"** (Esther 4:4), **and Rav says:** This means that **she began to menstruate.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Nidda* 39a) that **trepidation eliminates** the flow of menstrual **blood?** Presumably, the *sota* experiences trepidation. The Gemara answers: **Trepidation** generated by extended worry **contracts** the muscles and prevents the blood from flowing, but sudden **fear relaxes** the muscles and causes the blood to flow.

Abaye therefore offers a different explanation: *shema tifros niddah* — the concern is not that she will die, but that the physical trauma and shock of the experience will cause her to emit blood and become a *niddah*. A *niddah* is forbidden in *machaneh Leviyah* because *tumah yotzei migufo* — impurity that issues from the body — renders one prohibited from entering that domain. This is also why, in contemporary times, those who ascend to Har HaBayit are advised regarding their status with respect to both *ba'al keri* and *niddah*.

### Fear Types

This leads the *gemara* to ask: *l'meimra d'be'isusa marafya* — does fear cause the body to loosen and bleed? Yes: *dechtiv vatischalchal hamalka* — Esther trembled greatly — and *amar Rav shepirsah niddah* — Rav says that she became a *niddah* from the trembling.

But a difficulty arises: we have a *mishna* that rules that a woman hiding in a cave, afraid of bandits, need not fear that she has begun her period, because fear suppresses bleeding. Which is it — does fear cause bleeding or prevent it?

The *gemara* resolves this with a distinction: *pachda tzamit* — chronic anxiety, the kind that has been present for some time, causes the body to constrict and suppresses bleeding; *be'isusa marafya* — sudden shock causes the body to loosen and triggers bleeding. Rashi explains *pachda* as prolonged worry, as distinguished from the sudden seizure of fear. It is this sudden shock — *be'isuta* — that the *gemara* identifies as the concern when the woman drinks the *sotah* waters, and therefore the attendants must remove her from the *azara* before she becomes a *niddah* there.

One might note a secondary implication: a woman who wished to delay or obstruct the *sotah* proceedings could theoretically do so by ensuring she became a *niddah*, since the procedure cannot be conducted while she is in that state. Whether this would be treated analogously to simply refusing to drink — resulting in divorce without *ksuva* — is an interesting question left open.

## Suspension Durations

### Three Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* יֵשׁ לָהּ זְכוּת הָיְתָה וְכוּ׳. מַנִּי מַתְנִיתִין? לָא אַבָּא יוֹסֵי בֶּן חָנָן, וְלָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן יִצְחָק אִישׁ כְּפַר דָּרוֹם, וְלֹא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. דְּתַנְיָא: אִם יֵשׁ לָהּ זְכוּת תּוֹלָה לָהּ שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים, כְּדֵי הַכָּרַת הָעוּבָּר, דִּבְרֵי אַבָּא יוֹסֵי בֶּן חָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **If she has merit, it** delays punishment…for one year…for two years…for three years. The Gemara asks: **Whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna?** It is **not** the opinion of **Abba Yosei ben Ḥanan, and not** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Yitzḥak of Kefar Darom, and not** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael.** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **If she has merit, it delays** punishment **for her** for **three months, equivalent** to the time necessary **to recognize the fetus;** this is **the statement of Abba Yosei ben Ḥanan.**

The *gemara* asks: *man masnisin?* — which *tanna* authored our *mishna*, which states that *zechut* can suspend punishment for one, two, or three years? It turns out that at least three other *tannaim* hold positions inconsistent with this formulation. The *braisa* records: if she has merit, it suspends punishment for three months — *kedei hakcarat ha'ubar* — just long enough for a pregnancy to become visible. This is the view of Abba Yossi ben Chanan. His reading of the verse *v'niktah v'nizra'ah zera* is that *v'niktah* refers not only to the *tehorah* woman but also to the meritoriously guilty one, and the suspension lasts until *v'nizra'ah zera* — when a pregnancy would be recognizable, not when it is conceived.

### Nine Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן יִצְחָק אִישׁ כְּפַר דָּרוֹם אוֹמֵר: תִּשְׁעָה חֳדָשִׁים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִקְּתָה וְנִזְרְעָה זָרַע״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״זֶרַע יַעַבְדֶנּוּ יְסֻפַּר״, זֶרַע הָרָאוּי לְסַפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar ben Yitzḥak of Kefar Darom says:** Merit delays punishment for **nine months, as it is stated: "Then she shall be cleared, and shall conceive seed"** (Numbers 5:28). It is possible to infer from this that if she has merit she will be cleared temporarily, for the length of time required to conceive a child, **and there,** in Psalms, **it says: "A seed shall serve him; it shall be told** of the Lord unto the next generation" (Psalms 22:31). This indicates that the **seed** must **be fit to tell** of the Lord once it matures, and a child can live only if it is born after the culmination of nine months in the womb.

Rabbi Elazar ben Yitzchak ish Kfar Darom reads *v'nizra'ah zera* differently: *zera* refers to the time it takes for a child to be born. The suspension therefore lasts nine months. A secondary reading he offers draws from *zera ya'avdenu yesupar* — "the seed that serves him will be told" — i.e., *zera hara'uy lesaper*, seed mature enough to speak. This would extend the period further, but the primary position is nine months.

### Twelve Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לַדָּבָר — זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר, דִּכְתִיב: ״לָהֵן מַלְכָּא מִלְכִּי יִשְׁפַּר עֲלָיךְ וַחֲטָיָךְ בְּצִדְקָה פְרֻק וַעֲוָיָתָךְ בְּמִחַן עֲנָיִן הֵן תֶּהֱוֵי אַרְכָא לִשְׁלֵוְתָךְ״, וּכְתִיב: ״כֹּלָּא מְטָא עַל נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר מַלְכָּא״, וּכְתִיב: ״לִקְצָת יַרְחִין תְּרֵי עֲשַׂר״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael says:** Merit delays punishment for **twelve months. And although there is no** explicit **proof for the concept** of merit delaying punishment for twelve months, there is **an allusion to the concept, as it is written** that Daniel said to Nebuchadnezzar after interpreting Nebuchadnezzar's dream concerning the evil which would befall him: **"Therefore, O king, let my counsel be acceptable to you, and redeem your sins with charity, and your iniquities by showing mercy to the poor; and then there shall be an extension to your tranquility"** (Daniel 4:24). **And it is written: "All this came upon King Nebuchadnezzar"** (Daniel 4:25), **and it is written** in the following verse that this occurred: **"At the end of twelve months"** (Daniel 4:26).

Rabbi Yishmael holds: *shneim asar chodesh* — twelve months. Even though there is no direct *rava* for this, there is a *zecher ladavar* — a hint — from the story of Daniel and Nebuchadnezzar. Daniel advised Nebuchadnezzar: *lahen malka malki yishpar alach, v'chatayach b'tzedaka ferok, va'avayach bemichan inayin* — redeem your sins through righteousness, your iniquities through generosity to the poor — and Nebuchadnezzar's well-being would be extended. The verse then records: *u'liktzas yarchin terei asar* — after twelve months, all these things came upon Nebuchadnezzar. He had followed Daniel's advice, performed righteous acts, and thereby postponed the divine punishment by a full year. So merit forestalls punishment for twelve months.

### Three Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְאַשְׁכַּח קְרָא דְּאָמַר וְתָנֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה פִּשְׁעֵי אֱדוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** who states that merit delays punishment for one year, **and he found a verse which states and repeats** the possibility that punishment can be delayed, indicating that merit can delay punishment up to three times, **as it is written: "Thus says the Lord: For three transgressions of Edom,** yes, but for four, I will not reverse it" (Amos 1:11). Punishment can therefore be delayed for three consecutive periods of one year.

None of these positions, however, reaches three years. The *gemara* resolves this by saying that the *mishna* indeed follows Rabbi Yishmael's twelve-month view, but adds a multiplier from the verse: *ko amar Hashem: al shelosha pish'ei Edom v'al arba'ah lo ashivenu* — "for three transgressions of Edom and for four I will not reverse it." The verse implies that for three sins, God holds back punishment; the model of one year per instance is multiplied threefold, yielding three years. Even though this is only a *zecher ladavar* rather than a definitive proof, it accounts for the *mishna*'s formulation.

## Divine vs. Court

### Model Shift

The final movement of the *daf*'s discussion marks a significant conceptual shift. As the *gemara* elaborates on *zechus toleh*, it moves away from the specific dynamics of the *sotah* procedure — the *v'niktah v'nizra'ah zera* of the relevant *parshat sotah* — and toward a much more generic model: any person who deserves divine punishment may have that punishment forestalled by merit. The proof from Nebuchadnezzar's twelve-month reprieve is the clearest illustration: Nebuchadnezzar is not a *sotah*, not subject to any court proceeding, not even Jewish — yet the same principle of merit suspending divine punishment applies to him.

### God vs. Court

This shift has a positive dimension: it normalizes the *sotah* within a broader theology of divine justice and human merit. The guilty woman is not being treated as a unique category of sinner with her own special divine mechanism; she is simply a person whose deserved punishment can be forestalled, just like anyone else. This arguably *downplays* the hyperfocus on the adulterous woman as an exceptional figure.

At the same time, this shift pulls in a direction that complicates the court-supervised nature of the entire proceeding. If *zechut tolah* is understood as simply a matter of divine punishment operating between the woman and God — forestalled by merit, regardless of *teshuva* or any court action — then the entire procedure begins to look less like a legal determination and more like an occasion for divine intervention. As was pointed out in the earlier discussion of *teshuva*: the more one sees *zechus toleh* through this lens of purely divine punishment, the more it becomes a matter between her and God alone, with the court serving only as the context in which God acts — not as the body that determines her fate. The question of how this interacts with the husband's ability to resume marital relations, and whether the procedure has genuinely "proven" anything if the water does not take immediate effect, remains a deep tension within the entire *masechet* — one that the subsequent *dafim* will continue to address.
