# Shabbat 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Health & Hygiene

### Rav Huna & Rabba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא שְׁכִיחַתְּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, דִּמְחַדְּדָן שְׁמַעְתָּתֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי אֵיזִיל לְגַבֵּיהּ? דְּכִי אָזֵילְנָא לְגַבֵּיהּ מוֹתִיב לִי בְּמִילֵּי דְעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said to his son Rabba: What is the reason that you are not** to be **found** among those who study **before Rav Ḥisda, whose *halakhot* are incisive?** Rabba **said to him:** For **what** purpose **should I go to him? When I go to him, he sits me down** and occupies me **in mundane matters** not related to Torah.

The daf opens three lines from the top of 82a with a striking exchange between Rav Huna and his son Rabba. Rav Huna asks: *"Mai tama lo shachichat kammei de-Rav Chisda?"* — why are you not frequenting the presence of Rav Chisda? His Torah is sharp; you have much to learn from him.

### Mundane vs. Essential

Rabba demurs. He explains that whenever he visits Rav Chisda, the latter engages him in *milei de-alma* — mundane, everyday matters rather than Torah. The example Rabba gives is medical advice about bathroom habits, which he dismisses as beneath the level of discussion he seeks.

### Pelvic Anatomy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לִי: מַאן דְּעָיֵיל לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא לָא לִיתֵּיב בְּהֶדְיָא, וְלָא לִיטְרַח טְפֵי — דְּהַאי כַּרְכַּשְׁתָּא אַתְּלָת שִׁינֵּי יָתֵיב, דִילְמָא מִשְׁתַּמְטִי שִׁינֵּי דְכַרְכַּשְׁתָּא וְאָתֵי לִידֵי סַכָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** For example, **he said to me: One who enters a bathroom should not sit down immediately and should not exert** himself **excessively** because **the rectum rests upon three teeth,** the muscles that hold it in place, and there is concern **lest the teeth of** the **rectum dislocate** through exertion **and he come to danger.**

What precisely does Rav Chisda teach? *"Man de-ayil le-vei kisei"* — a person entering the bathroom should neither sit down suddenly nor exert himself excessively, because the rectum sits upon three muscles (*karkashta atlas shina yasiv*). If one pushes too hard or sits too abruptly, one of these muscles may become displaced, leading to genuine physical danger.

### Pikuach Nefesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הוּא עָסֵיק בְּחַיֵּי דִּבְרִיָּיתָא וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ בְּמִילֵּי דְעָלְמָא?! כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן זִיל לְגַבֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna **said** to his son Rabba: **He is dealing with** matters crucial to **human life, and you say** that he is dealing **with mundane matters?** Now that I know what you meant, **all the more so go before him.**

Rav Huna's response is immediate and decisive: *"Hu asik be-chayei de-briyata"* — Rav Chisda is occupying himself with the lives of God's creatures. How can this be called a mundane matter? On the contrary, *kol sheken* — all the more reason to go and learn from him. The Gemara does not record whether Rabba ultimately heeded his father, but the message is clear: concern for human health is not peripheral to Torah — it is central to it.

## Rock vs. Pottery

### Options Presented

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ לְפָנָיו צְרוֹר וָחֶרֶס, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: מְקַנֵּחַ בַּצְּרוֹר וְאֵין מְקַנֵּחַ בַּחֶרֶס, וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: מְקַנֵּחַ בַּחֶרֶס וְאֵין מְקַנֵּחַ בַּצְּרוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss these *halakhot*. **Rav Huna said:** One who relieves himself and needs to wipe and **has before him a stone and an earthenware** shard, **wipes with the stone and does not wipe with the earthenware** shard, since he might injure himself. **And Rav Ḥisda said: He wipes with the earthenware** shard **and does not wipe with the stone,** which is set-aside.

The discussion turns back to the *halacha* context established in recent dapim: on *Shabbos*, when one needs to relieve oneself and has before him both a rock and a piece of pottery, which should be used? The rock carries a *muktzeh* concern; the pottery carries safety concerns — it may be more abrasive or, as discussed previously, may make one susceptible to witchcraft.

### Rav Huna's View

Rav Huna rules: *"Mekaneach be-tzror ve-ein mekaneach be-cheres"* — use the rock and do not use the pottery. His reasoning, as established in the prior discussion, is the danger associated with pottery, whether from abrasion or from the witchcraft concern. The *muktzeh* concern yields before the danger concern.

### Rav Chisda's View

Rav Chisda takes the opposing position: *"Mekaneach be-cheres ve-ein mekaneach be-tzror"* — use the pottery and avoid the rock. He is less concerned with the safety issues and more attentive to the *muktzeh* dimension, preferring the option that presents fewer *Shabbos*-related complications.

### Baraita & Handles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיוּ לְפָנָיו צְרוֹר וָחֶרֶס — מְקַנֵּחַ בַּחֶרֶס וְאֵין מְקַנֵּחַ בַּצְּרוֹר, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרַב הוּנָא! תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַפְרָם בַּר פָּפָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְרַב הוּנָא: בְּאוֹגְנֵי כֵּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **If one had before him a stone and an earthenware** shard, **he wipes with the earthenware** shard **and does not wipe with the stone.** That is a **conclusive refutation** of the opinion of **Rav Huna. Rafram bar Pappa explained it before Rav Ḥisda in accordance** with the opinion **of Rav Huna:** It is not referring to earthenware shards, but to **the** smooth **rims of vessels,** which pose no danger.

A *baraita* is then cited: *"Hayu lefanav tzror ve-cheres, mekaneach be-cheres ve-ein mekaneach be-tzror"* — use the pottery, not the rock. This appears to support Rav Chisda and contradict Rav Huna. The Gemara registers this as a *tiyuvta* against Rav Huna's position. Rafram bar Pappa, however, resolves the difficulty in the presence of Rav Chisda himself: the *baraita* is speaking specifically of *ugnei keilim* — the handle of a vessel. A handle is smooth and narrow, lacking the sharpness of a broken shard, and can therefore be used without the danger that motivated Rav Huna's ruling in the first place. The apparent contradiction thus dissolves.

It is worth noting that the concern about pottery pulling out hairs — which might implicate the *melacha* of *toleish* — is not pressed at this stage, as the act would constitute a *davar she'eino mitkaven* that is not a *psik reisha*, and the Gemara appears unconcerned with it here.

## Rock vs. Grass

### Two Choices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ לְפָנָיו צְרוֹר וַעֲשָׂבִים, רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַב הַמְנוּנָא, חַד אָמַר: מְקַנֵּחַ בַּצְּרוֹר וְאֵין מְקַנֵּחַ בָּעֲשָׂבִים, וְחַד אָמַר: מְקַנֵּחַ בָּעֲשָׂבִים וְאֵין מְקַנֵּחַ בַּצְּרוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **There were before him a stone and grasses.** With regard to the preferred method to wipe on Shabbat, what is the ruling? There is a dispute between **Rav Ḥisda and Rav Hamnuna. One said: He wipes with the stone and does not wipe with the grasses; and one said: He wipes with the grasses and does not wipe with the stone.**

The Gemara now considers a parallel scenario: one must choose between a rock and grass. Both options present *Shabbos* complications. The rock is *muktzeh*; the grass raises the concern of *toleish* — uprooting a plant, a prohibited *melacha*.

### Attached Grass

Rashi resolves the *toleish* difficulty by explaining that the opinion permitting grass is speaking of grass that remains attached to the ground. One wipes oneself on the grass without pulling it up. This also ameliorates the *muktzeh* concern: since the grass has not been moved from its place, the *muktzeh* prohibition does not fully apply. The approach is not without complexity — questions arise as to whether moving an object that remains rooted constitutes *muktzeh* — but this is Rashi's resolution. Alternatively, the concern about grass may be entirely independent of *Shabbos* and rooted instead in the danger of abrasion or the flammability concern to be discussed momentarily.

### Rav Chisda vs. Hamnuna

The question of which to prefer — rock or grass — is the subject of a debate between Rav Chisda and Rav Hamnuna. One holds *"mekaneach be-tzror ve-ein mekaneach ba-asavim"* — use the rock, not the grass; the other holds the reverse, preferring grass to rock, either because the rock presents a greater *muktzeh* concern or because of the dangers associated with grass.

### Flammable Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַמְקַנֵּחַ בְּדָבָר שֶׁהָאוּר שׁוֹלֶטֶת בּוֹ שִׁינָּיו הַתַּחְתּוֹנוֹת נוֹשְׁרוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **One who wipes with something flammable, his lower teeth,** which hold the intestines in place, **fall out.**

A *baraita* challenges this: *"Ha-mekaneach be-davar she-ha-or sholet bo, shinav ha-tachtonot noshrot"* — one who wipes with something flammable will suffer damage to the lower teeth, understood to refer to the internal muscles or sphincter of the anus. The precise anatomical referent is uncertain, but the concern is clear: flammable materials cause damage to the body's interior. Furthermore, as noted from other sources, there is an additional concern that flammable materials make one susceptible to witchcraft. It is worth noting that the *Yerushalmi*, as cited by one of the *rishonim*, takes the opposite position from the *Bavli* on this point — recommending flammable rather than non-flammable material — though the *Bavli*'s position governs here.

### Moist vs. Dry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּלַחִין, הָא בִּיבֵשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult: This,** where it is permitted, is referring **to moist** grass; **that,** where it is prohibited, is referring **to dry** grass.

The Gemara resolves the tension with a clean distinction: *"Lo kashya — ha be-lachim, ha be-yevashim"* — there is no difficulty; the prohibition concerns dry grass, which is flammable, while moist grass is permissible, as it does not readily burn. The opinion permitting grass thus refers specifically to moist grass. The practical corollary — that this reasoning would render toilet paper problematic — is left implicit in the Gemara.

## Digestive Health

### Constipation Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנִּצְרָךְ לִפָּנוֹת וְאֵינוֹ נִפְנֶה, רַב חִסְדָּא וְרָבִינָא, חַד אָמַר: רוּחַ רָעָה שׁוֹלֶטֶת בּוֹ, וְחַד אָמַר: רוּחַ זוּהֲמָא שׁוֹלֶטֶת בּוֹ. תַּנְיָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר רוּחַ זוּהֲמָא שׁוֹלֶטֶת בּוֹ, דְּתַנְיָא: הַנִּצְרָךְ לִנְקָבָיו וְאוֹכֵל — דּוֹמֶה לְתַנּוּר שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהוּ עַל גַּב אֶפְרוֹ, וְזוֹ הִיא תְּחִלַּת רוּחַ זוּהֲמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss this topic. With regard to **one who needs to defecate and does not do so,** there is a dispute between **Rav Ḥisda and Ravina. One said: An evil spirit dominates him; and one said: An odor of filth dominates him. It was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with the one who said** that **an odor of filth dominates him, as it was taught: One who needs to defecate and eats is comparable to an oven that was heated on top of its ashes; and that is the onset of an odor of filth.**

The Gemara now addresses a related physiological concern: *"Ha-nitzrach le-panot ve-eino nifneh"* — what if a person needs to relieve himself but cannot? Rav Chisda and Ravina debate the consequence of eating while still needing to relieve oneself. One holds it causes *ruach ra'ah* — bad breath; the other holds it causes *ruach zu'ama* — general body odor affecting the entire body. A *baraita* supports the latter view, comparing one who eats before relieving himself to lighting an oven over its accumulated ashes: the waste of what was previously consumed must be cleared before new fuel is introduced efficiently. Eating atop an uncleared interior produces the beginning of bodily stench.

### Stand-Sit Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּצְרַךְ לִיפָּנוֹת וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִיפָּנוֹת, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: יַעֲמוֹד וְיֵשֵׁב, יַעֲמוֹד וְיֵשֵׁב. רַב חָנָן מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא אָמַר: יִסְתַּלֵּק לִצְדָדִין. רַב הַמְנוּנָא אָמַר: יְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בִּצְרוֹר בְּאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם. וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: יַסִּיחַ דַּעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the issue: **One who needed to defecate and is unable to do so, Rav Ḥisda said: He should stand and sit, stand and sit. Rav Ḥanan from Neharde'a said: He should move to the sides** and attempt to relieve himself in a different spot. **Rav Hamnuna said: He should manipulate with a stone in that place. And the Rabbis said: He should divert his thoughts** to other matters.

The more pressing question is practical: if one needs to relieve himself and cannot, what should be done? Rav Chisda prescribes: *"Ya'amod ve-yeshev, ya'amod ve-yeshev"* — stand and sit, stand and sit repeatedly. This is not mere calisthenics; the repeated motion engages the muscles above the intestines, causing them to move the intestinal contents and facilitating relief.

### Location Change

Rav Acha of Difti suggests: *"Yistalek le-tzdadim"* — change location. Moving to a different spot, which itself involves rising and resettling, may accomplish a similar result by disrupting the body's current position and stimulating movement.

### Stimulation Method

Rav Hamnuna recommends: *"Yemashmesh be-tzror be-oto makom"* — stimulate the relevant area externally with a rock. This use of a rock goes beyond its function as a wiping implement; it stimulates the nerve endings of the area, functioning somewhat as a suppository might — not by insertion but by external stimulation — and thereby facilitates relief.

### Distraction Method

The *rabbanan* suggest: *"Yassiach da'ato"* — distract the mind. Think about something else entirely. This is the talmudic precedent for having reading material in the bathroom.

### Mental Focus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּכִי מַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ לָא מִפְּנֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יַסִּיחַ דַּעְתּוֹ מִדְּבָרִים אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: All the more so that when he diverts his thoughts he will not** be able to **defecate.** Rav Ashi **said to him: He should divert his thoughts from other matters,** and focus exclusively on his effort to relieve himself.

Rav Acha son of Rava challenges Rav Ashi: surely mental distraction would be *counter*productive — if one's mind is elsewhere, how will the body perform what is needed? Rav Ashi clarifies: the intent is *massiach da'ato mi-devarim acherim* — to remove attention from everything *else* and focus entirely on the matter at hand. Whether the Gemara earlier genuinely meant distraction in the opposite sense remains somewhat open, but the final word here favors concentration over diversion.

### Practical Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי: לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי הַהוּא טַיָּיעָא דְּקָם וְיָתֵיב וְקָם וְיָתֵיב עַד דְּשָׁפֵךְ כְּקִדְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yirmeya from Difti said: I saw a certain Arab who stood and sat, stood and sat, until** it **poured** out of him **like a pot.** Apparently, that advice is effective.

Rav Yirmiyah of Difti offers empirical testimony: *"Ledidi chazi li hahu taya"* — I myself observed an Arab nomad who, when needing to relieve himself, would stand and sit, stand and sit repeatedly, *"ad de-shafich ke-kedeira"* — until he emptied himself like a pot. The observation validates Rav Chisda's stand-sit method in practice.

### Pre-Meal Protocol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַנִּכְנָס לִסְעוּדַת קֶבַע, יְהַלֵּךְ עֶשֶׂר פְּעָמִים שֶׁל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: אַרְבַּע פְּעָמִים שֶׁל עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת, וְנִפְנֶה, וְנִכְנָס וְיֹשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who wishes to enter** and partake of **a regular meal** that will last for some time, **should pace** a distance of **four cubits ten times, and some say, ten cubits four times,** in order to expedite the movement of the bowels, **and defecate, and enter, and sit in his place.**

The Gemara concludes this section with a *baraita* addressing preparation before a formal meal (*se'udat keva*). Since it would be both unhealthy and embarrassing to need to leave mid-meal to relieve oneself, one should take preparatory walks beforehand. One opinion: walk ten times, four *amot* each, then attempt to relieve oneself before entering. Another opinion: four walks of ten *amot* each. Rashi explains that each pause involves checking whether relief is possible, while the plain reading of the Gemara suggests one simply walks the prescribed distance and then makes the attempt before sitting down to eat.

## Pottery Sizes

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חֶרֶס — כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן בֵּין פַּצִּים לַחֲבֵרוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי לַחְתּוֹת בּוֹ אֶת הָאוּר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי לְקַבֵּל בּוֹ רְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One who carries out a shard of **earthenware** on Shabbat is liable if it is in a measure **equivalent** to that which is used **to place between one pillar and another** when piled on the ground to separate them; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says:** In a measure **equivalent** to that which is used **to stoke a fire with it. Rabbi Yosei says:** In a measure **equivalent** to that which is used **to hold a quarter** of a *log* **in it.**

The daf now concludes the *perek* with its final *Mishnah*, which concerns the minimum sizes of pottery shards for purposes of carrying on *Shabbos*. Rabbi Yehuda defines the minimum functional size as that which is sufficient to place between beams (*patzim*): wooden beams laid in construction have small gaps that cause them to shift; a shard inserted to fill such a gap serves a real purpose, and that is Rabbi Yehuda's measure.

### Rabbi Meir

Rabbi Meir sets a different standard: a shard large enough to serve as a poker for stirring fire — *kedei lachtot bo et ha-or*. One takes a piece of pottery and uses it to move around coals or burning wood in a hearth. This defines a usable size.

### Rabbi Yossi

Rabbi Yossi requires a more substantial piece: *kedei lekabel bo revi'it* — large enough to hold a *revi'it* of liquid. This is not merely a flat chip of pottery; it must be curved and shaped so as to actually contain liquid. This measure is thus more restrictive than the others in terms of minimum size.

### Biblical Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לַדָּבָר, זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר: ״לֹא יִמָּצֵא בִמְכִתָּתוֹ חֶרֶשׂ לַחְתּוֹת אֵשׁ מִיָּקוּד״. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה? ״וְלַחְשׂוֹף מַיִם מִגֶּבֶא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir said: Although there is no proof for the matter,** there is a biblical **allusion to** my opinion, as it is stated: "And He shall break it as a potter's vessel is broken, smashing it without sparing; **and there shall not be found among its pieces a shard to rake fire on the hearth"** (Isaiah 30:14). **Rabbi Yosei said to him:** Is there **proof from there?** The verse concludes: **"And to extract water from the cistern,"** indicating that earthenware is significant if it is large enough to hold water.

Rabbi Meir invokes a verse to support his measure, prefacing it with the characteristically modest formula: *"Af al pi she-ein raya la-davar, zecher la-davar"* — there is no absolute proof from this, but there is a scriptural hint. The verse is from Yeshayahu (chapter 30): *"Lo yimatzei bi-mchitato cheres lachtot eish miyaked"* — when it is shattered, there will not be found even a shard of pottery to stir a burning fire. This appears in a *kelalah* — a prophecy of devastation — warning that even this minimal, everyday use of pottery will be unavailable. For Rabbi Meir, this confirms that stirring fire is indeed the classic functional use of a pottery shard.

### Counter-Proof

Rabbi Yossi counters immediately: *"Misham raya?"* — is that a proof? Look at the very end of that same verse: *"Ve-lachsof mayim mi-geva"* — nor will there be found a shard to draw water from a pit. Pottery is equally used for drawing water, and that function requires a vessel capable of holding liquid — a larger, shaped shard. Rabbi Yossi thus uses the same verse to support his own more demanding measure.

### Comparative Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבִּי מֵאִיר נְפִישׁ, אוֹ שִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי נְפִישׁ? מִסְּבָרָא — שִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי נְפִישׁ, וּמִקְּרָא — שִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבִּי מֵאִיר נְפִישׁ. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ שִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי נְפִישׁ, לָיֵיט לַהּ בְּמָנָא זוּטְרָא, וַהֲדַר לָיֵיט לַהּ בְּמָנָא רַבָּה?! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי לַחְתּוֹת אֵשׁ מִיקִידָה גְּדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is **the measure** stated by **Rabbi Meir greater, or** is **the measure** stated by **Rabbi Yosei greater?** The Gemara responds: **It is reasonable** to say that **the measure** of **Rabbi Yosei is greater; however,** based on the **verse** it appears that **the measure** of **Rabbi Meir is greater. As, if it enters your mind** to say that **the measure** of **Rabbi Yosei** with regard to the shard of earthenware **is greater,** would the prophet first **curse him** by saying that **a small vessel** will not be found, **and then curse him** by saying that **a larger vessel** will not be found? **Abaye said: The mishna is also** referring to a large shard of earthenware required **to stoke the fire of a large conflagration.** Even in the mishna, Rabbi Meir's measure is larger.

The Gemara now poses a pointed question: *"Shi'ura de-Rabbi Meir nafish o shi'ura de-Rabbi Yossi nafish?"* — which measure is actually larger, Rabbi Meir's or Rabbi Yossi's? Logically, it seems obvious: drawing water requires a curved, containing shard, while stirring fire requires merely something firm enough to serve as a poker. Rabbi Yossi's measure should be larger.

Yet the sequence of the verse seems to suggest otherwise. The verse first mentions the fire-stirring shard and only afterwards the water-drawing shard. If Rabbi Yossi's measure were larger, the verse would be moving from a larger item to a smaller one — saying you won't find even the bigger thing, and then saying you also won't find the smaller thing. But curses and laments in *Tanach* typically proceed from the more significant deprivation to the less significant — from what is harder to believe to what follows from it. If the fire-stirring shard came first in the verse, logic suggests it is the more impressive or larger item.

The Gemara acknowledges, however, that biblical poetry is not always climactic in structure. There are verses that move in an anti-climactic direction — *"yarutzu ve-lo yigau, yelchu ve-lo yi-afu"* — first the greater exertion and then the lesser. The Gemara does not ultimately invoke this counter-principle here, leaving the textual argument in favor of Rabbi Meir's measure being larger. Abaye resolves the difficulty by suggesting that the Mishnah's fire-stirring refers specifically to a large fire (*eish mikida gedola*), for which a large piece of pottery is needed to avoid burning one's hand, while Rabbi Meir in practice may intend even a small shard for a small fire.

### Primary & Secondary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה. שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר! וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר ״לָא מִיבַּעְיָא״ קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא מִידֵּי דַּחֲשִׁיב לְאִינָשֵׁי דְּלָא לִישְׁתְּכַח לֵיהּ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ מִידֵּי דְּלָא חֲשִׁיב לְאִינָשֵׁי לָא לִישְׁתְּכַח לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **Rabbi Yosei said to him:** Is there **proof from there?** He cites proof for his opinion from the conclusion of that same verse. The Gemara comments: **Rabbi Yosei spoke well to Rabbi Meir. And** how does **Rabbi Meir** address that proof? He explains that the verse **is stated** employing the style of: **There is no need.** It should be understood as follows: **There is no need** to say that **an item** that is **significant to people,** e.g., a large shard of earthenware to stoke a fire, **shall not be found, but even an item** that is **insignificant to people,** i.e., a shard to extract water, **shall not be found.** Therefore, the conclusion of the verse does not contradict Rabbi Meir's opinion.

Rabbi Meir responds to Rabbi Yossi's counter-argument with the principle of *lo mibeya ka'amar* — the verse is structured as a *kal va-chomer* embedded in a curse: it goes without saying (*lo mibeya*) that even something important and primary — namely, a shard for stirring fire, the classic use of broken pottery — will not be found. But even the secondary, less important function — drawing water — will also be unavailable. The verse thus moves from the primary to the secondary use, not necessarily from larger to smaller in physical terms. Rabbi Meir's position remains that the fire-stirring size is the defining measure, and the fire-stirring function is the primary one. Rabbi Yossi's point is absorbed, but Rabbi Meir insists that the structural logic of the curse supports his framing: stirring the fire is the main purpose, and water-drawing is the subordinate one. With *"Hadran alach ha-motzi yayin,"* the *perek* formally concludes.

It is worth pausing to note how much of *Masechet Shabbos* is devoted to the laws of carrying. The very first *Mishnah* concerns carrying; several subsequent chapters address what animals and people may wear; and now multiple chapters have catalogued minimum sizes for carrying different substances. Beyond this, an entire *Masechet Eruvin* addresses carrying further. The primary *halachic* relevance of these minimum sizes is narrow — they bear chiefly on the obligation to bring a *chatas* offering for inadvertent violation, a question relevant only when the *Beis ha-Mikdash* stands. In contemporary terms, they may touch on the boundary between *de-oraita* and *de-rabbanan* in edge cases involving illness or necessity. Yet the sheer volume of discussion devoted to this single *melacha* is, on any accounting, striking.

## Avodah Zarah Intro

### Interlude Purpose

What follows is an interlude *perek* — a full chapter inserted here not because its content is directly continuous with the laws of carrying, but because of a literary connection. The final *Mishnah* of the preceding *perek* concluded by quoting a verse from Yeshayahu, noting that a citation from *Nach* constitutes a *zecher la-davar* rather than a *raya*. The new *perek* opens with a *halacha* derived from a verse in the same chapter of Yeshayahu, creating the textual hook that anchors it here. Rashi suggests the entire *perek* is placed here because it contains one *Mishnah* about *bris milah* on *Shabbos*. Tosafot disagrees: *bris milah* is not the subject of the current unit of discussion and does not recur for many chapters. For Tosafot, the sole reason for this *perek*'s placement is the connection to the Yeshayahu verses cited just prior.

### Derivation Method

The *perek* is structurally unified by its method: it consists of a series of *halachot* introduced by *"minayin le-X"* — from where do we know that X is the case — followed by a verse from *Nach* as support. Normally, *halachic* derivations draw on verses from the *Torah*. What distinguishes this collection is that the proofs are drawn from *Nach* — from the prophets and writings — and from verses that are not *prima facie* about *halacha* at all, yet are applied to *halachic* conclusions. This type of collection, organized by structural similarity rather than unified subject matter, resembles other early *mishnaic* collections such as the *ein bein* series in *Megillah*, and likely represents an early stratum of *mishnaic* compilation. The first named authority is Rabbi Akiva, who is relatively late in the tannaitic period; the later anonymous *mishnayot* in the chapter may be of considerably earlier vintage.

## Avodah Zarah & Niddah

### Akiva's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִנַּיִין לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא כְּנִדָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תִּזְרֵם כְּמוֹ דָוָה צֵא תֹּאמַר לוֹ״ — מָה נִדָּה מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא, אַף עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Akiva said: From where** is it derived **that idolatry, e.g.,** a statue of a deity**, transmits impurity** imparted **by carrying** even when the person who carries it does not come into contact with it**, just as** a **menstruating woman** does? **As it is stated: "**And you will defile the silver overlays of your statues**,** and the golden plating of your idols**, you will cast them away as you would a menstruating woman [*dava*], you will tell it, get out"** (Isaiah 30:22). **Just as a menstruating woman transmits impurity** imparted **by carrying, so too, idolatry transmits impurity** imparted **by carrying.**

Rabbi Akiva opens: *"Minayin le-avodah zarah she-metamei be-massa ke-niddah?"* — from where do we know that *avodah zarah* causes *tumah* through carrying (*massa*), like a *niddah*?

This question requires some unpacking. The *Torah* never states explicitly that *avodah zarah* is *tamei*. It prohibits deriving benefit from it; it commands its destruction; but it does not assign it *tumah* in any explicit passage. The entire category of *tumat avodah zarah* is therefore, in all probability, rabbinic in origin. The further question — whether this *tumah* extends to *massa*, the mode of transmission associated with a *niddah* or *zav*, where one becomes *tamei* simply by carrying the *tamei* person or object without direct contact — is thus a question about the scope of a rabbinic enactment, supported not by a *Torah* verse but by a verse from *Nach*.

### Isaiah Comparison

The proof text is from Yeshayahu 30:22: *"Tizrem ke-mo davah"* — scatter them, cast them out, like a *davah*, a woman with a flow, a *niddah*. The verse commands Israel to treat their idols as one treats a *niddah* — with complete distancing and casting away.

### Logical Inference

Rabbi Akiva reasons: *"Mah niddah metameh be-massa — af avodah zarah metameh be-massa"* — just as a *niddah* causes *tumah* through *massa* (without direct contact), so too does *avodah zarah* cause *tumah* through *massa*. The comparison in the verse is taken as a legal analogy, not merely a rhetorical flourish.

### Niddah Taboos

The verse's use of the term *davah* — related to *doh*, flow — is the same term appearing in the *Torah*'s description of a *niddah*. What is striking, however, is not merely the legal derivation but the broader cultural backdrop. In *Tanach*, particularly in the prophetic literature, the *niddah* serves not only as a technical category of *tumah* but as a symbol of something to be radically distanced, even expelled. *"K'tumat ha-niddah richaktanu"* — which appears in *Tachanun* — captures this sense. The Ramban notes that menstrual taboos were widespread across the ancient world, and the strategic use Rachel made of her claimed *niddah* status to drive Lavan from her tent (*"lo uchal lakum mipanecha ki derech nashim li"*) suggests that in the cultural context, the claim was effective precisely because it invoked such distancing norms.

Within *halacha*, however, the Gemara's treatment of *hilchot niddah* is notably stripped of this taboo dimension. Reading through *Masechet Niddah*, one encounters *tumah* law, the prohibition of marital relations, and the *harchakot* aimed at preventing inadvertent violation — but nowhere does the Gemara invest menstruation with an aura of danger or contamination beyond the *halachic* category. When the Gemara discusses the *harchakot* — the distancing practices between husband and wife during *niddah* — it frames the concern in the most neutral terms: will one thing lead to another, in the same way one asks whether serving meat and milk at the same table will lead to mixing them? The analogy is almost deflating in its normalcy. Whatever taboo attitudes were circulating in the surrounding culture, the Gemara's legal discourse largely brackets them.

That said, these attitudes did surface in certain contexts. Among Ashkenazic communities in the medieval period, practices emerged — not as binding *halacha* but as custom — according to which women who were *niddot* would not enter the synagogue, would not recite *brachot*, or would not look at a *Sefer Torah*; faint echoes of this appear even in the Rema. Ethiopian Jewry, when first encountered in modern times, maintained a practice of women residing in separate quarters during *niddah*. The Samaritans maintain strict *niddah* separation to this day. These practices reflect the broader ancient reality the Ramban describes — and it is precisely against that backdrop that the Gemara's silence on the taboo dimension is itself a significant halachic statement.

The practical consequence for *kohanim* eating *terumah*, however, deserves note. Since anything a *niddah* sits on becomes an *av ha-tumah*, and a *kohen* eating *terumah* must maintain *tahara*, within a *kohen*'s household the *niddah* status of his wife created genuine, pervasive practical complications — not from taboo, but from sheer *halachic* logistics. Any surface she sat on, any bed they shared, became *tamei* at the highest level. For such a household, effective separation would have been practically necessary even without any taboo motivation, simply to allow the *kohen* to eat his *terumah*. The cultural and the *halachic* thus converge, without being identical.

## Avodah Zarah Wall

### Shared Wall Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תְּנַן הָתָם: מִי שֶׁהָיָה בֵּיתוֹ סָמוּךְ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְנָפַל, אָסוּר לִבְנוֹתוֹ. כֵּיצַד יַעֲשֶׂה? כּוֹנֵס לְתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וּבוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Since the *halakhot* of idolatry and the impurity it causes are beyond the scope of tractate *Shabbat*, the fundamentals of this *halakha* are cited from tractate *Avoda Zara*. **We learned** in a mishna **there: One whose house was adjacent to** a house of **idolatry,** sharing a common wall, **and** the dividing wall **fell, it is prohibited to rebuild it** as he would thereby have built a wall for idol worship. **What should one do? He moves four cubits into his own** land **and builds** the wall there.

The Gemara now turns to a *Mishnah* from *Masechet Avodah Zarah*. The case: one's house stands adjacent to a pagan temple (*avodah zarah*), and the shared wall collapses. The *Mishnah* rules it is forbidden to rebuild the wall in its original position, since doing so would simultaneously serve the *avodah zarah* property. The solution: enter four *amot* into one's own property and build from there, so the new wall serves only oneself. If the wall was jointly owned — half one's own, half the *avodah zarah*'s — then the partition is imagined down the middle of the wall, and one moves four *amot* inward from that line.

### Impurity Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה שֶׁלּוֹ וְשֶׁל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, נִידּוֹן מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה. אֲבָנָיו וְעֵצָיו וַעֲפָרָיו מְטַמְּאִים כְּשֶׁרֶץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שַׁקֵּץ תְּשַׁקְּצֶנּוּ״. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר כְּנִדָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תִּזְרֵם כְּמוֹ דָוָה״ — מָה נִדָּה מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא, אַף עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא.
>
> **Translation:** If the wall stood upon ground that **belonged to him and to the** house of **idolatry,** the area **is calculated as half and half** as far as moving into his property before rebuilding the wall, and one may build the wall four cubits from the middle of the wall. **Its stones and its wood and its dust** from the house of idolatry **transmit impurity like creeping animals,** and by rabbinic decree, one who touches them becomes impure like one who touches a creeping animal, **as it is stated:** "And you shall not bring an abomination into your house and become banned like it, **you shall utterly detest it [*shaketz teshaketzenu*]** and you shall utterly abhor it, for it is a banned object" (Deuteronomy 7:26). *Shaketz* is a term used with regard to creeping animals. **Rabbi Akiva says:** Idolatry transmits impurity **like a menstruating woman, as it is stated: "You will cast them away as you would a menstruating woman [*dava*]"** (Isaiah 30:22). **Just as a menstruating woman transmits impurity** imparted **by carrying,** as one who moves a menstruating woman without touching her becomes impure, **so too, idolatry transmits impurity** imparted **by carrying.**

The *Mishnah* then states: *"Avnav ve-eitzav ve-afaro metammim ke-sheretz"* — the stones, wood, and dust of that wall are *tamei* like a *sheretz*. This is remarkable in two respects. First, it is not the idol itself but materials merely associated with the pagan temple that carry *tumah*. Second, Tosafot raises the question: since *avodah zarah* that breaks naturally loses its status (worshippers reason that it cannot protect itself, so it is no longer an object of worship), why do these fallen stones retain their *tumah*? The *Yerushalmi* resolves this: the case is one where the worshippers continued to worship the stones and dust even after the collapse — treating the very rubble as sacred. It is thus not about the wall *qua* wall, but about material that was actively worshipped as *avodah zarah* even in its fragmented state. The source for the *tumah*: *"Shaketz teshaktzenu"* — you shall utterly abominate it. The verbal resonance between *sheketz* (abomination) and *sheretz* (creeping creature) generates the legal comparison: just as a *sheretz* causes *tumah* by contact, so does *avodah zarah*.

### Sheretz vs. Niddah

This, however, is the first opinion. Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *"Ke-niddah"* — not like a *sheretz* but like a *niddah*. The proof is the verse from Yeshayahu we have already encountered: *"Tizrem kmo davah."* The critical practical difference between the two comparisons: a *sheretz* causes *tumah* only through contact (*maga*), while a *niddah* causes *tumah* also through *massa* — carrying, without direct contact. The debate between the first opinion and Rabbi Akiva is thus, at first glance, about whether *avodah zarah* transmits *tumah* through contact alone or also through carrying.

### Rava's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה: בְּמַשָּׂא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּמְטַמְּאָה, דְּהָא אִתַּקַּשׁ לְנִדָּה. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּאֶבֶן מְסָמָא. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר כְּנִדָּה: מָה נִדָּה מְטַמְּאָה בְּאֶבֶן מְסָמָא — אַף עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מְטַמְּאָה בְּאֶבֶן מְסָמָא. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי כְּשֶׁרֶץ: מָה שֶׁרֶץ לֹא מְטַמֵּא בְּאֶבֶן מְסָמָא — אַף עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לֹא מְטַמְּאָה בְּאֶבֶן מְסָמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** to the essence of the dispute, **Rabba said: With regard to** impurity imparted **by carrying, everyone agrees** that idol worship **transmits impurity, as it is juxtaposed to a menstruating woman** in the verse. **Where they argue,** it is **with regard to** the *halakha* of **a very heavy stone.** There is a special law with regard to the ritual impurity of a *zav* and a menstruating woman. If they sit on an object, even if it is an object that cannot become ritually impure, and beneath that object is a vessel, even though the weight of the *zav* and the menstruating woman has no effect on the vessel, it becomes ritually impure. **Rabbi Akiva holds** that the impurity of idolatry is **like** the impurity of a **menstruating woman** in all respects; **just as a menstruating woman transmits impurity via a very heavy stone, so too, idolatry transmits impurity via a very heavy stone. And the Rabbis hold** that in this regard, the impurity of idolatry is **like** the impurity of **a creeping animal; just as a creeping animal does not transmit impurity via a very heavy stone, so too, idolatry does not transmit impurity via a very heavy stone.**

Rava, however, offers a more nuanced reading of this debate. He argues: *"Be-massa, de-kulei alma lo peligi de-metame"* — everyone agrees that *avodah zarah* causes *tumah* through *massa* (carrying). The comparison to *niddah* in the verse establishes this for all opinions. The debate between the Rabbanan and Rabbi Akiva, Rava contends, is not about the basic *halacha* of *massa* but about a more refined category: *even masama* — a special and stringent mode of *tumah* unique to *niddah* (and related *zav* categories). Rava reads the *machlokes* as follows: Rabbi Akiva, drawing the comparison to *niddah* fully, extends the analogy to include *even masama*; the Rabbanan, emphasizing the *sheretz* comparison, limit the analogy and exclude *even masama* from the scope of *avodah zarah*'s *tumah*. The Torah's dual comparison — both to *sheretz* and to *niddah* — reflects a partial equation, not a full one: *massa* yes; *even masama*, a matter of debate.

## Even Masama

### Rashi's View

What is *even masama*? The term refers to a special category of *tumah* transmission specific to the *niddah*, *zav*, and *zavah*. These individuals make not only what they sit on *tamei* (the *moshav* category) but also, through the special category of *even masama*, objects in a particular indirect spatial relationship to them. Rashi explains: if the *niddah* is seated on a surface but there is something beneath her — not directly touching her, with an air gap — that object also becomes *tamei*. The stone or object is underneath her without bearing her weight, yet it falls into this special category.

### Tosafot's View

Tosafot disputes Rashi's explanation, arguing that such a case of *tumah* does not independently exist in the sources. Tosafot instead explains *even masama* as follows: imagine a massive boulder. The *niddah* sits atop the boulder, and beneath the boulder lies a cushion or other object. Normally, *moshav* (seat-*tumah*) requires that the *niddah*'s weight actually press upon the object below. But when the object beneath is separated from the *niddah* by an enormous boulder, her weight contributes essentially nothing to the pressure on the lower object. Yet precisely this case — where the causal connection between the *niddah* and the object below is negligible due to the intervening mass — is what the special *halacha* of *even masama* addresses: even here, *tumah* is transmitted. It is derived from a dedicated verse and constitutes a *chiddush* precisely because the ordinary logic of *moshav* would not apply.

### Moshav vs. Masama

The distinction, then, between classic *moshav* and *even masama* is the presence or absence of meaningful weight transmission. Classic *moshav* works through the *niddah*'s bodily weight pressing down on the object beneath her — even through many layers, as in the Princess and the Pea scenario, where the *niddah* sits atop ten cushions and the bottom cushion nonetheless becomes an *av ha-tumah*. *Even masama* is the extension of this *halacha* to a case where the *niddah*'s weight barely registers on the lower object because of the boulder's intervening mass. This is the special, additional stringency that only *niddah* (and *zav* / *zavah*) carry — and it is this stringency that Rava identifies as the precise locus of the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbanan regarding *avodah zarah*.

## Whole vs. Dismembered

### Niddah Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא אִיתַּקַּשׁ לְשֶׁרֶץ? — לִמְשַׁמְּשֶׁיהָ. וּלְרַבָּנַן, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא אִיתַּקַּשׁ לְנִדָּה? — לְמַשָּׂא. וְלַקְּשֵׁיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לִנְבֵלָה! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, אֶלָּא: מָה נִדָּה אֵינָהּ לְאֵבָרִין, אַף עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אֵינָהּ לְאֵבָרִין. וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּבָעֵי רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא: עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה יֶשְׁנָהּ לְאֵבָרִין אוֹ אֵינָהּ לְאֵבָרִין? תִּיפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מֵהָא, דִּלְרַבָּנַן אֵינָהּ לְאֵבָרִין! רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אַלִּיבָּא דְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בָּעֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according **to Rabbi Akiva,** who holds that the impurity of idolatry is similar to that of a menstruating woman in all respects, **for what *halakha* was it juxtaposed to a creeping animal?** The Gemara explains: In his opinion, it was not stated in reference to idolatry itself, but rather **to its accessories,** objects used for the purposes of idolatry. The Gemara asks further: **And according to the Rabbis, for what *halakha* was it juxtaposed to a menstruating woman? To** teach that it transmits impurity through **carrying. And** instead of juxtaposing idolatry to both a menstruating woman and to creeping animals, **let the Torah juxtapose it to an** animal **carcass,** which transmits impurity through carrying and does not transmit impurity via a very heavy stone, since according to the Rabbis the law is the same for idolatry. The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so.** In that sense, juxtaposition to an animal carcass would suffice. **However,** the juxtaposition to a menstruating woman teaches: **Just as a menstruating woman** does **not** transmit impurity through **limbs,** as if the limb of a menstruating woman is supported by a vessel, the vessel does not become ritually impure (Ra'avad), **so too, idolatry does not** transmit impurity through **limbs,** as a severed part of an idol does not transmit impurity. The Gemara is puzzled by this: **But that which Rav Ḥama bar Guria raised as a dilemma: Does idolatry have** the capacity to transmit impurity through **limbs or does it not** have the capacity to transmit impurity through **limbs? Resolve** the dilemma **from this, as according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, it does not** transmit impurity through **limbs.** And the Gemara replies: Although that is so, **Rav Ḥama bar Guria raised the dilemma in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva** and the dilemma is unresolved.

Having established that everyone agrees *avodah zarah* causes *tumah* through *massa*, the Gemara asks: why compare specifically to *niddah* for this purpose? There are other objects that cause *tumah* through carrying — a *neveilah*, for instance, the carcass of an animal that was not *shechted* properly. Why not draw the comparison to *neveilah* instead?

### Wholeness Principle

The answer: *"Mah niddah einah le-eivarim — af avodah zarah einah le-eivarim."* The *niddah* comparison teaches something *neveilah* does not: *tumah* applies only to the *niddah* as a whole person. If, God forbid, a limb were severed from a *niddah*, that limb would carry *tumah* of *eiver min ha-chai* (a limb from a living creature), but it would not carry the *tumah* of *niddah*. The *niddah*'s *tumah* is bound to her wholeness as a person. *Neveilah*, by contrast, applies even to parts — individual limbs of a carcass can convey *tumah* when of sufficient size.

### Disassembly Rule

The comparison to *niddah*, then, introduces the principle that *avodah zarah*'s *tumah* similarly requires wholeness: an idol that has been disassembled — even if its components could in principle be reassembled — loses its *tumah* status while in its disassembled state. This is a specific *halachic* leniency within the laws of *tumah*, distinct from the *issur hana'ah* question, where disassembly may well not suffice to remove the prohibition.

### Chama's Question

Against this backdrop, the Gemara revisits a question posed by Rabbi Chama bar Gurya: *"Avodah zarah yeshnah le-eivarim o einah le-eivarim?"* — does *avodah zarah* retain its *tumah* when dismembered, or not? Given that we have just established the principle *einah le-eivarim* from the *niddah* comparison, why does Rabbi Chama bar Gurya still have a question?

### Two Opinions

The answer: Rabbi Chama bar Gurya is raising his question specifically according to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva. According to the Rabbanan in Rava's reading, the lesson learned from the *niddah* comparison is precisely the *wholeness* principle — *einah le-eivarim*. But for Rabbi Akiva, what is derived from the *niddah* comparison is the *even masama* category. The *wholeness* principle, for him, is not necessarily what the *niddah* comparison teaches about *avodah zarah*, leaving the question of dismembered *avodah zarah* genuinely open under his view.

## Debate Readings

### Eliezer's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: בְּאֶבֶן מְסָמָא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּלָא מְטַמְּאָה. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּמַשָּׂא: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר כְּנִדָּה, מָה נִדָּה מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא — אַף עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי כְּשֶׁרֶץ, מָה שֶׁרֶץ לֹא מְטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא — אַף עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לֹא מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא אִיתַּקַּשׁ לְשֶׁרֶץ? לִמְשַׁמְּשֶׁיהָ. וְרַבָּנַן לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא אִיתַּקַּשׁ לְנִדָּה? מָה נִדָּה אֵינָהּ לָאֵבָרִים, אַף עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אֵינָהּ לְאֵבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in another approach to this dispute, **Rabbi Elazar said: With regard to a very heavy stone, everyone agrees** that idolatry **does not transmit impurity** in that manner. **Where they disagree** is **with regard to** impurity imparted by **carrying. Rabbi Akiva holds** that the legal status of idolatry is **like** that of **a menstruating woman: Just as a menstruating woman transmits impurity through carrying, so too, idolatry transmits impurity through carrying. And the Rabbis hold** that the legal status of idolatry is **like** that of **a creeping animal: Just as a creeping animal does not transmit impurity through carrying, so too, idolatry does not transmit impurity through carrying.** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Akiva's** opinion, with regard **to what *halakha* was** idolatry **juxtaposed to a creeping animal?** The Gemara answers: With regard **to** the *halakha* that **its accessories** do not transmit impurity through carrying. The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the Rabbis,** with regard **to what *halakha* was** idolatry **juxtaposed to a menstruating woman?** The Gemara answers: **Just as a menstruating woman does not** transmit impurity **through** her **limbs, so too, idolatry does not** transmit impurity **through** its **limbs.**

Rabbi Eliezer offers an alternative structural reading of the entire debate, contrasting with Rava's. While Rava held that *even masama* is the point of dispute and that *massa* is agreed upon by all, Rabbi Eliezer argues the reverse: *"Be-even masama, de-kulei alma lo peligi de-lo metame"* — when it comes to *even masama*, everyone agrees that *avodah zarah* does *not* transmit *tumah* in that manner. The debate between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbanan, Rabbi Eliezer contends, is about the more basic question of *massa* — carrying — which is precisely what the *Mishnah* sounds like on its face. Rabbi Akiva, drawing the full comparison to *niddah*, holds that *avodah zarah* causes *tumah* through *massa*. The Rabbanan, emphasizing the *sheretz* analogy, hold that it does not.

### Appurtenances Rule

Under Rabbi Eliezer's reading, one must then ask: for Rabbi Akiva, who holds *avodah zarah* is fully compared to *niddah* including *massa* — what purpose does the *sheretz* comparison serve? The answer: *le-meshamesheiha* — the *sheretz* comparison governs the appurtenances of the *avodah zarah*, the objects used to serve or adorn it. The *avodah zarah* itself is like a *niddah* (subject to *massa*); the paraphernalia that attend it are only like a *sheretz* (subject to contact-*tumah* alone, not *massa*).

### Wholeness from Niddah

Conversely, for the Rabbanan under Rabbi Eliezer's reading — who hold that *avodah zarah* is like a *sheretz* and not subject to *massa* — what purpose does the *niddah* comparison serve at all? The answer: *"Mah niddah einah le-eivarim — af avodah zarah einah le-eivarim."* The *niddah* comparison teaches the *wholeness* principle: even the contact-based *tumah* the Rabbanan concede applies to *avodah zarah* is limited to the idol when whole. Dismembered, it loses even its *sheretz*-like *tumah*. The daf concludes here, with the discussion to continue on the following day.
