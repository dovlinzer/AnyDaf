# Bava Batra 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Measures & Custom

### Mishna Overview

The *mishna* at the center of today's daf addresses a deceptively broad topic: what it means to deal honestly with weights and measures. Honesty in this domain is not limited to ensuring that one's measuring vessels are physically accurate. It also encompasses a set of related obligations — cleaning out the measuring containers and wiping down the scales, and attending carefully to the very act of weighing or filling: whether one adds a small amount extra in the buyer's favor, whether the scale tips slightly toward the buyer, and whether a volume measure is filled level or heaped. All of these practices bear on the question of whether a buyer receives a fair measure.

### Large vs Small

The *mishna* establishes: *makom shenahagu lamod begasa al yimod bedaka* — in a place where the custom is to use a large measure when selling, one may not switch to using a small measure, and vice versa. The reasoning behind this rule is instructive. Since perfect precision is essentially unattainable in any act of measurement, there will always be some slight margin of error. The expectation, and the norm of honest dealing, is that this margin should consistently favor the buyer. When multiple small measures are used, that slight error occurs repeatedly — each individual measuring carries a small surplus — and the cumulative effect benefits the buyer more than a single large measurement would. This is why switching to a large measure in a place where small measures are the norm would disadvantage the buyer.

Equally striking, however, is the *mishna*'s concern in the reverse direction: one may not switch to small measures in a place where large measures are standard, even if this would benefit the buyer. The *mishna* clearly protects the seller's legitimate interests as well, not merely the buyer's.

### Lifnim Mishurat

Even in a place where the measuring practice is a level, flat fill, and even if the norms of that place are strictly followed, there remains a separate obligation to give something extra beyond the precise measure — a *lifnim mishurat hadin* consideration. This is distinct from the question of how the measuring itself is conducted. The question of whether the act of measuring is done in a balanced or slightly generous manner is one issue; the question of throwing in an additional unit as a gesture of honest generosity is a separate matter entirely.

### Smooth vs Heap

A *baraita* is then cited: *minayin she'ein mokechin bemakom shegodshin ve'ein godshin bemakom shemokechin* — how do we know that one may not smooth the top of a volume measure in a place where the custom is to heap, and may not heap in a place where the custom is to smooth? The source is the *pasuk*: *eifah shleimah vetzedek yihyeh lach* — "a complete and just measure shall be yours." "Complete," the *baraita* understands, means complete in accordance with the norms of the place. A heaped measure in a smoothing locale is not a complete measure; it exceeds the norm. A smoothed measure in a heaping locale falls short of it.

Why should this be a problem when both parties agree to it? The *Rashbam* explains that the concern is a social one. Even if buyer and seller privately arrange to deviate from the local norm, other people see the transaction visually but do not know its terms. They observe the flat measure and assume it is the going practice — and this disrupts the established norms of the marketplace, ultimately harming either buyers or sellers who thereafter expect the wrong standard.

### Eifah Shleima

The *baraita* continues: *uminayin she'im amar hareni mokech bemakom shegodshin ulephachot lo min hadamim* — how do we know that even if the seller says "I will smooth it rather than heap it, but I will charge a reduced price to compensate," one may not follow this approach? The *Rashbam* explains that the additional word in the *pasuk* — *vetzedek*, "and just" — prohibits even this arrangement. The reason is the same: *de'ika dechazi bimdidah velo yada lo bifchas velo betosefes* — observers see the manner of measuring but have no way of knowing whether a corresponding price adjustment was made. This visual discrepancy leads to disruption of honest practice throughout the marketplace. One student added a sharper formulation: this amounts to *rama'ut* — it actively invites deception, since other sellers, seeing the deviation, may adopt it without the compensating price adjustment. The *Rashbam* includes this concern implicitly, but the full force of the point is that the visual impression creates the opening for fraud.

*Tosafot* offers a somewhat different explanation of the underlying prohibition, which we will return to below.

A note from the *mishna* suggests that the difference between a heaped and a level measure is substantial — as much as a third more for the heap. This is a striking figure. If the deviation is that large, it would obviously need to be reflected in pricing; one cannot simply pretend the difference is negligible. Similarly, the *mishna* mentions later that the scale should tip a full *tefach* in the buyer's favor when weighing — which also seems like an enormous amount, one that would necessarily affect the pricing structure. These details suggest that whatever these measures mean in practice, they were understood as embedded in a larger system of pricing conventions, not as isolated acts of generosity.

### Even Shleima

The *baraita* then extends the identical analysis to weight measures. *Minayin she'ein me'aynin bimkom shemachri'in* — how do we know that one may not weigh to the exact balance (*me'aynin*, literally, "eye-level") in a place where the norm is to tip the scale (*machri'in*) in the buyer's favor? And the reverse: one may not tip the scale where the norm is to weigh precisely. The source is *even shleimah vatzedek yihyeh lach* — "a complete and just weight shall be yours." The word *even* (weight measure) parallels *eifah* (volume measure) exactly, and the same analysis applies. Exact balance, in a tipping-custom locale, defrauds the buyer. Tipping, in an exact-balance locale, defrauds the seller. And the same prohibition against compensating through adjusted pricing applies here as well, with *even shleimah vatzedek* as its source.

It is worth pausing on the linguistic observation that *machri'ah* — "to tip the scales" — is the same word used in halachic discourse for "to decide between competing opinions," because tipping in one direction is itself the act of deciding. The metaphor is built into the vocabulary.

## Midrash & Officials

### Rav Yehuda

The *gemara* then presents a remarkable *midrashic* reading of the *pesukim* in Devarim 25, attributed to Rav Yehuda Mi-Sura. The verse *lo yihyeh lecha b'veitecha eifah va'eifah* — "you shall not have in your house two measures" — is read as a prediction of poverty: *lo yihyeh lecha b'veitecha*, "you will have nothing in your house." Why will this person end up with nothing? *Mishum eifah va'eifah* — because he maintains two sets of measures in his house: the larger measure with which he buys and the smaller measure with which he sells. This is the classic "two sets of books" — one for acquisition and one for sale, each manipulated in the seller's favor.

The reading extends further: *lo yihyeh lecha b'chisecha* — "you will have nothing in your pocket" — because of *even va'even*, maintaining two sets of weights. But by contrast, *even shleimah vatzedek yihyeh lach* — if you maintain a just weight, *yihyeh lach*, you will have wealth. *Eifah shleimah vatzedek yihyeh lach* — and if you maintain a just volume measure, you too will be rewarded with prosperity.

This is a powerful *midrashic* inversion: the very phrase the deceiver hopes will describe his gains — *yihyeh lach* — becomes the description of the honest dealer's reward, while the cheater ends up with *lo yihyeh lach* precisely because of the deception he practiced.

The *gemara* then takes the final *pasuk*, *eifah shleimah vatzedek yihyeh lach*, and notes that the use of the singular pronoun *lach* (rather than *lachem*) is instructive in a surprising direction. Normally one might think the singular *lach* addresses the individual and the plural *lachem* addresses the community. But the *midrash halacha* often reads the singular as a communal address — the nation as a unified entity — while the plural distributes obligation to each individual. The parallel from *Sefirat HaOmer* is instructive: *u'sefartem lachem* imposes an individual obligation on each person to count, while *v'safarta lecha* regarding *yovel* is a communal obligation fulfilled through the *Beit Din*. So too here: *eifah shleimah vatzedek yihyeh lach* addresses the community as a whole, teaching that the community bears collective responsibility to ensure the integrity of weights and measures.

### Agardomin

This communal reading leads directly to the practical ruling: *melamed she'ma'amidim agardemin l'middot* — the community must appoint *agardomin* (market inspectors, from a Greek term meaning officer in charge of the market) to oversee weights and measures. These officials go from stall to stall, testing whether the measures are accurate — the ancient equivalent of the government inspectors who once visited butcher shops incognito to weigh a pound of meat after purchase.

However, *v'ein ma'amidim agardemin l'sha'arim* — one does not appoint such officials to regulate prices. The *Rashbam* explains: free-market competition is sufficient to bring prices to their natural level. If one vendor overcharges, a competitor will undercut him and attract the business. This principle implicitly assumes a competitive marketplace without collusion, but it is a strikingly early articulation of the idea that market prices regulate themselves while the integrity of the measuring instruments does not.

### Nasi Disagreement

The house of the *Nasi* in Bavel, however, took a different position: *debe Nesia ukmi agordemi bein le-middot bein le-shearim* — they appointed officials for both weights and measures *and* for prices. Shmuel, upon learning of this, sent a pointed message through his colleague Karna: *puk tani le-hu ma'amidin agordemi le-middot ve-ein ma'amidim agordemi le-shearim* — go teach the house of the *Nasi* that the *halacha* is that officials are appointed for measures but not for prices.

That Shmuel dispatched Karna to deliver this correction to the *Nasi*'s household is itself noteworthy. The *gemara* then reports that when Karna arrived, he instead taught *ma'amidin agordemi bein le-middot bein le-shearim* — the position of the *Nasi*'s house, which he had been sent to correct. Shmuel's response was sharp: *ma shimcha?* — "What is your name?" *Karna* — "Horn?" *Tepuk lei karna bein einai* — "A horn should sprout between your eyes." And, the *gemara* notes, *nafka lei karna bein einai* — it did. The *Rashbam* mentions a parallel account in *Gemara Shabbat* in which Shmuel was angered at Karna for a different reason; perhaps both incidents contributed to the result.

### Rav Yitzchak

The *gemara* then asks: when Karna taught the position of the *Nasi*'s house, whose opinion was he relying on? It would be unthinkable that he would teach an entirely unsupported view even under social pressure. The answer: *ki ha de'amar Rami bar Chama amar Rav Yitzchak ma'amidin agordemi bein le-middot bein le-shearim mipnei ha-ramaim* — there is indeed a tradition that officials are appointed even for prices, because of the *ramaim*, the deceivers. The *Rashbam* explains that this is not merely about setting prices, but about broader forms of market deception — selling lower-grade goods as higher-grade, misrepresenting quality, and similar frauds that a simple price check would not catch. More straightforwardly, collusion among sellers to fix prices artificially is a classic form of *ramaim* that free-market competition alone cannot remedy. It is worth noting that the *Rambam* rules in accordance with Rav Yitzchak's position — officials are appointed for both measures and prices.

### Price Gouging

This discussion of *ramaim* opens into a broader theme: the historical phenomenon of price gouging tied to religious observance. When a community is known to *need* certain goods — fish for *Shabbos*, *matzah* for *Pesach*, or whatever the religious occasion demands — unscrupulous vendors exploit that need to charge inflated prices. The *gemara* itself records cases of this kind, and the communal response was often dramatic: Rabbis and community leaders would issue a prohibition on purchasing the gouged item until the price came down. This was a form of organized communal pressure — effectively a boycott — to counteract the leverage that religious necessity created for dishonest sellers. Similar dynamics appear in later *teshuvot* regarding funeral expenses, where communities adopted the principle of maintaining minimal expenditures rather than allowing funeral homes to exploit the grief and guilt of mourners. The appointment of *agardomin* for prices, in Rav Yitzchak's view, is precisely the institutional response to such structural vulnerabilities.

## Weighing Procedure

### Standard Units

A *baraita* teaches: *hayah mevakesh mimenu litra shokel lo litra, chatzi litra shokel lo chatzi litra, revia litra shokel lo revia litra* — if someone wants to buy a pound, weigh it with a one-pound weight; half a pound, with a half-pound weight; a quarter pound, with a quarter-pound weight. The *gemara* asks: *mai kamashmalan* — what does this add? The answer is that the *baraita* establishes which specific weight denominations must be available and used: a full pound, a half, and a quarter. The reason for mandating these specific units is twofold. First, if only large weights are available, buyers may be forced to overbuy. Second, and more significantly, the differences between these denominations — half, quarter — are large enough that the weights cannot easily be confused or swapped. Weight units that are too close in size to one another create the risk that a slightly lighter weight will be substituted undetected.

### Three-Quarter Measure

The case of a three-quarter pound purchase presents an interesting problem. The *baraita* rules: *lo yomar lo shokel li shlosha reviei litra achat achat* — one should not make three separate quarter-pound weighings. Instead: *shokel litra umeiniach revia litra im habasar* — one places a one-pound weight on one side of the scale and adds a quarter-pound weight *together with* the meat on the other side, so that they balance. Why not simply use a half-pound weight and a quarter-pound weight on the counterbalancing side? The *Rashbam* explains the objection to three separate weighings: even in a locale where exact-balance (*me'aynin*) weighing is the norm, the honest dealer will always err ever so slightly in the buyer's favor, because perfect precision is unattainable. Three separate weighings would give the buyer three such tips in his favor, which is more than the norm provides for. Hence the single combined weighing.

### Ten Pounds

The same principle governs larger quantities. *Hayah mevakesh mimenu eser litrin* — if someone wants to buy ten pounds — *lo yomar lo shkol li achat achat v'yachria* — one should not make ten separate one-pound weighings, each with its own slight tip in the buyer's favor. Instead, *shoklan kulan b'vat achat v'noten hechra echad l'kulan* — weigh all ten pounds at once, with a single tip for the entire transaction.

### Scale Mechanics

A *baraita* now turns to the physical specifications of the scale itself, referring throughout to the *nefesh moznayim* — the entire scale assembly. For scales used to weigh the heaviest goods — the *gemara* will clarify these are the scales of smiths dealing in brass and iron — the following specifications apply: *tluya ba'avir gimmel tefachim v'gvoha min ha'aretz gimmel tefachim* — the suspended scale must be at least three *tefachim* below the ceiling point from which it hangs, and at least three *tefachim* above the ground. *V'hakaneh v'hamachta shela yod-bet tefachim* — the bar (*kaneh*) and the ropes (*machta*, or *masna*) together measure twelve *tefachim*.

The *Rashbam* explains this as follows: the bar is four *tefachim* in length, and the ropes on each side are four *tefachim* each, yielding four plus four plus four equals twelve. The practical reasoning behind the ceiling and floor clearances is that heavy goods, when placed on one side of an unloaded scale, will cause a dramatic drop; if the pan swings too close to the ceiling at the top or the ground at the bottom, it will be obstructed, distorting the reading. There is also a physical principle at work — the longer the ropes, the more sensitive the scale, since any slight imbalance causes a larger visible arc of movement.

### Scale Sizes

For sellers of lighter goods — specifically wool merchants and glass sellers (it is notable that glass was sold by weight) — the specifications are reduced. The scale hangs two *tefachim* from the ceiling, stands two *tefachim* from the ground, and the combined bar and ropes measure nine *tefachim* (bar of three *tefachim* plus ropes of three *tefachim* on each side). For a retail storekeeper or private householder selling still lighter quantities, the scale need only be one *tefach* from ceiling and ground, with a combined bar and rope measure of six *tefachim* (bar of two *tefachim* plus ropes of two *tefachim* on each side).

### Metal Scales

For the *turtani* — the seller of precious metals, essentially a smith or jeweler — the scale need only be three *etzba'ot* (fingers) from the ceiling and three *etzba'ot* from the ground, roughly half a *tefach*. For this scale, however, the *baraita* states: *vekaneh u'masna shelo eini yodea* — the dimensions of the bar and ropes are unknown. This is a striking admission. Why, having specified every other scale precisely, does the author not know this one? One might have expected that for precious metals — where the stakes of imprecision are highest — the specifications would be the most carefully defined. Indeed, one might reason that precisely because of the value involved, the ropes should be *longer*, not shorter, to maximize sensitivity. The *gemara* leaves this unresolved.

## Equipment Materials

### Pan Materials

A *baraita* rules: *ein osin mishkalos lo shel ba'atz v'lo shel avar v'lo shel gisteron v'lo shel shear minei matachos* — the pans of the scale (the *mishkalos*, and here the word refers to the pans, not the weights themselves) may not be made of tin, lead, mixed alloy (*gisteron*, per the *Rashbam*), or other metals. Instead, *ela oseh hu shel tsunma v'shel zechuchis* — one should make the pans of rock or glass.

Why not metal pans? *Tosafot* cites Rabbeinu Tam's explanation: the concern is specifically for scales used to weigh meat, fat, and other moist goods — *shelachluchis midabek bahen u'misyabesh* — because the residue from such goods adheres to the metal pan and dries there, creating a buildup that affects subsequent weighings. This concern parallels the earlier *mishna*-level obligation to wipe and clean measuring vessels; even the pan itself must be constructed of material that does not retain residue. It is not, as Rashi suggested, a concern about the metal wearing away — these are hard metals and will not abrade — but a concern about the *accumulation* of material on them.

### Leather Linings

An exception exists: *aval mishkalos sheshoklim bahen kesef v'zahav mutar la'asosan shel mataches ela shetzarich lachaposo or* — for scales used to weigh gold and silver, metal pans are permitted, because precious metal transactions do not involve moist goods and therefore carry no residue concern. However, these pans must be lined with leather, for a different reason: without a protective lining, the gold or silver being weighed might scrape against the metal pan and lose minute quantities in the process. The leather lining protects the integrity of the metal being weighed — much as precious gems and metals are presented today in velvet-lined cases to protect their surface.

### Cleaning Tools

The *baraita* next addresses the *machak* — the tool used to clean out a measuring container (such as a one-liter vessel used to measure wine or grain). *Ein osim hamachak shel dala'at* — one may not make it of a gourd, *mipnei shehu kal* — because it is too light and will not do a thorough job. *Velo shel matechet* — nor may it be made of metal, *mipnei shehu machbid* — because it is too heavy and may scrape away at the walls of the measuring vessel itself, enlarging its interior volume and thereby disadvantaging the seller in future transactions.

### Uniform Cleaning

The *baraita* further specifies: *ein osim et hamachak tziddo echad av vetziddo echad katzar* — one may not make the *machak* with one side wide and one side narrow. The *Rashbam* explains that a narrower edge can get into the nooks and crevices of the vessel more thoroughly, while a wider edge will clean less completely. If the tool has one of each, both buyer and seller have an interest in which end is used: the buyer will want the narrow end used to ensure the full volume is available, while the seller will prefer the wide end. This would reintroduce the very asymmetry the halacha seeks to prevent. The *machak* must be uniform, so that the manner of cleaning is consistent and neither party can manipulate it to their advantage.

### Cleaning Technique

Beyond the tool's construction, the *baraita* governs the speed of its application. *V'lo yimchok bevat achat shehamochek bevat achat ra lamocher* — one may not clean in a single rapid swipe, because a quick clean is disadvantageous to the seller. Nor, in the opposite direction, *v'lo yimchok me'at me'at she'ra lalokeach veyofeh lamocher* — may one clean very slowly and repeatedly, which disadvantages the buyer. The text here presents some difficulty: intuitively, one might have expected the directionality to run the other way — a quick clean that leaves residue would seem to harm the buyer by leaving less volume available, while an overly thorough clean would harm the seller. The *Rashbam*'s explanation of this passage is puzzling, and several attempts to re-read the passage as referring to the pans or the weights themselves (rather than the measuring container) do not resolve the difficulty cleanly.

### Rabban Yochanan

On all of these practices — each of which describes a method by which one party could subtly cheat the other — *Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* made a famous remark, preserved in a *mishna* in *Keilim*: *oy li im omer, oy li im lo omer* — "Woe to me if I say it, and woe to me if I do not say it." The dilemma: *im omer, shema yilmedu ha-rama'in* — if he publicizes these methods of cheating, he risks teaching the cheaters new tricks they had not thought of. But *v'im lo omer, shema yomru ha-rama'in ein talmidei chachamim beki'in b'ma'asei yadeinu* — if he stays silent, the cheaters will say that the *talmidei chachamim* have no idea how they operate, and they will continue with impunity.

The *gemara* records a dispute about what Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai ultimately decided. Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak rules: *amrah* — he did say it, and he based his decision on the *pasuk*, *ki yesharim darchei Hashem, tzaddikim yelchu vam u'fosha'im yikashlu vam* — "the paths of God are straight; the righteous walk in them and the wicked stumble upon them." Torah knowledge is meant to guide the righteous, and that purpose cannot be abandoned because the wicked might misuse it. The disclosure of cheating methods is necessary for honest people to protect themselves, and the responsibility for its misuse lies with the one who misuses it.

## Scripture Derivations

### Land Measurement

The *baraita* now turns to the verse *lo sa'asu avel bamishpat bamiddah bamishkal u'vamsurah* — "you shall not do injustice in judgment, in measure, in weight, or in liquid measure." The *baraita* expounds each term. *B'middah* — in measure — refers to land measurement: *shelo yimdod l'echad biyemos hachamah v'la'echad biyemos hageshamim* — one may not measure land for one party in the summer and for another in the rainy season. The reason is that measuring ropes absorb moisture in the rainy season and stretch, so the same rope will give a different reading in winter than in summer. Using different seasonal conditions for different parties is a form of deception, even if the physical instrument — the rope — is the same object.

### Weight Storage

*B'mishkal* — in weights — teaches: *shelo yatmin mishklosav bamelach* — one may not store the weights in salt. *Rashi* interprets this as a concern that salt makes the weights heavier, which would benefit the buyer at the seller's expense. *Tosafot*, however, cites Rabbeinu Tam's objection: the context, by analogy to the subsequent liquid-measure case, is clearly speaking about the seller's conduct. Rabbeinu Tam therefore explains that salt actually makes the weights *lighter* — it draws out moisture from the material, reducing their mass — which benefits the seller at the buyer's expense.

### Liquid Measures

*U'vamsurah* — in liquid measures — the *baraita* teaches: *shelo yartiah* — one may not pour with froth. Pouring frothy liquid into a measure means that part of what is being sold is foam rather than substance; the buyer is receiving less than the full volume he paid for. Notably, it is not the measuring vessel itself that is false, but the manner of using it — and the transgression is still prohibited by this *pasuk*.

### Tosafot Question

*Tosafot* raises the question that applies to all of these cases: *pshita d'gezel gamur hu* — obviously, each of these practices constitutes outright theft. Why does the Torah need a special *pasuk* to prohibit them? Does *lo tigzol* not already cover every case? *Tosafot*'s answer is precise: *d'asa kra l'chayeiv m'shaat hartichah af al pi she'adayin lo madad* — the *pasuk* creates liability from the *moment of the improper act* even before the actual theft is consummated. In the case of pouring with froth, the transgression begins the moment one pours with froth, even before one has collected payment or completed the transaction. A parallel formulation appears in the discussion in *Eizehu Neshech*: *lav d'chsiv b'mishkalos lama li litmon mishkalos even u'melach hagzeilah mealyei hi* — if you are maintaining fraudulent weights, that is already full-fledged *gezeilah*, so why the extra *lav*? The answer there too is: *laavor lav m'shaat asiyah* — you transgress the prohibition at the moment you *create* the fraudulent weights, before you have used them in any transaction at all.

### Kal Vachomer

The *baraita* closes with a *kal vachomer*: *v'kal vachomer mah mesurah she'hi echad mishloshim v'shishah b'log hikpid alav Torah* — the *mesurah* is one of the smallest liquid measures, one thirty-sixth of a *log*, an amount whose monetary value is negligible in absolute terms. Yet the Torah is exacting about even this tiny measure. *Kal vachomer* — how much more so with the larger measures listed in the *baraita* — the *hin*, the half-*hin*, the third-*hin*, the quarter-*hin*, the *log*, the half-*log*, the quarter-*log*, the half-*tuman*, the *uchla* — each of which represents a more substantial volume, and correspondingly greater potential for harm when used dishonestly. The daf ends here, and the exposition of these various measures will continue in the next session.
