# Shevuot 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nichbal Case

### Mishna Nichbal Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶחְבָּל כֵּיצַד. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְחַבֵּל בְּעַצְמוֹ, אֲבָל בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְחַבֵּל בְּעַצְמוֹ – נוֹטֵל שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **How** does this *halakha* apply to **one who was injured?** If witnesses testified about the injured person that he entered into the domain of the defendant whole, but left injured, the injured party may take an oath and receive compensation. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The Sages **taught** that he needs to take an oath in order to receive compensation **only** if he was injured **in a place where he is able to injure himself, but** if he was injured **in a place where he is unable to injure himself, he receives** compensation **without** taking **an oath.**

The Gemara on 46b introduces the case of *nichbal keitzad* — the injured party, and how such a person may collect damages. The Mishna had already listed the *nichbal* among those who take a *shvua d'rabbanan* to collect, and here the Gemara elaborates on the specifics of that mechanism. The Mishna explains: *hayu mei'idim oto shenichnas tachat yado shalem veyatza chavul* — two witnesses testify that a person entered a room with someone else uninjured and emerged injured, yet no one actually witnessed the attack itself. The injured party then accuses the other person, *chavalta bi*, and the other denies it, *lo chavalti*. In such a case, *harei ze nishba v'enotel* — the injured party may take a *shvua* and collect his damages.

It is worth pausing to note what is at stake in the entire framework of *nishba v'notel*, the *shvua d'rabbanan* that enables a claimant to take an oath and collect even without proof. This goes squarely against the fundamental *d'oraisa* principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — that whoever seeks to extract from another must bring proof. Here, in lieu of proof, the Rabbis enabled the taking of a *shvua* in cases where they were concerned people would otherwise be left without remedy. All of these cases are therefore exceptional relative to ordinary *dinei mamonot*.

### Self-Infliction Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא בְּכוֹתֶל נִתְחַכֵּךְ! תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: שֶׁעָלְתָה לוֹ נְשִׁיכָה בְּגַבּוֹ וּבֵין אַצִּילֵי יָדָיו. וְדִלְמָא אַחֵר עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? דְּלֵיכָּא אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And let us be concerned** that **perhaps he scraped against a wall** and caused the injury himself. The Gemara explains: **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** that the mishna is referring to a case **where,** for example, **he has a bite on his back or on his elbows,** which must have been caused by someone else. The Gemara challenges: **And perhaps a different** person **did** it **to him,** and not the defendant. The Gemara explains: This is a case **where** there **is no other** person with him besides the defendant.

The Gemara now clarifies what precise circumstances are required for this *shvua* to operate. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel teaches: *lo shanu ela bemakom sheyachol lachabel be'atzmo* — this *halacha* applies only where it would have been possible for the person to injure himself, so that the *shvua* serves to confirm that he did not self-inflict but was rather injured by the other party. *Aval bemakom she'eino yachol lachabel be'atzmo notel shelo bishvua* — in a case where self-injury would have been physically impossible, he may collect even without a *shvua*, since the *shvua* must serve some confirmatory function, and where there is no other logical explanation for the injury, that function is already satisfied.

### Injury Location Proof

The Gemara immediately raises an objection: *venichush dilma bekotel nitchabet* — perhaps he was simply cut on the wall? Even if the person could not injure himself, he might have scraped against a rock or wall, meaning the presence of another person in the room does not by itself prove that person caused the injury. To this the Gemara responds with a teaching from Rabbi Chiyya: *she'alta lo neshicha begavo uvein atzilei yadav* — the injury was a bite on the back or on the inner elbow, a location where it would be impossible both for the person to have injured himself and equally impossible for the injury to have been caused by contact with a wall or any inanimate object. The injury's location thus proves human agency.

### Third Party Exclusion

A further difficulty remains: *ve'dilma acher avid leih* — perhaps some unknown third party caused the injury, not the accused? The Gemara resolves this: *delika acher* — it must be in a case where evidence establishes that no other person was present. The conclusion is thus that for a *nichbal* to take the *shvua*, three conditions must be satisfied: the injury must be of a type the person could theoretically have caused himself, it must be one that could not have been caused by an inanimate object, and there must be proof that no third party was present. Only then do we have confirmation that either the person injured himself or the other party did it, and the *shvua* resolves that question in favor of the claimant.

## Suspect Party Reversal

### Chashud Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁנֶּגְדּוֹ חָשׁוּד [וְכוּ׳], וַאֲפִילּוּ שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא. מַאי ״אֲפִילּוּ שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא״?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: How does this *halakha* apply to **one whose opposing** litigant is **suspect** with regard to the taking of an oath and therefore is not permitted to take the oath? One is suspected if he had been found to have taken a false oath, whether it was an oath of testimony or an oath on a deposit, which are prescribed by Torah law, **or even an oath** taken **in vain.** The Gemara asks: For **what** reason does the mishna emphasize: **Even an oath** taken **in vain?**

The Gemara now turns to the central topic of the chapter: *ukesheknegedo chashud al hashvua* — what if the opposing litigant is suspect with respect to oaths? Under ordinary circumstances, Reuven and Shimon are in court together and Reuven would take a *shvua d'oraisa*. If Reuven cannot take that *shvua* because he is suspect, the *shvua* reverts — at the *d'rabbanan* level — to Shimon, who takes a *shvua d'rabbanan* and collects in his place. The Gemara states that the category of *chashud al hashvua* applies *va'afilu shvuat shav* — even to someone who is suspect in the matter of *shvuat shav*. The formulation is the characteristic *lo mibayya* construction: not only do we say this applies to oaths involving denial of a monetary claim, but even to *shvuat shav*, which is a denial of a purely factual or verbal matter with no monetary dimension. Even such a person is considered *lo meheiman* — untrustworthy — and cannot take an oath in court.

### Shav vs. Bitui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא הָנָךְ, דְּאִית בְּהוּ כְּפִירַת מָמוֹן; אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ הָא נָמֵי, דִּכְפִירַת דְּבָרִים בְּעָלְמָא הוּא – לָא מְהֵימַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The mishna **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary. It is not necessary** to state that one who is suspected of falsifying **these** oaths is disqualified from taking oaths, **as they entail the denial of a monetary** claim, i.e., due to the false oath someone incurs financial loss, **but** it is necessary to state that **even that** oath, an oath taken in vain, **which** involves **merely the repudiation of one's verbal** commitment, nevertheless grants one the status of a person who is **not credible** and who is disqualified from taking oaths.

This prompts the question: *ve'litni nammi shvuat bittui* — why doesn't the Mishna mention *shvuat bittui* as well? One who has violated a *shvuat bittui* about the future — swearing to do something and then failing to do it — is also a kind of unreliable oath-taker. The Gemara's answer is precise: *ki katani shvua de'chi ka mishtava beshikra ka mishtava* — the Mishna discusses only oaths where at the moment of swearing the person already knew he was lying. *Shvuat shav*, by its nature, involves a falsehood that is known to be false at the very moment the oath is taken. *Shvuat bittui*, by contrast, often involves a future intention: *aval shvuat bittui de'ika lemeimar de'bekushta ka mishtava* — it is entirely possible that at the time of the oath the person genuinely intended to fulfill it, and only later circumstances intervened. Such a person is not necessarily a liar even if he ends up violating the oath. Therefore the Mishna does not list *shvuat bittui* as a basis for *chashud al hashvua*.

### Rashi: Past Oaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי נָמֵי שְׁבוּעַת בִּטּוּי! כִּי קָתָנֵי, שְׁבוּעָה דְּכִי קָא מִשְׁתְּבַע – בְּשִׁקְרָא קָא מִשְׁתְּבַע; אֲבָל שְׁבוּעַת בִּטּוּי, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר דִּבְקוּשְׁטָא קָא מִשְׁתְּבַע – לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let** the *tanna* **also teach** that falsifying **an oath on an utterance,** which is also an oath without monetary consequences, disqualifies one from taking oaths. The Gemara responds: **When** the *tanna* **teaches** which types of false **oaths** disqualify a person, it includes only oaths with regard to **which when one takes the oath, he is** at that time **falsely taking the oath. But** with regard to **an oath on an utterance,** such as an oath that he will eat a specific fruit that day, **where it can be said that he took the oath with true** intention and intended to eat that fruit, but ultimately failed to do so, the *tanna* **does not teach,** as one who does not fulfill such an oath retains his credibility to take oaths, since he did not consciously lie.

One might still object: does this not apply only to *shvuat bittui* about the future? What about *shvuat bittui* about the past — *achalti v'lo achalti* — where at the moment of the oath the person already knows whether he is telling the truth? The Gemara resolves this: *tanna shvuat shav v'chol dedami leh* — when the Mishna said *shvuat shav*, it intended everything comparable to *shvuat shav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח ״אוֹכַל״ וְ״לֹא אוֹכַל״; ״אָכַלְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? תָּנָא שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא וְכֹל דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: It **works out well** to make this distinction for one who is liable for an oath on an utterance like: **I will eat, or: I will not eat,** where it is possible that he was not lying when he took the oath. But with regard to oaths about the past like: **I ate, or: I did not eat, what can be said,** since he certainly took a false oath? The Gemara answers: **Teach** the mishna, i.e., understand it to mean: **An oath** taken **in vain and any** oath **that is similar to it,** i.e., that is clearly a falsehood, disqualifies one from further oath taking.

Rashi captures this elegantly in three words: *kol shvua lesha'avar* — every oath pertaining to the past. That is, both *shvuat shav* and the past-tense category of *shvuat bittui* share the characteristic that the person knew at the moment of the oath whether he was telling the truth. If someone is confirmed to have violated such an oath — through witnesses, through having brought a *korban*, or through some other form of public accountability — that confirmation renders him *chashud al hashvua*, and he is disqualified from taking oaths in court, causing the *shvua* to revert to the other litigant. Strikingly, this category applies to oaths taken entirely outside of court, in interpersonal or purely factual contexts — yet such violations render a person unfit to take oaths in litigation as well.

## Disqualifying Categories

### Disqualified List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה אֶחָד מֵהֶן מְשַׂחֵק בְּקוּבְיָא. הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? תְּנָא פְּסוּלָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְקָתָנֵי פְּסוּלָא דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **one of** the litigants **was a dice player,** or one who lends with interest, or among those who fly pigeons, or among the vendors of produce that grew during the Sabbatical Year, then the litigant opposing him takes an oath and receives payment of his claim. The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **these additional** examples of a person who is suspect with regard to oath taking? The Gemara explains: The mishna first **teaches** examples of people who are **disqualified by Torah law, and** then **teaches** examples of those who are **disqualified by rabbinic law.**

Continuing from the top of 47a, the Gemara addresses the Mishna's list of additional categories of individuals disqualified from taking oaths, causing the *shvua* to revert to the opposing litigant: *haya echad mehem mesachek bekuvya* — a dice player; *malveh b'ribit* — one who lends with interest; *u'mafrichei yonim* — dove keepers who exploit others' birds; *v'socharei shevi'is* — those who sell *shemita* produce. For all such individuals, *shekenegdo nishba v'notel* — the other party takes the *shvua* and collects. Even in a case that would ordinarily call for a *shvua d'oraisa* from the disqualified party, the *shvua* reverts to the opponent, who takes a *shvua d'rabbanan* and collects.

### Two Levels Psul

The Gemara asks: *hai tu lama li* — why must the Mishna enumerate all of these cases? The answer: *tana pesulei d'oraisa v'katani pesulei d'rabbanan* — the Mishna wished to list first a *psul d'oraisa*, namely *chashud al hashvua* arising from *shvuat shav*, which represents a disqualification at the Torah level. Then it listed gamblers and similar figures, who represent a category that the Rabbis created — a *psul d'rabbanan*. It is worth noting that these figures are usually discussed in the context of *psul eidus*, disqualification from testimony. Here we see that such individuals are equally disqualified from taking oaths in court. The criteria overlap, since both functions depend on reliability and believability. One area of distinction worth noting is that women, for instance, cannot serve as witnesses in monetary cases without the parties' consent, but they can certainly be litigants who take oaths — so the two categories, while overlapping, are not fully identical.

## Both Parties Suspect

### Yossi vs. Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן חֲשׁוּדִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: הֵיכִי תְּנַן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא יָדַעְנָא. הִלְכְתָא מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא יָדַעְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **both** litigants **were suspect,** the oath returned to its place; this is the statement of Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Meir says: Since neither can take an oath, they divide the disputed amount. **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: How is it** actually **taught?** What is Rabbi Yosei's opinion and what is Rabbi Meir's opinion? Rav Naḥman **said to him: I do not know.** Rava asked him: **What is the *halakha*?** Rav Naḥman **said to him: I do not know.**

The Mishna then addresses the case of *hayu shneihem chashudim* — what if both litigants are suspect and neither may take a *shvua*? The Mishna records a *machlokes*: *chazra hashvua limkoma* — the *shvua* returns to its place — in the name of Rabbi Yossi. Rabbi Meir says: *yachloku* — they simply split the disputed amount. *Yachloku* of course recalls the first Mishna of Bava Metzia and the opinion associated with Sumchus, and appears in various places across the Talmud.

### Rava's Inquiry

Rava posed a question to Rav Nachman: *heichi tnan* — whose opinion does our Mishna follow? And separately, *hilchesa mai* — what is the practical ruling? To both questions, Rav Nachman replied *lo yadna* — I do not know. The distinction between the two questions is subtle: *heichi tnan* seems to be asking in a more theoretical sense which opinion the Mishna endorses, while *hilchesa mai* asks for the bottom-line practical ruling. There may also have been some textual uncertainty about which Tanna held which position. Notably, Rav Nachman was well known for his sharpness in monetary law, so his double declaration of *lo yadna* suggests this encounter took place at an earlier stage of his career, before he had access to certain traditions that would clarify the matter.

### Confirming Yachloku

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יַחְלוֹקוּ. וְכֵן תָּנֵי רַב זְבִיד בַּר אוֹשַׁעְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יַחְלוֹקוּ. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: תָּנֵי רַב זְבִיד אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יַחְלוֹקוּ. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי: עֲבַד רַב נַחְמָן עוֹבָדָא – יַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated** that **Rav Yosef bar Minyumi says** that **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabbi Yosei says: They divide** the disputed amount. **And similarly, Rav Zevid bar Oshaya teaches** that **Rabbi Yosei says: They divide** the disputed amount. The Gemara records a slightly different version of this tradition: **There are** those **who say** that **Rav Zevid teaches** that **Rabbi Oshaya says** that **Rabbi Yosei says: They divide** the disputed amount. **Rav Yosef bar Minyumi said** that **Rav Naḥman ruled** in an actual **case** that the litigants **divide** the disputed amount.

Subsequently, Rabbi Yossi bar Minyumi quoted Rav Nachman as having said: *Rabbi Yossi omer yachloku* — Rabbi Yossi is the one who says *yachloku*. This is confirmed by Rav Zvid bar Oshaya, and another version attributes the same tradition to Rabbi Yehoshua, who was in Eretz Yisrael and may have had access to a different textual tradition of the Mishna. Furthermore, Rav Yosef bar Minyumi reports: *avad Rav Nachman uvda yachloku* — Rav Nachman actually ruled in practice that *yachloku*. The convergence of these traditions indicates both that Rav Nachman ultimately concluded the *halacha* is *yachloku*, and that he understood this to be the opinion of Rabbi Yossi, whom we generally follow in disputes with Rabbi Meir.

### Text Inversion Theory

The implication is that our standard text of the Mishna — which attributes *chazra hashvua limkoma* to Rabbi Yossi and *yachloku* to Rabbi Meir — may have the opinions reversed. That inversion is what created the confusion, since normally we follow Rabbi Yossi over Rabbi Meir, and once Rav Nachman received traditions indicating that Rabbi Yossi's position was actually *yachloku*, the path to his ruling became clear.

## Limkomah Interpretations

### Rav & Shmuel View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָזְרָה שְׁבוּעָה לִמְקוֹמָהּ. לְהֵיכָן חָזְרָה? אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי, רַבּוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁבְּבָבֶל אָמְרוּ: חָזְרָה שְׁבוּעָה לְסִינַי, רַבּוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל אָמְרוּ: חָזְרָה שְׁבוּעָה לַמְחוּיָּב לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § Rabbi Yosei rules in the mishna that in a case where both sides are suspect and cannot take an oath, **the oath returned to its place.** The Gemara asks: **To where did it return?** What is meant by the oath returning to its place? **Rabbi Ami said** that **our Sages in Babylonia say:** The **oath returned to Sinai,** where God administered an oath to the Jewish people that they would keep the mitzvot of the Torah, including the prohibition against robbery. The litigant who is robbing the other will be punished by God, not the court. **Our Sages in Eretz Yisrael said: The oath returned to** the one **who** was initially **liable to** take **it,** i.e., the defendant, and since he is disqualified from taking an oath, he must pay.

Given that *chazra hashvua limkoma* is now understood to be the opinion of Rabbi Meir — and therefore not the adopted *halacha* — the Gemara nonetheless proceeds to analyze what the phrase would mean if applied. The question is: *l'heichan chazra* — where exactly does the *shvua* return to? Rabbi Ami reports: *Raboseinu shebiBavel amru chazra shvua l'Sinai* — the Rabbis of Babylonia say the *shvua* returns to Sinai, meaning it simply evaporates. Without an operative *shvua*, the case reverts to a normal monetary dispute without any *shvua* mechanism: no oath is taken, and without proof, no one can collect. As Rav Papa clarifies, *Raboseinu shebiBavel* throughout the Talmud refers specifically to Rav and Shmuel.

### Rabbi Abba View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: רַבּוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁבְּבָבֶל – רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, רַבּוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – רַבִּי אַבָּא. רַבּוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁבְּבָבֶל רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל – דִּתְנַן: וְכֵן הַיְּתוֹמִין, לֹא יִפָּרְעוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: מִמַּאן? אִילֵּימָא מִלֹּוֶה – אֲבוּהוֹן שָׁקֵיל בְּלָא שְׁבוּעָה, וְאִינְהוּ בִּשְׁבוּעָה?! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְכֵן הַיְּתוֹמִין מִן הַיְּתוֹמִין – לֹא יִפָּרְעוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** that the term: **Our Sages in Babylonia,** refers to **Rav and Shmuel;** and the term: **Our Sages in Eretz Yisrael,** refers to **Rabbi Abba.** The fact that the term: **Our Sages in Babylonia,** refers to **Rav and Shmuel** is **as we learned** in the mishna (45a): **And likewise, orphans may collect** a loan **with** a promissory note inherited from their father **only by** taking **an oath; and we discussed it** (see 48a), asking: **From whom** do they collect a debt by taking an oath? **If we say** that they collect this way **from the borrower,** i.e., those who borrowed from their fathers, that would not make sense. **Their father would take** payment with the promissory note **without** taking **an oath, and they** collect only **by means of** taking **an oath?** Don't orphans have privileged status? **Rather, this** is what the mishna **is saying: And even orphans do not collect** with their father's promissory note **from** the borrower's **orphans, except by means of** taking **an oath.**

The Rabbis of Eretz Yisrael, by contrast — identified specifically as Rabbi Abba — hold: *chazra shvua limchuyav lah* — the *shvua* returns to the party who was originally obligated to take it. Since that party cannot swear, the effect is that he must pay instead. The logic is that we treat the situation as if that party had been given the choice of swearing or paying, and having forfeited the ability to swear, he must pay. In a case where Reuven would have taken a *shvuat ed echad* to deny a loan claim but cannot because he is *chashud al hashvua*, the *shvua* reverts to him directly — and his inability to take it translates into an obligation to pay.

## Orphans' Loan Case

### Orphans' Oath Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁמֵּת מַלְוֶה בְּחַיֵּי לֹוֶה; אֲבָל מֵת לֹוֶה בְּחַיֵּי מַלְוֶה – כְּבָר נִתְחַיֵּיב מַלְוֶה לִבְנֵי לֹוֶה שְׁבוּעָה, וְאֵין אָדָם מוֹרִישׁ שְׁבוּעָה לְבָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav and Shmuel both say** that the Sages **taught** that the lender's orphans need to take an oath in order to be paid by the borrower's orphans **only when** the **lender died during the lifetime of** the **borrower,** and the lender's children had an opportunity to collect directly from the borrower without taking an oath. **But** if the **borrower died during the lifetime of** the **lender,** the **lender has already become liable** to take **an oath to the children of** the **borrower,** since one can collect from orphans only by means of an oath, **and a person cannot bequeath an oath,** i.e., a debt that requires the taking of an oath in order to be collected, **to his children,** and no payment is made. The lender's children cannot take the oath that their father would have taken, that the promissory note has not been paid. The only oath they can take is that their father never told them that it had been paid, and that is insufficient once the father became liable to take an oath. From here it is clear that Rav and Shmuel hold that when no oath can be taken, the oath returns to Sinai, and the court takes no action.

To demonstrate the practical difference between these two approaches, the Gemara cites the Mishna: *v'chein hayetomim lo yifra'u ela bishvua* — orphans should not repay loans except by taking a *shvua*. The Gemara immediately asks what case this refers to. It cannot mean that whereas the borrowing father took the money without a *shvua*, his orphaned heirs must repay with one — that would be anomalous. Rather, the Mishna must be speaking of orphans paying back loans to other orphans: *hayetomim min hayetomim lo yifra'u ela bishvua*.

### Borrower Dies First

Rav and Shmuel both rule: *lo shanu ela shemet malve bechaye loveh* — this applies only when the lender died during the borrower's lifetime. In that scenario, the borrower became obligated to pay the lender's heirs, the debt having transferred to the estate, and he would have to take a *shvua* in connection with that repayment. But if it is the lender who died first: *aval met loveh bechaye malve, kevar nitchayev malve livne loveh* — the borrower is already obligated to the lender's heirs, and that obligation transfers straightforwardly. The case involving orphans on both sides, then, arises specifically when the borrower died first and the lender died afterward, leaving two sets of *yetomim* facing each other.

### Oath Non-Inheritance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵימָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״מָנֶה לְאַבָּא בְּיַד אָבִיךָ״, וְאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״חַמְשִׁין אִית לֵיהּ וְחַמְשִׁין לֵית לֵיהּ״ – מָה לִי הוּא וּמָה לִי אֲבוּהָא? אֶלָּא לָאו דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״מָנֶה לְאַבָּא בְּיַד אָבִיךָ״, אָמַר לֵיהּ: ״חַמְשִׁין יָדַעְנָא וְחַמְשִׁין לָא יָדַעְנָא״?
>
> **Translation:** Rabba continues: **Granted, if you say** that **his father,** in **a case like this, would be liable** to take an oath, due to his partial admission, then the **verse was necessary to exempt the heirs** from taking the oath. **But if you say** that in **a case like this, his father is also exempt** from taking an oath, **why do I** need **a verse about** exempting the **heirs?** Evidently, an oath reverts to one who is liable to take it, and when he cannot take that oath he must pay the claim against him.

Here the crucial principle emerges: *v'ein adam morish shvua livno* — a person cannot bequeath a *shvua* obligation to his heirs. The *shvua* is an interpersonal obligation, something that cannot pass from father to son the way a monetary debt can. The heirs lack the personal knowledge of the details of their father's financial relationship with the lender — they can only swear *shelo pikadnu abba*, that their father never informed them that he had repaid the loan, which is simply not the same *shvua* that the father himself would have been obligated to take. Rashi formulates this precisely: a person cannot bequeath to his heirs money that carries a *shvua* obligation — *ain adam morish livno mammon shehu mitchell ayev alav shvua* — because the oath has an irreducibly interpersonal character that cannot be inherited. This point also surfaces in Masechet Kiddushin in the second *perek*, where related concepts are discussed.

### Rav & Shmuel Conclusion

According to Rav and Shmuel, since the *shvua* cannot be inherited and yet would have been required for the debt to be collected, the *shvua* evaporates — *chazra shvua l'Sinai* — and with it, effectively, the liability of the heirs to pay in a case where no operative *shvua* can be administered. This is the position of the Babylonian Rabbis: the disappearance of the *shvua* mechanism dissolves the ability to adjudicate the case in favor of the claimant.

## Stolen Metal Case

### Theft Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּחֲטַף נְסָכָא מֵחַבְרֵיהּ; אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אַמֵּי, יְתֵיב רַבִּי אַבָּא קַמֵּיהּ. אַיְיתִי חֲדָא סָהֲדָא דְּמִחְטָף חַטְפֵהּ מִינֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אִין, חֲטַפִי – וְדִידִי חֲטַפִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The fact that the term: **Our Sages in Eretz Yisrael,** refers to **Rabbi Abba** is as the following story illustrates. **As** there was **a certain man who snatched a piece of cast metal from another.** The one from whom it was taken **came before Rav Ami** while **Rabbi Abba was sitting before him,** and he **brought one witness** who testified **that** the other man **did,** in fact, **snatch it from him.** The one who snatched it **said to him: Yes,** it is true that **I snatched** it, **but I** merely **snatched** that which was **mine.**

The Gemara now presents the famous case of *nascha de'Rabbi Abba* — Rabbi Abba's stolen-metal case — which appears also in Bava Batra 33b and serves here to confirm Rabbi Abba's position. The facts: *dehahu gavra dechataph nascha michavrei* — a man snatched a piece of silver from another. *Aytei chad sahada de'mechataph chataph minei* — one witness testified that he had snatched it from the alleged victim. However, the defendant responded: *in chataph, bedidi chataph* — yes, I snatched it, but I snatched what was originally mine; he was claiming he was recovering his own property from someone who had wrongfully taken it.

### Rabbi Ami's Dilemma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הֵיכִי לִידַיְּינוּ דַּיָּינֵי לְהַאי דִּינָא? נֵימָא לֵיהּ ״זִיל שַׁלֵּים״ – לֵיכָּא תְּרֵי סָהֲדִי. נִפְטְרֵיהּ – אִיכָּא חַד סָהֲדָא. נֵימָא לֵיהּ ״זִיל אִישְׁתְּבַע״ – כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״מִיחְטָף חֲטַפִי״, הָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּגַזְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said: How should judges rule in this case?** If they were to **say to** the one who snatched the metal: **Go pay** for it, that would not be the correct ruling, because **there are not two witnesses** who saw him snatch it, and the court does not force payment based on the testimony of one witness. If they were **to** accept his claim and **exempt him** entirely, that would not be the correct ruling, because **there is one witness** who testified against him. If they were to **say to him: Go take an oath,** which is the usual response to counter the testimony of one witness, **once he said** that he did in fact **snatch** it, and there is no proof that it is his, **he is like a robber,** and the court does not allow a robber to take an oath.

Rabbi Ami, before whom the case came, was sitting with Rabbi Abba observing as a student. Rabbi Ami articulated the dilemma: *heichi ledayanu dayanei lehai dina* — how can judges adjudicate this case? If we tell him to pay — *zil shalem* — there are not two witnesses; a single witness cannot impose monetary liability by himself. If we simply exempt him — *niftereih* — there is still one witness who saw him snatch it. And if we tell him to swear — *zil ishtava* — which would normally follow from a single witness, the defendant has forfeited that path: *keivan de'amar mechataph chataph, hava lei ke'gazlan* — because he admitted to snatching it, he is treated like a *gazlan*, and a *gazlan* cannot take the *shvua* of one-witness denial. The choice of the formulation *hava lei ke'gazlan*, rather than *hava lei gazlan*, has attracted much discussion, but is left here as a point of interest.

### Abba's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא: הָוֵה מְחוּיָּב שְׁבוּעָה וְאֵין יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע, וְכׇל הַמְחוּיָּב שְׁבוּעָה וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע – מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said to him: He** is one who **is liable** to take **an oath who is unable to take an oath, and anyone who is liable** to take **an oath who is unable to take an oath** is liable to **pay.** This illustrates that Rabbi Abba holds that the oath returns to its place, i.e., to the defendant, who is disqualified from taking oaths, and that consequently he must pay.

Rabbi Abba responded with a general principle: *hava mechuyav shvua v'ein yachol lishava* — he is obligated to take a *shvua* but cannot swear. *V'chol hamechuyav shvua v'eino yachol lishava, meshalem* — anyone who is obligated to take a *shvua* and cannot do so must pay. This is the operative meaning of *chazra shvua limchuyav lah* as understood by the Rabbis of Eretz Yisrael: the *shvua* does not evaporate but returns to the party who was obligated to take it, and since that party cannot swear, he pays. Rabbi Abba's case thus provides concrete proof of the Eretz Yisrael position.

The contrast with Rav and Shmuel's approach is now vivid. Under Rav and Shmuel's view, if the *shvua* mechanism fails — because the person cannot swear — the *shvua* disappears and the liability dissolves. Under Rabbi Abba's view, the inability to swear does not dissolve the case; it converts the obligation from swearing to paying.

### Rava's Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי אַמֵּי: ״שְׁבֻעַת ה׳ תִּהְיֶה בֵּין שְׁנֵיהֶם״ – וְלֹא בֵּין הַיּוֹרְשִׁין. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: It stands to reason** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Abba; as Rabbi Ami teaches** this *baraita*: The verse states that **"the oath of the Lord shall be between them both"** (Exodus 22:10), **but not between their heirs. What are the circumstances** in which one would be liable to take an oath, but his heirs would be exempt?

Rava states explicitly: *kevaseih de'Rabbi Abba mistabra* — Rabbi Abba's approach makes better sense. In a case where a party is *mechuyav shvua* and cannot take it, it stands to reason that payment follows. The Babylonian *amora* Rava, operating later than Rav and Shmuel, thus sides with the Eretz Yisrael position of Rabbi Abba.

## Rabbi Ami's Teaching

### Psuk Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵימָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״מָנֶה לְאַבָּא בְּיַד אָבִיךָ״, וְאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״חַמְשִׁין אִית לֵיהּ וְחַמְשִׁין לֵית לֵיהּ״ – מָה לִי הוּא וּמָה לִי אֲבוּהָא? אֶלָּא לָאו דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״מָנֶה לְאַבָּא בְּיַד אָבִיךָ״, אָמַר לֵיהּ: ״חַמְשִׁין יָדַעְנָא וְחַמְשִׁין לָא יָדַעְנָא״?
>
> **Translation:** Rabba continues: **Granted, if you say** that **his father,** in **a case like this, would be liable** to take an oath, due to his partial admission, then the **verse was necessary to exempt the heirs** from taking the oath. **But if you say** that in **a case like this, his father is also exempt** from taking an oath, **why do I** need **a verse about** exempting the **heirs?** Evidently, an oath reverts to one who is liable to take it, and when he cannot take that oath he must pay the claim against him.

The *psuk* of *shvuat Hashem tihyeh bein shneihem* — God's oath shall be between them — is the source invoked by Rabbi Ami for the principle that *shvuot* operate *bein shneihem v'lo bein hayorshin* — between the original parties, not between their heirs. The Gemara asks: *heichi dami* — what precisely is the case that makes this *drasha* necessary?

### Uncertain Borrowing

The case is specified: one orphan comes to court and says *maneh l'abba beyad avicha* — my father lent your father a hundred dollars. The responding orphan says *chamishin yadana v'chamishin lo yadana* — fifty I know about, fifty I do not know about. This is a case of *lo yadana*, which is a doubtful, uncertain claim — not an outright denial. The importance of the distinction between *lo yadana* and an outright denial is that a flat denial of part of a claim — *modeh b'miktzat* — would generate a *shvua d'oraisa*, while a *lo yadana* response does not, since uncertainty is not a sufficiently firm position from which a *shvua* can be taken. The *shvua* of *modeh b'miktzat* requires that the person swear with certainty about what he denies; if he says he does not know, he lacks that certainty.

### Psuk Necessity

The Gemara then reasons: *bishlama aviv k'hai gavna mechayev* — if we assume that in such a case the father would himself have been obligated to take a *shvua d'oraisa* (as in the parallel case of *modeh b'miktzat* where certainty exists), *itzrich kra l'miftar gabei yorshim* — then the *pasuk* is needed to teach us that the heirs are nonetheless exempt from that *shvua* because a *shvua* cannot be inherited. But *ella ee amart aviv k'hai gavna nami patur* — if you would say that even the father in such a case of *lo yadana* is already exempt because the *shvua* cannot attach to a doubtful claim, *kra gabei yorshim lama li* — then the *pasuk* serves no purpose with respect to the orphans. The *pasuk*'s necessity thus proves that Rabbi Abba's position is correct: there is a category of case in which a *shvua* would have been operative for the father but cannot be transferred to the heirs, and it is precisely for that case that the *pasuk* teaches that the heirs are exempt — and, by implication, that the party who cannot swear must pay.

## Rav & Shmuel Derasha

### Both Parties Affected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל – הַאי ״שְׁבֻעַת ה׳״ מַאי קָא דָּרְשִׁי בֵּיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״שְׁבֻעַת ה׳ תִּהְיֶה בֵּין שְׁנֵיהֶם״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַשְּׁבוּעָה חָלָה עַל שְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rav and Shmuel,** who hold that one who cannot take an oath does not have to pay, and therefore there is no difference between the heirs and the father, **what do they derive from this** verse: **"The oath of the Lord** shall be between them both" (Exodus 22:10)? The Gemara answers: **It is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Shimon ben Tarfon says:** The verse: **"The oath of the Lord shall be between them both," teaches that** when one litigant imposes an oath on the other, and he takes a false oath, **the oath applies to them both,** i.e., they are both held responsible for the desecration of God's name.

Having established what Rabbi Ami uses the *pasuk* of *shvuat Hashem tihyeh bein shneihem* for — and what Rabbi Abba's position implies — the Gemara asks: *v'Rav u'Shmuel hai shvuat Hashem mai k'darshi bei* — how do Rav and Shmuel interpret this *pasuk*, if they use it differently from Rabbi Ami? The answer: *mibayei leih l'kid'tanya* — they need it for the following *baraita*. Shimon ben Tarfon — apparently the son of Rabbi Tarfon, a figure who appears rarely in the literature — teaches: *shvuat Hashem tihyeh bein shneihem melamed she'hashvua chala al shneihem* — the *pasuk* teaches that the *shvua* lands on both of them. The precise meaning of this is somewhat difficult, but the Artscroll explains it as a moral-spiritual principle: if the defendant swears falsely, both the defendant and the plaintiff are subject to divine retribution — the defendant for invoking God's name in vain, and the plaintiff for his negligence in entrusting his property to an unreliable person, thereby bringing about a desecration of God's name. This is clearly a moral reading of the *pasuk* rather than a strictly *halachic* one. It is a striking *drasha*, and its precise implications are not fully transparent from the text.

### Tarfon's Other Teachings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַזְהָרָה לָעוֹקֵב אַחֵר נוֹאֵף, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תִּנְאָף״ – לֹא תַּנְאִיף. ״וַתֵּרָגְנוּ בְּאׇהֳלֵיכֶם״ – שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: תַּרְתֶּם וְגִינִּיתֶם בְּאׇהֳלוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם. ״עַד הַנָּהָר הַגָּדוֹל נְהַר פְּרָת״ – שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: קְרַב לְגַבֵּי דְהִינָא, וְאִידְּהַן. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: עֶבֶד מֶלֶךְ – כְּמֶלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Since this Sage was mentioned, the Gemara cites some of his other statements. **Shimon ben Tarfon says:** With regard to the **prohibition of following after an adulterer,** i.e., providing him with assistance in carrying out adultery, **from where** is it derived? **The verse states: "You shall not commit adultery [*lo tinaf* ]"** (Exodus 20:13). If the verse is vocalized slightly differently, it may be read: **You shall not cause adultery [*lo tanif* ].** Commenting on the verse describing the response of the Jewish people to the spies' slander of Eretz Yisrael: **"And you murmured [*vatteragenu*] in your tents** and said: Because the Lord hated us, He has brought us forth out of the land of Egypt, to deliver us into the hand of the Amorites, to destroy us" (Deuteronomy 1:27), **Shimon ben Tarfon says:** "*Teragenu*" is interpreted as though it is composed of two Hebrew expressions: **You explored [*tartem*]** the land, **and: You disparaged [*ginnitem*]** it, **in the tent of the Omnipresent.** With regard to the verse: **"As far as the great river, the river Euphrates"** (Deuteronomy 1:7), **Shimon ben Tarfon says:** Although it is not the largest river, the Euphrates is called great in accordance with the adage: **Draw close to the** one **anointed with oil and become anointed** as well. Because the Euphrates is close to Eretz Yisrael, it is called great. The **school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** a similar idea: **The servant of a king is like a king.**

The Gemara continues with additional teachings of Shimon ben Tarfon, gathered here apparently because this is almost the only place he appears. He teaches: *azhara l'okeiv achar noef minayin* — how do we derive a prohibition not only against adultery itself but against facilitating another's adultery? Rashi explains *okeiv achar hamenaef* as someone who acts as a kind of agent or procurer, hiring himself out to arrange adulterous encounters for another. Shimon ben Tarfon derives the prohibition from the verse *lo tinaf* — read as *lo tanif*, do not cause others to commit adultery, not only do not commit it yourself.

There follow two additional *drashot* from Shimon ben Tarfon. One involves the *pasuk* of *vatargenu b'ohaleichem* from Devarim 1, describing the *meraglim*: *tartem u'ganitem b'ahalo shel makom* — you scouted the land and you drove away God's presence from among you. Another involves the verse *ad hanahar hagadol nehar perat* — the great river, the Euphrates. Shimon ben Tarfon explains why a relatively small river is called great: the principle expressed in *debei Rabbi Yishmael* that *eved melech k'melech* — the servant of a king is like the king. Being near the land of Israel confers greatness. These *drashot* are admittedly unusual, and the Gemara brings them together simply because Shimon ben Tarfon is mentioned so rarely that all his teachings are clustered here.

## Storekeeper Case

### Three-Party Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַחֶנְוָנִי עַל פִּינְקָסוֹ כּוּ׳. תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי: טוֹרַח שְׁבוּעָה זוֹ לָמָּה? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי חִיָּיא (בַּר אַבָּא), תְּנֵינָא: שְׁנֵיהֶם נִשְׁבָּעִין וְנוֹטְלִין מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **the storekeeper** relying **on his ledger** takes an oath and receives payment. If an employer tells a storekeeper to pay his laborers, and the storekeeper claims he paid them, while the laborers claim that they did not receive payment, both the storekeeper and the laborers take oaths and receive payment from the employer. **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: Why** is there the **bother** with **this oath,** that it is imposed upon both of them? **Rabbi Ḥiyya said to him: We learn** in the mishna (see 45a) that **both of them take an oath and receive** payment of their claims **from the employer.**

The Gemara now turns to the Mishna's case of *chanvani al pinkaso* — the storekeeper's ledger, returning to the text of the Mishna at 45a. The scenario involves three parties: a householder (*ba'al habayit*), a storekeeper (*chanvani*), and workers (*po'alim*). The householder instructed the storekeeper to advance wages to his workers, recording the transaction on his wax tablet. The storekeeper claims he paid the workers; the workers deny having received payment. The Mishna states that *hu nishba v'notel* — the storekeeper swears and collects from the householder — and *v'hein nishba'im v'notlin* — the workers swear and collect as well, leaving the householder paying twice. The lesson, practically speaking, is that a householder who makes such arrangements without keeping proper records risks losing doubly.

### Rabbi's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ, אוֹ לָא קִיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: פּוֹעֲלִין נִשְׁבָּעִין לַחֶנְוָנִי. וְאִם אִיתָא, לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Did** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **accept from him** that this is the *halakha*, **or did he not accept it from him? Come** and **hear as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: The laborers take an oath to the storekeeper** that he had not paid them. **And if it is so** that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi accepted Rabbi Ḥiyya's ruling, then Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **should have** instead said that the laborers take an oath **to the employer.**

Rabbi raises the objection: *torach shvua zo lamah* — why do we impose a *shvua* at all in this situation? This resonates with the earlier objection in Bava Metzia associated with Ben Nanas: if the storekeeper says he paid and the workers say they never received payment, someone is obviously lying. Rashi connects Rabbi's question directly to Ben Nanas's principle — *keitzad elu v'elu ba'im lidei shvuat shav* — how can we allow both parties to come to a *shvuat shav*? The concern is that by enabling both to swear and collect, we are institutionalizing a mechanism through which, in any given case, one party will necessarily be swearing falsely.

### Both Parties Swear

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba responded to Rabbi with the ruling of the Mishna: *shneihem nishba'im v'notlim miba'al habayit* — both parties swear and collect from the householder. The question then becomes whether Rabbi accepted this answer — *kiblah minei o lo kiblah minei* — or not. A *baraita* is cited to clarify.

### Rabbi's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: פּוֹעֲלִים נִשְׁבָּעִין לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת – בְּמַעֲמַד חֶנְוָנִי; כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִיכַּסְפוּ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Do not conclude that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi did not accept Rabbi Ḥiyya's ruling. Rather, interpret his statement as follows: **The laborers take an oath to the employer in the presence of the storekeeper, so that they will feel ashamed** to lie **with him** present, since he knows whether or not he paid them.

The *baraita* indicates: *Rebbe omer po'alim nishba'im l'chanvani* — Rabbi's own position is that the workers swear to the storekeeper directly. Under this arrangement, the workers' oath runs to the storekeeper, and the storekeeper would not then separately swear to the householder — eliminating one layer of potentially contradictory oaths. The objection to this reading, however, is that if the oath were directed to the householder it would have to say so, implying that Rabbi's position removes the householder entirely from the oath mechanism.

### Rava's Solution

Rava offers yet another formulation: *po'alim nishba'im l'ba'al habayit b'ma'amad chanvani* — the workers swear to the householder, but specifically in the presence of the storekeeper. The storekeeper himself does not take a separate oath. The reasoning is *ki heichi d'lichsof minei* — so that the presence of the storekeeper, who knows the truth of the matter, will exert social and moral pressure on the workers to tell the truth. This restructuring avoids the problem of two contradictory oaths while still providing a mechanism for resolution.

## Conflicting Witnesses

### Contradiction Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: שְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים הַמַּכְחִישׁוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ – אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: זוֹ בָּאָה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ וּמְעִידָה, וְזוֹ בָּאָה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ וּמְעִידָה. רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: בַּהֲדֵי סָהֲדֵי שַׁקָּרֵי לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** about a similar topic that if there were **two sets of witnesses who contradict one another,** and it is clear that one set must be testifying falsely, **Rav Huna says: This** set can **come by itself and testify** about other cases, **and that** set can **come by itself and testify.** Neither set of witnesses is disqualified for future testimony, since there is no way of knowing which was lying. **Rav Ḥisda said: Why do I** need to become involved **with lying witnesses?** Since each set of witnesses is possibly untrustworthy, both sets are disqualified.

The Gemara uses the storekeeper case as a springboard to introduce a broader problem: *shtei kitei eidim hamachishot zo et zo* — two sets of witnesses who contradict each other. The scenario is one in which two independent groups of witnesses offer testimony that cannot both be true.

### Rav Huna View

Rav Huna rules: *zo ba'ah bifnei atzmah u'meidah v'zo ba'ah bifnei atzmah u'meidah* — each group comes independently and testifies, and each is believed for the case it addresses. That is, the two sets of testimony are treated as independent and are each accorded weight in their respective contexts.

### Rav Chisda View

Rav Chisda's position leaves the matter in a more unresolved state — something akin to the case of *shtei shvilim*, where two paths of equal uncertainty leave the matter in a kind of legal suspension or result in splitting the outcome. The Gemara breaks off at this point, with the analysis of conflicting witnesses to be continued in the next session.
