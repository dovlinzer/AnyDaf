# Taanit 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Adda bar Abba Merit

### Intro: Falling Buildings

Today's *daf* is 21. We pick up in the middle of 20b, continuing with the discussion of Rav Adda bar Abba — the rabbi whose extraordinary *zechus* allowed those who accompanied him to walk past falling buildings and shaky walls without fear. As we already saw in an earlier story, he was not even fully aware that people were deliberately routing themselves near him to benefit from his protection. The *gemara* now turns to explore the source of his merit, and what emerges is a portrait of complete immersion in *Torah* — which, in a way, explains why he might have been somewhat oblivious to what was happening around him.

### Students Ask Long Life

The passage opens with the words *itmar* — "it was said." *Sha'alu talmidav* — his students asked him: *bameh ha'arachta yamim* — in what merit did you live to such an old age? Note that whereas the earlier story focused on his protecting others from falling buildings, here the question is about the merit that granted him long life. His students wanted to understand what deeds or qualities had sustained him.

### Merit: Torah & Respect

*Amar lahem* — he said to them: *miyami lo hikpadeti betoch beiti* — in my entire life, I never became upset about things within my house, which presumably refers to his wife or family members. *Velo tza'adti bifnei mi shegadol mimeni* — I never took even a single step ahead of someone greater than me. *Velo hirharti bemevo'ot hametunafot* — I never thought *Torah* thoughts in filthy alleyways or places inappropriate for *Torah*, such as a bathroom. *Velo halachti daled amot belo Torah u'velo tefillin* — and I never walked four *amot* without *Torah* and without *tefillin*. Perhaps the exception being when he passed through those same filthy alleyways — but in any case, he was always wrapped up, physically, literally, and metaphorically, in *Torah*. *Tefillin* itself represents *Torah*, so he was always bound to *Torah* in both senses.

*Velo yashanti be'veit hamedrash lo shinat keva velo shinat arai* — I never went to sleep in the *Beit Hamedrash*, not a temporary doze and not a sustained sleep. This reflects both his respect for the sanctity of the *Beit Hamedrash* and his perpetual engagement in learning. *Velo sashti betakalat chaveri* — I never rejoiced in the stumbling of my friend. There is a German word for that: *Schadenfreude*. *Velo karati lachaveri bechanichato* — and I never called my friend by a *kinuy*, a nickname. There are two versions given — *bechanichato* and *bechanichato* — one referring to a nickname he gave himself, another referring to a nickname others gave him; or alternatively, one meaning a nickname others used, and the other referring to a family name or surname, a *shem mishpachah*, that even though it may technically be the person's name, calling someone casually by their last name can fall short of the full respect they deserve.

### Nicknames & Respect

There is an interesting *Tosafot* on the word *bechanichato*. *Tosafot* explains that the first version refers to *kinuy shekorin oto bnei adam lignai* — a nickname used somewhat in a derogatory sense. This raises a question about whether there is a *geder* — a boundary — against using even a non-derogatory nickname if it still falls short of full respect. The second version, *Tosafot* explains, refers to *kinuy shem mishpachto* — a family surname, what is called a *shem livai*, or *shem chanichat avotav*, as discussed in *Gittin* on 85 or 88. As an example, *Tosafot* cites *Rabbi Avraham Ibn Ezra* — not because his father's name was Ezra, but because everyone in his family bore the name *Ibn Ezra* as a family name. *Tosafot* adds *davka lignai* — that it is only prohibited if done in a derogatory way, as when one dismissively says, "Hey, Ibn Ezra." *U'leshevach mutar* — if used respectfully, as we say "the *Ibn Ezra* says," it is permitted. What is striking here is that *Tosafot* needed to illustrate the concept of a family name using the *Ibn Ezra* as an example, which reflects the fact that family names were not yet prevalent in the era of the *Ba'alei Tosafot* — and it is equally notable that the *Ibn Ezra*, who was not primarily a *gemara* commentator, is brought as the illustration.

Looking at the full list of Rav Adda bar Abba's qualities, it is not immediately obvious what their single common denominator is. But taken together, one discerns a beautiful balance: on the one hand, complete, uninterrupted immersion in *Torah* — he never went four *amot* without it, never dozed in the *Beit Hamedrash*, never allowed inappropriate thoughts to intrude; and on the other hand, a profound humility and sensitivity toward others — he never stepped ahead of his betters, never rejoiced at another's misfortune, never denied anyone their full dignity of address. In this way, the story reads as something of a constructive contrast to an earlier account of a *talmid chacham* whose immersion in *Torah* bred a certain haughtiness. Here is someone equally absorbed in *Torah* — perhaps so absorbed that he did not notice others sheltering in his shadow — yet who simultaneously knew his place, gave others their due, and treated every person with respect.

## Rav Huna: Community Works

### Building Inspection

The *gemara* continues. Rava said to Rafram bar Pappa: *leima lan mar me-hanei milei ma'alyata de-hava avid Rav Huna* — tell us some of the great things that Rav Huna used to do. Having just heard a list of great deeds, the *gemara* now presents another such catalogue, this one focused on Rav Huna's communal works.

Rafram bar Pappa responded: *be-ankusei lo dechirna* — I don't remember things from his youth. *Be-seivutei dechirna* — but from his older years, I remember. *De-chol yoma de-eiva* — every cloudy day, *havu mafki leh be-guharkei de-dahava* — they would bring him out in a golden seat, carried aloft. One's eyebrows naturally go up at the mention of a gold throne — it sounds rather ostentatious. But let us see what he did with it. *Ve-sayer leh le-chulah mata* — he would inspect the entire city. *Ve-chol ashista de-hava rei'ata* — every wall that was shaky — and here again we return to the recurring theme of shaky walls — *hava satar lah* — he would demolish it. *Ei efshar le-mar benei lah* — if the owners had the means, they were required to rebuild it themselves, since a hazardous wall was their responsibility. *Ve-i lo efshar, benei lah ihu mi-dideih* — and if they lacked the means, he would rebuild it from his own funds.

One commentator suggests that Rav Huna deliberately went out in the gold chair because he would pass through non-Jewish neighborhoods, and the display of wealth gave him credibility — a man of such means who concerned himself with the community's welfare would be taken seriously and followed. There is also the reading that it was a display of authority, a kind of official inspection. In any case, the picture is striking: here is someone who takes considerable *kavod* for himself, traveling in a golden seat, and yet is entirely and proactively engaged with the material welfare of the community, using his own funds to ensure public safety.

### Price Support Markets

*U-ve-chol panya de-ma'alei shabbata* — every *erev Shabbat*, *hava meshader shlucha le-shuka* — he would send a messenger to the marketplace. *Ve-chol yarka de-hava payish lehu le-ginai* — any produce or food that was left over late Friday afternoon, which clearly could not be kept without refrigeration and would be discarded, *zavin leh* — he would buy it up. *Ve-shadi leh le-nahara* — and throw it into the river. He had no use for the food; the purpose was to encourage merchants to bring their full stock to market, so that the community would have ample food available for *Shabbat* and sellers would not fear that their wares would go unsold.

### Why Not Give Poor

The *gemara* raises an obvious question: *ve-liteiv le-aniyim* — why not give the leftover food to the poor rather than throw it into the river? The *gemara* answers: *zimnin de-samcha da'atayhu ve-lo atu le-mizban* — because then people would assume that Rav Huna would buy everything up and distribute it, and so they would not bother to purchase food for *Shabbat* themselves. But why would that be a problem — let him just do that? Rashi explains: *de-nisamchu ahanei u-ve-li lo atu le-mizban* — if people relied on the end-of-day giveaway, and Rav Huna only bought what was left over at the last moment, perhaps there would be nothing left and they would have nothing to eat for *Shabbat*. He did not want them to become overly dependent on that.

The *gemara* continues: *ve-lishdyeih le-behemah* — well, at least give it to animals? The answer: *kasavar ma'achal adam ein ma'achilin livheima* — he held that one does not feed human food to animals. This is somewhat paradoxical, since the food is going to waste regardless. Apparently, feeding human food to animals is considered more disrespectful to the food — or perhaps more dehumanizing in some sense — than simply discarding it. One might raise the *pasuk* of *l'ger asher b'isharecha titnena o machor l'nochri*, which speaks of giving *treif* meat to non-Jews, but *treif* meat is outside the boundary of what one's community would eat, which makes that a different case.

*V'lo yizbeney klal* — so why bother buying it at all? Because *nimtzas machshilan l'atid lavo* — not buying it would harm the economy and discourage merchants from bringing their goods to market, which would ultimately hurt the community.

The story presents an interesting tension: on the one hand, Rav Huna goes out in a gold chair, which represents a certain ostentatiousness of wealth; on the other hand, he is deeply concerned with communal well-being and acts entirely from his own funds. And the whole logic of the market intervention — why not give it to the poor, why not feed it to animals — reflects a careful, practical engagement with economic reality. These *chachamim* are not miracle workers. They operate entirely in a real-world mode, which reads, in some sense, as a sustained counterpoint to the *Choni* model.

### Public Health Care

*Ki hava leh milsa d'asuta* — when Rav Huna had some type of remedy or medicine, *hava malei kuza* — he would fill a container, *v'taley lei b'sifa d'dasha* — and hang it outside his house, *v'amar kol d'tzarich l'eisei v'lishkol* — so that anyone who needed the medicine could come and take it. This served two purposes: it made medicine available to the community, and it allowed people to take it privately, without having to announce that they were sick or come to him directly.

*V'ika d'amrei* — some say the version is slightly different: he knew the matters relating to *shivsa* — a harmful spirit said by the *gemara* to afflict one who fails to wash hands — *hava manach kuza d'maya*, and he would fill a vessel of water, *v'amar kol d'tzarich l'eisei v'liel, d'lo l'istakein* — so that anyone who needed water to wash their hands should come and do so, that they should not be endangered. In either version, Rav Huna is attending to public health concerns, making provisions available to all without requiring them to seek him out personally.

### Hospitality Meals

*Ki hava karich rifta* — when he would eat bread, when he would break bread for a meal, *hava patach l'vaveih* — he would open his doors, *v'amar kol man d'tzarich l'eisei v'lechol* — and announce: anyone who needs to eat should come and eat. It evokes the *Seder* night declaration: *kol dichfin yeitei veyeichol* — whoever is hungry, let him come and eat.

### Rava Response Limits

Rava responded: *kuleihu matzinan mekaymi* — I could fulfill all of these practices — though one wonders whether Rava had the financial means, yet presumably he felt he could. *Livar meiha* — except for that last one, *d'lo matzina l'me'avad* — which I cannot do. *Mishum d'nafishei benei Mechuza* — because the population of Mechuza is simply too large. He could not open his doors to everyone without being completely overwhelmed. The word in the text appears to be *l'vaveih* — his doors — rather than his heart, that is, he opened his gates and called anyone who wished to enter. Rava acknowledges that even the most admirable model has its practical limits.

## Ilfa & Yochanan

### Leave for Business

At the top of 25a appears what is now a well-known story. *Ilfa v'Rav Yochanan havah garsi b'Oraisa* — Ilfa and Rav Yochanan were learning *Torah*, *dachika lehu milta tuva* — but they were suffering greatly from financial hardship. *Amru: neikum v'neizil v'na'avid iska* — they said: perhaps we should set aside the learning and go into business, we have to start earning money. *U'nekayeim b'nafshein: efes ki lo yihyeh becha evyon* — and we should fulfill in ourselves the verse "there shall be no poor among you." That is, *lo yihyeh becha* — you yourself should not be poor. You have a responsibility not to remain impoverished, and going out to support yourself is a legitimate obligation.

*Azlu, osevi tusei guda* — they went and, on their journey away from the *Beis Medrash*, were sitting beneath a bridge — and again notice the recurring theme: shaky walls, shaky bridges. We have already encountered the motif of the shaky wall whose collapse was held in abeyance by the merit of a great man. The background is very much present here: sitting under a shaky bridge, without sufficient merit, is not a safe place to be.

### Angel Debate

*Havah ka karchi rifta* — they were eating. *Asu terei malachei hashareis* — two heavenly angels came. *Shama Rav Yochanan* — Rav Yochanan overheard the angels speaking to each other. One said to the other: *nishdi aleihu hai guda v'niktlineihu* — let us cast this bridge upon them and kill them. Why? *Shemanichim chayei olam v'oskim b'chayei sha'ah* — because they have abandoned eternal life, the learning of *Torah*, and are occupying themselves with the temporal life of making money. Such a grave abandonment warrants death. *Amar lei idach: shavkeinu* — the second angel replied: leave them be. *D'ika behu chad, d'kaima lei sha'ata* — because there is one among them for whom the time is standing, meaning he is destined to rise to great heights and must not be harmed.

### Yochanan Returns

*Rav Yochanan shama, Ilfa lo shama* — Rav Yochanan heard this conversation; Ilfa did not. *Amar lei Rav Yochanan l'Ilfa: shama mar midi?* — Did you hear anything? *Amar lei: lo* — No. *Mi'desham'ei ana v'Ilfa lo shama, shma mina l'didi kaima li sha'ata* — since I heard it and Ilfa did not, it must be that they were speaking about me; my time has come in terms of *Torah* greatness. *Amar lei Rav Yochanan: ahadar* — I am going back to the *Beis Medrash*. *V'okei b'nafshai: ki lo yechdal evyon mikerev ha'aretz* — and I will fulfill in myself not the first verse but the second: "the poor shall never cease from the land." There will always be poor people; let it be me. I will be one of the poor, and I will go back and learn *Torah*.

### Ilfa Success

*Rav Yochanan hadar* — Rav Yochanan returned to the *Beis Medrash*. *Ilfa lo hadar* — Ilfa did not return. *Ad d'ata Ilfa* — by the time Ilfa came back, after what may have been many years out in the world earning his fortune, *amlikh Rabbi Yochanan* — Rabbi Yochanan had become king, meaning he had been appointed *Rosh Yeshiva*. As Rashi notes on the third narrow line of his commentary — *minuhu Rosh Yeshiva* — and it was the custom that whoever was appointed *Rosh Yeshiva*, *hayu migadlin oto mi-she-lahen u-ma'ashirin oto* — they would elevate him and make him wealthy from their own resources. Rashi cites a parallel with the *Kohen Gadol*, where the verse *veha-Kohen ha-Gadol mei-echav gadleihu mi-shel echav* is interpreted to mean that his brothers would help make him the greatest among them. So Rabbi Yochanan's poverty was no longer a concern — his position brought with it the material support he needed. He was given a desk, an office, and the community's backing.

### Ilfa Regrets

*Amru lo* — they said to Ilfa: *ei otiv Mar ve-garis, lo havei malikh Mar* — if you, the master, had remained in the *Beis Medrash* and continued learning, it would have been you who was appointed *Rosh Yeshiva*, not Rabbi Yochanan. You were sharper, you knew more — had you only stayed, it would have been you. One can imagine how that made him feel.

### Mast Challenge

*Azal tali nafshia be-askarya de-sefunta* — so he went and suspended himself from the mast of the ship, the great central pole. *Amar: ei ika de-sha'il li be-matnita de-Rabbi Hiyya ve-Rabbi Oshaya* — if anyone can ask me anything from the *braitot* of Rabbi Hiyya and Rabbi Oshaya — who are the ones who edited the *braitot* and the *Tosefta* — *de-lo pashitna lei mi-matnitin* — and I cannot demonstrate that it is already rooted and anchored in a *Mishnah*, *nafilna mi-askarya de-sefunta ve-tavana* — then I will cast myself from the mast and drown. It is a little dramatic, admittedly, but the point is clear: I will prove that my mastery is complete — not only do I know *Mishnah* and *braita*, I can show you how they are all organically connected.

### Deathbed Wishes

Someone took him up on his challenge. *Tana lei ha-omer tenu shekel le-vanai be-shabbat* — he was taught the following *braita*: if someone is dying and leaves a trust for his children, instructing the trustees to provide each child with one *shekel* per week. *Ve-hem re'uyim latet lahem sela* — but the children's reasonable needs require a *sela*, which is worth more than a *shekel*. What does one do? *Notnim lahem sela* — one gives them the *sela*. The principle invoked is *yesh bi-khlal matayim maneh*: if he said "give them a hundred," he did not say "give them only a hundred" — two hundred is still a hundred, it is already contained within it. Therefore one may give more.

*Ve-im amar al titnu lahem ela shekel* — but if he explicitly said "give them no more than a *shekel*," *ein notnim lahem ela shekel* — then one may give them only a *shekel*, no more.

Now, if he said give them a certain amount weekly, but added: *metu yirshu acherim tachteihem* — if they die, others should take their place as beneficiaries of the trust — then *bein she-amar tenu bein she-amar al titnu, ein notnim lahem ela shekel*: whether he said "give them" or "don't give them more," you give them only the stated amount. Why? Because the money is not ultimately all going to these children — there are other intended recipients — and therefore one cannot apply the same interpretive latitude. The implied intent was that the stated amount was precise, not that the children should eventually receive the full fund.

### Knowledge Mastery

*Amar lei: ha mani Rabbi Meir hi* — this *braita*, Ilfa declared, follows the position of Rabbi Meir. For Rabbi Meir holds that *mitzvah lekayem divrei hamet* — it is a *mitzvah* to fulfill the instructions of the deceased. The entire *braita* is working out the parameters of how to interpret what the dying person actually meant — does saying "give them a hundred" mean exactly one hundred, or at least one hundred, and does the answer depend on other conditions he stipulated? But all of that elaboration rests on the foundational premise that one must honor the deceased's instructions, a principle established in the *Mishnah* under Rabbi Meir's ruling.

This is not exactly the same point as the *Mishnah*, of course — there is considerably more elaboration in the *braita* than in the *Mishnah* itself. But Ilfa's claim is that he has all the material organized: he knows not only the *braitot* but can locate them, situate them within the *Mishnayot*, and demonstrate which *Mishnah* each *braita* is working out. What he is demonstrating is less a display of sharp analytic ability and more a command of the *corpus* in its entirety — organized, linked, categorized, and coherent. This is distinct from the person the *gemara* sometimes describes dismissively as a *chamor nosei sefarim*, a donkey laden with books — someone who knows a great deal of information but has not integrated it. What Ilfa demonstrates is mastery in the sense of having it all meaningfully organized and interconnected.

One student in the shiur noted astutely that the *gemara* does not use the terms *braita* or *Tosefta* here — it says "their *Mishnah*" (*matnitin de-Rabbi Hiyya*) as opposed to "our *Mishnah*" (*matnitin*). This is correct. The use of *matnitin* — our *Mishnah* — carries the implication of canonicity and authority. Linking everything back to *our Mishnah* arguably strengthens rather than levels the hierarchy: all the other compilations are in some sense derivative of or secondary to the canonical *Mishnah*. Nowhere in the *gemara* do we sense that *braita* and *Mishnah* stand on an equal playing field.

The broader point of the Ilfa and Rav Yochanan story seems clearly to be a critique of Ilfa's choice. We have just been reading about the importance of using one's funds and resources for the community — and there is much to be said for taking care of the material world. But that should not be read as a license to abandon *Torah* entirely in favor of business. Rav Yochanan, who returned to the *Beis Medrash* and accepted poverty, ultimately became *Rosh Yeshiva* and had his poverty resolved through his position. Ilfa, who went out to seek his fortune and succeeded, came back to find himself displaced — and rather than reflecting quietly on what he had lost, he climbed a ship's mast to make a spectacle of his learning. The critique falls clearly on him.

One student offered a beautiful additional reading: the *braita* about deathbed wishes and the trust fund may itself be a kind of mirror for the story. The deceased leaves a limited fund, and the question is whether one stretches the interpretation to give the heirs more, or adheres to the stated terms. Rav Yochanan, in effect, said: I will accept the *divrei hamet*, I will live within the constraints, I will be poor — *ki lo yechdal evyon* — and not try to stretch beyond what is given. Ilfa tried to stretch, to find more, and ultimately came back to find that the canonical appointment had been filled by another.

## Nachum Ish Gamzu

### Physical Afflictions

The *gemara* now brings the stories of Nachum Ish Gamzu. *Amru alav al Nachum Ish Gamzu shehaya suma mishtei einav* — they said about him that he was blind in both eyes. *Gidem mishtei yadav* — he had lost both arms. *Kiteia mishtei raglav* — both his legs were cut off. *Vechol gufo malei shechin* — and his entire body was covered with boils and sores. *Vehaya mutal beveit ra'ua* — and — another link to the recurring theme — he was in a shaky house. *Veraglei mitato munachim besefalim shel mayim* — the legs of his bed were placed in buckets of water, *kdei shelo ya'alu alav nemalim* — so that ants could not climb up onto him, since he had no limbs with which to push them away.

### House Protection

*Pa'am achat hayta mitato munachat beveit ra'ua* — once, his bed was in a shaky house. *Bikshu talmidav lifnot mitato* — his students wanted to move his bed out first so the house would not collapse on him — paralleling the earlier story where there was concern about rescuing vessels from a shaky structure. *Ve'achar kach lifnot et hakelim* — and only then remove the other vessels.

*Amar lahem: banai, panu et hakelim ve'achar kach panu et mitati* — he said to them: my children, take out the vessels first and then take me out. *Shemuvtach lachem shekol zman she'ani babayit ein habayit nofel* — for I assure you, as long as I am in the house it will not fall down. Here, in contrast to the earlier Rav Adda bar Abba story where his *Torah* protected him without his awareness, Nachum Ish Gamzu is fully conscious of the protection surrounding him. The painful irony, of course, is that the house is not falling — and yet look at what has already befallen his body.

*Pinu et hakelim ve'achar kach pinu et mitato venafal habayit* — they followed his instructions: took out the vessels, took him out, and the house collapsed. A direct parallel to the earlier story.

### Question Afflictions

*Amru lo talmidav* — his students then said to him: *Rebbi, vechi me'achar shetzadik gamur ata, lama alta lecha kach* — Rabbi, since you are so righteous that your merit protected the house from falling, how could such terrible things have happened to your body?

### Poor Man Neglect

*Amar lahem banai, ani garamti le'atzmi* — he said: my children, I brought it upon myself. *Shepa'am achat hayiti mehalekh baderekh leveit chami* — once I was traveling on the way to my father-in-law's house. *Vehaya imi masui gimel chamorim: echad shel ma'achal, echad shel mishteh, ve'echad shel minei megadim* — and with me I carried the load of three donkeys: one of food, one of drink, and one of delicacies. The evocation of *Eved Avraham* traveling laden to his future father-in-law's house is difficult to miss.

*Ba ani echad ve-amad li ba-derech ve-amar li: Rabbi, parneseni* — a poor person came and stood before him on the road and said: my master, support me, feed me. *Amarti lo: hamten ad she-efrok min ha-chamor* — I said to him: wait a moment while I unload it from the donkey. I have bundles of food; let me untie them and I will give you something. *Ve-lo hispakti lifrok min ha-chamor ad she-yatzata nishmato* — but I did not manage to unload from the donkey before the man died of hunger.

One student suggested that Nachum may not have meant "wait while I untie the bundle right here on the road" but rather "wait until I reach my destination and unpack" — a reading that would make the sin considerably more serious. Rather than meeting the man's immediate need, he was in effect saying: come see me later, in the office. If that reading is correct, the guilt is far more understandable.

### Self-Imposed Curses

*Halachti ve-nafalti al panav ve-amarti* — I went and fell upon his face and said: *einai she-lo chasu al einecha, yasumu* — my eyes that had no compassion on your eyes, may they go blind. *Yadai she-lo chasu al yadecha, yitgadmu* — my hands that had no compassion on your hands, may they be cut off. *Raglai she-lo chasu al raglecha, yitkate'u* — my legs that had no compassion on your legs, may they be cut off. *Ve-lo nitkarrera da'ati ad she-amarti: kol gufi yihiye malei shechin* — and I was still not calmed until I said: let my entire body be filled with boils.

*Amru lo: oy lanu she-re'inucha be-kach* — they said to him: woe to us that we have seen you in such a state. *Amar lahem: oy li im lo re'ituni be-kach* — he said: woe to me had you not seen me like this, for then I would not have been properly punished for my wrongdoing.

This is a striking passage, and a genuinely difficult one. The question of what exactly he did wrong is not simple. His food was packed in bundles on the donkeys — what was he supposed to do? One might argue he should have anticipated encountering poor people and kept food more readily accessible. One might also note, as was suggested in discussion, that the word *hamten* — "wait" — already signals a distancing. A different person might have said yes immediately, might have been thinking ahead about how to help rather than how to manage his luggage. The contrast with Rav Huna, who went out daily, proactively and in his gold chair, inspecting the city for how he could be useful, is perhaps the deeper point: here is someone absorbed in his personal journey, traveling to his father-in-law laden with goods, not thinking of himself as someone who might have a communal obligation along the way.

And yet his reaction — self-imposing the most extreme physical afflictions imaginable — seems difficult to justify, let alone to hold up as a model. To an extent, the *gemara* studying *Masechet Ta'anit* has not been especially focused on *teshuvah* through bodily self-punishment; that theme is largely absent from the *masechet*'s dominant framework. And to impose upon oneself blindness, the loss of limbs, and a body covered in sores over what might have been, at worst, a momentary hesitation, seems far beyond any proportionate response. It is difficult to read this straightforwardly as an instructive model. That said, the *gemara* does not seem to be presenting it as a counter-example either — the tone is not critical of Nachum Ish Gamzu, even if his response strikes us as extreme.

## Gam Zu Letovah

### Name Etymology

*Amai karu lei Nachum Ish Gamzu?* — Why was he called Nachum Ish Gamzu? *De-chol milta de-hava salka lei amar gam zu letovah* — because whatever happened to him he would say: *gam zu letovah*, this too is for the best. Holding that alongside everything we just read about his afflictions, this is an impressive statement.

### Caesar Gift

*Zimna chada bau leshadurei Yisrael doron levei keisar* — once, the Jewish people wanted to send a gift to the house of Caesar. *Amru man yeizil yezil Nachum Ish Gamzu, demelumad benissin* — they said: let Nachum Ish Gamzu go, for he is accustomed to having miracles occur to him. One of the miracles, of course, that had occurred to him was that his prayers for self-affliction were fulfilled — he did not cut off his own arms, so presumably the power of his prayer brought about what happened to his body. Whether this episode took place before or after his terrible physical state is not entirely clear. In any case, they knew that miracles attended him.

*Shadru bideih malei sifta de'avnin tavan umargaliyot* — they sent in his hands a full crate of precious jewels and gems. *Azal bat behahu dira* — he traveled and lodged for the night at a certain inn. *Beleilya kamu hanach daryei veshaklinhu lesifta* — in the night the other lodgers got up and took everything from his crate. *Umalunhu afra* — and filled it with dirt.

*Ki mata hatam, sareinhu lesifta, chazyu demalu afra* — when he arrived at Caesar's house, they opened the crate and saw it was full of dirt. *Ba'ah malka lemaktalinhu lekulhu* — the king wanted to kill Nachum and his entire retinue. *Amar kamachaychi bi Yehuda'ei* — the Jews are mocking me. *Amar gam zu letovah* — this too will be for the best.

### Magic Dust

*Ata Eliyahu* — Elijah the prophet appeared. Perhaps he was waiting to see how Nachum would respond, and now that Nachum had maintained his faith, *Eliyahu* came. *Idmi lei kechad mineihu* — he appeared as one of the Caesar's own retinue. *Ve-amar lei: dilma hai afra me'afra de-Avraham avuhon* — perhaps this is the dust of Avraham their forefather. *Dechi havah shadi afra havah sayyifei* — for when Avraham would throw dust it would turn to swords, *vegila havah girei* — and when he would throw straw it would turn to arrows. The *pasuk* in Yeshayahu — *mi he'ir mimizrach*, who was stirred from the east — is understood to refer to Avraham, that his dust was like his swords and his straw was like his bow.

### Subdue Country

*Havah chada medinta dela matzu lemichbasha* — there was a certain country the Caesars had not yet been able to conquer. *Badku mineih vechavshuha* — they tested this dirt against that country and were able to subdue it, for when thrown it turned to swords and destroyed the enemy. *Ayiluhu levei ganzeih* — they brought Nachum Ish Gamzu and his retinue into the treasure house. *Umaluhu lesifteih avnin tavan umargaliyot* — and filled his crate with precious jewels — the very thing he had come to bring them, they now gave him. *Veshadruhu beyikra raba* — and sent him out with great honor. It is hard not to notice the parallel with Avraham going down to Egypt and being sent out with great wealth.

### Innkeepers Killed

*Ki atu batu behahu dira* — when he returned, he lodged again at the same inn. *Amru leh: mai ayis behadach de'avdi lach yikra kulei hai?* — they said to him: what did you bring to Caesar that he gave you such honor? *Amar lehu: mai de-shakli mehacha amti lahatam* — whatever I took from here when I left, that is what I brought there. Understanding this to mean the dirt of their inn was somehow magical, *setaru ledireihu* — they knocked down their entire inn, *ve-amtinu levei malka* — and brought it to Caesar. *Amru leh: hai afra dayti hacha min didan hu* — the dirt that Nachum brought you was our dirt. *Badkuhu vela ashkachuh* — they tested it and it was not magical. *Vkatlinhu lehanhu darya* — and they were all killed.

### Faith Critique

Everything, then, came out for the best — at least for Nachum and the Jewish people, if not for the innkeepers. But it is worth pausing to consider what the *gam zu letovah* approach carries with it. It is a beautiful and deeply faithful stance — in the face of catastrophe, to hold firm to the conviction that God's hand is guiding events and it will all turn out well. And for Nachum himself, that faith was vindicated. But the approach can also mask a detachment from responsibility. If everything is already going to be for the best, one might not feel sufficient urgency to guard one's jewels, to take practical precautions, or — and here we return to the earlier story — to respond immediately and decisively when a hungry man appears on the road. The *Yosef* model, one might say, is *gam zu letovah*: God's plan will unfold. The *Yehuda* model is to step forward, take responsibility, and act. Taken as an all-encompassing life philosophy, *gam zu letovah* risks, at its edges, a kind of withdrawal from the urgent demands of the world around us.

## Dever Definition

### Dever Parameters

The *gemara* now returns to its legal discussion: *eizu dever* — what constitutes pestilence (*dever*) severe enough to warrant fasting? The *Mishnah* established a baseline: a city that sends out 500 foot soldiers and loses three people to disease over three days. *Tanu rabanan* — the *gemara* now expands this with a *braita*: a city like Akko with 1,500 able-bodied men — interestingly called a *kfar* (village) despite its size — from which nine people die over three days, one after another, *harei zeh dever* — that is pestilence. *Beyom echad o bearbaa yamim ein zeh dever* — if all the deaths occur in a single day, or conversely if spread over four days, it is not considered *dever*.

The reasoning on the time dimension deserves attention. The *gemara* correctly calculates: if 500 people means three deaths in three days, then 1,500 people still means three deaths per day — it is the same rate, which is still nine deaths over three days, not nine deaths over nine days. The point being that the severity threshold is a *daily rate*, not a cumulative number spread across more days. On the other side, if all nine die in one day, even though that seems more severe, it may represent a freak occurrence rather than a persistent epidemic — and if spread over four days, perhaps the situation is not severe enough to qualify.

A city with 500 men, like a smaller village, requires three deaths over three consecutive days to qualify. The same time conditions apply: a single day or four-day spread does not constitute *dever*.

### Drokrat Case

*Drokrat* was a city with 500 able-bodied men. *V'yatzeu mimenu shalosh meisim b'yom echad* — and three people died all in one day. *Gazar Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda tanisa* — Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda ruled that this constituted *dever* and declared a fast.

### Nachman Dispute

*Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: k'man k'Rabbi Meir?* — Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak challenged him: whose opinion are you following in ruling that three deaths in one day equals three deaths over three days? That is the position of Rabbi Meir, who argues with respect to a goring ox that if the ox gored three people in a single day it is a *muad* — a habitual gorer. The general rule is that an ox becomes *muad* by goring once a day over three consecutive days. But Rabbi Meir reasons: *rikhak negichosov chayav* — if spreading out the gorings makes it *muad*, then *kirev negichosov lo kol sheken* — if they are compressed into a shorter time, how much more so is the ox more dangerous? Nevertheless, the counter-position holds that intensity in a single day does not establish the persistent habit required for the designation. So too here: three deaths in one day may indicate something intense but not an ongoing epidemic.

## Anonymous Merit

### Move Invitation

*Amar lei Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda l'Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: leisay mar l'gaban* — Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda invited Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak to move to his city. The implicit suggestion was that Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda's city was a more significant place of *Torah*, and why remain in a smaller, less distinguished place?

### Person Honors Place

*Amar lei: tanina* — he replied: I will teach you a *braisa*. *Rabbi Yosi omer lo mekomo shel adam mechabdo ela adam mechabed es mekomo* — a person's place does not give him honor; it is the person who gives honor to his place.

*Sheken matzinu b'Har Sinai* — we find this demonstrated by Har Sinai. *Shekol zman shehashchina shruya alav* — as long as the Divine Presence dwelled upon it — *amra Torah: gam hatzon v'habakar al yiru el mul hahar hahu* — the *Torah* forbade even the animals from grazing near the mountain. *Nistalka shchina heimenu* — but when the *Shechina* departed from it, *amra Torah: bimshoch hayovel heimah yaalu v'har* — then people could ascend the mountain freely. It was never the mountain that was significant in itself; it was God's presence that mattered. Once that presence lifted, the mountain was just a mountain.

*V'chen matzinu b'Ohel Moed shebamidbar* — similarly with the *Ohel Moed* in the wilderness. *Shekol zman shehu natuy* — as long as it was erected and spread, *amra Torah: v'yishalchu min hamachaneh kol tzarua* — the *Torah* required all *tamei* people to be expelled from the camp, the *Ohel Moed* conferring holiness on the surrounding space. *Hukplu hayerios* — once the curtains were folded and the *Ohel Moed* dismantled, *hutru zavin u'metzorain l'ikanes sham* — all those who were *tamei* could freely enter that space. The space itself had no inherent sanctity; it was the *Ohel Moed*'s presence that created the significance.

Thank you very much — I am content where I am. My presence gives honor to my place; I do not need a place to confer honor on me. *Amar lei: i hachi neisay mar l'gavei mar* — so Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda then said: well, if that is the principle, perhaps I should move to you. It is somewhat humorous: the very principle you just cited argues that you honor your place, so why should I be the one to move? *Amar lei: mutav yavo maneh ben pras eitzel maneh ben maneh* — no, it is more fitting for a hundred the son of fifty to come to a hundred the son of a hundred. *V'al yavo maneh ben maneh eitzel maneh ben pras* — the son of a hundred should not go to the son of fifty. That is, Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda's father was himself a *Rav*, whereas Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's father was not. So the one with more distinguished lineage should not be moving toward the one of lesser lineage — rather, the lesser should come toward the greater. Whether Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak genuinely considered Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda the more distinguished person, or whether he was gracefully deflecting the earlier invitation by turning it on its head, is a matter of how one reads the exchange.

### Sura Shovel Lender

*Besura hava de-varta be-shivuvoteih de-Rav lo hava* — in Sura there was pestilence, but in Rav's own neighborhood there was none. *Savru minah mishum zechuteih de-Rav de-nafish* — the people assumed it was owing to Rav's great merit that his neighborhood was spared — again evoking the earlier stories of great men whose merit protected against physical harm. But now observe how the *gemara* pushes back. *Itchazi lehu be-chalma* — it was shown to them in a dream: *rav nafisha zechuteih tuva, ha milta zutra lei le-Rav* — Rav's merit is indeed great; this matter is too minor to require Rav's *zechus* to address it. *Ela mishum hahu gavra de-shayel mara ve-zavila likvura* — rather, this was on the merit of a certain unnamed person who would lend out a *mara* and *zavila* — a shovel and a digging tool — to help with burials. Because he performed this act of anonymous *chesed* at the moment of death, his merit was sufficient to protect the neighborhood from the plague.

### Drokrat Oven Woman

*Be-Drokert hava delikta u-ve-shivuvoteih de-Rav Huna lo hava delikta* — in Drokrat there was a fire, but in Rav Huna's neighborhood there was none. *Savru minah bizchuta de-Rav Huna de-nafish* — the people attributed this to Rav Huna's great merit. *Itchazi lehu be-chalma: hai zutra lei le-Rav Huna* — it was shown to them in a dream: this is too minor a matter for Rav Huna's merit. *Ela mishum hahi iteta de-mechamema tanura u-meshayela le-shivuvoteih* — rather, it was on the merit of a certain woman, unnamed, who, when she heated her oven and got it burning, would let her neighbors use the already-heated oven. The fuel was spent, the oven was already hot — come and use it, do not waste your own wood. That small, anonymous, daily act of generosity was enough to save the neighborhood from fire.

These two closing stories are a beautiful counterpoint to everything that came before. We are not denying that great rabbis possess great *zechus* and that *zechus* can protect from significant harm. The *gemara* does not say otherwise — it says these particular events were too minor for such great *zechus* to be needed. But the implication resonates: the small, quiet, anonymous deeds of ordinary people carry a power of their own. The woman who lets her neighbor use her heated oven. The man who lends out a shovel for burials. Neither of them climbed a ship's mast or was carried through town in a gold chair. No one knows their names. And yet the protection they generated — from pestilence, from fire — was real, tangible, and sufficient.
