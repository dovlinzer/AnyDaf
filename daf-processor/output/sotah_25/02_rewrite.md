# Sotah 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overes Al Das & Warning

### Definition of Overes Al Das…

The daf opens with a fresh topic, framed as an *ibayahu* — a question raised by the rabbis. Before examining that question, however, it is worth establishing the meaning of the central term: *overes al das*, a woman who transgresses the norms of married life. This concept originates in a *mishnah* in *Kesubos* that distinguishes between *das Moshe* — biblical norms — and *das Yehudis* — the recognized practices of Jewish women. The category is not about a woman who sins against God; it is specifically about behaviors that affect the marriage itself. The Gemara's examples of *overes al das Moshe* include a woman who does not go to the *mikvah* or who feeds her husband non-kosher food. Notably, the Gemara does not include *chilul Shabbos* in this list, because a woman who violates Shabbos has sinned between herself and God — that transgression does not intrinsically damage the marital relationship. By contrast, *overes al das* describes conduct that compromises the intimacy of the marriage, or that, in the idiom of the Mishnah, attracts unwanted attention from other men.

The Mishnah enumerates behaviors such as going out with uncovered hair, nursing in public, or engaging in flirtatious interactions with men outside the household. Translated into contemporary terms, these are behaviors perceived as undermining the private sphere of the marriage — drawing the gaze, or worse, the involvement, of other men, whether practically or in the imagination. These behaviors resemble the *sotah* situation in spirit, though they fall short of it in substance: a woman who has not committed adultery, and has not even been subjected to *kinuy* and *stirah*, may nonetheless be acting in ways that garner her husband's jealousy and compromise the relationship. If the husband seeks to divorce her on account of such conduct, he may do so without paying her *kesuvah* — she is considered to be at fault, and it is a fault divorce. That is the legal framework of *overes al das*.

### Warning Requirement?

The Gemara now poses its question: does a woman who is *overes al das* require warning — *hasra'ah* — in order to forfeit her *kesuvah*? The framing of the question is important. The Gemara is not asking whether *hasra'ah* is required in the same sense as in criminal law, where warning is necessary to establish *meizid* and enable corporal punishment. Rather, the Gemara is asking whether the husband — or *beis din* — must first inform the woman that her behavior is unacceptable and that continued conduct will lead to divorce without a *kesuvah*, before that consequence can take effect.

The Gemara lays out two sides of the question. On one hand, *keivan d'overes al das hi lo ba'ei hasra'ah*: since she has already acted inappropriately in a way that damages the marriage, perhaps no warning is needed — she has forfeited the *kesuvah* through her own conduct. On the other hand, perhaps she does require warning, *d'hadra bah* — so that she has the opportunity to mend her ways. This second framing is revealing: the concern is not that her conduct was technically insufficient, but that a broader, more humane consideration applies. Breaking up a marriage is a serious matter, and perhaps the healthier approach — one that the rabbis who establish the rule would endorse — is to first attempt repair rather than rushing to the most severe consequence. Warning signals that the situation can still be corrected, and that divorce without a *kesuvah* should be a last resort rather than an automatic outcome.

It is worth noting that this same logic does not apply where adultery has actually occurred. In that case there is a *halachic* reality — the woman is forbidden to her husband — and no amount of willingness to repair the relationship can set that aside. But *overes al das* operates in a different register: there is no absolute *halachic* prohibition, the relationship is not destroyed in a formal sense, and so the possibility of repair is genuine. Additionally, even if a husband wishes to claim that his wife was *overes al das* and refuse to pay her *kesuvah*, he would need to bring objective evidence before a court — the *kesuvah* is an obligation, and he cannot simply declare himself exempt.

One might also ask whether *shogeg* — the woman not realizing she was forbidden from acting in a certain way — bears on the question. The Gemara does not frame the issue in those terms. It does not say that *hasra'ah* is needed because she was acting unknowingly and therefore should not be penalized as harshly. Instead, the focus is on the broader consideration of preserving the marriage. It is nonetheless worth observing that the degree to which conduct damages a marriage may be affected by whether it was done knowingly or in ignorance — a woman who defies a known standard has communicated something different about her relationship than one who simply did not understand what was expected of her.

### Arusah/Shomeret Yavam

The Gemara attempts to resolve the question from a source. *Ta shema*: an *arusah* and a *shomeret yavam* do not drink and do not collect a *kesuvah*. What is accomplished by the husband performing *kinuy* in such cases? The Gemara initially suggests: *lav l'afsidei kesuvaseh* — it must be to cause her to forfeit her *kesuvah*. If so, this would prove that *kinuy* is required for that purpose, meaning that the mere fact of her going into seclusion with another man is not sufficient — you need the additional step of *kinuy*, which functions as a form of *hasra'ah*. Why else would the husband need to go through the process of warning her if she were going to lose her *kesuvah* simply by having gone into seclusion?

Abaye pushes back: *lo, le'osrah alav* — perhaps the purpose of the *kinuy* is not to cause her to lose her *kesuvah* at all. Rather, the husband performs *kinuy* because he wants to impose upon her the status of *sotah*, making her forbidden to him. This is a distinct legal goal — it is not about the money, but about creating a formal forbidden status. The *kinuy* can be used, in other words, not as a financial measure but as an act of imposing a religious-legal status that goes beyond the civil consequence of losing the *kesuvah*.

Rav Papa offers another answer: *lehashkosah k'she'yinasu* — the husband performs *kinuy* now, while she is an *arusah*, intending that if she later goes into seclusion after *nisuin*, he will be able to make her drink on the basis of the earlier warning. There is a *baraisa* that makes this explicit: one cannot make an *arusah* drink while she is an *arusah*, but one can perform *kinuy* while she is an *arusah* so that if she goes into seclusion after *nisuin*, she becomes a *sotah* on the basis of that prior warning. This is a legitimate strategic use of *kinuy* — keeping one's options open — and therefore the *kinuy* in the case of an *arusah* is fully explained without needing to appeal to the *kesuvah* at all.

Rava then offers a much stronger proof. *Ta shema*: the *mishnah* enumerates cases of *issurei lavin* — women whose marriages, though formally binding, are biblically prohibited. These include, for example, a widow married to a *Kohen Gadol*, a divorcée or *chalutza* married to an ordinary *kohen*, a *mamzeres* or *nesinah* married to a Yisrael, or a daughter of Israel married to a *mamzer* or *nasin*. In all these cases, the *mishnah* rules that such women do not drink and do not collect their *kesuvah*. Yet the husband still goes through the process of *kinuy*. Why?

This is a brilliant point because it eliminates both of Abaye's and Rav Papa's answers simultaneously. The answer *le'osrah alav* — to make her forbidden to him — cannot apply here, because she is already forbidden by a biblical prohibition; *hai issura deka'i* — the prohibition already stands. And the answer *lehashkosah k'she'yinasu* — to make her drink after *nisuin* — also cannot apply, because they are already in a state of *nisuin*; there is no future *nisuin* to wait for. So why is *kinuy* necessary at all? *Ella lav l'afsidei kesuvaseh* — it must be solely to cause her to forfeit her *kesuvah*. And the implication is that the *stirah* alone — the mere fact of her going into seclusion — would not have been sufficient. Something more was needed, and that something more is the *kinuy*, which here functions as a warning.

Rav Yehuda of Diskarta then steps in to rescue the earlier approach: *le'osrah labo'el k'ba'al*. The purpose of *kinuy* in the cases of *issurei lavin* is to make her forbidden to the man she is suspected of having slept with, just as she is forbidden to her husband. A *sotah* — a woman suspected of adultery — becomes forbidden both to her husband and to the man she is suspected of. If the husband performs *kinuy*, establishes the *sotah* process, and then for whatever reason divorces her without her ever having a chance to drink — because he never brought her to the *Beis HaMikdash*, or the *Beis HaMikdash* was destroyed — she remains forbidden to that other man. This is a significant and independent legal consequence of *kinuy*, entirely separate from the *kesuvah*. Therefore, one still cannot prove that *hasra'ah* is required for the *kesuvah* from these cases.

### Beit Din Acts for Incapa…

The Gemara pivots to a cleaner proof. *Ta shema*: the *mishnah* teaches that *beis din* steps in and performs *kinuy* on behalf of a husband in certain cases — when the husband became a deaf-mute, became insane or mentally incapacitated, or is imprisoned. And the *mishnah* specifies the purpose explicitly: *lifoslah miksuvashah* — to cause the wife to lose her *kesuvah*. This is not framed as being about making her forbidden to her husband or forbidden to the suspected paramour. It is stated plainly that *beis din* acts for this specific purpose.

This seems decisive. If going into seclusion were sufficient on its own to cause her to forfeit the *kesuvah*, why would *beis din* need to perform *kinuy* at all? The witnesses to her *stirah* could simply testify, and she would lose the *kesuvah* without any further step. The fact that *beis din* found it necessary to go through the process of *kinuy* indicates that *stirah* alone is insufficient — some form of warning is required, and in the absence of a capable husband, *beis din* steps in to provide that warning through *kinuy*.

### Conclusion: Warning Need…

The Gemara, however, entertains the possibility of distinguishing this case. Perhaps the reason *hasra'ah* is required for the *beis din* cases, even though in ordinary circumstances it would not be needed, is that the husband is absent. When the husband is not present — when he is deaf, insane, or imprisoned — the wife's behavior will naturally be less constrained, and her conduct therefore does not carry the same weight of defiance as it would if she were knowingly acting against her present husband's wishes. In that context, one might say that she cannot be penalized without warning, since her behavior is not as blameworthy. But when the husband is present and she still acts this way, it is worse — and perhaps there no warning would be needed.

The Gemara rejects this distinction. The conclusion stands: *ba'ei hasra'ah* — warning is required for a woman to lose her *kesuvah* on account of *overes al das*. This is a resolution that, remarkably, does not appear explicitly in *Kesubos*, where the entire topic of *overes al das* is discussed at length. The foundational question of what kind of evidence or warning is needed to forfeit the *kesuvah* is answered here, in *Sotah* — making this *sugyah* an essential clarification of material studied in a different tractate entirely.

## Husband's Right to Waive

### If Husband Doesn't Mind?

The Gemara now raises a second *ibayahu*, which is in many ways the more fundamental question. The scenario: the wife has been *overes al das*, but the husband does not object — *lo kapid*. He is aware of her behavior and is not troubled by it, or at least does not wish to end the marriage on account of it. Can he simply continue the marriage as if nothing happened, or does the court step in and require that the marriage be dissolved?

*Kapida* — or *hakpada* — is perhaps best rendered as something one finds deeply objectionable, something that genuinely disturbs. The question is whether the husband's subjective disposition — his not finding the behavior objectionable — has legal force in this context. The Gemara frames this as: *mi amrinan b'kapida d'ba'al tala Rachmana* — do we say that the whole structure of *overes al das* is contingent on the husband's *kapida*? And if he is *lo kapid*, the whole framework simply does not apply?

Or do we say, on the other side, *keivan deka kapid kapid* — meaning, since this behavior is the sort of thing that *should* bother him, it matters even if he says it does not? Rashi explains this phrase as: since this is conduct that is objectively objectionable, his claimed indifference is beside the point. This can be understood in two ways. One is psychological: perhaps he says he does not mind, but we know that deep down he does, and the marriage is being damaged even if he is not willing to acknowledge it. The other is societal: even if he genuinely does not mind, there is a broader communal interest in not allowing such behavior to be implicitly sanctioned by the continuation of the marriage. The court's involvement in marking certain conduct as unacceptable is not merely for the benefit of this particular husband; it sends a message about the standards of marriage in the community.

This is admittedly a striking position — the idea that a rabbinical court would compel the dissolution of a marriage that both parties wish to continue. But the comparison is apt: *halacha* already determines whether marriages can continue at all, and *beis din* is already empowered to intervene in ways that override the personal preferences of the individuals involved. The question is only how far that authority extends into the domain of conduct that, while objectionable, does not create an absolute *halachic* prohibition.

### Personal vs. Objective

The Gemara seeks to resolve the question from the *mishnah* about *beis din* stepping in for a deaf-mute, insane, or imprisoned husband. If the law were entirely subjective — if the husband's *kapida* were the sole determining factor — then *beis din* would have no basis to act on his behalf, since the husband might recover or be released from prison and then express that he does not mind his wife's behavior. How can *beis din* presume to act against his potential interests?

The Gemara responds with *stama demilta*: generally speaking, *keivan d'avras adas*, since she has violated these norms, we assume that *nicha lei* — it is in his interest for *beis din* to act. The presumption is that a normal husband would want his wife's *kesuvah* forfeited under such circumstances, and *beis din* acts on that presumption. This answer does not resolve the underlying debate — it does not tell us definitively whether a fully capable husband who explicitly states he does not mind can preserve the marriage — but it demonstrates that the mere theoretical possibility of his indifference does not paralyze *beis din* from acting in his name. The question of *ratzah lekayem mekayem* remains formally open at this stage of the discussion, though the trajectory of the *sugyah* implicitly moves toward an answer.

## Revoking Kinuy

### Retract Kinuy?

The Gemara now turns to a third *ibayahu*, this one concerning the *kinuy* in the *sotah* context proper rather than the *overes al das* framework. The husband has issued a *kinuy* — he has formally warned his wife not to enter into private seclusion with a specific man — and then he changes his mind. He becomes *mochel*: he forgives, or withdraws, his warning. Can the *kinuy* be revoked?

The Gemara frames the question around the phrase *tala Rachmana*, which now applies literally, since we are dealing with the *sotah* case: *kinuy* is rooted in the husband's jealousy and *hakpada*. One side says: since it was all driven by his objection, and he has now stopped objecting, the *kinuy* dissolves — *kinuyo machul*. The other side says: once he has created this formal legal act, he cannot simply retract it — *lo matzi machil leh*. This second position may be grounded in the idea that the *kinuy* is not merely the expression of a subjective emotional state but the invocation of a formal legal process that, once set in motion, operates independently of whether he continues to feel aggrieved.

More substantively, one can frame this as a question about what the *kinuy* fundamentally is. At the beginning of the *masechet*, this tension was already present: is *sotah* all about the husband's jealousy and *hakpada*, an outgrowth of the subjective dynamics of the marriage, or has the Gemara transformed it into an objective legal proceeding driven by *raglayim ladavar* and the involvement of *beis din*? If the former, then when he stops being jealous, the whole structure collapses. If the latter, then the process, once initiated, belongs to an objective domain and is no longer his to withdraw.

### Revoke After Stira?

The Gemara then raises the more challenging case: can the husband revoke his *kinuy* even after the wife has gone into seclusion — after *stirah* has occurred? At that point she has already become a *sotah*. He now says: I retroactively retract my *kinuy*. Can he do this?

The stakes of this question are high. If revocation is possible even after *stirah*, then the entire *sotah* process is ultimately dependent on the husband's ongoing willingness to pursue it, and the objective legal reality established by *stirah* counts for little. If revocation is not possible after *stirah*, then the moment she enters seclusion, the case passes from the subjective realm of his jealousy into the objective domain of *beis din*, and his feelings at that point become legally irrelevant.

The Gemara attempts several proofs. First it revisits the *mishnah* about *beis din* acting on behalf of a deaf-mute, insane, or imprisoned husband: if the husband could revoke his *kinuy* after the fact, then what *beis din* did — acting in his name — could be undone by him the moment he recovered. Would *beis din* really take an action that was so easily reversible? The Gemara answers: *stama demilta mistafi umaski keBeis Dina* — the presumption is that the husband will go along with what *beis din* has done. *Beis din* leads, and the husband follows. This is not because he is compelled to agree, but because the ordinary presumption is that he will follow the court's lead, and the rare case of someone refusing to do so does not prevent *beis din* from acting.

### Proof from Scholars

The Gemara then cites the *mishnah* about escorting the *sotah* to court with two Torah scholars, *shema yavo aleha baderech* — lest the husband succumb to temptation and have relations with her along the way, which would be a violation. But if the husband can simply revoke his *kinuy* and thereby permit himself to have relations with her — *achil lekinuyacha u'va'ol*, as the scholars could instruct him — then why is the concern about illicit relations relevant at all? He has a *heter* readily available!

The Gemara resolves this by explaining precisely why *talmidei chachamim* are specifically designated for this escort duty: because they know the *halacha*. If it appears that the husband is inclined to have relations with his wife, the scholars can inform him of the proper way to accomplish this — by first revoking his *kinuy*. So the purpose of the scholars is not to prevent any possibility of relations at all costs, but to ensure that whatever course of action the husband takes is done in a legally proper manner. This source, then, does not prove that revocation is impossible; it merely establishes that revocation is a knowledgeable act requiring proper instruction.

### Rabbi Ushaya's Teaching

The Gemara finds its resolution in a teaching of Rabbi Ushaya. He reports three things that were whispered to him by the young men of Jerusalem. First: *ba'al shemachal al kinuyo kinuyo machul* — a husband who revokes his *kinuy* has effectively revoked it. The *kinuy* is undone. Second: a *zaken mamre* — a sage who defies the ruling of the high court — if *beis din* wishes to be *mochel* and forgo the prosecution of his case, they may do so. Third: a *ben sorer u'moreh* — a wayward and rebellious son — if the parents wish to be *mochel*, they may do so.

These three cases share a common structure: in each, the authority of an institution is being challenged or undermined, and the question is whether the *personal* parties who were wronged can waive the institutional consequence. Rabbi Ushaya's position is that in all three cases, *mechilah* is effective. When Rabbi Ushaya brought these teachings to his colleagues in the south, they agreed with him on two of the three: the *sotah* case and the *ben sorer u'moreh* case. They disagreed on the *zaken mamre*: *shelo yarbu machloket beYisrael* — because the institution of the supreme court is not a private matter. There is one court for all of society, and its authority must be protected for the sake of social order. The defiance of the *zaken mamre* is not an offense against the personal dignity of the court's judges that they could choose to waive; it is a structural threat to the entire system of halachic authority. The parents in *ben sorer u'moreh* and the husband in *sotah*, by contrast, are each contending with their own private relationship, and they retain the authority to be *mochel* on it.

The Gemara notes the parallel to the Ramban's observation that four places in the Torah — *zaken mamre*, *ben sorer u'moreh*, *eidim zomemim*, and *navi sheker* — all invoke the phrase *vehanish'arim yishme'u veyira'u*, indicating that the punishment is not solely proportional to the gravity of the individual sin, but serves the broader purpose of protecting institutional authority in society. What is telling is that *sotah* is conspicuously absent from that list: *ki yikne ish et ishto* is explicitly rooted in the husband's jealousy, making *sotah* the paradigmatic case of a law that is anchored in the subjective realm of personal relationships, not in the protection of social institutions.

*Shema minah*: the husband can revoke his *kinuy*. The Gemara accepts this conclusion.

### Before vs. After Stira

Now that the principle of revocability is established, the Gemara returns to the harder question: can the husband revoke his *kinuy* after *stirah* has already occurred? Two Amoraim — Rav Acha and Ravina — debate this. One says: *kodem stirah machul, ve'achar stirah eino machul* — revocation is effective only before she goes into seclusion, not after. Once *stirah* has occurred, an objective *raglayim ladavar* has been created; the case enters the domain of *beis din*, and the husband's retroactive change of heart cannot undo it. The other says: *achar stirah nami machul* — even after *stirah* he can revoke, reflecting the view that the entire *sotah* structure remains fundamentally about his jealousy and subjective disposition, even after *stirah*.

The Gemara then resolves the debate. *Mistabra keman de'amar eino machul* — it is most reasonable to hold that after *stirah* he cannot revoke. The proof comes from the debate between Rabbi Yosi and the Sages recorded in a *baraisa*. Rabbi Yosi held that we need not send scholars to escort the *sotah* to court, because the husband can be trusted to control himself just as he controls himself when his wife is a *niddah*. The Sages responded: you cannot compare the cases. With *niddah*, there is relief in sight — she will eventually become permitted to him again — and so he will restrain himself. But with a *sotah*, she will never be permitted to him again — *ein lah heter* — and therefore he may not be able to restrain himself.

The Gemara seizes on this argument. If the husband can revoke his *kinuy* even after *stirah*, then there *is* a *heter* available to him — he can simply be *mochel* on the *kinuy* and resume relations whenever he wishes. In that case, the Sages' argument would collapse, because the husband would know he always has a ready remedy. The fact that the Sages argue, and that the Gemara treats their argument as compelling, implies that no such *heter* exists after *stirah*. *Ella lav shema mina achar stirah eino machul shema mina.*

The conclusion is thus a clean and elegant one: before *stirah*, the *kinuy* is entirely in the domain of the husband's subjective jealousy, and he may revoke it at will. After *stirah* — once she has gone into seclusion and the objective *raglayim ladavar* has been created — the case passes into the domain of *beis din*, and the husband no longer has the power to halt it unilaterally.

## Husband Dies Before Drink

### Shammai vs. Hillel

The Gemara turns the page and introduces a new scenario: the husband died before the *sotah* was able to drink the water. The process was initiated, the *kinuy* was made, the *stirah* occurred, but the husband did not survive to see the *sotah* drink. Now the question of *kesuvah* arises: is the woman entitled to collect her *kesuvah* from the husband's estate, or does she forfeit it?

*Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* dispute this. The underlying principle at issue is a general rule in monetary law: *Beis Shammai* holds *shtar ha'omed ligbos kegavui dami* — a document that is standing for collection is considered as if already collected. The *kesuvah* is such a document — it is an outstanding obligation to the woman, and since she holds it, she is in a position of possession. The burden of proof therefore rests on the estate to demonstrate that she is not entitled to it, i.e., that she actually committed adultery. Since that can no longer be proven — the *sotah* process was interrupted by the husband's death — she collects her *kesuvah*.

*Beis Hillel* disagrees: *shtar ha'omed ligbos lav kegavui dami*. The document standing for collection is not considered as if already collected. In this framework, the estate is still considered *muchzak* — in possession — and the burden of proof lies with the woman to establish her innocence. Since the *sotah* process was never completed and she cannot now prove her innocence, she does not collect.

What is striking about this formulation is that the question is framed not around whether she is currently a *sotah* or currently forbidden to her husband, but around the deeper question of whether she committed adultery. The existence of the *sotah* process and its incompletion are almost beside the point; the *kesuvah* dispute ultimately comes down to who bears the burden of proof on the underlying factual question. The *tosafos* on this passage are extensive, attempting to reconcile this *machlokes* with other *sugyos* in which the same rule of *shtar ha'omed ligbos* appears to operate differently, noting that the surrounding circumstances make a significant difference in how the rule is applied.

## Special Cases: Sotah

### Infertile Women

The final section of the daf addresses a *mishnah* whose analysis was begun earlier: can a husband make a woman a *sotah* when she is incapable of bearing children? This is the position of Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha: *machlokes be'akarah u'zekeinah* — the dispute in the *mishnah* between the Sages and Rabbi Eliezer concerns specifically an *akarah*, an infertile woman, or a *zekeinah*, an elderly woman. The Sages held that since the husband has an obligation to have children, and this woman cannot provide that, the marriage is not a fully viable one, and therefore she may not drink. Rabbi Eliezer disagreed, arguing that the husband can always marry an additional wife to fulfill that obligation, so this marriage is not thereby rendered invalid.

### Akarah vs. Aylonit

However, all of this — the entire debate — applies only to an *akarah* or *zekeinah*. The case of an *aylonit* — a woman who from birth has been constitutionally incapable of bearing children — is in a different category entirely, and *kuli alma lo pligi*: everyone agrees that an *aylonit* does not drink. The reason is not the question of whether the husband must divorce her to fulfill his obligation, but a direct exclusion from the biblical text itself. The Torah uses the phrase *venizre'a zara* — "and she shall bear seed" — in describing the *sotah* ordeal. *Mi shedarka lehazri'a*: the *parasha* of *sotah* applies only to a woman who is in the category of one who can potentially bear seed.

An *akarah* or *zekeinah* was once capable of bearing children, or at least was naturally in the category of those who might; their infertility is a medical condition, not an intrinsic feature of their identity. An *aylonit*, by contrast, is defined precisely by her congenital inability to bear children — that is the very essence of the category. She is therefore structurally excluded from *yatza zo she'ein darka lehazri'a* — she is outside the *parasha* of *sotah* entirely, regardless of any questions about the viability of the marriage or the husband's other obligations.
