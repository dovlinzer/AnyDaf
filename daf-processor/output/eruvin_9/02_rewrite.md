# Eruvin 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Under the Korah

### Intro: Under Korah

Our daf begins at the bottom of 8b, where the Gemara has been engaged in a complex discussion about *mavoyos* opening into *chatzeros* and various spatial configurations. The Gemara now turns to a more focused question: may one carry underneath the *korah* — the beam that spans the opening of a *mavoy*?

The Gemara had entertained opinions in both directions on this question, and then observed that the answer does not actually depend on the underlying rationale for the *korah*. Some hold that the *korah* functions as a *heker* — a recognizable sign or marker — while others hold that it functions as a *mechitzah*, a halachic partition. Yet regardless of which theory one adopts, the same question remains: does the wall (or the *heker*) operate from the inner face of the beam, or from the outer face? That question can be resolved either way under either theory. So the debate about *heker* versus *mechitzah* does not, by itself, determine whether carrying beneath the beam is permitted.

## Under the Korah (II)

### Rav Chisda: Inside/Outsi…

Rav Chisda states that whatever one's position on the *korah*, there is one point of universal agreement: *bein lechayayim* — opposite the *lechi*, the vertical post that marks the side of the *mavoy* opening — is forbidden (*she'assur*). The prohibition holds regardless of how one understands the function of the *lechi*.

This prompts a natural question: why should the *lechi* be treated differently from the *korah*? After all, the same inside/outside debate that applies to the *korah* could equally apply to the *lechi*. Tosafos explains that the *lechi* is categorically different because, unlike the *korah* — which must be at least a *tefach* wide — a *lechi* may be of infinitesimal thinness. Since the *lechi* is permitted to be arbitrarily narrow, the rabbis ruled that one may not use the space directly opposite it. Were they to permit that space, a person might easily step over an extremely thin post without even noticing, rendering the *lechi* functionally meaningless. Notably, the Gemara itself does not offer this explanation; Tosafos supplies it as a logical rationale for the distinction.

### Rami bar Chama: External…

Rami bar Chama poses a challenge to Rav Chisda. The case: *na'atz shtay yetaydot bishnay kotlay mavoy mibachutz* — someone drove two pegs into the outer face of the two walls of the *mavoy* and rested the beam on them, so that the beam sits flush against the outer face of the walls rather than resting directly on top of them. What is the law in this scenario?

Rav Chisda's answer is ingeniously constructed. He says: *ledvray hamatir assur uledvray ha'ossur muttar* — according to the opinion that normally permits carrying under the beam, this case is forbidden; according to the opinion that normally forbids it, this case is permitted. The logic: the permissive view holds that the beam functions through its outer face. But when the beam is flush against the outer face of the *mavoy* wall, its outer face is now pointing away from the *mavoy* — not connected to it at all. So the permissive view would forbid this configuration. Conversely, the restrictive view relies on the inner face of the beam, and in this case the inner face is flush against the *mavoy* wall — suggesting it should actually be valid. A possible objection is that even the outer face, being within three *tefachim*, could invoke the principle of *lavud* and be considered as though connected. But Rav Chisda apparently holds that physical adjacency alone is insufficient here — the beam must be directly and positionally on top of the *mavoy*.

### Rava: Direct Placement

Rava goes further and argues that even the opinion that normally forbids carrying beneath the beam — the one that relies on the inner face — would also forbid this configuration. His reasoning: *ba'inan korah gabay mavoy veleka* — we require that the beam be directly above the *mavoy*, and here it is not. The beam rests against the side of the walls, not atop them, and that does not satisfy the halachic definition of a *korah*.

### Tzurat HaPetach

Rava draws an analogy to *tzurat hapetach*, the doorframe configuration used as a halachic enclosure. It is well established that in a *tzurat hapetach*, the upper horizontal element must pass directly above the two vertical posts — it cannot be offset to the side. This is precisely why one cannot simply use existing utility poles as a *tzurat hapetach* when the crossbar does not run directly above them. The same principle governs the *korah*: it must sit directly atop the walls of the *mavoy*, not flush against their outer face. Rava offers no further theoretical explanation; he simply identifies this positional requirement as part of the technical definition that *Chazal* assigned to a valid *korah*, much as they assigned it to a *tzurat hapetach*.

### Extended/Suspended Beam

Rav Ada bar Matna raises a challenge to Rava from a *braita*. The *braita* states: *haita koraso meshucha o tluyah* — if the *korah* was *meshucha* (extended) or *tluyah* (suspended). The initial assumption is that *meshucha* means the beam is pulled away from the *mavoy* — protruding outward from the face of the wall, not sitting directly on top — and *tluyah* means the beam is suspended without reaching the side walls on either end. The *braita* then rules: if the gap is less than three [*tefachim*], one need not bring another beam (*ein tzarich lehavi korah acheres*); if it is three or more, another beam is required.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel dissents and says the threshold is four *tefachim*, not three. This is striking because *lavud* — the principle that objects within three *tefachim* are halachically considered connected — is generally cited as a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, an oral tradition from Sinai. Yet Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appears to dispute the measurement. The Rambam holds that there can be no disputes regarding a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, and this Gemara is among those that sit in tension with that position.

### Lavud Connection

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction with Rava's ruling by reinterpreting the *braita*. *Idi v'idi mibifnim* — in both cases (*meshucha* and *tluyah*), the beam remains within the interior of the *mavoy*. The distinction between the two is not inside versus outside, but rather: *meshucha mi-ruach achat* — the beam is separated from one wall, not extending all the way to one side; *tluyah mi-shtei ruchot* — it is suspended without reaching either wall on either side.

Why must the *braita* teach both cases? Because one might have thought: from one side, we can apply *lavud* and consider the beam as though it reaches the wall. But perhaps we would not apply *lavud* in two directions simultaneously — that is, we would not extend the beam imaginarily to both sides at once. The *braita* teaches *kamashma lan* that *lavud* can indeed be applied in both directions.

### Rav Ashi: Diagonal Dista…

Rav Ashi offers an alternative reading: *meshucha ve-hi tluyah* — the beam is simultaneously extended away from the edges and elevated above the top of the walls. It is both horizontally displaced and vertically elevated. The scenario is: pegs are driven into the walls at an angle (*akumot*), and the beam rests on them — neither touching the side walls (*she'ein begovahan gimel* — the vertical gap is less than three *tefachim*) nor spanning all the way to the edges (*she'ein be-akmimutan gimel* — the diagonal distance is less than three *tefachim*).

The question Rav Ashi addresses is whether one may apply *lavud* in two directions at once — imaginarily lowering the beam to the level of the wall-top and simultaneously extending it to reach the edge. The concern is analogous to a case in *Sukkah*: can *lavud* attach something to an imaginary extension? The principle is that *lavud* can connect two physical objects within three *tefachim*, but one cannot first imaginarily extend one element and then apply *lavud* to that imaginary extension. However, Rav Ashi explains that this concern is resolved because the diagonal distance — combining both the vertical drop and the horizontal gap — is itself within three *tefachim*. Since the physical edge of the beam is within three *tefachim* of the corner of the wall along the diagonal, *lavud* can be applied directly from one physical point to another, without any intermediate imaginary step.

Tosafos notes the asymmetry with the *Sukkah* case, where *Chazal* permit an imaginarily extended wall to function as a valid wall even without *lavud*. Tosafos explains that the two domains have different operative criteria. In the *mavoy*, one requirement is that the *korah* must be touching — or within three *tefachim* of — the top of the wall. The very fact that the Gemara earlier ruled that a beam flush against the outer face of the *mavoy* is disqualified, even though one might apply *lavud* there, demonstrates that the *mavoy* imposes its own technical standards not fully reducible to those of *Sukkah*.

## Opposite the Lechi

### Lechi Forbidden

Having established that Rav Chisda held the space opposite the *lechi* to be universally forbidden, the Gemara now turns to a *braita* taught by Rav Zakkai before Rabbi Yochanan: *bein halachayayim ve-tachas hakorah nidun ke-karmelis* — the space opposite the posts and beneath the beam is treated as a *karmelis*, not as *reshut hayachid*. In other words, carrying in that space falls outside the halachic benefit of the *mavoy*'s *eruv* designation.

### Rav Yochanan Rejects

Rabbi Yochanan's response is blunt: *puk tani levra* — go teach that outside, meaning, this is not a teaching we accept here. Evidently, Rabbi Yochanan's position is that carrying opposite the *lechi* and beneath the beam is in fact permitted.

### Abaye: Qualified Permit

Abaye says: *mistavra milta d'Rabbi Yochanan tachas hakorah* — the reasoning of Rabbi Yochanan makes sense with respect to beneath the beam. There, one can plausibly permit carrying. *Aval bein lechayayim assur* — but opposite the *lechi*, Abaye maintains, is forbidden, consistent with what Rav Chisda established.

### Rava: Permit (Qualified)

Rava disagrees: *bein lechayayim nami mutar* — opposite the *lechi* is also permitted. Rava is thus the lone authority who consistently permits both spaces.

### Makom Ptur Argument

Rava now explains his basis. He cites a tradition brought by Rav Dimi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: a space that is less than four by four *tefachim* is a *makom ptur* — a domain-free zone — and both those in *reshut harabbim* and those in *reshut hayachid* may place objects upon it, provided they do not use it to transfer between the two domains. Rava reasons: even if one considers the space opposite the *lechi* to fall outside the formal *reshut hayachid* of the *mavoy*, since it is a narrow strip less than four *tefachim*, it should have the status of a *makom ptur* — a neutral zone where carrying is permitted from either direction.

### Abaye Refutes Ptur

Abaye refutes this: *hatam b'gavoha gimmel* — a *makom ptur* of that kind applies only when the space is elevated at least three *tefachim* above the floor of the *reshut harabbim*, such as a post or mailbox-like protrusion. A strip of ground-level space cannot be a *makom ptur*. Ground-level space belongs entirely to one of the recognized domains — either *reshut hayachid* or *reshut harabbim* (or *karmelis*). A narrow strip at floor level has no independent neutral status.

### Doorway Needs Lechi

Abaye then marshals a positive proof from a ruling of Rav Chama bar Guria in the name of Rav: *toch hapesach tzarich lechi acher l'hatiro* — if one wants to use the space within a doorframe of a *mavoy*, one needs an additional *lechi* to permit it. This appears to be strong evidence that the space opposite — or rather, inside — the *lechi* framework of the door cannot be used without a separate *lechi*. If the doorframe's own *lechis* were sufficient to permit the adjacent space, no additional *lechi* would be needed.

One might try to distinguish: perhaps the doorframe is four by four *tefachim* wide, giving it the character of an independent domain, and that is why it requires extra treatment. But Rav Chanin bar Rava says in the name of Rav that *af al pi she'ein bo arba'ah al arba'ah* — even if the doorframe interior is less than four by four *tefachim*, a separate *lechi* is still required. This forecloses the distinction.

### Karmelis vs. Public

Rava, undeterred, responds: *hasam d'patuach le-karmelis* — those cases all involve a *mavoy* opening into a *karmelis*, and in that scenario I concede that the space opposite the *lechi* is problematic. But when the *mavoy* opens into a *reshut harabbim*, carrying opposite the *lechi* is still permitted.

The Gemara is startled: what — it is *better* when the *mavoy* opens into a full *reshut harabbim* than when it opens into the less severe *karmelis*? *Yatva b'ara ve-diyura b'shmei shamaya* — is the more grounded domain dwelling in the heaven and the less severe domain dwelling on earth? The categories seem inverted.

### Matza Min Et Mino

The Gemara affirms that yes, counterintuitively, the situation is worse when it opens into a *karmelis*. The principle invoked is *matza min et mino ve-ne'or* — one kind found its like kind and it stirred. The phrase is borrowed from the laws of *kashrus*, where a forbidden substance that had been nullified (*batel*) can be "reawakened" when more of the same forbidden substance is added, combining to produce a perceptible and therefore prohibited taste.

Applied here: the narrow strip of space opposite the *lechi* is in a gray zone — it sits at the cusp of the *mavoy*'s *reshut hayachid*, right at the edge, technically within the walls but not squarely within the *mavoy*'s *hechsher*. If the space outside is a *karmelis*, this ambiguous strip is attracted to the *karmelis* — it finds its like kind and becomes annexed to it, making it prohibited. But if outside is a full *reshut harabbim*, the contrast is stark. That narrow strip, right within the walls and adjacent to a bustling public domain, is not going to be reclassified as part of the *reshut harabbim*. The contrast actually preserves its association with the *mavoy* and keeps it permissible.

### Lechi Conclusion

In summary: the space opposite the *lechi* is treated as a genuine point of controversy. Most of the Talmudic evidence — the various *braisos* and rulings cited throughout this passage — indicates that it is forbidden. Rav Chisda stated at the outset that everyone agrees it is forbidden. Abaye concurs. Rava alone permits it, but must systematically address each piece of contrary evidence by distinguishing cases where the *mavoy* opens into a *karmelis* — in those cases he concedes prohibition — from cases where it opens into a *reshut harabbim*, where he continues to maintain that the space is permitted.

## Consecutive Lechis

### Multiple Lechis

The Gemara now introduces a new dimension to the *lechi* discussion. Rav Huna b'rei deRav Yehoshua cites Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *mavoy sheritzfo belachayayim* — a *mavoy* into which consecutive *lechis* were placed, one after another, all the way along the opening, with gaps between them that are each *pachot pachot me'arba* — individually less than four *tefachim*, but more than three. This scenario, Rabbi Yochanan says, is the subject of the dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbanan.

### RSBG: Lavud 4

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that *lavud* applies up to four *tefachim*. Accordingly, all those consecutive *lechis* with gaps of less than four *tefachim* would be treated as connected to one another — effectively merging into a single thick *lechi*.

### Lavud: 3 vs 4

The Rabbanan hold that *lavud* applies only up to three *tefachim*. Since the gaps are between three and four *tefachim* — beyond the *lavud* threshold of the Rabbanan — each post remains an independent *lechi* without being merged with its neighbors.

### Proof Method

Why does this dispute matter? The Gemara reasons that the only way this dispute is meaningful is if we accept that one cannot use the space opposite the *lechi*. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who applies *lavud* at four *tefachim*, all the posts merge into one long thick *lechi*, and one can only carry up to the inner face of the innermost *lechi* (*mishtamesh ad chuda pnima'i*). According to the Rabbanan, who do not apply *lavud* at that distance, the posts do not merge — only the outermost post counts as the *lechi*, and one may carry up to the inner face of that outer post (*mishtamesh ad chuda pnima'i shel chitzon*). But if carrying opposite a *lechi* were simply permitted, the whole debate would be pointless — one could carry everywhere regardless of whether the posts merge. The debate therefore constitutes proof that *aval bein lechayayim d'chulei alma assur* — opposite the *lechi* is forbidden according to all.

Rava, ever consistent, responds: *hasam nami d'patuach le-karmelis* — that case too involves a *mavoy* opening into a *karmelis*, and in that situation I agree the space is forbidden. The Gemara then reprises the *matza min et mino* argument, arriving at the same conclusion as before.

### Rav Ashi: Independent

Rav Ashi offers a different reading of the consecutive-*lechis* scenario that avoids using it as proof against Rava at all. He reads the case as: *ritzfo belachayayim pachos pachos me'arba bemeshech arba amos* — the consecutive *lechis* extended across a run of four *amos* into the *mavoy*. Why does four *amos* matter? Because four *amos* is the minimum halachic size for an independent *mavoy*. If Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel applies *lavud* and merges all those posts into one solid wall, that wall — spanning four *amos* — effectively defines a new *mavoy* within the original *mavoy*. What was a *lechi* is now a wall, and a new interior space of four *amos* is created that has the character of its own *mavoy*, requiring its own *lechi* to be permitted.

### New Mavoi Issue

Thus, according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — *de'amar amrinan lavud, havei leh mavoi, vetzarich lechi acher lehatiro* — applying *lavud* transforms the consecutive posts into a wall, and that wall creates an independent *mavoy*, which in turn requires its own *lechi*. According to the Rabbanan — *lo amrinan lavud, lo tzarich lechi acher* — no *lavud* is applied, no wall is formed, and no new *mavoy* is created; a single *lechi* at the entrance continues to suffice. In this reading, the dispute is entirely about the consequences of *lavud* for *mavoy* formation, and tells us nothing at all about whether one may carry opposite a *lechi*. Rava's position is therefore unaffected.

## Lechi Visibility

### Valid: Inside Visible

When Ravin came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he transmitted a ruling of Rabbi Yochanan: *nira mibachutz ve-shaveh mibifnim* — if a *lechi* is visible on the outside but flush with the wall on the inside, *eino nidon mishum lechi* — it does not count as a *lechi*. This ruling now becomes the pivot for a new discussion. The Gemara introduces the opposing case as the clear baseline: *nira mibifnim ve-shaveh mibachutz* — if it is visible on the inside and flush on the outside, *nidon mishum lechi* — it certainly qualifies. When a person standing inside the *mavoy* can see a protrusion from the wall, the *lechi* is doing its job as a *heker* for those who are carrying within the *mavoy*.

### Disputed: Outside Visible

The disputed case is the reverse configuration: the protrusion is visible to someone standing outside the *mavoy* — it appears as something jutting from the wall — but for those inside the *mavoy*, the surface is flush and the protrusion is invisible. Is that sufficient?

### Rabbi Chiya: Both Valid

A *braita* of Rabbi Chiya's school resolves this directly: *kotel shetzido echad kanus mechaveiro* — a wall in which one side is set back relative to the other. Whether the configuration is *nira mibachutz ve-shaveh mibifnim* — visible outside and flush inside — or *nira mibifnim ve-shaveh mibachutz* — visible inside and flush outside — in both cases *nidon mishum lechi*. Rabbi Chiya explicitly teaches that outside visibility is sufficient.

### Inference Method

The Gemara asks: does the fact that Rabbi Chiya taught this *braita* prove his own ruling? After all, Rabbi Yochanan also must have known this *braita* and yet rejected it — *shemeia lei ve-lo savar lah* — he heard it and did not accept it. Perhaps Rabbi Chiya similarly taught it without personally subscribing to it.

The Gemara rejects this parallel: *bishlamah Rabbi Yochanan lo savar lah, mishum hachi lo tni lah* — it is understandable that Rabbi Yochanan did not hold by this *braita*, because he did not teach it. But Rabbi Chiya, *ee itah delo savar lah, lamah lei le-mitniyah* — if he did not believe it, why would he go around teaching it? The act of teaching constitutes an endorsement. Rabbi Chiya's position is therefore established: a *lechi* visible only from the outside is valid.

### Rabba bar RH: Sufficient

Rabba bar Rav Huna states explicitly: *nira mibachutz ve-shaveh mibifnim nidon mishum lechi* — a *lechi* visible on the outside and flush on the inside is valid. He aligns with Rabbi Chiya's ruling.

### Chatzer Application

Rava now proposes a support for this position from the *mishna* of *chatzer ktana she-nifretza le-gedola* — a small courtyard that opened up into a large one. As discussed in earlier dapim, when the wall between a small *chatzer* and a large one falls, the large courtyard remains permitted (since from its perspective this is merely one opening among many, and it retains the character of a *reshut hayachid*), while the small courtyard is forbidden (since its entire wall is open and it is treated as subsumed within the large one).

Rava asks: if visibility from the outside is sufficient for a *lechi*, then the small *chatzer* should be permitted! The edge of the small *chatzer*'s wall, where it meets the large one, protrudes relative to the large *chatzer*'s wall — visible from the outside (the large courtyard), even if not from the inside (the small courtyard). That should constitute a *lechi* and resolve the small *chatzer*'s problem.

### Rav Zeira: Configuration

Rav Zeira explains why Rava's support fails: *be-nichnasim kotlei ktana le-gedola* — the case of the *mishna* is where the walls of the small *chatzer* extend inward into the large one. In that configuration, there is no protrusion at the edge — the walls run inward and there is simply a break where the wall ends. Nothing juts outward from the plane of the large courtyard's wall. Hence there is no *lechi* at all, whether viewed from inside or outside.

The Gemara then asks: even so, *velima lavud* — the inner edge of the small courtyard's wall may be within three *tefachim* of the outer wall of the large courtyard. Could we not apply *lavud* and treat that gap as if the wall extends flush, thereby creating a notional *lechi* through *lavud*? The Gemara raises a specific measurement scenario to show that this does not always work — *bemuflag mikotel zeh bishnayim u-mikotel zeh be'arba'ah* (Ravina) — the inner wall may be two *tefachim* from one side and four *tefachim* from the other, so that *lavud* cannot be applied on the four-*tefachim* side.

The Gemara then asks: even so, *velima lavud me-ruach achat ve-tishtrei* — at least apply *lavud* on the side where the gap is only two *tefachim*, creating a *lechi* on that one side, and let the small *chatzer* be permitted on that basis.

### Rabbi: Two Planks

The Gemara answers: *Rabbi hee de'amar be-ina shenei pasim* — this is the position of *Rebbi*, who requires two planks — one on each side — and not merely one. While a *mavoy* requires only a single *lechi* at its opening, a *chatzer* according to *Rebbi* requires a plank on both sides of its opening. A single *lechi* via *lavud* on one side alone would therefore be insufficient. This matter, the Gemara signals, will be pursued further.
