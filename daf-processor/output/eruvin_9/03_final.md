# Eruvin 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Under the Korah

### Intro: Under Korah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ תַּחַת הַקּוֹרָה? רַב וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמְרוּ: מוּתָּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ תַּחַת הַקּוֹרָה. שְׁמוּאֵל וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמְרוּ: אָסוּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ תַּחַת הַקּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised** before the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **utilizing** and carrying in the area **beneath the** cross **beam** spanning the opening of an alleyway, which the beam permits carrying? Opinions differ on the matter. **Rav, Rabbi Ḥiyya, and Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is permitted to utilize** the area **beneath the** cross **beam. Shmuel, Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi, and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: It is prohibited to utilize** the area **beneath the** cross **beam.**

Our daf begins at the bottom of 8b, where the Gemara has been engaged in a complex discussion about *mavoyos* opening into *chatzeros* and various spatial configurations. The Gemara now turns to a more focused question: may one carry underneath the *korah* — the beam that spans the opening of a *mavoy*?

The Gemara had entertained opinions in both directions on this question, and then observed that the answer does not actually depend on the underlying rationale for the *korah*. Some hold that the *korah* functions as a *heker* — a recognizable sign or marker — while others hold that it functions as a *mechitzah*, a halachic partition. Yet regardless of which theory one adopts, the same question remains: does the wall (or the *heker*) operate from the inner face of the beam, or from the outer face? That question can be resolved either way under either theory. So the debate about *heker* versus *mechitzah* does not, by itself, determine whether carrying beneath the beam is permitted.

## Under the Korah (II)

### Rav Chisda: Inside/Outside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּבֵין לְחָיַיִם שֶׁאָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: All concur** that utilizing the area **between the side posts** placed at the entrance to an alleyway to permit carrying **is prohibited,** for a side post functions as a partition, and therefore one may only use the space up to its inner edge, but no further.

Rav Chisda states that whatever one's position on the *korah*, there is one point of universal agreement: *bein lechayayim* — opposite the *lechi*, the vertical post that marks the side of the *mavoy* opening — is forbidden (*she'assur*). The prohibition holds regardless of how one understands the function of the *lechi*.

This prompts a natural question: why should the *lechi* be treated differently from the *korah*? After all, the same inside/outside debate that applies to the *korah* could equally apply to the *lechi*. Tosafos explains that the *lechi* is categorically different because, unlike the *korah* — which must be at least a *tefach* wide — a *lechi* may be of infinitesimal thinness. Since the *lechi* is permitted to be arbitrarily narrow, the rabbis ruled that one may not use the space directly opposite it. Were they to permit that space, a person might easily step over an extremely thin post without even noticing, rendering the *lechi* functionally meaningless. Notably, the Gemara itself does not offer this explanation; Tosafos supplies it as a logical rationale for the distinction.

### Rami bar Chama: External Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: נָעַץ שְׁתֵּי יְתֵידוֹת בִּשְׁנֵי כּוֹתְלֵי מָבוֹי מִבַּחוּץ, וְהִנִּיחַ קוֹרָה עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda: What** is the *halakha* in a case where a person **inserted two pegs in the two alleyway walls,** one in each wall, **on the outside** of the entrance facing the public domain, **and he placed a** cross **beam on top of** the pegs, such that the beam is attached to the front of the alleyway walls instead of on top of them? Does this cross beam permit carrying within the alleyway?

Rami bar Chama poses a challenge to Rav Chisda. The case: *na'atz shtay yetaydot bishnay kotlay mavoy mibachutz* — someone drove two pegs into the outer face of the two walls of the *mavoy* and rested the beam on them, so that the beam sits flush against the outer face of the walls rather than resting directly on top of them. What is the law in this scenario?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְדִבְרֵי הַמַּתִּיר — אָסוּר, לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹסֵר — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: According to the statement of** the one **who permits** utilizing the area beneath the cross beam, carrying within the alleyway **is prohibited,** for he holds that the cross beam's outer edge is the critical one, and here this outer edge is positioned outside the alleyway and therefore cannot permit it. Whereas **according to the statement of** the authority **who prohibits** utilizing the area beneath the cross beam, carrying in the alleyway **is permitted,** for the cross beam's inner edge is attached to the entrance of the alleyway.

Rav Chisda's answer is ingeniously constructed. He says: *ledvray hamatir assur uledvray ha'ossur muttar* — according to the opinion that normally permits carrying under the beam, this case is forbidden; according to the opinion that normally forbids it, this case is permitted. The logic: the permissive view holds that the beam functions through its outer face. But when the beam is flush against the outer face of the *mavoy* wall, its outer face is now pointing away from the *mavoy* — not connected to it at all. So the permissive view would forbid this configuration. Conversely, the restrictive view relies on the inner face of the beam, and in this case the inner face is flush against the *mavoy* wall — suggesting it should actually be valid. A possible objection is that even the outer face, being within three *tefachim*, could invoke the principle of *lavud* and be considered as though connected. But Rav Chisda apparently holds that physical adjacency alone is insufficient here — the beam must be directly and positionally on top of the *mavoy*.

### Rava: Direct Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹסֵר נָמֵי אָסוּר — בָּעִינַן קוֹרָה עַל גַּבֵּי מָבוֹי, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava,** however, disagreed and **said: Even according to the opinion of** the one **who prohibits** utilizing the area beneath the cross beam, carrying in the alleyway **is prohibited, for we require** that **the** cross **beam** that permits the alleyway be placed **on top of** the walls of **the alleyway, and** it **is not.** A cross beam that merely touches the alleyway from the outside does not permit it.

Rava goes further and argues that even the opinion that normally forbids carrying beneath the beam — the one that relies on the inner face — would also forbid this configuration. His reasoning: *ba'inan korah gabay mavoy veleka* — we require that the beam be directly above the *mavoy*, and here it is not. The beam rests against the side of the walls, not atop them, and that does not satisfy the halachic definition of a *korah*.

### Tzurat HaPetach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָה לְרָבָא: הָיְתָה קוֹרָתוֹ מְשׁוּכָה אוֹ תְּלוּיָה, פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה — אֵין צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא קוֹרָה אַחֶרֶת. שְׁלֹשָׁה — צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא קוֹרָה אַחֶרֶת. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבָּעָה — אֵין צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא קוֹרָה אַחֶרֶת, אַרְבָּעָה — צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא קוֹרָה אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Mattana raised an objection to Rava** from a *baraita*: **If the** cross **beam** being used to render an alleyway permitted for carrying **is drawn away** from the alleyway walls **or suspended** in the air, the following distinction applies: If the cross beam is **less than three** handbreadths from the walls, **one is not required to bring a different** cross **beam,** for it is considered attached to the walls based on the principle of *lavud*, which views two solid surfaces as connected if the gap between them is less than three handbreadths wide. However, if the distance is **three** or more handbreadths from the walls, **he is required to bring a different** cross **beam** in order to permit carrying in the alleyway. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** who holds that the principle of *lavud* applies to a gap of up to four handbreadths wide, **says:** If the cross beam is **less than four** handbreadths from the wall, **one is not required to bring a different** cross **beam;** but if the distance is **four** handbreadths from the wall, **he is required to bring a different** cross **beam.**

Rava draws an analogy to *tzurat hapetach*, the doorframe configuration used as a halachic enclosure. It is well established that in a *tzurat hapetach*, the upper horizontal element must pass directly above the two vertical posts — it cannot be offset to the side. This is precisely why one cannot simply use existing utility poles as a *tzurat hapetach* when the crossbar does not run directly above them. The same principle governs the *korah*: it must sit directly atop the walls of the *mavoy*, not flush against their outer face. Rava offers no further theoretical explanation; he simply identifies this positional requirement as part of the technical definition that *Chazal* assigned to a valid *korah*, much as they assigned it to a *tzurat hapetach*.

### Extended/Suspended Beam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו — מְשׁוּכָה מִבַּחוּץ, וּתְלוּיָהּ מִבִּפְנִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wishes to clarify the *baraita*: **What, is it not** that when the *baraita* speaks of a cross beam that is **drawn away** from the alleyway walls, it is referring to a cross beam that is distanced from the alleyway walls and situated **on the outside** in the public domain, similar to the case of the cross beam resting on pegs mentioned above? **And** when it speaks of a cross beam that is **suspended,** isn't it referring to a cross beam that is distanced from the alleyway walls and placed **on the inside** in the alleyway? This interpretation contradicts Rava's statement above that disqualifies such a cross beam.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, אִידִי וְאִידִי מִבִּפְנִים. מְשׁוּכָה — מֵרוּחַ אַחַת, וּתְלוּיָה — מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this interpretation: **No,** both **this,** the cross beam that is drawn away, **and that,** the crossbeam that is suspended, are located **on the inside** of the alleyway. The difference between them is that the cross beam that is **drawn away** is distanced from the wall **from one direction,** while a **suspended** cross beam does not lie on the alleyway walls at all, but is distanced from them **from both directions.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מֵרוּחַ אַחַת אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד, מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת — לָא אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Lest you say** that if the cross beam is distanced from the wall **from one direction, we say** that the principle of ***lavud*** applies, and it is as if the cross beam is joined to the wall; but if it is distanced from the wall **from two directions, we do not say** that the principle of ***lavud*** applies. The *baraita*, therefore, comes and **teaches us** that there is no difference in this regard.

The *braita* states: *haita koraso meshucha o tluyah* — if the *korah* was *meshucha* (extended) or *tluyah* (suspended). The initial assumption is that *meshucha* means the beam is pulled away from the *mavoy* — protruding outward from the face of the wall, not sitting directly on top — and *tluyah* means the beam is suspended without reaching the side walls on either end. The *braita* then rules: if the gap is less than three [*tefachim*], one need not bring another beam (*ein tzarich lehavi korah acheres*); if it is three or more, another beam is required.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel dissents and says the threshold is four *tefachim*, not three. This is striking because *lavud* — the principle that objects within three *tefachim* are halachically considered connected — is generally cited as a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, an oral tradition from Sinai. Yet Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appears to dispute the measurement. The Rambam holds that there can be no disputes regarding a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, and this Gemara is among those that sit in tension with that position.

### Lavud Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מֵרוּחַ אַחַת אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד, מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת — לָא אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Lest you say** that if the cross beam is distanced from the wall **from one direction, we say** that the principle of ***lavud*** applies, and it is as if the cross beam is joined to the wall; but if it is distanced from the wall **from two directions, we do not say** that the principle of ***lavud*** applies. The *baraita*, therefore, comes and **teaches us** that there is no difference in this regard.

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction with Rava's ruling by reinterpreting the *braita*. *Idi v'idi mibifnim* — in both cases (*meshucha* and *tluyah*), the beam remains within the interior of the *mavoy*. The distinction between the two is not inside versus outside, but rather: *meshucha mi-ruach achat* — the beam is separated from one wall, not extending all the way to one side; *tluyah mi-shtei ruchot* — it is suspended without reaching either wall on either side.

Why must the *braita* teach both cases? Because one might have thought: from one side, we can apply *lavud* and consider the beam as though it reaches the wall. But perhaps we would not apply *lavud* in two directions simultaneously — that is, we would not extend the beam imaginarily to both sides at once. The *braita* teaches *kamashma lan* that *lavud* can indeed be applied in both directions.

### Rav Ashi: Diagonal Distance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מְשׁוּכָה וְהִיא תְּלוּיָה, וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּעַץ שְׁתֵּי יְתֵידוֹת עֲקוּמּוֹת עַל שְׁנֵי כּוֹתְלֵי מָבוֹי, שֶׁאֵין בְּגוֹבְהָן שְׁלֹשָׁה, וְאֵין בְּעַקְמוּמִיתָן שְׁלֹשָׁה. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אוֹ ״לָבוּד״ אָמְרִינַן, אוֹ ״חֲבוֹט״ אָמְרִינַן, לָבוּד וַחֲבוֹט לָא אָמְרִינַן. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The *baraita* refers to a cross beam that is **drawn away** from the walls **and** also **suspended** in the air. **And what are the circumstances** where this would be the case? **For example, where he inserted two bent pegs on** the tops of **the two alleyway walls,** and **the height** of the pegs from the top of the walls **is less than three** handbreadths, **and their bend** inward **is less than three handbreadths,** and a cross beam rests on top of them. **Lest you say** that **we either say *lavud*,** i.e., we consider the cross beam to be virtually extended and thus connected to the wall, **or we say *ḥavut*,** pressed down, that we consider the cross beam to be pressed down vertically; but **we do not say** both ***lavud* and *ḥavut*.** The *baraita* therefore **teaches us** that even in that case we say that any item adjacent to another with a gap of less than three handbreadths between them is considered connected to it, whether to the side or below, and even in both directions at once.

Rav Ashi offers an alternative reading: *meshucha ve-hi tluyah* — the beam is simultaneously extended away from the edges and elevated above the top of the walls. It is both horizontally displaced and vertically elevated. The scenario is: pegs are driven into the walls at an angle (*akumot*), and the beam rests on them — neither touching the side walls (*she'ein begovahan gimel* — the vertical gap is less than three *tefachim*) nor spanning all the way to the edges (*she'ein be-akmimutan gimel* — the diagonal distance is less than three *tefachim*).

The question Rav Ashi addresses is whether one may apply *lavud* in two directions at once — imaginarily lowering the beam to the level of the wall-top and simultaneously extending it to reach the edge. The concern is analogous to a case in *Sukkah*: can *lavud* attach something to an imaginary extension? The principle is that *lavud* can connect two physical objects within three *tefachim*, but one cannot first imaginarily extend one element and then apply *lavud* to that imaginary extension. However, Rav Ashi explains that this concern is resolved because the diagonal distance — combining both the vertical drop and the horizontal gap — is itself within three *tefachim*. Since the physical edge of the beam is within three *tefachim* of the corner of the wall along the diagonal, *lavud* can be applied directly from one physical point to another, without any intermediate imaginary step.

Tosafos notes the asymmetry with the *Sukkah* case, where *Chazal* permit an imaginarily extended wall to function as a valid wall even without *lavud*. Tosafos explains that the two domains have different operative criteria. In the *mavoy*, one requirement is that the *korah* must be touching — or within three *tefachim* of — the top of the wall. The very fact that the Gemara earlier ruled that a beam flush against the outer face of the *mavoy* is disqualified, even though one might apply *lavud* there, demonstrates that the *mavoy* imposes its own technical standards not fully reducible to those of *Sukkah*.

## Opposite the Lechi

### Lechi Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי זַכַּאי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בֵּין לְחָיַיִם וְתַחַת הַקּוֹרָה נִידּוֹן כְּכַרְמְלִית. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פּוֹק תְּנִי לְבַרָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zakkai taught** the following *baraita* **before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** The area **between the side posts and beneath the** cross **beam** has the **legal status** of a ***karmelit,** and it is forbidden to carry in it. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: Exit** and **teach** this *halakha* **outside,** i.e., this *baraita* is not in accordance with the accepted *halakha*, and therefore it should not be made part of the regular learning in the study hall.

Having established that Rav Chisda held the space opposite the *lechi* to be universally forbidden, the Gemara now turns to a *braita* taught by Rav Zakkai before Rabbi Yochanan: *bein halachayayim ve-tachas hakorah nidun ke-karmelis* — the space opposite the posts and beneath the beam is treated as a *karmelis*, not as *reshut hayachid*. In other words, carrying in that space falls outside the halachic benefit of the *mavoy*'s *eruv* designation.

### Rav Yochanan Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: פּוֹק תְּנִי לְבַרָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: Exit** and **teach** this *halakha* **outside,** i.e., this *baraita* is not in accordance with the accepted *halakha*, and therefore it should not be made part of the regular learning in the study hall.

Rabbi Yochanan's response is blunt: *puk tani levra* — go teach that outside, meaning, this is not a teaching we accept here. Evidently, Rabbi Yochanan's position is that carrying opposite the *lechi* and beneath the beam is in fact permitted.

### Abaye: Qualified Permit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִילְּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן תַּחַת הַקּוֹרָה, אֲבָל בֵּין לְחָיַיִן אָסוּר. וְרָבָא אָמַר: בֵּין לְחָיַיִם נָמֵי מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara records a dispute with regard to the scope of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement: **Abaye said: Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement is reasonable** with regard to the area **beneath the** cross **beam,** as only the area beneath the cross beam should be considered a private domain, **but between the side posts,** carrying **is** indeed **prohibited,** in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Zakkai. **And Rava said:** The entire statement of Rabbi Zakkai is to be rejected, as Rabbi Yoḥanan asserted, and **even** in the area **between the side posts** carrying **is permitted.**

Abaye says: *mistavra milta d'Rabbi Yochanan tachas hakorah* — the reasoning of Rabbi Yochanan makes sense with respect to beneath the beam. There, one can plausibly permit carrying. *Aval bein lechayayim assur* — but opposite the *lechi*, Abaye maintains, is forbidden, consistent with what Rav Chisda established.

### Rava: Permit (Qualified)

Rava disagrees: *bein lechayayim nami mutar* — opposite the *lechi* is also permitted. Rava is thus the lone authority who consistently permits both spaces.

### Makom Ptur Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ — דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה, מוּתָּר לִבְנֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְלִבְנֵי רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לְכַתֵּף עָלָיו, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַחֲלִיפוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where do I** know to **say this,** that carrying is permitted even between the side posts? **For when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A place that has** an area of **less than four by four** handbreadths and is located between a public and private domain but belongs to neither has the status of an exempt domain with regard to carrying on Shabbat. Therefore, **it is permitted for** both **the people in the public domain** as well as **the people in the private domain to** use it for **loading** their burdens **onto their shoulders, so long as they do not exchange** objects with one another. Therefore, a place having an area of less than four handbreadths is not considered a *karmelit*, but rather an exempt domain, where carrying is permitted. Consequently, the area between the side posts should likewise be considered an exempt domain, and carrying should be permitted within it.

Rava now explains his basis. He cites a tradition brought by Rav Dimi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: a space that is less than four by four *tefachim* is a *makom ptur* — a domain-free zone — and both those in *reshut harabbim* and those in *reshut hayachid* may place objects upon it, provided they do not use it to transfer between the two domains. Rava reasons: even if one considers the space opposite the *lechi* to fall outside the formal *reshut hayachid* of the *mavoy*, since it is a narrow strip less than four *tefachim*, it should have the status of a *makom ptur* — a neutral zone where carrying is permitted from either direction.

### Abaye Refutes Ptur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי, הָתָם בְּגָבוֹהַּ שְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye** said that this offers no proof, as **there,** with regard to Rav Dimi's statement, the area being discussed **is** at least **three** handbreadths **high,** setting it apart from the other domains. It is therefore considered a domain in its own right, and has the *halakha* of an exempt domain.

Abaye refutes this: *hatam b'gavoha gimmel* — a *makom ptur* of that kind applies only when the space is elevated at least three *tefachim* above the floor of the *reshut harabbim*, such as a post or mailbox-like protrusion. A strip of ground-level space cannot be a *makom ptur*. Ground-level space belongs entirely to one of the recognized domains — either *reshut hayachid* or *reshut harabbim* (or *karmelis*). A narrow strip at floor level has no independent neutral status.

### Doorway Needs Lechi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ — דְּאָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: תּוֹךְ הַפֶּתַח, צָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara considers the position of Abaye: **Abaye said: From where do I** know to **say this,** that the area between the side posts has the *halakha* of a *karmelit*? **For Rav Ḥama bar Guria said that Rav said:** The area **within the opening,** i.e., the doorway between two entrance posts that serve as side posts to permit carrying in the alleyway, **requires another side post** in order **to permit** carrying there, for the entrance posts alone do not suffice. This demonstrates that it is forbidden to carry in the space between the side posts without another side post.

Abaye then marshals a positive proof from a ruling of Rav Chama bar Guria in the name of Rav: *toch hapesach tzarich lechi acher l'hatiro* — if one wants to use the space within a doorframe of a *mavoy*, one needs an additional *lechi* to permit it. This appears to be strong evidence that the space opposite — or rather, inside — the *lechi* framework of the door cannot be used without a separate *lechi*. If the doorframe's own *lechis* were sufficient to permit the adjacent space, no additional *lechi* would be needed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: דְּאִית בֵּיהּ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה, וְהָאָמַר רַב חָנִין בַּר רָבָא אָמַר רַב: תּוֹךְ הַפֶּתַח, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה צָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say that** this is a case where the doorway **has** an area **four by four** handbreadths, and therefore an additional side post is required to permit carrying there, this is not a valid argument. **For didn't Rav Ḥanin bar Rava say that Rav said:** The area **within the opening** itself, **even if it does not have** an area of **four by four** handbreadths, **requires an additional side post** in order **to permit** carrying within it. This indicates that the area between the side posts is not to be used.

One might try to distinguish: perhaps the doorframe is four by four *tefachim* wide, giving it the character of an independent domain, and that is why it requires extra treatment. But Rav Chanin bar Rava says in the name of Rav that *af al pi she'ein bo arba'ah al arba'ah* — even if the doorframe interior is less than four by four *tefachim*, a separate *lechi* is still required. This forecloses the distinction.

### Karmelis vs. Public

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא — הָתָם דְּפָתוּחַ לְכַרְמְלִית.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava** replies that a distinction must be made between the cases: **There,** the case of Rav's ruling refers to a scenario where the alleyway's entrance **opens to a *karmelit*,** and thus the space between the entrance posts is also viewed as a *karmelit*, and an additional side post is required.

Rava, undeterred, responds: *hasam d'patuach le-karmelis* — those cases all involve a *mavoy* opening into a *karmelis*, and in that scenario I concede that the space opposite the *lechi* is problematic. But when the *mavoy* opens into a *reshut harabbim*, carrying opposite the *lechi* is still permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מַאי — שְׁרֵי? יַצִּיבָא בְּאַרְעָא וְגִיּוֹרָא בִּשְׁמֵי שְׁמַיָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **But** if the entrance opens **to a public domain, what** would be the *halakha*? Would it be **permitted** to carry there even without an additional side post? If so, it follows that the *halakha* of a *karmelit* is more stringent than that of a public domain. However, this seems untenable, for carrying in a *karmelit* is prohibited only by rabbinic decree, owing to the similarity between a *karmelit* and the public domain. This is similar to a situation where **a permanent resident is** down **on the ground, while a stranger is** raised up **to the highest heaven,** the very opposite of the appropriate state of affairs.

The Gemara is startled: what — it is *better* when the *mavoy* opens into a full *reshut harabbim* than when it opens into the less severe *karmelis*? *Yatva b'ara ve-diyura b'shmei shamaya* — is the more grounded domain dwelling in the heaven and the less severe domain dwelling on earth? The categories seem inverted.

### Matza Min Et Mino

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, מָצָא מִין אֶת מִינוֹ וְנֵיעוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Yes,** it is possible that this is the ruling, for we can say that **it has found its own type and been awakened.** In other words, as the area within the entranceway is not a defined domain, it doesn't have the status of an independent domain. Therefore, when it opens into a *karmelit*, to which it is similar, its status is negated, and it joins with the *karmelit* to form a single unit. However, when it opens into a public domain, it cannot join with it because it is not similar to a public domain, which has a totally different set of laws; and therefore it is considered part of the alleyway, and it is permitted to carry within it.

The Gemara affirms that yes, counterintuitively, the situation is worse when it opens into a *karmelis*. The principle invoked is *matza min et mino ve-ne'or* — one kind found its like kind and it stirred. The phrase is borrowed from the laws of *kashrus*, where a forbidden substance that had been nullified (*batel*) can be "reawakened" when more of the same forbidden substance is added, combining to produce a perceptible and therefore prohibited taste.

Applied here: the narrow strip of space opposite the *lechi* is in a gray zone — it sits at the cusp of the *mavoy*'s *reshut hayachid*, right at the edge, technically within the walls but not squarely within the *mavoy*'s *hechsher*. If the space outside is a *karmelis*, this ambiguous strip is attracted to the *karmelis* — it finds its like kind and becomes annexed to it, making it prohibited. But if outside is a full *reshut harabbim*, the contrast is stark. That narrow strip, right within the walls and adjacent to a bustling public domain, is not going to be reclassified as part of the *reshut harabbim*. The contrast actually preserves its association with the *mavoy* and keeps it permissible.

### Lechi Conclusion

In summary: the space opposite the *lechi* is treated as a genuine point of controversy. Most of the Talmudic evidence — the various *braisos* and rulings cited throughout this passage — indicates that it is forbidden. Rav Chisda stated at the outset that everyone agrees it is forbidden. Abaye concurs. Rava alone permits it, but must systematically address each piece of contrary evidence by distinguishing cases where the *mavoy* opens into a *karmelis* — in those cases he concedes prohibition — from cases where it opens into a *reshut harabbim*, where he continues to maintain that the space is permitted.

## Consecutive Lechis

### Multiple Lechis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרָבָא: וְאַתְּ לָא תִּסְבְּרָא דְּבֵין לְחָיַיִן אָסוּר? וְהָאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מָבוֹי שֶׁרְצָפוֹ בִּלְחָיַיִן פָּחוֹת פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבָּעָה — בָּאנוּ לְמַחְלוֹקֶת רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, the son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rava: And you do not hold** that in the area **between the side posts** carrying **is prohibited? But didn't Rabba bar bar Ḥana say that Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If **an alleyway was lined with side posts, each** one set more than three but **less than four** handbreadths apart from its neighbor, **we have arrived** in this matter **at the dispute** between **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis** with regard to the measure of *lavud*.

The Gemara now introduces a new dimension to the *lechi* discussion. Rav Huna b'rei deRav Yehoshua cites Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *mavoy sheritzfo belachayayim* — a *mavoy* into which consecutive *lechis* were placed, one after another, all the way along the opening, with gaps between them that are each *pachot pachot me'arba* — individually less than four *tefachim*, but more than three. This scenario, Rabbi Yochanan says, is the subject of the dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbanan.

### RSBG: Lavud 4

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דְּאָמַר: אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד, מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ עַד חוּדּוֹ הַפְּנִימִי שֶׁל לֶחִי הַפְּנִימִי. לְרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי: לָא אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד, מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ עַד חוּדּוֹ הַפְּנִימִי שֶׁל חִיצוֹן. אֲבָל בֵּין לְחָיַיִן דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** How so? According **to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** who **said** that if the gap between two items is less than four handbreadths, **we say** that the principle of ***lavud*** applies; all the side posts are considered a single side post. **He may** therefore only **utilize** the alleyway **up to the inner edge of the innermost side post,** but no more. However, according to **the Rabbis,** who **say** that **we do not say** the principle of ***lavud*** applies unless the gap is less than three handbreadths, he **may utilize** the alleyway **up to the inner edge of the outermost** side post. This discussion demonstrates that the argument revolves around the question as to which side post establishes the permitted area. **But** with regard to the area **between the side posts, all agree** that carrying **is prohibited.**

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that *lavud* applies up to four *tefachim*. Accordingly, all those consecutive *lechis* with gaps of less than four *tefachim* would be treated as connected to one another — effectively merging into a single thick *lechi*.

### Lavud: 3 vs 4

The Rabbanan hold that *lavud* applies only up to three *tefachim*. Since the gaps are between three and four *tefachim* — beyond the *lavud* threshold of the Rabbanan — each post remains an independent *lechi* without being merged with its neighbors.

### Proof Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא, הָתָם נָמֵי דְּפָתוּחַ לְכַרְמְלִית.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava** answers that **there too,** it refers to a case where the alleyway's entrance **opens to a *karmelit*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מַאי — שְׁרֵי? יַצִּיבָא בְּאַרְעָא וְגִיּוֹרָא בִּשְׁמֵי שְׁמַיָּא! אִין, מָצָא מִין אֶת מִינוֹ וְנֵיעוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** if the entrance opens **to a public domain, what** is its legal status – would carrying be **permitted?** If so, the *halakha* of a *karmelit* is more severe than that of a public domain. Once again, this can be likened to a situation where **a permanent resident is** down **on the ground, while a stranger** is raised up **to the highest heaven.** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** indeed, this is the ruling, but one should not be perplexed, as we have explained: **it has found its own type and been awakened.**

Why does this dispute matter? The Gemara reasons that the only way this dispute is meaningful is if we accept that one cannot use the space opposite the *lechi*. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who applies *lavud* at four *tefachim*, all the posts merge into one long thick *lechi*, and one can only carry up to the inner face of the innermost *lechi* (*mishtamesh ad chuda pnima'i*). According to the Rabbanan, who do not apply *lavud* at that distance, the posts do not merge — only the outermost post counts as the *lechi*, and one may carry up to the inner face of that outer post (*mishtamesh ad chuda pnima'i shel chitzon*). But if carrying opposite a *lechi* were simply permitted, the whole debate would be pointless — one could carry everywhere regardless of whether the posts merge. The debate therefore constitutes proof that *aval bein lechayayim d'chulei alma assur* — opposite the *lechi* is forbidden according to all.

Rava, ever consistent, responds: *hasam nami d'patuach le-karmelis* — that case too involves a *mavoy* opening into a *karmelis*, and in that situation I agree the space is forbidden. The Gemara then reprises the *matza min et mino* argument, arriving at the same conclusion as before.

### Rav Ashi: Independent Mavoi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁרְצָפוֹ בִּלְחָיַיִם פָּחוֹת פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבָּעָה בְּמֶשֶׁךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides an alternative explanation of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement. **Rav Ashi said:** According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, carrying in the area between the side posts is actually permitted. The dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis concerning the principle of *lavud* is in a case **where** there was an alleyway **that one lined with side posts, each** positioned **less than four** handbreadths from the next, and the side posts extend **for a length of four cubits.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דְּאָמַר: אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד — הָוֵה לֵיהּ מָבוֹי, וְצָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ. וּלְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי: לָא אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד — לֹא צָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who said** that for a gap of up to four handbreadths **we say** that the principle of ***lavud*** applies, all the side posts are considered a single side post, and since the side post in that case is four cubits long, **it is** considered a separate **alleyway;** therefore, it **requires an additional side post to permit** carrying in **it. And according to the Rabbis, who say** that **we do not say** that the principle of ***lavud*** applies unless the gap is less than three handbreadths, this area **does not require an additional side post to permit** carrying within it.

Rav Ashi offers a different reading of the consecutive-*lechis* scenario that avoids using it as proof against Rava at all. He reads the case as: *ritzfo belachayayim pachos pachos me'arba bemeshech arba amos* — the consecutive *lechis* extended across a run of four *amos* into the *mavoy*. Why does four *amos* matter? Because four *amos* is the minimum halachic size for an independent *mavoy*. If Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel applies *lavud* and merges all those posts into one solid wall, that wall — spanning four *amos* — effectively defines a new *mavoy* within the original *mavoy*. What was a *lechi* is now a wall, and a new interior space of four *amos* is created that has the character of its own *mavoy*, requiring its own *lechi* to be permitted.

### New Mavoi Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דְּאָמַר: אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד — הָוֵה לֵיהּ מָבוֹי, וְצָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ. וּלְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי: לָא אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד — לֹא צָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who said** that for a gap of up to four handbreadths **we say** that the principle of ***lavud*** applies, all the side posts are considered a single side post, and since the side post in that case is four cubits long, **it is** considered a separate **alleyway;** therefore, it **requires an additional side post to permit** carrying in **it. And according to the Rabbis, who say** that **we do not say** that the principle of ***lavud*** applies unless the gap is less than three handbreadths, this area **does not require an additional side post to permit** carrying within it.

Thus, according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — *de'amar amrinan lavud, havei leh mavoi, vetzarich lechi acher lehatiro* — applying *lavud* transforms the consecutive posts into a wall, and that wall creates an independent *mavoy*, which in turn requires its own *lechi*. According to the Rabbanan — *lo amrinan lavud, lo tzarich lechi acher* — no *lavud* is applied, no wall is formed, and no new *mavoy* is created; a single *lechi* at the entrance continues to suffice. In this reading, the dispute is entirely about the consequences of *lavud* for *mavoy* formation, and tells us nothing at all about whether one may carry opposite a *lechi*. Rava's position is therefore unaffected.

## Lechi Visibility

### Valid: Inside Visible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִיתְּמַר: נִרְאֶה מִבִּפְנִים וְשָׁוֶה מִבַּחוּץ — נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי. נִרְאֶה מִבַּחוּץ וְשָׁוֶה מִבִּפְנִים, רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי, חַד אָמַר: נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵינוֹ נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי.
>
> **Translation:** An amoraic dispute **was stated:** If a side post is **visible from the inside,** protruding from the wall of the alleyway, **but** it appears to be **even** with the wall **from the outside,** it **is considered a side post.** However, if a side post is **visible from the outside** protruding from the wall, **but** it appears to be **even** with the wall **from the inside,** there is a disagreement between **Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, with regard to its status. **One said: It is considered** to have the legal status **of a side post. And** the other **one said: It is not considered** to have the legal status **of a side post.**

When Ravin came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he transmitted a ruling of Rabbi Yochanan: *nira mibachutz ve-shaveh mibifnim* — if a *lechi* is visible on the outside but flush with the wall on the inside, *eino nidon mishum lechi* — it does not count as a *lechi*. This ruling now becomes the pivot for a new discussion. The Gemara introduces the opposing case as the clear baseline: *nira mibifnim ve-shaveh mibachutz* — if it is visible on the inside and flush on the outside, *nidon mishum lechi* — it certainly qualifies. When a person standing inside the *mavoy* can see a protrusion from the wall, the *lechi* is doing its job as a *heker* for those who are carrying within the *mavoy*.

### Disputed: Outside Visible

The disputed case is the reverse configuration: the protrusion is visible to someone standing outside the *mavoy* — it appears as something jutting from the wall — but for those inside the *mavoy*, the surface is flush and the protrusion is invisible. Is that sufficient?

### Rabbi Chiya: Both Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּסְתַּיֵּים דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא הוּא דְּאָמַר ״נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי״, דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כּוֹתֶל שֶׁצִּידּוֹ אֶחָד כָּנוּס מֵחֲבֵירוֹ, בֵּין שֶׁנִּרְאֶה מִבַּחוּץ וְשָׁוֶה מִבִּפְנִים, וּבֵין שֶׁנִּרְאֶה מִבִּפְנִים וְשָׁוֶה מִבַּחוּץ נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי, תִּסְתַּיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Conclude that Rabbi Ḥiyya** is the one **who said** that **it is considered** to have the legal status **of a side post, as Rabbi Ḥiyya taught:** In the case of **a wall** at the entrance to an alleyway **whose one side is more recessed than the other, whether** the recess **is visible from outside** the alleyway **but** appears to be **even from the inside, or** the recess **is visible from the inside but** appears to be **even from the outside,** it **is considered** to have the legal status **of a side post.** The Gemara states: Indeed, **conclude** that Rabbi Ḥiyya is the one who said it has the legal status of a side post.

A *braita* of Rabbi Chiya's school resolves this directly: *kotel shetzido echad kanus mechaveiro* — a wall in which one side is set back relative to the other. Whether the configuration is *nira mibachutz ve-shaveh mibifnim* — visible outside and flush inside — or *nira mibifnim ve-shaveh mibachutz* — visible inside and flush outside — in both cases *nidon mishum lechi*. Rabbi Chiya explicitly teaches that outside visibility is sufficient.

### Inference Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִי לָא שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ הָא? אֶלָּא שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ וְלָא סָבַר לַהּ, רַבִּי חִיָּיא נָמֵי לָא סָבַר לַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who explicitly said that a side post of that kind is not considered a side post, **did he not hear this** *halakha*? The *Tosefta* was widely known. **Rather, he heard it, but he does not hold** in accordance with **it.** Perhaps, then, **Rabbi Ḥiyya also does not hold** in accordance with **it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי?! בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לָא סָבַר לַהּ — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי לָא תָּנֵי לַהּ. אֶלָּא רַבִּי חִיָּיא, אִי אִיתָא דְּלָא סָבַר לַהּ, לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמִיתְנָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **What is this** comparison? **Granted, Rabbi Yoḥanan does not hold** in accordance with **that** *halakha*. **That is why he did not teach it. But Rabbi Ḥiyya, if it is** true **that he does not hold** in accordance with **it, why would he teach it?**

The Gemara asks: does the fact that Rabbi Chiya taught this *braita* prove his own ruling? After all, Rabbi Yochanan also must have known this *braita* and yet rejected it — *shemeia lei ve-lo savar lah* — he heard it and did not accept it. Perhaps Rabbi Chiya similarly taught it without personally subscribing to it.

The Gemara rejects this parallel: *bishlamah Rabbi Yochanan lo savar lah, mishum hachi lo tni lah* — it is understandable that Rabbi Yochanan did not hold by this *braita*, because he did not teach it. But Rabbi Chiya, *ee itah delo savar lah, lamah lei le-mitniyah* — if he did not believe it, why would he go around teaching it? The act of teaching constitutes an endorsement. Rabbi Chiya's position is therefore established: a *lechi* visible only from the outside is valid.

### Rabba bar RH: Sufficient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: נִרְאֶה מִבַּחוּץ וְשָׁוֶה מִבִּפְנִים — נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna said:** If a side post is **visible from the outside,** protruding from the wall of the alleyway, **but** appears to be **even** with the wall **from the inside, it is considered** to have the legal status **of a side post.**

Rabba bar Rav Huna states explicitly: *nira mibachutz ve-shaveh mibifnim nidon mishum lechi* — a *lechi* visible on the outside and flush on the inside is valid. He aligns with Rabbi Chiya's ruling.

### Chatzer Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה, וּמוֹתְבִינַן אַשְּׁמַעְתִּין: חָצֵר קְטַנָּה שֶׁנִּפְרְצָה לִגְדוֹלָה — גְּדוֹלָה מוּתֶּרֶת וּקְטַנָּה אֲסוּרָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא כְּפִתְחָהּ שֶׁל גְּדוֹלָה. וְאִם אִיתָא, קְטַנָּה נָמֵי תִּשְׁתְּרֵי בְּנִרְאֶה מִבַּחוּץ וְשָׁוֶה מִבִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: And we raise an objection to our** own ***halakha*** from a mishna: With regard to **a small courtyard that was breached** along the entire length of one of its walls so that it opens **into a large** courtyard, in **the large one** it **is permitted** to carry **and** in **the small one** it **is prohibited** to carry. This is **because** the breach **is considered an entrance of the large** courtyard. The wall of the smaller courtyard was breached along its entire length, therefore there is no visible partition from inside the smaller courtyard. However, the partition is noticeable from the outside, i.e., in the large courtyard, since the breach is flanked on both sides by the remaining segments of the wall of the large courtyard. **And if it is so,** that a partition that is visible from the outside is considered a partition, carrying in **the small** courtyard **should also be permitted** in this case, **as** the wall **is visible from the outside but** appears to be **even from the inside.**

Rava now proposes a support for this position from the *mishna* of *chatzer ktana she-nifretza le-gedola* — a small courtyard that opened up into a large one. As discussed in earlier dapim, when the wall between a small *chatzer* and a large one falls, the large courtyard remains permitted (since from its perspective this is merely one opening among many, and it retains the character of a *reshut hayachid*), while the small courtyard is forbidden (since its entire wall is open and it is treated as subsumed within the large one).

Rava asks: if visibility from the outside is sufficient for a *lechi*, then the small *chatzer* should be permitted! The edge of the small *chatzer*'s wall, where it meets the large one, protrudes relative to the large *chatzer*'s wall — visible from the outside (the large courtyard), even if not from the inside (the small courtyard). That should constitute a *lechi* and resolve the small *chatzer*'s problem.

### Rav Zeira: Configuration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: בְּנִכְנָסִין כּוֹתְלֵי קְטַנָּה לִגְדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said:** This mishna is referring to a case **where the walls of the small** courtyard **protrude into the large one,** i.e., the breached wall of the small courtyard is not in line with the wall of the large one. Therefore, even when viewed from the outside there are no walls visible, and that is why carrying is prohibited there.

Rav Zeira explains why Rava's support fails: *be-nichnasim kotlei ktana le-gedola* — the case of the *mishna* is where the walls of the small *chatzer* extend inward into the large one. In that configuration, there is no protrusion at the edge — the walls run inward and there is simply a break where the wall ends. Nothing juts outward from the plane of the large courtyard's wall. Hence there is no *lechi* at all, whether viewed from inside or outside.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לָבוּד וְתִשְׁתְּרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let us say** that the principle of ***lavud*** applies, **and** then carrying **will be permitted** even in the small courtyard. The ends of the breached wall should be considered attached to the side walls of the large courtyard, rendering the wall of the large courtyard visible. Then it will be permitted to carry in the small courtyard based on the principle governing side posts visible from the outside.

The Gemara then asks: even so, *velima lavud* — the inner edge of the small courtyard's wall may be within three *tefachim* of the outer wall of the large courtyard. Could we not apply *lavud* and treat that gap as if the wall extends flush, thereby creating a notional *lechi* through *lavud*? The Gemara raises a specific measurement scenario to show that this does not always work — *bemuflag mikotel zeh bishnayim u-mikotel zeh be'arba'ah* (Ravina) — the inner wall may be two *tefachim* from one side and four *tefachim* from the other, so that *lavud* cannot be applied on the four-*tefachim* side.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לָבוּד מֵרוּחַ אַחַת, וְתִשְׁתְּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let us say** that the principle of ***lavud*** applies **from one direction,** then carrying **will be permitted** even in the small courtyard.

The Gemara then asks: even so, *velima lavud me-ruach achat ve-tishtrei* — at least apply *lavud* on the side where the gap is only two *tefachim*, creating a *lechi* on that one side, and let the small *chatzer* be permitted on that basis.

### Rabbi: Two Planks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: בָּעִינַן שְׁנֵי פַּסִּין. דְּתַנְיָא: חָצֵר, נִיתֶּרֶת בְּפַס אֶחָד, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁנֵי פַּסִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said** that in order to permit carrying in a courtyard that was breached, **we require two** upright **boards,** one on either side of the breach. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: If **a courtyard** was breached and opens into the public domain, and the width of the breach does not exceed ten cubits, it **becomes permitted** to carry there, even **with** only **one** upright **board** remaining on one side of the breach. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** It is permitted only **with two** upright **boards** remaining, one on each side of the breach.

The Gemara answers: *Rabbi hee de'amar be-ina shenei pasim* — this is the position of *Rebbi*, who requires two planks — one on each side — and not merely one. While a *mavoy* requires only a single *lechi* at its opening, a *chatzer* according to *Rebbi* requires a plank on both sides of its opening. A single *lechi* via *lavud* on one side alone would therefore be insufficient. This matter, the Gemara signals, will be pursued further.
