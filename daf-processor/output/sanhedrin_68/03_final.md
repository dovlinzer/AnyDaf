# Sanhedrin 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening & Arba Mitot

### Daf Overview & Kishuf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § In the mishna, **Rabbi Akiva says** in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua that two people can each gather cucumbers by sorcery, one of whom is exempt, as he merely deceives the eyes, and one of whom is liable, as he performs real sorcery.

Today's *daf* is Sanhedrin 68, and it serves a dual purpose: completing *perek* Arba Mitot and beginning the *perek* of *ben sorer u-moreh*. We pick up with the final lines of the *mishnah*, where Rabbi Akiva addresses the *issur* of *kishuf* — witchcraft. Rabbi Akiva's ruling distinguishes between two scenarios: if one actually performs an act of sorcery, one is *chayav*; if the act was mere illusion, one is *patur*. The example given is making it appear as though one has gathered an entire field of vegetables — if actually done, there is a *chiyuv sekilah*; if only an illusion, one is exempt.

It is worth noting that the *gemara* earlier, in dealing with the *issur* of *kishuf* a few *dafim* back, cited one interpretation according to which even creating the illusion of performing sorcery — without actually doing anything — could constitute an *issur* of *lo te'onenu*. Different understandings exist regarding exactly what *lo te'onenu* prohibits, and interesting questions arise about how it applies in contemporary contexts where audiences are generally aware that no real magic is being performed. In any event, this would be at most an *issur d'oraita* without rising to the level of a *chiyuv misah*.

## Rabbi Akiva's Source

### Source Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מֵרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ גְּמִיר לַהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: כְּשֶׁחָלָה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, נִכְנְסוּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וַחֲבֵירָיו לְבַקְּרוֹ. הוּא יוֹשֵׁב בְּקִינוֹף שֶׁלּוֹ, וְהֵן יוֹשְׁבִין בִּטְרַקְלִין שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Akiva learn** these *halakhot* **from Rabbi Yehoshua? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **When Rabbi Eliezer took ill, Rabbi Akiva and his colleagues came to visit him. He was sitting on his canopied bed [*bekinof* ], and they were sitting in his parlor [*biteraklin*];** they did not know if he would be able to receive them, due to his illness.

The *gemara* opens 68a with a challenge to the *mishnah*. Since Rabbi Akiva cites Rabbi Yehoshua as his source, the *gemara* asks: *amar Rabbi Akiva me-Rabbi Yehoshua gemar lei?* — did Rabbi Akiva really learn this from Rabbi Yehoshua? A *brayta* is cited suggesting he actually learned it from Rabbi Eliezer, his primary *rebbe*. The *gemara* thus poses a tension: the *mishnah* attributes the teaching to Rabbi Yehoshua, while the *brayta* points to Rabbi Eliezer.

## Opening & Arba Mitot (II)

### Illusion vs. Actual Deed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת הָיָה, וְנִכְנַס הוּרְקָנוֹס בְּנוֹ לַחְלוֹץ תְּפִלָּיו. גָּעַר בּוֹ וְיָצָא בִּנְזִיפָה. אָמַר לָהֶן לַחֲבֵירָיו: כִּמְדוּמֶּה אֲנִי שֶׁדַּעְתּוֹ שֶׁל אַבָּא נִטְרְפָה. אָמַר לָהֶן: דַּעְתּוֹ וְדַעַת אִמּוֹ נִטְרְפָה! הֵיאַךְ מַנִּיחִין אִיסּוּר סְקִילָה וְעוֹסְקִין בְּאִיסּוּר שְׁבוּת?
>
> **Translation:** **And that day was Shabbat eve, and** Rabbi Eliezer's **son Hyrcanus entered to remove his phylacteries,** as phylacteries are not worn on Shabbat. His father **berated him, and he left reprimanded.** Hyrcanus **said to his** father's **colleagues: It appears to me that father went insane,** since he berated me for no reason. Rabbi Eliezer heard this and **said to them: He,** Hyrcanus, **and his mother went insane. How can they neglect** Shabbat preparations with regard to **prohibitions** punishable by **stoning,** such as lighting the candles and preparing hot food, **and engage in** preparations concerning **prohibitions by rabbinic decree,** such as wearing phylacteries on Shabbat?

The clarification is that Rabbi Akiva in fact learned from both. He received the teaching from Rabbi Eliezer, and he also received it — with fuller comprehension — from Rabbi Yehoshua. This resolution will be elaborated upon after the *gemara* recounts the famous narrative surrounding Rabbi Eliezer's death.

## Rabbi Akiva's Source (II)

### Rabbi Eliezer Background

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁרָאוּ חֲכָמִים שֶׁדַּעְתּוֹ מְיוּשֶּׁבֶת עָלָיו, נִכְנְסוּ וְיָשְׁבוּ לְפָנָיו מֵרָחוֹק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Since the Sages perceived** from this retort **that his mind was stable, they entered and sat before him at a distance of four cubits,** as he was ostracized (see *Bava Metzia* 59b). It is forbidden to sit within four cubits of an ostracized person.

To provide context for the *brayta*, it is necessary to recall who Rabbi Eliezer was and what defined his final years. Rabbi Eliezer is the central figure of the *tanur shel Achnai* episode: all the *chachamim* ruled that a particular oven was *tamei*, while Rabbi Eliezer ruled it *tahor*. He caused the walls of the *beit midrash* to bend inward, the river to flow backward, and even brought forth a heavenly voice affirming his position — yet the rabbis replied *lo bashamayim hi*, and the majority ruling prevailed. Because Rabbi Eliezer refused to accept the principle of majority rule, he was placed in *cherem*, and the other rabbis were barred from entering his space or learning with him directly. It is against this backdrop that the deathbed narrative unfolds.

## Rabbi Eliezer's Deathbed

### Hurcanus Incident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: לָמָּה בָּאתֶם? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה בָּאנוּ. אָמַר לָהֶם: וְעַד עַכְשָׁיו לָמָּה לֹא בָּאתֶם? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא הָיָה לָנוּ פְּנַאי. אָמַר לָהֶן: תָּמֵיהַּ אֲנִי אִם יָמוּתוּ מִיתַת עַצְמָן. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: שֶׁלִּי מַהוּ? אָמַר לוֹ: שֶׁלְּךָ קָשָׁה מִשֶּׁלָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **said to them: Why have you come? They said to him: We have come to study Torah,** as they did not want to say that they came to visit him due to his illness. Rabbi Eliezer **said to them: And why have you not come until now? They said to him: We did not have spare time.** Rabbi Eliezer **said to them: I would be surprised if these** Sages **die their own death,** i.e., a natural death. Rather, they will be tortured to death by the Romans. **Rabbi Akiva said to him: How will my** death come about? Rabbi Eliezer **said to him: Your** death will be **worse than theirs,** as you were my primary student and you did not come to study.

*Kesheh-chalah Rabbi Eliezer* — when Rabbi Eliezer fell ill, Rabbi Akiva and his colleagues came to visit him. He was seated beneath his canopy bed while they remained in the outer chamber of his house. It was a Friday, *erev Shabbat*, and his son Hurcanus entered to remove his *tefillin*, since one does not wear *tefillin* on *Shabbat*. Rabbi Eliezer rebuked him sharply, and Hurcanus left in a state of distress. He turned to the assembled colleagues and said: it appears that my father's mind has become confused — perhaps a sign of the delirium of a dying man, given that he would not allow the removal of his *tefillin* before *Shabbat*.

Rabbi Eliezer, who had overheard this, responded that his *da'at* was entirely sound — *miyushevet* — and that it was his son and perhaps his son's mother who had the problem. He then posed a sharp rhetorical question: *heich manichin issur sekila ve-oseqin be-issur shevut?* — how is it that you are neglecting a concern that could rise to the level of *sekilah*, namely the obligation to light *Shabbat* candles before nightfall, while busying yourselves with my *tefillin*, which at worst would be an *issur d'rabonon*?

The reasoning is as follows: in the era of the *gemara*, the requirement to light candles before *Shabbat* was understood as ensuring that lights were burning in the home before *Shabbat* arrived, and one had to take care to do so with sufficient time. As for *tefillin*, one does not wear them on *Shabbat* because *Shabbat* is not *zman tefillin*, and there is an *issur shevut* — an *issur d'rabonon* — related to going outdoors wearing them into a *reshut ha-rabbim*. Even that would only be *d'rabonon*, since even without *zman tefillin*, wearing them in the house is akin to a *malbush*, a garment. So the worst that could follow from Rabbi Eliezer's still wearing *tefillin* was a rabbinic prohibition, whereas the proper priority at that moment should have been the *Shabbat* candles. You have your priorities entirely wrong.

When the *chachamim* perceived that his *da'at* was indeed sound — and perhaps also recognized that he was still his characteristically pointed self — they entered and sat before him, but at a distance of four *amos*, since he remained in *nidduy* and they were not permitted to enter within his four *amos*. He asked them: why have you come? They said: we came to learn Torah from you. He replied: and why did you not come until now? They answered that they had not had the opportunity — *lo hayah lanu pnai* — clearly not wishing to name the actual reason, dancing around the *nidduy* that had kept them apart.

### Harsh Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָטַל שְׁתֵּי זְרוֹעוֹתָיו וְהִנִּיחָן עַל לִבּוֹ, אָמַר: אוֹי לָכֶם שְׁתֵּי זְרוֹעוֹתַיי, שֶׁהֵן כִּשְׁתֵּי סִפְרֵי תוֹרָה שֶׁנִּגְלָלִין! הַרְבֵּה תּוֹרָה לָמַדְתִּי, וְהַרְבֵּה תּוֹרָה לִימַּדְתִּי. הַרְבֵּה תּוֹרָה לָמַדְתִּי, וְלֹא חִסַּרְתִּי מֵרַבּוֹתַי אֲפִילּוּ כַּכֶּלֶב הַמְּלַקֵּק מִן הַיָּם. הַרְבֵּה תּוֹרָה לִימַּדְתִּי, וְלֹא חִסְּרוּנִי תַּלְמִידַי אֶלָּא כְּמִכְחוֹל בִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **raised his two arms and placed them on his heart,** and he **said: Woe to you, my two arms, as they are like two Torah scrolls that are** now **being rolled up,** and will never be opened again. **I have learned much Torah, and I have taught much Torah. I have learned much Torah, and I have not taken away from my teachers,** i.e., I have not received from their wisdom, **even like a dog lapping from the sea. I have taught much Torah, and my students have taken away from me,** i.e., they have received from my wisdom, **only like** the tiny amount that **a paintbrush** removes **from a tube** of paint.

Rabbi Eliezer then said: *tamei ani im yamutu misat atzman* — I would be astonished if you were to die a natural death. This is a strikingly harsh statement, and it is one that carries terrible historical resonance given what we know of the fate of Rabbi Akiva and his contemporaries in the era of martyrdom. Rabbi Akiva then asked directly: *sheli mahu?* — and what of mine? Rabbi Eliezer replied: *shelcha kasha mishel-lahen* — yours will be worse than theirs. And of course we know the horrific nature of Rabbi Akiva's death. Rashi explains that Rabbi Akiva's fate was considered the most severe because he had the greatest opportunity to learn from Rabbi Eliezer, and therefore the loss sustained by the *nidduy* and the estrangement was greatest in his case.

### Lament on Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁאֲנִי שׁוֹנֶה שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת הֲלָכוֹת בְּבַהֶרֶת עַזָּה, וְלֹא הָיָה אָדָם שׁוֹאֲלֵנִי בָּהֶן דָּבָר מֵעוֹלָם. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁאֲנִי שׁוֹנֶה שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת הֲלָכוֹת, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: שְׁלֹשֶׁת אֲלָפִים הֲלָכוֹת, בִּנְטִיעַת קִשּׁוּאִין, וְלֹא הָיָה אָדָם שׁוֹאֲלֵנִי בָּהֶן דָּבָר מֵעוֹלָם, חוּץ מֵעֲקִיבָא בֶּן יוֹסֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **Moreover, I can teach three hundred *halakhot* with regard to a snow-white leprous mark [*bebaheret*], but no person has ever asked me anything about them.** He could not find a student who could fully understand him in those matters. **Moreover, I can teach three hundred *halakhot*, and some say** that Rabbi Eliezer said **three thousand *halakhot*, with regard to the planting of cucumbers** by sorcery, **but no person has ever asked me anything about them, besides Akiva ben Yosef.**

At that moment, Rabbi Eliezer took his two arms — *natal shtei zero'osav* — and placed them upon his heart. He said: *oy lachem shtei zero'osai* — woe to you, my two arms, for they are like two *sifrei Torah* that have been rolled up. The image is powerful: an arm, in its shape, resembles a closed scroll — sealed, containing everything within it, but inaccessible unless opened. And that was precisely his lament: he carried within himself a vast expanse of *Torah*, but no one was opening him up to receive it.

He continued: *harbeh Torah lamadeti velo chasarti merabbosai afilu kechelev hammelakkeik min hayam* — I learned so much Torah and yet I did not diminish my teachers even as much as a dog lapping water from the sea. The image here is not really about taking something away from one's teachers, but about the vastness of what remained. What he received, even with all that he learned, was but a drop in the ocean. And then for his students: *velo chissaruni talmidai ella kemikchol bishapaferess* — my students did not take from me more than an eyelash applicator drawn from its tube. Notably, the proportion is somewhat larger — the eyelash applicator does take more than a dog from the sea — and Rabbi Eliezer uses this slightly less severe image when speaking of what his students received from him rather than about himself; he reserves the harsher self-deprecation for his own relationship to his teachers. In either case, the essential point is the same: I had an ocean of *Torah* to give, and only the tiniest fraction was ever transmitted.

### Gourds Halachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּעַם אַחַת אֲנִי וָהוּא מְהַלְּכִין הָיִינוּ בַּדֶּרֶךְ, אָמַר לִי: רַבִּי, לַמְּדֵנִי בִּנְטִיעַת קִשּׁוּאִין. אָמַרְתִּי דָּבָר אֶחָד, נִתְמַלְּאָה כׇּל הַשָּׂדֶה קִשּׁוּאִין. אֲמַר לִי: רַבִּי, לִמַּדְתַּנִי נְטִיעָתָן, לַמְּדֵנִי עֲקִירָתָן. אָמַרְתִּי דָּבָר אֶחָד, נִתְקַבְּצוּ כּוּלָּן לְמָקוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer described the incident: **Once he and I were walking along the way,** and **he said to me: My teacher, teach me about the planting of cucumbers. I said one statement** of sorcery, and **the entire field became filled with cucumbers. He said to me: My teacher, you have taught me** about **planting them; teach me** about **uprooting them. I said one statement and they all were gathered to one place.**

Rabbi Eliezer then made his lament explicit. He said: *ela she-ani shoneh shlosh meiot halachot b'vaheret azah* — I have three hundred *halachot* concerning the bright *tzara'at* spot known as *baheret azah*, *v'lo hayah adam she-she'alani bahen davar me'olam* — and not a single person ever asked me about them. The image of the two arms as rolled-up *sifrei Torah* finds its fullest expression here: all of that knowledge, sealed inside, with no one to open it up.

And further: *v'lo od* — and not only this — *she-ani shoneh bishlosh meiot halachot*, some say *shloshet alafim halachot*, in the planting of *kishu'in* — gourds, or cucumbers — *v'lo hayah adam she-she'alani bahen davar me'olam chutz me-Akiva ben Yosef*. Three hundred *halachot*, or according to some versions three thousand, concerning the planting of gourds — and not a single person ever asked him about them, with one exception: Rabbi Akiva.

### Magic Encounter

*Pa'am achat ani v'hu hayinu mehalchin baderech* — once, Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva were walking along the way, and Rabbi Akiva said to him: *Rabbi, lamdeni bi-netiat kishu'in* — Rabbi, teach me something of what you know about the planting of gourds. It seems, reading the context, almost as though Rabbi Akiva was asking in order to give Rabbi Eliezer the opportunity to teach these *halachot* that no one else had ever requested — not because he was necessarily in need of this knowledge himself.

*Amarti davar echad v'nitmalleh kol hasadeh kishu'in* — Rabbi Eliezer said one word and the entire field filled with cucumbers. And then Rabbi Akiva said: you have taught me the planting — *lamdeni akiratan* — now teach me the uprooting. *Amarti davar echad v'nitkabbetzu kullam l'makom echad* — Rabbi Eliezer said another word and they all gathered into one place.

This immediately raises a serious question: how could Rabbi Eliezer actually perform this act? Is there not a *chiyuv sekilah* for actually doing *kishuf*? This is distinct from the *tzara'at* material, which involves genuinely detailed *halachic* questions about what renders a spot *tamei* or *tahor*. The knowledge of *kishuf* is of a different character — it is practical magical knowledge, the performance of which the *Torah* makes a capital offense. The tragedy here is layered: not only was Rabbi Eliezer's *Torah* sealed away due to the *nidduy* and the estrangement from his students, but even the vast knowledge he possessed raises questions about its halachic relevance and what it truly empowered his students with.

This passage also reflects a broader portrait of Rabbi Eliezer that emerges from multiple sources. The *mishnah* in Pirkei Avot describes him as a *bor sud she-eino m'abed tipah* — a plastered cistern that does not lose a drop. A cistern, of course, has no spring of its own; it holds only what was poured into it, and does not generate new water. This is contrasted with the *ma'ayan hamisgaber*, the ever-strengthening wellspring. And the *gemara* in Sukka and elsewhere notes that Rabbi Eliezer would say he never taught anything that he had not received from his teachers. His entire understanding of *Torah she-be'al peh* was one of receiving and transmitting, not of human creativity. This is precisely what drove the *tanur shel Achnai* conflict: he could not accept *lo bashamayim hi*, the principle of rabbinic authority and creative interpretation. He had his tradition; it was true; no majority vote could change that. And so he died as he lived — committed utterly to what he had received, lamenting what he had been unable to pass on.

### Vessel Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הַכַּדּוּר וְהָאִמּוּם וְהַקָּמֵיעַ וּצְרוֹר הַמַּרְגָּלִיּוֹת וּמִשְׁקוֹלֶת קְטַנָּה, מַהוּ? אָמַר לָהֶן: הֵן טְמֵאִין, וְטַהֲרָתָן בְּמָה שֶׁהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** After these comments, the Sages **asked him** questions of *halakha*: **What is** the *halakha*, with regard to ritual impurity, of **a ball** made of leather and stuffed with rags, **and** likewise **a last,** the frame on which a shoe is fashioned, which is made of leather and stuffed with rags, **and** likewise **an amulet** wrapped in leather, **and a pouch for pearls,** wrapped in leather, **and a small weight,** which is wrapped in leather? Rabbi Eliezer **said to them: They are susceptible to impurity, and their purification is** effected by immersing them in a ritual bath **as they are,** as there is no need to open them up.

The sages, perhaps seizing the opportunity to elicit some of the *Torah* he had lamented no one ever asked him about, now posed to him several practical *halachic* questions. They asked about *hakadur, ha'amos, v'hakamia, umishkolet ktana* — a leather ball packed tightly with stuffing, a cobbler's form used to shape shoes, a small pouch used as a charm to hold jewels (Rashi understands this as an amulet for preventing illness, where the stones are never meant to be removed), and a small weight covered in leather. The general principle governing leather vessels is that a *kli* can become *tamei* only if it has a *beit kibul*, a receptacle. The question in each of these cases is whether an object that was hollow and thus had a receptacle, but was then filled and sealed with stuffing not meant to be removed, retains its status as a vessel with a *beit kibul*.

This was a known dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim*, and the sages wished to confirm that he still held his position. Rabbi Eliezer affirmed that they remain *tamei* — *hen tme'im* — and added that their purification is achieved with the stuffing in place: *vetaharatan b'ma shehen*. The stuffing does not constitute a *chatzitza* that would prevent *tevilah* from being effective. This may seem ironic, since the entire reason the object is considered to have a *beit kibul* is because it contains filling — yet for purposes of *tevilah*, that same filling is not treated as a separate entity creating an interposition. Nevertheless, that is Rabbi Eliezer's ruling, consistent throughout, as inflexible and certain as ever.

### Final Ruling & Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְעָל שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי הָאִמּוּם, מַהוּ? אָמַר לָהֶן: הוּא טָהוֹר, וְיָצְאָה נִשְׁמָתוֹ בְּטׇהֳרָה. עָמַד רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עַל רַגְלָיו וְאָמַר: הוּתַּר הַנֶּדֶר, הוּתַּר הַנֶּדֶר!
>
> **Translation:** They asked him further: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **a shoe that is on a last?** Is it considered a complete vessel, which needs no further preparation, and is therefore susceptible to impurity? Rabbi Eliezer **said to them: It is pure, and** with this word, **his soul left** him **in purity. Rabbi Yehoshua stood on his feet and said: The vow is permitted; the vow is permitted;** i.e., the ostracism that was placed on Rabbi Eliezer is removed.

They then asked about *min'al she'al gabei ha'amos mahu* — what is the status of a completed shoe that is still resting on the cobbler's form? It is fully finished, but has not yet been removed from the form. Is it already considered a completed *kli* capable of becoming *tamei*, or does removal from the form constitute the final step of completion? This too was a matter of dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim*, and again he was asked whether he maintained his position.

*Amar lahen hu tahor* — he said to them: it is *tahor*. And as he spoke that word — *yatza nishmatoh b'tahara* — his soul departed in purity. The symmetry is striking: the great dispute that defined his final decades was the *tanur shel Achnai*, in which all the *chachamim* ruled *tamei* and he alone ruled *tahor*. He died with the word *tahor* on his lips. He lived exactly as he died — rigorously consistent in every position, unwavering. A pure ending, one might say, entirely of a piece with the life.

### Nidduy Ended & Eulogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת פָּגַע בּוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בֵּין קֵסָרִי לְלוֹד. הָיָה מַכֶּה בִּבְשָׂרוֹ עַד שֶׁדָּמוֹ שׁוֹתֵת לָאָרֶץ. פָּתַח עָלָיו בְּשׁוּרָה וְאָמַר: אָבִי אָבִי רֶכֶב יִשְׂרָאֵל וּפָרָשָׁיו. הַרְבֵּה מָעוֹת יֵשׁ לִי וְאֵין לִי שׁוּלְחָנִי לְהַרְצוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva was not present at the time of his death. **At the conclusion of Shabbat, Rabbi Akiva encountered** the funeral procession on his way **from Caesarea to Lod.** Rabbi Akiva **was striking his flesh** in terrible anguish and regret **until his blood flowed to the earth. He began to** eulogize Rabbi Eliezer **in the row** of those comforting the mourners, **and said: "My father, my father, the chariot of Israel and its horsemen"** (II Kings 2:12). **I have many coins, but I do not have a money changer** to whom **to give them,** i.e., I have many questions, but after your death I have no one who can answer them.

*Amad Rabbi Yehoshua al raglav v'amar hutar haneder* — Rabbi Yehoshua arose and declared: the *neder* is undone, the *nidduy* is released. Even in death, there are rules that can apply to someone who was under excommunication, and Rabbi Yehoshua formally declared that with Rabbi Eliezer's death, the *nidduy* was concluded.

*Lemotzaei Shabbat paga bo Rabbi Akiva min Keisari l'Lod* — on *motzaei Shabbat*, Rabbi Akiva encountered the funeral procession making its way from Caesarea to Lod — it appears Rabbi Eliezer had died in Caesarea, though he was from Lod, and was being brought there for burial. *Hayah makkeh bivesaro ad shedamo shotet la'aretz* — Rabbi Akiva was striking his own flesh in grief until his blood poured to the ground. Tosafot raises the question of the *d'oraita* prohibition of *veseret lanefesh lo titenu bivesarchem*, the prohibition against making cuts in one's flesh for the dead — though it appears this was not a deliberate incision but an expression of anguished grief.

*Patach alav bishura* — he opened with a eulogy in the processional formation. He quoted the words Elisha spoke when Elijah departed: *avi avi rechev Yisrael ufarashav* — my father, my father, the chariot of Israel and its horsemen. And then he added his own words: *harbeh ma'ot yesh li ve'ein li shulchani lehartzo'tam* — I have so much coinage and no money changer to exchange it. I have no one to help me clarify and make usable all of the teachings I carry.

The image chosen by Rabbi Akiva is telling. A money changer does not create new wealth; he takes existing coinage and changes it — verifies its authenticity, breaks it into smaller denominations, makes it usable. Rabbi Akiva, who represented the diametrically opposite pole from Rabbi Eliezer — all human creativity, all *chiddush* — here describes what he lost in Rabbi Eliezer in terms of the most conservative possible function: someone who could test his coins, help him verify and clarify. It is an extraordinarily fitting and moving valediction.

## Educational Witchcraft

### Learning & Understanding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא, מֵרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר גַּמְרַהּ? גַּמְרַהּ מֵרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְלָא סַבְרַהּ. הֲדַר גַּמְרַהּ מֵרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וְאַסְבְּרַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the matter at hand: **Apparently,** Rabbi Akiva **learned** the *halakhot* of gathering cucumbers through sorcery **from Rabbi Eliezer,** not from Rabbi Yehoshua. The Gemara answers: **He learned it from Rabbi Eliezer but he did not understand it. Later he learned it from Rabbi Yehoshua, and** Rabbi Yehoshua **explained it to him.**

After this deeply layered and powerful narrative, the *gemara* returns to its original question: *alma me-Rabbi Eliezer gamra* — apparently Rabbi Akiva learned the principle about *kishuf* and the planting of *kishu'in* from Rabbi Eliezer, not from Rabbi Yehoshua. The *gemara* resolves the tension as follows: *gamra me-Rabbi Eliezer v'lo savra* — he learned it from Rabbi Eliezer, but did not fully understand it. *Hadar gamra me-Rabbi Yehoshua ve-asbera nihalei* — he then learned it again from Rabbi Yehoshua, and Rabbi Yehoshua explained it to him so that he understood it fully. This resolution is entirely apt given everything we now know of Rabbi Eliezer: the transmitter of received knowledge who was not necessarily the one to open it up and make it comprehensible.

### Witchcraft Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: הָעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֶׂה – חַיָּיב! לְהִתְלַמֵּד שָׁאנֵי, דְּאָמַר מָר: ״לֹא תִלְמַד לַעֲשׂוֹת״ – לַעֲשׂוֹת אִי אַתָּה לָמֵד, אֲבָל אַתָּה לָמֵד לְהָבִין וּלְהוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How could** Rabbi Eliezer **have performed that** act of sorcery? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **one who performs an act** of sorcery **is liable?** The Gemara answers: Performing sorcery not in order to use it, but in order **to teach oneself** the *halakhot* **is different,** and it is permitted; **as the Master says** that it is derived from the verse: **"You shall not learn to do** like the abominations of those nations. There shall not be found among you…one who uses divination, a soothsayer, an enchanter, or a sorcerer" (Deuteronomy 18:9–10), so that **you shall not learn,** i.e., it is prohibited for you to learn, in order **to do, but you may learn,** i.e., it is permitted for you to learn, in order **to understand** the matter yourself **and teach** it to others.

*Heichi avdi hachi?* — but now the *gemara* presses a harder question. How could Rabbi Eliezer actually have performed this act? *Ve'anan tenan oseh ma'aseh chayav* — the *mishnah* itself rules that one who performs *kishuf* as an actual deed, not merely an illusion, is *chayav*. And it sounds very much as though what Rabbi Eliezer did on that road was for real.

The *gemara* answers: *v'shani* — this case is different. The act was performed for educational purposes, to understand and to teach, not in order to actually practice sorcery. This is derived from the *pasuk*: *lo tilmad la'asot* — you shall not learn to do as the abominations of the nations. *La'asot iy ata lamed* — you may not learn in order to do. *Aval ata lamed lehavin ule'horot* — but you may learn for the purpose of understanding and teaching. And the *gemara* concludes that on this basis one may even actually perform the act, if doing so is genuinely for educational purposes. This is, to say the least, a striking ruling — that the very act which carries a *chiyuv sekilah* may be performed when the intent is understanding and transmission rather than practice.

With that, the *gemara* recites *hadran alach Arba Misos*, and *perek* Arba Misos concludes. Though the *perek* is named for the four methods of execution, its content has in practice focused almost entirely on the cases requiring *sekilah* — and even as we move forward, that continues, as *ben sorer u-moreh* is likewise a case of *sekilah*. The extraordinary depth devoted to *ben sorer u-moreh* in the *mishnayot* and *gemara* — far exceeding the treatment of the other cases — is itself significant, and is presumably connected to the famous principle that *ben sorer u-moreh* never actually occurred. The *Torah* gave it so that one could study it and receive reward: *drush v'kabel sechar*.

## Ben Sorer u-Moreh Intro

### Future Conduct

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה, מֵאֵימָתַי נַעֲשֶׂה בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה?
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Torah describes the punishment given to a son who steals money from his parents to eat a gluttonous meal of meat and wine in the company of lowly men. If his parents bring him to court for this act, he is exhorted to desist and is punished with lashes. If he repeats the same misdeed and is again brought to court by his parents within the same three-month period, he is considered a stubborn and rebellious son [*ben sorer umoreh*]. He is liable to receive the death penalty, which in this case is execution by stoning. **From when does a stubborn and rebellious son become** liable to receive the death penalty imposed upon **a stubborn and rebellious son?**

The new *perek* begins on 68b. *Ben sorer u-moreh* is another category requiring *sekilah*, and the *mishnah* opens by asking: *eimatai na'aseh ben sorer u-moreh?* — when does a young man actually become a *ben sorer u-moreh*? What is the period of culpability?

Before examining the technical criteria, it is important to note a foundational principle that the *gemara* will make explicit: the *ben sorer u-moreh* is not executed for the gravity of his current sin. Eating and drinking to excess, disobeying his parents — these are not crimes that in themselves would ordinarily warrant death. Rather, *al shem sofo neherag* — he is executed on account of his future. The *Torah* looks ahead and perceives that he is on a trajectory toward becoming a murderous bandit, and decrees that it is better for him to die while still innocent than to be killed after he has become guilty.

This is an obviously striking and counterintuitive principle, and it is clearly connected to why the *gemara* insists that *ben sorer u-moreh* never actually happened — the halakhic system surrounds the case with so many conditions and restrictions that in practice no one could ever be executed under it. But the principle is important to state up front.

The Ramban adds a crucial dimension here. In the section of *Shoftim*, there are several *mitzvot* where the *Torah* adds the phrase *v'chol Yisrael yishme'u v'yira'u* — and all of Israel will hear and fear. Ramban reads this as indicating that part of the purpose of the death penalty in these cases is not the gravity of the individual's sin per se, but the broader societal function of protecting key institutions. One such case is *zaken mamre*, the elder who defies the ruling of the Sanhedrin: the harsh punishment exists to protect the authority of the court itself, which is a foundational social institution. Similarly here, the parental relationship is an institution whose authority the *Torah* invests with enormous significance, and the punishment of the *ben sorer u-moreh* is designed to uphold it in the broader social consciousness — *v'chol Yisrael yishme'u v'yira'u*.

## Age Requirements

### Hair Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁיָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, וְעַד שֶׁיַּקִּיף זָקָן הַתַּחְתּוֹן וְלֹא הָעֶלְיוֹן, אֶלָּא שֶׁדִּבְּרוּ חֲכָמִים בִּלְשׁוֹן נְקִיָּה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יִהְיֶה לְאִישׁ בֵּן״ – בֵּן וְלֹא בַּת, בֵּן וְלֹא אִישׁ. קָטָן פָּטוּר, שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לִכְלַל מִצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **From when he grows two** pubic **hairs,** which are a sign of puberty and from which time he is considered an adult, **until he has grown a beard around.** The reference here is to **the lower** beard surrounding his genitals, **and not the upper** beard, i.e., his facial hair, **but the Sages spoke in euphemistic terms. As it is stated: "If a man has** a stubborn and rebellious **son"** (Deuteronomy 21:18), which indicates that the penalty for rebelliousness is imposed upon **a son, but not** upon **a daughter;** and upon **a son, but not** upon a fully grown **man. A minor** under the age of thirteen is **exempt** from the penalty imposed upon a stubborn and rebellious son, **because he has not** yet **reached** the age of **inclusion in mitzvot.**

The *mishnah* specifies the window of culpability: *mi-she'yavi shtei se'arot* — from the time he produces two pubic hairs, meaning from age 13 when he becomes *halachically* obligated — *v'ad sheyakif zakan* — until his beard fills out. The *mishnah* immediately clarifies: *ha-tachton v'lo ha-elyon* — the lower beard, not the upper beard, meaning his pubic hair. *Ella she-dibru chachamim bilshon nekiyah* — but the Rabbis expressed themselves euphemistically and called it a beard.

### Ben v'lo Ish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ קָטָן מְנָלַן דְּפָטוּר? מְנָלַן?! כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא: שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לִכְלַל מִצְוֹת! וְתוּ, הֵיכָא אַשְׁכְּחַן דְּעָנַשׁ הַכָּתוּב, דְּהָכָא לִיבְעֵי קְרָא לְמִיפְטְרֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara inquires about the source of the *halakha* taught in the mishna: **From where do we** derive **that a minor** is **exempt** from the punishment imposed upon a stubborn and rebellious son? The Gemara comments: This question is puzzling: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? **The reason** is **as is taught** in the mishna: **Because he has not** yet **reached** the age of **inclusion in mitzvot. And furthermore, where do we find that the verse punishes** a minor, **so that** a special **verse should be required here** in order **to exempt him?**

How does the *Torah* establish these boundaries? The *mishnah* cites the *pasuk*: *ki yihyeh l'ish ben* — when a man has a son who is *sorer u-moreh*. From this the *mishnah* derives: *ben v'lo bat* — a son and not a daughter; *ben v'lo ish* — a son and not a fully grown man. So on one side, he must be old enough to be obligated in *mitzvot*, and on the other side, he must not yet have fully developed physically into a man.

This raises an immediate question: the *mishnah* ends by noting that *katan patur shelo ba l'chlal mitzvot* — a minor is exempt because he has not yet entered the realm of *mitzvot*. But why do we even need to state this? And why is the *gemara*'s opening question — *katan minailan d'patur?* — framed as though the default assumption might be that a minor should be *chayav*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲנַן הָכִי קָאָמְרִינַן: אַטּוּ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה עַל חֶטְאוֹ נֶהֱרָג? עַל שֵׁם סוֹפוֹ נֶהֱרָג! וְכֵיוָן דְּעַל שֵׁם סוֹפוֹ נֶהֱרָג, אֲפִילּוּ קָטָן נָמֵי? וְעוֹד, ״בֵּן״ וְלֹא אִישׁ – קָטָן מַשְׁמַע!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **This** is what **we are saying: Is this to say** that **a stubborn and rebellious son is killed for a sin** that he already committed? But, as will be explained (71b), **he is killed for what** he will become **in the end.** The Torah understands that since the boy has already embarked on an evil path, he will continue to be drawn after his natural tendencies and commit many offenses that are more severe. It is therefore preferable that he should be killed now so that he may die in relative innocence, rather than be put to death in the future bearing much more guilt. **And since he is executed for what he will** become **in the end,** one might have thought that **even a minor as well** can be sentenced to the death penalty as a stubborn and rebellious son. **And furthermore,** the exclusion: **"A son," but not a man, indicates** that **a minor** is in fact included in the *halakha*, as he is not yet a man.

The *gemara* explains: *ki katani ta'ama* — the *mishnah* does state the reason right there. But the deeper point is this: the *ben sorer u-moreh* is not executed for his current sin. *Atu ben sorer u-moreh achatei neherag?* — do you think he is being killed for what he actually did? No — *al shem sofo neherag*, he is killed for what he will become. And if that is so, then why should he have to be technically an adult? The logic of the punishment is prospective, not tied to present liability — *afilu katan nami*, perhaps even a minor should be included. Moreover, the *pasuk* itself says *ben v'lo ish*, a son and not a man, which literally suggests someone who is not yet a man at all — namely, a minor. Both logic and the *pasuk* thus point toward including minors.

### Vigor Window

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: דְּאָמַר קְרָא, ״וְכִי יִהְיֶה לְאִישׁ בֵּן״ – בֵּן הַסָּמוּךְ לִגְבוּרָתוֹ שֶׁל אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** A minor is exempt from the punishment imposed upon a stubborn and rebellious son, **as the verse states: "If a man has a son"** (Deuteronomy 21:18), which indicates that the *halakha* applies to **a son who is close to** the stage of having **the strength of a man,** i.e., close to full maturity, but not to a younger boy.

The resolution comes from *Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: the *pasuk* reads *ki yihyeh l'ish ben*, and the juxtaposition of *le'ish ben* teaches *ben hasamuch ligvurato shel ish* — a son who is close to the vigor of a man. Rashi suggests reading the phrase almost in reverse: *ki yihyeh ben le'ish* — when the son has just become a man. The window of culpability is thus the period after he has technically reached *halachic* adulthood by producing two pubic hairs, but before he has fully filled out physically into an *ish*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד שֶׁיַּקִּיף זָקָן הַתַּחְתּוֹן כּוּ׳. תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: עַד שֶׁיַּקִּיף עֲטָרָה. כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: הַקָּפַת גִּיד, וְלֹא הַקָּפַת בֵּיצִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that a boy can be sentenced as a stubborn and rebellious son only **until he has grown a lower beard. Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** a *baraita* that states: **Until** the hair **surrounds the corona. When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said** in explanation of Rabbi Ḥiyya's statement: The reference in the mishna is to hair **surrounding the penis and not** to hair **surrounding** the sac holding the **testicles,** which grows later.

*Ad sheyakif zakkan ha-tachton v'lo ha-elyon* — until the lower beard fills out, not the upper. Rabbi Chiya taught this using the term *atara* — the crown — as a further euphemism. And when Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he specified further: *hakafat gid v'lo hakafat beitzim* — the hair surrounding the shaft, not the hair surrounding the testicles.

## Minor Paternity

### Rav Chisda's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: קָטָן שֶׁהוֹלִיד, אֵין בְּנוֹ נַעֲשֶׂה בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יִהְיֶה לְאִישׁ בֵּן״ – לְאִישׁ בֵּן, וְלֹא לְבֵן בֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥisda says:** In the case of **a minor who fathered** a child, **his son cannot become a stubborn and rebellious son, as it is stated: "If a man has** a stubborn and rebellious **son,"** which indicates that the *halakha* applies only if **a man has a son, but not** if **a son,** i.e., one who is not yet a man, **has a son.**

Rav Chisda introduces a further dimension of the requirement. *Katan sheholid ein beno na'aseh ben sorer u-moreh* — if a minor fathered a child, that child cannot become a *ben sorer u-moreh*. The derivation comes from the same *pasuk*: *ki yihyeh l'ish ben* — *le'ish ben v'lo le'ben ben* — when a man has a son, but not when a child has a son. The son of someone who himself was a minor when he fathered that child does not qualify.

### Verse Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב! אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא: ״כִּי יִהְיֶה בֵּן לְאִישׁ״. מַאי ״כִּי יִהְיֶה לְאִישׁ בֵּן״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְכִדְרַב חִסְדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** How can Rav Ḥisda derive his *halakha* from this verse? Doesn't **he require this** verse **to** teach us that **which Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says,** namely, that a minor is exempt from the punishment imposed upon a stubborn and rebellious son? The Gemara answers: This verse teaches two *halakhot*, as, **were** the intention to teach only the *halakha* that a minor is exempt, **let the verse say: If there be a son to a man.** For **what** reason does the verse introduce a change into the normal word order and state: **"If a man has a son"? Conclude from it** that the verse serves **to** teach the ruling **of Rav Ḥisda.**

The *gemara* challenges this immediately: is this not the same *pasuk* used by *Rav Yehuda amar Rav* to teach the *vigour window*? How can the same juxtaposition of *le'ish ben* be used to teach both that the father must be an adult and that the son must be at the transitional moment of early manhood?

The *gemara* responds with a precise textual observation. Had the *Torah* wished only to teach Rav Chisda's point — that it must be a man's son, not a child's son — it could have written *ki yihyeh ben ish*, which would naturally carry the connotation of *ben le'ish* and not *ben le'ben*. The fact that the *Torah* wrote *ki yihyeh l'ish ben* — placing *l'ish* before *ben* — allows both teachings to be derived simultaneously. *Shema mina tarti*: both that we are speaking of a son who has just reached the vigor of manhood, and that the father must himself have been an adult, not a minor, at the time of fathering.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבָּה, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: קָטָן אֵינוֹ מוֹלִיד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם אֵין לָאִישׁ גֹּאֵל״. וְכִי יֵשׁ לְךָ אָדָם בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ גּוֹאֵל? אֶלָּא, בְּגֶזֶל הַגֵּר הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** Rav Ḥisda's statement **disagrees with** the opinion **of Rabba, as Rabba says** in connection with one who returns stolen property after having taken a false oath that he did not steal it: **A minor cannot father** a child, **as it is stated: "But if the man has no relative** to whom restitution may be made, let the trespass that is recompensed to the Lord be the priest's" (Numbers 5:8). **And is there any man in the Jewish people who does not have a relative?** All members of the Jewish people are related, as they all descend from the patriarch Jacob, and therefore every person has a relative who is fit to inherit from him. **Rather, the verse is speaking of the robbery of a convert,** who is treated like a newborn child and considered to have no ties to his natural parents or relatives. If he did not father any children after his conversion, he is a man with no relatives at all, and therefore if he dies, property that had been stolen from him must be returned to a priest.

The *gemara* notes that Rav Chisda's ruling implicitly assumes that a minor *can* father a child — otherwise the rule would be superfluous. This places Rav Chisda in tension with Rava, who holds that a minor *cannot* father a child at all. Rava derives this from the laws of *gezel ha-ger*: when someone steals from a convert, denies it under oath, later admits the theft, and the convert has since died, the thief must return the money to whoever is the convert's heir. If there is no heir — *v'ein l'ish go'el* — the money goes to the *kohen*.

The *gemara* notes that ordinarily, virtually every Jew has some relative who can serve as heir. The only case in which one could have no heir is a convert — a *ger* — who has left behind no Jewish relatives and has no children. The *pasuk* says *ish* — a man — implying that if the deceased was an adult man, one must investigate whether he has heirs. But if he was a minor — *katan* — *i ata tzarich lachzor alav*, you need not investigate, because *be-yadu'a she-ein lo go'alim* — it is a certainty that he has no heirs. And why is that a certainty? Because a minor cannot father a child. That is Rava's derivation: a minor is biologically and *halachically* incapable of fathering a child, and therefore no investigation into his potential heirs is necessary.

The *gemara* thus sets up a genuine dispute between Rav Chisda and Rava regarding whether a minor can father a child, and how we will see this play out will be taken up on the next *daf*.
