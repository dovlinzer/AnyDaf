# Hullin 70b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Overview

### Context & Transition

The opening pages of this *perek* have dealt with *shechita* and *kashrus*-related questions surrounding the mother and her fetus: when slaughtering a mother cow, one may eat the fetus found inside, with attendant discussions about protruding limbs and the status of a firstborn. Now the *daf* pivots to an entirely different domain—*tumah v'taharah*—while retaining the same underlying phenomenon of the mother and the fetus within her womb.

### Mishnah: Fetus Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּהֵמָה שֶׁמֵּת עוּבָּרָהּ בְּתוֹךְ מֵעֶיהָ וְהוֹשִׁיט הָרוֹעֶה אֶת יָדוֹ וְנָגַע בּוֹ, בֵּין בִּבְהֵמָה טְמֵאָה בֵּין בִּבְהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה – טָהוֹר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: בִּטְמֵאָה – טָמֵא, וּבִטְהוֹרָה – טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **an animal whose fetus died in its womb and the shepherd reached his hand** into the womb **and touched** the fetus, **both in** the case of **a non-kosher animal and in** the case of **a kosher animal** the fetus does not have the status of an animal carcass that imparts ritual impurity, and the shepherd remains **ritually pure. Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: In** the case of **a non-kosher** animal it is **impure, and in** the case of **a kosher** animal it is **pure.**

The *mishnah* at the top of 70b opens: *behemah shemeisah v'ubar b'toch me'eha*—an animal that has died while its fetus is still in the womb, a stillborn. Were such a fetus to have been born and died on the outside, it would be a *nevelah*, and one who touches it would become *tamei* at the level of a *rishon*. The question the *mishnah* poses is whether that *tumah* exists while the fetus remains inside the living mother's womb.

The shepherd—*Tosafot* notes that the one assisting in the birthing of cattle is referred to in the masculine because of this very term—reaches his hand inside and touches the dead fetus. The *mishnah* rules: *bein b'vehemah tme'ah bein b'vehemah tehorah, tahor*—whether the mother is a kosher or non-kosher animal, the one who touches the fetus is *tahor*. The dead fetus in the womb, regardless of species, does not yet communicate *tumah*.

This ruling stands in direct contrast to the subsequent *mishnah*, which discusses a human stillbirth: there, if a midwife reaches her hand into the womb and touches the fetus, she becomes *tme'ah*, treated as though she has touched a corpse even before the fetus has been delivered. The underlying distinction turns on the concept of *balua*—literally "swallowed up," absorbed within another body. From the perspective of the mother, the fetus is still entirely contained within her and has not yet entered the outside world. But from the perspective of the midwife or shepherd who inserts a hand into the womb, the situation is different: that person is now in direct contact with the fetus in a shared space, not separated from it by any intervening body. Nevertheless, the *mishnah* holds that for an animal fetus, even this direct contact does not produce *tumah*, whereas for a human fetus it does—and the *gemara* will explain why.

## Tanna Kamma vs. R. Yose

### R. Yose: Kosher/Non-Kosher Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְכׇל הוֹלֵךְ עַל כַּפָּיו בְּכׇל הַחַיָּה הַהֹלֶכֶת וְגוֹ׳״ – מְהַלְּכֵי כַּפַּיִם בְּחַיָּה טִמֵּאתִי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, what is the reason** for his ruling? **Rabbi Yitzḥak said that the verse states** with regard to non-kosher animals that do not have cloven hooves: **"And whatever walks on its paws, among any [*bekhol*] undomesticated animal [*ḥayya*] that walks** on all fours, they are impure for you; whoever touches their carcass shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 11:27). Rabbi Yosei HaGelili expounds this verse as follows: Those animals **that walk on** their **paws,** i.e., that do not have cloven hooves, which are **inside** the body of **an animal, I rendered impure for you.** Rabbi Yitzḥak interprets the term *bekhol* as meaning: Inside the body of, and the word *ḥayya* as meaning: A live animal. Accordingly, he understands the verse to be referring to a dead fetus found inside a living animal.

*Rabbi Yose HaGlili* dissents from the anonymous first opinion: *b'tme'ah tamei, uv'tehorah tahor*—by a non-kosher animal, such as a horse, touching the dead fetus in the womb renders one *tamei*; by a kosher animal, such as a cow, it does not. This distinction, which might initially seem strange given that both a dead horse and a dead cow generate the same *tumah* of *nevelah*, becomes intelligible upon reflection.

### Logic: Shechita & Edibility

The key consideration is what would happen if the mother were slaughtered. If the mother cow were subject to *shechita* while the dead fetus was inside her, that fetus would be treated as a piece of meat belonging to the mother and could be eaten—this was precisely the ruling discussed earlier in the *perek*. By a horse, of course, *shechita* is irrelevant because the animal can never be eaten in any case.

Rabbi Yose HaGlili's position therefore rests on the following logic: since the dead fetus inside a kosher mother cow could, through the mother's *shechita*, become permissible for consumption—regarded simply as a piece of meat attached to the mother—we cannot yet classify it as an animal corpse. And if it cannot yet be called a *nevelah*, it cannot communicate the *tumah* of a *nevelah*. This reasoning is also why the logic of the *mishnah* is not trivial: once you assert that the dead fetus in the mother's womb is not a *nevelah*, that status cannot suddenly reverse itself. The only way the mother's *shechita* can render the fetus permissible is if one maintains from the outset that a dead fetus in the womb is not yet a *nevelah*. The two positions are interdependent.

### Tanna Kamma: All Equal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: קַל וָחוֹמֶר, אִם הוֹעִילָה אִמּוֹ לְהַתִּירוֹ בַּאֲכִילָה, לֹא תּוֹעִיל לוֹ לְטַהוֹרֵיהּ מִידֵי נְבֵלָה?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the ruling **of the first *tanna*? Rav Ḥisda said:** It is apparent through **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If** being inside **its mother is effective to permit it for consumption** through the slaughter of its mother even if the fetus was found dead inside the womb, then **should** being inside its mother **not** also **be effective to render it pure from** the impurity of **an animal carcass?**

The *tanna kamma* agrees with Rabbi Yose HaGlili's reasoning for the kosher animal but goes further, extending the same ruling to the fetus of a non-kosher animal. The *tanna kamma* holds that since kosher and non-kosher animals are treated identically for *tumah* purposes—both corpses being classified as *nevelah* and generating the same *tumah*—what is true of one must be true of the other. If the dead fetus of a kosher animal in the womb is *tahor*, the same applies to the dead fetus of a non-kosher animal.

## Rav Chisda: Kal VaChomer

### Kal VaChomer Logic

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama d'Tanna Kamma*—what is the reasoning of the *tanna kamma* who says that even the dead fetus of a non-kosher animal does not cause *tumah*? Rav Chisda answers: it is a *kal vachomer*. The argument proceeds in two steps, the first of which also supports Rabbi Yose HaGlili.

The *kal vachomer* is formulated as follows: *im ho'il imo lehatirah ba'achilah, lo to'il lo letaharo midei nevelah*—if the mother is capable of making this dead fetus permissible for eating, should she not certainly be able to remove it from the status of *nevelah* for purposes of *tumah*? The point is that rendering something fit for consumption is in some sense a more demanding standard than simply determining *tumah* status. If the mother's *shechita* can achieve the more demanding result of permitting the fetus for consumption, how much more so should it prevent the fetus from being classified as a *nevelah* that causes *tumah*. This is the *kal vachomer* that explains why the dead fetus of a kosher animal in its mother's womb does not render one *tamei*.

### Kosher Animal: Tahor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה, בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a rationale for this *halakha* with regard to **a kosher animal; from where do we** derive that the same applies to **a non-kosher animal?**

The *gemara* thus establishes: *ashkachan behemah tehorah*—we have accounted for the kosher animal. This is the basis for Rabbi Yose HaGlili as well as the *tanna kamma* with respect to the kosher species. The next question is how to extend this ruling to the non-kosher animal.

## Non-Kosher Fetus Derivation

### Q: Non-Kosher Status

*Behemah tme'ah minayin*—and from where do we know this ruling applies to the non-kosher animal? In the case of a horse or other non-kosher species, the mother's *shechita* is irrelevant; the fetus inside can never be rendered permissible for eating. The *kal vachomer* does not apply. So why would the dead fetus of a non-kosher animal also be *tahor* in the womb?

### Pasuk: Vechi Yamut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכִי יָמוּת מִן הַבְּהֵמָה״ – זוֹ בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה, ״אֲשֶׁר הִיא לָכֶם לְאׇכְלָה״ – זוֹ בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה, אִיתַּקַּשׁ בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה לִבְהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה: מָה בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה – עוּבָּרָהּ טָהוֹר, אַף בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה – עוּבָּרָהּ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **the verse states:** "And when a domesticated animal dies, of those that you eat, one who touches its carcass shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 11:39). The Gemara interprets the verse as follows: **"And when a domesticated animal dies," this** is referring to **a non-kosher animal; "of those that you eat," this** is referring to **a kosher animal.** The verse thereby **juxtaposes a non-kosher animal to a kosher animal** with regard to imparting impurity of a carcass, and teaches that **just as** with regard to **a kosher animal, its fetus** that died in its womb is **pure,** as derived above through an *a fortiori* inference, **so too,** with regard to **a non-kosher animal, its** dead **fetus** is **pure.**

The *gemara* cites a *pasuk*: *v'chi yamut min habehemah asher hi lachem l'ochlah, hanogea b'nivlatah yitma ad ha'arev*—"and if it dies from the animal that is for you to eat, one who touches its corpse is *tamei* until evening." This verse addresses the *tumah* of a *nevelah* of a kosher animal. The *gemara* now applies a *drasha* to this verse: although the verse concludes with *asher hi lachem l'ochlah*—"which you may eat"—one may pause at the phrase *min habehemah*, "from an animal," which can encompass any animal, kosher or non-kosher. Thus: *min habehemah*—*zu behemah tme'ah*, a non-kosher animal as well; *asher hi lachem l'ochlah*—*zu behemah tehorah*, a kosher animal.

### Hekesh: Kosher/Non-Kosher

This reading creates a *hekesh*: it links kosher and non-kosher animals together with respect to the *tumah* of an animal corpse. The *gemara* then spells out the implication: *mah behemah tehorah ubaratah tahor*—just as by a kosher animal the dead fetus inside is *tahor* (since, as demonstrated by the *kal vachomer*, it could become permissible through the mother's *shechita*), *af behemah tme'ah ubaratah tahor*—so too, by a non-kosher animal, the dead fetus in the womb is *tahor*.

The structure of the argument is now clear. Logically, one can only prove on independent grounds that the dead fetus of a kosher animal does not cause *tumah*—that is Rabbi Yose HaGlili's position. The *tanna kamma* goes further by using this *hekesh* to extend the ruling to the non-kosher animal: since *tumah* of *nevelah* is identical for both species, we compare them and apply the same rule to both fetuses. Rabbi Yose HaGlili does not accept this extension, maintaining the distinction. The *tanna kamma*'s position depends entirely on the *hekesh* to cross that line.

## Refining Hekesh Criteria

### Challenge: Hooves vs. Kosher Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, קָלוּט בִּמְעֵי פָרָה לִיטַמֵּא, דִּמְהַלְּכֵי כַפַּיִם בְּחַיָּה הוּא? מְהַלְּכֵי כַפַּיִם בִּמְהַלְּכֵי אַרְבַּע, וְהַאי מְהַלְּכֵי אַרְבַּע בִּמְהַלְּכֵי שְׁמֹנֶה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** then a dead fetus with **non-cloven** hooves found **inside a cow should be impure,** despite the fact that it is from a kosher species, **as it is** an animal that **walks on** its **paws** that is **inside** the body of a kosher **animal.** The Gemara explains: The verse renders impure an animal that **walks upon** its four **paws** that is **inside** an animal **that walks** on **four** paws. **But this is** a case of an animal that **walks** on **four inside** an animal that **walks on eight.** Since the mother cow's hooves are entirely split into two parts, it actually walks on eight parts and is not the animal referred to by the verse.

The *gemara* is not yet satisfied. The verse used by the *tanna kamma* to create the *hekesh* does not actually speak in terms of kosher versus non-kosher. Rather, it invokes the language of *mahalchei kapayim*—things that walk on paws. This raises an immediate question: should the relevant criterion be kosher status, or the physical characteristic of having split hooves versus non-split hooves?

The *gemara* explores this problem through a series of cases. The first is *kalut b'mei para*—a cow with non-split hooves (*kalut*), an aberration, found as a fetus within its mother. Would one say that because this fetus walks on paws—non-split hooves—even though it is the fetus of a kosher animal, it would cause *tumah* within the womb? According to the logic that the *pasuk* speaks about paws, one might think so: *d'mahalchei kapayim b'chayah hu*—it is a paw-walker inside of an animal.

### Holech BeChol Holech

The *gemara* responds by attending to the full verse: *v'chol holech al kapav b'chol hachayah haholechet al arba*. The phrase *haholechet al arba*—"that goes on four"—at the end of the verse is significant. A cow with split hooves is considered to be going, in effect, on eight, since each split hoof counts as two. Therefore, *tumah* is only generated when a paw-walker is found inside of a four-footed creature—that is, one whose hooves are not split. A non-split-hooved fetus inside a normal cow, which is "eight-footed," would not fall under this rule: *v'hai mahalchei arba v'mahalchei shmonah hu*—it is a four-footed within an eight-footed, and the verse requires four-within-four.

### Further Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרָה בִּמְעֵי גָמָל לֹא תְּטַמֵּא, דִּמְהַלְּכֵי שְׁמֹנֶה בִּמְהַלְּכֵי אַרְבַּע הוּא, ״הוֹלֵךְ״, ״וְכׇל הוֹלֵךְ״ – לְרַבּוֹת פָּרָה בִּמְעֵי גָמָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection: If that is so, then a dead fetus in the form of **a cow,** which has eight parts to its legs, **inside the womb of a camel,** a non-kosher animal with non-cloven hooves, **should not be impure, as it is** an animal that **walks on eight inside** an animal that **walks on four,** and the verse is not referring to it. The Gemara responds that the verse could have stated: **Walks upon,** but instead states: **"And whatever walks upon,"** i.e., the addition of the word "whatever" serves **to include** a dead **cow** fetus **in the womb of a camel,** teaching that it is impure.

The *gemara* now pushes further. *para b'mei gamal*—a fetus resembling a cow, with split hooves, found within the womb of a camel. The camel has no split hooves and is thus "four-footed." The cow-like fetus with split hooves would be "eight-footed." So here we have an eight-footed within a four-footed. Is this covered by the *pasuk*?

The *gemara* answers: *holech b'chol holech*—the verse says not merely "that goes" but "everything that goes," *v'chol*, which extends the rule to include even this aberrant case. The inclusive language of the verse thus covers a split-hooved fetus inside a non-split-hooved mother.

But the *gemara* is still not satisfied, because the analysis still tracks hooves rather than kosher status. It therefore poses the case of *kalut b'mei kelutah*—a fetus with non-split hooves inside a mother that also has non-split hooves, an aberration of a kosher animal. This is a case of four-footed within four-footed, which fits the verse precisely—and yet both mother and fetus are kosher animals. According to the hoof-based reading, this should cause *tumah*. The *gemara* responds: here, one returns to Rav Chisda's *kal vachomer*. Since this is ultimately a kosher fetus inside a kosher mother, the mother's *shechita* could render the fetus permissible for eating, and therefore it cannot be treated as a *nevelah* causing *tumah*. The *kal vachomer* overrides the apparent implication of the verse.

### Rav Achtva'i Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָדְבוּי בַּר אַמֵּי: חֲזִיר בִּמְעֵי חֲזִירְתָּא לָא לִיטַמֵּא, דִּמְהַלְּכֵי שְׁמֹנֶה בִּמְהַלְּכֵי שְׁמֹנֶה הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥadvoi bar Ami objects to this** explanation that the verse renders impure only animals that walk on four inside animals that walk on four: If that is so, a dead **pig** fetus **inside the womb of a female pig should not be impure, as** a pig has cloven hooves, and so **it is** an animal that **walks on eight inside** an animal that **walks on eight,** and so the verse is not referring to it.

At this point, *rav achtva'i bar ami* raises a decisive objection. The entire discussion has been struggling to locate a case where the hoof-based criterion yields the same result as the kosher/non-kosher criterion. But, he notes, there is an obvious counterexample that has been overlooked: the pig. A pig has split hooves but is not kosher. Therefore, *chazir b'mei chazirta*—the fetus of a pig inside a sow—presents the case of a split-hooved creature inside a split-hooved creature: *d'mahalchei shmonah meimalchei shmonah hu*, an eight-footed within an eight-footed. According to the hoof-based reading, this case *should not* cause *tumah*. But there is no logical basis for saying that the dead fetus of a pig in its mother's womb is *tahor*—neither the *kal vachomer* (which depends on the mother's *shechita* being effective) nor any other consideration applies. This demonstrates conclusively that the dividing line cannot be hooves; it must be kosher versus non-kosher status.

## Rav Nachman: Vayikra Source

### Pasuk Omission Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, מֵהָכָא: ״נֶפֶשׁ כִּי תִגַּע בְּכׇל טָמֵא אוֹ בְנִבְלַת חַיָּה טְמֵאָה אוֹ בְּנִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה אוֹ בְּנִבְלַת שֶׁרֶץ טָמֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that Rabbi Yosei HaGelili's opinion is derived **from here:** The verse states with regard to the sliding-scale offering brought by one who was unaware that he was impure when he ate consecrated food or entered the Temple: **"A person who touched anything impure, or the carcass of a non-kosher undomesticated animal, or the carcass of a non-kosher domesticated animal, or the carcass of an impure creeping animal,** and is guilty, it having being hidden from him that he is impure" (Leviticus 5:2).

Having established that the hoof-based approach fails, the *gemara* turns to a different source. *ela amar rav nachman bar yitzchak me'hacha*—rather, Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak derives the ruling from a different verse. The verse in question is from the beginning of *vayikra*, in the context of the *korban oleh v'yored*: *nefesh ki tiga b'chol tamei, o b'nivlat chayah tme'ah, o b'nivlat behemah tme'ah, o b'nivlat sheretz tamei*—"a soul that touches anything *tamei*, or the corpse of a non-kosher wild animal, or the corpse of a non-kosher domesticated animal, or the corpse of a *sheretz*." This verse lists various categories of *tumah* that trigger liability. Strikingly, what the verse does *not* mention is the corpse of a *kosher* animal.

### Fetus-Specific Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה מְטַמְּאָה, וּבִטְהוֹרָה לֹא מְטַמְּאָה? אֶלָּא אֵיזֶה זֶה? זֶה עוּבָּר, שֶׁבִּטְמֵאָה טָמֵא, וּבִטְהוֹרָה טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The verse presents a difficulty: **Does** only **a carcass of a non-kosher animal impart impurity, and** that **of a kosher** animal **not impart impurity?** The *halakha* is that both do. **Rather, what is this** animal to which the verse is referring? **This is a fetus, which** if **inside a non-kosher** animal is **impure, and** if **inside a kosher** animal is **pure.**

The *gemara* asks the obvious question: *v'chi nivalat behemah tme'ah metamei, u'tehorah lo metame'ah*—does only the corpse of a non-kosher animal cause *tumah*, while the corpse of a kosher animal does not? Of course not—there is an explicit verse elsewhere that establishes the *tumah* of a kosher animal's corpse. Why, then, is the kosher animal's corpse absent from this passage?

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's answer is elegant: the omission is intentional. There exists precisely one context in which the corpse of a non-kosher animal creates *tumah* but the corpse of a kosher animal does not—and that is the case of the fetus in the womb. The verse, by listing only non-kosher animals, is specifically teaching about fetal status.

### Resolution: Non-Kosher Only

*Ela eizehu zeh—ubar she'bitme'ah tamei u'vitehorah tahor*: which case is this? This is the fetus—where the fetus of a non-kosher animal causes *tumah* and the fetus of a kosher animal does not. This is the position of Rabbi Yose HaGlili, and Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's verse provides a clean *scriptural* basis for it: the *torah* itself encodes the asymmetry between kosher and non-kosher fetuses precisely in this *parshah* by mentioning only the non-kosher animal's corpse.

## Both Verses Needed

### Purpose of Hooves Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵאַחַר דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִדְּרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק לְמָה לִי? אִי לָאו דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: כּוּלֵּיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי הוּא דַּאֲתָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And since** this *halakha* **is derived from** the statement of **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak, why do I** need the proof **of Rabbi Yitzḥak** from the verse: "And whatever walks on its paws, among any undomesticated animal that walks on all fours" (Leviticus 11:27)? The Gemara explains: **Were it not** for the derivation of **Rabbi Yitzḥak, I would say** that **the entire** verse expounded by Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak **comes to** teach only **that which Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi derived from it, as cited by the Gemara later on. Rabbi Yitzḥak's statement **teaches us** that the verse is also to be expounded as Rav Nachman bar Yitzḥak explained.

The *gemara* now raises a natural follow-up: *ume'achar denafkah lei mi'd'Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, d'Rabbi Yitzchak lamah li*—once we can derive the whole ruling from Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's verse, what is still the purpose of the verse used by Rabbi Yitzchak, which spoke about paw-walkers inside of animals? If Rav Nachman's *pasuk* already establishes that only the non-kosher fetus causes *tumah*, why do we need Rabbi Yitzchak's verse at all?

### Combined Verses: Distinguishing Criteria

The *gemara* answers: *ei lav d'Rabbi Yitzchak, hava amina kullah k'Rabbi hu de'asa*—had it not been for Rabbi Yitzchak's verse, one might have concluded that the entire point of the verse in *vayikra* that mentions non-kosher animals is not to address the question of fetuses at all, but rather to convey a different *drasha* attributed to *rabbi* (whose teaching will be encountered shortly). Without Rabbi Yitzchak's verse drawing our attention to the possibility that a fetus within a mother might have distinct *tumah* status, we would not have known to read the *vayikra* verse in the way Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes.

It is the *combination* of both verses that does the work. Rabbi Yitzchak's verse, which speaks of a creature inside another creature (*bechol hachayah*), alerts us to the possibility of differential fetal *tumah* status. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's verse then provides the textual demonstration that this differential exists and that the dividing line is kosher versus non-kosher. Neither verse alone is sufficient; together they establish that fetuses of non-kosher animals cause *tumah* while fetuses of kosher animals do not.

## Behema/Chaya Terminology

### Terms Introduced

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: נַמְתִּי לוֹ לְבֶן עַזַּאי, לָמַדְנוּ נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה, וְנִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה, נִבְלַת חַיָּה טְמֵאָה שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yonatan,** a student of Rabbi Yishmael, **says: I said [*namti*] to ben Azzai: We learned that a carcass of a kosher domesticated animal imparts impurity** from the verse: "And when a domesticated animal dies, of those that you eat, one who touches its carcass shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 11:39). **And** we learned that **a carcass of a non-kosher domesticated animal imparts impurity,** and **that a carcass of a non-kosher undomesticated animal imparts impurity,** from the verse: "A person who touched anything impure, or the carcass of a non-kosher undomesticated animal, or the carcass of a non-kosher domesticated animal" (Leviticus 5:2).

The preceding discussion involved verses that speak of any *behemah* that dies or any *chayah* that dies, and this brings the *gemara* to a brief but illuminating digression concerning the terms *behemah* and *chayah* in *tanach*. These terms can be used in a technical sense—where *behemah* refers to domesticated animals and *chayah* to non-domesticated ones—or in a non-technical sense, where both terms simply mean "animal" in general. The *gemara* now explores how this ambiguity affects the determination of which animal corpses cause *tumah*.

### Tanya: R. Yonatan/Ben Azzai

*A braita* is introduced: *amar Rabbi Yonatan, namti lo l'Ben Azzai*—Rabbi Yonatan said, I spoke to Ben Azzai. Rabbi Yonatan recounts that he told Ben Azzai: we have learned that the corpse of a kosher *behemah* causes *tumah*—this is explicit in the verse *v'chi yamut min habehemah asher hi lachem l'ochlah, hanogea b'nivlatah yitma ad ha'erev*. We also know that the corpse of a non-kosher *behemah* causes *tumah*, and that the corpse of a non-kosher *chayah* causes *tumah*—both are stated explicitly in the *vayikra* passage.

### Gap: Kosher Chaya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִבְלַת חַיָּה טְהוֹרָה לֹא לָמַדְנוּ מִנַּיִן? נָם לִי: ״כָּל הוֹלֵךְ עַל כַּפָּיו בְּכׇל הַחַיָּה הַהֹלֶכֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** But **we did not** yet **learn** about **a carcass of a kosher undomesticated animal. From where** is it derived that it imparts impurity? Ben Azzai **said to me** that this is derived from the verse: "And **whatever walks on its paws, among any undomesticated animal that walks** on all fours" (Leviticus 11:27). The inclusive term "among any" serves to include even a carcass of a kosher undomesticated animal among those that impart impurity.

What is missing from all of these explicit sources is the corpse of a *kosher chayah*—a deer, for example. The *torah* gives us the *tumah* of a kosher *behemah*, a non-kosher *behemah*, and a non-kosher *chayah*, but never explicitly states that a dead kosher *chayah* causes *tumah*. Rabbi Yonatan asks Ben Azzai: *nevelat chayah tehorah lo lamadnu—minayin*, from where do we know that a kosher wild animal's corpse also causes *tumah*?

## Ben Azzai's Derasha

### Ben Azzai's Logic

Ben Azzai responds by citing the verse: *kol haholech al kapav b'chol hachayah haholechet al arba*—"everything that goes on its paws among all the *chayot* that go on four." Ben Azzai reads *b'chol hachayah* as introducing an additional category: not merely the paw-walkers, but any *chayah* that goes on four—including kosher *chayot* such as a deer. The dead corpse of any such *chayah*, he argues, causes *tumah*, as derived from the conclusion of the verse: *kol hanogea b'nivlatam yitma ad ha'erev*.

### R. Yonatan Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַמְתִּי לוֹ: וְכִי נֶאֱמַר ״וְכׇל חַיָּה״? וַהֲלֹא לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא ״בְּכׇל הַחַיָּה״, לִמְהַלְּכֵי כַּפַּיִם בְּחַיָּה הוּא דַּאֲתָא. נָם לִי: וּמָה יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר בְּדָבָר הַזֶּה?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yonatan continues. **I said to** ben Azzai: **But does it state: And any undomesticated animal? Isn't it stated only: "Among any undomesticated animal"?** And as explained earlier, this **comes to** teach **that** a dead fetus in the form of any of the animals that **walk on** their **paws inside an undomesticated animal imparts impurity. He said to me: And what does** Rabbi **Yishmael,** your teacher, **say about this matter?**

Rabbi Yonatan rejects this *drasha*. He says: *v'chi ne'emar v'chol chayah—halo lo ne'emar ela b'chol hachayah*—the verse does not say "and every *chayah*"; it says "among all the *chayah*." The straightforward *pshat* of the verse is that it speaks of one category—paw-walkers—found among all the *chayot* that go on four. The phrase *b'chol hachayah* is not introducing a separate, additional category of animals; it is a qualifier indicating context. *lemahalchei kapayim b'chayah hu de'asar*—the verse is only forbidding the paw-walking animals found in that context. There is no second category being included. Rabbi Yonatan insists that this *drasha* is not *pshat*, and therefore he does not accept it.

## R. Yishmael Approach

### R. Yishmael Offered

Ben Azzai, recognizing that his *drasha* has been rejected, turns to Rabbi Yonatan and asks: *u'mah Yishmael omer ba'davar hazeh*—what does your teacher, Rabbi Yishmael, say about this question? The exchange is telling: Ben Azzai comes from the school of Rabbi Akiva, and Rabbi Yonatan is aligned with the school of Rabbi Yishmael. The implicit tension is between the more expansive hermeneutic approach associated with Rabbi Akiva—willing to derive additional categories from unusual phrasing—and the more *pshat*-oriented approach associated with Rabbi Yishmael, which Ben Azzai's rejected *drasha* exemplified.

Rabbi Yonatan then conveys Rabbi Yishmael's answer. He cites once more: *v'chi yamut min habehemah asher hi lachem l'ochlah*—and this is where Rabbi Yishmael's *drasha* operates. The phrase *min habehemah* indicates even a non-kosher animal, and *asher hi lachem l'ochlah* indicates a kosher one. But this does not yet account for the *chayah*.

### Inclusion Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַמְתִּי לוֹ: ״וְכִי יָמוּת מִן הַבְּהֵמָה״ – זוֹ בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה, ״אֲשֶׁר הִיא לָכֶם לְאׇכְלָה״ – זוֹ בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה, לָמַדְנוּ חַיָּה בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה, וּבְהֵמָה בִּכְלַל חַיָּה. חַיָּה טְהוֹרָה בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה, חַיָּה טְמֵאָה בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **I said to him** that Rabbi Yishmael derives all these *halakhot* from the verse: "And when a domesticated animal dies, of those that you eat, one who touches its carcass shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 11:39), as follows: **"And when a domesticated animal [*habehema*] dies," this** is referring to **a non-kosher animal; "of those that you eat," this** is referring to **a kosher animal.** And **we learned** that **a *ḥayya* is included** in the category of **a *behema*,** i.e., the term *behema* can also refer collectively to both domesticated and undomesticated animals. **And** likewise, **a *behema* is included** in the category of **a *ḥayya*. Furthermore, a kosher *ḥayya* is included** when the Torah makes reference to **a kosher *behema*,** and **a non-kosher *ḥayya* is included** when the Torah makes reference to **a non-kosher *behema*.**

The key principle Rabbi Yishmael employs is: *chayah bichlal behemah u'vehemah bichlal chayah*—wherever the *torah* speaks of a *behemah*, this includes a *chayah*, and wherever it speaks of a *chayah*, this includes a *behemah*, unless the text explicitly draws a distinction between them. This is not a *drasha* in the narrow sense of finding a hidden inclusion; it is a broader hermeneutic principle about how biblical language operates. The *torah* uses these terms fluidly, and absent a clear distinction, one implies the other.

### Application to Categories

The practical application follows directly: *chayah tehorah bichlal behemah tehorah*—a kosher *chayah* is included in the category of a kosher *behemah*. *chayah tme'ah bichlal behemah tme'ah*—a non-kosher *chayah* is included in a non-kosher *behemah*. And conversely, *behemah tme'ah bichlal chayah tme'ah* and *behemah tehorah bichlal chayah tehorah*—the relationship runs in both directions. Thus, whenever the *torah* records the *tumah* of a *behemah*'s corpse, the *chayah* is included, and vice versa. The apparent gap—the missing source for the *tumah* of a dead kosher *chayah*—is no gap at all; it is covered by the explicit *tumah* of the kosher *behemah*, since the two categories are interchangeable unless otherwise specified.

This approach reflects Rabbi Yishmael's general orientation toward a more fluid, contextual reading of biblical language, as opposed to a hyper-precise parsing of each word. The Torah says *behemah*; it means any animal. It says *chayah*; it also means any animal. The technical distinction between domesticated and non-domesticated exists, but it applies only when the context demands it.

## Ben Azzai's Regret

### Lament & Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַלָּשׁוֹן הַזֶּה אָמַר לִי: חֲבָל עַל בֶּן עַזַּאי שֶׁלֹּא שִׁימֵּשׁ אֶת רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** Accordingly, although the verse here is referring to a *behema*, it is understood to be referring collectively to both a *behema* and a *ḥayya*, and teaches that the carcasses of both types impart impurity. **And** upon hearing this, ben Azzai **said to me in these words: Woe [*ḥaval*] unto ben Azzai, who did not serve Rabbi Yishmael.**

The *braita* closes on a poignant note. Ben Azzai, having heard Rabbi Yonatan transmit Rabbi Yishmael's approach, exclaims about himself: *chaval al Ben Azzai shelo shimash et Rabbi Yishmael*—woe to Ben Azzai, that he never had the opportunity to learn directly from Rabbi Yishmael. He regrets that his entire formation was within the school of Rabbi Akiva, which led him toward a different hermeneutic—one that Ben Azzai now recognizes as potentially too limiting. Rabbi Yishmael's approach, with its principle of *chayah bichlal behemah* and its comfort with non-technical usage of biblical terminology, offered an answer that was both more elegant and more grounded in *pshat* than Ben Azzai's own proposed *drasha*. It is a rare and striking moment of self-criticism from one of the great Tannaim, acknowledging that a different master's path might have served him better.
