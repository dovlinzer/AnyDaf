# Sotah 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dirt Source & Location

### Pre-laid vs. Fresh Dirt

The daf opens with a question about the source of the dirt used in the *sotah* ritual. The verse states *asher yihyeh be-karka ha-mishkan* — the dirt that will be on the ground of the *mishkan* — which raises the question of whether the *kohen* must actually dig the dirt from the ground of the *mishkan*, or whether it suffices to bring dirt in and pre-lay it there. As we saw at the close of the previous daf, the possibility of pre-laying dirt — placing it beneath the floor tiles rather than digging it afresh — is on the table.

### Two Tannaitic Positions

Two tannaitic positions emerge from the *braisa* at the top of the daf. Issi ben Menachem holds that *be-karka ha-mishkan* establishes a requirement that the dirt be placed on the ground — that is, the dirt must rest on the floor of the *mishkan* — but does not require it to be dug from there. Issi ben Yehudah takes a broader interpretive step: he understands *be-karka ha-mishkan* not as a procedural requirement about the dirt's placement, but as a geographic marker whose function is to exclude what might otherwise have been excluded, thereby teaching that the *sotah* ritual is valid not only in the *mishkan* itself.

### Geographic Scope

According to one version of Issi ben Yehudah's position, the verse extends the validity of the ritual to Shilo, Nov, and Givon — the intermediate sites of the *mishkan* before the permanent Temple was built. Rashi is uncomfortable with this extension and crosses it out, since the *sotah* procedure resembles the offering of an individual — a *korban yachid* — and there is a question whether such offerings could be brought at Nov and Givon, which had the status of a *bamah* and permitted individual altars. Tosafot, however, defends the inclusion, explaining that this reflects the view that a *korban yachid* could in fact be brought at Nov and Givon. In any case, the verse also extends the ritual to the *beit olamim* — the permanent *beit hamikdash*.

### Three Interpretive Models

Returning briefly to the discussion from the previous daf, it is worth noting that the *gemara*'s treatment of the *sotah* ritual reflects three distinct conceptual frameworks. The plain sense of the *pesukim* frames the ritual as a mechanism for determining guilt or innocence. The *mishnah*'s emphasis, by contrast, presumes guilt and frames the ritual as punishment. A third lens — and the one particularly relevant here — is that of purification: the *sotah* brings a *korban*, she stands in the section of the Temple where *teme'im* stood (at the Sha'ar Nicanor, the place where those afflicted with *tzara'at* and women after childbirth would stand), and she undergoes a rite at the *beit hamikdash*. This lens of *tumah* and purification runs through the discussion in ways that are not immediately obvious from the text, and the *gemara* itself draws on the *sotah*'s category of *tumah* as a precedent for laws of *safek tumah* in entirely unrelated contexts — such as the rules of *safek tumah be-reshus ha-rabim* and *safek tumah be-reshus ha-yachid*, which are derived from the *sotah*'s act of secluding herself in a *reshus ha-yachid*.

### Beit HaMikdash Inclusion

One might ask: why does the *torah* need a verse to include the *beit hamikdash*? Is the permanent Temple not always presumed to be included wherever the *torah* says *mishkan*? Tosafot addresses this directly and offers an illuminating answer: the difficulty is that the verse says *be-karka ha-mishkan* — the ground of the *mishkan* — and the *beit hamikdash*, unlike the *mishkan*, has a paved floor. One might therefore have thought that the *beit hamikdash* is specifically excluded, since the verse emphasizes *karka* — ground — and the Temple's interior is covered with stone flooring. The special inclusion is therefore necessary to override that potential exclusion.

## Ashes vs. Dirt Question

### No Dirt Scenario

The *gemara* now raises a new question: *ein sham afar, mahu sheyiten efer?* — what if there is no dirt available? May ashes be used in its place? This question arises within the context already established: we have concluded that the dirt need not be dug from the ground; at most, it must be placed upon the ground. Given that latitude, might ashes — which the *torah* itself sometimes calls *afar* — also qualify?

### Beit Shammai Position

*Aliba de-Beit Shammai lo tivaei lach* — according to Beit Shammai, there is no question at all. Beit Shammai holds *lo matzinu efer she-karui afar*: ashes are never called *afar*. The two words — *afar* (dirt) and *efer* (ashes) — are categorically distinct, and no verse establishes that *efer* falls under the rubric of *afar*. The question therefore simply does not arise for Beit Shammai.

### Beit Hillel Position

The question becomes pressing according to Beit Hillel, who holds *matzinu efer she-karui afar* — there is indeed a case where ashes are called *afar*. The source is the verse *ve-lakach me-afar sereifat ha-parah* — "he shall take from the *afar* of the burning of the *parah*" (Numbers 19:17). The *parah adumah* leaves ashes, yet the verse calls them *afar*. Beit Hillel reads this as evidence that the word *afar* can encompass *efer*. Beit Shammai responds that *afar sereifa* is a compound term meaning "the dust of burning," which is a way of referring to ashes — but the word *afar* standing alone does not mean ashes.

### Blood-Covering Halacha

The practical application of this dispute extends to *kisui ha-dam* — the commandment to cover blood after *shechita*, as the verse says *ve-kissehu be-afar* (Leviticus 17:13). May ashes be used? Beit Shammai says no, since ashes are not called *afar*. Beit Hillel says yes, since *afar sereifat ha-parah* demonstrates that *efer* can be called *afar*. According to Beit Hillel, one may fulfill *kisui ha-dam* with ashes.

### Dirt Type Requirement

The *gemara* now asks whether the reasoning that allows ashes for *kisui ha-dam* according to Beit Hillel would also permit them for the *sotah*. One might argue that it should, since in both cases the operative word is *afar*. But the *gemara* raises a counterargument: *af al gav de-ikarei afar, hacha be-karka ha-mishkan ktiv* — even granting that *efer* can be called *afar*, the *sotah* verse specifically says *be-karka ha-mishkan*, which implies a connection to the nature of *karka*. Perhaps the contextual emphasis on *karka* restricts the *afar* here to something that genuinely resembles ground, and ashes — however legitimately called *afar* in another context — do not qualify.

### Two Readings

Rashi explains *be-karka ha-mishkan* to mean that even if you may take dirt out of a basket without digging, it must still be the type of substance that *could* be the *karka ha-mishkan* — it must genuinely resemble ground. Alternatively, one may read *be-karka ha-mishkan* as conveying the territorial teaching of Issi ben Yehudah and Issi ben Menachem — not a qualitative requirement about the nature of the dirt itself — in which case the word *afar* might remain open enough to include *efer* according to Beit Hillel. Whether *be-karka ha-mishkan* is a qualitative restriction on the substance or merely a territorial and procedural teaching is thus the crux of the question left open at this point.

## Halacha Okevet HaMikra

### Rabbi Yishmael's Princip…

The *gemara* now pivots to a celebrated ruling: *ta shema, de-amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Yishmael* — come and hear, as Rabbi Yochanan says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael. *Be-shelosha mekomot halacha okevet mikra* — in three places the *halacha* undermines the plain sense of scripture. The word *okevet* — which may be translated as "undermines" or "overrides" — captures the idea that in these three cases, if one were to follow the simple reading of the verse, one would arrive at a *halacha* different from what the tradition mandates. Rashi explains that *halacha* here means *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — an ancient received tradition — and it is this received tradition that stands in apparent tension with the plain sense of the text.

### Kissui HaDam

The first case is *kisui ha-dam*. The *torah* says *be-afar* — with dirt — yet the *halacha* is *be-chol davar* — with anything that can grow seeds. This is not a limitless expansion; one cannot, for example, cover blood with a book. But the tradition permits substances that resemble dirt in its seed-growing capacity, such as ground pottery shards, even though they are not literally *afar*.

### Nazir Hair Cutting

The second case as quoted in the *gemara* is the *nazir*. The verse states *ta'ar lo ya'avor al rosho* — a razor shall not pass over his head — yet the *halacha* extends the prohibition to any instrument that removes hair. The *nazir* is forbidden from cutting his hair with anything, not only a razor.

### Get Writing Surface

The third case is the *get*. The verse uses the word *sefer* — a document or book — yet the *halacha* permits writing a *get* on any surface that is detached from the ground: a leaf, an animal's horn, the arm of a slave. The received tradition does not confine the *get* to parchment or any particular material.

### Rashi's Girsa Correction

Rashi takes issue with the second case as presented — the *ta'ar* of the *nazir* — and argues that this is actually not a case of the *halacha* undermining scripture but of the *halacha* expanding and intensifying scripture. The *torah* says a razor is forbidden; the *halacha* adds that all instruments are forbidden. This is being more *machmir* than the verse, not contradicting it. Rashi acknowledges a somewhat forced explanation — that it "undermines" in the sense that lashes would be administered for violations involving other instruments even though the verse does not explicitly require them, thereby raising the specter of lashing an innocent person — but Rashi finds this unsatisfying.

Rashi therefore prefers a different *girsa* (textual reading), citing the *Yerushalmi* in Tractate Kiddushin, where the three cases are: *sefer* — the *get*; *afar* — *kisui ha-dam*; and *martzea* — the awl used to pierce the ear of the slave who refuses his freedom (Exodus 21:6). As quoted by Rashi: *be-masechet Kiddushin Yerushalmi matzati mishna zu* — the three cases are the *sefer*, the *afar*, and the *martzea*. The *halacha* by the slave's ear is that any piercing instrument works — *afilu sul ve-sira* — even a thorn or similar implement. Rashi adds: *vehu nireh be-einai me'od de-hach de-ta'ar tosefet hi* — it seems very clear to him that the *ta'ar* version is an addition that does not belong, since it adds to rather than contradicts the verse.

### Why These Three Cases

Rashi raises a deeper question: given that *midrash halacha* regularly reinterprets verses — sometimes expanding them, sometimes narrowing them — what makes these three cases distinctive enough to be labeled *halacha okevet mikra*? The answer Rashi provides is that in all the other cases where the *halacha* diverges from the plain sense, there is a *drasha* — a hermeneutical derivation embedded in the verse — that allows one to say the alternate reading is present within the *mikra* itself. One may disagree about whether a given *drasha* is compelling, but there is a method that links the *halacha* back to the text.

Rabbi Yishmael, however, holds that for these three cases, no such *drasha* exists. Other *tanna'im* do offer *drashot* for these laws — they find ways to derive *be-chol davar* for *kisui ha-dam* from the verse, ways to read the *sefer* of *get* as including any surface, and so on. But Rabbi Yishmael does not accept those *drashot*. As Rashi writes: *de-Rabbi Yishmael lo mashme' lei kra'i de-vechasav lah de-marbina minei le-rabot kol davar* — Rabbi Yishmael does not read the verse "and he shall write for her" as yielding an expansion to include all surfaces. *Ella halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai samchina* — the only basis is the received tradition. And any verse that appears to support it is merely an *asmachta*. *Hilkach, she'ar midrashim... lo chashiv lei Rabbi Yishmael ke-halacha okevet* — all other *drashot* throughout the *torah* do not constitute *halacha okevet* in Rabbi Yishmael's view, because in those cases there is a legitimate hermeneutical derivation that accounts for the *halacha*. But these three he cannot derive. The tradition stands and the simple sense of the verse is genuinely overridden. Rabbi Yishmael is prepared to live with that disparity.

## Midrash HaLacha Origins

### Tradition vs. Derivation

This discussion opens a broader question that is genuinely debated among *rishonim* and modern scholars alike: in *midrash halacha*, did *Chazal* begin with a received tradition about what the *halacha* is and then return to the *pesukim* to locate support for it, or did they begin with the text and derive the *halacha* through hermeneutics? The answer is almost certainly not uniform — sometimes one approach, sometimes the other — and the evidence internal to the *gemara* often points toward which method was operative in a given case.

### Forced Drasha Evidence

When a *drasha* appears highly forced — when one wonders whether any independent reader of the verse would have arrived at this interpretation — it is a strong signal that *Chazal* started with the practice and worked backward. The classic example is the *gemara*'s discussion of *pikuach nefesh docheh Shabbos*: it is manifest that the *halacha* was known and established, and the *gemara* is proposing various possible pesukim to serve as its textual anchor. When the *drasha* seems genuinely embedded in the verse, one may more plausibly conclude they started with the text and reasoned forward.

### Rabbi Yishmael's Stance

What is remarkable about Rabbi Yishmael's position is that it goes even further. Most situations where *Chazal* start with the tradition can still, in the end, produce a *drasha* — perhaps forced, perhaps creative, but one that anchors the *halacha* in the *pasuk* retroactively. Rabbi Yishmael is saying something more striking: even after the fact, even knowing the *halacha*, he cannot in good conscience say that the *pasuk* contains it. It is pure tradition, and the verses remain in apparent contradiction to it. This is why the term *okevet* — undermining — is apt for Rabbi Yishmael in a way it would not be for others: he is the one for whom the *halacha* genuinely overrides and does not resolve with the *mikra*. Other *tanna'im* have *drashot* that resolve the tension; for Rabbi Yishmael, the tension remains.

## Ashes Omission Explained

### Why Not Ashes?

The *gemara* now returns to the question of ashes in the *sotah* ritual and poses a pointed challenge. If ashes can in fact serve as *afar* for the *sotah* according to Beit Hillel — and if this represents a case where the *halacha* expands beyond the plain sense of *afar* — why is this not on Rabbi Yishmael's list of three places where *halacha okevet mikra*? This is, as Tosafot notes, a genuinely puzzling question for multiple reasons. First, Rabbi Yishmael presumably holds like Beit Shammai that *efer* is never called *afar*, and therefore there would be no expansion at all — no *okevet* to record. Second, if one follows Beit Hillel and *efer* is legitimately called *afar*, then the *halacha* is actually consistent with the *mikra*, not overriding it — and so again there is nothing to list. Tosafot struggles with the question, yet the *gemara* poses it and proceeds to answer it.

### Abbreviated List Theory

The *gemara*'s first answer is *tana ve-shayer* — the list is incomplete; items were left off. The list says "three" but is not exhaustive; there are additional cases that also exemplify *halacha okevet mikra* beyond the three recorded. Ashes for *sotah* may be one such omission.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *u-mai shayer de-hai shayer?* — if you claim the list is abbreviated, there must be more than one omitted item, because one would never compile a list and leave off only a single case. If you're going to say things were left off, show us another omission as well.

The *gemara* proposes: *shayer metzora* — the shaving of the *metzora* was also left off. The relevant verse, from the seventh day of the *metzora*'s purification process, reads: *ve-haya ba-yom ha-shvi'i yegalach et kol se'aro* — and on the seventh day he shall shave all his hair. The verse then specifies: *et rosho ve-et zakano ve-et givot einav* — his head, his beard, and his eyebrows. It then returns to a general formulation: *ve-et kol se'aro yigalach* — all his hair shall he shave. This is a classic structure of *klal u-frat u-klal* — general, specific, and general — from which we derive *ein atah dan ella ke-ein ha-frat*: we include anything similar to what was specified. The *frat* describes places where hair is gathered and visible (*mekom kinus se'ar ve-nireh*), so we include the pubic hair (*se'ar ha-raglayim*, as Rabbi explains), while excluding underarm hair (*beit ha-shachi*) — which, even when naked, is not visible unless one raises one's arms — as well as individual stray hairs elsewhere on the body.

The plain sense of the verse, following this *klal u-frat u-klal*, would limit the shaving on day seven to the gathered, visible areas. Yet the *halacha* — *megalcho ke-dala'at* — requires shaving the *metzora* completely bald, like a gourd. This appears to be another case of *halacha okevet mikra*.

### Addition vs. Contradicti…

But the *gemara* rejects this reasoning. *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* objects: *ki ka chashiv halacha okevet mikra* — when Rabbi Yishmael lists cases of *halacha okevet*, he means cases where the *halacha* genuinely contradicts and displaces the plain sense of the verse. *Ha okevet midirabanan hi* — this case undermines not the plain sense of the *mikra* but the *drasha* of the *mikra*. The *mikra* itself says *kol se'aro* — all his hair — which on its face sounds comprehensive. It is only because of the *klal u-frat u-klal* derivation that we would narrow it to gathered areas. The *halacha* of shaving like a gourd contradicts the narrowed *drasha*, not the plain verse. This is therefore not the same category as the three cases Rabbi Yishmael listed.

Rav Pappa offers a second reason the *metzora* case does not belong on the list: *ki chashiv halacha okevet ve-okaret* — the three listed cases involve situations where following the *halacha* means you would *not* be fulfilling the *mikra*'s instruction. Writing a *get* on a leaf means you are not writing it on a *sefer*; covering blood with pottery shards means you have not covered it with *afar* in the simple sense. The *halacha* displaces what the verse required. But by the *metzora*, shaving the entire body *includes* the areas the verse specified — one is doing the bunches of hair *and more*. The *halacha* is *okevet u-mosefet* — it overrides but adds — and one who shaves the *metzora* completely has certainly also shaved his head, beard, and eyebrows. It does not undermine the *mikra*; it fulfills it and exceeds it.

### Metzora Derasha Methods

The *gemara* records the full *drasha* for both hermeneutical approaches. According to *klal u-frat u-klal* (Rabbi Yishmael's method): *klal* — *ve-et kol se'aro*; *frat* — *et rosho ve-et zakano ve-et givot einav*; *klal* — *ve-et kol se'aro yigalach*. *Ain atah dan ella ke-ein ha-frat*: we include *mekom kinus se'ar ve-nireh* — places where hair gathers and is visible. Rabbi rules that this includes *se'ar ha-raglayim* — the pubic hair — because it is visible when unclothed. It excludes *beit ha-shachi* (underarm hair) and individual strands across the body.

According to *ribbuy u-miut u-ribbuy* (Rabbi Akiva's method): *riba* — *ve-et kol se'aro* (expansion); *mi'et* — the listed body parts (limitation); *riba* — *ve-et kol se'aro yigalach* (expansion again). When you have *ribbuy, miut, ve-ribbuy*, you include virtually everything, excluding only the most categorically different thing. *Ma mi'et* — what is excluded? *Mi'et se'ar she-betoch ha-chotem* — the nose hair. Everything else is included, and one shaves him like a gourd.

### Rabbi Akiva vs. Rabbi Yi…

This analysis generates Rav Ashi's third and cleanest answer to why the *metzora* shaving does not belong on Rabbi Yishmael's list. *Ha matnita mani? Rabbi Yishmael hi* — the *braisa* listing the three cases of *halacha okevet* is the position of Rabbi Yishmael himself. *De-darash klal u-frat* — Rabbi Yishmael applies *klal u-frat u-klal*, which yields on day seven only the shaving of bunches of hair. According to Rabbi Yishmael, that is precisely what the *halacha* of day seven *is*: you do not shave the *metzora* like a gourd on day seven. The position that requires shaving like a gourd on day seven belongs to Rabbi Akiva, *de-darash ribbuy u-miut* — who reads *ribbuy, miut, ve-ribbuy* and therefore includes the whole body. This is not *halacha* against *drasha*; it is simply Rabbi Yishmael's *drasha* against Rabbi Akiva's *drasha*. There is no *okevet* at all from Rabbi Yishmael's perspective.

We thus have three separate reasons why the *metzora* shaving is not on Rabbi Yishmael's list: first, it contradicts only the *drasha* and not the plain verse; second, it adds to rather than displaces the *mikra*'s requirement; and third, it is not Rabbi Yishmael's *halacha* in the first place.

### Partial Resolution

With all three answers in place, we return to the original question: why are ashes not on the list? No fully satisfying resolution emerges. The *gemara* offers the partial answer of Rav Huna bar Ashi quoting Rav: *ein sham afar, mevi rakvuvit yerek* — if there is no dirt, one may bring decomposed vegetable matter and use it to sanctify the water. This is not ashes; it is something that grew from the ground and naturally decomposed, and it retains enough of the character of *karka* to be considered a form of *afar*.

But the *gemara* immediately qualifies: *ve-lo hi* — that is not a true equivalence. *Rikvuvit yerek hu de-havei afar* — decomposed vegetable can be considered *afar*. *Efer lo havei afar* — ashes cannot. So even the partial resolution expands *afar* only modestly — to naturally decomposed organic matter — and explicitly stops short of including ashes. The question of ashes remains without a clean resolution, and the question of why they do not appear on Rabbi Yishmael's list remains similarly open.

## Visibility Requirement

### Three Visible Items

The *gemara* now introduces a *braisa*: *shelosha tzerichim she-yeira'u* — three things must be visible. These are: the *afar* of the *sotah* when placed in the water; the *efer parah* — ashes of the *parah adumah* — when placed in the water; and the *rok yevamah* — the spit of the *yevamah* in the *chalitzah* ceremony. The verse by *chalitzah* states *ve-yarkah be-fanav* and *le-einei ha-zekenim*, requiring that the *zekenim* actually see the spit. In each of these cases, the relevant substance must be visible when combined with the water or present before the observers.

### Bird Blood Addition

*Mishum Rabbi Yishmael amru* — in the name of Rabbi Yishmael, they added a fourth: *af dam tzippor* — the blood of the bird used in the *metzora*'s purification. When the bird is slaughtered and its blood falls into the water, that blood must be visible.

### Bird Blood Source

The *gemara* provides the derivation. The verse states: *ve-taval otam be-dam ha-tzippor ha-shechutah u-va-mayim ha-chayim* — he shall dip them in the blood of the slaughtered bird and in the living water. A *braisa* is then cited: one might have thought you dip only in the blood, without water — therefore the verse says *ba-mayim* (in the water). One might have thought you dip only in the water, without blood — therefore the verse says *be-dam* (in the blood). *Ha keitzad?* — how is this resolved? *Mevi mayim she-dam tzippor nikar bahem* — you bring water in which the blood of the bird will remain recognizable. The blood and the water are combined, but the blood must remain *nikar* — recognizable, visible — within the mixture. Rabbi Yishmael accordingly includes this among the things that must be seen.

## Blood-Water Mixture

### Blood Prominence

Rabbi Yishmael's requirement that the blood be *nikar* in the water is not merely a technical formality. The verse says *be-dam ha-tzippor ha-shechutah* — in the blood of the slaughtered bird — rather than simply "in them," and Rabbi Yishmael reads this as establishing that the blood must retain its essential character. You are fundamentally dipping into *blood* — water is added to it, but blood is what the dipping is about.

### Revi'it Measure

The practical consequence of this position is a limit on the amount of water: *revi'it*. If more than a *revi'it* of water is added, the blood will be so diffused that it can no longer be said to be *nikar*. At a *revi'it* or less, the blood remains visibly and substantively present.

### Mamshut Principle

Tosafot deepens this analysis. The word *nikar* does not mean merely that one can detect that blood was once present, the way a forensic trace might indicate a prior substance. Tosafot explains that the blood must retain its *mamashus* — its essential, physical substantiality. The question is framed this way: are we looking at blood that has had some water added, or at water that has had some blood added? Rabbi Yishmael requires the former. There must be enough blood relative to the water that the mixture can still be characterized as *dam* — blood — into which one is dipping. The verse *ve-taval otam be-dam ha-tzippor* obligates one to dip into *blood*, and therefore the *mamashus* of the blood must remain. This is why the water is limited to a *revi'it*: at that volume, one can still say the blood is present as blood, not merely as a colorant dissolved into water. This point parallels broader questions of *bittul* — at what point has a substance ceased to be present in its essential form and become merely a trace?

### Simple Mixture View

The Rabbis who do not share Rabbi Yishmael's position — and who accordingly did not include the bird's blood in their list of items requiring visibility — understand the verse differently. *Ha-hu le-gufei* — the separate mention of blood and water is not to establish that the blood must predominate; rather, it teaches the simple fact that blood and water must be combined. The instruction *be-dam u-va-mayim* means: dip in a mixture of both. The blood need not retain its *mamashus*; what matters is that both components are present.

Rabbi Yishmael objects: if the verse only intended to say "combine them," it should have written *ve-taval bahem* — dip in them — rather than specifying *be-dam* and then *ba-mayim* separately. The separate specification of *be-dam* teaches that the blood must be prominent — *le-nikar*. The Rabbis counter: if the verse had only written *ve-taval bahem* — in them — one might have thought the dipping in blood and the dipping in water are done separately in sequence, first one and then the other. The explicit mention of both together teaches that they must be combined. Rabbi Yishmael responds that this teaching — the need to combine them — is already derived from a different verse: the verse *ve-shachatu et ha-tzippor ha-achat el keli cheres al mayim chayim* — the bird is slaughtered over a vessel of water, so the blood naturally falls into the water, producing an automatic combination. The Rabbis reply that one could technically comply with *shachatu al mayim chayim* while preventing the blood from mixing — for instance, by slaughtering near the vessel and diverting the blood into a separate container. Therefore a verse is still needed to teach the requirement of combination.

## Bird Size Problem

### Extreme Cases

Rav Yirmiya poses a challenge to Rabbi Zeira targeting the *revi'it* measure. *Gedola u-madachat et ha-mayim* — consider a very large bird: its blood will be so abundant that a *revi'it* of water will be entirely overwhelmed, with no meaningful presence of water remaining. *Ktana ve-nimchit mipnei ha-mayim* — consider a very small bird: its blood will be so scant that even a *revi'it* of water will overwhelm it, and no meaningful presence of blood will remain. How can a single fixed measure of a *revi'it* work across the full range of bird sizes?

### Sparrow Standard

Rabbi Zeira's response carries an implicit rebuke: *lav amina lach lo teippek nafshach levar me-hilchata* — haven't I told you not to try to outsmart the *halacha* in this way? The *revi'it* measure was calibrated specifically for the *tzippor dror* — the sparrow — which is the standard bird used for this ritual.

### Normal Range Solution

Within the range of sparrows, Rabbi Zeira explains, the measure holds: *ein lach gedola she-madachat et ha-mayim* — there is no sparrow large enough that its blood will overwhelm a *revi'it* of water — *ve-ein lach ktana she-nimchit mipnei ha-mayim* — and there is no sparrow small enough that its blood will be overwhelmed by a *revi'it* of water. The *shiur* of a *revi'it* was established with the normal range of sparrows in mind, and within that range it is an accurate and workable measure. Extreme hypotheticals about oversized or undersized birds are not the target audience of the *halacha*.

## Mixing Order Debate

### Dirt-First Rule

The *gemara* returns to the *sotah* ritual and the question of sequence. A *braisa* states: *hikdim afar le-mayim — pasul* — if the dirt was placed in the vessel before the water, the mixture is invalid. The verse *ve-natan el ha-mayim* — "and he shall put into the water" — implies that the water must already be present when the dirt is added.

### Rabbi Shimon's View

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *Rabbi Shimon machshir* — Rabbi Shimon validates the mixture even if the dirt was placed first. His reasoning is derived from a comparison to the *parah adumah*, where, as we will see, both orderings have textual support, and he reads the two rituals as linked by a *gezeira shava*.

### Gezeira Shava

The connection between the two rituals rests on the shared word *afar*. The *sotah* verse speaks of *afar*, and the *parah adumah* verse speaks of *afar sereifat ha-parah* — also called *afar* by the verse, as Beit Hillel established. Rabbi Shimon draws a *gezeira shava* between the two uses of *afar* to allow each ritual to teach something about the other.

### Reverse Direction

What does the *sotah* teach about the *parah adumah*? That the substance must be visible on top of the water — *afar al gabei mayim*. The *sotah* ritual implies that the dirt or its equivalent rests visibly upon the water's surface. Rabbi Shimon applies this teaching to the *parah adumah* as well: when the ashes are placed in the water, they too must be *nikar* — visible — on the water.

### Order Flexibility

And in the reverse direction: what does the *parah adumah* teach the *sotah*? That the order of mixing can be reversed. Since the *parah adumah* has textual support for both orderings — as the *gemara* now explains — the *gezeira shava* extends that flexibility to the *sotah* as well. Even if the dirt is placed before the water in the *sotah* ritual, Rabbi Shimon holds it remains valid.

## Red Heifer Verses

### Alav - Ashes First

The *gemara* now examines the two verses in the *parah adumah* passage that seem to point in opposite directions regarding the order of mixing. The first verse is *ve-natan alav mayim chayim el keli* — "he shall put upon it living water into a vessel." The word *alav* — "upon it" — implies that the ashes are already present when the water is added, meaning the ashes go first.

### El Keli - Water First

The second reading emerges from the phrase *mayim chayim el keli* — "living water into a vessel." If one reads this phrase as indicating that the living water is placed into the vessel at the outset, the implication is that the water is in the vessel first, and the ashes are added afterward. Thus the same verse seems to support both sequences: *alav* implies ashes first; *el keli* implies water first.

### Flexible Order

Rabbi Shimon reconciles the two: *ratza zeh noten, ratza zeh noten* — one may do it either way. The two formulations in the verse are not contradictory but complementary, each validating a different order. By the *parah adumah* both sequences are valid, and through the *gezeira shava*, both sequences become valid for the *sotah* as well.

## Rabbis' Order Rule

### Machshir on Top

The Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Shimon in both cases. Their principle is: *mimah matzinu be-chol makom machshir le-mala* — we find universally that the *machshir* — the potent, active ingredient — goes on top. The water is the base solution, a passive medium. The special substance — whether dirt, ashes, or blood — is the *machshir*, the component that actually activates the ritual. And that component must be placed second, on top of the water already in the vessel.

### Reinterpretation

Given this principle, the Rabbis reinterpret *ve-natan alav* to mean something other than "the ashes are placed first." Once the water is already in the vessel, the ashes are added, and then *alav* — upon it — the water is stirred over and mixed through the ashes. The word *alav* describes not an initial placement of ashes but a subsequent folding of the water over the ashes after they have been added. The *machshir* (the ashes) still goes second — after the water — and then the mixing is described by *alav*.

### Three Examples

The Rabbis find support for water-first in three parallel cases. By *sotah*, the plain sense of the verse requires water first: *ve-natan el ha-mayim* means you put the dirt into the water already present. By the *parah adumah*, the Rabbis read *mayim chayim el keli* as mandating water first in the vessel. And by the blood of the *metzora*'s bird, *al mayim chayim* — upon living water — similarly implies the water is there first and the blood descends into it. In all three, the water precedes the special substance.

### Water as Base

The underlying logic is consistent: the non-water element — dirt, ashes, blood — is in each case the special, ritual-defining component, the *machshir*. The water is simply the medium. The ritual logic therefore demands that the medium be in place first, and the defining ingredient be introduced into it. This ordering is *me'akev* according to the Rabbis — it is not merely a preferred sequence but a requirement, and reversing it invalidates the mixture. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, drawing on the *gezeira shava* and the flexibility encoded in both verses of the *parah adumah*, holds that either order is valid in both rituals.
