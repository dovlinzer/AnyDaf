# Yevamot 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Woman Remarries

### Perek Overview

Daf 90 falls within the *perek* of *Ha'isha Raba*, one of the more challenging *perakim* in Yevamot — challenging both because the logic of the first *mishna* is genuinely difficult to follow, and because the *sugyot* themselves are dense and complicated. The *perek* introduces the sweeping idea of *yesh koach l'chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah*, as well as the whole framework of believing an *ed echad*. It is also, notably, one of the *perakim* of the *masechet* most intensively studied in the *yeshivot*.

### Mishna's Logic

The *halacha* in the *mishna* is as follows: a woman receives a report that her husband has died and she proceeds to remarry. If she does so *birshut beit din* — with the permission of the court — and the husband subsequently reappears alive, she suffers a cascade of negative consequences: she must receive a *get* from both husbands, any child born is a *mamzer*, and she forfeits all of her financial rights. However, if she remarried *shelo birshut beit din* — without court authorization, operating unilaterally — she simply returns to her first husband without suffering any of these consequences.

This boggles the mind on two fronts. First, why the negative consequences at all? And second, why is her situation worse when she acted *birshut* — with official backing — than when she acted *shelo birshut*, which would seem to be the more reckless course?

### One vs. Two Witnesses

The very first line of the *gemara* — spoken in the voice of the *stam*, not a specific *amora* — resolves the distinction by reinterpreting the terms. *Birshut beit din*, it proposes, refers to a case in which she remarried on the basis of a single witness, and it was the *beit din* that granted permission to do so on that thin evidence. *Shelo birshut beit din*, the case without negative consequences, refers to a situation in which there were actually two witnesses — no special *heter* was even necessary, and therefore there was no court involvement. So what initially appears to be a harsher case (*shelo birshut*) is actually grounded in stronger evidence.

### Consequences as Mechanism

This reframing still leaves the underlying question unresolved: even granting that there was only a single witness, why do all these consequences follow? If *beit din* gave her permission, why should she bear such severe penalties?

The *stam* of the *gemara* introduces the principle of *dayka u-mintsva*: we permit a woman to remarry on the strength of a single witness only because we rely on the assumption that she will thoroughly investigate the matter before proceeding. She knows, in other words, that if the husband returns, devastating consequences await her — and it is precisely this knowledge of those consequences that motivates her to investigate carefully. The negative consequences are thus framed not as punishments but as the mechanism that underwrites and legitimates the radical leniency of allowing remarriage on the basis of *ed echad* in the first place.

This explanation is difficult on its own terms. One wonders how a woman stranded in distant *Bavel* is supposed to conduct such an investigation, and indeed some *acharonim* argue that she should therefore be formally warned of the consequences before remarrying — something the *gemara* never actually requires. The explanation strains, but it is the *stam*'s answer.

## Friedman's Reading

### Stripping Stam

The scholarly reading offered by Shamma Friedman of Bar-Ilan University proposes a different approach. His method involves stripping away the *stam* layer of the *gemara* to recover what the *amoraim* themselves understood — a methodology associated with the academic study of Talmud. When one does this, he argues, there is no trace whatsoever among the *amoraim* of the radical reinterpretation that *birshut* means one witness and *shelo birshut* means two witnesses. That reading appears to be a *stam* innovation with no amoraic foundation. The *mishna* should instead be read *k'pshuto*.

### Reinterpreting Terms

On this reading, *birshut* and *shelo birshut* mean precisely what they say: in both cases the woman may have acted on the same quality of evidence (whether one witness or two is not the operative distinction), but in the *birshut* case she brought the evidence before *beit din* and received an official ruling, while in the *shelo birshut* case she acted on her own without seeking a *psak*. The question then remains: why the vastly more severe consequences when she operated through the court?

### Natural Consequences

Friedman's answer is that the consequences are not a *knas* — not a punishment — at all. The *pshat* of the *mishna* is that when she acts *birshut beit din*, the court officially recognizes the second marriage. That is the *chiddush* of *chachamim*: in the name of *mishum agunah akilu bah rabanan*, the rabbis validate a second marriage on grounds that would ordinarily be insufficient. Having done so, if the first husband reappears, the woman's *halachic* status is not that she has committed adultery — she acted with full *beit din* authorization — but rather that she is now, in an unprecedented situation, legally married to two men simultaneously.

### Married to Two

All of the consequences that follow are not punishments but the natural *halachic* fallout of this unprecedented status. She needs a *get* from each husband because she is married to both. She forfeits financial rights from each because the entitlements of one marriage are incompatible with a simultaneous second marriage. *Halacha* has simply never encountered this creature before, and the consequences are the necessary result of working out what it means.

### No Recognition

When she acted *shelo birshut beit din*, by contrast, the second marriage was never officially recognized. She slept with another man believing her husband to be dead, and in that sense she is something of an *anusah* — she did not willfully commit adultery. But since the court never validated the second marriage, it remains legally invisible; she returns to her first husband, and there is nothing to undo. The second man's *kiddushin* was never acknowledged as a marriage in the eyes of *halacha*.

This reading preserves the plain meaning of *birshut* and *shelo birshut*, and renders the consequences comprehensible as logical rather than punitive. It also arguably reflects better on the institution: the rabbis were not designing a punitive apparatus to intimidate women into investigating carefully; they were simply confronting the *halachic* reality generated by their own leniency on behalf of *agunot*.

### Audience Q&A

Several questions arise naturally from Friedman's reading. One might ask: the first marriage was also just two witnesses without court involvement — why should the court's validation of the second marriage create a categorically different status? The answer is that the second marriage requires court permission because a woman cannot simply acquire a second husband while still legally married to a first; it is the *beit din*'s ruling that creates the recognition, not the act of *kiddushin* alone.

A deeper question concerns incentive structures: does Friedman's reading not create a strong incentive to avoid the *beit din* altogether, since operating *shelo birshut* produces none of these consequences? The *mishna* itself addresses this, at least partially, by noting that the woman who acted *birshut beit din* does not bring a *korban* and is not considered to have sinned, whereas the woman who acted unilaterally would still owe a *korban*. And practically, social pressure to have marriages recognized by the court remains powerful — people do not typically wish to enter marriages that society will not acknowledge.

As for the status of a child born in the *shelo birshut* scenario: a child born to the first husband would not be a *mamzer*, since she returns to that marriage; a child born from the unrecognized second union, however, would still be a *mamzer*, since that was an act of adultery, however unintentional.

One might finally ask why, if this is the better reading, the *stam* of the *gemara* did not adopt it. That question is difficult to answer definitively; at some point, reconstruction of editorial motivations becomes speculation. What can be said is that the *stam* consistently pushes toward a stricter, more punitive reading throughout this *perek*, treating the consequences as a *knas*, and reads both the *mishna* and the statements of the *amoraim* through that lens.

## Why Ed Echad?

### Three Approaches

Setting aside the question of how to read the *mishna*, the deeper issue lurking behind the entire discussion is: on what basis do we believe an *ed echad* in this context at all? Torah law requires two witnesses for matters of consequence, yet the entire architecture of this *perek* rests on permitting remarriage on the testimony of one. This is a major question, and the *rishonim* offer three distinct answers.

The first is *yesh koach l'chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah* — the rabbis simply have the power to uproot a Torah rule. This is the position associated with Tosafot, and it helps explain why the *sugya* on *yesh koach* appears here: not merely to address the *mamzer* question raised by the *gemara*, but to justify the entire framework of *ed echad* in this context.

The second approach, associated with Rashi, is *hafka'as kiddushin*: the rabbis have a particular power in the domain of marriage to uproot *kiddushin* retroactively. This is not a sweeping power to override Torah law in general, but a targeted capacity tied to the institution of marriage itself.

The third approach, offered by the Ra'ah (and rejected by Tosafot), is that once there is sufficient circumstantial evidence, it rises to the level of *anan sa'adi* — something so self-evident that it operates on a *d'Oraisa* level without any rabbinic override at all. The combination of *ed echad* and the woman's theoretical capacity to investigate, the Ra'ah argues, creates that threshold. Tosafot rejects this: it is simply not a genuine *anan sa'adi*, and to claim otherwise is to stretch the category beyond recognition.

There is also a *Sifrei* that attempts to anchor *ed echad* directly in a biblical verse: *lo yakum ed echad b'ish l'chol avon u'l'chol chatas* — one witness shall not stand against a man for any sin or transgression. The *Sifrei* reads *l'chol avon u'l'chol chatas* as limiting: one witness does not suffice for those matters, but it does suffice for testimony about a woman's marital status. This would give a biblical basis for the *ed echad* leniency here. But the question of why we believe *ed echad* remains genuinely difficult, and *yesh koach la'akor davar* is one serious answer — all the more striking given that the very *sugya* we are in the middle of cannot definitively establish that principle.

## Limiting the Mishna

### Rav's Position

Against the background of the *stam*'s sweeping and stringent reading of the *mishna*, the *amoraim* themselves attempt to limit its scope. Rav's position is perhaps the most radical: where there were two witnesses who testified to the husband's death, even if the husband subsequently reappears alive, she may remain married to the second husband. The *gemara* cannot straightforwardly accept this, since the husband is standing there in front of everyone, so it qualifies Rav's statement by framing it as a case of competing testimonies — two witnesses say he is dead, others say this man before them is the husband — and adjudicates between them.

But the Yerushalmi, as Tosafot notes, reads Rav's statement more simply. It accepts the apparent legal fiction: two witnesses have testified he is dead, and even if the man appears before us and every person in town recognizes him, we create a legal fiction and say he is not the man. Two witnesses are as authoritative as a hundred, and patently obvious reality cannot simply override their testimony. The implication is stunning: once she has remarried on the basis of two witnesses, even if he returns, she stays with the second husband, and none of the *mishna*'s negative consequences apply.

### Shmuel's Position

Shmuel offers a different limiting principle: when she herself contradicts the witnesses — when she denies that this man is her husband, or makes some claim against the testimony — she also does not suffer the full consequences of the *mishna*. The *gemara* reads this in a similar frame to Rav's position, involving competing testimonies about identity. But read straightforwardly, Shmuel may simply be saying that when the woman herself disputes the matter, whether by claiming the returnee is not her husband or in some broader sense denying the situation, her position carries weight sufficient to change the outcome. Together, Rav and Shmuel chart two paths for limiting the *mishna*'s harsh sweep.

### Stam Rejects Limits

The *stam* of the *gemara*, however, consistently resists these limiting moves. Rather than accepting Rav's or Shmuel's positions as valid limitations on the *mishna*, the *stam* repeatedly narrows the scope of those amoraic statements themselves, so that the *mishna*'s consequences remain applicable in nearly all cases. The dynamic is a recurring one throughout this *perek*: the *amoraim* try to limit the *mishna*, and the *stam* pushes back, insisting on a broader, more stringent application of the *mishna*'s rule. The *stam* thus consistently presents itself as more *machmir* than the *amoraim*, reading the case as a *knas* and treating the sweep of the consequences as essentially unqualified.

## Why Ed Echad? (II)

### Tosafot's View

Returning to the question of why *ed echad* is believed here, Tosafot's approach, noted above as *yesh koach la'akor davar min ha-Torah*, carries an important nuance. Tosafot does not simply say the rabbis can uproot Torah law whenever they wish. Rather, Tosafot's position is that when an innovation has some logical support behind it — when it *appears* to operate within Torah categories even if technically it does not — then the rabbis may effectuate it without it constituting a genuine *akira d'Oraisa*.

The paradigm case is the discussion elsewhere in this *perek* about a *kohen* being *metamei* to a minor wife (*ketana*) whose *kiddushin* is rabbinically dissoluble. The *gemara* justifies this by analogy to *meis mitzvah*. Tosafot objects: she is not really a *meis mitzvah* — a *kohen* cannot be *metamei* to every relative who happens to have no other heirs simply by invoking that category. But Tosafot explains that because it is *sort of like* a *meis mitzvah*, because there is a logical basis to the parallel, it does not constitute a clear *akira d'Oraisa*. The same logic applies to *ed echad*: because the woman is assumed to investigate (*dayka u-mintsva*), and because there is therefore some rational basis for treating the testimony as sufficient, the act of relying on it is not a naked uprooting of Torah law. There is enough of a fig leaf to place the innovation in a category short of outright *akira*.

### Rashi's View

Rashi's approach points in a similar direction but rests on a distinct mechanism. Rashi's explanation of why *ed echad* suffices here is *afkinu rabbanan l'kiddushin minei* — the rabbis have the power of *hafka'as kiddushin*, the uprooting of the marital bond. Critically, Rashi does not regard this as an unlimited power. The rabbis exercise *hafka'as kiddushin* only when there is, in the phrase used to describe it, a fig leaf — some mechanism that makes it appear as though something legitimate is happening. They will not simply declare a marriage dissolved by fiat. But when there is a *get*, even a defective one, or when there is testimony that the husband has died, that serves as the fig leaf: behind the scenes the rabbis perform *hafka'as kiddushin*, but it *looks* like the *get* is operating, or it *looks* like the husband is confirmed dead. The machinery of *hafka'as kiddushin* thus turns on the presence of some apparent legal basis.

### Ra'ah's View

The Ra'ah's approach, as noted in the earlier discussion of the three *rishonim*, holds that once the combination of *ed echad* and the woman's investigative capacity creates sufficient circumstantial evidence, the matter rises to the level of *anan sa'adi* — as self-evident as direct testimony — and therefore operates on a full *d'Oraisa* level without any need for rabbinic override. The appeal of this approach is that it avoids invoking the controversial power of *yesh koach* entirely. Its weakness, which leads Tosafot to reject it, is that the evidentiary basis here is simply not strong enough to genuinely qualify as *anan sa'adi* in any rigorous sense. Calling it that understates the real legal risk being taken.

## Teruma Case

### Repayment Rule

The *gemara* now turns to an extended series of cases designed to test the principle of *yesh koach beyad chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah*. The first involves the laws of *teruma*. The baseline *halacha* is that a non-*kohen* who eats *teruma* unintentionally (*beshogeg*) must repay an equivalent amount of food to the *kohen*, whereupon that food acquires the status of *teruma* in its stead. The *braita* under discussion concerns the specific case where the *teruma* he ate was *tehora* — ritually pure — and he repays with *chullin temei'in* — impure non-*teruma* food.

### Rabbi Meir Dispute

The dispute is between *Sumchus* in the name of Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim*. *Sumchus* says: if the non-*kohen* repaid with *chullin temei'in beshogeg* — inadvertently — the repayment is valid and he is done; if he did so *bemezid* — deliberately — the repayment is invalid (*ein tashlumo tashlumin*). The *chachamim* say: in either case, *beshogeg* or *bemezid*, the repayment takes effect — the *chullin temei'in* becomes *teruma temei'ah* — but he must then go back and repay again with *chullin tehorim*.

This creates an obvious problem: why should the *chachamim* require a second payment? The *chullin temei'in* became *teruma* in its stead — that works *d'Oraisa*. And why, according to Rabbi Meir, should the repayment be invalid *bemezid*? If *d'Oraisa* the food transferred ownership to the *kohen* the moment it was handed over, on what basis do the rabbis say it did not?

The *gemara* raises the problem in a pointed way. If the *kohen* received the *chullin temei'in*, and they are now his property on a biblical level, and he then uses that money — say, to betroth a woman — that betrothal should take effect. For the rabbis to declare *ein tashlumo tashlumin* and strip the *kohen* of what is biblically his, retroactively making that betrothal invalid, appears to be a clear case of *yesh koach beyad chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah*.

Before taking up the *gemara*'s response, it is worth noting that Tosafot raises a threshold question: why does the *gemara* need to use the case of betrothal to dramatize the problem? Why not simply point to the more straightforward *halachic* consequence — that a *kohen* who eats the supposedly-*teruma* food while *tamei* would be liable or exempt based on whether we treat it as *teruma* or not? Tosafot's discussion of this is instructive, though not pursued at length here.

### Raba's Solution

Raba (or, some say, the anonymous *kadi*) proposes that the *braita* is missing a clause, and must be reconstructed as follows (*chasori mechasra ve-hacha katni*): the beginning of the *braita* is actually speaking of a different case — where the non-*kohen* ate *teruma temei'ah*. There, he can certainly repay with *chullin temei'in* of any kind, since he ate something that was already impure. The real case being debated — about valid and invalid repayment, *beshogeg* versus *bemezid* — concerns eating *teruma tehora*. When one eats *teruma tehora* and repays with *chullin temei'in*, the *kohen* is genuinely damaged: he ate something pure and usable, and he is getting back something that becomes *teruma temei'ah*, which he cannot eat and can only burn. That is where the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* lies.

### Negating Payment

The *gemara*'s core question remains: even under Raba's reconstruction, how can the rabbis — whether by declaring *ein tashlumo tashlumin* (Rabbi Meir's position on *mezid*) or by requiring a second payment (the *chachamim*'s position) — negate or complicate what works *d'Oraisa*? The biblical transfer of ownership happened. The betrothal with that money would be valid. How can rabbinic law undo that?

This is where the *sugya* explicitly asks whether *yesh koach beyad chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah* applies.

### Rav Acha's Answer

The *gemara* answers with a statement from Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika: *kensu shogeg atu mezid ikka beinaiahu* — the real difference between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* is not about the *mezid* case at all; everyone agrees that in the *mezid* case, the repayment took effect and the *kohen* owns what he received. The dispute is about the *shogeg* case. The *chachamim* say that even *beshogeg*, the payment takes effect and you must give a second time. Rabbi Meir is lenient: *beshogeg*, the payment takes effect and you are done — no second payment required. Rabbi Meir imposes the additional burden of a second payment only in the *mezid* case, as a *knas*, but even there, the original transfer of ownership stands.

Under this reading, no one is actually declaring that the *d'Oraisa* transfer of ownership was void. The question is only whether a rabbinic fine (*knas*) attaches — a second payment. No one claims that the *kohen* doesn't own what he received, so the betrothal concern dissolves. There is no *akira d'Oraisa* here at all.

### Hefker Requirement

This resolution prompts a natural question: if the real issue is just about ownership of money, why didn't the *gemara* simply invoke the principle of *hefker beit din hefker*? The rabbis could declare the original payment ownerless and resolve the ownership problem that way, without needing to invoke *yesh koach la'akor davar min ha-Torah* at all.

Tosafot addresses this directly. In a formulation that cuts to the heart of *hefker beit din hefker*'s proper scope, Tosafot says: *de'hacha ein shayach lomar kein* — that principle simply does not apply here, and the reason is telling. *Demah pasha kohen she'yesh lanu lehafkir mamono* — what did the *kohen* do wrong that we should strip him of his property? *Hefker beit din hefker* in its authentic application is a punitive mechanism: it is used to fine someone who did something wrong. It is not a free-floating power to redistribute ownership whenever convenient. The *kohen* here did nothing wrong; the person who paid him improperly is the one at fault, and yet we would be taking money from the *kohen* to solve the problem. Tosafot holds that *hefker beit din hefker* cannot be applied in that direction.

This is a significant point. The principle of *hefker beit din hefker*, rooted in texts from Yehoshua and in early amoraic usage, was originally conceived as a coercive or punitive tool — seizing the property of someone who flouted communal authority. Over time the *stam* of the *gemara* expanded it into a broader mechanism for manipulating property ownership. Tosafot here pulls it back toward its original context: you can use *hefker beit din hefker* against someone who did something wrong, not to reallocate assets from an innocent party.

## Blood Case

### Blood Atonement

The *gemara* moves to its second extended case bearing on *yesh koach*. The case involves *dam* — the blood of a *korban* — that became *tamei* and was then sprinkled on the *mizbeiach*. The general *halacha* is that the *tzitz* worn by the *kohen gadol* effects acceptance (*meratzeh*) for offerings that became *tamei*: *bedi'eved*, the sprinkling works and the owners receive *kappara*. However, if the *kohen* who sprinkled the blood did so *bemezid* — intentionally, knowing it was *tamei* and violating the rule — the rabbis declare that the owners do not receive *kappara* and presumably must bring a new offering.

### Tzitz Contradiction

This seems to be a direct case of *yesh koach la'akor davar min ha-Torah*: the *braita* itself states that the *tzitz* effects acceptance *bein beshogeg bein bemezid bein be'ones bein beratzon bein beyachid bein betzibbur* — in every case, regardless of intent, willfulness, or whether it is an individual or communal offering. *D'Oraisa*, the sprinkling works and the owners are atoned. Yet the rabbis declare *bemezid lo urtza* — it does not work — and the owners bring a second *korban*, which would constitute *chullin ba'azarah* given that *d'Oraisa* they were already atoned. This appears to be a clear example of the rabbis uprooting a biblical law.

### R. Yose's Answer

Rabbi Yose bar Chanina resolves the contradiction. *Lo urtza*, he says, does not mean that the owners fail to receive *kappara*. They do receive *kappara*; the *tzitz* works on the *d'Oraisa* level and they need not bring another *korban*. What *lo urtza* means is that the *kohanim* are not permitted to eat the meat of the offering. The *tzitz* atones for the owners, but it does not grant permission for the *kohanim* to eat.

### Gemara Objection

The *gemara* presses this answer. Even if we accept that the owners are atoned and no second *korban* is required, we are still uprooting a *mitzvah*: the verse states *ve'achalu osam asher kuppar bahem* — the *kohanim* shall eat it, through which the owners are atoned. The eating of the meat by the *kohanim* is itself a biblical obligation, and by telling the *kohanim* not to eat, the rabbis are preventing the performance of a *mitzvas aseh d'Oraisa*. How is that permissible?

### Shev Ve'al Ta'aseh

The *gemara* answers: *shev ve'al ta'aseh shani*. There is a fundamental distinction between the rabbis actively uprooting a biblical law — causing something prohibited to be treated as permitted, or something unmarried to be treated as married — and the rabbis passively preventing a positive commandment from being fulfilled. In the latter case, they instruct: sit still and do not perform this act. The non-performance is the violation, but it is a passive non-performance, and the rabbis possess the authority to say *shev ve'al ta'aseh* even where a *mitzvas aseh d'Oraisa* thereby goes unperformed. That is the conclusion that emerges from this *sugya*: *yesh koach la'akor davar min ha-Torah be-shev ve'al ta'aseh*.

It should also be noted: the passive-active distinction here is not simply the distinction between a *lav* and an *aseh*. In this very case there is in fact a *lav* — the prohibition of *nosar* (*lo sosiru mimmenu ad boker*) — yet what the rabbis are doing is still classified as *shev ve'al ta'aseh*, because the rabbis are not making anyone actively transgress; they are simply preventing an act from being performed.

## Shev Ve'al Ta'aseh

### Seven Examples

The *gemara* now presents a series of cases — the questioner had prepared them as proofs that *yesh koach la'akor davar min ha-Torah* extends beyond *shev ve'al ta'aseh* — each of which involves the rabbis preventing the performance of a biblical *mitzvah* through a rabbinic prohibition. There are seven such cases, and the questioner introduces them one by one.

### Arel Example

The first case involves an *arel* — more precisely, a convert (*ger*) who underwent *berit milah* on the eve of Pesach. The *halacha* is that such a person is treated rabbinically as though he has just separated from a corpse and requires a seven-day *taharah* process — even though *d'Oraisa* no such requirement exists. The consequence is that the rabbis, by imposing this seven-day waiting period, prevent him from bringing the *korban pesach* — an enormously significant positive commandment — that very night. This looks like a case of the rabbis uprooting a biblical *mitzvah* by means of a rabbinic rule.

### Haza'ah Example

The second case involves *haza'ah*: sprinkling the ashes of the *parah adumah* on a person on the seventh day of his purification process. If that seventh day falls on *erev Pesach* which is *Shabbos*, and sprinkling on *Shabbos* is rabbinically prohibited, then the person cannot complete his purification, cannot become *tahor* in time, and therefore cannot bring the *korban pesach* that night. The rabbinically prohibited *haza'ah* has thus prevented a *d'Oraisa mitzvah*. Similar logic drives the other examples: carrying a knife (*izmel*) for *berit milah* through a *karmelis* is rabbinically forbidden, preventing performance of *milah* if the knife was not pre-positioned; wearing *tzitzit* on a linen garment is rabbinically prohibited out of concern one will wear it at night when the *mitzvah* does not apply and thereby violate *shatnez*, so the rabbis say don't put on *tzitzit* at all; the two sheep (*kivsei atzeret*) brought on *Shavuos* that fell on *Shabbos* require sprinkling of blood but the rabbis say not to sprinkle, preventing the eating of their meat; *shofar* on *Rosh Hashana* and *lulav* on *Sukkos* when those days fall on *Shabbos* are not taken.

### Concession

All of these cases, the questioner had assumed, would prove that the rabbis can actively override a *d'Oraisa mitzvah*. But once the *gemara* establishes the principle of *shev ve'al ta'aseh*, the questioner concedes: every single one of these cases is precisely *shev ve'al ta'aseh* — the rabbis issue a prohibition, and a *mitzvah* thereby goes unperformed, but no active uprooting of Torah law takes place. None of them, therefore, proves *yesh koach* in the stronger sense of actively overriding a biblical rule.

## Navi Comparison

### Elijah Case

The *gemara* considers whether a *navi* can violate Torah law, citing the paradigmatic case of Elijah at Mount Carmel. Elijah had his followers build an altar outside Jerusalem during a period when the *beit hamikdash* was standing — an act that constitutes building a *bamah*, ordinarily prohibited. Yet the *navi* commanded it and God accepted it. This seems to demonstrate that a *navi* has the power to override a Torah prohibition. If so, one might ask: can we learn from a *navi*'s authority to *chazal*'s authority — if *nevuah* allows temporary override, does rabbinic authority include the same?

### Special Mitzva

The *gemara* rejects the inference. *Shani hatam dechtiv elav tishme'un* — that case is different, because there is a specific biblical commandment to listen to a *navi*. The *navi*'s authority to issue temporary rulings is itself grounded in a *mitzvas aseh d'Oraisa* (*elav tishme'un*), not in any general principle of overriding Torah law. One cannot derive from this a general principle applicable to the rabbinic system.

### Migdar Milta

Moreover, even the *navi*'s ability to override is categorically different in nature. What Elijah did was a *hora'at sha'ah* — a ruling for an exigent moment — not a systemic or permanent modification of the law. And the *gemara* characterizes it as *migdar milta*: a protective measure necessitated by exigent circumstances, designed to be limited in time and scope. *Migdar milta* is categorically distinct from *yesh koach* in the sense of a structural rabbinic power to uproot Torah law as part of the ongoing legal system.

## Shev Ve'al Ta'aseh (II)

### Tzitzit Clarification

One of the cases from the earlier list — *sadin b'tzitzit* — raises a conceptual question. If a person wears a linen garment and the rabbis tell him not to put *tzitzit* on it, is that really *shev ve'al ta'aseh*? After all, wearing a four-cornered garment without *tzitzit* is a failure to perform a *mitzvah* while actively wearing the garment — that sounds more like *kum ve'aseh* (active failure) than passive inaction.

Tosafot addresses this by rethinking the conceptual structure of the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit*. The obligation, Tosafot argues, does not attach at the moment of putting the garment on; the obligation is to have *tzitzit* on a garment one *is wearing*. The act of donning the garment does not yet trigger the *mitzvah* — the *mitzvah* is a continuous one that takes effect once the garment is on. Therefore, the person who wears the linen garment without *tzitzit* is not actively committing a violation in the act of putting it on; he is passively failing to fulfill the *mitzvah* that runs throughout the time he wears it. That is *shev ve'al ta'aseh*: the rabbis, by prohibiting *tzitzit* on linen, cause him to passively not fulfill the ongoing obligation, rather than requiring him to actively do something wrong.

## Get Annulment

### Get Scenario

The *gemara* presents the case of a husband who annuls a *get* not in the presence of his wife or the *shaliach* carrying it. The Rabbis legislated that such annulment is forbidden (*lo yevatel*), in order to prevent a situation where the *shaliach* delivers the *get*, the wife remarries thinking she is divorced, and only later does it emerge that the *get* had been annulled beforehand — producing a situation of *mamzer* and chaos. Having enacted this prohibition, the question is what happens when the husband violates it and annuls anyway.

### Annulment Debate

There is a dispute. *Bitlo mevutal*, says *Rebbi* (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi): even though he violated the rabbinic rule, the annulment takes effect. The *get* is void and the woman remains his wife.

### Rabban Shimon

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: *eini yachol levatloh* — he cannot annul it; the annulment does not work. Nor can he add conditions to the *get*. His reasoning: *she'im kein mah koach beit din yafeh* — if we permit him to ignore the rabbinic rule and annul anyway, what force does the rabbinic enactment have? To protect the institution and the woman, the rabbis must back up their rule by refusing to recognize an annulment that was performed in violation of it.

This sounds like a powerful case of *yesh koach beyad chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah*: *d'Oraisa* the annulment is effective, and the rabbis are declaring it void, which means a woman who is biblically still married (because the *get* is *batel*) is treated as divorced — a woman potentially free to remarry who is in fact an *eishet ish*.

### Hafka'as Answer

The *gemara* rejects the inference. This is not *yesh koach beyad chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah* in the general sense. Rather, it is a specific instance of *hafka'as kiddushin*: *man de'mekadesh ada'ata de'rabbanan mekadesh, afkinu rabbanan le'kiddushin minei*. Anyone who marries does so on the understanding (*ada'ata*) that the rabbis sanction the marriage, and if the rabbis retract that sanction, the marriage itself unravels retroactively. This is not a sweeping power to override Torah law; it is a targeted capacity within the domain of marriage, grounded in the understanding that *kiddushin* are always conditioned on rabbinic approval.

### Redefining Act

But this raises a further question. The mechanism of *hefker beit din hefker* can dissolve ownership of property — explaining why the *kohen gadol's* ring no longer belongs to him — but what about a marriage effected through *bi'ah* rather than money? If the *kiddushin* were performed through an act of intercourse, there is no property to retroactively un-own. The *gemara*'s answer: *shavyuha rabbanan le'vi'aso bi'as znus* — the rabbis retroactively redefine the act of intercourse as *bi'as znus*, fornication, not *bi'as kiddushin*. It was never an act of marriage; therefore no marriage ever came into existence. This is not uprooting a marriage from the outside; it is retroactively redefining what the act was.

### Fig Leaf Limit

The Rashba makes explicit a constraint that Rashi implies: *hafka'as kiddushin* is not an unlimited power. The rabbis will perform it only when there is a fig leaf — some apparent mechanism that makes it look as though the marriage was legitimately dissolved. In the *get* case, that fig leaf is the existence of the *get* itself: even though *d'Oraisa* the *get* was annulled and therefore void, the rabbis can perform *hafka'as kiddushin* behind the scenes while it *appears* that the *get* operated. In a case of pure recalcitrance, with no *get* at all, the fig leaf is absent, and *hafka'as kiddushin* cannot be invoked.

This has significant contemporary resonance: some who advocate using *hafka'as kiddushin* in cases of *get* refusal propose that a *get* be simultaneously tendered — precisely to create the fig leaf the Rashba requires.

### Tosafot Link

Tosafot makes an important observation about the structure of the *gemara*'s answer. The *gemara* says this case is resolved through *hafka'as kiddushin*, not through *yesh koach la'akor davar min ha-Torah*. But Tosafot argues that the two are not as separate as they appear: the power of *hafka'as kiddushin* itself is grounded in *yesh koach beyad chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah*. What makes *hafka'as kiddushin* acceptable where a naked *yesh koach* would not be is that the presence of some apparent mechanism — the redefinition of the ring's ownership, the redefinition of the act of intercourse — makes it look as though no clear *akira d'Oraisa* is taking place. It is *yesh koach* operating through a fig leaf, and therefore not obviously a violation of Torah categories. The distinction, as Tosafot acknowledges, is subtle — perhaps uncomfortably so — but it is the line Tosafot draws between a permissible use of rabbinic power and a blatant uprooting of Torah law.

## Maaseh Cases

### R. Elazar

The *gemara*'s final set of cases involves the statement of Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov that *beit din* gives lashes and penalties not prescribed by Torah law — not in order to violate Torah, but *la'asot seyag la-Torah*, to build a protective fence. This is the idea of *migdar milta* in its most direct form.

### Horse Riding

The first *maaseh*: during the period of Greek dominance, a man was found riding a horse on *Shabbos* — a rabbinic violation, not a biblical one. He was brought before *beit din* and stoned. *Lo mipnei she-ra'uy lekach* — not because he legally deserved that punishment — *ela she-hasha'ah tzericha lekach* — but because the times demanded it. Assimilation and religious laxity were rampant, and a dramatic response was necessary to restore communal discipline.

### Public Relations

The second *maaseh*: a man had relations with his wife under a fig tree in public view. He was brought before *beit din* and flogged — again, not because halakhic law prescribed that penalty for that act, but because the social circumstances (*hasha'ah tzericha lekach*) required a public demonstration that such behavior would not be tolerated.

### Migdar Limit

The *gemara*'s response is the same as before: *migdar milta shani*. These punishments are not evidence of a general structural power for *chachamim* to override Torah law. They are exigent-circumstance interventions, targeted responses to specific crises, not systematic changes to the *halachic* order. They belong to the category of *hora'at sha'ah*, not *yesh koach* as a permanent legal principle.

## Conclusion

### Conceded Uses

The *sugya* ends without a decisive resolution on the broadest question of *yesh koach beyad chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah*, but it does establish certain clear conclusions. Two categories of rabbinic power over Torah law are definitively conceded: *shev ve'al ta'aseh* — the rabbis may issue prohibitions that result in a *mitzvas aseh d'Oraisa* going unperformed, without this constituting an active *akira* — and *migdar milta* — *beit din* may impose non-Torah punishments and take extraordinary measures in response to exigent circumstances, as a protective fence around communal observance.

### Tosafot Condition

According to Tosafot, there is also a third category that falls short of outright *akira*: cases in which there is some apparent mechanism, some fig leaf, that makes the innovation look internally consistent with Torah categories rather than nakedly contrary to them. When such a mechanism is present, the rabbis may effectively uproot a Torah rule without it being characterized as *yesh koach* in the problematic sense. Both *hafka'as kiddushin* and the leniency of *ed echad* in our *perek* are examples of this type, in Tosafot's view.

### Inconclusive

What remains unresolved is whether *yesh koach beyad chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah* applies in a broader, undisguised sense — where there is no *shev ve'al ta'aseh*, no *migdar milta*, and no fig leaf mechanism. The *gemara* cannot prove this, and it cannot definitively deny it. The *teku* is implicit.

### Summary

The *rishonim* debate how to *pasken* on this question. Many cases are effectively conceded by all parties. The dispute turns on whether, in principle, the rabbis possess a structural power to uproot Torah law in a systematic rather than exigent way, and whether that power — if it exists — requires some covering mechanism or can operate openly. This *sugya* is among the most significant in all of *Shas* for understanding the scope and limits of rabbinic authority, and the answers one gives here have far-reaching implications for how to approach many of the most difficult problems in contemporary *halacha*.
