# Avodah Zarah 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Eliezer's Prohibit…

### Intro & Dama Story

Daf 24 continues to grapple with the fundamental question of purchasing animals from non-Jews for use as sacrifices, and with the concerns about acts of bestiality that animate so much of this discussion. The previous daf concluded with the remarkable story of Dama ben Netinah — the ethically exemplary non-Jew who declined to disturb his sleeping father even to retrieve the precious stone needed for the *efod*, and who thereby forfeited a substantial profit. That narrative served as a striking counterbalance to the rather unflattering portrait of non-Jewish morality that had dominated the preceding discussion.

### Rabbi Eliezer & Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵיטָב? כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִיקְפֹּץ עֲלַיְהוּ זָבֹינָא. וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא בְּבָמַת יָחִיד, וְכִדְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, דְּאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: מִנַּיִן לְעוֹלָה נְקֵבָה שֶׁהִיא כְּשֵׁרָה בְּבָמַת יָחִיד? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקַּח שְׁמוּאֵל טְלֵה חָלָב אֶחָד וַיַּעֲלֵהוּ עוֹלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is different** about **the best** animals? If the animals were sold for their value, why sell those animals in particular, rather than several inferior-quality animals? The Gemara explains that they did so **in order that buyers would jump at** the opportunity to buy superior-quality livestock. In other words, it is easier to sell one superior-quality animal than several inferior-quality ones. **And what is the difficulty?** In other words, the additional problem with the incident, that the animals were female, which is cited as proof that there were extenuating circumstances, is not in fact difficult at all. The Gemara elaborates: **Perhaps** the cows were offered **upon a private altar, and** this is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Adda bar Ahava, as Rav Adda bar Ahava says: From where** is it derived **that a female burnt-offering is fit** to be sacrificed **upon a private altar? As it is stated: "And Samuel took a milking lamb, and sacrificed it [*vaya'alehu*] for a burnt-offering** unto the Lord"** (I Samuel 7:9).

The story of Dama was introduced into the Gemara for a specific halachic reason. Rabbi Eliezer holds that the *parah adumah* may not be purchased from a non-Jew, deriving this from the verse *daber el bnei Yisrael veyikchu elecha parah adumah* — "Speak to the children of Israel and let them take for you a red heifer." The Gemara had pressed this position by noting a parallel verse concerning *terumah* — *daber el bnei Yisrael veyikchu li terumah* — and yet we know that *terumah* can come from non-Jews. This led into the Dama story, since the very end of that narrative involves the sages purchasing a *parah adumah* from him.

### Gemara's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, קָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: לְשָׁנָה אַחֶרֶת נוֹלְדָה לוֹ פָּרָה אֲדוּמָּה בְּעֶדְרוֹ, נִכְנְסוּ חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶצְלוֹ. אָמַר לָהֶם: יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי בָּכֶם שֶׁאִם אֲנִי מְבַקֵּשׁ מִכֶּם כׇּל מָמוֹן שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם אַתֶּם נוֹתְנִין לִי, עַכְשָׁיו אֵינִי מְבַקֵּשׁ מִכֶּם אֶלָּא אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן שֶׁהִפְסַדְתִּי בִּשְׁבִיל אַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And furthermore, the latter clause teaches: In a subsequent year, a red heifer was born in** Dama's **herd** and **the Sages of Israel approached him,** seeking to purchase the heifer. Dama **said to them: I know concerning you that if I** were to **ask from you all the money in the world, you would give** it **to me. Now I am requesting from you only that** amount of **money which I lost by** refraining from waking **my father.**

The Gemara now presses further. Reading the continuation of the Dama story in the *baraita* on daf 24a, we find that the following year a *parah adumah* was born in Dama's flock, and the sages came to him. He said to them: "I know that if I were to name any price in the world you would pay it — but all I ask is that you reimburse me for the loss I suffered on account of my father." In the end, they purchased the *parah adumah* from him. This constitutes a direct challenge to Rabbi Eliezer: if the verse *veyikchu* requires that the purchase be made specifically from a member of *bnei Yisrael*, how could the sages have acquired it directly from a non-Jew?

### Middleman Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, עַל יְדֵי תַּגָּרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל זְבוּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** it was **through** the agency of **Jewish merchants** that **they purchased** the heifer.

The Gemara resolves this: *hatam al yedei tagrei Yisrael zavnu* — they did not purchase it directly from Dama; rather, a Jewish merchant served as an intermediary. Dama sold to the Jewish middleman, and the Jewish middleman in turn sold to the Temple. The verse *daber el bnei Yisrael veyikchu* thus governs only the direct acquisition by the Temple itself, which must be from a Jew. The non-Jewish original owner remains permissible so long as the formal purchase is transacted through a Jewish party. In this respect the *tagrei Yisrael* functions as a kind of "sale Jew" — a Jewish cutout who *kasher*s the transaction, analogous in structure (if not in subject matter) to the familiar concept of a *shabbos goy*.

## Rabbi Eliezer's Concerns

### Bestiality Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לָא חָיֵישׁ לִרְבִיעָה? וְהָתַנְיָא, אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מַעֲשֶׂה וּלְקָחוּהָ מִן הַגּוֹי, וְדָמָא שְׁמוֹ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רֶמֶץ שְׁמוֹ. אָמַר לָהֶן רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה? יִשְׂרָאֵל הָיוּ מְשַׁמְּרִין אוֹתָהּ מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנּוֹלְדָה! רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר תַּרְתֵּי אִית לֵיהּ: קִיחָה, וְחָיֵישׁ נָמֵי לִרְבִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** According to Sheila, Rabbi Eliezer's reason for not allowing a red heifer to be purchased from gentiles is based on a derivation from a verse rather than a concern that a person may have engaged in bestiality with it. The Gemara therefore asks: **And is Rabbi Eliezer not concerned about** the possibility that a person has engaged in **bestiality** with the animal? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that the Rabbis who hold that the heifer may be purchased from a gentile **said to Rabbi Eliezer:** There was **an incident** in which Jews **purchased** the red heifer **from a gentile, and Dama was his name. And some say Remetz was his name.** In response to this claim, **Rabbi Eliezer said to them:** Can one bring **a proof from there?** Certainly not, as **Jews were safeguarding it from the time that it was born,** to ensure that a person did not engage in bestiality with it. Contrary to the opinion of Sheila, it is evident from here that Rabbi Eliezer was concerned about this possibility. The Gemara explains that **Rabbi Eliezer has two** requirements: The **purchase** of the red heifer must be from a Jew, **and he is also concerned about** the possibility that a person had engaged in **bestiality** with the animal.

At this point the Gemara raises what is really the more fundamental question. A student might well ask: even granting that the verse is satisfied by using a Jewish middleman, what about the concern regarding acts of bestiality? If Rabbi Eliezer's objection to purchasing animals from non-Jews extends — as the *baraita* establishes — to all sacrifices and not merely to the *parah adumah*, then the concern cannot be rooted in the verse alone, since that verse is specific to the *parah adumah*. Evidently the broader prohibition on purchasing any sacrificial animal from a non-Jew must rest on a distinct concern: that non-Jews perform acts of bestiality with their animals, which would invalidate those animals for sacred use. If that is the case, then routing the purchase through a Jewish middleman solves nothing — the animal was still owned and presumably abused by the non-Jew.

The Gemara makes this explicit by quoting the *baraita* more fully. When the sages challenged Rabbi Eliezer — "But there was a case in which they purchased a *parah adumah* from a non-Jew named Dama!" — Rabbi Eliezer responded: *Yisrael hayu u'mishamrim otah mishe'noldah* — Jews were watching over it from the moment it was born, ensuring that no such act was ever performed upon it. This line is not addressing the verse issue at all; it is addressing the bestiality concern. And as Tosafot notes, Rabbi Eliezer's response here presupposes both issues simultaneously.

### Tartei — Twin Concerns

The Gemara draws the explicit conclusion: *Rabbi Eliezer tartei it leh* — Rabbi Eliezer has two distinct concerns operating in parallel. The first is the textual restriction of *kichah* — the requirement derived from *veyikchu* that the formal acquisition be by *bnei Yisrael*. This concern is specific to the *parah adumah*. The second is *vi'yah* — the concern about acts of bestiality — and because of this concern, Rabbi Eliezer extends his prohibition to all sacrificial animals.

### Both Issues Applied

The practical consequence of Rabbi Eliezer holding both concerns simultaneously is that the *parah adumah* presents a double requirement. For ordinary sacrifices, the bestiality concern can be resolved by having a *mashgiach* — a Jewish supervisor who monitors the animal from birth to ensure no prohibited act is performed. If that has been done, the animal may be purchased directly from the non-Jew (subject to the Rabbis' permissive position, of course). But for the *parah adumah*, resolving the bestiality concern through *mashgiach*-supervision is insufficient on its own — one must *also* transact the purchase through a Jewish middleman, on account of the independent textual requirement. Both problems must be solved; neither alone is sufficient.

## Bestiality & Pregnancy

### Fetus Contamination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: יִשְׂרָאֵל הָיוּ מְשַׁמְּרִין אוֹתָהּ מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנּוֹלְדָה. וְנֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא רָבְעִי לְאִמַּהּ כִּי הֲוָה מְעַבְּרָה! דְּאָמַר רָבָא: וְולַד הַנּוֹגַחַת אֲסוּרָה — הִיא וּוְלָדָהּ נָגְחוּ, וְולַד הַנִּרְבַּעַת אֲסוּרָה — הִיא וּוְלָדָהּ נִרְבְּעוּ. אֵימָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל הָיוּ מְשַׁמְּרִין אוֹתָהּ מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנּוֹצְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes Rabbi Eliezer's statement. **The Master said: Jews were safeguarding** the red heifer purchased from Dama ben Netina **from the time that it was born.** The Gemara asks: **And let us be concerned** that **perhaps** gentiles **engaged in bestiality with the mother while she was pregnant, as Rava says: The offspring of** an animal that **gored** and killed a Jew **is forbidden** as an offering, as it is considered as if **she and her offspring** together **gored.** And similarly, **the offspring of** an animal that was the object of **bestiality is forbidden** because it is considered as if a person **engaged in bestiality** with both **her and her offspring.** The Gemara answers: **Say** that Rabbi Eliezer meant that **Jews were safeguarding it from the time that it was conceived.**

The Gemara now takes the analysis considerably further. Even granting that the sages had stationed a Jewish supervisor to watch the animal from birth, there is a residual concern: what if the mother had been subjected to an act of bestiality while she was pregnant? The fetus, after all, was present in the womb at that moment. On the principle of *ubar yerech imo* — that the fetus is considered a limb of the mother, a single halachic unit — one might argue that an act done to the mother while she was pregnant was, in a meaningful sense, done to the fetus as well.

Rava articulates this principle explicitly. He states: *vlad nogachat asurah* — if a cow that gores has a fetus in her womb, both she and her fetus are treated as having performed the goring (*hi v'valdah nagchu*). Similarly, *vlad hanirbaat asurah* — if an act of bestiality is performed upon a pregnant animal, the fetus is treated as having been subjected to that act as well (*hi v'valdah nirbu*). This is a striking application of *ubar yerech imo* that carries the taint of the act directly into the next generation, so long as the act occurred during the pregnancy.

Assuming Rabbi Eliezer accepts Rava's principle, it follows that simply watching the animal from the moment of its birth would be inadequate. One would have had to watch the *mother* during the period of her pregnancy, to ensure that nothing was done to her in that critical window. This complicates the scenario enormously: how did the supervisors know which animal to watch during pregnancy, before they knew the animal would give birth to a *parah adumah*?

### Invalidity Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא רַבְעוּהָ לְאִמַּהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא! דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הַפְּסוּלִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ — וַלְדוֹתֵיהֶן מוּתָּרִין, וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let us be concerned** that **perhaps they engaged in bestiality with the mother from the outset,** i.e., before conception, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Temura* 30b): With regard to **all** animals **that are disqualified for the altar,** such as one that was the object of bestiality, sacrifice of **their offspring is permitted; and it is taught with regard to** this case that **Rabbi Eliezer prohibits** their offspring from being sacrificed on the altar.

The Gemara anticipates a possible rejoinder. The *Mishnah* teaches that *kol hapesulim legabei mizbe'ach* — all animals that are themselves disqualified for the altar — have offspring that are *mutar*, permitted. In other words, if the mother's invalidity does not transmit to her offspring, then even if the mother was subjected to bestiality before she became pregnant, her offspring would be unaffected. If so, the concern reduces only to acts during the actual pregnancy, which is a much narrower window.

But the Gemara immediately notes that Rabbi Eliezer himself disputes this ruling and holds that the invalidity *does* transmit to the offspring. This reopens the problem: according to Rabbi Eliezer, even if the act occurred prior to the pregnancy, the resulting offspring would still be tainted.

### Transmission Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַחְלוֹקֶת כְּשֶׁנִּרְבְּעוּ כְּשֶׁהֵן מוּקְדָּשִׁין, אֲבָל כְּשֶׁהֵן חוּלִּין — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּרִין. אֶלָּא לְרַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַחְלוֹקֶת כְּשֶׁנִּרְבְּעוּ כְּשֶׁהֵן חוּלִּין, אֲבָל כְּשֶׁהֵן מוּקְדָּשִׁין — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֲסוּרִין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the difficulty: Granted, **this works out well according to** the opinion of **Rava, as Rava says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** The **dispute** between the Rabbis and Rabbi Eliezer applies only **when** a person **engaged in bestiality** with the animals **when they were consecrated** and then they conceived, as Rabbi Eliezer holds that in that case the offspring are disqualified from being sacrificed as offerings; **but** if someone engaged in bestiality with the animals **when they were non-sacred, everyone agrees** that the offspring **are permitted.** Accordingly, it needs to be ascertained only that no one engaged in bestiality with the mother after she was consecrated. **But according to** the opinion of **Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana,** who says that **Rav Naḥman says:** The **dispute** applies only **when** a person **engaged in bestiality** with the animals **when they were non-sacred, but** if someone engaged in bestiality with them **when they were consecrated, everyone agrees** that the offspring **are forbidden, what is there to say?**

Rava, citing Rav Nachman, offers a crucial qualification. The debate over whether invalidity transmits to offspring, he says, applies specifically when the mother incurred her invalidity while she was already *mukdash* — sanctified as a Temple animal. But if the mother was *chullin* — unsanctified, as would certainly be the case here — then *divrei hakol mutar*: everyone agrees the invalidity does not transmit. This would neatly resolve the problem; since the mother of the *parah adumah* was never sanctified, acts done to her before pregnancy would not affect her offspring.

However, Rav Huna bar Chinena reads Rav Nachman's report differently. According to his version, the debate between the Rabbis and Rabbi Eliezer applies specifically when the mother was *chullin*, and Rabbi Eliezer holds that even in the unsanctified state, the invalidity transmits. If the mother was *mukdash* when she incurred the invalidity, everyone agrees it transmits. This is a considerably more stringent reading of Rabbi Eliezer's position, and it reopens the problem fully.

### When Watching Begins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל הָיוּ מְשַׁמְּרִין אוֹתָהּ לְאִמָּהּ מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנּוֹצְרָה. וְנֵיחוּשׁ דִּילְמָא רַבְעוּהָ לְאִמָּא דְּאִמַּהּ! כּוּלֵּי הַאי לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Say** that **Jews were safeguarding it** as well, i.e., **the mother** of the red heifer, **from the time that it was conceived.** The Gemara challenges: **And let us be concerned** that **perhaps** someone **engaged in bestiality with the mother of the** heifer's **mother.** The Gemara responds: **We are not concerned** to **such an extent.**

According to Rav Huna bar Chinena's version of the dispute, simply watching the mother during the pregnancy is not sufficient — Rabbi Eliezer's position would require that one have monitored the mother from the moment of *her* own conception, to ensure that no act of bestiality was ever performed on her. And indeed the Gemara arrives at precisely this formulation: *Yisrael hayu mishamrim otah le'imah mishe'notzrah* — Jews watched over the mother from the moment the mother herself was conceived. At which point the Gemara asks the inevitable follow-up: what about the mother of the mother? To this the Gemara responds, *kulei hai lo chaishinan* — we do not extend the inquiry that far back. There is, in other words, a point at which the chain of suspicion must terminate — the grandmother's generation is beyond the scope of the concern.

## Red Heifer Breeding

### Mother's History

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: יִשְׂרָאֵל הָיוּ מְשַׁמְּרִין אוֹתָהּ מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנּוֹצְרָה. מְנָא יָדְעִינַן? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: כּוֹס אָדוֹם מַעֲבִירִין לְפָנֶיהָ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעוֹלֶה עָלֶיהָ זָכָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the revised version of Rabbi Eliezer's statement. **The Master said** above: **Jews were safeguarding** the red heifer **from the time that it was conceived.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we know** that the mother would give birth to a red heifer, which would warrant safeguarding her? **Rav Kahana says: They would pass a red cup before her while the male mounts her,** an act that would cause the offspring to be red.

Even having established that the inquiry terminates at the grandmother's generation, a new question presses: if the sages were monitoring the mother from the moment of the mother's own conception, how did they know in advance which animal to monitor? A *parah adumah* is an extraordinarily rare phenomenon. What gave them reason to station a supervisor next to a particular cow during pregnancy, before they had any indication that the offspring would be a *parah adumah*?

Rav Kahana offers an answer: *kos adom ma'avirin lefaneyha besha'ah she'oleh aleha hazachar* — they would pass a red cup before the animal at the moment of conception, while the male was mounting the female. The ancient belief — familiar from the story of Yaakov in Bereishit — was that whatever the female animal perceived visually at the moment of conception would influence the coloration of the offspring. By ensuring the animal was looking at something red, one could engineer a red offspring with some reliability. This is why the breeders would know to watch a particular animal: they had deliberately induced a red calf.

This belief enjoyed wide currency in the ancient world and extended to human beings as well. The Gemara elsewhere records that Rabbi Yochanan would position himself near the *mikveh* so that women leaving to rejoin their husbands would see his famously beautiful face, and their children would inherit his looks. The Yaakov story itself is more nuanced: Yaakov may have been attempting to use this technique, but the angel's subsequent revelation suggests that the actual result was brought about by divine intervention, not by the sticks and visual stimuli Yaakov had deployed.

### Expense Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי דָּמֶיהָ יְקָרִין? הוֹאִיל וּשְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת פּוֹסְלוֹת בָּהּ. וּמַאי שְׁנָא דִּידְהוּ? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: בְּמוּחְזֶקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** and the birth of a red heifer can be induced in such a simple manner, **why is its price so expensive?** The Gemara answers: **Since two hairs** of a different color **render** the red heifer **unfit,** this method is unreliable. The Gemara asks another question: **And what is different** about **these** cows? Why did the Jews resort to purchasing the red heifer from a gentile when they could have induced its conception themselves? **Rav Kahana said:** Dama ben Netina owned a herd of cattle that **were known** to beget red heifers, and therefore they preferred to employ the aforementioned method with one of his cows.

The Gemara immediately raises an objection to Rav Kahana's explanation: if the red cup technique reliably produces red offspring, why were *parah adumah* animals so extraordinarily expensive? If any farmer could simply hold a red cup before his cow at conception and produce a red heifer, the market should be flooded with candidates and the price should be negligible.

The resolution is that it is one thing to produce a red cow, and quite another to produce a cow that is *perfectly* red — one that lacks even two black hairs, since two black hairs suffice to disqualify the animal. The red cup technique might yield a generally reddish animal with some frequency, but a cow that is *completely* free of black hairs is a matter of genetics, not visual stimulus alone.

Rav Kahana therefore adds the concept of *muchzeket* — a known genetic line. The sages would identify a family of cattle with a proven track record of producing deeply, uniformly red offspring, and it was this genetic pedigree that gave them reason to station supervisors next to a particular animal. The red cup technique could improve the odds of redness, but only a *muchzeket* line could give confidence that the offspring would be flawless enough to qualify. In practical terms, this means the red cup explanation can largely be set aside as the primary rationale: the sages knew which animals to watch because they had identified the right genetic lineage, and the history of that line justified the investment of supervision going back, in some versions of the concern, to the mother's own conception.

## Challenges to R. Eliezer

### Ami & Nappacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אַקִּלְעָא דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא, פְּתַח חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ וַאֲמַר: וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹסֵל בְּכׇל הַקָּרְבָּנוֹת כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa were sitting on Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa's porch. One of them began and said** that the *baraita* teaches: **And, similarly, Rabbi Eliezer would disqualify all offerings** purchased from gentiles, due to the concern that a person had engaged in bestiality with the animals.

The Gemara now shifts to a broader challenge to Rabbi Eliezer's position. Rav Ami and Rav Yitzchak Nappacha were sitting together on Rav Yitzchak Nappacha's *kila* — Rashi explains this as a kind of antechamber or porch before the main room. One of them opened the discussion in an expansive, *midrashic* mode (*patach chad minayhu v'amar*), stating that Rabbi Eliezer's disqualification applied to all sacrificial animals without exception.

### Kedar Flocks Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּתַח אִידַּךְ מִינַּיְיהוּ וְאָמַר: מַאי אוֹתִיבוּ לֵיהּ חַבְרוֹהִי לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? ״כׇּל צֹאן קֵדָר יִקָּבְצוּ לָךְ וְגוֹ׳״! אָמַר לָהֶן רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: כּוּלָּם גֵּרִים גְּרוּרִים הֵם לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **The other** one **of them began and said: What did Rabbi Eliezer's colleagues respond to him?** They quoted the verse: **"All flocks of Kedar shall be gathered together unto you,** the rams of Nebaioth shall minister unto you; they shall come up with acceptance upon My altar" (Isaiah 60:7). This verse clearly indicates that offerings will be accepted from gentiles. **Rabbi Eliezer said to them: In the future all** gentiles will **be calculating converts,** i.e., those who converted for personal gain, and as converts, they will not be suspected of engaging in bestiality.

The other then asked: but what did Rabbi Eliezer's colleagues say in response to his position? The obvious challenge comes from the verse in Yeshayahu: *kol tzon Keidar yikavtzu lach* — all the flocks of Kedar shall be gathered to you. These are the animals of the Ishmaelite tribes, and the verse clearly anticipates their being brought as sacrifices. If Rabbi Eliezer prohibits purchasing animals from non-Jews for sacrifice, how does he read this verse?

Rabbi Eliezer's answer is sweeping: *kulam geirim gerurim heim le'atid lavo* — in the messianic future, all the nations will have converted. The animals of Kedar will be brought as sacrifices, but by that time those who bring them will be full Jews, and the animals will have been raised and supervised in accordance with Jewish law. The verse is not describing the present legal order but the transformed reality of the End of Days.

This response opens onto one of the great theological questions of Jewish eschatology: what is our vision of the messianic era? Is it a world in which everyone becomes Jewish? A world in which Jews remain Jews and non-Jews remain non-Jews but all acknowledge God? Or — most radical of all — a world in which even the distinction of Jews and their particular *mitzvot* is dissolved, in line with the opinion that the *mitzvot* will be *batelot le'atid lavo*, abrogated in the future perfect world?

### Worship Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי קְרָא? ״כִּי אָז אֶהְפֹּךְ אֶל עַמִּים שָׂפָה בְרוּרָה וְגוֹ׳״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְדִלְמָא מֵעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הוּא דַּהֲדוּר בְּהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: ״לְעׇבְדוֹ שְׁכֶם אֶחָד״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: What is the verse** from which it is derived that they will convert? The verse states: **"For then will I turn to the peoples a pure language,** that they may all call upon the name of the Lord, to serve Him with one consent" (Zephaniah 3:9). This verse indicates that all nations will worship God. **Abaye said to him: But perhaps it is** only **from idol worship that they** will **withdraw,** while they will still engage in forbidden sexual relations, including bestiality? **Rav Yosef said to him:** That cannot be, as **it is written** at the conclusion of the verse: **"To serve Him with one consent,"** which indicates that the gentiles will accept all of God's mitzvot.

Rav Yosef cites the verse: *ki az ehpoch el amim safah berurah likro kulam besheim Hashem* — "For then I will transform the nations to speak a pure language, that they may all call upon the Name of God." Abaye challenges this: perhaps the verse means only that the nations will abandon idolatry (*mei'avodas kochavim*), without adopting the full obligations of Jewish observance — in which case the bestiality concern would remain relevant. Rav Yosef responds: *le'ovdo shechem echad* — "to serve Him with one shoulder." The nations will serve God in complete unity with Israel, implying that they will observe all the *mitzvot* and will be, in every meaningful sense, indistinguishable from Jews in their religious obligations. The Gemara then notes that Rav Pappa and Rav Zevid each taught variant versions of which of these figures raised the question and which provided the answer, but the substantive content is consistent between the versions.

### Historical Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״וַיֹּאמֶר מֹשֶׁה גַּם אַתָּה תִּתֵּן בְּיָדֵנוּ זְבָחִים וְעֹלוֹת״! קוֹדֶם מַתַּן תּוֹרָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a series of verses which present difficulties with regard to the claim that animals purchased from gentiles may not be used as offerings. The Gemara **raises an objection** from the following verse: **"And Moses said: You must also give into our hand offerings and burnt-offerings** that we may sacrifice unto the Lord our God" (Exodus 10:25). Moses demanded that Pharaoh provide the Jews with offerings, despite the fact that he was a gentile. The Gemara explains: The *halakha* **prior to the giving of the Torah** was **different,** as at that time it was acceptable to use animals taken from gentiles as offerings.

The Gemara turns from eschatological verses to historical cases, and presses Rabbi Eliezer with a series of biblical episodes where it appears that animals owned by non-Jews were used as sacrifices. The first is Moshe's demand of Pharaoh: *gam ata titein b'yadeinu zvachim v'olos* — you too shall provide us with animals for sacrifices. Rabbi Eliezer's answer is *kodem matan Torah shani* — that was before *matan Torah*, when the relevant restrictions had not yet come into force.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וַיִּקַּח יִתְרוֹ חֹתֵן מֹשֶׁה עֹלָה וּזְבָחִים לֵאלֹהִים״! יִתְרוֹ נָמֵי קוֹדֶם מַתַּן תּוֹרָה הֲוָה. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: יִתְרוֹ קוֹדֶם מַתַּן תּוֹרָה הֲוָה, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: יִתְרוֹ לְאַחַר מַתַּן תּוֹרָה הֲוָה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אֶלָּא, יִתְרוֹ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל זְבַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the verse: **"And Yitro, Moses' father-in-law, took a burnt-offering and offerings for God"** (Exodus 18:12). Yitro was not a Jew, and yet he brought offerings for God. The Gemara answers: The incident with **Yitro was also prior to the giving of the Torah.** The Gemara observes: Granted, **this works out well according to the one who says** that the incident with **Yitro was prior to the giving of the Torah. But according to the one who says** that the incident involving **Yitro was after the giving of the Torah, what is there to say?** How could they accept offerings from him? **Rather,** it must be that **Yitro purchased** the animals **from a Jew.**

The second case is Yisro: *vayikach Yisro chosein Moshe olah u'zvachim l'Elohim* — Yisro took a burnt offering and sacrifices to God. Rabbi Eliezer can deploy the same answer if Yisro came before *matan Torah*. But the Gemara notes that this depends on a well-known dispute. The narrative of Yisro's arrival is positioned before *matan Torah*, yet the events described — Moshe sitting in judgment over the people, administering *chukei Elohim v'torosav* — only make sense after *matan Torah*. Tosafot (and Ramban on the Torah) resolve this by suggesting that Yisro arrived before *matan Torah* but the narrative encompasses events that occurred after: the Torah reports his arrival in full rather than splitting the story across the account of Sinai. If Yisro came before *matan Torah*, the pre-Sinaitic exemption applies; if he came after, the answer is that Yisro purchased the animals from Jews — *Yisro miYisrael zavin* — just as in the case of Dama.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וַיֹּאמֶר שָׁאוּל מֵעֲמָלֵקִי הֱבִיאוּם אֲשֶׁר חָמַל הָעָם עַל מֵיטַב הַצֹּאן וְהַבָּקָר (הַמִּשְׁנִים וְהַכָּרִים וְעַל כׇּל הַצֹּאן) לְמַעַן זְבֹחַ לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״! מַאי ״מֵיטַב״? דְּמֵי מֵיטָב. וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵיטָב? כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִיקְפֹּץ עֲלַיְהוּ זָבֹינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further states: **Come** and **hear** another objection from a verse: **"And Saul said: They have brought them from the Amalekites; for the people spared the best of the flock and of the herd, to sacrifice unto the Lord your God"** (I Samuel 15:15). The verse states explicitly that the Israelites intended to sacrifice animals previously owned by gentiles. The Gemara explains: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **"The best"?** This is referring to **the** monetary **value of the best** livestock. The intention was not to sacrifice the animals themselves, but to sell them and use the proceeds of the sale to purchase other animals to sacrifice as offerings. The Gemara inquires: **And what is different** about **the best** animals? If the animals were sold for their value, why sell those animals in particular, rather than several inferior-quality animals? The Gemara explains that they did so **in order that buyers would jump at** the opportunity to buy superior-quality livestock.

The third case is Shaul and the Amalekites. Shmuel confronts Shaul: what is the sound of the sheep and cattle I hear? The people had spared the best of the Amalekite flocks, ostensibly *lema'an zvo'ach l'Hashem Elokecha* — in order to sacrifice them to God. Rabbi Eliezer reads *meitav* creatively: rather than bringing the animals themselves as sacrifices, the plan was to sell them and use the proceeds to purchase sacrificial animals (*ki heichi delikftzu alaihu zvini*). This is candidly a strained reading, but it allows Rabbi Eliezer to avoid a direct contradiction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֲרַוְנָה אֶל דָּוִד יִקַּח וְיַעַל אֲדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ (אֶת) הַטּוֹב בְּעֵינוֹ (וְאֶת) [רְאֵה] הַבָּקָר לָעֹלָה וְהַמֹּרִגִּים וּכְלֵי הַבָּקָר לָעֵצִים״! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אֲרַוְנָה גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another objection from a verse: **"And Araunah said unto David: Let my lord the king take and offer up what seems good unto him; behold the cattle for the burnt-offering, and the threshing instruments [*morigim*] and the accoutrements of the cattle for the wood"** (II Samuel 24:22). Apparently, David was willing to accept oxen as offerings from a gentile. **Rav Naḥman says: Araunah was a gentile who resided in Eretz Yisrael and observed the seven Noahide mitzvot [*ger toshav*].**

The fourth case is Aravna the Jebusite, who offered David his cattle directly for an *olah*. Here Rav Nachman supplies the answer: *Aravna ger toshav hayah* — Aravna was a *ger toshav*, a resident alien who had formally accepted the seven Noahide commandments. The sexual prohibitions are among those laws, and therefore there was no concern that his animals had been subjected to acts of bestiality. This is a relevant distinction: the concern about non-Jewish animals is not about idolaters per se — the Gemara had established on the preceding daf that mere recognition of God does not resolve the concern — but about those who have formally undertaken the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach*, which include the relevant sexual prohibitions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מוֹרִגִּים? אָמַר עוּלָּא: מִטָּה שֶׁל טוּרְבִּיל. מַאי מִטָּה שֶׁל טוּרְבִּיל? עִזָּא דְּקוּרְקְסָא דְּדָיְישָׁן. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי קְרָא? ״הִנֵּה שַׂמְתִּיךְ לְמוֹרַג חָרוּץ חָדָשׁ בַּעַל פִּיפִיּוֹת תָּדוּשׁ הָרִים וְתָדֹק וּגְבָעוֹת כַּמֹּץ תָּשִׂים״.
>
> **Translation:** Tangentially, the Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the term **"*morigim*,"** mentioned in this verse? **Ulla said:** It is **a *turbil* bed.** The Gemara asks: **What** is **a *turbil* bed?** It is **a serrated board [*kurkesa*]** used **for threshing. Rav Yosef said: What is the verse** from which the meaning of *morigim* is derived? It is derived from the verse: **"Behold, I have made you a new threshing board [*morag*] having sharp teeth; you shall thresh the mountains, and beat them small, and shall make the hills as chaff"** (Isaiah 41:15).

The Gemara also pauses to explain the *morigim* — the threshing implements — that Aravna offered alongside his cattle. Ulla explains: a *mita shel Turbil*, a bed of toothed pegs. Rav Yosef locates the term in the verse in Yeshayahu: *hinei samtich lemorag charutz chadash ba'al pipiyot* — behold I have made you like a new sharp threshing sledge full of teeth — and the subsequent verses confirm its use for crushing grain out of husks.

## Age Threshold

### Gevul Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: גְּבוּל יֵשׁ לָהּ, פְּחוּתָה מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים — נֶעֱקֶרֶת, בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים — אֵינָהּ נֶעֱקֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § After concluding its discussion of the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, the Gemara cites another resolution of the contradiction between the mishna and the *baraita*. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: There is** a clear **demarcation** in the case of an animal with whom a man engaged in bestiality. If she is **less than three years** old, **she becomes barren** as a result of penetration, but if she is already **three years** old, **she does not become barren.**

Rabbi Yochanan now introduces a more measured position that occupies a middle ground between Rabbi Eliezer's blanket prohibition and the Rabbis' apparent blanket permission. *Gevul yesh lah* — there is a boundary to the Rabbis' permissive ruling. The Rabbis permit purchasing animals from non-Jews on the reasoning that non-Jews will not subject their own animals to acts of bestiality, since doing so renders the animals infertile and economically worthless. If that is the logic, then it has natural limits defined by age.

### Infertility Logic

The principle Rabbi Yochanan articulates is: *pachot mibat gimmel shanim ne'ekeret* — a female animal under three years old becomes infertile if subjected to such an act. Because non-Jews are motivated by economic self-interest to protect the fertility of their animals, they will not perform such acts on animals younger than three years. It is therefore safe to purchase animals under three years old from non-Jews, since the concern is operationally neutralized.

### Three-Year Cutoff

*Bat gimmel shanim einah ne'ekeret* — once an animal reaches three years, such an act will no longer render it infertile. At that point, the economic deterrent no longer operates, and one must therefore worry that non-Jews will perform such acts with animals of that age. Accordingly, animals three years old and older may not be purchased from non-Jews for sacrificial use.

### Verse Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ כֹּל הָנֵי תְּיוּבָתָא, שַׁנִּי לְהוּ: פְּחוּתָה מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים. תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְאֶת הַפָּרוֹת הֶעֱלוּ עֹלָה לַה׳״ — בִּפְחוּתָה מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **They raised all of those refutations** from the aforementioned verses which indicate that animals purchased from gentiles may be brought as offerings, and **he answered them** by claiming that the animals being offered were **less than three years** old. The Gemara reexamines one of the objections. **Come** and **hear,** as the verse states: **"And they sacrificed the cattle as a burnt-offering unto the Lord"** (I Samuel 6:14). Rabbi Yoḥanan explained that although **in** that incident the cattle had been owned by Philistines, they were **less than three years** old, and it was therefore presumed that the Philistines had not engaged in bestiality with them.

This position is then challenged by all the same verses that challenged Rabbi Eliezer — Moshe's demand of Pharaoh, Yisro's sacrifice, the Amalekite spoils, Aravna's cattle, the Philistine cows. The straightforward answer to the first set of these challenges is simple: *shani lehu pachot mibat gimmel shanim* — in all those cases, the animals involved were under three years old. That resolves the cases. But one case resists this answer.

## Philistine Cows Prob (II)

### Offspring Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן: אִם כֵּן, הַיְינוּ ״וְאֶת בְּנֵיהֶם כָּלוּ בַבָּיִת״ — פְּחוּתָה מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים? וּפְחוּתָה מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים מִי קָא יָלְדָה? וְהָתַנְיָא: פָּרָה וַחֲמוֹר מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ — וַדַּאי לַכֹּהֵן, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a refutation of Rabbi Yoḥanan's answer: **Rav Huna, son of Rabbi Natan, objects to this: If so,** then **this is** also true with regard to the verse: "And they took two nursing cows and tied them to the cart **and shut up their calves at home"** (I Samuel 6:10). According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, the verse is necessarily referring to cows that are **less than three years** old. **And can** a cow that is **less than three years** old **give birth? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a cow or a donkey** purchased from a gentile when they were less **than three years** old, the first of their offspring born after the purchase is **certainly** reserved **for the priest,** who is entitled to the firstborn of a cow or donkey owned by a Jew. **From this** point **forward,** i.e., if they were older than three years at the time of the sale, it is **uncertain** whether or not the offspring is the firstborn.

The Philistine cows present a specific difficulty that the three-year answer cannot address. The biblical narrative specifies that when the Philistines sent the *aron* back on the cart pulled by the cows, their calves were kept penned up at home. The cows, remarkably, did not turn back toward their young but proceeded straight to the Israelite camp. However, if these cows already had calves, they had already given birth — and if they had already given birth, they were necessarily older than three years, since, as the Gemara is about to establish, cows cannot give birth before three years of age. This directly contradicts Rabbi Yochanan's three-year solution.

## Age Threshold (II)

### Case Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְשַׁנִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, עוֹלָה נְקֵבָה מִי אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Rather, it is clear as we initially answered,** i.e., Rabbi Yoḥanan's suggestion is rejected, and the actions in that verse were due to a provisional edict. The Gemara adds: **This also stands to reason, as, if you do not say so,** one can raise a further difficulty with this episode: **Is there a female burnt-offering?**

The Gemara's initial response to the Philistine cow problem is the same answer it gave to the other cases: those cows were under three years old. But this answer immediately generates a contradiction, as the subsequent discussion will show.

## Philistine Cows Prob (II)

### Bechor Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא בְּבָמַת יָחִיד, וְכִדְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, דְּאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: מִנַּיִן לְעוֹלָה נְקֵבָה שֶׁהִיא כְּשֵׁרָה בְּבָמַת יָחִיד? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקַּח שְׁמוּאֵל טְלֵה חָלָב אֶחָד וַיַּעֲלֵהוּ עוֹלָה״. ״וַיַּעֲלֵהוּ״ — זָכָר מַשְׁמַע! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: ״וַיַּעֲלֶהָ״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **And what is the difficulty?** In other words, the additional problem with the incident, that the animals were female, which is cited as proof that there were extenuating circumstances, is not in fact difficult at all. The Gemara elaborates: **Perhaps** the cows were offered **upon a private altar, and** this is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Adda bar Ahava, as Rav Adda bar Ahava says: From where** is it derived **that a female burnt-offering is fit** to be sacrificed **upon a private altar? As it is stated: "And Samuel took a milking lamb, and sacrificed it [*vaya'alehu*] for a burnt-offering** unto the Lord"** (I Samuel 7:9). The Gemara raises a difficulty: But the word ***vaya'alehu*** is masculine, which **means** that the lamb was **a male. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says:** Although the word is read in the masculine, **it is written** in the feminine form, ***vaya'alah*,** which teaches that even a female lamb may be sacrificed on a private altar.

The Gemara cites a *Mishnah*: if a cow or donkey gives birth within its third year (*mibat gimmel*), the offspring is definitively a *bechor* — a firstborn — and goes to a *kohen*. The logic is that, prior to three years of age, a cow cannot have given birth; therefore if she gives birth in her third year, this must be her first birth, and the calf is certainly a *bechor*.

The problem is now fully articulated. If cows cannot give birth before three years — as the *Mishnah* assumes — then any cow that has already had a calf must be at least three years old. The Philistine cows had calves. Therefore they were at least three years old. Therefore Rabbi Yochanan's three-year cutoff cannot be used to rescue this case: these cows were already beyond the age limit.

### Special Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, עַל יְדֵי תַּגָּרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל זְבוּן. וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא בְּבָמַת יָחִיד, וְכִדְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, דְּאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: מִנַּיִן לְעוֹלָה נְקֵבָה שֶׁהִיא כְּשֵׁרָה בְּבָמַת יָחִיד? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקַּח שְׁמוּאֵל טְלֵה חָלָב אֶחָד וַיַּעֲלֵהוּ עוֹלָה״. ״וַיַּעֲלֵהוּ״ — זָכָר מַשְׁמַע! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: ״וַיַּעֲלֶהָ״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** [This follows from the earlier segment discussing the Dama case; see above.]

The Gemara concludes: *ela michvarta kedishinu me'ikara* — the better answer, and the answer we should have stayed with from the beginning, is the one given for Rabbi Eliezer: *hora'at sha'ah hayah* — it was a special divine ruling for that moment, not governed by the ordinary rules. The Gemara confirms this reasoning: *hachi nami mistavra* — this indeed makes sense — because the cows were female and an *olah* ordinarily requires a male animal. The violation of two distinct rules simultaneously — a female *olah*, and an animal from non-Jews over three years old — makes it evident that the entire episode constituted a *hora'at sha'ah*, a temporary divine dispensation.

The Gemara considers whether the female issue alone might be resolved without recourse to *hora'at sha'ah*, by invoking the latitude of *bamot yachid* — private altars, which existed before the *Beit HaMikdash* and permitted females as *olot*, as demonstrated by Rav Adda bar Ahava from the verse about Shmuel's sacrifice. But this avenue is ultimately set aside, and the *hora'at sha'ah* answer stands as the *michvarta* — the clearest and most satisfying resolution.

### Nature Changed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָתַנְיָא: פָּרָה וַחֲמוֹר מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ — וַדַּאי לַכֹּהֵן, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — סָפֵק. אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְשַׁנִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, עוֹלָה נְקֵבָה מִי אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** [Primarily contextual; the full discussion of *nishtanah hateva* emerges from the problematic status of the three-year rule.]

Before moving on, the Gemara's assumption that cows cannot give birth before three years old invites a comment from Tosafot that touches on a methodological issue of broad importance. Tosafot observes that in their own time (*me'aseh bechol yom*) it was entirely routine for a cow of two years to give birth. If so, how can the Gemara's assumption — that birth before three years is impossible — be accepted?

Tosafot's answer invokes the principle of *nishtanah ha'et* — the times have changed. The biological reality of cows in the era of the Gemara was different from what Tosafot observed in their own world. This principle, sometimes formulated as *nishtanah hateva*, is not itself a Talmudic concept but emerged in the Geonic period as a tool for reconciling the empirical claims of the Gemara with observable reality. When the Gemara's descriptions of the natural world diverge from what later authorities saw, two responses are possible: either the rabbis were mistaken about the science, or the natural world has genuinely changed. Rambam held without hesitation that the rabbis were not granted supernatural knowledge in matters of science and could err in empirical claims — *mada'* was not transmitted from Sinai. Many *rishonim*, however, were reluctant to attribute error to the rabbis in areas that bore on *halacha*, and therefore preferred the *nishtanah hateva* answer.

Tosafot here adopts the *nishtanah hateva* approach and cites parallels elsewhere in the Talmud where similar divergences appear. It should be noted that in some cases this answer has genuine plausibility: when the Gemara addresses agricultural or zoological phenomena specific to the climate and fauna of Mesopotamia, *rishonim* living in France or Germany were sometimes dealing with genuinely different sub-species or growing conditions. In other cases — the classic example being the Gemara's permission to kill lice on *Shabbat* on the grounds that they reproduce by spontaneous generation — the *nishtanah hateva* answer strains credulity, since spontaneous generation was never a biological reality. For those cases, the more candid response is that the rabbis accepted the prevailing scientific understanding of their era, which happened to be incorrect.

## Cows' Song

### Vayisharnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּשַּׁרְנָה הַפָּרוֹת בַּדֶּרֶךְ עַל דֶּרֶךְ בֵּית שֶׁמֶשׁ וְגוֹ׳״, מַאי ״וַיִּשַּׁרְנָה״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: שֶׁאָמְרוּ שִׁירָה. וְרַב זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה אָמַר רַב: שֶׁיִּשְּׁרוּ פְּנֵיהֶם כְּנֶגֶד אָרוֹן וְאָמְרוּ שִׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara further analyzes the episode involving the cows sent by the Philistines. The verse states: **"And the cattle took the straight [*vayyisharna*] way, on the way to Beit Shemesh;** they went along the highway, lowing as they went" (I Samuel 6:12). The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the word ***vayyisharna*? Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Meir:** It means **that they recited a song [*shira*]. And Rav Zutra bar Toviyya** says that **Rav says:** It means **that they straightened [*yishru*] their faces** so that they were **opposite the Ark and recited a song.**

Having discussed the Philistine cows at length, the Gemara concludes the daf with a *midrashic* excursus on a single word in that biblical narrative. The verse states: *vayisharnah haparot baderech al derech Beit Shemesh* — the cows went straight (*yashar*) on the road toward Beit Shemesh, bringing the *aron*. The Gemara reads *vayisharnah* not merely as "going straight" but through a wordplay on *shirah* — song. The cows, on this reading, opened their mouths and sang as they pulled the *aron*.

### Song Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שִׁירָה אָמְרוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: ״אָז יָשִׁיר מֹשֶׁה וּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דִּידֵיהּ אָמַר: ״וַאֲמַרְתֶּם בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא הוֹדוּ לַה׳ קִרְאוּ בִשְׁמוֹ וְגוֹ׳״. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מִזְמוֹרָא יַתְמָא, ״מִזְמוֹר שִׁירוּ לַה׳ שִׁיר חָדָשׁ כִּי נִפְלָאוֹת עָשָׂה הוֹשִׁיעָה לּוֹ יְמִינוֹ וּזְרוֹעַ קׇדְשׁוֹ״. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: ״ה׳ מָלָךְ יִרְגְּזוּ עַמִּים״. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: ״ה׳ מָלָךְ גֵּאוּת לָבֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what song did they recite? Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Meir:** They recited the song that follows the verse: **"Then sang Moses and the children of Israel** this song unto the Lord" (Exodus 15:1). **And Rabbi Yoḥanan himself says** that it was: **"And on that day shall you say: Give thanks unto the Lord, proclaim His name,** declare His doings among the peoples, make mention that His name is exalted" (Isaiah 12:4). **And Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says** that it was **an orphaned psalm,** i.e., a psalm whose author and the event to which it makes reference are not specified. The psalm begins with: **"A Psalm. O sing unto the Lord a new song, for He has done marvelous things; His right hand, and His holy arm, have wrought salvation for Him"** (Psalms 98:1). **Rabbi Elazar says** that it was the psalm beginning with: **"The Lord reigns; let the peoples tremble"** (Psalms 99:1). **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that it was the Psalm beginning: **"The Lord reigns; He is clothed in majesty"** (Psalms 93:1).

A dispute breaks out among the Tannaim over what song the cows sang. Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Meir says they sang *Az yashir Moshe u'vnei Yisrael* — the Song of the Sea, the great song of triumph over Egypt's army. This reading emphasizes the defeat of an enemy: just as the Egyptians suffered for oppressing Israel, so the Philistines suffered for seizing the *aron*.

Rabbi Yochanan himself (in his own name, as opposed to in the name of Rabbi Meir) says they sang *Va'amartem bayom hahu hodu l'Hashem kiru bishmo* — "And you shall say on that day: give thanks to God, call upon His Name." This is a song of thanksgiving for salvation rather than a song of victory over enemies.

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says they sang what he calls a *mizmor yasom* — an orphan psalm. This is a psalm in *Tehillim* that lacks the standard introductory attribution: it begins neither with *Mizmor l'David* nor *livnei Korach* nor any statement of authorship or purpose. It launches directly into its content. The *mizmor* in question — *Mizmor shiru l'Hashem shir chadash ki niflaot asa* — celebrates God's wondrous deeds and salvation, again emphasizing divine praise.

Rabbi Eliezer says the song was *Hashem malach yirgezu amim* — God reigns, let the nations tremble. Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani says it was *Hashem malach ge'ut lavesh* — God reigns, He has clothed Himself in majesty.

### Nappacha's Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אָמַר: ״רוֹנִּי רוֹנִּי הַשִּׁיטָה, הִתְנוֹפְפִי בְּרוֹב הֲדָרֵךְ, הַמְחוּשֶּׁקֶת בְּרִיקְמֵי זָהָב, הַמְהוּלָּלָה בִּדְבִיר אַרְמוֹן, וּמְפוֹאָרָה בַּעֲדִי עֲדָיִים״.
>
> **Translation:** [From daf 24b, segment 17] **Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa says:** They did not recite a verse found in the Bible, but rather, the following song: **Sing, sing, acacia; ascend in all your glory; overlaid with golden embroidery, exalted by the book [*devir*] of the palace, and magnificent with jewels.** The song alludes to the Ark of the Covenant, which was made of acacia wood and covered with gold. The expression: Book of the palace, is a reference to the Torah scroll that was placed in the Ark.

Rabbi Yitzchak Nappacha's version stands apart from all the others, as he does not cite an existing verse from *Tanach* but rather a distinct poetic composition. The song he attributes to the cows — or, as we will shortly see, to the Israelites — reads as follows: *Roni roni hashitah* — sing, sing, O acacia tree, from which the *aron* was fashioned. *Yitnofi berov hadrech* — be exalted in your magnificent glory. *Hamuchsheket berikmei zahav* — adorned with golden filigree. *Hamehulala bidvir armon* — praised in the *dvir armon*, the inner sanctuary. *Umefuara ba'adi adiyim* — and glorified with precious jewels.

The phrase *dvir armon* typically refers to the inner sanctuary of the *Beit HaMikdash*, but since the *Beit HaMikdash* had not yet been built at this point in the narrative, the Gemara will resolve this by reading *dvir* in a different sense — which leads directly into the etymological excursus at the daf's conclusion.

### Rav Ashi's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי מַתְנֵי לַהּ לְהָא דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק, אַהָא ״וַיְהִי בִּנְסֹעַ הָאָרֹן וַיֹּאמֶר מֹשֶׁה קוּמָה ה׳״, יִשְׂרָאֵל מַאי אָמְרוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: ״רוֹנִּי רוֹנִּי הַשִּׁיטָה״ וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** [From daf 24b, segment 18] **Rav Ashi teaches this** statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak in relation to this** verse: **"And it came to pass, when the Ark set forward, that Moses said: Rise up, O Lord,** and let Your enemies be scattered" (Numbers 10:35). The Gemara asks: **What did the Jewish people recite** at this juncture? **Rabbi Yitzḥak says** that they recited: **Sing, sing, acacia,** ascend in all your glory; overlaid with golden embroidery, exalted by the book of the palace, and magnificent with jewels.

Rav Ashi clarifies that the song attributed to Rabbi Yitzchak Nappacha was not, in fact, the song the cows sang. It was the song the Israelites sang. The verse *vayehi binsoa ha'aron vayomer Moshe* — "And when the *aron* set out, Moshe would say, *Kuma Hashem*" — describes Moshe's words as the *aron* began to move. The Gemara then asks: what did the Israelites say in response? Rabbi Yitzchak Nappacha's poetic composition, on this reading, was the Israel people's song of greeting to the *aron*, not the cows' song. The cows' song is the subject of the Tannaitic dispute above; Rabbi Yitzchak's song belongs to a different occasion entirely.

### Persian Etymology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: כְּמַאן קָרוּ פָּרְסָאֵי לְסָפְרָא ״דְּבִיר״? מֵהָכָא, ״וְשֵׁם דְּבִיר לְפָנִים קִרְיַת סֵפֶר״. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כְּמַאן קָרוּ פָּרְסָאֵי לְנִידָּה ״דַּשְׁתָּנָא״? מֵהָכָא, ״כִּי דֶרֶךְ נָשִׁים לִי״.
>
> **Translation:** [From daf 24b, segments 19–20] § Apropos the mention of the term *devir*, the Gemara discusses its etymology. **Rav said: On what** basis **do the Persians call a book [*sifra*]** by the term ***devir*?** They derive it **from here: "Now the name of Debir [*devir*] beforehand was Kiriath Sefer"** (Judges 1:11). Since the name *devir* was changed to Kiriath Sefer, the Persians referred to a *sifra*, i.e., a book, as *devir*. The Gemara examines the etymology of another term coined by the Persians. **Rav Ashi said: On what** basis **do the Persians call a menstruating woman** by the term ***dashtana*?** It is **from here,** a verse in which Rachel claims to be a menstruating woman: **"For the manner of women is upon me [*derekh nashim li*]"** (Genesis 31:35). The word *dashtana* is a shortened form of the phrase *derekh nashim*.

Having read *dvir armon* in Rabbi Yitzchak's song as referring not to the Temple chamber but to something written — the *luchos* or the Torah scroll housed in the *aron* — the Gemara pauses for a brief etymological note. Rav asks: on what basis did the Persians adopt the word *dvir* to mean a book? The Gemara answers: *meihacha* — from the verse *veshem haDvir lifnim Kiryat Sefer* — "and the former name of Dvir was Kiryat Sefer." Since *sefer* means a book and the place was also called *Dvir*, the word *dvir* acquired the connotation of a written text. This explains how Persian came to use *dvir* for a book.

Rav Ashi appends a parallel question: why did the Persians call a *niddah* — a menstruant woman — *dashtana*? The answer is traced to the verse in which Rachel says to Lavan, *ki derech nashim li* — "for the way of women is upon me." The word *dashtana* is linked to *derech nashim* through shared consonants. The Gemara notes that this Persian term for a menstruant woman was indeed used in some of the Geonic literature as an alternative term for *niddah*, preserving the Aramaic-Persian linguistic bridge that the Talmud here traces to a biblical source.
