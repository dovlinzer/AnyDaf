# Niddah 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Seven Cleansers Intro

### Opening & Context

Today's daf is 62. We begin at the very bottom of 61a — actually the *mishnah* on 61b. In that *mishnah* we had an extended discussion about *tolah*, attributing a stain to another cause. Somewhat unconnected to that discussion, the *mishnah* also presents the major third leniency for *ketamim*: the role of color.

Until now we had seen two such leniencies. The first concerns size — the *gris*, the minimum threshold of a grain. The second concerns the nature of the garment or vessel: one that is not *mekabel tumah*, that cannot become *tamei*, does not generate a stain problem. And on the previous daf we saw the third leniency — that on non-white, colored clothing a stain is not a problem — though without any explicit reason being given in the text. One can speculate that it has to do with the ability to identify the color, though the *mishnah* itself does not say so. Interestingly, it was noted historically that although there was concern about permitting colored clothing in the period following the *Churban* — because it might seem too festive — the Sages specifically allowed it precisely because it would serve as a leniency regarding stains. Notably, all three of these criteria also appear in the laws of *nega'im*, the different system of spotting and staining discussed elsewhere, which is interesting to observe even if the *mishnah* does not explain the connection.

We now continue with our *mishnah* at the bottom of 61b, which turns to the question of how to eliminate a stain. The *mishnah*'s interest here is not in how to restore a garment to a presentable appearance, but in using the removal process as a method for determining what *kind* of stain we are dealing with.

### Seven Substances

The *mishnah* states: *shiv'ah sammanim ma'avirin al ha-ketem* — one passes, meaning one applies and rubs, seven types of cleansing substances over the stain. They are as follows: *rok tafel*, tasteless spit, meaning spit from a person who has not eaten recently; *mei grisin*, water from beans — specifically from the chewing of a certain type of bean whose peel fell off naturally rather than by human hand; *mei raglayim*, urine that has turned rancid; *neter*, a form of lye; *borit*, another classic cleanser; *kammoniya*, a particular substance of uncertain nature; and *ashlag*, similarly obscure.

Regarding *neter*, the *gemara* specifies *neter Alexandri* — the Alexandrian variety — and explicitly excludes *neter Antipatris*. The distinction is significant: Alexandria was a major scientific and commercial center, and its *neter* was prized as the genuine article, while the Antipatris variety, produced in southern Judea — the city named for the father of Herod — was considered an inferior local imitation. The situation is not unlike the difference between a name-brand product and a poor substitute.

### Substance Etymology

*Kammoniya* is a Greek word — *Kimolia* — referring to some kind of white substance or earth, named after the island where it was first used. It may correspond to what is known in Arabic as *al-kali*, possibly saltwort, a group of plants that grow in salt marshes and alkaline soils. *Ashlag* is a Persian word for what appears to be the ashes of grass or plants. The *gemara* records that Shmuel investigated this substance by asking seafarers (*nachutei yamma*) who told him it is called *ashlaga* and is found in the crevices of pearl-forming shells, removable only with an iron instrument — which raises obvious practical questions about accessibility.

As for *neter*, it appears to be sodium carbonate, which functions as a water softener and facilitates cleaning. *Borit* is more complicated. Rav Yehuda identifies it as *ahla*, another classic cleanser mentioned in Tanakh. But the *gemara* immediately challenges this from a *baraita* that lists *borit* and *ahla* as two separate items. So perhaps *borit* is *kavrissa* — sulfur, as Rashi suggests. But that too is challenged: the *mishnah* in *Shevi'it* includes *borit* among substances to which the laws of *shemitah* apply, and only things that grow from the ground are subject to *shemitah*, whereas sulfur is a mineral. The *gemara* therefore returns to identifying *borit* as *ahla* and resolves the apparent duplication by positing that there are two varieties of *ahla*. In any case, all of these substances function as soap-like cleansers, and the *mishnah* employs them not for their cosmetic utility but for their evidentiary value in *halacha*.

## Mishnah Scenario

### Stain Doesn't Remove

How is all of this halachically relevant? The *mishnah* presents the following scenario. A woman found a stain on her garment, and the garment — now rendered *tamei* by the presumed blood — was immersed in a *mikveh*. After immersion, *tehorot* were placed upon it — say, *terumah* grain resting on the garment. Subsequently, the seven cleansers were applied to the stain: *he'evir alav shiv'ah sammanim v'lo avar* — and the stain did not come out. In this case, *harei zeh tzeva* — this is a [non-blood] dye. The fact that the seven cleansers failed to remove the stain is taken as evidence that it is not a bloodstain, because these substances would remove blood but not necessarily other colored matter. Therefore, everything is permitted — *tahorot tahorot* — and there is no need to re-immerse.

### Stain Removes/Pales

If, however, after applying the seven cleansers the stain did come out — *avar* — or even if it merely paled — *she-diheh* — then *harei zeh ketem*, this is indeed a stain. Now a problem emerges. Although the garment was immersed in the *mikveh*, the stain was still present on it at the time of immersion. A stain does not cease to be *tamei* simply because the garment was brought into a *mikveh* — just as a *sheretz* placed in a *mikveh* does not become *tahor*. The proper procedure is to remove the stain *before* immersion and then immerse the now-clean garment. In this case, the stain remained during immersion, and therefore *ve-ha-tahorot teme'ot* — the *terumah* items are *tamei* — and one must now immerse the garment again, now that the stain has been removed.

The *mishnah* operates in the world of *tumah ve-taharah*. This was a lived reality: in the household of a *kohen*, or even for someone who was careful to eat *chullin be-taharah*, any garment or vessel that a *niddah* had contact with would have to be kept separate or immersed before being used in connection with food or *terumah*. Wooden vessels, garments, tables — all could become *tamei* and in principle require *tevilah*, though there are categories of large immovable objects that fall under a different legal framework.

The evidentiary logic is straightforward: whether a stain comes out or not with these particular substances is the test of whether it is blood. If it comes out, we know it was blood, which retroactively shows that the stain was present during immersion and that the immersion was therefore insufficient. If it does not come out, we take that as evidence that it was not blood, and the prior immersion stands.

### Implicit Assumptions

Reading the *mishnah* closely reveals several implicit assumptions worth flagging. The first is that one must remove the stain before immersion — the *mishnah*'s case is built on a scenario where this was not done, which is precisely what generates the problem in the second case. The second assumption, more subtle, is embedded in the language *she-diheh* — that paling counts as a meaningful stage of removal. This raises the question: is it necessary for the stain to disappear entirely, or is there a point at which sufficient paling renders the stain halachically "no longer present"? The *mishnah* implies that even paling is legally significant, and this is something the *gemara* will develop at length.

There is also a third implicit point worth noting. If we read the *mishnah* at face value, the non-removal of the stain by the seven cleansers proves *definitively* that it is not blood. But if that is so, why was immersion necessary at all? If we knew it was not blood even before immersion, the garment should have been permitted without any *mikveh*. One possible reading is that the *mishnah* is simply presenting the extreme version of the second case — emphasizing that even having immersed, one has not helped the situation if the stain was blood. But *Tosafot* raises the deeper question: perhaps the failure of the cleansers is not absolute proof of non-blood status, but only strong evidence. In that reading, the combination of factors — the likelihood that it is not blood, plus the prior immersion — together produces the leniency. This question of whether the cleansers *prove* the stain is not blood or merely *indicate* it will drive much of the *gemara*'s subsequent discussion.

## Seven Cleansers Intro…

### Application Method

The *mishnah* now specifies how these substances must be applied: *vetzarich lechacheish shalosh pe'amim lechol echad ve'echad* — one must rub three times with each of the seven substances in sequence. Rashi describes the rubbing motion as *shiruv*, working the two parts of the garment against each other. If the substances were applied out of order — *he'eviran shelo ke-sidran* — or if all seven were applied simultaneously — *she-he'evir shiv'ah sammanim ke'echad* — the process is worthless: *lo asa ve-lo klum*. The application must follow this specific prescribed sequence, each substance applied individually three times.

This level of precision is striking, and one might legitimately question whether this procedure was ever widely followed in practice. The requirement of seven very specific substances, applied in a precise order, three rubbings each, raises practical obstacles. If one did not avail oneself of these cleansers, the garment would presumably remain in its halachically ambiguous state — not that the law was "ignored," but simply that the procedure was difficult enough that it may have remained largely in the theoretical realm.

At this point it is worth returning to a detail from the *mishnah* about *mei grisin* — the bean water. The specification that the bean's peel fell off naturally, *chalukat nefesh*, rather than being removed by a person, and that the bean itself be of a particular variety, reflects the high degree of specificity governing these substances. Similarly, *mei raglayim* must be rancid — *she-hechmitzu* — rather than fresh urine.

The *gemara* also revisits the identification of *borit*. As noted, *Rav Yehuda* says it is *ahla*, and the *gemara* reconciles the apparent conflict with the *baraita* that lists them separately by positing two varieties of *ahla* — *trei gavnei ahla*. This taxonomic exercise reflects the general pattern here: the Sages were working to identify substances that were known in practice but described in the *mishnah* only by traditional names, some of Greek or Persian origin, whose precise identification had become uncertain.

The broader question of whether this procedure ever had widespread practical application is worth holding. In a world where one needed seven specific substances, applied in a particular order, with three rubbings each — and where failure to follow the procedure exactly rendered it void — it seems likely that many households simply could not access or prepare all seven in the proper form. The halakhic principle was articulated, its procedure codified, but its real-world implementation may have been limited.

## Tsafon & Proofs

### Tsafon Problem

A *baraita* now introduces the substance *tsafon*, which was apparently a particularly powerful cleanser. The case: the seven cleansers were applied, the stain did not come out, and then *tsafon* was applied and it *did* come out. What is the halachic status?

One might think that if the stain came out with *tsafon*, this proves it was blood — the *tsafon* revealed what the seven substances could not. The *gemara* rejects this reasoning: *tsafon nami ma'avar* — *tsafon* removes non-blood stains as well. Because *tsafon* is so powerful, the fact that it removed the stain cannot serve as evidence that the stain was blood. It would remove anything.

The *gemara* therefore reframes the *baraita*. The case is not that all seven were applied and then *tsafon* was added as an eighth. Rather: six of the seven were applied and the stain did not come out; then *tsafon* was used in place of the seventh substance, and it came out. In this scenario, one cannot claim that the failure of the first six proves it is not blood — perhaps had the proper seventh substance been used, the stain would have come out. And the fact that *tsafon* removed it proves nothing, since *tsafon* would remove anything. Therefore the status remains *tamei*, because the seven-substance procedure was never properly completed.

### Second Application

A second *baraita* addresses a different scenario: the seven cleansers were applied and the stain did not come out — at which point, as we have learned, this is evidence that it is not blood. The substances were then applied a second time — *shanan ve-avar* — and this time the stain did come out. Does this retroactively problematize what was done between the first and second application?

The *baraita* rules: *tahorotav tehorot* — everything remains permitted. The fact that the stain did not come out the first time constitutes sufficient evidence that it is not blood, and the subsequent removal on the second attempt does not undermine that initial determination.

## Kfida/Concern

### First Laundering

Rabbi Zeira now introduces a significant qualification that adds a new dimension to this analysis. The ruling that *tahorotav tehorot* applies only to *tehorot* that were used between the first laundering and the second — that is, during the window after the first application of cleansers failed and before the second application was attempted. If the first laundering did not remove the stain, the woman used the garment for *tahor* purposes, and only then laundered it again — all is well for the period of use between the two launderings.

### Second Laundering

However, if *tehorot* were prepared *after* the second laundering — after the stain had already been removed — *tahorotav teme'ot*. This seems almost paradoxical: the stain is now gone, yet the items are *tamei*. Why? *She-harei hikpid alav ve-avar* — the fact that she laundered it a second time demonstrates that she was concerned about the stain, that she regarded it as still present. The very act of attempting to remove it a second time shows that she treated it as an existing, problematic stain.

This introduces an entirely new halakhic category: *makpid* — being concerned about, investing effort to remove — as distinct from *mevatel* — effectively nullifying the stain by no longer treating it as something that exists. Whether one is *makpid* or *mevatel* now emerges as an independent factor, separate from the question of whether it is blood or not blood.

### Status Paradox

The most striking aspect of Rabbi Zeira's ruling is the apparent on-and-off nature of the stain's halakhic status. Between the first and second launderings, the stain was treated as non-present — the evidence of the first failed laundering rendered it presumably not blood, and the woman did not concern herself with it. During that period, things were *tahor*. Then she laundered a second time, showed renewed *kfida*, and the stain came out — retroactively restoring it, it seems, to the status of a problematic stain from that point forward.

The question is genuinely troubling: if the garment had already achieved *tahor* status based on the first laundering's failure, how can a subsequent act of concern transform something that was *tahor* back into something *tamei*? The *gemara* seems to be saying that the stain ceased to have halakhic reality during the period of non-concern, but as soon as renewed concern was demonstrated — as soon as the woman showed she still regarded it as a stain by laundering again — it regained its identity as a stain from that point forward, requiring a renewed *tevilah* now that it has been removed. This is an extremely unusual mode of halakhic reasoning, and the *gemara* acknowledges its counterintuitive nature.

## Definite Blood

### Rabbi Chiyya

Rabbi Abba asks Rav Ashi: can it really be that the determination depends entirely on *kfida* — on what one happens to care about? Rav Ashi responds affirmatively, citing a *baraita* of Rabbi Chiyya. Rabbi Chiyya's teaching is this: *dam ha-niddah vadai* — even if the blood is definitively identified as *niddah* blood — if the seven cleansers are applied and it is rendered pale, and the woman is *mevatel* it — is not concerned about it — then it loses its halakhic status.

This is a remarkable extension. Until this point, the whole framework of the seven cleansers was understood as a mechanism for determining whether a stain *is* blood or not. Here, Rabbi Chiyya says that even when there is no doubt that it *is* blood — *vadai* — the combination of making it pale and not being concerned about it causes it to cease being halachically considered as blood.

It is important to note that the *mevatel* process is not purely subjective. It is not that any subjective attitude transforms the status. The stain must have been worked on with the seven substances, brought to a sufficiently pale state, and only then — at that point — the additional factor of *bitul*, of non-concern, comes into play.

### Two Issues

The *gemara* has now clarified that there are in fact two distinct issues at work in this entire sugya, and they must be carefully distinguished. The first issue is evidentiary: can we prove from the behavior of the stain under these cleansers that it is not blood at all? This was the framework of the *mishnah*, which presented the seven cleansers as a test for the nature of the substance. The second issue — introduced through Rabbi Zeira and now made explicit by Rabbi Chiyya — is independent of that evidentiary question: even granted that it *is* blood, can the combination of paling and non-concern remove its halakhic status?

These two issues produce different analyses. The first is about identification of the substance; the second is about the threshold at which an identified substance stops being considered halachically "present." The rest of the *gemara* turns on precisely this distinction.

## Reish Lakish Question

### Pottery Vessel Case

The *gemara* now brings a *mishnah* from *Kelim* regarding pottery vessels — *charasin* — that were used by a *zav* and absorbed his bodily fluids, which are an *av ha-tumah*. These vessels then fell into the airspace of an oven — *naflu la-avir ha-tanur* — and the oven was subsequently heated. The fluids are no longer visibly present in the vessels; they have been absorbed into the pottery walls. The *mishnah* rules: *ha-tanur tamei* — the oven is *tamei* — because *sof mashkeh la-tzet* — the absorbed fluids will ultimately come out when the oven is heated, at which point they will render the oven's airspace *tamei*.

### Reish Lakish Position

Reish Lakish analyzes this ruling with an important distinction. The rule applies, he says, only when the fluids involved are *mashkin kalim* — lighter fluids, meaning those that carry only a first-degree (*rishon*) impurity rather than being an *av ha-tumah* themselves, such as blood or breast milk that came into contact with a *zav* or *zavah*. In that case, the oven's *tumah* is only *de-rabbanan*, since a *rishon* cannot Biblically render a vessel *tamei* — only an *av* can do so. For such *mashkin kalim*, one waits for them to actually emerge before treating the oven as *tamei*. But for *mashkin chamurim* — the primary fluids of the *zav* such as urine or spit, which are themselves an *av ha-tumah* — *af al pi she-lo husak ha-tanur* — even if the oven was not fired up and the fluids have not emerged — the oven is already *tamei*. Reish Lakish's position is that fluids absorbed in the walls of a vessel are considered "present" as long as they are capable of emerging. Even while still embedded in the pottery, they constitute an *av ha-tumah* in the airspace of the oven.

## R. Yochanan Response

### Light vs. Heavy

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. He does not accept Reish Lakish's distinction between *mashkin kalim* and *mashkin chamurim*. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the rule is the same for both: *im husak ha-tanur hen, ve-im lav lav* — if the oven was fired up and the fluids are on their way out, yes; if not, no. The absorbed state, regardless of the Biblical severity of the fluids, does not constitute "presence" in the halakhic sense. For Rabbi Yochanan, the absorbed fluids do not render the oven *tamei* until they actually emerge.

### Heated Vessel

The debate, then, is whether absorption in the walls of a vessel constitutes halakhic "presence" of the substance. Reish Lakish holds that if the fluids are Biblically significant and capable of emerging, they are considered present even while absorbed. Rabbi Yochanan holds that actual emergence — or at least imminent emergence under heating — is required before they are considered present.

### Mishna vs. Baraita

Rabbi Yochanan then challenges Reish Lakish from our *mishnah*: the case where the seven cleansers were applied, the stain did not come out, and the *tehorot* are permitted. If we accept Reish Lakish's position — that absorbed substances remain "present" — how can this *mishnah* permit? The stain is still in the garment; it simply did not respond to the cleansers. According to Reish Lakish, it should still be considered present and problematic.

The *gemara* answers on behalf of Reish Lakish: *hanicha li-ketamim de-rabbanan* — this is only *ketamim*, which are Rabbinic in nature, and for *de-rabbanan* matters one may be lenient. This answer raises the deeper question of what the *mishnah*'s permission is actually based on. Is it based on the certainty that the stain is not blood? Or is it based on the combination of uncertainty (maybe not blood) and non-emergence (what is there is not coming out)? The *gemara* at this point suggests that even for Rabbi Yochanan, the *mishnah*'s leniency may rest partly on the Rabbinic status of *ketamim*, not solely on the evidentiary value of the cleansers.

This is precisely the question that *Tosafot* grapples with at length. The *mishnah* may not be asserting that non-removal definitively proves non-blood status. Rather, it may be that the combination of the likelihood that it is not blood, together with the principle that what is not emerging is not considered present — at least at the Rabbinic level — produces the leniency.

## Rashi Dual Argument

### Not Blood Proof

Rashi offers a two-pronged account of why the *mishnah* permits in the case where the stain did not come out. The first argument is straightforward: the failure of the seven cleansers to remove the stain indicates that it is probably not blood. Blood responds to these cleansers; a different substance might not. So the non-removal constitutes evidence, perhaps strong evidence, that what we are dealing with is not blood at all.

### Not Present Argument

Rashi's second argument is distinct and more subtle. Even if the stain *is* blood, the fact that it could not be removed by the seven cleansers demonstrates that whatever remains in the garment is not emerging — it is deeply embedded, inaccessible through normal cleansing efforts. And if it is not coming out, it is not considered halachically "present." This second argument mirrors the broader debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish about absorbed substances: Rabbi Yochanan's position is that what cannot emerge is not considered present, and Rashi applies that principle here to explain the *mishnah*'s leniency.

The two arguments thus operate on different levels. The first says: the cleansers tell us something about what the substance *is*. The second says: the cleansers tell us something about the substance's *accessibility*, which determines its halachic status regardless of what it is. Rashi is in effect offering both as complementary grounds for the *mishnah*'s ruling.

This double argument creates the structure through which to understand the Rabbi Yochanan–Reish Lakish debate. The *mishnah* works, according to Rabbi Yochanan, because either or both of these factors apply: the stain may not be blood, and/or what remains is not considered present. Reish Lakish, by contrast, maintains that for a Biblically significant substance, absorbed status does not negate presence — the substance is still there, still capable of emerging under sufficient conditions, and therefore still a problem. The *mishnah*'s leniency, on his account, must be limited to the Rabbinic realm of *ketamim*.

The further question raised by all of this is about the intermediate cases — what if some of the stain came out and some did not, or what if it paled but did not disappear? The *mishnah* explicitly contrasts the case of no change with the case of coming out or paling, which implies that the first case involves no change whatsoever. If nothing at all changed — the stain neither came out nor paled — then one can argue either that it is not blood (since blood responds to these cleansers) or that whatever is there is deeply embedded and not coming out. But if there was some change — even paling — the stain showed a response to the cleansers, which is evidence that it is blood, and one cannot rely on the "not present" argument either, since some portion of it was mobile enough to respond.

## Presence Debate

### Blood in House

The *gemara* now brings another case to probe the debate: *revi'is dam she-nivle'ah ba-bayit* — a *revi'is* (approximately three and a half fluid ounces) of blood that was absorbed into a house. This is the quantity of blood from a corpse that causes *tumas ohel* — the impurity of overhanging. If this blood is absorbed into, say, the floorboards of a house, is the house *tamei*? One version says yes — *ha-bayit tamei*; another version says no — *ha-bayit tahor*.

### Temporal Resolution

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction with a temporal distinction: *ha bi-kheilim de-me'ikara ha bi-kheilim de-vasof* — the two rulings refer to different stages in time. The ruling that the house is *tamei* refers to vessels or items that were in the house before the blood was fully absorbed — they were exposed to the blood while it was still in the open, and they are indeed *tamei*. The ruling that the house is *tahor* refers to items brought into the house after the blood was already absorbed into the floor — at that point, since the blood is no longer in the open airspace, new items brought in are not affected. This resolution supports Rabbi Yochanan's position: once the blood is absorbed, it is no longer "present" for the purpose of affecting subsequently introduced items.

### Blood in Garment

The *gemara* then cites a related case: blood absorbed in a garment — *nivle'ah bi-khsut*. The rule stated is: *im mitkabeset ve-yotzei mimmenah revi'is dam teme'ah* — if a normal laundering would cause a *revi'is* of blood to emerge from the garment, the garment is *tamei*; *ve-im lav tehorah* — if not, it is *tahor*. At first glance, this seems to support Reish Lakish: the fact that the blood *could* come out — even before it actually does — renders the garment *tamei*.

### Easy vs. Difficult

The *Rosh* resolves this in favor of Rabbi Yochanan by focusing on the word *mitkabeset* — normal laundering. The case refers to blood that would come out through an ordinary, easy laundering process. In such a case, even Rabbi Yochanan agrees that the blood is still considered present — it is readily accessible and not deeply embedded. Rabbi Yochanan's leniency applies specifically to substances that only emerge under conditions of great difficulty — such as firing an oven, or using the extraordinary cleanser *tsafon*, or extensive rigorous treatment. In such cases, Rabbi Yochanan holds the substance is not considered present. But blood that a simple laundering would release — that is considered present by everyone, since it is effectively still "surface-accessible."

The Reish Lakish–Rabbi Yochanan debate, properly understood, concerns only the middle category: substances that have been absorbed deeply enough that simple methods will not remove them, yet remain theoretically extractable through more intensive means. Does that theoretical extractability constitute "presence"? Reish Lakish says yes; Rabbi Yochanan says no — at least when the Biblical status is not fully at stake.

The garment case is then explained as *de-rabbanan* as well — the blood in question is *dam tevusah*, the blood that emerges during the dying process, which may have exited the body while the person was still alive. Since its status as *metamei be-ohel* is only Rabbinic, the leniency of "easy laundering" as the threshold is calibrated for the Rabbinic realm, not necessarily for fully Biblical impurity.

## Rav Papa Formula

### Cannot Emerge

Rav Papa now synthesizes the debate into a clean three-part formula. The first case: the substance cannot come out and one is not concerned about it — *eino yakhol la-tzet ve-lo hikpid alav*. In this case, *divrei ha-kol tahor* — everyone, including Reish Lakish, agrees that it is *tahor*. Even Reish Lakish only argued that absorbed substances are "present" when they are at least theoretically capable of emerging. If emergence is not possible even in principle, there is nothing to debate.

### Can Emerge & Concerned

The second case: the substance can come out, and one *is* concerned about it — *yakhol la-tzet ve-hikpid alav*. Here too, *divrei ha-kol tamei* — everyone agrees it is *tamei*. If the substance is accessible enough to emerge and the owner is actively trying to get it out — demonstrating through their own conduct that they regard it as present and significant — then there is no basis for leniency.

### Can Emerge & Not Concern…

The third and contested case: the substance can come out — but only through difficult extraction — and one is *not* concerned about it, is *mevatel* it. This is where Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan part ways. Reish Lakish holds: *keivan de-yakhol la-tzet af al gav de-lo hikpid alav* — since there is a way to extract it, regardless of the owner's indifference, it remains physically considered present, and the garment remains *tamei*. Rabbi Yochanan holds: *af al gav de-yakhol la-tzet, im hikpid alav in, i lo lo* — even if it can come out, only if one is concerned about it does it count; if one is not concerned, it does not.

This final formulation reconnects to the teaching of Rabbi Chiyya that was introduced earlier. According to Rabbi Yochanan's position, even definitively identified *niddah* blood, once it has been brought to a sufficiently pale state through the seven cleansers and one no longer regards it as a present stain — *mevatel* it — ceases to be considered a halachically existing substance. It is at that point, once extraction would require great effort and the owner is indifferent to the effort, that Rabbi Yochanan holds the substance is no longer "there" in any meaningful halakhic sense, and the garment or vessel is *tahor*.
