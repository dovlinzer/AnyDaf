# Ketubot 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wife's Labor Ownership

### Intro: Labor Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּקְדִּישׁ מַעֲשֵׂה יְדֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ — הֲרֵי זוֹ עוֹשָׂה וְאוֹכֶלֶת. הַמּוֹתָר, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: הֶקְדֵּשׁ, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הַסַּנְדְּלָר אוֹמֵר: חוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If one consecrates his wife's earnings,** meaning anything she produces, such as thread that she spins from wool, which, according to the Sages' ordinance, belongs to her husband, **she may work and sustain** herself from her earnings, as the consecration is ineffective. However, there is a dispute with regard to **the surplus,** meaning any earnings she produces in excess of the amount she is required to produce for her husband. **Rabbi Meir says:** The surplus is **consecrated** property, and **Rabbi Yoḥanan the Cobbler says:** The surplus is also **non-sacred.**

The daf opens by returning to a discussion begun at the bottom of 58b. The *mishnah* establishes that if a husband attempts to sanctify the labor of his wife's hands, the act is void. The *gemara* explored two distinct reasons for this ruling, both of which turn on the foundational question: who is fundamentally the owner of the wife's labor?

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

Two approaches emerge. Rashi understands that since the wife retains the power to refuse her husband's support — opting out of the arrangement by which he feeds her in exchange for her labor — she is considered the fundamental owner, and his ownership never fully takes hold, even when she does not in fact refuse. Tosafot, by contrast, limits this reasoning: only when she actually refuses to accept support from him does ownership revert to her; so long as the arrangement is operative, he controls her labor. A second approach altogether frames the case differently — it is the husband who is in control, but who has chosen to forfeit his claim by failing to provide for her, and it is this forfeiture that strips him of entitlement.

### Motar: Meir vs. Yochanan

The closing section of the *mishnah* presents a dispute regarding the *motar* — the amount that the wife produces beyond the minimum she is obligated to provide. Rabbi Meir holds that if the husband sanctifies this excess, it is *kadosh*; Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar holds it is not. The *gemara* now turns to examine what precisely underlies this disagreement.

## Rav & Shmuel View

### After Death Kiddushin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לָא תֵּימָא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר אָדָם מַקְדִּישׁ דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם. אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְכוּפָּהּ לְמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ — נַעֲשָׂה כְּאוֹמֵר לָהּ "יִקְדְּשׁוּ יָדַיִךְ לְעוֹשֵׂיהֶם". וְהָא לָא אֲמַר לַהּ הָכִי! כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר: אֵין אָדָם מוֹצִיא דְּבָרָיו לְבַטָּלָה — נַעֲשֶׂה כְּאוֹמֵר לָהּ "יִקְדְּשׁוּ יָדַיִךְ לְעוֹשֵׂיהֶם".
>
> **Translation:** This opinion of Rav Huna **disputes** that **of Reish Lakish, as Reish Lakish said: Do not say** that **the reason for** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir** is that **since he maintains** that **a person may consecrate an object that has not** yet **come into the world,** the consecration can take effect even on her future earnings. **Rather,** say that **the reason for** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir** is that **since he can compel her** to produce **her earnings** for him, it is as if he had a certain legal claim to the products of her hands, i.e., her earnings. Consequently, **he is considered as if he had said to her: Your hands are consecrated to the One Who Made Them,** and the consecration can therefore take effect on something that already exists. **But he did not say this to her;** rather, he said that he was consecrating her earnings. **Since we heard** that **Rabbi Meir said: A person does not say things for naught,** and according to this principle, when one says something that has no halakhic meaning, it is interpreted as if he had said something that does have halakhic relevance, **he is considered as if he had said to her: Your hands are consecrated to the One Who Made Them.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹתָר, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר הֶקְדֵּשׁ. אֵימַת קָדוֹשׁ? רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַויְיהוּ: מוֹתָר לְאַחַר מִיתָה קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that with regard to **the surplus, Rabbi Meir says: It is consecrated** property. The Gemara asks: According to this opinion, **when** does the surplus amount become **consecrated? Rav and Shmuel both said: The surplus is consecrated** only **after** the woman's **death.**

Rav and Shmuel, speaking in tandem, offer a striking reading of Rabbi Meir's position. They interpret *motar le-achar misah kadosh*: the *kiddushin* takes effect only after the wife dies. Their reasoning reconstructs the case as one in which the husband is not, in fact, entitled to the *motar* during the wife's lifetime — the scenario being that he has failed to fulfill one of his obligations, thereby forfeiting his right to her excess labor. The husband will eventually come into possession of those earnings only when he inherits them after her death. At that point, the sanctification he declared during her lifetime finally takes effect, retroactively, once the property enters his domain.

This reading renders Rabbi Meir's position nearly vacuous as a statement about *motar* per se. The real issue is not who owns the excess labor but whether one can sanctify something before it comes into one's possession.

### Davar Shelo Ba L'olam

Under this framework, the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar reduces entirely to a question of *davar shelo ba l'olam* — whether a person can sanctify something that has not yet entered the world or entered his ownership. Rabbi Meir holds that such a sanctification can take effect retroactively; Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar holds it cannot. The question of who owns the *motar* is, on this reading, a side condition of the case rather than the core of the debate.

## Rav Adda: Alive Kiddushin

### Motar Sanctified Alive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר: מוֹתָר מֵחַיִּים קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: The surplus is consecrated while** she is still **alive.**

Rav Adda bar Abba dissents: *motar mechayim kadosh* — the *motar* is sanctified even during the wife's lifetime. On his reading, the *mishnah* is dealing with a case where the husband is in fact entitled to the *motar*, and the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar is genuinely about who owns the excess labor.

### Forfeiture Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָוֵי בַּהּ רַב פָּפָּא: בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּמַעֲלֶה לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת וּמַעֲלֶה לָהּ מָעָה כֶּסֶף לִצְרָכֶיהָ — מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לְאַחַר מִיתָה קָדוֹשׁ? וְאֶלָּא כְּשֶׁאֵין מַעֲלֶה לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת וְלֹ מַעֲלֶה לָהּ מָעָה כֶּסֶף לִצְרָכֶיהָ — מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר מֵחַיִּים קָדוֹשׁ? לְעוֹלָם בְּמַעֲלֶה לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת וְאֵינוֹ מַעֲלֶה לָהּ מָעָה כֶּסֶף לִצְרָכֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa discussed it,** as he was perplexed by this dispute: With regard **to what** do they argue? **If we say** that the husband **provides for her sustenance and** additionally **provides her with a silver *ma'a*** coin every week **for** the rest of **her needs,** as he is obligated to do, then **what is the reason for** the opinion of **the one who said it is consecrated** only **after** her **death?** As the husband has fulfilled all of his obligations and is consequently the owner of his wife's earnings, he should be capable of consecrating them. **And** if we **rather** say that **he does not provide for her sustenance and does not provide her with a silver *ma'a* for her needs,** and she must consequently support herself entirely, then **what is the reason for** the opinion of **the one who said** that **it is consecrated in her lifetime?** It is possible that at some point she will not find sufficient employment, and since her husband does not provide for her sustenance or her other needs, she will need the money for herself and there will not be any surplus at all. **Actually,** one must explain that they are discussing a situation where **he provides for her sustenance, but does not provide her with a silver *ma'a* for her needs,** and this is their dispute: **Rav and Shmuel maintain** that the main enactment was that **they established** [sustenance in exchange for her earnings, and pin money in exchange for the surplus].

The *gemara* then asks: precisely in which scenario does the husband forfeit his right to the *motar*? It frames the question by examining the system of reciprocal obligations. He is paying the *mezonot* — the regular food bill — but not the *ma'ah kesef*, the fixed weekly pin money he is obligated to provide for her personal use. The question becomes whether, in that configuration, he retains rights to the *motar* or not, and this is what the Tannaitic dispute is about.

## Correspondence Debate

### Common-to-Common Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: מִידֵּי דִּשְׁכִיחַ מִמִּידֵּי דִּשְׁכִיחַ. וּמָר סָבַר: מִידֵּי דְּקִייץ מִמִּידֵּי דְּקִייץ. מְזוֹנוֹת תַּחַת מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ, וּמָעָה כֶּסֶף תַּחַת מוֹתָר, וְכֵיוָן דְּלָא קָא יָהֵיב לַהּ מָעָה כֶּסֶף — מוֹתָר דִּידַהּ הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do they disagree?** The Gemara explains: One **Sage,** i.e., Rav and Shmuel, **holds that** they established **something common** in exchange **for something common.** Consequently, they established sustenance, which is common, in exchange for earnings, which are also common. **And** one **Sage,** Rav Adda bar Ahava, **holds that** they established **something** with **a fixed amount** in exchange **for something** with a **fixed amount.** Consequently, since a silver *ma'a* is a fixed amount and the quota of a woman's earnings is also fixed, they established one in exchange for the other. [Sustenance in exchange for surplus, and pin money in exchange for her earnings.] **And since he does not give her a silver *ma'a* the surplus is hers,** unless some of it is left after her death, in which case the husband inherits it.

The *gemara* now articulates the underlying structural question: what exchange corresponds to what? One view holds *midi deshachich mimidi deshachich* — common things correspond to common things. The *mezonot*, the food bill, is a ubiquitous expense and a constant presence in every household; similarly, the wife's baseline labor — the minimum she is obligated to produce — is a regular, ever-present obligation. These two common quantities therefore correspond to one another: the food bill is the exchange for the minimum *ma'aseh yadeha*. Likewise, the *ma'ah kesef* — the pin money, less essential, more variable in its significance — corresponds to the *motar*, the excess labor that also varies and does not always arise. So on this view: regular food bill for regular labor, pin money for excess labor.

To understand what *ma'aseh yadeha* means concretely, the *gemara* later makes clear that it encompasses not only domestic chores — cooking, baking, laundry — which produce no separate income, but also productive work such as weaving and seamstress activity, through which the wife could earn money beyond household upkeep. Her minimum obligation in this latter category is quantified: the weight of five *sela'im* of warp thread in Judea, equivalent to ten *sela'im* in the Galilee, where coins were of a different weight. This is the minimum amount of productive spinning and weaving she is expected to contribute weekly.

### Fixed-to-Fixed Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה סָבַר: תִּקְּנוּ מְזוֹנוֹת תַּחַת מוֹתָר, וּמָעָה כֶּסֶף תַּחַת מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ, וְכֵיוָן דְּקָא יָהֵיב לַהּ מְזוֹנֵי, מוֹתָר דִּידֵיהּ הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava maintains** that **they established sustenance in exchange for the surplus, and a silver *ma'a* in exchange for her earnings. And since he provides her with sustenance, the surplus is his,** and therefore the sanctity takes effect on it immediately when she produces the surplus.

The opposing view holds *midi dekaitz mimidi dekaitz* — fixed amounts correspond to fixed amounts. The pin money is a precisely specified sum, a fixed quantity. The minimum *ma'aseh yadeha* is also a fixed obligation — she must produce exactly a certain amount, neither more nor less. These two fixed quantities therefore pair with one another. What is unfixed — the *mezonot*, which can fluctuate depending on the week's needs and expenses, and the *motar*, which can vary enormously depending on how much extra work the wife takes on — these two variable quantities correspond to each other. On this reading: pin money for minimum labor, variable food bill for excess labor.

This second approach makes a compelling point. The *motar* in a given week might be trivial, or it might be enormous — a wife who is exceptionally productive could generate a sum vastly exceeding any minimum. The food bill, similarly, is not truly a constant; it rises and falls. So it is more natural to pair the fixed with the fixed and the fluctuating with the fluctuating.

### Practical Consequences

The *nafka mina* between these two positions becomes clear in the case of a refusal. If the husband stops paying one of his obligations, the question of what he loses access to depends on which view one adopts. If the common-to-common view is correct, refusing to pay the food bill costs him access to her minimum labor; refusing the pin money costs him the *motar*. If the fixed-to-fixed view is correct, the correspondences are reversed. The same logic applies symmetrically: if the wife refuses to perform a given category of labor, the husband is relieved of the corresponding obligation.

## Source Reconciliation

### Pin Money Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אִם אֵינוֹ נוֹתֵן לָהּ מָעָה כֶּסֶף לִצְרָכֶיהָ — מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ שֶׁלָּהּ. אֵימָא: מוֹתַר מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ שֶׁלָּהּ. וְהָא עֲלַהּ קָתָנֵי: מָה הִיא עוֹשָׂה לוֹ — מִשְׁקַל חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים שְׁתִי בִּיהוּדָה! הָכִי קָאָמַר: מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ כַּמָּה הָוֵי, דְּלִידַּע מוֹתָר דִּידַהּ כַּמָּה — מִשְׁקַל חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים שְׁתִי בִּיהוּדָה, שֶׁהֵן עֶשֶׂר סְלָעִים בַּגָּלִיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts another proof from a mishna: **Come** and **hear: If he does not give her a silver *ma'a* for her needs, her earnings** belong **to her.** This indicates that the earnings were established in exchange for the silver *ma'a*, as Rav Adda bar Ahava contended. The Gemara rejects this by emending the text of the mishna: **Say: The surplus of her earnings** belongs **to her.** The Gemara challenges the emendation: **But it is taught** in the continuation **of this** mishna: **What** is the fixed amount that **she** must **earn for him?** She must spin the **weight** of **five *sela*** of threads of the **warp in Judea,** which is the equivalent of ten *sela* according to the measurements of the Galilee. This clause implies that the mishna is not discussing the surplus but rather the quota of her required earnings. **This is what it is saying: How much is** the required amount of **her earnings,** so **that** one **can know how much** of what she produces constitutes **the surplus,** and to this the mishna replied: **The weight** of **five *sela*** of threads of the **warp in Judea, which is** the equivalent of **ten *sela*** according to the measurements of **the Galilee.**

### Gemara Reinterprets

The *gemara* resists this reading. *Eima motar ma'aseh yadeha shelah* — what she retains is not the core *ma'aseh yadeha* but the *motar*, the excess beyond the minimum. The description of the minimum, the *gemara* argues, is included only so that one can calculate what exceeds it: *ma'aseh yadeha kama havei, de-layeida motar didah kama* — it tells you what the minimum is precisely so that you know how much is above and beyond it. The *mishnah*, in other words, describes the baseline not to define what she keeps, but to define the threshold above which she keeps the surplus.

This is admittedly a reading that imposes something on the plain meaning of the text. The *gemara* acknowledges the tension: different Tannaitic formulations seem to pull in different directions on whether it is the *ma'ah kesef* that corresponds to the minimum labor or to the *motar*. The question of correspondence is genuinely unresolved across the sources.

## Shmuel's Ruling

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הַסַּנְדְּלָר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Shmuel said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan the Cobbler.** A husband may not consecrate his wife's earnings at all, as they have not yet come into being.

The *gemara* now records Shmuel's ruling: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar* — we rule like Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar, who holds that the husband's sanctification of the *motar* does not take effect, i.e., it remains *chullin*. Under the framework of Rav and Shmuel — where the case is one in which the husband is not entitled to the *motar* until he inherits it — ruling like Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar means affirming that one cannot be *makdish* a *davar shelo ba l'olam*.

### Konam vs. Kiddushin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי? וְהָתְנַן: "קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאֲנִי עוֹשָׂה לְפִיךָ" — אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהָפֵר. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָפֵר, שֶׁמָּא תַּעֲדִיף עָלָיו יָתֵר מִן הָרָאוּי לוֹ. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי אָמַר: יָפֵר, שֶׁמָּא יְגָרְשֶׁנָּה וּתְהֵא אֲסוּרָה לַחֲזוֹר. וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Shmuel** actually **say this?** But **didn't we learn** in a mishna: If a woman said: **What I make to feed you,** i.e., what I earn for you, **is** forbidden **like an offering** [***konam***], the husband **does not need to nullify** this vow. She has a prior obligation to work for him, and therefore the *konam* cannot take effect on something that does not belong to her. **Rabbi Akiva says:** Even so, the husband **should nullify** the vow, **lest she produce more** earnings **than is appropriate for him,** and the *konam* will then take effect on the surplus amount. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said: He should nullify** the vow for a different reason, **lest he divorce her.** Since she rendered her earnings forbidden to him, **she will be prohibited from remarrying** him after her divorce, as it would then be impossible for him to avoid benefiting from his wife's earnings. **And Shmuel said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri,** who maintains that the wife can render her future earnings prohibited to her husband before these earnings have come into being, with the prohibition to go into effect after she divorces.

But the *gemara* immediately raises a contradiction. Shmuel also rules *halacha ke-Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* in a different *mishnah*, which involves a woman who declares *konam she'ani oseh lefichah* — using the vow-formula *konam*, a shorthand for *korban*, she forbids the labor of her hands to her husband. The *Tanna kamma* holds that the husband need not annul this vow because it is inherently void: she does not own her labor, and one cannot forbid what one does not own. Rabbi Akiva holds he must annul it, lest she produce more than the minimum, in which case the excess — which is hers — would be caught by the vow. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds he must annul it because if he subsequently divorces her, the vow will take effect at the moment of divorce, and once operative, it cannot be retroactively annulled; it would then prevent them from remarrying.

Shmuel rules like Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, which implies the vow can take effect at the moment of divorce — i.e., that a *konam* declared now over something she does not yet own can take hold later when she acquires it. But this seems to contradict ruling like Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar, who denies that a *davar shelo ba l'olam* can be sanctified. Why should a *konam* succeed where *hekdesh* fails?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי, לְהַעְדָּפָה. וְלֵימָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי לְהַעְדָּפָה! אִי נָמֵי: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא! אִי נָמֵי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא! אֶלָּא, אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: קֻוֽנָּמוֹת קָאָמְרַתְּ?
>
> **Translation:** **When Shmuel said** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri,** he was referring only to **the surplus.** The Gemara asks: If so, **let him say** explicitly that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri with regard to the surplus,** or **alternatively,** he should say that the ***halakha* is not in accordance with the first *tanna*,** or **alternatively,** he should simply say: The ***halakha* is in accordance with Rabbi Akiva.** **Rather,** explain it differently, as **Rav Yosef said: Did you speak** about ***konamot*** to prove your contention that one can consecrate objects that have not yet come into the world?

The *gemara* attempts to rescue the consistency of Shmuel's rulings by suggesting he only meant to rule like Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri regarding the concern about *hadafa* — the possibility she produces extra labor — effectively aligning him with Rabbi Akiva rather than with Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's reasoning about the delayed effect of the vow. But this answer is immediately rejected as strained: if that were Shmuel's meaning, he should simply have said the ruling follows Rabbi Akiva, or that it does not follow the *Tanna kamma*. The *gemara* therefore abandons this resolution and turns to Rav Yosef for a different approach.

## Woman's Vow Case

### Konam at Divorce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי קוּנָּמוֹת, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאָדָם אוֹסֵר פֵּירוֹת חֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו, אָדָם מַקְדִּישׁ דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** ***Konamot* are different,** as they have a special status, **since a person can prohibit another's produce to himself.** If one says to another: Your produce is *konam* to me, it is prohibited for him to eat that produce, although it does not belong to him and the prohibition will apply to it only when it reaches his domain. This indicates that a *konam* has unique power that enables **a person** to **consecrate an object that has not** yet **come into the world,** which according to Rabbi Yoḥanan the Cobbler is an exception to the principle. Consequently, Shmuel's ruling in accordance with Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri with regard to *konamot* is not relevant to his opinion on the issue of a wife's earnings.

The underlying question is why the *konam* should be able to take effect at the moment of divorce. When the woman declares her labor forbidden to her husband, she does not own that labor — he is currently entitled to it. So how can her vow have any grip? The answer requires explaining what is distinctive about *konamot* relative to ordinary *hekdesh*.

### Motar Yedei Hadachak

One possibility raised by the *gemara* is that even while he is entitled to the standard *motar*, there exists an additional category — *motar yedei hadachak*, an extraordinary surplus produced when the woman pushes herself beyond all normal expectations, working late into the night. That extreme excess remains hers even during the marriage, and it is to that excess that Rabbi Akiva is referring when he says the vow may take hold.

### Divorce Trigger Rule

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's position goes further. He argues that the vow's concern is not about what she produces during the marriage but about what happens afterward. Even if the vow is entirely inoperative while she is married, it is poised to take effect the moment the marriage ends. Once the *get* is given, she regains ownership of her labor, and the *konam* she declared at the outset snaps into effect. At that point, her labor is forbidden to her ex-husband. The problem is not the present but the future: if they then wish to remarry, it will be too late for him to annul the vow — a vow can only be annulled on the day it is made. Therefore, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri rules that the husband must annul the vow now, before this scenario ever materializes.

## Konamot vs. Hekdesh

### Identity Change Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: בְּאוֹמֶרֶת "יִקְדְּשׁוּ יָדַי לְעוֹשֵׂיהֶם", דְּיָדַיִם אִיתַנְהוּ בָּעוֹלָם. וְכִי קָאָמְרָה הָכִי מִי מַקְדְּשָׁה? הָא מְשַׁעְבְּדָא לֵיהּ! דְּאָמְרָה לְכִי מִיגָּרְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara rejects that explanation and instead explains as **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** The mishna does not refer to a case where she said: My earnings are *konam* to you, but rather to one **when she says: My hands are consecrated to the One Who Made Them,** and this *konam* can take effect **because the hands** do **exist in the world.** The Gemara asks: **When she says this** in such terms **does it become consecrated? She is subjugated to** her husband with regard to her earnings, so how can she consecrate that which is not hers? The Gemara answers: **She says** that the consecration will take effect **when she will get divorced** from her husband.

Rav Yosef explains: *shani konamos* — *konamot* are different in kind from ordinary *hekdesh*. Normal *hekdesh* is *kedusha shel damim* — a transfer of ownership, where the Temple treasury becomes the owner of the sanctified object in the same way any other legal entity is an owner. One cannot transfer ownership of something one does not own, which is why *hekdesh* cannot work on a *davar shelo ba l'olam* or *davar shelo ba lirshuto*. But *konamot* operate through a different mechanism, comparable to *kedushat haguf* — they change the very identity of the object rather than merely reassigning its ownership.

### Identity Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: קוּנָּמוֹת קָא אָמְרַתְּ? שָׁאנֵי קוּנָּמוֹת, דִּקְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף נִינְהוּ, וְכִדְרָבָא. דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הֶקְדֵּשׁ, חָמֵץ וְשִׁחְרוּר — מַפְקִיעִין מִידֵי שִׁיעְבּוּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the contradiction between the two rulings of Shmuel must be resolved in a different manner. **Rav Ashi said: Did you speak** about ***konamot*? *Konamot* are different,** since **they are** a type of **inherent sanctity,** and therefore the *konam* can take effect on an item that is subjugated to another person, **in accordance with** the *halakha* articulated by **Rava. As Rava said: Consecration,** the prohibition of **leavened bread** on Passover, and the **liberation** of a slave **can** all **abrogate a lien on property.** If property was mortgaged to another person, and then the owner consecrated it, or if leavened bread was mortgaged and Passover arrived and it became prohibited to benefit from it, or if a slave was mortgaged and then liberated by his owner, the lien is abrogated. Since *konam* is a form of consecration, it can take effect on an item even when it is subjugated to another when the owner prohibited it, similar to the case of mortgaged property.

This principle has wider ramifications, articulated by Rava: *hekdesh, chametz, ve-shichrur mafkin mi-yedei shibud* — sanctifying an object as a *korban*, rendering *chametz* forbidden on Passover, and freeing a slave all remove existing liens, because these acts do not merely change an object's owner but change its identity. A creditor who holds a lien on an animal cannot collect once the animal becomes a *korban*, because a *korban* is not simply property under different ownership — it has been taken out of the ordinary world of ownable things entirely. Similarly, a lien on *chametz* that becomes *assur b'hana'ah* on Passover is extinguished; and a lien on a slave evaporates upon manumission, because a free person cannot be the subject of a lien.

The power of *konamot* is that they operate through this identity-altering mechanism. A woman who declares *konam* on her hands is not merely transferring ownership of her labor; she is invoking the framework of *kedushat haguf*, which operates more fundamentally than a simple change in title.

## Abaye's Challenge

### Fruit Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: בִּשְׁלָמָא אָדָם אוֹסֵר פֵּירוֹת חֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו — שֶׁכֵּן אָדָם אוֹסֵר פֵּירוֹתָיו עַל חֲבֵירוֹ. יֶאֱסֹר דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לְעוֹלָם עַל חֲבֵירוֹ, שֶׁכֵּן אֵין אָדָם אוֹסֵר פֵּירוֹת חֲבֵירוֹ עַל חֲבֵירוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** This analogy cannot serve as a proof. **Granted, a person can prohibit another's produce to himself,** but this is possible **because a person can prohibit his own produce to another.** In both cases there is at least one aspect of the prohibition that is in his domain, either when he forbids another's produce to himself, or when he forbids produce in his own possession to others. However, one cannot **prohibit an object that has not** yet **come into the world to another, since a person cannot prohibit another's produce to another.** Just as he cannot make a *konam* and render prohibited to another person produce that is not in his possession, he also cannot render prohibited to another person produce that has not yet come into the world. If so, how can a woman render her earnings prohibited to her husband by a *konam* if those earnings have not yet come into the world?

Rav Yosef's initial formulation of why *konamot* are more powerful than *hekdesh* draws on the following observation: *mitokh she-adam oser peirot chaveiro alav, adam makdish davar shelo ba lirshuto* — since a person can, by vow, forbid another's fruit to himself, it follows that *nedarim* can reach beyond what one owns. Therefore, the woman can forbid her future labor even though she does not currently own it, since *konamot* belong to the realm of *nedarim*.

Abaye objects with precision. He distinguishes two types of *neder*-formulas: I can forbid my fruit to you; I can also forbid your fruit to me. The common thread that makes both possible is that in each case, at least one party has some connection — either the owner forbids his own property, or the person becoming forbidden is the subject of the restriction. What one cannot do is forbid your fruit to you. That would require no connection whatsoever to either the owner or the one being restricted. Now apply this to the woman's case: her labor is his, and she is trying to make it forbidden to him. That is precisely the forbidden configuration — she is making his property forbidden to him, which has no parallel in the world of *nedarim*. The analogy to forbidding another's fruit therefore fails.

## Sanctify Hands Solution

### Hands Exist Now

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּאִילּוּ הַשְׁתָּא לָא קַדִּישׁ וּלְקַמֵּיהּ קַדִּישׁ? אָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעַאי: אַלְּמָה לָא? אִילּוּ הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ "שָׂדֶה זוֹ שֶׁאֲנִי מוֹכֵר לְךָ לִכְשֶׁאֶקָּחֶנָּה מִמְּךָ תִּיקְדַּשׁ", מִי לָא קָדְשָׁה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is there anything** one would do **that if** done **at present,** the **consecration** could **not** take effect **and in the future** the **consecration** could take effect? **Rabbi Elai said: Why not? If one told another: This field that I am selling to you will be consecrated when I buy it** back **from you, doesn't** that field **become consecrated** when he buys it back? It appears that one can cause an item to become consecrated in the future although at present he cannot consecrate it.

Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua offers a different approach: *be-omeret yakdishu yadai le-oseihem* — what if the woman frames her declaration as sanctifying her hands themselves, not the labor they will produce? The hands already exist in the world. The problem with *davar shelo ba l'olam* is that the object of sanctification is not yet present; if what she is sanctifying is the hands themselves, which are physically present now, perhaps that resolves the *lo ba l'olam* difficulty. The *gemara* engages this idea: *d'yadayim isnhu b'olam* — indeed, the hands are present.

### Ownership Objection

But there is a second problem independent of whether the hands exist: she does not own them, in the relevant sense. While she owns her body in a fundamental way, the monetary rights in the work her hands produce currently belong to her husband. Even if we have resolved the *lo ba l'olam* issue by pointing to the physical existence of the hands, the ownership problem remains. He has the entitlement; she cannot sanctify what is his.

## Delayed Formula

### Future Ownership Trigger

The *gemara* therefore refines the formula: *d'amra lichshe'etgaresh* — she declares her hands sanctified *when she gets divorced*. By specifying the future moment of divorce, she is not trying to sanctify something she currently owns but rather the ownership she will regain once the marriage ends. This is more defensible: she does have a base, residual ownership in her own body and labor — an ownership that is currently suppressed by the husband's entitlement but that will resurface upon divorce. She is sanctifying specifically that latent, future-oriented ownership right now.

### Resurfacing Ownership

The analogy offered is to China's rights over Hong Kong during the lease period: the underlying ownership existed and was real, even though it could not be exercised for ninety-nine years. The reversionary right was a genuine legal interest that could be assigned or acted upon during the lease period. Similarly, this woman holds a genuine, if dormant, ownership interest in her own labor — the interest she will have when single — and that interest is what she is sanctifying now. This formulation appears promising, and it is precisely what Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's ruling presupposes: that the vow has no present effect but is primed to activate at the moment of divorce.

## Uncertainty Problem

### Death Before Divorce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם, בְּיָדוֹ לְהַקְדִּישָׁהּ. הָכָא, אֵין בְּיָדָהּ לְגָרֵשׁ אֶת עַצְמָהּ. הָא לָא דָּמְיָא אֶלָּא לָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ "שָׂדֶה זוֹ שֶׁמָּכַרְתִּי לְךָ, לִכְשֶׁאֶקָּחֶנָּה מִמְּךָ — תִּיקְדַּשׁ", דְּלָא קָדְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya objects to this: Are** these cases **comparable? There,** with regard to a field, since the field that he is selling belongs to him at the time of the sale, **it is in his power to consecrate it** now. Therefore, he can effect sanctity on it at a later point as well. But **here,** with regard to a woman, **it is not in her power to divorce herself.** Consequently, the analogy is invalid. Rather, **this is comparable only to** a different case, when **one says to another: This field that I** already **sold to you will be consecrated when I buy it** back **from you.** In that case the field **is not consecrated,** as, when he said this the field was not in his possession, and one cannot consecrate an object that has not yet come into the world.

The *gemara* poses a serious difficulty: *umi ika midi de-lo hashta lo kadish ul'kame kadish?* — is there really such a thing as a sanctification that does not work now but will work later? Rabbi Elai responds affirmatively and supplies an analogy. If someone says to his friend: "This field that I am selling you — when I repurchase it from you, it shall be sanctified," the *gemara* takes it as obvious that this would be effective. The seller has an interest in the field — the right to buy it back — and sanctifies that interest now for the moment it is exercised. Similarly, she has an interest that will resurface upon divorce, and she sanctifies it now.

But Rabbi Yirmiyah immediately challenges the analogy. In the field case, the one making the declaration is the owner at the moment of speaking — he owns the field before he sells it, so his act of sanctification at that moment is grounded in real ownership. Moreover, he has the power to bring the sanctification about: he can choose to repurchase the field. The woman is in a fundamentally different position in both respects.

## No Control Objection

### Lack of Control

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם, גּוּפָא וּפֵירוֹת בִּידָא דְלוֹקֵחַ. הָכָא, גּוּפַהּ בִּידַהּ הוּא. הָא לָא דָּמְיָא אֶלָּא לָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ "שָׂדֶה זוֹ שֶׁמִּשְׁכַּנְתִּי לְךָ, לִכְשֶׁאֶפְדֶּנָּה מִמְּךָ — תִּיקְדַּשׁ", דְּקָדְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this: Is it comparable? There,** the field **itself and** its **produce** are **in the possession of the buyer,** and therefore the seller cannot consecrate them. **Here, her body is in her possession,** as she owns her hands. Rather, **this is comparable only to** a case where **one says to another: This field that I mortgaged to you** and from whose produce you are benefiting **will be consecrated when I redeem it from you.** The *halakha* is **that it is consecrated,** since the field itself was not transferred to another's ownership.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם, בְּיָדוֹ לִפְדּוֹתָהּ. הָכָא, אֵין בְּיָדָהּ לְגָרֵשׁ עַצְמָהּ! הָא לָא דָּמְיָא אֶלָּא לָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ "שָׂדֶה זוֹ שֶׁמִּשְׁכַּנְתִּי לְךָ לְעֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים, לִכְשֶׁאֶפְדֶּנָּה מִמְּךָ — תִּיקְדַּשׁ", דְּקָדְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, objects to this: Is it comparable? There,** in the case where he mortgaged the field, **it is in his power to redeem it,** whereas **here,** with regard to a woman who renders her earnings prohibited to her husband, **it is not in her power to divorce herself** from her husband. **This is only comparable to one who says to another: This field that I have mortgaged to you for ten years will be consecrated when I redeem it from you.** The *halakha* is **that it is consecrated.** Similarly, in this case, despite the fact that her earnings belong to her husband, when she will be divorced they will revert to her, and since her hands have always belonged to her, she can consecrate her earnings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם, לְעֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים מִיהָא בְּיָדוֹ לִפְדּוֹתָהּ. הָכָא, אֵין בְּיָדָהּ לְגָרֵשׁ עַצְמָהּ לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi objects to this: Is it comparable? There,** after **ten years in any case it will be in his power to redeem it,** whereas **here,** with regard to a woman, **it is never in her power to divorce herself** from her husband. Consequently, there is no way for her to consecrate her future earnings.

Rabbi Yirmiyah reformulates the field analogy in a way that strips out the favorable elements. The apt comparison, he says, is not to a field being sold with a repurchase clause declared before the sale, but to someone who has already sold the field and then declares, post-sale, that when he repurchases it, it shall be sanctified. There he has no current ownership, and no guaranteed ability to repurchase. That, Rabbi Yirmiyah argues, is void — and that is the woman's situation. She has no current ownership of her labor's monetary rights, and she has no power to bring about the divorce that would restore them. She could die before him; he could never choose to divorce her. The future ownership is genuinely uncertain, not merely delayed.

Rav Pappa pushes back by introducing a distinction: in the field case, the buyer owns both the *guf* — the body of the field — and the *peirot* — its produce. He owns everything. Here, the woman owns her own body — *gufa beyadah hu* — even while he owns the *peirot*, the entitlement to her labor. This is analogous, Rav Pappa suggests, to a field given as collateral for a loan: the borrower retains title while the lender holds and benefits from the field's produce. In such a case, the borrower can sanctify the field even now, because he retains title. So too the woman, who retains ownership of her body, might be able to sanctify her hands.

Rav Sheshet son of Rav Idi further refines this: the better analogy is to a field given as collateral for a fixed term — say, ten years — during which the borrower cannot redeem it. After ten years, however, the right of redemption returns automatically. Even so, the *gemara* accepts, the borrower can sanctify the field for after the ten-year period, because he knows with certainty that the right will eventually return to him.

Rav Ashi then makes the decisive objection: *hatam le-achar eser shanim miha beyado lifdotah* — after ten years, the borrower will certainly regain control. The certainty of eventual repossession grounds the sanctification. But the woman has no such certainty: *ein beyado legaresh atzmah l'olam* — she has no ability to divorce herself, ever. If he lives and refuses to divorce her, she will never regain her labor rights. There is therefore no sufficiently grounded reversionary interest to anchor the sanctification.

## Rav Ashi Identity

### Identity vs. Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִקְדְּשׁוּ מֵהַשְׁתָּא? אַלְּמוּהָ רַבָּנַן לְשִׁיעְבּוּדֵיהּ דְּבַעַל, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא תִּיקְדַּשׁ מֵהַשְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If it is so, that a *konam* can remove the lien on property, **let her earnings become consecrated from now,** even before her husband divorces her. The Gemara answers: **The Sages reinforced the husband's lien in order that it not become consecrated now.** However, since in general a *konam* can take effect on mortgaged items, it can take effect on her earnings after she leaves her husband's jurisdiction.

Rav Ashi ultimately returns to and reformulates Rav Yosef's approach, applying it with greater precision. *Shani konamos dekedushat haguf ninhu* — *konamot* are different because they operate through *kedushat haguf*, identity-level sanctity, rather than *kedusha shel damim*, which is merely ownership-level sanctity. This is the principle underlying Rava's statement: *hekdesh, chametz, ve-shichrur mafkin mi-yedei shibud* — these three — consecration as a *korban*, *chametz* on Passover, and freeing a slave — dissolve all liens, because they alter the object's identity rather than merely transferring title. A lien is a right attached to an owner's property; when the property ceases to be ownable property in the ordinary sense, the lien has nothing to attach to.

The *konam* the woman declares operates through this mechanism. She is not trying to transfer ownership of her labor to some third party. She is, as it were, changing the identity of her hands — making them like a *korban*, not in a literal sense (her hands will not be used in the Temple), but in the legal-structural sense that the *neder* invokes *kedushat haguf*. This is more powerful than what the husband attempted in the *mishnah*'s original case, where he sought to sanctify the *motar* through *hekdesh* — a simple ownership transfer — which fails when the object is not in his possession.

Yet even this more powerful mechanism has limits. The *gemara* asks: if *kedushat haguf* can overcome liens, why does the woman's *konam* not take effect immediately, dissolving the husband's rights on the spot? The answer: *amru rabanan le-shibuda de-ba'al ki heichi de-lo tikdesh me-hashtah* — the Rabbis strengthened the husband's lien specifically so that the *konam* cannot take effect during the marriage. This is a rabbinic reinforcement of the husband's entitlement, designed to prevent the identity-altering power of *konamot* from unraveling the marital arrangement mid-marriage. The result is a carefully constructed compromise: the *konam* is real and will take effect upon divorce, but the Rabbis ensured that during the marriage, the husband's lien is robust enough to suppress it.

The *gemara* acknowledges that the mechanics here are not fully transparent. A *konam* is not truly a *korban* — the woman's hands are not going to perform Temple service. The identity change is structural rather than literal. And the exact boundary between "the husband's lien is strong enough to suppress it now" and "it will take effect at divorce" is left somewhat inexact. Nonetheless, the conclusion holds: the husband cannot sanctify his wife's labor that he is not entitled to until she dies — following Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar — and the wife can use a *konam*-formula to forbid her labor to her husband in a way that takes effect upon divorce, through the mechanism of *kedushat haguf*, with the Rabbis' having ensured it does not unravel the marriage prematurely.

## Wife's Obligations

### Core Duties List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ מְלָאכוֹת שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה עוֹשָׂה לְבַעְלָהּ: טוֹחֶנֶת, וְאוֹפָה, וּמְכַבֶּסֶת, מְבַשֶּׁלֶת, וּמְנִיקָה אֶת בְּנָהּ, מַצַּעַת לוֹ הַמִּטָּה, וְעוֹשָׂה בַּצֶּמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these are tasks that a wife** must **perform for her husband: She grinds** wheat into flour, **and bakes, and washes clothes, cooks, and nurses her child, makes** her husband's **bed, and makes** thread **from wool** by spinning it.

Having resolved the extended discussion of ownership and sanctification, the *gemara* at last arrives at the next *mishnah*, which enumerates the wife's actual domestic obligations. *Ve-eilu melachot she-ha-isha osa le-vaalah* — these are the labors the wife performs for her husband. The list is comprehensive and reflects a traditional household structure: she grinds the wheat (*tochenet*), bakes (*ofa*), does the laundry (*mechabbeset*), cooks (*mevashelet*), nurses the children (*meineika et benah*), makes the beds (*matzaat lo ha-mitah*), and works with wool (*osa ba-tzemer*). This last item — working with wool, weaving, and seamstress work — is particularly significant, as it represents the category of productive labor that generates actual income beyond mere household upkeep.

### Slaves Reduce Duties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִכְנִיסָה לוֹ שִׁפְחָה אַחַת — לֹא טוֹחֶנֶת וְלֹא אוֹפָה וְלֹא מְכַבֶּסֶת. שְׁתַּיִם — אֵין מְבַשֶּׁלֶת, וְאֵין מְנִיקָה אֶת בְּנָהּ. שָׁלֹשׁ — אֵין מַצַּעַת לוֹ הַמִּטָּה, וְאֵין עוֹשָׂה בַּצֶּמֶר. אַרְבַּע — יוֹשֶׁבֶת בְּקָתֶדְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **If she brought him one maidservant,** i.e., brought the maidservant with her into the marriage, the maidservant will perform some of these tasks. Consequently, the wife **does not** need to **grind, and does not** need to **bake, and does not** need to **wash clothes.** If she brought him **two** maidservants, **she does not** need to **cook and does not** need to **nurse her child** if she does not want to, but instead may give the child to a wet nurse. If she brought him **three** maidservants, **she does not** need to **make his bed and does not** need to **make** thread **from wool.** If she brought him **four** maidservants, **she may sit in a chair [*katedra*]** like a queen and not do anything, as her maidservants do all of her work for her.

The *mishnah* then introduces a sliding scale keyed to the number of *shefachot* — maidservants — the wife brings into the marriage as part of her dowry. If she brings one servant, she is relieved of the three most laborious tasks: grinding, baking, and laundry. Two servants relieve her also of cooking and nursing. Three servants relieve her of making the beds and the seamstress work as well.

### Four Slaves Freedom

If she brings four servants, *yoshevet be-katedra* — she sits on her throne. *Katedra* is the Roman-derived term for a formal seat of authority, the root from which "cathedral" derives. Rashi explains that sitting on the *katedra* means not merely that she has no formal obligations, but that she need not even perform the smallest incidental task — fetching something from another room, bringing an object at her husband's request. Every conceivable request falls to the fourth servant. She is entirely exempt.

## Rabbi Eliezer Spin

### Idleness Leads Zeimah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ הִכְנִיסָה לוֹ מֵאָה שְׁפָחוֹת — כּוֹפָהּ לַעֲשׂוֹת בַּצֶּמֶר, שֶׁהַבַּטָּלָה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי זִימָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says: Even if she brought him a hundred maidservants, he can compel her to make** thread **from wool, since idleness leads to licentiousness.** Consequently, it is better for a woman to be doing some kind of work.

Rabbi Eliezer dissents. Even if a wife brings a hundred servants into the marriage, the husband may still compel her to do the seamstress work — spinning and weaving. His rationale: *she-ha-batalah mevi'ah lidei zimmah* — idleness leads to licentiousness. If she has nothing productive to occupy her, she will seek out trouble. The choice of seamstress work as the irreducible minimum is notable; it is purposeful labor that keeps the hands and mind engaged without being as physically strenuous as grinding or laundry.

## Rabban Shimon Shiamum

### Vow Against Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמַּדִּיר אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִלַּעֲשׂוֹת מְלָאכָה — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה, שֶׁהַבַּטָּלָה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי שִׁיעֲמוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Even one who vows** that **his wife** is prohibited **from doing** any **work must divorce** her **and give** her the payment for her **marriage contract, since idleness leads to idiocy.**

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel takes an entirely different perspective, and from a different direction. He addresses the case of a husband who takes a vow — *madir ishah milasot melachah* — forbidding his wife's labor to himself. If he cannot benefit from her work, then requiring her to do that work makes no sense.

### Divorce Consequence

Rabban Shimon rules: *yotzi ve-yiten ketubah* — he must divorce her and pay the *ketubah*. The reason he gives is arresting: *she-ha-batalah mevi'ah lidei shiamum* — idleness leads to *shiamum*, going out of one's mind, a kind of psychological disintegration. This is a notably different moral framework from Rabbi Eliezer's. Rabbi Eliezer is concerned that idleness will lead the wife to sin; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is concerned that idleness is itself harmful to the wife — that productive engagement is a basic human need, and that a situation in which she is barred from all meaningful work is intolerable for her wellbeing. The contrast between *zimmah* and *shiamum* captures two entirely different orientations: moral surveillance versus concern for the person.

## Gemara Interpretation

### Grinding Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טוֹחֶנֶת סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: מַטְחֶנֶת. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּרִיחְיָא דִּידָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the mishna's choice of terminology the Gemara asks: **Could it enter your mind** that she **grinds** the wheat into flour? Ordinarily, grinding is performed in a mill using millstones that are rotated by water or by animals, so the woman herself does not actually grind the wheat. The Gemara answers: **Rather, say** that **she supervises the grinding** by bringing wheat to the mill and ensuring that it is ground properly. Alternatively, **if you wish, say** instead: She can grind the wheat herself **with a hand mill.**

The *gemara* raises an immediate question about the first item on the *mishnah*'s list: *tochenet salka deitach* — can you really mean she grinds wheat herself? Grinding grain was the province of millstones and water-wheels. The *gemara* resolves this with two options: *ela eima matchenet* — she causes it to be ground, meaning she brings the wheat to the mill, or alternatively, it refers to a case of *reichaya de-yada* — a hand mill, a scenario where hand-grinding is in fact expected.

### Not Like Rabbi Hiyya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא, דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אֵין אִשָּׁה אֶלָּא לְיוֹפִי, אֵין אִשָּׁה אֶלָּא לְבָנִים. וְתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אֵין אִשָּׁה אֶלָּא לְתַכְשִׁיטֵי אִשָּׁה. וְתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיְּעַדֵּן אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ — יַלְבִּישֶׁנָּה כְּלֵי פִשְׁתָּן. הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיַּלְבִּין אֶת בִּתּוֹ — יַאֲכִילֶנָּה אֶפְרוֹחִים וְיַשְׁקֶנָּה חָלָב סָמוּךְ לְפִירְקָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya, as Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches: A wife is only for beauty,** and **a wife is only for children,** but not for household tasks. **And Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches: A wife is only for** wearing **a woman's finery. And Rabbi Ḥiyya** similarly **teaches: One who wishes to beautify his wife should clothe her** in **linen garments,** and **one who wishes to whiten his daughter** so that she will have a fair complexion, **should feed her young chickens, and should give her milk to drink toward the time of her maturity.**

The *gemara* then notes that the *mishnah*'s comprehensive list of domestic obligations does not accord with the view of Rabbi Hiyya. The *mishnah* assigns the wife significant labor responsibilities; Rabbi Hiyya's perspective implies a very different understanding of what a wife is primarily for.

## Rabbi Hiyya View

### Three Purposes

Rabbi Hiyya taught three formulations: *ein ishah ela le-yofi* — a woman is only for beauty; *ein ishah ela le-banim* — a woman is only for children; *ein ishah ela le-tachshitei ishah* — a woman is only for adornment. These are not presented as legal rulings in context but as statements of attitude — they echo the voice of the women who danced in the fields on Tu B'Av and Yom Kippur as recorded elsewhere, each group calling out what men ostensibly seek in a wife. Nevertheless, the *gemara* takes these statements seriously enough to note that if Rabbi Hiyya's view were adopted as a legal baseline, the wife would not be expected to perform work that might compromise her beauty or her ability to bear children, and the *mishnah*'s extensive catalog of obligations would not apply. Our *mishnah*, the *gemara* concludes, does not follow Rabbi Hiyya.

### Husbands' Duties

Rabbi Hiyya also taught practical guidance that flows from these premises. *Ha-rotzeh she-yadin et ishto* — if a husband wishes his wife to have a radiant complexion, *yalbishena klei pishtan* — he should clothe her in linen garments. And *ha-rotzeh she-yalbin et bito* — if a man wishes his daughter to have fair skin so that she will be attractive in marriage, *ya'achilenu efrochim ve-yashkenu chalav samuch le-pirkah* — he should feed her chicks and give her milk to drink near the time of her maturity. The *gemara* closes with these notes of practical domestic counsel, a fitting if somewhat surprising coda to a daf that began with the most intricate questions of ownership, sanctification, and identity.
