# Bava Metzia 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rocheiv - Kinyan Status

### Rocheiv Definition

We pick up at the bottom of 8b, in the middle of a discussion about whether somebody riding an animal — which, as the *gemara* explains, means sitting on an animal while holding the reins — constitutes a valid *kinyan*. The *gemara*'s term is *rachuv*, though one would more naturally say *rocheiv*; in any case, the question is whether *rocheiv* by itself, meaning merely sitting on the animal while holding the reins, is a *kinyan*. A separate and more obvious case is what the *gemara* calls *manhig b'raglav* — where the rider uses his legs to propel the animal forward — which everyone concedes is a valid *kinyan*. The present discussion concerns the narrower case of passive sitting with reins alone.

### Camel/Donkey Debate

The *gemara* now cites a *brraita*: *shnayim she'hayu moshchin bagamal umanhigin bachamor* — if two people were pulling a camel (where the natural method is to walk in front while the camel follows, which is what *meshicha* means in this context) or leading a donkey from the side or behind. The *brraita* adds a further case: *o hayah echad moshich ve'echad manhig* — within a single animal, one person leading from the front and another driving from behind simultaneously. One might have thought that the more natural method for the given animal would constitute the superior *kinyan*, overriding the less natural one in a case of competition. The *brraita* answers: *bemidah zo kanu* — in this way they both acquire. Even the less natural method is a valid *kinyan*, and neither party's act trumps the other's.

### Natural Control

This raises the broader question of why naturalness should matter at all. One might think that any act demonstrating control over an animal — getting it to move, regardless of method — should suffice. The answer is that a *kinyan* must express genuine *ba'alus*, ownership. Getting an animal to move in a bizarre or incompetent manner does not demonstrate ownership; it demonstrates confusion. Like someone who steps on a motorized scooter, manages to make it lurch briefly, and promptly loses control of it entirely — the motion is there but the ownership is not. That, presumably, is the underlying logic of the debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda.

### Tanna/Rabbi Yehuda

*Rebbi Yehuda omer*: a *kinyan* only works if one uses the natural method for that particular animal — *meshicha* for the camel, *hanhaga* for the donkey. The *tanna kamma* disagrees and holds that even the reverse method is valid. The *gemara* then asks: if the *tanna kamma* is indeed permitting all methods for all animals, why does the *brraita* separate the camel and the donkey at all? Why not simply state that whether a camel or a donkey, any method of leading or driving constitutes a *kinyan*? The *gemara* answers: *ika chad tzad d'lo kanu* — there is one method, applied to one specific animal, that does not work. The *brraita* thus cannot collapse all cases together, because doing the unnatural thing for one of the two animals fails. Which animal and which method? The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *ika d'amri meshicha bachamor* — some say the problematic case is dragging a donkey; *ve'ika d'amri hanhaga bagamal* — others say it is driving a camel. The *tanna kamma* thus differs from *Rebbi Yehuda* only in degree: whereas *Rebbi Yehuda* disqualifies the unnatural method for both animals, the *tanna kamma* disqualifies it for only one — but does not specify which.

## Brraita Analysis

### Disputed Method

The *gemara* now attempts to derive the *halacha* of *rocheiv* from another angle, examining the concluding clause of the same *brraita* — the *seifa* — which states *bemidah zo kana*. The *gemara* asks: *bemidah zo lema'utei mai?* What does this phrase come to exclude? The obvious candidate is *rachuv* — mere sitting on the animal — which would suggest that only *meshicha* or *manhig* constitute a *kinyan*, not passive riding. The *gemara* rejects this reading: *lo lema'utei ipcha* — no, it comes to exclude the reverse, unnatural method. To this, the *gemara* objects: *ee hachi, hainu Rebbi Yehuda* — that is exactly what *Rebbi Yehuda* says! The *tanna kamma*'s position must therefore be more nuanced: *lo ika beineihu tzad echad delo kani* — the difference between them is that according to the *tanna kamma*, one specific method for one specific animal fails, while the other animal tolerates both methods. The two opinions thus diverge along a narrower line than it first appeared. Crucially, neither the earlier nor this later reading of the *brraita* sheds any light on *rocheiv* — the case of merely sitting on the animal. That question remains unresolved.

### Rider & Reins

The *gemara* turns to a new *brraita* that explicitly addresses *rocheiv*: *echad rochev chamor ve'echad tofus bemosira* — one person is sitting on the donkey while another person is holding the bridle or reins. The *brraita* concludes: *ze kana chamor ve'ze kana mosira* — this one acquires the donkey and that one acquires the reins. At first glance, this is direct evidence that *rocheiv* constitutes a *kinyan* on the donkey. The *gemara* deflects: perhaps we are not dealing with passive sitting at all, but with *manhig b'raglav* — actually riding and causing the animal to move with one's legs — which is an unambiguous *kinyan*. That said, if it is genuinely *manhig b'raglav*, the *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: *nikni nami rochov bemosira* — why does the rider not also acquire the reins? If the rider is doing a real *meshicha* on the donkey and causing it to move, should the reins not come along as part of the act?

### Animal vs. Reins

The conceptual difficulty here is whether the reins should be considered a single unit with the donkey — in which case a *kinyan* on the donkey automatically includes the reins — or whether they are conceptually distinct. If distinct, then holding the reins handed over by a seller constitutes a *kinyan* on the reins specifically because the act of transfer was directed at them. But picking up stray reins lying on the ground would not be a *kinyan* on the animal itself — *meshicha* on the animal requires the animal to move. The *brraita* as it stands therefore creates a puzzle: if the rider is doing *manhig b'raglav*, why does he not get the reins as well? And if picking up the reins is a *kinyan* on the reins, what kind of *kinyan* is it, and does the rider's movement assist it?

## Reins Asymmetry

### Asymmetry Question

The *gemara*'s framing of the problem generates a striking asymmetry. It seems to assume that the person picking up the reins from the ground needs assistance from the rider to complete his *kinyan*, and that the rider's movement provides this assistance. Yet the reverse is not true: the picker's lifting does not assist the rider. Why should the relationship be asymmetrical? If *hagbaha* can assist *meshicha*, why cannot *meshicha* assist *hagbaha*? This is the *gemara*'s animating question across the next several discussions.

### First Girsa

The *gemara* proposes a revision of the *brraita*: *eima ze kana chamor vechatzi mosira veze kana chatzi mosira* — perhaps the rider acquires the donkey and half the reins, and the person holding them acquires the other half. The idea is that the rider's movement of the donkey assists the person on the ground in the sense of a *ben daat* — a person capable of acting on behalf of another — performing a partial *hagbaha*. But this reading is immediately troubled by an objection: why would the rider need help from the person on the ground at all, if we are imagining that the act of riding constitutes a complete *kinyan* on the donkey and its accessories? The *gemara* has not yet settled whether the reins are conceptually bundled with the donkey or separate.

### Hagbaha Half

The problem crystallizes: the person who picks up the reins from the ground is performing *hagbaha* on only part of an object. The other half of the reins was already suspended — attached at the far end to the bridle in the donkey's mouth. He did not lift that part; it was never on the ground. The *gemara* asks whether *hagbaha* on half an object, when the other half is already suspended, constitutes a complete *kinyan hagbaha*. Furthermore, the asymmetry is not easily dissolved. Even if one grants that the rider's movement of the donkey causes the reins to shift, that shifting is not *hagbaha* — a *kinyan hagbaha* requires that all parts of the object be lifted off the ground, not merely that they move horizontally. Conversely, if one imagines the picker's lifting as somehow combining with the rider's forward motion, the mechanism by which vertical lift and forward motion merge into a single *kinyan* is far from clear. The *gemara* does not resolve this directly but acknowledges the difficulty.

### Second Girsa

A further revision is proposed: *eima ze kana chamor ve'kulah mosira veze kana mah she'tafus b'yado* — the rider acquires the donkey and the entire bridle-and-reins unit as a single package, and the person on the ground acquires only what is literally in his hand, which is treated as if severed from the rest. This reading is elegant: once the rider is *koneh* the donkey, the reins follow as an integrated part of that *kinyan*, and the rider needs no assistance from the person on the ground. The ground-picker, in turn, gets what is in his hand because what one physically holds is treated as an independent object. The *gemara* rejects this reading on a different ground: *im timtzei lomar magvia metzia l'chavero kanah chavero* — the principle that lifting an object on behalf of another person creates a *kinyan* for that other person applies only when the lifter acts *ada'ata d'chavrei*, with the intention of acquiring it for the other. Here, the person picking up the reins is doing so for himself — *ada'ata d'didei ka magbah lah*. He would not perform the lifting to benefit the rider unless his own acquisition depended on it. If the rider is going to get the full reins as a package with the donkey, independent of the picker's action, then the picker has no incentive to act as the rider's agent in any sense. The two *kinyanim* must be mutually interdependent, or the arrangement collapses.

### Third Girsa

*Amar Rav Ashi*: a third revision. The rider acquires the donkey and the bit directly in the animal's mouth — the part of the bridle that is organically connected to the animal as a unit — and the ground-picker acquires only what is in his hand. *V'hashaar* — and the remainder, the middle portion of the reins suspended in the air between the two — *lo kanah lo zeh v'lo zeh*, neither acquires it. It remains ownerless, and anyone could theoretically come and grab it. Since the rider does not need the ground-picker's assistance (the bit is part of the donkey unit), and the picker cannot get assistance from the rider's movement (for reasons relating to the nature of *hagbaha*), the middle portion floats without an owner. This reading has the virtue of consistency: each party gets precisely what falls within their own defined sphere of *kinyan*, with no reciprocal reliance required.

### Rav Abbahu

*Amar Rav Abbahu*: one need not revise the *brraita* at all. The original reading stands: the ground-picker acquires the full reins. Why? *Hov yachol lenatko ulhavi'o etzlo* — because he could yank it and bring the whole thing to himself. Even though the far half of the reins was never lifted off the ground by him, and even though it is attached to the donkey's mouth, the key is that *in principle* he could yank the reins and lift the entire object. *Rashi* understands this principle broadly: if I lift part of an object and the remaining portion is such that a yank would bring it entirely aloft, it is as though I lifted it all. This is even more readily applicable when part of the object starts suspended, since a downward yank from a suspended position is easier than lifting dead weight off the floor. *Rav Abbahu*'s position is that partial *hagbaha*, combined with the capacity to complete the lift through a single additional act, suffices as a complete *kinyan hagbaha*. On this reading, the ground-picker requires no assistance from the rider at all.

### Talis Rejection

The *gemara* dismisses *Rav Abbahu*'s principle with force: *tadi Rav Abbahu b'dushei* — *Rav Abbahu*'s statement is untenable. The *reductio*: imagine a *talis* lying half on the floor and half on a table or pillar. One person lifts the half that is on the floor; a second person lifts the half that is on the pillar. Would you say the first person is *koneh* and not the second, simply because the first could have yanked the whole thing to himself? *Ha'hoeil veyachol lenatko ulehavi'o etzlo* — surely the capacity to yank cannot be what determines ownership, since both parties in this case could equally claim it. *Tosafot* notes that this rejection is not entirely self-evident — the case of a *talis* half on the floor and half on a table seems no less plausible as a *kinyan* than what *Rav Abbahu* originally proposed. Nevertheless, the *gemara* treats the result as absurd and concludes that *Rav Abbahu*'s principle is wrong. A partial *hagbaha* does not become a complete *kinyan* merely because completion was theoretically possible. The ground-picker's act therefore remains insufficient, and the *brraita* cannot be read as *Rav Abbahu* suggests. The question of whether *rocheiv* constitutes a *kinyan* remains open.

## Field vs. City

### Rabbi Eliezer

The *gemara* continues its search for proof regarding *rocheiv*. A *brraita* in the name of *Rabbi Eliezer*: *rochev basadeh u'manhig ba'ir kanah* — riding in the field, or leading in the city, constitutes a valid *kinyan*. The implication is that riding in the city does not work, and leading in the field — though presumably also valid — is not mentioned in this pairing. The operative distinction appears to be context and naturalness: in a city, people do not ride animals. *Rashi* explains the reason as *mishum tzniyusa* — a matter of propriety; in Rome, for instance, riding animals through the city was actually prohibited due to crowding. Whatever the precise reason, riding in a city is not the natural manner of exercising ownership over an animal in that setting.

### Manhig Forms

The *gemara* notes a structural oddity: if *rocheiv* in the field is simply another form of *manhig* — specifically *manhig b'raglav* — then why does the *brraita* need to mention both *rochev basadeh* and *manhig ba'ir* as separate categories? The answer: *trei gavnei manhig* — there are two types of *manhig*, and each works in only one of the two settings. This is why they must be enumerated separately rather than collapsed into a single rule.

### Rav Kahana

*Amar Rav Kahana*: the reason riding does not work in a city is *lefi she'ein darkan shel bnei adam lirkov ba'ir* — because it is not the normal practice of people to ride animals in a city. *Tosafot* raises the question: if *rocheiv* means genuinely active riding — getting the animal to move, *manhig b'raglav* — why should the urban setting make a difference? In a field, passive sitting on an animal might be made meaningful by the fact that the animal is moving; in a city, if it is also moving under the rider's direction, the act should be equally effective. *Rav Kahana*'s answer must be that the lack of social normalcy of riding in an urban context strips the act of its character as a demonstration of *ba'alus*, even if it does produce motion.

### Unnatural Acts

*Amar Rav Ashi l'Rav Kahana*: this principle proves too much. Consider *higbia arneki b'Shabbos* — someone who lifts a wallet on *Shabbos* to acquire it. *She'ein darkan shel bnei adam lehagbia arneki b'Shabbos* — people do not ordinarily handle wallets on *Shabbos*. Would you say the *kinyan* fails because the act is contextually abnormal? Surely not. *Ela mai avad avad* — if you did it, you did it; you violated *Shabbos* but the *kinyan* is complete. By the same logic, if someone rides a donkey in a city in a manner that genuinely causes it to move, *v'kani* — it should work, and the social abnormality of the setting should be irrelevant.

### Seller Restriction

The *gemara* resolves this challenge: *b'mekach u'memkar askinan* — we are not speaking of finding a *metzia* but of a sale. *D'amar lei kani k'derech bnei adam konim* — the seller explicitly stipulated that the buyer must effect the acquisition in the normal manner. The answer is admittedly somewhat forced, since no such stipulation appears on the face of the *brraita*. But the distinction is meaningful: in the case of a *metzia*, any act of *kinyan*, however contextually unusual, is fully effective. It is only in the context of a sale, where the seller's implicit or explicit conditions structure the transaction, that performing the *kinyan* in an abnormal way can undermine the transfer.

### Special Conditions

The *gemara* adds several refinements. *V'ei reshus harabim hi kani* — if the transfer takes place in a fully public domain rather than a side street, even riding in a city works. *V'ei adam chashuv hu kani* — if the buyer is a distinguished person, riding works even in the city, because leading an animal by foot would be beneath his dignity. For such a person, riding is the natural expression of ownership. Conversely, *v'ei isha hi kanyia* — if the buyer is a woman, riding is more natural than leading. *Rashi* explains that a woman exercises better physical control over an animal from atop it than from the ground. And *v'ei zila hu kani* — if the buyer is a lowly person, riding is natural for him as well, since he has no social standing to protect by avoiding it. The combined picture is that the contextual abnormality of riding in a city is not a fixed legal disqualification but a default that can be overridden by the personal circumstances of the parties or the nature of the space. As always, the *gemara* could deflect any attempt to derive the *halacha* of *rocheiv* from the *brraita*, since all these cases can be read as *manhig b'raglav* rather than passive sitting.

## Animal vs. Goods

### Drag for Goods

*Ba'ei Rabi Elazar*: if you tell your friend to drag an animal in order to acquire the vessels loaded on it — where the seller is transferring the goods but not the animal — what is the *din*? *Meshicha* is a valid *kinyan* on inanimate objects. The question is whether performing *meshicha* on the animal that carries the goods constitutes *meshicha* on the goods themselves.

### Phrasing Issue

The *gemara* first objects to the phrasing of the question: *liknos*, "in order to acquire," is not the same as saying *kni*, "acquire." The questioner is framing the dragging as purposive but has not articulated the seller's *kinyan* grant clearly. The *gemara* adjusts: *meshoch behemah zu ve'kni keilim she'alehah* — drag this animal and thereby acquire the vessels on it. With the phrasing corrected, the substantive question remains. *Tosafot* clarifies what makes this non-trivial: when one performs *meshicha* on an inanimate object, the person's direct force causes the object to move. When one performs *meshicha* on a donkey, one does not physically shlepp it — one yanks the reins and the donkey moves under its own power. The grain on the donkey's back then moves because the donkey moved it. Is that indirect causation a *meshicha* sufficient to make one *koneh* the grain? That is the question.

### Rava Deflection

Before the question is answered, *Rava* interposes a meta-question. You are asking whether dragging the animal creates a *kinyan* on the goods alone — but you seem to be taking for granted that it would work if the seller were transferring both the donkey and the goods together. *Rava* challenges this assumption: *chatzer mehalekhes hi* — if the basis for acquiring the grain along with the donkey is *kinyan chatzer*, the reasoning is that once you own the donkey, the goods sitting in it are as though sitting in your property. But a moving *chatzer* — a *chatzer mehalekhes* — does not work. The principle governing *kinyan chatzer* is that only a stationary *chatzer* under the owner's stable control can serve as a locus of acquisition. A living animal that moves of its own accord does not qualify.

### Kinyan Chatzer

The *gemara* presses this further: one might suggest that the *kinyan chatzer* takes effect at the moment the donkey happens to be standing still. The *gemara* rejects this: *ve'chol she'ilu mehalech lo kanyah, omed ve'yoshev lo kanyah* — the principle is that anything capable of moving on its own cannot function as a *chatzer* even when momentarily stationary. The disqualification is categorical, not situational. The reason, as will be elaborated in the following section, is that the defining problem with a *chatzer mehalekhes* is autonomous will, not present motion. Anything that can choose to move is not fully under the owner's dominion, and *kinyan chatzer* requires complete dominion. *Rava* thus redirects the entire discussion before the original question about *meshicha* on the goods alone is ever answered — and the *gemara* does not return to answer it.

## Chatzer Rules

### Moving Chatzer

The *gemara* now develops the principle of *chatzer mehalekhes lo kanyah* at length. The underlying logic is that *kinyan chatzer* is derived from the principle that a person's property acts as an extension of the person's hand. Just as picking something up with your hand acquires it, having something fall into your domain can acquire it for you. But this analogy holds only when the domain is firmly under your control. A living being — an animal or a human slave — has autonomous will. It can decide to walk away. The *chatzer* is therefore not truly under the owner's dominion in the way a courtyard or a stationary container is. This is why even when such a being happens to be standing still, the potential for movement — the autonomous will — disqualifies it as a *chatzer*.

The *gemara* is thus precise: it is not the fact of movement that disqualifies a moving *chatzer*, but the capacity for self-directed movement. This distinction is essential to understanding the cases that follow.

### Boat & Basket

*Amar lei Rav Pappa l'Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua l'Rava*: if this principle holds, consider a boat sailing along in the water. If fish leap into the boat, is the owner not *koneh* them because the boat is a *chatzer mehalekhes*? *Amar lei*: *sfina minach nayach, umaya hi deka mamtu lah* — the boat is stationary. It is the water that moves beneath it. The boat itself does not propel itself; it is an inanimate object that happens to be carried by the current. As an inanimate object with no autonomous will, it does not suffer from the disqualification of *chatzer mehalekhes*.

A second challenge from *Ravina* to *Rav Ashi*: if a woman is walking through a public domain and a *get* is thrown into her basket or her cloak, does she not receive the *get* because the basket is moving? *Amar lei*: *hasam minach nayach, ve'ee deka masgia* — again, the basket is stationary. It is she who is moving it. The basket has no autonomous will; it goes where she carries it. It is fully under her control in every meaningful sense.

### Self-Moving vs. Owned

The principle emerges clearly. The disqualification of *chatzer mehalekhes* applies exclusively to things that move under their own autonomous will — living beings such as animals and humans. A cart, a car, a truck, a boat carried by current, a basket carried by a person — all of these are inanimate objects that the owner moves or that are moved by external forces. They do not move themselves. They are therefore not *chatzer mehalekhes* in the legally relevant sense and function as fully valid *chatzer* for purposes of *kinyan chatzer*.

The one exception confirmed by this analysis is the case at hand: a living animal carrying goods. Because the animal can and does move of its own accord, it cannot serve as a *chatzer*, and loading goods onto it does not create a *kinyan* on those goods through *kinyan chatzer*, even if the owner of the animal is simultaneously performing a *kinyan* on the animal itself. The original question of *Rabbi Elazar* — whether *meshicha* on the animal creates *meshicha* on the goods — remains unanswered by the *gemara*.

## Magbiah Metzia

### Rider Case

*Hayah rochev al gabei behemah vera'ah metziah* — a person riding an animal sees a lost object lying in the road. Not wishing to dismount, he calls to a friend walking nearby: *tenah li* — go pick that up for me.

### Friend's Claim

The friend picks up the object. *Natalah ve'amar ani zachisi bah, zachah bah* — if the friend picks it up and declares "I took possession of it for myself," he keeps it. This is so even though the rider asked him to retrieve it. However, *im mishenetnah lo amar ani zachisi bah, lo amar klum* — if after already handing the object over to the rider, the friend then says "I had actually taken it for myself," his claim is meaningless. He is believed when the object is still in his hand; once he has transferred it, he cannot retroactively claim ownership.

What is left unstated but apparently implied is that had the friend picked it up *for the rider* as directed, the rider would have acquired it. The question — which the *gemara* will soon debate — is whether that implication is correct, and how exactly to read the friend's declaration.

### Peah Case

A parallel case from another tractate: *mi shelikat et ha-pe'ah* — a person picks up *pe'ah*, the corner of the field designated for the poor, *ve'amar harei zo liploni ani* — and declares he is taking it for a specific poor person. *Rebbi Eliezer amar zachu lo* — it works; the poor person acquires it. *VaChachamim omrim yitnenah le'ani'im sheba'rishon* — the Rabbis say it does not work; the *pe'ah* remains available to any poor person who was there first. *Rashi* notes that even if the picker is the owner of the field, he cannot pick up his own *pe'ah*; we are dealing with a third party.

### Migo Analysis

*Amar Ulla amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: the debate between *Rebbi Eliezer* and the Rabbis concerns a case where the person picking up the *pe'ah* is a wealthy person (*ashir*) acting on behalf of a poor person. The logic of *Rebbi Eliezer*'s position requires two steps. First: *migo d'iba'i mafkar nichsei vehavei ani vechazi leh* — since in principle this person could renounce all his property and thereby become poor, and would then be entitled to take the *pe'ah* himself, the *pe'ah* is theoretically relevant to him. Second: *migo dezachi lenafshei, zachi nami lachavreih* — since he could theoretically acquire it for himself (in that hypothetical impoverished state), he can acquire it for someone else. The Rabbis respond: *chad migo amrinan, trei migo lo amrinan* — one degree of *migo* we will invoke; two degrees we will not. A single-step inference — I could take it for myself, therefore I can take it for another — is acceptable. A double inference — I could in theory become someone who could take it for myself, therefore I can take it for another — is not. On this reading, the dispute is specifically about a wealthy person acting for a poor person. The implication of *Ulla*'s reading is that if both parties are poor — *me'ani le'ani* — everyone agrees it works, since only one degree of *migo* is required.

### Both Poor

*Amar lei Rav Nachman le'Ulla*: why frame it that way? Perhaps the debate is *me'ani le'ani* — both the picker and the intended recipient are poor — and the underlying question is simply whether the principle of *magbiah metziah lechavero kanah chavero* applies at all. Can one person pick up an ownerless object on behalf of another and thereby transfer ownership to that other person? That is the raw question, and perhaps *Rebbi Eliezer* and the Rabbis debate precisely this.

### Core Debate

*Rav Nachman* finds support for his framing in our *mishnah* itself. With regard to a *metzia* (lost object), *hakol ani'im etzlah* — everyone stands in the position of a poor person with respect to *pe'ah*, since *pe'ah* may only be taken by the poor while a *metzia* may be taken by anyone. The *migo* structure therefore maps directly: as a poor person is to *pe'ah*, so is every person to a *metzia*. Everyone is equally entitled to take a *metzia*, so there is no structural barrier at the first step.

If the reading of the *mishnah* is that *magbiah metziah lechavero lo kanah chavero* — picking up a *metzia* on behalf of another does not transfer ownership — then the friend's declaration *ani zachisi bah* means: "I picked it up with the intention of keeping it for myself, and since my picking it up for you does not work anyway, I am keeping it." This reads the *mishnah* as affirming that the friend can always retain the object regardless of original intention. Our *mishnah* would then be consistent with the Rabbis of the *pe'ah* debate, representing the view that *magbiah metziah lechavero* does not work.

Alternatively, if everyone — including the Rabbis — agrees that *magbiah metziah lechavero kanah chavero*, then the *mishnah* must be read differently.

### Mishnah Reading

*Amar lei*: *matnitin d'amar techilah* — the *mishnah* is speaking about a case where the friend declares from the outset, *before handing it over*, that he picked it up for himself. On this reading, the friend is not saying "I changed my mind"; he is saying "I never acted as your agent in the first place — I picked it up for myself from the start." And he is believed because the object is still in his hand. Had he conceded that he picked it up for the rider, the rider would own it — because on this reading, *magbiah metziah lechavero does* work. The question thus comes down to what *ani zachisi bah* means in the *mishnah*: is the friend claiming he picked it up for himself initially, or is he retracting a prior intention to act on the rider's behalf?

The two readings of the *mishnah* are thus directly at stake in the debate over *magbiah metziah lechavero kanah chavero*, and we leave off here with the question unresolved, to be taken up at the start of the next session.
