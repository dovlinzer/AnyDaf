# Tamid 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ba'al Keri & Mikvah

### Ba'al Keri Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵירַע קֶרִי לְאֶחָד מֵהֶן – יוֹצֵא וְהוֹלֵךְ לוֹ בִּמְסִיבָּה הַהוֹלֶכֶת לוֹ תַּחַת הַבִּירָה. וְנֵרוֹת דּוֹלְקִין מִכָּאן וּמִכָּאן. עַד שֶׁהוּא מַגִּיעַ לְבֵית הַטְּבִילָה. וּמְדוּרָה הָיְתָה שָׁם. וּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא שֶׁל כָּבוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** If **a seminal emission befell one of** the priests, rendering him ritually impure and unfit for service, **he would leave** the Chamber of the Hearth, **and he would walk through the circuitous passage that extended beneath the Temple,** as he could not pass through the Temple courtyard, due to his impurity. **And** there were **lamps burning on this** side **and on that** side of the passage. He would walk through the passage **until he reached the Chamber of Immersion. And there was a fire** burning **there** to warm the priests after they had immersed, **and** also **a bathroom of honor,** so that the priests could urinate before immersion.

The final topic of the previous session concerned the nocturnal watch in the *beit hamikdash*. The *kohanim* on duty would spend the night in the *beit hamoked*, the large chamber with its warming fire. If one of them experienced a seminal emission during the night and thereby became a *ba'al keri*, the halakhic consequences were significant. A *ba'al keri* is prohibited not only from entering the *mikdash* proper but from being present anywhere on *har habayit* at all.

This point carries considerable practical relevance for the contemporary Temple tours that bring visitors onto *har habayit*. The common justification offered for such tours is that *tumeas meit* — impurity contracted through contact with a corpse — does not disqualify one from *har habayit*. That is correct as far as it goes: a *tmei meit* is indeed permitted on *har habayit*, provided he does not enter the area of the *mikdash* itself. This is the basis on which many groups ascend, having first immersed in a *mikvah*. However, a *ba'al keri* — and likewise a woman who is a *niddah* — is categorically prohibited from *har habayit* entirely. The distinction is between *tumah* that originates externally, through contact with an outside source, versus *tumah yotzeis migufo*, impurity that emanates from within one's own body. The latter category carries a stricter prohibition that extends to all of *har habayit*, not merely the *mikdash* proper.

### Tunnel System

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּמְסִיבָּה הַהוֹלֶכֶת לוֹ תַּחַת הַבִּירָה
>
> **Translation:** through the circuitous passage that extended beneath the Temple

Since the *ba'al keri* cannot traverse *har habayit*, the Temple employed a tunnel system for precisely this situation. The principle underlying the tunnels is the well-established rule that rooftops and underground passages — *lo nitkadshu* — were not sanctified with the *kedusha* of the *mikdash*, and by extension, presumably also not with the *kedusha* of *har habayit* itself. This is certainly what must be said to make the tunnel system coherent. The *kohen* who became a *ba'al keri* could therefore travel underground to reach the *mikvah* without transgressing the prohibition against being on *har habayit* in a state of *tumah yotzeis migufo*.

### Immersion Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָרַד, וְטָבַל, עָלָה וְנִסְתַּפֵּג, וְנִתְחַמֵּם כְּנֶגֶד הַמְּדוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** After the priest **descended and immersed** in the ritual bath, he **ascended and dried himself** with a towel, **and warmed himself opposite the fire.**

The *mishnah* describes what follows: *yarad v'taval* — he descends into the *mikvah*, which was naturally situated in a pit in the ground. *Alah v'nistafeg* — he emerges and dries himself off. *V'nitchamem kineged hamedurah* — he warms himself by the fire. This presupposes that there was a bonfire near the *mikvah* itself, since emerging from cold water in the middle of the night, unclothed, demands warming. Recall that the *beit hamoked* also had its own fire, which is where the *kohanim* were sleeping.

### Return & Wait

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא, וְיָשַׁב לוֹ אֵצֶל אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים, עַד שֶׁהָיוּ שְׁעָרִים נִפְתָּחִים, יוֹצֵא וְהוֹלֵךְ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** He** then **came** back to the Chamber of the Hearth **and sat with his brethren the priests until** dawn, when the **gates** of the Temple courtyard **would be opened.** He would then **leave** the Temple **and go on his** way.

After drying and warming himself, *u'va v'yashav lo eitzel echav hakohanim* — he returns and sits with his fellow *kohanim*. This is the shift of *kohanim* who will be working the following morning, and so he returns to the *beit hamoked* to wait with them. He remains there *ad she-hayu shearim niftachim* — until the gates of the *azara* open at daybreak. At that point, *yotzei v'holech lo* — he departs and goes home, because he cannot perform any *avoda* that day.

### Tvul Yom Status

The reason he cannot work is that despite having immersed in the *mikvah*, he is still a *tvul yom*. Immersion on its own does not restore full *tahara* until nightfall of that same day. The question then arises: why did he not simply go home in the middle of the night rather than waiting until morning? One might suggest that he went through a tunnel to the *mikvah* and could have exited on the other side without needing to re-enter through the gates — and indeed, if the *mikvah* had a separate exit, that point has some force. But the general answer given is that we do not want to embarrass this *kohen* by forcing him to make his way home in the middle of the night.

This raises a sharper question, however. A *tvul yom* who is a *ba'al keri* remains prohibited on *har habayit* until nightfall, even after immersion. So how was he permitted to remain in the *beit hamoked* at all? The distinction between types of *tumah* is again relevant here. *Tumah* contracted from an external source permits one on *har habayit* but not in the *mikdash* proper. But *tumah yotzeis migufo* — like that of a *ba'al keri* — prohibits one from *har habayit* itself, and this prohibition continues even after immersion until nightfall. So the question stands.

### Rabbinic Leniency

Two answers are offered. The first is that the prohibition of a *ba'al keri* on *har habayit* is only *derabanan*, and the Rabbis waived it in this circumstance so as not to subject this *kohen* to embarrassment by compelling him to leave in the middle of the night. The second answer is that he does not technically remain on *har habayit* at all. The *meforshim* suggest that even the space of the *beit hamoked* where he sits may be a rooftop or a subterranean level — somewhere that does not technically carry *kedushat har habayit*. On balance, the simpler explanation is likely the first: it is *derabanan*, and it was waived.

### Kedusha Levels

This discussion of rooftops and tunnels opens a broader topic of considerable practical importance. The principle that rooftops and underground spaces *lo nitkadshu* — were not sanctified — means that *kedushat har habayit* attaches to the surface of *har habayit*, understood as an extension of the floor of the *mikdash* itself. Just as the sanctity of the *mikdash* is conceived as centered on the floor of the *mikdash*, so too *kedushat yerushalayim* and *kedushat har habayit* are understood as extensions of that floor, applying to the surface of those areas.

An interesting parallel arises regarding *yerushalayim* itself. For some *halachot*, *yerushalayim* is treated as an extension of the *mikdash* floor — for example, the question of whether *kodshim kalim* such as the *korban pesach* may be eaten on a second floor, where one might say the elevated space does not share the same status as the ground-level extension of the *mikdash*. For other *halachot*, such as *maaser sheni*, the status of *yerushalayim* is conceived differently and an *aliyah* may be more permissible. These are distinct discussions with their own *rishonim*.

Returning to the tunnels: the question of what lies beneath the present surface of *har habayit* is a genuinely fascinating one. The general rule is that underground spaces were not sanctified. But a sharper question emerges: what if the ground level has shifted? If what was once the surface of *har habayit* is now underground due to accumulation of debris or construction over the centuries, does the original surface retain its *kedusha*? This remains an open question without a clear answer. The simpler case — steps descending from the surface — presumably remains part of *har habayit*, but the exact demarcation of where ground level ends and non-sanctified space begins is not entirely resolved by the sources.

## Terumas HaDeshen

### First Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁהוּא רוֹצֶה לִתְרוֹם אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, מַשְׁכִּים וְטוֹבֵל עַד שֶׁלֹּא יָבֹא הַמְמוּנֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** Among the members of the priestly family who are to serve in the Temple that day, **whoever wants to remove** the ashes **from the altar rises early and immerses** himself in a ritual bath, as required of anyone who enters the Temple courtyard. He must immerse **before the appointed** priest **arrives,** as the appointed priest oversees the lottery that determines which priests perform the various rites of the Temple service, and the first of those lotteries determines who will be charged with the removal of the ashes.

Having concluded the account of the night watch, the *mishnah* turns to the beginning of the morning's activities. The first *avoda* of the day in the *mikdash* was *terumat hadeshen* — the removal of the ashes from the outer *mizbeach*. This is rooted in the beginning of *Tzav*: *v'herim et hadeshen asher tochal ha'eish et ha'olah al hamizbeach v'samo eitzel hamizbeach* — the fire burns the *olah* throughout the night, and the first act of the morning is removing those ashes. This service was performed before sunrise, approximately at the time of the cockcrowing, which corresponds roughly to *alot hashachar*, about 72 minutes before sunrise.

### Pre-lottery Immersion

Because the right to perform *terumat hadeshen* was assigned by lottery, any *kohen* wishing to participate in the lottery had to be prepared in advance. Even a *kohen* who had not experienced any *tumah* during the night was required to immerse in a *mikvah* before entering the *azara* to perform *avoda*. Thus, *mi shehayah rotzeh litrom et hamizbeach mashkim v'tovel* — whoever wished to perform *terumat hadeshen* arose early and immersed — *ad shelo yavo hamemuneh* — before the appointed official arrived to conduct the lottery.

### Official's Arrival

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי בְּאֵיזֶה שָׁעָה בָּא הַמְמוּנֶּה? לֹא כׇּל הָעִתִּים שָׁווֹת, פְּעָמִים שֶׁהוּא בָּא מִקְּרוֹת הַגֶּבֶר, אוֹ סָמוּךְ לוֹ מִלְּפָנָיו אוֹ מֵאַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And at what time does the appointed** priest **arrive? The times** of his arrival **are not all the same.** There are **times that he comes at the call of the rooster [*hagever*], or** he might come at **an adjacent** time, either **before** the call of the rooster **or after it.**

One might ask at what time the appointed official would arrive, so that the *kohanim* could calibrate their preparation. The *mishnah* answers: *lo kol ha'ittim shavin* — the times were not always the same. *Pe'amim shehu ba mikriat hagever* — sometimes he came at the time of the cockcrowing — *o samuch lo, milefanav umilacharayiv* — or somewhat before or after. The timing was unpredictable, which meant that a *kohen* who wished to participate had to be ready early enough to account for this variability.

### Lottery Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְמוּנֶּה בָּא, וְדָפַק עֲלֵיהֶן, וְהֵן פָּתְחוּ לוֹ, אָמַר לָהֶן: מִי שֶׁטָּבַל – יָבֹא וְיָפִיס, הֵפִיסוּ, מִי שֶׁזָּכָה – זָכָה בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The appointed** priest **arrived** at the Chamber of the Hearth, where the priests of the patrilineal family were assembled, **and he knocked** on the gate **to** alert **them** to open the gate for him. **And** when **they opened** the gate **for him, he said to them: Whoever immersed** in the ritual bath **may come and participate in the lottery. They** then **conducted the lottery,** and **whoever won** that lottery **won** the privilege to perform the rite of the removal of the ashes.

When the official arrived, he knocked on the door of the *beit hamoked* where the *kohanim* had spent the night. *Vehem patchu lo* — they opened the door for him. He then announced: *mi shetaval yavo veyafis* — whoever has immersed, come and participate in the lottery. *Hefisu* — they conducted the lottery. The method, familiar from the parallel account in *Yoma*, involved all the *kohanim* standing in a circle, one removing his hat to mark the starting point, and then counting outward from a number called out at random. Whoever the count landed upon won the right to perform *terumat hadeshen*: *mi shezacha zacha lo* — he who won, it was his.

### Lottery Location

The first lottery was conducted inside the *beit hamoked* itself, in the room where the *kohanim* had been sleeping and where the fire burned. The *meforshim* note that while there is a general sensibility of *beit elokeinu nehalech beragesh* — performing such proceedings in the sacred space to reflect eagerness — the exposed courtyard of the *azara* was not used for this particular lottery. Two reasons are given. First, the process required a *kohen* to remove his hat, and standing in the *azara* without the proper *bigdei kehuna* is not respectful. Second, having the *kohanim* stand in a circle means some would inevitably have their backs to the *mizbeach* or the *kodesh kodashim*, which is likewise not appropriate. The subsequent lotteries, conducted once the *kohanim* were already at work, took place in the *lishkat hagazit* — the Chamber of Hewn Stone — which was also where the *sanhedrin* sat. This chamber was adjacent to the *azara* but not fully exposed within it, striking the balance between sacred space and dignified procedure.

It should be noted that Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, in *Midos*, differs regarding the arrangement for the *ba'al keri* discussed earlier, holding that after immersion the *kohen* exits through a designated tunnel rather than remaining in the *beit hamoked*. This is a minority position and not the view followed in our *mishnah*.

## Sources & Zones

### Guard Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אָמַר קְרָא ״וְהַחֹנִים לִפְנֵי הַמִּשְׁכָּן קֵדְמָה לִפְנֵי אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד מִזְרָחָה מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו שֹׁמְרִים מִשְׁמֶרֶת הַמִּקְדָּשׁ לְמִשְׁמֶרֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the priests would keep watch in three locations in the Temple. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Abaye said** that they are derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "And those that were to camp before the Tabernacle eastward, before the Tent of Meeting toward the sunrise, were Moses and Aaron and his sons, keeping the watch of the Sanctuary, for the watch of the children of Israel,** and the non-priest who came near was to be put to death" (Numbers 3:38).

The *gemara* now asks: *mena hani mili* — from where do we know that *kohanim* are obligated to guard the *mikdash*? The *rambam* counts this as a *mitzvas aseh*, though there is a dispute as to whether the obligation applies only at night or both day and night. The *gemara* seeks the scriptural basis.

### Abaye's Source

*Amar Abaye amar kra* — Abaye derives it from a *pasuk* in *Bamidbar*: *vehachonim lifnei hamishkan kedmah lifnei ohel moed mizrachah, Moshe v'Aharon uvanav, shomrim mishmeret hamikdash lemishmeret bnei Yisrael* — those who encamp before the *mishkan* to the east, before the *ohel moed* to the east, are Moshe, Aharon, and his sons, who guard the watch of the *mikdash* for the watching of *bnei Yisrael*. This establishes the principle of *shimur* and, since Moshe belonged to the *shevet levi*, it establishes that both *kohanim* and *levi'im* are involved in this obligation.

### Zone Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אִין, שִׁימּוּר בְּעָלְמָא אַשְׁכְּחַן דְּבָעֵי, כֹּהֲנִים וּלְוִיִּם שִׁימּוּר. מִיהוּ מַתְנִיתִין קָתָנֵי: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת הַכֹּהֲנִים שׁוֹמְרִים בְּבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְהַלְוִיִּם בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאֶחָד מָקוֹם, וְאִילּוּ קְרָא, כֹּהֲנִים וּלְוִיִּם בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי כְּתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **Yes, we have found** in this verse **that in general** the Torah **requires a watch** to be kept. Furthermore, the verse indicates that it is the **priests and Levites** who are required to perform the **watch,** as it states the precedent of Moses, who was a Levite, and Aaron, who was a priest. **But the mishna** (*Middot* 1:1) **teaches: The priests** would **keep watch in three places in the Temple** courtyard…**and the Levites in twenty-one places.** According to the mishna, the priests and Levites kept watch in different locations, **whereas** in the **verse** the **priests and** the **Levites are written together,** indicating that they kept watch in the same places.

One might accept that this *pasuk* establishes the requirement of *shimur* and establishes that *kohanim* and *levi'im* are involved — since *Moshe v'Aharon uvanav* encompasses both. However, the *mishnah* in our tractate states that *kohanim* guard in three places, and the *mishnah* in *Midos* states that *levi'im* guard in 21 places. The *pasuk*, by contrast, groups them all together without indicating separate zones. The question is therefore: where do we derive that there are fundamentally different zones for *kohanim* and *levi'im*?

### Zone Distinction (continued)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״וְהַחֹנִים לִפְנֵי הַמִּשְׁכָּן קֵדְמָה לִפְנֵי אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד מִזְרָחָה מֹשֶׁה וַהֲדַר אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו שֹׁמְרֵי מִשְׁמֶרֶת הַמִּקְדָּשׁ״. אַהֲרֹן – בְּחַד מָקוֹם, וּבָנָיו – בִּשְׁנֵי מְקוֹמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** that **this** is what the verse **is saying: "And those that were to camp before the Tabernacle eastward, before the Tent of Meeting toward the sunrise, were Moses,"** indicating that the Levites keep watch. **And then** the verse states with regard to the separate watch kept by the priests: **"Aaron and his sons, keeping the watch of the Sanctuary."** Furthermore, the verse indicates that **Aaron** keeps watch **in one place, and his sons** keep watch **in two** other **places,** from which it is derived that the priests keep watch in three different places.

The *gemara* answers by parsing the *pasuk* itself more closely. The verse contains two distinct clauses: *vehachonim lifnei hamishkan* — referring to those who *encamped* before the *mishkan* — and then *Aharon uvanav shomrei mishmeret hamikdash* — Aharon and his sons as *shomrim* of the *mikdash*. The word *chonim* — encamping, dwelling at the perimeter — applies to Moshe, representing the *levi'im*, stationed on the outer perimeter. The word *shomrim* — active guarding — applies to Aharon and his sons, the *kohanim*, stationed in the *mikdash* proper. The use of two distinct verbs, *hachonim* and *shomrim*, signals two separate functions and two separate zones. *Chonim lachud, shomrim lachud* — each term is independent.

### Three Places

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? מִדִּכְתִיב ״וְהַחֹנִים״, וּכְתִיב ״שֹׁמְרִים״. ״חוֹנִים״ לְחוֹד, וְ״שׁוֹמְרִים״ לְחוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** is it derived that the verse should be interpreted in this manner? This is derived **from** the fact **that it is written: "And those that were to** camp…were Moses," **and it is written** separately: "Aaron and his sons, **keeping the watch."** This indicates that those **who** were to **camp** and thereby keep watch **are discrete, and** those **keeping the watch are discrete,** i.e., they perform different watches in separate places.

The remaining question is how we know the *kohanim* specifically guard in three distinct places, as the *mishnah* requires. One might read *Aharon uvanav* as all being together in a single zone. The *gemara* responds: *lo salka daitach* — this cannot be correct. The *pasuk* groups *Moshe v'Aharon uvanav* together, and just as Moshe — a single individual — occupied his own dedicated space, so too each member of *Aharon uvanav* occupies his own dedicated space. *Aharon bechad makom uvanav bishnei mekomot* — Aharon in one place and his sons in two, yielding three distinct locations.

This derivation is admittedly not without its difficulties. The analogy would seem to imply that all the *levi'im* are in one place in the same way that all the *kohanim* would be in one place, which is not what the *mishnah* in *Midos* says — the *levi'im* guard 21 distinct positions. The *gemara* here is providing a partial answer, and a more satisfying resolution follows.

### Three Places (continued)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא כּוּלְּהוּ בְּחַד מָקוֹם לְחוֹדֵיהּ! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ: מָה מֹשֶׁה – בְּחַד מָקוֹם לְחוֹדֵיהּ, אַף אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו – בְּחַד מָקוֹם לְחוֹדֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: One can **say** that **all of** the priests keep watch **in one place** that is **discrete** from the watches of the Levites, but not in three separate places. The Gemara explains: That possibility should **not enter your mind,** as the verse juxtaposes the watches of Moses and Aaron. This indicates that **just as Moses** keeps watch **in one place discretely, so too, Aaron and his sons** each keep watch **in one place discretely,** and they do not keep watch together.

### Rav Ashi's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר, מִסֵּיפֵיהּ דִּקְרָא: ״שֹׁמְרִים מִשְׁמֶרֶת ... לְמִשְׁמֶרֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that the *halakha* that the priests keep watch in three places is derived **from the end of the verse,** which states: "Moses and Aaron and his sons, **keeping [*shomerim*] the watch [*mishmeret*]** of the Sanctuary, **for the watch [*lemishmeret*]** of the children of Israel." The verse uses three terms from the root *shin*, *mem*, *reish*, which means to watch, indicating that there should be three separate watches.

Rav Ashi offers an alternative derivation. He notes that this is the first Amoraic statement encountered in tractate *Tamid*, which is otherwise remarkably *stam* in character — something that reflects its narrative, descriptive nature rather than the theoretical *halacha* debate that dominates most *gemaras*. *Rav Ashi amar miseifa dikra* — Rav Ashi derives the three places from the end of the *pasuk* itself. The *pasuk* employs the word *mishmeret* three times: *shomrim mishmeret*, *mishmeret hamikdash*, and *lemishmeret bnei Yisrael*. Three instances of *mishmeret* in a single verse indicate three separate and distinct watch positions. This is the more direct and elegant derivation for the number three.

One might ask whether this entire *sugya* is perhaps an *asmachta* — a rabbinic enactment supported post-hoc by a scriptural verse rather than genuinely derived from it — particularly since the *pasuk* speaks of the *ohel moed* and *mishkan*, institutions quite different in structure from the permanent *beit hamikdash* with its locked gates and chambers. This is a question worth exploring among the *rishonim*, though the *gemara*'s plain presentation treats the derivation as substantive. As with many areas of *Torah sheba'al peh*, the conclusion is one that *pshat hasukim* alone would not have yielded.

## Watch Chambers

### Two Chambers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית אַבְטִינָס וּבֵית הַנִּיצוֹץ. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: עֲלִיּוֹת מַמָּשׁ הֲווֹ, אוֹ דִלְמָא דַּהֲווֹ גְּבִיהִי מִגְבָּהּ כַּעֲלִיּוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **the Chamber of Avtinas and the Chamber of the Spark** were upper stories. Concerning this, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Were** these halls **actual upper stories? Or perhaps they were** merely very **tall,** built upon pillars, and therefore were **like upper stories.**

The *gemara* now turns to the physical locations of the three watch chambers. *Beit Abtinas* and *beit hanitzotz* were two of the three places where *kohanim* kept watch. All three chambers opened into the *azara* proper — the *mikdash* in its primary sense — since the obligation is *shomrim mishmeret hamikdash* and the *azara* is the heart of that sanctity. To orient these chambers: the *azara* and *ezrat kohanim* constitute the *mikdash* proper in the biblical sense, with *ezrat nashim* carrying only *derabanan* elevated status. These chambers were accordingly positioned around this space. *Beit hanitzotz* was located to the north, and the gate associated with it — *shaar hanitzotz* — provided access from that direction.

It is worth noting here that for the purposes of Temple tours that are conducted on *har habayit* today, the portions that are avoided are those identified with the *azara* proper. The area of *ezrat nashim* and *ezrat yisrael* are technically distinct: *ezrat yisrael* and *ezrat kohanim* share the same biblical level of *kedushat mikdash*, and women would enter those spaces only for specific purposes such as bringing a *korban* or as a *sotah*. Generally, *ezrat nashim* was accessible to all, including the question of non-Jews — a matter treated in the *mishnah* in *Kelim* regarding access *lifnim me'hacheil* that deserves separate examination.

### Aliyot Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דִּתְנַן: שֶׁבַּצָּפוֹן שַׁעַר הַנִּיצוֹץ כְּמִין אַכְסַדְרָא הָיָה, וַעֲלִיָּיה בְּנוּיָה עַל גַּבָּהּ, שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים שׁוֹמְרִים מִלְמַעְלָה, וְהַלְוִיִּם מִלְּמַטָּה. וּפֶתַח הָיָה לוֹ לַחוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Come** and **hear** a resolution, **as we learned** in a mishna that discusses the seven gates to the Temple courtyard (*Middot* 1:5): There were three gates **that were in the north,** and the first of these was the **Gate of the Spark.** It **was like a portico [*akhsadra*], and** there was **an upper story built atop it,** where the honor guard stood, **with the priests watching from above,** in the upper story; **and the Levites** would keep watch **from below. And** the hall **had an entrance toward the non-sacred** area of the Temple Mount. This mishna indicates that the Chamber of the Spark was an actual upper story.

The *mishnah* had stated that in *beit abtinas* and *beit hanitzotz* there were *aliyot* — upper levels — from which *kohanim* kept watch. The *gemara* raises the question: *aliyot mamash* — were these literal second stories, true upper floors built atop the chambers? Or does the term *aliyot* here mean something more modest — elevated platforms, raised structures that were *gevihim ka'aliyot*, high like *aliyot*, but not actual independent upper stories in the architectural sense?

### Portico Design

A *mishnah* is cited to resolve this question: *ta shema detnan*. In the north of the *azara*, the *shaar hanitzotz* is described as follows: *kemin achsadra haya* — it was like a portico. A portico is an open colonnaded structure: columns supporting a walkway or covered area above, with an open space below. *Ve'aliyah bnuya al gabah* — and an *aliyah* was built upon it — *shehakohanim shomrim milmala* — from which the *kohanim* kept watch above — *vehaleviyim milmata* — while the *levi'im* were below. *Upetach haya lo lachol* — and it opened outward to the non-sanctified area, meaning the opening faced outward toward *har habayit* rather than inward toward the *azara*.

This *mishnah* answers the question. What is called an *aliyah* here is not a full second story of a building in the conventional sense, but rather the walkway atop the columns of the portico structure. The *kohanim* would stand or walk along this elevated walkway above the columns, keeping watch over the area. The *levi'im* would be stationed at the gate level below. This is what is meant by *aliyot* in this context.

Picturing the structure: the *shaar hanitzotz* forms a gate, with columns on either side supporting an elevated platform or walkway above — something architecturally reminiscent of the great colonnaded structures of the Herodian period. Indeed, since this is Herod's Temple, the presence of such architectural elements is not surprising. The *kohanim* patrol above on this walkway, while the *levi'im* guard at the gate below.

### Access & Kedusha

A critically important halakhic point emerges from the detail that *upetach haya lo lachol* — the opening faced outward. The *kedusha* that attaches to a chamber in the *mikdash* complex is determined by the direction of its access. A space that opens inward toward the *azara* takes on *kedushat mikdash*; a space whose access is from the outside — from *har habayit* — is considered to have *kedushat har habayit* rather than *kedushat mikdash*. This is somewhat analogous, though not precisely parallel, to the question of which side a *mezuzah* is placed on based on the direction of entry.

This principle is what creates the famous division within the *beit hamoked* itself. The *beit hamoked* had one gate opening toward the *azara* and one gate opening outward. Because it had both, the *beit hamoked* was divided in its *kedusha* — half the room has *kedushat mikdash* and half has *kedushat har habayit*. It is for this reason that the *kohanim* sleeping in the *beit hamoked* slept only in the outer half. The direction of access, in short, is the determining factor for the level of sanctity the space carries.

### Kohanim/Levi'im

The arrangement at *beit hanitzotz* thus places the *kohanim* above — on the walkway atop the portico — and the *levi'im* below, at the gate. Though they occupy different vertical positions, they are guarding the same location together, each from their respective post. This physical arrangement reflects the broader principle of distinct but complementary zones for *kohanim* and *levi'im* in the *shimur* of the *mikdash*.

The question of what function the chamber behind the gate actually served remains somewhat unclear. If all the openings face outward, how would those working inside the *mikdash* make use of these chambers? Perhaps there was a small interior access point not described explicitly, or perhaps one moved laterally between rooms by passing through an adjacent chamber that did open inward. The name *beit hanitzotz* — the chamber of the spark — is itself unclear. Some suggest it housed a secondary fire used to add flame to the *mizbeach*, others that it simply provided warmth. Both functions would have been equally available in the *beit hamoked*, so the precise purpose of *beit hanitzotz* remains uncertain.

## Levi'im Source

### Baraita Posuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְיִלָּווּ עָלֶיךָ וִישָׁרְתוּךָ״ – בַּעֲבוֹדָתְךָ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that the priests and Levites kept watch on different stories, derived? This is derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states that God said to Aaron: "And your brethren also, the tribe of Levi, the tribe of your father, you shall bring near with you, **that they may accompany you and serve you"** (Numbers 18:2). **The verse is speaking of** the Levites accompanying Aaron in **your,** i.e., Aaron's, **service** of keeping watch in the Temple, which is the only service performed by both priests and Levites. Since the verse speaks of the watch of the Levites only as an accompaniment to the watch of the priests, the Levites do not keep watch from above like the priests, but only from below.

Having established the *kohanim*'s obligation of *shimur* from *Bamidbar*, the *gemara* now seeks the source for including the *levi'im* in the watch alongside *kohanim* in the same space. A *baraita* is cited: *veyilavu alecha veyeshorshucha* — they, the *levi'im*, shall accompany you and serve you. The word *ba'avodatecha* — in your *avoda* — implies that they take a share of the *avoda* performed by the *kohanim*.

### Two Readings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בַּעֲבוֹדָתְךָ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בַּעֲבוֹדָתָם! כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְנִלְווּ עָלֶיךָ וְשָׁמְרוּ לְכׇל כֵּלָיו וּלְכֹל עֲבֹדָתוֹ״ – הֲרֵי בַּעֲבוֹדָתָם אָמוּר. הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וְיִלָּווּ עָלֶיךָ וִישָׁרְתוּךָ״ – בַּעֲבוֹדָתְךָ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. הָא כֵּיצַד? כֹּהֲנִים שׁוֹמְרִים מִלְמַעְלָה, וּלְוִיִּם מִלְּמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **Do you say** that **the verse speaks of** the Levites' accompanying the priests in **your service,** of the priests keeping watch, **or** is it **only** stating that the Levites should accompany the priests **through their** own **service** of bearing the sacred vessels? The *baraita* explains that **when** the verse **states: "And they shall accompany you, and they shall keep** the watch of the Tent of Meeting" (Numbers 18:4), this is referring to keeping watch **of all** the Temple's **vessels and all its service,** and consequently **their** own **service** of bearing the sacred vessels **is** already **stated. How,** then, **do I realize** the meaning of the previous verse: **"That they may accompany you and serve you"** (Numbers 18:2)? **The verse is speaking of** the Levites accompanying the priests in **your service,** of the priests keeping watch in the Temple. **How so?** The **priests keep watch from above, and** the **Levites** accompany them **from below.**

Two interpretations of this verse are possible. The first reading is that the *levi'im* take a direct part in the *avoda* of the *kohanim* — that is, they literally share in the *kohanim*'s work. The second reading is that the *levi'im* accompany the *kohanim* and help them by doing their own work — their own separate duties — which alleviates the burden on the *kohanim*. On this second reading, *veyeshorshucha* does not mean they share in the *kohanim*'s tasks but rather that they serve the *kohanim*'s interests by fulfilling their own independent role, thereby reducing the *kohanim*'s responsibility.

### Resolution

The *baraita* resolves this by noting that another verse already addresses the *levi'im*'s own duties: *veshamru lechol klei hamikdash lechol kelav u'lechol avodato* — they shall guard all the vessels of the *mikdash* and all its work. Since this verse already accounts for the *levi'im*'s independent obligations, the additional verse — *veyilavu alecha veyeshorshucha* — must be teaching something beyond their own work. That extra verse teaches that the *levi'im* share specifically in the *kohanim*'s obligation of *shimur*. Not in the *avoda* of the altar or the inner service, which only *kohanim* perform, but in the one element of the *kohanim*'s responsibility that is accessible to *levi'im*: the guarding of the *mikdash*. *Keitzad? Kohanim shomrim milma'la u'levi'im milmata* — how so? The *kohanim* watch from above, the *levi'im* from below. They occupy distinct positions but share a single act of *shimur*.

## Beit HaMoked

### One Guard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית הַמּוֹקֵד כִּיפָּה, וּבַיִת גָּדוֹל הָיָה. וְחַד שׁוֹמֵר הוּא דַּהֲוָה בְּבֵית הַמּוֹקֵד? וּרְמִינְהִי: שְׁנֵי שְׁעָרִים הָיוּ בְּבֵית הַמּוֹקֵד, אֶחָד פָּתוּחַ לַחֵיל, וְאֶחָד פָּתוּחַ לָעֲזָרָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בָּזֶה שֶׁנִּכְנָסִין לָעֲזָרָה – פִּשְׁפָּשׁ קָטָן הָיָה לוֹ, שֶׁבּוֹ נִכְנָסִין לִבְלוֹשׁ הָעֲזָרָה! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כֵּיוָן דְּגַבֵּי הֲדָדֵי הֲווֹ קָיְימִי – סַגִּי לְהוּ בְּחַד שׁוֹמֵר, דָּוֵי לְהָכָא וְדָוֵי לְהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the third place in which the priests would keep watch, the mishna states: In **the Chamber of the Hearth** the ceiling was round like a **cupola, and it was a large hall.** The Gemara asks: **But was there** only **one watchman in the Chamber of the Hearth? And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Middot* 1:7): **The Chamber of the Hearth had two gates; one,** on the north side, was **open to the rampart, and one,** on the south side, was **open to the courtyard. Rabbi Yehuda said: This** gate **that was open to the courtyard had a small wicket** in the door of the gate, **through which** the priests would **enter** in order **to examine the courtyard** and ensure that everything was in order. The Chamber of the Hearth had two gates, and therefore there should have been two watchmen there. **Abaye said: Since** the two gates **stood next to each other,** i.e., directly opposite one another, it **was sufficient for them** to be watched **by one watchman,** who could **look here and look there.**

The *mishnah* states that the *beit hamoked* — the primary guard chamber, distinguished by its dome and its large size — had one guard. This prompts an immediate difficulty. A *baraita* teaches that there were two gates in the *beit hamoked*: *echad patuach l'chol v'echad patuach l'azara* — one opening outward and one opening into the *azara*. If there were gates in two directions, would one not expect two guards, or at minimum two guard positions?

Abaye resolves this: *keivan degabei hadadi havu kaimei sagi lehu bechad shomer* — since the two doors were positioned close to one another, a single guard sufficed. *Davi lehacha vedavi lehacha* — he could look in one direction and then the other without difficulty. The guard, whether stationed on the rooftop or on an elevated platform of the *beit hamoked*, could scan one gate and then the other with a simple turn. The room was fundamentally one space, and a single guard covering both ends was adequate.

This also corrects an earlier impression: it appears from the *gemara* that each of the three watch locations was staffed by literally one person, not a larger complement of guards. The *mishnah*'s language of *bishlosha mekomot* — three places — speaks of locations, not numbers of personnel, but the *gemara*'s discussion implies a single guard per station.

### Trapdoor

The *baraita* records an additional detail regarding the inner gate, the one opening into the *azara*. Rabbi Yehuda teaches: *pishpash katan haya lo shebo nichnasim livlosh ha'azara* — there was a small trapdoor within the large gate, through which one could pass in order to *livlosh* — to scout or inspect — the *azara*. As the commentators explain, this small door served a supervisory function: those responsible for oversight could slip through the small trapdoor without making a conspicuous entrance through the main gate. By entering quietly through the small door, they could check on the guards unobtrusively to ensure they were attentive and properly performing their duties, without the guards being forewarned by the sound or sight of the large gate being opened.

### Stone Steps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּקָּף רוֹבְדִין שֶׁל אֶבֶן. רוֹבְדִין מַאי נִיהוּ? גִּזְרָתָא דְאִצְטְבָתָא דְּסָלְקִי בְּהוּ לְאִצְטְבָתָא. וּמִי הֲווֹ מִיפַּלְגִי אֲבָנִים? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְהַבַּיִת בְּהִבָּנוֹתוֹ אֶבֶן שְׁלֵמָה וְגוֹ׳״!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the Chamber of the Hearth was **surrounded** by **rows of stone** that protruded from the walls and that served as benches. The Gemara asks: **What are** these **rows** of stone? They are rows of **chiseled stones by which** the priests **ascend to** another row of **stone,** where they could lie down. The Gemara asks: **And would they split** the **stones** in the process of building the Temple? **But isn't it written: "For the Temple, when it was being built,** was built **of whole stone** made ready at the quarry; and there was neither hammer nor ax nor any tool of iron heard in the Temple, while it was being built" (I Kings 6:7)?

Returning to the *mishnah*, it describes the *beit hamoked* as *mukaf rovdim shel even* — surrounded by stone steps or hewn stone platforms. As the commentators explain, these *rovdim* were the stone steps leading up to the sleeping niches set into the walls at a higher level, where the senior *kohanim* — the elders of the *beit av* — would sleep. The *gemara* asks: *mai nihu rovdim?* — what exactly are these? *Gizizta de-itztaba desaliku lehu le-itztaba* — they are hewn stone structures for a platform, steps by which one ascended to the sleeping platform above.

### Stone Cutting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דִּמְתַקְּנִי וּמַיְיתִי מֵעִיקָּרָא אַבְנֵי זוּטְרָתָא וְאַבְנֵי רַבְרְבָתָא, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״אַבְנֵי עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת וְאַבְנֵי שְׁמֹנֶה אַמּוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** It was prohibited to use iron implements to cut the stones only inside the Temple area. In this case it was permitted to have chiseled stones, **as** the builders **prepared** the stones in another location. **And** the builders **initially brought small stones and large stones** with which to build the Temple, **as it is written:** "And the foundation was of costly stones, even great stones, **stones of ten cubits, and stones of eight cubits"** (I Kings 7:10). Accordingly, the larger stones were used for the lower tier, while the smaller stones were for the higher tier, forming protruding benches upon which the priests could sleep.

A significant halakhic question immediately arises. How could these stones have been hewn at all? The *pasuk* states *habayit behibanoto even shleimah umasa nivnah* — the Temple was built of complete, undressed stones — and regarding the *mizbeach* the Torah states explicitly *lo tivneh atehen gazit* — you shall not build it of hewn stones. Moreover, the verse in *Melachim* recording the construction of *Shlomo*'s Temple states that no sound of iron tools was heard within the entire structure. If hewn stones were forbidden in the *mikdash*, how were these *rovdim* constructed?

The *gemara* answers: *amar Abaye demetaknei umayeiti mei'ikara avnei zutrayata v'avnei ravrevata* — Abaye explains that they prepared and brought from the outset both small stones and large stones, as the verse itself references: *avnei eser amot v'avnei shmoneh amot* — stones of ten *amot* and stones of eight *amot*. The *mefaresh* elaborates: the cutting and shaping of the stones was performed *chutz la'azara* — outside the *azara* proper, even if still on *har habayit*. It was only within the *azara* itself that the prohibition against iron tools applied. *Mechutz la'azara mutar lechatchan u'lehashchizan b'barzel* — outside the *azara* it was permitted to cut and smooth them with iron. *Aval mishebau la'azara, assur lehanif aleihem barzel* — but once they entered the *azara*, it was forbidden to apply iron to them. The verse *lo nishma babayit behibanoto* — no sound was heard *babayit* — means specifically within the *azara*, the *bayit* in the narrow sense. Outside the *azara*, even on *har habayit*, the work could proceed with iron tools. The stones were cut and dressed to size before being brought in, and once within the *azara* no further cutting was done.

This pre-fabrication principle — preparing materials to their final dimensions at a site removed from the construction location — is an elegant solution, and it anticipates by two millennia what many modern construction operations employ as standard practice.

### Sleep Arrangements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִקְנֵי בֵּית אָב יְשֵׁנִים שָׁם. וְאַמַּאי? לְעַיְּילִי מִטּוֹת! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְעַיּוֹלֵי מִטּוֹת לְבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **the elders of the patrilineal family** that would serve in the Temple the following day would **sleep there,** on the rows of stone. The Gemara asks: **But why** did the priests sleep on the stones? **Let them bring beds** into the Chamber of the Hearth. **Abaye said:** It is **not proper conduct to bring beds into the Temple.**

The *mishnah* records that *ziknei beit av hayyu yeshenim sham* — the elders of the *beit av* slept there, in those niches or elevated platforms of the *beit hamoked*. The *gemara* asks: *amai?* — why niches? Why not bring beds? *Amar Abaye: lav orach ara le'alu'ei mitot l'beit hamikdash* — it is not dignified or appropriate to bring beds into the *beit hamikdash*. Even though the *kohanim* were sleeping in the outer half of the *beit hamoked*, the half that carried *kedushat har habayit* rather than *kedushat mikdash* proper, nevertheless the room is within the *beit hamikdash* complex, and bringing furniture such as beds into that space is not befitting its sanctity.

### Rovim vs Pirchei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּרְחֵי כְהוּנָּה אִישׁ כִּסְתּוֹ בָּאָרֶץ. אַמַּאי הָתָם קָרֵי לְהוּ ״רוֹבִים״, הָכָא קָרֵי לְהוּ ״פִּרְחֵי כְהוּנָּה״? אָמְרִי: אִין, הָתָם, דְּלָא מָטוּ לְמֶעְבַּד עֲבוֹדָה – קָרֵי לְהוּ ״רוֹבִים״, הָכָא, דְּמָטוּ לְהוּ לְמֶעְבַּד עֲבוֹדָה – קָרֵי לְהוּ ״פִּרְחֵי״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that with regard to the **young men of the priesthood, each** of them would sleep with **his garment on the ground.** The Gemara asks: **Why** is it that **there,** in the earlier mishna, the *tanna* **calls them young** [***rovim***] priests, whereas **here,** the *tanna* **calls them the young men of the priesthood [*pirḥei khehunna*]? Is there a distinction between these different terms?** The Sages **say: Yes,** there is a distinction. **There,** with regard to the priests who keep watch in the Chamber of Avtinas and in the Chamber of the Spark, the mishna is referring to priests **who have not reached** the age at which they are eligible **to perform** the Temple **service,** and therefore the *tanna* **calls them young** priests. The older priests would not keep watch, as they preferred to perform the Temple service. **Here,** with regard to the priests who sleep in the Chamber of the Hearth in order to be ready to perform the morning rites, the mishna is referring to priests **who have reached** the age at which **they** are eligible **to perform** the Temple **service,** and consequently the *tanna* **calls them the young men** of the priesthood.

The younger *kohanim*, *upirchei kehuna ish ksuto ba'aretz* — the junior *kohanim* slept on the ground, each with his garment beneath him. The *gemara* then raises an interesting terminological question: in the earlier context, those doing the watch were referred to as *rovim*, while here, the younger *kohanim* sleeping on the floor are called *pirchei kehuna*. Why the different terminology?

The *rambam* and the *meiri* had interpreted *rovim* in the *mishnah* to mean archers — *kohanim* stationed with bows and arrows, which is contextually apt for a guard function. The *gemara* here, however, treats *rovim* as derived from the root meaning youth — young ones, *rabya* — those who have not yet reached the age of performing *avoda*. *Pirchei kehuna*, by contrast, from the word *perach* — a blossom — describes those who are already at the threshold of full priestly service, about to flower into their role.

The *gemara* explains: *hatam dela matu lemiavad avodah, kari lehu rovim* — those not yet of age to perform *avoda* are called *rovim*. *Hacha dematu lemiavad avodah, karu lehu pirchei kehuna*, for they have already blossomed into readiness. The *pirchei kehuna* sleeping on the ground are the younger *kohanim* who will participate in the morning's lottery and are preparing to perform the day's *avodot*. If *rovim* are understood as those not yet fit for *avoda*, then the guards stationed on the rooftops were remarkably young — a striking conclusion that sits in some tension with the *rambam*'s concern for *derech kavod* in the *mikdash*'s guard arrangements.
