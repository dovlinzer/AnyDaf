# Shabbat 117 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sefer Torah 85 Letters

### 85 Letters Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי בְּהֶיתֵּרָא קָטָרַח, נַצֵּיל טְפֵי?
>
> **Translation:** Now, since one is exerting himself to move permitted objects, let us rescue more.

The daf opens by continuing a discussion about a *sefer Torah* that retains eighty-five letters scattered throughout it. Such a *sefer Torah* maintains the status of *kedushat sefer Torah* and would be rescued on *Shabbat*. This principle is derived from the passage of *Vayehi Binsoa Ha'aron*, which functions as a kind of independent mini-*sefer Torah* in its own right, given that it contains exactly eighty-five letters.

### Rabban Shimon View

The *Gemara* now asks: *man tanna de-palig alei de-Rabbi* — who is the *tanna* that disagrees with Rabbi's position regarding the *Vayehi Binsoa* passage as an independent *sefer Torah*? The answer is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. As a *baraita* teaches: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that eventually — in the era of *yemos hamashiach* — this *parsha* will be relocated and written in its proper place. According to this view, it does not belong in *Parshat Naso* but in *Bamidbar*, where the arrangement of the *degalim* and the movements of the camps are described. Why then was it written where it is? *Kedei lehafzik bein puraniyot rishona le-puraniyot sheniya* — in order to separate between the first set of transgressions and punishments and the second set.

### Two Punishments

The second *puraniyot* is clear: immediately after *Vayehi Binsoa Ha'aron*, the Torah states *vayehi ha'am kemit'onenim* — the people began to complain, demanding food and water and eventually meat, as is described at length in this week's *parsha*. But what is the first *puraniyot*? The verse immediately preceding *Vayehi Binsoa Ha'aron* states *vayisu me-har Hashem* — they traveled from the mountain of God.

### Departure Complaint

Rabbi Hama bar Hanina expounds: *she-saru me-acharei Hashem* — that in departing from *Har Sinai*, they had already turned away from God. Even the very act of departure carried within it a sense of abandonment.

### Rashi Tosafot

Rashi, commenting on the phrase *me-acharei Hashem*, explains that within three days of their journey, the *asafsuf* — the mixed multitude — had already begun to crave and desire, and this craving represents the beginning of the process that would culminate in the explicit demand for meat. According to Rashi, it was one continuous process: the desiring began the moment they left *Har Sinai* and reached its culmination when they declared *lama zeh yatzanu mi-Mitzrayim*. The *Vayehi Binsoa* passage serves to introduce a pause into that process.

Tosafot, however, questions Rashi's explanation (referred to as *bakuntres*) and offers a different reading of *puraniyot rishona* in the name of the *Ri*. Rather than referring to the beginning of the craving, Tosafot cites a *midrash*: *vayisu she-nas'u me-Har Sinai derech sheloshet yamim ke-tinok ha-yotzei mi-beit ha-sefer* — they traveled from *Har Sinai* like a child running out of school when the day is done, *she-boreach lo ve-holech lo*, fleeing and going on their way. They ran from *Har Sinai* because they had absorbed so much Torah there and were eager to be free of it. It is this unseemly haste in departing — this flight from the mountain of God — that constitutes the first *puraniyot*. Against that backdrop, *Vayehi Binsoa* introduces a note of order and divine accompaniment: *kuma Hashem ve-yafutzu oyvecha* — even within the movement of the camp, there is a sense of following God rather than fleeing Him.

One might ask: if the text would have read as entirely positive without this interpretive framework — since the passage about Jethro and the invitation *telech imanu* that precedes it is encouraging in tone — is the rabbinic reading perhaps artificially creating a problem in order to offer an artificial solution? Furthermore, since there is a second and arguably a third *puraniyot* that follow in close succession — the *mitonim* and then the *asafsuf asher bekirbo hit'avu ta'avah* — why not place the separator between those? The response to this is that the rabbis were responding to two distinct observations: first, the *nuns* that precede and follow the passage, which bracket it off from its surroundings and demand explanation; and second, a genuine sense that the passage would have fit more naturally in the earlier chapters of *Bamidbar* where the movements of the camps are described. Whether the answers offered are fully satisfying remains open to discussion.

### Proper Location

The *Gemara* asks *heichan mekomah* — where does this passage truly belong? Rav Ashi answers: *bidgalim* — among the description of the camps and their arrangement, a *parsha* earlier in *Bamidbar*, where the basic structure of travel and movement is laid out. That concludes the discussion of a *sefer Torah* retaining eighty-five letters. The question of whether those eighty-five letters must appear in one place or may be scattered remains a matter of debate, left unresolved.

## Gilyonim Status

### Margin Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת, מַפְשִׁיטִין אֶת הַפֶּסַח עַד הֶחָזֶה — דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מַפְשִׁיטִין אֶת כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sages taught in a *baraita*: If the fourteenth of Nissan occurs on Shabbat, and the Paschal lamb is offered but not roasted until Shabbat ends, one flays the Paschal lamb up to the breast to enable removal of the parts of the animal that are offered on the altar on Shabbat. One flays the rest of the animal after Shabbat. This is the statement of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka. And the Rabbis say: One flays it in its entirety.

The *Gemara* now turns to a new question: *ha-gilyonim shel sefer Torah* — what is the status of the margins of a *sefer Torah*? *Matzilim otan mipnei hadleka o ein matzilim otan mipnei hadleka* — do we rescue the blank margins from a fire on *Shabbat*, or do we not? A *sefer Torah* has a top margin, a bottom margin, space between columns, space between *parshiyot*, and blank parchment at the very beginning and very end of the scroll — all of this is *gilyonim*. Rashi further includes parchment from which the writing has been scraped away, leaving blank surface underneath. Does any of this retain *kedusha* even without writing?

### Worn Erased

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין קוֹרִין כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּזְמַן בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַן בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ — קוֹרִין. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַן בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ אֵין קוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: And why does one not read the Writings on Shabbat? Due to suspension of Torah study in the study hall. Rav said: They only taught that it is prohibited to read from the Writings on Shabbat during the hours of study in the study hall; but when it is not during the hours of study in the study hall, one may read them. And Shmuel said: Even when it is not the hours of study in the study hall one may not read from the Writings on Shabbat.

The *Gemara* brings an attempted proof: a *baraita* teaches that a *sefer Torah shebalah* — a worn-out *sefer Torah* — is rescued from fire if it contains enough letters to form eighty-five, as in *parshat vayehi binsoa ha'aron*, but not otherwise. The *Gemara* challenges: *v'amai* — why not rescue it even with fewer than eighty-five letters, *teipokei lei mishum gilyon didei* — on account of the parchment itself on which the writing had rested? The *Gemara* answers: *balah shani* — a worn-out scroll is different, because worn out implies the parchment itself has disintegrated; there is no remaining surface from which to raise the question.

The *Gemara* then cites a parallel *baraita* about a *sefer Torah shenimchak* — a scroll whose writing was erased — using identical language: it is rescued if it retains eighty-five letters, but not otherwise. Again the *Gemara* challenges: *v'amai teipokei lei mishum gilyon didei* — even with fewer than eighty-five letters, should not the remaining blank parchment, from which the letters were erased, have *kedusha*? The *Gemara* responds: *mekom haksav lo kamiba'ei lei* — I am not asking about the place where writing once stood. *Dechi kadosh agav ksav hu dekadosh* — the parchment beneath the writing was sanctified only by virtue of the writing upon it. *Azal ksav azla lei kedushatei* — when the writing disappears, its *kedusha* disappears as well. This is counterintuitive: one might have thought the parchment that bore the actual letters would have greater sanctity than the blank margins, but the *Gemara* establishes the opposite. The writing-surface's *kedusha* is entirely derivative of the writing, and therefore collapses once the writing is gone.

### Blank Spaces

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בֵּין בִּמְקוֹם בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, בִּזְמַן בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ — אֵין קוֹרִין, שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַן בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ — קוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** And Shmuel said: Whether it is in the place of the study hall or it is not the place of the study hall, one may not read anywhere when it is during the hours of study in the study hall; but when it is not during the hours of study in the study hall, one may read.

The live question that remains is precisely the intentionally blank portions: *shema'alah veshematah* — the margin above and below; *shebein parsha leparsha* — the space left between *parshiyot*, whether a *ptucha* or a *stumah*; *shebein daf ledaf* — the space between columns; *shebitchilat hasefer veshebesof hasefer* — the blank parchment at the very beginning and end of the scroll. These areas were deliberately left blank as part of the *sefer Torah*. Perhaps their *kedusha* is not derivative of writing and remains intact in their blank state — even independent of the presence of any written text. This is the live question the *Gemara* is working toward.

The *Gemara* presses: *v'teipokei lei mishum hahu* — if blank margins have independent *kedusha*, then even a *sefer Torah* with fewer than eighty-five letters should be rescued on account of those margins. The *Gemara* deflects: *degayez v'shadi* — perhaps that was a case where all the whitespace had been cut away. This is an admittedly difficult scenario to envision — removing space between columns would leave a scroll in an unintelligible state — but the *Gemara* deploys it to keep the question alive.

### Tumah Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם כְּדַאֲמַרַן מֵעִיקָּרָא, וּשְׁמוּאֵל כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה. דְּתַנְיָא: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ כִּתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ אֵין קוֹרִין בָּהֶן — אֲבָל שׁוֹנִין בָּהֶן וְדוֹרְשִׁין בָּהֶן. נִצְרַךְ לְפָסוּק — מֵבִיא וְרוֹאֶה בּוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ כִּתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ אֵין קוֹרִין בָּהֶן — כְּדֵי שֶׁיֹּאמְרוּ: בְּכִתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ אֵין קוֹרִין, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן בְּשִׁטְרֵי הֶדְיוֹטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi said: Actually, the dispute is as we stated initially, and Shmuel said what he said in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Neḥemya. As it was taught in a *baraita*: Although the Sages said with regard to sacred writings that they may not be read on Shabbat, one may study the midrash on them and teach them before the congregation; if one requires a verse that is written in the Writings, he brings a book and looks in it. Rabbi Neḥemya said: Why did they say that sacred writings are not read on Shabbat? So that people will say: Sacred writings may not be read, all the more so that is the case with ordinary documents, i.e., contracts and letters.

The *Gemara* brings a *mishnah* from *Yadayim*: *ha-gilyonim she-m'ala u'she-m'ata* — the margins above and below, *shebein parshah l'parshah, shebein daf l'daf, shebitchilat hasefer u'vesof hasefer* — *metame'in et ha-yadayim* — even touching only the margins causes ritual impurity, *tumah*, which is the mark of *kitvei kodesh*. This appears to demonstrate that margins have *kedusha* of their own. The *Gemara* rebuffs: *dilma agav sefer Torah shani* — perhaps this is only because they are currently attached to an intact *sefer Torah*. The *tumah* derives from the whole *sefer Torah*, not from the margins themselves. The margins' status as part of an intact *sefer Torah* does not prove their independent status when detached.

### Heretical Scrolls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִצִּיל פַּת נְקִיָּה — אֵין מַצִּיל פַּת הַדְרָאָה, פַּת הַדְרָאָה — מַצִּיל פַּת נְקִיָּה. וּמַצִּילִין מִיּוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים לַשַּׁבָּת, אֲבָל לֹא מִשַּׁבָּת לְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים. וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר מִשַּׁבָּת לְיוֹם טוֹב, וְלֹא מִשַּׁבָּת לַשַּׁבָּת הַבָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in the *Tosefta*: If one rescued sufficient fine bread for his needs, he may not then rescue coarse bread [*hadra'a*], bread made from flour and bran. However, if one rescued coarse bread, he may then rescue fine bread. And one may rescue bread on Yom Kippur for the purpose of Shabbat; however, one may not rescue bread on Shabbat for the purpose of Yom Kippur. And, needless to say, one may not rescue bread on Shabbat for the purpose of a Festival, and one may not rescue bread on Shabbat for the purpose of the next Shabbat.

The *Gemara* cites another *baraita*: *ha-gilyonim v'sifrei minim* — the margins and the books of heretics — *ein matilin otam mipnei hadleika* — are not rescued from a fire. *Ela nisrafin bi-m'koman hen v'hazkaroteihen* — they are burned in their place, along with the names of God written within them.

This is a striking juxtaposition: why are *gilyonim* placed alongside *sifrei minim*? The pairing makes more sense if *sifrei minim* here refers, as Tosafot assumes, to a *sefer Torah* written by a *min* — so both items under discussion, however radically different in character, fall within the world of *sifrei Torah*. The *Gemara* initially reads *gilyonim* as referring to the margins of a *sefer Torah* and asks: but if *sifrei minim* themselves are not rescued, would we even need to ask about margins? The *Gemara* reframes: *hachi ka'amar* — the *baraita* is saying that a *sefer Torah* written by a *min* is treated like ordinary blank parchment — like a *gilyon* — and therefore may be left to burn. On this reading, the *baraita* contains no actual ruling about the *gilyonim* of a *sefer Torah* at all.

The word *gilyonim* in the context of *sifrei minim*, however, may carry a meaning quite different from its other usages. Professor Saul Lieberman and other scholars have argued that *gilyonim* in this particular *baraita* does not refer to margins at all, but rather to the Gospels — *Evangelion* in Greek, meaning "good report." The *Gemara* itself, in a passage found in other textual witnesses (cited here from the *Dikdukei Sofrim*), preserves a wordplay: *Rabbi Meir havei karei lei Aven Gilyon* — Rabbi Meir would call it *aven gilyon*, the "nothingness of *gilyon*"; *Rabbi Yochanan havei karei lei Avon Gilyon* — Rabbi Yochanan would call it *avon gilyon*, the "sin of *gilyon*." Both are clearly polemical reformulations of *Evangelion*. On this reading, the *baraita* about *gilyonim v'sifrei minim* is speaking of two categories of early Christian literature — the Gospels and other heretical texts — rather than about the margins of a *sefer Torah*. The *minim*, throughout these passages, most often refers to Jewish Christians, and the texts attributed to them include not only their own scriptural writings but also, at times, actual *sifrei Torah* that they produced. All of these the *Gemara* subjects to harsh treatment — not rescued from fire, and perhaps actively burned.

Throughout this entire discussion, the weight of evidence runs against the *gilyonim* having independent *kedusha*. Every proof the *Gemara* cites either fails on its own terms or is reinterpreted to avoid the conclusion. The question is left formally unresolved, but the direction is clear.

## Heretic Books

### Burning Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁכַח פַּת בַּתַּנּוּר וְקִידֵּשׁ עָלָיו הַיּוֹם — מַצִּילִין מְזוֹן שָׁלֹשׁ סְעוּדוֹת, וְאוֹמֵר לַאֲחֵרִים: בּוֹאוּ וְהַצִּילוּ לָכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** If one forgot bread in the oven and did not remove it until the day of Shabbat was sanctified, he may rescue enough food for three meals from the oven. And, one may say to others: Come and rescue bread for yourselves.

The *Gemara* now examines the *baraita* about *sifrei minim* more closely. *Ha-gilyonim v'sifrei minim* — the books of the heretics — *ein matilin otam mipnei hadleika, ela nisrafin bi-m'koman hen v'hazkaroteihen* — they are not rescued from fire; rather they are burned in place, along with the divine names written within them. Rabbi Yose holds that on a weekday, one should first cut out the *azkarot* — the names of God — and place those in *geniza*, then actively burn the remainder.

### Tarfon Extreme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לְעוֹלָם יַשְׁכִּים אָדָם לְהוֹצָאַת שַׁבָּת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה בַּיּוֹם הַשִּׁשִּׁי וְהֵכִינוּ אֵת אֲשֶׁר יָבִיאוּ״, לְאַלְתַּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda said: A person should always rise early on Friday in order to prepare all of the expenditures for Shabbat, as it is written with regard to the collection of the manna: "And it shall be on the sixth day, and they will prepare that which they have brought" (Exodus 16:5), indicating that the children of Israel would begin preparing the food for Shabbat immediately upon collecting the manna in the morning.

Rabbi Tarfon goes considerably further. He declares — using the idiomatic oath *ekapeiach et banai*, rendered variously as "bury my children" or "may I be cut down" — that were these books ever to come into his hands, he would burn them together with the names of God written within them. *She-afilu hashem katuv bahen* — even though God's name appears there, it carries no *kedusha* in their context, it is mere letters. His reasoning is stark: *she-afilu adam rodef acharav lehorgo v'nachash ratz lehachisho, nichnas l'veit avoda zara v'ein nichnas l'vateihen shel elu* — even if a person were fleeing a murderer or a serpent about to strike, he would sooner enter a house of idol worship than enter a house of these *minim*.

Why such severity? *Shehallelu makkirim vekofrim* — these individuals know God and yet deny Him, whereas idolators simply do not know. The *minim*, having emerged from within the Jewish world and sharing its discourse, its texts, its conceptual frameworks, represent a far more pernicious threat than outright paganism. The threat from within is always greater than the threat from without.

The verse cited in connection with these *minim* is *ve'achar hadelet vehamazuza sampt zichronech* — behind the door and the lintel they have placed the memory of You — meaning that they knew God but placed Him behind their backs. The *Gemara* also cites the verse from Psalms: *halo mesanecha Hashem esnah uvikkomimecha eskotat, tachlis sinah senasim* — it is Your enemies I will hate, and those who rise against You I will fight with utmost hatred; they were enemies to me.

It must be noted that Rabbi Tarfon's position on burning the divine names, and indeed these passages generally, have been invoked in later halachic discussions about engaging with Christian or heterodox literature. How far these positions extend, and to what communities and historical circumstances they apply, remains a matter of ongoing halachic deliberation. The nature of the threat, so viscerally described here in the context of a nascent Christianity drawing Jews away from their faith, must be weighed against quite different historical circumstances in later generations.

### Yishmael Kal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: בְּשַׁבָּת חַיָּיב אָדָם לִבְצוֹעַ עַל שְׁתֵּי כִכָּרוֹת, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֶחֶם מִשְׁנֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abba said: On Shabbat a person is obligated to break bread in his meal over two loaves of bread, as it is written: "And it happened on the sixth day, they collected double the bread, two *omer* for each one" (Exodus 16:22).

Rabbi Yishmael offers a *kal vachomer* in support of the position that these books and the divine names within them should be destroyed: *u-ma la'asot shalom bein ish le'ishto amra Torah shemi shenichtav b'kedusha yemacheh al hamayim* — to make peace between husband and wife, the Torah permits the divine name, written in sanctity, to be erased into water in the rite of the *sotah*. These *minim*, by contrast, *shenatnu kinah v'eivah v'tachrus bein Yisrael l'avihem shebashamayim* — have introduced jealousy and enmity and competition between Israel and their Father in heaven. *Al achat kama v'kama* — how much more so should one destroy the name written in their texts, in order to remove the harm they cause. Professor Saul Lieberman, in his commentary on the *Tosefta*, notes that Rabbi Yishmael may be making an even sharper point: even if the name was written *bikdusha* — with sincere religious intent — it does not matter; the existential threat overrides it.

### Non-Rescue Policy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: חֲזֵינָא לֵיהּ לְרַב כָּהֲנָא דְּנָקֵט תַּרְתֵּי וּבָצַע חֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi said: I saw that Rav Kahana took two loaves in his hand and broke one, not both at once.

The *baraita* concludes with a sweeping declaration: just as one does not rescue these books from fire, *kach ein matzilin osam lo min hamapoles v'lo min hamayim v'lo midavar hame'avdan* — one does not rescue them from a collapsing building, from water, or from any other cause of destruction. Tosafot reads this as relating back to the more lenient view that does not mandate active burning: even that position agrees that on *Shabbat*, the concern is not merely that one might extinguish a fire and violate a *melacha*; rather, rescue is prohibited in every circumstance, because even on a weekday the maximum concession is to cut out the divine names and burn the rest.

## Bei Avidan

### Library Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? נָפְלָה דְּלֵיקָה כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כַּמָּה סְעוּדוֹת חַיָּיב אָדָם לֶאֱכוֹל בַּשַּׁבָּת — שָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** How so? If a fire ignited before the meal on Shabbat evening, one rescues food for three meals; if a fire ignited Shabbat morning, he rescues food for two meals; if a fire ignited in the afternoon, he rescues food for one meal. With regard to meals on Shabbat, the Sages taught in a *baraita*: How many meals is a person obligated to eat on Shabbat? Three.

The *Gemara* now turns to a question posed by Yosef bar Chanin to Rabbi Abbahu: *hanei sifrei devei Avidan* — what about the books of *Bei Avidan*? *Matzil osam mipnei hadleika o ein matzil osam* — are they rescued from a fire or not? *In velav v'rafya b'yadei* — "yes, no," Rabbi Abbahu vacillated; the matter was uncertain in his hands and he would not issue a definitive ruling.

### Avidan Definition

What is *Bei Avidan*? Rashi explains it as a center for disputations — books composed by *minim* for the purpose of debating with Jews, and the place where such debates were held was called *Bei Avidan*. Rabbeinu Chananel offers a fuller picture: *Bei Avidan* was a known gathering place — essentially a comprehensive library or institution of learning — that held the wisdom literature of all nations, *mi-kol uma*, and among its holdings were also *Sifrei Torah*, *Nevi'im*, and *Ketuvim* in *Lashon HaKodesh*. Scholars from all nations would gather there and engage in intellectual exchange, *v'nos'im v'nos'nim b'divrei chochma*. The question of rescue is therefore the question of the *Sifrei Torah* and biblical texts found there, whose authorship was unknown — *umi kesavah o Yisrael o zutam* — whether written by a Jew, in which case they would have *kedusha*, or not. Since the provenance could not be established, Rabbi Abbahu could not rule either way.

Some have attempted to connect *Bei Avidan* with the Greek *Odeum*, a structure that served both as a venue for theatrical performance and for philosophical disputation. Others suggest the name itself may carry a polemical valence, derived from the root *avad* — destruction — though the *Gemara*'s own treatment of it is notably ambivalent rather than uniformly condemnatory.

### Rav Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: כָּל הַמְעַנֵּג אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת נוֹתְנִין לוֹ נַחֲלָה בְּלִי מְצָרִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָז תִּתְעַנַּג עַל ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Yosei: With regard to anyone who delights in the Shabbat, God gives him a boundless portion, i.e., a very large reward, as it is stated: "If you keep your feet from violating Shabbat, from pursuing your affairs on My holy day, and you call Shabbat a delight, the Lord's holy day honored, and you honor it by not going your own way, or attending to your own matters or speaking idle words. Then you shall delight in the Lord and I will cause you to ride on the heights of the world, and to feast"

Rav would not go to *Bei Avidan* — *Rav lo azil l'Bei Avidan* — and certainly not to *Bei Netzarphei*, which Rashi explains was an outright *beis avoda zarah*, though the name may also be a play on *Notzri*, meaning a Gentile Christian gathering place or perhaps a church. Shmuel, by contrast, would not go to *Bei Netzarphei* — presumably because of its explicitly religious character, or out of concern for personal safety — but he was willing to attend *Bei Avidan*, the broader intellectual forum.

When Rava was asked why he had not come to *Bei Avidan* — *mai ta'ama lo asit l'Bei Avidan* — he replied that a certain palm tree along the route was inconvenient. When they offered to uproot it for him, he said that would itself be inconvenient. The repeated deflections make clear what is really going on: Rava would not attend, and was navigating real social pressure — pressure from non-Jews who participated in these gatherings — with tactful excuses. Mar bar Rav Yosef declared, *ana minaihu v'lo mistafina minaihu* — I am of their company and I have no fear of them — and indeed he attended. One time he went and they sought to endanger him, which suggests the concern about personal safety was quite real.

The question of what it means to be "afraid" in this context is layered. It may refer to fear for one's physical safety when actually speaking one's mind, as in the aftermath of the famous disputation at Barcelona where the Ramban's forthright advocacy effectively forced him to make *aliyah*. It may also refer to the subtler danger of feeling compelled to make theological concessions — a concern that animated Rabbi Joseph B. Soloveitchik's deep reservations about interreligious dialogue in any form. These *Gemarot* have accordingly been invoked in discussions about participation in interfaith settings, and opinions vary considerably about the scope of their application.

### Ima Shalom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּהֲוָה שְׁקִיל שְׁמָא דְּלָא מְקַבֵּל שׁוּחְדָּא. בְּעוֹ לְאַחוֹכֵי בֵּיהּ. עַיַּילָא לֵיהּ שְׁרָגָא דְּדַהֲבָא, וַאֲזוּל לְקַמֵּיהּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: בָּעֵינָא דְּנִיפְלְגוּ לִי בְּנִכְסֵי דְּבֵי נָשַׁי. אֲמַר לְהוּ: פְּלוּגוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, כְּתִיב לַן: בִּמְקוֹם בְּרָא, בְּרַתָּא לָא תֵּירוֹת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִן יוֹמָא דִּגְלִיתוּן מֵאַרְעֲכוֹן, אִיתְנְטִילַת אוֹרָיְיתָא דְּמֹשֶׁה וְאִיתִיְהִיבַת עֲווֹן גִּלְיוֹן, וּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: בְּרָא וּבְרַתָּא כַּחֲדָא יִרְתוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **who disseminated about himself the reputation that he does not accept bribes. They wanted to mock him and reveal his true nature. She privately gave him a golden lamp, and she and her brother came before him, approaching him as if they were seeking judgment. She said to the philosopher: I want to share in the inheritance of my father's estate. He said to them: Divide it. Rabban Gamliel said to him: It is written in our Torah: In a situation where there is a son, the daughter does not inherit. The philosopher said to him: Since the day you were exiled from your land, the Torah of Moses was taken away and the *avon gilyon* was given in its place. It is written in the *avon gilyon*: A son and a daughter shall inherit alike.

The *Gemara* now relates the famous story of Ima Shalom, wife of Rabbi Eliezer and sister of Rabban Gamliel, set at the end of the first century in *Eretz Yisrael*. There was a certain *filosofa* in their neighborhood — Rashi identifies him as a *min*, while Tosafot, in the name of Rabbeinu Shamma, specifies that *filosofa* is a Jew who came from Greece, the word being Greek for *dost hachochma*, lover of wisdom, *philos-sophia*. This man had a reputation for scrupulous honesty: *de-hava shakil sh'ma d'lo m'kabel shochda* — he had a *shem tov* for never accepting a bribe.

Ima Shalom and Rabban Gamliel decided to expose him. She sent him a golden candelabrum as a gift and appeared before him claiming she was entitled to a share of the maternal inheritance — she and Rabban Gamliel being brother and sister. The judge ruled: *plugu* — divide it. Rabban Gamliel objected: *kesiv lan bi-mkom b'ra barta lo tirus* — according to our Torah, where there is a son, a daughter does not inherit. The judge replied: *min yoma de-galitu me-arkhon, ishtari oraita de-Moshe, ve-yehibat sifra achrina* — from the day you were exiled from your land, the Torah of Moses was annulled and another book was given in its place. Some textual versions identify this "other book" explicitly as *avon gilyon* — the Gospels — continuing the wordplay established earlier. And in that book, he claimed, *bera ve-barta ke-chada yartun* — son and daughter inherit equally. No such verse appears in Christian scripture; what is likely being alluded to is a Pauline sentiment — something like "neither Jew nor Greek, neither slave nor free, neither male nor female" (Galatians 3) — understood as implying general equality.

The next day, Rabban Gamliel arrived with a Libyan donkey as a larger bribe. The judge now reversed himself, finding that the Torah of Moses in fact precluded a daughter's inheritance in the presence of a son. He justified his reversal by saying: *shafilit le-seifa de-safra* — I looked to the end of the book — and found the verse: *ana lo le-mipchat min oraita de-Moshe ateiti, ve-lo le-usufi al oraita de-Moshe ateiti* — I have not come to diminish the Torah of Moses, nor have I come to add to it. This is a saying attributed to Jesus. Therefore, the Torah's own rules govern.

Ima Shalom said to him: *nehor nehorach ke-sharga* — let your light shine like a candle — reminding him of the original bribe. Rabban Gamliel completed the point: *ata chamra u-vatash le-sharga* — the donkey came and kicked over the candle. The donkey's bribe extinguished the candle's. The larger purpose was to publicize his corruption publicly before all who knew of his reputation for incorruptibility.

The story is simultaneously a pointed mockery of Christian supersessionism. Christianity presents itself as superseding the Torah — yet its own foundational text has Jesus declaring he has come neither to diminish nor to add to the Torah of Moses. The contradiction between claiming to replace the Torah and claiming to uphold it is held up for ridicule. The joke is sharp, but the context — made as it was in the shadow of the Hadrianic persecutions soon to follow — was one of considerable danger, and the audacity of the critique should be appreciated against that backdrop.

## Ketuvim Reading

### Study Hall Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּהֵיכָא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ לָא שָׁדֵי לְהוּ, אֵיתוֹיֵי נָמֵי מַיְיתִינַן — מִי דָּמֵי?! הָתָם — אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי נָפְלָה דְּלֵיקָה, הָכָא — אַדְּהָכָא וְהָכִי לִישְׁדִּינְהוּ. אֶלָּא אָמַר מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: לְעוֹלָם כִּדְאָמְרִינַן מֵעִיקָּרָא. וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ: הָכָא טִלְטוּל וְהָכָא מְלָאכָה — כְּגוֹן דְּלָא קָבָעֵי לֵיהּ לְעוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** As it is not stated in the mishna, from where do we derive that it is permitted on Shabbat to bring a casing containing money from the outside in order to save a Torah scroll? If you say that from the fact that in a situation where the casing has money in it one does not throw it away but brings it out with the casing, when the casing is outside and has money in it, one may also bring the money along with it; is it comparable? There, with regard to the Torah scroll's casing with money inside, if one tarries in order to empty the money from the case, in the meantime the fire might catch the Torah scroll and burn it; whereas here, with regard to bringing in the casing, in the meantime he could have thrown it away. Rather, Mar bar Rav Ashi said: Actually, it is as we said initially. The Sages equated carrying the Torah casing with flaying the hide of the Paschal lamb. And as for what was difficult for you, that here, with regard to the Torah casing, moving alone is involved, whereas here, with regard to a Paschal lamb, a prohibited labor is involved, it can be explained as referring to a case where one does not need the hide of the Paschal lamb. Therefore, he is exempt.

The *Gemara* turns now to the *mishnah*'s question of *u-mipnei mah ein korin* — why is one not permitted to read from *Ketuvim* on *Shabbat*? The answer given is *bittul beis ha-medrash* — it would undermine attendance at the study hall.

### Rav Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּ״פְסִיק רֵישֵׁיהּ וְלָא יְמוּת״ — דְּשָׁקֵיל לֵיהּ בְּבַרְזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But Abaye and Rava both said: Rabbi Shimon concedes in cases categorized as cut off its head and will it not die, i.e., an action with an inevitable consequence. When an action has an inevitable consequence, even Rabbi Shimon, who normally exempts a person for performing an action with an unintended consequence, maintains the one is liable. Rather, we must say that one flays it strip by strip, and thereby he does not benefit from the hide. It therefore does not constitute the prohibited labor of stripping the hide.

Rav states: *lo shanu ella bi-zman beis ha-medrash* — the prohibition applies only during the time of the *beis ha-medrash*, which Rashi identifies as the period right after *davening* and before the meal. Outside that window, *korin* — reading *Ketuvim* is permitted, since there is no longer any concern about keeping people away from the *beis ha-medrash*. Shmuel, however, holds *afilu she-lo bi-zman beis ha-medrash ein korin* — even outside those hours, one may not read.

### Nehardea Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהֵיכָן מַצִּילִין אוֹתָן וְכוּ׳. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי מְפוּלָּשׁ, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְפוּלָּשׁ? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: שָׁלֹשׁ מְחִיצּוֹת וּשְׁנֵי לְחָיַיִם — זֶהוּ מָבוֹי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְפוּלָּשׁ. שָׁלֹשׁ מְחִיצּוֹת וְלֶחִי אֶחָד — זֶהוּ מָבוֹי הַמְפוּלָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: And to where may one rescue them? Into an alley that is closed, which, if it is surrounded on three sides, is considered to be a private domain by Torah law. Ben Beteira says: Even into an open alley. The Gemara asks: What are the circumstances of an alley that is open, and what are the circumstances of an alley that is not open? Rav Ḥisda said: An alley that has three walls and two posts at its entrance is an alley that is not open; one that has three walls and one post is an alley that is open.

The *Gemara* challenges Shmuel's position: *ve-ha Nahardea atraih de-Shmuel havah, u-ve-Nahardea pasik sidra bi-chtuvim be-minchata de-shabta* — Nahardea was Shmuel's own city, and yet there they would read from *Ketuvim* at *mincha* time on *Shabbat*, which is outside the *beis ha-medrash* hours. The *Gemara* resolves: *ella i itmar hachi itmar* — if Shmuel said anything, here is what he actually said. The reformulation: Rav holds that the prohibition applies only *bi-makom beis ha-medrash* — in the *beis ha-medrash* itself, where people should be listening to the *drasha* rather than reading; but outside its walls, *korin*. Shmuel holds that even *she-lo bi-makom beis ha-medrash* — even outside the *beis ha-medrash* building — *bi-zman beis ha-medrash ein korin*, if it is the time when the *beis ha-medrash* is in session, you should not be reading *Ketuvim* elsewhere either — you should come in. But *she-lo bi-zman beis ha-medrash, korin* — outside those hours you may read. And that is entirely consistent with the practice in Nahardea: they read *Ketuvim* at *mincha* time because that was beyond the *beis ha-medrash* hours.

Rav Ashi clarifies: *l'olam k'da'amran meikara* — the positions are as originally stated, without the revision. Shmuel is not expressing his own view that one may not read outside the *beis ha-medrash* hours; he is giving voice to the position of Rabbi Nechemia, and it is *Shmuel k'Rabbi Nechemia*.

### Nehemia Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה: שָׁלֹשׁ מְחִיצּוֹת וְלֶחִי אֶחָד ״מְפוּלָּשׁ״ קָרֵית לֵיהּ? וְעוֹד, לְרַבָּנַן נַצִּיל לְתוֹכוֹ אוֹכָלִין וּמַשְׁקִין! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה: שְׁתֵּי מְחִיצּוֹת וּשְׁנֵי לְחָיַיִן — זֶהוּ מָבוֹי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְפוּלָּשׁ. שְׁתֵּי מְחִיצּוֹת וְלֶחִי אֶחָד — זֶהוּ מָבוֹי הַמְפוּלָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba said to him: Did you call an alley that has three walls and one post open? Even if Rabbi Eliezer does not permit carrying there, it is still not considered to be open but closed. And furthermore, according to the Rabbis, if this is so, let us rescue food and drinks by carrying them there as well. Since the Sages only permitted carrying in an alley that is not open, and because, according to all opinions, it is permitted to carry in a closed alley, one should also be allowed to save food and water, and not only a Torah scroll, by carrying them there. Rather, Rabba said: An alley that has two walls and two posts at both entrances to the alley is an alley that is not open. If it has two walls and one post, it is an alley that is open.

As the *baraita* teaches: *af al pi she'amru kisvei hakodesh ein korin bahem, aval shonin bahem ve-dorshin bahem* — even though one may not read from *kitvei kodesh*, one may learn from them and deliver *drashos* based on them. One reading of *ein korin* is that it prohibits a formal ritual reading but not oral discussion; another interpretation is that it prohibits reading from the written text while permitting oral engagement *ba'al peh*. The point in any case is that *Ketuvim* should not be read as one would become absorbed in a book. *Nitzrach lefasuk, meivi veroeh bo* — if one genuinely needs to look up a *pasuk*, one may do so.

Rabbi Nechemia's reason for the prohibition is entirely different from that of Rav and Shmuel: *kedei sheyomru kisvei hakodesh ein korin kol shekein bishtarei hedyotos* — so that people will say, if even *kitvei hakodesh* may not be read, certainly mundane documents and secular texts may not be read on *Shabbat*. On this approach, the prohibition is not about protecting attendance at the *beis ha-medrash*. It is a stringency applied to *Ketuvim* in order to signal that *Shabbat* is not a day for reading all manner of secular material. Accordingly, the prohibition applies regardless of time and place — whether or not the *beis ha-medrash* is in session.

### Secular Documents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַרְוַיְיהוּ אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. דְּתַנְיָא, יָתֵר עַל כֵּן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי בָתִּים בִּשְׁנֵי צִדֵּי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — עוֹשֶׂה לֶחִי מִיכָּן וְלֶחִי מִיכָּן, אוֹ קוֹרָה מִיכָּן וְקוֹרָה מִיכָּן, וְנוֹשֵׂא וְנוֹתֵן בָּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** And both of them hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. As it was taught in a *baraita*: Furthermore, Rabbi Yehuda said: One who has two houses opposite each other on two sides of the public domain, if he chooses, he may create a private domain for himself in the area of the public domain. He may place a ten-handbreadth high post from here, perpendicular to the public domain. This creates a symbolic wall which, in the *halakhot* of alleyways, has the legal status of a wall. And he may place an additional post from here, on the other side, and that has the same legal status as if he closed the public domain on all of its sides. Or, he can implement a different solution appropriate for alleyways by placing a beam extending from here, from one end of one house, to the end of the house opposite it. This creates a symbolic partition across the width of the street. And he may place a beam extending from here, from the other side of the house. According to Rabbi Yehuda, in that way, one is permitted to carry objects and place them in the area between the symbolic partitions, as he would in a private domain. The Rabbis said to him: One may not establish an *eiruv* in the public domain in that way.

The question then arises: what falls within the category of *shtarei hedyotos* — secular documents — whose reading on *Shabbat* is implicitly being forbidden? Rashi explains: *kegon shel cheshbonot* — accounting documents, business records, and also ordinary letters and personal correspondence, anything that is secular reading even if not strictly commercial.

Tosafot engages this question at some length. Regarding letters specifically, Rabbeinu Tam rules that if one does not yet know the contents, one may read it — perhaps there is an urgent matter requiring attention on *Shabbat* itself. If one already knows the contents, Tosafot reasons it is not really *shtarei hedyotos* in the operative sense, since one does not need what is written. Whatever the precise resolution, Tosafot ends with a broader statement attributed to Rabbeinu Yehuda: *osan milchamos haksuvim b'la'az* — reading stories of wars written in the vernacular, meaning novels or historical narratives in the everyday language, *nireh l'Rabbeinu Yehuda de'asur l'ayen bahen* — appears to be prohibited. The reason given is *dlo gara me'ha d'tanya perek shoel* — it is no less severe than the prohibition against reading the inscription beneath an image or portrait on *Shabbat*. And he adds, strikingly: *ve'afilu bechol lo yadati mi hitir* — even on a weekday, I do not know who permitted it — because of *moshav leitzim*, the prohibition against frivolous, debasing pastimes.

This is an observation that has its parallel in the *Shulchan Aruch* in the laws of *Shabbat*, where the discussion of prohibited secular reading similarly expands into a digression about reading romances and similar literature even during the week. The scope of what one may read on *Shabbat* — and on weekdays — emerges as a question with genuine halachic contours, shaped not only by concerns about the *beis ha-medrash* but by broader considerations about what kinds of engagement with the written word are appropriate for someone committed to a life of Torah.
