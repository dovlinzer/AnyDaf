# Zevachim 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mechusar Begadim

### Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וּמָטוּ בָּהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְחָגַרְתָּ אוֹתָם אַבְנֵט אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו, וְחָבַשְׁתָּ לָהֶם מִגְבָּעֹת, וְהָיְתָה לָהֶם כְּהֻנָּה לְחֻקַּת עוֹלָם״ – בִּזְמַן שֶׁבִּגְדֵיהֶם עֲלֵיהֶם, כְּהוּנָּתָם עֲלֵיהֶם; אֵין בִּגְדֵיהֶם עֲלֵיהֶם, אֵין כְּהוּנָּתָם עֲלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The *kohen mechusar begadim* — the kohen who is not properly attired in the priestly garments — and the consequences this has for his *avodah*. A priest **lacking** the requisite priestly **vestments** disqualifies the rites he performs. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **Rabbi Avuh says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says, and** some **determined it** to be stated **in the name of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon: As the verse states: "And you shall gird them with belts, Aaron and his sons, and bind mitres on them; and they shall have the priesthood by a perpetual statute"** (Exodus 29:9). The verse indicates that **when their vestments are on them, their priesthood is upon them,** but if **their vestments are not on them, their priesthood is not upon them** and their rites are disqualified.

Masechet Zevachim, daf 18a, opens by returning to the discussion of the *kohen mechusar begadim* — the kohen who is not properly attired in the priestly garments — and the consequences this has for his *avodah*. The previous session had examined two primary *pesukim*: one from Shemot 29 in Parshat Tetzaveh, addressing the priestly vestments, and another from Vayikra, following the incident of Nadav and Avihu, addressing the *kohen* who performs the *avodah* while intoxicated. A third passage, from Parshat Ki Tisa, concerns the washing of hands and feet. All three passages share the phrase *chok olam* or *chukat olam*, providing the basis for a *gezeirah shavah* that links them together.

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״חוּקָּה״–״חוּקָּה״ לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה. אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי עֲבוֹדָה דְּזָר חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ מִיתָה, אֲבָל עֲבוֹדָה דְּאֵין זָר חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ מִיתָה – אֵימָא לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. אַשְׁכְּחַן מְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים, שְׁתוּיֵי יַיִן מְנָלַן? אָתְיָא ״חוּקָּה״–״חוּקָּה״ מִמְּחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים. וְהָא תַּנָּא ״וּלְהַבְדִּיל בֵּין וְגוֹ׳״ קָא נָסֵיב לַהּ! מִקַּמֵּי דְּלֵיקוּם גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states "statute"–"statute"** in order **to derive** a **verbal analogy.** The Gemara explains: **If** one derived the *halakha* only **from** the verse regarding one lacking vestments, **I would say: This matter,** i.e., that the rites of one who lacks the requisite vestments are disqualified, applies only to **a rite for which a non-priest is liable** to receive the punishment of **death** at the hand of Heaven, since that is the topic of the passage discussing those who drank wine. **But** with regard to **a rite for which a non-priest is not liable** to receive **death** at the hand of Heaven, **I will say** that they are **not** subject to this *halakha*. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that the *halakha* applies to all rites. The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that **one lacking** the requisite **vestments** disqualifies all rites, even those for which a non-priest is not liable to receive the punishment of death at the hand of Heaven; **from where do we** derive that the *halakha* is the same for **those who drank wine,** as the passage in Leviticus (10:9–10) addresses only rites for which a non-priest receives the death penalty? The Gemara responds: It **is derived** by verbal analogy between the word **"statute"** used there and the word **"statute"** from** the verses discussing **one lacking** the requisite **vestments.** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't the *tanna*** of the aforementioned *baraita* **derive** that the rites of those who drank wine are disqualified from the verse: **"That you may put difference** between the holy and the common" (Leviticus 10:10), and not by verbal analogy to a priest lacking the requisite vestments? The Gemara responds: "That you may put difference between the holy and the common" is the source of this *halakha* only **before** the **verbal analogy stands.** Once the verbal analogy is derived, it is the source of the *halakha* with regard to those who drank wine as well.

The *gemara* now arrives at the word *ashkechan* — "I have found" — near the middle of the fourth line from the top of daf 18a. Having established the case of *mechusar begadim*, the *gemara* asks: *shtuyei yayin minalan* — from where do we derive that a drunk *kohen* invalidates the *avodah*? The answer is *atiah chuka chuka mi-mechusar begadim*: we learn it through a *gezeirah shavah* on the word *chukah*, drawing the laws of intoxication into parallel with those of improper dress. The passage from Vayikra regarding *u-lehavdil bein ha-kodesh u-vein ha-chol* is also invoked in this connection.

### Learning Direction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנָּא מְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים הוּא דְּקָא יָלֵיף מִשְּׁתוּיֵי יַיִן! הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁלֹּא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּין מְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים – לִשְׁתוּיֵי יַיִן וְשֶׁלֹּא רָחוּץ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״חוּקָּה״–״חוּקָּה״ לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But doesn't the *tanna*** of the *baraita* **derive** the *halakha* with regard to **one lacking** the requisite **vestments** itself **from** the case of **those who drank wine?** The Gemara responds: Actually, the disqualification of rites performed by one who drank wine is derived from the case of one lacking vestments. And **this** is what the *tanna* **is saying: From where** is it derived **that there is no distinction between one lacking** the requisite **vestments and those who drank wine and one whose hands and feet are not washed,** and that all three disqualify all rites? **The verses state** the word: **"Statute," "statute,"** in order **to derive** a **verbal analogy.**

The *gemara* then probes the direction of the learning: does the law of *mechusar begadim* derive from *shtuyei yayin*, or the reverse? The resolution is that the *gezeirah shavah* was established first from the existing *pesuk*, and only afterward was the *gezeirah shavah* itself applied. The deeper point of the *gezeirah shavah* is this: *minayin she-lo nechelku bein mechusar begadim le-shtuyei yayin ve-she-lo rachutz yadayim ve-raglayim* — from where do we know that there is no distinction among these three categories: one who lacks the proper garments, one who has consumed wine, and one whose hands and feet are unwashed? The answer is: *talmud lomar chuka chuka le-gezeirah shavah* — all three are bound together under the same *chukah*, equally disqualifying the *avodah*.

### No Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״לְהַבְדִּיל״ לְמָה לִי? לְכִדְרַב – דְּרַב לָא מוֹקֵים אָמוֹרָא עֲלֵיהּ מִיּוֹמָא טָבָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ, מִשּׁוּם שִׁכְרוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the *halakha* that one who drank wine disqualifies the service is derived from the verbal analogy, **why do I** need the verse: **"That you may put difference** between the holy and the common"? The Gemara responds: The verse is necessary **in accordance with** the custom **of Rav, as Rav** would **not place an interpreter before him,** i.e., he would not lecture in public, **from** the time that he drank wine on **one Festival** day **until** the **other,** the second Festival day, **due to drunkenness.** Rav was concerned that he would not issue a proper ruling, because it was customary to drink wine on the Festivals, and the verse states: "And that you may put difference between the holy and the common, and between the impure and the pure. And that you may teach the children of Israel all the statutes which the Lord has spoken" (Leviticus 10:10–11), indicating that one who drank wine may not issue a halakhic ruling.

This raises the question: once we have the *gezeirah shavah*, why do we still need the independent *pesukim*? The *gemara* answers that one of those verses — the passage in Vayikra about *u-lehavdil bein ha-kodesh u-vein ha-chol* — carries its own independent legal function, beyond what the *gezeirah shavah* alone could teach. The point is that the *gezeirah shavah* unifies the three cases, showing that in each of these domains — garments, wine, and hand-washing — there is no internal distinction: all failures under each category are equally invalidating.

### Rav's Translator

The *gemara* notes an additional lesson derived from the *pasuk* regarding *shtuyei yayin*: *de-Rav lo mokim amora aleih mi-Yom Tov le-chavereih mishum shichrut* — Rav would not position his translator beside him from one *Yom Tov* to the next, lest it appear that he was teaching *halacha* while possibly under the influence of wine. Rav's teaching method involved having an intermediary who would explain and expand upon his words to the audience — a practice that naturally would be suspended on any day when drinking was obligatory or likely. The pedagogical irony here is rich: by conspicuously removing his translator on those occasions, Rav was in effect teaching his students, through action, that one must not deliver *halachic* rulings when one has been drinking.

## Garment Sources

### Vayikra 1:7

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַכַּתִּי מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא?! מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא: ״וְנָתְנוּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֵן״ – בְּכִיהוּנּוֹ; לִימֵּד עַל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁלָּבַשׁ בִּגְדֵי כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט וְעָבַד, עֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה! אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי עֲבוֹדָה דִּמְעַכְּבָא כַּפָּרָה, אֲבָל עֲבוֹדָה דְּלָא מְעַכְּבָא כַּפָּרָה – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Still,** is the disqualification of rites performed by one lacking the requisite vestments **derived from here,** i.e., from the verse: "And you shall gird them with belts, Aaron and his sons" (Exodus 29:9)? **It is derived from there: "And the sons of Aaron the priest shall put** fire upon the altar" (Leviticus 1:7). The superfluous term "the priest" serves to indicate that he may serve only **in his priestly state.** The verse therefore **teaches** that **with regard to a High Priest who wore the vestments of an ordinary priest and performed** sacrificial **rites, his service is disqualified.** The Gemara responds: **If** one derived the *halakha* only **from there, I would say: This matter** applies only to **service that is indispensable for** effecting **atonement. But service that is not indispensable for** effecting **atonement,** e.g., putting fire upon the altar, is **not** subject to the *halakha*. Therefore, the verse (Leviticus 1:7) indicates that the *halakha* applies even to rites that are not indispensable.

The *gemara* now turns to an independent source for the laws of *mechusar begadim*, examining several *pesukim* from the opening of Vayikra. The first is Vayikra 1:7: *v'natnu bnei Aharon ha-kohen ve-archu etzim al ha-esh* — "And the sons of Aharon the kohen shall place wood upon the fire." The *gemara* asks: why is the word *ha-kohen* necessary here? We already know that the sons of Aharon are *kohanim*. The answer is that the *gemara* reads *bekihuno* — "in their priesthood" — meaning while properly invested in their priestly vestments. This superfluous term is therefore understood as a deliberate textual signal that the *kohanim* must be in their garments when performing the *avodah*.

### Deficiency Implication

From this superfluous *ha-kohen*, the *gemara* derives: *bekihuno limed al Kohen Gadol shelavash bigdei Kohen Hedyot v'avad avodato psulah* — it teaches that a *Kohen Gadol* who donned the garments of a *kohen hedyot* and performed the *avodah*, his service is *pasul*. This is the case of *chisur* — deficiency — where the *Kohen Gadol* is wearing less than his full complement of eight garments. One might think that this applies only to *avodot* that are *meakev kapara* — those that are indispensable to the atonement process — but not to auxiliary acts of service. The verse in Vayikra 1:7, however, describes the placing of wood on the altar — an act that is *not* among the *avodot meakev kapara* — and yet the same requirement applies. This extends the *halacha* of *mechusar begadim* beyond the core acts of atonement to the full range of priestly service. It is worth noting that this derivation appears in a *beraita* in the Sifra, *Torat Kohanim*, lending it additional authoritative weight.

### Vayikra 1:8

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא?! מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא: ״וְעָרְכוּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֲנִים אֵת הַנְּתָחִים וְגוֹ׳״ – ״הַכֹּהֲנִים״ בְּכִיהוּנָן; מִכָּאן לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁלָּבַשׁ בִּגְדֵי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְעָבַד – עֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה! אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי חִיסּוּר, אֲבָל יִיתּוּר – לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But still,** is the *halakha* **derived from here,** i.e., from all of the previous sources? **It is derived from there: "And the sons of Aaron, the priests, shall lay the pieces,** and the head, and the fat, in order upon the wood that is on the fire, which is upon the altar" (Leviticus 1:8). The superfluous term **"the priests"** serves to indicate that the priests may serve only **in their priestly state. From here** one derives that **with regard to an ordinary priest who wore the vestments of** the **High Priest and performed** sacrificial **rites, his service is disqualified.** The Gemara responds: **If** one derived the *halakha* only **from there, I would say: This matter** applies only to **a lack** of vestments, e.g., a High Priest who wore fewer than his requisite eight vestments, **but an excess** of vestments, e.g., an ordinary priest who wore more than his requisite four, is **not** subject to the *halakha*. This verse therefore **teaches us** that the *halakha* applies even to an excess of vestments.

The *gemara* continues: *ve-akati meihacha meihatam nafka* — but if we already have this source, can we not derive everything we need from it? It then introduces the next verse, Vayikra 1:8: *ve-archu bnei Aharon ha-kohanim et ha-netachim asher al ha-mizbeach* — "And the sons of Aharon, the *kohanim*, shall arrange the pieces upon the altar." Here again the word *ha-kohanim* appears, and again it is read as *ha-kohanim be-khunam* — the *kohanim* while in their priestly service.

### Excess Implication

From the second superfluous *ha-kohanim*, the *gemara* derives the opposite case: *mikan le-kohen hedyot shelavash bigdei kohen gadol v'avad avodato psulah* — a *kohen hedyot* who donned the garments of a *Kohen Gadol* and performed the *avodah*, his service too is *pasul*. This is the case of *yitur* — excess, wearing more than one's proper complement.

### Two Sources Needed

Why are two separate *pesukim* necessary? The *gemara* explains: if we had only the first source, we might conclude that the *halacha* applies only to *chisur* — deficiency in garments — but not to *yitur* — excess. A *kohen hedyot* wearing the *Kohen Gadol*'s garments is not missing anything; he has more than he needs. One might therefore think this would not be problematic. And conversely, had we only the second source, we might think only excess is addressed. It takes both *pesukim* together to establish that the principle is not one of quantity but of fitness: each *kohen* must wear precisely the garments designated for his level of service — neither more nor less.

## Rabbanan Position

### Condition List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיוּ מְרוּשָּׁלִין, מְסוּלָּקִין, מְשׁוּחָקִים – וְעָבַד; עֲבוֹדָתוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה. לָבַשׁ שְׁנֵי מִכְנָסַיִם, שְׁנֵי אַבְנֵטִים, חָסֵר אַחַת, יָתֵר אַחַת, אוֹ שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ רְטִיָּה עַל מַכַּת בְּשָׂרוֹ תַּחַת בִּגְדוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ מְטוּשְׁטָשִׁין אוֹ מְקוֹרָעִין – וְעָבַד; עֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** If the priest's vestments **were dragging** on the ground, or **raised up [*mesulakin*]** far from the ground, or **frayed, and** the priest **performed** sacrificial **rites** while wearing them, **his service is valid.** If **he wore two** pairs of **trousers** or **two belts,** or if he was **lacking one** of his requisite vestments, or if he wore **one extra** vestment, **or** in a case **where** a priest **had a bandage on a wound on his body under his vestment** such that the bandage acted as an interposition between the vestments and his skin, **or** if he wore vestments **that were soiled or ripped, and he performed** sacrificial **rites, his service is disqualified.**

A *tanna devei rabbanan* is now cited: *hayu merushalin mesulakin meshuchakim ve-avad avodato keshera* — if the garments were *merushalin* (too long, dragging), *mesulakin* (too short, raised), or *meshuchakin* (threadbare, worn), and the *kohen* performed the *avodah*, his service is nonetheless *kasher*. According to the *rabbanan*, these variations in the condition of the garments do not rise to the level of invalidating the *avodah*.

### Invalidating Conditions

The same *beraita* then enumerates what does invalidate the *avodah*: if the *kohen* wore two pairs of pants or two *avnetim* (belts); if he was missing one garment or added one beyond his proper complement; if he had a bandage (*retiyah*) on a wound beneath his garment, creating a *chatzitzah* — a separation between garment and body; or if the garments were *metushtashin* (soiled with mud) or *mekora'in* (torn). In all these cases, the *avodah* is *pesulah*.

### Mido Derivation

The source for the requirement that the garments fit the *kohen* precisely is the word *mido* in Parshat Tzav (Vayikra 6:3): *velavash ha-kohen mido bad* — the *kohen* shall wear his garment of linen. The word *mido* is read as *ke-midato* — according to his measure, his size. The garments must be made to fit the individual *kohen* precisely.

### Soiled and Torn

Rashi explains the cases of *metushtashin* and *mekora'in*: *metushtashin* means soiled with mud, and *mekora'in* means torn. Rashi comments on the torn garments: *ve-afilu einan chadashim, de-lav bigdo ha-ra'ui la-avodah, de-be'inan le-chavod u-le-tiferet* — even if they are not entirely new, what we require is that the garment be fitting for the *avodah*, and for that we need garments that reflect *kavod ve-tiferet* — honor and splendor. A threadbare garment that remains intact may still possess that dignity; a soiled or torn garment does not.

## Shmuel vs. Rav

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְרוּשָּׁלִין – כְּשֵׁרִין, מְסוּלָּקִין – פְּסוּלִין. וְהָתַנְיָא מְסוּלָּקִין כְּשֵׁרִין! אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן שֶׁסִּילְקָן עַל יְדֵי אַבְנֵט, כָּאן דְּלֵיתְנִיהוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** If the vestments are **dragging** on the ground, **they are fit,** but if they are **raised up** above the ground, **they are unfit.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in the above *baraita* that even if the vestments were **raised up they are fit? Rami bar Ḥama says:** This is **not difficult. Here,** the *baraita* deems them fit in a case **where** the priest **raised them up by** his **belt,** although they were initially the proper length; **there,** Shmuel deems them unfit in a case where **they do not initially** cover the priest's feet **at all.**

The *gemara* now records the opinion of Shmuel, transmitted by Rav Yehudah: *merushalin kesherin, mesulakin pesulin* — garments that are too long are *kasher*, garments that are too short are *pasul*. This is immediately challenged: but the *beraita* cited above rules that *mesulakin* are *kasher*! The *gemara* thus presents a direct contradiction between Shmuel and the *beraita*.

### Rami's Resolution

Rami bar Chama resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — kan she-silkan al yedei avnet, kan de-litin nafish* — there is no difficulty. In one case, the garment appeared short because it was gathered up by the *avnet* (the priestly belt), and in the other case, the fabric itself is genuinely insufficient in length. When the garment is the proper length but has been bunched up by the belt, it is *kasher*; when it is genuinely too short — when there is simply not enough material — it is *pasul*. The *avnet* thus serves a dual function: as a required component of the priestly dress and as a practical tool for adjusting the garment's fit. A longer garment gathered by the belt remains valid; but a garment that lacks material cannot be remedied by the belt, since cinching it would only make it shorter still.

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה – פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says:** In **both this** case **and that** case, whether they were dragging or raised up, **they are unfit.**

Rav takes a more stringent position: *echad zeh ve-echad zeh pesulin* — both too long and too short are *pasul*. This means that for Rav, the garments must fit correctly from the outset, and neither excess nor deficiency is tolerated. The underlying logic is consistent with the broader principle of *kavod ve-tiferet*: garments that do not fit properly are unworthy of the *avodah*, in either direction.

### Belt Function

Rashi's comment on Rav's position clarifies: *echad merushal ve-echad mesulakim yoter midai she-lo al yedei avnet* — whether too long or too short, when this is not a result of the *avnet*'s adjustment, it is *pasul*. This preserves the coherence of Rav's view: the *avnet* may play a role in fit, but only within limits. What the *avnet* cannot do is remedy a garment that is fundamentally the wrong size.

### Rav Huna Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אִיקְּלַע לְאַרְגִּיזָא, רְמָא לֵיהּ בַּר אוּשְׁפִּיזְכָנֵיהּ: מִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מְרוּשָּׁלִין כְּשֵׁרִין וּמְסוּלָּקִין פְּסוּלִין?! וְהָתַנְיָא: מְסוּלָּקִין – כְּשֵׁרִין! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בַּר מִינַּהּ דְּהַהִיא, דְּשַׁנְּיַיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא. אֶלָּא לְרַב – קַשְׁיָא! וְכִי תֵּימָא: מַאי מְרוּשָּׁלִין – מְסוּלָּקִין עַל יְדֵי אַבְנֵט, וְאַבְנֵט מֵיגָז אָגֵיז; אֶלָּא מְסוּלָּקִין קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts: **Rav Huna happened** to come **to Argiza. The son of his innkeeper [*oshpizekhaneih*] raised a contradiction** before **him: Did Shmuel** actually **say** that if the vestments are **dragging** on the ground, **they are fit, but** if they are **raised up** above the ground, **they are unfit? But isn't it taught** in the *baraita* that even if the vestments were **raised up they are fit?** Rav Huna **said to him:** Raise a contradiction from any source **apart from this** *baraita*, **as Rami bar Ḥama** already **answered** that **it** does not contradict Shmuel's statement, as it applies only to vestments that were initially the proper length. The Gemara asks: **But** doesn't the *baraita* pose **a difficulty for Rav,** who deems the vestments unfit even if they were dragging? **And if you would say: What** is the meaning of the word: **Dragging,** in the *baraita*? It means that they would initially drag but were **raised up by a belt** to the proper length, **and** they are fit since the **belt** effectively **trims** them, **but** then the term: **Raised up,** in the *baraita* poses **a difficulty.** Why should the *baraita* deem raised vestments fit? If the *baraita* is referring to vestments that were initially the proper length and were then raised up by a belt, then shouldn't they be unfit as the belt trims them?

The *gemara* records that Rav Huna happened upon a place called *Argiza*, where the son of his host posed the apparent contradiction: Shmuel holds that *merushalin* are *kasher* and *mesulakin* are *pasul*, yet a *beraita* rules *mesulakin kasher*? Rav Huna responded: *bar mina* — leave us alone with this; it has already been resolved by Rami bar Chama. The *gemara* notes, however, that while Rami bar Chama's resolution settles the question for Shmuel, it still creates a difficulty for Rav. One might propose on Rav's behalf that what the *beraita* calls *merushalin* refers to garments raised by the *avnet*, and that the *avnet* itself compensates for the additional length — but the *gemara* notes that if the garment was too long to begin with and was shortened by the *avnet*, the *avnet* itself is now covering that excess. What remains problematic, even after Rami bar Chama's answer, is the case for Rav where the garment is *mesulakin* without any *avnet* adjustment — genuinely short fabric with no mechanism for remedy. The question of whether an *avodah* performed in imperfectly fitting garments is *pasul be-di'avad* or only problematic *le-chatchila* runs throughout this discussion.

## Tzitzit Parallel

### Four Corners Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי: מְרוּשָּׁלִין שֶׁלֹּא סִילְּקָן – תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״עַל אַרְבַּע כַּנְפוֹת כְּסוּתְךָ״ – אַרְבַּע וְלֹא שָׁלֹשׁ. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא אַרְבַּע וְלֹא חָמֵשׁ? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אֲשֶׁר תְּכַסֶּה בָּהּ״ – הֲרֵי בַּעֲלַת חָמֵשׁ אָמוּר; הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים אַרְבַּע? אַרְבַּע וְלֹא שָׁלֹשׁ. וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת בַּעֲלַת חָמֵשׁ וּלְהוֹצִיא בַּעֲלַת שָׁלֹשׁ? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי בַּעֲלַת חָמֵשׁ – שֶׁיֵּשׁ בִּכְלַל חָמֵשׁ אַרְבַּע, וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי בַּעֲלַת שָׁלֹשׁ – שֶׁאֵין בִּכְלַל שָׁלֹשׁ אַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yirmeya of Difti says:** The case of **dragging** vestments **that** the priest **did not raise is** the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"On the four corners of your garment"** (Deuteronomy 22:12), from which it can be inferred: **Four, but not three,** i.e., a three-cornered garment is exempt from the obligation of ritual fringes. One may ask: **Or** perhaps **it is only** specifying **four, but not five? When it says** in the same verse: **"With which you cover yourself,"** a garment **of five** corners **is mentioned** as obligated. If so, **how do I realize** the meaning of: **"Four** corners"? It means **four, but not three. And what did you see** that led you **to include** a garment **of five** corners **and to exclude** a garment **of three** corners? **I include** a garment **of five** corners **as four is included in five, and I exclude** a garment **of three** corners **as four is not included in three.**

The *gemara* now imports an analogy from the laws of *tzitzit* in order to shed light on the garment debate. The relevant verse is Devarim 22, which commands: *al arba kanfot kesutcha asher techaseh bah gedilim taaseh lach* — "On the four corners of your garment with which you cover yourself, you shall make *gedilim*." Two terms in this verse appear to point in opposite directions: the phrase *arba kanfot* ("four corners") suggests a specific, bounded requirement, while *asher techaseh bah* ("with which you cover yourself") suggests a broader, more expansive application. The *gemara* presents two competing readings of this tension.

The first *tanna* asks: does the phrase "four corners" mean four and not three, or four and not five? This question determines whether the verse functions as a *ribui* (expansion) or a *miut* (restriction). The *tanna* reasons: since the verse adds *asher techaseh bah* — anything with which you cover yourself — this phrase comes to include even a five-cornered garment in the obligation of *tzitzit*. Why? Because *marbe ani ba'alat chamesh she-yesh bi-khlal chamesh arba* — I can include a five-cornered garment since five inherently contains four. By contrast, I can exclude a three-cornered garment: *she-ein bi-khlal shalosh arba* — three does not include four. The better reading, then, is that *arba* means four and not three, while the *ribui* extends the obligation even to garments with more than four corners.

### Five Corners Included

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״עַל אַרְבַּע כַּנְפוֹת כְּסוּתְךָ״ – אַרְבַּע וְלֹא שָׁלֹשׁ, אַרְבַּע וְלֹא חָמֵשׁ. מַאי, לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: יָתֵר כְּמַאן דְּאִיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: כְּמַאן דְּלֵיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי? לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כְּמַאן דְּאִיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי; וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּרַבִּי רַחֲמָנָא: ״אֲשֶׁר תְּכַסֶּה בָּהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita* that the verse states: **"On the four corners of your garment,"** from which it may be inferred: **Four, but not three,** and also **four, but not five,** i.e., only a four-cornered garment is obligated in the mitzva of ritual fringes. Rabbi Yirmeya continues: **What, is it not** that these *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to this: That** one **Sage,** who deems a five-cornered garment exempt, **holds** that **an extra** item **is considered as though it exists** and cannot be ignored, **and** one **Sage,** who deems it obligated, **holds** that it **is considered as though it does not exist** and the garment has only four corners? Accordingly, the first Sage deems a dragging priestly vestment unfit, since one cannot ignore the extra fabric, while the second Sage deems it fit since the extra fabric is treated as immaterial. The Gemara responds: **No, everyone** agrees that an extra piece of a garment **is considered as though it exists,** and therefore dragging vestments are unfit. **And** according to the *tanna* of the second *baraita*, it **is different here,** with regard to ritual fringes, **as the Merciful One amplifies** the *halakha* to obligate even five-cornered garments with the words **"with which you cover yourself."**

A second *beraita* holds the opposite: *arba velo shalosh, arba velo chamesh* — four and not three, four and not five. Only a four-cornered garment is obligated; garments with three or five corners are entirely exempt. The *gemara* asks whether this dispute reflects the broader question of *yeter ke-man de-ita dami* — does excess count as present, or as absent? That is, does a fifth corner mean the garment still "has" four corners and is therefore obligated, or is it so different from the paradigm that the obligation does not apply?

The *gemara* ultimately rejects this framing: *lo, de-kulei alma yeter ke-man de-ita dami* — everyone agrees that what is present is present. You cannot ignore a fifth corner by pretending it does not exist. The disagreement between the two *tanna'im* on *tzitzit* is thus not a dispute about excess as such. Rather, the different readings stem from how each *tanna* interprets the term *asher techaseh bah*: one derives from it a *ribui* that includes all covering garments, including those with more than four corners; the other does not read that term as a *ribui* at all.

This matters for the *kohen's* garments as well: one cannot argue that an excess of garment fabric should simply be "ignored" as if it were not there. Just as a five-cornered garment cannot be treated as a four-cornered one, a garment that is too long or too short cannot be treated as if it were the proper fit.

### Visibility Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, הַאי ״אֲשֶׁר תְּכַסֶּה בָּהּ״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״וּרְאִיתֶם אֹתוֹ״ – פְּרָט לִכְסוּת לַיְלָה. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא פְּרָט לִכְסוּת סוֹמֵא? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אֲשֶׁר תְּכַסֶּה בָּהּ״ – הֲרֵי כְּסוּת סוֹמֵא אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וּרְאִיתֶם אֹתוֹ״? פְּרָט לִכְסוּת לַיְלָה. וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת כְּסוּת סוֹמֵא וּלְהוֹצִיא כְּסוּת לַיְלָה? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי כְּסוּת סוֹמֵא – שֶׁיֶּשְׁנָהּ בִּרְאִיָּה אֵצֶל אֲחֵרִים, וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי כְּסוּת לַיְלָה – שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְאִיָּה אֵצֶל אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the other** Sage, who deems five-cornered garments exempt, **what does he do with this** verse: **"With which you cover yourself"?** The Gemara responds: **He requires it for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And it shall be unto you for a fringe, **that you may look upon it"** (Numbers 15:39). The phrase: "That you may look upon it," serves to **exclude a night garment** from the obligation of ritual fringes, as the fringes on such a garment cannot be seen. One might ask: **Or is it only** to **exclude the garment of a blind person,** who is unable to see the ritual fringes? **When it states** in the verse: **"With which you cover yourself"** (Deuteronomy 22:12), the obligation of ritual fringes for **the garment of a blind person is mentioned.** If so, **how do I realize** the meaning of the phrase: **"That you may look upon it"?** It serves to **exclude a night garment.** The *baraita* continues: **And what did you see** that led you **to include the garment of a blind person and to exclude a night garment** and not the reverse? **I include the garment of a blind person because it is** at least **visible to others, and I exclude a night garment because it is not visible,** even **to others.**

The *gemara* turns to the other verse about *tzitzit*, from Bamidbar 15: *u-re'item oto ve-haya lachem le-tzitzit* — "And you shall see it, and it shall be a *tzitzit* for you." From the word *u-re'item* — "and you shall see" — the *gemara* derives a visibility requirement. This raises the question: does *u-re'item* exclude nighttime garments (since one cannot see at night), or does it exclude the garments of a blind person (since he cannot see at all)?

### Night vs. Blind

The *gemara* resolves the question: *asher techaseh bah* comes to include the garments of a blind person in the obligation of *tzitzit*, because a blind person's garments are visible to others even if not to himself. And *u-re'item* serves to exclude nighttime garments, because at night neither the wearer nor others can see the *tzitzit*. The principle is: *marbe ani kesut suma she-yeshna be-re'iyah etzel acherim* — I include the blind person's garments since they exist in the realm of visibility from the perspective of others; *umotzi ani kesut layla she-einah be-re'iyah etzel acherim* — and I exclude night garments since they are not visible to anyone. This is, as noted, what makes *tzitzit* a *mitzvat aseh she-ha-zman grama*, a time-bound positive commandment applicable only during the day.

### Textual Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ – נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ״אֲשֶׁר״. וְאִידַּךְ – ״אֲשֶׁר״ לָא דָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the other** Sage, who derives that a five-cornered garment requires ritual fringes from the phrase: "With which you cover yourself," from where does he derive that the garment of a blind person requires ritual fringes? The Gemara responds: **He derives it from** the word **"which"** in the phrase, as that term itself connotes an amplification of the *halakha*. The Gemara asks: **And** what does **the other** Sage do with this word? The Gemara responds: **He does not interpret** the word **"which"** as an amplification.

One *tanna* derives the inclusion of five-cornered garments from the word *asher* in *asher techaseh bah*, reading it as a textual expansion. The other *tanna* does not treat *asher* as a source for *drash* at all — *asher lo darish* — and therefore draws no expansion from it.

## Linen Requirements

### Bad Qualities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בַּד – שֶׁיִּהְיוּ שֶׁל בּוּץ; ״בַּד״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ חֲדָשִׁים; ״בַּד״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ שְׁזוּרִים; ״בַּד״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ חוּטָן כָּפוּל שִׁשָּׁה; ״בַּד״ – שֶׁלֹּא יִלְבַּשׁ שֶׁל חוֹל עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** with regard to the priestly vestments that the term: "Linen [*bad* ]" (Leviticus 6:3), used in the verse indicates several properties of the garments: The verse states **"linen"** to indicate **that they** must **be** made **of fine linen [*butz*]; "linen," that they** must **be new; "linen," that** their thread must **be twisted** of several plies; **"linen," that their thread** must **be folded six** times; **"linen," that** the priest may **not wear non-sacred** clothes along **with them.**

The *gemara* cites a new *tanu rabbanan* enumerating the qualities required of the priestly linen. The word *bad* appears repeatedly in the Torah in connection with the priestly garments — once in Parshat Tetzaveh, twice in Parshat Tzav, and four times in Parshat Acharei Mot — and each repetition is taken to teach something. The *beraita* derives five requirements: *sheyihyu shel butz* — the fabric must be fine linen; *sheyihyu chadashim* — new; *sheyihyu shezurim* — twisted or woven; *sheyihyu chutan kaful shisha* — the threads folded sixfold; and *shelo yilbash chol imahem* — no secular garment may be worn together with them.

### New vs. Worn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: בִּשְׁלָמָא שֶׁיְּהוּ שֶׁל בּוּץ – הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: בּוּץ אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא. אֶלָּא ״בַּד״ שֶׁיְּהוּ חֲדָשִׁים – חֲדָשִׁים אִין, שְׁחָקִין לָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא: מְשׁוּחָקִין – כְּשֵׁרִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: Granted,** the statement **that they** must **be of fine linen** is understood; **this** requirement **teaches us** that if they are of **fine linen** they **are** fit, but if they are of **something else** they are **not. But** with regard to the statement: **Linen, that they** must **be new,** one can infer that if they are **new** they **are** fit but if they are **frayed** they are **not. But isn't it taught** in the *baraita* (18a) that even if they were **frayed** they **are fit?**

Abaye raises a challenge to Rav Yosef: the requirement that the garments be *chadashim* — new — implies that worn garments are *pasul*. Yet we have the *beraita* cited earlier, stating that *meshuchakim kesherin* — worn garments are *kasher*! This is a direct contradiction.

### Bad Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, ״בַּד״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ חוּטָן כָּפוּל שִׁשָּׁה?! ״בַּד״ – חַד חַד לְחוֹדֵיהּ מַשְׁמַע! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: בְּגָדִים שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״בַּד״ – צְרִיכִין שֶׁיְּהוּ שֶׁל בּוּץ, חֲדָשִׁים, שְׁזוּרִין, שֶׁיְּהֵא חוּטָן כָּפוּל שִׁשָּׁה; יֵשׁ מֵהֶן לְמִצְוָה, יֵשׁ מֵהֶן לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him: And according to your reasoning** that the *baraita* intends to derive all of these requirements from the word linen, such that all of these requirements are indispensable, how can one understand the requirement: **Linen, that their thread** must **be folded six** times? The word ***bad*** itself **means each one on its own** (see Exodus 30:34). **Rather, this** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** Those **garments with regard to which it is stated: Linen, must be** made **of fine linen,** and they must be **new** and **twisted,** and **their thread** must **be folded six** times. **Some of** these requirements constitute **a mitzva** *ab initio*, and **some of them are indispensable.**

Rav Yosef deflects the challenge by reframing it. He points out that the word *bad* itself, etymologically, suggests singularity — related to *levad* and *badad*, meaning alone or separate. *Bad bad chad chad le-chudei mashma* — each instance of *bad* denotes something single, individual, and non-splitting. This refers to the nature of the flax stalk: it grows as a single, discrete stalk, unlike wool, which splits and branches. The word *bad* thus characterizes the material's origin and structure, not a prohibition of worn garments. What the *beraita* is saying is: wherever the Torah uses the word *bad* in reference to priestly garments, it is specifying a material with these particular qualities — fine, new, twisted, sixfold thread, and worn without secular admixture.

### Chumra vs. Meakev

The *gemara* then distinguishes among these requirements: *yesh me-hen le-mitzvah ve-yesh me-hen le-akev* — some of these are ideally required (*le-mitzvah*, as a *chumra*) while others are *me-akev* — indispensable, such that their absence invalidates the *avodah*. The precise demarcation between the two categories is left to be worked out, but the overall point is that not every specification derived from the repetition of *bad* carries equal weight in *halacha*.

### Bad is Flax

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי דְּהַאי ״בַּד״ – כִּתָּנָא הוּא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵף בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: דָּבָר הָעוֹלֶה מִן הַקַּרְקַע בַּד בְּבַד. אֵימָא עַמְרָא! עַמְרָא מִיפְּצֵל. כִּיתָּנָא נָמֵי מִיפְּצֵל! עַל יְדֵי לָקוּתָא מִיפְּצֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** is it known **that this** material ***bad* is** produced from the **flax** plant? **Rabbi Yosef, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** The verse is referring to **an item that grows from the ground** with **each** stalk growing **on its own [*bad bevad*],** i.e., it does not split into multiple stalks. The flax plant fulfills this criterion. The Gemara asks: Why not **say** that it is **wool?** The Gemara responds: The individual **wool** fibers **split** into smaller fibers. The Gemara rejects this: But **flax also splits.** The Gemara responds: **It splits** only **by being struck.** Wool, by contrast, splits naturally.

The *gemara* then asks: how do we know that the term *bad* refers specifically to flax? The word in the Torah just says *bad*. From where do we know it is not wool or some other material? Rabbi Yosef b'Rabbi Chanina answers: *davar ha-oleh min ha-karka bad be-vad* — we need a material that grows from the ground with each stalk growing on its own, *bad be-vad*, not splitting into multiple strands. Flax naturally fulfills this criterion. Wool does not: *amra miftzal* — wool splits and branches. One might still ask: does not flax also split? The *gemara* answers: *al yedei lakuta miftzil* — flax only separates when beaten in processing, not naturally of its own accord. Its natural state is the single stalk, and that is what qualifies it as *bad*.

### Yechezkel Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״פַּאֲרֵי פִשְׁתִּים יִהְיוּ עַל רֹאשָׁם, וּמִכְנְסֵי פִשְׁתִּים יִהְיוּ עַל מׇתְנֵיהֶם, לֹא יַחְגְּרוּ בַּיָּזַע״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says** that the identity of *bad* is derived **from here:** The verse states with regard to the priestly vestments: **"They shall have linen [*pishtim*] crowns upon their heads, and shall have linen breeches upon their loins; they shall not gird themselves with *yaza*"** (Ezekiel 44:18). The word *pishtim* is clearly referring to flax.

Ravina offers a further source for identifying *bad* as flax, citing Yechezkel 44:18: *pe'arei pishtim yihyu al rosham u-michnesei pishtim yihyu al motneihem ve-lo yachgeru be-yaza* — "They shall wear linen (*pishtim*) crowns upon their heads and linen pants on their waists, and they shall not gird themselves in a place of sweat." *Pishtim* — flax — is explicitly named in Yechezkel as the material of the priestly garments, and *pishtim* is unambiguously flax.

### Ravina Citation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרָבִינָא: וְהָא עַד דַּאֲתָא יְחֶזְקֵאל – מְנָלַן? וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דָּבָר זֶה – מִתּוֹרַת מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ לֹא לָמַדְנוּ, מִדִּבְרֵי יְחֶזְקֵאל בֶּן בּוּזִי לָמַדְנוּ: ״כׇּל בֶּן נֵכָר עֶרֶל לֵב וְעֶרֶל בָּשָׂר לֹא יָבֹא אֶל מִקְדָּשִׁי לְשָׁרְתֵנִי״; עַד שֶׁבָּא יְחֶזְקֵאל מְנָלַן? אֶלָּא גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ – וַאֲתָא יְחֶזְקֵאל וְאַסְמְכֵיהּ אַקְּרָא; הָכָא נָמֵי גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Ravina: But before Ezekiel came, from where did we** derive the identity of *bad*? Ravina responded: **And according to your reasoning,** one could ask the same of **that which Rav Ḥisda said** with regard to the prohibition against Temple service by one who is uncircumcised or an apostate: **We did not learn this** following **matter from the Torah of Moses, our teacher; we learned** it **from the words of Ezekiel, son of Buzi: "No stranger, uncircumcised in heart or uncircumcised in flesh, shall enter into My Sanctuary to serve Me"** (Ezekiel 44:9). **Until Ezekiel came, from where did we** derive this? **Rather,** this *halakha* **is learned as a tradition** and therefore was observed for generations, **and Ezekiel came and gave it support by** writing **a verse. Here too, it is learned as a tradition,** and Ezekiel came and gave it support by writing a verse.

Rav Ashi raises an objection to Ravina's proof: *ve-ha ad de-ata Yechezkel minalan* — but Yechezkel was written some 480 years after the *mishkan* was established and many centuries after the laws of the priestly vestments were given at Sinai. How could the earlier generations have derived this requirement from Yechezkel? The same question applies to Rav Chisda's teaching elsewhere in our *masechet*: *davar zeh mi-torat Moshe Rabbeinu lo lamadnu, mi-divrei Yechezkel ben Buzi lamadnu* — there are things we did not learn from the Torah of Moshe, but from the words of Yechezkel. The answer is: *ela gemara gemiri lei* — the practice was already known by tradition. Yechezkel did not originate the requirement; he provided a scriptural anchor (*asmachta*) for a practice that had been transmitted all along: *ve-ata Yechezkel ve-asmachei akra* — Yechezkel came and gave it a textual grounding, so that later generations would have a *pasuk* to point to, even though the practice itself predated his prophecy.

## Belt Placement

### Sweat Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״לֹא יַחְגְּרוּ בַּיָּזַע״? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא יַחְגְּרוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁמְּזִיעִין. כִּדְתַנְיָא: כְּשֶׁהֵם חוֹגְרִין, אֵין חוֹגְרִין לֹא לְמַטָּה מִמׇּתְנֵיהֶן, וְלֹא לְמַעְלָה מֵאַצִּילֵיהֶן, אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד אַצִּילֵי יְדֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase in the verse: **"They shall not gird themselves with *yaza*"? Abaye said: They shall not gird themselves in a place** in **which** people **sweat [*mezi'in*]. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **When they gird** themselves with the belt, **they may not gird** themselves **below their loins nor above their elbows,** but **rather** **at the level of their elbows.**

The *gemara* returns to the verse from Yechezkel: *ve-lo yachgeru be-yaza* — they shall not gird themselves with *yaza*. Abaye interprets: *lo yachgeru be-makom she-maziin* — they must not place the *avnet* in the part of the body where one sweats. This is the practical *halacha* derived from the verse: the belt must not be positioned at a spot where perspiration would accumulate beneath it, for this would compromise both the dignity and the physical integrity of the priestly garment.

### Elbow Height

A *beraita* elaborates: *ein chogrin lo le-mata mi-motneihem ve-lo le-mala me-atzileihem* — when the *kohanim* gird themselves, they must not do so below the waist and not above the height of the elbows. The area below the waist and the area above the elbows are places where folds of flesh may collect and cause sweating. *Ela ke-neged atzilei yedeihem* — the correct position is level with the elbows. This is where the *avnet* must sit.

### King Izgadar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אָמַר לִי הוּנָא בַּר נָתָן: זִימְנָא חֲדָא הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּאִיזְגַּדַּר מַלְכָּא וַהֲוָה מִדְּלֵי לִי הֶמְיָינַאי, וְתַיְתְיֵיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, וַאֲמַר לִי: ״מַמְלֶכֶת כֹּהֲנִים וְגוֹי קָדוֹשׁ״ כְּתִיב בְּכוּ. כִּי אֲתַאי קַמֵּיהּ דְּאַמֵּימָר, אֲמַר לִי: אִקַּיַּים בְּךָ ״וְהָיוּ מְלָכִים אֹמְנַיִךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: Huna bar Natan said to me: Once, I was standing before Izgadar, king** of Persia, **and my belt was raised** above its appropriate height, **and he lowered it** into place **and said to me: "A kingdom of priests, and a holy nation"** (Exodus 19:6), **is written about you;** therefore, you should always look dignified. **When I came before Ameimar** and recounted this incident, **he said to me: With regard to you,** God's promise to Israel: **"And kings shall be your foster fathers"** (Isaiah 49:23), **was fulfilled.**

Rav Ashi transmits a remarkable story in the name of Huna bar Natan. On one occasion, Huna bar Natan was standing before *Izgadar*, the Persian king, when his belt (*hemyani*) was not sitting properly. The king himself reached over and adjusted it for him. Then *Izgadar* said to him: it is written about you, before the giving of the Torah in Shemot 19, *mamlechet kohanim ve-goy kadosh* — "a kingdom of priests and a holy nation." The implication is clear: the proper wearing of one's garments and the dignity of one's bearing are not merely requirements of the *kohen* alone, but of the entire Jewish people, who are themselves designated a *mamlechet kohanim*.

### Priestly Nation

Huna bar Natan then brought this exchange before Amemar, who said to him: *akayem becha* — fulfilled in you is the verse from Yeshayahu 49: *ve-hayu melachim omnecha* — "And kings shall be your foster parents," meaning kings will guide you and teach you who you are. It should be noted that Huna bar Natan was himself the *Reish Galuta* at the time of Amemar and Rav Ashi, which gives this story an added dimension: even the leader of the exile, standing before a gentile king, is reminded through that encounter of Israel's fundamental vocation. The entire extended discussion of priestly garments — their fit, their material, their proper placement — thus closes with a broadening of perspective: the dignity encoded in these precise requirements is not the exclusive province of those who serve in the *Beit Hamikdash*, but reflects the calling of an entire nation.
