# Kiddushin 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chashmonai Testimony

### Slave Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי (אֲתָא הַהוּא בַּר דִּינֵיהּ מִנְּהַרְדָּעֵי). אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא בַּר דִּינֵיהּ לְרַב יְהוּדָה: לְדִידִי קָרֵית לִי ״עַבְדָּא״, דְּאָתֵינָא מִבֵּית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי מַלְכָּא?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּל דְּאָמַר מִדְּבֵית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי קָאָתֵינָא – עַבְדָּא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the story: **Meanwhile, that litigant arrived from Neharde'a. That litigant said to Rav Yehuda: You call me a slave? I, who come from the house of the Hasmonean kings?** Rav Yehuda **said to him: This is what Shmuel says: Anyone who says: I come from the house of the Hasmonean** kings, **is a slave.** As will be explained, only slaves remained of their descendants.

The daf begins in the middle of 71b, six lines down from the top of the wide text, at the word *Chashmonai*. We are in the midst of a narrative involving Rav Yehuda and Rav Huna, centered on a case of *din Torah* that arose when a certain man came to Pumbedita, insulted Rav Yehuda's attendant, and was himself known to speak disparagingly of others — calling people slaves. In response, Rav Yehuda declared this man to be a slave. The broader backdrop of this story is the principle of *kol haposel bemumo posel* — one who invalidates others does so because of his own defect.

As established in the previous daf, Rav Huna challenged Rav Yehuda's declaration, arguing that even if Shmuel taught *kol haposel bemumo posel*, this teaching should at most make one *choshesh* — cautiously alert — but not empower a formal declaration of slavery. At that very moment, the man in question entered the room and declared indignantly that he was a descendant of *Beit Chashmonai*. Rav Yehuda seized on this: precisely as I said! Because Shmuel himself taught that anyone who claims descent from *Beit Chashmonai* is a slave, since the original Hasmonean dynasty was entirely killed off, and it was only Herod — an Edomite slave — who remained and identified with that lineage. Therefore, any contemporary claim to Hasmonean descent is itself evidence of slave ancestry.

### Kol Haposel Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר לְהָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: כׇּל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁמּוֹרֶה הֲלָכָה וּבָא, אִם קוֹדֶם מַעֲשֶׂה אֲמָרָהּ – שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא אִיכָּא רַב מַתְנָה דְּקָאֵי כְּווֹתִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman, who heard this exchange, **said to** Rav Yehuda: **Does the Master not hold in accordance with this** *halakha* **that Rabbi Abba says** that **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **any Torah scholar who proceeds to teach** a ruling of ***halakha*** with regard to a particular issue, **if he said it before an action** that concerns himself occurred, **they** should **listen to him,** and his ruling is accepted. **But if not,** if he quoted the *halakha* only after he was involved in an incident related to the *halakha* he is quoting, **they do not listen to him,** due to his personal involvement? Your testimony with regard to what Shmuel ruled should be ignored, as you stated it only after the incident. Rav Yehuda **said to** Rav Naḥman: **There is Rav Mattana, who stands by my** report, since he has also heard this ruling of Shmuel.

The Gemara now raises a pointed challenge to Rav Yehuda's reliance on this teaching. Rav Huna said to him: do you not hold by the principle that Rav Abba cited in the name of Rav, who cited it in the name of Rav Huna — who cited it in the name of Rav? This is already somewhat striking: Rav Huna is quoting a teaching transmitted in his own name, from Rav, the classic *bar plugta* of Shmuel. The teaching states: *kol talmid chacham shemoreh halacha uva* — any *talmid chacham* who advances a new legal ruling, *im kodem maaseh amra shomin lo* — if he taught it before the relevant case arose, he is believed. But *im lav ein shomin lo* — if he produces the ruling only at the moment when the case conveniently arises before him, we are somewhat suspicious of it.

This is a remarkable piece of Talmudic skepticism. The Gemara acknowledges that a scholar might fabricate a tradition in the name of his teacher precisely to respond to a case at hand. In our story, the suspicion is compounded: Rav Yehuda was not merely citing a teaching that happened to be relevant — he was invoking it specifically to vindicate his own prior ruling. He declared the man a slave, and now he produces a teaching of Shmuel that justifies that declaration. One might fairly ask: why have we never heard this teaching before?

### Rav Matna Confirms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מַתְנָה לָא חַזְיַיהּ לִנְהַרְדְּעָא תְּלֵיסַר שְׁנֵי. הָהוּא יוֹמָא אֲתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּכִיר מָר מַאי אֲמַר שְׁמוּאֵל כִּי קָאֵי חֲדָא כַּרְעָא אַגּוּדָּא וַחֲדָא כַּרְעָא בְּמַבָּרָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּל דְּאָמַר מִדְּבֵית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי מַלְכָּא קָאָתֵינָא – עַבְדָּא הוּא, דְּלָא אִישְׁתְּיוּר מִינַּיְיהוּ אֶלָּא הַהִיא רְבִיתָא דְּסָלְקָא לְאִיגָּרָא וְרָמְיָא קָלָא וְאָמְרָה: כֹּל דְּאָמַר מִבֵּית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי אֲנָא – עַבְדָּא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Rav Mattana had not seen** the city of **Neharde'a** for **thirteen years. That** very **day he arrived.** Rav Yehuda **said to him:** Does **the Master remember what Shmuel said when he was standing** with **one foot on the bank and one foot on the ferry?** Rav Mattana **said to him: This is what Shmuel said** at that time: **Anyone who says: I come from the house of the Hasmonean** kings, **is a slave, as none remained of them except for that young girl who ascended to the roof and raised her voice and said:** From now on, **anyone who says: I come from the house of the Hasmonean** kings, **is a slave.** Other than this girl, the only members of the family who remained were descendants of Herod, and he was an Edomite slave.

To this challenge, Rav Yehuda responded: *ha'ika Rav Masna dekay kivasay* — Rav Matna holds like me. Rav Matna can corroborate that this is indeed a teaching of Shmuel. It then happened that Rav Matna, who had not been in Nehardea — the location of these proceedings — for the past thirteen years, arrived that very day. *Hahu yoma* — by pure coincidence, on that exact day, he came to Nehardea.

Rav Yehuda then had to prompt Rav Matna without leading him, and he did so elegantly: do you remember what Shmuel taught *ki kachada* — on that occasion when he was stepping onto the ferry, one foot still on the bank and one foot already on the vessel? The memorable physical circumstance made the teaching verifiable and distinctive. Rav Matna confirmed: *hachi amar Shmuel* — Shmuel said: *kol d'omar midebeis Chashmonai moga chasina avda hu* — anyone who says he descends from the royal house of the Hasmoneans is a slave.

### Woman on Roof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְפַלָה מֵאִיגָּרָא וּמִיתָה. אַכְרוּז עֲלֵיהּ דְּעַבְדָּא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The girl then **fell from the roof and died,** leaving only slaves from the Hasmoneans. With the confirmation of the report of the statement of Shmuel, **they** also **publicized** in Neharde'a **about him,** i.e., that man who claimed to come from the Hasmonean kings, **that he was a slave.**

Rav Matna then provided the reason for Shmuel's ruling. He recalled that after Herod's violent overthrow of the Hasmonean house, only one young woman remained from the original dynasty. When she saw what Herod had done — that the entire family had been slaughtered — she went up to the roof, *v'ramya kala*, and called out: let it be known that anyone who claims descent from the house of *Chashmonai* is a slave, for there is no one left. And then, *nafla m'igra umesa* — she fell from the roof and died. With this, the entire Hasmonean lineage was extinguished, and Herod — the Edomite slave — was the only one who identified himself with that heritage.

Rav Yehuda had now been vindicated on all fronts.

### Ketuvot Torn Up

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא יוֹמָא אִקַּרְעָן כַּמָּה כְּתוּבָּתָא בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא. כִּי קָא נָפֵיק, נָפְקִי אַבָּתְרֵיהּ לְמִירְגְּמֵיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי (שְׁתִיקוּ) [שָׁתְקִיתוּ] – שְׁתִיקוּ, וְאִי לָא מְגַלֵּינָא עֲלַיְיכוּ הָא דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל תַּרְתֵּי זַרְעֲיָיתָא אִיכָּא בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא: חֲדָא מִיקַּרְיָא דְּבֵי יוֹנָה, וַחֲדָא מִיקַּרְיָא דְּבֵי עוֹרַבְתִּי. וְסִימָנָיךְ: טָמֵא – טָמֵא, טָהוֹר – טָהוֹר. שַׁדְיוּהּ לְהָהוּא רִיגְמָא מִידַיְיהוּ וְקָם אַטְמָא בִּנְהַר מַלְכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: On **that day, several marriage contracts were torn up in Neharde'a,** as many had their marriages annulled after having discovered that they had married slaves. **When** Rav Yehuda **was leaving** Neharde'a, **they pursued him,** seeking **to stone him,** as because of him it was publicized that their lineage was flawed. Rav Yehuda **said to them: If you are silent,** remain **silent. And if** you will **not** remain silent, **I will reveal about you this** statement **that Shmuel said: There are two** lines of **offspring in Neharde'a. One is called the dove's house, and one is called the raven's house. And your mnemonic** with regard to lineage is: The **impure** bird, the raven, is **impure,** meaning flawed, and the **pure** one, the dove, is **pure,** meaning unflawed. Upon hearing this, **they threw** all **those** stones that they were intending **to stone him** with **from their hands,** as they did not want him to reveal who had a flawed lineage. **And** as a result of all of the stones thrown into the river, **a dam arose in the Malka River.**

*Achrizu alei d'avda hu* — they announced about this man that he was indeed a slave, exactly as Rav Yehuda had originally declared. The consequences were severe: *hahu yoma akran kama ksuva beNardaa* — on that day, many *ketubot* were torn up in Nehardea.

Rashi explains that the members of this man's family, having now been identified as slaves, could not have contracted valid *kiddushin*, and so those marriages required invalidation. There is an obvious problem, however: the man's testimony applies only to himself — there is no independent, objective evidence that implicates his family members. How could their marriages be invalidated on the basis of his own admission? One commentator resolves this by suggesting that the *ketubot* were torn up for a different reason: he had served as a signatory on those documents, and now that he had been declared *pasul*, his testimony was rendered invalid, requiring the documents to be redrawn. Yet the narrative suggests something more sweeping.

As Rav Yehuda departed Nehardea to return to Pumbedita, the families whose marriages had been disrupted ran after him intending to stone him — literally, stones were in their hands. He turned to them and said: *ei shtiku shtiku* — if you stay silent, stay silent. But if you continue making a fuss, *maglina alaichu* — I will reveal something about you that is even worse. What could be worse than being declared slaves? Shmuel, he said, also taught: *tarti zarayasa ika beNardaa* — there are two families in Nehardea, one called the house of the dove and one called the house of the raven — *v'simanach tamei tamei tahor tahor* — the impure family bears the name of the impure bird, and the pure family bears the name of the pure bird. Those descended from the raven are of invalid lineage — *mamzerim*, slaves, or the like.

How exactly this threat made things worse if they were already declared slaves is somewhat unclear from the text; perhaps the formal *halachic* ruling against the wider family had not yet been made and remained merely a suspicion. Regardless, the message landed. *Shadu alahu rigma* — they threw down the stones they had intended to hurl at him, and so many stones fell into the *Nehar Malka* that they formed a dam in the river.

## Invalid Families

### Announced Invalidities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַכְרִיז רַב יְהוּדָה בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא: אַדָּא וְיוֹנָתָן – עַבְדֵי. יְהוּדָה בַּר פָּפָּא – מַמְזֵירָא, בָּטִי בַּר טוֹבִיָּה בְּרָמוּת רוּחָא לָא שָׁקֵיל גִּיטָּא דְחֵירוּתָא. מַכְרֵיז רָבָא בְּמָחוֹזָא: בַּלָּאֵי, דַּנָּאֵי, טַלָּאֵי, מַלָּאֵי, זַגָּאֵי – כּוּלָּם לִפְסוּל. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: גּוֹבָאֵי – גִּבְעוֹנָאֵי, דּוּרְנוּנִיתָא – דָּרָאֵי נְתִינָאֵי. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַאי בֵּי כוּבֵּי דְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא – כּוּלָּם דְּעַבְדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues the discussion of those with a flawed lineage: **Rav Yehuda proclaimed in Pumbedita: Adda and Yonatan,** known residents of that town, are **slaves; Yehuda bar Pappa** is a ***mamzer*; Bati bar Tuviyya, in his arrogance, did not accept a bill of manumission** and is still a slave. **Rava proclaimed in** his city of **Meḥoza: Balla'ai, Danna'ai, Talla'ai, Malla'ai, Zagga'ai: All** these families **are** of **flawed** lineage. **Rav Yehuda** likewise **says: Gova'ai,** the inhabitants of a place called Gova, are in fact **Gibeonites,** and their name has been corrupted. Similarly, those people known as **Dorenunita** are from **the village of Gibeonites,** and they may not marry Jews with unflawed lineage. **Rav Yosef says:** With regard to **this** place called **Bei Kuvei of Pumbedita,** its residents **are all** descendants **of slaves.**

The foregoing story raises an important question: if Rav Yehuda knew about the invalidity of these Nehardean families, why had he been withholding the information? We will return shortly to the principle of *mishpacha keivan shenitm'ah nitm'ah* — once a family's lineage has been submerged, one leaves it submerged. That principle stands in tension with what we have just witnessed. In the present story, the declaration of slavery came only as a response to the man's own behavior — *kol haposel bemumo posel* — and it happened to be borne out. Nevertheless, the Gemara now pivots in the opposite direction, moving into a series of proactive public announcements about people and places of problematic lineage.

*Machriz Rav Yehuda* — Rav Yehuda publicly announced, according to one textual tradition in Pumbedita and according to another in Nehardea: *Ada veYonatan avdi* — these two individuals are slaves; *Yehuda bar Pappa mamzer* — this person is a *mamzer*; *Bati bar Tuvia berama srucha deshakil gitei* — this wealthy man, universally assumed to be of excellent *yichus*, was actually offered a *shtar shichrur*, a deed of manumission, but refused to accept it because of his pride in his high social standing. Nonetheless, he is halachically a slave. Here Rav Yehuda is not merely responding to a case — he is proactively labeling individuals as *pasul*.

### Rava's Declarations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת עֲבָדִים, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אַרְבַּעַת אֲלָפִים עֲבָדִים הָיוּ לוֹ לְפַשְׁחוּר בֶּן אִימֵּר, וְכוּלָּם נִטְמְעוּ בַּכְּהוּנָּה. וְכׇל כֹּהֵן שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ עַזּוּת פָּנִים אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא מֵהֶם. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כּוּלְּהוּ יָתְבָן בְּשׁוּרָא דְבִנְהַרְדְּעָא. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אִם רָאִיתָ כֹּהֵן בְּעַזּוּת מֵצַח – אַל תְּהַרְהֵר אַחֲרָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעַמְּךָ כִּמְרִיבֵי כֹהֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: Four hundred slaves, and some say four thousand slaves, were owned by Pashḥur ben Immer,** a priest in the time of Jeremiah, **and** due to their greatness **they were assimilated into the priesthood** and became known as priests. **And any priest who has** the trait of **insolence is only from them. Abaye said: They all sit in the rows** of honor **that are in** the city of **Neharde'a.** The Gemara comments: And this statement **disagrees with** the statement **of Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar says: If you see an insolent priest, do not speculate about him** that he may be of flawed lineage, **since it is stated: "For your people are as those who strive with a priest"** (Hosea 4:4), which indicates that priests had a reputation for being cantankerous.

*Machriz Rava beMechuza* — Rava similarly made public announcements in Mechuza, his own city: *Belai Danai Talai Malai Zagai kulam lepsul* — these families are all of invalid status, whether *mamzer*, *avdus*, or some other form of disqualification. Rav Yehuda further identified whole towns: *Gvai* is a corruption of *Givonai* — the inhabitants are really *Givonim*, of problematic lineage. *Durnunisa* is *Darei Netinai* — the village of *Netinim*. The *Netinim* and the *Givonim* are the same. And Rav Yosef added that a suburb of Pumbedita, *Beikvei*, was entirely composed of slaves.

These broad geographic labelings are deeply troubling, moving beyond the identification of individuals to the invalidation of entire communities. The Gemara draws a parallel to the actions of Ezra, who similarly identified and sorted the *psulim* when he returned from Bavel to Eretz Yisrael.

### Slave Intermarriage

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *arba meos avadim, ve'amrei lei arba alafim avadim hayu lo lePishchar ben Imar* — four hundred slaves, and some say four thousand slaves, belonged to a man named Pishchar ben Imar. *Vekulam nitmu bikehuna* — all of them intermarried among *kohanim*. Rashi explains that as slaves of a *kohen*, they were permitted to eat *terumah* and were thus able to pass themselves off as *kohanim*, eventually intermarrying into priestly families. The practical consequence is alarming: a *kohen* we encounter today might actually descend from one of these slaves. But how far does that suspicion extend — should we be suspicious of every *kohen*?

The Gemara offers a suggested marker: *vekol kohen sheyesh bo azus panim eino ela mehem* — any *kohen* who displays brazenness is probably from among these slaves and is not a legitimate *kohen*.

### Abaye's Pushback

Abaye pushes back on this, first noting practically that all of these slave-descended pseudo-*kohanim* can be found in a particular position of authority in a specific locale — they are identifiable. But he then challenges the underlying principle directly. This view, that brazenness is a marker of illegitimate priestly descent, *pliga d'Rabbi Elazar* — contradicts the position of Rabbi Elazar. Rabbi Elazar taught: *im ra'isa kohen b'azus metzach al taharher acharav* — if you see a *kohen* who acts with brazenness, do not suspect thereby that he is an illegitimate *kohen*. The proof is from the verse *v'amcha k'mrivai kohen* — your people are like those who contend with a *kohen*, which indicates that a *kohen* can be contentious; it takes two parties to have a quarrel. So brazenness alone cannot be taken as evidence of disqualified lineage.

We find ourselves, then, in a state of tension: a great deal of labeling and suspecting, with only a small degree of pushback thus far — though the pushback will grow considerably.

## Aggada on Lineage

### Divine Presence & Yichus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין בַּר רַב אַדָּא אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַנּוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה שֶׁאֵינָהּ הוֹגֶנֶת לוֹ, כְּשֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָתוֹ מֵעִיד עַל כׇּל הַשְּׁבָטִים וְאֵין מֵעִיד עָלָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁבְטֵי יָהּ עֵדוּת לְיִשְׂרָאֵל״, אֵימָתַי הָוֵי עֵדוּת לְיִשְׂרָאֵל – בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַשְּׁבָטִים שִׁבְטֵי יָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses an idea raised earlier. **Rabbi Avin bar Rav Adda says** that **Rav says:** Concerning **anyone who marries a woman who is not suited for him** to marry, **when the Holy One, Blessed be He, rests His Divine Presence** upon the Jewish people, **He testifies with regard to all the tribes** that they are His people, **but He does not testify with regard to he** who married improperly, **as it is stated: "The tribes of the Lord, as a testimony to Israel"** (Psalms 122:4). **When is it a testimony to Israel? When the tribes are the tribes of the Lord,** but not when their lineage is flawed.

The Gemara now turns to *aggadic* material bearing on the themes of genealogy and the Divine Presence. Rav Avin bar Rav Adda said in the name of Rav: *kol hanosei ishah she'eino hogenes lo* — anyone who marries a woman of lesser lineage than himself, *kshe'hakadosh baruch hu mashreh shechinaso maid al kol hashvatim v'ein maid alav* — when God causes His *Shechina* to dwell among the people, He testifies on behalf of all the *shevatim* but will not testify about or identify with that family. The verse cited is *shivtei Yah edus l'Yisrael* — the tribes of God, testimony to Israel. The *Shechina* gives testimony about Israel only when they are truly *shivtei Yah*, when the tribal and family structures are properly maintained and not adulterated.

A subtle question arises: does "adulterated" here refer to marrying someone of inferior but still halachically valid status — in which case this *aggadah* is making a sweeping statement about social hierarchy — or does it refer to marrying someone of formally invalid status, a *mamzer* or slave, who would genuinely disqualify the family line? Given the immediate context of the discussion about *mamzeirim* and *avdus*, the latter reading seems more likely, but the text does not foreclose the former.

### Shechina Dwelling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כְּשֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָתוֹ – אֵין מַשְׁרֶה אֶלָּא עַל מִשְׁפָּחוֹת מְיוּחָסוֹת שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּעֵת הַהִיא נְאֻם ה׳ אֶהְיֶה לֵאלֹהִים לְכֹל מִשְׁפְּחוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל״, ״לְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״לְכֹל מִשְׁפְּחוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: When the Holy One, Blessed be He, rests His Divine Presence, He rests it only upon families of** unflawed lineage **among Israel, as it is stated: "At that time, says the Lord, will I be the God of all the families of Israel"** (Jeremiah 30:25). **Of all Israel, is not stated, but "of all the families,"** which includes only those of unflawed lineage, the renowned families of Israel.

Rabbi Chama in the name of Rabbi Chanina sharpens this further: *kshe'hakadosh baruch hu mashreh shechinaso, ein mashreh ela al mishpachos meyuchasos shebeYisrael* — when God causes His *Shechina* to rest, it rests only upon families of distinguished lineage within Israel. The verse is from Jeremiah: *ba'eis hahi neum Hashem eheyeh l'Elokim l'chol mishpachos Yisrael* — at that time, says God, I will be a God to all the *mishpachos* of Israel. Notably, the verse does not say *l'chol Yisrael* but *l'chol mishpachos* — to all the families, meaning those who have preserved their family distinctions and *yichus*. This is a stronger statement than the previous one; here the *Shechina* does not merely withhold its testimony, but actively does not dwell except upon families of recognized lineage.

### Native vs Convert

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״[וְהֵמָּה] יִהְיוּ לִי לְעָם״, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: זוֹ מַעֲלָה יְתֵירָה יֵשׁ בֵּין יִשְׂרָאֵל לְגֵרִים. דְּאִילּוּ בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל כְּתִיב בְּהוּ: ״וְהָיִיתִי לָהֶם לֵאלֹהִים וְהֵמָּה יִהְיוּ לִי לְעָם״, וְאִילּוּ בְּגֵרִים כְּתִיב: ״מִי הוּא זֶה עָרַב אֶת לִבּוֹ לָגֶשֶׁת אֵלַי נְאֻם ה׳. וִהְיִיתֶם לִי לְעָם וְאָנֹכִי אֶהְיֶה לָכֶם לֵאלֹהִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse from Jeremiah ends with the words **"And they shall be my people." Rabba bar Rav Huna says: This is a higher standard** that differentiates **between** those born as **Jews and converts, as with regard to** those born as **Jews it is written about them: "And I will be their God, and they shall be My people"** (Ezekiel 37:27), **whereas with regard to converts it is written: "For who is he that has pledged his heart to approach unto Me? says the Lord. And you shall be My people, and I will be your God"** (Jeremiah 30:21–22). This teaches that converts are not drawn close to God, as indicated by the words "And I will be your God," until they first draw themselves near to God, as indicated by the subsequent phrase "And you shall be my people."

Rabbah bar Rav Huna draws a distinction between a native-born Israelite and a *ger*: *zo ma'alah yesh bein Yisrael l'geirim* — there is a level of superiority that the native-born Jew holds over the convert. When Scripture speaks about Israel, it says *v'hayisi lahem l'Elokim v'heimah yihyu li l'am* — I will be for them a God, and they will be for Me a nation. The relationship with God is stated first; it preexists and draws Israel near. When Scripture addresses converts, it says: *mi hu zeh arav es libo l'higeshal elai v'hayisem li l'am v'anochi eheyeh lachem l'Elokim* — whoever stirs his heart to come close to Me, you will be for Me a nation and I will be for you a God. Here the initiative must come from the convert; the divine relationship follows rather than precedes their approach.

Rashi reads this as descriptive: the native-born Jew is born into an existing covenantal relationship that draws him naturally toward God, while the *ger* must first generate the motivation from within himself. One might fairly read this as an extraordinary tribute to the spiritual achievement of the convert — the initiative required is immense. However, the Gemara's framing is plainly hierarchical, and it cannot easily be read otherwise.

### Converts as Scabs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ: קָשִׁים גֵּרִים לְיִשְׂרָאֵל כְּסַפַּחַת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִלְוָה הַגֵּר עֲלֵיהֶם וְנִסְפְּחוּ עַל בֵּית יַעֲקֹב״ – כְּתִיב הָכָא ״וְנִסְפְּחוּ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם ״לַשְּׂאֵת וְלַסַּפַּחַת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥelbo says: Converts are** as **difficult for the Jewish people as a scab.** The proof is **that it is stated: "And the convert shall join himself with them, and they shall cleave [*venispeḥu*] to the house of Jacob"** (Isaiah 14:1). **It is written here "*venispeḥu*," and it is written there,** among the types of leprosy: **"And for a sore and for a scab [*sappaḥat*]"** (Leviticus 14:56). The use of a term with a similar root indicates that converts are like a scab for the Jewish people.

Rabbi Chelbo makes a striking and harsh statement: *kashim gerim le'Yisrael kesappachat* — converts are as difficult for Israel as a scab. The verse is *venilva hager aleihem venispachu al beit Yaakov* — the stranger will cleave to them and be added onto the house of Jacob. The word *venispachu* is connected exegetically to *sappachat*, the skin affliction mentioned among the forms of *tzara'at*.

What does this mean? Rashi, working within the context of tractate Yevamot, explains that converts are sometimes insufficiently conversant with the tradition and may lead others astray. Tosafot, however, connects the statement to the immediately preceding discussion: if the *Shechina* dwells only upon *mishpachos meyuchasos*, then the presence of *geirim* among Israel — who do not have the same chain of *yichus* — diminishes that quality. This is an extremely harsh reading of the concept of *yichus*, one that operates not merely as a halachic category but as a marker of social and spiritual hierarchy.

Tosafot then cites, though without attribution, an interpretation by one Rav Avraham Hager — a man who was himself a convert yet whose interpretation made its way into Tosafot — who read the statement in precisely the opposite spirit: because converts are new to the tradition and approach it with passion rather than complacency, they put to shame the native-born Jews who are not so *medakdek bemitzvot*. This is a beautiful inversion, and Tosafot records it seriously.

There is no question that this entire sugyah on *yichus* sits in considerable tension with a more egalitarian ethos, and equally with other Talmudic passages that speak in glowing terms about *geirim* and invoke the commandments of *ve'ahavtem et hager* and *kager ka'ezrach*. Both currents are present in the Talmud, and the tension between them is real.

## Keep It Hidden

### Money Purifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כֶּסֶף מְטַהֵר מַמְזֵרִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֵּשֶׁב מְצָרֵף וּמְטַהֵר כֶּסֶף״. מַאי ״מַגִּישֵׁי מִנְחָה בִּצְדָקָה״? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: צְדָקָה עָשָׂה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עִם יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁמִּשְׁפָּחָה שֶׁנִּטְמְעָה – נִטְמְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: Money purifies *mamzerim*.** Money causes rich *mamzerim* to become assimilated with Jews of unflawed lineage, since other families marry them despite their flawed lineage. In the future, God will not single them out as *mamzerim*, **as it is stated: "And he shall sit as a refiner and purifier of silver,"** which teaches that money, i.e., silver, purifies them. **What,** then, is the connection to the next part of the verse: **"They that shall offer to the Lord offerings in righteousness"? Rabbi Yitzḥak says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, performed** an act of **righteousness with the Jewish people** by establishing **that a family that has become assimilated** with Jews of unflawed lineage remains **assimilated.** They are not removed from their tribe despite their flawed lineage.

Continuing at the bottom of amud bet, Rabbi Chama bar Chanina teaches: *kshe'hakadosh baruch hu metaher shvatim* — when God comes to purify the tribes, *shivto shel Levi metaher techila* — He begins with the tribe of Levi. The prooftext is from Malachi: *veyashev metzaref umetaher kesef vetiher es bnei Levi v'zikak osam k'zahav v'chakesef* — He will sit as one who smelts and purifies silver, and He will purify the sons of Levi, refining them like gold and silver, and they will then be able to bring offerings to God in righteousness.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, however, turns this passage on its head: *kesef metaher mamzeirim* — it is money that purifies *mamzeirim*. This is a striking inversion of the imagery. The verse's metaphor of silver purification is reread not as an analogy for genealogical purification but as a literal reference to wealth. Rashi explains the reference: wealthy individuals — for the sake of money — married into the tribe of Levi despite having invalid status, and the point Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is making is not that money literally confers kashrut, but rather that once people have intermarried, the genealogical record is absorbed and everything becomes *kasher* by virtue of being submerged. That is the operative mechanism of purification — not the money itself, but the intermingling that money enabled.

The Ri"f, however, reads *kesef metaher mamzeirim* more literally and more cleverly: the halachic mechanism by which a *mamzer* can produce non-*mamzer* offspring is through marriage to a *shifcha* — a female slave — whose child, being a slave, can then be freed and thereby exits the status of *mamzer* entirely. The money involved is the purchase price of the slave. This is an elegant halachic reading.

### Mixed Family Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל אֲרָצוֹת עִיסָּה לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל עִיסָּה לְבָבֶל. בִּימֵי רַבִּי בִּקְּשׁוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת בָּבֶל עִיסָּה לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל. אָמַר לָהֶן: קוֹצִים אַתֶּם מְשִׂימִים לִי בֵּין עֵינַי? רְצוֹנְכֶם, יִטָּפֵל עִמָּכֶם רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר חָמָא.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to **the** matter **itself** that was discussed earlier, the lineage of the Jews in various lands, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The lineage of residents of **all lands is muddled** compared **to** that of the residents of **Eretz Yisrael, and** the lineage of residents of **Eretz Yisrael is muddled** compared **to** that of **Babylonia.** The Gemara relates: **In the days of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **they sought to establish** the lineage of the Jews in **Babylonia** as **muddled** relative **to** that of **Eretz Yisrael.** In other words, the people of Eretz Yisrael wanted their lineage to be considered superior to that of the residents of Babylonia, so that if people from Eretz Yisrael would wish to marry Babylonians, they would have to investigate the lineage of the Babylonians. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was descended from Hillel, a Babylonian, so **he said to** those who put forth this suggestion: **Are you placing thorns between my eyes?** Do you wish to insult me? **If you wish, Rabbi Ḥanina bar Ḥama will join you** and explain it to you.

Rabbi Yitzchak draws out the implication of the verse's closing phrase — *v'hayu l'Hashem magishei mincha bitzdaka*, they will bring offerings to God in righteousness: *tzadaka asa hakadosh baruch hu im Yisrael* — God performed an act of charity for Israel — *shemishpacha shenitme'ah nitme'ah* — that once a family has been submerged into the general population, it remains submerged. This is the principle of *mishpacha shenitme'ah nitmeah*, written with an *ayin*, meaning merged rather than defiled. God's act of righteousness consists precisely in not requiring Israel to investigate and uproot every genealogical impurity that has been absorbed into the community. If people present themselves as *Levi'im* or *Yisraelim*, and their intermarriage has made it impossible to know otherwise, they are accepted as such.

With this, the Gemara begins to shift explicitly against the mode of proactive identification that characterized the earlier part of the *sugya*. The two tendencies — publicly labeling *psulim* on the one hand, and accepting the status quo on the other — have now both been articulated, and the latter is gaining ground.

## Boundaries & Assumpt (II)

### Shmuel's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְפַּל עִמָּהֶם רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר חָמָא, אָמַר לָהֶם: כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מֵרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם אָבִיו: כׇּל אֲרָצוֹת עִיסָּה לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל עִיסָּה לְבָבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Ḥama joined them** and **said to them: This** is the tradition that **I received from Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, who says in the name of his father,** who was from Eretz Yisrael: The lineage of residents of **all lands is muddled** compared **to** that of **Eretz Yisrael, and** the lineage of residents of **Eretz Yisrael is muddled** compared **to** that of **Babylonia.**

The Gemara introduces a teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel that returns to the geographic framework established earlier in the *perek*: *kol ha'aratzos issa l'Eretz Yisrael* — all other lands are like *issa*, dough, compared to Eretz Yisrael. The contrast drawn earlier was that when Ezra departed from Bavel, he left it like *solet nekiyah* — refined, sifted flour, with the bran entirely removed — meaning that all those of problematic lineage had departed with him, and what remained in Bavel was pure. All other lands lack this quality; they are like dough, mixed and unpurified. But *v'Eretz Yisrael issa l'Bavel* — Eretz Yisrael is itself like dough compared to Bavel. Even Eretz Yisrael, which contains *psulim* in known and recorded form, has a less certain pedigree than Bavel overall.

In the time of Rabbi, the question arose whether to reverse this ranking — to say that Bavel had perhaps deteriorated over time and should be considered *issa* relative to Eretz Yisrael. Rabbi responded sharply: *kotzim atem matilim li bein einai* — you are placing thorns in my eyes. Since Rabbi was descended from Hillel, and Hillel came from Bavel, any downgrading of Babylonian *yichus* would undermine Rabbi's own genealogical standing and his claim of descent from the house of David. Accordingly, he deferred the matter to Rabbi Chanina bar Chama, who engaged those raising the challenge and reported: *kach mikubalni mi'Rabbi Yishmael be'Rabbi Yossi she'amar mishum aviv* — this is the tradition I received from Rabbi Yishmael in the name of his father: all lands are dough compared to Eretz Yisrael, and Eretz Yisrael is dough compared to Bavel. The tradition was thus confirmed independently, which was important precisely because Rabbi himself could not have reported it without appearing self-interested.

### Diversion Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּימֵי רַבִּי פִּנְחָס בִּקְּשׁוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת בָּבֶל עִיסָּה לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל. אָמַר לָהֶם לַעֲבָדָיו: כְּשֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר שְׁנֵי דְבָרִים בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ טְלוּנִי בַּעֲרִיסָה וְרוּצוּ. כִּי עָיֵיל אָמַר לָהֶם: אֵין שְׁחִיטָה לָעוֹף מִן הַתּוֹרָה. אַדְּיָתְבִי וְקָמְעַיְּינִי בַּהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: כׇּל אֲרָצוֹת עִיסָּה לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל עִיסָּה לְבָבֶל. נְטָלוּהוּ בַּעֲרִיסָה וְרָצוּ. רָצוּ אַחֲרָיו וְלֹא הִגִּיעוּהוּ. יָשְׁבוּ וּבָדְקוּ עַד שֶׁהִגִּיעוּ לְסַכָּנָה וּפֵירְשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further relates with regard to the same issue: **In the days of Rabbi Pineḥas, they sought to establish** the lineage of **Babylonia** as **muddled** relative **to** that of **Eretz Yisrael. He said to his servants: When I have said two statements in the house of study, pick me up on a stretcher and run,** so that I will not be attacked for my statements. **When he entered** the house of study **he said to** those studying there: **Slaughter of a bird is not** obligatory **by Torah law.** **While they were sitting and scrutinizing this** novel *halakha*, **he said to them:** The lineage of residents of **all lands is muddled** compared **to** that of **Eretz Yisrael, and** the lineage of residents of **Eretz Yisrael is muddled** compared **to** that of **Babylonia.** His servants **picked him up on a stretcher and ran.** Those that were in the house of study **pursued him but could not catch him.** Nevertheless, **they sat and examined** the lineage of various families in order to determine whether in fact the lineage of the residents of Eretz Yisrael was problematic, **until they reached** powerful families. It was **dangerous** to accuse them of flaws due to their power, **and they withdrew** from their inspections.

A parallel episode occurred in the time of Rabbi Pinchas. When the same attempt arose — to reclassify Bavel as inferior to Eretz Yisrael — Rabbi Pinchas devised a remarkable stratagem. He told his servants: when I say two things in the *beit hamidrash*, rush me out immediately, carry me out on my stretcher and run. He went in and announced: *ein shechita l'of min haTorah* — the requirement of ritual slaughter for birds is not Biblically mandated; it is only rabbinic. This is a provocative ruling that does not represent the final *halacha*, but it was sufficiently surprising to throw the assembled scholars into a state of confusion and discussion. While they were processing this startling claim, he quickly added: *kol ha'aratzot issa l'Eretz Yisrael v'Eretz Yisrael issa l'Bavel* — and then his servants ran him out on the stretcher before anyone could ask follow-up questions.

The scholars pursued him but could not catch up. When they sat back down and began investigating which families in Eretz Yisrael might be problematic — since Rabbi Pinchas had implied that there were some *psulim* there — they found themselves approaching dangerous territory. *Ad she'higiu l'sakana* — until they reached a point of danger: Rashi explains that they began discovering that some very prominent families might have questionable *yichus*, at which point they realized it was better to stop looking. *U'pirshu* — and they ceased.

## Keep It Hidden (II)

### Rabbi Yochanan Stays Silent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֵיכְלָא, בְּיָדֵינוּ הִיא, אֲבָל מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה שֶׁהֲרֵי גְּדוֹלֵי הַדּוֹר נִטְמְעוּ בָּהּ. סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִשְׁפָּחָה שֶׁנִּטְמְעָה – נִטְמְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** as an oath: By the **Sanctuary! It is in our power** to reveal the identity of a family that has a flawed lineage, **but what can I do, as the greatest of the generation are assimilated into it?** Consequently, I will not reveal its name. The Gemara comments: Rabbi Yoḥanan **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says: A family that has become assimilated** remains **assimilated,** and one should not reveal their flawed status.

Rabbi Yochanan stated, taking an oath by the *Beit HaMikdash*: *b'yadeinu hu* — it is within our capacity to identify certain families of invalid lineage — *aval mah e'eseh she'gdolei hador nitme'u bo* — but what can I do, since great figures of this generation have already been intermingled with those families? To reveal the invalidity would call into question the *yichus* of major communal figures. And so Rabbi Yochanan kept silent. This is the clearest expression yet of the principle of *mishpacha shenitme'ah nitmeah*: even when knowledge exists, the decision is made not to deploy it. Rabbi Yochanan *savar lah k'Rabbi Yitzchak* — held in accordance with Rabbi Yitzchak, who taught explicitly: *mishpacha shenitmeah nitmeah* — once a family has been merged in, it stays merged in.

### Forced Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: מִשְׁפַּחַת בֵּית הַצְּרִיפָה הָיְתָה בְּעֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן וְרִיחֲקָהּ בֶּן צִיּוֹן בִּזְרוֹעַ. עוֹד אַחֶרֶת הָיְתָה וְקֵירְבָהּ בֶּן צִיּוֹן בִּזְרוֹעַ. כְּגוֹן אֵלּוּ – אֵלִיָּהוּ בָּא לְטַמֵּא וּלְטַהֵר, לְרַחֵק וּלְקָרֵב – כְּגוֹן אֵלּוּ דְּיָדְעִין, אֲבָל מִשְׁפָּחָה שֶׁנִּטְמְעָה – נִטְמְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: We too learn** in the mishna (*Eduyyot* 8:7): **There was a family** known as **Beit HaTzerifa in Transjordan, and** a person called **ben Tziyyon forcefully distanced it** and proclaimed that its lineage was flawed, although its lineage was unflawed. **There was another** one **that ben Tziyyon forcefully drew near,** although its lineage was flawed. The mishna adds: Known families **such as these, Elijah comes to** declare **impure and to** declare **pure, to distance and to draw near.** Abaye continues: When the mishna states: **Such as these,** it means those **whose** status **we know. But a family that has become assimilated,** whose flawed lineage is unknown to the public, **has** already **become assimilated,** and not even Elijah will publicize its flaw.

Abaye notes a corroborating *baraita* from tractate Eduyot: there was a family called *Beit Hatzerifah* on the far side of the Jordan which was actually of valid lineage, but a powerful man named Ben Tziyon used his authority — *b'zroa*, with force — to get everyone to treat them as *pasul*. Conversely, there was another family that was genuinely *pasul*, and Ben Tziyon again used his power to force people to treat them as *kasher*. The *Mishnah* then states: cases like these, *Eliyahu ba l'tamei u'l'taher l'rachek u'l'karev* — Eliyahu will come to set right, declaring the unjustly invalidated to be valid and the unjustly validated to be *pasul*.

The Gemara comments: this *mishnah* about Eliyahu refers specifically to *d'yedian* — situations that are known to be problematic and have been handled improperly. But *aval mishpacha shenitmeah* — when a family's invalid lineage has genuinely been absorbed and lost to public knowledge — *nitmeah*, it stays that way, and no rectification is expected.

### Secret Traditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּאנָא: עוֹד אַחֶרֶת הָיְתָה, וְלֹא רָצוּ חֲכָמִים לְגַלּוֹתָהּ. אֲבָל חֲכָמִים מוֹסְרִים אוֹתוֹ לִבְנֵיהֶם וּלְתַלְמִידֵיהֶן פַּעַם אַחַת בְּשָׁבוּעַ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ פַּעֲמַיִם בְּשָׁבוּעַ. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מִסְתַּבְּרָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר פַּעַם אַחַת בְּשָׁבוּעַ. כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר אִם לֹא אֲגַלֶּה מִשְׁפָּחוֹת״ – יִהְיֶה נָזִיר, וְלֹא יְגַלֶּה מִשְׁפָּחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Sage **taught** (*Tosefta*, *Eduyyot* 3:4): **There was another** family with flawed lineage, **but the Sages did not want to reveal its** identity to all. **But the Sages transmit its** name **to their children and to their students once every seven years, and some say twice every seven years,** to prevent them from marrying into their family. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: It stands to reason in accordance with the one who says** that they transmit it **once every seven years, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Nazir* 1:2): One who says: **I am hereby a nazirite if I do not reveal** the names of **families** of flawed lineage among the Jewish people, **he should be a nazirite and not reveal** the identity of such **families.** This shows that such information should be kept secret as much as possible.

A *baraita* then teaches: *oda cheres haysa v'lo ratzu chachamim l'galosa* — there was another invalid family that the Sages did not wish to publicize. Nevertheless, the Sages transmitted this information to their children and students *pa'am achas b'shavua* — once every seven years; some say twice every seven years, so that some record would survive until the time of Eliyahu HaNavi.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak rules that the preferable position is once every seven years — the less spoken about such matters, the better. The preference for restraint is reinforced by a *baraita*: if a person says, "I hereby take upon myself *nezirut* on the condition that I will not reveal genealogical problems," we say *yehei nazir v'lo yigaleh mishpachos* — let him be a *nazir* and not reveal the family information. Rather than allowing him to choose between the *nezirut* and disclosure, we direct him to accept the *nezirut* and remain silent.

At this juncture, the Gemara draws an illuminating parallel. Rabbah bar Bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the four-letter Divine name — *shem ben arba otiyos* — the Sages transmitted to their students *pa'am achas b'shavua*, once every seven years; some say twice every seven years. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak again prefers the position of once every seven years, deriving support from the verse *zeh shemi le'olam* — this is My name forever — which is written *le'aleim*, suggesting it should remain hidden. The parallel is pointed: just as the transmission of God's secret name is hedged with restraint and solemnity, so too the transmission of genealogical secrets about invalid families.

Rava once sought to teach the four-letter name publicly to his students. A certain elder stopped him by quoting *le'aleim ksiv* — it is written to be concealed.

Rav Avina raises an apparent contradiction: one verse says *zeh shemi le'olam* — this is My name forever (which we read *le'illum*, hidden), while another says *zeh zikhri* — this is how I am to be remembered. God's response, as it were, is: *lo kshe'ani nikhtav ani nikra* — I am not read the way I am written. I am written with the *yud-keh-vav-keh* but read as *Adon*. The written name is for concealment; the spoken name is for public invocation. The name itself — *shemi* — is *le'aleim*, hidden. The name by which I am addressed — *zikhri* — is known.

The Gemara then describes a progression. Originally, the twelve-letter name of God was transmitted to everyone. Once those who were insufficiently guarded became more numerous and the name began to be misused, it was transmitted only to the *tzenu'in shebekehuna* — the particularly modest among the *kohanim*. These would weave the name into their melodic recitation during *Birkat Kohanim*, blending it into the singing so that the other *kohanim* — who were simultaneously stretching out the four-letter name — could not discern it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, שֵׁם בֶּן שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה אוֹתִיּוֹת הָיוּ מוֹסְרִין אוֹתוֹ לְכׇל אָדָם, מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הַפָּרִיצִים – הָיוּ מוֹסְרִים אוֹתוֹ לִצְנוּעִים שֶׁבַּכְּהוּנָּה, וְהַצְּנוּעִים שֶׁבַּכְּהוּנָּה מַבְלִיעִים אוֹתוֹ בִּנְעִימַת אֲחֵיהֶם הַכֹּהֲנִים. תַּנְיָא אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: פַּעַם אַחַת עָלִיתִי אַחַר אֲחִי אִמִּי לַדּוּכָן, וְהִטֵּיתִי אָזְנִי אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל וְשָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁהִבְלִיעַ שֵׁם בִּנְעִימַת אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Initially,** the Sages **would transmit the twelve-letter name** of God **to any person. When the uninhibited ones** who used the name disrespectfully **increased, they would transmit it** only **to discreet** members **of the priesthood, and the discreet** members **of the priesthood** would pronounce the name during the Priestly Benediction. They would **conceal it** by saying it **during the sweet** melody **of their priestly brothers,** so that it would not become publicly known. **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Tarfon,** who was himself a priest, **said:** On **one occasion I ascended after my mother's brother to the platform** to give the Priestly Benediction, **and I inclined my ear near the High Priest, and I heard him conceal the name during the sweet** melody **of his priestly brothers.**

Rabbi Tarfon testified personally: once, when he accompanied his maternal uncle to the *duchan* for *Birkat Kohanim*, he tilted his ear toward the *kohen gadol* — or perhaps, given the chronological difficulties with this testimony, toward a senior *kohen* — and overheard him weaving in the twelve-letter name during the melody.

The forty-two letter name — still more sublime and potent — was transmitted only under stringent conditions: the recipient must be *tzanua* — modest and trustworthy with secrets — *v'anav* — humble — *v'omed bechatzi yamav* — having reached at least the midpoint of life (approximately 35, assuming a lifespan of 70) — *v'eino ko'es* — not given to anger — *v'eino mishtaker* — not prone to intoxication — and *v'eino ma'amid al midotav* — one who does not press his claims insistently when wronged. The reason for these requirements, as Rashi explains, is that such a name carries enormous power to do damage, and a person who cannot control his temper or his drinking might use it to curse another. The reward for those who know the name and guard it with purity is that they become *ahuv lemala* — beloved above — *venichmad lemata* — cherished below — and *nochal shenei olamim*, inheriting both this world and the world to come.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שֵׁם בֶּן אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁתַּיִם אוֹתִיּוֹת אֵין מוֹסְרִין אוֹתוֹ אֶלָּא לְמִי שֶׁצָּנוּעַ וְעָנָיו, וְעוֹמֵד בַּחֲצִי יָמָיו, וְאֵינוֹ כּוֹעֵס, וְאֵינוֹ מִשְׁתַּכֵּר, וְאֵינוֹ מַעֲמִיד עַל מִדּוֹתָיו. וְכׇל הַיּוֹדְעוֹ, וְהַזָּהִיר בּוֹ וְהַמְשַׁמְּרוֹ בְּטׇהֳרָה – אָהוּב לְמַעְלָה, וְנֶחְמָד לְמַטָּה, וְאֵימָתוֹ מוּטֶּלֶת עַל הַבְּרִיּוֹת, וְנוֹחֵל שְׁנֵי עוֹלָמִים – הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה וְהָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: The forty-two-letter name** of God **may be transmitted only to one who is discreet, and humble, and stands at** at least **half his life, and does not get angry, and does not get drunk, and does not insist upon his rights** but is willing to yield. There is no concern that such a person might reveal the name in a fit of anger or drunkenness. **And anyone who knows** this name **and is careful with it and guards it in purity is beloved above and treasured below; and fear of him is cast upon the creatures; and he inherits two worlds, this world and the World-to-Come.**

## Boundaries & Assumpt (II)

### Shmuel's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּסָבָא: בָּבֶל בְּחֶזְקַת כְּשֵׁרָה עוֹמֶדֶת, עַד שֶׁיִּוָּדַע לָךְ בַּמֶּה נִפְסְלָה. שְׁאָר אֲרָצוֹת – בְּחֶזְקַת פְּסוּל הֵן עוֹמְדוֹת, עַד שֶׁיִּוָּדַע לָךְ בַּמֶּה נִכְשְׁרָה. אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל מוּחְזָק לְפָסוּל – פָּסוּל, מוּחְזָק לְכָשֵׁר – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the issue of lineage: **Shmuel says in the name of** a certain **elder:** A family in **Babylonia has a presumptive status of unflawed** lineage **until it becomes known to you in what way it was** rendered of **flawed** lineage. Conversely, a family from **other lands has a presumptive status of flawed** lineage **until it becomes known to you in what way it was** rendered **unflawed.** As for families in **Eretz Yisrael,** one who **has a presumptive status of flawed** lineage is of **flawed** lineage, whereas one who **has presumptive status of unflawed** lineage is of **unflawed** lineage.

Shmuel said in the name of an elder: *Bavel bechezkat ksheira omedet ad sheyivada lecha bameh nifsala* — Bavel stands in the presumption of being kasher unless you know specifically on what basis a given person became *pasul*. By contrast, other lands — *bechezkat pasul hein omdot ad sheyivada lecha bamah nichsharah* — stand in the presumption of invalidity unless you know how the person became *kasher*. Eretz Yisrael occupies a middle position: *muchzak lepasul pasul, muchzak lekasher kasher* — if known to be *pasul*, they are *pasul*; if known to be *kasher*, they are *kasher*.

This last formulation seems internally contradictory — it establishes no default. The Gemara asks: *hagufei kashya* — is this not self-contradictory? The statement *muchzak lepasul pasul* implies that absent that knowledge the person is *kasher*, making *kasher* the default; yet *muchzak lekasher kasher* implies the opposite. Rav Huna bar Tachalifa resolves this in the name of Rav: *kan lehasi'o isha kan lehotzi isha miyado* — one clause speaks to permitting him to marry, and the other speaks to compelling a divorce after the fact. Before marriage to someone of distinguished lineage, a man from Eretz Yisrael must establish his *yichus*; but once married, only demonstrated *psul* can undo it. Rashi and Tosafot both connect this to the specific case of marrying into a family of elevated status, not to ordinary marriages — a normal person is not required to prove his lineage before marrying.

### Diversion Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפַאּ קַשְׁיָא: אָמְרַתְּ מוּחְזָק לְפָסוּל – פָּסוּל. הָא סְתָמָא – כָּשֵׁר. וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: מוּחְזָק לְכָשֵׁר – כָּשֵׁר. הָא סְתָמָא – פָּסוּל! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן – לְהַשִּׂיאוֹ אִשָּׁה, כָּאן – לְהוֹצִיא אִשָּׁה מִיָּדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara is puzzled by this last statement: **This matter itself is difficult:** First, **you said** that a family that **has a presumptive status of flawed** lineage is of **flawed** lineage, indicating that a family with **unspecified** status is of **unflawed** lineage. **And then** you **teach:** A family that **has a presumptive status of unflawed** lineage is of **unflawed** lineage, indicating that a family with **unspecified** status is of **flawed** lineage. **Rav Huna bar Taḥalifa said in the name of Rav:** This is **not difficult.** **Here,** in the final clause, it is referring **to marrying** a woman to **him,** and the *halakha* is that a family with no presumptive status requires investigation before one of them marries. **There,** in the penultimate clause, it is referring **to** the court **removing a woman from him.** The husband is not forced to divorce her unless it has been proven that they may not remain married.

Rav Yosef adds: *kol sheyichaso beVavel masi'in lo isha* — anyone whose lineage identifies him as Babylonian, one may marry him off to a woman of good *yichus*. However, *ve'idna de'ika rama'ei chaishinan* — now that there are deceivers who can learn to speak Babylonian even without having Babylonian origins, we are suspicious and this linguistic marker can no longer be relied upon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְעֵירִי הֲוָה קָא מִישְׁתְּמִיט מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דַּהֲוָה אָמַר לֵיהּ: נְסֵיב בְּרַתִּי. יוֹמָא חַד הֲווֹ קָאָזְלִי בְּאוֹרְחָא, מְטוֹ לְעוּרְקְמָא דְמַיָּא, אַרְכְּבֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אַכַּתְפֵּיהּ וְקָא מְעַבַּר לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אוֹרָיְיתַן כְּשֵׁרָה, בְּנָתִין לָא כְּשֵׁרָן? מַאי דַּעְתָּיךְ, אִילֵּימָא מִדִּתְנַן: עֲשָׂרָה יוּחֲסִין עָלוּ מִבָּבֶל כָּהֲנֵי לְוִיֵּי – אַטּוּ כָּהֲנֵי לְוִיֵּי וְיִשְׂרְאֵלֵי כּוּלְּהוּ סְלִיקוּ? כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאִישְׁתְּיוּר מֵהָנֵי אִישְׁתְּיוּר נָמֵי מֵהָנֵי! אִישְׁתְּמִיטְתֵּיהּ הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא עָלָה עֶזְרָא מִבָּבֶל עַד שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ כְּסוֹלֶת נְקִיָּיה וְעָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: The Sage **Ze'eiri,** a Babylonian, **was avoiding Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who was from Eretz Yisrael, **since** the latter kept **saying to him: Marry my daughter. One day,** when **they were walking along the way, they arrived at** a large **puddle of water.** Ze'eiri **lifted Rabbi Yoḥanan upon his shoulders and carried him over** the puddle out of respect. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: Our Torah is fit** and worthy of honor in your eyes, and yet **our daughters are not fit? What is your reason** for not wanting to marry my daughter? **If we say a reason** not to marry my daughter is **from that which we learned** in a mishna (69a): There were **ten** categories of **lineage** among the Jews who **ascended from Babylonia: Priests, Levites,** Israelites, as well as many of flawed lineage, and you are concerned about the *mamzerim* among those who live in Eretz Yisrael, **is that to say** that **all of the priests, Levites, and Israelites ascended** to Eretz Yisrael? Certainly, Jews of unflawed lineage remained in Babylonia. **Just as there remained from these** unflawed categories in Babylonia, **there also remained** individuals **from these,** the flawed categories. Therefore, marrying only Babylonians will not alleviate your concern. The Gemara comments: In fact, **this statement of Rabbi Elazar escaped** Rabbi Yoḥanan: **Ezra did not ascend from Babylonia until he made it like fine flour, and** then **he ascended.** Accordingly, the people who remained in Babylonia were all of unflawed lineage.

The Gemara now relates the story of Ze'iri, who persistently avoided Rabbi Yochanan — because Rabbi Yochanan wished to give him his daughter in marriage. Rabbi Yochanan was from Eretz Yisrael; Ze'iri was from Bavel. One day, they were traveling together and came to a stream. Ze'iri, as the devoted student, carried Rabbi Yochanan on his shoulders through the water. Rabbi Yochanan, frustrated by the persistent evasion, said to him: our Torah of Eretz Yisrael is good enough for you — *oraitin kesheira* — but our daughters are not? What is your reasoning? Are you relying on the *Mishnah* that teaches that ten categories of lineage made the ascent from Bavel with Ezra — *kohanim*, *Levi'im*, *Yisraelim*, and so forth — and inferring therefrom that Bavel is genealogically purer than Eretz Yisrael?

But one cannot infer this from the *Mishnah*, Rabbi Yochanan argues. *Atu Kahanei Levi'ei ve-Yisra'ei kulu siliku?* Just because *psulim* came with Ezra, the *Mishnah* also states that *kohanim*, *levi'im*, and *yisraelim* came with him — but that does not mean they all left Bavel. There are still *kohanim*, *levi'im*, and *yisraelim* in Bavel. And correspondingly, *psulim* also remain in Bavel. You are no better than we are.

The Gemara notes that Rabbi Yochanan had in that moment forgotten the teaching of Rabbi Elazar: *lo ala Ezra miBavel ad she'asah k'soles nekiyah v'alah* — Ezra did not ascend from Bavel until he had made it like refined flour, meaning he specifically removed all the *psulim*, even if he did not remove all the legitimate Israelites.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אִיקְּלַע לְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא לְבֵי רַב יְהוּדָה, חַזְיֵיהּ לְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה דְּגָדֵל וְלָא נָסֵיב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא קָא מַנְסֵיב לֵיהּ מָר אִיתְּתָא לִבְרֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי יָדַעְנָא מֵהֵיכָא אֶנְסְבַיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַטּוּ אֲנַן מִי יָדְעִינַן מֵהֵיכָא קָאָתֵינַן? דִּילְמָא מֵהָנָךְ דִּכְתִיב: ״נָשִׁים בְּצִיּוֹן עִנּוּ בְּתֻלֹת בְּעָרֵי יְהוּדָה״. וְכִי תֵּימָא נׇכְרִי וְעֶבֶד הַבָּא עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל הַוָּלָד כָּשֵׁר, וְדִילְמָא מֵהָנָךְ דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ: ״הַשֹּׁכְבִים עַל מִטּוֹת שֵׁן וּסְרֻחִים עַל עַרְשׂוֹתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: **Ulla arrived in Pumbedita to the house of Rav Yehuda. He observed that Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, was grown up and was unmarried.** Ulla **said to** Rav Yehuda: **What is the reason** that **the Master does not marry a woman to his son?** Rav Yehuda **said to him: Do I know from where I** can find a woman **to marry** him? I am concerned about flawed lineage. Ulla **said to him: Is that to say that we know where we come from?** Can we be sure that our lineage is unflawed? **Perhaps** we are **from those** about **whom it is written: "They have ravished the women in Zion, the maidens in the cities of Judah"** (Lamentations 5:11). Perhaps we are descended from women ravished by gentiles. **And if you would say** that you are not concerned about that possibility, since you maintain that in the case of **a gentile or a slave who engaged in sexual intercourse with a Jewish woman,** the lineage of **the offspring is unflawed, perhaps** we come **from those about whom it is written: "That lie upon beds of ivory, and stretch themselves upon their couches"** (Amos 6:4).

Ulla came to Pumbedita and visited Rav Yehuda. He observed that Rav Yehuda's son Rabbi Yitzchak was already grown but had not yet married. Rav Yehuda explained: *mi yadana me'heicha yinasiv* — I do not know where to marry him off, because the question of *yichus* is so fraught. Ulla pushed back vigorously: *atu anan mi yad'inan me'heicha kasina?* — Do we even know where we ourselves come from? Perhaps we descend from women who were violated during the *Churban HaBayis*, as the verse in Lamentations attests: *nashim b'Tzion inu b'sulos b'arei Yehuda*. Rav Yehuda responded: but such a union would still produce a *kasher* child halachically. Ulla countered: what about other disqualifying unions that occurred among the first Temple generation — the Gemara cites the verse *ha'shochvim al mitos shen u'sruchim al arsosam*, which Rabbi Abbahu interprets not as a reference to mere indecency but as describing widespread wife-swapping — eating and drinking together, pushing beds together, exchanging wives — *u'masrichim arsoseihem b'shichvas zera she'eina shelahem*, and thereby filling their beds with semen that was not theirs. Given such pervasive adultery in the generation of the first Temple, there are certainly *mamzeirim* embedded in many Jewish family trees. So how does Rav Yehuda know that anyone is pure enough for his son?

### Silence as Yichus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל בָּתַר שְׁתִיקוּתָא, כִּי הַאי דְּבָדְקִי בְּנֵי מַעְרְבָא: כִּי מִינְּצוּ בֵּי תְרֵי בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי חָזוּ הֵי מִנַּיְיהוּ דְּקָדֵים וְשָׁתֵיק – אָמְרִי: הַאי מְיוּחָס טְפֵי. אָמַר רַב: שְׁתִיקוּתֵיהּ דְּבָבֶל – הַיְינוּ יִחוּסָא. אִינִי? וְהָא אִיקְּלַע רַב לְבֵי בַּר שָׁפֵי חַלָּא וּבְדַק בְּהוּ, מַאי לָאו בְּיַחֲסוּתָא? לָא, בִּשְׁתִיקוּתָא. הָכִי קָאָמַר לְהוּ: בְּדוּקוּ אִי שָׁתְקִי אִי לָא שָׁתְקִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said to** Ulla: If so, **what shall we do?** How can we clarify which families are of unflawed lineage? Ulla **said to him: Go after the silence, like the way the people of the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **examine: When two** people **quarrel with each other, they observe which of them becomes silent first.** Then **they say: This** silent party **is of finer lineage.** **Rav says: The silence of Babylonia is its lineage.** In other words, this is an effective method of examining a person's lineage in Babylonia as well. The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But Rav arrived at the house of the son of a vinegar strainer and examined them. What, is it not** that he conducted an examination **into** their **lineage?** The Gemara answers: **No,** he conducted an examination **into their silence. This is** what Rav **said to** those conducting the examination: **Examine whether they become silent** when they quarrel **or** whether **they do not become silent.**

Rav Yehuda acknowledged the point and asked: so what should I do if I still want to find a suitable match? The answer given is: *zil basar shtikusa* — follow the families that are quiet, that do not argue and pick fights. Rashi explains this as based on the premise — whether understood genetically or sociologically — that families of compromised lineage tend toward contentiousness, either as a character trait passed down through their ancestry or as a result of having historically been marginalized by society, cultivating a defensive and argumentative culture.

The people of Eretz Yisrael used a similar method of investigation: *ki matzu bei srei bahadi hadadei* — when two families were in dispute, they would watch to see which one was first willing to be silent, to step back and not press the fight. *Hai meyuchas tefei* — the one more willing to stop fighting has the better *yichus*. What begins as an index of *yichus* becomes, practically speaking, a direct focus on *middos*. The inquiry about lineage has been quietly displaced by an inquiry about character.

Rav states: *shetikutei deBavel hainu yichusa* — the silence of Bavel is itself its *yichus*. The willingness to not fight is not merely a sign of good lineage; it is what the lineage actually means in practice. When challenged that Rav himself went to investigate a certain family, the Gemara clarifies: he did not investigate their genealogical records; he investigated whether they were *shtiki* — non-argumentative. That was his method of verification.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אִם רָאִיתָ שְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁמִּתְגָּרִים זֶה בָּזֶה – שֶׁמֶץ פְּסוּל יֵשׁ בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן, וְאֵין מַנִּיחִין אוֹתוֹ לִידַּבֵּק אֶחָד בַּחֲבֵירוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אִם רָאִיתָ שְׁתֵּי מִשְׁפָּחוֹת הַמִּתְגָּרוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ – שֶׁמֶץ פְּסוּל יֵשׁ בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן, וְאֵין מַנִּיחִין אוֹתָהּ לִידַּבֵּק בַּחֲבֶרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: If you see two people feuding with each other, there is a trace of unfitness in one of them.** In other words, there are grounds to suspect that the lineage of one of them is flawed. Consequently, that one **is prevented** by Heaven **from joining the other** through marriage, and that leads them to feud with each other. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: If you see two families feuding with each other, there is a trace of unfitness in one of them, and** that family **is prevented** by Heaven **from joining the other.**

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *im ra'ita shenei bnei adam shemitgarim zeh ba'zeh, shemetz pasul yesh be'echad mehen* — if you see two people perpetually feuding, there is a scintilla of invalidity in one of them, *v'ein manichin oto lidabek bechavertah* — and from Heaven, they are made to quarrel so that the families should not intermarry. It is worth noting that this logic works better if the concern is something like a hereditary disease — both families are carriers and should not intermingle — than if the concern is purely about lineage status, where the problem would apply equally to any marriage partner. Nevertheless, the principle stands: contentiousness is treated as a sign, if not a symptom, of underlying genealogical trouble.

### Bavel Bri'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא סָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: בָּבֶל – בְּרִיאָה. מֵישׁוֹן – מֵיתָה. מָדַי – חוֹלָה. עֵילָם – גּוֹסֶסֶת. וּמָה בֵּין חוֹלִין לְגוֹסְסִין? רוֹב חוֹלִין לְחַיִּים, רוֹב גּוֹסְסִים לְמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa the Elder says in the name of Rav: Babylonia** is **healthy** with regard to lineage and clear of suspicion. **Mishon** is **dead,** meaning that all its inhabitants have flawed lineage. **Media** is **sick,** and **Eilam** is **moribund.** The Gemara clarifies: **And what** is the difference **between sick and moribund? Most sick** people recover **to** a healthy **life,** whereas **most** of those who are **moribund** are destined **for death.** Likewise, the majority of the residents of Media had unflawed lineage, while the majority of those living in Eilam had flawed lineage.

Rav Pappa savya said in the name of Rav: *Bavel bri'ah* — Bavel is healthy, meaning its *yichus* is sound. *Meshan mitah* — this neighboring region is dead, meaning virtually all its inhabitants are of suspect lineage — *mamzeirim* and the like. *Madai mi'idi cholah* — Media is sick, meaning mostly *kasher* but with some *psulim* present. *Ve'Eilam hi goseset* — Elam is like one in the final stages of dying, meaning mostly *pasul* with some *kasher* present. The difference between *cholah* and *goseset* is simply probabilistic: most sick people live, while most of those on their deathbed die.

## Geographic Limits

### East/West Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד הֵיכָן הִיא בָּבֶל? רַב אָמַר: עַד נָהָר עֲזָק, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: עַד נְהַר יוּאָנִי. לְעֵיל בְּדִיגְלַת עַד הֵיכָא? רַב אָמַר: עַד בַּגְדָּא וְאַוְונָא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: עַד מוּשְׁכְּנֵי. וְלֹא מוּשְׁכְּנֵי בַּכְּלָל? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מוּשְׁכְּנֵי הֲרֵי הִיא כַּגּוֹלָה לְיוּחֲסִין! אֶלָּא: עַד מוּשְׁכְּנֵי, וּמוּשְׁכְּנֵי בַּכְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** After having determined that those from Babylonia are presumed to have unflawed lineage, the Gemara clarifies what the borders of Babylonia are with regard to this issue. **Until where does** the width of **Babylonia** extend? **Rav said: Until the River Azak,** which empties into the Euphrates. **And Shmuel said: Until the River Yo'ani,** which also empties into the Euphrates. The Gemara asks: **Until where does** the border extend **upward,** meaning northward, **on the Tigris? Rav said: Until** the places called **Bagda and Avna. And Shmuel said: Until Mushekanei.** The Gemara asks: **But** according to Shmuel, **isn't Mushekanei included** in Babylonia? **But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba say** that **Shmuel says: Mushekanei is like the exile,** meaning it is like Pumbedita in central Babylonia, **with regard to lineage? Rather,** Shmuel meant: **Until and including Mushekanei.**

The Gemara now turns to the question of the precise geographic boundaries of Bavel — a necessary question given that Bavel has been established as the gold standard of *yichus*. *Ad heichan hi Bavel?* Rav says: until the *Nahar Azak* on the east. Shmuel says: until the *Nahar Yoni*. Rashi identifies this as the discussion of the eastern boundary. The primary territory of Bavel is understood to be bounded by the Tigris and the Euphrates — *bein shenei neharot*, between the two rivers — but the question here is how far the recognized region extends beyond those two rivers in each cardinal direction.

### Northern Limits

Going north along the Tigris: Rav says until *Bagda* and *Avna*. Shmuel says until *Mushchani* — but not including *Mushchani* itself. Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba then says in the name of Shmuel: *Mushchani harei hi keGolah* — Mushchani is like *Golah*. In this context, *Golah* is not exile in the pejorative sense but rather a name for Pumbedita, the city most closely associated with purity of *yichus*. So Mushchani is treated as having the status of Pumbedita — genuine Babylonian *yichus*.

### Euphrates Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְתַחְתִּית בְּדִיגְלַת עַד הֵיכָא? אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל: עַד אַפַּמֵיָיא תַּתָּאָה. תַּרְתֵּי אַפַּמֵיָיא הָוְיָין: חֲדָא עִילִּיתָא וַחֲדָא תַּתַּיְיתָא, חֲדָא כְּשֵׁירָה וַחֲדָא פְּסוּלָה, וּבֵין חֲדָא לַחֲדָא פַּרְסָה, וְקָא קָפְדִי אַהֲדָדֵי וַאֲפִילּוּ נוּרָא לָא מוֹשְׁלִי אַהֲדָדֵי. וְסִימָנָיךְ דִּפְסוּלְתָּא הָא דְּמִישְׁתַּעֲיָא מֵישָׁנִית. לְעֵיל בִּפְרָת עַד הֵיכָא? רַב אָמַר: עַד אַקְרָא דְתוּלְבַּקְנֵי. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: עַד גִּישְׁרָא דְּבֵי פְרָת. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: עַד מַעַבַּרְתָּ[א] דְגִיזְמָא. לָיֵיט אַבָּיֵי וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יוֹסֵף אַדְּרַב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further clarifies: **How far does** the border extend **downward,** meaning southward, **on the Tigris? Rav Shmuel said: Until** the city of **Lower Appamya.** The Gemara comments: **There are two** cities called **Appamya, the upper one and the lower one.** In terms of the lineage of their residents, **one is unflawed and the other is flawed, and they are separated by** a distance of **a parasang [*parsa*]. And they are particular with regard to one another.** The residents of the two cities avoid each other to the extent that **they do not even loan each other fire,** to prevent them from developing a closeness with each other. **And your mnemonic** to remember which is which is that **the unfit one** is **that** one **that speaks the Mishon dialect.** As stated above, Mishon is considered dead with regard to lineage. The Gemara asks: **How far does** the border extend **upward,** meaning northward, **on the Euphrates? Rav said: Until the fortress** of **Tulbaknei. And Shmuel said: Until the bridge over the Euphrates. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Until the crossing at Gizma.** The Gemara relates: **Abaye would curse, and some say** it was **Rav Yosef** that would curse, **one** who ruled in accordance with the opinion **of Rav,** as he held that Rav extended the border too far north.

Going north along the Euphrates: Rav says until *Akra deTulbanki*. Shmuel says until the bridge of *Perat*. Rabbi Yochanan says until the crossing of *Gizma*.

Abaye, and some say Rav Yosef, cursed Rav's position for extending the boundary too far north. One might ask: why curse Rav and not Shmuel, whose position went even farther north? The answer given is that the curse applied to Rav *and certainly* to Shmuel — the curser was criticizing those who extended the valid boundary too far, and Shmuel's more expansive position was included by implication.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַב לָיֵיט, אַדִּשְׁמוּאֵל לָא לָיֵיט? אֶלָּא לָיֵיט אַדְּרַב, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן אַדִּשְׁמוּאֵל. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם אַדְּרַב לָיֵיט, אַדִּשְׁמוּאֵל לָא לָיֵיט, וְגִישְׁרָא דְּבֵי פְרָת לְתַתַּאיה הֲוָה קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara is puzzled by this statement: Why did he **curse one** who ruled in accordance with the opinion **of Rav,** but he **did not curse one** who ruled in accordance with the opinion **of Shmuel?** But the bridge over the Euphrates is further north than the fortress of Tulbaknei. **Rather,** he **would curse one** who ruled in accordance with the opinion **of Rav, and all the more so one** who ruled in accordance with the opinion **of Shmuel. And if you wish, say: In fact, he cursed one** who ruled in accordance with the opinion **of Rav,** but **he did not curse one** who ruled in accordance with the opinion **of Shmuel, and the bridge over the Euphrates** actually **stood lower down,** i.e., farther south. In their times the bridge was to the south of Tulbaknei, and Abaye agreed that it was in Babylonia.

Alternatively, there is no contradiction at all: when Shmuel made his ruling, the bridge of *Perat* was actually located further south than its current position. The Persians subsequently moved it northward. *Ha'idna hu de'illyuyei Parsa'ei* — it is only now that the Persians have situated it further north, but Shmuel's original designation referred to the lower location, which was actually less expansive than Rav's.

### Chavil Yama

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא: חֲבֵיל יַמָּא – תְּכֵילְתָּא דְבָבֶל. שׁוּנְיָא וְגוּבְיָא – תְּכֵילְתָּא דַּחֲבֵיל יַמָּא. רָבִינָא אָמַר: אַף צִיצוֹרָא. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, חָנָן בֶּן פִּנְחָס אוֹמֵר: חֲבֵיל יַמָּא – תְּכֵילְתָּא דְּבָבֶל, שׁוּנְיָא וְגוּבְיָא וְצִיצוֹרָא – תְּכֵילְתָּא דַּחֲבֵיל יַמָּא. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וְהָאִידָּנָא אִיעָרַבִי בְּהוּ כּוּתָאֵי. וְלָא הִיא, אִיתְּתָא הוּא דִּבְעָא מִינַּיְיהוּ, וְלָא יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ. מַאי חֲבֵיל יַמָּא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: זוֹ פְּרָת דְּבוֹרְסִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Abba said:** The province of **Ḥaveil Yamma is the glory of Babylonia** with regard to lineage; **Shunya and Guvya** are **the glory of Ḥaveil Yamma. Ravina said:** The town of **Tzitzora** is **also** like Shunya and Guvya. **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Ḥanan ben Pineḥas says: Ḥaveil Yamma is the glory of Babylonia; Shunya and Guvya and Tzitzora** are **the glory of Ḥaveil Yamma. Rav Pappa says: And nowadays, Samaritans have assimilated with them,** and their lineage is problematic. The Gemara comments: **And** that **is not so.** Rather, one Samaritan **requested** to marry **a woman from them and they would not give** her **to him,** which led to the rumor that Samaritans had assimilated with them. The Gemara asks: **What** is this region called **Ḥaveil Yamma? Rav Pappa said: This** is the area near the **Euphrates** adjacent **to Bursi.**

Rami bar Abba teaches: *Chavil Yama techla deBavel* — *Chavil Yama* is the *techeiles*, the finest quality, of Bavel. And within *Chavil Yama*, the cities of *Shunya* and *Guvya* are the crème de la crème. Ravina adds: even *Tzitzura* is included. A *baraita* corroborates: Chanan ben Pinchas said the same — *Chavil Yama techla deBavel*, and *Shunya, Guvya, and Tzitzura* are the finest within it.

Rav Papa then introduces a contemporary qualification: *ve'ha'idna arevu behu Kutai* — in his time, *Kutim* — Samaritans — had intermingled there, diminishing its *yichus*. The Gemara immediately challenges this: *velo hi* — it is not so. The Gemara explains that a certain individual wanted to marry a woman from that region and they refused him, and he then began raising the claim of Samaritan infiltration. Rashi notes that it is very difficult to attribute to Rav Papa the *motzi la'az* of slandering an entire community out of pique at a personal rejection. Rashi therefore prefers the reading that it was a *Kuti* who sought to marry there and was refused, and the episode led to some concern about Samaritan presence — but not substantiated enough to genuinely compromise the *yichus* of *Chavil Yama*.

### Shot Mishat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא מִן שׁוֹט מֵישׁוֹט, עָמַד רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא עַל רַגְלָיו וְאָמַר: שׁוֹט מֵישׁוֹט בֵּין הַנְּהָרוֹת עוֹמֶדֶת. וְכִי בֵּין הַנְּהָרוֹת עוֹמֶדֶת, מַאי הָוֵי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר עוּקְבָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: בֵּין הַנְּהָרוֹת הֲרֵי הִיא כַּגּוֹלָה לְיוּחֲסִין. וְהֵיכָא קָיְימָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מֵאִיהִי דְקִירָא וּלְעֵיל. וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַד מַעְבַּרְתָּא דְגִיזְמָא! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רְצוּעָה נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who said to** the Sages: **I am from** a place called **Shot Mishot. Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa stood on his feet and said: Shot Mishot is located between the** Tigris and Euphrates **Rivers.** The Gemara asks: **And if it is located between the rivers, what of it?** What *halakha* is this relevant for? **Abaye said** that **Rabbi Ḥama bar Ukva says** that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** The area **between the rivers is like the exile,** meaning Pumbedita, **with regard to lineage.** The Gemara inquires: **And where is** the area between the rivers **located** for the purpose of this *halakha*? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: From Ihi Dekira and upward,** i.e., northward. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: Until the crossing at Gizma** but no further? **Abaye said: A strip extends** from that region past Ihi Dekira.

The Gemara concludes with the location of *Chavil Yama* itself: *Zu Prat DeBorsi* — it is the region of *Prat DeBorsi*. A man who declared himself to be from *Shot Mishat* prompted Rav Yitzchak Nafcha to clarify: *agra d'amshat Shot Mishat bein hanaharot omedet* — the advantage of *Shot Mishat* is that it stands between the rivers, between the Tigris and the Euphrates, and is therefore classic Bavel with good *yichus*.

The Gemara then asks: what exactly constitutes *bein hanaharot* — between the rivers? Abaye said in the name of Rav Chama bar Ukva, who said in the name of Rabbi Yose in the name of Rabbi Chanina: *bein hanaharot kegolah leyuchsin* — being between the rivers is like *Golah*, like Pumbedita, for purposes of *yuchasin*. Any place between the Tigris and the Euphrates has this elevated status.

Rabbi Yochanan specifies: the region of *bein hanaharot* begins from *Dikra* going northward, and extends until the crossing of *Gizma*. But Abaye adds: *retzu'ah nafka* — there is a strip that extends beyond, reaching further north and including *Shot Mishat* within the zone of *bein hanaharot*. Even though *Shot Mishat* lies north of *Dikra*, the strip extension brings it within the territory of Bavel and grants it the status of *bein hanaharot* for purposes of *yuchasin*.
