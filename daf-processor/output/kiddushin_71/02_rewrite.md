# Kiddushin 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chashmonai Testimony

### Slave Declaration

The daf begins in the middle of 71b, six lines down from the top of the wide text, at the word *Chashmonai*. We are in the midst of a narrative involving Rav Yehuda and Rav Huna, centered on a case of *din Torah* that arose when a certain man came to Pumbedita, insulted Rav Yehuda's attendant, and was himself known to speak disparagingly of others — calling people slaves. In response, Rav Yehuda declared this man to be a slave. The broader backdrop of this story is the principle of *kol haposel bemumo posel* — one who invalidates others does so because of his own defect.

As established in the previous daf, Rav Huna challenged Rav Yehuda's declaration, arguing that even if Shmuel taught *kol haposel bemumo posel*, this teaching should at most make one *choshesh* — cautiously alert — but not empower a formal declaration of slavery. At that very moment, the man in question entered the room and declared indignantly that he was a descendant of *Beit Chashmonai*. Rav Yehuda seized on this: precisely as I said! Because Shmuel himself taught that anyone who claims descent from *Beit Chashmonai* is a slave, since the original Hasmonean dynasty was entirely killed off, and it was only Herod — an Edomite slave — who remained and identified with that lineage. Therefore, any contemporary claim to Hasmonean descent is itself evidence of slave ancestry.

### Kol Haposel Teaching

The Gemara now raises a pointed challenge to Rav Yehuda's reliance on this teaching. Rav Huna said to him: do you not hold by the principle that Rav Abba cited in the name of Rav, who cited it in the name of Rav Huna — who cited it in the name of Rav? This is already somewhat striking: Rav Huna is quoting a teaching transmitted in his own name, from Rav, the classic *bar plugta* of Shmuel. The teaching states: *kol talmid chacham shemoreh halacha uva* — any *talmid chacham* who advances a new legal ruling, *im kodem maaseh amra shomin lo* — if he taught it before the relevant case arose, he is believed. But *im lav ein shomin lo* — if he produces the ruling only at the moment when the case conveniently arises before him, we are somewhat suspicious of it.

This is a remarkable piece of Talmudic skepticism. The Gemara acknowledges that a scholar might fabricate a tradition in the name of his teacher precisely to respond to a case at hand. In our story, the suspicion is compounded: Rav Yehuda was not merely citing a teaching that happened to be relevant — he was invoking it specifically to vindicate his own prior ruling. He declared the man a slave, and now he produces a teaching of Shmuel that justifies that declaration. One might fairly ask: why have we never heard this teaching before?

### Rav Matna Confirms

To this challenge, Rav Yehuda responded: *ha'ika Rav Masna dekay kivasay* — Rav Matna holds like me. Rav Matna can corroborate that this is indeed a teaching of Shmuel. It then happened that Rav Matna, who had not been in Nehardea — the location of these proceedings — for the past thirteen years, arrived that very day. *Hahu yoma* — by pure coincidence, on that exact day, he came to Nehardea.

Rav Yehuda then had to prompt Rav Matna without leading him, and he did so elegantly: do you remember what Shmuel taught *ki kachada* — on that occasion when he was stepping onto the ferry, one foot still on the bank and one foot already on the vessel? The memorable physical circumstance made the teaching verifiable and distinctive. Rav Matna confirmed: *hachi amar Shmuel* — Shmuel said: *kol d'omar midebeis Chashmonai moga chasina avda hu* — anyone who says he descends from the royal house of the Hasmoneans is a slave.

### Woman on Roof

Rav Matna then provided the reason for Shmuel's ruling. He recalled that after Herod's violent overthrow of the Hasmonean house, only one young woman remained from the original dynasty. When she saw what Herod had done — that the entire family had been slaughtered — she went up to the roof, *v'ramya kala*, and called out: let it be known that anyone who claims descent from the house of *Chashmonai* is a slave, for there is no one left. And then, *nafla m'igra umesa* — she fell from the roof and died. With this, the entire Hasmonean lineage was extinguished, and Herod — the Edomite slave — was the only one who identified himself with that heritage.

Rav Yehuda had now been vindicated on all fronts.

### Ketuvot Torn Up

*Achrizu alei d'avda hu* — they announced about this man that he was indeed a slave, exactly as Rav Yehuda had originally declared. The consequences were severe: *hahu yoma akran kama ksuva beNardaa* — on that day, many *ketubot* were torn up in Nehardea.

Rashi explains that the members of this man's family, having now been identified as slaves, could not have contracted valid *kiddushin*, and so those marriages required invalidation. There is an obvious problem, however: the man's testimony applies only to himself — there is no independent, objective evidence that implicates his family members. How could their marriages be invalidated on the basis of his own admission? One commentator resolves this by suggesting that the *ketubot* were torn up for a different reason: he had served as a signatory on those documents, and now that he had been declared *pasul*, his testimony was rendered invalid, requiring the documents to be redrawn. Yet the narrative suggests something more sweeping.

As Rav Yehuda departed Nehardea to return to Pumbedita, the families whose marriages had been disrupted ran after him intending to stone him — literally, stones were in their hands. He turned to them and said: *ei shtiku shtiku* — if you stay silent, stay silent. But if you continue making a fuss, *maglina alaichu* — I will reveal something about you that is even worse. What could be worse than being declared slaves? Shmuel, he said, also taught: *tarti zarayasa ika beNardaa* — there are two families in Nehardea, one called the house of the dove and one called the house of the raven — *v'simanach tamei tamei tahor tahor* — the impure family bears the name of the impure bird, and the pure family bears the name of the pure bird. Those descended from the raven are of invalid lineage — *mamzerim*, slaves, or the like.

How exactly this threat made things worse if they were already declared slaves is somewhat unclear from the text; perhaps the formal *halachic* ruling against the wider family had not yet been made and remained merely a suspicion. Regardless, the message landed. *Shadu alahu rigma* — they threw down the stones they had intended to hurl at him, and so many stones fell into the *Nehar Malka* that they formed a dam in the river.

## Invalid Families

### Announced Invalidities

The foregoing story raises an important question: if Rav Yehuda knew about the invalidity of these Nehardean families, why had he been withholding the information? We will return shortly to the principle of *mishpacha keivan shenitm'ah nitm'ah* — once a family's lineage has been submerged, one leaves it submerged. That principle stands in tension with what we have just witnessed. In the present story, the declaration of slavery came only as a response to the man's own behavior — *kol haposel bemumo posel* — and it happened to be borne out. Nevertheless, the Gemara now pivots in the opposite direction, moving into a series of proactive public announcements about people and places of problematic lineage.

*Machriz Rav Yehuda* — Rav Yehuda publicly announced, according to one textual tradition in Pumbedita and according to another in Nehardea: *Ada veYonatan avdi* — these two individuals are slaves; *Yehuda bar Pappa mamzer* — this person is a *mamzer*; *Bati bar Tuvia berama srucha deshakil gitei* — this wealthy man, universally assumed to be of excellent *yichus*, was actually offered a *shtar shichrur*, a deed of manumission, but refused to accept it because of his pride in his high social standing. Nonetheless, he is halachically a slave. Here Rav Yehuda is not merely responding to a case — he is proactively labeling individuals as *pasul*.

### Rava's Declarations

*Machriz Rava beMechuza* — Rava similarly made public announcements in Mechuza, his own city: *Belai Danai Talai Malai Zagai kulam lepsul* — these families are all of invalid status, whether *mamzer*, *avdus*, or some other form of disqualification. Rav Yehuda further identified whole towns: *Gvai* is a corruption of *Givonai* — the inhabitants are really *Givonim*, of problematic lineage. *Durnunisa* is *Darei Netinai* — the village of *Netinim*. The *Netinim* and the *Givonim* are the same. And Rav Yosef added that a suburb of Pumbedita, *Beikvei*, was entirely composed of slaves.

These broad geographic labelings are deeply troubling, moving beyond the identification of individuals to the invalidation of entire communities. The Gemara draws a parallel to the actions of Ezra, who similarly identified and sorted the *psulim* when he returned from Bavel to Eretz Yisrael.

### Slave Intermarriage

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *arba meos avadim, ve'amrei lei arba alafim avadim hayu lo lePishchar ben Imar* — four hundred slaves, and some say four thousand slaves, belonged to a man named Pishchar ben Imar. *Vekulam nitmu bikehuna* — all of them intermarried among *kohanim*. Rashi explains that as slaves of a *kohen*, they were permitted to eat *terumah* and were thus able to pass themselves off as *kohanim*, eventually intermarrying into priestly families. The practical consequence is alarming: a *kohen* we encounter today might actually descend from one of these slaves. But how far does that suspicion extend — should we be suspicious of every *kohen*?

The Gemara offers a suggested marker: *vekol kohen sheyesh bo azus panim eino ela mehem* — any *kohen* who displays brazenness is probably from among these slaves and is not a legitimate *kohen*.

### Abaye's Pushback

Abaye pushes back on this, first noting practically that all of these slave-descended pseudo-*kohanim* can be found in a particular position of authority in a specific locale — they are identifiable. But he then challenges the underlying principle directly. This view, that brazenness is a marker of illegitimate priestly descent, *pliga d'Rabbi Elazar* — contradicts the position of Rabbi Elazar. Rabbi Elazar taught: *im ra'isa kohen b'azus metzach al taharher acharav* — if you see a *kohen* who acts with brazenness, do not suspect thereby that he is an illegitimate *kohen*. The proof is from the verse *v'amcha k'mrivai kohen* — your people are like those who contend with a *kohen*, which indicates that a *kohen* can be contentious; it takes two parties to have a quarrel. So brazenness alone cannot be taken as evidence of disqualified lineage.

We find ourselves, then, in a state of tension: a great deal of labeling and suspecting, with only a small degree of pushback thus far — though the pushback will grow considerably.

## Aggada on Lineage

### Divine Presence & Yichus

The Gemara now turns to *aggadic* material bearing on the themes of genealogy and the Divine Presence. Rav Avin bar Rav Adda said in the name of Rav: *kol hanosei ishah she'eino hogenes lo* — anyone who marries a woman of lesser lineage than himself, *kshe'hakadosh baruch hu mashreh shechinaso maid al kol hashvatim v'ein maid alav* — when God causes His *Shechina* to dwell among the people, He testifies on behalf of all the *shevatim* but will not testify about or identify with that family. The verse cited is *shivtei Yah edus l'Yisrael* — the tribes of God, testimony to Israel. The *Shechina* gives testimony about Israel only when they are truly *shivtei Yah*, when the tribal and family structures are properly maintained and not adulterated.

A subtle question arises: does "adulterated" here refer to marrying someone of inferior but still halachically valid status — in which case this *aggadah* is making a sweeping statement about social hierarchy — or does it refer to marrying someone of formally invalid status, a *mamzer* or slave, who would genuinely disqualify the family line? Given the immediate context of the discussion about *mamzeirim* and *avdus*, the latter reading seems more likely, but the text does not foreclose the former.

### Shechina Dwelling

Rabbi Chama in the name of Rabbi Chanina sharpens this further: *kshe'hakadosh baruch hu mashreh shechinaso, ein mashreh ela al mishpachos meyuchasos shebeYisrael* — when God causes His *Shechina* to rest, it rests only upon families of distinguished lineage within Israel. The verse is from Jeremiah: *ba'eis hahi neum Hashem eheyeh l'Elokim l'chol mishpachos Yisrael* — at that time, says God, I will be a God to all the *mishpachos* of Israel. Notably, the verse does not say *l'chol Yisrael* but *l'chol mishpachos* — to all the families, meaning those who have preserved their family distinctions and *yichus*. This is a stronger statement than the previous one; here the *Shechina* does not merely withhold its testimony, but actively does not dwell except upon families of recognized lineage.

### Native vs Convert

Rabbah bar Rav Huna draws a distinction between a native-born Israelite and a *ger*: *zo ma'alah yesh bein Yisrael l'geirim* — there is a level of superiority that the native-born Jew holds over the convert. When Scripture speaks about Israel, it says *v'hayisi lahem l'Elokim v'heimah yihyu li l'am* — I will be for them a God, and they will be for Me a nation. The relationship with God is stated first; it preexists and draws Israel near. When Scripture addresses converts, it says: *mi hu zeh arav es libo l'higeshal elai v'hayisem li l'am v'anochi eheyeh lachem l'Elokim* — whoever stirs his heart to come close to Me, you will be for Me a nation and I will be for you a God. Here the initiative must come from the convert; the divine relationship follows rather than precedes their approach.

Rashi reads this as descriptive: the native-born Jew is born into an existing covenantal relationship that draws him naturally toward God, while the *ger* must first generate the motivation from within himself. One might fairly read this as an extraordinary tribute to the spiritual achievement of the convert — the initiative required is immense. However, the Gemara's framing is plainly hierarchical, and it cannot easily be read otherwise.

### Converts as Scabs

Rabbi Chelbo makes a striking and harsh statement: *kashim gerim le'Yisrael kesappachat* — converts are as difficult for Israel as a scab. The verse is *venilva hager aleihem venispachu al beit Yaakov* — the stranger will cleave to them and be added onto the house of Jacob. The word *venispachu* is connected exegetically to *sappachat*, the skin affliction mentioned among the forms of *tzara'at*.

What does this mean? Rashi, working within the context of tractate Yevamot, explains that converts are sometimes insufficiently conversant with the tradition and may lead others astray. Tosafot, however, connects the statement to the immediately preceding discussion: if the *Shechina* dwells only upon *mishpachos meyuchasos*, then the presence of *geirim* among Israel — who do not have the same chain of *yichus* — diminishes that quality. This is an extremely harsh reading of the concept of *yichus*, one that operates not merely as a halachic category but as a marker of social and spiritual hierarchy.

Tosafot then cites, though without attribution, an interpretation by one Rav Avraham Hager — a man who was himself a convert yet whose interpretation made its way into Tosafot — who read the statement in precisely the opposite spirit: because converts are new to the tradition and approach it with passion rather than complacency, they put to shame the native-born Jews who are not so *medakdek bemitzvot*. This is a beautiful inversion, and Tosafot records it seriously.

There is no question that this entire sugyah on *yichus* sits in considerable tension with a more egalitarian ethos, and equally with other Talmudic passages that speak in glowing terms about *geirim* and invoke the commandments of *ve'ahavtem et hager* and *kager ka'ezrach*. Both currents are present in the Talmud, and the tension between them is real.

## Keep It Hidden

### Money Purifies

Continuing at the bottom of amud bet, Rabbi Chama bar Chanina teaches: *kshe'hakadosh baruch hu metaher shvatim* — when God comes to purify the tribes, *shivto shel Levi metaher techila* — He begins with the tribe of Levi. The prooftext is from Malachi: *veyashev metzaref umetaher kesef vetiher es bnei Levi v'zikak osam k'zahav v'chakesef* — He will sit as one who smelts and purifies silver, and He will purify the sons of Levi, refining them like gold and silver, and they will then be able to bring offerings to God in righteousness.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, however, turns this passage on its head: *kesef metaher mamzeirim* — it is money that purifies *mamzeirim*. This is a striking inversion of the imagery. The verse's metaphor of silver purification is reread not as an analogy for genealogical purification but as a literal reference to wealth. Rashi explains the reference: wealthy individuals — for the sake of money — married into the tribe of Levi despite having invalid status, and the point Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is making is not that money literally confers kashrut, but rather that once people have intermarried, the genealogical record is absorbed and everything becomes *kasher* by virtue of being submerged. That is the operative mechanism of purification — not the money itself, but the intermingling that money enabled.

The Ri"f, however, reads *kesef metaher mamzeirim* more literally and more cleverly: the halachic mechanism by which a *mamzer* can produce non-*mamzer* offspring is through marriage to a *shifcha* — a female slave — whose child, being a slave, can then be freed and thereby exits the status of *mamzer* entirely. The money involved is the purchase price of the slave. This is an elegant halachic reading.

### Mixed Family Rule

Rabbi Yitzchak draws out the implication of the verse's closing phrase — *v'hayu l'Hashem magishei mincha bitzdaka*, they will bring offerings to God in righteousness: *tzadaka asa hakadosh baruch hu im Yisrael* — God performed an act of charity for Israel — *shemishpacha shenitme'ah nitme'ah* — that once a family has been submerged into the general population, it remains submerged. This is the principle of *mishpacha shenitme'ah nitme'ah*, written with an *ayin*, meaning merged rather than defiled. God's act of righteousness consists precisely in not requiring Israel to investigate and uproot every genealogical impurity that has been absorbed into the community. If people present themselves as *Levi'im* or *Yisraelim*, and their intermarriage has made it impossible to know otherwise, they are accepted as such.

With this, the Gemara begins to shift explicitly against the mode of proactive identification that characterized the earlier part of the *sugya*. The two tendencies — publicly labeling *psulim* on the one hand, and accepting the status quo on the other — have now both been articulated, and the latter is gaining ground.

## Boundaries & Assumptions

### Shmuel's Teaching

The Gemara introduces a teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel that returns to the geographic framework established earlier in the *perek*: *kol ha'aratzos issa l'Eretz Yisrael* — all other lands are like *issa*, dough, compared to Eretz Yisrael. The contrast drawn earlier was that when Ezra departed from Bavel, he left it like *solet nekiyah* — refined, sifted flour, with the bran entirely removed — meaning that all those of problematic lineage had departed with him, and what remained in Bavel was pure. All other lands lack this quality; they are like dough, mixed and unpurified. But *v'Eretz Yisrael issa l'Bavel* — Eretz Yisrael is itself like dough compared to Bavel. Even Eretz Yisrael, which contains *psulim* in known and recorded form, has a less certain pedigree than Bavel overall.

In the time of Rabbi, the question arose whether to reverse this ranking — to say that Bavel had perhaps deteriorated over time and should be considered *issa* relative to Eretz Yisrael. Rabbi responded sharply: *kotzim atem matilim li bein einai* — you are placing thorns in my eyes. Since Rabbi was descended from Hillel, and Hillel came from Bavel, any downgrading of Babylonian *yichus* would undermine Rabbi's own genealogical standing and his claim of descent from the house of David. Accordingly, he deferred the matter to Rabbi Chanina bar Chama, who engaged those raising the challenge and reported: *kach mikubalni mi'Rabbi Yishmael be'Rabbi Yossi she'amar mishum aviv* — this is the tradition I received from Rabbi Yishmael in the name of his father: all lands are dough compared to Eretz Yisrael, and Eretz Yisrael is dough compared to Bavel. The tradition was thus confirmed independently, which was important precisely because Rabbi himself could not have reported it without appearing self-interested.

### Diversion Strategy

A parallel episode occurred in the time of Rabbi Pinchas. When the same attempt arose — to reclassify Bavel as inferior to Eretz Yisrael — Rabbi Pinchas devised a remarkable stratagem. He told his servants: when I say two things in the *beit hamidrash*, rush me out immediately, carry me out on my stretcher and run. He went in and announced: *ein shechita l'of min haTorah* — the requirement of ritual slaughter for birds is not Biblically mandated; it is only rabbinic. This is a provocative ruling that does not represent the final *halacha*, but it was sufficiently surprising to throw the assembled scholars into a state of confusion and discussion. While they were processing this startling claim, he quickly added: *kol ha'aratzot issa l'Eretz Yisrael v'Eretz Yisrael issa l'Bavel* — and then his servants ran him out on the stretcher before anyone could ask follow-up questions.

The scholars pursued him but could not catch up. When they sat back down and began investigating which families in Eretz Yisrael might be problematic — since Rabbi Pinchas had implied that there were some *psulim* there — they found themselves approaching dangerous territory. *Ad she'higiu l'sakana* — until they reached a point of danger: Rashi explains that they began discovering that some very prominent families might have questionable *yichus*, at which point they realized it was better to stop looking. *U'pirshu* — and they ceased.

## Keep It Hidden…

### Rabbi Yochanan Stays Sil…

Rabbi Yochanan stated, taking an oath by the *Beit HaMikdash*: *b'yadeinu hu* — it is within our capacity to identify certain families of invalid lineage — *aval mah e'eseh she'gdolei hador nitme'u bo* — but what can I do, since great figures of this generation have already been intermingled with those families? To reveal the invalidity would call into question the *yichus* of major communal figures. And so Rabbi Yochanan kept silent. This is the clearest expression yet of the principle of *mishpacha shenitme'ah nitme'ah*: even when knowledge exists, the decision is made not to deploy it. Rabbi Yochanan *savar lah k'Rabbi Yitzchak* — held in accordance with Rabbi Yitzchak, who taught explicitly: *mishpacha shenitmeah nitmeah* — once a family has been merged in, it stays merged in.

### Forced Status

Abaye notes a corroborating *baraita* from tractate Eduyot: there was a family called *Beit Hatzerifah* on the far side of the Jordan which was actually of valid lineage, but a powerful man named Ben Tziyon used his authority — *b'zroa*, with force — to get everyone to treat them as *pasul*. Conversely, there was another family that was genuinely *pasul*, and Ben Tziyon again used his power to force people to treat them as *kasher*. The *Mishnah* then states: cases like these, *Eliyahu ba l'tamei u'l'taher l'rachek u'l'karev* — Eliyahu will come to set right, declaring the unjustly invalidated to be valid and the unjustly validated to be *pasul*.

The Gemara comments: this *mishnah* about Eliyahu refers specifically to *d'yedian* — situations that are known to be problematic and have been handled improperly. But *aval mishpacha shenitmeah* — when a family's invalid lineage has genuinely been absorbed and lost to public knowledge — *nitmeah*, it stays that way, and no rectification is expected.

### Secret Traditions

A *baraita* then teaches: *oda cheres haysa v'lo ratzu chachamim l'galosa* — there was another invalid family that the Sages did not wish to publicize. Nevertheless, the Sages transmitted this information to their children and students *pa'am achas b'shavua* — once every seven years; some say twice every seven years, so that some record would survive until the time of Eliyahu HaNavi.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak rules that the preferable position is once every seven years — the less spoken about such matters, the better. The preference for restraint is reinforced by a *baraita*: if a person says, "I hereby take upon myself *nezirut* on the condition that I will not reveal genealogical problems," we say *yehei nazir v'lo yigaleh mishpachos* — let him be a *nazir* and not reveal the family information. Rather than allowing him to choose between the *nezirut* and disclosure, we direct him to accept the *nezirut* and remain silent.

At this juncture, the Gemara draws an illuminating parallel. Rabbah bar Bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the four-letter Divine name — *shem ben arba otiyos* — the Sages transmitted to their students *pa'am achas b'shavua*, once every seven years; some say twice every seven years. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak again prefers the position of once every seven years, deriving support from the verse *zeh shemi le'olam* — this is My name forever — which is written *le'aleim*, suggesting it should remain hidden. The parallel is pointed: just as the transmission of God's secret name is hedged with restraint and solemnity, so too the transmission of genealogical secrets about invalid families.

Rava once sought to teach the four-letter name publicly to his students. A certain elder stopped him by quoting *le'aleim ksiv* — it is written to be concealed.

Rav Avina raises an apparent contradiction: one verse says *zeh shemi le'olam* — this is My name forever (which we read *le'illum*, hidden), while another says *zeh zikhri* — this is how I am to be remembered. God's response, as it were, is: *lo kshe'ani nikhtav ani nikra* — I am not read the way I am written. I am written with the *yud-keh-vav-keh* but read as *Adon*. The written name is for concealment; the spoken name is for public invocation. The name itself — *shemi* — is *le'aleim*, hidden. The name by which I am addressed — *zikhri* — is known.

The Gemara then describes a progression. Originally, the twelve-letter name of God was transmitted to everyone. Once those who were insufficiently guarded became more numerous and the name began to be misused, it was transmitted only to the *tzenu'in shebekehuna* — the particularly modest among the *kohanim*. These would weave the name into their melodic recitation during *Birkat Kohanim*, blending it into the singing so that the other *kohanim* — who were simultaneously stretching out the four-letter name — could not discern it.

Rabbi Tarfon testified personally: once, when he accompanied his maternal uncle to the *duchan* for *Birkat Kohanim*, he tilted his ear toward the *kohen gadol* — or perhaps, given the chronological difficulties with this testimony, toward a senior *kohen* — and overheard him weaving in the twelve-letter name during the melody.

The forty-two letter name — still more sublime and potent — was transmitted only under stringent conditions: the recipient must be *tzanua* — modest and trustworthy with secrets — *v'anav* — humble — *v'omed bechatzi yamav* — having reached at least the midpoint of life (approximately 35, assuming a lifespan of 70) — *v'eino ko'es* — not given to anger — *v'eino mishtaker* — not prone to intoxication — and *v'eino ma'amid al midotav* — one who does not press his claims insistently when wronged. The reason for these requirements, as Rashi explains, is that such a name carries enormous power to do damage, and a person who cannot control his temper or his drinking might use it to curse another. The reward for those who know the name and guard it with purity is that they become *ahuv lemala* — beloved above — *venichmad lemata* — cherished below — and *nochal shenei olamim*, inheriting both this world and the world to come.

## Boundaries & Assumptio…

### Ze'iri's Avoidance

Shmuel said in the name of an elder: *Bavel bechezkat ksheira omedet ad sheyivada lecha bameh nifsala* — Bavel stands in the presumption of being kasher unless you know specifically on what basis a given person became *pasul*. By contrast, other lands — *bechezkat pasul hein omdot ad sheyivada lecha bamah nichsharah* — stand in the presumption of invalidity unless you know how the person became *kasher*. Eretz Yisrael occupies a middle position: *muchzak lepasul pasul, muchzak lekasher kasher* — if known to be *pasul*, they are *pasul*; if known to be *kasher*, they are *kasher*.

This last formulation seems internally contradictory — it establishes no default. The Gemara asks: *hagufei kashya* — is this not self-contradictory? The statement *muchzak lepasul pasul* implies that absent that knowledge the person is *kasher*, making *kasher* the default; yet *muchzak lekasher kasher* implies the opposite. Rav Huna bar Tachalifa resolves this in the name of Rav: *kan lehasi'o isha kan lehotzi isha miyado* — one clause speaks to permitting him to marry, and the other speaks to compelling a divorce after the fact. Before marriage to someone of distinguished lineage, a man from Eretz Yisrael must establish his *yichus*; but once married, only demonstrated *psul* can undo it. Rashi and Tosafot both connect this to the specific case of marrying into a family of elevated status, not to ordinary marriages — a normal person is not required to prove his lineage before marrying.

Rav Yosef adds: *kol sheyichaso beVavel masi'in lo isha* — anyone whose lineage identifies him as Babylonian, one may marry him off to a woman of good *yichus*. However, *ve'idna de'ika rama'ei chaishinan* — now that there are deceivers who can learn to speak Babylonian even without having Babylonian origins, we are suspicious and this linguistic marker can no longer be relied upon.

The Gemara now relates the story of Ze'iri, who persistently avoided Rabbi Yochanan — because Rabbi Yochanan wished to give him his daughter in marriage. Rabbi Yochanan was from Eretz Yisrael; Ze'iri was from Bavel. One day, they were traveling together and came to a stream. Ze'iri, as the devoted student, carried Rabbi Yochanan on his shoulders through the water. Rabbi Yochanan, frustrated by the persistent evasion, said to him: our Torah of Eretz Yisrael is good enough for you — *oraitin kesheira* — but our daughters are not? What is your reasoning? Are you relying on the *Mishnah* that teaches that ten categories of lineage made the ascent from Bavel with Ezra — *kohanim*, *Levi'im*, *Yisraelim*, and so forth — and inferring therefrom that Bavel is genealogically purer than Eretz Yisrael?

But one cannot infer this from the *Mishnah*, Rabbi Yochanan argues. *Atu Kahanei Levi'ei ve-Yisra'ei kulu siliku?* Just because *psulim* came with Ezra, the *Mishnah* also states that *kohanim*, *levi'im*, and *yisraelim* came with him — but that does not mean they all left Bavel. There are still *kohanim*, *levi'im*, and *yisraelim* in Bavel. And correspondingly, *psulim* also remain in Bavel. You are no better than we are.

The Gemara notes that Rabbi Yochanan had in that moment forgotten the teaching of Rabbi Elazar: *lo ala Ezra miBavel ad she'asah k'soles nekiyah v'alah* — Ezra did not ascend from Bavel until he had made it like refined flour, meaning he specifically removed all the *psulim*, even if he did not remove all the legitimate Israelites.

Ulla came to Pumbedita and visited Rav Yehuda. He observed that Rav Yehuda's son Rabbi Yitzchak was already grown but had not yet married. Rav Yehuda explained: *mi yadana me'heicha yinasiv* — I do not know where to marry him off, because the question of *yichus* is so fraught. Ulla pushed back vigorously: *atu anan mi yad'inan me'heicha kasina?* — Do we even know where we ourselves come from? Perhaps we descend from women who were violated during the *Churban HaBayis*, as the verse in Lamentations attests: *nashim b'Tzion inu b'sulos b'arei Yehuda*. Rav Yehuda responded: but such a union would still produce a *kasher* child halachically. Ulla countered: what about other disqualifying unions that occurred among the first Temple generation — the Gemara cites the verse *ha'shochvim al mitos shen u'sruchim al arsosam*, which Rabbi Abbahu interprets not as a reference to mere indecency but as describing widespread wife-swapping — eating and drinking together, pushing beds together, exchanging wives — *u'masrichim arsoseihem b'shichvas zera she'eina shelahem*, and thereby filling their beds with semen that was not theirs. Given such pervasive adultery in the generation of the first Temple, there are certainly *mamzeirim* embedded in many Jewish family trees. So how does Rav Yehuda know that anyone is pure enough for his son?

### Silence as Yichus

Rav Yehuda acknowledged the point and asked: so what should I do if I still want to find a suitable match? The answer given is: *zil basar shtikusa* — follow the families that are quiet, that do not argue and pick fights. Rashi explains this as based on the premise — whether understood genetically or sociologically — that families of compromised lineage tend toward contentiousness, either as a character trait passed down through their ancestry or as a result of having historically been marginalized by society, cultivating a defensive and argumentative culture.

The people of Eretz Yisrael used a similar method of investigation: *ki matzu bei srei bahadi hadadei* — when two families were in dispute, they would watch to see which one was first willing to be silent, to step back and not press the fight. *Hai meyuchas tefei* — the one more willing to stop fighting has the better *yichus*. What begins as an index of *yichus* becomes, practically speaking, a direct focus on *middos*. The inquiry about lineage has been quietly displaced by an inquiry about character.

Rav states: *shetikutei deBavel hainu yichusa* — the silence of Bavel is itself its *yichus*. The willingness to not fight is not merely a sign of good lineage; it is what the lineage actually means in practice. When challenged that Rav himself went to investigate a certain family, the Gemara clarifies: he did not investigate their genealogical records; he investigated whether they were *shtiki* — non-argumentative. That was his method of verification.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *im ra'ita shenei bnei adam shemitgarim zeh ba'zeh, shemetz pasul yesh be'echad mehen* — if you see two people perpetually feuding, there is a scintilla of invalidity in one of them, *v'ein manichin oto lidabek bechavertah* — and from Heaven, they are made to quarrel so that the families should not intermarry. It is worth noting that this logic works better if the concern is something like a hereditary disease — both families are carriers and should not intermingle — than if the concern is purely about lineage status, where the problem would apply equally to any marriage partner. Nevertheless, the principle stands: contentiousness is treated as a sign, if not a symptom, of underlying genealogical trouble.

### Bavel Bri'ah

Rav Pappa savya said in the name of Rav: *Bavel bri'ah* — Bavel is healthy, meaning its *yichus* is sound. *Meshan mitah* — this neighboring region is dead, meaning virtually all its inhabitants are of suspect lineage — *mamzeirim* and the like. *Madai mi'idi cholah* — Media is sick, meaning mostly *kasher* but with some *psulim* present. *Ve'Eilam hi goseset* — Elam is like one in the final stages of dying, meaning mostly *pasul* with some *kasher* present. The difference between *cholah* and *goseset* is simply probabilistic: most sick people live, while most of those on their deathbed die.

## Geographic Limits

### East/West Limits

The Gemara now turns to the question of the precise geographic boundaries of Bavel — a necessary question given that Bavel has been established as the gold standard of *yichus*. *Ad heichan hi Bavel?* Rav says: until the *Nahar Azak* on the east. Shmuel says: until the *Nahar Yoni*. Rashi identifies this as the discussion of the eastern boundary. The primary territory of Bavel is understood to be bounded by the Tigris and the Euphrates — *bein shenei neharot*, between the two rivers — but the question here is how far the recognized region extends beyond those two rivers in each cardinal direction.

### Northern Limits

Going north along the Tigris: Rav says until *Bagda* and *Avna*. Shmuel says until *Mushchani* — but not including *Mushchani* itself. Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba then says in the name of Shmuel: *Mushchani harei hi keGolah* — Mushchani is like *Golah*. In this context, *Golah* is not exile in the pejorative sense but rather a name for Pumbedita, the city most closely associated with purity of *yichus*. So Mushchani is treated as having the status of Pumbedita — genuine Babylonian *yichus*.

### Euphrates Limits

Going north along the Euphrates: Rav says until *Akra deTulbanki*. Shmuel says until the bridge of *Perat*. Rabbi Yochanan says until the crossing of *Gizma*.

Abaye, and some say Rav Yosef, cursed Rav's position for extending the boundary too far north. One might ask: why curse Rav and not Shmuel, whose position went even farther north? The answer given is that the curse applied to Rav *and certainly* to Shmuel — the curser was criticizing those who extended the valid boundary too far, and Shmuel's more expansive position was included by implication.

Alternatively, there is no contradiction at all: when Shmuel made his ruling, the bridge of *Perat* was actually located further south than its current position. The Persians subsequently moved it northward. *Ha'idna hu de'illyuyei Parsa'ei* — it is only now that the Persians have situated it further north, but Shmuel's original designation referred to the lower location, which was actually less expansive than Rav's.

Abaye asked Rav Yosef: what about the western side of the Euphrates — how far east into that territory does the Bavel zone extend? Rav Yosef understood the question to be about the town of *Biram*, located to the east of the *Perat*. He answered: *meyuchasei dePumbedita miBiram nasvei* — people of distinguished *yichus* from Pumbedita used to marry women from *Biram*. So *Biram* is fully within the acceptable zone.

Rav Papa states: *kemachloket leyuchasin kach machloket legittin* — the same disagreement that governs the parameters of Bavel for purposes of *yuchasin* also governs the parameters of Bavel for *gittin*, specifically regarding whether a messenger delivering a *get* from Bavel to Eretz Yisrael must declare *bifanai nechtam* — it was signed in my presence. Since Bavel was as conversant in *halacha* as Eretz Yisrael, this declaration was unnecessary within those borders.

Rav Yosef disagrees: the debate applies only to *yuchasin*. For *gittin*, everyone agrees the boundary is the second lake of the bridge — *Arva Tinyana deGishra* — a known, specific location.

### Chavil Yama

Rami bar Abba teaches: *Chavil Yama techla deBavel* — *Chavil Yama* is the *techeiles*, the finest quality, of Bavel. And within *Chavil Yama*, the cities of *Shunya* and *Guvya* are the crème de la crème. Ravina adds: even *Tzitzura* is included. A *baraita* corroborates: Chanan ben Pinchas said the same — *Chavil Yama techla deBavel*, and *Shunya, Guvya, and Tzitzura* are the finest within it.

Rav Papa then introduces a contemporary qualification: *ve'ha'idna arevu behu Kutai* — in his time, *Kutim* — Samaritans — had intermingled there, diminishing its *yichus*. The Gemara immediately challenges this: *velo hi* — it is not so. The Gemara explains that a certain individual wanted to marry a woman from that region and they refused him, and he then began raising the claim of Samaritan infiltration. Rashi notes that it is very difficult to attribute to Rav Papa the *motzi la'az* of slandering an entire community out of pique at a personal rejection. Rashi therefore prefers the reading that it was a *Kuti* who sought to marry there and was refused, and the episode led to some concern about Samaritan presence — but not substantiated enough to genuinely compromise the *yichus* of *Chavil Yama*.

### Shot Mishat

The Gemara concludes with the location of *Chavil Yama* itself: *Zu Prat DeBorsi* — it is the region of *Prat DeBorsi*. A man who declared himself to be from *Shot Mishat* prompted Rav Yitzchak Nafcha to clarify: *agra d'amshat Shot Mishat bein hanaharot omedet* — the advantage of *Shot Mishat* is that it stands between the rivers, between the Tigris and the Euphrates, and is therefore classic Bavel with good *yichus*.

The Gemara then asks: what exactly constitutes *bein hanaharot* — between the rivers? Abaye said in the name of Rav Chama bar Ukva, who said in the name of Rabbi Yose in the name of Rabbi Chanina: *bein hanaharot kegolah leyuchsin* — being between the rivers is like *Golah*, like Pumbedita, for purposes of *yuchasin*. Any place between the Tigris and the Euphrates has this elevated status.

Rabbi Yochanan specifies: the region of *bein hanaharot* begins from *Dikra* going northward, and extends until the crossing of *Gizma*. But Abaye adds: *retzu'ah nafka* — there is a strip that extends beyond, reaching further north and including *Shot Mishat* within the zone of *bein hanaharot*. Even though *Shot Mishat* lies north of *Dikra*, the strip extension brings it within the territory of Bavel and grants it the status of *bein hanaharot* for purposes of *yuchasin*.
