# Zevachim 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mixed Asham/Shelamim

### Mishnah Setup

The *mishnah* presents the following scenario: an *asham*, which is by nature a male sacrifice, becomes mixed up with a *shelamim*. The *asham* is male, a *chatas* is female, an *olah* is male, and a *shelamim* may be either male or female — which is precisely why the *shelamim* is the one sacrifice with which any other may become confused. The *Gemara* preceding today's daf had already worked through which combinations of sacrifices could realistically become intermixed, given that certain categories are exclusively male and others exclusively female. But anything can get mixed up with a *shelamim*.

### R. Shimon's Solution

Rabbi Shimon's proposed solution is elegantly practical: slaughter both animals in the north — the required location for an *asham* — and have male *kohanim* eat them. In doing so, whichever animal turns out to be the *asham* is handled correctly, and there is nothing halachically wrong with slaughtering a *shelamim* in the north or having male *kohanim* eat it. As for the blood, since only a *chatas* requires the blood to be applied above, both the *asham* and the *shelamim* have their blood applied in the same location, so no conflict arises there either.

### Rabbis' Objection

The *Chachamim* object that this approach is unacceptable because it invokes the principle of *ain mevi'im kodshim l'beit hapsul* — one does not place sacrifices into a context of invalidity. By treating both animals as an *asham*, the *shelamim* (whichever animal it turns out to be) is now subject to more restrictive conditions than it would otherwise face: it may only be eaten by male *kohanim*, and more significantly, it may only be eaten for one day rather than two. This dramatically increases the likelihood that some of the meat will become *nosar* and require burning. Restricting a *shelamim* in this manner constitutes bringing it into a house of invalidity.

### Redemption Solution

The *Chachamim* offer an alternative. One waits until the animals develop a blemish, at which point they can be redeemed. The owner then designates the proceeds: whichever animal is the *asham*, its value is directed toward this money, which is then used to purchase a proper *asham*; and whichever animal is the *shelamim*, its value is similarly directed to purchase a proper *shelamim*. This process restores clarity about which animal is which. Rabbi Shimon's implicit counter is: do you have a better solution? The *Chachamim* answer that they do — redemption upon the onset of a blemish — but it is understood that if no such solution existed, even the *Chachamim* might concede that Rabbi Shimon's approach would be preferable to simply allowing the animals to go to waste. The debate, then, is specifically about whether one may adopt Rabbi Shimon's approach when an alternative does in fact exist.

## Kodshim L'Beis Hapes (II)

### Shvi'is/Terumah Case

A *tanna* taught a *baraita* before Rav: produce money infused with the sanctity of the sabbatical year (*kdushas shvi'is*) may not be used to purchase *terumah* — *ain lokchim b'dameha trumah mipnei shemima'atin achilasah* — because doing so restricts the time in which the *terumah* may be eaten. The mechanism is this: *shvi'is* produce carries an obligation of *biyur* — when the relevant species of fruit is no longer available in the field, one must eliminate any such produce held at home. This sanctity transfers to money received in exchange for *shvi'is* produce and, in turn, to whatever is purchased with that money. If one buys *terumah* with such money, the *terumah* now acquires *kdushas shvi'is* and may be subject to the *biyur* deadline, thereby limiting the time available for its consumption. This, the *baraita* teaches, constitutes *ain mevi'im kodshim l'beit hapsul*.

It is worth pausing here to note that this principle, though formulated in the context of *kodshim* — sacrificial offerings — is being applied by the *Gemara* equally to non-sacrificial sanctified items such as *terumah* and *ma'aser sheni*. The principle of not placing something holy into a context that diminishes its sanctity is operative across the full range of halachic categories.

### Rava's Position

When the Rabbis heard this *baraita*, they observed to Rava that its author must not follow Rabbi Shimon — since Rabbi Shimon in the *mishnah* permits placing *kodshim* into a context of invalidity and would presumably also permit purchasing *terumah* with *shvi'is* money. Rava pushed back: even Rabbi Shimon, he insisted, would agree that one may not do so in this case. The key distinction is *d'i'avad* — after the fact. When did Rabbi Shimon permit treating a mixed *shelamim* as though it were an *asham*? Because the situation had already arisen — the animals were already mixed, the problem already existed, and Rabbi Shimon was proposing the best available solution to a pre-existing difficulty. That, however, is categorically different from taking perfectly ordinary *shvi'is* money and deliberately purchasing *terumah* with it. There is no pre-existing problem demanding a solution. Rabbi Shimon would never permit one to proactively place something in a diminished halachic state — *lechatchilah lo*. He permits it only when the circumstances already demand it.

## Tavlin Challenge

### Mishna Tavlin

Abaye challenges Rava's position. A later *mishnah* teaches that *kohanim* are permitted to eat sacrificial meat in any manner they choose — *l'ochlan tzloyim shelukim mevushalim* — roasted, double-boiled, or cooked — and they may season it with any spices, whether *tavlei chulin* (non-sacred spices) or even *tavlei terumah* (spices that are *terumah*). This is the ruling of Rabbi Shimon.

### Proof from Spices

Why is this a significant ruling? Because if a *kohen* takes a *terumah* onion and throws it into a pot of *chatas* stew, the *terumah* onion, now mixed with the *kodshim*, can only be eaten for a single day — the time restriction of the *chatas*. Ordinarily, *terumah* has its own eating window, but once combined with the *chatas*, it is constrained to the more restrictive *kodshim* timeline. This is a classic case of bringing *kodshim* into a house of invalidity — the *terumah* is the *kodshim* whose eating time is now being limited — and yet Rabbi Shimon permits it *lechatchilah*. This appears to be a strong proof against Rava's position.

## Tavlin Challenge (II)

### Rava Dismisses

Rava dismisses the challenge. *Tavlin* — vegetables such as onions and garlic used as spices — are only *terumah* on a rabbinic level; their *terumah* status is not biblical. Rava's position is therefore more nuanced than it initially appeared: Rabbi Shimon may well permit proactively limiting something whose sanctity is only *d'Rabbanan*, but he would still not allow it *lechatchilah* when the sanctity being restricted is *d'Oraisa*.

## Kodshim L'Beis Hapes (II)

### Rabbinic vs. Biblical

Rava's qualification, then, is twofold: Rabbi Shimon permits bringing *kodshim* into a context of invalidity only (1) when there is already a pre-existing problem — *bedi'eved* in the sense that the difficult situation has already arisen — or (2) when the sanctity being restricted is merely rabbinic. A proactive restriction of something possessing biblical sanctity Rabbi Shimon would not sanction. One might ask whether the *mishnah* of *ma'aser* supports this distinction. The *Gemara* cites another teaching: *ein lokchim terumah b'kesef ma'aser mipnei shemema'et b'achilasah* — one does not purchase *terumah* with *ma'aser sheni* money because it restricts the ability to eat it. *V'Rabbi Shimon mattir* — and Rabbi Shimon permits it.

Here a dispute between Rashi and Tosafot emerges regarding precisely what is being restricted. Rashi holds that the restriction falls on the *terumah* itself: *terumah* can normally be eaten anywhere, whereas *ma'aser sheni* can only be eaten in Jerusalem — so by purchasing *terumah* with *ma'aser sheni* money, one creates *terumah* that inherits the locational restriction of *ma'aser sheni*, limiting where it may be consumed. Tosafot reads the restriction in the opposite direction: the *ma'aser sheni* itself is being restricted, since *ma'aser sheni* is ordinarily available to anyone, but after it is used to purchase *terumah*, what remains can now only be eaten by *kohanim*. Either way, the restriction imposed is of a biblical character — *ma'aser sheni* and the prohibition against eating it outside Jerusalem are biblical — and yet Rabbi Shimon permits this *lechatchilah*. This appears to be a compelling proof that Rabbi Shimon allows *lechatchilah* limitation of even biblically sanctified items.

Having established that proof, the *Gemara* then pivots: though the point has been demonstrated, there are in fact other *mishnayos* that make the same point, and the subsequent discussion explores why Abaye chose this particular *mishnah* as his proof rather than those alternatives.

## Shvi'is/Terumah Oil

### Rav Yosef's Question

When Abaye later came before Rav Yosef and reported the exchange — how Rabbah had maintained that Rabbi Shimon does not allow it *lechatchilah*, and how Abaye had proven otherwise from the *mishnah* of *ma'aser sheni* — Rav Yosef asked: why did you not challenge Rabbah from this other *mishnah*? The reference is to a *mishnah* in *Shvi'is*: *ein m'vashlin yerek shel Shvi'is b'shemen shel terumah* — one does not cook a *shvi'is* vegetable in *terumah* oil — *shelo yavi'u kodshim l'veit hapsul*, so as not to bring *kodshim* into a house of invalidity. Notably, the very phrase *mevi'im kodshim l'veit hapsul* appears here explicitly, and it is applied to items that are not *korbanot* — demonstrating that the principle extends across all categories of sanctified items. *V'Rabbi Shimon mattir* — Rabbi Shimon permits it. In this case, it is the *terumah* oil whose eating window is being restricted by being cooked together with the *shvi'is* vegetable, and Rabbi Shimon allows this *lechatchilah*.

### Abaye's Answer

Abaye explained why he had not raised this case. He had already challenged Rabbah from the *mishnah* of *tavlin* — the case of putting *terumah* onions into the *chatas* stew — and Rabbah had dismissed it by saying *hanach l'trumas tavlin d'Rabbanan*: vegetable-based *terumah* is only rabbinic, so Rabbi Shimon's leniency in that case tells us nothing about biblical sanctity. The same dismissal would have applied here, Abaye reasoned: the *shvi'is* vegetable is also only rabbinically restricted, so Rabbah would simply have said *hacha nami trumas yerek d'Rabbanan* and moved on.

### Rav Yosef's Rebuttal

Rav Yosef disagreed with this reasoning. The case of the *shvi'is* vegetable and *terumah* oil is not symmetrical in the way Abaye assumed. When you cook them together, what is being restricted? Not the vegetable. The *terumah* oil is what now has its eating window shortened by virtue of being cooked with the *shvi'is* vegetable. And *terumah* oil is explicitly biblical — the Torah itself specifies *dagan tirosh v'yitzhar* (grain, wine, and oil) as the three categories subject to *terumah* obligations. Rabbah's dismissal — "this is only rabbinic" — would have applied to cases where the sanctity being constricted is rabbinic. Here, the sanctity being constricted is that of the *terumah* oil, which is unquestionably biblical. Rabbi Shimon nonetheless permits it *lechatchilah*. If the *mishnah* had been written the other way — *yerek shel terumah b'shemen shel Shvi'is*, a *terumah* vegetable cooked in *shvi'is* oil — then one could indeed say that what is being restricted is only the rabbinic sanctity of the vegetable, and Rabbah's dismissal would hold. But as actually written, the case is a powerful proof that Rabbah could not have deflected.

### Bedi'eved Debate

Abaye then explains why he did not use this case: he had already challenged Rabbah from the *mishnah* of the mixed sacrificial animals — the *asham* and *shelamim* — and Rabbah had answered *d'i'avad*: once the animals are already mixed, Rabbi Shimon is simply finding the best available solution to a pre-existing problem. Abaye feared Rabbah would apply the same *d'i'avad* logic to the carrot-and-oil case: perhaps the carrot was already soaking in the oil before anyone made a decision, and Rabbi Shimon is only permitting it after the fact, not as an initial choice. Rav Yosef rejects this reading. If the scenario in that *mishnah* were one where the carrot and oil were already mixed, then the Rabbis who disagree with Rabbi Shimon would have no leg to stand on — what else could one possibly do with a carrot already soaking in oil? The only reason the Rabbis could meaningfully prohibit it is if it is being addressed as an initial act, *lechatchilah*.

Abaye responds that even so, Rabbah would have said the Rabbis' objection makes sense because there is an alternative: squeeze the oil out of the carrot and salvage it. Rav Yosef counters — via Ravina — that this alternative is not really viable: if you squeeze thoroughly, *nischot tuva*, you destroy the carrot, which is *shvi'is* produce that must not be wasted; if you squeeze lightly, *nischot portza*, oil remains mixed in the carrot. There is no clean way to separate them. Therefore the case cannot be read as *bedi'eved*, and it does stand as proof that Rabbi Shimon allows it *lechatchilah* — and the proof from the *terumah* oil *mishnah* was a strong one that Abaye could indeed have deployed against Rabbah.

## Metzora Case

### Metzora Scenario

A new *mishnah* introduces another scenario that bears on the same question. The case involves a man who may or may not be a *metzora muchlat* — a fully declared *metzora* — who needs to bring the appropriate sacrifice on the eighth day to complete his purification process. The uncertainty might arise from questions about the original declaration by a *kohen*, and it creates a halachic quandary: how can one bring the mandatory sacrifice of a *metzora* when it is unclear whether the obligation even applies? Rabbi Shimon teaches: on the day following the man's seven-day process, he brings his *asham* and his *log* of oil, and makes stipulations.

### Stipulation Process

The stipulation is structured as follows: if this man is truly a *metzora*, then this animal is his *asham* and this is his *log* of oil for the *metzora* ritual. If he is not a *metzora*, then the *asham* is reconstituted as a *shelamim nedavah* — a freewill *shelamim*. One might ask: why not designate the animal as a freewill *olah* rather than a *shelamim*? Because an *olah* is entirely burned on the altar — there would be no meat for the *kohanim* to eat, which is wasteful. By making it a *shelamim*, the meat is consumed, and the *kohanim* benefit. A *chatas* is impossible both because it is female and because it requires a specific sin-obligation as its trigger.

### Ritual Procedure

The animal is slaughtered in the north, as required for an *asham* — and since a *shelamim* may also be slaughtered in the north without any violation, the location creates no conflict. Blood is applied to the man's ear (*matan bahonos*), as required for a *metzora asham*, and if it turns out to be a *shelamim*, no harm is done. *Semicha* is performed — Tosafot notes a discussion about whether *semicha* is required for an *asham* at the biblical level or only rabbinically. *Nesachim* — the wine, flour, and oil libations — are brought with the stipulation: if this is a *shelamim*, these are its *nesachim*; if it is an *asham*, they are brought as an independent voluntary *korban*. The *chazeh v'shok* (breast and thigh) are waved, and the meat is eaten for a day and a night — the time restriction of the *asham*.

### Abaye's Challenge

This *mishnah* is Abaye's next challenge to Rava. Rava had insisted that Rabbi Shimon permits limiting the sanctity of *kodshim* only *bedi'eved* — when a problem already exists. But here, Rabbi Shimon is proactively instructing the man to bring an animal that might be a *shelamim* while treating it with all the restrictions of an *asham*, thereby limiting the time it can be eaten *lechatchilah*. Rava's response is that this is not a *lechatchilah* case in the pure sense. True, the *korban* itself is not in a pre-existing problematic state — it is not mixed up or damaged. But the man himself is in a problematic state: he needs to achieve *taharah* and cannot do so without this procedure. *Tikunei gavra shani* — fixing a person's situation is different. Rava's position is that Rabbi Shimon allows the restriction of sanctity when circumstances — either the state of the *korban* or the state of the person — make it necessary. This does not prove that Rabbi Shimon would permit it as a pure *lechatchilah* where no such necessity exists.

## Oil Problem

### Oil Status Question

Having addressed the *asham*-or-*shelamim* question, the *Gemara* turns to the *log* of oil. The *metzora* ritual requires the *kohen* to pour the oil over his palm, sprinkle some toward the *kodesh*, and apply portions to the *metzora*'s ear, thumb, and big toe, before pouring the remainder on the man's head. If the man truly is a *metzora*, this works seamlessly. But if he is not a *metzora*, what becomes of the oil? One cannot simply leave it unsanctified, because bringing unsanctified oil (*chulin*) into the *azarah* and using it in a quasi-ritual context would violate the prohibition of *chulin ba'azarah*.

### Oil Stipulation

The solution, the *Gemara* proposes, is to sanctify the oil on a condition — *bitnai*. If the man is a *metzora*, the oil is sanctified as *metzora* oil. If he is not, it is sanctified as a voluntary offering — in the manner of a *mincha*-like donation of oil. The halacha permits one to bring oil as an independent voluntary *korban*: a *kmitzah* (handful) is taken and burned on the altar, and the remaining oil is eaten by the *kohanim*, much as with any *mincha*. The owner stipulates accordingly: if this man is a *metzora*, this oil serves his *metzora* ritual; if not, it is a voluntary oil offering to the altar. Either way the oil is sanctified, and the violation of *chulin ba'azarah* is avoided.

### Kmitzah/Refill

The *Gemara* then works out the procedural sequence. Since the oil may serve as a voluntary offering, the *kmitzah* must be performed — a handful is taken for the altar. But taking the *kmitzah* first depletes the *log*, and the *metzora* ritual requires a full *log* of oil. The solution is that one refills it, and the *Gemara* cites a *mishnah* in support: *hasar halog ad shelo yatzak* — if the *log* was diminished before the *kohen* poured it onto his palm, *yimalenu* — he refills it. So the procedure is: take the *kmitzah*, burn it on the altar, refill the *log*, and then proceed with the *metzora* ritual of sprinkling and application. The *Gemara* assumes this order — *kmitzah* and burning first, *metzora* ritual second — though the precise reason for this assumption will become significant in the discussion that follows.

## Remnant Oil Problem

### Remnant Rule

The *Gemara* now identifies a serious obstacle to this procedure. There is a general principle: *kol shemimenu l'ishim harei hu b'val taktiru* — anything of which a portion has been offered on the altar fire, the remainder may not itself be burned on the altar. The paradigm case is a *mincha*: once the *kmitzah* has been burned, the *shiyarayim* (remnant) cannot be returned to the altar fire. This is not merely a practical limitation but a formal prohibition.

### Order Dilemma

The *Gemara* traces out the dilemma. If you perform the *kmitzah*, then carry out the *metzora* sprinkling ritual, and only afterward burn the *kmitzah* on the fire — you have introduced the sprinkling between the *kmitzah* and the burning. This creates a different problem: *shiyarayim shechasru bein kmitzah l'haktarah v'ein maktirin et hakometz aleihem* — the remnant oil diminished between the taking of the *kmitzah* and its burning, and one may not burn the *kometz* when the *shiyarayim* have been compromised between those two steps. So burning the *kmitzah* after the sprinkling is impermissible. The alternative — burning the *kmitzah* first, then performing the *metzora* ritual — runs directly into the rule of *kol shemimenu l'ishim*: once part of the oil has gone to the fire, the remaining oil is the *shiyarayim*, and it cannot be used for another ritual act.

### Rashi's View

Rashi's interpretation of the prohibition here is striking. The verse says *lo saktiru mimenu isheh l'Hashem* — once a portion of a sacrificial item has been offered on the altar, the remainder may not be offered. Rashi reads *b'val taktiru* not merely as a prohibition against physically burning the remnant on the altar fire, but as a prohibition against using the remnant in any *mikdash* ritual act that is analogous to offering on the fire. Specifically, Rashi equates the sprinkling of the oil on the *metzora* and toward the *kodesh* with an act of *haktarah* — an act of bringing something before God in the Temple context. On this reading, the sprinkling is not merely a peripheral ritual act but a form of presentation analogous to burning a *korban* on the fire. Therefore, once the *kmitzah* has been burned, the remaining oil — now the *shiyarayim* — cannot be used for the *metzora* ritual any more than it could be burned again on the altar.

## Fuel/Water Loophole

### Fuel Principle

The solution is offered by Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Shimon, drawing on a principle stated by Rabbi Eliezer: *l'reiach nichoach ee ata ma'aleh, aval ata ma'aleh l'shem eitzim* — there are things one may not burn on the altar as a *korban* offering, but which one may nonetheless place on the altar fire as fuel. When something cannot be offered in the conventional sacrificial sense, it may still be fed to the altar fire as mere wood — as a material that sustains the fire rather than as an act of sacrificial presentation.

### Our Application

Applied to the case of the *metzora* oil: if the man turns out not to be a *metzora*, and the oil has already had its *kmitzah* burned, then the remaining oil cannot be used for the *metzora* ritual in any formal sense — that would violate *kol shemimenu l'ishim*. However, according to Rashi, what one does is declare that if the man is not a *metzora*, the subsequent ritual actions — the sprinkling, the application to the ear and head — are not being performed as *korban* rituals at all. They are performed as if one is simply pouring water on the man. Just as burning something on the altar fire as mere fuel (*eitzim*) does not constitute a sacrificial act and therefore does not violate the relevant prohibitions, so too performing the sprinkling as a non-ritual act — as though it were simply water — circumvents the prohibition of using *shiyarayim* for a *mikdash* rite. The stipulation covers both possibilities: if the man is a *metzora*, the ritual is fully valid; if not, the physical acts are carried out but without sacrificial intent, and no prohibition is violated.

## Fuel/Water Loophole (II)

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

Rashi and Tosafot read the loophole differently, and the difference flows from their divergent understandings of what the underlying problem is.

## Remnant Oil Problem (II)

### Tosafot's View

According to Tosafot, the prohibition of *kol shemimenu l'ishim* is not about using the remnant oil for sprinkling — that is never what the *Gemara* was worried about. Rather, Tosafot understands the problem to be about burning on the altar itself. If the oil has been designated as *metzora* oil — sanctified for a particular ritual role — then burning it on the altar (as the *kmitzah*) is analogous to burning the *shiyarayim* of a *mincha* on the altar, which is forbidden. Just as the remnant of a *mincha* may not be burned because it has its own *korban* function (being eaten by the *kohanim*), so too oil designated for the *metzora* ritual may not be burned on the altar, because it has its own assigned ritual function. The loophole, on Tosafot's reading, is that the burning of the oil in the *kmitzah* is done not as a sacrificial act with respect to *metzora* oil, but as mere fuel — *l'shem eitzim* — thereby removing it from the category of the forbidden. According to Rashi, the loophole addresses the sprinkling: what would otherwise be a prohibited use of *shiyarayim* in a ritual act is reframed as mere water. According to Tosafot, the loophole addresses the burning: what would otherwise be a prohibited burning of ritually designated oil is reframed as mere fuel. Either way, the mechanism of treating a prohibited act as either fuel or water enables the entire dual procedure — *kmitzah* plus *metzora* ritual — to be carried out without violation.
