# Bava Metzia 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lost Get - Simanim

### Rabba's Lost Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִירְכַס לֵיהּ גִּיטָּא בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא. אֲמַר: אִי סִימָנָא – אִית לִי בְּגַוֵּיהּ, אִי טְבִיעוּת עֵינָא – אִית לִי בְּגַוֵּיהּ. אַהְדְּרוּהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ. אֲמַר: לָא יָדַעְנָא אִי מִשּׁוּם סִימָנָא אַהְדְּרוּהּ נִיהֲלִי, וְקָא סָבְרִי סִימָנִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. אִי מִשּׁוּם טְבִיעוּת עֵינָא אַהְדְּרוּהּ נִיהֲלִי, וְדַוְקָא צוּרְבָּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן. אֲבָל אִינִישׁ דְּעָלְמָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana lost a bill of divorce,** which had been given to him to deliver, **in the study hall.** When it was found, **he said: If** they request **a distinguishing mark, I have** one **for it. If** it depends on **visual recognition, I have** methods of recognition **for it. They returned** the bill of divorce **to him. He said** afterward: **I do not know if they returned it to me due to the distinguishing mark** that I supplied, **and they hold** that **distinguishing marks** are used to return lost items **by Torah law,** or **if they returned it to me due to** my **visual recognition, and** it was **specifically** because I am **a Torah scholar,** as Torah scholars are relied upon when they say that they recognize an item, **but an ordinary person** would **not** be relied upon to recognize the item and have it returned to him.

The daf opens at the last line of 18b and continues into 19a. Rabba bar Bar Chana lost a *get* in the *beis medrash*. The Aramaic word *ircas* simply means "lost" — it appears elsewhere in the phrase *kesuvah d'ircasa*, the replacement *kesuvah* written when the original has been lost, in which the scribe records that this document is written in place of the one that went missing.

When Rabba bar Bar Chana sought to recover his *get*, he offered two separate grounds for identification: *amar i simana is li b'gavei i tveias eina is li b'gavei* — either he could provide a *siman*, an identifying mark, or, if preferred, he could simply recognize it by sight. He was prepared to satisfy whichever standard would be required of him.

### Simanim vs. Recognition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: מָצָא גֵּט אִשָּׁה בַּשּׁוּק, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה – יַחְזִיר לְאִשָּׁה. אֵין הַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה – לֹא יַחְזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself** cited above: If one **found a woman's bill of divorce in the marketplace,** in a case **when the husband admits** that he wrote and gave it, the finder **must return** it **to the wife.** If **the husband does not admit** to this, the finder **may neither return** it **to this one,** the husband, **nor to that one,** the wife.

They returned the *get* to him. Rabba bar Bar Chana then says: *lo yadana im mishum simana ahadrunei nihalei* — I do not know whether they returned it to me on account of the *siman*, *v'kasvari simanim d'Oraisa*, because they hold that *simanim* are Biblically valid, or whether they returned it because of *tveias eina*, personal recognition.

This requires some unpacking. The *siman* he had was not a *siman muvhak* — not an unmistakable, distinctive mark such as a hole immediately beside a specific letter. It was an ordinary *siman*, the kind that could describe other documents as well: perhaps the document was slightly wrinkled at the edges or had an unremarkable perforation. For ordinary lost property, such a *siman* suffices to return the object. But here the question is not merely about returning lost property — it is about whether this is the same *get* that may be used to effect a divorce. For that purpose, greater certainty is required, and a non-distinctive *siman* would only be adequate if *simanim* are valid at the Biblical level, not merely as a Rabbinic accommodation.

The alternative possibility is that they returned it based on *tveias eina* — the capacity to recognize an object by sight without being able to articulate a specific mark. Most people can look at something and say "yes, that is mine," even without explaining why. The concern with relying on this faculty is that one cannot verify it from the outside; a person might claim recognition simply because they want the object. Accordingly, the Gemara notes, *v'davka tzurba m'rabanan* — recognition by sight would only be accepted from a *talmid chacham*, someone whose honesty can be relied upon. It would not be used to return an item to an ordinary person who might be fabricating the claim.

## Get Dating Concern

### Braita on Get Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה מִיהַת יַחְזִיר לָאִשָּׁה. וְלֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא כָּתַב לִיתֵּן בְּנִיסָן וְלֹא נָתַן לָהּ עַד תִּשְׁרֵי, וַאֲזַל בַּעַל זַבֵּין פֵּירֵי מִנִּיסָן וְעַד תִּשְׁרֵי, וּמַפְּקָא לְגִיטָּא דִּכְתִיב בְּנִיסָן, וְאָתְיָא לְמִטְרַף לָקוֹחוֹת שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** the *baraita* states that **when the husband admits** that he wrote and gave it, the finder **must return** it **to the wife.** The Gemara challenges: **But let us suspect** that **perhaps he wrote** the bill of divorce intending **to give** it **in Nisan, but did not give** it **to her until Tishrei, and** the **husband went** and **sold** the **produce** of his wife's property in the interim, **between Nisan and Tishrei,** since the divorce had not yet taken effect. **And** the wife might then **produce the bill of divorce, which he wrote in Nisan, and come to repossess** the produce from the **purchasers unlawfully.**

The Gemara now returns, as a *gufa*, to the *braita* in *Gittin* that was cited earlier. *Matza get isha bashuk* — if one finds a woman's *get* in the marketplace: *bazman shehaba modeh yachzir l'isha* — when the husband admits that it fell from the wife, one returns it to her. *Ein habaal modeh lo yachzir lo lazeh v'lo lazeh* — but if he does not admit this, if he says he had not yet given it to her, one returns it to neither party.

### Nisan-Tishrei Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּתַן עֵינָיו לְגָרְשָׁהּ שׁוּב אֵין לַבַּעַל פֵּירוֹת – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יֵשׁ לַבַּעַל פֵּירוֹת עַד שְׁעַת נְתִינָה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **This works out well according to the one who says** that **once he has decided to divorce her, the husband no longer has** the rights to his wife's **produce.** Since the husband had no right to sell the produce, the wife repossessed it rightfully. **But according to the one who says** that **the husband has** rights to his wife's **produce until the** actual **time of giving** the bill of divorce, **what is there to say?**

The Gemara now raises a concern about the first case. Even when the husband admits, why do we return it? *V'leichosh shema katav litten b'Nisan v'lo natan ad Tishrei* — let us be concerned that he wrote the *get* in Nisan but did not actually give it until Tishrei. The context here is the husband's rights to the *peirot* — the usufruct, the fruits of the landed property his wife brought into the marriage. As long as they remain married, he retains the right to benefit from that property, though she holds title. Those rights do not expire until the divorce actually takes effect.

### Wife's Claim on Fruit

Between Nisan and Tishrei, suppose the husband sold a harvest — a thousand boxes of cherries, say — which he was fully entitled to sell at the time, since the divorce had not yet occurred. Now the wife possesses a *get* dated Nisan. *U'mapka l'gita d'kativ b'Nisan* — she produces this *get* and claims she has been divorced since Nisan. She then goes to the purchaser of the cherries and argues: my husband sold you produce that was not his to sell, since I was already divorced from Nisan — those cherries were mine. *V'atya l'mitraf lakuchos shelo kadin* — she will attempt to seize property from the purchasers without just cause.

### Loses Rights on Intention

The Gemara notes that this concern is resolved according to one opinion. *Hanei l'man d'amar kivan she'natan einav l'gareshah shuv ein lo baal peirot shapir* — according to the view that from the moment the husband has decided and resolved to divorce his wife, he immediately loses the right to the *peirot* even before the divorce actually takes place. Since he wrote the *get* in Nisan, his intention was clear from that moment, and he therefore had no right to sell the fruit from Nisan onward — so the wife's claim would in fact be legitimate and no injustice would result.

### Rights Until Divorce

*Ella le-man d'amar yesh la-ba'al peiros ad sha'as nesina* — but according to the position that the husband retains rights to the *peirot* until the moment of actual divorce, *mai ika le-meimar* — what can one say? Why are we not concerned that the *get* was written earlier than it was given, and why do we not hesitate to return it to the woman?

### Demand Proof of Receipt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אָתְיָא לְמִטְרַף אָמְרִינַן לַהּ: אַיְיתַי רְאָיָה אֵימַת מְטָא גִּיטָּא לְיָדְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When she comes to repossess** the produce, **we say to her:** First **bring proof** as to **when the bill of divorce came into your possession,** and then we will allow you to repossess the sold produce.

The Gemara resolves this: *amarna ki asya le-mitraf amrinan lah aisi raya eimas mata gitta l'yadech* — when she comes to claim the cherries from the purchaser, we will say to her: bring proof of exactly when you actually received the *get*. The date inscribed says Nisan, but prove that you received it in Nisan and not later. Ordinarily a woman holding a *get* is presumed to have been divorced as of the date written in the document, and no such demand is made. But since this was a *get* that had been found and returned to her, people will remember this — they will recall that the document was not originally in her possession but was recovered and given back to her — and they will know to demand that she establish the actual date of receipt. This assumption is admittedly striking: how exactly everyone is supposed to recall that this particular *get* had been found and returned is unclear, and one might speculate that the *beis din* would place some notation on the document to that effect. Nevertheless, this is how the Gemara frames the solution.

## Gittin vs. Debt Docs

### Why Different Rules?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִשְּׁטָרֵי חוֹב? דִּתְנַן: מָצָא שְׁטָרֵי חוֹב, אִם יֵשׁ בָּהֶן אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – לֹא יַחְזִיר. וְאוּקֵימְנָא כְּשֶׁחַיָּיב מוֹדֶה, וּמִשּׁוּם שֶׁמָּא כָּתַב לִלְווֹת בְּנִיסָן וְלֹא לָוָה עַד תִּשְׁרֵי וְקָא טָרֵיף לָקוֹחוֹת שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** in **what** way **is** it **different from promissory notes? As we learned** in a mishna (12b): With regard to one who **found promissory notes, if they include a property guarantee** for the loan, **he may not return** them to the creditor. **And we interpreted** the mishna as referring to a case **where the liable party admits** that he has not yet repaid the debt, **and** the reason the promissory note cannot be returned is **due to** the possibility that **perhaps he wrote** it intending **to borrow** money **in Nisan, but** ultimately **did not borrow** it **until Tishrei, and** the creditor might therefore use the promissory note to **unlawfully repossess** property that the debtor sold between Nisan and Tishrei from the **purchasers.**

The Gemara now asks: if this is a viable solution — return the document and ensure that any monetary claim will be met with a demand for proof — then why does the same approach not apply to documents of debt? *U-mai shna shtar chov* — why should a *shtar chov* be any different? The Mishna taught, as was studied earlier, *matza shtarei chov im yesh bahen achrayus nechasim lo yachzir* — if one finds debt documents that carry liens on property, one does not return them.

### Debt Doc Concern

This ruling was established even in the case where the debtor himself admits that the document belongs to the creditor. The reason is the concern of *shema kasav lilvos b'Nissan v'lo lova ad Tishrei* — perhaps the document was written in anticipation of a loan in Nisan but the money was not actually lent until Tishrei. If the creditor now uses this document, it will appear as though his liens on property take effect from Nisan, when in reality they can only begin from Tishrei when the loan was actually extended. *V'katraf delo kedin* — he will seize property sold by the debtor between Nisan and Tishrei, to which he has no rightful lien.

### Proof for Debt Docs?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי לִיהְדַּר, וְכִי אָתֵי לְמִטְרַף נֵימָא לֵיהּ: אַיְיתִי רְאָיָה אֵימַת מְטָא שְׁטַר חוֹב לְיָדָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** According to the Gemara's suggestion with regard to a bill of divorce, **there,** in the case of a promissory note, it **should also be returned, and when** the creditor **comes to repossess** the debtor's property that was sold in the interim, **let** the court **say to him:** First **bring proof** as to **when** the **promissory note came into your possession.**

The Gemara raises the question symmetrically: *amri hatam nami l'yahadar* — let us return the *shtar chov* there as well, *v'chi asya l'mitraf amrinan lei aisi raya eimas mata shtar chov l'yadech* — and when the creditor comes to exercise his liens, we will say to him: prove when the money was actually lent. If we can impose this demand in the case of the *get*, we can impose it equally in the case of the debt document. Why should there be any difference?

### Get: Remembered Finding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי, הָכָא גַּבֵּי גֵּט אִשָּׁה אָתֵי לוֹקֵחַ וְתָבַעה, אָמַר: הַאי דְּ[אַ]הְדְּרוּהּ נִיהֲלַהּ רַבָּנַן לְגִיטָּא, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא תִּעֲגִין וְתֵיתִיב. הַשְׁתָּא דְּקָא אָתְיָא לְמִטְרַף – תֵּיזִל וְתַיְתֵי רְאָיָה אֵימַת מְטָא גִּיטָּא לִידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** that it is not comparable. **Here, with regard to a woman's bill of divorce,** the **purchaser** will **come and demand** that the wife prove when it was given to her, as he will **say** to himself: The fact **that the Sages returned the bill of divorce to her** was only **so that she would not dwell** alone as **a deserted wife** and not be able to remarry for lack of a bill of divorce. **Now that she is coming to repossess** the property her husband sold me, **she should go and bring proof** as to **when the bill of divorce came into her possession.**

The Gemara answers: *amri hacha gabei get isha asi lakuach v'sava* — here, in the case of the *get*, the purchaser who bought the cherries will come and make demands. He will remember that the *get* was a document that had been lost and returned, and on the strength of that knowledge he will know to challenge the wife's claim. The *get* was returned to her specifically so she could remarry — not with any view toward the question of who owns the fruit. Therefore, *hashata de'kasya le'mitraf teiteiv ve'teisi raya eimat mata gita leyada* — now that she attempts to use the *get* to establish a date of divorce for the purpose of claiming the fruit, let her sit and bring proof of exactly when she received it. The purchaser, knowing the background, will know to demand this.

### Debt: Assumed Kosher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא גַּבֵּי שְׁטַר חוֹב לָא אָתֵי לוֹקֵחַ וְתָבַע, [אָמַר] מִדְּאַהְדְּרוּהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לִשְׁטַר חוֹב, פְּשִׁיטָא לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא אַהְדְּרוּהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ – לְמִטְרַף הוּא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קָמוּ רַבָּנַן בְּמִילְּתָא, וּמִקַּמֵּי דִּידִי מְטָא שְׁטָרָא לִידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **here, with regard to a promissory note, the purchaser will not come and demand** proof, because he will infer **from** the fact **that the Sages returned** the **promissory note to him** that it is **obviously** valid from the date written in it. After all, **for what *halakha* did** the court **return it to him? It** was clearly in order **to repossess** property with it. Therefore, he will **conclude from it: The Sages clarified the matter and** determined that, in fact, this promissory **note came into the possession of** the creditor **prior to my** purchase of property from the debtor.

By contrast, *lo ati lokeach u'tava* — in the case of a *shtar chov*, the purchaser of the debtor's land will not come and challenge. Why? *Mide'ahadru nihaleih rabbanan ve'shtar chov peshita* — because when a debt document was found and the rabbis returned it to the creditor, the purchaser will reason: what other possible purpose could there be for returning a *shtar chov* to a creditor other than to allow him to collect the loan and exercise the liens? *Le'mai hachi ahadru nihaleih le'mitraf hu shma mina kamu rabbanan bemilta* — since the document was clearly returned for the purpose of enforcement, the purchaser concludes that the rabbis must have investigated and determined that everything was in order, that the loan genuinely dates to Nisan, and that the liens are fully valid. *Umikmi didi mata shtra le'yadei* — he assumes the document was verified before being returned. He will not challenge it, and the creditor will unjustly seize property to which he has no rightful lien.

This is why the *shtar chov* cannot be returned, while the *get* can. When a *get* is produced to claim ownership of fruit, people will still remember to challenge the dating because everyone understands the *get* was returned for marital purposes, not for monetary ones. When a *shtar chov* is returned, however, its return carries the implicit message that the liens are valid and the creditor has performed due diligence — and no one will think to question it further.

## Manumission Logic

### Master Admits/Denies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מָצָא שְׁטַר שִׁחְרוּר בְּשׁוּק, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָרַב מוֹדֶה – יַחְזִיר לָעֶבֶד, אֵין הָרַב מוֹדֶה – לֹא יַחְזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Bills of **manumission of slaves** that are found should not to be returned. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one **found a bill of manumission in the marketplace,** in a case **when the master admits** that he gave the bill to the slave, **one should return** it **to the slave.** If **the master does not admit** to it, **one should neither return** it **to this** person, the master, **nor to that** person, the slave.

The Mishna stated that a *shtar shichrur* — a writ of manumission — found in the marketplace should not be returned to the slave, since perhaps the master wrote it but had not yet given it and may have changed his mind. A *braita* elaborates: *matza shtar shichrur bashuk bizman she'harav modeh yachzir le'eved* — when the master admits that the document fell from the slave, or acknowledges that it should be given to the slave, it is returned to him. *Eino harav modeh lo yachzir lo le'zeh velo le'zeh* — if the master does not admit this, it is returned to neither party.

### Slave's Property Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָרַב מוֹדֶה מִיהַת יַחְזִיר לָעֶבֶד, וְאַמַּאי? נֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא כָּתַב לִיתֵּן לוֹ בְּנִיסָן וְלֹא נָתַן לוֹ עַד תִּשְׁרֵי, וַאֲזַל עַבְדָּא וּקְנָה נִכְסִין מִנִּיסָן וְעַד תִּשְׁרֵי, וַאֲזַיל הָרַב וְזַבְּנִינְהוּ וּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ לְשִׁחְרוּר דִּכְתִב בְּנִיסָן, וְקָא טָרֵיף לָקוֹחוֹת שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In any event,** the *baraita* states that **when the master admits** that he gave the bill of manumission to the slave, the one who found it **should return** it **to the slave. But why** should he return it? **Let us suspect** that **perhaps he wrote** the bill of manumission intending **to give** it **to him in Nisan, but** he **did not give** it **to him until Tishrei, and the slave went and bought property** in the interim, **between Nisan and Tishrei,** at which time he was still a slave, in which case the property belongs to his master, **and the master** then **went and sold** that property. **And** if the bill of manumission is returned to the slave, he might **produce** the bill of **manumission, which** his master **wrote in Nisan,** in order to claim that the property was not his master's to sell, **and repossess** the property from the **purchasers unlawfully.**

The Gemara raises the same Nisan-Tishrei concern here. Even when the master admits, why do we return the document? *Neichash shema katav litein lo be'Nisan velo natan lo ad Tishrei* — let us be concerned that the document was written in Nisan but not actually given until Tishrei. *Ve'azal avda ve'kanah nechasim mi'Nisan ad Tishrei* — during that interval, the slave went and purchased property. Whatever a slave acquires belongs to his master; the slave has no independent legal personhood in property terms.

### Retroactive Freedom Claim

*Ve'azal harav ve'zabneinhu* — the master, who owns these acquisitions, went ahead and sold the property that the slave had purchased between Nisan and Tishrei. Then the slave receives the manumission document. Though he was actually freed in Tishrei, the document is dated Nisan. *Umapik leih le'shichrura de'kativ be'Nisan ve'taraf lakochei shelo kadin* — the slave now produces this document dated Nisan and approaches those to whom the master sold the property, claiming that he was already free in Nisan, that the purchased goods were therefore his and not the master's, and that the master had no right to sell them.

### Freedom as Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר זְכוּת הוּא לָעֶבֶד שֶׁיּוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת רַבּוֹ לְחֵירוּת, וּכְאַבָּיֵי דְּאָמַר: עֵדָיו בַּחֲתוּמָיו זָכִין לֵיהּ, שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חוֹב הוּא לָעֶבֶד שֶׁיּוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת רַבּוֹ לְחֵירוּת, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **This works out well according to the one who says** that **it is in a slave's interest to leave his master's authority and attain freedom and in accordance with** the opinion of **Abaye, who says** that when a document serves the interests of its intended recipient, **its witnesses, with their signatures, acquire** it **on his behalf.** Accordingly, a slave attains freedom at the moment his bill of manumission is signed, even if it is given to him at a later date. Therefore, the *halakha* in the *baraita* works out **well. But according to the one who says** that **it is against a slave's interests to leave his master's authority and attain freedom, what is there to say?**

The Gemara notes that this concern dissolves according to one position. *Hani l'man d'amar zchus l'eved sheyotzei mitachas yad rabo l'cheirus* — according to the view that being freed from one's master is an unqualified benefit to the slave, and following the ruling of Abaye that *edei bachasumav zachin lo* — that when witnesses sign a document that confers a pure benefit upon someone, their very act of signing is as though they have delivered the document to him on his behalf — the principle of *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* applies. The slave is freed from the moment the *shtar shichrur* is signed, and there would never be a gap between Nisan and Tishrei to worry about.

### Not Absolute Benefit

*Ella l'man d'amar chov l'eved sheyotzei mitachas yad rabo l'cheirus* — but according to the view that being freed is not an unqualified benefit to the slave — it need not mean it is an absolute detriment, only that it carries some downside: the slave loses his master's household, his board, and certain leniencies in other areas of conduct — then *zachin* cannot be applied, and the slave is not freed until the document is physically delivered to him. In that case, the gap between Nisan and Tishrei is real, and *mai ika l'meimar* — what can one say to justify returning the document?

### Proof of Freedom Date

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכִי אָתֵי לְמִטְרַף אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: אַיְיתִי רְאָיָה אֵימַת מְטָא שִׁחְרוּר לְיָדָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers **that when** the slave **comes to repossess** the property, **we say to him: Bring proof** as to **when** the bill of **manumission reached your possession** and you were freed.

*Dechi asi l'metraf amrinan lei aisei raya eimas mta shtar shichrur leyadacha* — the Gemara answers precisely as it did with the *get*: when the slave comes to use the writ of manumission to claim property, we will remember that it was a document found and returned to him, and we will know to demand proof of exactly when he was actually freed. The same logic applies: the *shtar shichrur* was returned so that the slave could establish his freedom, not for the purpose of monetary claims. If he then attempts to use its date to reclaim property, he will be required to demonstrate when the document actually reached his hands. And just as with the *shtar chov*, the Gemara would not extend this solution to a debt document, because returning a *shtar chov* carries the implicit endorsement that the liens are valid — an endorsement that a purchaser will not think to question.

## Will & Gift Docs

### Basic Rule on Docs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּיָיתֵיקֵי מַתָּנָה וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזוֹ הִיא דְּיָיתֵיקֵי – דָּא תְּהֵא לְמֵיקַם וְלִהְיוֹת שֶׁאִם מֵת נְכָסָיו לִפְלוֹנִי. מַתָּנָה – כֹּל שֶׁכָּתוּב בּוֹ ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If one found **wills [*deyaytiki*]** or deeds of **gift,** he should not return them. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **What is** considered **a *deyaytiki*** and is collected by the designated recipient after the death of the giver? It is a deed that states: **This deed will be to stand [*da tehe lemeikam*] and exist as** proof that **if** this person **dies, his property** is to be given **to so-and-so.** An ordinary deed of **gift,** by contrast, is **any** deed **in which it is written:** This gift is given **from today and after** the **death** of the giver.

The Mishna and an associated *braita* both address the case of found documents relating to bequests and gifts — *dayatiki*, *apotkiyos*, and *matana* — and establish that they are not returned to the recipient without explicit instruction from the giver.

### What Is Dayatiki

The *braita* asks: *eizeihu dayatiki* — what is a *dayatiki*? *Da tehei l'meikam u'l'mihvei* — it is an acronym for "this shall stand and take effect," meaning it functions as a will. It records the instructions of a *shechiv meira* — a person on his deathbed — directing that upon his death his property should pass to a designated person. The principle underlying it is *divrei shechiv meira k'chusurin v'chimsurin dami* — the words of a dying person are treated as if they have been written and delivered, meaning that his verbal instruction alone suffices to effect the transfer. It takes effect automatically at death, without requiring a formal act of conveyance.

### What Is Matana

*Matana* — a gift by a healthy person — is defined as any document that says *me-hayom ve-le-achar mita*: the recipient acquires title today, but the right to benefit from the property begins only after the giver's death. The giver of such a document retains the right to the *peirot* throughout his lifetime; ownership, however, vests in the recipient immediately.

### Title/Benefit Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא אִי כְּתִיבָה ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״ הוּא דְּקָנֵי, וְאִי לָא – לָא קָנֵי?! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵיזוֹ הִיא מַתְּנַת בָּרִיא שֶׁהִיא כְּמַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע, דְּלָא קָנֵי אֶלָּא לְאַחַר מִיתָה, כֹּל שֶׁכָּתוּב בָּהּ ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Apparently, only if it is written** in the deed: **From today and after** the **death** of the giver, the recipient **acquires** the gift, **and otherwise, he does not acquire** the gift. Is there no deed of gift that is effective even without the clause: And after my death? **Abaye said** that **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: What** deed of **gift of a healthy person is** considered **like** the deed of **gift of a person on his deathbed,** in **that** the recipient **acquires** it **only after** the **death** of the giver? It is **any** deed **in which it is written:** This gift is given **from today and after** the giver's **death.**

The Gemara asks why a gift must be phrased in this bifurcated way — title now, benefit after death — and whether it would not be simpler for a healthy person simply to give everything outright. Abaye explains: *eizo hi matnat bari she-hi ke-matnat shchiv me-ra* — the question is, what form does a healthy person's gift take when he wishes it to function like a deathbed bequest, i.e., when he wants the recipient to derive benefit only after his death?

The answer is that a healthy person cannot simply say "you receive this property after I die," because at the moment of his death he is no longer a legal entity capable of effecting a transfer. The conveyance cannot be completed by a person who no longer exists. Therefore, if a healthy person wishes to give something that will only become beneficial to the recipient upon his death, he has two options: he can stipulate that the transfer takes effect a moment before his death, or he can transfer title now while retaining the right to all benefit — *peirot* — until he dies. The formula *me-hayom ve-le-achar mita* accomplishes exactly this: title passes today, benefit passes at death. This is what distinguishes the gift of a healthy person from a standard deathbed bequest, which operates entirely through the Rabbinic mechanism of *divrei shechiv meira*.

### Braita Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּלָא אָמַר ״תְּנוּ״. הָא אָמַר ״תְּנוּ״ – נוֹתְנִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that these documents may not be returned to the one who is presumed to have lost them, as perhaps the one who wrote them reconsidered and decided not to deliver them. The Gemara infers: **The reason** that these deeds may not be returned is **that** the one who wrote them **doesn't say** to the finder: **Give** them to their intended recipient. **But** if **he says: Give** them, the finder **must give** them.

A *braita* then states: *matza deitiki apotkiyos u-matanot af al pi she-shneihem modim lo yachazir lo le-ze velo le-ze* — if one finds a *dayatiki*, an *apotiki*, or a gift document, even if both parties admit to its validity, one does not return it to either side.

## Deathbed vs. Healthy

### Retractable Nature

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: מָצָא דְּיָיתֵקָאוֹת, אַפּוֹתֵיקָאוֹת וּמַתָּנוֹת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם מוֹדִין – לֹא יַחְזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה! אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא בְּבָרִיא, וְהָא בִּשְׁכִיב מְרַע.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** to that inference from a *baraita* that states that if one **found wills,** or deeds of **designated repayment, or** deeds of **gift, even if both** the one who wrote the deed and its intended recipient **agree** that it is valid, **he should return** it **neither to this** person **nor to that** person. **Rabbi Abba bar Memel said:** This is **not difficult.** **This** *halakha* applies **in** a case of a gift given by **a healthy person, and that** *halakha* applies **in** a case of a gift given by **a person on his deathbed.**

The Gemara now reconciles the apparent tension between the Mishna — which implies that if the *shechiv meira* says "give it," one gives it — and the *braita*, which says that even when both parties admit, one does not return these documents. The resolution turns on a fundamental distinction between the two categories of document.

A deathbed bequest — *matnat shchiv mera* — is *bar hadara*, retractable. Until the person actually dies, his most recent expressed wish always supersedes any prior one. This is the nature of a will: each subsequent instruction invalidates what came before. Accordingly, if a *shechiv meira* on his deathbed gives instructions that property should go to Reuven, and then later gives instructions that it should go to Shimon, Shimon receives the property because the later instruction overrides the earlier one. If he then changes his mind yet again in favor of Reuven, Reuven will receive it. Whatever he says last is what governs.

### Later Supersedes Earlier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי: הָא אָמַר תְּנוּ נוֹתְנִין – בִּשְׁכִיב מְרַע, דְּבַר מִהְדַּר הוּא. דְּאָמְרִינַן, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: דִּלְמָא כַּתְבַהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא לְהַאי וְאִמְּלִיךְ וְלָא יַהֲבַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, וַהֲדַר כַּתְבַהּ לְאִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא וְיַהֲבַיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ – הַשְׁתָּה קָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ מֵהָהוּא דְּיַהֲבַיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ. אִי בְּמַתְּנַת בָּרִיא יַהֲבַיהּ לֵיהּ – לֵית לֵיהּ פְּסֵידָא, דְּכִי נָפְקָא תַּרְתֵּי, בָּתְרָיְיתָא זָכֵי, דְּהָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ מִקַּמָּיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The mishna that teaches that** if the giver **says: Give** it to its intended recipient, the finder **must give** it, applies **in** a case of a gift given by **a person on his deathbed, who is capable of retracting** his gift. Therefore, the finder must give the deed to the recipient, **as we say: What is there to say** as a reason for not returning the deed? One might suggest that **perhaps** the giver **initially wrote** a deed of gift **for this** person, **but** then **reconsidered and did not give it to him, and then he wrote** a second deed of gift **for another person and** thereby **gave** his property **to him.** And **now** that his first deed was found, he wishes to **retract** his gift to **that** second person **to whom he gave** the property. This attempt to retract his latter gift will not succeed. **If he gave** his property **to** the recipient of the second deed of gift **as the gift of a healthy person,** then the second recipient **incurs no loss** by the first deed being given to its intended recipient. This is **because, when the two** deeds **are produced** in court, the recipient of **the later** one **acquires** the property, **as** the owner evidently **retracted the first** gift. Since one who gave away his property while on his deathbed can subsequently retract his gift, the second recipient acquires the property.

This principle is stated explicitly: *de-amrinan mai ika le-meimar* — what precisely is the concern? The concern is that perhaps originally he wrote and expressed his deathbed bequest for Reuven in January, then had regrets and never delivered the document to Reuven, then subsequently in February gave his deathbed bequest to Shimon and actually delivered that document. Now he regrets having given it to Shimon and wants it to go to Reuven.

The Gemara answers: whether he gave to Shimon as a deathbed bequest or as a healthy person's gift, Shimon loses nothing — *leis leih pesida de-kama batar batar zachi* — because the later document always prevails, as by giving to Shimon he effectively retracted from Reuven. Therefore, when the *shechiv meira* is alive, we can safely give the found document to Reuven without fear of injustice to Shimon.

### Gift Is Final

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בְּמַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע נָמֵי יַהֲבַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, לֵית בַּהּ פְּסֵידָא – דְּבָתְרָיְיתָא זָכֵי, דְּקָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ מִקַּמָּיְיתָא. כִּי קָתָנֵי בְּבָרַיְיתָא: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם מוֹדִים לֹא יַחְזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה – בְּבָרִיא, דְּלָאו בַּר מִהְדַּר הוּא,
>
> **Translation:** **So too, if he gave it to** the second person **as the gift of a person on his deathbed, he incurs no loss.** This is **because** the recipient of **the later** deed **acquires** the property, **as** the giver evidently **retracted** his gift to **the first** recipient. And **when the *baraita* teaches** that **even if both** the one who wrote the deed and its intended recipient **agree** that it is valid, the one who found it **should neither return** it **to this** person **nor to that** person, it is referring **to** the case of a gift given by **a healthy person, who is not able to retract** his gift.

A healthy person's gift, by contrast, is final and irrevocable — *dela bar mehadar hu*. It is not superseded by a later gift; rather, the earlier gift takes precedence. If a healthy person gave title to Reuven in January and then purported to give the same property to Shimon in February, Reuven owns it — the January document controls. The later conveyance cannot override the earlier completed transfer.

### Two-Gift Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמְרִינַן: דִּלְמָא כַּתְבַהּ לְהַאי מֵעִיקָּרָא וְאִמְּלִיךְ וְלָא יַהֲבַהּ לֵיהּ, וַהֲדַר כַּתְבַהּ לְאִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא וְיַהֲבַהּ לֵיהּ – הַשְׁתָּה קָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ מֵהָהוּא דְּיַהֲבַהּ לֵיהּ וְסָבַר: מִהְדָּר לָא מָצֵינָא הָדַרְנָא בִּי, אֵימַר לְהוּ דַּאֲנָא לְהַאי יַהֲבִתַהּ, וְנַיְהְדְּרוּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ כְּתָבָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּכִי מַפֵּיק הַאי כְּתָבָא, דְּקָדֵים זָכֵה בֵּיהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, one may not return the deed, **as we say** that **perhaps** the giver **initially wrote** a deed of gift **for this** person **but** then **reconsidered and did not give it to him, and then he wrote** a second deed of gift **for another person and** thereby **gave** his property **to him;** and **now** he wishes to **retract** his gift to **that** second person **to whom he gave** the property, **thinking:** Since **I cannot retract** the gift legally, **I will say to** the court **that I gave** the first deed of gift **to this** first person, **and they will return the deed** of gift **to him, in order that when he produces this deed** of gift, **which** is dated **earlier, he will** thereby **acquire** the property.

The concern in the case of a healthy person's gift is therefore as follows. In January, a person wrote a gift document in favor of Reuven but never gave it to him. In February, he gave a gift document to Shimon and actually delivered it. Now he has regrets and wishes he had given it to Reuven instead. The January document, which was written but never given, is now found.

### False Claim Risk

The danger is this: the giver, in collusion with Reuven, claims that he actually did give the January document to Reuven at the time it was written. If the document is returned to Reuven and Reuven produces it bearing a January date, Reuven's document predates Shimon's February document. Since the earlier gift takes precedence for a healthy person's gift, Reuven would win — unjustly depriving Shimon of property he had legitimately received.

### Exploit Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ אֲנַן: הַאי כְּתָבָא לָא יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ לְהַאי. דִּלְמָא מִכְתָּב כְּתַבְתְּ, מֵיהָב לָא יְהַבְתְּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, וִיהַבְתַּהּ לְאִינִישׁ אַחְרִינָא וְקָא הָדְרַתְּ בֵּיהּ. אִי לָא יְהַבְתַּהּ לְאִינִישׁ אַחְרִינָא וְקָא בָּעֵית דְּתִתְּבַהּ לְהַאי, כְּתֹב לֵיהּ הַשְׁתָּא כְּתָבָא אַחֲרִינָא וְיַהֲבֵיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, דְּאִי יָהֲבַתְּ לְאִינִישׁ אַחְרִינָא לֵית בַּהּ פְּסֵידָא, דְּקָדֵים זָכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, we say to** the giver: **We will not give this document to this** person, as **perhaps you wrote** it but **did not give it to him, and** then **you gave** the property **to another person, and you** now wish to **retract** your gift to **him** unlawfully. Therefore, **if** in fact **you did not give** this property as a gift **to another person, and you wish to return it to this** person, then do the following: **Write another deed** of gift **for him now and give it to him,** so **that if you did** previously **give** the property **to another person,** he will **incur no loss, as the earlier** recipient **acquires** the gift.

*Kasavar ki mafik hai ktava dekadim* — the giver calculates that if Reuven produces this document dated January, it will establish that he received the gift before Shimon did, *zachi beh hu*, and they will split the proceeds. This fraudulent scheme is precisely what the Gemara fears. The Gemara therefore says: we will not give this document to Reuven. If the giver genuinely wants Reuven to have the property and there truly is no Shimon in the picture, let him write a new document now and give it to Reuven today. *Dei yahavtih leinish achrina leika pseida de-kama zachi* — because if there is in fact a Shimon who already received an earlier gift, the earlier document will prevail and Shimon will be protected. This is why, for a healthy person's gift, even if both parties admit, the found document is not returned.

## Alive vs. Dead Giver

### Apparent Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב זְבִיד: וְהָא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דְּיָיתֵקָאוֹת קָא תָנֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: הָא וְהָא בִּשְׁכִיב מְרַע, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בֵּיהּ, וְהָא בִּבְרֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid objects to this** distinction between the mishna and *baraita*, asking: **But don't this** mishna **and that** *baraita* both **teach** *halakhot* with regard to **wills?** How can Rabbi Abba bar Memel explain that the *baraita* is referring to the gift of a healthy person? **Rather, Rav Zevid said** that both **this** mishna **and that** *baraita* are referring **to** the gift of **a person on his deathbed, and** nevertheless, the contradiction between them is **not difficult; this** mishna is referring **to him,** the giver himself, who authorizes the return of the will to its intended recipient, **and that** *baraita* is referring **to** a case where the giver died, and **his son** is the one who is authorizing the return of the will.

Rav Zvid challenges the foregoing resolution. *Idi ve-idi dayateki katani* — both the Mishna and the *braita* speak of a *dayatiki*, a deathbed bequest. Yet the Mishna implies that when the *shechiv meira* says "give it," one gives it, while the *braita* says that even when both parties admit, one does not give it. The previous answer attributed the Mishna to a *shechiv meira* and the *braita* to a healthy person's gift, but Rav Zvid observes that both sources explicitly discuss the same category of document. How can the contradiction be resolved while keeping both within the domain of deathbed bequests?

### Key Difference

*Ela amar Rav Zvid ha ve-ha be-shchiv mera ve-lo kashya ha beh ve-ha bivrei* — Rav Zvid resolves this by introducing a different distinction: both sources indeed deal with a *shechiv meira*'s bequest, but they differ on whether the *shechiv meira* himself is still alive or has already died and it is now his son who is the relevant party.

### Alive: Later Wins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָא אָמַר ״תְּנוּ״ נוֹתְנִין, בְּדִידֵיהּ דְּבַר מִהְדָּר הוּא, דְּאָמְרִינַן: אִי נָמֵי יַהֲבַהּ לְאִינִישׁ אַחְרִינָא לֵית בַּהּ פְּסֵידָא, דְּקַמָּא וּבָתְרָא – בָּתְרָא זָכֵי, דְּהָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ מִקַּמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The mishna, which** indicates that if the giver **says: Give** it to the recipient, the finder must **give** it to him, is referring **to** a case where the giver **himself** authorizes giving the will, **as he is capable of retracting** it. Therefore, there is no harm in giving the will to the recipient, **as we say** that **even if** in the meantime **he** already **gave** the property **to another person,** the latter recipient **incurs no loss.** This is **because** in a case where there are two wills, **a first one and a last one,** the recipient of **the last one acquires** the property, **as** the owner evidently **retracted the first** will.

When the *shechiv meira* is still alive and says "give it," we can safely return the document to Reuven. *De-mahadar hu* — he retains the power to retract. If in fact he never gave the document to Reuven and subsequently gave the property to Shimon, his act of giving it to Shimon constitutes a retraction of his instruction to Reuven. *Leis beh pesida de-kama batar batar zachi* — Shimon will not be harmed, because the later gift will supersede the earlier one, and Shimon will prevail over Reuven regardless of the January date on the found document. There is therefore no risk of injustice, and the document may be returned.

### Dead: Son Can't Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָא תָנֵי בְּבָרַיְיתָא: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם מוֹדִים, לֹא יַחְזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה, בִּבְרֵיהּ. דְּאָמְרִינַן: דִּלְמָא כְּתַב אֲבוּהּ לְהַאי, וְאִמְּלִיךְ וְלָא יַהֲבַיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, וּבָתַר אֲבוּהּ כְּתַב אִיהוּ לְאִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא וְיַהֲבַהּ לֵיהּ, וְהַשְׁתָּא קָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ מֵהָהוּא. סָבַר: מִהְדָּר לָא מָצֵינָא הָדַרְנָא בִּי. אֵימַר לְהוּ דְּאַבָּא יַהֲבַהּ לֵיהּ לְהַאי, וְנִתְּבוּ לֵיהּ כְּתָבֵיהּ, וְנֵיזִיל וְנַפֵּיק מִינֵּיהּ דְּהוּא זָכֵי וְנִפְלוֹג בַּהֲדֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** And **when the *baraita* teaches** that **even if both** the one who wrote the deed and its intended recipient **agree** that it is valid, the one who found it **should neither return** it **to this** person **nor to that** person, it is referring **to** a case where the one who wrote it died, and it is **his son** who authorizes its return to the recipient. In that case, the deed may not be returned, **as we say** that **perhaps his father wrote** the deed of gift **for this** person **and** then **reconsidered and did not give it to him, and after his father** died, the son **wrote** a deed of gift giving the property **to another person and gave it to him. And now** the son wishes to **retract that** gift, **thinking:** Since **I cannot retract** the gift legally, **I will say to** the court **that my father gave** his deed of gift **to this** first person, **and they will return him his deed** of gift, **and he will** then **go and appropriate** the property **from the one who** legally acquired the property, **as he will** be successful in **acquiring** it, **and I will divide** it **with him.**

When the *shechiv meira* has died, however, the situation is fundamentally different. The father died without having retracted his expressed intention to give the property to Reuven. The son inherited the estate. The son then gave the property to Shimon. Now the son says that the document — his father's deathbed bequest to Reuven, found after the father's death — should be returned to Reuven.

Here the crucial asymmetry emerges: a gift made by the son to Shimon does not supersede the father's bequest to Reuven. The father never retracted; it was only the son who conveyed to Shimon. Since a *shechiv meira*'s later instruction overrides an earlier one only when both come from the same person, the son's gift to Shimon carries no superseding power over the father's bequest to Reuven. If the father's document is now given to Reuven with its January date, Reuven will hold a bequest from the father that was never retracted — and that will take the property away from Shimon unjustly.

### Fraud Scenario

The real sequence of events may have been: the father expressed a deathbed bequest to Reuven in January but never gave the document. The father died. The son inherited and gave the property to Shimon. Now the son regrets this. He cannot simply retract his gift to Shimon — *mehadar lo mtzitna hadrina bi* — having made a completed gift, he cannot simply declare he has changed his mind. So instead he conspires with Reuven: he will claim that his father did give the document to Reuven at the time it was written. If the document is returned to Reuven, Reuven can produce it as evidence of the father's bequest, one that was never retracted by the father and that the son's gift to Shimon cannot override. *Veniflog behadeh* — the son and Reuven split the proceeds, while Shimon loses the property he had legitimately received.

### No Return if Dead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ אֲנַן: הַאי כְּתָבָא לָא יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ לְהַאי, דְּדִלְמָא מִכְתָּב כַּתְבֵיהּ אֲבוּהּ מֵיהָב לָא יַהֲבַהּ לֵיהּ, וִיהַבְתֵּיהּ אַתְּ לְאִינִישׁ אַחְרִינָא וְקָא הָדְרַתְּ בֵּיהּ. אֶלָּא אִי קוּשְׁטָא קָא אָמְרַתְּ, דִּיהַב לֵיהּ אֲבוּךְ – זִיל אַתְּ הַשְׁתָּה כְּתֹב לֵיהּ שְׁטָרָא אַחֲרִינָא, דְּאִי נָמֵי לָא יַהֲבַהּ לֵיהּ אֲבוּהּ וּכְתַבְתֵּיהּ אַתְּ לְאִינִישׁ אַחְרִינָא – לֵית בַּהּ פְּסֵידָא, דְּקַמָּא וּבָתְרָא – קַמָּא זָכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore, we say to** the son: **We will not give this deed to this** person, **as perhaps** your **father wrote it** but **did not give it to him, and** then **you gave** the property **to another person, and** now **you** wish to **retract** your gift. **Rather, if you are telling the truth that your father gave him** this property, then **you** should **go now** and **write another deed** of gift **for him,** so **that even if** your **father did not give him** this property, **and you wrote** a deed of gift giving this property **to another person,** he will **incur no loss.** This is **because** in a case where there are two deeds of gift, **a first one and a last one,** the recipient of **the first one acquires** the property.

*Hilkach amrinan leh anan hai shtara lo yahavinan leh lehai* — therefore we say: we will not give this document to Reuven. *De'dilma michtav katav avuh meihav lo yahav leh* — perhaps your father wrote it for Reuven but never actually handed it over. *Ve'yahavt at l'ne achrini ve'kahadart bach* — and you, the son, gave the property to Shimon and now regret it.

If, however, the son insists that there is no Shimon and that the father truly did intend to give this to Reuven, the Gemara responds: *zil at hashta ktov leh shtara achrina* — then go and write Reuven a new document now and give it to him today. *D'ei nami lo yahiv leh avuch ve'katavt at hashta l'ne achrina leit bei psida* — because if the father never gave it and you are now giving it, dating the document to today creates no injustice.

### Son's Later Doc

If the son's concern is that Reuven will be disadvantaged because the new document is dated later than some hypothetical Shimon's document, the Gemara answers: that is precisely the correct outcome. *De'kama batar kama zachai* — the earlier document wins. If there genuinely is a Shimon who holds an earlier gift, Shimon should prevail, and a new document for Reuven dated today will properly yield to Shimon's earlier claim. If there is no Shimon, then Reuven will receive the property without any competing claim. Either way, the solution of writing a fresh document today is safe — it ensures that Reuven can receive what he is owed without creating the risk of defrauding anyone who may have a legitimate prior claim.
