# Bekhorot 47b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bat Kohen/Levi Exemption

### Two Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: לְוִיָּיה שֶׁיָּלְדָה — בְּנָהּ פָּטוּר מֵחֲמֵשׁ סְלָעִים. דְּאִיעַבַּר מִמַּאן? אִילֵימָא דְּאִיעַבַּר מִכֹּהֵן וּמִלֵּוִי, מַאי אִירְיָא לְוִיָּיה? אֲפִילּוּ יִשְׂרְאֵלִית נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** With regard to the daughter of **a Levite who gave birth** to a firstborn boy, **her son is exempt from** the obligation to give **five *sela*** coins to the priest for his redemption, as the child is considered to be the son of a Levite and Levites are exempt from this obligation. The Gemara asks: **From whom did she become pregnant? If we say that she became pregnant from a priest or a Levite, why** does Rav Adda bar Ahava refer **specifically** to the daughter of **a Levite?** The *halakha* would be the same **even** for **an Israelite woman** who became pregnant from a priest or a Levite.

The *sugya* opened near the bottom of 47a with the question of whether a child born to a *kohenet* or *leviyah* — the feminine forms of these identities — is exempt from *pidyon haben*. Two explanations had been advanced. The first holds that the exemption applies only in the rare case where the father is not Jewish. If the father is Jewish, the child's identity follows the father; but if the father is a non-Jew, the child's identity is defined to some degree by the mother. Such a child would be a kind of *pasul Levi* or quasi-*kohen*, but that quasi-status is sufficient to exempt from *pidyon haben*, with the exemption thus running through the child's identity. The second explanation — the one we rule by, cited in the name of Rava — holds that even when the father is Jewish, the exemption has nothing to do with the child possessing *kohen* or *Levi* identity. Rather, it is sufficient that the mother herself is a *bat kohen* or *bat Levi*, because *pidyon haben* is governed by *peter rechem*, the opening of the womb, and here the mother's status is what matters.

One might ask, regarding that first explanation, whether a child with a non-Jewish father and a *Levi* mother would receive *ma'aser*. This is never explored elsewhere in the *sugyot*, and one would have to conclude the answer is no — although the *Gemara* does use the phrase *Levi pasul*, that designation is strained, since the child's father is not a *Levi*. The most one can say is that some quasi-*Levi* status attaches, but nothing in the tradition suggests that *ma'aser* would be directed to such a person.

### Mishna Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מִי שֶׁלֹּא הָיוּ לוֹ בָּנִים, וְנָשָׂא אִשָּׁה שֶׁכְּבָר יָלְדָה, עוֹדָהּ שִׁפְחָה וְנִשְׁתַּחְרְרָה, עוֹדָהּ גּוֹיָה וְנִתְגַּיְּירָה, וּמִשֶּׁבָּאתָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל יָלָדָה — וְלָדָהּ בְּכוֹר לַנַּחֲלָה וְאֵינוֹ בְּכוֹר לַכֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty concerning the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **We learned** in the mishna: **One who did not have sons and he married a woman who had already given birth;** or if he married a woman who gave birth when **she was still** a Canaanite **maidservant and she was** then **emancipated;** or one who gave birth when **she was still a gentile and she** then **converted, and when** the maidservant or the gentile **came to** join **the Jewish people she gave birth** to a male, that **offspring is a firstborn with regard to inheritance but is not a firstborn with regard to** redemption from **a priest.**

The *Gemara* brings the *Mishna* to bear on this question. The *Mishna* presents several scenarios in which a child is definitely obligated in *pidyon haben* but the identity of the particular child is uncertain. One scenario involves a man who had prior children and then married a woman who had not yet given birth — so there will be a *pidyon haben* obligation on her side, as the child is her *peter rechem*, but no double-portion inheritance on the father's side since he already had children. Another scenario involves a woman who converted while pregnant, or was freed from slavery while pregnant, and then gave birth — the child is a *peter rechem* for the mother and is therefore obligated in *pidyon haben*.

The case that occupies us here is where the child of this obligated mother is mixed up with the son of a *kohenet* or *leviyah*, who is not obligated in *pidyon haben*. The father must therefore perform *pidyon haben*, since one of these two is definitely his son and definitely obligated — he simply does not know which one. Additionally, the *Mishna* addresses a woman whose husband died or divorced her and who remarried within three months, subsequently giving birth: the paternity is unknown, so neither father performs the *pidyon haben*, but the child himself is obligated. Such a child also does not receive the double-portion inheritance, because from each potential father's family, the brothers will say he is not their brother.

The upshot of the *Mishna* is that a child born to a *kohenet* or *leviyah* is clearly exempt from *pidyon haben* — the entire structure of the mixed-up case only makes sense on that premise.

### Linguistic Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָלְדָה מִמַּאן? אִילֵימָא מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלֹּא הָיוּ לוֹ בָּנִים — מַאי אִירְיָא גִּיּוֹרֶת וְשִׁפְחָה? אֲפִילּוּ בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the mishna: This maidservant or convert, whose child that was born when she came to join the Jewish people is a firstborn for inheritance, **from whom,** i.e., from what type of father, **did she bear** him? **If we say** she bore him **from a Jew who did not** previously **have sons,** the initial subject of this clause of the mishna, **why** does it make reference **specifically** to **a gentile or a maidservant,** indicating that this son is a firstborn for inheritance because the ones born when she was not Jewish are disregarded? The *halakha* would be the same **even** with regard to **a Jewish woman** who had already given birth.

The *Gemara* now draws an inference from the very language of the *Mishna*: *michlal de-kohenet u-leviyah ptura* — from the fact that the *Mishna* specifies *kohenet* and *leviyah*, it is clear that their children are exempt. But now the *Gemara* presses further. *Di-avar mi-man?* — Who is the father? If you say the father is a *kohen* or *Levi*, then why specify *kohenet* and *leviyah*? Even a *bat Yisrael* married to a *kohen* or *Levi* would produce an exempt child. The very choice of the mother's designation implies that the exemption flows from the mother's own identity. This would seem to confirm the second explanation — that the mother's status alone is sufficient, even with a Jewish father.

Yet the *Gemara* then proposes: *ella di-avar mi-ovdei kochavim* — perhaps after all it is the case of a non-Jewish father, as the first explanation holds, and the exemption does flow through the child's quasi-identity derived from the mother.

### Rav Pappa's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּאִיעַבַּר מִגּוֹי. וְלָא תֵּימָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין מְזַהֲמִין אֶת הַוָּלָד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְזַהֲמִין אֶת הַוָּלָד, לֵוִי פָּסוּל מִיקְּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** This is referring to the daughter of a Levite **who became pregnant from a gentile.** In such a case, the son's status is determined by that of the mother, and therefore the son is exempt from the redemption of the firstborn. **And do not say** this is the *halakha* only **according to the one who says** that in such a situation **one does not disqualify the child** at all due to his gentile father, and he is of fit lineage. **Rather, even according to the one who says** that **one does disqualify the child** due to the gentile father, nevertheless, one also follows the mother **and he is** therefore **called a Levite** of **flawed** lineage, and is exempt from redemption.

This is where the difficulty crystallizes, because the *Mishna* includes not only *leviyah* but also *kohenet*. Rav Pappa raises the challenge. He tells us that Rabbah — or Rava, as some read — once posed a test question to his students: if a *bat kohen* becomes pregnant from a non-Jew, what is the rule regarding *pidyon haben*? Rav Pappa says he answered that it should be no different from the case of a *leviyah* who has a child from a non-Jew — citing the ruling of Rav Adda bar Ahava that if a *leviyah* gives birth, her son is exempt from the five *sela'im*. If that exemption holds for a *leviyah* with a non-Jewish father, surely the same should apply to a *bat kohen* in the same situation. Rav Pappa reports that his teacher replied: you have failed the test. The cases are not comparable.

### Bat Levi vs Bat Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְוִיָּה בִּקְדוּשְׁתַּהּ קָיְימָא, דְּתַנְיָא: לְוִיָּה שֶׁנִּשְׁבֵּית אוֹ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה בְּעִילַת זְנוּת — נוֹתְנִין לָהּ מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר וְאוֹכֶלֶת. אֶלָּא כֹּהֶנֶת, כֵּיוָן דְּאִי בָּעֵיל לַהּ, הָוְיָא זָרָה!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa continues: **And Rabba said to me: How can** these cases **be compared? Granted,** if the daughter of **a Levite** has a child from a gentile, he is considered a Levite with regard to redemption since his mother **retains her sanctity. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a Levite woman who was captured,** leading to a concern that one of her captors might have engaged in sexual intercourse with her, **or** even **if** a Levite woman definitely **engaged in licentious sexual intercourse,** one nevertheless **gives her** first **tithe and she may eat** it. **But** with regard to the daughter of **a priest, since if** a gentile **engages in intercourse with her she becomes** like **a non-priest** and may no longer partake of *teruma*, her son from a gentile should be considered like an Israelite and be obligated in the redemption of a firstborn.

The distinction is as follows. *Bishlama leviyah bikdushata kaima* — a *bat Levi* who has relations with a non-Jew retains her *Levi* status entirely. A *baraita* makes this explicit: if a *leviyah* is taken captive — in which context we assume she was violated — or she has illicit sexual relations, we still give her *ma'aser* and she continues to eat it. Her *Levi* status is unaffected. This is worth noting in a broader context: just as we learned when studying *matnot kehuna* that *zroa, lechayayim, v'keiva* can be given directly to a *bat kohen* — a *kohenet* in her own right — so too *ma'aser* can be given directly to a *bat Levi* as one directly entitled to it. And since her status remains intact after relations with a non-Jew, one can understand how her child from that non-Jew would carry a quasi-*Levi* identity sufficient to exempt from *pidyon hebben*.

But the *bat kohen* is in a categorically different position. *Keivan d'niv'ala, havya zara* — once she has relations with a non-Jew, she loses her *kehuna* status entirely and becomes a *zarah*. It should be noted that even a *bat Yisrael* who has relations with a non-Jew is forbidden thereafter to a *kohen*, because the *Torah*'s definition of *zona* — as the *Rambam* frames it — is not literally a prostitute but refers to an act of sexual union that could not have occurred in a marital context. Since marriage between a Jew and a non-Jew is halachically impossible, such a union renders even a *bat Yisrael* forbidden to a *kohen*. For a *bat kohen*, however, the same act that would make a *bat Yisrael* forbidden to a *kohen* does something more: it creates a contradiction to her *kehuna* identity itself. It undermines her status as a *bat kohen* at its root.

One might wonder: even granting that she loses her *kohen* status, every *kohen* is also a *Levi* — should she not retain at least a *leviyah* status? *Tosafot* addresses this directly and rejects the idea. A *kohenet* does not hold two separate statuses that can be disaggregated. Her entire identity is *kohenet*, and if that is compromised, it is compromised in its entirety. There is no residual *leviyah* standing to fall back on.

It is worth pausing here on an asymmetry that runs through all of this. A male *kohen* who has forbidden relations — with a non-Jew, a *grusha*, a *challala* — does not lose his *kehuna* status. He has violated serious *issurim*, and his children from such unions will be *challalim* without *kedushat kehunah*, though they are not worse off than a normal *Yisrael*. But his own sanctity remains intact. The woman, by contrast, suffers with great severity: her entire status can be annulled by the very same act. This asymmetry is part of the broader halachic reality in which the woman's sexual history is defining in ways the man's is not.

### Non-Jew Father Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, דְּאָמַר דְּאִיעַבַּר מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, מוֹקֵי לַהּ בִּדְאִיעַבַּר מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, אֶלָּא לְרַב פָּפָּא בְּמַאי מוֹקֵי לָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **Mar, son of Rav Yosef,** citing **in the name of Rava, who says** that Rav Adda bar Ahava, who deems the son of a Levite woman exempt from the obligation of redemption, was referring to a case **where** she **became pregnant from an Israelite.** This is because he can **interpret** the mishna **as** referring **to** the daughter of a priest or the daughter of a Levite **who became pregnant from an Israelite. But according to** the opinion of **Rav Pappa,** who maintains that the son of a priest's or Levite's daughter who became pregnant from an Israelite is subject to the obligation of redemption from a priest, **with regard to what** case does he **interpret** the mishna?

The conclusion is now clear. The *Gemara* says: *hanicha l'mar brei d'Rav Yosef mishme d'Rava d'amar d'ivra mi-Yisrael* — for the opinion cited in the name of Rava, which holds that even with a Jewish father the *kohenet* or *leviyah* mother exempts her child based on *peter rechem* and the mother's status alone, the *Mishna* poses no difficulty. Even with a fully Jewish father on both sides, there is no *pidyon haben* because the mother's *kohenet* or *leviyah* status operates independently.

But according to the view that the exemption requires a non-Jewish father, the *Mishna*'s inclusion of *kohenet* is untenable. A non-Jewish father combined with a *bat kohen* mother destroys her *kohenet* standing — and therefore there is nothing to derive the child's quasi-*kohen* status from. The non-Jewish father explanation works for *bat Levi* alone, and fails entirely when applied to *bat kohen*.

## Wife, Not Daughter

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְעוֹלָם דְּאִיעַבַּר מִכֹּהֵן, וְהִיא בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לַהּ ״כֹּהֶנֶת״? דִּבְנָהּ כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna is not referring to the daughter of a priest but to a woman **who became pregnant from a priest.** Therefore, her son is exempt from redemption, **and** yet **she** herself is **an Israelite woman. And why does** the mishna **call her** the daughter of **a priest [*kohenet*]? Because her son is a priest.**

The *Gemara* states: *hanicha l'mar brei d'Rav Yosef mishme d'Rava d'amar d'ivra mi-Yisrael* — according to the ruling we follow, which is that of Rava, the exemption applies even when the father is a full *Yisrael*, because the mother's status as *kohenet* or *leviyah* is independently sufficient to exempt the child through *peter rechem*.

### Reinterpreting Terms

But what of the opposing view — that held by Rav Pappa — according to which the exemption requires a non-Jewish father, and therefore the *kohenet* case cannot function? The *Gemara* provides an answer: *l'olam d'ivra mi-kohein* — in the *kohenet* case in the *Mishna*, the father is simply a *kohen*, and that is why the child is exempt. The child is a *kohen*, and the exemption flows from the child's own status.

Then why does the *Mishna* call her a *kohenet*? *V'amai kari lei kohenet? De'benah kohen* — it calls her *kohenet* because her son is a *kohen*. That is, according to this reading, when the *Mishna* says *kohenet* and *leviyah*, it does not mean *bat kohen* and *bat Levi* at all.

### Logical Consistency

It means the wife of a *kohen* and the wife of a *Levi*. According to this view, a *bat kohen* as mother would *never* exempt her child from *pidyon haben*, because the only scenario in which her identity could theoretically confer quasi-*kohen* status on the child — a non-Jewish father — is precisely the scenario that destroys her *kohenet* standing. The *Mishna*'s language *kohenet* and *leviyah* therefore refers to the marital relationship, not the woman's own lineage.

We do not rule this way. The accepted *halacha* is that *bat kohen* and *bat Levi* exempt across the board, defined not only through the father but through the mother as well, because here the mother's status genuinely matters for *peter rechem*.

## Paternity Uncertainty

### Kohen Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: כֹּהֵן שֶׁמֵּת וְהִנִּיחַ בֵּן חָלָל — רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: הַבֵּן חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת אֶת עַצְמוֹ, רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֵין חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת אֶת עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated** that in the case of **a priest who** married a divorcée and then **died and left a son** who was **a priest disqualified due to flawed lineage [*ḥalal*]** and has the status of a non-priest, **Rav Ḥisda says: The son is obligated to redeem himself. Rabba bar Rav Huna says: He is not obligated to redeem himself.**

The *Gemara* now presents a new case. A *kohen* who was married to a woman who had not previously given birth — and in this case the *kohen* himself is a *challal*, meaning he does not possess *kedushat kehunah*. The child he has with her is not a *kohen*, so the father would be obligated in *pidyon haben* — although practically speaking, since he is a *kohen* himself, he simply moves the five *shekalim* from one pocket to the other. But the interesting question arises when the father dies.

### Rav Chisda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי הֵיכָא דְּמֵת הָאָב בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: הַבֵּן חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת אֶת עַצְמוֹ, דְּהָא לֹא זָכָה אָבִיו בְּפִדְיוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree when the father died within thirty days** of the birth of the son, before the obligation of redemption had taken effect. **Rav Ḥisda says the son is obligated to redeem himself, as his father did not acquire his redemption** money.

Rav Chisda rules that the son is obligated to redeem himself. The *Gemara* establishes that if the father died after thirty days had elapsed since the son's birth, there is no dispute: the son is not obligated, because *sheharei zachah aviv b'fidyono* — once day thirty arrived and the father was a *kohen*, it is as if he automatically discharged the obligation, having in effect already "given" the *pidyon* to himself. The debate concerns only when the father died within thirty days — before day thirty arrived and before any *pidyon haben* was performed.

Rav Chisda holds that in that situation the son is obligated to redeem himself, because *lo zachah aviv b'fidyono* — the father never discharged the obligation, no *pidyon* was ever performed, and the obligation has therefore devolved upon the son.

### Rabbah Bar Huna Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֵין הַבֵּן חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת עַצְמוֹ, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָתֵינָא מִכֹּחַ גַּבְרָא דְּלָא מָצֵית לְאִשְׁתַּעוֹיֵי דִּינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna says the son is not obligated to redeem himself, as** he can **say to** a priest who demands the redemption money from him: **I come due to the strength of a man with whom you cannot enter into judgment,** i.e., if my father were alive he would have separated five *sela* coins and taken the money for himself, and I inherit his claim.

Rabbah bar Huna disagrees: the son is not obligated. His argument is phrased in the language of civil litigation: *de'amar lei, atina mikoach gavra delo matzis le'ishtuyei dina bahadei* — the son says to any *kohen* who would come to claim the *pidyon*, "I derive my obligation from a man you could never have brought to court." The father was himself a *kohen*; no *kohen* in the world could have successfully demanded the *pidyon haben* from him, since he could always designate himself as the recipient. Since the obligation originated with the father — the son's obligation being derivative of the father's — and since no *kohen* could ever have enforced a claim against the father, that same incapacity extends to the son.

This argument is striking precisely because of what it reveals about the *Gemara*'s conceptualization of *pidyon haben*: the entire matter is being framed in the language of *dinei mamonot*, a civil debt. The *mitzvah* dimension — the ritual act of giving — is being set aside in favor of the question of enforceability. One might challenge this framing in two ways. First, it is debatable whether the obligation truly "started" with the father if he died before day thirty, before it formally crystallized. Second, even granting that the obligation derives from the father, a purely civil framing seems to miss something: if *pidyon haben* is a ritual act and not merely a debt, then the question of whether a *beis din* could have sued the father may be beside the point.

Underlying this debate is a more fundamental question: whose obligation is *pidyon haben* at its core? One could conceptualize it as fundamentally the father's obligation, which devolves upon the son only secondarily when the father is unavailable. Or one could frame it the other way — that fundamentally it is the son's obligation, but since the son is not old enough to perform it, the father fulfills it on his behalf. These two framings lead to different conclusions about the son's derived liability.

### Conversion Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: נִתְגַּיְּירָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת — בְּכוֹר לַכֹּהֵן, וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא לֵיהּ: אָתֵינָא מִכֹּחַ גַּבְרָא דְּלָא מָצֵית לְאִשְׁתַּעוֹיֵי דִּינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ! שָׁאנֵי גּוֹי דְּלֵית לֵיהּ חַיִיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a challenge to the opinion of Rabbah bar Rav Huna: **We learned** in the mishna: If one married a woman who **converted while** she was **pregnant,** having been impregnated by a gentile, the child is **a firstborn with regard to** redemption from **a priest** and must redeem himself. **But why** is this the *halakha*? **Let** this son **say to** the priest: **I come due to the strength of a man with whom you cannot enter into judgment,** as his father is a gentile, from whom the redemption money cannot be claimed. The Gemara responds: The case of **a gentile is different, as he has no lineage [*ḥayis*],** i.e., this man is not considered his father's son at all.

The *Gemara* tests Rabbah bar Huna's argument by citing our *Mishna*: in the case of a pregnant woman who converted, the *Mishna* states that the child is *bechor la-kohen* — obligated in *pidyon haben*. But the *Gemara* asks: why? Why shouldn't the son make exactly the same argument — "I come from a man you could never have taken to court"? The biological father was a non-Jew and was not obligated in *pidyon haben* at the time; then he converted, but as a convert he has, in the halachic framework, a new identity and is no longer halachically the father. There is thus no father against whom a claim could ever have been brought. If Rabbah bar Huna's logic exempts the *challal*'s son, why does it not exempt the son of the convert?

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן יָאסִינְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כֹּהֵן שֶׁמֵּת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים וְהִנִּיחַ בֵּן חָלָל — הַבֵּן חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת אֶת עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא זָכָה הָאָב בְּפִדְיוֹנוֹ. מֵת לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם — אֵין הַבֵּן חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁהֲרֵי זָכָה הָאָב בְּפִדְיוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** On this matter, the Gemara notes that **Rabbi Shimon Yasinia says** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says,** in accordance with the opinion of Rav Ḥisda: With regard to **a priest who died within thirty** days of the birth of his son **and left a son** who was **a *ḥalal*, the son is obligated to redeem himself, as the father** did **not acquire his redemption** money. If the father **died after thirty days** following the birth of the son, **the son is not obligated to redeem himself, as his father** already **acquired his redemption** money.

The *Gemara* resolves the question: *shani oved kochavim d'leis lei chiyus* — a non-Jew is categorically different because he has no halachic familial relationship to the child at all. There was never a recognized halachic father in the picture. When there is no halachic father, the obligation does not derive from any prior paternal claim — it arises fresh and directly with the son himself. Therefore, the son of the converted woman cannot deflect the obligation by invoking a father from whom no *kohen* could have collected, because there was no halachic father in the first place.

This yields a clear principle: when there is no halachic father — whether because the father is a non-Jew, or because of anonymous artificial insemination, or other such cases — the obligation of *pidyon haben* originates with and belongs to the son from the outset. When there is a halachically recognized father, the obligation starts with the father, and only when he is unavailable does it devolve upon the son.

The conclusion, attributed to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish in the name of Rabbi Shimon Yasina, is that when a *kohen challal*'s father died within thirty days, the son is obligated to redeem himself — *shelo zachah av b'fidyono*, the father had not discharged the obligation while alive. After thirty days, he does not need to redeem himself — *sheharei zachah av b'fidyono*, because on day thirty the father's very presence as a *kohen* constituted constructive fulfillment of the obligation.

## Yakir Doctrine

### Uncertain Births

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִכְתְּבוּ הַרְשָׁאָה לַהֲדָדֵי! וְכִי תֵּימָא: מַתְנִיתִין שֶׁלֹּא בְּהַרְשָׁאָה — הָא מוֹקְמִינַן לַהּ קַמַּן דִּבְהַרְשָׁאָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let** the son with uncertain status and the son who follows him **write an authorization to one another,** by which each grants the other the right to claim his own share. In this manner they can claim the double portion together, as one of them is certainly the firstborn. **And if you would say** that **the mishna** is dealing with a case **where** there was **no authorization, we interpret it later on** (see 49a) as referring **to** a situation **where** there was **an authorization.**

The *Mishnah* now addresses the question of who qualifies as a *bechor* for both inheritance and *pidyon haben* simultaneously. The *Mishnah* presents cases where a child is *bechor* for both — but the interesting scenario is where births are uncertain and no one has an unambiguous claim.

Consider two couples whose infants are mixed up. Reuven and Shimon each had a first son, but the children were confused in the hospital and nobody knows whose is whose. Neither child will succeed in claiming an inheritance: each time one of them approaches his putative father's brothers, they will push him away and say he may be the other man's son. So he gets nothing from that family, and the same happens at the other.

One might ask: why not solve this through a *harsha'ah*, a power of attorney? Reuven's doubtful son gives his power of attorney to Shimon's doubtful son, and that son goes to Shimon's family and says: either I am your brother and entitled to my portion, or if you claim I am not your brother, then I am acting under the authority of Reuven's son — and if I am he, then I have an inheritance claim against Reuven's family. By coordinating their claims, between the two of them they should be able to extract at least one *bechor* portion. The *Gemara* notes this possibility and acknowledges that in another *Mishna* there is indeed a discussion of resolving uncertain inheritance through *harsha'ah*.

Why, then, does it not work here? The *Gemara* cites this as proof for the principle of Rabbi Yannai.

### Rabbi Yannai's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יַנַּאי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: הוּכְּרוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִתְעָרְבוּ — כּוֹתְבִין הַרְשָׁאָה זֶה לָזֶה, לֹא הוּכְּרוּ וּבְסוֹף נִתְעָרְבוּ — אֵין כּוֹתְבִין הַרְשָׁאָה זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna, which indicates that there is no firstborn with regard to inheritance here, **supports** the opinion **of Rabbi Yannai, as Rabbi Yannai says:** In a case where two sons of two men became confused and it is unknown who is the father of whom, if the two sons **were** initially **recognized and were ultimately intermingled, each writes an authorization to the other,** and the other sons in each family cannot claim that these sons are not their brother. If **they were not** initially **recognized and were ultimately intermingled, each does not write an authorization to the other,** as even initially neither of them had any certain right to their father's inheritance.

Rabbi Yannai states: *hukru u-levasof nitarvu, kotvin harsha'ah zeh la-zeh* — if there was a moment when it was known whose child was whose, and only subsequently the children were confused, they may give each other power of attorney. But *lo hukru u-levasof nitarvu* — if there was never even a single moment of identification, if the births occurred in darkness and no one ever knew who belonged to whom, then *ein kotvin harsha'ah zeh la-zeh* — they may not give each other power of attorney, and neither can claim any inheritance.

The reasoning is profound: in order to hold a valid claim as a son for purposes of inheritance, there must have been at least a momentary *yakir* — a moment of recognition, of the father's having identified this child as his. That is the force of the word *yakir* in the *Torah* in the passage about the firstborn. Inheritance status is not merely a biological fact; it requires halachic actualization through recognition. Without that moment of *yakir*, the child never acquired the standing of an heir in the first place, and therefore has no valid claim to transfer through *harsha'ah*.

The concept of *yakir* is a major theme in *Bava Batra*, which deals with the laws of inheritance at length. The question of whether DNA evidence could constitute a form of *yakir* is a genuinely open question — presumably recognition need not occur at the moment of birth — though the use of DNA in *halacha* raises serious independent concerns, since widespread paternity testing could expose a far greater number of *mamzerim* than the tradition has been willing to acknowledge.

### No Yakir, No Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן מִי שֶׁלֹּא שָׁהֲתָה אַחֲרֵי בַּעְלָהּ שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And likewise** a woman **who did not wait three months after** the death of **her husband** and she married and gave birth and it is unknown whether the son was born after a pregnancy of nine months and is the son of the first husband, or after seven months and is the son of the second husband, the son is a firstborn with regard to redemption from a priest but is not a firstborn with regard to inheritance.

This brings us back to the case of the woman who remarried within three months of her first husband's death or divorce, subsequently gave birth, and the paternity is unknown. There was never a moment of *yakir*: no father ever identified this child as his son in any recognized halachic sense. Therefore, the *harsha'ah* solution is unavailable. The child will receive no inheritance at all — not merely no double portion. As Rabbi Yirmiyah clarifies, when the *Mishnah* says the subsequent son of either father is "not a *bechor* for inheritance," it does not mean he receives a normal single portion. It means that the doubtful child, by potentially being the *bechor*, prevents any later-born son from claiming the *bechor* double portion either — because they can never rule out that the earlier doubtful son was truly the firstborn. The result is that nobody receives a firstborn portion, and the doubtful child receives nothing at all.

## Miscarriage Cases

### Non-Viable Births

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵי זֶהוּ בְּכוֹר לַנַּחֲלָה וְלַכֹּהֵן? הַמַּפֶּלֶת שַׁפִּיר מָלֵא מַיִם, מָלֵא דָּם, מְלֵא גְּנִינִין, הַמַּפֶּלֶת כְּמִין דָּגִים וַחֲגָבִים שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים, וְהַמַּפֶּלֶת לְיוֹם אַרְבָּעִים — הַבָּא אַחֲרֵיהֶם בְּכוֹר לַנַּחֲלָה וּלְכֹהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Which is** the offspring **that is a firstborn** both **with regard to inheritance and with regard to** redemption from **a priest?** In the case of a woman **who miscarried a gestational sac full of water,** or one **full of blood,** or one **full of pieces of flesh;** or one **who miscarries** a mass **resembling a fish, or grasshoppers,** or **repugnant creatures, or creeping animals, or** one **who miscarries on the fortieth day** after conception, **the** son **who follows** any of **them is a firstborn with regard to inheritance and with regard to** redemption from **a priest.**

The *Mishnah* turns to a different category: cases in which something is expelled from the womb but does not affect the mother's *peter rechem* status. If a woman expels an embryonic sac filled with water, or blood, or varying colors — with no discernible human form — or if what she expels resembles fish, grasshoppers, or other non-human creatures, or if the expulsion occurs on or before day forty of pregnancy, then *haba achareihem* — the child subsequently born to this woman — remains a *bechor le-nachala u-le-kohen*, a firstborn for both inheritance and *pidyon haben*. The *chiddush* is that even though something emerged from her womb, it is not counted as a *peter rechem* event: these expulsions do not rise to the level of halachic human birth, and therefore the mother's *peter rechem* status is entirely unaffected.

### Day 40 Threshold

The designation of day forty as the threshold is significant well beyond this context. Before day forty, the embryo is not yet considered halachically formed as a human being — a principle with relevance across a wide range of halachic discussions, most notably in questions surrounding abortion. Up to that point, what emerges from the womb is not treated as having opened it in any legally cognizable sense.

## Cesarean Section

### Chachamim View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן, וְהַבָּא אַחֲרָיו — שְׁנֵיהֶן אֵינָן בְּכוֹר, לֹא לַנַּחֲלָה וְלֹא לַכֹּהֵן. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הָרִאשׁוֹן לַנַּחֲלָה, וְהַשֵּׁנִי לְחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of a boy **born by caesarean section and the** son **who follows him, both of them are not firstborn, neither with regard to inheritance nor with regard to** redemption from **a priest. Rabbi Shimon says: The first** son is a firstborn **with regard to inheritance** if he is his father's first son, **and the second** son is a firstborn with regard to redemption from a priest **for five *sela*** coins, because he is the first to emerge from the womb and he emerged in the usual way.

The *Mishnah* presents the case of *yotzei dofen* — a child delivered by Caesarean section — and the child born after it. The ruling of the *Chachamim* is that *shneihem einam bechor lo le-nachala velo le-kohen*: neither of these children is a *bechor* — not for inheritance and not for *pidyon haben*.

### Yalda Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִאשׁוֹן לַנַּחֲלָה לֹא — ״וְיָלְדוּ לוֹ״ בָּעֵינַן, לְחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים נָמֵי לָא — ״פָּטַר רֶחֶם״ בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that neither a son born by caesarean section nor the son who follows him are firstborn, neither with regard to inheritance nor with regard to redemption from a priest. The Gemara explains: The **first** son, born by caesarean section, is **not** considered the firstborn **with regard to inheritance,** as **we require** fulfillment of the verse: **"And they have borne him** children" (Deuteronomy 21:15), written in the context of inheritance, and a caesarean section is not called a birth. Likewise, **with regard to** the redemption with **five *sela*** coins, this son is **also not** considered the firstborn, as **we require** fulfillment of the verse: "Whatever **opens the womb"** (Exodus 13:2), and he did not open his mother's womb.

The exclusion of the C-section child from the double-portion inheritance is surprising at first glance. This child is, biologically, *reishit ono* — the father's firstborn. Why should the mode of delivery matter for inheritance? The *Gemara* derives it from the word *veyaldu* in the verse *veyaldu lo banim* — "and they bore him children." The word *yalda*, giving birth, is understood here to connote natural vaginal birth specifically. This reading is supported by a *gezeirah shavah* with the passage in *Vayikra* regarding a woman's *tum'at leidah*, where the verse says *ishah ki tazria veyalda zachar vetame'ah shivat yamim*, and the principle derived is *ad she-yoleda mi-makom she-mazra'at* — she gives birth only from the place where the seed enters, meaning vaginal delivery alone. The same definitional narrowness of *leidah* that governs *tum'at leidah* according to the *Chachamim* governs the firstborn's eligibility for double-portion inheritance.

### Second Child Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֵׁנִי לַנַּחֲלָה לֹא — ״רֵאשִׁית אוֹנוֹ״ בָּעֵינַן, לְחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים נָמֵי לָא — קָסָבַר: בְּכוֹר לְדָבָר אֶחָד לָא הָוֵי בְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the **second** son who follows him is **not** considered the firstborn **with regard to inheritance,** as **we require** fulfillment of the verse: "For he is **the first of his strength,** the right of the firstborn is his" (Deuteronomy 21:17), and this son is not the first. In addition, he is **also not** considered the firstborn **with regard to** the redemption of **five *sela*** coins, as the *tanna* of the mishna **holds: A firstborn for** only **one matter,** i.e., he opened his mother's womb but is not her first child, **is not considered a firstborn** with regard to redemption.

The second child — the one born vaginally after the C-section child — is clearly not a *bechor* for inheritance: *reishit ono beinan*, he is not the father's first child, and there is no basis for the double portion. That much is straightforward.

### Unified Bechor

But what about *pidyon haben*? He is, after all, the first child to emerge through *peter rechem* — the first to open the womb naturally. Should he not be obligated in *pidyon hebben*? The *Chachamim* say no: *bechor le-chol davar echad lo havei bechor* — one cannot be a *bechor* in only one respect. The concept of *bechor* requires a unified status. While it is true that one can be a *bechor* for the mother without being a *bechor* for the father — the mother's firstborn is the one relevant for *pidyon haben* — within the single domain of the mother, one cannot be the first to open the womb without also being the first child. This child is the second child of the mother. Even though he is the first to emerge through the natural opening, since he is not the first child born to this mother, he cannot constitute a *peter rechem* event sufficient to obligate *pidyon hebben*.

So the *Chachamim*'s ruling is comprehensive: the C-section child gets neither the double portion nor *pidyon haben*; and the subsequent vaginally-born child gets neither the double portion (not the father's first) nor *pidyon haben* (not the mother's first child, even though first through the natural opening).

### Rabbi Shimon Disagrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הָרִאשׁוֹן לַנַּחֲלָה, וְהַשֵּׁנִי לְחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: ״תֵּלֵד״ לְרַבּוֹת יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Shimon says: The first** son is a firstborn **with regard to inheritance** if he is his father's first son, **and the second** son is a firstborn with regard to redemption from a priest **for five *sela*** coins. The Gemara explains: The verse states: "And they have borne him children," and a caesarean section is not called a birth. **Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says** with regard to a verse that deals with the ritual impurity of a woman after childbirth: "But if **she bears** a girl" (Leviticus 12:5), that the apparently superfluous term "she bears" serves **to include** a child **born by caesarean section,** and the same applies with regard to a firstborn for inheritance.

Rabbi Shimon takes a different view across the board. *Rabbi Shimon le-ta'ameih* — Rabbi Shimon is consistent with his broader position that even a C-section delivery is included in the word *yalda*. Just as Rabbi Shimon holds that a woman who delivers by C-section becomes *teme'ah leidah*, because he does not read *leidah* as requiring vaginal birth, so too here the first child — born by C-section — qualifies as the father's *bechor* and receives the double-portion inheritance. The word *yalda* in all its occurrences includes C-section birth in Rabbi Shimon's view.

### Rabbi Shimon on Second

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשֵּׁנִי לְחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים, קָסָבַר: בְּכוֹר לְדָבָר אֶחָד הָוֵי בְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Shimon also rules that **the second** son is a firstborn with regard to redemption from a priest **for five *sela*** coins, as he **maintains: A firstborn for** only **one matter,** i.e., one who opened his mother's womb but is not her first child, **is** considered **a firstborn** with regard to the redemption of the firstborn, despite the fact that he is not his mother's first child.

However, Rabbi Shimon acknowledges that for *pidyon haben*, the operative phrase is not *yalda* but *peter rechem* — the opening of the womb — and that plainly refers to the natural vaginal opening. The C-section child did not open the womb in that sense. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon holds that the second child — the one born vaginally — is the *peter rechem* and does obligate *pidyon haben*. And for Rabbi Shimon, *bechor le-davar echad havei bechor*: being a *bechor* in one dimension is sufficient. The second child is *peter rechem* even though he is not the mother's first child, and that is enough to create an obligation of *pidyon haben*.

### Ruling: Follow Chachamim

Rabbi Shimon's position has a certain intuitive logic. Yet the *halacha* follows the *Chachamim*. The practical ruling is that a C-section birth produces no *pidyon haben* obligation and no double-portion inheritance — neither for the C-section child nor for the vaginally-born child who follows.
