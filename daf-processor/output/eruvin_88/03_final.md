# Eruvin 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chananya's Tiberias Lenien…

### Intro & Chananya Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים הִתִּיר רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא בֶּן עֲקַבְיָא לְאַנְשֵׁי טְבֶרְיָא: מְמַלְּאִים מַיִם מִגְּזוּזְטְרָא בַּשַּׁבָּת, וְטוֹמְנִין בְּעֵצָה, וּמִסְתַּפְּגִין בַּאֲלוּנְטִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Our Sages taught** a *baraita*: **Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akavya permitted three activities to the inhabitants of Tiberias: They may draw water** from the sea **through** a hole cut out of **a balcony on Shabbat, and they may insulate** produce **in the pods** of legumes, **and they may dry themselves** on Shabbat **with a towel [*aluntit*].**

Daf 88 opens by wrapping up a brief digression that occupied the close of the previous daf. The Gemara had been discussing the remarkable leniencies granted by Chananya ben Akavia to the people of Tiberias in connection with the problem of drawing water from a balcony that protrudes over a body of water. Ordinarily, the halachic fiction of suspended walls (*mechitzos taluyos*) is not employed to create a valid *reshut hayachid*. Chananya went considerably further than even those who would permit suspended walls: he ruled that a flat extension over the water could itself be treated as though all of its edges were bent downward around the hole through which a bucket is lowered, thereby creating virtual walls without requiring any actual physical walls at all. This extraordinary leniency was confined to the people of Tiberias (*anshei Tveryah*), either because of the acute practical need arising from the dense population living along the shores of the Kinneret, or because the natural banks of the Kinneret already constituted a partial wall that made the additional leniency more defensible.

### Walls & Extensions

The nature of this first leniency — the wall-like treatment of the flat extension and the downward projection of walls — has already been analyzed. The Gemara now turns to present the second and third rulings Chananya issued in connection with the people of Tiberias, both of which appear at the opening of daf 88, amud 1, beginning with the words *u'mistapkin b'aluntit*.

### Legume Refuse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטוֹמְנִין בְּעֵצָה מַאי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: הִשְׁכִּים לְהָבִיא פְּסוֹלֶת, אִם בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו טַל — הֲרֵי הוּא בְּ״כִי יוּתַּן״, וְאִם בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁלֹּא יִבָּטֵל מִמְּלַאכְתּוֹ — אֵינוֹ בְּ״כִי יוּתַּן״. וּסְתָם אַנְשֵׁי טְבֶרְיָא כְּמִי שֶׁלֹּא יִבְטַל מִמְּלַאכְתּוֹ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this *baraita*: **And they may insulate** produce **in the pods** of legumes; **what is** this *halakha*? **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: If one **rose early** in the morning **to bring residue** from the field, e.g., the straw of wheat or the stalks or pods of legumes, in order to store his produce in them, the following distinction applies: **If** he rose early **because** the residue still **has dew on it,** and he wants to use this moisture for his produce, **this** instance **is considered** to be in the following category: **If** any water **be put.** **And if** one rose early only **in order not to neglect his** usual **work,** this **is not considered** an instance of **if it be put.** **And normally,** **the inhabitants of Tiberias** are considered **like one** who does so in order **not to neglect his** usual **work.**

The second ruling concerned the refuse of legumes — perhaps the pods or outer casings — and the question of whether they were susceptible to *tumah*. In brief, Chananya ruled that they were not, because the people of Tiberias gathered them early in the morning not out of any interest in keeping them moist — which would be the operative consideration for *tumah* susceptibility — but simply because they needed to clear them away before beginning the workday. It should be noted that this was not a leniency particular to the people of Tiberias in any meaningful sense; it was the straightforward application of a general principle to their specific circumstances. This raises a broader reflection on what we even mean by *kula* and *chumra*: such terms are always relative to one's baseline assumption about the *halacha*. Someone doing what is actually required by the law is neither lenient nor stringent — they are simply following the *halacha* as they understand it.

### Towel Drying on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִסְתַּפְּגִין בַּאֲלוּנְטִית מַאי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: מִסְתַּפֵּג אָדָם בַּאֲלוּנְטִית, וּמַנִּיחָהּ בַּחַלּוֹן. וְלֹא יִמְסְרֶנָּה לָאוֹלְיָירִין מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחֲשׁוּדִין עַל אוֹתוֹ דָּבָר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף מְבִיאָהּ בְּיָדוֹ לְתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara turns to the third activity that Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akavya permitted for the inhabitants of Tiberias: **And they may dry themselves with a towel. What is** this *halakha*? **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **A person** who washed himself in cold water on Shabbat or a Festival **may dry himself with a towel and place it on a window,** as there is no concern that he perform the prohibited labor of wringing out the towel. **And he may not give** the towel **to the bathhouse attendants [*olayerin*] because they are suspected with regard to that matter,** as they might wring out the towel before giving it to other bathers. Furthermore, one may not bring the towel home because if he does so, he might forget and wring it out. **Rabbi Shimon said: He may even bring** the towel **in his hand to his house,** as there is no concern lest he wring it.

The third ruling is the one introduced by the phrase *u'mistapkin b'aluntit*: Chananya permitted the people of Tiberias to dry themselves with towels. The Gemara asks: what exactly does this mean? A *baraita* is cited that clarifies: a person may dry himself with a towel after bathing in cold water on *Shabbat*, leave the towel in the window to dry — the prohibition against squeezing (*sechitah*) being understood throughout — and must not hand the towel over to the bathhouse attendants, because those attendants are suspected of wringing out towels. The concern is *lifnei iver* or a form of facilitating a violation: even if the attendants are themselves acting improperly on *Shabbat*, one should not contribute to the situation by placing the towel in their hands.

This raises a natural question about the scenario: if the bath in question was in a bathhouse, how were the bathers permitted to be in a bathhouse at all on *Shabbat*? The Gemara's discussion assumes cold water, which sidesteps the prohibition against bathing in hot water on *Shabbat*, but exactly how this plays out in an actual bathhouse setting — and what the attendants were doing there on *Shabbat* — remains somewhat unclear. The concern articulated is not that the bather should rebuke the attendants for working on *Shabbat*, but rather the narrower point that one should not place a wet towel in their hands.

The *baraita* continues: *divrei Rabbi Shimon*, this is Rabbi Shimon's view. And Rabbi Shimon further adds — *af meivi beyado letoch beito* — that one may even carry the towel home. The concern that one might come to squeeze the towel while carrying it is dismissed. The practical upshot is that this ruling about towel-drying is not a specialized enactment for the people of Tiberias; it is a general *halacha* that happened to come up frequently in their context, presumably because they regularly bathed in or near the Kinneret. The distinction between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon is that the *tanna kamma* requires leaving the towel at the bathhouse (placing it in a window to dry), while Rabbi Shimon permits carrying it home.

## Drawing vs. Pouring

### Rav Huna's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְמַלּאוֹת, אֲבָל לִשְׁפּוֹךְ — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna: They taught** the leniency of partitions surrounding a hole in a balcony **only** with regard **to drawing** water through the hole; **but to pour** waste water down the hole, it is **prohibited.**

The Gemara now returns to the central topic with a ruling from Rabbah bar Rav Huna: *lo shanu ela lemallot* — the permission discussed in connection with the balcony over the water applies only to drawing water (*mallot*) from the water below. *Aval lishpoch assur* — to pour water downward from the balcony is forbidden.

One might initially suppose the distinction is based on need: there is greater necessity for drawing water than for disposing of it, and therefore the leniency applies only where necessity warrants it. But the Gemara will not take that path. The distinction will instead be grounded in the directionality and behavior of the water itself.

When one draws water up through the hole in the balcony, the water, at the moment of drawing, is contained within the virtual *reshut hayachid* created by the fictional walls extending downward. It stays there. But when one pours water down, the water strikes the surface below and flows outward — out of the virtual *reshut hayachid* and into the surrounding body of water, which has the status of a *karmelis*. Essentially, the act of pouring down results in transferring the water from a *reshut hayachid* to a *karmelis*, and even if unintended, that flow is the predictable and regular consequence of the action.

### Rav Shizvi's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שֵׁיזְבִי: וְכִי מָה בֵּין זֶה לְעוּקָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizvi raised an objection against this** *halakha*: **And what is** the difference **between this** case of a hole in the balcony **and** that of **a pit [*uka*]** used in a courtyard for waste water? The Sages rule in the next mishna below that one who digs a pit with a capacity of two *se'a* in a small courtyard that is less than four cubits may pour waste water into the courtyard on Shabbat, even if the pit was full before Shabbat. He need not be concerned that this will cause water to flow out of the courtyard into the public domain on Shabbat.

Rav Shizvi raises a challenge: *vechi mah bein zeh le-ukkah* — what is the difference between this case and the case of the pit (*uka*) that we will encounter in the Mishnah at the bottom of this daf? The Mishnah there deals with a small courtyard from which waste water threatens to run off into the *reshut harabim*. The remedy prescribed is to dig a pit in the courtyard: one pours the water into the pit, and even if the pit is already somewhat full and water will run off into the *reshut harabim* regardless, the act is permitted — because one's intention was to put the water into the pit, not into the *reshut harabim*, and the diversion of water from there is incidental.

The parallel seems to undercut Rabbah bar Rav Huna's ruling. Why should pouring water from the balcony be forbidden on the grounds that it will flow out into the *karmelis*? If we permit pouring into a pit in the courtyard even when we know the water will flow from the pit into the *reshut harabim*, the same logic should apply here: one's act is directed at the virtual *reshut hayachid* below, and whatever flows from there into the *karmelis* is incidental.

### Resolution: Absorption v…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי תָּיְימִי, וְהָנֵי לָא תָּיְימִי.
>
> **Translation:** **These** waters, which are poured out into the courtyard, **are** likely to be **absorbed** into the ground, and it is therefore uncertain that the water will indeed leave the courtyard. **But these,** the water poured through the hole into the body of water under the balcony, **will not be absorbed.** Therefore, one knows with certainty that the water will flow out beyond the permitted boundary.

The Gemara's answer is captured in the phrase *hani tayeimi vehani lo tayeimi* — the waters in the one case cease and the waters in the other do not. In the case of the courtyard pit, the fundamental design of the pit is that the water absorbs into the earth. Even if there is currently water standing in the pit, that is a temporary state; the ground will eventually absorb it. Therefore, the pit regularly and typically has available capacity, and one's act of pouring into it is genuinely an act of putting water into a receiving vessel that will hold it. By contrast, when water is poured down from the balcony onto the open surface of the Kinneret, there is no such absorption. The water flows outward continuously and immediately. There is never a state of rest within the virtual *reshut hayachid*. The water's movement out into the *karmelis* is not an occasional overflow but the constant, predictable reality. It is therefore treated as though one is pouring directly into the *karmelis*, even if technically the water passes through the *reshut hayachid* first.

### Alternative: No Distinct…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: לָא תֵּימָא לְמַלּאוֹת הוּא דִּשְׁרֵי, לִשְׁפּוֹךְ אָסוּר, אֶלָּא לִשְׁפּוֹךְ נָמֵי שְׁרֵי. אָמַר רַב שֵׁיזְבִי: פְּשִׁיטָא — הַיְינוּ עוּקָה! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי תָּיְימִי וְהָנֵי לָא תָּיְימִי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **Rabba bar Rav Huna** actually **said: You should not say that it is** only **drawing** water through the hole in the balcony **that is permitted,** while **pouring** waste water through it **is prohibited; rather, pouring** waste water through the hole **is also permitted. Rav Sheizvi said:** This is **obvious,** as **this is** exactly the same as the *halakha* of the **pit** discussed in the next mishna. The Gemara rejects this argument: It is necessary to specify both *halakhot*, **lest you say** there is a difference between the cases, as **these,** the water poured in the courtyard, **are** likely to be **absorbed** into the ground, **whereas these,** the water poured through the hole in the balcony, **will not be absorbed.** Rabba bar Rav Huna therefore **teaches us** that we do not distinguish between the two cases.

The Gemara then presents an alternative version of Rabbah bar Rav Huna's ruling: *lo teima lemalos hu deshari lishpoch assur* — do not say that drawing is permitted and pouring is forbidden. Rather, *lishpoch nami shari* — pouring too is permitted. Rav Sheshes remarks that this is obvious — *hainu uka*, this is simply the case of the pit. The Gemara then notes: one might have thought the distinction *hani tayeimi vehani lo tayeimi* applies — that the pit case is better because the water is eventually absorbed, and the balcony case is worse because it is not — and therefore, *kamashmalan*, the ruling comes to teach us that we do not draw that distinction. According to this second view, there is no meaningful difference between drawing and pouring, and the balcony case is treated the same as the pit: once the act is directed at a valid receptacle within the *reshut hayachid*, what subsequently happens to the water is not the actor's concern.

## Two Balconies & Ownership

### Rav Huna on Proximity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּסְמוּכָה, אֲבָל בְּמוּפְלֶגֶת — עֶלְיוֹנָה מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said** that **Rav said: They taught** that one balcony renders it prohibited for residents of the other **only where** the one balcony **is near** the other, i.e., horizontally within four handbreadths. **But** if each balcony is **separated** by four handbreadths from the other, so that the residents of each balcony can use the other only by means of the air, the residents of **the upper** balcony **are permitted** to draw water, while the residents of the lower one are prohibited from doing so.

The Mishnah now introduces the case of two balconies (*gezustraos*), one above the other, where the upper balcony has constructed the proper apparatus and the lower has not. The Gemara had previously established that in such a configuration, because the lower balcony can access the upper one — the occupants can come up, or perhaps jump across — the territory is considered contested, and neither may use the well without an *eiruv*.

Rav Huna, citing Rav, rules: *lo shanu ella bismucha* — the ruling that the lower balcony creates a problem for the upper applies only when the two are within four *tefachim* of each other horizontally. If the lower balcony is more than four *tefachim* away horizontally (*mupleges*), the upper one is unaffected and may use its apparatus freely. This reflects the principle Rav articulated elsewhere: *ein adam oser al chaveiro derech avir* — one cannot restrict another's domain through access that requires traversal of open air alone. A gap of four or more *tefachim* is considered effectively impassable for practical purposes, and therefore the lower balcony's occupants are not considered to have meaningful access to the upper one.

### Gezel & Churvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא, וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: יֵשׁ גָּזֵל בְּשַׁבָּת. וְחוּרְבָּה מַחֲזִיר לַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya said, and Rav Yosef said** that **Rabbi Oshaya said:** The *halakha* of **stealing applies to Shabbat** domains, **and a ruin must be returned to its owner.**

A new principle is now introduced, cited in two versions attributing it either to Rabbi Chiya or to Rabbi Oshaya (with Rabbah citing Rav in one version and Rabbi Yosef citing Rabbi Oshaya in another): *yesh gezel beshabbos, vechurva machzir lebaalim* — there is (as it were) *gezel* on *Shabbat*, and a ruin returns to its owners.

What does this mean? Rashi explains the context as follows. Imagine two courtyards — belonging to A and B respectively — without a shared *eiruv*, and between them an abandoned lot (*churva*) belonging to B. In practice, during the week, B allows A to use the lot. On *Shabbat*, can A carry within it as though it were part of A's courtyard? This question engages the intersection of *Choshen Mishpat* and *hilchos eiruvin*: does A's habitual use — their quasi-possession — give them enough of a foothold to treat the lot as part of their *reshut* for purposes of *Shabbat* carrying?

The phrase *yesh gezel beshabbos* — meaning, as the Gemara will clarify, *yesh din gezel beshabbos* — suggests that the laws of *gezel* remain operative on *Shabbat*. This is not to say that one is prosecuted for *gezel* in a monetary court; rather, it means that even on *Shabbat*, A's use of the lot is not treated as a valid acquisition (*kniyas*) of the space. The lot is still legally B's, and A's informal use does not transform it into part of A's domain. And *churva machzir lebaalim* — the ruin returns to its true owner — completes the point: regardless of who is practically using it, it belongs to B, and therefore A cannot carry in it without a proper *eiruv*.

### Resolving Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא: אָמְרַתְּ ״יֵשׁ גָּזֵל בְּשַׁבָּת״ — אַלְמָא קָנְיָא, ״וְחוּרְבָּה מַחֲזִיר לִבְעָלִים״ — אַלְמָא לָא קָנְיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara registers surprise: **This** ruling **itself is difficult,** i.e., it is self-contradictory. **You** first **said** that the *halakha* of **stealing applies to Shabbat** domains, which at this point is understood by the Gemara as referring to the following case: A person's house adjoins the ruin of another, and he observes that the ruin has been left deserted by its owner. If this person uses the ruin during the week, on Shabbat he may treat it as though it were his own, by carrying objects from his own house into the ruin and vice versa. **From here** we can infer that a stolen place **is acquired** for the purpose of Shabbat domains, although it does not belong to the person for other purposes. However, you subsequently said that **a ruin must be returned to its owner,** and **from here** we can infer that a ruin **is not acquired** for the purpose of Shabbat domains by the person who used it during the week, and therefore he may not carry objects from his own house into the ruin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: יֵשׁ דִּין גָּזֵל בְּשַׁבָּת, כֵּיצַד — דְּחוּרְבָּה מַחֲזִיר לַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: We should not understand this statement as suggested above, but rather **this is** what Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Oshaya **are saying: The *halakha* of** returning **stolen** property **applies to Shabbat** domains. **How so?** This means **that a ruin must be returned to its owner.** In other words, one who uses a ruin during the week does not acquire it even for the purpose of Shabbat domains.

At first glance, *yesh gezel beshabbos* and *churva machzir lebaalim* appear to contradict each other. The first phrase seems to say A acquires the lot (*kanya*), the second seems to say A does not (*lo kanya*). The Gemara resolves this: *hachi kaamar* — what it really means is *yesh din gezel beshabbos*, the *din* (law) of *gezel* applies. That is, A's use of the lot is considered a form of *gezel* — in the sense that it is not a genuine acquisition — and therefore *churva machzir lebaalim*, the lot is treated as reverting to its true owner, B. The two phrases are thus consistent: the law of *gezel* applies on *Shabbat*, meaning the informal use creates no valid ownership, and so the lot returns, in legal contemplation, to its actual owner. A cannot carry in it as though it were part of A's courtyard.

The introduction of this principle represents a significant conceptual shift. Up to this point, the entire analysis of contested space had been framed around questions of practical access — who can reach the space, how easily, at what cost. Now the Gemara introduces the criterion of ownership and asserts that, in at least some contexts, ownership overrides practical access entirely.

### Balconies & Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה, וּמוֹתְבִינַן אַשְּׁמַעְתִּין: וְכֵן שְׁתֵּי גְזוּזְטְרָאוֹת זוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִזּוֹ וְכוּ׳. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ יֵשׁ דִּין גָּזֵל בְּשַׁבָּת, אַמַּאי אֲסוּרוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: And we** ourselves **raised an objection** against **our** own **teaching,** as we learned in the mishna. **And likewise,** if there are **two balconies, one above the other,** they prohibit one another. **But if you say** that **the *halakha*** against **stealing applies on Shabbat,** which means one may not use the domain of another, and he acquires no rights to it if he does so, **why** are the two balconies **prohibited** from using it. The lower one has no right to make use of the upper one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁעָשׂוּ מְחִיצָה בְּשׁוּתָּפוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: We are dealing here with** a situation **where, for example,** the residents of the upper balcony and the residents of the lower balcony **jointly erected a partition** for the upper balcony. Consequently, the residents of the lower balcony share the right to use it with the residents of the upper one.

The Gemara now applies this principle back to the case of the two balconies: *umotvinan ashmatin* — we challenge the ruling just stated. If *yesh din gezel beshabbos* — if ownership is what matters — why does the Mishnah say the lower balcony's presence restricts the upper? Even if the lower occupants regularly access the upper balcony's apparatus, that access is merely de facto use. It does not give them ownership. The upper balcony belongs to its owner. Why should the lower occupants' use create a restriction on what is unambiguously the upper owner's property?

Rav Sheshes answers: *hacha bemai askinan kegon she'asu mechitza beshutfus* — we are speaking of a case where the lower balcony's occupants contributed to the construction of the walls that enable the apparatus to function. The walls are therefore jointly owned. It is not merely that the lower occupants use the upper space; they have a genuine proprietary interest in the walls that make the space valid. This co-ownership of the walls is what creates the contested status.

This is admittedly a somewhat *heichi timtza* — a technically constrained — answer. The Mishnah itself does not mention shared construction of the walls, and one would have expected it to do so if that were the operative fact. But the Gemara accepts this as the reading of the Mishnah necessary to reconcile it with the principle of *yesh din gezel beshabbos*.

### Challenge: Own Balcony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כִּי עָשׂוּ לַתַּחְתּוֹנָה נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so,** in a case **where they erected** a separate partition **for the lower** balcony, the residents of the upper balcony should **likewise** be prohibited to use it. As the residents of the lower one are partners in the upper one, they should prohibit its residents from using it.

The Gemara now raises a difficulty: *ihachi ki asu letachtona nami*? If the problem in the Mishnah is that the lower balcony has co-ownership of the walls, why does the Mishnah imply that the problem is resolved when the lower balcony constructs its own apparatus? The straightforward understanding of the Mishnah is that once each party has made their own setup, there is no competition — A will use A's apparatus and B will use B's — and therefore both may use freely. That reasoning makes sense if the issue is practical competition. But if B has a proprietary stake in the walls of A's apparatus, then even after B makes their own, B still has a co-ownership interest in A's walls. Why should B's making of their own apparatus dissolve that ownership claim?

### Renunciation of Interest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּעָשׂוּ לַתַּחְתּוֹנָה — גַּלּוֹיֵי גַּלִּי דַּעְתַּהּ דַּאֲנָא בַּהֲדָךְ לָא נִיחָא לִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Since they erected** a separate partition **for the lower** balcony, **they** each thereby **revealed their intention** to the residents of the upper balcony **that: It is not my wish to be** partners **with you.** Consequently, they no longer prohibit the residents of the upper balcony from using it.

The Gemara's answer: *kivan de'asu letachtona, gilluy gallia deitei de-hana bahadach lo nichle* — once the lower party constructs their own apparatus, they have made a clear and manifest declaration of intent (*gilluy da'at*) that they are no longer interested in the upper one. By investing in their own facility, they effectively renounce whatever interest or claim they had in the upper party's walls. The co-ownership is thereby dissolved by the act of construction itself.

The broader significance of this entire discussion should not be lost. The Gemara spent considerable time — the better part of the preceding week's learning — developing a framework based entirely on practical access and competition. Now, at the conclusion of that discussion, the Gemara introduces ownership as an independent and overriding criterion. A space that is owned by one party belongs to that party for purposes of *Shabbat* carrying, regardless of who else uses it in practice. This reframing requires a complete re-reading of the Mishnah: what appeared to be a question of who uses the balcony is revealed to be a question of who owns the walls. Ownership, not use, is the determinative criterion — at least where the ownership question is real.

## Water Disposal & Pits

### Mishnah on Pits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חָצֵר שֶׁהִיא פְּחוּתָה מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — אֵין שׁוֹפְכִין בְּתוֹכָהּ מַיִם בְּשַׁבָּת, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן עָשׂוּ לָהּ עוּקָה מַחְזֶקֶת סָאתַיִם מִן הַנֶּקֶב וּלְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a courtyard that is less than four cubits** by four cubits in area, **one may not pour** waste **water into it on Shabbat, unless a pit was fashioned** to receive the water, and the pit **holds two *se'a*** in volume **from its edge below.**

We now arrive at the final Mishnah of the eighth *perek*, which deals with the disposal of waste water. It is worth noting, as a preliminary matter, that the entire subsequent discussion proceeds without any concern about *zoreia* — the prohibition against sowing — even though pouring water onto a dirt surface might seem to implicate agricultural *melachos*. In practice, if one is pouring onto bare, hard-packed dirt in a courtyard where nothing is growing, there is no issue of *zoreia*. The concern about adding water to loose particulate matter — which might constitute *lisha* (kneading) by binding separate particles into a unified mass — does not apply when the surface is hard earth rather than sand or loose soil. The Mishnah's silence on these concerns is therefore understandable.

The Mishnah states: *chatzer shepichusa me'arba amos ein shofchin betocha mayim beShabbos* — a courtyard that is less than four *amos* may not have water poured into it on *Shabbat*. The reasoning is as follows. When a courtyard is of sufficient size, one pours waste water into the courtyard and the bulk of it is absorbed into the ground; whatever small amount runs off into the *reshut harabim* is entirely unintended and statistically improbable. But when the courtyard is very small — less than four *amos* in the relevant dimension — the water will almost certainly run from the courtyard into the *reshut harabim*, and even though one did not intend to pour into the *reshut harabim*, it is treated rabbinically as though one effectively did.

One might ask whether this should depend on how many households are disposing water and how much water each uses. The Gemara does not pursue that line of analysis; it treats the four-*amah* threshold as a fixed standard. In any case, a courtyard of less than four by four *amos* — roughly six feet by six feet — is unlikely to serve many households to begin with.

If the courtyard is too small, the Mishnah prescribes a remedy: *ella im kein asu lah uka machazeket sasayim* — one must construct a pit (*uka*) capable of holding two *se'ah* of water. Two *se'ah* is assumed to be the normal daily water disposal of a single household. According to Rav Chaim Na'eh, a *se'ah* is 8.29 liters, so two *se'ah* is approximately 16.58 liters — equivalent to roughly eight of the standard two-liter bottles. This is not a particularly large volume; the cavity in question would be perhaps a foot by a foot by a foot in rough approximation.

### Pit Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין מִבַּחוּץ, בֵּין מִבִּפְנִים. אֶלָּא שֶׁמִּבַּחוּץ — צָרִיךְ לִקְמוֹר, מִבִּפְנִים — אֵין צָרִיךְ לִקְמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** This *halakha* applies **whether** the pit was fashioned **outside** the courtyard **or** whether it was dug **inside** the courtyard itself. The **only** difference is as follows: **If** the pit was dug **outside** in the adjoining public domain, **it is necessary to arch over** it, so that the water will not flow into the public domain. If it was dug **inside** the courtyard, **it is not necessary to arch over** it.

The Mishnah specifies: *asu lah uka machazeket sasayim min hanekev ulemattah* — the pit must be capable of holding two *se'ah* measured from the opening (*nekev*) downward. Even if the pit currently contains standing water that has not yet been absorbed, as long as the cavity itself — the total volume from the rim downward — is sufficient to hold two *se'ah*, the condition is met. The key measurement is the size of the cavity, not the available empty space at any given moment.

The Mishnah further specifies: *bein mibachutz bein mibipnim* — the pit may be located either inside the courtyard or outside it. However, *ella shebachutz tzarich likmor* — if the pit is located outside the courtyard, in the *reshut harabim*, it must be covered (*kamur*), whereas a pit inside the courtyard does not need to be covered.

This provision — that a pit in the *reshut harabim* must be covered — requires explanation. The most obvious scenario is that one situates the pit inside the courtyard, near or adjacent to the boundary with the *reshut harabim*, and the cavity extends underground beneath the *reshut harabim*. The water enters the pit from within the courtyard and drains underground into the larger cavity beneath the public domain. If left open — that is, if the pit were uncovered — it would appear as though one were pouring directly into the *reshut harabim*, since the visible opening in the *reshut harabim* would obviously be receiving water. Covering the pit resolves this appearance problem: from the surface of the *reshut harabim*, nothing is visible, and whatever occurs below the surface is hidden.

A second and related explanation, attributed to Rashi as cited by the *Beis Meir*, focuses on the status of the pit as a *makom patur*. A depression in the ground that is more than three *tefachim* deep and less than four by four *tefachim* in area constitutes a *makom patur* — an exempt space that is neither a *reshut hayachid* nor a *reshut harabim* and from which carrying is Biblically neutral. If such a pit is located in the *reshut harabim*, it may serve as an intermediate stopping point for the water, thereby eliminating the concern about pouring from a *reshut hayachid* into a *reshut harabim*. However, if the pit is uncovered, debris from the *reshut harabim* — stones, dirt, refuse — may fall in and gradually raise the floor of the pit to less than three *tefachim* below ground level. At that point, the pit loses its *makom patur* status and becomes part of the *reshut harabim* around it. Covering the pit prevents this accumulation and ensures that the *makom patur* status is maintained.

Both explanations have merit, and it is somewhat striking that the *rishonim* do not elaborate more fully on this point. There is also a broader conceptual question: a covered underground tunnel or cavity — to use a modern analogy, something like a utility vault beneath a public street — is it considered part of the *reshut harabim* at all, given that it is covered and has no direct public access from above? If access to a space is primarily from below or from the side rather than from the surface of the *reshut harabim*, the question of its domain classification becomes genuinely complex. The covering of the pit may work precisely by severing any visual or functional connection to the *reshut harabim* above, thereby allowing the cavity to function independently as a *makom patur*.

In any event, the principle is ironic and worth noting: the Mishnah effectively prefers that one construct a drainage channel that goes underground beneath the *reshut harabim* rather than simply pour water into a large open courtyard and let it run off naturally. The former, with appropriate covering, is permitted; the latter, if the courtyard is too small, is not.

### R. Eliezer ben Yaakov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: בִּיב שֶׁהוּא קָמוּר אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — שׁוֹפְכִים לְתוֹכוֹ מַיִם בַּשַּׁבָּת. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ גַּג אוֹ חָצֵר מֵאָה אַמָּה — לֹא יִשְׁפּוֹךְ עַל פִּי הַבִּיב, אֲבָל שׁוֹפֵךְ הוּא לַגַּג, וְהַמַּיִם יוֹרְדִין לַבִּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** In the case of a drainage **ditch whose** first **four cubits are arched over in the public domain, one may pour** waste **water into it on Shabbat. And the Rabbis say: Even** if **a roof or a courtyard** is **a hundred cubits** in area, **one may not pour** water directly **onto the mouth of the** drainage **ditch. However, he may pour** it **upon the roof,** from which **the water spills into the drain** of its own accord.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov now offers a different standard: *biv hakamurarba amos bireshus harabim shofchin lesocho mayim beShabbos* — a drainage pipe (*biv*) that is covered and extends four *amos* into the *reshut harabim* suffices to permit pouring. On its face, this sounds similar to the previous ruling about covered pits, but Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov specifies four *amos* rather than the two-*se'ah* standard of the Mishnah's first ruling. Four *amos* is a substantially larger space than a cavity holding two *se'ah* — the latter being roughly the size of a half-*amah* cube, whereas four by four *amos* is a sizable expanse. The Gemara will analyze what lies behind this discrepancy.

### Chachamim on Drainage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ גַּג אוֹ חָצֵר מֵאָה אַמָּה — לֹא יִשְׁפּוֹךְ עַל פִּי הַבִּיב, אֲבָל שׁוֹפֵךְ הוּא לַגַּג, וְהַמַּיִם יוֹרְדִין לַבִּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages say that **even if one has a roof or a courtyard a hundred *amos* in size, one may not pour water directly into the drainage pipe. However,** one **may pour the water onto the roof itself, and the water will then run on its own into the drainage pipe.**

The Sages (*Chachamim*) then state: *afilu gag o chatzer meiah amah lo yishpoch al pi habiiv* — even if one has a roof or a courtyard a hundred *amos* in size, one may not pour water directly into the drainage pipe. This is because the drainage pipe channels water directly into the *reshut harabim*, and pouring into the mouth of the pipe is functionally equivalent to pouring into the *reshut harabim* regardless of the distance the water travels before reaching its outlet. The mere fact that there is a hundred-*amah* expanse of roof between the person pouring and the street does not matter; the act is directed at the pipe, and the pipe is directed at the *reshut harabim*.

However, *aval shofech hu legag vehamayim yordim labiiv* — one may pour the water onto the roof itself, and the water will then run on its own into the drainage pipe. As long as one's act is directed at the roof surface — a valid domain — and the subsequent flow into the pipe is incidental and not the immediate result of one's pour, the Sages permit it.

### Courtyard & Portico

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶחָצֵר וְהָאַכְסַדְרָה מִצְטָרְפִין לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. וְכֵן שְׁתֵּי דְיוֹטָאוֹת זוֹ כְּנֶגֶד זוֹ, מִקְצָתָן עָשׂוּ עוּקָה וּמִקְצָתָן לֹא עָשׂוּ עוּקָה, אֶת שֶׁעָשׂוּ עוּקָה — מוּתָּרִין, אֶת שֶׁלֹּא עָשׂוּ עוּקָה — אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **A courtyard and a portico,** a roofed but unwalled structure in front of a house, **combine for the four cubits** by virtue of which it is permitted to pour water even into a courtyard that lacks a pit. **And likewise,** with regard to **two upper stories [*deyotaot*], one opposite the other** in the same small courtyard, if the residents of **one** of them **fashioned a pit** in the courtyard, **and** the residents of **the other did not fashion a pit, those who fashioned a pit are permitted** to pour their waste water into the courtyard, whereas **those who did not fashion a pit are prohibited** to do so.

The Mishnah then adds two further provisions. First: *hachatzer veha'achsadra mitztarfin learba amos* — a courtyard and an adjacent *achsadra* (a covered portico or colonnade) may be combined to meet the four-*amah* threshold. If neither alone is four *amos* but together they total the requisite area, the combination suffices.

Second: the Mishnah addresses the case of two balconies facing each other, some of whose occupants have made a pit and some have not — *miktzasan asu uka umiktzasan lo asu uka*. Those who made the pit may pour; those who did not may not. This is relatively straightforward: the entitlement to pour water into the courtyard is contingent on having satisfied the pit requirement, and it is determined on an individual basis. One party's compliance does not extend to cover another.

## Intent vs. Absorption

### Rava: Intent & Dust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבָּה: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָדָם עָשׂוּי לְהִסְתַּפֵּק סָאתַיִם מַיִם בְּכׇל יוֹם, בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — אָדָם רוֹצֶה לְזַלְּפָן, פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע — שׁוֹפְכָן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that a courtyard four by four cubits in area does not require a pit? **Rabba said: Because a person ordinarily uses two *se'a* of water a day,** and with regard to a courtyard **of** at least **four cubits** by four cubits, **a person wants to sprinkle** the water on the ground to prevent any dust from rising. Consequently, even if in practice the water does flow out of the courtyard, this effect is not necessarily his intention. But if the courtyard is **less than four cubits** by four cubits in area, **one** simply **pours** the water out, as the place is not fit for sprinkling. Therefore, **if one fashioned a pit, it is permitted** to pour out water; but if **not, it is prohibited** to do so, as one certainly intends for the water to flow outside.

The Gemara now probes the underlying rationale for the four-*amah* standard. Rava explains: *adam asuy l'histapek sa'atayim mayim bechol yom* — a person typically uses two *se'ah* of water per day that require disposal, *u've'arba amot adam rotzeh le-zalfan* — and if one's courtyard is four by four *amos*, one actually wants the water to remain there. Why? Because a dirt courtyard generates dust, and one periodically sprinkles water on the ground precisely to suppress the dust. In a courtyard of four by four *amos*, the water serves a useful purpose and the owner genuinely wishes to retain it. Therefore, disposing of water into such a courtyard is not an act of getting rid of it; it is an act of using it. There is no concern that it will run into the *reshut harabim* in a problematic way, because the owner is content for it to remain.

In a courtyard smaller than four by four *amos*, by contrast, either the dust problem is minimal or the volume of water becomes excessive for the available surface area, and the owner simply wants to get rid of the water. The act of disposal is therefore directed at removing the water from one's domain, and the likelihood of it running into the *reshut harabim* becomes the operative concern. According to Rava, then, the four-*amah* threshold is fundamentally a matter of *intent*: does the owner want the water in the courtyard or not?

### R. Zeira: Absorption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — תָּיְימִי, פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — לָא תָּיְימִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira** offered a different reason and **said:** In a courtyard of **four cubits** by four cubits, the water is likely to be **absorbed** into the ground. If it is **less than four cubits** in size, the water **will not be absorbed** but will flow out.

Rabbi Zeira disagrees. The four-*amah* standard has nothing to do with whether the owner wants the water. It is a question of whether the courtyard can physically absorb the water. *Arba amot ta'amei* — four *amos* is the threshold because a surface of that area has sufficient capacity to absorb two *se'ah* of water. *Pachot me'arba amot lo taimei* — less than that, it will not absorb fully, and the water will inevitably run off. For Rabbi Zeira, the determinative consideration is physical reality — the absorption capacity of the ground — not the owner's subjective intention.

### Abye's Nafka Mina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֲרִיךְ וְקַטִּין אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical **difference between** these two explanations? **Abaye said: There is** a difference **between them** with regard to a **long and narrow** courtyard. As the area of this courtyard is also sixteen square cubits, it likewise absorbs the water. Rabbi Zeira would therefore rule that it does not require a pit. However, as this courtyard is not in need of sprinkling, it requires a pit according to Rabba.

Abaye identifies the practical difference (*nafka mina*) between the two positions: *arikh v'katin ika beinaihu* — a courtyard that is long and narrow rather than square. If the operative criterion is total area (Rabbi Zeira's view), then a courtyard with a total area of sixteen square *amos* — whether it is four by four or eight by two — has the same absorption capacity, and the *halacha* should be the same in both cases. If, however, the criterion is whether the owner wants to sprinkle the water and keep it in the courtyard (Rava's view), then a long narrow courtyard, even if its area equals sixteen square *amos*, is not the type of space for which one would sprinkle water to suppress dust. Rashi notes simply that a long narrow space is not suited for sprinkling, though the reason is not fully articulated.

The Gemara then attempts to prove the matter from the Mishnah and a *baraita*. The Mishnah that combines a courtyard with a *portico* seems to favor Rabbi Zeira: combining two spaces of different shapes to achieve a threshold of sixteen square *amos* is a matter of total area, not square dimensions. Rav Zeira defends Rava by explaining that the *portico* runs the full length of the courtyard, so when combined they produce a shape that is four by four — a proper square — and therefore no challenge to Rava's position results.

The *baraita*, on the other hand, explicitly states: *chatzer she'ein ba arba amot al arba amot* — a courtyard that is not four *amos* by four *amos*, using the standard formulation that specifies dimensions in both directions. This seems to support Rava, for whom proportions matter, and to challenge Rabbi Zeira, for whom total area is all that counts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: חָצֵר וְאַכְסַדְרָה מִצְטָרְפִין לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי זֵירָא נִיחָא, אֶלָּא לְרַבָּה קַשְׁיָא! תַּרְגְּמָא רַבִּי זֵירָא אַלִּיבָּא דְרַבָּה בְּאַכְסַדְרָה מְהַלֶּכֶת עַל פְּנֵי כָּל הֶחָצֵר כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **A courtyard and a portico combine for the** requisite **four cubits,** permitted the pouring of water into a courtyard that lacks a pit. The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Zeira,** this **works out well,** as the total area is large enough to absorb the water. **However, according to Rabba it is difficult,** for when the courtyard is joined with the portico it is no longer in the shape of a square, and it is therefore unfit for sprinkling. **Rabbi Zeira explained** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabba,** by saying that it is referring **to a portico that extends along the entire courtyard,** so that it adds to its width alone. Consequently, the courtyard and the portico together form a square of four by four cubits, an area that is fit for sprinkling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: חָצֵר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — אֵין שׁוֹפְכִין לְתוֹכָהּ מַיִם בְּשַׁבָּת. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבָּה נִיחָא, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי זֵירָא קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* that can decide this dispute. With regard to **a courtyard that is not four cubits by four cubits** in area, **one may not pour water into it on Shabbat.** The Gemara assumes that the *baraita*, which teaches that one may pour water only into a courtyard that it is four by four cubits, is precise in its wording. **Granted, according to Rabba,** this **works out well,** as he maintains that it is prohibited to pour water into a long and narrow courtyard. **However, according to Rabbi Zeira,** who maintains that the critical factor is the area of the courtyard, this is **difficult.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי זֵירָא: הָא מַנִּי רַבָּנַן הִיא, וּמַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Zeira** can **say to you:** In accordance with **whose** opinion is **this** *baraita*? **It** is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis** at the end of the mishna, who maintain that the area of the courtyard is of no importance, **whereas our** unattributed **mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov,** according to whom the area is the decisive factor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי דּוּחְקֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא לְאוֹקֹמַהּ לְמַתְנִיתִין כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב? אָמַר רָבָא: מַתְנִיתִין קְשִׁיתֵיהּ, מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״חָצֵר שֶׁהִיא פְּחוּתָה״, לִיתְנֵי: ״חָצֵר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what forced Rabbi Zeira to establish the mishna in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov? Rava said: The mishna was difficult for him. Why** did the *tanna* **specifically teach** his ruling with respect to **a courtyard that is less** than four cubits, from which it can be inferred that if it has an area of four by four cubits it is permitted to pour water, even if it is not square in shape? **Let** the mishna **teach: A courtyard that is not four cubits by four cubits,** i.e., one that is not square shaped, even if it includes an area of sixteen square cubits.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב הִיא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, shouldn't one conclude from this** argument that the unattributed section of the mishna is in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov?** The Gemara summarizes: Indeed, **conclude from this** that it is so.

### Rava's Proof

Rava then offers a proof from the language of the Mishnah itself. The Mishnah uses the phrasing *chatzer shepachuta me'arba amos* — a courtyard less than four — rather than the standard *chatzer she'ein ba arba amot al arba amot* (a courtyard that is not four by four). This is notable. If the Mishnah intended to require minimum dimensions in each direction — a proper square of at least four by four — it would have used the familiar formulation. Instead, it simply says "less than four," which most naturally refers to total magnitude rather than specific dimensions in each direction. From this, Rava argues, one may infer that the Mishnah is concerned with area rather than proportions, and therefore the Mishnah aligns with Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's absorption-based approach.

### Drainage Pipe Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: בִּיב הַקָּמוּר אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — שׁוֹפְכִים לְתוֹכוֹ מַיִם בַּשַּׁבָּת. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ גַּג אוֹ חָצֵר מֵאָה אַמָּה — לֹא יִשְׁפּוֹךְ עַל פִּי הַבִּיב, אֲבָל שׁוֹפֵךְ הוּא לַגַּג, וְהַמַּיִם יוֹרְדִין לַבִּיב.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** If the first four cubits of a drainage **ditch were arched over** in the public domain, it is permitted to pour waste water into it on Shabbat. However, the Rabbis say: One may pour water only upon the roof, from which it will spill into the drain of its own accord.

The Gemara draws together the discussion by connecting it explicitly to the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *Chachamim* regarding pouring into the drainage pipe. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov permits pouring directly into a covered drainage pipe of four *amos* in the *reshut harabim*, because for him the operative question is whether there is sufficient capacity for absorption. Once there is enough space — whether in the ground of a courtyard or in the volume of an underground pipe — absorption is theoretically possible, and that suffices. It does not matter that one is pouring directly into the mouth of the pipe; what matters is the physical reality of what can be absorbed.

The *Chachamim*, by contrast, prohibit pouring directly into the drainage pipe even when the roof is a hundred *amos* long. For them, the operative question is intent and the direction of one's act. Pouring into the pipe is pouring toward the *reshut harabim*, because the pipe's function is to carry water there. The theoretical possibility of absorption along the way is irrelevant to how we classify the act. Only by pouring onto the roof — a space where one can genuinely wish for the water to remain — does the act become permitted.

The Gemara thus elegantly maps the abstract dispute between Rava and Rabbi Zeira onto the concrete disagreement between the *Chachamim* and Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov: the *Chachamim* hold that intent and the direction of one's action are determinative (Rava's approach), while Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds that physical absorption capacity is what matters (Rabbi Zeira's approach).

## Rainy vs. Dry Season

### Baraita: Rainy Season

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, אֲבָל בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים — שׁוֹפֵךְ וְשׁוֹנֶה וְאֵינוֹ נִמְנָע.
>
> **Translation:** A *tanna* **taught: In what** case **is this statement,** that a pit is required, **said? In the summer, but in the rainy season, one may pour and repeat, and** he need **not hold back.**

A *baraita* is cited that introduces a significant relaxation of all the foregoing requirements: *bameh devarim amurim* — when do all these requirements apply? *Bi'ymei hachama* — in the summer, the dry season. *Aval bi'ymei hageshamim* — but in the rainy season, *shofech veshoneh ve'eino nimna* — one may pour as much water as one wishes and need not refrain at all, without any pit requirement whatsoever.

### Rava Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבָּה: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָדָם רוֹצֶה שֶׁיִּבָּלְעוּ מַיִם בִּמְקוֹמָן. אִי מִשּׁוּם קִלְקוּל חֲצֵירוֹ — הָא מִיקַּלְקְלָא וְקָיְימָא. וְאִי מִשּׁוּם גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ ״צִנּוֹרוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי מְקַלֵּחַ מַיִם״ — סְתָם צִנּוֹרוֹת מְקַלְּחִים הֵם.
>
> **Translation:** What is the reason? Rava said: A person is equally willing for the water to be absorbed on the spot. If you say he wants the waste water to flow out into the public domain because he is concerned about spoiling his courtyard, it is already spoiled by the rainwater. If you say it should be prohibited due to a decree lest people say that so-and-so's gutter is flowing with water on Shabbat, gutters ordinarily flow with water in the rainy season, as stated above.

Rava explains the reason for this blanket dispensation in the rainy season along two lines. First, the concern that one wants to remove the water from the courtyard — because a small courtyard becomes too wet and the owner wishes to expel the water — dissolves entirely during the rainy season, when the courtyard is already saturated from rainfall. One does not particularly care whether the additional water stays or goes. Second, the concern that it will appear as though one is deliberately sending water into the *reshut harabim* — that observers will say "his drainage pipe is discharging into the street" — also dissolves, because in the rainy season all drainage pipes are discharging into the street. Water in the *reshut harabim* is unremarkable; it is there from the rain regardless. Neither the subjective intent concern nor the objective appearance concern is operative in the rainy season.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וַהֲרֵי שׁוֹפְכִין, דְּאָדָם רוֹצֶה שֶׁיִּבָּלְעוּ, וְקָתָנֵי ״לֹא יִשְׁפּוֹךְ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** With regard to **waste** water poured into a drainage ditch, **that a person wants it to be absorbed** in the ditch itself, rather than flow out, **and** yet the mishna **teaches** that **one may not pour** water into the ditch.

Abaye challenges Rava's explanation. Rava said that in normal circumstances, when one has a four-by-four courtyard and pours water in, the act is permitted because the owner wants the water there — he wants it to suppress the dust. If so, one could ask: even when the courtyard is too small, why should overuse be a concern? In the Mishnah's framework, even with a pit, there is a maximum amount one may pour corresponding to the pit's capacity. Why does that limit exist at all, if in principle one simply wants the water to stay in the courtyard and absorb?

Rava's answer is implicit in the structure: in the summer, when a courtyard is less than four *amos*, the owner does not want the water there — the space is too small and it becomes a nuisance — so the water runs out, and that creates the appearance problem. The pit is the prescribed solution, and its size (two *se'ah*) is calibrated to the maximum reasonable daily disposal. In the rainy season, as Rava explained, neither the desire to expel nor the appearance problem applies, and therefore no pit is necessary at all.

### Rav Nachman's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, עוּקָה מַחֲזִיק סָאתַיִם — נוֹתְנִין לוֹ סָאתַיִם, מַחֲזִיק סְאָה — נוֹתְנִין לוֹ סְאָה. בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, מַחֲזִיק סָאתַיִם — נוֹתְנִין לוֹ סָאתַיִם, סְאָה — אֵין נוֹתְנִין לוֹ כָּל עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: In the rainy season,** with regard to **a pit that holds two *se'a*, we grant him** permission to pour **two *se'a*** of water into it. If it **holds** only **one *se'a*, we grant him one *se'a*.** However, **in the summer,** if the pit has a capacity of **two *se'a*, we grant him two *se'a*;** if it holds only **one *se'a*, we do not grant him** permission to pour any water **at all.**

Rav Nachman takes a more measured position. He does not concede that the rainy season eliminates all requirements; he maintains that even then some standard of proportionality applies. *Ukma machzik sasayim notnim lo sasayim* — if the pit holds two *se'ah*, one may pour in two *se'ah* of water. *Machzik seah* — if the pit holds only one *se'ah* — normally, in the dry season, a pit of only one *se'ah* would be entirely insufficient and one could pour nothing at all. But in the rainy season, that smaller pit suffices: *notnim lo seah* — one may pour in one *se'ah*. The leniency of the rainy season manifests not in eliminating the pit requirement altogether but in allowing smaller pits to suffice proportionally. 

The inverse question naturally arises: in the dry season, if one has a pit of only one *se'ah*, should one at least be permitted to pour in that one *se'ah*? No, rules Rav Nachman: *gezeirah dilma ati leiten lah sasayim* — we are concerned that one will pour the full two *se'ah* of the typical daily usage, exceeding the pit's capacity. In the dry season, where the risks are real and the standards are strict, we do not permit partial use of an undersized pit, lest one become accustomed to using it and eventually overuse it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה נָמֵי, מַחֲזִיק סְאָה נִיתֵּיב לֵיהּ סְאָה! גְּזֵרָה דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לִיתֵּן לֵיהּ סָאתַיִם. אִי הָכִי בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים נָמֵי לִיגְזוֹר! הָתָם מַאי נֵיחוּשׁ לַהּ? אִי מִשּׁוּם קִלְקוּל — הָא מִיקַּלְקְלָא וְקָיְימָא. אִי מִשּׁוּם גְּזֵירָה ״שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ צִנּוֹרוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי מְקַלֵּחַ מַיִם״ — סְתָם צִנּוֹרוֹת מְקַלְּחִין הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **In the hot season as well,** if the pit **holds one *se'a*, let us grant him one *se'a*,** for if he pours only this amount of water, it will not flow out into the public domain. The Gemara answers: This is prohibited due to **a decree lest he come to put two *se'a* into it.** The Gemara asks: **If so, in the rainy season let us also apply the** same **preventive measure.** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the rainy season, there is no reason to prohibit the practice, for if one pours more water into a pit than it can take, **about what need we be concerned? If** you say he wants the waste water to flow out into the public domain **because** he is concerned about **spoiling** his courtyard, **it is already spoiled** by the rainwater. **If** you say it should be prohibited due to **a decree lest** people **say** that **so-and-so's gutter is flowing with water** on Shabbat, **gutters ordinarily gush** with water in the rainy season, as stated above.

And why, Abaye asks, do we not apply the same precautionary logic in the rainy season — why not prohibit even the proportional use of a small pit lest one overuse it? The Gemara returns to the same dual rationale: *ee mishum kilkul chatzeiro, ha mekulkal veka'ei* — if one were concerned about the courtyard being ruined by excess water, the courtyard is already ruined by the rain. *Ee mishum gezeirah shema yomru tzinoro shel ploni mekaleach mayim, stam tzinorot mekalachim hem* — if one were concerned about the appearance of one's pipe discharging into the street, all pipes are discharging in the rainy season. There is simply no occasion for either concern to take hold.

### Abaye: Large Pits OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הִילְכָּךְ, אֲפִילּוּ כּוֹר וַאֲפִילּוּ כּוֹרַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Therefore,** in accordance with this reasoning, one can pour **even a *kor* and even two *kor*** of waste water into a small pit. As all gutters flow with water in the rainy season, there is no cause for any concern.

Abaye draws a final conclusion: *hilkach* — therefore — the dispensation in the rainy season is not merely about the minimum thresholds but about any amount whatsoever. *Afilu kor va'afilu korayim* — even a *kor* or two *korim* of water — vastly exceeding the two-*se'ah* standard — may be poured in the rainy season, because the foundational concerns that generated all the requirements of pits and dimensions and covered drainage pipes simply do not apply when it is raining. Whatever variation exists among the Amoraim — whether no pit is needed at all (the *baraita*'s position), or a smaller proportional pit suffices (Rav Nachman's position) — they all agree that the rainy season significantly relaxes the standard, and Abaye's conclusion is that the relaxation is effectively unlimited once the underlying rationale has dissolved.
