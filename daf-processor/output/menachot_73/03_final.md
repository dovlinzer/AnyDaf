# Menachot 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Menachot with Kemitzah

### Ten Menachot Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ מְנָחוֹת נִקְמָצוֹת, וּשְׁיָרֵיהֶן לַכֹּהֲנִים: מִנְחַת הַסֹּלֶת, וְהַמַּחֲבַת, וְהַמַּרְחֶשֶׁת, וְהַחַלּוֹת, וְהָרְקִיקִין, מִנְחַת גּוֹיִם, וּמִנְחַת נָשִׁים, וּמִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר, וּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא, וּמִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these are the meal offerings** from which **a handful is removed and** the **remainder** of the offering is eaten **by the priests: The meal offering of fine flour; and the** meal offering prepared in a **pan; and the** meal offering prepared in a **deep pan;** and **the** meal offering baked in an oven that is brought entirely of **loaves; and the** meal offering baked in an oven that is brought entirely of **wafers;** the **meal offering of gentiles; and** the **meal offering of women; and the *omer* meal offering,** i.e., the measure of barley brought as a communal offering on the sixteenth of Nisan; **and the meal offering of a sinner; and** the **meal offering of jealousy,** brought by a *sota*.

The new perek opens with a classification of which menachos require kmitzah, identifying those whose remainder is eaten by the kohanim: *ve-elu menachos nikmatzos u-shiyureihen la-kohanim*. The list includes minchas hasoles, the machavas, the marcheshes, the chalos, and the rekikhin — the five standard menachos enumerated at the beginning of Vayikra, representing flour, frying pan, saucepan, loaves, and wafers (the two variations of ma'afeh tanur). The mishna adds minchas ovdei kokhavim (brought by non-Jews), minchas nashim (brought by women — not a separate halakhic category in the Torah, yet grouped here for completeness), minchas ha-omer, *minchas chotei*, and *minchas kena'os*. The final three are all brought from barley: the omer offering, the korban of the oleh ve-yored at the end of *Parshas Vayikra*, and the *minchas kena'os* of the sotah. Notably, the last two lack oil and levonah.

### Rabbi Shimon: Minchat Ko…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים נִקְמֶצֶת, וְקוֹמֵץ קָרֵב לְעַצְמוֹ, וְשִׁירַיִם קְרֵיבִים לְעַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** Although its remainder is not eaten by priests, as meal offerings of priests are burned in their entirety, as it is written: "And every meal offering of the priest shall be offered in its entirety; it shall not be eaten" (Leviticus 6:16), nevertheless, with regard to the **meal offering of a sinner** brought **by** one of the **priests, a handful is removed. And the handful is sacrificed** on the altar **by itself, and** the **remainder is sacrificed** on the altar **by itself.**

Rabbi Shimon introduces a position regarding a category conspicuously absent from the list: the *minchas kohen*. Since a *minchas kohen* is burned entirely, one might assume that no kmitzah is performed — the kmitzah's function is to designate what goes on the fire while the remainder is eaten, and if everything is burned, the distinction collapses. According to the Chachamim, the entire *minchas kohen* is treated as the korban, analogous to a single undifferentiated offering to God.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *minchas chotei shel kohanim nikmetzes ve-hakometz karev le-atsmo ve-hashiyurayim krevim le-atsman* — even for a *minchas kohen* one performs kmitzah, and the kometz is offered as a distinct ritual act, after which the remainder is also burned separately. The conceptual point appears to be that even in the case of a *minchas kohen*, the kmitzah retains its intrinsic halakhic significance as the formal act of dedication to God. The remainder would ordinarily be eaten by a kohen other than the owner, but since here the owner is himself the mekareiv, it cannot be eaten; it is therefore consumed on the fire — yet as a categorically separate act from the offering of the kometz itself.

### Rav Papa's Ten Menachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כֹּל הֵיכָא דִּתְנַן – עֶשֶׂר תְּנַן, מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מֵרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: מֶחֱצָה חַלּוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה רְקִיקִין יָבִיא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Pappa stated** a principle with regard to all the *mishnayot* in tractate *Menaḥot*: **Anywhere that we learned** in a mishna that one brings a meal offering, **we learned** that one must bring **ten** items of the same type, either loaves or wafers. The Gemara asks: **What is** Rav Pappa **teaching us,** as this is already stated by Rabbi Yehuda in the mishna (76a)? The Gemara answers: This statement of Rav Pappa serves **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon, who said:** One who takes a vow to bring a meal offering baked in an oven must bring ten items. If he wishes, he may bring ten loaves or ten wafers, and if he wishes **he may bring half** of them as **loaves and** the other **half** as **wafers.** Rav Pappa **teaches us that** the *tanna* of the mishna maintains **that** one may **not** do so; all ten must be of the same type.

*Amar Rav Papa: kol heikha de-tenan eser, tenan* — wherever the mishna enumerates menachos, it counts exactly ten. The Gemara asks: what is this intended to teach? *Le-apukei Rabbi Shimon de-amar mechtza chalos mechtza rekikhin yavi* — to exclude Rabbi Shimon's view that a minchas ma'afeh tanur could consist of five chalos and five rekikhin (rather than ten of one type), which would produce an eleventh category. Rav Papa teaches that the mishna implicitly rejects this position.

Rashi offers two readings of the "ten" principle. The first, and preferable, reading counts the ten menachos as follows: solet, machavas, marcheshes, chalos, and rekikhin (five), plus ovdei kokhavim, nashim, omer, chotei, and kena'os (five more). Since there are exactly ten, Rabbi Shimon's mixed category of half-chalos and half-rekikhin would constitute an eleventh — and the mishna's insistence on ten implicitly refutes him. The second reading holds that the point is simply that each category comprises ten full loaves, such that Rabbi Shimon's case of chalos and rekikhin would yield only five of each.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁיָרֵיהֶן לַכֹּהֲנִים, מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיבָא – כְּתִיבָא, וּדְלָא כְּתִיבָא – כְּתִיב בַּהּ: ״וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה הַקְרֵב אוֹתָהּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן וְגוֹ׳ וְהַנּוֹתֶרֶת מִמֶּנָּה יֹאכְלוּ אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And their remainder** is eaten **by the priests.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara challenges the question: That **which is written** explicitly, **is written.** In the case of several meal offerings, including the gift meal offering brought by an individual, the Torah clearly states that the priests eat the remainder. **And** with regard to that **which is not written** explicitly, **it is written of it: "And this is the law of the meal offering: The sons of Aaron shall offer it** before the Lord…And he shall take up from it his handful…and shall make the memorial part of it smoke upon the altar….**And the remainder of it shall Aaron and his sons eat"** (Leviticus 6:7–9). These verses establish the principle that the priests eat the remainders of meal offerings, even where the Torah does not state this explicitly. Therefore, it is unnecessary to ask for a source.

The mishna further states *ve-shiyureihen la-kohanim* — the remainder of all these menachos is eaten by the kohanim. The Gemara asks: from where is this derived? *De-chtiva chtiva* — for those menachos where the Torah explicitly prescribes kmitzah, the halacha is written directly; *u-delo chtiva, chtiva bi* — for those where it is not explicit, the general pasuk in Tzav applies: *ve-zos tihye toras hamincha* — this is the law of the mincha — *ve-hanoseres mimena yokhlu Aharon u-vanav* — what remains is eaten by Aaron and his sons. The rule is thus universally established: where there is kmitzah, the remainder belongs to the kohanim.

## Remainder Eaten by Kohan…

### Sources for Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאָה חִיטִּין – לָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לַן, כִּי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לַן – בָּאָה שְׂעוֹרִין. בָּאָה שְׂעוֹרִין נָמֵי, מִדְּנִקְמֶצֶת – שְׁיָרֶיהָ לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the original question: With regard to a meal offering that **comes** from **wheat, we did not ask,** as that is included in the cited verse. **When we asked** it was with regard to a meal offering that **comes** from **barley,** i.e., the *omer* meal offering and the meal offering brought by a *sota*: From where is it derived that the remainders of those offerings are given to the priests? The Gemara responds: With regard to one that **comes** from **barley also, since a handful** of it **is removed,** as the Torah states with regard to all meal offerings (see Leviticus 6:8), clearly **its remainder** is given **to the priests,** as why else would a handful be removed and offered?

The Gemara refines the question. There is no difficulty regarding wheat-flour menachos: *behai lo kamibaya lan* — we are not asking about the standard case. *Ki kamibaya lan d'seorim* — our question concerns the menachos brought from barley flour. Does *ve-zos toras hamincha* extend to those as well? As Tosafos notes, this is not merely a formal question. Wheat flour is standard human food; barley was conventionally fodder for animals. It might therefore be argued that the concept of remainder-eaten-by-kohanim does not apply to the barley menachos.

### Barley Menachot Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן, לָא קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לַן; כִּי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לַן – אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: אִיכָּא מִנְחָה דְּמִיקַּמְצָא וְלָא מִיתְאַכְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **According to** the opinion **of the Rabbis,** who hold that the priests eat the remainder of all meal offerings from which a handful is removed, **we did not ask** about the source. **When we asked** it was **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon, who said: There is** a type of **meal offering of which a handful is removed but** its remainder **is not eaten.**

One might respond: the very fact that kmitzah is performed on the barley menachos implies that the remainder is eaten — for what else would the kmitzah be designating? *Ki d'seorim nami, m'na yad'inan d'shayeraya l'kohanim* — surely the act of kmitzah itself signals that the remainder serves a distinct purpose, namely being consumed.

The Gemara rejects this inference: *aliba d'Rabbanan lo kamibaya lan* — according to the Chachamim, this argument is indeed valid, since they hold that wherever kmitzah is performed the remainder is always eaten. Their position entails a strict equivalence between kmitzah and subsequent consumption. But *ki kamibaya lan aliba d'Rabbi Shimon* — the question is acute according to Rabbi Shimon, *d'amar ika minchah d'mikamtza v'lo misachla* — who holds that there can be a mincha where kmitzah is performed yet the remainder is not eaten, namely the *minchas kohanim*. According to Rabbi Shimon, then, the performance of kmitzah on the barley menachos does not by itself prove that their remainder is eaten. A separate source is required.

### Kemitzah Implies Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְכׇל מִנְחָה בְלוּלָה בַשֶּׁמֶן וַחֲרֵבָה לְכׇל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן תִּהְיֶה״. אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לִבְלוּלָה שֶׁל חִיטִּין – תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לִבְלוּלָה שֶׁל שְׂעוֹרִין, וְאִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לַחֲרֵבָה שֶׁל חִיטִּין – תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לַחֲרֵבָה שֶׁל שְׂעוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers. **Ḥizkiyya said:** The source is from a verse, **as the verse states: "And every meal offering, mixed with oil, or dry, shall all the sons of Aaron have"** (Leviticus 7:10). The verse is analyzed: **If** it **is not** necessary for **the matter of a mixed** meal offering **of wheat,** which is derived from the verse cited earlier, **apply it** to the **matter of a mixed** meal offering **of barley,** i.e., the meal offering of the *omer*. **And if** the verse **is not** necessary for **the matter of a dry** meal offering **of wheat,** which is derived from the verse cited earlier, **apply it** to the **matter of a dry** meal offering **of barley,** i.e., the meal offering of a *sota*.

Chizkiyah supplies the derivation from a pasuk in Tzav: *ve-chol mincha belulah vashamen va-charevah le-chol benei Aharon tihyeh ish ke-achiv* — any mincha mixed with oil or that is dry shall belong to all of the sons of Aaron, each equally as his brother. This pasuk mentions two types: one mixed with oil (belulah) and one dry (charevah). Since the cases of belulah she-shel chitim (wheat mixed with oil) and charevah she-shel chitim (dry wheat mincha) are already established elsewhere, the pasuk cannot be coming to teach them. The principle of im eino inyan therefore redirects: *tenahu inyan li-velulah shel se'orim* — the reference to belulah teaches that the minchas ha-omer (a barley mincha mixed with oil) is eaten by the kohanim; *tenahu inyan le-charevah shel se'orim* — the reference to charevah teaches the same for the minchas sotah (a dry barley mincha). The remainder of both barley menachos is thus established as eaten by benei Aharon.

## Equal Division Among Koh…

### Rabbi Shimon: Kemitzah A…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? הָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין חוֹלְקִין מְנָחוֹת כְּנֶגֶד זְבָחִים –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And this** verse, **does it come to** teach **this** *halakha*? **Isn't** the verse **necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that** priests **may not receive a share** of **meal offerings in exchange for** a share of animal **offerings,** i.e., one may not exchange his share of a meal offering for the equivalent value of meat from an animal offering of which he did not receive a share?

The Gemara immediately challenges Chizkiyah's derivation: *ve-hai le-hachi hu de-asa?* Is this pasuk really coming to teach that the barley menachos are consumed by the kohanim? Perhaps it is needed for a much more elaborate teaching about the mechanics of distribution, as developed in a lengthy braita. The braita addresses the question of how the kohanim divide sacrificial portions among themselves. *Minayin she-ein cholkin menachos keneged zevachim* — how do we know that one cannot offset menachos against animal sacrifices, giving some kohanim the flour portions while others receive the meat portions? *Talmud lomar: ve-chol mincha asher te-afeh ba-tanur le-chol benei Aharon tihyeh ish ke-achiv* — the Torah insists on equal division of the menachos to all benei Aharon, from which we learn that menachos must be divided within their own category.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל הַמִּנְחָה אֲשֶׁר תֵּאָפֶה בַּתַּנּוּר לְכׇל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן תִּהְיֶה אִישׁ כְּאָחִיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states: "And every meal offering that is baked in the oven…shall all the sons of Aaron have, each man like the other"** (Leviticus 7:9–10). This verse emphasizes that the sons of Aaron must divide the meal offering equally among themselves, without exchanging it for a portion of any other offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יַחְלְקוּ מְנָחוֹת כְּנֶגֶד זְבָחִים, שֶׁלֹּא קָמוּ תַּחְתֵּיהֶן בְּדַלּוּת, אֲבָל יַחְלְקוּ מְנָחוֹת כְּנֶגֶד עוֹפוֹת, שֶׁהֲרֵי קָמוּ תַּחְתֵּיהֶן בְּדַלּוּת – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל נַעֲשָׂה בַּמַּרְחֶשֶׁת לְכׇל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן תִּהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **they may not receive a share** of **meal offerings in exchange for** portions of animal **offerings since they do not substitute for them in** the case of **poverty.** One who is too poor to afford to bring an animal offering, e.g., in the case of a sin offering determined on a sliding scale, does not bring a meal offering in its stead. Since meal offerings are not brought in place of animal offerings, there is clearly no connection between them. **But** perhaps **they may receive a share** of **meal offerings in exchange for** portions of **bird offering**s, **since they do substitute for them in** the case of **poverty.** If one is so destitute that he cannot afford to bring a bird offering he brings a meal offering. Therefore, **the** same **verse states: "And all that is prepared in the deep pan…shall all the sons of Aaron have,"** again emphasizing that all must have an equal share in that meal offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יַחְלְקוּ מְנָחוֹת כְּנֶגֶד עוֹפוֹת, שֶׁהַלָּלוּ מִינֵי דָמִים וְהַלָּלוּ מִינֵי קְמָחִים, יַחְלְקוּ עוֹפוֹת כְּנֶגֶד זְבָחִים, שֶׁהַלָּלוּ וְהַלָּלוּ מִינֵי דָמִים – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעַל מַחֲבַת לְכׇל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן תִּהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **they may not receive a share** of **meal offerings in exchange for** portions of **bird offering**s **since these,** i.e., bird offerings, **are types of** offerings that involve **blood** sprinkled on the altar, **and those,** i.e., meal offerings, **are types** of offerings made **of flour. But** perhaps **they may receive a share** of portions of **bird offering**s **in exchange for** portions of animal **offerings, since both** categories are **types of** offerings that involve **blood** sprinkled on the altar. Therefore, **the** same **verse states: "And on a pan…shall all the sons of Aaron have,"** a seemingly superfluous phrase, which teaches that one may not receive a share even of bird offerings in exchange for portions of animal offerings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יַחְלְקוּ עוֹפוֹת כְּנֶגֶד זְבָחִים, שֶׁהַלָּלוּ עֲשִׂיָּיתָן בַּיָּד, וְהַלָּלוּ עֲשִׂיָּיתָן בִּכְלִי, אֲבָל יַחְלְקוּ מְנָחוֹת כְּנֶגֶד מְנָחוֹת, שֶׁאֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ עֲשִׂיָּיתָן בַּיָּד – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל מִנְחָה בְלוּלָה בַשֶּׁמֶן ... לְכׇל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן תִּהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **they may not receive a share** of **bird offering**s **in exchange for** portions of animal **offerings because** with regard to **these,** i.e., the birds, **their processing,** i.e., killing, is executed **by hand,** by pinching the nape of the neck, **and** with regard to **those,** i.e., the animals, **their processing,** i.e., killing, is executed **with a utensil,** by slaughtering with a knife. **But** perhaps **they may receive a share** of **meal offerings in exchange for** portions of other **meal offerings, since the processing of** both **these and those** are carried out **by hand.** Therefore, **the** next **verse states: "And every meal offering mixed with oil…shall all the sons of Aaron have"** (Leviticus 7:10).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יַחְלְקוּ מַחֲבַת כְּנֶגֶד מַרְחֶשֶׁת, וּמַרְחֶשֶׁת כְּנֶגֶד מַחֲבַת, שֶׁזּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂיהָ קָשִׁין וְזוֹ מַעֲשֶׂיהָ רַכִּין, אֲבָל יַחְלְקוּ מַחֲבַת כְּנֶגֶד מַחֲבַת וּמַרְחֶשֶׁת כְּנֶגֶד מַרְחֶשֶׁת – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וַחֲרֵבָה לְכׇל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן תִּהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **they may not receive a share** of a meal offering prepared on **a pan in exchange for** portions of a meal **offering** prepared in **a deep pan, or** portions of a meal offering prepared in **a deep pan in exchange for** portions of a meal **offering** prepared on **a pan, since the actions** with **this pan** result in **a hard** product, **and the actions** with **that** deep pan result in **a soft** product. **But** perhaps **they may receive a share** of a meal offering prepared on **a pan in exchange for** the portions of a different meal offering prepared on **a pan, or** a share of a meal offering prepared in **a deep pan in exchange for** portions of a different meal offering prepared in **a deep pan.** Therefore, **the** same **verse states: "Or dry, shall all the sons of Aaron have"** (Leviticus 7:10).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יַחְלְקוּ בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, אֲבָל יַחְלְקוּ בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ כְּאָחִיו״, ״וְאִם עַל תּוֹדָה״ – כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין חוֹלְקִין בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, כָּךְ אֵין חוֹלְקִים בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that they **may not receive a share of offerings of the most sacred order,** e.g., meal offerings, in exchange for a portion of another similar offering, **but they may receive a share of offerings of lesser sanctity** in exchange for a portion of another similar offering. Therefore, **the** same **verse states** with regard to meal offerings: "Shall all the sons of Aaron have, **one as well as another"** (Leviticus 7:10), **and** near it appears the verse: **"If** he offers it **for a thanks offering"** (Leviticus 7:12), from which is derived: **Just as one may not receive a share** of one offering in exchange for a portion of another similar offering in the case **of offerings of the most sacred order, so too, one may not receive a share** of one offering in exchange for a portion of another similar offering in the case **of offerings of lesser sanctity,** e.g., a thanks offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אִישׁ״ – אִישׁ חוֹלֵק, וַאֲפִילּוּ בַּעַל מוּם, וְאֵין קָטָן חוֹלֵק, וַאֲפִילּוּ תָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further expounds this verse: It states: **"One** as well as another **[*ish*** *ke'aḥiv*]," which teaches that with regard to priests, **a man [*ish*]** who is an adult **receives a share even** if he is **blemished, but** a priest who is **a minor may not receive a share even** if he is **unblemished.** This *baraita* evidently interprets the verse: "And every meal offering, mixed with oil, or dry, shall all the sons of Aaron have, one as well as another" (Leviticus 7:10), as referring to the prohibition against priests exchanging shares of offerings. If so, how does Ḥizkiyya state that this verse is referring to the priests' eating of the remainder of the *omer* offering and the meal offering of a *sota*?

### Chizkiyah's Derivation

*[Continued from above]*

The braita proceeds through a series of progressively more refined exclusions, each taught by an additional word or phrase in the pesukim. Perhaps menachos cannot be divided against zevachim because they are categorically unrelated, but they could be divided against bird sacrifices, since birds can substitute for a mincha in the case of a *Korban oleh ve-yored*? *Talmud lomar ve-chol na'aseh ba-marcheshet* — the mention of the marcheshes insists that even this division is prohibited. Perhaps bird sacrifices and animal sacrifices, both being blood offerings, could be apportioned against one another? *Talmud lomar ve-al machavat* — yet another item in the pasuk insists otherwise. Perhaps different types of menachos could be traded off against each other within the category of flour offerings? *Talmud lomar ve-chol mincha belulah ba-shemen* — no, each sub-category must be divided within itself. Perhaps at least two identical menachos — one machavat against another machavat — could be pooled together and divided? *Talmud lomar ve-charevah* — even this is excluded. The final conclusion is that each individual sacrifice must be divided separately among the kohanim. 

The magnitude of this requirement is striking: in the *Beis HaMikdash* on a busy day, every single sacrifice — of every type, brought by every individual — would need to be individually subdivided among all the eligible kohanim. Tosafos suggests an alternative reading of *lo yachloku*: rather than requiring active individual division of each korban, the principle may mean that no formal apportionment takes place at all — the portions are simply made available for any and all kohanim to partake, without a structured allocation. Tosafos wavers between this reading and the more demanding literal one.

The braita proceeds to apply the same principle to *kodshim kalim*. *Yachol *lo yachloku* be-*kodshei kodashim* aval yachloku be-kodshim kalim* — perhaps the restrictions apply only to *kodshei kodashim* (consumed only within the *Beis HaMikdash*), but the breast and thigh of the shelamim, which may be eaten anywhere in Yerushalayim and might seem more like personal property, could be divided differently? *Talmud lomar ish ke-achiv ve-im al todah* — the pasuk continues with the todah and shelamim, extending the principle: *ke-shem she-ein cholkin be-*kodshei kodashim*, kach ein cholkin be-kodshim kalim*.

### Ravina: Korach Portion

*[Continued from above]*

The braita concludes with one further teaching derived from the word *ish*: *ish cholek va-afilu *ba'al mum*, ve-ein katan cholek va-afilu tam* — an adult kohen receives his portion even if he is a *ba'al mum* (physically blemished), while a minor kohen does not receive a portion even if he is unblemished. The right to the sacrificial portions belongs to adult kohanim, not to minors.

### Inclusive Language in Ko…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא מִ״כׇּל״ נָפְקָא, וְהָא אַפֵּיקְתֵּיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אֶלָּא, הָהוּא מִ״וְכׇל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: With regard to the prohibition against exchanging priestly shares, **that is derived from** the term: **"Every** meal offering." By contrast, Ḥizkiyya derives his principle with regard to these two meal offerings from the rest of the verse. The Gemara asks: **But** haven't you already **derived** from the word "every" **that which Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** states, that when one vows to offer a meal offering baked in an oven, all the baked items must be of a uniform type, either loaves or wafers (see 63b)? The Gemara answers: **Rather, that** *halakha* concerning the exchange of shares of offerings is derived **from** the addition of the word "and," in the term: **"And every [*vekhol*]** meal offering."

The Gemara now asks: given all this, how can Chizkiyah's derivation stand? If the pasuk *ve-chol mincha belulah vashamen va-charevah le-chol benei Aharon* is entirely consumed by the braita's teachings about division, it cannot simultaneously teach that the barley menachos are eaten by the kohanim. *Hahu mikol nafka* — the Gemara responds that the additional teachings about division are derived from the word *kol* (in *ve-chol*), while the fundamental teaching that the barley menachos are consumed — *belulah... va-charevah... le-chol benei Aharon* — remains intact as the plain meaning of the pasuk. And the earlier teaching cited in connection with a different perek (*le-Rabbi Yosi be-Rabbi Yehuda*) is derived from the extra *vav* of *ve-chol*. Thus the same pasuk yields three distinct layers of derivation: the *vav* for the prior teaching, the *kol* for the rules of division, and the plain meaning for the entitlement of the kohanim to the barley menachos.

## Non-Jews Bringing Sacrif…

### Rav Huna: Non-Jews Limit…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: אָתְיָא מִדְּתָנֵי לֵוִי, דְּתָנֵי לֵוִי: ״לְכׇל קׇרְבָּנָם וּלְכׇל מִנְחָתָם וּלְכׇל חַטָּאתָם וּלְכׇל אֲשָׁמָם״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ravina said:** According to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, the source for the *halakha* that the *omer* offering and the meal offering of a *sota* are eaten **comes from** the *baraita* **that Levi teaches, as Levi teaches:** The verse states with regard to the priestly gifts: "This shall be yours of the most sacred items, reserved from the fire: **Every offering of theirs, and every meal offering of theirs, and every sin offering of theirs, and every guilt offering of theirs,** which they may restore to Me, shall be most holy for you and for your sons" (Numbers 18:9). The word "every" in each clause includes a number of additional offerings that are eaten by the priests.

*Ravina amar: asya mi-detani Levi* — Ravina offers a cleaner derivation, from what Levi teaches, based on the pasuk in Bamidbar 18 following the episode of Korach, where the Torah reaffirms the priestly entitlements in sweeping terms: *le-chol korbanam, le-chol minchasam, le-chol chatasam, u-le-chol ashamam* — all their korbanos, all their menachos, all their sin offerings, all their asham offerings — which they return to Me, holy of holies, it is for you and your sons. The broad framing of *le-chol minchasam* straightforwardly includes the barley menachos among the priestly portions, without the complex series of redirections required by Chizkiyah's approach.

### Rav Shizbi's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כׇּל קׇרְבָּנָם״ – לְרַבּוֹת לוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: ״מִן הָאֵשׁ״ כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Torah states: **"Every offering of theirs," to include** the ***log* of oil** that accompanies the guilt offering **of** a recovered **leper,** teaching that it is also eaten by the priests. As, it might **enter your mind to say** that since **the Merciful One writes** in this verse: **"From the fire,"** this would exclude this oil, which is not brought onto the altar. Therefore, the verse **teaches us:** "Every offering," to include the leper's oil.

The Gemara then examines what each phrase in this pasuk comes to include beyond what would otherwise have been known. *Kol korbanam* — this includes the log of oil of the metzora in the purification process. Since the preceding words speak of *min ha-esh* (portions derived from the fire, i.e., from the mizbeach), one might have thought only offerings involving the mizbeach confer rights to the kohanim. *Kol korbanam* extends the principle to the metzora's oil, which is used ritually but not burned on the mizbeach.

### Rabbi Yochanan: Debate R…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְכׇל מִנְחָתָם״ – לְרַבּוֹת מִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר וּמִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: ״וְאָכְלוּ אֹתָם אֲשֶׁר כֻּפַּר בָּהֶם״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהַאי לְהַתִּיר קָא אָתְיָא, וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי לְבָרֵר קָא אָתְיָא – קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues expounding the verse: **"And every meal offering of theirs,"** serving **to include the *omer* meal offering, and the meal offering of jealousy** brought by a *sota*, teaching that they are also eaten by the priests. As, it might **enter your mind to say** that since **the Merciful One states: "And they shall eat those wherewith atonement was made"** (Exodus 29:33), the verse thereby indicates that the priests may eat only those offerings that help the owner achieve atonement. **And this** *omer* **comes to permit** eating from the new grain (see Leviticus 23:9–14), not to achieve atonement; **and** concerning **the other** offering, i.e., the meal offering of a *sota*, **as well,** it **comes to clarify** whether or not the accused woman is guilty of adultery, but not to achieve atonement. Therefore, the verse **teaches us:** "Every meal offering," to teach that these two meal offerings are included.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְכׇל חַטֹּאתָם״ – לְרַבּוֹת חַטַּאת הָעוֹף, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: נְבֵילָה הִיא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues to expound the verse. The verse states: **"And every sin offering of theirs," to include a bird sin offering,** teaching that it is also eaten by the priests. As, it might **enter your mind to say:** The priests may not eat it because **it is an unslaughtered animal carcass,** as it is killed by pinching the nape of the neck (see Leviticus 5:8), not by conventional slaughter. Therefore, the verse **teaches us:** "Every sin offering," teaching that bird sin offerings are included.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְכׇל אֲשָׁמָם״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע. אֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע – בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״כִּי כַּחַטָּאת הָאָשָׁם הוּא לַכֹּהֵן״!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues to expound the verse. The Torah states: **"And every guilt offering of theirs," to include the guilt offering of the nazirite** who has become ritually impure (see Numbers 6:12) **and the guilt offering of the leper,** teaching that they are also eaten by the priests. The Gemara objects: With regard to **the guilt offering of the leper, it is explicitly written with regard to it: "For as the sin offering is the priest's, so is the guilt offering"** (Leviticus 14:13), which already teaches that it is eaten by the priests.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְרַבּוֹת אֲשַׁם נָזִיר כַּאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: לְהַכְשִׁיר קָא אָתֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** the verse serves **to include** the **guilt offering of the nazirite,** stating that its status is **like** the **guilt offering of the leper.** As, it might **enter your mind to say:** The guilt offering of the nazirite is not sacrificed for atonement, but rather **it comes to prepare** the nazirite to begin his period of naziriteship anew, and therefore its meat would not be eaten by the priests. Therefore, the verse **teaches us:** "Every guilt offering," teaching that the guilt offering of the nazirite is included.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר יָשִׁיבוּ״ – זֶה גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר, ״לְךָ הִיא וּלְבָנֶיךָ״ – שֶׁלְּךָ הִיא וּלְבָנֶיךָ, אֲפִילּוּ לְקַדֵּשׁ בּוֹ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: "This shall be yours of the most sacred items…**which they may restore"; this** is referring to an item **stolen** from **a convert** who has no heirs and subsequently dies. In this case, the stolen item is given to the priests together with an additional one-fifth of its worth. The phrase **"for you and for your sons"** means that **it is yours and your sons'** personal property, and it may be used **even to betroth a woman with it,** and it does not belong to the Temple treasury.

*Le-chol minchasam* — all their menachos. *Le-rabbos minchas omer u-minchas kena'os* — this explicitly includes the minchas ha-omer and the *minchas kena'os* of the sotah. The hava amina would have been that only offerings connected to atonement (*asher kupar bahem*) are eaten by the kohanim; the omer is not brought for kapara but to render the new grain permissible (*le-hatir ka'asya*), and the minchas sotah is brought not as a definitive atonement but as a clarificatory test (*levarer ka'asya*). *Minchasam* teaches that even these are eaten by the kohanim — and since both happen to be barley menachos, this is a much cleaner source for the very question we have been addressing, as Tosafos acknowledges. *Le-chol chatasam* includes the bird chatas: one might have thought that since the bird is not slaughtered through normal shechita but rather by the kohen's fingernail at the back of the neck, it would be considered a neveilah and forbidden; *chatasam* teaches that the kohanim eat it nonetheless. *Le-chol ashamam* includes the asham of the nazir and the asham of the metzora. Since *ki ke-chatas ha-asham hu la-kohen* is written explicitly regarding the metzora's asham, the derivation from *ashamam* must come to include the nazir's asham. The hava amina for excluding the nazir's asham is that it comes *le-hachshir* — to reconstitute the nezirus after tumah — rather than as a straightforward atonement offering.

The pasuk concludes *asher yashivu* — that which they return. This is interpreted as referring to *gezel ha-ger*: if one swore falsely to a ger that he had not stolen from him, he must return the stolen property with an added fifth and bring an asham. If the ger dies without heirs, the restitution money goes to the kohen. Finally, *lecha hu u-le-vanecha* — it is yours and your sons' — teaches the degree of ownership the kohen has in his portion: *afilu le-kadesh bo et isha*, it is sufficiently his property that he could use it to effect kiddushin, a point elaborated upon in Maseches Kiddushin.

## Non-Jews Sending Offerin…

### 'Ish' Verse Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: שַׁלְמֵי הַגּוֹי – עוֹלוֹת, אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא – גּוֹי לִבּוֹ לַשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna said:** **Peace offerings** volunteered by **gentiles** are sacrificed as **burnt offerings,** which are burned completely upon the altar. With regard to the source for this *halakha*, **if you wish, cite a verse; and if you wish, propose a logical argument. If you wish, propose a logical argument:** Concerning **a gentile** who volunteers an offering, the intent of **his heart is** that the offering should be entirely sacred **to Heaven,** and he does not intend for any of it to be eaten.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא, ״אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיבוּ לַה׳ לְעוֹלָה״ – כֹּל דִּמְקָרְבִי עוֹלָה לֶיהֱוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, cite a verse:** "Any man [*ish ish*] who is of the house of Israel, or of the strangers in Israel, that brings his offering, whether it be any of their vows, or any of their gift offerings, **which they will offer to the Lord as a burnt offering"** (Leviticus 22:18). The doubled term *ish ish* teaches that the offerings of a gentile are accepted, and the verse thereby teaches that **any** offering **that** gentiles volunteer to be **sacrificed should be a burnt offering.**

The Gemara transitions to a new topic, introduced because it will shortly engage our mishna directly. *Amar Rav Huna: shlamei ovdei kochavim olot* — if a non-Jew dedicates an animal as a shelamim, it is treated as an olah. Rav Huna offers two justifications. The first is a sevara: *ovdei kochavim libehu la-shamayim* — a non-Jew, when dedicating something to God, intends to give it over entirely to heaven. The notion of eating from a sacrifice in God's presence may seem, from a non-Jewish perspective, insufficiently absolute — he therefore effectively means olah even when he says shelamim.

The second justification is from the pasuk itself. The Gemara cites Vayikra 22, which begins: *ish ish mi-beis Yisrael u-min ha-ger be-Yisrael asher yakriv korbano le-chol nidreihem u-le-chol nidvotam asher yakrivu l'Hashem le-olah* — any man from the house of Israel or from the sojourner among Israel who brings his offering, for all their vows and all their freewill offerings that they bring to Hashem, as an olah. It is striking that although the pasuk heavily emphasizes benei Yisrael, Chazal derive from *ish ish* that even non-Jews may bring offerings. And the end of the pasuk — *asher yakrivu l'Hashem le-olah* — teaches that whatever a non-Jew brings is treated as an olah: *kol de-mikrevi olah lehavei*.

### Rabbi Shimon's Ordinance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא: גּוֹי שֶׁהִתְנַדֵּב לְהָבִיא שְׁלָמִים, נְתָנָן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל – יִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹכְלָן, נְתָנָן לְכֹהֵן – הַכֹּהֵן אוֹכְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥama bar Gurya raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to **a gentile who volunteered to bring a peace offering,** if he **gave it to an Israelite,** the **Israelite eats it;** if he **gave it to a priest, the priest eats it.** Evidently, the gentile's peace offering is eaten, like the peace offering of a Jew.

Rav Chama bar Guria challenges from a Tosefta: *oved kokhavim she-hitnadev le-havi shelamim* — if a non-Jew took a vow to bring a shelamim, *netanam le-Yisrael, Yisrael okhlam; netanam le-kohen, kohen okhlam* — if he gives it to a Yisrael, a Yisrael eats it; if to a kohen, a kohen eats it. This teaching seems to confirm that a non-Jew can bring a shelamim, directly contradicting Rav Huna.

### Nesachim Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי קָא אָמַר, עַל מְנָת שֶׁיִּתְכַּפֵּר בָּהֶן יִשְׂרָאֵל – יִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹכְלָן, עַל מְנָת שֶׁיִּתְכַּפֵּר בָּהֶן כֹּהֵן – כֹּהֵן אוֹכְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** To answer the challenge to Rav Huna's statement, **Rava said: This** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** If a gentile volunteered a peace offering **in order to achieve atonement** on behalf of **an Israelite** who is already obligated to bring a peace offering, then **the Israelite eats** of the offering. If the gentile volunteered it **in order to achieve atonement** on behalf of **a priest** who is already obligated to bring a peace offering, then **the priest eats** of the offering. By contrast, Rav Huna's statement teaches that when a gentile volunteers his own peace offering, it is treated as a burnt offering.

Rava resolves the Tosefta: *al menat she-yitkapru bahen Yisrael, Yisrael okhlam; al menat she-yitkapru bahen kohen, kohen okhlam* — the non-Jew is not bringing his own shelamim. Rather, he is dedicating his animal to serve as the shelamim of a specific Yisrael or a specific kohen. The animal originates with the non-Jew but is consecrated as the korban of the named recipient. The non-Jew is thus not the owner of the shelamim in any halachic sense; he merely provides the animal. This resolves the contradiction: Rav Huna's ruling addresses a non-Jew's own sacrifice, whereas the Tosefta addresses a case of vicarious dedication.

### Reconciling Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁיזְבִי: אֵלּוּ מְנָחוֹת נִקְמָצוֹת וּשְׁיָרֵיהֶן לַכֹּהֲנִים – מִנְחַת גּוֹיִם. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, הָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizevi raises an objection** from the mishna: **These are the meal offerings** from which **a handful is removed and their remainder** is eaten **by the priests…the meal offering of gentiles.** If the priests may eat the remainder of the meal offerings of gentiles, it is logical that the peace offerings of gentiles should also be given to the priests to eat, as the right of the priests to eat from meal offerings and peace offerings is identical. To resolve this objection, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This is **not difficult. This** statement in the mishna that the priests eat the meal offerings of gentiles is the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** and **that** ruling of Rav Huna that the peace offerings of gentiles are not eaten is the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.**

Rav Shizbi then challenges from our own mishna: *elu menachos nikmatzos ve-shiyureihen la-kohanim — minchas ovdei kokhavim* — the mishna explicitly includes the mincha of a non-Jew among those subject to kmitzah, whose remainder is eaten by the kohanim. This indicates that a non-Jew can bring a mincha — a sacrifice other than an olah — and this challenges Rav Huna's position.

## Rabbi Yosi vs. Rabbi Akiva

### Rabbi Yochanan resolves dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִישׁ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״? לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַגּוֹיִם שֶׁנּוֹדְרִין נְדָרִים וּנְדָבוֹת כְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse cited previously states: "Any man [*ish ish*] who is of the house of Israel, or of the strangers in Israel, that brings his offering, whether it be any of their vows, or any of their gift offerings, which they will offer to the Lord as a burnt offering." The verse is now analyzed: The verse could have stated: **A man [*ish*]. Why** does **the verse state** the double expression **"*ish ish*"?** This serves **to include the gentiles,** demonstrating **that they can vow** to bring **vow offerings and gift offerings like a Jew** can.

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the difficulty: *lo kashya — ha Rabbi Yosi HaGlili ve-ha Rabbi Akiva* — there is no contradiction. Rav Huna's statement reflects the position of Rabbi Akiva, while our mishna (which permits a non-Jew to bring a mincha) follows Rabbi Yosi HaGlili. The two Amoraim are simply aligned with opposing tannaitic positions.

### Derasha from Ish Ish pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיבוּ לַה׳ לְעוֹלָה״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא עוֹלָה, שְׁלָמִים מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״נִדְרֵיהֶם״. תּוֹדָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״נִדְבוֹתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** When the verse states: **"Which they will offer to the Lord as a burnt offering," I have** derived **only** that a gentile can vow to bring **a burnt offering. From where** is it derived that a gentile can vow to bring **a peace offering? The verse states: "Their vows." From where** is it derived that he can bring **a thanks offering? The verse states** the seemingly superfluous clause: **"Their gift offerings."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת הָעוֹפוֹת, וְהַיַּיִן, וְהַלְּבוֹנָה, וְהָעֵצִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״נִדְרֵיהֶם״, ״לְכׇל נִדְרֵיהֶם״, ״נִדְבוֹתָם״, ״לְכׇל נִדְבוֹתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From where** is it derived that the verse means **to include** that a gentile can bring **birds** as burnt offerings, **and wine** libations, **and the frankincense, and the wood** for the arrangement upon the altar? **The verse states** not only: **"Their vows,"** but also the more comprehensive term: **"Any of their vows";** and the verse states not only: **"Their gift offerings,"** but also the more comprehensive term: **"Any of their gift offerings."**

The underlying dispute is developed from a braita on the pasuk already cited. *Ish ish mi-beis Yisrael u-min ha-ger... asher yakrivu l'Hashem le-olah* — the braita first establishes, from *ish ish*, that non-Jews may make vows and freewill offerings as Jews do. It then proceeds to expand the scope of permitted offerings through successive derivations: *nidreihem* teaches that vows include shelamim; *nidvotam* extends it to todah; *le-chol nidreihem* and *le-chol nidvotam* include bird offerings, wine, frankincense, and wood — anything a Jew may consecrate and offer, a non-Jew may likewise consecrate. The question then is: why does the pasuk conclude *le-olah*, specifying olah?

### Rabbi Yosi HaGlili position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עוֹלָה״, ״עוֹלָה״ – פְּרָט לִנְזִירוּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיבוּ לַה׳ לְעוֹלָה״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא עוֹלָה בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states:** "They will offer to the Lord as **a burnt offering"?** The *baraita* answers: This teaches that a gentile can bring a standard **burnt offering,** to **the exclusion of** a burnt offering of **naziriteship.** Since a gentile is unable to assume the status of a nazirite, he is also unable to bring the offerings of a nazirite. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Rabbi Akiva says:** When the verse states: **"Which they will offer to the Lord as a burnt offering,"** it indicates that **nothing other than a burnt offering alone** may be brought by a gentile.

According to Rabbi Yosi HaGlili: *olah perat le-nezirut* — the word *le-olah* excludes only the olah of a nazir. Since a non-Jew cannot become a nazir, he cannot bring the nazir's olah. This is the only restriction Rabbi Yosi HaGlili derives; in all other respects, a non-Jew may bring the full range of offerings.

### Rabbi Akiva position

*[Continued from above]*

Rabbi Akiva reads the pasuk differently: *asher yakrivu l'Hashem le-olah — ein li ela olah bilvad* — only an olah. The concluding phrase is definitive and restrictive: a non-Jew may bring only an olah. Rabbi Akiva does not explain how he accounts for the expansive language earlier in the pasuk, but the debate is clear: our mishna permitting a non-Jew's mincha follows Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, while Rav Huna's ruling follows Rabbi Akiva.

## Non-Jews and Nedirut

### Nezirut exclusion source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי פְּרָט לִנְזִירוּת, מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא, ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאָמַרְתָּ אֲלֵהֶם אִישׁ כִּי יַפְלִא לִנְדֹּר נֶדֶר נָזִיר לְהַזִּיר״ – בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל נוֹדְרִין, וְאֵין הַגּוֹיִם נוֹדְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the analysis of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, the Gemara asks: **And this exclusion of** a burnt offering of **naziriteship,** is it **derived from here,** in the verse cited? Is it not **derived from there: "Speak to the children of Israel, and say to them: When a man…shall clearly utter a vow, the vow of a nazirite"** (Numbers 6:2); this is interpreted to mean that **the children of Israel** can **vow** to become nazirites, **but the gentiles cannot vow** to become nazirites? Therefore, the exclusion of gentiles from bringing the burnt offering of a nazirite is not learned from the term "a burnt offering."

The Gemara asks: does Rabbi Yosi HaGlili really need our pasuk to exclude a non-Jew from the nazir's olah? A separate source already establishes that non-Jews cannot become nezirim: *dabber el benei Yisrael ve-amarta alehem ish ki yafli lindor neder nazir le-hazzir — benei Yisrael nodrim ve-ein ovdei kochavim nodrim*. The Gemara answers: if we had only the pasuk in Nezirus, one might have concluded that the restrictions apply to the korban alone — a non-Jew cannot bring the korban of a nazir — but that nezirus itself might take effect upon him. The pasuk in our context teaches that the nezirus cannot take effect at all; the pasuk in Nezirus teaches that he cannot bring the korban. Both teachings are therefore necessary, though the Gemara leaves somewhat open why the exclusion from the korban cannot simply follow from the impossibility of becoming a nazir.

### Rabbi Shimon's seven enactments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מֵהָתָם – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: קׇרְבָּן הוּא דְּלָא לַיְיתֵי, אֲבָל נְזִירוּת חָלָה עֲלַיְיהוּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If** the exclusion was derived **from there,** i.e., the verse in Leviticus, which is referring to offerings, **I would say:** It **is the offering** of nazirites **that** the gentiles **cannot bring, but naziriteship takes effect upon them** if they vow to become a nazirite. Therefore, the exclusion of naziriteship by the verse in Numbers **teaches us** that a gentile cannot become a nazirite at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דִּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: שִׁבְעָה דְּבָרִים הִתְקִינוּ בֵּית דִּין, וְזֶה אֶחָד מֵהֶן: גּוֹי שֶׁשִּׁלַּח עוֹלָתוֹ מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם וְשִׁילַּח עִמָּהּ נְסָכֶיהָ – קְרֵיבִין מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וְאִם לָאו – קְרֵיבִין מִשֶּׁל צִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses a related matter. **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which we learned** in a mishna (*Shekalim* 7:6): **Rabbi Shimon said: The court instituted seven ordinances** with regard to the financial aspects of offerings and consecrations. **And this** ordinance, namely, that the cost of the libations accompanying the sacrifice of a found sacrificial animal is borne by the public, **is one of them.** These are the other ordinances: If **a gentile sent his burnt offering from a country overseas,** and **he sent with it** money for the purchase of the **libations** that must accompany it, the libations **are offered at his** expense. **And if** the gentile did **not** cover the cost of the libations, it is a condition of the court that the libations are **sacrificed at the public's** expense, with funds taken from the Temple treasury. Evidently, a gentile can offer libations as well as burnt offerings.

The Gemara asks: *ke-man azla ha de-tenan* — which tannaitic opinion underlies the teaching of Rabbi Shimon that *beis din* enacted seven enactments, one of which is: if a non-Jew sent an olah from overseas and sent with it the accompanying nesachim, they are offered from his own funds; if he did not send the nesachim, they are covered from public funds. This teaching implicitly presupposes that a non-Jew can send an olah — the question is only whether his nesachim must accompany it or may be funded communally. The Gemara initially suggests this follows Rabbi Yosi HaGlili rather than Rabbi Akiva, since Rabbi Akiva would seem to prohibit even the accompanying nesachim. But the Gemara replies: *afilu teima Rabbi Akiva — olah ve-chol chavratah* — even Rabbi Akiva would permit the nesachim that accompany an olah, since they are integral to the olah offering itself.

### Nesachim requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, וְלָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא! אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, עוֹלָה וְכׇל (חַבְרָתַהּ) [אַבְזָרַהָא].
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this mishna rules in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.** The Gemara rejects this assumption: **You** may **even say** that this mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** and he holds that a gentile can bring **a burnt offering and all its accessories,** including the libations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תְּנָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֶזְרָח״ – אֶזְרָח מֵבִיא נְסָכִים, וְאֵין הַגּוֹי מֵבִיא נְסָכִים. יָכוֹל לֹא תְּהֵא עוֹלָתוֹ טְעוּנָה נְסָכִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כָּכָה״. מַנִּי? לָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי וְלָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught that which the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to libations: "All who are **home born** shall do these things after this manner" (Numbers 15:13), which teaches that those who are **home born,** i.e., Jews, can **bring libations** as a separate offering, **but a gentile cannot bring** such **libations.** One **might** have thought that a gentile's **burnt offering should not require** the standard accompanying **libations.** Therefore, **the verse states: "So** it shall be done for one bull" (Numbers 15:11), which indicates that every offering requires libations. **Whose** opinion is this? It is **not** that of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and not** that of **Rabbi Akiva.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – הָא אָמַר אֲפִילּוּ יַיִן נָמֵי! אִי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – הָא אָמַר: עוֹלָה – אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the question: **If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, doesn't he say** that a gentile may **even** bring **wine** by itself, and not only as a libation? **If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, doesn't he say** that with regard to **a burnt offering, yes,** a gentile may bring it, but with regard to **something else** other than the offering itself, **no,** a gentile may not bring it?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – סְמִי מֵהַהִיא ״יַיִן״, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – עוֹלָה וְכׇל (חַבְרָתַהּ) [אַבְזָרַהָא].
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili; and if you wish, say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva. If you wish, say** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** and **omit from that** *baraita* that the *tanna* allows gentiles to bring **wine,** as he holds that gentiles cannot bring wine by itself. **And if you wish, say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** and interpret his opinion to be that a gentile may bring **a burnt offering and all its accessories.**

A further braita raises a related question: *ezrach mevi nesachim ve-ein ovdei kochavim mevi nesachim* — a citizen brings nesachim but a non-Jew does not. One might then conclude that even the olah of a non-Jew cannot come with nesachim. *Talmud lomar kachah* — the Torah insists on the proper procedure, establishing that the olah of a non-Jew does require nesachim; but a non-Jew cannot independently dedicate nesachim on their own.

### Competing interpretations resolved

*[Continued from above]*

The Gemara asks: *mani* — whose view does this braita reflect? Apparently neither Rabbi Yosi HaGlili nor Rabbi Akiva straightforwardly: if it is Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, he explicitly permits a non-Jew to bring wine independently, yet this braita excludes wine; if it is Rabbi Akiva, he permits only olah, yet this braita permits olah with its nesachim. The Gemara resolves the apparent incompatibility in both directions. *Ei ba'it eima Rabbi Yosi HaGlili — sami mikan yayin*: the braita may follow Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, with wine alone excluded (derived from the word *ezrach*), though this exclusion of wine is admittedly difficult given his own earlier formulation. The preferable resolution is *ei ba'it eima Rabbi Akiva — olah ve-chol chavratah*: the braita follows Rabbi Akiva, who permits an olah together with everything integral to it, but prohibits any other korban or independent dedication of nesachim. This accounts for the braita's position cleanly: a non-Jew brings an olah with its accompanying nesachim, but he may not dedicate nesachim independently, nor any sacrifice other than an olah.
