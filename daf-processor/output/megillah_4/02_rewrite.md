# Megillah 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hierarchy: Megilla

### Rava: Megilla > Avodah

Today's daf is 4. We pick up in the middle of 3b, still in the midst of discussions about the hierarchy of which activities carry greater weight: the *avodah* in the *Beis HaMikdash*, *Talmud Torah* of the individual, *Talmud Torah* of the *rabbim*, and *mikra megilla*. This fits into the larger theme about the tremendous weight given to the *megilla* — a weight that has been explored almost as one would treat a biblical text, with the innovation of the holiday and the *mitzvah* of *mikra megilla* treated not as a novelty but as something of absolute standing.

Rava opens: *pshita li avodah umikra megilla mikra megilla adif* — it is clear to me that if it comes to a choice between the *avodah* in the *Beis HaMikdash* and reading the *megilla*, reading the *megilla* takes precedence. This is derived from the principle established by Rav Yosef bar Chanina, which we examined on the previous daf, learned from the phrase *mishpacha mishpacha* — even the priestly and levitical families suspend their service. What makes this striking is that we are speaking about a *galus* holiday: even after returning to *Eretz Yisrael* with the *Beis HaMikdash* rebuilt, one might have supposed the whole enterprise of *mikra megilla* would appear trivial. Nevertheless, even the *avodah* — the central religious act of Temple service — is set aside for the sake of *mikra megilla*.

### Megilla > Talmud Torah

Rava continues: *Talmud Torah umikra megilla mikra megilla adif* — and if it comes to a choice between Torah study and *mikra megilla*, *megilla* again takes precedence. *Talmud Torah* is, of course, what takes the place of *korbanot* in the absence of the Temple, and yet *megilla* comes first. This is derived from the principle established by *Beis Rabbi*, that one is *mevatel Talmud Torah* for this purpose. While the *Gemara* earlier appeared to limit the *kal vachomer* specifically to *Talmud Torah d'yachid*, in the end the *Gemara* does not draw a sharp distinction, and it remains at least ambiguous whether *Talmud Torah d'rabbim* is likewise set aside for *mikra megilla*. In any case, *mikra megilla* takes precedence over everything considered so far.

Rava then adds a further point that is clear to him: *Talmud Torah umes mitzvah mes mitzvah adif* — between Torah study and attending to a *mes mitzvah*, a corpse with no one else to bury it, the *mes mitzvah* takes precedence. The source is a *Beraita*: *mevatlin Talmud Torah lehotza'as hameis ulehachnasas kallah* — one sets aside Torah study to escort a body to burial and to escort a bride, because these are acts whose honor increases with greater participation, whether in mourning or in rejoicing.

Tosafot notes, however, that the *hachnasat kallah* mentioned here is not the same as *mes mitzvah*. The statement does not say one acts specifically for *mes mitzvah*; rather, as Tosafot explains, *lav davka mes mitzvah* — the rule applies to all the dead of the world, not only to a *mes mitzvah*. The practical qualification is that this does not mean everyone leaves the *yeshiva* for every funeral. The question of whether it is *lefi kevodo* — appropriate to the honor of the deceased — matters. But where more people are needed for a burial to be conducted properly, and no one else is available, that overrides *Talmud Torah* even without reaching the specific category of *mes mitzvah*.

Turning to *mes mitzvah* proper: *Avodah umes mitzvah mes mitzvah adif* — if it comes to a choice between the *avodah* and a *mes mitzvah*, the *mes mitzvah* takes precedence. A *kohen* would leave the *avodah* in the *Beis HaMikdash* to attend to it. The source is the verse *velachoto* in the context of the *nazir*, who may not contract corpse-impurity even for an immediate relative. A *kohen gadol* is similarly prohibited, as is a *nazir*. Yet the *Torah*'s repetition — specifying father, mother, brother, sister — even after having already stated the general prohibition for any relative, teaches through each additional word that one would still become *tamei* for a *mes mitzvah*. And the final word, *velachoto*, yields the most extreme case.

A *Beraita* elaborates: *velachoto mah talmud lomar* — why does the verse say "to his sister"? To tell you: not to your sister, but yes to a *mes mitzvah*. The extreme example given is a person on his way to slaughter his *korban pesach* and to perform *bris milah* for his son — two urgent *mitzvos*, one of which cannot be made up later — who then learns that an immediate relative has died. One might think he should involve himself in the burial. No: the verse says he may not become *tamei* to a relative. But perhaps he likewise may not become *tamei* even for a *mes mitzvah*? The verse therefore teaches: *le'achoto hu de'eino metame aval metame lemes mitzvah* — to his sister he does not become *tamei*, but he does become *tamei* for a *mes mitzvah*, even at the cost of the *korban pesach* and the *bris milah*, and even if the person in question is a *nazir* who is also a *kohen gadol*. The *Gemara* is making an extremely powerful statement: all these accumulated levels of *kedusha* and all these other *mitzvos* yield to the imperative of *mes mitzvah*.

## Met Mitzvah Debate

### Nazir Textual Proof

The textual derivation itself warrants attention. The *Torah* states the general rule that the *nazir* may not come into contact with any dead, and then elaborates: father, mother, brother, sister. Since the general prohibition was already stated, the *Torah*'s enumeration of specific relatives serves to teach that these are the ones for whom he does not become *tamei* — but only because they are relatives. For a *mes mitzvah*, however, he would. And with each successive relative mentioned, the case becomes more extreme: even to one's sister he will not become *tamei*, and yet he will become *tamei* to a *mes mitzvah* regardless of what other *mitzvos* must thereby be forfeited.

### Rava's Question

Rava has now established that *megilla* surpasses *avodah* and *Talmud Torah*, and that *mes mitzvah* similarly surpasses *avodah* and *Talmud Torah*. The one relationship left unresolved is that between *mikra megilla* and *mes mitzvah*. So *baei Rava* — Rava poses the question: *mikra megilla umes mitzvah hai minaihu adif* — which of these two takes precedence? Given the extraordinary weight assigned to *mes mitzvah* throughout the preceding discussion, it would seem difficult to argue that *megilla* could outweigh it. Yet the answer is not foregone.

## Hierarchy: Megilla…

### Pirsumei Nisa

The *Gemara* now articulates for the first time the principle underlying *megilla*'s remarkable weight: *mikra megilla adif mishum pirsumei nisa* — reading the *megilla* takes precedence because of the publicizing of the miracle. This is an important point that extends beyond the *megilla* itself. The concern is not merely that a *mitzvah* be performed, but that everyone actively participates together — for the sake of *pirsumei nisa*, the public proclamation of the miracle. And this may well explain what we observed on the previous daf: why the *kohanim* leave the *Beis HaMikdash*, why the *yeshiva* students leave the *beis medrash*. The demand is not just to read the *megilla* privately at a convenient time. It is that everyone comes together, because the essence of the *mitzvah* is the communal act of publicizing the miracle. Perhaps, then, this same logic would cause *megilla* to override even *mes mitzvah*.

## Met Mitzvah Debate…

### Kevod Habriyot

Yet the *Gemara* entertains the counter-argument: *o dilma mes mitzvah adif mishum kevod habriyos* — perhaps *mes mitzvah* takes precedence on account of the honor due to God's creatures. The theme of *kevod habriyos* here names what is at stake: a body lying unattended, treated with disregard, is a profound affront to human dignity. After deliberating, *sofo debayei ahadar pashiteih* — after raising the question, Rava came to a conclusion: *mes mitzvah adif*. The reason: *gadol kevod habriyos shedocheh lo sa'aseh shebaTorah* — the principle of *kevod habriyos* is so weighty that it overrides a negative prohibition in the *Torah*.

This ruling rests on a *Gemara* in *Berachos*. The primary context there is that a person of great dignity — a scholar or elder — for whom it would be beneath their station to personally retrieve a lost object would not do so even for their own property. Such a person may therefore set aside not only the positive *mitzvah* of *hashevas aveida* but also the negative prohibition of *lo suchal lehitalem* — one may not avert one's eyes from an *aveida*. The *Gemara* there limits this significantly, and the application to *de'oraisa* prohibitions is quite narrow. But the significance of the principle remains. The common assumption — that ritual activities directed toward God are of greater religious weight than those directed toward human beings — is here turned on its head. Here, a *mitzvah* whose foundation is *kevod habriyos*, the honor owed to a human being even in death, overrides all of the others.

One might ask: but didn't we just establish that *Talmud Torah keneged kulam* — Torah study outweighs everything? The response is that the teaching of *Talmud Torah keneged kulam* speaks to the *sechar* of the *mitzvah*, not to the question of momentary precedence. For this particular moment, one must address the immediate situation. The more striking case is *avodah*, since one might ask whether the *avodah* is truly lost or merely delayed — and indeed, *Talmud Torah* often yields to other *mitzvos* precisely because it can be performed at another time, as part of the broader principle that *oseik bemitzvah patur min hamitzvah*.

## Walled City Status

### Kerach & Neighbors

With these hierarchies established, the *Gemara* turns to a series of statements by Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi addressing specific *halachos* of *mikra megilla*. The first: *kerach vechol hasamuch lo vechol hanireh imo nidon kekerach* — a walled city (*kerach*), and any place near it and any place visible together with it, is judged like the walled city and reads on the fifteenth of Adar.

A *Braisa* refines this: *samuch af al pi she'eino nireh, nireh af al pi she'eino samuch* — either condition suffices. Being close even without being visible, or being visible even without being close. The *Gemara* asks: how can something be visible without being close? The answer: *deyavsa berosh hahar* — if the city sits atop a mountain, a village far below may have a clear line of sight to it, and the distance is no obstacle.

The reverse case — nearby but not visible — is explained by Rabbi Yirmiya: *sheyeshevet benachal* — the city sits in a wadi, and what lies at ground level above cannot be seen from within it, even though it is not far. The precise meaning of *nira imo* — "visible with it" — admits of some discussion. It may mean both places are visible from a third vantage point rather than each from the other, but the *Gemara*'s answer concerning the mountain suggests the perspective is that of the city itself.

### Wall-Then-Settle Rule

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi further states: *krach veyashevu ulebasof hukaf nidon kekfar* — a *kerach* that was first settled and only afterward walled is treated like a village. The wall must precede the settlement; if settlement came first, the later wall does not confer *kerach* status.

The source is the verse concerning houses in walled cities in the laws of *Yovel*: *ve'ish ki yimkor beis moshav ir chomah* — if a person sells a house of settlement in a walled city. The verse specifies *beis moshav* within an *ir chomah*, implying the *ir chomah* existed prior to it being a *beis moshav*. Rashi reads this statement as applying only to the laws of houses in walled cities and not to *hilchos megilla*, where, he maintains, the order of settlement and walling would be irrelevant. But Tosafot disagrees with Rashi, and indeed the flow of the *Gemara* suggests otherwise.

What the *Gemara* appears to be doing is borrowing a pre-existing category from *batei arei chomah* — the laws of houses in walled cities — and applying it to the definition of what constitutes a walled city for *megilla*. This is a recognizable rabbinic technique: rather than constructing a new definition from scratch, one borrows and repurposes a prior category. A parallel example appears at the beginning of *Gittin*, where the parameters of *Eretz Yisrael* for the obligation of saying *bifnai nichtav uvifnai nechtam* are derived from the laws of *trumos umaasros*, even though the considerations are quite different. The convenience of reusing a definition outweighs the imperfect fit.

This also helps explain the otherwise puzzling requirement of *mukaf choma mi'yimos Yehoshua bin Nun* — walled from the time of Joshua son of Nun. This criterion is not arbitrary. It is a *halacha* that exists in other contexts. The *Mishnayos* at the beginning of *Keilim* list levels of *kedusha* in *Eretz Yisrael*, and between *Yerushalayim* and the remainder of the land stands an intermediate category: walled cities from the time of Joshua son of Nun. Such cities carry the status of *machaneh Yisrael*: just as the *metzora* must be sent outside the *machaneh*, so too must the *metzora* be sent outside these walled cities. This pre-existing *halacha* — which already attached significance to walled cities specifically from the time of Joshua's conquest — is what the *Gemara* borrows and applies here. The criterion of *yimos Yehoshua bin Nun* is not invented for *megilla*; it is imported from an already operative legal category.

### Asara Batlanim

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi adds a further requirement: *krach she'ein bo asara batlanim nidon kekfar* — a *kerach* that does not have ten *batlanim*, ten people who sit available for communal needs at all times, is treated as a village. The *Gemara* asks what novelty this adds, since the later *Mishnah* already states: *eizu hi ir gedola kol sheyesh bah asara batlanim* — what is a large city? Any that has ten *batlanim*; fewer than that, it is a village.

The answer is: *krach itztricha lei* — the novelty is specifically regarding a *kerach*, a walled city or major metropolitan center. One might have thought that a *kerach*, which draws enormous foot traffic from outside its walls, never lacks for people and therefore need not have *asara batlanim* as a resident requirement. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches that even so, *asara batlanim* is required.

The question is what status the *asara batlanim* requirement governs. The later *Mishnah* uses it to determine whether a place is an *ir gedola* reading on the proper date or a *kfar* that reads on a market day. The question here is whether the *Gemara* means that without *asara batlanim*, even a city walled from the time of *Yehoshua bin Nun* cannot read on the fifteenth. Tosafot resists this reading. Tosafot holds that a city genuinely walled from the time of *Yehoshua bin Nun* requires no further conditions — the *Gemara* would have stated explicitly that even such a city loses its status without *asara batlanim*. Tosafot therefore reads the case as involving a city with a wall, but not one from the time of *Yehoshua bin Nun*, and the question is whether such a city has at least the status of an *ir* for reading on the fourteenth, as opposed to being demoted to a *kfar* that reads on a market day. The plain reading of the *Gemara* likely goes the other way — that even the question of reading on the fifteenth requires *asara batlanim* — but Tosafot explicitly rejects this.

### Destroyed City

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi continues: *krach she-charav u-le-sof yashav nidon ke-krach* — if a *kerach* was destroyed and subsequently resettled, it retains the status of a *kerach*.

The *Gemara* asks: what precisely does "destroyed" mean? If it means the walls were knocked down while people continued to live there, and the walls were later rebuilt, one might read the statement as saying: only when people are present does it retain its status. But a *Beraita* cites Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yose interpreting the phrase *asher lo chomah* — "which has a wall" — by reading it both ways: *lo* with a *vav*, meaning it has a wall, and *lo* with an *alef*, meaning it does not. The implication is that even if the city currently has no wall, as long as it once had one, it retains the status of a house in a walled city. The same principle, borrowed once again from *batei arei chomah*, applies here: a city need not have its wall standing right now to retain its status. This has significant practical implications. One cannot assume that a city with a wall today reads on the fifteenth — it must be determined whether the wall dates to the time of *Yehoshua bin Nun*. But equally, one cannot assume that a city without a wall today fails to qualify — it may once have had such a wall.

If the destruction of walls is irrelevant to status, what kind of destruction matters? The *Gemara* concludes: *she-charav me-asara batlanim* — it lost its ten *batlanim*. A city that once had *asara batlanim* and lost them loses its status, and when it regains them it regains the status.

The *Gemara* has thus introduced two principles borrowed from *batei arei chomah* and applied to *megilla*: first, the wall must precede the settlement; second, the absence of a wall today does not strip a city of its prior status. These, together with the underlying criterion of *yimos Yehoshua bin Nun*, form a coherent system largely constructed by transplanting pre-existing legal categories.

### City Examples

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi cites a specific tradition: *Lod ve-Ono ve-Gai ha-Charashim* — these three cities, mentioned in *Tanach* in connection with the tribe of Binyamin, were *mukafot chomah mi-yemos Yehoshua bin Nun*. He had a reliable tradition to this effect.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: were these walls built by Joshua? A verse in *Divrei HaYamim* states that the sons of Elpaal — Eber, Misham, and Shemer — built *Ono* and *Lod* and its daughters. Since Elpaal lived at the time of *pilegesh be-Giv'ah*, long after Joshua, these walls would appear to postdate Joshua's conquest. The *Gemara* counters: but perhaps it was Asa who built them, as derived from a verse indicating that Asa built fortified cities in Yehudah and Binyamin.

Rabbi Elazar resolves the apparent contradiction: all three accounts are accurate. These cities were indeed walled from the time of *Yehoshua bin Nun*. The walls were destroyed in the time of *pilegesh be-Giv'ah*, when Binyamin was devastated and its cities ruined. Elpaal then came and rebuilt those walls. Over time they deteriorated further, and Asa came and reinforced them. The verse supporting Asa's role — *va-yomer li-Yehudah nivneh et he-arim ha-eleh* — indicates that "these cities" already existed; he was improving, not building from nothing. All three figures acted upon walls that originally dated to Joshua.

This leaves open a number of important practical *halacha* questions. What does one do in the absence of a tradition? If a city has a wall but one does not know whether it dates to *Yehoshua bin Nun*, does one assume everything defaults to the fourteenth, or does doubt require reading on both the fourteenth and fifteenth? And what of cases like *Yerushalayim* today, where there is continuous urban development far beyond the ancient walls — is the expanded city *samuch ve-nireh* to the walled portion throughout? These questions are left for further analysis.

## Women & Megilla

### Af Hen Hayu

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi states: *nashim chayavot be-mikra megilla she-af hen hayu be-oto ha-nes* — women are obligated in the reading of the *megilla*, because they too were included in that miracle. Although *mikra megilla* is a *mitzvas aseh she-hazman gerama* from which women would ordinarily be exempt, their obligation derives from the fact that they were equally subject to the decree and equally part of the salvation.

This same formulation — *af hen hayu be-oto ha-nes* — appears in two other contexts: the obligation of women in lighting *Chanukah* candles, and their obligation in the *arba kosos* on Pesach night. Three questions arise. First, what exactly does the phrase mean? The straightforward reading is that women were among those saved by the miracle, which would seem to apply to virtually every *mitzvah* commemorating a national deliverance. Why is the principle invoked only in these three cases?

A second, more expansive reading is offered by the Rashbam, cited in Tosafot: *she-ikar hanes haya al yadan* — the miracle was primarily effected through women. For *Purim*, this refers to Esther; for *Chanukah*, to Yehudit; for Pesach, to the famous *Gemara* that Israel was redeemed in the merit of the righteous women of that generation. Tosafot raises a difficulty with this reading: the language *af hen* — "even they" — implies a secondary role rather than a primary one. Though one could read *af* as "also instrumental" rather than "primarily responsible," the Rashbam's interpretation faces this linguistic challenge.

One answer to why specifically these three, offered by Tosafot, is that the principle of *af hen hayu be-oto ha-nes* operates on the rabbinic level. It is a *rabbanan* mechanism for including women in *mitzvos* that are themselves rabbinic in origin. Women's biblical obligation in *matzah*, for example, is derived separately. This principle is reserved for *rabbanan mitzvos*, which is why *Sukkah* and *lulav* are not subject to it.

The Rav Soloveitchik offers what is arguably the more satisfying answer. The three *mitzvos* in question share a specific and distinctive character: they are *mitzvos shel pirsum hanes*. How do we know that *mikra megilla* and lighting *Chanukah* candles carry this specific identity? Because they are the only two *mitzvos* for which one recites the *bracha* of *she'asa nissim*. No other *mitzvah* carries this *bracha*. The entire character of these *mitzvos* — the lighting of *neiros Chanukah* for all to see, the public reading of the *megilla* — is oriented toward *pirsumei nisa*. If the goal is to publicize the miracle, one wants maximum participation. And if women were part of the *nes*, then excluding them would undermine the very purpose of the *mitzvah*. The *arba kosos* similarly carries this character: drinking wine and reclining are the acts that demonstrate *bnei chorin*, that make freedom publicly manifest, rather than the eating of *matzah* and *maror*, which, while commemorative, are not in the same way acts of *pirsum*. Both the *nes* dimension and the *pirsumei* dimension work together to explain the inclusion of women in these specific *mitzvos*.

### Women Motzi Men?

The practical *halacha* that flows from this is debated. Tosafot notes: *mikan mashma she-nashim motzi'os et ha-acherim yedei chovatan* — the language of the *Gemara* implies that women can discharge the obligation of others, including men. The *Gemara* does not say women are obligated *to hear* the *megilla* — a formulation that would suggest a lesser level of obligation — but rather that they are obligated to read it, implying equal standing. A *Beraita* in *Arachin* states *hakol kesheirim likros et ha-megilla* — everyone is eligible to read the *megilla* — and the *Gemara* there clarifies that *hakol* comes to include women. This implies they may discharge the obligation even for men.

However, Tosafot raises a difficulty from a *Tosefta*: a *tumtum*, a person of indeterminate sex, cannot discharge the obligation of another *tumtum*, because the first may be female and the second male. This appears to presuppose that a woman cannot discharge a man's obligation. On this basis, *Hilchos Gedolos* rules: *isha motzi'a mina aval lo anashim* — a woman may discharge the obligation of another woman, but not of a man. Under this view, women's obligation is different in kind from men's — they are obligated to hear but not to read, which is a different level of obligation.

This ruling of *Hilchos Gedolos* runs against the simple reading of the *Gemara* and against many *Rishonim* who rule that women are fully and equally obligated. Yet many *Rishonei Ashkenaz* follow Tosafot in following *Hilchos Gedolos*, and this is the source of the ongoing discussion about women reading the *megilla* for men.

## Night & Day Reading

### Shabbat Purim

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi states: *Purim she-chal lihyot be-Shabbos sho'alin ve-dorshin be-inyano shel yom* — if *Purim* falls on *Shabbos*, even though the *megilla* is not read, one is obligated to teach and expound on the matters of the day. Rashi specifies that this means not merely discussing *hilchos Purim* in the abstract, but delivering a *shiur* on the *megilla* itself, as a substitute for the act of reading it.

The *Gemara* asks: what is the novelty here? Even on *Yom Tov* the same principle applies, as a *Beraita* states that Moshe ordained that Israel should inquire and expound the matters of each day: *hilchos Pesach be-Pesach, hilchos Atzeres be-Atzeres, hilchos chag be-chag*. Tosafot explains the actual *chiddush*: for *Yom Tov*, one begins discussing the *halachos* thirty days before the festival, and one continues teaching on the day itself. For *Purim*, there is no such thirty-day advance preparation; the entire obligation is concentrated on the day. It would thus be obvious that one must discuss *Purim* matters on *Purim* itself. The true novelty is different: one might have thought that expounding on the *megilla* on *Shabbos* is prohibited, on the grounds that studying it intensely might lead one to take out the *megilla* scroll, which cannot be carried on *Shabbos* — the same concern that, according to Rabbah, underlies the prohibition of reading the *megilla* itself on *Shabbos*. The *Gemara* therefore teaches that this concern does not apply to a *derasha*: delivering a *shiur* on the *megilla* does not engender the fear of carrying, since one does not bring a *megilla* to a lecture the way one brings it to a reader.

This teaching also connects to the principle of *pirsumei nisa*. The reading of the *megilla* is the primary act of publicizing the miracle. When that act cannot take place on the correct day, the obligation to engage the community with the content of the day — through *derasha* — reflects the same underlying imperative.

### Leil v'Yom

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi also states: *chayav adam likros es ha-megilla ba-layla u-lishnosah va-yom* — a person must read the *megilla* at night and repeat it in the day. The source is a verse from the *mizmor* beginning *Lamenatze'ach al Ayelet HaShachar* — understood to have been said in the context of Esther — which reads: *Elokai ekra yomam ve-lo ta'aneh ve-layla ve-lo dumiyah li*: God I call out to You by day and You do not answer, and by night I will not be silent. Rashi explains: the reading of the *megilla* at night and day is a *zeicher la-nes* — a commemoration of the miracle — recalling that in those days they cried out to God day and night. The *pesuk* thus frames *mikra megilla* in terms of the cry that preceded the miracle: one cannot fully appreciate the *nes* without appreciating the *tzara* that came before it.

### Shanota Meaning

When people heard the statement *u-lishnosah va-yom*, they interpreted *lishnosah* in its familiar rabbinic sense: *nikrevah ba-layla ve-nimni masnisa de-yomah* — we will read it at night and learn the *mishnaios* of *megilla* in the day. *Lishnosah* was understood as a form of *mishna* — a technical term for the rabbinic oral tradition — rather than simply "to repeat it."

Rabbi Yirmiyah corrected this: *lidi di mefarsha li minei de-Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* — it was explained to me by Rabbi Chiya bar Abba that the word simply means *kegone de-amri inshi, kol parsha da ve-asnayyah* — "let me go over this passage and repeat it." *Lishnosah* derives from *sheni* — doing something a second time — not from *leshanen* in the sense of rabbinic study. The intent is simply to read the *megilla* twice: once at night and once during the day.

The *hava amina* here is itself instructive. That it was even momentarily conceivable to substitute *Mishnah* learning for the day reading of the *megilla* reflects the broader context: the *megilla* arrives at the juncture between the era of *nevuah* and the era of rabbinic transmission, between *Ezra HaSofer* and the beginnings of *Torah she-ba'al peh*. The idea that *mishna* learning could stand in for *mikra megilla* captures something of the ambiguity of this transitional period.

### Constant Praise

A parallel statement is cited: Rabbi Chelbo said in the name of Ulla Bira — *chayav adam likros es ha-megilla ba-layla u-leshonosa ba-yom* — one must read the *megilla* at night and repeat it in the day, based on a different verse: *lema'an yezamercha chavod ve-lo yidom, Hashem Elokai le'olam odeka* — so that my soul may sing to You and not be silent; Lord my God, I will thank You forever. Here the emphasis is on continuous praise throughout the day, framing the two readings as an act of unceasing thanksgiving for the miracle. Together with the previous source, which stressed the cry of distress, the two *pesukim* jointly frame the *mitzvah*: the *tzara* that preceded the *nes* and the praise that follows it.

One point of practical consequence must be noted. The phrasing throughout — read at night and "repeat" in the day — implies that the night reading is primary and the day reading secondary. Yet all the *mishnaios* that follow treat the *mitzvah* of *megilla* as a daytime *mitzvah*; the question of when the *mitzvah* begins is addressed entirely in terms of the day. The correct understanding, as some *Acharonim* frame it, is that the daytime reading is the primary *mitzvah*, grounded in the *psukim*, while the nighttime reading is a pure *rabbanan* addition. Tosafot presses the language of the *Gemara* to arrive at the same conclusion. The practical implication is significant: if one can attend only one reading, one should attend the day reading rather than the night reading. In practice, many are insufficiently careful about attending the day reading after having attended the night before — but it is the day reading that is *halachically* primary.

## Village Market Days

### Early Reading Reason

The *Gemara* now introduces Rabbi Chanina's explanation of the institution allowing villages to read the *megilla* early, on market days: *Chachamim he-kilu al ha-kefarim lihyos makdimin le-yom ha-knisa* — the Sages were lenient with the villages to allow them to advance the reading to the market day. The stated reason is *kedei she-yispeku mayim u-mazon la-acheihem she-ba-krachim* — so that they could provide water and food to their brethren in the large cities.

This framing is striking. It positions the villages in an entirely secondary role: their early reading is arranged not for their own convenience but so that on *Purim* itself they will be free to staff their stores and supply provisions to the large cities. They are, in effect, told to complete their religious obligation in advance so as to be available to serve others on the proper day. It is worth noting that while Rashi on the first daf of the tractate states that the market day was chosen because the *batei dinim* were convened then and villagers came to town, Rabbeinu Chananel frames it differently: people came to the large towns on Mondays and Thursdays in order to pray with a *tzibbur* and hear the *Sefer Torah* read. In either case, these were days when villagers were already traveling to town.

### Corrected Reading

The *Gemara* challenges this framing. If the early reading is intended to benefit the large cities, then on weeks when *Purim* falls on a Monday or Thursday, the villages should be pushed even further back — to a prior Thursday or Monday — so as to be fully available on *Purim* itself. The *Gemara* tests this against several cases. When *Purim* falls on a Monday, both villages and large towns read on that same Monday; the villages are not pushed back to the previous Thursday. When *Purim* falls on a Thursday, similarly everyone reads on Thursday. In neither case are the villagers reading early for the stated purpose.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *lo teima kedei she-yispeku* — do not read it as "so that they should provide." Rather: *aval ela eima mipnei she-mesapkim* — because they regularly provide. The inversion is significant. The early reading is not instituted *in order that* the villagers should serve the large cities; rather, *because* villagers characteristically come in on market days and provide for the larger communities, the Sages were kind to them and extended them the leniency of reading early on those same days. The institution is a favor to the villages, not to the cities.

### Conditional Application

Rabbi Yehudah's statement clinches this reinterpretation: *ematai bimkom she-nichnasim be-sheni uve-chamishi* — the leniency of early reading applies only in places where villagers regularly come in on Mondays and Thursdays. *Aval makom she-ein nichnasim be-sheni uve-chamishi ein korin ela bizmana* — but if villagers in a given locale do not regularly come in on those days, they have no dispensation to read early and must read on the proper day.

If the entire purpose were to benefit the large cities, this limitation would make no sense. The *Gemara* asks as much: if we are doing all this for the betterment of the large cities, what difference does it make whether the villagers normally come in on those days? Make them come in anyway and be available to serve. The fact that the leniency is conditional on the villages' regular practice of coming in demonstrates conclusively that the institution is for the villages' own benefit. As Rabbeinu Chananel explains, the underlying logic is that on a market day the villagers are already in town — in effect, if this provision were not made, they would simply not come in on Thursday, reasoning that they had to come anyway the next day for *Purim*. Allowing them to read on the market day keeps the markets functioning and rewards the villages for the service they already provide.
