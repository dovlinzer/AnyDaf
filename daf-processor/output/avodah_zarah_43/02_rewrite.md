# Avodah Zarah 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah & Overview

### Mishnah Review

We pick up at the bottom of 42b, in the middle of a broader discussion that benefits from being surveyed as a whole before diving into the details. The *mishnah* states: *hamotze kelim ve'aleihem tzurat chama, tzurat levana, tzurat drakon — yolichem le'yam hamelach* — if one finds vessels upon which are depicted the form of the sun, the form of the moon, or the form of a dragon, one must take them to the Dead Sea. The *mishnah* then proceeds to distinguish between idolatrous objects that are *mechubad* — honorable or respectable — and those that are *mevuzeh* — disgraceful — a distinction that will occupy us later in the discussion.

It is worth pausing at the outset to appreciate the scope of the problem. The *mishnah*'s prohibition on the *tzurat levana* immediately raises a difficulty: we know from Tractate Rosh Hashanah that Rabban Gamliel kept images of the moon in his attic in order to show simple witnesses what shape they had observed in the sky, so they could properly testify to the new month. Similarly, the prayer books contain references to *Rav hitkin tzurat halevana* — Rav established the form of the moon in the liturgy. Where exactly are the lines between objects we encounter, symbolic depictions, and tools used for legitimate purposes? This is the overarching project of our *daf*.

### Rav Sheshet's Chart

A few lines below the *mishnah*, Rav Sheshet attempts to organize the various stringencies into a coherent framework. The result is essentially a chart of what is permitted and what is forbidden across three domains. Regarding the *mazalot* — the constellations — everything is permitted except for the sun and the moon, *chama u'levana*. Regarding *partzufim* — faces — everything is permitted except for a human face, *partzuf adam*. And regarding *tzurot* — figures or shapes generally — everything is permitted except for the dragon, *drakon*.

### Three Categories

This chart, however, immediately raises a question: with respect to which legal situation does each clause of Rav Sheshet's statement apply? The *gemara* identifies three distinct categories that must be kept in mind throughout: *oseh* — one who makes such an image; *oved* — one who worships it; and *motze* — one who finds it. Each of these categories carries different legal implications, and the permissibility or prohibition of any given figure depends heavily on which category we are discussing.

If Rav Sheshet's statement that "all constellations are permitted except the sun and the moon" refers to one who *makes* them, the question is immediate: how can any constellation be permitted to make at all? We have an explicit *pasuk* at the end of *Parshat Yitro* in *Shemot* chapter 20: *lo taasun iti elohei chesef v'elohei zahav lo taasu lachem* — "You shall not make with Me gods of silver, and gods of gold you shall not make for yourselves." The word *iti* — "with Me" — is notably unusual; it seems to say "do not make something 'with Me'" or "alongside Me." The *gemara* understands this as a prohibition against making images of God's heavenly *shamashim*, His celestial attendants. If so, making *any* constellation image would be forbidden, making Rav Sheshet's statement difficult to understand as referring to *making*.

The *gemara* therefore concludes that the first clause of Rav Sheshet's statement — about *mazalot* — must refer to *motze*, one who finds. This connects directly to the *mishnah*: *hamotze kelim va'aleihem tzurat chama...* — when one finds vessels bearing these images. But this creates a new tension with the middle clause. The *gemara* probes whether the statement can be internally consistent, or whether we must understand the beginning and end (*reisha* and *seifa*) as referring to *motze* and the middle (*metziaita*) as referring to *oseh*.

Abaye accepts this division: *reisha v'seifa b'motze, u'metziaita b'oseh* — the beginning and end address one who finds, while the middle clause about human faces addresses one who makes. Rava offers an alternative: the entire statement of Rav Sheshet refers to *motze*, and the middle clause — permitting all faces except a human face — represents the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. For Rabbi Yehuda, as a *baraita* records, adds two further figures to the list of prohibited images: *dmut meinika* — a nursing mother — and *Sar Apis*.

The *meinika* is described as a mother nursing the entire world, *al shem Chava she'meinika kol ha'olam kulo* — in the name of Eve, who nurses all of creation. This figure is prohibited specifically when she is depicted holding a child and nursing it. The *Sar Apis* figure — identifiable with the Egyptian deity Serapis — is described as a minister who feeds and appeases the whole world, *al shem Yosef she'sar umefayes et kol ha'olam kulo*, associated with Joseph who governed Egypt's food supply during the famine. This figure is forbidden specifically when depicted holding a *geriva* — a dry-measure vessel — in the manner of one engaged in his work. The commentators note that Steinsaltz suggests the nursing figure may alternatively represent Isis from Egyptian mythology or Dionysus being nursed by nymphs, though the association with a universal mother goddess is the central concern.

## Dragon Definition

### Spikes Between Joints

A *baraita* opens the question: *ezehu tzurat drakon?* — what constitutes the form of a dragon? Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar defines it: *kol she'yesh lo tzitzin bein parakav* — any creature that has spikes between its joints. Rashi explains that *tzitzin* is the Aramaic translation of *snapir* — fin — and alternatively understood as hair-like projections. The image is of spikes or protrusions emerging from between the vertebrae, the joints of the spine — very much what we would recognize today as a dragon or, one might say, a crocodilian creature. Rabbi Assi demonstrated by gesture that the relevant location is specifically *bein pirkei tzavar* — between the joints of the neck. Rav Chama bar Rabbi Chanina rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — the law follows Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. Tosafot ask what other opinion could have been contemplated that would require such a ruling, and answer that even if one were to find a dissenting *tanna*, the definition remains as given: the dragon is that figure bearing these distinctive fin-like spikes between its vertebrae.

### Ring Story

Rabba bar bar Chana — the same figure famous for his fantastical *aggadot* in Bava Batra, whose name always signals that something remarkable is coming — relates the following: *amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*, I was once walking behind Rabbi Elazar HaKappar on the road and we found a ring with a dragon image upon it. They then encountered a minor gentile, and Rabbi Elazar HaKappar said nothing to him. They subsequently encountered an adult gentile, and Rabbi Elazar HaKappar said to him: *batla* — revoke its status.

The legal problem underlying this story is significant. If one finds an object that constitutes *avodah zarah*, picking it up could be a form of *kinyan* — an act of acquisition — and one cannot simply dispose of it without first nullifying its idolatrous status. That nullification, *bitul*, must be performed by a gentile; a Jew cannot nullify idolatrous status. Hence Rabbi Elazar HaKappar's attempt to have a gentile revoke the status of the ring. The adult gentile initially refused — *lo batla* — and so Rabbi Elazar HaKappar slapped him across the face, at which point the gentile relented and revoked its status.

### Three Lessons

From this incident, the *gemara* derives three lessons — *shma mina tlat*.

The first: *oved kochavim mevatel avodat kochavim shelo v'shel chaveiro* — a gentile can nullify idolatrous status not only of his own objects but even of objects belonging to someone else. The non-owner has the authority to perform *bitul*.

The second: *yodeia b'tiv shel avodat kochavim u'meshamsheiha mevatel, v'she'eino yodeia b'tiv avodat kochavim u'meshamsheiha eino mevatel* — one who understands the nature of idolatry and its attendant practices can perform *bitul*, but one who lacks this understanding cannot. This explains why the minor was passed over: a child who has not yet developed sufficient understanding of the nature of idolatrous worship cannot effectively revoke its status. This lesson simultaneously limits the first one — while a non-owner can perform *bitul*, that non-owner must possess the requisite knowledge.

The third lesson: *oved kochavim mevatel be'al korcho* — a gentile can be coerced into revoking idolatrous status even against his will. The slap, unconventional as it was, was effective and permissible.

There is a noted debate in the *Rambam* and *Shulchan Aruch* about whether a minor who does in fact understand the nature of idolatry can perform *bitul*. The *gemara* itself implies not. The *Rambam* rules that a gentile can nullify even an idol that is not his own, but requires that the gentile be an idol worshiper — a non-idolatrous gentile would lack the standing to perform this nullification.

## Yeush & Ownership

### Owner's Despair

A challenge is now raised against Rabbi Elazar HaKappar's entire procedure. We know from the laws of lost objects in Bava Metzia that if an item is lost in a public area where the owner is unlikely to recover it — such as a city plaza, *platyia* — the owner is presumed to have despaired of recovering it, *mityashim*. The *mishnah* there teaches: one who saves something from a lion, a bear, a tiger, military forces, a flooding river, or a tidal surge, or who finds something on a well-traveled road or in a public gathering place — these belong to the finder, because the owners have despaired of recovering them. If so, why did Rabbi Elazar HaKappar need to arrange for *bitul* at all? The ring, found presumably in a public area, should already be treated as ownerless by virtue of the owner's *yeush*!

### Yeush Split

Abaye provides the critical distinction: *nihi d'min mityaiesh m'isura mi mityaiesh* — true, the owner may have despaired with respect to *ownership*, but has he despaired with respect to the *issur*, the idolatrous prohibition? These are two separate matters that *yeush* addresses independently.

### Future Worship

Abaye elaborates: the owner reasons as follows. If a gentile finds this ring, he will simply take it and worship it — *palach lah*. If a Jew finds it, since the object is valuable, the Jew will sell it to a gentile who will then worship it. In either case, the idol will continue to be worshiped. Therefore, while the original owner has abandoned hope of physically recovering his property, he has *not* abandoned his expectation that the object will continue to function as an idol. The idolatrous *status* remains intact even after *yeush* dissolves the ownership. Two things attach to an object of *avodah zarah* — ownership and idolatrous status — and *yeush* affects only the former. This is why Rabbi Elazar HaKappar still needed to obtain a declaration of *bitul* from a gentile: without it, the ring retained its prohibited character regardless of who held it.

## Rabban Gamliel

### Moon Charts

The *gemara* now introduces a case from Tractate Rosh Hashanah: *dmut tzorot levanot hayu lo l'Rabban Gamliel b'aliyato b'tavla bakotel* — Rabban Gamliel had images of the shapes of the moon in his attic, on a tablet on the wall. With these, he would show ordinary witnesses — *hedyotot* — saying to them: "Did you see it like this, or like this?" The purpose was calendrical: witnesses who came to testify about the new moon often could not articulate precisely what shape they had observed, and Rabban Gamliel's chart allowed them to identify what they had seen.

The problem, of course, is glaring. The *mishnah* we have been studying explicitly prohibits *tzurat levana* — the form of the moon. How could Rabban Gamliel, of all people, maintain such images in his home?

### Lo Ta'asun Iti

The *gemara* focuses on the foundational prohibition derived from *Shemot* 20. The *pasuk* says: *lo taasun iti* — the word *iti* being ambiguous, as noted earlier. The *gemara* understands this as a prohibition against making images of God's celestial *shamashim*, the beings who serve before Him on high. This sets the stage for a series of attempts by Abaye to define the scope of this prohibition in a way that would leave room for Rabban Gamliel's moon charts.

### High & Low

The discussion introduces a distinction between *shamashim shel ma'ala* — the upper celestial servants — and *shamashim shel mata* — the lower celestial servants. The *Aseret Hadibrot* in the Ten Commandments amplifies the prohibition further: *lo ta'aseh lecha pesel v'chol tmunah asher bashamayim mima'al, asher ba'aretz mita'at, va'asher bamayim mita'at la'aretz* — you shall not make any graven image or likeness of what is in heaven above, on earth below, or in the water beneath the earth. This stratified cosmology — upper heavens, earth, water beneath — encompasses an enormous range of beings, and the challenge is to find within it some space for legitimate representation.

A parallel *baraita* reinforces the difficulty: one may not construct a house in the form of the *heichal*, a porch in the form of the *ulam*, a courtyard in the form of the *azara*, a table in the form of the Temple table, or a *menorah* in the form of the Temple *menorah*. He may, however, make a *menorah* of five branches, six, or eight — but not seven. The implicit principle is that exact replication of what belongs exclusively to the sacred realm is forbidden. Rabbi Yosi bar Yehuda adds: one may not make a *menorah* even of wood in the manner the Hasmoneans did. The *gemara* notes that the Hasmoneans, in the early days after the rededication of the Temple, fashioned their *menorah* from iron rods covered with tin; when they grew wealthier, they made one of silver, and wealthier still, of gold — showing that they worked their way up through materials as resources permitted.

## Abaye's Solutions

### Lower Servants Only

Abaye's first attempt to resolve the problem of Rabban Gamliel's moon images proposes that the prohibition of *lo taasun iti* applies only to the lower celestial servants, not the upper ones. The moon, on this reading, would be an upper celestial servant and therefore *not* subject to the prohibition — which would explain how Rabban Gamliel could possess such images.

### Chariot Challenge

This proposal is immediately challenged. A *baraita* teaches: *lo taasun kidmut shamashay hameshamshim lefanai bamarom kegon ofanim v'serafim v'chayot hakodesh u'malachei hasharet* — do not make images of My attendants who serve before Me on high, namely the *ofanim*, *serafim*, *chayot hakodesh*, and ministering angels. These are explicitly the upper celestial figures. Abaye's first reading — that only the lower servants are forbidden — thus collides with a text that forbids the upper servants. Moreover, if the prohibition is limited to the four-faced figures of the heavenly *chariot* — the lion, the ox, the eagle, and the human — this would paradoxically *permit* images of the sun and moon, which is contrary to the *mishnah*. And it would also suggest that the human face is permitted, which contradicts Rav Sheshet's ruling that *partzuf adam* is specifically forbidden.

### God's Image

Rav Yehuda, the son of Rav Yehoshua, reports a teaching he heard from Rabbi Yehoshua: the word *iti* in the *pasuk* should be read not as *iti* — "with Me" — but as *oti* — "Me." Thus: *lo taasun oti* — do not make *Me*. What does it mean to make an image of God? God's image is the human being — *b'tzelem Elohim bara oto*. The prohibition, on this reading, is specifically against making a human face, *partzuf adam*. Other celestial *shamashim* would then be permitted. This reading elegantly explains why a human face is uniquely singled out while other constellations and figures are not — because the human face specifically represents the divine image.

### Decalogue Forbids

This solution is itself challenged. The *baraita* teaches that the verse *asher bashamayim* — "that is in the heavens" — comes to include the sun, the moon, the stars, and the constellations, while *mima'al* — "above" — comes to include the ministering angels. On its face, this would suggest that a blanket prohibition covers the making of any celestial image at all, including the moon. How, then, can any of these be permitted?

## Make vs. Worship

### Worship Not Make

The *gemara* resolves this tension with a crucial distinction: *ki tanya hahi le'ovdam* — that *baraita* is referring to *worship*, not to *making*. The prohibition stated in the *Aseret Hadibrot* against images in heaven above and earth below is a prohibition against *worshipping* those things — it is not a prohibition against *forming* images of them. The category of *oseh* — making — and the category of *oved* — worshipping — are governed by different texts and carry different scope.

### Even Tiny Worms

This interpretation carries a striking implication. If the verse addresses worship, then its coverage must extend to absolutely everything that exists — no created thing may be worshipped. The *gemara* makes this vivid: *afilu shilshul katan nami* — even the tiniest worm is included in the prohibition against worship. The verse *asher ba'aretz* — "that is on the earth" — comes to include seas, rivers, mountains, and hills, while *mita'at* — "beneath" — comes to include even the smallest worm. No living thing, however insignificant, may be the object of worship.

### Making Allowed?

With the worship prohibition thus understood, the question pivots: if *making* these images is not addressed by the verses about worship, who then permitted Rabban Gamliel to *make* or *own* moon images at all? Surely the prohibition against making images of celestial *shamashim* — derived from *lo taasun iti* — would still apply? This is the question that drives the final section of this discussion.

## Gamliel Solutions

### Others Made It

The *gemara* offers its first answer: *shani Rabban Gamliel de'acherim asu lo* — Rabban Gamliel's case is different, because others made those images for him. He himself did not violate the prohibition against making, since the images were produced by someone else. This is a significant distinction: the prohibition falls on the *maker*, not on the one who subsequently owns or uses the object. Owning an image that one did not make — and that one does not worship — apparently carries no prohibition.

This answer implies a broader principle: making is forbidden, worshipping is forbidden, finding requires *bitul* and proper disposal, but owning — when one neither made nor worships — is in itself permissible. As the *gemara* puts it: one may keep one's dragons.

### Disfigure Idol

The *gemara* then introduces a related case to probe this logic. Rav Yehuda had a signet ring made by others, and Shmuel said to him: *Shinnana* — a term of address — *sami einei d'din*, "destroy the eyes of this one." The instruction was to disfigure the ring so as to reduce or eliminate its idolatrous character. The reason: the ring's seal was *bolet* — in relief, projecting outward — so that when used to stamp an impression, it left an engraved image. A *baraita* rules: a ring whose seal is *bolet* may not be worn on the finger, but may be used to seal documents; a ring whose seal is *shokei'a* — sunken, intaglio — may be worn but may not be used to seal. The concern about wearing a ring with a projecting image is one of *chashda* — the appearance of carrying an idolatrous object.

The *gemara* then asks whether Shmuel would truly be concerned about mere appearance — *chashda* — given that the father of Shmuel and Levi used to pray in the synagogue of Shaf v'Yativ in Neharde'a, which contained an *andarta* — a statue or commemorative image, most likely of a king — erected within it. They were apparently unconcerned about *chashda*. The *gemara* answers: *rabbim shani* — a public context is different. Where many people are present, it is evident that no idolatrous intent is involved, and appearances are not misconstrued.

### Public Context

This principle of *rabbim* — publicity — is then applied back to Rabban Gamliel. The *gemara* asks: but Rabban Gamliel's attic was private; how does the public-context principle help him? The answer: *keivan deNasi hu, shchichi rabbim gabei* — since he was the *Nasi*, the political and religious leader of his generation, many people came to him regularly. His home, functionally, operated as a quasi-public domain. The presence of the moon charts in his attic was therefore more akin to a public setting than a private one.

### Incomplete Figures

An alternative answer is also offered: *deperakim hava* — the moon images he possessed were in pieces, fragmentary. Complete figures are what the prohibition targets; incomplete or partial representations do not carry the same status. This parallels the ruling about Rav Yehuda's ring — disfigure it, make it incomplete — and extends that logic to Rabban Gamliel's charts.

### Education Exemption

Finally, and perhaps most compellingly, the *gemara* offers a last answer: *lehilamed shani* — for purposes of teaching, the case is categorically different. This is derived from a verse in *Devarim*: *lo tilmad la'asot* — you shall not learn *to do* these things. The inference drawn is: you may not learn *in order to do*, but you *may* learn in order to understand and in order to teach others. The *gemara* states explicitly: *aval ata lamed lehavin ulehorot* — you may learn in order to comprehend and in order to instruct. Rabban Gamliel's use of moon images was for precisely this educational purpose — to enable witnesses to accurately describe what they had seen in the sky — and this exempts him from the prohibition.

This final answer deserves particular attention. It is striking that educational purpose can override what would otherwise be a clear prohibition. One might have expected the opposite: that a great *Nasi* would be held to a higher standard, not given additional latitude. Yet the *gemara* establishes that *lehilamed* — for the sake of learning and teaching — certain things are permitted that would otherwise be forbidden. The principle carries far beyond this specific case.

## Honored vs. Disgrace

### Shimon's Rule

Returning to the *mishnah*'s broader framework, Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel establishes a fundamental distinction: *she'al hamichubadim asurim, she'al hamevuzin mutarim* — items associated with honored, respectable objects are forbidden; items associated with disgraced, contemptible objects are permitted. The criterion is not the image itself in isolation but the context and dignity of the object upon which it appears.

### Rav: Above/Below

Rav interprets this distinction in terms of physical location relative to food or liquid: *michubadim lemala min hamayim, mevuzim lemata min hamayim* — objects positioned above the water line are considered honored; objects positioned below, where things become murky and degraded, are considered disgraced.

### Shmuel: Vessel Type

Shmuel disagrees with this positional criterion. In his view, both above and below can be either honored or disgraced — the determining factor is not location but the type of vessel involved. Objects worn or carried on the body in a dignified manner are *mechubad*; objects associated with cooking and domestic labor are *mevuzeh*.

### Item Examples

A *baraita* is cited that supports Shmuel's interpretation. Among the *mechubadim* — the honored items — are *shirin* (bracelets), *nezamim* (nose rings), and *taba'ot* (finger rings). Among the *mevuzim* — the disgraced items — are *yurot* (cooking pots), *kumkumsin* (kettles — the word *kumkum* survives in modern Hebrew), *machamei chamin* (water heaters), *sadinim* (linen garments), and *mitpachot* (kerchiefs or hand towels). The principle is consistent with Shmuel's reading: the dignity of the object derives from its social use — adornment on the person versus utilitarian domestic function.

## Destroy Idols

### Grind & Scatter

A final *mishnah* introduces Rabbi Yose's approach to the destruction of idols. Rabbi Yose rules: *shochek v'zoreh laruach, o metil layam* — one may grind the idol and scatter it to the wind, or cast it into the sea. This offers an alternative to the *mishnah*'s requirement of consignment to *Yam HaMelach*, addressing the practical difficulty of transporting an idolatrous object long distances to the Dead Sea.

### Mixing Problem

The *gemara*, however, identifies a problem with grinding and scattering to the wind. If an idol is ground into powder and scattered, the powder will inevitably mix with soil and compost — *zevel* — which is then used as fertilizer. A Jew who benefits from produce grown in that soil would thereby benefit, however indirectly, from *avodah zarah*. The prohibition against deriving benefit from idolatrous objects seems to extend even to this attenuated chain of benefit.

### Nothing Clings

The verse *lo yidbak b'yadcha m'uma min hacharem* — nothing of the condemned thing shall cling to your hand — is cited in response to this concern. The language evokes not merely legal prohibition but something closer to spiritual contamination: even the most attenuated contact with what has been consecrated to idolatry is to be avoided. The *gemara* treats this as analogous to an invisible but real danger — just as one would not casually disperse a hazardous material on the grounds that only a negligible amount remains, so too with the residue of *avodah zarah*.

### Golden Calf

The discussion concludes with a reference to the *Eigel Zahav* — the Golden Calf — and Moshe's response to it. Moshe ground the calf, scattered it upon the water, and made the Israelites drink, in a procedure deliberately parallel to the *sotah* trial. This acted as a kind of divine inquiry: those who had worshipped the calf would be identified through the effects of the water, just as the *sotah* water reveals guilt or innocence. The parallel between the Golden Calf episode and the laws of *avodah zarah* examined throughout this *daf* illuminates the gravity with which the *gemara* approaches the entire subject — and with this comparison, the discussion on 43 reaches its conclusion.
