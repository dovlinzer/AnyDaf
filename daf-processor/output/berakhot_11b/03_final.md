# Berakhot 11b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Review: Shema Positions

### Humility & Angels

The previous session covered several halachot relating to Shemoneh Esrei, all transmitted in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chanina citing Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov: standing in a low place, standing with one's feet together, and not eating before davening. The first two concern how one positions oneself physically, but beyond the mechanics, they speak to a deeper orientation of humility — standing in a low place evokes a sense of distance between oneself and God. At the same time, the requirement to stand with feet together, like the angels, speaks to aspiring toward the angelic. This captures the essential tension in davening: on one hand, we draw close to God; on the other, the vast gap between the human and the Divine is never forgotten.

### Zman Shema

The discussion of the time for Shema concluded that we rule leniently up to three hours into the day. The Hagra takes an even more lenient position, measuring three hours from sunrise rather than from alot hashachar. There remain significant questions about exactly when the zman begins, but the discussion of times was wrapped up and the Gemara moved into the next Mishnah — the debate between *beit shammai* and Beit Hillel concerning how one positions oneself for Shema: lying down at night or standing for the day according to Beit Shammai's more literal reading of the pasuk, versus the Chachamim's approach which is really about the time of day rather than the bodily posture.

### Brachos Around Shema

The session concluded with the times and positions for Shema and moved into the brachos before and after it. In the morning there are *shtei brachos* before and achat after; at night *shtei brachos* before and shtei after. The question turns to identifying what those brachos actually are.

### Yotzer Or & Borei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּשַּׁחַר מְבָרֵךְ שְׁתַּיִם לְפָנֶיהָ וְאַחַת לְאַחֲרֶיהָ. וּבָעֶרֶב מְבָרֵךְ שְׁתַּיִם לְפָנֶיהָ וּשְׁתַּיִם לְאַחֲרֶיהָ. אַחַת אֲרוּכָּה וְאַחַת קְצָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: From the laws of the recitation of *Shema* itself, the mishna proceeds to discuss the blessings recited in conjunction with *Shema*. Here, the order is established: **In** the **morning** when reciting *Shema*, **one recites two blessings beforehand,** the first on the radiant lights and the second the blessing on the love of Torah, **and one thereafter,** which begins with: True and Firm [*emet veyatziv*]. **And in the evening one recites two blessings beforehand,** on the radiant lights and on the love of God, **and two thereafter,** the blessing of redemption: True and Faithful [*emet ve'emuna*], and the blessing: Help us lie down. With regard to the blessing: True and Faithful, **whether** one recites it in its **long** formula **and whether** one recites it in its **short** formula, he fulfills his obligation (*Tosafot*).

The morning bracha is Yotzer Or Uvorei Choshech. The Gemara notes that this is a pasuk from Yeshayahu — Yotzer Or Uvorei Choshech — but the end of that pasuk in Yeshayahu reads Oseh Shalom Uvorei et HaRa, meaning God creates evil, which raises theological questions and was also intended to reject dualistic notions of divinity. Nevertheless, we euphemize, substituting Uvorei et HaKol. The word Choshech is retained in its literal sense because we want to mention both day and night: God creates both light and darkness, just as at night we say Golel Or Mipnei Choshech Vechoshech Mipnei Or — the first bracha of each tefillah mentions both the present time and its counterpart.

### Ahava Rabbah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי מְבָרֵךְ? אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר וּבוֹרֵא חֹשֶׁךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara begins by determining the formula of the two blessings preceding the morning *Shema*. The Gemara asks: **What blessing does one recite?** **Rabbi Ya'akov said in the name of Rabbi Oshaya:** The blessing focuses on the verse: **"Who forms light and creates darkness, Who makes peace and creates evil, I am the Lord Who does all these things" (Isaiah 45:7).**

The second bracha is Ahava Rabbah in the morning and Ahavat Olam in the evening. The Gemara raised the question of which to say, with debate between the two formulations. The theme of both connects tightly to Shema: God has loved us — Ahava Rabbah, Ahavat Olam — and we are to love God, V'ahavta et Hashem Elokecha. Moreover, the theme of Ahavat Olam and Ahava Rabbah is Torah — that God has given us Torah — and this parallels the central theme of Shema itself: *veshinantam levannecha vedibarta bam*, the mitzvah of Talmud Torah.

### Double Duty Brachos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא: ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר וּבוֹרֵא נוֹגַהּ״! כְּדִכְתִיב קָאָמְרִינַן. אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה ״עֹשֶׂה שָׁלוֹם וּבוֹרֵא רָע״, מִי קָא אָמְרִינַן כְּדִכְתִיב?! אֶלָּא כְּתִיב ״רַע״ וְקָרֵינַן ״הַכֹּל״ לִישָּׁנָא מְעַלְּיָא, הָכָא נָמֵי לֵימָא ״נוֹגַהּ״, לִישָּׁנָא מְעַלְּיָא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כְּדֵי לְהַזְכִּיר מִדַּת יוֹם בַּלַּיְלָה וּמִדַּת לַיְלָה בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Let him say** the following formula instead: **Who forms light and creates brightness,** so as not to mention darkness, which has negative connotations. **We say** the blessing **as** the verse **is written** in the Bible and do not alter the formula that appears in the verse. **But if so,** what about the continuation of the verse: **"Who makes peace and creates evil"? Do we say** this blessing **as it is written** in the Bible? **Rather, it is written evil and we euphemistically recite** the blessing **all things** to avoid mention of evil. **Here, too, let us euphemistically say brightness** instead of darkness. **Rather, Rava said:** The reason we recite: "Who creates darkness" is **in order to mention the attribute of day at night and the attribute of night during the day,** and thereby unify day and night as different parts of a single entity.

Throughout this discussion, a key question is whether these brachos function primarily as birchos Kriyas Shema or as birchos Shacharis and Maariv. To some degree, a meaningful distinction exists between the two brachos. Yotzer Or is fundamentally a bracha of Shacharis and Maariv — *yotzer haMeorot*, Yotzer Or Uvorei Choshech, *maariv aravim* — whereas Ahava Rabbah and Ahavat Olam are much more directly connected to Shema. Yet they regularly function as a package. As an illustration: someone davening Shacharis after sof zman Kriyas Shema still recites Ahavat Olam and Yotzer Or, because they continue to function as brachos of Shacharis even when they no longer serve as brachos of Kriyas Shema. These brachos truly do double duty — and, as will become apparent, perhaps triple duty.

### Practical Shema Issues

This dual function has direct practical implications. The brachos of Shema are not classic *birchos haMitzvah* in the manner of, say, taking a lulav — their language is not *asher kidshanu b'mitzvosav*. They function both as *birchos haShachar* and as birchos Kriyas Shema, and accordingly one does not recite Shema with these brachos outside the context of davening. If someone says Shema before davening, they do so without the brachos.

Yet because there is a special kiyum of saying Shema *im Brachosecha*, interesting halakhic questions arise. If a person fears missing zman Kriyas Shema, they should say Shema without the brachos rather than miss the time entirely. But ideally, Shema is said with the brachos and with tallis and tefillin, since the tallis is reflected in the third parsha of Shema and the tefillin in the first. This leads to practical trade-offs: if the zman is approaching, should one say Shema immediately or quickly put on tallis and tefillin and daven rapidly so as to say Shema with the brachos? If one can make the zman in time, the ideal is to say Shema with the brachos and with tallis and tefillin; if one fears missing the zman, one says Shema first. Some suggest making a tenai — stipulating that the earlier recitation counts only if the later one will not — though this raises its own questions about whether one can conditionally fulfill a mitzvah.

One might also ask: if someone is going to miss the zman, why not simply recite *asher kidshanu b'mitzvosav v'tzivanu likro shema* as a classic birchat hamitzvah? The answer is that this is not what the Sages instituted. The brachos of Shema were not instituted as classic *birchos haMitzvah*, a matter to be taken up in fuller discussion later.

## Ahava Rabbah & Birkat

### Learning Before Shema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ מַאי הִיא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: ״אַהֲבָה רַבָּה״. וְכֵן אוֹרִי לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְרַבִּי פְּדָת בְּרֵיהּ, ״אַהֲבָה רַבָּה״. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אֵין אוֹמְרִים ״אַהֲבַת עוֹלָם״, אֶלָּא ״אַהֲבָה רַבָּה״. וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי אַהֲבַת עוֹלָם, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאַהֲבַת עוֹלָם אֲהַבְתִּיךְ עַל כֵּן מְשַׁכְתִּיךְ חָסֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is** the formula of **the other** blessing recited before *Shema*? **Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: An abounding love [*ahava rabba*]. And Rabbi Elazar instructed his son, Rabbi Pedat,** to **also** say: **An abounding love.** **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: **One does not recite: An eternal love [*ahavat olam*]; rather,** one recites: **An abounding love. And the Rabbis say** that one recites: **An eternal love, and so it says: "And an eternal love I have loved you, therefore I have drawn you with kindness"** (Jeremiah 31:3).

With this context established, the Gemara transitions into a discussion of *birkat haTorah*. Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel states: *hishkim lishnos* ad shelo kara Kriyas Shema — if one rises early to learn before having recited Kriyas Shema, *tzarich l'varech* — one must recite *birkat haTorah*. Mishekara Kriyas Shema — once one has already said Shema, *ein tzarich l'varech* — one need not recite *birkat haTorah*, shekevar niftar b'Ahava Rabba — because one has already been discharged through Ahava Rabbah.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הִשְׁכִּים לִשְׁנוֹת, עַד שֶׁלֹּא קָרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע — צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ. מִשֶּׁקָּרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע — אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ, שֶׁכְּבָר נִפְטַר בְּ״אַהֲבָה רַבָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The blessing: An abounding love, is about God's love for us and includes praise for His giving us the Torah. Therefore, **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: One who arose to study, until he recites *Shema* he must recite a** special **blessing** over the Torah. **If he** already **recited *Shema* he need not recite** that **blessing, as he has exempted** himself **by** reciting the blessing of: **An abounding love,** which includes the components of the blessing over the Torah.

This reveals that Ahava Rabbah functions not merely as a bracha of Shacharis and not merely as a birchas Kriyas Shema, but also as a *birkat haTorah*. The language of Ahava Rabbah explains why: V'silamdem chukei chayim, v'ten b'libeinu l'havin u-l'haskil lishmoa lilmod u-l'lamed lishmor v'la'asos u-l'kayem es kol divrei Talmud Torasecha b'ahava — it is saturated with the themes of Torah, of teaching and learning. This directly reflects the themes of Shema itself: God loves us by teaching us Torah; we love God by learning Torah and doing mitzvos, as expressed in the first parsha through *veshinantam levannecha vedibarta bam* and in the second parsha through the mitzvos. Because the central theme of the first parsha of Shema is Talmud Torah, Ahava Rabbah — which encapsulates this theme — functions as a *birkat haTorah* as well.

### Mikra vs Midrash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לַמִּקְרָא צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ, וְלַמִּדְרָשׁ — אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned the blessing recited over Torah, the Gemara focuses on a dispute over what constitutes Torah in terms of requiring a blessing. **Rav Huna said: For** the study of **Bible, one must recite a blessing,** as it is the word of God, **and for** halakhic **midrash,** the derivation of *halakhot* from verses, **one need not recite a blessing.**

Rav Huna draws a distinction: le-Mikra *tzarich l'varech* — if one is learning pesukim one must make a bracha first (assuming one has not yet said Ahava Rabbah); u-le-Midrash eino *tzarich l'varech* — but if one is learning the rabbinic derasha of the pesukim, meaning how halacha is derived from them, one does not.

### Mishna & Talmud Disputes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: לַמִּקְרָא וְלַמִּדְרָשׁ צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ, לַמִּשְׁנָה — אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף לַמִּשְׁנָה נָמֵי צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ [אֲבָל לַתַּלְמוּד, אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ]. וְרָבָא אָמַר: אַף לַתַּלְמוּד צָרִיךְ (לַחֲזוֹר וּלְבָרֵךְ) [לְבָרֵךְ].
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said: For Bible and midrash,** which includes *halakhot* derived from verses themselves, **one must recite a blessing; for Mishna,** which is only comprised of halakhic rulings issued by the Sages, **one need not recite a blessing.** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even for Mishna,** which includes final, binding halakhic rulings, **one must recite a blessing as well, but for Talmud,** which comprises a study of the Mishna and the rationales for its rulings, **one need not recite a blessing.** **And Rava said: Even for Talmud,** which is the means to analyze the significance of the *halakhot*, and is the only form of Torah study that leads one to its true meaning, **one must recite a blessing.**

Rabbi Elazar extends the requirement: le-Mikra u-le-Midrash *tzarich l'varech* — one must make a bracha for both Mikra and Midrash — u-le-Mishna eino *tzarich l'varech* — but not for learning Mishna. Rabbi Yochanan goes further: af le-Mishna nami *tzarich l'varech* — even for Mishna one must make a bracha — aval le-Talmud eino *tzarich l'varech* — but not for learning Gemara, which is analysis of the Mishna. Rava goes furthest of all: af le-Talmud *tzarich l'varech* — even for Talmud one must make a bracha. Some have the girsa tzarich lachzor l'varech, framing the question as whether after saying Ahava Rabbah one must make a further bracha, but this is not the standard girsa.

### Primary Object Debate

This debate is striking. In virtually any other context in the Gemara, learning Gemara is considered the highest form of kiyum Talmud Torah — the real Talmud Torah. Why, then, is there any question about whether one makes *birkat haTorah* on it?

The answer lies in the nature of *birkat haTorah* itself. There are two ways to understand the bracha. It may function as a *birchas hamitzvah* — *asher kidshanu b'mitzvosav* V'Tzivanu La'asok B'Divrei Torah — in which case it applies wherever the mitzvah of Talmud Torah is performed, and Gemara learning would certainly be included. Alternatively, it may function as a *birchas hashevach* — *asher bachar banu* V'natan Lanu Torato Torat Emet — a response to the gift of Torah that God has given us. When we experience something magnificent, we respond with a bracha. But what God gave us most directly is Mikra — the written text. The further one moves from Mikra — through Midrash, then Mishna, then Talmud — the further one is from the direct object of divine revelation. The Gemara, using the Mishna as its focal point rather than the pesukim, is furthest removed. In terms not of the mitzvah of Talmud Torah but of the cheftza — the object of Torah to which we respond with shevach — Mikra has the greatest claim.

So the dispute among the Amoraim may hinge on which dimension of *birkat haTorah* is primary: if it is the *birchas hamitzvah*, the requirement extends to Gemara naturally; if the emphasis is on *birchas hashevach* for the object of divine revelation, then Gemara stands at the far end of the chain of derivation and its status is less clear.

### Rav Chiya's Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי: זִימְנִין סַגִּיאִין הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב לְתַנּוֹיֵי פִּרְקִין בְּ״סִפְרָא דְבֵי רַב״, הֲוָה מַקְדֵּים וְקָא מָשֵׁי יְדֵיהּ, וּבָרֵיךְ, וּמַתְנֵי לַן פִּרְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** This statement is supported by the practical *halakha* derived from observation of Rav's practice. His student, **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi, said: Many times I stood before Rav to study our chapter in the *Sifra*,** also known as *Torat Kohanim*, the halakhic midrash on Leviticus, **of the school of Rav,** and I saw that Rav **would first wash his hands,** then **recite a blessing,** and only then **he would teach us our chapter.** This demonstrates that even before their study of *Torat Kohanim*, which, due to Rav's explanation of the reasons behind the *halakhot*, was the equivalent of studying Talmud, one must recite a blessing.

The Gemara cites a relevant practice: Rav Chiya bar Ashi reports that many times he stood before Rav, who would be teaching a chapter from the Sifra d'vei Rav — the books of the house of Rav, though it is not certain this phrase carries its specific technical meaning here. First thing in the morning, Rav would wash his hands — netilat yadayim — then make a bracha, and then teach. Since this was done before saying Ahava Rabbah and before Kriyas Shema, Rav had to recite *birkat haTorah* independently. The content of what he then taught was Gemara — Talmud — which demonstrates that one does indeed make *birkat haTorah* before Talmud, not only before Mikra.

### Nusach Birkat Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מְבָרֵךְ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: ״אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ לַעֲסוֹק בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What** formula of **blessings does he recite?** There is a dispute over the formula of the blessings as well. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The formula of this blessing is like the standard formula for blessings recited over other mitzvot: Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, **Who sanctified us with his mitzvot and commanded us to engage in matters of Torah.**

Mah mivarech — what is the text of this bracha? Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel says: *asher kideshanu* be-mitzvotav ve-tzivanu la'asok be-divrei Torah. Different girsaot among the Geonim exist — some have *al divrei Torah* — but our girsa is la'asok, to involve oneself in Torah learning. This is notable: even in this *birchas hamitzvah*, the language is not simply "to learn" but la'asok — to labor, to invest oneself with real engagement. This is the classic *birchas hamitzvah*.

### Ha'arev Na Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מְסַיֵּים בַּהּ הָכִי ״הַעֲרֵב נָא ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ אֶת דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָתְךָ בְּפִינוּ וּבְפִיפִיּוֹת עַמְּךָ בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל וְנִהְיֶה אֲנַחְנוּ וְצֶאֱצָאֵינוּ וְצֶאֱצָאֵי עַמְּךָ בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל כֻּלָּנוּ יוֹדְעֵי שְׁמֶךָ וְעוֹסְקֵי תוֹרָתֶךָ בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ הַמְלַמֵּד תּוֹרָה לְעַמּוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan concludes** the blessing by adding **the following: Lord our God, make the words of Your Torah sweet in our mouths and in the mouths of Your people, the house of Israel, so that we and our descendants and the descendants of Your people, the house of Israel, may be those who know Your name and engage in Your Torah. Blessed are You, Lord, Who teaches Torah to His people Israel.**

Rabbi Yochanan appends an additional passage: Ha'arev na Hashem Elokeinu et divrei Toratecha be-finu u-ve-fiyot amcha Beit Yisrael — make the words of Your Torah sweet in our mouths and in the mouths of Your people Israel — ve-niyeh anachnu ve-tze'etza'einu ve-tze'etza'ei amcha Beit Yisrael kulanu yode'ei shemcha ve-oskei Toratecha — and may we and our children and our children's children all know Your name and involve ourselves in Your Torah — *baruch atah hashem* hamelamed Torah le-amo Yisrael.

This is not a *birchas hamitzvah*; it is a different kind of bracha entirely. It is a request — a bakashah — that God give us the desire and the delight to learn Torah. Strikingly, this mirrors the language of Ahava Rabbah: V'ten be-libeinu l'havin u-l'haskil lilmod u-le-lamed. The chatimah — *baruch atah hashem* hamelamed Torah le-amo Yisrael — is an extension constituting a further bracha.

### Three Blessing Forms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הַמְנוּנָא אָמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר בָּחַר בָּנוּ מִכׇּל הָעַמִּים וְנָתַן לָנוּ אֶת תּוֹרָתוֹ. בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ נוֹתֵן הַתּוֹרָה״. אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: זוֹ הִיא מְעוּלָּה שֶׁבַּבְּרָכוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Hamnuna said** an additional formula: **Who has chosen us from all the peoples and given us His Torah. Blessed are You, Lord, Giver of the Torah.** With regard to this formula, **Rav Hamnuna said: This** concise blessing **is the most outstanding of all the blessings** over the Torah, as it combines thanks to God for giving us the Torah as well as acclaim for the Torah and for Israel.

Rav Hamnuna adds a third formulation: *asher bachar banu* mikol ha-amim ve-natan lanu et Torato, *baruch atah hashem* notein ha-Torah — You have chosen us from all nations and given us Your Torah. Rav Hamnuna declares: zu hi me'ula she-ba-berachot — this is the finest of the blessings. Why? Perhaps because of the theme of chosenness; perhaps also because it is a bracha said independently even outside this context, as we recite it before each aliyah to the Torah. The Gemara concludes: hilkach nemrinhu le-chulhu — therefore, say them all. And indeed we do.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ לֵימְרִינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Since several formulas for the blessing over Torah were suggested, each with its own distinct advantage, the Gemara concludes: **Therefore, let us recite them all** as blessings over the Torah.

### Synthesis & Integration

Reviewing all three formulations clarifies why Ahava Rabbah functions as a *birkat haTorah*: it contains all three of these elements within it. This threefold structure of *birkat haTorah* also illuminates the earlier debate about which form of Torah study requires the bracha.

The three elements are: first, the *birchas hamitzvah* — Asher Kidshanu V'Tzivanu — which applies wherever the mitzvah of Talmud Torah is performed, naturally including Gemara. Second, the *birchas hashevach* — *asher bachar banu* — a response to the gift of Torah itself, where Mikra as the direct word of God has primary claim. Third, the bakashah of Ha'arev Na — a request for the desire to engage with Torah — which is its own dimension, something rare and moving in that most of our requests in Shemoneh Esrei concern material needs, while here the center of our spiritual aspirations becomes itself the subject of petition: grant us the will to truly invest in Torah.

There is a significant machloket between the *shulchan aruch* and the Vilna Gaon regarding which of these three elements is primary in *birkat haTorah*, and this generates a range of practical questions. Tosafot already raises the question: if one wakes in the middle of the night, must one repeat *birkat haTorah*? If one stops learning during the day and resumes, must one make another bracha? If *birkat haTorah* is a *birchas hamitzvah*, then — as with leaving a sukkah and returning — each new resumption should require a new bracha. Tosafot, however, holds that even as a *birchas hamitzvah*, Talmud Torah is fundamentally different: we are always obligated to be learning, and even when we are not, we are perpetually in the process of returning to it. It is a continuous mitzvah, never truly interrupted. Therefore no new bracha is required upon resumption — and there is something beautiful in that framing: at least in principle, we should always be in the process of getting back to Talmud Torah.

If the emphasis falls instead on the shevach dimension, then *birkat haTorah* is like other birchos hashevach which are recited once daily, as with *birchos haShachar* — one does not recite HaNoten LaSechvi Binah each time a rooster crows.

A related question, debated between the *shulchan aruch* and the Magen Avraham, is whether *birkat haTorah* is required when one is thinking Torah without speaking: one performs the mitzvat Talmud Torah by thinking Torah, but there is no verbal object to which the bracha responds. Which element dominates determines the answer.

Returning to the practical bottom line: according to the peshat of this Gemara, and apparently the ruling of many Rishonim, as long as one does not plan to learn before davening, Ahava Rabbah discharges the obligation of *birkat haTorah* — Ahava Rabbah contains all three of those elements. In practice, however, given the centrality of these brachos and the power of articulating what Torah means to us, the custom developed to ensure we recite *birkat haTorah* explicitly by learning a bit before Shema. This raises its own questions: if someone is rushing and says birchos Kriyas Shema to ensure Kriyas Shema bizmanah, does Ahava Rabbah count as *birkat haTorah*? Does it require specific kavana? The Yerushalmi suggests it may count only if one learns extra Torah after Shema — making clear that the bracha was not merely a birchas Kriyas Shema. If no additional Torah is learned, it is framed only as a birchas Kriyas Shema. Several Rishonim therefore require either kavana or post-Shema Torah learning for Ahava Rabbah to discharge the *birkat haTorah* obligation, though the matter remains debated.

## Beit HaMikdash Service

### Memuneh's Command

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: אָמַר לָהֶם הַמְמוּנֶּה: ״בָּרְכוּ בְּרָכָה אַחַת!״. וְהֵם בֵּרְכוּ. וְקָרְאוּ עֲשֶׂרֶת הַדִּבְּרוֹת, ״שְׁמַע״, ״וְהָיָה אִם שָׁמוֹעַ״, ״וַיֹּאמֶר״. וּבֵרְכוּ אֶת הָעָם שָׁלֹשׁ בְּרָכוֹת, ״אֱמֶת וְיַצִּיב״, וַעֲבוֹדָה, וּבִרְכַּת כֹּהֲנִים. וּבְשַׁבָּת מוֹסִיפִין בְּרָכָה אַחַת לַמִּשְׁמָר הַיּוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to dealing with the blessings that accompany *Shema*, and describes the practice in the Temple. **We learned there,** in a mishna in tractate *Tamid*: In the morning **the** deputy High Priest **appointed** to oversee activity in the Temple, **said to** the priests who were members of the priestly watch [*mishmar*] on duty that week: **Recite a single blessing.** The members of the priestly watch **recited a blessing, and read the Ten Commandments, *Shema*, *VeHaya im Shamoa* and *VaYomer*,** the standard recitation of *Shema*. Additionally, **they blessed the people** with **three blessings.** These blessings were: **True and Firm,** the blessing of redemption recited after *Shema*; ***Avoda*,** service, the special blessing recited over God's acceptance of the sacrifices with favor, similar to the blessing of Temple Service recited in the *Amida* prayer; **and the priestly benediction,** recited in the form of a prayer without the outstretched hands that usually accompany that blessing (*Tosafot*). **And on Shabbat one blessing is added to** bless **the outgoing priestly watch,** as the watch serving in the Temple was replaced on Shabbat.

The Gemara now cites a Mishnah from elsewhere — tnan hatam. Those who followed the previous cycle of the Daf will recall that Masechet Tamid concluded with an extended description of the daily service in the Beit HaMikdash and precisely how the morning was organized. At one stage, after the commencement of the korban tamid, the memuneh — the officer appointed over the kohanim — addressed them: Barchu bracha achat — recite one blessing. They did so.

They then read the Aseres HaDibros, and then the three parshiyot of Shema: Shema, V'haya Im Shamoa, and Vayomer.

They then blessed the people with three blessings: Emes V'yatziv (which concludes with Gaal Yisrael), V'avodah, and *birkas kohanim*. The bracha of V'avodah raises its own question since our current formulation, *hamachazir shchinaso l'tzion*, was obviously not the one in use. Rashi suggests two possibilities: either She'oscha levadcha b'yirah naavod — the bracha recited before the duchan blessing, invoking that the kohanim's service should be directed entirely to God — or a formulation such as Baruch hamikabel avodas amo Yisrael b'ratzon, a form of retzei, accepting the sacrifice. In any case it was a bracha for the acceptance of the avodah. Their Shemoneh Esrei was therefore compressed: Gaal Yisrael, Retzei, and *birkas kohanim* — not the duchan *birkas kohanim*, which occurred at different times, but nevertheless a component of this abbreviated Shemoneh Esrei.

### Aseres Hadibros

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״בְּרָכָה אַחַת״? כִּי הָא דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אַבָּא אִקְּלַעוּ לְהָהוּא אַתְרָא, בְּעוֹ מִנַּיְיהוּ: מַאי ״בְּרָכָה אַחַת״? לָא הֲוָה בִּידַיְיהוּ. וַאֲתוֹ שַׁיְילוּהוּ לְרַב מַתְנָה לָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ. אֲתוֹ שַׁיְילוּהוּ לְרַב יְהוּדָה. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, ״אַהֲבָה רַבָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** Certain details in this mishna are not sufficiently clear. First, **what is the single blessing** that the deputy High Priest instructed the guards to recite? The Gemara relates: It is **like** the incident **where Rabbi Abba and Rabbi Yosei bar Abba happened to** visit **a certain** unnamed **place,** and the people there **asked them: What is the single blessing** mentioned in the mishna? They **did not have** an answer **readily available.** So **they came and asked Rav Mattana, and he** too **did not have** an answer **readily available. They came and asked Rav Yehuda,** and **he told them: Shmuel said as follows: An abounding love** is the single blessing recited by the priestly watch.

Returning to the opening of the Mishnah: mah bracha achat — what was the single blessing before Shema? When Rabbi Avin and Rabbi Yosef arrived at a certain place, the people there asked them this question. They did not know. They went and asked Rav Masnah, who also did not know. They then asked Rav Yehuda, who reported in the name of Shmuel: Ahava Rabbah. Rabbi Zerika, citing Rabbi Ami citing Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, says: Yotzer Or.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי זְרִיקָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר״. כִּי אֲתָא רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, אָמַר, הָא דְּרַבִּי זְרִיקָא לָאו בְּפֵירוּשׁ אִתְּמַר אֶלָּא מִכְּלָלָא אִתְּמַר. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זְרִיקָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת בְּרָכוֹת אֵין מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zerika said** that **Rabbi Ami said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said** a different answer: This single blessing is: **Who creates light.** That was how Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish's statement was received in Babylonia, yet **when Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that this *halakha* was not a direct quote of a statement by Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish. **That which Rabbi Zerika said was not stated explicitly** by Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, but **rather it was inferred from** another statement. **As Rabbi Zerika said** that **Rabbi Ami said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said:** From the expression: Recite a single blessing, in the mishna in tractate *Tamid*, **it follows** that failure to recite one of the **blessings** recited before *Shema* **does not prevent** one from reciting the **other.** This means that if only one of the blessings was recited, the obligation to recite that blessing was fulfilled, as the two blessings are not mutually dependent.

Both options have their logic. If the Temple service is conceived as a distilled version of Shema and Shemoneh Esrei — and no more — then Ahava Rabbah is the natural choice, being the bracha most directly connected to Shema. If, on the other hand, the goal is a minimal representation of all of davening — Shema plus Shemoneh Esrei — then Yotzer Or adds the component of the morning service, the praise for creation that the other brachos represent. One compelling point is that the kohanim were particularly concerned with not appearing to worship the sun, having been in proximity to the Temple precincts and the rising sun; declaring Yotzer Or Uvorei Choshech affirmed their allegiance to God rather than the heavenly bodies.

There is also the question of whether their davening was primarily a private obligation or had some public dimension — the Mishnah elsewhere indicates that people would daven together with the kohanim — which may also bear on which bracha was appropriate.

### Emes Veyatziv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר״ הֲווֹ אָמְרִי, הַיְינוּ דִּבְרָכוֹת אֵין מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ, דְּלָא קָא אָמְרִי ״אַהֲבָה רַבָּה״. אֶלָּא, אִי אָמְרַתְּ ״אַהֲבָה רַבָּה״ הֲווֹ אָמְרִי, מַאי בְּרָכוֹת אֵין מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ? דִּלְמָא הַאי דְּלָא אָמְרִי ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר״ מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מְטָא זְמַן ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר״, וְכִי מְטָא זְמַן ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר״ הֲווֹ אָמְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The conclusion was drawn from Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish's statement that he held that the single blessing recited was: Who creates light. The considerations that led the Sages to that conclusion were: **Granted, if you say that they would recite: Who creates light,** then the conclusion of Reish Lakish, that failure to recite one of the **blessings** recited before *Shema* **does not prevent one** from reciting the **other,** is understandable, as they recited: Who creates light, **and did not recite: An abounding love,** and they nonetheless fulfilled their obligation. **However, if you say that they** would omit: Who creates light, and **would recite: An abounding love,** on **what** basis would you conclude that failure to recite one of the **blessings** recited before *Shema* **does not prevent** one from reciting the **other?** In that case, one could offer another reason why only a single blessing is recited. **Perhaps** the fact **that they did not recite: Who creates light was because the time for the recitation of: Who creates light, had not yet arrived,** as the sun had yet to rise. The blessings of the priestly watch are recited in the early morning hours, long before sunrise. **However,** afterward, **when the time** to recite: **Who creates light arrived, they would recite it.** From the conclusion drawn by Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, that failure to recite one of the blessings recited before ***Shema*** does not prevent one from reciting the other, it is clear that the blessing recited by the members of the priestly watch was: Who creates light.

The Gemara then reports: when Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef came, he noted that what Rabbi Zerika said — that it was Yotzer Or — was not stated explicitly but was inferred. De'amar Rabbi Zerika amar Rabbi Ami amar Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: zos omeres brachos *ein me'akvos* zo es zo — the fact that only one bracha was said proves that the brachos do not hold each other back. If one says only one of the two morning brachos before Shema, one is yotzei that one bracha; saying it does not require having said the other.

### One Blessing Debate


The inference works only if the single bracha was Yotzer Or. If they said Yotzer Or without Ahava Rabbah, one can demonstrate that Yotzer Or is valid independently. But if one argues they said Ahava Rabbah, the inference fails to prove anything: perhaps the reason they omitted Yotzer Or had nothing to do with whether one holds the other back. Perhaps they simply had not yet reached the proper time for Yotzer Or.

The Gemara pursues this: ei amart bishlama Yotzer Or — if one says the single bracha was Yotzer Or, hainu brachos *ein me'akvos* — the proof follows, since they said Yotzer Or without saying Ahava Rabbah. Ela ei amart Ahava Rabbah — but if one says they said Ahava Rabbah, how does that prove brachos do not hold each other back? Dilma — perhaps the reason they omitted Yotzer Or was simply because its time had not yet arrived. The Gemara then considers: granted, perhaps when the time of Yotzer Or came they would have said it. Mai brachos *ein me'akvos*? Then the principle would mean only that the order does not matter — not that one bracha is fully valid without the other.

### Brachos Not Mkablos

Implicit in this discussion is that the kohanim said Shema before the time of Yotzer Or — that is, before sunrise. Rashi quotes the Gemara in Yoma that one who said Shema with the kohanim was not yotzei the mitzvah of *shema because* they said it too early. Some read this as indicating that the zman Shema — at least according to one view — does not begin until sunrise, so saying it earlier does not fulfill the obligation.

Our standard ruling requires at least misheyakir, and we do not hold that sunrise is required for Shema. However, the Gemara here implies that Yotzer Or may indeed require sunrise. We do not rule this way either — we hold that Yotzer Or may be said from misheyakir — but there remains a serious question about someone davening before amud hashachar (approximately 72 minutes before sunrise, from when Shema is technically permitted): does one say Yotzer Or at that time? Some say yes, some say no. The double nature of the brachos is again relevant here. Ahava Rabbah is closely bound to Shema itself and would presumably be said even if one is davening before sunrise, since it is a birchas Kriyas Shema. But Yotzer Or is simultaneously a bracha of the morning and of creation — and perhaps the sun's appearance is a prerequisite for its recitation, at least according to the reading that Yotzer Or requires sunrise. This could yield a situation where one recites Ahava Rabbah before sunrise without Yotzer Or, with the understanding that they serve different functions even as brachos of the same Shema.

### Inference Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְשַׁבָּת מוֹסִיפִין בְּרָכָה אַחַת לַמִּשְׁמָר הַיּוֹצֵא. אָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ: מִשְׁמָר הַיּוֹצֵא אוֹמֵר לַמִּשְׁמָר הַנִּכְנָס: ״מִי שֶׁשִּׁכֵּן אֶת שְׁמוֹ בַּבַּיִת הַזֶּה, הוּא יַשְׁכִּין בֵּינֵיכֶם אַהֲבָה וְאַחְוָה וְשָׁלוֹם וְרֵיעוּת״.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in a mishna in tractate *Tamid* that **on Shabbat a single blessing is added to** bless **the outgoing priestly watch.** The Gemara asks: **What is** that **single blessing? Rabbi Ḥelbo said:** As they finished their service, **the outgoing priestly watch would say to the incoming priestly watch: May He who caused His Name to dwell in this house cause love and brotherhood, peace and camaraderie to dwell among you.**

The Mishnah continues: u'v'Shabbos mosifin bracha achas l'mishmar hayotze — on Shabbat they would add one blessing for the *mishmar hayotze*, the outgoing watch. Rabbi Chelbo explains: *mishmar hayotze* omer l'mishmar hanichnas — the outgoing mishmar would address the incoming. What would they say? Mi sheshachan et shemo babayit hazeh, hu yashken beineichem ahava veachva veshalom vereut — May He whose name dwells in this House cause to dwell among you love, brotherhood, peace, and friendship. It is a beautiful blessing — perhaps especially fitting at the transition point when the incoming mishmar is about to face a demanding week of sacrificial service with all the pressures and divisions of labor it entails.

### Yotzer Or Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אַף בִּגְבוּלִין בִּקְּשׁוּ לִקְרוֹת כֵּן, אֶלָּא שֶׁכְּבָר בִּטְּלוּם מִפְּנֵי תַּרְעוֹמֶת הַמִּינִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Even in the outlying areas,** outside the Temple, **they sought to recite** the Ten Commandments **in this manner** every day, as they are the basis of the Torah (Rambam), **but they had already abolished** recitation of the Ten Commandments **due to the grievance of the heretics,** who argued that the entire Torah, with the exception of the Ten Commandments, did not emanate from God (Jerusalem Talmud). If the Ten Commandments were recited daily, that would lend credence to their claim, so their recitation was expunged from the daily prayers.

The Gemara reports: Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel says: af bagvulin bikshu likros kein — they wanted to establish the practice of reading the Aseres HaDibros in the communities outside the Beit HaMikdash as well — ela shekvar bitlum mipnei tarumos haminin — but they had already abolished it because of the complaint of the heretics.

This is instructive. The impulse to say the Aseres HaDibros before Shema makes sense: Shema is itself a distillation of the Torah's central demands — love God, learn Torah, do the mitzvos. The Aseres HaDibros is another such distillation, a declaration of our responsibilities and a kind of re-enactment of Maamad Har Sinai and kabbalas ol malchus shamayim. But the practice was suppressed because of tarumos haminin — the heretical objection. Rashi in the unedited text identifies these minim as talmidei Yeshu — Hebrew Christians. Our standard printed Rashi reads akum, which here must be understood as reflecting censorship, since minim classically denotes Hebrew Christians who kept halacha while believing in Jesus. Their critique would have been to single out the Ten Commandments as the sole genuine revelation, thereby implicitly delegitimizing the rest of Torah. This maps onto the later reality that Christianity, even while rejecting the broader corpus of halacha, continued to embrace the Ten Commandments.

Despite the evident appeal of the practice, subsequent Amoraim who wished to reinstitute it — Rabba bar bar Chana in Sura and Ameimar in Neharde'a — were in each case told by Rav Chisda and Rav Ashi respectively: kvar bitlum mipnei tarumos haminin. The liturgy as established is not to be altered, even when the original reason for the prohibition may not apply in a particular locale.

## Ending Determines All

### Shabbat Addition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָקוֹם שֶׁאָמְרוּ לְהַאֲרִיךְ — אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְקַצֵּר, לְקַצֵּר — אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְהַאֲרִיךְ. לַחְתּוֹם — אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי שֶׁלֹּא לַחְתּוֹם, שֶׁלֹּא לַחְתּוֹם — אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לַחְתּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** However, the general principle is: **Where** the Sages **said** to recite **a long** blessing, **one may not shorten it,** and so too, wherever they said to recite **a short** blessing, **one may not lengthen it.** Where the Sages said that a blessing **must conclude** with a second blessing at the end, **he may not fail to conclude** with that blessing. Similarly, if the Sages said that a blessing must **not conclude** with a second blessing, **one may not conclude** with a blessing.

The Gemara moves into a discussion of the actual nusach of brachos and what happens when something goes wrong. It begins: pshita — it is obvious — if someone is holding a cup of wine and thinks it is mead, and begins the bracha with the intention of saying *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro*, but then realizes it is wine and ends with Borei Pri HaGafen — yatza. Why is this obvious? Because even had he completed Shehakol, he would have been yotzei — de-nami im amar *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro* yatza, as we learned: al kulam im amar *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro* yatza. The opening intention to say Shehakol was never an error in the first place.

### Ten Commandments Ban

The more interesting case is the reverse: one holds mead and thinks it is wine, begins with the intention to say Borei Pri HaGafen, but then realizes the truth and ends with *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro*. The question is: batar ikkar bracha azlinan — do we follow the beginning of the bracha — or batar chatima azlinan — do we follow the end? The opening intention was for the wrong bracha, but the text concluded correctly.

Tosafot notes that this cannot simply be a question about lack of kavana — we are generally yotzei mitzvos without kavana. Rather, the issue here is that one had an active contrary intention at the opening: one specifically framed the bracha as Borei Pri HaGafen, and the question is whether that framing invalidates the bracha regardless of whether the words were ultimately correct.

### Wine & Mead Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שַׁחֲרִית, פָּתַח בְּ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר״ וְסִיֵּים בְּ״מַעֲרִיב עֲרָבִים״ — לֹא יָצָא. פָּתַח בְּ״מַעֲרִיב עֲרָבִים״ וְסִיֵּים בְּ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר״ — יָצָא. עַרְבִית, פָּתַח בְּ״מַעֲרִיב עֲרָבִים״ וְסִיֵּים בְּ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר״ — לֹא יָצָא. פָּתַח בְּ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר״ וְסִיֵּים בְּ״מַעֲרִיב עֲרָבִים״ — יָצָא. כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: הַכֹּל הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַחִתּוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Come and hear** a proof from what was taught in a *baraita* with regard to a similar case: If, in **the morning prayer**, one **began** the blessings prior to the recitation of *Shema* appropriately **with: Who creates light, and concluded with** the formula of the evening prayer: **Who brings on evenings, he did not fulfill** his obligation. However, if one did the opposite, and **commenced with: Who brings on evenings, and concluded with: Who creates light, he fulfilled** his obligation. Similarly, if, in **the evening prayer**, **one commenced** the recitation of *Shema* **with: Who brings on evenings and concluded with: Who creates light, he did not fulfill** his obligation. If **one commenced with: Who creates light and concluded with: Who brings on evenings, he fulfilled** his obligation. The *baraita* summarizes that **the general principle is: Everything follows the conclusion** of the blessing. Based on this principle, the question with regard to a blessing recited over food and drink posed above can be resolved.

A baraita provides the answer through the case of the morning and evening brachos: *shacharis patach beYotzer* Or *vesiyem beMaariv Aravim* — *lo yatza*. If one opened with Yotzer Or in mind and ended with *maariv aravim* — not yotzei. *patach beMaariv aravim* *vesiyem beYotzer* Or — yatza. If one opened with the wrong intention but ended correctly — yotzei. The same applies in reverse for Maariv. Klal shel davar: hakol holech achar hachatima — it all goes by how you end.

### Switched Intentions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּקָאָמַר ״בָּרוּךְ יוֹצֵר הַמְּאוֹרוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** This proof is rejected: **There,** in the case of the blessing recited over the radiant lights, **it is different, as one recites: Blessed**…Who **forms the radiant lights,** and similarly, in the evening one recites: Blessed…Who brings on evenings. Since these are long blessings that conclude with a second blessing summarizing their content, one could assert that everything follows the conclusion. However, in the case of short blessings, such as: By Whose word all things came to be, or: Who creates the fruit of the vine, ostensibly, if there is a problem with the first part of the blessing, the entire blessing is nullified.

The Gemara resists this conclusion, noting: shani hatam dekaamar Baruch Yotzer Meor — the case of Yotzer Or is different because it is a long bracha, an extended paragraph. When the baraita says patach, it may mean that *baruch* אתה ה' יוצר אור was said at the very opening of the paragraph with a wrong mindset, but by the time one reached the chatima — *baruch* אתה ה' יוצר המאורות — the mindset was correct. So the bracha may not be a straightforward proof that wrong intent at the beginning is irrelevant; perhaps it only works here because the wrong intent affected only the opening paragraph while the concluding *baruch* at the end was said with correct intent.

The Gemara tests this against the positions of Rav and Rabbi Yochanan. According to Rav — kol bracha she'ein bo Hazkaras HaShem einah bracha — a bracha must contain the name of Hashem, which the concluding *baruch atah hashem* *yotzer haMeorot* provides. This is consistent. But according to Rabbi Yochanan — kol bracha she'ein bo malchus einah bracha — without Melech HaOlam it is not a bracha. The only place Melech HaOlam appears is in the opening *baruch atah hashem* Eloheinu Melech HaOlam, which was said with the wrong mindset. If that opening is discounted, should the whole bracha fail?

### Chatima Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מִדַּת לַיְלָה בַּיּוֹם כִּדְאָמְרִינַן: ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר וּבוֹרֵא חֹשֶׁךְ״, אֶלָּא מִדַּת יוֹם בַּלַּיְלָה, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״גּוֹלֵל אוֹר מִפְּנֵי חֹשֶׁךְ וְחֹשֶׁךְ מִפְּנֵי אוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues and asks: **Granted, the attribute of night** is mentioned **during the day, as we say: Who forms light and creates darkness, but where do you find the attribute of day** mentioned **at night?** In the blessing over the radiant lights recited at night there is no mention of "Who forms light." **Abaye said:** Nevertheless, the attribute of day is mentioned at night in the words: **Rolling away light before the darkness and darkness before the light.**

Rabbah bar Ulla explains why this is not a problem: kedei lehazkir middas yom balaylah u-middas laylah bayom — both brachos, morning and evening, are structured to mention both day and night. Since each bracha intentionally contains elements of both times, even when one begins with the wrong intention — thinking it is *maariv aravim* when saying Yotzer Or — that opening is not entirely without content, because Yotzer Or by design speaks of both light and darkness. It is analogous to the Shehakol case: just as Shehakol is always valid for any food, the opening of one bracha has some relevance even when said in the wrong context, because both brachos encompass both times. Therefore the case remains insufficient to prove the general principle that wrong intent at the opening is always irrelevant.

The Gemara then returns to the klal shel davar, the overarching rule, and notes: klal shel davar le'asuyei mai — what does the general rule include? Lo le'asuyei ha de'amran — not simply to restate the case just discussed. Rather, it includes a new case: bread and dates.

### Long Brachos Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא: כְּגוֹן דַּאֲכַל תַּמְרֵי, וְקָסָבַר נַהֲמָא אֲכַל, וּפְתַח בִּדְנַהֲמָא, וְסַיֵּים בִּדְתַמְרֵי [— יָצָא]. דַּאֲפִילּוּ סַיֵּים בִּדְנַהֲמָא — נָמֵי יָצָא. מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּתַמְרֵי נָמֵי מֵיזָן זָיְינִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No; this** general principle **is** only **necessary** to teach a special **case**, **where one ate dates and thought that he ate bread, and commenced** reciting the blessing **thinking they were bread.** Upon realizing that they were dates, **he concluded** the blessing **with that** which is recited **over dates.** In that case **he fulfilled** his obligation, as **even had he concluded** the blessing **with that** which is recited **over bread, he** would have **fulfilled his obligation.** **What is the reason** that had he concluded with the blessing recited over bread he would have fulfilled his obligation to recite a blessing over dates? This is **because dates also provide** a person **sustenance.** While *ab initio* one should not recite the blessing for bread over dates, after the fact, if one did so, he fulfilled his obligation. It is with regard to this particular situation that the *baraita* established the principle: Everything follows the conclusion of the blessing. Ultimately, the dilemma regarding a blessing with an inappropriate opening and an appropriate conclusion remains unresolved. The Gemara proceeds to discuss the formula for the blessings recited along with *Shema*.

The case is as follows: one eats dates and thinks it is bread. One begins with the intention to say *hamotzi lechem min ha'aretz*, then realizes and ends with Borei Pri HaEtz. Hainu bahu — this is exactly the same case we have been discussing: one opened with the wrong intention, ended with the right bracha text. The klal tells us it goes by the end. Fine, the principle is confirmed.

But the opposite case is more interesting: one eats dates and thinks it is bread, begins intending to say Hamotzi, and then ends with the bracha for dates — Borei Pri HaEtz. Yatza — this works. Why, if one was thinking Hamotzi at the beginning? De'afilu siyem bidnahama nami yatza — because even if one had completed Hamotzi for dates, one would still be yotzei, since dates also satiate — tamrei nami mizni zanei. Since Hamotzi would have worked even for dates, the original intent was never truly wrong, and the final correct formulation certainly works.

### Day & Night Coverage

Rashi and Tosafot understand the entire discussion as concerning one's mental intent: the text was said correctly, but the wrong bracha was in mind at the outset. Several Rishonim, however, find this difficult — why should mental intent matter if the text was correct? The Ramban offers a different reading. He understands adaata dehachi as referring not to wrong mental intent but to wrong words actually spoken. The scenarios describe situations where one said the wrong bracha aloud and then corrected oneself. The question is whether a toch k'dei dibbur correction — a verbal self-correction made within the brief span defined as toch k'dei dibbur — is effective.

According to the Rambam's reading, the conclusion of the sugya — hakol holech achar hachatuma — means: as long as one says the right words at the end, a toch k'dei dibbur correction of the wrong words said at the beginning is effective. If one said the wrong thing and corrected it within toch k'dei dibbur, the correction stands. If one said the right thing but then miscorrected it — saying the right bracha and then immediately adding a wrong one — the wrong correction does not undo the correct recitation. This is also how we rule in practice regarding toch k'dei dibbur corrections: saying the correct bracha and then incorrectly correcting oneself does not invalidate what was already said correctly, but saying the wrong bracha and then correcting it within toch k'dei dibbur does work.
