# Yoma 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Order of Limbs

### Animal's Walking Order

The Gemara on 25b picks up in the middle of the page, roughly twenty to twenty-five lines from the bottom, with a *tanu rabbanan* introducing a debate about the order in which the limbs of the *tamid* were brought to the *kevesh* — the ramp leading to the altar — and, by extension, to the altar itself. Ben Azzai, speaking before Rabbi Akiva, reports that the limbs were brought in the order in which the animal would walk: *derech hilucho*. The Gemara then specifies what that order looked like. The head moves first, propelled by the right hind leg — so the head and the right hind leg form the first grouping. Then comes the chest and the throat (*hachazeh vehagerah*), which move together with the upper body connected to the head. The two forearms (*shtei yadayim*) follow, then the two flanks (*shtei defanot*), and finally the tail, rump, and left hind leg (*ha'oketz veharagel*). This, as Ben Azzai understood it, reflects the natural locomotion of the animal.

### Flaying Order

Rabbi Yose disagrees and holds that the limbs were brought in the order of *derech hafshato* — the order in which the animal is flayed. The animal was hung by its legs and flayed downward toward the head. Accordingly, the head is brought first — not because it is flayed first, but because it is severed separately. Then comes the right hind leg, the tail and rump, the left hind leg, the two flanks, the two forearms, and finally the chest and throat. The flaying thus proceeds from the hanging legs downward, and that sequence governs the order of the bringing.

### Dismembering Order

Rabbi Akiva holds that the order followed *derech nitucho* — the manner of dismembering the animal. The head and one hind leg are first, then the two forearms, the chest and throat, the two flanks, and finally the tail and remaining hind leg. This is strikingly similar to Ben Azzai's order, with the primary difference lying in where the two forearms appear in the sequence. Whether there is a genuinely natural sequence to the dismembering, or whether it reflects a technical choice made by the butchers, is something the Gemara leaves largely implicit.

### Quality/Importance

Rabbi Yose HaGlili takes a different conceptual approach altogether. He holds that the order followed *derech iluyo* — the order of importance or quality. The head is brought first, then the hind leg, the chest, the throat, the two flanks, the tail and rump, the other hind leg, and the two forearms last. Here too the two forearms appear near the end, which will become significant momentarily.

### Quality Analysis

The Gemara then introduces a verse from Yechezkel: *kol netach tov yareich vechasef* — "every choice cut, the thigh and the shoulder." This raises an immediate difficulty. If the thigh is considered a choice cut, how can the hind leg appear toward the bottom of the list in an ordering that claims to prioritize quality? The Gemara answers: *hahi be'chshusha* — that verse in Yechezkel refers to a lean animal, where the thigh is indeed the choicest portion. But in a fatty animal, the thigh is not the choice cut, and that is the context relevant to the *tamid*.

Rava then offers a unifying principle: *bein tanna didan u'vein Rabbi Yose, basar iluya de'bisra azlinan* — whether you take our *Tanna* whose list appears in the *Mishnah*, or Rabbi Yose HaGlili, both agree that the governing criterion is the quality of the meat. The difference between them is simply how "quality" is measured. *Mar azil basar eivra de'bisra* — one authority follows the size of the cut. The other, *u'mar azil basar shamna de'bisra* — follows the fattiness or richness of the cut. Our *Tanna* places the forearms (*shtei yadayim*) earlier in the sequence because they represent a large cut; Rabbi Yose HaGlili places them last because, while sizable, they are not especially rich in fat.

### Conceptual Meaning

Stepping back from the comparison of lists, the conceptual stakes of this debate are worth considering. The most intuitive position is the last one: you are presenting something on the *mizbe'ach*, and naturally you wish to present the choicest portions first. Whether "choicest" means largest or fattiest is a secondary question; the principle of prioritizing the finest cuts is readily comprehensible.

The most striking and unusual position is Ben Azzai's: mimicking the way the animal walks. One illuminating way to understand this is that by recreating the animal's natural movement — even in the sequence of severed limbs — you are insisting on conceptualizing the offering as an animal, a living being, rather than as so much dressed meat. The further one moves toward pure quality rankings, the more the offering looks like a butcher's display of premium cuts. Ben Azzai's approach insists on the animal's identity as the thing being given. The positions based on the order of flaying or dismembering occupy a middle ground — neither mimicking life nor explicitly ranking by quality, but simply following the technical sequence that the preparation of the animal itself dictated.

## Head & Fat First

### Head Priority

The Gemara now asks a question that applies across all the opinions: *dechulei alma mihat rosh karev bereisha* — everyone seems to agree the head is brought first. What is the source for this? A *braita* provides it: *menayan lerosh upader shekadmu lechol ha'evarim* — how do we know the head and the fat precede all the other limbs? The verse says *et rosho v'et pidro v'arach* — "its head and its fat, and you shall arrange it on the fire." The juxtaposition of head and fat with the command to place them on the fire establishes their priority.

But then the Gemara notes that the verse mentions *peder* — the fat — twice. The second mention, in the verse *et hanetachim et harosh v'et hapader*, generates a further teaching from a *braita*. The head itself is not a particularly choice cut — it is mostly bone with relatively little meat. So why is it brought first at all? And once it is brought, you bring it alongside a large, meaty limb to compensate for its own lack of meat. That is the function of bringing the hind leg together with the head: *mishum derisha nefishei bei tarmayot, kariv regla bahadei* — because the head is full of bones, you bring the leg alongside it.

### Aesthetic Covering

The second mention of *peder* teaches something different: the manner in which the head is presented. The *braita* asks *keitzad haya oseh* — how would one do this? The answer is *notein et hapader al beit hashchita* — one would take the fat and arrange it around the place where the neck was severed, and then bring it up to the altar in that form. *V'zehu derech kavod shel ma'ala* — this is the respectful way to present it before God. Rather than presenting a severed head with an exposed bloody neck, the *kohen* would wrap the area of the cut with fat, so that what was presented appeared as a composed and dignified offering.

### Aesthetics & Sanctity

This practice raises a broader question about the role of aesthetics in the *Mikdash*. The entire service involves a certain tension: on the one hand, the blood of the animal is central to the ritual — *ki hadam hu hanefesh*, the blood represents the life-force — and the whole act is inherently bound up with slaughter. On the other hand, the *Mikdash* is pervaded by a concern for *hod v'hadar*, beauty and elevation: the gold, the priestly garments, the careful ordering of every element of the service. The wrapping of the severed neck with fat is a small but telling example of this balance. The act of slaughter is not concealed in any deeper sense — the people standing in the *azarah* and the *kohanim* carrying out the service were all entirely aware of what had occurred — but the presentation of the offering is calibrated to feel elevated rather than stark. The concern is not to pretend the animal was not killed, but to ensure that the mode of presentation reflects the dignity appropriate to an offering before the *Shechinah*.

## 3rd & 4th Lotteries

### Incense Lottery

The *Mishnah* continues: *hapayis hashlishi* — the third lottery. Unlike the first two lotteries, which were open to all *kohanim*, the third lottery was restricted to *chadashim* — those who had never previously offered the *ketoret*. The announcement would go out: whoever has not yet had the privilege of offering the incense, come and take part in the lottery. The *Gemara* will discuss whether this lottery was repeated in the afternoon.

The fourth lottery, *harevii*, was open to everyone — *chadashim im yeshanim*, new and veteran *kohanim* alike. It determined who would bring the limbs from the *kevesh* up to the altar itself and place them on the fire. It is worth noting that a later *Rashi* appears to indicate this final stage involved only a single *kohen*, which creates a striking contrast with the nine *kohanim* who carried the limbs to the ramp. The *Mishnah* here does not specify the number.

### Wealth Blessing

A *tanna* teaches: *me'olam lo shana adam bah* — no one ever offered the *ketoret* twice. This was not a miraculous outcome; it was a deliberate policy. Why? Rabbi Chanina explains: *mipnei she-me'asheret* — because the *ketoret* brings wealth, and they therefore wanted to give every *kohen* an opportunity.

Rav Papa asks Abaye for the basis of this claim. One possible source: the verse in *Ve'zot HaBeracha* addressed to Levi says *yasimu ketoret be'apecha vechalil al mizbechecha* — they shall place incense before You and whole-offerings on Your altar — followed immediately by *barech Hashem cheilo* — may God bless his wealth. The juxtaposition suggests that offering the incense brings divine blessing of fortune. But the very same verse also mentions the *olah*, the burnt offering. If the blessing follows from the verse, why is the restriction limited to the *ketoret* and not extended to the *olah* as well?

The answer: *hahi shchicha, v'ha lo shchicha* — the *olah* is common, the *ketoret* is rare. Rashi explains the logic: we know that not everyone in the world can be wealthy simultaneously. If the verse's blessing of wealth applied to every *kohen* who brought an *olah*, then every *kohen* would be rich — which is plainly not the case. The blessing must therefore attach to something rare. The *Tosafot Yeshanim* offers a different resolution: even if the *olah* also brings the blessing of wealth, there was no need to restrict participation in the *olah* lottery to new *kohanim*, since there are so many *olot* offered each day that every *kohen* will have ample opportunity. The restriction to first-timers only makes sense for the *ketoret*, which occurs just twice daily and where competition for the privilege would otherwise mean that some *kohanim* would accumulate multiple turns while others never had one.

## Tribal Origins

### Levi & Yissachar

From the blessing associated with the *ketoret* lottery, the Gemara pivots to a striking observation by Rava: *lo mishkachat tzurba merabbanan de-mori ela de-ati mi-shevet Levi o mi-shevet Yissachar* — you will not find a nascent Torah scholar who rules correctly on matters of *halacha* unless he derives from the tribe of Levi or the tribe of Yissachar. The source for Levi is the verse *yoru mishpatecha le-Ya'akov* — they shall teach Your laws to Jacob — which assigns the role of *hora'ah*, legal instruction, to the tribe of Levi. Before the rabbinic class emerged in the period of the *Bayit Sheni*, it was the *kohanim levi'im* who served as the religious and legal authorities of the Jewish people, both in their role in the *Beit HaMikdash* and as teachers of Torah. The verse *lehoros et Bnei Yisrael bein hatamei u'vein hatahor* — to instruct Israel between the impure and the pure — is addressed to the *kohanim*. In the wake of the *churban*, the rabbinic class assumed this role, but the Gemara traces the continuity back to those to whom it was originally entrusted.

The source for Yissachar: *u'mibnei Yissachar yodei vinah la'ittim lada'at mah ya'aseh Yisrael* — from the children of Yissachar, those who know wisdom of the times, to know what Israel should do. In the rabbinic imagination, Yissachar epitomized the forerunner of the Torah sage, devoted entirely to study and the determination of *halacha*.

### Judah vs. Others

The Gemara then asks: what about the tribe of Yehuda? The verse says *Yehuda mechokeki* — Yehuda is my lawgiver. Surely that implies legal authority as well?

The response draws a crucial distinction: *asukei shma'ata aliba de'hilchasa* is the criterion under discussion — the ability to work through the full *shakla ve'tarya* and arrive at the correct *halacha*. Yehuda's verse uses the word *mechokek*, which denotes a lawgiver or legislator. The verses associated with Levi and Yissachar use language of *hora'ah* — teaching, instructing, knowing what to do — which implies not merely the issuing of rulings but the capacity to arrive at what is actually correct. There is a meaningful difference between legislative authority and the judicial-interpretive capacity to determine the right application of the law in practice.

This maps onto historical reality as well. The *nesi'im* — from Hillel through Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and his descendants — derived from the tribe of Yehuda. They held the political and religious authority of the *nasi*, representing something closer to legislative and executive power. That is a different function from the *hora'ah* that flows from Levi and Yissachar.

### Lineage for Scholars

An important qualification must not be overlooked. Rava does not say that every great *tanna* or *amora* who ever reached the correct *halacha* came from Levi or Yissachar — that would be plainly untrue as a matter of recorded history. The statement is specifically about a *tzurba merabbanan*, a student, someone still early in their development as a scholar. If such a person is already ruling correctly — if someone still at the level of *tzurba merabbanan* is consistently arriving at sound *psak* — then, Rava is saying, it must be in the lineage. You can infer: this person must come from Levi or Yissachar. Once a scholar reaches full stature as a sage, his authority and accuracy derive from his own *Torah* and do not require this genealogical accounting. The statement is about nascent scholars, not about the ultimate authority of the great *poskim*.

## Afternoon Tamid

### Same Kohen Both

Rav Yochanan rules: *ein mafisin al tamid she'bein ha'arbayim* — there is no separate lottery for the afternoon *tamid*. Rather, whoever won the relevant lottery in the morning serves the same function in the afternoon: *kohen she'zachah bo bashacharit zachah bo ba'arvit*.

### Shabbat Exception

The Gemara challenges this from a *braita*: *keshem she'mafisin bashacharit kach mafisin bein ha'arbayim* — just as they conduct a lottery in the morning, so too in the afternoon. After some textual maneuvering over whether the *braita* is referring to the *tamid* (masculine) or the *ketoret* (feminine), a further *braita* is cited that explicitly uses both genders — one for the *tamid* and one for the *ketoret* — implying separate afternoon lotteries for both. Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak resolves the difficulty: *hacha be'Shabbat askinan, ho'il u'mishmarot mitchadshos* — these *braitot* are speaking about *Shabbat*, because on *Shabbat* the priestly shifts change. The outgoing *mishmar* completes the morning service, and the incoming *mishmar* takes over for the afternoon. Since a new group of *kohanim* is serving in the afternoon, a new lottery is indeed necessary. On a regular weekday, when the same *mishmar* serves all day, the morning lottery governs the afternoon as well.

The connection to the *lechem hapanim* is relevant here: the changing of the *mishmarot* occurred on *Shabbat*, with the incoming shift placing the new *lechem hapanim* on the *shulchan* and the outgoing shift removing the old. That transition marked the formal handoff between shifts.

### Single Morning Lottery

The Gemara then raises a structural concern: if we had previously entertained the possibility that separate afternoon lotteries were conducted every day — assigning different *kohanim* for the afternoon *tamid* and the afternoon *ketoret* — we would have exceeded the established count of four lotteries. The Gemara resolves this: *maiti kulhu mitzafra* — everything was assigned in the morning lottery itself. The morning lottery determined not only who would serve in the morning but who would serve in the afternoon. Different people could be assigned to the afternoon roles, but the assignment was made as part of the single morning lottery, not through a separate afternoon procedure. In the *maskanah*, the position is that on an ordinary weekday, the same *kohanim* who served in the morning served in the afternoon; a new lottery was only conducted on *Shabbat* when the *mishmarot* changed.

## 3rd & 4th Lotteries (II)

### Ramp-to-Altar

The fourth lottery was open to all — *chadashim im yeshanim* — and determined who would carry the limbs from the *kevesh* to the top of the *mizbe'ach* and place them on the fire. The *Mishnah*'s position is that this was a distinct lottery, separate from the lottery that determined who carried the limbs to the *kevesh* in the first place.

### Same vs. New Person

*Masnisin d'lo k'Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — the *Mishnah* does not follow the view of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who holds in the *Mishnah* of *Tamid* that the *kohen* who brought the limbs to the *kevesh* was the same one who then brought them up to the altar: *hamaleh evarim le'kevesh hu maaleh osam le'mizbe'ach*. This makes intuitive sense as a matter of continuity — you complete what you started.

The Gemara frames the dispute as a disagreement between two principles. One view holds: *birov am hadrat melech* — the greater the number of *kohanim* actively engaged in the service, the more honor it reflects. Many people wanting to participate in each stage of the *avodah* is itself an expression of devotion and royal honor. The other view holds: *bimkom Shechinah lav orach ar'a* — in the presence of the *Shechinah*, it is not respectful conduct to bring something to a certain point and then leave it for someone else to complete. Rashi explains: it appears as though you are simply leaving your task unfinished and letting another *kohen* clean up after you. The more respectful course is to see the task through yourself.

Rava then draws out the logical implications for the lottery count: *lo d'Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov aisa d'Rabbi Yehuda, v'lo d'Rabbi Yehuda aisa d'Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — the position of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the position of Rabbi Yehuda are mutually exclusive, because combining them would eliminate one of the four lotteries. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who says the same *kohen* brings the limbs from the *kevesh* to the altar, has no fourth lottery on this account — so his fourth lottery must come from a *machta* lottery (the fire pan for the *ketoret*), which Rabbi Yehuda rejects. Conversely, Rabbi Yehuda, who holds there is no separate *machta* lottery, requires the fourth lottery to come from the ramp-to-altar bringing — which Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov rejects. The Gemara adds: *v'ei meshkachat tanna d'sanei chamesh* — if you ever find a *tanna* who counts five lotteries, he would have to reject both Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and Rabbi Yehuda simultaneously, accepting both a separate *machta* lottery and a separate ramp-to-altar lottery.

## Number of Kohanim

### Base of Nine

A new *Mishnah* turns to the question of how many *kohanim* were involved in bringing the *tamid* to the altar. The range given is: nine, ten, eleven, or twelve — *lo pachut ve'lo yoter*. The base number of nine corresponds to the limbs of the animal itself.

### Ten on Sukkot

On *Sukkot*, a tenth *kohen* was added, carrying the *tzeluchit shel mayim* — the pitcher of water for the *nisuch hamayim*, the water libation performed together with the morning *tamid*.

### Eleven in PM

In the afternoon, the count rose to eleven. The base of nine plus two additional *kohanim* carrying the two logs (*shnei gizirei etzim*) to be placed on the altar fire. *Tosafot* notes that logs were also placed on the fire in the morning, but that was handled by the *kohen* who performed the *terumat hadeshen* and did not require additional personnel at the time of the *tamid* proper.

### Eleven Shabbat

On *Shabbat*, the count also reached eleven — but for a different reason. Two additional *kohanim* carried the two cups of *levonah* (*shnei bezichei levonah*) from the *lechem hapanim* on the *shulchan*, which were burned on the *mizbe'ach* on *Shabbat* morning. This accounts for the two extra, bringing the total to eleven.

### Twelve Max

The maximum of twelve was reached on *Shabbat* during *Sukkot* — *Shabbat she'betoch hachag*. On such a day, the morning service combined the two *levonah* cups of *Shabbat* and the water libation pitcher of *Sukkot*, adding three to the base of nine and reaching twelve. No single scenario beyond this combination yields a higher number for the morning *tamid*.

### Water Morning Only

The Gemara establishes that the *nisuch hamayim* on *Sukkot* was performed exclusively with the morning *tamid*, not the afternoon. The proof comes from the *Mishnah* itself: the *Mishnah* gets to twelve only by combining the two *Shabbat levonah* cups with the one *Sukkot* water pitcher, for a total of three additions over the base nine. If the water libation could be performed with the afternoon *tamid* as well, you could reach twelve on any weekday of *Sukkot* without needing *Shabbat* at all — nine plus the two afternoon logs plus the water pitcher equals twelve. Since the *Mishnah* does not list that as a scenario, it clearly cannot be done in the afternoon. Rav Ashi adds a further proof from a *Mishnah* describing how the *kohen* performing the water libation was instructed to lift his hands so the crowd in the *azarah* could see him pouring — implying the congregation was present and watching, which was characteristic of the morning service.

### Tzadduki Incident

The reason the crowd insisted on watching the *kohen* pour the water was rooted in a historical episode. The *nisuch hamayim* has no explicit biblical source and was entirely rejected by the *Tzedukim*, who denied the validity of oral tradition. On one occasion, a *Tzeduki kohen* — and the Gemara records a tradition that this may have been Alexander Yannai — went through the motions of the water libation but, rather than pouring the water into the designated funnel on the *mizbe'ach*, poured it over his own feet, either to show disdain for the practice or because he simply refused to perform what he regarded as an invalid rite. The reaction of the assembled crowd was immediate: *urgamuhu kol ha'am be'esrogayhem* — all the people stoned him with their *esrogim*. The crowd had their *lulav* and *esrog* with them, as is customary during the *Sukkot* service, and pelted him accordingly. This incident explains why the assembled worshippers thereafter demanded to see the *kohen* lift his hands and visibly perform the pouring.

The mention of the *esrogim* also serves as evidence that the *nisuch hamayim* took place in the morning, since that is when the people were gathered with their *arba minim*. Rashi notes that if the *esrogim* were mentioned only incidentally, the *Gemara* would not have bothered noting them; their inclusion underscores that the morning timing is itself legally significant.

### Afternoon Logs

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai provides the scriptural basis for the requirement to bring two logs with the afternoon *tamid*, with two *kohanim* carrying them. The verse says *v'archu eitzim* — "and they shall arrange the wood." Given that the morning wood-arrangement is already covered by the verse *u'vi'er aleha hakohen eitzim baboker baboker* — the *kohen* shall kindle wood upon it each morning — the second mention of arranging wood (*v'archu*) would be redundant if it also referred to the morning. The principle of *tana'ahu inyan letamid shel bein ha'arbayim* applies: assign the otherwise superfluous verse to the afternoon *tamid*. Moreover, the plural form *v'archu* indicates that two *kohanim* perform this task.

The Gemara considers and dismisses the possibility that both verses refer to the morning service, with the redundancy simply meaning that the wood is placed twice in the morning — once by one *kohen* and once by two. This reading is rejected because if that were the intent, the Torah would have used the same verb both times, either repeating *u'vi'er* or *v'arach/v'archu*. The use of two different verbs — *u'vi'er* for the morning and *v'archu* for the second instance — signals two different occasions, and the plural in the second instance establishes the two-*kohen* requirement for the afternoon.

### Total Thirteen-Sixteen

Rabbi Chiyya teaches that the full count of *kohanim* serving the morning *tamid* — from the very beginning of the service — ranges from thirteen to sixteen. This follows directly from adding the four *kohanim* who performed the preliminary *avodot* to the nine-to-twelve range of the *Mishnah* just discussed. The four preliminary roles were: the *shechita*, the *zerika* of the blood, the cleaning of the inner *mizbe'ach* (*mizbe'ach hapnimi*), and the cleaning of the *menorah*. Added to the nine-to-twelve of the bringing, the full range is thirteen to sixteen. A *braita* is then cited that counts seventeen, and the Gemara notes this would require rejecting both Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and Rabbi Yehuda simultaneously — accepting both a separate *machta* lottery and a separate ramp-to-altar lottery — thereby adding one more *kohen* to the maximum count.
