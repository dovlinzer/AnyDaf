# Yoma 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Order of Limbs

### Animal's Walking Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד דֶּרֶךְ הִלּוּכוֹ? הָרֹאשׁ וְהָרֶגֶל, הֶחָזֶה וְהַגֵּרָה, וּשְׁתֵּי יָדַיִם, וּשְׁתֵּי דְפָנוֹת, הָעוֹקֶץ וְהָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: **In what manner** is the animal brought up according to **the way it walks?** It is in this manner: **The head and the** right hind **leg** are brought up first. The other parts follow this sequence: **The breast and the neck, and** then **the two forelegs, and** then **the two flanks,** then **the tail and the** left hind **leg.**

The Gemara on 25b picks up in the middle of the page, roughly twenty to twenty-five lines from the bottom, with a *tanu rabbanan* introducing a debate about the order in which the limbs of the *tamid* were brought to the *kevesh* — the ramp leading to the altar — and, by extension, to the altar itself. Ben Azzai, speaking before Rabbi Akiva, reports that the limbs were brought in the order in which the animal would walk: *derech hilucho*. The Gemara then specifies what that order looked like. The head moves first, propelled by the right hind leg — so the head and the right hind leg form the first grouping. Then comes the chest and the throat (*hachazeh vehagerah*), which move together with the upper body connected to the head. The two forearms (*shtei yadayim*) follow, then the two flanks (*shtei defanot*), and finally the tail, rump, and left hind leg (*ha'oketz veharagel*). This, as Ben Azzai understood it, reflects the natural locomotion of the animal.

### Flaying Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: דֶּרֶךְ הֶפְשֵׁטוֹ הָיָה קָרֵב. כֵּיצַד דֶּרֶךְ הֶפְשֵׁטוֹ? הָרֹאשׁ וְהָרֶגֶל, הָעוֹקֶץ וְהָרֶגֶל, וּשְׁתֵּי דְפָנוֹת, וּשְׁתֵּי הַיָּדַיִם, הֶחָזֶה וְהַגֵּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: It was sacrificed** according to **the way it was skinned. In what manner** is the animal brought up according to **the way it was skinned?** It is in this manner: **The head and the** right **leg** are brought first, then **the tail and the** left **leg** together, then the **two flanks, and** then **the two forelegs,** then **the breast and the neck.**

Rabbi Yose disagrees and holds that the limbs were brought in the order of *derech hafshato* — the order in which the animal is flayed. The animal was hung by its legs and flayed downward toward the head. Accordingly, the head is brought first — not because it is flayed first, but because it is severed separately. Then comes the right hind leg, the tail and rump, the left hind leg, the two flanks, the two forearms, and finally the chest and throat. The flaying thus proceeds from the hanging legs downward, and that sequence governs the order of the bringing.

### Dismembering Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: דֶּרֶךְ נִיתּוּחוֹ הָיָה קָרֵב. כֵּיצַד דֶּרֶךְ נִיתּוּחוֹ? הָרֹאשׁ וְהָרֶגֶל, וּשְׁתֵּי יָדַיִם, הֶחָזֶה וְהַגֵּרָה, וּשְׁתֵּי דְפָנוֹת, וְהָעוֹקֶץ וְהָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: It was sacrificed** according to **the way it was cut up** into limbs after the skinning. **In what manner** is the animal brought up according to **the way it was cut up?** It is in this manner: **The head and the** right **leg** first, **and** then **the two forelegs,** then **the breast and the neck, and** then **the two flanks, and** then **the tail and the** left **leg.**

Rabbi Akiva holds that the order followed *derech nitucho* — the manner of dismembering the animal. The head and one hind leg are first, then the two forearms, the chest and throat, the two flanks, and finally the tail and remaining hind leg. This is strikingly similar to Ben Azzai's order, with the primary difference lying in where the two forearms appear in the sequence. Whether there is a genuinely natural sequence to the dismembering, or whether it reflects a technical choice made by the butchers, is something the Gemara leaves largely implicit.

### Quality/Importance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: דֶּרֶךְ עִילּוּיוֹ הָיָה קָרֵב. כֵּיצַד דֶּרֶךְ עִילּוּיוֹ? הָרֹאשׁ וְהָרֶגֶל, הֶחָזֶה וְהַגֵּרָה, וּשְׁתֵּי הַדְּפָנוֹת, וְהָעוֹקֶץ וְהָרֶגֶל, וּשְׁתֵּי הַיָּדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: It was sacrificed according to** the **quality** of its various sections, so that the best pieces were taken up first. **In what manner** is the animal brought up according to **the quality** of the pieces? It is in this manner: **The head and the** right **leg,** then **the breast and the neck, and** then **the two flanks,** then **the tail and the** left **leg, and** then the **two forelegs.**

Rabbi Yose HaGlili takes a different conceptual approach altogether. He holds that the order followed *derech iluyo* — the order of importance or quality. The head is brought first, then the hind leg, the chest, the throat, the two flanks, the tail and rump, the other hind leg, and the two forearms last. Here too the two forearms appear near the end, which will become significant momentarily.

### Quality Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״כׇּל נֵתַח טוֹב יָרֵךְ וְכָתֵף״? הָהִיא בִּכְחוּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question with regard to Rabbi Yosei HaGelili's position: **But isn't it written: "Every good piece, the thigh and the shoulder"** (Ezekiel 24:4), indicating that these are the best cuts of the animal? Why doesn't Rabbi Yosei HaGelili say that these are offered first (Rabbeinu Ḥananel)? The Gemara answers: **That** verse is speaking of a **lean** sheep, whose thigh and shoulder are indeed the best pieces. However, in the sheep brought for the daily offering, which were of the finest quality, there were other parts that were of higher quality.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בֵּין תַּנָּא דִּידַן וּבֵין רַבִּי יוֹסֵי — בָּתַר עִילּוּיָא דְבִשְׂרָא אָזְלִינַן. מָר אָזֵיל בָּתַר אִיבְרָא דְבִישְׂרָא, וּמָר אָזֵיל בָּתַר שֻׁמְנָא דְבִישְׂרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the reason behind the order of the pieces in the mishna. **Rava said: Both the *tanna*** of **our** mishna **and Rabbi Yosei** HaGelili agree that **we follow the** relative **quality of the meat** in the various pieces. The difference in opinion between them is that one **Sage,** the *tanna* of this mishna, **follows the limb,** i.e., the amount, **of the meat;** the other **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **follows the fattiness of the meat.**

The Gemara then introduces a verse from Yechezkel: *kol netach tov yareich vechasef* — "every choice cut, the thigh and the shoulder." This raises an immediate difficulty. If the thigh is considered a choice cut, how can the hind leg appear toward the bottom of the list in an ordering that claims to prioritize quality? The Gemara answers: *hahi be'chshusha* — that verse in Yechezkel refers to a lean animal, where the thigh is indeed the choicest portion. But in a fatty animal, the thigh is not the choice cut, and that is the context relevant to the *tamid*.

Rava then offers a unifying principle: *bein tanna didan u'vein Rabbi Yose, basar iluya de'bisra azlinan* — whether you take our *Tanna* whose list appears in the *Mishnah*, or Rabbi Yose HaGlili, both agree that the governing criterion is the quality of the meat. The difference between them is simply how "quality" is measured. *Mar azil basar eivra de'bisra* — one authority follows the size of the cut. The other, *u'mar azil basar shamna de'bisra* — follows the fattiness or richness of the cut. Our *Tanna* places the forearms (*shtei yadayim*) earlier in the sequence because they represent a large cut; Rabbi Yose HaGlili places them last because, while sizable, they are not especially rich in fat.

### Conceptual Meaning

Stepping back from the comparison of lists, the conceptual stakes of this debate are worth considering. The most intuitive position is the last one: you are presenting something on the *mizbe'ach*, and naturally you wish to present the choicest portions first. Whether "choicest" means largest or fattiest is a secondary question; the principle of prioritizing the finest cuts is readily comprehensible.

The most striking and unusual position is Ben Azzai's: mimicking the way the animal walks. One illuminating way to understand this is that by recreating the animal's natural movement — even in the sequence of severed limbs — you are insisting on conceptualizing the offering as an animal, a living being, rather than as so much dressed meat. The further one moves toward pure quality rankings, the more the offering looks like a butcher's display of premium cuts. Ben Azzai's approach insists on the animal's identity as the thing being given. The positions based on the order of flaying or dismembering occupy a middle ground — neither mimicking life nor explicitly ranking by quality, but simply following the technical sequence that the preparation of the animal itself dictated.

## Head & Fat First

### Head Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהַת רֹאשׁ קָרֵב בְּרֵישָׁא, מְנָא לַן? דְּתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִין לְרֹאשׁ וּפֶדֶר שֶׁקּוֹדְמִין לְכׇל הָאֵבָרִים — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ וְאֶת פִּדְרוֹ וְעָרַךְ״. וְאִידַּךְ: ״פָּדֶר״ אַחֲרִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Although the Sages disagree as to the order of bringing up the various pieces, **at any rate everyone agrees** that the **head is sacrificed first. From where do we** derive this? **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that** when sacrificing an animal **the head and the fat precede all the** other **limbs? The verse states:** "And he shall cut it up into its pieces **and its head and its fat, and** the priest **shall arrange** them on the wood that is on the fire upon the altar" (Leviticus 1:12). The Gemara asks: **And the other** mention of **fat** before this, where the verse states: "The pieces, the head and the fat" (Leviticus 1:8),

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי אֲתָא? לְכִדְתַנְיָא: כֵּיצַד הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה? נוֹתֵן אֶת הַפֶּדֶר אַבֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה וּמַעֲלֵהוּ, וְזֶה הוּא דֶּרֶךְ כָּבוֹד שֶׁל מַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **what does** that **come** to teach us? The Gemara explains: **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **In what manner would** the priest placing the pieces on the altar **do** so? **He** would **place the fat** right **over the place of slaughter,** that is, on the cut neck, **and bring it up** that way, **and that is the** most **respectful way toward the Most High,** that the bloody point of slaughter not be exposed.

The Gemara now asks a question that applies across all the opinions: *dechulei alma mihat rosh karev bereisha* — everyone seems to agree the head is brought first. What is the source for this? A *braita* provides it: *menayan lerosh upader shekadmu lechol ha'evarim* — how do we know the head and the fat precede all the other limbs? The verse says *et rosho v'et pidro v'arach* — "its head and its fat, and you shall arrange it on the fire." The juxtaposition of head and fat with the command to place them on the fire establishes their priority.

But then the Gemara notes that the verse mentions *peder* — the fat — twice. The second mention, in the verse *et hanetachim et harosh v'et hapader*, generates a further teaching from a *braita*. The head itself is not a particularly choice cut — it is mostly bone with relatively little meat. So why is it brought first at all? And once it is brought, you bring it alongside a large, meaty limb to compensate for its own lack of meat. That is the function of bringing the hind leg together with the head: *mishum derisha nefishei bei tarmayot, kariv regla bahadei* — because the head is full of bones, you bring the leg alongside it.

### Aesthetic Covering

The second mention of *peder* teaches something different: the manner in which the head is presented. The *braita* asks *keitzad haya oseh* — how would one do this? The answer is *notein et hapader al beit hashchita* — one would take the fat and arrange it around the place where the neck was severed, and then bring it up to the altar in that form. *V'zehu derech kavod shel ma'ala* — this is the respectful way to present it before God. Rather than presenting a severed head with an exposed bloody neck, the *kohen* would wrap the area of the cut with fat, so that what was presented appeared as a composed and dignified offering.

### Aesthetics & Sanctity

This practice raises a broader question about the role of aesthetics in the *Mikdash*. The entire service involves a certain tension: on the one hand, the blood of the animal is central to the ritual — *ki hadam hu hanefesh*, the blood represents the life-force — and the whole act is inherently bound up with slaughter. On the other hand, the *Mikdash* is pervaded by a concern for *hod v'hadar*, beauty and elevation: the gold, the priestly garments, the careful ordering of every element of the service. The wrapping of the severed neck with fat is a small but telling example of this balance. The act of slaughter is not concealed in any deeper sense — the people standing in the *azarah* and the *kohanim* carrying out the service were all entirely aware of what had occurred — but the presentation of the offering is calibrated to feel elevated rather than stark. The concern is not to pretend the animal was not killed, but to ensure that the mode of presentation reflects the dignity appropriate to an offering before the *Shechinah*.

## 3rd & 4th Lotteries

### Incense Lottery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפַּיִיס הַשְּׁלִישִׁי: חֲדָשִׁים לִקְטוֹרֶת בֹּאוּ וְהָפִיסוּ. וְהָרְבִיעִי: חֲדָשִׁים עִם יְשָׁנִים — מִי מַעֲלֶה אֵבָרִים מִן הַכֶּבֶשׁ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Before **the third lottery,** the appointee declared: Let only those priests who are **new to** offering **the incense** come and **participate in the lottery** for the incense. **The fourth** lottery was open to those **new** to the service along **with** those **old** hands who had already performed it, to determine **who** would **take the limbs up from the ramp,** where they had been placed earlier, **to the altar.**

The *Mishnah* continues: *hapayis hashlishi* — the third lottery. Unlike the first two lotteries, which were open to all *kohanim*, the third lottery was restricted to *chadashim* — those who had never previously offered the *ketoret*. The announcement would go out: whoever has not yet had the privilege of offering the incense, come and take part in the lottery. The *Gemara* will discuss whether this lottery was repeated in the afternoon.

The fourth lottery, *harevii*, was open to everyone — *chadashim im yeshanim*, new and veteran *kohanim* alike. It determined who would bring the limbs from the *kevesh* up to the altar itself and place them on the fire. It is worth noting that a later *Rashi* appears to indicate this final stage involved only a single *kohen*, which creates a striking contrast with the nine *kohanim* who carried the limbs to the ramp. The *Mishnah* here does not specify the number.

### Wealth Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: מֵעוֹלָם לֹא שָׁנָה אָדָם בָּהּ. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲשֶׁרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: A Sage **taught** in the *Tosefta*: **No person ever performed** the service of the incense **twice,** as a new priest was always found for this service. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that they were insistent that no priest should be assigned this task more than once in his life? **Rabbi Ḥanina said:** It is **because it brings wealth** to the one who performs it. Since bringing the incense was a blessing for wealth, it was decided that as many different priests as possible should have an opportunity to do this service.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב: ״יָשִׂימוּ קְטוֹרָה בְּאַפֶּךָ״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״בָּרֵךְ ה׳ חֵילוֹ״, אִי הָכִי עוֹלָה נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״וְכָלִיל עַל מִזְבְּחֶךָ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: What is the reason** for this assertion that the one who burns the incense becomes wealthy? **If we say it is because it is written: "They shall put incense before You** and whole burnt-offerings on Your altar" (Deuteronomy 33:10), **and it is written** immediately **after that: "Bless, O Lord, his substance"** (Deuteronomy 33:11), **if so,** we should **also** make the same assertion concerning those who perform the sacrifice of **a burnt-offering, since it is written** in that same verse: **"And whole burnt-offerings on Your altar."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא שְׁכִיחָא וְהָא לָא שְׁכִיחָא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him:** There is a difference between the two: **This,** the sacrifice of a burnt-offering, **is frequent, and that,** the burning of incense, **is infrequent.** There were many burnt-offerings, both obligatory and voluntary, brought during the course of a day, whereas the incense was burned only twice a day. It is logical to assume that the blessing of riches was not extended to the many priests who participated in the burnt-offerings, but to the few priests who performed the burning of the incense.

A *tanna* teaches: *me'olam lo shana adam bah* — no one ever offered the *ketoret* twice. This was not a miraculous outcome; it was a deliberate policy. Why? Rabbi Chanina explains: *mipnei she-me'asheret* — because the *ketoret* brings wealth, and they therefore wanted to give every *kohen* an opportunity.

Rav Papa asks Abaye for the basis of this claim. One possible source: the verse in *Ve'zot HaBeracha* addressed to Levi says *yasimu ketoret be'apecha vechalil al mizbechecha* — they shall place incense before You and whole-offerings on Your altar — followed immediately by *barech Hashem cheilo* — may God bless his wealth. The juxtaposition suggests that offering the incense brings divine blessing of fortune. But the very same verse also mentions the *olah*, the burnt offering. If the blessing follows from the verse, why is the restriction limited to the *ketoret* and not extended to the *olah* as well?

The answer: *hahi shchicha, v'ha lo shchicha* — the *olah* is common, the *ketoret* is rare. Rashi explains the logic: we know that not everyone in the world can be wealthy simultaneously. If the verse's blessing of wealth applied to every *kohen* who brought an *olah*, then every *kohen* would be rich — which is plainly not the case. The blessing must therefore attach to something rare. The *Tosafot Yeshanim* offers a different resolution: even if the *olah* also brings the blessing of wealth, there was no need to restrict participation in the *olah* lottery to new *kohanim*, since there are so many *olot* offered each day that every *kohen* will have ample opportunity. The restriction to first-timers only makes sense for the *ketoret*, which occurs just twice daily and where competition for the privilege would otherwise mean that some *kohanim* would accumulate multiple turns while others never had one.

## Tribal Origins

### Levi & Yissachar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן דְּמוֹרֵי אֶלָּא דְּאָתֵי מִשֵּׁבֶט לֵוִי אוֹ מִשֵּׁבֶט יִשָּׂשכָר. לֵוִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״יוֹרוּ מִשְׁפָּטֶיךָ לְיַעֲקֹב״. יִשָּׂשכָר, דִּכְתִיב: ״(וּבְנֵי) יִשָּׂשכָר יוֹדְעֵי בִינָה לַעִתִּים לָדַעַת מַה יַּעֲשֶׂה יִשְׂרָאֵל״. וְאֵימָא יְהוּדָה נָמֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״יְהוּדָה מְחוֹקְקִי״! אַסּוֹקֵי שְׁמַעְתָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְהִילְכְתָא קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos this passage in Deuteronomy, **Rava said: You do not find a young Torah scholar who gives** halakhic **instruction unless he comes from the tribe of Levi or from the tribe of Issachar.** The assertion with regard to the tribe of **Levi** is **as it is written: "They shall teach Jacob Your ordinances** and Israel Your law" (Deuteronomy 33:10). And the assertion with regard to the tribe of **Issachar** is **as it is written: "And of the children of Issachar, men that had understanding of the times, to know what Israel should do"** (I Chronicles 12:33). The Gemara asks: **And say** that scholars come from the tribe of **Judah also, as it is written: "Judah is my lawgiver"** (Psalms 60:9). Rava answers: While it is true that the tribe of Judah also taught Torah, in my statement **I was speaking** only **of those who** can **draw conclusions according to the *halakha*.** Although Judah produces great scholars, men capable of translating abstract analysis of the Torah into legal principles come from the two tribes mentioned.

From the blessing associated with the *ketoret* lottery, the Gemara pivots to a striking observation by Rava: *lo mishkachat tzurba merabbanan de-mori ela de-ati mi-shevet Levi o mi-shevet Yissachar* — you will not find a nascent Torah scholar who rules correctly on matters of *halacha* unless he derives from the tribe of Levi or the tribe of Yissachar. The source for Levi is the verse *yoru mishpatecha le-Ya'akov* — they shall teach Your laws to Jacob — which assigns the role of *hora'ah*, legal instruction, to the tribe of Levi. Before the rabbinic class emerged in the period of the *Bayit Sheni*, it was the *kohanim levi'im* who served as the religious and legal authorities of the Jewish people, both in their role in the *Beit HaMikdash* and as teachers of Torah. The verse *lehoros et Bnei Yisrael bein hatamei u'vein hatahor* — to instruct Israel between the impure and the pure — is addressed to the *kohanim*. In the wake of the *churban*, the rabbinic class assumed this role, but the Gemara traces the continuity back to those to whom it was originally entrusted.

The source for Yissachar: *u'mibnei Yissachar yodei vinah la'ittim lada'at mah ya'aseh Yisrael* — from the children of Yissachar, those who know wisdom of the times, to know what Israel should do. In the rabbinic imagination, Yissachar epitomized the forerunner of the Torah sage, devoted entirely to study and the determination of *halacha*.

### Judah vs. Others

The Gemara then asks: what about the tribe of Yehuda? The verse says *Yehuda mechokeki* — Yehuda is my lawgiver. Surely that implies legal authority as well?

The response draws a crucial distinction: *asukei shma'ata aliba de'hilchasa* is the criterion under discussion — the ability to work through the full *shakla ve'tarya* and arrive at the correct *halacha*. Yehuda's verse uses the word *mechokek*, which denotes a lawgiver or legislator. The verses associated with Levi and Yissachar use language of *hora'ah* — teaching, instructing, knowing what to do — which implies not merely the issuing of rulings but the capacity to arrive at what is actually correct. There is a meaningful difference between legislative authority and the judicial-interpretive capacity to determine the right application of the law in practice.

This maps onto historical reality as well. The *nesi'im* — from Hillel through Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and his descendants — derived from the tribe of Yehuda. They held the political and religious authority of the *nasi*, representing something closer to legislative and executive power. That is a different function from the *hora'ah* that flows from Levi and Yissachar.

### Lineage for Scholars

An important qualification must not be overlooked. Rava does not say that every great *tanna* or *amora* who ever reached the correct *halacha* came from Levi or Yissachar — that would be plainly untrue as a matter of recorded history. The statement is specifically about a *tzurba merabbanan*, a student, someone still early in their development as a scholar. If such a person is already ruling correctly — if someone still at the level of *tzurba merabbanan* is consistently arriving at sound *psak* — then, Rava is saying, it must be in the lineage. You can infer: this person must come from Levi or Yissachar. Once a scholar reaches full stature as a sage, his authority and accuracy derive from his own *Torah* and do not require this genealogical accounting. The statement is about nascent scholars, not about the ultimate authority of the great *poskim*.

## Afternoon Tamid

### Same Kohen Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין מְפַיְּיסִין עַל תָּמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם, אֶלָּא כֹּהֵן שֶׁזָּכָה בּוֹ בְּשַׁחֲרִית זוֹכֶה בּוֹ עַרְבִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: They did not hold** a separate **lottery** for the slaughtering and sacrifice of the **daily afternoon offering. Rather,** the same **priest who won** a particular privilege for **the morning** offering **wins** the privilege for the corresponding task in the **evening,** i.e., for the **afternoon** service. In this way, the morning lottery covered both services.

Rav Yochanan rules: *ein mafisin al tamid she'bein ha'arbayim* — there is no separate lottery for the afternoon *tamid*. Rather, whoever won the relevant lottery in the morning serves the same function in the afternoon: *kohen she'zachah bo bashacharit zachah bo ba'arvit*.

### Shabbat Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּפַיְּיסִין שַׁחֲרִית כָּךְ מְפַיְּיסִין בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם! כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא — בִּקְטוֹרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Just as they hold a lottery in the morning, so too, they hold a lottery in the afternoon.** This shows that there was a separate lottery for the daily afternoon offering. The Gemara answers: **When that** *baraita* **was taught,** it referred only **to the incense,** which, as stated above, was given to a different priest each time it was offered.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּפַיְּיסִין לוֹ שַׁחֲרִית כָּךְ מְפַיְּיסִין לוֹ עַרְבִית! אֵימָא ״לָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Just as they hold a lottery for it [*lo*] in the morning, so too, they hold a lottery for it [*lo*] in the afternoon.** The masculine pronoun *lo* indicates that it is not referring to the incense, which is a feminine noun in Hebrew, but to the daily afternoon offering, which is described by a masculine noun. The Gemara answers: Change the wording of the *baraita* and **say: *Lah*,** using the feminine pronoun instead of the masculine *lo*, so that it is indeed referring to the incense.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּפַיְּיסִין לוֹ שַׁחֲרִית כָּךְ מְפַיְּיסִין לוֹ עַרְבִית, וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁמְּפַיְּיסִין לָהּ שַׁחֲרִית כָּךְ מְפַיְּיסִין לָהּ עַרְבִית!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **But wasn't it taught** in another *baraita*: **Just as they hold a lottery for it [*lo*] the morning, so too, they hold a lottery for it [*lo*] in the afternoon; and just as they hold a lottery for it [*lah*] in the morning, so too, they hold a lottery for it [*lah*] in the afternoon.** This *baraita* makes the statement twice, once using the masculine pronoun and once using the feminine pronoun, which shows that there was a separate lottery in the afternoon not only for the incense but also for the daily offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: הָכָא בְּשַׁבָּת עָסְקִינַן, הוֹאִיל וּמִשְׁמָרוֹת מִתְחַדְּשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak said:** There is no contradiction. **Here,** in this last *baraita*, **we are dealing with Shabbat,** when a second lottery in the afternoon was necessary, **since the priestly rotations are renewed** each Shabbat. On Shabbat the outgoing watch of priests performs the morning service, and the incoming watch performs the afternoon service. Therefore, the same priest could not perform the service of both the morning and afternoon offerings, necessitating a second lottery on that day to designate priests for the various afternoon tasks.

The Gemara challenges this from a *braita*: *keshem she'mafisin bashacharit kach mafisin bein ha'arbayim* — just as they conduct a lottery in the morning, so too in the afternoon. After some textual maneuvering over whether the *braita* is referring to the *tamid* (masculine) or the *ketoret* (feminine), a further *braita* is cited that explicitly uses both genders — one for the *tamid* and one for the *ketoret* — implying separate afternoon lotteries for both. Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak resolves the difficulty: *hacha be'Shabbat askinan, ho'il u'mishmarot mitchadshos* — these *braitot* are speaking about *Shabbat*, because on *Shabbat* the priestly shifts change. The outgoing *mishmar* completes the morning service, and the incoming *mishmar* takes over for the afternoon. Since a new group of *kohanim* is serving in the afternoon, a new lottery is indeed necessary. On a regular weekday, when the same *mishmar* serves all day, the morning lottery governs the afternoon as well.

The connection to the *lechem hapanim* is relevant here: the changing of the *mishmarot* occurred on *Shabbat*, with the incoming shift placing the new *lechem hapanim* on the *shulchan* and the outgoing shift removing the old. That transition marked the formal handoff between shifts.

### Single Morning Lottery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאי דִּסְלֵיק אַדַּעְתִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא, נְפִישִׁי לְהוּ פְּיָיסוֹת! מַיְיתֵי כּוּלְּהוּ מִצַּפְרָא אָתוּ, דְּזָכֵי בֵּיהּ שַׁחֲרִית — זָכֵי, דְּזָכֵי בְּעַרְבִית — זָכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according **to what we thought initially,** that there was a separate lottery each day for the daily afternoon offering, **there would be** too **many lotteries,** as the mishna states that there were just four lotteries daily. How was it conceivable even to consider such a possibility? The Gemara answers: The thought was that **all** the priests **would come** and assemble just once, **in the morning,** for both lotteries, and the priest **who would win** the lottery for sacrificing **the** daily **morning** offering **would win** that privilege for the morning only, and the priest **who would win** the lottery for sacrificing **the** daily **afternoon** offering **would win** the privilege for the afternoon.

The Gemara then raises a structural concern: if we had previously entertained the possibility that separate afternoon lotteries were conducted every day — assigning different *kohanim* for the afternoon *tamid* and the afternoon *ketoret* — we would have exceeded the established count of four lotteries. The Gemara resolves this: *maiti kulhu mitzafra* — everything was assigned in the morning lottery itself. The morning lottery determined not only who would serve in the morning but who would serve in the afternoon. Different people could be assigned to the afternoon roles, but the assignment was made as part of the single morning lottery, not through a separate afternoon procedure. In the *maskanah*, the position is that on an ordinary weekday, the same *kohanim* who served in the morning served in the afternoon; a new lottery was only conducted on *Shabbat* when the *mishmarot* changed.

## 3rd & 4th Lotteries…

### Ramp-to-Altar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפַּיִיס הַשְּׁלִישִׁי: חֲדָשִׁים לִקְטוֹרֶת בֹּאוּ וְהָפִיסוּ. וְהָרְבִיעִי: חֲדָשִׁים עִם יְשָׁנִים — מִי מַעֲלֶה אֵבָרִים מִן הַכֶּבֶשׁ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Before **the third lottery,** the appointee declared: Let only those priests who are **new to** offering **the incense** come and **participate in the lottery** for the incense. **The fourth** lottery was open to those **new** to the service along **with** those **old** hands who had already performed it, to determine **who** would **take the limbs up from the ramp,** where they had been placed earlier, **to the altar.**

The fourth lottery was open to all — *chadashim im yeshanim* — and determined who would carry the limbs from the *kevesh* to the top of the *mizbe'ach* and place them on the fire. The *Mishnah*'s position is that this was a distinct lottery, separate from the lottery that determined who carried the limbs to the *kevesh* in the first place.

### Same vs. New Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב. דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: הַמַּעֲלֶה אֵיבָרִים לַכֶּבֶשׁ, הוּא מַעֲלֶה אוֹתָן לַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov. As we learned** in a mishna in tractate *Tamid* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** The priest **who takes the limbs up to the ramp is the one who takes them up** from the ramp **to the altar.** In contrast, according to the mishna discussed here, it is implied that a different priest won the privilege for the latter service in the lottery.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: ״בְּרׇב עָם הַדְרַת מֶלֶךְ״, וּמָר סָבַר: מְקוֹם שְׁכִינָה לָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do they disagree? One Sage,** the *tanna* of the mishna discussed here, **holds** that it is proper to follow the verse: **"In the multitude of people is the king's glory"** (Proverbs 14:28). It is a glorification of God for many priests to participate in the service, so different priests were assigned the task of taking the limbs to the ramp, and others were tasked with carrying them up the ramp to the altar. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, **holds** that it is **not proper conduct** in **the place of the Divine Presence** to have two sets of priests for these tasks, as it gives the appearance that the first set does not want to be bothered to take the limbs up to the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אִית לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְלָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִית לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב. דְּאִם כֵּן, בָּצְרוּ לְהוּ פְּיָיסוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov,** who holds that the same priest who brought the limbs to the ramp also brought them up to the altar, **is not of** the same opinion as **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that there is no separate lottery for the privilege of carrying the coal pan for the incense. **And** conversely, **Rabbi Yehuda is not of** the same opinion as **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov.** As, **if it would be so** that these two Sages agreed with each other, **there would be too few lotteries;** there would be only three lotteries rather than four. Rather, one must say that according to Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, who maintains that there was no lottery held for taking the limbs up to the altar, there was a fourth lottery to determine who would carry the coal pan; and according to Rabbi Yehuda, who maintains that there was no lottery for carrying the coal pan, there must have been a lottery for carrying the limbs up to the ramp.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ תַּנָּא דְּתָנֵי חָמֵשׁ — הָהוּא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you find a *tanna*** in a *baraita* **who teaches** that there were **five** lotteries for the Temple service, the opinion of **that** *tanna* would be **in accordance with neither** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov nor** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** as these five lotteries would include one for carrying the coal pan and another one for taking the limbs up to the altar.

*Masnisin d'lo k'Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — the *Mishnah* does not follow the view of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who holds in the *Mishnah* of *Tamid* that the *kohen* who brought the limbs to the *kevesh* was the same one who then brought them up to the altar: *hamaleh evarim le'kevesh hu maaleh osam le'mizbe'ach*. This makes intuitive sense as a matter of continuity — you complete what you started.

The Gemara frames the dispute as a disagreement between two principles. One view holds: *birov am hadrat melech* — the greater the number of *kohanim* actively engaged in the service, the more honor it reflects. Many people wanting to participate in each stage of the *avodah* is itself an expression of devotion and royal honor. The other view holds: *bimkom Shechinah lav orach ar'a* — in the presence of the *Shechinah*, it is not respectful conduct to bring something to a certain point and then leave it for someone else to complete. Rashi explains: it appears as though you are simply leaving your task unfinished and letting another *kohen* clean up after you. The more respectful course is to see the task through yourself.

Rava then draws out the logical implications for the lottery count: *lo d'Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov aisa d'Rabbi Yehuda, v'lo d'Rabbi Yehuda aisa d'Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — the position of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the position of Rabbi Yehuda are mutually exclusive, because combining them would eliminate one of the four lotteries. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who says the same *kohen* brings the limbs from the *kevesh* to the altar, has no fourth lottery on this account — so his fourth lottery must come from a *machta* lottery (the fire pan for the *ketoret*), which Rabbi Yehuda rejects. Conversely, Rabbi Yehuda, who holds there is no separate *machta* lottery, requires the fourth lottery to come from the ramp-to-altar bringing — which Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov rejects. The Gemara adds: *v'ei meshkachat tanna d'sanei chamesh* — if you ever find a *tanna* who counts five lotteries, he would have to reject both Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and Rabbi Yehuda simultaneously, accepting both a separate *machta* lottery and a separate ramp-to-altar lottery.

## Number of Kohanim

### Base of Nine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ תָּמִיד קָרֵב בְּתִשְׁעָה, בַּעֲשָׂרָה, בְּאַחַד עָשָׂר, בִּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר. לֹא פָּחוֹת וְלֹא יוֹתֵר. כֵּיצַד? עַצְמוֹ בְּתִשְׁעָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The **daily offering is sacrificed** and its limbs are carried **by nine** priests, as mentioned in a previous mishna. These nine carry the limbs and the accompanying libations and meal-offerings. Occasionally, the service is performed **by ten** priests, occasionally **by eleven,** and sometimes **by twelve** priests; **no fewer** than nine **and no more** than twelve. **How so?** The daily offering **itself** is sacrificed **by nine** priests, as explained earlier.

A new *Mishnah* turns to the question of how many *kohanim* were involved in bringing the *tamid* to the altar. The range given is: nine, ten, eleven, or twelve — *lo pachut ve'lo yoter*. The base number of nine corresponds to the limbs of the animal itself.

### Ten on Sukkot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּחָג, בְּיַד אֶחָד צְלוֹחִית שֶׁל מַיִם — הֲרֵי כָּאן עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **On the festival** of *Sukkot* a priest **in** whose **hand** is **a jug of water** for the water libation is added, and **there are ten** priests. On *Sukkot*, a water libation is poured on the altar in addition to the standard wine libation.

On *Sukkot*, a tenth *kohen* was added, carrying the *tzeluchit shel mayim* — the pitcher of water for the *nisuch hamayim*, the water libation performed together with the morning *tamid*.

### Eleven in PM

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם בְּאַחַד עָשָׂר: הוּא עַצְמוֹ בְּתִשְׁעָה, וּשְׁנַיִם בְּיָדָם שְׁנֵי גְּזִירֵי עֵצִים.
>
> **Translation:** **In the** daily **afternoon** offering, **eleven** priests participate in the service. How so? The daily offering **itself** is sacrificed **by nine, and** there are an additional **two** priests **in whose hands** are **two logs** that are placed on the altar. The mitzva of placing the two logs in the morning was assigned in the first lottery, as the Gemara explained earlier.

In the afternoon, the count rose to eleven. The base of nine plus two additional *kohanim* carrying the two logs (*shnei gizirei etzim*) to be placed on the altar fire. *Tosafot* notes that logs were also placed on the fire in the morning, but that was handled by the *kohen* who performed the *terumat hadeshen* and did not require additional personnel at the time of the *tamid* proper.

### Eleven Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּשַּׁבָּת בְּאַחַד עָשָׂר: הוּא עַצְמוֹ בְּתִשְׁעָה, וּשְׁנַיִם בְּיָדָם שְׁנֵי בְּזִיכֵי לְבוֹנָה שֶׁל לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **On Shabbat, eleven** priests participate. How so? The daily morning offering **itself** is performed **by nine, and** there are an additional **two** priests **in whose hands** are **two vessels of frankincense** that accompany **the shewbread.** This frankincense is burned on Shabbat.

On *Shabbat*, the count also reached eleven — but for a different reason. Two additional *kohanim* carried the two cups of *levonah* (*shnei bezichei levonah*) from the *lechem hapanim* on the *shulchan*, which were burned on the *mizbe'ach* on *Shabbat* morning. This accounts for the two extra, bringing the total to eleven.

### Twelve Max

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְשַׁבָּת שֶׁבְּתוֹךְ הַחַג בְּיַד אֶחָד צְלוֹחִית שֶׁל מַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And on Shabbat that** occurs **within the festival** of *Sukkot* there is an additional priest **in whose hand** is a **jug of water** for the water libation, for a total of twelve priests.

The maximum of twelve was reached on *Shabbat* during *Sukkot* — *Shabbat she'betoch hachag*. On such a day, the morning service combined the two *levonah* cups of *Shabbat* and the water libation pitcher of *Sukkot*, adding three to the base of nine and reaching twelve. No single scenario beyond this combination yields a higher number for the morning *tamid*.

### Water Morning Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא וְאִיתֵּימָא רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין מְנַסְּכִין מַיִם בֶּחָג אֶלָּא בְּתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר. מִמַּאי? מִדְּקָתָנֵי: וּבְשַׁבָּת שֶׁבְּתוֹךְ הֶחָג בְּיַד אֶחָד צְלוֹחִית שֶׁל מַיִם. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ בְּתָמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם מְנַסְּכִין, בַּחוֹל נָמֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Abba, and some say** it was **Rami bar Ḥama, and some say** it was **Rabbi Yoḥanan, said: On the festival** of *Sukkot* **they pour the water libation only** during the sacrifice of **the daily morning offering** and not in the afternoon. **From where** is this derived? It is derived **from** the fact **that it is taught** in the mishna: **And on Shabbat that** occurs **within the festival** of *Sukkot* there is a priest **in whose hand** is a **jug of water,** bringing the number of participating priests to twelve. **And if it should enter your mind** to say that **they pour** water **during the daily afternoon offering** also, if **so, you find** that there were twelve priests on **a weekday as well,** that is, during the intermediate days of the Festival: Nine priests for the daily offering, two to carry the logs, and one to pour the water.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: וְלַמְנַסֵּךְ אוֹמֵר לוֹ ״הַגְבַּהּ יָדֶיךָ״, שֶׁפַּעַם אַחַת נִסֵּךְ עַל גַּבֵּי רַגְלָיו, וּרְגָמוּהוּ כׇּל הָעָם בְּאֶתְרוֹגֵיהֶן. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: We too have learned** this in a different mishna, that the water libation was offered only in the morning, as it was taught: **And they would say to the pourer: Raise your hand** so everyone will see as you pour the water into the aperture on the altar, in accordance with the proper procedure. This was done **because one time** a Sadducee priest, who did not accept that there is a mitzva of water libation, **poured** the water **onto his feet,** whereupon **all the people pelted him with their *etrogim*** in anger. Since the episode involved *etrogim*, it is apparent that it took place in the morning, when people have their *etrogim* with them. Since the mishna mentions the fact that it was *etrogim* that were used to pelt the priest, it is apparently coming to teach that the water libation takes place only in the morning. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** that it is so.

The Gemara establishes that the *nisuch hamayim* on *Sukkot* was performed exclusively with the morning *tamid*, not the afternoon. The proof comes from the *Mishnah* itself: the *Mishnah* gets to twelve only by combining the two *Shabbat levonah* cups with the one *Sukkot* water pitcher, for a total of three additions over the base nine. If the water libation could be performed with the afternoon *tamid* as well, you could reach twelve on any weekday of *Sukkot* without needing *Shabbat* at all — nine plus the two afternoon logs plus the water pitcher equals twelve. Since the *Mishnah* does not list that as a scenario, it clearly cannot be done in the afternoon. Rav Ashi adds a further proof from a *Mishnah* describing how the *kohen* performing the water libation was instructed to lift his hands so the crowd in the *azarah* could see him pouring — implying the congregation was present and watching, which was characteristic of the morning service.

### Tzadduki Incident

The reason the crowd insisted on watching the *kohen* pour the water was rooted in a historical episode. The *nisuch hamayim* has no explicit biblical source and was entirely rejected by the *Tzedukim*, who denied the validity of oral tradition. On one occasion, a *Tzeduki kohen* — and the Gemara records a tradition that this may have been Alexander Yannai — went through the motions of the water libation but, rather than pouring the water into the designated funnel on the *mizbe'ach*, poured it over his own feet, either to show disdain for the practice or because he simply refused to perform what he regarded as an invalid rite. The reaction of the assembled crowd was immediate: *urgamuhu kol ha'am be'esrogayhem* — all the people stoned him with their *esrogim*. The crowd had their *lulav* and *esrog* with them, as is customary during the *Sukkot* service, and pelted him accordingly. This incident explains why the assembled worshippers thereafter demanded to see the *kohen* lift his hands and visibly perform the pouring.

The mention of the *esrogim* also serves as evidence that the *nisuch hamayim* took place in the morning, since that is when the people were gathered with their *arba minim*. Rashi notes that if the *esrogim* were mentioned only incidentally, the *Gemara* would not have bothered noting them; their inclusion underscores that the morning timing is itself legally significant.

### Afternoon Logs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִין לְתָמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם שֶׁטָּעוּן שְׁנֵי גְּזִירֵי עֵצִים בִּשְׁנֵי כֹהֲנִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָרְכוּ עֵצִים״, אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבִעֵר עָלֶיהָ הַכֹּהֵן עֵצִים בַּבֹּקֶר בַּבֹּקֶר וְעָרַךְ עָלֶיהָ״, תְּנֵיהוּ עִנְיָן לְתָמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: From where** is it derived **that the daily afternoon offering requires** that **two logs** be brought along with it, and that they must be brought **by two priests? As it is stated** with regard to the burnt-offering: "The sons of Aaron the priest shall put fire on the altar **and lay out wood** [***etzim***] in order upon the fire" (Leviticus 1:7). The word *etzim* is plural, which teaches that two logs are called for. **If this is not applicable to the daily morning offering, as it is** already **written about** the morning offering explicitly: **"And the priest shall burn wood on it every morning, and arrange** the burnt-offering **on it"** (Leviticus 6:5), **apply it to the daily afternoon offering.** The verse therefore teaches us that two logs should be added before that offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר, וְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: עֲבֵיד וַהֲדַר עֲבֵיד! אִם כֵּן, נֵימָא קְרָא ״וּבִעֵר״ ״וּבִעֵר״.
>
> **Translation:** **But say** that **this and this,** i.e., both verses cited above, are dealing with **the daily morning offering, and** that **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **Perform** the arrangement of wood **and then return and perform** it again. In other words, perhaps the Torah's intention is that two logs be arranged on the altar twice in the morning, and that accounts for the two verses. The Gemara rejects this possibility: **If** it were **so,** the text **should** use the same expression both times and **say: "And he shall burn** wood on it," and again: **"And he shall burn** wood on it." Since the text does not do so, but instead employs two different verbs, saying: "They shall lay out wood" once and: "He shall burn wood" the second time, this indicates that the Torah is describing two different times.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וּבִעֵר״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא חַד — אִין, תְּרֵי — לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּנַעְבֵּיד חַד וְנַעְבֵּיד תְּרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this inference: **If the Merciful One had written** in the Torah: **"And he shall burn** wood on it" twice, **I would have said** it means that **one** priest should arrange the wood, **not two.** The change of terminology to a plural verb is therefore necessary because **it teaches us** that **one** priest **should perform** it the first time **and two** priests **should perform** it the second time, but they are both performed in the morning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, נֵימָא קְרָא: ״וּבִעֵר״ ״וּבִעֲרוּ״, אִי נָמֵי: ״וְעָרַךְ״ ״וְעָרְכוּ״, מַאי ״וּבִעֵר״ ״וְעָרְכוּ״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כִּדְקָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **If so,** if this were what the Torah wished to indicate, **the verse should say: "And he shall burn** wood" in the singular, and then, in the second verse, say: **And they shall burn** wood, in the plural, using the same verb both times, changing only the number of the verb. **Or, alternatively,** the verse should say: **And he shall lay out** wood, in the singular, and then, in the second verse, say: **"And they shall lay out** wood," in the plural. **What** is the reason the Torah uses two different verbs in the two verses, stating: **"And he shall burn** wood" **and then: "And they shall lay out** wood"? **Learn from this as we have said,** that the Torah in these two verses is referring to two separate times of day, and the verse: "They shall lay out wood [*etzim*]" is referring to the daily afternoon offering, mandating that at that time "they," i.e., two priests, shall lay out *etzim*, the plural term for wood, referring to two logs.

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai provides the scriptural basis for the requirement to bring two logs with the afternoon *tamid*, with two *kohanim* carrying them. The verse says *v'archu eitzim* — "and they shall arrange the wood." Given that the morning wood-arrangement is already covered by the verse *u'vi'er aleha hakohen eitzim baboker baboker* — the *kohen* shall kindle wood upon it each morning — the second mention of arranging wood (*v'archu*) would be redundant if it also referred to the morning. The principle of *tana'ahu inyan letamid shel bein ha'arbayim* applies: assign the otherwise superfluous verse to the afternoon *tamid*. Moreover, the plural form *v'archu* indicates that two *kohanim* perform this task.

The Gemara considers and dismisses the possibility that both verses refer to the morning service, with the redundancy simply meaning that the wood is placed twice in the morning — once by one *kohen* and once by two. This reading is rejected because if that were the intent, the Torah would have used the same verb both times, either repeating *u'vi'er* or *v'arach/v'archu*. The use of two different verbs — *u'vi'er* for the morning and *v'archu* for the second instance — signals two different occasions, and the plural in the second instance establishes the two-*kohen* requirement for the afternoon.

### Total Thirteen-Sixteen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: פַּיִיס פְּעָמִים שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר, פְּעָמִים אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, פְּעָמִים חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר, פְּעָמִים שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya taught: Sometimes thirteen** priests were involved in sacrificing the daily offering, all these tasks being assigned in the second lottery, as the mishna taught earlier. But **sometimes fourteen** priests are chosen in this manner to participate, since on *Sukkot* an additional priest is chosen to pour the water libation. And **sometimes fifteen** priests are chosen, on Shabbat, when two priests are tasked with burning the frankincense in the vessels. And **sometimes sixteen** priests are chosen, on Shabbat that occurs during *Sukkot*, when three extra priests are added: One to pour the water and two to burn the frankincense.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא שִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to Rabbi Ḥiyya's statement: **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that sometimes there are **seventeen** priests involved in the daily offering?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, אֶלָּא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **That** *baraita* is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov but is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** The seventeenth task of the daily morning offering referred to in the *baraita* is taking up the pieces of the offering from the ramp to the altar. According to Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, this task was not assigned to a new priest but was performed by the same priests who had brought the pieces to the ramp. The *baraita*, which does assign this task to a seventeenth priest, is therefore not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov. As the Gemara explained earlier, the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov are mutually exclusive; consequently, since the *baraita* contradicts the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, it must be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Ḥiyya, however, adopted the view of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, and for this reason he taught that the maximum number of tasks assigned through the second lottery is only sixteen.

Rabbi Chiyya teaches that the full count of *kohanim* serving the morning *tamid* — from the very beginning of the service — ranges from thirteen to sixteen. This follows directly from adding the four *kohanim* who performed the preliminary *avodot* to the nine-to-twelve range of the *Mishnah* just discussed. The four preliminary roles were: the *shechita*, the *zerika* of the blood, the cleaning of the inner *mizbe'ach* (*mizbe'ach hapnimi*), and the cleaning of the *menorah*. Added to the nine-to-twelve of the bringing, the full range is thirteen to sixteen. A *braita* is then cited that counts seventeen, and the Gemara notes this would require rejecting both Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and Rabbi Yehuda simultaneously — accepting both a separate *machta* lottery and a separate ramp-to-altar lottery — thereby adding one more *kohen* to the maximum count.
