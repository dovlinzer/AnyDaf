# Menachot 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shtei HaLechem Alone

### Tanu Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם הַבָּאוֹת בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן – יוּנְפוּ, וּתְעוּבַּר צוּרָתָן, וְיֵצְאוּ לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that according to Rabbi Akiva failure to sacrifice the two sheep brought as peace offerings does not prevent sacrifice of the loaves. Consequently, if there are no sheep, the loaves are sacrificed by themselves. Concerning this **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where **the two loaves** are **brought by themselves, they should be waved.** They should then be left overnight so that **their form decays,** i.e., they become disqualified, **and they are** then **brought out to the place of burning,** like any disqualified offering.

The daf opens in the middle of *amud bet* of daf 46 with a *baraisa*: *shtei halechem haba'os bifnei atzman* — if the *shtei halechem* are brought by themselves, without the *shnei kevasim*. This proceeds within the position of Rabbi Akiva, who holds that the *shtei halechem* may be brought without the two lambs. But the question immediately arises: where is the *matir*, the permitting element that is offered on the *mizbeach* to render the bread permitted? The *baraisa* prescribes a peculiar middle course: *yunfu* — let them be waved — *v'tu'avar tzurasan* — let their form be degraded, meaning let them remain overnight so that they become invalid through *lina* — *v'yeitz'u l'veis hasereifa* — and then, now that they are *nosar*, they are burned.

### Rav Rav Ava Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי לַאֲכִילָה אָתְיָין – לֵיכְלִינְהוּ, אִי לִשְׂרֵיפָה אָתְיָין – לִשְׂרְפִינְהוּ לְאַלְתַּר, לְמָה לְהוּ עִיבּוּר צוּרָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Whichever way you** look at it, this is difficult: **If** the loaves are **brought** and waved in order **to be eaten, let** the priests **eat them** rather than burn them. **If** they are **brought** only **to be burned, let** the priests **burn them immediately. Why are they** left overnight so that they undergo **a decay of form,** i.e., become disqualified?

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this with a *manafshach* — make up your mind. *Ee l'achila asyan* — if according to Rabbi Akiva the *shtei halechem* brought alone can be eaten, since they require no *matir*, then *lichlu* — eat them. *Ee l'sereifa asyan* — if Rabbi Akiva holds they cannot be eaten because they have no *matir*, then *lishrifinu l'alter* — burn them immediately. What is this intermediate position of waiting overnight? *Lamma lahu ibbur tzurah* — why the delay until the next morning?

### Rabbah's Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: לְעוֹלָם לַאֲכִילָה אָתְיָין, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִזְדַּמְּנוּ לָהֶן כְּבָשִׂים לְשָׁנָה הַבָּאָה, וְיֹאמְרוּ: אֶשְׁתָּקַד לֹא אָכַלְנוּ לֶחֶם בְּלֹא כְּבָשִׂים, עַכְשָׁיו נָמֵי נֵיכוֹל. וְאִינְהוּ לָא יָדְעִי, דְּאֶשְׁתָּקַד לָא הֲווֹ כְּבָשִׂים, אִינְהוּ שָׁרְיָין נַפְשַׁיְיהוּ, הַשְׁתָּא דְּאִיכָּא כְּבָשִׂים – כְּבָשִׂים הוּא דְּשָׁרוּ לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: Actually,** the loaves are **brought** and waved in order **to be eaten.** Nevertheless, the Sages instituted a rabbinic **decree** that they not be eaten out of concern **lest sheep become available to** the nation **the following year, and they** might **say: Didn't we eat the loaves without** any accompanying **sheep last year [*eshtakad*]? Now too, we will eat** the loaves without sacrificing sheep. **And they will not know that** the reason they were permitted to eat the loaves without sacrificing sheep **last year** is that **there were no sheep,** and therefore the two loaves **permitted themselves** to be eaten, i.e., they could be eaten without the sacrifice of the sheep. But **now that there are sheep, it is** the sacrifice of **the sheep that permits** the loaves to be eaten. Since loaves brought without sheep are fit by Torah law and may not be eaten due to rabbinic decree, they may not be burned until they become disqualified by remaining overnight.

Rabbah resolves the difficulty: *l'olam l'achila asyan* — fundamentally, according to Rabbi Akiva, the *shtei halechem* when brought alone may in principle be eaten, even without a *matir* placed on the *mizbeach*. Why then do we not eat them? *Gezeirah shema yizdamnu lahen kevasim l'shana haba'ah* — it is a rabbinic enactment (*gezeirah*) lest in a future year lambs be available. If this year people eat the bread alone, what will they say next year when lambs are present? *V'yomru* — they will say: last year we ate the bread without lambs; let us do so again. *V'innu lo yadi d'ishtakad lo hava kevasim* — they will not realize that last year there were no lambs and the bread was its own *matir*, whereas this year, *kivan d'ika kevasim, kevasim hu d'sharei lei* — when lambs are present, even Rabbi Akiva agrees that the *zrikas hadam* of the lambs is required to permit the bread for eating.

Therefore we do not eat the bread, but we also cannot simply burn it immediately, since it is *kodesh kodashim* and is in principle edible. We must wait until it becomes invalid through *lina* — overnight — at which point it is *nosar* and may be burned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּתְנַן: אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: הֵעִיד בֶּן בּוּכְרִי בְּיַבְנֶה: כׇּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁשּׁוֹקֵל – אֵינוֹ חוֹטֵא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא כׇּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁאֵינוֹ שׁוֹקֵל – חוֹטֵא, אֶלָּא שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים דּוֹרְשִׁין מִקְּרָא זֶה לְעַצְמָן: ״וְכׇל מִנְחַת כֹּהֵן כָּלִיל תִּהְיֶה לֹא תֵאָכֵל״. הוֹאִיל וְעוֹמֶר וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים שֶׁלָּנוּ הֵן, הֵיאַךְ נֶאֱכָלִין?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: From where do I say** this, i.e., what is the source for my statement? It is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Shekalim* 1:4): **Rabbi Yehuda said** that **ben Bukhri testified** before the Sages **in Yavne: Any priest who contributes his** half-**shekel is not** considered **a sinner,** despite the fact that he is not obligated to do so. Rabbi Yehuda added that **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said to** ben Bukhri: **That is not** the case, **rather, any priest who does not contribute his** half-**shekel** is considered **a sinner,** as they are obligated in this mitzva like all other Jews. **But the priests** who do not contribute the half-shekel **interpret this** following **verse to their own** advantage in order to excuse themselves from the mitzva. The verse states: **"And every meal offering of the priest shall be wholly made to smoke; it shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 6:16). Those priests claim as follows: **Since the *omer*** offering **and the two loaves,** i.e., the public offering of two loaves from the new wheat, brought on the festival of *Shavuot*, **and the shewbread** placed on the Table in the Sanctuary each Shabbat, which are all meal offerings, **are ours,** then if we contribute half-shekels we will have partial ownership of these communal offerings, as they are purchased with the half-shekels. **How,** then, **can they be eaten?** They would then be regarded as priests' meal offerings, which must be wholly burned.

Rabbah further asks: *m'na amina la* — how do I know that according to Rabbi Akiva the *shtei halechem* brought alone may in principle be eaten? From the *Mishna* regarding Ben Buchri's testimony in *Yavneh*. The *Mishna* records a dispute about whether *kohanim* are obligated to contribute their half-*shekel* to the communal funds. Rabbi Yehudah cites the testimony of Ben Buchri that a *kohen* who contributes does not sin; Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai counters that one who does not contribute does sin. The *kohanim* sought to exempt themselves by invoking the verse *v'chol minchat kohen kalil tihiyeh lo tei'achel* — every *mincha* of a *kohen* must be entirely burned and may not be eaten — arguing: since the *shtei halechem* and the *lechem hapanim* would partly belong to us, they would have to be burned rather than eaten, and therefore we should not be required to contribute.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא בְּבָאוֹת עִם הַזֶּבַח – אַטּוּ תּוֹדָה וְלַחְמָהּ מִי לָא מְנַדְּבִי כֹּהֲנִים וְאָכְלִי לְהוּ? אֶלָּא לָאו בְּבָאוֹת בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן, וְקָתָנֵי: ״הֵיאַךְ הֵן נֶאֱכָלִין״, אַלְמָא לַאֲכִילָה אָתְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba discusses this mishna: **What are the circumstances** of **these two loaves? If we say** that the mishna is referring **to** a case **where** they are **brought with the animal offering,** i.e., the two sheep brought as peace offerings, why shouldn't the loaves be eaten? **Is that to say** that **priests cannot volunteer** to bring **a thanks offering and its loaves and eat them?** Just as the loaves that accompany a thanks offering may be eaten, even if brought by a priest, the same *halakha* should apply to the two loaves when they accompany sheep brought as peace offerings. **Rather,** is it **not** referring to a case **where** the two loaves are **brought by themselves,** and the mishna **teaches** that the priests claimed: **How** can **they be eaten? Apparently,** in principle the loaves **come to be eaten,** but due to rabbinic decree they are not eaten and are left overnight until their form decays.

The *Gemara* asks: which *shtei halechem* are they discussing? If it is the *shtei halechem* that comes with the *shnei kevasim*, their argument collapses — after all, *toda* bread is not burned even when a *kohen* brings a *toda*, because the Torah does not call that bread a *mincha*. The *kohanim* could not use the *shtei halechem* that comes with the lambs as an argument for burning, since everyone already knows that such bread is eaten. Therefore they must be discussing *b'vo'ot bifnei atzman* — the *shtei halechem* brought alone, according to Rabbi Akiva. And the *baraisa* asks: *heich ne'echelu* — how would they be eaten? The implicit assumption is that even when brought alone, they are eaten. Thus Rabbah has a proof that according to Rabbi Akiva the *shtei halechem* brought alone are fundamentally *l'achila*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם בְּבָאוֹת עִם הַזֶּבַח, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ מִתּוֹדָה וְלַחְמָהּ – לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה לָא אִיקְּרוּ ״מִנְחָה״, שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם אִיקְּרוּ ״מִנְחָה״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּהַקְרִיבְכֶם מִנְחָה חֲדָשָׁה לַה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabba in response: **Actually,** the mishna can be interpreted as referring **to** loaves **brought with the animal offering,** and therefore it does not prove that when the two loaves are brought by themselves they may be eaten. **And as for that which** is **difficult for you** based **on** the case of **a thanks offering and its loaves,** the resolution is that the **loaves of a thanks offering are not called a meal offering,** and therefore even when a priest brings a thanks offering, the loaves may be eaten. By contrast, **the two loaves** of *Shavuot* **are called a meal offering, as it is stated** with regard to the two loaves: "Also in the day of the first fruits, **when you bring a new meal offering to the Lord"** (Numbers 28:26). Therefore, the priests held that if they would donate half-shekels, the two loaves would not be permitted to be eaten.

Abaye, however, pushes back: even if the *kohanim* were arguing about the *shtei halechem* that comes with the lambs, their argument still holds, because the bread of *toda* is not called a *mincha*, whereas the *shtei halechem* is explicitly called *mincha* in the verse *b'hakrivchem mincha chadasha l'Hashem*. Thus the proof is deflected.

### Rav Yosef Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לִשְׂרֵיפָה אָתְיָין, וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּלָא שָׂרְפִינַן – לְפִי שֶׁאֵין שׂוֹרְפִין קָדָשִׁים בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said** a different response to Rabba's proof: **Actually,** when the two loaves of *Shavuot* are brought by themselves **they come to be burned,** i.e., they may not be eaten. **And this is the reason that we do not burn them** until the following day: It is **because one may not burn consecrated** items **on a Festival.**

Rav Yosef offers an entirely different account. *L'olam l'sereifa asyan* — according to Rabbi Akiva, the *shtei halechem* when brought alone must be burned. So why not burn them immediately? *Lefi she-ein sorfin kodashim beyom tov* — because the *shtei halechem* is brought on *Shavuos*, and one may not burn *kodashim* on *Yom Tov*. The waiting, then, is not a matter of wanting to allow for *lina* and *nosar*, but simply a consequence of the prohibition against burning *kodashim* on *Yom Tov*.

### Abaye Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם – לָאו מִצְוָתָן בְּכָךְ, הָכָא – דְּמִצְוָתָן בְּכָךְ, לִישְׂרְפִינְהוּ, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַפַּר וְשָׂעִיר שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **Is** the burning of the two loaves **comparable** to the burning of other consecrated items, such that the loaves may not be burned right away for this reason? **There,** in the case of other consecrated items, **this is not their mitzva,** i.e., they are supposed to be eaten, but if they become disqualified they must be burned. Conversely, **here,** in the case of the two loaves of *Shavuot* that are brought by themselves, **where this is their mitzva,** i.e., they are supposed to be burned, **let** the priests **burn them** on the Festival, **just as is** the *halakha* in the case **of the bull and the goat of Yom Kippur,** which are burned on Yom Kippur despite the fact that it is a Festival.

Abaye challenges Rav Yosef directly. *Mi dami?* — You cannot compare the two cases. The general rule against burning *kodashim* on *Yom Tov* applies when the burning is incidental — when the *halacha* is that the item should be eaten but something went wrong (e.g., it became *tamei*) and therefore it must be burned after *Yom Tov*. But here, if Rabbi Akiva's position is that the *mitzvah* of the day is precisely to burn these loaves, then that burning is itself the *mitzvah* and we would perform it on *Yom Tov*, just as we burn the *par v'sa'ir* of *Yom Kippur* even when it falls on *Shabbos*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִזְדַּמְּנוּ לָהֶם כְּבָשִׂים לְאַחַר מִכָּאן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: תִּינַח כׇּל זְמַן הַקְרָבָתָם, לְבָתַר הָכִי לִשְׂרְפִינְהוּ! מַאי ״תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתָן״ נָמֵי דְּקָתָנֵי? – צוּרַת הַקְרָבָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef said:** The reason the loaves are left overnight is due to a rabbinic **decree** not to burn them immediately, **lest sheep become available to** the nation **afterward,** i.e., later in the day, in which case the loaves could be waved with them and then eaten. **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: That **works out well** for the **entire time** period when **they** may be **sacrificed,** i.e., until the afternoon daily offering is sacrificed. But **after that, let them burn** the loaves immediately and not wait until the next day. Rav Yosef replied: **What** is the meaning of the phrase in the *baraita* **that teaches** that the loaves must be left until **their form decays?** It means that they must be left until **the form of their sacrifice** has passed, i.e., until after the time when the sheep could be sacrificed.

Rav Yosef therefore reformulates his *gezeirah*: the reason we do not burn them immediately is not the prohibition of burning *kodashim* on *Yom Tov*, but rather *gezeirah shema yizdamnu lahem kevasim le-achar mikan* — lest lambs be found later that same day. If one burns the *shtei halechem* immediately and then lambs are located before the end of the day, the bread will have been destroyed needlessly.

Abaye presses further: *teinach zman hakrava* — granted, during the time when the lambs could still be brought one should not burn the bread. But *le-vasar hakhi lisreifinhu* — after nightfall, when lambs can no longer be brought, why not burn them then? Why wait until morning? The *Gemara* resolves this by reinterpreting the phrase *tu'avar tzurasan*. Rather than meaning the form of the loaves degrades overnight, it means *tu'avar tzuras hakravasan* — the period during which they could be offered passes, i.e., nightfall. Once night falls and the lambs can no longer be brought, burn them. So the waiting is not until the next morning but only until the end of the day.

### Rava's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לַאֲכִילָה אָתְיָין, וּגְזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבָּה, וְלָאו מִטַּעְמֵיהּ, אֶלָּא מִקְּרָא. וְאָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״מִמּוֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם תָּבִיאּוּ לֶחֶם תְּנוּפָה וְגוֹ׳ בִּכּוּרִים לַה׳״, מָה בִּכּוּרִים בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן, אַף שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן, וּמִינַּהּ: מָה בִּכּוּרִים לַאֲכִילָה, אַף שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם נָמֵי לַאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that there is a different response to Rabba's proof: When the two loaves of *Shavuot* are brought by themselves, by Torah law **they come to be eaten, but** due to rabbinic **decree** they are not eaten and are left overnight until they are disqualified. The reason for the decree is **due to** that **which Rabba** said, i.e., due to the concern that the following year sheep will be available and nevertheless the nation will bring the two loaves without sheep. **But** the proof that by Torah law the loaves may be eaten is **not from** Rabba's line of **reasoning,** i.e., from the mishna in *Shekalim*; **rather,** it is **from a verse.** **And Rava said** by way of explanation: **From where do I state** this *halakha*? From the fact **that it is written** with regard to the two loaves: **"You shall bring out of your dwellings** two **loaves of waving** of two tenth parts of an ephah; they shall be of fine flour, they shall be baked with leaven, **for first fruits to the Lord"** (Leviticus 23:17). **Just as first fruits** are brought **by themselves,** without an accompanying animal offering, **so too the two loaves** are brought **by themselves** when there are no sheep available. **And** learn **from this** comparison to first fruits that **just as first fruits** are brought **to be eaten, so too the two loaves** are **also** brought **to be eaten,** even in the absence of the sheep brought as peace offerings.

Rava agrees with Rabbah that the *shtei halechem* brought alone are fundamentally *l'achila* and that we do not eat them only because of the same *gezeirah* Rabbah articulated — lest people confuse the practice with the case when lambs are present and think they can eat the bread without *zrikas hadam*. *U-velav mi-tamei* — but Rava does not accept Rabbah's proof. He has a stronger scriptural basis.

### Bikurim Comparison

Rava's proof is from the verse itself: *moshvoseichem tavi'u lechem tenufa shtayim shnei esronim... bikurim l'Hashem* — you shall bring a *lechem tenufa* as *bikurim* to God. Because the *shtei halechem* is called *bikurim*, Rava derives: just as *bikurim* come by themselves without any animal sacrifice, so too the bread may come by itself. And just as *bikurim* are eaten, so too the *shtei halechem* is fundamentally designated for eating. This is a clean and compelling proof. The reason we do not eat it in practice remains the *gezeirah* — so that no one will confuse the practice of eating bread alone with eating bread when lambs are present without the requisite *zrikas hadam*.

## Shechita and Zerika

### Rabbi vs. Rabbi Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כִּבְשֵׂי עֲצֶרֶת אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶת הַלֶּחֶם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁחִיטָה. כֵּיצַד? שְׁחָטָן לִשְׁמָן, וְזָרַק דָּמָן לִשְׁמָן – קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם. שְׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן, וְזָרַק דָּמָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – לֹא קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם. שְׁחָטָן לִשְׁמָן, וְזָרַק דָּמָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – הַלֶּחֶם קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁחוֹט לִשְׁמָן וְיִזְרוֹק דָּמָן לִשְׁמָן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites further discussion of the two sheep and the two loaves of *Shavuot*: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The** two **sheep** of ***Shavuot* consecrate the** two **loaves** that accompany them **only by** means of their **slaughter. How so?** If **one slaughtered** the sheep **for their** own **sake,** as the peace offerings that are supposed to be sacrificed on *Shavuot*, **and** then the priest **sprinkled their blood** on the altar **for their** own **sake,** then **the loaves are consecrated.** But if **one slaughtered them not for their** own **sake, and** the priest **sprinkled their blood not for their** own **sake, the loaves are not consecrated.** If **one slaughtered them for their** own **sake and he sprinkled their blood not for their** own **sake, the loaves are** partially **consecrated, but they are not** fully **consecrated.** This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says:** The loaves are **never consecrated** at all **until one slaughters** the offerings **for their** own **sake and sprinkles their blood for their** own **sake.**

The *Gemara* now turns to a related but distinct question: when exactly does the *shtei halechem* acquire its *kedushah*? We noted in passing when discussing the *toda* that the *shechita* of the accompanying animal sanctifies the bread — not merely connecting it to the animal through *hukkeku ze la-ze*, but elevating it from ordinary Temple ownership (*kedushas damim*) to the higher sanctity of a *korban* proper (*kedushas haguf*), with all attendant consequences: it cannot be redeemed, it becomes invalid if taken outside *Yerushalayim*, and so on. The *Gemara* now focuses this question on the *shtei halechem* of *Shavuos*.

A *baraisa* is cited: *kivsei atzeres ein mekadeshim es halechem ela bishchita* — the lambs of *Atzeres* sanctify the bread only through *shechita*. The *baraisa* then spells out the cases. If the lambs were slaughtered *lishmo* — with proper intention — and the blood was sprinkled *lishmo*, the bread is sanctified. If both were done *shelo lishmo*, the bread is not sanctified. The mixed cases remain open. In the case of *shachatan lishmo ve-zarak daman shelo lishmo* — where the *shechita* was proper but the *zerika* was not — the ruling is *kadosh v'eino kadosh* — it is sanctified yet not sanctified. This is the position of Rabbi. Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *le-olam eino kadosh ad sheyishchot lishmo ve-yizrok daman lishmo* — there is no *kedushah* at all until both a valid *shechita* and a valid *zerika* have occurred.

### Rabbi's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? דִּכְתִיב ״וְאֶת הָאַיִל יַעֲשֶׂה זֶבַח שְׁלָמִים לַה׳ עַל סַל הַמַּצּוֹת״, לְמֵימְרָא דִּשְׁחִיטָה מְקַדְּשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who holds that the slaughtering of the sheep partially consecrates the loaves even without the sprinkling of the blood? The Gemara answers: It is based on the fact **that it is written** concerning the ram brought by the nazirite when he completes his naziriteship: **"And he shall offer the ram for a sacrifice [*zevaḥ*] of peace offerings to the Lord, with the basket of unleavened bread"** (Numbers 6:17). Since the verse uses the word *zevaḥ*, which also means slaughter, and the verse then makes reference to the loaves, **that is to say that** it is specifically the **slaughter** that **consecrates** the loaves that accompany the offering. Similarly, the slaughter of the sheep brought as peace offerings on *Shavuot* consecrates the accompanying two loaves, as the *halakha* of the loaves of *Shavuot* is derived from that of the loaves of the nazirite.

What is the source for Rabbi's position? The verse regarding the *Nazir* states: *ve-es ha-ayil ya'aseh zevach shelamim l'Hashem al sal hamatzos* — he shall make the ram a *zevach shelamim* to God, together with the basket of *matzos*. Rabbi reads *ya'aseh zevach* as: it becomes *l'Hashem* through the act of *zevicha* — slaughter — and at that same moment the *matzos* are drawn along with it. Thus the *shechita* alone sanctifies the bread.

### R. Elazar Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: ״יַעֲשֶׂה״ – עַד שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה כָּל עֲשִׂיּוֹתָיו. וְרַבִּי נָמֵי הָכְתִיב ״יַעֲשֶׂה״? אִי כְּתִיב ״זֶבַח יַעֲשֶׂה״ – כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ, הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב ״יַעֲשֶׂה זֶבַח״ – בְּמָה יַעֲשֶׂה? בִּזְבִיחָה. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הָכְתִיב ״זֶבַח״? הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים שֶׁצָּרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא לֶחֶם בִּשְׁעַת שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what is the reasoning of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that the slaughtering and sprinkling of the blood together consecrate the loaves? The Gemara answers: He derives it from the term: **"He shall offer,"** which he understands to mean that the loaves are not consecrated **until** the priest **performs all of the actions** included in the sacrificial rites of that offering, including the sprinkling of the blood. The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **also, isn't it written: "He shall offer,"** which indicates that the loaves are consecrated only once the blood has been sprinkled on the altar? The Gemara answers: **If** it were **written: A sacrifice [*zevaḥ*] he shall offer,** it would be **as you are saying** that he should slaughter it and then perform a further action, i.e., sprinkling the blood, in order to consecrate the loaves. **Now that it is written: "He shall offer** the ram for **a sacrifice [*zevaḥ*],"** it should be understood as: **By what** means **should he offer** the ram in order to consecrate the loaves? **By** means of **slaughtering [*zeviḥa*].** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, isn't it written: "A sacrifice [*zevaḥ*],"** indicating that slaughtering alone consecrates the loaves? The Gemara answers: **He requires that** expression **for** that **which Rabbi Yoḥanan** says, **as Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **everyone,** including Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **concedes that the loaves must be** in existence **at the time of the slaughter.**

Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon agrees the verse is the source but reads it differently: *ya'aseh* means you must do *kol asiyato* — all the acts associated with making it a *shelamim*, including both *shechita* and *zerika*. Only then is the bread sanctified.

And how does Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon handle the word *zevach* that Rabbi uses to emphasize *shechita*? He holds that word is needed for a separate *halacha*: the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan that *hakol modim shetzarich sheyei lechem b'sha'at shechita* — everyone agrees that the bread must be present at the time of *shechita*. Even though for Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon the operative moment of sanctification is the *zerika*, the *shechita* is still a critical juncture at which the bread must be in existence. The word *zevach* in the verse teaches this requirement.

### Kadosh V'Eino

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ״? אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ גָּמוּר. רָבָא אָמַר: קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ נִיתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **What** is meant by Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's statement in the *baraita* that if one slaughtered the sheep for their own sake and sprinkled their blood not for their own sake, then the loaves are partially **consecrated, but they are not** fully **consecrated? Abaye says:** The loaves are **consecrated** by means of the slaughtering, **but** their consecration is **not complete. Rava says:** The loaves are fully **consecrated** by means of the slaughtering, **but** they are **not** thereby **permitted** to be eaten.

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai kadosh v'eino kadosh* — what exactly is this intermediate state according to Rabbi? Abaye says: *kadosh v'eino gamur* — it is sanctified but not fully so. Rava says: *kadosh v'eino nitar* — it is fully sanctified but not yet permitted for eating. Rava's reading is conceptually clean: the *shechita* confers complete *kedushas haguf* upon the bread. You cannot redeem it, and it becomes invalid if taken outside. What the *zerika* adds is purely the *matir* — the permitting element that allows it to be eaten. Until the *zerika*, the bread has full *kedushas haguf* but cannot yet be eaten. Abaye's reading places the bread at an earlier, incomplete stage of *kedushah* — not yet fully *kedushas haguf* even after *shechita*.

### Nafka Mina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ לְמִיתְפַּס פִּדְיוֹנוֹ: לְאַבָּיֵי – לָא תָּפֵיס פִּדְיוֹנוֹ, לְרָבָא – תָּפֵיס פִּדְיוֹנוֹ. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרָבָא, הַיְינוּ דְּאִיכָּא בֵּין רַבִּי לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. אֶלָּא לְאַבָּיֵי, מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין רַבִּי לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אִיכָּא [בֵּינַיְיהוּ] לְאִיפְּסוֹלֵי בְּיוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between them?** Everyone concedes that the loaves may not be eaten as a result of this slaughtering. The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them** is the ability **to transfer** sanctity to **their redemption** money. **According to Abaye,** who holds that the loaves are not completely consecrated, they **do not transfer** sanctity to **their redemption** money if one tries to redeem them for money. **According to Rava,** who holds that the loaves are completely consecrated, they **transfer** sanctity to **their redemption** money. The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to Rava,** who holds that according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi the loaves are completely sanctified, **that is** the difference **between** the opinions of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi and **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon.** But **according to Abaye, what** difference **is there between** the opinions of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi and **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon?** The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them** is **with regard to** whether the loaves are **rendered unfit by** means of **leaving** the Temple courtyard after the slaughtering of the offering. According to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, the loaves are rendered unfit if they leave the courtyard. According to Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that the loaves are not consecrated, they are not rendered unfit if they leave the courtyard.

What is the practical difference between Abaye and Rava? *Ika beinaihu l'mitpas pidyono* — whether the bread can be redeemed. Note that Rashi ultimately rejects the reading of this *sugya* as it appears in our text and establishes the correct reading as the inverse: according to Abaye, since the *kedushah* is not yet complete after *shechita* alone, *pidyon* — redemption — remains possible. According to Rava, since the *shechita* confers complete *kedushas haguf*, redemption is no longer possible after *shechita*.

A further practical difference is identified: *l'ipsulei b'yotzei* — whether the bread becomes invalid if taken outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. According to Abaye, the *shechita* confers enough of a *kedushah* that taking the bread outside invalidates it, even though it does not yet reach the level that bars redemption. According to Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon, the bread has no *kedushas haguf* whatsoever until *zerika* — it can be redeemed and is not rendered invalid by *yotzei*.

### Abaye-Rava Debate

To summarize the three-way positions: according to Rava's reading of Rabbi, *shechita* alone creates complete *kedushas haguf* — no *pidyon* and *pasul* by *yotzei*; according to Abaye's reading of Rabbi, *shechita* creates partial *kedushah* — not enough to bar *pidyon* but enough that *yotzei* invalidates it; according to Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon, there is no *kedushas haguf* whatsoever until both *shechita* and *zerika* — *pidyon* is possible and *yotzei* does not invalidate. The *Gemara* then asks: given that for Abaye the bread after *shechita* can still be redeemed, in what way does Rabbi actually differ from Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon? The answer: *l'ipsulei b'yotzei* — by *yotzei*. For Rabbi, even on Abaye's reading, *shechita* confers enough of a *kedushah* that taking the bread outside the *Beis Hamikdash* does invalidate it, which is not the case according to Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon.

## Mixed Shechita-Zerika

### Shechita Zerika Mix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק מֵרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: כִּבְשֵׂי עֲצֶרֶת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן לִשְׁמָן, וְזָרַק דָּמָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – אוֹתוֹ הַלֶּחֶם מַהוּ בַּאֲכִילָה?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues the discussion of the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, concerning the consecration of the two loaves by means of the slaughter and sprinkling of the blood of the sheep of *Shavuot*. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak raised a dilemma before Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba:** In the case of a communal peace offering of two **sheep** that accompany the two loaves on ***Shavuot* that one slaughtered for their sake but** the priest **sprinkled their blood not for their sake,** concerning **those** accompanying **loaves, what is** the *halakha* **with regard to eating** them?

The *Gemara* now turns to the mixed cases — specifically, the scenario of *shachatan lishmo ve-zarak daman shelo lishmo*: *shechita* performed with proper intention and *zerika* performed without proper intention. Can the bread be eaten?

### R. Elazar Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן? אִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – הָאָמַר זְרִיקָה הִיא דִּמְקַדְּשָׁא! אִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי – בֵּין לְאַבָּיֵי בֵּין לְרָבָא, קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ נִיתָּר הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion was this dilemma raised? **If** it was raised **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, doesn't he say** that **it is** the **sprinkling** of the blood **that consecrates the loaves?** Consequently, if the blood was not properly sprinkled, it is clear that the loaves are unfit and may not be eaten. And **if** it was raised **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **both according to** the opinion **of Abaye and according to** that of **Rava,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that the loaves **are consecrated but are not permitted** to be eaten.

According to Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon, the answer is straightforward. Since for him the *zerika* is entirely responsible for sanctifying the bread — *hagmar zerika hi d'makadsha* — and this *zerika* was *shelo lishmo* and thus invalid, the bread never attained *kedushah*. It can be redeemed and eaten only after *pidyon*; it certainly cannot be eaten directly as a *korban*.

### Rabbi's Rule

According to Rabbi, the *shechita* was valid and therefore conferred *kedushas haguf* upon the bread. The *zerika*, however — even though it was *shelo lishmo* — remains the *matir*, the permitting element. Between Abaye and Rava both agree: *kadosh v'eino nitar* — it is sanctified but not permitted for eating. The *shechita* having been valid, the bread has its *kedushah*, but since the *zerika* was not valid, there is no *matir* and the bread cannot be eaten.

## Piggul Status

### Bread Yotzei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אַלִּיבָּא דְּהַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתָנֵי אֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם שֶׁיָּצְאוּ בֵּין שְׁחִיטָה לִזְרִיקָה, וְזָרַק דָּמָן שֶׁל כְּבָשִׂים חוּץ לִזְמַנָּן – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין בַּלֶּחֶם מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ בַּלֶּחֶם מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rather,** one must say that the question was asked **in accordance with** the opinion **of this** following ***tanna*. As the father of Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba teaches** in a *baraita*: In a case where **the two loaves left** the Temple courtyard **between** the **slaughtering** of the offering **and** the **sprinkling** of its blood, **and** then the priest **sprinkled the blood of** the **sheep** with the intent that their meat would be eaten **beyond their** designated **time,** the sheep are rendered *piggul*. With regard to the loaves, **Rabbi Eliezer says: The loaves do not** become prohibited **due to** the prohibition of ***piggul*. Rabbi Akiva says: The loaves do** become prohibited **due to** the prohibition of ***piggul*.**

The *Gemara* introduces a more complex scenario. A *tanna* — the father of Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba — teaches: *shtei halechem sheyatzu bein shechita l'zerika* — the bread of the *shtei halechem* was taken out of the *Beis Hamikdash* between *shechita* and *zerika*. Assuming we follow Rabbi, the *shechita* already conferred *kedushas haguf*, and so taking the bread outside rendered it *pasul* through *yotzei*. Then *zerika* of the blood of the lambs was performed with the intention of eating the offering *chutz l'zmano* — beyond its proper time — making it a *zerika* of *piggul*. Does the *piggul* status attach also to the bread, which is already invalid by virtue of *yotzei*?

### R. Eliezer Position

Rabbi Eliezer holds: *ein balechem mishum piggul* — the bread does not receive *piggul* status. An already-invalid item cannot be further defined by a *zerika* of *piggul*.

### R. Akiva Position

Rabbi Akiva holds: *yesh balechem mishum piggul* — the bread does receive *piggul* status, even though it was already *pasul* through *yotzei*.

### Rav Sheshet Explain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי כְּרַבִּי סְבִירָא לְהוּ, דְּאָמַר: שְׁחִיטָה מְקַדְּשָׁא. מִיהוּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר אֵין זְרִיקָה מוֹעֶלֶת לַיּוֹצֵא, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר זְרִיקָה מוֹעֶלֶת לַיּוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: These *tanna'im*,** Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva, both **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said:** The **slaughtering consecrates** the loaves by itself. Consequently, if the loaves are taken out of the Temple courtyard after the sheep are slaughtered, the loaves become disqualified. **But** they disagree as to the following: **Rabbi Eliezer** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as** Rabbi Eliezer **says** that **sprinkling** the blood **is not effective with regard to** offerings that **left** the Temple courtyard. Since the loaves left the courtyard before the sprinkling of the blood, the intent of the priest while sprinkling the blood that the offering be eaten outside of its designated time does not render the loaves *piggul*. **And Rabbi Akiva** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as Rabbi Akiva says** that **sprinkling** the blood **is effective with regard to** offerings that **left** the Temple courtyard. Therefore, the intent of the priest while sprinkling the blood that the offering be eaten outside of its designated time renders the loaves *piggul*, even though they left the courtyard.

Rav Sheshet explains that both these *tanna'im* hold like Rabbi — that *shechita* sanctifies the bread. Their debate is the well-known dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva about whether *zerika* has any effect on something already *pasul*: *Rabbi Eliezer l'ta'ameih d'amar ein zerika mo'eles l'yotzei* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that *zerika* does not operate on something that is *yotzei*. *Rabbi Akiva l'ta'ameih d'amar zerika mo'eles l'yotzei* — Rabbi Akiva holds that *zerika* does operate on *yotzei*, and therefore a *zerika* of *piggul* can impose *piggul* status even on bread that has already been invalidated by *yotzei*.

### Imurim Meila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: אֵימוּרֵי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים שֶׁיָּצְאוּ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר וְטָמֵא. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וְחַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר וְטָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Me'ila* 6b): In the case of **sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity that left** the Temple courtyard **before the sprinkling of the blood, Rabbi Eliezer says:** The sprinkling of the blood is completely ineffective with regard to these portions, and therefore one is **not** liable for **misusing them. And** if **one** eats **them, he is not liable due to** the prohibitions of ***piggul*, *notar*, or** of partaking of sacrificial meat while one is **ritually impure. Rabbi Akiva says:** The sprinkling is effective and therefore, one is liable for **misusing them. And** if **one** eats **them he is liable due to** the prohibitions of ***piggul*, *notar*, or** of partaking of sacrificial meat while one is **ritually impure.**

The *Gemara* brings a parallel from a *mishna* at the top of 47b. The *imurim* — the innards designated for the *mizbeach* — of *kodashim kalim*: there is no *meila* by *kodashim kalim* in general, since they are considered to belong to the owners and are eaten outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. The only exception is the *imurim*, which go on the *mizbeach* and become fully God's — but only once the *zerika* has rendered them fit for the *mizbeach*. If the *imurim* were taken outside the *Beis Hamikdash* before *zerika*, becoming *pasul* through *yotzei*, and then *zerika* was performed — does the *zerika* raise them to the status where *meila* applies? And similarly, if that *zerika* was a *zerika* of *piggul*, do the *imurim* receive *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* status?

Rabbi Eliezer says: *ein mo'alin bahen* — no *meila* applies, and they are not subject to *piggul*, *nosar*, or *tamei*, because they are already *pasul*. Rabbi Akiva says: *mo'alin bahen* — *meila* does apply, and they are subject to *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei*. This is the same structural debate.

### Rav Papa Qestion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: וּמִמַּאי דְּכִי אִיתַנְהוּ אַבָּרַאי פְּלִיגִי? דִּילְמָא בִּדְאִיתַנְהוּ אַבָּרַאי, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּאֵין זְרִיקָה מוֹעֶלֶת לַיּוֹצֵא, וּבַהֲדַר עַיְּילִינְהוּ פְּלִיגִי, דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי, דְּאָמַר: שְׁחִיטָה מְקַדְּשָׁא, וְאִיפְּסִלוּ לְהוּ בְּיוֹצֵא. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: שְׁחִיטָה לָא מְקַדְּשָׁא, וְלָא מִיפַּסְלִי בְּיוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this** understanding of the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Eliezer. **From where** do we know that **they disagree** in a case **where** the loaves **are outside** the courtyard at the time of the sprinkling? **Perhaps in** a case **where** the loaves **are** still **outside** the courtyard **everyone agrees that sprinkling** the blood **is not effective with regard to** offerings that **left** the Temple courtyard, **and they disagree in** a case **where** the loaves left the courtyard and **one then brought them** back into the courtyard before the sprinkling. **As Rabbi Eliezer holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said:** The **slaughter** of the sheep **consecrates** the loaves, **and** therefore the loaves **became disqualified by leaving** the courtyard after the sheep were slaughtered. Consequently, even if they were brought back into the courtyard before the sprinkling of the blood they cannot become *piggul* because they have already been disqualified for a different reason. **And Rabbi Akiva** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, who said:** The **slaughter** of the sheep **does not consecrate** the loaves at all before the sprinkling of the blood. Since the loaves were not yet consecrated, **they do not become disqualified by leaving** the Temple courtyard.

Rav Papa challenges whether the dispute of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva in our context necessarily concerns the case of bread that is *yotzei* outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. Perhaps, he suggests, when it comes to *yotzei* bread everyone would agree that the *zerika* of *piggul* does not apply — the *imurim* and the bread are simply different. Perhaps their debate concerns a different configuration entirely: *de'ailu pligi* — when the bread went out and then came back in. In that case, the question is whether the bread ever became *pasul* at all, which depends on the underlying dispute of Rabbi and Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Eliezer in the *piggul* debate holds like Rabbi — the *shechita* creates *kedushas haguf*, and therefore taking it outside creates *yotzei*, and bringing it back in does not undo the invalidation. So the bread is *pasul* and the *zerika* of *piggul* cannot reach it. Rabbi Akiva holds like Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon — the *shechita* does not create *kedushas haguf*, so taking it outside before *zerika* does not invalidate it, and the bread is perfectly valid when it returns; the *zerika* of *piggul* therefore can define it as *piggul*.

### Gemara Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא כְּרַבִּי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: שְׁחִיטָה מְקַדְּשָׁא לְהוּ, הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּקָדְשִׁי לְהוּ בִּשְׁחִיטָה, וְאָתְיָא זְרִיקָה קָבְעָה לְהוּ בְּפִיגּוּל. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ, כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: זְבִיחָה לָא מְקַדְּשָׁא, זְרִיקַת פִּיגּוּל מִי מְקַדְּשָׁא? וְהָאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל, אָמַר רַב: זְרִיקַת פִּיגּוּל אֵינָהּ מְבִיאָה לִידֵי מְעִילָה, וְאֵינָהּ מוֹצִיאָה מִידֵי מְעִילָה. אֵינָהּ מְבִיאָה לִידֵי מְעִילָה – בְּאֵימוּרֵי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים. וְאֵינָהּ מוֹצִיאָה מִידֵי מְעִילָה – בִּבְשַׂר קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים. לָאו אִיתּוֹתַב דְּרַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is this** interpretation of the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Eliezer? It does not fit what they say. **Granted, if you say** that **Rabbi Akiva holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said:** The **slaughter** of the sheep **consecrates** the loaves, **that is what Rabbi Akiva** means when he **says that** the loaves **are consecrated by** the **slaughter** of the sheep **and** then the **sprinkling** that was done with the intent to consume the offering after its appointed time **comes and renders** the loaves ***piggul*. But if you say** that Rabbi Akiva holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, who said:** The **slaughter** of the sheep **does not consecrate** the loaves without the sprinkling of the blood, **does sprinkling** with an intent that renders the sheep ***piggul*** actually **consecrate** the loaves? **But doesn't Rav Giddel say** that **Rav says: Sprinkling** with an intent that renders an offering ***piggul* does not cause** items **to** become subject to the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated property, **and it does not remove** items **from** being subject to the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated property? Rav explains: The *halakha* that it **does not cause** items **to** become subject to the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated property applies **with regard to sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity.** The prohibition against misusing consecrated property applies to: "The sacred items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15). Consequently, it does not apply to offerings of lesser sanctity, as the meat is the property of those who brought the offering, and the sacrificial portions are disqualified by the sprinkling performed with improper intent. **And** the *halakha* that **it does not remove** items **from** being subject to the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated property applies **with regard to meat of offerings of the most sacred order,** such as a sin offering, a guilt offering, or a communal peace offering. Since the sprinkling of the blood was not valid, the meat, which would have become permitted for the priests to eat, retains the status of "the sacred items of the Lord," and the prohibition against misuse of consecrated property still applies. The Gemara rejects this explanation: **Wasn't that which Rav Giddel** says that **Rav says conclusively refuted?** Consequently, one cannot ask a question based on this statement.

The *Gemara* rejects this reinterpretation. If Rabbi Akiva holds like Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon that the *shechita* does not sanctify the bread, then for the *zerika* of *piggul* to make the bread *piggul*, the *zerika* would have to do two things simultaneously: first confer *kedushas haguf* upon the bread (since there is none yet), and then impose *piggul* status. But this cannot work. The principle established by Rav Gidel in the name of Rav is: *zerikas piggul einah mevia lidei meila ve-einah motzia midei meila* — a *zerika* of *piggul* does not change an item's *meila* status. It cannot elevate something to *meila* status, nor can it remove something from *meila* status, precisely because it is an invalid *zerika* and cannot alter levels of *kedushah*. If so, a *zerika* of *piggul* certainly cannot confer initial *kedushas haguf* on the bread. Rabbi Akiva's position, then, must be that the bread already had *kedushas haguf* — which means he holds like Rabbi, not like Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon. And the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva is indeed the classic dispute of whether *zerika mo'eles l'yotzei*.

The *Gemara* notes that the proof from Rav Gidel may itself be subject to challenge elsewhere, but that complication does not unsettle the main analysis here. The initial question posed — whether a *zerika shelo lishmo* could somehow render the bread of *shtei halechem* permissible by analogy to *piggul* affecting an already-*pasul* item — is clearly inapt: the cases are fundamentally different in kind. The *piggul* debate concerns imposing an additional negative status on something already *pasul*; one cannot reason from that to a *zerika shelo lishmo* positively permitting something to be eaten.

## Lost Bread Case

### Lost Bread Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מֵרַבִּי זֵירָא: כִּבְשֵׂי עֲצֶרֶת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן לִשְׁמָן, וְאָבַד הַלֶּחֶם – מַהוּ שֶׁיִּזְרוֹק דָּמָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן לְהַתִּיר בָּשָׂר בַּאֲכִילָה?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another dilemma concerning the sheep and loaves of *Shavuot*. **Rabbi Yirmeya raised a dilemma before Rabbi Zeira:** In a case of the two **sheep of *Shavuot* that one slaughtered for their own sake,** thereby establishing a bond between the sheep and the loaves, **and the loaves were** then **lost,** if the blood of the sheep would be sprinkled for their sake, the meat would not be permitted to be eaten because the loaves were lost. That said, **what is** the *halakha* **with regard to whether the priest may sprinkle their blood not for their own sake** but rather for the sake of a peace offering **in order to permit** the **meat** of the sheep **to be eaten?**

A new question is posed: *kivsei Atzeres sheshachtan lishmon ve'avad halechem* — the lambs of *Atzeres* were slaughtered *lishmo* and then the bread was lost. Since the bread and the lambs are *me'akev zeh es zeh* — indispensable to one another — the *korban* cannot proceed as the *shnei kevasim* of *Shavuos*. But can one perform *zerika* of the blood *shelo lishmo* in order to reclassify the lambs as ordinary *shelamim* and thereby permit the meat to be eaten? This is reminiscent of the earlier discussion about the *toda* where doing *zerika shelo lishmo* after the bread was lost was shown to be valid.

### Kasher Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יֵשׁ לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ כָּשֵׁר לִשְׁמוֹ, וְכָשֵׁר שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ?! וְלָא?! וַהֲרֵי פֶּסַח קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת, דְּאֵינוֹ כָּשֵׁר לִשְׁמוֹ, וְכָשֵׁר שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira **said to** Rabbi Yirmeya: **Do you have anything that is not fit** if the sacrificial rites are performed **for its own sake, and** yet it is **fit** if the sacrificial rites are performed **not for its own sake?** This is certainly not a logical option. Rabbi Yirmeya responded: **And** is there **no** precedent for this? **But there is** the **Paschal offering before midday** on the fourteenth of Nisan, **which is not fit** if it is slaughtered **for its own sake,** as it is before the proper time for the Paschal offering, **and** yet it is **fit** if it is slaughtered **not for its own sake** but rather for the sake of a peace offering.

The question carries a hidden conceptual difficulty: *yesh lach davar she'eino kasher lishmo vekasher shelo lishmo?* — do you ever have something that is invalid if done for its designated purpose yet valid if done for a different purpose? The *zerika lishmo* here would be invalid because the bread is gone and the *korban* is defective as *kivsei Atzeres*. But the *zerika shelo lishmo* — doing it as an ordinary *shelamim* — might make it valid.

One might answer from the case of the *korban Pesach*: before *chatzos* on the fourteenth of Nisan, a *shechita* performed *lishmo* as a *Pesach* is invalid (since the *Pesach* is only slaughtered in the afternoon), yet a *shechita shelo lishmo* as a *shelamim* would be perfectly valid. So there is indeed a precedent for something invalid *lishmo* yet valid *shelo lishmo*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָא אָמֵינָא: יֵשׁ לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁנִּרְאֶה לִשְׁמוֹ, וְנִדְחֶה מִלִּשְׁמוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ כָּשֵׁר לִשְׁמוֹ, וְכָשֵׁר שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira replied: **This is what I** was **saying: Do you have anything that was fit** to be sacrificed **for its own sake,** like these sheep of *Shavuot* that were slaughtered before the loaves were lost, **and was** then **rejected from** being sacrificed **for its own sake,** like these sheep when the loaves were lost, **and is not fit** if it is sacrificed **for its own sake, and** yet it is **fit** if it is sacrificed **not for its own sake?**

The *Gemara* then refines the question: the real concern is *yesh lach davar shenira lishmo venishchat lishmo venidcha milishmo* — something that was fit for its purpose, was slaughtered for its purpose, got onto the track of its proper identity, and was then pushed off that track (because the bread was lost) — *ve'eino kasher lishmo vekasher shelo lishmo?* Can such a thing be saved by doing it *shelo lishmo*? The *Pesach before chatzos* case does not fully answer this, since there the animal was not yet slaughtered *lishmo* before the change.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וַהֲרֵי פֶּסַח אַחַר זְמַנּוֹ, בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya responded: **And** is there **no** precedent for this? **But there is** the **Paschal offering,** which was fit to be sacrificed for its own sake during its designated time, and **after its** designated **time, during** the **rest of the days of the year before midday,** it is not fit to be sacrificed as a Paschal offering but it is fit to be sacrificed as a peace offering.

### Gemara Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי תּוֹדָה, דְּרַחֲמָנָא קַרְיַיהּ ״שְׁלָמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira answered: The **thanks offering is different, as the Merciful One called it a peace offering** (see Leviticus 7:13). Just as a peace offering is sacrificed without loaves, so too a thanks offering may sometimes be sacrificed without loaves. Therefore, the loss of the loaves does not render the thanks offering disqualified, and this case is not comparable to the case of the two sheep and two loaves of *Shavuot*.

And yet the *Gemara* notes that the *toda* case does seem to fit: the *toda* was slaughtered *lishmo* and then the bread was lost, yet we have established that doing the *zerika shelo lishmo* is valid. So the question becomes: why not do the same here with the *shtei halechem*?

### Toda Unique

The *Gemara* answers with the same principle introduced earlier: *shani Toda derachmana karya shelamim* — the *toda* is categorically different because the Torah itself calls the *toda* a type of *shelamim*. Therefore if the *toda* cannot function as a *toda* (because the bread is lost), it can revert to functioning as a *shelamim*, which is its Toraitic identity. The *shnei kevasim* of *Shavuos*, however, even though they too are a form of *shelamim* (*shalmei tzibur*), derive their entire identity from their relationship with the bread. Without the bread, one cannot actively reassign their identity by performing a *zerika shelo lishmo*, and it appears that even *bedi'eved* such a *zerika* would not be valid.

## Four Loaves Case

### Four Loaves Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שָׁחַט שְׁנֵי כְּבָשִׂים עַל אַרְבַּע חַלּוֹת – מוֹשֵׁךְ שְׁתַּיִם מֵהֶן וּמְנִיפָן, וְהַשְּׁאָר נֶאֱכָלוֹת בְּפִדְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another discussion concerning the sheep and loaves of *Shavuot*. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one **slaughtered** the **two sheep** as required but they were accompanied **by four loaves** rather than the requisite two loaves, **he draws two of** the loaves from the four **and waves them** together with the sheep, **and the rest** of the loaves **are** permitted to be **eaten through redemption.**

The *Gemara* presents a new scenario in a *baraisa*: *shechat shnei kevasim al arba chalos* — someone slaughtered the two lambs over four loaves of bread rather than two. What is the status?

### Rabbi Unknown Two

According to Rabbi, the *shechita* confers *kedushas haguf* on two of the four loaves. But which two? In the abstract, two out of four have *kedushas haguf* and two do not — but their identities are indeterminate. The *baraisa* says: take two, perform *tnufa* (waving), and the remaining two may be eaten after *pidyon* (redemption), since the *shechita* connected only to two loaves, and the other two carry only *kedushas damim* and can be redeemed.

### Chulin Ba'Azarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא: הָא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי. דְּאִי רַבִּי, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר שְׁחִיטָה מְקַדְּשָׁא, דְּפָרֵיק לְהוּ – הֵיכָא? אִי דְּפָרֵיק לְהוּ מֵאַבָּרַאי, כֵּיוָן דִּכְתִיב ״לִפְנֵי ה׳״, אִיפְּסִיל לְהוּ בְּיוֹצֵא. אִי גַּוַואי, הָא מְעַיֵּיל חוּלִּין לָעֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages said the following **before Rav Ḥisda: This** *baraita* **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who holds that the slaughter of the sheep grants the loaves inherent sanctity, and in this case two of the loaves have inherent sanctity but it is not known which ones. **As, if** the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **since he says** that the **slaughter** of the sheep **consecrates** the loaves with inherent sanctity, when the *baraita* states **that he redeems** the loaves, **where** does he redeem them? The process of redemption would be to place all four loaves in front of him and state that whichever two of the loaves do not have inherent sanctity are redeemed for money. **If he redeems them outside** of the Temple courtyard, **since it is written:** "And the priest shall wave them with the bread of the first fruits for a wave offering **before the Lord,** with the two lambs" (Leviticus 23:20), **he disqualifies** the two loaves that possess inherent sanctity **by** causing them to **leave** the courtyard, at which point they are no longer "before the Lord." Conversely, **if** he redeems them **inside** the courtyard, once the two loaves that do not possess inherent sanctity are redeemed, he violates the prohibition against **bringing non-sacred** items **into the** Temple **courtyard.**

The *rabbanim* before Rav Chisda object: this cannot go according to Rabbi. According to Rabbi, the *shechita* already created *kedushas haguf* in two of the four loaves. When you attempt to redeem the two loaves that lack *kedushas haguf*, you face an insoluble problem. If you take them outside the *Beis Hamikdash* to redeem them there, the two that *do* have *kedushas haguf* will become *pasul* through *yotzei* — since you don't know which two they are, you must take all four outside, and two of them are *kedushat haguf* and will be invalidated. If you attempt the redemption inside the *Beis Hamikdash*, you will wind up with two non-sanctified loaves (*chulin*) inside the *azarah*, which is a separate prohibition of *chulin ba'azarah*. Moreover, since you cannot identify which two are which, the *kohanim* will end up eating all four as if they were *kodshim*, which means they are eating *chulin* as *kodshim*.

### Rav Chisda Fix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב חִסְדָּא: לְעוֹלָם כְּרַבִּי, וּפָרֵיק לְהוּ גַּוַואי, וְחוּלִּין מִמֵּילָא קָא הָוְויָין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said to** them: **Actually,** the *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and** one **redeems** the loaves **inside** the courtyard. Nevertheless, it is not considered to be a violation of the prohibition against bringing non-sacred items into the courtyard because the **non-sacred** loaves **came** into the courtyard **by themselves,** i.e., they were already there when they became non-sacred and were not actively brought into the courtyard in their non-sacred state.

Rav Chisda attempts to save the case: *le-olam k'Rabbi* — it can still work according to Rabbi — *ufarak lehu gavai* — do the redemption inside the *Beis Hamikdash*. And as for the problem of *chulin ba'azarah*: *vechulin m'meila k'havian* — they do not actively bring *chulin* into the *azarah*; the loaves were all *kodesh* and then one happened to become *chulin* through the redemption. The situation arises by itself, not through any deliberate act of bringing *chulin* in.

### Ravina Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְהָתַנְיָא, כְּשֶׁהוּא פּוֹדָן – אֵין פּוֹדָן אֶלָּא בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to this very case: **When he redeems** the loaves, **he** may **redeem them only outside** of the courtyard? This contradicts Rav Ḥisda's claim that according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi one redeems the loaves inside the courtyard.

Ravina challenges Rav Chisda before Rav Ashi from a *baraisa*: *k'shehu podan ein podan ela bachutz* — when one redeems the loaves, the redemption must be performed outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. This directly contradicts Rav Chisda's solution of redeeming inside.

### Concede R.Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא וַדַּאי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאִי רַבִּי – הָא אִיפְּסִלוּ לְהוּ בְּיוֹצֵא!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi answered: **This** *baraita* **is certainly** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, because if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, when he brings the loaves outside the courtyard **he thereby disqualifies them by** causing them to **leave** the courtyard.

Faced with this *baraisa*, the *Gemara* concedes: *ha vadai Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon hi* — the *baraisa* describing the case of four loaves must be following Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon and not Rabbi. This also explains why the *baraisa* only requires waving two, not all four: according to Rabbi, if two out of four have unknown *kedushas haguf*, one would logically have to wave all four, since it is unknown which two are the ones of *kedushas haguf*.

### R.Elazar Works

According to Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon, the solution is elegant. Since *shechita* alone does not create *kedushas haguf* — the bread requires both *shechita* and *zerika* before it has *kedushas haguf* — all four loaves after *shechita* have no *kedushas haguf* whatsoever. They have only *kedushas damim*. One may therefore freely choose which two to redeem, take them outside the *Beis Hamikdash* (where there is no problem of *yotzei* since they have no *kedushas haguf*), redeem them there, and then proceed to wave the remaining two and do the *zerika* — which will then confer *kedushas haguf* on those two. The case works perfectly within Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon's framework.

### Toda Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מִיהָא דְּאִיתְּמַר: תּוֹדָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ עַל שְׁמוֹנִים חַלּוֹת – חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: קָדְשׁוּ אַרְבָּעִים מִתּוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לֹא קָדְשׁוּ אַרְבָּעִים מִתּוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: Let us say** that the *baraita*, which states that if the sheep of *Shavuot* are slaughtered with four loaves instead of two, two of the four are invested with inherent sanctity, **is a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan that was stated** with regard to **a thanks offering that one slaughtered** accompanied **by eighty loaves** rather than the required forty. In that case, **Ḥizkiyya says: Forty of the eighty** loaves **are consecrated, and Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Not** even **forty of** the **eighty** loaves **are consecrated.**

Rav Acha the son of Rav raises a further question to Rav Ashi: does this analysis not create a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan? For Rabbi Yochanan taught, regarding a *toda* slaughtered over eighty loaves instead of forty: *lo kidshu arbaim mitoch shmonim* — forty loaves do not become sanctified out of eighty; if the wrong quantity of bread is brought, none of it becomes *kodesh*. In our case everyone is assuming that when you bring four loaves over two lambs, two of the four do become *kodesh*. Is that not in tension with Rabbi Yochanan?

### Resolution Two Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָאו מִי אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר ״לִיקְדְּשׁוּ אַרְבָּעִים מִתּוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים״ דְּקָדְשָׁה, הָכָא נָמֵי דְּאָמַר ״לִיקְדְּשׁוּ תַּרְתֵּי מִתּוֹךְ אַרְבַּע״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Wasn't it stated with regard to this** dispute that **Rabbi Zeira says: Everyone,** even Rabbi Yoḥanan, **concedes** that in a case **where** the individual bringing the offering **said: Let forty of** the **eighty** loaves **be consecrated, that** forty **are consecrated? Here too,** one can say that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where one said: Let two of the four** loaves **be consecrated.**

The *Gemara* resolves this. Rabbi Zeira taught: *hakol modim heicha d'amar likdeshu arbaim mitoch shmonim d'kadshu* — everyone agrees, even Rabbi Yochanan, that if one explicitly designates forty out of eighty to be sanctified, they are sanctified. Rabbi Yochanan only said that when one attempts to sanctify all eighty (an incorrect quantity), none of them become *kodesh*. *Hacha nami* — here too, the four-loaves case is understood as one in which the person designated two out of the four to be sanctified: *d'amar likdeshu tarti mitoch arba* — two out of four. In that case, even Rabbi Yochanan would agree that those two become *kodesh*, even if their precise identities are unknown. The remaining question — how you navigate which two, how you perform *tnufa*, how you redeem — is resolved differently according to Rabbi and Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon, as outlined above.
