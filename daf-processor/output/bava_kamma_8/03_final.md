# Bava Kamma 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Meitav Sadeihu Overview

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עִידִּית וְזִיבּוּרִית – נִזָּקִין בְּעִידִּית, וּבַעַל חוֹב וּכְתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה בְּזִיבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** If he has only **superior-quality and inferior-quality** land, **damages** are paid **from** the **superior-quality** land, **and** payments owed to **a creditor and** payments of **a woman's marriage contract** are made **from** the **inferior-quality** land.

We begin on Daf 8, picking up from the bottom of 7b, in the midst of a sustained discussion about the rules governing collection of damages — *nezikin*. When a *mazik* pays the person he has harmed, the *nizak*, and payment is made in land, the Torah requires that the *nizak* receive the best quality land: *meitav sadeihu*. This principle has been the subject of ongoing debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael — specifically, whether *meitav* refers to the best land of the *mazik* or the best land of the *nizak*.

### Akiva vs. Yishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא מְצִיעָא: בֵּינוֹנִית וְזִיבּוּרִית – נִזָּקִין וּבַעַל חוֹב בְּבֵינוֹנִית, וּכְתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה בְּזִיבּוּרִית. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּשֶׁלּוֹ הֵן שָׁמִין; תֵּעָשֶׂה בֵּינוֹנִית שֶׁלּוֹ – כְּעִידִּית, וְיִדְחֶה בַּעַל חוֹב אֵצֶל זִיבּוּרִית!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains how this *baraita* poses a challenge: **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches** in the **middle clause:** If he owns only **intermediate-quality and inferior-quality** land, payments for **damages and** payments owed to **a creditor** are made **from** the **intermediate-quality** land, **and** payments of **a woman's marriage contract** are made **from** the **inferior-quality** land. This clause demonstrates that the quality of land is appraised objectively based on the standard of the world at large. The reason for this is that **if you say** that the court **appraises** land **based on** the quality of the land of the one liable for the damage, **let his intermediate-quality** land **be classified as superior-quality** land, as that is the best land he owns, **and** consequently, the **creditor should be directed to** collect the debt from the **inferior-quality** land.

The prior discussion established the positions of Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael regarding from whose perspective "best" is measured. We enter the Gemara already having framed the contours of that debate, with the question now sharpening into something more precise.

### Bishlo vs. Beshel Olam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ עִידִּית, וּמְכָרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **With what are we dealing here?** This is a case **where** at the time the borrower took the loan **he** also **had superior-quality** land **and he** subsequently **sold it.** Accordingly, no matter how land is appraised, at the time that the loan was given his intermediate-quality land would be classified as being of intermediate quality. Consequently, the creditor's lien on the borrower's land, which took effect from the moment the loan was given, takes effect with regard to the intermediate-quality land. Therefore, the creditor retains his right to collect from that land, regardless of the fact that by the time of collection it was the borrower's best land and at that point could be reclassified as land of superior quality.

Even granting that we measure *meitav* from the *mazik*'s perspective, a further question arises. Suppose the *mazik* possesses land that is of a quality exceeding the universal standard for Grade A — what might be called "triple A." Must he pay with Grade A as defined by an objective, universal benchmark (*beshel olam*), or must he pay with whatever is genuinely his personal best (*bishlo*)? The scenario where the debtor has only Grade B and lower is not the interesting case — obviously he pays with the best he has. The tension surfaces when his personal best surpasses the standard.

### Gemara's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ עִידִּית, וּמְכָרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And so says Rav Ḥisda:** The *baraita* deals with a case **where** at the time he took the loan **he** also **had superior-quality** land **and he** subsequently **sold it.**

The Gemara resolves this immediately. The Torah says *meitav sadeihu* — **his** best land. The Gemara presses the point directly: *rachmana amar meitav sadeihu, v'at amarta beshel olam shamin* — the Torah says his best land, and yet you propose we measure by a universal standard? Clearly, the operative standard is subjective: it is his best, *bishlo*.

## Three Creditor Types

### Braitah Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא – מִדְּקָתָנֵי אַחֲרִיתִי: בֵּינוֹנִית וְזִיבּוּרִית – נִזָּקִין בְּבֵינוֹנִית, בַּעַל חוֹב וּכְתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה בְּזִיבּוּרִית. קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **This too stands to reason, from** the fact **that another** *baraita* **teaches:** If a debtor has only **intermediate-quality and inferior-quality** land, **damages** are collected **from** the **intermediate-quality** land, while payments to **a creditor and** payments of **a woman's marriage contract** are made from the **inferior-quality** land. These two *baraitot* **are difficult,** as they contradict **each other.** The *baraita* above teaches that in this case the creditor collects the debt from the intermediate-quality land, whereas this *baraita* teaches that he collects the debt from the inferior-quality land.

With the subjective-versus-objective question freshly raised, the Gemara introduces a *braitah* that complicates matters. The *braitah* sets out the framework for who collects what quality of land across three different categories of creditors.

### Collection Hierarchy

The basic rule is as follows. *Nezikin* — the collection of damages — is paid from *idis*, Grade A, the best quality land. A *baal chov*, a creditor collecting repayment of a loan, collects from *beinonis*, Grade B, the middle quality. A *kesubas ishah*, a wife collecting her marriage settlement, collects from *ziboris*, Grade C, the lowest quality.

### Policy Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: כָּאן שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ עִידִּית וּמְכָרָהּ, כָּאן – שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לוֹ עִידִּית וּמְכָרָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it** that **here,** in the *baraita* above, it is a case where **he** also **had superior-quality** land at the time that he took the loan **and** he subsequently **sold it,** while **there,** in the *baraita* subsequently cited, it is a case where **he did not have superior-quality** land **and** he did not **sell it.** In such a case, since his intermediate-quality land was his best land, it is classified as superior-quality land and accordingly, no lien took effect with regard to it.

Each of these gradations reflects a considered policy judgment. The Torah itself frames lending money to the poor as the paradigmatic form of *tzedakah* — not outright giving, but interest-free loans, which may ultimately be forgiven. Lending is thus difficult enough to motivate; the rabbis were concerned not to create further disincentives. Permitting a *baal chov* to collect from only the lowest quality land would discourage lending altogether. At the same time, allowing collection from the highest quality land risks predatory lending — a creditor might extend a loan precisely because he covets a borrower's prime land and anticipates a default. The middle ground of *beinonis* balances these concerns. As for the *kesubah*, the Gemara reasons, in line with the discussion in Masechet Kesubos, that social incentives favor men requiring greater encouragement to marry; accordingly the wife's settlement is drawn from the lowest-quality land.

### Single Grade Cases

The *braitah* then works through a series of scenarios. If the debtor possesses only Grade A land, *kulam govin min ha-idis* — all three categories of creditor collect from Grade A. There is no alternative. Likewise, if he has only Grade B, all collect from Grade B; if only Grade C, all collect from Grade C. These cases are straightforward.

### Mixed Grade Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לוֹ עִידִּית וּמְכָרָהּ; וְלָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּשָׁוְיָא בֵּינוֹנִית שֶׁלּוֹ כְּעִידִּית דְּעָלְמָא, וְכָאן דְּלָא שָׁוְיָא בֵּינוֹנִית שֶׁלּוֹ כְּעִידִּית דְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the contradiction between the *baraitot* can be resolved differently: Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* concern cases where the borrower **did not have superior-quality** land **and sell it. And** it **is not difficult** because **this** second *baraita* concerns a case where **his intermediate-quality** land **is equivalent** in quality **to the superior-quality** land **of the world** at large; therefore, it is classified as superior-quality land and the creditor has no right to collect from it. **And here,** the first *baraita* concerns a case in **which his intermediate-quality** land **is not equivalent** in quality **to the superior-quality** land **of the world at large** but is similar to the intermediate-quality land of the world at large; consequently, the creditor has a claim to it.

The analysis grows more interesting when the debtor holds multiple grades. If he has Grade A and Grade B: *nezikin* takes Grade A, and both the *baal chov* and the *kesubas ishah* take Grade B — the wife receives an upgrade since there is no Grade C available.

If he has only Grade B and Grade C: *nezikin* and the *baal chov* both collect from Grade B, and the *kesubas ishah* takes Grade C. The *nezikin* creditor is downgraded by necessity.

The genuinely interesting case is when the debtor holds only Grade A and Grade C — no Grade B at all. Here, *nezikin* takes Grade A and *kesubas ishah* takes Grade C. What of the *baal chov*? There is enough Grade A remaining after the *nezikin* collection, and enough Grade C as well, to satisfy him from either. The person paying would prefer to offer Grade C. The ruling is that the *baal chov* is downgraded — he gets Grade C. As Rashi explains: *mi-d'oraita ba'al chov b-ziborit* — from the Torah's perspective, the *baal chov* is actually only entitled to *ziboris*. The rabbis upgraded him to *beinonis* out of concern for *ne'ilat delet* — not closing the door on potential lenders. *Ve-heikha d'leit leh beinonis leit leh takanta* — where there is no Grade B available, the rabbinic enactment does not apply, and he reverts to the biblical baseline. The *baal chov* is thus the only figure whose position is genuinely variable; the *kesubas ishah* can only be upgraded when there is no Grade C, and the *nezikin* claimant is simply downgraded when there is no Grade A. Only the *baal chov* occupies a middle position where the question of upgrade versus downgrade is non-trivial — and the ruling is to downgrade.

## Braitot Contradiction

### Subjective Standard Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה בֵּינוֹנִית שֶׁלּוֹ כְּבֵינוֹנִית דְּעָלְמָא, וְהָכָא בְּהָא פְּלִיגִי – מָר סָבַר: בְּשֶׁלּוֹ הֵן שָׁמִין. וּמָר סָבַר: בְּשֶׁל עוֹלָם הֵן שָׁמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* concern a case **where** the borrower's **intermediate-quality** land, which is the best land he owns, **is equivalent** in quality **to the intermediate-quality** land **of the world** at large, **and here,** the *baraitot* **disagree about this:** One **Sage,** the *tanna* of the second *baraita*, **holds** that the court **appraises** the debtor's land **based on** the quality of **his** other land, so that his best land is classified as superior-quality land and the creditor has no claim on it. And the other **Sage,** the *tanna* of the first *baraita*, **holds** that the court **appraises** the debtor's land **based on** the quality of land in **the world** at large, so that his land is classified as intermediate-quality land and the creditor is entitled to claim it.

The Gemara now trains its scrutiny on the scenario of Grade B and Grade C only, and raises a pointed problem. The middle case of the *braitah* — *beinonis v-ziboris* — teaches that *nezikin* and the *baal chov* both collect from Grade B. But if we accept the subjective standard, *bishlo hen shamin*, then the *baal chov*'s Grade B *is* Grade A from the debtor's personal perspective. In that case, *ta'aseh beinonis shelo ke-idit* — his Grade B becomes Grade A — and therefore *nezikin* should be entitled to it exclusively, pushing the *baal chov* down to *ziborit*. The *braitah*'s ruling that they share Grade B seems to presuppose an objective standard.

### Lien Resolution

The Gemara answers: *hacha bemai askinan kegon de-it lei idit u-machra* — we are speaking of a case where the debtor previously held Grade A land and then sold it off. When the loan was taken out, Grade A, B, and C all existed. At that point, the *baal chov* acquired a lien specifically on the Grade B property, since that was the quality to which he was entitled. Even after Grade A was sold off — causing Grade B to functionally become the best remaining — the *baal chov* does not lose the lien he had already acquired. His rights to Grade B are locked in from the time of the loan. This is confirmed by Rav Chisda, who explains the case the same way: *kegon de-hava lei idit u-machra* — it is a case where Grade A had existed and been sold.

### Braitot Contradiction Continued

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: בִּדְעוּלָּא פְּלִיגִי – דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה – בַּעַל חוֹב בְּזִיבּוּרִית, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּחוּץ תַּעֲמוֹד, וְהָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר אַתָּה נֹשֶׁה בוֹ יוֹצִיא אֵלֶיךָ אֶת הַעֲבוֹט הַחוּצָה״ – מָה דַּרְכּוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם לְהוֹצִיא לַחוּץ – פָּחוּת שֶׁבַּכֵּלִים. וּמָה טַעַם אָמְרוּ בַּעַל חוֹב בְּבֵינוֹנִית – כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּנְעוֹל דֶּלֶת בִּפְנֵי לֹוִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** Another resolution to the contradiction between the *baraitot* is that the *baraitot* **disagree with regard to** the opinion of **Ulla, as Ulla says: By Torah law, a creditor** collects **from inferior-quality** land, **as it is stated: "You shall stand outside, and the man you have a claim against will bring his collateral out to you"** (Deuteronomy 24:11). One can infer: **What** item would **a person typically** choose **to bring out** for use as collateral and potential payment? Certainly it is the **most inferior of his utensils.** The verse thereby indicates that a creditor collects from inferior-quality land. **But** if so, for **what reason** did the Sages **say** that **a creditor** collects **from intermediate-quality** land? They instituted this ordinance **so as not to lock the door in the face of** potential **borrowers,** as, if creditors were limited to collecting from inferior-quality land they would be hesitant to offer loans in the first place.

This resolution sets the stage for a stark contradiction between two *braitot*. Both deal with a debtor holding only Grade B and Grade C. The first *braitah* rules that *nezikin* and the *baal chov* collect from Grade B while the *kesubas ishah* collects from Grade C. A second *braitah* rules the opposite for the *baal chov*: *nezikin* gets Grade B, while both the *baal chov* and the *kesubas ishah* are relegated to Grade C. These two positions directly contradict each other regarding the *baal chov*'s entitlement.

### Ever-Had-A Theory

The first resolution: both *braitot* operate under the subjective standard, *bishlo hen shamin*, and differ only on whether Grade A had ever existed. Where Grade A had previously existed and been sold, the *baal chov* retains his lien on Grade B and collects there — that is the case of the first *braitah*. Where Grade A never existed at all and only Grade B and Grade C were always present, the *baal chov* was never entitled to anything beyond Grade C. Under the subjective standard, what had been Grade B was always effectively Grade A, leaving only Grade C for the *baal chov* — that is the case of the second *braitah*. This resolution preserves the subjective standard throughout and explains the divergence entirely on the factual question of whether Grade A was ever in the picture.

### Standards Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר אִית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא דְּעוּלָּא, וּמָר לֵית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא דְּעוּלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina explains: One **Sage,** the *tanna* of the first *baraita*, **is of** the opinion that the ruling is in accordance with the **ordinance** cited **by Ulla** that a creditor collects from intermediate-quality land. **And** the other **Sage,** the *tanna* of the second *baraita*, **is not of** the opinion that the ruling is in accordance with the **ordinance** cited **by Ulla,** so the creditor has a right to collect from only inferior-quality land, in accordance with Torah law.

The Gemara offers two further resolutions. The second resolution (*ve-iba'it eima*): we need not invoke the sold-off Grade A at all. Instead, both *braitot* reflect the objective standard, *beshel olam*, and the difference turns on whether this particular debtor's Grade B land happens to equal Grade A in universal market terms. If his Grade B is world-standard Grade A, the *baal chov* is not entitled to it; if his Grade B is genuinely world-standard Grade B, the *baal chov* collects there.

The third and most elegant resolution: the two *braitot* are simply a direct dispute over the subjective versus objective standard. The *braitah* in which the *baal chov* collects Grade B assumes *beshel olam* — the objective standard — and therefore Grade B remains Grade B, within his entitlement. The *braitah* in which the *baal chov* is relegated to Grade C assumes *bishlo* — the subjective standard — so Grade B is reclassified as Grade A, and the *baal chov* has never had a claim above Grade C. The two *braitot* map precisely onto the framing question established at the outset of the discussion.

## Ula's Teaching

### Ravina's Approach

Ravina offers yet another reading. The dispute between the two *braitot*, he says, is not about subjective versus objective standards but about whether to accept the teaching of Ula.

### Ula's Principle

Ula holds that from the Torah's perspective, *dvar Torah, ba'al chov be-ziborit* — a creditor is biblically entitled only to the lowest quality land.

### Torah Source

The source is the verse *bachutz ta'amod* — you shall stand outside — and *yotzi eleicha et ha-avot hachutza*, he shall bring the collateral out to you. The creditor stands at the door; it is the debtor's initiative to bring out what he will. *Mah darko shel adam lehotzi lachutz — pachos she-ba-kelim* — what does a person naturally bring out first? The worst of his possessions. This teaches that the creditor has no claim beyond what the debtor chooses to offer, which will be the lowest quality.

### Tosafot Challenge

Tosafot raise an objection: we established just a few pages earlier that all livestock is considered *meitav* — all of it quality — so the verse about collateral cannot straightforwardly teach a principle about the quality of land payment. Tosafot resolve this by distinguishing: when a person brings out vessels or goods, it is natural to bring the inferior item first; and similarly, when it comes to land, it is natural to offer the *ziboris*. The principle thus stands, even if the analogy from livestock does not hold. The point the verse conveys is that the initiative belongs to the debtor, and initiative means he offers the worst.

### Ne'ilat Delet Takana

Given Ula's position, why does the *baal chov* ever receive Grade B? *U-mah ta'am amru ba'al chov bi-veinonit? Kedei shelo tin'al delet bifnei lovin* — the rabbis upgraded him to *beinonis* so as not to close the door before potential lenders. This is the *takana* of *ne'ilat delet*.

### Takana Scope

Under Ravina's reading, the disagreement between the two *braitot* is a debate over whether Ula's *takana* applies. One *braitah* accepts the *takana* and grants the *baal chov* Grade B; the other rejects or limits the *takana* and leaves him at Grade C. Tosafot add nuance: the *braitah* that gives him only Grade C may not be rejecting the *takana* altogether, but rather holding that when the *baal chov* faces competition from other creditors in a distribution, we readily revert him to the biblical baseline. The question is thus not whether the *takana* exists, but precisely when — and in what competitive contexts — it ceases to apply.

## Property Sales Cases

### Braitah Sales

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מָכַר לְאֶחָד אוֹ לִשְׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם כְּאֶחָד – כּוּלָּן נִכְנְסוּ תַּחַת הַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta* (*Ketubot* 12:3): If one owes money to pay damages, repay a loan, and pay a woman's marriage contract, and he owns inferior-quality, intermediate-quality, and superior-quality land, and he sells all of his land, then if he **sold** all of the land **to one** person **or to three people simultaneously, all** the buyers **replace the** land's prior **owner** with regard to repayment of his debts. At the time when each debt was created, each creditor gained a lien on the plots of the debtor's land, from which they have a right to collect. As a lien remains in effect even if the land is sold, then even after the sale, each creditor can collect from the plots of land upon which they have a lien. Accordingly, damages are collected from the superior-quality land, the loan is repaid from the intermediate-quality land, and the marriage contract is paid from the inferior-quality land.

The Gemara now introduces a new *braitah* addressing a different dimension of the problem: what happens when the debtor, instead of holding his property and facing his creditors, sells it off to purchasers? The *braitah* considers cases where property is sold to one buyer or to three buyers, either on the same day or on different days.

### Same-Day Sales

If property is sold to three buyers on the same day — whether all three grades to one buyer, or Grade A to one, Grade B to another, and Grade C to a third — *kulam nichnesu tachat habaalim*, they all step into the seller's position. Each buyer is treated as though he were the original owner of his respective parcel, and the creditors collect accordingly: *nezikin* from whoever holds Grade A, *baal chov* from whoever holds Grade B, and *kesubas ishah* from whoever holds Grade C. Sales transacted on the same day are treated as simultaneous, since in the absence of a date-and-hour specification in the deed — which was only done in Jerusalem — there is no way to establish priority.

### Sequential Sales

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה – כּוּלָּן גּוֹבִין מִן הָאַחֲרוֹן. אֵין לוֹ – גּוֹבֶה מִשֶּׁלְּפָנָיו, אֵין לוֹ – גּוֹבֶה מִשֶּׁלִּפְנֵי פָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** If he sold his plots of land **one after the other, all** the creditors **collect from the last** buyer, regardless of the quality of the land he purchased. If that buyer **does not have** sufficient land purchased from the debtor to pay all the debts, the creditors take the land he has and **collect** the remaining sums **from** the one **who** purchased land **before him,** and if he also **does not have** sufficient land, they **collect** the rest **from** the one **before** the one **before him,** i.e., the first buyer. The Sages instituted that creditors may not collect from land that a debtor sold, even though it is technically still liened to them, as long as the debtor still has sufficient means with which to repay the debt (see *Gittin* 48b). Accordingly, after the sale of the first plot of land, the creditors' rights to collect are restricted to the land that remains in the possession of the debtor, and consequently they gain a lien on it, irrespective of its quality. Therefore, even if that land is subsequently sold, their rights to collect are restricted to that land, and only if the value of that land is insufficient to cover the cost of the debt may they utilize the original liens they had on the plots of land that were sold first.

If, however, the property was sold on different days — first Grade A to one buyer, then Grade B, then Grade C — the rule changes dramatically. *Kulam govin min ha-acharon* — all the creditors must collect from the last buyer, the one who purchased most recently. If that buyer lacks sufficient funds, they proceed to the second buyer; and if necessary, to the first.

### Primary Liability

The governing principle is that the person who owes the money bears primary responsibility for repayment. Creditors cannot pursue those who purchased liened property before exhausting their claims against the original debtor. The liened property in the hands of a buyer is always a fallback, not a first resort.

### Buyer Deflection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מְכָרָן לְאֶחָד״ הֵיכִי דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: With regard to the first case in the *baraita*, where he **sold** all the land **to one** person, **what are the circumstances?**

Each successive buyer can redirect the creditors to the later buyer's property. The reasoning: when the first buyer purchased, all the property that was subsequently sold still remained in the seller's possession. At that moment, the creditors' rights were effectively focused on what the seller still held. As each sale occurred, rights migrated to the last parcel standing. *Hinachti lecha makom ligvot heimenu* — I left you a place to collect from — each earlier buyer can say to the creditors.

## Single vs. Multiple

### Sequential Purchase Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵימָא בְּבַת אַחַת – הַשְׁתָּא לִשְׁלֹשָׁה, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ קָדֵים, אָמְרַתְּ כּוּלָּן נִכְנְסוּ תַּחַת הַבְּעָלִים; מְכָרָן לְאֶחָד מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** that he **simultaneously** sold all of the land to one person, that is difficult. **Now** that the *baraita* teaches that if he sold the land **to three** different people simultaneously, **where it is possible to say** that the sale to **one of them preceded** the other sales, yet **you say** the *halakha* is that **all** the buyers **replace the** land's prior **owner** equally, and the possibility that one might have proceeded the other is disregarded, **is** it **necessary** to teach that the *halakha* is the same in a case where all the plots of land were simultaneously **sold to one** person?

The Gemara analyzes why the *braitah* needed to specify the case of selling to one person, since that case appears obvious. The Gemara's response: the genuine *chiddush* lies in the sequential purchase case.

### Last Buyer Rule

When a single buyer — call him Michael — purchased Grade A, Grade B, and Grade C on three separate days, the rule of *kulam govin min ha-acharon* would appear to require all creditors to collect from the last parcel Michael bought, regardless of its quality. If Michael bought Grade C last, all creditors are apparently forced to collect from Grade C.

### Objection to Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא – בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it **is obvious** that the *baraita* is referring to a case where the plots of land were sold **one after the other.**

This raises a difficulty. If Michael bought Grade A last, one might argue that all creditors — including the *baal chov* and the *kesubas ishah* — should be forced to collect from Grade A, since that was the last parcel remaining, to which all rights had migrated. Why should the normal distribution between Grade A, B, and C be restored?

### Buyer Leverage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא שְׁלֹשָׁה – דְּכֹל חַד וְחַד אָמַר לֵיהּ: הִנַּחְתִּי לְךָ מָקוֹם לִגְבּוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But what is different** about the latter clause of the *baraita*, where the plots of land were sold to **three** buyers, one after the other, and the creditors collect only from the last buyer? It is different in **that each one** of the first two buyers **can say to** any creditor who tries to seize the land they purchased: Do not collect my land, as **I left a place for you,** a plot of land in the debtor's possession, **from which to collect.** In other words, when the first two buyers purchased their land a plot of land remained with the debtor, and therefore the creditor's rights of collection are restricted to that land.

The Gemara attempts an answer: Michael has leverage. He can say to the creditors, either you take what you are each properly entitled to — *nezikin* from A, *baal chov* from B, *kesubas ishah* from C — or he will simply return the Grade C property to the original seller, at which point all creditors will be confined to Grade C. This threat creates a practical pressure to accept the distributive arrangement.

### Leverage Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי נָמֵי – אַכֹּל חַד וְחַד לֵימָא לֵיהּ: הִנַּחְתִּי לְךָ מָקוֹם לִגְבּוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its question: In **this** case **as well,** where all the plots of land were sold to a single buyer, **with regard to each one** of the first two plots of land that were sold, **let** the buyer **say to** any creditor who tries to collect: With my purchase of the first two plots of land, **I left a place for you,** a plot of land in the debtor's possession, **from which to collect.** Therefore, even though the debtor subsequently sold the buyer all of the land, the creditor's rights of collection are restricted to the last plot of land the debtor sold the buyer, irrespective of its quality. Why then does the *baraita* rule that in this case each creditor collects from the land that was originally liened to him?

The Gemara rejects this reasoning. If leverage were the operative mechanism, Michael could use the same threat against the *nezikin* claimant and deny him Grade A. Moreover, the leverage breaks down entirely when the original seller has died and his heirs are orphans. In that case, if Michael returns the Grade C to the orphans, creditors cannot collect newly inherited property from them — the orphans take possession of it free of prior liens. Michael therefore has no genuine threat, yet the distribution of A, B, and C must still apply. The leverage theory is thus insufficient.

## Takana Protection

### Takana Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁלָּקַח עִידִּית בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: כְּגוֹן שֶׁלָּקַח עִידִּית בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **With what are we dealing here?** This is a case **where** the individual buyer **purchased** the **superior-quality** land **last.** Accordingly, the buyer is satisfied with allowing each creditor to collect from the land that was previously liened to him, rather than having them each collect from the superior-quality land. **And so says Rav Sheshet:** It is a case **where** the individual buyer **purchased** the **superior-quality** land **last.**

The Gemara, now through Rav Ashi, provides the true foundation. The rabbis enacted a *takana*: *ein nifrain mi-nechasim meshuabadim bemakom she-yesh bnei chorin* — one may not collect from liened property when free property is available. So long as the original debtor holds unsold property, creditors cannot pursue the buyers.

### Takana Purpose

This enactment was instituted specifically to protect purchasers. When a person buys property from a debtor, he should not face the threat of creditors seizing what he bought, so long as the original debtor can pay from his remaining assets. The *takana* thus channels all creditor rights toward whatever property the debtor last held — in this scenario, Grade A, which Michael bought last.

### Waiver Right

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, לֵיתוֹ כּוּלְּהוּ וְלִיגְבּוֹ מֵעִידִּית!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, let them all come and collect from** the **superior-quality** land, as their rights to collect should be restricted to it, since it was purchased last.

Rava teaches: *kol ha-omer ee efshi be-takanas chachamim kegon zu, shomein lo* — anyone who says he does not wish to avail himself of a rabbinic *takana* that was enacted solely for his benefit may waive it, and we honor that waiver. Michael, recognizing that this *takana* now works *against* his interests — since it concentrates all creditor rights on his Grade A — can declare that he does not want the protection of this *takana*.

### Waivable Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמַר לְהוּ: אִי שָׁתְקִיתוּ וְשָׁקְלִיתוּ כְּדִינַיְיכוּ – שָׁקְלִיתוּ, וְאִי לָא – מַהְדַּרְנָא שְׁטָרָא דְּזִיבּוּרִית לְמָרֵיהּ, וְשָׁקְלִיתוּ כּוּלְּכוּ מִזִּיבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Because he can say** the following threat **to them: If you are silent** and do not insist on collecting from the superior-quality land, **and take** the land of a quality that is due to you **according to your** standard **legal rights,** as in a case where you collect directly from the debtor, i.e., the injured party will collect from the superior-quality land, the creditor from the intermediate-quality land, and the woman will collect her marriage contract from the inferior-quality land, then **take** that land and I will allow you to do so. **But if not,** and you insist on taking the superior-quality land that I purchased last, then **I will return the bill** of sale **of the inferior-quality** land **to its** prior **owner,** i.e., the debtor, **and you will all** be forced to collect **from** the **inferior-quality** land, because whenever the debtor has land in his possession you are able to collect the debts only from him.

Rava's principle is that a *takana* instituted to protect a specific party belongs to that party, who may therefore decline it when it has become disadvantageous. Once Michael waives the protection, the creditors revert to their natural entitlements, and the distribution proceeds normally: *nezikin* from Grade A, *baal chov* from Grade B, *kesubas ishah* from Grade C.

### Woman's Waiver

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בְּנִזָּקִין נָמֵי נֵימָא הָכִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that this is the reason for the ruling in the *baraita*, then **also with regard to damages, let** the buyer **say this** to the injured party. He could threaten to return the bill of sale of the inferior-quality land to the debtor, which would force the injured party to collect directly from the debtor's inferior-quality land. Since the buyer can do so, he can effectively force the injured party to collect from the purchased intermediate-quality land instead of fulfilling his right to collect from superior-quality land. Yet, the *baraita* teaches that in this case each party collects from the land that was originally liened to him, which means that damages are collected from the superior-quality land, indicating that this threat would not be effective.

Rav Huna provides the illustrative analogy: *yechola isha she-tomar le-va'alah eini nizonet ve-eini osa* — a wife may declare to her husband that she will neither accept his support nor perform labor for him. The rabbinic institution of spousal support was enacted primarily to protect the wife; in exchange, the husband receives the benefit of her earnings. Where the wife is a highly paid professional and finds that this arrangement works to her detriment — he receives far more than he provides — she may waive the support and retain her own income. The *takana* belonged to her protection, and she may decline it. So too, the *takana* protecting Michael from creditor pursuit belongs to his protection, and he may decline it when it works against him.

## Buyer Retains A

### Michael's Distribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּיַתְמֵי, דְּלָאו בְּנֵי פֵרָעוֹן נִינְהוּ, וְשִׁיעְבּוּדָא דִּילֵיהּ עֲלֵיהּ דִּידֵיהּ רַמְיָא;
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with** a case in which the debtor died and his inheritors are his **orphans, who are not subject to** an obligation to **repay** the debts of their father. When one dies, his monetary debts are not inherited by his children. **And** therefore, **the lien of** the creditor **rests** solely **upon the buyer** of the land. Any lien that was in effect with regard to the land he owned at the time he incurred the obligation remains, and his creditors can therefore collect what they are owed from that land, even if it is in the possession of a buyer.

The Gemara now works through specific applications. Consider: Michael bought Grade A, Grade B, and Grade C from the original debtor. He then sold Grade B and Grade C to Jonathan, retaining Grade A for himself.

### All Get A

In this case, all three categories of creditors — *nezikin*, *baal chov*, and *kesubas ishah* — collect from Michael's Grade A.

### Last Property Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ לֵיכָּא לְמֵימַר הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore, one cannot say** that the ruling in the *baraita* is based on the fact that the buyer can threaten to return the bill of sale to the debtor, or in this case, to his orphans. In this case, even if he were to return the bill of sale, the creditors would not be able to collect from the land now in the orphans' possession, as they received it anew, not as an inheritance from their father. Therefore, the creditors can still collect from the plots of land that remain with the buyer.

Why? Because Grade A was the last property in Michael's possession after he had disposed of Grade B and C. The creditors' rights were already fundamentally anchored to Grade A, which was the last parcel remaining. Michael cannot redirect creditors to Jonathan by waiving the *takana*, because the *takana* exists to protect the purchaser — Jonathan — not Michael. Michael cannot invoke Jonathan's protective right to push the creditors off onto him. Since Michael has only Grade A and cannot deflect the creditors elsewhere, they all collect from it.

## Buyer Sells A

### Michael Sells A

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמַר לְהוּ: טַעְמָא מַאי אֲמוּר רַבָּנַן אֵין נִפְרָעִין מִנְּכָסִין מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּנֵי חוֹרִין – מִשּׁוּם תַּקַּנְתָּא דִּידִי; אֲנָא בְּהָא תַּקַּנְתָּא לָא נִיחָא לִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another explanation of the *Tosefta*: **Rather,** not all the creditors collect from the superior-quality land, which was the last plot of land purchased from the debtor, **because** the buyer **can say to them: What is the reason** that **the Sages said one is not paid from liened** property, i.e., property liened to a creditor that has been sold by the debtor, **whenever there is unsold** property still in the debtor's possession? It is **due to an ordinance** created solely **for my** benefit, as I should not be expected to pay from the land I purchased when the debtor is still able to pay. If that ordinance were to be in effect in this case, all of their liens would be in effect with regard to the superior-quality land, as that was purchased last. In this case **I do not** find **this ordinance satisfactory to me,** and I would prefer for the lien of each creditor to remain in effect with regard to the land it had been on initially, and I will give each of you that land.

Now consider the reverse case: Michael bought Grade A, Grade B, and Grade C from the original debtor. He then sold Grade A to Jonathan, retaining Grade B and Grade C for himself.

### Missing A Problem

The complication: all three creditors had rights anchored on Grade A. Now that Grade A has moved to Jonathan, what becomes of those rights? Do all creditors chase Jonathan for Grade A?

### Abaye's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרָבָא – דְּאָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר אִי אֶפְשִׁי בְּתַקָּנַת חֲכָמִים כְּגוֹן זוֹ – שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: This fact that one is not required to avail himself of the financial benefit provided by a rabbinic ordinance **is in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava. As Rava says: Anyone who says: I do not want** to avail myself **of** the financial benefit provided by **an ordinance of the Sages, such as this** one, **one listens to him.**

Abaye's initial inclination: yes — *atu kulehu gavu me-idit* — let them all collect from Jonathan's Grade A, since that is where their rights were concentrated.

### Rava's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״כְּגוֹן זוֹ״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** does Rava mean by saying: An ordinance of the Sages, **such as this** one?

Rava disagrees. When Michael held Grade A together with Grade B and Grade C, he had the power to waive the *takana* and distribute the creditors normally — *nezikin* to A, *baal chov* to B, *kesubas ishah* to C. He could have exercised that power at any moment while he held all three parcels.

### Waiver Power Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַב הוּנָא – דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא, יְכוֹלָה אִשָּׁה שֶׁתֹּאמַר לְבַעְלָהּ: אֵינִי נִיזּוֹנֶית, וְאֵינִי עוֹשָׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: His statement is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Huna. As Rav Huna says:** The Sages enacted that a husband must sustain his wife. They also enacted that he receives her earnings in exchange. Yet, **a wife is** legally **entitled to say to her husband: I will not be sustained** by you, **and** in turn **I will not work** i.e., you will not keep my earnings. Since his obligation to sustain her is only for her benefit, she may forgo her sustenance and retain her earnings for herself.

The principle: *mah machar rishon le-sheni kol zechut she-tavo le-yado* — when a seller conveys property, he conveys with it every right that was available to him in that property. Since Michael could have redistributed the creditor claims away from Grade A onto Grade B and Grade C, and since that power was his to exercise, when he sold Grade A to Jonathan he transferred it with precisely that understanding — as if he had already exercised that redistributive power. The sale of Grade A to Jonathan thus carries only the *nezikin* lien, not the liens of the *baal chov* and the *kesubas ishah*.

### Distribution Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, מָכַר לוֹקֵחַ בֵּינוֹנִית וְזִיבּוּרִית, וְשִׁיֵּיר עִידִּית לְפָנָיו – לֵיתוֹ כּוּלְּהוּ וְלִיגְבּוֹ מֵעִידִּית;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a limitation on the buyer's ability to refuse the benefit of the rabbinic ordinance: **It is obvious** that if the **buyer sold,** of the land he purchased from the debtor, the **intermediate- and inferior-quality** land to another buyer **and retained in his possession** only the **superior-quality** land, which he had purchased last, **then all** the creditors **may come and collect from** the **superior-quality** land.

The result: Jonathan holds Grade A subject only to the *nezikin* claim. The *baal chov* and the *kesubas ishah* revert to Michael and collect from his Grade B and Grade C respectively. The transfer of Grade A to Jonathan is treated as though Michael had already waived the *takana* and allocated the creditors to their proper grades before the sale took place.
