# Nazir 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Partner Dies: Stranger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: יְבַקֵּשׁ אֶחָד מִן הַשּׁוּק שֶׁיִּדּוֹר כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בְּנָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The previous mishna described how two nazirites sacrifice offerings of impurity and purity, in a situation in which one of them has become impure but they do not know which one. This mishna discusses what must be done if **one of them dies** before bringing his offerings. **Rabbi Yehoshua said:** The surviving nazirite **asks someone in the marketplace,** a non-nazirite, **to vow to be a nazirite corresponding to him,** i.e., under the same conditions as his own naziriteship, so that he can bring offerings together with him.

The *mishnah* at the top of 59b returns to a scenario whose basic architecture we have encountered before: two people, one of whom has become *tamei*, though it is unknown which one. To resolve this uncertainty, they entered into a partnership, bringing their various *korbanos* with the stipulation that each offering should work for whichever partner truly needs it. This was the arrangement of the earlier *mishnah*: the *korban tumah* works for whoever is *tamei*, and the *korban tahara* works for whoever is *tahor*, with the process playing out over two sets of rounds separated by thirty days.

### Partnership & Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: אִם טָמֵא הָיִיתִי — הֲרֵי אַתָּה נָזִיר מִיָּד, וְאִם טָהוֹר הָיִיתִי — הֲרֵי אַתָּה נָזִיר אַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. וְסוֹפְרִין שְׁלֹשִׁים, וּמְבִיאִין קׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה וְקׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה. וְאוֹמֵר: אִם אֲנִי הוּא הַטָּמֵא — קׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה שֶׁלִּי, וְקׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה שֶׁלְּךָ. וְאִם אֲנִי הוּא הַטָּהוֹר — קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה שֶׁלִּי, וְקׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה בְּסָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **And he says** to him as follows: **If I was impure, you are hereby a nazirite immediately; and if I was pure, you are hereby a nazirite after thirty days. And they** both proceed to **count thirty days and bring an offering of impurity and an offering of purity. And** the nazirite who was defined as having uncertain impurity **says: If I am the impure** one, **the offering of impurity is mine and the offering of purity is yours; and if I am the pure one, the offering of purity is mine and the offering of impurity** we brought shall be **of uncertain** status.

To review the mechanics of that earlier arrangement: a wait of thirty days is required before bringing the *korban tumah*, because the person who is *tahor* cannot shave within thirty days of his *nezirut*, and shaving accompanies the *korbanos*. After thirty days, the partnership brings the *korban tumah* — which works for whichever one is *tamei* — and the *korban tahara* works for whichever one is *tahor*. After another thirty days, a final *korban tahara* is brought, and the process is complete.

### Partner Death Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסוֹפְרִים שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וּמְבִיאִין קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה, וְאוֹמֵר: אִם אֲנִי הַטָּמֵא — קׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה שֶׁלִּי, וְקׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה — שֶׁלְּךָ, וָזֶה קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָתִי. וְאִם אֲנִי הוּא הַטָּהוֹר — קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה שֶׁלִּי, וְקׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה בְּסָפֵק, וְזֶהוּ קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָתְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And they** subsequently **count** another **thirty days and bring an offering of purity, and** the first nazirite **says: If I** was **the impure** one, **the offering of impurity** that we sacrificed at the end of the first thirty days was **mine, and the offering of purity** we brought then was **yours; and this** offering I am bringing now is **my offering of purity. And if I** was **the pure** one, and the deceased nazirite was impure, **the offering of purity** we brought thirty days ago was **mine, and the offering of impurity** we brought earlier was **of uncertain** status, **and this is your offering of purity.**

The *mishnah* now introduces a complication: one of the two partners died. The surviving partner no longer has anyone with whom to make these joint stipulations. What is he to do?

The instinctive answer would be that he reverts to the situation of any ordinary *nazir* who has become *safek tamei*: he waits thirty days — for the person who is *tahor* cannot shave before thirty days — and then brings what he can bring *besafek*. For his *korban tumah* he brings a *chatas ha'of besafek*, since the Sages permitted bringing a *chatas ha'of* even under conditions of doubt in a way that a standard animal *chatas* may not be brought. For his *korban tahara* he brings an *olat behemah bitnai*: if he is *tahor*, this counts as his *korban tahara*; if he turns out to be *tamei*, it is simply a *nedavah*, a voluntary gift. Then after another thirty days he brings the remainder of the *korban tahara*, with similar conditional stipulations governing each component.

One might note that there is also a *shelamim* among the *korbanos* of *korban tahara* — this is indeed the case and should be included in the reckoning.

### Yehoshua: Stranger

What the *mishnah* actually records, however, is the position of Rabbi Yehoshua, who prescribes a more elaborate solution. He says: *yevakesh she'chamino shuk* — go find a stranger in the marketplace, perhaps offer him something for his trouble, and ask him to take on *nezirut* alongside you. The purpose is to reconstitute a two-person framework so that the full complement of *korbanos* can be brought with appropriate stipulations.

### Conditional Stipulations

The stipulations work as follows. At the time of entering the arrangement with the stranger, the surviving partner declares: if I am the *tamei* one, then you — the stranger — become a *nazir* immediately (*harei atah nazir miyad*). If I am the *tahor* one, then you become a *nazir* after thirty days (*harei atah nazir acharei shloshim yom*).

They then count thirty days together and bring both a *korban tumah* and a *korban tahara*. At this point the surviving partner says: if I was the *tamei* one, the *korban tumah* is mine and the *korban tahara* is yours — you have completed your *nezirut tahara*. If I was the *tahor* one, the *korban tahara* is mine and the *korban tumah* was brought *besafek* — it does not belong definitively to anyone. He then adds: after another thirty days we bring a further *korban tahara*, and if I was the *tamei* one, that first *korban tumah* was mine, that *korban tahara* was yours, and this final *korban tahara* is mine. If I was the *tahor* one, the earlier *korban tahara* was mine, and this final *korban tahara* is yours.

### Why Wait Thirty Days

One might wonder: why must the stranger wait thirty days at all? If the surviving partner may have to act as a *nazir* for the entire sixty-day period anyway, what is accomplished by deferring the stranger's *nezirut* until thirty days in, in the scenario where the survivor turns out to be *tahor*?

The answer concerns the cutting of hair. At the thirty-day mark, both parties will shave. In scenario one — the survivor is *tamei* — the stranger completed his *nezirut tahara* in thirty days and is entitled to shave. That works smoothly. But if the survivor is *tahor*, then the *korban tahara* at thirty days belongs to the survivor, and the stranger has not yet brought his own *korban tahara*. If the stranger's *nezirut* had already begun at the outset, he would have no valid *korban tahara* at the thirty-day mark and could not shave. We would then need to introduce yet another animal — an *olah bitnai* — just to permit him to shave.

### Original Tamei Scenario

In scenario one, where the survivor is *tamei*: the *korban tumah* at thirty days is his, the *korban tahara* at thirty days belongs to the stranger (whose *nezirut* began immediately), and the stranger shaves and concludes his *nezirut*. After another thirty days the survivor brings his own *korban tahara* and shaves. The mechanism works cleanly — both people bring a full complement of *korbanos* and neither is splitting his *korban tahara* across two periods.

### Original Tahor Scenario

In scenario two, where the survivor is *tahor*: at thirty days, the *korban tahara* belongs to the survivor. The *korban tumah* was brought *besafek* — it counts as nobody's obligatory offering and was conditional. The stranger's *nezirut* has not yet begun, which means he has not violated his *nezirut* by shaving. He cuts his hair not as the ritual shaving of a concluding *nazir*, but simply as a person who has not yet taken on *nezirut* — there is no violation. His *nezirut* then begins after this shaving. Thirty days later, the stranger brings his *korban tahara* and completes his *nezirut* properly.

The mechanism for permitting the shaving, then, is not that the haircut carries positive ritual significance for the stranger. It is purely preventive: by stipulating that his *nezirut* does not begin until after thirty days in this scenario, we ensure that when he does cut his hair at the thirty-day mark, he is not violating a *nezirut* that has not yet commenced.

### Hair Cutting Justification

The question of why cutting the stranger's hair is permissible at all — given that he is not yet completing a *nezirut* — connects to the broader discussion of *hakafas kol harosh* treated in the previous day's *sugya*, and the conclusion there governs here as well.

The deeper question underlying Rabbi Yehoshua's entire scheme remains pointed: if the survivor is, at bottom, simply a *safek tamei nazir*, why does he need to recruit anyone from the marketplace at all? He could simply do what any *safek tamei nazir* does. The advantage Rabbi Yehoshua claims is that at the end of the process each party has brought a complete, undivided *korban tahara*. By the single-person method, the *olat behemah* is brought in the first period as part of the *korban tahara* and the *chatas* and *shelamim* only later, splitting the *korban tahara* across two stages. Under Rabbi Yehoshua's scheme, each *korban tahara* is brought whole. That, at minimum, is the gain — though as we shall see, this advantage came under challenge.

## Ben Zoma Alternative

### Zoma: Who Agrees?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ בֶּן זוֹמָא: וּמִי שׁוֹמֵעַ לוֹ שֶׁיִּדּוֹר כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בְּנָזִיר? אֶלָּא: מֵבִיא חַטַּאת הָעוֹף וְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה, וְאוֹמֵר: אִם טָמֵא הָיִיתִי — הַחַטָּאת מֵחוּבָתִי, וְהָעוֹלָה נְדָבָה. וְאִם טָהוֹר הָיִיתִי — הָעוֹלָה מֵחוּבָתִי, וְהַחַטָּאת מִסָּפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **Ben Zoma said to** Rabbi Yehoshua: **And who will listen to him to vow to be a nazirite corresponding to him?** How can one design a *halakha* on the assumption that a non-nazirite will agree to be a nazirite for a lengthy term? **Rather,** a different procedure is available: After thirty days of naziriteship **he brings a bird sin-offering and an animal burnt-offering, and says: If I was impure, the sin-offering is for my obligation** as an impure nazirite, **and the burnt-offering** is a regular **gift** offering. **And if I was pure, the burnt-offering is for my obligation** as a pure nazirite, **and the sin-offering** is **of uncertain** status.

Ben Zoma registered a pointed practical objection to Rabbi Yehoshua's scheme: *umi she'shomea lo she'yidor kenegdo benazir?* — who in the world is going to agree to become a *nazir* together with this fellow and submit to this entire complicated arrangement of stipulations? One might offer payment, but as a practical matter the mechanism is deeply unwieldy.

### Zoma: Bird & Animal

Ben Zoma therefore proposed: forget the dead partner entirely. The survivor is simply a *safek tamei nazir*. Treat him as such.

### First Period: Besafek

In the first period, the survivor brings a *chatas ha'of* and an *olat behemah*, and declares: if I was *tamei*, the *chatas ha'of* counts toward my obligation and the *olah* is a *nedavah*; if I was *tahor*, the *olah* counts toward my obligation and the *chatas* was brought *besafek* — which is permitted. This is the standard mechanism available to a *safek tamei nazir*: a *chatas ha'of besafek* and an *olah bitnai*.

### Second Period: Complete

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסוֹפֵר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וּמֵבִיא קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה, וְאוֹמֵר: אִם טָמֵא הָיִיתִי — הָעוֹלָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה נְדָבָה, וְזוֹ חוֹבָה. וְאִם טָהוֹר הָיִיתִי — הָעוֹלָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה חוֹבָה, וְזוֹ נְדָבָה, וְזוֹ שְׁאָר קׇרְבָּנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And he counts** another **thirty days, and brings an offering of purity, and says: If I was impure, the first burnt-offering** I brought should be considered **a gift** offering, **and this** one I am bringing now is for my **obligation. And if I was pure, the first burnt-offering** I brought is for my **obligation** as a pure nazirite, **and this** one I am bringing now is **a gift** offering. **And these,** i.e., the sin-offering and peace-offering I am sacrificing now, comprises **the rest of my offerings** that I was obligated to bring earlier.

After thirty days, the survivor brings a full *korban tahara*, declaring: if I was *tamei* earlier, that first *olah* was a *nedavah* and this *olah* now counts toward my obligation of *korban tahara*, together with the *chatas* and *shelamim*. If I was *tahor* earlier, that first *olah* was already part of my obligation, and this *olah* is a *nedavah*, while the *chatas* and *shelamim* complete my *korban tahara*. The thirty-day wait before bringing the final *korban tahara* is required because, since one cannot know whether the person is *tamei*, one must wait out the minimum period of *nezirut tahara* before the *korban tahara* can be valid — one must first permit the possibility that the full period has elapsed.

### Yehoshua: No Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: נִמְצָא זֶה מֵבִיא קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו לַחֲצָאִים. אֲבָל הוֹדוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים לְבֶן זוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua said:** According to your opinion, **it turns out** that **this** nazirite **brings his offerings in halves,** i.e., in stages. If he was pure, he brings his burnt-offering thirty days before the rest of his offerings. **However, the Rabbis agreed with ben Zoma,** and disregarded the concern about splitting up the offerings.

Rabbi Yehoshua's response to Ben Zoma was: *nimtza zeh mevi korbonosav lachatza'in* — according to your method, this person's *korban tahara* is split in two. The *olah* comes in the first period and the *chatas* and *shelamim* come in the second. Under my scheme, each person brings his full *korban tahara* intact and undivided.

### Sages Agree with Zoma

The *mishnah* records that the Sages agreed with Ben Zoma. That objection about splitting the *korban* was apparently not compelling enough to justify the extraordinary complexity of recruiting a stranger. The *Gemara* reinforces this: it asks, in effect, *ve'leisei* — so what? Let him bring his *korban* in two stages. What is really so terrible about that?

### Yehuda: Sharpening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְלַיְיתֵי! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אֶלָּא לְחַדֵּד בָּהּ אֶת הַתַּלְמִידִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna taught that Rabbi Yehoshua countered ben Zoma's opinion by pointing out that his solution would cause the nazirite to bring his offerings in stages. The Gemara asks: What is wrong with that? **And let him bring** the offerings in stages. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Rabbi Yehoshua said** this comment **only to sharpen** the minds of **the students.** He did not really maintain that it is prohibited to act in this manner; rather, he wanted to test his disciples to see if they were aware of the *halakha*.

This leads to the *Gemara*'s explanation: *amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel, lo amar Rabbi Yehoshua ella lechaded bo es hatalmidim* — Rabbi Yehoshua only said this in order to sharpen his students, to provoke them to think and respond. This explanation is itself somewhat puzzling. Normally one might invoke the principle of *lechaded es hatalmidim* for a position that the teacher does not actually hold, floated as a pedagogical provocation. But Rabbi Yehoshua here articulated a full *psak* position with detailed working-out of stipulations. It is not at all clear what the *Gemara* means when it says he said it only to sharpen his students.

### Nachman: Fat Problems

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַאי לֶיעְבֵּיד לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְדַקַּיָּה דְּלָא לֵיסְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said,** in a light-hearted vein: **What will Rabbi Yehoshua do with the intestines** of **his** animals so **that they will not spoil?** If he insists that the offerings of purity must all be brought at the same time, the only way to do so is to wait thirty days after slaughtering the burnt-offering before burning its intestines, which is certainly impractical.

Rav Nachman offered an additional critique from a different angle. The *Gemara* records: *amar Rav Nachman mai avid lei Rabbi Yehoshua ledakei dlo yisru* — what should Rabbi Yehoshua do that his fat should not go off? The precise meaning of this remark is not entirely clear. *Tosafos* notes that the phrase is unexplained (*lo uparash*) and attempts an interpretation not based on a figurative expression. Both *Tosafos* and the *Rosh* understand the remark as referring to the fats of the *korbanos* themselves: because the two *nezirים* are sharing *korbanos* and must perform *tenufah* together, there will be an unavoidable delay in burning the fats and other parts of the offerings, leading to practical problems with those sacrificial portions. Whatever benefit Rabbi Yehoshua's method achieves by keeping each *korban tahara* undivided is offset by the problems that arise from the delays the shared arrangement necessarily introduces. In any case, the *Gemara*'s overall assessment appears to be that Rabbi Yehoshua's elaborate partnership mechanism is unnecessary and that Ben Zoma's straightforward approach is both simpler and equally valid.

## Safek Tamei & Metzora

### Complex Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָזִיר שֶׁהָיָה טָמֵא בְּסָפֵק וּמוּחְלָט בְּסָפֵק — אוֹכֵל בְּקָדָשִׁים אַחַר שִׁשִּׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a nazirite who,** on the first day of his naziriteship, **was impure** from a corpse as a matter of **uncertainty and** was also **a confirmed leper** as a matter of **uncertainty,** i.e., it was uncertain whether or not he had leprosy, how can he fulfill the shaving obligations of a pure nazirite and an impure leper? The problem facing this nazirite is that a leper must shave both when he begins his purification process and at the close of it, one week later. However, a leper may not partake of sacrificial food, but a nazirite may. Therefore, **he may partake of sacrificial food sixty days after** he may have become impure, when the uncertainty with regard to leprosy will have been clarified. He shaves for the first time for his leprosy after thirty days, and for the second time thirty days later, the shaving of the end of the purification process; at which point he brings the offerings of a purified leper and may partake of sacrificial food.

The next *mishnah* introduces a substantially more intricate case. A *nazir* finds himself in a double state of uncertainty: he may have contracted *tumah* from a corpse (*tamei meis*) and he may also be a *metzora*. These are two independent doubts, giving rise to four possible actual statuses: he might be purely *tahor*, he might be only *tamei meis*, he might be only a *metzora*, or he might be both *tamei meis* and a *metzora*.

### Single-Status Scenarios

To appreciate why the combined doubt creates such a protracted ordeal, it helps to trace what each individual certainty would have required. If he were definitively *tahor*, he would complete his thirty-day *nezirut*, shave, and bring his *korban tahara*. If he were definitively *tamei meis*, he would undergo the seven-day purification with the *eifer parah adumah*, shave after seven days, wait thirty days of *nezirut tahara*, shave again, and bring his *korban tahara* — a total of two shavings spread across roughly thirty-seven days. If he were definitively a *metzora*, the *metzora* purification process involves a first shaving (accompanied by the bird ritual outside the *Beis ha'mikdash*) followed by a seven-day wait and a second shaving, after which the *korban tahara* of *nezirut* would follow. If he were both *tamei meis* and a *metzora*, all of these processes would need to run in sequence.

It is important to note the distinction between the bird ritual of *metzora* stage one — the dipping of birds in blood, the sprinkling, and the sending-free — and the *chatas ha'of* brought later. The first is not a *korban* at all; it is a purification ritual performed outside the *Beis ha'mikdash*, comparable in character to the *eifer parah adumah*. The *chatas ha'of* of stage two is a *korban*.

### First Wait: Tahor Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוֹתֶה יַיִן, וּמִטַּמֵּא לַמֵּתִים אַחַר מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **But** he **may drink wine and become impure from the dead,** effectively ending his naziriteship, only **after 120 days.** This is because he might have been a full-fledged leper, which means that his shavings count toward his leprosy, not his naziriteship. Consequently, after the first sixty days he must observe another thirty days of naziriteship and shave again. Even then he has yet to fulfill all his obligations, as he might have been impure from a corpse, which means his shaving after ninety days was for his impurity. He must therefore remain a nazirite for another thirty days, before shaving one final time at the end of 120 days to fulfill his naziriteship obligation.

Given that he does not know which status he is in, the *mishnah* rules that he cannot shave at the outset. The reason is that he might be a *nazir tahor* — a completely pure *nazir* who has no license to shave before thirty days have elapsed. This possibility alone forces every other possible scenario to wait. No shaving can occur before the first thirty days are complete.

### Four Stages Mapped

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁתִּגְלַחַת הַנֶּגַע דּוֹחֶה תִּגְלַחַת הַנָּזִיר בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא וַדַּאי. אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא סָפֵק — אֵינוֹ דּוֹחֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna notes: The reason that he cannot shave for his leprosy after seven days and perform the second shaving of a leper seven days later is **because the shaving of leprosy overrides** the prohibition of **the shaving of a nazirite** only **when his** status as **a leper** is **definite. However, when his** status as **a leper** is **uncertain,** the shaving **does not override** his naziriteship, and therefore he must wait thirty days before each of his shavings for leprosy.

This produces four sequential thirty-day periods, each corresponding to one of the four possible statuses. The logic of why there must be four periods, rather than fewer, follows from what might be happening at each stage.

At stage one, three possibilities converge: he might be *tamei meis* (whose first shaving is now available after the thirty-day wait, since the *tamei meis* process is effectively complete after seven days of purification but had to wait here because of the *nazir tahor* possibility); he might be at *metzora* stage one (the bird ritual); and he might be a *nazir tahor* (completing his *nezirut tahara* of thirty days). Accordingly, at the first shaving he brings: the *tzipporim* (bird ritual) for the possibility of *metzora* stage one, a *chatas ha'of* for the possibility of *tamei meis* (which can be brought *besafek*), and an *olat behemah* for the possibility of *nazir tahor* (which can be brought *bitnai*).

After the first shaving, one might think: surely we can now proceed quickly to the second shaving after seven days, since a *metzora* needs only seven days between shavings. The problem is that if the person was *tamei meis*, his first shaving was only the beginning of his *nezirut tahara*. A *nazir tamei* who shaves must then observe thirty days of *nezirut tahara* before his final shaving. That possibility forces everyone to wait another thirty days before the second shaving.

At the second shaving, the relevant possibilities are: *metzora* stage two (the *chatas ha'of* of *metzora* and the full set of *korban taharah* for that stage) and the *nazir tahor* of thirty days who might now be completing his *nezirut tahara* as a continuation from after the first shaving. And so the pattern continues.

After the second shaving, if the person were a definite *metzora* and nothing else, one might think he is finished. But the rule is that time spent as a *metzora* does not count toward one's *nezirut*. When the *metzora* purification concludes, the *nezirut* resumes from where it left off — or, in certain configurations, begins fresh. This means that after the *metzora* process, a full thirty-day *nezirut tahara* must still be observed. That generates the third stage.

At the third shaving, the *metzora*-plus-*tahor* possibility may conclude its *nezirut tahara*, and the *metzora*-plus-*tamei meis* possibility brings its remaining offerings. After the third shaving, only the *metzora*-plus-*tamei meis* possibility remains, requiring one final thirty-day period of *nezirut tahara*. This produces the fourth and final stage.

### Permitted Acts Timeline

The practical results the *mishnah* enumerates follow from this structure. After sixty days — two complete thirty-day periods — the person is definitively out of *metzora* status. The *metzora* purification process, however lengthy it became due to the uncertainties, is complete by day sixty. Therefore, from day sixty onward, he may eat *kodshim* and engage in activities that *tumah* would prohibit, since his *tumah* from *meis* would have concluded much earlier (after seven days, once the *eifer parah adumah* was applied). After one hundred and twenty days — four complete thirty-day periods — he is definitively out of his *nezirut* and may drink wine and contract *tumah* from the dead.

### Year-Long Nezirut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בִּנְזִירוּת מוּעֶטֶת, אֲבָל בִּנְזִירוּת בַּת שָׁנָה — אוֹכֵל בְּקָדָשִׁים לְאַחַר שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sage **taught: In what** case **is this statement** of the mishna **said? With regard to a short naziriteship** of thirty days. **However, with regard to a naziriteship of a year, he partakes of sacrificial food** only **after two years.** He cannot shave until a year has passed, in case he is not a leper; he may shave the second time only a year later, in case he was impure during the first year and this second year was his naziriteship observed in purity. At this stage he may partake of sacrificial meat, for if he was a full-fledged leper he has shaved twice.

A *braisa* extends this framework to the case of a year-long *nezirut*. The principle is stated concisely: *bameh devarim amurim benezirut muetet* — the sixty-day and one-hundred-twenty-day figures apply to a standard thirty-day *nezirut*. But if the person took on a year-long *nezirut*, then he may eat *kodshim* only after two years and may drink wine and contract *tumah* only after four years.

### Each Stage: 30 Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוֹתֶה יַיִן וּמִיטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים לְאַחַר אַרְבַּע שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Yet if the first two shavings were for his leprosy, he has not shaved for his naziriteship at all, and therefore he must observe a third year of naziriteship and shave. He then must observe another year of naziriteship and shave again, as perhaps his third shaving was for impurity and the fourth will be for his naziriteship in purity. **And** consequently **he may drink wine and become impure from the dead** only **after four years.**

The arithmetic is direct. The entire reason each stage required a thirty-day wait was that at each possible juncture, there might begin a fresh *nezirut tahara* whose minimum duration is thirty days. If the *nezirut tahara* is a year in length, then each of those four stages becomes a year-long wait. The four possible statuses — purely *tahor*, *tamei meis* alone, *metzora* alone, *tamei meis* and *metzora* together — each give rise to a *nezirut tahara* that begins at a different stage, and it is the length of that *nezirut tahara* that determines the interval between shavings. With a year-long *nezirut*, each interval is a year, and four such intervals total four years.

## Four Shavers Korbanos

### Four Shavings Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: וּמְגַלֵּחַ אַרְבַּע תִּגְלָחִיּוֹת. תִּגְלַחַת רִאשׁוֹנָה — מֵבִיא צִפּוֹרִים,
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught with regard to** the *halakha* of the mishna (*Tosefta* 6:1): **And he shaves** with **four** separate acts of **shaving,** each time for its own reason. How so? For his **first shaving,** after the thirty days of an unspecified naziriteship or at the end of the period he vowed to observe naziriteship, he **brings** two **birds** of a leper: One is slaughtered over spring water and the other is sent away (Leviticus 14:4–7).

The *braisa* following the *mishnah* specifies the *korbanos* brought at each of the four shavings in the year-long complex case. The underlying logic at each stage is the same: one brings whatever *korbanos* can be validly offered given the set of statuses that remain possible at that juncture.

### First Shaving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַטַּאת הָעוֹף, וְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה. שְׁנִיָּה — מֵבִיא חַטַּאת הָעוֹף וְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה, שְׁלִישִׁית — מֵבִיא חַטַּאת הָעוֹף וְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה, רְבִיעִית — מֵבִיא קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** he also brings **a bird sin-offering** for his shaving of impure naziriteship, as he may have contracted impurity from a corpse, **and an animal burnt-offering** for his shaving of purity, as he may not have contracted impurity from a corpse. For his **second** shaving he **brings a bird sin-offering and an animal burnt-offering.** For his **third** shaving he again **brings a bird sin-offering and an animal burnt-offering.** And for the **fourth** shaving he **brings an offering of purity.**

At the first shaving — after the initial thirty-day period — three possibilities remain open. The person might be at *metzora* stage one, in which case the *tzipporim* ritual is performed. He might be a *nazir tamei*, in which case a *chatas ha'of* is brought (permitted *besafek*). He might be a *nazir tahor* completing his *nezirut tahara*, in which case an *olat behemah* is brought *bitnai*. Accordingly, the first shaving involves: *tzipporim*, *chatas ha'of*, and *olat behemah*.

### Second Shaving

At the second shaving — after another thirty-day period — the possible statuses are: *metzora* stage two (bringing a *chatas ha'of* as one of the *metzora* stage two *korbanos*) and *nazir tahor* completing a *nezirut tahara* that began after the first shaving (bringing an *olat behemah bitnai*). The second shaving thus involves *chatas ha'of* and *olat behemah*.

### Third Shaving

At the third shaving, the remaining possibilities again include those whose *nezirut tahara* would begin at this stage — specifically the configurations involving *metzora* whose *nezirut* has now resumed and the remaining possibilities not yet concluded. The *korbanos* at the third shaving are again *chatas ha'of* and *olat behemah*, corresponding to the combinations of statuses still in play.

### Fourth Shaving

At the fourth and final shaving, only one possibility remains: the person was both a *metzora* and a *nazir tamei*, and this final thirty-day period was his *nezirut tahara*. At this stage there is no remaining doubt requiring conditional offerings — the full *korban tahara* is brought. This is the *korban tahara* in its complete form, with *olah*, *chatas*, and *shelamim*, concluding the entire four-stage process. The full analysis of precisely which *korbanos* at the third and fourth shavings correspond to which possible statuses will be taken up in the next session.
