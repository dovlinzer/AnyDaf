# Kiddushin 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father vs. Father+Daught…

### Mishna Intro & Reish Lak…

The daf opens in the middle of 43b, approximately fifteen lines from the bottom, at the break in the text. The *mishna* states: *ha-ish mekadesh et bito* — a man may betroth off his daughter when she is a *na'arah*, meaning between the ages of twelve and twelve-and-a-half. Even though she is halachically an adult at this stage, her father retains power over her with respect to marriage. The *gemara* will explore the precise degree to which the *na'arah* herself also possesses some form of independent agency, or whether authority resides exclusively with her father.

The *gemara* opens by citing a *mishna* from Gittin: *na'arah me'orasa, hi ve-aviha mekablin et gitah* — a betrothed *na'arah* or her father may receive her *get*. Thus, following *kiddushin* without *nissu'in*, a *get* may be delivered either to the daughter or to the father.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Principle

Rabbi Yehuda dissents from this ruling: *ein shtei yadayim zachot ke-echas* — two hands cannot act as one. There must be a single controlling entity. Accordingly, Rabbi Yehuda holds that only the father may receive the *get*, not the daughter. The dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *tanna kamma* turns on whether the father and daughter constitute two equally empowered recipients, or whether one is implicitly acting as agent for the other — or whether, more fundamentally, control resides wholly in the father.

### Watching & Protecting Get

A separate *halakha* is introduced: anyone who cannot protect and watch a *get* cannot be divorced. This raises the question of whether the requirement is about the act of receiving the *get* — such that if the recipient cannot guard it, the delivery is ineffective — or whether it is a question about the woman's status, specifically whether she must understand that the divorce means she will no longer be considered married. This has implications for cases involving a *shota* or a *ketanah*, and it is debated among the *Rishonim*, though the *gemara* here does not directly explore it. The focus of the sugya is the earlier question of whether the *na'arah* and her father share equally in the power to receive the *get*.

### Reish Lakish Parallel

Reish Lakish rules: *ke-machlokes le-gerushin kach machlokes le-kiddushin* — the very same dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *tanna kamma* regarding *gerushin* applies equally to *kiddushin*. His reasoning is that once a girl reaches *na'arah* and is technically an adult, she may have equal standing alongside her father in both contexts — the father may act on her behalf and she may act on her own behalf, much as two parties who both hold power of attorney while also retaining the right to sign in their own name.

### Rabbi Yochanan's View

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees sharply: *machlokes le-gerushin aval kiddushin divrei hakol aviha velo hi* — the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *tanna kamma* applies only to *gittin*, but regarding *kiddushin*, everyone agrees that authority belongs exclusively to the father. Rabbi Yochanan will offer a principled reason for this distinction, to which the *gemara* now turns.

---

## Will vs. Delivery Logic

### Consent Distinction

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina explains the reasoning behind Rabbi Yochanan's position. By *kiddushin*, where the process requires *da'at* — agreement, will, and decision — that authority is vested in the father alone: *kiddushin demida'atah aviha velo hi*. By *gerushin*, however, a woman can be divorced *be'al korchah*, against her will; the husband delivers the *get* and the divorce takes effect regardless of her consent. Because *gerushin* does not require any active exercise of will on the receiving side, the question of who receives the *get* is a question not of power but of delivery — who serves as the correct address.

### Gerushin Returns to Fath…

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina articulates the distinction as follows: *gerushin de-machneset atzmah li-reshut aviha, bein hi uvein aviha* — by *gerushin*, the effect of the divorce is that the woman re-enters her father's authority. She is still a *na'arah*, the *kiddushin* was without *nissu'in*, and the *get* returns her to her father's domain. Because her return benefits the father's sphere of control, the *rabanan* regard her as an extension of the father in the receiving act. Whether she or he receives the *get*, the outcome flows back to him.

### Kiddushin Leaves Father

The contrast is stark: *kiddushin de-mafkaat atzmah mi-reshut aviha* — *kiddushin* removes her from her father's domain. It is a transaction that diminishes his authority over her. Therefore *aviha velo hi*: only the father can initiate or consent to that process. The *na'arah* cannot be seen as an extension of her father when the very transaction at hand is one that pulls her away from him. This, according to Rabbi Yochanan, is why the *rabanan* agree with Rabbi Yehuda in the case of *kiddushin*, even though they dispute him in the case of *gittin*.

---

## Ma'amar Exception

### Ma'amar Counterexample

The *gemara* immediately challenges Rabbi Yochanan's framework. *Ve-harei ma'amar* — what about *ma'amar*? When a married woman's husband dies without children, the surviving brothers are obligated in *yibbum*. Biblically, the only operative act is *yibbum* itself; however, the Rabbis instituted a form of rabbinic *kiddushin* called *ma'amar*, by which one of the brothers can, as it were, claim her, thereby preventing the other brothers from performing *yibbum*. This *ma'amar* functions as a *kiddushin* that takes her further away from her father's domain — *de-mafkaat atzmah mi-reshut aviha*, precisely the condition Rabbi Yochanan identified as limiting authority to the father alone.

### Ketanah vs. Na'arah

Yet the *mishna* regarding *ma'amar* distinguishes between a *ketanah* and a *na'arah*: *ketanah min ha-erusin, ein osin bah ma'amar ela mida'at aviha* — if she was a minor who had been betrothed through *erusin* and her husband died, *ma'amar* may only be performed with the father's consent. However, *ve-hana'arah, bein mida'at atzmah bein mida'at aviha* — if she is a *na'arah*, the *ma'amar* is valid whether done with her agreement or with her father's agreement. This appears to directly challenge Rabbi Yochanan: *ma'amar* removes her from her father's domain just as *kiddushin* does, and yet the *mishna* permits it to be effected through the *na'arah* herself, not only through the father.

The *gemara* accordingly suspends the original framing: given this challenge from *ma'amar*, the in-domain / out-of-domain principle cannot serve as the fundamental explanation. *Ela i itamar hachi itamar* — if this tradition was stated, here is the correct version. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina's explanation of Rabbi Yochanan's position must be restated:

*Kiddushin de-mida'atah, aviha velo hi; gerushin de-va'al korchah, bein hi uvein aviha.* The real distinction is not about whether she is moving into or out of the father's domain, but about whether the transaction requires *da'at* — active will and consent. *Kiddushin* requires consent; therefore only the father, who holds the authority, can exercise that consent. *Gerushin* operates *be'al korchah*, against the woman's will if necessary; therefore neither she nor the father is exercising power — they are simply the delivery address, and it makes no difference which of them receives the *get*.

### Ba'al Korcha Resolution

With this reframing, *ma'amar* can be explained. The *gemara* asks: does *ma'amar* not require *da'at* as a form of rabbinic *kiddushin*? The answer: *hatam be-ma'amar de-va'al korchah* — *ma'amar* can be performed against her will, just as *yibbum* itself can be effectuated against her will. The *zikah*, the quasi-*kiddushin* that transfers from the deceased husband to the surviving brothers, means that the relationship has already been partially established; the subsequent acts of *yibbum* or *ma'amar* are merely actualizations of a pre-existing bond. Just as *yibbum* is effective even without consent — though it is not permitted in such circumstances — *ma'amar* similarly operates *be'al korchah*, rendering the receiving party once again simply the delivery address rather than an active agent of will.

### Rabbi vs. Rabbanan

The *gemara* notes that this explanation follows the position of Rabbi rather than the *rabanan*. A *baraita* records: *ha-oseh ma'amar bi-yevamto shelo mida'atah* — one who performs *ma'amar* with his *yevamah* without her consent: *Rabbi omer kanah*, Rabbi holds that it takes effect, while *va-chachamim omrim lo kanah*, the Sages say it does not. Rabbi's reasoning is *gemar mi-bi'ah di-yevamah*: just as *bi'ah* with the *yevamah* takes effect even against her will, so too *ma'amar* takes effect without consent. The *rabanan*, however, hold that *ma'amar* is derived from *kiddushin* — *mah kiddushin mida'atah af hacha nami mida'atah* — and therefore requires consent just as *kiddushin* does. Though the *halakha* follows the *rabanan*, the *baraita* allows the *gemara* to explain the *mishna* that permits *ma'amar* through the *na'arah* as following Rabbi's view.

---

## Mishna Reconciliatio (II)

### Mishna Conclusion

The *gemara* now notes that this entire framework actually works well, based on the closing phrase of that *mishna*: *midekatani seifa ma she-ein ken be-kiddushin* — the *mishna* itself concludes by contrasting *ma'amar* with *kiddushin*, noting that what applies to *ma'amar* does not apply to *kiddushin*. This closing phrase powerfully corroborates Rabbi Yochanan's position: *kiddushin* is different, and only the father controls it. *Ma'amar* is the exception, and the *mishna* itself flags it as such.

This raises a sharp challenge to Reish Lakish. If *kiddushin* belongs only to the father and *ma'amar* is handled differently, that structure implies that the *rabanan* — who in Reish Lakish's reading would say both she and the father have equal say in *kiddushin* — should agree by *kiddushin* that it can be effected through the daughter. Yet the *mishna* implies otherwise. The *gemara* asks: *neima tehvei tiyufta de-Reish Lakish* — should we declare this a refutation of Reish Lakish?

Reish Lakish responds: *ha mani Rabbi Yehuda hi* — this *mishna* follows Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yehuda is the one who says *ein shtei yadayim zachot ke-achat*; it is always only the father, whether for *gittin* or for *kiddushin*. Under Rabbi Yehuda's view, even *gittin* goes only to the father, so *kiddushin* naturally does as well. The *mishna*'s contrast between *ma'amar* and *kiddushin* is therefore a contrast within Rabbi Yehuda's framework where both *gittin* and *kiddushin* are exclusively the father's domain, with *ma'amar* as the lone exception.

This, however, generates a new question. If Reish Lakish attributes the *mishna* to Rabbi Yehuda, then under Rabbi Yehuda's view, both *kiddushin* and *gittin* go only to the father. Why then does *ma'amar* permit the daughter? The *gemara* asks further: if it is Rabbi Yehuda's position, the *mishna* should have contrasted *ma'amar* not only with *kiddushin* but also with *gerushin* — *ma she-ein ken be-kiddushin, ma she-ein ken be-gerushin miba'ei lei*. The *gemara* responds: *in hachi nami* — indeed that is so. The reason the *mishna* phrases it as a contrast to *kiddushin* specifically is because *ma'amar* resembles *kiddushin*: *aidi de-tana ma'amar de-dami le-kiddushin, tani nami ma she-ein ken be-kiddushin* — since *ma'amar* is a form of *kiddushin*, the *mishna* contrasts it with *kiddushin*. But the real implication is that *ma'amar* is the exception to the rule that applies to both *kiddushin* and *gittin*.

### Zekuka V'Omedet

This leads to the question: why does *ma'amar* allow the *na'arah* herself to be the recipient even according to Rabbi Yehuda? The *gemara* answers: *ho'il ve-zekuka ve-omedet* — because she is already bound and standing. The *zikah*, the quasi-*kiddushin* that attached to her upon her husband's death, means that the subsequent step of *ma'amar* is simply an actualization of a pre-existing bond. It is uniquely easy for this relationship to take effect, and therefore a transaction directed at the *na'arah* herself is sufficient to actualize it. Under Rabbi Yehuda's system, this makes *ma'amar* a total exception: both *gittin* and *kiddushin* go exclusively to the father, but *ma'amar* — because of *zekuka ve-omedet* — can go to the daughter herself.

---

## Hashta Framework

### Rabbi Yochanan Confirmed

*Ha-shta de-asit le-hachi* — now that we have established that *ma'amar* is exceptional because of *zekuka ve-omedet*, the challenge that *ma'amar* posed to Rabbi Yochanan dissolves as well. The *gemara* states: *Rabbi Yochanan nami lo tikshei lach me-ikara* — you need not have troubled Rabbi Yochanan from the outset with the *ma'amar* objection. Whether one employs the original explanation of into-the-domain versus out-of-the-domain, or the revised explanation of *be'al korchah* versus *mida'atah*, *ma'amar* stands apart from both frameworks by virtue of *zekuka ve-omedet*: *shani ma'amar ho'il ve-zekuka ve-omedet*. The upshot is that Rabbi Yochanan's core position — that by *kiddushin* all the *rabanan* agree authority is the father's alone, with *gittin* forming an exception because it operates *be'al korchah* — remains intact.

To summarize the two positions as they now stand: Reish Lakish holds that for the *rabanan*, the *na'arah* and her father are equally empowered in all these contexts — both can receive a *get*, both can effect *kiddushin*, both can receive a *ma'amar*. The *mishna* limiting power to the father alone reflects only Rabbi Yehuda's stricter view. Rabbi Yochanan holds that for the *rabanan*, the father is the primary and essentially exclusive locus of authority; *gittin* forms an exception because delivery of a *get* operates *be'al korchah*, rendering the recipient merely a mailbox; and *ma'amar* is further explained through *zekuka ve-omedet*.

---

## Mishna Reconciliatio (II)

### Tanna Identification

With Rabbi Yochanan's position now confirmed, the *gemara* turns to the attribution of the later *mishna* in the *perek* — the one concerning a man who says to a woman: *hitkadshi li be-tamra zu, hitkadshi li be-zo* — "be betrothed to me with this date, be betrothed to me with this one." When the word "be betrothed" is repeated each time, each date constitutes a separate act of *kiddushin*, and validity depends on whether any individual date is worth a *perutah*. If, however, he says "be betrothed to me with this and this and this," omitting the repeated verb, the acts are treated as one combined transaction, and even if none individually is worth a *perutah*, their combined value suffices.

The *gemara* asks: *man tana hitkadshi hitkadshi* — whose view is this, that the *mishna* hinges on whether the verb is repeated? Rava answers: *Rabbi Shimon hi* — it is Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Shimon holds by *shevuot* that a person is only liable separately for each one if he says "I swear" for each one individually — *ad she-yomar shvuah le-chol echad ve-echad*. Our *mishna* similarly requires the repeated verb to create separate acts. This is not the view of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds by *shevuot* that even without repeating the word *shevuah*, each oath target constitutes a separate liability — *shvuah lo lecha, lo lecha, lo lecha, chayav al kol achat ve-achat*. If Rabbi Yehuda were the *tanna* of this *mishna*, then even without repeating the verb "be betrothed," each date would be a separate act of *kiddushin*, which contradicts our *mishna*.

A *baraita* confirms this: *zeh ha-klal, klal eino chayav ela achat; perat, chayav al kol achat ve-achat* — this is Rabbi Meir's formulation. Rabbi Yehuda explicitly says that even a general construction yields separate liabilities for each. Rabbi Shimon requires the word *shvuah* to be repeated for each one. The *gemara* concludes: the later *mishna* is Rabbi Shimon's. This means Reish Lakish cannot attribute our first *mishna* — which limits *kiddushin* authority to the father — to Rabbi Yehuda, since the second *mishna* in the same *perek* is demonstrably not Rabbi Yehuda. The *gemara* therefore resolves: *kula Rabbi Shimon hi, u-vi-shlichus savar lah ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — the entire *perek* follows Rabbi Shimon, but on the specific question of who controls *kiddushin* and *gittin*, Rabbi Shimon happens to agree with Rabbi Yehuda's position. The two debates — about repeated verbs in betrothal language and about who controls the *na'arah*'s *kiddushin* — are entirely independent, and the fact that Rabbi Shimon agrees with Rabbi Yehuda on the latter does not require them to share any deeper framework.

---

## First Mishna Test

### Father Alone Principle

The *gemara* now subjects the competing positions to a test from the very first *mishna* of the *perek*: *ish mekadesh et bito keshe-hi na'arah, bo u-vishucho* — a man can betroth off his daughter when she is a *na'arah*, whether he does so himself or through his *shaliach*. The *mishna* states *bo u-vishucho in*, meaning yes to him and his *shaliach*, implying *bo u-vishucho lo* — not her and not her *shaliach*. This strongly implies that only the father has authority in *kiddushin*, not the *na'arah* herself. This aligns perfectly with Rabbi Yochanan's position.

### Reish Lakish Defense

The *gemara* asks: *ve-tiyuvta de-Reish Lakish* — is this a refutation of Reish Lakish? Reish Lakish responds once again: *ha nami Rabbi Yehuda hi* — this too follows Rabbi Yehuda. Under the *rabanan*, whom Reish Lakish reads as granting equal power to both father and daughter in both *kiddushin* and *gittin*, the *na'arah* could indeed make *kiddushin* on her own. The *mishna* that limits this to the father alone reflects only Rabbi Yehuda's stricter position. The *gemara* then challenges this attribution, pointing out that *u-mi matzit mokmat la ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — can you really attribute our *mishna* to Rabbi Yehuda? The later *mishna* in the same *perek* about betrothing with repeated dates is demonstrably Rabbi Shimon, not Rabbi Yehuda, as established above. If the *stam* voice of the *mishna* is Rabbi Shimon elsewhere in the *perek*, one cannot attribute the opening *mishna* to Rabbi Yehuda. The *gemara* concludes as described: *kula Rabbi Shimon hi*, but Rabbi Shimon holds like Rabbi Yehuda on the specific question of the *na'arah*'s authority.

---

## Beit Midrash Consensus

### Rav Avin's Report

The *gemara* narrates a brief episode that encapsulates how the *beit midrash* community resolved this debate. One day Rav Assi did not attend the *beit midrash*. He encountered Rabbi Zeira and asked him for the substance of that day's discussion. Rabbi Zeira replied that he himself had also not attended, but that Rabbi Avin had gone, and Rabbi Avin reported: *chavrusah kulah ke-Rav Yochanan* — the entire group came together in agreement with Rabbi Yochanan. By *gittin*, perhaps the *na'arah* can receive the *get* — because she is *be'al korchah*, merely a mailbox. But by *kiddushin*, only the father; the *na'arah* has no authority.

### Screaming Crane

*Ve-tzovach Reish Lakish ke-kruchiya* — and Reish Lakish shrieked like a crane in protest. He cited the *pasuk* that equates divorce and remarriage — *ve-yatza ve-hayeta* — and argued that if the *na'arah* can receive the *get* in divorce, she should equally be able to effect *kiddushin* in marriage. *Ve-leika de-ashgach bei* — and no one paid attention to him. The consensus held with Rabbi Yochanan.

It is worth noting that this phrase — *tzovach ke-kruchiya ve-leit de-ashgach bei* — became a recurring expression among the *Rishonim* and later *poskim* when describing a situation in which a scholar protests a prevailing practice and is simply ignored.

### Rav Avin Identity

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds a caveat: *ana lo Rav Avin bar Rebbe Chiya ve-lo Rav Avin bar Kahana, ella Rav Avin stam* — he does not know whether the reporting Rav Avin was Rav Avin the son of Rabbi Chiya or Rav Avin the son of Kahana; he only knows it was a Rav Avin. The *gemara* asks: *le-mai nafka mina* — what practical difference does the identity make? *Le-mirmi didei adidei* — it matters for purposes of identifying contradictions between his statements. If one wishes to challenge his testimony by citing something he said elsewhere, the precise identity of the speaker must be known.

## Shaliach Question

### Hand vs. Courtyard

The *gemara* now poses a new question, which Rava directs to Rav Nachman: *na'arah mahu she-ta'aseh shaliach lekabbel gitah miyad ba'alah* — may a *na'arah* appoint a *shaliach* to receive her *get* from her husband's hand? We have established that according to Rabbi Yochanan, a *na'arah* can at least receive her own *get* — the *rabanan* permit delivery to her even if only because she functions as a valid address. But can she go further and delegate that receptive function to a *shaliach* of her own choosing?

Rava frames the question through a conceptual dichotomy: *keyad aviha damya o ke-chatzer aviha damya* — is she like her father's hand, or like her father's *chatzer*? A *chatzer* — a courtyard — is property: things placed within it belong to its owner through the passive mechanics of *kinyan chatzer*, but it exercises no independent function. A hand, in contrast, is an extension of a person's active agency. If she is like her father's hand, then — *mah yado mashvei shaliach, af hi nami mashveia shaliach* — just as the father's hand represents his power and he can therefore appoint a *shaliach*, so too she could appoint a *shaliach*. If she is merely like his *chatzer*, she has no active function at all and could not appoint a *shaliach*.

### Until Reaches Hand

The *gemara* notes that the very premise of *chatzer* as a complete analogy already strains credulity. After all, the *gemara* states: *ve-ad de-mata gita le-yadah lo migarshana* — until the *get* reaches her hand, she is not divorced. This implies that her hand is already the operative instrument for receiving the *get*; calling her merely a passive *chatzer* seems incompatible with this.

### Slave Watchman Cases

Rava himself addresses the question by analogy from a case involving a slave. If someone wrote a *get* and placed it in the hand of his wife's slave: if the slave is sleeping and she is watching over him, *harei zeh get* — it is a valid *get*, because the slave functions like a *chatzer mishta'meret*, a guarded courtyard. When property is secured within a guarded space, *kinyan* is effected through the owner of the space. But *na'ur, eino get* — if the slave is awake, it is not a valid *get*. An awake slave can walk away, and is therefore not truly under the woman's control; he cannot serve as her *chatzer mishta'meret*.

The *gemara* draws the implication: if we were to treat the *na'arah* as literally her father's *chatzer*, then when the *get* is delivered to her hand, that should also fail — *dahavei lei chatzer hamishtameret shelo le-da'at aviha* — because she is independently alert and in control of what is on her person, not passively guarded by the father. A *chatzer* whose occupant acts autonomously is not a valid *chatzer mishta'meret*. Therefore, the very fact that delivery to her hand is concededly valid proves she is not merely her father's *chatzer*.

### Hand Resolution

The *gemara* concludes: *ela peshita de-keyad aviha damya* — it is clear that she is like her father's hand, an extension of his person. But the question remains whether that extension is powerful enough that she can make a *shaliach*. Rav Nachman rules: *eina osa shaliach* — she cannot appoint a *shaliach*. She is her father's hand — she can receive — but she does not possess independent power sufficient to delegate further.

### Baraita Challenge

The *gemara* challenges this ruling from a *baraita* from Gittin: *ketanah she-amrah hitkabel li giti eino get ad she-yagi'a get le-yadah* — if a minor says "receive my *get* for me," it does not work until the *get* actually reaches her own hand, because she cannot appoint a *shaliach*. However, *ha-na'arah harei zeh get* — if she is a *na'arah*, it implies that she can appoint a *shaliach* and the *get* is valid when the *shaliach* receives it. This appears to directly contradict Rav Nachman's ruling.

### Final Shaliach Rule

The *gemara* resolves the challenge: *hacha be-mai askinan, be-she-ein lah av* — this *baraita* is discussing a case where the *na'arah* has no father. When there is no father, the *na'arah* is her own agent and has full authority over herself; naturally she may appoint a *shaliach*. But this cannot conclude the matter, because the *baraita*'s continuation describes a father who says "go receive a *get* for my daughter," suggesting that a father is present.

The *gemara* therefore invokes *chisorei mechasra ve-hachi katani* — the *baraita* is incomplete as stated and must be reconstructed as follows: *ketanah she-amra hitkabel li giti eino get ad she-yagi'a get le-yadah* — the *ketanah* who says "receive my *get*" cannot make a *shaliach*, and it is not effective until the *get* reaches her own hand. *Ha-na'arah harei zeh get* — the *na'arah* can do so; *bameh devarim amurim, ke-she-ein lah av* — this applies when there is no father. *Aval yesh lah av, ve-amar aviha tzei ve-kabel get le-vitti, im ratza ba'al la-chazor bo, lo yachzor* — but if there is a father and the father appoints a *shaliach* to receive the *get* for his daughter, that *shaliach* is effective and the husband cannot retract. The implied corollary is that if there is a father, only he can make a *shaliach*, not the *na'arah*.

The *gemara* thus closes with a coherent ruling that aligns with Rabbi Yochanan's position throughout: fundamentally, all authority resides with the father. By *gittin*, the *rabanan* permit delivery of the *get* to the *na'arah* herself because she functions as *yad aviha*, an extension of her father's person — and it is the valid address for delivery. But she cannot appoint a *shaliach*, because that would require independent power she does not possess. And since the entire basis for her receiving the *get* is not her own authority but her status as her father's extended person operating *be'al korchah*, this also suggests — as the *gemara* notes in passing — that delivery of a *get* to a *ketanah* even when a father is present might similarly be valid, a question left open by the earlier discussion.
