# Kiddushin 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Methods Overview

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאַף בִּשְׁטָר? וְדִין הוּא: וּמָה כֶּסֶף, שֶׁאֵין מוֹצִיא – מַכְנִיס, שְׁטָר, שֶׁמּוֹצִיא – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמַּכְנִיס?
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived that a woman can be acquired **even by** means of **a document?** The Gemara answers: This **is a logical derivation,** made by an *a fortiori* inference: **And if money, which does not release** a woman from her husband, nevertheless can **bring** her **into** a husband's domain by betrothal, **is it not logical that a document, which releases** a woman from her husband in the form of a bill of divorce, **can bring** her **into** a husband's domain by betrothal?

We begin toward the bottom of 4b, carrying into 5a. The daf finds us already in the midst of a larger discussion: what are the *limudim* — the derivations — for each of the three different ways of effecting *kiddushin*? Those three ways are *kesef*, *shtar*, and *biah*. The *gemara* has already worked through most of the proofs for *kiddushei kesef* and has begun its treatment of *kiddushei biah*.

### Mishnah vs. Gemara Order

One immediately notices a structural peculiarity: the order of derivations in the *gemara* differs from the order presented in the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* proceeds *kesef*, *shtar*, *biah*; the *gemara* treats *shtar* last. We will encounter this reordering directly as we work through the *sugyot* today.

## Kiddushei Biah

### Uv'alah Drasha

The *gemara* cites the *pasuk*: *ki yikach ish isha uv'alah* — "when a man takes a wife and *ba'alah*," where *uv'alah* functions as an active verb, meaning he becomes her *ba'al*, or in a more graphic sense, he consummates the relationship. From this word the *gemara* derives: *uv'alah melamed sheniknet b'biah* — the word *uv'alah* teaches that the *kinyan* of *kiddushin* can be effected through a sexual act.

### Kiddushin vs. Nissuin

It is worth noting at this juncture that several *dappim* later the *gemara* will raise a crucial question: *biah kiddushin oseh o biah nissuin oseh?* — does that initial act of *biah* effect both stages of marriage simultaneously, jumping across both *kiddushin* and *nissuin*, or does it effect only *kiddushin*, leaving *nissuin* to be completed through a separate step? We register this question here, as it bears on everything that follows.

### Kal Vachomer: Yevamah

But the *gemara* immediately asks: is the word *uv'alah* even necessary? Could we not derive *kiddushei biah* through *kal vachomer*? The *gemara* proposes: *v'halo din hu* — is this not a logical inference? A *yevamah* cannot be acquired through *kesef* and yet she is acquired through *biah* (the act of *yibbum* is effected exclusively through *biah*). Surely then, in a normal marriage, where we already know *kesef* works, *biah* should work *kal vachomer* — money being more broadly applicable demonstrates that the avenue is even wider, and *biah* should follow.

### Three Marriage Cases

The *gemara* throughout this *sugya* continually compares and contrasts three distinct cases: *yibbum*, *amah ivriyah*, and normal marriage. *Yibbum* is the situation where a man's brother has died childless; the surviving brother and the widow are already in a particular legal relationship — they must either consummate the *yibbum* or dissolve it through *chalitzah*. The *amah ivriyah* — a minor girl indentured by her father — occupies an intermediate position: it is primarily a labor arrangement, though as we shall see, the *gemara* insists it is not a marital relationship, while *tosafot* will complicate that picture considerably. These three cases interact with the three modes of *kiddushin* and with the comparison between *kiddushin* and *gerushin* — the *hekesh havayah l'yetziah* — and sorting out which comparisons are primary and which secondary goes to the core question of what the institution of *kiddushin* actually is and how it is effected.

### Amah Ivriyah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמָה הָעִבְרִיָּה תּוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁנִּקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף וְאֵין נִקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה. מָה לְאָמָה הָעִבְרִיָּה, שֶׁאֵין קִנְיָנָהּ לְשׁוּם אִישׁוּת, תֹּאמַר בְּזוֹ, שֶׁקִּנְיָנָהּ לְשׁוּם אִישׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: The case of **a Hebrew maidservant proves** otherwise, **as she is acquired through money and** yet she **cannot be acquired through intercourse.** The Gemara dismisses this refutation: **What** is distinct about **a Hebrew maidservant** is **that her acquisition is not for the sake of marriage,** as she is acquired as a servant. **Will you say** the same **with regard to this** woman, **whose acquisition is for the sake of marriage?**

The *kal vachomer* from *yevamah* is challenged: *amah ivriyah tochiach* — the case of the indentured servant refutes the inference. The *amah ivriyah* can be acquired through *kesef* and yet cannot be acquired through *biah*: the transfer of her labor rights cannot be effected through a sexual act. The *kal vachomer* thus collapses.

The *gemara* anticipates a reply: *mah l'amah ha'ivriyah she'ein kinyanah leshum ishut* — the *amah ivriyah* is not a marital relationship; perhaps we should limit her counter-example to non-marital contexts and allow the inference to proceed for relationships that are *leshum ishut*. The *gemara* responds that this is precisely why we need the *pasuk*: *talmud lomar uv'alah* — the word *uv'alah* is required to teach that a normal marriage can be effected through *biah*, because the *kal vachomer* cannot carry us there on its own.

### Rav Ashi Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵהֵיכָא קָא מַיְיתֵית לַהּ – מִיְּבָמָה. מָה לִיבָמָה, שֶׁכֵּן זְקוּקָה וְעוֹמֶדֶת, תֹּאמַר בָּזוֹ, שֶׁאֵין זְקוּקָה וְעוֹמֶדֶת. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּבְעָלָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** One could say: **What** is unique about **a *yevama*** is that she **is bound** to the *yavam* **and stands** waiting for him to act, and for this reason sexual intercourse is sufficient to render her his wife. **Will you say** the same **with regard to this** woman, **who is not bound and** is not **standing** waiting for anyone? It is therefore possible that intercourse is not enough to acquire a woman in ordinary circumstances. Consequently, the *a fortiori* inference does not bear close scrutiny. For this reason an additional proof is required, that **the verse states: "And engages in sexual intercourse with her."**

But why can the *kal vachomer* not carry us there? *Rav Ashi* explains: the difficulty lies not only with the *amah ivriyah* counterexample but with the *yevamah* premise itself. *Mah li'yevamah sheken zekukah ve'omedet* — what makes *yibbum* distinctive is the *zikah*, the pre-existing bond that forms between the *yavam* and the *yevamah* from the moment of the brother's death. The *yevamah* is already in a state of *zekukah ve'omedet* — she stands in a legal relationship with the *yavam* before any act of *biah* occurs. That antecedent *zikah* sets the stage and enables *biah* to consummate what is already, in some sense, a developing relationship. In normal marriage there is no such pre-existing *zikah*; the two parties are strangers in the legal sense. Therefore the *kal vachomer* fails from both ends: the *yevamah* premise is unique in its *zikah*, and the *amah ivriyah* counterexample undermines any broader application. All three cases — *yibbum*, *amah ivriyah*, and normal marriage — are legally distinct from one another. Hence: *talmud lomar uv'alah*.

It is worth noting the background *machloket tanna'im* of *yesh zikah o ein zikah*, and the question of how substantial that *zikah* really is — whether according to some *tanna'im*, including Rabbi Akiva, the *zikah* is comparable in force to *kiddushin* itself, such that *yibbum* brings the relationship from a quasi-*mekudeshet* state to completion of *nissuin*. That discussion lies in the background of *Rav Ashi*'s resolution.

### Tosafot Ye'ud

*Tosafot*, commenting toward the bottom of 4b, revisits the *amah ivriyah* and complicates the *gemara*'s categorical dismissal of her relationship as non-marital. One of the reserved rights of the master over an *amah ivriyah* is *ye'ud*: he may designate her to marry himself or to marry his son. In this arrangement, the *kesef kiddushin* is retroactively absorbed into the original purchase price — the money paid at the time of the sale serves double duty. This *ye'ud* is unmistakably a marital dimension of the *amah ivriyah*'s status. *Tosafot* therefore notes that while the *gemara* has categorically rejected the notion of *amah ivriyah she'einah leshum ishut*, the institution of *ye'ud* introduces genuine marital elements into the picture, and there are several additional points *tosafot* develops on this question.

## Kiddushei Shtar

### Shtar Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְכֶסֶף, שֶׁכֵּן פּוֹדִים בּוֹ הֶקְדֵּשׁ וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי. תֹּאמַר שְׁטָר, שֶׁאֵין פּוֹדִים בּוֹ הֶקְדֵּשׁ וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנָתַן הַכֶּסֶף וְקָם לוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this claim: **What** is unique about **money** is that it can effect acquisition in many contexts, **as one can redeem with it consecrated** property **and second tithe. Will you say** that the same also applies to **a document, with which one cannot redeem consecrated** property **and second tithe?** The proof that one may redeem consecrated property only by means of money is **that it is written: "And he will give the money and it will be assured to him"** (see Leviticus 27:19). Consequently, this *halakha* cannot be derived by logical reasoning.

Moving to 5a, the *gemara* turns to *kiddushei shtar*, introducing the question with the word *af* — "also": *u'minayin she'af b'shtar?* — from where do we know that *kiddushin* can be effected by giving a *shtar*, a document? The use of *af* signals that *shtar* is perhaps the least intuitive of the three methods, and we will see why throughout this *sugya*. The *gemara* follows the same structural template we have observed throughout: first propose a *kal vachomer*, then reject it, then introduce an independent *limud*. Only once all three independent *limudim* have been established will the *gemara* attempt to derive each method from the combination of the other two — the *tzad hashaveh* argument — and that discussion will unfold on *amud bet*. The underlying operating principle of the entire *sugya* is that it would be preferable to learn through *kal vachomer*, which requires no expenditure of *pesukim*, and resort to *drashot* only when necessary.

### Kal Vachomer: Kesef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִין הוּא: וּמָה כֶּסֶף, שֶׁאֵין מוֹצִיא – מַכְנִיס, שְׁטָר, שֶׁמּוֹצִיא – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמַּכְנִיס?
>
> **Translation:** This **is a logical derivation,** made by an *a fortiori* inference: **And if money, which does not release** a woman from her husband, nevertheless can **bring** her **into** a husband's domain by betrothal, **is it not logical that a document, which releases** a woman from her husband in the form of a bill of divorce, **can bring** her **into** a husband's domain by betrothal?

The proposed *kal vachomer*: *mah kesef she'ein motzi machnis* — money, which cannot be used to effect divorce, can nonetheless be used to enter into marriage. A *get* can only be a document; one cannot give *kesef* for divorce. *Shtar*, by contrast, can end a marriage — it is the very instrument of divorce. If money, which lacks the power to dissolve, can create the marital bond, surely *shtar*, which has the power to dissolve, should *kal vachomer* be able to create it as well?

This is a striking argument, and worth pausing over. The *Rashba* frames it in terms of *koach deheteira adif*: the power required to create a *heter* where there was none is far greater than the power to create a prohibition. One can generate *issurim* relatively easily — through *nedarim*, through the principle of *shavei anafshei kachaticha de'issura*, and so forth. But to permit what is forbidden is impossible absent a genuine legal mechanism. The power of a *get* to accomplish exactly that — to permit a woman who would otherwise be a married woman to remarry — is extraordinary. *Shatarot* generally carry enormous legal weight in *halacha*: *Rabbi Yochanan* holds that witnesses who sign a *shtar* are treated as if their testimony had already been examined by a *beit din*, without requiring the usual *drisha ve'chakira*. So the suggestion that *shtar's* power to dissolve should yield *kal vachomer* a power to create is a serious argument, even if it will ultimately be rejected.

### Kesef Uniqueness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיָצְאָה״ ״וְהָיְתָה״ – מַקִּישׁ הֲוָיָה לִיצִיאָה: מָה יְצִיאָה בִּשְׁטָר – אַף הֲוָיָה נָמֵי בִּשְׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "And she leaves** his house and goes **and becomes** another man's wife" (Deuteronomy 24:2). This verse **juxtaposes becoming,** i.e., betrothal, **to leaving,** i.e., divorce. **Just as leaving** is performed **through a document,** i.e., a bill of divorce, **so too, becoming** can be performed **through a document.**

The *kal vachomer* fails because *kesef* has a unique quality that *shtar* lacks: *mah l'kesef sheken podim bo hekdesh u'ma'aser sheni* — money can be used for *pidyon*, to transfer the sanctified status of *ma'aser sheni* or *hekdesh* onto the coins themselves. *Ma'aser sheni*, which normally must be brought to Jerusalem and consumed there, can be redeemed onto a silver coin — *v'natan hakesef v'kam lo* — allowing the money to be brought instead and used to purchase food in Jerusalem. *Hekdesh* similarly can be redeemed. No *shtar* can accomplish this. *Rashi* raises the *hava amina*: what kind of *shtar* would one have imagined using for *ma'aser sheni*? Perhaps a *shtar chov* — a lien instrument, where one writes "I am obligated to bring five silver coins to Jerusalem for *ma'aser sheni*." The *Rashba*, however, objects strongly: a *shtar kiddushin* and a *get* are not *shtarot chov*. They are *shtarot kinyan* — documents that describe and effect the transaction directly, as when one writes *harei perot elu peduyim b'shtar zo*. The *Rashba*'s point is that the *hava amina* must be understood in terms of the correct category of *shtar*.

The *pidyon* comparison is also interesting in light of the earlier equation between *kiddushin* and *hekdesh* noted on 2b: *de'assar lah achulei alma kehekdesh* — the very word *kiddushin* draws on the parallel between marital sanctity and consecrated property, and the transfer of status that *kiddushin* effects is in some ways analogous to the transfer accomplished through *pidyon*. But the parallel remains imperfect, which is why the *kal vachomer* from *kesef* ultimately fails.

### Hekesh Havaya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיָצְאָה״ ״וְהָיְתָה״ – מַקִּישׁ הֲוָיָה לִיצִיאָה: מָה יְצִיאָה בִּשְׁטָר – אַף הֲוָיָה נָמֵי בִּשְׁטָר. וְאַקֵּישׁ נָמֵי, יְצִיאָה לַהֲוָיָה: מָה הֲוָיָה בַּכֶּסֶף – אַף יְצִיאָה בְּכֶסֶף!
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "And she leaves** his house and goes **and becomes** another man's wife" (Deuteronomy 24:2). This verse **juxtaposes becoming,** i.e., betrothal, **to leaving,** i.e., divorce. **Just as leaving** is performed **through a document,** i.e., a bill of divorce, **so too, becoming** can be performed **through a document.** The Gemara asks: **And let one also juxtapose leaving to becoming,** in the opposite direction: **Just as becoming is** performed **through money, so too, leaving** can be effected **through money.**

Having rejected the *kal vachomer*, the *gemara* turns to an independent *limud*. The *pasuk* in the context of divorce reads: *v'yatza v'hayta l'ish acher* — she leaves and she can become the wife of another man. The *gemara* derives: *makish havayah l'yetziah* — comparing entry into marriage (*havayah*) to exit from marriage (*yetziah*). *Mah yetziah b'shtar af havayah nami b'shtar* — just as a woman is divorced through a *get*, so too she can be married through a *shtar kiddushin*. This is the *hekesh havayah l'yetziah*, and unlike the *kal vachomer*, it is a genuine *hekesh* drawn from the language of *mikra*.

### Sanigor Katigor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: יֹאמְרוּ: כֶּסֶף מַכְנִיס, כֶּסֶף מוֹצִיא, סָנֵיגוֹר יֵעָשֶׂה קָטֵיגוֹר? אִי הָכִי, שְׁטָר נָמֵי יֹאמְרוּ: שְׁטָר מוֹצִיא, שְׁטָר מַכְנִיס, קָטֵיגוֹר יֵעָשֶׂה סָנֵיגוֹר?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says:** If so, people **will say: Money brings in and money releases;** if so, can **an advocate [*saneigor*] become a prosecutor [*kateigor*]?** It is improper for the same mode that draws man and woman together to be used for the termination of their relationship. The Gemara asks: **If so,** then with regard to **a document too, they will say: A document brings in and a document releases;** if so, can **a prosecutor become an advocate?**

A natural question follows: could we not apply the *hekesh* in the reverse direction — *v'ikish nami yetziah l'havayah* — and derive from *kiddushei kesef* that divorce could also be accomplished through *kesef*? *Mah havayah b'kesef af yetziah b'kesef* — just as one enters marriage with money, perhaps one could also exit marriage with money?

*Abaye* rejects this: *yomru kesef machnis kesef motzi* — people would say the same mechanism that creates the marriage dissolves it. This is *sanigor ya'aseh katigor* — the defense attorney has become the prosecuting attorney. The instrument by which a woman is bound has become the instrument by which she is released. It is worth noting that *kiddushei kesef* is the only method of *kiddushin* actually practiced in the time of *Chazal*, which is precisely why this concern arises: people would readily associate the two acts if both involved giving money.

### Distinct Shtar Documents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִילֵּי דְּהַאי שְׁטָרָא לְחוֹד וּמִילֵּי דְּהַאי שְׁטָרָא לְחוֹד. הָכָא נָמֵי, הַאי כַּסְפָּא לְחוֹד וְהַאי כַּסְפָּא לְחוֹד! טִיבְעָא מִיהָא חַד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The words of this** betrothal **document are discrete,** i.e., distinct, **and the words of this** divorce **document are discrete,** i.e., the two documents differ in content. The Gemara asks: **Here too, this money is discrete and this money is discrete,** as one coin is for the betrothal and a different coin is used for the divorce. The Gemara answers: **In any event, the coin** itself **is one,** i.e., there is no noticeable difference between the coin used for betrothal and one that would be used for divorce. The same cannot be said with regard to documents, as particular texts serve specific purposes, and the same document could not be used for both betrothal and divorce.

The *gemara* then raises an analogous concern about *shtar*: does not the *hekesh* cut both ways? If we derive *kiddushei shtar* from the fact that *yetziah* is accomplished through *shtar*, have we not simultaneously derived that *yetziah* could also be accomplished through the *shtar* used in *kiddushin* — *katigor ya'aseh sanigor*? The *gemara* dismisses this concern: *milei d'hai shtar l'chud u'milei d'hai shtar l'chud* — these are two entirely different *shtarot*. The words written in a *get* are unmistakably those of a *get*; the words written in a *shtar kiddushin* are unmistakably those of *kiddushin*. No one could confuse them. Each document is a unique and explicitly identifiable object. The *hekesh* therefore derives *kiddushei shtar* without creating any confusion about the instruments of divorce and marriage.

### Fungibility Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* טִיבְעָא מִיהָא חַד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:* **In any event, the coin** itself **is one,** i.e., there is no noticeable difference between the coin used for betrothal and one that would be used for divorce.

This resolution raises a further question: why can we not apply the same logic to *kesef* and say that *kiddushei kesef* coins are distinct from divorce coins, just as the two *shtarot* are distinct? The *gemara* answers: *tiba michad hu* — money has a single nature. Coins are fungible. A five-dollar bill is indistinguishable from any other five-dollar bill; if one is lost, any equivalent sum suffices as replacement. The very essence of *kesef* is its interchangeability. A *shtar*, by contrast, is a unique, specific object. One could never mistake a *get* for a *shtar kiddushin* because each is a particular document with particular language. But money given for *kiddushin* and money given for divorce would look and feel identical, and the principle of *ein kategor na'aseh sanegor* — which we apply, for instance, to prohibiting a cow's horn for a *shofar* on *Rosh Hashanah*, and to prohibiting the *Kohen Gadol* from wearing gold into the *Kodesh Kodashim* on *Yom Kippur* — compels us not to use the same medium for such antithetical purposes.

### Rabbi Yose HaGalili

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רָבָא אָמַר: אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכָתַב לָהּ״ בִּכְתִיבָה מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת, וְאֵינָהּ מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת בְּכֶסֶף. וְאֵימָא: בִּכְתִיבָה מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת – וְאֵינָהּ מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת בִּכְתִיבָה! הָא כְּתִיב ״וְיָצְאָה״ ״וְהָיְתָה״ מַקִּישׁ וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:* **Rava said:** The *halakha* that a woman cannot be divorced by means of money is derived from a different source. **The verse states: "And he writes her** a scroll of severance" (Deuteronomy 24:3). This indicates that a woman **is divorced** only **through writing and she is not divorced through money.** The Gemara asks: **But** as this point is not stated explicitly and is inferred from the verse, one can instead **say** the following interpretation: **She is divorced through writing, and she is not betrothed through writing.** The Gemara rejects this suggestion. It is **written: "And she leaves and becomes"** (Deuteronomy 24:2), which **juxtaposes** betrothal to divorce, indicating that betrothal can be effected with a document.

A loose end requires attention. *Rava* offers an alternative derivation that divorce cannot be accomplished through *kesef*: *amar kra v'chatav lah* — the *pasuk* explicitly says "and he shall write for her," meaning *b'chtivah mitgareshet ve'einah mitgareshet b'kesef* — she is divorced only through writing, not through money. We spent three months in Masechet Gittin on this principle.

But then: *ve'eima b'chtivah mitgareshet ve'einah mitkaddeshet b'chtivah?* — why don't we use the same *pasuk* to exclude *shtar* from *kiddushin* rather than from divorce? The *gemara* answers that the *pasuk* of *v'yatza v'hayta* — the *hekesh havayah l'yetziah* — connects *kiddushin* to *gerushin*, and the question of which direction the *mi'ut* runs, and from which domain to which, is precisely the substance of the back-and-forth we have traced.

This raises the question of *Rabbi Yose HaGalili*, whose *drasha* in Gittin 21a uses the verse *v'chatav lah* for an entirely different *derasha*, deriving that she cannot be divorced with money. Since he deploys that verse elsewhere, where does *Rabbi Yose HaGalili* learn the prohibition on *get b'kesef*? *Amar kra sefer keritut* — the *pasuk* says *sefer keritut*, a "bill of severance," implying *sefer kartah v'ein davar acher kartah* — only a document effects divorce, nothing else. *V'rabanan hai keritut mai avdi lei* — how do the Rabbis then use the word *keritut* rather than simply *karat*? *Mibaei lei l'davar hakoret beino l'beina* — they require the extra form to teach that the *get* must constitute a complete severance between the husband and wife at the time of divorce. This is the *baraita* we encountered recently: *harei zeh gitech al m'nat shelo tishti yayin al m'nat shelo telchi l'veit avich l'olam ein zeh keritut* — a divorce conditioned on permanent constraints is not a valid *keritut* because the husband retains residual power over her. *Kol shloshim yom harei zeh keritut* — if the condition is limited to thirty days, it is a valid severance. The *Rashba* in a *teshuvah* addresses the boundary cases — what about fifty years? What about conditions that end with the death of one of the parties? *Tosafot* here likewise engages with those questions. For *Rabbi Yose HaGalili*, by contrast, the doubled form *keritut* over *karat* yields both *drashot* simultaneously. *V'rabanan karat keritut lo mashma lehu* — the Rabbis do not read the derivation from singular to plural. *Chada mechada lo atya* — one cannot be derived from the other.

## Learned One from Two

### Deriving Shtar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* חֲדָא מֵחֲדָא לָא אָתְיָא, תֵּיתֵי חֲדָא מִתַּרְתֵּי! הֵי תֵּיתֵי? לָא לִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בִּשְׁטָר, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ – מָה לְהָנָךְ שֶׁכֵּן הֲנָאָתָן מְרוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:* **One** mode of betrothal **cannot be derived from another** mode of betrothal, as explained in the *baraita*, but why not **let one be derived from** the other **two** methods? The Gemara answers: This is not possible, as, **which** method **will be derived** from the others? If you say: **Let the Merciful One not write** that a woman can be betrothed **through a document, and** one can **derive** this mode of acquisition **from these** modes of acquisition, i.e., intercourse and money, this claim can be refuted. **What** is unique **about these** other modes is that **their benefit is great,** relative to a document, as a woman experiences no enjoyment upon receiving a document.

Having established that each of the three methods cannot be derived from either of the other two individually — *chada mechada lo atya* — the *gemara* asks whether one might be derivable from the combination of the other two: *teiti chada mitarti*. Could *shtar* be derived from *biah* and *kesef* together?

### Hana'ah Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לָא לִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בִּשְׁטָר, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ – מָה לְהָנָךְ שֶׁכֵּן הֲנָאָתָן מְרוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:* If you say: **Let the Merciful One not write** that a woman can be betrothed **through a document, and** one can **derive** this mode **from these** modes of acquisition, i.e., money and intercourse, this claim can be refuted as well, as, **what** is unique **about these** is that **their benefit is great.**

No. *Biah* and *kesef* share a common feature that *shtar* lacks: *hana'atan merubah* — they both involve *hana'ah*, benefit or pleasure. Money has intrinsic value; there is tangible benefit in receiving it. *Biah* involves physical pleasure. A *shtar*, by contrast, is a purely legal object. It can be worth less than a *pruta*. It carries no intrinsic *hana'ah* whatsoever. Because *shtar* lacks this shared quality of *biah* and *kesef*, we cannot derive it from their combination.

This raises a subtle question addressed by the *Sha'ar HaMelech* in a different context: if *biah* works as a mode of *kiddushin* because it involves *hana'ah*, what would be the status of *kiddushin* effected through anal intercourse, which *Chazal* understand to involve diminished *hana'ah*? He suggests the resolution may be that *biah* does not actually work through a transfer of *hana'ah* at all — rather, it is the *ma'aseh kinyan* itself, the legal act, that matters. The *gemara*'s statement that *biah* is characterized by *hana'atan merubah* would then describe a general feature of the category, not a mechanism on which the *kinyan* depends. This is an important conceptual question about how much the *gemara*'s formulation reflects an intrinsic requirement versus a descriptive characterization.

### Deriving Kesef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לָא נִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בְּבִיאָה, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ – מָה לְהָנָךְ שֶׁכֵּן קִנְיָנָן מְרוּבֶּה.
>
> **Translation:* And if you say: **Let the Merciful One not write** that a woman can be acquired **through sexual intercourse, and** one can **derive** this mode **from these** modes of acquisition, i.e., money and a document, this too is not possible, as, **what** is unique **about these** modes of betrothal is that **their acquisition is great.**

Could *kesef* be derived from *biah* and *shtar* combined?

### Biah Not Normal Kinyan

No. *Biah* and *shtar* share a quality that *kesef* lacks: *kinyanan merubah* — *kesef* and *shtar* are general-purpose instruments of *kinyan*, applicable across many domains of acquisition including the purchase of land and the acquisition of slaves (topics the rest of the *perek* will address). *Biah*, by contrast, is not a normal mode of *kinyan*. One does not acquire a piece of land by *biah*. It is an act specific to particular marital and quasi-marital contexts. And since *biah* lacks the broader *kinyan* applicability shared by *kesef* and *shtar*, the combination cannot yield *kesef*.

### Biah Chazakah Analogy

*Tosafot* on 3a entertains — and rejects — the suggestion that *biah* might be compared to *chazakah* in the acquisition of land, whereby one demonstrates ownership by plowing, fencing, or traversing the property. Like *biah*, *chazakah* is a physical act that demonstrates the reality of a relationship between the acquirer and what is being acquired. The parallel is genuinely interesting, and it touches on sensitive questions about how the woman's role in *kiddushei biah* relates to the role of the field in *kinyan chazakah*. *Tosafot* rejects the analogy, noting that these are fundamentally different legal acts — though the comparison is serious enough to warrant extended treatment.

### Deriving Biah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לָא נִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בְּכֶסֶף, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ – מָה לְהָנָךְ שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ.
>
> **Translation:* If you say: **Let the Merciful One not write** that a woman can be acquired **through money, and** one can **derive** this mode **from these** other modes of acquisition, this is also not possible, as **what** is unique **about these** is that they apply **against her will.**

Could *biah* be derived from *kesef* and *shtar* combined?

### Be'al Korchah

No. *Kesef* and *shtar* share a quality that *biah* lacks in the marital context: *yeshnan b'al korchah* — they can operate *b'al korchah*, without the woman's consent. A husband can give his wife a *get* against her will (prior to *Rabbeinu Gershom*'s *takkanah*). *Biah* as a mode of *kiddushin* in a normal marriage cannot occur *b'al korchah* — valid marital *biah* requires the woman's *da'at*.

The *gemara* notes that *yibbum* seems to complicate this: the *biah* of *yibbum* can be *b'al korchah* in some sense, because the *yevamah's* agreement to the legal entry into a marital relationship with this particular man is not required at the moment of *yibbum*, given that the *zikah* already creates a pre-existing bond. But that is specifically *yibbum*, not a normal marital *biah*, and the distinction holds.

Regarding *kesef b'al korchah*: *tosafot* raises the case of *amah ivriyah*, where a father sells his minor daughter into indenture — seemingly imposing the arrangement upon her. But *tosafot* resolves this by noting that the father in this case is the minor's legal agent; because she is not yet a *bar da'at*, there is no *ratzon* of her own that is being overridden. The father's *da'at* substitutes for hers, and it is therefore not classified as *b'al korchah* in the technical sense.

## Rav Huna Chuppah

### Chuppah Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חוּפָּה קוֹנָה מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה כֶּסֶף שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל בִּתְרוּמָה – קוֹנֶה, חוּפָּה שֶׁמַּאֲכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתִּקְנֶה?
>
> **Translation:* **Rav Huna says:** The ceremony of the wedding **canopy effects acquisition** of the woman, as is derived **through an *a fortiori*** inference: **If money, which does not enable** the wife **to partake of *teruma*,** as the daughter of a non-priest betrothed to a priest may not yet partake of *teruma*, **effects acquisition** of a woman for betrothal, **is it not logical that** a wedding **canopy, which enables her to partake of *teruma*,** as once a woman has entered the wedding canopy it is permitted for her to partake of *teruma* due to her husband the priest, **should** also **effect acquisition** of a woman by her husband?

We have now concluded the discussion of the three established modes of *kiddushin*. Each requires its own independent *limud*; none can be derived from any other singly; and each possesses a unique quality — *shtar* has no *hana'ah*, *biah* is not a general-purpose *kinyan*, and *kesef* does not exist *b'al korchah* in a marital context — that defeats any attempt at *tzad hashaveh* derivation from the combination of the other two. It is striking to consider that three distinct processes all yield the same legal outcome and yet have such fundamentally different character.

*Rav Huna* now proposes something new: *chuppah koneh m'kal vachomer* — *chuppah* should be able to effect *kiddushin* through *kal vachomer*. *Chuppah* is the rabbinic term for *nissuin*, the second stage of marriage, and the *rishonim* have a wide-ranging *machloket* — something like seven positions — regarding what *chuppah* actually refers to: wrapping the couple in a shared garment (as in some *Sephardi* practice), spreading a canopy over them, veiling the bride, *yichud*, the act of *biah*, or entering the shared home. Each identification carries significant legal implications.

### Kesef Terumah

The *kesef terumah* example is challenged, but let us note Rav Huna's original formulation first: *chuppah koneh m'kal vachomer umah kesef she'eino ma'achil b'terumah koneh, chuppah she'ma'achelet b'terumah eino din she'tikneh* — money, which does not grant the woman the right to eat *terumah*, nonetheless effects *kiddushin*. *Chuppah*, which does grant that right (when a woman enters *nissuin* with a *kohen*), should certainly effect *kiddushin kal vachomer*.

### Woman Eating Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְכֶסֶף אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל? וְהָאָמַר עוּלָּא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, אֲרוּסָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכֹהֵן כִּי יִקְנֶה נֶפֶשׁ קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ״, וְהַאי ״קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ הוּא״.
>
> **Translation:* The Gemara asks: **And does** betrothal **money not enable** a woman **to partake of** *teruma*? **But didn't Ulla say: By Torah law the daughter of a non-priest** who is **betrothed** to a priest may **partake of *teruma*** immediately upon her betrothal, **as it is stated: "But if a priest buy any soul, the acquisition of his money,** he may eat of it" (Leviticus 22:11), **and this** woman **is** considered **the acquisition of his money.**

The *gemara* challenges the premise: does *kesef* truly not allow the woman to eat *terumah*? *V'ha amar Ulla devar Torah arusa bat Yisrael ochelet b'terumah* — *Ulla* holds that *mid'oraita*, a woman who is only *mekudeshet* — *arusah* — may eat *terumah*, because the *pasuk* states *v'kohen ki yikneh nefesh kinyan kaspo* — a *kohen's* wife is his *kinyan kaspo*, and since marriage is effected as *kinyan kaspo*, she is entitled to eat *terumah* from the point of *kiddushin*. She is *kinyan kaspo* already from that stage.

### Rabbinic Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמַה טַּעַם אָמְרוּ אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת – גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִמְזְגוּ לָהּ כּוֹס בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ וְתַשְׁקֶנּוּ לְאָחִיהָ וְלַאֲחוֹתָיהּ.
>
> **Translation:* **And what is the reason** that the Sages **said** that **she may not partake** of *teruma*? It is a rabbinic **decree lest they pour her a cup** of *teruma* wine **in her father's house,** where she is staying before her marriage, **and she give to her brothers and sisters to drink,** as it is prohibited for them to partake of *teruma*. If so, by Torah law a woman may partake of *teruma* once she has been betrothed with money. Consequently, the above *a fortiori* inference that entering a wedding canopy can effect betrothal is invalid.

The reason *miderabbanan* an *arusah* cannot eat *terumah* is a *gezeirah*: *shema yimzegu lah kos b'veit aviha v'tashkeihu l'acheiha v'l'achayoteiha* — perhaps her family will pour her a cup of *terumah* wine in her father's house and she will share it with her siblings, who are not married to *kohanim* and may not eat *terumah*. The rabbinic prohibition on *terumah* for an *arusah* is thus a prophylactic measure, not a reflection of her actual status *mid'oraita*. So *kal vachomer*, the *kesef* also allows eating *terumah* — Rav Huna's premise is undermined.

### Biah Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא פְּרֵיךְ הָכִי: וּמָה כֶּסֶף שֶׁאֵינוֹ גּוֹמֵר – קוֹנֶה, חוּפָּה שֶׁגּוֹמֶרֶת – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתִּקְנֶה? מָה לְכֶסֶף, שֶׁכֵּן פּוֹדִים בּוֹ הֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי. בִּיאָה תּוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:* **Rather,** the Gemara emends Rav Huna's statement and says that **he refutes** the opinion that entering a wedding canopy does not effect betrothal **as follows: If money, which does not complete** the acquisition of a woman, as a young woman remains under her father's authority with regard to certain issues, nevertheless **effects acquisition** of her for the stage of betrothal, **is it not logical that** a wedding **canopy, which completes** the marriage, since it entirely removes a young woman from her father's authority, **can effect acquisition** for betrothal on its own? The Gemara rejects this claim: **What** is unique **about money is that** it can effect acquisition in many contexts, **as one can redeem with it consecrated** property **and second tithe.** This is not so with regard to a wedding canopy, which is ineffective in effecting any acquisition other than marriage. Therefore, in the case of betrothal, money can effect betrothal while a wedding canopy cannot. The Gemara counters: The *halakha* of **sexual intercourse proves** otherwise, as this act serves to effect acquisition of a woman despite the fact that it is not a valid mode of acquisition in any other case.

The *gemara* then reformulates the *kal vachomer* correctly: *umah kesef she'eino gomer koneh, chuppah she'gomeret eino din shetikneh* — money, which does not complete the marriage (it only effects *kiddushin*, not *nissuin*), nonetheless creates the *kinyan* of *kiddushin*. *Chuppah*, which completes the marriage by effecting *nissuin*, should *kal vachomer* be able to effect *kiddushin* as well. The counter from *biah* is raised: *biah tochiach* — *biah* shows that a mode of *kinyan* can work for *kiddushin*. And *mah l'biah sheken konah b'yevamah* — *biah* is distinctive in that it effects a *kinyan* in the context of *yibbum*, which has no parallel for *chuppah*. *Kesef yochiach* — and around the cycle we go, each counterexample being refuted by the other. *Lo rai zeh k'rai zeh v'lo rai zeh k'rai zeh* — each has its defects but they are not identical defects.

### Kesef Yochiach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְחָזַר הַדִּין: לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן: שֶׁקּוֹנִין בְּעָלְמָא וְקוֹנִין כָּאן, אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא חוּפָּה שֶׁקּוֹנָה בְּעָלְמָא, וְקוֹנָה כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:* **And** once again, **the derivation has reverted** to its starting point: **The aspect of this** mode, money, **is not like the aspect of that** mode, intercourse, **and the aspect of that** mode, intercourse, **is not like the aspect of this** mode, money. **Their common denominator is that they generally effect acquisition, and they effect acquisition here,** with regard to betrothal. **Likewise, I will bring** the mode of a wedding **canopy, which generally effects acquisition,** rendering a woman as married, **and** therefore it should also **effect acquisition here.**

The *gemara* works through the *tzad hashaveh*: *hatzad hashaveh shebahen shekonin b'alma v'koneh kan* — the common denominator of *kesef* and *biah* is that they are both instruments of *kinyan* in the general sense and they effect *kiddushin*. *Af ani avi chuppah shekonin b'alma v'koneh kan* — I will bring *chuppah* also as an instrument of *kinyan* in the general sense, and it too should effect *kiddushin*.

### Tzad HaShaveh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מָה לְצַד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן הֲנָאָתָן מְרוּבָּה. שְׁטָר יוֹכִיחַ. מָה לִשְׁטָר, שֶׁכֵּן מוֹצִיא בְּבַת יִשְׂרָאֵל. כֶּסֶף וּבִיאָה יוֹכִיחוּ.
>
> **Translation:* The Gemara rejects this: **What** about the fact that **the common denominator of** money and intercourse is **that their benefit,** i.e., pleasure, **is great?** The Gemara says: The case of **a document proves** otherwise, as no great pleasure is derived from receiving a document, and yet it can be used to acquire a woman. The Gemara answers: **What** is unique about **a document is that it can release a Jewish woman** from her husband in the form of a bill of divorce. The Gemara answers: **Money and intercourse prove** otherwise, as they do not release a woman and yet they are valid modes of acquisition for betrothal.

The *tzad hashaveh* argument is pressed further. *Mah l'tzad hashaveh shebahen sheken hana'ah merubah* — but the common denominator of *biah* and *kesef* is that they involve *hana'ah*, which *chuppah* does not. *Shtar yochiach* — let *shtar* serve as the refutation: *shtar* has no *hana'ah* and still effects *kiddushin*. *Mah l'shtar sheken motzi* — *shtar* is unique in that it can dissolve a marriage through *get*, which *chuppah* cannot do.

### Hana'ah Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְחָזַר הַדִּין: לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן: שֶׁקּוֹנִין בְּעָלְמָא וְקוֹנִין כָּאן – אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא חוּפָּה, שֶׁקּוֹנָה בְּעָלְמָא וְקוֹנָה כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:* **And** once again, **the derivation has reverted** to its starting point: **The aspect of this** mode **is not like the aspect of that** mode, **and the aspect of that** mode **is not like the aspect of this** mode. **Their common denominator is that they generally effect acquisition and they effect acquisition here,** with regard to betrothal. **Likewise, I will bring** the mode of a wedding **canopy, which generally effects acquisition, and** therefore it should also **effect acquisition here.**

*Kesef u'biah yochiach* — *kesef* and *biah* will show that the capacity to dissolve is not required. *V'hazar hadin* — the argument cycles again. *Lo rai zeh k'rai zeh v'lo rai zeh k'rai zeh* — each method, again, has a disqualifying factor the others lack.

### Unique Factors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מָה לְצַד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן: שֶׁקּוֹנִין בְּעָלְמָא וְקוֹנִין כָּאן – אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא חוּפָּה, שֶׁקּוֹנָה בְּעָלְמָא וְקוֹנָה כָּאן. מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ. וְרַב הוּנָא – כֶּסֶף מִיהָא בְּאִישׁוּת לָא אַשְׁכְּחַן בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ.
>
> **Translation:* **What** about the fact that **the common denominator of** all three modes of acquisition is **that they are** effective in certain situations **against her will?** A bill of divorce, sexual intercourse in the case of a *yevama*, and money with regard to a Hebrew maidservant all effect acquisition of a woman against her will. Therefore, the mode of a wedding canopy cannot be derived from these methods, as a wedding canopy is effective only when the woman enters it willingly. **And** in response to this claim **Rav Huna** would answer: **In any event, with regard to marriage, we have not found** a case in which a woman can be acquired through **money against her will.** Consequently, it is possible to learn from the modes of money, a document, and intercourse that a wedding canopy likewise effects betrothal.

*Mah l'tzad hashaveh shebahen sheken yeshnan b'al korchah* — the common denominator of *shtar* and *biah* is that both can exist in a context of *b'al korchah*, which *kesef* — and likewise *chuppah* — cannot. *V'Rav Huna kesef mi ha b'isha lo ashkechan b'al korchah* — and *Rav Huna* has no answer to the fact that *kesef* does not exist in a marital context that is *b'al korchah*. The *kal vachomer* cannot be completed.

### Rava Objection One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁתֵּי תְשׁוּבוֹת בַּדָּבָר. חֲדָא, דְּ״שָׁלֹשׁ״ תְּנַן וְ״אַרְבַּע״ לָא תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:* **Rava said:** There are **two refutations of** this **matter,** i.e., it is possible to refute Rav Huna's opinion in two ways. **One** opinion is **that we learned** in the mishna that a woman can be acquired through **three** modes of acquisition, **and we did not learn** that there are **four** modes. This indicates that there are no other ways to acquire a woman apart from the three listed in the mishna.

*Amar Rava shtei teshuvot b'davar* — *Rava* raises two independent objections to *Rav Huna*. First: *chada d'shalosh tnan v'lo arba* — the *mishnah* explicitly states *ha'isha niknet b'shalosh drachim*, a woman can be married in three ways, not four. *Chuppah* is not among the three.

### Rava Objection Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְעוֹד: כְּלוּם חוּפָּה גּוֹמֶרֶת אֶלָּא עַל יְדֵי קִידּוּשִׁין, וְכִי גָּמְרִינַן חוּפָּה שֶׁלֹּא עַל יְדֵי קִידּוּשִׁין מֵחוּפָּה שֶׁעַל יְדֵי קִידּוּשִׁין?
>
> **Translation:* **And furthermore,** Rava disagrees with the main point of the proof, which was based on the fact that a wedding canopy completes a marriage: **Doesn't** entering a wedding **canopy complete** a marriage **only by** means of an act of **betrothal,** which precedes the wedding canopy? **And can one derive** that entering a wedding **canopy** effects acquisition **without betrothal from** the case of entering a wedding **canopy that** effects acquisition **by** means of **betrothal?** Consequently, entering a wedding canopy alone cannot effect a betrothal.

Second, and more fundamentally: *v'od klum chuppah gomeret ela al yedei kiddushin* — *chuppah* only effects *nissuin* in the wake of prior *kiddushin*. It presupposes *kiddushin*; it does not operate independently. How then can *Rav Huna* propose a *kal vachomer* that *chuppah* — which can complete marriage — should be able to do what the other methods do? *Chuppah* completing marriage is a function that operates only once *kiddushin* has already occurred. It cannot be bootstrapped into being a method of *kiddushin* itself.

### Abaye Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הָא דְּקָאָמְרַתְּ ״שָׁלֹשׁ״ תְּנַן וְ״אַרְבַּע״ לָא תְּנַן – תַּנָּא מִילְּתָא דִּכְתִיבָא בְּהֶדְיָא קָתָנֵי, מִילְּתָא דְּלָא כְּתִיבָא בְּהֶדְיָא לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:* **Abaye said to** Rava in response to his two claims: With regard to **that which you said,** that **we learned** in the mishna **three** modes **and we did not learn** that there are **four** modes, this is no proof, as the ***tanna* teaches** only **a matter that is explicitly written** in the Torah, and **does not teach a matter that is not explicitly written** in the Torah, such as a wedding canopy.

*Abaye* defends *Rav Huna* against *Rava*'s first objection: *tana milta d'chtiva b'hedya katanei, milta delo chtiva b'hedya lo katanei* — the *mishnah* lists only what is explicitly derivable from *pesukim*. *Chuppah*, being derived through *kal vachomer* rather than from explicit scriptural language, was not listed — but this is *tana veshayer*, the *tanna* listed only a subset. The *mishnah*'s enumeration of three therefore does not refute the possibility of a fourth.

This is perhaps not the most compelling answer, and *Rava*'s second objection remains formidable.

### Rav Huna Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ ״כְּלוּם חוּפָּה גּוֹמֶרֶת אֶלָּא עַל יְדֵי קִידּוּשִׁין״ – רַב הוּנָא נָמֵי הָכִי קָאָמַר: וּמָה כֶּסֶף שֶׁאֵינוֹ גּוֹמֵר אַחַר כֶּסֶף – קוֹנֶה, חוּפָּה שֶׁגּוֹמֶרֶת אַחַר כֶּסֶף – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתִּקְנֶה?
>
> **Translation:* **And** with regard to **that which you said: Doesn't** entering a wedding **canopy complete** a marriage **only by** means of an act of **betrothal, this is also what Rav Huna is saying,** i.e., Rav Huna incorporates this claim into his reasoning: **If money, which does not complete** a marriage **after money,** i.e., after a woman has been betrothed through money an additional monetary gift cannot render her a fully married woman, **effects acquisition** of the woman in the form of betrothal, **is it not logical that** entering a wedding **canopy,** which is more powerful than money in that it **completes** a marriage **after money, should effect acquisition** and be used to perform betrothal by itself?

*Abaye* then addresses the second objection on *Rav Huna*'s behalf: *Rav Huna* can actually incorporate *Rava*'s challenge into the *kal vachomer* and make it even stronger. *Mah kesef she'eino gomer achar kesef koneh* — money cannot effect *nissuin* even after *kiddushei kesef* has been performed; giving a second ring does not constitute *nissuin*. *Chuppah shegomeret achar kesef eino din shetikneh* — since *chuppah* does work after *kesef* (it effects *nissuin* following *kiddushei kesef*), should it not *kal vachomer* be able to effect *kiddushin* as well?

### Tosafot HaRosh

This leads into the *Tosafot HaRosh*'s question: when *Rav Huna* says *chuppah koneh m'kal vachomer*, does he mean *chuppah* effecting *kiddushin* and *nissuin* together in a single act, or does he mean *chuppah* effecting *kiddushin* alone — requiring some separate act for *nissuin* afterward? This is a fundamental question about the shape of *Rav Huna*'s position and it remains unresolved within the *sugya*.

We note that while *Rava*'s objections are serious and we generally rule like *Rava* over *Abaye*, the *Rif* may rule like *Rav Huna* — a notable exception. The practical *nafka minah* is rare, but one can imagine the question arising in a tragic case where, for example, a *kallah* collapsed and died immediately after *bede'ken* and before a conventional *kiddushin* — and the question becomes whether any form of *chuppah* was completed, and whether the *kohen* husband is permitted to be *mitamei* for her as a full wife. Such cases do not arise often, but the theoretical weight of *Rav Huna*'s position is not nothing.

## Kiddushei Kesef Mechanics

### Man's Action Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד בְּכֶסֶף? נָתַן לָהּ כֶּסֶף אוֹ שָׁוֶה כֶּסֶף, וְאָמַר לָהּ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְאוֹרֶסֶת לִי״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ לִי לְאִינְתּוּ״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. אֲבָל הִיא שֶׁנָּתְנָה, וְאָמְרָה הִיא: ״הֲרֵינִי מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לְךָ״, ״הֲרֵינִי מְאוֹרֶסֶת לְךָ״, ״הֲרֵינִי לְךָ לְאִינְתּוּ״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:* § After discussing the sources for the modes of acquisition listed in the mishna, the Gemara analyzes these *halakhot* in greater detail. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 1:1): **How** is betrothal performed **through money?** If a man **gave** a woman **money or** an item **worth money, and** he **said to her: You are hereby betrothed [*mekuddeshet*] to me,** or: **You are hereby betrothed [*me'oreset*] to me,** or: **You are to me as a wife,** then **she is betrothed. But** if **she** is the one **who gave** the money to him, **and she said: I am hereby betrothed [*mekuddeshet*] to you,** or: **I am hereby betrothed [*me'oreset*] to you,** or: **I am hereby to you as a wife,** then **she is not betrothed.**

The *gemara* now pivots to the practical mechanics of *kiddushei kesef*. A *baraita* is cited: *natan lah kesef o shaveh kesef v'amar lah harei at mekudeshet li, harei at me'ureset li, harei at li le'intu* — he gives her money or its equivalent and says one of several designated formulas: "You are *mekudeshet* to me," "You are betrothed to me," "You are my wife." *Harei zu mekudeshet* — she is thereby married. The *kiddushin* requires both the act of giving and an accompanying verbal declaration, an *amira*.

### Woman's Declaration Und…

The *baraita* continues: *aval hi she'natnah lo v'amrah hi* — but if she gives the ring to him and she is the one who speaks — *lo havei kiddushin* — they are not married. Two variables have changed: she gave rather than he, and she spoke rather than he. This reflects the *pasuk* of *ki yikach ish isha* — the man takes the wife, giving him the more active legal role in the process.

### Rav Pappa Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: טַעְמָא דְּנָתַן הוּא וְאָמַר הוּא, הָא נָתַן הוּא וְאָמְרָה הִיא – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אֲבָל הִיא שֶׁנָּתְנָה לוֹ וְאָמְרָה הִיא – לָא הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין. טַעְמָא דְּנָתְנָה הִיא וְאָמְרָה הִיא, הָא נָתַן הוּא וְאָמְרָה הִיא – הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין!
>
> **Translation:* **Rav Pappa objects to this:** This *baraita* contains an internal contradiction. The first part of the *baraita* states: If a man gave a woman money and said to her: You are hereby betrothed to me, from which it may be inferred **that the reason** the woman is betrothed is that **he gave** her money **and he said** the appropriate formula. This leads to the conclusion that if **he gave** her money **and she said** the formula, **she is not betrothed.** Now **say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **But** if **she** is the one **who gave** the money to him, **and she said:** I am hereby betrothed to you, then **it is not** a valid **betrothal.** Rav Pappa infers: **The reason** that it is not a valid betrothal is that **she gave** money to him **and she said** the appropriate formula, from which it may be inferred that if **he gave** money to her **and she said** the appropriate formula, then **this is** a valid **betrothal.**

*Rav Pappa* is troubled by the *baraita*: *ta'ama d'natan hu v'amar hu* — the reason the first case works is that he gave and he said. From the *reisha* one might infer: if he gave but she said — that would not work. From the *seifa* one might infer: the reason it fails is that she gave and she said — but if he gave and she said, it would work. The two halves of the *baraita* appear to contradict each other on this intermediate case, and *Rav Pappa* wants to know the answer.

### Reisha Davka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רֵישָׁא דַּוְקָא, סֵיפָא כְּדִי נַסְבַהּ. וְתָנֵי סֵיפָא מִילְּתָא דְּסָתְרָא לַהּ לְרֵישָׁא?
>
> **Translation:* The Gemara explains: **The first clause** of the *baraita* is **exact,** and therefore it is correct to infer that which was not explicitly stated from this ruling. By contrast, **the latter clause** of the *baraita* was **cited for no reason,** i.e., it was simply formulated in the opposite manner of the first clause, and the *baraita* is not exact in the wording of this case. Therefore, one should not analyze this clause too carefully and infer *halakhot* from it. The Gemara asks: **And** would the *baraita* **teach** in **the latter clause a matter that contradicts the first clause?**

The *gemara* resolves: *reisha davka* — the first clause is precise. For valid *kiddushin*, the man must both give and declare. The *seifa* simply came along for parallel formulation and does not yield independent inferences.

### Woman's Amira Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: נָתַן הוּא וְאָמַר הוּא – פְּשִׁיטָא דְּהָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין. נָתַן הוּא וְאָמְרָה הִיא – נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁנָּתְנָה הִיא וְאָמְרָה הִיא, וְלָא הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: נָתַן הוּא וְאָמַר הוּא – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. נָתְנָה הִיא וְאָמְרָה הִיא – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. נָתַן הוּא וְאָמְרָה הִיא – סְפֵיקָא הִיא וְחָיְישִׁינַן מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:* **Rather,** the Gemara retracts the previous explanation in favor of the following. **This is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** If **he gave** the money **and he said** the formula, it **is obvious that it is** a valid **betrothal.** If **he gave** the money **and she said** the formula, **it is considered as though she gave** the money **and she said** the formula, **and** therefore **it is not** a valid **betrothal. And if you wish, say** a different explanation of the *baraita*: If **he gave** the money and **he said** the formula, **she is betrothed.** If **she gave** the money **and she said** the formula, **she is not betrothed** at all. If **he gave** the money **and she said** the formula, the ruling **is uncertain, and by rabbinic law we are concerned** that this might actually be a betrothal.

But the *gemara* elaborates more carefully: if the man gives the money and the woman is the one who speaks, this is treated *ke'mi she'natnah hi v'amrah hi* — as if she had given it and said it. Her act of *amira* when he gives effectively makes the act hers, such that it is not *kiddushin*.

### Safek Kiddushin

An alternative reading: *natan hu v'amrah hi safeka hi v'chaishinan miderabbanan* — if he gives and she speaks, this may be *safek kiddushin*. That is, there is genuine doubt whether her declaration plays the role of the required *amira*. Under this view, were she to go and marry another man, she would require a *get* from both. The first view would say it is simply not *kiddushin* at all; the second view treats it as *safek*. This unresolved question about the *amira*'s role — and whether the woman's participation in the declaration can either substitute for or undermine the man's declaration — speaks to the broader question of what precisely the verbal component of *kiddushin* accomplishes.

## Shmuel Amira Rule

### Required Formulations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּקִידּוּשִׁין, נָתַן לָהּ כֶּסֶף וְשָׁוֶה כֶּסֶף וְאָמַר לָהּ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְאוֹרֶסֶת״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ (לִי) לְאִינְתּוּ״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. ״הֲרֵינִי אִישֵּׁךְ״, ״הֲרֵינִי בַּעְלֵיךְ״, ״הֲרֵינִי אֲרוּסֵיךְ״ – אֵין כָּאן בֵּית מֵיחוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:* The Gemara continues to discuss the language of betrothal. **Shmuel says: With regard to betrothal,** if **he gave her money or** an item **worth money, and said to her: You are hereby betrothed [*mekuddeshet*],** or: **You are hereby betrothed [*me'oreset*],** or: **You are hereby as a wife,** then **she is betrothed.** If he said: **I am hereby your man,** or: **I am hereby your husband,** or: **I am hereby your betrothed,** then **there is no room for concern here.** In these cases there is no possibility that it might be a valid betrothal, as betrothal is effective only if its formulation defines the relationship in terms of the woman's connection to the man, not the reverse.

*Shmuel* addresses the *amira* requirements directly. If the man gives *kesef* or its equivalent and says *harei at mekudeshet* — "you are *mekudeshet*" — or *harei at me'ureset*, or *harei at le'intu* — "you are my wife" — she is married. These formulations share a common feature: they describe a change in the *woman's* status. She becomes *mekudeshet*, she becomes *me'ureset*, she becomes *le'intu*.

### Man Status Doesn't Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְכֵן בְּגֵירוּשִׁין, נָתַן לָהּ וְאָמַר לָהּ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְשׁוּלַּחַת״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מוּתֶּרֶת לְכׇל אָדָם״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. ״אֵינִי אִישֵּׁךְ״, ״אֵינִי בַּעְלֵיךְ״, ״אֵינִי אֲרוּסֵיךְ״ – אֵין כָּאן בֵּית מֵיחוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:* **And similarly, with regard to divorce,** if a husband **gave** his wife a bill of divorce **and said to her: You are hereby sent away,** or: **You are hereby divorced,** or: **You are hereby permitted to** marry **any man,** then **she is divorced.** If he said: **I am not your man,** or: **I am not your husband,** or: **I am not your betrothed,** then **there is no room for concern,** as a bill of divorce is effective only if its formulation defines the relationship in terms of the woman's connection to the man, not the reverse.

But if the man says *hareini isheich* — "I am your husband" — or *hareini ba'aleich* — "I am your *ba'al*" — or *hareini aruseich* — "I am betrothed to you" — *ein kan beit michush* — there is not even cause for concern. This is simply not *kiddushin*. The man's status does not change through the act of *kiddushin*. He can go and marry another wife; nothing in his legal standing is transformed. It is the woman's status that changes: she becomes forbidden to all other men, she requires a *get* to remarry, and an entire structure of obligations and prohibitions attaches to her. When the man declares a change in his own status rather than hers, the declaration is legally inert.

### Status Change Principle

The same principle applies in *gerushin*: *v'chatav lah v'natan b'yadah* — the *pasuk* requires him to write *for her* and give it *into her hand*. If a man says *harei at meshulachat* — "you are sent away" — or *harei at megureshet* — "you are divorced" — or *harei at muteret l'chol adam* — "you are permitted to every man" — *harei zu megureshet*, she is divorced. These formulas change the woman's status. But if he says *eini ishech, eini ba'alech, eini aruseich* — "I am no longer your husband, I am no longer your *ba'al*, I am no longer betrothed to you" — this is again *ein kan beit michush*. He has described a change in his own status, not hers, and a divorce requires changing hers.

### Yadayim Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: לְמֵימְרָא דְּסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל יָדַיִם שֶׁאֵין מוֹכִיחוֹת, הָוְיָין יָדַיִם?
>
> **Translation:* **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: Is this to say that Shmuel holds** that **ambiguous intimations,** i.e., incomplete expressions that can be understood only from their context, **are** considered like unambiguous **intimations?** In the cases listed by Shmuel, the woman is betrothed despite the fact that the man did not say: You are hereby betrothed to me, but merely: You are hereby betrothed. The statement itself does not include the detail that the speaker intends to betroth her to himself, and yet Shmuel maintains that the betrothal is valid.

*Rav Pappa* asks *Abaye*: does this ruling of *Shmuel* imply that *Shmuel* holds *yadayim she'einan mochiyot havyan yadayim* — that an indirect expression, even one that does not explicitly denote the intended legal act, constitutes a valid declaration? After all, *Shmuel* rules that saying *harei at mekudeshet* without specifying *li* — "to me" — is sufficient. Is this not accepting an incomplete expression?

### Nazir Yadayim Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָתְנַן: הָאוֹמֵר ״אֱהֵא״ – הֲרֵי זֶה נָזִיר. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: וְדִילְמָא ״אֱהֵא בְּתַעֲנִית״ קָאָמַר? וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, וְהוּא שֶׁהָיָה נָזִיר עוֹבֵר לְפָנָיו. טַעְמָא דִּנְזִיר עוֹבֵר לְפָנָיו, הָא לָאו הָכִי – לָא!
>
> **Translation:* Rav Pappa asks: **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Nazir* 2a) that **one who says: I shall be, is a nazirite? And we discussed** this ruling: **But perhaps** he meant **to say: I will be in a fast? And Shmuel said** that **this** mishna **is** referring to a particular set of circumstances, that he said: I shall be, **when a nazirite was passing before him.** In that context it is clear the individual meant that he too will be a nazirite. Rav Pappa analyzes this statement: **The reason** that he is a nazirite is only due to the fact **that a nazirite passes before him.** But **if this** were **not** the case, **no,** his statement would not be considered a naziriteship vow. This indicates that according to Shmuel, ambiguous intimations are not considered like unambiguous intimations.

*Rav Pappa* raises the counterexample from *Nazir*: if a person sees a *nazir* passing by and says *aha* — pointing at him — is he himself a *nazir*? *Shmuel* rules: *ve'hu she'hayah nazir over lefanav* — only if the *nazir* was actually passing by at that moment. *Ta'ama d'nazir over lefanav hu, lav hachi lo* — the reason it works is that the *nazir* was physically present, making the reference unambiguous; without that context, the exclamation would not suffice. This suggests *Shmuel* holds *yadayim she'einan mochiyot einan yadayim* — imprecise expressions are not valid declarations.

### Status Change Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דְּאָמַר ״לִי״. אִי הָכִי, מַאי קָמַשְׁמַע לַן? הָנֵי לִישָּׁנֵי בָּתְרָאֵי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: הָכָא כְּתִיב ״כִּי יִקַּח״ – וְלֹא שֶׁיַּקַּח אֶת עַצְמוֹ. וְהָכָא כְּתִיב ״וְשִׁלְּחָהּ״ – וְלֹא שֶׁיְּשַׁלַּח אֶת עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:* The Gemara rejects this: **With what are we dealing here?** Shmuel is referring to a case **where he said** the formulation and added the phrase: **To me.** For example, he said to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me. The Gemara asks: **If so, what is** Shmuel **teaching us?** If the man stated the full formula it is obvious that she is betrothed, as he used the standard expression of betrothal. The Gemara answers: **These last expressions** are **what he teaches us.** The novelty of Shmuel's statement is that with regard to the second set of pronouncements there is no concern at all that a valid betrothal or divorce might have been performed. The Gemara explains why according to Shmuel these pronouncements are not of concern. **Here,** in the case of betrothal, it is **written: "When** a man **takes** a woman" (Deuteronomy 24:1), which indicates that the man is acting to change the status of the woman, **and** it is **not** written **that he takes himself** or gives himself to her, as in the case of one who says: I am hereby your man. **And** likewise, it is **written here,** with regard to divorce: **"And sends her"** (Deuteronomy 24:1), **and** it is **not** written **that he sends himself** from her, as in the case of one who says: I am not your man.

The *gemara* resolves: *Shmuel*'s ruling about *kiddushin* teaches not that imprecise expressions are valid, but rather something about whose status must be described. The *chiddush* is that the man must declare a change in the *woman's* status — not his own. *Hacha k'siv ki yikach v'lo she'yikach et atzmo* — the *pasuk* says *ki yikach ish isha*, "a man shall take a wife" — the act of taking is directed toward her, not toward himself. He cannot "take himself" to her. And *v'hacha k'siv v'shilchah v'lo she'yishlach et atzmo* — the *pasuk* of divorce says *v'shilchah*, "and he shall send her away" — he cannot divorce himself from her. In both marriage and divorce, it is the woman's legal status that is the object of the act, and the verbal declaration must reflect that.
