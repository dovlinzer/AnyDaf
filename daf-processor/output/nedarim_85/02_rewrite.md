# Nedarim 85 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah & Annulment

### Benefit Forbidden

The *mishnah* with which we open Daf 85 is one we have encountered before, still situated within the broader discussion of which *nedarim* a husband may annul on behalf of his wife — specifically, what qualifies as *d'varim shebeino l'veina* (matters between them) or *d'varim shel inui nefesh* (matters of self-denial). The case the *mishnah* presents is the following: a woman declares "*konam she'ani osa al pi abba, v'al pi avicha, v'al pi achi, v'al pi achicha*" — that anything she produces shall be forbidden in benefit to her father, her husband's father, her brother, or her husband's brother.

### Tanna Kamma

The *Tanna Kamma* rules that the husband cannot annul this *neder*, and on two distinct grounds. First, it does not qualify as *inui nefesh*, since she is not forbidding any pleasure to herself. Second, it does not qualify as *d'varim shebeino l'veina*: although the practical consequences may certainly annoy the husband — the couple cannot host her father or his brother for a meal — the *neder* itself does not directly forbid anything as between the two of them. The further ruling of the *mishnah* is that if she were instead to say "*she'ani osa al picha*" — what I produce is forbidden to you, the husband — he does not need to annul even that, because the husband already holds a lien on his wife's *ma'aseh yadayim* (the products of her labor). Since it is not fully hers to forbid, the *neder* cannot effectively take hold on something that is already his.

### Rabbi Akiva

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees: "*yafer, shema ta'adif alav yoter min hara'uy lo*" — he should annul the *neder*, lest she produce more than she is strictly obligated to provide. Recall the *mishnah* in *Ketubot* that enumerates the minimum labor a wife is required to perform. If she exceeds that minimum, the surplus — the *hadafah* — is something the husband has no strict legal entitlement to, even if in practice he would benefit from it. The *neder* would therefore take effect on that excess. *Rabbi Akiva* thus provides two reasons for annulment: first, it qualifies as *d'varim shebeino l'veina*; and second, he risks transgressing inadvertently if he benefits from her extra production.

### Rabbi Yochanan

*Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* takes a different position: "*yafer, shema yigarshena v'tei'asur alav*" — he should annul it lest he divorce her and she become forbidden to him. His concern is not the *hadafah* at all; he may well accept that the husband is entitled to her extra production. His worry is forward-looking: right now the *neder* does not take effect because the husband holds a lien on her *ma'aseh yadayim*. But if they were to divorce, that lien dissolves, the labor returns to her ownership, and the *neder* — held in a kind of abeyance — would then kick in and take effect. The consequence would be that they could not remarry, since it would be untenable to live together with him unable to benefit from her work. Better, therefore, to annul it now and remove it entirely, rather than leave it suspended.

This position of *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* implicitly establishes something important: an annulment grounded in *d'varim shebeino l'veina* remains effective even after divorce, at least for the period in which she is not yet remarried. If the annulment would evaporate upon divorce, the entire concern of *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* would be pointless — for the annulment would need to be operative precisely in that post-divorce moment.

What the *Gemara* is now going to focus on, however, is a more fundamental question: before we even get to the husband's annulment, how does this *neder* manage to remain in abeyance at all? If her *ma'aseh yadayim* are not hers at the time she makes the vow, should the *neder* not be null and void on the spot? Why is it not simply extinguished the moment it is uttered?

## Shmuel & Ownership

### Shmuel's Ruling

"*Amar Shmuel, halacha k'Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*" — Shmuel rules in accordance with *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*, affirming both that the annulment is effective in the post-divorce period and that we must be concerned about what happens if they divorce, even though the *neder* has not taken effect at the time of its utterance.

The *Gemara* immediately draws a comparison to *hekdesh*: "*l'meimra d'kasavar Shmuel adam makdish davar shelo ba l'olam?*" — is Shmuel thereby implying that a person can sanctify something that does not yet exist? The parallel is direct: when she declares her *ma'aseh yadayim konam*, those products are not yet hers — they belong to the husband by right. Yet *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* holds that the *neder* will take effect once she reclaims them upon divorce, just as if someone were to declare an animal he does not yet own as *hekdesh*, expecting it to take effect once he acquires it.

### Hekdesh Challenge

But this creates a contradiction, for the *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: "*hamakdish ma'asei yedei ishto, harei zu osa v'ocheles*" — if a husband himself sanctifies his wife's labor as *hekdesh*, she may continue working and consuming the proceeds without concern. The reason: at minimum in one reading of the case, the husband is not providing her food, and therefore he holds no entitlement to her *ma'aseh yadayim*. Since his rights to her labor depend on his maintenance of her, absent that maintenance his *hekdesh* cannot take hold on something he does not own. She also has the recourse of declaring "*eini nizonis v'eini osa*" — I will not be supported by you and I will not work for you — thereby freeing herself from the arrangement and leaving him with no claim to her labor.

The *baraita* continues with the *mosar* — the surplus beyond her basic obligation. Here, curiously, the text seems to assume the husband *is* entitled to it, with *Rabbi Meir* declaring it *hekdesh* and *Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar* declaring it *chullin*, non-sanctified. The underlying dispute, as the *Gemara* in *Ketubot* works out through a rather forced *ukimta*, appears to concern whether the husband's *hekdesh* can take effect on something not yet produced — a *davar shelo ba le'olam*. *Rabbi Meir* holds it can; *Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar* holds it cannot.

"*V'amar Shmuel halacha k'Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar*" — and Shmuel rules like *Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar*: one cannot sanctify a *davar shelo ba le'olam*. This is striking. In one place Shmuel rules like *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*, apparently validating a *neder* on something not yet in the woman's possession, even potentially years in the future. In another, he rules like *Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar*, explicitly rejecting the capacity to make *hekdesh* on something not yet existing. How are these two rulings consistent?

One might attempt to resolve this by reading Shmuel's ruling in favor of *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* narrowly — perhaps he was only endorsing *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*'s dismissal of *Rabbi Akiva*'s concern about the *hadafah*, affirming that the husband is entitled to the surplus as well and that there is therefore no danger of inadvertent transgression. But the *Gemara* rejects this reading: if that were all Shmuel intended, he should have simply ruled like the *Tanna Kamma* — who says the same thing — or stated explicitly that the law does not follow *Rabbi Akiva* regarding the *hadafah*. Saying "the law follows *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*" plainly implies endorsement of that opinion's central and distinctive claim: that the husband must annul lest the *neder* take effect upon divorce. And that claim presupposes that such a *neder* can remain in abeyance and later take effect — which looks very much like sanctifying a *davar shelo ba le'olam*.

### Konamos Different

*Rav Yosef* offers a resolution that will be familiar from an earlier *sugya*: "*shani konamos*" — vows expressed through the *konam* formula are different. The principle he invokes is that a person can make another's fruit forbidden to himself, even without owning it. Since *nedarim* can operate on objects one does not own — making them forbidden *to oneself* — they can also apply to things that do not yet exist. This is in contrast to *hekdesh*, where the act of sanctification is fundamentally one of ownership transfer, which requires that one actually possess the object being transferred.

## Hands as Entity

### Abaye Challenge

*Abaye* presses back on *Rav Yosef*'s solution with precision: "*bishlama adam oser peiros chaveiro alav* — granted, I can make your fruit forbidden to me, *sheharei adam oser peirosav al chaveiro* — because you can also make your own fruit forbidden to someone else." The logic requires that at least one of two relationships hold: either the person making the *neder* controls the object (forbidding his own fruit to others), or he controls himself (forbidding himself from another's fruit). But there must be a genuine hook somewhere.

What the woman is attempting, however, is something different: she is trying to make her *ma'aseh yadayim* — which (a) does not yet exist, and (b) she will not be entitled to until some indefinite future point if and when she is divorced — forbidden *to her husband*. That is doubly problematic. She neither controls the object (it is not yet produced and belongs to the husband by lien) nor is she making it forbidden to herself. The rule that *nedarim* work on things one doesn't own applies only when the one making the *neder* is making it forbidden to himself. But forbidding something to another person — "*eino oser peiros chaveiro al chaveiro*" — that she cannot do. So we are back to the fundamental question: how does *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*'s position work at all?

### Physical Hands

The *Gemara* answers: "*ela amar Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua b'omeres yikdeshu yadai l'oseihem*" — the resolution lies in the formulation of the vow itself. When the woman declares her hands forbidden, she is not making a vow about an abstract future object that does not exist. She is making a vow about her hands — the physical, present, tangible hands — declaring them forbidden for what they produce, *l'ma'aseihem*. The hands are here, in the world, right now. Whatever the precise phrasing — and the *Gemara* notes that this phrase may have originated in the context of actual *hekdesh* sanctification, where *l'oseihem* could refer to the Creator, but is here being repurposed or applied by analogy — the operative point is "*yadai isneihu b'olam*": the hands exist in the world.

This answer addresses one dimension of the *davar shelo ba le'olam* problem — that the product has not yet been produced. By re-framing the object of the *neder* as the hands themselves rather than their future output, the *Gemara* resolves the question of non-existence. But the second dimension of the problem immediately reasserts itself: "*meshubada yadehah l'ba'al*" — her hands are subject to the husband's lien. If the *neder* is to take effect only *lich'she-tisgaresh*, upon divorce, we are once again dealing with a future contingency that she does not presently control. How, given Shmuel's position that one cannot sanctify a *davar shelo ba le'olam*, does this work?

## Analogies

### Field Analogy

*Rabbi Ila* attempts to demonstrate through analogy that the concept should be intuitively workable. He envisions someone saying to a friend: "*sadeh zu she-ani mocher lach, lech-she-ekachena mimmeka takdish*" — "this field I am about to sell you, when I buy it back from you I will sanctify it." *Rabbi Ila* assumes that this pre-sanctification would indeed take effect at the moment of repurchase. The intuitive basis is that at the time of the declaration, the field is fully in the owner's control; he could sanctify it right now if he wished. He is therefore performing a genuine act of sanctification in the present, with the effect deferred to the moment the field returns to his control after the intervening sale.

### Yirmiyah Challenge

*Rabbi Yirmiyah* challenges this analogy sharply: "*mi dami?*" — how can you compare the two cases? In the field case, at the moment the declaration is made, the field is entirely in the owner's control — "*hashta be-yadeih*" — and he could sanctify it immediately if he chose. But is a married woman similarly positioned with respect to her *ma'aseh yadayim*? She is not. The moment she is married, she no longer has the capacity to freely sanctify her labor. The more apt analogy, *Rabbi Yirmiyah* argues, would be: "*sadeh zu she-macharti lach, lech-she-ekachena mimmeka takdish*" — "this field I have already sold you, when I buy it back it will be sanctified." But this formulation exposes the problem rather than solving it: if you have already sold the field, you cannot now sanctify it. The past ownership is irrelevant; what matters is the present, and right now you have no rights to it. The same is true for the woman.

### Collateral Analogy

*Rav Pappa* takes a different approach, arguing that *Rabbi Yirmiyah*'s counter-analogy is too extreme in the other direction. When one sells a field outright, "*pesika miltayhu*" — the matter is entirely settled, the field is completely out of the seller's hands. But the woman's situation is not like that. The husband has a lien on her *ma'aseh yadayim*, but it is not the case that her hands have been fully alienated from her. She retains title; it is just that the husband's lien encumbers all benefits flowing from that title. A better analogy, *Rav Pappa* proposes, is: "*sadeh zu she-mashkanti lach, lech-she-efdenah mimmeka takdish*" — "this field that I have given you as collateral, when I redeem it from you it will be sanctified." In this case the field has not been sold; the owner retains title, but the holder of the collateral is entitled to all its produce. This maps neatly onto the wife's situation: she retains title to her hands, but the husband, by virtue of the marital arrangement, is entitled to all their productive output. *Rav Pappa* assumes intuitively that since title remains with the owner, one has sufficient grounds to make a declaration today that will take effect upon redemption.

### Control Objection

*Rav Sheshet bar Rav Idi* objects: "*mi dami? sadeh beyado l'fdotah*" — the field analogy still fails because in the collateral case, it is in the owner's power to redeem the field. If he gathers enough money, he can pay off the lender and reclaim the field. The decision to redeem is his. But "*ishah beyadah l'garesh?*" — is it in the woman's power to bring about a divorce? It is not. The husband decides whether to divorce her. She cannot compel the situation. Since she is not in a position to reclaim her *ma'aseh yadayim* through her own action, how can her present declaration have any operative force?

*Rav Sheshet* then attempts a refined analogy: a field given as collateral for a period of ten years, during which redemption is contractually prohibited. If the owner declares "*lichi afdena mimcha titkadesh*" — "when I do redeem it after the ten years it will be sanctified" — that should work, since after the ten-year period has elapsed the right to redemption automatically comes into effect. At least there is a fixed endpoint. But this only sharpens the problem with the woman's case.

### No End-Point

*Rav Ashi* presses further: "*hatam kayitz*" — in the field case, there is a defined terminus. After ten years, the period of encumbrance ends by its own terms. But "*ishah mi it lah kaitza?*" — by the woman, there is no fixed end-point at all. The marriage could last indefinitely. There is no moment that is structurally guaranteed to arrive at which she will regain her *ma'aseh yadayim*. If the woman lacks the capacity to redeem her labor and there is no fixed point at which the situation must resolve in her favor, what possible basis does she have for making a declaration today that will take effect in that indeterminate future?

## Kedushat Haguf

### Identity Transform

Having exhausted the analogies, *Rav Ashi* now provides the genuine resolution: "*shani konamos, d'hachi kedushat haguf dami*" — *konamos* are different, because they operate like *kedushat haguf*.

To understand this, one must distinguish between two modes of sanctification. *Kedushat damim* — sanctification of monetary value — is what occurs when one donates a field or animal to *bedek habayit*, Temple maintenance. The object is not fundamentally transformed; it simply changes ownership, in effect entering *hekdesh*'s account, to be sold and converted into funds. Because this is a transaction in the domain of ownership, all the standard rules governing ownership apply: one can only sanctify what one owns, liens can block the transfer, and so forth.

*Kedushat haguf* — sanctification of the body or substance of the object — is categorically different. When one consecrates an animal as a *korban*, its very identity is transformed. It is no longer merely a cow that belongs to *hekdesh*; it has become an *olah*, a sacrifice. The *kedushah* has redefined what it is, not merely who holds title to it. Because the sanctification is operating on the plane of identity rather than ownership, the ordinary rules of ownership and lien do not apply in the same way.

The *Gemara* invokes the principle stated by Rava: "*hekdesh chametz veshichrur mafki'a midei shi'abud*" — sanctification, *chametz*, and the freeing of a slave override existing liens. A *konam*, *Rav Ashi* argues, functions like *kedushat haguf* — it transforms the identity of the object rather than simply transferring its ownership. And because the woman retains title to her hands even though the husband holds a lien on their output, she has just enough of a foothold to invoke this identity-transforming power. The husband's monetary lien cannot block a process that is not operating in the monetary domain of ownership.

### Slave Example

The case of the slave provides the most vivid illustration. Suppose a creditor holds a lien on a slave owned by his debtor. If the debtor were to attempt to sell that slave to a third party, the creditor could intervene: the lien encumbers the slave, the debtor lacks the freedom to transfer ownership against the creditor's interest. But if the debtor frees the slave, the result is different. Freeing a slave is not a transfer of ownership from one party to another — it is a transformation of the slave's fundamental status. He is no longer a slave; he is now a free man. The creditor cannot assert that his monetary lien gives him rights over a free person. The process of changing the slave's status operates on a different plane from the monetary arrangements, and because the owner still retains title despite the lien, the transformation takes effect. The creditor may still be owed money, and in principle he retains a claim, but he may well lose the practical ability to enforce it through that particular asset.

### Hekdesh Chametz

The same logic applies when an encumbered animal is consecrated as a *korban*. The creditor cannot interpose his monetary lien to prevent the animal from achieving its new identity as a sacrifice. And similarly with *chametz*: if someone failed to destroy his *chametz* before *Pesach* and the *chametz* is now *assur behana'ah*, a non-Jewish creditor operating within the halachic framework cannot claim his lien on the *chametz* remains actionable, because the *chametz*'s identity has fundamentally changed. Its transformation into something forbidden overrides prior monetary claims.

In each of these cases, the key feature is that the one effecting the transformation retains some form of title even if a lien encumbers the object's use. That residual title is sufficient to activate the identity-transforming power, and once activated, monetary liens fall away as irrelevant.

Applied to the *mishnah*: the woman's declaration of *konam* on her hands is, by the mechanism *Rav Ashi* describes, not merely a declaration of ownership-based prohibition but an invocation of *kedushat haguf*-like power through the *konam* formula. Since she retains title to her hands even during the marriage — it is only the *benefit* of her labor that the husband has a lien on — that title is sufficient for the *konam* to take hold and transform their status. The husband's lien, being a monetary arrangement, cannot block a process of identity transformation.

## When Does It Work?

### Divorce Question

This resolution, however, immediately generates a new difficulty. The *Gemara* asks: "*ee hachi, lama lee shema yigarshena?*" — if *konamos* carry this identity-transforming power that can override liens, why does *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* frame his entire concern around the scenario of divorce? If the *konam* works by *kedushat haguf* and thereby mafki'a the husband's lien, it should take effect right now, not only upon divorce.

The *Gemara* responds by emending the reading of *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*: "*tani ve'od shema yigarshena*" — read the text as saying "and furthermore, lest he divorce her," meaning that the *konam* does take effect immediately, and the concern about divorce is an additional consideration layered on top, not the sole or primary mechanism. The *Ran* grapples with this interpretation, acknowledging that it sits somewhat uneasily in context. For if one accepts that the *konam* takes effect right now — which *Rav Ashi*'s principle would support — then the *Tanna Kamma* and *Rabbi Akiva* simply do not accept that the *konam* has this power. The *Tanna Kamma*, in particular, likely holds that while *kedushat haguf* does *mafki'a midei shi'abud* in cases of actual *korban*, *hekdesh*, or *shichrur*, a mere *neder* — even one formulated as a *konam* — does not carry that same transformative capacity, and therefore the husband's lien blocks it entirely.

*Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*'s response, as rendered after the *Gemara*'s emendation, is that the *konam* does work now by virtue of this principle, and *uve'od* — and even if one does not accept the full philosophical argument that it takes effect immediately — one should at the very least be concerned that it will become operative upon divorce. The circularity of that position is not lost on the *Gemara*: if one rejects the underlying principle, one would presumably reject the post-divorce concern as well, since both rest on the same foundation. How the *Ran* resolves this is its own discussion, but it is a notably striking way for the *sugya* to conclude — with the logical tension between the two positions left in some degree of productive suspension.
