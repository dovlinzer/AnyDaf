# Hullin 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Harlequin ducks & sinews

### Inkurei birds identified

The daf opens two lines from the top, continuing the discussion of conditions affecting birds that do not render them a *tereifah*. Having already addressed cases such as the intestines falling out but remaining attached, or the stomach rupturing, the Gemara now turns to further scenarios. The case at hand involves a basket of birds: *hahu tsana de'inkurei*, a basket of *inkurei* birds that came before Rava. The term *tsana* ordinarily means basket, but the identity of the *inkurei* birds requires explanation.

Rashi, citing the responsa of the Geonim, describes these birds as black waterfowl with white splotches on the forehead — *of shakhor hu*, a black bird, *veshel mayim hu*, a waterfowl, *uve-mitskho khavarbarot levanot*, with white markings on its forehead. Rashi explains that the name *inkurei* derives from a root meaning spotted or splotched, likening the appearance to puncture marks against the black background. He adds, remarkably specifically, *u-ve-ir ha-zot yesh mehem* — in this very city, referring to his location in France, such birds are found. ArtScroll identifies these as harlequin ducks, a description consistent with Rashi's characterization.

### Rava checks sinew cluster

The Gemara records that Rava examined these birds: *badke Rava bi-tsomet ha-gidim*, Rava checked the cluster of sinews in the bird's leg. Apparently some broken leg bones were present among the birds, bringing the discussion into the orbit of our sugya. *va'achshereih* — and he declared them kosher, meaning the cluster had not been severed. The implication, of course, is that had the cluster been severed, there would have been a halachic problem even in the case of a bird.

### Sinew cluster anatomy

To understand what is at stake, it is necessary to appreciate the anatomy of the *tsomet ha-gidim* and how it functions as a potential *tereifah* in mammals. By cows, sheep, and other large animals — although curiously absent from the *mishnayot* that enumerate the various *tereifot* in our chapter, though alluded to elsewhere in the Gemara and destined to become a central focus in the next *perek* — there exists a *tereifah* involving the broken hind leg. The relevant break is *min ha-arkuva u-lemala*, from the lower knee joint and above. In four-legged animals there are effectively two hinge points in the hind leg; the *arkuva* refers to the lower joint, essentially the ankle. A bone broken at or above that point renders the animal a *tereifah*, which is itself somewhat surprising since it does not appear to be a gravely serious injury, and it is not listed explicitly among the enumerated *tereifot* of our chapter. Nevertheless, this is what emerges from the *mishna* in the next *perek*.

Alongside the broken bone, there is a second *tereifah* in this anatomical region: *tsomet ha-gidim*, the cluster of sinews located near this lower ankle joint. If this cluster has been severed or removed, even without a break in the bone itself, the animal is a *tereifah*. Thus there are two overlapping *tereifot* in this region of the animal: the fractured hind leg above the ankle and the severed sinew cluster. The question that now drives the Gemara's analysis is whether these same *tereifot* apply by analogy to birds, given the general principle that whatever renders a mammal a *tereifah* likewise renders a bird a *tereifah*.

## Dislocated leg rules

### Rav Yehuda's teaching

The Gemara now introduces the teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *shmutat yad bivehemah kesherah, shmutat yerech bivehemah tereifah*. A dislocated forearm of a large animal is kosher, but a dislocated hind leg is a *tereifah*. This was in fact mentioned earlier in the sugya as one of the *sheva shmaytata* — the seven teachings — with the phrase *buka de'atma deshaf miduchteih*, the hind thigh bone that was dislocated from the hip. Thus the dislocated rear leg of a *behemah* is a *tereifah*.

### Application to birds

By logical extension, *shmutat yerech be'of tereifah* — a dislocated thigh bone of a bird is likewise a *tereifah*. Here a point of anatomical interest arises. Birds do not have both forelegs and hindlegs as quadrupeds do; rather, their wings correspond to the forelimbs, and their feet correspond to the hind legs. The thigh bone of a bird connects remarkably high — essentially near the spinal cord — which is anatomically coherent given that the hip joint in all vertebrates represents the base of the spinal structure. Thus the *tereifah* of the dislocated hind leg has been applied to the bird's leg by analogy to the cow's hind leg, just as the bird's wing corresponds to the cow's foreleg.

### Dislocated wing

The Gemara then extends the discussion to the wing: *shmutat gaff be'of tereifah*, a dislocated wing renders a bird a *tereifah*. This is immediately surprising. A dislocated foreleg in a mammal is kosher, and the wing is the bird's analog to the foreleg — so why would a dislocated wing be a *tereifah*?

The answer is that it is not intrinsically problematic, but rather: *chayshinan shema nikvah hareiah*, we are concerned that the dislocation of the wing may have punctured the lung. Given that the wing is located in roughly the same region of the body as the lung, the violence of the dislocation may have caused such a puncture. It is therefore not an independent *tereifah* but a concern about a secondary consequence.

This immediately raises a question: the *mishna* did not mention lungs as a concern for birds. Nor did it list many of the additional *tereifot* we have been discussing. The position that whatever applies to a *behemah* applies to a bird — itself largely absent from the explicit text of the *mishna* — enormously extends the list, and even within that framework the Gemara is now applying *tereifot* involving hind legs, which had not been explicitly enumerated even for mammals in this context. The remainder of this daf will challenge several of these applications.

Since the concern about a dislocated wing is only a potential secondary consequence — the lung may or may not have been punctured — it is possible to resolve the question empirically. And so: *shmuel amar tivdeik*, Shmuel says, check the lung. *v'chen amar rebbe yochanan tivdeik*, and Rabbi Yochanan likewise says, examine it. Do not declare it a *tereifah* outright; inspect the lung and rule accordingly.

## Do birds have lungs?

### Chizkiya vs. Yochanan

At this point the Gemara introduces a startling statement: *chizkiya amar ein reia l'of*, Chizkiya said that birds have no lungs. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *yesh lo*, they do have a lung, *v'yeshna k'aleh shel vered*, and it resembles a small rose leaf. It is located *bein agapayim*, between the wings.

### Lungs protected by ribs

The Gemara asks: *mai ein reia l'of?* What exactly does Chizkiya mean when he says birds have no lungs? If he means literally that birds possess no lungs whatsoever — *ilima d'leis lei klal* — that is empirically refuted: *ve'ha qhazina d'is lei*, we can see that they have them. Anyone who has ever cut open a chicken can observe the lung.

Perhaps then Chizkiya means that the lung is halachically irrelevant — *ela d'lo mitreifei* — that we do not apply lung-related *tereifot* to birds? But this too is untenable, since Levi's comprehensive teaching explicitly states that *treifa she'manu chachamim b'vehema k'negdan b'of*, all the *tereifot* enumerated for large animals apply equally to birds, and birds even have additional *tereifot* beyond those.

The Gemara therefore reinterprets Chizkiya: *ela hachi ka'amar, ein lo lo linpol v'lo l'chamer*. He must mean that in two specific scenarios one need not worry about the bird's lung. When an animal falls from a height and we require examination of the internal organs, perhaps one need not examine the lung of a bird, since the rib cage forms a protective dome around it. Similarly, when an animal is exposed to fire and the concern is *l'chamer* — whether the organs have become shriveled or discolored from heat — perhaps the ribs protect the lung sufficiently that it need not be examined. As Rav Chana explains: *ho'il v'rov tzaloteha m'ginot aleha*, because the majority of its ribs protect it, and Rashi notes that the ribs create an actual structural gap between themselves and the organs within, a protective dome rather than merely a physical barrier.

However, the Gemara objects: since Rabbi Yochanan responds to Chizkiya by specifying that the bird *does* have a lung, and that it looks like a small leaf located between the wings, it seems clear that Chizkiya was in fact claiming that birds have no lung at all. *miklal d'chizkiya savar d'leis lei* — by implication, Chizkiya truly believed birds lack lungs.

### Chizkiya dismissed

The Gemara then delivers its verdict. They say in the West — in Eretz Yisrael — in the name of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina: *midivrei shel Rebbe nikar she'eino baki b'tarnigolei* — from the words of this great *rebbe*, it is apparent that he was not well-versed in the anatomy of chickens. This is quite a striking formulation. The Gemara goes out of its way to preserve and then dismiss Chizkiya's opinion, and one can speculate as to how such an error arose. One possibility is that Chizkiya was entirely book-learned on this matter: noting that the extensive lists of *tereifot* by animals include lung-related conditions while the parallel discussion for birds does not, he may have inferred that birds simply have no lungs. Alternatively, even if he examined a bird, without knowing what to look for — having been accustomed to the large, prominent lung of a cow — he may have looked at a chicken and failed to recognize the small, leaf-like structure for what it was. As Rabbi Yochanan's description implies, someone who did not know what he was looking for could easily overlook it.

What is particularly instructive is that the Gemara records this opinion at all. Perhaps it does so precisely because others might make the same mistake, whether from insufficient anatomical knowledge or from reading the texts without looking at actual animals. The quick and unequivocal rejection — he simply did not know his chickens — stands as an important corrective.

## Dislocated thigh debate

### Rav Huna: kosher

Having resolved the digression about bird lungs, the Gemara returns to the core question of the dislocated leg. *amar rav huna amar rav: shmutat yerech be'of keshera* — Rav Huna said in the name of Rav that a dislocated thigh bone of a bird is in fact *keshera*, kosher. This directly contradicts the earlier statement that by analogy to a *behemah*, a dislocated thigh of a bird should be a *tereifah*. The Gemara now undertakes an extended analysis: do we compare birds to *behemot* in this regard, or not?

### Different traditions

Raba bar Rav Huna confronted his father with a conflicting tradition: *ve-ha-rabbanan de-atu mi-Pumbedita amar Rav Yehuda mishmeih de-Rav* — the sages who came from Pumbedita transmitted a teaching of Rav Yehuda in Rav's name stating precisely the opposite: *shmutat yerech be'of treifa*, that a dislocated thigh in a bird is a *tereifah*. Thus two traditions circulate in Rav's name, one permitting and one forbidding.

### Rivers metaphor

Rav Huna responded to his son: *beri, nahara nahara u-pashtei* — my son, every river and its tributaries. This phrase, encountered earlier in the Gemara (18b), typically conveys a form of halachic pluralism: different places maintain different practices, each rooted in legitimate authority, and we should be tolerant of that diversity. The difficulty here is that this is not a case of two communities following different authorities — it is a case of two traditions claiming to report what the same authority, Rav, actually said. To invoke *nahara nahara u-pashtei* here is somewhat unusual, since only one tradition can accurately reflect what Rav said.

Rashi attempts to reconcile this by proposing that Rav genuinely held that a dislocated thigh was kosher, consistent with Rav Huna's tradition. The competing tradition from Pumbedita reflects not a different ruling by Rav, but rather Rav's tolerance of communities that had adopted the stricter practice — he did not override their custom. Rashi thus frames it as a matter of *minhag*, though one must acknowledge that the term here is better understood as *nihug*, adopted practice, rather than custom in the narrow sense. What one community treated as *tereifah* and another treated as *kasher* is not merely a divergence of custom but of substantive halachic practice. And the difficulty Rav's son identified is real: these are not simply different practices in different places, they are different reports of what Rav himself said.

### Rabbi Abba's inquiry

The narrative continues with Rabbi Abba, who encountered Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba examining the *tsomet ha-gidim* of a bird. Rabbi Abba challenged him: *lama lei l'mar kulei hai* — why are you troubling yourself with this? Rav Huna said in Rav's name that a dislocated thigh of a bird is kosher, which would seem to imply that we do not draw the analogy between birds and *behemot* with respect to the hind leg, and therefore the *tsomet ha-gidim* of a bird should likewise present no concern.

Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba responded: *ana matnita yadana* — I know our *mishna*. The *mishna* states that *behema shenichtechu ragleha min ha'arkuva ulemata kesheira, min ha'arkuva ulemala pesula* — an animal whose leg is severed below the ankle joint is kosher, above the ankle is invalid — *vechen shenital tzomet hagidim*, and similarly if the cluster of sinews has been removed. And he possessed a tradition that Rav himself had said *vechen ba'of* — the same applies to birds. Thus Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba held that the comparison to *behemot* was operative, and if so, a severed *tsomet ha-gidim* is a problem in birds.

This creates an apparent internal contradiction within Rav's teachings: on one hand, Rav supposedly said that a dislocated thigh of a bird is kosher — implying we do not draw the analogy to *behemot*; on the other hand, Rav supposedly said that the severed *tsomet ha-gidim* of a bird is a *tereifah* — implying we do draw that analogy. Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba fell silent, unable to resolve the contradiction.

### Dislocated vs. severed

Rabbi Abba himself then proposed the resolution: *dilma shani leih bein shmuta l'chatucha* — perhaps for Rav there is a distinction between dislocated and severed. The analogy to *behemot* is not applied uniformly across all hind-leg *tereifot*. When it comes to a dislocated thigh, we do not draw the parallel to *behemot*, and therefore a dislocated thigh of a bird is kosher. When it comes to the severed *tsomet ha-gidim*, we do draw the parallel, and that remains a *tereifah* even in a bird. The application of mammalian *tereifot* to birds is thus selective rather than categorical in this domain.

### Rav's explicit rule

Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba then challenged Rabbi Abba: *v'at mefaraish shemateta d'Rav* — you are speculating about Rav's position based on your own *sevara*? To which Rabbi Abba responded: Rav did not need to be reconstructed through logical inference. *befeirosh amar rav: shmuta kesheira, chatucha pesula* — Rav explicitly stated this very distinction: dislocated is kosher, severed is *pasul*. And, Rabbi Abba adds, *v'al titmah* — do not be astonished by this. One cannot make a *kal v'chomer* argument from one type of injury to another: *chatucha mikan u'metah, chatucha mikan v'chayah* — you can sever an animal in one location and it will die, sever it in another location and it will survive. The biology of death from injury is not amenable to simple hierarchical inference, and so the distinction Rav drew is coherent on its own terms.

## Abba reports to Zeira

### Full recapitulation

When Rabbi Abba subsequently made his way up to Eretz Yisrael, he found Rabbi Zeira sitting and teaching: *amar Rav Huna amar Rav, shmutat hayerech b'of tereifa* — that a dislocated thigh in a bird is a *tereifah*. Rabbi Abba addressed him: *chayei d'mar, m'yoma d'salik mar l'hacha* — by the life of the master, from the day you made *aliyah* and left us in Bavel, let me tell you what transpired. *hevei lan pitcha puma kamei d'rav huna* — we had the opportunity to open our mouths before Rav Huna, to put the question to him directly. And *v'shailneih*, we asked him — and he told us *shmutat yerech ba'of kesheira*, that it is kosher.

Rabbi Abba then recounts the entire sequence of events: the encounter with Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba examining the *tsomet ha-gidim*; his challenge based on Rav Huna's ruling; Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba's counter-appeal to the *mishna* and his tradition that Rav said *vechen ba'of*; the silence that followed when the contradiction within Rav's teachings was pointed out; Rabbi Abba's own proposal that Rav distinguished between dislocated and severed; and finally the revelation that this was not merely logical inference but Rav's explicit formulation — *befeirosh amar Rav: shmuta kesheira, chatucha pesula*. The conclusion reached in Bavel was clear: the *tsomet ha-gidim* is a problem, but a dislocated thigh is not. What is striking throughout is the recurring theme of competing traditions — not merely halachic debates between authorities, but different reports claiming to represent what the same authority said.

### Zeira's traditions

Rabbi Abba then turned to Rabbi Zeira: *ve'at mah beyadecha* — and you, what traditions have you accumulated here in Eretz Yisrael? For his part, Rabbi Zeira reported two traditions. First: *rav chiya bar ashi amar rav, shmutat yerech ba'of treifa* — yet another tradition in Rav's name declaring a dislocated thigh to be a *tereifah*. Second, and moving beyond the debate about Rav: *rabbi yaakov bar idi amar rabbi yochanan, shmutat yerech ba'of treifa* — Rabbi Yochanan himself held that a dislocated thigh in a bird is a *tereifah*.

Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi added a remarkable comment: *ilmalei havei Rabbi Yochanan be'atreih dechorei bah chavruta lehatiro, lo parchi* — had Rabbi Yochanan been present in the place where his colleagues ruled to permit it, he would not have resisted — meaning, had he been in the presence of authorities of sufficient stature who ruled leniently, he would have deferred to them. The implication is that those who permitted it were of such weight that Rabbi Yochanan would have yielded.

### Chanina's chicken

This leads to the identification of those greater authorities. *de'amar rabbi chanina amar rebbi: shmutat yerech ba'of kesheira* — Rabbi Chanina transmitted a ruling of *rebbi* himself, the editor of the *mishna*, that a dislocated thigh is kosher. And this was not merely a theoretical position: Rabbi Chanina once had a chicken whose thigh was dislocated, he brought it before *rebbi*, and *rebbi* declared it permissible. Rabbi Chanina preserved the chicken in salt and used it as a teaching tool, taking it out before his students and saying *zeh hitir li Rebbi, zeh hitir li Rebbi* — this is the chicken that *rebbi* declared permissible for me. The concrete, tangible demonstration — here is the actual chicken — reflects something about the nature of halachic transmission, and about Rabbi Chanina's commitment to anchoring abstract rulings in physical, teachable reality.

Given all of this — the tradition from Rav's own explicit statement, the story of *rebbi*'s ruling, and the implication that Rabbi Yochanan would have deferred had he been present at such a ruling — the Gemara would seem to be heading unmistakably toward the conclusion that a dislocated thigh is kosher.

## Ruling: treifah

### Widespread practice

And yet: *leis hilchasa k'chol hani shmaatsa* — we do not rule in accordance with any of this entire preceding discussion.

### Trachea hole sizes

The Gemara pivots to a related discussion that illuminates the principle underlying this reversal. Rabbi Yossi ben Nehorai asked Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *kideira kama b'kama* — how large a hole in the trachea constitutes a *tereifah*? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi replied that it is an explicit *mishna*: up to the size of an Italian *issar* coin is acceptable; beyond that it is *pasul*.

### Sheep survival case

Rabbi Yossi ben Nehorai then challenged this with an empirical case: did we not have a young sheep in our neighborhood that had a hole in its trachea, and they fashioned for it a kind of reed covering to bandage the hole, *v'chayesa* — and it survived? How can an animal with a hole in the trachea above the permissible size be a *tereifah*, when this one lived even with such a hole?

The challenge rests on a principle that the Gemara has previously asserted: if an animal is truly a *tereifah*, it cannot survive even with medical intervention. Indeed, earlier in the Gemara, when discussing why a hunter's strike that kills an animal in a certain location does not generate a *tereifah* designation, the Gemara responded that if one were to apply medicine the animal would live — implying that a genuine *tereifah* cannot be saved by medicine. Here then is an animal with a textbook *tereifah* wound that was treated and survived.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's response is to cite the case of *rebbi shimon ben chalafta* and his chicken with the dislocated thigh, similarly treated with a reed splint and surviving. That case is a *halacha ravacha b'Yisrael* — a widespread practice throughout Israel — that a dislocated thigh in a bird is a *tereifah*, regardless of the empirical evidence of survival. How do we deal with the survival? *ela mai is lach l'meimar: toch shneim asar chodesh havei* — one must say it died within twelve months.

### Twelve-month rule

This is where the Gemara formally introduces and crystallizes the principle of the twelve-month timeframe: a *tereifah* does not necessarily die immediately. The claim is that a *tereifah* animal will not survive beyond twelve months. Thus when an animal that is halachically a *tereifah* is observed to have survived for some period, the response is not that the *tereifah* status is wrong, but that it will die within a year. *hacha nami toch shneim asar chodesh havei* — the same answer applies to the sheep with the tracheal hole: it survived for a time, but we must assume it died within the year.

This ruling carries significant weight beyond the specific case. After all of the extensive discussion — multiple traditions in Rav's name, the explicit ruling attributed to Rav himself, the story of *rebbi* and Rabbi Chanina's salted chicken — the Gemara nonetheless reverses course. The driving force appears to be not the theoretical halachic analysis but rather *halacha ravacha b'Yisrael*, the widespread established practice. The phrase *b'Yisrael* is telling: it suggests not merely rabbinic *psak* but something that has become the deeply embedded communal norm. How such a practice became entrenched — whether through cautious rabbis ruling *l'chumra*, through communities asking *shailos* that consistently produced stringent answers, or through some other dynamic — is not always easy to determine. But the tension between theoretical analysis and accepted practice is real, and the Gemara here strongly weights the latter.

The twelve-month rule itself opens a further layer of complexity. The Gemara's position seems to be that a *tereifah* animal will die within twelve months without exception. This is not merely a statistical claim — it appears to be treated as absolute. The implication is that no individual animal can be an exception, and that medical intervention cannot change the outcome. These positions will generate serious challenges, particularly from the *rishonim*, who will encounter animals with apparent *tereifah* conditions surviving well beyond a year, with and without intervention. The appropriate response to such evidence is already prefigured here: the Gemara does not entertain the possibility of exceptions, and dismisses the contrary evidence by assuming death occurred within the year. That methodological stance — the rejection of empirical counter-evidence in favor of halachic authority — will define the field of *tereifot* for the next two thousand years.

## Shimon tests halacha

### Down feather test

The Gemara introduces a character sketch: *amru alav al Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta she'askan bidvarim haya* — they said of Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta that he was deeply involved in testing things out, that he wanted to subject claims to empirical examination. The Gemara illustrates this with two episodes. The first concerns a position of Rabbi Yehuda stated in our *mishna*: *nitla hanotzah pesula* — if a bird has lost its down, it is *pasul*, a *tereifah*. Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta set out to disprove this.

He had a chicken from which the down had been removed. He kept it near the oven to maintain warmth, and covered it with a smelter's apron — a thick, heavy material used by ironworkers — to keep the bird warm and protected.

### Chicken survives

*Vegidlah knafayim acharonim yoseir min harishonim* — the chicken grew new feathers that were superior to the original ones, and it survived. From this Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta concluded that Rabbi Yehuda was wrong: a bird missing its down is not a *tereifah*, in support of the *chachamim* who disagreed with Rabbi Yehuda's position.

### Treifah improving

The Gemara raises a challenge to this line of reasoning: *vedilma kasavar Reb Yehuda treifa meshabbachas* — perhaps Rabbi Yehuda holds that even a *tereifah* animal can improve in some respects. If so, the chicken's recovery would not disprove *tereifah* status; it would merely demonstrate that a *tereifah* can experience improvement. The Gemara rejects this: *im kein bimidi dimitrefah bah* — even granting that other parts of a *tereifah*'s body might compensate for the original defect, no one would maintain that the specific organ or feature that defined the *tereifah* condition can be restored to a state better than it was originally. Here, the very feathers that were the supposed basis of the *tereifah* were restored in superior form. This goes beyond *treifa meshabbachas* in other dimensions of the animal's health — the injury itself reversed and improved upon itself. On what basis, then, can one call it a *tereifah* at all?

It is worth noting that in this episode, Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta's empirical test served to support the *chachamim* against Rabbi Yehuda — he was not attempting to overturn an established halachic consensus, but rather to vindicate one rabbinic position over another. The second episode is of a rather different character.

## Ant king experiment

### Proverbs experiment

The verse in *mishlei* (Proverbs) instructs: *leich el nemala atzel, re'ei deracheiha, vehocham* — go to the ant, O lazy one, see its ways and become wise. The verse then specifies: *asher ein lah katzin soteir umosheil* — it has no officer, judge, or ruler; yet it prepares its food in the summer without anyone commanding it. This is presented as a *mashal* about industriousness in the absence of external authority. Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta — *askan bidvarim* that he was — decided to test whether the premise of the verse was empirically accurate: *eizel achzei i vadai hu delais lah malka*, let me go and verify whether ants truly have no king.

### Scout ant tagged

*Azal bitkufas Tammuz* — he went in the summer season. He spread his cloak over an anthill to create shade on a sunny day. One ant emerged — a scout — and Rabbi Shimon tagged it with a distinguishing mark, much as a scientist might tag an animal for tracking purposes. The ant returned to the colony, and based on what followed, one can infer it communicated to its fellows that the area outside was shaded and safe: *nefalti tulla*, it's shady out there.

### Scout killed

All the ants emerged from the hill. At that moment Rabbi Shimon removed his cloak, exposing them all to direct sunlight. The ants, discovering they had been misled, turned on the scout: *nafalu lei v'katlei* — they fell upon it and killed it. From this, Rabbi Shimon concluded: *sh'ma mina, leis lehu malka* — ants have no king. The lynching proceeded without any judicial process or royal sanction. Had there been a king, *harman d'malka l'va'u* — the ants would have required a royal decree before executing the offending scout.

### Challenges to proof

Rav Acha the son of Rava challenged Rabbi Shimon's inference before Rav Ashi with three objections. First: *v'dilma malka hava behadayhu* — perhaps the king was himself among the mob that executed the scout, and thus royal authorization was implicitly present. Second: *harman d'malka hava nekita* — perhaps a standing royal decree already existed authorizing immediate execution of any ant that caused harm to the colony. Third: *bein malka l'malka hava* — perhaps the colony happened to be in an interregnum, a period between kings, accounting for the absence of formal royal process. This last challenge is underscored by the verse: *bayamim hahem ein melech b'Yisrael, ish hayashar b'einav ya'aseh* — in those days there was no king in Israel; each man did what was right in his own eyes. A system may normally have kings and law, but in the gaps between rulers, anarchy can prevail.

## Trust authority

### Trust Solomon

*S'moch a-heimnusa d'Shlomo* — just trust King Solomon. The Gemara's answer to Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta is not to refute his experiment on its merits, but to question whether the experiment was necessary at all. If Solomon said it, trust him. Do not conduct empirical tests to verify what authoritative tradition has already established.

The designation *askan bidvarim* — deeply involved in things, one who tests things out — carries a sense that is less than entirely complimentary. It reflects a disposition that the Gemara here seems to view with some ambivalence: a scholar who feels the need to verify received knowledge through direct observation. The Gemara's response to this disposition is fundamentally pre-empirical: knowledge comes from authority, and the authority of a Solomon or of the sages does not require experimental confirmation.

### Tosafot explains

Tosafot, however, reads Rabbi Shimon's motivation more charitably. He says: *shani hacha sheba levarer ulehodia eich yada Shlomo* — this case is different, because Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta came not to disprove Solomon but to confirm him, to demonstrate *how* Solomon knew what he knew. He was not a skeptic with an axe to grind; he accepted the truth of the verse and simply wanted to understand and illustrate the mechanism by which it was true. Even so, the Gemara's response holds: empirical confirmation is unnecessary. Even if you are trying to confirm rather than refute, you should simply trust authority without needing to verify it experimentally.

### Student mocks sages

A parallel case reinforces this point. In *masechet ta'anit*, a student hearing Rabbi Yochanan describe the wonders of the messianic era expressed doubt — implying that without seeing it he could not believe it. Subsequently, the student encountered something in the natural world that seemed to confirm the possibility of Rabbi Yochanan's description. Rabbi Yochanan rebuked him: *im lo ra'isa lo he'emanta, m'lagleg al divrei Chachamim ata* — if you could not believe without seeing it, you are a mocker of the words of the sages. The demand for empirical proof before accepting the teaching of sages is itself presented as a form of disrespect.

The contrast between this student and Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta is the contrast between skepticism and enthusiasm: the student doubted the sages until proven right; Rabbi Shimon accepted them and wanted to confirm them. Yet both are told the same thing: trust, and do not require empirical validation.

### Authority vs. observation

This brings into sharp relief a tension that runs through the entire daf and indeed through the halachic tradition more broadly. The field of *tereifot* as it developed over the following centuries proceeded precisely as the Gemara here would have it: empirical counter-evidence was routinely dismissed, animals that survived in apparent contradiction of *tereifah* status were assumed to have died within the year, and medical intervention was treated as halachically irrelevant. The *rishonim* would struggle with this when confronted with unavoidable empirical challenges — animals with textbook *tereifah* conditions surviving well beyond twelve months, with and without treatment — and they did so within the same methodological framework: not by revising the halachic categories based on new observations, but by finding ways to reconcile or deflect the observations in favor of the inherited tradition.

That framework is precisely what the Gemara here articulates and defends. The twelve-month rule, which the Gemara has now formally introduced as the operative timeframe for *tereifah* survival, will receive further development in the material ahead. The discussion to this point has established the rule's existence and its function as a bulwark against empirical challenge; the next section of the Gemara will examine more closely the specifics of that timeframe and what it demands of us as a halachic category.
