# Ketubot 102 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stepdaughter Support

### Perek 12 Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנּוֹשֵׂא אֶת הָאִשָּׁה וּפָסְקָה עִמּוֹ כְּדֵי שֶׁיָּזוּן אֶת בִּתָּהּ חָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים — חַיָּיב לְזוּנָהּ חָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who marries a woman, and she stipulated with him that he would sustain her daughter** from another man for **five years, is obligated to sustain her** daughter for **five years.**

We begin the twelfth *perek* of *Ketubot* at the bottom of 101b, with the opening *mishnah* of the new chapter. The preceding chapter dealt extensively with the widow's collection from the estate — how she negotiates with the orphans, how assets are liquidated, and the mechanics of collecting the lump-sum *ikar ketubah* at the dissolution of the marriage. We now shift back to the ongoing obligations that subsist during the marriage itself, the *tnai ketubot* — the conditions and responsibilities that the *ketubah* imposes as a living framework. This particular *mishnah*, however, occupies an interesting middle ground: it does not concern the standard *tnai ketubot* imposed automatically by rabbinic legislation, but rather special contractual arrangements a woman might negotiate at the outset of a marriage to protect interests that the standard *ketubah* does not cover.

### Initial Stipulation

The *mishnah* opens: *hanosei et ha'ishah upaskah imo* — a man marries a woman and she arranges with him a specific condition. The initiative here is hers. She has a daughter from a prior marriage or another relationship, and she stipulates before the wedding that her new husband must support that daughter for five years. The husband bears no automatic legal obligation toward a stepdaughter — she is not his child, and the standard conditions of the *ketubah* do not extend to her. So the wife makes this a precondition: *kedei sheyezanet bitah chamesh shanim* — that he undertake to feed her daughter for five years. He agrees in order to marry her. The *mishnah* states simply: *chayav lezanah chamesh shanim* — he is obligated to feed her for five years. The force and mechanism of that obligation is precisely what the *gemara* will examine.

### Remarriage & Dual Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיסֵּת לְאַחֵר, וּפָסְקָה עִמּוֹ כְּדֵי שֶׁיָּזוּן אֶת בִּתָּהּ חָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים חַיָּיב לְזוּנָהּ חָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים. לֹא יֹאמַר הָרִאשׁוֹן: לִכְשֶׁתָּבֹא אֶצְלִי אֱזוּנָהּ, אֶלָּא מוֹלִיךְ לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹתֶיהָ לַמָּקוֹם שֶׁאִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If, in the course of those five years they were divorced and the woman **was married to another** man, **and she stipulated with him that he would sustain her daughter** for **five years, he** too **is obligated to sustain her** for **five years. The first** husband **may not say: When she comes to me, I will sustain her. Rather, he brings her sustenance to her, to the place where her mother** lives.

The *mishnah* continues with the less obvious scenario. She subsequently divorces this first husband and remarries. With the second husband, she again stipulates: *paskah imo kedei sheyezanet bitah chamesh shanim* — you must agree to feed my daughter for five years. He agrees. The *mishnah* states that the second husband is also now obligated for five years of support — and critically, this obligation is entirely independent of the first husband's. The first husband's duty does not dissolve simply because she has remarried.

### First Husband's Liability

The first husband cannot argue: *lichishetavo etzli azanah* — let her come stay with me and I will feed her. He assumed he was obligated only for the duration of the marriage, but that is not what he stipulated. He committed to feeding the daughter for five years, full stop, with no limitation to the period of the marriage. Accordingly, *ella molic lah mezonoteha limkom she'imah* — he must bring the food to wherever the daughter is living, which is now at the second husband's home with her mother.

### Independent Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן לֹא יֹאמְרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם ״הֲרֵי אָנוּ זָנִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאֶחָד״, אֶלָּא אֶחָד זָנָה, וְאֶחָד נוֹתֵן לָהּ דְּמֵי מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise, both of them may not** jointly **say: We will sustain** the girl **as one** in a partnership. **Rather, one sustains her,** providing her with food, while **the other gives her the monetary** value **of the sustenance.**

*Vechain lo yomru shneihem* — nor may the two husbands collude and say: *harei anu zanin otah ke'echad* — between the two of us we will manage to keep her fed, and we will divide the responsibility. Each bears an independent, full obligation. Therefore: *ella echad zanah ve'echad noten lah dmei mezonot* — one actually provides the food, and the other, since she is already being fed, must pay the monetary equivalent of the food costs. Both obligations run simultaneously and neither cancels the other.

### After Stepdaughter Marries

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיסֵּת — הַבַּעַל נוֹתֵן לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת, וְהֵן נוֹתְנִין לָהּ דְּמֵי מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** If the daughter **was married** during this period, her **husband provides her** with the **sustenance** customarily provided by a husband for his wife, **and** the two men obligated to sustain her due to agreements with her mother **provide her with the monetary value of the sustenance.**

The *mishnah* continues: if the daughter herself marries, her husband becomes obligated to feed her, as is standard. Nevertheless, *vehein notnim lah dmei mezonot* — both former stepfathers must still pay the food costs. She is being fed at her husband's table, but both stepfathers continue to pay for that food. The situation resembles what occurs in secular legal contexts as well: if a divorced woman remarries, the prior husband's child support obligation does not automatically terminate simply because the child now lives in a new household.

### After Stepfather Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתוּ — בְּנוֹתֵיהֶן נִיזּוֹנוֹת מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין, וְהִיא נִיזּוֹנֶת מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא כְּבַעֲלַת חוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** If the two husbands of the mother **died, their daughters are sustained from unsold property, and she,** their wife's daughter, whom they agreed to sustain, **is sustained** even **from liened property** that was sold. This is **due to** the fact **that her** legal status is **like** that of **a creditor,** given that he is contractually obligated to support her.

*Meisu* — now both stepfathers have died. The *mishnah* addresses what happens when the estate is opened. The standard *tnai ketubot* includes the obligation to support natural daughters from the estate until they reach adulthood — *mezonot habanot*. So the natural daughters of both Reuven and Shimon are entitled to support from their respective estates. But the stepdaughter also has a claim against both estates for the remainder of her five years of support. How do these claims rank?

*Bnoteihem nizonot minechasim bnei chorin* — the natural daughters can only be supported from unencumbered property, meaning property that is still present in the estate and has not been sold off. *Vehi nizonis minechasim meshubadim* — but the stepdaughter can collect even from *meshubadim*, encumbered property, meaning property that was once in the estate but was subsequently sold to third parties. She has a lien that reaches those purchasers.

The reason is stated explicitly: *mipnei shehi kebaalas chov* — because she stands in the position of a creditor. Someone formally obligated himself to her with a debt, and she may collect from any property on which that debt created a lien. As for the natural daughters: their entitlement is one of the standard *tnai ketubot*, and *Chazal* explicitly ruled that this automatic obligation does not create liens on property sold from the estate. The reasoning is that the support obligation for daughters is inherently indeterminate — one cannot know in advance how many daughters there will be or for how many years the estate will need to support them. A purchaser of property from the estate would have no way to assess what liens might descend upon what he is buying. Protecting those purchasers from an unknowable and potentially unlimited exposure, the rabbis ruled that the daughters' claim applies only to property remaining in the estate. The stepdaughter's claim, by contrast, is a concrete, finite, specified debt — one girl, five years — and carries full liens accordingly.

It is worth noting that this distinction is actually to the natural daughters' benefit. Since they are in any case limited to property remaining in the estate, having an additional claimant who is similarly limited would intensify the competition. The fact that the stepdaughter's claim reaches sold property removes her from competition with the natural daughters over what remains.

### Smart People's Clause

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַפִּקְּחִים הָיוּ כּוֹתְבִים: עַל מְנָת שֶׁאָזוּן אֶת בִּתֵּךְ חָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁאַתְּ עִמִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **The perspicacious** ones **would write** an explicit stipulation into the agreement: I agree **on the condition that I will sustain your daughter for five years** only **as long as you are with me.**

The *mishnah* concludes with an observation: *hapikhin hayu kotvin* — prudent people, when accepting such an obligation, would write: *al menat she'ezon et bitkha chamesh shanim kol zman she'at imi* — on condition that I will feed your daughter for five years only as long as you are with me. *Rashi* explains the clause excludes three scenarios: if I die, if you die, or if we divorce — any of those terminates the obligation. A man who accepts this condition in unconstrained terms finds himself obligated regardless of what happens to the marriage; the wise man conditions it explicitly on the continuation of the marriage itself.

## Admissions & Documents

### Oral Admission Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִתְּמַר: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״חַיָּיב אֲנִי לְךָ מָנֶה״. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חַיָּיב, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It was stated** with regard to **one who says to another: I am obligated to** pay **you one hundred dinars,** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** He is **obligated** to pay, **and Reish Lakish said:** He is **exempt.**

The *gemara* opens with a dispute: *ha'omer l'chavero chayav ani lecha maneh* — if someone says to his friend, "I owe you a hundred *zuz*." Rabbi Yochanan says *chayav* — he is obligated. Resh Lakish says *patur* — he is exempt.

### Conversation vs Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּאָמַר לְהוּ ״אַתֶּם עֵדַיי״, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּקָפָטַר! אִי דְּלָא אָמַר לְהוּ ״אַתֶּם עֵדַיי״, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּקָמְחַיֵּיב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to clarify: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If he said to** the people present: **You are my witnesses, what is the reasoning of Reish Lakish, who exempts** him from payment? He confessed before witnesses that he owes the money. **If he did not say to them: You are my witnesses, what is the reasoning of Rabbi Yoḥanan, who obligates** him to pay?

The *gemara* asks: in precisely what case are they debating? If the person said *atem eidai* — "you be my witnesses" — designating bystanders to formally testify to his admission, then it is established elsewhere that there is no dispute: such a formal admission creates a binding obligation. Conversely, if he merely said it in passing conversation, with no designation of witnesses, it is likewise established elsewhere that such words have no legal weight. So what is the disputed case?

### Written Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּלָא קָאָמַר לְהוּ ״אַתֶּם עֵדַיי״, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ ״חַיָּיב אֲנִי לְךָ מָנֶה בִּשְׁטָר״. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חַיָּיב — אַלִּימָא מִילְּתָא דִשְׁטָרָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לְהוּ ״אַתֶּם עֵדִים״ דָּמֵי. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: פָּטוּר, לָא אַלִּימָא מִילְּתָא דִשְׁטָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** it is a case **where he did not say to** those present: **You are my witnesses.** However, **here we are dealing with** a case **where he said to** the other: **I am obligated to** give **you one hundred dinars,** and he did so **in a contract,** i.e., he gave him an unsigned contract in which he stated that he is obligated to give him one hundred dinars. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: He is obligated** to pay, since his **word** given through **a contract is** legally **as authoritative as one who said to** the bystanders: **You are my witnesses. Reish Lakish said: He is exempt** from payment, because his **word** given through **a contract is** legally **not** sufficiently **authoritative** to be considered a bona fide admission.

The *gemara* answers: *d'amar lei chayav ani lecha maneh b'shtar* — he wrote it in a document, without signing it, without witnesses to the document itself, but wrote in his own handwriting "I owe you a hundred *zuz*" and handed it over. Is such an informal written admission equivalent in weight to a formal oral declaration of *atem eidai*? That is the debate.

It is important to understand the underlying principle. Within *halacha*, a monetary admission creates a legal fact independent of the underlying reality. If someone formally admits to a debt — even one that did not actually occur — the admission itself constitutes an enforceable obligation. *Rashi* notes from other *gemarot* that a signed document with witnesses is unquestionably a valid *shtar*; even a signed document without witnesses creates a *shtar*, albeit without *shiabud*, without liens on sold property, since it is insufficiently public. The question here is narrower: an unsigned, unwitnessed piece of paper in one's own handwriting, handed over. Is that weighty enough to create an obligation?

Rabbi Yochanan holds: *alima milta d'shtara k'man d'amar lehu atem eidai dami* — the written document is as powerful as an explicit oral designation of witnesses. Resh Lakish holds: *lo alima milta d'shtara* — the document is not that powerful; without explicit witness designation, even a written admission does not obligate.

### Mishnah & Written Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַנּוֹשֵׂא אֶת הָאִשָּׁה וּפָסְקָה עִמּוֹ לָזוּן אֶת בִּתָּהּ חָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים, חַיָּיב לְזוּנָהּ חָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים. מַאי לָאו כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a challenge to the opinion of Reish Lakish. **We learned** in the mishna: **One who marries a woman, and she stipulated with him** that he is obligated **to sustain her daughter** for **five years, is obligated to sustain her** for **five years. What, is it not** that the mishna is discussing **a case like this,** where he gave her a contract that lacks proper signatures and it is nevertheless legally binding, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan? If you say otherwise, where is the novelty in the teaching of the mishna?

The *gemara* proposes that our *mishnah* itself supports the position of Rabbi Yochanan. The opening clause — that if a man stipulates to feed the daughter for five years he is obligated — should be unremarkable if he signed a formal *shtar*. The *chiddush*, the novelty, must lie in the mechanism of obligation. The *gemara* therefore reads the *mishnah* as speaking of a case where he wrote an informal written admission, and yet is held obligated — which would vindicate Rabbi Yochanan.

### Stipulations Before Wedding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, בִּשְׁטָרֵי פְסִיקָתָא, וְכִדְרַב גִּידֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** the mishna is referring to a case **of documents of stipulation** that record the amounts that parents agree to provide to their son or daughter, **and** this is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Giddel.**

The *gemara* declines this reading, however. It suggests instead that the *mishnah* concerns *bishtarei psikta* — stipulation documents, the type of financial arrangements negotiated and committed to prior to the wedding. Such obligations, the *gemara* argues, occupy a special legal category and become binding through a mechanism distinct from ordinary debt creation — even where ordinary means would be insufficient.

### In-Law Negotiations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: כַּמָּה אַתָּה נוֹתֵן לְבִנְךָ? כָּךְ וְכָךְ. וְכַמָּה אַתָּה נוֹתֵן לְבִתְּךָ? כָּךְ וְכָךְ. עָמְדוּ וְקִידְּשׁוּ — קָנוּ. הֵן הֵן הַדְּבָרִים הַנִּקְנִים בַּאֲמִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said:** When two families negotiate the terms of marriage for their respective children, one side says to the other: **How much do you give your son?** And the second side answers: **Such and such** amount. **How much do you give your daughter?** And the first side responds: **Such and such** amount. Then, if the son and daughter **arose and performed the betrothal,** all of these obligations are **acquired** and therefore binding. **These are** among **the things that are acquired through words** alone, without the need for an additional act of acquisition. The mishna is referring to a document that records such an agreement.

This category is illustrated from the teaching of Rav Giddel in the name of Rav. When the prospective in-laws — the *shver* and *shvigger* on both sides — gather before the wedding and negotiate their respective financial contributions (*kama ata noten l'vincha* — how much are you giving your son; *kama ata noten l'bitkha* — how much are you contributing to your daughter), and they agree to specific amounts, and then *amdu ve'kidshu* — the couple proceeds and the *kiddushin* takes place — *hen hen had'varim hanniknin b'amira*: these are matters that become binding through speech alone.

Ordinarily, a contractual obligation requires a formal act — a *kinyan*, such as the transfer of a *sudar* (a garment effecting *kinyan chalupin*), or the signing of a witnessed *shtar*. Here there was none of that. Yet the obligation is binding. Why? Because the verbal stipulations were conditions and prerequisites of the marriage, and when the marriage then occurred, the act of *kiddushin* itself validated and formalized those prior commitments. The *kiddushin* is the act that gives the words their binding force — this is what is called *kinyan amira* in this context, not a *kinyan* in the ordinary transactional sense, but the binding of words through the subsequent act to which they were attached as conditions.

One might ask: is the binding force here contingent on the father of the daughter pocketing the *kesef kiddushin*, receiving something tangible that creates his *gmirat da'at* — his settled intent to obligate himself? The *gemara* addresses this directly. Rava says the rule applies even when the daughter is a *bogeret* and pockets the money herself, so the father receives nothing tangible. And certainly it applies to the father of the son, who receives nothing at all from the *kiddushin*. Rather: *b'hahu hana'ah d'kamichtani ahadadi* — the benefit they both receive is the pleasure of having their children married to one another. That satisfaction is the thing of value that creates the *gmirat da'at* and makes the obligation binding.

The *gemara* also clarifies a terminological subtlety. It is noted that the phrase *shtar psikta* is somewhat ironic, since nothing was necessarily written down — the obligation arose through speech followed by *kiddushin*. The *shtar*, if any, was a subsequent recording of what had been agreed, but the *shtar* itself did not effect the obligation. What effected it was the spoken commitment followed by the act of marriage.

## Forms of Obligation

### Pidyon Haben Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, כָּתַב לְכֹהֵן: ״שֶׁאֲנִי חַיָּיב לְךָ חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים״ — חַיָּיב לִיתֵּן לוֹ חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים, וּבְנוֹ אֵינוֹ פָּדוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another challenge to the opinion of Reish Lakish, based upon the following mishna (*Bekhorot* 51a): If **he wrote to a priest** with whom he wants to perform the redemption of his firstborn son: **I am obligated to** pay **you five *sela*,** then **he is obligated to give him five *sela* and his son is not redeemed** even once he pays the money. This *baraita* apparently supports the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The *gemara* now brings a *tashma* — a case designed to test the scope of written obligations. Consider a father who owes five *sela'im* for *pidyon haben* — the redemption of the firstborn son. He writes to a *kohen*: *she'ani chayav lecha chamishah sela'im* — I owe you five *sela'im* — and hands him the document. He has not paid the money; he has only given an IOU. The ruling: *chayav litten lecha chamishah sela'im* — he now owes that *kohen* specifically five *sela'im*, but *uvno eino paduy* — his son is not redeemed, because the *pidyon haben* requires the actual transfer of money, not merely an acknowledged debt.

### IOU & Pre-existing Debt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דִּמְשׁוּעְבַּד לֵיהּ מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא: אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי כָּתַב? כְּדֵי לְבָרֵר לוֹ כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is different there, because he is obligated** to give the five *sela* **to him by Torah** law in order to fulfill his obligation of redeeming his firstborn son, even without writing a contract. The Gemara asks: **If that is so, why did he write** the contract at all? The Gemara answers: **In order to select for himself a** specific **priest** with whom to perform the redemption of his son.

The *gemara* asks: does this not prove that a simple written IOU creates a debt? He wrote it down, there was no signing, no witnesses mentioned, yet he owes. The *gemara* responds: *shani hasam d'meshubad lei mid'oraita* — the case there is different, because there already exists a Torah-mandated debt of five *sela'im*. The IOU does not create the obligation from nothing; it merely directs and specifies an existing obligation toward this particular *kohen*. The question of whether a written admission creates an obligation in a vacuum therefore remains unanswered by this case.

### Continuing the Pidyon Haben Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בְּנוֹ אַמַּאי אֵינוֹ פָּדוּי? כִּדְעוּלָּא. דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה — פָּדוּי לִכְשֶׁיִּתֵּן, וּמַאי טַעְמָא אָמְרוּ בְּנוֹ אֵינוֹ פָּדוּי? גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ פּוֹדִין בִּשְׁטָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, why is his son not redeemed** once he pays the money? The Gemara answers: This is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Ulla. As Ulla said, by Torah law** a son **is redeemed when** the father **gives** the money. **And for what reason did** the **Sages say: His son is not redeemed?** It is a rabbinic **decree** that was enacted **lest** people **say** that **one can redeem** a firstborn son **with documents,** i.e., by giving a document allowing the priest to collect a debt from a third party. This is not effective, since the Torah requires that one redeem his son with actual money.

The *gemara* then presses on the internal logic. If the father already owed five *sela'im* to some *kohen*, why does he need to write the IOU at all? *K'dei l'varer l'kohen* — to specify and designate which *kohen* will receive the payment. Fine, but then: *b'no amai eino paduy?* — if the IOU effectively directs the *pidyon* obligation to this *kohen*, and when he pays it he is satisfying that very obligation, why is his son not redeemed upon payment?

The *gemara* answers with the ruling of Ulla: *dvar Torah paduy l'khesheyitten* — technically, by Torah law, once he pays, the son is redeemed, because the payment does fulfill the *pidyon* obligation. The rabbinic ruling that declares the son *eino paduy* even after payment is a *gezeirah* — a protective enactment: *shema yomru podin b'shtarot* — lest people conclude that one may redeem a firstborn son by handing over a document rather than actual money. If people saw the IOU handed over at the *pidyon haben* ceremony and concluded that documents alone suffice, they would err about the fundamental requirement of actual transfer. Therefore the rabbis required payment of a second five *sela'im* to formally accomplish the redemption, making it clear that the IOU alone was insufficient.

### Guarantor Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, כְּתַנָּאֵי: עָרֵב הַיּוֹצֵא אַחַר חִיתּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת — גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish is **like** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** over the same matter in the following mishna (*Bava Batra* 175b): In a case where **a guarantor appears after the signatures** in **contracts,** i.e., someone wrote that he is a guarantor for a loan after the contract was signed, the creditor **collects** only **from** the **unsold property** of the guarantor. Since the guarantee is not viewed as though it were written in the document, it is like a loan by oral agreement, which is collected only from unsold property.

*Amar Rava: k'tana'ei* — this broader question of whether a written obligation creates a debt is in fact a dispute among the *Tanna'im* themselves. The case: *arev hayotzei achar chitum shtarot* — a guarantor whose commitment appears after the signatures of the witnesses. The loan document records that Reuven borrowed a hundred *zuz*; the witnesses sign. Then, beneath the witnesses' signatures, Shimon writes that he undertakes to guarantee the loan. The ruling: *goveh minechasim bnei chorin* — the lender may collect from property currently in Shimon's possession, but not from property Shimon has sold, because the guarantor's commitment does not carry liens. Without being part of the main, publicly attested document, the guaranty is binding but insufficient to create *shiabud* on sold property.

### Strangling Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בָּא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְאָמַר: גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין. אָמַר לוֹ בֶּן נַנָּס: אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה לֹא מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין וְלֹא מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **An incident came before Rabbi Yishmael, and he said:** The creditor **collects from unsold property. Ben Nannas said to him: He does not collect** from the guarantor at all; **not from unsold property, nor from liened property** that was sold, since what the guarantor wrote has no legal standing whatsoever.

*Ma'aseh ba lifnei Rabbi Yishmael* — a case of precisely this sort came before Rabbi Yishmael, and he ruled: *goveh minechasim bnei chorin* — you may collect from the guarantor's available property. Ben Nanas disagreed sharply: *eino goveh lo minechasim bnei chorin v'lo minechasim meshubadim* — you cannot collect anything from the guarantor, neither from property in his possession nor from property he has sold. Why? Ben Nanas offered his famous *mashal*: *harei shehayah chonek et chaveiro bashuk* — imagine someone was strangling his fellow in the marketplace over a debt. A third party intervenes and says: let go of him, and I will give you a hundred *zuz*. He lets go. *Patur* — the third party is not obligated to pay. *Shelo al emunato hilvahu* — the lender did not extend credit based on the guarantor's trustworthiness.

The *mashal* was puzzling until clarified through discussion: the strangling is not a metaphor for the lender withholding a loan — it represents the lender who has already lent the money and is now seeking to compel repayment from the borrower by force. The guarantor intervenes after the fact and offers to cover the debt. Since the lender's extension of credit predated the guaranty entirely, the guaranty was not a condition of the loan. *Shelo al emunato hilvahu* — the guarantor's promise was not what induced the lending. It is therefore not a serious, binding commitment.

The underlying legal principle is one of *gmirat da'at* — settled, serious intent. Ben Nanas holds that a commitment made after the debt is already established, when there are no special conditions making it obligatory, lacks the requisite *gmirat da'at*. The written signature does not suffice to supply that seriousness independently.

### Additional Guarantor Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: לָמָּה? אָמַר לוֹ: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה חוֹנֵק אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ בַּשּׁוּק, וּמְצָא[וֹ] חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר לוֹ: הַנַּח לוֹ, וַאֲנִי אֶתֵּן לָךְ — פָּטוּר, שֶׁלֹּא עַל אֱמוּנָתוֹ הִלְוָהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: Why?** Ben Nannas **said to him:** If someone **was strangling another in the marketplace** and demanding money that is owed to him, **and a friend** of the victim **found** him **and said to** the strangler: **Leave him** alone **and I will give you** what you are demanding from him, the friend of the victim is **exempt** from having to make any payment. This is because the creditor **did not lend the money based on his trust** in the friend of the victim, as the friend promised to repay the loan only after the money had been loaned. The same should apply in the case of the guarantor who comes after the contracts were already signed.

### Rabbi Yochanan vs Resh Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמַר כְּבֶן נַנָּס.
>
> **Translation:** Rava concludes: **Let us say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan stated** his ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** that the obligation that one accepts upon himself is binding, **and Reish Lakish stated** his ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **ben Nannas.**

The *gemara* now maps the *Tanna'im* onto the *Amoraim*: *leima Rabbi Yochanan d'amar k'Rabbi Yishmael, v'Reish Lakish d'amar k'Ben Nanas*. Rabbi Yochanan holds like Rabbi Yishmael — writing obligates, even when the commitment came after the loan was made, even without special preconditions; the act of writing is itself sufficient *gmirat da'at*. Resh Lakish holds like Ben Nanas — writing alone does not obligate; there must be special conditions, and a post-facto signature without those conditions is an empty gesture.

### Refining the Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּבֶן נַנָּס כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ — עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הָתָם אֶלָּא דְּשָׁיֵיךְ לֵיהּ לְשִׁיעְבּוּדָא דְאוֹרָיְיתָא, אֲבָל הָכָא לָא שָׁיֵיךְ שִׁיעְבּוּדָא דְאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **According to** the opinion **of ben Nannas, everyone agrees** that if he wrote in a contract: I owe you one hundred dinars, he is not obligated to pay. **When they disagree, it is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Yoḥanan** stated his ruling **in accordance with** the simple interpretation of the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael. And Reish Lakish** holds that **Rabbi Yishmael states** his opinion **only there,** in the case of the guarantor, **which relates to an obligation of Torah** law, since a guarantor is obligated by Torah law to pay. **But here,** where the case **does not relate** to **an obligation of Torah** law, as the man did not owe any money until he accepted this obligation upon himself, even Rabbi Yishmael would exempt him from paying.

The *gemara* refines the comparison, however. *Aliba d'Ben Nanas kulei alma lo pligi* — according to Ben Nanas, everyone agrees that a simple IOU creates no obligation. The question is only whether one can derive something from Rabbi Yishmael's position. Rabbi Yochanan says yes — Rabbi Yishmael holds that writing obligates in all cases. Resh Lakish says no — even Rabbi Yishmael's ruling is limited to situations where a pre-existing debt already exists. *Ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi Yishmael hatam ela d'shaycha lei l'shibuda d'Oraita* — Rabbi Yishmael only held the guaranty binding because there was already a real debt, and the guarantor was effectively including himself within that pre-existing obligation. Without a pre-existing debt, Rabbi Yishmael would agree that writing alone creates nothing. So both the *pidyon haben* case and the guarantor case involve pre-existing debts; neither proves that a bare IOU, written in a vacuum, obligates.

## Stipulation Binding

### Words + Marriage = Binding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: ״כַּמָּה אַתָּה נוֹתֵן לְבִנְךָ?״, ״כָּךְ וְכָךְ״. ״וְכַמָּה אַתָּה נוֹתֵן לְבִתְּךָ?״, ״כָּךְ וְכָךְ״. עָמְדוּ וְקִידְּשׁוּ קָנוּ, הֵן הֵן הַדְּבָרִים הַנִּקְנִים בַּאֲמִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to analyze **the** matter **itself** mentioned earlier: **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said:** When two families negotiate the terms of marriage for their respective children and one side says to the other: **How much do you give your son?** And the second side answers: **Such and such** amount. **How much do you give your daughter?** And the first side responds: **Such and such** amount. Then, if the son and daughter **arose and performed the betrothal,** all of these obligations are **acquired** and therefore binding. **These are** among **the things that are acquired through words** alone, without the need for an additional act of acquisition. The mishna is referring to a document that records such an agreement.

The *gemara* returns to the statement of Rav Giddel cited earlier. The in-laws gather, negotiate their financial contributions to the couple, agree on specific amounts, and then the couple proceeds to *kiddushin*. *Hen hen had'varim hanniknin b'amira* — these become binding obligations through speech alone, by virtue of the *kiddushin* that followed. The mechanism is *kinyan amira* — the words acquire binding force through the subsequent act of marriage, without any conventional *kinyan* of handkerchief or coin. Because both families wanted the marriage to occur, and committed financially in order to make it happen, and the marriage then occurred, the benefit they sought and received — the *hana'ah d'kamichtani ahadadi*, the joy of their children's marriage — serves as the consideration that seals the obligation.

### Ramifications & Variants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִילְּתָא דְּרַב בְּבִתּוֹ נַעֲרָה — דְּקָא מָטֵי הֲנָאָה לִידֵיהּ, אֲבָל בּוֹגֶרֶת דְּלָא מָטֵי הֲנָאָה לִידֵיהּ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Rav's statement is reasonable in** a case of a father **whose daughter** is **a young woman, since** the father **derives benefit** from this betrothal. The money given by the groom for the betrothal, as well as the rights to the bride's marriage contract, belong to the father of the bride. Consequently, he accepts through verbal agreement alone the obligation to pay the money he specified. **However,** in the case of **a grown woman, where the** father **does not derive benefit** from the betrothal because the rights to the betrothal money and marriage contract belong to the woman herself, **no,** the father does not become obligated to pay the money he specified through verbal agreement alone.

### Extended Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאֱלֹהִים! אָמַר רַב: אֲפִילּוּ בּוֹגֶרֶת. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, אֲבִי הַבֵּן מַאי הֲנָאָה אֲתָא לִידֵיהּ? אֶלָּא בְּהַהִיא הֲנָאָה דְּקָמִיחַתְּנִי אַהֲדָדֵי גָּמְרִי וּמַקְנִי לַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: **But by God! Rav said** his ruling **even** with regard to **a grown woman, as, if you do not say so,** in the case of **the father of the groom, what** monetary **benefit does he derive** from the betrothal? **Rather,** it must be explained that **in** exchange for **that benefit,** i.e., **that** the groom and bride **marry each other,** the fathers **fully transfer the rights** to the respective payments **to each other.**

### Can Oral Become Written?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: דְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ נִיתְּנוּ לִיכָּתֵב, אוֹ לֹא נִיתְּנוּ לִיכָּתֵב? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֹא נִיתְּנוּ לִיכָּתֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Are these matters,** i.e., verbal agreements concerning an upcoming marriage, **allowed to be written** down afterward in a proper contract, **or** are they **not allowed to be written** down afterward in a proper contract? Rav Ashi **said to him: They are not allowed to be written** down.

Ravina then poses a sharp question to Rav Ashi: *devarim halelu nitnu lehikatev v'lo nitnu lehikatev* — can these verbal stipulations, once they have become binding through *kiddushin*, subsequently be formalized in writing as a *shtar* with liens? The practical import is significant: a verbal obligation, even a binding one, does not carry *shiabud* over sold property. If one can convert it after the fact into a *shtar*, the creditor would gain the ability to collect from purchasers.

### No Retroactive Writing

Rav Ashi answers: *lo nitnu lehikatev* — they cannot be so written. Obligations that originated as verbal stipulations, even those that became binding through *kiddushin*, cannot be retroactively converted into a formal *shtar* with liens. The document must originate as a document to carry the weight of one.

### Testing the Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: הַפִּקְּחִים הָיוּ כּוֹתְבִים ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאָזוּן אֶת בִּתֵּךְ חָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁאַתְּ עִמִּי״! מַאי ״כּוֹתְבִין״ — אוֹמְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **raised an objection to** this from the mishna: **The perspicacious** ones **would write** an explicit stipulation into the agreement: I agree **on the condition that I will sustain your daughter for five years** only **as long as you are with me.** This indicates that one may document these verbal agreements. Rav Ashi responded: **What** is the meaning of the term **write** in this case? It means **say.**

Ravina then attempts a challenge from the *mishnah*: *hapikhin hayu kotvin* — the prudent people would write. Does this not demonstrate that these verbal stipulations were in fact written down? Rav Ashi responds: *mai kotvin — omrin* — the word *kotvin* here does not mean "write" in the literal sense; it means they would *say*. The *mishnah* uses the idiom of writing to describe a verbal formulation. This is unusual, and the *gemara* acknowledges as much, but draws a parallel: the *Mishnah* elsewhere says *ha-kosev l'ishto* — one who writes to his wife — and *Tanna Rabbi Chiya* reformulates this as *ha-omer l'ishto* — one who says to his wife — demonstrating that the Talmudic literature uses "writing" and "saying" interchangeably in certain contexts.

### Shtar Kiddushin Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָרֵי לֵיהּ לַאֲמִירָה כְּתִיבָה? אִין, וְהָתְנַן: הַכּוֹתֵב לְאִשְׁתּוֹ ״דִּין וּדְבָרִים אֵין לִי בִּנְכָסַיִיךְ״, וְתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina continued to ask: **Does** the *tanna* **refer to saying** as **writing?** Rav Ashi replied: **Yes. And so we learned** in the mishna (83a): **One who writes to his wife: I have no legal** dealings **or involvement in your properties, and Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** in explanation, that it means: **One who says to his wife.** This proves that verbal agreements are sometimes referred to in the Mishna as writing.

A further challenge is raised from another *tanna'itic* teaching: *ein kotvin shtarei eirusin v'nisuin ela mi-da'as shneihem* — documents of *eirusin* and *nisuin* may only be written with the consent of both parties. If both consent, they are written. Does this not imply that the pre-wedding financial stipulations — the *shtarei psikta* — can be written down as formal documents when both sides agree?

### Shtar Kiddushin, Not Stipulation Documents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין שְׁטָרֵי אֵירוּסִין וְנִשּׂוּאִין אֶלָּא מִדַּעַת שְׁנֵיהֶם. הָא מִדַּעַת שְׁנֵיהֶם כּוֹתְבִין. מַאי לָאו שְׁטָרֵי פְסִיקָתָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the following mishna (*Bava Batra* 167b): **One writes documents of betrothal and marriage only with the consent of both of them.** It may be derived from here that **with the consent of both of them, one** may **write** the documents. **What, is it not** that this mishna is discussing **documents of stipulation** that specify the agreements accepted by each side before the marriage?

### Unilateral Writing Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, שְׁטָרֵי אֵירוּסִין מַמָּשׁ. כִּדְרַב פָּפָּא וְרַב שֵׁרֵבְיָא. דְּאִיתְּמַר: כְּתָבוֹ לִשְׁמָהּ, וְשֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתָּהּ — רַבָּה וְרָבִינָא אָמְרִי: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב שֵׁרֵבְיָא אָמְרִי: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** the discussion concerns **actual betrothal documents.** In other words, in a case where a man betroths a woman by giving her a document that states: You are hereby betrothed to me, the document must be written with the consent of both the man and the woman, **in accordance with** the opinions of **Rav Pappa and Rav Sherevya. As it was stated:** If the husband **wrote** a betrothal document **for the sake of** a specific woman and gave it to her, **but** he wrote it **without her consent, Rabba and Ravina say: She is betrothed** to this man. **Rav Pappa and Rav Sherevya say: She is not betrothed.**

The *gemara* rejects this reading: *lo shtarei eirusin mamash* — the *mishnah* refers not to financial stipulations but to the actual *shtar kiddushin* itself, the document by which a man marries a woman through writing rather than through a ring or money.

## Liens Distinction

### Stepdaughter Creditor Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מֵתוּ — בְּנוֹתֵיהֶן נִיזּוֹנוֹת מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין, וְהִיא נִיזּוֹנֶת מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא כְּבַעֲלַת חוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests further: **Come** and **hear** another proof that verbal agreements may be written down, based upon the mishna: If the two husbands **died, their daughters are sustained from unsold property, and she,** their wife's daughter, whom they agreed to sustain for five years, **is sustained** even **from liened property** that was sold. This is **due to** the fact **that her** legal status is **like** that of **a creditor,** given that they are contractually obligated to pay her. The fact that she is able to repossess liened property indicates that the agreement is recorded in a document.

We return to the end of our *mishnah*. *Meisu* — when the stepfathers die, their natural daughters are supported from *nechasim bnei chorin* — property in the estate. The stepdaughter, however, *nizonis minechasim meshubadim* — may collect even from encumbered property, property that has been sold, *mipnei shehi kebaalas chov* — because she stands as a creditor with a formal debt against the estate. The *mishnah* is explicit: she has liens.

This is a striking feature of the *mishnah*. If her obligation arose as a verbal stipulation — the mechanism we have been analyzing throughout the *sugya* — how does she have liens? Verbal obligations, even those that become binding through *kiddushin*, do not carry *shiabud* over sold property. Liens require a public, formally attested *shtar*. The fact that the *mishnah* grants her liens therefore appears to imply that her obligation was in fact created by a formal *shtar* — a document with *kinyan* and witnesses — from the outset.

### Kinyan Requirement for Stepdaughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בְּשֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Here, we are dealing with** a case **where** the woman **acquired from** each husband the right to her daughter's sustenance, i.e., they performed an act of acquisition and did not suffice with a mere verbal agreement. Consequently, the agreement may be recorded in a document.

The *gemara* confirms this: *hacha b'mai askinan b'shekanu miyado* — the case here is one where a formal *kinyan* was performed. The *mishnah*, when it reaches the topic of liens and the stepdaughter's status as a creditor, is describing a scenario where the obligation was formalized in a classic *shtar* — *kinyan*, witnesses, the full apparatus of debt creation. The *gemara* then asks: if so, why do the natural daughters not have liens as well? Because *eihi d'leisa b'tnai beit din* — the stepdaughter is not within the standard automatic obligations that rabbinic law imposes on the husband. She is outside the *tnai beit din* entirely. Since there is no automatic obligation to her, the stepfather must create one from scratch; that requires a formal *kinyan* and a formal *shtar*, and naturally that formal *shtar* carries liens.

### Why Daughters Cannot Have Liens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בָּנוֹת נָמֵי! בְּשֶׁקָּנוּ לָזוֹ וְלֹא קָנוּ לָזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that a proper mode of acquisition was employed, then let the husbands' own **daughters also** repossess liened properties that were sold. The Gemara answers: The case is such **where they acquired** the right to receive sustenance **for this** daughter of the wife, **and they did not acquire** this right **for that** daughter, i.e., they did not perform an act of acquisition confirming their obligations to provide sustenance for their own daughters.

### Presumption Defense for Daughters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי פַּסְקָא? אִיהִי דַּהֲוַאי בִּשְׁעַת קִנְיָן — מַהֲנֵי לַהּ קִנְיָן, בָּנוֹת דְּלָא הֲווֹ בִּשְׁעַת קִנְיָן — לָא מַהֲנֵי לְהוּ קִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** makes it **necessary** to say that the case is one in which an acquisition was made on behalf of the wife's daughter and not on behalf of the husbands' own daughters? The Gemara answers: **She,** the wife's daughter from a previous marriage, **was present at the time of the transaction** when her mother was wed. Consequently, **for her the transaction is effective.** With regard to the **daughters** of the husbands, **who** were born after their parents' marriage and **were not present at the time of the transaction, for them the transaction is not effective.**

The natural daughters, by contrast, stand within the *tnai beit din* — their support is an automatically imposed obligation of the *ketubah*. Even if a father wished to create additional formal obligations to his natural daughters through a *kinyan* and *shtar*, the *gemara* holds that it would not generate liens in their favor. The reason: *eima tzerorei apistinhu* — purchasers of property from the estate can always presume, in the case of natural daughters, that the father made prior arrangements for them. Because the obligation to support daughters is so well established and deeply imposed, a purchaser may defend himself by arguing that the father surely set aside funds or made other arrangements; the daughters cannot overcome that presumption to collect from the purchaser's property.

### General Principle for Daughters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דַּהֲוַאי בִּשְׁעַת קִנְיָן, וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי — כְּגוֹן דְּגָרְשַׁהּ וְאַהְדְּרַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Are we not** also **dealing with** a situation where the husband's daughters **were present at the time of the transaction? And what are the circumstances** that would allow for such a reality? This could occur in a situation **such as where** each one **divorced** his wife **and then took her back,** and they had a daughter from their first marriage.

### Automatic Obligation Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: אִיהִי דְּלֵיתַאּ בִּתְנַאי בֵּית דִּין — מַהֲנֵי לַהּ קִנְיָן, בָּנוֹת דְּאִיתַנְהוּ בִּתְנַאי בֵּית דִּין — לָא מַהֲנֵי לְהוּ קִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the distinction is as follows: **She,** the wife's daughter, **is not included in the stipulation of the court** requiring a husband to support his daughters. Consequently, **for her the transaction is effective.** However, with regard to the husband's own **daughters, who are included in the stipulation of the court, for them the transaction is not effective.**

### Why the Presumption Applies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִגְרָע גָּרְעִי? אֶלָּא: בְּנוֹתָיו הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא — כֵּיוָן דְּאִיתַנְהוּ בִּתְנַאי בֵּית דִּין, אֵימַר צְרָרֵי אֶתְפְּסִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders about this: Because they are included in the stipulation of the court **they are worse off?** On the contrary, since the stipulation of the court demands that they be supported, they should wield more power. **Rather, this is the reason** that **his** own **daughters** do not collect from liened property: **Since they are included in the stipulation of the court,** and it is therefore the norm for fathers to take care to provide their support, **say that** their father **gave them bundles of money** while he was still alive. Due to this concern, they cannot repossess liened property. However, in the case of the wife's daughter, who is not included in the stipulation of the court, there is no concern that the husband gave her anything prior to his death.

The simpler dimension of this principle is already familiar: the obligation to support natural daughters is inherently indeterminate in scope — one cannot know at the time of purchase how many daughters there are or for how many years of support might remain. Protecting purchasers from an unknowable exposure, the rabbis ruled categorically that daughters' support claims reach only property in the estate.

### Summary: Shtar vs Default Obligation

The *sugya* thus arrives at a clean structural distinction. The stepdaughter's obligation is a formal *shtar* with *kinyan* and witnesses — created from scratch, outside the scope of any automatic obligation, carrying full *shiabud* and the right to collect from sold property. The natural daughters' entitlement is an automatic imposition of rabbinic law, part of the *tnai beit din* of the *ketubah* — never formalized in a *shtar*, carrying no liens, reachable only from property remaining in the estate.

The *mishnah* can also be read in its simplest sense as having always been about a formal *shtar*: he stipulated, she demanded, he signed a formal document with a *kinyan* — and that is why she is a creditor, that is why she has liens, that is the *peshita* of the *mishnah*. The *gemara*'s extended analysis sought to determine whether perhaps the opening clauses reflected a simpler or more ambiguous form of obligation, and found in the end that the *mishnah*'s grant of creditor status to the stepdaughter presupposes the full apparatus of formal debt creation. The verbal stipulations preceding *kiddushin* create real and binding obligations — but the kind of lien-bearing creditor status our *mishnah* describes belongs to the formal *shtar* alone.
