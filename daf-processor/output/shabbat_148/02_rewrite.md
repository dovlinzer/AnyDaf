# Shabbat 148 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bathing & Immersion

### Hot Water Rules

Today's daf is 148. Before proceeding to new material, it is worth spending a moment on the *halakhot* that emerged from the previous shiur, particularly around bathing.

The *mishna* there reflects the position of Rabbi Shimon, who permits bathing in hot water — but that ruling applies to *Yom Tov*, not *Shabbat*. On *Shabbat*, based on an earlier *gemara* that addressed the *issur* of bathing in hot water at length, the conclusion recorded in the *Shulchan Aruch*, the Rambam, and virtually all authorities is that bathing the entire body in hot water is prohibited — even by shower — due to concern that people would go to bathhouses and heat water on *Shabbat*. The relevant distinction is therefore as follows: washing individual limbs in preheated hot water is permitted, as is bathing the entire body in cold water; bathing the entire body in hot or warm water is not permitted, regardless of whether it takes the form of a bath or a shower.

A practical question arises about lukewarm water. Since producing lukewarm water requires mixing hot water with cold, and the hot water has been drawn on *Shabbat*, the prohibition appears to derive not merely from the temperature of the water as such, but from the use of the hot water itself. This suggests that even a warm shower remains problematic on *Shabbat* — which is of course what everyone practices. The underlying concern in all of these cases traces back to the *gezeira* regarding bathhouses.

On *Yom Tov* the analysis is different and rests on the concept of *tzorach kol haguf*. Since the majority of people — at least in America — bathe daily with hot water, many *poskim*, including this one, hold that a hot shower on *Yom Tov* is permitted. Even then, one must be careful not to squeeze out one's towel or hair.

### Immersion Customs

What about swimming on *Shabbat*? For *Ashkenazim*, there is a *minhag* going back roughly eight hundred years not to immerse the entire body even in cold water — whether in a bath, a swimming pool, or otherwise — with the sole exception of *mikveh*. The concern is incidental consequences such as squeezing hair or squeezing a towel. *Sefardim*, by contrast, do permit swimming on *Shabbat* provided one does not squeeze anything out. The *Ashkenazi minhag* forecloses the activity entirely due to the concern that one might come to do so. When there are health concerns, the rules differ.

### Removing Dirt

The previous shiur also addressed the question of removing dirt and stains from garments. Although the *gemara* there introduces qualifications — new garments, black garments, and the like — those qualifications must be read alongside an earlier *gemara* that prohibits rubbing a garment vigorously to remove embedded stains, regardless of the garment's type. How are these reconciled?

One approach holds that a genuine stain or something that truly embeds itself in the fabric is prohibited regardless of the garment type, while something trivial like dust presents a problem only with a new black garment where it is conspicuous. A second approach locates the difference in the manner of removal: vigorous rubbing is always prohibited, while a more casual wipe may only be problematic in certain circumstances.

In practice, the operative principle is this: removing something from the *surface* of a garment is generally permitted; rubbing out an embedded stain is problematic. Crusted matter may be scraped off the surface, but once one is working to extract a stain from within the fabric itself, that crosses the line. The use of water, it should be noted, is a separate concern entirely.

The term *makpid* as used in the earlier *gemara* means that the stain is so bad one would not wear the garment without removing it. Those severe restrictions apply more to trivial matters like dust. When one is genuinely working to extract something embedded, the prohibition applies regardless of those qualifications.

## Broken Bones

### Bone Restoration

The *mishna* states *ein machzirin et hashever* — one may not restore a broken bone on *Shabbat*. This is a striking ruling, since one might have expected that a broken bone would constitute at minimum a *sakanat eiver* and thus be treated with considerable leniency. Nevertheless, the *mishna* prohibits restoring it.

The *gemara* immediately introduces the view of Shmuel: Rav Chanana bar Taltiya cited Shmuel as ruling *halacha machzirin et hashever* — the *halacha* is that one may restore a broken bone. This is not an opposing *tannaic* source, not a *baraita* or a competing *mishna*, but simply Shmuel's personal ruling against the *mishna*.

### Rabbah's Encounter

The *gemara* then relates that Rabbah bar bar Chana came to Pumbedita but did not attend Rav Yehuda's *shiur*. Rav Yehuda dispatched his attendant — *Rashi* glosses *Ada Dayala* as some type of assistant — with instructions to compel Rabbah bar bar Chana to come. The attendant did so, essentially forcing him to attend. Upon arrival, Rabbah bar bar Chana found Rav Yehuda expounding the *mishna's* ruling: *ein machzirin et hashever*, you may not restore the bone on *Shabbat*. Rabbah bar bar Chana told him: Rav Chanana bar Taltiya said in the name of Shmuel that the *halacha* is *machzirin et hashever* — we do not rule like the *mishna*.

Rav Yehuda's response is telling. He says: Chana of ours — referring to Chana bar Taltiya who was from Pumbedita — and Shmuel of ours. Both the transmitter and the one in whose name it was said were from our own community, *v'lo shmia li* — and I never heard this teaching! *Rashi* reads Rav Yehuda's conclusion — was it not justified that I forced you to come? — as a genuine expression of gratitude. Because of this forced encounter, the entire academy was enlightened by a teaching that had circulated in their own community without reaching them.

### Dislocation Rules

The *gemara* then shifts from broken bones to dislocated limbs. The *mishna* rules that if one's hand or foot has been dislocated, one may not compress it in cold water, though one may wash it in the normal manner — and if it heals, it heals.

The *gemara* records that Rav Avya was sitting before Rav Yosef and made an unusual movement with his arm, asking: if one's hand is positioned like this, what may be done? Rav Yosef ruled it forbidden. He tried another position — also forbidden. In the process, Rav Avya actually injured his own hand. Rav Yosef then asked: what exactly was your question? The *mishna* already rules on this: one who has a dislocated hand or foot may not compress it in cold water but may wash it normally, and if it heals it heals. Why were you asking all these variations?

Rav Avya explained his reasoning: the *mishna* also says *ein machzirin et hashever*, and Shmuel ruled *machzirin et hashever*. If you can restore a broken bone, perhaps the same leniency should apply to a serious dislocation. Rav Yosef's reply was sharp: *kulehu bechada machta machtinhu* — do you think everything is bundled together into a single ruling? *Heicha d'itmar itmar v'heicha d'lo itmar lo itmar.* Where it was said, it was said; where it was not said, it was not said. Shmuel's leniency was limited to broken bones; there is no extension to dislocated limbs. The *halacha* regarding dislocation is construed strictly.

### Theory vs. Practice

This is quite difficult to understand from our vantage point. One can see why a broken bone might be considered more serious than a dislocation, but it is hard to understand either prohibition in light of the way *halacha* is actually ruled today. The accepted *pesak* is that even for a *choleh she'ein bo sakanah* — someone who is ill without danger to life — one may perform acts of healing on *Shabbat*, and may even violate other rabbinic restrictions (with questions about exactly how directly a *Yisrael* may violate a rabbinic prohibition and whether *amirah l'akum* suffices, but substantial leniencies exist). Someone with a broken bone or a dislocated shoulder would almost certainly fall into the category of *choleh kol haguf*, not *michush b'alma* — a mere minor discomfort.

It is therefore quite striking that both the *mishna* and the *gemara* take such strict positions here. The *gemara* does show an initial move toward leniency at least in the most extreme cases, and in practice contemporary *pesak* is quite lenient across this category. How precisely one gets from the *gemara's* strictness to current leniency is a question that would require a careful look at how the *rishonim* and *acharonim* explain and develop this *gemara*.

This concludes *Perek Chavit*, which covered food-related matters — some *de'oraita*, some *de-rabanan* — including concerns around squeezing food, squeezing a towel, bathing, clothing, and, at the end, *refuah*.

## Borrowing Loans

### Mishnah Cases

The next *perek* opens with *sho'el adam m'chaveiro* — one may borrow from a friend on *Shabbat* pitchers of wine or oil, provided one does not use the word *halveini* — "lend me." Similarly, a woman may borrow loaves of bread from her neighbor. The *mishna's* explicit identification of women in this context reflects the common halakhic literary practice of writing with men as the default subject; where a woman is the relevant actor in a common scenario, she is explicitly identified. This carries no legal significance: the rules are the same. Sometimes the *mishna* simply illustrates with representative cases — wine and oil as things men might deal with, bread as something associated with women's domestic domain. One should not read legal significance into the choice of examples where the *gemara* itself does not do so.

### Collateral Rules

The *mishna* continues: if the lender does not trust the borrower — he is worried the borrower will forget or fail to repay — he may have the borrower leave a garment (*tallis*) with him, and they settle accounts after *Shabbat*. One might initially ask whether this constitutes a *pikadon* — a formal deposit against a debt, which would be explicitly transactional in character. The key distinction is one of framing and context. Ordinarily, leaving collateral is the hallmark of a formal financial transaction: you owe me one hundred dollars, here is something of value as security. Here, the framing is entirely different: you leave your watch by me, and you pick it up after *Shabbat* — the settling of accounts is deferred and informal.

Crucially, the *gemara* will shortly clarify that the entire rabbinic concern in this area is not that commerce is intrinsically incompatible with *Shabbat* at the rabbinic level, but rather that these activities may lead to writing — a *de'oraita* violation. If that is so, then taking a *mashkon* makes excellent sense: the very existence of the collateral obviates the need for a written record. Even a more explicitly transactional arrangement might be acceptable if the *issur* is only about writing and not about commerce itself.

### She'ilah vs. Halva'ah

There is a significant distinction between *she'eilah* and *halva'ah*, and the *rishonim* understand it differently. *Rashi* holds that *shoel* implies borrowing for a short period with prompt return, whereas *loveh* implies a longer-term arrangement, creating greater concern that the parties will want to document the transaction. *Tosafot* offers what is arguably the more intuitive explanation: *she'eilah* — *shoel* — refers to borrowing a durable object that will be returned as itself. If you borrow my car, you return my car. *Halva'ah* — *loveh* — refers to borrowing something fungible that will be consumed: I lend you one hundred dollars, you spend it, and you return a different one hundred dollars. The paradigm of *halva'ah* is monetary lending, but it applies to anything fungible.

This distinction matters enormously for our *mishna*. When one borrows two eggs from a neighbor, technically this is *halva'ah*: you are not going to return the same two eggs, you will return two equivalent eggs. Yet the *mishna* permits it with the language of *she'eilah*. *Tosafot* explains that although the transaction is technically *halva'ah* in substance, framing it in the language of *she'eilah* — especially when it involves a physical object being taken and an equivalent returned — strongly suggests the domestic, short-term, non-commercial register. Things that are used and given back do not naturally call for documentation. Things that are spent and replaced do. By using the language of *she'eilah*, even if technically imprecise, one places the transaction in a category that does not lead to writing.

### Language Distinctions

The *gemara* poses the question directly: Rabbah bar Rav Chanan asked Abaye — what is the practical difference between saying *hashileini* and *halveini*? The *mishna* restricts the language, but why should the choice of words matter?

Abaye answers plainly: *hashileini lo ati l'michtav* — using the language of *hashileini* does not lead to writing it down; *halveini ati l'michtav* — *halveini* does lead to writing it down. This confirms what had already been suggested: the *Bavli* frames the concern around these business-adjacent activities on *Shabbat* not as an intrinsic problem but as a *gezeira* lest it lead to the *de'oraita* violation of writing. One could have argued that commercial-sounding language is intrinsically problematic because it evokes the world of *mekach umimkar* — commerce and transactions — which is antithetical to the spirit of *Shabbat*. The *Bavli*, characteristically, prefers to anchor the prohibition in concern for a consequential *de'oraita* violation. When one uses language that evokes the domestic sphere of short-term borrowing, it does not lead one to write; when one uses language that evokes formal lending, it does.

### Language Imprecision

The *gemara* is not entirely satisfied with this answer. It presses: *keivan d'bechol zimna d'ba'i l'meimar halveini v'amar lei hashileini* — since in ordinary weekday usage people use these terms imprecisely and are not *makpid* about which one they say, *v'lo kapdi ilei*, so even when someone says *hashileini* it may actually refer to a real loan, and *v'ati l'michtav* — people will end up writing things down even when they use the term *hashileini*. If the terms are used interchangeably in everyday speech, what protection does the preferred terminology actually provide?

### Shabbat Context

The *gemara's* resolution is elegant. On *Shabbat*, since *hashileini* is the only term the rabbis permit — *halveini* is not permitted — *minkara milta v'lo ati l'michtav*: the restriction is self-announcing. The very fact that one must remember to use only the term *hashileini* on *Shabbat* serves as a built-in reminder that one is in a *Shabbat* context governed by *Shabbat* norms. This creates a distinctive register for *Shabbat* borrowing. By establishing that only one term is valid, the rabbis effectively create a signal: every time a person consciously uses *hashileini*, they are reminded that this is *Shabbat*, that records are not to be written, and that the transaction is of the domestic, trust-based variety. The restriction on language thus functions both as a practical safeguard and as a contextual marker.

## Uvdin D'Chol

### Shinuy Principle

Rabbah bar Rav Chanan said to Abaye: the rabbis declared that anything relating to *Yom Tov* should be done with a *shinuy* — a variation — as much as possible. This principle applies to activities that, while technically permitted on *Yom Tov*, have the character of *uvdin d'chol* — weekday-type activities. Things that are laborious and mundane in nature, even if permitted, should be performed somewhat differently on *Yom Tov* to distinguish the day. The question is how this principle interacts with competing considerations.

### Water Bucket Case

The case in point involves women filling their water buckets (*chatzvayehu*) from the well. This was a laborious, thoroughly weekday activity. Why do we not require a *shinuy* here?

The *gemara* works through the options. Should those who normally use large buckets use small ones instead? Then they will have to make more trips — *ha ka mafsha behilucha* — increasing *tircha*, exertion. Should those who use small buckets switch to large ones? Then the load becomes heavier — *ha ka mafsha bemasavye*. Perhaps one should drape some type of cloth over part of the bucket as a physical *heker*? The cloth will get wet and one will come to squeeze it — *azil lidei schita*. A non-cloth cover? The rope holding it may break and one will come to tie a knot. Every proposed *shinuy* introduces a new *Shabbat* concern or an unacceptable increase in burden. Therefore — *hilkach lo efshar* — there is genuinely no viable option, and the requirement of *shinuy* does not apply here.

### Competing Values

This discussion brings two values into tension: *uvdin d'chol* on one side and *tircha* on the other. These are not the same concern. *Tircha* refers specifically to physical exertion and effort; *uvdin d'chol* refers to the quality of an activity as weekday-like, pulling one out of the sacred character of the day. The question is: when these values compete, which wins?

### Tosafot's Position

*Tosafot* addresses this question — though in connection with a parallel *sugya* in *Beitzah* rather than directly with our *gemara*. *Tosafot* holds that minimizing *uvdin d'chol* outweighs concerns about *tircha*: if one can make an activity genuinely feel less like a weekday activity by accepting more exertion, one should do so. The more important value is eliminating the weekday character of the activity.

Why then does our *gemara* not require more trips or a heavier bucket — accepting the *tircha* in order to introduce a *shinuy*? *Tosafot* answers: because in this case, going to the well and drawing water, however many trips one makes and whatever size bucket one uses, remains thoroughly *uvdin d'chol* in character. No variation in bucket size or number of trips transforms the experiential quality of the activity. The only value achieved by a forced *shinuy* here would be a *shinuy* for its own sake — not an actual reduction in the weekday character of the activity. That does not justify increasing trips or burden. But where a *shinuy* would genuinely make the activity feel less weekday-like, the reduction of *uvdin d'chol* would justify accepting the additional *tircha*.

### Modern Devices

This tension has direct application to a contemporary debate among *poskim* regarding labor-saving mechanical devices in the kitchen on *Yom Tov*. Assume that concerns of *tochen* (grinding) or *borer* (sorting) do not apply — is there an independent concern of *uvdin d'chol* in using, say, a grater or a peeler?

The *Shmirat Shabbat Kehilchata* holds that such use is forbidden because of *uvdin d'chol*: labor-saving devices, even when no other *melachah* is implicated, introduce a quality of weekday efficiency that is inappropriate for *Yom Tov*. There is a sense in which more primitive methods — involving genuine personal effort — correspond to a simpler, more *Yom Tov*-appropriate mode of being. Rav Moshe, by contrast, drawing on a line in *Shulchan Aruch*, argues that there is no basis for calling labor-saving devices *uvdin d'chol*: since when is saving effort a weekday-like activity? Absent other violations of *borer* or *tochen*, Rav Moshe permits it.

The underlying question — exactly what quality renders an activity *uvdin d'chol* — is genuinely unclear. What is clear from our *gemara* is that the interplay between *uvdin d'chol* and *tircha* is a live halakhic consideration, and that in some situations accepting more *tircha* to reduce *uvdin d'chol* is the correct balance.

## Enforcement Strategy

### Musical Instruments

The same Rabbah bar bar Chana said to Abaye: we taught in the *mishna* — *lo mesapkin v'lo metapchin v'lo merakdin b'Yom Tov* — one may not clap, bang rhythmically, or dance on *Yom Tov*. *Rashi* understands this prohibition as targeting a form of mourning behavior, but *Tosafot* disagrees and holds that the prohibition applies even to joyful music-making: even clapping and dancing out of happiness is forbidden on *Yom Tov*. The reason, as the *gemara* in *Beitzah* explains, is the concern that one will proceed to bring out musical instruments, fix them, or make them — a prohibited act.

Rabbah bar bar Chana then observes: *v'kachazeinan d'avdin v'lo amrinan lehu v'lo midi* — we see people doing this and we say nothing. How is that explained?

### Shogeg Principle

The *gemara* responds by citing Rava's related ruling that a person should not sit at the opening of an alley by the *lechi* — the vertical post serving as the halakhic marker — lest an object roll out and he run after it, forgetting the boundary and carrying from *reshut harabim* to *reshut hayachid*. Yet we see that people bring their water vessels and sit at precisely these openings and we say nothing. The answer in both cases is the same: *hanach l'Yisrael — mutav sheyihyu shogegin v'al yihyu mezidin* — leave the Jewish people alone; better that they sin in error than willfully. If rebuke will not be heeded, it is better to remain silent.

### Torah vs. Rabbinic

One might have thought this principle applies only to rabbinic prohibitions: where the violation is only *de-rabanan*, one can concede that unheeded correction accomplishes nothing. But where the violation is *de'oraita*, perhaps one must speak up regardless — both to voice protest and to work harder to ensure compliance. The *gemara* rejects this distinction sharply: *v'lo hi — lo shna b'drabanan v'lo shna b'doraita* — it makes no difference whether the violation is biblical or rabbinic; if the rebuke will not be effective, one remains silent in either case.

### Yom Kippur Example

The *gemara* proves this from *tosefet Yom Kippur*, the biblical obligation to begin *Yom Kippur* early. We observe people eating and drinking until it is literally dark — until the last possible moment, forgoing the addition entirely — and we say nothing. This is a *de'oraita* violation, yet the principle of *mutav sheyihyu shogegin* applies.

### Rishonim Nuances

The *rishonim* qualify this principle significantly. The principle cannot be absolute: if someone is committing a blatant, explicit *de'oraita* violation — theft, for instance — one cannot simply stand by in silence on the grounds that rebuke will be ineffective. The qualifications operate along several axes. First, the more serious and explicit the violation, the less plausibly the person can claim genuine *shogeg* — their status as an inadvertent sinner is weaker to begin with. Second, the more severe the violation, the greater the obligation to attempt correction even against the odds of success. Third, there is the independent dimension of voicing protest: even where one cannot realistically expect compliance, remaining entirely silent may signal acceptance. One must not create the impression that one endorses the conduct.

These considerations also intersect with the *mitzvah* of *tochecha* — the obligation of rebuke — and the question of its limits. The *gemara's* principle is genuinely one of discretion: sometimes silence is the wiser course. There is even a debate among later *poskim* about a *rishon* who concluded a responsum with the phrase *ela hanach lahem l'Yisrael v'chulu* — "and leave Israel alone" — and whether this invokes the *shogeg* principle (meaning what they are doing is wrong, but rebuke is futile) or the entirely different *hanach lahem l'Yisrael* from elsewhere: *im lav nevi'im heim bnei nevi'im heim* — if they are not prophets, they are descendants of prophets — meaning we can trust that they are acting correctly.

## Clapping Debate

### Tosefta vs. Bavli

Returning to the prohibition against clapping and dancing: it is worth noting that the *Tosefta's* presentation of this prohibition appears to frame it as an intrinsic problem of *uvdin d'chol*. The specific context in the *Tosefta* involves activities that farmers would do in their fields to scare birds away — making it paradigmatically a weekday labor-type activity. The prohibition, on that reading, is about the intrinsic character of the act, not about what it might lead to. The *Bavli*, as is its characteristic method, reframes the prohibition as a concern about consequences: clapping and dancing may lead one to produce or repair musical instruments — a prohibited *melachah*. The intrinsic *uvdin d'chol* dimension is displaced by a concern about a downstream *de'oraita* violation.

### Tosafot's Rationalization

This *Bavli* reframing has significant practical consequences. *Tosafot* acknowledges that in their day — as today — it was extremely common for people to ignore this prohibition entirely. Rather than applying the *shogeg* principle and saying that people are sinning but one should leave them alone, *Tosafot* goes further and rationalizes the widespread practice. Since the prohibition as framed by the *Bavli* is a *gezeirah* against clapping *because* it may lead to making or fixing musical instruments — and since in *Tosafot's* era (and arguably more so today) people do not trivially make or repair musical instruments in response to festive clapping — *Tosafot* suggests that the *gezeirah* may not apply in such circumstances.

### Consequences Framework

This approach is only available precisely because the *Bavli* has reframed the prohibition as a concern about consequences rather than an intrinsic problem. Had clapping been an intrinsic *issur* — whether as *uvdin d'chol* in the *Tosefta's* sense or for some other inherent reason — there would be no room for *Tosafot's* argument. It is the framing as a *gezeirah* tied to specific consequences that opens the door to arguing that those consequences are no longer realistic. This connects directly to the broader *uvdin d'chol* discussion: the *Bavli* consistently prefers to ground prohibitions in concern for downstream violations rather than in the intrinsic character of an activity, and that preference has real halakhic implications for how the prohibition can be understood and applied.

## Ribis & Bread

### Hillel's Ruling

The *gemara* continues. The *mishna* permits a woman to borrow loaves of bread from her neighbor using the appropriate language. The *gemara* asks: does this imply that during the week such borrowing is entirely permitted? If so, this appears to contradict the position of Hillel. The *gemara* cites a teaching: *v'chein hayah Hillel omeir* — Hillel used to say — a woman should not lend a loaf of bread to her friend *ad sheta'asena damim* — unless she first converts it into a monetary value. The concern is *peiros b'peiros* — lending something and receiving back the same category of item, with the risk that the item will have increased in value in the interim. *Shema yokru chitim v'nimtza ba lidei ribis* — perhaps the price of wheat will rise, and when the borrower returns a loaf of bread it will be more valuable than what was borrowed, constituting *ribis*.

### Fixed Prices

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *afilu teima Hillel — ha be'atra de-kayitz damei ha be'atra de-lo kayitz damei.* Even according to Hillel, there are two distinct situations. Where the price is fixed and well-known — *kayitz damei* — the *ribis* concern does not apply. Where the price is indeterminate — *lo kayitz damei* — Hillel's requirement to set a monetary value applies.

### Practical Leniency

*Rashi* explains the mechanism: if the price is fixed and universally known, then even if that price subsequently changes, everyone will know it has changed. The parties can therefore adjust accordingly — one returns the value rather than the object — and the *ribis* concern is avoided. If the price is indeterminate, there is no clear baseline, and the risk that an equivalent quantity represents a greater value is unmanageable without first establishing the monetary equivalent.

In practice this is a real issue for everyday neighborly borrowing. The *halacha* does require sensitivity to the concern even in contemporary circumstances, and it will be discussed at greater length when the relevant material in *Bava Metzia* is reached. Notably, in places where prices are regulated — such as certain staple goods in Israel — the concept of *kayitz damei* may apply with particular force, though even there periodic price adjustments must be accounted for.

## Yom Tov Loans

### Rav Yosef

The *gemara* turns to a formal dispute about the legal status of loans made on *Yom Tov*. Suppose one borrowed food on *Yom Tov* and subsequently refuses to repay. Can the lender bring the borrower to *din* — to *Beit Din*? Rav Yosef rules: *lo nitna l'itava* — the loan cannot be adjudicated in court. The entire arrangement is built on trust; it is not a formally enforceable obligation. The reasoning: *de-im amarta nitna l'itava* — if you were to say it is court-enforceable, *ati l'michtav* — the lender would write it down to secure their ability to collect. Converting the arrangement into a real enforceable debt is precisely what creates the pressure to document it. Keeping it on a trust basis means neither party expects written documentation.

### Rava

Rava disagrees: *nitna l'itava* — it is enforceable in court. His reasoning runs in the opposite direction: *de-im amarta lo nitna* — if you say it cannot be enforced — *lo ya'iv leh* — who will give on such a basis? No one will extend the loan if they have no recourse whatsoever. *V'ati limnumey mi-simchas Yom Tov* — and this will result in people being unable to participate in *simchas Yom Tov*. The concern for *simchat Yom Tov* frames this as a *Yom Tov* issue specifically, perhaps even more acutely than a *Shabbat* issue: on *Yom Tov* one is actively cooking and preparing food, and running out of an ingredient mid-preparation is more likely and more consequential. Enabling the *simchas Yom Tov* of the community requires that the lender have some genuine assurance of repayment.

### Mishnah Support

The *gemara* tests these positions against our *mishna*. The *mishna* rules: *eino ma'amino, maniach taliso etzlo* — if the lender does not trust the borrower, he may leave his *tallis* as collateral, *v'oseh imo cheshbon l'achar ha-Shabbos* — and they settle accounts after *Shabbat*.

The *gemara* asks: if the loan cannot be brought to court — as Rav Yosef holds — then we understand why the lender would want to hold the *tallis*: without court enforcement, the collateral is his only security. But if the loan is court-enforceable — as Rava holds — why would anyone bother with collateral? Just give the goods and sue if unpaid.

The *gemara* answers: *lo beina d'eikum bedina v'dayana* — the lender may simply prefer not to deal with the trouble of *Beit Din* and judges. Even if technically enforceable, one might still prefer the simplicity of holding collateral to avoid litigation. This response does not disprove court-enforceability; it merely explains why collateral remains attractive even where enforcement is available.

## Shemittah Cases

### Meat Distribution

The *gemara* continues with a new challenge. The case involves a butcher who slaughtered a cow and distributed the meat to customers on *Rosh Hashana* — the first day of the new year. This falls at the transition from the seventh (*shemittah*) year to the eighth. At the end of *shemittah*, outstanding debts are cancelled. The question turns on whether the first day of *Rosh Hashana* was the 30th of Elul (making it a day of the seventh year, the old year) or genuinely the 1st of Tishrei (the first day of the eighth year). *Im hayah chodesh me'ubar* — if it was a full month — the first day of *Rosh Hashana* was the 30th of Elul, the debt falls within the seventh year, and *shemittah* cancels it. *V'im lav* — if not — the debt arose on the genuine first of Tishrei, the eighth year has begun, and *shemittah* does not cancel it.

### Enforcement vs. Voluntary

The *gemara* poses a challenge to Rav Yosef's position. If *Yom Tov* loans are not court-enforceable — *lo nitna l'itava* — why does it matter whether *shemittah* cancels them? *Shemittah* cancels debts that are collectible in court; a debt that can never be brought to court is already unenforceable without *shemittah*. The *gemara* resolves this by identifying a special factor: *shani hasam de'iglai milsa de-chol hu* — in the scenario where *shemittah* cancels the debt, the first day of *Rosh Hashana* turned out to be the 30th of Elul — a weekday, not *Yom Tov*. The loan was therefore a regular weekday transaction, fully enforceable, and properly subject to *shemittah* cancellation. The *Yom Tov* loan rules were never in play.

The final clause of the case — *v'im lav, eino meshamet* — is then pressed into service on behalf of Rava's position. If the first day of *Rosh Hashana* was genuinely the 1st of Tishrei, *shemittah* does not cancel the debt, and the implication is that there is something remaining to collect. This suggests the *Yom Tov* loan *is* enforceable — otherwise *shemittah's* non-application would be meaningless. The *gemara* offers a nuanced response: *dihavi leh v'shakil* — the point is not about court-enforced collection but about voluntary repayment. Even if the lender cannot compel payment, the borrower may willingly come and repay. The distinction between the two clauses of the case is therefore this: when *shemittah* applies, the lender cannot simply accept payment when it is offered. When *shemittah* does not apply, the lender may straightforwardly accept whatever the borrower brings.

### Shemittah Procedure

This leads to an important *mishna* on the *shemittah* procedure. *Ha-machzir chov bishvi'it* — one who returns a debt during *shemittah* — the lender must say: *meshamet ani* — I hereby annul this debt. If the borrower then responds that he wishes to repay regardless, *yekabel mimenu* — the lender may accept. The obligation to annul derives from the verse *ve-zeh devar ha-shemittah*, which requires the lender to formally pronounce words of annulment. Mere silence or passive acceptance is insufficient; the lender must actively declare the annulment, after which voluntary repayment is permitted.

The practical upshot for our case: where a *Yom Tov* loan has been affected by *shemittah*, the lender cannot simply pocket the money when the borrower comes to pay. The lender must first declare annulment. Only if the borrower then insists on paying regardless may the money be accepted. Where *shemittah* has not applied, the lender accepts the money directly as soon as it is offered.

### Collateral Scheme

Rav Avya's approach to securing repayment is also noted. When distributing food on *Yom Tov*, Rav Avya would take collateral — *mashkona* — and the *gemara* here explicitly labels it as *mashkon*, confirming that at least where the concern is about writing rather than business *per se*, openly taking collateral is acceptable.

The *gemara* then describes a scheme attributed to Rabba bar Ulla: after *Yom Tov*, before ever pressing for repayment, he would approach the debtor and ask to borrow some object — say, a car — for an hour. Once he had that object in hand, he would hold onto it until the original debt was paid. This is a somewhat clever workaround: rather than holding collateral at the time of the original *Yom Tov* loan, he would acquire leverage over the debtor afterwards, thereby ensuring repayment without the complications of formal court proceedings.

## Erev Pesach

### Fixed-Time Korbanot

The *mishna* introduced the case of *Erev Pesach* falling on *Shabbat* and the arrangement of getting one's sheep while deferring payment. Rav Yochanan derives a broader principle: *makdishin pesachcha be-Shabbat v'chagigatecha be-Yom Tov* — one may sanctify a *korban Pesach* on *Shabbat* and a *chagiga* on *Yom Tov*, even though sanctifying *korbanot* is normally forbidden on both. The principle is *le-tzorech Shabbat* and *le-tzorech Yom Tov*: where there is a genuine need tied to that specific day, the sanctification is permitted. If *Erev Pesach* falls on *Shabbat* and one has not yet sanctified one's *korban Pesach*, one may do so.

### Pesach Example

The *gemara* attempts to derive support for Rav Yochanan's position from the *mishna*: *v'chein Erev Pesach biYerushalayim she-chal lihyot be-Shabbat, maniach talito etzlo v'notel et pishcho v'oseh imo cheshbon le-achar Yom Tov* — you get your sheep first on *Shabbat* and work out payment afterwards. The implicit assumption is that you then sanctify the sheep on *Shabbat* for use as your *korban Pesach* — thus proving that such sanctification is permitted.

### Pre-Sanctification

The *gemara* resists this reading by proposing: *heicha be-mai askinan — bi-mmaneh acherim imo al pishcho* — what we are actually discussing is not acquiring an unsanctified sheep and sanctifying it, but rather buying into a group (*chiburah*) that already has a sanctified *korban Pesach*. One who did not have an animal joins an existing group; the financial settlement deferred to after *Shabbat* represents one's buy-in fee. The sheep was already sanctified; no new act of *hekdesh* occurs on *Shabbat*.

The *gemara* acknowledges that this reading is forced. The *mishna's* plain language strongly suggests one is acquiring a sheep and then sanctifying it — not merely purchasing a share in something pre-sanctified.

### Rav Hoshaia

The *gemara* then cites a *baraita* of Rav Hoshaia that eliminates any ambiguity: *holech adam etzel roeh haragil etzlo v'noten lo taleh le-pischo umakdisho* — one goes to a shepherd, receives a sheep, and *explicitly sanctifies it* for one's *korban Pesach*, then settles accounts after *Shabbat*. The word *umakdisho* — and he sanctifies it — is unambiguous. Here too the *gemara* attempts to retreat: since one regularly buys from this shepherd, the shepherd had already anticipated the buyer's arrival and sanctified it in advance, so the buyer's act of sanctification is rabbinically supplementary at best. This reading is extraordinarily strained. All the cases point in the same direction: one acquires the sheep on *Shabbat* and sanctifies it. The *gemara's* attempts to explain them otherwise are not convincing, but they represent the *gemara's* effort to avoid a direct proof for Rav Yochanan from the *mishna* — even as Rav Yochanan's position remains substantively sound.

### General Principle

The *gemara* ultimately resolves the matter through a *stam mishna* that rules: *lo makdishin v'lo maarichin v'lo machrimin v'lo magbihin terumot umaserot — kol elu be-Yom Tov amru, kal vachomer be-Shabbat* — one does not sanctify, evaluate, consecrate, or separate *terumot* and *maaserot* on *Yom Tov*; these prohibitions apply on *Shabbat* as well, *a fortiori*. Rav Yochanan himself holds that *halacha* follows an unattributed *mishna*, and this *mishna* appears to prohibit sanctification on *Yom Tov* and *Shabbat* categorically — in apparent tension with his own ruling.

The resolution is straightforward: *lo kashya — kan be-chovot shekavuah lahen zman, kan be-chovot she'ein kavuah lahen zman.* The *mishna's* prohibition applies to obligations that are not time-bound — sanctifications and dedications that one could perform on any day. Rav Yochanan's permission applies to obligations that are bound to a specific day: the *korban Pesach* must be brought on the 14th of Nisan, the *chagiga* on *Yom Tov* itself. Where the obligation is fixed to today and cannot be fulfilled otherwise, sanctification is permitted on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*. Where there is no such temporal urgency, the prohibition stands. This is the general principle with which the *daf* concludes.
