# Zevachim 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Psul Person & Dachuy

### Psul Thrower

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפָסוּל. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ! תְּיוּבְתָּא. וְכוּלָּן שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ כּוּ׳. בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פָּסוּל – מַהוּ שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה שִׁירַיִם?
>
> **Translation:** **and** the offering is therefore **disqualified. The Gemara concludes: The refutation of** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** is indeed **a conclusive refutation.** § The mishna teaches: **And** with regard to **all of them,** in a case **where they collected** the blood with the intent to offer it beyond its designated time or outside its designated area, if there is blood of the soul that remains in the animal, the priest fit for Temple service should again collect the blood and sprinkle it on the altar. **Reish Lakish asked Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If an individual **unfit** for Temple service presented the blood, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the blood that remains in the animal?

The shiur picks up in the middle of daf 34b, continuing to work through the *mishnah*. The opening question concerned whether when a *pasul* person throws the blood, the remainder of the blood — the *shirayim* — loses its status and can no longer be used for *zrikat hadam*. The *mishnah* now moves on to a related but distinct scenario.

### Invalid Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִיבֵּל הַכָּשֵׁר וְנָתַן לַפָּסוּל כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna discussed three similar cases: If **the** priest **fit** for Temple service **collected** the blood in a vessel **and gave** the vessel **to an unfit** person, that person should return it to the fit priest.

The *mishnah* establishes that if various invalid persons perform the *avodah*, the *korban* is not invalidated outright — one simply performs a redo. The case given is *kibbel hakasher venatan lapasul*: a valid *kohen* received the blood and handed it to an invalid person, and the invalid person then handed it back to the valid *kohen*. The ruling is that a redo is performed; it is as though the invalid person's action was a nullity, and the *korban* itself is not compromised.

### Multiple Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן פָּסוּל, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַאי ״פָּסוּל״ – טָמֵא, דַּחֲזֵי לַעֲבוֹדַת צִיבּוּר; אֲבָל שְׂמֹאל – לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן שְׂמֹאל – דְּאִית לֵיהּ הֶכְשֵׁירָא בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים; אֲבָל כְּלֵי חוֹל – לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן כְּלֵי חוֹל – מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲזוּ לְקַדּוֹשִׁינְהוּ; אֲבָל הָנָךְ – אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it was **necessary** to mention all of these cases, **as had** the mishna **taught us** only the case where he gave the blood to **an unfit** individual, **I would say: What** does the term: **Unfit,** mean? It means **an impure** priest, who is not completely unfit, **as** he **is fit for communal service** in the Temple when the priests or the entire community are impure, and that is why the blood is not disqualified; **but** if the priest moved the blood to his **left** hand, which under no circumstances may be used to perform the rite of collecting and carrying the blood, it is **no** longer valid. **And had** the mishna **taught us** only with regard to moving the blood to his **left** hand that he should return it to his right hand and present it, one would assume that the reason is **that** the left hand **has validity on Yom Kippur,** since the High Priest carries the spoon of incense with his left hand, **but** if he poured the blood into **a non-sacred vessel** then it is **not** valid. **And had** the mishna **taught us** only with regard to **a non-sacred vessel** that the blood is not disqualified, one could suggest that perhaps it is **because** the vessels **are suitable to be consecrated, but** with regard to **these,** i.e., an unfit individual and the left hand, which have no possibility of becoming fit for service, **say** that the blood does **not** remain valid. Therefore, it is **necessary** for all of the cases to be mentioned.

The *gemara* explains that all of the scenarios listed in the *mishnah* are necessary — *tzricha* — because each one might otherwise be read as categorically distinct. Had the *mishnah* given only the example of a *tamei* person, one might have thought the ruling is specific to *tumah*, because *tumah* carries a certain leniency: *tamei* persons may perform the service for communal fixed-time *korbanot* when the majority of the community is *tamei* — *d'chazi l'avodat tzibur*. One might therefore conclude that since *tumah* is sometimes permissible, an act performed in a state of *tumah* does not actively invalidate. But then one might say that using the left hand — *smolo* — would actively invalidate, since the left hand is virtually never permitted. Yet even there, one can point to *Yom Kippur*, when the *kohen gadol* carries the fire pan and incense with both hands — one in each — so that even the left hand has one context of validity. And if the *mishnah* had given only the case of a non-sanctified vessel — *kli chol* — one might assume that since the vessel can be instantly sanctified simply by declaration (*mishum d'chazi lakadishinhu*), it is likewise not an active invalidation, whereas *tumah* or the left hand, which are less trivially corrected, might genuinely invalidate. Each scenario is therefore necessary: whatever the nature of the invalidity, as long as it does not rise to the level of an invalid *zrika* itself, the *korban* is not rendered permanently invalid — one simply performs the service again.

### Dachuy Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֶיהֱוֵי לֵיהּ דָּחוּי!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara questions the actual *halakha*: **But let** the blood **be** considered **rejected** when placed in the hand of the unfit individual, the left hand, or the non-sacred vessel.

Having established that these cases call for a redo rather than outright invalidation, the *gemara* presses further: why does *dachuy* not apply? *Dachuy* — the principle that something which entered a state of disqualification is considered "pushed away" and cannot return to valid status — is one of the foundational concepts in the laws of *kodshim*, as pervasive in this domain as *pigul* itself. If the blood passed through a *pasul* state — say, while it was in the left hand — it was at that moment unfit for *avodah*. Should it not be considered permanently rejected?

### Chanana Mitzri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הָא מַנִּי – חָנָן הַמִּצְרִי הוּא, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ דְּחוּיִין. דְּתַנְיָא, חָנָן הַמִּצְרִי אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ דָּם בַּכּוֹס, מֵבִיא חֲבֵירוֹ וּמְזַוֵּוג לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: This is** what **Rav Yirmeya of Difti says in the name of Rava:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Ḥanan the Egyptian, who does not** subscribe to the *halakha* of **rejection,** but holds that a sacrifice that was rejected temporarily is not rejected entirely. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Ḥanan the Egyptian says:** In the event that the scapegoat of Yom Kippur was lost, **even** if the **blood** of its partner that is sacrificed to God has already been collected **in the cup,** the blood is not rejected, but rather **he brings** another scapegoat as **its counterpart, and pairs it with** the goat that has already been slaughtered, and the blood is sprinkled.

*Ravina* addresses this to *Rav Ashi*, citing a statement of *Rabbi Yirmiah mi-Difti* in the name of *Rava*: the ruling follows the position of *Chanana HaMitzri*, who rejects the category of *dachuy* entirely — *d'leis lei dechuyin*. The *gemara* demonstrates this from a *braita*: *Chanana HaMitzri omer afilu dam b'kos mevi chaveiro u'mezaveig lo* — even if the blood of one of the two *Yom Kippur* goats has already been collected in a vessel and the paired goat is subsequently lost, one simply brings a replacement goat and pairs it with the existing blood, proceeding as normal. *Chanana HaMitzri* holds that even inanimate objects — blood already collected — do not become rejected merely because they entered a temporary state of unavailability.

To appreciate this position fully, it is worth noting the range of views on the question of the paired *Yom Kippur* goats. One opinion holds that even a living animal can be subject to *dachuy* — *dachuy b'ba'alei chayim* — so that if one goat is lost, the remaining living goat is also rejected and an entirely new pair is required. A second opinion holds *ein dachuy b'ba'alei chayim* — living things are not subject to *dachuy* — but once the goat has been slaughtered and the blood collected, if its pair is then lost, the blood must be discarded and the entire process restarted, since inanimate blood can be rejected. *Chanana HaMitzri* goes furthest of all, holding that there is no *dachuy* even by inanimate objects.

### Kol She'biyado

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כֹּל שֶׁבְּיָדוֹ – לָא הָוֵי דָּחוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says** that there is another explanation to the mishna: **Anything that is in his power** to rectify **is not** considered **rejected,** as in these cases where he can simply return the blood to its proper place.

*Rav Ashi* offers an independent principle that avoids the need to invoke *Chanana HaMitzri*'s sweeping rejection of *dachuy* altogether. He states: *kol she'biyado lo havi dachuy* — anything whose correction is within one's power does not attain the status of *dachuy* in the first place. If something can be trivially remedied, it never truly entered a state of rejection; it was merely a momentary technical difficulty. Thus blood in the left hand, which can be immediately transferred back to the right hand, was never really "pushed away."

One might wonder: is going to fetch a replacement goat not also "within one's power"? The *gemara* implicitly distinguishes — the principle requires not merely that a fix is possible, but that the fix is essentially immediate and effortless. *Rashi* more or less spells this out: going to procure another animal is not sufficiently trivial to qualify as *kol she'biyado*, and therefore the blood in the *Yom Kippur* case is indeed considered *dachuy* by those who hold of that principle. Switching the blood from left hand to right hand, by contrast, is the paradigm of a trivial correction.

The *gemara* concludes that *Rav Ashi*'s principle is the more sensible one — *k'vashtei d'Rav Ashi mistabra* — and moreover that it can be demonstrated from the position of *Rabbi Yehuda* himself, who is elsewhere known to hold the most expansive view of *dachuy*, including *dachuy b'ba'alei chayim*. A *braita* shows that *Rabbi Yehuda* himself maintains *kol she'biyado lo havi dachuy*, because he rules that on *Erev Pesach* blood that had been spilled on the floor of the *azarah* can be scooped up and thrown on the altar. If it were truly *dachuy*, the spilled blood would be permanently rejected. The very fact that *Rabbi Yehuda* permits this fix demonstrates that when a correction is within one's power, the object never enters *dachuy* status. *Shema mina kol she'biyado lo havi dachuy — shema mina*.

## Pesach Blood Spill

### Mixed Blood Fix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כּוֹס אֶחָד הָיָה מְמַלֵּא מִדַּם הַתַּעֲרוֹבֶת, שֶׁאִם יִשָּׁפֵךְ אֶחָד מֵהֶם – נִמְצָא שֶׁהוּא מַכְשִׁירוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וַהֲלֹא לֹא נִתְקַבֵּל בִּכְלִי!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself: It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** A priest **would fill one cup with the mixed blood,** so **that if one of** the cups with the collected blood of one of the Paschal offerings **were to spill,** it would be **found that this** cup of the mixed blood that was sprinkled **would render** the sacrifice **valid.** The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Yehuda: But** the mixed blood **was not collected in a vessel.**

Having cited *Rabbi Yehuda*'s ruling in passing, the *gemara* now turns to examine it in full. The *braita* is quoted directly: *Rabbi Yehuda omer, kos echad haya malei midam hata'aruvot* — on *Erev Pesach*, when hundreds of thousands of *korbanot* were being slaughtered in rapid succession, it was inevitable that some blood would be spilled before reaching the altar. *Rabbi Yehuda*'s solution is elegant: at the end of the day, before *Erev Pesach* concludes, one scoops up from the blood pooled on the floor of the *azarah* — where all the bloods have commingled — and throws it on the altar. Since the blood of any *korban* that was spilled will be present within that mixture, this throwing serves retroactively to place each *korban*'s blood on the altar. *Rabbi Yehuda* relies on the principle *ein dam m'vateil dam* — blood does not nullify blood — so that no individual blood loses its identity when mixed with other blood of the same substance.

### Vessel Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא יָדְעִי? אֶלָּא שֶׁמָּא לֹא נִתְקַבֵּל בִּכְלִי! אָמַר לָהֶן: אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָמַרְתִּי אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁנִּתְקַבֵּל בִּכְלִי. וְהוּא גּוּפֵיהּ מְנָא יָדַע? כֹּהֲנִים זְרִיזִין, וְעָבְדִין הַיָּיא וּמִשְׁתַּפְכִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do** the Rabbis **know** that the blood was not collected in a vessel? Perhaps it was collected and it spilled out. **Rather,** this is what they are saying: **Perhaps it was not collected in a vessel.** Rabbi Yehuda **said to them: I too said** that the blood should be sprinkled **only when it was collected in a vessel.** The Gemara asks: **But he,** Rabbi Yehuda, **himself, from where did he know** if the blood was indeed collected? The Gemara answers: He relies on the fact that **priests are vigilant,** and perform their service properly and collect all of the blood. **But they work swiftly, and** the blood therefore **spills** from the cup.

The *chachamim* challenge *Rabbi Yehuda*: *v'halo lo nitkabel b'kli* — how does one know that the spilled blood was originally received in a valid vessel? Perhaps the blood went directly from the animal's neck onto the floor without ever being caught in a *kli sharet*, in which case gathering it now cannot fix the problem, and one might even be introducing invalid blood onto the *mizbeach*. *Rabbi Yehuda* responds — as the *braita* continues at the top of 35a — *af ani lo amarti ela b'nitkabel b'kli*: he concedes that he is addressing only the case where the blood was caught in a vessel and subsequently spilled. The *gemara* then asks how *Rabbi Yehuda* can know which scenario applies in practice. The answer is that this is a standard operating procedure performed at the end of every *Erev Pesach*: *d'kohanim zrizim hem* — *kohanim* are careful and meticulous, so we can assume that blood was always caught in a vessel — but *avdi hai*, they work with great speed, and accidents happen in the rush, so blood gets spilled. The procedure is designed to address those cases of spilled but properly received blood.

The underlying difficulty in the exchange — why there is any concern about throwing the mixture onto the altar even if it contains some blood that cannot be fixed — seems to rest on an assumption that there is some prohibition against placing invalid blood on the *mizbeach*, even in trace amounts within a valid mixture. That assumption drives the entire debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *chachamim*, though its precise basis is not made fully explicit by the *gemara* here.

### Invalid Blood Bittul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דַּם הַתַּמְצִית מְעוֹרָב בּוֹ! רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: דַּם הַתַּמְצִית קָרוּי דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't blood of exudate,** which is unfit to be sprinkled upon the altar, **mixed with** the blood of the soul on the floor? The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yehuda** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says** that **blood of exudate is considered blood.**

The *gemara* raises a further question: the blood pooled on the floor would inevitably include blood from animals whose slaughter was simply proceeding normally — valid blood that ended up on the floor but had already been thrown on the altar, as well as blood of doubtful status. This raises the concern of invalid blood causing *bittul* — annulling or erasing the presence of the valid blood that one needs to apply to the altar. Here, *Rabbi Yehuda*'s principle of *ein dam m'vateil dam* does the work: since all the substance in question is blood, no particular portion of blood loses its identity to any other, and the valid blood is considered fully present within the mixture.

### Dam Hatamtzit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְעִנְיַן כַּפָּרָה שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְכַפֵּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי הַדָּם הוּא הַנָּפֶשׁ״ – דָּם שֶׁהַנֶּפֶשׁ יוֹצְאָה בּוֹ קָרוּי דָּם, שֶׁאֵין הַנֶּפֶשׁ יוֹצְאָה בּוֹ אֵין קָרוּי דָּם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this answer: **But doesn't Rabbi Elazar say** that **Rabbi Yehuda concedes with regard to atonement that** presenting blood of exudate **does not effect atonement, as it is stated:** "For the soul of the flesh is in the blood; and I have given it to you upon the altar to make atonement for your souls; **for it is the blood** that makes atonement by reason **of the soul"** (Leviticus 17:11). This verse indicates that **blood with which the soul leaves** the animal, i.e., that spurts immediately upon slaughter, **is called blood;** but blood **with which the soul does not leave** the animal, i.e., blood of exudate, **is not called blood.** If so, even according to Rabbi Yehuda, blood of exudate is unfit for presenting upon the altar, and collecting blood from the floor should therefore be ineffective.

A further difficulty is raised: the pooled blood on the *azarah* floor would also include *dam hatamtzit* — blood that flows from the animal after the primary life force has departed, which is not *dam hanefesh* and which does not go on the altar. Would the presence of this blood compromise *Rabbi Yehuda*'s procedure? The *gemara* answers: *Rabbi Yehuda l'ta'amei d'amar dam hatamtzit karuy dam* — *Rabbi Yehuda* consistently holds that even the non-life blood is called blood in the full halachic sense. A *braita* is cited to this effect: *dam hatamtzit b'azhara*, meaning one violates a negative prohibition for eating it; *Rabbi Yehuda omer b'karet* — *Rabbi Yehuda* goes further and says one incurs *karet*, treating it as fully equivalent to *dam hanefesh* for the purposes of the prohibition.

A complication arises, however, from a statement attributed to *Rabbi Elazar*: *modeh Rabbi Yehuda l'inyan kapparah she'eino mechaper* — even *Rabbi Yehuda* concedes that when it comes to *kapparah*, atonement, only *dam hanefesh* atones, as the verse states *ki hadam hu banefesh yechaper*. Blood through which the life force departs is what effects atonement; blood through which it does not depart does not. The *gemara* therefore reframes the concern: the problem is not that *dam hatamtzit* is being placed on the altar in an impermissible way, but rather that since *dam hatamtzit* does not effect *kapparah*, its presence in the mixture risks causing *bittul* — making the valid blood appear absent. And again, *Rabbi Yehuda*'s answer is *ein dam m'vateil dam*: since valid blood is present in the mixture, there is no *bittul*, and the throwing achieves its purpose.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: אֵין דָּם מְבַטֵּל דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yehuda** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as he says: Blood does not nullify blood.** Therefore, the blood in the cup that is fit for sprinkling on the altar is not nullified by the blood of exudate, and the entire mixture may be sprinkled on the altar.

### Drain Stopper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לְדִבְרֵיכֶם, לָמָּה פּוֹקְקִין הָעֲזָרָה? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: שֶׁבַח הוּא לִבְנֵי אַהֲרֹן שֶׁיְּהַלְּכוּ עַד אַרְכוּבּוֹתֵיהֶן בַּדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the continuation of the *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda said to** the Rabbis: **According to your statement** that the mixed blood on the floor may not be used, **why do they plug** the drain of **the** Temple **courtyard** on Passover eve and not allow the blood to flow out through it? The Rabbis **said to him: It is** a source of **praise for the sons of Aaron that they should walk in blood up to their ankles,** thereby demonstrating their love for the Temple service.

*Rabbi Yehuda* now turns the tables on his opponents. *L'divreichem lama pokekim ha'azarah?* — according to your view, why did the *azarah* have its drainage channel stopped up on *Erev Pesach*? The *azarah* had a channel through which blood and refuse could drain away; on *Erev Pesach* this channel was blocked, causing the blood to pool on the floor rather than drain out. If one cannot use the pooled blood to remedy spills — whether because of concerns about invalid blood or concerns about *bittul* — why would there be any purpose in stopping the drain? *Rabbi Yehuda* infers that the stopping of the drain was specifically intended to enable his procedure. The *chachamim* respond: *shevach hu livnei Aharon sheyelechu ad arkuvoteihem bedam* — it is a glory for the sons of *Aharon* to walk ankle-deep in blood during the *avodah*. This is a striking answer: the display of the sheer volume of *korbanot* offered, manifested in the depth of blood through which the *kohanim* wade, is itself considered honorable. The sensibility at work is entirely antithetical to modern instincts, which tend toward concealment of the physical realities of slaughter, but it appears to reflect an embrace of the sanctified nature of the *avodah* in its full, unfiltered reality.

### Kohanim Platforms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דָּם הָוֵי חֲצִיצָה! לַח הוּא, וְלָא הָוֵי חֲצִיצָה. דִּתְנַן: הַדָּם וְהַדְּיוֹ וְהַדְּבַשׁ וְהֶחָלָב – יְבֵישִׁין חוֹצְצִין, לַחִין אֵין חוֹצְצִין. וְהָא קָא מִיתַּוְּוסִי מָאנַיְיהוּ, וּתְנַן: הָיוּ בְּגָדָיו מְטוּשְׁטָשִׁין וְעָבַד – עֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה! וְכִי תֵּימָא דִּמְדַלּוּ לְהוּ, וְהָתַנְיָא: ״מִדּוֹ״ – כְּמִדָּתוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא יְחַסֵּר וְשֶׁלֹּא יוֹתִיר! בְּהוֹלָכַת אֵבָרִים לַכֶּבֶשׁ, דְּלָאו עֲבוֹדָה הִיא. וְלָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְהִקְרִיב הַכֹּהֵן אֶת הַכֹּל״ – זוֹ הוֹלָכַת אֵבָרִים לַכֶּבֶשׁ! אֶלָּא בְּהוֹלָכַת עֵצִים לַמַּעֲרָכָה, דְּלָאו עֲבוֹדָה הִיא. וְלַעֲבוֹדָה הֵיכִי אָזְלִי? אָזְלִי אַאִיצְטְבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **But don't their garments become soiled? And we learned** in a *baraita*: If a priest's **garments were soiled and he performed** a Temple **rite** while wearing them, **his rite is disqualified. And if you would say that they raised** their garments so that they would stay clean, **but isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: "And the priest shall put on **his** linen **garment [*middo*** *vad*]" (Leviticus 6:3). The term *middo*, literally, his measure, teaches that his garment must be **according to his measure [*kemiddato*],** i.e., **that it should not be** too **short and that it should not be** too **long.** If the priest raises his garment, it will no longer be exactly his size. The Gemara answers that the priests would walk in the blood only **while carrying the** sacrificial **limbs to the ramp** of the altar, **which is not** actually **a rite,** but only preparation for a rite. The Gemara asks: **And** is it **not** one of the rites? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **"And the priest shall bring it all** and burn it upon the altar" (Leviticus 1:13); **this is** referring to **the carrying of the limbs to the ramp? Rather,** the priests would walk in the blood only **while carrying wood to the arrangement** of wood on the altar, **which is not a rite.** The Gemara asks: **But** if the floor of the Temple was full of blood; **how did they walk to** perform **the** actual **rites** without soiling their garments? The Gemara answers: **They would walk on platforms** raised above the floor, so that the blood would not reach their garments.

Once the *gemara* accepts that *kohanim* were in fact wading through blood on *Erev Pesach*, a series of practical difficulties presents itself. First: *veha kamitnafi manayhu* — their *bigdei kehunah* would become soiled, and a *mishnah* teaches explicitly that if a *kohen*'s garments were soiled — *hayu begadav metushtashim* — his *avodah* is invalid. One might suggest that the *kohanim* hiked up their garments, but a *braita* states *mado bad k'midato* — the priestly garment must be worn as tailored, and the *gemara* elsewhere indicates this requires wearing it down to the ankles. Second: would not the blood pooling between their feet and the floor constitute a *hatzitzah* — an interposition — that would invalidate their standing? The *gemara* resolves this: *lach velo havi hatzitzah* — a liquid does not constitute a *hatzitza*, as a *mishnah* establishes that blood, ink, honey, and milk only create an interposition when dried.

For the *begadim* question, the *gemara* offers the resolution: *beholachat evarim lakevesh* — the wading in blood would occur specifically during the carrying of limbs to the ramp, which the *gemara* initially suggests does not require full priestly garments. This is challenged, since a verse applies the term *kohen* to this very act, implying that *bigdei kehunah* are required. The *gemara* ultimately restricts the ruling to *beholachat etzim lema'aracha delav avoda hi* — carrying wood to the altar-fire, which is not a technical priestly *avodah* and does not require *bigdei kehunah*. The resulting picture is somewhat remarkable: for all other moments of the *avodah*, the *kohanim* would have moved about on a series of raised platforms built into the floor of the *azarah*, stepping between them to avoid walking in the blood, while the one occasion on which they would wade through blood — carrying wood — was chosen precisely because it does not involve a technical *avodah*. It is a curious architectural and logistical arrangement, constructed to make room for what the *chachamim* call a *shevach* for the sons of *Aharon*.

## Pigul Intro

### Abnormal Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַזֶּבַח לֶאֱכוֹל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכּוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל, וּלְהַקְטִיר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכּוֹ לְהַקְטִיר – כָּשֵׁר. וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who slaughters an offering** with the intent **to eat,** beyond its designated time or outside its designated area, **an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one does **not partake** of it, e.g., the portions of the offering consumed on the altar, **or** with the intent **to burn,** beyond its designated time or outside its designated area, **an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one does **not burn** it on the altar, e.g., the meat of the offering, the offering is **fit, and Rabbi Eliezer deems** it **unfit.**

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishnah* on *pigul*, a subject from which there is no escaping in *Kodshim*. The *mishnah* states: *hashochet et hazevach le'echol davar she'ein darko le'echol lehaktir davar she'ein darko lehaktir* — if one slaughters the sacrifice intending to eat something not normally eaten, or to burn something on the altar that is not normally burned, the *korban* is *kasher*. The "not normally eaten" could mean physically inedible, such as a hoof, or it could mean normally destined for the altar rather than for human consumption. Similarly, intending to burn the meat that is normally eaten, or to eat the *emurim* that are normally burned, does not create *pigul*. *Rabbi Eliezer* takes a stricter position — *Rabbi Eliezer posel* — holding that even misdirected intent between the domain of the altar and the domain of human consumption invalidates, though he does not go so far as to declare it *pigul* with its attendant *karet*.

### No Combination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֶאֱכוֹל דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל, וּלְהַקְטִיר דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לְהַקְטִיר – פָּחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת; כָּשֵׁר. לֶאֱכוֹל כַּחֲצִי זַיִת וּלְהַקְטִיר כַּחֲצִי זַיִת – כָּשֵׁר, שֶׁאֵין אֲכִילָה וְהַקְטָרָה מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** One who slaughters an offering with the intent **to eat,** beyond its designated time or outside its designated area, **an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one does **partake** of it, **or** with the intent **to burn,** beyond its designated time or outside its designated area, **an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one does **burn** it on the altar, but his intent was to partake or burn **less than an olive-bulk,** the offering is **fit.** If his intent was **to eat half an olive-bulk and to burn half an olive-bulk** beyond its designated time or outside its designated area, the offering is **fit, because eating and burning do not join together.**

The *mishnah* continues: if one intends to eat something normally edible or burn something normally burned, but the intended amount is less than a *kezayit* — *pachot mikezayit* — the *korban* remains *kasher*, since *pigul* requires intent involving a *kezayit*. A further case: if one intends to eat half a *kezayit* at the wrong time and burn half a *kezayit* on the altar at the wrong time, combining to a full *kezayit* between the two — the *korban* is still *kasher*, because *she'ein achila vehaktara mitztarfin* — eating and burning on the altar do not combine to constitute the requisite *pigul* intent. These two acts belong to categorically different domains and cannot be aggregated.

### Inedible Parts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַזֶּבַח לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת מִן הָעוֹר, וּמִן הָרוֹטֶב, וּמִן הַקֵּיפֶה, וּמִן הָאָלָל, וּמִן הַעֲצָמוֹת, וּמִן הַגִּידִין, וּמִן הַקְּרָנַיִם, וּמִן הַטְּלָפַיִם – חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ; כָּשֵׁר, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר וְטָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who slaughters an offering** with the intent **to eat, beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area, an olive-bulk from the hide, or from the gravy, or from the spices** that amass at the bottom of the pot together with small amounts of meat, **or from a tendon in the neck, or from the bones, or from the tendons, or from the horns, or from the hooves,** the offering is **fit and one is not liable** to receive *karet* **for it,** neither **due to** the prohibition of ***piggul*,** if the sacrificial rites were performed with the intent to partake of the offering beyond its designated time, **nor** due to the prohibition of **leftover** meat beyond the designated time, **nor** due to the prohibition against partaking of the meat while **ritually impure.**

The *mishnah* then lists a series of parts of the animal that are inedible and toward which a *pigul* intent therefore has no effect: *min ha'or* — the skin; *min harotav* — the broth or gravy produced when the meat is cooked; *min hakipa* — the sediment at the bottom of a pot; *min ha'alal* — the neck sinew, which is too tough to eat; *min ha'atzamot* — the bones; *min hagidim* — the sinews; *min hakarnayim* — the horns; and *min hatelafayim* — the hooves. Intending to eat any of these outside the proper time or place — *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo* — leaves the *korban kasher* and creates no *pigul*. The principle is clear: *pigul* intent requires that the object of the intent be something that is genuinely eaten.

The *mishnah* adds: *ve'ein chayavin aleihem mishum pigul notar v'tamei* — one incurs no liability for *pigul*, *notar*, or *tumah* in connection with these parts. If the animal became *pigul* because the *kohen* intended to eat the meat at the wrong time, one who eats the horns or hooves is not liable. If the animal became *notar* — left over past its permitted time — eating the hooves incurs no *notar* liability, because these parts are not considered meat in any halachically meaningful sense. This principle has well-known relevance to later discussions about the status of gelatin derived from bones and hooves.

### Fetus & Placenta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין לֶאֱכוֹל שְׁלִיל אוֹ שִׁילְיָא בַּחוּץ – לֹא פִּיגֵּל. הַמּוֹלֵק אֶת הַתּוֹרִין לֶאֱכוֹל בֵּיצֵיהֶן בַּחוּץ – לֹא פִּיגֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who slaughters sacrificial** female animals with the intent **to eat** the **fetus** of those animals **or** their **placenta outside** the designated area, **he has not rendered** the offering ***piggul*.** Likewise, in the case of **one who pinches doves,** i.e., slaughters them for sacrifice by cutting the napes of their necks with his fingernail, with the intent **to eat their eggs** that are still in their bodies **outside** the designated area, **he has not rendered** the offering ***piggul*.** This is because the fetus, the placenta, and the eggs are not considered part of the body of the animal or the bird.

The *mishnah* addresses *mukdashim* — which *Rashi* explains consistently refers here to female consecrated animals, since the context always involves something unique to females. If one slaughters these female consecrated animals intending to eat *shalil* or *shilya* — fetus or placenta — outside the proper time, *lo pigel* — no *pigul* is created. The reason, as will be developed in the *gemara*, is that the fetus and placenta are not considered part of the *korban* proper; they are separate entities. An intent directed at a separate entity cannot make the mother *pigul*.

The *mishnah* similarly states that if one performs *melikah* on turtledoves intending to eat their eggs outside the proper time, this also does not create *pigul*. The eggs found inside the bird are, like the fetus, a separate entity from the bird itself.

### Milk & Eggs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲלֵב מוּקְדָּשִׁין וּבֵיצֵי תוֹרִין – אֵין חַיָּיבִים עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר וְטָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** For the same reason, one who consumes **the milk of sacrificial** female animals **or the eggs of doves is not liable** to receive *karet* **for** it, neither **due to** the prohibition of ***piggul*, nor** due to the prohibition of ***notar*, nor** due to the prohibition against partaking of the meat while **ritually impure.**

A final clause is added: *chalav hamukdashim u'veitzei torin ein chayavin aleihem mishum pigul v'notar v'tamei* — the milk of consecrated animals and the eggs of turtledoves do not carry liability for *pigul*, *notar*, or *tumah*. Even if the mother animal has become *pigul* — because the *kohen* intended to eat a *kezayit* of its meat at the wrong time — one who consumes the milk is not liable for *pigul*. The milk and eggs, although edible, are not part of the *korban* and are treated as separate entities.

What is conspicuously absent from this final section is any ruling about the *fetus* in the reverse scenario: when the intent was about the *mother*, does the *fetus* become *pigul*? The *mishnah* tells us that an intent about the milk and eggs does not make the mother *pigul*, and an intent about the fetus does not make the mother *pigul*. But what happens when the mother is made *pigul* — does the fetus become forbidden? The silence of the *mishnah* here is what the *gemara* will immediately investigate. The question also arises regarding the practical status of milk and eggs produced by consecrated animals: they are not *pigul*, not *notar*, not *tamei* — but are they simply *chullin*? If the animal is consecrated, whatever it produces is presumably still owned by the *beit hamikdash*, even if it lacks the specific *kedushah* of the *korban* itself. This touches on the broader question of *gidulei hekdesh* by *kodshei mizbe'ach*, a matter that would require separate investigation.

## Pigul Asymmetry

### Elazar Asymmetry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: פִּיגֵּל בַּזֶּבַח – נִתְפַּגֵּל הַשְּׁלִיל. בַּשְּׁלִיל – לֹא נִתְפַּגֵּל הַזֶּבַח. פִּיגֵּל בָּאָלָל – נִתְפַּגְּלָה מוּרְאָה. בַּמּוּרְאָה – לֹא נִתְפַּגֵּל אָלָל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Elazar says:** If one **renders an offering *piggul*** by slaughtering it with the intent to partake of it beyond its designated time, its **fetus is rendered *piggul*** as well, and one who eats it is liable to receive *karet*. But if he intended to partake **of the fetus** beyond its designated time, **the offering has not been rendered *piggul*.** Likewise, if **he rendered** a bird offering ***piggul* by** having intent to partake of **a tendon in the neck,** which is edible, beyond its designated time, then **the crop** of the bird **is rendered *piggul*** as well, although it is not considered an item that is usually eaten, since there are people who eat it. But if he intended to partake **of the crop** beyond its designated time, then **a tendon in the neck is not rendered *piggul*.**

The *gemara* opens with the statement of *Rabbi Elazar*: *pigul b'zevach nitpagel shalil; u'vishalil lo nitpagel zevach* — if the animal is made *pigul*, the fetus becomes forbidden; but if one had a *pigul* thought about the fetus, the animal does not become *pigul*. This makes explicit what the *mishnah* left implicit by its silence. What is striking here is the asymmetry: the relationship between mother and fetus is not treated symmetrically for purposes of *pigul*.

### Rashi Explanation

*Rashi* explains the asymmetry as follows. In order to make an animal *pigul*, the intent must concern something that is normally eaten. The fetus is not normally eaten under ordinary circumstances — it is at most in the category of *ne'echal al yedei hadechak*, something consumed only in conditions of difficulty or extremity. Therefore, a *pigul* intent directed at the fetus lacks sufficient grounding in normal eating to make the mother *pigul*. However, once the mother has been made *pigul* through a properly directed intent — an intent about something normally eaten — then any part of the animal that is even somewhat edible becomes *pigul*. The fetus, marginally edible, falls within this broader sweep. The asymmetry thus turns on the distinction between what is edible enough to *generate* a *pigul* intent versus what is edible enough to *become pigul* once the animal is already so designated.

### Bird Parts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּיגֵּל בָּאֵימוּרִין – נִתְפַּגְּלוּ פָּרִים. בַּפָּרִים – לֹא נִתְפַּגְּלוּ אֵימוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** If **he rendered** the offering ***piggul*,** i.e., had improper intent **by** intending to burn **the sacrificial portions** that are consumed on the altar beyond their designated time, then **the** rest of the **bulls are rendered *piggul*.** Therefore, if one partakes of their meat, he is liable to receive *karet* due to the prohibition of *piggul*. But if he intended to partake **of** the rest of **the bulls** beyond their designated time, since *piggul* does not apply to them, as they are not destined to be consumed on the altar or to be eaten, **the sacrificial portions** that are consumed on the altar **are not rendered *piggul*.**

The same asymmetry appears in a parallel case involving birds. *Pigul b'alal nitpaglah mora* — if one had a *pigul* intent about the neck sinew (*alal*) of a bird, which in a bird is considered edible, even the *mora* — the crop or stomach — becomes *pigul*. But *bimora lo nitpaglah alal* — if one had a *pigul* intent about the *mora*, the rest of the bird does not become *pigul*. *Rashi* again explains this through the lens of *ne'echal al yedei hadechak*: the *mora* is edible only under duress, so it cannot serve as the source of a *pigul* intent, but it can become *pigul* once the bird has been so designated through a properly grounded intent.

### Tosafot View

*Tosafot* offers a cleaner explanation, though one that does not map as neatly onto the case of the fetus. *Tosafot* holds that *pigul* intent is only operative when it concerns a part of the animal that the Torah commands to eat or to burn on the altar. The *mora* — the crop of the bird — is neither commanded to be eaten nor commanded to be burned; what one normally does with it is discard it. Because even under proper circumstances the *mora* has no prescribed destination, a *pigul* intent directed at it is not a meaningful misdirection. *Pigul* is, at its core, the misdirecting of something heading toward the altar or toward human consumption — and the *mora* is heading neither place. However, once the bird has been made *pigul* through a genuine misdirection of something commanded, the *mora*, as a physically edible part of the animal, is swept up in the resulting prohibition.

### Burned Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: וְשָׁוִים שֶׁאִם חִישֵּׁב בַּאֲכִילַת פָּרִים וּבִשְׂרֵיפָתָן – לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם. מַאי, לָאו הָא חִישֵּׁב בָּאֵימוּרִים – נִתְפַּגְּלוּ פָּרִים? לָא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the following *baraita* **supports** the opinion of Rabbi Elazar: **And** even those who hold that *piggul* applies to the bulls that are burned **agree that if he intended to partake of the bulls or** to **burn them** beyond their designated time **he did not do anything.** This is because these bulls are not eaten or burned on the altar, and intentions can render an offering *piggul* only in the cases of offerings that are eaten or burned on the altar. The Gemara infers: **What, is it not** possible to infer **that** if **he intended with regard to the sacrificial portions** of the bulls that are consumed on the altar to burn them beyond their designated time, the **bulls are rendered *piggul*,** which is in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Elazar? The Gemara rejects this: **No,**

The case of *parim hanisrafim* — the offerings whose blood is brought into the inner sanctum and whose meat is burned outside the *beit hamikdash* rather than eaten — illustrates *Tosafot*'s principle most sharply. *Pigul b'emurim nitpaglah paree* — a *pigul* thought about the *emurim* (the entrails that are burned on the altar) makes the meat *pigul* as well, even though the Torah commands that the meat be burned outside. But *pigul baparim lo nitpaglu emurim* — a *pigul* thought about the meat that is to be burned outside does not make the *emurim pigul*, because the meat of *parim hanisrafim* is not directed toward the altar or toward human consumption; it is neither to be eaten by people nor burned on the altar. A *pigul* thought about it is therefore categorically irrelevant — it is not misdirecting something heading to a commanded destination. *Lo asa velo klum* — one has accomplished nothing.

### Baraita Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָא חִישֵּׁב בָּאֵימוּרִין – נִתְפַּגְּלוּ אֵימוּרִין עַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** derive instead as follows: **But** if **he intended, with regard to the sacrificial portions** of the bulls, to burn them beyond their designated time, the **sacrificial portions themselves are rendered *piggul*,** and one is liable to receive *karet* for eating them. The flesh of the bulls is never rendered *piggul*.

*Leima mesayei lei* — the *gemara* now attempts to support this principle from a *braita*. The *braita* states: *veshavin she'hamachashev b'achilat parim u'visreifatan lo asa velo klum* — there is agreement that one who thinks about eating the meat of *parim hanisrafim* or burning it at the wrong time has accomplished nothing. This directly supports the principle that a thought about the meat that is commanded to be burned outside is not a *pigul* thought.

What the *braita* does not address directly is the corollary: what happens if a *pigul* thought was directed at the *emurim*? If the *chachamim* hold that there *is pigul* by *parim hanisrafim* — which they do, in contrast to *Rabbi Shimon* who denies *pigul* to any offering whose blood is not placed on the outer altar — then the only source of *pigul* for *parim hanisrafim* must be a thought about the *emurim*. So the *gemara* asks: does this prove the asymmetry — that a *pigul* thought about the *emurim* makes the meat *pigul*, but not vice versa? *Mai lav hachi ka'amar: b'emurim nitpaglu haparim?*

### Emurim Thought

The *gemara* declines the inference: *lo* — not necessarily. It is possible to read the *braita* symmetrically. The *braita*'s ruling about the meat may exhaust the discussion: when your thought concerns the meat, neither the animal nor the meat itself is made *pigul*. When your thought concerns the *emurim*, the *emurim* themselves become *pigul* — but perhaps the meat does not. *Hachi ka'amar: b'emurim nitpaglu emurim atzman* — the *emurim* become *pigul*, and if you eat them, you incur *karet* for *pigul* even though they are items normally destined for the altar rather than human consumption. But this does not necessarily mean the meat becomes *pigul* as well. The asymmetry that *Rabbi Elazar* describes — the *pigul* of the *emurim* sweeping in the meat but not vice versa — is not yet proven.

The *gemara* then attempts another avenue of proof. A *braita* dealing with *parim hanisrafim* states that *me'ila* — the prohibition of benefiting from consecrated property — applies from the time of sanctification; that once slaughtered, the meat becomes susceptible to *tumah*, *tevul yom*, *mechusar kippurim*, and *linah*; and that *me'ila* continues to apply until the meat completely chars. The *gemara* focuses on *linah* — the meat becoming *notar* by remaining overnight. If the meat of *parim hanisrafim*, which is commanded to be burned outside the Temple and is not eaten, can become *notar*, perhaps this proves that the meat can also become *pigul*, since *pigul* functions as a kind of conceptual projection of wrong-time thinking onto the reality of the act. This analogy is articulated as *migo depasla belinah pasla bemachshava* — since it is disqualified through *linah*, it is disqualified through [wrong-time] intent as well.

This jump from the world of action to the world of thought — treating *pigul* as a mental analog of *notar* — is taken almost entirely for granted by the *gemara*, which is itself striking. The *gemara* counters, however, that the *braita*'s reference to *linah* may concern only the *emurim*, not the meat. The *sof* of the *braita*, which speaks of *me'ila* applying until the meat chars, concerns the meat — but *reisha u'veseifa*, the earlier and later clauses can each operate in their own domain: *linah* applies to the *emurim*; *me'ila* until charring applies to the meat. The *gemara* acknowledges this is a somewhat strained reading — the more natural *pshat* would have the *linah* apply to the meat as well — but this is the formal resolution. The asymmetry proposed by *Rabbi Elazar* thus remains affirmed but not yet definitively proven from a *braita*. The *gemara* will continue to circle back to this question as the *sugya* develops.

## Non-Pigul Items

### Hair & Body Parts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: פָּרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים וּשְׂעִירִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן מִשֶּׁהוּקְדְּשׁוּ. נִשְׁחֲטוּ – הוּכְשְׁרוּ לִיפָּסֵל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם וּבִמְחוּסַּר כִּיפּוּרִים וּבְלִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (*Me'ila* 9a): In the case of the **bulls that are burned and** the **goats that are burned,** one who derives benefit **from them** is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property **from** the time **that they were consecrated.** Once **they were slaughtered, they are susceptible to being rendered disqualified** for sacrifice **through** contact with **one who immersed that day, and through** contact with **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, **and through** being **left overnight** without the requirements of the offering having been fulfilled.

The *braita* now introduces a *braita* that catalogs items which neither create *pigul* nor become *pigul*: *tzemer sheberoshei kvasim* — the wool on the heads of sheep (which is not burned with the skin and is not edible); *se'ar shebezakan hatyashim* — the hair of the beards of goats; *ha'or* — skin; *harotav* — broth; *hakipa* — sediment; *ha'alal* — the neck sinew; *hamora'ah* — the crop of the bird; *ha'atzamot* — bones; *hagidim* — sinews; *hakarnayim* — horns; *hatalpayim* — hooves.

### Separate Entities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹתֵיב (רָבָא) [רַבָּה]: וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין מְפַגְּלִין וְאֵין מִתְפַּגְּלִין: צֶמֶר שֶׁבְּרָאשֵׁי כְבָשִׂים, וּשְׂעַר שֶׁבִּזְקַן תְּיָישִׁים, וְהָעוֹר, וְהָרוֹטֶב, וְהַקֵּיפֶה, וְהָאָלָל, וְהַמּוּרְאָה, וְהָעֲצָמוֹת, וְהַגִּידִין, וְהַקְּרָנַיִם, וְהַטְּלָפַיִם, וְהַשְּׁלִיל, וְהַשִּׁילְיָא, וַחֲלֵב הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין, וּבֵיצֵי תוֹרִין. [כּוּלָּן] לֹא מְפַגְּלִין וְלֹא מִתְפַּגְּלִין; וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר וְטָמֵא; וְהַמַּעֲלֶה מֵהֶן בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba raises an objection** to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar from a *baraita*: **And these** are portions of the offering **that do not render** an offering ***piggul*, and they do not become *piggul*:** The **wool that is on the head of** the **lambs,** the **hair that is in the beard of** the **goats, the hide, the gravy, the spices** that amass at the bottom of the pot together with small amounts of meat, **a tendon in the neck, the crop, the bones, the tendons, the horns, the hooves, the fetus, the placenta, the milk of sacrificial** animals, **and the eggs of doves.** The *baraita* continues: **All of these do not render** other items ***piggul*** in the case of intent to consume them after their designated time, **and they are not rendered *piggul*, and one is not liable** to receive *karet* **for them,** neither **due to** the prohibition of ***piggul*,** nor the prohibition of ***notar*, nor** the prohibition against partaking of the meat while **ritually impure. And one who offers up** one **of these outside** the Temple is **exempt,** since they are not fit to be sacrificed on the altar.

The *braita* then adds: *v'hashalil v'hashilya* — fetus and placenta; *chalav hamukdashim* — the milk of consecrated animals; and *beitzei torin* — the eggs of turtledoves. These items are treated as separate entities, neither making the animal *pigul* nor becoming *pigul* themselves.

### No Obligation

The *braita* concludes: *kullan lo mifaglin v'lo mitpaglin, v'ein chayavin aleihem mishum pigul notar v'tamei* — none of these make the animal *pigul*, none become *pigul*, and one incurs no liability for *pigul*, *notar*, or *tamei* in connection with any of them. The *braita* further states that one who offers them outside the Temple — *hama'aleh meihen bachutz* — is *patur*, since these are not items normally sacrificed. The logic behind the exemption from *notar* for the fetus, even when the whole animal was left overnight, is that the *korban* is constituted only by the mother — the fetus is simply not part of the *korban*. It is viewed as analogous to the milk: just as the milk in the udder is not *korban* meat, the fetus is not treated as *korban* meat either, regardless of whether it is physically edible.

### Mother Thought

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו לֹא מְפַגְּלִין הַזֶּבַח, וְלֹא מִתְפַּגְּלִין מֵחֲמַת זֶבַח?
>
> **Translation:** Rabba explains: **What,** does the *baraita* **not** mean that all of these items **do not render the offering *piggul*** if one intends to partake of them beyond the designated time, **and they are** also **not rendered *piggul* due to** the rest of the **offering** having been rendered *piggul*? This includes the fetus and placenta, and is therefore not in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Elazar that the rest of the offering does render them *piggul*.

*Rabbah* now raises a challenge from this very *braita*. The *braita* states *kullan lo mifaglin v'lo mitpaglin* — all of these items neither make *pigul* nor become *pigul*. This appears to include the fetus in an absolutely symmetric statement: a *pigul* thought about the fetus does not make the mother *pigul*, and the mother being made *pigul* does not make the fetus *pigul*. This contradicts *Rabbi Elazar*'s asymmetry, according to which if the mother is made *pigul*, the fetus does become *pigul*.

### Baraita Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; לֹא מְפַגְּלִין אֶת הַזֶּבַח, וְלֹא מִתְפַּגְּלִין מֵחֲמַת עַצְמָן. אִי הָכִי, הָא דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: כּוּלָּן לֹא מְפַגְּלִין וְלֹא מִתְפַּגְּלִין – הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** it means that **they do not render the offering *piggul*, and they are not rendered *piggul* due to themselves** if one had intent to partake of them beyond the designated time. But such intent with regard to the offering can render them *piggul*. The Gemara asks: **If so,** concerning **that which is taught** in **the latter clause: All of these do not render** other items ***piggul* and they are not rendered *piggul*, why do I also** need **this?** Doesn't it teach that they are not rendered *piggul* due to the rest of the offering?

The *gemara* resolves this by restricting the scope of the *braita*'s statement. *Lo mifaglin v'lo mitpaglin machmat atzman* — the *braita* is addressing only the scenario in which the *pigul* thought is directed *at* these objects themselves. When the thought is about the fetus, it does not make the mother *pigul*, and it does not make the fetus *pigul* either. When the thought is about the hair or hooves, it neither makes the animal *pigul* nor the hair and hooves *pigul* themselves. The *braita* is not addressing the scenario in which the thought is about the mother: *Rabbi Elazar* would maintain his position that in that scenario, the fetus does become *pigul*. The apparent redundancy of the phrase *kullan lo mifaglin v'lo mitpaglin* is noted, but the *gemara* explains it as a throwaway construction — *aidie d'ba'i l'mitni notar v'tamei*, since the *braita* wanted to add the ruling about *notar* and *tamei*, it deployed the familiar trio of *pigul, notar, tamei* even though the *pigul* component was somewhat redundant in context. Similarly, since the *braita* wanted to state that one is *patur* for offering these outside the Temple, it packaged in the phrase about *pigul* as a formulaic accompaniment.

### Rava Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל – הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא אַיְּידֵי דְבָעֵי לְמִיתְנָא נוֹתָר וְטָמֵא, תְּנָא פִּיגּוּל; הָכָא נָמֵי, אַיְּידֵי דְבָעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי: הַמַּעֲלֶה מֵהֶן בַּחוּץ, תְּנָא נָמֵי: וְכוּלָּן לֹא מְפַגְּלִין וְלֹא מִתְפַּגְּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **And according to your reasoning** that the additional statement is necessary, then with regard to that which it says a third time: **One is not liable** to receive *karet* **for** them **due to** the prohibition of ***piggul*, why do I also** need **this? Rather,** the third statement does not teach an additional *halakha*, but **since** the *tanna* **wants to teach** that one is not liable to receive *karet* due to violating the prohibition of ***notar* and** the prohibition against partaking of the meat while **ritually impure, he taught** the *halakha* with regard to ***piggul*** as well. **Here too,** it can be explained similarly: **Since** the *tanna* **wants to teach** the *halakha* that **one who offers up** one **of these** items **outside** the Temple is exempt, **he also taught: And all of these do not render** other items ***piggul* and they are not rendered *piggul*.** It may be that the rest of the offering does in fact render them *piggul*, as Rabbi Elazar stated.

*Rava* offers a further support for *Rabbi Elazar*'s position directly from the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* tells us that a *pigul* thought about the fetus does not make the mother *pigul*, and that a *pigul* thought about the mother does not make the milk *pigul*. But the *mishnah* pointedly says nothing about what happens to the fetus when the mother is made *pigul*. *Af anan nami tenina* — we can infer from the *mishnah* itself: since the *mishnah* lists the milk and eggs as items that do not become *pigul* even when the mother does, but omits the fetus from this list in the parallel context, the implication is that the fetus *does* become *pigul* when the mother is made *pigul*. *Ela shema mina: kan machmat zevach, kan machmat atzman* — clearly there is an asymmetry, and it is well grounded in a close reading of the *mishnah*. When the thought is directed at the fetus, it neither makes the mother *pigul* nor itself *pigul*. When the thought is directed at the mother, the fetus — unlike the milk and eggs — does become *pigul*. *Shema mina*.

The underlying rationale, as noted earlier, divides between *Rashi* and *Tosafot*. For *Tosafot*, the principle is that *pigul* intent must misdirect something commanded toward the altar or toward human consumption, and the fetus has no such commanded destination. For *Rashi*, it is a matter of degree of edibility: the fetus is only *ne'echal al yedei hadechak* and therefore lacks the requisite centrality to serve as a source of *pigul*, but it is edible enough to be swept in when the mother is already *pigul*. The *emurim* thought case fits *Tosafot*'s framework more cleanly; the fetus case sits more naturally with *Rashi*.

## Blemished Animals

### Akiva Permits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: וּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין; רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מַכְשִׁיר בְּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִים. אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא הִכְשִׁיר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֶלָּא בְּדוּקִּין שֶׁבָּעַיִן, הוֹאִיל וּכְשֵׁירִים בְּעוֹפוֹת. וְהוּא שֶׁקָּדַם הֶקְדֵּישָׁהּ אֶת מוּמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* cited by Rabba is now discussed in a different context: **We learned** in a mishna **there** (84a) that if disqualified offerings are nevertheless brought upon the altar, they are removed, **and blemished** animals are also included in this *halakha*. **Rabbi Akiva deems blemished** animals **fit,** and if they were brought upon the altar they are not removed. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Akiva deemed fit only** an animal with a blemish **on the cornea of the eye** and similar blemishes that are not noticeable, **since they are valid in** the case of **birds. And** in addition, this **is** the *halakha* provided **that its consecration preceded its blemish,** i.e., when the animal was consecrated it was still entirely fit.

The final section of the daf opens a new topic through a *mishnah* cited from elsewhere: the question of whether a blemished animal placed on the altar must be taken down. *Rabbi Akiva machshir b'va'alei mumim* — *Rabbi Akiva* holds that a blemished animal placed on the altar may remain. *Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *Rabbi Akiva*'s ruling applies only when the blemish did not involve a missing limb — *ela b'dukin sheba'ayin*, literally something like a cataract of the eye, though more broadly any non-disqualifying blemish. The reason is derived from the parallel to birds: among birds, no blemish invalidates except a missing limb. Since by birds a comparable blemish would not invalidate, and since by birds we already know the animal may remain on the altar, the same leniency extends to animals with non-limb-missing blemishes.

### Bird Parallel

The limiting principle from birds runs in both directions: it provides the basis for *Rabbi Akiva*'s leniency, and it defines its outer boundary. A missing limb remains disqualifying because even by birds a missing limb would invalidate.

### Female Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בְּעוֹלַת נְקֵבָה, דִּכְמַאן דִּקְדַם מוּמָהּ לְהֶקְדֵּישָׁהּ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says: And Rabbi Akiva concedes with regard to a female burnt offering** that was brought upon the altar **that** since a burnt offering may be only a male animal, **it is comparable to** a case **where its blemish preceded its consecration,** since the disqualifying factor was there from the outset. Therefore, if it was brought upon the altar it must be removed.

*Rabbi Akiva* would also concede — *umodeh Rabbi Akiva b'olat nekeiva* — that a female animal consecrated as an *olah* must be taken down even if placed on the altar, because its disqualification is intrinsic from the moment of consecration. *D'keman d'kodam mum lehakdasha dami* — it is as though the blemish preceded the consecration: since the animal was never fit for the *mizbeach* as an *olah* even for a moment, it falls outside the scope of *Rabbi Akiva*'s leniency, which applies only when the animal was first consecrated in a fit state and subsequently became blemished.

### Zeira Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי זֵירָא: הַמַּעֲלֶה מֵהֶן בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. הָא מֵאִימָּן – חַיָּיב; וְהֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? בְּעוֹלַת נְקֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raises an objection** to this from the *baraita* cited by Rabba: **One who offers up** one **of these outside** the Temple is **exempt,** as they are not fit to be sacrificed on the altar. **But** it can be inferred that if he were to sacrifice **from** the flesh of the **mother of** one of **these** outside the Temple, he would be **liable. And how can you find these** circumstances? It is only **in** the case of **a female burnt offering.** This is because with regard to a peace offering, which may also be from a female animal, its flesh is not burned on the altar but is eaten. Therefore, there would be no liability for sacrificing it outside the Temple.

*Rabbi Zeira* poses a challenge from the *braita* about the fetus. As established earlier, if one offers the fetus outside the Temple one is *patur*; the implication — *ha me'imurei iman chayav* — is that one would be *chayav* for offering the mother's *emurim* outside the Temple. But for one to be *chayav* for offering something outside the Temple, that thing must be something that would in some context be valid if offered inside. Since we are discussing a fetus born of a female *korban*, the mother is female. If the mother is an *olah*, a female *olah* — *olat nekeiva* — must come down from the altar according to *Rabbi Akiva*'s own concession. How then can one be *chayav* for offering the female *olah*'s meat outside the Temple, if even inside the Temple it would not be permitted to remain?

### Meat vs Emurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא קָסָבַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא עוֹלַת נְקֵבָה אִם עָלְתָה לֹא תֵּרֵד, הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ אִם עָלְתָה תֵּרֵד, הָא מַנִּי? אֵימָא: הַמַּעֲלֶה מֵהֶן בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר, הָא מֵאֵימוּרֵי אִימָּן – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira explains his objection: **Granted, if you say** that **Rabbi Akiva holds** with regard to **a female burnt offering** that **if it ascended** onto the altar **it does not descend,** in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? It **is** that of **Rabbi Akiva.** This is because one is liable for sacrificing any offering outside the Temple that may be sacrificed on the altar, including one that was brought upon the altar inappropriately but is not removed. **But if you say** that with regard to a female burnt offering **if it ascended** the altar **it** must **descend,** and it entirely lacks the status of an offering fit to be sacrificed on the altar, in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? The Gemara answers: **Say** that the inference should be as follows: **One who offers up** one **of those** items **outside** the Temple is **exempt, but** if he sacrificed **from the sacrificial portions of** the **mother of** one of **these,** he is **liable.** This *halakha* can apply to both a sin offering and a peace offering, for which a female may be brought and whose sacrificial portions are burned on the altar.

The *gemara* resolves this by shifting the referent. Rather than reading the *braita*'s implication as *chayav* for the meat of the mother, the *gemara* reads it as *chayav* for the *emurim* of the mother. One is *chayav* outside the Temple for burning *emurim* on an external fire, and *emurim* are brought from any *korban* — including *shelamim* or *chatas* — which may be female. There is thus no need to invoke a female *olah* at all; the *braita*'s ruling applies to the *emurim* of a female *shelamim* or *chatas*, which are entirely permissible. The *gemara* does note the difficulty with this reading: *veha mehem katani* — the *braita* speaks of "them," referring to the fetus, with the contrast being the mother, implying the parallel is between the fetus's meat and the mother's meat, not the mother's *emurim*. The *gemara*'s final resolution is to read *hamale me'imureihen bachutz patur* — if one offers the *emurim of the fetus* outside, one is *patur* — while the implication for the mother concerns her *emurim*, not her meat. This is admittedly a difficult reading of the *braita*, and the straightforward *pshat* would be that the ruling concerns the meat of a female *olah* — in which case *Rabbi Akiva* would need to hold, contrary to his stated concession, that a female *olah* placed on the altar may remain. The *sugya* ends with this tension unresolved, and the daf closes here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״מֵהֶן״ קָתָנֵי – וְאִימָּן דּוּמְיָא דִּידְהוּ! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הַמַּעֲלֶה מֵאֵימוּרֵיהֶן בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר, הָא מֵאֵימוּרֵי אִימָּן – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** the *baraita* **teaches** this *halakha* with the term: **Of** one of **these,** indicating that it is referring to one who sacrifices the flesh, and not the sacrificial portions alone. **And** the case of **their mother** is **similar to** that of **them,** and is therefore referring to sacrificing the flesh of the mother outside the Temple, and not the sacrificial portions. **Rather, say** as follows: **One who offers up** part **of their sacrificial portions outside** the Temple is **exempt, but** one who offered up some **of the sacrificial portions of** the **mother of** one of **these** is **liable.**
