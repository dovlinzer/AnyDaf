# Bava Batra 174 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arevim: Concept & Obliga…

### Opening & Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֶפָּרַע מִמִּי שֶׁאֶרְצֶה״ – יִפָּרַע מִן הֶעָרֵב. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אִם יֵשׁ נְכָסִים לַלֹּוֶה – בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ לֹא יִפָּרַע מִן הֶעָרֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **But if** the creditor **said to** the debtor: I am lending the money **on the condition that I will collect** the debt **from whomever I wish,** i.e., either the debtor or the guarantor, he **can collect** the debt **from the guarantor. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: If the debtor has property** of his own, then **whether** in **this** case, where the creditor stipulated this condition, **or that** case, where he did not, he **cannot collect** the debt **from the guarantor.**

Today's *daf* is 174, and we pick up at the bottom of 173b at the two dots. We are in the *locus classicus* of the discussions of *arevim* — of guarantors. This is where the topic receives its central treatment in *Shas*, though naturally it arises in other places as well. The *Mishna* introduced the concept that a person may serve as a guarantor for another's loan, and the *Gemara* raised two foundational questions about this institution before we could proceed.

### Kinyan & Asmachta Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: עָרֵב דְּמִשְׁתַּעְבַּד, מַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי; לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי דְּאָמַר: אַסְמַכְתָּא קָנְיָא – עָרֵב מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד. לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: אַסְמַכְתָּא לָא קָנְיָא – עָרֵב לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ameimar said:** The issue of **whether** or not **a guarantor** in fact **becomes obligated** to repay the loan he has guaranteed is **a dispute** between **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei. According to Rabbi Yosei, who says** that **a transaction with inconclusive consent [*asmakhta*] effects acquisition, a guarantor becomes obligated** to repay the loan, whereas **according to Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that **an *asmakhta* does not effect acquisition, a guarantor** does **not become obligated** to repay the loan. Any obligation one undertakes that is dependent on the fulfillment of certain conditions that he does not expect will be fulfilled, in this case the debtor's default on the loan, is considered an *asmakhta*.

The *Gemara*'s two questions concern the very basis upon which a guarantor can be bound at all. The first question is: how can a person obligate himself merely through a verbal commitment, without performing a *kinyan*? As a general rule, one cannot create a legally binding obligation through words alone — a formal act of acquisition is required. We will see later in the *Gemara* that in certain circumstances a *kinyan* is indeed necessary, but in other circumstances — particularly when the guarantorship is undertaken at the moment the loan is extended — the verbal commitment alone suffices. The second question concerns *asmachta*: a guarantor's promise resembles a conditional undertaking that one hopes never to be called upon to fulfill. One commits to it in order to induce the lender to extend credit, and not out of genuine present resolve — precisely the structure that renders an *asmachta* non-binding.

### Trust & Integrity Soluti…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: הָא מַעֲשִׂים בְּכׇל יוֹם, דְּאַסְמַכְתָּא לָא קָנְיָא וְעָרֵב מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Ameimar** that he was conflating these two issues: **But** it is **a daily occurrence,** i.e., it is taken for granted, **that an *asmakhta* does not effect acquisition, and** it is also taken for granted that **a guarantor becomes obligated** to repay the loan he has guaranteed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בְּהָהוּא הֲנָאָה דְּקָא מְהֵימַן לֵיהּ – גָּמַר וּמִשְׁתַּעְבַּד נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said: Through that satisfaction that** the guarantor feels when the creditor **trusts him** and loans the money based on his guarantee, the guarantor **resolves** to **obligate himself** to repay the loan. Guaranteeing a loan is unlike a usual case of an obligation undertaken that is dependent on the fulfillment of certain conditions that he does not expect will be fulfilled, in which the commitment is not considered a real one. Here, the one obligating himself experiences a sense of satisfaction when the money is loaned to the debtor, and due to that, fully commits to fulfill his obligation.

As to the first issue, the *Gemara* derives the institution of *areivut* from *psukim*, establishing that the Torah itself recognizes such an institution. The relevant *psukim* do not make entirely explicit that verbal agreement alone suffices — indeed, one of them speaks of a handshake, which is at least somewhat more than purely verbal commitment — but from these verses the institution is grounded. As to the *asmachta* problem, the *Gemara* initially entertains the possibility that guarantorship might indeed be unenforceable on grounds of *asmachta*, but then rejects this: since we rule that *asmachta* is not binding, and yet guarantorship demonstrably is binding, we must explain the distinction. The *Gemara* answers that the case of guarantorship differs from a standard *asmachta* because of the *hana'ah* — the benefit — the guarantor receives. One might frame this more precisely: when a lender extends money to the borrower on the strength of the guarantor's word, the guarantor has placed his own integrity and trustworthiness on the line. The lender's act of reliance transforms the guarantor's commitment from a speculative conditional promise into a genuine, wholehearted obligation. The guarantor truly means to commit, because the very trust reposed in him demands that he live up to it. Accordingly, even though *asmachta* is generally not *kanya*, in this context it is.

### Mishna: Order of Collect…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמַר ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֶפָּרַע מִמִּי שֶׁאֶרְצֶה״ כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין נְכָסִים לַלֹּוֶה, אֲבָל יֵשׁ נְכָסִים לַלֹּוֶה – לֹא יִפָּרַע מִן הֶעָרֵב.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **But if** the creditor **said** to the debtor: I am lending the money **on the condition that I will collect** the debt **from whomever I wish,** he can collect the debt from the guarantor. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They taught** this **only when the debtor has no property** of his own from which to repay the loan, **but** if **the debtor has property** the creditor **cannot collect** the debt **from the guarantor.**

Having established the institution itself, the *Gemara* clarified one further point from the *Mishna*: when the *Mishna* says not to go to the *areiv*, it does not mean one may never go to the guarantor at all — it means one may not go to the *areiv* first. The *loveh*, the borrower, must be pursued first. The question now becomes: what precisely governs the ordering, and under what conditions may the guarantor be approached?

## Collection Order: Debtor…

### Mishna & Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ עַל יְדֵי עָרֵב – לֹא יִפָּרַע מִן הֶעָרֵב. וְאִם אָמַר ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֶפָּרַע מִמִּי שֶׁאֶרְצֶה״ – יִפָּרַע מִן הֶעָרֵב. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּשֶׁאֵין נְכָסִים לַלֹּוֶה; אֲבָל יֵשׁ נְכָסִים לַלֹּוֶה – לֹא יִפָּרַע מִן הֶעָרֵב. וְקַבְּלָן, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ נְכָסִים לַלֹּוֶה – יִפָּרַע מִן הַקַּבְּלָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: The mishna **is incomplete and this** is what **it is teaching: One who lends** money **to another with** the assurance of **a guarantor cannot collect** the debt **from the guarantor** before first claiming the debt from the debtor. **But if** the creditor **said** to the debtor: I am lending the money **on the condition that I will collect** the debt **from whomever I wish,** he **can collect** the debt **from the guarantor. In what** case **is this statement said? When the debtor has no property** of his own from which to repay the debt; **but** if **the debtor has property,** the creditor **cannot collect** the debt **from the guarantor.** This is the *halakha* with regard to a standard guarantor, **but** in the case of **an unconditional guarantor, even if the debtor has property** of his own, the creditor **can collect** the debt **from the unconditional guarantor.**

The *Mishna* states that if the lender stipulated — *al menat she-efra mi-mi she-ertzeh*, "on condition that I may collect from whomever I wish" — then the lender may go directly to the guarantor. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* disagrees and holds that even with this retained right, the lender may not go to the guarantor if the *loveh* has property. *Rabbah bar bar Chana* then reports in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *lo shanu* — the rule permitting collection directly from the guarantor when the retained right was stipulated applies only *ella she-ein nechasim la-loveh*, when the borrower lacks property. But if the borrower has property, *lo yifra min ha-areiv* — one may not go to the guarantor first.

The *Gemara* immediately objects: but this is exactly the position of *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* stated at the end of the *Mishna*! If that is merely *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s view, the inference is that the *Tanna Kamma* holds that it makes no difference — even if the *loveh* has property and the retained right was stipulated, one may go straight to the guarantor. To accommodate *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling, the *Gemara* says *chisorei mechasra ve-hachi katani* — the *Mishna* is incomplete and must be read as containing an additional clause. The reconstructed *Mishna* reads: if you lend money through a guarantor without any stipulation, you may not go to the *areiv* first. If you stipulate that you may collect from whomever you wish, you may go to the *areiv* — but this applies only when the borrower has no obvious property. However, if the borrower does have obvious property, you must pursue the borrower first even when you retained the right to choose.

This raises a natural question. If the retained right permits you to go to the *areiv* only when the borrower lacks obvious property — then in the basic case without any stipulation, when exactly may the lender approach the guarantor at all? The answer requires careful attention to what "lacks property" means in each context. There appear to be two distinct levels of inability to collect. One is that there are simply no obvious, visible assets — no real estate, no apparent holdings. The second is that collection has genuinely proven fruitless: the borrower has been brought to court, his assets have been investigated, and nothing has been found; or he has fled; or he refuses to appear before the court. In the basic case without stipulation, the *areiv* may be approached only when collection from the borrower has truly proven impossible — one of these latter, more extreme scenarios. When the retained right is stipulated, the lender may approach the *areiv* already at the earlier threshold of no obvious assets.

### Five Gradations (Tosafot)

*Tosafot* formalizes these distinctions into a precise taxonomy, enumerating five gradations of guarantor status. This enumeration appears directly across from the *Rabbeinu Gershom* at the top of the page, beginning with the words *v'chamesh midos b'arevut* — there are five levels of guarantorship.

### Level 1: Basic Areiv

The first level is the basic guarantor — a case where someone becomes a guarantor without stipulating any retained right: *ha-malveh yedei areiv v'lo asni mi mi she-ertzeh*. In such a case, *toveah ha-loveh techilah b'veit din* — the lender must pursue the borrower first in court, whether or not the borrower appears to have assets. Only if it proves impossible to compel the borrower — *v'im lo yuchal l'khof et ha-loveh v'lo tza'is dina*, if he cannot be coerced or refuses to appear — may the lender then approach the guarantor's household.

### Level 2: Retained Right

The second level is a guarantor where the lender stipulated that he retains the right to collect from whomever he chooses — *im hisneh mi she-ertzeh efra* — and the borrower has no obvious assets, which *Tosafot* specifies as meaning primarily no *karka*, no real estate. In that case, *nifra min ha-areiv techilah* — the lender may go to the guarantor immediately. Critically, this is an option, not an obligation: *im yirtzeh ladun im areiv techilah, v'im yirtzeh min ha-loveh* — if the lender prefers to pursue the guarantor, he may; if he prefers to pursue the borrower, he may do that as well.

### Level 3: Debtor Has Asse…

The third level is also a guarantor with a retained right — but here the borrower does have obvious property, meaning real estate or other identifiable assets. In this case, *afilu asni, nifra min ha-loveh techilah* — even though the retained right was stipulated, the lender must pursue the borrower first.

### Level 4: Kablan Status

The fourth level is the *kablan*. A *kablan* is one who says, in effect: give the money to Michael and we will treat it as if you gave it to me — *ten lo va-ani nosein lecha*. By using this formulation, the *kablan* places himself on the front line: it is as if he himself is the borrower. Accordingly, *bein yesh lo nechasim la-loveh bein ein lo* — whether or not the borrower has obvious assets — *nifra min ha-areiv techilah*, the lender may go directly to the *kablan* first if he so chooses.

### Level 5: Direct Transfer

The fifth and final level is *nosei v'nosein b'yad* — where the lender actually hands the money to the guarantor, and the guarantor physically transfers it to the borrower. In this scenario, the legal relationship between the lender and the borrower is entirely severed. *Yifra min ha-areiv* — the lender's only recourse is to the guarantor — *v'lo min ha-loveh*, and not from the borrower at all, *she-ein malveh al ha-loveh klum* — the lender has no legal relationship whatsoever with the borrower.

The distinction between the fourth and fifth levels is worth pausing over. At the *kablan* level, the *kablan* says "give it to Michael and treat it as if I received it" — Michael is still in the room and receives the money directly from the lender, but the *kablan* by his formulation inserts himself as the primary obligor. In the *nosei v'nosein b'yad* case, the money passes through the guarantor's hands physically: the lender gives it to the guarantor, and the guarantor then hands it to the borrower. As a matter of *halakha*, what makes the difference is not who is present in the room but through whose hands the money actually passes. So long as the lender gives the money directly to the borrower, the borrower retains some legal relationship to the lender, even if secondary. Only where the money is genuinely first received by the guarantor and then conveyed is the borrower fully out of the picture.

*Tosafot*'s enumeration here is clearly motivated by *halakha le-ma'aseh*: practical guidance for real transactions. At the bottom of that same *Tosafot*, he even ventures into Old French to discuss certain linguistic markers relevant to categorization — noting, for instance, that one who does not use the language of a loan, *ela lashon pir'on*, is more in the *kablan* category — and he concludes by addressing the mechanics of performing a *kinyan* in this context, including the question of *keli shel makneh* versus *keli shel koneh*.

### Rav Huna: Language Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: ״הַלְוֵהוּ וַאֲנִי עָרֵב״; ״הַלְוֵהוּ וַאֲנִי פּוֹרֵעַ״; ״הַלְוֵהוּ וַאֲנִי חַיָּיב״; ״הַלְוֵהוּ וַאֲנִי נוֹתֵן״ – כּוּלָּן לְשׁוֹן עַרְבוּת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses which expressions confer upon a person the status of a standard guarantor, and which confer the status of an unconditional guarantor. **Rav Huna says** that if one says to a potential creditor: **Lend** money to **him and I** am **a guarantor,** or: **Lend** money to **him and I** will **repay** the debt, or: **Lend** money to **him and I** am **obligated** to repay the debt, or: **Lend** money to **him and I** will **give** the money back to you, **all these are expressions** that confer the status of a standard **guarantee.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״תֵּן לוֹ וַאֲנִי קַבְּלָן״; ״תֵּן לוֹ וַאֲנִי פּוֹרֵעַ״; ״תֵּן לוֹ וַאֲנִי חַיָּיב״; ״תֵּן לוֹ וַאֲנִי נוֹתֵן״ – כּוּלָּן לְשׁוֹן קַבְּלָנוּת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** If one says: **Give** money **to him and I** am **an unconditional guarantor,** or: **Give** money **to him and I** will **repay** the debt, or: **Give** money **to him and I** am **obligated** to repay the debt, or: **Give** money **to him and I** will **give** the money back to you, **all these are expressions** that confer the status of **an unconditional guarantee.** The usage of the word give, as opposed to lend, confers the status of an unconditional guarantor.

With the conceptual structure in hand, the *Gemara* turns to a critical practical question: what precise language places a person in the category of *areiv* versus *kablan*? This is the ruling of *Rav Huna*. He teaches: if David says *halveihu va-ani areiv* — "lend him the money and I will be the guarantor" — that is the paradigmatic *areiv* formulation. And the same is true if instead of the word *areiv* he uses any of several variations: *halveihu va-ani poreia* ("lend him and I will pay"), *halveihu va-ani chayav* ("lend him and I will be obligated"), *halveihu va-ani eten* ("lend him and I will give") — *kullan lashon arevut hen*, all of these are the language of guarantorship. What they have in common is the verb *halveihu*: lend him, Michael, the money. By using the language of lending to Michael, David implies that Michael is the primary obligor — the one who owes the money — while David takes a secondary position.

Conversely, *ten lo va-ani kablan* — "give it to him and I will be the *kablan*" — is the paradigmatic *kablan* formulation. And similarly: *ten lo va-ani poreia*, *ten lo va-ani chayav*, *ten lo va-ani eten* — *kullan lashon kablanut hen*, all are the language of *kablanut*. The governing distinction, then, is the verb describing what the lender does with the borrower: "lend him" implies the borrower is the primary obligor; "give it to him" does not carry that implication. One can illustrate this intuitively: if a father accompanies his daughter to a store and says "give her a candy," the daughter is not the one who owes the storekeeper money — the father is. "Give it to him" does not inherently imply the recipient is the debtor.

### Amoraim Debate: Labels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״הַלְוֵהוּ וַאֲנִי קַבְּלָן״; ״תֵּן לוֹ וַאֲנִי עָרֵב״ – מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What** is the *halakha* if one says: **Lend** money to **him and I** am **an unconditional guarantor?** On the one hand, the word lend is used, as opposed to give, but on the other hand, he explicitly states that he will be an unconditional guarantor. What is the *halakha* if one says: **Give** money **to him and I** am **a guarantor?** On the one hand, the word give is used, as opposed to lend, but on the other hand, he states that he will be a guarantor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: לְשׁוֹן עַרְבוּת – עַרְבוּת, לְשׁוֹן קַבְּלָנוּת – קַבְּלָנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says** in resolving this dilemma: When one employs **the language of** a standard **guarantee,** it is a standard **guarantee,** even if he also used the word give. And when one employs **the language of an unconditional guarantee,** it is **an unconditional guarantee,** even if he also used the word lend.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: כּוּלָּן לְשׁוֹן קַבְּלָנוּת הֵן, בַּר מֵ״הַלְוֵהוּ וַאֲנִי עָרֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: All of** the expressions mentioned in this discussion **are expressions of an unconditional guarantee, except for: Lend** money to **him and I** am **a guarantor.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כּוּלָּן לְשׁוֹן עַרְבוּת הֵן, בַּר מִ״תֵּן לוֹ וַאֲנִי נוֹתֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: All** of the expressions mentioned in this discussion **are expressions of** a standard **guarantee, except for: Give** money **to him and I** will **give** the money back to you.

The *Gemara* then raises the harder cases — the *ibayu lehu* — where the two key elements point in opposite directions. What if David says *halveihu va-ani kablan* — "lend him and I'll be a *kablan*"? Or inversely, *ten lo va-ani areiv* — "give it to him and I'll be a guarantor"? Now both the verb and the explicit label are each present, and they pull toward different categories. Which controls?

*Rav Yitzchak* rules: *lashon arevut arevut, lashon kablanut kablanut* — the label governs. If you explicitly called yourself an *areiv*, you are an *areiv*; if you called yourself a *kablan*, you are a *kablan*. This is a sensible position: when a person explicitly names his role, that declaration controls, regardless of what the surrounding verb might imply.

*Rav Chisda* takes the opposite extreme: *kulam lashon kablanut hen bar me-halveihu va-ani areiv*. Everything, including the cases in the *Baraita*, is to be treated as *kablanut* — with a single exception. The only case that qualifies as a basic *areiv* is the most paradigmatic one: *halveihu va-ani areiv*, both the verb "lend him" and the explicit label "I am an *areiv*." Any deviation from this exact formulation — lend him and I will pay, lend him and I will give, and certainly any formulation with "give it to him" — is categorized as *kablanut*. *Rav Chisda* thus defaults everything toward the *kablan* category, requiring a very specific double formulation to establish the more limited *areiv* role.

*Rava* takes precisely the opposite default: *kulam lashon arevut hen*, everything is *arevut*, *bar miten lo va-ani noten* — except the clearest *kablan* formulations, specifically "give it to him and I will give" or "give it to him and I am the *kablan*." Unless the language falls squarely within the *kablan* category — both the "give it to him" verb and the *kablan* framing — *Rava* treats the commitment as mere *arevut*. Any echo of the lending relationship, any language that implies the borrower owes the money, places the guarantor in the *areiv* category.

The underlying debate is about presumptions: what should we assume a guarantor intends when his language is ambiguous? One argument for defaulting to *kablanut* is societal — making it easier for a creditor to reach the guarantor encourages lending, precisely the rationale invoked in secular cosigning law. The counterargument runs the other way: why should we assume that David wishes to step in more than the minimum necessary? He agreed to stand behind Michael; why presume he meant to stand in Michael's place? The *Gemara* does not resolve which of these *Amoraim* we follow here.

### Direct Money = Debtor St…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר בַּר אַמֵּימָר לְרַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי אָמַר אַבָּא: ״תֵּן לוֹ וַאֲנִי נוֹתֵן״ – אֵין לַמַּלְוֶה עַל הַלֹּוֶה כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Ameimar said to Rav Ashi: My father said the following:** If one says: **Give** money **to him and I** will **give** the money back to you, this expression binds the guarantor to such an extent that **the lender has no** claim **against the debtor at all;** his only option is to collect the debt from the guarantor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא; לָא מִיפְּטַר לֹוֶה מִינֵּיהּ דְּמַלְוֶה, עַד שֶׁיִּשָּׂא וְיִתֵּן בַּיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this last statement: **But** that **is not so.** Rather, **the debtor is not exempted from** dealing with the **creditor unless** the guarantor **takes** the money from the creditor **and gives** it to the debtor **with** his own **hand.**

One final clarification closes this sugya. *Mar bar Ameimar* reports to *Rav Ashi* in the name of *Ameimar*: if David says *ten lo va-ani noten* — "give it to him and I will give it to you" — or similarly the paradigmatic *kablan* formulation, then *ein la-malveh al ha-loveh klum*, the lender has no claim against the borrower at all. The *Gemara* immediately qualifies this: *v'la hi*, that is not so. *Lo miptar loveh minei de-malveh*, the borrower does not escape his obligation to the lender, *ad de-shakil v'yahiv be-yad* — unless and until the money was actually received by the guarantor and then physically handed over. This is precisely what distinguishes *kablan* from *nosei v'nosein b'yad*. As long as the lender gives the money directly to the borrower — whatever language David used — Michael retains some obligation. Only when the money genuinely passed through David's hands, with David receiving it and then conveying it to Michael, is Michael fully removed from the legal picture.

## Property as Guarantor

### Seized Property Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּיָּינָא דְּאַחֲתֵיהּ לְמַלְוֶה לְנִכְסֵי [דְלֹוֶה] מִקַּמֵּי דְּלִתְבְּעֵיהּ לְלֹוֶה, סַלְּקֵיהּ רַב חָנִין בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יֵיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was once **a certain judge who permitted a creditor to enter the debtor's property** and collect it for his debt **before lodging a claim against the debtor** himself. **Rav Ḥanin, son of Rav Yeiva,** overruled that judge and **expelled** the creditor from the seized property.

The *Gemara* now turns to a related but conceptually distinct situation that illuminates a broader principle. A certain judge allowed a creditor to seize the debtor's property without first giving the debtor an opportunity to pay directly. The debtor was apparently absent — away visiting, so to speak — and the court allowed the creditor to proceed directly against his real estate. *Rav Chani brei de-Rav Yeiva* reversed the ruling, holding that the seizure was improper: the creditor had not first attempted direct collection from the borrower. *Rava* famously remarked that no one but *Rav Chani brei de-Rav Yeiva* would have had the insight to reverse the ruling in this way — it required real sharpness to recognize that the *beit din*'s decision needed to be overturned.

### Nichsei K'Areiv Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאן חַכִּים לְמֶעְבַּד כִּי הָא מִילְּתָא, אִי לָאו רַב חָנִין בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יֵיבָא! קָסָבַר: נִכְסֵיהּ דְּבַר אִינִישׁ – אִינּוּן מְעָרְבִין יָתֵיהּ; וּתְנַן: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ עַל יְדֵי עָרֵב – לֹא יִפָּרַע מִן הֶעָרֵב, וְקַיְימָא לַן: לֹא יִתְבַּע עָרֵב תְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** When **Rava** heard about this he **said: Who** is **wise** enough **to perform such a matter,** i.e., to issue this ruling, **if not Rav Ḥanin, son of Rav Yeiva? He holds that a person's property is a guarantee for him,** i.e., it acts as a guarantor for the loan if the debtor does not repay it; **and we learned** in the mishna: **One who lends** money **to another with** the assurance of **a guarantor cannot collect** the debt **from the guarantor. And we established** that the mishna means that he **cannot collect** the debt **from the guarantor at the outset,** before seeking payment from the debtor himself. So too, he cannot collect the debt by taking the debtor's property without first seeking payment from the debtor himself.

*Rava* then articulates the conceptual foundation: *nichsei de-var inish inun me'arvin leh* — a person's property functions as his guarantor. This is a striking idea and worth unpacking carefully. When a lender extends money to a borrower, the lender's confidence rests partly on the knowledge that the borrower has assets — a house, for example — which can ultimately be seized if he defaults. Those assets are, in a sense, the borrower's co-signer: they stand behind him and give the lender security. This is precisely the logic of liens. But if the borrower's property functions as his *areiv*, then the *halakha* of *areiv* applies to it: just as one may not go to the guarantor before attempting direct collection from the borrower, so too one may not go straight to the property — treating the lender-borrower relationship as if it is merely a relationship between the lender and the borrower's assets — before first attempting to engage the borrower directly.

The reasoning reflects a fundamental ordering of obligations. The lender's primary legal relationship is with the borrower personally — Michael owes the money. Only when that personal relationship breaks down, when Michael has defaulted and cannot or will not pay, does the lender's recourse shift to Michael's property, now operating as the backup guarantor. Seizing the property directly, bypassing the personal demand, skips over the primary relationship and moves prematurely to the secondary one. This is conceptually the same error as going to the *areiv* before trying the *loveh*. Property is the *areiv*; the borrower's personal obligation comes first.

### Attempt Direct First

The practical *halakha* that follows is therefore: before seizing a debtor's property, the creditor must first attempt direct collection from the borrower. Giving the borrower the opportunity to pay — even if he would pay by handing over his house — keeps the transaction within the primary lender-borrower relationship. Only after that relationship has failed, only after the borrower has defaulted and refused or been unable to respond, may the creditor bypass him and proceed directly against the property. This is not merely procedural courtesy; it reflects the conceptual architecture of the lien itself.

## Guarantor Reimbursement…

### Orphans Case: Rav Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא עָרְבָא דְּיַתְמֵי, דְּפַרְעֵיהּ לְמַלְוֶה מִקַּמֵּי דְּלוֹדְעִינְהוּ לְיַתְמֵי;
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was once **a certain guarantor for orphans** whose father borrowed money and died, and the orphans were minors, and this guarantor **repaid** the debt **to the creditor before informing the orphans** that he was repaying the debt for them. The guarantor now sought reimbursement from the orphans.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: פְּרִיעַת בַּעַל חוֹב מִצְוָה, וְיַתְמֵי לָאו בְּנֵי מֶיעְבַּד מִצְוָה נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Repaying a creditor** is **a mitzva, and orphans** who are minors **are not obligated in performing a mitzva.** They therefore do not have to repay any debts owed by their deceased father until they reach the age of majority.

The *Gemara* now turns to the question of a guarantor who has actually paid the creditor and seeks reimbursement from the borrower's estate. The specific case involves a guarantor who had stood behind a father's loan; the father died, and the guarantor went ahead and paid off the creditor — but he did so before informing the orphans. He now seeks to recover from the orphans. *Rav Pappa* rules: *priat baal chov mitzvah* — repaying a creditor is a *mitzvah* — *ve-yatmi lav bnei ma'avid mitzvah ninhu*, and the orphans, being minors, cannot yet be compelled to perform this *mitzvah*, since they are underage.

### Rav Pappa: Two Readings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר: אֵימַר צְרָרֵי אַתְפְּסֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** also **said** that the orphans do not have to repay the guarantor until reaching majority, but for a different reason: **Say** that perhaps the deceased **gave bundles** of money **to** his creditor before his death, and therefore some or all of the debt has been paid. As long as they are minors, the heirs would not be aware of this payment, but upon reaching majority there is a chance that they may examine their father's papers and discover that their father had done this. Therefore, payment is deferred until they reach majority.

This ruling admits of two fundamentally different readings among the *Rishonim*, and the difference between them is substantial. The *Rashbam* reads *Rav Pappa* simply: the orphans owe the guarantor reimbursement, but because they are minors they cannot yet be brought to court to pay. The obligation exists; collection must wait until they come of age.

The *Rambam* and other *Rishonim* read the ruling very differently and more interestingly. On their reading, the problem is not merely procedural delay — it is that the debt to the creditor was not yet *actionable* at the time the guarantor paid. Minors cannot be compelled to pay the father's debts. Therefore, when the guarantor stepped in and paid the creditor, he was paying a debt that, at that moment, could not legally be demanded. And this matters: the right of a guarantor to seek reimbursement from the borrower is not a freestanding legal claim — it is embedded in the institution of *areivut* itself, and it is operative only when the guarantor acts *in the capacity of a guarantor*, discharging an obligation that was genuinely due. If the guarantor pays something that was not yet obligatory, he is not acting as a guarantor; he is simply doing a favor. And a person who voluntarily pays another's debt out of generosity has no legal claim for reimbursement. On this reading, the orphans owe the guarantor nothing — not merely not yet, but not at all.

The phrase *mikamei de-odienhu le-yatmi* — that he paid before informing the orphans — is accordingly of limited significance on either reading. Had he informed them, they might have chosen to pay voluntarily, but this would not change the legal analysis. The debt was either not yet actionable (second reading) or the orphans were simply minors who could not yet be brought to court (first reading).

### Rav Huna: Tzrari Issue

*Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehoshua* offers a different reason entirely why the guarantor cannot collect from the orphans: *eimar tzerarei atfasei* — perhaps the father had already entrusted bound-up money to the creditor. The concern of *tzrari* is that a debtor may have already prepaid his loan in some form — perhaps he handed the creditor a valuable object with the understanding that it would cover the debt if other payment was not forthcoming — and we simply do not know. Since we cannot be certain the debt remained outstanding, and since we cannot trust the orphans' ability to investigate and contest their father's old business dealings, we do not allow the guarantor to collect from them.

This concern differs from the question of minority. One might ask: if the concern is simply that the orphans are minors, they will eventually grow up — and surely once they are adults, the *tzerarei* issue would be just as murky as it is now. If anything, the *tzerarei* concern seems equally problematic regardless of age. Accordingly, *Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehoshua*'s rationale is not primarily about the age of the orphans at all — it is about the uncertain status of the debt itself. On the *Rashbam*'s reading of *Rav Pappa*, we also reach a similar conclusion: the concern over *tzerarei* means we cannot be confident the loan needed to be paid, so the guarantor who paid it cannot necessarily claim reimbursement. But the upshot differs: *Rav Pappa*'s version leads to a wait-until-they're-adults conclusion; *Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehoshua*'s version points toward a more fundamental inability to collect at all if the debt's status is genuinely in doubt.

### When Can Collect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** the opinion of Rav Pappa and that of Rav Huna, son of Yehoshua, as both agree that the orphans are exempt from repaying the debt until they reach majority?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ כְּשֶׁחַיָּיב מוֹדֶה. אִי נָמֵי, דְּשַׁמְּתוּהוּ וּמִת בְּשַׁמְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The difference **between them** is in a case **where the one who owed** the money, i.e., the deceased, **admits** explicitly just before his death that he did not yet repay any of his loan. **Alternatively,** there is a difference between them in a case **where** the court **excommunicated** the deceased for not cooperating with the court in repaying his debt, **and he died** while still **in** a state of **excommunication,** which demonstrates that the debt had not been repaid prior to his death. In both of these cases Rav Pappa would exempt the minor orphans from payment, as minors are not obligated to perform mitzvot, while Rav Huna, son of Yehoshua, would require them to repay the debt immediately, as there is no possibility that their father had repaid the debt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם: שַׁמְּתוּהוּ וּמִת בְּשַׁמְתֵּיהּ, הִלְכְתָא כְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages of Eretz Yisrael **sent** a ruling **from there** to Babylonia: In a case where the court had **excommunicated** a debtor for not cooperating in repaying his debt, **and** the debtor **died** while still **in** a state of **excommunication, the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** and the orphans must repay the debt immediately.

The *Gemara* identifies cases where the guarantor *can* collect from the orphans: *k'she-chayav modeh* — when the borrower, while alive, explicitly admitted that he still owed the money and had not prepaid — and *de-shamtuh u-meis be-shamta* — when the borrower was placed in *niddui* for failing to pay his debts and refusing repeated summonses to *beit din*, and he died while still under that *niddui*. In the latter scenario, the very fact of the *niddui* makes it evident he had not satisfied the loan: had he paid, he would have sought to have the *niddui* lifted. In both of these scenarios, the concern of *tzerarei* evaporates — we have certainty that the loan remained outstanding. Therefore, the guarantor who paid it was genuinely acting in his capacity as *areiv*, and the *Gemara* rules — citing a ruling sent from *Eretz Yisrael* — *hilchasa ke-Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehoshua*: the law follows *Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehoshua*, and in these cases the guarantor may collect from the orphans even if they are minors.

*Tosafot* raises a sharp structural point in this connection. If the orphans are minors and the guarantor is proceeding against their property, one might object: even if we treat the property as the borrower's *areiv*, don't we have to first try to engage the borrower — i.e., the orphans — directly before going to their property? *Tosafot* answers: the rule that you must try the borrower before going to the *areiv* presupposes a *loveh* who is available and capable of responding. Just as a creditor need not wait years for a borrower who has fled to France to return before approaching the guarantor, so too the creditor — or in this case the *areiv* seeking reimbursement — need not wait until the minors come of age before proceeding against their property. The inability to engage them now is functionally equivalent to the borrower having no available resources: the guarantor is entitled to proceed against the property immediately.

### When Cannot Collect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: עָרֵב שֶׁהָיָה שְׁטַר חוֹב יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ – אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה. וְאִם כָּתוּב בּוֹ: ״הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי מִמְּךָ״ – גּוֹבֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 11:15): **A guarantor** for a debt **who has the promissory note** for that debt **in his possession,** indicating that he has repaid the debt, and now seeks reimbursement from the debtor, **cannot collect** if the debtor has died and the obligation for the debt has fallen to his minor heirs. **But if it is written** by the creditor **in** the promissory note: **I received** repayment **from you,** the guarantor, then the guarantor **can collect** the debt from the minor heirs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ – מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ כְּשֶׁחַיָּיב מוֹדֶה, אֶלָּא לְרַב פָּפָּא – קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, you find** a case where the minor heirs would be required to repay the debt, such as **when the one who owed** the money, i.e., the deceased, **admits** explicitly just before his death that he did not yet repay the loan. **But according to** the opinion of **Rav Pappa,** who maintains that minor heirs never have to repay their father's debts, it is **difficult.** What is the case discussed in the *baraita*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, לְהָכִי טְרַח וּכְתַב לֵיהּ ״הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **different there,** as it is **for this** reason that the creditor **troubled himself and wrote for** the guarantor: **I received** repayment from you. Once the creditor writes this in the promissory note, it attains the status of a promissory note of the guarantor held directly against the debtor, and even Rav Pappa agrees that a debt that is recorded in a promissory note can be collected from the deceased's property immediately, even from the minor heirs.

The *Gemara* then confronts a *baraita* that introduces a complicating element: *arev she-hayah shtar chov yotzei mi-tachas yado*, if the guarantor paid the creditor and received the original *shtar chov* as proof of payment, *eino goveh* — he cannot collect from the orphans. This is consistent with what we have established: the loan of minors was not yet actionable and the guarantor cannot collect. But the *baraita* continues: *v'im kasav bo hikbalti*, if the shtar contained a statement of *hikbalti* — "I have received payment" — then *hikbalti mimcha goveh*, the guarantor can collect.

The *Gemara* works through how this fits each position. For *Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehoshua*, whose concern is *tzerarei*, there is a path: when the father admitted the debt (*chayav modeh*), the guarantor may collect even from minor orphans — though the *Gemara* acknowledges this does not fully explain why *hikbalti* specifically is required. The harder problem is for *Rav Pappa*: if the issue is that minors cannot be compelled to fulfill a *mitzvah*, how does the addition of *hikbalti* in the document change anything?

The *Gemara* answers: *shani hasam de-itarchi v-kasav lei hikbalti* — the creditor went to the effort of writing *hikbalti* precisely in order to enable the guarantor to collect from the orphans. The *Rashbam* explains: it is possible to collect on a *shtar* from minor orphans, just not on an oral debt. By writing *hikbalti* in the document and handing it to the guarantor, the creditor effectively transferred the documentary claim — creating for the guarantor a *shtar* against the orphans' estate that can be enforced even against minors. *Tosafot* finds this unsatisfying: if the fundamental problem is the orphans' minority, why should a *shtar* versus an oral claim make a difference? And what right does the creditor have to unilaterally confer collection rights against the orphans simply by writing a word in his document? *Tosafot* therefore reads the passage differently — that *hikbalti* was written not by the creditor but by the borrower himself before he died, in some form of pre-authorization empowering the guarantor to collect even in his absence. This reading is complex and the *Rishonim* acknowledge the difficulty.

## Non-Jews & Conclusion

### Non-Jew Creditor Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא עָרְבָא דְּגוֹי דְּפַרְעֵיהּ לְגוֹי מִקַּמֵּי דְּלִתְבְּעִינְהוּ לְיַתְמֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי אָמַר אֲבִימִי מֵהַגְרוֹנְיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חָיְישִׁינַן לִצְרָרֵי – הָנֵי מִילֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל; אֲבָל גּוֹי, כֵּיוָן דְּבָתַר עָרְבָא אָזֵיל – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן לִצְרָרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain guarantor** who had guaranteed a loan given **by a gentile, who repaid the gentile** creditor **before** the gentile creditor **claimed** repayment **from the orphans** who survived the debtor. The guarantor now sought reimbursement from the orphans. **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: This is** what **Avimi of Hagronya said in the name of Rava: Even according to the one who says** that **we are concerned for** the possibility that the deceased may have given **bundles** of money to the creditor before his death, **this statement** applies only in the case of **a Jewish** creditor. **But** in the case of **a gentile** creditor, **since** according to gentile law he is entitled to **go** directly **to a guarantor, we are not concerned for** the possibility that the deceased may have given **bundles** of money. The debtor would not repay the gentile before the loan is due, as the latter has the right to collect directly from the guarantor, and would thereby receive double payment.

The final section applies the *tzerarei* analysis to a case involving a non-Jewish creditor. A guarantor stood behind a Jewish borrower's loan to a non-Jew; the father died, leaving orphans. The guarantor paid off the non-Jewish creditor before proceeding against the orphans. Now — can he collect from the orphans?

### Rav Mordechai: No Tzrari

*Rav Mordechai* reports to *Rav Ashi* in the name of *Rav*: even according to those who would normally be concerned about *tzerarei* — that the father may have prepaid the loan — in the case of a non-Jewish creditor, *lo chashninan le-tzerarei*, we are not concerned. The reason: *kivan de-basar areva azil* — non-Jewish law allows the creditor to go straight to the guarantor. The father knew this. Would the father be foolish enough to prepay a loan to a non-Jew, knowing that the non-Jew would in any case go straight to the guarantor? Of course not. The dynamics of non-Jewish lending practice make the *tzerarei* concern inapplicable, because a rational borrower would have no incentive to prepay a non-Jewish creditor when the burden of collection would fall on the guarantor anyway. Therefore, the guarantor may collect from the orphans.

### Objection: Still Concern…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְּרַבָּה! אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לָא חָיְישִׁינַן לִצְרָרֵי – הָנֵי מִילֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל; אֲבָל גּוֹיִם, כֵּיוָן דְּדִינַיְיהוּ בָּתַר עָרְבָא אָזְלִי, אִי לָאו דְּאַתְפְּסֵיהּ צְרָרֵי מֵעִיקָּרָא – לָא הֲוָה מְקַבֵּל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to** Rav Mordekhai: **On the contrary, even according to the one who says** that **we are not concerned for** the possibility that the deceased may have given **bundles** of money, **this statement** applies only in the case of **a Jewish** creditor. **But** in the case of **a gentile** creditor, **since** according to gentile **law they** are entitled to **go** directly **to a guarantor, no** guarantor **would accept** upon himself to guarantee such a loan **if the debtor had not given bundles** of money as collateral to the gentile creditor **from the outset.**

The *Gemara* rejects this reasoning decisively, and the counterargument is striking precisely because it flips the logic. *Adaraba* — on the contrary. Even according to those who would not normally be concerned about *tzerarei* with a Jewish creditor, we *are* concerned when the creditor is a non-Jew. Why? *Kivan de-danei basar areva azlei* — since non-Jewish law goes straight to the guarantor, no borrower would agree to have a guarantor for a non-Jewish creditor *unless* he had first given the creditor some form of collateral or prepayment — *tzerarei* — to protect the guarantor from immediate pursuit. *Ila de-atvei tzerarei, me-ikara lo hava mikabeli* — had the borrower not given the non-Jewish creditor some bound-up money or security, the guarantor would never have agreed to take on the role in the first place. No one would cosign for a non-Jewish creditor who can proceed directly against the cosigner without first trying the borrower, unless the borrower took steps to shield the guarantor. Therefore, when there is a non-Jewish creditor, we have a heightened concern that *tzerarei* existed — not a diminished one. The guarantor accordingly cannot collect from the orphans.

### Final Halakha & Shtar

The *Gemara* concludes with a comprehensive summary: *ve-hilcheta be-chulhu ke-Rav Pappa u-ke-Rav Huna berei de-Rav Yehoshua* — the law follows both *Rav Pappa* and *Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehoshua* throughout, with three exceptions where the law diverges from *Rav Pappa*. These three exceptions are: the case of a non-Jewish creditor (where, contrary to *Rav Pappa*'s implied position, the guarantor cannot collect because we are in fact concerned about *tzerarei*); the case where the borrower died while in *niddui* (where the guarantor can collect from the orphans even though they are minors, since the debt is unambiguously established); and the case of *chayav modeh*, where the borrower admitted the debt before death (where again the guarantor may collect). In these latter two cases the law does not follow *Rav Pappa*'s position that the minority of the orphans categorically bars collection — when the debt's existence is beyond doubt, collection may proceed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ בִּשְׁטָר – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים. עַל יְדֵי עֵדִים – גּוֹבִין מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who lends** money **to another by means of** a promissory **note can collect** the debt **from liened property** that had been sold to others by the debtor after the loan was granted. One who lends money **by means of witnesses,** without recording the loan in a promissory note, **can collect** the debt only **from unsold property.**

The *Gemara* then concludes with the opening clause of the next *Mishna*: one who lends to his fellow with a *shtar* may collect from *nechasim meshu'abadim* — liened property — while one who lends *al peh*, by oral agreement alone, may collect only from *nechasim bnei chorin*, unencumbered property.
