# Sanhedrin 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Speaking Order: Money vs…

### Intro: Monetary & Capital

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, הַטְּמָאוֹת וְהַטְּהָרוֹת כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב: אֲנָא הֲוַאי בְּמִנְיָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי, וּמִינַּאי דִּידִי הֲווֹ מַתְחֲלִי בְּרֵישָׁא. וְהָא אֲנַן ״מַתְחִילִין מִן הַגָּדוֹל״ תְּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** In cases of **monetary law,** and likewise in **the** cases of **ritual impurity and purity,** the judges commence expressing their opinions from the greatest of the judges. **Rav says: I was among the quorum of judges in the school of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and they would commence from me,** i.e., I was **first** when ascertaining the opinions of the judges. The Gemara questions this statement: **But we learned** in the mishna that the judges **commence** expressing their opinions **from the greatest** of the judges, and Rav was one of the junior judges of that court.

Today's daf is 36. We begin with the *Yerushalmi*, Perek 4, Halacha 7. The *mishnah* there covers *dinei mamonos*, *taharos*, and *tumaos* — topics we have already encountered in the *Bavli*. The rule stated is: *matchilin min ha-gadol* — in monetary matters you begin with the elder of the court — whereas in *dinei nefashos*, capital cases, you begin from the side, from the more junior members. The *mishnah* then states *hakol kasher ladun dinei mamonos* — everyone is eligible to judge monetary cases — but not everyone may judge capital cases; only *Kohanim*, *Leviyim*, and *Yisraelim* whose daughters may marry *Kohanim*, to the exclusion of, for example, a *chalal* or others with lineage disqualifications.

### Lo Ta'aneh: Two Readings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת מַתְחִילִין מִן הַצַּד. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב אַחָה בַּר פָּפָּא: אָמַר קְרָא, ״לֹא תַעֲנֶה עַל רִב״. לֹא תַעֲנֶה עַל רַב.
>
> **Translation:** In cases of **capital law,** the judges **commence** issuing their opinions **from the side** benches, where the least significant judges sit. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Aḥa bar Pappa says: The verse states: "Neither shall you answer in a cause [*al riv*]"** (Exodus 23:2), and the Sages interpret: **Neither shall you answer after** the **Master [*al rav*],** i.e., do not dispute the opinion of the greatest among the judges. Therefore, were the judges to commence issuing their opinions from the greatest of them, and he would say that the accused is liable, no judge would acquit him.

The *Gemara* opens with a dispute about the verse *lo ta'aneh al riv*. The word *riv* is written defectively — without a *yud* — so it can be read as *rav*, "elder." Rabbi reads the verse as a prohibition against responding after an elder has spoken: *she-lo saaneh acharei ha-rav ella kodem la-rav* — you may not answer after the elder; you must speak before him. The implication is that in capital cases, since you cannot respond once the elder has spoken, the junior members must speak first.

Rabbi Yosei ben Chanina reads the same defective spelling in the opposite direction: *she-lo saaneh kodem la-rav* — do not speak up before the elder, *ella le-achar ha-rav* — only afterward. On this reading, the honor afforded to the elder in monetary cases is that he speaks first, and the others follow.

### Honor in Court: Mirror I…

How do we reconcile these two readings into a coherent system? The answer is that they form a mirror image. In monetary cases, the honor owed to the senior member is expressed by allowing him to speak first; the others may then speak afterward, and as *Tosafos* notes, the conversation does not simply end there. In capital cases, however, because the paramount concern is that every voice be heard independently — so that no one is inhibited from disagreeing with a more distinguished colleague — the honor is accorded differently: you are not permitted to respond after the elder has spoken, and therefore the junior members must speak first. The elder, by speaking last, is in fact given a position of greater weight, while simultaneously the system ensures that all other opinions are aired without the chilling effect of deference.

### Rav Abba: 100 Voices Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: שׁוֹנֶה אָדָם לְתַלְמִידוֹ, וְדָן עִמּוֹ בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: A person may teach his student** the relevant material **and** then **judge** cases of **capital law with him,** and this student can participate in the deliberations and serve as one of the judges.

The *Gemara* then cites Rav Abba: *lo ta'aneh afilu achar me'ah* — you may not violate this rule even after a hundred people have already done so. The *Radvaz* explains this most precisely. Imagine that in a monetary case, where the elder should have spoken first, a hundred people have already jumped in and spoken before him. One might reason: I am not actually speaking before the elder — he has already been bypassed by everyone else — so my speaking now hardly adds to the problem. Or consider the reverse in capital cases: the elder spoke first as he should not have, and everyone has already responded in violation of the rule. One might say: at this point I am not responding directly to the elder; I am merely reacting to what the last speaker said. Both rationalizations are rejected. Every person who continues to contribute to the violation — whether by speaking before the elder when they should not, or by responding after the elder when they should not — is responsible for their own infraction, regardless of how many have transgressed before them. The message is that one cannot exempt oneself simply because others have already created the problem.

### Dineinu v. Dineihem Deba…

Rav Chilkiah in the name of Rabbi Simon reports a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish: *dineinu ke-dineihem* — are our procedural rules about the order of speaking comparable to the practices of non-Jewish courts — or not? The one who says they are comparable has no difficulty. But the one who says our rules are distinctly our own must contend with scriptural evidence suggesting that this practice was in fact universal.

### Non-Jewish Precedents

The evidence adduced is twofold. First, *vayomer Yehuda* — Yehuda spoke first among his brothers regarding what to do with Yosef, which was effectively a capital deliberation. Yehuda was not the eldest, and yet he spoke first. Second, *vayomer Memuchan* — among the seven advisors of Achashveirosh, Memuchan was listed last yet spoke first on the question of what to do with Vashti, which had capital dimensions (even if ultimately she was not executed). If the one who holds *ein dineinu ke-dineihem* — that our rules are not like theirs — how does he answer these proofs? He says: *ra'u divrei Yehuda, ra'u divrei Memuchan* — the text records only what Yehuda and Memuchan said not because they literally spoke first, but because their words were the ones that commanded attention. Everyone else may have spoken before them; the Torah and the narrative simply recorded the opinions that proved decisive. That is a perfectly reasonable reading of both texts.

### David & Naval Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מֵהָכָא: ״וַיֹּאמֶר דָּוִד לַאֲנָשָׁיו חִגְרוּ אִישׁ [אֶת] חַרְבּוֹ וַיַּחְגְּרוּ אִישׁ [אֶת] חַרְבּוֹ וַיַּחְגֹּר גַּם דָּוִד אֶת חַרְבּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The source of this practice is **from here:** When David decided to punish Nabal the Carmelite, the verse states: **"And David said to his men: Every man gird his sword. And every man girded his sword, and David also girded his sword"** (I Samuel 25:13). That was a case of capital law, and David, the greatest among them, was last.

The *Gemara* then asks: what is the actual source that *dinei nefashos* begins from the junior side? Shmuel HaZaken taught before Rav Acha: *vayomer David le-anashav chigru ish charbo* — David told his men, each man should gird his sword, *vayachgru ish charbo*, and they all did so, *vayachgor gam David et charbo*, and David too girded his sword, and thereafter they deliberated regarding Naval. The girding of the sword is read here as a metaphor for the process of deliberation — evoking the image of debate as a kind of combat in the *beit midrash*. Crucially, everyone else acted first, and David last; this is the source that in a capital adjudication the more senior figure speaks at the end.

### Psul Mishpacha Cases

Finally, Rav Teima bar Pappayas in the name of Rav Hoshaiah rules: *af be-psul mishpacha matchilin min ha-tzad* — even when the case does not involve a person's physical life but concerns personal status — whether someone is a *mamzer* or some other *psul mishpacha* designation — this too is treated as analogous to a capital matter, and accordingly the more junior members of the court speak first there as well.

## Monetary Cases: Who Qual…

### Hakol Kasherim Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִין לָדוּן דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת. הַכֹּל לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **All are fit to judge** cases of **monetary law.** The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the mishna's employing the expansive term **all? Rav Yehuda says:** It serves **to include a child born from an incestuous or adulterous relationship [*mamzer*]** in the category of those qualified to judge cases of monetary law.

The *mishnah* states *hakol kesherim ladun dinei mamonos* — everyone is eligible to judge monetary cases. The *Gemara* then explores who precisely this includes, beginning with the teaching of Rabbi Yehuda.

### Rav Yehuda: Mamzerim & W…

Two statements of Rabbi Yehuda are placed together. First, even *mamzerim* are eligible to sit in monetary judgment. Second, *ein medakdekin be-yayin nesech* — one is not excessively strict in cases involving *yayin nesech*. The connection between these two statements is not immediately apparent and may simply represent two independent rulings of Rabbi Yehuda that have been compiled together. The *rishonim* explain the *yayin nesech* ruling to mean not that one is careless, but that in borderline situations — for example, where a non-Jew touches wine in a manner that is entirely incidental and contextually insignificant — one need not apply the strictest possible reading. One might also observe, intriguingly, that wine is shortly to be used as a metaphor for the *Sanhedrin* itself, and so there may be an implicit suggestion here that in certain *batei din* contexts one is not maximally restrictive about who may participate.

### Shor Haniskal Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: עֲשָׂרָה דְּבָרִים יֵשׁ בֵּין דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת לְדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, וְכוּלָּן אֵין נוֹהֲגִין בְּשׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל, חוּץ מֵעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says: There are ten ways** in which cases of **monetary law** are **different from** cases of **capital law,** as was taught in the beginning of the chapter, **and none of them is practiced with regard to** a court hearing concerning **an ox that is** to be **stoned,** as it is treated as a case of monetary law, **except for** the requirement that the animal be judged by **twenty-three** judges, like in cases of capital law.

The verse *lo tateh mishpat evyoncha be-rivo* — do not pervert the judgment of the poor person — is read here as: do not vote to condemn a human being by a majority of one; you need a majority of two. *Ei atah matehu* — you may not do so for a human being. *Aval atah matteh be-dino shel shor haniskal* — but for an ox that must be stoned, you may indeed tip the judgment by a majority of one, and in fact all the other rules constructed to protect a possibly innocent human life do not apply.

### Abbahu: Only 23 Required

Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan clarifies: *u-milvad devarim she-bein dinei mamonos le-dinei nefashos* — beyond the one point of distinction that applies to *shor haniskal*, namely the requirement of 23 judges. That is the single borrowing from *dinei nefashos*: we treat the ox as we would treat a person for purposes of the formal *beit din* of 23, because there is a verse that compares the procedure. But every other distinction that exists between capital and monetary cases — the majority required, the order of deliberation, whether judgment may be completed in a single day — does not apply. In fact, the logic runs in reverse: rather than protecting the *shor haniskal* from a guilty verdict, we arguably want to ensure that a dangerous animal is indeed removed from our midst.

### Nine vs. Eleven Differen…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה? הָא תִּשְׁעָה הָווּ! הָא עֲשָׂרָה קָתָנֵי! מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִין, וְעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה – חֲדָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Are there really **ten** ways in which cases of monetary law are different from cases of capital law? **There are** only **nine** differences recorded in the mishna. The Gemara questions this: **But** the mishna **teaches ten** differences, not nine. The Gemara clarifies: Although there appear to be ten, there are in fact nine, **because** the *halakha* that **not all are fit** to judge cases of capital law **and** the *halakha* that **twenty-three** judges are required for cases of capital law **are one.** The reason not all are fit to judge cases of capital law is that the court of twenty-three is derived from the command to Moses: "And they shall bear the burden of the people with you" (Numbers 11:17), which indicates that only those "with you," i.e., similar in lineage to Moses, can serve on that court (see 17a).

The *Gemara* asks: *kama inun* — how many differences are there between *dinei mamonos* and *dinei nefashos* as listed in the *mishnah*? *Anan taninan tish'a* — our *mishnah* lists nine. But Rabbi Chiya teaches eleven.

### Chiya's Additions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִיכָּא אַחֲרִיתִי, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין מוֹשִׁיבִין בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין זָקֵן וְסָרִיס וּמִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּנִים. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מוֹסִיף: אַף אַכְזָרִי. וְחִילּוּפֵיהֶן בְּמֵסִית, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״לֹא תַחְמֹל וְלֹא תְכַסֶּה עָלָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **But there is another** difference, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 7:5): The court **does not seat on the Sanhedrin** a very **old person or one who is castrated or one who has no children,** as those who did not recently raise children may lack compassion. **Rabbi Yehuda adds: Even a cruel person** is not eligible. The Gemara comments: **And the opposite of this** is the *halakha* **with regard to one who entices** others to engage in idol worship, **as the Merciful One states** concerning him: **"Neither shall you spare, neither shall you conceal him"** (Deuteronomy 13:9).

Rabbi Chiya's two additional distinctions are: a *saris* — a eunuch — and anyone who has not had children is eligible for monetary cases but not for capital cases. As Rashi and the Rambam explain, a person who has never raised children may lack the patience, compassion, and tolerance that come with that experience and that are necessary for rendering life-and-death judgments. These are admittedly sweeping generalizations, but they function as protective mechanisms to guard against the possibility of an innocent person being condemned by judges who cannot bring the requisite human understanding to their role.

Rabbi Abbahu then adds in the name of Rabbi Yochanan two further distinctions: someone under twenty years of age, and someone who has not produced two pubic hairs — both of which, while superficially surprising (suggesting even a minor might adjudicate monetary cases), reflect the principle that a certain level of maturity is required for capital adjudication but not for civil matters.

### Two Judgments Rule

Rabbi Yosi ben Chanina adds yet further, reaching a total of thirteen differences. The additional distinction he contributes is: *danim shenei dinei mamonos be-yom echad ve-ein danim dinei nefashos be-yom echad* — you may judge two monetary cases on the same day, but you may not judge two capital cases on the same day. Rabbi Avin adds that even an adulterer and adulteress — *no'ef ve-no'efet* — even though they are implicated in the same incident, may not be judged on the same day, though as the *Bavli* elsewhere notes, this applies specifically when their respective verdicts would differ (*sekilah* in one case, *sereifah* in another depending on the circumstances), and there may be some instances when they could be judged together.

Tracking the count toward thirteen: the *saris* and the childless man are two, the issues of age (under twenty and two pubic hairs) are two more, and the rule about two judgments in one day is an additional distinction beyond the eleven — bringing the total to thirteen.

## Rav's Case & Rabbi's Mod…

### Rav in Rabbi's Court

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי הִלֵּל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי וָולֶס: שָׁאנֵי מִנְיָינָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי, דְּכוּלְּהוּ מִנְיָנַיְיהוּ מִן הַצַּד הֲווֹ מַתְחֲלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba, son of Rava, says, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Hillel, son of Rabbi Valles,** who says: **The counting** of the vote in the court **in the school of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **is different, as all of their** deliberations and the **counting** of the vote **would commence from the side** benches, where the least significant judges sit. This was because Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was held in such high esteem that once he expressed his opinion, no one would be so brazen as to contradict him.

We now turn to the *Bavli*, picking up on daf 36 at roughly the midpoint of the page, directly paralleling what we have seen in the *Yerushalmi*. The *Gemara* introduces a personal anecdote: Rav says, *ana havina be-minyana de-vei Rabbi* — I was present in a vote in Rabbi's *beit din*, in a particular matter involving a *sikrikon* ruling in the area of *Gittin*, which was treated as a monetary case. Despite this being a monetary case — where the elder should speak first — they began with Rav: *u-minai didi havu maschali be-reisha*. Yet the *mishnah* explicitly states that in monetary cases one begins with the greatest. How do we account for this?

### Rav Papa: Rabbi's Humili…

Rav Papa bar Rav Avya — or, as the *Gemara* offers as an alternative attribution, Rav Hillel bar Rabbi Valles — explains: *shani minyana de-Rabbi* — the proceedings of Rabbi's court were different — *de-kulhu mi-menai u-me-hatzad havu maschali* — because Rabbi, in his extraordinary *anavah*, his humility, did not assert his prerogative to speak first even in a monetary case where that honor was rightfully his. He voluntarily yielded his right to the others.

### Torah & Power Combined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי הִלֵּל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי וָולֶס: מִימוֹת מֹשֶׁה וְעַד רַבִּי לֹא מָצִינוּ תּוֹרָה וּגְדוּלָּה בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to the greatness of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **Rabba, son of Rava, says, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Hillel, son of Rabbi Valles,** who says: **From the days of Moses and until** the days of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **we do not find** unparalleled greatness in **Torah** knowledge **and** unparalleled **greatness** in secular matters, including wealth and high political office, combined **in one place,** i.e., in a single individual.

Since the humility of Rabbi has come up, Rav Papa digresses to speak also of Rabbi's greatness. He states: *mi-yemot Moshe ve-ad Rabbi lo matzinu Torah u-gedulah be-makom echad* — from the time of Moshe Rabbeinu until Rabbi, we never found Torah greatness and political greatness — the supreme authority in both domains — unified in a single person. Moshe was the political leader of the nation and also its greatest Torah figure; Rabbi similarly was the *nasi* and the foremost Torah authority of his generation.

### Competing Authorities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? הָא הֲוָה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ! הֲוָה אֶלְעָזָר. וְהָא הֲוָה פִּנְחָס! הֲווֹ זְקֵנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** was there **not** such a person? **Wasn't there Joshua,** who was unparalleled in both domains? The Gemara answers: During his time **there was Elazar,** who was Joshua's equal in Torah knowledge. The Gemara asks: **But wasn't there Pinehas,** who outlived Elazar? The Gemara answers: **There were the Elders,** who were equal to Pinehas in Torah knowledge.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this with a series of counterexamples. What about Yehoshua? He was both political leader and a great Torah figure. The answer: *havai Elazar* — Elazar was also present, a competing Torah authority, so Yehoshua was not the sole supreme figure in both domains simultaneously.

What about Pinchas, after Yehoshua? *Havu zekenim* — the elders as a collective were there; he was not the exclusive authority.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הֲוָה שָׁאוּל! הֲוָה שְׁמוּאֵל. וְהָא נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ! כּוּלְּהוּ שְׁנֵיהּ קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further objects: **But wasn't there Saul,** who was unparalleled in both domains? The Gemara answers: **There was Samuel,** who was Saul's equal in Torah knowledge. The Gemara asks: **But didn't** Samuel **die** in Saul's lifetime, leaving Saul the leading figure in both domains? The Gemara answers: **We** meant to **say** that from the days of Moses until the days of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi there was no other single individual who reigned supreme in Torah and greatness for **all the years** that he was the leader of the Jewish people.

What about King Shaul? *Havai Shmuel* — Shmuel was a competing religious authority throughout at least part of Shaul's reign. But even when Shmuel died, the *Gemara* notes: *kulhu shenei ka'amarinan* — we are speaking of the entire span of someone's career. Even for a period where Shaul might have been the sole authority, Shmuel had been a competing figure during other years of Shaul's reign. The standard is that the person must have been the unchallenged supreme authority in both domains for their entire career.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הֲוָה דָּוִד! הֲוָה עִירָא הַיָּאִירִי. וְהָא נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ! כּוּלְּהוּ שְׁנֵיהּ קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't there David,** who was both the greatest Torah authority and the most powerful temporal authority of his day? The Gemara answers: **There was Ira the Yairite,** who was David's equal in Torah knowledge. The Gemara objects: **But didn't** Ira the Yairite **die** in David's lifetime? The Gemara answers: **We** meant to **say** that there was no other single individual who reigned supreme in Torah and greatness for **all the years** that he was the leader of the Jewish people.

What about David? *Havai Ira HaYairi*, who is viewed in the rabbinic tradition as a competing Torah authority — and when Ira died, the rule again is that we need this unity to have prevailed *kulhu shenei*, throughout the entire career. What about Solomon? *Havai Shimi ben Gera*, also a great Torah scholar — though Solomon had him killed. Still, the *Gemara* applies the same principle: *kulhu shenei kayamin* — while Shimi was alive, there was a competing figure, and that disqualifies the period.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הֲוָה שְׁלֹמֹה! הֲוָה שִׁמְעִי בֶּן גֵּרָא. וְהָא קַטְלֵיהּ! כּוּלֵּיהּ שְׁנֵיהּ קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't there Solomon,** who was unparalleled in both domains? The Gemara answers: During his day **there was Shimi ben Gera,** who was Solomon's master in Torah knowledge. The Gemara objects: **But didn't** Solomon **kill him** at the beginning of his reign (see I Kings, chapter 2)? The Gemara answers: **We** meant to **say** that there was no other single individual who reigned supreme in Torah and greatness for **all the years** that he was the leader of the Jewish people.

What about Chizkiah? *Havai Shevna*, a great Torah authority — who was subsequently killed. Same analysis applies. What about Ezra? *Havai Nechemiah ben Chakalyah*, again a competing political and Torah figure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא הֲוָה חִזְקִיָּה! הֲוָה שֶׁבְנָא. וְהָא אִיקְּטִיל! כּוּלְּהוּ שְׁנֵיהּ קָאָמְרִינַן. וְהָא הֲוָה עֶזְרָא! הֲוָה נְחֶמְיָה בֶּן חֲכַלְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further objects: **Wasn't there Hezekiah,** who was both the leading Torah scholar of his age and also the king of his people? The Gemara answers: **There was Shebnah** in that generation, who was Hezekiah's equal in Torah knowledge. The Gemara asks: **But wasn't he killed** in the war against Sennacherib? The Gemara answers: **We** meant to **say** that there was no other single individual who reigned supreme in Torah and greatness for **all the years** that he was the leader of the Jewish people. The Gemara asks: **But wasn't there Ezra,** who was the greatest Torah Sage of his day and the leader of the Jewish people? The Gemara answers: **There was Nehemiah, son of Hacaliah,** who was his equal.

### Adda Bar Ahava: Rabbi to…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: אַף אֲנִי אוֹמֵר, מִימוֹת רַבִּי עַד רַב אָשֵׁי לֹא מָצִינוּ תּוֹרָה וּגְדוּלָּה בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד. וְלָא? וְהָא הֲוָה הוּנָא בַּר נָתָן! הוּנָא בַּר נָתָן מִיכָּף הֲוָה כַּיִיף לֵיהּ לְרַב אָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava says: I also say** a similar statement, that **from the days of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and until** the days of **Rav Ashi we do not find** unparalleled greatness in **Torah** knowledge **and** unparalleled **greatness** in secular matters, including wealth and high political office, combined **in one place,** i.e., in a single individual. The Gemara asks: **But** was there **not** such a person? **But wasn't there Huna bar Natan,** who lived during the time of Rav Ashi and enjoyed both great Torah scholarship and great wealth? The Gemara answers: **Huna bar Natan was subordinate to Rav Ashi,** who was his superior in both domains.

Adda bar Ahava then offers a parallel observation: *mi-yemot Rabbi ve-ad Rav Ashi lo matzinu Torah u-gedulah be-makom echad* — from the time of Rabbi until Rav Ashi, we do not find Torah greatness and political greatness combined in one person. Rav Ashi himself apparently held both. The *Gemara* challenges: was there not Huna bar Natan, who was very close to the royal court and thus wielded political influence? As *Tosafos* notes, Huna bar Natan had significant proximity to power. The *Gemara* responds: *Huna bar Natan mikaf hava kayaf le-Rav Ashi* — he submitted to and deferred to the authority of Rav Ashi. He was therefore not the supreme authority in the Torah domain; that remained with Rav Ashi exclusively.

## Lo Ta'aneh Source & Logic

### Lo Ta'aneh Verse Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב אַחָה בַּר פָּפָּא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״לֹא תַטֶּה מִשְׁפַּט אֶבְיֹנְךָ בְּרִיבוֹ״. מִשְׁפַּט אֶבְיוֹנְךָ אִי אַתָּה מַטֶּה, אֲבָל אַתָּה מַטֶּה מִשְׁפָּט שֶׁל שׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Aḥa bar Pappa says: As the verse states: "You shall not incline the judgment of your poor in his cause"** (Exodus 23:6). He explains: **You may not incline the judgment of,** i.e., exert effort to find liable, **your poor, but you may incline the judgment of an ox that is** to be **stoned.** The reason for the procedural differences between cases of monetary law and cases of capital law is to render it more likely that one accused of a capital transgression will be acquitted. This is not a factor when judging the ox.

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Bavli*'s own presentation of the source for the procedural rules of capital and monetary cases. The *mishnah* states: in capital cases one begins from the junior side. Where does this come from? Rabbi Acha bar Papa cites the verse: *lo tateh mishpat evyoncha be-rivo* — do not pervert the judgment of the poor person in his dispute — and reads *rivo* as *rabo*, his elder. Do not respond after the elder has spoken.

### Monetary: Respond or Not?

This immediately raises a question: does the same prohibition apply in monetary cases? If it does, then the monetary *beit din* effectively becomes a court of one, because once the elder speaks, no one may respond. And yet we know that in monetary cases you do afford honor by letting the elder speak first — implying that others do speak afterward. How can both things be true?

### Tosafot's Two Solutions

*Tosafos* offers two answers. The first: in monetary cases one may respond, but only in the form of a question — not by stating outright "I disagree," but by asking "might it not be the case that...?" This approach would still substantially hamper free debate, which makes it somewhat unsatisfying. The second answer of *Tosafos* is that the prohibition against responding altogether is confined to capital cases and does not apply to monetary cases at all. The precise reasoning for this limitation is not fully spelled out, but the functional result is the mirror image structure that we described.

### Yerushalmi Alternative

The *Yerushalmi* offers a cleaner resolution. On its reading, *lo ta'aneh* does not mean "do not respond after the elder"; it means "do not speak up before the elder" — that is, afford him the honor of speaking first, and thereafter others may freely offer their opinions. This interpretation preserves the full integrity of monetary-case deliberation without any chilling effect on subsequent speakers.

### Mirror: Respect Mechanis…

The mirror image is then fully explained. In monetary cases, the elder speaks first — that is the honor afforded to him — and the junior members may respond and share their views freely thereafter. In capital cases, the concern for the accused's innocence demands that even after the elder speaks, no one feel inhibited from disagreeing. The way the system ensures this is by having the elder speak last. And the way the elder's honor is maintained is through the rule that one may not respond once he has spoken — his word carries finality. By speaking last and having his word stand, the elder retains his dignity, while the process of reaching that point has been an entirely open deliberation. The two systems are thus symmetrical expressions of the same underlying respect for both honor and justice.

## Teacher-Student Voting

### Rabbah: Student Negates…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּי קָאָמַר רַב, כְּגוֹן רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי, דְּלִגְמָרֵיהּ דְּרַב הֲווֹ צְרִיכִי, וְלִסְבָרֵיהּ דְּרַב לָא הֲווֹ צְרִיכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When Rav says** his statement, he is referring to not every student, but only those **such as Rav Kahana and Rav Asi, who needed** to learn **the** halakhic **traditions of Rav, but they did not need** to learn **the reasoning of Rav,** as they were capable of conducting their own analysis.

The *Gemara* cites Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, deriving from the same verse about David and his men girding their swords — understood as a metaphor for deliberation before the judgment of Naval — that *shoneh adam le-talmido ve-dan imo be-dinei nefashos*: a person may teach his student and the student may then count as a distinct vote alongside his teacher in a capital case. Rashi explains this explicitly as meaning the student is counted as a separate vote. This is in itself a striking statement, because ordinarily one cannot simply add a person to the *Sanhedrin* ad hoc; the student would have to be a duly constituted member. But beyond the question of how the student gets to vote at all, the *Gemara*'s focus is on a different and more subtle question: why does the student's vote count as a *separate* vote, and not merely as a second expression of his teacher's view?

### Challenge: Purity Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַטְּהָרוֹת וְהַטְּמָאוֹת, הָאָב וּבְנוֹ, הָרַב וְתַלְמִידוֹ – מוֹנִין לָהֶם שְׁנַיִם. דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת וְדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת וְדִינֵי מַכּוֹת, קִידּוּשׁ הַחֹדֶשׁ וְעִיבּוּר שָׁנָה – אָב וּבְנוֹ, הָרַב וְתַלְמִידוֹ, אֵין מוֹנִין לָהֶן אֶלָּא אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 7:2): In cases of **ritual purity and impurity,** if two of the judges are **a father and his son,** or **a teacher and his student,** the court **counts them** as **two** opinions. By inference, in cases of **monetary law and** cases of **capital law, and** cases of **laws** involving the punishment **of lashes,** as well as court proceedings concerning **sanctification of the month and the intercalation of the year,** if two of the judges are **a father and his son,** or **a teacher and his student,** the court **counts them** as **only one** opinion, as it is assumed the son or student will merely echo the opinion of his father or teacher. This contradicts the ruling of Rav.

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from *halakhic* sources dealing with *taharos* and *tumaos* — matters of purity and impurity, kashrut questions — where, since a single rabbi can render the ruling without a formal *beit din*, the rules of counting are much more relaxed. In those contexts, a father and son, or a rabbi and student, may indeed be counted as two, because there is no rigorous requirement of a distinct formal vote from each member.

### Formal Cases: One Count

However, the source continues: *aval dinei mamonos u-dinei nefashos u-dinei makos kiddush ha-chodesh ve-ibbur ha-shanah* — but in monetary cases, capital cases, lashes, sanctification of the new month, and intercalation of the year — *av u-veno ha-rav ve-talmido ein monin lahem ela echad* — a father and son, or a rabbi and student, count only as one, because one naturally assumes the student is simply following his teacher's lead. Unless, of course, they actually voted differently — in which case they plainly are distinct voters — but when they agree, the default assumption is that the student's agreement is not independent.

One might ask: how can a father and son both be on a *beit din* at all, when relatives are ordinarily disqualified from sitting together? Rashi resolves this by saying precisely this rule is the operative disqualification — they can never count as two — and effectively they do not both sit as full independent members. *Tosafos* suggests the case may involve *kiddush ha-chodesh*, for which relatives may be eligible. The father-son question can be set aside; the core issue is the rabbi-student relationship.

### Papa: When Independent?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּי קָאָמַר רַב, כְּגוֹן רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי, דְּלִגְמָרֵיהּ דְּרַב הֲווֹ צְרִיכִי, וְלִסְבָרֵיהּ דְּרַב לָא הֲווֹ צְרִיכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When Rav says** his statement, he is referring to not every student, but only those **such as Rav Kahana and Rav Asi, who needed** to learn **the** halakhic **traditions of Rav, but they did not need** to learn **the reasoning of Rav,** as they were capable of conducting their own analysis.

The *Gemara* on 36b resolves the tension by drawing a crucial distinction. When did Rav say that a rabbi and student count as a separate vote? Only in a case where the student possesses genuine intellectual independence from his teacher.

### Kahana & Ashi Model

The paradigm offered is Rav Kahana and Rav Ashi in relation to Rav. They needed Rav's *gemara* — his transmitted traditions and teachings — because in that era, without written texts, all received knowledge had to be obtained directly from one's teacher. They depended on their teacher for the *material*. But they did not need their teacher's *analysis*: *li-svarei de-Rav lo havu tzerikhi* — they worked through the reasoning independently. If a student truly brings his own analytical capacity and can reach conclusions without being guided by his teacher's reasoning, then his vote is genuinely distinct and counts separately.

This point has interesting resonances beyond the specific *Sanhedrin* context. In determining how many *poskim* hold a particular position, one must ask whether a series of later authorities — *Tosafos*, the *Rosh*, the *Mordechai*, the *Terumat HaDeshen* — are truly independent voices or whether they are all flowing from the same source, in which case they may represent, in some sense, a single opinion that has been amplified rather than independently corroborated. Genuine independence of analysis is what creates a genuinely separate vote.

## 10 Differences: Mamo (II)

### Abbahu: Ten Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: עֲשָׂרָה דְּבָרִים יֵשׁ בֵּין דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת לְדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, וְכוּלָּן אֵין נוֹהֲגִין בְּשׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל, חוּץ מֵעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says: There are ten ways** in which cases of **monetary law** are **different from** cases of **capital law,** as was taught in the beginning of the chapter, **and none of them is practiced with regard to** a court hearing concerning **an ox that is** to be **stoned,** as it is treated as a case of monetary law, **except for** the requirement that the animal be judged by **twenty-three** judges, like in cases of capital law.

Rabbi Abbahu states: there are ten differences between *dinei mamonos* and *dinei nefashos* enumerated in our *mishnah*, and all of them do not apply to a *shor haniskal*. The *shor haniskal* requires a *beit din* of 23 because we formally liken its execution to that of a person, but every other protective rule that makes *dinei nefashos* distinct from *dinei mamonos* — the super-majority requirement, the order of deliberation, the prohibition on completing judgment the same day — none of those apply. Quite the opposite: the goal in the case of a *shor haniskal* is to effectively remove a potentially dangerous animal from society, not to protect it from conviction.

### Challenge: Only Nine?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה? הָא תִּשְׁעָה הָווּ! הָא עֲשָׂרָה קָתָנֵי! מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִין, וְעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה – חֲדָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Are there really **ten** ways in which cases of monetary law are different from cases of capital law? **There are** only **nine** differences recorded in the mishna. The Gemara questions this: **But** the mishna **teaches ten** differences, not nine. The Gemara clarifies: Although there appear to be ten, there are in fact nine, **because** the *halakha* that **not all are fit** to judge cases of capital law **and** the *halakha* that **twenty-three** judges are required for cases of capital law **are one.** The reason not all are fit to judge cases of capital law is that the court of twenty-three is derived from the command to Moses: "And they shall bear the burden of the people with you" (Numbers 11:17), which indicates that only those "with you," i.e., similar in lineage to Moses, can serve on that court (see 17a).

The *Gemara* immediately challenges Rabbi Abbahu: *ha tish'a havyan* — there are only nine in our *mishnah*, not ten. This is a striking moment because the *Yerushalmi* similarly counted nine.

### Resolution: Criteria Uni…

The *Gemara* then responds with an equally pointed counter-challenge: *ha asura havyan* — there are actually ten! The dispute is about how to count. The *Gemara* resolves it by explaining: *mishum de-ein hakol kesherin le-esrim u-shelosha khada hi* — the criterion of who may judge and the requirement of 23 are a single unified point, not two separate distinctions. Once you establish that this is a *Sanhedrin* of 23, the eligibility requirements for who may serve on it are implicitly determined. They are two sides of the same coin, and so they count as one difference, not two. Once we recalibrate and treat that as nine, Rabbi Abbahu's claim of ten requires identification of a tenth difference.

### Zaken & Saris Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִיכָּא אַחֲרִיתִי, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין מוֹשִׁיבִין בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין זָקֵן וְסָרִיס וּמִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּנִים. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מוֹסִיף: אַף אַכְזָרִי. וְחִילּוּפֵיהֶן בְּמֵסִית, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״לֹא תַחְמֹל וְלֹא תְכַסֶּה עָלָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **But there is another** difference, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 7:5): The court **does not seat on the Sanhedrin** a very **old person or one who is castrated or one who has no children,** as those who did not recently raise children may lack compassion. **Rabbi Yehuda adds: Even a cruel person** is not eligible. The Gemara comments: **And the opposite of this** is the *halakha* **with regard to one who entices** others to engage in idol worship, **as the Merciful One states** concerning him: **"Neither shall you spare, neither shall you conceal him"** (Deuteronomy 13:9).

The *Gemara* supplies it: *ain moshivin be-Sanhedrin zaken ve-saris* — one does not seat on the *Sanhedrin* an elderly person or a *saris*. Rashi explains that someone so advanced in age has forgotten, *she-shakhakh kevar tza'ar giddul banim* — the experience and pain of raising children — and may lack the emotional sensitivity required. A *saris*, and similarly anyone who has never had children, has not undergone those formative experiences. As we noted earlier, these are sweeping generalizations, but they function as protective rules to reduce the risk of unjust convictions in capital matters.

### Yehuda: Cruelty Criteria

Rav Yehuda adds that the cruel — *achzarim* — are also excluded from the *Sanhedrin*. This is the exclusion one would most intuitively want, though it is also the hardest to quantify. And there is an interesting inversion: *ve-chilufeihen be-meisis* — the opposite rule applies in the case of a *meisis*, one who seduces others to idolatry. There, precisely, you would want the stern and unyielding judges, because the Torah explicitly commands *lo tachmol ve-lo techakeh alav* — do not have compassion, do not conceal anything in his favor. The *Sanhedrin* for that case is intended to achieve conviction, not to protect the accused, and the composition of the court reflects that goal.

## Money: Mamzer & Ger Incl…

### Hakol Kasherim Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִין לָדוּן דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת. הַכֹּל לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **All are fit to judge** cases of **monetary law.** The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the mishna's employing the expansive term **all? Rav Yehuda says:** It serves **to include a child born from an incestuous or adulterous relationship [*mamzer*]** in the category of those qualified to judge cases of monetary law.

The *mishnah* says *hakol kesherim ladun dinei mamonos* — everyone is eligible to judge monetary cases. The *Gemara* asks: *hakol le-asuyei mai* — what does "everyone" come to include beyond what we already know?

### Rav Yehuda: Mamzerim OK

Rav Yehuda answers: *le-asuyei mamzer* — to include a *mamzer*. In capital cases, those who are disqualified from the lineage perspective are anyone who cannot marry into the Jewish community in the standard way — such as a *chalal*. But for monetary cases, the circle is wider still: even a *mamzer*, who cannot marry another Jew at all, is nonetheless eligible to sit in civil judgment.

### Already Taught Mamzerim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תְּנֵינָא חֲדָא זִימְנָא: כׇּל הָרָאוּי לָדוּן דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת רָאוּי לָדוּן דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, וְיֵשׁ רָאוּי לָדוּן דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת וְאֵין רָאוּי לָדוּן דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַמְזֵר. חֲדָא לְאֵתוֹיֵי גֵּר, וַחֲדָא לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **But we** already **learn** this *halakha* **one time,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: **All who are fit to judge** cases of **capital law are fit to judge** cases of **monetary law, but there are those who are fit to judge** cases of **monetary law and are not fit to judge** cases of **capital law. And we discussed it: What is included** in the expansive term all employed by the *baraita*? **And Rav Yehuda says:** It serves **to include a *mamzer*.** The Gemara responds: **One** of the two sources serves **to include a convert,** who is qualified to judge only in cases of monetary law, **and one** of the two sources serves **to include a *mamzer*.**

The *Gemara* objects: *ha tanyina chada zimna* — we have already taught this. An earlier *mishnah* states that anyone fit to judge capital cases is fit to judge monetary cases, and there are those fit for monetary but not capital — and Rav Yehuda himself already explained that this category includes a *mamzer*. So what is Rav Yehuda adding by saying it again?

### Ger vs. Mamzer Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גֵּר – דְּרָאוּי לָבֹא בַּקָּהָל, אֲבָל מַמְזֵר אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מַמְזֵר – דְּבָא מִטִּיפָּה כְּשֵׁרָה, אֲבָל גֵּר דְּלֹא בָּא מִטִּיפָּה כְּשֵׁרָה אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** both the mishna and *baraita* are **necessary,** as the *halakha* taught by one source cannot be derived from the *halakha* taught by the other source. **As, if** the *tanna* **taught us** the fitness to judge cases of monetary law only with regard to **a convert,** one could say that a convert is like a born Jew concerning this, **since** he is **fit to enter into the congregation,** i.e., marry a Jew of fit lineage, **but** with regard to **a *mamzer*,** who is not fit to enter into the congregation, **say** that he **cannot** serve as a judge. **And if** the *tanna* **taught us** the fitness to judge cases of monetary law only with regard to **a *mamzer*,** one could say that a *mamzer* is fit to judge, **as he came from seed** of **unflawed** lineage, **but** with regard to **a convert, who does not come from seed** of **unflawed** lineage, **say** that he **cannot** serve as a judge. Therefore, both sources are **necessary.**

The *Gemara* resolves: *chada le-asuyei ger, ve-chada le-asuyei mamzer* — one teaching comes to include a *ger*, a convert, and the other to include a *mamzer*, and *tzericha* — both are necessary. Each teaches a distinct principle that cannot be derived from the other. Had we only been told that a *ger* may judge monetary cases, one might have thought: true, a *ger* can marry a Jew, so his inclusion makes sense — but a *mamzer*, who cannot marry a Jew at all, might be seen as too far outside the community to be included. Conversely, had we only been told about a *mamzer*, one might have reasoned: a *mamzer* comes from Jewish parents, *mi-tipa kesheira*, so he retains a Jewish identity connection — but a *ger*, who does not come from Jewish parentage at all, might be excluded. Each ruling thus does genuine work, and both are needed.

### Ger Jurisdiction Dispute

There is an interesting dispute between Rashi and *Tosafos* on the precise scope of a *ger*'s eligibility. Another *Gemara* states that a *ger* may sit in judgment on a case involving a fellow *ger*. The question is whether the two teachings — the general inclusion of a *ger* in *dinei mamonos*, and the specific allowance to judge a fellow *ger* — are alternatives or cumulative requirements. Rashi reads them as alternatives: a *ger* may judge any monetary case, or may even sit in capital cases when the accused is also a *ger*. *Tosafos* reads them as cumulative and highly restrictive: a *ger* may only sit in monetary cases, and even then only when the case involves another *ger* — which is not at all what the plain reading of this *Gemara* suggests, and which is quite a dramatic limitation.

## 10 Differences: Mamo (II)

### Purity from Song of Songs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר, מַאי קְרָא: ״כֻּלָּךְ יָפָה רַעְיָתִי וּמוּם אֵין בָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar says: What is the verse** from which it is derived? It states: **"You are all fair, my love; and there is no blemish in you"** (Song of Songs 4:7).

The *Gemara* asks: what is the verse source for the requirement that members of the *Sanhedrin* for capital cases be free from lineage blemish? The *Gemara* cites Ameimar: *kulach yafa ra'ayati u-mum ein bach* — you are entirely beautiful, my beloved, there is no blemish in you — drawn from *Shir HaShirim*. This is applied to the *beit din*: as they must be pure in righteousness, so too they must be free from *mum*, understood here as a lineage blemish. The *Gemara* raises the possibility that *mum* here means a physical blemish rather than a lineage one. But that possibility does not survive scrutiny.

### Yitro's V'Nisu Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא הָתָם מִשּׁוּם שְׁכִינָה? אֶלָּא, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אָמַר קְרָא ״וְנָשְׂאוּ אִתָּךְ״ – אִתָּךְ בְּדוֹמִין לָךְ לֶיהֱוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **But perhaps there,** those who were with Moses had to be free of any blemish **due to** the **Divine Presence,** which was going to rest upon them, but this is not a requirement for judges to serve on the Sanhedrin. **Rather, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: The verse states:** "So shall they make it easier for you **and bear the burden with you"** (Exodus 18:22). The term **"with you"** is explained to mean: **They shall be similar to you,** without blemish. This verse is referring to the appointment of regular judges, upon whom the Divine Presence does not rest, and teaches that all members of the Sanhedrin must be whole in body, and the verse from Song of Songs teaches that they must have unflawed lineage as well.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers an alternative derivation: *ve-nasu itach* — from the passage in which Yisro advises Moshe to appoint judges who will carry the burden together with him. *Itach*, "with you," is read as *be-domin lach* — ones who are similar to you. In that context, the similarity is understood to pertain to lineage purity rather than physical characteristics. And crucially, unlike the *Shechina* argument that could have been raised against the *Shir HaShirim* source — perhaps the need to resemble Moshe was specific to the context of divine presence resting on the seventy elders — here in Yisro's practical counsel to Moshe about ordinary judicial appointments, the same standard of *be-domin lach* applies, making it a more generalizable rule.

## Sanhedrin Seating Layout

### Half-Circle Design

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ סַנְהֶדְרִין הָיְתָה כַּחֲצִי גּוֹרֶן עֲגוּלָּה, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהוּ רוֹאִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. וּשְׁנֵי סוֹפְרֵי הַדַּיָּינִין עוֹמְדִים לִפְנֵיהֶם, אֶחָד מִיָּמִין וְאֶחָד מִשְּׂמֹאל, וְכוֹתְבִין דִּבְרֵי מְחַיְּיבִין וְדִבְרֵי מְזַכִּין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשָׁה, אֶחָד כּוֹתֵב דִּבְרֵי הַמְזַכִּין, וְאֶחָד כּוֹתֵב דִּבְרֵי הַמְחַיְּיבִין, וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי כּוֹתֵב דִּבְרֵי הַמְזַכִּין וְדִבְרֵי הַמְחַיְּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A Sanhedrin** of twenty-three **was** arranged in the same layout **as half of a circular threshing floor, in order that** all the judges **will see one another** and the witnesses. **And two judges' scribes stand before** the court, **one on the right and one on the left, and they write the statements of those who find** the accused **liable and the statements of those who acquit** the accused. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** There were **three** scribes. **One writes** only **the statements of those who acquit** the accused, **one writes** only **the statements of those who find** him **liable, and the third writes** both **the statements of those who acquit** the accused **and the statements of those who find** him **liable,** so that if there is uncertainty concerning the precise wording that one of the scribes writes, it can be compared to the words of the third scribe.

The next *mishnah* describes the physical structure of the *Sanhedrin*. *Sanhedrin haysa ke-chatzi agula goren agula* — the *Sanhedrin* sat in the shape of half a threshing floor, which is to say, a half-circle. The threshing floor itself was round; the court replicated half of that form.

### Visibility Purpose

The purpose of this arrangement was *ke-dei she-yiyu ro'im zeh et zeh* — so that the judges could all see one another. This served multiple functions simultaneously: it enabled equal visibility among all the judges, gave each member an equivalent view of the witnesses and the litigants, and created a sense of equality in the deliberative body even while a hierarchy was maintained within it. No judge was behind another or out of sight.

### Two Scribes System

*U-shnei sofrei ha-dayanim omdim lifneihem* — two scribes of the court stood before the judges, one on the right and one on the left at the edges of the half-circle. They would write everything: *divrei ha-mezakkim ve-divrei ha-mechayevim* — the arguments for acquittal and the arguments for conviction — producing duplicate records so that each could serve as verification of the other.

### Yehuda: Three Scribes

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and says there should be three scribes. Writing everything is burdensome, so the labor should be divided: one scribe records only the arguments for acquittal, a second records only the arguments for conviction, and the third — with the most demanding role — writes everything. This third record serves as the master verification: in any case of doubt about what was actually argued, the comprehensive record provides the authoritative check. The workload of the primary recording is thus distributed while the integrity of the full record is maintained.

### Three Student Rows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁלֹשׁ שׁוּרוֹת שֶׁל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים יוֹשְׁבִין לִפְנֵיהֶן, כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מַכִּיר אֶת מְקוֹמוֹ. הוּצְרְכוּ לִסְמוֹךְ – סוֹמְכִין מִן הָרִאשׁוֹנָה. אֶחָד מִן הַשְּׁנִיָּה בָּא לוֹ לָרִאשׁוֹנָה, אֶחָד מִן הַשְּׁלִישִׁית בָּא לוֹ לַשְּׁנִיָּה. בּוֹרְרִים לָהֶן עוֹד אֶחָד מִן הַקָּהָל וּמוֹשִׁיבִין אוֹתוֹ בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית. וְלֹא הָיָה יוֹשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן, אֶלָּא יוֹשֵׁב בְּמָקוֹם הָרָאוּי לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And three rows of Torah scholars sit before** the judges, and **each and every one** among those sitting **recognizes his place,** i.e., they are seated in accordance with their stature. When the court **must ordain** an additional judge, e.g., if a judge dies during the proceedings or in the case of a court without a decisive majority (see 40a), the court **ordains** the greatest Torah scholar **from the first** row. As a seat in the first row is now vacant, **one** Torah scholar **from the second** row **comes to the first** row, and **one** Torah scholar **from the third** row **comes to the second** row, and the court **selects another** Torah scholar **from among the assembled and they seat him in the third** row. **And** this Torah scholar who moves from the second row to the first row **would not sit in the place of the first** Torah scholar, who joined the court, **rather, he** would **sit in the place appropriate for him,** i.e., at the end of that row, in accordance with his stature.

Beyond the half-circle of judges, there were three additional half-circles seated in front of the court: three rows of *talmidei chachamim*. These were concentric, one inside the other, forming a nested series of arcs. This is not merely an arrangement for observers; as the *Gemara* will make clear shortly, these rows served a specific function in the process of adding members to the *Sanhedrin* when necessary.

### Hierarchy & Place

Despite the egalitarian geometry of the half-circle, *kol echad ve-echad makir et mekomo* — every person knew his place. There was a clear hierarchy: which of the three rows you sat in, where within your row you were positioned — all of this was understood and observed. The egalitarianism of the circle, where every judge can see every other, coexists with a precise and acknowledged order of rank. As we will see in the next *mishnah*, this hierarchy has practical procedural consequences.

## Adding Judges & Semicha

### Replacing a Judge

If a judge was required to leave the court — whether because he died, had to recuse himself, or declared *eini yode'a*, I do not know — and the *Sanhedrin* needed to bring in a replacement, a formal process was required.

### Semicha from Moshe

It becomes clear here, with a subtlety that could easily be overlooked, that placing someone on a *Sanhedrin* for capital cases requires granting them *semicha*. This is the ordination that traces back to Moshe Rabbeinu — the authority of *elohim*, representing in some sense divine delegated authority. Without that formal chain of ordination, a judge cannot adjudicate a capital case. So when a vacancy arises, the replacement must be given *semicha* before taking his seat.

### Somchin Min HaRishona

*Somchin min ha-rishona* — one ordains from the first row, that is, from the innermost of the three rows of students seated before the court. Within that inner row, the candidate is the one who is at the head — the front of the half-circle as defined by its internal hierarchy.

### Promotion Cascade

What follows is a cascade of promotions. Once the top person from the inner row is elevated to the *Sanhedrin*, everyone in the inner row shifts forward one position. This creates a vacancy at the end of the inner row. The top person from the second row then moves up to fill that last seat in the inner row. This in turn creates a vacancy at the end of the second row, which is filled by the top person from the third row, leaving a vacancy at the end of the third row.

### Selection from Community

*Borim lahem od echad min ha-kahal* — one selects an additional person from the broader community, someone who has now been identified as worthy of entering this circle of students and potential future judges. This person is brought in to fill the newly opened seat.

### Proper Placement Rule

The person selected from the community does not simply sit in whichever seat is vacant. *Ela yoshev ba-makom ha-rauy lo* — he sits in the place appropriate to his level. The empty seat at the end of the third row happens to be the bottom of the hierarchy, which is in fact where a newcomer belongs. But the principle is stated as a general rule: you do not tell someone to jump to a seat that is not his by rank simply because it happens to be empty. The cascade ensures that everyone advances properly, and the newcomer enters at the position befitting him. The *mishnah* closes with the observation that egalitarianism and hierarchy are not in tension here — they are two aspects of a single, carefully ordered system.
