# Hullin 124 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Leather & Flaying

### Flayed Skin Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמַּפְשִׁיט בַּבְּהֵמָה וּבַחַיָּה, בַּטְּמֵאָה וּבִטְהוֹרָה, בַּדַּקָּה וּבַגַּסָּה, לְשָׁטִיחַ – כְּדֵי אֲחִיזָה. הָא יָתֵר מִכְּדֵי אֲחִיזָה – טָהוֹר, אַמַּאי? לִיגְזַר, דִּילְמָא לָא אָתֵי לְמֶעְבַּד אֶלָּא כְּדֵי אֲחִיזָה, וְקָא נְגַע בְּטוּמְאָה, וְקָא מְטַהֲרִינַן לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a refutation to Rabba bar Avuh's explanation of the mishna in tractate *Kelim* from the mishna here: In the case of **one who flays a domesticated animal or an undomesticated animal, a ritually impure** animal **or a ritually pure** animal, **a small** animal **or a large** animal, if he is flaying the animal **for** the purpose of using the hide as **a carpet,** the halakhic status of the hide remains that of flesh until he has flayed **the measure of grasping** the hide. The Gemara infers: **But** after one has flayed **more than a measure of grasping** the hide, the hide is **pure. Why** is this so? **Let** the Sages **decree** that even a hide that was flayed more than the measure of grasping is susceptible to impurity **lest** one who intends to flay more than a measure of grasping **come to complete only a measure of grasping, and he is** thereby **touching** a source of **impurity, and we** might mistakenly **deem him pure.**

The sugya picks up near the bottom of 123b, where Rabbi Yirmiya poses a challenge. The broader context is a statement attributed to Resh Lakish holding that the principle of a torn garment losing its *tumah* status applies only to cloth, not to leather — because leather is a strong (*chalim*) material and therefore retains its integrity even when the majority has been torn, unless it is completely rent. Rabbi Yirmiya challenges this from a *mishnah* dealing with one who flays an animal — a *behemah* or *chayah*, whether *tehorah* or *teme'ah*, large or small — for the purpose of making a mat (*l'shatiach*). The *mishnah* establishes that once the amount necessary for grasping (*k'dei achizah*) has been removed, the flayed portion is no longer considered connected to the remainder. The *Gemara* asks: why should that be so? Let us say that since the leather is strong (*chalim*), the flayed portion should still be regarded as connected to the rest of the skin — just as Resh Lakish's principle would suggest.

### Tosafot's Analogy

Tosafot is deeply troubled by the underlying analogy. The comparison that the *Gemara* appears to be drawing — between rending a leather garment and flaying skin from an animal — is, in Tosafot's view, fundamentally strained. When Resh Lakish says that a leather garment retains its integrity because of its strength, he is speaking of a garment that has been partially torn but remains connected at the bottom: the whole piece is still, in some sense, one unit. That is entirely different from the case of flaying, where skin is being actively separated from flesh. One cannot simply say that because skin will eventually become leather, the flayed portion is still to be considered "connected" to the meat beneath. Tosafot attempts an explanation of what the analogy might be, but ultimately acknowledges that it is a challenging comparison.

### Avin's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַבִּי אָבִין: רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן עוֹשֶׂה נִיפּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Avin interpreted** the mishna and explained that **one** piece after **one** piece, i.e., each piece that is flayed is **rendered fallen** and disconnected from the flesh, as it will never be reattached to the flesh, unlike the case of a torn hide, which may be sewn back together.

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty through Rabbi Avin's principle: *rishon rishon nafal* — as each piece is removed, it is as though it has already fallen off. Piece by piece, the flaying constitutes a continuous act of disconnection. The situation is thus categorically different from the case of the leather garment: there, the garment is being torn but remains a single connected unit at its base; here, each increment of skin removed has already, in effect, separated itself. This distinction, while perhaps somewhat circular, draws a meaningful line between a partial tear that leaves an intact remainder and an act of progressive removal that produces disconnection at each stage.

### Neck Skin Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: עוֹר שֶׁעַל הַצַּוָּאר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי אוֹמֵר אֵינוֹ חִבּוּר. אַמַּאי? הָא חֲלִים וְקָאֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֵימָא סֵיפָא, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: חִבּוּר. אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּשׁוֹמֵר הֶעָשׂוּי לִנָּתֵק מֵאֵלָיו קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: הָוֵי שׁוֹמֵר, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא הָוֵי שׁוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raises an objection** to the opinion of Reish Lakish from another ruling in the mishna: With regard to **the hide over the neck, Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri says: It is not** considered to have **a connection** to the flesh, as the hide is merely loosely connected to the neck itself, and it is pure. According to Reish Lakish, **why** is this so? **Doesn't** the hide **exist** in its **repaired** state, i.e., isn't it still connected to the neck itself? If an impure hide that was torn and can be repaired remains impure, this should certainly be the *halakha* with regard to hide that was never moved from its initial state. **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: Instead of objecting to the opinion of Reish Lakish due to the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, **say the latter clause** of the mishna: **And the Rabbis say:** The hide covering the neck is considered to have **a connection** to the flesh, a statement that supports the opinion of Reish Lakish according to your reasoning. **Rather, Abaye said:** The dispute between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yoḥanan is irrelevant to the dispute in the mishna between Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri and the Rabbis. In the mishna, **they disagree with regard to** whether an appendage, such as the hide of the neck, that **protects** the flesh but is **prone to become detached by itself** is considered to be protection. One **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that **it is** considered to be **protection, and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, **holds** that **it is not** considered to be **protection.**

The *Gemara* then introduces a further challenge from Rabbi Yosef. The *mishnah* discusses the skin of the neck (*or shel tzavar*): if one has been flaying an animal from the feet upward and has reached the neck, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds that the skin remaining on the neck is *not* considered connected (*eino chibur*). The *Gemara* asks: why not? Here the analogy to Resh Lakish may in fact be somewhat better — the skin is still physically attached to the flesh, and if leather is strong, surely what remains on the neck should be considered a single unit with the rest. Abaye, however, redirects the inquiry: rather than asking from Rabbi Avin's principle, look at the end of the *mishnah*, where the *chachamim* say the neck skin *is* considered connected (*chibur*) — which would indeed support Resh Lakish. Abaye then explains that the debate in the *mishnah* over the neck skin has nothing to do with Resh Lakish's principle at all. The question there concerns a *shomer* — the skin serving as a protective covering for the meat — that is of a kind that will detach on its own (*ha'asui l'natek me'elav*). Once the animal has been flayed all the way up to the neck, gravity and circumstance mean the remaining neck skin will naturally fall away even without continued effort. The dispute between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and the *chachamim* is then about whether something that is still physically attached, but will inevitably detach on its own, counts as a *shomer*. One view holds that it is still a *shomer* because it remains connected now; the other holds it is not a *shomer* because its detachment is only a matter of time.

### Natural Detachment

What is striking about the *mishnah* as a whole, as Abaye's reading reveals, is the elegant tension between its two halves. The opening case involves skin that has already been physically removed, yet is still considered connected — because it is close to the flesh and part of one continuous piece, even the flayed section is treated as attached. The closing case involves skin that is still physically on the animal, yet is considered disconnected — because it will fall away on its own. The criterion throughout is not mere physical attachment but something closer to functional or meaningful connection, and the *mishnah*'s two rulings bracket that concept from opposite directions.

## Pottery Oven Case

### Oven Purification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: תַּנּוּר שֶׁנִּטְמָא כֵּיצַד מְטַהֲרִין אוֹתוֹ? חוֹלְקוֹ לִשְׁלֹשָׁה, וְגוֹרֵר אֶת הַטְּפֵילָה עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises an objection** to the opinion of Reish Lakish from a mishna (*Kelim* 5:7): With regard to **an oven that has become impure, how does one purify it? One divides** the oven **into three** parts **and scrapes off the** layer of **plaster** that surrounds the oven **until** the oven itself merely rests **on the ground** and is not held in place by plaster.Breaking the oven in such a manner renders the oven pure because it is no longer considered a vessel.

Rabbi Yirmiya now introduces a new challenge to Resh Lakish's principle. If the previous analogy — leather garment to flayed skin — was strained by the similarity of material but difference of scenario, this new challenge operates in the reverse: similar scenarios of breaking a vessel, but involving very different materials. If one is going to say that leather is strong and therefore preserves its integrity even when partially damaged, the same logic might apply to pottery, which is also a strong material. The *mishnah* in *Keilim* that the *Gemara* now examines deals with a *tanur*, a clay oven of the kind common in the time of *Chazal*: a large pottery vessel, made separately and then plastered to the ground. Because it was originally fashioned as an independent object, a pottery *tanur* can become *tamei* — the *Torah* itself states, *tanur ve-khirayim yutatz, tme'im hem*, that an oven and stove are *tamei* and must be shattered.

The *mishnah* asks: if a *tamei tanur* is to be purified, how does one do so? The answer is: *chalko li-shlosha* — break it into three pieces. The reasoning is that if it were broken into only two pieces, one would constitute the majority and would retain the status of the original vessel. With three pieces, no single piece holds the majority, and the vessel's identity is dissolved. In addition, *ve-gorer et ha-tpila* — one must scrape away the plaster that attaches it to the ground — *ad she-yehei ba-aretz*, until it is fully released from the ground.

### Rabbi Meir's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לֹא לִגְרוֹר אֶת הַטְּפֵילָה וְלֹא עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּאָרֶץ, אֶלָּא מְמַעֲטוֹ מִבִּפְנִים מֵאַרְבָּעָה טְפָחִים. כִּי מְמַעֵט לַהּ מֵאַרְבָּעָה מִיהָא טָהוֹר, אַמַּאי? לֵימָא: הָא חֲלִים וְקָאֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says: It is unnecessary to scrape off the** layer of **plaster, and** it is certainly **not** necessary to remove it **until** the oven rests **on the ground. Rather, one** makes cuts in the oven itself, **reducing** its size **from within** the layer of plaster, i.e., without removing the layer of plaster, until the unbroken part is less **than four handbreadths.** Rabbi Meir holds that although breaking off a minority of the structure of the oven is insufficient, **in any event when one reduces** the size of the oven to less **than four** handbreadths in height the oven is rendered **pure. Why** is this so, according to the opinion of Reish Lakish? **Let us say** that **this** oven **exists** in **a repairable** state, as the plaster holds it together, and according to Reish Lakish it should therefore be considered connected and remain impure.

Rabbi Meir dissents. He holds that one need not remove the plaster or bring the oven down to the ground; it is sufficient to reduce the oven's interior measurement to less than four *tefachim* (*arba tefachim*). The minimum size for an oven to be considered a halakhically significant vessel is four *tefachim*; once the interior has been reduced below that threshold, the object no longer qualifies as an oven, and it loses its status. The measurement, Rabbi Meir specifies, is *mi-bifnim* — from the inside. This detail may reflect the principle that for clay vessels (*klei cheres*), susceptibility to *tumah* is measured from the interior, since pottery vessels become *tamei* only through their inner space.

The *Gemara* then asks directly: but according to Rabbi Meir, why is it sufficient to get the oven below four *tefachim*? One should say *chalim v'kai* — it is strong and retains its integrity — just as Resh Lakish would reason about leather. Even if you reduce it in size, pottery is still a strong material. Rava intervenes and reframes the entire discussion. He argues that in fact both the *chachamim* and Rabbi Meir agree, based on Resh Lakish's principle that a strong material requires complete rupture, that once an oven has become *tamei*, one must break it into three pieces and remove the plaster entirely to achieve *taharah*. The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* is not about the method of purification once the oven is *tamei*; it concerns a different question entirely.

### Preemptive Purification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְאֵימָא מִדְּרַבָּנַן – גּוֹרֵר אֶת הַטְּפֵילָה עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּאָרֶץ. אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר, תַּנּוּר שֶׁנִּטְמָא כֵּיצַד מְטַהֲרִין אוֹתוֹ? דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל חוֹלְקוֹ לִשְׁלֹשָׁה, וְגוֹרֵר אֶת הַטְּפֵילָה עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּאָרֶץ. וְהָרוֹצֶה שֶׁלֹּא יָבֹא תַּנּוּרוֹ לִידֵי טוּמְאָה, כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? חוֹלְקוֹ לִשְׁלֹשָׁה, וְגוֹרֵר אֶת הַטְּפֵילָה עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּאָרֶץ. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לֹא לִגְרוֹר אֶת הַטְּפֵילָה וְלֹא עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּאָרֶץ, אֶלָּא מְמַעֲטוֹ מִבִּפְנִים מֵאַרְבָּעָה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rabbi Yirmeya: Instead of objecting to the opinion of Reish Lakish from the statement of Rabbi Meir, **state** a proof for his opinion **from** the statement **of the Rabbis** who disagree with Rabbi Meir and hold that the oven becomes pure only if **one scrapes off the** layer of **plaster until** the oven rests **on the ground.** Apparently, the Rabbis hold that the oven is rendered pure only if it is completely and irreparably broken, which supports the opinion of Reish Lakish according to your reasoning. **Rather, Rava said:** Not only is there no proof against the opinion of Reish Lakish from this mishna, as the statement of the Rabbis supports his opinion, but Rabbi Meir may even accept the opinion of Reish Lakish; as **this** is what the mishna **is saying: How does one purify an oven that became impure? Everyone,** even Rabbi Meir, **agrees** that one **divides it into three** parts **and scrapes off the** layer of **plaster until** the oven rests **on the ground.** **And** anyone **who wishes that his oven not become** susceptible to **impurity, how does he act?** The Rabbis hold that he goes through the same process as is necessary in order to purify an impure oven: From the outset, **he divides** the oven **into three** parts **and scrapes off the** layer of **plaster until** the oven rests **on the ground. Rabbi Meir says:** With regard to an oven that has not yet become impure, **it is unnecessary to scrape off the** layer of **plaster, and** it is certainly **not** necessary to remove it **until** the oven rests **on the ground. Rather, one reduces** the size of the oven **from within** the layer of plaster until the unbroken part is less **than four handbreadths** in height.

The question Rava identifies as the actual subject of the dispute is this: what must one do *before* the oven becomes *tamei* in order to prevent it from ever acquiring the status of a vessel? Suppose someone knows a *meit* will be brought into the house and wishes to ensure the oven never becomes *tamei* in the first place — is there a simpler way to strip the object of its identity as an oven before the *tumah* event occurs? The *chachamim* say no: even for that preventive purpose, one must break the oven into three pieces and remove the plaster. Rabbi Meir says that in this preemptive context, it is sufficient to reduce the size to below four *tefachim*, because an oven below that threshold never attains the identity of an oven to begin with. The asymmetry reflects the underlying principle: once *tumah* has been acquired by a fully formed vessel, Resh Lakish's logic requires complete destruction to undo it; but if the object was never fully constituted as a vessel, no complete destruction is necessary.

### Functional Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: חוֹלְקוֹ לִשְׁלֹשָׁה. וּרְמִינְהוּ: תַּנּוּר, תְּחִלָּתוֹ אַרְבָּעָה, וּשְׁיָרָיו אַרְבָּעָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים בְּגָדוֹל, אֲבָל בְּקָטָן – תְּחִלָּתוֹ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, מִשֶּׁתִּגָּמֵר מְלַאכְתּוֹ – שְׁיָרָיו בְּרוּבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** §The Gemara discusses the mishna in tractate *Kelim* cited above. **The Master said:** An impure oven is rendered pure when one **divides it into three** parts, such that no one part contains the majority of the oven. But one cannot purify the oven by dividing it into two parts because one of the parts would contain the majority of the oven. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** to this mishna from another mishna (*Kelim* 5:1): A clay **oven** in **its original state,** once it is finished being built, is susceptible to ritual impurity if it is **four** handbreadths tall. **And** with regard to an oven that became impure and was subsequently broken, if **its remains** include a piece **four** handbreadths tall, that piece remains impure. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **in** the case of **a large** oven, **but in** the case of **a small** oven, in **its original state, any size** is sufficient for it to be susceptible to impurity. **Once its construction is completed,** if the oven became impure and was subsequently broken, **its remains** are still impure **in** a case where they contain **the majority of** the oven.

Implicit in Rabbi Meir's position is a significant conceptual claim: if an object cannot function for the purpose for which it was made, it has no identity as a *kli* at all. This is, as the *Gemara* acknowledges, a surprising proposition. The thirty chapters of *Masechet Keilim* are largely occupied with exactly this web of questions — how closely an object must resemble its original function to retain or acquire vessel-status, what role the maker's intent plays, and how different minimum-size thresholds operate at the stage of initial constitution versus after breakage. Rabbi Meir's position here is that functional incapacity is legally dispositive: an oven too small to cook in is simply not an oven, and Maimonides reads the law accordingly.

### Breaking Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה ״כׇּל שֶׁהוּא״? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: טֶפַח, שֶׁכֵּן עוֹשִׂים תַּנּוּרֵי בָּנוֹת טֶפַח. טַעְמָא דְּאִיכָּא שְׁיָרָיו אַרְבַּע, הָא לֵיכָּא שְׁיָרָיו אַרְבַּע – טָהוֹר. אָמְרִי: הָתָם, דְּצַלְקֵיהּ מִצְלָק; הָכָא, דְּעַבְדֵיהּ גִּיסְטְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And how** small is the size defined by the mishna as **any size?** The **school of Rabbi Yannai says:** One **handbreadth, as** people **make** toy **ovens one handbreadth tall.** The Gemara infers: The Rabbis in that mishna hold that with regard to a large impure oven that breaks, any remaining piece that measures four handbreadths remains impure. Evidently, **the reason** that the oven remains impure is **because there are** pieces of **its remains** that measure **four** handbreadths, **but** if **there are no remains** of the oven measuring **four** handbreadths, even if a piece contains the majority of the oven, the oven is rendered **pure.** This opinion is not consistent with the opinion of the Rabbis in the previously cited mishna. The Sages **said** in response: **There,** with regard to the opinion of the Rabbis that if there are no remains measuring four handbreadths then the oven is pure, that mishna is discussing a case **where one cut** the oven horizontally such that the pieces do not stand one on top of the other in a stable manner. **Here,** with regard to the opinion of the Rabbis that an impure oven is rendered pure only when no one piece constitutes the majority of the oven, the mishna is discussing a case **where one rendered** the oven **a shard [*gistera*]** by cutting it in half vertically, in which case a piece that contains the majority of the oven can stand on its own.

The *Gemara* now examines a *braita* from *Keilim* that on its surface appears to contradict Rava's reframing. The *braita* states that a *tanur* whose initial minimum size is four *tefachim* also retains its status as long as its remnant (*shiyuro*) is four *tefachim* — this is Rabbi Meir's position. The *chachamim* qualify: the four-*tefachim* standard applies to a large oven; for a small oven, even the smallest size suffices from the outset, and the remnant retains its status as long as the majority remains. This *braita* appears to show the *chachamim* agreeing with Rabbi Meir that reducing an oven below four *tefachim* renders it *tahor* — which conflicts with Rava's claim that the *chachamim* require complete breaking regardless.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing between two methods of breaking. When the oven is cut *widthwise* (*tzalkei mitzlak*), the resulting piece retains functional utility — the bottom portion with its full circumference may still serve as an oven — and in that case the *chachamim* maintain their stricter position, requiring the oven to be broken into thirds rather than relying on the four-*tefachim* threshold. When the oven is cut *lengthwise* (*gistera*), what remains is far less functional, and there the *chachamim* concede that reduction below four *tefachim* suffices. It should be noted that Rashi reads the two cases in the opposite direction — he assigns the stricter requirement to the lengthwise cut and the more lenient standard to the widthwise — and his reasoning on this point is difficult to follow.

### Braita Measurements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: שְׁיָרָיו בְּרוּבּוֹ, רוּבּוֹ דְּטֶפַח לְמַאי הָוֵי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁיָרֵי גָּדוֹל בְּרוּבּוֹ, וְהָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן אַרְבָּעָה? לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּתַנּוּרָא בַּר תִּשְׁעָה, הָא בְּתַנּוּרָא בַּר שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** §The Gemara discusses the previously cited mishna in tractate *Kelim*. **The Master said:** In the case of a small oven, in its original state, any size is sufficient for it to be susceptible to impurity. And once its construction is completed, if the oven became impure and was subsequently broken, **its remains** are still impure if they contain **the majority** of the oven. The Gemara asks: Rabbi Yannai explained that the phrase: Any size, is referring to a measure of one handbreadth. **For what** purpose **is** a piece of an oven the size of **the majority of** one **handbreadth** usable? Since such a small piece is not functional; why should it remain impure? **Abaye said:** The statement of the mishna: Its remains are still impure if they contain the majority of the oven, is not discussing a small oven, but rather is teaching that **the remains of a large** oven remain impure if they contain **the majority of** the oven. The Gemara asks: **But didn't the Rabbis say** in the mishna that any remaining piece of a large oven measuring **four** handbreadths remains impure? The Gemara answers: That is **not difficult. That** statement of the Rabbis that the remains of a large oven remain impure if they contain the majority of the oven is referring **to an oven** measuring **nine** handbreadths. **That** statement of the Rabbis that any remaining piece of a large oven measuring four handbreadths remains impure is referring **to an oven** measuring **seven** handbreadths.

The *braita* continues to specify: for a large oven, the standard of four *tefachim* governs both initial constitution and the size of the remnant after breakage. For a small oven, the *chachamim* say *techilato kol she-hu* — any size can constitute such an oven initially — and its remnant retains status as long as the majority remains. The *Gemara* asks: what is the practical minimum of *kol she-hu*? The school of Rabbi Yannai answers: *tefach* — even a single handbreadth. This invites an obvious question: what purpose could an oven of a single *tefach* possibly serve?

### Play Ovens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל שֶׁהוּא מִשֵּׁם אֶחָד – מִצְטָרֵף וְטָמֵא, מִשְּׁנֵי שֵׁמוֹת – טָהוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say** according to the opinion of Reish Lakish that the hide is considered **significant** and therefore remains impure.

The *Gemara* answers: *she-ken osin tanurim benot tefach* — people make play ovens (*tanurim benot tefach*) the size of a *tefach*. This is a charming detail: children's play ovens, comparable in concept to the miniature kitchen sets familiar from later generations, were functional enough within the world of children's play that they were recognized as genuine *keilim* for the purposes of *tumah*. Rashi understands the word *benot* as referring to the oven itself — it is an oven of the daughter, i.e., a child's oven — and glosses it with the term *le-tinokot*, for children. Whether such an oven was genuinely usable in any practical sense, or whether its recognition as a *kli* rested entirely on its serviceability for the purpose for which it was made (children's play), is itself a conceptual question about the nature of vessel-identity: if it is made to serve as a real oven in the world of children's play, then play ovens have their own minimum size, and as long as the object functions for that purpose, it qualifies.

One might ask: if breaking the oven into three pieces causes it to lose its vessel-identity, why does not the first break already transfer *tumah* to the larger remaining piece, and then the second break to the larger of those two, and so on? The answer seems to be that even though the larger piece may technically retain the *tumah* of an oven, it is not clear that we would still call it an oven once it is non-functional as such. Moreover, one act of breaking can produce three pieces simultaneously, bypassing the question of sequential stages. The broader principle at work — that the minimum-size thresholds for initial constitution and for post-breakage significance may differ — is extensively treated in the preceding pages of the *Gemara*.

### Reconciling Views

The *Gemara* finally synthesizes the positions. The remnant of a small oven — one that started as small as a *tefach* — retains its status as long as the majority (*rubo*) remains. But then the *Gemara* asks: *ruba d'tefach l'mai chazi*? What can you actually do with the majority of a *tefach*-sized oven? It is completely non-functional even for children's play. Abaye clarifies: when the *chachamim* say the remnant is measured by majority, they are referring back to the earlier case of a *large* oven, not a small one. For a large oven, the *chachamim* hold that once the majority has been removed, the remainder loses its status. The apparent contradiction with the passage where the *chachamim* seemed to agree with Rabbi Meir's four-*tefachim* standard is resolved by distinguishing oven sizes: for an oven of nine *tefachim*, the *chachamim* hold it sufficient to have removed the majority even if the remnant exceeds four *tefachim*; for an oven of seven *tefachim*, reduction below four *tefachim* suffices. In this special context — where the oven is being broken widthwise (or, according to Rashi's reading, in the other orientation) — the *chachamim* are lenient and permit either standard, while Rabbi Meir always insists on the four-*tefachim* threshold regardless.

The entire discussion of the *tanur* was introduced to test Resh Lakish's principle. The *Gemara* ultimately holds that the pottery oven cases do not contradict him, because the debate between the *chachamim* and Rabbi Meir concerns preemptive action rather than post-*tumah* purification. Once *tumah* has been acquired, everyone agrees with the logic of Resh Lakish: a strong material requires complete destruction to be rendered *tahor*.

## Leather Importance

### Leather Retains Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא אָמְרִי לַהּ: אָמַר רַב הוּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: וַאֲפִילּוּ שִׁיֵּיר בָּהּ כְּדֵי מַעְפּוֹרֶת. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא טַלִּית, אֲבָל עוֹר – חֲשִׁיב, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ עוֹר נָמֵי לָא חֲשִׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** §The Gemara returns to discuss the mishna in tractate *Kelim* (28:8) previously mentioned in the Gemara (123a–b): In the case of a ritually impure garment that one begins to tear, once the majority of the garment is torn, the two sections are no longer considered to have a connection, and the garment is pure. Rav Huna said in the name of Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi Yosei, that even if the majority of the garment is torn, if a part of the garment the measure of a scarf is left intact, the garment remains impure. **Some say another version** of Rav Huna's statement: **Rav Huna said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei:** If the majority of the garment is torn, the garment is rendered pure **even** if one **left** an untorn piece **the measure of a scarf.** With regard to that statement of Rav Huna, **Reish Lakish said:** The Sages **taught** that the garment is pure even if one left an untorn piece the measure of a scarf **only** with regard to a torn **garment. But** with regard to **a hide,** if the majority was torn and a piece the measure of a scarf remains, the piece is considered **significant** and the hide remains impure. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even** with regard to a torn **hide,** if a piece the size of a scarf remains it is **not** considered **significant** and the hide is therefore rendered pure.

The *Gemara* now presents an alternative formulation (*lashon acher*) of the same underlying principle. Rav Huna, citing Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yose, restates the opening position: if a torn garment still retains enough material for a wrap (*k'dei ma'aforet*), it does not become *tahor* even if the majority has been rent. Resh Lakish then qualifies: this was said only about cloth (*tallit*), but leather (*or*) is *chashiv* — more significant — and therefore retains its identity by virtue of its importance rather than merely its physical strength.

### Leather vs. Cloth

The shift in language is worth noting. Earlier, the principle was framed in terms of leather being *chalim* — strong — which invited comparisons to other strong materials like pottery and led to a range of difficult questions. Here the language changes: leather is *chashiv*, important, a more significant material than cloth, one that people do not readily discard even when substantially damaged. Because of this significance, it is not regarded as having lost its identity until it is completely rent. This framing carries a somewhat different conceptual weight: the question is not merely physical durability but the object's standing in human use and valuation.

### Intent & Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: עוֹר טָמֵא מִדְרָס, חִישֵּׁב עָלָיו לִרְצוּעָה וְסַנְדָּלִין – כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּתַן בּוֹ אִיזְמֵל טָהוֹר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיַּמְעִיטֶנּוּ מֵחֲמִשָּׁה טְפָחִים. כִּי מְמַעֵט מִיהָא טָהוֹר, אַמַּאי? לֵימָא חֲשִׁיב! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּקָא בָעֵי לֵיהּ לְמוֹשַׁב זָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** from that which is taught in the mishna (*Kelim* 26:9): With regard to **a hide** that is **impure** with impurity imparted by **treading,** if the owner **intended with regard to** the hide **to** fashion it into **straps and sandals,** then **when he applies a scalpel to** the hide, the hide becomes **pure;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And the Rabbis say:** The hide does not become pure **until he reduces** the hide to a measure of less **than five handbreadths. In any event, when one reduces** the hide to a measure of less than five handbreadths, everyone agrees that it becomes **pure. Why** is this so? **Let us say** that the hide is considered **significant** and therefore remains impure. Reish Lakish answered: **Here, we are dealing with** a case **where one needs** the cut hide **for a seat** that he wishes to designate for **a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*].** Since a piece of hide measuring less than five handbreadths cannot be used for a seat, it is not considered significant in such a case.

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Resh Lakish from a *mishnah*: a piece of *tamei* leather that had been used as a mat and acquired *tumah* from a *zav* sitting on it — once one intends to cut it into straps and sandals and applies a blade (*izmel*) to it, it immediately becomes *tahor*. This is the view of Rabbi Yehudah. The *chachamim* say one must reduce it to less than five *tefachim* before it becomes *tahor*. But in either case, the question arises: according to Resh Lakish, why does a single cut, or even reduction to five *tefachim*, suffice? If leather is so significant that it requires complete rending, surely it should retain its *tumah* until fully destroyed. The *Gemara* responds by distinguishing intent. Here, the owner has defined a very narrow, specific intended use for the leather — cutting it into straps and sandals — and that narrowly defined intent is sufficient to strip the object of its identity as a garment. A normal piece of leather, without such a clearly limited defined purpose, would retain its identity because of its importance, until completely rent. But when the owner mentally commits to a specific diminished use, that act of mind alone begins to dissolve the object's status as a significant leather item.

## Skin & Meat Significance

### Hair as Shomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עוֹר שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר, הַנּוֹגֵעַ בְּצִיב הַיּוֹצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ, וּבְשַׂעֲרָה שֶׁכְּנֶגְדּוֹ – טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a hide** of an unslaughtered carcass **upon which there is an olive-bulk of flesh, one who touches a strand** of flesh **emerging from** the flesh **or a hair** that is on the side of the hide **opposite** the flesh **is ritually impure.** Although he did not touch an olive-bulk of the flesh, he is rendered impure with the impurity of an unslaughtered carcass. The reason is that the strand of flesh has the same status as the flesh itself, and the hair is considered protection to the flesh, which also has the same status as the flesh with regard to one who touches it.

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishnah*. If a piece of skin (*or*) has on it a *kezayit* of meat from a *neveilah*, and one touches a strand of meat (*tziv*) trailing from it, or a hair (*sa'arah*) on the other side of the skin, one becomes *tamei*. This establishes the concept that even a hair or a trailing strand can serve as a *shomer* — a protector or connector — transmitting *tumah* from the meat through the skin, because it is considered meaningfully connected to the significant piece of meat on the other side.

### Rabbi Yishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ עָלָיו כִּשְׁנֵי חֲצָאֵי זֵיתִים, מְטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא וְלֹא בְּמַגָּע, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא בְּמַגָּע וְלֹא בְּמַשָּׂא. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בִּשְׁנֵי חֲצָאֵי זֵיתִים שֶׁתְּחָבָן בְּקֵיסָם וֶהֱסִיטָן, שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא. וּמִפְּנֵי מָה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מְטַהֵר בָּעוֹר? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהָעוֹר מְבַטְּלָן.
>
> **Translation:** If **upon** the hide **there were two half olive-bulks,** the hide **imparts the impurity** of an unslaughtered carcass **by means of carrying,** because one moves them together, **but not by means of contact** with the flesh, because one touches them separately; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says:** The hide does not impart impurity, **neither by means of contact nor by means of carrying. And Rabbi Akiva concedes in** the case of **two half olive-bulks where one skewered them with a wood chip and moved them that he is impure. And for what** reason **does Rabbi Akiva deem** one **ritually pure** in a case where he moved both half olive-bulks with **the hide,** as in that case, too, he moved them together? **It is because the hide** separates between them and **nullifies them.**

The *mishnah* then addresses a more complex case: the skin has on it not a full *kezayit* but *shenei chatzei zeitim* — two half-*zeisim* — of *neveilah* meat. Rabbi Yishmael says: this combination renders *tamei* through carrying (*massa*) but not through touching (*maga*). The reason is that touching two separate pieces constitutes two separate acts of *maga*, and since neither half alone reaches the minimum measure of a *kezayit*, neither act of touching alone produces *tumah*. Carrying, by contrast, is a single act that moves both halves together, and since in total they equal a *kezayit*, one becomes *tamei* through *massa*.

### Rabbi Akiva's Principle

Rabbi Akiva takes a stricter position: the two half-*zeisim* on leather render *tamei* neither through touching nor through carrying. However, he himself concedes — and the *mishnah* makes this explicit — that if the same two half-*zeisim* were threaded on a twig (*kisom*) and one moved them, one *would* be *tamei*. This concession makes clear that Rabbi Akiva's leniency in the case of leather is not a general principle about two halves failing to combine. In general, Rabbi Akiva holds that two half-*zeisim* *do* combine to create *tumah*. The reason he is lenient specifically on the leather case is articulated by the *mishnah* itself: *mipnei she-ha'or mevatlan* — because the leather negates their significance. The leather is more substantial than the pieces of sub-*kezayit* meat resting upon it, and that material significance causes the leather to be *mevatel* the meat, rendering the halves as if they were not there.

### Bitul from Flaying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא פְּלָטַתּוּ חַיָּה, אֲבָל פְּלָטַתּוּ סַכִּין – בָּטֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that in the case of a hide of an unslaughtered carcass upon which there is an olive-bulk of flesh, the flesh is not nullified by the hide, and therefore one who touches a strand of flesh emerging from the flesh is ritually impure. With regard to this section of the mishna, **Ulla said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The Sages **taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case where **an animal severed** the piece of flesh, e.g., a dog bite. **But** if a person used **a knife** to **sever** the flesh, the flesh **is nullified** by the hide because the person nullified the flesh via his action.

Ulla, citing Rabbi Yochanan, adds a further qualification: the significance of even small amounts of meat on leather is maintained only when the meat was torn away by an animal (*peletato chayah*). But if the meat remained on the skin as a result of flaying with a knife (*peletato sakin*), it is *batel* — negated — to the leather, and even Rabbi Yishmael would agree that such meat has no halakhic significance. The act of the knife, in this view, represents a kind of human discarding: the flesher removed what was meant to be removed, and what is left behind has been, in effect, abandoned to insignificance.

## Rav Nachman's Objection

### Extreme Bitul Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְעוּלָּא: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אֲפִילּוּ כְּתַרְטָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. וַאֲפִילּוּ כְּנַפְיָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָאֱלֹהִים! אִם אָמַר לִי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִפּוּמֵּיהּ – לָא צָיֵיתְנָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to Ulla: Did Rabbi Yoḥanan say** this *halakha* **even** with regard to a large piece of flesh the size of **a *tarta*,** i.e., a quarter of a *kav*? Ulla **said to him: Yes.** Rav Naḥman was surprised and asked: **And** did he say it **even** with regard to a piece of flesh the size of **a sifter?** Ulla **said to him: Yes.** Rav Naḥman swore and **said to him: By God!** Even **if Rabbi Yoḥanan had said** this statement **to me** directly **from his mouth, I would not have listened to him.**

Rav Nachman challenges Ulla sharply. He asks: did Rabbi Yochanan really say that *peletato sakin* — meat that came off by the knife — is *batel* to the leather, even if the quantity of meat is as large as a *tarta* (which Rashi identifies as a quarter of a *kav*, a substantial amount)? Ulla confirms: yes. Even if the quantity is as large as a *kenafya* — a large sieve used for sifting flour — would Rabbi Yochanan say it is *batel*? Again, Ulla confirms. At this, Rav Nachman declares: *chai Elokeim*, by God, even if I had heard this directly from Rabbi Yochanan's own mouth, I would not accept it. The claim that even a large, significant quantity of meat is *batel* simply because it came off the knife rather than from an animal's tearing strikes him as halakhically untenable. A *kezayit* of meat is a *kezayit* of meat; it should not lose its significance merely because of how it came to rest on the leather.

### Rabbi Ami's Reaction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סְלֵיק רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַמֵּי, אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּיה קַמֵּיהּ: הָכִי אֲמַר עוּלָּא, וְהָכִי אַהְדַּר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמִשּׁוּם דְּרַב נַחְמָן חַתְנֵיהּ דְּבֵי נְשִׂיאָה הוּא, מְזַלְזֵל בִּשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן?!
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Oshaya ascended** from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael **he found Rabbi Ami,** and **he said this *halakha* before him: This is what Ulla said and this is what Rav Naḥman responded to him.** Rav Oshaya **said to** Rabbi Ami: **And** just **because Rav Naḥman is the son-in-law of the family of the *Nasi*, can he demean the halakhic statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan?**

When Rabbi Oshia traveled to Eretz Yisrael and related this exchange to Rabbi Ami — recounting what Ulla had said and how Rav Nachman had responded — Rabbi Ami was offended. He said: is it because Rav Nachman is the son-in-law of the house of the *Nasi* that he feels entitled to be so dismissive (*mezalzel*) of a teaching in the name of Rabbi Yochanan? The sharpness of Rav Nachman's rejection — "I would not accept it even from Rabbi Yochanan's mouth" — struck Rabbi Ami as an act of *chutzpah* that could only be explained by the social prestige Rav Nachman enjoyed through his family connection to the *Nasi*'s house. The story is notable for what it reveals about the social dynamics of the Babylonian *batei midrash* and the complex relationship between rabbinic authority and aristocratic standing.

### Yochanan Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִמְנִין אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ דְּיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר לַהּ אַסֵּיפָא: הָיוּ עָלָיו שְׁנֵי חֲצָאֵי זֵיתִים – מְטַמְּאִים בְּמַשָּׂא וְלֹא בְּמַגָּע, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא בְּמַגָּע וְלֹא בְּמַשָּׂא. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא פְּלָטַתּוּ חַיָּה, אֲבָל פְּלָטַתּוּ סַכִּין – בָּטֵיל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר אַסֵּיפָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. וְאֶלָּא עוּלָּא אַרֵישָׁא אַמְרַהּ נִיהֲלַיְכוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָאֱלֹהִים, אִי אָמַר לִי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ – לָא צָיֵיתְנָא לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Another **time** Rav Oshaya **found** Rabbi Ami **sitting and saying this** *halakha* **with regard to the latter clause** of the mishna: If **upon** the hide **there were two half olive-bulks,** the hide **imparts** the **impurity** of an unslaughtered carcass **by means of carrying but not by means of contact** with the flesh, because he touches them separately and moves them together; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says:** It imparts impurity **neither by means of contact nor by means of carrying.** With regard to this section of the mishna, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The Sages **taught** Rabbi Yishmael's opinion that the hide imparts the impurity of an unslaughtered carcass by means of carrying **only** in a case where **an animal severed** the half olive-bulks of flesh from the animal. **But** if a person used **a knife** to **sever** the half olive-bulks of flesh, the flesh **is nullified.** If Rav Naḥman had heard that the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan was stated with regard to a case of half olive-bulks of flesh, he would not have been surprised that this *halakha* also applies to pieces of flesh that amount together to the size of a *tarta* or a sifter. Rav Oshaya **said to** Rabbi Ami: **Does the Master teach** this *halakha* **with regard to the latter clause** of the mishna but not with regard to the first section of the mishna that discusses the case of a complete olive-bulk of flesh? Rabbi Ami **said to him: Yes. But did Ulla say to you** this halakhic statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan in Babylonia **with regard to the first clause** of the mishna? Rav Oshaya **said to him: Yes.** Rabbi Ami **said to him:** If so, Rav Naḥman was justified in his surprise at the *halakha* of Rabbi Yoḥanan. **By God,** even **if Joshua, son of Nun, had said** this *halakha* **to me in his name,** i.e., from his own mouth, **I would not have listened to him.**

The story, however, does not end there. At a later occasion, Rabbi Oshia found Rabbi Ami teaching Rabbi Yochanan's statement — but this time applying it to the *seifa*, the second part of the *mishnah*, where the case involves two half-*zeisim* rather than a full *kezayit*. In that context, Rabbi Yochanan's claim that *peletato sakin* is *batel* makes perfect sense: it is only saying that when the meat left by the knife is less than a *kezayit* — specifically two half-*zeisim* in the case of the *mishnah* — it is *batel* to the leather. Rabbi Ami had understood Rabbi Yochanan's teaching to apply only in this limited register. Rabbi Oshia then says to him: you are teaching it on the *seifa*, on the case of less than a *kezayit* — but Ulla taught it on the *reisha*, on the case of a full *kezayit* or more. Rabbi Ami is startled. Now understanding just how extreme Ulla's transmission of Rabbi Yochanan's statement was, he declares: *HaElokim*, even if I had heard this from the mouth of Yehoshua bin Nun himself, I would not accept it. The same Rabbi Ami who had been offended by Rav Nachman's dismissiveness, once he grasped the actual content of the claim, made an even stronger declaration of rejection.

### Merudad Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין וְכׇל נָחוֹתֵי, אַמְרוּהָ אַרֵישָׁא. וְאֶלָּא קַשְׁיָא? כִּדְאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בִּמְרוּדָּד, הָכָא נָמֵי בִּמְרוּדָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rabin and all those descending** from Eretz Yisrael **came** to Babylonia, **they stated** this *halakha* of Rabbi Yoḥanan **with regard to the first clause** of the mishna. The Gemara objects: **But** the matter is **difficult.** If an olive-bulk of flesh is nullified by being severed with a knife, the same should be true for larger measurements, such as a *tarta*, which is unreasonable, as people would not usually disregard such a large amount. The Gemara resolves this difficulty **in accordance with that which Rav Pappa said** with regard to a different matter: The reference is **to a thin** layer of flesh attached to the hide. **Here, too,** the Gemara concludes that Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement is referring **to a thin** layer of flesh attached to the hide. When a person severs such a piece of flesh along with the hide, even if the total volume of the flesh is an olive-bulk, or even a much larger measure, it is insignificant and is nullified by the hide.

When Rabin and the other scholars who came from the west (*nechusei yama*) arrived, they confirmed: yes, Rabbi Yochanan's teaching was indeed stated on the *reisha*, applying even to quantities larger than a *kezayit*. But they offered the key to understanding it: *k'damar Rav Pappa bimrudad* — as Rav Pappa would later explain, the case involves *merudad*, meat that has been spread thin. Rav Pappa's definition of *merudad* is meat that is technically a *kezayit* or more in total volume but is spread out so thinly across the surface of the leather — like the thin layer of flesh that clings to a peel when one peels a vegetable — that it has no practical significance as a discrete quantity of meat. In such a case, no matter how much it totals in the abstract, its thinness renders it *batel* to the leather beneath it. This resolution clarifies the outer limits of the principle: it is not that any quantity of meat left by a knife is automatically *batel*, but rather that meat which is spread so thin as to be practically invisible as a food substance loses its halakhic significance relative to the leather.

## Touching Sides Matter

### Front vs. Back Touch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר בַּר פְּדָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מֵאַחֲרָיו, אֲבָל מִלְּפָנָיו – יֵשׁ נוֹגֵעַ וְחוֹזֵר וְנוֹגֵעַ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין נוֹגֵעַ וְחוֹזֵר וְנוֹגֵעַ, וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְרַבִּי דּוֹסָא בֶּן הַרְכִּינָס אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Bar Padda says:** The Sages **taught** that Rabbi Yishmael holds that that hide does not impart impurity by means of contact **only** with regard to one who touched the hide **on the outside. But** if one directly touched the pieces of flesh **inside** the hide, even though he did not touch any one piece measuring an olive-bulk, he is impure. This is because **there is** a principle that if one **touches** an impure item measuring less than an olive-bulk **and again touches** another impure item measuring less than an olive-bulk, he becomes impure, as the two instances of contact join together to constitute contact with the requisite measure of an olive-bulk. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: There is no** such principle that if one **touches** an impure item **and again touches** another impure item that the two instances of contact join together to constitute contact with the requisite measure of an olive-bulk. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan follows his** line of **reasoning, as Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas said the same thing,** i.e., maintained the same principle.

Bar Padda introduces a distinction that refines the case of two half-*zeisim* on leather. He says: the ruling that touching does not render *tamei* was stated only with respect to touching from behind (*me'achorah*) — that is, touching the skin side rather than the meat. When one touches the skin side of the leather, the skin itself stands between the person and the meat, and since the skin is not a *shomer* for a mere half-*kezayit*, the touch does not communicate *tumah*. But if one touches from the front (*milfaneha*) — directly touching the meat itself — then, according to Bar Padda, two separate acts of touching (*yesh nogea ve-chozer ve-nogea*) do combine, and one would become *tamei*.

### Two Acts Don't Combine

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. He holds that *ein nogea ve-chozer ve-nogea* — there is no principle by which two separate acts of touching combine. Whether one touches the meat from the front or the skin from behind is irrelevant to the question of combination; two separate acts of touching two separate half-*zeisim* never produce *tumah*, regardless of which side is touched. The only act of conveyance in our *mishnah* that does combine two halves is *massa* — carrying — which is structurally a single act that moves both pieces together.

## Yochanan's Alignment

### Dosa's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן; רַבִּי דּוֹסָא בֶּן הַרְכִּינָס – דִּתְנַן: כָּל הַמְטַמְּאִין בְּאֹהֶל, שֶׁנֶּחְלְקוּ וְהִכְנִיסָן לְתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת – רַבִּי דּוֹסָא בֶּן הַרְכִּינָס מְטַהֵר, וַחֲכָמִים מְטַמְּאִים.
>
> **Translation:** The statement of **Rabbi Yishmael is that which we said:** Two instances of contact do not join together to constitute contact with the requisite measure of impurity. The statement of **Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas is** that **which we learned** in a mishna (*Oholot* 3:1): With regard to **any** part of a corpse that **imparts impurity in a tent,** i.e., that imparts impurity to any other item that is under the same roof, if that body part was **divided** into two pieces, each measuring less than an olive-bulk, but together they constitute an olive-bulk, **and one placed** both pieces **inside the house,Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas** rules that the two pieces do not join together to constitute the requisite measure of an olive-bulk. Therefore, he **deems** everything inside the house **pure. And the Rabbis** rule that the two pieces of the corpse join together to constitute an olive-bulk, and therefore they **deem** everything inside the house **impure.**

Rabbi Yochanan further notes that his position aligns with an earlier parallel ruling. He states that Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas said essentially the same thing. Rabbi Yishmael's position is what we have already seen: two half-*zeisim* touched separately do not combine. The parallel attributed to Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas comes from a case involving *ohel* — the *tumah* of a corpse spreading under a roof. If a *kezayit* from a corpse was divided into two halves and both halves were brought into a house, Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas rules the house remains *tahor*, while the *chachamim* say it is *tamei*. Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas reasons: just as one cannot become *tamei* by being *ma'ahil* — serving as a canopy — over one half and then the other half in separate acts, these two halves, even under the same roof, do not combine. This is analogous, in Rabbi Yochanan's view, to Rabbi Yishmael's ruling that two separate acts of touching do not combine.

### Tosafot's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו אָמַר רַבִּי דּוֹסָא בֶּן הַרְכִּינָס הָתָם, אֵין מַאֲהִיל וְחוֹזֵר וּמַאֲהִיל, הָכָא נָמֵי אֵין נוֹגֵעַ וְחוֹזֵר וְנוֹגֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Didn't Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas say** in that mishna **there** that **there is no** such principle that a tent **overlies** an impure item **and again overlies** another impure item such that the two instances join together to constitute the requisite measure for impurity imparted in a tent? **Here, too,** Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas would agree with Rabbi Yishmael that **there is no** such principle that if one **touches** an impure item **and again touches** another impure item that the two instances join together to constitute contact with the requisite measure of an olive-bulk.

Tosafot raises a sharp objection to this analogy. In our *mishnah*, everyone agrees that *carrying* two half-*zeisim* together renders *tamei*, because carrying is a single act that moves both pieces. One would think that being *ma'ahil* over two halves under one roof is far more similar to carrying — both are single encompassing acts — than to two separate acts of touching. Why then does Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas compare being *ma'ahil* over two halves to two separate touchings rather than to carrying? Tosafot's resolution is that the analogy is not really about "one act versus two acts" at all. For those who hold, like Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas, that two half-*zeisim* do not combine, the ruling applies whether the act is a single one or multiple ones — whether touching, being *ma'ahil*, or anything else. The only exception is carrying, and that exception is not grounded in the logic of "one act" but in a specific *pasuk*. Even physically speaking, an act of carrying might be considered a single encompassing act just as being *ma'ahil* is — yet the *Torah* establishes a special rule for *massa* that overrides the general principle.

### Splitting Prevention

The deeper principle, on Tosafot's reading, is this: when a *kezayit* is split into two halves, those halves simply do not combine for *tumah* — whether by touching, by *ohel*, or by any other mode. The exception of *massa* is a *chiddush* derived from a specific verse, not a logical extension of any "single-act" theory. This reframing shifts the conceptual center of gravity: the question is not about counting acts but about whether a divided *kezayit* retains its power to transmit *tumah* in a given category of *tumah*-transmission. Only *massa* — by force of a scriptural derivation — treats the divided *kezayit* as still effective.

## Touching-Carrying Link

### Verse Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: ״בְּנִבְלָתָם״ – וְלֹא בְּעוֹר שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו שְׁנֵי חֲצָאֵי זֵיתִים. יָכוֹל אַף בְּמַשָּׂא? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְהַנּוֹשֵׂא יִטְמָא״ – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״הַנּוֹגֵעַ״ ״וְהַנּוֹשֵׂא״ – אֶת שֶׁבָּא לִכְלַל מַגָּע, בָּא לִכְלַל מַשָּׂא; לֹא בָּא לִכְלַל מַגָּע, לֹא בָּא לִכְלַל מַשָּׂא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ukva bar Ḥama raises an objection** to the statement of bar Padda that Rabbi Yishmael maintains that two instances of contact with two pieces measuring less than an olive-bulk join together to constitute contact with the requisite measure of an olive-bulk. His objection is based on that which is taught in a *baraita*: It is written: "And by these you shall become impure; whoever touches **their carcass** shall be impure until evening. And whoever carries the carcass of them shall wash his clothes and be impure until evening" (Leviticus 11:24–25). It is derived from the term "their carcass" that one who touches the carcass itself becomes impure, **but** one who touches **a hide that has upon it two half olive-bulks** of flesh does **not** become impure. One **might** have thought that a hide that has upon it two half olive-bulks of flesh does not impart impurity **even by means of carrying.** Therefore, the continuation of **the verse states: "And whoever carries** the carcass of them **shall** wash his clothes and **be impure** until evening," from which it is derived that one who carries a carcass, even by means of the hide, becomes impure; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says:** The verse juxtaposes **"whoever touches"** with **"and whoever carries,"** indicating that **that which enters the category of** impurity via **contact, enters the category of** impurity via **carrying; that which does not enter the category of** impurity via **contact, does not enter the category of** impurity via **carrying.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the scriptural basis for Rabbi Akiva's principle. The *Torah* uses two verbs side by side in the context of *neveilah*: *ha-nogea be-nivlatam* — one who touches their carcass — and *ve-hanosei et nivlatam* — one who carries their carcass. The juxtaposition of *maga* (touching) and *massa* (carrying) in the same context suggests a systematic relationship between the two categories.

### Akiva's Deduction

From this juxtaposition, Rabbi Akiva derives: *kol she-ba li-khlal maga, ba li-khlal massa; lo ba li-khlal maga, lo ba li-khlal massa* — whatever can make one *tamei* through touching can make one *tamei* through carrying; whatever cannot make one *tamei* through touching cannot make one *tamei* through carrying. Carrying, on this view, is conceptually a form of indirect touching — a more remote means of contact — and therefore requires the same potential for *tumah*-transmission through direct contact. If the object cannot render one *tamei* by being touched, it should not be able to render one *tamei* through the more attenuated act of carrying.

### Touching Meat Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, הֲרֵי בָּא לִכְלַל מַגָּע מִלְּפָנָיו? אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר, אֶת שֶׁבָּא לִכְלַל מַגָּע בְּכׇל צַד – בָּא לִכְלַל מַשָּׂא, לֹא בָּא לִכְלַל מַגָּע בְּכׇל צַד – לֹא בָּא לִכְלַל מַשָּׂא.
>
> **Translation:** Based on this *baraita*, one can object to the statement of bar Padda: **And if it is so** that Rabbi Yishmael maintains that two instances of contact with two pieces of flesh measuring less than an olive-bulk join together to constitute contact with the requisite measure of an olive-bulk, then the case of a hide that has upon it two half olive-bulks of flesh also **enters the category of** impurity transmitted by means of **contact** when one directly touches the flesh **inside** the hide. Therefore, why does Rabbi Akiva disagree with Rabbi Yishmael by stating that in this case there is no transmission of impurity by means of carrying because there is no transmission of impurity by means of contact? **Rava said** that **this** is what Rabbi Akiva **is saying: That which enters the category of** impurity via **contact in every manner,** even by touching the hide on the outside, **enters the category of** impurity via **carrying; that which does not enter the category of** impurity via **contact in every manner, does not enter the category of** impurity via **carrying.**

The *Gemara* now confronts this principle with a difficulty arising from Bar Padda's earlier distinction. Bar Padda had said that if one touches the meat of the two half-*zeisim* directly — from the front — one *does* become *tamei*. If that is so, then Rabbi Yishmael should be able to say: the two halves *can* render *tamei* through touching (from the front), and therefore Rabbi Akiva's principle should conclude that they render *tamei* through carrying as well. But Rabbi Yishmael says they *do* render *tamei* through carrying. So Rabbi Akiva's challenge to Rabbi Yishmael — that if you cannot become *tamei* through touching, you cannot become *tamei* through carrying — seems to have no force, since Rabbi Yishmael would agree that touching the meat directly does render *tamei*.

### Rava's Resolution

Rava resolves the difficulty by refining the principle. Rabbi Akiva's requirement is not merely that one *can* become *tamei* through touching the object from *some* side; it is that one must be able to become *tamei* by touching it *from any side* (*be-chol tzad*). If there exists even one side from which touching does not render *tamei* — specifically, the skin side in this case — then the condition for *massa*-*tumah* is not met, and one cannot become *tamei* through carrying either. The logical basis for this more demanding formulation is less immediately intuitive: why should the impossibility of becoming *tamei* from the skin side foreclose the possibility of *tumah* through carrying, if touching the meat directly *does* render *tamei*? Rava's resolution stands as the *Gemara*'s answer to defend Bar Padda's distinction, but the underlying reasoning requires careful study.

## Sealed Bone Case

### Sealed Marrow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב אַוְיָא סָבָא מֵרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: קוּלִית סְתוּמָה, לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, מַהוּ שֶׁתְּטַמֵּא? אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אֶת שֶׁבָּא לִכְלַל מַגָּע – בָּא לִכְלַל מַשָּׂא, לֹא בָּא לִכְלַל מַגָּע – לֹא בָּא לִכְלַל מַשָּׂא, וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּבָא לִכְלַל מַגָּע מִלְּפָנָיו. אוֹ דִלְמָא לֵית לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** §The Gemara continues to discuss the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael. **Rav Avya the Elder asked Rabba bar Rav Huna:** The mishna below teaches that with regard to **a sealed thigh bone** of an unslaughtered carcass and of a creeping animal, where the bone is intact to the extent that there is no access to the marrow, which contains marrow inside but no flesh outside, one who touches it remains ritually pure because it does not enter the category of impurity via contact. **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, what is** the *halakha* as to whether it **imparts impurity** via carrying? Perhaps **Rabbi Yishmael maintains** the principle: **That which enters the category of** impurity via **contact, enters the category of** impurity via **carrying; that which does not enter the category of** impurity via **contact, does not enter the category of** impurity via **carrying. And** therefore, **here,** in the *baraita* cited above, **this is the reason** that Rabbi Yishmael taught that one who carries a hide that has upon it two half olive-bulks of flesh becomes impure even though it does not enter the category of impurity via contact if one touched the outside of the hide: It is **because it enters the category of** impurity via **contact** if one directly touched the flesh **inside** the hide. Therefore, a sealed thigh bone, which does not enter the category of impurity via contact in any manner, does not enter the category of impurity via carrying either. **Or perhaps** Rabbi Yishmael **does not maintain** this principle, and he would maintain that one who carries a hide that has upon it two half olive-bulks of flesh becomes impure even if it were not the fact that it enters the category of impurity via contact if one directly touched the flesh inside the hide. And therefore, Rabbi Yishmael holds that a sealed thigh bone imparts impurity via carrying even though it does not enter the category of impurity via contact in any manner.

Rav Avia Saba poses a question to Rabba bar bar Rav Huna that presses the principle of Rabbi Akiva to its limit. Consider a *kulis stuma* — a sealed thigh-bone with a *kezayit* of marrow inside it. The marrow is *neveilah* and would render *tamei* if one could touch it. But the bone is closed, uncracked, and intact; there is no way to touch the marrow without cracking the bone open. The question is: does carrying the bone render one *tamei* through *massa*? If one follows Rabbi Akiva's principle — that *massa* requires the potential for *maga* — and if it is literally impossible to touch the marrow (since the bone seals it completely), does that impossibility of *maga* foreclose *tumah* through *massa*? Or does Rabbi Yishmael reject that principle, holding that *massa* is an independent category that can render *tamei* even where *maga* is impossible?

The question is further sharpened by reference to Bar Padda's distinction. Bar Padda had said that Rabbi Yishmael is lenient on touching from the skin side but would agree that touching the meat directly renders *tamei* — and that this is precisely why Rabbi Yishmael says you are *tamei* through carrying: the potential for *maga* (from the front) still exists. On that reading, Rabbi Yishmael in fact agrees with Rabbi Akiva's principle; he just applies it differently. But in the case of the sealed bone, there is no side from which the marrow can be touched at all. So the question becomes: does Rabbi Yishmael agree with Rabbi Akiva that the complete impossibility of *maga* forecloses *massa*? Or does he hold that *massa* is a genuinely independent category that requires no *maga*-potential whatsoever?

### Evasion Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עוֹרְבָא פָּרַח. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא בְּרֵיהּ: וְלָאו הַיְינוּ רַב אַוְיָא סָבָא מִפּוּמְבְּדִיתָא, דִּמְשַׁבַּח לַן מָר בְּגַוֵּיהּ דְּגַבְרָא רַבָּה הוּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנִי הַיּוֹם ״סַמְּכוּנִי בָּאֲשִׁישׁוֹת״, וּבְעָא מִינַּאי מִילְּתָא דְּבָעֵי טַעְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** In an effort to evade the question, Rabba bar Rav Huna distracted Rav Avya the Elder and **said to him:** Look, **a raven flies** in the sky. **Rava, son of** Rabba bar Rav Huna, **said to** his father: **But isn't this Rav Avya the Elder of Pumbedita, whom the Master would praise to us,** saying **that he is a great man?** If so, why did you treat him in that manner and evade his question? Rabba bar Rav Huna **said to him: Today I am** in a state best described by the verse: **"Let me lean against the stout trunks;** let me couch among the apple trees" (Song of Songs 2:5), meaning: I am tired, **and he asked me** about **a matter that requires reasoning** and careful examination, and therefore I could not provide an immediate answer.

Rabba bar bar Rav Huna's response to the question is memorable: *orba parach* — oh look, a bird flew by. He simply changes the subject. His son Rava is taken aback and rebukes him: is this not Rabbi Avia Saba of Pumbedita, whom you yourself regularly praise as a great *talmid chacham*? How can you dismiss his question so? Rabba bar bar Rav Huna explains himself: *ani hayom samchuni ba'ashishot* — today I have been supported by flasks (which Rashi reads as an expression of exhaustion). He had just delivered a major *drasha* — apparently a *Shabbat HaGadol* lecture — and was utterly spent. He simply did not have the mental energy at that moment to engage with a subtle question about whether Rabbi Yishmael accepts Rabbi Akiva's principle. The story has an appealing human quality: even the greatest scholars reach a point of exhaustion after intensive public teaching, and changing the subject to a passing bird is apparently a time-honored method of graceful evasion. The question about the sealed bone, for its part, is left unresolved by the *Gemara*.

## Ulla's Position

### Nosei Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: שְׁנֵי חֲצָאֵי זֵיתִים שֶׁתְּחָבָן בְּקֵיסָם, אֲפִילּוּ מוֹלִיךְ וּמֵבִיא כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ – טָהוֹר. מַאי טַעְמָא? כְּתִיב ״וְנִשָּׂא״ וְקָרֵינַן ״נוֹשֵׂא״, בָּעֵינַן ״נוֹשֵׂא״ וְהוּא דְּנִישָּׂא בְּבַת אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** §**Ulla says:** With regard to **two half olive-bulks that one skewered with a wood chip, even** if one **moves them back and forth the entire day,** he does not contract impurity via carrying, and **he is pure.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason?** With regard to impurity transmitted by carrying **it is written:** "And one who carries [*vahannosei*] its carcass shall wash his clothes and be impure until evening" (Leviticus 11:40). The words "and who carries" in the term "and one who carries" is **written** *vav*, *nun*, *sin*, *alef*, which can be read ***venisa*,** meaning: Is carried; **but** according to the traditional vocalization **we read** the word as ***nosei*,** meaning: Carries. From here it is derived that with regard to impurity transmitted by carrying **we require** that one **carry [*nosei*]** the requisite measure of impurity of a carcass, i.e., an olive-bulk, **and that** that olive-bulk **be capable of being carried [*nissa*] all at once,** without the assistance of a utensil. This requirement is not met with regard to two half olive-bulks that one skewered with a wood chip, which are carried only with the assistance of a utensil.

Ulla now introduces a position that appears to stand in direct conflict with the *mishnah*. He asserts: *shenei chatzei zeitim she-thavan be-kisom, afilu molich u-meivi kol ha-yom kulo, tahor* — two half-*zeisim* threaded on a twig, even if one carries them back and forth all day, one remains *tahor*. Ulla's reasoning is derived from the word *nosei* in the verse — the *Torah* says *ve-hanosei*, one who carries. Ulla reads this to imply: *ba'inan nosei ve-hu de-nisai* — we require a form of carrying in which the object *carries itself*, i.e., in which the pieces are sufficiently connected that picking up one causes the other to come along by virtue of their natural connection. Two separate half-*zeisim* on a twig are genuinely distinct pieces; if one were to pick up one half alone (without the twig), the other would not follow. Therefore they do not meet the definition of *nosei* required for *massa*-*tumah*.

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הָיוּ עָלָיו שְׁנֵי חֲצָאֵי זֵיתִים – מְטַמְּאִים בְּמַשָּׂא וְלֹא בְּמַגָּע, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. אַמַּאי? וְהָא לָאו נִישָּׂא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects to Ulla's statement: **We learned** in the mishna that if **upon** the hide **there were two half olive-bulks,** the hide **imparts the impurity** of an unslaughtered carcass **by means of carrying but not by means of contact** with the flesh; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Why** does it impart impurity by means of carrying? **This** case **does not** fulfill the requirement that an olive-bulk of impure flesh **be capable of being carried** all at once without the assistance of a utensil, as the hide is needed to carry them.

The *Gemara* immediately objects: *tennan* — we have learned in the *mishnah* — *hayu alav shenei chatzei zeitim, metamei be-massa ve-lo be-maga, divrei Rabbi Yishmael*. Rabbi Yishmael explicitly says that two half-*zeisim* on leather *do* render *tamei* through *massa*. This is a flat contradiction of Ulla's position.

### Reconciliation Attempts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בִּמְרוּדָּד. תָּא שְׁמַע: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בִּשְׁנֵי חֲצָאֵי זֵיתִים שֶׁתְּחָבָן בְּקֵיסָם וֶהֱסִיטָן שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא. אַמַּאי? וְהָא לָאו נִישָּׂא הוּא! הָכִי נָמֵי בִּמְרוּדָּד. כְּתַנָּאֵי: אֶחָד הַנּוֹגֵעַ וְאֶחָד הַמֵּסִיט, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַף הַנּוֹשֵׂא. אַטּוּ נוֹשֵׂא לָאו מֵסִיט הוּא? אֶלָּא לָאו הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֶחָד הַנּוֹגֵעַ וְאֶחָד הַמֵּסִיט בְּלֹא נִישָּׂא, וַאֲתָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְמֵימַר: וְהוּא דְּנִישָּׂא, וּמַאי ״אַף״? אֵימָא: וְהוּא דְּנִישָּׂא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** The mishna is discussing a case **where a thin** layer of flesh was attached to the hide. Despite the fact that there is not one piece of flesh the size of an olive-bulk, the two half olive-bulks are connected by a strip of thin flesh which enables the two pieces to be carried at once without the assistance of the hide. Therefore, the pieces impart impurity by means of carrying but not by means of contact. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a refutation to the opinion of Ulla from that which is stated in the mishna: **Rabbi Akiva concedes in** the case of **two half olive-bulks where one skewered them with a wood chip and moved them that he is impure. Why? This** case **does not** fulfill the requirement that an olive-bulk of impure flesh **be capable of being carried** all at once without the assistance of a utensil. The Gemara rejects this refutation: **Here too,** with regard to the statement of Rabbi Akiva, the mishna is discussing a case **of a thin** layer of flesh connecting the two pieces. The opinion of Ulla is **like** one side of a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: With regard to two pieces of flesh, neither of which measures an olive-bulk, both **one who touches and one who moves** the pieces is impure. **Rabbi Eliezer says: Even one who carries** the pieces is impure. The Gemara asks: What is added by the statement of Rabbi Eliezer? **Is that to say** that **carrying is not** the same as **moving?** **Rather, isn't this** what the *baraita* **is saying:** Both **one who touches and one who moves** pieces of flesh is impure even if an olive-bulk of flesh **is not capable of being carried** without the assistance of a utensil? **And Rabbi Eliezer comes to say:** One becomes impure only if an olive-bulk of flesh **is capable of being carried** without the assistance of a utensil. The Gemara asks: **But** if the intention of Rabbi Eliezer's statement is to qualify the statement of the Rabbis, **what** is the meaning of the word **even?** Rather, **say** the statement of Rabbi Eliezer differently: One is impure only if an olive-bulk of flesh **is capable of being carried** without the assistance of a utensil.

The *Gemara* attempts to reconcile: *Rav Pappa said, bi-merudad* — the case in the *mishnah* involves *merudad*, meat spread so thin across the leather that the two halves are in fact physically continuous along the surface. On that reading, they do constitute a single connected piece and therefore satisfy Ulla's requirement that the carried object *carry itself*. Tosafot, however, is dissatisfied: if the meat is genuinely *merudad* and continuous, it is no longer two separate half-*zeisim* in any meaningful sense, and it is difficult to understand why the *mishnah* would describe it in those terms. A second attempt is made from Rabbi Akiva's concession in the *mishnah*: *modeh Rabbi Akiva bi-shenei chatzei zeitim she-thavan be-kisom ve-hisitan she-hu tamei* — Rabbi Akiva agrees that two half-*zeisim* on a twig, when moved, render *tamei*. Again the *Gemara* objects: *amai? ve-ha lo nisai hu* — but they do not carry themselves, they are not one piece. And again the answer is *bi-merudad*. Tosafot raises the same difficulty: if it is *merudad*, why describe them as two halves? The *Gemara* then entertains the possibility that the entire question is itself a debate among *tannaim*: whether the *nosei* requirement demands that the object carry itself, or whether any act of physical conveyance suffices. On this reading, Ulla's position would reflect one *tannaitic* opinion, and the *mishnah* would reflect another. What is clear in any case is that Ulla's formulation of *massa*-*tumah* is striking, and the *Gemara*'s effort to avoid a direct contradiction with the *mishnah* is strained — with Tosafot explicitly noting that the proposed reconciliations do not fully succeed. Beyond Ulla's specific point, the *sugya* has now established two major questions: whether two half-*zeisim* combine for *tumah* by touching, by *ohel*, or by *massa*; and whether the capacity to become *tamei* through *maga* is a necessary prerequisite for *tumah* through *massa*. Both questions remain live as the *daf* concludes.
