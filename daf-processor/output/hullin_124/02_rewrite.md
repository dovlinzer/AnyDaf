# Hullin 124 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Leather & Flaying

### Flayed Skin Problem

The sugya picks up near the bottom of 123b, where Rabbi Yirmiya poses a challenge. The broader context is a statement attributed to Resh Lakish holding that the principle of a torn garment losing its *tumah* status applies only to cloth, not to leather — because leather is a strong (*chalim*) material and therefore retains its integrity even when the majority has been torn, unless it is completely rent. Rabbi Yirmiya challenges this from a *mishnah* dealing with one who flays an animal — a *behemah* or *chayah*, whether *tehorah* or *teme'ah*, large or small — for the purpose of making a mat (*l'shatiach*). The *mishnah* establishes that once the amount necessary for grasping (*k'dei achizah*) has been removed, the flayed portion is no longer considered connected to the remainder. The *Gemara* asks: why should that be so? Let us say that since the leather is strong (*chalim*), the flayed portion should still be regarded as connected to the rest of the skin — just as Resh Lakish's principle would suggest.

### Tosafot's Analogy

Tosafot is deeply troubled by the underlying analogy. The comparison that the *Gemara* appears to be drawing — between rending a leather garment and flaying skin from an animal — is, in Tosafot's view, fundamentally strained. When Resh Lakish says that a leather garment retains its integrity because of its strength, he is speaking of a garment that has been partially torn but remains connected at the bottom: the whole piece is still, in some sense, one unit. That is entirely different from the case of flaying, where skin is being actively separated from flesh. One cannot simply say that because skin will eventually become leather, the flayed portion is still to be considered "connected" to the meat beneath. Tosafot attempts an explanation of what the analogy might be, but ultimately acknowledges that it is a challenging comparison.

### Avin's Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty through Rabbi Avin's principle: *rishon rishon nafal* — as each piece is removed, it is as though it has already fallen off. Piece by piece, the flaying constitutes a continuous act of disconnection. The situation is thus categorically different from the case of the leather garment: there, the garment is being torn but remains a single connected unit at its base; here, each increment of skin removed has already, in effect, separated itself. This distinction, while perhaps somewhat circular, draws a meaningful line between a partial tear that leaves an intact remainder and an act of progressive removal that produces disconnection at each stage.

### Neck Skin Question

The *Gemara* then introduces a further challenge from Rabbi Yosef. The *mishnah* discusses the skin of the neck (*or shel tzavar*): if one has been flaying an animal from the feet upward and has reached the neck, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds that the skin remaining on the neck is *not* considered connected (*eino chibur*). The *Gemara* asks: why not? Here the analogy to Resh Lakish may in fact be somewhat better — the skin is still physically attached to the flesh, and if leather is strong, surely what remains on the neck should be considered a single unit with the rest. Abaye, however, redirects the inquiry: rather than asking from Rabbi Avin's principle, look at the end of the *mishnah*, where the *chachamim* say the neck skin *is* considered connected (*chibur*) — which would indeed support Resh Lakish. Abaye then explains that the debate in the *mishnah* over the neck skin has nothing to do with Resh Lakish's principle at all. The question there concerns a *shomer* — the skin serving as a protective covering for the meat — that is of a kind that will detach on its own (*ha'asui l'natek me'elav*). Once the animal has been flayed all the way up to the neck, gravity and circumstance mean the remaining neck skin will naturally fall away even without continued effort. The dispute between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and the *chachamim* is then about whether something that is still physically attached, but will inevitably detach on its own, counts as a *shomer*. One view holds that it is still a *shomer* because it remains connected now; the other holds it is not a *shomer* because its detachment is only a matter of time.

### Natural Detachment

What is striking about the *mishnah* as a whole, as Abaye's reading reveals, is the elegant tension between its two halves. The opening case involves skin that has already been physically removed, yet is still considered connected — because it is close to the flesh and part of one continuous piece, even the flayed section is treated as attached. The closing case involves skin that is still physically on the animal, yet is considered disconnected — because it will fall away on its own. The criterion throughout is not mere physical attachment but something closer to functional or meaningful connection, and the *mishnah*'s two rulings bracket that concept from opposite directions.

## Pottery Oven Case

### Oven Purification

Rabbi Yirmiya now introduces a new challenge to Resh Lakish's principle. If the previous analogy — leather garment to flayed skin — was strained by the similarity of material but difference of scenario, this new challenge operates in the reverse: similar scenarios of breaking a vessel, but involving very different materials. If one is going to say that leather is strong and therefore preserves its integrity even when partially damaged, the same logic might apply to pottery, which is also a strong material. The *mishnah* in *Keilim* that the *Gemara* now examines deals with a *tanur*, a clay oven of the kind common in the time of *Chazal*: a large pottery vessel, made separately and then plastered to the ground. Because it was originally fashioned as an independent object, a pottery *tanur* can become *tamei* — the *Torah* itself states, *tanur ve-khirayim yutatz, tme'im hem*, that an oven and stove are *tamei* and must be shattered.

The *mishnah* asks: if a *tamei tanur* is to be purified, how does one do so? The answer is: *chalko li-shlosha* — break it into three pieces. The reasoning is that if it were broken into only two pieces, one would constitute the majority and would retain the status of the original vessel. With three pieces, no single piece holds the majority, and the vessel's identity is dissolved. In addition, *ve-gorer et ha-tpila* — one must scrape away the plaster that attaches it to the ground — *ad she-yehei ba-aretz*, until it is fully released from the ground.

### Rabbi Meir's View

Rabbi Meir dissents. He holds that one need not remove the plaster or bring the oven down to the ground; it is sufficient to reduce the oven's interior measurement to less than four *tefachim* (*arba tefachim*). The minimum size for an oven to be considered a halakhically significant vessel is four *tefachim*; once the interior has been reduced below that threshold, the object no longer qualifies as an oven, and it loses its status. The measurement, Rabbi Meir specifies, is *mi-bifnim* — from the inside. This detail may reflect the principle that for clay vessels (*klei cheres*), susceptibility to *tumah* is measured from the interior, since pottery vessels become *tamei* only through their inner space.

The *Gemara* then asks directly: but according to Rabbi Meir, why is it sufficient to get the oven below four *tefachim*? One should say *chalim v'kai* — it is strong and retains its integrity — just as Resh Lakish would reason about leather. Even if you reduce it in size, pottery is still a strong material. Rava intervenes and reframes the entire discussion. He argues that in fact both the *chachamim* and Rabbi Meir agree, based on Resh Lakish's principle that a strong material requires complete rupture, that once an oven has become *tamei*, one must break it into three pieces and remove the plaster entirely to achieve *taharah*. The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* is not about the method of purification once the oven is *tamei*; it concerns a different question entirely.

### Preemptive Purification

The question Rava identifies as the actual subject of the dispute is this: what must one do *before* the oven becomes *tamei* in order to prevent it from ever acquiring the status of a vessel? Suppose someone knows a *meit* will be brought into the house and wishes to ensure the oven never becomes *tamei* in the first place — is there a simpler way to strip the object of its identity as an oven before the *tumah* event occurs? The *chachamim* say no: even for that preventive purpose, one must break the oven into three pieces and remove the plaster. Rabbi Meir says that in this preemptive context, it is sufficient to reduce the size to below four *tefachim*, because an oven below that threshold never attains the identity of an oven to begin with. The asymmetry reflects the underlying principle: once *tumah* has been acquired by a fully formed vessel, Resh Lakish's logic requires complete destruction to undo it; but if the object was never fully constituted as a vessel, no complete destruction is necessary.

### Functional Identity

Implicit in Rabbi Meir's position is a significant conceptual claim: if an object cannot function for the purpose for which it was made, it has no identity as a *kli* at all. This is, as the *Gemara* acknowledges, a surprising proposition. The thirty chapters of *Masechet Keilim* are largely occupied with exactly this web of questions — how closely an object must resemble its original function to retain or acquire vessel-status, what role the maker's intent plays, and how different minimum-size thresholds operate at the stage of initial constitution versus after breakage. Rabbi Meir's position here is that functional incapacity is legally dispositive: an oven too small to cook in is simply not an oven, and Maimonides reads the law accordingly.

### Breaking Methods

The *Gemara* now examines a *braita* from *Keilim* that on its surface appears to contradict Rava's reframing. The *braita* states that a *tanur* whose initial minimum size is four *tefachim* also retains its status as long as its remnant (*shiyuro*) is four *tefachim* — this is Rabbi Meir's position. The *chachamim* qualify: the four-*tefachim* standard applies to a large oven; for a small oven, even the smallest size suffices from the outset, and the remnant retains its status as long as the majority remains. This *braita* appears to show the *chachamim* agreeing with Rabbi Meir that reducing an oven below four *tefachim* renders it *tahor* — which conflicts with Rava's claim that the *chachamim* require complete breaking regardless.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing between two methods of breaking. When the oven is cut *widthwise* (*tzalkei mitzlak*), the resulting piece retains functional utility — the bottom portion with its full circumference may still serve as an oven — and in that case the *chachamim* maintain their stricter position, requiring the oven to be broken into thirds rather than relying on the four-*tefachim* threshold. When the oven is cut *lengthwise* (*gistera*), what remains is far less functional, and there the *chachamim* concede that reduction below four *tefachim* suffices. It should be noted that Rashi reads the two cases in the opposite direction — he assigns the stricter requirement to the lengthwise cut and the more lenient standard to the widthwise — and his reasoning on this point is difficult to follow.

### Braita Measurements

The *braita* continues to specify: for a large oven, the standard of four *tefachim* governs both initial constitution and the size of the remnant after breakage. For a small oven, the *chachamim* say *techilato kol she-hu* — any size can constitute such an oven initially — and its remnant retains status as long as the majority remains. The *Gemara* asks: what is the practical minimum of *kol she-hu*? The school of Rabbi Yannai answers: *tefach* — even a single handbreadth. This invites an obvious question: what purpose could an oven of a single *tefach* possibly serve?

### Play Ovens

The *Gemara* answers: *she-ken osin tanurim benot tefach* — people make play ovens (*tanurim benot tefach*) the size of a *tefach*. This is a charming detail: children's play ovens, comparable in concept to the miniature kitchen sets familiar from later generations, were functional enough within the world of children's play that they were recognized as genuine *keilim* for the purposes of *tumah*. Rashi understands the word *benot* as referring to the oven itself — it is an oven of the daughter, i.e., a child's oven — and glosses it with the term *le-tinokot*, for children. Whether such an oven was genuinely usable in any practical sense, or whether its recognition as a *kli* rested entirely on its serviceability for the purpose for which it was made (children's play), is itself a conceptual question about the nature of vessel-identity: if it is made to serve as a real oven in the world of children's play, then play ovens have their own minimum size, and as long as the object functions for that purpose, it qualifies.

One might ask: if breaking the oven into three pieces causes it to lose its vessel-identity, why does not the first break already transfer *tumah* to the larger remaining piece, and then the second break to the larger of those two, and so on? The answer seems to be that even though the larger piece may technically retain the *tumah* of an oven, it is not clear that we would still call it an oven once it is non-functional as such. Moreover, one act of breaking can produce three pieces simultaneously, bypassing the question of sequential stages. The broader principle at work — that the minimum-size thresholds for initial constitution and for post-breakage significance may differ — is extensively treated in the preceding pages of the *Gemara*.

### Reconciling Views

The *Gemara* finally synthesizes the positions. The remnant of a small oven — one that started as small as a *tefach* — retains its status as long as the majority (*rubo*) remains. But then the *Gemara* asks: *ruba d'tefach l'mai chazi*? What can you actually do with the majority of a *tefach*-sized oven? It is completely non-functional even for children's play. Abaye clarifies: when the *chachamim* say the remnant is measured by majority, they are referring back to the earlier case of a *large* oven, not a small one. For a large oven, the *chachamim* hold that once the majority has been removed, the remainder loses its status. The apparent contradiction with the passage where the *chachamim* seemed to agree with Rabbi Meir's four-*tefachim* standard is resolved by distinguishing oven sizes: for an oven of nine *tefachim*, the *chachamim* hold it sufficient to have removed the majority even if the remnant exceeds four *tefachim*; for an oven of seven *tefachim*, reduction below four *tefachim* suffices. In this special context — where the oven is being broken widthwise (or, according to Rashi's reading, in the other orientation) — the *chachamim* are lenient and permit either standard, while Rabbi Meir always insists on the four-*tefachim* threshold regardless.

The entire discussion of the *tanur* was introduced to test Resh Lakish's principle. The *Gemara* ultimately holds that the pottery oven cases do not contradict him, because the debate between the *chachamim* and Rabbi Meir concerns preemptive action rather than post-*tumah* purification. Once *tumah* has been acquired, everyone agrees with the logic of Resh Lakish: a strong material requires complete destruction to be rendered *tahor*.

## Leather Importance

### Leather Retains Identity

The *Gemara* now presents an alternative formulation (*lashon acher*) of the same underlying principle. Rav Huna, citing Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yose, restates the opening position: if a torn garment still retains enough material for a wrap (*k'dei ma'aforet*), it does not become *tahor* even if the majority has been rent. Resh Lakish then qualifies: this was said only about cloth (*tallit*), but leather (*or*) is *chashiv* — more significant — and therefore retains its identity by virtue of its importance rather than merely its physical strength.

### Leather vs. Cloth

The shift in language is worth noting. Earlier, the principle was framed in terms of leather being *chalim* — strong — which invited comparisons to other strong materials like pottery and led to a range of difficult questions. Here the language changes: leather is *chashiv*, important, a more significant material than cloth, one that people do not readily discard even when substantially damaged. Because of this significance, it is not regarded as having lost its identity until it is completely rent. This framing carries a somewhat different conceptual weight: the question is not merely physical durability but the object's standing in human use and valuation.

### Intent & Status

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Resh Lakish from a *mishnah*: a piece of *tamei* leather that had been used as a mat and acquired *tumah* from a *zav* sitting on it — once one intends to cut it into straps and sandals and applies a blade (*izmel*) to it, it immediately becomes *tahor*. This is the view of Rabbi Yehudah. The *chachamim* say one must reduce it to less than five *tefachim* before it becomes *tahor*. But in either case, the question arises: according to Resh Lakish, why does a single cut, or even reduction to five *tefachim*, suffice? If leather is so significant that it requires complete rending, surely it should retain its *tumah* until fully destroyed. The *Gemara* responds by distinguishing intent. Here, the owner has defined a very narrow, specific intended use for the leather — cutting it into straps and sandals — and that narrowly defined intent is sufficient to strip the object of its identity as a garment. A normal piece of leather, without such a clearly limited defined purpose, would retain its identity because of its importance, until completely rent. But when the owner mentally commits to a specific diminished use, that act of mind alone begins to dissolve the object's status as a significant leather item.

## Skin & Meat Significance

### Hair as Shomer

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishnah*. If a piece of skin (*or*) has on it a *kezayit* of meat from a *neveilah*, and one touches a strand of meat (*tziv*) trailing from it, or a hair (*sa'arah*) on the other side of the skin, one becomes *tamei*. This establishes the concept that even a hair or a trailing strand can serve as a *shomer* — a protector or connector — transmitting *tumah* from the meat through the skin, because it is considered meaningfully connected to the significant piece of meat on the other side.

### Rabbi Yishmael

The *mishnah* then addresses a more complex case: the skin has on it not a full *kezayit* but *shenei chatzei zeitim* — two half-*zeisim* — of *neveilah* meat. Rabbi Yishmael says: this combination renders *tamei* through carrying (*massa*) but not through touching (*maga*). The reason is that touching two separate pieces constitutes two separate acts of *maga*, and since neither half alone reaches the minimum measure of a *kezayit*, neither act of touching alone produces *tumah*. Carrying, by contrast, is a single act that moves both halves together, and since in total they equal a *kezayit*, one becomes *tamei* through *massa*.

### Rabbi Akiva's Principle

Rabbi Akiva takes a stricter position: the two half-*zeisim* on leather render *tamei* neither through touching nor through carrying. However, he himself concedes — and the *mishnah* makes this explicit — that if the same two half-*zeisim* were threaded on a twig (*kisom*) and one moved them, one *would* be *tamei*. This concession makes clear that Rabbi Akiva's leniency in the case of leather is not a general principle about two halves failing to combine. In general, Rabbi Akiva holds that two half-*zeisim* *do* combine to create *tumah*. The reason he is lenient specifically on the leather case is articulated by the *mishnah* itself: *mipnei she-ha'or mevatlan* — because the leather negates their significance. The leather is more substantial than the pieces of sub-*kezayit* meat resting upon it, and that material significance causes the leather to be *mevatel* the meat, rendering the halves as if they were not there.

### Bitul from Flaying

Ulla, citing Rabbi Yochanan, adds a further qualification: the significance of even small amounts of meat on leather is maintained only when the meat was torn away by an animal (*peletato chayah*). But if the meat remained on the skin as a result of flaying with a knife (*peletato sakin*), it is *batel* — negated — to the leather, and even Rabbi Yishmael would agree that such meat has no halakhic significance. The act of the knife, in this view, represents a kind of human discarding: the flesher removed what was meant to be removed, and what is left behind has been, in effect, abandoned to insignificance.

## Rav Nachman's Objection

### Extreme Bitul Rejected

Rav Nachman challenges Ulla sharply. He asks: did Rabbi Yochanan really say that *peletato sakin* — meat that came off by the knife — is *batel* to the leather, even if the quantity of meat is as large as a *tarta* (which Rashi identifies as a quarter of a *kav*, a substantial amount)? Ulla confirms: yes. Even if the quantity is as large as a *kenafya* — a large sieve used for sifting flour — would Rabbi Yochanan say it is *batel*? Again, Ulla confirms. At this, Rav Nachman declares: *chai Elokeim*, by God, even if I had heard this directly from Rabbi Yochanan's own mouth, I would not accept it. The claim that even a large, significant quantity of meat is *batel* simply because it came off the knife rather than from an animal's tearing strikes him as halakhically untenable. A *kezayit* of meat is a *kezayit* of meat; it should not lose its significance merely because of how it came to rest on the leather.

### Rabbi Ami's Reaction

When Rabbi Oshia traveled to Eretz Yisrael and related this exchange to Rabbi Ami — recounting what Ulla had said and how Rav Nachman had responded — Rabbi Ami was offended. He said: is it because Rav Nachman is the son-in-law of the house of the *Nasi* that he feels entitled to be so dismissive (*mezalzel*) of a teaching in the name of Rabbi Yochanan? The sharpness of Rav Nachman's rejection — "I would not accept it even from Rabbi Yochanan's mouth" — struck Rabbi Ami as an act of *chutzpah* that could only be explained by the social prestige Rav Nachman enjoyed through his family connection to the *Nasi*'s house. The story is notable for what it reveals about the social dynamics of the Babylonian *batei midrash* and the complex relationship between rabbinic authority and aristocratic standing.

### Yochanan Clarified

The story, however, does not end there. At a later occasion, Rabbi Oshia found Rabbi Ami teaching Rabbi Yochanan's statement — but this time applying it to the *seifa*, the second part of the *mishnah*, where the case involves two half-*zeisim* rather than a full *kezayit*. In that context, Rabbi Yochanan's claim that *peletato sakin* is *batel* makes perfect sense: it is only saying that when the meat left by the knife is less than a *kezayit* — specifically two half-*zeisim* in the case of the *mishnah* — it is *batel* to the leather. Rabbi Ami had understood Rabbi Yochanan's teaching to apply only in this limited register. Rabbi Oshia then says to him: you are teaching it on the *seifa*, on the case of less than a *kezayit* — but Ulla taught it on the *reisha*, on the case of a full *kezayit* or more. Rabbi Ami is startled. Now understanding just how extreme Ulla's transmission of Rabbi Yochanan's statement was, he declares: *HaElokim*, even if I had heard this from the mouth of Yehoshua bin Nun himself, I would not accept it. The same Rabbi Ami who had been offended by Rav Nachman's dismissiveness, once he grasped the actual content of the claim, made an even stronger declaration of rejection.

### Merudad Solution

When Ravin and the other scholars who came from the west (*nechusei yama*) arrived, they confirmed: yes, Rabbi Yochanan's teaching was indeed stated on the *reisha*, applying even to quantities larger than a *kezayit*. But they offered the key to understanding it: *k'damar Rav Pappa bimrudad* — as Rav Pappa would later explain, the case involves *merudad*, meat that has been spread thin. Rav Pappa's definition of *merudad* is meat that is technically a *kezayit* or more in total volume but is spread out so thinly across the surface of the leather — like the thin layer of flesh that clings to a peel when one peels a vegetable — that it has no practical significance as a discrete quantity of meat. In such a case, no matter how much it totals in the abstract, its thinness renders it *batel* to the leather beneath it. This resolution clarifies the outer limits of the principle: it is not that any quantity of meat left by a knife is automatically *batel*, but rather that meat which is spread so thin as to be practically invisible as a food substance loses its halakhic significance relative to the leather.

## Touching Sides Matter

### Front vs. Back Touch

Bar Padda introduces a distinction that refines the case of two half-*zeisim* on leather. He says: the ruling that touching does not render *tamei* was stated only with respect to touching from behind (*me'achorah*) — that is, touching the skin side rather than the meat. When one touches the skin side of the leather, the skin itself stands between the person and the meat, and since the skin is not a *shomer* for a mere half-*kezayit*, the touch does not communicate *tumah*. But if one touches from the front (*milfaneha*) — directly touching the meat itself — then, according to Bar Padda, two separate acts of touching (*yesh nogea ve-chozer ve-nogea*) do combine, and one would become *tamei*.

### Two Acts Don't Combine

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. He holds that *ein nogea ve-chozer ve-nogea* — there is no principle by which two separate acts of touching combine. Whether one touches the meat from the front or the skin from behind is irrelevant to the question of combination; two separate acts of touching two separate half-*zeisim* never produce *tumah*, regardless of which side is touched. The only act of conveyance in our *mishnah* that does combine two halves is *massa* — carrying — which is structurally a single act that moves both pieces together.

## Yochanan's Alignment

### Dosa's Case

Rabbi Yochanan further notes that his position aligns with an earlier parallel ruling. He states that Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas said essentially the same thing. Rabbi Yishmael's position is what we have already seen: two half-*zeisim* touched separately do not combine. The parallel attributed to Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas comes from a case involving *ohel* — the *tumah* of a corpse spreading under a roof. If a *kezayit* from a corpse was divided into two halves and both halves were brought into a house, Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas rules the house remains *tahor*, while the *chachamim* say it is *tamei*. Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas reasons: just as one cannot become *tamei* by being *ma'ahil* — serving as a canopy — over one half and then the other half in separate acts, these two halves, even under the same roof, do not combine. This is analogous, in Rabbi Yochanan's view, to Rabbi Yishmael's ruling that two separate acts of touching do not combine.

### Tosafot's Challenge

Tosafot raises a sharp objection to this analogy. In our *mishnah*, everyone agrees that *carrying* two half-*zeisim* together renders *tamei*, because carrying is a single act that moves both pieces. One would think that being *ma'ahil* over two halves under one roof is far more similar to carrying — both are single encompassing acts — than to two separate acts of touching. Why then does Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas compare being *ma'ahil* over two halves to two separate touchings rather than to carrying? Tosafot's resolution is that the analogy is not really about "one act versus two acts" at all. For those who hold, like Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas, that two half-*zeisim* do not combine, the ruling applies whether the act is a single one or multiple ones — whether touching, being *ma'ahil*, or anything else. The only exception is carrying, and that exception is not grounded in the logic of "one act" but in a specific *pasuk*. Even physically speaking, an act of carrying might be considered a single encompassing act just as being *ma'ahil* is — yet the *Torah* establishes a special rule for *massa* that overrides the general principle.

### Splitting Prevention

The deeper principle, on Tosafot's reading, is this: when a *kezayit* is split into two halves, those halves simply do not combine for *tumah* — whether by touching, by *ohel*, or by any other mode. The exception of *massa* is a *chiddush* derived from a specific verse, not a logical extension of any "single-act" theory. This reframing shifts the conceptual center of gravity: the question is not about counting acts but about whether a divided *kezayit* retains its power to transmit *tumah* in a given category of *tumah*-transmission. Only *massa* — by force of a scriptural derivation — treats the divided *kezayit* as still effective.

## Touching-Carrying Link

### Verse Comparison

The *Gemara* now turns to the scriptural basis for Rabbi Akiva's principle. The *Torah* uses two verbs side by side in the context of *neveilah*: *ha-nogea be-nivlatam* — one who touches their carcass — and *ve-hanosei et nivlatam* — one who carries their carcass. The juxtaposition of *maga* (touching) and *massa* (carrying) in the same context suggests a systematic relationship between the two categories.

### Akiva's Deduction

From this juxtaposition, Rabbi Akiva derives: *kol she-ba li-khlal maga, ba li-khlal massa; lo ba li-khlal maga, lo ba li-khlal massa* — whatever can make one *tamei* through touching can make one *tamei* through carrying; whatever cannot make one *tamei* through touching cannot make one *tamei* through carrying. Carrying, on this view, is conceptually a form of indirect touching — a more remote means of contact — and therefore requires the same potential for *tumah*-transmission through direct contact. If the object cannot render one *tamei* by being touched, it should not be able to render one *tamei* through the more attenuated act of carrying.

### Touching Meat Question

The *Gemara* now confronts this principle with a difficulty arising from Bar Padda's earlier distinction. Bar Padda had said that if one touches the meat of the two half-*zeisim* directly — from the front — one *does* become *tamei*. If that is so, then Rabbi Yishmael should be able to say: the two halves *can* render *tamei* through touching (from the front), and therefore Rabbi Akiva's principle should conclude that they render *tamei* through carrying as well. But Rabbi Yishmael says they *do* render *tamei* through carrying. So Rabbi Akiva's challenge to Rabbi Yishmael — that if you cannot become *tamei* through touching, you cannot become *tamei* through carrying — seems to have no force, since Rabbi Yishmael would agree that touching the meat directly does render *tamei*.

### Rava's Resolution

Rava resolves the difficulty by refining the principle. Rabbi Akiva's requirement is not merely that one *can* become *tamei* through touching the object from *some* side; it is that one must be able to become *tamei* by touching it *from any side* (*be-chol tzad*). If there exists even one side from which touching does not render *tamei* — specifically, the skin side in this case — then the condition for *massa*-*tumah* is not met, and one cannot become *tamei* through carrying either. The logical basis for this more demanding formulation is less immediately intuitive: why should the impossibility of becoming *tamei* from the skin side foreclose the possibility of *tumah* through carrying, if touching the meat directly *does* render *tamei*? Rava's resolution stands as the *Gemara*'s answer to defend Bar Padda's distinction, but the underlying reasoning requires careful study.

## Sealed Bone Case

### Sealed Marrow

Rav Avia Saba poses a question to Rabba bar bar Rav Huna that presses the principle of Rabbi Akiva to its limit. Consider a *kulis stuma* — a sealed thigh-bone with a *kezayit* of marrow inside it. The marrow is *neveilah* and would render *tamei* if one could touch it. But the bone is closed, uncracked, and intact; there is no way to touch the marrow without cracking the bone open. The question is: does carrying the bone render one *tamei* through *massa*? If one follows Rabbi Akiva's principle — that *massa* requires the potential for *maga* — and if it is literally impossible to touch the marrow (since the bone seals it completely), does that impossibility of *maga* foreclose *tumah* through *massa*? Or does Rabbi Yishmael reject that principle, holding that *massa* is an independent category that can render *tamei* even where *maga* is impossible?

The question is further sharpened by reference to Bar Padda's distinction. Bar Padda had said that Rabbi Yishmael is lenient on touching from the skin side but would agree that touching the meat directly renders *tamei* — and that this is precisely why Rabbi Yishmael says you are *tamei* through carrying: the potential for *maga* (from the front) still exists. On that reading, Rabbi Yishmael in fact agrees with Rabbi Akiva's principle; he just applies it differently. But in the case of the sealed bone, there is no side from which the marrow can be touched at all. So the question becomes: does Rabbi Yishmael agree with Rabbi Akiva that the complete impossibility of *maga* forecloses *massa*? Or does he hold that *massa* is a genuinely independent category that requires no *maga*-potential whatsoever?

### Evasion Story

Rabba bar bar Rav Huna's response to the question is memorable: *orba parach* — oh look, a bird flew by. He simply changes the subject. His son Rava is taken aback and rebukes him: is this not Rabbi Avia Saba of Pumbedita, whom you yourself regularly praise as a great *talmid chacham*? How can you dismiss his question so? Rabba bar bar Rav Huna explains himself: *ani hayom samchuni ba'ashishot* — today I have been supported by flasks (which Rashi reads as an expression of exhaustion). He had just delivered a major *drasha* — apparently a *Shabbat HaGadol* lecture — and was utterly spent. He simply did not have the mental energy at that moment to engage with a subtle question about whether Rabbi Yishmael accepts Rabbi Akiva's principle. The story has an appealing human quality: even the greatest scholars reach a point of exhaustion after intensive public teaching, and changing the subject to a passing bird is apparently a time-honored method of graceful evasion. The question about the sealed bone, for its part, is left unresolved by the *Gemara*.

## Ulla's Position

### Nosei Definition

Ulla now introduces a position that appears to stand in direct conflict with the *mishnah*. He asserts: *shenei chatzei zeitim she-thavan be-kisom, afilu molich u-meivi kol ha-yom kulo, tahor* — two half-*zeisim* threaded on a twig, even if one carries them back and forth all day, one remains *tahor*. Ulla's reasoning is derived from the word *nosei* in the verse — the *Torah* says *ve-hanosei*, one who carries. Ulla reads this to imply: *ba'inan nosei ve-hu de-nisai* — we require a form of carrying in which the object *carries itself*, i.e., in which the pieces are sufficiently connected that picking up one causes the other to come along by virtue of their natural connection. Two separate half-*zeisim* on a twig are genuinely distinct pieces; if one were to pick up one half alone (without the twig), the other would not follow. Therefore they do not meet the definition of *nosei* required for *massa*-*tumah*.

### Contradiction

The *Gemara* immediately objects: *tennan* — we have learned in the *mishnah* — *hayu alav shenei chatzei zeitim, metamei be-massa ve-lo be-maga, divrei Rabbi Yishmael*. Rabbi Yishmael explicitly says that two half-*zeisim* on leather *do* render *tamei* through *massa*. This is a flat contradiction of Ulla's position.

### Reconciliation Attempts

The *Gemara* attempts to reconcile: *Rav Pappa said, bi-merudad* — the case in the *mishnah* involves *merudad*, meat spread so thin across the leather that the two halves are in fact physically continuous along the surface. On that reading, they do constitute a single connected piece and therefore satisfy Ulla's requirement that the carried object *carry itself*. Tosafot, however, is dissatisfied: if the meat is genuinely *merudad* and continuous, it is no longer two separate half-*zeisim* in any meaningful sense, and it is difficult to understand why the *mishnah* would describe it in those terms. A second attempt is made from Rabbi Akiva's concession in the *mishnah*: *modeh Rabbi Akiva bi-shenei chatzei zeitim she-thavan be-kisom ve-hisitan she-hu tamei* — Rabbi Akiva agrees that two half-*zeisim* on a twig, when moved, render *tamei*. Again the *Gemara* objects: *amai? ve-ha lo nisai hu* — but they do not carry themselves, they are not one piece. And again the answer is *bi-merudad*. Tosafot raises the same difficulty: if it is *merudad*, why describe them as two halves? The *Gemara* then entertains the possibility that the entire question is itself a debate among *tannaim*: whether the *nosei* requirement demands that the object carry itself, or whether any act of physical conveyance suffices. On this reading, Ulla's position would reflect one *tannaitic* opinion, and the *mishnah* would reflect another. What is clear in any case is that Ulla's formulation of *massa*-*tumah* is striking, and the *Gemara*'s effort to avoid a direct contradiction with the *mishnah* is strained — with Tosafot explicitly noting that the proposed reconciliations do not fully succeed. Beyond Ulla's specific point, the *sugya* has now established two major questions: whether two half-*zeisim* combine for *tumah* by touching, by *ohel*, or by *massa*; and whether the capacity to become *tamei* through *maga* is a necessary prerequisite for *tumah* through *massa*. Both questions remain live as the *daf* concludes.
