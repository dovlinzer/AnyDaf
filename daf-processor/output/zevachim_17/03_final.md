# Zevachim 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tevul Yom Sources

### Tevul Yom Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְבוּל יוֹם מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי סִימַאי אוֹמֵר: רֶמֶז לִטְבוּל יוֹם שֶׁאִם עָבַד חִילֵּל – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״קְדֹשִׁים יִהְיוּ וְלֹא יְחַלְּלוּ״;
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that sacrificial rites performed by **one who immersed that day** are disqualified. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: It is derived **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Simai says: From where** in the Torah is the **allusion** with regard **to** a priest **who immersed that day, that if he performed** the Temple **service he desecrated** that service? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "They shall be sacred to their God and they shall not desecrate** the name of their God" (Leviticus 21:6).

We pick up two lines from the top of 17a, where the Gemara continues its enumeration of individuals whose status invalidates the *korbanot* if they perform the *avoda*. Having covered the cases of a non-*kohen*, and an *onein* — a *kohen* who has just suffered a bereavement — the Mishnah turns to the *tevul yom*: a person who was *tamei*, immersed in a *mikvah*, and is now waiting for nightfall to achieve complete *taharah*. This figure occupies an intermediate state — no longer fully *tamei*, but not yet fully *tahor*.

The baseline for invalidation of the *avoda* by a *tamei* person is derived from the *pasuk* (Leviticus 22:2), *veyinazru mikodeshei benei Yisrael velo yehallelu* — that the *Bnei Yisrael* shall separate from the holy things, lest they desecrate them. This source establishes that a fully *tamei* person who performs the *avoda* has desecrated it. But what of the *tevul yom*?

### Kedoshim Yihyu Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְטָמֵא – דְּנָפֵיק מִ״וְּיִנָּזְרוּ״, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לִטְבוּל יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **If** this verse **is not** written with regard to the **matter of an impure** priest who performed the Temple service, **as** that *halakha* is **derived for us from** the verse: **"That they separate themselves** from the sacred items of the children of Israel" (Leviticus 22:2), then **apply it** to the **matter of** a priest **who immersed that day** who performed the Temple service. Although he is no longer impure in every sense, the priest remains impure in the sense that he is prohibited from partaking of *teruma* and sacrificial food, and from entering the Temple.

The Gemara cites a *braisa* in the name of Rabbi Simai: *remez le-tevul yom she-im avad hillel minalan* — where is the textual basis for the principle that a *tevul yom* who performed the *avoda* has desecrated it? The answer is drawn from the *pasuk* in Leviticus 21:6: *kedoshim yihyu leloheihem velo yehallelu shem eloheihem ki et ishei Hashem hem makrivim vehayu kodesh* — they shall be holy unto their God, and shall not desecrate the name of their God, for they offer the fire-offerings of God and shall be holy.

The Gemara reasons: *im eino inyan le-tamei* — since a fully *tamei* person's invalidation is already derived from *veyinazru*, this verse cannot be read as addressing that case. Therefore the verse must be redirected — *tena-u inyan le-tevul yom* — to speak of someone who bears only a residual degree of *tumah*, having immersed but not yet waited for nightfall. The operative term is *kedoshim yihyu*: they must be holy, and someone in the state of a *tevul yom* has not yet arrived at full *kedushah*.

One might challenge this reading by noting that the preceding verse (Leviticus 21:5) addresses the prohibitions against making baldness on the head, shaving the corners of the beard, and making incisions in the flesh, and the phrase *kedoshim yihyu velo yehallelu* follows immediately. Could the verse be addressing a *kohen* who has violated those bodily prohibitions and thereby desecrates the *avoda*? The Gemara responds with a counter-question: *tevul yom de-im avad be-misah minalan* — from where do we know that a *tevul yom* who performs the *avoda* is deserving of death (understood here as *misa bidei shamayim*, death at the hands of Heaven)?

### Chalul Chalul Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּגָמַר ״חִילּוּל״–״חִילּוּל״ מִתְּרוּמָה; דְּפָסֵיל בִּתְרוּמָה – מַחֵיל עֲבוֹדָה, דְּלָא פָּסֵיל בִּתְרוּמָה – לָא מַחֵיל עֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As** it is **derived** by verbal analogy between **profanation** mentioned in this context and **profanation from *teruma*,** as the verse states in this context: "And not profane the name of their God" (Leviticus 21:6), and the verse states with regard to *teruma*: "Lest they bear sin for it, and die therein, if they profane it" (Leviticus 22:9). It can be inferred from this verbal analogy that one **who disqualifies *teruma*,** i.e., one who immersed that day, **profanes** the Temple **service,** and one **who does not disqualify *teruma*,** i.e., one who creates a bald spot or one who destroys his beard, **does not profane** the **service.**

The answer is *de-gamir hillul hillul mi-terumah* — we derive it through a *gezeirah shava* from the word *yehallelu* appearing in the context of *terumah*. The relevant *pasuk* in *terumah* (Leviticus 22:9) reads *u-meisu vo ki yehallelu-hu* — they shall die thereby if they desecrate it — and this is understood to address, among other cases, a *tevul yom* who eats *terumah*, who is thereby liable to *misa bidei shamayim*. Since the word *challul* appears both in the *terumah* context and in the *kodshim* context, the Gemara links the two: just as a *tevul yom* who eats *terumah* is liable to *misa bidei shamayim*, so too a *tevul yom* who performs the *avoda* in the *Mikdash* is liable.

The Gemara then draws a further conclusion from this link: *d'pasul bi-terumah mechal avodah* — whoever invalidates *terumah* also desecrates the *avoda*, and *d'lo pasul bi-terumah lo mechal avodah* — whoever does not invalidate *terumah* does not desecrate the *avoda*. This is admittedly not a perfect equivalence. The *Shittah Mekubetzet* raises a sharper difficulty: it appears that a *tevul yom* does not himself *posel* in *terumah* in the conventional sense — which seems to directly contradict the operative principle being used here. The Gemara nonetheless proceeds with this framework, using the *chalul chalul* link to import the category of *tevul yom* into the *kodshim* context, thereby establishing that a *tevul yom* who performs the *avoda* invalidates it.

It bears noting that this *pasuk* — *kedoshim yihyu* — is being read in an extremely specific and concrete *halachic* sense. On the level of *peshat*, the verse is speaking about the conduct and moral bearing of the *kohanim* in relation to God's name, not about technical invalidation of *korbanos*. The Gemara's reading is a *halachic derasha* that moves well beyond the plain meaning of the text.

## Three Cases Needed

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: לְמָה לִי דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא טָמֵא, וּטְבוּל יוֹם, וּמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba said: Why do I** need **that** which **the Merciful One wrote,** i.e., that **an impure** priest, **and one who immersed that day, and one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering all disqualify the rites they perform? Wouldn't it have been enough to teach the *halakha* in only one case?

With the sources for *tamei*, *tevul yom*, and *mechusar kippurim* each established, Rava raises a question: *lama li d'chtav Rachmana tamei u-tevul yom u-mechusar kippurim* — why did the Torah need to specify all three of these states? If one person in a weaker state of *tumah* invalidates the *avoda*, surely one who is more *tamei* does as well. It should have been sufficient to mention the weakest case — *mechusar kippurim*, someone who has completed a seven-day purification process and immersed, lacking only the *korban* of the eighth day — and from there infer that the more *tamei* cases certainly invalidate as well.

### Tamei Baseline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכִי; דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא טָמֵא – שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמֵּא בִּטְבוּל יוֹם; מְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים לָא אָתֵי מִינֵּיהּ – שֶׁכֵּן פָּסוּל בִּתְרוּמָה. בִּמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים; טְבוּל יוֹם לָא אָתֵי מִינֵּיהּ – שֶׁכֵּן מְחוּסָּר מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba explains: All three **are necessary, as** in each case there is a stringency not present in the others. Therefore, **had the Merciful One written** only that **an impure** priest desecrates the service, one might say that this is **because he imparts impurity** to others, and since the other two cases do not, one cannot derive them from the case of an impure priest. And had the Merciful One written the *halakha* only **with regard to one who immersed that day,** then the case of **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering **could not be derived from it, since** the former **is unfit to** partake of ***teruma*** while the latter is not. And had the Merciful One written the *halakha* only **with regard to one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, then the case of **one who immersed that day could not be derived from it, since** only the former **has not yet** performed a necessary **action,** while one who immersed that day must simply wait for nightfall in order to become fully pure.

The Gemara notes, and Rashi makes the point explicit, that the Torah could not simply have omitted *tamei* from the discussion. Since the Torah does not explicitly label the *tevul yom* as such, nor does it explicitly call out *mechusar kippurim* — these categories are derived through *derashot* and linkages — whenever an extra *pasuk* appears dealing with someone whose *tumah* compromises their status, the natural reading would be that it addresses a fully *tamei* person. The baseline is *tamei*, so the Torah must establish that case first, and only then can the additional *pesukim* be understood as extending the invalidation to the weaker cases.

The practical consequence is that once the *tamei* case is established as the necessary starting point, the real question is why all three were explicitly addressed rather than two. The Gemara therefore asks: *tzrichi* — all three are necessary, each for a different reason.

Had the Torah written only the case of a fully *tamei* person, one could not have inferred *tevul yom* or *mechusar kippurim*, because a fully *tamei* person *she-ken metameh* — he himself transmits *tumah* to others, a higher level of *tumah*.

### Obstacle Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵחֲדָא לָא אָתֵי; תֵּיתֵי חֲדָא מִתַּרְתֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Still, why are all three necessary? Granted, **from one** of these cases the other two **cannot be derived,** but **let one be derived from** the other **two.**

Had the Torah written only the case of a *tevul yom*, one could not have inferred *mechusar kippurim*. The reason: *she-ken posel bi-terumah* — a *tevul yom* retains sufficient *tumah* to invalidate *terumah*.

To understand this, consider the structure of *tumah* transmission. An *av ha-tumah*, such as a *zav*, transmits *tumah* to what he touches, creating a *rishon*. That *rishon* can render food a *sheni*. A *sheni* can only *metamei terumah* — causing it to become a *shelishi*. A *tevul yom* who has immersed occupies the level of a *sheni*: he can *metamei terumah* but not ordinary *chulin* food.

After nightfall on the day of immersion — the transition from *tevul yom* to *mechusar kippurim*, for someone like a *zav* who had undergone a seven-day process — the person's level drops further, to approximately that of a *shelishi*. He can only *metamei kodshim*; he can no longer *metamei terumah*. This is the weaker state.

Yet, counterintuitively, one cannot simply infer the *mechusar kippurim* case from the *tevul yom* case, even though the *mechusar kippurim* is weaker in *tumah*. The reason is that the *mechusar kippurim she-ken mechusar ma'aseh* — he still lacks an act, specifically the bringing of a *korban*. The *tevul yom*, by contrast, needs only to wait for nightfall; his transition to full *taharah* happens by itself. One might have thought that since a *mechusar kippurim* requires a positive *ma'aseh* to complete his purification — an act of *kaparah* — there is an ongoing obstacle before him of greater weight, and therefore he should perhaps not be compared to the *tevul yom*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהֵי לָא לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא? לָא לִכְתּוֹב בִּמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ – מָה לְהָנָךְ, שֶׁכֵּן פְּסוּלִים בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Which** of the three **should the Merciful One not write?** If one suggests: **Let** the Merciful One **not write** the *halakha* **with regard to one who has not yet** brought an **atonement** offering **and derive** it **from these** other two, an impure priest and one who immersed that day, one can reply: **What** is notable **about these?** They are notable **in that they are unfit to** partake of ***teruma*.** Since one who has not yet brought an atonement offering may partake of *teruma*, perhaps he does not disqualify rites he performs.

Similarly, had the Torah written only *mechusar kippurim*, one could not infer *tevul yom*: *she-ken mechusar ma'aseh* — the *mechusar kippurim* needs an active step (the bringing of the *korban*) to change his status, whereas the *tevul yom* needs merely the passage of time. One might have thought: the *mechusar kippurim* invalidates because his process has an outstanding obligation; the *tevul yom*, who simply needs to wait, might not invalidate.

### Residual Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָא לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בִּטְבוּל יוֹם, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ; דְּמַאי פָּרְכַתְּ – מָה לְהָנָךְ שֶׁכֵּן מְחוּסָּרִים מַעֲשֶׂה? סוֹף סוֹף קְלִישָׁא לַהּ טוּמְאָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** say: **Let the Merciful One not write** the *halakha* **with regard to one who immersed that day and derive** it **from these** other two, an impure priest and one who has not yet brought an atonement offering. **As, what can you** say to **refute** this? One cannot reply: **What** is notable **about these;** they are notable **since they have not yet** performed a necessary **action,** because **ultimately their impurity,** i.e., the impurity of one who has not yet brought an atonement offering, **is weak** when compared to one who immersed that day, and relatively speaking, one who has not yet brought an atonement offering is not considered to be lacking the performance of an action.

The Gemara then entertains whether one should have written only *tamei* and *mechusar kippurim* and derived *tevul yom* from the combination. The Gemara answers: *mai parchat mah lehannach she-ken mechusar ma'aseh* — one would counter that both *tamei* and *mechusar kippurim* require an active step to change their status (*mikvah* immersion and *korban*, respectively), and therefore one cannot infer the *tevul yom* who requires no act whatsoever.

The Gemara then counters this counter: *sof sof klishus tumasan* — but even granting that both require a *ma'aseh*, the *mechusar kippurim*'s *tumah* is so weak that one could have inferred *tevul yom* from these two cases. The *Shittah Mekubetzet* offers a clarification: the *ma'aseh* of immersion in the *mikvah* (required for the *tamei* person) is fundamentally different from the *ma'aseh* of bringing a *korban* (required for the *mechusar kippurim*). Immersion is an act that weakens the *tumah*; bringing a *korban* is an act of *kaparah* — atonement. These are qualitatively different acts, and therefore throwing both of them into the comparison does not yield a simple inference to the *tevul yom* who needs no act.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר מְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים דְּזָב – כְּזָב דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rabba **holds** that **a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*] who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering is still **considered** to have the impure status of **a *zav*.** The impurity of one who has immersed but has not yet brought an atonement offering is therefore considered stronger than that of one who immersed that day but requires no atonement.

Why, then, is the *mechusar kippurim* case still necessary? *Kasavar mechusar kippurim de-zav ke-zav dami* — because a *mechusar kippurim* who was a *zav* retains the personal status of a *zav*. Even though his level of *tumah* has become highly residual — so residual that he can only *metamei kodshim* — the source of that *tumah* was something very potent. His *tumah* is, in a sense, residual radioactivity rather than residual germs. The *tevul yom*, by contrast, is someone whose original *tumah* was of a more ordinary kind. Just because the *tevul yom* invalidates the *avoda* does not mean we can infer that the *mechusar kippurim de-zav*, whose residual *tumah* carries the weight of a *zav*'s identity, equally invalidates. And conversely, just because the *mechusar kippurim* invalidates — weighty though his personal status remains — one cannot infer the *tevul yom*, whose *tumah* level is higher but whose personal identity is less weighty. All three cases must therefore be stated.

## Mechusar Kippurim Status

### Residual Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים דְּזָב כְּזָב דָּמֵי – תַּנָּאֵי הִיא; דְּתַנְיָא: שְׂרָפָהּ אוֹנֵן וּמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים – כְּשֵׁרָה. יוֹסֵף הַבַּבְלִי אוֹמֵר: אוֹנֵן – כְּשֵׁרָה, מְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים – פְּסוּלָה. מַאי, לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – מָר סָבַר: מְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים דְּזָב כְּזָב דָּמֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: לָאו כְּזָב דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** the matter of whether **a *zav* who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering is still **considered a *zav* is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **an acute mourner or one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering **burned** the red heifer, it is **fit. Yosef the Babylonian says:** If **an acute mourner** burned it, it is **fit,** but if **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering burned it, it is **disqualified. What, is it not that they disagree with regard to this:** One **Sage,** Yosef the Babylonian, **holds** that **a *zav* who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering **is considered** a full-fledged ***zav*** and therefore disqualifies the red heifer, **and** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that a *zav* who has not yet brought an atonement offering **is not considered a *zav*,** but is instead considered like one who immersed that day, who is fit to burn the red heifer?

The Gemara has now invoked the principle *mechusar kippurim de-zav ke-zav dami* — that a *mechusar kippurim* who was a *zav* retains the personal status of a *zav*. This is a conceptually striking claim. The *mechusar kippurim* has completed his seven-day count, he has immersed, nightfall has passed — the only thing separating him from complete *taharah* is the bringing of a *korban* on the eighth day. His *tumah* in practical terms is extremely weak: it can only *metamei kodshim*, not *terumah*, not ordinary food. And yet the Gemara asserts that in some sense he retains the identity of a *zav*.

What does this residual identity entail? Two *nafka minot* are considered. The first — raised by *Tosafos* though *Tosafos* believes everyone actually agrees on it — concerns a person who is *mechusar kippurim* when a *korban pesach* is being brought in a state of *tumah*. The rule is that when the entire community is *tamei met* and the *korban pesach* is offered in *tumah* (*tumah hutrah betzibur*), a *zav* — someone whose *tumah* derives from the body itself (*tumah ha-yotzei mi-gufo*) — is nonetheless excluded and cannot bring the *korban pesach* together with them. The question is: if someone was a *zav*, completed his process, and is now merely *mechusar kippurim*, can he join the community in the *korban pesach* under *tumah hutrah betzibur*? If *mechusar kippurim ke-zav dami* — if his personal status is still that of a *zav* — he remains excluded.

A second, highly practical *nafka mina* concerns access to Har HaBayit. Those who advise visitors to ascend the Temple Mount in an *al-pi halacha* fashion note that ordinary *tamei met* status does not disqualify one from *Machane Leviyah* — the broader precincts of the Temple Mount — whereas *tumah ha-yotzei mi-gufo* (such as *ba'al keri*, *niddah*, *zav*) does. The common guidance is that men should immerse in a *mikvah* before ascending. But here is the subtlety: if someone was a *ba'al keri* and immersed that morning, he is now a *tevul yom*. If his *tevul yom* status carries with it the residual personal identity of a *ba'al keri*, he may still be disqualified from ascending the Temple Mount. *Rashi* considers this the relevant *nafka mina* of the debate; *Tosafos* disagrees with *Rashi* and believes that on this particular question all opinions agree that he is disqualified.

In any case, the conceptual question is clear: when a person has gone through most of a purification process but has not fully concluded it, does their personal identity (*b'gavra*) continue to reflect their former status? Or has that earlier identity dissolved, leaving behind only a weak, impersonal residue of *tumah*?

### Parah Adumah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא – כְּזָב דָּמֵי; וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִזָּה הַטָּהֹר״ – מִכְּלָל שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא; לִימֵּד עַל טְבוּל יוֹם שֶׁכָּשֵׁר בַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No, everyone** agrees that he **is considered a *zav*, and here** the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to this** matter, **as it is written** with regard to the rite of the red heifer: **"And the pure person shall sprinkle"** the water of purification (Numbers 19:19). The preceding verse already states that the one performing the service must be ritually pure. Therefore, by stating "pure" this verse emphasizes that he needs be pure enough only to perform the rite of the red heifer specifically. **By inference,** one derives **that he** may **be impure** in some way that disqualifies him for other rites. This **teaches that one who immersed that day is fit** to participate **in** the rite of the red **heifer.**

The Gemara now presents a *braisa* that reveals this to be a dispute among *Tannaim*. The context is the *parah adumah*. A *braisa* states: *srefah onen u-mechusar kippurim kesherah* — if the *parah adumah* was burned by a *kohen* who was an *onein* or a *kohen* who was *mechusar kippurim*, the burning is valid. Yosef HaBavli disagrees in part: *onen kasher, mechusar kippurim pesulah* — an *onein* is valid, but a *mechusar kippurim* who burned the *parah adumah* has invalidated it.

The *parah adumah* is not a *korban* offered inside the *Beis HaMikdash*; it belongs to the category of *kodshei bedek ha-bayis* and is performed outside. For this reason, the standard disqualifications that apply to *avoda* inside the *Mikdash* — such as the disqualification of an *onein* — do not automatically apply. Nevertheless, the *parah adumah* does carry its own requirement: the *pasuk* describing it refers to the person performing it as *hatahor* — one who is *tahor*.

The initial reading of the *braisa* suggests that the debate between the first *tanna* and Yosef HaBavli tracks precisely the question of *mechusar kippurim ke-zav dami*: one holds that the *mechusar kippurim* retains the identity of a *zav* and therefore cannot be considered *tahor* for purposes of *parah adumah*; the other holds he has lost that identity and is sufficiently *tahor*.

### Vehizah Hatahor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר: טוּמְאָה דְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ, וּמָר סָבַר: טוּמְאָה דְּהָךְ פָּרָשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna'im* disagree as to the extent of this *halakha*: One **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that it is referring to any state of **impurity** mentioned **in the entire Torah,** i.e., anyone who immersed that day due to any impurity may participate in the rite of the red heifer. **And** one **Sage,** Yosef the Babylonian, **holds** that it is referring specifically to one who was in the state of **impurity** mentioned **in this passage,** i.e., impurity contracted from a corpse, which the red heifer purifies.

The Gemara, however, rejects this reading of the dispute, and the reasoning is important. The *pasuk* describing the *parah adumah* (Numbers 19:19) reads: *vehizah hatahor al ha-tamei* — the *tahor* person shall sprinkle upon the *tamei* person. The Gemara reads this in a striking way: *michlal she-hu tamei* — the fact that the *pasuk* emphasizes that the person is *tahor* implies that he had recently been *tamei*. In other words, the *pasuk* is specifically referring to someone who was *tamei* and is now in the process of becoming *tahor* — a *tevul yom*. This tells us that a *tevul yom* is valid to perform the *avoda* of the *parah adumah*.

This reading, the Gemara notes, was not merely academic. The *Mishnah* elsewhere describes how the *Chachamim* would go to extraordinary lengths to frustrate the *Tzedukim* on this point. The *Tzedukim* maintained that a *tevul yom* was invalid for the burning of the *parah adumah*; the *Chachamim* would raise children from birth in conditions of strict *taharah* specifically to perform the *parah adumah*, and then on the very day of the burning they would render these children *tamei* and have them immerse, so that they would be *tevulei yom* — specifically to demonstrate, against the *Tzeduki* position, that *vehizah hatahor* includes and even highlights the *tevul yom*.

### Two Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ, אוֹנֵן וּטְבוּל יוֹם דִּטְמֵא שֶׁרֶץ – דְּקִילִי, אָתוּ בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִטְּבוּל יוֹם דְּמֵת; אֲבָל מְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים דְּזָב – דַּחֲמִיר, שֶׁכֵּן טוּמְאָה יוֹצְאָה עָלָיו מִגּוּפוֹ; לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** according to Yosef the Babylonian, with regard to **an acute mourner and one who immersed that day** after becoming **impure** due to contact with the carcass of **a creeping animal, since** they **are** treated more **leniently, they are derived *a fortiori* from** the case of **one who immersed that day** to remove impurity contracted from **a corpse,** and they are fit to participate in the rite of the red heifer. **But** with regard to **a *zav* who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, **who** is treated more **stringently, since** his **impurity emerges onto him from his body** rather than being imparted from without, one does **not** derive that he is fit to participate in the rite of the red heifer.

The context of *vehizah hatahor* is explicitly *tumat meit* — the *tumah* of a corpse, which is the subject of the entire *parasha* of *parah adumah*. A person rendered *tamei met* does not need a *korban* to complete his purification; he requires the sprinkling of the *parah adumah* ashes on the third and seventh days, immersion in a *mikvah*, and nightfall. He is never *mechusar kippurim*. So when the *pasuk* states *vehizah hatahor* — implying the performer had just been *tamei* — it is straightforwardly referring to a *tevul yom* of a *tamei met*, and by *kal va-chomer* it includes the lighter forms of *tumah* (such as *tevul yom* of a *tamei sheretz* or *ba'al keri*).

The question is whether this allowance extends to a *mechusar kippurim de-zav*. Here the *parah adumah* context creates a genuine debate:

The first position (*tumah de-chol ha-Torah kulluh*) holds that the verse's allowance of someone who was recently *tamei* extends to all forms of *tumah* across the Torah — including a *mechusar kippurim* who was formerly a *zav*. Any person who was *tamei* and is now in an advanced stage of *taharah* may perform the *avoda* of the *parah adumah*.

The second position (*tumah de-hach parshah*) holds that the allowance is contextually limited to the *tumah* discussed in this specific *parasha* — that is, *tumat meit*. Since a *mechusar kippurim de-zav* derives from a different and in certain respects weightier form of *tumah* — specifically *tumah ha-yotzei mi-gufo*, *tumah* that issues from the body — it is not encompassed by *vehizah hatahor*. The *zav* is indeed weightier in certain respects: one who is *tamei met* may still enter *Machaneh Leviyah*, the precincts of Har HaBayit, whereas a *zav* or *ba'al keri* may not. And the *mechusar kippurim de-zav* also requires a *korban* to complete his purification — a more demanding threshold. Therefore, a *mechusar kippurim de-zav* who performs the *avoda* of the *parah adumah* has invalidated it.

### Resolution

The Gemara concludes its analysis by clarifying that the debate between the first *tanna* and Yosef HaBavli is *not* about the general question of whether *mechusar kippurim ke-zav dami* as a matter of personal status. Everyone may well agree that the *mechusar kippurim de-zav* retains the personal identity of a *zav* in general. The debate is specifically and only about the reach of the *pasuk* *vehizah hatahor*: does the allowance it extends to those who were recently *tamei* encompass even someone with the identity and history of a *zav*, or is its scope limited to the *tumah* of this *parasha*?

This distinction is important. Questions about ascending Har HaBayit, or about eligibility for *korban pesach be-tumah*, are separate *nafka minot* that depend on the general question of *mechusar kippurim ke-zav dami* as a personal status. The *parah adumah* debate, by contrast, is about a specific exegetical question: how far does the *pasuk*'s special leniency extend? These are two different discussions, and the resolution of the *braisa* does not resolve the broader dispute.

## Mechusar Begadim

### Bigdei Kehunah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וּמָטוּ בָּהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְחָגַרְתָּ אוֹתָם אַבְנֵט אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו, וְחָבַשְׁתָּ לָהֶם מִגְבָּעֹת, וְהָיְתָה לָהֶם כְּהֻנָּה לְחֻקַּת עוֹלָם״ – בִּזְמַן שֶׁבִּגְדֵיהֶם עֲלֵיהֶם, כְּהוּנָּתָם עֲלֵיהֶם; אֵין בִּגְדֵיהֶם עֲלֵיהֶם, אֵין כְּהוּנָּתָם עֲלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that a priest **lacking** the requisite priestly **vestments** disqualifies the rites he performs. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **Rabbi Avuh says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says, and** some **determined it** to be stated **in the name of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon: As the verse states: "And you shall gird them with belts, Aaron and his sons, and bind mitres on them; and they shall have the priesthood by a perpetual statute"** (Exodus 29:9). The verse indicates that **when their vestments are on them, their priesthood is upon them,** but if **their vestments are not on them, their priesthood is not upon them** and their rites are disqualified.

The Gemara now turns to the case of *mechusar begadim* — a *kohen* who is not wearing the full complement of *bigdei kehunah*. Rav Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, transmitted also in the name of Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon, derives the rule from the *pasuk* in Exodus 29:9: *ve-chagarta osam avnet Aharon u-vanav ve-chavashta lahem migbaos ve-hayesa lahem kehunah le-chukas olam* — and you shall gird them with the belt, Aharon and his sons, and put the miters upon them, and the *kehunah* shall be unto them an everlasting statute.

### Garments Create Status

The principle derived from this *pasuk* is: *bizman she-bigdeihem aleihem kehunasam aleihem, ein bigdeihem aleihem ein kehunasam aleihem* — when their garments are upon them, their *kehunah* is upon them; when their garments are not upon them, their *kehunah* is not upon them, and they are therefore like a *zar* — a non-*kohen* — performing the *avoda*.

This makes intuitive sense. The entire ritual of donning the *bigdei kehunah* — described both in *Shemos* and in the seven-day inauguration process (*miluim*) — was the mechanism by which the *kohanim* were incorporated into the apparatus of the *Mikdash*. Just as the *Mikdash* itself was anointed and sanctified, so too the *kohanim* were anointed and invested through their garments. The *kohen gadol*, when there is no *shemen ha-mishchah*, achieves his status through *merubeh begadim* — wearing the eight garments — as discussed in *Horayos*. The garments are not merely a uniform; they are constitutive of the *kohen*'s functional identity as part of the *Mikdash* service.

### Scope of Ruling

One should not misread the principle *ein bigdeihem aleihem ein kehunasam aleihem* as though it dissolves the *kehunah* status of a *kohen* for all purposes. Throughout *Shas*, the *Gemara* consistently applies the restrictions of *kehunah* — the prohibition against marrying a *gerushah*, the restrictions related to *tumat meit* — without any suggestion that these depend on wearing *bigdei kehunah*. The principle applies specifically to the *avoda* in the *Mikdash*.

An instructive illustration appears in the *Mordechai*, who cites a story involving Rabbeinu Tam. When a student challenged Rabbeinu Tam for asking a *kohen* to pour him a glass of water — invoking the *Yerushalmi*'s ruling that one who makes personal use of a *kohen* is as though he has committed *me'ilah*, trespass against sanctified property — Rabbeinu Tam responded with the principle of *ein bigdeihem aleihem ein kehunasam aleihem*. The point is precise: the *Yerushalmi* describes using a *kohen* for personal service as analogous to *me'ilah*, and *me'ilah* is a category that applies to the *kelim* of the *Mikdash*, its sacred vessels. A *kohen* who is wearing his *bigdei kehunah* functions as a *kli* of the *Mikdash*, part of its sacred apparatus — and in that context you may not make personal use of him. When not wearing his *bigdei kehunah*, that relationship to the *Mikdash*'s apparatus is suspended. The ruling is entirely about the *kohen*'s connection to *avodat ha-Mikdash*, not about his general status as a member of *shevet Levi* bearing the obligations and privileges of *kehunah*.

### Wine Pasuk Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא – מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא?! מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא – דְּתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לִשְׁתוּיֵי יַיִן שֶׁאִם עָבַד חִילֵּל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יַיִן וְשֵׁכָר אַל תֵּשְׁתְּ וְגוֹ׳, וּלְהַבְדִּיל בֵּין הַקֹּדֶשׁ וּבֵין הַחֹל״. מְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים וְשֶׁלֹּא רָחוּץ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם, מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** is this *halakha* **derived from here? It is derived from there, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that if those who drank wine performed** sacrificial **rites they have desecrated** the service? **The verse states** with regard to the priests: **"Drink no wine or strong drink,** you, nor your sons with you, when you go into the Tent of Meeting, so that you not die; it shall be a statute forever throughout your generations. **That you may put difference between the holy and the common"** (Leviticus 10:9–10). The *baraita* continues: With regard to **one lacking** the requisite **vestments and one whose hands and feet are not washed, from where** is it derived that their rites are disqualified as well?

The Gemara then raises a challenge: *ve-ha me-hacha nafka, me-hasam nafka* — is this actually derived from the *pasuk* about *bigdei kehunah*? We could instead derive it from the *pasuk* about wine. The *pasuk* (Leviticus 10:9) states: *yayin ve-sheichar al tesht atah u-vanecha itacha be-vo'achem el ohel mo'ed ve-lo tamutu* — wine and strong drink you shall not drink, you and your sons with you, when you come to the Tent of Meeting, and you shall not die — and then *u-lehavdil bein ha-kodesh u-vein ha-chol* — that you shall distinguish between the holy and the profane. From this verse we learn that a *kohen* who is *shoteh yayin*, drunk, desecrates the *avoda*. A *braisa* then extends this by *gezeirah shava*: the *pasuk* by drinking uses the language *chukat olam*, and so too by the *bigdei kehunah* it says *chukas olam*, and by washing the hands and feet it also says *le-chukat olam*. From this linkage, all three cases — being drunk, not wearing the *begadim*, and not having washed the hands and feet — are treated in parallel.

### Two Lists of Avodot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי עֲבוֹדָה דְּזָר חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ מִיתָה, אֲבָל עֲבוֹדָה דְּאֵין זָר חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ מִיתָה – אֵימָא לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **If** one derived the *halakha* only **from there, I would say: This matter,** i.e., that the rites of one who lacks the requisite vestments are disqualified, applies only to **a rite for which a non-priest is liable** to receive the punishment of **death** at the hand of Heaven, since that is the topic of the passage discussing those who drank wine. **But** with regard to **a rite for which a non-priest is not liable** to receive **death** at the hand of Heaven, **I will say** that they are **not** subject to this *halakha*. Therefore, the verse (Exodus 29:9) **teaches us** that the *halakha* applies to all rites.

However, the Gemara explains why the derivation from *ein bigdeihem aleihem ein kehunasam aleihem* is essential and cannot be replaced by the *gezeirah shava* from the wine *pasuk* alone. Had we relied only on the wine *pasuk*, we would have said: *hanei milei avoda de-zar chayav aleha misa* — this applies only to an *avoda* for which a *zar* who performs it is liable to the death penalty. The wine *pasuk* uses the language *ve-lo tamutu*, connecting it to a category of *avoda* that carries *chiyuv misa*. A *zar* who performs certain *avodot* is indeed liable to *misa bidei shamayim*, and those *avodot* are specified by the *pasuk* *va-avadetem* as *avoda tama* — a completed act: things like *zerika* (the sprinkling of blood), *haktara* (the burning of the sacrifice), *nisuch ha-mayim* and *nisuch ha-yayin* (the wine and water libations). These are the culminating acts.

The four central *avodot ha-dam* — *shechita*, *kabala*, *holacha*, and *zerika* — are a different list. Of these, *shechita* is *kesheira be-zar* and valid even when performed by a non-*kohen*. The remaining three — *kabala*, *holacha*, and *zerika* — are the *avodot* that can invalidate a *korban* if performed improperly. But the *chiyuv misa* attaches to the different, overlapping list of *avoda tama*.

Had we derived the rule about *mechusar begadim* solely from the *gezeirah shava* to the wine *pasuk*, we would have concluded that the disqualification of a *mechusar begadim* applies only to the *avodot* for which a *zar* incurs *misa* — that is, *zerika* and its associated acts. We would not have known that *kabala* and *holacha* are also disqualified when performed by a *kohen* without his *begadim*. It is precisely the derivation from *ein bigdeihem aleihem ein kehunasam aleihem* — the principle that without his garments the *kohen* is like a *zar* for all purposes of the *avoda* — that extends the disqualification to *kabala ve-eilach*, all the *avodot* from *kabala* onward that are reserved for a *kohen*. The derivation from the *bigdei kehunah pasuk* is therefore indispensable, establishing that the *kohen*'s status as valid for service depends entirely on the presence of his garments, for the full range of *avodot* rather than only those associated with the *chiyuv misa*.
