# Shabbat 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Klal Gadol: Carrying

### Hotza'ah Centrality

The daf begins at the bottom of 75b, at the *mishna*. Having already treated the *klal gadol* concerning the thirty-nine *melachot*, the *gemara* dispatched that material with relative brevity — though the remainder of the *masechet* will return to elaborate on those categories in various ways. We now turn to another *klal gadol*, and what is striking is its subject matter: rather than a principle touching the totality of *shabbos*, we are pulled immediately back into the world of *hotza'ah*, of carrying. Three chapters of the *masechet* have already addressed this topic, beginning at the very opening, and now the entire next chapter will continue there as well. The centrality of *hotza'ah* to the discussions of *shabbos* is worth pausing to recognize — indeed, the very next *masechet*, *Eruvin*, is devoted to it to a significant degree.

### Kashur Le-Hatznia

The *mishna* states: *kol ha-kasher le-hatznia matznin kamohu ve-hotzio be-shabbos chayav* — anything that is fit to be set aside, and which people normally do set aside in that amount, if one carries it out on *shabbos* one is *chayav chatas*. The abstract criterion for *hotza'ah* liability is thus twofold: first, the object must be of a type that is not ordinarily discarded — it is the kind of thing people keep rather than throw away; second, the specific amount carried must itself be a quantity normally preserved and not discarded. Even if an object is valuable in principle, a negligible quantity of it might be tossed out, and that quantity would fall below the *shiur*. The *mishna* then states: *ve-kol she-eino kasher le-hatznia ve-ein matznin kamohu* — anything that fails either criterion — *eino chayav*, one is not liable, *ela ha-matznio* — except for the person who actually set it aside.

### Objective/Subjective

This last clause is quite surprising. Almost universally, a *shiur* is an objective measure, end of story. Occasionally we might speak of a range across classes of people — the garment that a wealthy person discards but a poor person will still use as a patch — but here the *mishna* descends to the individual level: if *you* personally have set something aside as significant, then *you* are *chayav* for it even if no one else would bother with it. The example is illuminating: most people would discard a half-eaten piece of gum, but if you have set it aside for your own later use, it holds subjective significance for you, and you are *chayav*.

What is equally fascinating is the hyper-focus on *hotza'ah* in relation to minimum sizes. Every one of the thirty-nine *melachot* requires its own *shiur*, and some were mentioned in the preceding *mishna* and *gemara* — yet for some reason it is specifically *hotza'ah* around which an enormous body of analysis concerning minimum sizes has crystallized, spanning the remaining *mishnayos* here and the entire next *perek*.

The *gemara* then explains what the language *kasher le-hatznia* — fit to be set aside — comes to exclude. *Rav Pappa* says: *lafukei dam nida* — to exclude menstrual blood. A woman in the midst of her cycle discards the soiled material; no one has any use for it, and it is not the type of thing normally preserved. *Mar Ukva* says: *lafukei atzei ashera* — to exclude wood of an *ashera*, a tree used in idolatrous worship, which is prohibited in benefit and must be destroyed. On *Mar Ukva*'s view, menstrual blood could arguably be set aside — *matznia lei lashunra*, one might preserve it for a cat to consume. The response is *kivan de-chalsha lo matznia lei*: since it causes illness, one would not in fact set it aside. *Rashi* here broadens the point, explaining that feeding human blood to a cat causes the person who fed it to become ill — and *Rashi* does not limit this to menstrual blood specifically but to *dam ha-adam* generally.

*Tosafot* notes that in other *gemaras*, *atzei ashera* is actually treated as something one would set aside, since one has an obligation to burn it — the *mitzvah* of destruction gives one reason to hold onto it. Yet this *gemara* places it squarely in the category of objects with no meaningful use.

### Exclusions Debated

The *gemara* then cites *Rabbi Yose bar Chanina*, who observes that this *mishna* is *d'lo k'Rabbi Shimon* — it does not accord with the view of *Rabbi Shimon* (who is, by default, *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*). For *Rabbi Shimon*, as we will read in a forthcoming *mishna*, *lo amru kol ha-shi'urin halalu ela le-matzniyeihen* — all the minimum *shi'urim* about to be enumerated in the coming *mishnayos* are not stated for the general population. They apply only to the class of people for whom such amounts are genuinely meaningful, or perhaps only to someone who specifically set the item aside. For everyone else, *Rabbi Shimon* holds that a larger amount would be required for liability. Our *mishna*, by contrast, treats these minimums as universal — anyone who carries them out is *chayav* — while simultaneously holding that an individual who assigns personal significance to a smaller amount is *chayav* for even less.

*Rabbi Elazar* adds a further nuance: the *mishna* also does not accord with *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*, as taught in a *baraita*. That *baraita* rules that if an object that is not generally fit to be set aside — and whose standard quantity is not normally preserved — is nonetheless set aside by a particular individual, and then someone *else* carries it out, that other person is *chayav* by virtue of the first person's act of designation. *Nitchayev zeh be-machshava shel zeh* — the second person incurs liability through the subjective significance established by the first. This position, while presenting its own difficulties from the standpoint of fairness, has a certain appeal: once an individual has created subjective significance, it radiates outward and governs others as well, rather than remaining a purely personal standard. Our *mishna*, however, does not adopt this position.

## Animal Feed Sizes

### Cow/Camel/Sheep

The *mishna* continues: *hamotzi teven ke-melo pi para* — if one carries out straw, the minimum amount is what fits in the mouth of a cow, since straw is what cows eat, and the cow's mouth provides the relevant measure. *Atza ke-melo pi gamal* — a different type of fodder, the straw of legumes (as *Rav Yehuda* explains: *teven she-minei kitniyot*), which cows do not eat but camels do, goes by the camel's mouth. Since the camel is a larger animal with a correspondingly larger mouth, the minimum *shiur* is bigger. *Amir ke-melo pi taleh* — another type of fodder, perhaps grain stalks of a different variety, goes by a sheep's mouth. The principle is that one always looks to the smallest animal that eats the food in question, because that smallest animal's mouthful is the minimal unit of significance.

### Goat/Figs/Dried

*Asavim ke-melo pi gedi* — grass goes by the mouth of a goat. A goat has a smaller mouth than a sheep, and since goats eat grass, and the goat represents the smallest relevant consumer, the measure is accordingly smaller.

*Alei shum va-alei betzalim lachim ke-grogeret* — moist leaves of garlic or onion, which fall into the category of human food, go by the size of a *grogeret*, a pressed fig. The general rule is that all human food is measured by the *grogeret*. Why is the pressed fig and not a *kezayit* the standard? Presumably because a *kezayit*, while sufficient to define an act of eating halachically, does not rise to the level of a quantity that provides even minimal satisfaction, and thus falls short of the significance required for *hotza'ah* liability.

An interesting question arises: what if an item is both human food and animal food, and the relevant animal eats a smaller quantity? Would the animal-food measure lower the *shiur*? The *mishna* appears to hold that once something qualifies as human food, the *grogeret* standard applies. *Yeveshim ke-melo pi gedi* — if those same leaves have dried out and are no longer suitable for human consumption, the *shiur* reverts to a goat's mouthful, since a goat is the smallest animal that would eat them.

The rule, then, is clear in principle: identify the smallest relevant consumer of the item. If a larger animal also eats it, that is irrelevant — one goes by the smallest mouth among those that consume it. A point worth noting: the animals in view are domestic animals, those one actually cares about feeding. Wild animals that are not normally kept would not confer significance in the same way, because what gives the food its objective value is that people are in the business of feeding these creatures.

### Camel Intent Debate

The *gemara* then raises the question of subjective intent in the context of animal feed. *Ki ata Rav Dimi amar*: if one carries out straw in the amount of a cow's mouthful, but one's intention is to feed it to a camel — since camels also eat ordinary straw, even if the primary association of straw is with cows — what is the *halacha*? The camel requires more than a cow's mouthful to constitute a significant feeding; so does the fact that the carrier's subjective purpose is the camel lower the significance of what he is carrying, and thereby exempt him?

*Rabbi Yochanan* initially said: *chayav* — one is liable, because the objective minimum for a cow's-mouth feeding remains intact regardless of one's personal intent. *Reish Lakish* said: *patur* — exempt, because for this person, who is focused on the camel, the amount he is carrying does not yet rise to significance.

The *gemara* notes that at night *Rabbi Yochanan* had said *chayav*, but in the morning — *hadar bei* — he reversed himself, aligning with *Reish Lakish*. *Rav Yosef* approved of the reversal. *Abaye*, however, pushed back: *adrabba*, he argued — the original position made more sense, because *de-chazi le-para*, there is an objective reality that this amount is fit for a cow. How does the carrier's subjective desire to feed a camel undo an objective minimum that exists for the rest of the world?

*Ravin*, upon arriving from *Eretz Yisrael*, then clarified how the debate was actually formulated. When one carries out straw in the amount of a cow's mouthful to feed a camel — *de-kulei alma lo peligi de-chayav*: there is no dispute; everyone agrees one is *chayav*. The objective minimum for a cow's-mouth feeding stands, and the intent to use it for a camel does not diminish it.

### Forced Eating

The actual dispute between *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Reish Lakish*, *Ravin* clarifies, concerns the reverse case: one carries out *atza* — the legume straw normally eaten only by camels — in an amount equal only to a cow's mouthful, intending to feed it to one's cow. The question is whether the carrier's subjective intent to use camel fodder for a cow can raise the *shiur* and render him *chayav* for what is ordinarily a sub-minimum amount of that item.

*Rabbi Yochanan* says *patur* — exempt — and *Reish Lakish* says *chayav*. The crux of *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position is the principle *achila al yedei d'chaka lo shma achila* — a forced or coerced eating does not constitute eating. Even if one forces one's cow to consume this fodder it does not naturally want, that act is not recognized as a genuine feeding. And since the act one proposes to perform with this item is considered halachically meaningless, the subjective intent cannot confer significance on the amount, and one cannot become *chayav* on the basis of that intent.

This is the key distinction: subjective reality can reduce a *shiur* — one who personally sets aside even a small amount of something creates significance for oneself — but only when the act one intends to perform with it would itself be recognized as meaningful. If the intended use is one the *halacha* does not acknowledge — such as force-feeding an animal something it cannot properly digest — then even genuine subjective intent cannot generate *chiyuv*. *Reish Lakish* disagrees: *achila al yedei d'chaka shma achila*, such a feeding is still considered feeding, and therefore the subjective intent is operative.

*Tosafot* notes that the question of *achila al yedei d'chaka* arises in other contexts as well — there is a *sugya* in *Bava Kamma* concerning torts where *achila al yedei d'chaka* is treated as *achila* — and the principle has applications in the laws of *Yom Kippur* and elsewhere.

## Non-Combination Rule

### Stricter/Lenient

The *mishna* concludes: *ve-ein mitztarfin ze im ze mipnei she-lo shavu be-shi'ureihem* — the different categories of animal feed cannot combine with one another because their minimum sizes differ. If one has three-quarters of a cow's-mouth measure of straw and supplements it with some *atza* — the legume fodder measured by a camel's mouth — the two quantities cannot be added together to reach the cow's-mouth minimum. The *mishna* thus rules that combination across categories of differing *shi'urim* is not permitted.

*Rav Yosef bar Chanina* then qualifies this principle: *ein mitztarfin la-chamur she-bahem aval mitztarfin la-kal she-bahem* — one cannot combine to the more stringent category, but one can combine to the more lenient. The "more stringent" category here is the one with the smaller minimum — the cow's-mouth measure — because it takes less to reach liability. The "more lenient" is the larger minimum — the camel's mouth — because more is required. So one cannot use camel-fodder to supplement a sub-minimum amount of cow-straw to reach the cow's-mouth *shiur*. But one can use cow-straw to supplement a sub-minimum amount of camel-fodder to reach the camel's-mouth *shiur*, because the stricter material is being deployed toward a more lenient threshold.

What is notable at this point is that the analysis has shifted from the concrete reality of what the camel would actually eat toward a more formal register. The *gemara* is no longer asking whether the camel would consume the combined mixture — it might not eat the cow-straw at all — but is instead applying an abstract rule: the stricter category may be used toward the more lenient. This is worth flagging as a significant structural feature of how *halacha* operates.

### Common Shiur

The *gemara* now challenges this principle with a *mishna* from the laws of *tumah*. The *mishna* there teaches: *ha-beged gimal al gimal* — a cloth garment must be three by three *tefachim* to become susceptible to *tumas moshav* (the impurity conveyed by the seat or lying-place of a *zav*); *ha-sak arba al arba* — sackcloth requires four by four; *ha-or chamesh al chamesh* — leather, five by five; *mapatz shesh al shesh* — a mat, six by six *tefachim*. A *baraita* then teaches that *ha-beged ve-ha-sak ve-ha-sak ve-ha-or ve-ha-or ve-ha-mapatz mitztarfin ze im ze* — cloth and sackcloth, sackcloth and leather, leather and matting can combine across categories. This seems to contradict the rule that different *shi'urim* cannot combine.

*Rabbi Shimon* explains why combination is permitted there: *mipnei she-re'uyim le-tamei moshav* — because they are all fit to convey the impurity of *moshav*. At first glance this sounds circular — we are trying to determine whether the combined amount creates a sufficient *moshav* item, and we are told they combine because they can create a *moshav*. *Rashi* (in *Sukkah*) and *Tosafot* here clarify: the point is that although each material has a different standard minimum, there exists a scenario in which they all share the same minimum — namely, if one deliberately cut out a swath of material specifically designated as a seat. In that case, even a one-by-one-*tefach* piece of any of these materials carries significance. Since in that scenario they all share a common minimum, they can combine across categories even in the ordinary scenario.

*Tosafot* adds a further nuance: combination is permitted only between adjacent categories — cloth with sackcloth, sackcloth with leather — but one cannot leapfrog a category by combining cloth directly with matting. The reasoning for this restriction is admittedly difficult to explain clearly.

The conclusion the *gemara* draws is: *ta'ama de-ra'uy le-tamei moshav* — the reason combination is permitted there is precisely because there exists a common *shiur* across the categories. *Aval eino ra'uy le-tamei moshav* — if there were no such common *shiur*, combination across categories would not be permitted.

### Formal Categories

*Rava* then applies this logic to the animal-feed categories: *hacha nami chazi le-dugma* — here too, there is a scenario in which all the different foods share a common minimum. If one intends to use any of these materials as a sample — a merchant showing a prospective buyer what type of feed is available, for instance — then even the tiniest quantity, *kol she-hu*, is significant. Since all the categories share this common minimum in the sample-use scenario, that formal connection allows combination across categories even in the context of feeding animals.

This is the formal rule operating at full abstraction: it is not that the camel would eat the combined mixture. It is that these categories share a common *shiur* in some recognized scenario, and that formal commonality licenses their combination in other contexts.

Here lies a broader observation worth making explicit. What historically generated these minimum sizes was the concrete reality of animals — cows, camels, sheep, goats — and the amounts their mouths hold. But once those minimums were crystallized into halachic categories, they became formal, abstract measures. We no longer ask in every case whether the camel will eat this combination. We ask whether the formal rules governing these size-categories permit combination. Once a definition or legislation — whether of Torah or rabbinic origin — has crystallized, one applies the rules of the category as it now stands, not necessarily the historical rationale that generated it.

This raises a genuinely interesting question: if societal norms were to shift so that, say, animal husbandry practices changed entirely, would the minimum sizes change accordingly? One could argue that, to the extent these sizes reflect living realities that must remain active, a societal shift would alter the *shiur*. Or one could argue that once crystallized, the category is fixed. The answer is not obvious, and the present discussion of subjective reality complicates it further — since uniquely in *hotza'ah*, subjective personal significance can reduce the *shiur* in a way that has no real parallel among the other *melachot*.

## Human Food Standard

### Grogeret Standard

*Hamotzi ochalin ke-grogeret chayav u-mitztarfin ze im ze* — one who carries out food is *chayav* if the amount equals a *grogeret*, a pressed fig, and different types of food combine with one another. The reasoning for combination is explicit: *mipnei she-shavu be-shi'ureihem* — because they share the same minimum. Even if the various foods would never be eaten together — a bit of peanut butter, a bit of something else entirely — they combine formally to the *grogeret* standard, because all human food shares that threshold. This is again the formal category at work.

### Shells/Seeds/Bran

The *mishna* then specifies exclusions: *chutz mi-klipatan ve-ha-garinin ve-uktzeihen* — shells, seeds, and stems or stalks of food do not combine with the food and are not themselves considered food. *Ve-subin u-mursan* — two grades of bran are similarly excluded. These are components not ordinarily eaten and thus fall outside the category of human food.

### Legume Shells

*Rabbi Yehuda* carves out an exception: *chutz mi-klipei adashim she-mitbashlot imahem* — the shells of lentils that are cooked together with the lentils are included, since in that context they are effectively consumed along with the food. The *gemara* asks whether beans (*pulin*) are similarly included. A *baraita* suggests that *Rabbi Yehuda* includes both lentils and beans. The resolution: *ha be-chadti ha be-atiki* — when the beans are fresh, their shells are cooked with them and are thus included; when old, the shells are discarded and excluded. Why are old bean-shells not used? *Rabbi Abbahu* offers the reason: *mipnei she-nir'in ke-zvuvim be-ke'ara* — because they turn dark and look like flies in the dish, making the food unappetizing even if technically edible.

The *gemara* also raises the question of bran: is it true that bran does not combine with food? We have a *mishna* stating that *chameshet reva'im kemach ve-od chayavin be-challa* — the minimum amount of flour for *challa* obligation includes the fine bran and coarse bran along with the flour proper. *Abaye* responds: *she-ken ani ochel pito be-isa belusa* — a poor person may eat bread made with bran-laden dough. Yes — but that is not enough to elevate bran to objective food-status for the purposes of *hotza'ah*. Only the individual who specifically sets bran aside as a food for himself generates subjective significance; the mere existence of a class of poor people who consume it does not establish bran as a universally recognized food. This again raises the pointed question of how broadly or narrowly one defines the population whose habits define the objective standard.

## Liquids Shiurim

### Wine/Milk/Other

The *mishna* at the beginning of the next *perek* turns to liquids, where different *shi'urim* apply depending on the liquid's characteristic use. *Yayin ke-dei mzigat ha-kos* — wine: the minimum is the amount needed to mix a cup. The wine of that era was dense and potent; it was customarily mixed with water (either two-to-one or three-to-one) before drinking. The minimum, therefore, is the quantity of this unmixed, dense wine sufficient to produce a full cup of mixed wine — roughly a mouthful. *Chalav ke-dei gmi'a* — milk: the minimum is a swallow. *Devash ke-dei litein al ha-katit* — honey: the minimum is the amount placed on a wound as a medicinal treatment, either human or animal. *Shemen ke-dei lasuch eiver katan* — oil: the minimum is the amount to anoint a small limb, such as a pinky. *Mayim ke-dei lashuf bahem et ha-kilor* — water: the minimum is the amount needed to soften a poultice applied to a wound, *Rashi* specifying an eye-patch.

What is notable is that most of these liquids are measured not by drinking but by non-drinking applications — medicinal, cosmetic. Even when used as a drink, different liquids carry different minimums: wine is measured one way, milk another.

### Revi'it Standard

*U-shear kol ha-mashkin be-revi'it* — all other liquids not specifically enumerated go by a *revi'it*, a quarter of a *log*, approximately three and a half ounces. *Ve-chol ha-shofchin be-revi'it* — even waste-water, dirty or foul water, goes by a *revi'it*. The use-case for foul water, as the *gemara* will explain, exists as well.

*Rabbi Shimon* disputes all the subdivisions: *kulam be-revi'it* — all liquids go by a *revi'it*, making no distinction between wine, milk, honey, oil, or water. Furthermore, *Rabbi Shimon* repeats his broader position: *ve-lo ne'emru kol ha-shi'urin halalu ela le-matzni'eihem* — all these minimums apply only to those for whom such amounts are significant, or to those who specifically set them aside. For others, a larger amount would be needed for liability — the *mishna*'s universal application is rejected.

## Dense Wine Amount

### Quarter Revi'it

The *gemara* now establishes the precise minimum for dense wine. A *tanna* teaches: *ke-dei mzigas kos yafeh* — the amount to make a *nice* cup of wine, meaning a full and proper cup. The *gemara* asks: what is a *nice cup*? *Kos shel beracha* — the cup used for *birkat ha-mazon*, which is a *revi'it*. *Rav Nachman* then cites *Rabba bar Abbuha*: the cup of *beracha* requires a *rova revi'it* — a quarter of a *revi'it* — of dense wine, *ke-dei she-yimzagnu ve-ye'amid al revi'it* — so that when diluted with water it reaches a full *revi'it*. If a quarter of a *revi'it* of dense wine, combined with water, yields a full *revi'it*, then the ratio of water to wine is three to one — three-quarters water, one-quarter wine.

*Rava* then observes: *af anan nami tanyana* — we too have taught this. The *mishna* says wine goes by *ke-dei mzigat kos*, and the *tanna* glosses this as *ke-dei mzigas kos yafeh* — a nice cup. The *mishna* further states that all other liquids go by a *revi'it*. Mixed wine — wine already diluted — falls under the general liquid category and goes by a *revi'it*. Therefore, the minimum of dense wine is the quantity needed to produce a *revi'it* of mixed wine, and since *Rava* holds that a proper mixture requires three parts water to one part wine, the minimum of dense wine is one-quarter of a *revi'it*.

### Water in Pitcher

*Abaye* raises two objections. First: a *baraita* in the context of *nida* law describes mixed wine as wine from the *Sharon* valley mixed with two parts water — a two-to-one ratio, not three to one. If the standard ratio is two to one, the minimum of dense wine would be one-third of a *revi'it*, not one-quarter. Second — and more structurally challenging: *mayim be-kad mitztarfin?* The water that will eventually be mixed with this wine is still sitting in the pitcher. If the dense wine, standing alone, cannot be drunk as-is and requires water to be useful, how does one count it as a significant minimum? The significance seems to depend on water that has not yet been combined with it.

### Rava: Intent

*Rava* responds to both challenges. On the ratio question: *yayin ha-Sharoni lachud d'reifi* — the *Sharon* wine used for checking bloodstains is simply a weaker wine, incapable of absorbing as much water. Alternatively, *hasam mishum chazusa* — that *baraita* is concerned with matching a color for *nida* inspection, not with the normal drinking ratio. For drinking purposes, *ba'ei tfei* — one needs more water, and the ratio is three to one.

On the water-in-the-pitcher objection, *Rava* replies: *le-inyan Shabbos midi de-chashiv ba'inan ve-ha nami chashiv* — for purposes of *Shabbos*, the question is what counts as significant, and this too counts as significant. The dense wine, in the quantity of one-quarter of a *revi'it*, is the precise ingredient that will be used to produce the meaningful cup. The fact that the water is still in the pitcher does not diminish the significance of the wine, because the wine is already designated for a recognized, purposive use. Just as a small concentrated packet intended to produce a full beverage is itself a significant item, the quarter-*revi'it* of dense wine is significant because of the cup it is going to become.

*Abaye* must ultimately agree that the water need not be combined — the *mishna* itself is explicit that the minimum is the amount needed to make a cup of wine, not that the water has already been added. What *Abaye* seems to have resisted is the conclusion that one-quarter of a *revi'it* is a significant amount; he inclined toward a two-to-one ratio and therefore a third of a *revi'it*. *Rava* has demonstrated both that the three-to-one ratio is correct and that the significance of the dense wine derives from its intended use, fully realized once it is mixed — and that suffices for *hotza'ah* liability on *Shabbos*.
