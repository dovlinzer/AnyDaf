# Bava Kamma 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Animal in Chatzer

### Tashma: Third Party

The *gemara* opens with a *tashma* beginning on the fourth line of 48a. The case runs as follows: someone brought his animal into another person's property without permission — *hechnish shoro l'chatzer ba'al habayit shelo birishut* — and then a third party's animal entered the property and gored the first animal. The *gemara* declares that someone is *patur*. The immediate question is: who? If one party is *patur*, presumably another is *chayav*. Is it the owner of the third animal who caused the damage, or is it the owner of the *chatzer*?

The *gemara* then states: *ve'im hechnish birishut*, if the animal was brought in with permission, then *chayav* — again leaving unspecified who bears the liability. The *gemara* sharpens the difficulty: *man patur man chayav?* Are we really saying that the *ba'al hachatzer* is *patur* when he gave no permission, but *chayav* when he did? That seems extreme — the property owner did nothing, so why should the mere fact of granting permission make him responsible for damage caused by some third party? The *gemara* resolves the ambiguity: *patur ba'al hashor vechayav ba'al hashor* — it is the third-party animal owner who is either *patur* or *chayav*. But this raises a further question: if the third party obviously had no permission regardless, why should it matter whether the first animal-owner had permission or not?

### Rabbi Tarfon View

The *gemara* answers: *ha mani Rabbi Tarfon hi* — this must be according to the position of Rabbi Tarfon. Rabbi Tarfon holds that even a *shor tam*, which normally pays only *chatzi nezek*, pays *nezek shalem* — full damages — if it caused damage in *reshut hanizak*, the private domain of the injured party. According to Rabbi Tarfon, *chatzi nezek* for a *shor tam* applies only in *reshut harabim*.

The key move here is that the granting of *reshut* — permission to bring the animal into the *chatzer* — functions not as a matter of simple courtesy, but as a legal determination of domain status. When the property owner grants permission for another's animal to be present, the domain effectively takes on characteristics of *reshut harabim* from the perspective of the animal owner. Without that permission, the animal's presence is unauthorized, and the *chatzer* retains its status as *chatzer hanizak* — making it a case where the third party's goring animal would owe *nezek shalem* under Rabbi Tarfon's rule. With permission, it becomes more like *reshut harabim*, and the third-party owner pays only *chatzi nezek*.

One might object that technically *chatzer hanizak* should mean the property of the person who suffered the loss — and here it is not the first animal-owner's property at all. The *gemara* acknowledges the force of this point. The resolution is that the grant of *reshut* confers a kind of partial ownership, somewhat analogous to having been granted the right to place produce in another's domain, which itself resembles a form of *shutafut* — partnership. The *rishonim* also note the distinction between *chatzer hashutfim* where one partner damages another, versus this case involving a third party's animal operating in jointly-used space.

It is worth noting, of course, that Rabbi Tarfon's position was ultimately rejected. The fact that this *braita* can only be made sensible by reading it through Rabbi Tarfon's *shitta* is itself a difficulty. According to the *Rabbanan* who disagree with Rabbi Tarfon — holding that a *shor tam* pays only *chatzi nezek* even in *reshut hanizak* — there would be no difference between permission and no permission: in either case the third party pays *chatzi nezek*, and the distinction drawn in the *braita* would be meaningless.

### Woman Baking Bread

The *gemara* transitions to a related case: *hahi itteta de'alata lemifa behahu beita* — a certain woman went to a house in order to bake bread there. The goat belonging to the owner of that house came, ate her dough, became ill, and died — *chavil umit*. Rava ruled that she must pay for the goat: *chiyava Rava leshalumei dmei barcha*.

One might ask whether Rava is here disagreeing with Rav, who had said *havaya lah shelo tochal* — the animal itself bears some responsibility for overeating, and a person who leaves food out cannot be held liable if an animal eats to excess and perishes. The *gemara* answers: in the case Rav discussed, the person had come *shelo birishut* — without permission — and therefore *lo kibla aleiah netiruta*: she had not accepted upon herself any responsibility for the homeowner's property or animals. Here, however, she came *birishut* — with permission — and therefore *kibla aleiah netiruta*: by entering with the homeowner's sanction, she accepted a certain level of *shmira* over what occurs in the home while she is there.

This is counterintuitive. Surely the trespasser should bear greater culpability. Yet the *gemara* explains the logic: the trespasser accepted no responsibility over the homeowner's property whatsoever. If the homeowner's goat eats her dough, that is the goat's doing; the homeowner cannot complain that she failed to guard his animal. By contrast, the person who enters with permission enters into a relational framework that carries obligations, and those obligations extend to watching over the property and animals of the homeowner.

### Grinding vs. Baking

The *gemara* raises a further complication. There is a parallel case: a woman came to grind wheat at the homeowner's property *shelo birishut*, and the homeowner's animal ate the wheat and died. In that case the *gemara* says *patur* — the animal owner is not liable. And if the animal was damaged in the process, *chayaves* — she is liable for the damage to the animal. The reasoning: *ta'ama shelo birishut* — it was without permission. The implication seems to be that if she had come *birishut* to grind wheat, the result might be *patur* — and one might therefore ask what distinguishes grinding from baking.

The *gemara* answers with a remarkable observation rooted in *tzniut*: *litchon chitin keivan dela ba'ei tzniuta* — grinding wheat does not require any heightened level of modesty. Since the homeowner can remain present while she grinds, he retains responsibility for guarding his own animal. But baking bread is different. As Rashi explains, *le'efot ba'ei tzniuta* — baking requires *tzniut* because she must roll up her sleeves while kneading the dough: *shemegaleh zero'oteiha*. Since a woman would not do this with men present, the homeowner will naturally absent himself, leaving her alone in the space. Because he steps away, *marvata dechatzer mesalkei nafshaihu*, and in his absence she becomes the responsible party over whatever occurs: *hacha ledida ramia netiruta*.

Thus, when she comes to bake with permission, the homeowner knows he will leave; she knows he will leave; and implicitly she has assumed responsibility for the premises in his absence — including responsibility for his animals. If the goat then eats her dough and dies, she is liable. If the goat merely ate the dough without dying, neither party is liable for the dough — the homeowner does not owe her compensation, since she should have guarded the goat from eating it.

Crucially, the distinction between baking and grinding only matters when there *was* permission. If she came *shelo birishut* in either case, she assumed no responsibility, and the analysis is the same regardless of whether she was baking or grinding.

## Rava's Cases

### Animal Digs Holes

Rava presents three cases, each probing different dimensions of liability for stationary damage. The first: *hichnis shoro l'chatzer ba'al habayit shelo birishut vechafar bah borot schichin ume'arot* — someone brought his animal into another's property without permission, and the animal dug holes and pits in the ground. *Ba'al hashor chayav binzkei chatzer* — the animal's owner must pay for the damage to the ground. That is straightforward. But then: *uba'al chatzer chayav binzkei habor* — the owner of the property becomes liable for future damage caused by the *bor*.

This requires explanation. How does the property owner become liable for a *bor* he did not dig? The *gemara* adds that even though there is a *drasha* — *ki yichreh ish bor velo shor bor* — that the *bor* category applies only when a person digs the pit, not when an animal digs it, still in this case there is a transfer of responsibility.

### Ravad Explanation

The *Ravad* offers a sharp explanation. Once the animal owner has paid the property owner for the damage caused by the digging — *deba'al hashor shalem lei leba'al chatzer nizkei hachafira* — it is as if, from the perspective of the animal owner, the holes have been filled in: *ukman demalya dami*. The payment extinguishes his obligation. But the property owner, having received compensation, now holds pits that are functionally his — and since he has not filled them in, *uba'al chatzer deshavkei velo malya havei lei ke'ilu chafira ihu mechayev*: his failure to fill them renders it as though he himself dug them.

The *gemara* adds the principle: *keivan de'it lei lemalyai velo malya kman decharyei dami* — since it was within his power to fill the holes and he failed to do so, he is treated as though he created the *bor* himself. This establishes a doctrine of constructive ownership of damage through neglect of a remedial obligation.

Rashi takes a somewhat different approach, anchoring the liability in the concept of *hefker*: the *bor* must in some sense belong to no one — or must have been abandoned — in order to function as *bor* under the rubric of that *av nezek*. This connects to a broader dispute between Rav and Shmuel about whether *bor* liability requires that the hazard have been rendered *hefker*.

### Animal Defecates

Rava's second case: *hichnis shoro l'chatzer ba'al habayit shelo birishut* — and again the animal was brought into the property without permission. The animal then *ravtzat* — a term that requires clarification. One might initially think *ravtzat* simply means the animal lay down. But Rav Papa clarifies: *mai ravtzat? sheherbitz glalim* — the animal defecated in the property — *venitnefu kelav shel ba'al habayit*: and the clothing or objects of the property owner were soiled as a result. The owner of the animal is *patur*.

Why *patur*? *Dehavu glalim bor* — the animal waste is treated as a *bor*, a stationary hazard. And since it is *bor*, the exemption applies: *v'lo matzinu bor shechayav bo et hakeilim* — the *bor* category does not generate liability for damage to *keilim*. Since the only thing damaged here was the owner's clothing or possessions — which count as *keilim* — no liability attaches.

### Rashi: Waste as Bor

Rashi's extended comment here illuminates several points. In a parallel formulation, Rashi notes: *ukegon denafela gamlo v'lo he'emida mechayvinan leh mishum bor* — if your camel fell over and you did not promptly upright it, it is treated as a *bor*. Even though you did not dig anything — *af al gav d'ihu lo karah, ela hagamal k'rahu* — since *bishat nefilah didehu hu*: at the moment the hazard was created, the animal or its waste belonged to you, and *v'alei lesalukei* — it was your obligation to remove it. Rashi thus identifies two criteria for *bor* liability arising from an animal's actions: the hazard must have belonged to the responsible party at the moment of creation, and it must have been within his power and obligation to eliminate it. *Kemo d'karaya ihu dami* — it is as if he himself created the hazard.

The question arises: why is this not treated as *tzrorot* — the category of pebbles flung by an animal? The answer connects to an earlier discussion in the *masechta*: the animal derives benefit from relieving itself, and therefore the waste is more analogous to *shen* than to *keren*, and *tzrorot* as a mechanism does not apply in the same way.

As for Rav's position that *bor* requires *hefker* status — *shetam glalim afkorei mafkar lehu*: animal waste is typically considered ownerless at the moment it leaves the animal's body, so even according to Rav the *hefker* requirement would normally be satisfied in such a case.

### Person Trespass

Rava's third case shifts from animals to persons. *Nichnas l'chatzer ba'al habayit shelo b'reshut* — a person entered the property of another without permission. *Vehizik et ba'al habayit, o ba'al habayit huzak bo, chayav* — whether the trespasser damaged the homeowner, or the homeowner was injured by stumbling over the trespasser, the trespasser is liable. *Heziko ba'al habayit patur* — but if the homeowner injured the trespasser, he is *patur*. That seems natural: the trespasser bears responsibility. But can the homeowner really injure the trespasser with impunity?

### Rav Papa Clarifies

Rav Papa qualifies: *lo amrun ela d'lo hava yada bei* — this applies only if the homeowner did not know the trespasser was there. This connects to the dispute between Ramban and Tosafot regarding *ones gamur* — a case of complete and unknowing force. Ramban holds that even in a case of total ignorance one remains liable, while Tosafot holds one is *patur*. Rav Papa's statement here — that *d'lo hava yada bei* is precisely what generates the *patur* — constitutes an interesting proof for Tosafot's position.

If the homeowner *did* know the trespasser was present, *heiziko ba'al habayit chayav*: injuring him carries liability. *Mai ta'ama?* — *mishum d'amar lei, nihi d'it lach reshu l'afukei, l'azukei leit lach reshu*: the trespasser can say, you have the right to remove me from your property, but you have no right to injure me. This principle is notably distinct from the *bava d'machteret* case in *Sanhedrin*, where one who tunnels into another's home with apparent violent intent may be treated as a *rodef*. Here, by contrast, the trespasser has no evident violent purpose — he may simply have wandered in, or helped himself to someone's space without authorization. In such a case, the homeowner has a legal obligation to pursue a non-violent remedy, and if he resorts to force he bears liability for the resulting injury.

The *gemara* notes that Rava and Rav Papa here follow their characteristic reasoning: *shneihem b'reshut o shneihem shelo b'reshut* — if both parties are present either with permission or both without permission, and they injure each other, they are liable; if they are accidentally injured by each other, they are *patur*. But if one is present *birishut* and one *shelo birishut* — one has rights in the space and one does not — the one with rights is *patur* when accidentally harmed and *chayav* for damage, while the one without rights is *chayav* for damage, subject always to Rav Papa's qualifier about *ones gamur*.

## Dead Animal in Bor

### Water Spoils

The *gemara* now takes up a case from the *Mishna*: *nafal labor v'hivish memav chayav* — an animal fell into a *bor* and the water became spoiled; the animal's owner is liable for the ruined water. Rava qualifies: *lo shanu ela shehivish b'shaat nefila* — the owner is liable only if the water was spoiled at the moment the animal fell in. *Aval l'achar nefila patur* — but if the water spoiled afterward, as the carcass gradually decomposed over time, the owner is *patur*.

Why? *Havai shor bor* — the dead animal carcass, once left in place, becomes a *bor* — a stationary hazard — and what it damages is the water, which is classified as *keilim*. Since *bor* does not generate liability for damage to *keilim*, the owner is exempt. Rashi explains: *kol chafatzim k'kelim* — any objects, anything other than living animals or persons, is categorized as *keilim* for purposes of the *bor* exemption.

One might ask whether the animal owner bears some responsibility to remove the carcass before it rots and ruins the water. The *gemara* does not impose such an obligation. Extracting a large animal carcass from a cistern is no simple matter, and the *halakhic* structure simply exempts the owner from liability for the ensuing damage once the dead animal has become a stationary *bor*.

### Shmuel: All Defects

This analysis, the *gemara* notes, works straightforwardly according to Shmuel — *d'amar kol takala bor hu*: Shmuel holds that any stationary hazard constitutes a *bor*, so there is no difficulty in treating the rotting carcass as such. But Rav requires that the *bor* have been rendered *hefker* in order to trigger that category of liability. The owner of the dead ox presumably still owns it — or at least has not formally declared it ownerless — so how can it function as *bor* according to Rav?

The *gemara* answers for Rav: *amrei stam glalim afkorei mafkar lehu* — as with animal waste, most people abandon their interest in the carcass of a drowned animal at the point where it becomes unusable. The implicit *hefker* satisfies Rav's requirement. However, the *gemara* then sets this answer aside in favor of a deeper one.

### Grama: Smell Only

*Ela itmar hachi itmar* — the *gemara* reformulates Rava's ruling: *lo shanu ela shehiv'ish migufo* — one is liable only if the water was spoiled by the physical substance of the animal itself, by direct contact. *Aval hiv'ish m'reicho* — but if the water became repugnant only from the smell emanating from the carcass, without the body of the animal actually contaminating the water, then *patur*. *Mai ta'ama? gerama b'alma hu* — it is merely indirect causation, and *gerama* in the laws of damages does not generate monetary liability, even if it carries a moral and prohibitory dimension.

### Ramban Challenge

This conclusion strikes the Ramban as highly problematic. In his *Kuntres Dina d'Garmi*, the Ramban argues that the *gemara*'s ruling cannot be correct as stated. The smell emanates directly from the carcass; the spoilage of the water is an entirely inevitable consequence; there is no meaningful break in the causal chain. By the Ramban's definitions, this should be *garmi* — medium-level indirect causation for which one is liable — not *gerama*, which denotes a more remote and attenuated causal connection.

Ramban notes first that the *Rif* does not cite this *sugya* at all, suggesting it may not be operative *le-halacha*. He then offers a conceptual resolution: perhaps the distinction between *grama* and *garmi* applies only when a *person* causes damage. The fine gradations of indirect causation may be legally meaningful only where human agency is at issue, but not where the question is whether an animal's carcass — itself a passive object — caused damage through an emanation. In the domain of *nizkei mamon* produced by animal-generated hazards, there may be no operative category of *garmi* at all, only the binary of direct damage versus *gerama*.

## Death in Bor

### Father/Son Death

The *Mishna* states: if the property owner's father or son was in the *bor* and the animal fell in and killed him, the animal's owner must pay *kofer*. The *gemara* immediately presses: *v'amai ha tam hu?* Why is *kofer* owed — the animal is presumably a *tam*! Who has ever heard of an animal becoming *muad* for falling into holes?

### Rav: Muad Required

Rav answers: *be-mu'ad lippol al benei adam bi-vurot askinan* — we must be dealing with an animal that had already done this multiple times and was therefore *muad* specifically for falling onto people in cisterns. An animal that has repeatedly fallen into cisterns onto people — been extricated each time — and done so three times has achieved *muad* status for this particular behavior. This is unusual but not impossible, given that *borot* in the ancient context were wide cisterns, not narrow shafts.

### Rav Yosef: Accident

Rav Yosef offers a different resolution. *De-chaza yeroka ve-nafal* — the animal saw some vegetables growing at the lip of the *bor*, leaned over to eat them, and fell in accidentally. This is a *toladah* of *shen* — the animal was acting out of its natural appetite for food, with no *kavana lehazik*. Therefore the animal would not be condemned to death even if *muad* status were reached. But the owner still owes *kofer* because the animal was *muad* for this pattern of falling, even if the falling was not in the category of *keren*. The animal three times in a row had been grazing at the edge of a *bor* and tumbled in — that repetition confers *muad* status, and *kofer* follows.

### Shmuel/Rabbi Yossi

Shmuel takes an entirely different approach: *ha mani Rabbi Yossi HaGalili hi* — the *Mishna* must be following the position of Rabbi Yossi HaGalili. What does Rabbi Yossi HaGalili say? *Tam m'shalem chatzi kofer* — a *tam* animal can pay a form of *kofer*, namely *chatzi kofer*, half the standard *kofer* payment. There is no need to posit a *muad* animal at all. The *Mishna*, according to Shmuel, reflects Rabbi Yossi HaGalili's view that *chatzi kofer* exists as a category: just as a *tam* pays *chatzi nezek* rather than *nezek shalem*, so too it pays *chatzi kofer* rather than full *kofer* for causing death. In a sense this is conceptually elegant — the *tam/muad* distinction tracks consistently across both property damage and capital cases.

### Ulla Combines

Ulla proposes a third reading: *Ulla amar Rabbi Yossi HaGalili hi de'amar keRabbi Tarfon* — the *Mishna* must combine the positions of Rabbi Yossi HaGalili *and* Rabbi Tarfon. Rabbi Yossi HaGalili provides that a *tam* can pay some form of *kofer*; Rabbi Tarfon provides that in *chatzer hanizak* a *tam* pays full damages equivalent to a *muad*. Combining these two *shitot*, a *tam* animal that kills someone *in chatzer hanizak* would owe the full *kofer* — not merely *chatzi kofer*. This, says Ulla, is why the *Mishna* says *kofer* without qualification, implying full *kofer*, rather than the *chatzi kofer* that Rabbi Yossi HaGalili's position alone would yield.

Rabbi Akiva Eiger, in his *Gilyon HaShas*, notes that he does not understand why the combination of these two *shitot* is necessary in the way Ulla presents it, and leaves the matter as *tzarich iyun*.

### Why Father/Son?

The *gemara* asks: *bishlama le-Ulla*, Ulla's reading explains why the *Mishna* specifically mentions the father or son of the *ba'al habayit* as the victim. Because it is precisely their familial relationship that establishes they were in the *bor* with permission — they are sufficiently part of the household that the *chatzer* is effectively *chatzer hanizak* from their perspective, enabling the combined application of Rabbi Yossi HaGalili and Rabbi Tarfon.

But for Shmuel — *mani irya aviv u-veno?* — why does the *Mishna* need to specify father or son? If the ruling is simply that a *tam* pays *chatzi kofer* anywhere according to Rabbi Yossi HaGalili, then the same would apply to any victim, not only relatives of the property owner. Shmuel answers: *orcha de'milta katani* — the *Mishna* is simply describing a realistic scenario, not making a legal distinction. Who would typically be found in someone's private *bor*? Most naturally, a family member. The specification carries no legal weight in Shmuel's reading.

Ulla's position, while legally coherent, requires combining two minority opinions — those of Rabbi Yossi HaGalili and Rabbi Tarfon — neither of which represents the accepted *halacha*, and which had never previously been explicitly combined. This makes Ulla's reading considerably strained. Shmuel's reading is simpler but leaves the phrase *aviv u-veno* as a mere narrative detail with no legal import.

## Permission & Watching

### Rav vs. Shmuel

The final section of the *Mishna* states: *ve'im hichnis birishut ba'al hachatzer chayav* — if the animal was brought in with permission of the property owner, he is liable. There is a *machloket* between the *Tanna Kamma* and *Rebbi* about what this means. The *Mishna* ends with *Rebbi* saying: *bekulan eino chayav ad sheyekabbel alav lishmor* — in all these cases, the property owner is not liable unless he explicitly accepted upon himself responsibility to watch — that is, unless he became a *shomer*.

Rav rules *hilcheta ke-Tanna Kamma* — *halacha* follows the *Tanna Kamma*: mere permission suffices to impose liability on the property owner, without any explicit acceptance of *shemira*. Shmuel rules *hilcheta ke-Rebbi* — *halacha* follows *Rebbi*: explicit acceptance of *shemirah* is required; *stam* permission alone does not transfer liability.

### Braita: Watching

A *braita* bears on this dispute. The *braita* presents two formulations. First: *kenos shorecha u-shemoro* — "bring in your ox and watch it." In this case, *hizik chayav, huzak patur* — if the animal caused damage, the property owner is liable; if the animal was injured, the animal owner is not liable — because the property owner accepted *shemirah*. Second: *kenos shorkha v'ani ashmerenu* — "bring in your animal and I will watch it." Here, *huzak chayav, hezik patur* — if the animal was injured, the property owner is liable; if the animal caused damage, the property owner is *patur*.

### The Contradiction

This *braita* seems internally contradictory. In the *reisha*, the animal owner says "watch it" (*shemoro*), and the property owner is liable. In the *seifa*, the property owner says "I will watch it" (*ani ashmerenu*), and the property owner is equally liable. But the *reisha* appears to be a case where only the animal owner requested watching and the property owner never explicitly agreed — a more passive form of acceptance — while the *seifa* is an explicit affirmative undertaking. One would expect that the explicit "I will watch it" creates greater liability than the implicit case. Yet if both produce the same result for the property owner, the distinction seems to generate a striking problem: in the *stam* case — where no one said anything — is the property owner *more* liable than in the *reisha* where he was asked? The *gemara* notes this paradox: *d'bistam mekabel alei netiruta* — in the *stam* case he presumably accepted upon himself responsibility, which would make him *chayav*, yet the *reisha* where he was at least asked and said nothing would make him *patur* on the animal owner's reading. This seems backward.

### Rava: Form Matters

Rava resolves the contradiction by reading the entire *braita* as reflecting the position of *Rabbanan* — the *Tanna Kamma* — throughout. The difference between *reisha* and *seifa* is not about who made a formal commitment, but about who was speaking. In the *reisha*, the animal owner said *shemoro* — "watch it" — but we do not hear the property owner agree. There was no formal acceptance of *shemirah* by the property owner, and therefore the liability remains with the animal owner. The *ha stama chayav ba'al hachatzer* that the *gemara* inferred — that in a *stam* case the property owner would be liable — reflects the *Rabbanan*'s position that mere permission itself creates an implicit acceptance. The *seifa*, where the property owner affirmatively says *ani ashmerenu*, is the clearer case of explicit acceptance, producing clear *shemirah* liability. Thus *reisha* and *seifa* are consistent: in the *reisha* no acceptance was made; in the *seifa* it was explicitly made; and *stam* permission falls in between, where *Rabbanan* would say liability attaches anyway.

### Rav Papa: Rabbi View

Rav Papa offers an alternative: *kula Rabbi* — the entire *braita* reflects *Rebbi*'s position, and moreover *Rebbi* agrees with Rabbi Tarfon that a *tam* animal in *chatzer hanizak* pays full *nezek shalem*.

Under this reading, the animal owner in the *reisha* said *shemoro* precisely because he was concerned about Rabbi Tarfon's rule. His animal is a *tam*, but if it causes damage in someone else's *chatzer* — which would be *chatzer hanizak* — he would owe *nezek shalem* rather than *chatzi nezek*. He therefore asks the property owner to watch the animal, in hopes of shifting responsibility. The property owner's failure to explicitly agree means he does not become a *shomer*, and the animal owner retains liability — but only for *nezek shalem*, since the property owner did not grant him ownership rights in the land that would make it *chatzer hashutafin*.

### Why Not Land Rights?

This leads to a question within Rav Papa's reading. If the animal owner's goal is to reduce his exposure from *nezek shalem* to *chatzi nezek*, why does he ask the property owner to watch his animal rather than simply asking the property owner to lease him a portion of the *chatzer*? If he held joint rights in the property, the *chatzer* would become *chatzer hashutafin*, and even according to Rabbi Tarfon a *tam* in *chatzer hashutafin* pays only *chatzi nezek*.

The *gemara* answers: *lo masar lei makom b'chatzer* — he did not grant him any portion of the property. Therefore the *chatzer* remains *chatzer hanizak* and Rabbi Tarfon's full-liability rule applies. The animal owner's request to have the animal watched — *shemoro* — was itself an implicit request equivalent to asking for property rights: *aknuiei aknei lei makom b'chatzer*. All the animal owner sought was a mechanism to escape full liability: either have someone else formally take responsibility for the animal, or establish joint ownership of the space. Asking for *shemirah* functioned, in Rav Papa's reading, as a proxy for the latter. Since the property owner did not explicitly agree — as *Rebbi* requires — no transfer of liability occurred, and the animal owner remains *chayav* for any damage his animal causes in the *chatzer*.
