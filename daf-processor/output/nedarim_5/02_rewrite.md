# Nedarim 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shmuel's Position

### Reading the Mishnah

The Gemara on the previous daf had been working through a statement of Shmuel, who held that the Mishnah's language was obscure. The Mishnah had listed a series of formulations — *mudrani mimcha*, *mufrehani mimcha*, *she'ochal lcha*, *she'at'oam lcha* — each of which functions as a *yad*, an abbreviated or elliptical statement that attaches to a full *neder*. The question was how to read that Mishnah: does each formulation work independently, or does Shmuel require them in combination?

### Mudrani Mimcha

The Gemara demonstrated that each formulation works on its own, which forced a modification of Shmuel's position. As refined, Shmuel holds that when one says *mudrani mimcha* — a broad, generic statement of separation — without any qualifying predicate, the *neder* operates in both directions simultaneously. That is, not only is your property forbidden to me, but my property becomes forbidden to you as well. It is only when the speaker adds the phrase *she'ochal lcha* — specifying the things of yours that I would eat — that the prohibition is directionally limited: your property is forbidden to me, but mine remains permitted to you. The added qualifier thus narrows, rather than broadens, the scope of the *neder*.

### Rabbi Yossi bar Chanina

With this understanding in hand, the Gemara brings a statement of Rabbi Yossi bar Chanina, who teaches: *mudrani himcha shneihem assurin* — if one says "I am separated from you," both parties are forbidden. This parallels the revised reading of Shmuel. But the Gemara immediately raises a challenge from the Mishnah. The Mishnah states: *hareini alecha cherem* — behold, I am to you *cherem*, meaning my property bears the status of *cherem* relative to you. In that case, the Mishnah rules *hamudar assur* — the *mudar*, the person to whom the *neder* is directed, is forbidden to benefit from the *magdir*'s property — but the *magdir* himself remains permitted to benefit from the *mudar*'s property. The question is sharp: if we interpret *mudrani mimcha* as working in both directions, why does this formulation, *hareini alecha cherem*, operate only in one direction?

### Reconciling Views

The Gemara's resolution is that the cases in the Mishnah are not *stam*, not plain and unqualified. In each instance, the speaker went on to provide an explicit clarification. For instance, when the Mishnah says *hareini alecha cherem*, it turns out the speaker also said *ve'ani alecha lo* — "but I to you, no." And conversely, when the Mishnah says *ve'at alai cherem* — "you are to me *cherem*" — that is also qualified by the addition that the reverse does not apply. In both cases the outcome was already explicit.

The Gemara then asks: *aval stama mai* — but what if nothing explicit was added? The answer follows: in the unqualified case, *hu assur vechavero mutar* — only one direction applies. This appears to stand in direct tension with Rabbi Yossi bar Chanina, who said that *mudrani himcha* generates bilateral prohibition. How can the unqualified use of *cherem*-language operate unilaterally when *mudrani mimcha* operates bilaterally?

The Gemara resolves this through a careful restatement of Rabbi Yossi bar Chanina's position: *mudrani lcha shneihem assurin; mudrani mimcha hu assur vechavero mutar*. The critical distinction lies in the directional particle. *Mudrani lcha* — "I am separated to you" — is syntactically ambiguous. It can be read as "my property is forbidden to you," but it can equally be read, by supplying a *misha* prefix, as "I am removing myself from your property as well." Because *lcha* lacks a directional prefix that firmly fixes one reading, the phrase is genuinely open in both directions and therefore generates bilateral prohibition. *Mudrani mimcha*, by contrast — "I am separated from you" — contains the prefix *mimcha*, which unambiguously establishes the direction of removal: from your property. There is no legitimate grammatical way to read *mimcha* as simultaneously generating a prohibition running in the opposite direction. Hence it is unilateral.

### Lach vs. Mimcha

This resolution, however, circles back to the original difficulty with Shmuel's formulation. Shmuel had said that one needs to combine *mudrani mimcha* with *she'ani ochel lach* — the things of yours that I will eat. But if *mimcha* already operates in only one direction, what is the additional work being done by *she'ani ochel lach*? The need for that qualifier must mean something other than fixing directionality. The Gemara accordingly offers a reformulation: *hachi itmar* — here is what Shmuel actually meant. If one says *she'ani ochel lach*, the prohibition is limited to *achilah*, eating. *Mudrani mimcha* alone, without any qualifier, would be broader — potentially an *issur hana'ah*, a prohibition against all forms of benefit. The qualifying phrase *she'ani ochel lach* narrows the scope of the *neder* to the domain of eating rather than all benefit.

## Specificity Principle

### Scope of Prohibition

This leads to a general and important principle. If someone makes a *neder* using generic language — *cherem*, *konam*, *neder* — without specifying the nature of the prohibition, the default is *assur b'hana'ah*: any and all forms of benefit are forbidden. If one wishes to limit the *neder* to *achilah* alone, one must say so explicitly.

### Achilah vs. Hana'ah

The phrase *she'ani ochel lach v'she'ani toeim lach* — the things of yours that I will eat and that I will taste — establishes that the operative prohibition is one of eating. Since *te'imah*, tasting, is itself a subset of *achilah*, citing both narrows the *neder* to the category of consumption and excludes all other forms of benefit. Without that explicit narrowing, the prohibition stands at the broader level of *hana'ah*.

### Ambiguity Problem

A further issue emerges. One might assume that *mudrani mimcha*, stated alone, at least generates a *neder* — even if a maximally broad one. But the Gemara raises a more radical possibility: perhaps *mudrani mimcha* does not constitute a *neder* at all. The phrase may not even be referring to the forbidden status of objects. *Mudrani mimcha* could mean nothing more than: I am distancing myself from you. I do not want to conduct *masa u'matan*, business dealings, with you. I refuse to stand within your *arba amos*. I will not speak with you. All of these are modes of social and personal separation, and none of them necessarily involves the prohibition of an object under the framework of *neder*. The phrase is thus radically ambiguous — it might mean that your objects are *assur* to me for eating, or for benefit, or it might simply mean I want nothing to do with you personally. Given that ambiguity, who is to say that any *neder* was made at all? It is only when the speaker says *she'ani ochel lach* — the things of yours that I will eat — that we know unambiguously that the speaker intends to forbid objects in the domain of *achilah*.

## Yad Concept

### Three Levels of Yad

The Gemara now crystallizes a general conceptual framework. There are three distinct levels to consider when evaluating an elliptical speech act, a *yad*. The first is a *yad mochiach* — a statement where what the speaker means is entirely clear even without explicit articulation. An illustrative case: at a *chuppah*, when the *chosson* says *harei at mekudeshet* but fails to add *li* — to me — the omission does not necessarily invalidate the *kiddushin*. Since the entire ceremony is oriented around this man marrying this woman, the *li* is implicit; the *yad* is *mochiach*, clear from the entire context. The second level is a *yad she'einah mochiach*: the statement most likely means what we think it means, but there is some possibility it means something else. For instance, *harei at mekudeshet* said outside the context of a *chuppah* — in a world where men sometimes send agents — might refer to a *shaliach* acting on behalf of a third party. The meaning is probable but not unambiguous. The third level is no *yad* at all: the statement is so open-ended that no particular meaning can be ascribed to it.

### Ambiguous Yad Invalid

The Gemara now asks: does a *yad she'einah mochiach* — an ambiguous but probably-clear statement — constitute a valid *yad* or not? The formulation it proposes is: *leima kasavar Shmuel yadayim she'einan mochichot lo havyan yadayim* — perhaps Shmuel's entire position rests on the principle that handles which are not unambiguous do not count as handles at all. *Mudrani mimcha* falls into this category: most likely it is a *neder*, but it might mean nothing more than social distancing. That ambiguity, however slight, disqualifies it as a valid speech act, and without the clarifying phrase *she'ani ochel lach*, nothing takes effect.

### Get Parallel

The Gemara confirms this: *ein, Shmuel mokim lah matnitin k'Rabbi Yehuda* — yes, Shmuel is interpreting our Mishnah in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda's position. For the Mishnah in the laws of *gittin* teaches: *gufo shel get harei at muteret l'chol adam* — the core text of a *get* is "behold you are permitted to all men." Rabbi Yehuda, however, holds that one must add the phrase *v'den d'yehevei lichi minai sefer teiruchin v'igeret shevukin* — "and this shall be to you from me a writ of severance and a document of release." Without this addition, says Rabbi Yehuda, the statement *harei at muteret l'chol adam* is insufficient, because it does not unambiguously identify the document itself as the instrument effecting the divorce. Perhaps the man is merely making a verbal declaration of release, and the written text is simply that declaration recorded. Only when the text says explicitly "this document shall be to you from me" does it become clear that the document itself is the operative instrument. This is a direct application of *yadayim she'einan mochichot lo havyan yadayim*.

The Gemara asks: *amai dachik Shmuel l'ukma l'matnitin k'Rabbi Yehuda* — why did Shmuel strain to interpret our Mishnah according to Rabbi Yehuda, a minority opinion? We have *baraitot* that explicitly say *havyan yadayim* — ambiguous handles do work. Why not interpret the Mishnah according to the majority *Rabbanan*, who hold *havyan yadayim*? Rava answers: the language of the Mishnah itself forced Shmuel's hand. The Mishnah says *she'ani ochel lach v'she'ani toeim lach* — things of yours that I will eat, things of yours that I will taste — including the word *lach*, to you. But why is *lach* necessary? The speaker is already addressing the person directly and has already said *mimcha* — from you. The word *lach* is superfluous unless it is signaling a principle: when making a *neder*, the language must be maximally clear and unambiguous. The superfluous *lach* is itself evidence that the Mishnah demands *yadayim mochichot*, and that is why Shmuel read the Mishnah as he did.

### Ran's Explanation

The Gemara proceeds to state the *amora'ic* debate directly. *Abaye amar havyan yadayim* — Abaye holds that an ambiguous but probable *yad* is a valid speech act. *Rava amar lo havyan yadayim* — Rava holds that any ambiguity disqualifies the speech act entirely. Rava reports that Rav Ibi provided him with the proof from the verse *nazir l'hazir l'Hashem*. The word *l'hazir* teaches, through the principle of *hekkesh*, that *yadei nezirut* — elliptical statements indicating a *nazir* vow — must satisfy the same standard as *nezirut* itself. And *nezirut* requires *hafla'ah* — articulation and clarity. The *hafla'ah* requirement is derived from the root *l'hafli*, meaning to articulate distinctly and concretely. Just as the *neder* of *nezirut* must be made with complete clarity, so too must the *yad* of *nezirut* be unambiguous. And since we learn the *yad* of *neder* from the *yad* of *nazir*, the same standard applies across the board.

The Ran makes an important conceptual clarification regarding the *get* debate. When the Gemara says the *Rabbanan* do not require the text of the *get* to be explicit, this should not be misread as the *Rabbanan* accepting ambiguity. Rather, the *Rabbanan* hold that what renders the text clear is not the intrinsic language of the statement alone, but the surrounding context. There is a distinction between a *yad mochiach* — where the statement itself, read in isolation, is unambiguous — and a case where the statement is technically a *yad she'einah mochiach*, but the context supplies the clarity. In the latter case, according to the *Rabbanan*, the *yad* still works — not because ambiguity is acceptable, but because the context provides the *mochiach* that the language lacks. The implication is that even the *Rabbanan* may require some form of clarity; they simply allow context to supply it. The live debate between Abaye and Rava, then, is specifically about cases where even context does not resolve the ambiguity — whether that residual ambiguity disqualifies the speech act entirely.

## Rava's Argument

### Nazir L'hazir

Rava's proof rests on the verse *nazir l'hazir l'Hashem*. The word *l'hazir* — to vow as a *nazir* — is the source for the principle that elliptical statements (*yadayim*) are legally operative in the context of *nezirut*. The Gemara performs a *hekkesh*, a textual linkage: *makish yadei nezirut l'nezirut* — the *yad* of *nezirut* is compared to *nezirut* itself. From this comparison follows the principle: *mah nezirut b'hafla'ah af yadei nezirut b'hafla'ah* — just as full *nezirut* requires *hafla'ah*, so too must a *yad* of *nezirut* satisfy the standard of *hafla'ah*.

### Hafla'ah Requirement

*Hafla'ah* is a technical term in this context, derived from the root *l'hafli*, meaning to articulate or declare distinctly. The word appears in connection with speech acts — *neder* and *shevuah* — and it conveys the requirement that such acts be performed with concreteness and clarity. Things created through *hafla'ah* are, by definition, created through fully articulated speech acts. The *hafla'ah* standard thus requires not merely that the speaker had the intention to make a *neder*, but that the words themselves express that intention with sufficient clarity to constitute the operative act.

### Conditional Nazir

The practical implication of the *hafla'ah* requirement is illustrated through the case of *hareini nazir im zeh nazir* — "I will be a *nazir* if this person is a *nazir*." One opinion holds that such a vow is ineffective even if we subsequently discover that the person in question was indeed a *nazir*, because at the moment the vow was uttered there was genuine uncertainty whether it was taking effect. The concept of *hafla'ah* requires that the speech act be determinate at the moment of utterance. If the efficacy of the statement depends on a fact not yet known, the speech act lacks the requisite clarity — not merely because of the speaker's subjective uncertainty, but because the act itself is constitutively conditional and indeterminate. Whether one rules strictly on this question is a matter of some debate, but the conceptual point is clear: *hafla'ah* mandates that the speech act be unambiguous in its operation at the moment it occurs.

### Applying to Neder

From the *hafla'ah* requirement governing *nezirut* itself, the Gemara derives through *hekkesh* that the same standard governs a *yad* of *nezirut*. And since the *yad* of *neder* is learned from the *yad* of *nezirut*, the *hafla'ah* standard applies to *nedarim* as well. The consequence is Rava's position: a *yad she'einah mochiach* — a *yad* with any residual ambiguity — does not meet the *hafla'ah* standard and therefore cannot function as a valid speech act. The *neder* does not take effect.

## Abaye vs. Rava

### Abaye's View

Abaye holds *havyan yadayim*: even an ambiguous *yad*, one where the meaning is probable but not certain, constitutes a valid and operative speech act. A degree of ambiguity does not negate the *neder*.

### Rava's View

Rava holds *lo havyan yadayim*: any degree of ambiguity disqualifies the *yad* entirely. The speech act must be unambiguous in order to take legal effect. This is Rava's application of the *hafla'ah* principle derived through the *nazir* *hekkesh*.

### Get Connection

The Gemara asks whether Abaye and Rava are essentially replicating the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Rabbanan* in the Mishnah about the text of a *get*. The Mishnah rules that the core text of a *get* is *harei at muteret l'chol adam*. Rabbi Yehuda requires the addition *v'den d'yehevei lichi minai sefer teiruchin v'igeret shevukin* — "and this shall be to you from me a writ of severance and a document of release." The Gemara maps the positions: *Abaye de-amar k'Rabbanan* — Abaye rules like the *Rabbanan*, who do not require explicit articulation; *Rava de-amar k'Rabbi Yehuda* — Rava rules like Rabbi Yehuda, who does. The debate over *yadayim she'einan mochichot* thus appears to be the same debate, applied to different legal contexts.

### Kritut Distinction

Abaye, however, refuses this mapping of his position to the *Rabbanan*. He says: *ana de-amri afilu k'Rabbi Yehuda* — I rule even according to Rabbi Yehuda's position, and yet I still hold *havyan yadayim*. How? Because Rabbi Yehuda's stringency in the law of *get* derives from a special requirement unique to *gittin* — the Torah requirement of *kritut*, severance. *Kritut* demands that the divorce effect a complete and total rupture of the marital bond. Every ambiguity, every loose thread, every possible residual connection must be eliminated. Given that uniquely elevated standard, Rabbi Yehuda requires the text of the *get* to be explicitly unambiguous. But this is not a general statement about speech acts. In ordinary contexts — *nedarim*, *nezirut* and the like — Rabbi Yehuda might well accept an ambiguous *yad* as operative. The *kritut* requirement is a specifically heightened standard, and its demands cannot be extrapolated to areas outside the law of *get*. Therefore Abaye can hold *havyan yadayim* universally while simultaneously accepting Rabbi Yehuda's position regarding *gittin* as applying only in that specific context.

### Rava's Response

Rava in turn responds symmetrically: *ana de-amri afilu k'Rabbanan* — I hold even according to the *Rabbanan*, and yet I still hold *lo havyan yadayim*. The *Rabbanan* do not require explicit articulation in the text of the *get* — but this is not because they are tolerant of ambiguity. It is because in the context of a *get*, there is no ambiguity at all. *Ein adam megaresh ishet chaveiro* — a man does not divorce another man's wife. Given the entire context of a *get* proceeding, who is divorcing whom is perfectly clear, as is the fact that this document is the instrument of divorce. The *Rabbanan* say the text is sufficient not because they permit ambiguity, but because there is no ambiguity here. The context supplies the clarity that the intrinsic language might otherwise lack. In general, Rava still requires *yadayim mochichot* — the position that ambiguity nullifies the speech act — but contextual clarity can satisfy that requirement. The *Rabbanan*'s leniency in *gittin* is not a statement about accepting ambiguity; it is a statement that in this particular case there is none.

### Ran's Distinction

The Ran presses on this point and introduces a conceptual refinement. The Gemara states: *ad kan lo ka'palgei Rabbanan de-be-yadayim mochichot* — the *Rabbanan* say you do not need *yadayim mochichot* by *get*. But this seems to contradict the explanation just offered — that the *Rabbanan* do require *yadayim mochichot*, they just hold that the requirement is satisfied by context. The Ran resolves this by distinguishing between two different sources of clarity. A *yad mochiach* refers to a case where the statement itself — the intrinsic semantic content of the language, read apart from any context — is unambiguous. When it is the context, rather than the statement itself, that provides the clarity, this is not technically a *yad mochiach* at all; it is contextual *mochiach*. The text of a *get* as written — *harei at muteret l'chol adam* — is not, in isolation, a *yad mochiach*. It is a *yad she'einah mochiach* by its intrinsic language alone. What the *Rabbanan* hold is not that *yadayim she'einan mochichot* are valid in general, but rather that in the case of a *get*, contextual clarity — *ein adam megaresh ishet chaveiro* — is an independent and sufficient source of *mochiach* that operates entirely apart from the text. Therefore when the Gemara says the *Rabbanan* do not require *yadayim mochichot*, it means precisely that: the statement's intrinsic *yadayim* do not have to be *mochichot*, because context provides its own distinct form of clarity.

The upshot for the Abaye-Rava debate is this. Both agree that when there is clear context or a clear statement, the speech act is fully operative. The disagreement is confined to cases where even context does not eliminate the ambiguity — where the statement remains genuinely open to multiple interpretations and no external factor resolves it. In that situation, Abaye holds the speech act is still valid, while Rava holds it is not. This is the precise scope of *yadayim she'einan mochichot lo havyan yadayim* according to Rava, and the conceptual distinction the Ran draws makes clear why the *Rabbanan*'s position in *gittin* does not commit them to accepting ambiguity in general.
