# Bava Batra 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Store in Courtyard

### Mishnah: Store in Courty…

The *mishnah* at the bottom of 20b introduces a set of zoning questions that are highly practical and immediately relatable: what commercial activity may one conduct in a shared residential *chatzer*? The *mishnah* opens with the case of *chanut she-be-chatzer* — a store opened within a courtyard shared by multiple households. The ruling is that the other residents may object: *yachol limchot beyado*. The basis for objection, as the *mishnah* phrases it, is "I cannot bear all those coming and going" — the disruption caused not merely by noise, but by the influx of outside foot traffic into what is fundamentally a private residential space.

One might note that the *mishnah*'s language — "I cannot sleep because of those entering and exiting" — seems to limit the objection to hours when sleep is disrupted. It seems likely, however, that this is not the intended limitation. The real concern is the intrusion of outsiders into the *chatzer*, not only the noise generated during nighttime hours.

### Commercial vs. Domestic

The *mishnah* then draws a sharp distinction between a commercial enterprise and domestic craft production. *Aval oseh kelim, yotze u-mocheir ba-shuk* — one may manufacture vessels within one's own home and take them out to sell in the marketplace. The neighbors cannot object on grounds of noise, whether the sound of a hammer, a millstone, or children. *V'eino yachol limchot beyado*.

The underlying principle, familiar to anyone who has lived in an apartment building, is that noise *stam* — incidental noise produced within one's own domain — is generally tolerable and not legally actionable. What crosses the line is opening a commercial enterprise that draws a stream of outside visitors into the shared space. An interesting edge case presents itself: what if one were to host a silent meditation retreat — no noise whatsoever, but heavy foot traffic? The *mishnah* seems to suggest the objection is to the combination of outsiders and noise; yet the two factors may not be fully severable. In any event, the core distinction the *mishnah* draws is clear: outside traffic yes, ambient noise no.

### Gemara Question

The *gemara* immediately asks: *mai shna reisha u-mai shna seifa*? Why is it that in the first clause (*reisha*) one can object to outside traffic, yet in the last clause (*seifa*) one cannot object to the sound of children? The *gemara* initially assumes *tinokot* refers to children coming in to make purchases — which would seem to be exactly the kind of outside foot traffic the *reisha* prohibits. Why then is it permitted?

### Abaye: Neighboring Court…

Abaye resolves the difficulty by reframing the case of the *seifa*: *seifa atiyan l'chatzer acheret* — the store in the *seifa* was opened not in this *chatzer* but in a neighboring one. Imagine two adjacent courtyards opening onto the same *mavoi*. If the residents of the neighboring *chatzer* have no objection to a store operating among them, a resident of the adjacent *chatzer* cannot complain that the resulting foot traffic in the shared alleyway disturbs him. People are not entering his *chatzer* at all. There is a limit to what one may object to: interference must be direct and within one's own space.

### Rava: School Case

Rava, however, offers a superior resolution. He points out that Abaye's reading is strained — if the *seifa* were simply about a neighboring *chatzer*, there would be no reason to specify children. *Ela amar Rava: seifa atan l'tinokos shel beis rabban* — the *seifa* is discussing an entirely different case: a school, a *beis rabban*, being opened in the courtyard. This is the *chidush*. A school generates precisely the kind of bustle and commotion one might expect to object to — children arriving in the morning, departing in the afternoon, noise throughout the day — and yet the residents of the *chatzer* cannot prevent it. The reason, as the *gemara* explains, flows directly from the *takkanah* of Yehoshua ben Gamla.

## Education Takkanah

### Yehoshua ben Gamla

*U-me-hiskanat Yehoshua ben Gamla va-eilach* — from the *takkanah* of Yehoshua ben Gamla onward, schools cannot be blocked. The *gemara* records that Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *bram zachor oso ha-ish le-tov, Yehoshua ben Gamla shemo, she-ilmalei hu nishtakach Torah mi-Yisrael* — this man should be remembered for good, for were it not for him, Torah would have been forgotten among the Jewish people. His achievement was the establishment of universal, publicly funded primary education.

### Private Education

What was the situation before his *takkanah*? *She-batchilah mi she-yeish lo av melamdo Torah, mi she-ein lo av lo hayah lomeid Torah* — whoever had a father who could teach him Torah received an education; whoever lacked a father capable of doing so did not learn at all. Education was conceived as a purely personal obligation: *ve-limadetem osam* — you shall teach *them*, understood as the father teaching his own children. The communal dimension was absent entirely.

### Jerusalem Schools

The *gemara* then traces a progression through several stages. In response to the inadequacy of a purely private model, schools were established centrally in Jerusalem: *she-yehu moshivin melamdei tinokos bi-Yerushalayim*. The prooftext was *ki mi-Tzion teitzei Torah* — from Zion Torah shall go forth. The obligation had been shifted from the individual father to a collective institution, but it remained geographically concentrated.

Yet this too proved insufficient. *Ve-adayin*: even with schools in Jerusalem, *mi she-yeish lo av hayah ma-alo u-melamdo* — a child with a capable father who could bring him up to Jerusalem benefited, while *mi she-ein lo av lo hayah oleh ve-lomeid* — a child without such a father still could not make the journey and remained uneducated. Accessibility was formally improved but practically still selective.

A subsequent stage expanded the system to the districts: *hitkinu she-yehu moshivin be-chol pelech u-felech* — in every major region. But even this was insufficiently universal, for the schools remained centralized within each large district, and students were only enrolled from the ages of sixteen and seventeen. By that point, the *gemara* observes, the students were old enough to resent the discipline: *u-mi she-hayah rabo ko'es alav, mavit bo ve-yatze* — if the teacher rebuked him, he would rebel and walk out.

### Universal Education

It was Yehoshua ben Gamla who finally broke through these limitations: *ad she-ba Yehoshua ben Gamla ve-tiken she-yehu moshivin melamdei tinokos be-chol medina u-medina u-ve-chol ir va-ir* — he established that teachers of young children should be appointed in every province and in every town. And he lowered the entry age: *u-machnisin oso ke-ven shesh u-ke-ven sheva* — children were to be enrolled at age six or seven, young enough to form the habits of mind and submission to learning that later schooling cannot instill.

### Schools Protected

The practical consequence of this *takkanah* for our *mishnah* is now clear. Since every town is obligated to maintain a school, anyone who wishes to open a school in his *chatzer* cannot be blocked by the other residents, regardless of the disruption it causes. This remains a striking ruling: one might argue that while the *takkanah* requires a school somewhere in the town, it does not specify that it must be placed in *this particular* courtyard, subjecting its residents to all the attendant noise. The *gemara* nonetheless accepts this consequence as following from the *takkanah*'s force.

### Jerusalem Environment

*Tosafot* adds a dimension of considerable depth here. Quoting the explanation *lefi she-hayah ro'eh kedushah gedolah ve-Kohanim oskin ba-avodah*, *Tosafot* explains that the reason Jerusalem was the site of the first communal schools was not merely logistical centrality. The presence of the *Beis ha-Mikdash*, the sight of *Kohanim* engaged in the *avodah*, and the immersive religious environment of the entire city cultivated *yir'as Shamayim* in students in a way that instruction alone could not. *Haya mechaven libo yoter le-yir'as Shamayim*, the student's heart was drawn more naturally toward reverence. *Tosafot* draws a parallel from *ma'aser sheni*: the very purpose of requiring one to consume the *ma'aser sheni* in Jerusalem was to place the person in an environment where everyone around him was engaged in *melechet Shamayim*, and *haya gam hu mechaven le-yir'as Shamayim ve-osek ba-Torah* — he too would naturally orient himself toward Torah. The point is a powerful one about the irreducible role of environment in religious formation. Instruction transmitted within four walls is one thing; the culture and values modeled by the surrounding world are another, and arguably more powerful, force.

## School Administration

### Enrollment & Feeding

The *gemara* records that Rav said to Rav Shmuel bar Shilat: *ad shit lo tekabel* — do not accept students younger than six years old. *Tosafot* notes that six or seven depends on the individual child's readiness to be separated from home. Once enrolled, *askei lehu ke-tora* — feed them like an ox. The imagery is deliberately chosen: not like a camel, which is force-fed with great vigor, but like an ox, which is fed more gently. The *mefarshim* explain the combination: fill the young child with as much Torah and knowledge as possible, but do so *be-nachat* — with gentleness. At this age, the capacity for retention is at its peak, and the educational approach should be to saturate the child with material — *Tanach*, *Mishnah*, by rote — while the analytical faculties are still developing. There may even be a wordplay here, as one participant noted: *safei lei ke-tora*, feed him like an ox — and *tora* sounds remarkably like *Torah* with a different vowel.

### Discipline Standards

When discipline is necessary, Rav advises: *ki machit le-yanuka, lo timchei ela be-arketa de-mesana* — if you must strike a child, do so only with a shoelace, meaning a light, symbolic gesture of correction, not with anything that would cause real physical harm.

### Inclusion Policy

The *gemara* then addresses children who are struggling academically. *Dekari kari* — those who can read, will read. *Delo kari* — if a child cannot yet read, do not expel him from the class. *Havsei le-chavrei* — let him sit next to his peers. *Rashi* elaborates: there is no need to respond to a struggling student with escalating discipline — perhaps the child is simply not yet ready. Do not remove him from your presence; let him sit with others in a communal setting and absorb from the environment. *Ve-sofo latet lev* — in time, he will focus and learn. This reflects a careful balance: taking the educational mission with great seriousness while maintaining a measure of gentleness toward each individual child.

### Baraita Challenge

The *gemara* now raises a challenge from a *baraita*: *echad mi-bnei chatzer she-bikesh la'asot rofe uman ve-gardi u-melamed tinokot, bnei chatzer me'akvin alav* — if one of the courtyard's residents wishes to practice as a doctor (*Rashi* interprets this as *mohel*), a bloodletter, a weaver, or a schoolteacher, the other residents may stop him. This seems to directly contradict the ruling that a Jewish school cannot be blocked.

A second *baraita* makes the same point in the context of two residents: *shnayim she-yoshvim be-chatzer u-vikesh echad mehem la'asot rofe ve-uman ve-gardi u-melamed tinokot, chavero me'akev alav* — his fellow resident may prevent him. And a third *baraita* adds: one who owns a house in a shared *chatzer* may not rent it to a doctor, a bloodletter, or a *sofer*. All three *baraitot* appear to allow blocking the opening of a school.

### Non-Jewish Schools

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *hacha ba-mai askinan? be-tinokot de-akum* — these *baraitot* are speaking of a school for non-Jewish children. A Jew who wished to open a school for non-Jewish students has no special dispensation overriding the objections of the courtyard's residents. The overriding *takkanah* of Yehoshua ben Gamla applies only to Jewish schools, which serve the communal obligation of Torah education. A school for non-Jewish children carries no such privileged status and is treated like any other business bringing outside traffic.

It is worth noting that the *gemara*'s resolution does not necessarily reflect a historical reality of Jews running schools for non-Jewish children; it may simply be the *gemara*'s legal mechanism for squaring the *baraitot* with the ruling derived from Yehoshua ben Gamla's *takkanah*.

### Multi-Grade Schools

The third *baraita* also prohibits renting to a *sofer*. The *gemara* resolves this too: *hacha ba-mai askinan? be-sofer masa* — we are speaking of a *sofer masa*. *Rashi* explains this to mean not a single-classroom school but a multi-grade schoolhouse — a larger, more disruptive institution than a single class. While the *takkanah* requires every town to have a school, it does not require any particular *chatzer* to absorb the noise and traffic of a full multi-grade institution. Residents may block that.

*Tosafot*, however, offers what is probably the better reading. *Tosafot* notes that there is another version of the *gemara* that reads not *sofer* but *sapar* — a barber — making the case simpler. But more importantly, *Tosafot* argues that *sofer masa* should be understood as the communal scribe, the person who drafts legal documents for the entire town (*koseiv shtaros ha-ir*). A communal scribe brings a constant stream of outside visitors to conduct legal business, and therefore falls under the general prohibition against commercial enterprises that generate outside traffic in a *chatzer*. He has no special dispensation comparable to a school.

One further point arises from *Tosafot*'s treatment of the question of renting to a non-Jew more broadly. *Tosafot* observes that *mashma d'im lo haya sofer, hava shari le-haskir le-armai* — had it not been for the business-traffic objection, it would be permissible to rent a courtyard house to a non-Jew. The concern about non-Jews as a physical threat (*d'amar lei aryei arveisa ametzarai* — you have brought a lion to lie at my border), which appears elsewhere in *Bava Basra*, is addressed by *Tosafot* as applying specifically when a Jewish buyer was available and the seller bypassed him — not as a categorical prohibition against any proximity to non-Jewish neighbors.

## Class Sizing

### Class Size Numbers

Rava establishes the class-size standards as follows: *sach mikrei dardekei esrin ve-chamsha yanukei* — the standard number of students per teacher is twenty-five. *Ve-i ika arba'in, mokeiman reish duchna* — if there are forty students, one appoints an assistant teacher. *Ve-i ika chamshin, motvinan trei* — if there are fifty students, one appoints two teachers. This framework raises an immediate logical difficulty: if twenty-five is the standard class size and an assistant is needed at forty, what is the status of classes with twenty-six through thirty-nine students? The *gemara* does not address this directly, and the gap opens the door to significantly different readings among the *rishonim*.

### Rambam Reading

*Rambam* and the *Rishonei Sefarad* read the passage as setting a firm maximum. Up to twenty-five students: one teacher alone suffices. More than twenty-five: an assistant must be added. At forty: the assistant alone no longer suffices; a full second teacher is required. At fifty: two teachers are insufficient; additional support is needed. On this reading, the maximum class size under a single teacher is twenty-five, and any number above that mandates additional staffing.

### Tosafot Reading

*Tosafot* reads the passage differently and, as is noted, probably more naturally with the *gemara*'s own text. On *Tosafot*'s reading, twenty-five is not a maximum but a threshold: a teacher handles up to forty students, and it is only at forty that an assistant becomes necessary. At fifty, two teachers are required. The flexibility between twenty-five and forty is real; one teacher can manage up to forty children. This represents a considerably larger permissible class size.

### Ashkenazi Position

The *Rishonei Ashkenaz* offer a third reading that may reflect practical differences in the educational institutions of their communities. On their view, twenty-five is the minimum number of students below which a town cannot be required to establish a school at all. Fewer than twenty-five children does not trigger the obligation; one may bus those children to a school in the neighboring town. Once there are twenty-five students, the town must open its own school. Between twenty-five and forty, one teacher manages the class; at forty an assistant is needed; at fifty two teachers are required. This reading integrates the immediately preceding *halacha* about busing children between towns, and is likely the most contextually coherent reading of the passage as a whole.

## Teacher Quality

### Job Security

Rava rules: *hai makrei yenukei de-garis ve-hai achrina de-garis tefei, lo mesalkinan lei* — if there is a current teacher and another teacher who knows more, we do not fire the existing teacher to hire the better one. The concern is *dilma atei lidei peshiuta* — perhaps the incoming teacher, having secured the position, will become complacent and perform less diligently than the one he displaced.

### Competition Incentive

Rav Dimi of Nehardea disagrees sharply. *Kol sheken de-garis tefei* — to the contrary, competitive pressure is precisely what motivates better performance. *Kinat soferim tarbe chochma* — the competitive zeal among scholars increases wisdom. If a teacher knows that a better-qualified candidate can displace him, he will work harder to remain indispensable. The incoming teacher, knowing the same principle applies to him, will also strive to perform at his best, lest yet another superior candidate come along and take his position.

### Breadth vs. Precision

Rava then addresses a different personnel question: *hanei trei makrei dardekei chad garis ve-chad lo dayek ve-chad dayek ve-lo garis* — two schoolteachers: one covers a great deal of material but is not careful (*dayek*) in following up to ensure students have absorbed it correctly; the other is very attentive and precise but covers less ground. Which is preferable?

Rava's initial position is *masvinan hahu de-garis ve-lo dayek* — prefer the one who covers more material, even though he is less attentive. *Shabeshta mi-meila nafak* — errors will work themselves out over time. The broader base of knowledge is the more valuable asset.

Rav Dimi of Nehardea again disagrees: *masvinan de-dayek ve-lo garis* — prefer the one who is more attentive, even though he covers less. *Shabeshta kivan de-al al* — once an error has entered a student's mind, it stays. The initial formation of knowledge is more critical than its breadth; inaccuracies instilled early are nearly impossible to eradicate.

It is worth clarifying precisely what the contrast between *garis* and *dayek* means here. *Rashi* uses the language *she-lomeid harbe* — who learns much — rather than *she-melameid harbe*, suggesting *garis* refers to the teacher's own breadth of knowledge rather than the pace of classroom instruction. One participant offered the more illuminating reading: both teachers are competent, but one moves through a great deal of material at pace without carefully monitoring student comprehension, while the other moves more slowly, covering less ground but ensuring what is taught is thoroughly grasped. This reading sharpens the practical tension considerably: one teacher is filling students with volume; the other is ensuring that what they receive is accurate and retained. The point of principle cuts in favor of the latter: it is better to teach less and teach it correctly than to teach more and embed errors.

### Joab Story

The *gemara* illustrates this with a striking narrative. The verse reads (*Melachim*): *ki sheishet chodashim yashav sham Yoav ve-chol Yisrael ad hichrit kol zachar be-Edom* — for six months Yoav and all Israel remained there until they had cut down every male in Edom. When Yoav returned before David, David asked him: *mai ta'ama avadt hachi* — why did you do this, killing only the males? Yoav replied: *de-chtiv timche et zachar Amalek* — because the verse says *zachar*, which he had learned to read with a *patach*, meaning "the male." David responded: *ve-hanan zecher karinan* — but we read it *zecher*, with a *tzeireh*, meaning "the memory" of Amalek — the entire people, not only the males. Yoav replied: *ana zachar akreyun* — that is how my teacher read it to me.

They went and asked the teacher. *Heichi akreitan?* — How did you teach him to read it? *Tosafot* makes a critical point here: the teacher answered *zecher* — with the correct vowelization. He did not make an error in what he taught; Yoav simply heard it incorrectly, and the teacher never followed up to verify that the student had received the material accurately. That is the sharper and more instructive read: *Tosafot* emphasizes that the failure was pedagogical, not a mistake in the teacher's own knowledge. The teacher taught correctly but failed in his *dayek*, his attentiveness to whether the student truly understood.

David then drew his sword to execute the teacher, citing *arur oseh melechet Hashem remiyah* — cursed is one who performs God's labor deceitfully, who cuts corners in sacred work. The teacher asked to be spared the death penalty and be left instead with the curse. David then cited the end of that same verse: *ve-arur monea charbo mi-dam* — cursed too is one who withholds his sword from blood. The *gemara* records that opinions differ as to whether the teacher was ultimately executed. The story powerfully illustrates Rav Dimi's point: a mistake in foundational learning, left unverified, had catastrophic consequences. The loss was irreversible.

## No-Warning Professions

### Professional List

Rava rules: *makrei yenuka, shatla, tavcha, ve-umana, ve-sofer mata — kulan ke-mutarin ve-omdin daminhu* — a schoolteacher, a sharecropper, a butcher, a bloodletter, and a communal scribe are all considered to stand in a state of perpetual forewarning. They may be dismissed on the spot without the standard procedural warning that an employer must give an employee before termination.

### Irreversible Loss

The unifying principle is *kol psida de-lo hadar* — any loss that cannot be recovered. A schoolteacher who transmits an error creates an intellectual defect in the student that, as we have seen, is nearly impossible to undo. A butcher who slaughters improperly produces a *neveilah* — the loss is complete and unrecoverable. A bloodletter performing a medical procedure that goes wrong may cost a life. A communal scribe who drafts a document incorrectly — writing one hundred where the document should read one thousand, for example — creates a legal instrument that cannot simply be corrected. The sharecropper presents a somewhat harder case to explain on these grounds, but the principle as stated is clear: wherever professional negligence produces an irreversible consequence, the worker is treated as having been perpetually forewarned, and immediate dismissal is legally permissible without prior notice.

## Business Competition

### Rav Huna Mill Case

Having addressed the world of schools and teachers, the *gemara* pivots to a broader set of questions about commercial competition. Rav Huna rules: *hai bar mavoi de-oki reicheia, ve-ata bar mavoi chavreih ve-ka moki gavei — dina hu de-me'akev ilei* — if a resident of a *mavoi* has set up a mill, and another resident of the same *mavoi* now seeks to open a competing mill nearby, the original miller may legally prevent him. *Ka paskat lei le-chayuti* — you are cutting off my livelihood.

It is important to note the shift from the earlier discussions. The *chatzer* concerned noise and outside foot traffic in a shared private space. The *mavoi* — larger, encompassing multiple *chatzerot* — concerns something different: the right to protection from economic competition. This is the domain of what would later be conceptualized as *hasagat gevul*, encroaching on another's livelihood, though the *gemara* does not use that phrase explicitly.

### Fish Hunting

The *gemara* seeks to support Rav Huna's position from a *halacha* regarding fish hunting: *marchikin metzudat ha-dag min ha-dag ke-melo ritzat ha-dag* — one must distance one's fish trap from another's by the distance a fish runs. Rabbah bar Rav Huna specifies this as *ad parsa* — up to a *parsa*, which is four *mil*, approximately 8,000 *amah*, or roughly two and a half miles. If a fisherman has staked out a location and is actively drawing fish there — feeding them, setting his nets — all others must stay away from that zone. The one who was there first has a kind of possessory claim on that productive space.

### Fish vs. Customers

The *gemara* pushes back, however: *shani dagim de-yahavi siyara* — fish are different, because the fish are being drawn to a specific location by the fisherman's preparatory work. *Rashi* elaborates: *batuach hu she-yikadenu, havay lei ke-man de-mata le-yadei* — the fisherman is effectively certain to capture those fish; it is as though they are already in his hand. In that sense, the other fisherman who intrudes is causing a concrete, tangible loss — something already nearly possessed is being taken away. *Aval hacha mi she-ba etzli yavo, mi she-ba etzlecha yavo* — but in the case of competing mills, customers are not pre-claimed. My customers may come to me; your customers may come to you. No customer is the equivalent of a fish already in the net. The fish precedent therefore cannot directly support Rav Huna's position.

### Unfair Competition

Ravina then raises a different angle: *leima Rav Huna de-amar ke-Rabbi Yehuda?* — perhaps Rav Huna's position that one cannot open a competing mill is consistent only with Rabbi Yehuda's view in a *mishnah* elsewhere. That *mishnah* records Rabbi Yehuda's opinion that a shopkeeper may not distribute roasted grain and nuts to children as promotional incentives, because it draws customers to his shop unfairly. *Va-chakhamim matirin* — the sages permit it, reasoning that the competitor can do the same thing: if you give out nuts, I can give out nuts too. Rabbi Yehuda restricts unfair competitive practices; the sages allow them. Perhaps Rav Huna's protection of the original miller reflects Rabbi Yehuda's restrictive view, and would not be accepted according to the majority.

### Fair vs. Unfair

The *gemara* responds: *afilu teima Rabbanan* — even if you hold like the *Rabbanan* in that *mishnah*, Rav Huna's ruling could still stand. *Ad kan lo paligi Rabbanan alei de-Rabbi Yehuda hasam ela de-amar lei: ana mapalginan amgozai ve-at palgi kusta* — the sages disagreed with Rabbi Yehuda only in the context where both parties are already in business and the question is whether one may employ a particular promotional tactic, because the competitor can use the same tactic. That is a debate about the rules of fair competition between two existing enterprises. But here — *aval hacha afilu Rabbanan modu, de-amar lei ka paskat lei le-chayuti* — the situation is categorically different: there is already an established business, and someone new wishes to open a competing one and directly undercut the first party's livelihood. Even the sages might agree that this crosses a line.

The underlying tension is a real one. A free-market instinct argues that the consumer benefits from more businesses, more competition, and lower prices, and that no one should be able to hold a monopoly simply by virtue of having arrived first. The protectionist instinct argues that a person who has built a business and a livelihood deserves some security against being immediately undercut. The *gemara* here is at least provisionally following Rav Huna's position that the prior occupant of the economic space deserves protection — a position that will be developed further in the discussion that follows.
