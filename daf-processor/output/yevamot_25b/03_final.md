# Yevamot 25b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Get from Overseas

### Get Overseas Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, וְאָמַר: "בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם" — לֹא יִשָּׂא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. "מֵת", "הֲרַגְתִּיו", "הֲרַגְנוּהוּ" — לֹא יִשָּׂא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: "הֲרַגְתִּיו" — לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא אִשְׁתּוֹ, "הֲרַגְנוּהוּ" — תִּנָּשֵׂא אִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: An agent **who brought a bill of divorce from a country overseas and said: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence,** as required in order to establish the bill of divorce as valid, **may not marry** the **wife,** i.e., the divorcée. Since the validity of the bill of divorce is based upon his testimony, marrying the divorcée creates the impression that he had an ulterior motive for his testimony. Similarly, a witness who testified that a certain man **died,** or testified: **I killed him,** or: **We killed him, may not marry** that man's **wife. Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he testified: **I killed him, his wife may not be married** at all based on that evidence, as his testimony is unreliable, but if he said: **We killed him, his wife may be married** to anyone other than those witnesses.

The *mishnah* at the bottom of 21a opens with a case that initially seems to belong in *Masechet Gittin*: *hamavee get mimedinat hayam* — someone who brings a *get* from overseas. As any student of *Gittin* knows, when delivering a *get* from overseas the messenger must declare *bifnay nechtav u'bifnay nechtam* — that the document was written and signed in his presence, thus validating its execution. The *mishnah* here establishes that this messenger, having delivered the testimony required to render the *get* valid, may not subsequently marry the newly divorced woman. Allowing him to do so would cast retrospective suspicion on the reliability and sincerity of his testimony.

### Death/Murder Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** [continuing from above]
>
> **Translation:** [same as above]

The *mishnah* continues with related cases. If the witness says *mes hu* — the husband died — or *haragtee* — I killed him — or *haragnu* — we killed him — he likewise may not marry the woman, because in each instance he has testified to the fact of the husband's death and thereby enabled the woman to remarry. Permitting him to marry her would undermine the credibility of his testimony.

What is striking, of course, is that the *mishnah* raises no concern about the confession of murder itself. The legal focus remains entirely on the question of whether his subsequent marriage to the woman would taint the testimony that allowed her to remarry. We will return to the confession below.

*Rabbi Yehuda* draws a distinction: if the witness says *haragtee* — I killed him — the woman may not remarry this particular man; but if he says *haragnu* — we killed him — she may. The precise meaning of this distinction is clarified by the *gemara*.

### Woman's Investigation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דְּמִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, דַּעֲלֵיהּ קָסָמְכִינַן. אֲבָל מֵאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, דְּלָאו עֲלֵיהּ קָסָמְכִינַן — יִשָּׂא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. וְהָא מֵת, דְּלָאו עֲלֵיהּ קָסָמְכִינַן, דְּאָמַר מָר: אִשָּׁה דַּיְיקָא וּמִינַּסְבָא, וְקָתָנֵי: לֹא יִשָּׂא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara clarifies: **The reason that** the agent may not marry the divorcée applies specifically in a case where he brings the bill of divorce **from a country overseas, as** in this case **we,** the court, **rely upon his** testimony to validate the bill of divorce. **But** an agent who brings a bill of divorce **from Eretz Yisrael** need not make any verbal declaration, and **since we,** the court, **do not rely upon his** testimony but upon the written bill of divorce alone, **he may marry his wife,** i.e., the divorcée, since it does not arouse suspicion. The Gemara challenges this: **But** also in the case of a witness who said that the husband **died, we,** the court, **do not rely** solely **upon** his testimony. **As the Master said: A woman is exacting** in her investigation of the truth of the testimony that her husband died before she **marries** again, and it is primarily on that basis that she is permitted to remarry. **Yet it is taught that he** still **may not marry his wife.**

The *gemara* begins by probing the logic of the *mishnah*'s opening case. It observes: *ta'ama d'mimedinat hayam* — the reason the witness cannot marry the woman is that it is specifically because he comes from overseas, meaning we are relying on his testimony (*de-alav kasamchinan*). But if he delivers a *get* from within *Eretz Yisrael*, where no such declaration is required, and we are not formally relying on him (*d'lav alav kasamchinan*), then he may marry the woman.

The *gemara* then turns to the case of testifying to the husband's death, and here a sharp difficulty arises. When the *gemara* says *d'lav alav kasamchinan* — we are not relying on him — that seems plainly wrong. Of course we are relying on him; he is the one who said the husband is dead! The *gemara* resolves this by invoking a well-known principle: *isha dayka u'minsava* — the woman herself investigates before she remarries. Because the consequences of remarrying while a husband is still alive are so severe, we trust the woman to undertake her own thorough investigation following the witness's testimony. In this framing, it is ultimately her investigation, not his testimony alone, that bears the weight of the permission to remarry. This is quite a radical *gemara*: the entire operative mechanism is relocated to the woman's own diligence.

### Get vs. Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לֵיכָּא כְּתָבָא, הָכָא אִיכָּא כְּתָבָא. דִּתְנַן: מָה בֵּין גֵּט לְמִיתָה — שֶׁהַכְּתָב מוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The two cases are not comparable, as **there,** in the case when a witness testifies that the husband has died, **there is not anything written** as proof, and therefore he may not marry the widow. However, **here,** in a case where an agent brings a bill of divorce from Eretz Yisrael, **there is** a **written** document that is valid without any testimony. **As we learned** in a mishna (117a): **What is the difference between a bill of divorce and death?** Why does the court rely upon those men who are not trusted as witnesses to the death of a husband if they act as agents to bring a bill of divorce, even from overseas, such that they must give testimony that it was written and signed in their presence? The difference is **that** with regard to a bill of divorce **the writing proves** their testimony.

If that is so, one might ask: if we are not really relying on the witness's testimony in the death case — since the woman's investigation is doing the real work — why can't he marry her? The *gemara* answers: *hatam leka ksava, hacha ika ksava* — there is still a meaningful difference between the two cases. When someone testifies to the husband's death, he is still presenting factual testimony, even if it is subsequently bolstered by the woman's investigation. Delivering a *get* within *Eretz Yisrael*, by contrast, requires no testimonial declaration whatsoever; the messenger is doing nothing more than physically transmitting a document.

As the *mishnah* itself puts it, *mah bein get lemita* — what is the difference between divorce and death? *She'haksav mochiach* — the written document proves itself. A signed document from *Eretz Yisrael* carries inherent presumptive validity.

### Document Validation

The basis for this presumption is the *gemara*'s principle that *edim she'bishtar kemi she'nechkerah edusam b'veis din* — witnesses whose signatures appear on a document are treated as though their testimony has already been fully examined in court. Even without what we might today call notarization, two signatures on a document establish its validity as a matter of law. It is only when a document arrives from outside *Eretz Yisrael* — from *chutz la'aretz* — that questions may arise about the reliability of the signatures, and external verification in the form of *bifnay nechtav u'bifnay nechtam* becomes necessary.

## Murder & Witness

### Murder Confession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "מֵת", "הֲרַגְתִּיו", "הֲרַגְנוּהוּ" — לֹא יִשָּׂא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. הוּא נִיהוּ דְּלֹא יִשָּׂא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, הָא לְאַחֵר תִּנָּשֵׂא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna stated that if the witness said with regard to the husband that **he died,** or: **I killed him,** or: **We killed him,** then **he may not marry** the **wife** of the deceased. The Gemara infers that **he,** the witness **himself, may not marry** the **wife; this** implies that **to another she may be married** on the basis of his testimony.

The *gemara* now draws out a crucial implication from the *mishnah*. It states: *hu nihu d'lo yisa et ishto* — it is specifically he who may not marry the woman — *ha l'acher tinase* — but she may marry a different man. This makes clear that the testimony is in fact legally acceptable. The *mishnah* never says that because the man confessed to murder his testimony is disqualified. On the contrary, the testimony stands; the only concern is preventing *him* from marrying *her*, lest it appear that the testimony was self-serving.

This is consistent with the broader halachic principle — extensively developed throughout *Yevamot* — that for the purpose of *edut isha*, testimony enabling a woman to remarry, the usual standards of testimony are relaxed considerably. A single witness suffices, and the *gemara* elsewhere extends this to women, slaves, and even hearsay testimony.

### Rape Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: "פְּלוֹנִי רְבָעַנִי לְאוֹנְסִי" — הוּ וְאַחֵר מִצְטָרְפִין לְהׇרְגוֹ. "לִרְצוֹנִי" — רָשָׁע הוּא, וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: "אַל תָּשֶׁת יָדְךָ עִם רָשָׁע לִהְיוֹת עֵד חָמָס"?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: **Didn't Rav Yosef say:** With regard to one who testified that **so-and-so sodomized me against my will,** then **he** who testified to being the victim of the sexual assault **and another** bystander witness can **combine** as a pair of witnesses in order **to put** the assailant **to death** for homosexual intercourse. But if he testified: I was **willingly** sodomized by so-and-so, then **he is wicked** by his own admission, since he willingly transgressed. **And the Torah said: "Put not your hand with the wicked to be a corrupt witness"** (Exodus 23:1). If one renders himself unfit as a witness by admitting to murder, how can his testimony be accepted to permit the wife to remarry?

However, a difficulty arises from a ruling of *Rav Yosef*. *Rav Yosef* addresses a case where a man testifies that another man committed a sexual act with him *le'onsi* — against his will, meaning he was violated. In that case, he and a second witness may combine (*hu ve'acher mitztarfim lehargo*) to testify against the perpetrator and have him executed. Since the victim was not a willing participant, he bears no guilt and retains his status as a valid witness.

But if the man says the act was *birtzoni* — performed willingly — then he has admitted to being a willing participant in a grave transgression. The *Torah* declares *al tashet yadcha im rasha liheyot ed chamas* — do not join a wicked person to be a corrupt witness. He has effectively disqualified himself as a witness, and his testimony cannot be combined with that of another to prosecute anyone.

### Splitting Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: שָׁאנֵי עֵדוּת אִשָּׁה דַּאֲקִילּוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן — וְהָאָמַר רַב מְנַשֶּׁה: גַּזְלָן דְּדִבְרֵיהֶם — כָּשֵׁר לְעֵדוּת אִשָּׁה, גַּזְלָן דְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה — פָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **testimony** that **a woman's** husband died **is different, as the Sages ruled leniently** in such matters and perhaps accepted testimony of a wicked witness in such cases, **didn't Rav Menashe say:** Although one who is considered **a robber according to the words** of the Sages is unfit for other forms of testimony, **he is fit** as a witness **for testimony** that **a woman's** husband died. **A robber according to Torah law is unfit** as a witness even **for testimony** that **a woman's** husband has died.

This raises the question at the heart of the *sugya*: the principle of *palginan dibura* — can we split his words? Can we take the portion of his testimony that concerns the other person and set aside the portion that incriminates himself? In *Rav Yosef*'s case, the answer is no. The man's declaration of willing participation renders him a *rasha* and invalidates him as a witness entirely; we cannot bracket his self-incrimination and retain the rest of his testimony. This would seem to apply equally to our case: if a man confesses to murder, he has declared himself a *rasha*, and should be disqualified for *edut isha* just as for any other testimony.

### Invalid Witnesses

The position just introduced by *Rav Menashya* adds a further layer. He holds that a *gazlan d'divreihem* — someone rabbinically disqualified as a witness, such as a gambler — is nonetheless valid for *edut isha*, because the rabbis were lenient in matters of testimony concerning a woman's ability to remarry. However, a *gazlan d'divrei Torah* — someone biblically disqualified as a witness — is invalid even for *edut isha*.

One might wonder, as *Tosafot* does, whether there is a distinction between different categories of biblical disqualification. One type of invalid witness is someone disqualified because he has sinned for financial gain — his testimony is suspect because it may be purchased. That concern is intelligible in the context of *edut isha*. But another type of disqualified witness is simply a habitual sinner (*neveila leteiavon*), someone who sins out of appetite rather than greed. Why should that person necessarily be invalid for *edut isha*? The *gemara* here does not draw that distinction, and the difficulty remains, at least implicitly.

In our case, if the witness confessed to murder, he would appear — by his own account — to be biblically disqualified, and according to *Rav Menashya* he should be invalid even for *edut isha*.

### Adam Karov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב מְנַשֶּׁה: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבָּנַן — וְטַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן הָכָא כִּדְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: אָדָם קָרוֹב אֵצֶל עַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין אָדָם מֵשִׂים עַצְמוֹ רָשָׁע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **Rav Menashe** could have **said to you: I am speaking even according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis.** Although the Rabbis did not allow one who was wicked by Torah law to testify for a woman, a witness who admitted: I killed him, is nevertheless believed. **And the rationale of the Rabbis here is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava, as Rava** disputed Rav Yosef's opinion and **said:** Even if one said that he was willingly sodomized by this man, he is not believed concerning his own actions, because **a person is his own relative.** Consequently, he may not testify about himself, just as the testimony of any relative is disqualified. **And** furthermore, **a person does not make himself wicked.** His testimony with regard to his own actions is inadmissible because he is his own relative, but his testimony is accepted both to put a sodomizer to death or to render it permitted for a woman to remarry by saying that he killed her husband.

The *gemara* then raises the possibility that *Rav Menashya* must be following *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position in the *mishnah*, which disqualifies the witness who says *haragtee*. The *gemara* rejects this. *Rav Menashya* himself says: *ana de'amri afilu Rabbanon* — I can maintain my position even according to the Rabbis who accept the testimony. The reason the Rabbis accept it is based on *Rava*'s principle: *adam karov etzel atzmo, ve'ein adam mesim atzmo rasha* — a person is as close to himself as his nearest relative, and just as a relative's testimony about himself is inadmissible, a person cannot render himself a *rasha* through his own declaration. This is the mechanism of *palginan dibura*: we split his words, discard the self-incriminating portion as legally inert, and retain only the testimony concerning the other person — the fact that the husband is dead. Self-incrimination in matters of *mita* or *malkot* is halachically irrelevant; it requires external testimony to establish criminal status.

### Rav Yosef/Menashya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא רַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר לְךָ רַב יוֹסֵף: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבָּנַן, וְשָׁאנֵי עֵדוּת אִשָּׁה, דַּאֲקִילּוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן. וְרַב מְנַשֶּׁה דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** according to this explanation that the opinion **that Rav Yosef spoke is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda?** The Gemara rejects this: **Rav Yosef** could have **said to you: I am speaking even according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, as** in my opinion, **testimony** enabling **a woman** to remarry **is different in that the Rabbis ruled more leniently** and they even accept testimony from a completely wicked individual. However, **Rav Menashe, who** renders unfit one who is wicked by Torah law from testimony enabling a woman to remarry, **spoke in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who differentiates even in such testimony between one who is considered wicked according to Torah law and one who is considered wicked by rabbinic law.

The *gemara* then considers whether *Rav Yosef* — who refuses to split the testimony in his case — must be following *Rabbi Yehuda* in the *mishnah*. Again the answer is no. *Rav Yosef* says *ana de'amri afilu Rabbanon*: I too can work with the position of the Rabbis. But my case is different: *shani edut isha de'akilu bah Rabbanon* — testimony concerning a woman's remarriage is a domain where the Rabbis were lenient, and it is specifically in that domain that we permit *palginan dibura*, splitting the testimony to retain only what is relevant. In the case of prosecuting someone for a capital offense, *Rav Yosef* holds, we do not split the words.

The *gemara* thus arrives at a nuanced picture. *Rav Menashya* holds that we do apply *palginan dibura* — he disagrees with *Rav Yosef* — and therefore even a witness who has testified against himself can have the relevant portion of his testimony accepted. But *Rav Menashya* also holds that a biblically disqualified witness cannot testify for *edut isha*; what saves the murderer-confessor is that his self-incrimination is discarded and he is therefore not treated as biblically disqualified. *Rav Yosef* holds that we do not apply *palginan dibura* — the testimony cannot be split — but is nonetheless lenient because even a biblically disqualified witness may testify for *edut isha*. The *gemara* suggests that one could in principle combine both leniencies, and in the *mishnah*'s case according to the *Tanna Kamma*, it is plausible that both factors work together: the *edut isha* context permits us to apply *palginan dibura*, thereby accepting the relevant portion of the testimony while discarding the self-incriminating portion.

## Tanna/R. Yehuda

### Haragi vs. Haragnu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרַגְתִּיו כּוּ׳ הֲרַגְנוּהוּ תִּנָּשֵׂא כּוּ׳. מַאי שְׁנָא "הֲרַגְתִּיו" וּמַאי שְׁנָא "הֲרַגְנוּהוּ"? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, בְּאוֹמֵר: אֲנִי הָיִיתִי עִם הוֹרְגָיו.
>
> **Translation:** In the mishna it is taught that the court accepts testimony from one who said: **I killed him,** or: **We killed him,** while Rabbi Yehuda differentiates between one who said: I killed him, whose testimony is not accepted, and one who said: We killed him, whose testimony is accepted and the woman **may be married** to others. The Gemara asks: **What is different between: I killed him, and: We killed him?** Isn't he a murderer by his own admission as well when he testifies: We killed him? **Rav Yehuda said:** Do not understand that by saying: We killed him, he included himself among the murderers. Rather, it is referring to a case **where he said: I was with his murderers,** but he was not an active participant to the murder.

Returning to *Rabbi Yehuda*'s distinction in the *mishnah*: why does it matter whether he says *haragtee* — I killed him — or *haragnu* — we killed him? Even in the latter case, he was part of a murder. The *gemara* answers: *amar Rav Yehuda be'omer ani hayisi im horgav* — it does not mean "I murdered him." It means "I was there, I was among those who killed him" — present, perhaps involved in the planning, but not the one who literally committed the act.

### Part of Group

On this reading, *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is entirely coherent. When a man says "I was part of the group that killed him," he has not rendered himself a *rasha* in the biblical sense; he has not confessed to being the direct killer. His testimony about the husband's death therefore remains valid for *edut isha*, and the woman can remarry anyone except him.

### Bandit Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מַעֲשֶׂה בְּלִסְטִים אֶחָד שֶׁיָּצָא לֵיהָרֵג בִּמְגִיזַת קַפּוֹטְקְיָא, וְאָמַר לָהֶם: "לְכוּ אִמְרוּ לָהּ לְאֵשֶׁת שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן כֹּהֵן: ׳אֲנִי הָרַגְתִּי אֶת בַּעְלָהּ בִּכְנִיסָתִי לְלוֹד׳", וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: ׳בִּכְנִיסָתוֹ לְלוֹד׳, וְהִשִּׂיאוּ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ! אָמַר לָהֶם: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה?! בְּאוֹמֵר: "אֲנִי הָיִיתִי עִם הוֹרְגָיו".
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita* that this is the basis for Rabbi Yehuda's distinction: **They told Rabbi Yehuda:** There was **an incident involving an armed bandit [*listim*] who was taken out to be executed in** the **passage [*megizat*] of Cappadocia, and he said to** those present: **Go and tell the wife of Shimon the Priest** that **I killed her husband as I entered Lod. And some say** that he said: **As he entered Lod. And they married off his wife** on the basis of this testimony. This implies that the court accepts testimony from the murderer himself. Rabbi Yehuda **said to them:** You derive **proof from there?** The case was **that he said: I was with his murderers,** but not that he himself murdered the woman's husband.

The *gemara* then challenges *Rav Yehuda* — the *amora* who articulated this interpretation — with a *baraisa*. A certain bandit (*listis*) was being taken out for execution in *Kapotkia*, and he said to those present: go and tell the wife of *Shimon ben Kohen* that I killed her husband when I was entering *Lud*. They went and told her, and she was subsequently remarried on the basis of this testimony. Here, apparently, a man who was plainly being executed for murder — and who explicitly said *ani haragti*, I killed him — had his testimony accepted, and the woman remarried. This seems to contradict *Rav Yehuda*'s interpretation that *haragnu* is only acceptable because it means "I was part of the group, not the actual killer."

*Rav Yehuda* responds: *misham raya?* — do you bring a proof from that case? There too, the man was saying *ani hayisi im horgav* — I was among the killers — not that he personally committed the act.

### Non-Jewish Courts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא "לִסְטִים" קָתָנֵי! שֶׁנִּתְפַּס עַל יְדֵי לִסְטִיּוּת. וְהָא "יָצָא" לֵיהָרֵג קָתָנֵי! בֵּי דִינָא דְּגוֹיִם, דְּלָא דָּיְיקִי וְקָטְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: How could Rabbi Yehuda understand the incident in such a way? **But it is taught** in the *baraita* that the witness himself was **an armed bandit.** The Gemara answers: **He was captured for** a charge of **armed banditry.** The Gemara asks: **But it is taught that he was taken out to be executed,** implying that he was found guilty of murder. The Gemara answers: That was **a gentile court, and they execute without being precise.** One who is among a gang of bandits is executed by a gentile court regardless of whether or not he himself was a murderer. This *baraita* therefore provides evidence that Rabbi Yehuda admits the testimony of such a witness only if he says: I was with his murderers.

But the *gemara* presses further: the text says he was being taken out to be executed (*hayotzei lehayeireig*). Does this not prove that he was a convicted murderer? The *gemara* answers: *bedina de'umot ha'olam dlo dayki vekatli* — non-Jewish courts do not apply the rigorous standards of evidence required by Jewish law; they may execute on the basis of suspicion or insufficient evidence. His execution by a non-Jewish court does not establish him as halachically defined as a *rasha*.

The *gemara* notes, however, that this caveat is instructive: according to *Rav Menashya*'s position that a biblically disqualified witness is invalid even for *edut isha*, everyone agrees that a witness definitively established as a murderer through proper *beit din* proceedings could not testify for *edut isha*. It is only because his status was established by a non-Jewish court — which is halachically insufficient — that his testimony remains acceptable. What divides the *Tanna Kamma* and *Rabbi Yehuda* is therefore the question of self-incriminating confession: the *Tanna Kamma* disregards it via *palginan dibura* and *adam karov etzel atzmo*, while *Rabbi Yehuda* treats the self-declaration of "I killed him" as effective self-disqualification, though he accepts "I was among the killers."

## Sage & Vow

### Vow Annulment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הֶחָכָם שֶׁאָסַר אֶת הָאִשָּׁה בְּנֶדֶר עַל בַּעְלָהּ — הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִשָּׂאֶנָּה. מֵיאֲנָה, אוֹ שֶׁחָלְצָה בְּפָנָיו — יִשָּׂאֶנָּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא בֵּית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A **Sage who** refused to release a woman from a vow that **rendered the wife forbidden to her husband by** that **vow,** resulting in her being divorced from her husband, **may not marry her,** so as to avoid suspicion that he rendered her forbidden to her husband in order to marry her himself. However, a judge before whom a woman **performed refusal** when she was a minor, declaring that she did not desire the husband chosen for her by her family, **or before whom** she **performed *ḥalitza*, may marry her because** he was only one member of **the court,** thereby alleviating suspicion.

The next *mishnah* introduces another category of prohibited marriage. *Chacham she'asar et ha'ishah bineder al ba'alah* — a sage who, when asked to annul a woman's vow, ruled that the vow could not be annulled, with the consequence that the vow itself forbids her to her husband. The case is more extreme than simply a husband threatening divorce; the *neder* itself renders her halachically unavailable to her husband, making continued cohabitation impossible. Once the husband then divorces her, the sage who rendered that ruling may not marry her: *harei zeh lo yisaena*. His ruling directly brought about the divorce, and his subsequent marriage to the woman would cast retroactive doubt on the sincerity and integrity of his ruling.

### Beit Din Acts

The *mishnah* then carves out an exception. If the same sage was part of a *beit din* before which a minor performed *mi'un* — verbal refusal of a marriage contracted by her mother or brothers — or before which a *yevama* performed *chalitza*, in those cases he may subsequently marry the woman: *yisaena mipnei she'hu beis din*. The decisive factor is that he did not act alone; he was one member of a *beit din* and therefore cannot be said to have unilaterally engineered the woman's release. Whether the reasoning is that appearances are less problematic when a group acted together, or that collective deliberation is less susceptible to manipulation, the *mishnah* draws a clear line: independent action raises suspicion; action as part of a *beit din* does not.

### Mumcheh Expert

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָא הִתִּירָה — יִשָּׂאֶנָּה. בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בְּחַד — חַד מִי מָצֵי מַתִּיר? וְהָאָמַר (רַב אָמַר) רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם, תָּנָא: הַתָּרַת נְדָרִים בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה! וְאֶלָּא, בִּתְלָתָא. מִי חֲשִׁידִי? וְהָתְנַן: מֵיאֲנָה אוֹ שֶׁחָלְצָה בְּפָנָיו — יִשָּׂאֶנָּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא בֵּית דִּין. לְעוֹלָם בְּחַד, וְכִדְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּיָחִיד מוּמְחֶה, הָכָא נָמֵי: בְּיָחִיד מוּמְחֶה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna taught that a Sage who rendered a woman forbidden to her husband may not then marry her. The Gemara deduces from here: **This** implies that if **he** rendered **her permitted** to her husband and she was later widowed or divorced, then **he may marry her.** The Gemara clarifies this: **With what are we dealing? If we say** that he was **a single** judge and not part of a court, **can a single** judge **dissolve** vows? **But didn't Rav say** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin said** that **Rav Amram said: It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Dissolution of vows** requires a court of **three** judges? **Rather,** could it be a case **of three** judges rather than one? In such a case, **would they be suspect** of distorting judgment? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: If **she performed refusal or performed *ḥalitza* before him, he may marry her because he** functioned as a member of **a court** of three? This teaches that there is no suspicion of a judge in a court of three. **Actually,** you should explain that this case is **that of a single** judge, **and** it is **as Rav Ḥisda said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** Vows may be dissolved even **by a single expert,** and a three-member court is not always necessary. **Here too,** it is referring to **a single expert** refusing to nullify her vow.

The *gemara* probes the *mishnah*'s case more carefully. If the *chacham* acted alone in ruling on the *neder*, how is that valid at all? Does not *hataras nedarim* require a *beit din* of three? The *gemara* cites the ruling: *tana hataras nedarim b'shlosha* — the annulment of vows requires three. And if we assume it was done by a *beit din* of three, why would any single member be suspect? The *gemara* answers: *le'olam b'chad* — the *mishnah* deals with a single individual. The basis for this is the ruling of *Rav Chisda* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *b'yachid mumcheh* — a single recognized expert (*mumcheh*) can annul vows independently, just as three laypeople can. The *mishnah* is thus dealing with the case of a solitary *mumcheh* who ruled that the vow could not be annulled; since he acted alone, he is subject to suspicion and may not marry the woman.

### Witnesses Self-Interest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי חֲשִׁידִי? וְהָתְנַן: עֵדִים הַחֲתוּמִים עַל שְׂדֵה מִקָּח, וְעַל גֵּט אִשָּׁה לֹא חָשׁוּ חֲכָמִים לְדָבָר זֶה? הִיא גּוּפַהּ קָמַשְׁמַע לַן, לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִמַּאן דְּאָמַר מֵיאוּן בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן מֵיאוּן בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** If so, in **what** way **is** this case **different from that which we learned** in a *baraita*: If **witnesses signed on the** document of **sale of a field or on a woman's bill of divorce, the Sages were not concerned about this matter** if one of the witnesses subsequently purchased the field or married the divorcée. Since there are two witnesses, there is no suspicion that they collaborated for the benefit of one of them. The Gemara answers: If there were two judges there would also be no concern; however, **this** mishna **itself** comes **to teach us** that a refusal must be performed before a full court, **to exclude** the opinion of **the one who said that refusal may be performed before two. This teaches us** that **refusal** must be performed before **three** judges.

The *gemara* then raises a case to test how broadly we extend the concern for group composition. A *baraita* states: *ein hem me'idim* — when two witnesses have signed a sale document for a field, or signed a woman's *get*, the sages were not concerned (*lo chashu chachamim l'davar zeh*) that a witness might subsequently purchase the field or marry the woman. Even when only two are involved — which is less than a *beit din* of three — since the individual is not acting alone, there is no disqualifying suspicion.

The *gemara* resolves this by noting that the *mishnah*'s reference to *beit din* is not primarily about requiring three rather than two; rather, it is clarifying in passing that *mi'un* requires a *beit din* of three, against those who hold that two suffice. The underlying principle for permitting remarriage, however, is simply that the individual did not act independently — and two suffices for that purpose.

## Knas Yotzi Debate

### Kahana vs. Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: כָּנַס, מַהוּ שֶׁיּוֹצִיא? רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: כָּנַס — מוֹצִיא. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כָּנַס — אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא. תָּנֵי לְהוּ רַב זוּטֵי דְּבֵי רַב פַּפֵּי כְּדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר כָּנַס אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא.
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages with regard to one who was prohibited from marrying a certain woman: If he nevertheless **married** her despite the prohibition, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **he must divorce** her? **Rav Kahana said:** If **he married** her, **he must divorce** her. **Rav Ashi said:** If **he married** her, **he need not divorce** her. **Rav Zuti from the school of Rav Pappi taught** the Sages a *baraita* **in accordance with the statement of the** one who **said that** if **he married** her, **he need not divorce** her.

The *gemara* now turns to what appears to be the implicit question underlying all these *mishnayot*: *kanas mah she'yotsi* — if one of these men went ahead and married the woman despite the prohibition, must he divorce her? *Rashi* notes that the question applies to both the case of the *chacham* and the *neder*, and the case of the one who brought the *get* or testified to the husband's death, though the *Rashba* limits it to the final *mishnah* alone.

*Rav Kahana* says *kanas motzi* — he must divorce. *Rav Ashi* says *kanas eino motzi* — he need not divorce. A *baraisa* taught by *Rav Zuti* in the academy of *Rav Papa* supports *Rav Ashi*'s position. When the rabbis asked *Rav Ashi* whether his ruling was a received tradition (*gemara*) or a logical derivation (*sevara*), he replied that it can be inferred from the *mishnah* itself.

### Rumor Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַב אָשֵׁי: גְּמָרָא, אוֹ סְבָרָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ, מַתְנִיתִין הִיא: הַנִּטְעָן מִשִּׁפְחָה וְנִשְׁתַּחְרְרָה, מִגּוֹיָה וְנִתְגַּיְּירָה — הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִכְנוֹס, וְאִם כָּנַס — אֵין מוֹצִיא. אַלְמָא בִּרְנָנָה — לָא מַפְּקִינַן, הָכָא נָמֵי — בִּרְנָנָה לָא מַפְּקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis said to Rav Ashi:** With regard to the *halakha* that you said, that if he married her he need not divorce her, was it based upon **tradition or** is it your own **conclusion? He said to them: It is the mishna.** I reached this conclusion from the wording of the mishna, which taught that **one suspected** by others of engaging in sexual relations **with** a Canaanite **maidservant and she** was subsequently **set free, or with a gentile woman and she** subsequently **converted may not marry** that woman. **But if he did marry her, they,** the judges of the court, **do not remove** her from him. **Apparently, we do not remove her** from her husband owing to suspicion **due to rumor** alone. **Here too, we do not remove her** from her husband **due to a rumor.**

The proof is from an earlier *mishnah* dealing with cases of suspected relationships — conversion, suspected adultery, and the like — where the *mishnah* rules: *im kanas eino motzi* — if he married her after the fact, he need not divorce. The principle derived is *birnana lo mafkinan* — we do not force divorce on the basis of rumor and communal suspicion alone. That same principle, the *gemara* concludes, applies here: *hacha nami birnana lo mafkinan*.

### Vow vs. Get

One might object, however, that the cases are not truly parallel. In the earlier cases of suspected adultery and conversion, even if the suspicion were true, the woman was still legally divorced or widowed; the only concern was reputational. Here, in the case of the *get* or the death testimony, if the underlying concern were true — if the *get* were invalid or the husband were still alive — the woman would be *eishes ish*, a married woman, which is a far more serious problem than mere appearances. This explains why the *Rashba* restricts the *gemara*'s ruling to the case of the *neder*: there, even if the *chacham* manipulated his ruling, the husband still gave a *get*, and the woman's divorce is halachically valid. The only issue is one of appearances and suspicion, to which *birnana lo mafkinan* properly applies. In the *get* and death-testimony cases, by contrast, there may be a genuine doubt about the woman's legal status — and that is a substantially weightier concern.

## Marriage Allowed

### Wife Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְכוּלָּם שֶׁהָיוּ לָהֶם נָשִׁים וּמֵתוּ — מוּתָּרוֹת לִינָּשֵׂא לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And** for **all of these** who were involved in permitting the wife to remarry, i.e., the judge, the agent who brought a bill of divorce, and the one who testified for a woman that her husband died, if **they had wives** at the time of the ruling or the testimony **and** their wives **died** thereafter, then those women they had set free **are permitted to be married to them.** There is no concern that while their wives were still alive these individuals set their eyes upon another woman.

The following *mishnah* addresses when these same individuals — the *chacham* who could not annul the vow, the one who delivered the *get*, the one who testified to the husband's death — may in fact marry the woman. *Ve'kulam she'hoyu lahem nashim u'mesu mutarot le'hinasay lahem* — if all of these men were already married at the time they performed the relevant act, and their wives subsequently died, then the women they enabled to remarry are permitted to them. The logic is sociological: even in a society where polygamy was technically permitted, it was exceedingly rare in practice, and was essentially limited to men wealthy enough to support multiple households. The *mishnah* therefore takes it as a practical given that a married man would not seek to marry an additional woman. If he was already married when he delivered the *get* or testified to the husband's death, we do not suspect that he engineered the whole situation in order to marry her. One might ask: couldn't he simply have planned to divorce his own wife? The *mishnah* here says nothing about that scenario — and the *gemara* addresses it directly below.

### Marital Strife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתוּ — אִין, נִתְגָּרְשׁוּ — לָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הִלֵּל לְרַב אָשֵׁי, וְהָתַנְיָא: אֲפִילּוּ נִתְגָּרְשׁוּ! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דַּהֲוַאי קְטָטָה, הָא דְּלָא הֲוַאי קְטָטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that if any of the men had wives who subsequently died, they may marry those women freed by them. The Gemara deduces from here: **If** the wives of those who rendered the woman permitted **died, yes,** they are permitted to marry the woman that they freed for marriage; but if the wives **were divorced, no,** it is prohibited. In such a case, marrying the woman that one had rendered permitted would raise suspicions that he had in fact planned to marry her all along. **Rav Hillel said to Rav Ashi: Yet it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Even if they were divorced** from their first wives they are permitted to marry the women they freed. The Gemara responds: **This** is **not difficult: This** mishna is referring to a case **when there was a quarrel** between the husband and the wife at the time that he freed the other woman, as then there is legitimate concern that he was already interested in her. **That** case of the *baraita* **was when there was no quarrel** between them at the time, and therefore the divorce clearly resulted from some other reason.

The *gemara* draws a pointed inference: *mesu in, nisgarshu lo* — the *mishnah* permits marriage only when his wife died, not when he divorced her. If he divorced his wife, we can suspect that it was all part of a scheme: he manipulated the *get* or the death testimony, and then divorced his own wife so that he could marry this other woman, while the sequence created the appearance of propriety.

*Rav Hillel* then challenges this from a *baraisa* which states *afilu nisgarshu* — even if they divorced their wives, it is permitted. The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya, ha de-havei ketata, ha de-la havei ketata* — it depends on whether there was prior marital strife (*ketata*) or not.

*Rashi*'s reading here is counterintuitive and worth examining carefully. One might assume that if the couple was already fighting before the *get* was delivered, the suspicion would be heightened — the man was already contemplating divorce and could therefore have planned to marry this other woman all along. *Rashi* says the opposite: if they were already fighting beforehand, then his divorce of his wife was not precipitated by this woman. He would have divorced his wife regardless. The plan to leave his wife predated the woman's *get*, and therefore we do not say it was all engineered for her sake.

The *gemara* then offers a second answer: *iba'it eima, ha de'argil hu, ve'ha de'argilah hee* — even without any pre-existing fighting, it matters who was the one pressing for divorce. If he was the one pushing for the divorce of his own wife, he cannot then marry the woman, because it looks as though he engineered everything. If she — his own wife — was the one pushing for the divorce, we say he was not the architect of the situation, and he may marry the other woman. The *gemara* elsewhere acknowledges that interpersonal dynamics can be manipulated to induce one's spouse to seek a divorce, but the *mishnah* apparently does not press suspicion that far.

### Intervening Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁנִּישְּׂאוּ לַאֲחֵרִים, וְנִתְגָּרְשׁוּ אוֹ שֶׁנִּתְאַלְמְנוּ — מוּתָּרוֹת לִינָּשֵׂא לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **all of these** women who were prohibited from marrying a certain man due to some suspicion, if **they were** subsequently **married to others and** then **were divorced or widowed** from the second husband, **they are permitted to be married to them,** i.e., to the judge, messenger, or witness who permitted her to remarry.

The *mishnah* further states: *ve'khulan she-nisu le'acherim ve'nisgarshu o she'nisalmenu mutarot le'hinase lahem* — if these women went ahead and married other men, and those second husbands then divorced them or died, the original witnesses may now marry them. Since the woman first married someone else, no one could reasonably conclude that the witness manipulated the original *get* or death testimony in order to marry her himself — for if he truly had wanted to marry her, he would not have wanted her to marry another man in the interim.

### Relatives Allow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן מוּתָּרוֹת לִבְנֵיהֶם אוֹ לַאֲחֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And all of these** women who were prohibited from marrying the man who freed them due to some suspicion **are permitted to the sons or to the brothers** of those who set them free.

Finally: *ve'khulan mutarot li'vneihem o le'acheihem* — all of these women may marry the sons or brothers of the witnesses who enabled their remarriage. We do not extend suspicion so far as to imagine that a man would falsify testimony or manipulate a vow ruling so that a *relative* of his could marry the woman. The concern we have is self-interested scheming; we do not carry it over to third parties.

### Women Visit; Men Don't

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: הַנִּטְעָן מִן הָאִשָּׁה, אָסוּר בְּאִמָּהּ וּבְבִתָּהּ וּבַאֲחוֹתָהּ. נְשֵׁי לְגַבֵּי נְשֵׁי שְׁכִיחָן דְּאָזְלָן. גַּבְרֵי לְגַבֵּי גַּבְרֵי לָא שְׁכִיחָן. אִי נָמֵי: נְשֵׁי דְּלָא אָסְרָן שְׁכִיבָתָן אַהֲדָדֵי, לָא קָפְדִי אַהֲדָדֵי. גַּבְרֵי דְּאָסְרָן שְׁכִיבָתָן אַהֲדָדֵי, קָפְדִי אַהֲדָדֵי. אִי הָכִי, אָבִיו נָמֵי? לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא אָבִיו, דִּבְזִיז בְּנֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ, אֲבָל בְּנוֹ, דְּלָא בְּזִיז אָבִיו מִינֵּיהּ — אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **And all of these** women who were prohibited from marrying the man who freed them due to some suspicion **are permitted to the sons or to the brothers** of those who set them free. The Gemara asks: In **what** way **is** this case **different from that which we learned** in the *Tosefta* (4:5) that **one suspected** of adultery **with** a specific **woman is prohibited** not only from marrying her, but also **from** marrying **her mother, and her daughter, and her sister.** Yet here we allow his sons and brothers to marry the woman despite the suspicion. The Gemara answers: There is a distinction between the situations, for **it is common for women** to be **at** the house of other **women** and to stay overnight. Therefore, there is concern that a relative of the alleged adulterer's wife, with whom he was suspected of misconduct, might frequent his house and he might be tempted to repeat his transgression. On the other hand, **it is not common for men** to be **at** the house of other **men,** so that even if she was married to his relative, the one suspected of misconduct would not generally sleep at the house of the husband. **Alternatively,** a different argument could be made: **Women are not so strict with one another, as lying with them** and having sexual relations with them **does not render them mutually forbidden.** In other words, if a man commits adultery with his wife's close relative, his wife does not become forbidden to him, so she may not pay attention to his behavior with the woman under suspicion. However, **men are strict with one another, as lying with them** and having sexual relations with the other man's wife **does render them,** the husband and wife, **mutually forbidden.** In other words, if another man has relations with a married woman, she is forbidden to her husband, and so men pay close attention to what the others are doing. The Gemara asks: **If so, one's father** should be permitted to marry a woman set free by his son **as well.** So why does the mishna say: Their sons or their brothers, and not: Their fathers? The Gemara answers: Certainly his father is allowed as well, but the mishna **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary. It is not necessary** to mention the case of **his father** because he is most certainly permitted to marry a woman set free by his son, **as his son is embarrassed [*baziz*] before him** and so would not come to sleep with his father's wife. **But I might say that** since **the father is not embarrassed before his son,** she **may not** be married to the son of one for whom there is suspicion. Therefore, **this comes to teach us** that there is no such concern.

The *gemara* then raises a challenge from a *baraisa* that seems to go further in its prohibitions. Someone suspected of an affair with a woman is forbidden to marry her mother, daughter, or sister — *asur be'imah u've'vitah u've'achotah*. If we extend concern so broadly in that case, why do we not similarly prohibit the brother or son of the witness from marrying the woman?

The answer, as *Rashi* clarifies, is important. The prohibition against marrying the mother, daughter, or sister in the suspicion case is not because the man has already sinned by sleeping with the woman before marriage. Rather, the concern is prospective: once he is married to the mother or daughter, the woman herself will visit — *nashi l'gabei nashi shekichan de'azlan* — because women in a family regularly visit one another. He will then have opportunity to continue the affair, and at that point it will constitute the biblical prohibition of *eishes ish* or the forbidden union of a wife and her close female relative.

The same logic does not apply when it is a brother or son who marries the woman. *Gavri l'gabei gavri lo shekichan* — men do not visit each other's households with the same frequency and intimacy. The opportunity for an illicit encounter is therefore substantially less.

The *gemara* offers an additional distinction: *nashi d'lo asran shechivatan ahadadi lo kapdi ahadadi* — women are not halachically barred from their husbands simply because the husband slept with their relative, and therefore the wife is not vigilantly guarding against this. *Gavri d'asran shechivatan ahadadi kapdi ahadadi* — men, by contrast, are very much on guard, because if a brother-in-law were to sleep with his wife, she would become forbidden to her husband and the marriage would have to end. That vigilance itself diminishes the opportunity.

A further question: if so, why does the *mishnah* permit the *father* of the witness to marry the woman? Surely the father is also a male relative. The *gemara* answers: the *mishnah* uses the formulation *lo tzricha* — it need not even be stated. It is obvious that the father may marry the woman, because a son is embarrassed to initiate an affair with his own father's wife. The *mishnah* finds it necessary to state only the case of the son, where one might have thought that the father — feeling a parental sense of authority or access — might take advantage, and therefore comes to teach that even the son's case is permitted.

## Four Brothers

### Sisters & Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַרְבָּעָה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶם נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וּמֵתוּ הַנְּשׂוּאִים אֶת הָאֲחָיוֹת — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת. וְאִם קָדְמוּ וְכָנְסוּ — יוֹצִיאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **four brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters, and the ones married to the sisters died, then those** sisters must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.** Since both sisters require levirate marriage with each of the surviving brothers, a levirate bond exists between each sister and the brothers. Each of them is considered the sister of a woman with whom each brother has a levirate bond and is therefore forbidden to him by rabbinic law. **And if they married** the sisters **before** consulting the court, **they should divorce** them, for the Sages decreed that in this situation they may not remain married.

The final *mishnah* of the *amud* returns to territory that has been covered multiple times in *Yevamot*, though it bears revisiting. *Arba'ah achim* — four brothers. Two of them, let us call them Reuven and Shimon, are married to two sisters, Rachel and Leah. Both Reuven and Shimon die. *Harei elu choltzot ve'lo mityabmot* — the surviving brothers, Levi and Yehuda, may not perform *yibum* with either sister; they may only perform *chalitza*.

### Achat Zekukato

The reason is the prohibition of *achot zekukato*. *Zika* — the quasi-marital bond that the *Torah* creates between a *yevama* and her *yavam* — connects Levi to both Rachel and Leah simultaneously. Rachel is the sister of the woman to whom Levi stands in a *zika* relationship, and *vice versa*. Therefore neither may be taken in *yibum*.

### One Ervah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה אַחַת מֵהֶן אֲסוּרָה עַל הָאֶחָד אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — אָסוּר בָּהּ, וּמוּתָּר בַּאֲחוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If **one of** the sisters **was forbidden to one** of the brothers due to **a prohibition against forbidden relations** because she was a relative of his wife or a relative on his mother's side, then **he is forbidden to** marry **her but permitted to** marry **her sister.**

The *mishnah* then refines the analysis. *Haysa achat me'hen asurah alav issur ervah* — if one of the women was an *ervah* to one of the brothers — his own daughter, for example — then that *yavam* is free to perform *yibum* with the other sister. An *issur ervah* removes the *zika* connection entirely, releasing him to marry the remaining *yevama*.

### Sister Options

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה אֲסוּרָה עַל זֶה אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — הָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה, וְהָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה. וְזוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ: אֲחוֹתָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא יְבִמְתָּהּ — אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** If **one of those** women **was forbidden to this** one brother due to **a prohibition against forbidden relations and the second** woman **was forbidden to that** second brother due to **a prohibition against forbidden relations,** then **she who is forbidden to this** brother **is permitted to that** brother, **and she who is forbidden to that** brother **is permitted to this** one. **And this is** the case that was referred to when **they said: When her sister is** also **her *yevama*,** i.e., in a case where two sisters are also *yevamot* and therefore happened for levirate marriage before two brothers, **she either performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage.** This must be referring to a case where each sister is forbidden to one of the brothers due to a prohibition concerning forbidden relatives. In this case, each sister has a levirate bond only with the one brother to whom she is permitted, and the prohibition against marrying the sister of a woman with whom one has a levirate bond does not apply. Therefore, each brother can either perform the act of *ḥalitza* or consummate the levirate marriage with the sister to whom he is not related.

In that case: *achota k'she'hi yevimtah, o choletzet o mityabmenet* — when the sister stands in the relationship of both sister and *yevama*, she has full options: either *chalitza* or *yibum*. With one woman an *ervah* and thus out of the picture, the other is a straightforward *yevama* with no competing *zika*.

### Zika Question

The *mishnah* also addresses the case where the prohibition is merely *issur mitzvah* or *issur kedusha* — a rabbinic prohibition or a lesser *lav* — rather than a full *issur ervah*. In that case, the weaker prohibition is insufficient to sever the *zika*, and the woman remains *zekukato*. Only a genuine *ervah* can remove the *zika* bond entirely.

One might naturally ask: even granting all of the above, why not simply have Levi perform *yibum* with Rachel and Yehuda perform *yibum* with Leah? They come from two separate marriages (*mi'trei batei ka'atu*), and there is no *tzara* issue. Let each *yavam* take one *yevama*.

### Rava Prevention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: יֵשׁ זִיקָה. דְּאִי אֵין זִיקָה, מִכְּדִי הָנֵי מִתְּרֵי בָתֵּי קָאָתְיָין — הַאי לְיַיבֵּם חֲדָא וְהַאי לְיַיבֵּם חֲדָא? לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ אֵין זִיקָה, וּמִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר אָסוּר לְבַטֵּל מִצְוַת יְבָמִין. דִּלְמָא אַדִּמְיַיבֵּם חַד — מָיֵית אִידַּךְ, וְקָמְבַטֵּל מִצְוַת יְבָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara deduces from the *halakha* cited in the mishna: **Conclude from here** that the **levirate bond is substantial.** That is, the very obligation of levirate marriage creates a bond that is similar to marriage. **For if the levirate bond were not substantial,** why would these two women not enter into levirate marriage? **After all, these** two women **come from two households,** as each had a different husband, and they both require levirate marriage. **Let this** brother **consummate the levirate marriage with one** sister **and let that** brother **consummate the levirate marriage with** the **other** sister. The fact that the mishna requires *ḥalitza* in this situation indicates that the levirate bond is substantial and resembles marriage to the extent that each sister is forbidden to each brother due to the prohibition against marrying the sister of a woman to whom one has a levirate bond. The Gemara rejects this: **Actually, I** could **say to you** that according to this *tanna* **the levirate bond is not substantial,** and yet they are prohibited from entering levirate marriage for a different reason. It is **because** the *tanna* **holds that it is prohibited to negate the mitzva of levirate marriage.** It is prohibited to act in a way that would lead to a situation where the mitzva to perform levirate marriage is negated. How would this situation arise? **Perhaps before one** brother **consummates the levirate marriage, the other** brother **dies,** and only one brother remains. In that case, the second sister would also happen before him for levirate marriage, **and** by performing levirate marriage with one sister **he would** thereby **negate the mitzva of levirate marriage** with the other sister. When the remaining brother marries one of the sisters, the mitzva to enter into levirate marriage or perform *ḥalitza* is automatically negated from the second sister, as she is then forbidden to him as his wife's sister.

The *gemara* raises this question directly: *shma mina yesh zika* — the *mishnah*'s ruling implies that the concept of *zika* is operative, for otherwise, with no *zika*, there would be no obstacle to each *yavam* performing *yibum* with one of the sisters.

However, the *gemara* then considers the opposite position: *le'olam eima lach ein zika* — perhaps there is no *zika*. If so, what prevents each *yavam* from performing *yibum* with one woman? *Rava* offers an explanation: *mishum d'kasavar asur levatel mitzvas yavam* — it is prohibited to bring about a situation in which the *mitzvah* of *yibum* might be nullified. If, for example, Levi proceeds to perform *yibum* with Rachel, Leah now must wait for Yehuda. If Yehuda then dies in the interim, Leah is left without a *yavam* to perform either *yibum* or *chalitza*, and the *mitzvah* has been effectively nullified. If, on the other hand, Levi performs *chalitza* with Rachel, then even if Yehuda dies, Levi remains available and can perform *chalitza* with Leah as well, ensuring that neither woman is left in a state of unresolved *zika*. It is to prevent the potential nullification of the *mitzvah* that we require *chalitza* in this case — a far-reaching and admittedly somewhat attenuated rationale, but one that the *gemara* presents as Rava's resolution.
