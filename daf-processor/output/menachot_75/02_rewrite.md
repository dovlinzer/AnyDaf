# Menachot 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Types Menachot

### Overview & Categories

The Mishna on Daf Ayin-Daled Amud Bet describes the process by which Minchos are prepared. It states that all Minchos are first made by placing oil in the vessel, then adding flour and mixing it with oil, and finally performing Yetzikah — pouring oil on afterwards. The Gemara opens by noting that this applies specifically to Minchos that are made in a vessel, and proceeds to ask: what does that qualifier exclude?

### Minchat Soles

Rav Pappa answers that it excludes the Minchat Ma'afeh Tanur — the oven-baked Mincha. This is, on its face, a puzzling answer, and the Meforshim struggle with it. One might object on two grounds: first, the Minchat Ma'afeh Tanur does include oil according to the Torah, so it is not obviously different from the other Minchos in this respect; second, an oven is itself a vessel, so the criterion of "made in a vessel" would seem to apply equally to it. Tosafot addresses this by suggesting that perhaps according to this opinion the oven was not considered a Kli Shareis — but even so, that does not readily explain how the Mishna's language of "made in a vessel" succeeds in excluding it.

One of the Meforshim of the Rambam offers a different approach: when the Mishna states that "all Minchos made in a vessel" follow these three stages, the implication is that some things do not follow this rule — even if they technically are made in a vessel. The difficulty with this reading is obvious: if the thing we are excluding meets the stated criterion, the exclusion is logically strained. Rather than forcing this interpretation, the more productive approach is simply to understand what the Mishna is telling us affirmatively: these three oil stages — oil first in the vessel, then flour mixed with oil, then oil poured on afterwards — apply to the Minchat Marcheshes, Minchat Machvas, and Minchat Soles, but not to the Minchat Ma'afeh Tanur. The specific stage that does not apply to the oven-baked Mincha is, as we shall see, the final pouring of oil.

### Machvas & Marcheshes

Before turning to the Gemara's derivation, it is worth clarifying the four types of Minchos presented in Vayikra. The first is the Minchat Soles — simply flour and oil, not baked. The Rambam, notably, does not include water in the Minchat Soles, which makes sense: if one does not bake it, there is no mechanism by which it could become Chametz, and so no need for water to be present. The resulting product is essentially a raw dough of flour and oil; the Kohanim would eat the remainder after the Kometz is taken. The second and third types are the Machvas and Marcheshes — shallow and deep frying pans respectively. These do include water, because they do have the halakhic possibility of becoming Chametz, and the flour and oil and water mixture is then fried in one of the two pans. The fourth type is the Ma'afeh Tanur — flour, oil, and water are combined into a dough and baked in an oven. It is this last type that does not follow the same three oil stages as the others.

### Ma'afeh Tanur Offering

The Gemara cites a Tanna DeVei: the Braita opens by quoting the verse regarding the Marcheshes — "Soles bashamen te'aseh" — and derives from the phrase "bashamen" that oil is first placed in the vessel before the flour. Then, via a Gezeira Shava on the word "Korbanacha" — which appears both by the Machvas (Pasuk Hey) and by the Marcheshes (Pasuk Zayin) — the Gemara links the two: just as the Marcheshes requires oil to be placed in the vessel first, so too the Machvas; and just as the Machvas requires Yetzikah and Belilah — pouring and mixing — so too the Marcheshes.

To understand where the Yetzikah and Belilah come from by the Machvas, one must read Pesukim Hey and Vav together. Pasuk Hey says "Solet blulah bashamen" — the flour is mixed with oil. Pasuk Vav then says "Patot otah pitim, v'yatzakta aleihem shemen" — break it into pieces and pour oil over them. So by the Machvas: you mix the flour with oil (and water, since Chametz and Matzah are relevant), fry the dough, break it into pieces afterwards, and then pour oil on the broken pieces. From the Marcheshes: oil is already in the vessel before the flour goes in. Combining the two descriptions, one derives all three stages: (1) oil in the vessel first, (2) flour added and mixed with oil, and (3) oil poured on afterwards.

The Gemara will proceed to extend these three stages to the Minchat Soles as well — even though there is no frying or baking involved — and to exclude the oven-baked Mincha from the third stage. The precise reasoning for that exclusion will become clear as the discussion of the Minchat Ma'afeh unfolds.

## Three Oil Stages

### Pesukim Analysis

The Mishna and the subsequent Gemara discussion center on a Machloket between Rabbi (Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi) and the Chachamim concerning when the Belilah — the primary mixing of oil — takes place by the oven-baked Mincha.

The Braita states: "Solet belulah — melamed shenivelelet solet." The Chachamim understand this to mean that the flour itself is mixed with oil: at the flour stage, before any baking, one adds oil to the flour, mixes it together thoroughly (along with water, to enable the possibility of Chametz), kneads it, bakes it, breaks the baked product into pieces, and then performs the final pouring of oil. Rabbi, however, disputes this. He reads the operative phrase not from the Machvas but from the Ma'afeh Tanur, where the Torah says "chalot matzot belulot bashamen" — baked loaves mixed with oil. From this Rabbi derives that the Belilah, the serious mixing with oil, is performed on the baked product, not the flour. His view of the process is therefore: first place oil in the vessel, then add flour with water only (no additional oil at the flour stage), knead it, bake it, break it into pieces, and only then — once the loaves are broken — mix them thoroughly with a large quantity of oil, and subsequently pour the remaining oil on top before performing the Kemitza.

It is noted that this is a striking position, since the plain reading of the verse by the Machvas — "solet blulah bashamen," flour mixed with oil — seems to describe the flour stage, not the baked stage. Rabbi is reading the paradigm of the oven-baked Mincha back into the general description. The Tosefta adds that in the end Rabbi retracted his position and agreed with the Chachamim.

### Oil Stages Explained

The Chachamim respond to Rabbi with a counter-example from the Todah offering: "Vehalo lachmei Todah nemar bahen chalot" — by the bread of the Todah the Torah also uses the language of "chalot" mixed with oil, "ve'i efshar levololan keshehen chalot" — yet it is impossible to mix them after they are already baked.

The Gemara clarifies why: Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak explains that the Todah brings forty loaves total, and twenty of those loaves receive only a Revi'is of oil — approximately three and a half ounces — split among them. Once loaves are baked they are highly absorbent, and three and a half ounces of oil distributed across twenty loaves cannot create any meaningful or coherent mixing. "Heich mislachlachas" — there is simply no way it would work. The oil must therefore have been added at the flour stage. This dismantles Rabbi's argument: you cannot use the language of "chalot belulot" to prove that mixing occurs after baking, because the very case that uses that language — the Todah — demonstrates that post-baking mixing is practically impossible.

### Combining Sources

Following the Chachamim's position, the Gemara sets out the proper procedure for the Machvas and Marcheshes: as described by the Braita cited earlier, the כיצד עושה is as follows. One first places oil in the vessel, adds flour, then adds more oil to the flour. Water is also added — though not mentioned explicitly in the Torah, it is required to create the possibility of Chametz versus Matzah. The dough is kneaded, then fried (on the flat pan or deep pan respectively). After frying, the product is broken into pieces using the Pesisa, and finally oil is poured on top. It is from this complete procedure that the three stages are drawn and applied to the Minchat Soles as well — with the modification that for the Soles there is no baking or frying stage, so the Rambam's view that water was not added there (and thus no possibility of Chametz arose) fits naturally.

The standard amount is one Issaron of flour — roughly equivalent to a five-pound bag — and one Log of oil, which is approximately fourteen ounces (four times a Revi'is, which is roughly three and a half ounces, the standard measure for a Kiddush cup on Shabbat). According to the Chachamim, that oil goes into the flour stage, not into a post-baking mixing, making Rabbi's position the more difficult one practically as well as textually.

## Rabbi vs. Chachamim

### Rabbi's Position

Returning to the Ma'afeh Tanur, the oven-baked Mincha, the Gemara clarifies the difference between its two forms. The Torah says: "Solet challos matzot belulot bashamen, u'rekikei matzot meshuchim bashamen" — either loaves mixed with oil, or wafers anointed with oil. The Gemara understands this as establishing two fundamentally different treatments of oil. The Challos — the loaves — have oil incorporated into the dough at the flour stage, just as by the Machvas and Marcheshes, and they are then baked. The Rekikin — the wafers — are baked simply from a dough of flour and water, with no oil added beforehand, and only after they come out of the oven is oil applied to them. The difference in terminology is precise: Belilah, mixing, applies to the Challos; Meshichah, anointing, applies to the Rekikin.

This is precisely why the Ma'afeh Tanur does not follow the three-stage model of the other Minchos. For the other Minchos, oil appears at all three stages — first in the vessel, then mixed with the flour, then poured on after frying or baking. For the Ma'afeh Tanur, the Torah specifies two distinct and mutually exclusive treatments: the Challos get their oil during mixing, the Rekikin get their oil afterwards. The Gemara therefore concludes: "Challos te'unos belilah, ve'rekikin meshichah" — Challos require the mixing, Rekikin require the anointing.

### Todah Problem

The Braita then raises what might seem like a logical argument: since the Challos require Belilah, and the Rekikin require Meshichah, could one not argue by Kal VaChomer that the Rekikin — which already get anointed with oil at the end — should also receive oil at the mixing stage? After all, if the Challos, which do not get anointed, nevertheless receive mixed oil, surely the Rekikin, which do get anointed, should also receive oil at mixing? The Torah answers by specifying "Challos belulos velo rekikin belulin" — only the Challos are mixed. Similarly, the reverse Kal VaChomer: perhaps the Challos, which already get mixed, should also get the post-baking anointing? The Torah answers: "Rekikin meshuchim velo challos meshuchos" — only the Rekikin are anointed.

Rava then provides the justification for why we do not simply learn them from one another — as we do with the Machvas and Marcheshes — and require both treatments for both. He employs a "lo lishtamit kra" argument: the verse never, in any context, refers to "Challos meshuchos" or "Rekikin belulin." It only ever specifies Challos with Belilah and Rekikin with Meshichah. Since the Torah consistently maintains this distinction and never inverts it, we should understand the exclusive assignment as intentional.

This is, however, a notably weak form of argument from silence. The strength of a lo lishtamit argument depends on whether one would have expected the alternative to have been stated somewhere had it been true. In this case, the Torah discusses the Ma'afeh Tanur only once, and by analogy we do apply Machvas to Marcheshes even though neither verse states the other's rule explicitly. The argument from silence here thus carries considerably less logical force than it might in other contexts — a point the Gemara implicitly acknowledges by framing it as Rava's response, rather than a direct textual derivation.

### Process Explained

Having established that the Rekikin receive Meshichah rather than Belilah, the Gemara asks: "Keitzad moshchan?" — how exactly does one anoint them? The answer is "k'min ki" — in the shape of a Chi. Rav Kahana explains this means k'min ki Yevani — the Greek letter Chi, which is an X. After this anointing, whatever oil remains from the Log is eaten as Shirayim.

It is worth noting that Rashi and Tosafot display some uncertainty about the exact shape. Tosafot records that in one version of Rashi's commentary on Chumash the shape is described as a Tet, and in another as a Gimmel — though Tosafot correctly notes neither is quite right, while acknowledging that a Gimmel, with its diagonal stroke and attached line, comes closest among Hebrew letters to approximating an X. The Rambam, however, clearly identifies the pattern as an X.

## Challos vs. Rekikin

### Measurements

The Gemara now turns to a case, formulated under Rabbi Shimon's assumption that a Ma'afeh Tanur offering may be brought as half Challos and half Rekikin. A Braita states: if one brings a Mincha that is half Challos and half Rekikin, one brings a Log of oil and divides it in two. Half — approximately seven ounces — goes toward the Challos, where it is mixed into the dough at the flour stage. The other half goes toward the Rekikin, which are baked first as plain dough and then anointed afterwards.

However, the Braita adds a further detail: "U'mosheach es ha'rekik pnei kulo" — when anointing the Rekikin, one anoints the full outer surface of the loaf. After performing this anointing, the remaining oil — and there will be a significant amount remaining if one has seven ounces and is merely coating the surface — is returned to the Challos. This means the Rekikin must be processed first, so that whatever oil is left over can be folded back into the Challos dough before baking.

### Two Baked Types

Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Shimon disagrees. He holds that the anointing is done "k'min ki" — in the X-pattern only — and the remaining oil is not returned to the Challos but rather eaten by the Kohanim. This is a striking position: normally everything in the Mincha is understood to be incorporated into the final product. Rabbi Shimon apparently holds that where the modality of oil application is defined as anointing — Meshichah — rather than mixing, the oil that is not absorbed into the wafer itself does not need to be incorporated into the Mincha as a whole. It is analogous, he suggests, to the Log shemen of the Metzora, which is brought separately and partially eaten rather than fully absorbed. The majority view, by contrast, requires the full Log to be consumed within the Mincha offering, whether by repeated anointing until the oil is exhausted, or by return to the Challos.

A subsequent Braita addresses the case of bringing Rekikin alone, without any Challos. Here one has a full Log of oil — fourteen ounces — and one is applying it only through anointing. The first opinion holds that one anoints and anoints again repeatedly until the entire Log is absorbed: "Umoshchan v'chozer umoshchan ad she'yichleh kol hashemen she'be'log." Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Shimon repeats his position: make a single X, and the remainder is eaten by the Kohanim.

## Ptita Requirement

### Anointing Method

The next Mishna states: "Kol haMinchos hana'asos b'kli te'unos Pesisa" — all Minchos made in a vessel require breaking into pieces. This rule applies broadly: the Machvas, Marcheshes, and oven-baked Minchos all require Pesisa, though not the Minchat Soles in the usual sense, since it is a raw dough and the verse "patot otah pitim" does not apply to flour.

### Half-and-Half Mix

The Gemara asks "l'ma'utei mai" — what does this qualifier exclude? Rav Pappa answers: it excludes the Shtei HaLechem and Lechem HaPanim. Neither of these communal offerings requires Pesisa.

The Braita derives the requirement from the verse by the Machvas: "Patot otah pitim — Mincha hi" — it is a Mincha, which might suggest the rule extends to all Minchos, including the Shtei HaLechem and Lechem HaPanim. The word "otah" — "it," in the singular and limiting — restricts the application and excludes certain categories. Similarly, "v'yatzakta aleha shemen — Mincha hi" might have suggested extending the final oil-pouring to the Ma'afeh Tanur; "aleha," "on it," limits it, and "hi" introduces a second restriction.

Two reasons are given for why Pesisa does not apply to the Shtei HaLechem and Lechem HaPanim. First, the primary function of Pesisa is to enable Kemitza — once the Mincha is broken into small pieces, the Kohen can take his three-finger handful. The Shtei HaLechem and Lechem HaPanim do not require Kemitza at all: the Lechem HaPanim has its Levonah burned, and the Shtei HaLechem is accompanied by the Kivsei Atzeres. Second, these are communal offerings and are thus outside the framework of the individual Mincha described in Parshat Vayikra. It is the combination of both factors — no Kemitza and communal status — that removes them from the Pesisa requirement. The Omer, by contrast, is also a communal offering but does require Pesisa, because it does require Kemitza.

### Full Rekikin

Rava then addresses the question of why the two rules — Pesisa and the final oil pouring — have different scopes. Pesisa extends to the Ma'afeh Tanur; the final oil-pouring does not. Why does the single word "otah" exclude only the Shtei HaLechem and Lechem HaPanim from Pesisa, while the oil rule requires two exclusionary terms ("aleha" and "hi") to exclude the Ma'afeh Tanur?

Rava's answer is that the Ma'afeh Tanur has two components — Challos and Rekikin — and thus it needs two limiting words to exclude both of them from the general rule of post-baking oil pouring. The Shtei HaLechem and Lechem HaPanim, being single categories, need only one limiting word to exclude them from Pesisa. Since the Torah itself describes the oil of the Ma'afeh Tanur differently for Challos and Rekikin, we do not derive the oil stages of the Ma'afeh Tanur from the Machvas and Marcheshes — we accept the Torah's own account of how oil is applied there.

## Bracha on Menachot

### Ptita Derivation

The next Mishna describes the method of Pesisa — the breaking into pieces — for different categories of Mincha. For a Minchat Yisrael — brought by a non-Kohen — the procedure is: "Kofel echad l'shnayim v'shnayim l'arba" — fold it once in half, then fold that in half again, producing four sections, and then "mavdil" — separate the pieces. The baked or fried product, whether it resembles something like a flat bread or a pita, is folded into quarters and then the quarters are pulled apart.

### Folding Process

For a Minchat Kohanim, one folds it in the same manner — one fold then another, making four — but one does not actually separate the pieces. Since a Minchat Kohanim is burned entirely on the Mizbe'ach and no Kemitza is taken, there is no practical need to achieve discrete pieces. Yet the Chachamim hold that one still performs the folding, without the final separation.

### Kohanim Offerings

The Mishna states that for the Minchat Kohen Gadol — the Chavitin brought half in the morning and half in the afternoon — "lo haya mekapla," he would not fold it at all, according to the first position. The Gemara, however, reconciles this with a Braita that says you do fold the Minchat Kohen Gadol, by attributing different meanings to each source. Rava explains: "Eino mekapel l'arba, aval mekapel l'shnayim" — the Mishna means he does not fold it into four, but he does fold it once into two. This is consistent with the Torah's description of the Kohen Gadol's Mincha as "tufinei minchat pitim" — the word "pitim" appears once, not the fuller phrase "patot otah pitim" which implies a double fold. One instance of "pitim" yields one fold.

### Rabbi Shimon View

Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the Chachamim on two counts. First, he holds that the Minchat Kohanim and Minchat Kohen Gadol require no Pesisa whatsoever: "Ein bahem ptita, mipnei she'ein bahem kmitza" — because there is no Kemitza, there is no Pesisa. "V'chol she'ein ba kmitza ein bahem ptita." Second, he holds that for those Minchos that do require Pesisa, the pieces should be broken down to the size of a Kezayit — olive-sized — rather than merely folded into quarters. At olive-size, one can readily perform Kemitza from the pieces.

Rabbi Shimon's two positions are internally consistent: the entire logic of Pesisa is to enable Kemitza. Therefore, if Kemitza is absent, Pesisa is absent entirely; and where Kemitza is performed, the pieces must be small enough to actually allow it. The Chachamim, by contrast, hold that Pesisa carries independent significance beyond merely enabling Kemitza — otherwise, why require it for the Minchat Kohanim with only folding and no separation?

## Deriving Pesisa Requirem…

### Hamotzi Source

The Gemara derives the proper method of Pesisa from the verse "patot otah pitim." The word "pitim" might have implied breaking each piece again — pieces of pieces. The word "otah" — "it," referring to the original — limits this: you break the original once into pieces, but you do not then take those pieces and break them again. "V'lo ptiteha l'pitim" — do not break the pieces into further pieces.

At the same time, "pitim" — the plural — implies more than just two pieces. So the derivation runs: "Yachol l'shnayim" — maybe just two? No, "pitim" implies more. "Yachol ya'asenah perurin" — maybe crumbs, tiny fragments? No, "otah" requires that the original integrity be maintained. The result is the procedure described in the Mishna: the Minchat Yisrael is folded into halves and then quarters — two folds, producing four sections.

### Abaye Challenge

The Braita then states that the Minchat Kohanim and Minchat Kohen Gadol were folded. The Mishna appears to say the Kohen Gadol did not fold at all. As Rava resolves: the Mishna means not folded into four; the Braita means he did fold into two. Both sources are compatible once we understand that the Mishna denies only the full four-fold process, not all folding.

This reconciliation highlights the underlying principle: the Chachamim see folding as possessing value independent of Kemitza — since even the Minchat Kohanim, where there is no Kemitza and no separation, involves folding. Rabbi Shimon, who sees Pesisa as exclusively instrumental to Kemitza, would have no use for any folding in a Minchat Kohanim.

## Bread Crumbs & Blessings…

### Crumb Status

Rav Yosef now introduces a ruling that connects directly to contemporary Halacha. He states: "Hai chavitza d'it beh perurin kezayit, mivarchinan aleha hamotzi lechem min ha'aretz" — a Chavitza that contains crumbs each of which is a Kezayit, one recites Hamotzi over it. If the crumbs are smaller than a Kezayit, one recites Borei minei mezonot.

The Chavitza requires clarification. Rashi understands it as a cooked or baked product containing bread crumbs. Tosafot, however, argues from the subsequent Gemara that the Chavitza was not rebaked or recooked — because rebaking could independently transform the Bracha — and that the later Gemara comparison to the broken Mincha pieces mixed with oil makes clear that the Chavitza is bread crumbs combined with honey or oil without further cooking. We proceed with Tosafot's understanding: it is crumbs embedded in a honey or oil mixture, not rebaked.

The practical analog is familiar: croutons or bread crumbs mixed into other ingredients. What Bracha does one recite? Rav Yosef's answer is that it depends on the size of the crumbs: Kezayit-sized crumbs yield Hamotzi; smaller crumbs yield Mezonot.

### Final Bracha Rule

Rav Yosef derives this principle from a Braita. The Braita describes someone bringing Minchos in Yerushalayim: upon beginning the offering, he says "Baruch shehecheyanu v'kiyemanu v'higi'anu lazman hazeh." The mere fact that a Shehecheyanu is recited over the bringing of a Korban is itself arresting — it is almost never discussed in this context. Whether it is the Yisrael who owns the Korban or the Kohen who performs the service is a matter of debate. Rashi offers two possibilities. Tosafot argues for the Kohen, noting that an individual Kohen, serving on rotation, would typically serve only twice a year, making each occasion genuinely significant in a seasonal, cyclical way — precisely the type of event over which Shehecheyanu is appropriate.

The Braita continues: when he then takes the Mincha pieces to eat them — "natlan v'achlan" — he recites Hamotzi. This clearly refers to the Kohen eating his portion, since only the Kohen eats the Minchos. And what do we know about those Minchos? From the earlier Mishna: "V'chulan potasim kezayis" — following the view that pieces can be as small as a Kezayit. So we have little pieces of baked bread, each at least a Kezayit, mixed with oil — and over these the Kohen makes Hamotzi. From this Rav Yosef infers the general rule about the Chavitza.

## Mincha Crumbs & Motzi Br…

### Braita on Blessed Offering

Abaye, however, challenges Rav Yosef's derivation. He argues that the source cannot be relied upon because of the opinion of the Tanna of the School of Rabbi Yishmael, not mentioned in our Mishna, who holds that the Pesisa process goes far further — pieces are broken down until they return essentially to flour: "Mefarchan ad shemachziran lesoltan." If we follow that view, the resulting material would no longer be recognizable bread, and one might then ask whether the Kohen would recite Hamotzi on it at all. If the answer were no, then Rav Yosef cannot cite the Kohen's Hamotzi as a reliable source for the minimum crumb-size principle.

### Abaye's Crumb-Size Question

Abaye then suggests: perhaps according to the Tanna of the School of Rabbi Yishmael, the Kohen indeed would not recite Hamotzi on such thoroughly pulverized material. The Gemara entertains this possibility in order to sharpen the question.

### Combined Crumbs Retain Motzi

To rebut the suggestion that Hamotzi cannot be recited on very small crumbs, the Gemara cites a Braita: "Liket mikulan kezayit v'achalo" — if one gathered from all the crumbs a Kezayit and ate it, then if it was Chametz on Pesach, one incurs Karet; and if it was Matzah on Pesach, one fulfills one's obligation. Tosafot understands this Braita as referring to the five grain species generally — crumbs from Chametz that are gathered to a Kezayit-total retain their identity as Chametz, and similarly Matzah crumbs gathered to a Kezayit fulfill the Mitzvah. The implication is that even small individual crumbs, when combined, are still considered bread for halakhic purposes.

### Braita on Chametz & Matzah Crumbs

The Gemara, however, pushes back against this citation. One might say: "Hacha b'mai askinan — b'she'ersan" — perhaps the Braita refers only to the case where the crumbs were re-moistened with water and kneaded back into a single dough — "aisa shleima." In that case, of course they would retain their identity as bread; it would be one unified mass. The Gemara, however, disproves this: the Braita concludes "vehu she'achlan bichdei achilas pras" — the condition is that one ate them within the time-frame of eating a half-loaf. If the crumbs had been reassembled into a single dough, the text would have said "she'achalah" — it, singular — not "she'achlan" — them, plural. The plural form indicates they remained separate crumbs. Therefore, even as separate crumbs, gathering them to a Kezayit retains Hamotzi and fulfills the Matzah obligation.

### Pesika Standard: Shape of Bread

The Gemara then resolves the apparent conflict between Rav Yosef's condition (crumbs must be Kezayit-sized) and the Braita showing that even sub-Kezayit crumbs can retain Hamotzi. Rav Sheshes rules: "Afilu perurin she'ein bahen kezayis" — even crumbs smaller than a Kezayit, one makes Hamotzi over them. Rava then qualifies: "Vehu d'ika toresa denahama alaihu" — only if they still bear the form, the recognizable shape, of bread.

The Gemara harmonizes all the sources: the requirement for a Kezayit applies when the bread crumbs never came from a large loaf to begin with — they were always small units. In such a case, they require Kezayit-size to warrant Hamotzi. But if they came from "lekhem gadol" — a large loaf — they retain Hamotzi even as small crumbs, so long as they still bear the form of bread. The Mincha of the Kohen clearly originated from a larger baked product; therefore, even once broken into tiny pieces with oil mixed in, Hamotzi is recited.

Rava's formulation — "toresa denahama" — is the most practically difficult criterion in the laws of Brachot. The principle is clear in the abstract: it is not enough that a food is made from flour and water; it must look like bread. Crackers and pretzels, made from identical ingredients, do not receive Hamotzi precisely because they fail this criterion. What exactly "looks like bread" means in practice is a major and ongoing question, but the rule itself is anchored here: Hamotzi requires both the correct composition and the recognizable form of bread.
