# Eruvin 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Amot & 2000

### Meir vs. Yehuda

We pick up several lines into the daf with the statement of Rami bar Chama. By way of review: the preceding discussion had established the terms of the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda regarding the designation of *shvisas Shabbat* — the declaration of where one intends to begin Shabbat. Rabbi Meir holds that one may establish Shabbat at one's current physical location without bread only if one is an *ani*, meaning one who is not beginning from home. Rabbi Yehuda holds that even an *ashir* — someone starting from home, with food readily available — may rely on physical presence alone, without requiring a loaf of bread. An *ani*, in this technical sense, is one who begins his Shabbat designation not from his own home, and thus has no accessible bread; an *ashir* is one who does have that access.

That debate, the Gemara clarified, applies specifically when one is physically present at the spot one wishes to designate. But in the special case of the mishnah — where one designates a *makom shvisah* at a spot one is not physically standing — both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda agree that this is permitted only for the *ani*, only for the person who did not begin the eruv process from home. This principle will bear directly on the mishnayot that follow.

### Distance Eruv

Rami bar Chama raises a question arising from these discussions. When one is physically present and declares *tehei shvisasi bimkomi* — let my Shabbat rest be in this place — it is established that one receives a square of four *amot* surrounding oneself, plus 2000 *amot* extending outward from the edges of that square, yielding in practice 2004 *amot*. The question is whether the same geometry applies when one designates an eruv through a loaf of bread placed at a distant location. Does one measure a four-*ama* square around the bread and then 2000 *amot* beyond it — just as one measures the city first and then extends 2000 *amot* beyond the city's boundary — or does one simply measure 2000 *amot* from the bread itself, without any preliminary square?

### Bread Leniency

Rava responds: *ta shma* — come and hear from the language of the mishnah itself. The mishnah states that the use of bread was instituted not as a requirement but as a *kula* — a leniency — for the *ashir*, so that he would not need to go out in person (*shelo yeitzei beraglav*). This being so, if using bread meant that one would receive only 2000 *amot* without the additional four-*ama* square, the supposed leniency would carry a hidden cost, rendering it in some respects a *chumra* rather than a *kula*.

The Gemara, however, deflects this argument. Even if bread comes with a slight disadvantage in terms of total *techum*, it is still overwhelmingly beneficial: one need not go out personally, one need not wait near the spot as darkness falls, and a representative may deposit the eruv without personal effort. The convenience outweighs the marginal geometric cost. The Gemara therefore leaves unresolved whether bread-placement generates a surrounding four-*ama* square. What is clear is that physical presence at a spot does generate that square, in precise parallel to the way a city generates its own area first, before the 2000 *amot* are measured outward.

### Squaring & Compass

Before turning to the next mishnah, it is worth consolidating some of the arresting conclusions introduced on the previous daf. We ordinarily speak of 2000 *amot* as if that is simply a distance measured from where one stands. But we have now learned that: (a) even when one starts outside a city, the 2000 *amot* begins from the edge of a four-*ama* square centered on the person, not from the person's precise point; (b) it remains debated whether that square is four-by-four with the person at its center, or eight-by-eight; and (c) the square is oriented to the compass points, not simply to whatever direction one happens to be facing.

This squaring principle will be central to the fifth perek which we are about to begin. The Gemara also raised, in the context of carrying four *amot* in a *reshut harabim* and the minimum size of a *reshut hayachid* of four *tefachim* by four *tefachim*, the possibility that these measurements are also always understood relative to compass-aligned squares. One opinion — attributed to Rabbeinu Tam — holds that the four-by-four space of an object is always conceived as a compass-aligned square, so that carrying an object due north constitutes carrying it four *amot*, while carrying it at a 45-degree angle to the compass would involve the diagonal of that square, approximately 5.6 *amot*. A related view holds that an object occupying a space that is larger than four-by-four but is rotated relative to the compass may not qualify as a *reshut hayachid* if no compass-aligned four-by-four square can be inscribed within it. Rashi's position is that a square is required, full stop. These are striking departures from the way we ordinarily think about carrying and spatial definitions.

## Traveler Case

### Setup

The mishnah introduces the following case: someone left City A on Friday afternoon, intending to spend Shabbat in City B. Before he reached City B, his friend persuaded him to turn around and return home to City A. His intention, however, remained to reach City B the following day. The question is whether his eruv designation is effective.

The mishnah rules: *hu mutar* — he himself is permitted to travel toward City B on Shabbat — *ve'chol bnei ha'ir assurim* — but all the other residents of City A are forbidden to do so. The reason the townspeople cannot travel is not merely that they failed to make an eruv. Even if they had explicitly stayed home and announced their intention to designate a distant tree as their eruv, it would not have worked. This is because designating a distant spot — being physically absent from the eruv location and using no bread — is, as we established, permitted only for an *ani*. The residents of City A are each an *ashir*; they began the process from home. Only the traveler, who began his Shabbat designation in the course of an actual journey, is treated as an *ani* and may designate a distant spot.

### Ani Status

Rav Huna clarifies the precise conditions: the case involves someone who owns a house in City A and a house in City B, with the two cities separated by two *techumei Shabbat* — that is, 4000 *amot* — and a landmark (such as a tree) at the midpoint. Since this traveler had already set out on his journey (*keivan d'nafik lei l'orcha*), he is considered an *ani* for these purposes. He did not begin the process of eruv-making from his home; he was already in motion. The act of beginning a journey, *hechezik baderech*, removes him from the category of one whose eruv starts at home.

### Ashir Cannot

The other residents of City A, by contrast, never left home. They remain *ashirim* in the halachic sense — they have access to bread, they are rooted at their point of departure — and therefore they cannot designate a distant spot without bread or physical presence. Their verbal declarations of intention, however sincere, are legally ineffective.

### Chamor Gamal

Rabbi Meir's position introduces a significant complication. He says: *kol she'hu yachol le'areiv velo eireiv* — anyone who could have made a valid eruv and failed to do so — *harei zeh chamor gamal*, is like a donkey being pulled by a camel driver in both directions at once. What does this mean in practice?

Consider the geography: City A on one side, City B on the other, with 2000 *amot* in each direction to a central landmark. According to Rabbi Yehuda, our traveler is an *ani* and his eruv designation works. He gains the 2000 *amot* toward City B on Shabbat. According to Rabbi Meir, however, this traveler is considered an *ashir* — he started his journey from his home in City A, and therefore he had the ability to make a proper eruv with bread. Having failed to do so effectively, he forfeits the benefit of his home base in City A without gaining the benefit of a valid eruv at the landmark. What remains to him? Only the 2000 *amot* of overlap — the stretch he would have access to regardless.

Rashi illustrates a simpler version of this principle: if one placed an eruv at the 2000-*ama* marker and it was eaten or rolled away before Shabbat, the eruv failed. Rabbi Meir holds that the mere attempt to make an eruv in a given direction is itself costly — it constitutes a forfeiture of the equivalent 2000 *amot* in the opposite direction — regardless of whether the eruv ultimately succeeded. The person paid the price of attempting the eruv in terms of lost mobility, even when the eruv itself brought no benefit. This is a striking and, as we shall see, far-reaching position.

One might be tempted to explain Rabbi Meir's ruling in terms of *safek* — since we are uncertain whether the eruv worked, we apply a conservative approach and grant only the intersection of the two possible ranges. But the language of the mishnah — *kol she'hu yachol le'areiv velo eireiv* — does not point to uncertainty; it speaks of a case where the eruv definitively failed. We will return to this below.

## Rav Huna Analysis

### Journey Begins

We have already quoted Rav Huna's explanation above, in context: *keivan d'nafik lei l'orcha havei lei ani* — once the traveler departed on his journey, he acquires the status of *ani* and may designate a distant spot. This is the key halachic mechanism: the journey itself transforms his status. The eruv designation that follows is therefore legally effective, in contrast to the designation made by a townsperson who never left home.

### Baraita

A *baraita* confirms this ruling directly: in the case of one who has houses in two cities separated by two *techumei Shabbat*, *keivan she'hechezik baderech kana eruv divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — once he has begun his journey, his eruv is acquired, in the words of Rabbi Yehuda. The phrase *hechezik baderech* — literally, grasped the road, or established oneself on the road — is the operative criterion: having taken those first steps of the journey, one is no longer considered to be eruv-making from home.

### Rabbi Yosi

Rabbi Yosi the son of Rabbi Yehuda (*Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda*) adds a further ruling: *afilu matza'o chaveiro v'amar lo lin po* — even if his friend found him and said, do not travel tonight, it is too cold or too hot, sleep here — *lemachar mashkim ve'holech*, the following morning he may still travel on Shabbat. This is presented as *yitir al ken*, a step beyond Rabbi Yehuda's position. But this requires explanation: it appears at first glance to be the same scenario as the mishnah itself, and moreover, it is not clear wherein the novelty lies relative to what Rabbi Yehuda already said.

## Rabbah vs. Yosef

### Rabbah View

Rava explains the nature of the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda. Following one reading of Rashi, he states: *kuli alma lo pligi de'tzarich* — everyone agrees that verbal designation (*lomar*) of the specific location is necessary. One cannot rely on the Gemara "reading one's mind" to determine where precisely one intended to establish Shabbat. We have seen throughout this masechet that the designation must be precise; one cannot merely say "somewhere near the wall" or "somewhere near the tree." So verbal designation is universally required.

The debate, according to Rava, is over *lehachzik* — whether one must actually begin the journey (*hechzik baderech*) or whether mere intention suffices. Rabbi Yehuda requires at minimum the single step out the door. The journey of a thousand miles, so to speak, begins with that first step; without it, one is still an *ashir*. Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda holds that even if one's friend found one still at home — not yet having taken a single step — the mere fact that one was planning the journey and had designated a spot verbally is sufficient to grant the status of *ani* and render the eruv effective. This is the genuine *chiddush*.

The question of verbal designation, on this reading, is settled: everyone requires it. What remains contested is the threshold of physical departure.

### Yosef View

Rav Yosef reads the dispute in precisely the opposite way. He says: *lehachzik, kuli alma lo pligi de'tzarich* — everyone agrees that one must actually begin the journey; mere planning without stepping outside is insufficient. The debate, according to Rav Yosef, is over *lomar* — verbal designation. Rabbi Yehuda requires it; one must explicitly declare the spot. Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda holds that once the journey has begun, no verbal designation is needed; we may infer the intended location from the traveler's evident purpose. This is a striking position, given how much emphasis the preceding mishnayot placed on precision of designation.

Thus the dispute between Rabbah and Rav Yosef concerns which of the two components — beginning the journey and verbal designation — is universally required and which is the subject of the tannaitic debate. Rabbah says: verbal designation is universally required, and the debate is about whether physical departure is also necessary. Rav Yosef says: physical departure is universally required, and the debate is about whether verbal designation is also necessary.

### Ulla

The Gemara asks: *ke'man azla ha de'amar Ulla?* — Whose view is consistent with the statement of Ulla? Ulla states: one who *hechzik baderech* and was turned back by his friend is *mutar u'muchzar u'muchzak* — permitted, turned back, and established. The Gemara notes the apparent tension: if he is *muchzar* — turned back and restored to his home base — why is he *muchzak*, still considered to have established an eruv? And conversely, if he is *muchzak*, meaning his status as one on a journey persists, why is he *muchzar*?

The Gemara resolves this: *af al pi she'muchzar muchzak* — even though he has been turned back, his eruv status is intact. Now, whose position does this align with? The Gemara concludes it follows Rav Yosef's reading of Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda: physical departure (*hechzik baderech*) is the universal requirement — hence the explicit mention that he began his journey — and verbal designation is not required — hence the fact that no mention is made of any declaration. Ulla's statement thus endorses the view that what matters is the journey, not the words.

### Ishtata Story

The Gemara supports this further with a narrative. Rav Yehuda bar Ishtata brought a basket of fruit to Rav Natan bar Oshaya on *erev Shabbat* and was about to return home. Rav Natan bar Oshaya waited deliberately until Rav Yehuda bar Ishtata had taken one step down from the upper level — *ad d'nachit darga* — thereby establishing *hechzik baderech*, having begun his journey. At that moment, Rav Natan bar Oshaya said to him: *beitkha* — make this your home tonight, stay here, go home tomorrow on Shabbat itself. The story thus deliberately recreates the scenario of the mishnah: departure followed immediately by a reversal prompted by another's invitation.

The notable feature of this account is that there appears to have been no verbal designation by Rav Yehuda bar Ishtata. The Gemara therefore asks: *ke'man?* — whose view does this reflect? It answers: Rav Yosef's reading of Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda, who requires the journey but not verbal designation. The Gemara adds, however, that it is also consistent with Rava's reading of Rabbi Yehuda — because perhaps Rav Natan bar Oshaya did, in fact, verbally instruct Rav Yehuda bar Ishtata to designate a particular spot at the moment he invited him to stay. The story does not exclude this possibility.

### Conclusion

In sum, the operative criteria for the *ani* who may designate a distant eruv location are: (a) that the journey has genuinely begun — one has stepped out of one's home — and (b) the question of verbal designation remains contested. What seems clear is that the journey is essential. Whether designation may be waived is left somewhat open. It is worth noting how conceptually strange it would be to dispense with verbal designation entirely, given the precision the Gemara has consistently demanded in specifying the location of the *makom shvisah*.

## Meir Penalty

### Parallel

The Gemara notes that Rabbi Meir's *chamor gamal* ruling has already appeared in an earlier mishnah, and asks why it must be restated here. The earlier case involved one who placed an eruv at the 2000-*ama* marker and the eruv was then destroyed *bein hashmashos* — in the twilight period between Friday and Shabbat — leaving a genuine *safek* as to whether the eruv took effect. In that case, Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda both rule *chamor gamal*: since one does not know which of the two 2000-*amot* ranges applies, one receives only their intersection, the middle 2000.

That is a straightforward application of the *safek* principle. But the present case in the mishnah — the traveler who tried to designate a spot and failed — is not framed as a *safek*. The mishnah says *kol she'hu yachol le'areiv velo eireiv*: anyone who could have made an eruv and did not. This sounds not like uncertainty but like certain failure.

### Sheshet

Rav Sheshet draws the distinction explicitly: *lo teima ta'ama de'Rabbi Meir safek eireiv safek lo eireiv hu de'havei chamor gamal* — do not say that Rabbi Meir applies *chamor gamal* only when there is a doubt whether the eruv took effect. *Aval vadai lo eireiv lo havei chamor gamal* — one might have thought that if the eruv definitely failed, there would be no *chamor gamal*. Not so. *Ella afilu vadai lo eireiv havei chamor gamal* — even when it is certain the eruv failed, one still receives only the overlap range. The language of the mishnah — *yachol le'areiv velo eireiv*, he could have made an eruv and did not — reflects a case of certain failure, and Rabbi Meir's penalty applies nonetheless.

### Cost

The implication of this position is sharp. According to Rabbi Meir, the mere attempt to create an eruv in a given direction is itself costly, regardless of outcome. By reaching toward the eruv location, one forfeits the 2000 *amot* in the opposite direction. Even if the eruv is later known with certainty to have failed before Shabbat began, the person is still left with only the middle 2000 *amot*. This is not a *safek* calculus yielding an intersection; it is a penalty for having tried. It is, as Rashi reads it, a particularly rigorous ruling, and the Gemara makes clear that we do not rule this way.

## Exiting Techum

### One Step Out

The mishnah states: *mi sheyatza chutz la'techum* — one who stepped outside the *techum* — *afilu ama achat lo yikanes* — even one *ama*, he may not reenter. This echoes the earlier mishnayot regarding the traveler who walked outside the *techum* and was thereby limited to four *amot*. The present mishnah makes clear that there is no tolerance whatsoever: a single step over the *techum* boundary locks one out. One now has four *amot* from wherever one is standing outside, but the 2000 *amot* of the *techum* from one's city are inaccessible.

### Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees: *shtayim yachol likhanes, shalosh lo yikanes* — if one has overstepped by up to two *amot*, one may reenter; if by three, one may not. His reasoning: the moment one steps outside the *techum*, one immediately acquires four *amot* of personal space. If one has stepped only one or two *amot* beyond the boundary, those four *amot* extend back across the *techum* line, creating an overlap. Since one's allocated space straddles the boundary, one may step back. The *chakhamim* hold that notwithstanding any such overlap, having crossed the line at all precludes reentry.

### One Foot

Rabbi Chanina extends the analysis to an even more granular case: one foot inside the *techum* and one foot outside — *raglo achat betokh ha'techum ve'raglo achat chutz la'techum*. According to the *chakhamim*'s strict rule that even one step outside locks one out, it follows that one may not pull the outside foot back in. The Gemara supports this from the verse *im tashiv mishabbat raglecha* (Isaiah 58:13), stressing *raglecha* in the singular — even one foot that has crossed must be stopped.

A *baraita*, however, states that in this scenario one may reenter. The Gemara explains: *ha manni acherim hi* — this follows the minority opinion of *Acherim*, who hold *lemakom she'rubo nizdaker* — one is drawn toward wherever the majority of one's body is located. Since one foot is still inside, one's bodily center of gravity and weight remain within the *techum*, and one has not yet truly crossed the line.

A parallel tradition then reports that Rabbi Chanina himself stated this leniency, citing the same verse but reading *raglayich* in the plural — only when both feet have crossed does the restriction apply. This reading yields the lenient ruling: one foot out is not yet a full crossing.

The Gemara notes an apparent contradiction: a *baraita* attributes to Rabbi Eliezer both the position of his mishnah (two *amot* permissible) and an apparently stricter position (even one *ama* impermissible). The Gemara resolves this: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. The ruling of one *ama* permissible and two *amot* impermissible reflects the distinction between having just lifted one foot into the second *ama* versus having fully planted oneself in the second *ama*. "You can go back" means you are still in the first *ama* zone; "you cannot go back" means you have genuinely stepped into the third *ama*.

### Measurement

There is a further *baraita* in which Rabbi Eliezer says *afilu ama achat lo yikanes* — even one *ama*, one may not go back in — identical to the *chakhamim*'s position in the mishnah. Is this not contradictory to his mishnah ruling? The Gemara answers: *ki tanya hahi lemoded* — that *baraita* addresses an entirely different scenario: the measurer. The mishnah rule — two *amot* are permissible — addresses *bedi'avad*, where someone inadvertently stepped across the *techum* boundary. The *baraita* addresses the one who is deliberately measuring off his 2000 *amot* from a point outside the city. When one starts outside the city, one measures 2000 and stops precisely there — even if the end point falls inside a cave, inside a building, or inside a city. One does not gain the right to enter that structure. The mishnah (*detnan*) makes clear: *notnim lo alpayim ama afilu sof midato kala bime'arah* — we give him his 2000 *amot* even if his measurement ends in the middle of a cave, meaning he gets the 2000 *amot* but nothing further.

So the two rulings address distinct situations: one is the *lechatchila* instruction to a measurer not to step one *ama* beyond the calculated 2000, and the other is the *bedi'avad* situation of someone who has already crossed and wonders whether reentry is possible.

## Forced Outside

### Forced Stay

The final mishnah of the fourth perek: *mi shehotzi'uhu chutz la'techum afilu ama achat lo yikanes* — one who was forced or found himself outside the *techum* when Shabbat began, even if only one *ama* outside, may not reenter. The practical meaning: if one ends up outside the city as Shabbat falls, and one's personal 2000-*ama* allotment from that point does not reach the city limits, one cannot enter the city. Even if one's house is just beyond that line, one is restricted to the 2000 *amot* from one's current location.

### Shimon Buffer

Rabbi Shimon dissents: *afilu chamesh esrei ama yikanes* — even if one is fifteen *amot* outside the *techum*, one may enter. His reason: *she'ein hamshuchot mamtzi'ot et hamidot mipnei hato'im* — the measuring ropes do not yield perfectly accurate measurements, on account of errors. The result is that the formal markers placed at the *techum* boundary are already set fifteen *amot* short of the actual boundary, as a built-in buffer to account for human imprecision. Therefore, if one finds oneself within fifteen *amot* of the marker at the onset of Shabbat, one is in reality still within the *techum*.

### Buffer Rationale

The Gemara explains the precision of the fifteen-*ama* figure. The measuring rope used for *techum* measurements is fifty *amot* long. Two people hold the rope — one at each end — and they walk forty repetitions to measure 2000 *amot*. But each person, at each measurement, occupies a *tefach* of the rope with his grip. Since both ends of the rope are held, two *tefachim* are lost per measurement. Over forty measurements, that is eighty *tefachim* of total rope that are not actually measured as distance. Since there are six *tefachim* per *ama*, eighty *tefachim* equals just over thirteen *amot*. Rashi adjusts slightly — accounting for a *tefach* and a bit per grip — and arrives at the full fifteen *amot*. The upshot: the measurer consistently understates the true distance by this margin, and therefore everyone has an effective buffer of fifteen *amot* beyond the marked boundary before they have actually crossed the real *techum*.

## Perek 5 Intro

### City Boundaries

We now begin the fifth perek — *Keitzad me'abrin et he'arim* — How does one extend the boundaries of a city? The word *me'abrin* (from *ubar*, embryo) evokes the image of giving a city an expanded, pregnant form: one takes the inhabited core and surrounds it with a structured, squared-off perimeter from which the 2000 *amot* are then measured. The perek will deal at length with how precisely to define and square off a city's dimensions, a topic directly relevant to everything we have been discussing.

### Structures

The mishnah begins by enumerating the types of features that complicate a city's outline. *Beit niknas beit yotzei* — houses that jut inward or outward from the general city wall, creating irregular lines. *Pogum niknas pogum yotzei* — indentations or protrusions in a fortification or structure. *Gedudiyot gevohot asarah tefachim* — destroyed structures that are still ten *tefachim* high; Rashi explains these as ruins of buildings or turrets. *Gesharim* — bridges. *Nefashot* — tombstones and funerary structures. All of these are included in the city's measured area, provided that each has a *beit dira* — some form of habitation associated with it. By "habitation," the mishnah means some ongoing human use: the cemetery example is explained by the presence of the caretaker's booth within which he sleeps and keeps watch. The dead bodies themselves, needless to say, do not count toward habitation.

### Outermost Points

The method for measuring the city begins with identifying the outermost inhabited points. One surveys the city and marks every location — including the structures enumerated above — where there is some form of genuine habitation. The most distant such points in every direction define the outer perimeter of the city. From these furthest inhabited points, *motzi'in et hamida kenegdan* — one extends the measuring line outward, opposite those points. This yields a rough perimeter of the city's actual extent.

### Squaring Off

Once the outermost inhabited points have been identified, the mishnah prescribes: *ve'osin ota ke'min tablo meruva'at* — one fashions the city's boundary into a square tablet, *kedei shenishtaker et hazaviyot* — so that one gains the benefit of the corners. Squaring off the city means that rather than tracing a jagged, irregular outline around all the inhabited points, one draws a rectangle — or, more precisely, a square aligned to the compass points — that encompasses all those points. The corners of that square extend beyond the inhabited area, and the 2000 *amot* are measured outward from each side of that square.

The practical consequence is significant. A city like Manhattan, for example, which runs at roughly a 45-degree angle to true north, would if squared off along the compass axes yield a much larger base area than if one simply inscribed a rectangle along the city's own axis. The rules of *techum* measurement follow the compass, not the city's natural orientation. We rule that the squaring-off is performed according to the compass points, and we will continue to examine the implications of this in the material ahead.
