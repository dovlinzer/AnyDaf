# Bava Metzia 87 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Abraham & Angels

### Custom & Minhag

The *aggadeta* of this *daf* continues in the middle of the story of Abraham and the angels, which arose through the *mishna*'s discussion of whether an employer who contracts to feed his workers must provide lavish meals. The reasoning was that since workers are children of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, one who contracts to feed them might be obligated to provide near-infinite quantities. This opened the door to the extensive *aggadic* material about Abraham's feast and his generosity toward the angels.

Rav Tanchum bar Chanilai teaches: *le'olam al yeshaneh adam min haminhag* — a person should never deviate from the prevailing custom. This is a striking echo of the *mishna*'s principle of *hakol keminhag hamedina*, though here it carries a more personal, behavioral meaning: when in a particular setting, conduct yourself according to its norms. The illustration is twofold. When Moses ascended to heaven, he did not eat bread, as the verse states: *lechem lo achal umayim lo shata* — bread he did not eat and water he did not drink — conforming to the way of the angels. And when the angels of the divine service descended below, *vayochlu lechem* — and they ate bread — conforming to the way of human beings.

### Angels Eating

The *gemara* immediately raises an objection: *achlu salka da'atcha* — is it conceivable that they actually ate? Angels have no digestive nature. The resolution offered is *nir'u kemi she'achlu veshatu* — they appeared as though they ate and drank. They made it look that way, out of respect for the customs of the place and the dignity of their host.

### Abraham's Merit

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *kol mah she'asa Avraham lemalachei hasharet be'atzmo, asa Hakadosh Baruch Hu levanav be'atzmo* — whatever Abraham personally did for the ministering angels, God personally repaid to Abraham's descendants, the Jewish people. And conversely, whatever Abraham did through an intermediary, God provided for Israel through an intermediary.

### Textual Midrash

The *gemara* now matches the acts of Abraham to God's corresponding acts through a series of textual parallels. Abraham ran to the cattle himself — *el habakar ratz Avraham* — and correspondingly, God sent the wind directly: *veruach nasa me'et Hashem*, gathering the quail for Israel. Abraham took butter and milk — *vayikach chem'a vechalav* — and God directly declared: *hineni mamtir lachem lechem min hashamayim*, I will rain down bread from heaven. The connection seems to be a wordplay: *chem'a* echoes *lechem*, and two of the letters of *chalav* (lamed-chet-mem) recur in *lechem*. Abraham stood and attended to them beneath the tree — *vehu omed aleihem tachat ha'etz* — and God declared: *hineni omed lefanecha sham al hatzur*, I will stand before you at the rock, providing water. Abraham accompanied them on their way — *ve'Avraham holech imam leshalcham* — and God walked before Israel by day: *vaHashem holech lifneihem yomam*.

Now what did Abraham do through an intermediary? He said *yukach na me'at mayim* — let some water be taken — delegating the task. Correspondingly, God provided water to Israel through Moshe striking the rock: *vehikita batzur veyatz'u mayim mimenu veshata ha'am*. God's own standing present for the people corresponds to Abraham's personal attendance; the bringing of the water was through an intermediary, just as Abraham had delegated that task.

### Rabbi Chama Dispute

This set of textual parallels, the *gemara* notes, *upliga deRabbi Chama bar Chanina* — disputes Rabbi Chama bar Chanina. For Rabbi Chama bar Chanina, and so too taught the *tana devei Rabbi Yishmael*, taught a different set of three correspondences: *beschar shlosha zachu lishlosha* — by the merit of three acts, Israel merited three gifts. For the butter and milk (*chem'a vechalav*), they merited the *man* — the manna. Again, this likely turns on the same *lechem*/*chalav* wordplay. For Abraham's standing and attending to them (*vehu omed*), they merited the pillar of cloud (*amud anan*). For the *yukach na me'at mayim*, they merited the well of Miriam. The difference between this list and the previous one is the matching of Abraham's personal attendance: here it is matched to the pillar of cloud, whereas in the earlier list it was matched to God's standing at the rock. The two *amoraim* thus parse the correspondences differently.

## Feet Washing

### Yukach Mayim

The *gemara* now proceeds to expound the individual verses of the story *pasuk* by *pasuk*, much of which is familiar from Rashi's commentary on the *parasha*. *Yukach na me'at mayim verachatzu ragleichem* — let some water be taken and wash your feet.

### Arab Practices

Rabbi Yannai the son of Rabbi Yishmael reports that the angels responded: *ve'chi be'aravayin chashadetanu she'hem mishtachavim le'avak ragleihem* — do you think we are Arabs, who prostrate to the dust on their feet? And then, as if to explain why this accusation lands differently here, the *gemara* notes *kevar yatza mimeni Yishmael* — Yishmael has already gone out from me. This exchange is worth pausing over, since the identity of the speaker is itself Rabbi *Yishmael*, and the accusation is pegged to *Yishmael*, his ancestor — a detail the *gemara* seems to find pointed. As for the historical phenomenon itself, a note from the Maharal's second explanation suggests that in Abraham's era, the Arab tribes were nomadic peoples who worshipped a god of the roads, and thus treated the dust on their feet — residue of those roads — as something to bow toward. Whether historically accurate or not, the interpretive thrust is that Abraham's request to wash their feet was misread as though he suspected them of this idolatrous practice. Their protest, and Abraham's tacit acknowledgment via the mention of Yishmael, ends the exchange abruptly without resolution. The story moves on without Abraham offering a counter-explanation, which itself is left somewhat open. Clearly foot-washing as a gesture of hospitality need not have carried any such connotation.

## Third Day Healing

### Divine Sensitivity

The *gemara* returns to the beginning of the *parasha*. *Vayera eilav Hashem be'elonei Mamre vehu yoshev petach ha'ohel ke'chom hayom* — God appeared to him in the plains of Mamre, and he was sitting at the entrance of the tent in the heat of the day. Rabbi Chama the son of Rabbi Chanina explains: *oto hayom yom shelishi shel milah shel Avraham hayah* — that day was the third day of Abraham's *brit milah*, which is the day of greatest pain, as we learn from the narrative of Shimon and Levi's attack on Shechem on the third day.

God came to perform *bikkur cholim* — to visit the sick — and in order that Abraham should not be burdened by wayfarers on this painful day, God caused the heat of the day to be extreme, so that no travelers would be on the road. *Hotzi Hakadosh Baruch Hu chamah min nartikah kedei shelo lehatri'ach oto tzaddik be'orchim*. Nevertheless, Abraham, who was desperate to bring in guests, sent his servant *Eliezer* out to find wayfarers. Eliezer went out and found no one. When he returned and reported this, Abraham declared: *lo meheimna lach* — I do not trust you — and went to look for himself. This prompted the *gemara* to cite the popular saying: *leit heimanuta be'avdei* — servants cannot be trusted. One may note an alternative and appealing reading: perhaps Eliezer was complicit in protecting Abraham, not truly searching, so that Abraham would not have to go out himself.

When Abraham went out, he saw God standing at the gate — *dekayei ababa* — and this is why Abraham said *al na ta'avor me'al avdecha*, God please do not pass by your servant. Abraham was, in effect, welcoming God as his guest.

Then *keivan dechaza deka asar veshari*, when God saw that Abraham was untying and retying his bandages from the *milah*, God said it is not appropriate to remain in these circumstances. *Amar lav orach ar'a lemikim hacha.* And at that moment, *vayisa einav vayar vehinei shelosha anashim nitzavim alav* — he lifted his eyes and saw three men standing near him. The angels had initially approached and stood close to him, but when they saw his pain, they stepped back, giving him space. That is why the verse says he *ran* to greet them even though they had been nearby — they had withdrawn out of sensitivity.

This is a remarkable model of *bikkur cholim*. The angels, and indeed God's presence itself, withdrew when they perceived that Abraham was in pain and compromised. Sometimes the right form of visiting the sick is to step back, to not impose one's presence when the person needs space. As has been aptly observed, the principle here is a kind of pastoral sensitivity: you enter the room, but if you see the patient's dignity is compromised, you step back. This is a lesson for all who perform *bikkur cholim*.

The precise relationship between God's own visitation and the angels' arrival is not entirely clear in the *gemara*. One possibility is that God's visit was manifested through the angels' presence. Another possibility, reflected in the *midrash* cited by Tosafot, is that they were distinct: Abraham asked God to wait while he went to welcome the angels and bring them in. Tosafot discusses this: *hashechina hayeta rotza lehistalek me'al Avraham* — the divine presence wished to withdraw — but then the *midrash* suggests that Abraham asked God to wait, *ad sheyachnis otan*, while he attended to the angels. This reading distinguishes the divine visit from the angelic one.

### Angels' Names

The *gemara* asks: *mani hashelosha anashim?* — who were these three men? *Michael veGavriel veRefael.* Michael came to bring the good tidings to Sarah. Refael, whose name evokes *refuah* — healing — came to heal Abraham from his *brit milah*. Gabriel, whose name evokes *gevurah* — strength — went to overturn Sodom.

A textual difficulty is immediately raised: the verse says *vayavo'u shenei hamalakhim Sdoma ba'erev* — only two angels came to Sodom. Why two, if only Gabriel was sent to destroy the city? The answer is that Michael went along to save Lot. This accords with Michael's role as a bearer of good tidings. The proof that only Gabriel destroyed the cities is that the verse says *vayahafoch et he'arim ha'el* in the singular — *he* overturned — not *vayahafechu* in the plural.

Tosafot raises a difficulty here from the principle in *Bereishit Rabbah* that a single angel cannot perform two missions. How then could Michael both announce the good news to Sarah and save Lot? Tosafot offers two answers. First, the missions were sequential, not simultaneous: the first mission ended when he informed Sarah, and only then did a new mission begin — *az hitchil shelichut acheret*. This is permitted; it is performing two tasks simultaneously that is forbidden. Second, Tosafot notes that *Bereishit Rabbah* in some versions assigns the saving of Lot to Refael, not Michael. In that version, healing Abraham and saving Lot are connected types of action — *refuah vehatzalah inyan echad* — and are considered a single category of mission.

### Accept/Refuse

By Abraham, the verse records that when he offered hospitality, they immediately accepted: *ken ta'aseh ka'asher dibarta*. By Lot, by contrast, *vayiftzar bam me'od* — he had to press them greatly before they agreed. Rabbi Elazar derives from this: *mikan she'mesarvin lekatan ve'ein mesarvin legadol* — from here we learn that one may decline an initial offer from someone of lower standing, but one does not refuse a person of distinction. It would be offensive to a *chashuv* person if their hospitality were casually turned down; with someone of lesser standing, a degree of initial reluctance is appropriate social conduct.

## Righteous vs Wicked

### Promise & Fulfill

The verse says Abraham promised *pat lechem* — a piece of bread — yet *el habakar ratz Avraham* — he ran to the cattle and prepared a full feast. Rabbi Elazar teaches: *mikan shetzadikim omrim me'at ve'osim harbeh* — from here we learn that the righteous promise little and do much.

### Ephron's Greed

The wicked, by contrast, promise much and do not even deliver a little. The paradigm case is Ephron. He announced grandly that the field was worth four hundred *shekel* of silver — *mah hi beini uveinecha* — essentially suggesting it was a negligible sum. Yet in the end, *vayishkol Avraham le'Efron et hakesef* — Abraham weighed out the full sum — and not merely the standard *shekel*, but coins of the type *over lasocher*, accepted by every merchant. The *gemara* further notes that Ephron ultimately insisted on *kantari* — a coin of greater weight — *de'ika duchta dekaru lei letikla kantari*, in places the *shekel* is called a *kantari*, indicating the payment was even larger than it might first appear. He said he would give it for nothing and in the end demanded a great deal.

### Bread & Purity

The verse says Abraham told Sarah to take *kemach* — flour — yet a moment later the text says *solet* — fine flour. Rather than reading these as a single description, the *gemara* treats them as two distinct things. Rabbi Yitzchak derives: *mikan she'ha'ishah tzarah einah be'orchim yoter min ha'ish* — from here we learn that a woman tends to be more reluctant than a man to give food to guests. The *gemara* seems to suggest that Abraham specified both *kemach* and *solet* precisely because Sarah was inclined to give only the coarser flour. One might note that this generalization could stem from a practical reality: women managing the household provisions know exactly how much food is on hand and must feed the family, while men can more easily make lavish promises.

The bread promised never appears on the table — the verse records that Abraham served the butter, the milk, and the meat, but not the bread. Where did it go? *Efrayim Maksha'ah*, the student of Rabbi Meir, teaches in the name of Rabbi Meir: *Avraham Avinu ochel chullin betaharah hayah* — Abraham maintained a practice of eating even non-sanctified food in a state of purity. And that day, *Sarah Imeinu oto hayom pirsa niddah* — Sarah had her period return, her youth miraculously restored. As a result, her touching of the bread rendered it *tamei*, and since Abraham would not eat something *tamei*, he could not serve it to his guests. This is itself a striking point: even if the guests themselves would not have been troubled by *tumah*, Abraham's personal practice prevented him from serving them something he himself would not eat. There is something profound in that — to treat a guest as less than you would treat yourself is in some sense a diminishment of their dignity.

The question is raised whether Abraham understood his guests to be angels. The *gemara* suggests he did not — or at least was uncertain. One detail offered is that Abraham stood and attended to them while they ate, which may indicate he joined them at table so they should not feel uncomfortable eating while he stood apart. The verse *vehu omed aleihem tachat ha'etz vayochelu* is most naturally read as: he stood over them attending, and they ate.

### Courtesy Duty

The angels then asked: *ayeh Sarah ishtecha* — where is Sarah your wife? The *gemara* says this was to teach: *le'hodi'a she'Sarah Imeinu tzenu'ah hayetah* — that Sarah our matriarch was modest, remaining in the tent. But why would the angels ask something they obviously already knew? Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav (and some attribute it to Rav Yitzchak): *yod'im hayu malachei hasharet she'Sarah Imeinu ba'ohel hayetah* — they certainly knew she was in the tent — but they asked *kedei lechababah al ba'alah* — in order to make her dear to her husband, so that Abraham would be reminded of her modesty. Rabbi Yosei the son of Rabbi Chanina offers a different reason: *kedei leshadar lah kos shel berachah* — in order to include her by sending her the cup of *birkat hamazon*.

This second reason is particularly powerful. In a social setting where men and women eat in entirely separate spaces, a woman is easily made invisible in the proceedings that concern her most. By asking after her, by directing the message to her even in her absence, and by sending her the *kos shel berachah* from the concluding blessing, the angels were finding a way to make Sarah a subject rather than merely an object of the conversation. The good tidings, after all, were addressed to her.

The verse *vayomru eilav* — and they said *to him* — has dots written over the letters *alef*, *yud*, and *vav* in the Torah scroll. A *tanna* in the name of Rabbi Yosei asks: why the dots? *Limdah Torah derech eretz* — the Torah is teaching proper conduct — *sheyish'al adam be'achsanyah shelo* — that when you are hosted, you should ask after your hostess. Tosafot presses further: those three dotted letters spell *ayav* — the masculine form of *where is he* — suggesting the reverse is also implied. Just as a guest should ask the host about his wife, so a guest should ask a hostess about her husband. The Torah is teaching gender-equal courtesy in inquiry.

The *gemara* raises a challenge: doesn't Shmuel teach *ein sho'alin bish'lom isha klal* — one does not inquire about a woman's welfare at all — lest it lead to inappropriate familiarity? The *gemara* resolves: *al yedei ba'alah shani* — asking through her husband is different and entirely permissible. Nobody would impute impropriety to a guest who asks the host: "How is your wife? Please send her my regards." The prohibition is only against direct inquiry to the woman herself, which could lead to excessive personal interaction.

## Sarah's Renewal

### Miraculous Nursing

Rav Chisda expounds *acharei beloti hayeta li ednah* — after I have been worn away, shall I have youth? He reads it not as a rhetorical question but as a description of an actual event: *achari she'nisbalah habasar ravu hakmatim nit'adnah habasar venistapshetu hakmatim* — after the flesh had worn away and the wrinkles had multiplied, the flesh became supple and the wrinkles smoothed out. *Vechazar hayofi limkomo* — the beauty returned to its place. (Rashi, in his French gloss, identifies the word *kmatim* — wrinkles — with a French term that, to a modern reader, brings to mind the word "prunes.")

The verse says *va'tedaber Sarah* — Sarah spoke of nursing: *vatomer mi milel le'Avraham heynika vanim Sarah* — who would have declared to Abraham that Sarah would nurse children? But why *banim* in the plural — Sarah nursed only one child. Rabbi Levi explains: on the day that Abraham weaned Isaac, he made a great feast. The nations of the world began to mock, saying they had seen this elderly couple produce a foundling from the marketplace, a child they claimed as their own, and were making a grand celebration to legitimize the deception. What did Abraham do? He invited all the great people of that generation and their wives — notably, he invited their wives specifically — and Sarah's milk flowed miraculously: *veniftchu dadeha kishnei ma'ayanot* — her breasts opened like two wellsprings — and she nursed all of the infants who had been brought. That is the meaning of *heynika vanim* — she nursed *children*, in the plural. This was the proof that she had truly given birth.

### Isaac's Features

The skeptics were not satisfied. They continued to mock: even if Sarah gave birth, perhaps Abraham — at one hundred years old — was not the father. Immediately *nehpach klastar panav shel Yitzchak venidmeh le'Avraham* — Isaac's facial features transformed and he resembled Abraham unmistakably. At that, everyone conceded: *Avraham holid et Yitzchak*. This is in fact what the verse in *parashat Toldot* proclaims: *Eleh toldot Yitzchak ben Avraham Avraham holid et Yitzchak* — and this declaration, according to the *midrash*, reflects the universal acknowledgment that followed the transformation of Isaac's face. The phenomenon of an infant's features shifting over time to resemble a parent is itself quite natural, which gives this *midrash* a certain grounding.

## Origins of Aging

### Abraham's Prayer

*Ad Avraham lo haya zikna* — until Abraham there was no old age. People aged and died, but the aging was not reflected in their bodily appearance. This created a practical problem: *man dehava ba'i lemishta'ei bahadi Avraham mishta'ei bahadi Yitzchak* — someone who wanted to speak with Abraham would accidentally speak with Isaac, since they looked identical. And vice versa. Father and son were indistinguishable. So *ata Avraham ba'ei rachamei vehava zikna* — Abraham prayed for old age to be visibly present, and there was old age — as the verse states: *ve'Avraham zaken ba bayamim*, which is the first time the Torah explicitly uses *zaken* to describe someone's condition.

One might raise the question: didn't the verse earlier say *adoni zaken* — my master is old — which predates this moment? And indeed the *gemara* does not quote that verse. The *gemara* seems to be working with the idea that the bodily condition of aging was not yet visible, even if people experienced diminished capacities. There is also the consideration that Sarah's own words *acharei beloti* — after I have been worn away — and the *gemara*'s own reading of them, suggest visible physical change did exist. The *gemara* apparently holds that while certain capacities declined with age, the outward appearance of being aged — the kind that would allow you to distinguish a 100-year-old from a 60-year-old — was not present until Abraham prayed for it.

This is a profound mythic idea. Abraham was not asking to look old for honor's sake alone, though in a society that reveres age, that would certainly be a consideration. He was asking that his life experience, his decades, be legible on his face. There is something deeply counter-cultural about this — contemporary culture endlessly seeks to appear younger. Here, the very first prayer for visible aging is an act of claiming one's identity, of saying that one's journey through time should be written on one's body.

### Jacob's Sickness

*Ad Yaakov lo hava chulsha* — until Jacob there was no sickness as a precursor to death. People simply died. Jacob prayed that illness should precede death — *ata Yaakov ba'ei rachamei vehava chulsha* — as the verse records: *vayomer le'Yosef hinei avicha choleh* — and he said to Joseph, your father is ill. This is the first occurrence in the Torah of someone being sick. The purpose is not obscure: illness preceding death gives a person the opportunity to prepare — mentally, spiritually, practically — for what is coming. It is a gift of awareness, of transition, of the ability to put affairs in order and to say what needs to be said.

### Elisha's Illness

*Ad ata Elisha lo hava dechalash ve'itpach* — until Elisha came, no one became ill and then recovered. All illness led directly to death. *Ata Elisha ba'ei rachamei ve'itpach* — Elisha prayed and recovered, introducing the possibility of recovery from illness. The textual proof is *ve'Elisha chala et chalyo asher yamut bo* — the final illness he would die from — which implies *michlal dechali chali achrinei* — that he had been sick from other illnesses before, illnesses from which he had recovered.

The *Talmud* specifies: *tanu rabbanan, shlosha cholayim chala Elisha* — our Rabbis taught that Elisha suffered three illnesses. One for pushing away Gechazi with both hands — for completely rejecting someone where one should push away with the left but draw close with the right. One for inciting the bears against the children who mocked him. And one that brought about his death: *shenemar ve'Elisha chala et chalyo asher yamut bo*.

The introduction of recoverable illness also gives rise to the whole dynamic of *teshuva*, prayer, and healing — the realization that sickness is not automatically terminal opens a space for spiritual response.

## Workers' Rights

### Stipulations

The *gemara* now returns to *halakha*, picking up the thread of workers' entitlements and employer stipulations. The case discussed is a father seeking to limit his obligation to feed workers before they begin: *al menat she'ein lachem ela pat vekitnit bilvad* — on condition that I will give you only bread and legumes. Rav Acha the son of Rav Yosef raises a pointed grammatical question: *l'afchuzei — pat kitnit tnan o pat vekitnit tnan* — does the *mishna* mean bread *made of* legumes (a single item: legume-bread), or bread *and* legumes (two separate items)? The answer is that it requires a *vav* — *pat vekitnit* — and indeed that *vav* is essential to the meaning, like the pole used to keep a boat in the middle of the river away from the shore. Without the *vav*, the two items collapse into one.

Rabban Gamliel teaches: even if one stipulates to feed workers, one need not exceed the standard — *hakol keminhag hamedina*. The *gemara* asks what exactly this means. It cites a *mishna*: if an employer hires a worker and says pay him as one of the ordinary workers in town, what does that mean if there is a range of wages? Rabbi Yehoshua says: give him the lower end of the range. The Sages say: *meshamnin beineihem* — calculate the median. The *gemara* implies that the *minhag* is not the minimum; the standard means something closer to the average. The minimum is the floor of the range, not its representative value.

### Mishna Frame

The *mishna* now turns to a different dimension of workers' rights — not stipulated feeding arrangements, but the Torah's own entitlement for workers to eat from the produce they are harvesting. *Ve'elu ochlim min haTorah* — the following workers eat by Torah right. The categories are organized along two axes. For *mechubar lekarka* — produce still connected to the ground — the worker may eat *bish'at gmar melacha* — at the time of the final labor, i.e., the harvest. For *talush min hakarka* — produce already detached from the ground — the worker may eat only *ad shelo nigmra melachto* — before the completion of the process. These two are mirror images of each other.

Additionally, the entitlement applies only to *davar shegidulo min ha'aretz* — something that grows from the ground. The parallel list of those who may *not* eat reverses each of these conditions: one working with produce still connected to the ground but not at the harvest stage may not eat; one working with harvested produce after the final processing is complete may not eat; and one working with something that does not grow from the ground may not eat.

### Biblical Source

*Mena hanei milei?* — what is the biblical source for all this? The verse states: *ki tavo bekerem re'echa ve'achalta anavim* — when you enter your neighbor's vineyard you may eat grapes. From this we know the rule for a vineyard. But how do we extend it to other produce? By analogy: just as a vineyard involves produce that grows from the ground and the worker eats during the final labor, so too for anything with those features.

However, the *gemara* immediately challenges this: perhaps only a vineyard qualifies because it has the special obligation of *olelot* — the incompletely formed grape clusters given to the poor — suggesting a general heightened set of entitlements for vineyard workers. To counter this, the *gemara* invokes a second verse: *ki tavo bekamat re'echa vekatafta melilot beyadecha* — when you enter your neighbor's standing grain, you may pluck kernels by hand — *ve'el chermesh lo tanif al kamat re'echa* — but you may not swing your sickle against your neighbor's standing grain. This establishes the same entitlement for grain. Now we have two paradigms — vineyard and grain — from which to generalize.

### Extending Produce

But perhaps these two categories are still limited in scope, since both have distinctive characteristics: grain (*kamah*) is subject to *challah*, and vineyard (*kerem*) is subject to *olelot*. Each one's special feature could be the reason for the entitlement. The *gemara* applies the standard hermeneutical move of *hatzad hashaveh* — the common denominator: both grow from the ground and in both cases the worker eats during the final labor. From this common denominator we generalize.

A further challenge: both grain and grapes produce offerings for the *mizbe'ach* — flour and wine. Perhaps the entitlement is limited to produce brought to the altar, which would include olives (producing oil for the *mincha*) but not more. To this, Rav Pappa observes that olives are themselves called *kerem* — *kerem zayit* — in the verse *vayav'er migadish ve'ad kamah ve'ad kerem zayit*. So olives are included via *kerem* directly. But *kerem stam*, an unqualified *kerem*, refers to grapes; *kerem zayit* is the olive grove. And beyond olives, how do we extend to all produce, including things not offered on the *mizbe'ach*?

### Sickle & Standing

Shmuel answers: *amar kra ubachermesh* — the verse says "sickle." *Lerabos kol ba'alei chermesh* — to include everything to which one brings a sickle, beyond the categories of altar-offerings. One might object that the verse's mention of *chermesh* is needed for a different *derashah*: *bish'at chermesh tochal, shelo bish'at chermesh lo tochal* — you may eat only at the time of the harvest, not at other times. But the *gemara* rejects this: the timing restriction is already derived from *ve'el kelyecha lo siten* — do not put it into your own vessel — which implies you eat only while delivering into the *owner's* vessel, i.e., during the harvest. So *chermesh* is available to do the broader inclusionary work of extending the entitlement to all produce harvested with a sickle.

But what of produce that is not harvested with a sickle? Rav Yitzchak answers: *amar kra kamah* — the verse says *standing grain*, *lerabos kol ba'alei kamah* — to include everything that stands and grows. One might object that *kamah* was derived via a *gezeirah shavah* to mean specifically grain subject to *challah*. The *gemara* resolves this: that restrictive reading of *kamah* was necessary only *mikamei denaysi chermesh* — before we had *chermesh* to expand the category. Once *chermesh* has already expanded the entitlement to include all produce brought with a sickle, even things not subject to *challah*, the word *kamah* is no longer needed to establish that limitation. *Kamah* is now free to serve its expansive function: *lerabos kol davar kamah* — to include anything that stands and grows, with or without a sickle, provided it grows from the ground. The *gemara* will continue from this point in the next session.
