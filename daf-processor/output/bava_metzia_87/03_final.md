# Bava Metzia 87 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Abraham & Angels

### Custom & Minhag

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אַל יְשַׁנֶּה אָדָם מִן הַמִּנְהָג, שֶׁהֲרֵי מֹשֶׁה עָלָה לַמָּרוֹם וְלֹא אָכַל לֶחֶם, מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת יָרְדוּ לְמַטָּה וְאָכְלוּ לֶחֶם. וְאָכְלוּ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: נִרְאוּ כְּמִי שֶׁאָכְלוּ וְשָׁתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A person should never deviate from the** local **custom, as Moses ascended to** heaven **on high and did not eat bread** while he was there, whereas **the ministering angels descended down** to this world, as guests visiting Abraham, **and they ate bread.** You say: **And they ate** bread? Can it **enter your mind** that they actually ate food? **Rather, say** that **they** merely **appeared as though they ate and drank.**

The *aggadeta* of this *daf* continues in the middle of the story of Abraham and the angels, which arose through the *mishna*'s discussion of whether an employer who contracts to feed his workers must provide lavish meals. The reasoning was that since workers are children of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, one who contracts to feed them might be obligated to provide near-infinite quantities. This opened the door to the extensive *aggadic* material about Abraham's feast and his generosity toward the angels.

Rav Tanchum bar Chanilai teaches: *le'olam al yeshaneh adam min haminhag* — a person should never deviate from the prevailing custom. This is a striking echo of the *mishna*'s principle of *hakol keminhag hamedina*, though here it carries a more personal, behavioral meaning: when in a particular setting, conduct yourself according to its norms. The illustration is twofold. When Moses ascended to heaven, he did not eat bread, as the verse states: *lechem lo achal umayim lo shata* — bread he did not eat and water he did not drink — conforming to the way of the angels. And when the angels of the divine service descended below, *vayochlu lechem* — and they ate bread — conforming to the way of human beings.

### Angels Eating

The *gemara* immediately raises an objection: *achlu salka da'atcha* — is it conceivable that they actually ate? Angels have no digestive nature. The resolution offered is *nir'u kemi she'achlu veshatu* — they appeared as though they ate and drank. They made it look that way, out of respect for the customs of the place and the dignity of their host.

### Abraham's Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה אַבְרָהָם לְמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת בְּעַצְמוֹ – עָשָׂה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְבָנָיו בְּעַצְמוֹ. וְכׇל מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה אַבְרָהָם עַל יְדֵי שָׁלִיחַ – עָשָׂה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְבָנָיו עַל יְדֵי שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Every action that Abraham performed himself for the ministering angels, the Holy One, Blessed be He, performed Himself for** Abraham's **descendants. And every action that Abraham performed through a messenger, the Holy One, Blessed be He,** likewise **performed for his descendants through a messenger.**

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *kol mah she'asa Avraham lemalachei hasharet be'atzmo, asa Hakadosh Baruch Hu levanav be'atzmo* — whatever Abraham personally did for the ministering angels, God personally repaid to Abraham's descendants, the Jewish people. And conversely, whatever Abraham did through an intermediary, God provided for Israel through an intermediary.

### Textual Midrash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶל הַבָּקָר רָץ אַבְרָהָם״, ״וְרוּחַ נָסַע מֵאֵת ה׳״. ״וַיִּקַּח חֶמְאָה וְחָלָב״, ״הִנְנִי מַמְטִיר לָכֶם לֶחֶם מִן הַשָּׁמָיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Abraham, the verse states: **"And Abraham ran to the herd"** (Genesis 18:7), bringing the meat himself, and in reference to God's actions for Abraham's descendants the verse states: **"And there went forth a wind from the Lord,** and brought across quails from the sea" (Numbers 11:31), that God brought meat to them. In reference to Abraham, the verse states: **"And he took curd and milk"** (Genesis 18:8), and God says to the Jewish people: **"Behold, I will cause to rain bread from heaven for you"** (Exodus 16:4), which shows that God gave food to the Jewish people.

The *gemara* now matches the acts of Abraham to God's corresponding acts through a series of textual parallels. Abraham ran to the cattle himself — *el habakar ratz Avraham* — and correspondingly, God sent the wind directly: *veruach nasa me'et Hashem*, gathering the quail for Israel. Abraham took butter and milk — *vayikach chem'a vechalav* — and God directly declared: *hineni mamtir lachem lechem min hashamayim*, I will rain down bread from heaven. The connection seems to be a wordplay: *chem'a* echoes *lechem*, and two of the letters of *chalav* (lamed-chet-mem) recur in *lechem*. Abraham stood and attended to them beneath the tree — *vehu omed aleihem tachat ha'etz* — and God declared: *hineni omed lefanecha sham al hatzur*, I will stand before you at the rock, providing water. Abraham accompanied them on their way — *ve'Avraham holech imam leshalcham* — and God walked before Israel by day: *vaHashem holech lifneihem yomam*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יֻקַּח נָא מְעַט מַיִם״, ״וְהִכִּיתָ בַצּוּר וְיָצְאוּ מִמֶּנּוּ מַיִם וְשָׁתָה הָעָם״.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, Abraham performed certain actions through an agent. He said: **"Let now a little water be fetched"** (Genesis 18:4), and correspondingly the verse states in reference to Moses, God's messenger: **"And you shall strike the rock, and there shall come water out of it, that the people may drink"** (Exodus 17:6).

Now what did Abraham do through an intermediary? He said *yukach na me'at mayim* — let some water be taken — delegating the task. Correspondingly, God provided water to Israel through Moshe striking the rock: *vehikita batzur veyatz'u mayim mimenu veshata ha'am*. God's own standing present for the people corresponds to Abraham's personal attendance; the bringing of the water was through an intermediary, just as Abraham had delegated that task.

### Rabbi Chama Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: בִּשְׂכַר שְׁלֹשָׁה – זָכוּ לִשְׁלֹשָׁה. בִּשְׂכַר ״חֶמְאָה וְחָלָב״ – זָכוּ לַמָּן; בִּשְׂכַר ״וְהוּא עֹמֵד עֲלֵיהֶם״ – זָכוּ לְעַמּוּד הֶעָנָן; בִּשְׂכַר ״יֻקַּח נָא מְעַט מַיִם״ – זָכוּ לִבְאֵרָהּ שֶׁל מִרְיָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** in stating this, Rav **disagrees** with **that** statement **of Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina. As Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says, and likewise the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: In reward for three** acts of hospitality that Abraham performed for the angels, his descendants **merited three** rewards. The Gemara elaborates: **In reward for** providing them with **curd and milk,** the Jewish people **merited the manna; in reward for: "And he stood [*omed*] by them,"** the Jews **merited the pillar [*amud*] of cloud; in reward for** Abraham saying: **"Let now a little water be fetched,"** they **merited the well of Miriam.**

This set of textual parallels, the *gemara* notes, *upliga deRabbi Chama bar Chanina* — disputes Rabbi Chama bar Chanina. For Rabbi Chama bar Chanina, and so too taught the *tana devei Rabbi Yishmael*, taught a different set of three correspondences: *beschar shlosha zachu lishlosha* — by the merit of three acts, Israel merited three gifts. For the butter and milk (*chem'a vechalav*), they merited the *man* — the manna. Again, this likely turns on the same *lechem*/*chalav* wordplay. For Abraham's standing and attending to them (*vehu omed*), they merited the pillar of cloud (*amud anan*). For the *yukach na me'at mayim*, they merited the well of Miriam. The difference between this list and the previous one is the matching of Abraham's personal attendance: here it is matched to the pillar of cloud, whereas in the earlier list it was matched to God's standing at the rock. The two *amoraim* thus parse the correspondences differently.

## Feet Washing

### Yukach Mayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יֻקַּח נָא מְעַט מַיִם וְרַחֲצוּ רַגְלֵיכֶם״, אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי בְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֲאֵל, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וְכִי בְּעַרְבִיִּים חֲשַׁדְתָּנוּ, שֶׁהֵם מִשְׁתַּחֲוִים לַאֲבַק רַגְלֵיהֶם, כְּבָר יָצָא מִמֶּנּוּ יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its analysis of the verse: **"Let now a little water be fetched and wash your feet"** (Genesis 18:4). **Rabbi Yannai, son of Rabbi Yishmael, said** that the guests **said to** Abraham: **Are you suspicious that we are Arabs who bow to the dust of their feet? Yishmael has already issued from him,** i.e., your own son acts in this manner.

The *gemara* now proceeds to expound the individual verses of the story *pasuk* by *pasuk*, much of which is familiar from Rashi's commentary on the *parasha*. *Yukach na me'at mayim verachatzu ragleichem* — let some water be taken and wash your feet.

### Arab Practices

Rabbi Yannai the son of Rabbi Yishmael reports that the angels responded: *ve'chi be'aravayin chashadetanu she'hem mishtachavim le'avak ragleihem* — do you think we are Arabs, who prostrate to the dust on their feet? And then, as if to explain why this accusation lands differently here, the *gemara* notes *kevar yatza mimeni Yishmael* — Yishmael has already gone out from me. This exchange is worth pausing over, since the identity of the speaker is itself Rabbi *Yishmael*, and the accusation is pegged to *Yishmael*, his ancestor — a detail the *gemara* seems to find pointed. As for the historical phenomenon itself, a note from the Maharal's second explanation suggests that in Abraham's era, the Arab tribes were nomadic peoples who worshipped a god of the roads, and thus treated the dust on their feet — residue of those roads — as something to bow toward. Whether historically accurate or not, the interpretive thrust is that Abraham's request to wash their feet was misread as though he suspected them of this idolatrous practice. Their protest, and Abraham's tacit acknowledgment via the mention of Yishmael, ends the exchange abruptly without resolution. The story moves on without Abraham offering a counter-explanation, which itself is left somewhat open. Clearly foot-washing as a gesture of hospitality need not have carried any such connotation.

## Third Day Healing

### Divine Sensitivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֵּרָא אֵלָיו ה׳ בְּאֵלֹנֵי מַמְרֵא וְהוּא יֹשֵׁב פֶּתַח הָאֹהֶל כְּחֹם הַיּוֹם״. מַאי ״כְּחוֹם הַיּוֹם״? אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם יוֹם שְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁל מִילָה שֶׁל אַבְרָהָם הָיָה, וּבָא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לִשְׁאוֹל בְּאַבְרָהָם, הוֹצִיא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא חַמָּה מִנַּרְתִּיקָהּ כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יַטְרִיחַ אוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק בְּאוֹרְחִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara expounds another verse involving Abraham: **"And the Lord appeared to him by the terebinths of Mamre, as he sat in the tent door in the heat of the day"** (Genesis 18:1). The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **"the heat of the day"? Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: That day was the third day after Abraham's circumcision, and the Holy One, Blessed be He, came to inquire** about the well-being **of Abraham. The Holy One, Blessed be He, removed the sun from its sheath in order not to bother that righteous one with guests,** i.e., God made it extremely hot that day to allow Abraham to recover from his circumcision, as he would not be troubled by passing travelers whom he would invite into his tent.

The *gemara* returns to the beginning of the *parasha*. *Vayera eilav Hashem be'elonei Mamre vehu yoshev petach ha'ohel ke'chom hayom* — God appeared to him in the plains of Mamre, and he was sitting at the entrance of the tent in the heat of the day. Rabbi Chama the son of Rabbi Chanina explains: *oto hayom yom shelishi shel milah shel Avraham hayah* — that day was the third day of Abraham's *brit milah*, which is the day of greatest pain, as we learn from the narrative of Shimon and Levi's attack on Shechem on the third day.

God came to perform *bikkur cholim* — to visit the sick — and in order that Abraham should not be burdened by wayfarers on this painful day, God caused the heat of the day to be extreme, so that no travelers would be on the road. *Hotzi Hakadosh Baruch Hu chamah min nartikah kedei shelo lehatri'ach oto tzaddik be'orchim*. Nevertheless, Abraham, who was desperate to bring in guests, sent his servant *Eliezer* out to find wayfarers. Eliezer went out and found no one. When he returned and reported this, Abraham declared: *lo meheimna lach* — I do not trust you — and went to look for himself. This prompted the *gemara* to cite the popular saying: *leit heimanuta be'avdei* — servants cannot be trusted. One may note an alternative and appealing reading: perhaps Eliezer was complicit in protecting Abraham, not truly searching, so that Abraham would not have to go out himself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁדְּרֵיהּ לֶאֱלִיעֶזֶר לְמִיפַּק לְבָרָא. נְפַק וְלָא אַשְׁכַּח, אָמַר: לָא מְהֵימְנָא לָךְ. הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי תַּמָּן: לֵית הֵימָנוּתָא בְּעַבְדֵי. נְפַק אִיהוּ חַזְיֵיהּ לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא דְּקָאֵי אַבָּבָא. הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״אַל נָא תַעֲבֹר מֵעַל עַבְדֶּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** Despite the intense heat, Abraham wanted to invite guests. **He sent Eliezer** his slave **to go outside** to see if there were any passersby. Eliezer **went out but did not find** anyone. Abraham **said to him: I do not believe you.** The Gemara comments: **This** demonstrates the popular adage **that** people **there,** i.e., in Eretz Yisrael, **say: Slaves do not have any credibility.** The Gemara continues: Abraham **himself went out and saw the Holy One, Blessed be He, standing at the entrance** to his tent. **This is as it is written:** "My Lord, if now I have found favor in your eyes, **do not leave Your servant"** (Genesis 18:3), i.e., God's presence was there, and Abraham asked Him for permission to attend to the travelers.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזָא דְּקָא אָסַר וְשָׁרֵי, אֲמַר: לָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְמֵיקַם הָכָא. הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשָּׂא עֵינָיו וַיַּרְא וְהִנֵּה שְׁלֹשָׁה אֲנָשִׁים נִצָּבִים עָלָיו וַיַּרְא וַיָּרׇץ לִקְרָאתָם״. מֵעִיקָּרָא אֲתוֹ קָמוּ עֲלֵיהּ, כִּי חַזְיוּהּ דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ צַעֲרָא, אֲמַרוּ: לָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְמֵיקַם הָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Once** God **saw** Abraham **tying and untying** the bandage on his circumcision, God **said:** It is **not proper conduct to stand here,** i.e., it is not respectful to Abraham even for God to stand there. **This is as it is written: "And he lifted up his eyes and looked, and, behold, three men stood over him; and when he saw them, he ran to meet them"** (Genesis 18:2). The verse first states that they stood over him, and then it says that he ran to meet them. The Gemara reconciles this apparent contradiction: **Initially, they came and stood over him. Upon seeing that he was in pain, they said:** It is **not proper conduct to stand here.**

When Abraham went out, he saw God standing at the gate — *dekayei ababa* — and this is why Abraham said *al na ta'avor me'al avdecha*, God please do not pass by your servant. Abraham was, in effect, welcoming God as his guest.

Then *keivan dechaza deka asar veshari*, when God saw that Abraham was untying and retying his bandages from the *milah*, God said it is not appropriate to remain in these circumstances. *Amar lav orach ar'a lemikim hacha.* And at that moment, *vayisa einav vayar vehinei shelosha anashim nitzavim alav* — he lifted his eyes and saw three men standing near him. The angels had initially approached and stood close to him, but when they saw his pain, they stepped back, giving him space. That is why the verse says he *ran* to greet them even though they had been nearby — they had withdrawn out of sensitivity.

This is a remarkable model of *bikkur cholim*. The angels, and indeed God's presence itself, withdrew when they perceived that Abraham was in pain and compromised. Sometimes the right form of visiting the sick is to step back, to not impose one's presence when the person needs space. As has been aptly observed, the principle here is a kind of pastoral sensitivity: you enter the room, but if you see the patient's dignity is compromised, you step back. This is a lesson for all who perform *bikkur cholim*.

The precise relationship between God's own visitation and the angels' arrival is not entirely clear in the *gemara*. One possibility is that God's visit was manifested through the angels' presence. Another possibility, reflected in the *midrash* cited by Tosafot, is that they were distinct: Abraham asked God to wait while he went to welcome the angels and bring them in. Tosafot discusses this: *hashechina hayeta rotza lehistalek me'al Avraham* — the divine presence wished to withdraw — but then the *midrash* suggests that Abraham asked God to wait, *ad sheyachnis otan*, while he attended to the angels. This reading distinguishes the divine visit from the angelic one.

### Angels' Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן נִינְהוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה אֲנָשִׁים – מִיכָאֵל וְגַבְרִיאֵל וּרְפָאֵל. מִיכָאֵל שֶׁבָּא לְבַשֵּׂר אֶת שָׂרָה, רְפָאֵל שֶׁבָּא לְרַפֵּא אֶת אַבְרָהָם, גַּבְרִיאֵל אֲזַל לְמֶהְפְּכַיהּ לִסְדוֹם. וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וַיָּבֹאוּ שְׁנֵי הַמַּלְאָכִים סְדֹמָה בָּעֶרֶב״! דַּאֲזַל מִיכָאֵל בַּהֲדֵיהּ לְשֵׁזְבֵיהּ לְלוֹט. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּהֲפֹךְ אֶת הֶעָרִים הָאֵל״, וְלָא כְּתִיב ״וַיַּהַפְכוּ״ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Who are these three men?** They are the angels **Michael, Gabriel, and Raphael: Michael, who came to announce** to **Sarah** that she was to give birth to a son; **Raphael, who came to heal Abraham** after his circumcision; and **Gabriel,** who **went to overturn Sodom.** The Gemara asks: **But it is written: "And the two angels came to Sodom in the evening"** (Genesis 19:1). The Gemara answers **that Michael went along with** Gabriel to Sodom **to save Lot.** The Gemara notes: The language **is also precise, as it is written: "And he overturned those cities"** (Genesis 19:25), **and it is not written: They overturned** those cities. **Conclude from it** that only one angel overturned Sodom.

The *gemara* asks: *mani hashelosha anashim?* — who were these three men? *Michael veGavriel veRefael.* Michael came to bring the good tidings to Sarah. Refael, whose name evokes *refuah* — healing — came to heal Abraham from his *brit milah*. Gabriel, whose name evokes *gevurah* — strength — went to overturn Sodom.

A textual difficulty is immediately raised: the verse says *vayavo'u shenei hamalakhim Sdoma ba'erev* — only two angels came to Sodom. Why two, if only Gabriel was sent to destroy the city? The answer is that Michael went along to save Lot. This accords with Michael's role as a bearer of good tidings. The proof that only Gabriel destroyed the cities is that the verse says *vayahafoch et he'arim ha'el* in the singular — *he* overturned — not *vayahafechu* in the plural.

Tosafot raises a difficulty here from the principle in *Bereishit Rabbah* that a single angel cannot perform two missions. How then could Michael both announce the good news to Sarah and save Lot? Tosafot offers two answers. First, the missions were sequential, not simultaneous: the first mission ended when he informed Sarah, and only then did a new mission begin — *az hitchil shelichut acheret*. This is permitted; it is performing two tasks simultaneously that is forbidden. Second, Tosafot notes that *Bereishit Rabbah* in some versions assigns the saving of Lot to Refael, not Michael. In that version, healing Abraham and saving Lot are connected types of action — *refuah vehatzalah inyan echad* — and are considered a single category of mission.

### Accept/Refuse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּפְצַר בָּם מְאֹד״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִכָּאן שֶׁמְּסָרְבִין לְקָטָן, וְאֵין מְסָרְבִין לַגָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **"And he urged them greatly"** (Genesis 19:3), only after which they acquiesced? **Rabbi Elazar says: From here** we learn **that one may decline** the request of **a lesser man, but one may not decline** the request of **a great man.**

By Abraham, the verse records that when he offered hospitality, they immediately accepted: *ken ta'aseh ka'asher dibarta*. By Lot, by contrast, *vayiftzar bam me'od* — he had to press them greatly before they agreed. Rabbi Elazar derives from this: *mikan she'mesarvin lekatan ve'ein mesarvin legadol* — from here we learn that one may decline an initial offer from someone of lower standing, but one does not refuse a person of distinction. It would be offensive to a *chashuv* person if their hospitality were casually turned down; with someone of lesser standing, a degree of initial reluctance is appropriate social conduct.

## Righteous vs Wicked

### Promise & Fulfill

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״וְאֶקְחָה פַת לֶחֶם״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶל הַבָּקָר רָץ אַבְרָהָם״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִכָּאן שֶׁצַּדִּיקִים אוֹמְרִים מְעַט וְעוֹשִׂים הַרְבֵּה, רְשָׁעִים אוֹמְרִים הַרְבֵּה, וַאֲפִילּוּ מְעַט אֵינָם עוֹשִׂים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues analyzing the same passage. **It is written: "And I will fetch a morsel of bread,** and satisfy your heart" (Genesis 18:5), **and it is written: "And Abraham ran to the herd,** and fetched a calf tender and good" (Genesis 18:7). **Rabbi Elazar said: From here** we learn **that** the **righteous say little and do much,** whereas the **wicked say much and do not do even a little.**

The verse says Abraham promised *pat lechem* — a piece of bread — yet *el habakar ratz Avraham* — he ran to the cattle and prepared a full feast. Rabbi Elazar teaches: *mikan shetzadikim omrim me'at ve'osim harbeh* — from here we learn that the righteous promise little and do much.

### Ephron's Greed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן, מֵעֶפְרוֹן. מֵעִיקָּרָא כְּתִיב: ״אֶרֶץ אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שֶׁקֶל כֶּסֶף״, וּלְבַסּוֹף כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁמַע אַבְרָהָם אֶל עֶפְרוֹן וַיִּשְׁקֹל אַבְרָהָם לְעֶפְרֹן אֶת הַכֶּסֶף אֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר בְּאׇזְנֵי בְנֵי חֵת אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שֶׁקֶל כֶּסֶף עֹבֵר לַסֹּחֵר״, דְּלָא שְׁקַל מִינֵּיהּ אֶלָּא קִנְטְרֵי, דְּאִיכָּא דּוּכְתָּא דְּקָרֵי לֵיהּ לְתִיקְלָא ״קִנְטֵירָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do we** derive this principle that the wicked say much and do not do even a little? We derive it **from Ephron. Initially, it is written** that Ephron said to Abraham: **"A piece of land worth four hundred shekels of silver,** what is that between me and you?" (Genesis 23:15). And **ultimately it is written: "And Abraham listened to Ephron; and Abraham weighed to Ephron the silver, which he had named in the hearing of the children of Heth, four hundred shekels of silver, current money with the merchant"** (Genesis 23:16), i.e., shekels that could be used in any location. This teaches **that** not only did Ephron take shekels from Abraham, **he took from him only centenaria [*kantarei*],** i.e., superior coins, **as there is a place where they call a shekel a centenarius.**

The wicked, by contrast, promise much and do not even deliver a little. The paradigm case is Ephron. He announced grandly that the field was worth four hundred *shekel* of silver — *mah hi beini uveinecha* — essentially suggesting it was a negligible sum. Yet in the end, *vayishkol Avraham le'Efron et hakesef* — Abraham weighed out the full sum — and not merely the standard *shekel*, but coins of the type *over lasocher*, accepted by every merchant. The *gemara* further notes that Ephron ultimately insisted on *kantari* — a coin of greater weight — *de'ika duchta dekaru lei letikla kantari*, in places the *shekel* is called a *kantari*, indicating the payment was even larger than it might first appear. He said he would give it for nothing and in the end demanded a great deal.

### Bread & Purity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב ״קֶמַח״ וּכְתִיב ״סֹלֶת״. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִכָּאן שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה צָרָה עֵינֶיהָ בְּאוֹרְחִים יוֹתֵר מִן הָאִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "Make ready quickly three measures of flour, fine flour" (Genesis 18:6). The Gemara questions the apparent redundancy. **It is written: "Flour," and it is** also **written: "Fine flour." Rabbi Yitzḥak says: From here** we learn **that a woman is more stingy with guests than a man.** Sarah wanted to use merely flour, and Abraham persuaded her to use fine flour.

The verse says Abraham told Sarah to take *kemach* — flour — yet a moment later the text says *solet* — fine flour. Rather than reading these as a single description, the *gemara* treats them as two distinct things. Rabbi Yitzchak derives: *mikan she'ha'ishah tzarah einah be'orchim yoter min ha'ish* — from here we learn that a woman tends to be more reluctant than a man to give food to guests. The *gemara* seems to suggest that Abraham specified both *kemach* and *solet* precisely because Sarah was inclined to give only the coarser flour. One might note that this generalization could stem from a practical reality: women managing the household provisions know exactly how much food is on hand and must feed the family, while men can more easily make lavish promises.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב ״לוּשִׁי וַעֲשִׂי עֻגוֹת״, וּכְתִיב ״וַיִּקַּח חֶמְאָה וְחָלָב וּבֶן הַבָּקָר״, וְאִילּוּ לֶחֶם לָא אַיְיתִי לְקַמַּיְיהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its analysis of the verses. **It is written: "Knead it, and make cakes"** (Genesis 18:6), and two verses later **it is written: "And he took curd, and milk, and the calf** which he prepared" (Genesis 18:8). Abraham served these items to the guests, **and yet he did not bring bread before them** despite having instructed Sarah to prepare baked goods.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אֶפְרַיִם מִקְשָׁאָה תַּלְמִידוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר: אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ אוֹכֵל חוּלִּין בְּטׇהֳרָה הָיָה, וְשָׂרָה אִמֵּנוּ אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם פֵּירְסָה נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Efrayim Miksha'a, disciple of Rabbi Meir, says in the name of Rabbi Meir: Abraham, our forefather, would eat non-sacred food** only when he was **in** a state of **ritual purity,** i.e., he treated his food as though it were consecrated to God. **And Sarah, our foremother, menstruated that day,** which rendered the baked goods ritually impure, preventing Abraham from handling them. Therefore, they could not serve bread to their guests.

The bread promised never appears on the table — the verse records that Abraham served the butter, the milk, and the meat, but not the bread. Where did it go? *Efrayim Maksha'ah*, the student of Rabbi Meir, teaches in the name of Rabbi Meir: *Avraham Avinu ochel chullin betaharah hayah* — Abraham maintained a practice of eating even non-sanctified food in a state of purity. And that day, *Sarah Imeinu oto hayom pirsa niddah* — Sarah had her period return, her youth miraculously restored. As a result, her touching of the bread rendered it *tamei*, and since Abraham would not eat something *tamei*, he could not serve it to his guests. This is itself a striking point: even if the guests themselves would not have been troubled by *tumah*, Abraham's personal practice prevented him from serving them something he himself would not eat. There is something profound in that — to treat a guest as less than you would treat yourself is in some sense a diminishment of their dignity.

The question is raised whether Abraham understood his guests to be angels. The *gemara* suggests he did not — or at least was uncertain. One detail offered is that Abraham stood and attended to them while they ate, which may indicate he joined them at table so they should not feel uncomfortable eating while he stood apart. The verse *vehu omed aleihem tachat ha'etz vayochelu* is most naturally read as: he stood over them attending, and they ate.

### Courtesy Duty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמְרוּ אֵלָיו אַיֵּה שָׂרָה אִשְׁתֶּךָ וַיֹּאמֶר הִנֵּה בָאֹהֶל״, לְהוֹדִיעַ שֶׁשָּׂרָה אִמֵּנוּ צְנוּעָה הָיְתָה. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יִצְחָק: יוֹדְעִים הָיוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת שֶׁשָּׂרָה אִמֵּנוּ בָּאֹהֶל הָיְתָה, אֶלָּא מַאי ״בָּאֹהֶל״ – כְּדֵי לְחַבְּבָהּ עַל בַּעְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"And they said to him: Where is Sarah your wife? And he said: Behold, in the tent"** (Genesis 18:9). The Gemara explains that this verse serves **to inform** us **that Sarah, our foremother, was a modest woman,** as she remained inside while the guests were present. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says, and some say** it is **Rabbi Yitzḥak** who says: **The ministering angels,** who visited Abraham in the guise of travelers, **knew that Sarah, our foremother, was inside the tent. Rather, what** was the purpose of their eliciting Abraham's response: **In the tent?** It was **in order to endear her to her husband,** by accentuating Sarah's modesty.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: כְּדֵי לְשַׁגֵּר לָהּ כּוֹס שֶׁל בְּרָכָה. תָּנֵי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לָמָּה נָקוּד עַל אַיּוֹ שֶׁבְּ״אֵלָיו״ – לִימְּדָה תּוֹרָה דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ, שֶׁיִּשְׁאַל אָדָם בָּאַכְסַנְיָא שֶׁלּוֹ. וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין שׁוֹאֲלִין בִּשְׁלוֹם אִשָּׁה כְּלָל! עַל יְדֵי בַּעְלָהּ שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** They inquired about her **in order to send her** the **cup of blessing.** It is customary to recite Grace after Meals over a cup of wine, which is then distributed to those present. **It is taught in the name of Rabbi Yosei: Why are there dots** in the Torah scroll **upon the letters *alef*, *yod*,** and ***vav* in** the word **"to him [*eilav*]"?** These letters spell *ayo*, which means: Where is he? **The Torah is teaching the proper etiquette,** which is **that a person should inquire of his hostess** about his host, just as he should inquire about the welfare of his hostess from the host. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Shmuel say: One may not inquire about the welfare of a woman at all,** as this is immodest? The Gemara answers: A greeting **by means of her husband is different.** Asking a husband about his wife is not considered immodest.

The angels then asked: *ayeh Sarah ishtecha* — where is Sarah your wife? The *gemara* says this was to teach: *le'hodi'a she'Sarah Imeinu tzenu'ah hayetah* — that Sarah our matriarch was modest, remaining in the tent. But why would the angels ask something they obviously already knew? Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav (and some attribute it to Rav Yitzchak): *yod'im hayu malachei hasharet she'Sarah Imeinu ba'ohel hayetah* — they certainly knew she was in the tent — but they asked *kedei lechababah al ba'alah* — in order to make her dear to her husband, so that Abraham would be reminded of her modesty. Rabbi Yosei the son of Rabbi Chanina offers a different reason: *kedei leshadar lah kos shel berachah* — in order to include her by sending her the cup of *birkat hamazon*.

This second reason is particularly powerful. In a social setting where men and women eat in entirely separate spaces, a woman is easily made invisible in the proceedings that concern her most. By asking after her, by directing the message to her even in her absence, and by sending her the *kos shel berachah* from the concluding blessing, the angels were finding a way to make Sarah a subject rather than merely an object of the conversation. The good tidings, after all, were addressed to her.

The verse *vayomru eilav* — and they said *to him* — has dots written over the letters *alef*, *yud*, and *vav* in the Torah scroll. A *tanna* in the name of Rabbi Yosei asks: why the dots? *Limdah Torah derech eretz* — the Torah is teaching proper conduct — *sheyish'al adam be'achsanyah shelo* — that when you are hosted, you should ask after your hostess. Tosafot presses further: those three dotted letters spell *ayav* — the masculine form of *where is he* — suggesting the reverse is also implied. Just as a guest should ask the host about his wife, so a guest should ask a hostess about her husband. The Torah is teaching gender-equal courtesy in inquiry.

The *gemara* raises a challenge: doesn't Shmuel teach *ein sho'alin bish'lom isha klal* — one does not inquire about a woman's welfare at all — lest it lead to inappropriate familiarity? The *gemara* resolves: *al yedei ba'alah shani* — asking through her husband is different and entirely permissible. Nobody would impute impropriety to a guest who asks the host: "How is your wife? Please send her my regards." The prohibition is only against direct inquiry to the woman herself, which could lead to excessive personal interaction.

## Sarah's Renewal

### Miraculous Nursing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַחֲרֵי בְלֹתִי הָיְתָה לִּי עֶדְנָה״, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אַחַר שֶׁנִּתְבַּלָּה הַבָּשָׂר וְרַבּוּ הַקְּמָטִין – נִתְעַדֵּן הַבָּשָׂר וְנִתְפַּשְּׁטוּ הַקְּמָטִין, וְחָזַר הַיּוֹפִי לִמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the verses that describe Sarah at the time: "And Sarah laughed within herself, saying: **After I am waxed old [*veloti*] shall I have pleasure [*edna*]"** (Genesis 18:12). **Rav Ḥisda says: After the skin had worn out [*nitballa*] and become full of wrinkles, the skin once again became soft [*nitadden*] and her wrinkles smoothed out, and** Sarah's **beauty returned to its place.**

Rav Chisda expounds *acharei beloti hayeta li ednah* — after I have been worn away, shall I have youth? He reads it not as a rhetorical question but as a description of an actual event: *achari she'nisbalah habasar ravu hakmatim nit'adnah habasar venistapshetu hakmatim* — after the flesh had worn away and the wrinkles had multiplied, the flesh became supple and the wrinkles smoothed out. *Vechazar hayofi limkomo* — the beauty returned to its place. (Rashi, in his French gloss, identifies the word *kmatim* — wrinkles — with a French term that, to a modern reader, brings to mind the word "prunes.")

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב ״וַאדֹנִי זָקֵן״, וּכְתִיב ״וַאֲנִי זָקַנְתִּי״ – דְּלָא מוֹתֵיב הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא כִּדְקָאָמְרָה אִיהִי.
>
> **Translation:** **It is written** that Sarah said: **"And my lord is old"** (Genesis 18:12), **and it is written:** "And the Lord said to Abraham: Why did Sarah laugh, saying: Shall I certainly bear a child, **and I am old?"** (Genesis 18:13). This verse indicates that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, did not repeat** to Abraham **that** which Sarah actually **said,** that her husband is old. Why did God change the wording of her statement so that she was referring to herself?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֲאֵל: גָּדוֹל שָׁלוֹם, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שִׁינָּה בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתִּצְחַק שָׂרָה בְּקִרְבָּהּ וְגוֹ׳ וַאדֹנִי זָקֵן״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל אַבְרָהָם וְגוֹ׳ וַאֲנִי זָקַנְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Peace is** of such **great** importance **that even the Holy One, Blessed be He, altered** the truth for the sake of preserving peace, **as it is stated: "And Sarah laughed within herself,** saying: After I am waxed old shall I have pleasure, **and my lord is old," and it is written: "And the Lord said to Abraham:** Why did Sarah laugh, saying: Shall I certainly bear a child, **and I am old?"**

The verse says *va'tedaber Sarah* — Sarah spoke of nursing: *vatomer mi milel le'Avraham heynika vanim Sarah* — who would have declared to Abraham that Sarah would nurse children? But why *banim* in the plural — Sarah nursed only one child. Rabbi Levi explains: on the day that Abraham weaned Isaac, he made a great feast. The nations of the world began to mock, saying they had seen this elderly couple produce a foundling from the marketplace, a child they claimed as their own, and were making a grand celebration to legitimize the deception. What did Abraham do? He invited all the great people of that generation and their wives — notably, he invited their wives specifically — and Sarah's milk flowed miraculously: *veniftchu dadeha kishnei ma'ayanot* — her breasts opened like two wellsprings — and she nursed all of the infants who had been brought. That is the meaning of *heynika vanim* — she nursed *children*, in the plural. This was the proof that she had truly given birth.

### Isaac's Features

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב ״וַאדֹנִי זָקֵן״ וּכְתִיב ״וַאֲנִי זָקַנְתִּי״ – דְּלָא מוֹתֵיב הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא כִּדְקָאָמְרָה אִיהִי... וַתֹּאמֶר מִי מִלֵּל לְאַבְרָהָם הֵנִיקָה בָנִים שָׂרָה״, כַּמָּה בָּנִים הֵנִיקָה שָׂרָה? אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שֶׁגָּמַל אַבְרָהָם אֶת יִצְחָק בְּנוֹ עָשָׂה סְעוּדָה גְּדוֹלָה. הָיוּ כׇּל אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם מְרַנְּנִים וְאוֹמְרִים: רְאִיתֶם זָקֵן וּזְקֵנָה שֶׁהֵבִיאוּ אֲסוּפִי מִן הַשּׁוּק וְאוֹמְרִים: בְּנֵינוּ הוּא, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁעוֹשִׂין מִשְׁתֶּה גָּדוֹל לְהַעֲמִיד דִּבְרֵיהֶם! מָה עָשָׂה אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ – הָלַךְ וְזִימֵּן כׇּל גְּדוֹלֵי הַדּוֹר, וְשָׂרָה אִמֵּנוּ זִימְּנָה אֶת נְשׁוֹתֵיהֶם. וְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת הֵבִיאָה בְּנָהּ עִמָּהּ, וּמְנִיקָתָהּ לֹא הֵבִיאָה, וְנַעֲשָׂה נֵס בְּשָׂרָה אִמֵּנוּ וְנִפְתְּחוּ דַּדֶּיהָ כִּשְׁנֵי מַעֲיָינוֹת, וְהֵנִיקָה אֶת כּוּלָּן. וַעֲדַיִן הָיוּ מְרַנְּנִים וְאוֹמְרִים: ״אִם שָׂרָה הֲבַת תִּשְׁעִים שָׁנָה תֵּלֵד, אַבְרָהָם בֶּן מֵאָה שָׁנָה יוֹלִיד״? מִיָּד נֶהְפַּךְ קְלַסְתֵּר פָּנִים שֶׁל יִצְחָק וְנִדְמָה לְאַבְרָהָם, פָּתְחוּ כּוּלָּם וְאָמְרוּ: ״אַבְרָהָם הוֹלִיד אֶת יִצְחָק״.
>
> **Translation:** In reference to Sarah having given birth to Isaac, the verse states: **"And she said: Who would have said to Abraham that Sarah should nurse children?"** (Genesis 21:7). The Gemara asks: **How many children did Sarah nurse?** Why does the verse use the plural form when she had only one child? **Rabbi Levi says: That day when Abraham weaned his son Isaac, he prepared a great** celebratory **feast. All of the nations of the world were gossiping and saying** to each other: **See** this **old man and old woman who brought a foundling from the market and are saying: He is our son, and moreover they are making a great feast to bolster their claim.** **What did Abraham, our forefather, do? He went and invited all of the great** men **of** that **generation, and Sarah, our foremother, invited their wives. Each and every one** of the wives **brought her child with her but did not bring her wet nurse. And a miracle occurred to Sarah, our foremother, and her breasts were opened like two springs, and she nursed all** of these children. **And still** those people **were gossiping and saying** to each other: Even **if Sarah,** at **ninety years of age, can give birth, can Abraham,** at **one hundred years of age, father** a child? **Immediately, the countenance of Isaac's face transformed and appeared** exactly like that **of Abraham. Everyone exclaimed and said: "Abraham fathered Isaac"** (Genesis 25:19).

The skeptics were not satisfied. They continued to mock: even if Sarah gave birth, perhaps Abraham — at one hundred years old — was not the father. Immediately *nehpach klastar panav shel Yitzchak venidmeh le'Avraham* — Isaac's facial features transformed and he resembled Abraham unmistakably. At that, everyone conceded: *Avraham holid et Yitzchak*. This is in fact what the verse in *parashat Toldot* proclaims: *Eleh toldot Yitzchak ben Avraham Avraham holid et Yitzchak* — and this declaration, according to the *midrash*, reflects the universal acknowledgment that followed the transformation of Isaac's face. The phenomenon of an infant's features shifting over time to resemble a parent is itself quite natural, which gives this *midrash* a certain grounding.

## Origins of Aging

### Abraham's Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד אַבְרָהָם לֹא הָיָה זִקְנָה. מַאן דַּהֲוָה בָּעֵי לְמִשְׁתַּעֵי בַּהֲדֵי אַבְרָהָם – מִשְׁתַּעֵי בַּהֲדֵי יִצְחָק. בַּהֲדֵי יִצְחָק מִשְׁתַּעֵי בַּהֲדֵי אַבְרָהָם. אֲתָא אַבְרָהָם בְּעָא רַחֲמֵי וַהֲוָה זִקְנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַבְרָהָם זָקֵן בָּא בַּיָּמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues discussing Abraham: **Until Abraham, there was no aging,** i.e., old age was not physically recognizable. Consequently, **one who wanted to speak to Abraham** would mistakenly **speak to Isaac,** and vice versa: An individual who wanted to speak **to Isaac** would **speak to Abraham,** as they were indistinguishable. **Abraham came and prayed for mercy, and aging was** at last noticeable, **as it is stated: "And Abraham was old, well stricken in age"** (Genesis 24:1), which is the first time that aging is mentioned in the Bible.

*Ad Avraham lo haya zikna* — until Abraham there was no old age. People aged and died, but the aging was not reflected in their bodily appearance. This created a practical problem: *man dehava ba'i lemishta'ei bahadi Avraham mishta'ei bahadi Yitzchak* — someone who wanted to speak with Abraham would accidentally speak with Isaac, since they looked identical. And vice versa. Father and son were indistinguishable. So *ata Avraham ba'ei rachamei vehava zikna* — Abraham prayed for old age to be visibly present, and there was old age — as the verse states: *ve'Avraham zaken ba bayamim*, which is the first time the Torah explicitly uses *zaken* to describe someone's condition.

One might raise the question: didn't the verse earlier say *adoni zaken* — my master is old — which predates this moment? And indeed the *gemara* does not quote that verse. The *gemara* seems to be working with the idea that the bodily condition of aging was not yet visible, even if people experienced diminished capacities. There is also the consideration that Sarah's own words *acharei beloti* — after I have been worn away — and the *gemara*'s own reading of them, suggest visible physical change did exist. The *gemara* apparently holds that while certain capacities declined with age, the outward appearance of being aged — the kind that would allow you to distinguish a 100-year-old from a 60-year-old — was not present until Abraham prayed for it.

This is a profound mythic idea. Abraham was not asking to look old for honor's sake alone, though in a society that reveres age, that would certainly be a consideration. He was asking that his life experience, his decades, be legible on his face. There is something deeply counter-cultural about this — contemporary culture endlessly seeks to appear younger. Here, the very first prayer for visible aging is an act of claiming one's identity, of saying that one's journey through time should be written on one's body.

### Jacob's Sickness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד יַעֲקֹב לָא הֲוָה חוּלְשָׁא. אֲתָא יַעֲקֹב בְּעָא רַחֲמֵי וַהֲוָה חוּלְשָׁא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לְיוֹסֵף הִנֵּה אָבִיךָ חֹלֶה״. עַד דַּאֲתָא אֱלִישָׁע לָא הֲוָה דְּחָלֵישׁ וְאִתְּפַח. אֲתָא אֱלִישָׁע בְּעָא רַחֲמֵי וְאִתְּפַח, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וֶאֱלִישָׁע חָלָה אֶת חׇלְיוֹ אֲשֶׁר יָמוּת בּוֹ״ – מִכְּלָל דְּחָלָה חֹלִי אַחֲרִיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Until Jacob, there was no illness** leading up to death; rather, one would die suddenly. **Jacob came and prayed for mercy, and illness was** brought to the world, allowing one to prepare for his death, **as it is stated: "And one said to Joseph: Behold, your father is sick"** (Genesis 48:1), which is the first time that sickness preceding death is mentioned in the Bible. **Until Elisha, one did not fall ill and** then **heal,** as everyone who fell ill would die. **Elisha came and prayed for mercy and he was healed, as it is written: "Now Elisha fell ill with his illness from which he was to die"** (II Kings 13:14). **By inference,** one can derive that **he** had previously **fallen ill** with **other illnesses** from which he did not die.

*Ad Yaakov lo hava chulsha* — until Jacob there was no sickness as a precursor to death. People simply died. Jacob prayed that illness should precede death — *ata Yaakov ba'ei rachamei vehava chulsha* — as the verse records: *vayomer le'Yosef hinei avicha choleh* — and he said to Joseph, your father is ill. This is the first occurrence in the Torah of someone being sick. The purpose is not obscure: illness preceding death gives a person the opportunity to prepare — mentally, spiritually, practically — for what is coming. It is a gift of awareness, of transition, of the ability to put affairs in order and to say what needs to be said.

### Elisha's Illness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳלָאִין חָלָה אֱלִישָׁע – אֶחָד שֶׁדְּחָפוֹ לְגֵיחֲזִי בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו, וְאֶחָד שֶׁגֵּירָה דּוּבִּין בַּתִּינוֹקוֹת, וְאֶחָד שֶׁמֵּת בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וֶאֱלִישָׁע חָלָה אֶת חׇלְיוֹ אֲשֶׁר יָמוּת בּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Elisha fell ill with three illnesses: One** was due to the fact **that he pushed Gehazi away with both hands,** i.e., he banished Gehazi without granting him a chance to repent (see II Kings, chapter 5). **One** was due to the fact **that he incited bears against young children** (see II Kings 2:23–25). **And one** was the illness **from which he died, as it is stated: "Now Elisha fell ill of his illness from which he was to die"** (II Kings 13:14).

*Ad ata Elisha lo hava dechalash ve'itpach* — until Elisha came, no one became ill and then recovered. All illness led directly to death. *Ata Elisha ba'ei rachamei ve'itpach* — Elisha prayed and recovered, introducing the possibility of recovery from illness. The textual proof is *ve'Elisha chala et chalyo asher yamut bo* — the final illness he would die from — which implies *michlal dechali chali achrinei* — that he had been sick from other illnesses before, illnesses from which he had recovered.

The *Talmud* specifies: *tanu rabbanan, shlosha cholayim chala Elisha* — our Rabbis taught that Elisha suffered three illnesses. One for pushing away Gechazi with both hands — for completely rejecting someone where one should push away with the left but draw close with the right. One for inciting the bears against the children who mocked him. And one that brought about his death: *shenemar ve'Elisha chala et chalyo asher yamut bo*.

The introduction of recoverable illness also gives rise to the whole dynamic of *teshuva*, prayer, and healing — the realization that sickness is not automatically terminal opens a space for spiritual response.

## Workers' Rights

### Stipulations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא עַד שֶׁלֹּא יַתְחִילוּ בַּמְּלָאכָה צֵא וֶאֱמוֹר לָהֶם עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לָכֶם עָלַי אֶלָּא פַּת וְקִטְנִית כּוּ׳. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף לְרַב חִסְדָּא: פַּת קִטְנִית תְּנַן, אוֹ פַּת וְקִטְנִית תְּנַן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָאֱלֹהִים! צְרִיכָה וָיו כִּי מוּרְדְּיָא דְלִבְרוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna (83a) teaches that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Matya said to his son: **Rather, before they begin** engaging **in** their **labor, go out and say to them:** The stipulation that food will be provided is **on the condition that you have** the right to claim **from me only** a meal of **bread and legumes,** which is the typical meal given to laborers. **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Yosef, said to Rav Ḥisda:** Did **we learn: Bread of legumes [*pat kitnit*],** i.e., inferior-quality bread made of legumes, **or** did **we learn: Bread and legumes [*pat vekitnit*]?** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: By God!** That word *vekitnit* **requires** at its beginning the letter ***vav*** as large **as an oar [*mordeya*]** made **of cypress** wood [*deliberately*], i.e., *pat vekitnit* is undoubtedly the correct version.

The *gemara* now returns to *halakha*, picking up the thread of workers' entitlements and employer stipulations. The case discussed is a father seeking to limit his obligation to feed workers before they begin: *al menat she'ein lachem ela pat vekitnit bilvad* — on condition that I will give you only bread and legumes. Rav Acha the son of Rav Yosef raises a pointed grammatical question: *l'afchuzei — pat kitnit tnan o pat vekitnit tnan* — does the *mishna* mean bread *made of* legumes (a single item: legume-bread), or bread *and* legumes (two separate items)? The answer is that it requires a *vav* — *pat vekitnit* — and indeed that *vav* is essential to the meaning, like the pole used to keep a boat in the middle of the river away from the shore. Without the *vav*, the two items collapse into one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הַכֹּל כְּמִנְהַג הַמְּדִינָה. הַכֹּל לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי הָא דִּתְנַן: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֵל וְאָמַר לוֹ כְּאֶחָד וְכִשְׁנַיִם מִבְּנֵי הָעִיר – נוֹתֵן לוֹ כַּפָּחוּת שֶׁבַּשְּׂכִירוּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** The son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Matya **did not need to state** this, as the principle is: **Everything is in accordance with the regional custom.** The Gemara asks: This term: **Everything,** serves **to add what?** What is the *tanna* including by this term? The Gemara answers: It serves **to add that which we learned** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who hires a laborer and said to him:** I will pay you **as one or two of the residents of the city** are paid, **he gives him** wages in accordance **with the lowest wage** paid in that region. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua. The Rabbis say: One divides** the difference **between** the highest and lowest paid wages, thereby giving the wages to this laborer according to the average of the regional custom. This *halakha* is alluded to in the statement of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

Rabban Gamliel teaches: even if one stipulates to feed workers, one need not exceed the standard — *hakol keminhag hamedina*. The *gemara* asks what exactly this means. It cites a *mishna*: if an employer hires a worker and says pay him as one of the ordinary workers in town, what does that mean if there is a range of wages? Rabbi Yehoshua says: give him the lower end of the range. The Sages say: *meshamnin beineihem* — calculate the median. The *gemara* implies that the *minhag* is not the minimum; the standard means something closer to the average. The minimum is the floor of the range, not its representative value.

### Mishna Frame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ אוֹכְלִין מִן הַתּוֹרָה: הָעוֹשֶׂה בִּמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע בִּשְׁעַת גְּמַר מְלָאכָה, וּבְתָלוּשׁ מִן הַקַּרְקַע עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמְרָה מְלַאכְתּוֹ, וּבְדָבָר שֶׁגִּידּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ. וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין אוֹכְלִים: הָעוֹשֶׂה בִּמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna details the *halakha* that a laborer is permitted to eat from the produce with which he is working. **And these** laborers **may eat by Torah law:** A laborer **who works with** produce **attached to the ground at the time of the completion of** its **work,** e.g., harvesting produce; **and** a laborer who works **with** produce **detached from the ground before the completion of its work,** i.e., before it is sufficiently processed and thereby subject to tithes. **And** this is the *halakha* provided that they are working **with an item whose growth is from the land. And these** are laborers who **may not eat:** A laborer who **works with** produce **attached to the ground**

The *mishna* now turns to a different dimension of workers' rights — not stipulated feeding arrangements, but the Torah's own entitlement for workers to eat from the produce they are harvesting. *Ve'elu ochlim min haTorah* — the following workers eat by Torah right. The categories are organized along two axes. For *mechubar lekarka* — produce still connected to the ground — the worker may eat *bish'at gmar melacha* — at the time of the final labor, i.e., the harvest. For *talush min hakarka* — produce already detached from the ground — the worker may eat only *ad shelo nigmra melachto* — before the completion of the process. These two are mirror images of each other.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאֵין גְּמַר מְלָאכָה, וּבְתָלוּשׁ מִן הַקַּרְקַע מֵאַחַר שֶׁנִּגְמְרָה מְלַאכְתּוֹ, וּבְדָבָר שֶׁאֵין גִּידּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **not at the time of the completion of** its **work,** i.e., while it is still growing; **and** a laborer who works **with** produce **detached from the ground after the completion of its work,** when it is sufficiently processed and therefore subject to tithes; **and** a laborer who works **with an item whose growth is not from the land.**

Additionally, the entitlement applies only to *davar shegidulo min ha'aretz* — something that grows from the ground. The parallel list of those who may *not* eat reverses each of these conditions: one working with produce still connected to the ground but not at the harvest stage may not eat; one working with harvested produce after the final processing is complete may not eat; and one working with something that does not grow from the ground may not eat.

### Biblical Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי תָבֹא בְּכֶרֶם רֵעֶךָ וְאָכַלְתָּ״. אַשְׁכְּחַן כֶּרֶם, כֹּל מִילֵּי מְנָא לַן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that a laborer may eat from produce attached to the ground, derived? The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "When you come into your neighbor's vineyard, then you may eat** grapes until you have enough at your own pleasure; but you shall not put any in your vessel" (Deuteronomy 23:25). The Gemara asks: **We find** a source for **a vineyard; from where do we** derive that a laborer may likewise eat from **any** other **type** of produce?

*Mena hanei milei?* — what is the biblical source for all this? The verse states: *ki tavo bekerem re'echa ve'achalta anavim* — when you enter your neighbor's vineyard you may eat grapes. From this we know the rule for a vineyard. But how do we extend it to other produce? By analogy: just as a vineyard involves produce that grows from the ground and the worker eats during the final labor, so too for anything with those features.

### Extending Produce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּמְרִינַן מִכֶּרֶם: מָה כֶּרֶם מְיוּחָד, דָּבָר שֶׁגִּידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע, וּבִשְׁעַת גְּמַר מְלָאכָה פּוֹעֵל אוֹכֵל בּוֹ – אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁגִּידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע, בִּשְׁעַת גְּמַר מְלָאכָה פּוֹעֵל אוֹכֵל בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **We derive** it **from** a comparison to the case of **a vineyard: Just as a vineyard is unique** in that it is **an entity whose growth is from the ground, and the laborer eats** from **it at the time of the completion of** its **work,** i.e., when he is harvesting the grapes, **so too** with regard to **any entity whose growth is from the ground** and it is **at the time of the completion of** its **work, a laborer may eat** from **it.**

However, the *gemara* immediately challenges this: perhaps these two categories are still limited in scope, since both have distinctive characteristics: grain (*kamah*) is subject to *challah*, and vineyard (*kerem*) is subject to *olelot*. Each one's special feature could be the reason for the entitlement. The *gemara* applies the standard hermeneutical move of *hatzad hashaveh* — the common denominator: both grow from the ground and in both cases the worker eats during the final labor. From this common denominator we generalize.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְכֶרֶם – שֶׁכֵּן חַיָּיב בְּעוֹלֵלוֹת! גָּמְרִינַן מִקָּמָה. קָמָה גּוּפַהּ מְנָא לַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי תָבֹא בְּקָמַת רֵעֶךָ וְקָטַפְתָּ מְלִילוֹת בְּיָדֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this derivation: **What** is notable **about a vineyard?** It is notable **in that** the owner of a vineyard is **obligated in** the mitzva of ***olelot*,** the obligation to leave incomplete clusters of grapes for the poor (see Leviticus 19:10). Accordingly, one should not be able to derive the *halakha* of other types of produce from the *halakha* of a vineyard. The Gemara explains: **We derive** the *halakha* that a laborer may eat from other crops **from** the *halakha* that he may eat **standing** grain. The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive that he may eat **standing** grain **itself?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "When you come into your neighbor's standing grain, then you may pluck ears with your hand;** but you shall not move a sickle on to your neighbor's standing grain" (Deuteronomy 23:26).

A further challenge: both grain and grapes produce offerings for the *mizbe'ach* — flour and wine. Perhaps the entitlement is limited to produce brought to the altar, which would include olives (producing oil for the *mincha*) but not more. To this, Rav Pappa observes that olives are themselves called *kerem* — *kerem zayit* — in the verse *vayav'er migadish ve'ad kamah ve'ad kerem zayit*. So olives are included via *kerem* directly. But *kerem stam*, an unqualified *kerem*, refers to grapes; *kerem zayit* is the olive grove. And beyond olives, how do we extend to all produce, including things not offered on the *mizbe'ach*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְקָמָה שֶׁכֵּן חַיֶּיבֶת בַּחַלָּה! וּמִמַּאי דְּהַאי קָמָה קָמָה דְּמִתְחַיֶּיבֶת בְּחַלָּה הִיא? דִּלְמָא כֹּל קָמָה קְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **What** is notable **about standing** grain? It is notable **in that** the owner of dough prepared from grain is **obligated in** the mitzva of ***ḥalla*.** The Gemara asks an incidental question: **And from where** do you know **that this standing** grain mentioned in the verse **is** the same **standing** grain **whose** owner is **obligated in** the mitzva of ***ḥalla*? Perhaps the Merciful One is discussing any standing** produce, not only the five grains from which *ḥalla* must be separated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָתְיָא ״קָמָה״ ״קָמָה״, כְּתִיב הָכָא ״כִּי תָבֹא בְּקָמַת רֵעֶךָ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם ״מֵהָחֵל חֶרְמֵשׁ בַּקָּמָה״. מָה הָתָם קָמָה דְּמִיחַיְּיבָא בְּחַלָּה, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי קָמָה דְּמִיחַיְּיבָא בְּחַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The matter **is derived** by means of a verbal analogy between the term **"standing"** written here and the term **"standing"** written elsewhere. **It is written here: "When you come into your neighbor's standing grain"** (Deuteronomy 23:26), **and it is written there,** with regard to harvesting the barley for the *omer* offering: "Seven weeks you shall count for yourself; **from the time the sickle is first put to the standing grain"** (Deuteronomy 16:9). **Just as there,** in the verse referring to the harvesting of the *omer*, it is the owner of **standing** grain **who is obligated in** the mitzva of ***ḥalla*,** as barley is one of the five grains, **so too here,** with regard to a laborer, it is discussing **standing** grain **whose** owner **is obligated in** the mitzva of ***ḥalla*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְקָמָה שֶׁכֵּן חַיֶּיבֶת בְּחַלָּה! כֶּרֶם יוֹכִיחַ. מָה לְכֶרֶם שֶׁכֵּן חַיָּיב בְּעוֹלֵלוֹת! קָמָה תּוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its discussion by reiterating its earlier question. The comparison between standing grain and other produce **can be refuted** as follows: **What** is notable **about standing** grain? It is notable **in that** the owner of dough prepared from grain is **obligated in** the mitzva of ***ḥalla*.** The Gemara answers: **Let** the case of **a vineyard prove** that this comparison is valid, as the mitzva of *ḥalla* does not apply to the produce of a vineyard, and yet a laborer may eat from it. The Gemara asks: **What** is notable **about a vineyard?** It is notable **in that** its owner is **obligated in** the mitzva of ***olelot*.** The Gemara responds: **Let** the case of **standing** grain **prove** that this is not a decisive factor, as its owner is not obligated in the mitzva of *olelot* and even so a laborer may eat from it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחָזַר הַדִּין: לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה. הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁכֵּן דָּבָר שֶׁגִּידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע, וּבִשְׁעַת גְּמַר מְלָאכָה פּוֹעֵל אוֹכֵל בּוֹ. אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁגִּידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע בִּשְׁעַת גְּמַר מְלָאכָה פּוֹעֵל אוֹכֵל בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Since no exact comparison can be drawn to either a vineyard or standing grain alone, the Gemara suggests a combined solution: **The inference has reverted** to its starting point. **The aspect of this** case, a vineyard, **is not like the aspect of that** case, standing grain. **Their common denominator is that** each one **grows from the earth and at the time of the completion of** its **work** the **laborer may eat** from **it. So too,** with regard to **any type** of produce **that grows from the earth, at the time of the completion of** its **work, a laborer may eat** from **it.**

### Sickle & Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן יֵשׁ בָּהֶן צַד מִזְבֵּחַ. וַאֲתָא נָמֵי זַיִת, דְּאִית בֵּיהּ צַד מִזְבֵּחַ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is unique **about their common denominator?** It is unique **in that they have an aspect** relating to the **altar,** i.e., the products of both a vineyard and standing grain differ from other types of produce in that they are both offered on the altar. Wine is brought for libations and flour in meal-offerings. The Gemara suggests tangentially: **An olive** should **also be derived** through this category of those products which a laborer may eat, **as it** too **has an aspect** relating to the **altar,** in the oil of meal-offerings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזַיִת בְּמָה הַצַּד אָתֵי? הוּא גּוּפֵיהּ כֶּרֶם אִיקְּרִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּבְעֵר מִגָּדִישׁ וְעַד קָמָה וְעַד כֶּרֶם זָיִת״! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: ״כֶּרֶם זַיִת״ – אִקְּרֵי, ״כֶּרֶם״ סְתָמָא – לָא אִקְּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this suggestion: **And** is the *halakha* of **an olive derived from the common factor** of the two types of produce mentioned earlier? But **it itself is called** the fruit of **a vineyard [*kerem*], as it is written: "And he burned up both the piles of produce and the standing grain, and also the olive yards [*kerem zayit*]"** (Judges 15:5). **Rav Pappa said:** This verse does not mean that an olive is considered the product of a vineyard, as in the verse **it is called olive yard [*kerem zayit*],** and it is **not called a plain vineyard.** Therefore, the *halakha* of olives must be derived by analogy from the common denominator.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא! אֶלָּא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אָמַר קְרָא ״וְחֶרְמֵשׁ״ לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי חֶרְמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its discussion: **In any case,** it is **difficult,** as there still has not been found a source according to which the *halakha* that a laborer may eat when he is working applies to all types of produce. **Rather, Shmuel said: The verse states** with regard to a laborer who may eat produce: **"But** you shall not move **a sickle"** (Deuteronomy 23:26). This serves **to include all** types of produce **that are** cut **with a sickle.**

Shmuel answers: *amar kra ubachermesh* — the verse says "sickle." *Lerabos kol ba'alei chermesh* — to include everything to which one brings a sickle, beyond the categories of altar-offerings. One might object that the verse's mention of *chermesh* is needed for a different *derashah*: *bish'at chermesh tochal, shelo bish'at chermesh lo tochal* — you may eat only at the time of the harvest, not at other times. But the *gemara* rejects this: the timing restriction is already derived from *ve'el kelyecha lo siten* — do not put it into your own vessel — which implies you eat only while delivering into the *owner's* vessel, i.e., during the harvest. So *chermesh* is available to do the broader inclusionary work of extending the entitlement to all produce harvested with a sickle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״חֶרְמֵשׁ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: בִּשְׁעַת חֶרְמֵשׁ – אֱכוֹל, שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת חֶרְמֵשׁ – לָא תֵּיכוֹל! הָהוּא מִ״וְּאֶל כֶּלְיְךָ לֹא תִתֵּן״ נָפְקָא. תִּינַח דְּבַר חֶרְמֵשׁ, דְּלָאו בַּר חֶרְמֵשׁ מְנָא לַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But this** word **"sickle" is necessary** to teach a different *halakha* with regard to a laborer: **At the time of the sickle,** i.e., when the work has been completed and the produce is being picked, **you may eat.** But **when it is not** yet **the time of the sickle, you may not eat.** If so, how can Shmuel use the term "sickle" as the source for the *halakha* that a laborer may eat all kinds of produce that are cut with a sickle? The Gemara answers: **That** *halakha*, with regard to when a laborer may eat, **is derived from** the verse: **"But you may not put any in your vessel"** (Deuteronomy 23:25), as the Gemara will explain later. Therefore, the word "sickle" is not required to teach that *halakha* and can be used as the source of the *halakha* that a laborer may eat all kinds of produce that are cut with a sickle, as stated by Shmuel. The Gemara asks: Shmuel's derivation **works out well** for any type of produce **that requires a sickle** for its harvest. But **from where do we** derive that the same applies to a type of produce **that does not require a sickle** for its harvest?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אָמַר קְרָא ״קָמָה״ לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי קֹמָה. וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ: ״קָמָה״, קָמָה דְּמִיחַיְּיבָא בְּחַלָּה! הָנֵי מִילֵּי מִקַּמֵּי דְּנֵיתֵי ״חֶרְמֵשׁ״, הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵי ״חֶרְמֵשׁ״ – אִיתְרַבִּי לֵיהּ כֹּל דְּבַר חֶרְמֵשׁ, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מִיחַיַּיב בְּחַלָּה. ״קָמָה״ לְמָה לִי – לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי קֹמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that the *halakha* concerning which produce a laborer may eat is derived from a different source. **The verse states: "Standing [*kama*]"** (Deuteronomy 23:26), and the unmodified term *kama* serves **to include any standing** produce. The Gemara asks: **But didn't you say** earlier that the term **standing** is referring specifically to **standing** produce **whose** owner is **obligated in** the mitzva of ***ḥalla*,** and not to other produce? The Gemara answers: **That matter** applies **before we derived** a *halakha* from the mention of **"sickle." Now that** a *halakha* **was derived from "sickle," any type** of produce that requires **a sickle** for its harvesting **is included,** as stated earlier, **and** this applies **even though** the owner of that particular produce **is not obligated in** the mitzva of ***ḥalla*.** Accordingly, **why do I** need the term **"standing"?** It serves **to include any standing** produce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּנָפְקָא לַן מֵחֶרְמֵשׁ וּמִקָּמָה ״כִּי תָבֹא בְּכֶרֶם רֵעֶךָ״ לְמָה לִי? אָמַר רָבָא, לְהִלְכוֹתָיו. כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״כִּי תָבֹא״, נֶאֱמַר כָּאן בִּיאָה, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״לֹא תָבוֹא עָלָיו הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּפוֹעֵל הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אַף כָּאן בְּפוֹעֵל הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And now that** we **have derived** the *halakha* concerning which produce a laborer is entitled to eat both **from** the mention of **"sickle" and from "standing," why do I** need the earlier verse: **"When you come into your neighbor's vineyard,** then you may eat grapes until you have enough at your own pleasure; but you may not put any in your vessel" (Deuteronomy 23:25)? The Gemara answers that **Rava said:** This verse is required **for its** unique ***halakhot*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that the phrase **"when you come [*tavo*]"** is interpreted as follows: **Coming [*bia*] is stated here, and coming is also stated there:** "In the same day you shall give him his wages, **and the sun shall not go down [*tavo*] upon it"** (Deuteronomy 24:15). **Just as there,** in Deuteronomy, chapter 24, **the verse is speaking of a laborer, so too here,** in Deuteronomy, chapter 23, **the verse is speaking of a laborer,** despite the fact that this detail is not stated explicitly in the verse.

But what of produce that is not harvested with a sickle? Rav Yitzchak answers: *amar kra kamah* — the verse says *standing grain*, *lerabos kol ba'alei kamah* — to include everything that stands and grows. One might object that *kamah* was derived via a *gezeirah shavah* to mean specifically grain subject to *challah*. The *gemara* resolves this: that restrictive reading of *kamah* was necessary only *mikamei denaysi chermesh* — before we had *chermesh* to expand the category. Once *chermesh* has already expanded the entitlement to include all produce brought with a sickle, even things not subject to *challah*, the word *kamah* is no longer needed to establish that limitation. *Kamah* is now free to serve its expansive function: *lerabos kol davar kamah* — to include anything that stands and grows, with or without a sickle, provided it grows from the ground. The *gemara* will continue from this point in the next session.
