# Sanhedrin 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yoreh Yoreh & Licensing

### Yoreh Yoreh Origins

The daf opens by revisiting the discussion begun on the previous amud concerning the origins and purpose of *yoreh yoreh*. The Gemara had established that rabbis require authorization — *notel reshut* — in order to be exempt from liability if they issue an incorrect ruling in *dinei mamonot*. The Gemara further noted that authority granted from Eretz Yisrael does not extend to Bavel. Against that backdrop, the stories of Rabbi Chiyya's nephews traveling between Eretz Yisrael and Bavel provide the classical basis for the institution of *yoreh yoreh* and *yadin yadin* — the formal authorization to rule on *issur v'heter* and *dinei mamonot* respectively.

### Licensing Purposes

The question that naturally arises is: if authority from Eretz Yisrael does not help for Bavel, why was the licensing of *yoreh yoreh* needed at all for communities outside that framework? The Gemara resolves this by noting that the original context concerned border communities, for whom such licensing was specifically relevant.

This, however, points to a broader purpose of *yoreh yoreh* that has nothing to do with *dinei mamonot* or the compulsion of litigants to appear before a court. When someone comes to a rabbi with a *halachic she'elah* in *issur v'heter*, there is no question of exemption from payment for an erroneous ruling. Rather, the licensing serves a different function entirely: it is a verification of competence and fitness to serve. Not every *talmid chacham*, however knowledgeable, is necessarily fit to serve as a communal rabbi and to *pasken* for others. The Gemara illustrates this with the story of a rabbi who articulated his words imprecisely and thereby misled people into error. The institution of *yoreh yoreh* thus emerged as a mechanism by which the community could be assured that a person presenting himself as a rabbinic authority had been vetted and licensed by his own *rav*.

### Rabbi's Inspection

The Gemara presents the story of *Rebbi* arriving in a community and discovering that the local rabbi had been ruling incorrectly. The text picks up with the phrase *be-osa sha'ah gazru*: at that moment they established the rule, *talmid al yoreh ella im ken notal reshut mi-rabbo* — a student may not issue rulings unless he has received explicit authorization from his teacher. The framing here is personal and individual: it is not primarily a communal concern but a personal prohibition incumbent on the student himself. Regardless of how great a *talmid chacham* one may be, the authority to *pasken* for others must be formally conferred by one's *rav*, who thereby testifies that the student possesses not only the necessary knowledge but also the judgment and communicative skill to render rulings reliably.

### Tanchum Story

A further dimension of this principle is illustrated by the story of Tanchum the son of Rabbi Ami, who arrived in the town of Chasor and taught publicly that it is permitted to rinse grain on Pesach — a process of wetting the grain to loosen the husk — without concern that this would cause the grain to become *chametz*. The townspeople, remarkably well-versed in their *halacha*, challenged him by citing a *braita*: *talmid al yoreh halacha be-makom rabbo* — a student may not issue rulings in the place of his *rebbe*. They identified that Rabbi Mani of Tzor was present in that very town. Tanchum replied: *lav ada'ati* — I was unaware that my *rebbe* was here; had I known, I would not have ruled.

### Distance Requirement

The *braita* the townspeople cited is precise: the prohibition applies unless the student is at least three *parsas* distant from his *rebbe*, corresponding to the breadth of the entire camp of Israel in the wilderness. This demonstrates that the restriction on issuing rulings is not merely a matter of not contradicting one's teacher face-to-face; it extends across an entire geographic zone defined by that three-*parsa* radius. Even a student who has been fully licensed and authorized is nonetheless prohibited from *paskening* within his *rebbe*'s domain, as doing so is considered a breach of respect for the *rebbe*'s authority. Tanchum's immediate acknowledgment of this principle reflects how deeply embedded the norm was in rabbinic culture.

This raises the further question of what precisely constitutes *psak* for these purposes — and whether there exists a form of authorization that would permit a student to rule even within his *rebbe*'s presence. Some understand the deeper significance of *semicha* — ordination — precisely in these terms: not merely as a license to *pasken* at all, but as a grant of authority sufficient to permit *paskening* even in the *rebbe*'s domain.

### Conditional Authority

Having established that partial authority is valid — as seen from the earlier case where one could be authorized for *dinei mamonot* but not for *matir bechorot* — the Gemara turns to a related question: what about conditional authority, such as a license limited by time or by circumstance?

The Gemara cites a proof: Rabbi Yochanan said to Rav Shemen, *harei ata bi-reshutenu ad she-tavo etzelenu* — you have our authority until you return to us. This is, on its face, a geographically bounded grant: you are authorized to rule while you are away from us. The Yerushalmi reads this more broadly, however, treating all forms of limitation — whether by time, place, or subject matter — as equivalent expressions of partial or conditional authorization. One might have thought that since the act of granting *reshut* constitutes an acknowledgment of the student's competence, it should not be possible to confine that competence by time or geography. The Gemara and Yerushalmi, however, see all of these as structurally identical: they are variations of a limited grant, and they are all valid.

This concludes the Gemara's discussion of licensing and the granting of rabbinic authority.

## Court Composition

### Shmuel's View

The Gemara returns to the question of how many judges are required to constitute a valid *beit din* for *dinei mamonot*. Shmuel states: *bei shenayim she-dannu dineihem din ella she-nikra beit din chatzuf* — if two people judged, their judgment is binding, but they are called an impudent court. The position of Shmuel is thus that two judges, while not ideal and technically contemptuous of proper procedure, nonetheless produce a legally valid ruling after the fact.

### Unknown Judge

The Gemara opens a challenge against Shmuel's position by citing a ruling of Rav Nachman. The case is as follows: if a *beit din* of three is constituted and two of the three rule one way — whether to acquit or to obligate — while the third says *eini yodea*, "I don't know," the judges must be supplemented with additional members. The challenge to Shmuel is straightforward: if two judges suffice, why does a panel of three devolve into an invalid court simply because one member declares himself unable to rule? Even if the third voted the opposite way, two against one would still resolve the case — so why should the declaration of ignorance require expanding the panel?

The Gemara answers: *shani hatam de-me'ikara da'ata di-tlata yasvi* — that case is different, because from the outset the court was constituted as a unit of three. When you define the adjudicating body as three, the outcome must emerge from that entire unit. The statement of *eini yodea* is not a vote; it is an abdication, and it leaves the court functionally incomplete even if two valid votes remain. As the Gemara puts it, this is worse than beginning with two: a court that starts as three and is reduced to two effective voices has not concluded its deliberation. By contrast — *hacha lav ada'ata di-tlata yasvi* — when one begins with only two and never constituted the panel as three, Shmuel's ruling applies and the judgment stands.

### Shimon ben Gamliel

A further challenge is raised from a *braita* in which Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel states: *ha-din bi-shelosha, peshara bi-shenayim* — formal judgment requires three, while compromise (*peshara*) requires only two. He adds, *yafeh koach peshara mi-koach ha-din* — the power of compromise exceeds that of judgment in one respect: if two judges issued a formal ruling, the litigants may withdraw from it since it lacks binding force, whereas if two judges effected a *peshara*, the litigants cannot retract, since they have accepted those two as their arbiters. This directly contradicts Shmuel, who holds that formal judgment by two is binding. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, judgment by two is explicitly non-binding.

One might attempt to save Shmuel by arguing that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's view is disputed and that Shmuel rules in accordance with the opposing opinion. The Gemara preempts this.

### Abbahu Disputes

Rabbi Abbahu declares: *shenayim she-dannu le-divrei ha-kol ein dineihem din* — if two judged, their judgment is universally invalid. Rabbi Abbahu thus frames Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position not as a minority opinion but as a universal ruling with which all authorities agree. This forecloses Shmuel's escape route: one cannot claim that Shmuel follows an opposing opinion if, as Rabbi Abbahu maintains, there is no opposing opinion.

The Gemara responds with characteristic directness: *gavra agavra karemita* — you are simply pitting one authority against another. Rabbi Abbahu holds that two is universally invalid; Shmuel holds that two is valid though contemptuous. These are two Amoraim in genuine dispute, and the Gemara leaves them as such.

The landscape of *beit din* composition thus stands as follows: three is the classical and undisputed standard; two is debated, with Shmuel permitting it as *beit din chatzuf* and others rejecting it entirely; and one — specifically, a *yachid mumche* — was addressed in an earlier Gemara in a way that seemed to permit it by consensus. Tosafot explains the earlier ruling of *yachid mumche* as a special rabbinic enactment (*takanat de-Rabbanan*) designed to expand access to *dinei mamonot* adjudication, which is why that Gemara specifically emphasized the requirement of *mumche*. The present discussions of two and three do not concern the *mumche* case at all.

## One Judge Valid?

### Kiblu Aleihu

The Gemara now introduces a *braita* that complicates the picture. The text reads: *dan et ha-din, ve-zikah et ha-chayav, ve-chiyav et ha-zakai, timei et ha-tahor, ve-tiher et ha-tamei* — someone rendered judgment and cleared the guilty, convicted the innocent, declared impure what was pure, or declared pure what was impure. The ruling continues: *mah she-asah asui u-meshalem mi-beito* — what he has done is done, and he must compensate from his own property. The verbs throughout are in the singular, pointing to a single judge. How can a single non-*mumche* individual issue a binding ruling?

The Gemara resolves this with a principle that should resonate from the Yerushalmi: *de-kiblu aleihu* — the litigants accepted this individual's authority. Acceptance by the parties (*kiblu aleihu*) grants jurisdiction to an individual who would otherwise have none.

### Error & Liability

A difficulty immediately presents itself. If the parties accepted this person's authority, why does the Gemara speak of error and financial liability at all? Acceptance of an arbitrator's authority normally renders the outcome binding, much like contractual arbitration. If *kiblu aleihu* places the matter in the hands of this individual, why is his ruling subject to challenge on grounds of error?

### Din Torah Requirement

The Gemara provides the key: *de-amru leih dayant lan din Torah* — the parties accepted his authority, but they stipulated that he must adjudicate according to objective Torah law. They did not surrender themselves to his personal discretion. The acceptance grants him jurisdiction; the demand for *din Torah* preserves the objectivity of the outcome. Because they required *din Torah*, it remains possible to identify an error, and the judge remains exposed to financial liability if he rules incorrectly. The ruling is not automatically insulated by the acceptance, because the acceptance itself was conditional on the application of objective *halacha*.

### Reconciliation

The full framework can now be articulated. There are three distinct scenarios. First, the *yachid mumche*: an established expert judge, ideally holding *notel reshut*, who can adjudicate even without the parties' consent, is exempt from payment (*patur mi-leshalem*) if he errs, since his authority derives from his recognized expertise and formal authorization. Second, a panel of two, which Shmuel considers binding though contemptuous and others consider invalid altogether — these discussions address compelled jurisdiction, independent of whether *kiblu aleihu* applies. Third, the *yachid she-eino mumche* who adjudicates by virtue of *kiblu aleihu* with a demand for *din Torah*: the acceptance gives him jurisdiction, but the requirement of objective law means that an incorrect ruling creates liability (*chayav le-shalem*). This is contrasted with *peshara* or *bitzua*, where *kiblu aleihu* without a demand for *din Torah* means whatever the arbitrator decides is fully binding, with no exposure to liability for error.

## Errors in Rulings

### Taah Bidvar Mishna

The Gemara now turns to the internal analysis of the *braita*'s case. Rav Safra poses the question to Rabbi Abba: *de-ta'ah be-mai* — in what kind of error are we dealing? The Gemara considers first the case of *ta'ah bi-dvar mishna* — an error in black-letter law, something explicit and settled. In such a case, Rabbi Sheshet citing Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi rules: *ta'ah bi-dvar mishna, chozer* — the ruling is reversed. If the judge made a clear, demonstrable error against settled law, the judgment is not merely voidable; it is undone entirely. The consequence is that the judge does not pay from his own pocket, because there is no loss to compensate — the original legal situation is restored by reversing the erroneous ruling. David gives the money back to Michael; Michael never suffers a final loss.

One might ask who determines that the error occurred. The Gemara does not specify a formal appeals mechanism. It may be that the judge himself recognizes the mistake, or that the broader rabbinic community identifies it — the Gemara leaves this question open. What is clear is that the reversal is the operative remedy: *chozer*.

### Shikul Hadaat Error

The Gemara then considers the alternative: *ela de-ta'ah be-shikul ha-da'at* — an error in judgment, a discretionary call. Rav Papa explains the case: it involves a dispute between two *tannaim* or two *amoraim* of equal weight, where neither position has been formally designated as authoritative — *ve-lo ismar hilcheta lo ke-mar ve-lo ke-mar*. In such a situation, ruling in accordance with either opinion seems entirely legitimate as a matter of personal judgment. So what makes the ruling an error?

The answer: *ve-sugya de-alma aliba de-chad mineihu* — the general practice among judges (*hiluch dina*, as Rashi explains) has settled, without formal canonization, in favor of one of the opinions. The judge went and ruled according to the other one — *ve-zal ve-avad ke-idach*. That is what the Gemara means by a *shikul ha-da'at* error.

### Error Paradox

This answer, however, is genuinely puzzling. If there has been no formal ruling and both positions remain technically live, on what grounds is the judge's ruling an error? One possible reading is that the judge was simply unaware of the prevailing practice among his contemporaries — had he known, he might well have followed the consensus. But if he was aware and consciously chose the minority position, it is not at all obvious why that constitutes a punishable mistake, since he is following a legitimate opinion in a non-settled dispute.

The Gemara's conclusion is that this type of error is *not chozer* — the ruling stands, because it cannot be characterized as plainly wrong. Yet the judge is *chayav le-shalem* — liable to compensate — because the ruling is sufficiently deviant from accepted practice to constitute culpable error. Exactly where the threshold lies between legitimate minority ruling and actionable mistake remains, even after all analysis, genuinely difficult to pin down.

### Canonization

This Gemara and the *rishonic* discussion surrounding it carry implications that extend far beyond the immediate case. The distinction between *ta'ah bi-dvar mishna* and *ta'ah be-shikul ha-da'at* becomes the framework within which later authorities discuss the binding force of different levels of legal tradition. A fundamental question debated among the *rishonim* is whether ruling against a consensus of the *geonim* constitutes *ta'ah bi-dvar mishna* — and therefore triggers automatic reversal — or merely a *shikul ha-da'at* deviation. This is ultimately a question about the canonical authority of the *geonic* tradition. Similarly: can a *rishon* such as the Rambam or Ramban rule against geonic consensus without being characterized as having erred in black-letter law? Can one rule according to the Yerushalmi against the Bavli, or against the *Shulchan Aruch*? Each of these questions is, at its root, a question about what counts as *dvar mishna* — what has been sufficiently canonized that departing from it is not an exercise of judgment but an error. The Gemara does not answer these questions, but by constructing this category, it supplies the vocabulary and conceptual structure within which *poskim* across the centuries have conducted precisely this debate.

## Peshara vs Din

### Parallel Requirements

The Gemara now proposes to link the Tannaitic debate about *peshara* to the underlying question of court composition for *din*. The suggestion is that the dispute about how many judges are required for *peshara* may reflect a parallel disagreement about how many are required for *din* itself — the assumption being that *peshara* and *din* are governed by the same compositional rules.

### Three Peshara Views

The Gemara presents three positions. Rabbi Meir holds that *bitzoa* — compromise, binding arbitration — requires three judges. The *Chachamim* hold that *peshara* requires only one. And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, as noted earlier, holds that *peshara* requires two. If one assumes that the court composition requirements for *peshara* mirror those for *din*, the positions would map onto one another: Rabbi Meir's three for *peshara* reflects three for *din*; the *Chachamim*'s one for *peshara* reflects one for *din*.

The Gemara rejects this inference, however. One cannot conclude from Rabbi Meir's position that he requires only two for *din* simply because the *Chachamim* who require one for *peshara* implicitly require one for *din*. Rather, the Gemara says, *de-chulei alma din bi-shelosha* — everyone agrees that formal *din* requires three. The debate among the *tannaim* is not about the compositional requirements of *din* at all, but about whether *peshara* is analogous to *din* in this respect.

### Two as Witnesses

The position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — that *peshara* requires two — receives an important clarification. Rabbi Acha the son of Rav Ika (or, in another version, Rav Yeimar bar Shelamya) explains: the one who says two for *peshara* in fact concedes that one would suffice. The reason two is recommended is not structural necessity but practical wisdom: *kedei le-hava lei sahidei* — so that witnesses will be available. Without a second person present, each litigant may walk away from the proceeding claiming that the arbitrator ruled differently, and there will be no one to confirm what the actual decision was. Having a second participant allows the two of them to serve as witnesses to the ruling's content. Two, in this view, is not a compositional requirement but a procedural safeguard.

### Peshara Summary

The Gemara thus arrives at a settled picture. When it comes to *din*, three is the universally acknowledged standard; the debates about two and one apply within that framework, with the *yachid mumche* representing a special rabbinic dispensation. When it comes to *peshara*, the operative positions reduce to two: three, which assimilates *peshara* to *din* and demands a full *beit din*, reflecting a view that *peshara* involves genuine legal evaluation of the merits; and one, which treats *peshara* as a distinct process that does not require a formal court. Two is not a genuine third option but rather a procedural recommendation — to have a witness — layered over the substantive position that one suffices. The tension between these views reflects a deeper question about the nature of *peshara* itself: whether it is a form of adjudication that happens to be more flexible, or whether it is something categorically different from *din*, demanding different rules altogether.
