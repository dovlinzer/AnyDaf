# Shevuot 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Shades & Combination

### Mishna vs. Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַאן תַּנָּא מַרְאוֹת נְגָעִים? דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. דְּאִי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר: זוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִזּוֹ וְזוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִזּוֹ, אִם כֵּן טִיהַרְתָּ סִיד הֵיכָל מִלְּצָרְפַּחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina said: Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** this mishna, which delineates the different **shades of leprous marks** into two groups? It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva. As if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva** one would be presented with a difficulty: **Since** with regard to which shades can combine together, Rabbi Akiva **says:** The different shades should be ordered **this** one **above this** one, **and that** one **above that** one, i.e., according to their degrees of brightness, from the most bright to the darkest shade, as follows: Snow white, i.e., *baheret*; wool white, i.e., *se'et*; lime; and egg membrane; and only two adjacent shades can combine together. But **if so, you have rendered pure** a mark that is white like the **lime** plaster **of the Sanctuary** walls and is smaller than a *geris*, as **it cannot be combined** with any other shade of white.

The opening discussion on 6a takes up a problem that has been building across the preceding folio: the *mishna* in *Masechet Negaim*, which lists the four shades of white that constitute *tzaraat*, appears to be at odds with the position of Rabbi Akiva. The heart of the tension lies in a statement attributed to Rav Chanina (5b): *man tanna mar'ot nega'im dela k'Rabbi Akiva* — "who is the *tanna* who taught the shades of the *negaim*? It is not Rabbi Akiva." The reason, as Rav Chanina continues, is that Rabbi Akiva holds *zu l'mala mizu v'zu l'mala mizu* — each shade is ranked above the next in a continuous ascending scale — and if that were the operating principle, one would end up rendering the lime-plaster color of the *heichal* walls permanently pure, a result that cannot be accepted.

To understand what is at stake, the four shades must be kept clearly in view. *Baheret* is the color of snow — the brightest white. *Se'et* is the color of wool. *Sid*, the lime-plaster color, is a *toldah*, a derivative of *baheret*. *Krum beitzah*, the color of an egg membrane, is the dimmest of the four. According to the *Tanna Kamma*, *sid* cannot combine with *se'et* because they belong to different species — *sid* is a derivative of *baheret*, not of *se'et*. Nor can *sid* combine directly with *baheret*, its parent category, because they are not adjacent in the ranked list. The Gemara therefore concludes: *ela mechavarta d'matnitin dela k'Rabbi Akiva* — it appears quite clear, as the Gemara puts it with a term (*mechavarta*, from the root for "white") that carries a certain resonance in the context of a discussion about shades of white, that the *mishna* does not accord with Rabbi Akiva.

### R. Yehoshua's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, שָׁאַל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מֵרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ מַרְאוֹת נְגָעִים שְׁנַיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבָּעָה? אָמַר לוֹ: וְאִם לָאו, מָה יֹאמְרוּ? יֹאמְרוּ מִקְּרוּם בֵּיצָה וּלְמַעְלָה טָמֵא!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: **Rabbi Yosei said** that **Yehoshua, son of Rabbi Akiva, asked of Rabbi Akiva: For what** reason **did** the Sages **say** that the different **shades of** leprous **marks are two** types **that are four,** and proceed to specify their names? Rabbi Akiva **said to him: But if not** that, **what** else **could they say?** Rabbi Yehoshua answered him: **Let them say** that any mark of a degree of brightness **from** that of **an egg membrane and above is ritually impure.**

The Gemara then asks: where exactly do we derive Rabbi Akiva's distinctive position? The answer comes from a *baraita* introduced by *meha d'tanya* — "from what was taught." Rabbi Yosi relates that Yehoshua the son of Rabbi Akiva — who is generally known as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha — posed a question to his father: *mipnei mah amru mar'ot nega'im shnayim shehen arba'ah* — why did the Sages say that the shades of *negaim* are "two which are really four"? Why enumerate all four categories explicitly? The *mishna* could simply have said *mikrum beitzah ulmala tamei* — anything whiter than an egg membrane is *tamei* — and the entire range of qualifying shades would be captured in a single formulation. The style of Tannaitic composition tends toward brevity; the principle *derech hatanna lkatzer* means that *tanna'im* typically compress their rulings and leave the reader to infer. So why the full enumeration?

### R. Akiva's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: לוֹמַר שֶׁמִּצְטָרְפִים זֶה עִם זֶה. אָמַר לוֹ, וְיֹאמְרוּ: "מִקְּרוּם בֵּיצָה וּלְמַעְלָה טָמֵא, וּמִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה"! אָמַר לוֹ: לוֹמַר לָךְ, כׇּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּקִי בָּהֶן וּבִשְׁמוֹתֵיהֶן, אֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה אֶת הַנְּגָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **said to him:** They specified the four different shades in order **to say that they combine with each other** in that order, i.e., each one with its adjacent shade. Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him:** If so, **let them** simply **say:** Any mark of a degree of brightness **from** that of an **egg membrane and above is ritually impure, and they combine with each other.** Rabbi Akiva **said to him:** They specified their names in order **to tell you: Any priest who is not an expert in** distinguishing between **them and** in identifying **their names is not** authorized to **inspect the** leprous **marks** and make a decision regarding them.

Rabbi Akiva's response is twofold. First, *lomar shemitztarfin ze im ze* — to teach that the different shades can combine with one another, such that a *nega* spanning adjacent color categories can still reach the requisite minimum size of a *gris*. *Krum beitzah* can combine with *sid*; *sid* can combine with *krum beitzah* or *se'et*; *se'et* can combine with *sid* or *baheret*. Each shade combines with its neighbors in the ordered sequence — the principle of *zu l'mala mizu* expressed in practice.

But Rabbi Akiva adds a second point when pressed further: even if one were to state both the threshold (*mikrum beitzah ulmala tamei*) and the combination rule (*umitztarfin ze im ze*) in a single formulation, there would still be a reason to enumerate the four shades individually.

### Names & Recognition

That reason is the following *halacha*: *kol kohen she-eino baki bahen uvishmoteihen eino ro'eh et hanegaim* — a *kohen* who is not expert both in the shades themselves and in their names is not qualified to inspect *negaim*. This is a striking ruling. One might have assumed that visual competence alone suffices — if the *kohen* can correctly identify whether a given spot is *tamei* or *tahor*, why should it matter whether he can name the category? Yet Rabbi Akiva insists that the taxonomical and linguistic dimension is essential. The *kohen* must not only perceive the color but be able to classify it and name it correctly.

This emphasis on naming recurs across many areas of *halacha*. In the laws of *Shabbat*, knowing the name of a *melachah* is part of how the violation is constituted. At the end of *Makkot*, a *hatraah* requires specifying to the violator the name of the *aveirah* they are about to commit. Language plays a constitutive, not merely descriptive, role in halakhic categories.

## Kohen's Expertise

### Who Becomes Tamei

A related *halacha* from Rambam's *Hilchot Tumat Tzaraat* (chapter 9) illuminates the structure of the *tzaraat* inspection process. The first *halacha* states: *hakol mitam'in benegaim* — everyone can become *tamei* from *nega tzaraat*, including even an infant from the day of birth and slaves. However, non-Jews and even a *ger toshav* who has accepted the *sheva mitzvot* are excluded. The Rambam then adds: *vechol hanegaim adam ro'eh otan chutz minigei atzmo* — one can inspect all *negaim* except one's own. This is partly a practical constraint — one cannot see one's entire body — but also a formal structural requirement: the inspection involves a *metamei*, the inspector, standing in opposition to the *mitamei*, the one being inspected. The two roles cannot be played by the same person. There is a dialogical, relational dimension to the process of becoming *tamei* from *tzaraat* that is built into the halakhic framework itself.

### Kohen as Mouthpiece

The second relevant *halacha* from the Rambam addresses a case where the *kohen* lacks the required expertise. If a non-*kohen* — a *Yisrael* or a *Levi* — possesses the halakhic knowledge but lacks the priestly status, while the available *kohen* lacks the knowledge, the two can operate in combination: the expert advises and the *kohen* serves as the formal mouthpiece, making the declaration of *tamei*, *tahor*, *musgar*, or *muchlat*. The formal validity of the declaration derives entirely from the *kohen*'s statement, even though the substantive expertise comes from elsewhere. This produces an interesting parallel to the Talmudic passage at the end of *Sanhedrin* involving the lame man and the blind man — each contributes what the other lacks — and there the image is deployed to explain why God judges body and soul together.

### Minor/Shoteh Kohen

The Rambam extends this principle further in a direction that may seem surprising: *afilu haya hakohen katan* — even if the *kohen* is a minor — *o shoteh* — or even someone who is mentally incapacitated, a *shoteh* in the full halakhic sense whose capacity to interact functionally with the world is severely compromised — *hachacham omer lo v'hu machlit o poter o soger* — a Torah scholar can instruct this person and the *kohen* can make the legally effective declaration. Despite the general rule that a minor and a *shoteh* lack the standing to create halakhic consequences, the declaration is valid.

One might ask whether this contradicts Rabbi Akiva's requirement that the *kohen* be expert in the shades and their names. The resolution appears to be that there are two distinct tracks. When the *kohen* relies on his own abilities, he must himself be an expert. When he relies on someone else who is the expert, his role becomes purely formal — the required mouthpiece through whom the declaration must pass — and the expertise requirement is satisfied vicariously. The formal and substantive dimensions of the *kohen*'s function are separable.

## R. Yehoshua Korcha

### Korcha = R. Akiva

The *baraita* cited above identifies the questioner as *Yehoshua beno shel Rabbi Akiva* — Yehoshua the son of Rabbi Akiva. This same figure appears throughout *Shas* under the name Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha. The *Ge'onim* and Rashi in numerous places across the Talmud identify these as the same person, explaining that Rabbi Akiva was known by the nickname *Korcha* — the bald one — as attested by the Gemara in *Bechorot*. Rabbi Akiva began studying Torah at the relatively advanced age of forty, lived to an unusually old age, and may well have been bald by the time his reputation was fully established; once the nickname became entrenched, his son would naturally be known as *Yehoshua ben Korcha*.

### Tosafot Objection

Tosafot, however, object to this identification on grounds of propriety. To call one of the greatest *tanna'im* of his generation by an embarrassing nickname — effectively calling him "baldy" — seems deeply problematic. The Gemara itself treats conferring a degrading *kinuy* on another person as an extremely serious matter. Tosafot therefore conclude that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha must be a different person whose father was literally named *Korcha*, citing a *Gemara* in *Megillah* in support.

Against Tosafot, one might observe that the tradition identifying them as one and the same is too pervasive to dismiss easily. It is possible that Rabbi Akiva's extraordinary humility — rooted in his origins as an illiterate shepherd who began learning Torah at forty — made him genuinely indifferent to such a nickname. Rabbi Akiva is always paired in rabbinic imagination with Moshe Rabbeinu as the great Torah teacher who overcame impossible odds. Perhaps that humility extended to accepting, or at least not objecting to, a playful epithet. And once the nickname became common currency, it would naturally attach to his son's name as well.

As for who this otherwise-unknown person named *Korcha* might be if not Rabbi Akiva — *Korcha* is itself a strange name, and the identification with Rabbi Akiva remains the most economical explanation.

### Age Problems

A further difficulty involves chronology. Tosafot note that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha appears in conversation with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who blessed him that he should live to at least half of Rabbi Akiva's years — that is, to sixty. If Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha was younger than sixty at the time of that blessing, then working backwards, the timeline grows strained: Rabbi Akiva would have had to father a child when he was well past one hundred years old, which is difficult to accept straightforwardly. The math does not work cleanly, and Tosafot leverage this as an argument against the identification. Nonetheless, the genealogical evidence embedded in the sources pointing toward this identification remains strong, and it may be that some of the *gemarot* involved carry a degree of imprecision that makes exact chronological reconstruction unreliable.

## Tanna Kamma Return

### Dilmah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא "שְׂאֵת וְתוֹלַדְתָּהּ״, "בַּהֶרֶת וְתוֹלַדְתָּהּ״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **But perhaps** Rabbi Akiva does hold that the only combinations possible are ***a se'et* and its secondary** mark, i.e., an egg membrane–colored mark; and **a *baheret* and its secondary** mark, i.e., a lime-colored mark. And perhaps Rabbi Yehoshua in fact proposed that the Sages should use a dual formulation, but the *baraita* cites only the first half of his suggestion.

Returning to the substantive halakhic question: could the *mishna* perhaps be read according to the *Tanna Kamma*'s model — that is, *se'et* and its *toldah* combine, and *baheret* and its *toldah* combine, but there is no cross-combination? The Gemara raises this reading (*vedilma se'et vetoldatah baheret vetoldatah*) and asks whether it can be sustained.

### Milk & Blood Mashal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִדְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מָשָׁל דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְאַרְבָּעָה כּוֹסוֹת שֶׁל חָלָב, אֶחָד נָפְלוּ לְתוֹכוֹ שְׁתֵּי טִיפִּין שֶׁל דָּם, וְאֶחָד נָפְלוּ לְתוֹכוֹ אַרְבַּע טִיפִּין שֶׁל דָּם, וְאֶחָד נָפְלוּ לְתוֹכוֹ שְׁמוֹנֶה, וְאֶחָד נָפְלוּ לְתוֹכוֹ שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה טִיפִּין – וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה טִיפִּין – שֶׁכּוּלָּן מַרְאוֹת לוֹבֶן הֵן, אֶלָּא שֶׁזֶּה לְמַעְלָה מִזֶּה וְזֶה לְמַעְלָה מִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Akiva's opinion can be inferred **from that** which **Rabbi Ḥanina** says, **as Rabbi Ḥanina says:** The following is **an analogy** to illustrate the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva: To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to four cups of milk,** and **two drops of blood fell into one** of them, **and four drops of blood fell into** another **one** of them, **and eight** drops of blood **fell into** another **one, and twelve drops** of blood **fell into** the last **one. And some say** that **sixteen drops** fell into the last cup. This is a suitable analogy, **as** the milk in **all of** the cups still **has** a similar **shade of white, but** the cups can be ordered according to their degrees of brightness, **as this** one is **above this** one, **and that** one is **above that** one. Rabbi Ḥanina's analogy would appear to portray Rabbi Akiva's opinion in the same way it was cited earlier.

The problem is that this reading runs into Rabbi Chanina's *mashal* explaining Rabbi Akiva's position. Rabbi Chanina describes Rabbi Akiva's view through a *mashal* of four cups of milk: into one go two drops of blood, into a second go four drops, into a third go eight, and into a fourth go twelve. (A variant reading has sixteen in place of twelve, which would make each step a clean doubling — two, four, eight, sixteen — and the Gemara notes this *ve'amrei lah* reading, suggesting the variant may have been motivated by a preference for geometric doubling.) All four cups remain white — *shekulan mar'ot lavan hen* — but each is a slightly more reddish white than the previous, a continuous gradation: *ela shezeh lemalah mizeh vezeh lemalah mizeh*. This is the visual picture underlying Rabbi Akiva's conception: the four shades form a single unbroken spectrum, not two separate paired families.

### Patuach & Chaluk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – בְּפָתוּךְ; בְּחָלוּק מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this. The Torah states that a "reddish-white affliction" (Leviticus 13:42), not just a flawless white one, renders a person ritually impure. Therefore, the Gemara suggests: **Say that you heard Rabbi Akiva** express this opinion **with regard to** combining different shades of a mark that is **mixed [*befatukh*]** with red, which is the case most similar to the analogy offered by Rabbi Ḥanina, but **with regard to** different shades of **flawless** white, **have you heard** Rabbi Akiva express this opinion?

The question then becomes whether Rabbi Akiva's principle of combination (*mitztarfin ze im ze*) applies when the two shades in question are *patuach* — adjacent in the spectrum — or also when they are *chaluk* — separated, non-adjacent. Rashi explains *patuach* as *me'urav*, mixed or together. The Gemara works through the question of whether hearing that non-adjacent shades combine also tells us that adjacent shades combine, and vice versa. The difficulty is that if the shades are of different families — if *se'et* and *baheret* are genuinely distinct species — it is not obvious that adjacency in the spectrum is sufficient to permit combination. The Gemara ultimately uses this analysis as part of establishing the full scope of Rabbi Akiva's position.

## R. Akiva's Colors

### Beheret = Snow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּבַהֶרֶת עַזָּה הִיא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אָמַר קְרָא: "אִם בַּהֶרֶת לְבָנָה הִיא" – הִיא לְבָנָה, וְאֵין אַחֶרֶת לְבָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara considers the source from which the different shades of marks are derived: **And from where do we** derive **that *baheret* is an intense** white color? **Abaye said: The verse states:** "And **if it is a white *baheret*"** (Leviticus 13:4), which indicates that **it** alone **is** a bright **white and there is no other** as **white** as it.

The next *baraita* offers Rabbi Akiva's own description of the color scale. *Shel baheret az kesheleg* — the *baheret* color is as vivid and bright as snow. *Veshel sid deheh heimena* — and the *sid* (lime-plaster) color is darker than, or dimmer than, *baheret*.

### Se'et Omission

A question immediately arises: Rabbi Akiva mentions *baheret* (snow) and *sid* (lime-plaster), but makes no mention of *se'et* (wool color). The *baraita* should have said that the wool color is darker than snow, if that is Rabbi Akiva's view. The Gemara acknowledges this: *in hachi nami* — yes, that is correct, the *baraita* should have mentioned it. The omission is notable partly because it bears on the earlier debate about whether Rabbi Akiva would place *sid* in direct combination with *baheret* or would interpose *se'et* between them.

### Rabbi Nathan Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר, לֹא שֶׁאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא "שֶׁל סִיד דֵּיהָה הֵימֶנָּה", אֶלָּא "שֶׁל צֶמֶר דֵּיהָה הֵימֶנָּה".
>
> **Translation:** **They said** in response to this: **Yes, it is indeed so** that Rabbi Akiva continues by referring to the reddish variations of a wool-white mark, **and it is taught** likewise in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says,** referring to that mishna: It is **not** correct **that Rabbi Akiva said:** The reddish variation **of lime is darker than it; rather,** he said that the reddish variation **of a** wool-white mark **is darker than it.**

Rabbi Nathan preserves an alternative version of the *baraita*: rather than saying that *sid* is darker than snow, he says it is *se'et* — the wool color — that is darker than snow. This is a textual variant that directly affects how Rabbi Akiva's ordering is understood.

### Abaye's Drasha

The Gemara then asks how we know that *baheret* is the brightest of all the colors. Abaye cites a *pasuk*: *ve'im baheret levanah hi* — "and if the *baheret* is white." From this Abaye derives: *hi levanah ve'ein acheret levanah* — it, the *baheret*, is white, and none of the others are white in the same sense. They belong to the white family, but only *baheret* is truly white. It should be noted that Abaye is not deploying a formal *middah* of *midrash*; he is reading the *pasuk* straightforwardly as an *asmachta* — a Scriptural support for a conclusion that was already known from other grounds.

## Four Negaim

### Beheret Amukah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "בַּהֶרֶת" – עֲמוּקָּה, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: "וּמַרְאֶהָ עָמֹק מִן הָעוֹר"; כְּמַרְאֵה חַמָּה הָעֲמוּקָּה מִן הַצֵּל. "שְׂאֵת" – אֵין שְׂאֵת אֶלָּא גָּבוֹהַּ, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: "עַל כׇּל הֶהָרִים הָרָמִים וְעַל כׇּל הַגְּבָעוֹת הַנִּשָּׂאוֹת". "סַפַּחַת" – אֵין סַפַּחַת אֶלָּא טְפֵילָה, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: "וְאָמַר סְפָחֵנִי נָא".
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The bright shade of **a *baheret*** makes it appear **deeper** than the surrounding skin, **and so** the verse **states: "And its appearance is deeper than the skin"** (Leviticus 13:25). This is **like the appearance of** an area illuminated by the **sun, which** appears **deeper than** the area in **the shade.** The darker shade of **a *se'et*** makes it appear as though it is raised above the surrounding skin; this is indicated by the fact that the word ***se'et*** means **nothing other than raised, and so** the verse **states: "Upon all the high mountains and upon all the raised up [*hanissaot*] hills"** (Isaiah 2:14). The words *hanissaot* and *se'et* share the same Hebrew root and both refer to something raised up. In the verse: "For a *se'et* and for a *sappaḥat*" (Leviticus 14:56), the word ***sappaḥat*** means **nothing other than secondary, and so** the verse **states: "And he will say: Append me [*sefaḥeni*] please** to one of the priestly classes to eat a piece of bread" (I Samuel 2:36). This teaches that there is a leprous mark that is secondary and appended to a *se'et*. This is a mark that is the color of an egg membrane.

The Gemara continues with a *baraita* of the Rabbanan describing each of the four *negaim* in turn. *Baheret amukah* — the *baheret* appears deep, or recessed. The *pasuk* says *amok me'or besaro* — it is beneath the level of the skin — and the Gemara connects this to a visual phenomenon: *ke'mareh chamah amukah min hatzel* — just as a brightly illuminated area outdoors appears deeper, farther away, than a shaded area. The *baheret*'s extreme whiteness creates this optical impression of recession. This reflects a genuine feature of visual perception: bright, high-contrast areas are interpreted by the brain and eye as more distant. The observation is reminiscent of the viral internet debate over the blue-and-black (or white-and-gold) dress, where different viewers perceived the same object entirely differently depending on how they processed background lighting. The *baheret*'s brightness makes the skin around it appear to recede, producing the halakhic term *amukah*.

### Se'et Elevated

*Se'et*, by contrast, conveys elevation: *ein se'et ela gevohah* — *se'et* means nothing other than height. The root *nasa* — to carry, to lift — gives *se'et* its meaning of something raised or elevated. The *masuk* in Yeshayahu, *al kol he'harim ha'ramim ve'al kol hage'va'ot hanissa'ot* — upon all the high mountains and all the elevated hills — uses the same root. Where *baheret*'s extreme brightness makes it look deep, *se'et*'s dimmer tone makes it appear elevated or raised above the skin level.

### Sapachat Derivative

*Sapachat* means something secondary or derivative: *ein sapachat ela tifeilah*. The same root appears in modern Hebrew in the word *nispach* — an appendix to a book. The *sapachat* colors are those that are subordinate or derivative — the secondary shades attached to the primary ones.

### Secondary Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן טְפֵילָה לַשְּׂאֵת; טְפֵילָה לַבַּהֶרֶת מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: נֶאֶמְרָה "לְבָנָה" בַּשְּׂאֵת, וְנֶאֶמְרָה "לְבָנָה" בַּבַּהֶרֶת; מָה לְבָנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בַּשְּׂאֵת – יֵשׁ לָהּ טְפֵילָה, אַף לְבָנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בַּבַּהֶרֶת – יֵשׁ לָהּ טְפֵילָה. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: הֵטִיל הַכָּתוּב לַסַּפַּחַת בֵּין שְׂאֵת לַבַּהֶרֶת, לוֹמַר לָךְ: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁטְּפֵילָה לַשְּׂאֵת, כָּךְ טְפֵילָה לַבַּהֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **We found** a source for a mark that is **secondary to a *se'et*; from where do we** derive that there is also a mark that is **secondary to a *baheret*? Rabbi Zeira said: "White"** (Leviticus 13:10) **is stated with regard to a *se'et* and "white"** (Leviticus 13:4) **is stated with regard to a *baheret*.** This teaches that **just as** the shade of **white stated with regard to a *se'et* has a secondary** mark, i.e., the mark that is the color of an egg membrane, **so too,** the shade of **white stated with regard to a *baheret* has a secondary** mark, i.e., the lime-colored mark. A different source for this **was taught in a *baraita*: The verse** (see Leviticus 13:2) **placed** the word ***sappaḥat* between *se'et* and *baheret*, to say to you: Just as** there is a shade that is **secondary to a *se'et*, so too,** there is a shade that is **secondary to a *baheret*.**

How do we know that there is a *sapachat* — a secondary shade — for *baheret* specifically, given that the derivation of a *sapachat* for *se'et* is more explicit in the text? Rabbi Zeira applies a *gezeirah shavah*-style comparison: the Torah uses the word *levanah* (white) in connection with both *se'et* and *baheret*. Just as the *se'et* has a secondary color (the egg membrane color), so too the *baheret* must have a secondary color (the lime-plaster color). The *matnitin* formulates this more directly: the Torah mentions the word *sapachat* in proximity to both *se'et* and *baheret*, teaching that there is a secondary color for each of the two primary *negaim*.

### White Wool Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂאֵת כְּצֶמֶר לָבָן. מַאי "צֶמֶר לָבָן"? אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: צֶמֶר נָקִי בֶּן יוֹמוֹ, שֶׁמְכַבְּנִין בּוֹ לְמֵילָת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna in *Nega'im* cited above states: **A *se'et*** is **like white wool.** The Gemara asks: **What** is meant by **white wool? Rav Beivai says** that **Rav Asi says:** It is the color of **clean wool** from a lamb **that** is **wrapped up [*mekhabnin*]** in a cover when it is **one day old** in order to protect it from being sullied, so that the wool will be suitable **for** producing **a fine wool** garment.

What precisely is the white wool that serves as the standard for *se'et*? Rav Bibi citing Rav Assi says: *tzemer naki ben yomo* — clean wool from a sheep on the day of its birth. A newborn lamb is licked clean by its mother and has not yet been exposed to the environment, so its wool is at its purest white. The Rambam notes that one who wishes to preserve that pristine quality for use in *milat* (the soft downy inner wool) would wrap the newborn lamb in a leather covering to protect the fleece from soiling. The comparison is thus not to aged or dirty wool, but to the untouched wool of a one-day-old lamb — the whitest natural wool that exists.

## Combination Mashalim

### Kings & Officers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מָשָׁל דְּרַבָּנַן – לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לִתְרֵי מַלְכֵי וְלִתְרֵי אִיפַּרְכֵי; מַלְכּוֹ שֶׁל זֶה לְמַעְלָה מִמַּלְכּוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, וְאִיפַּרְכוֹ שֶׁל זֶה לְמַעְלָה מֵאִיפַּרְכוֹ שֶׁל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § In continuation of his statement cited above **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** The following is **an analogy** to illustrate the opinion **of the Rabbis,** i.e., the opinion expressed in the mishna in *Nega'im* (1:1) that both a *baheret* and a *se'et* have marks that are secondary to them. **To what is this matter comparable? To two kings and to two governors [*iparkhei*]** where, with regard to their supremacy, **the king of this** governor **is above the king of that** governor, **and the governor of this** king **is above the governor of that** king. The two kings are analogous to a *baheret* and a *se'et* and their two governors are, respectively, a lime-colored mark and an egg membrane–colored mark. Accordingly, the order of supremacy is: *Baheret*, *se'et*, lime-colored mark, egg membrane–colored mark.

A series of *mashalim* is now offered by different Amoraim to make vivid the contrasting combination patterns of the four shades. Rabbi Chanina provides the first, explaining the *Rabbanan*'s position. The four shades are compared to two kings and two viziers (*litre malkhei ulitre ifrchei*). One king is more powerful than the other, and each king has his own officer. The key point is that each king ranks above his own vizier, not above the other king's vizier. This maps onto the *Tanna Kamma*'s model: *baheret* (king one) combines only with *sid* (its vizier), and *se'et* (king two) combines only with *krum beitzah* (its vizier). Cross-combination — *baheret* with *krum beitzah* or *se'et* with *sid* — is not permitted, just as one king does not outrank the other's officer. The *mashal* is acknowledged to be imperfect in some of its details, but it provides a useful mnemonic structure.

### Four Ranks Mashal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן מַלְכָּא וְאַלְקַפְטָא, רוּפִילָא וְרֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא. הַאי זֶה לְמַעְלָה מִזֶּה הוּא! אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן מַלְכָּא וְרוּפִילָא, וְאַלְקַפְטָא וְרֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a different analogy. **Rav Adda bar Abba said: For example: A king, and a chief officer [*alkafta*], the vizier [*rofila*], and the Exilarch;** each person in the list is more powerful than the subsequent one. The Gemara asks: But **this** analogy suggests that the shades are ordered, **this** one **above that** one, which is Rabbi Akiva's opinion. **Rather,** a suitable analogy is, **for example: A king and the vizier; and a chief officer and the Exilarch.** This list splits the four into two groups, each group containing one person who is subordinate to the other.

Rav Adda bar Abba offers a different *mashal* with four distinct hierarchical ranks: *malka* (king), *alkafta* (a high official similar to a king but subordinate), *rufila* (the grand vizier of the king), and *resh galuta* (the exilarch, still an important figure but below the grand vizier). The ranking, according to Rashi, is that the king is above the *alkafta*, and the *rufila* is the king's vizier, while the *resh galuta* is subordinate to the *alkafta*. The Gemara works through which ranks are paired: the king goes with the *rufila*, and the *alkafta* goes with the *resh galuta* — again a pattern of one going with three and two going with four. This captures the same underlying structure as the *Rabbanan*'s position.

### Persia & Rome

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שַׁבּוּר מַלְכָּא וְקֵיסָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: הֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ עֲדִיף? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּחוּרְשַׁיָּא קָא אָכֵיל לֵיהּ. פּוֹק חֲזִי טִיבְעָא דְּמַאן סַגִּי בְּעָלְמָא – דִּכְתִיב: "וְתֵאכֻל כׇּל אַרְעָא וּתְדוּשִׁנַּהּ וְתַדְּקִנַּהּ". אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ רוֹמִי חַיֶּיבֶת, שֶׁטִּיבְעָהּ יָצָא בְּכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a different analogy. **Rava said:** The previous suggestion is not precise because all these positions, apart from the king, are subordinate to the king. A more precise analogy would be, **for example: King Shapur,** the king of Persia, with his subordinate; **and** the Roman **emperor** with his subordinate. **Rav Pappa said to Rava: Which of them is greater,** King Shapur or the Roman emperor? Rava **said to him: Does he eat in the forest,** i.e., do you live disconnected and unaware of events in the world at large? **Go out** and **see whose coin circulates throughout the world,** which is an indicator of a government's influence, **as it is written** with regard to the fourth empire described in Daniel's dream of the future powers of the world: **"It shall devour the whole earth and tread upon it and break it into pieces"** (Daniel 7:23), and **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is the guilty** empire of **Rome whose coin circulates throughout the entire world.**

Rava proposes a more dramatic comparison: the four shades are like *Shvor Malka* (the Persian emperor) and *Keysar* (the Roman emperor). This brings the political realities of the Babylonian Amoraic world into the classroom. Rava lived in Babylonia under Persian rule; he names the Persian emperor first.

### Power Comparison

Rav Pappa presses Rava: *hai mineihu adif* — which of the two is actually more powerful? By putting Persia first, Rava implied Persian supremacy. His response is pointed: *puk chazi tivuta de'man sagi be'alma* — go out and see whose influence has spread throughout the world. The implicit answer is Persia, at least in Rava's estimation and at that historical moment.

Rava then cites a *pasuk* from Daniel — *ve'teichal kol ar'a ve'tedushenah ve'tedikenah* — and the Gemara notes that Rabbi Yochanan, who lived in the Land of Israel under Roman rule, interpreted this verse as referring to Rome. The contrast is illuminating: Rava in Persia points to Persian ascendancy; Rabbi Yochanan in Roman Palestine reads the apocalyptic verse of Daniel as applying to Rome. The four kingdoms described in Daniel — Babylonia, Persia, Greece, and Rome — map onto this four-part structure, and the tension between Persia and Rome that runs through the Talmudic period eventually shifts, in the medieval period, into the tension between the Muslim and Christian worlds.

Rabbi Yochanan adds: *Romi chayavet she'tiv'ah yatza be'chol ha'olam kulo* — Rome is liable because its *tiva'ah*, its stamp, its characteristic mark, has spread throughout the entire world.

### Garment Mashal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן גְּלִימָא דַּעֲמַר וְשַׁחְקֵיהּ, סְדִינָא דְּכִיתָּנָא וְשַׁחְקֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a different analogy. **Ravina said: For example,** a new white **woolen garment and a frayed** one; a new **linen sheet and a frayed one.**

Ravina, several Amoraic generations later, offers a final *mashal* grounded in everyday material observation: *glima de'amar ve'shafsei de'sina de'kitna ve'shachakei* — a wool garment and a linen garment, new and worn. When wool and linen are brand new, both are very white. As each ages and is worn, it darkens and discolors. The wool garment in its new state corresponds to one shade; as it wears, it transitions to its paired category. The linen garment does the same in its own pair. The point, as with all the preceding *mashalim*, is that one goes with three and two goes with four — the *Rabbanan*'s model of two independent paired families, not Rabbi Akiva's continuous spectrum.

A parenthetical note: the minimum size for a *nega* to be halakhically significant is a *gris* — the size of a bean, roughly equivalent to nine lentils or a coin of approximately the size of a nickel or dime, depending on the *posek*. The question of combination between adjacent shades is therefore a question about whether two spots of different colors can be combined to reach that minimum size threshold. In *Hilchot Niddah* the *gris* functions as a *de'rabbanan* standard, and the *Shulchan Aruch* advises finding the largest available bean to be more lenient; here it operates as a *de'oraita* requirement, which makes the overlap between the two contexts a subject deserving further exploration.

## Korban Scope

### Baraita Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בְּטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna (2a) states: In cases of defiling the Temple or its sacrificial foods **in which** one **had awareness at the beginning** and awareness at the end, but had a lapse of awareness in between while he actually transgressed, this person is liable to bring a sliding-scale offering. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that the verse** that describes the liability for a sliding-scale offering (see Leviticus 5:2–4) **speaks of nothing other than** the **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods? While the verse mentions that a violation was committed due to a lapse of awareness of one's state of impurity, it does not mention which transgression was violated.

The final *sugya* on this *daf* turns to the scope of the *korban oleh veyored* — the variable offering that scales with the offender's means — in the context of *yedi'ot hatumah*, awareness of one's impure state. A *baraita* is cited: *minayin she'ein hakatuv medaber ela betumat mikdash vekodashav* — from where do we know that when the Torah speaks of bringing a *korban oleh veyored* for *yedi'at hatumah*, it refers specifically to *tumat mikdash vekodashav* — the impurity of the Temple and its sancta — rather than to *tumah* in general or to other categories of prohibited contact?

### Warning & Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִין הוּא – הוֹאִיל וְהִזְהִיר וְעָנַשׁ עַל הַטּוּמְאָה, וְחַיָּיב קׇרְבָּן עַל הַטּוּמְאָה; מָה כְּשֶׁהִזְהִיר וְעָנַשׁ עַל הַטּוּמְאָה – לֹא הִזְהִיר וְעָנַשׁ אֶלָּא עַל טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, אַף כְּשֶׁחִיֵּיב קׇרְבָּן עַל הַטּוּמְאָה – לֹא חִיֵּיב אֶלָּא עַל טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And it is a logical inference: Since** the Torah has explicitly **prohibited and** also **prescribed punishment for the** intentional **defiling** of something sacred **and has rendered one liable** to bring **an offering for the** unwitting **defiling** of something sacred, it follows that **just as when it prohibited and** also **prescribed punishment for the defiling** of something sacred **it prohibited and** also **prescribed punishment only for the** intentional **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods; **so too, when it rendered one liable** to bring **an offering for the defiling** of something sacred **it rendered one liable** to do so **only for the** unwitting **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods.

The *baraita* advances a logical argument: *ho'il vehizhir ve'anash al hatumah vehayav korban al hatumah* — since the Torah warned about *tumah* (*hizhir*) and imposed punishment (*anash*) for deliberate violation (*b'zadon*), and also requires a *korban* for inadvertent violation (*b'shogeg*), the warning, punishment, and *korban* must all refer to the same category of violation. The warning and punishment, as Rashi notes, apply only to *zadon tumah* in the context of *mikdash vekodashav* — entering the *Beit HaMikdash* or eating *korbanot* while *tamei*. There is no general prohibition against becoming *tamei*; the prohibition attaches to the subsequent action that desecrates something sacred. Therefore the *korban* obligation must be coextensive with that same category of *tumat mikdash vekodashav*.

### Terumah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא תְּרוּמָה – שֶׁהִזְהִיר וְעָנַשׁ! לָא אַשְׁכְּחַן עֲוֹן מִיתָה דְּחַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ קׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But say** instead that the obligation to bring a sliding-scale offering is referring to a ritually impure person who partook of **the sacred portion of produce grown in Eretz Yisrael that is designated to be given to a priest [*teruma*], as** the Torah also **has** explicitly **prohibited and prescribed punishment** for this. One who partakes of *teruma* while ritually impure is liable to receive death at the hand of Heaven (see Leviticus 22:9). The Gemara answers: It cannot be referring to *teruma*, because **we do not find a sin** whose punishment for an intentional violation is **death,** with regard to **which** one **is liable** to bring **an offering** for its unwitting violation. Sin-offerings, of which the sliding-scale offering is one type, are brought only for transgressions whose intentional violation is punishable by *karet*.

One might object that *terumah* should also be included: eating *terumah* while *tamei* is a prohibited act for which there is both a warning and a punishment — *mita bidei shamayim*, a divinely imposed death — and for which one might expect a *korban oleh veyored* to apply as well. The Gemara acknowledges that eating *terumah* while *tamei b'shogeg* does incur a *korban*, but that *korban* is a *korban kavua* — a fixed offering, specifically a *korban chatat* requiring an animal sacrifice. The *korban oleh veyored*, by contrast, is a variable offering that applies specifically to the categories listed together in the Torah.

### Terumah Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: "בָּהּ"; "בָּהּ" – לְמַעוֹטֵי תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It cannot be referring to one who partakes of *teruma* while ritually impure, as **the verse** concerning a sliding-scale offering **states:** "Or if he will touch impurity of a man in any manner of his impurity **through which** he can become impure" (Leviticus 5:3). The verse states **"through which" to exclude** an impure person who partakes of ***teruma*** from liability to bring a sliding-scale offering.

The exclusion of *terumah* from the scope of the *korban oleh veyored* is derived textually through the juxtaposition in the Torah of *tumat mikdash vekodashav* with *shmi'at kol* (hearing a voice — the oath of testimony, *shevuat ha'edut*, treated in the fourth *perek* of this *masechta*) and *bitui sfatayim* (utterance of the lips — *shevuat bitui*, treated in the third *perek*). These three categories — *tumat mikdash vekodashav*, *shevuat ha'edut*, and *shevuat bitui* — are linked together in the Torah's formulation, and all three share the *korban oleh veyored* as the consequence for inadvertent violation. *Terumah* is excluded from this grouping: the text is read as *bah bah* — a limiting particle — to signal that *terumah*, despite carrying its own severe consequences, does not fall under the *korban oleh veyored* framework. The distinction in sanctity is relevant: *korbanot* must be eaten either within the *Beit HaMikdash* or within Jerusalem, and the level of purity required is correspondingly higher. The more severe *karet* punishment applies to *tumat mikdash vekodashav* when done deliberately; *mita bidei shamayim* applies to *terumah*. Different levels of sanctity, different punishment structures, and different *korban* obligations reflect a coherent underlying framework, even if the precise derivation may seem formally compressed at first glance.
