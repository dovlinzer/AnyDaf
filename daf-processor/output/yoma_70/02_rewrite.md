# Yoma 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yetzer Hara Avodah Zarah

### Yetzer Hara Removal

The daf opens in the middle of a well-known *aggadata* rooted in the *pesukim* in Ezra, where the text describes the people crying out to God in a great cry at the time of the rebuilding of the Second *Beit HaMikdash*. The *Gemara* explains that their cry was directed at the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah*, which they identified as the root cause of the First Temple's destruction. They prayed for its elimination, and God delivered it to them — they sealed it in a lead container and destroyed it. Having done so, they sought to do the same with the *yetzer hara* of sexual desire, but quickly recognized that this would be catastrophic: it is the very drive that sustains procreation and the continuity of human civilization. So they left it, albeit constrained.

While most discussion of this *aggadata* focuses on the second part — the *yetzer hara* of sexuality — there is something equally striking in the first part, concerning the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah* itself, and it is worth pausing on two dimensions of it.

### Historical Context

The first dimension is historical. *Chazal* tell us that the First Temple fell due to *avodah zarah*, *gilluy arayos*, and *shefichas damim*, whereas the Second Temple fell due to *sinas chinam*. And indeed, when one reads the *Nevi'im*, the refrain is constant: *od ha'am mezabbechim umekatterim babbamos rak habbamos lo saru* — the people continued sacrificing and burning incense at the *bamot*; *avodah zarah* is a pervasive concern throughout the First Temple period. In the Second Temple period, by contrast, it essentially disappears as a communal sin. This reflects a broader historical reality: the ancient world was undergoing a gradual waning of serious pagan belief, laying the groundwork for the monotheistic faiths that would follow. The *aggadata*, then, is at least partly capturing this genuine historical shift, even though the *pesukim* in Nechemiah that frame the story do not explicitly mention *avodah zarah* — they allude more generally to repeated past sinfulness.

### Keruvim & Physical Conne…

The second dimension is more striking: the *Gemara* describes the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah* as emerging like a lion of fire from the *Kodesh Kodashim*. What is the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah* doing in the *Kodesh Kodashim*?

Different interpretations exist, but the most immediate one follows naturally from what the *Gemara* itself discussed not long before: the *Kodesh Kodashim* as the site of a profound and very physical manifestation of God's connection to Israel. The *Kohen Gadol* enters into the inner chamber — and the *Gemara* had already developed the imagery of that entry in terms of intimate encounter — and central to that encounter are the *keruvim*, those statues intertwined like lovers, which God showed to *Bnei Yisrael* at the *regel* as a demonstration of the relationship between God and Israel.

Now, on one hand, the entire thrust of Judaism is the rejection of physical images of the divine, the awareness of how easily such imagery corrupts the idea of God. And yet: the *Mikdash* contains tremendous physicality, and at its very heart, in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, stand the *keruvim* — not God Godself, perhaps, but statues positioned right there in the holiest space, directly adjacent to the *aron*. These figures are not God, but they represent God's chariot or entourage, and they embody the intense longing for a direct, tangible mode of connection with the divine.

This is precisely the double-edged nature of the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah*. Just as the *yetzer hara* of sexuality is not simply evil — it drives procreation and sustains the world, and must be channeled rather than eliminated — so too the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah* is not simply corrupt. It represents the human drive for immediacy and intimacy with God, the desire to connect with something utterly transcendent through means that are familiar and sensory. That is part of what was happening in the *Kodesh Kodashim* — in a constructive way, arguably — but it is also precisely what, when left uncultivated and unchanneled, collapses into paganism and idolatry. When this *yetzer* was destroyed and left the *Kodesh Kodashim*, something profound changed in the Jewish relationship with God. And it is worth noting what else departed from the *Kodesh Kodashim* in the *Bayit Sheni*: the *aron* and the *keruvim* were gone. The Second Temple's *Kodesh Kodashim* contained only the foundation stone. That entire dimension of physical-intimate connection — with all its dangers and all its power — departed together.

### Rav Kook Leibowitz Debate

This tension finds vivid expression in a remarkable juxtaposition between Rav Kook and Yeshayahu Leibowitz. Both are responding to the same *aggadata*, and their disagreement is fundamental.

### Rav Kook's Position

Rav Kook, writing in *Adar HaYakar*, mourns the loss. He writes: *asher me'az shebitel yitzra de'avodah zarah, nistalek koach hachayim hara'anan sheyesod ha'ahava ha'elokit min ha'olam* — from the time the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah* was annulled, the flourishing life-force that is the foundation of divine love departed from the world. He elaborates: *koach ahava elokit kechol koach tiv'i bilti me'ubad shebenefesh ha'adam, tzarich hu lelimud shel pituach vechissun vehadracha* — the power of divine love, like any uncultivated natural force within the human soul, requires cultivation, training, and guidance. Left uncultivated, it runs wild and degenerates into paganism. But the answer is not to eradicate it. If you eradicate it, you lose the passion, the drive, the immediate and emotional connection to God. The religion becomes, in Rav Kook's telling, shriveled, sterile, and morose — a consequence he attributes in part to centuries of exile among the nations.

### Leibowitz Critique

Yeshayahu Leibowitz, in his entry on *avodah zarah* in a lexicon of Jewish ideas, takes the diametrically opposite view — and turns his critique directly on Rav Kook. He writes: *ha'agada mesaperet she'Anshei Knesset HaGedolah hatzlichu lehadbir et yitzra de'avodah zarah beYisrael* — the *aggada* says they succeeded in destroying the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah*. *Ach ein ele ela divrei agada* — but that is just a story. *Me'olam lo pasak be'olama shel haYahadut hama'avak hanu'ash shel ha'emuna baHashem Echad neged hamshicha hativ'it shel ha'adam ubechlal ze gam ha'adam miYisrael la'elilut* — never has the disheartening struggle in Judaism against the natural human pull toward idolatry come to an end, including among Jews, even behind the mask of belief in one God.

Leibowitz argues that the failure to sustain a clean distinction between *kodesh* and *chol*, between the genuinely transcendent and the human world, keeps reasserting itself. He indicts, in turn, Christianity, *Kabbalah*, *Chasidut*, the *Kuzari*, the Maharal — and then, pointedly, Rav Kook himself, whose thought, Leibowitz contends, has turned the Jewish people into a kind of physical manifestation of the divine. The *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah*, Leibowitz insists, is alive and well — and Rav Kook and his followers are its latest expression.

The tension these two thinkers articulate is profound: on one side stands the drive for purity of faith, the rejection of imagery and physicality in the approach to God; on the other stands the human impossibility of connecting with something utterly unlike oneself. We are physical beings, and to reach toward something completely *other* requires some form of bridge. Whether losing the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah* was a gain or a loss, and whether it was truly lost at all, remains a genuine and unresolved debate.

### Apollo-Dionysian Tension

This tension maps onto a structure the Greeks also recognized: the Apollonian-Dionysian divide. The human being carries within a Dionysian impulse — a pull toward non-rational, passionate, sensory connection — which must be bridled by what Apollo represents: rational structure and discipline. Judaism has always had to negotiate between these poles. The passionate, immediate, embodied dimension of religious experience is not simply a corrupting force; it is a real and powerful human need. The challenge is to channel it without either suppressing it entirely or allowing it to run unconstrained into idolatrous excess.

## Men Great Assembly

### Three Attributes

The *Gemara* transitions to a statement from the school of *Eretz Yisrael*: *b'ma'arava matnu hachi* — in the West they taught as follows. Rav Gidel says the word *gadol* refers to the fact that they used God's ineffable name during the reading of the *Torah* at the great gathering with Ezra. Rav Matna says *gadol* refers to the description of God as *ha'Eil hagadol hagibor v'hanora* — great, powerful, and awesome — in the *pasuk* in Nechemia.

The *Gemara* notes that Rav Matna's statement connects to a teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *lama nikra shemam Anshei Knesset HaGedolah?* — Why were they called the Men of the Great Assembly? *She'hechziru atarah l'yoshnah* — because they returned the crown to its former glory.

### Prophets & Their Times

The explanation unfolds as follows. When Moshe spoke, he described God as *ha'Eil hagadol hagibor v'hanora* — great, powerful, and awesome. Then Yirmeyahu came, at the time of the Temple's destruction, with non-Jews dancing and desecrating God's sanctuary, and said: *ayeh nora'otav?* — Where is God's awesome power, that these nations feel free to act this way? And so Yirmeyahu did not say *nora*. (Rashi cites the *pasuk* in Yirmeyahu 32: *ha'Eil hagadol v'hagibor Hashem Tzeva'os shmo* — he said *gadol* and *gibor*, but not *nora*.)

Then Daniel came, during the exile, with non-Jews subjugating God's children: *ayeh gevurosav?* — Where is God's might, if He allows His children to be subjected to foreign domination? And so Daniel did not say *gibor*. The *pasuk* in Daniel reads *ha'Eil hagadol v'hanora* — *gadol* and *nora*, but not *gibor*.

Then the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* came and restored all three terms. But they did not merely restore them mechanically — they reinterpreted them. *Zu hi gevuraso, shekovesh es yitzro* — this is precisely God's might: that He subdues His own desire. He does not lash out immediately at the wicked. *Shemosein erech apayim lareshaim* — He extends patience to evildoers, granting them opportunity for *teshuvah* and allowing human beings the full exercise of free will. And *ve'elu nora'osav* — this is also God's awesomeness: *she'ilmaley mora'osho shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu, heich u'umah achas yachola lehiskayaim bein ha'umos?* — were it not for the awe that God inspires, how could a single small people have survived among the great nations across such a long exile? Even amid catastrophe, some residual awe and fear of God and of the Jewish people has prevented their total annihilation.

It is worth noting that the one term never omitted by either Yirmeyahu or Daniel was *gadol* — so *gadol* required no restoration. But the other two terms were returned, and that restoration is what earned the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* their name. This is borne out by the fact that when Ezra reads the *Torah* publicly, he uses all three: *ha'Eil hagadol hagibor v'hanora*.

### Truth in Liturgy

The *Gemara* then asks: how could Yirmeyahu and Daniel — who are here called *rabanan* — have omitted words that Moshe had established as the way to describe God? After all, this formulation, while not a formal *mitzvah*, was an institution, a way of addressing God that Moshe had set. Where did they get the *chutzpah* to remove a term?

Rabbi Elazar answers: *mitoch sheyodein baHakadosh Baruch Hu she'amiti hu, lefikach lo kizvu bo* — because they knew that God is *amiti*, that God is about truth. (This connects back to the earlier moment in the *aggadata* when the little note of *emes* fell from heaven in response to their prayer.) Since God is fundamentally about truth, they could not speak falsely about God. The Maharsha clarifies: this does not mean, *chas veshalom*, that Moshe was wrong. God truly is great, mighty, and awesome. But in Yirmeyahu's time, God was not *demonstrating* His awesomeness in a visible way, and in Daniel's time, He was not demonstrating His might. To praise God for attributes He was not manifesting in their era would have been a form of falsehood — not an error about God's nature, but a lie about their own experience of God's presence in history.

### Tradition vs. Truth

This *Gemara* carries powerful implications for how one relates to liturgy. The question it raises — how do you say things in prayer that do not feel true to your own religious experience? — is one that many people grapple with. One approach is to avoid *kavanah* altogether; a person who sent a letter to a certain rabbi responded to a sermon about deepening *kavanah* in *davening* by saying, frankly, that the only way he survived *davening* was by not having *kavanah* — that attempting *kavanah* would surface too many points of dissonance. Another approach is to modify the *davening* itself, as various movements have done. The classical *Torah she-ba'al peh* approach is to re-interpret — to find a reading of the words that allows them to remain true to one's religious experience, to *trans-value* rather than abandon the practice. That too is a form of what the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* were doing: maintaining the tradition, the continuity, the shared formulation — while insisting that what one says about God must remain anchored in truth.

## Torah Reading Rules

### Jumping Rules

The *Gemara* now returns to the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur*. The *Kohen Gadol* reads from the *Torah* before the assembled people — *V'kara acharei mot v'ach be-asor* — the *parsha* of *Acharei Mot*, which describes the *avodah*, and the *pasuk* in *Emor*, *ach be-asor*, which describes the *Mussaf*. These two passages are not adjacent in the *Torah*, which immediately raises a difficulty.

The *Gemara* cites the rule: *m'dalgin b'Navi v'ein m'dalgin b'Torah* — one may skip when reading from the *Navi* (as in the *haftarah*), but one may not skip when reading the *Torah*. How, then, can the *Kohen Gadol* scroll past intervening portions and read from a different section?

The *Gemara* resolves this by introducing the role of the *turgeman*, the translator. The distinction turns on whether the reader is able to reach the new passage before the translator finishes rendering the previous passage. If the skip can be executed without causing an obvious pause — without the congregation having to wait — then it is permitted, even in the *Torah*. This is a striking answer, because one might have expected the prohibition to stem from concern that people would mistakenly believe the two passages are textually adjacent. The *Gemara* locates the concern instead in the interruption itself: the *torach tzibur* of waiting, the disruption to the continuous flow of the reading.

### Single Topic Exception

A further qualification is introduced. The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *medalguim ba-Torah b'inyan echad* — in the *Torah* one may skip if one remains within the same subject matter. *U'vi-Navi bishnei inyanim* — in the *Navi* one may skip even across different subjects. And in both cases, one must do so without causing the translator to pause. Abaye clarifies the rule accordingly: the distinction between single and multiple topics applies to *Torah* readings.

### Absorption & Continuity

Rashi explains the underlying concern in a way that is worth attending to carefully. He writes that *Torah* verses are laden with commandments, consequences, and obligations — *divrei Torah hi atarot v'onashim u'mitzvot* — and *v'tzrichim sheyikansu be-lev hashomim*: they must enter into the hearts of the listeners. They must be absorbed. If a person is jumping between different subjects — *uchshe-ha'adam yotze me-shitta le-shitta acheret eino no'ach lefanav* — it becomes impossible to absorb what one is hearing; the listener is pulled in too many directions at once. This is the *locus* of the concern. It is not, according to Rashi, that people might walk away thinking two passages are adjacent in the text. The concern is the listener's capacity to integrate what is being read to them. As long as both passages address the same theme — as *Acharei Mot* and the relevant section of *Emor* both address the *Yom Kippur avodah* — that concern is resolved, and the skip is permitted.

### Trei Asar Order

The *Gemara* adds an additional ruling: *v'ein medalguim mi-Navi le-Navi* — one may not jump from one prophetic book to another. One does not begin a reading in Yirmeyahu and conclude it with a *pasuk* from Yeshayahu. However, *u'vi-Navi shel Shneim Asar medalguim* — within the *Trei Asar*, the twelve minor prophets, one may jump from one book to another, since halachically they constitute a single *sefer*. This is in fact the basis for the practice on *Shabbat Shuva* of reading the *haftarah* from Hoshea, Yoel, and Micha — different authors, but all part of *Trei Asar* and therefore treated as a single work.

The one constraint is *uvilvad shelo yedaleg mi-sof hasefer l'tchilato* — one may not go backwards within the same *sefer*. The reading must proceed in sequential order. The *Shabbat Shuva* practice indeed reads Hoshea, Yoel, and Micha in their canonical sequence, which satisfies this requirement.

### Avoiding Scroll Delay

Having established that one may read from two separate sections of the *Torah* provided the skip is executed without pause, the *Gemara* addresses the second part of the *Kohen Gadol*'s reading: the portion from *Pinchas*, which contains the *Mussaf* offerings of *Yom Kippur*. Rather than scrolling the *Sefer Torah* to that passage, the *Kohen Gadol* rolls up the scroll, holds it, and recites the portion from *Pinchas* by heart — *b'al peh*. The reason, as Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua explains, is *lefi she'ein golelim Sefer Torah batzibur* — one does not roll a *Sefer Torah* in public in the middle of a reading, because it forces the congregation to wait. The *Gemara* later identifies this concern under the heading of *kvod hatzibur*.

### Torah Integrity

Before he recites the passage by heart, the *Kohen Gadol* makes a declaration: *yatir mima shekarati lifneichem katuv kan* — more than what I have read before you is written here. The purpose of this declaration is *kedei shelo le-hotzi la'az al Sefer Torah* — so that no one should come to suspect that the portion from *Pinchas* was missing or defective in the scroll. He makes clear: the passage exists in the *Sefer Torah*, it is complete, there is nothing wrong with it — I am simply choosing to recite it from memory rather than scroll to it.

### Switching Scrolls

One might ask: why not simply bring a second *Sefer Torah* opened to *Pinchas*, and read from it directly, rather than reciting from memory? The *Gemara* considers this and rejects it for multiple reasons.

Rav Huna bar Yehuda says: *mishum pigmo shel rishon* — because people will assume there was a defect in the first *Sefer Torah*, and that a second had to be brought to replace it. This is a concern of *pegam*, stigma, on the original scroll. The *Gemara* confirms this principle by citing Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha's ruling regarding *Rosh Chodesh Tevet* that falls on *Shabbat* during *Chanukah*: when three separate people each receive an *aliyah* from three different *Sifrei Torah*, there is no *pegam* — each person's reading is complete and self-contained, and nobody has abandoned one scroll in the middle of their own *aliyah*. But if one person were to move from one *Sefer Torah* to another in the middle of his own *kriah*, that is *pegam* — it looks like something went wrong with the first.

Reish Lakish gives a different reason: *mishum bracha she'eina tzricha* — because switching to a new *Sefer Torah* would require making an additional *bracha*, and that *bracha* would be unnecessary. This ruling is itself noteworthy. It raises the question of what exactly the structure of the *brachot* surrounding the *Kohen Gadol*'s *Torah* reading looks like. He clearly recites a *bracha* at the conclusion — *al haTorah*, similar to the closing *bracha* of a standard *aliyah*. But did he also recite an opening *bracha*, such as *asher bachar banu*? The text does not say. What Reish Lakish's concern implies is that the presence of a new *Sefer Torah* would trigger the obligation of another *bracha* — as if the physical *Sefer Torah* being read from is itself an object of the blessing, not merely the reading as an abstract act. Whether this reflects an opening *bracha* that goes unmentioned, or something else about the relationship between the blessing and the physical scroll, is left unresolved. What is clear is that switching scrolls is treated as demanding a new *bracha*, and that unnecessary *bracha* — *she'eina tzricha* — is something to be avoided.

## Eight Blessings

### Blessing Structure

Following the *Torah* reading, the *Kohen Gadol* recites eight *brachot*. The *Gemara* enumerates them: *al haTorah* — on the *Torah*, recited in the manner of the closing *bracha* after an *aliyah*; *al ha'avodah* — on the Temple service; *al hahoda'ah* — thanksgiving; *al mechilat ha'avon* — for the forgiveness of sin; *al haMikdash* — for the Temple; *al hakohanim* — for the priests; *al Yisrael* — for Israel; and *al shear tefillah* — a final catch-all prayer.

Several of these correspond to *brachot* familiar from *Shemoneh Esrei*. *Al ha'avodah* corresponds to *Retzei*, which closes with *hamachazir schinato leTzion*; though Rashi suggests that in the Temple context, the closing formulation would reflect the active acceptance of sacrifices, something like *she'otecha levadecha beyirah na'avod* — as said in *birkat kohanim*. *Al hahoda'ah* corresponds to *Modim*, the expression of gratitude that follows God's acceptance of the offerings. *Al mechilat ha'avon* is a *bracha* specific to *Yom Kippur* — *mochel la'avonoteinu vela'avonot amo beit Yisrael ma'avir ashmoteinu bechol shanah veshanah melech mochel vesole'ach* — which the *Kohen Gadol* recites once the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* is complete.

The remaining four *brachot* — for the *Mikdash*, the priests, Israel, and the general prayer — are not elaborated upon in the *Gemara* here, though Rashi notes that the Yerushalmi preserves their texts.

It is also worth noting that some versions of the *Mishnah* include a *bracha* for *Yerushalayim*, which would bring the total to nine. In the version before us, and in other witnesses to the *Mishnah*, *Yerushalayim* is absent, yielding eight.

### Shomea Tefillah

The eighth *bracha*, *shear tefillah*, is defined by a *baraita*: *rinah tchinah bakashah milfanecha, al amcha Yisrael shetzrichim lehivashea, vechotem beshome'a tefillah* — a song, a supplication, a request before God for Israel who requires salvation, sealed with the blessing *shome'a tefillah*. This is the blessing we know from the weekday *Shemoneh Esrei*, the one that falls just before *Retzei*.

*Shome'a tefillah* functions as the great catch-all. We have spent the length of *Shemoneh Esrei* making specific, named requests. *Shome'a tefillah* says: please accept all of that — and beyond it. There are things we need that we cannot fully articulate, petitions we have no language for; *shome'a tefillah* invokes God as the one who hears prayer in its fullness, beyond what can be said.

### Sacrifice to Prayer

What is striking about this ordering is the inversion relative to the standard *Shemoneh Esrei* structure. In our daily *tefillah*, *shome'a tefillah* precedes *Retzei*: the generic plea for God to hear our prayers comes before the specific request for God to accept the Temple service. Here, in the *Kohen Gadol*'s eight *brachot*, the order is reversed: it begins with *al ha'avodah* — with *Retzei*, with the *korbanot* — and only after that does it move to *shear tefillah*, the generic prayer. This reflects the direction of the moment: we begin from within the world of *korbanot* and the *Mikdash*, from the sacrificial rite just completed, and from that foundation we move outward toward the broader prayer for Israel's needs. It is the *Mikdash* grafting the *beit knesset* onto itself, rather than the other way around.

There is also a noteworthy structural observation made by the Tosafot Yeshanim (cited in an earlier part of the tractate): the major reason for the public *Torah* reading at this juncture is in order to give *brachot* on the *avodah* — to frame the entire preceding ritual with the language of blessing and *tefillah*. In that reading, the eight *brachot* are the culminating expression of everything that has just taken place in the *Kodesh Kodashim*.

## Garment Sequence

### First Linen Set

The *Mishnah* now traces the sequence of garment changes the *Kohen Gadol* undergoes throughout *Yom Kippur*. Each change involves the sanctification of the hands and feet before removing the previous garments, immersion in the *mikvah*, drying off, donning the new garments, and sanctifying the hands and feet again once dressed — five complete cycles in all.

The first set of garments is the *bigdei lavan*, the white linen garments, which the *Kohen Gadol* wears for the *Torah* reading and for the central *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* — the incense offering in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, the blood sprinklings, and the associated rites described in *Acharei Mot*.

### First Gold Set

After completing the *avodah* in the white garments, the *Kohen Gadol* immerses and changes into his *bigdei zahav*, the gold garments. He then performs the two rams — his own ram (*eilo*) and the ram of the people (*ayil ha'am*), both of which are *olot* — as well as the *Mussaf* offering.

## Mussaf Timing Disput (II)

### Rabbi Eliezer Split

Rabbi Eliezer holds that the *Mussaf* offering is divided: part of it is brought before the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur*, and part of it after. Specifically, according to Rabbi Eliezer's position as it appears in the *Mishnah*, the seven lambs of the *Mussaf* are brought at one point — positioned alongside the *avodah* — while the remaining components of the *Mussaf* (the ox for the *olah* and the goat offered outside on the outer *mizbe'ach*) are brought at another time.

### Rabbi Akiva Morning

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He holds that the seven lambs — and, in the more developed formulation of his position — all the components of the *Mussaf* that can be brought in the morning are brought together with the morning *tamid*, before the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* even begins. The logic, as we shall see developed below, is that the *Mussaf* is always linked to the morning *tamid*, and the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* is a separate ritual with its own internal sequence.

---

## Garment Sequence (II)

### Second Linen Set

Following his first wearing of the gold garments, the *Kohen Gadol* immerses once more and dons the white linen garments a second time. The sole act performed in this second set of white garments is the removal of the fire pan (*kaf*) and the spoon (*machta*) — the vessels used for the incense offering that were left inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* from the earlier *avodah*. With their removal, the interior *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* is fully concluded.

### Third Gold Set

He then immerses a third time and dons the gold garments again. In this third set, he performs the afternoon incense offering (*ketoret shel bein ha'arbayim*) and the lighting of the *nerot* — the *menorah* lamps. The *tamid shel bein ha'arbayim* would also accompany this set, though the *Mishnah*'s language here focuses on the incense and lamps.

### Personal Garments

At the conclusion of all his service, the *Kohen Gadol* performs a final sanctification of the hands — this time without immersing in the *mikvah*, since the *avodah* is complete — removes his *bigdei zahav*, and changes into his own personal clothes, *bigdei atzmo*. A retinue then accompanies him home, and he hosts a celebration for his loved ones: *v'yom tov haya oseh l'ohavav b'shaa sheyatza b'shalom min hakodesh* — a day of rejoicing, upon having emerged safely from the *Kodesh*.

## Mussaf Timing Disput (II)

### Mishnah Ambiguity

With the *Mishnah*'s account of the garment sequence now laid out, the *Gemara* returns to examine the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva in detail — and finds that the *Mishnah* itself leaves a great deal unresolved.

First, there is a textual ambiguity in Rabbi Akiva's statement. He says: if the seven lambs were brought with the morning *tamid*, then the ox for the *olah* and the goat offered outside were brought with the afternoon *tamid*. But the *Gemara* asks: does this mean (a) the seven lambs in the morning and the ox and goat together in the afternoon, or (b) the seven lambs and the ox in the morning, with only the goat in the afternoon? While Tosafot argues that the plural form of the verb resolves this ambiguity, the *Gemara* treats it as genuinely open.

More substantially, the *Gemara* identifies what Rabbi Eliezer's position in the *Mishnah* leaves entirely unspecified. Rabbi Eliezer mentioned that the seven lambs (part one of the *Mussaf*) are brought at a particular juncture, but never stated where the ox (*par ha'olah*) and the goat offered outside (*se'ir hana'aseh bachutz*) are brought. Rashi notes that the goat is equally unaddressed.

Furthermore, both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva leave unspecified the question of *imurei chatat* — when the intestinal portions (*eimurim*) of the bull and goat of the *Yom Kippur* service are placed on the outer *mizbe'ach*. Recall that the carcasses of the bull and goat were removed and sent to be burned outside the city, but their *eimurim* — the sacrificial portions — must be offered on the *mizbe'ach*. Neither position in the *Mishnah* specifies when this occurs.

Rava therefore concludes: *lo mashchachat leh metakanta* — you will not find a fully delineated account of the ordering in the *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* simply does not provide enough detail to reconstruct the complete sequence with certainty. If one wants a fully specified account, one must turn elsewhere.

### Baraita Clarification

Rava identifies two such sources: a *baraita* from the school of Shmuel presenting a version of Rabbi Eliezer's position, and the *Tosefta* presenting Rabbi Akiva's position. He adds a caveat: the Rabbi Eliezer who appears in the *baraita* from the school of Shmuel does not hold exactly the same position as the Rabbi Eliezer of the *Mishnah*. One should not press the contradiction between them; they represent different traditions.

The *baraita* from the school of Shmuel, attributed to Rabbi Eliezer, states: *yatzah v'asah eilo v'eil ha'am v'imurei chatat* — after the *avodah* proper, he goes out and brings the two rams (*eilo v'ayil ha'am*) and the *imurei chatat*, the intestinal portions of the bull and goat. Then: *aval par ha'olah v'shiva k'vasim v'se'ir hana'aseh bachutz im tamid shel bein ha'arbayim* — the ox for the *olah*, the seven lambs, and the goat offered outside are all brought with the afternoon *tamid*.

This version of Rabbi Eliezer is notable because it shares with Rabbi Akiva the commitment to keeping the *bigdei zahav* section after the *avodah* focused exclusively on *Yom Kippur*-related matters — here, only the *imurei par v'se'ir* and the two rams, which are the organic conclusion of the *Yom Kippur* service. The entire *Mussaf* in this version is deferred to the afternoon.

### Scriptural Linking

The *Tosefta*'s version of Rabbi Akiva's position is: *par ha'olah v'shiva k'vasim im tamid shel shachar hayu k'revim* — the ox for the *olah* and the seven lambs are brought with the morning *tamid*. The scriptural basis is *milvad olas haboker asher le'olas hatamid* — "in addition to the morning burnt offering which is the *tamid*" — which Rabbi Akiva reads as establishing that whenever the *Mussaf* is brought, it must accompany the morning *tamid*. The structural principle at work throughout Rabbi Akiva's position is this: when passages of the *Torah* link two offerings together, they are performed together.

The goat offered outside is then brought after the *avodah*, since the *pasuk* describes it as *milvad chatat hakippurim* — in addition to the *Yom Kippur chatat* — which indicates it comes after the *Yom Kippur chatat* is complete.

### Akiva Ordering

The full ordering according to the *Tosefta*'s version of Rabbi Akiva is as follows. First, the morning *tamid* together with most of the *Mussaf* — the ox (*par ha'olah*) and the seven lambs — since Scripture links the *Mussaf olah* to the morning *tamid*. Then, the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* — the incense offering in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, the blood sprinklings, and all the attendant rites. Immediately after the *avodah*, the goat offered outside (*se'ir hana'aseh bachutz*) — since it is described as *milvad chatat hakippurim* and must therefore follow the *Yom Kippur chatat*. After that, the two rams: *eilo v'ayil ha'am*, the rams connected to the *Yom Kippur* service. Then the *imurei chatat* — the intestinal portions of the bull and goat placed on the *mizbe'ach*, completing the handling of the *Yom Kippur* offerings. And finally, the afternoon *tamid* — *tamid shel bein ha'arbayim*.

This ordering according to Rabbi Akiva places nearly the entire *Mussaf* at the front, alongside the morning *tamid*, before the *avodah* begins. Only the goat offered outside is held until after the *avodah*, by virtue of its scriptural linkage to the *chatat* of *Yom Kippur*. The *imurei chatat* and the rams close out the *Yom Kippur* rites entirely before the afternoon *tamid* concludes the day.

The precise positions of Rabbi Eliezer in the *baraita* and Rabbi Akiva in the *Tosefta* thus represent two coherent and fully elaborated orderings of the day's sacrificial service — orderings that the *Mishnah* itself, as Rava observed, leaves largely implicit. The *Gemara* will continue to develop the analysis of these positions in the following daf.
