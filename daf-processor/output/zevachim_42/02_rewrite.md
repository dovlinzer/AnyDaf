# Zevachim 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reish Lakish's Reading

### Meir vs. Chachamim

The daf opens by returning to the central debate between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* concerning *pigul* — the invalidation of a sacrifice through an improper intention expressed during one of the four key sacrificial acts. The question at stake is the concept of *mefaglin bechatzi matir*: whether a *pigul* thought expressed during only one component of a composite *matir* act is sufficient to create *pigul*, or whether the intention must accompany every component.

To appreciate the structure of this debate, it is necessary to understand what constitutes a *chatzi matir*. The *matir* acts are the four pivotal moments that render a *korban* valid: *shechita*, *kabbalah*, *holachah*, and *zerikah*. Within some of these acts, there are discrete sub-components — for instance, applying blood to one of four corners of the altar, or severing one of the two organs (*kaneh* or *veshet*) during *shechita*. Each such discrete component is a *chatzi matir*. Importantly, however, not every partial act qualifies. A single step taken while walking the blood toward the altar is not a *chatzi matir*, because it is not a discrete, bounded component of *holachah* — it is merely part of a continuous act. The concept of *chatzi* here does not mean exactly half; it refers to any discrete sub-unit within a composite *matir* act.

The *pshat* of the *mishnah* is clear: Rabbi Meir holds *mefaglin bechatzi matir* — a *pigul* thought on even one discrete component suffices to create *pigul*. The *chachamim* disagree, requiring *ad sheyefagel bechol hamatir* — the *pigul* intention must accompany every component of the *matir* act.

### First Act Only

It is in this context that Reish Lakish proposes a striking reinterpretation of Rabbi Meir. Reish Lakish argues that even Rabbi Meir does not truly hold *mefaglin bechatzi matir* in the broadest sense. Rather, Rabbi Meir's position is that a *pigul* thought expressed during the *first* component suffices — because the first act is understood to define and encompass all subsequent acts. Since every subsequent act is performed *al da'as* the first, the first declaration of intent retroactively covers the whole process. A *pigul* thought expressed in the middle of the process, by contrast, does not — according to Reish Lakish's reading of Rabbi Meir — create *pigul*.

Rashi notes that "thought" here means articulated thought — a verbalized intention — not a merely internal mental state.

### Yom Kippur Blood

To appreciate the full scope of the debate, the *gemara* catalogues the 43 blood applications of Yom Kippur. The *kohen gadol* performs one-and-seven applications on the *kaporet* and one-and-seven on the *parochet*, separately for the *par* (bull) and the *sa'ir* (goat), yielding 32 applications. He then mixes the bloods and applies four to the corners of the golden altar and seven across its top, yielding another 11 — for a total of 43. The same structure applies to the *par* of the *kohen hamashiach* and the *par he'alem davar shel tzibbur*, both of which involve 11 applications of blood.

The *baraita* states that Rabbi Meir says *pigul* applies whether the improper thought occurred during the first, second, or third set of applications. This is already a striking formulation — Rabbi Meir is *more stringent* here than by a standard outer-altar *chatat*, for which a *pigul* thought after the first application does not create *pigul*. By the inner-altar applications, since all are required, Rabbi Meir holds that a *pigul* thought on any one of them suffices. The *chachamim* are correspondingly *more lenient* than by the outer *chatat*: they require the *pigul* intention at every single application.

### Baraita Challenge

The *baraita* thus presents an immediate challenge to Reish Lakish. The text explicitly states that a *pigul* thought during the second or third set of applications — not the first — still produces *pigul* according to Rabbi Meir. The *gemara* presses the point: *katani miha* — it states plainly, *pigul bein berishona bein beshniya bein beshlishis*, "whether the first, or the second, or the third." This appears to directly contradict Reish Lakish's claim that Rabbi Meir limits *pigul* to a thought expressed during the first act.

Reish Lakish is therefore compelled to reinterpret. His proposal, attributed in the *gemara* to Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avin, is that the *baraita* is not discussing a straightforward sequence of blood applications, but rather the unusual case where blood spilled partway through the Yom Kippur service and the *kohen gadol* performed a second *shechita* to obtain more blood. In Rashi's reading, for instance, blood might have spilled between the *kaporet* applications and the *parochet* applications. The *kohen* would then slaughter a second animal to obtain fresh blood and continue. When the *baraita* says the *pigul* thought occurred "during the second" or "during the third," it means the *pigul* thought was expressed *during the shechita* of the second or third animal — not during a middle blood application. Since *shechita* is itself a complete, discrete *matir* act, this is not *chatzi matir* at all. Even on Reish Lakish's reading of Rabbi Meir, a *pigul* thought during a full *shechita* suffices — and so the *baraita* is not a counterproof.

This reading is, as the *gemara* itself will acknowledge, extremely forced (*dachuk*). But it represents Reish Lakish's attempt to preserve his interpretation.

## Spilled Blood Scenario

### Rashi's Reading

Rashi's scenario, then, is this: the blood spills at some point mid-process on Yom Kippur — say, between the *kaporet* and the *parochet* applications. A second animal must now be slaughtered. The *baraita*'s reference to a *pigul* thought "during the second" or "during the third" refers to a *pigul* thought expressed at the moment of *shechita* of the second or third animal slaughtered to replenish spilled blood. In that case, the *pigul* thought is attached to a *shechita* — itself a full, unambiguous *matir* act — not to a mere component within a larger blood application process. Thus, Rabbi Meir can be read as requiring a *pigul* thought on a full act, and the apparent counterproof dissolves.

The *gemara* then asks the obvious follow-up: *ei hachi*, if that is so — if the *shechita* here is a full *matir* act — then why do the *chachamim* say it does *not* create *pigul*? If we accept the framework that *shechita* is always a full, discrete *matir*, what basis could there be for the *chachamim*'s leniency?

### Gemara Challenge

The *gemara* formulates this challenge precisely. If, as Reish Lakish insists, we are speaking of a *pigul* thought during *shechita* — which is a single, complete *matir* — then *mai ta'ama de-Rabbanan*? What is the rationale of the *chachamim* who would not recognize this as *pigul*? The scenario now seems to involve a straightforwardly valid *pigul* thought on a complete act, and yet the *chachamim* exempt. Something in the structure of this unusual case must give the *chachamim* grounds to distinguish it from the standard case.

### Rava Identifies

Rava resolves this by identifying: *man Chachamim? Rabbi Eliezer.* The *chachamim* in this unusual reading of Reish Lakish are none other than Rabbi Eliezer. The case has an inherent complexity: ordinarily, the entire Yom Kippur *avodah* requires only a single *shechita*. This second *shechita* is being performed solely because blood spilled — it is not the inaugural *shechita* of the *korban* itself, but rather a supplementary one, performed in the middle of an ongoing blood application process. Is this second *shechita* a full *matir* in the same sense as the first? Or is it somehow a partial *matir* — a *chatzi matir* — by virtue of the fact that it is contributing to a process already underway, stepping in where the first *shechita* left off?

This question — whether a *shechita* performed mid-process to replenish spilled blood is a full *matir* or a *chatzi matir* — is genuinely interesting and would have constituted a meaningful debate on its own. What makes the association with Rabbi Eliezer more complex, however, is the analogy the *gemara* now attempts to draw.

## Rabbi Eliezer Analogy

### Eliezer Full Amount

The *mishnah* cited in this context enumerates items that are entirely consumed on the *mizbeach*: the *kometz* (the handful taken from the *mincha*), the *levonah* (frankincense accompanying the *mincha*), the *ketores* (incense burned on the inner altar), *minchas kohanim*, *minchas kohen mashiach*, and *minchas nesachim*. With respect to all of these, the general rule is that if one brings even a *kezayit* of any of them outside the *Beis Hamikdash* — the prohibition of *shechutei chutz* or *ha'alas chutz* — one is liable.

Rabbi Eliezer, however, exempts: *poter ad she-yakriv es kullan*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that one is not liable until one offers up the *entire* amount outside — a *kezayit* alone does not suffice. Rashi explains this as follows: if part of the item was already offered on the inner altar in the *Beis Hamikdash* and then a *kezayit* of the remainder was offered outside, liability may follow, because the act of bringing the partial amount outside can be connected to and measured against the prior inside offering. But if nothing was offered inside and someone brings only a *kezayit* outside, without ever having brought the full amount inside, then this *kezayit* is not a sufficiently meaningful act of *hakravah*. Since the normative requirement is to bring the entire *kometz* or *levonah* on the altar, a mere *kezayit* outside — disconnected from any inside offering — does not rise to the level of a culpable act of *ha'alas chutz*. One is liable outside only for an act of a type that would have generated liability inside, and since bringing a partial *kometz* on the inside altar accomplishes nothing definitive, a *kezayit* of it outside likewise does not.

### Tosafot Comparison

Tosafot draws an inference from this analogy that illuminates the broader relationship between *pigul* and *shchutei chutz*. Tosafot argues that the standards for creating *pigul* are, as a general rule, *higher* than the standards for liability in *shchutei chutz*. The evidence: according to the *chachamim* who hold *ein mefaglin bechatzi matir* — and, under Reish Lakish's reading, Rabbi Meir as well — a *pigul* thought about the *kometz* alone does not create *pigul*, because the *levonah* is a co-*matir* of the *mincha*. Both the *kometz* and the *levonah* are required to permit the remainder, and therefore a *pigul* thought on only one of them is insufficient. Yet by *ha'alas chutz*, one is liable even for a *kezayit* of the *mincha* — a fraction, not the full *kometz* let alone the full *mincha*. The threshold for *pigul* is thus demonstrably higher than for *chutz*.

The practical implication of this asymmetry, Tosafot argues, is a *kal vachomer*: if one is exempt from liability for *chutz* by bringing a partial amount outside, then one certainly cannot create *pigul* with a comparably partial act. Specifically, applying this to the *shechita*-in-the-middle scenario: if Rabbi Eliezer exempts from *chutz* liability because the *kezayit* of the *kometz* outside is insufficiently meaningful without the full amount — then, *kal vachomer*, Rabbi Eliezer would hold that the second *shechita* performed mid-process cannot create *pigul*. It is, in some sense, a partial *matir*, and by the *kal vachomer* from *chutz*, where even more is sometimes required, *pigul* would certainly not attach.

The analogy, however, is admittedly imprecise. A *kezayit* of the *kometz* is genuinely a *chatzi matir* — a partial unit of the permitting substance. The second *shechita* in the Yom Kippur spilling scenario is, by contrast, a complete act of *shechita* — a full discrete act — that happens to occur in the middle of an ongoing process. The comparison between these two cases is strained, and both Rashi and Tosafot do not fully articulate how the analogy holds. The *gemara* will ultimately abandon it.

### Blood Exception

The *gemara* then raises a complication that undermines the entire structure of the Eliezer analogy. Rava points out: *u-modeh Rabbi Eliezer be-damim* — Rabbi Eliezer concedes when it comes to blood. As a *mishnah* states, Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon both hold *mi-makom she-pasak misham hu matchil*: if blood spills mid-process, one slaughters a new animal and resumes from the point of interruption rather than restarting the entire *avodah* from the beginning. This ruling reflects a view that each completed blood application inside the *Beis Hamikdash* accomplishes something meaningful in its own right — because if it did not, there would be no basis to "pick up where one left off." Since each application is not negated by the subsequent spilling, even a partial blood application has real and durable significance.

Given this, Rabbi Eliezer's exemption for a *kezayit* of the *kometz* outside cannot be extended by simple analogy to the blood. A *kezayit* of *kometz* offered inside accomplishes nothing identifiable — it does not "move" the *korban* forward in the way that a blood application does. But a partial blood application, even if incomplete, is considered to carry real weight. Therefore, by *ha'alas chutz*, Rabbi Eliezer would actually hold that even a small amount of blood brought outside generates liability. His exemption applies only to the *kometz* and like items, not to blood.

### Spilling Rule

This complicates the attempted analogy beyond repair. Tosafot draws the conclusion that one cannot extract from Rabbi Eliezer's position on *chutz* a clean, predictable rule about *pigul*. Rabbi Eliezer's rulings on *chutz* are internally differentiated: he exempts for a *kezayit* of *kometz* outside, but he would hold liable for a small quantity of blood outside. This asymmetry within his own view of *chutz* means that one cannot draw a straight line from his *chutz* position to whatever his *pigul* position might be in the second-*shechita* scenario. The *gemara* abandons the analogy.

What remains is a genuinely interesting underlying question: if the principle is *ein mefaglin bechatzi matir*, and if *shechita* is ordinarily a full *matir*, what happens when a *shechita* is performed in the middle of an ongoing blood-application process to replenish spilled blood? Is that *shechita* a full *matir* or a *chatzi matir*? This question would have constituted a real and meaningful debate. The difficulty is that linking it to Rabbi Eliezer's rulings about *chutz* introduces more confusion than clarity, and the *gemara* leaves this line of analysis behind.

## Meir's True Position

### Bracketing Problem

The *gemara* now returns to what is plainly the *pshat* of the *mishnah* — that Rabbi Meir holds *mefaglin bechatzi matir* without qualification — and puts aside the forced re-readings Reish Lakish has proposed. With this as the foundation, the *gemara* raises a fundamental structural problem about how *pigul* operates in the context of *chatzi matir*.

The general principle is: *kehartza'as kasher ken hartza'as pasul* — just as a valid *korban* requires all its *matirim* to be properly performed, so too a *pasul* (invalid) *korban* — including a *pigul korban* — requires that all the *matirim* apart from the *pigul* act itself be performed properly. One is only liable for *pigul* if the rest of the *avodah* was done correctly: *ad sheyikrevu kol matirav*. The *pigul* thought on one act "brackets" that act, but the rest must be kosher.

The *gemara* now asks: how does this work in a case of *chatzi matir*? Say the *kohen* has a *pigul* thought during the first of the blood applications on the *kaporet*. The blood is now *pasul* — the act of *keivas dam* on the *kaporet* is invalidated by the *pigul* thought. But now, when the *kohen* continues to the *parochet* and applies the same blood, he is applying *pasul* blood. How is this "doing the rest properly"? The blood applied to the *parochet* is, at this stage, no better than water — *mayim be'alma* — it cannot accomplish anything, because the blood itself has been invalidated. Therefore, the condition of *ad sheyakrivu kol matirav* — that all the *matirim* be performed correctly — seems to be violated, not by the *pigul* thought itself, but by the *pasul* blood that continues to be sprinkled after the *pigul* thought.

One might wonder: isn't this the same problem for any case of *pigul*? Even in the standard case, if a *pigul* thought occurs during *shechita*, the blood that comes from that *shechita* is *pasul*. Yet we routinely say that *zerikah* must be performed properly for *pigul* to take effect. This seems to present the same paradox: you are applying *pasul* blood, so how is the *zerikah* kosher?

The answer is that in the standard case, we "bracket" the *pigul* act — we treat the *shechita* as a self-contained event and assess the rest of the *avodah* separately from it. Bracketing the *shechita*'s *pigul* status, the *zerikah* was technically performed properly. This bracketing works because the *pigul* act is a complete, discrete unit: the *shechita* is a whole act, and the *zerikah* is a separate whole act. We can quarantine the *pigul* reality to one act and assess the others independently.

### Rashi Solution

Rashi argues that this bracketing breaks down in the *chatzi matir* case. When the *pigul* thought occurs in the middle of the blood-application process — say, at the first application on the *kaporet* — we cannot cleanly bracket that one application and assess the rest of the process separately. The reason is that all of these applications are part of a single composite act. We cannot label part of a single act as *pigul* and the rest as kosher; the entire act resists being given a single unitary label. Since the act as a whole is neither fully *pigul* nor fully kosher, we cannot bracket the *pigul* portion and say the rest was done properly. Instead, we must evaluate the whole act honestly: part of it was done with a *pigul* intention and part without. The result is not *pigul* — which requires the full bracketing operation — but simply *pasul*: the *korban* is invalid, not because of *pigul*, but because the act was partially compromised.

The *gemara*'s difficulty, on Rashi's reading, thus flows from the very definition of *chatzi matir*: because the *pigul* event is embedded within a composite act, the act cannot be unambiguously labeled *pigul* for purposes of the bracketing operation. Without successful bracketing, the condition of *ad sheyakrivu kol matirav* cannot be met, and the *pigul* status cannot be established. This, then, is what makes the case puzzling.

### Rava Final Answer

Rava resolves the difficulty by rejecting the premise that discrete acts are required for bracketing to work. He first suggests that the case in question involves multiple animals — four bulls and four goats — where spilled blood at various points necessitated additional *shechitos*, creating genuinely discrete stages. In those circumstances, the bracketing is unproblematic, since each stage is independently bounded.

But Rava then goes further and says: *afilu teima bepar echad use'ir echad* — even if one takes the case at face value, with a single bull and a single goat, the resolution is the same. The mechanism of *pigul meratzeh* — the principle by which *pigul* operates to "permit" the *korban* for purposes of liability — applies even to an intra-act *pigul* thought. Just as we bracket the *pigul* reality when it attaches to a discrete whole act, we bracket it equally when it attaches to a component within a composite act. The rest of the process is evaluated as if the *pigul* event is quarantined, and if the remainder was performed correctly, the *pigul* takes effect. There is no categorical distinction between bracketing a whole act and bracketing part of an act — the *gemara*'s proposed distinction between discrete and non-discrete scenarios does not hold, and Rava's final answer is that even within a single continuous process, *pigul* on one component can be bracketed and the rest assessed as properly performed.

## Blood Counts

### Mixing Question

Having established the framework for the 43 blood applications of Yom Kippur, the *gemara* notes that a *baraita* records a different count: 47. The discrepancy hinges on the question of when the bloods of the *par* and the *sa'ir* are mixed together. After the applications on the *kaporet* and *parochet* for each animal separately (yielding 32 applications in total), the *kohen* mixes the bloods and performs the remaining applications on the golden altar — four on its corners and seven across its top, for 11 additional applications, giving 43.

The debate is: *ha-keman de'amar me'arvin lekranot, ha-keman de'amar ein me'arvin lekranot* — one opinion holds that the bloods are mixed *before* the corner applications, yielding 11 applications with the combined blood and a total of 43. A second opinion holds that the corner applications are performed separately with each blood before mixing — *ein me'arvin lekranot* — which doubles the four corner applications to eight and yields 47. The relevant *pasuk* is noted: the phrase *mi-dam ha-par u-mi-dam ha-sa'ir* at the corners could be read as indicating separate applications of each, while the subsequent phrase *min ha-dam* for the seven upper applications indicates a unified blood. This textual evidence tracks the structural debate precisely.

### Sheirim Debate

A third *baraita* records yet another count: 48. The additional application arises from the question of whether the *sheirim* — the residual blood poured at the base of the outer altar — is *me'akev*, an indispensable prerequisite. If this final spilling is halachically required and not merely supplementary, it would count as an additional application and bring the total to 48 (or to 48 from 47, depending on which view of the mixing one follows).

This connects to a broader question about inner *chata'ot* generally: is the spilling of *sheirim* at the *yesod* of the outer altar indispensable to the *avodah*, or merely a desirable additional act that does not invalidate the offering if omitted? Our *mishnah* in *Eizehu Mekoman* states explicitly that omitting the *sheirim* does not invalidate — *ve-im lo natan lo ikev*. The *baraita* that yields 48 appears to represent the dissenting view that the *sheirim* is *me'akev* for the Yom Kippur *avodah*.

## Menachot Challenge

### Mincha Baraita

Having exhausted the main lines of challenge to Reish Lakish from the Yom Kippur *baraita*, the *gemara* raises yet another challenge from an entirely different domain — the *mincha* offering. The *baraita* discusses the case of two *matirim* in the *mincha*: the *kometz* and the *levonah*. Either one alone can create *pigul* according to Rabbi Meir, since each is a *matir* in its own right. The *baraita* then presents two sub-cases: a *pigul* thought during the *kometz* with silence during the *levonah*, and silence during the *kometz* with a *pigul* thought during the *levonah*. Rabbi Meir says *pigul* in both cases; the *chachamim* require both.

### Sequence Evidence

The *gemara* focuses on the second sub-case: silence during the *kometz*, followed by a *pigul* thought during the *levonah* alone. This is explicit evidence that a *pigul* thought occurring *after* a silent first act still constitutes *pigul* for Rabbi Meir. The *levonah* is not the first *matir* — the *kometz* was taken first, in silence. Yet Rabbi Meir says the *pigul* thought on the *levonah* alone suffices. This directly contradicts Reish Lakish's claim that Rabbi Meir requires the *pigul* thought to occur at the *first* act.

The *gemara* presents this challenge with the formula *katani miha*: "it states nonetheless" — the *baraita* explicitly gives a case where the first act was done in silence and only the second carried the *pigul* thought, and Rabbi Meir rules *pigul*.

### Rejection

Reish Lakish attempts his usual response: read *kvar natan et ha-levonah be-machshavah* — understand the *baraita* as saying that the *levonah* had *already* been given with a *pigul* thought (i.e., the *levonah* was first), and the subsequent mention of the *kometz* in silence is describing what came after. On this reading, the *baraita* is not describing a case of a first-act silence followed by a second-act *pigul* thought; rather, the *pigul* thought was on the *levonah* first, and the *kometz* in silence came afterward.

The *gemara* rejects this in two steps. First: *chada de-hainu reisha* — this reading would make the second case merely redundant with the first case already stated in the *baraita*. There is no reason to repeat the same scenario. Second, and more decisively: another *baraita* adds the explicit words *ve-achar kach* — "and afterward" — making the sequence unambiguous. First the *kometz* in silence, and *then* afterward the *levonah* with the *pigul* thought, and still Rabbi Meir says *pigul*.

The *gemara* ends with *kashya* — this is a difficulty for Reish Lakish. The editorial understatement is notable. The accumulated weight of evidence — from the Yom Kippur *baraita*, from the *mincha baraita*, and from the explicit sequential language of the supplementary *baraita* — makes it abundantly clear that Rabbi Meir's *pshat* position is straightforwardly *mefaglin bechatzi matir*: a *pigul* thought on any discrete component of a *matir* act, whether first or last, creates *pigul*. Reish Lakish's rereadings, for all their ingenuity, cannot be sustained against the textual evidence.

## Exempt from Pigul

### Matir Exempt

The *gemara* now transitions to a new *mishnah*. The *mishnah* opens: *ve-elu devarim she-ein chayavin aleihem mishum pigul* — "and these are the things about which there is no liability for *pigul*." This requires some orientation. Once a *korban* has been successfully rendered *pigul*, eating a *kezayit* of it triggers *chiyuv karet*. But not every part of the *korban* is subject to this liability. The basic rule is that *pigul* liability applies to the parts of the *korban* that are "edible" in the relevant sense — that is, parts designated for eating, whether by human beings or by the *mizbeach* (*achilas mizbeach*). This includes meat, entrails, and the like.

However, a fundamental exception applies: the *matir* itself is not subject to *pigul* liability. The very item through whose agency *pigul* was created is exempt from the resulting prohibition. The logic is that the *matir* is the agent of the *pigul* status — it is the thing that "permits" the rest, and through which the improper intention operated — but the *issur* of *pigul* does not retroactively attach to it as an object of prohibition.

The blood, for instance: a *pigul* thought during *zerikah* (the blood application) does create *pigul* for the meat of the *korban*, because the intention expressed during blood application is an intention regarding the *achilas* of the meat. Yet the blood itself, being the *matir*, is not the object of the *pigul issur*. Separately, eating blood would carry its own prohibition — but not the specific prohibition of eating *pigul*.

### Blood Kometz

The *mishnah* enumerates the items that are exempt from *pigul* liability on these grounds: the *kometz*, the *levonah*, the *ketores*, *minchas kohanim*, *minchas kohen mashiach*, and the blood. Each of these serves as a *matir* — it is the operative instrument that renders other portions of the *korban* either edible (for humans) or acceptable on the *mizbeach*. Since they are *matirim*, they are exempt from the *pigul* liability that their own activity generates for others.

The *kometz* is the handful taken from the *mincha* and burned on the altar: it permits the remainder of the *mincha* flour to be eaten by the *kohanim*. The *levonah* similarly accompanies the *mincha* and functions as a co-*matir*. The *ketores* is the incense burned on the inner altar. The *minchas kohanim* and *minchas kohen mashiach* are fully consumed on the *mizbeach* — there is no remainder to be "permitted" for eating — and so they function as *matir atzman*, permitting themselves. Blood is the paradigmatic *matir* for animal sacrifices. All of these are therefore outside the scope of *pigul* liability as objects.

### Nesachim Issue

The *mishnah* then adds another category: *ve-hanesachim ha-ba'im bifnei atzman* — the wine and flour offerings (*nesachim*) when they are brought independently, not on the same day as the animal sacrifice with which they are associated. The normal model of *nesachim* is that the wine, flour, and oil are brought together with the *korban behemah* on the day of the animal sacrifice. However, the *halacha* permits these to be brought on a later day, independently. When they are brought *bifnei atzman* — on their own, without the animal — what is the *matir* for them?

In the independent case, there is no external *matir* — no animal whose *zerikah* permits something else. The *nesachim* themselves are fully offered on the *mizbeach*, and they are considered *matir atzman* in the same sense as *minchas kohanim*: the act of bringing them to the *mizbeach* is itself the full act, permitting nothing else since there is nothing else. They therefore fall into the category of *matir* items exempt from *pigul* liability.

### Meir Chachamim

*Divrei Rabbi Meir* — this is the position of Rabbi Meir. The *chachamim*, however, extend the exemption further: *ve-chachamim omrim af ha-ba'im im ha-behemah* — even when the *nesachim* are brought together with the animal on the same day, they are still exempt from *pigul* liability. The reasoning is subtle: since in principle these same *nesachim* could have been brought on a different day — independently — they are always considered to possess the character of *matir atzman*. Even when brought alongside the animal, they could have stood alone. This theoretical independence is sufficient to classify them as *matir*-type items and exclude them from *pigul* liability, even in the case where they are in practice accompanying an animal *korban*.

The *gemara* will examine this position more closely in subsequent discussion. Here we conclude the *mishnah* at this stage.
