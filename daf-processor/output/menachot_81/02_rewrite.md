# Menachot 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Animals Analysis

### Three Substitutes Setup

The daf opens in the middle of a broader discussion about the relationship of lachmei todah to the todah itself, particularly regarding what happens when a substitute is created. The Gemara introduces a parallel case involving a chatas. The text reads: *ve-chein le-inyan chataot* — the same halacha applies to a chatas.

The case unfolds as follows. You separated a chatas — *hifrish chatato* — and it was lost. You then designated a second animal in its place, and then the second was also lost, so you designated a third. Then the first one was found. Now all three stand before you. The relevant background halacha is that by a todah, a substitute that is not ultimately used does not receive bread; by a chatas, the unused substitute must die — *meisa*.

### Rava Pairwise

Rava's position is that you analyze the animals in sequential pairs. If you use the first animal for your chatas — *nitkaper berishona* — then the second dies, since it was the substitute chatas that went unused. But looking at the two-three pair: since the second never actually functioned as a chatas, the third was never truly a substitute chatas, and therefore need not die — it may graze until it develops a mum and is then redeemed — *tire*. Conversely, if you use the third animal, the second dies due to the three-two pairing, and the first is permitted to graze, again because the second never functioned as a chatas. If, however, you use the middle animal — *nitkaper bamtza'is* — then in the one-two pairing the first was the substitute for the second, and in the two-three pairing the third was the substitute for the second; both therefore have the status of an unused substitute chatas, and *shteihan yamusu* — both must die.

Abaye disagrees entirely. His position is *kulu chalifin de-hadadi ninhu* — they are not simply pairs; everything was created to replace the original lost animal, and they are all constant replacements of one another. Therefore, regardless of which animal you use, the other two are unused substitute chatot and must both die.

### Abaye All Die

The Gemara asks: *mai ve-chein* — why was it necessary to state this whole second case about a chatas? The parallel to the todah is obvious. The answer is that one might have thought Rava's logic applies only to a todah, because by a todah there always exists the possibility of simply being makreiv a new todah — if you designate another animal without a standing obligation it may be considered a brand-new todah rather than a substitute. That is what allows Rava to say that certain animals, not being paired with an ultimately used todah, are treated as independent todot and therefore do bring bread. But by a chatas, *de-leika le-meimar marbe be-chataot* — you cannot simply multiply chatot — so perhaps Rava would have conceded to Abaye's position. The Gemara therefore teaches us — *ka mashma lan* — that Rava maintains his pairing logic even by a chatas: if the animal something is paired with never functioned as a chatas, then that animal never acquired the status of a substitute chatas and need not die; it is simply allowed to graze.

## Rabbi Chiya Mixed

### Why Repeat Chatas

With that clarification behind us, Rabbi Chiya introduces an entirely new and considerably more vexing case — one that will consume the remainder of this daf and the first amud of the next before the Mishna resumes. *tani rabbi chiya*: you have a todah and its tmura. One brings bread and one does not — a tmura retains only a derivative kedusha and never receives bread — but they have become mixed up, so you no longer know which is which. Then one of them dies. Now you are left with one animal of uncertain identity.

*Chavratah ein lah takanah* — the remaining animal has no solution. You cannot bring it with bread, because if it is the tmura, the bread would constitute chulin b'azarah. You cannot bring it without bread, because if it is the todah, a todah brought without bread does not fulfill the obligation.

### Chiya Case Intro

The Gemara asks: *heichi na'avid* — what should be done? Should you bring bread with it? Perhaps it is the tmura, in which case the bread is chulin, and the problem of chulin b'azarah arises. Should you not bring bread? Then if it is the todah, *dilma toda hi*, you fail to fulfill the obligation.

This is an opportunity for Tosafot to analyze when exactly something becomes chulin b'azarah. Tosafot explains that the prohibition is not triggered simply by carrying non-sanctified items into the azarah. Rather, chulin b'azarah applies when the item is being presented as part of a korban. What makes these loaves fall into that category? The answer leads directly to the next point.

### Chullin Ba'azarah

At this juncture the Gemara makes explicit something that had not been stated until now: four of the loaves that are given to the kohen — out of the forty total — are waved together with the todah, just as the breast and thigh of a shelamim are waved. Since those four loaves are involved in the tenufah ritual, they are being actively presented as part of the korban. Tosafot further assumes that even before the formal ritual of tenufah, the very act of bringing the loaves into the Mikdash and associating them with the korban frames them as being presented in a korban context. This is what makes bringing them conditionally — knowing that they might not be required — a chulin b'azarah problem.

One might ask: why not bring the animal and then say conditionally that if this is the todah these loaves are its bread, and if not, then this bread is merely stam — ordinary food — and no chulin b'azarah results? The problem is precisely that. If you did not sanctify the loaves, they are chulin, and you are presenting chulin as part of a korban. If you did sanctify them conditionally — saying if I need them they are lachmei todah, and if I do not they are not — then in the case where they are not needed, you have brought sanctified-conditionally-unsanctified bread into the azarah in the context of a korban, which is still a chulin b'azarah concern.

The Gemara is quite interested in this puzzle. The amoraim seem to have treated it almost as an engaging riddle — how can one possibly solve this problem?

## Rabbi Chiya Mixed…

### Chullin Defined

The Gemara now systematically considers a series of attempted solutions and rejects each one. It opens: *heichi na'avid* — what shall we do?

First: why not simply bring bread and state conditionally, *ei hach d'kayma toda hi* — if the surviving animal is the todah, this is its bread; *v'ei lo*, and if not, let the bread revert to being chulin? The response is immediate: *v'chi machnisin chulin l'azarah* — you may not bring chulin into the azarah! Presenting the bread as part of the korban while it might not be sanctified is exactly the chulin b'azarah problem.

The Gemara then introduces a more sophisticated solution through the phrase *amru lemadim lifnei Rebbi* — students said before Rebbi. They proposed: bring a new behemah along with bread, and stipulate as follows. If the surviving animal is the tmura, the new animal is the todah and the bread is its bread. If the surviving animal is the todah, the bread belongs to that original todah and the new animal — *tehei achrayus* — should be a backup korban. The logic is elegant: a backup, like a chalifei todah, does not require bread. So regardless of which way the uncertainty resolves, exactly one animal needs bread, and the bread is present.

Rebbi responds: *lo tzericha de'amar harei zo* — the case here is precisely one where the person only said *harei zo*, designating a specific animal, rather than *harei alai*, accepting a personal obligation. The concept of achrayus — of designating a backup animal — operates only within the framework of a personal obligation. If I have no personal obligation, there is nothing for the backup to back up. Since he said harei zo, not harei alai, the concept of achrayus is inapplicable and the solution collapses.

One might ask: why not then simply say harei alai, create a personal obligation, and use the achrayus structure? The Gemara will address this at the very end of today's discussion.

### Initial Solutions

The Gemara now considers another proposal. Bring a new behemah with bread and stipulate: if the surviving animal is the tmura, *harei zu todah v'ha lachmah* — the new animal is the todah with its bread. If the surviving animal is the todah, the bread goes to it, and *v'ha tehei shelamim* — let the new animal be a shelamim.

This has real appeal. Either the new animal is a todah with bread, or it is a shelamim, and either way everything is kadosh and the bread is associated with an appropriate korban. No chulin b'azarah problem arises. However, Rebbi rejects this as well: *mishum d'ka memayet b'achilah d'shelamim* — because you would be limiting the eating time of the shelamim. A todah is eaten only for one day; a shelamim for two. If you designate the new animal as a shelamim but must treat it as a possible todah to be safe, you will restrict its eating to one day, thereby reducing the allotted eating time and potentially creating notar. The principle is *ein mevi'in kodashim l'veit hapesul* — you do not bring a korban into a situation in which less than its full eating time is available.

A third proposal is then put forward, this time by Levi directly to Rebbi. Bring a behemah with bread, and stipulate: if the surviving animal is the tmura, the new one is the todah with its bread. If the surviving one is the todah, the bread goes to it, and *v'ha tehei motar d'todah* — let the new animal be the motar of the todah, its leftover, which per our Mishna does not receive bread.

### Conditional Bread

Rebbi's response to Levi is famous for its sharpness: *k'midumeh ani she-ein lo moach b'kadkado* — it appears to me that whoever would suggest such a thing has no brain in his skull. The rebuke is softened somewhat by the distancing formulation — Rebbi says "he," not "you" — but the point is clear. *v'chi mafrishin techilah l'mosarot* — you cannot designate something ab initio as a motar. Motar is a result, not a category of kedusha one can impose in advance. You cannot sanctify an animal with the kedusha of notar todah from the outset. And as noted, the concept of achrayus is also unavailable here because the vow was harei zo, not harei alai.

### Backup Rejected

The discussion now shifts to the amoraic period. Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta was sitting before Rav Nachman and proposed yet another solution: bring a behemah with bread and stipulate as follows. If the surviving animal is the tmura, the new one is the todah and the bread is its bread. If the surviving animal is the todah, the bread goes to that todah, and *v'ha tehei tmura* — let the new animal be a tmura of the original todah, which would not require bread.

Rav Nachman's reply is even sharper than Rebbi's: *ani mari, arba'im b'katfeih v'chasher* — my master, forty lashes on his back and he thinks this is permitted? The Torah explicitly prohibits making a tmura: *lo yachalifenu v'lo yamir oto*. The proposal is to solve a halachic problem by having the person violate a lav and incur lashes. That is no solution at all.

## Bread Conditional

### Rejection Chullin

The discussion continues among the amoraim. Rav Ila fell ill and Abaye and the rabanan came to pay a bikur cholim visit. As was customary, they sat and discussed Torah. One of them raised the following: *im ita l'de'Rabbi Yochanan* — if we rule like Rabbi Yochanan, who said *chutz l'chomat ha'azarah kadosh* — that the lachmei todah may be situated outside the wall of the azarah and still be considered kadosh — then the whole chulin b'azarah problem disappears. Earlier in the perek there had been a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish on exactly this point: does the bread that accompanies the todah need to be inside the Mikdash at the time of the offering, or may it be anywhere within Yerushalayim? Rabbi Yochanan held that it may be anywhere within Yerushalayim.

The proposed solution: bring the bread and leave it outside the azarah wall. Stipulate: if the surviving animal is the todah, this bread is its bread — sitting validly outside the azarah per Rabbi Yochanan. If the animal is not the todah and the bread is not needed, then *lo lichol aleih kedusha* — the kedusha simply does not take effect, and there is no chulin b'azarah problem because the bread was never inside the azarah. If the kedusha did take effect, one eats the bread within the day as befits lachmei todah, and all is well.

### Backup Suggested

The Gemara immediately identifies the flaw: *mishum d'ika arba'ah l'hanif* — because there are the four loaves that must be given to the kohen and waved as a tenufah together with the animal. This is what makes their status as part of the korban so acute. Those four loaves must be inside the Mikdash for the tenufah. If you do the tenufah outside — *linfinhu avrai* — this is invalid, because whenever tenufah is required it must be performed *lifnei Hashem*, before God, meaning inside. If you bring them inside — *l'gavai* — *ka mayti chulin l'azarah* — you are back to the chulin b'azarah problem, since those loaves might not be sanctified. *hilkach lo efshar* — therefore this solution cannot work either.

### Split Conditional

Rav Sheshet brei d'Rav Idi raises yet another approach. He invokes an earlier discussion in the perek about Chizkiyah, who held that *kidshu arba'im mitoch shmonim* — if you bring eighty loaves and explicitly designate forty of them as kadosh, that designation takes effect on forty out of the eighty. Tosafot notes that this is not disputed when stated explicitly; the earlier debate concerned only ambiguous cases.

The proposed solution: bring a new behemah and eighty loaves, and stipulate as follows. If the surviving animal is the todah, *ha nami tehvei todah* — let this new animal also be a todah, and these eighty loaves serve both of them, forty each. If the surviving animal is the tmura, the new animal is the todah, and again the eighty loaves accompany it with forty being kadosh. The other forty are *achrayus* — backup loaves — and are not chulin b'azarah, because bringing items that serve a functional purpose for the korban, as backups or supports, is analogous to the famous account of Hillel, who would bring his animal into the Mikdash before being makdish it — since it was brought as part of the korban process, it was not considered chulin b'azarah.

The Gemara's objection concerns the four loaves that must be given to the kohen. If there are potentially two todot, eight loaves must be given to the kohen. But if some of those loaves were not actually required — because only one todah was ultimately present — then you have limited whose hands they can be eaten in, restricting them to kohanim rather than allowing them to be eaten by any Israelite. The Gemara treats this, to some degree, as the concern about *ein mevi'in kodashim l'veit hapesul* in relation to the four loaves, though the precise formulation is debated in the grisaot — some versions read *d'arba'ah*, of the four, and some read differently. Either way the Gemara does not find this satisfactory.

It is worth noting that the Gemara seems less troubled by the concern of performing unnecessary rituals — the tenufah — with items that do not require it, than it is by chulin b'azarah. As long as everything is kadosh, performing a ritual with something that technically did not require that ritual is not treated as a comparable problem. The chulin b'azarah issue is what drives the analysis throughout.

### Time Limit Problem

Rav Ashi said to Rav Kahana: consider another earlier ruling. Rabbi Yochanan said: *hifrish chatas me'uberes veyalda, ratza bah mitkaper ratza bivladah mitkaper* — if someone designated a pregnant animal as his chatas and it gave birth, he may choose to use either the mother or the offspring for atonement. The kedusha attaches equally to both, since the animal was sanctified while pregnant. This is not a case of creating a substitute — no human act of temurah was performed — but the child naturally inherits the full kedusha of the mother.

By analogy, one might solve the todah problem as follows. The whole difficulty arose because the vow was harei zo rather than harei alai, so the concept of achrayus was unavailable. But you do not need achrayus here. Get a pregnant behemah, sanctify it as a todah, wait for it to give birth, bring eighty loaves, and stipulate as follows. If the surviving original animal is the tmura, then both the mother and the child are todot, and the eighty loaves serve both. If the surviving animal is the todah, the mother is also a todah, the eighty loaves go to the two todot, and the child is motar todah — the leftover of a todah — which does not bring bread.

The key insight is that unlike Levi's rejected proposal, you are not designating anything ab initio as motar todah. Rather, you are creating motar todah naturally through the birth process. And unlike the standard halacha where motar todah does not receive bread, Rabbi Yochanan's principle — *ratza bah mitkaper ratza bivladah mitkaper* — grants the owner the choice to treat the child as a full todah in its own right or to treat it as motar todah. That flexibility is precisely what makes the conditional stipulation viable.

### Levi Motar Proposed

A sharp question arises from the audience: what about oto v'es beno — the prohibition against slaughtering a mother and its offspring on the same day? If you are bringing the mother and the child together, you cannot sacrifice both on the same day.

The answer is clear: bring the mother on one day and bring the child together with the uncertain original animal on the next day. The bread functions properly throughout: the mother definitively requires forty loaves; on the second day, either the leftover is the todah and the child is the motar todah, with the forty loaves going to the todah, or the leftover is the tmura and the child is a full todah, with the loaves going to it. The two days separate the two slaughterings and the atonement vow for the chatas requires the animal to be within a year, whereas a todah has no upper age limit, so the mother's eligibility is not a concern.

However, the Gemara ultimately rejects even this solution. *man leima lan* — who told us that Rabbi Yochanan means *im shiyuro meshuyar*, that the owner has a genuine choice to designate both mother and child as full independent todot? Perhaps Rabbi Yochanan's intent is only that the child can be used to fulfill the obligation — functioning through *adam mitkaper b'shvach hekdesh*, being atoned through the enhanced value of the hekdesh — but if the child is used, the mother automatically becomes motar todah, rather than both being simultaneously equal. If so, Rabbi Yochanan's ruling simply means that one of them serves as the todah and the other is its motar, without the owner having a free choice to configure them as two distinct todot. That limited reading of Rabbi Yochanan's opinion destroys the solution, because we cannot then say that in either branch of the stipulation both animals could simultaneously carry the full kedusha of a todah.

### Rabbi Rejects

The sugya reaches its final proposed solution in the amoraic period. Ravina came to Mahuza, where Rav Dimi bereih de-Rav Huna of Mahuza posed the following to him. The entire problem, he noted, stems from the fact that the vow was harei zo rather than harei alai — because without a personal obligation, the concept of achrayus is unavailable. So why not simply create a personal obligation? Say harei alai, take a new neder for a todah, bring two new animals and eighty loaves, and stipulate as follows. If the surviving original animal is the tmura, these two new animals are both todot, and the eighty loaves serve them. If the surviving original animal is the todah, then the first new animal — sanctified by harei alai — is also a todah, with the forty loaves going to the original and forty to the new one; and the third animal, designated conditionally, is an achrayus — a backup — which does not require bread.

This is structurally clean: three animals, but regardless of how the uncertainty resolves, exactly two require bread and one does not, and the eighty loaves cover the two that need them.

Ravina's response invokes the verse from Koheles: *tov asher lo sidor mi-she-tidor ve-lo teshalem* — better that you not make a vow than that you make one and fail to fulfill it. The Torah discourages making unnecessary nedarim. And you are proposing that this person should stand up and take an additional neder simply to solve a technical halachic impasse?

Tosafot acknowledges that in some circumstances — for example, where failing to solve the problem would permanently prevent a nezir from completing his nazirite term and bringing his korbanos — we might permit and even encourage a creative neder to resolve the situation. But here, the consequence of leaving the problem unsolved is merely that one animal remains in an uncertain state: it cannot be brought, and so it grazes until it develops a mum, is redeemed, and the money goes to הדיוט purposes. That outcome is not so severe as to justify encouraging a neder. And so the Gemara leaves Rabbi Chiya's case without a clean resolution.

It is striking that of all the proposed solutions, this last one is in some ways the most natural — and the Gemara's objection to it is somewhat unusual, given that the Gemara throughout the entire Talmud routinely assumes that people take nedarim and never once protests the practice the way it does here. But perhaps, as the discussion suggests, the Gemara was reluctant to allow what had been an engaging halachic puzzle to be resolved by so simple a measure as an ad hoc neder.

## Backup Without Obligation

### Temura Proposed

With the extended discussion of Rabbi Chiya's case complete, the Gemara now turns to the next Mishna. The Mishna states: *ha'omer harei alai todah* — one who takes an obligation to bring a todah — *yavi hi ve-lachmah min ha-chullin* — must bring both the animal and its bread from non-sanctified money. This stands in contrast to ma'aser sheni. One might think that since ma'aser sheni funds are designated to buy food and eat it in Yerushalayim — which is precisely what a todah and its bread are — one could use ma'aser sheni money to fund the todah. The Mishna rules otherwise: the principle is *ein mevi'im davar min ha-chovah mima'os ma'aser sheni* — you cannot discharge a personal obligation using ma'aser sheni funds. You may only use such funds for non-obligatory korbanot.

This principle carries significant weight for certain poskim when addressing the contemporary question of ma'aser kesafim — whether one may use money set aside as ma'aser to pay for obligations such as children's tuition or shul membership. The extent to which ma'aser kesafim is modeled on ma'aser sheni — and specifically on the prohibition of using it to discharge pre-existing obligations — varies among poskim, but the present principle is a key source in that discussion.

### Temura Violation

The Mishna continues with variations. If one says *todah alai min ha-chullin ve-lachmah min ha-ma'aser* — I obligate myself to bring the todah from chullin but the bread from ma'aser — the stipulation is ineffective and both must come from chullin: *yavi hi ve-lachmah min ha-chullin*. The reason is that the todah is the ikar and the bread follows its status, as we established yesterday. Since the todah must come from chullin, the bread must as well, regardless of what the owner specified about the bread.

Conversely, if one says *todah min ha-ma'aser ve-lachmah min ha-chullin* — the todah from ma'aser and the bread from chullin — this works. But this formulation is best understood as one where the person never took a personal obligation in the first place. He is not saying *harei alai*, creating a chiyuv; he is saying, in effect, "Let this ma'aser money be used for a todah," investing it with the kedusha of a todah without a standing personal obligation. Since there is no personal obligation being discharged, the prohibition of using ma'aser funds for chovah does not apply.

If one says *hi ve-lachmah min ha-ma'aser* — both the animal and the bread from ma'aser — then one brings exactly as stated, since that is fully permitted in the scenario where there is no personal obligation.

Furthermore, the Mishna notes that even when use of ma'aser sheni funds for the bread is permitted, *lo yavi mechittei ma'aser sheni* — one should not use actual wheat of ma'aser sheni to bake the loaves; rather, one uses money of ma'aser sheni — *mimot ma'aser sheni* — to purchase the bread. The reason for this distinction will be explained in the Gemara.

### Outside Bread Idea

Rav Huna opens the Gemara on this Mishna with a related ruling: *ha'omer harei alai lachmei todah* — one who says I obligate myself to bring bread of a todah. What is the effect of such a declaration? The Gemara answers: *mevi todah ve-lachmah* — he must bring the todah with its bread. He cannot bring the bread alone, because everyone knows that lachmei todah without a todah is not a coherent entity. Therefore, when he said "lachmei todah," he must have meant the todah with its bread. The Gemara adds: *vehai todah ve-lachmah ka'amar* — by saying "bread of a todah," he was in effect articulating the complete obligation, since speaking of the end product — the bread — encompasses the primary item upon which it depends. *sof milta nakat* — he described the terminal component, but the whole package is implied.

### Four Loaves Inside

The Gemara then examines the internal logic of the Mishna's various stipulations. The Mishna taught that if one says *todah min ha-ma'aser ve-lachem min ha-chullin*, one brings as stipulated. One might ask: since the word "bread" can sometimes implicitly encompass "bread together with the todah" — as Rav Huna taught — why when someone says the bread is from chullin does that not extend to mean both the bread and the todah must come from chullin?

The Gemara resolves this: *shani hasam* — that case is different. Once he already said "the todah is from ma'aser," by the time he reaches the clause about the bread, the todah obligation has already been separately articulated. The subsequent mention of bread is therefore understood as referring only to the bread component, in the manner of someone who says "I will bring bread to discharge the todah obligation of so-and-so" — *harei alai lachem liftor todato shel ploni* — meaning the bread alone, not the whole package.

The Gemara then asks: by the same logic, when one says *todah min ha-chullin ve-lachem min ha-ma'aser*, why should the todah clause contaminate the bread clause? He already said the todah comes from chullin — could we not treat the todah clause as referring only to the animal, allowing the bread to remain from ma'aser as stipulated? The Gemara dismisses this: *bishlama lachem le-miftar todah ati* — bread is naturally secondary and functions to fulfill an obligation created by a standing todah. But *todah le-miftar lachem mi ati* — you would never frame the todah as secondary and the bread as primary. The todah is structurally the ikar, and you cannot read the declaration as if the todah were a subordinate element accompanying independently committed bread.

### Eighty Proposed

A baraita is cited: *ha'omer harei alai todah belo lachem ve-zevach belo nesachim, kofin oto mevi todah ve-lachmah, zevach u-nesacheiha* — if someone declares he will bring a todah without bread or a zevach without nesachim, we compel him to bring the complete package. No korban can be brought with its attendant obligations stripped away by stipulation; this is matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah.

One might infer from this baraita that the obligation to bring bread exists only because the person said the word "todah" — and that therefore if one had said only "bread" without mentioning "todah," one would not be obligated to bring the todah. The Gemara rejects this inference: *hu ha-din af al gav de-lo amar todah* — even if one had said only "bread," one would be obligated to bring the todah with it. The reason the baraita mentions the todah formulation is structural. The baraita needed to pair the todah-without-bread case with the zevach-without-nesachim case. It could not formulate the latter as "nesachim without a zevach," because nesachim may in certain contexts be brought independently, and therefore a declaration of nesachim alone would not imply a zevach. The parallel construction required mentioning the todah explicitly, but this does not mean that saying only "bread" would be legally different. The Gemara's conclusion on both sides is the same: the todah and its bread are a unified whole, and neither can be taken without the other.

The Gemara will continue tomorrow with the substantive analysis of the ma'aser sheni provisions that form the heart of this Mishna.
