# Sukkah 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Former Vessels

### Bed Legs & Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי בַּר טַבְיוֹמֵי: סִכְּכָהּ בִּבְלָאֵי כֵלִים פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss **the** matter **itself** cited above. **Rabbi Ami bar Tavyomei said: If one roofed** the *sukka* **with worn,** incomplete, **vessels,** the *sukka* is **unfit.**

The *gemara* picks up with *gufa* — returning to the original statement under analysis — six lines from the top of the *daf*. The background to this discussion is the *mishna*'s reference to using the legs of a bed as *schach*, which the *mishna* appeared to assume were susceptible to *tumah*. The *gemara* had raised the difficulty: how can detached bed legs be *mekabel tumah*? They are no longer part of a vessel; they are merely wood, and wood requires a receptacle-form to be susceptible. The *gemara* connected this to a debate found in a *mishna* in *Kelim* concerning a disassembled or partially broken bed. One opinion there holds that even a partially disassembled bed can still become *tamei*, because it is destined to be reassembled — and as *Tosafot* notes, because it retains some functional use in its current state. This would apply to the case of half a bed with two legs still attached.

That explanation, however, is set aside. The *gemara* proposes a fundamentally different approach: the bed legs no longer can become *tamei*, having been detached and rendered non-functional as a vessel. But perhaps, precisely because they *once were* a vessel, they remain invalid as *schach* — permanently labeled by their history. The *gemara* now pursues this question directly.

### Worn-Out Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בְּלָאֵי כֵלִים? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַטְלָנִיּוֹת שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶם שָׁלֹשׁ עַל שָׁלֹשׁ, דְּלָא חַזְיָין לֹא לַעֲנִיִּים וְלֹא לַעֲשִׁירִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are** these **worn vessels? Abaye said:** They are small **cloths that do not have** an area of **three by three** fingerbreadths, **which,** due to their size, **are not suited** for use **either by the poor or by the wealthy.**

*Gufa* — returning to the original statement: Rav Ami bar Tavyumi teaches that if one used *bivlei kelim* — worn-out vessels or worn-out clothing — as *schach*, the *sukkah* is invalid. Even though these items are no longer *halachically* considered vessels or garments, having deteriorated past any measure of usability and therefore no longer susceptible to *tumah*, they were once vessels, once clothing — and that history disqualifies them from serving as *schach*.

### Cloth Size Measures

Abaye clarifies what is meant by *bivlei kelim*: pieces of cloth (*matlaniyot*) that are less than three finger-widths by three finger-widths, and which are not fit for use either by wealthy people or by the poor. This requires understanding the *gemara*'s three-tier framework for cloth size.

The largest threshold is *shlosha al shlosha tefachim* — three *tefachim* by three *tefachim*, roughly nine inches by nine inches. A wealthy person discards anything smaller than this; for that person, such a fragment has ceased to function as clothing. A poor person, however, will still use smaller pieces for patching garments. The lower threshold is *shalosh al shalosh* — three finger-widths by three finger-widths, roughly an inch and a half by an inch and a half. Anything smaller than this, nobody uses — not the wealthy, not the poor. Such a piece is beneath any standard of utility.

The *gemara* exploits the gendered grammar of Hebrew to mark this distinction precisely: *tefach* is masculine, so "three *tefachim*" is *shlosha* — the number taking its feminine form in agreement with the masculine noun, per the grammatical inversion. *Shalosh al shalosh*, with the feminine numeral, refers to fingers, a different unit entirely. The notation is not incidental; it encodes the distinction between the two thresholds.

The *halacha* for *schach* follows from this: even once a piece of cloth has fallen below the smallest threshold — below *shalosh al shalosh* finger-widths — so that literally no one would use it and it is not *mekabel tumah* under any measure, it remains *pasul* for *schach* because it originated as a *keli*, as usable clothing. This has immediate practical application: if one were to gather wood from a discarded broken table left out on the street before *Sukkot*, that wood would in fact be problematic as *schach* under this teaching.

### Sedge Mat Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי בַּר טַבְיוֹמֵי: מַחְצֶלֶת שֶׁל שִׁיפָא וְשֶׁל גֶּמִי, שְׁיָרֶיהָ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּפְחֲתוּ מִכְּשִׁיעוּרָהּ — אֵין מְסַכְּכִין בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami bar Tavyomei:** In the case of **a mat** made **of** different types of vegetation, e.g., **papyrus and reed grass, even though its remnants were reduced from** the requisite **measure** for contracting ritual impurity, **one** may **not roof** the *sukka* **with them.**

The *gemara* now brings a *baraita* that runs parallel to the ruling of Rav Ami bar Tavyumi. If one has a *machatzelet* — a mat woven from *shaifa*, a type of sedge or rush, papyrus or reed grass — and what remains is a remnant smaller than the standard *shiur*, that remnant is invalid as *schach*. The relevant *shiur* here is not a *shiur* for *schach* itself, but the *shiur* for *tumah*: a mat must be at least six *tefachim* by six *tefachim* — roughly a foot and a half by a foot and a half — to be considered a functional vessel susceptible to *tumah*. Once the mat has broken down to a piece smaller than this, it is no longer *mekabel tumah* and is no longer classified as a vessel. Nevertheless, *ein mesachachin bahem* — one may not use it as *schach*, because it originated from something that once had the status of a vessel. The same principle that applies to worn-out clothing applies equally to worn-out mats.

### Reed Mat Sizes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַחְצֶלֶת הַקָּנִים, גְּדוֹלָה — מְסַכְּכִין בָּהּ, קְטַנָּה, — אֵין מְסַכְּכִין בָּהּ. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַף הִיא מְקַבֶּלֶת טוּמְאָה, וְאֵין מְסַכְּכִין בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If **a mat of reeds** is **large** and not designated for sleeping, but is suited only for roofing, **one** may **roof** the *sukka* **with it.** However, the status of **a small** mat, which can be utilized for sleeping, is that of a vessel, and **one** may **not roof** the *sukka* **with it. Rabbi Eliezer says:** The status of **even** a large mat is that of a vessel. It **is capable of contracting ritual impurity, and** therefore **one** may **not roof** his *sukka* **with it.**

The *gemara* turns to a related but structurally different case. A mat made from reeds or sticks: *gedolah mesachachin bah* — a large one may be used as *schach*; *ketanah ein mesachachin bah* — a small one may not. This is, at first glance, counterintuitive. Everything discussed until now has followed the logic that the larger the item, the more it functions as a vessel, the more it is *mekabel tumah*, and therefore the more it is disqualified as *schach*. Here the relationship is inverted.

The resolution lies in a distinction about functional identity. A small reed mat — large enough for one person to sit on — is classified as a sitting mat, a *keli* designed for human use, and is therefore *mekabel tumah*. A very large reed mat is not used for sitting; people use it as a wall covering, a roofing material, or a partition. It ceases to be a *keli* in the relevant sense and becomes a structural material — not *mekabel tumah*, and therefore *kosher* as *schach*. The greater its size, the more clearly it is identified as something other than a vessel. This point is directly relevant to contemporary practice: the bamboo *schach* mats now sold commercially, or the type sold in home improvement stores, raise precisely this question — are they sitting mats (*mekabel tumah*) or structural coverings? The *gemara* resolves the question by size.

Rabbi Eliezer dissents. Even a large reed mat, he says, remains *mekabel tumah* — people do in fact use them for sitting, and therefore even the large ones cannot be used as *schach*. This debate will be developed further in the *gemara* in subsequent days.

The bottom line established by this section for the *hilkhot schach*: once something has been a vessel, it retains its disqualification from serving as *schach* even after it no longer meets the threshold for *tumah* susceptibility.

## Haystack Sukkah

### Rav Huna's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַחוֹטֵט בְּגָדִישׁ. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין שָׁם חָלָל טֶפַח בְּמֶשֶׁךְ שִׁבְעָה, אֲבָל יֵשׁ שָׁם חָלָל טֶפַח בְּמֶשֶׁךְ שִׁבְעָה — הֲרֵי זֹה סוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: In the case of **one who hollows out** and creates a space inside **a stack of grain,** it is not a *sukka*. **Rav Huna said:** The Sages **taught** that it is not a *sukka* **only** in a case **where there is not a space one handbreadth** high **along seven** handbreadths upon which the grain was piled. **However, if there is a space** measuring **one handbreadth** high **along seven** handbreadths upon which the grain was piled, and now, by hollowing out the stack, one is raising the existing walls and not forming a new space, **it is** a fit ***sukka*.**

The *gemara* now moves to the second part of the *mishna*: the case of one who hollows out a cavity inside a haystack in order to create a *sukkah*. The structure yields walls and a roof made of hay, which in principle should be valid *schach*. The problem, however, is *taaseh v'lo min ha'asuy*: the *sukkah* — meaning specifically the *schach* — must be made through a direct act. Here the roof was created not by any positive act of placing *schach* but indirectly, as a consequence of removing the interior of the haystack. It was, in a sense, pre-made by the removal rather than made directly.

Rav Huna resolves the problem: *lo shanu* — this was only taught to be invalid in the standard case, *ela she'ein sham challal tefach bemeshech shivah* — where there was no pre-existing cavity of one *tefach* in height extending over an area of seven by seven *tefachim*, the minimum dimensions of a *sukkah*. But *aval yesh sham challal tefach bemeshech shivah* — if there was already such a cavity, *harei zu sukkah* — it is valid.

What does this mean in practice? The ordinary case is a solid haystack that one hollows out entirely, creating the *schach* only through the act of removal. But suppose the haystack was initially piled on top of some kind of platform, so that there was already a cavity of seven by seven *tefachim* in area and one *tefach* in height at the outset, before any digging. In that case, when the hay was first placed over the cavity, an act of making *schach* was performed: one placed hay over a space of *sukkah* dimensions. The deficiency at that point was only in wall height — the cavity was only one *tefach* high, not ten *tefachim*. But that is a wall problem, not a *schach* problem. Kosher *schach* was established at the moment the hay was placed over the one-*tefach*-high space, because a *tefach* of height already constitutes an *ohel* — a tent — for purposes of *tumah* law and, by extension, for establishing *schach* identity. The subsequent digging to create the full ten *tefachim* of height merely completes the walls; it does not create the *schach* anew.

### Mishna/Baraita Conflict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַחוֹטֵט בְּגָדִישׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ סוּכָּה — הֲרֵי זֹה סוּכָּה. וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן אֵינָהּ סוּכָּה! אֶלָּא לָאו, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כִּדְרַב הוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **That is also taught** in a *baraita*: **One who hollows out a stack of grain to make himself a *sukka*, it is a *sukka*.** The Gemara wonders: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **it is not a *sukka*? Rather, is it not** correct to **conclude from it, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Huna,** that in certain circumstances it is possible to hollow out a stack of grain and establish a fit *sukka*? The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that this is the case.

The *gemara* notes that this resolution is confirmed independently. *Tanya nami hachi* — a *baraita* teaches the very same thing, stating that one who hollows out a haystack (*ha-chotet begadish la'asot lo sukkah*) has a valid *sukkah*. But this seems to contradict the *mishna*, which rules it invalid. Rather than treating this as a *machloket*, the *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *ela lav shema mina keRav Huna* — the *baraita* that validates it is discussing the case with a pre-existing cavity of one *tefach* over seven by seven *tefachim*, while the *mishna* that invalidates it is discussing the ordinary case without any such pre-existing cavity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּרָמֵי לַיהּ מִירְמֵא. תְּנַן: הַחוֹטֵט בְּגָדִישׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ סוּכָּה — אֵינָהּ סוּכָּה. וְהָא תַּנְיָא: הֲרֵי זוֹ סוּכָּה! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁם חָלָל טֶפַח בְּמֶשֶׁךְ שִׁבְעָה, כָּאן בְּשֶׁאֵין שָׁם חָלָל טֶפַח בְּמֶשֶׁךְ שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Some raised** this matter as **a contradiction** between the mishna and the *baraita*. **We learned** in the mishna: **One who hollows out a stack of grain in order to make himself a *sukka*, it is not a *sukka*. But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **this is a *sukka*? Rav Huna said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** where it is a *sukka*, it is a case **where there is a space** measuring **one handbreadth** high **along seven** handbreadths, while **there,** where it is not a *sukka*, it is a case **where there is not a space one handbreadth** high **along seven** handbreadths.

Some transmit this slightly differently: the statement of Rav Huna began not as a commentary on the *mishna* but as a direct resolution of the contradiction between the *mishna* and the *baraita*. The substance, however, is identical. *Lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. *Kan sheyesh sham challal tefach bemeshech shivah* — the *baraita* speaks where there was such a cavity, *kan she'ein sham challal bemeshech shivah* — the *mishna* speaks where there was none. *Shema mina* — this is a solid proof.

### Tosafot Analysis

*Tosafot* observes a beautiful instance of *leshitateh* — a *amora* holding a consistent position across different contexts. Four lines down in the wide text of *Tosafot*, he notes: *mashkachat lemeimar Rav Huna leta'ameih* — one can say that Rav Huna here follows the same reasoning he articulated earlier in the *gemara*, approximately six days earlier in the *daf yomi* cycle, in the context of *bein sukkah lesukkah tefach* — the question of one *sukkah* placed beneath another. That discussion concerned the minimum height by which the upper *schach* must be separated from the lower *schach* in order to be identified as an independent *sukkah* in its own right. Rav Huna ruled there that once the upper *schach* is one *tefach* higher, it already acquires the independent identity of *schach*. The same principle operates here: a one-*tefach* space already has the identity of *schach* — it is not yet a *kosher sukkah* due to insufficient wall height, but it has established the requisite identity of *schach*. Once Rav Huna makes the walls ten *tefachim* the *sukkah* is complete.

*Tosafot* further notes: *veshama man de'amar hatam arba hacha modeh* — it is possible that an opinion that requires four *tefachim* of separation in the *sukkah-under-sukkah* case might still agree with Rav Huna here, because the considerations differ. As for Shmuel, who requires ten *tefachim* of separation in the *sukkah-under-sukkah* case — *Tosafot* reasons that Shmuel's stringency there derives from his view that one needs a full *sukkah* beneath a *sukkah*, not merely *schach* beneath *schach*. In the present case, the question is only about *schach* identity, and so Shmuel might well agree. The connecting thread is the same Rav Huna who said that one *tefach* of separation yields *schach* identity also says here that one *tefach* of height over a *sukkah*-sized area constitutes the making of *schach*.

### Schach Before Walls

This ruling raises a directly practical question that is debated among the *acharonim*: if one places *kosher schach* on the frame of a *sukkah* before erecting the walls, does *taaseh v'lo min ha'asuy* apply? Was the *schach* "made" before the *sukkah* was a *sukkah*?

The present *gemara* strongly suggests that this is permissible. The case here is nearly identical: *kosher schach* was established when the walls were only one *tefach* high, and the walls were then completed to ten *tefachim* afterward. The *schach* was not re-laid; it was considered valid as it was, and the subsequent wall construction completed the *sukkah*. As *Rashi* analogizes, this parallels the earlier *gemara* concerning a nine-*tefach*-high *sukkah* where one digs a hole in the floor to achieve the required ten *tefachim* — there too, the *schach* was kosher and the walls were completed afterward. The implication is that *schach* placed before fully kosher walls are in place does not require the ritual of lifting and re-laying; it was placed *kosher* at the time of placing, and the subsequent wall construction does not retroactively impair it.

One might distinguish: here there was at least a one-*tefach* wall at the time the *schach* was placed, whereas if one places *schach* on a bare frame there are no walls at all yet. But the straightforward reading of this *gemara* suggests the principle is sound regardless — *schach* may precede fully completed walls.

A separate question was raised concerning whether the haystack's hay would qualify as *schach* at all, given that very dense *schach* that does not allow rain through is considered a roof rather than *schach*. *Tosafot* addressed this in an earlier passage, ruling that the relevant criterion is not whether sunlight penetrates but whether rain penetrates. A haystack, it would seem, does allow rain through, and therefore the hay can qualify as *schach*.

## Wall Heights

### Top-Down Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְשַׁלְשֵׁל דְּפָנוֹת מִלְמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה, אִם גָּבוֹהַּ מִן הָאָרֶץ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים — פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who lowers the walls** of the *sukka* **from up downward, if** the lower edge of the wall is **three handbreadths above the ground,** the *sukka* is **unfit.**

The *gemara* turns to the next *mishna*, which addresses the height requirements for *sukkah* walls constructed in different ways. *Hamshalshel dfanot* — one who lowers walls downward, meaning one who hangs a curtain or partition starting from near the top of the *schach* and letting it drop toward the ground: *im gavoha min ha'aretz shlosha tefachim pesulah* — if the bottom edge of the wall is three or more *tefachim* above the ground, the *sukkah* is invalid. The reason is the principle of *lavud*: within less than three *tefachim*, a gap is considered legally closed — the two surfaces are treated as if joined. Three *tefachim* or more cannot be bridged by *lavud*, and the wall is no longer considered attached to the ground. There is also a related *mechitza* rationale: a gap of three *tefachim* or more allows goats to pass beneath, and something that cannot stop even animals from passing under it cannot be called a wall. But most simply, it is the application of *lavud*.

### Bottom-Up Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִלְּמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה, אִם גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** However, if one constructs the wall from **down upward, if** the wall is **ten handbreadths high,** even if it does not reach the roofing, the *sukka* is **fit.**

Building a wall from the ground up: *im gavoah asarah tefachim kosher* — once the wall reaches ten *tefachim*, it is valid even if it does not reach the *schach*. This might seem to require explanation, since in principle a *sukkah* wall should be adjacent to the *schach*. The answer involves the principle of *gud asik* — but first, one might have proposed a simpler reason: the wall is physically below the *schach*, not outside it, and perhaps mere presence beneath the *schach* suffices. The more precise answer, however, is *gud asik mechitzta* — once a wall achieves the status of a valid *mechitza*, we envision it as extending upward indefinitely, closing the gap between its top and the *schach*. The wall does not have to physically touch; its legal extension reaches the *schach*.

### Magic Wall Extension

The *gemara* introduces the two complementary principles of *gud achis* and *gud asik* — what the lecturer characterizes as the "magic extending walls" of *sukkah* law, analogous to the "magic bending wall" of *dofen akuma*. *Gud achis mechitzta* means we pull the wall downward; *gud asik* means we extend it upward. The word *gud* is glossed by *Rashi* as meaning to stretch, though it also carries a connotation of wall.

The logic of the *mishna* now becomes clear: once a wall is a valid *mechitza* — which requires it to be attached to the ground and ten *tefachim* high — *gud asik* extends it up to the *schach*, and there is no deficiency. The wall functions completely. One who grasps this will immediately ask: if we can use *gud asik* to extend a bottom-up wall to the *schach*, why can we not use *gud achis* to extend a top-down wall down to the ground, even when it is more than three *tefachim* away? The *mishna* already tells us the answer — the rabbis say no — and the next opinion challenges that.

### Rabbi Yose Two Ways

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמִּלְּמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, כָּךְ מִלְמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: Just as** a wall built **from down upward** must be **ten handbreadths, so too,** in a case where one lowers the wall **from up downward,** it must be **ten handbreadths** in length. Regardless of its height off the ground, it is the wall of a fit *sukka*, as the legal status of a ten-handbreadth partition is that of a full-fledged partition in all areas of *halakha*.

Rabbi Yose says: *kesheim she'mimata lema'ala asarah tefachim kosher, kach mimaala lemata asarah tefachim kosher* — just as ten *tefachim* of wall from the ground upward is sufficient without reaching the *schach*, so too ten *tefachim* of wall from above downward is sufficient without reaching the ground. Rabbi Yose holds that any ten-*tefach* span in a vertical plane constitutes a *mechitza*, and therefore even a wall suspended in mid-air can be extended downward by *gud achis* to reach the ground.

The rabbis disagree. Their view is that the fundamental concept of a *mechitza* is a wall at ground level. A suspended wall in mid-air, unconnected to the ground, never acquires the identity of a *mechitza* in the first place. *Gud asik* can operate upward once a wall is grounded, because it is extending an established *mechitza*. But there is no base identity to extend downward from a hanging curtain. One might further note that this implies an interesting question according to Rabbi Yose: if ten *tefachim* of wall anywhere in the vertical plane is a *mechitza*, could one extend it both up and down simultaneously? That will be addressed when the *gemara* returns to the question.

## Suspended Walls

### Well Boundary Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר מְחִיצָה תְּלוּיָה מַתֶּרֶת, וּמַר סָבַר מְחִיצָה תְּלוּיָה אֵינָהּ מַתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle do Rabbi Yosei and the Rabbis **disagree?** The Gemara explains: One **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that **a suspended partition,** even if it does not reach all the way down, **renders it permitted** to carry on Shabbat, like a full-fledged partition. **And** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **a suspended partition does not render it permitted** to carry on Shabbat.

The *gemara* now asks: *bimai kamipligi* — what exactly is the conceptual dispute between the rabbis and Rabbi Yose? The *gemara* frames it in terms borrowed from *hilkhot Shabbat*: *mar savar mechitza teluyah materet* — one holds that a suspended *mechitza* makes the space it encloses permissible, and *umar savar mechitza teluyah einah materet* — the other holds it does not. The word *materet* — permissible — is the language of *Shabbat* law, where a *mechitza* of ten *tefachim* creates a *reshut hayachid*, a private domain, permitting carrying within it. The *gemara* is thus assuming that the debate about suspended walls is the same debate whether it arises in the context of *sukkah* or *Shabbat*.

To explore this, the *gemara* cites a *mishna* in *Eruvin*: if there is a well situated between two adjacent courtyards, each of which has made its own *eruv* separately, one may not draw water from the well on *Shabbat* — *ein memale'in mimenu beShabbat*. The situation is as follows: two courtyards share a common area, each is a *reshut hayachid* in its own right, and the well sits on the boundary between them. Drawing water from the shared well means taking from the domain of one courtyard into the other, which is prohibited since they have not combined their *eruvin*. Both courtyards are *reshut hayachid*; carrying between two separate *reshuyot hayachid* that have not made a joint *eruv* is a *de-rabbanan* prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: בּוֹר שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת — אֵין מְמַלְּאִין מִמֶּנָּה בְּשַׁבָּת אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן עָשָׂה לָהּ מְחִיצָה עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, בֵּין מִלְמַעְלָה בֵּין מִלְּמַטָּה, בֵּין בְּתוֹךְ אוֹגְנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there,** in tractate *Eiruvin*: In the case of **a cistern that is** located **between two courtyards,** situated partly in each courtyard, **one may draw** water **from it on Shabbat only** if **a partition ten handbreadths high was erected** specifically **for** the cistern to separate the water between the domains, lest the residents of one courtyard draw water from the domain of the other courtyard. This partition is effective **whether it is above,** and lowered toward the water; **whether** it is **below,** in the water; **or whether it is within the** airspace of the cistern below the **rim,** above the surface of the water.

*Ela im kein* — unless one constructed a *mechitza* of ten *tefachim* within the well itself, whether positioned near the top of the well or near the bottom, or right beneath the lip — then access to the water is permitted.

### Water Leniency

The *mishna*'s ruling raises an obvious question: why does a wall positioned inside the well permit drawing water, while the ordinary wall already running between the two courtyards above the well does not? In either case — whether the wall is inside the well near the top or near the bottom — the wall is not physically at the water level; in both cases we would be saying we envision the wall as extending all the way down to the water. What, then, is the practical difference?

The answer lies in a principle specific to water access. The *gemara* articulates this as *kalu hu shehetiru chachamim bamayim* — a leniency that the Sages instituted specifically to preserve access to water. Water was not a convenience in the world of *Chazal*; access to water was a critical necessity. Accordingly, the Sages permitted certain applications of the principle of envisioning walls as extending downward — specifically in contexts where a *mechitza* was constructed expressly to enable access to water. A wall built inside the well, within the well itself, is built specifically for that purpose, and therefore the water leniency applies: we envision it as extending to the water. The wall running above the well between the courtyards was not constructed for that purpose, and so the special leniency does not apply to it.

In practical terms, there is an analogous case: a person on a balcony or ship adjacent to a body of water may construct a small enclosure on the balcony and lower a bucket through it to draw water, because the enclosure is built specifically to enable water access, and we envision the walls as extending down to the water, making the water-drawing a transfer within a single *reshut hayachid*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים מִלְמַעְלָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים מִלְּמַטָּה. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא תְּהֵא מְחִיצָה גְּדוֹלָה מִן הַכּוֹתֶל שֶׁבֵּינֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says** that this is the subject of an early dispute of *tanna'im*. **Beit Shammai say:** The partition that permits drawing water may be placed **below; and Beit Hillel said** it must be placed **above. Rabbi Yehuda said: A partition** for the cistern **should be no more** stringent **than the wall** serving as a partition **between** the two courtyards.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel frames the question differently, presenting it as a dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*: *Beit Shammai omrim milma'ala* — the wall may be near the top of the well, *uBeit Hillel omrim milmata* — it must be near the water. *Tosafot* notes that in the manuscripts of *Eruvin*, the *girsaot* read the positions in reverse — *Beit Shammai* as the *machmir* requiring the wall near the water, and *Beit Hillel* as the *meikil* allowing it near the top — which would be the more expected alignment with their usual tendencies. The reading in *Sukkah* chapter 109 of *Shabbat* presents it differently, and *Tosafot* observes the discrepancy across the texts.

### Rav Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא תְּהֵא מְחִיצָה גְּדוֹלָה מִן הַכּוֹתֶל שֶׁבֵּינֵיהֶן. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּשִׁיטַת רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אֲמָרָהּ, דְּאָמַר מְחִיצָה תְּלוּיָה מַתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: A partition** for the cistern **should be no more** stringent **than the wall** serving as a partition **between** the two courtyards. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Rabbi Yehuda stated** his opinion **in** accordance with **the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, who said** that **a suspended partition permits** one to carry, and therefore the wall between the courtyards suffices to divide the cistern as well.

Now comes the statement that is directly relevant to the debate in our *mishna*. Rav Yehuda says: *lo tehei mechitza gedolah min hakosel shebeinehem* — should this wall inside the well have more legal force than the great wall that runs between the courtyards above? If we permit access to the well on the basis of the interior wall, ought we not be able to permit it on the basis of the already-existing exterior wall above?

Rav Yehuda is thus asserting that the exterior wall can serve to extend downward and create a *reshut hayachid* in the water — not as a water leniency, but as a general principle. A suspended wall can, as a matter of general *halacha*, be extended downward. This positions Rav Yehuda as holding that *mechitza teluyah materet* as a general rule, not merely as a water-specific dispensation.

### Cross-Context Debate

Rav Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan notes: *Rav Yehuda beshitat Rabbi Yose amra* — Rav Yehuda's position in *Eruvin* corresponds precisely to Rabbi Yose's position in our *mishna* in *Sukkah*. Both hold that a suspended *mechitza* is legally effective — Rav Yehuda in the context of *Shabbat* and carrying, Rabbi Yose in the context of *sukkah* walls. Since the underlying conceptual position is the same — *mechitza teluyah materet* — the two rulings should reflect the same view.

### Rava's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא, לָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, וְלָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. לָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הָתָם, אֶלָּא בְּעֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת דְּרַבָּנַן. אֲבָל הָכָא, סוּכָּה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this equation. **And that is not so,** as **neither does Rabbi Yehuda hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, nor does Rabbi Yosei hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.** The Gemara elaborates: **Neither does Rabbi Yehuda hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, as Rabbi Yehuda states** his opinion that a suspended partition suffices **only there, with** regard to **the joining of the courtyards,** which is an obligation **by rabbinic** law. **However, here,** with regard to ***sukka*,** which is **by Torah** law, a suspended partition does **not** suffice.

The *gemara* says *velo hi* — this is not necessarily so. The *gemara* resists the equivalence and holds that one might accept the principle for *Shabbat* and not for *sukkah*, or for *sukkah* and not for *Shabbat*.

The reasoning: *lo Rav Yehuda savar lah keRabbi Yose* — Rav Yehuda would not necessarily hold with Rabbi Yose in *sukkah*. *Ad kan lo ka'amar Rav Yehuda hatam ela be'eruvei chatzeirot de-rabbanan* — Rav Yehuda there in *Eruvin* is speaking only in the context of the joint *eruv* of courtyards, which is a rabbinic prohibition. Both courtyards are *reshuyot hayachid* already; the issue of drawing from one to the other is only a *de-rabbanan* concern. For a rabbinic prohibition, Rav Yehuda is willing to allow the suspended wall. *Aval hacha sukkah de-oraita* — but here, where *sukkah* is a biblical obligation, he would not extend the leniency of the suspended wall to a biblical-level question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הָכָא, אֶלָּא בְּסוּכָּה דְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה. אֲבָל שַׁבָּת, דְּאִיסּוּר סְקִילָה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Nor does Rabbi Yosei hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as Rabbi Yosei states** his opinion that a suspended partition suffices **only here, with regard to a *sukka*, which is a positive mitzva. However,** in the case of carrying between courtyards on **Shabbat, which is a prohibition** that is punishable by **stoning, no,** a suspended partition does not suffice.

Conversely, *velo Rabbi Yose savar keRav Yehuda* — Rabbi Yose would not necessarily agree with Rav Yehuda in *Shabbat*. *Ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi Yose hacha ela be-sukkah de-mitzvas aseh* — Rabbi Yose's leniency by *sukkah* reflects the fact that the worst consequence of a flawed *sukkah* is failing to fulfill a positive commandment, a *mitzvas aseh*. *Aval Shabbat de-skila lo* — but *Shabbat*, where violations can carry the penalty of death and the entire system of *Shabbat* law is enormously consequential, he would be more stringent even in a case that is technically only *de-rabbanan*.

This is a striking resolution. Rather than a conceptual distinction — one might have argued, for instance, that *sukkah* requires a wall that makes the space *habitable*, which demands ground-to-*schach* closure in a way that *Shabbat mechitzot* do not — the *gemara* here grounds the divergence simply in degrees of stringency and leniency. The *Shabbat* case is more lenient because it is only *de-rabbanan*; the *sukkah* case is more lenient because the worst outcome is only a failed *mitzvas aseh*. Each authority applies his principle selectively based on the severity of the legal domain involved.

### Tzipori Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תֹּאמַר: מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה בְּצִיפּוֹרִי, עַל פִּי מִי נַעֲשָׂה? לֹא עַל פִּי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, אֶלָּא עַל פִּי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. וּמַאי מַעֲשֶׂה — דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: פַּעַם אַחַת שָׁכְחוּ וְלֹא הֵבִיאוּ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, לְמָחָר פֵּירְסוּ סְדִינִין עַל גַּבֵּי הָעַמּוּדִים, וְהֵבִיאוּ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה וְקָרְאוּ בּוֹ. פֵּירְסוּ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? מֵהֵיכָן הֱבִיאוּם בְּשַׁבָּת! אֶלָּא, מָצְאוּ סְדִינִין פְּרוּסִין עַל גַּבֵּי הָעַמּוּדִים וְהֵבִיאוּ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה וְקָרְאוּ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if you say:** Since Rabbi Yosei does not hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda with regard to the laws of Shabbat, **according to whose** opinion was the **action that was taken in Tzippori performed,** where they relied on suspended partitions even on Shabbat? The Gemara answers: It was **not** performed **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** but **rather on the authority of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei.** **And what** was that **incident? When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said: One time they forgot and did not bring a Torah scroll** to the synagogue **on Shabbat eve** prior to the onset of Shabbat. **The next day,** on Shabbat, to avoid violating the prohibition against carrying, **they spread** and suspended **sheets on posts** that were fixed along the path from the house in which the Torah scroll was stored to the synagogue, establishing partitions. **And they brought a Torah scroll** along that path **and read from it.** The Gemara asks: **Does it enter your mind** that **they spread** the sheets on Shabbat? Carrying before the partitions were established was prohibited. **From where did they bring** these sheets **on Shabbat? Rather, they found sheets** already **spread on the posts, and they brought a Torah scroll and read from it.**

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: there is a famous story that took place in *Tzipori* — the city of Rabbi Yose — in which a suspended *mechitza* was used to permit carrying on *Shabbat*. Does this not prove that Rabbi Yose applied his principle equally to *Shabbat*?

*Al pi mi na'asah* — on whose authority was this done? *Lo al pi Rabbi Yose* — not Rabbi Yose's. *Ela al pi Rabbi Yishmael beRabbi Yose* — it was done on the authority of his son, Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose. Rabbi Yose himself had already passed away when this ruling was given in *Tzipori*. One therefore cannot derive Rabbi Yose's own view on the *Shabbat* application from the story; the ruling was his son's. Perhaps his son extended the principle to *Shabbat*, but Rabbi Yose himself — from what we know — applied it only to *sukkah*.

The story itself is as follows: on one occasion, the community forgot to bring the *Sefer Torah* from its storage location before *Shabbat* to the place where it was needed for reading. The *Sefer Torah* was in one location, the *beit knesset* was across the *mavoi*, and without a *shituf mevo'ot* having been arranged beforehand, carrying through the shared space was prohibited (*de-rabbanan*). The solution: *matzu sadinim perusim al gabei amudim* — they found sheets already spread across posts, which created the necessary enclosure, and they were then able to bring the *Sefer Torah* and read from it.

The *gemara* questions the original formulation — how could they have *spread* the sheets on *Shabbat* at all? Carrying implies an already-existing *reshut hayachid*, which requires the walls to be in place first; making the walls is what one is trying to do. The answer given is that the sheets were already spread — found in place rather than actively arranged on *Shabbat* itself. Perhaps someone had hung out laundry, and the community realized it could serve as the necessary *mechitza*. Laundry spread between posts over a sufficient span is a natural example of a *mechitza teluyah*.

The question of whether one may make a *mechitza* on *Shabbat* is addressed at this point. *Rashi* rules that making a *mechitza* — hanging a partition — is not categorized as making an *ohel* on *Shabbat* and is therefore permitted; he crossed out the alternative *girsah* in the *gemara* that seemed to raise an *ohel* concern. *Tosafot* disagrees. *Tosafot* acknowledges that other *gemarot* seem to permit wall-making on *Shabbat*, and introduces a crucial distinction: one may erect a partition on *Shabbat* for purely functional purposes — privacy, screening, aesthetic purposes. What one may not do is create a *mechitza hamattir*, a partition that serves a *halachic* function of changing the legal status of a space. Rabbeinu Tam formulates this explicitly: *venireh leRabbeinu Tam di-mina tzad nami shayach asiyat ohel vekhe-hai gavna de-mechitza zo mo'elet lehattir* — a wall that is built specifically to render a space permissible in a *halachic* sense is itself categorized as a form of making an *ohel* and is prohibited. A wall built for privacy is not.

This is in fact the accepted *halacha*. The application to contemporary synagogue *mechitzot* is instructive: a *mechitza* erected in a synagogue for the purpose of allowing prayer — creating a separate space for women — is permitted on *Shabbat* because its function is understood as providing privacy rather than effecting a *halachic* status change. However, if the *mechitza* functions *halachically* as a formal *mechitza* in the *Shabbat* sense — for instance, a *mechitza* of only ten *tefachim* used to create a *reshut hayachid* — one would be prohibited from erecting it on *Shabbat* itself, because it is precisely a *mechitza hamattir*. Indeed, there is a tradition that the Rav Soloveitchik held that ten *tefachim* is the sufficient height for a *mechitza*, and Rav Yehuda Herzl Henkin publicly articulated the same position, though he added that it may not be a suspended fabric partition — precisely because of the *mechitza teluyah* issues developed throughout this *gemara*.

## Mechitza Function

### Permitting Walls

*Tosafot*'s principle — that a *mechitza* intended to create a *halachic* permission (*mechitza hamattir*) may not be erected on *Shabbat* — emerges directly from the *Tzipori* story and the surrounding *gemara*. The act of hanging sheets, when done to create a domain that permits carrying, constitutes *asiyat ohel* in the relevant halachic sense, even though in physical terms one is only suspending a fabric curtain. What triggers the prohibition is the *function* the wall is meant to perform, not its material form.

### Privacy vs. Status

The operative distinction is between a wall that serves a *halachic* status-changing function and one that serves a practical or social function. Hanging a curtain to change in privacy, to separate a space visually, or to prevent passersby from seeing within — these do not create a *mechitza hamattir* and are permitted on *Shabbat*. Hanging a curtain specifically to constitute the third or fourth wall of a *reshut hayachid*, or to permit an act that was otherwise prohibited, is the type of wall the prohibition targets.

### Shul Mechitza Rules

This distinction plays out directly in the *halacha* of synagogue *mechitzot*. A *mechitza* installed to create the environment for prayer functions more like a privacy partition — it enables prayer to take place in an appropriate setting — rather than as a wall that *halachically* transforms the nature of the space in the *Shabbat* sense. That is why it may be erected on *Shabbat*. Where the *mechitza* is sized and positioned to serve its formal *halachic* function — specifically the minimum of ten *tefachim* required by the law of *mechitzot* — the equation changes. A ten-*tefach mechitza* that is treated as formally operative under *Shabbat* law cannot be put up on *Shabbat* itself. The higher *mechitzot* common in most synagogues, understood as privacy or decorum structures rather than *halachic* status-changers, do not raise this concern.

## Lavud Solutions

### Mid-Height Mat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר אֲבִימִי: מַחְצֶלֶת אַרְבָּעָה וּמַשֶּׁהוּ — מַתֶּרֶת בְּסוּכָּה מִשּׁוּם דּוֹפֶן. הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? תָּלֵי לֵיהּ בָּאֶמְצַע, פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה לְמַטָּה וּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה לְמַעְלָה, וְכׇל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה כְּלָבוּד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Avimi said: A mat** that is **four handbreadths and a bit** wide can **permit** the use of a *sukka* **as a wall.** The Gemara explains: **How does one accomplish** this? **He suspends it in the middle** of a space ten handbreadths high, with **less than three** handbreadths **below** it **and less than three** handbreadths **above** it. **And** the principle states: The legal status of **any** objects that have a gap of **less than three** handbreadths between them **is as if** they were **joined [*lavud*].**

Rav Chisda, citing Avimi, states: *machatzelet arba'ah umashehu materet besukkah mishum dofen* — a mat of four *tefachim* and a bit (*umashehu*) renders a *sukkah* valid as a wall. The context is a *sukkah* of exactly ten *tefachim* in height. One takes such a mat and hangs it precisely in the middle of the wall space — *tolehu be'emtza*. Positioned at the center, its lower edge is less than three *tefachim* from the ground, and its upper edge is less than three *tefachim* from the *schach*. *Lavud* applies in both directions: *pachot mishlosha lemata, pachot mishlosha lema'ala* — less than three below and less than three above. Since any gap of less than three *tefachim* is treated as closed by *lavud*, the mat effectively spans the entire ten *tefachim*, constituting a complete wall.

### Double Lavud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: חַד לָבוּד אָמְרִינַן, תְּרֵי לָבוּד לָא אָמְרִינַן — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This is **obvious.** The principle of joining with regard to a gap of less than three handbreadths is well known. There is no need to teach this *halakha*. The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **we state** the principle of ***lavud* once** with regard to a particular surface but **we do not state** the principle of *lavud* **twice** to consider it joined in different directions, Avimi **teaches us** that one may implement the principle twice.

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: is this not obvious? The *gemara* responds: *mahu deteima chad lavud amrinan, trei lavud lo amrinan* — one might have thought that *lavud* can be invoked once but not twice — that applying the principle simultaneously at both ends of the same surface is a step too far. The ruling of Rav Chisda therefore teaches *kamashma lan* — that double *lavud* at opposite ends is in fact permitted.

### Seven-Tefach Mat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מַחְצֶלֶת שִׁבְעָה וּמַשֶּׁהוּ מַתֶּרֶת בְּסוּכָּה מִשּׁוּם דּוֹפֶן. כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא, בְּסוּכָּה גְּדוֹלָה. וּמַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן — דִּמְשַׁלְשְׁלִין דְּפָנוֹת מִלְמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Avimi from a *baraita*: **A mat** that is **seven handbreadths and a bit** wide can **permit** use of a *sukka* **as a wall.** Apparently, a mat can serve as the wall of a *sukka* only when the principle of joined objects is implemented once. The Gemara answers: **When that *baraita* was taught, it was with regard to a large *sukka*,** one considerably higher than ten handbreadths. One suspends the mat from a bit less than three handbreadths from the roofing, and it is considered a fit *sukka* wall although it is a significant distance off the ground. **And what does it teach us?** It teaches **that one** may **lower walls from up downward, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.**

A *baraita* is then cited that seems to contradict this: *machatzelet shivah umashehu materet basukkah mishum dofen* — a mat of seven *tefachim* and a bit is needed to render the *sukkah* valid. This implies that double *lavud* cannot be used and only a single *lavud* at one end is permitted, requiring the mat itself to cover seven of the ten *tefachim*, with *lavud* closing the remaining less-than-three.

### Gud Achis Application

The *gemara* resolves: *lo kashya* — there is no contradiction. *Ki tanya hahi besukkah gedolah* — that *baraita* speaks about a large *sukkah*, not a standard ten-*tefach sukkah*. In a large *sukkah*, the mat must be seven and a bit. Why? Because in a large *sukkah*, one cannot position the mat in the middle and apply *lavud* to both ends — there are constraints on how this works when the sukkah height is greater than ten *tefachim*. Instead, the mat is positioned near the top of the wall space, with *lavud* closing the gap upward to the *schach*. That gives the mat *schach*-adjacent validity and yields ten *tefachim* of wall counting from the top down. But then, to close the remaining space between the bottom of the mat and the ground, one must invoke *gud achis* — extending the wall downward — and that only follows according to Rabbi Yose. Hence the *baraita* implicitly follows Rabbi Yose's position, which permits suspended walls to extend downward. There is also another *girsah* noted by *Rashi* in which the mat is placed near the ground with *lavud* at the bottom, yielding ten *tefachim* from the ground up, and then *gud asik* extends the wall upward to the *schach*. That case is less controversial, since *gud asik* extends an already-grounded wall, which the rabbis permit. The more surprising *chiddush* in the standard *girsah* is the top-down case requiring *gud achis* — which follows only Rabbi Yose.

### Vertical Plank Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: פַּס אַרְבָּעָה וּמַשֶּׁהוּ — מַתִּיר בְּסוּכָּה מִשּׁוּם דּוֹפֶן. וּמוֹקֵים לֵיהּ בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים סָמוּךְ דּוֹפֶן, וְכׇל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה סָמוּךְ לַדּוֹפֶן כְּלָבוּד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos forming a *sukka* wall based on the principle of *lavud*, the Gemara cites that **Rabbi Ami said: A board** that measures **four** handbreadths **and a bit** can **permit** the use **of a *sukka*,** serving **as a wall, and** it is effective if **one establishes it less than three** handbreadths **from the** adjacent **wall. And** the principle states: The legal status of **any** objects with a gap of **less than three** handbreadths between them **is as if** they were **joined.**

Rav Yosef adds: *pas arba'ah arba'ah* — a vertical plank (*pas*) of four and a bit *tefachim* likewise renders the *sukkah* valid as a wall. While the *machatzelet* — the mat discussed above — runs horizontally between the *schach* and the ground, a *pas* here refers to a plank positioned vertically along the wall in the plane of the *sukkah*'s width or length. A *sukkah*'s minimum dimension is seven *tefachim* in each direction. If one wall is complete and the second wall needs to be formed, a vertical plank of four *tefachim* and a bit placed within less than three *tefachim* of the existing wall creates a total of more than seven *tefachim* through *lavud* — the plank's four-plus *tefachim* combined with the closing of the sub-three-*tefach* gap on the side. *Umokmi lah bepachot mishlosha tefachim samuch ladofen* — it is positioned within three *tefachim* of the wall, and *vechol pachot mishlosha tefachim kelavud dami* — any gap of less than three *tefachim* is considered closed.

### Seven-Tefach Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן — שִׁיעוּר מֶשֶׁךְ סוּכָּה קְטַנָּה שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is he teaching us?** The principle of *lavud* is well known. The Gemara answers: **He comes to teach us** that the minimum **measure** of the horizontal **extension** of the wall **of a small *sukka* is seven** handbreadths.

*Mai kamashma lan* — what does Rav Yosef's ruling teach us that we did not already know? *Ha kamashma lan* — it teaches us *shiur meshech sukkah ketanah shivah* — that the minimum dimension of a *sukkah* is seven *tefachim*. This fundamental measurement of *sukkah* law is, perhaps unexpectedly, embedded in and derivable from this precise *halacha* about vertical planks and *lavud*. An unusual vehicle for transmitting the basic minimum of *sukkah* dimensions — but that, concludes the *daf*, is the *chiddush* contained within it.
