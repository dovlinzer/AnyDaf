# Bava Kamma 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father & Daughter Injury

### Shevach Neurim Only

The daf opens by completing a *sugya* that began toward the end of the previous page. The *Gemara* had been exploring the question of whether a father collects compensation payments when his daughter is injured. The conclusion reached is that the *Torah* gave the father only *shevach neurim* — the ordinary benefits of his daughter's minority, namely the right to betroth her, to sell her as a Hebrew slave if she is a minor, and rabbinically to collect any lost item she finds — but not the payments for the injury itself.

*Reish Lakish* states this explicitly: *lo zachata Torah l'av ela shevach neurim bilvad* — the *Torah* gave the father no more than the benefit of her youth. *Rabbi Yochanan*, by contrast, holds that the father would collect even for a *ptziah*, a minor wound that does not rise to the level of a serious injury.

The *Gemara* immediately questions this: what payment could there possibly be for such a minor wound? After all, even the foundational question raised by *Rabbi Elazar* that launched this entire *sugya* concerned a genuine injury (*chavala*) that reduced the daughter's monetary value — her attractiveness as a prospective bride or her worth as a Hebrew slave. *Rabbi Yose bar Chanina* resolves the difficulty: the case *Rabbi Yochanan* envisions is where someone struck her in the face, causing a wound that, even if not permanent, temporarily diminishes her desirability and thus her father's ability to marry her off advantageously or sell her into service.

This resolution sharpens what is genuinely at stake in the *sugya*. The underlying assumption, now made explicit, is that payments for *tzaar* (pain), embarrassment, and suffering go to the daughter herself — those belong to her alone. What might conceivably flow to the father is only loss tied directly to his financial interests: specifically, the diminution of her value as a bride or slave. The debate was never about general compensation payments; it was always about that narrow category of harm that strikes at what the father himself stood to gain. With this clarification, the *sugya* concludes.

### Chovel B'Eved Knaa'ni

The *Gemara* now turns to a new *mishna*: *hachovel b'eved knaani shel acheirim chayav* — one who injures another person's Canaanite slave is liable. The slave is considered the master's property, and the tortfeasor must pay the full range of damages to the master.

### Boshet for Slaves

*Rabbi Yehuda*, however, holds that the payment for *boshet* (embarrassment) is not included. There is no *boshet* owed for injuring a slave. Notably, *Rabbi Yehuda* had similarly excluded a blind person categorically from the *chiyuv* of *boshet* in an earlier passage; there the *Gemara* traced the categorical exclusion through a broader analysis of areas where *Rabbi Yehuda* considers a blind person outside the scope of certain *mitzvot* obligations. An analogous analysis will now unfold for the slave.

## Eved: Achvah vs. Mitzvot

### Eved Lo Ba'Achvah

What is *Rabbi Yehuda*'s reasoning? The *Gemara* cites the *pasuk*: *ki yinatzu anashim yachdav ish v'achiv* — "when men fight together, a man and his brother" (Devarim 25:11). From this verse, the obligation of *boshet* is derived. *Rabbi Yehuda* reads the phrase *ish v'achiv* restrictively: *b'mi sheyeish lo achvah* — the laws of *boshet* apply only to one who possesses *achvah*, brotherhood. *Yatza eved she-ein lo achvah* — a slave is excluded because he has no brotherhood.

What does it mean that a slave has no *achvah*? Two distinct explanations emerge from the *Rishonim*. *Rashi* explains that a slave is not *ba b'kahal* — he cannot marry into the Jewish community and is therefore not part of it in the relevant sense. One might immediately ask: should a *mamzer* likewise be excluded from *boshet*, given that a *mamzer* is also barred from marrying into the broader community? The *Gemara* therefore turns to a different explanation. A slave has no *achvah* because *mutar ba-achoto uv'eishet achiv* — he is permitted to marry his own sister and his brother's wife. The recognized structures of family and kinship simply do not apply to him. While non-Jews are bound by the *sheva mitzvot b'nei Noach*, which includes the prohibition against incest, *Chazal* understood that the Canaanite slave occupies a distinct legal status in which no family structure is recognized at all. He has no *achvah* because there is no legally cognizable family relationship.

*Tosafot* formulates this point somewhat differently: *ein yotzei chalatzav k'ruyim achim* — the slave's own children are not considered siblings to one another. The family ties do not run laterally either. Whether this functions as a *gezeirat hakatuv* or reflects a more substantive principle about the nature of *boshet* as a form of communal embarrassment requiring some recognized community — that remains to be explored.

### Achiv Hu B'Mitzvot

The Rabbis respond to *Rabbi Yehuda* with the principle *achiv hu b'mitzvot* — a slave is one's brother in *mitzvot*. Even if no family structure is recognized, a Canaanite slave who has been immersed in the *mikveh* becomes obligated in *mitzvot* at the level of a Jewish woman. If he is subsequently freed, he becomes fully obligated as a Jewish male. He is therefore part of the religious community, and that community membership is what the word *achiv* captures.

### HaMeiri: Achiv B'Mitzvot

A striking extension of this principle appears in the *HaMeiri*, who in several places advances the view that non-Jews who abide by the moral tenets of their own religion — who live according to a framework of *din* and *mussar* — may be considered *achiv b'mitzvot* relative to Jews in certain areas of *halacha*. This is a bold and controversial position, but it flows from the same conceptual structure: the category of *achvah* may be constituted not only by biological kinship but by shared religious obligation and moral framework.

### Zomemim & Eved

The *Gemara* now presses *Rabbi Yehuda* with a consequence of his position. If a slave is not within the category of *achiv*, then *zomemim* — conspiratorial false witnesses — who testified against a slave should not be put to death. The relevant verse prescribes that *eidim zomemim* receive *kegamam lazot l'achiv* — whatever they sought to do to their brother. If a slave is not one's brother, the punishment of *eidim zomemim* would not apply to testimony against him.

The *Gemara* treats this consequence as untenable. *Tosafot* notes that if this were correct, testimony against a slave could never function at all: valid testimony requires the possibility of *hazamah*, and if *hazamah* can never apply to witnesses against a slave, those witnesses can never be qualified. Yet we know that testimony against slaves is accepted. How then does *Rabbi Yehuda* resolve this?

## Eved & Eidus

### Rav Sheishes on Zomemim

*Rava* citing *Rav Sheishes* resolves the problem by invoking a broader verse: *uvi'arta hara mikirbecha* — "you shall destroy the evil from your midst." This phrase provides an independent, expansive basis for the punishment of *eidim zomemim* that is not limited by the word *achiv*. Therefore, even according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, *zomemim* who testified against a slave are subject to punishment. The exclusion based on *achvah* pertains specifically to *boshet*, not to the general category of *eidim zomemim*.

### Meluchah: Mikerev Achecha

The *Gemara* now raises a parallel difficulty, this time pressing the Rabbis. If the Rabbis hold that a slave is *achiv b'mitzvot* — a religious brother — why cannot a slave serve as king? The verse states *mikerev achecha tasim alecha melech* — "from the midst of your brethren you shall appoint a king over you" (Devarim 17:15), and the *Gemara* understands this to exclude a slave and even a *ger* (convert) from the monarchy. But if a slave is considered a religious brother, the word *achecha* should include him.

The *Gemara* responds by reframing the question. Rather than answering on its own terms, it notes that a *ger* would pose the same difficulty for *everyone* — for *Rabbi Yehuda* as much as for the Rabbis. A *ger* has family relations after conversion (his children are related to him) and according to *Rabbi Yehuda*'s own criteria, a *ger* ought to qualify as *achiv*. Yet a *ger* too cannot be king. The answer, the *Gemara* concludes, is that *mikerev achecha* for the purposes of kingship is read through a heightened lens: *mimuvchar shebe'achecha* — from the most distinguished among your brethren, the most naturally integrated. Kingship requires a qualitatively deeper form of communal belonging that excludes both the *ger* and the *eved*, regardless of their *mitzvot* obligations.

### Four Categories Excluded

The *Gemara* then raises yet another challenge for the Rabbis: if a slave is *achiv b'mitzvot*, why is he disqualified from *eidus* (testimony)? The disqualification of testimony is derived from the verse *vehinei ed sheker he'ed sheker ana be'achiv* — the false witness testified falsely against his brother. If a slave is one's religious brother, the word *achiv* should include him and his testimony should be admissible.

*Ulla* responds that the disqualification of slaves from testimony cannot rest on *achvah* at all. Rather, it flows from a *kal vachomer* from a woman: a woman is excluded from testimony on the basis of *shenei ha'anashim* — "the two men" — and if a woman, who *is* eligible to enter the Jewish community through marriage, is excluded, then certainly a slave, who is not eligible to enter the community through marriage, should be excluded.

The *Gemara* then subjects this *kal vachomer* to rigorous scrutiny. Perhaps the exclusion of women is specifically because women do not undergo *milah* — in which case a slave, who does undergo *milah*, should not be excluded by analogy. The *Gemara* counters with a *katan* (minor): a minor is also circumcised but is disqualified from testimony. Perhaps then the operative factor is the minor's youth — but a slave is not a minor. A woman (*ishah tochiach*) — a woman is obligated in *mitzvot* yet excluded from testimony, so *mitzvot* obligation alone is not the qualifying criterion for testimony.

### Shoresh Ha'Psul

The *Gemara* applies the standard *talmudic* methodology: since neither a woman nor a minor by itself captures the full principle of exclusion, one seeks the common denominator — *hatzad hashaveh shebahen* — and that is *einam b'chol hamitzvot*: they are not fully obligated in the entire range of *mitzvot*. A minor is not yet obligated in any *mitzvot*; a woman is exempt from *mitzvot aseh shehazman gerama* (time-bound positive commandments). Since neither is fully obligated, and both are excluded from testimony, the operative principle is incomplete *mitzvot* obligation.

A slave, even a male slave, is obligated in *mitzvot* only at the level of a woman — not fully obligated as a Jewish male. Therefore he too falls under this principle and is excluded from testimony.

The *Gemara* pushes back once more: perhaps the common denominator of woman and minor is not *mitzvot* obligation but *eino ish* — neither is fully a man in the legal sense. A slave is a man (*ish*). The *Gemara* then considers whether adding a *gazlan* (robber) to the mix helps: the *gazlan* is disqualified because his own actions rendered him a *rasha* — this disqualification stems from personal conduct, not from any categorical deficiency. A slave did nothing to earn his disqualification.

Combining *gazlan* with woman or minor, the *Gemara* seeks a principle that explains all three. It cannot be *eino ish* (since the *gazlan* is a man), and it cannot be *ma'asav garmu lo* (since the woman and minor did nothing wrong). The only common denominator that works for all three — *ishah*, *katan*, and *gazlan* — is that none of them is fully performing all *mitzvot*. The *gazlan* is not observant; the woman is exempt from certain *mitzvot*; the minor is not yet obligated. And a slave, obligated only at the level of a woman, falls squarely within this category.

### Lo Yumtu Avot Al Banim

*Mar brei deRavina* offers an entirely different scriptural derivation for the exclusion of a slave from testimony. The verse states *lo yumtu avot al banim* — "fathers shall not die for their sons, nor sons for their fathers" (Devarim 24:16). Ordinarily this is understood to mean that relatives may not testify against each other. But *Mar brei deRavina* reads it differently: *lo yumtu al pi avot she-ein lahem chiyus banim* — no one may be put to death on the testimony of fathers who have no legally recognized relationship to their children. And such a father is precisely the Canaanite slave, who is not considered related to his offspring.

The *Gemara* asks: why does the *Torah* use the generic *banim* rather than *bnehem* (their sons)? Had it said *bnehem*, we would know only that sons cannot testify against their specific fathers. By using the more open *banim*, the verse teaches that the disqualification extends to a category of person whose relationship to children is altogether unrecognized.

### Ger vs. Eved

One might think this same derivation would disqualify a *ger* from testimony — after all, a *ger* is also not related to his father. The *Gemara* refutes this. The verse contains two parallel clauses: *avot al banim* (fathers who have no relation to their sons) and *banim al avot* (sons who have no relation to their fathers). The first clause — *avot al banim*, a father who is not related to his children — applies uniquely to a slave, because a slave has no recognized relationship either upward to his own parents or downward to his children. A *ger*, by contrast, has no relationship upward (his father is not halachically his father after conversion) but does have a relationship downward — once he converts, his children are his children. He is a *banim* who lacks *avot*, not an *avot* who lacks *banim*.

The *Torah*'s inclusion of both clauses is thus deliberate and precise: *avot al banim* excludes the slave (who lacks relation in both directions), while *banim al avot* is written only to create a stylistic parallel, not to exclude the *ger*. Had the *Torah* wanted to exclude the *ger*, it would have written only *banim lo yumtu al avot* without the possessive suffix, which would have been sufficient to invalidate both *ger* and *eved* — the *ger* directly, and the *eved* via a *kal vachomer* (since an *eved* lacks both upward and downward relation, he is certainly to be excluded if a *ger* who lacks only one direction is excluded). The fact that the *Torah* instead used *avot al banim* — the clause that uniquely fits only the slave — signals that the *ger* is *kasher le-eidus*.

The parallel *banim lo yumtu al avot* is present purely because the *Torah* writes in balanced, parallel language, mirroring the structure of the first clause. Its presence carries no additional legal exclusion. The conclusion stands: a *ger* is fully valid as a witness; it is only the slave — who has no *yichus* either upward or downward — who is disqualified.

This prompts *Tosafot* to raise a broader concern: how far does *Rabbi Yehuda*'s exclusion of the slave from *achvah* actually reach? If *achvah* is the operative word in laws of *ribbis*, *ona'ah*, *gonev nefesh*, and even *tzedaka*, are all of these to be understood as inapplicable to a slave? *Tosafot* acknowledges the difficulty without fully resolving it. He notes that *gonev nefesh* may be categorically limited to Jews based on the phrase *b'nei Yisrael* even for the Rabbis. As for *ribbis* and *ona'ah*, there is a technical point that a slave has no independent property and therefore these prohibitions simply have no application. But the question of *tzedaka* is pointed: is a slave not included in *achicha he'ani* — your poor brother? There may be a hint from the first chapter of *Gittin* that the obligation of *tzedaka* toward a slave is qualitatively different, though this is not fully demonstrated. What *Tosafot* leaves us with is the sharp recognition that once we accept *Rabbi Yehuda*'s categorical exclusion, its ramifications ripple through a remarkable range of interpersonal obligations.

## Gift of Melog Property

### Ketivatah L'Nechsa

The *Gemara* now introduces a case study that will anchor the remainder of the daf. The mother of *Rav Shmuel bar Abba* from Hagronia was married to *Rabbi Abba*. Before her death, she wrote over — gifted — all of her property to her son, *Rav Shmuel bar Abba*. The purpose of this transfer was to ensure that her property would pass to her own son rather than be inherited by her husband and then divided among his heirs. After she died, *Rav Shmuel bar Abba* brought the matter before *Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba*, who ruled that the property was indeed his — the mother's transfer was effective and the son was entitled to the property. (That *Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba* may himself be a stepbrother of the claimant goes unremarked by the *Gemara*.)

*Rabbi Abba* — the husband who stood to lose — then went and related the facts to *Rabbi Hoshaia*, who in turn reported them to *Rav Yehuda*.

### Samuel's Psak

*Rav Yehuda* responded with a ruling in the name of his teacher *Shmuel*: *ha'isha she'makhrah b'nichsei melog b'chayei ba'alah u'meitah, haba'al motzi miyad halakochos* — a woman who sold or transferred her *nichsei melog* property during her husband's lifetime, and then died, the husband may reclaim it from the purchasers.

The concept of *nichsei melog* requires explanation. This is property the wife brings into the marriage over which the title (*kinyan haguf*) remains hers, while the husband receives the right to benefit from the property's produce (*kinyan peiros*). To use a concrete illustration: if she brings an orchard into the marriage, she owns the orchard itself, but he collects the fruit each season. Alternatively, if she brings an apartment building, she holds the title while he collects the rent.

*Shmuel*'s ruling is that she cannot unilaterally transfer or sell this property while her husband is alive, because his *kinyan peiros* gives him a stake in it. Therefore the transfer the mother made to her son is ineffective, and *Rabbi Abba* should be able to recover the property.

### Mishnah: Kotev Nechsav

*Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba* was not moved by this challenge. He cited a *Mishna* that he regarded as directly on point. The *Mishna* (in *Ketubot*) discusses a case of *hakotev nechsav livno le'achar moto* — a father who writes his property over to his son to take effect "from today and after I die." The legal effect of this language is that the son acquires the title (*kinyan haguf*) immediately, while the father retains the right to the *peiros* until his death.

The structure is perfectly parallel to *nichsei melog*: the son holds the *kinyan haguf* while the father holds the *kinyan peiros*, exactly as the wife holds the *kinyan haguf* of her *melog* property while the husband holds the *kinyan peiros*.

The *Mishna* states:

- *haben eino yakhol limkor mipenei shehen bireshut av* — the son cannot sell the property, since the father still controls the *peiros*.
- *ha'av eino yakhol limkor mipenei shehen ketuvin laben* — the father cannot sell, since the title is written over to the son.

Neither party can complete a sale on their own. However, if the father goes ahead and sells his rights, the purchaser acquires the *peiros* until the father dies. If the son sells his title, the purchaser acquires nothing until the father dies — but the sale of the title itself is effective.

The crucial inference is: *ki meit av miha itei lalokeiach* — when the father does eventually die, the purchaser from the son does receive the full property. This is true even *af al gav de'meit haben b'chayei ha'av* — even if the son dies while the father is still alive, so that the full possession never passed through the son's hands during his lifetime. The son's sale of his title worked even then.

This case maps directly onto the mother's situation: she transferred her title to *Rav Shmuel* even while her husband retained the *kinyan peiros*. The *Mishna* demonstrates that such a transfer of title is valid.

### Kinyan Peiros & Guf

The *Mishna*'s conclusion — that the purchaser from the son receives the property when the father dies, even when the son predeceased the father — is the subject of a fundamental dispute between *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Resh Lakish*.

### Resh Lakish: Lav K'Kinya…

*Rabbi Yochanan* holds *lo kana lokeach* — the purchaser from the son does not acquire. His reasoning: since the father holds *kinyan peiros*, the father is considered a co-owner or even the primary owner. The son cannot sell a title while the father's rights effectively prevent the son from exercising any ownership. *Kinyan peiros ke-kinyan haguf dami* — the right to benefit from the produce is legally equivalent to title ownership. The sale the son attempted while the father was alive and active is therefore void. The purchaser could only receive the property if the father died first, allowing full possession to pass to the son before the sale took effect.

*Resh Lakish*, by contrast, holds *kana lokeach* — the purchaser does acquire. The son's title is real and transferable independent of the father's *kinyan peiros*. *Kinyan peiros lav k'kinyan haguf dami* — the right to the produce does not constitute ownership of the property. The father's retention of *peiros* does not impair the son's ability to convey title. Therefore, even if the son sold his title while the father was alive and then predeceased the father — so that full possession never passed through the son's hands — the purchaser (*Reuven*) will receive the property once the father dies.

The *Mishna*'s ruling that the purchaser from the son does receive the property when the father eventually dies, even in the scenario where the son died first, aligns with the position of *Resh Lakish*.

### Analogous Transfer

The relevance to the mother's case becomes explicit. The *Gemara* notes that both *Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba* (who ruled in favor of *Rav Shmuel*) and *Rav Yehuda* (who initially challenged that ruling on the basis of *Shmuel*'s opinion) in fact both hold like *Resh Lakish* — that *kinyan peiros lav k'kinyan haguf dami*. This is the accepted principle in *halacha*.

*Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba*, in invoking the *Mishna*, was therefore pressing *Rav Yehuda*: if you hold like *Resh Lakish* — as everyone does — then you must accept that the son's sale or the mother's transfer of title works even while the party with *kinyan peiros* is still alive. The father's (or husband's) *kinyan peiros* does not prevent the transfer of title. Therefore *Rav Shmuel*'s mother's gift was effective, and *Rav Shmuel* is entitled to the property once *Rabbi Abba* dies.

The *Gemara* makes this reasoning explicit: if one were to hold *kinyan peiros ke-kinyan haguf dami*, then even the *Mishna*'s case would be incoherent — why would the purchaser from the son ever get the property, even when the father dies first? The answer is that the son's sale was void ab initio. Yet the *Mishna* clearly validates the purchaser's eventual acquisition. This can only be consistent with *kinyan peiros lav k'kinyan haguf dami*. Since everyone accepts the *Mishna* and everyone holds like *Resh Lakish*, the mother's transfer to her son should be fully effective.

### Zu Eina Doma L'Mishnasenu

When this counterargument was reported back to *Rav Yehuda*, he responded in the name of *Shmuel*: *zu eina doma l'mishnasenu* — this case is not analogous to our *Mishna*. *Shmuel* maintained that the mother-to-son transfer is categorically different from the father-to-son scenario the *Mishna* addresses. The precise nature of that distinction — why the mother-son transfer cannot be resolved by the same principle that governs the father-son case — is left to be elaborated in the continuation of the *sugya* on the following daf.
