# Bava Kamma 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father & Daughter Injury

### Shevach Neurim Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא זִכְּתָה תּוֹרָה לָאָב – אֶלָּא שֶׁבַח נְעוּרִים בִּלְבָד. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ פְּצִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And so says Reish Lakish: The Torah granted the father only the profits of** her **youth,** and nothing else. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The father receives **even** the compensation for his daughter's **wound.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּצִיעָה סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךְ?! אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ אֶלָּא חֲבָלָה – דְּאַפְחֲתַהּ מִכַּסְפַּהּ; אֲבָל פְּצִיעָה – דְּלָא אַפְחֲתַהּ מִכַּסְפַּהּ, לָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Does it enter your mind** that the father receives compensation for his daughter's **wound,** which, unlike an injury, does not diminish his daughter's value? **Even Rabbi Elazar raised his dilemma only** with regard to **an injury,** by **which he lowered her monetary** value, diminishing the amount of money that her father will receive for her betrothal. **But** with regard to **a wound,** by **which he did not lower her monetary** value, **Rabbi Elazar did not** even **raise a dilemma.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: שֶׁפְּצָעָהּ בְּפָנֶיהָ, וְאַפְחֲתַהּ מִכַּסְפַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said:** Rabbi Yoḥanan is discussing a case **where** someone **wounded her on her face, and he** thereby **lowered her monetary** value, as this affects the amount of money that her father will receive for her betrothal.

The daf opens by completing a *sugya* that began toward the end of the previous page. The *Gemara* had been exploring the question of whether a father collects compensation payments when his daughter is injured. The conclusion reached is that the *Torah* gave the father only *shevach neurim* — the ordinary benefits of his daughter's minority, namely the right to betroth her, to sell her as a Hebrew slave if she is a minor, and rabbinically to collect any lost item she finds — but not the payments for the injury itself.

*Reish Lakish* states this explicitly: *lo zachata Torah l'av ela shevach neurim bilvad* — the *Torah* gave the father no more than the benefit of her youth. *Rabbi Yochanan*, by contrast, holds that the father would collect even for a *ptziah*, a minor wound that does not rise to the level of a serious injury.

The *Gemara* immediately questions this: what payment could there possibly be for such a minor wound? After all, even the foundational question raised by *Rabbi Elazar* that launched this entire *sugya* concerned a genuine injury (*chavala*) that reduced the daughter's monetary value — her attractiveness as a prospective bride or her worth as a Hebrew slave. *Rabbi Yose bar Chanina* resolves the difficulty: the case *Rabbi Yochanan* envisions is where someone struck her in the face, causing a wound that, even if not permanent, temporarily diminishes her desirability and thus her father's ability to marry her off advantageously or sell her into service.

This resolution sharpens what is genuinely at stake in the *sugya*. The underlying assumption, now made explicit, is that payments for *tzaar* (pain), embarrassment, and suffering go to the daughter herself — those belong to her alone. What might conceivably flow to the father is only loss tied directly to his financial interests: specifically, the diminution of her value as a bride or slave. The debate was never about general compensation payments; it was always about that narrow category of harm that strikes at what the father himself stood to gain. With this clarification, the *sugya* concludes.

### Chovel B'Eved Knaa'ni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַחוֹבֵל בְּעֶבֶד כְּנַעֲנִי שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים – חַיָּיב וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי יִנָּצוּ אֲנָשִׁים יַחְדָּו אִישׁ וְאָחִיו״ – בְּמִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אַחְוָה, יָצָא עֶבֶד שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אַחְוָה. וְרַבָּנַן – אָחִיו הוּא בְּמִצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **One who injures a Canaanite slave belonging to others** is **liable** for all of the five types of indemnity. Rabbi Yehuda says: Canaanite slaves do not have humiliation, so one who injures a slave pays only the other four types of indemnity. **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda?** The Gemara explains: The **verse states** with regard to the indemnity of humiliation: **"When men strive together, a man and his brother"** (Deuteronomy 25:11). The formulation "and his brother" teaches that the payment of compensation for humiliation is applicable **with regard to someone who has** a relationship of **brotherhood** with a Jew. This is **excluding** a Canaanite **slave, who does not have** a relationship of **brotherhood** with Jews. **And the Sages,** who hold that a Canaanite slave is included in the *halakhot* of compensation for humiliation, maintain that **he is** the Jews' **brother with regard to** the observance of **mitzvot,** as a Canaanite slave is obligated to observe most mitzvot.

The *Gemara* now turns to a new *mishna*: *hachovel b'eved knaani shel acheirim chayav* — one who injures another person's Canaanite slave is liable. The slave is considered the master's property, and the tortfeasor must pay the full range of damages to the master.

### Boshet for Slaves

*Rabbi Yehuda*, however, holds that the payment for *boshet* (embarrassment) is not included. There is no *boshet* owed for injuring a slave. Notably, *Rabbi Yehuda* had similarly excluded a blind person categorically from the *chiyuv* of *boshet* in an earlier passage; there the *Gemara* traced the categorical exclusion through a broader analysis of areas where *Rabbi Yehuda* considers a blind person outside the scope of certain *mitzvot* obligations. An analogous analysis will now unfold for the slave.

## Eved: Achvah vs. Mitzvot

### Eved Lo Ba'Achvah

What is *Rabbi Yehuda*'s reasoning? The *Gemara* cites the *pasuk*: *ki yinatzu anashim yachdav ish v'achiv* — "when men fight together, a man and his brother" (Devarim 25:11). From this verse, the obligation of *boshet* is derived. *Rabbi Yehuda* reads the phrase *ish v'achiv* restrictively: *b'mi sheyeish lo achvah* — the laws of *boshet* apply only to one who possesses *achvah*, brotherhood. *Yatza eved she-ein lo achvah* — a slave is excluded because he has no brotherhood.

What does it mean that a slave has no *achvah*? Two distinct explanations emerge from the *Rishonim*. *Rashi* explains that a slave is not *ba b'kahal* — he cannot marry into the Jewish community and is therefore not part of it in the relevant sense. One might immediately ask: should a *mamzer* likewise be excluded from *boshet*, given that a *mamzer* is also barred from marrying into the broader community? The *Gemara* therefore turns to a different explanation. A slave has no *achvah* because *mutar ba-achoto uv'eishet achiv* — he is permitted to marry his own sister and his brother's wife. The recognized structures of family and kinship simply do not apply to him. While non-Jews are bound by the *sheva mitzvot b'nei Noach*, which includes the prohibition against incest, *Chazal* understood that the Canaanite slave occupies a distinct legal status in which no family structure is recognized at all. He has no *achvah* because there is no legally cognizable family relationship.

*Tosafot* formulates this point somewhat differently: *ein yotzei chalatzav k'ruyim achim* — the slave's own children are not considered siblings to one another. The family ties do not run laterally either. Whether this functions as a *gezeirat hakatuv* or reflects a more substantive principle about the nature of *boshet* as a form of communal embarrassment requiring some recognized community — that remains to be explored.

### Achiv Hu B'Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – זוֹמְמֵי עֶבֶד לֹא יֵהָרְגוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַעֲשִׂיתֶם לוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם לַעֲשׂוֹת לְאָחִיו״! אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּבִעַרְתָּ הָרָע מִקִּרְבֶּךָ״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If that is so,** then **according to Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that slaves are not in the category of brothers, **conspiring witnesses** who testify that a Canaanite **slave** committed a sin punishable by capital punishment **should not be put to death** by the court, **as it is written: "You shall do unto him as he conspired to do unto his brother"** (Deuteronomy 19:19), and the slave is not considered a brother. **Rava said** that **Rav Sheshet said: The** same **verse states** with regard to conspiring witnesses: **"And you shall put away the evil from your midst,"** indicating that the punishment for conspiring witnesses should be applied **in any case,** including when they testified about a slave.

The Rabbis respond to *Rabbi Yehuda* with the principle *achiv hu b'mitzvot* — a slave is one's brother in *mitzvot*. Even if no family structure is recognized, a Canaanite slave who has been immersed in the *mikveh* becomes obligated in *mitzvot* at the level of a Jewish woman. If he is subsequently freed, he becomes fully obligated as a Jewish male. He is therefore part of the religious community, and that community membership is what the word *achiv* captures.

### HaMeiri: Achiv B'Mitzvot

A striking extension of this principle appears in the *HaMeiri*, who in several places advances the view that non-Jews who abide by the moral tenets of their own religion — who live according to a framework of *din* and *mussar* — may be considered *achiv b'mitzvot* relative to Jews in certain areas of *halacha*. This is a bold and controversial position, but it flows from the same conceptual structure: the category of *achvah* may be constituted not only by biological kinship but by shared religious obligation and moral framework.

### Zomemim & Eved

The *Gemara* now presses *Rabbi Yehuda* with a consequence of his position. If a slave is not within the category of *achiv*, then *zomemim* — conspiratorial false witnesses — who testified against a slave should not be put to death. The relevant verse prescribes that *eidim zomemim* receive *kegamam lazot l'achiv* — whatever they sought to do to their brother. If a slave is not one's brother, the punishment of *eidim zomemim* would not apply to testimony against him.

The *Gemara* treats this consequence as untenable. *Tosafot* notes that if this were correct, testimony against a slave could never function at all: valid testimony requires the possibility of *hazamah*, and if *hazamah* can never apply to witnesses against a slave, those witnesses can never be qualified. Yet we know that testimony against slaves is accepted. How then does *Rabbi Yehuda* resolve this?

## Eved & Eidus

### Rav Sheishes on Zomemim

*Rava* citing *Rav Sheishes* resolves the problem by invoking a broader verse: *uvi'arta hara mikirbecha* — "you shall destroy the evil from your midst." This phrase provides an independent, expansive basis for the punishment of *eidim zomemim* that is not limited by the word *achiv*. Therefore, even according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, *zomemim* who testified against a slave are subject to punishment. The exclusion based on *achvah* pertains specifically to *boshet*, not to the general category of *eidim zomemim*.

### Meluchah: Mikerev Achecha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, לְרַבָּנַן – עֶבֶד יְהֵא כָּשֵׁר לַמַּלְכוּת! אָמְרִי: וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, תִּיקְשֵׁי לָךְ גֵּר – לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל! אֶלָּא אָמַר קְרָא: ״מִקֶּרֶב אַחֶיךָ״ – מִמּוּבְחָר שֶׁבְּאַחֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another challenge: **If that is so,** then **according to the Sages,** who hold that slaves are in the category of brothers, a Canaanite **slave should be fit for kingship,** as it is written: "One from among your brothers shall you set a king over you" (Deuteronomy 17:15). The Sages **say** in response: **But according to your reasoning,** the *halakha* disqualifying **a convert** from being king **should pose a difficulty for you according to all** opinions, both according to the opinion of the Sages and of Rabbi Yehuda: Why is a convert not fit to be a king, as he is certainly in the category of brother? The Gemara answers: **Rather, the verse states: "One from among your brothers** shall you set a king over you," which indicates that the king must be **from the choicest among your brothers,** which disqualifies both converts and slaves.

The *Gemara* now raises a parallel difficulty, this time pressing the Rabbis. If the Rabbis hold that a slave is *achiv b'mitzvot* — a religious brother — why cannot a slave serve as king? The verse states *mikerev achecha tasim alecha melech* — "from the midst of your brethren you shall appoint a king over you" (Devarim 17:15), and the *Gemara* understands this to exclude a slave and even a *ger* (convert) from the monarchy. But if a slave is considered a religious brother, the word *achecha* should include him.

The *Gemara* responds by reframing the question. Rather than answering on its own terms, it notes that a *ger* would pose the same difficulty for *everyone* — for *Rabbi Yehuda* as much as for the Rabbis. A *ger* has family relations after conversion (his children are related to him) and according to *Rabbi Yehuda*'s own criteria, a *ger* ought to qualify as *achiv*. Yet a *ger* too cannot be king. The answer, the *Gemara* concludes, is that *mikerev achecha* for the purposes of kingship is read through a heightened lens: *mimuvchar shebe'achecha* — from the most distinguished among your brethren, the most naturally integrated. Kingship requires a qualitatively deeper form of communal belonging that excludes both the *ger* and the *eved*, regardless of their *mitzvot* obligations.

### Four Categories Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, לְרַבָּנַן – יְהֵא עֶבֶד כָּשֵׁר לְעֵדוּת, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִנֵּה עֵד שֶׁקֶר הָעֵד, שֶׁקֶר עָנָה בְאָחִיו״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges further: **If that is so,** then **according to the Sages,** who hold that slaves are in the category of brothers, a Canaanite **slave should be fit to bear witness, as it is written: "And behold, the witness is a false witness, and has testified falsely against his brother"** (Deuteronomy 19:18). If a slave is considered a brother, why is he not eligible to testify in court?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: עֵדוּת לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ; אָתְיָא עֵדוּת בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵאִשָּׁה: וּמָה אִשָּׁה, שֶׁהִיא רְאוּיָה לָבֹא בַּקָּהָל – פְּסוּלָה לְעֵדוּת; עֶבֶד, שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לָבֹא בַּקָּהָל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁפָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת?
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: You cannot say** that a slave is fit to bear **witness,** because the *halakha* concerning the **testimony** of a slave **is derived by an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakha* concerning the testimony of **a woman: And just as a woman, who is fit to enter into the congregation,** i.e., to marry a Jew of fit lineage, **is** nevertheless **disqualified from bearing witness,** so too, with regard to **a slave, who is not fit to enter the congregation, is it not logical that** he is **disqualified from bearing witness?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְאִשָּׁה – שֶׁכֵּן אֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לְמִילָה; תֹּאמַר בְּעֶבֶד שֶׁהוּא רָאוּי לְמִילָה?! קָטָן יוֹכִיחַ – שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּמִילָה, וּפָסוּל לְעֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters this derivation: **What** is notable **about a woman?** She is notable **in that she is not fit to** undergo the mitzva of **circumcision.** Would **you say** that from the fact that a woman is disqualified from bearing witness one can derive that the same is true **for a slave, who is fit to** undergo **circumcision?** The Gemara responds: The *halakha* of **a minor shall prove** that one's fitness to undergo circumcision is not germane to one's qualification to bear witness, **as** a minor **is** included **in** the mitzva to undergo **circumcision, and** yet he **is disqualified from bearing witness.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְקָטָן – שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּמִצְוֹת; תֹּאמַר בְּעֶבֶד, שֶׁהוּא בְּמִצְוֹת?! אִשָּׁה תּוֹכִיחַ – שֶׁיֶּשְׁנָהּ בְּמִצְוֹת, וּפְסוּלָה לְעֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** This is countered: **What** is notable **about a minor?** He is notable **in that he is not** obligated **in** the performance of **mitzvot.** Would **you say** that from the fact that a minor is disqualified from bearing witness one can derive that the same is true **for a slave, who is** obligated **in** the performance of **mitzvot?** The Gemara responds: The *halakha* of **a woman shall prove** the matter, **as she is** obligated **in** the performance of **mitzvot and is disqualified from bearing witness.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחָזַר הַדִּין: לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִרְאִי זֶה; הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁכֵּן אֵינָן בְּכׇל הַמִּצְוֹת, וּפְסוּלִין לְהָעִיד; אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא אֶת הָעֶבֶד – שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּכׇל הַמִּצְוֹת, וּפָסוּל לְהָעִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **And the derivation has reverted** to its starting point. **The aspect of this** case **is not like the aspect of that** case, **and the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case, as each case has its own unique *halakha*. **Their common denominator** is **that** both a woman and a minor **are not** obligated **in** the performance of **all the mitzvot and are disqualified from bearing witness. I will also bring** the case of **the slave** for inclusion in this *halakha*, **as he is** also **not** obligated **in** the performance of **all the mitzvot and is disqualified from bearing witness.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶם – שֶׁכֵּן אֵינוֹ אִישׁ; תֹּאמַר בְּעֶבֶד, שֶׁהוּא אִישׁ?!
>
> **Translation:** This derivation is rejected: **What** is notable **about their common denominator?** It is notable **in that** a woman or a minor **is not a man,** and this is why they are disqualified from bearing witness. Would **you say** that from the fact that they are disqualified from bearing witness one can derive that the same is true **for a slave, who is a man?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא תֵּיתֵי מִגַּזְלָן. מָה לְגַזְלָן – שֶׁכֵּן מַעֲשָׂיו גָּרְמוּ לוֹ; תֹּאמַר בְּעֶבֶד – שֶׁאֵין מַעֲשָׂיו גָּרְמוּ לוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers a different derivation: **Rather, derive** the *halakha* that a slave is disqualified from bearing witness **from** that of **a robber,** who is disqualified from bearing witness. The Gemara objects: **What** is notable **about a robber?** He is notable **in that his actions caused him** to be disqualified. Would **you say** that from the fact that a robber is disqualified from bearing witness one can derive that the same is true **for a slave, whose actions did not cause him** to be unfit?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא תֵּיתֵי מִגַּזְלָן וּמֵחַד מֵהָנָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather, derive** the *halakha* **from a robber and from one of these** other two, either a woman or a minor. Their common denominator is that they do not observe all the mitzvot and are disqualified from bearing witness. So too, a slave does not observe all the mitzvot and is disqualified from bearing witness.

The *Gemara* then raises yet another challenge for the Rabbis: if a slave is *achiv b'mitzvot*, why is he disqualified from *eidus* (testimony)? The disqualification of testimony is derived from the verse *vehinei ed sheker he'ed sheker ana be'achiv* — the false witness testified falsely against his brother. If a slave is one's religious brother, the word *achiv* should include him and his testimony should be admissible.

*Ulla* responds that the disqualification of slaves from testimony cannot rest on *achvah* at all. Rather, it flows from a *kal vachomer* from a woman: a woman is excluded from testimony on the basis of *shenei ha'anashim* — "the two men" — and if a woman, who *is* eligible to enter the Jewish community through marriage, is excluded, then certainly a slave, who is not eligible to enter the community through marriage, should be excluded.

The *Gemara* then subjects this *kal vachomer* to rigorous scrutiny. Perhaps the exclusion of women is specifically because women do not undergo *milah* — in which case a slave, who does undergo *milah*, should not be excluded by analogy. The *Gemara* counters with a *katan* (minor): a minor is also circumcised but is disqualified from testimony. Perhaps then the operative factor is the minor's youth — but a slave is not a minor. A woman (*ishah tochiach*) — a woman is obligated in *mitzvot* yet excluded from testimony, so *mitzvot* obligation alone is not the qualifying criterion for testimony.

### Shoresh Ha'Psul

The *Gemara* applies the standard *talmudic* methodology: since neither a woman nor a minor by itself captures the full principle of exclusion, one seeks the common denominator — *hatzad hashaveh shebahen* — and that is *einam b'chol hamitzvot*: they are not fully obligated in the entire range of *mitzvot*. A minor is not yet obligated in any *mitzvot*; a woman is exempt from *mitzvot aseh shehazman gerama* (time-bound positive commandments). Since neither is fully obligated, and both are excluded from testimony, the operative principle is incomplete *mitzvot* obligation.

A slave, even a male slave, is obligated in *mitzvot* only at the level of a woman — not fully obligated as a Jewish male. Therefore he too falls under this principle and is excluded from testimony.

The *Gemara* pushes back once more: perhaps the common denominator of woman and minor is not *mitzvot* obligation but *eino ish* — neither is fully a man in the legal sense. A slave is a man (*ish*). The *Gemara* then considers whether adding a *gazlan* (robber) to the mix helps: the *gazlan* is disqualified because his own actions rendered him a *rasha* — this disqualification stems from personal conduct, not from any categorical deficiency. A slave did nothing to earn his disqualification.

Combining *gazlan* with woman or minor, the *Gemara* seeks a principle that explains all three. It cannot be *eino ish* (since the *gazlan* is a man), and it cannot be *ma'asav garmu lo* (since the woman and minor did nothing wrong). The only common denominator that works for all three — *ishah*, *katan*, and *gazlan* — is that none of them is fully performing all *mitzvot*. The *gazlan* is not observant; the woman is exempt from certain *mitzvot*; the minor is not yet obligated. And a slave, obligated only at the level of a woman, falls squarely within this category.

### Lo Yumtu Avot Al Banim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא יוּמְתוּ אָבוֹת עַל בָּנִים״ – לֹא יוּמְתוּ עַל פִּי אָבוֹת שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם חַיִיס בָּנִים. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ כִּדְאָמְרִינַן: ״לֹא יוּמְתוּ אָבוֹת עַל בָּנִים״ – בְּעֵדוּת בָּנִים; לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״לֹא יוּמְתוּ אָבוֹת עַל בְּנֵיהֶם״! מַאי ״בָּנִים״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּלֹא יוּמְתוּ עַל פִּי אָבוֹת שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם חַיִיס בָּנִים.

What** does the verse teach by stating only: **"Children,"** without the word their? **Learn from** this formulation **that** people **shall not be put to death based on** the testimony of **fathers who do not have** common **lineage with** their **children.**

But** if that were the case, it would **also** be the case that **a convert** would be **disqualified from bearing witness,** as one who converts no longer shares common lineage with his parents.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! גֵּר, נְהִי דְּאֵין לוֹ חַיִיס לְמַעְלָה, לְמַטָּה יֵשׁ לוֹ חַיִיס; לְאַפּוֹקֵי עֶבֶד, דְּאֵין לוֹ חַיִיס לֹא לְמַעְלָה וְלֹא לְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **How can** these cases **be compared?** With regard to **a convert, although he has no** common **lineage** with those **above** him, i.e., his parents, **he** does **have** common **lineage** with those **below** him, i.e., his children. This serves **to exclude a slave, who has no** common **lineage either** with those **above** him **or** with those **below** him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ גֵּר פָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״לֹא יוּמְתוּ אָבוֹת עַל בְּנֵיהֶם״ לְכִדְאָמְרִינַן – לֹא יוּמְתוּ בְּעֵדוּת בָּנִים; וְנִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״וּבָנִים לֹא יוּמְתוּ עַל אָבוֹת״ – דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תְּרֵי: חֲדָא – לֹא יוּמְתוּ בָּנִים בְּעֵדוּת אָבוֹת, וְאִידַּךְ – לֹא יוּמְתוּ עַל פִּי בָּנִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם חַיִיס אָבוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages prove that a convert is fit to bear witness: **As, if** it **enters your mind** that **a convert is disqualified from bearing witness,** then **let the Merciful One write: Fathers shall not be put to death for their children, according to** the interpretation **that we say:** Fathers **shall not be put to death based on the testimony of** their **children; and let the Merciful One** also **write: "Nor shall children be put to death for fathers," from which you learn two** *halakhot*: **One,** that **children shall not be put to death by** the **testimony of** their **fathers, and another,** that people **shall not be put to death based on** the testimony of **children who do not have** common **lineage with** their **fathers,** i.e., converts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעֶבֶד – נָפְקָא לֵיהּ בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִגֵּר; וּמָה גֵּר, דִּלְמַעְלָה הוּא דְּאֵין לוֹ חַיִיס, אֲבָל לְמַטָּה יֵשׁ לוֹ חַיִיס – פָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת; עֶבֶד, שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חַיִיס לֹא לְמַעְלָה וְלֹא לְמַטָּה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא פָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the statement: **And** the *halakha* with regard to **a slave** being disqualified from bearing witness **is derived by an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakha* of **a convert: And just as** with regard to **a convert, that it is** the case concerning those **above** him **that he has no** common **lineage, but** with those **below** him **he** does **have** common **lineage,** and he is **disqualified from bearing witness,** then with regard to **a slave, who has no** common **lineage** with those **above** him **or** those **below** him, all the more so **is it not logical that he is disqualified from bearing witness?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִדִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״לֹא יוּמְתוּ אָבוֹת עַל בָּנִים״ – דְּמַשְׁמַע: לֹא יוּמְתוּ עַל פִּי אָבוֹת שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חַיִיס בָּנִים; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, עֶבֶד, שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חַיִיס לֹא לְמַעְלָה וְלֹא לְמַטָּה – הוּא דְּפָסוּל לְעֵדוּת; אֲבָל גֵּר, כֵּיוָן דְּיֵשׁ לוֹ חַיִיס לְמַטָּה – כָּשֵׁר לְעֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the statement: **Rather, due to** the fact **that the Merciful One** did not write the verse that way, but instead **wrote: "Fathers shall not be put to death for children," which indicates** that people **shall not be put to death based on** the testimony of **fathers who do not have** common **lineage with** their **children, learn from** this formulation that with regard to **a slave, who has no** common **lineage** with those **above** him **or** those **below** him, **he** is the one **who is disqualified from bearing witness. But** with regard to **a convert, since he has** common **lineage** with those **below** him, he is **fit to bear witness.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״וּבָנִים לֹא יוּמְתוּ עַל אֲבוֹתֵיהֶם״, לְמָה לִי דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״וּבָנִים לֹא יוּמְתוּ עַל אָבוֹת״ – דְּמַשְׁמַע: לֹא יוּמְתוּ עַל פִּי בָּנִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם חַיִיס אָבוֹת? אַיְּידֵי דִּכְתַב: ״לָא יוּמְתוּ אָבוֹת עַל בָּנִים״, כְּתַב נָמֵי: ״וּבָנִים לֹא יוּמְתוּ עַל אָבוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: Let the Merciful One write: Nor shall children be put to death for their fathers, why do I** need **that** which **the Merciful One wrote: "Nor shall children be put to death for fathers," which indicates** that people **shall not be put to death based on** the testimony of **children who do not have** common **lineage** with their **fathers?** The Gemara answers: **Since** the Merciful One **wrote: "Fathers shall not be put to death for children,"** He **also wrote** in the same manner: **"Nor shall children be put to death for fathers."**

### Ger vs. Eved

One might think this same derivation would disqualify a *ger* from testimony — after all, a *ger* is also not related to his father. The *Gemara* refutes this. The verse contains two parallel clauses: *avot al banim* (fathers who have no relation to their sons) and *banim al avot* (sons who have no relation to their fathers). The first clause — *avot al banim*, a father who is not related to his children — applies uniquely to a slave, because a slave has no recognized relationship either upward to his own parents or downward to his children. A *ger*, by contrast, has no relationship upward (his father is not halachically his father after conversion) but does have a relationship downward — once he converts, his children are his children. He is a *banim* who lacks *avot*, not an *avot* who lacks *banim*.

The *Torah*'s inclusion of both clauses is thus deliberate and precise: *avot al banim* excludes the slave (who lacks relation in both directions), while *banim al avot* is written only to create a stylistic parallel, not to exclude the *ger*. Had the *Torah* wanted to exclude the *ger*, it would have written only *banim lo yumtu al avot* without the possessive suffix, which would have been sufficient to invalidate both *ger* and *eved* — the *ger* directly, and the *eved* via a *kal vachomer* (since an *eved* lacks both upward and downward relation, he is certainly to be excluded if a *ger* who lacks only one direction is excluded). The fact that the *Torah* instead used *avot al banim* — the clause that uniquely fits only the slave — signals that the *ger* is *kasher le-eidus*.

The parallel *banim lo yumtu al avot* is present purely because the *Torah* writes in balanced, parallel language, mirroring the structure of the first clause. Its presence carries no additional legal exclusion. The conclusion stands: a *ger* is fully valid as a witness; it is only the slave — who has no *yichus* either upward or downward — who is disqualified.

This prompts *Tosafot* to raise a broader concern: how far does *Rabbi Yehuda*'s exclusion of the slave from *achvah* actually reach? If *achvah* is the operative word in laws of *ribbis*, *ona'ah*, *gonev nefesh*, and even *tzedaka*, are all of these to be understood as inapplicable to a slave? *Tosafot* acknowledges the difficulty without fully resolving it. He notes that *gonev nefesh* may be categorically limited to Jews based on the phrase *b'nei Yisrael* even for the Rabbis. As for *ribbis* and *ona'ah*, there is a technical point that a slave has no independent property and therefore these prohibitions simply have no application. But the question of *tzedaka* is pointed: is a slave not included in *achicha he'ani* — your poor brother? There may be a hint from the first chapter of *Gittin* that the obligation of *tzedaka* toward a slave is qualitatively different, though this is not fully demonstrated. What *Tosafot* leaves us with is the sharp recognition that once we accept *Rabbi Yehuda*'s categorical exclusion, its ramifications ripple through a remarkable range of interpersonal obligations.

## Gift of Melog Property

### Ketivatah L'Nechsa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן פְּגִיעָתָן רָעָה. אִימֵּיהּ דְּרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַבָּא מֵהַגְרוֹנְיָא הֲוָת נְסִיבָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַבָּא, כְּתַבְתִּינְהוּ (לְנִכְסֵי) [לְנִכְסַהּ] לְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַבָּא בְּרַהּ. בָּתַר דִּשְׁכִיבָא,
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna (87a) teaches: With regard to **a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor, an encounter with them is disadvantageous,** since one who injures them is liable. But if they were the ones who injured, they are exempt. The Gemara relates an incident: **The mother of Rav Shmuel bar Abba from** the city of **Hagroneya was married to Rabbi Abba. She wrote,** i.e., signed over, her **property to Rav Shmuel bar Abba, her son. After she died,**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַבָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא, אוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּנִכְסֵי. אֲזַל רַבִּי אַבָּא אַמְרַהּ לְמִילְּתָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא, אֲזַל רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא אַמְרַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁמָּכְרָה בְּנִכְסֵי מְלוֹג בְּחַיֵּי בַּעְלָהּ, וָמֵתָה – הַבַּעַל מוֹצִיא מִיַּד הַלָּקוֹחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Abba went before Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba** to claim his mother's property. Rabbi Yirmeya **established him** as the owner **of the property,** in accordance with what was written in the document. **Rabbi Abba,** the mother's husband, **went** and **said** a report of **the matter before Rav Hoshaya. Rav Hoshaya went** and **said** a report of the matter **before Rav Yehuda.** Rav Yehuda **said to** Rav Hoshaya: **This** is what **Shmuel says:** In a case of **a woman who sold her usufruct property,** which belongs to her but whose profits her husband garners, **in her husband's lifetime, and** then she **died, the husband repossesses** the property **from the purchasers.** The granting of her property by means of the document is analogous to a sale, and Rabbi Abba can repossess the property from Rav Shmuel bar Abba.

The *Gemara* now introduces a case study that will anchor the remainder of the daf. The mother of *Rav Shmuel bar Abba* from Hagronia was married to *Rabbi Abba*. Before her death, she wrote over — gifted — all of her property to her son, *Rav Shmuel bar Abba*. The purpose of this transfer was to ensure that her property would pass to her own son rather than be inherited by her husband and then divided among his heirs. After she died, *Rav Shmuel bar Abba* brought the matter before *Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba*, who ruled that the property was indeed his — the mother's transfer was effective and the son was entitled to the property. (That *Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba* may himself be a stepbrother of the claimant goes unremarked by the *Gemara*.)

*Rabbi Abba* — the husband who stood to lose — then went and related the facts to *Rabbi Hoshaia*, who in turn reported them to *Rav Yehuda*.

### Samuel's Psak

*Rav Yehuda* responded with a ruling in the name of his teacher *Shmuel*: *ha'isha she'makhrah b'nichsei melog b'chayei ba'alah u'meitah, haba'al motzi miyad halakochos* — a woman who sold or transferred her *nichsei melog* property during her husband's lifetime, and then died, the husband may reclaim it from the purchasers.

The concept of *nichsei melog* requires explanation. This is property the wife brings into the marriage over which the title (*kinyan haguf*) remains hers, while the husband receives the right to benefit from the property's produce (*kinyan peiros*). To use a concrete illustration: if she brings an orchard into the marriage, she owns the orchard itself, but he collects the fruit each season. Alternatively, if she brings an apartment building, she holds the title while he collects the rent.

*Shmuel*'s ruling is that she cannot unilaterally transfer or sell this property while her husband is alive, because his *kinyan peiros* gives him a stake in it. Therefore the transfer the mother made to her son is ineffective, and *Rabbi Abba* should be able to recover the property.

### Mishnah: Kotev Nechsav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא מַתְנִיתָא יָדַעְנָא, דִּתְנַן: הַכּוֹתֵב נְכָסָיו לִבְנוֹ לְאַחַר מוֹתוֹ – הַבֵּן אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִמְכּוֹר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן בִּרְשׁוּת הָאָב; וְהָאָב אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִמְכּוֹר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן כְּתוּבִין לַבֵּן. מָכַר הָאָב – מְכוּרִים עַד שֶׁיָּמוּת. מָכַר הַבֵּן – אֵין לוֹ לַלּוֹקֵחַ עַד שֶׁיָּמוּת הָאָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said that** *halakha* **before Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba,** who had ruled that the property belongs to Rav Shmuel bar Abba. **He said to them: I know the mishna** that supports my opinion, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 136a): In the case of **one who writes** a document transferring ownership of **his property to his son,** stating that the transfer should take effect immediately but the son will have use of it only **after** the father's **death, the son cannot sell** the property **due to** the fact **that it is** still **in the father's possession. And the father,** even though he retained the right to use the property, **cannot sell** the property **due to** the fact **that it is written** as belonging **to the son.** If **the father sold** the property, then **it is sold** to the extent that the purchaser may use it **until** the father **dies.** If **the son sold** the property during his father's lifetime, **the purchaser does not have** the right to use the property **until the father dies.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מָיֵית אָב מִיהָא אִית לֵיהּ לְלוֹקֵחַ, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּמֵת הַבֵּן בְּחַיֵּי אָב – דְּלָא אֲתוֹ לִידֵי הַבֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba infers: **In any event,** if the son sold the property during his father's lifetime, **when the father dies the purchaser has** rights to the property. **And** this would be so **even though** it is a case **where the son died in the father's lifetime, where** the property **never came into the son's possession.**

*Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba* was not moved by this challenge. He cited a *Mishna* that he regarded as directly on point. The *Mishna* (in *Ketubot*) discusses a case of *hakotev nechsav livno le'achar moto* — a father who writes his property over to his son to take effect "from today and after I die." The legal effect of this language is that the son acquires the title (*kinyan haguf*) immediately, while the father retains the right to the *peiros* until his death.

The structure is perfectly parallel to *nichsei melog*: the son holds the *kinyan haguf* while the father holds the *kinyan peiros*, exactly as the wife holds the *kinyan haguf* of her *melog* property while the husband holds the *kinyan peiros*.

The *Mishna* states:

- *haben eino yakhol limkor mipenei shehen bireshut av* — the son cannot sell the property, since the father still controls the *peiros*.
- *ha'av eino yakhol limkor mipenei shehen ketuvin laben* — the father cannot sell, since the title is written over to the son.

Neither party can complete a sale on their own. However, if the father goes ahead and sells his rights, the purchaser acquires the *peiros* until the father dies. If the son sells his title, the purchaser acquires nothing until the father dies — but the sale of the title itself is effective.

The crucial inference is: *ki meit av miha itei lalokeiach* — when the father does eventually die, the purchaser from the son does receive the full property. This is true even *af al gav de'meit haben b'chayei ha'av* — even if the son dies while the father is still alive, so that the full possession never passed through the son's hands during his lifetime. The son's sale of his title worked even then.

This case maps directly onto the mother's situation: she transferred her title to *Rav Shmuel* even while her husband retained the *kinyan peiros*. The *Mishna* demonstrates that such a transfer of title is valid.

### Kinyan Peiros & Guf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר: לָא שְׁנָא מֵת הַבֵּן בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב – דְּלָא אֲתוֹ לִידֵיהּ דְּבֵן, לָא שְׁנָא מֵת הָאָב בְּחַיֵּי הַבֵּן – דַּאֲתוֹ לִידֵיהּ דְּבֵן; קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba's analysis is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, who says:** There is **no difference** if **the son died in the father's lifetime, where** the property **never came into the son's possession,** and there is **no difference** if **the father died in the son's lifetime, where the** property **came into the son's possession.** In any event, the **purchaser acquired** the property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִתְּמַר: מָכַר הַבֵּן בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב, וּמֵת הַבֵּן בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב – רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לֹא קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ, רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara records a dispute between the *amora'im* with regard to this point. **As was stated:** In a case where **the son sold** the property **in the father's lifetime, and the son died in the father's lifetime, Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The purchaser did not acquire** the property. **Reish Lakish says: The purchaser acquired** the property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר לֹא קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ – אָמַר לָךְ: כִּי קָתָנֵי מַתְנִיתִין ״מָכַר הַבֵּן – לֹא קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ עַד שֶׁיָּמוּת הָאָב״, וְכִי מָיֵית הָאָב אִית לֵיהּ לְלוֹקֵחַ – דְּלֹא מֵת הַבֵּן בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב, דַּאֲתוֹ לִידֵי הַבֵּן. אֲבָל מֵת הַבֵּן בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב, דְּלָא אֲתוֹ לִידֵיהּ דְּבֵן – כִּי מָיֵית אָב נָמֵי לֵית לֵיהּ לְלוֹקֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The purchaser did not acquire** the property, because he could have **said to you: When the mishna teaches** that if **the son sold** the property during his father's lifetime **the purchaser does not acquire** any rights to use the property **until the father dies, and** one could infer that **when the father dies the purchaser has** rights to the property, the mishna is discussing a case **where the son did not die during the father's lifetime,** so **that** the property **came into the son's possession** upon the father's death, before the purchaser acquired it. **But** if **the son died in the father's lifetime,** so **that** the property **did not come into the son's possession,** then **even when the father dies the purchaser does not have** rights to the property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קָא סָבַר: קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת – כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי, וְכִי זַבֵּין – לָאו דִּידֵיהּ זַבֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Apparently,** Rabbi Yoḥanan **holds** that **ownership of** the rights to use an item and to **its produce is considered to be like ownership of the item itself.** Even though the property itself did not belong to the father, it is as though the father owned the property, because all of the produce belonged to him in practice. Therefore, **when** the son **sold** the property, **he sold** property **that did not belong to him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ – כִּי קָתָנֵי מַתְנִיתִין: ״מָכַר הַבֵּן אֵין לַלּוֹקֵחַ עַד שֶׁיָּמוּת הָאָב״ – כִּי מָיֵית אָב מִיהַת אִית לֵיהּ לְלוֹקֵחַ; לָא שְׁנָא לָא מֵת הַבֵּן בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב – דַּאֲתוֹ לִידֵיהּ דְּבֵן, וְלָא שְׁנָא מֵת הַבֵּן בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב – דְּלָא אֲתוֹ לִידֵיהּ דְּבֵן; קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says** that the **purchaser acquired** the property based on this claim: **When the mishna teaches** that if **the son sold** the property during his father's lifetime **the purchaser does not have** any right to use the property **until the father dies,** and one could infer that **in any event, when the father dies the purchaser has** rights to the property, the mishna means that there is **no difference** if **the son did not die in the father's lifetime, where the** property **came into the son's possession, and** there is **no difference** if **the son died in the father's lifetime, where the** property **did not come into the son's possession.** In either case, the **purchaser acquired** the property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת לָאו כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי, וְכִי (קָא) זַבֵּין – דִּידֵיהּ (קָא) זַבֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Apparently,** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish **holds** that **ownership of** the rights to use an item and to **its produce is not considered to be like ownership of the item itself. And** therefore **when** the son **sold** the property, **he sold** property **that belonged to him.**

The *Mishna*'s conclusion — that the purchaser from the son receives the property when the father dies, even when the son predeceased the father — is the subject of a fundamental dispute between *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Resh Lakish*.

### Resh Lakish: Lav K'Kinya…

*Rabbi Yochanan* holds *lo kana lokeach* — the purchaser from the son does not acquire. His reasoning: since the father holds *kinyan peiros*, the father is considered a co-owner or even the primary owner. The son cannot sell a title while the father's rights effectively prevent the son from exercising any ownership. *Kinyan peiros ke-kinyan haguf dami* — the right to benefit from the produce is legally equivalent to title ownership. The sale the son attempted while the father was alive and active is therefore void. The purchaser could only receive the property if the father died first, allowing full possession to pass to the son before the sale took effect.

*Resh Lakish*, by contrast, holds *kana lokeach* — the purchaser does acquire. The son's title is real and transferable independent of the father's *kinyan peiros*. *Kinyan peiros lav k'kinyan haguf dami* — the right to the produce does not constitute ownership of the property. The father's retention of *peiros* does not impair the son's ability to convey title. Therefore, even if the son sold his title while the father was alive and then predeceased the father — so that full possession never passed through the son's hands — the purchaser (*Reuven*) will receive the property once the father dies.

The *Mishna*'s ruling that the purchaser from the son does receive the property when the father eventually dies, even in the scenario where the son died first, aligns with the position of *Resh Lakish*.

### Analogous Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲנַן הַשְׁתָּא, בֵּין רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא וּבֵין רַב יְהוּדָה – כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ סְבִירָא לְהוּ. וְקָאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי, כִּי מָיֵית אָב וּמָיֵית הַבֵּן בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב, אַמַּאי אִית לֵיהּ לְלוֹקֵחַ? כִּי (קָא) זַבֵּין הַאי – לָאו דִּידֵיהּ (קָא) זַבֵּין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the discussion of the opinion of Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba. **And now** for **us, whether** if one discusses the opinion of **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba and whether** one discusses the opinion of **Rav Yehuda, they** both **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish,** that ownership of the rights to use an item and to its produce is not considered to be like ownership of the item itself. **And Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says: If** it **enters your mind** that **ownership of** the rights to use an item and to **its produce is considered to be like ownership of the item itself,** then **when the father dies and the son** had **died in the father's lifetime, why does the purchaser have** rights to the property? **When** the son **sold** it, **didn't he sell** property **that did not belong to him?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת לָאו כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from** the mishna that **ownership of** the rights to use an item and to **its produce is not considered to be like ownership of the item itself?** Therefore, Rav Shmuel bar Abba should receive the property transferred to him by his mother, as the fact that the husband owned the rights to its produce does not limit her ability to transfer her property to her son.

The relevance to the mother's case becomes explicit. The *Gemara* notes that both *Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba* (who ruled in favor of *Rav Shmuel*) and *Rav Yehuda* (who initially challenged that ruling on the basis of *Shmuel*'s opinion) in fact both hold like *Resh Lakish* — that *kinyan peiros lav k'kinyan haguf dami*. This is the accepted principle in *halacha*.

*Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba*, in invoking the *Mishna*, was therefore pressing *Rav Yehuda*: if you hold like *Resh Lakish* — as everyone does — then you must accept that the son's sale or the mother's transfer of title works even while the party with *kinyan peiros* is still alive. The father's (or husband's) *kinyan peiros* does not prevent the transfer of title. Therefore *Rav Shmuel*'s mother's gift was effective, and *Rav Shmuel* is entitled to the property once *Rabbi Abba* dies.

The *Gemara* makes this reasoning explicit: if one were to hold *kinyan peiros ke-kinyan haguf dami*, then even the *Mishna*'s case would be incoherent — why would the purchaser from the son ever get the property, even when the father dies first? The answer is that the son's sale was void ab initio. Yet the *Mishna* clearly validates the purchaser's eventual acquisition. This can only be consistent with *kinyan peiros lav k'kinyan haguf dami*. Since everyone accepts the *Mishna* and everyone holds like *Resh Lakish*, the mother's transfer to her son should be fully effective.

### Zu Eina Doma L'Mishnasenu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהְדְּרוּהָ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, אֲמַר לְהוּ, הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: זוֹ אֵינָהּ דּוֹמָה לְמִשְׁנָתֵנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **brought back** Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba's reply **before Rav Yehuda.** Rav Yehuda **said to them: This is** what **Shmuel said: This** *halakha*, that a husband can repossess the property that his wife sold before she died, **is not similar to** the *halakha* of **our mishna** concerning a father who transfers his property to his son while retaining the right to garner the profits.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, בִּשְׁלָמָא אִי תָּנֵי אִיפְּכָא: ״הַכּוֹתֵב נְכָסָיו לְאָבִיו״ – אִיכָּא לְמִפְשַׁט מִינַּהּ דְּקִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת לָאו כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning** for distinguishing between the two cases? **Rav Yosef said: Granted, if** the mishna had **taught the opposite** and stated that in the case of **one who writes** a document transferring ownership of **his property to his father** after his death, with the son retaining the right to garner the profits until then, and the father sold the property during his son's lifetime, the purchaser has rights to the property after the son dies, then **it is** possible **to resolve from** the mishna **that ownership of** the rights to use an item and to **its produce is not considered to be like ownership of the item itself.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הַשְׁתָּא דְּקָתָנֵי: ״הַכּוֹתֵב נְכָסָיו לִבְנוֹ״, מִשּׁוּם דְּרָאוּי לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef continues: **But now that** the mishna **teaches** specifically: **One who writes** a document transferring ownership of **his property to his son,** the ability of the son to sell the property is **due to** the fact **that** the son **is fit to inherit** from **him** and already has a right to the property, and not just due to the existence of the document. In the case of Rav Shmuel bar Abba as well, the woman's property is inherited by her husband, not her son, so she cannot transfer it to Rav Shmuel bar Abba.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אַטּוּ בְּרָא יָרֵית אַבָּא, אַבָּא לָא יָרֵית בְּרָא?! אֶלָּא לְאַבְרוֹחִינְהוּ לְנִכְסֵי מִבְּרֵיהּ קָא אָתֵי; הָכָא נָמֵי, לְאַבְרוֹחִינְהוּ לְנִכְסֵי מֵאֲחוּהּ אָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **Is this to say that a son inherits** from **a father,** but **a father does not inherit** from **a son?** There is no difference between their rights to inherit from one another. **Rather,** even if the mishna taught a *halakha* concerning a son transferring his property to his father, the son's reason for doing so could have been that **he comes to hide his property from his** own **son** and wants his father to inherit from him instead. **Here too,** when a father transfers his property to his son, **he comes to hide his property from** the son's **brothers,** as the father has other sons but does not want them to inherit from him. Therefore, the mishna's choice of case cannot serve as a basis for inference.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי ״אֵינָהּ דּוֹמָה לְמִשְׁנָתֵנוּ״? מִשּׁוּם תַּקָּנַת אוּשָׁא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: בְּאוּשָׁא הִתְקִינוּ, הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁמָּכְרָה בְּנִכְסֵי מְלוֹג בְּחַיֵּי בַּעְלָהּ, וּמֵתָה – הַבַּעַל מוֹצִיא מִיַּד הַלָּקוֹחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **Rather, what** was Shmuel's reason for saying that the right of a husband to the property that his wife sold before she died **is not similar to our mishna** that states that a father does not have rights to property sold by his son? The difference is **due to** the rabbinic **ordinance of Usha,** an ordinance instituted with regard to a husband's rights to his wife's property. **As Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says: In** the town of **Usha** the Sages **instituted** that in the case of **a woman who sold her usufruct property in her husband's lifetime and** then **died, the husband repossesses** it **from the purchasers.** This ordinance was instituted only for the benefit of a husband, but not for the benefit of a father or son.

When this counterargument was reported back to *Rav Yehuda*, he responded in the name of *Shmuel*: *zu eina doma l'mishnasenu* — this case is not analogous to our *Mishna*. *Shmuel* maintained that the mother-to-son transfer is categorically different from the father-to-son scenario the *Mishna* addresses. The precise nature of that distinction — why the mother-son transfer cannot be resolved by the same principle that governs the father-son case — is left to be elaborated in the continuation of the *sugya* on the following daf.
