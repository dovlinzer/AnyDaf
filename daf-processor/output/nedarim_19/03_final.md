# Nedarim 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stam Nedarim Discrepancy

### Opening Discrepancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ סְתַם נְדָרִים לְהַחֲמִיר, וּפֵירוּשָׁם לְהָקֵל. כֵּיצַד? אָמַר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי כְּבָשָׂר מָלִיחַ״ ״כְּיֵין נֶסֶךְ״, אִם בְּשֶׁל שְׁלָמִים נָדַר — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Unspecified vows** are treated **stringently, but their specification,** if specification is necessary, is treated **leniently. How so?** If one **said: This** item is prohibited **to me like salted meat,** or: This item is prohibited to me **like** the **wine** used for **libations, if he vowed in reference to** meat or libations **of a peace-offering,** i.e., if he claimed that his intention was that the item will be forbidden to him like the salted meat of an offering, or like wine that is used for libations on the altar, it is **forbidden,** as he associated the item of the vow with an item forbidden by means of a vow, i.e., the offering.

The present daf continues a discussion of the principle of *stam nedarim le-hachmir, peirushan le-hakel* — that an ambiguous formulation of a *neder*, even when both possible interpretations would constitute a binding vow, is given the stricter construction. The *mishnah* dealt with cases where the language could be understood in two ways, one rendering the object a *davar hanadur* and the other not, and ruled that when the meaning is ambiguous we adopt the stricter interpretation. At the same time, the *mishnah* indicates that *peirusham le-hakel* — if the person were to clarify that he intended the lenient meaning, we would believe him. This raises the question of why we do not simply ask the person every time. Two explanations were offered: either the person is no longer available to ask, or the person genuinely had no specific intention, using language he heard others use without committing to one reading over another, in which case we supply the stricter meaning as a matter of legal principle.

The preceding *mishnah* also recorded a debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda concerning regional variation — specifically, how the standard meaning of the words *cherem* and *teruma* differs between *Galil* and *Yehuda*, such that the same term may produce a strict result in one region and a lenient result in the other.

### Three Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָתְנַן: סְפֵק נְזִירוּת לְהָקֵל?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara raises a problem with the principle stated in the mishna that unspecified vows are treated stringently. **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Taharot* 4:12): **Uncertainty** with regard to **naziriteship** is treated **leniently?** And naziriteship is a type of vow.

The *gemara* opens by pressing a contradiction. We say *stam nedarim le-hachmir*, yet a different *mishnah* rules that *safek nezirut le-hakel* — a doubtful *nezirut* is resolved leniently. Since *nezirut* is itself a species of *neder*, why should the two be treated differently? The *gemara* identifies three possible directions for resolving this tension. The first would distinguish between cases where the doubt arises from the ambiguity of external circumstances versus cases where the doubt arises from the meaning of the vow's language. The second would draw a distinction intrinsic to *nezirut* itself, given its particular characteristics. The third — which the *gemara* actually pursues — is to declare the discrepancy a *machlokes tannaim*.

### Rabbi Zeira Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הָא רַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ חַיָּיתוֹ וּבְהֶמְתּוֹ — הִקְדִּישׁ אֶת הַכּוֹי. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לֹא הִקְדִּישׁ אֶת הַכּוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said:** This is **not difficult. That** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** and **this** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with him. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One who consecrates his undomesticated animal and his domesticated animal has consecrated the *koy*** as well, although it is uncertain whether it is an undomesticated or a domesticated animal. **Rabbi Eliezer says: He has not consecrated the *koy*.**

Rabbi Zeira offers the resolution: *lo kashya, ha Rabbi Eliezer, ha Rabbanan* — there is no true contradiction, because the two rulings reflect the positions of Rabbi Eliezer and the *Rabbanan* respectively. The source is a *baraita*: if one consecrates all his *chaya* (wild animals) or all his *behema* (domesticated animals) to *hekdesh*, the question arises whether a *koi* — a hybrid creature of disputed zoological classification — is included. Rabbi Eliezer says it is not consecrated; the *Chachamim* say it is.

## Koi Consecration Cas (II)

### Koi Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר מָמוֹנוֹ מְעַיֵּיל לִסְפֵיקָא, גּוּפֵיהּ נָמֵי מְעַיֵּיל. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר מָמוֹנוֹ לָא מְעַיֵּיל לִסְפֵיקָא, גּוּפֵיהּ נָמֵי לָא מְעַיֵּיל לִסְפֵיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The** Rabbis **who said** that one **puts his possessions into** a state of **uncertainty,** and therefore a *koy* is included in the aforementioned vow, hold that one **enters himself into** a state of uncertainty **as well.** Therefore, uncertainty with regard to naziriteship is treated stringently. **And** Rabbi Eliezer, **who said** that one **does not put his possessions into** a state of **uncertainty,** and therefore the phrase: Undomesticated and domesticated animals, refers only to definitely undomesticated and definitely domesticated animals, holds that **all the more so,** one **does not enter himself into uncertainty either.** Therefore, uncertainty with regard to naziriteship is treated leniently.

The *koi* is an animal whose status is genuinely uncertain, some holding it to be a *min bifnei atzmo* — a distinct species — rather than simply a doubtful case between *chaya* and *behema*. According to the *Ran* or *Tosafos*, Rabbi Eliezer holds that the *koi* is indeed a *min bifnei atzmo*, meaning it belongs to neither category, and therefore even if one says he is consecrating all his *chayos* and all his *behemos*, the *koi* would not be consecrated — it falls into neither. The *Chachamim*, by contrast, hold that the *koi* definitely falls into one of the two categories; we simply do not know which one, and therefore if one consecrates both categories it is certainly *kadosh*.

The *gemara* reads this dispute as reflecting a broader disagreement about what to do with a *safek* in the context of *hekdesh* — and since *nedarim* draws on the model of *hekdesh*, the same dispute should govern *nedarim* and *nezirut* alike. The *Chachamim*, who are *machmir* about consecrating property under conditions of doubt, would similarly be *machmir* when one makes himself a *nazir* under doubtful conditions. Rabbi Eliezer, who is *meikel* about consecrated property under doubt, would likewise be *meikel* about *nezirut*.

At this point the *gemara* makes a significant observation. One might have thought that there is a natural distinction between *nedarim*, which govern the status of objects, and *nezirut*, which governs a person's own status — and that people are more reluctant to impose obligations upon themselves than upon their property. Indeed, this distinction would have neatly resolved the original contradiction: for objects, we rule *stam nedarim le-hachmir*; for one's own person, *safek nezirut le-hakel*. The *gemara* explicitly signals awareness of this possible answer but sets it aside, preferring instead to treat both cases under a single framework and declare it a *machlokes tannaim*.

Woven into this discussion is the concept of *ma'eil inish nafshei le-sfeika* — whether a person would willingly subject himself to a doubtful status — and *ma'eil mamonei le-sfeika* — whether a person would willingly subject his property to such doubt. Until this point, one might have assumed that *stam nedarim le-hachmir* is purely a legal interpretive principle — a rule for how courts construe ambiguous language — with no reference to the vower's subjective intent. But the *gemara*'s introduction of the *ma'eil* framework suggests that the question of what a person would have wanted is itself part of the legal analysis. This can be understood as follows: rather than asking about this particular individual's intent, we adopt a general interpretive presumption — that people making these types of declarations typically intend either the broader or the narrower meaning — and apply that presumption uniformly as a matter of law. The *Ran* supports the more formalist reading: even if the person says he had no specific meaning in mind, the legal principle determines the meaning of his words regardless.

## Safek Bechoros Digre (II)

### Safek Bechoros

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא לִסְפֵק נְזִירוּת לְהָקֵל — כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: סְפֵק בְּכוֹרוֹת, אֶחָד בְּכוֹרֵי אָדָם וְאֶחָד בְּכוֹרֵי בְהֵמָה, בֵּין טְמֵאָה בֵּין טְהוֹרָה — הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabbi Zeira: **In what** manner **did you establish** the mishna that states **that uncertainty** with regard to **naziriteship** is treated **leniently?** You established it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer. Say the latter clause** of that mishna: If there is **uncertainty** with regard to **firstborns, whether human firstborns, or animal firstborns, whether non-kosher** firstborns, i.e., the firstborn of a donkey, **or** the firstborn of **kosher** animals, **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.** In other words, the priest cannot take the redemption money from the father of the child, or the animal from its owner, and conversely, if the father or owner mistakenly gave it to a priest, he does not get it back.

Abaye challenges the identification of *safek nezirut le-hakel* with Rabbi Eliezer. If that leniency is Rabbi Eliezer's position, then the same *mishnah* that rules *safek nezirut le-hakel* should reflect his position throughout. Yet the *mishnah* also rules on *safek bechoros* — cases of doubtful firstborn status — and the *gemara* cites a *baraita* stating that a *safek bechor* is forbidden for shearing and labor. If a cow gave birth to twins and it is unknown whether the firstborn was male or female, the male calf's *bechor* status is uncertain. In terms of monetary law (*Choshen Mishpat*), the principle of *hamotzi me-chaveiro alav ha-rayah* applies — the *kohen* cannot collect without proof. But in terms of *issur ve-heter*, since *bechorah* is a biblical obligation, *safek d'oraita le-chumra* applies and the animal is forbidden for shearing or work. This is a straightforward *safek d'oraita*, with nothing to do with Rabbi Eliezer's position — and yet it rules strictly, in contrast to the leniency by *nezirut*. The question stands: why are some doubtful cases treated leniently and others strictly?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: וַאֲסוּרִים בְּגִיזָּה וַעֲבוֹדָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita* **in that regard: But with regard to shearing and working** these uncertain animal firstborns, they are **forbidden,** just like definite firstborns. This indicates a difference between the monetary issue, with regard to which it is ruled that the animal cannot be taken from the owner by the priest, and the prohibition, which applies despite the uncertainty. Evidently, even the *tanna* of this mishna does not hold that all uncertainties with regard to consecration are to be treated leniently.

## Kedusha Types

### Kedusha Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי קָא מְדַמֵּית קְדוּשָּׁה הַבָּאָה מֵאֵלֶיהָ, לִקְדוּשָּׁה הַבָּאָה בִּידֵי אָדָם?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira **said to** Abaye: This is not difficult. **Why do you compare sanctity that emerges by itself,** i.e., the sanctity of a firstborn, which results from objective reality and not human intent, **to sanctity that emerges by** the volition of **a person** and is dependent on his intention? Only with regard to the latter type of sanctity can it be established that a person does not intend to consecrate an item in an uncertain manner.

The *gemara* answers by drawing a fundamental distinction: *ami ka-medamit kedusha ba'ah mei-eilya li-kedusha ba'ah bi-yedei adam* — why are you comparing a *kedusha* that arrives on its own to a *kedusha* that is conferred by a human act? The *bechor* animal either is or is not a *bechor* — its sanctity, if it exists, arrived independently of any human declaration. The question is purely one of fact: was the male calf born first? There is one factual reality, unknown to us, and based on that reality the animal is either definitively *assur* or definitively *muttar*. This is a classic *safek d'oraita*, and the rule *safek d'oraita le-chumra* applies.

### Facts vs. Interpretation

By contrast, when a person makes a *neder* or declares himself a *nazir* using ambiguous language, there is no unresolved factual question — the facts are fully known. The person spoke certain words. The question is entirely one of legal interpretation: how do we construe those words? What scope do we assign to that language? This is not a question about what happened in the world; it is a question about the legal meaning of a speech act. And if we adopt the legal principle that we interpret such language narrowly — *stam nezirut le-hakel* — then the *kedusha* was simply never conferred. The rule of *safek d'oraita le-chumra* does not apply because we are not uncertain about a fact; we have chosen, as a matter of interpretive law, to give the declaration its narrower construction.

This distinction — facts versus interpretation — is analytically powerful and the *gemara* presents it as resolving the question about *safek bechoros*. The case of *bechoros* cannot be compared to *nedarim* or *nezirut* because in *bechoros* we are uncertain about a fact, while in *nedarim* we are choosing how to interpret language.

## Safek Bechoros Digre (II)

### Liquids Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי קַשְׁיָא, הָא קַשְׁיָא: סְפֵק מַשְׁקִין, לִיטָּמֵא — טָמֵא, לְטַמֵּא אֲחֵרִים — טָהוֹר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר כִּדְבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if** Rabbi Zeira's answer **is difficult, this** is what **is difficult.** It is stated in that same mishna: In the case of **liquid** with regard to which there is **uncertainty** whether it became ritually impure through contact with someone who was ritually impure, the *halakha* is as follows: It is considered **impure** with regard to its **being impure** in and of itself, but it is considered **pure** with regard to its ability **to render other** items **impure.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And Rabbi Elazar would also say in accordance with the statements of** Rabbi Meir.

Having resolved the *bechoros* question, the *gemara* turns to a more difficult challenge from the same *mishnah*. The *mishnah* rules: *safek mashkin le-tamei tamei, le-tamei acheirim tahor* — if there is doubt whether liquid was rendered *tamei*, we rule it *tamei* regarding itself but *tahor* regarding its capacity to transmit *tumah* to other objects. This is the ruling of Rabbi Meir, and the *mishnah* adds that Rabbi Eliezer agreed with this ruling. But the *gemara* challenges: does Rabbi Eliezer really hold that liquids become *tamei* under conditions of doubt? For there is a *baraita* stating that Rabbi Eliezer holds *ein tumah le-mashkin kol ikar* — there is no biblical basis for *tumah* of liquids whatsoever. The proof is the famous testimony of Rabbi Yose ben Yoezer of Tzereda, who testified about the *ayyal kimtza* locust that it is *dakhan* (the Aramaic equivalent of *tahor*, meaning kosher for consumption), and that *mashkin beit mitbachaya* — the liquids in the Temple slaughterhouse — are likewise *dakhan*, meaning they do not transmit *tumah*. The underlying principle is that *tumat mashkin* is only rabbinic, and in the *Beis HaMikdash* we revert to the biblical rule, eliminating the concern entirely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לִיטָּמֵא טָמֵא? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין טוּמְאָה לְמַשְׁקִין כׇּל עִיקָּר. תֵּדַע: שֶׁהֲרֵי הֵעִיד יוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה עַל אַיָּל קַמְצָא דְּכַן, וְעַל מַשְׁקִין בֵּית מִטְבְּחַיָּא דְּכַן?
>
> **Translation:** According to Rabbi Zeira's assertion that the rulings of this mishna with regard to uncertainty are in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, this causes a difficulty: **But does Rabbi Eliezer hold** that with regard to liquid of uncertain ritual status **being impure, it is** considered **impure?** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** By Torah law, **impurity does not apply to liquids at all? Know** that this is so, **as Yosei ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida testified concerning** a grasshopper species called ***eil kamtza*** that it is **kosher, and concerning the liquids of the** Temple **slaughterhouse** that **they are pure.** Liquids are susceptible to ritual impurity only by rabbinic law, and liquids in the Temple were not included in this decree so as not to cause additional impurity there. Since Rabbi Eliezer holds that by Torah law liquids are not susceptible to impurity, how can it be his opinion that liquids of uncertain ritual status are considered impure?

If *tumat mashkin* is only rabbinic, then in a case of doubt whether liquid was touched, the rule of *safek d'rabanan le-kula* should apply, and we should be lenient. Yet Rabbi Eliezer, if he agrees with the *mishnah*'s ruling, appears to be strict even in this rabbinic doubt. How can that be reconciled with his leniency by the *koi*?

### Shmuel Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר: דְּכַן מִלְּטַמֵּא אֲחֵרִים, אֲבָל טוּמְאַת עַצְמָן יֵשׁ בָּהֶן, שַׁפִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This works out well according to Shmuel, who said** that the meaning of Rabbi Eliezer's ruling that the liquids of the Temple slaughterhouse are **pure** is that they cannot **render other** items impure, **but they themselves are** susceptible to **impurity.** Accordingly, liquids are susceptible to impurity by Torah law; only their ability to render other items impure is by rabbinic law. The ruling in the mishna that liquid of uncertain ritual status is considered impure is therefore consistent with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, and Rabbi Zeira's answer works out **well.**

The *gemara* offers a resolution attributed to Shmuel: the testimony of Rabbi Yose ben Yoezer should be understood as stating that the Temple liquids are pure only in the sense that they do not make other things *tamei* — *le-tamei acheirim* — but they themselves do become *tamei* at the biblical level. According to Shmuel, *tumat mashkin* at the level of the liquid becoming *tamei* itself is biblical; only its capacity to transmit *tumah* to other objects is rabbinic. This maps perfectly onto the *mishnah*'s split ruling: for the liquid becoming *tamei* itself, it is a *safek d'oraita* and we rule strictly; for the liquid making other things *tamei*, it is a *safek d'rabanan* and we rule leniently. *Safek d'oraita le-chumra, safek d'rabanan le-kula* — this is a clean and precise application of the principle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַב דְּאָמַר דְּכַן מַמָּשׁ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **However, according to Rav, who said** that they are **actually pure,** i.e., they are not susceptible to impurity, **what is there to say?** The mishna that is lenient with regard to uncertain naziriteship and stringent with regard to liquid of uncertain ritual status is clearly not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer.

However, according to Rav, who holds that *mashkin* are completely *tahor* at the biblical level (*dakhan mamash*), the entire *tumah* of liquids is rabbinic — yet Rabbi Eliezer is still strict when there is doubt. The *gemara* finds this difficult: if it is all rabbinic, why would Rabbi Eliezer be strict by *safek mashkin* yet lenient in the case of the *koi*? The *Ran* acknowledges that the comparison is not rigorous — the *bechoros* distinction (facts versus interpretation) does not map cleanly onto this new question, and the *gemara* seems to be reasoning more loosely, along the lines of: if Rabbi Eliezer is strict even about a rabbinic *safek* of liquids, it would be strange for him to be lenient about *safek nezirut*. This is admittedly not a tight argument. The *gemara* itself appears to treat it as something less than fully compelling.

In any event, the *gemara* concludes by abandoning the approach of Rabbi Zeira entirely. The whole framework identifying *safek nezirut le-hakel* with Rabbi Eliezer's position may have been based more on his particular view of the *koi* as a *min bifnei atzmo* than on a general principle about doubtful *kedusha*, and it does not survive scrutiny. The *gemara* therefore returns to the original problem fresh.

## Yehuda-Shimon Debate

### Grain Condition Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְהָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. דְּתַנְיָא: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר אִם יֵשׁ בִּכְרִי הַזֶּה מֵאָה כּוֹר״, וְהָלַךְ וּמְצָאוֹ שֶׁנִּגְנַב אוֹ שֶׁאָבַד — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Zeira's answer should be rejected, and the contradiction between the mishna here, which states that unspecified vows should be treated stringently, and the mishna in tractate *Teharot*, which states that uncertain naziriteship should be treated leniently, should be resolved as follows: **That** mishna, in *Teharot*, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and this** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that if someone says: **I am hereby a nazirite if there are in this heap** of grain **one hundred *kor*, and he went** to measure the heap **and found that it was stolen or that it was lost** and cannot be measured, **Rabbi Yehuda permits** him to perform actions forbidden to a nazirite, as he holds that this uncertain naziriteship does not take effect. **And Rabbi Shimon prohibits** him from doing so, as he maintains that it does take effect. This indicates that Rabbi Shimon is of the opinion that uncertain naziriteship is treated stringently.

The *gemara* proposes a new resolution: *ha Rabbi Yehuda, ha Rabbi Shimon* — the discrepancy between *stam nedarim le-hachmir* and *safek nezirut le-hakel* reflects a debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon specifically about *nezirut*. The source is a *baraita* from *Masechet Nazir*:

The person then goes to measure the grain and finds it was stolen or lost. There is now no way ever to determine whether the condition was met.

### Yehuda Matir Shimon Oser

Rabbi Yehuda says *matir* — the person is not a *nazir*. Rabbi Shimon says *oser* — the person is a *nazir*. This is a direct dispute about *safek nezirut*: Rabbi Yehuda rules leniently, Rabbi Shimon rules strictly. The *gemara* identifies Rabbi Yehuda's lenient position with *safek nezirut le-hakel* and Rabbi Shimon's strict position with the principle of *stam nedarim le-hachmir*, treating *neder* and *nezirut* as parallel throughout.

It is worth pausing to note an important conceptual difficulty that the *gemara* does not fully address. There is a significant difference between the question of how to interpret ambiguous language — which is the subject of *stam nedarim le-hachmir* — and the question of what to do when a clearly-intended vow is conditioned on a fact that remains unknowable. In the *stam nedarim* discussion, the person's language is ambiguous; we must choose a legal construction for words of uncertain meaning. In the grain case, the person clearly intended to become a *nazir* if a condition was met; the question is simply whether that condition was ever satisfied. These are logically distinct questions — one turns on interpretive principle, the other on factual uncertainty — and one could easily hold differently in the two cases. The *gemara*, however, treats them as equivalent and lumps them together as a single debate about whether *safek* produces stringency or leniency in the realm of *nedarim* and *nezirut*.

## Rava Explanation

### Safek Chamer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָמֵי דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מִי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא מְעַיֵּיל אִינִישׁ נַפְשֵׁיהּ לִסְפֵיקָא? וּרְמִינְהִי, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סְתַם תְּרוּמָה בִּיהוּדָה — אֲסוּרָה, וּבַגָּלִיל — מוּתֶּרֶת, שֶׁאֵין אַנְשֵׁי הַגָּלִיל מַכִּירִין אֶת תְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. טַעְמָא דְּאֵין מַכִּירִין, הָא מַכִּירִין — אֲסוּרִין!
>
> **Translation:** And the Gemara **raises a contradiction** between the statement **of Rabbi Yehuda and** another statement **of Rabbi Yehuda. Did Rabbi Yehuda** actually **say** that **a person does not enter himself into** a state of **uncertainty,** and therefore as long as the volume of the heap is unknown, naziriteship does not take effect? **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the mishna, where **Rabbi Yehuda says: Unspecified *teruma* in Judea is forbidden but in the Galilee it is permitted, as the people of the Galilee are not familiar with the collection of the chamber.** The Gemara infers: **The reason** it is permitted is **that they are not familiar;** **but** where **they are familiar,** it is **forbidden,** even if the person mentioned *teruma* without specification, and there is still uncertainty with regard to which *teruma* he was referring.

The *gemara* then raises a contradiction against Rabbi Yehuda himself: *mi amar Rabbi Yehuda lo ma'eil inish nafshei le-sfeika* — does Rabbi Yehuda really say that a person would not willingly subject himself to doubtful status? This is offered as the logical underpinning of his leniency in the grain case: since being a *safek nazir* is worse than being a *vadai nazir*, a person would never have intended for an ambiguous condition to bind him. Yet the same Rabbi Yehuda elsewhere states *stam terumah bi-Yehudah assurah* — that an ambiguous declaration of *teruma* in *Yehuda* is forbidden — and the *mishnah* records his position that *stam nedarim le-hachmir* applies whenever the language is genuinely ambiguous. This shows that Rabbi Yehuda endorses *stam nedarim le-hachmir* as a general principle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: גַּבֵּי כְּרִי קָסָבַר כׇּל שֶׁסְּפֵיקוֹ חָמוּר מִוַּדַּאי — לָא מְעַיֵּיל נַפְשֵׁיהּ לִסְפֵיקָא, דְּאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי נָזִיר וַדַּאי, מְגַלֵּחַ וּמֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן וְנֶאֱכָל. עַל סְפֵיקוֹ, לָא מָצֵי מְגַלַּח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that the contradiction can be resolved as follows: **With regard to** the case of a vow conditioned on the volume of **a heap,** Rabbi Yehuda **holds** that **wherever uncertainty is more stringent than certainty, one does not enter himself into** a state of **uncertainty.** Uncertain naziriteship is more stringent than definite naziriteship, **as while a definite nazirite** has a remedy, i.e., at the end of his naziriteship he **shaves** his hair **and brings an offering and it is eaten, one cannot shave** his hair **for uncertain** naziriteship. He cannot bring an offering in case he is not a nazirite, which would render his offering a non-sacred animal in the Temple courtyard. Since he cannot bring an offering he may not shave.

Rava resolves the contradiction: *gabi keri kasavar kol safek chamur mi-vadai, lo ma'eil nafshei le-sfeika* — specifically regarding the grain pile case, the reason for leniency is that being a *safek nazir* is in fact worse than being a *vadai nazir*, and therefore a person would never have intended for his conditional vow to create that status. A *vadai nazir* completes his *nezirut* after thirty days, shaves his head, and brings his *korbanot*. A *safek nazir*, by contrast, cannot bring his *korbanot* — this would be *safek chulin ba-azarah* — and therefore can never formally conclude his *nezirut*. He is permanently suspended in an unresolvable doubtful status. Since this situation is demonstrably worse than being a definite *nazir*, the interpretive presumption is that no person would have wished to enter it. Therefore, any ambiguity in the condition is resolved in favor of the vow never having taken effect.

For a *neder*, by contrast, there is no analogous problem. If one's *neder* is treated as binding *mi-safek*, the consequence is that the object is prohibited — but this is a manageable restriction, not an impossible situation. One can live with a *safek neder* in a way that one cannot live with *safek nezirut*. Therefore by *nedarim* we say *stam le-hachmir*, while by *nezirut* we say *le-hakel*.

This resolution is elegant and analytically compelling. It means that the answer to the *gemara*'s original question — why *stam nedarim le-hachmir* but *safek nezirut le-hakel* — does not require positing a *machlokes tannaim* at all. The difference is intrinsic to the subject matter: a doubtful *neder* is tolerable, while a doubtful *nezirut* is a trap. Therefore we always interpret *nezirut* conditions in the more lenient direction, not as a matter of general dispute, but because of the specific nature of *nezirut* and its consequences.

### Nazir Olam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה לְרָבָא: אָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נְזִיר עוֹלָם״, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Yehuda said to Rava:** According to your answer, that Rabbi Yehuda holds that he is not a nazirite only because uncertain naziriteship is more stringent than definite naziriteship, if one **said:** If there are a hundred *kor* in this heap **I am hereby a permanent nazirite, what** is the *halakha*? In this case, uncertainty is apparently not more stringent than certainty, as the naziriteship will never end even if it is definite.

Rav Huna bar Yehuda pressed Rava: what if the conditional vow was phrased as *Haraini nazir olam* — a *nazir* forever? In that case, why would *safek* be worse than *vadai*? A *nazir olam* by definition never concludes his *nezirut*; the inability to bring concluding *korbanot* is no additional burden. There should therefore be no reason to resolve the doubt leniently.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: נְזִיר עוֹלָם נָמֵי סְפֵיקוֹ חָמוּר מִוַּדַּאי, דְּאִילּוּ וַדַּאי הִכְבִּיד שְׂעָרוֹ — מֵיקֵל בְּתַעַר, וּמֵבִיא שָׁלוֹשׁ בְּהֵמוֹת. וְאִילּוּ סְפֵיקוֹ — לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: Even** with regard to **a permanent nazirite, his uncertainty is more stringent than certainty** with regard to a different *halakha*: **As** when **the hair of a definite** nazirite **is** too **heavy** for him **he may lighten** the hair **with a razor and bring** the **three animal** offerings that a nazirite brings when he has completed his term of naziriteship, before continuing to observe naziriteship; **whereas** in a case of **uncertainty** he **cannot** do so. Since it is not certain that he is a nazirite, he cannot bring these offerings and is therefore prohibited from shaving. Therefore, uncertainty is more stringent than certainty with regard to a permanent nazirite as well.

Rava answered that even by *nazir olam* the *safek* is worse than the *vadai*. A definite *nazir olam* has a special leniency: *hikhvid se'aro meikel be-ta'ar* — if his hair becomes too burdensome, he may trim it by bringing three *korbanot*, even though this does not terminate the *nezirut*. This is an unusual *halacha* that recognizes the extraordinary burden of lifelong *nezirut* and permits periodic relief through the *korban* process. A *safek nazir olam*, however, cannot avail himself of this leniency — bringing the *korbanot* would again raise the problem of *safek chulin ba-azarah*. Thus even *nazir olam* as a *safek* is worse than *nazir olam* as a *vadai*.

### Nazir Shimshon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נְזִיר שִׁמְשׁוֹן״ מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna further asked him: If one **said:** If there are one hundred *kor* in this heap, **I am hereby a nazirite like Samson** (Judges, chapters 13–16), i.e., he would be like Samson, whose permanent naziriteship could not be dissolved and who had no remedy at all, even by bringing offerings, and therefore could never shave, **what** is the *halakha*? Uncertainty is definitely not more stringent than certainty in this case.

Rav Huna bar Yehuda pressed further: what about a case where the person declared *Haraini nazir Shimshon* conditionally? *Nazir Shimshon* is a special category derived from the life of Shimshon, who was a *nazir* yet became *tamei la-meitim* and could never cut his hair. From Shimshon we learn that there is a form of *nezirut* in which one may become *tamei* to the dead — unlike a standard *nazir* — but can never cut his hair under any circumstances, even with the leniency that applies to *nazir olam*. By *nazir Shimshon*, neither a definite nor a doubtful status carries the leniency of hair-trimming, so there would again seem to be no difference between *vadai* and *safek*. Why then should the condition not be binding?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נְזִיר שִׁמְשׁוֹן לָא תַּנְיָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: תַּנְיָא נְזִיר שִׁמְשׁוֹן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי תַּנְיָא — תַּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him:** The concept of **a nazirite** like **Samson is not taught.** It was not mentioned at all by the Sages, as the naziriteship of Samson could not have been created through a vow. It was a one-time, divine order that cannot be emulated. Rav Huna **said to him: But didn't Rav Adda bar Ahava say** that the concept of **a nazirite** like **Samson is taught** in a *baraita*, which shows that it takes effect? Rava **said to him: If** this *baraita* **is taught,** it **is taught,** and I cannot take issue with it. Apparently, Rabbi Yehuda acknowledges that in this case even uncertain naziriteship takes effect.

The *gemara* responded: *nazir Shimshon lo tanya* — the *mishnah* does not deal with the case of *nazir Shimshon*; it was not contemplated in the standard ruling. Abaye bar Avin replied that there is in fact a *baraita* that does address this scenario — a conditional vow of *nazir Shimshon* — and Rabbi Yehuda still rules leniently even there. Rava conceded: *im tanya, tanya* — if there is such a *baraita*, it stands, though he remained unpersuaded by its logic.

## Ki Yafla Principle

### Tarfon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הָהִיא — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין אֶחָד מֵהֶם נָזִיר — לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא נִיתְּנָה נְזִירוּת אֶלָּא לְהַפְלָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** a different resolution to the contradiction between the statements of Rabbi Yehuda. **That** *baraita*, in which Rabbi Yehuda treats uncertain naziriteship leniently, **is** the statement of **Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Tarfon. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a number of people wager on the truth of a statement, and they stipulate that whoever is correct will be a nazirite, **Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Tarfon: None of them is a nazirite** no matter who is correct, **because naziriteship was given** to take effect **only through explicitness** of intent. A vow of naziriteship takes effect only if it is taken unconditionally. Therefore, in the case of the heap, since the speaker was uncertain of its volume at the time the vow of naziriteship was taken, the vow does not take effect.

Rav Ashi offers yet another approach. The leniency of *safek nezirut le-hakel* is not Rabbi Yehuda's own position at all — it is a ruling transmitted by Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Tarfon. The *baraita* reads: *Rabbi Yehuda mishum Rabbi Tarfon omer, ein echad me-hem nazir* — neither person is a *nazir*. The case described involves two people walking and seeing a figure in the distance: one says "I will be a *nazir* if that is so-and-so" and the other says "I will be a *nazir* if that is not so-and-so." Since one of the two propositions must be true, it seems that one of them must be a *nazir*. Nevertheless, Rabbi Tarfon rules that neither becomes a *nazir*.

### Ki Yafla Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי מַאי אִירְיָא שֶׁנִּגְנַב אוֹ שֶׁאָבַד? אֶלָּא, לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּנִגְנַב אוֹ שֶׁאָבַד, קָסָבַר מְעַיֵּיל אִינִישׁ נַפְשֵׁיהּ לִסְפֵיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, why** is it **specifically** stated **that** the heap **was stolen or that it was lost?** Even if it was still present and measured, the naziriteship would not have taken effect as it was conditioned and was not a clear expression. The Gemara answers: **Rather,** that detail was established **to convey the far-reaching nature** of the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon, that even** if it is the case **that it was stolen or that it was lost** and consequently cannot be measured, nevertheless **he holds** that **a person enters himself into uncertainty,** and therefore the vow takes effect.

The reason: *she-lo nitna nezirut ela le-hafla'ah* — *nezirut* was given only for *hafla'ah*, a term derived from the biblical verse *ish o isha ki yafli lindor neder nazir le-hazzir la-Hashem* (Bamidbar 6:2). The word *yafli* is understood to mean an articulate, precise, and unambiguous declaration. *Hafla'ah* is the concept underlying all speech-act *kedusha* — *neder*, *shevua*, *nezirut*, and *hekdesh* are all effectuated through *hafla'ah*. Here, however, *ki yafli* is read as a requirement for clarity: if there is any ambiguity at the moment of declaration, the *nezirut* does not take effect. This is a pure legal principle, entirely independent of what the person wanted or what is in his mind. Even if the person believed with certainty that it was or was not the person in the distance — and was objectively correct — *nezirut* does not bind when the declaration was made in a context of uncertainty apparent to observers. *Nezirut* requires that it be unambiguously established from the outset.

This principle, rooted in the *pasuk*, provides the cleanest and most legally grounded answer to the question. It is not about what a person would or would not want; it is not about the consequences of doubtful *nezirut*; it is a textual requirement that *nezirut* take effect only through unambiguous declaration.

### Grain Lost Detail

The *gemara* notes a textual precision in the grain case: the *baraita* specifically says the grain was stolen or lost. If the principle of *ki yafli* means that *nezirut* cannot take effect whenever there is any ambiguity at the time of vowing, then even if the grain were present and measurable, since it was not clear at the time whether the condition would be met, the *nezirut* should not be binding — and the detail of theft or loss would be unnecessary. The *gemara* answers: you are correct that according to Rabbi Yehuda, citing Rabbi Tarfon, even if the grain were present and measurable, the person would not be a *nazir*, since the declaration was made in a state of uncertainty. The *baraita* specifies the case of theft or loss as a *chiddush* for Rabbi Shimon's strict position: Rabbi Shimon holds that a person is willing to enter into doubtful status (*ma'eil inish nafshei le-sfeika*), and therefore even though the condition can never be verified, the *nezirut* takes effect. The more striking *chiddush* is actually Rabbi Tarfon's leniency, but the *baraita* is framed around the case that provides the greatest *chiddush* for the strict position.

## Stam Teruma Regions (II)

### Teruma Strict Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סְתַם תְּרוּמָה בִּיהוּדָה — אֲסוּרָה, וּבַגָּלִיל — מוּתֶּרֶת, שֶׁאֵין אַנְשֵׁי הַגָּלִיל מַכִּירִין אֶת תְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. סְתַם חֲרָמִים, בִּיהוּדָה — מוּתָּרִין, בַּגָּלִיל — אֲסוּרִין, שֶׁאֵין אַנְשֵׁי הַגָּלִיל מַכִּירִין אֶת חֶרְמֵי הַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: Unspecified *teruma* in Judea is forbidden.** However, **in the Galilee it is permitted, as the people of the Galilee are unfamiliar with the collection of the chamber.** When they say *teruma* they are referring to the *teruma* allotted to the priests, which is familiar to them. Conversely, **unspecified dedications in Judea are permitted,** but **in the Galilee they are forbidden, as the people of the Galilee are unfamiliar with dedications** allotted **to the priests,** so when they say dedication they are referring to dedication to Heaven.

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishnah* regarding regional linguistic variation. Rabbi Yehuda says: *stam terumah bi-Yehudah assurah, uva-Galil muteret* — an ambiguous declaration of *teruma* in *Yehuda* is forbidden, but in *Galil* it is permitted. The reason: in *Yehuda*, the word *teruma* can mean either *terumat hagoren* (the ordinary agricultural *teruma*) or *terumat halishka* (the Temple chamber contribution), and since *terumat halishka* is a *davar hanadur* while *terumat hagoren* is not, the ambiguity generates a genuine doubt, and *stam nedarim le-hachmir* applies. In *Galil*, however, the people are not familiar with *terumat halishka* — the term *teruma* in that region refers exclusively to *terumat hagoren*, which is not a binding *neder*. Since the word has only the lenient meaning in that locale, the declaration is not binding.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סְתַם תְּרוּמָה בִּיהוּדָה — אֲסוּרָה, וּבַגָּלִיל — מוּתֶּרֶת, שֶׁאֵין אַנְשֵׁי הַגָּלִיל מַכִּירִין אֶת תְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. טַעְמָא דְּאֵין מַכִּירִין, הָא מַכִּירִין — אֲסוּרִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers from this: **The reason** it is permitted is **that they are not familiar** with the collection of the chamber; **but** where **they are familiar,** it is **forbidden** — *ha makirim, assurin*. This confirms that Rabbi Yehuda endorses the principle of *stam nedarim le-hachmir* in any case of genuine ambiguity.

The *gemara* infers from this: if in *Galil* the people *were* familiar with *terumat halishka*, making the word ambiguous in their region as well, the declaration would be forbidden — *ha makirim, assurin*. This confirms that Rabbi Yehuda endorses the principle of *stam nedarim le-hachmir* in any case of genuine ambiguity.

## Stam Charamim Region (II)

### Charamim Permitted Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְתַם חֲרָמִים, בִּיהוּדָה — מוּתָּרִין, וּבַגָּלִיל — אֲסוּרִין, שֶׁאֵין אַנְשֵׁי הַגָּלִיל מַכִּירִין אֶת חֶרְמֵי הַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, **unspecified dedications in Judea are permitted,** but **in the Galilee they are forbidden, as the people of the Galilee are unfamiliar with dedications** allotted **to the priests,** so when they say dedication they are referring to dedication to Heaven.

Immediately following is the parallel ruling about *charamim*: *stam charamim bi-Yehudah mutarin, uva-Galil assurin* — an ambiguous declaration of *cherem* in *Yehuda* is permitted, while in *Galil* it is forbidden. The reasoning: in *Galil*, the people are unfamiliar with *charmei kohanim* — the type of dedication that goes to the *kohanim* rather than to *hekdesh* — so the word *cherem* can only mean *cherem* dedicated to the *Beis HaMikdash*, which is a binding *neder*. In *Yehuda*, the word *cherem* could mean either *cherem* to *hekdesh* or *charmei kohanim*, and since the latter is not a *neder*, the ambiguity renders it permissible.

But this is the problem. If in *Yehuda* the word *cherem* can mean either the strict form (*cherem* to *hekdesh*) or the lenient form (*charmei kohanim*), why are we lenient? By the logic of *stam nedarim le-hachmir*, whenever there is genuine ambiguity we should be strict.

## Stam Teruma Regions (II)

### Yehuda Consistency

The proof that Rabbi Yehuda holds *stam nedarim le-hachmir* despite the leniency in *Galil* for *teruma* was already established — in *Galil* the word is not ambiguous, so there is nothing to be *machmir* about. The rule *stam nedarim le-hachmir* applies precisely when there is genuine ambiguity. Rabbi Yehuda's position is therefore entirely consistent: where there is real ambiguity he is strict; where the word has only one meaning in that locale he is lenient. This is not a *kula* in *stam nedarim*; it is a determination that the word is not, in that context, a *stam* case at all.

## Stam Charamim Region (II)

### Elazar b'Tzadok

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: סֵיפָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סְתַם תְּרוּמָה בִּיהוּדָה — אֲסוּרָה, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק אוֹמֵר: סְתַם חֲרָמִים בַּגָּלִיל — אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: The latter clause is** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok,** not of Rabbi Yehuda, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **unspecified *teruma* in Judea is forbidden. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, says** that **unspecified dedications in the Galilee are forbidden.**

The anomaly of the *cherem* ruling — where Rabbi Yehuda appears to be lenient despite genuine ambiguity in *Yehuda* — is resolved by Abaye: *seifa Rabbi Elazar be-Rebbi Tzadok hee* — the final ruling about *charamim* in *Yehuda* is not Rabbi Yehuda's position at all. It is the position of Rabbi Elazar be-Rebbi Tzadok. Rabbi Elazar be-Rebbi Tzadok holds that in *Yehuda* the word *cherem* is permissible precisely because there is ambiguity — and implicit in his ruling is the position that *stam nedarim le-hakel*.

This is a striking conclusion. The entire lengthy *sugya* grappled with the question of who holds *safek nezirut le-hakel*, passing through the positions of Rabbi Eliezer, the distinction between *kedusha ba'ah mei-eilya* and *kedusha al yedei adam*, the debate of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, Rava's explanation based on *safek chamer mi-vadai*, and Rabbi Tarfon's principle of *ki yafli* — and now, at the very end of the *daf*, the *gemara* points to Rabbi Elazar be-Rebbi Tzadok as someone who apparently says *stam nedarim le-hakel* directly. As the *Ran* notes, one could have explained the entire discussion by attributing *safek nezirut le-hakel* and the corresponding lenient positions to this view.

One could, alternatively, read Rabbi Elazar be-Rebbi Tzadok more narrowly: perhaps he agrees in principle with *stam nedarim le-hachmir* but holds that in *Yehuda* the word *cherem* is not genuinely ambiguous — that the usage of *charmei kohanim* is so prevalent there that the word *cherem* alone refers exclusively to that category, and anyone who wishes to say *cherem* to *hekdesh* would say *charmei mikdash* explicitly. On this reading, Rabbi Elazar be-Rebbi Tzadok is making a linguistic determination, not announcing a general leniency in *stam nedarim*. The *gemara*, however, does not adopt this reading and appears to understand his ruling as reflecting a general position that *stam nedarim le-hakel* — a position that, after all the preceding analysis, turns out to have a clear talmudic advocate. The connection between *nedarim* and *nezirut* underlying this entire discussion runs through the shared hermeneutical principle of *hafla'ah*, derived through a *gezeira shava* linking the language of *neder* and *nezer* — which is why the rules governing one apply to the other.
