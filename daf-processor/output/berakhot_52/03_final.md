# Berakhot 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kiddush & Wine Order

### Blessing Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: דְּבָרִים שֶׁבֵּין בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל בִּסְעוּדָה. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַיּוֹם וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַיַּיִן. שֶׁהַיּוֹם גּוֹרֵם לַיַּיִן שֶׁיָּבֹא, וּכְבָר קָדַשׁ הַיּוֹם וַעֲדַיִין יַיִן לֹא בָּא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta*: These are the **matters** of dispute **between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel with regard to** the *halakhot* of **a meal: Beit Shammai say:** When one recites *kiddush* over wine, **one recites a blessing over the** sanctification of the **day and recites a blessing over the wine thereafter as the day causes the wine to come** before the meal. And Beit Shammai offer an additional reason: **The day has** already **been sanctified and the wine has not yet come.**

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita* addressing the debates between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* concerning the proper conduct of a meal. The discussion begins at the very bottom of 51b, where the *gemara* takes up the first issue: at *kiddush*, which *berakhah* comes first — *mekadesh ha-shabbat* or *borei peri ha-gafen*?

### Beit Shammai View

*Beit Shammai* says: first you recite the *berakhah* on the day — *mekadesh ha-shabbat* — and only then the *berakhah* on the wine. Two reasons support this position. First, *she-hayom gorem le-yayin she-yavo*: the very reason you are reciting *borei peri ha-gafen* at all is because of the day — because it is *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* that occasions the *kiddush* and thus necessitates the wine. Although we have seen that the general practice was to have wine before a meal even on an ordinary occasion, on *Shabbat* one would certainly be drinking in connection with *kiddush*, and it is the day that creates that necessity. Second, *u-khevar kideish ha-yom ve-adayin yayin lo ba*: in simple chronological terms, *Shabbat* already arrived before any wine was poured, so the day itself preceded the wine.

### Beit Hillel View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַיַּיִן וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַיּוֹם. שֶׁהַיַּיִן גּוֹרֵם לַקְּדוּשָּׁה שֶׁתֵּאָמֵר. דָּבָר אַחֵר: בִּרְכַּת הַיַּיִן תְּדִירָה, וּבִרְכַּת הַיּוֹם אֵינָהּ תְּדִירָה. תָּדִיר וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ תָּדִיר — תָּדִיר קוֹדֵם. וַהֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say: One recites a blessing over the wine and recites a blessing over the day thereafter,** because **the wine causes the sanctification to be recited.** Were there no wine, *kiddush* would not be recited. **Alternatively,** Beit Hillel say: **The blessing over wine is** recited **frequently, and the blessing over the day is not** recited **frequently,** and there is a general principle: When **a frequent** practice and **an infrequent** practice clash, **the frequent practice takes precedence** over the infrequent practice. The *Tosefta* concludes: **The *halakha* is in accordance with the statement of Beit Hillel.**

*Beit Hillel* reverses the order, saying *borei peri ha-gafen* comes first, and offers two reasons of its own. The first is *she-ha-yayin gorem la-kiddushah she-tei'amer*: while the purpose is indeed to sanctify the day, it is the wine that enables the *kiddush* to be recited at all — the wine is the vehicle through which *kiddush* is expressed — and therefore the wine takes precedence. One might note that *kiddush* can technically be recited over bread or even over *chamar medinah* in the absence of wine, but the argument holds: whatever the *kiddush* is recited over is the instrument that introduces it. The second reason is the principle of *tadir*: *birkat ha-yayin tedirah, birkat ha-yom einah tedirah* — the *berakhah* over wine is a frequent, constant obligation, whereas the *berakhah* of *mekadesh ha-shabbat* occurs only on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*. When one mitzvah is *tadir* and the other is not, the *tadir* takes precedence.

It is worth noting at the outset — and *Tosafot* makes this point explicitly, with the *gemara* itself raising the question later — that by *benching* (*birkat ha-mazon*) we do precisely the reverse: the *benching* is recited and only afterward do we say *borei peri ha-gafen*. There, holding the *kos* while reciting *birkat ha-mazon* and concluding with *borei peri ha-gafen* conveys the sense that the blessing is being said over the wine, even though it comes at the end. Both orderings, in other words, can plausibly frame the experience as one of blessing said over wine — which underscores why neither argument in this debate is wholly decisive on its own.

### Halakhah & Bas Kol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״דָּבָר אַחֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא הָתָם תַּרְתֵּי וְהָכָא חֲדָא, הָכָא נָמֵי תַּרְתֵּי נִינְהוּ: בִּרְכַּת הַיַּיִן תְּדִירָה וּבִרְכַּת הַיּוֹם אֵינָהּ תְּדִירָה, תָּדִיר וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ תָּדִיר — תָּדִיר קוֹדֵם. וַהֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל. פְּשִׁיטָא, דְּהָא נָפְקָא בַּת קוֹל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is alternatively?** Why did Beit Hillel cite an additional reason? The Gemara responds: **And if you say** that **there** Beit Shammai cite **two** reasons, **and here** Beit Hillel offer only **one,** therefore Beit Hillel said **they are two** reasons **here as well: The blessing over wine is** recited **frequently and the blessing over the day is not** recited **frequently.** When **a frequent** practice and **an infrequent** practice clash, **the frequent** practice **takes precedence** over the infrequent practice. It was taught in the *Tosefta*: **The *halakha* is in accordance with the statement of Beit Hillel.** The Gemara remarks: **It is obvious, as a Divine Voice emerged** and proclaimed that the *halakha* is always in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel. Why did the *Tosefta* tell us here that the *halakha* is in accordance with their opinion?

The *baraita* concludes: *ve-halakhah ke-divrei Beit Hillel* — we rule like *Beit Hillel*. The *gemara* immediately asks: *peshita* — is this not self-evident? We always rule like *Beit Hillel*. Why state it?

The answer invokes a well-known tradition: *de-hanpik bas kol* — a heavenly voice (*bas kol*) emerged and declared that the *halakhah* follows *Beit Hillel*. This arose from an underlying procedural dispute: *Beit Hillel* was greater in number, while *Beit Shammai* was reputed to be sharper in analysis. The question was whether *halakhah* follows the majority by sheer numbers or follows the more intellectually rigorous school. Naturally, *Beit Hillel* argued for the majority while *Beit Shammai* argued for superior reasoning. The *bas kol* resolved the matter by declaring that the *halakhah* follows *Beit Hillel*. Elsewhere the tradition attributes *Beit Hillel*'s merit to their humility — they would always present *Beit Shammai*'s position before their own — which perhaps reflects a willingness to genuinely consider the opposing view.

### Why Beit Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קוֹדֶם בַּת קוֹל. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא לְאַחַר בַּת קוֹל, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אֵין מַשְׁגִּיחִין בְּבַת קוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers two answers: **If you wish, say** that this *Tosefta* was taught **before the Divine Voice** emerged and proclaimed that general principle. **And if you wish, say** instead, that this *Tosefta* was indeed taught **after the Divine Voice** emerged, **and** this *Tosefta* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, who said,** with regard to the Divine Voice that emerged and proclaimed that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer in the case of the oven of *akhnai* (*Bava Metzia* 59b), that **one disregards a Heavenly Voice.**

If we always rule like *Beit Hillel*, why must the *baraita* say so here? The *gemara* offers two answers. The first: *kodem bas kol* — this *baraita* may predate the establishment of the general rule. Before the *halakhah* had crystallized that we follow *Beit Hillel* in all cases, each individual dispute still required an explicit ruling. There is, interestingly, something about these debates concerning the conduct of a meal — and as we will see, their deep entanglement with *tumah va-taharah* — that gives them an archaic quality, suggesting they may indeed have been formulated at an early stage of the tradition.

The second answer: *le-achar bas kol* — even granting that the *bas kol* had already been issued, one might follow the view of *Rabbi Yehoshua*, who holds that we do not defer to a *bas kol*. This brings in the famous story from *Bava Metzia* of the *tanuro shel achnai*: *Rabbi Eliezer* declared a particular oven *tahor* while all the other sages ruled it *tamei*. *Rabbi Eliezer* invoked a series of miracles — a river flowing backward, the walls of the *beit ha-midrash* bending — and finally a *bas kol* emerged declaring that the *halakhah* follows *Rabbi Eliezer* in all matters. At that point, *Rabbi Yehoshua* rose and declared *lo ba-shamayim hi* — it is not in heaven; the Torah has been given to us to interpret, and heavenly voices do not override the halakhic process. According to this view, one might take that principle all the way and say we do not follow the *bas kol* even in the *Beit Hillel* ruling, and thus we must explicitly state the ruling here.

### Lo BaShamayim Hi

The principle of *lo ba-shamayim hi* thus represents a far-reaching stance on the autonomy of the rabbinic process. *Rabbi Yehoshua* was not denying the reality of the *bas kol*; he was asserting that the Torah was given to human interpretation, and that even a genuine heavenly communication cannot override the majority. The *gemara* acknowledges that some might extend this position even to the *Beit Hillel* ruling, holding that the question of which school to follow remains genuinely open — though this is emphatically not the *gemara*'s own conclusion.

### Tosafot Resolution

*Tosafot* addresses an apparent tension: if we all agree with *Rabbi Yehoshua* in the *achnai* case, why do we accept the *bas kol* declaring *halakhah* like *Beit Hillel*? *Tosafot* draws a sharp distinction. In the *achnai* case, the majority of sages was already *against* *Rabbi Eliezer*; he was attempting to use a miraculous voice to *override* the majority. Everyone agrees you do not do that. In the *Beit Hillel* case, the *bas kol* was not overriding the majority — *Beit Hillel* already *was* the majority. The *bas kol* was rather settling a procedural dispute that could not otherwise be resolved by the usual tools of majority rule, since each side was claiming that the proper procedure supported its own position. Or, put another way: it was affirming the majority principle itself against *Beit Shammai*'s competing claim that superior reasoning should carry greater weight. The *bas kol* was thus siding with the majority, not supplanting it — and that is a case everyone agrees to accept.

## Havdalah Order

### Havdalah Sequence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי דְּבִרְכַּת הַיּוֹם עֲדִיפָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַנִּכְנָס לְבֵיתוֹ בְּמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת, מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַיַּיִן וְעַל הַמָּאוֹר וְעַל הַבְּשָׂמִים, וְאַחַר כָּךְ אוֹמֵר הַבְדָּלָה. וְאִם אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא כּוֹס אֶחָד, מַנִּיחוֹ לְאַחַר הַמָּזוֹן וּמְשַׁלְשְׁלָן כּוּלָּן לְאַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** As to the substance of these statements, the Gemara asks: **Do Beit Shammai hold** that **the blessing over the day takes precedence? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One who enters his house at the conclusion of Shabbat recites a blessing over the wine,** then over **the candle,** then over **the spices, and recites *havdala* thereafter? And if he has only one cup** of wine, **he leaves it for after the** last Shabbat **meal and arranges all** of the blessings: Grace after Meals, the blessings of *havdala* and the blessing over wine **together thereafter.**

The *gemara* now turns to an apparent contradiction. A *baraita* teaches: one who enters his home on *Motzaei Shabbat* recites *borei peri ha-gafen*, then *borei me'orei ha-eish*, then *besamim*, and then *havdalah*. This ordering — wine first, then fire, then spices, then the *havdalah* blessing itself — presents an immediate difficulty: if *Beit Hillel* rules that wine comes first, that is consistent. But the order of fire before spices in this *baraita* does not match current practice, where spices precede the *ner*.

The *baraita* continues: if one has only one cup of wine available and wishes to eat a meal — meaning *havdalah* and *birkat ha-mazon* will need to share that single cup — one sets the cup aside until after the meal, then chains together all the blessings following *birkat ha-mazon*: *borei peri ha-gafen*, *ner*, *besamim*, and *havdalah*, all after the *kos* of *birkat ha-mazon*.

### Attribution to Beit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא, מִמַּאי דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי הִיא? דִּלְמָא בֵּית הִלֵּל הִיא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And this** *baraita*, **from where** is it ascertained **that it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Shammai? Perhaps it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Hillel.**

Before addressing the substance, the *gemara* asks: whose position is this *baraita*? Is it *Beit Shammai* or *Beit Hillel*? It cannot simply be assumed to be *Beit Hillel* on the basis that wine comes first, since we have seen that *Beit Hillel* also holds wine first. To identify the attribution, the *gemara* invokes the framework of *Rabbi Yehuda*, who in a *baraita* of his own offers a different account of the *Beit Shammai*–*Beit Hillel* dispute regarding the combination of *havdalah* and *birkat ha-mazon*.

### Proving Shammai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּקָתָנֵי מָאוֹר וְאַחַר כָּךְ בְּשָׂמִים, וּמַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאִית לֵיהּ הַאי סְבָרָא — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, דְּתַנְיָא אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל הַמָּזוֹן שֶׁבִּתְחִלָּה וְעַל הַבְדָּלָה שֶׁהִיא בַּסּוֹף, עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ — עַל הַמָּאוֹר וְעַל הַבְּשָׂמִים. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מָאוֹר וְאַחַר כָּךְ בְּשָׂמִים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: בְּשָׂמִים וְאַחַר כָּךְ מָאוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **It cannot enter your mind** that this *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel, **as it was taught** at the beginning of the *baraita*: **Light** and **spices thereafter. And who, did you hear, adopts that reasoning? Beit Shammai. As it was taught** in a *Tosefta* that **Rabbi Yehuda said: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not dispute that** Grace after **Meals is** recited **first and that *havdala* is** recited **last. With regard to what did they disagree?** With regard to the blessings recited in the middle of *havdala*, the blessings **over the light and over the spices. Beit Shammai say: Light and spices thereafter; and Beit Hillel say: Spices and light thereafter.**

According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* do not actually disagree about *birkat ha-mazon* coming first and *havdalah* coming last; both agree on that framework. Their dispute is only about the order of *ner* and *besamim* within *havdalah*: *Beit Shammai* says *me'or* first, then *besamim*; *Beit Hillel* says *besamim* first, then *me'or* — which is the practice we follow. The *baraita* in question has *me'or* before *besamim*, which according to *Rabbi Yehuda* is the position of *Beit Shammai* alone. Furthermore, the *baraita* places *birkat ha-mazon* before the entire *havdalah* sequence — which is also consistent with *Rabbi Yehuda*'s account, since he holds that both schools agree *birkat ha-mazon* precedes *havdalah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי הִיא וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דִּילְמָא בֵּית הִלֵּל הִיא וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another challenge: **And from where** do you ascertain **that this** *baraita* **is** the opinion of **Beit Shammai in accordance with** the interpretation of **Rabbi Yehuda? Perhaps it is** the opinion of **Beit Hillel in accordance with** the interpretation of **Rabbi Meir.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּקָתָנֵי הָכָא בְּמַתְנִיתִין, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נֵר וּמָזוֹן בְּשָׂמִים וְהַבְדָּלָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: נֵר וּבְשָׂמִים מָזוֹן וְהַבְדָּלָה, וְהָתָם בְּבָרַיְיתָא קָתָנֵי אִם אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא כּוֹס אֶחָד, מַנִּיחוֹ לְאַחַר הַמָּזוֹן, וּמְשַׁלְשְׁלָן כּוּלָּן לְאַחֲרָיו. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי הִיא וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **It cannot enter your mind** that this *baraita* is the opinion of Beit Hillel in accordance with the interpretation of Rabbi Meir. **As it was taught here in the mishna,** which like all unattributed *mishnayot* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir: **Beit Shammai say:** One recites the blessing over the **candle,** then the Grace after **Meals** blessing, then the blessing over the **spices,** and finally the blessing of ***havdala*. And Beit Hillel say:** The order is **candle, spices,** Grace after **Meals, and *havdala*. And there, in the *baraita*, it was taught: And if he has only one cup** of wine, **he leaves it for after the** last Shabbat **meal and arranges all** of the blessings: Grace after Meals, the blessings of *havdala*, and the blessing over wine **together thereafter.** According to the *baraita*, all of the blessings follow Grace after Meals. Since the *baraita* and the mishna do not correspond, **conclude from here** that the *baraita* **is** the opinion of **Beit Shammai, in accordance with** the interpretation of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

This process of identification works through a process of elimination: our *mishnah* (which is *stam*, attributed to *Rabbi Meir*) has a different arrangement and places *birkat ha-mazon* in the middle of the *havdalah* elements. So the *baraita* cannot reflect the *mishnah*'s position. The only framework in which *birkat ha-mazon* unambiguously precedes all of *havdalah* is that of *Rabbi Yehuda*, and within *Rabbi Yehuda*'s framework, only *Beit Shammai* places *me'or* before *besamim*. Therefore the *baraita* is *Beit Shammai* according to *Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Wine Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא. קָא סָבְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי שָׁאנֵי עַיּוֹלֵי יוֹמָא מֵאַפּוֹקֵי יוֹמָא. עַיּוֹלֵי יוֹמָא, כַּמָּה דְּמַקְדְּמִינַן לֵיהּ עֲדִיף. אַפּוֹקֵי יוֹמָא, כַּמָּה דִּמְאַחֲרִינַן לֵיהּ עֲדִיף, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לֶהֱוֵי עֲלַן כְּמַשּׂוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** **And, nevertheless,** after the Gemara has proven that the *baraita* corresponds to the opinion of Beit Shammai as interpreted by Rabbi Yehuda, the contradiction between Beit Shammai's statement in the *baraita* and their statement in the *Tosefta* **is difficult.** The Gemara responds: **Beit Shammai hold** that **the arrival of the day** of Shabbat or a Festival **is different from the departure of the day.** As with regard to **the arrival of the day, the more that we can advance it, the better;** with regard to **the departure of the day, the more we postpone it, the better, so that** Shabbat **should not be like a burden to us.**

Having established that the *baraita* represents *Beit Shammai*, a sharp contradiction emerges. *Beit Shammai* holds that by *kiddush*, the *berakhah* of the day — *mekadesh ha-shabbat* — precedes *borei peri ha-gafen*, because the day is primary. Yet here, by *havdalah*, the very same *Beit Shammai* begins with *borei peri ha-gafen* — wine first. Why the asymmetry? The *gemara* states flatly: *mikal makom kasha* — the difficulty stands.

To clarify the logical structure: once we have demonstrated that the *baraita* reflects *Rabbi Yehuda*'s framework and that within that framework the ordering of *me'or* before *besamim* belongs to *Beit Shammai*, the question is validated. *Beit Shammai* is saying that wine comes first at *havdalah*. But by *kiddush*, *Beit Shammai* insists the day comes first. What principle accounts for this reversal?

### Euley vs. Afukey

The *gemara* answers: *kasavar Beit Shammai, shani euley yoma me-afukey yoma* — *Beit Shammai* holds that entering into the day is fundamentally different from going out from the day. This distinction drives the asymmetry.

### Shabbat Symbolism

*Euley yoma kama de-makdeminan lei adif* — when *Shabbat* is beginning, the sooner we designate and sanctify it the better. We are eager for *Shabbat* to begin. Therefore, we name the day first — *mekadesh ha-shabbat* — before the wine, to express that eagerness and to sanctify the day at the earliest possible moment.

*Afukey yoma kama de-machrinan lei adif* — when *Shabbat* is departing, the later we formally conclude it the better. We do not wish to rush its ending. Therefore, we recite *borei peri ha-gafen* first and only then *ha-mavdil bein kodesh le-chol*, postponing the formal ending of *Shabbat* by even a few moments. It is not merely about the seconds involved in saying *borei peri ha-gafen* — it is about the symbolism. We begin *Shabbat* eagerly and we reluctantly let it go.

This principle has practical halakhic expressions elsewhere. For example, on *Motzaei Shabbat* in *shul*, the *Chanukah* candles are lit before *havdalah* is recited, so that even though candles are already being kindled — which presupposes *Shabbat* has ended — the formal *havdalah* blessing is delayed slightly. By the time one returns home, *havdalah* has already been recited in *shul*, but the sequence in *shul* itself reflects this reluctance to end *Shabbat* too quickly.

The final phrase the *gemara* adds is: *ki heichi di-la lehavei alan ke-masa'i* — so that it should not appear as though *Shabbat* is a burden we are eager to be rid of. Postponing the formal conclusion, even briefly, expresses that *Shabbat* is cherished and not a weight to be lifted.

## Meal Before Havdalah

### One Cup Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי בִּרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן טְעוּנָה כּוֹס? וְהָא תְּנַן: בָּא לָהֶם יַיִן לְאַחַר הַמָּזוֹן, אִם אֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא אוֹתוֹ כּוֹס, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַיַּיִן וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַמָּזוֹן, מַאי לָאו דִּמְבָרֵךְ עִילָּוֵיהּ וְשָׁתֵי לֵיהּ? לָא, דִּמְבָרֵךְ עִילָּוֵיהּ וּמַנַּח לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The above discussion referred to Beit Shammai's opinion with regard to Grace after Meals recited over a cup of wine. The Gemara poses the question: **And do Beit Shammai hold** that **Grace after Meals requires a cup** of wine? **Didn't we learn** in the mishna: **Wine came before** the diners **after the meal; if only that cup** of wine **is there, Beit Shammai say: One recites a blessing over the wine and recites a blessing over the food,** Grace after Meals, **thereafter. What? Is it not that he recites a blessing over** the wine **and drinks it,** leaving Grace after Meals without wine? The Gemara rejects this: **No.** The mishna means that **he recites a blessing over** the cup of wine **and leaves it** to drink from it after Grace after Meals.

Before proceeding, the *gemara* raises a question implicit in the *baraita*'s scenario. The *baraita* described someone who comes home on *Motzaei Shabbat* with only one cup available and proceeds to eat a meal, after which *birkat ha-mazon* and *havdalah* are combined over that single *kos*. But one should immediately ask: is a person permitted to eat before *havdalah*?

In the *mishnah*'s case the difficulty is less acute, since the person may have begun eating during the third *Shabbat* meal before *Shabbat* ended and was simply continuing — there is a rule permitting one who began eating before *Shabbat* concluded to continue eating. But the *baraita*'s case presents someone arriving home on *Motzaei Shabbat*, clearly intending to begin a fresh meal before making *havdalah*. Why would this be permitted?

### Tosafot on Timing

*Tosafot* addresses this directly. He concludes that this *gemara* is indicating that even though one is generally prohibited from eating before *havdalah*, the imperative to recite *birkat ha-mazon* over a *kos* (*al ha-kos*) is weighty enough to override that prohibition. The reasoning is striking: if this person were to make *havdalah* over the wine first and then eat, he would have no cup remaining for *birkat ha-mazon*. To preserve the ability to recite *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos*, it is therefore preferable to eat first — violating the ordinary prohibition against eating before *havdalah* — and to then use the cup for both *birkat ha-mazon* and *havdalah* together.

This is a remarkable inference. In ordinary practice today, the obligation of *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos* is not typically felt to carry such weight. Yet *Tosafot* sees no other way to read this *gemara*: the only explanation for permitting eating before *havdalah* in this case is that *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos* is of sufficient importance to justify it. The *gemara* in *Pesachim* dealing with a nearly identical *sugya* confirms that even if one did eat before *havdalah*, one may still subsequently recite *havdalah* — the very scenario of this *baraita* serving as a proof.

### Kos Requirement

This leads *Tosafot* to a further inquiry about the scope of the *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos* requirement. How many people eating together trigger the obligation? Some hold it only applies with ten, others suggest minimally three (so that a *zimmun* is present). But *Tosafot* observes that if the requirement were only with three, the *baraita*'s entire scenario could be avoided: rather than eating before *havdalah*, one could simply eat alone — no *zimmun*, no *kos* obligation — and make *havdalah* first. The fact that the *baraita* presents eating before *havdalah* as the preferred solution implies that the obligation of *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos* applies even to an individual eating alone. This is quite far-reaching. *Tosafot* himself acknowledges that the prevalent custom (*minhag ha-olam*) is to use a *kos* only with three — or in some communities only with ten — yet the internal logic of the *gemara* seems to push toward an obligation even for one.

### Pagum & Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר מָר הַמְבָרֵךְ צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּטְעוֹם! — דְּטָעֵים לֵיהּ. וְהָאָמַר מָר טְעָמוֹ פְּגָמוֹ! — דְּטָעֵים לֵיהּ בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Didn't the Master say** that **one who recites a blessing is required to taste?** The Gemara answers: Indeed, this refers to a case **where he tasted it.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Didn't the Master say** that one who **tasted** the cup of wine **disqualified it** and it is no longer suitable to be used for a cup of blessing? The Gemara answers: This does not refer to a case where he drank the wine; but rather, where **he tasted it with his hand** or poured a bit into another cup and drank from it.

*Tosafot* presses further: are there no other solutions that would avoid the need to eat before *havdalah*? Two possibilities suggest themselves.

First, one could make *havdalah*, take a small sip, and then use the remaining wine for *birkat ha-mazon*. The problem is that once someone has drunk from a cup, it is *pagum* — considered impaired — and a *pagum* cup is disqualified for a *kos shel berakhah*. The *baraita*'s failure to propose this solution thus implies that *pagum* is indeed disqualified *le-khatehilah*, and there is no easy workaround.

Second, one could add wine or water to the cup to restore it. Adding wine clearly repairs a *pagum* cup. The question of adding water is more nuanced: *Tosafot* cites the *Yerushalmi* that *Rabbi Yonah* would taste from a cup and then add a small amount of water, thereby rendering it as though it were a fresh cup. Other *Tosafot* are uncertain whether water suffices, partly because this very *gemara* implies there was no simple fix available — otherwise, why permit eating before *havdalah* at all? In contemporary practice, the questions of adding wine and adding water to a *pagum* or insufficiently filled cup remain practically relevant. One who has made *kiddush* from a shared cup and wishes to make *kiddush* again need not pour an entirely fresh cup — adding wine to the partly drunk cup removes its status as *pagum* and reconstitutes it as a valid *kos*. Similarly, when only a small amount of wine remains in a bottle and the cup cannot be filled to the required level, adding water may also be acceptable, as the *halakhah* historically permitted mixing wine with water.

## Wine After Meal

### Mishna Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לָהֶם יַיִן אַחַר הַמָּזוֹן, אִם אֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא אוֹתוֹ כּוֹס, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַיַּיִן וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַמָּזוֹן. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַמָּזוֹן וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַיַּיִן.
>
> **Translation:** **Wine came before** the diners **after the meal; if only that cup** of wine **is there, Beit Shammai say: One recites a blessing over the wine and recites a blessing over the food,** Grace after Meals, **thereafter. And Beit Hillel say: One recites a blessing over the food and recites a blessing over the wine thereafter.**

The *gemara* now turns to analyze the *mishnah* itself on a related point. The *mishnah* states: if wine became available after the meal — meaning at the end of the meal, when only one cup is present — *Beit Shammai* says you recite the *berakhah* over it and then recite *birkat ha-mazon*, while *Beit Hillel* says the reverse: *birkat ha-mazon* first, then the *berakhah* over the wine.

### Mishna Meaning

The *gemara* initially reads *Beit Shammai*'s position as: you recite *borei peri ha-gafen*, drink the wine, and *then* say *birkat ha-mazon* — meaning you drink first without using the wine as a *kos* for *birkat ha-mazon*. This would imply that *Beit Shammai* does not require *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos*. But this reading creates a tension with the earlier *baraita* about *havdalah*, where *Beit Shammai* seemed to insist on exactly such a combination.

### Taste & Pagum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר מָר הַמְבָרֵךְ צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּטְעוֹם! — דְּטָעֵים לֵיהּ. וְהָאָמַר מָר טְעָמוֹ פְּגָמוֹ! — דְּטָעֵים לֵיהּ בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Didn't the Master say** that **one who recites a blessing is required to taste?** The Gemara answers: Indeed, this refers to a case **where he tasted it.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Didn't the Master say** that one who **tasted** the cup of wine **disqualified it** and it is no longer suitable to be used for a cup of blessing? The Gemara answers: This does not refer to a case where he drank the wine; but rather, where **he tasted it with his hand** or poured a bit into another cup and drank from it.

The *gemara* therefore rejects the initial reading. *Lo* — that is not what the *mishnah* means. Rather, *Beit Shammai* is saying: recite *borei peri ha-gafen*, set the cup down without drinking, and then use that same cup for *birkat ha-mazon*. Opening *birkat ha-mazon* with *borei peri ha-gafen* would be the reverse of what we do.

But this generates its own problem: *ha-mevarech tzarich li-to'am* — one who recites a *berakhah* must taste from the object immediately. If you recite *borei peri ha-gafen* and then proceed to recite the entirety of *birkat ha-mazon* before drinking, that is too long an interval. The *gemara* responds: *di-ta'amei* — he did taste it. He recited *borei peri ha-gafen* and took a taste. But then: *ta'amo pagmo* — tasting from the cup renders it *pagum*, impaired, and a *pagum* cup cannot serve as a *kos shel berakhah*. The *gemara* therefore says: *di-ta'amei bi-yadei* — he dipped his finger in and licked it. He did not drink directly from the cup; he obtained a taste through his finger, which does not constitute a drink from the cup and does not render it *pagum*.

This precise mechanism — dipping a finger to taste without creating a *pagum* — has a practical parallel. Rabbis who recite *borei peri ha-gafen* before *birkat eirusin* or as part of the *sheva berakhot* ceremony face a similar problem: *borei peri ha-gafen* is ordinarily a *birkat ha-nehenin*, requiring that the one reciting it derive benefit. Unlike *kiddush*, where the blessing is embedded in a ritual context and can be recited on behalf of others without the reciter drinking, a standalone *borei peri ha-gafen* requires personal benefit. The solution sometimes employed is for the officiant to lightly shake the cup so a drop lands on a finger, and to lick that finger after handing the cup to the *chatan* and *kallah* — a technically valid if precarious resolution.

## Cup Minimum

### Amount Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר מָר כּוֹס שֶׁל בְּרָכָה צָרִיךְ שִׁעוּר, וְהָא קָא פָּחֵית לֵיהּ מִשִּׁיעוּרֵיהּ! — דִּנְפִישׁ לֵיהּ טְפֵי מִשִּׁיעוּרֵיהּ. וְהָא אִם אֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא אוֹתוֹ כּוֹס קָתָנֵי! — תְּרֵי לָא הָוֵי וּמֵחַד נְפִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Didn't the Master say: A cup of blessing requires** a minimum **measure** of wine? By drinking the wine, **doesn't he diminish** the cup **from** containing **its** minimum **measure?** The Gemara answers: This refers to a case where **the cup contained more than the** required **measure** of wine. The Gemara asks: **Wasn't it taught** explicitly: **If he has only one cup** of wine? If there is more than the required measure of wine, he does not have only one cup. The Gemara responds: Indeed, **there are not two** cups, **but** there is **more than one.**

A further technical difficulty: the *mishnah* specifies *im ein sham ela oso ha-kos* — if there is only that one cup. A *kos shel berakhah* requires a minimum measure (*shi'ur*). If only one cup is present and some of the wine is used for tasting, how can the remaining wine still meet the minimum requirement?

### Cup Size

The *gemara* resolves this: the cup held more than one minimum measure but less than two. It was not exactly a single *revi'it* but somewhat more — enough that after dipping a finger and tasting, what remains still meets the minimum.

### Finger Dip

The practical application follows: by dipping only a finger and licking it rather than taking a proper sip, the amount removed from the cup is negligible. The cup retains more than the minimum required measure, it has not been rendered *pagum* through direct drinking, and it can still serve as the *kos shel berakhah* for *birkat ha-mazon*.

## Wine After Meal…

### Baraita Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַיַּיִן וְשׁוֹתֵהוּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְבָרֵךְ בִּרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **Didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach** in a *baraita* that **Beit Shammai say: He recites a blessing over the wine and drinks it, and recites Grace after Meals thereafter?**

The *gemara* introduces a *baraita* from the school of *Rabbi Hiyya*: *Beit Shammai* says one recites *borei peri ha-gafen* over the wine, drinks it, and then recites *birkat ha-mazon*. This *baraita* appears to say explicitly what the *gemara* had just denied — that *Beit Shammai* permits drinking the wine first and only then proceeding to *birkat ha-mazon*, without using the wine as a *kos shel berakhah*.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא תְּרֵי תַּנָּאֵי, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that **two *tanna'im*** hold **in accordance with Beit Shammai** and differ with regard to their opinion.

The *gemara* concludes: *ela trei tana'ei aliba de-Beit Shammai* — there is a dispute among *tannaim* about what *Beit Shammai*'s position actually is. According to one reading of *Beit Shammai* — the one reflected in the earlier *havdalah* discussion — *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos* is required. According to the *baraita* from *Rabbi Hiyya*'s school, *Beit Shammai* may not require it at all. This leaves the intriguing possibility that in this dispute, *Beit Hillel* is actually the *stricter* position, insisting on *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos*, while one version of *Beit Shammai* does not. The question of whether the obligation of *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos* applies to one person, three, or ten thus remains subject to serious debate among the *Rishonim*.

## Cup Minimum…

### Fixing Pagum

*Tosafot* addresses the question of repairing a *pagum* cup directly. He writes that the disqualification of a *pagum* cup applies only where an alternative is available. Where no alternative exists — *iy efshar* — one may recite a *berakhah* even over a *pagum* cup. He then adds, citing the *Yerushalmi*, that one may add a small amount of water or wine to the cup to restore it: *Rabbi Yona* would taste from a cup and then add any amount of water or wine, after which he would recite the *berakhah* over it.

Whether water alone suffices is disputed: other passages in *Tosafot* raise the possibility that only wine repairs a *pagum* cup, since the very scenario of the *baraita* — eating before *havdalah* to enable *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos* — implies that simply adding water was not considered a sufficient remedy. If adding water were unambiguously effective, one could make *havdalah*, take a sip, add water, and use the cup for *birkat ha-mazon* — and no one would need to eat before *havdalah* at all. The *baraita*'s silence on this alternative is itself evidence. In current practice, adding wine to a partially drunk or insufficiently filled cup is standard and well-established. A *kos* used for *kiddush* by one person can readily be refilled with additional wine and used by another without concern for *pagum*. The addition reconstitutes the cup as whole, no longer defined by its having been drunk from.

## Blessing Asymmetry

### Order Difference

A fundamental question now presents itself. By *kiddush* and *havdalah*, we begin with *borei peri ha-gafen* and then recite the central blessing — *mekadesh ha-shabbat* or *ha-mavdil bein kodesh le-chol*. But by *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos*, the order is inverted: we recite *birkat ha-mazon* in full and only at the very end say *borei peri ha-gafen* and drink. Why the asymmetry?

### Hefsek Theory

*Tosafot* raises this question and begins his answer: *iy mevarech kodem Birkat ha-Mazon* — if we were to recite *borei peri ha-gafen* before *birkat ha-mazon*, then *birkat ha-mazon* would constitute a *hefsek* — an interruption — between the *berakhah* and the drinking. The *gemara* itself could not entertain the possibility of reciting *borei peri ha-gafen* and then waiting until the end of *birkat ha-mazon* to drink; it assumed you would have to drink immediately. Why? Because an extended recitation like *birkat ha-mazon* — three or four *berakhot* — would constitute too long an interruption between the blessing and its fulfillment.

### Psychological End

*Tosafot* offers an additional reason that goes beyond the mechanical question of length.

### Intent Differs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** De-bishlama bi-sh'ar berakhot lo akar da'atei mi-shtiya — birkat ha-mazon ika akiras da'as mi-shtiya.
>
> **Translation:** *De-bishlama bi-sh'ar berakhot lo akar da'atei mi-shtiya* — when one recites *kiddush* or *havdalah*, saying those blessings is not a declaration that one is finished. *Kiddush* signals that one is *about to eat*; even *havdalah*, in a sense, signals that one is about to drink. The person's intention to continue with the meal or the wine remains intact. But *birkat ha-mazon ika akiras da'as mi-shtiya* — *birkat ha-mazon* is a declaration of completion. Reciting *birkat ha-mazon* means: I am done eating. If one had first said *borei peri ha-gafen* and then declared through *birkat ha-mazon* that the meal is over, one could no longer coherently drink the wine afterward — the declaration of closure would have intervened and severed the connection. Therefore the wine must come *after* *birkat ha-mazon*, not before, so that drinking remains a valid sequel to the blessing.

### Infinite Loop

*Tosafot* raises one further, characteristically sharp observation. If *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos* applies to every individual eating alone, one might wonder: after drinking the wine at the conclusion of *birkat ha-mazon*, does that drinking of wine require its own subsequent *birkat ha-mazon al ha-kos*, and so on infinitely? *Tosafot* addresses this by invoking *Rabban Gamliel*'s position that a full *birkat ha-mazon* is only required for one of the *shivat ha-minim*, but even aside from that, the chain terminates: the wine drunk after *birkat ha-mazon* is not itself part of a meal requiring *birkat ha-mazon* in turn.

## Hand Washing

### Shammai Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נוֹטְלִין לַיָּדַיִם וְאַחַר כָּךְ מוֹזְגִין אֶת הַכּוֹס, שֶׁאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר מוֹזְגִין אֶת הַכּוֹס תְּחִלָּה, גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יִטָּמְאוּ מַשְׁקִין שֶׁאֲחוֹרֵי הַכּוֹס מֵחֲמַת יָדָיו, וְיַחְזְרוּ וִיטַמְּאוּ אֶת הַכּוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a *Tosefta* where this issue is discussed in greater detail: **Beit Shammai say: One washes** his **hands and mixes water** with the wine **in the cup thereafter, as if you say** that **one mixes water** with the wine **in the cup first,** his hands will remain ritually impure, as the Sages **decreed** that unwashed hands have second degree ritual impurity status as if they touched something rendered ritually impure by a creeping animal. Consequently, there is room for concern that **the liquid that** inevitably drips **on the outside of the cup might become ritually impure due to his hands, and** those liquids **will in turn render the cup ritually impure.**

The *gemara* now enters an extended discussion of *tumah va-taharah* as it applies to the conduct of a meal — material that is less directly operative in current practice but that illuminates the underlying logic of several *halakhot* related to washing and the use of vessels. The *mishnah* presents a debate: *Beit Shammai* says you wash your hands first and only then mix and pour the cup of wine; *Beit Hillel* says the reverse.

A *baraita* elaborates: *Beit Shammai omrim: notlin yadayim ve-akhar kakh mozgin et ha-kos* — first wash hands, then pour and mix the wine. *She-im attah omer mozgin et ha-kos techilah* — if you first pour the wine without washing your hands, there is a concern: *gezeirah shema yitme'u mashkin she-achorei ha-kos machmat yadav* — the droplets of wine on the outside of the cup will become *tamei* through the hands.

### Sheni Problem

To understand this, one must appreciate the world that *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* inhabited: a world of *taharah* consciousness even at ordinary meals, where unwashed hands are treated as *sheni le-tumah* — second-degree derivative impurity. In the rabbinic system, when a *sheni* touches a liquid, that liquid becomes a *rishon*. This is a stringency applied specifically to liquids, reflecting the fact that in the biblical *tumah* system, certain bodily fluids — those of a *zav*, a *zavah*, and similar — are an *av ha-tumah* at the highest level. Liquids were therefore treated with heightened stringency, and even rabbinic contact of a *sheni* with liquid elevates that liquid to *rishon*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַּמּוּ יָדַיִם לְכוֹס! — יָדַיִם שְׁנִיּוֹת הֵן, וְאֵין שֵׁנִי עוֹשֶׂה שְׁלִישִׁי בְּחוּלִּין אֶלָּא עַל יְדֵי מַשְׁקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If the concern is with regard to ritual impurity to the cup, why mention the liquids on the outside of the cup? **Let** his **hands render the cup ritually impure** directly. The Gemara answers: **Hands have second** degree ritual impurity status, **and** there is a general halakhic principle that an object of **second** degree ritual impurity status **cannot confer third** degree ritual impurity status **upon non-sacred items,** as opposed to *teruma* or consecrated food, **except by means of liquids.** By rabbinic decree, liquids that come into contact with second degree ritual impurity assume first degree ritual impurity status and, consequently, can render non-sacred items impure.

The mechanism of *Beit Shammai*'s concern is therefore: unwashed hands (*sheni*) touch the wine droplets on the outside of the cup (*mashkin she-achorei ha-kos*), making those droplets a *rishon*. Those *rishon* droplets then render the cup itself a *sheni*. And *Beit Shammai* holds that one may not use a vessel that has become a *sheni* in the course of a meal.

One might ask: why is liquid the necessary intermediary? Why do the hands not make the cup *tamei* directly? The answer: *yadayim sheiniyot hen, ve-ein sheni oseh shlishi be-chullin* — hands are only a *sheni*, and a *sheni* cannot produce a *shlishi* in non-consecrated food or objects. It can only elevate a liquid to *rishon*, and the *rishon* liquid then makes the vessel a *sheni*. The vessel itself cannot be affected directly by a *sheni*; the liquid is the required conduit.

### Hillel Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מוֹזְגִין אֶת הַכּוֹס וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹטְלִין לַיָּדַיִם. שֶׁאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר נוֹטְלִין לַיָּדַיִם תְּחִלָּה, גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יִטָּמְאוּ מַשְׁקִין שֶׁבַּיָּדַיִם מֵחֲמַת הַכּוֹס, וְיַחְזְרוּ וִיטַמְּאוּ אֶת הַיָּדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say: One mixes water** with the wine **in the cup and** only **washes** his **hands thereafter, as if you say** that **one washes** his **hands first,** there is **a decree lest the liquid** from the outside of the cup that dampened **one's hands will be rendered ritually impure due to the cup** which is liable to be impure, **and** the liquid **will in turn render his hands ritually impure.**

*Beit Hillel* reverses the sequence: *mozgin et ha-kos ve-akhar kakh notlin yadayim* — pour the wine first, then wash hands. *She-im attah omer notlin yadayim techilah* — if you wash first, *gezeirah shema yehyu mashkin she-be-yadayim machmat ha-kos* — the liquid remaining on your freshly washed (but not yet dry) hands will become *tamei* through contact with the cup, *ve-yachzeru ve-yitame'u et ha-yadayim* — and will then render your hands *tamei*.

### Hillel Logic

The key to understanding this reversal is that *Beit Hillel* holds an entirely different premise: it is *permitted* to use a vessel whose exterior has become *tamei* through liquid contact. Since *Beit Hillel* permits using such vessels, the concern that the cup will become a *sheni* does not trouble them — a *sheni* vessel is not a problem. But precisely because such vessels are in use, there is now a real concern that the cup, as a *sheni*, will make the liquid on one's freshly washed hands into a *rishon*, which in turn will render the hands themselves *sheni*. Therefore *Beit Hillel* says: pour the wine first, and only then wash hands — because after washing, you should not handle the potentially-*tamei* cup until the washing is behind you.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּנְטַמְּיַיהּ כֶּסֶת לְיָדַיִם! — אֵין כְּלִי מְטַמֵּא אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Even without the liquid, **let the cushion render the towel ritually impure** directly? The Gemara answers: There is a principle: **A vessel does not render** another **vessel ritually impure.**

### Liquid Conduit

The *gemara* probes the mechanism. One might ask: why does the cup not make the hands *tamei* directly, without liquid as an intermediary? Answer: *ein kli metamei adam* — a vessel, even a *rishon*, cannot render a person *tamei* directly. Only through liquid does the transfer occur. Similarly, why does the cup not make the liquid *inside* it into a *rishon*, so that *Beit Hillel*'s permission to use such vessels becomes irrelevant? Here the *gemara* introduces an important technical principle.

### Outside Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בִּכְלִי שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ אֲחוֹרָיו בְּמַשְׁקִין עָסְקִינַן, דְּתוֹכוֹ טָהוֹר וְגַבּוֹ טָמֵא. דִּתְנַן: כְּלִי שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ אֲחוֹרָיו בְּמַשְׁקִין אֲחוֹרָיו טְמֵאִים, תּוֹכוֹ וְאוֹגְנוֹ וְאׇזְנוֹ וְיָדָיו טְהוֹרִין. נִטְמָא תּוֹכוֹ — נִטְמָא כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Here we are dealing with a vessel** that only **the outside of which has been rendered ritually impure by liquids** by rabbinic law and not by Torah law. In that case, **the inside** of the cup **is pure and the outside is impure. As we learned** in a mishna: **A vessel whose outer side is rendered ritually impure by liquid,** only **the outer side** of the vessel **is impure,** while its inner side, and its rim, the edge of the vessel that protrudes outwards, and its ear-shaped handle, and its straight handles are pure. However, if the inside of the vessel became ritually impure, it is all ritually impure.

The *gemara* explains: *hacha bi-kli she-nitme'u achorav be-mashkin askinan — tocho tahor ve-gavo tamei* — we are dealing specifically with a vessel whose *exterior* became *tamei* through liquid contact, while the *interior* remains *tahor*. This is a distinct rabbinic rule: if liquid makes the outside of a vessel *tamei*, only the outside is affected. *Nitma tocho, nitma kulo* — if the inside becomes *tamei*, the whole vessel is *tamei* — but when only the outside is reached by the *tamei* liquid, the inside is unaffected.

The *mishnah* articulates this explicitly: *kli she-nitme'u achorav be-mashkin* — a vessel whose outside was made *tamei* through liquid — *achorav teme'in* — only the outside is *tamei* — *tocho ve-ogno ve-ozno ve-yadav tehorin* — its inside, its lip, its ear, its handle, remain *tahor*. If the inside became *tamei*, the entire vessel follows.

This rule is itself a marker of rabbinic origin and intent. Biblically, a vessel can only become a *rishon* — not a *sheni* — and the idea that only half a vessel is *tamei* has no biblical parallel. The rabbis constructed this asymmetry deliberately to signal that these stringencies are rabbinic, lest people treat them as biblical and, for example, burn *terumah* that came into contact with such vessels. By making the rule internally strange — the outside *tamei*, the inside *tahor* — the rabbis communicated that the entire framework is a rabbinic construction and should not be treated with maximal stringency.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי אָסוּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בִּכְלִי שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ אֲחוֹרָיו בְּמַשְׁקִין, גְּזֵרָה מִשּׁוּם נִיצוֹצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Although the decrees of Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai are different, they are based on realistic contingencies and on concerns shared by both parties. The Gemara seeks to clarify: **With regard to what do they disagree?** What is the crux of their dispute? The Gemara explains: **Beit Shammai hold: It is prohibited to use a vessel the outer side of which has been rendered ritually impure by liquids.** This prohibition stems from **a decree** of the Sages, due to concern **for drips** of liquid that would fall from inside the vessel to its outer side, as those drips themselves would be rendered ritually impure by virtue of their contact with the outer side of the vessel.

For *Beit Hillel*'s practical reasoning: since only the outside of the cup is *tamei*, the wine inside it remains *tahor*. The cup is usable; the food and drink within it are not affected. *Beit Hillel* therefore permits using such vessels. *Beit Shammai*, however, refuses: *assur le-hishtamesh bi-kli she-nitma achorav be-mashkin, gezeirah mishum nitzotzo* — one may not use such a vessel, because there is a concern that liquid will splatter from the outside onto food, rendering the food *tamei*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי מוּתָּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בִּכְלִי שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ אֲחוֹרָיו בְּמַשְׁקִין, אָמְרִי נִיצוֹצוֹת לָא שְׁכִיחִי, וְאִיכָּא לְמֵיחַשׁ שֶׁמָּא יִטָּמְאוּ מַשְׁקִין שֶׁבַּיָּדַיִם מֵחֲמַת הַכּוֹס, וְיַחְזְרוּ וִיטַמְּאוּ אֶת הַיָּדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel hold: One is permitted to use a vessel the outer side of which has been rendered ritually impure by liquid,** as they **say: Drips are uncommon,** and decrees are not issued on the basis of an uncommon case. Because Beit Hillel permit the use of a vessel of that kind, **there is concern lest the liquid on one's hands will be rendered ritually impure due to the cup.**

### Core Dispute

The *gemara* now states the fundamental disagreement in clear terms. *Beit Shammai savri: assur le-hishtamesh bi-kli she-nitma achorav be-mashkin* — one may not use a vessel whose exterior has been made *tamei* by liquid, on account of concern about splatter (*nitzotzo*). Since *Beit Shammai* therefore ensures the table-vessels are *tahor*, there is no concern that the vessel will make the hands *tamei* — the vessel is clean to begin with. The only concern is that unwashed hands will contaminate the vessel. So: wash hands first, then pour.

*Beit Hillel savri: mutar le-hishtamesh bi-kli she-nitma achorav be-mashkin* — one may use such a vessel, because *nitzotzot lo shechichi* — splatter is uncommon and we do not legislate against it. Since *Beit Hillel* permits using vessels whose exterior is *tamei*, the possibility that the cup is already a *sheni* is a real scenario. Therefore the concern runs the other direction: the cup may make your wet hands *tamei*. So: pour first, then wash.

### Immediately Before

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: תֵּכֶף לִנְטִילַת יָדַיִם סְעוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** Beit Hillel hold that one mixes the water with the wine in the cup and then washes his hands due to the principle: **Immediately** after **the washing of the hands** comes **the meal.** Therefore, he mixes the water and wine in the cup, then he washes his hands, and then he immediately proceeds to the meal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״דָּבָר אַחֵר״? הָכִי קָאָמְרִי לְהוּ בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי: לְדִידְכוּ דְּאָמְרִיתוּ אָסוּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בִּכְלִי שֶׁאֲחוֹרָיו טְמֵאִין דְּגָזְרִינַן מִשּׁוּם נִיצוֹצוֹת, אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי הָא עֲדִיפָא, דְּתֵכֶף לִנְטִילַת יָדַיִם סְעוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the point of Beit Hillel adding: **Alternatively?** The Gemara answers: **Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai as follows:** Even **according to you, who said** that **it is prohibited to use a vessel the outer side of which is ritually impure as we issued a decree due to** concern for **drips, even so,** our opinion **is preferable** to yours, **as** our opinion adheres to the principle: **Immediately** after **the washing of the hands** comes **the meal.**

*Beit Hillel* adds a second, independent reason — *davar acher*: *teichef li-netilat yadayim seudah* — hand-washing should lead directly into the meal. The washing of hands has its purpose in relation to the bread, and that washing should immediately precede the *hamotzi*. If one were to wash hands first and only then pour the wine, the wine-pouring would intervene between the washing and the meal, which is undesirable. Therefore: pour the wine, then wash, and proceed directly to the meal.

The *gemara* asks why *Beit Hillel* needs this additional reason when the *tumah* argument already supports their position. The answer: *Beit Hillel* is also addressing *Beit Shammai* on *Beit Shammai*'s own terms. Even if you accept *Beit Shammai*'s premise that such vessels are *assur* — and therefore there is no concern about the cup making the hands *tamei* — *Beit Hillel* says: my ordering is *still* preferable, because the washing should immediately precede the meal. The argument from *tadir la-netilat yadayim seudah* stands independently of the *tumah* dispute.

This final point is not merely theoretical. It bears directly on the *Ashkenazi* custom of washing hands before *kiddush*, then reciting *borei peri ha-gafen*, and only then *hamotzi*. *Tosafot* in *Pesachim* discusses this practice and acknowledges the difficulty from this *gemara*, which seems to favor hand-washing immediately before the bread. He resolves the tension on two grounds: first, the *gemara* here was concerned with the elaborate process of *mezigat ha-kos* — mixing water with wine in precise proportions — which would constitute a genuine *hefsek* between washing and eating; simply reciting *borei peri ha-gafen* after washing is not the same kind of interruption. Second, *kiddush be-makom seudah* — *kiddush* by its very nature introduces and is integrated into the meal; it is not an intervening act but part of the meal's initiation. The *gemara*'s concern does not apply when *kiddush* is the transition directly into eating.

## Towel Placement

### Shammai: Table

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מְקַנֵּחַ יָדָיו בַּמַּפָּה וּמַנִּיחָהּ עַל הַשֻּׁלְחָן. שֶׁאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר עַל הַכֶּסֶת, גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יִטָּמְאוּ מַשְׁקִין שֶׁבַּמַּפָּה מֵחֲמַת הַכֶּסֶת, וְיַחְזְרוּ וִיטַמְּאוּ אֶת הַיָּדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** In a *Tosefta*, **the Sages taught** in greater detail: **Beit Shammai say:** After washing, **one dries his hands with a cloth and places it on the table, as if you say** that he should place the cloth **on the cushion,** there is room to issue a **decree lest the liquids on the cloth,** which is wet because he used it to dry his hands, **become ritually impure due to** their contact **with the cushion, and** the liquids **would in turn render the hands** of anyone who touches the towel **ritually impure.**

The *mishnah* continues with a debate about where one places the towel used to dry the hands after washing. *Beit Shammai* says: place the towel on the table. *Beit Hillel* says: place it on the couch (*keset*) — the reclining surface beside you.

A *baraita* elaborates: *Beit Shammai omrim: mekaneach yadav ba-mapa u-manichan al ha-shulchan* — wipe your hands on the towel and set it on the table. *She-im attah omer al ha-keset* — if you keep it on the couch, the concern is *gezeirah shema yitme'u mashkin she-ba-mapa machmat ha-keset* — the liquid on the damp towel will become *tamei* through the *tamei* couch, *ve-yachzeru ve-yitame'u et ha-yadayim* — and then those *rishon* liquids will render the hands *sheni*.

### Couch Risk

The structure of *Beit Shammai*'s concern is thus: the couch (*keset*) may be *tamei*. The wet towel placed on it retains liquid. That liquid becomes a *rishon* through contact with the *tamei* couch. The *rishon* liquid then makes the hands *sheni* when the towel is used again during the meal. Better to place the towel on the table, which *Beit Shammai* presumably regards as *tahor* — or at least less likely to create this chain of transmission.

### Hillel: Couch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עַל הַכֶּסֶת. שֶׁאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר עַל הַשֻּׁלְחָן, גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יִטָּמְאוּ מַשְׁקִין שֶׁבַּמַּפָּה מֵחֲמַת הַשּׁוּלְחָן וְיַחְזְרוּ וִיטַמְּאוּ אֶת הָאוֹכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say: One places it on the cushion** upon which he is sitting, **as if you say** that he should place it **on the table,** there is room to issue a **decree lest the liquids on the towel might be rendered ritually impure** by their contact **with the table,** and those liquids **in turn will render the food** placed on the table **ritually impure.**

*Beit Hillel* says the opposite: place the towel on the couch, not the table. *She-im attah omer al ha-shulchan* — if you place it on the table, there is a different concern: *gezeirah shema yitme'u mashkin she-ba-mapa machmat ha-shulchan* — the liquid on the towel will become *tamei* through the table, *ve-yachzeru ve-yitame'u et ha-ochlin* — and then those *rishon* liquids will render the food *tamei*.

### Table Risk

*Beit Hillel*'s concern thus runs in the opposite direction: the table, with its *sheni* status, makes the liquid on the towel a *rishon*, and that *rishon* liquid then contaminates the food placed on the table. The table poses a greater risk to the food than the couch poses to the hands.

### Liquid Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַמֵּא שׁוּלְחָן לְאוֹכָלִים שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ! — הָכָא בְּשׁוּלְחָן שֵׁנִי עָסְקִינַן, וְאֵין שֵׁנִי עוֹשֶׂה שְׁלִישִׁי בְּחוּלִּין אֶלָּא עַל יְדֵי מַשְׁקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let the table render the food upon it ritually impure** directly. The Gemara explains: **Here we are dealing with a table** that has **second** degree ritual impurity status, and an object of **second** degree ritual impurity status **can only confer third** degree ritual impurity status **upon non-sacred items by means of liquids.** By rabbinic decree, liquids that come into contact with second degree ritual impurity assume first degree ritual impurity status and, consequently, can render non-sacred items impure.

The *gemara* again probes the mechanism. Why isn't the table making the food *tamei* directly? *Hacha be-shulchan sheni askinan, ve-ein sheni oseh shlishi be-chullin* — the table is only a *sheni*, and a *sheni* cannot produce a *shlishi* in non-consecrated food directly; it can only do so through the intermediary of liquid. Similarly, why doesn't the couch make the towel *tamei* directly? *Ein kli metamei kli* — one vessel cannot render another vessel *tamei*. Even a *rishon* vessel cannot make a second vessel *sheni*; it can only do so through liquid. The liquid on the towel is the necessary conduit in both directions.

*Rashi* notes that one might ask: if you are reclining on the couch, won't the couch make *you* *tamei* directly? The answer: you are careful not to place your hands on the couch itself. Your hands do not touch the couch's surface; the concern only arises if the damp towel is placed there and then the liquid mediates the transfer back to your hands.

One might also wonder: what about the various liquids present in food — stews, gravies, broth? *Rashi* addresses this as well, suggesting that such liquids, being embedded within the food rather than existing as independent *mashkin*, do not function as the requisite conduit for *tumah* transfer in this context. Only liquids existing distinctly — pure water, wine, oil, and the like — serve as the conduit.

### Preferring Hands

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי אָסוּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בְּשׁוּלְחָן שֵׁנִי, גְּזֵרָה מִשּׁוּם אוֹכְלֵי תְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to clarify: **With regard to what do they disagree?** The Gemara answers: The basis of their dispute is that **Beit Shammai hold: It is prohibited to use a table** that has **second** degree ritual impurity status for purposes of eating because of **a decree due to those who eat *teruma*.** A table with that status renders *teruma* ritually impure through contact. To prevent priests who partake of *teruma* from unwittingly eating off a table of that sort, a decree was issued prohibiting its use even with non-sacred foods.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי מוּתָּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בְּשׁוּלְחָן שֵׁנִי, אוֹכְלֵי תְרוּמָה זְרִיזִין הֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel hold: It is permitted to use a table** that has **second** degree ritual impurity status, and we are not concerned about the priests. As **those who eat *teruma* are vigilant** and would ascertain the status of a table before eating.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: אֵין נְטִילַת יָדַיִם לְחוּלִּין מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** Beit Hillel hold that **there is no** requirement of **washing of the hands for non-sacred items by Torah law.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״דָּבָר אַחֵר״? הָכִי קָאָמְרִי לְהוּ בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי: וְכִי תֵּימְרוּ מַאי שְׁנָא גַּבֵּי אוֹכָלִין דְּחָיְישִׁינַן וּמַאי שְׁנָא גַּבֵּי יָדַיִם דְּלָא חָיְישִׁינַן? אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי הָא עֲדִיפָא, דְּאֵין נְטִילַת יָדַיִם לְחוּלִּין מִן הַתּוֹרָה. מוּטָב שֶׁיִּטָּמְאוּ יָדַיִם דְּלֵית לְהוּ עִיקָּר מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְאַל יִטָּמְאוּ אוֹכְלִים דְּאִית לְהוּ עִיקָּר מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the point of Beit Hillel adding the additional reason introduced with: **Alternatively?** The Gemara answers: **Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai as follows: And if you say, what is the difference with regard to food that we are concerned** that it might be rendered ritually impure by the cloth on the table; **and what is the difference with regard to hands that we are not concerned** that they might be rendered ritually impure by the cloth placed on the cushion? Beit Hillel continue: We can respond that **even so, this is preferable,** as **there is no** requirement of **washing of the hands for non-sacred items by Torah law. It is preferable that hands,** whose impurity **has no basis in Torah law, will become ritually impure** with second degree ritual impurity status, **and food,** whose impurity **has a basis in Torah law, will not become ritually impure.**

The *gemara* then articulates the fundamental dispute in structural terms parallel to the *hand-washing* debate. *Beit Shammai savri: assur le-hishtamesh be-shulchan sheni* — *Beit Shammai* holds that one may not use a table that is a *sheni* in the course of a meal, due to concern for *terumah* eaters (*gezeirah mishum ochlei terumah*): *kohanim* who eat *terumah* might inadvertently render it *shlishi* through the table's liquid. Since *Beit Shammai* does not permit using such a table, the table is presumed *tahor*, and the safer place for the wet towel is therefore the table rather than the potentially *tamei* couch.

*Beit Hillel savri: mutar le-hishtamesh be-shulchan sheni* — *Beit Hillel* permits using such a table, because *ochlei terumah zrizin heim* — those who eat *terumah* are careful and will not let the table contaminate their *terumah*.

*Beit Hillel* then adds the *davar acher*: even leaving aside the *tumah* framework, it is preferable that the hands become *tamei* rather than the food. The reasoning: *ein netilat yadayim le-chullin min ha-Torah* — the requirement to wash hands for ordinary eating has no biblical basis; it is entirely rabbinic. The entire concept of hands being a *sheni* while the rest of the body remains *tahor* is a rabbinic construct with no biblical analogue. Therefore, if one must choose between the towel making the hands *tamei* (which is essentially a rabbinic-upon-rabbinic concern) versus the table making the food *tamei* (which, even if only rabbinic in this case, has a biblical basis in the concept of food becoming *tamei*), it is far preferable to risk the hands. *Mutav she-yitme'u yadayim de-leisu ikar me-de-oraisa* — better that the hands become *tamei*, since the entire foundation of their *tumah* is rabbinic, *ve-al yitme'u ochlin de-is lei ikar me-de-oraisa* — rather than the food, whose *tumah* has a biblical root.

The result is that *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai* reason from opposite structural premises — whether a *sheni* vessel may be used at a meal — and arrive at opposite practical conclusions about towel placement. *Beit Shammai*, by prohibiting the *sheni* vessel and thus keeping the table clean, makes the table the safe location. *Beit Hillel*, by permitting the *sheni* vessel and worrying instead about the food, makes the couch the safer location — and adds that even if both locations present some risk, it is better for the risk to fall upon the hands, whose *tumah* is the most attenuated in the system, than upon the food.
