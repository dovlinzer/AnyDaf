# Bava Batra 150 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Karka & Kol Shehu

### Intro: All vs. Some

The *mishnah* on 150 establishes that the critical line separating a *matnat shechiv mera*—a deathbed gift that reverts if the giver recovers and requires no *kinyan*—from an ordinary gift is whether the giver transferred all of his property or only some of it. The *mishnah*'s specific formulation is *di-shiyar karka kol shehu*: if he retained even the smallest amount of land, the gift does not have the special status of a *matnat shechiv mera* and therefore remains binding even if he recovers.

### Rav Yehuda's Reinterpret…

Against this apparently straightforward reading, the *gemara* cites a statement in the name of Rav Yehuda Amar Rav and a parallel statement of Rav Yirmeyah bar Abba, proposing that *karka* in this context does not literally mean land, and *kol shehu* does not literally mean the smallest possible amount. Rather, according to this view, retaining any property sufficient to provide for one's ongoing support — whether land or otherwise — would suffice to remove the gift from the category of *matnat shechiv mera*. The reasoning is that when a person holds back enough to sustain himself, we understand that even if he recovers, the gift was intended as a genuine transfer, since he retained a basis for self-support.

### Eved Case Evidence

The *gemara* had attempted to bring evidence for the imprecision of the word *karka* from a *mishnah* concerning the freeing of a slave, where the term *karka* appeared to extend beyond literal land. This would support the claim that the *mishnah*'s use of *karka* need not be taken at face value.

### Context Argument

The *gemara* rebuffs this evidence, however, by arguing that context determines meaning. In the slave case, *karka* was used imprecisely because the broader surrounding discussion was already about *karka*, and the term was carried along by that context. But where a *mishnah* uses the word *karka* in isolation, without any surrounding context that would justify imprecision, it must be taken literally. The principle is sharp: if the *mishnah* is not responding to anything and is not embedded in a *karka* discussion, then *karka* means *karka*.

### First Shearer's Wool

A parallel challenge is then directed at the imprecision of the term *kol shehu*. The *gemara* asks whether it can really be claimed that every usage of *kol shehu* in rabbinic literature means a literally minimal amount, or whether sometimes that phrase, too, is imprecise. The proof case comes from the laws of *reshit hagez*, the obligation to give the first shearing of one's sheep to the *kohen*. Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas holds that the obligation requires the shearing of five sheep, each yielding a *maneh u'pras* — one and a half *maneh* — of wool. The Sages, by contrast, say that five sheep are required but that *kol shehu* from each suffices, meaning even the smallest amount per sheep is enough to trigger the obligation.

### Rav's Maneh Standard

The *gemara* then asks: how much is this *kol shehu*? Rav answers that it means a total of one *maneh u'pras* across all five sheep combined, with each sheep contributing at least a fifth of that total — that is, three-tenths of a *maneh* per sheep. This is not, in any ordinary sense, "the smallest possible amount." Rather, it is a defined and significant minimum. Yet the Sages called it *kol shehu*.

### Kol Shehu as Relative

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking the same contextual logic applied to *karka*. The Sages' use of *kol shehu* is not absolute — it is relative. Since Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas had established a very large standard (*maneh u'pras* per sheep, amounting to seven and a half *maneh* in total), the Sages, responding to that large figure with a dramatically smaller one, called their standard *kol shehu* — meaning: even a small amount suffices. The term is comparative, signaling a sharp departure from a large benchmark, not an assertion that there is literally no minimum at all.

### Literal Interpretation

The *gemara* then returns this logic to our *mishnah* about *matnat shechiv mera* and finds that neither of these imprecision arguments applies. Our *mishnah* is not embedded in a *karka* discussion, and it is not responding to any large competing standard. It stands alone, stating in a vacuum that *karka kol shehu* is the dividing line. In that context, there is no justification for reading *karka* as anything other than literal land, nor *kol shehu* as anything other than literally the smallest amount. The argument is, as the analysis acknowledges, a formidable one: the words must be taken at face value.

## Metaltelin Definition

### What's in Metaltelin

The *gemara* now turns to the question of what is included when a person — whether a *shechiv mera* or a healthy individual executing a *kinyan* — says, "All of my *metaltelin* should go to so-and-so." What does the word *metaltelin* encompass in *lashon bnei adam*, ordinary human speech?

The *gemara* answers: *manei tashmishtei kani* — the recipient acquires all the implements, objects, and possessions the person uses. This is an inclusive category of durable goods, broadly understood as "all his stuff." What is notably excluded, however, is food: wheat, barley, and by extension other foodstuffs. Even though legally — from a purely *halachic* standpoint — anything that is not land is *metaltelin*, the way people actually speak does not encompass food within that term. When a person says "*metaltelin*," they are thinking of durable objects, not their pantry. The Rashbam adds that livestock and presumably other animals are similarly excluded, since ordinary usage of *metaltelin* does not bring animals to mind either.

### Kol Metaltelin Expansion

When, however, a person says *kol metaltelin* — "all of my *metaltelin*" — the additional word *kol* operates to expand the category beyond what is ordinarily implied. *Kol metaltelin* includes even wheat, barley, and by extension other food. The extra word signals an intent to sweep in everything that is technically *metaltelin*, not merely what the word conventionally connotes.

### Millstone Distinction

The expansion introduced by *kol* goes further. *Kol metaltelin* includes even the upper millstone — *rechayim ha'elyona* — despite its great bulk and the fact that it would ordinarily not be thought of as a movable object. The reason it qualifies is that the upper millstone does in fact get transported on occasion: it might be removed for repair or resurfacing. Moreover, in its normal operation it moves in place. The lower millstone, *rechayim ha'tachtona*, is however excluded even from *kol metaltelin*, because it is never actually transported anywhere. If, on the other hand, one uses the phrasing *kol d'mitaltel* — anything that possesses the quality of being movable — then even the lower millstone is included, since it is theoretically capable of being moved.

### Slaves Classification

A pointed question is then raised regarding slaves. Legally, slaves have the *halachic* status of *karka* — they are acquired and treated in many respects like land and are excluded from numerous obligations that apply to *metaltelin*. Yet people do not typically think of slaves as land. The question is therefore: in *lashon bnei adam*, are slaves included in the word *metaltelin* or not — *kemakarkei o kemetalteli dami*? It is worth noting at the outset that these need not be mutually exclusive categories from the standpoint of ordinary usage. It is possible that people would include slaves under either term, *metaltelin* or *karka*, even if they are formally and exclusively one thing under *halacha*.

### Town Sale Case

An attempt is made to resolve the question from the *mishnah* in an earlier chapter of Bava Batra concerning the sale of a town. When a person sells a town, the sale includes houses, cisterns, wells, bathhouses, and olive presses — everything attached to the land — but does not include *metaltelin*. However, if the seller explicitly says he is selling the town and everything within it, the *mishnah* rules that even animals and slaves are included in the sale. This appears to suggest that animals and slaves normally fall into the *metaltelin* column rather than the *karka* column, since they require the expanded formulation "everything within it" to be transferred.

### Ambulatory Exception

The *gemara* pushes back on this inference. The word *afilu* — "even" — in the *mishnah*'s ruling about "everything within it" implies some novelty or difficulty about the inclusion of slaves and animals. If slaves and animals were simply ordinary *metaltelin*, no such emphasis would be needed. The *gemara* therefore proposes an entirely different framework: the reason slaves and animals are not automatically included in a sale of the town is not because of how they are categorized — as *karka* or *metaltelin* — but because they are ambulatory. *Metaltelin de-naydi* are fundamentally different from *metaltelin de-lo naydi*: objects that move on their own accord may not be "within" a place in any reliable sense, since they could walk out at any moment. Thus the *mishnah*'s special language about slaves and animals reflects their self-propelled nature, not their classification, and does not resolve the underlying question of whether slaves fall under *metaltelin* or *karka* in *lashon bnei adam*.

### Slave Freeing Case

A second attempt to resolve the question comes from Ravina's citation of the *mishnah* about freeing a slave. The *mishnah* states: *hakotev kol nechasav le'avdo, yatza ben chorin* — if a master writes to his slave, "All of my property goes to you," the slave goes free, because the slave is included in the master's possessions and thus receives himself. If, however, the master says "all my property except for a small amount of *karka*," the slave does not go free — perhaps because the retained *karka* is the slave himself, since slaves have the legal status of *karka*.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the first *tanna* on this point: he holds that *karka* means actual land, not a slave, and that the slave's legal classification as *karka* does not mean that ordinary speech would use the word *karka* to refer to a slave. For the slave to be excluded from the gift, the reservation would have to be of "one part in ten thousand" — an entirely abstract and undefined fraction — which could encompass the slave. But "except for a small amount of *karka*" would not, in Rabbi Shimon's view, be understood as excluding the slave.

The *tanna kamma*, however, holds that the word *karka* could be interpreted as referring to the slave, leaving open the possibility that the retained *karka* is the slave himself — and therefore the slave does not go free.

Now, crucially, Rav Dimi bar Yosef in the name of Rav Elazar states: *asu metaltelin shiyur etzel eved, ve-lo asu metaltelin shiyur etzel ketubah*. Retaining a small amount of *metaltelin* functions as a *shiyur* — a legally significant holding back — with respect to freeing a slave, just as retaining *karka* does. That is, if the master says "all my property except for a small amount of *metaltelin*," the slave also does not go free. This suggests that when a person says the word *metaltelin*, it is understood to potentially include a slave — the slave falls within that category in *lashon bnei adam* just as he falls within *karka*.

The second half of Rav Dimi's statement is that *metaltelin* does not function as a *shiyur* with respect to the *ketubah* case. This refers to the *halacha* whereby if a husband distributes all his property among his children and gives his wife a small amount of *karka*, this is treated as full payment of her *ketubah* — unless she objects — because the *ketubah* is fundamentally a lien on land. Since a *ketubah* is collected from *karka*, the small amount of *karka* given to the wife is seen as a *ketubah* payment. But if he gives her a small amount of *metaltelin* rather than *karka*, it is not treated as payment of the *ketubah*. The *ketubah*'s character as a land-based obligation means that *metaltelin* cannot substitute for *karka* in this context.

### Rava's Explanation

Rava explains to Rav Nachman why the two rules of Rav Dimi make sense together. By the slave: *avda metaltelin*, a slave is included in the term *metaltelin*; therefore, when the master retains "a little *metaltelin*," the retained quantity might be the slave himself, making it *metaltelin* for *metaltelin* — and that constitutes a legally significant *shiyur*. By the *ketubah*: *kesuvas isha mi-makarkei hu* — a *ketubah* is fundamentally collected from land; therefore, giving the wife *metaltelin* does not represent the kind of holding back from which she has lien rights, and hence it is not a *shiyur* in that context.

### Rav Nachman Kerisuta

Rav Nachman, however, challenges Rava's entire explanation of the slave case. He argues that the reason a slave does not go free when *metaltelin* is retained is not because a slave is classified as *metaltelin* in *lashon bnei adam*. The reason, rather, is *kerisuta* — the requirement that the document freeing the slave possess the quality of complete severance, analogous to a *get*.

This requires some unpacking. A *get* — a bill of divorce — operates not as a property transfer but as a change of status. It severs a relationship. We learn from the analogy to a *get* that the document freeing a slave must similarly be an instrument of complete severance, without any strings attached, whether physical or conceptual. The Talmud elsewhere discusses how if a *get* is given with a literal string attached by which the husband can retract it, it is invalid. Similarly, a *get* that contains a lifelong condition that contradicts full separation is invalid for lack of *kerisuta*.

Freeing a slave through the transfer of all one's property is unusual in that the mechanism is property transfer, yet it must also function as a status-altering document. When the master says, "I give you everything in this pile — which includes you — except for some undefined portion of it," that language introduces a quality of incompleteness into the document. Even if the retained portion does not refer to the slave himself, the statement "I am giving you everything but holding something back" prevents the document from being a complete severance. The result is not that the slave might be keeping himself — it is that the transfer is not absolute, and an instrument of manumission must be absolute.

Rav Nachman therefore concludes that the failure of the slave to go free when *metaltelin* is retained has nothing to do with whether slaves are categorized as *metaltelin* in ordinary speech. It has to do with the incompleteness of the act of severance when any undifferentiated portion of property is held back. Consequently, the question of whether slaves are included in the term *metaltelin* in *lashon bnei adam* remains unresolved.

## Five Categories

### Shechiv Mera

Rava in the name of Rav Nachman enumerates five categories of *halacha* in which there is a fundamental legal distinction — not merely a difference of degree — between transferring all of one's property and transferring only some of it. The principle is *chamisha ad sheyichtevu kol necheseihem*: in these five domains, the legal consequence shifts categorically depending on whether the entire estate is transferred or whether something is held back.

The first category is *shechiv mera*, which is the *mishnah* itself. *Shachiv mera she-katav kol nechasav le'acherim ve-shayer karka kol shehu*: if a deathbed person writes away all his property but retains even the tiniest amount of land, the gift remains valid even if he recovers, because it is treated as an ordinary gift, not a *matnat shechiv mera*. If, however, nothing is retained — no *karka kol shehu* is left over — then the full law of *matnat shechiv mera* applies and the gift is void upon recovery. We noted earlier that there was a debate in the *gemara* about whether *karka* in this formulation must be taken literally or whether *metaltelin* might also satisfy this condition; this point will be revisited.

### Eved Freedom

The second category is the slave. *Hakotev kol nechasav le'avdo, yatza ben chorin* — give the slave all your property and he goes free; *shiyar karka kol shehu*, retain a little *karka*, and he does not go free. We have discussed this at length: the two possible explanations are either that the retained *karka* might refer to the slave himself, or that the language lacks the complete severance required of an instrument of manumission. Either way, the distinction between 100 percent and anything less is halachically decisive. And it was agreed — in the course of the earlier discussion — that the same rule applies if *metaltelin* is retained: the slave does not go free.

### Wife Executor

The third category is a man's wife. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel states: *hakotev kol nechasav le'ishto, lo asah ela apitropas* — if a man writes all his property to his wife, we do not read this as an actual gift to her. It would be inconceivable that a man would completely disinherit his children in favor of his wife; therefore, we interpret the transfer of 100 percent of the property to her as a designation of her as executor of the estate rather than outright owner. But this rule applies only when he gives her 100 percent. If he gives her 99 percent, she owns 99 percent. Here too — though the *gemara* does not spell it out fully in this context — if even a little *metaltelin* remains with the children or elsewhere, that presumably functions as a *shiyur* that prevents the rule from applying.

### Wife's Ketubah

The fourth category is the sons — or more precisely, the interaction between the distribution to the sons and the wife's *ketubah*. *Hakotev kol nechasav le-vanav ve-chotev le-ishto karka kol shehu, avda kesuvatah* — if a man distributes all his property to his children while giving his wife even the smallest amount of land, she loses her *ketubah* claim. Three elements combine to produce this result: the gift to her is *karka*, which is the classic medium for *ketubah* payment; she did not object; and the gift occurs in the context of a complete divestiture of all his property. In those circumstances, her silence is treated as acceptance of that *karka* as full satisfaction of her *ketubah*.

This rule, however, operates only when all the property is distributed. If 99 percent is distributed and the husband retains 1 percent for himself, the wife's *ketubah* rights remain intact — she can still collect from what he is holding back, and therefore we do not assume she has waived anything.

### Property Protection

The fifth category is *mavrachat* — the device used by a woman to protect her property from her husband. A woman about to marry, wishing to prevent her husband from enjoying the *peiros* (fruits) of her property during her lifetime and from inheriting from her after her death, would transfer all of her property to a trusted third party — say, to a friend. The *halacha* fascinatingly allows this arrangement to function precisely as intended: the property is shielded from the husband, yet the third party does not actually own it. The woman retains her own beneficial ownership, but vis-à-vis the marriage relationship, it is as though she has no property. This is a remarkable legal device: the law does not force an all-or-nothing conclusion of either "she really owns it, so the husband gets his rights" or "the friend really owns it, so the woman loses it."

However, this protective mechanism operates only when all the property is transferred. If the woman retains even a small amount, we cannot treat the transfer as a *mavrachat* — a protective fiction — and it is instead taken at face value as an actual gift, presumably with all the complications that entails. The Rashbam does note that a specific textual formula in the *shtar* — *mehayom ve-lichshe'ertza*, "from today and whenever I choose to void it" — can signal the protective intent even if not all the property is transferred; but absent such language, it is only the transfer of 100 percent that triggers the *mavrachat* rule.

### Metaltelin in Five

Having enumerated all five categories, Rav Nachman's ruling continues: *u-v'chulhu, metalteli havei shiyur* — in all of these categories, retaining *metaltelin* constitutes a *shiyur*, a legally effective holding back, just as retaining *karka* does. That is, if even a small amount of *metaltelin* is left over, the transfer does not have the *halacha* of 100 percent and none of the five rules apply — with one exception.

Going through the list: by *shechiv mera*, retaining *metaltelin* is a *shiyur*, as suggested by the earlier *gemara* analysis. By the slave, retaining *metaltelin* prevents him from going free, whether because of the *metaltelin* classification of slaves or because of *kerisuta*, as discussed. By the wife as executor, the *halacha* does not apply if *metaltelin* is left over for the children. By *mavrachat*, if the woman leaves over *metaltelin*, the transfer is not treated as a protective fiction.

### Ketubah Exception

The exception is the *ketubah*. In all other cases, *metaltelin* functions as a *shiyur*. By *ketubah*, it does not. The reasoning: *d'bimkarkei takinu rabanan, bimtalteli lo takinu rabanan* — the Rabbis established the *ketubah* as a lien on land, not on *metaltelin*. Therefore, since the wife has no lien rights over *metaltelin*, if the husband retains a small amount of *metaltelin* for himself while distributing everything else among the children and giving her *karka*, she cannot collect from that *metaltelin*. Since she has no rights against it, its retention is, as far as she is concerned, equivalent to his retaining nothing. The transfer is therefore treated as a full divestiture — and her acceptance of the *karka* given to her is still treated as *ketubah* payment.

This distinction can be drawn more sharply by separating two distinct questions. One question is: what is the man giving her — is it *karka* or *metaltelin*? For *ketubah* payment, it must be *karka*. The second and parallel question is: what is the man retaining for himself? If he retains *karka*, she still has lien rights over it and therefore has not waived her *ketubah* claims against it. If he retains *metaltelin*, she has no lien rights over it at all, and therefore it is as though he retained nothing of relevance to her *ketubah* — the distribution is treated as complete. This is a fundamentally different logical structure from the other four categories, where *metaltelin* does constitute a *shiyur*, because in those cases the question of liens is irrelevant.

It is worth noting the observation raised in this context: the *ketubah* case is the only one of the five in which the person holding the claim — the wife — holds it in the nature of a bond or creditor's lien, *shibudim*. In all the other categories, the relevant consequences have nothing to do with lien rights, and therefore the *metaltelin*/*karka* distinction does not affect those outcomes.

One might also ask whether the Geonic-era enactment extending *ketubah* collection to *metaltelin* would affect this *halacha*. After the *geonim*, *metaltelin* became subject to *ketubah* liens, and it may well be that this *halacha* would need to be revisited in light of that development.

### Amimar's Liens

Amimar adds a refinement. Even where the husband retains *metaltelin*, if those particular *metaltelin* are explicitly specified in the *ketubah* as subject to the wife's lien — *metaltalei di-chtivi bi-ktuva ve-itnhu be-'aineiyahu* — and they remain extant, then that retained *metaltelin* does constitute a *shiyur*. The reason is that, since she has explicit lien rights over these specific items, their retention by the husband means she still has something she can collect from. Once she retains that ability to collect, we do not treat the distribution as a full divestiture, and her acceptance of the small *karka* is not treated as waiver of the *ketubah*. It is a narrow but logically consistent carve-out from the general rule: the *metaltelin* exception applies only when the wife has no lien rights over the *metaltelin* in question. When she does, the general rule reasserts itself.

## Nechasim Definition

### Slaves in Nechasim

Having examined the scope of *metaltelin* and the five categories, the *gemara* now turns to analyze the scope of the word *nechasim* — possessions or property — particularly the phrase "all of my *nechasim*." How inclusive is this term? The discussion proceeds by identifying specific types of property and demonstrating that each falls within the word *nechasim*.

The first: slaves. *Avda ikri nechasai* — a slave is called *nechasim*. The proof: *hakotev kol nechasav le'avdo, yatza ben chorin* — as established earlier, if a master writes to his slave "all of my *nechasim*" go to you, the slave goes free, because he is included within the master's *nechasim* and thereby receives himself.

### Land in Nechasim

The second category: *ara'a ikri nechasai* — land is also called *nechasim*. One might have supposed that *nechasim* refers primarily or exclusively to movable possessions, not to real property. But the *mishnah* in the second chapter explicitly speaks of *nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayus* — property that has liens — which refers to land. Land is acquired through *kessef*, *shtar*, and *chazakah*. The *mishnah*'s use of the phrase *nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayus* to denote land confirms that the word *nechasim* encompasses real property.

### Metaltelin in Nechasim

Third: *gelima ikri nechasai* — even a garment, or movable property in general, is called *nechasim*. The same *mishnah* that speaks of property with liens continues with *nechasim she-ein lahem achrayus* — property without liens, which refers to *metaltelin* — and states that such property is acquired through *meshicha*, the act of drawing it toward oneself. The *mishnah*'s use of *nechasim* for *metaltelin* without liens thus confirms that *nechasim* includes chattels as well.

### Cash/Zuzim

Fourth: *zuzei ikri nechasai* — even cash is included in *nechasim*. One might have thought that "possessions" refers to tangible objects — land, goods, slaves — but not to coins or liquid currency. The *gemara* rejects this and demonstrates that cash falls within the word *nechasim* as well. The proof is derived from a *mishnah*'s statement that property without liens can be acquired together with property that has liens — through *kessef*, *shtar*, and *chazakah*. This mechanism — *kinyan agav* — allows the transfer of *metaltelin* and cash alongside a transfer of land. That the *mishnah* describes this as *nechasim she-ein lahem achrayus niknin im nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayus* shows that cash falls under the rubric of *nechasim*.

### Rav Pappa Story

The application of *kinyan agav* to the transfer of cash is illustrated by the well-known story of Rav Pappa. Rav Pappa was owed twelve thousand *zuzei* by a party in the distant locale of Bei Chuzai. Rav Shmuel bar Acha was traveling to that region, and Rav Pappa wished him to go and collect the debt on his behalf. To give him proper standing to do so — and to ensure that payment to Rav Shmuel bar Acha would discharge the debtors' obligation — a formal legal mechanism was required.

The mechanism used is *harsha'ah*, power of attorney, which in *halacha* operates not merely as an authorization but as an actual transfer of ownership: Rav Pappa transferred the debt to Rav Shmuel bar Acha so that Rav Shmuel bar Acha would be the legal owner of the claim and entitled to collect it in his own right, with a separate provision preventing him from keeping the funds for himself. The transfer of the cash claim was accomplished *agav asifa de-veiteh* — through the back porch of his house — meaning that Rav Pappa transferred a small amount of real property, and the monetary claim was transferred along with it via *kinyan agav*. When Rav Shmuel bar Acha returned successfully, Rav Pappa was so gratified that he went out to the town of Tavach to greet him. This story confirms that cash is considered *nechasim* and can be transferred through the *agav* mechanism alongside land.

### Shtarot/Documents

Fifth: *shtar ikrei nechasei* — writs, documents, and monetary instruments are also included in *nechasim*. The idea that documents representing debts owed to me — *shtarot* — are themselves a species of property that can be given away when one says "all of my *nechasim*" is established through a three-way discussion of types of *shtarot* connected to land.

Rav Abba bar Yitzchak identifies two such cases. In the first, a person instructs witnesses: "Take possession of this field for so-and-so, and write him a *shtar* documenting the gift." In this case, the gift of the field takes effect immediately through the *kinyan* the witnesses perform on the recipient's behalf, but the authorization to write the *shtar* can still be retracted — the *shtar* and the gift are severable. In the second case, the person says: "Take possession of the field for so-and-so on the condition that you write him the *shtar*." Here the gift is contingent on delivery of the *shtar*, and if the authorization is retracted before the *shtar* is written, the gift of the field itself can also be undone.

### Presigned Shtar

Rav Chiya bar Avin in the name of Rav Huna adds a third case. A seller, eager to finalize a transaction, instructs witnesses to write and sign a bill of sale transferring his field to the buyer — even in the buyer's absence, which the *mishnah* permits, since the *shtar* favors the buyer, and one may act on behalf of another to their benefit without their presence. The *shtar* is now signed and held. Later, the buyer performs a *chazakah* on the field. At the moment the *chazakah* takes effect, *nikna shtar kol makom she-hu* — the *shtar* is acquired by the buyer wherever it physically is. The buyer owns the *shtar* and can demand its delivery.

How do we know that the buyer's *chazakah* on the field carries with it ownership of the *shtar*? From the *mishnah*: *nechasim she-ein lahem achrayus niknin im nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayus be-kessef u-ve-shtar u-ve-chazakah*. The *mishnah* teaches that property without liens — here interpreted as a *shtar* — is acquired together with property that has liens, when the latter is acquired through the standard mechanisms. The *shtar* documenting the field sale is thus acquired automatically alongside the field when the buyer performs his *chazakah*.

This passage thus establishes that the word *nechasim* is broad enough to encompass *shtarot* — documentary instruments. The *amoraim* differ in how they interpret the phrase *nechasim she-ein lahem achrayus* in the *mishnah*: one reads it as referring to cash, another reads it as referring to *shtarot*. Either way, the breadth of the term *nechasim* to include both cash and documentary instruments is confirmed. The discussion of further items that may fall within *nechasim* continues into the subsequent daf.
