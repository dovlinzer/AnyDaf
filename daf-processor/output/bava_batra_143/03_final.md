# Bava Batra 143 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zachin Le'ubar

### Intro & Fetus Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לִדְבֵיתְהוּ: ״נִכְסַי לִבְנֵי דְּיִהְווּ לִי מִינִּיךְ״. אֲתָא בְּרֵיהּ קַשִּׁישָׁא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הַהוּא גַּבְרָא מַאי תֶּיהְוֵי עֲלֵיהּ?״ אָמַר לֵיהּ: ״זִיל קְנִי כְּחַד מִבְּרָא״. הָנָךְ – וַדַּאי לָא קָנוּ, דְּאַכַּתִּי לֵיתַנְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** man **who said to his wife,** before she conceived: **My property** is given **to the sons that I will have from you. His older son** from a previous marriage **came** and **said to him: That man,** i.e., me, **what will become of him,** i.e., will I receive nothing? **He said to** his son: **Go** and **acquire** a portion **like one of the sons** who will be born, i.e., you will receive a share as well. The Gemara comments: **Those** sons who were not yet born **certainly did not acquire** the property, and do not receive more than their share as heirs, **as they are not yet** in existence.

The daf opens by revisiting the conclusion of the previous sugya: *v'hilchasa hamizakeh le'ubar lo kanah* — one cannot actively transfer property to a fetus. Two subsidiary questions had been left hovering at the edges of that ruling. First, can a fetus inherit through the ordinary mechanism of *yerushah*? The thrust of the sugya, as understood by both the Rashbam and Tosafos, is that yes, a fetus can inherit. Second, what if the transfer is structured to take effect only at birth — can that work? That question was the subject of a dispute between Rav Huna and Rav Nachman and was not cleanly resolved. With those threads still open, the Gemara now proceeds to a new case.

### Future Children Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי – אִית חוּלָק לְטַלְיָא בִּמְקוֹם בְּנַיָּא, אוֹ לֵית לֵיהּ חוּלָק לְטַלְיָא בִּמְקוֹם בְּנַיָּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **this** son, **does the young man [*letalya*]** receive an additional **share** of the inheritance in a case **where** there are other **sons** from the second wife, since his father gave him an additional share, **or does the young man not** receive an additional **share** of the inheritance **where** there are other **sons?**

The Gemara introduces its case with the words *hahu d'amar lidvasaihu* — a certain man who said to his wife. This man had children from a first marriage and was now entering a second marriage. By way of *kinyan* — or possibly as a *matnas sh'chiv mei'ra* — he declared: *nechasai livni dehavu li minach*, all of my property should go to the children I will have from you. The complication is that those children do not yet exist; she is not even pregnant. We are not dealing with a *ubar* in the narrow sense — someone already conceived — but with children not yet conceived at all. The Rashbam is explicit that before there is even a *ubar*, everyone agrees that no transfer can take effect. So there is no question that those future children acquire nothing.

### Son's Equal Portion

Upon hearing of this arrangement, the older son from the first marriage came forward and protested: *hahu gavra mai tavei alei* — what will become of me? Am I to be disinherited in favor of half-brothers who do not yet exist? The father responded: *zil kani kechad mibra* — go perform a *kinyan* and you shall receive a portion equal to what each of those future children will receive. A *kinyan* was performed — presumably a *kinyan sudar* — and the son received, at least nominally, an equal stake in the estate alongside the future children.

This raises the central question of the passage. Those future children, *hanach vadai lo kanu* — they certainly acquired nothing, as there was no *ubar* to acquire. But what of the older son, *l'talya*, this young man who did perform a valid *kinyan*? *Hai ishtachach lei bemakom b'neya o la ishtachach lei bemakom b'neya* — does he have a portion alongside those children or not?

To make the stakes concrete: suppose the father dies leaving four hundred dollars and three sons from the second wife. Had the father's original arrangement been effective, each son would have received one hundred dollars, and the older son, having been given an equal portion, would also have received one hundred dollars. But since the future children's acquisition failed, does the older son's *kinyan* still entitle him to his one hundred dollars — after which the remaining three hundred are divided among the three inheriting sons — or does his *kinyan* collapse entirely because it was keyed to a portion that no one ever received?

## Abin vs. Abbahu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אָבִין וְרַבִּי מְיָישָׁא וְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה דְּאָמְרִי: אִית חוּלָק לְטַלְיָא בִּמְקוֹם בְּנַיָּא. רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פַּפִּי וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא דְּאָמְרִי: לֵית חוּלָק לְטַלְיָא בִּמְקוֹם בְּנַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** There are **Rabbi Avin, and Rabbi Meyasha, and Rabbi Yirmeya, who** all **say: The young man does** receive an additional **share** of the inheritance **where** there are other **sons.** And there are **Rabbi Abbahu, and Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappi, and Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa, who** all **say: The young man does not** receive an additional **share** of the inheritance **where** there are other **sons.**

The Gemara presents this as a debate of three against three. Rabbi Abin, Rabbi Meisha, and Rabbi Yirmiyah hold that the older son does retain his portion: *hai chulka d'talya bemakom b'neya*. His *kinyan* stands even though the future children acquired nothing. Opposed to them, Rabbi Abbahu, Rabbi Chanina bar Pappi, and Rabbi Yitzchak Nappacha hold that he does not: *lai chulka d'talya bemakom b'neya*. His *kinyan* fails along with the failed acquisition of the others.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ לְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתַן, אוֹ הִלְכְתָא כְּוָתַיְיכוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּשִׁיטָא דְּהִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתַן – דְּקַשִּׁישְׁנָא מִינַּיְיכוּ, וְלָאו הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתַיְיכוּ – דְּדַרְדְּקֵי אַתּוּן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִידֵּי בְּקַשִּׁישׁוּתָא תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא?! בְּטַעְמָא תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said to Rabbi Yirmeya:** Is **the *halakha* in accordance with our** opinion, **or** is **the *halakha* in accordance with your** opinion? Rabbi Yirmeya **said to** Rabbi Abbahu: It is **obvious that the *halakha*** is **in accordance with our** opinion, **as we are older than you, and the *halakha*** is **not in accordance with your** opinion, **as you are youngsters [*dardekei*].** Rabbi Abbahu **said to** Rabbi Yirmeya in reply: **Does the matter depend upon age? The matter depends upon the reason** behind the ruling.

Rabbi Abbahu then turns to Rabbi Yirmiyah and asks, somewhat playfully, *hilcheta kevatanu hilcheta kevataichu* — is the ruling like us or like you? He immediately supplies his own answer: *pshita de-hilcheta kevatanu* — it is obvious the ruling follows us. Why? *De-kashishna minaichu* — we are older than you. He adds, pointedly, *velav hilcheta kevataichu de-dardakei atun* — it is certainly not like you, you are merely children.

### Reasoning > Age

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא מַאי? זִיל לְגַבֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אָבִין – דְּאַסְבַּרְתַּהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, וְכַרְכֵּישׁ בָּהּ רֵישֵׁיהּ בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya asked him: **And what is** your **reason?** Rabbi Abbahu replied: **Go to Rabbi Avin, as I explained** this *halakha* **to him,** and **he nodded [*vekharkeish*] his head** in **the study hall** in approval **of this** explanation.

Rabbi Yirmiyah's response is direct and memorable: *midi bekashishuta talya milta? Beta'ama talya milta* — is it truly about age? It is about who has the more compelling reason. This phrase — that halakhic authority flows from the force of reasoning rather than from seniority — resonates beyond this particular dispute. But then Rabbi Yirmiyah goes further. He tells Rabbi Abbahu: *zil legabei Rabbi Avin, de'afreshah nihaleih, vekarkash beih reisheih bei midrasha* — go to Rabbi Avin; I explained my reasoning to him and he nodded his head in concession in the *beis medrash*.

This requires a moment of attention, because an important detail had initially been easy to miss. Rabbi Avin is not a neutral party — he belongs to the camp of Rabbi Abbahu, that is, he holds that the older son does *not* receive a portion. Rabbi Yirmiyah is saying that even a member of the opposing camp, a senior authority who had originally held the opposite view, conceded when confronted with the stronger argument. This dramatically reinforces his point: it is not age or camp affiliation that determines the *halachah* — it is the reasoning. And so Rabbi Yirmiyah went to Rabbi Avin, and Rabbi Avin, *amar lei ilu amar lei*, confirmed his concession and explained why he had changed his position.

## You & Donkey Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל לְגַבֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִילּוּ אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״קְנִי כַּחֲמוֹר״, מִי קָנֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya **went to** Rabbi Avin, who **said to him:** Rabbi Abbahu's reasoning is that **if** a father **said to** his son: **Acquire** an item **as a donkey** does, **does he acquire** it?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתְּמַר: ״קְנִי כַּחֲמוֹר״ – לֹא קָנָה. ״אַתְּ וַחֲמוֹר״ – רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: קָנָה מֶחֱצָה, וְרַב הַמְנוּנָא אָמַר: לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: קָנָה הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it was stated:** With regard to one who says to another: **Acquire** an item **as a donkey** does, he **does not acquire** any property, as a donkey cannot acquire property. But with regard to one who says: **You and a donkey** shall both acquire my property, there is a disagreement. **Rav Naḥman says:** The person **acquires** his **half** of the property, **and Rav Hamnuna says:** It is as though the giver **does not say anything.** Since the beneficiary was included with the donkey in the same acquisition, he does not acquire any property, just as the donkey does not acquire any property. **Rav Sheshet says:** The person **acquires all** the property. As the giver knew that a donkey cannot acquire property, he intended for the person, who was capable of acquiring property, to acquire all the property.

Rabbi Avin's explanation, which Rabbi Yirmiyah now presents, is built around the principle of *at vachamor* — you and a donkey. The logic works as follows: the father did not say to the older son, "I give you one hundred dollars." He said, "take a portion equal to what the other children receive." The gift was not denominated independently; it was defined entirely in relation to what those future children would get. And what did they get? Nothing. Therefore the older son receives a portion equal to nothing, which is nothing.

Rabbi Avin frames this as a case of *kni kachamor* — take possession as a donkey would. If someone says to another person, "acquire this just as a donkey would acquire it," the acquisition fails, because a donkey cannot perform a *kinyan*. The older son's *kinyan* was structurally equivalent: its content was defined as what the future children acquired, and since they acquired nothing, his *kinyan* has no content.

This then transitions into a broader discussion of the *at vachamor* principle itself — what happens when a transfer is directed jointly to a person and an entity incapable of acquiring? Three Amoraic positions are presented. *Rav Nachman amar kanah mechtza*: since he said "to you and to your donkey," the person gets half — the human half works even if the donkey's half does not. *Rav Hamnuna amar lo amar klum*: the transfer was conceived as a unit; since one element of the unit cannot acquire, the whole thing fails. *Rav Sheshet amar kanah hakol*: the person acquires everything, because it is obvious that when the donkey cannot receive its share, the intent of the full gift falls on the person alone.

It is worth noting that Rav Sheshet appears here, as elsewhere in this sugya, as the voice most willing to uphold acquisitions: he had previously favored the position that an *ubar* is *koneh*, and here he says *kanah hakol*, the remaining human party acquires the whole. The Rashbam explains Rav Sheshet's position psychologically — the donor obviously intended to give it all to the human recipient — though one might also frame it structurally: even if the transfer was conceived as a joint unit, since only one member of the corporate entity is left standing, that member acquires the full interest.

## At Vachamor Cases

### The Terumah Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵין לְךָ מַר בַּקִּישּׁוּת, אֶלָּא פְּנִימִי שֶׁבּוֹ. לְפִיכָךְ, כְּשֶׁהוּא תּוֹרֵם – מוֹסִיף עַל הַחִיצוֹן שֶׁבּוֹ, וְתוֹרֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: From where do I say this? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei says: There is nothing bitter in the cucumber other than its inner** part, which is sometimes bitter and inedible and unfit to be separated as *teruma*. **Therefore,** since one does not know if the cucumber he separates as *teruma* is bitter inside, **when he separates *teruma*,** he **adds** part of another cucumber as *teruma*, in addition **to the outer** part **of** the cucumber, which is edible. **And he separates** both **as *teruma*,** thereby ensuring that the *teruma* consists of sufficient edible cucumber.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? ״אַתְּ וַחֲמוֹר״ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet asks: **Why** does the separation of *teruma* take effect? **Is it** not analogous to one who states: **You and a donkey** shall acquire an item, since he designated as *teruma* both the outer part of the cucumber, which is fit to become *teruma*, and the inedible inner part, which is not fit to become *teruma*? Since this does not invalidate the separation of *teruma* with regard to the edible outer part, one can infer that although the donkey cannot acquire property, this does not prevent the person from acquiring the property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא תְּרוּמָה מְעַלַּיְיתָא הִיא – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: מִנַּיִן לַתּוֹרֵם מִן הָרַע עַל הַיָּפֶה – שֶׁתְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא תִשְׂאוּ עָלָיו חֵטְא, בַּהֲרִימְכֶם אֶת חֶלְבּוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ״ – וְאִם אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ, נְשִׂיאוּת חֵטְא לָמָּה? מִכָּאן לַתּוֹרֵם מִן הָרַע עַל הַיָּפֶה, שֶׁתְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies: **It is different there, as by Torah law** the bitter inner part **is** also **properly** considered ***teruma*,** even though by rabbinic law if one separated inferior produce as *teruma* he must again separate produce of superior quality as *teruma*. Therefore, when one designates the entire cucumber as *teruma*, he is not designating both fit and unfit produce. This is **as Rabbi Ile'a says: From where** is it derived **that** the *halakha* **with regard to one who separates *teruma* from poor-**quality produce **for superior-**quality produce is **that his *teruma* is** valid ***teruma*?** It is **as it is stated** with regard to *teruma*: **"And you shall bear no sin by reason of it, seeing that you have set apart from it the best thereof"** (Numbers 18:32). By inference, this indicates that it is a transgression to separate the inferior part of the produce as *teruma*. Rabbi Ile'a explains the proof: **And if** the inferior produce **is not sanctified** as *teruma*, **why** does it involve the **bearing of sin,** as it is a meaningless act? **From here** one can derive that **that** the *halakha* with regard to **one who separates *teruma* from poor**-quality produce **for superior-**quality produce is **that** although he acted improperly, **his *teruma* is** valid ***teruma*.**

The Gemara now brings the case of *terumah* from a spoiled gourd to test the limits of the *at vachamor* principle. Rabbi Yosei teaches: *ein lacha mar bekishut ela penimi shebo* — the only part of a certain type of gourd that is truly bitter and inedible is its inner portion; the outer portion remains edible. In the scenario presented (following Tosafos's reading based on the Yerushalmi): one had fifty such gourds and separated one gourd as *terumah*. Upon opening it, the inner portion is found to be sour and spoiled. Rabbi Yosei rules that one need only add a small amount from another gourd to supplement the outer portion — since the outer part is still edible, the bulk of the *terumah* stands.

The Gemara immediately asks: but is this not a case of *at vachamor*? When one set aside this gourd as *terumah* for the entire batch, the intent was that the whole gourd — inner and outer portions together — should function as a unit of *terumah*. Since the inner part is inedible and thus cannot properly serve as *terumah*, should not the whole designation collapse? If *at vachamor* means nothing works, then even the outer portion should not be valid *terumah*.

The Gemara answers: *shani hatam demide'oraisa terumah ma'alyasa hi* — the inner portion is in fact valid *terumah* by Torah law. How so? The Gemara cites Rabbi Ila's derivation: *minayin latoram min hara al hayafa sheterumaso terumah* — we know that one who separates from the inferior produce on behalf of the superior that the *terumah* is still *terumah*. The verse says: *velo sisu alav cheit beharimchem et chelbo mimenu* — you shall not bear sin by taking the choice portion from it. The implication is that if one takes the inferior rather than the choice, one does bear sin — meaning the designation is legally effective, even if improper. If the designation were entirely null, there would be no sin. So even the inner bitter portion is technically valid *terumah de'oraita*, though we require one to supplement it. The *at vachamor* problem therefore does not arise, because there is no element that fails to qualify — both portions are *terumah*, and we only require adding a supplement as a practical matter.

### The Five Women Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּחָמֵשׁ נָשִׁים וּבָהֶן שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְלִיקֵּט אֶחָד כַּלְכָּלָה שֶׁל תְּאֵנִים – וְשֶׁלָּהֶן הָיְתָה, וְשֶׁל שְׁבִיעִית הָיְתָה; וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵי כּוּלְּכֶן מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת לִי בְּכַלְכַּלָּה זֹאת״; וְקִבְּלָה אַחַת מֵהֶן עַל יְדֵי כּוּלָּן. אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אֵין אֲחָיוֹת מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cited Rav Hamnuna's opinion that if one says: You and a donkey shall acquire my property, he did not say anything. **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: Rav Avya raises an objection** to Rav Hamnuna's opinion from a mishna (*Kiddushin* 50b): **An incident** occurred **involving five women, and among them** were **two sisters, and one person gathered a basket of figs that belonged to them, and** the fruit **was of** the **Sabbatical** Year, **and he said: You are hereby all betrothed to me with this basket, and one of them accepted** it **on behalf of all of them. The Sages said: The sisters are not betrothed,** as it is prohibited to marry the sister of one's wife during her lifetime.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחָיוֹת הוּא דְּאֵין מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת – הָא נָכְרִיּוֹת מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת; וְאַמַּאי? ״אַתְּ וַחֲמוֹר״ הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** One can infer from the mishna: **It is** only the **sisters who are not betrothed, but** the **unrelated women** are **betrothed. But why** is that so? **It is** analogous to one who states: **You and a donkey** shall acquire an item. Since he tried to betroth two women ineligible for betrothal together with the eligible women, the betrothal should not take effect even with regard to the eligible women.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַיְינוּ דַּחֲזַאי רַב הוּנָא בַּר אַוְיָא בְּחֶלְמָא – דְּמוֹתֵיב רַב אַוְיָא תְּיוּבְתָּא. לָאו מִי אוֹקֵימְנָא דְּאָמַר: ״הָרְאוּיָה מִכֶּם לְבִיאָה תִּתְקַדֵּשׁ לִי״?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to** Rav Mordekhai: **This is why I saw Rav Huna bar Avya in a dream, because** you were going to tell me that **Rav Avya raised an objection** to Rav Hamnuna's opinion. But as for this objection, **didn't we interpret** the mishna in *Kiddushin* as referring to **where he says: Those of you who are eligible for** engaging in **sexual intercourse** with me **shall be betrothed to me?** Since the sisters were forbidden to him, they were not included in the betrothal in the first place, and there was no impediment to the betrothal of the eligible women taking effect. The case is therefore not comparable to the case discussed by Rav Hamnuna.

The Gemara proceeds to a far more striking case. *Ma'aseh bechamesh nashim* — a story of five women, *uvehen shtei achayos* — among them two sisters. A man entered the shared orchard of these five women, picked a basket of figs, and declared: *harei kulchem mekudashos li bekalkalah zo* — all five of you are hereby betrothed to me with this basket. One of the women accepted on behalf of all. The further complication: the figs were *shevi'is* produce, sabbatical-year fruit.

The sages ruled: *ein ha'achayos mekudashos* — the sisters are not betrothed, but the three unrelated women are.

Several principles are at work simultaneously. First, the *shevi'is* produce: one might have thought that *kedushas shevi'is* restricts such fruit from being used as *kiddushin* money, since the produce carries sanctity that limits its use. The Gemara establishes from this case that *shevi'is* produce can in fact be used for *kiddushin*. Second, the fact that he picked fruit from their own trees: this would ordinarily be theft, and stolen property cannot effect *kiddushin*. The reason it is not theft here is that *shevi'is* produce does not truly belong to the owners in the usual sense — anyone may take it. So we learn simultaneously that stolen property does not work for *kiddushin* and that *shevi'is* produce does.

Third, and most directly relevant to the *at vachamor* discussion: why is neither sister betrothed, even as an unknown one of the two? The answer lies in the principle of *kiddushin she'einah mesurah levi'ah* — a betrothal that can never be consummated halachically is not a valid *kiddushin*. For each sister, there is no way to identify which one he might be married to. If he were to consummate the marriage, he might be doing so with the sister he is not married to, thereby violating the Torah prohibition against relations with a wife's sister, an *issur kares*. Since neither sister's *kiddushin* could ever be permissibly consummated, *kiddushin she'einah mesurah levi'ah* applies to both, and neither is betrothed.

The Gemara then asks: but this man attempted to betroth all five simultaneously — should this not be *at vachamor*? Since the sisters cannot be betrothed, should not the entire act collapse, leaving none of the five women betrothed? The Gemara's answer is that the case demonstrates the opposite: the *at vachamor* principle does not necessarily void the remainder. The three unrelated women *are* betrothed. The failure of the betrothal for the two sisters does not contaminate the valid *kiddushin* of the other three.

This constitutes a significant proof that *at vachamor* does work — at minimum, it works for the elements of a joint transaction that are themselves capable of taking effect. Whether the remainder receives half (Rav Nachman), nothing (Rav Hamnuna), or everything (Rav Sheshet) may remain debated, but the five-women case establishes that at least partial effectiveness is real.

The response to this proof is remarkable: *hainu d'chazai Rav Huna bar Avia b'chalma* — that is why I saw Rav Avia in my dream last night. The speaker now understands that the dream was a message that Rav Avia had posed a challenge to the position that *at vachamor* never works. Dreams aside, the legal answer is then supplied: *lav mi oukimna d'amar hareuyos mikem l'via yiskadeshu li* — no, the Gemara in Kiddushin explains that the man said something more qualified than it appears. He said: those of you for whom it is possible to consummate the marriage should be betrothed to me. He explicitly conditioned the *kiddushin* on its being consummable, which is why neither sister is included. According to Shmuel, who maintains that *kiddushin she'einah mesurah levi'ah* is valid *kiddushin*, two sisters betrothed simultaneously would result in one of them being validly betrothed — we simply would not know which. The reason the *mishnah* rules otherwise is that the man's phrasing explicitly excluded women for whom consummation would be impossible. And once one recognizes that the man was *not* making a single indivisible five-way proposal but rather qualifying it from the outset, the *at vachamor* problem dissolves — he was never saying "all five as one unit," but rather "whichever of you qualifies." The proof for *at vachamor* working partially thus remains intact on the surface, even if the *mishnah* is ultimately explained otherwise.

## Wife & Children Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לַהּ לִדְבֵיתְהוּ: ״נִכְסַיי לִיךְ וְלִבְנִיךְ״, אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: קָנְתָה מֶחֱצָה. וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיְתָה לְאַהֲרֹן וּלְבָנָיו״ – מֶחֱצָה לְאַהֲרֹן מֶחֱצָה לְבָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** man **who said to his wife: My property is given to you and to your sons. Rav Yosef says:** In such a case, the wife **acquires half** of the property, even though the man did not specify how much of his property he was giving her. **And Rav Yosef said: From where do I say this? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The verse states with regard to the shewbread: **"And it shall be for Aaron and his sons"** (Leviticus 24:9). This means **half for Aaron** and **half for his sons.** One can infer from the *baraita* that when it is specified that a gift is to be divided between an individual and a group, the intention is that the individual receives half.

A new case is introduced: *hahu d'amar la l'devithu, nechasai lichi u'l'vnaich* — a man on his deathbed who said to his wife, "my property is to you and to my sons." The question immediately arises: how much does the wife receive? Does the formulation "you and my sons" mean she receives fifty percent while the sons divide the other fifty percent among themselves? Or does it mean she is included as equal to one son, receiving whatever each individual son would receive?

## Wife Share Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם, אַהֲרֹן בַּר חֲלוּקָּה הוּא; לְהָכִי פְּרַט בֵּיהּ רַחֲמָנָא – לְמִשְׁקַל פַּלְגָא. אִשָּׁה – לָאו בַּת יְרוּשָּׁה הִיא, דַּיָּה שֶׁתִּטּוֹל כְּאֶחָד מִן הַבָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **Granted, there,** in the case of the shewbread, **Aaron is eligible to receive a share** of the shewbread as a priest; it is **for this** reason that **the Merciful One specified him** separately, so **that he should take** more than other priests, i.e., **half.** But with regard to **a woman, who is not eligible to inherit** her husband's property, **it is sufficient for her to receive** a portion of the inheritance **as one of the sons,** not half of all the property.

Abaye objects. *Bishlama hatam, Aharon bar chaluka hu* — in that case it makes sense: even had the Torah not specified Aharon's share, he would have received some portion of the bread as a *kohen*. By singling him out explicitly, the Torah is granting him additional weight — specifically, half. *Ishah lav bat yerushah hi* — a wife, however, does not ordinarily inherit at all. Since she has no baseline entitlement, when the husband says "you and my sons," the most natural reading is simply that he is including her in something she would otherwise have been excluded from. *Dayo*, it is sufficient, says Abaye, to read this as granting her an equal share to one son — *shetitnol ke-echad min ha-banim*. She has been brought into the inheritance, but not elevated to the position of an equal partner with all the sons as a collective.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא עוֹבָדָא הֲוָה בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא, וְאַגְבְּיַהּ שְׁמוּאֵל פַּלְגָא! בִּטְבֶרְיָא, וְאַגְבְּיַהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן פַּלְגָא! וְתוּ, כִּי אֲתָא רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, אָמַר: הָהוּא דְּמֵי כְלִילָא דִּשְׁדוֹ דְּבֵי מַלְכָּא אַאַבּוּלֵי וְאַאִיסְטְרוּגֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי: נִיתְּבוּ אַבּוּלֵי פַּלְגָא וְאִיסְטְרוּגֵי פַּלְגָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **Is that so? But there was an incident in Neharde'a** where a man gave a gift to an individual and to a group of people, **and Shmuel collected half** the sum for the individual. There was a similar case **in Tiberias, and Rabbi Yoḥanan collected half** the sum for the individual. **And furthermore, when Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef came** from Eretz Yisrael **he said:** There was **a certain coronation tax that was placed by the royal house on the city councilmen [*a'abulei*] and on the prominent citizens [*ve'a'isterugei*]. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: Let the city councilmen give half and the prominent citizens half.** This indicates that when two groups are mentioned together, the intent is that each one represents half.

The Gemara challenges Abaye's position with actual precedent: *veha uvda hava bi-Nahardea, ve-adbeih Shmuel palga* — there was a case of this nature in Nehardea and Shmuel awarded the wife half. *Vahava uvda bi-Tverya, ve-adbeih Rabbi Yochanan palga* — there was a parallel case in Tiberias and Rabbi Yochanan also awarded her half. These are not merely theoretical positions; leading Amoraim actually ruled in practice that the wife receives fifty percent.

An additional source is brought: the case of a royal levy imposed on *avulei* and *istrugei* — the ultra-wealthy and the officer class — for the cost of a crown. Rabbi ruled that the *avulei* pay half and the *istrugei* pay half. When a decree says "A and B," it creates two equal halves. The Gemara, however, pushes back on the relevance of this comparison: *hatam me-ikara ki havo katvei a-avulei havo katvei* — originally, the obligation had been written solely upon the ultra-wealthy; the officers were merely assisting voluntarily. When the king discovered this and formalized both groups' obligations, the explicit singling out of the officers — who were previously voluntary participants — elevated them to equal status, just as Rabbi Abbahu's group argued about Aharon. But in the wife-and-sons case, the wife had no prior connection to the inheritance at all. Abaye's argument thus survives the analogy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, מֵעִיקָּרָא כִּי הֲווֹ כָּתְבִי – אַאַבּוּלֵי הֲווֹ כָּתְבִי; וְאִיסְטְרוּגֵי הֲווֹ מְסַיְּיעִי בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, וְיָדַע מַלְכָּא דַּהֲווֹ קָא מְסַיְּיעִי; הַשְׁתָּא מַאי דְּקָא כָּתְבִי אַאַבּוּלֵי וְאַאִיסְטְרוּגֵי, לְמֵימְרָא דְּהָנֵי פַּלְגָא וְהָנֵי פַּלְגָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **How can** the case concerning the tax **be compared** to the other cases? **There, initially, when they would write** the writ of assessment, **they would write** that it was placed **on the city councilmen** alone. **And the prominent citizens would assist them** in raising the sum, **and the king knew that they were assisting** them. Therefore, **now,** the meaning of **what they wrote:** It is placed **on the city councilmen and on the prominent citizens, is to say that these** should give **half** of the tax, **and those** should give **half.**

The Gemara also tests the question through the case of a *mincha* vow of a hundred *isaron* to be brought in two vessels. The rule is that no single vessel may hold more than sixty *isaron*, because the *bila* — the mixing of flour with oil — cannot be performed effectively beyond that volume. So one brings sixty in one vessel and forty in the other. If "A and B" always means fifty-fifty, why does the *halacha* not require — or at least allow *lechatchila* — an equal split of fifty and fifty? The Gemara replies: this is not a comparable case. When someone vowed a hundred *isaron* in two vessels, his underlying intent was always a single large *korban*; the two-vessel arrangement was only a concession to halachic necessity. Therefore, one fills the first vessel to its maximum capacity — sixty — and places only the overage in the second. The structure of the vow was never "two equal halves"; it was "one unit, divided only as required."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי זֵירָא: הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי מִנְחָה מֵאָה עִשָּׂרוֹן, לְהָבִיא בִּשְׁנֵי כֵלִים״ – מֵבִיא שִׁשִּׁים בִּכְלִי אֶחָד, וְאַרְבָּעִים בִּכְלִי אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raises an objection** to Rav Yosef's ruling from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Menaḥot* 12:7): With regard to **one who says: It is** incumbent **upon me** to bring **a meal-offering** of **one hundred tenths** of an ephah **in two vessels, he brings sixty-**tenths **in one vessel, and** the remaining **forty-**tenths **in one vessel,** as no more than sixty-tenths may be brought in a single vessel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הֵבִיא חֲמִשִּׁים בִּכְלִי אֶחָד וַחֲמִשִּׁים בִּכְלִי אֶחָד – יָצָא. אִם הֵבִיא אִין – לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא; וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ כׇּל כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא פַּלְגָא וּפַלְגָא הוּא, אֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And if he brought fifty-**tenths **in one vessel and fifty in one vessel, he fulfilled** his obligation. One can infer from the *baraita* that after the fact, **if he** had **brought** fifty-tenths in each vessel, **yes,** he fulfilled his obligation, but he may **not** do so ***ab initio*. And if it should enter your mind** to say that **every case like this is** divided **half and half,** it should be permitted to divide the meal-offering into equal halves **even *ab initio*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, אֲנַן סָהֲדִי דְּהַאי גַּבְרָא מֵעִיקָּרָא – לְקׇרְבָּן גָּדוֹל קָא מְכַוֵּין, וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר בִּשְׁנֵי כֵלִים – דְּיָדַע דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר לְאֵתוֹיֵי בִּכְלִי אֶחָד; כַּמָּה דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַיְיתִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara dismisses Rabbi Zeira's objection: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of the meal-offering, **it is clear to us that this person initially intended to** bring **a large offering, and** the reason **that he said** he will bring the offering **in two vessels** was **that he knew that it is not possible to bring** the entire offering **in a single vessel.** Therefore, **we bring,** in a single vessel, **as much as** it **is possible to bring,** which is sixty-tenths. In any other case it is possible that the division should be into two equal portions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בְּשָׂדֶה, עִנְיָן וּמֶחֱצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Yosef** in three matters where he disagreed with Rabba: **In** the matter of dividing **a field** between brothers (see 12b); in the matter of whether certain actions are limited by the court session or by the **topic** of discussion in the court (see 114a); **and** in the matter of **half,** i.e., this matter, whether an unspecified division into two parts is divided into two equal halves.

The *hilcheta* is then stated: *veHilcheta kevatei d'Rav Yosef* — the ruling follows Rav Yosef. When a man says "to you and to my sons," the wife receives half. The discussion of Shmuel's position on *kiddushin she'einah mesurah levi'ah* is also woven through this section, illustrating how the qualification of a transaction's terms can determine whether it operates as a single indivisible unit or as a series of independent designations.

## Ambiguous Designations

### Silk to the House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דְּשַׁדַּר פִּיסְקֵי דְשִׁירָאֵי לְבֵיתֵיהּ. אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הָרְאוּיִין לְבָנִים – לַבָּנִים, רְאוּיִין לְבָנוֹת – לַבָּנוֹת. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ כַּלָּתָא, אֲבָל אִית לֵיהּ כַּלָּתָא – לְכַלָּתֵיהּ שַׁדַּר. וְאִי בְּנָתֵיהּ לָא נְסִיבָן – לָא שָׁבַק בְּנָתֵיהּ וּמְשַׁדַּר לְכַלָּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites additional incidents concerning a gift whose method of division was not specified: There was **a certain** man **who sent swaths of silk to his home** as a gift. **Rabbi Ami says** that in such a case, **those** swaths that are **suitable for** the **sons'** garments are given **to the sons,** and those that are **suitable for** the **daughters'** garments are given **to the daughters.** The Gemara comments: **We said this only when he does not have daughters-in-law, but if he has daughters-in-law,** it is presumed that **he sent** it **for his daughters-in-law. And if his daughters are not married,** it is presumed that **he does not forsake his daughters and send to his daughters-in-law,** so the silk is given to his daughters.

The Gemara turns to a case where a man sent a consignment of silk — *piska d'shira'i* — to his household. No explicit instructions accompanied it. Rav Ami rules: *hara'uyim l'vanim l'vanim, hara'uyim l'vanos l'vanos* — whatever is suitable for the sons goes to the sons, whatever is suitable for the daughters goes to the daughters. The difficulty the Rashbam notes is that this works neatly only if the silk was already fashioned into garments; if it is raw bolts of fabric, it is not obvious how one determines what is "suitable" for which child.

The Gemara then introduces a limiting principle: *v'lo amran ela d'leis lei kalla* — this ruling applies only when the man has no daughter-in-law. If he does have a daughter-in-law, *l'kallateih shadar* — we presume he was sending it to her. However, *v'ei b'noseih lo nesivan* — if his own daughters are not yet married, he would not bypass his unmarried daughters to send silk to a daughter-in-law, since providing daughters with attractive clothing serves the pressing concern of enabling them to marry.

### Singular 'Sons'

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: ״נִכְסַיי לִבְנַיי״. הֲוָה לֵיהּ בְּרָא וּבְרַתָּא. מִי קָרוּ אִינָשֵׁי לִבְרָא ״בְּנַיי״ – וּלְסַלּוֹקֵי לִבְרַתָּא מֵעִישּׂוּר קָאָתֵי; אוֹ דִלְמָא, לָא קָרוּ אִינָשֵׁי לִבְרָא ״בְּנַיי״ – וּלְמוֹשְׁכָהּ לְבַרְתָּא בְּמַתָּנָה קָאָתֵי?
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who said to** those surrounding him: I leave **my property to my sons [*levanai*],** and **he had** only **a son and a daughter.** The question was raised: **Do people call** a single **son** in the plural: **My sons, and** in his will **he came to remove his daughter** even **from the tenth** of his property that would rightfully be her dowry? **Or perhaps people do not call** a single **son: My sons, and he came to include** his **daughter in the gift?**

A more complex question follows. A man declared: *nechasai l'vanai* — my property should go to my sons. He had one son and one daughter. *Mi kari inish l'v'ra banai* — would a person refer to a single son as "my sons"? And if the word is read as genuinely plural, might it be intended to include the daughter, treating *banim* as "children" rather than strictly "sons"?

One reason the man might have used this formulation even with a single son in mind is *l'saluki barta me'ishur nechasai* — to exclude his daughter from the *ishur nechasim*, the provision in the *kesubah* entitling daughters to one-tenth of the estate. By transferring the property as a gift rather than allowing it to pass by ordinary inheritance, he can circumvent *tnai kesubah* obligations — gifts fall outside the estate from which those obligations are calculated. Tosafos offers a related but distinct explanation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וּבְנֵי דָּן – חֻשִׁים״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא, דִּלְמָא כִּדְתָנָא דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה: שֶׁהָיוּ מְרוּבִּין כְּחוּשִׁים שֶׁל קָנֶה! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: ״וּבְנֵי פַלּוּא – אֱלִיאָב״. רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: ״וּבְנֵי אֵיתָן – עֲזַרְיָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** a proof that one does refer to a single son using the plural term sons, as it is written: **"And the sons of Dan: Hushim"** (Genesis 46:23). **Rava said to** Abaye: **Perhaps** this verse should be understood **as the school of Ḥizkiyya taught,** that Hushim is not the name of Dan's son but **that they,** Dan's sons, **were as numerous as** the **groups [*ḥushim*] of reeds. Rather, Rava said:** One can prove this usage of the term sons in the verse: **"And the sons of Pallu: Eliab"** (Numbers 26:8). **Rav Yosef said:** This can be proven from another verse: **"And the sons of Ethan: Azariah"** (I Chronicles 2:8).

Abaye attempts to resolve the question from a biblical source: *u-vnei Dan Chushim* — "the sons of Dan were Chushim." Since *Chushim* appears to be a single individual's name, and yet the verse uses the plural *bnei*, we see that one can use the plural "sons" when referring to a single son. Rava objects: perhaps *d'vei Chizkiya* taught that this verse means something altogether different — that the children of Dan were as numerous as reeds (*chushim shel kanei*), making Chushim a description rather than a proper name. Rava then offers a cleaner prooftext: *u-vnei Pallu Eliav* — the sons of Pallu were Eliav — where Eliav is clearly a single person. Rav Yosef adds: *u-vnei Esan Azarya* — similarly. The conclusion is that one does use the word *banim* in reference to a single son.

The consequence is poignant: since *banim* can refer to a single son, and since Hebrew lacks a gender-neutral word for "children" that would naturally distinguish sons from daughters — the word *banim* carries a grammatical male-gender default — the declaration *nechasai l'vanai* is interpreted as referring to sons. Even if there is only a single son, the property goes to him, and the daughter is excluded. The practical effect, depending on how one reads the relationship between a gift and *tnai kesubah* obligations, may be that the daughter loses even the tenth-of-the-estate entitlement.

### Grandson Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: ״נִכְסַאי לִבְנַאי״. הֲוָה לֵיהּ בְּרָא, וּבַר בְּרָא. קָרוּ אִינָשֵׁי לְבַר בְּרָא ״בְּרָא״, אוֹ לָא? רַב חֲבִיבָא אֲמַר: קָרוּ אִינָשֵׁי לְבַר בְּרָא ״בְּרָא״, מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לָא קָרוּ אִינָשֵׁי לְבַר בְּרָא ״בְּרָא״. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּמָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: הַמּוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מִבָּנִים – מוּתָּר בִּבְנֵי בָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who said to** those surrounding him: I leave **my property to my sons,** and **he had a son and a grandson,** i.e., his son's son. The question was raised: Do **people call a grandson a son, or** do they **not? Rav Ḥaviva said: People call a grandson a son. Mar bar Rav Ashi said: People do not call a grandson a son.** The Gemara comments: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Mar bar Rav Ashi:** With regard to **one prohibited by a vow from deriving benefit from** a certain person's **sons,** it is **permitted** for him to derive benefit **from** the **sons of** that person's **sons,** as they are not included in the term sons.

The final question in this series: a man declared *nechasai livnai*, and he had a son and a grandson. *Kare inshi l'var bra bra o lo* — does ordinary usage extend the word "son" to include a grandson?

*Rav Chaviva amar: kare inshi l'var bra bra* — yes, it is common usage to call a grandson a son. *Mar bar Rav Ashi amar: lo kare inshi l'var bra bra* — no, it is not common usage. A *braita* supports Mar bar Rav Ashi: *ha-mudar hana'ah m'banim, mutar bivnei banim* — one who is forbidden to derive benefit from "sons" is permitted to derive benefit from grandchildren. This establishes that in ordinary halakhic usage, *banim* does not include *bnei banim*.

The Rashbam notes, however, that this does not mean grandchildren are never treated as children for halakhic purposes — *aval le-inyan piryah ve-rivyah chashivei k'vanim*, for the obligations of *piryah ve-rivyah* they may count, and in contexts like *yibum*, even grandchildren or great-grandchildren can satisfy the requirement of having offspring. But when someone uses the word *banim* in an act of transfer or declaration, the ordinary linguistic meaning controls, and grandchildren are not included. Thus the grandson receives nothing from the declaration *nechasai livnai*, while the single son receives everything.

## Estate Improvements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים גְּדוֹלִים וּקְטַנִּים; הִשְׁבִּיחוּ גְּדוֹלִים אֶת הַנְּכָסִים – הִשְׁבִּיחוּ לָאֶמְצַע. אִם אָמְרוּ: ״רְאוּ מַה שֶּׁהִנִּיחַ אַבָּא, הֲרֵי אָנוּ עוֹשִׂין וְאוֹכְלִים״ – הִשְׁבִּיחוּ לְעַצְמָן. וְכֵן הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהִשְׁבִּיחָה אֶת הַנְּכָסִים – הִשְׁבִּיחָה לָאֶמְצַע. אִם אָמְרָה: ״רְאוּ מַה שֶּׁהִנִּיחַ לִי בַּעְלִי, הֲרֵי אֲנִי עוֹשָׂה וְאוֹכֶלֶת״ – הִשְׁבִּיחָה לְעַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who** died and **left** behind **adult and minor sons,** if **the adult** sons **enhanced the property, they enhanced** it so that the profit goes **to the middle,** i.e., it is distributed among all the heirs. **If** the adult sons **said** from the outset: **See that which** our **father left** behind; **we are** going to **engage** in business with our share of the property **and profit** from it, then **they enhanced** the property **for themselves. And similarly,** with regard to **a wife who enhanced the property** of her deceased husband, **she enhanced** it so that the profit goes **to the middle,** i.e., it is divided between her and the heirs. **If she said: See that which my husband left me; I am** going to **engage** in business with my share **and profit** from it, then **she enhanced** the property **for herself.**

A new *mishnah* opens: *hiniach banim gedolim u-ketanim* — a man died and left behind both older and younger sons. *Ve-hishpichu gedolim et ha-nechasim* — the older sons proceeded to make capital improvements to the undivided estate: planting, tending, harvesting.

*Hishpichu la-emtza* — those improvements are divided equally among all the heirs. This might seem counterintuitive. If one partner improves jointly-held property without prior agreement, the other partners ordinarily owe at least compensation for expenses. Why should the older sons simply absorb all the cost and labor while the younger sons share equally in the benefit?

Tosafos explains: in an ordinary partnership, one does not implicitly waive the right to reimbursement for expenses. But in the context of a shared inherited estate — where the heirs are in a family relationship and the estate has not yet been formally divided — a person who goes ahead and works the land without any declaration is presumed to be doing so on behalf of the estate as a whole. Out-of-pocket expenses could perhaps be recouped by submitting receipts, but labor and effort are not separately compensable when the person never announced any intent to profit individually.

*Ve-im amru re'u mah she-hiniach abba, harei anu osim ve-ochlim* — but if the older sons stood up and declared: look at what our father left; we intend to work it and to keep what we earn — *hishpichu le-atzmam*, they keep the improvement for themselves. Their declaration, met with silence from the younger sons or their representatives, functions as consent to a separate arrangement. The silence of those who could have objected is treated as acquiescence.

The *mishnah* adds a parallel: *ve-chen ha-isha she-hishpichah et ha-nechasim hishpichah la-emtza* — the same rule applies to a woman who was somehow a co-heir and made improvements without any declaration; the improvement is shared. *Im amrah re'u mah she-hiniach li ba'ali, harei ani osah ve-ochelet* — if she declared her intent to work it and keep the benefit, *hishpichah le-atzmah*, she retains it for herself. Notably, the *gemara* will need to explain how a woman comes to be in the position of co-heir at all, a question taken up in the continuation.
