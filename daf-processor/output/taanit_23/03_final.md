# Taanit 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rov Tovah & Shimon

### Pestilence Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל הַדֶּבֶר כּוּ׳. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים בַּשַּׁבָּת, אֲבָל בַּחוֹל — הוֹדוּ לוֹ, אוֹ דִלְמָא: לֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ כְּלָל? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: מַתְרִיעִין עַל הַדֶּבֶר בַּשַּׁבָּת, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בַּחוֹל. רַבִּי חָנָן בֶּן פִּיטוֹם תַּלְמִידוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין מַתְרִיעִין עַל הַדֶּבֶר כׇּל עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that **Shimon the Timnite says:** One may cry out on Shabbat **even for pestilence,** but the Rabbis did not agree with him. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does this mean that **the Rabbis did not agree with him** with regard to crying out in these cases **on Shabbat,** but if they occur **on a weekday they agreed with him? Or perhaps they did not agree with him at all,** as they maintain that one never cries out over pestilence. The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One cries out for** a plague of **pestilence on Shabbat, and needless to say on weekdays. Rabbi Ḥanan ben Pitom, the student of Rabbi Akiva, says in the name of Rabbi Akiva: One does not cry out for pestilence at all.** This opinion attributed to Rabbi Akiva is the ruling of the Rabbis in the mishna.

The *gemara* picks up in the middle of 27b, resuming its treatment of the *mishnah*'s list of calamities that warrant sounding the alarm — *matrian* — and the recitation of special *tefillot*, even on *Shabbat*. We had previously encountered the interesting position of Rabbi Yosi, who urged attention to practical remedies over *tefillot*, a position that may also have intersected with the question of whether an individual may fast on *Shabbat*.

The *mishnah* then introduces the view of Shimon HaTeimani: *af al hadever* — even pestilence warrants the alarm on *Shabbat* — *velo hodu lo chachamim*, and the Sages did not agree with him. The *gemara* raises the question: does their disagreement pertain specifically to *Shabbat*, while on a weekday everyone would concede that *dever* warrants a *taanit* and special *tefillot*? After all, we saw earlier how *dever* in one locale would prompt response even in distant communities because of the danger of its spread through caravans. Or perhaps the Sages reject the view *kol ikkar* — entirely, even on a weekday?

*Ta shma* — come and hear — from a *baraita*: *matrian al hadever b'Shabbat*, you do sound the alarm for *dever* on *Shabbat*, *v'ein tzarich lomar b'chol*, and it goes without saying on a weekday. Yet Chanan ben Pitom, a student of Rabbi Akiva, says in his teacher's name: *ein matrian al hadever kol ikkar* — you never recite the special *teruah* for *dever* at all. This is a striking position, offered without explanation, and the *gemara* treats it as the view of the Sages who dispute Shimon HaTeimani. It sits in notable tension with the earlier *gemara* that treated *dever* as precisely the kind of traveling danger that warranted communal response.

### No Rov Tovah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל כׇּל צָרָה שֶׁלֹּא תָּבוֹא עַל הַצִּבּוּר כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עַל כׇּל צָרָה שֶׁלֹּא תָּבוֹא עַל הַצִּבּוּר מַתְרִיעִין עָלֶיהָ, חוּץ מֵרוֹב גְּשָׁמִים. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מִתְפַּלְּלִין עַל רוֹב הַטּוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further states that they sound the alarm **on** account of any **trouble that should not befall the community,** a euphemism for trouble that may befall the community, except for an overabundance of rain. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **For any trouble that should not befall the community, they sound the alarm for it, except for an overabundance of rain.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Because one does not pray over an excess of good.** Since rain is generally good for the world, it is not appropriate to pray for it to stop, even when it falls in excess.

The *mishnah* continues: *al kol tzara shelo tavo al hatzibbor matrian aleha* — on any calamity, God forbid it should not come, one sounds the alarm — *chutz mirov geshamim*, except for too much rain. This exception serves as the transition into the extended treatment of Choni HaMa'agel, whose entire story revolves around precisely this issue.

A *baraita* reiterates: on any calamity you sound the alarm, except for too much rain. *Mai ta'ama?* What is the reason? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *lefi she'ein mitpalelin al rov hatova* — because one does not pray against an excess of good. Rabbi Yochanan frames this as a general principle, not limited to rain. Yet the formulation is somewhat tautological: you do not pray about it because it is an excess of good. The deeper logic requires unpacking.

One might ask why, if too much rain is practically harmful — flooding fields, ruining crops — it should be categorically off-limits as a subject of prayer. The principle seems to gesture at something beyond pragmatics. If rain carries symbolic weight as the manifest expression of divine favor and connection — the presence of rain meaning God is blessing the people, its absence meaning estrangement — then to cry out against rain, even when it causes damage, is to reject that expression of divine goodness. It is a form of *kafui tovah*, ingratitude, that undermines the entire symbolic framework.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִין שֶׁאֵין מִתְפַּלְּלִין עַל רוֹב הַטּוֹבָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָבִיאוּ אֶת כׇּל הַמַּעֲשֵׂר אֶל בֵּית הָאוֹצָר וְגוֹ׳״. מַאי ״עַד בְּלִי דָּי״? אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר רַב יוֹד: עַד שֶׁיִּבְלוּ שִׂפְתוֹתֵיכֶם מִלּוֹמַר ״דָּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: From where** is it derived **that one does not pray over an excess of good?** It **is stated: "Bring the whole tithe into the storeroom,** that there may be food in My house, and test Me now by this, said the Lord of hosts, if I will not open for you the windows of heaven, and pour out for you a blessing that there shall be more than sufficiency" (Malachi 3:10). **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **"That there shall be more than sufficiency [*ad beli dai*]"? Rami bar Ḥama said:** It means that the abundance will be so great **that your lips will be worn out [*yivlu*],** similar to the word *beli*, **from saying enough [*dai*].** In other words, even when a blessing is delivered in gross excess, one should not pray for it to cease, as the verse blesses the people with an excess.

Rabbi Yochanan then cites the verse in support: *vaharikofi lachem et aruvot hashamayim ad bli dai* — I will open the storehouses of heaven until there is *bli dai*. Rami bar Rav Yud interprets *ad bli dai* to mean until your lips grow weary of saying *dai* — enough, enough, enough. The proof is that there will be so much blessing that you will keep saying stop, but the *gemara* acknowledges the difficulty: if one is saying *dai*, one is in effect asking God to stop. Perhaps the distinction lies between the informal exclamation of *dai* and a formal act of prayer to halt the blessing, but the *gemara* does not resolve this cleanly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר רַב יוֹד: וּבַגּוֹלָה, מַתְרִיעִין עָלֶיהָ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: שָׁנָה שֶׁגְּשָׁמֶיהָ מְרוּבִּין, אַנְשֵׁי מִשְׁמָר שׁוֹלְחִין לְאַנְשֵׁי מַעֲמָד: תְּנוּ עֵינֵיכֶם בַּאֲחֵיכֶם שֶׁבַּגּוֹלָה, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא בָּתֵּיהֶם קִבְרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Rav Yud said:** This is true in Eretz Yisrael, but in the Diaspora, i.e., Babylonia, **they do sound the alarm over** excessive rain. The reason is that Babylonia is in a low-lying region, where excessive rain poses a real danger. **That** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: In **a year whose rains are abundant, the members of the priestly watch** in the Temple **would send** a message **to the members of the non-priestly watch: Cast your eyes on your brothers in the Diaspora** and have them in mind when you pray, so **that their houses** should not collapse from excessive rain and **become their graves.**

The *gemara* then qualifies the rule: *uva-golah*, in exile — that is, in Bavel — where conditions differ fundamentally, one does in fact sound the alarm for excessive rain. A *baraita* specifies that in a very rainy year, the *anshei mishmar* — the priestly rotation on Temple duty — would send word to the *anshei ma'amad*, the Israelite representatives, urging them to pray for their brethren in the *golah*, *shelo yihiyu bateihem kivreihem*, that their houses should not become their graves. The phrase, familiar from the *kohen gadol*'s *Yom Kippur* prayer, here conveys the genuine mortal danger of flooding.

The permission to pray against excessive rain in Bavel highlights, by contrast, why the prohibition applies in the Land of Israel. In Bavel, rain is never in dire scarcity; the wetlands mean the pendulum can swing either way and prayer in either direction carries no particular symbolic freight. In the Land of Israel, however, rain is perpetually and urgently needed. To ever voice the sentiment that there is too much would subtly corrode the sense of absolute dependence and dire necessity that characterizes Israel's relationship with rain throughout the year. The community's willingness to declare *enough* would undermine the very seriousness of its perennial cry for rain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: עַד הֵיכָן גְּשָׁמִים יוֹרְדִין וְיִתְפַּלְּלוּ שֶׁלֹּא יֵרְדוּ? אָמַר לָהֶם: כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּעֲמוֹד אָדָם בְּקֶרֶן אָפֵל וִישַׁכְשֵׁךְ רַגְלָיו בַּמַּיִם. וְהָתַנְיָא: ״יָדָיו״! רַגְלָיו כְּיָדָיו קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They asked Rabbi Eliezer: How much rain must fall** until **they** should **pray that it should not fall** anymore? **He said to them** by way of exaggeration: Enough rain must fall **so that a person stands at** the colossal cliff **Keren Ophel and dips his feet in water.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that Rabbi Eliezer said he must be able to place **his hands** in water? Rabbi Eliezer could answer: When **I said to you** that he must be able to place **his feet,** I meant **like his hands,** i.e., the water must be high enough that he can dip both his hands and feet into the water with ease.

They then asked Rabbi Eliezer: up to what point should rain descend before one would pray for it to stop? He answered: until a person could stand on the rock called *Keren Afel* — an enormously elevated outcropping — and dip his toes in the water, meaning the water would need to rise to an extraordinary height. A *baraita* variant mentions hands rather than feet, but the *gemara* harmonizes these as equivalent. Rabbah bar bar Chana vouches for the height of *Keren Afel*: he himself saw a nomad riding a camel and holding up a spear, and the figure appeared to him as an ant. The practical upshot is that one would never in fact reach the threshold for praying to stop rain in the Land of Israel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: לְדִידִי חַזְיָא לִי קֶרֶן אָפֵל, דְּקָם הַהוּא טַיָּיעָא כִּי רְכִיב גַּמְלָא וְנָקֵיט רוּמְחָא בִּידֵיהּ מִיתְחֲזֵי אִינִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the height of this great cliff, **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: I** personally **saw Keren Ophel,** and when I peered down I saw **that an Arab was positioned below,** and **while riding a camel and** holding **a spear in his hand, he looked like a worm [*iniva*].**

### Gashmim B'Itam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְנָתַתִּי גִּשְׁמֵיכֶם בְּעִתָּם״, לֹא שִׁכּוֹרָה, וְלֹא צְמֵאָה, אֶלָּא בֵּינוֹנִית. שֶׁכׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהַגְּשָׁמִים מְרוּבִּין — מְטַשְׁטְשִׁין אֶת הָאָרֶץ וְאֵינָהּ מוֹצִיאָהּ פֵּירוֹת. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״בְּעִתָּם״ — בְּלֵילֵי רְבִיעִיּוֹת וּבְלֵילֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: "Then I will give your rains in their season"** (Leviticus 26:4). This means that the earth will be **neither drunk nor thirsty; rather, a moderate** amount of rain will fall. **For as long as the rains are abundant, they muddy** the soil of **the land, and it does not give out** its **produce. Alternatively, "In their season"** means **on Wednesday eves,** i.e., Tuesday nights, **and on Shabbat eves,** i.e., Friday nights, because at these times people are not out in the streets, either due to fear of demonic forces that were thought to wander on Tuesday nights or due to the sanctity of Shabbat.

A *baraita* expounds the verse *v'natati gishmeichem b'itam* — I will give your rains in their proper time. What does *b'itam* mean? The first interpretation: not drunken — that is, not excessive — and not thirsty, not too sparse. Rather, a medium amount. For when rain is excessive, *metashteshin et ha'aretz*, it churns up the earth into mud, and the land cannot bring forth fruit. This *baraita* states explicitly what the earlier discussion implied: the divine promise of rain in its proper time envisions precisely the right quantity.

*Davar acher* — a second interpretation addresses the *timing* rather than the quantity. *B'leilei revi'iyot u'v'leilei shabbatot* — on Tuesday nights, the night going into Wednesday, and on Friday nights. The reasoning is that on those nights people are already at home: on *Shabbat* one returns before dark, and on Tuesday nights there was a traditional concern about going out that likewise kept people indoors. Thus the rain comes when no one is traveling and no one is inconvenienced. The point is not merely convenience, however; it is the *siman bracha*, the sign of blessing, that such rainfall represents. Rain at precisely the right time, accommodating the rhythms of human life, is not only practically beneficial but symbolically laden — a manifestation of God's attentive care for the people.

### Shimon's Era

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בִּימֵי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח שֶׁיָּרְדוּ לָהֶם גְּשָׁמִים בְּלֵילֵי רְבִיעִיּוֹת וּבְלֵילֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת, עַד שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ חִטִּים כִּכְלָיוֹת, וּשְׂעוֹרִים כְּגַרְעִינֵי זֵיתִים, וַעֲדָשִׁים כְּדִינְרֵי זָהָב, וְצָרְרוּ מֵהֶם דּוּגְמָא לַדּוֹרוֹת, לְהוֹדִיעַ כַּמָּה הַחֵטְא גּוֹרֵם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עֲוֹנוֹתֵיכֶם הִטּוּ אֵלֶּה וְחַטֹּאתֵיכֶם מָנְעוּ הַטּוֹב מִכֶּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **As we found** in **the days of Shimon ben Shetaḥ that rain** invariably **fell for them on Wednesday eves and on Shabbat eves, until wheat grew** as big **as kidneys, and barley** as big **as olive pits, and lentils as golden dinars. And they tied** up some **of** these crops as **an example [*dugma*] for** future **generations, to convey** to them **how much** damage **sin causes, as it is stated:** "The Lord our God, Who gives rain, the former rain and the latter rain, in its season that keeps for us the appointed weeks of the harvest. **Your iniquities have turned away these things, and your sins have withheld the good from you"** (Jeremiah 5:24–25).

The *baraita* continues: *sheken matzinu bimei Shimon ben Shatach* — we find that in the days of Shimon ben Shatach this is precisely what occurred. Rain fell on Tuesday nights and Friday nights, *ad she-na'asu chittin kichlayot* — until the wheat grains were as large as kidneys, the barley grains as large as olive pits, and the lentils as large as gold *dinarei*. The crops reached extraordinary proportions as an expression of divine blessing.

These specimens were preserved: *vetzarerum mehem dugma ledorot*, they bound them up as a model for future generations, *lehodia kama hachet gorem* — to demonstrate that in other years, when crops do not grow so abundantly, it is sin that causes the diminishment. The verses from Yirmiyahu are cited: *avonoteichem hitu eleh* — your iniquities have caused these things to turn away — *vechatoteichem mane'u hatov mikem*, and your sins have withheld the good from you.

The *gemara* notes that this emphasis on *siman bracha* runs throughout. The extraordinary produce of Shimon ben Shatach's era is not presented as mere agricultural luck; it is held up as evidence of what divine favor, expressed through perfectly timed and correctly apportioned rain, can produce.

### Herod's Rains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן מָצִינוּ בִּימֵי הוֹרְדוֹס, שֶׁהָיוּ עוֹסְקִין בְּבִנְיַן בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְהָיוּ יוֹרְדִין גְּשָׁמִים בַּלַּיְלָה, לְמָחָר נָשְׁבָה הָרוּחַ וְנִתְפַּזְּרוּ הֶעָבִים וְזָרְחָה הַחַמָּה, וְיָצְאוּ הָעָם לִמְלַאכְתָּן, וְיָדְעוּ שֶׁמְּלֶאכֶת שָׁמַיִם בִּידֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And we likewise found** that **in the days of Herod** that **they were occupied in the building of the Temple, and rain would fall at night. And the next day the wind would blow, the clouds would disperse, the sun would shine, and the people would go out to their work. And** as rain would fall only at a time when it would not interfere with their labor, the nation **knew** that **the work of Heaven** was being performed **by their hands.**

*Vechen matzinu bimei Hordos* — similarly in the days of Herod, who was no great *tzaddik* but who was engaged in rebuilding the *Beit HaMikdash*. The people were occupied with *binyan Beit HaMikdash*, and the rain would fall at night. In the morning, the wind would come, the clouds would scatter, the sun would shine, and everyone would go out to their work. The construction could proceed unimpeded, the days were bright and clear, and the nights brought the necessary rain.

*Veyad'u shemelachet shamayim biyadeihem* — and they knew that the work of heaven was in their hands, that their labor was godly work. The environment itself testified to the divine sanction of their enterprise. Here again the emphasis falls not merely on the convenience of nocturnal rain but on its character as *siman bracha* — and, correspondingly, on the terrifying alternative, *siman kelala*, when rain comes at the wrong time and in the wrong measure.

## Choni Circle Story

### Drought & Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁשָּׁלְחוּ לְחוֹנִי הַמְעַגֵּל וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פַּעַם אַחַת יָצָא רוֹב אֲדָר וְלֹא יָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים, שָׁלְחוּ לְחוֹנִי הַמְעַגֵּל: הִתְפַּלֵּל וְיֵרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים! הִתְפַּלֵּל, וְלֹא יָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **An incident** occurred in **which** the people **sent** a message **to Ḥoni HaMe'aggel.** This event is related in greater detail in the following *baraita*. **The Sages taught: Once, most of** the month of **Adar had passed but rain had** still **not fallen. They sent** this message **to Ḥoni HaMe'aggel: Pray, and rain will fall. He prayed, but no rain fell.**

With the *halacha* of *rov tovah* established, the *gemara* transitions to the figure who has been hovering at the edge of the discussion: Choni HaMa'agel. It is significant that the very era whose perfect rainfall the *gemara* just praised — the time of Shimon ben Shatach — is also the setting for the Choni story. The two are intertwined.

*Pa'am achat yatza rov Adar velo yardu geshamim* — once, most of Adar had already passed and rain had still not fallen. By this point the situation was dire: *Cheshvan*, *Kislev*, *Teves* had all gone by, and the prospect of rain providing any agricultural benefit was dwindling. They sent to Choni HaMa'agel: pray that the rains should come. He prayed — *hitpalel velo yardu geshamim* — and it did not rain.

### Circle & Torrents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָג עוּגָה וְעָמַד בְּתוֹכָהּ, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁעָשָׂה חֲבַקּוּק הַנָּבִיא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל מִשְׁמַרְתִּי אֶעֱמֹדָה וְאֶתְיַצְּבָה עַל מָצוֹר וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! בָּנֶיךָ שָׂמוּ פְּנֵיהֶם עָלַי, שֶׁאֲנִי כְּבֶן בַּיִת לְפָנֶיךָ. נִשְׁבָּע אֲנִי בְּשִׁמְךָ הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁאֵינִי זָז מִכָּאן עַד שֶׁתְּרַחֵם עַל בָּנֶיךָ. הִתְחִילוּ גְּשָׁמִים מְנַטְּפִין. אָמְרוּ לוֹ תַּלְמִידָיו: רַבִּי! רְאִינוּךְ וְלֹא נָמוּת, כִּמְדוּמִּין אָנוּ שֶׁאֵין גְּשָׁמִים יוֹרְדִין אֶלָּא לְהַתִּיר שְׁבוּעָתְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** Ḥoni escalated **by drawing a circle** in the dust **and standing inside it, in the manner that the prophet Habakkuk did, as it is stated: "And I will stand upon my watch and set myself upon the tower,** and I will look out to see what He will say to me, and what I shall answer when I am reproved" (Habakkuk 2:1). This verse is taken to mean that Habakkuk fashioned a kind of prison for himself where he sat. Ḥoni **said before** God: **Master of the Universe, Your children have turned their faces toward me, as I am like a member of Your household.** Therefore, **I take an oath by Your great name that I will not move from here until you have mercy upon Your children** and answer their prayers for rain. **Rain began to trickle** down, but only in small droplets. **His students said to him: Rabbi, we have seen** that **you** can perform great wonders, **but** this quantity of rain is not enough to ensure that **we will not die. It appears to us that** a small amount of **rain is falling only** to enable you **to dissolve your oath,** but it is not nearly enough to save us.

Finding simple prayer insufficient, Choni escalated. *Ag ugah*, he drew a circle, *ve'amad betochah*, and stood within it. The *gemara* cites a precedent: *kederech she'asah Chavakuk HaNavi* — as the prophet Chavakuk had done, as the verse says, *al mishmarti e'emodah* — I will stand at my post. The circle, then, is presented not as magic but as a technique of forceful, committed prayer — a way of planting oneself before God and refusing to move.

Choni addressed God: *Ribbono Shel Olam*, your children have turned their faces to me, for I am like a member of Your household. *Nishba ani beshimcha hagadol she'eini zaz mikan* — I swear by Your great name that I will not move from here *ad sheterachem al banecha*, until You have mercy on Your children. Prayer had become an ultimatum.

Rain began — but only in drips. His students called to him: *re'inucha velo namut* — we have seen you and let us not die, meaning: we look to you to save us. But they observed that the rain seemed to be falling only *lehatir shevuatacha*, merely to technically release his oath, not to provide what they actually needed. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: לֹא כָּךְ שָׁאַלְתִּי, אֶלָּא גִּשְׁמֵי בּוֹרוֹת שִׁיחִין וּמְעָרוֹת. יָרְדוּ בְּזַעַף, עַד שֶׁכׇּל טִפָּה וְטִפָּה כִּמְלֹא פִּי חָבִית. וְשִׁיעֲרוּ חֲכָמִים שֶׁאֵין טִפָּה פְּחוּתָה מִלּוֹג. אָמְרוּ לוֹ תַּלְמִידָיו: רַבִּי, רְאִינוּךְ וְלֹא נָמוּת, כִּמְדוּמִּין אָנוּ שֶׁאֵין גְּשָׁמִים יוֹרְדִין אֶלָּא לְאַבֵּד הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** Ḥoni **said** to God: **I did not ask for this, but** for **rain to** fill the **cisterns, ditches, and caves.** Rain **began to fall furiously, until each and every drop** was as big **as the mouth of a barrel, and the Sages estimated that no drop was less than a *log*** in size. **His students said to him: Rabbi, we have seen** that **you** can call on God to perform miracles **and we will not die,** but now **it appears to us that rain is falling only to destroy the world.**

Choni turned back to God and specified: *gishmei borot shichin ume'arot* — rain to fill the wells and cisterns. What followed was the opposite extreme: rain fell in violent torrents, *yardu beza'af*, until every drop was the size of a log — approximately twelve ounces — and the students feared that this was a rain *l'abed ha'olam*, to destroy the world.

### Gratitude Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְפָנָיו: לֹא כָּךְ שָׁאַלְתִּי, אֶלָּא גִּשְׁמֵי רָצוֹן בְּרָכָה וּנְדָבָה. יָרְדוּ כְּתִיקְנָן, עַד שֶׁעָלוּ כׇּל הָעָם לְהַר הַבַּיִת, מִפְּנֵי הַגְּשָׁמִים. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: רַבִּי, כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהִתְפַּלַּלְתָּ שֶׁיֵּרְדוּ, כָּךְ הִתְפַּלֵּל וְיֵלְכוּ לָהֶם. אָמַר לָהֶם: כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי שֶׁאֵין מִתְפַּלְּלִין עַל רוֹב הַטּוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Ḥoni again **said before** God: **I did not ask for this** harmful rain either, **but** for **rain of benevolence, blessing, and generosity.** Subsequently, the rains **fell in their standard manner, until all of the people** sought higher ground and **ascended to the Temple Mount due to the rain. They said to him: Rabbi, just as you prayed that** the rains **should fall, so too, pray that they should stop. He said to them: This is** the tradition that **I received, that one does not pray over an excess of good.**

Choni again addressed God: *lo kach sha'alti*, that is not what I asked for. *Gishmei ratzon bracha u'nedavah* — rains of goodwill, blessing, and generosity. The rain came down properly, *yardu k'tiknan*, until the entire population had to ascend to the Temple Mount to escape the rising water.

At this point his students urged him: just as you prayed for them to come, now pray for them to go. Here Choni invoked the principle: *kach mekubalani she'ein mitpalelin al rov hatova* — so I have received the tradition: one does not pray against an excess of good. Yet he did not leave the matter there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל פִּי כֵן, הָבִיאוּ לִי פַּר הוֹדָאָה. הֵבִיאוּ לוֹ פַּר הוֹדָאָה, סָמַךְ שְׁתֵּי יָדָיו עָלָיו, וְאָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! עַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהוֹצֵאתָ מִמִּצְרַיִם אֵינָן יְכוֹלִין לֹא בְּרוֹב טוֹבָה וְלֹא בְּרוֹב פּוּרְעָנוּת. כָּעַסְתָּ עֲלֵיהֶם — אֵינָן יְכוֹלִין לַעֲמוֹד, הִשְׁפַּעְתָּ עֲלֵיהֶם טוֹבָה — אֵינָן יְכוֹלִין לַעֲמוֹד, יְהִי רָצוֹן מִלְּפָנֶיךָ שֶׁיִּפָּסְקוּ הַגְּשָׁמִים, וִיהֵא רֶיוַח בָּעוֹלָם. מִיָּד נָשְׁבָה הָרוּחַ, וְנִתְפַּזְּרוּ הֶעָבִים, וְזָרְחָה הַחַמָּה, וְיָצְאוּ הָעָם לַשָּׂדֶה וְהֵבִיאוּ לָהֶם כְּמֵהִין וּפִטְרִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Ḥoni continued: **Nevertheless, bring me a bull.** I will sacrifice it as **a thanks-offering** and pray at the same time. **They brought him a bull** for **a thanks-offering. He placed his two hands on its** head **and said before** God: **Master of the Universe, Your nation Israel, whom You brought out of Egypt, cannot** bear **either an excess of good or an excess of punishment. You grew angry with them** and withheld rain, **and they are unable to bear** it. **You bestowed upon them** too much **good, and they were** also **unable to bear** it. **May it be Your will that the rain stop and that there be relief for the world. Immediately, the wind blew, the clouds dispersed, the sun shone, and everyone went out to the fields and gathered for themselves truffles and mushrooms** that had sprouted in the strong rain.

*Af al pi chen havi'u li par hoda'ah* — nevertheless, bring me a thanksgiving bull. He placed his two hands upon it and addressed God:

*Amcha yisrael she'hotzeta mimitzrayim einam yecholim l'kabel lo rov tova v'lo rov puranut* — Your people Israel whom You brought out of Egypt cannot bear either too much good or too much affliction. *Ka'asta aleihem* — when You were angry at them, they could not endure. *Hishpata aleihem tova* — when You overflowed goodness upon them, they cannot bear that either. Therefore: *yehi ratzon milfanecha she'yifsaku hageshamim*, may it be Thy will that the rains cease so that the world may have relief.

The shift here is significant on multiple levels. First, Choni models the very principle of *ein mitpalelin al rov tovah*: he refuses to request cessation of rain as though it were a calamity. Instead, he first performs the act of *hoda'ah*, thanksgiving, acknowledging that what God has sent is in fact entirely good — it is only the people's incapacity to receive it that is the problem. Second, and perhaps more important, the grammatical register of his address shifts. Earlier, Choni was demanding: *she'eini zaz mikan*, I will not move. Now he says *yehi ratzon milfanecha* — may it be Thy will. The imperative has become a petition. This is the moment everything turns. *Miyad nashva ruach venifzaru ha'avim* — immediately the wind blew, the clouds scattered, the sun shone, and the people went out to the fields and brought in mushrooms and fungi that had sprung from all the moisture.

One might also note a question about Choni's posture throughout the story: it is always his students who report what is happening — the drips, the torrents, the flooding. Whether he was absorbed in his circle in some meditative way, or whether he was simply responding entirely to external prompts rather than taking initiative, is left open. The point that he responds consistently to what people bring to him — beginning with the very act of going to him with the drought — does seem to be deliberately constructed.

### Shimon's Rebuke

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלַח לוֹ שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח: אִלְמָלֵא חוֹנִי אַתָּה, גּוֹזְרַנִי עָלֶיךָ נִידּוּי. שֶׁאִילּוּ שָׁנִים כִּשְׁנֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ שֶׁמַּפְתְּחוֹת גְּשָׁמִים בְּיָדוֹ שֶׁל אֵלִיָּהוּ, לֹא נִמְצָא שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם מִתְחַלֵּל עַל יָדְךָ, אֲבָל מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה לְךָ, שֶׁאַתָּה מִתְחַטֵּא לִפְנֵי הַמָּקוֹם וְעוֹשֶׂה לְךָ רְצוֹנְךָ, כְּבֵן שֶׁמִּתְחַטֵּא עַל אָבִיו וְעוֹשֶׂה לוֹ רְצוֹנוֹ, וְאוֹמֵר לוֹ: אַבָּא, הוֹלִיכַנִי לְרׇחְצֵנִי בְּחַמִּין. שׇׁטְפֵנִי בְּצוֹנֵן. תֵּן לִי אֱגוֹזִים, שְׁקֵדִים, אֲפַרְסְקִים וְרִמּוֹנִים — וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ. וְעָלֶיךָ הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״יִשְׂמַח אָבִיךָ וְאִמֶּךָ וְתָגֵל יוֹלַדְתֶּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Shimon ben Shetaḥ relayed to** Ḥoni HaMe'aggel: **If you were not Ḥoni, I would have decreed ostracism upon you. For were** these **years like the years of Elijah, when the keys of rain** were entrusted **in Elijah's hands,** and he swore it would not rain, **wouldn't the name of Heaven have been desecrated by your** oath not to leave the circle until it rained? Once you have pronounced this oath, either yours or Elijah's must be falsified. **However, what can I do to you, as you nag God and He does your bidding, like a son who nags his father and** his father **does his bidding. And** the son **says to** his father: **Father, take me to be bathed in hot water; wash me with cold water; give me nuts, almonds, peaches, and pomegranates. And** his father **gives him. About you, the verse states: "Your father and mother will be glad and she who bore you will rejoice"** (Proverbs 23:25).

Shimon ben Shatach now sent Choni a message. The juxtaposition with the earlier *baraita* about Shimon ben Shatach's era of perfect rain is pointed: this was the very man whose generation had been so righteous that rain fell in its ideal time and measure, and it is he who finds Choni's conduct deeply troubling.

*Ilmalei Choni ata gozrani alecha nidui* — were you not Choni, I would decree excommunication upon you. He frames his concern through a hypothetical: imagine we were living in the years of Eliyahu, *shemiftechot geshamim beyado shel Eliyahu* — when Eliyahu held the keys of rain and had decreed no rain until he said otherwise. You have now sworn you will not move until God sends rain. Eliyahu has sworn God will not send rain. *Nimtza shem shamayim mitchalel al yadecha* — God's name ends up being desecrated through you, because one of the oaths must be violated.

Beyond the Eliyahu hypothetical, there is a deeper concern: what is the larger religious impact when a person attempts publicly to force God's hand? If the demand fails, how do people interpret that? And if human beings appear able to compel divine action through ritual ultimata, something essential about the nature of prayer — as petition rather than coercion — is corrupted. The distinction between prayer and witchcraft, between *yehi ratzon milfanecha* and the performance of a ritual that mechanically forces nature to comply, is precisely what is at stake. It is worth noting that Shimon ben Shatach was known for his campaign against witchcraft; the *gemara*'s care to frame Choni's circle as parallel to Chavakuk's practice, and as something done by Moshe as well in other contexts, seems designed to address this very concern — to locate Choni's act within the domain of prayer rather than magic.

*Aval ma e'eseh lecha* — but what can I do with you? *She'ata mitchatei lifnei hamakom ve'oseh lecha retzonecha* — you behave improperly before God and He does your will anyway. *Keven shemitchatei al aviv* — like a son who pesters his father and the father indulges him. The father brings him hot baths and cold rinses, nuts and almonds and sweets, whatever he demands — *venoten lo*. And about you the verse says: *yismach avicha ve'imcha vetagel yoladtecha* — your father and your mother shall rejoice, and she who bore you shall be glad.

### Sanhedrin Praise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מָה שָׁלְחוּ בְּנֵי לִשְׁכַּת הַגָּזִית לְחוֹנִי הַמְעַגֵּל — ״וְתִגְזַר אֹמֶר וְיָקׇם לָךְ וְעַל דְּרָכֶיךָ נָגַהּ אוֹר״. ״וְתִגְזַר אֹמֶר״ — אַתָּה גָּזַרְתָּ מִלְּמַטָּה, וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְקַיֵּים מַאֲמָרְךָ מִלְמַעְלָה. ״וְעַל דְּרָכֶיךָ נָגַהּ אוֹר״ — דּוֹר שֶׁהָיָה אָפֵל הֵאַרְתָּ בִּתְפִלָּתֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: What** message did **the members of the Chamber of the Hewn Stone,** the Great Sanhedrin, **send to Ḥoni HaMe'aggel?** About you, the verse states: **"You shall also decree a matter, and it shall be established for you; and the light shall shine upon your ways.** When they cast down, you will say: There is lifting up, for He saves the humble person. He will deliver the one who is not innocent and he will be delivered through the cleanness of your hands" (Job 22:28–30). They interpreted: **"You shall also decree a matter"; you,** Ḥoni, **decree from below, and the Holy One, Blessed be He, fulfills your statement from above. "And the light shall shine upon your ways"; a generation that was in darkness, you have illuminated** it **with your prayer.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי הִשְׁפִּילוּ וַתֹּאמֶר גֵּוָה״ — דּוֹר שֶׁהָיָה שָׁפֵל הִגְבַּהְתּוֹ בִּתְפִלָּתֶךָ. ״וְשַׁח עֵינַיִם יוֹשִׁעַ״ — דּוֹר שֶׁשַּׁח בַּעֲוֹנוֹ הוֹשַׁעְתּוֹ בִּתְפִלָּתֶךָ. ״יְמַלֵּט אִי נָקִי״ — דּוֹר שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה נָקִי מִלַּטְתּוֹ בִּתְפִלָּתֶךָ. ״וְנִמְלַט בְּבֹר כַּפֶּיךָ״ — מִלַּטְתּוֹ בְּמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיךָ הַבְּרוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **"When they cast down, you will say: There is lifting up"; a generation that was cast down, you lifted it up with your prayer. "For He saves the humble person"; a generation that was humble in its transgression, you saved it through your prayer. "He will deliver the one who is not innocent"; a generation that was not innocent, you have delivered it through your prayer. "And he will be delivered through the cleanness of your hands"; you have delivered** an undeserving generation **through the clean work of your hands.**

Against Shimon ben Shatach's measured rebuke, the *Lishkat HaGazit* — the Sanhedrin sitting in the Chamber of Hewn Stone — sent Choni an unequivocal message of praise. They cited the verse: *vategzar omer veyakom loch* — you decree a word and it is fulfilled for you. *Ata gazarta milmatta vehaKadosh Baruch Hu mekayem imrecha milmala* — you decree below and the Holy One, Blessed be He, fulfills your word above. The principle of *tzaddik gozer vehaKadosh Baruch Hu mekayem* is here applied explicitly to Choni.

They continued: *ve'al drachecha naga or* — on your pathways light has shone. *Dor shehayah afel he'arta bitfilatecha* — a generation that was in darkness, you illuminated with your prayer. *Dor shehayah shafel higbahta bitfilatecha* — a generation brought low, you lifted up. *Dor shelo hayah naki bema'asav milatta bitfilatecha* — a generation whose deeds were not clean, you saved through your prayers, through the cleanness of your hands.

The contrast between Shimon ben Shatach and the Sanhedrin is striking. Shimon acknowledges that Choni got results and that God indulges him, but he regards the manner of the achievement as deeply problematic — a child pestering a father, not a servant properly addressing a master. The Sanhedrin offers unqualified gratitude and honor. Perhaps what is being captured is a genuine tension within the tradition: Choni is both a problem and a hero, and neither response fully cancels the other.

## Choni Sleep Story

### Seventy Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל יָמָיו שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק, הָיָה מִצְטַעֵר עַל מִקְרָא זֶה: ״שִׁיר הַמַּעֲלוֹת בְּשׁוּב ה׳ אֶת שִׁיבַת צִיּוֹן הָיִינוּ כְּחֹלְמִים״, אָמַר: מִי אִיכָּא דְּנָיֵים שִׁבְעִין שְׁנִין בְּחֶלְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates another story about Ḥoni HaMe'aggel. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: All the days** of the life **of that righteous man,** Ḥoni, **he was distressed over** the meaning of **this verse: "A song of Ascents: When the Lord brought back those who returned to Zion, we were like those who dream"** (Psalms 126:1). **He said** to himself: **Is there** really a person **who can sleep and dream for seventy years?** How is it possible to compare the seventy-year exile in Babylonia to a dream?

Rabbi Yochanan introduces the second Choni story: *kol yamav shel oto tzaddik haya mitztaer mikra zeh* — throughout this righteous man's life he was troubled by the following verse: *Shir hama'alot beshuv Hashem et shivat tzion hayinu kecholmim* — when God returned the captivity of Zion, we were like dreamers. Choni's question: *mi kadei danayim shivim shanin* — does anyone dream for seventy years? The verse associates the return from the Babylonian exile — a span of seventy years — with the experience of dreamers, and Choni could not understand how that was possible.

### Carob Tree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה אָזֵל בְּאוֹרְחָא, חַזְיֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה נָטַע חָרוּבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַאי, עַד כַּמָּה שְׁנִים טָעֵין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עַד שִׁבְעִין שְׁנִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּשִׁיטָא לָךְ דְּחָיֵית שִׁבְעִין שְׁנִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא חָרוּבָא בְּעָלְמָא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ. כִּי הֵיכִי דִּשְׁתַלוּ לִי אֲבָהָתִי — שְׁתַלִי נָמֵי לִבְרָאִי.
>
> **Translation:** **One day, he was walking along the road** when **he saw a certain man planting a carob tree.** Ḥoni **said to him: This** tree, **after how many years** will it **bear** fruit? The man **said to him:** It will not produce fruit **until seventy years** have passed. Ḥoni **said to him: Is it obvious to you that you will live seventy years,** that you expect to benefit from this tree? **He said to him: That man** himself **found a world** full **of carob trees. Just as my ancestors planted for me, I too am planting for my descendants.**

One day Choni was walking when he saw a man planting a carob tree. He asked him: how many years until this tree bears fruit? The man answered: seventy years. Choni asked: do you really think you will live another seventy years to benefit from it? The man replied: *hai gavra alma becharuva ashkachitei* — I found this world already possessing carob trees. *Heichi deshatlu li avahati shatli nami levarai* — just as those who came before me planted for me, I plant for my children.

The exchange is not incidental. The carob tree and its seventy-year horizon frame everything that follows. The question of what one plants for future generations — of what endures beyond the span of a single charismatic life — becomes the lens through which Choni's own story is now told.

### The Sleep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב, קָא כָּרֵיךְ רִיפְתָּא, אֲתַאי לֵיהּ שִׁינְתָּא, נִים. אַהְדַּרָא לֵיהּ מְשּׁוּנִּיתָא, אִיכַּסִּי מֵעֵינָא, וְנִים שִׁבְעִין שְׁנִין.
>
> **Translation:** Ḥoni **sat and ate bread. Sleep overcame him and he slept. A cliff formed around him, and he disappeared from sight and slept for seventy years.**

*Yatav vekarach rifta* — Choni sat down, ate a meal, and dozed off. A rock formation rose around him, concealing him from view, *venim shivim shanin* — and he slept for seventy years.

### Awakening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָם, חַזְיֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּהוּא קָא מְלַקֵּט מִינַּיְיהוּ, אָמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ הוּא דִּשְׁתַלְתֵּיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בַּר בְּרֵיהּ אֲנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דִּנְיַימִי שִׁבְעִין שְׁנִין. חֲזָא לַחֲמָרְ[תֵּ]יהּ דְּאִתְיְילִידָא לַיהּ רַמְכֵי רַמְכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Ḥoni **awoke, he saw a certain man gathering** carobs from that tree. Ḥoni **said to him:** Are **you the one who planted** this tree? The man **said to him: I am his son's son.** Ḥoni **said to him:** I can **learn from this that I** have **slept for seventy years,** and indeed **he saw that his donkey had sired several herds** during those many years.

When he awoke, he saw a man picking fruit from the very carob tree that had just been planted. *Amar lei at hu deshataltei* — are you the one who planted it? *Bar bareih ana* — I am his grandson. *Shma mina denim shivim shanin* — understand from this that I slept seventy years. His donkey too had produced multiple generations of offspring. What Choni had contemplated abstractly — the seventy years of the dreamers, the seventy years until the carob tree bears fruit — he had now literally lived through in unconscious sleep.

### Study Hall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל לְבֵיתֵיהּ אֲמַר לְהוּ: בְּרֵיהּ דְּחוֹנִי הַמְעַגֵּל מִי קַיָּים? אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ: בְּרֵיהּ לֵיתֵאּ, בַּר בְּרֵיהּ אִיתֵאּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא חוֹנִי הַמְעַגֵּל. לָא הֵימְנוּהוּ. אֲזַל לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, שַׁמְעִינְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּקָאָמְרִי: נְהִירָן שְׁמַעְתָּתִין כְּבִשְׁנֵי חוֹנִי הַמְעַגֵּל, דְּכִי הָוֵי עָיֵיל לְבֵית מִדְרְשָׁא — כֹּל קוּשְׁיָא דַּהֲווֹ לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן הֲוָה מְפָרֵק לְהוּ. אָמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא נִיהוּ, וְלָא הֵימְנוּהוּ, וְלָא עָבְדִי לֵיהּ יְקָרָא כִּדְמִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Ḥoni **went home and said to** the members of the household: **Is the son of Ḥoni HaMe'aggel alive? They said to him: His son is no** longer with us, but **his son's son is** alive. **He said to them: I am Ḥoni HaMe'aggel. They did not believe him. He went to the study hall,** where he **heard the Sages say** about one scholar: **His *halakhot* are as enlightening** and as clear **as in the years of Ḥoni HaMe'aggel, for when** Ḥoni HaMe'aggel **would enter the study hall he would resolve for the Sages any difficulty they had.** Ḥoni **said to them: I am he, but they did not believe him and did not pay him proper respect.**

He went to his house and asked whether the son of Choni HaMa'agel was still alive. They told him the son was gone but the grandson was living. *Amar lehu ana Choni HaMa'agel* — I am Choni HaMa'agel. *Lo he'eminu* — they did not believe him.

He went to the *beit midrash*. The rabbis there were saying: our learning today is as bright and clear as in the days of Choni HaMa'agel, for whenever Choni would enter the *beit midrash*, any difficulty the rabbis had he would resolve. *Amar lehu ana nahu* — he told them, I am he. *Lo heiminu* — they did not believe him. *Velo avdi leh yakra kide'miba'e leh* — and they did not give him the honor he deserved.

### Death & Chavruta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲלַשׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ, בְּעָא רַחֲמֵי, וּמִית. אָמַר רָבָא: הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: אוֹ חַבְרוּתָא אוֹ מִיתוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Ḥoni **became very upset, prayed for mercy, and died. Rava said: This** explains the folk saying **that people say: Either friendship or death,** as one who has no friends is better off dead.

*Chalash da'ateh* — he became despondent. *Ba'ei rachamei umit* — he prayed and he died. *Amar Rava: hainu de'amri inshi, o chavruta o mituta* — that is what people say: either companionship or death. He had no one who recognized him, no one who could receive him as who he was. He was alone in every direction — unrecognized at home, unrecognized in the *beit midrash*, unreachable by his own identity. The death that followed was not random; it was the logical conclusion of absolute irrelevance.

The story operates on several levels simultaneously. There is the motif of the power of Choni's words: he pondered seventy years, and he slept seventy years; he prayed to die, and he died. If his words carry such force, the responsibility attending them is immense. There is also the taming of Choni's power: where the first story showed him as a near-magical figure whose ultimata moved heaven, this story ends in tragedy and obscurity. His extraordinary power could not be transferred. When he returned, even a *beit midrash* that revered his memory could not recognize him in person, and the illumination that used to attend his presence could not be claimed by him in propria persona.

This connects directly back to the carob tree. The man planting the tree understood something Choni had not fully absorbed: that the question is not what you accomplish in your own lifetime but what you plant for those who come after. Choni's personal power was irreplaceable and therefore unrepeatable. What remains, and what the next stories will explore, is whether anything transmissible was planted — whether the *charuva*, the carob tree, of his legacy bore fruit two generations on.

## Abba Chilkiya

### Rabbis Arrive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא חִלְקִיָּה בַּר בְּרֵיהּ דְּחוֹנִי הַמְעַגֵּל הֲוָה, וְכִי מִצְטְרִיךְ עָלְמָא לְמִיטְרָא הֲווֹ מְשַׁדְּרִי רַבָּנַן לְגַבֵּיהּ וּבָעֵי רַחֲמֵי, וְאָתֵי מִיטְרָא. זִימְנָא חֲדָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ עָלְמָא לְמִיטְרָא, שַׁדּוּר רַבָּנַן זוּגָא דְּרַבָּנַן לְגַבֵּיהּ לְמִבְעֵי רַחֲמֵי דְּנֵיתֵי מִיטְרָא. אֲזוּל לְבֵיתֵיהּ וְלָא אַשְׁכְּחוּהּ, אֲזוּל בְּדַבְרָא וְאַשְׁכְּחוּהּ דַּהֲוָה קָא רָפֵיק, יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ שְׁלָמָא וְלָא אַסְבַּר לְהוּ אַפֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates another story, this time about Ḥoni HaMe'aggel's descendants, who were also renowned for their righteous deeds. **Abba Ḥilkiyya was the son of Ḥoni HaMe'aggel's son. And when the world was in need of rain they would send Sages to him, and he would pray for mercy, and rain would fall. Once the world was in need of rain,** and **the Sages sent a pair of Sages to him** so **that he would pray for mercy and rain would fall. They went to his house but they did not find him** there. **They went to the field and found him hoeing** the ground. **They greeted him,** but **he did not return their greetings.**

*Abba Chilkiya bar brei d'Honi HaMa'agel* — Abba Chilkiya, grandson of Choni HaMa'agel. The *gemara* introduces him with a capsule portrait: whenever the world needed rain, they would send to him, *u'va'ei rachamei*, and he would pray — and it would rain. No circle. No ultimatum. No dramatic escalation. Just prayer, and rain.

Once when rain was needed, the rabbis sent a pair of scholars to him. They went to his house and did not find him there. They went out to the field and found him hoeing. They greeted him — *shalom aleichem* — and he did not return the greeting. The narrative has begun, and it consists entirely of behaviors that will require explanation.

### Roof Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּפַנְיָא, כִּי הֲוָה מְנַקֵּט צִיבֵי, דְּרָא צִיבֵי וּמָרָא בְּחַד כַּתְפָּא, וּגְלִימָא בְּחַד כַּתְפָּא. כּוּלַּהּ אוֹרְחָא לָא סָיֵים מְסָאנֵי, כִּי מָטֵי לְמַיָּא סָיֵים מְסָאנֵיהּ. כִּי מְטָא לְהִיזְמֵי וְהִיגֵי דַּלִּינְהוּ לְמָנֵיהּ. כִּי מְטָא לְמָתָא, נָפְקָה דְּבֵיתְהוּ לְאַפֵּיהּ כִּי מִיקַּשְּׁטָא. כִּי מְטָא לְבֵיתֵיהּ, עַלַּת דְּבֵיתְהוּ בְּרֵישָׁא, וַהֲדַר עָיֵיל אִיהוּ, וַהֲדַר עָיְילִי רַבָּנַן. יְתֵיב וּכְרֵיךְ רִיפְתָּא וְלָא אֲמַר לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן תּוּ כְּרוּכוּ. פְּלַג רִיפְתָּא לְיָנוֹקֵי, לְקַשִּׁישָׁא — חֲדָא, וּלְזוּטְרָא — תְּרֵי. אֲמַר לַהּ לִדְבֵיתְהוּ: יָדַעְנָא דְּרַבָּנַן מִשּׁוּם מִיטְרָא קָא אָתוּ, נִיסַּק לְאִיגָּרָא וְנִיבְעֵי רַחֲמֵי, אֶפְשָׁר דְּמִרַצֵּי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וְיֵיתֵי מִיטְרָא וְלָא נַחְזֵיק טֵיבוּתָא לְנַפְשִׁין. סַקוּ לְאִיגָּרָא, קָם אִיהוּ בַּחֲדָא זָוִיתָא, וְאִיהִי בַּחֲדָא זָוִיתָא, קְדוּם סְלוּק עֲנָנֵי מֵהָךְ זָוִיתָא דִּדְבֵיתְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Toward evening, as he was gathering firewood, he placed the wood and hoe on one shoulder and his cloak on the other shoulder. Along the entire way he did not wear his shoes, but when he reached water he put on his shoes. When he reached an area filled with shrubs and thorns he lifted up his clothes. When he reached the city, his wife came out to greet him, adorned with finery. When he reached his house, his wife entered first, he entered afterward, and afterward the two Sages entered. He sat and ate bread, but he did not say to the Sages: Come and eat, as was customary and polite. He divided bread to his children; to the elder child he gave one piece and to the younger one he gave two. Abba Ḥilkiyya said to his wife: I know that these Sages have come due to the rain. Let us go up to the roof and pray for mercy. Perhaps the Holy One, Blessed be He, will be appeased, and it will rain, and we will not receive credit ourselves for the rainfall. They went up to the roof. He stood in one corner and she stood in the other corner. Clouds began to form on that side where his wife stood.

That evening, as he was finishing work, he gathered up chips of wood, carrying them and his hoe on one shoulder and his cloak on the other. The entire way home he walked without shoes. When he reached water he put on his shoes. When he reached thorns and thistles he lifted up his cloak. When he reached the outskirts of the town, his wife came out to meet him, dressed and adorned. When he arrived home, his wife entered first, then he entered, and then the rabbis followed. He sat down and broke bread and did not invite the rabbis to eat. He divided the bread among his children — giving one portion to the older child and two to the younger. He then turned aside and said quietly to his wife: *yad'na lehu l'rabbanan mishum mitra ka'azu* — I know these rabbis have come about rain. *Neisak l'igra v'niba'ei rachamei* — let us go up to the roof and pray. *Efshar d'martzei HaKadosh Baruch Hu v'yeisi mitra v'lo nachzik tivusa l'nafshen* — perhaps God will be pleased with our prayers and bring rain, and we need not take credit for it.

*Saku l'igra* — they went up to the roof. *Kam ihu b'chada zavisa v'hi b'chada zavisa* — he stood in one corner and she in another corner. The image recalls the *gemara*'s reading of *vaye'etar Yitzchak l'Hashem lenochach ishto*, with husband and wife each in their own corner — and what that story produces: *vaye'ater lo Hashem*, God responded to him. The parallel is deliberate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי נָחֵית אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַמַּאי אֲתוֹ רַבָּנַן? אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ: שַׁדַּרוּ לַן רַבָּנַן לְגַבֵּי דְּמָר לְמִיבְעֵי רַחֲמֵי אַמִּיטְרָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: בָּרוּךְ הַמָּקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא הִצְרִיךְ אֶתְכֶם לְאַבָּא חִלְקִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Abba Ḥilkiyya **when he descended, he said to** the Sages: **Why have the Sages come? They said to him: The** other **Sages have sent us to the Master,** so **that you should pray for mercy for rain. He said to them: Blessed is God, Who did not require you to** petition **Abba Ḥilkiyya,** as the sky has filled with clouds and rain is certainly on its way.

*Kadim salik ananei mehach zavisa d'divasiu* — the clouds rose first from his wife's corner. When Abba Chilkiya came down from the roof he said to the rabbis: *Baruch HaMakom shelo hitzrich etchem l'Abba Chilkiya* — blessed is God that you did not need Abba Chilkiya. It was already raining. But the rabbis were not persuaded by the deflection.

### Questions Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ: יָדְעִינַן דְּמִיטְרָא מֵחֲמַת מָר הוּא דַּאֲתָא, אֶלָּא לֵימָא לַן מָר הָנֵי מִילֵּי דִּתְמִיהָא לַן: מַאי טַעְמָא כִּי יָהֵיבְנָא לְמָר שְׁלָמָא לָא אַסְבַּר לַן מָר אַפֵּיהּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: שְׂכִיר יוֹם הֲוַאי, וְאָמֵינָא: לָא אֶיפַּגַּר. וּמַאי טַעְמָא דְּרָא מָר צִיבֵי אַחַד כַּתְפֵּיהּ וּגְלִימָא אַחַד כַּתְפֵּיהּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: טַלִּית שְׁאוּלָה הָיְתָה. לְהָכִי שְׁאַלִי, וּלְהָכִי לָא שְׁאַלִי. מַאי טַעְמָא כּוּלַּהּ אוֹרְחָא לָא סָיֵים מָר מְסָאנֵיהּ וְכִי מָטֵי לְמַיָּא סָיֵים מְסָאנֵיהּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: כּוּלַּהּ אוֹרְחָא חָזֵינָא, בְּמַיָּא לָא קָא חָזֵינָא. מַאי טַעְמָא כִּי מְטָא מָר לְהִיזְמֵי וְהִיגֵי דַּלִּינְהוּ לְמָנֵיהּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: זֶה מַעֲלֶה אֲרוּכָה, וְזֶה אֵינוֹ מַעֲלֶה אֲרוּכָה.
>
> **Translation:** They said to him: **We know that the rain has come on the Master's account. However, let the Master** please **say** and explain **to us these aspects** of your behavior **that are puzzling to us: What is the reason** that **when we greeted the Master, the Master did not return our greeting? He said to them: I am a day laborer,** hired for the day, **and I said** to myself that I may **not delay** my work to answer you. They further inquired: **And what is the reason** that **the Master carried the firewood on one shoulder and** his **cloak on** the other **shoulder? He said to them: It was a borrowed robe. I borrowed it for this** purpose, to wear it, **and I did not borrow it for that purpose,** to place wood on it. The Sages continued to ask Abba Ḥilkiyya about his unusual behavior. **What is the reason** that **the entire way the Master did not wear his shoes, but when he reached water he put on his shoes? He said to them: The entire way I can see** and take care where I walk, and therefore there is no need for me to wear out my shoes, but **in the water I cannot see.** Therefore, I put on my shoes to avoid hurting myself. They asked: **What is the reason** that **the Master reached shrubs and thorns, he lifted up his clothes? He said to them: This** flesh **will heal** if it is scratched by thorns, **but this** garment **will not heal** if it is torn.

The rabbis proceeded to enumerate each puzzling behavior they had observed: Why did you not return our greeting? Why did you carry the wood and cloak on separate shoulders? Why did you go barefoot but put on shoes at the water? Why did you lift your cloak at the thorns and thistles? Why did your wife come out adorned to meet you? Why did she enter the house first, then you, then us? Why did you not invite us to eat? Why did you give the younger child more bread? And finally: why did the clouds rise from your wife's corner before yours?

### Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא כִּי מְטָא מָר לְמָתָא נָפְקָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְּמָר כִּי מִיקַּשְּׁטָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ: כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא אֶתֵּן עֵינַי בְּאִשָּׁה אַחֶרֶת. מַאי טַעְמָא עָיְילָא הִיא בְּרֵישָׁא, וַהֲדַר עָיֵיל מָר אַבָּתְרַהּ, וַהֲדַר עָיְילִינַן אֲנַן? אֲמַר לְהוּ מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא בְּדִיקִיתוּ לִי. מַאי טַעְמָא כִּי כָּרֵיךְ מָר רִיפְתָּא לָא אֲמַר לַן ״אֵיתוֹ כְּרוּכוּ״? מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא נְפִישָׁא רִיפְתָּא, וְאָמֵינָא: לָא אַחְזֵיק בְּהוּ בְּרַבָּנַן טֵיבוּתָא בְּחִנָּם. מַאי טַעְמָא יְהֵיב מָר לְיָנוֹקָא קַשִּׁישָׁא חֲדָא רִיפְתָּא וּלְזוּטְרָא תְּרֵי? אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַאי — קָאֵי בְּבֵיתָא, וְהַאי — יָתֵיב בְּבֵי כְנִישְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** They further inquired: **What is the reason** that **when the Master reached the city, the Master's wife came out adorned** in her finery? **He said to them:** She dresses that way **so that** when I walk through the city **I will not set my eyes upon another woman.** They asked: **What is the reason** that **she entered first, and afterward the Master entered, and** only **afterward we entered? He said to them: Because you have not been checked** by me. I cannot be sure how you will act, and therefore I did not want you to be alone with my wife. The Sages were not done with their questions. **What is the reason** that **when the Master ate bread,** you **did not say to us: Come** and **eat?** He replied: **Because there is not enough bread** for guests, **and I said** to myself that **I should not gain credit from the Sages for nothing,** by offering you food I cannot serve you. They asked: **What is the reason** that **the Master gave the older child one piece** of bread **and the younger child two? He said to them: This** older child **stays at home,** and if he is hungry he can eat at any time, **but this** younger child **sits** and studies **in the synagogue,** and therefore he is hungrier.

Abba Chilkiya answered each question in turn. He had not greeted them because he was a *shechir yom*, a hired day laborer, and it would not be fair to the one paying him to take time off even for a greeting. He had carried the cloak separately from the wood because the cloak was borrowed — *talit she'ulah hi* — and he had borrowed it for wearing, not for any other use. He had gone barefoot the whole way because he could see the road and watch for snakes and scorpions; in water he could not see, so he put on shoes to protect himself. He had lifted his cloak at the thorns because *ze ma'aleh arucha v'ze einah ma'aleh arucha* — skin heals, cloth does not. His wife had come out adorned so that he would not cast his eyes on other women as he walked into the village. She had entered the house first, then he, because he did not know the rabbis' character and did not wish them walking behind his wife. He had not offered them bread because there was not enough — *mishum d'lo nefisha rifta* — and he did not want them to feel grateful to him for an offer he could not actually fulfill. He had given the younger child more bread because the younger child was away from the house all day, at the synagogue, and needed more.

### Wife's Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי טַעְמָא קְדֻים סְלוּק עֲנָנֵי מֵהָךְ זָוִיתָא דַּהֲווֹת קָיְימָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְּמָר לַעֲנָנָא דִידֵיהּ? מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיתְּתָא שְׁכִיחָא בְּבֵיתָא, וְיָהֲבָא רִיפְתָּא לַעֲנִיֵּי, וּמְקָרְבָא הֲנָיָיתַהּ. וַאֲנָא יָהֵיבְנָא זוּזָא, וְלָא מְקָרְבָא הֲנָיָיתֵיהּ. אִי נָמֵי: הָנְהוּ בִּירְיוֹנֵי דְּהָווּ בְּשִׁיבָבוּתַן, אֲנָא בָּעֵי רַחֲמֵי דְּלֵימוּתוּ, וְהִיא בָּעֲיָא רַחֲמֵי דְּלִיהְדְּרוּ בִּתְיוּבְתָּא [וַהֲדַרוּ].
>
> **Translation:** The two Sages had one final set of queries for Abba Ḥilkiyya. **And what is the reason** that the **clouds began to form on that side where the Master's wife stood** before your own side? He explained: **Because my wife is frequently at home, and she gives bread to the poor, and** therefore **her** provision of **benefit** to the needy is **immediate,** i.e., soon after the rains fall she is able to provide the needy with provisions. Accordingly, her prayers are answered without delay. In contrast, **I give money** to the poor, **and** consequently, **the benefit** of my gift **is not immediate,** i.e., it takes a lot of time before the rainfall results in my ability to give money to the poor. **Alternatively,** her prayers may have been answered first because when **certain hooligans [*biryonei*] were living in our neighborhood, I prayed that they should die, but she prayed that they should repent. And** indeed, **they repented.**

The final question was the most consequential: *u'mai ta'ama kadim salik ananei mehach zavita d'havat kayama deveiteihu d'mar* — why did the clouds rise from your wife's corner first?

*Mishum d'itata shchicha b'veita* — because my wife is at home all day. *V'yahava rifta l'anyei u'mkarva hanayata* — and she gives bread to the poor, and the benefit reaches them immediately. *V'ana yahivna zuzi v'lo mkarva hanayata* — I give money, and the benefit is not immediate. Her *tzedakah* is more direct, more personal, more immediately felt by its recipients, and therefore her merit is greater.

A second version is offered as well: *hanu biryonei d'havu b'shivevutan* — there were violent or powerful men in their neighborhood. Abba Chilkiya had prayed that they should die. His wife had prayed that they should repent — *d'lahadru b'tiuvta* — *v'hadru*, and they did repent. Her prayer was more generously aimed, and it was the one answered.

The portrait of Abba Chilkiya as a figure is complete, and the contrast with his grandfather could not be sharper. He seeks no credit. He goes up to the roof with his wife rather than standing alone in a circle. He is answered — but it is his wife who is answered first. The entire mechanism of why rain comes is located not in any charismatic personal power but in the quality of daily conduct: the bread given to the poor at the door, the prayer for an adversary's repentance rather than his death.

This is the institutionalization of what Choni was. The carob tree planted two generations ago has borne its fruit: not another circle-drawer issuing ultimata to heaven, but a righteous man and a righteous woman whose deeds speak for them in a grammar that the rabbis recognize and can transmit. That is what endures across generations — not charisma, but character. Not the force of an individual personality, but a way of being that can be planted, and grown, and inherited.
