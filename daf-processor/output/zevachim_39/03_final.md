# Zevachim 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Mishnah

### Opening

We are in the fourth *perek* of Masechet Zevachim, and the *gemara* of today spans the end of one portion of the *mishnah* and the beginning of another. To reorient ourselves, a brief review of the *mishnah* is in order before proceeding.

### Mishnah Review

The *mishnah* appears at the bottom of 36b and opens with the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding the *matanot* — the blood placements — on the external altar. Beit Shammai holds that of all offerings placed on the *mizbe'ach ha-chizon*, a single placement suffices for atonement, except for the *chatat*, which requires two *matanot*. Beit Hillel disagrees and rules that even the *chatat* achieves atonement with a single placement.

From this dispute follows a practical consequence: if the first placement was performed correctly and the second was performed with intent *chutz li-zmano* (beyond its proper time), atonement is nonetheless achieved. But if the first placement was performed with intent *chutz li-zmano* and the second with intent *chutz li-mkomo* (outside its proper location), the offering becomes *pigul* and carries liability for *karet*.

### External vs Internal

The second part of the *mishnah* shifts to the *mizbe'ach ha-pnimi* — the inner altar. Here the rule is fundamentally different: if even one of the required placements is missing, it is as though no atonement was achieved at all. Moreover, if even one placement was not performed correctly, the offering is *pasul* — though, notably, there is no liability for *karet* in such a case.

The contrast is sharp. On the *mizbe'ach ha-chizon*, a single correct placement suffices *bedi'avad*, even though *le-chatchila* all four should be performed. On the *mizbe'ach ha-pnimi*, however, all four placements must be performed correctly; failure in even one voids the entire service.

This distinction frames the arc of the *gemara* today: we are completing the discussion of how we treat the *mizbe'ach ha-chizon*, and will then turn to the *baraita* relevant to the *mizbe'ach ha-pnimi*.

## Yochanan: 3 Placements

### Timing & Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שָׁלֹשׁ מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁבַּחַטָּאוֹת – אֵינָן בָּאוֹת בַּלַּיְלָה, וּבָאוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה, וְהַמַּעֲלֶה מֵהֶן בַּחוּץ חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Despite the fact that, according to the opinion of Beit Hillel, the last **three placements in** the case of **a sin offering** are not indispensable to the atonement, they **may not be performed at night,** because the blood is invalidated at sunset. **And** these three placements **may be performed after** the **death** of the owner. If the owner of the offering died before any of the blood was placed on the altar, the blood may not be placed, and the offering is burned as a disqualified offering. But if he died after one placement, the priest may perform the other three placements, as he has already facilitated atonement by means of the first placement. **And** as it is a mitzva to perform these three placements on the altar, **one who offers** this blood **up** on an altar **outside** the Temple is **liable** to receive *karet*, the punishment received by one who offers a sacrifice outside the Temple.

We begin fourteen lines from the top of 38b. Rabbi Yochanan teaches: the last three placements of the *chatat* blood — that is, three of the four placements on the corners of the *mizbe'ach* — cannot be performed at night, cannot be performed after the death of the owner, and if someone presents them outside the Temple precincts, he is liable.

To unpack this: the *kohen* has four placements to perform on the four sides of the *mizbe'ach*. If, for example, after the first placement the sun sets, the blood is now invalid and the remaining three placements cannot be performed — *laylah* disqualifies them. Similarly, if the owner of the *korban* were to die after the first placement had already been applied, the remaining three placements — *le-achar mitah* — may not be performed.

### Outside Offering

The third ruling of Rabbi Yochanan is that if someone presents these three latter placements *ba-chutz* — outside the Temple — he is liable, parallel to the general rule governing offerings brought outside their proper place.

## Rav Papa: Stringent

### Rav Papa Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: יֵשׁ מֵהֶן כִּתְחִלָּתוֹ וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶן כְּסוֹפוֹ;
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: There are some** respects in which the blood of the last three placements of a sin offering is treated **like** the blood presented **at the outset,** i.e., like the blood of the first placement, **and there are some** respects in which the blood of the last three placements is treated **like** the blood presented **at the end,** i.e., like the remainder of the blood of a sin offering.

Rav Papa elaborates further on these three latter placements. He observes that they are not treated uniformly: some aspects of these placements are governed as though they were *ke-tchilatan* — like the first, essential placement — while other aspects are governed *ke-sofan* — like the remainder, the final stage of the service.

### Stringencies List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ, וְלַיְלָה, זָרוּת, וּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת, קֶרֶן, וְאֶצְבַּע, כִּיבּוּס, וְשִׁירַיִם – כִּתְחִלָּתָן;
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa elaborates: With regard to liability for presenting blood **outside** the Temple, **and** concerning the prohibition against presenting blood at **night,** and with regard to the disqualification of **a non-priest** from presenting blood and his liability for death at the hand of Heaven if he presents blood in the Temple, **and** with regard to the requirement that the blood that is to be presented must first be placed in **a service vessel,** and that the blood must be placed on the **corner** of the altar, **and** that the placement must be performed with the priest's **finger,** and concerning the obligation to **launder** a garment onto which the blood of a sin offering sprayed, **and** finally with regard to the requirement of pouring the **remainder** of the blood on the base of the altar, the last three placements are treated **like** blood presented at **the outset,** i.e., like the first placement.

The following are treated stringently, as though the three latter placements possess the full character of the first: presenting the blood *chutz* (outside the Temple), performing them at *laylah* (nighttime), having a *zar* (non-priest) perform them, failing to first receive the blood into a *kli sharet* (a sanctified service vessel), not applying the blood with the *etzba* (the finger), the obligation of *kibus* (laundering a garment that was sprayed with such blood), and the requirement to pour the *shirayim* — the remainder of the blood — on the *yesod*, the base of the altar. In all these respects, the three latter placements cannot be dispensed with or modified.

### Leniencies List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָאוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה, לָא שָׁרְיָא, וְלָא מְפַגְּלָא, וְלָא עָיְילָא לְגַוַּאי – כְּסוֹפָן.
>
> **Translation:** **But** these last three placements **may be performed after** the **death** of the owner; and they do **not** render the offering **permitted** for eating, as that was already achieved by means of the first placement; **nor** do they **render** the offering ***piggul*** if during these placements the priest intended to eat or burn the offering beyond its designated time; **and** similarly, they are **not** governed by the *halakha* that if the blood **enters inside** the Sanctuary the sin offering is disqualified. With regard to all these matters the blood of the last three placements is treated **like** the blood presented **at the end,** i.e., like the remainder of the blood of a sin offering.

On the other hand, three matters are treated leniently, as though these placements have the character of the end rather than the beginning. First, *lo sharya* — this does not mean the meat is forbidden; rather, it means that the three latter placements do not determine whether the meat is permitted to be eaten, since that was already established by the first valid placement. Second, *lo mefagla* — these placements do not carry *pigul* status, meaning improper intent during these placements does not render the offering *pigul*. Third, *lo ayla* — these placements are not governed by the rule that if blood enters the inner sanctuary the sin offering is disqualified.

## Laundering Source

### Rav Papa Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּתְנַן: נִיתַּז מִן הַצַּוָּאר עַל הַבֶּגֶד – אֵינוֹ טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס. מִן הַקֶּרֶן וּמִן הַיְסוֹד – אֵינוֹ טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס. הָא מִן הָרָאוּי לְקֶרֶן – טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the obligation to launder a garment onto which the blood of a sin offering sprayed, **Rav Pappa said: From where do I say** that this *halakha* applies even to the blood of the last three placements? It is **as we learned** in a mishna (93a): If the blood of a sin offering **sprayed** directly **from the neck** of the animal **onto a garment,** that garment **does not require laundering,** as the blood had never been received in a vessel. Likewise, if the blood sprayed onto the garment **from the corner** of the altar after having been placed there, **or from the base** of the altar after the remainder was poured there, the garment **does not require laundering.** It may be inferred from here that blood that sprayed from the corner of the altar does not require laundering. **But** blood that is **fit** to be placed **on the corner,** as it has not yet been placed, **requires laundering,** and the blood of the last three placements is indeed fit to be placed on the corner of the altar.

Rav Papa then asks: where does the requirement of *kibus* apply to blood from the last three placements? He sources this from a *mishnah* that will appear later in the tractate. That *mishnah* teaches: if blood of a *chatat* sprays directly from the neck of the animal onto a garment, it does not require *kibus*; similarly, blood that sprays from the *keren* (the corner of the altar) or from the *yesod* (the base) does not require laundering. The inference Rav Papa draws is: blood that is *ra'uy la-keren* — fit for the corner, even if it has not yet actually been placed there — does require *kibus*. This is how Rav Papa establishes the laundering requirement for the three latter placements.

### Scripture Yize

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, מִן הַיְסוֹד אֵינוֹ טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס – הָא מִן הָרָאוּי לַיְסוֹד טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס?! ״אֲשֶׁר יִזֶּה״ כְּתִיב – פְּרָט לָזֶה שֶׁכְּבָר הוּזָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **But according to your reasoning,** one can claim in the same manner that it is only blood that sprayed **from the base** of the altar that **does not require laundering; but** blood **that is fit for the base** of the altar, i.e., what remains of the blood after it has been placed on the corners, **requires laundering.** This is difficult, as **it is written:** "And **when there will be sprinkled [*yizze*]** of its blood upon any garment" (Leviticus 6:20). The future form of the word *yizze* serves to **exclude** this blood sprayed onto the garment from the corner of the altar, **as it has already been sprinkled.**

However, a scriptural challenge is raised. The verse in *Vayikra* 6:20 reads: *kol asher yiga bi-vsarah yikdash va-asher yize mi-damah al ha-beged asher yize aleha techabes be-makom kadosh* — "Whatever touches its flesh shall become holy, and if any of its blood is sprinkled on a garment, that which was sprinkled on it shall be laundered in a holy place." The critical word is *yize*, which is in the future tense. The *gemara* notes: since the verb is written in the future tense, it may exclude blood that has already been sprinkled — *asher yize* is written, indicating something yet to be applied, as opposed to blood that has already been placed.

### Rabbi Nechemya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה; דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם שֶׁהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** ruling? It is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya, as we learned** in a mishna (110b) that **Rabbi Neḥemya says:** With regard to the **remainder of the blood** of an offering, **which** was to be poured at the base of the altar, if **one presented it outside** the Temple, he is **liable.** Since Rabbi Neḥemya treats the remainder of the blood as blood with regard to liability for a service performed outside the Temple, he also treats the remainder of the blood of a sin offering as blood with regard to laundering. Accordingly, Rav Pappa's inference from the previously cited mishna is valid only according to Rabbi Neḥemya, but not according to the majority opinion of the Rabbis, who disagree with him.

Whose opinion underlies this framework? The *gemara* identifies it as that of Rabbi Nechemya, citing a *mishnah* that will appear later in Zevachim: Rabbi Nechemya holds that if one brings the *shiyarei ha-dam* — the remainder of the *korban's* blood that is to be poured on the *yesod* — outside the Temple, he is liable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה – לְעִנְיַן הַעֲלָאָה, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵי אַאֵיבָרִים וּפְדָרִים; לְעִנְיַן כִּיבּוּס – מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ?! אִין;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the proof even according to the opinion of Rabbi Neḥemya: **Say that you heard** that **Rabbi Neḥemya** ruled in this manner **with regard to the offering up** of the remainder of the blood outside the Temple, **just as** is the case **with regard to limbs and fats.** Even though the offering of the limbs and fats is not indispensable for atonement, one who offers them outside the Temple is liable. But **did you** also **hear him** say this **with regard to laundering,** that one must launder a garment sprayed with the remainder of the blood of a sin offering? The Gemara answers: **Yes,** we also heard Rabbi Neḥemya rule in this manner with regard to laundering.

The *gemara* then challenges whether Rabbi Nechemya's position regarding liability for offering this blood *ba-chutz* (comparing it to the case of *evarim u-fedarim*, the limbs and fats) necessarily extends also to *kibus*. The *gemara* answers: yes — we did hear Rabbi Nechemya rule with respect to *kibus* as well.

## Baraita: Base & Intent

### Bloods for Base

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: דָּמִים הַטְּעוּנִין יְסוֹד – טְעוּנִין כִּיבּוּס, וּמַחְשָׁבָה מוֹעֶלֶת בָּהֶן, וְהַמַּעֲלֶה מֵהֶן בַּחוּץ חַיָּיב;
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is as **it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **blood that requires** the **base** of the altar, presumably a reference to the remainder of the blood of a sin offering, which must be poured on the base of the altar, it **requires laundering; and** improper **intent is effective with regard to it,** i.e., if the priest poured such blood with the intent to partake of the sacrificial meat beyond its designated time, the offering is *piggul*; **and one who offers it up outside** the Temple is **liable.**

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* at the top of 39a. The *baraita* opens: bloods that require the *yesod* — *damim ha-te'unim yesod* — require *kibus*, have effective improper intent (*machshava mo'elet ba-hem*), and if one presents them outside the Temple, he is liable.

### Bloods to Aqueduct

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדָמִים הַנִּשְׁפָּכִין לָאַמָּה – אֵין טְעוּנִין כִּיבּוּס, וְאֵין מַחְשָׁבָה מוֹעֶלֶת בָּהֶן, וְהַמַּעֲלֶה מֵהֶן בַּחוּץ פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, with regard to **blood that is poured into the** Temple courtyard **drain** that passed through the Temple and emptied into the Kidron River, which is blood that has become disqualified, it **does not require laundering, and** improper **intent is not effective with regard to it, and one who offers it up outside** the Temple is **exempt.**

In contrast, bloods poured into the *amah* — the aqueduct that runs through the Temple courtyard and empties into the Kidron Valley (where, it is noted, the blood was sold to farmers to use as fertilizer for their fields) — do not require *kibus*, have no effective improper intent, and if one presents them outside the Temple, he is exempt.

Seeing the priestly garments, the spatial layout of the *azarah*, and the mechanics of the Temple service brings the questions we are working through — about distances, laundering obligations, and the flow of blood — into striking relief.

## Inner vs Outer

### Challenge to Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר מַעֲלֶה מֵהֶן בַּחוּץ חַיָּיב – רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה הִיא; וְקָאָמַר: טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: About **whom did you learn that he said** that **one who offers up** the remainder of the blood **outside** the Temple **is liable? It is Rabbi Neḥemya** who says this, **and he states** in this *baraita* that a garment that was sprayed with such blood **requires laundering.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַחְשָׁבָה מוֹעֶלֶת בָּהֶן?! וְהָתַנְיָא: יָצְאוּ שִׁירַיִם וְהַקְטָרַת אֵימוּרִין, שֶׁאֵין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַכַּפָּרָה – שֶׁאֵין מַחְשָׁבָה מוֹעֶלֶת בָּהֶן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** improper **intent effective with regard to** blood that must be poured on the base of the altar? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* discussing the intent that renders an offering *piggul*: The possibility of *piggul* applies only with regard to a service that is indispensable for atonement. This serves to **exclude** pouring the **remainder** of the blood on the altar **and burning** the **sacrificial portions** on the altar, actions **that are not indispensable for atonement,** concerning which the *halakha* is **that** improper **intent is not effective with regard to them.**

The *gemara* now asks: who is the authority for the ruling that offering the remainder of the blood outside incurs liability? It is Rabbi Nechemya. And the *baraita* we just cited attributes to that same blood both stringent rules (requiring *kibus*, effective improper intent, liability if brought outside) and lenient rules (blood poured into the *amah* is exempt, has no effective intent, requires no *kibus*). But this creates a tension: the *gemara* had earlier established — following from Rav Papa's framework — that the blood of the last three placements of the *chatat* is *lo sharya*, *lo mefagla*, and *lo ayla*. Yet the *baraita* seems to suggest that such bloods do carry effective improper intent. How can the *baraita* be the source Rav Papa relies upon if its very structure seems to contradict his leniencies?

Furthermore, the *gemara* raises a grammatical objection. The *baraita* uses the phrase *damim ha-te'unim yesod* — bloods that require the base. But the three latter placements of the *chatat* go to the *keren*, the corner of the altar, not to the *yesod*. If so, the phrase should be *damim ha-te'unim keren*. Perhaps one should read *nit'anim yesod* — bloods that become destined for the base — meaning the remainder that will ultimately be poured on the *yesod* after the corner placements are complete. The *gemara* then presses further: but did you not just say these bloods are *lo mefagla* — they do not render the offering *pigul*? And we have another *baraita* that explicitly excludes *shirayim* and the burning of the sacrificial portions (*haktarat imurin*) from actions that are *me'akvin* atonement, stating that improper intent is not effective with respect to them. This seems to confirm that the three latter placements have no effective improper intent — yet the *baraita* says they do. The tension is acute.

### Inner Altar Bloods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא – בְּשָׁלֹשׁ מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁבַּחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **when that** *baraita* **is taught,** stating that blood that requires the base requires laundering, it is not referring to the remainder of the blood after the placements have been completed. Instead, it is referring **to** the blood that is to be used for the last **three placements** of the blood **of a sin offering.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, טְעוּנִין יְסוֹד?! לְקֶרֶן אָזְלִי! אֵימָא: נִיטְעָנִין יְסוֹד. וּמַחְשָׁבָה מוֹעֶלֶת בָּהֶן?! הָאָמְרַתְּ: לָא שַׁרְיָא וְלָא מְפַגְּלָא וְלָא עָיְילָא לְגַוַּאי – כְּסוֹפָן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** is it correct to describe this blood as **requiring** the **base** of the altar? After all, this blood **goes to the corner** of the altar, not the base. The Gemara answers: **Say** that this means: Blood that becomes **required** for the **base,** i.e., blood that in the end, after the placements are completed, will be poured on the base of the altar. The Gemara further asks: **But is** improper **intent effective with regard to** the blood of the last three placements of the blood of a sin offering? **Didn't you say** that this blood does **not render** the offering **permitted** for eating, **nor** does it **render** the offering ***piggul*, and** it is **not** governed by the *halakha* that if the blood **enters inside** the Sanctuary the sin offering is disqualified? With regard to all these matters the blood of the last three placements is treated **like** the blood presented at **the end,** i.e., like the remainder of the blood of a sin offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא – בְּדָמִים הַפְּנִימִיִּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rather, when that** *baraita* **is taught,** stating that blood requiring the base requires laundering, and improper intent is effective with regard to it, and one who presents of it outside the Temple is liable, it is indeed referring to the remainder of the blood after the placements have been completed. It is stated not with regard to the remainder of the blood of a standard sin offering, but **with regard to** the remainder of **the blood of inner** sin offerings, which are brought on the inner altar located inside the Sanctuary.

The *gemara* resolves this by reinterpreting the *baraita* entirely. When the *baraita* was taught and spoke of *damim ha-te'unim yesod*, it was not referring to the remainder of the blood after the external altar placements. Rather, it refers to the blood of the *chatat* offerings brought on the *mizbe'ach ha-pnimi* — the inner altar inside the sanctuary. These are the bloods designated for the inner service, which require specific application on the *parochet* and the golden altar, and whose remainder is poured on the *yesod* of that altar. With respect to these inner bloods, the stringencies and leniencies described in the *baraita* are coherent and consistent.

### Why Not Inner/Outer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בְּדָמִים הַחִיצוֹנִים מַאי, פָּטוּר?! אַדְּתָנֵי דָּמִים הַנִּשְׁפָּכִין לָאַמָּה; לִיפְלוֹג וְלִיתְנֵי בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּדָמִים הַפְּנִימִיִּם, אֲבָל בְּדָמִים הַחִיצוֹנִים – פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if that is the case, **with regard to** the remainder of **the blood of external** sin offerings that are brought on the external altar, **what** is the *halakha*? Is one who presents them outside the Temple **exempt?** If so, **rather than teaching** the *halakha* of disqualified **blood that is poured into the** Temple courtyard **drain, let** the *baraita* **distinguish and teach** the *halakha* **within the** case of the remainder of the blood **itself,** in the following manner: **In what** case **is this statement said? In** a case of **the blood of** sin offerings brought on the **inner** altar. **But in** the case of **the blood of** sin offerings brought on the **external** altar, one who offers up such an offering outside the Temple is **exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם שֶׁהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב; וְלָא מַתְנֵי לֵיהּ תְּלָתָא פְּטוּרֵי לְבַהֲדֵי תְּלָתָא חִיּוּבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* could not have made such a distinction, for in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** ruling? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya, who says:** With regard to the **remainder of the blood** of a sin offering brought on the external altar, in a case **where one presented it outside** the Temple, he is **liable. And** therefore, were the *tanna* to contrast the *halakha* of the blood of sin offerings brought on the external altar with that of the blood of sin offerings brought on the inner altar, **he would not** have been able to **teach three** rulings of **exemptions corresponding to three** rulings of **liabilities,** as Rabbi Neḥemya maintains that even with regard to the remainder of the blood of a sin offering brought on the external altar, if one presents it outside the Temple he is liable. Therefore, the *tanna* preferred to compare the *halakhot* of the remainder of the blood of inner sin offerings to the disqualified blood that is poured into the Temple courtyard drain, so that he could list three lenient rulings alongside three stringent ones.

This resolution, however, prompts a further question. If the *baraita*'s two-part structure — blood requiring the *yesod* vs. blood poured into the *amah* — is really a distinction between inner altar blood and the disqualified blood of the outer service, why didn't the *baraita* simply say so? Why not state explicitly: "In what cases does this apply? To inner altar blood — but outer altar remainder blood is exempt"?

The answer is that the *baraita* follows the opinion of Rabbi Nechemya, who holds that *shiyarei ha-dam* of the outer *chatat* brought outside the Temple does incur liability. If so, the remainder blood of the outer *chatat* would be liable in at least one of the three categories (offering outside), but not in all three — it would not require *kibus* and would not have effective improper intent in the same way. Rabbi Nechemya therefore could not neatly contrast "three liabilities for inner blood" against "three exemptions for outer blood," because the outer blood sits in an intermediate position. The *tanna* therefore preferred to contrast inner altar blood — where all three stringencies apply — against the blood poured into the *amah*, where all three leniencies apply, in order to present a clean and parallel structure of three against three.

## Ravina: Corner Literal

### Ravina's Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: מִן הַקֶּרֶן – מַמָּשׁ, מִן הַיְסוֹד – מִן הָרָאוּי לַיְסוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss the statement of Rav Pappa, that a garment sprayed by blood from the last three placements of the blood of a sin offering requires laundering, and to his proof from the mishna that states that if the blood of a sin offering sprayed onto a garment from the corner of the altar or from the base of the altar, the garment does not require laundering. **Ravina says,** in answer to the objection raised against Rav Pappa above, that according to Rav Pappa the mishna (93a) should be understood as follows: The term: **From the corner,** means from the corner, **literally,** after the blood was placed there, and therefore Rav Pappa could infer from this that blood that is fit to be placed on the corner, including the blood to be used for the last three sprinklings, requires laundering. But the term: **From the base** of the altar, does not mean from the base, literally. Rather, it means: **From** blood **that is fit for the base** of the altar, i.e., from the remainder of the blood, which is to be poured on the base.

We now return to the earlier discussion of Rav Papa's proof from the *mishnah* — the ruling that blood from the *keren* or the *yesod* does not require *kibus*. Ravina addresses an objection to Rav Papa's inference. Ravina explains: *min ha-keren mamash* — "from the corner" means literally blood that was actually placed on the corner of the altar; whereas *min ha-yesod* — "from the base" — means blood that is *ra'uy la-yesod*, fit or intended for the base, i.e., the remainder of the blood that will be poured there. According to this reading, the *mishnah* is distinguishing two categories: blood that has already been placed on the corner (which does not require *kibus*), and blood that is designated for the base (which also does not require *kibus* since it is the *shirayim*). From this, Rav Papa's inference follows: blood that is *ra'uy la-keren* — fit for the corner but not yet applied — does require *kibus*.

### Tachlifa Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר גַּזָּא לְרָבִינָא: אֵימָא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי רָאוּי הוּא! הַאי מַאי? הַשְׁתָּא רָאוּי לַקֶּרֶן – אָמְרַתְּ לָא; רָאוּי לַיְסוֹד מִיבַּעְיָא?! אֶלָּא מִן הַקֶּרֶן – מִן הַקֶּרֶן מַמָּשׁ, מִן הַיְסוֹד – מִן הָרָאוּי לַיְסוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Taḥlifa bar Gazza said to Ravina:** One can **say** a different explanation, that both **this and that,** i.e., the term: From the corner, and the term: From the base, are referring to blood that **is fit** for the corner or the base, in which case the mishna teaches that a garment sprayed by the blood that was to be used for the last three placements of a sin offering does not require laundering, contrary to the opinion of Rav Pappa. Ravina replied: **What is this** claim? **Now** that **you say** that blood which is **fit for the corner** does **not** require laundering, **is** it **necessary** to state that the same applies to blood that is merely **fit for the base** of the altar? That ruling would be unnecessary. **Rather,** it must be that the term: **From the corner,** means **from the corner, literally,** i.e., that the blood has already been placed there, whereas the term: **From the base,** means **from** blood **that is fit for the base,** i.e., from the remainder of the blood, which is to be poured on the base.

Rav Tachlifa bar Gaza objects to Ravina's interpretation. He suggests an alternative reading: both terms — "from the corner" and "from the base" — refer to blood that is *ra'uy*, fit or designated for those locations, rather than blood that has literally been placed there. On this reading, the *mishnah* would be teaching that blood intended for the corner and blood intended for the base both do not require *kibus* — which would actually undermine Rav Papa's proof.

### Ravina Counters

Ravina rejects this alternative. If both phrases mean *ra'uy* — fit for — then the *mishnah* would be saying that blood fit for the corner and blood fit for the base both do not require laundering. But this would be redundant: if blood that is *ra'uy la-keren* does not require *kibus*, it is certainly unnecessary to tell us separately that blood which is merely *ra'uy la-yesod* also does not — since the blood designated for the base is of lesser status than that designated for the corner. The redundancy forces us to interpret the two phrases differently. Therefore: *min ha-keren* means literally blood that was placed on the corner; *min ha-yesod* means blood fit and intended for the base. And from this distinction, Rav Papa's proof stands: blood that is *ra'uy la-keren* — fit for the corner — does indeed require *kibus*.

## Inner Altar Start

### Mishnah Part 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַנִּיתָּנִין עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְעָשָׂה כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה״ – מָה בָּא לִלְמוֹד?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **all** the offerings **whose** blood is to be **placed on the inner altar,** which are the bull and goat of Yom Kippur, the bull for an unwitting sin of the anointed priest, the bull for an unwitting communal sin, and the goat for an unwitting communal sin of idol worship, if the priest omitted even one of the placements, it is as though he did not facilitate atonement. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The Torah first discusses the bull for an unwitting sin of the anointed priest and afterward the bull for an unwitting communal sin, concerning which it states: "And he shall do with the bull, as he did with the bull for a sin offering" (Leviticus 4:20). It may be asked: This phrase: **"And he shall do…as he did," what** does this **come to teach?** All the details stated with regard to the first bull, i.e., that of the anointed priest, seem to be stated explicitly with regard to the second bull as well.

We now return to the second part of the *mishnah*, which addresses the *mizbe'ach ha-pnimi*. As we reviewed at the outset: all four placements on the inner altar are indispensable, and if even one is missing, it is as though no atonement was achieved. This is the principle the *gemara* will now seek to ground in a scriptural source by way of a *baraita*.

### Rashi Intro

Rashi draws our attention to the phrase *ve-asah ka-asher asah* — "and he shall do as he did" — which appears in the context of the *par helam davar*, the bull brought when the community sins based on an erroneous ruling of the *beit din*. Rashi notes that this phrase is superfluous: after the Torah has detailed all the blood placements of this offering, it is unnecessary to add "and he shall do to this bull as he did to the *par ha-chatat*," since all the relevant procedures have already been spelled out. Indeed, Rashi states explicitly: *ella kula li-drasha* — the verse is entirely available for *drasha*. The one exception Rashi carves out is for the portions of the animal — the lobe of the liver and the two kidneys — which are not explicitly listed for the *par helam davar* and must be learned from the *par kohen mashiach* mentioned earlier in the same passage. Everything else in the verse is available for derivation.

The relevant verse is *Vayikra* 4:20: *ve-asah la-par ka-asher asah le-par ha-chatat ken ya-aseh lo ve-chipper aleihem ha-kohen ve-nislach lahem* — "And he shall do to the bull as he did to the bull of the sin offering; so shall he do to it, and the priest shall make atonement for them and they shall be forgiven."

## Baraita Derivation

### Baraita Opens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִכְפּוֹל בְּהַזָּאָתוֹ; וְלָמַד שֶׁאִם חִיסַּר אַחַת מִכׇּל הַמַּתָּנוֹת – לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְּלוּם. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא מַתַּן שֶׁבַע, שֶׁמְּעַכְּבוֹת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם; מַתַּן אַרְבַּע מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, the verse comes **to repeat the** *halakha* of the **sprinkling of** the blood, as though it were written twice with regard to the same bull. This repetition of the *halakha* indicates that the sprinkling is indispensable, thereby **teaching that if** the priest **omitted one of the placements he has done nothing. I have** a derivation **only** with regard to the **seven placements** on the Curtain separating between the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies, that they are indispensable, **as** these seven **are indispensable in all cases,** as the Gemara will explain (40a). **From where** is it derived that the same applies to the **four placements** on the inner altar? **The verse states: "So shall he do"** (Leviticus 4:20).

The *baraita* opens by asking what the phrase *ve-asah ka-asher asah* comes to teach. The answer: it teaches us about the repetition of the sprinkling — that if even one of all the blood placements was omitted, it is as though nothing was done at all, *lo asah velo klum*.

### Seven Placements

The *baraita* then poses a structural question: I have a derivation only for the seven sprinklings on the *parochet* — the curtain dividing the sanctuary from the *kodesh ha-kodashim* — which are indispensable in all cases. But where do I derive that the same applies to the four placements on the *mizbe'ach ha-pnimi*, the inner altar? The answer comes from the phrase *ken ya-aseh* — "so shall he do" — which extends the principle of indispensability to those four placements as well.

### Par Yom HaKippurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לַפָּר״ – זֶה פַּר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים,
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The verse states: "And he shall do **with the bull"** (Leviticus 4:20); **this** alludes to a different bull whose service is similar, namely **the bull of Yom Kippur.**

The *baraita* then proceeds to decode the verse phrase by phrase. The word *la-par* — "to the bull" — alludes to the *par yom ha-kippurim*, the Yom Kippur bull, whose service is analogous and whose sprinklings are likewise indispensable. The phrase *ka-asher asah le-par* — "as he did to the bull" — refers to the *par kohen mashiach*, the bull of the anointed *kohen gadol*, establishing the same principle of indispensability for that offering. The word *chatat* — "sin offering" — then brings in the *se'irei avodat kochavim*, the goats brought as sin offerings for the communal sin of idolatry, whose blood is likewise sprinkled in the sanctuary and whose flesh is burned.

### Ribuy u'Mi'ut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה לְפַר״ – זֶה פַּר כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ. ״הַחַטָּאת״ – אֵלּוּ שְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה אַף שְׂעִירֵי הָרְגָלִים וּשְׂעִירֵי רָאשֵׁי חֳדָשִׁים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **"As he did with the bull"** (Leviticus 4:20); **this** is a reference to the **bull** for an unwitting sin **of the anointed priest,** teaching that all of the sprinklings of the blood of this bull are also indispensable. **"A sin offering"; these are** the **goats for** an unwitting communal sin of **idol worship,** teaching that they are offered in the same manner as the bull for an unwitting communal sin, their blood being sprinkled in the Sanctuary and their flesh burned. One **might** have thought **that I** should **include also the goats of the Festivals and the goats** sacrificed on the **New Moons,** which are communal offerings as well, i.e., that their service should be performed inside the Sanctuary like that of the bull for an unwitting communal sin. Therefore, **the verse states:** "So shall he do **with this"** (Leviticus 4:20), which indicates that this service is performed only with this animal and not with the goats of the Festivals or the goats of the New Moons.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת אֵלּוּ וּלְהוֹצִיא אֶת אֵלּוּ? אַחַר שֶׁרִיבָּה הַכָּתוּב וּמִיעֵט, מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי אֶת אֵלּוּ שֶׁמְּכַפְּרִין עַל עֲבֵירַת מִצְוָה יְדוּעָה, וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי אֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין מְכַפְּרִין עַל עֲבֵירַת מִצְוָה יְדוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what did you see to include these** goats for an unwitting communal sin of idol worship **and to exclude those** goats sacrificed on the Festivals and the New Moons? The Gemara answers: **After** noting **that the verse included** some offerings **and excluded** others, one can say: **I include these** goats for an unwitting communal sin of idol worship, **as they atone for the known transgression of a mitzva,** i.e., idol worship, and therefore they are similar to the bull for an unwitting communal sin, which is brought for an erroneous ruling of the Sanhedrin with regard to a specific mitzva. **And I exclude those** goats sacrificed on the Festivals and the New Moons, **as they do not atone for the known transgression of a mitzva,** but rather they atone for the unwitting defilement of the Temple or its sacrificial foods.

One might then extend the principle further and include the goats brought for the three festivals (*se'irei ha-regalim*) and the goats of the new month (*se'irei rashei chodashim*), since these too are communal offerings. However, the phrase *ken ya-aseh lo* — "so shall he do to it" — functions as a *mi'ut*, a specification that excludes some offerings. After the verse has both included and excluded, how do we know which to include and which to exclude? The *baraita* answers: we apply a principle of *ribuy u-mi'ut*. We include those offerings that atone for a *aveirat mitzvah yedu'ah* — a known, specific transgression — and exclude those that do not. The goats of the festivals and the new moon atone for *tum'at mikdash ve-kodashav* — defilement of the sanctuary and its consecrated objects — which is by nature not a "known" transgression in the relevant sense. The goats of idolatry, by contrast, atone for a known and specific sin. Hence they are included; the festival and new-moon goats are excluded.

### Scripture Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכִפֶּר״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא סָמַךְ. ״וְנִסְלַח״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן שִׁירַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* resumes its interpretation of the verse. **"And** the priest **shall make atonement";** this teaches that atonement is achieved **even if** the Elders **did not place** their **hands** on the head of the bull as they are commanded to do (see Leviticus 4:15). **"And** they **shall be forgiven";** this teaches that atonement is achieved **even if** the priest **did not place** the **remainder** of the blood on the base of the altar, as is required (see Leviticus 4:7).

The *baraita* then continues to the second half of the verse. The word *ve-chipper* — "and he shall make atonement" — teaches that atonement is achieved even if there was no *semichah*, no laying on of hands by the elders on the head of the bull (as prescribed in *Vayikra* 4:15). And the word *ve-nislach* — "and they shall be forgiven" — teaches that atonement is nonetheless achieved even if the *kohen* did not place the *shirayim* on the *yesod* (as prescribed in *Vayikra* 4:7). Both *semichah* and *shirayim* are thus declared non-indispensable for the atonement of these offerings.

### Gemara Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ לִפְסוֹל בְּהַזָּאוֹת, וּלְהַכְשִׁיר בִּסְמִיכָה וְשִׁירַיִם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara again asks: **And** as the verse does not specify which aspects of the service are included and which are excluded, **what did you see to disqualify** the offering **in** the case of the seven **sprinklings;** how did you derive that the phrase "And he shall do…as he did" teaches that the seven sprinklings are indispensable? **And** what did you see **to render** the offering **fit in** the absence of **placing hands** on the head of the offering **and** pouring out the **remainder** of the blood on the base of the altar, based on the words "And the priest shall make atonement…and they shall be forgiven"?

The *gemara* presses the *baraita*: how does one derive from the phrase *ve-asah ka-asher asah* that the seven sprinklings are indispensable, while simultaneously deriving from *ve-chipper* and *ve-nislach* that *semichah* and *shirayim* are not? The verse does not itself specify which elements are indispensable and which are not — so on what basis is this distinction made?

### Answer: Ma'akvin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: פּוֹסֵל [אֲנִי] בְּהַזָּאוֹת – שֶׁמְּעַכְּבוֹת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, וּמַכְשִׁיר אֲנִי בִּסְמִיכָה וְשִׁירַיִם – שֶׁאֵין מְעַכְּבוֹת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **You should say** the following logical argument: I **disqualify** the offering **in** the absence of the seven **sprinklings, as** these seven sprinklings **are indispensable in all cases,** as will be explained (40a), **and I render** the offering **fit in** the absence of **placing hands** on the head of the offering **and** in the absence of the pouring of the **remainder** of the blood onto the base of the altar, **as** these **are not indispensable in all cases,** and therefore it is reasonable to conclude that they are not indispensable here either.

The *gemara* answers with a logical argument: I disqualify the offering in the absence of the seven sprinklings because the seven sprinklings are *ma'akvin be-chol makom* — indispensable in all cases, universally and without exception. This will be elaborated further on 40a. Conversely, I render the offering valid despite the absence of *semichah* and *shirayim* because these are *ein ma'akvin be-chol makom* — they are not indispensable in all cases. Since there exist contexts in which *semichah* or *shirayim* is absent yet the offering is nonetheless valid, they cannot be classified as absolutely indispensable, and therefore their omission does not void the atonement.

The *gemara* will continue to analyze this *baraita* in greater detail on 40a, where the principle of what is and is not *me'akev* will be examined at length.
