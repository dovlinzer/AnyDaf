# Sukkah 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Carrying Concern

### Intro: Lulav on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַמַּאי? טִלְטוּל בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, וְלִידְחֵי שַׁבָּת! אָמַר רַבָּה: גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יִטְּלֶנּוּ בְּיָדוֹ וְיֵלֵךְ אֵצֶל בָּקִי לִלְמוֹד,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Apropos the prohibition against taking a *lulav* on Shabbat, the Gemara asks: **Why** is this prohibited? After all, taking the *lulav* **is merely moving** the object and is prohibited due to the rabbinic prohibition of set-aside. Since the mitzva to take the *lulav* is a mitzva by Torah law, **let it override** this relatively minor **Shabbat** prohibition. **Rabba said:** This prohibition is **a decree lest one take** the *lulav* **in his hand and go to an expert to learn** how to wave the *lulav* or how to recite its blessing,

The daf opens at the very top of 43a, picking up a discussion that had begun at the close of 42b. The *mishna* describes how the *lulav* was taken both in the *Beis Hamikdash* and outside of it on *Shabbos* when the first day *Yom Tov* fell on *Shabbos*. The more pressing question the *gemara* must first resolve, however, is a different one: why is the *lulav* not taken on *Shabbos* when *Shabbos* falls during *Chol Hamoed*?

The *gemara* on 42b puts the question directly: *amai* — why should it be a problem even during the week when *Shabbos* falls on *Chol Hamoed*? Taking the *lulav* is *tiltul b'alma* — merely a question of *muktzah* — so let *Shabbos* override the *muktzah* concern and allow it.

### Rabba's Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיַעֲבִירֶנּוּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְשׁוֹפָר, וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דִמְגִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **and** in doing so **carry it four cubits in the public domain,** thereby violating a severe Torah prohibition. **And that is the reason for** the prohibition against sounding the ***shofar*** on Shabbat, **and that is the reason for** the prohibition against reading the **Scroll** of Esther when Purim coincides with Shabbat.

Rabba answers: *gezeirah shema yitlenu beyado veyeileich eitzel baki lilmod* — we are concerned that a person will pick up his *lulav* and walk out of his house to find an expert who can teach him the *brachos* and the *na'anuim*, and in doing so will inadvertently carry it *daled amos b'reshus harabim*. That is why the *lulav* is not taken on *Shabbos* during *Chol Hamoed* — because it might lead to a biblical violation of carrying.

It is worth noting that this is Rabba's explanation; it is not stated in the *mishna* itself. The *Yerushalmi* may not share this reasoning, and other possibilities exist. Nevertheless, Rabba's explanation is taken as the operative rationale. The *mishna* does hint at this concern: it describes how the *lulav* was brought to the *Beis Hamikdash* in advance, and it acknowledges that one who accidentally carried it on *Shabbos* for the purpose of the *mitzvah* is exempt from a *chatas* — which itself implicitly acknowledges the very real danger of carrying.

### Shofar & Megillah

The *gemara* immediately extends Rabba's reasoning: *v'hainu ta'ama d'shofar v'hainu ta'ama d'megillah* — this same concern explains why the *shofar* is not blown on *Shabbos* when *Rosh Hashanah* falls on *Shabbos*, and why the *megillah* is not read on *Shabbos* when the fourteenth of Adar falls on *Shabbos* in walled cities. In each case, the fear is that a person will carry the instrument or scroll through a *reshus harabim*. The *mishnayos* themselves state these prohibitions plainly; what Rabba supplies — and what the *gemara* now extends — is the unifying rationale.

### Reshus Harabim

This *gemara* opens a significant side discussion about the nature of *reshus harabim*. There is a well-known debate among the *rishonim* about what constitutes a biblical *reshus harabim*. The *geonim* add the requirement that 600,000 people must regularly traverse it — a criterion that does not appear anywhere in the *gemara* itself. The practical consequence of that criterion is significant: it renders a genuine biblical *reshus harabim* exceedingly rare, which in turn allows for an *eiruv* in most circumstances.

One pointed critique of this position, raised by some *rishonim*, is drawn precisely from our context. The *gemara* here makes *gezeiros* that entirely suspend biblical *mitzvos* — not taking the *lulav*, not blowing *shofar*, not reading the *megillah* — specifically out of concern for the biblical prohibition of carrying in a *reshus harabim*. Such a sweeping *gezeirah* would only make sense if *reshus harabim* were a common, everyday reality. If a *reshus harabim* were as rare as the 600,000 criterion would suggest, it is difficult to understand why the rabbis would have suspended biblical *mitzvos* to guard against so remote a concern. This remains a genuine challenge to the *geonic* position.

There is also a question, discussed by both Rashi and *Tosafos*, about why the concern is framed as carrying *daled amos b'reshus harabim* rather than the more immediate act of stepping out of one's house — which would itself constitute the primary violation. *Tosafos* explains that crossing the threshold of one's house is more noticeable and surrounded by more signals that remind one it is *Shabbos*, whereas once a person is already in the street with the *lulav* in hand, the transition is less perceptible.

## First Day vs. Chol HaMed

### Question on First Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי יוֹם רִאשׁוֹן נָמֵי! רִאשׁוֹן, הָא תַּקִּינוּ לֵיה רַבָּנַן בְּבֵיתוֹ. הָתִינַח אַחַר תַּקָּנָה, קוֹדֶם תַּקָּנָה מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** on **the first day** of *Sukkot* that coincides with Shabbat **too** one should not take the *lulav* due to this concern. The Gemara answers: With regard to the **first** day, **the Sages instituted** that one should take the four species **in his house.** Since the Sages already prohibited one from taking the *lulav* out of the house, he will remember that it is prohibited and will not come to take it elsewhere to learn to wave it or to recite the blessing. The Gemara asks: **This** works out **well after the ordinance** that one takes the *lulav* in his house was instituted. However, **prior to** introducing the **ordinance, what is there to say** in explaining why it is permitted to take the *lulav* on the first day?

With Rabba's concern established, the *gemara* raises a sharp question: *ei hachi* — if this is the rationale, then even the first day of *Yom Tov* when it falls on *Shabbos* should be prohibited. Yet the *mishna* makes clear that in the *Beis Hamikdash*, and even outside of it, the *lulav* was taken on *Shabbos* when *Yom Tov* fell on *Shabbos*. Why does the concern of carrying not apply equally on the first day?

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, רִאשׁוֹן דְּאִיתֵיהּ מִן הַתּוֹרָה בִּגְבוּלִין — לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן. הָנָךְ דְּלֵיתַנְהוּ מִן הַתּוֹרָה בִּגְבוּלִין — גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara rejects the previous explanation and explains the distinction differently. On the **first** day, when the mitzva of taking the *lulav* even **in the outlying areas** and not just in the Temple **is** in effect **by Torah** law, **the Sages did not issue a decree** to prohibit taking the *lulav* on the first day and permitted the mitzva to be performed even in the outlying areas. However, with regard to **these** other days of *Sukkot*, when the mitzva of taking the *lulav* **is not in effect by Torah** law **in the outlying areas** and the *lulav* is taken there only to commemorate the practice in the Temple, **the Sages issued a decree** to prohibit taking the *lulav* on the other days.

The *gemara* resolves this with a fundamental distinction. The first day of *Yom Tov* — *yom rishon d'isah minei haTorah bigvulin* — carries a biblical *mitzvah* even outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, for everyone everywhere. Given the weight of a biblical obligation, the rabbis ruled: *lo gazru behu rabanan* — they did not extend the *gezeirah* to this day. The *mitzvah* is important enough that we trust people to take the necessary precautions; we are not going to suppress the biblical *mitzvah* out of anxiety that someone might forget.

The other days of *Chol Hamoed*, by contrast — *d'leisnhu minei haTorah bigvulin* — carry no biblical *mitzvah* outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. There, *gazru behu rabanan* — the rabbis applied the *gezeirah* and the *lulav* is not taken on *Shabbos*.

This distinction also explains a subtlety in the *gemara*'s language. The *gemara* specifies "biblical outside the *Beis Hamikdash*" rather than simply "biblical" because the *mishna* is describing practice *inside* the *Beis Hamikdash*. Within the *Beis Hamikdash*, taking the *lulav* is biblical even on *Chol Hamoed* — yet even there, on *Shabbos*, it is not done. The reason: since it is not biblical outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, the *mitzvah* does not carry sufficient weight in the broader world to override the rabbinic concern. The concern of carrying therefore prevails on *Chol Hamoed*, even in the *Beis Hamikdash* itself.

An important clarification must be stated: allowing the *lulav* on the first day does not mean that carrying it is *mutar*. Carrying on *Shabbos* remains absolutely prohibited. The question is only whether the rabbis will trust people to take the necessary precautions — bringing the *lulav* to the *Beis Hamikdash* or to *shul* the day before — rather than suppressing the biblical *mitzvah* entirely. Just as a *bris milah* on *Shabbos* requires all preparations to be made beforehand, so too with *lulav*: prepare in advance, do not carry, but do perform the *mitzvah*.

### Takanah of Bringing Home

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הָאִידָּנָא נָמֵי! אֲנַן לָא יָדְעִינַן בְּקִיבּוּעָא דְיַרְחָא. אִינְהוּ, דְּיָדְעִי בְּקִיבּוּעָא דְיַרְחָא, לִידְחוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** it is **so** that the mitzva on the first day is a mitzva by Torah law even in the outlying areas, **today too** one should take the *lulav* on the first day of *Sukkot* that coincides with Shabbat. The Gemara answers: **We do not know when** precisely **the establishment of the month** was determined by the court. Therefore, it is possible that the day observed as the first day of *Sukkot* is not *Sukkot* at all. Certainly, one does not violate the rabbinic decree to fulfill a mitzva that is not definitely a mitzva by Torah law. The Gemara asks: If so, with regard to the people of Eretz Yisrael, who sanctify the month based on eyewitness testimony and **who know when** precisely **the establishment of the month** was determined by the court, **let them override** Shabbat for the mitzva of *lulav* on the first day of *Sukkot* even today.

The *gemara* pushes further. Once the *takanah* was instituted — following the violent episode in the *Beis Hamikdash* — requiring people to bring the *lulav* home rather than leave it, there is no longer any concern that someone will walk out carrying it. That explains why after the *takanah* the first day could be done on *Shabbos* without concern. But the *gemara* asks: *kodem ha-takkana mai* — what about before the *takanah*? The *mishna* is clear that even before the *takanah* they would take the *lulav* on *Shabbos* on the first day; that is why they brought it to *shul* on *erev Shabbos* in advance. So even without that particular institutional safeguard, the first-day biblical *mitzvah* was sufficient reason to allow it.

The *gemara* ultimately answers: *mai ika l'meimar* — the answer is the straightforward distinction. *Rishon d'isah minei haTorah bigvulin, lo gazru behu rabanan*. On the first day, which is biblical everywhere, the rabbis did not apply the *gezeirah*. On *Chol Hamoed*, which is not biblical outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, they did. That is the operative principle.

### Modern Practice Outside…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין הָכִי נָמֵי. (דְּתָנֵי) חֲדָא: בְּיוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת כׇּל הָעָם מוֹלִיכִין אֶת לוּלְבֵיהֶן לְהַר הַבַּיִת, (וְתַנְיָא) אִידַּךְ: לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: כָּאן — בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים, כָּאן — בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so,** and that is their practice, **as** it **was taught** in **one** mishna: **On the first day of the Festival that occurs on Shabbat, all the people bring their *lulavim* to the Temple Mount** on Friday. **And we learned** in **another** mishna: They bring their *lulavim* **to the synagogue. Learn from** the change in formulation that **here,** where the mishna says that they bring their *lulavim* to the Temple Mount, it is referring to **when the Temple is in existence, and there,** where the mishna says that they bring their *lulavim* to the synagogue, it is referring to **when the Temple is not in existence.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that this is so.

The *gemara* then turns to contemporary practice. If the first day is biblical and the rabbis allow it, why do we not take the *lulav* nowadays when *Yom Tov* falls on *Shabbos*? The *gemara* answers: *anan lo yedinan bekivu'a deyarcha* — we do not know when *Rosh Chodesh* was established. This does not mean literal ignorance — we have a fixed calendar — but it reflects the institutional reality of *galus*: outside *Eretz Yisrael*, we live as though we were in the era of the *Beis Hamikdash* when those outside Israel could never be certain which day *Rosh Chodesh* had been declared. We therefore observe two days of *Yom Tov* and inhabit, as it were, a state of virtual uncertainty. Because we cannot definitively say that today is certainly the first day — the biblical day — the earlier allowance does not apply to us.

### Practice in Israel

The *gemara* then asks: what about those in *Eretz Yisrael* who do know when *Rosh Chodesh* is and who observe only one day of *Yom Tov*? For them the uncertainty does not exist — *inhu d'yeidi bekivu'a deyarcha*, they live in the reality of knowing. Should they not take the *lulav* on *Shabbos* on the first day? The *gemara* answers: *ein hachi nami* — indeed they do, or should.

### Mishna Proof

The *gemara* supports this from two *mishnayos*. One *mishna* states that when *Yom Tov* fell on *Shabbos* they would bring their *lulavim* to the *Beis Hamikdash* on *erev Shabbos*. A second *mishna*, at the end of the previous chapter, states that they would bring them to *shul* on *erev Shabbos*. The apparent contradiction — *Beis Hamikdash* or *shul*? — is resolved by the *gemara*: *kan b'zman she'Beis Hamikdash kayam, kan b'zman she'ein Beis Hamikdash kayam*. One *mishna* describes the period of the *Beis Hamikdash*, when people brought their *lulavim* there on Friday; the other describes the period after the *Beis Hamikdash*, when people brought them to their local *shuls* on Friday. The *gemara*'s implication is clear: even after the *Beis Hamikdash* was destroyed, those in *Eretz Yisrael* would still take the *lulav* on *Shabbos* on the first day, bringing it to *shul* the day before as preparation.

This is, at this point in the *gemara*, the working conclusion — though it will ultimately be revisited and rejected later in the *daf*. We do not in fact take the *lulav* in *Eretz Yisrael* today even when the first day falls on *Shabbos*, and the *gemara* will explain why. But for now, the *gemara* reads the *mishna* as evidence that the practice existed and was expected to continue.

## Carrying Concern…

### Tosafos: Shofar vs. Lulav

At this point it is worth stepping back and asking why the *mishna* treats *lulav* as more readily permitted on *Shabbos* than *shofar*. After all, if *Rosh Hashanah* and *Sukkos* fall in the same year — and the same calendar — why is it that when *Rosh Hashanah* falls on *Shabbos*, the *shofar* was never blown locally outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, while by *Sukkos* the *mishna* assumes the *lulav* would be taken on *Shabbos* outside the *Beis Hamikdash* as well?

*Tosafos* offers two answers. The first is calendrical. By *Rosh Hashanah*, only those in the immediate vicinity of the *Beis Hamikdash* knew which day had been declared *Rosh Hashanah* — the news had not spread. By *Sukkos*, two weeks had passed since *Rosh Hashanah* was established, giving communities time to learn the date. Since the allowance to take the *lulav* on *Shabbos* rests on the certainty that today is the biblical first day, greater certainty about the date translates into greater latitude. With *shofar*, that certainty was largely absent, which tipped the balance toward caution.

*Tosafos*'s second answer addresses the intrinsic nature of the act. Shaking a *lulav* is essentially a matter of *muktzah* at the rabbinic level — moving an object. In the scale of *Shabbos* concerns, it is not a deeply disruptive act. Blowing a *shofar*, however, is a skilled musical act. While it is not technically a *melacha*, it has the character of craft and skilled labor, which is inherently more at odds with the atmosphere of *Shabbos*. It was therefore obvious from the outset that *shofar* would not be blown locally on *Shabbos*, whereas *lulav* — being a lesser intrusion on *Shabbos* and involving a clearer calendar date — was permitted on the first day.

## Rabbi Eliezer's Position

### Source: Ulekachtem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתֵיהּ מִן הַתּוֹרָה בִּגְבוּלִין מְנָא לַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וּלְקַחְתֶּם״. שֶׁתְּהֵא לְקִיחָה בְּיַד כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that by Torah** law the mitzva of *lulav* on the first day **is** in effect even **in the outlying areas?** The Gemara answers: **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: "And you shall take for yourselves on the first day the fruit of a beautiful tree, branches of a date palm, and boughs of a dense-leaved tree, and willows of the brook, and you shall rejoice before the Lord your God seven days" (Leviticus 23:40). The Sages parse the phrases and terms in the verse. In the phrase **"And you shall take,"** the plural form of you is used, indicating **that there** should **be taking in the hand of each and every one,** and no one can fulfill the obligation on another's behalf.

Having established the practical *halacha*, the *gemara* now asks where the biblical source is for the *mitzvah* of *lulav* applying outside the *Beis Hamikdash* on the first day. This might seem obvious, but it is worth noting that the greater innovation is actually the *Beis Hamikdash*'s practice for the full seven days — that is learned from *lifnei Hashem*, which is understood as referring to the *Beis Hamikdash*. The first-day obligation for everyone, by contrast, is the plain reading of the verse.

The *gemara* cites a *braisa*: *ulekachtem* — you shall take. The plural form teaches *she-tei lekicha biyad kol echad v'echad*, that each individual must take it himself, not through the collective act of one person. *Lachem* — for yourselves — teaches *mishelachem*, that it must belong to you: hence the rules against a borrowed or stolen *lulav* on the first day. Then *bayom harishon* — on the first day — and even on *Shabbos*: *va'afillu b'Shabbos*. The *harishon*, the definite article *heh*, then teaches that it is only the first day that can override the Shabbat concerns, not the other days.

### Bayom Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לָכֶם״ — מִשֶּׁלָּכֶם, לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַשָּׁאוּל וְאֶת הַגָּזוּל. ״בַּיּוֹם״ — וַאֲפִילּוּ בַּשַּׁבָּת. ״רִאשׁוֹן״ — אֲפִילּוּ בִּגְבוּלִין. ״הָרִאשׁוֹן״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאֵינוֹ דּוֹחֶה אֶלָּא יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** They continue to expound the verse. **Yourselves** indicates **from your own, to exclude a borrowed or stolen** *lulav*. **On the day** comes to emphasize that there is a mitzva by Torah law to take the *lulav* on each day of the Festival, **even on Shabbat.** The word **first,** used with no qualification as to where the *lulav* is to be taken, indicates that this obligation is in effect everywhere on the first day, **even in the outlying areas. The first,** with the definite article for emphasis, is restrictive and **teaches that** the mitzva of taking the *lulav* **overrides** Shabbat **only** on **the first day of the Festival.**

The *gemara* initially reads this as a *prisumat d'pasuq*, a derivation from the language of the verse. But it immediately raises a challenge: *michdi tiltul b'alma hu, itztarich kra l'mishrei tiltul?* If taking the *lulav* is only a matter of *muktzah* — a rabbinic concern — why would a biblical verse need to permit it? A verse teaches us about biblical matters; it would be strange for a biblical derivation to address a rabbinic *gezeirah*. This is the classic problem of an *asmachta*: the *gemara* suspects that the verse is merely being used as a hook for a rabbinic rule.

Rabba — the *amora* — reframes the derivation. He says the verse is not being used to permit *muktzah* at all; rather, *nitzrecha ela l'machshirei lulav* — the verse is necessary to permit the preparations for *lulav* even when those preparations would involve a genuine biblical violation of *Shabbos*. The verse is a real *drasha*, not an *asmachta* — but it is a *drasha* that follows the position of a particular *tanna*, as the *gemara* now makes clear.

### Eliezer's Chiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״בַּיּוֹם״ — וַאֲפִילּוּ בַּשַּׁבָּת. מִכְּדֵי טִלְטוּל בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמִישְׁרֵי טִלְטוּל? אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְמַכְשִׁירֵי לוּלָב, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּהַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: לוּלָב וְכׇל מַכְשִׁירָיו דּוֹחִין אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **The Master said: On the day,** indicates **even on Shabbat.** The Gemara asks: **Now, since** taking the four species **entails merely moving** the object and is prohibited due to the rabbinic prohibition of set-aside, **is a verse needed to permit moving** the *lulav*? Obviously, the Torah does not address prohibitions that are not by Torah law. **Rava said:** Indeed, the verse **is necessary only for** actions that are **facilitators** of the performance of the mitzva of ***lulav*,** i.e., to permit actions necessary to prepare a *lulav* for the mitzva, such as severing it from the tree, which may be performed on Shabbat. **And** that is **in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*** who permits doing so on Shabbat, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: ***Lulav* and all** the actions that are **its facilitators override Shabbat;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.**

The *braisa* states: *lulav v'chol machshirav dochin es ha-Shabbos, divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — the *lulav* and all its preparatory acts override *Shabbos*, in the words of Rabbi Eliezer. This is a striking and far-reaching position with two dimensions. First, there is the general principle, familiar from *bris milah*, that once an act itself overrides *Shabbos*, its preparations may override *Shabbos* as well. Rabbi Eliezer applies this even to *machshirei milah* — he famously holds that if one did not prepare the knife before *Shabbos*, one may even chop down a tree to make a fire to heat the knife, because the preparations for *milah* override *Shabbos* just as *milah* itself does.

But *lulav* presents an even greater *chiddush*. With *bris milah*, the act itself is already known to override *Shabbos* — the extension to preparations follows logically. With *lulav*, however, there is no prior basis in the *Torah* for it to override *Shabbos* at all. Rabbi Eliezer is saying two things simultaneously: first, that the act of *lulav* itself overrides *Shabbos*; and second, that all the preparations for *lulav* override *Shabbos*. This is a radical double *chiddush*. The reasoning parallels the principle of *be-mo'ado* by *pesach* — just as *be-mo'ado* means even on *Shabbos* with respect to the *korban Pesach*, so *bayom harishon* by *lulav* means even on *Shabbos*, whatever one needs to do.

We do not *pasken* like Rabbi Eliezer.

### Majority View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? אָמַר קְרָא: ״בַּיּוֹם״, וַאֲפִילּוּ בַּשַּׁבָּת. וְרַבָּנַן —הַאי בַּיּוֹם מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: ״בַּיּוֹם״, וְלֹא בַּלַּיְלָה. וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר ״בַּיּוֹם״ וְלֹא בַּלַּיְלָה מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִסֵּיפָא דִקְרָא: ״וּשְׂמַחְתֶּם לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״ — יָמִים וְלֹא לֵילוֹת. וְרַבָּנַן — אִי מֵהָתָם הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: לֵילַף ״יָמִים״ ״יָמִים״ מִסּוּכָּה. מָה לְהַלָּן — יָמִים וַאֲפִילּוּ לֵילוֹת, אַף כָּאן נָמֵי יָמִים — וַאֲפִילּוּ לֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the rationale** for the statement **of Rabbi Eliezer?** It is as **the verse states: On the day,** indicating that the obligation exists every day of the Festival, **and even on Shabbat.**  The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis, what do they do with** the verse: **On the day?** The Gemara answers: **They require it** to teach that the mitzva of taking the *lulav* is specifically **during the day and not at night.**  The Gemara asks: **And from where does Rabbi Eliezer** derive that the *lulav* is taken **during the day and not at night?** The Gemara answers: **He derives it from the end of the verse: "And you shall rejoice before the Lord your God seven days"** (Leviticus 23:40), indicating that the obligation to take the *lulav* is during the **days and not** during the **nights.**  The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis,** why don't they derive it from that verse? The Gemara answers: **If** it was derived **from there, I would have said: Derive days** written with regard to *lulav* **from days** written with regard to ***sukka*** by means of a verbal analogy; **just as there,** with regard to *sukka*, it means **days and even nights, here too,** with regard to *lulav*, it means **days and even nights.**

The *gemara* then asks: *ve-rabbanan hai bayom mai avdi lei* — what do the sages do with the word *bayom* if they do not use it to permit *lulav* preparations on *Shabbos*? Their answer: *bayom ve-lo ba-layla* — *bayom* teaches that *lulav* is a daytime *mitzvah*, not a nighttime one. It is required during the day but not at night. The word *bayom* is fully occupied with this teaching, and there is no need — and no basis — to derive from it that *lulav* overrides *Shabbos*.

Rabbi Eliezer, in turn, needs a different source for the rule that *lulav* is only a daytime *mitzvah*. He derives it from the end of the verse: *u-smachtem lifnei Hashem Elokeichem shivat yamim* — rejoice before God seven days. The word *yamim* in context means days, and *yamim ve-lo leilot*, days but not nights.

The sages then ask: why could Rabbi Eliezer not have derived the daytime restriction from *yamim* as well, without requiring *bayom*? Because, the *gemara* explains, *yamim* appears by *sukkah* too — *ba-sukkot teshvu shivat yamim* — and there the word *yamim* includes the nights. If *yamim* by *sukkah* includes nighttime, one might have thought *yamim* by *lulav* also includes nighttime. Therefore, Rabbi Eliezer needs the singular *bayom* specifically by *lulav* to teach that *lulav* is daytime only — distinguishing *lulav* from *sukkah* in this respect.

## Sukkah & Miluim

### Gezeira Shava Teshvu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסוּכָּה גּוּפַהּ מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בַּסּוּכּוֹת תֵּשְׁבוּ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״, ״יָמִים״ — וַאֲפִילּוּ לֵילוֹת. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר ״יָמִים״ — וַאֲפִילּוּ לֵילוֹת, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא ״יָמִים״ וְלֹא לֵילוֹת? וְדִין הוּא: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״יָמִים״, וְנֶאֱמַר בְּלוּלָב ״יָמִים״, מָה לְהַלָּן — ״יָמִים״ וְלֹא לֵילוֹת, אַף כָּאן — ״יָמִים״ וְלֹא לֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to ***sukka* itself, from where do we** derive that the mitzva is observed at night as well? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita* that it is written: **"You shall reside in *sukkot* for seven days"** (Leviticus 23:42), from which they derived: **Days and even nights.** The *tanna* continues the discussion: Do **you say days and even nights; or** perhaps the meaning **is only days and not nights? And it** may be inferred **logically** that the latter is correct. **It is stated here,** with regard to *sukka*: **"Days." And it is stated with regard to *lulav*: "Days." Just as there,** with regard to *lulav*, the meaning is **days and not nights, so too here,** with regard to *sukka*, the meaning is **days and not nights.** That is one possibility.

This leads the *gemara* to ask: where do we know that *sukkah* applies at night? The *braisa* takes up the question. The verse says *ba-sukkot teshvu shivat yamim* — dwell in the *sukkah* seven days. Does *yamim* include the nights, or not? One argument pulls toward daytime only: just as *lulav* is a daytime *mitzvah*, and *lulav* also uses *yamim* for its seven-day period in the *Beis Hamikdash*, perhaps we should derive *sukkah* from *lulav* and conclude that *sukkah* is also daytime only.

The counter-argument: compare *sukkah* instead to *miluim* — the seven days during which the *kohanim* dwelled in the *mishkan* during the inaugural consecration period. There the verse explicitly says *yom va-layla* — day and night. Through a *gezeira shava* on the word *teshvu* — which appears both in *ba-sukkot teshvu* and in the *miluim* passage — we link the two: just as *miluim* required dwelling day and night, *sukkah* requires dwelling day and night.

### Continuous vs. Momentary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״יָמִים״ וְנֶאֱמַר בְּמִלּוּאִים ״יָמִים״. מָה לְהַלָּן — ״יָמִים״ וַאֲפִילּוּ לֵילוֹת, אַף כָּאן — ״יָמִים״ וַאֲפִילּוּ לֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Or,** perhaps, **go this way** and say the opposite. **It is stated here,** with regard to *sukka*: **Days, and it is stated with regard to the inauguration** of the Tabernacle: "And at the door of the Tent of Meeting you shall reside day and night seven **days"** (Leviticus 8:35). **Just as there,** with regard to the inauguration of the Tabernacle, the meaning is **days and even nights, so too here,** with regard to *sukka*, the meaning is **days and even nights.** A source exists for either possibility.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִרְאֶה לְמִי דּוֹמֶה: דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ כׇּל הַיּוֹם מִדָּבָר שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ כׇּל הַיּוֹם, וְאַל יוֹכִיחַ דָּבָר שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ שָׁעָה אַחַת. אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ לְדוֹרוֹת מִדָּבָר שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ לְדוֹרוֹת, וְאַל יוֹכִיחוּ מִלּוּאִים שֶׁאֵין נוֹהֲגִין לְדוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Let us see to which** of the paradigms the mitzva of *sukka* is **comparable.** Perhaps **one derives a matter whose mitzva is** in effect **the entire day,** *sukka*, **from** another **matter whose mitzva is** in effect **the entire day,** the inauguration of the Tabernacle, **and do not let a matter whose mitzva is** in effect for **a brief moment,** *lulav*, **prove** otherwise. **Or** perhaps **go this way** and say the opposite: **One derives a matter whose mitzva is** in effect **throughout the generations,** *sukka*, **from** another **matter whose mitzva is** in effect **throughout the generations,** *lulav*, **and do not let** the **inauguration that is not in practice throughout** the **generations,** as it was in effect only at the establishment of the Tabernacle, **prove** otherwise.

The *baraita* provides an elegant logical justification for preferring the *miluim* comparison over the *lulav* comparison. *Davar she-mitzvato kol ha-yom mi-davar she-mitzvato kol ha-yom* — derive something whose *mitzvah* is continuous throughout the day from something whose *mitzvah* is similarly continuous, rather than from *lulav*, which is a momentary act, a single fulfillment in time. *Sukkah*, like *miluim*, involves ongoing dwelling — a continuous obligation, not a one-time act. The structural similarity between *sukkah* and *miluim* makes the comparison compelling, while *lulav* is categorically different in that respect.

### Sukkah Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תֵּשְׁבוּ״ ״תֵּשְׁבוּ״ — לִגְזֵרָה שָׁוָה. נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״תֵּשְׁבוּ״, וְנֶאֱמַר בְּמִלּוּאִים ״תֵּשְׁבוּ״. מָה לְהַלָּן — ״יָמִים״ וַאֲפִילּוּ לֵילוֹת, אַף כָּאן — ״יָמִים״ וַאֲפִילּוּ לֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Since it is impossible to determine the more appropriate source based on logical inference, derive the matter as **the verse states: "You shall reside," "you shall reside,"** by means **of a verbal analogy. It is stated here,** with regard to *sukka*: **"You shall reside** in *sukkot* seven days" (Leviticus 23:42), **and it is stated with regard to the inauguration** of the Tabernacle: "And at the door of the Tent of Meeting **you shall reside** day and night seven days" (Leviticus 8:35). **Just as there,** with regard to the inauguration, the meaning is **days and even nights, so too here,** with regard to *sukka*, the meaning is **days and even nights.**

The conclusion is clear: *sukkah* applies both day and night, derived from *miluim* via *gezeira shava* on *teshvu*. And consequently, the word *bayom* by *lulav* does not inform anything about *sukkah*. Rather, *bayom* is needed precisely to distinguish *lulav* from *sukkah*: *sukkah* includes nights; *lulav* does not. And for Rabbi Eliezer, who uses *bayom* to teach that *lulav* preparations override *Shabbos*, he derives the daytime restriction of *lulav* from *yamim* at the end of the verse.

This wraps up the underlying *sugya* about Rabbi Eliezer's radical position: he genuinely holds that *lulav* overrides biblical *Shabbos* violations, a view the sages reject, and that rejection is what allows the sages to use *bayom* for the more modest teaching of daytime-only.

## Aravah on Day Seven

### Publicize as Biblical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲרָבָה בִּשְׁבִיעִי מַאי טַעְמָא דָּחֲיָא שַׁבָּת? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּדֵי לְפַרְסְמָהּ שֶׁהִיא מִן הַתּוֹרָה. אִי הָכִי, לוּלָב נָמֵי לִידְחֵי, כְּדֵי לְפַרְסְמוֹ שֶׁהוּא מִן הַתּוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: The altar is encircled with the **willow branch** for **seven** days. **How** so? If the seventh day of performing the mitzva of the willow branch occurs on Shabbat, since on that day the mitzva of the willow branch is a mitzva by Torah law, it overrides Shabbat and the mitzva of the willow branch is then performed seven days. The Gemara asks: With regard to the mitzva of the **willow branch on the seventh** day, **what is the reason** that **it overrides Shabbat? Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is **in order to publicize that it is** a mitzva that **applies by Torah** law, since it is not written explicitly in the Torah. The Gemara raises an objection: **If so, *lulav* too should override** Shabbat in the Temple on the other days of *Sukkot* as well and not only on the first day **in order to publicize that it is** a mitzva **by Torah** law all seven days, since that too is not written explicitly in the Torah.

The *gemara* now turns to the second part of the *mishna*: the *aravah* ritual. The *mishna* states that *aravah* was performed in the *Beis Hamikdash* for six or seven days, depending on whether *Shabbos* fell on the seventh day. The *gemara* asks: *aravah b'shvi'i mai ta'ama dachya Shabbos* — why does *aravah* override *Shabbos* on the seventh day? We have already established the concern of carrying. What makes the seventh day of *aravah* special enough to override that concern?

The answer is cited from Rabbi Yochanan: *k'dei l'farsma shehi min haTorah* — in order to publicize that the *aravah* ritual is biblical. This is itself non-obvious. The *Torah* nowhere explicitly commands the *aravah* ritual in the *Beis Hamikdash*. It is either derived from the doubled plural *arvei nachal* — one for the *lulav*, one for the *mikdash* — or it is a *halacha l'Moshe m'Sinai*. Either way, the ritual is considered biblical. Because it might easily be mistaken for a mere custom, it was important to demonstrate its biblical status by allowing it to be performed even on *Shabbos* — showing that we do not suppress it even when *Shabbos* falls on that day.

### Challenge: Lulav Too

The *gemara* challenges this: *ei hachi, Lulav nami lidchi k'dei l'farsma shehu min haTorah* — if publicizing biblical status justifies overriding the carrying concern, why not also let *lulav* be taken on *Shabbos* during *Chol Hamoed* in the *Beis Hamikdash*, to publicize that the *lulav* obligation in the *Beis Hamikdash* during *Chol Hamoed* is biblical?

### Public vs. Limited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוּלָב — גְּזֵרָה מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבָּה. אִי הָכִי, עֲרָבָה נָמֵי נִגְזוֹר! עֲרָבָה — שְׁלוּחֵי בֵּית דִּין מַיְיתִי לַהּ. לוּלָב — לַכֹּל מָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: One is prohibited from taking the ***lulav*** on Shabbat by rabbinic **decree due to** the concern expressed **by Rabba** (42b) lest he take the *lulav* in his hand and go to an expert to learn how to wave the *lulav* and thereby carry it in the public domain. The Gemara objects: **If so,** with regard to the **willow branch as well let us issue a decree** due to the same concern. The Gemara answers: The two cases are different. With regard to the **willow branch, agents of the court bring it** to the priests who perform the mitzva in the Temple, and they carefully prepare the willow branch prior to the onset of Shabbat and will not come to carry it in a prohibited manner on Shabbat. However, performance of the mitzva of ***lulav* is incumbent upon every individual.** Therefore, there is concern lest one unwittingly perform the prohibited labor of carrying on Shabbat.

The *gemara* answers: *Lulav gezeira mishum d'Rabba* — the carrying concern applies with full force to *lulav* because *lulav* is a public *mitzvah*. The *mishna* describes throngs of people bringing their *lulavim* to the *Beis Hamikdash*. With so many people involved, the concern that someone will inadvertently carry is acute.

*Aravah*, by contrast — *shluchei beis din maysei lah* — was handled by designated agents of the *beis din*, who gathered the branches from the place called Motza. Once the branches were collected, it was the *kohanim* who placed them by the sides of the *mizbe'ach*. The entire ritual was conducted in a controlled, supervised, institutionally managed manner, not by the general public. The carrying concern is therefore far less pressing, and we can allow it to be done on *Shabbos* for the purpose of publicizing its biblical status.

*Tosafos* adds a related point: *lulav* is fundamentally a *mitzvah* that belongs to everyone. Once the rabbis disallow it on *Shabbos* for the public at large, they cannot carve out an exception allowing only *kohanim* to do it in the *Beis Hamikdash*. The *aravah* ritual, by contrast, began as a *kohen*-exclusive altar ritual from the outset. It is not that we are making a restricted exception — the ritual is inherently limited in its performers. Permitting it on *Shabbos* therefore does not require the same institutional caution as permitting *lulav*.

### Why Day Seven Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כׇּל יוֹמָא נָמֵי לִידְחֵי! אָתֵי לְפַקְפּוֹקֵי בְּלוּלָב. וְלִידְחֵי בְּיוֹם טוֹב רִאשׁוֹן! לָא מוֹכְחָא מִלְּתָא, אָמְרִי: לוּלָב הוּא דְּקָא דָחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If so,** i.e., because the willow branch is supplied by agents of the court there is no concern that Shabbat will be desecrated, **let** the mitzva of the willow branch **override** Shabbat on **every day** of the Festival **as well.** The Gemara answers: In that case people **would come to raise doubts about** the significance of the mitzva of ***lulav*,** as, unlike the mitzva of the willow branch, it would override Shabbat on only one day of the Festival and not on all seven. The Gemara asks: **And let** the mitzva of the willow branch **override** Shabbat **on the first day of the Festival,** just as the mitzva of *lulav* does, and not on the seventh day. The Gemara answers: **The matter** of publicizing that the mitzva of willow branch is a mitzva by Torah law **would not be apparent,** as people **would say** that **it is** really the mitzva of ***lulav* that overrides** Shabbat, and once *lulav* is permitted the willow branch is permitted as well.

Having established why *aravah* can be done on *Shabbos* at all, the *gemara* raises another question: *kol yoma nami lidchi* — since *aravah* is biblical every day, why not let it override the carrying concern every time *Shabbos* falls during *Sukkos*? Why limit it to day seven?

The *gemara* answers: *ati lisfakuei b'lulav* — allowing *aravah* to override *Shabbos* on any day would undermine the primacy of *lulav*. If *aravah* can be done on *Shabbos* whenever it occurs during *Sukkos* but *lulav* can only be done on *Shabbos* on the first day, it would appear that *aravah* takes precedence over *lulav*. That is unacceptable.

The *gemara* then asks: then make it the same day — when the first day of *Yom Tov* falls on *Shabbos*, do both *lulav* and *aravah*, and give *aravah* no independent day. But the *gemara* rejects this as well: *lo mukcha miltah* — the whole point is to demonstrate the independent importance of *aravah*. If it only occurs alongside *lulav*, people will say *Lulav hu d'kadachi* — it is the *lulav* driving things, and the *aravah* ritual receives no independent recognition.

### Day Seven Distinct

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִידְחֵי בְּחַד מֵהָנָךְ? כֵּיוָן דְּקָא מַפְּקַתְּ לַהּ מֵרִאשׁוֹן, אוֹקְמַהּ אַשְּׁבִיעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** the mitzva of the willow branch **override** Shabbat **on one of these** other days of *Sukkot*; why specifically the seventh day? The Gemara answers: **Once you moved it from the first** day, **establish it on the seventh** day, which is also a unique day of *Sukkot*, and not on one of the other intermediate days of *Sukkot*.

The *gemara* concludes: give *aravah* one distinct day that is not day one. Why specifically day seven? *Kivan d'kamfatkeis lomei rishon, ukmah shevi'i* — once it cannot be the first day, the natural choice is the seventh. Any middle day would fail to be memorable and distinct; the beginning and the end are the natural anchors. Day seven, being the other extreme of the festival, is the obvious candidate.

What is notable here is the implicit logic about *Hoshana Rabba*. The *gemara*'s reasoning works somewhat in reverse of the usual intuition. One might naturally assume that day seven became *Hoshana Rabba* — the climactic day of *Sukkos* with seven circuits around the *mizbe'ach* — because it was already the great culminating moment of the festival. But the *gemara*'s logic suggests something different: the seventh day was chosen as the special day for *aravah* on *Shabbos* partly by process of elimination. It was not going to be day one, so day seven was selected. The special character of *Hoshana Rabba* may thus have emerged from this institutional choice rather than having been the independent basis for it.

## Hoshana Rabba Calendar

### Question: Why Not Now

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הָאִידָּנָא נָמֵי לִידְחֵי! אֲנַן לָא יָדְעִינַן בְּקִיבּוּעָא דְיַרְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** i.e., if the mitzva of the willow branch is so significant that it overrides Shabbat, **let it override** Shabbat **today as well,** even though the Temple is not standing. The Gemara answers: **We do not know** when precisely **the establishment of the month** was determined by the court. Therefore, it is possible that the day observed as the seventh day of *Sukkot* is not the seventh day at all. Certainly, one does not violate the rabbinic decree to fulfill a mitzva that is not definitely a mitzva by Torah law.

The *gemara* now asks the natural follow-up: *ei hachi ha-idna nami lidchi* — if the principle allows *aravah* to be done on *Shabbos* on day seven, why do we not do the *Hoshana Rabba* ritual on *Shabbos* today? The question is acknowledged to be somewhat strange, since we already explained that *aravah* was only allowed on *Shabbos* because it was a limited ritual performed by *shluchei beis din* and *kohanim* — not a broad public practice. Yet the *gemara* entertains the question, perhaps because even in some controlled form it could theoretically be maintained.

One initial response echoes the earlier discussion: *anan lo yedinan bekivu'a deyarcha* — outside *Eretz Yisrael* we live in virtual uncertainty about which day is which, and thus cannot invoke the earlier allowance. And those in *Eretz Yisrael* who do know — *inhu d'yeidi bekivu'a deyarcha, lidchu* — why do they not do it?

### Bar Hadya Report

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינְהוּ, דְּיָדְעִי בְּקִיבּוּעָא דְיַרְחָא — לִידְחֵי! כִּי אֲתָא בַּר הֶדְיָא, אָמַר: לָא אִיקְּלַע. כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין וְכׇל נָחוֹתֵי, אָמְרִי: אִיקְּלַע וְלָא דָּחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, with regard to the people of Eretz Yisrael, **who know the establishment of the month, let them override** Shabbat for the mitzva of willow branch on the seventh day of *Sukkot* even today. **When bar Hedya came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said:** That is not a practical question, as the seventh day **does not coincide** with Shabbat, since the Sages fixed the calendar to avoid that possibility. **When Ravin and all those** emissaries **who descended** to Babylonia, or who originally left Babylonia for Eretz Yisrael and returned, **came, they said: It does coincide** with Shabbat, **but it does not override** Shabbat.

The *gemara* answers with a historical report: *ki ata Bar Hadya amar* — when Bar Hadya came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported: *lo ikla* — it never happened. *Hoshana Rabba* never actually fell on *Shabbos*. This is not coincidental.

### Lo Adu Rosh

The reason is the fixed calendar and its governing mnemonic: *lo adu Rosh* — *Rosh Hashanah* never falls on Sunday (*alef*), Wednesday (*dalet*), or Friday (*vav*). Since the first day of *Sukkos* always falls on the same day of the week as *Rosh Hashanah*, and *Hoshana Rabba* is day seven of *Sukkos*, one can calculate: for *Hoshana Rabba* to fall on *Shabbos*, the first day of *Sukkos* would have to be a Sunday. But *lo adu Rosh* prevents *Rosh Hashanah* — and hence *Sukkos* — from ever falling on Sunday. Therefore, in our fixed calendar, *Hoshana Rabba* can never fall on *Shabbos*. The calendar was deliberately arranged to prevent this from ever occurring, which means the theoretical question of doing *aravah* on *Shabbos* never becomes practically relevant.

### Ravin's Testimony

A competing report complicates matters: *ki ata Ravin* — when Ravin came from *Eretz Yisrael* with those who made *yerida* from Israel to Bavel, they reported: *ikla* — it did happen. *Hoshana Rabba* did once fall on *Shabbos* — *v'lo idchi*, and they did not let it override. This is a genuine challenge. According to the *mishna*, it should have overridden. Why did they not do it?

This opens the next major *sugya*: whether the *aravah* ritual the *mishna* refers to is the same as what we practice today at all.

## Aravah Ritual Debate

### Altar vs. Marching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא קַשְׁיָא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאן לֵימָא לַן דַּעֲרָבָה בִּנְטִילָה? דִּלְמָא בִּזְקִיפָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** then it is **difficult;** why doesn't the mitzva of the willow branch override Shabbat on the seventh day today? **Rav Yosef said: Who will say to us** definitively **that** the mitzva of **the willow branch** is performed **by taking** it? **Perhaps it is** performed **by standing** the branches **upright** against the altar. Since there is no altar today, the mitzva does not override Shabbat.

Rav Yosef responds to the difficulty raised by Ravin's testimony. *Man leima lan d'aravah b'netilah* — who says that the *aravah* ritual the *mishna* says overrides *Shabbos* refers to holding the *aravah* and marching with it, as we do today in *Hoshana Rabba*? *Dilma b'zekifah* — perhaps it refers to the *zekifa*, the act of propping the branches against the sides of the *mizbe'ach*, as described in the *mishna* on 45a. If the ritual that was permitted on *Shabbos* was specifically the *mizbei'ach* ritual — sticking the *aravah* branches around the altar — and that ritual is not performed today at all since we have no *Beis Hamikdash*, then our current practice of marching with the *aravah* is simply a different, later custom. The absence of the *mizbei'ach* ritual today explains why it is not done on *Shabbos*: the thing the *mishna* permitted on *Shabbos* no longer exists.

### Abaaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: לוּלָב וַעֲרָבָה שִׁשָּׁה וְשִׁבְעָה. מַאי לָאו, כְּלוּלָב: מָה לוּלָב בִּנְטִילָה — אַף עֲרָבָה בִּנְטִילָה! מִידֵּי אִירְיָא? הָא כִּדְאִיתֵיהּ וְהָא כִּדְאִיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rav Yosef from the mishna, which states: The ***lulav*** is taken **and** the altar is encircled with **the willow branch** either **six or seven** days. **What, is it not** learned from the juxtaposition of these mitzvot in the mishna that the mitzva of the willow branch is **like** the mitzva of ***lulav*** in that **just as** the mitzva of ***lulav*** is performed **by taking** it, **so too,** the mitzva of the **willow branch** is performed **by taking** it and not by standing it upright? He answered him: **Are the cases** necessarily **comparable?** Perhaps **this** mitzva of *lulav* is **as it is,** by means of taking, **and this** mitzva of the willow branch is **as it is,** by means of standing it upright.

Abaaye objects. The *mishna* itself says *lulav v'aravah shishah v'shivah* — *lulav* and *aravah* six and seven days, in the same breath. The implication is that *aravah* and *lulav* are parallel: *mah lulav b'netilah af aravah b'netilah* — just as *lulav* involves holding and taking, so too *aravah* involves holding and taking. This undermines Rav Yosef's claim that the relevant *aravah* ritual was the *zekifah* rather than the *netilah*.

The *gemara* responds: *mi'iri, hachi issa v'hachi issa* — the two rituals are each governed by their own parameters. The *mishna*'s pairing of *lulav* and *aravah* for six and seven days does not necessarily equate their form; each could refer to its own distinct mode of observance.

### Explicit Marching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: בְּכׇל יוֹם מַקִּיפִין אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ פַּעַם אַחַת, וְאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שֶׁבַע פְּעָמִים. מַאי לָאו, בַּעֲרָבָה? לָא, בְּלוּלָב. וְהָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: בַּעֲרָבָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הוּא אָמַר לָךְ בַּעֲרָבָה, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא בְּלוּלָב. אִתְּמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: בְּלוּלָב. רַב שְׁמוּאֵל [בַּר נָתָן] אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: בַּעֲרָבָה. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: בַּעֲרָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rav Yosef from a mishna: **On every day** the people **circle the altar one time, and** on **that day,** the seventh day of the willow branch, they circle it **seven times. What, is** the mishna **not** referring to circling the altar **with the willow branch** in hand? He answered him: **No,** it is referring to circling the altar **with a *lulav*.** Abaye objects: **But didn't Rav Naḥman say** that **Rabba bar Avuh said:** They would circle the altar **with the willow branch?** Rav Yosef **said to him: He said to you with the willow branch;** however, my authority is no less than his, as we are both *amora'im*, **and I say** that they circle the altar **with a *lulav*. It was stated** that this was the subject of dispute between other *amora'im* as well. **Rabbi Elazar says:** They circle the altar **with a *lulav*. Rav Shmuel bar Natan said** that **Rabbi Ḥanina said:** They circle the altar **with the willow branch. And likewise, Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said:** They would circle the altar **with the willow branch.**

Abaaye presses again with a stronger proof. The *mishna* explicitly states: *bechol yom makkifin es ha-mizbe'ach paam achas v'oso hayom sheva pe'amim* — every day they march around the *mizbe'ach* once, and on that day seven times. This explicitly describes a marching ritual, and the natural reading is that it was performed with the *aravah*. The *mishna* thus describes the *aravah* ritual as marching, not as propping branches — which directly contradicts Rav Yosef's distinction.

The *gemara* again deflects: *lo b'lulav* — perhaps the marching described in the *mishna* was performed with the *lulav*, not the *aravah*. The *aravah* ritual was the *zekifah* — the placement by the altar — while the marching around the *mizbe'ach* was a *lulav* ceremony. On this reading, the *mishna* describes two separate practices: the *aravah* was propped by the altar (a *kohen* ritual), and the marching was a *lulav* ritual. Rav Nachman is cited as holding that the marching was indeed an *aravah* ritual, while others disagree.

### Marching with Lulav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: בַּר אוּרְיָא, תָּא וְאֵימָא לָךְ מִלְּתָא מְעַלַּיְתָא דַּהֲוָה אָמַר אֲבוּךְ. הָא דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הַיּוֹם מַקִּיפִין אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ פַּעַם אַחַת, וְאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם מַקִּיפִין אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ שֶׁבַע פְּעָמִים, הָכִי אֲמַר אֲבוּךְ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בְּלוּלָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rabba bar bar Ḥana: Son of Torah [*bar urya*], come and I will tell you an outstanding statement that your father would say.** With regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna: On **every day** the people **circle the altar one time, and on that day,** the seventh day of the willow branch, **they circle the altar seven times; this** is what **your father said in the name of Rabbi Elazar:** They circle the altar **with a *lulav*.**

Rava concludes the discussion by citing a teaching in the name of his interlocutor's father — Rabbi Elazar. *Hachi amar abuch mishme d'Rabbi Elazar b'lulav* — your father used to say in the name of Rabbi Elazar that the marching ritual was performed with the *lulav*, not the *aravah*.

### Altar Ritual Missing

The upshot of this extended debate is that the *gemara* is inclined toward the view that the marching around the *mizbe'ach* was a *lulav* ritual, not an *aravah* ritual. The *aravah* ritual that was permitted on *Shabbos* — the one the *mishna* says could override the carrying concern — was specifically the *zekifah*, the propping of branches at the sides of the altar. That ritual is performed only by *kohanim*, only in the *Beis Hamikdash*, and it no longer exists. Our current *Hoshana Rabba* practice of marching with the *aravah* is a later custom that does not carry the same status. That is why, even according to Ravin's report that *Hoshana Rabba* once fell on *Shabbos*, it was not performed — the ritual that the *mishna* had permitted on *Shabbos* was not the ritual they were doing. And in our day, between the fixed calendar ensuring *Hoshana Rabba* never falls on *Shabbos* and the absence of the *mizbei'ach* ritual entirely, the question becomes purely theoretical.
