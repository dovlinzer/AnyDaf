# Kiddushin 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kutim Marriage Prohibiti…

### Three Tradition Versions

The sugya opens on 76b still working to understand the *braisa* of Rabbi Eliezer — or, more precisely, the baraita associated with his school — and the Gemara introduces another possibility through a chain of transmission: *ki ata Ravin amar Rabbi Chiya bar Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan*, with two alternative versions of the chain (*ve'amri lah amar Rabbi Abba bar Zavda amar Rabbi Chanina*; *ve'amri lah amar Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*). Three possible chains of transmission for one tradition. The *davar* that the tradition actually addresses is itself a subject of *machlokes Rishonim*: whether it concerns the *issur* for *Kutim* to marry Jews, or specifically *Kuti beKutit* — a *Kuti* marrying another *Kuti*. The Ritva brings both possibilities and explains how all three opinions might be read as bearing on *Kuti beKutit*, which is the *braisa* originally under discussion. The explanation he ultimately accepts — and the more likely reading — is that the first two opinions concern *Kuti beYisrael*, while only the third opinion addresses *Kuti beKutit*.

### R. Yishmael: Invalid Con…

The first opinion in this tradition is that of Rabbi Yishmael: *Kutim gerei arayos hein* — the Samaritans are invalid converts, their conversion did not take. The Ramban has much to say about this, though a full treatment belongs in *Yevamos*, where we hold that *gerei arayos* are in fact valid *geirim*. Why *gerei arayos* appears here as a defective category is a question the Ramban wrestles with at length.

### Disqualified Priests

The tradition also addresses *ha-Kohanim she-nitme'u bahen* — the *Kohanim* who lived and became intermingled among the Samaritans. According to Rabbi Yishmael, *Kohanim psulim hayu*, they were disqualified priests, as derived from the verse *vayaasu lahem miktzosam Kohanei bamos* (they made for themselves priests of the high places from among their number). Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan explains the *drasha*: *miktzosam* is read as a play on *kotz* (thorn), yielding *min ha-kotzim sheba'am* — the corrupt elements of the nation. *Umeshum hachi paslinhu* — that is why they are *pasul*. This was not the only context in which priests maintained *bamot*; Sefer Melachim records such practices widely. But according to *Chazal*, doing so in an official capacity was already prohibited at that point.

### R. Akiva: Valid Converts

Rabbi Akiva represents the second opinion: *Kutim gerei emes hein* — the Samaritans actually underwent a valid conversion. And *ha-Kohanim she-nitme'u bahen Kohanim kesherim hayu* — the *Kohanim* who dwelt among them were good, valid *Kohanim*. The same verse (*vayaasu lahem miktzosam Kohanei bamos*) is cited, but Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan reads it differently: *min ha-bechirim sheba'am* — from the finest of the nation, not the thorns. So *miktzosam* is read as *miktzas* — a portion, the choice ones. The *bamot* themselves did not *posul* the *Kohanim* in either reading; the question is only how the verse describes them.

Yet if the Samaritans are valid converts and the *Kohanim* among them were fit, *ve'ela mipnei mah asrum* — why are they forbidden in marriage? The answer: *mipnei she-hayu meyabmim es ha-arusos u-fotrim es ha-nesuos* — because they would perform *yibum* with a woman who had only been *arusah* (betrothed) and release from *yibum* a woman who had been fully married through *nisuin* — the opposite of normative halakha.

Rashi already notes at the top of 76a: *u-fotrim es ha-nesuos min ha-chalitza*, and observes that *u-le-Rabbi Akiva bnei chayavei lavin mamzeirim heim* — according to Rabbi Akiva's well-known *shita* that a forbidden union arising from an *issur lav* (as distinct from an *issur kares*) produces a *mamzer*, this aberrant *yibum* practice generates *mamzeirim*.

### Kuti Yibum Practices

The *drasha* the *Kutim* used to arrive at this unusual practice: they derived it from the *pasuk* in the Torah — *ki yeshvu achim yachdav u-mes echad mehem u-ven ein lo, lo sihiyeh eshes ha-mes ha-chutza l'ish zar, yevamah yavo aleha* (Devarim 25:5). The *Kutim* focused on the phrase *lo sihiyeh eshes ha-mes ha-chutza l'ish zar* — she who sat outside (i.e., who had not yet entered the marital home) should not go to a stranger: *heicha de-nafka chutzah hi, lo sihiyeh l'ish zar* — she who went out, who was only *arusah*, requires *yibum*. But *aval hacha de-lo yatva chutzah* — the woman who did not sit outside, who was fully married, *tihyeh l'ish zar* — she may go to another man, she needs no *yibum*. This is the inverse of what we hold. And it is halakhically problematic, particularly given Rabbi Akiva's position that relations in violation of a *lav* produce *mamzerim*, which is what grounds the *pesul* in this case.

### Third Opinion: Halakhic…

*Ve-yesh omrim* — the third opinion in these three traditions — gives a different explanation: *lefi she-ein beki'in be-dikdukei mitzvos* — that the Samaritans are not careful about the details of the commandments. This formulation initially strikes one as unusually broad; after all, many people are not expert in the details of every *mitzvah* without being thereby considered *pesulim*. The specific content of what they are not *baki* in will require further clarification.

### R. Elazar & Matzah Proof

The Gemara asks: who is this anonymous *yesh omrim*? *Amar Rav Iddi bar Avin — ve-Rabbi Elazar hi*. The text before us actually reads *Rabbi Eliezer*, which is the reading preserved through the Maharshal. The Rashash — Rabbi Shmuel Strashun — emends the text to *Rabbi Elazar*, which makes better sense in this *sugya*; the Oz VeHadar edition notes this variant on the margin.

The proof: *de-tanya, matzat Kutim muteret, ve-adam yotzei bah yedei chovato be-Pesach* — a *braisa* teaches that *matzah* made by a Samaritan is permitted on *Pesach* and one fulfills one's obligation with it, even though other items may be permitted on *Pesach* without enabling *yetzias yedei chovah*. *Ve-Rabbi Elazar oser* — Rabbi Elazar forbids it. The *braisa* explains his reasoning: *lefi she-ein beki'in be-dikdukei mitzvos*. This is the same formulation as the *yesh omrim*, identifying Rabbi Elazar as its author. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel then adds: *kol mitzvah she-hecheziku bah Kutim harbeh medakdekim bah yoter mi-Yisrael* — any commandment they took upon themselves, they observed with greater care than Jews did. A striking observation, and arguably true of certain kinds of religious dedication even today.

The Gemara then asks: *ve-la-hacha, mai ein beki'in?* What specifically are the *Kutim* not expert in that creates a *pesul* for *Kuti be-Kutit*? Knowing or not knowing the laws of *matzah* says nothing about their *yichus*. The answer: *lefi she-ein beki'in be-torat kiddushin ve-geirushin* — they do not know the laws of marriage and divorce properly. They may think someone was properly divorced when no valid *get* was given, or that a marriage did not require a *get*, producing *safek mamzerut* in subsequent generations. Rabbi Elazar already holds that *safek be-safek* — multiple layers of doubt — is a serious problem, which explains why he would rule that even *Kuti be-Kuti* is prohibited.

One might wonder why a tradition about *dikdukei mitzvos* refers only to the specific domain of *ishus*. It would perhaps have been more natural for the tradition to say *she-ein yodim tiv gittin ve-kiddushin*. But the text transmits what it transmits — this is clearly a widely-circulated tradition, as evidenced by its three separate chains of transmission — and the Gemara explains it as applying specifically to the realm of *ishus*.

### Kiddushin & Gittin Issues

In summary, the three opinions in the tradition concerning the *Kutim* reflect three distinct roots of the *pesul*: according to Rabbi Yishmael, they are *gerei arayos* and their conversion was never valid; according to Rabbi Akiva, they are valid converts but their aberrant *yibum* practices generate *mamzerim* under his *shita*; and according to the *yesh omrim* identified as Rabbi Elazar, they lack mastery of the laws of *kiddushin ve-geirushin*, producing *safek mamzerim* across generations.

## Mamzer & Slave Causes

### Mamzer Admixture

Beyond the three opinions in the tradition, the Gemara presents two additional *Amoraic* explanations for the *Kutim*'s disqualified status. *Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha*: *mamzer mei-achoso u-mamzer mei-eishes ish nit'arvu bahen* — a *mamzer* born from incest and a *mamzer* born from adultery became mixed into the Samaritan community, creating *mamzer* contamination throughout.

### Why Both Mamzer Types

The Gemara asks: *mai kamashma lan* — what is this *Amora* teaching us? Perhaps he intends to establish that *mamzerut* arises from *chayavei krisos* (rather than from *chayavei lavin* or *misas beis din*). If so, *lama li tarti* — why mention both? Why both the product of incest (*achoso*) and the product of adultery (*eishes ish*)? One such case would suffice to establish the principle. The answer: *ma'aseh she-hayah kach hayah* — this is historical fact. Both actually occurred. A product of incest and a product of adultery each independently became part of the Samaritan community, and therefore both are stated. History is not reshaped for the sake of logical economy.

### Slave Admixture

*Ve-Rava amar*: a different explanation — *eved ve-shifchah nit'arvu bahen* — a male slave and a female slave became intermingled with the Samaritan community. *Issura mishum mai? Mishum shifchah* — the core halakhic problem is the *shifchah*: a free person who has a child with a female slave produces offspring who appear free but are not, generating *mamzerut*-type complications downstream. *Lama li tarti* — again, why mention both the *eved* and the *shifchah* if only the *shifchah* creates the *issur*? *Ma'aseh she-hayah kach hayah* — same answer. Both events actually occurred in this community. Historical fact stands as reported.

## Bedika for Priestly Marr…

### Bedika Rationale

With the Samaritan *sugya* concluded, the Gemara introduces a new *Mishnah* dealing with *bedika* — genealogical investigation — which is no longer practiced in the form described here. We do have *chezkat kashrut* as a baseline, and the *shitas chachamim* that emerges in the *sugya* holds that everyone stands in that presumption. The rationale for the *bedika* requirement as described in the *Mishnah* is cited in the Ritva's name from the Yerushalmi, brought by the Ramban: *zo knas she-kansu* — this is an enactment imposed as a kind of *knas*, a penalty, *kedei she-yehei adam davek be-shivto u-ve-mishpachto* — so that a person would cleave to his tribe and to his family. The Ritva explains: *be-din hu she-lo yehu tzrichin bedika klal* — logically, no one should need to investigate *yichus* issues at all, neither *psulei kehunah* nor *psulei mamzerut*. But *Chazal* enacted the requirement to encourage closeness to one's defined family and tribal community.

Tosafot disagree with this framing. For the Yerushalmi's explanation to work, the *Mishnah* must address both *psulei kehunah* and *psulei kahal* (i.e., *mamzerut*) — which is how the Ramban and his students understood it. Tosafot maintain that the *Mishnah* concerns only *psulei kehunah*, and they explain the entire *sugya* along different lines, as we shall see.

### Presumption & Investigat…

The baseline presumption underlying all of this is *chezkat kashrut* — everyone stands in the presumption of fitness. The Rambam rules accordingly in *Issurei Biah* 19: *kol mishpachot be-chezkat kashrut hen omdot, mutar li-sa mehem le-chatchila* — one may marry from any family without prior investigation. The requirement to investigate is triggered only when a *ir'ur* — a challenge — has been raised. Specifically, the Rambam rules based on the Yerushalmi that if two witnesses testify that a *mamzer* or *chalal* or someone with *avdut* status has become intermixed in a particular family (*mishpacha kotzim*), that family becomes suspect and one may not marry from it until completing the *bedika* of the four mothers, which is eight ancestors.

### Four Mothers Requirement

The *Mishnah* states: *ha-nose isha Kehenet tzarich livdok achareha arba imahot she-hein shemonah* — one who marries a *Kohenet* must investigate four mothers, which are eight. The structure of this requirement traces back through four distinct maternal-and-maternal-grandfather pairs. Beginning from the woman herself: her mother and her mother's mother (*ima ve-em ima*); her maternal grandfather's mother (*em avi ima* and *ima* of that line); her father's mother (*em aviha ve-ima*); and her paternal grandfather's mother (*em avi aviha ve-ima*). Four pairs — *arba she-hein shemonah*. The investigative burden this would impose is staggering by any modern standard; the disruption of European Jewish record-keeping in the twentieth century alone would render such investigation impossible for virtually everyone.

### Leviya & Yisraelit

The *Mishnah* continues: *Leviyah ve-Yisraelit mosifin aleihen od achat* — for a *Leviyah* or an ordinary Israelite woman, one adds yet another unit of investigation beyond the baseline. The *Mishnah* has two textual traditions: *Leviyah ve-Yisraelit* (as in Rashi and Tosafot) and *Leviyim ve-Yisraelim* (as in the Sephardic texts), and this variant drives the underlying dispute about what the *Mishnah* addresses. On the Tosafot reading (*Leviyah ve-Yisraelit*), the added investigation reflects the fact that non-*Kohen* families are less careful about genealogical record-keeping that bears on *kehunah* fitness; a *Kohen* marrying a *Leviyah* or *Yisraelit* must therefore check further to ensure his children will not be *psulim le-kehunah*. On the Sephardic reading (*Leviyim ve-Yisraelim*), the *Mishnah* extends to *psulei kahal* as well — *mamzerut* itself — and the added requirement reflects the historically lesser care that non-priestly families took with their *yichus*: *de-chein sagi yoter be-levi'im ve-yisre'elim min ha-Kohanim, lefi she-ein medakdekim kol kach be-yichusan ve-einam medabkim be-mishpachoteihem*.

## Exceptions to Bedik (III)

### Temple Service Exemptions

The *Mishnah* then lists categories that exempt one from further investigation: *ein bodkin lo min ha-mizbe'ach u-lema'lah* — one need not investigate beyond a *Kohen* who performed *avodah* at the altar; *ve-lo min ha-duchan u-lema'lah* — nor beyond a *Levi* who served on the *duchan*; *ve-lo min ha-Sanhedrin u-lema'lah* — nor beyond a member of a *Sanhedrin*. The Gemara explains each: a *Kohen* who served at the *mizbe'ach* — *i lav de-badkuhu, lo havu maskei lei* — had the *Kohen* not been checked first, he would never have been permitted to ascend to begin with. The same logic applies to the *duchan*: *de-amar mar she-sham hayu yoshvim meyuchasei kehunah u-meyuchasei leviyah* — only those with proper *yichus* in *kehunah* and *leviyah* would have been seated there. The investigation was already performed; finding such an ancestor means the checking has already been done.

The case of *Sanhedrin* is more conceptually complex. The intuitive question is: why should serving on the *Sanhedrin* establish *yichus*? Judicial appointment is presumably based on intellectual capacity, not lineage. *Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — or more precisely, the principle transmitted through him — is explicated by *Rav Yosef*: *tani Rav Yosef, keshem she-beis din menukim be-tzedek, kach menukim mi-kol mum* — just as the court must be clean in justice, so too they must be clean of any blemish. This is not merely an observation; it establishes a halakhic standard that membership on the court itself constitutes evidence that the relevant *bedika* was already performed.

## Women Checked, Not M (II)

### Insult & Yichus

The Gemara raises a pointed question: *mai shena be-nashei badki u-mai shena be-gavrei lo badki* — why does the *Mishnah* speak only of investigating women and not men? The answer draws on a social observation. When women quarrel with one another (*dechi nashei de-mintzu bahadi hadadei*), they accuse each other of specific immoral acts — improper personal conduct. If such an accusation is true, the *yichus* problem it creates (in subsequent generations) would be known, because the conduct itself generates *kola* — public rumor. When men quarrel (*gavra de-nitzei bahadi hadadi*), they attack each other's *yichus* — your father, your mother were not who you think they were. If that accusation has any basis, it too generates *kola*. So in both cases, if there truly is a *yichus* problem, it will have been broadcast already. The asymmetry is that a woman's insult attacks her adversary's personal behavior, not her genealogy — the genealogical problem for the children remains concealed. A man's insult attacks genealogy directly, making the problem public. The Ran on the Rif explains this distinction clearly, and it is somewhat peculiar but it is what the Gemara says.

## Bedika Upon Challen (III)ge

### Chazaka Default

The implicit question remains: why is *bedika* not automatically required regardless of any challenge? The answer is the *chezkat kashrut* — all families stand in a presumption of fitness. *Kol mishpachot be-chezkat kashrut hen omdot*. This is the *shitas chachamim* that forms the halakhic baseline. The investigation is triggered not by the mere act of marriage but by a specific *ir'ur* — a challenge to status.

---

## Bedika Upon Challen (III)ge…

### Rav's Lenient View

The Gemara notes that women do not check men in any event. Beyond the *kola* argument, this is supported by Rav's ruling, cited as *amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: *lo huzharu kesherot le-hinasei li-psulim* — fit women were not warned against marrying men who are *psulim le-kehunah*. There is no *issur* for a *Yisraelit* to marry a *Kohen* who has a *pesul kehunah* in his background. If there is no prohibition, there is correspondingly no rabbinic obligation to investigate.

---

## Women Checked, Not M (II)

### No Obligation for Women

On Rav's view, a woman faces no *issur* in marrying a *Kohen* who may be *pasul le-kehunah*, and so the entire framework of investigation does not apply to her in examining a prospective husband. The *Mishnah*'s silence about women checking men is therefore explicable even without the *kola* argument: there is simply nothing to investigate for, because she incurs no prohibition. This is a significant leniency in Rav's position, though it is a difficult one to reconcile with the general spirit of the *knas* as the Ramban understood it — which is one reason the Ramban's approach generates its own difficulties.

## Bedika Variants

### Twelve-Generation Variant

The Gemara records a variant tradition: *Rav Adda bar Abba tani arba imot she-hein shtem esrei* — Rav Adda bar Abba has a version in which the four mothers yield not eight but twelve. On each node, one additional maternal ancestor is added: *al kol zu mosif lidok od em le-ma'alah*. Another version goes further: *tana matnitin tani arba imot she-hein shesh esrei* — four which are sixteen. The practical question of how one actually conducts such an investigation — what form of testimony is required, how one verifies a claim generation by generation — is not addressed here, and remains genuinely unclear. What is clear is that this is a rabbinic enactment, entirely *divrei chachamim*.

The Gemara works through how Rav Adda bar Abba's version relates to the *Mishnah*. His version (*arba she-hein shtem esrei*) fits neatly with the *Mishnah*'s language of *od achat* by the *Leviyah u-vat Yisrael* — one additional unit beyond the base of eight, which when added produces twelve. But the other variant (*arba she-hein shesh esrei*) appears to conflict with the *Mishnah*. The Gemara resolves this: *mai od achat? Zuga echad* — *od achat* does not mean one single ancestor but one additional *pair* — *ima ve-em ima* — yielding sixteen rather than twelve. Thus both versions can be harmonized with the *Mishnah*'s language if read appropriately.

### R. Meir vs. Sages

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: the entire *Mishnah* as we have it — with all its variants of the investigation requirement — represents the position of *Rabbi Meir*. *Ve-chachamim omrim: kol mishpachot be-chezkat kashrut hen omdot* — the Sages hold that all families stand in the presumption of fitness and no automatic investigation is required. The *halacha* follows the Sages.

## Bedika Upon Challen (III)ge…

### Investigation on Ir'ur

This creates an apparent difficulty: *Rav Chama bar Gurya amar Rav* states that the *Mishnah* — which we attributed to Rabbi Meir — actually describes the rule *ke-she-korei lo ir'ur*, when a challenge has been raised. But if that is what the *Mishnah* means, then even the Sages would agree that investigation is required once a challenge is raised; *ve-chachamim modeh le-Rabbi Meir* in that circumstance.

There are two ways to resolve the tension between the two statements of Rav. The first: they are simply mutually exclusive — *matni ha, lo matni ha* — one cannot hold both simultaneously. The second, preferred version: *amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav* in its fuller formulation is *zo divrei Rabbi Meir, ve-chachamim omrim kol mishpachot be-chezkat kashrut hen omdot*; and the separate statement of *Rav Chama bar Gurya amar Rav* establishes that *im koreh alav ir'ur, tzarich livdok* — even the Sages concede that once a challenge is raised, investigation is mandatory. On this reading, both statements of Rav cohere: the baseline is *chezkat kashrut* per the Sages, but *ir'ur* from two witnesses triggers the full *bedika* of four mothers (eight ancestors). The *ir'ur* in question is testimony from two people that a *mamzer*, *chalal*, or person of *avdut* status has become intermixed in the family. This is how the Rambam rules, and it reflects his application of *halacha ke-batrai*.

## Exceptions to Bedik (III)

### Court Purity Standard

Returning to the *Mishnah*'s exemptions: *ve-lo min ha-Sanhedrin u-lema'lah* — no investigation is required once one finds a *Sanhedrin* ancestor. The Gemara establishes the textual basis for this. *Mareimar* cites *Shir ha-Shirim* 4:7: *kulach yafah ra'ayati u-mum ein bach* — "You are entirely beautiful, my beloved, and there is no blemish in you" — read as referring to the *zekenim* of Israel: they are beautiful and bear no blemish. One might object that the verse refers to a physical blemish. Therefore *Rav Acha bar Yaakov* adds a second proof: the verse *ve-hityatzvu sham imach* (Bamidbar 11:16) — "and they shall stand there with you" — from which we derive *imach be-domin lach*: they must be *domeh le-Moshe* in being free of any *yichus* defect.

## Yichus in Public Lif (II)

### Domim Lecha Principle

The *drasha* of *domim lach* is a requirement that *Sanhedrin* members resemble Moshe — not in intellect alone, but in *yichus*. Rashi explains that this requirement applied to the *Sanhedrin she-be-ota sha'ah* specifically because the *shechina* rested upon the *zekenim* by virtue of the prophetic experience at that moment, and so the particular resonance of being *domeh le-Moshe* was contextually acute. The Gemara then brings *Rav Nachman's* verse: *ve-hakel me-alecha ve-nas'u itach* (from Yitro's advice in Shemot) — *ve-nas'u itach be-domin lach* — the requirement to resemble Moshe emanates even from Yitro's original counsel and is not limited to the singular prophetic moment. This returns the requirement to its ongoing, *le-dorot* application.

### Dayan Categories

The *Mishnah* then mentions *shotrei rabbim* — lower-level public officials or judges. The Gemara clarifies: this refers to *dayanim be-alma*, ordinary judges who are not the twenty-three of *Sanhedrin ketana*. The Gemara then asks: *lemeimra de-lo mokminan mi-psulim* — does this imply that we do not appoint *psulim* as *dayanim*? But a *braisa* states: *hakol kesherim ladun dinei mamonot, ve-ein hakol kesherim ladun dinei nefashot* — everyone is eligible to judge monetary cases; not everyone is eligible for capital cases. So *psulim* can be *dayanim* for *dinei mamonot* — how do we reconcile this with the *Mishnah*'s requirement of *yichus* for *shotrei rabbim*?

*Rav Yehuda* explains: *hakol* in that *braisa* comes to include even a *mamzer* — a *mamzer* can serve as a *dayan* for *dinei mamonot*. The apparent contradiction is resolved by *Abaye*: the *shotrei rabbim* referenced in the *Mishnah* refers specifically to judges in *Yerushalayim*, who had a special standard of *yichus*, not to all *dayanim be-mamonot* everywhere. *Ve-chein tani Rabbi Shimon bar Zevida be-kidushei d'bei Levi be-Yerushalayim* — a tradition from the academy of Levi confirms that *Yerushalayim* was extraordinarily careful about *yichus*, a standard beyond what the general rule would require.

---

## Exceptions to Bedik (III)a…

### Municipal Officials

The *Mishnah* extends the exemption to *gabai tzedaka* — charity collectors — and those who served in similar municipal roles. The Gemara asks why their past service establishes *yichus*. The answer: *keivan de-minatzei bahadi inshi* — since they are constantly fighting with people. Their role requires them to pressure people to give, even on *erev Shabbat*, and people resist and quarrel. And as we saw earlier, when men quarrel they attack each other's *yichus*. If the *gabai tzedaka* had any genealogical defect, *iplugi iplugi kala* — it would have come out in the course of those disputes.

### Public Dispute Revelation

It is important to appreciate that *gabai tzedaka* were not merely volunteers collecting donations. The Rambam rules, based on the Yerushalmi, that *tzedaka* was a genuine tax: one could have one's property seized for failure to give, and the *beis din* could impose lashes. The *gabai tzedaka* had real coercive authority and therefore generated real resentment — which is precisely why their disputes would reliably expose any hidden *yichus* problems. They are more analogous to tax collectors than to fundraisers, and the disputes they generated were correspondingly adversarial.

## Yichus in Public Lif (II)

### Mekrev Achecha Standard

A related episode: *ushpizchanie de-Rav Adda bar Ahava giyora hava* — the host of Rav Adda bar Ahava was a *ger*. *Ve-hava kemintzi ihu ve-Rav Bibi* — he and Rav Bibi came into dispute over which of them would hold the position of authority in the town (*shraruta de-mata*). They came before Rav Yosef, who ruled based on the verse *som tasim alecha melech mi-kerev achecha* (Devarim 17:15): *kol mesimot she-ata mesim, lo yehe ela mi-kerev achecha* — any public appointment must come from among one's people.

Rav Adda bar Abba responded: does this not apply even when *imo mi-Yisrael* — even if the *ger*'s mother is Jewish? The implication would be that having a Jewish mother counts as *mi-kerev achecha*. Rav Yosef disagreed: *imo mi-Yisrael, mi-kerev achecha karina bei* — yes, if his mother is Jewish, he is indeed called *mi-kerev achecha*. Accordingly, Rav Bibi — a *gavra rabba* — was assigned oversight of *milei de-shmaya*, the religious affairs of the community, and the *ger* was assigned *milei de-mata*, the civic matters. *Abaye* draws a separate lesson from the episode: one who hosts a *talmid chacham* should choose someone like Rav Adda bar Abba, who knows how to argue on behalf of his host's merit.

### Ger Restrictions

The Gemara then notes a regional contrast: *u-ve-ma'arava, afilu reish korei lo mokmei minaihu* — in the West, in Eretz Yisrael, they would not appoint a *ger* even to the position of *reish korei*, the official in charge of weights and measures in the market. *Be-Neharde'a afilu reish gargouta lo mokmei minaihu* — in Neharde'a, they would not appoint a *ger* even to oversee the town's irrigation works. Different communities applied the *mi-kerev achecha* principle with varying stringency, ranging from excluding *gerim* from all public authority to permitting them in purely civic or non-judicial roles.

## Rambam & Ger Problem

### Rambam's Requirement

The formal halakhic crystallization of the *yichus* requirement for *Sanhedrin* membership appears in the Rambam, *Sanhedrin* chapter 2, *halacha* 1. One might expect the Rambam to require only that *Sanhedrin* members be *chachamim u-nevonim*, wise and discerning — indeed he says this, and adds that they must be *muflagim be-chochmat ha-Torah*, of broad knowledge in *Torah*, *ba'alei de'a meruba*, possessing wide understanding, and even knowledgeable in adjacent fields: *refua, cheshbon, tekufot u-mazalot, itztagninut, ve-darchei ha-me'onenim ve-kosemim u-mechashefim ve-havlei avoda zara* — medicine, mathematics, astronomy, astrology, and the methods of sorcerers and idolaters, *kedei she-yihyu yodim ladun otam*. The *Kesef Mishneh* explains the astronomical requirement: the *Sanhedrin* must perform *ibur shana* and *kiddush ha-chodesh*, which require genuine astronomical competence. But the Rambam then adds: *ve-ein ma'amidin be-Sanhedrin... ela Kohanim, Levi'im, ve-Yisraelim ha-meyuchasim ha-ra'uyim le-hasi li-kehunah* — one may only appoint to the *Sanhedrin* those of proper *yichus*, specifically those whose daughters are fit to marry *Kohanim*. The verse cited is *ve-hityatzvu sham imach*, yielding *domim lach be-chochma u-ve-yira u-ve-yachas*. The source for *yira* is not apparent from this *sugya* itself.

### Gerim in Sanhedrin

This formulation creates an immediate and serious historical difficulty. In *Pirkei Avot*, Shemaya and Avtalyon appear as links in the chain of transmission from Sinai. The Gemara in *Sanhedrin* identifies them as *gerim*. Rabbi Akiva likewise is described elsewhere as descending from a *ger* family, and he lacked *zechut avot* — ancestral merit — in a way that implies his family did not have generations of Jewish lineage. Yet Shemaya and Avtalyon manifestly served on and led the *Sanhedrin*. How can the Rambam's requirement be reconciled with this historical fact?

### Two Sanhedrin Functions

The *Tumim* — Rabbi Yehonatan Eibeschutz in his commentary on *Choshen Mishpat*, *siman* 7, *sif katan* 1 — addresses this directly. His resolution is that the *Sanhedrin Gedolah* bears two distinct functions. The first encompasses the full range of judicial authority: *dinei nefashot*, judgment of a *Zaken Mamre*, appointments of kings, and all capital and constitutional matters. For this function, *gerim* are unfit — *pesulim* — and cannot participate. The second function is to serve as the *ikar Torah she-ba'al peh*, the institutional repository and authority for oral law. In this capacity, as the Rambam articulates in *Hilchot Mamrim* chapter 1, *halacha* 1: *Beis Din ha-Gadol she-bi-Yerushalayim heim ikar Torah she-ba'al peh ve-heim amudei ha-hora'ah, u-me-hem chok u-mishpat yotzei le-chol Yisrael* — they are the pillars of legal teaching for all of Israel. For this role, *gerim* are not only permitted but may be the most qualified, as was the case with Shemaya and Avtalyon. On the *Tumim*'s understanding, when the *Sanhedrin Gedolah* convened for capital proceedings, Shemaya and Avtalyon would step aside, replaced by others with the required *yichus*, while the bench was maintained at its full number through substitution — analogous to the modern practice of courts sitting with temporary or substitute judges.

### Soloveitchik's Two Roles

Rav Soloveitchik advances a structurally similar but conceptually more elegant distinction, as formulated and transmitted by Rav Aharon Lichtenstein. The *Sanhedrin Gedolah* has two aspects: the *hilchati-torani* function and the *mishpati-tekesi* function. The *hilchati-torani* function — determining what the Torah requires, ruling on novel halakhic questions, preserving and transmitting the *mesorah* — is a matter of pure learning and *yiras Shamayim*. For this function, *yichus* is irrelevant. The principle that *afilu mamzer talmid chacham gadol mi-Kohen Gadol am ha-aretz* applies here without qualification. The *mishpati-tekesi* function — appointing a king, establishing courts throughout the land, judging entire tribes, trying a *navi sheker*, as enumerated in *Sanhedrin* chapter 5, *halacha* 1 — is a different matter entirely. In this role, the *Sanhedrin Gedolah* functions as *nitzigei Klal Yisrael*, the representative body of the Jewish people. It is this representative capacity that demands proper *yichus*: one who represents *Klal Yisrael* must be *mi-kerev achecha* in the fullest sense. Shemaya and Avtalyon were the supreme halakhic authorities of their generation — the *gedolim she-be-dor* — for the *hilchati-torani* aspect. For the *mishpati-tekesi* aspect, their *ger* status made them unfit, and Rav Soloveitchik suggests that these two functions may not even require the same body: one could have a separate group of seventy-one constituted specifically for the formal representational-governmental role, distinct from those sitting as the halakhic council, both legitimately called *Sanhedrin Gedolah* yet composed of entirely different people, each wearing, so to speak, a different hat.
