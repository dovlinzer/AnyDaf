# Yevamot 112 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Mishnah

### Opening Remarks

We begin today with the *mishnah* that opens the new *perek* of *cheresh*, found on 112b. Much of this *mishnah* has already appeared in pieces throughout the earlier *perakim*, so we move through it with some dispatch.

### Cheresh & Pika'chat

The *mishnah* opens: *cheresh shenasa pika'chat* — a deaf person who married a hearing woman — *upike'ach shenasa chereshet* — or a hearing person who married a deaf woman — *im ratza lehotzi yotzi ve'im ratza lekayem yekayem* — if he wishes to divorce he may do so, and if he wishes to maintain the marriage he may do so.

### Rabbinic Marriage

The underlying principle is that where one party to the marriage is a *cheresh* — a deaf-mute — that person is not considered a *bar da'at* in the halachic sense, and therefore there is no *kidushin* at the biblical level. The *kidushin* is purely *derabbanan*. But the same rabbinic framework that creates the marriage also authorizes its dissolution. *Keshem shekones biremizah kach hu motzi biremizah* — just as the marriage was contracted through gestures, so too the divorce may be effected through gestures. The rabbis who established the institution of rabbinic marriage likewise sanction the rabbinic divorce.

### Became Deaf

The *mishnah* then addresses a different scenario: *pike'ach shenasa pika'chat venistacharesha* — a hearing man who married a hearing woman, and she subsequently became deaf. In this case the *kidushin* was *de'oraita* from the outset, since both parties were fully competent at the time of marriage. Nevertheless, *im ratza yotzi ve'im ratza yekayem* — the husband may divorce her or maintain the marriage — because divorce is a unilateral act performed by the husband. So long as he retains his status as a *bar da'at*, he is capable of issuing a valid *get*, regardless of whether she has since lost hers.

### Woman Shotah

However, *nishtatit lo yotzi* — if she became a *shotah*, mentally incompetent, he may not divorce her. This is a rabbinic enactment designed to protect her from exploitation. A woman who cannot reason for herself and cannot protect herself from sexual abuse or predation must remain bound within a marriage that affords her some shelter and protection.

### Man Cheresh

The reverse situation is far more severe. *Nitcharesh hu o nishtateh* — if the husband became a *cheresh* or a *shoteh* after the marriage — *eino motzi olamit* — he can never divorce her. The *get* must be issued by the husband, and since he is no longer a *bar da'at*, he lacks the legal capacity to do so, however capable he may be in a purely practical sense.

## Asymmetry in Divorce

### R. Yochanan's Q

The *gemara* records the challenge posed by Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri: *mipnei mah ha'ishah shenitcharesha yotza veha'ish shenitcharesh eino motzi?* — Why is it that when she becomes deaf after a *de'oraita* marriage she can still be divorced, yet when he becomes deaf he can never divorce her? The asymmetry demands explanation.

### Agent vs Object

The response is crisp: *eino domeh ha'ish megeresh la'ishah mitgareshet* — the man who divorces is not comparable to the woman who is divorced. *Ha'ishah yotza lirtzonah veshela lirtzonah* — a woman is divorced whether she wills it or not; she is in the role of the recipient, the object of the act, not its agent. Since her own *da'at* is not required for the divorce to take effect, the fact that she has lost it is irrelevant. *Veha'ish eino motzi ela lirtzono* — the man, by contrast, divorces only through his own will and legal agency. He must be a *bar da'at* to do so. Once he has lost that status, the avenue of divorce is permanently closed.

### Ben Gudgada

Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada then offers testimony: *he'id Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada al hachereshet shehishi'ah aviha sheyotza beget* — he testified concerning a deaf-mute woman whose father had contracted her marriage while she was a minor. Because the father effected the *kidushin*, and the father was a fully competent agent, the marriage had *de'oraita* standing — even though the woman herself was a *chereshet*. And yet the *gemara* affirms that she may be divorced through a *get*. Here again, since she is the recipient rather than the agent of the divorce, her status as a *chereshet* presents no obstacle.

### Tosafot Evidence

*Tosafot* observes something striking about the phrasing *amru lo af zu kayotza bah* — "they said to him, this case is likewise." *Tosafot* reads this as indicating that the Sages actually summoned Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada to the *beit midrash* — *hevi'u levet hamidrash lehavi re'ayah ledivreihem* — he was not ordinarily among those present, but they brought him specifically to use his testimony as evidence for their position. It is a vivid picture of the Sages marshalling a witness to clinch an argument.

## Two Brothers Cases

### Setup

The *mishnah* now works through the many permutations of two brothers married to two sisters, where deafness is distributed variously among the parties. Because the *mishnah* introduces the cases systematically, we follow its structure. First: *shnei achim chereshim nesu'im lishtei achayot* — two deaf brothers married to two sisters, whether both sisters are hearing (*pikchot*), both deaf (*cherashot*), or one of each. Similarly, where both sisters are deaf and the brothers vary, or where one brother is deaf and one is hearing.

### Exemption

In all of these configurations — as long as both brothers are involved and the sisters are sisters — *harei elu peturot min hachalitzah umin hayibum*: the surviving widow is exempt from both *chalitzah* and *yibum*. This is because she is in a relationship of *achot isha* — wife's sister — with the surviving brother, and that prohibition operates here. Moreover, since all the relevant marriages are rabbinic, the entire structure operates on a single plane. If the women were unrelated (*nochriyot*), then *yibum* would be possible, and even a subsequent *get* — though *chalitzah* remains unavailable whenever one of the parties to that ceremony is a *cheresh*.

### One Each

We turn now to the case of *shnei achim echad cheresh ve'echad pikeach* — one deaf brother, one hearing brother — married to two sisters, both of whom are hearing.

### Deaf Dies

*Met cheresh ba'al hapikechat* — the deaf brother, who was married to the hearing woman, dies. The hearing brother is now faced with his wife's sister, a relationship of *achot isha*. But his own marriage is a *de'oraita kidushin* — he and his wife are both fully competent. So *tetzei mishum achot isha* — she walks away on the basis of the biblical prohibition of wife's sister. Straightforward.

### Hearing Dies

*Met pikeach ba'al hapikechat* — now the hearing brother dies. The surviving deaf brother finds himself in the position of owing *yibum* to his brother's widow. But that woman is also his own wife's sister, creating the problem of *achot isha*. However, his own marriage was only *derabbanan*. So at the *de'oraita* level, she is not truly his wife's sister, but rabbinically she is. He can neither perform *yibum* — that would be relations with his wife's sister — nor dismiss her through *chalitzah*, since he is a *cheresh* and *chalitzah* requires full competence from both parties.

### Stuck

The result is expressed starkly: *motzi et ishto beget ve'eshet achiv asurah lo le'olam* — he must divorce his own wife (since he cannot maintain that marriage while a *zikah* — levirate bond — connects him to her sister), and his brother's wife is forbidden to him forever. He is caught: he must relinquish his own wife, yet he cannot release or acquire his sister-in-law. This is one of the most severe results the *mishnah* presents.

## Two Hearing Brothers

### Setup

We now consider *shnei achim pikchim nesu'im lishtei achayot* — two hearing brothers married to two sisters, but here the sisters differ: one is a *chereshet*, one is a *pikachat*. *Met pikeach ba'al hachereshet* — the brother married to the deaf sister dies.

### Sister-in-Law

The surviving brother is married to the hearing sister. He now faces his wife's sister as his potential *yevamah*. Since his own marriage is fully *de'oraita*, the prohibition of *achot isha* applies with full biblical force — *tetzei mishum achot isha*. The widow walks away without *chalitzah* or *yibum*.

### Hearing Dies

*Met pikeach ba'al hapikechat* — the other scenario: the brother married to the hearing sister dies. The surviving brother is married to the deaf woman — a rabbinic marriage. He now has a *mitzvah* of *yibum* with the hearing sister-in-law. His own marriage, being only *derabbanan*, does not create a biblical *achot isha* prohibition.

### Chalitzah

He therefore divorces his own wife — he cannot remain with her given the *zikah* — and then has recourse to *chalitzah* with the sister-in-law. Since he is hearing, *chalitzah* is available to him. *Ve'eshet achiv bechalitzah* — he performs *chalitzah* and releases her properly.

## Mixed Permutations

### All Mixed

The final configuration involving two sisters: *shnei achim echad cheresh ve'echad pikeach* married to *shtei achayot achat chereshet ve'achat pikachat*, with the deaf brother married to the deaf woman and the hearing brother to the hearing woman.

### Deaf Dies

*Met cheresh b'chereshet* — the deaf man married to the deaf woman dies. What does the hearing brother do? He is biblically married to his wife; the woman now falling into *zikah* is his wife's sister. *Tetzei mishum achot isha* — she walks away. Clean case.

### Hearing Dies

*Met pikeach b'pikachat* — the hearing man married to the hearing woman dies. Now the deaf man is faced with *yibum* obligations toward his brother's widow (a *de'oraita* obligation, since the deceased was biblically married). He must divorce his own wife — since maintaining that marriage while his brother's wife is bound to him by *zikah* would constitute *achot zekukato*. Yet he cannot perform *chalitzah* because he is a *cheresh*, and he cannot perform *yibum* because it would constitute relations with his ex-wife's sister. *Ve'eshet achiv asurah le'olam* — his brother's wife is forever forbidden to him.

## Unrelated Women

### Setup

We now move to cases of two brothers married to two *nochriyot* — unrelated women — so the problem of *achot isha* does not arise. One brother is a *cheresh*, one is a *pikeach*; both women are hearing.

### Deaf Dies

*Met cheresh b'pikachat* — the deaf brother dies. His widow enters *zikah* with the hearing brother. This is a rabbinic obligation of *yibum* — since the deceased's *kidushin* was only *derabbanan*. Since the women are unrelated, the hearing brother may do either *chalitzah* or *yibum* as he chooses.

### Hearing Dies

*Met pikeach b'pikachat* — the hearing brother dies. Now it is the deaf brother who faces *yibum* with his brother's widow. This is a *de'oraita* obligation of *yibum* — the deceased was biblically married. The women are unrelated, so there is no *achot isha* complication. However, the *cheresh* cannot perform *chalitzah*. His only avenue is *yibum*.

### Permanent Bond

Here the *mishnah* delivers a conclusion that is both precise and jarring: *kones ve'eino motzi le'olam* — he performs *yibum*, and can never divorce her afterward. This is because *yibum* is a *de'oraita* act — it carries over the biblical *kidushin* from the deceased brother — and since the *cheresh* is not a *bar da'at*, he cannot issue a valid *get*. The contrast with the opening case of the *mishnah* is intentional and essential. When a *cheresh* initiates a marriage, it is a rabbinic *kidushin* that can be dissolved rabbinically. But when a *cheresh* performs *yibum*, the bond he acquires is *de'oraita*, and he is thereafter permanently unable to sever it. The woman is truly bound.

This distinction — raised by a student's question — is the central halachic point threading through all these permutations: rabbinic marriage, rabbinic divorce; biblical *yibum*, no divorce possible. And for the woman who has no access to *chalitzah* and whose husband-by-*yibum* cannot write a *get*, the situation is one of permanent inescapable attachment.

### Hearing Brothers

The final set of cases: *shnei achim pikchim* — two hearing brothers — married to two unrelated women, one hearing and one a *chereshet*. *Met pikeach b'chereshet* — the brother married to the deaf woman dies. The surviving hearing brother faces *yibum* with the *chereshet*. He cannot perform *chalitzah* because she is deaf and *chalitzah* requires full participation from both parties. So *kones* — he does *yibum* — and *ve'im ratza lehotzi yotzi* — he may then give her a *get* if he wishes, since he is a hearing man and the *de'oraita* rule permits a hearing husband to divorce even a deaf wife.

*Met pikeach b'pikachat* — the other scenario: the brother married to the hearing woman dies. Now the surviving hearing brother, who is married to the deaf woman, faces *yibum* with the hearing sister-in-law. Since she is unrelated to his own wife, and since he is fully competent, he has all options: *o choletz o meyabem* — either *chalitzah* or *yibum*.

The final permutation with one hearing and one deaf brother and one of each type of woman confirms the same logic. When the *cheresh* married to the *chereshet* dies and the hearing man faces *yibum* with the *chereshet*: *kones, ve'im ratza lehotzi yotzi* — does *yibum*, can subsequently divorce. When the hearing man married to the hearing woman dies and the *cheresh* faces *yibum* with the *chereshet*: *kones ve'eino motzi le'olam* — does *yibum*, can never divorce.

The entire architecture of these cases ultimately rests on two pillars established at the *mishnah*'s outset: a woman need not be a *bat da'at* for a *get* to be valid; and a *cheresh* who performs *yibum* acquires a *de'oraita* bond he can never dissolve.

## Ketubah Institutions

### Rami's Question

The *gemara* now transitions to a beautiful expository passage, initiated by Rami bar Chama: *mah shena cheresh v'chereshet d'tikinu lehu rabanan nisu'in, u'mah shena shoteh v'shotah d'lo tikinu lehu rabanan nisu'in?* What is the difference between a *cheresh* and a *chereshet*, for whom the Rabbis did institute a framework of rabbinic *nisu'in*, and a *shoteh* and *shotah*, for whom they did not? The *gemara* knows this distinction is real because a *baraita* teaches explicitly that if a *shoteh* or a minor married women and those women died childless, the widows are exempt from *chalitzah* and *yibum* entirely — meaning even rabbinic *nisu'in* was never established for them.

### Institution Stability

The *gemara* answers: *cheresh v'chereshet dekayma takeinta derabanan, tikinu lehu rabanan nisu'in* — for a deaf man or woman, the rabbinic institution will hold; therefore they established it. *Shoteh v'shotah d'lo kayma takeinta derabanan* — for someone mentally incompetent, the institution will not hold, because *ein adam dar im nachash bikfifah* — a person cannot live with a snake under the same roof. Life with a mentally unstable person is so fraught that any institution of marriage built upon it would be inherently fragile and unworkable. The rabbis therefore declined to create the institution at all.

### Minors

But this raises the obvious question: *u'mai shna katan d'lo tikinu rabanan nisuin?* Why was no rabbinic marriage instituted for a minor boy? Unlike a *shoteh*, a minor boy presents no danger of an unstable household — he will grow up. The *gemara* responds: *cheresh d'lo ati lichlal nisuin, tikinu rabanan nisuin* — for a *cheresh*, who will never on his own arrive at the capacity for biblical marriage, the Rabbis created a rabbinic substitute, lest he be entirely excluded from married life. Rashi adds that the concern is also *pru u'rvu* — enabling him to fulfill the spirit of procreation, even if not as a formal *mitzvah* obligation given his status. *Katan d'ati lichlal nisuin, lo tikinu rabanan nisuin* — a minor boy who will grow up and become capable of *de'oraita* marriage needs no rabbinic institution. The answer is simple: wait. There is no urgency, no institutional gap to fill.

### Ketubah Contrast

The *gemara* then asks: *u'mai shna ktana d'asya lichlal nisuin, ve'takinu rabanan nisuin?* A minor girl will also eventually mature into the capacity for biblical marriage — so why did the Rabbis establish rabbinic *kidushin* for her? The answer: *hasam shelo yinhagu bah minhag hefker* — so that she not be treated as ownerless, that is, to protect a girl without a father from sexual exploitation. Without a husband, such a girl is dangerously vulnerable. Establishing a marital framework — even a rabbinic one — provides her a measure of protection.

The *gemara* then poses a further contrast: *u'mai shna ktana d'memaenes, u'mai shna chereshet d'lo memaana?* Why does a *ktana* have the right of *miun* — the option to refuse and nullify the marriage simply by declaring she does not want it — while a *chereshet* does not? The answer: *de'im ken mimni ve'lo nasbi lah* — because if a *chereshet* could exercise *miun* indefinitely throughout the marriage, no one would want to marry her. The uncertainty would be too great. For a *ktana*, by contrast, the window of *miun* is time-limited; once she reaches maturity the marriage becomes fully binding and the right of *miun* lapses. That temporal limit makes the institution workable.

### Terumah Gezeirah

The *gemara* continues: *u'mai shna ktana d'achla b'terumah, u'mai shna chereshet d'lo achla b'terumah?* Why may a minor girl married to a *kohen* eat *terumah*, while a *chereshet* married to a *kohen* may not? The *gemara* establishes this from the testimony of Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada, who testified about a *chereshet* whose father had married her off (*al hachereshet shehishi'ah aviha*) — that she may be divorced with a *get* — and about a minor girl married to a *kohen* that she eats *terumah*. The implicit contrast is that a *chereshet* does not.

The *gemara* explains the asymmetry through *gezeirah*: *gezeirah shema yashi cheresh b'chereshet* — we do not permit the *chereshet* married to a hearing *kohen* to eat *terumah* because of concern that people will then allow the same when she is married to a deaf *kohen*. But if she were married to a *cheresh kohen*, the marriage would be only rabbinic — a valid concern. One might counter: even so, let her eat *terumah*, since it is only a rabbinic prohibition and she is not personally obligated in *mitzvot*; just as we say *katan ochel neveilot hi* — a minor eating something forbidden is not subject to the same strictures as an adult. But the *gemara* pushes further.

### Reverse Gezeirah

The deeper concern is *gezeirah shema yashi cheresh b'pikachat* — we fear that if we permit a *chereshet* married to a hearing *kohen* to eat *terumah*, it will lead people to permit the reverse: a hearing woman married to a deaf *kohen* eating *terumah*. And that woman is an adult obligated in *mitzvot*, eating food that is forbidden to her. Even if one argues that at least the rabbinic *terumah* should be permissible — since we waived certain rabbinic concerns in the case of the *ktana* — the *gemara* says no: *gezeirah dilma ati le'meichal b'terumah d'oraisa* — we fear the slide from rabbinic *terumah* to biblical *terumah*. Once such a woman is in the habit of eating *terumah*, it will easily extend to eating produce to which the *de'oraita* prohibition applies, and she, as a fully obligated adult, would be committing a biblical violation.

The reason the same *gezeirah* does not apply to the *ktana* is subtle. Rashi explains: in the case of the *cheresh*, both the marriage of a *chereshet* to a hearing man and the reverse — a hearing woman to a deaf *kohen* — involve the same rabbinic *kidushin*; the cases are structurally parallel and people will naturally move between them. But with minors, no rabbinic *kidushin* exists for a minor *boy* — only for a minor *girl*. Since the institution is not symmetrical, the slide to the mirror-image case does not occur. (The only exception would be a minor boy who performs *yibum*, but that is an entirely different mechanism.)

## Ketubah & Agency

### No Ketubah

A *baraita* now establishes: *cheresh v'shoteh she'nas'u pikchot* — a *cheresh* or a *shoteh* who married hearing women — *ein lahem aleihem klum* — have no *ketubah* obligations at all toward those wives. Even though the Rabbis created a framework of rabbinic *kidushin* and *nisu'in* for the *cheresh*, they did not extend that institution to include a *ketubah*. The *shoteh* has no rabbinic *nisu'in* at all, so the absence of a *ketubah* follows trivially. But the *cheresh* case is more pointed: he has a recognized rabbinic marriage, yet no *ketubah*.

### Recovery

The *baraita* continues: *v'ratzu lekayem yesh lahem ketubah* — if they subsequently recover — the *cheresh* regaining hearing, the *shoteh* regaining mental competence — and they choose to remain married, then a *ketubah* obligation now applies, since the marriage is now *de'oraita*.

### Voluntary Ketubah

The reverse case: *pikeach she'nasa chereshet o shotah* — a hearing, fully competent man who married a deaf or mentally incompetent woman. Here the Rabbis established no institution of *ketubah* either. However, *afilo kasav lah mea maneh kesuvasah kayemes* — if he chose to write a *ketubah* for her, even for a very large sum, it is binding. Because he is a *bar da'at*, his voluntary assumption of a financial obligation is fully valid. The formula the *gemara* uses to explain this is *mipnei she'ratza lazuk binchasav* — because he wished to encumber his estate; that is entirely his prerogative.

The reason the Rabbis did not institute a *ketubah* for the deaf woman is the same consideration raised earlier: *de'im kein, mimne velo nasbi lah* — lest the *ketubah* obligation make her unmarriageable, the Rabbis deliberately did not create it. Making her easier to marry outweighs giving her the standard protection.

This prompts a sharp question: when it is the *cheresh* who is the husband and the hearing woman who is the wife — why do we not insist that she at least have the normal *ketubah*? We said we waive the *ketubah* for the deaf woman to incentivize men to marry her. Fine. But by equally withholding the *ketubah* when he is the *cheresh*, are we not creating a disincentive for a hearing woman to marry him? The *gemara*'s answer: *yoter mishe'ish rotzeh liso, ishah rotza lehinase* — more than a man wishes to marry, a woman wishes to be married. Since women are more desirous of marriage, no additional incentive is needed to attract a woman to marry even a *cheresh*, and therefore no rabbinic *ketubah* is established. This does not, however, adequately explain why she receives no protection at all — why not at least the standard protection? The *gemara* does not fully resolve this, and the most one can say is that since the *cheresh* is not considered a *bar da'at*, imposing a financial obligation on him presents difficulties — though that reasoning also sits uneasily, since we trust him enough to establish the marriage in the first place.

### Rav Malkio

The *gemara* then records a practical episode: there was a *cheresh* in the neighborhood of Rav Malkio, who married a woman and wrote her a substantial *ketubah* of 400 *zuz* from his own estate. Rav Malkio permitted this.

### Rava's Praise

Rava greeted this ruling with admiration: *man chacham keRav Malkio* — who is as wise as Rav Malkio? The reasoning: *ilu ratza shifcha leshamsho mi lo zavninan leh* — if this *cheresh* wanted to acquire a maidservant to attend to his domestic needs, we would clearly recognize that transaction as binding, since we allow him to engage in financial dealings necessary for the conduct of his life. A *ketubah* that creates a similar financial bond — and that motivates a woman to care for him as his wife — is *kal va'chomer* appropriate. The fact that this creates a genuine financial obligation on him is precisely the point: Rav Malkio understood that enabling the *cheresh* to voluntarily undertake such an obligation serves both his dignity and the practical stability of the marriage.

## Deaf Status Debate

### No Asham Talui

The *gemara* now pivots to a fundamental question about the halachic status of the *cheresh* himself. Rav Chiyya bar Ashi said in the name of Shmuel: *eishes cheresh ein chayavin aleha asham talui* — the wife of a *cheresh*, if another man cohabits with her, does not give rise to an *asham talui* — the sacrifice brought for doubtful commission of a *de'oraita* sin. The implication is significant: the *kidushin* of a *cheresh* is not even a doubtful *de'oraita* bond; it is purely rabbinic, and so no adultery at the biblical level — not even a doubtful one — has been committed.

### Five Excluded

The *gemara* attempts to support this position from the well-known list of five who cannot separate *terumah*: *chamisha lo yitremu, ve'im tormu ein trumatan truma* — five people may not take *terumah*, and if they do, their separation has no halachic standing — not merely rabbinic invalidity, but complete nullity even at the biblical level. The five are: a *cheresh*, a *shoteh*, a minor, one who separates from produce that does not belong to him, and a non-Jew separating from a Jew's produce. If the *cheresh*'s act of *terumah* has zero standing — not even a doubtful one — this would suggest that a *cheresh* is categorically and definitively excluded from the realm of legal agency, confirming Shmuel's ruling.

### Safek Theory

But the *gemara* resists this inference. It suggests that Shmuel may actually hold *like* Rabbi Eliezer — that a *cheresh* is not definitively outside the category of *bar da'at*, but rather a *safek bar da'at*, a person of doubtful status — and yet still rules that no *asham talui* is owed for cohabiting with his wife. The two positions need not be contradictory, for a technical reason in the laws of *asham talui*.

### R. Eliezer

The *gemara* cites Rabbi Eliezer's position explicitly: *tanas Rabbi Yitzchak omer mishum Rabbi Eliezer, trumas cheresh lo teitzei l'chulin* — the *terumah* separated by a *cheresh* does not become ordinary *chulin* — *mipnei shehu safek* — because it is of doubtful status. Rabbi Eliezer thus explicitly treats the *cheresh* as a *safek* rather than a definitive non-agent. His *terumah* is neither fully valid *terumah* nor fully invalid; it occupies an uncertain middle ground.

### Asham Talui Tech

Now the technical question: if Shmuel holds like Rabbi Eliezer that a *cheresh* is a *safek bar da'at*, why does Shmuel rule that no *asham talui* is owed for cohabiting with his wife? After all, Rabbi Eliezer himself takes a broad view of *asham talui* — he holds (*detanya*) that one who eats the *chelev* of a *koy*, an animal of doubtful classification between domesticated and wild, is liable for an *asham talui*, even though there is no specific second object that was definitely forbidden. Rabbi Eliezer does not require two pieces — one forbidden, one permitted — to generate liability for *asham talui*. So if a *cheresh* is a *safek bar da'at* according to Rabbi Eliezer, and Rabbi Eliezer holds that abstract doubt suffices for *asham talui*, why would Shmuel deny the *asham talui* in this case?

The answer is that Shmuel holds like Rabbi Eliezer in *one* respect — that a *cheresh* is a *safek* — but disagrees with him in *another* respect: Shmuel holds that *asham talui* requires *chaticha mishtei chatichot* — there must be two concrete objects, one permitted and one forbidden, and the doubt must be which specific object was consumed. An abstract doubt about the nature of a person's status — as in the case of the *cheresh*'s wife — does not trigger *asham talui* according to Shmuel, even if the act might theoretically be a doubtful *de'oraita* violation. *Shmuel savar Rabbi Eliezer b'chada u'puleig lei b'chada.*

### Explicit Safek

The *gemara* then offers an alternate version of Shmuel's statement: *ika d'amri, amar Rav Chiyya bar Ashi amar Shmuel, eishes cheresh chayavin aleha asham talui* — there is a version in which Shmuel explicitly rules that one *is* liable for an *asham talui* for cohabiting with the wife of a *cheresh*. In this version, Shmuel unambiguously holds that a *cheresh* is a *safek bar da'at* and that the *de'oraita* status of the *kidushin* is genuinely in doubt. The apparent contradiction with the *mishnah* about five who cannot take *terumah* is resolved: Shmuel holds like Rabbi Eliezer, who treats the *cheresh*'s *terumah* not as null but as doubtful.

## R. Eliezer's Logic

### Rav Ashi's Q

Rav Ashi raises a pointed question: *mah ta'ama d'Rabbi Eliezer* — what precisely is the reasoning behind Rabbi Eliezer's position that a *cheresh* is a *safek*? Two possibilities present themselves, and the difference between them has profound practical consequences.

### Weak Intellect

The first possibility: *mifsha pshita lei d'cheresh da'ata klisha hu* — it is clear to Rabbi Eliezer that a *cheresh* has weaker *da'at*, a diminished intellectual capacity. But this alone does not suffice to remove a person from the category of *bar da'at*. People vary in intelligence, and legal agency does not require a minimum IQ. Diminished intellectual capacity, as such, would not be disqualifying.

### Clear vs Weak

What would be disqualifying is not weakness of intellect but the inability to think *clearly* — *tzalul*. The question therefore sharpens: *u'mi mesafka lei ee da'ata tzlusa ee lav da'ata tzlusa* — is Rabbi Eliezer's uncertainty whether the *cheresh*'s thought process is clear (*da'ata tzlula*) or not? If that is the question, then there is a stable — if unknown — fact about a given *cheresh*: either he thinks clearly or he does not. We just do not know which. And that is what generates the *safek*.

### Category vs Case

This leads to the first interpretive possibility: that Rabbi Eliezer is making a categorical claim — all *chereshim* are of an unknown but stable mental status, and as a category we cannot determine whether they fall on the *bar da'at* side or not. The second interpretive possibility is more dramatic: *o dilma, pshita lei de'daiteih klisha ve'lav daiteih tzelula hu, ve'hacha hainu ta'ama, kivan de'it itim chalin ve'itim shota* — perhaps Rabbi Eliezer actually knows that the *cheresh* does not think clearly, but his uncertainty is different: a *cheresh* has variable states of mind. At certain moments he is *chalim* — lucid, thinking clearly; at other moments he is effectively in a state of *shoteh*. The *safek* is not a stable unknown fact about his category; it is an irreducible variability about this individual person at any given moment.

### Divorce Question

The practical difference between these two interpretations crystallizes in the question of whether a *cheresh* can divorce his wife after having married her: *le'hotzi ishto beget* — can he write a *get*?

### Variable Mind

*I amarta chada data hu* — if we say that all *chereshim* have a single, consistent (if unknown) state of mind, then *ke'kidushin kach gerushin* — the *kidushin* and the *gerushin* are parallel. Whatever status the marriage had, the divorce has the same status. If the *kidushin* was *de'oraita*, the *gerushin* is *de'oraita*; if *derabbanan*, the *gerushin* is *derabbanan*. In either case, the divorce is valid in whatever domain it operates.

But *ve'i amarta itim chalin itim shota* — if the second interpretation is correct, and we are dealing with variable states of mind in the individual, then *kidushei matzi mekadesh, ve'gerushei lo matzi megaresh* — he can contract the marriage because we allow the act on the assumption that he was lucid at that moment, but he cannot then divorce, because perhaps when he performed the *kidushin* he was in a moment of clarity, while now that he seeks to write the *get* he is in a moment of impaired clarity. The two moments cannot be assumed to be equivalent, and therefore the *get* cannot be confirmed as the mirror of the *kidushin*.

### Teyku

The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teyku* — the question remains open. Whether a *cheresh* can write a *get* after marriage, according to the variable-mind interpretation of Rabbi Eliezer, stands undecided.

### Gaonim View

What is critical to appreciate is the significance of this entire passage for later halachic development. Throughout *Shas*, the trio of *cheresh, shoteh, ve'katan* is listed as categorically excluded from legal agency. Here, however, the *gemara* is actively exploring a position — attributed to Rabbi Eliezer and possibly to Shmuel — that a *cheresh* is a *safek bar da'at*, not a categorical non-agent. And the second interpretation of Rabbi Eliezer goes further: it opens the possibility that in a given case, based on the evidence of how this individual person presents, a determination might be made.

There are *Gaonim* who appear to rule in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer's position and who indicate that if there is evidence that a particular *cheresh* functions as a *bar da'at* — that his mental processes are clear, that he communicates and reasons — he would be treated accordingly. This is the seed from which later halachic authorities, over the subsequent centuries as schools for the deaf developed and as sign language became a recognized medium of full communication, began to reconsider the category. The *gemara* itself does not take us there — the question ends in *teyku* — but the conceptual architecture is already present in Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning as Rav Ashi has unpacked it.
