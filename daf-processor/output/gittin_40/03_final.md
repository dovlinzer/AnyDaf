# Gittin 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Property vs. Status

### Ownership vs. Relationship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּפֵירוּשׁ שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ, אוֹ מִכְּלָלָא שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ? מַאי כְּלָלָא? דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, אָמְרוּ לִפְנֵי רַבִּי: אָמַר ״נִתְיָיאַשְׁתִּי מִפְּלוֹנִי עַבְדִּי״, מַהוּ? אָמַר לָהֶם: אוֹמֵר אֲנִי, אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה אֶלָּא בִּשְׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Did you hear it explicitly** said by Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, **or did you hear it by inference,** i.e., did you infer it from some other statement of his? Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi asked him: **What inference** could I have drawn? Rabbi Zeira answered: **As Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says** that **they said before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: If one **says: I have despaired of** recovering **so-and-so, my slave, what** is the *halakha*? **He said to them: I say** that his slave **has no remedy other** than **via a bill** of manumission.

### Ger's Slaves

The opening case involves a *ger* — a convert — who dies without heirs. Since his biological family has no halakhic standing and he has no children, his estate passes to no one; his property becomes ownerless. What happens to his slaves? The *Tanna Kamma* holds that the adult slaves simply go free: they take possession of themselves, and since there is no master remaining who could give them a *shtar shichrur*, the entire framework of personal-status obligation dissolves. *Abba Shaul*, however, draws a distinction: adult slaves can take possession of themselves as property and thereby achieve freedom, but minor slaves lack the legal capacity to acquire property — they cannot take possession of themselves — and therefore remain in a kind of suspended, vulnerable status, susceptible to seizure by others.

### Explicit or Inferred?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? גָּמַר ״לָהּ״–״לָהּ״ מֵאִשָּׁה – מָה אִשָּׁה בִּשְׁטָר, אַף עֶבֶד נָמֵי בִּשְׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues explaining the possible inference. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **He derived** this by means of a verbal analogy, understanding the meaning of **"to her [*lah*],"** written with regard to a maidservant in the verse: "Nor was freedom given to her" (Leviticus 19:20), **from** the meaning of **"for her [*lah*],"** written with regard to **a wife:** "And he writes for her a bill of divorce" (Deuteronomy 24:3). **Just as a wife** is released from her husband **via a bill** of divorce, **so too, a slave** is **also** emancipated only **via a bill** of manumission.

### Mafkir & Shtar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָא דָיְיקַתְּ מִינַּהּ, כְּאִשָּׁה – מָה אִשָּׁה אִיסּוּרָא וְלָא מָמוֹנָא, אַף עֶבֶד נָמֵי אִיסּוּרָא וְלָא מָמוֹנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **You** heard this statement **and deduced from this** comparison that with regard to his master's death, a slave is **comparable to a wife. Just as a wife** is released by the death of her husband from **a prohibition but not** from **a monetary** bond, **so too, a slave** is **also** emancipated by his master's death from **a prohibition but not** from **a monetary** bond. Therefore, an adult slave, who can acquire ownership of himself, is freed entirely by means of the death of his master. However, in the case of a minor slave, who cannot acquire possession of his own person, the monetary bond remains, in accordance with the opinion of Abba Shaul.

The *Gemara* identifies the source of the inference as a separate teaching of *Rabi Yehoshua ben Levi*. He reported that a case was brought before *Rebbi*: if someone declares *nityashti mi-ploni avdi* — "I give up hope of ever recovering my slave," or more precisely, "I renounce ownership of my slave" — what is the *halacha*? *Rebbi* responded: *omer ani, ein lo takana ela bi-shtar* — in my view, there is no remedy for this slave except through a *shtar shichrur*. The slave can no longer be compelled to work — no one owns his labor — and yet his personal status remains unresolved. He cannot marry freely; he is neither fully a slave nor a free man. A formal document of manumission is required to change his personal status.

A debate between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* underlies this scenario. *Tosafot* maintains that in such a case the slave remains fully a slave in terms of personal status, capable of continuing his prior marital relationship with a slave woman, even though no one can claim his labor. *Rashi* holds that he exists in a genuine in-between status — he cannot be married to either a slave or a free woman — and that this limbo persists until a *shtar* is issued.

### Woman & Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מִכְּלָלָא מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְּרַבָּה! דּוֹק מִינַּהּ לְאִידַּךְ גִּיסָא: מָה אִשָּׁה – בֵּין גְּדוֹלָה בֵּין קְטַנָּה, אַף עֶבֶד נָמֵי – בֵּין גָּדוֹל בֵּין קָטָן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּפֵירוּשׁ שְׁמִיעַ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi said to him: **And if** this was derived **by inference, what** of it? Rabbi Zeira **said to him:** If you derived this by inference from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's statement, then, **on the contrary, deduce from this to the other side** and say as follows: **Just as a wife** is released by her husband's death **whether** she is **an adult or a minor, so too, a slave** is **also** emancipated when his master dies **whether** he is **an adult or a minor,** in opposition to the opinion of Abba Shaul. **He said to him: I heard explicitly** that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Abba Shaul.

*Rabi Yochanan* then explains *Rebbi*'s reasoning: *gamar lah lah me'isha* — he derives it through a *gezeira shava* linking the word *lah* in the context of a woman's *get* and the word *lah* in the context of a female slave's manumission. Just as a woman's personal status is changed exclusively through a document — a *get* — so too a slave's personal status requires a document. The point being driven home is that even once the property dimension of slavery has dissolved, a distinct personal-status dimension remains, and that dimension demands its own formal act: the giving of a *shtar*. This is, as the *Gemara* notes, beautifully underscored by the analogy to a woman: *ma isha bi-shtar, eved nami bi-shtar* — just as a woman's status is changed through a *get*, a slave's status is changed through a *shtar shichrur*.

### Inference Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: בְּפֵירוּשׁ שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ, אוֹ מִכְּלָלָא שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ? מַאי כְּלָלָא? דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, אָמְרוּ לִפְנֵי רַבִּי: ״נִתְיָיאַשְׁתִּי מִפְּלוֹנִי עַבְדִּי״, מַהוּ? אָמַר לָהֶם: אוֹמֵר אֲנִי, אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה אֶלָּא בִּשְׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** says: **The *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul. Rabbi Zeira said to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba: Did you hear it explicitly** that Rabbi Yoḥanan said it, **or did you hear it by inference?** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba asked: **What inference** could have been drawn? Rabbi Zeira answered: **For Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says** that **they said before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: If one **says: I have despaired of** recovering **so-and-so, my slave, what** is the *halakha*? **He said to them: I say** that his slave **has no remedy other** than **via a bill** of manumission.

Now, how does this teaching connect to *Abba Shaul*'s ruling about the *ger*'s slaves? The *Gemara* explains that one might have inferred *Abba Shaul*'s position from *Rabi Yehoshua ben Levi*'s teaching by noting the following parallel: just as a woman's situation involves only personal status (*issur*) and not property (*mamon*), so too the case *Rabi Yehoshua ben Levi* describes is one in which only personal status remains after the property dimension has been renounced. Applying this to *Abba Shaul*'s scenario — when the *ger* dies and his adult slaves take possession of themselves — the property dimension is resolved through their self-acquisition, and only the personal-status issue lingers. But the minor slaves cannot resolve the property dimension, and therefore the status dimension cannot be addressed either. One might have inferred from *Rabi Yehoshua ben Levi*'s teaching that the two dimensions are sequentially dependent: the property issue must first be addressed before the personal-status issue can be resolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָא דָיְיקַתְּ מִינַּהּ, כְּאִשָּׁה – מָה אִשָּׁה בֵּין גְּדוֹלָה בֵּין קְטַנָּה, אַף עֶבֶד נָמֵי בֵּין גָּדוֹל בֵּין קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **You** heard this statement **and deduced from this** comparison that with regard to his master's death, a slave is **comparable** to **a wife. Just as a wife** is released by her husband's death **whether** she is **an adult or a minor, so too, a slave** is **also** emancipated **whether** he is **an adult or a minor,** and there is no distinction between a minor slave who receives a bill of manumission and a minor slave whose master dies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מִכְּלָלָא מַאי? אַדְּרַבָּה! דּוֹק מִינַּהּ לְהָךְ גִּיסָא: מָה אִשָּׁה – אִיסּוּרָא וְלָא מָמוֹנָא, אַף עֶבֶד נָמֵי – אִיסּוּרָא וְלָא מָמוֹנָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּפֵירוּשׁ שְׁמִיעַ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba asked him: **And if** this was derived **by inference, what** of it? Rabbi Zeira said to him: If you derived this by inference, then, **on the contrary, deduce from this to the other side** and say as follows: **Just as a wife** is released by the death of her husband from **a prohibition and not** from **a monetary** bond, **so too, a slave** is **also** emancipated by his master's death from **a prohibition and not a monetary** bond. **He said to him: I heard** this **explicitly** from Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The *Gemara* immediately pushes back: *vi'im mi-klala, mai?* — and if it was only an inference, what is the problem? The answer: *adarabba dok mina l'ipkha* — on the contrary, you could have inferred the opposite. The comparison to a woman teaches that a *get* applies whether the woman is an adult or a minor. If we apply that symmetry to the slave scenario under *Abba Shaul*, one might equally infer that the status change applies to adult and minor slaves alike. Since the inference is genuinely indeterminate — it can support either ruling — we cannot rely on it as the basis for the *halacha*. Accordingly, *Rabi Yaakov bar Idi* acknowledges: *b'feirush shemia li* — I heard it stated explicitly. The ruling that we follow *Abba Shaul* is not inferred; it was transmitted directly.

### Two Versions

The *Gemara* then presents a competing tradition. *Rabi Chiyya bar Abba* transmitted in the name of *Rabi Yochanan*: *ein halacha k'Abba Shaul* — we do not rule like *Abba Shaul*, and both adult and minor slaves of the *ger* go free. *Rav Zeira* poses the same question to *Rabi Chiyya bar Abba*: was this heard explicitly or inferred? And the *Gemara* runs through precisely the same structure as before — the inference from the woman analogy could equally support ruling against *Abba Shaul* (since a *get* works for a minor girl as well), but it could also support ruling like *Abba Shaul* (since the woman analogy highlights the *issur*-only dimension, suggesting that status change requires the property dimension to be resolved first). In the end, *Rabi Chiyya bar Abba* also declares: *b'feirush shemia li* — I too heard it explicitly, from *Rabi Yochanan*, that we do not rule like *Abba Shaul*.

What emerges from this entire passage, even if the precise inferential logic is elusive, is a profound conceptual framework. The *Gemara* is working with two separate dimensions of the master-slave bond. One dimension is proprietary: the master owns the slave's body and labor as one owns property. The other is a personal-status dimension: a legally defined relationship that generates its own set of *issurim* — obligations and prohibitions — independent of ownership. The question that animates the whole discussion is whether these two dimensions are separable, and if so, how the dissolution of one affects the other.

One important framing: a *shtar shichrur* might function primarily by changing the slave's personal status, with the property dimension dissolving as a natural consequence. Alternatively, it might work primarily by extinguishing the property claim, with the personal-status change following. If it works through status, then even where property ownership has already dissolved — as in the *mafkir* case — the status relationship may persist and still require a formal act. If it works through property, then dissolving the property claim without a formal act of redemption leaves the status unaddressed. Both directions are at play in this *sugya*.

The analogy to *kiddushin* is relevant here as well: the *Gemara*'s insistence that a woman is not owned as property — that the marital bond is a personal-status relationship, not a proprietary one — is underscored by the fact that *gerushin* operates through a document alone. *Ma isha bi-shtar* — only through the *get* does her status change. A husband holds no property right in his wife analogous to that which a master holds in his *eved Kena'ani*; what he holds is a personal-status claim. The *Gemara* here is careful never to lose sight of the fact that even an *eved Kena'ani*, though owned as property in the fullest sense, remains a human being — a point the *Torah* underscores by making a master liable if he beats his slave to death.

## Shtar vs. Kesef

### Self-Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר, אָמַר לָהֶם: אוֹמֵר אֲנִי, אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה אֶלָּא בִּשְׁטָר. וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אוֹמֵר אֲנִי, אַף הוּא – נוֹתֵן דְּמֵי עַצְמוֹ וְיוֹצֵא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמוֹכְרוֹ לוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** In connection with the *baraita* cited above, the Gemara discusses the matter itself. **The Master said** above: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to them: I say** that his slave **has no remedy other** than **via a bill** of manumission. The Gemara challenges: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: I say** that **even** the slave **himself can give his own monetary value and is emancipated, due to** the fact **that it is as if** the Temple treasurer **sold him to himself.** This demonstrates that a slave can be emancipated by paying money in addition to receiving a bill of manumission.

Having established the basic framework, the *Gemara* now turns to the question of *how* a slave is formally freed. Two mechanisms appear in the sources: *shtar* — a deed of manumission — and *kesef* — monetary redemption. The case that focuses the discussion is derived from the *pasuk* regarding a *shifcha kena'anit*, the non-Jewish female slave: *o chufsha lo nitan lah* — or her freedom has not been given to her. This *pasuk* also mentions *hafdeih*, redemption, suggesting that being redeemed — whether by a third party paying the master or by the slave herself paying — is a recognized mode of manumission.

*Rebbi*'s ruling cited in an earlier context had been that a slave who was consecrated (*makdish*) to the *Beis HaMikdash* can pay the *gizbarim* for his own redemption and thereby go free. The *Gemara* now clarifies this position: the two modes are *o be'kesef o be'shtar* — either through monetary redemption or through a deed of manumission. However — and this is the crucial point — when an owner has already been *mafkir* his slave, renouncing all property ownership, the option of *kesef* is no longer available. Redemption only makes sense when there is a property claim to be extinguished; one cannot redeem something from an owner who no longer owns it. The only path remaining for such a slave is a *shtar shichrur*. This also illuminates the broader conceptual point: if *kesef* works, it focuses on dissolving the property claim, after which the personal status follows. If *shtar* works, it directly addresses the personal status, and the property dimension dissolves as a consequence.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: אוֹ בְּכֶסֶף אוֹ בִּשְׁטָר; וְהַאי – פְּקַע לֵיהּ כַּסְפֵּיהּ. וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מֵהַאי תַּנָּא –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This is what he is saying:** A slave can be emancipated **either via money or via a bill** of manumission. **And** with regard to **this** master, who despaired of recovering his slave, **his monetary** hold over this slave **is abrogated,** and he can be emancipated only via a bill of manumission. **And** this statement, that generally a slave can be emancipated either via a bill of manumission or by paying money, serves **to exclude** the opinion **of this *tanna*** in the following *baraita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: יָכוֹל יְהֵא כֶּסֶף גּוֹמֵר בָּהּ, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁשְּׁטָר גּוֹמֵר בָּהּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהׇפְדֵּה לֹא נִפְדָּתָה״ –
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says in the name of Rabbi Akiva:** One **might** have thought that paying **money completes** the emancipation of a Hebrew maidservant, **just as a bill** of manumission **completes her** emancipation. **The verse states,** concerning a Jewish man who engages in sexual intercourse with a maidservant who had been designated to cohabit with a Hebrew slave: "And whoever lies carnally with a woman that is a bondmaid designated for a man, **and not at all redeemed,** nor was freedom given to her; there shall be inquisition; they shall not be put to death, because she was not free" (Leviticus 19:20). In this case, neither the Jewish man nor the maidservant is liable to receive the death penalty, in contrast to a man who engages in sexual intercourse with a married woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹרְעָה כָּל הַפָּרָשָׁה כֻּולָּהּ לְ״לֹא חוּפָּשָׁה״, לוֹמַר לָךְ: שְׁטָר גּוֹמֵר בָּהּ, וְאֵין הַכֶּסֶף גּוֹמֵר בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The** *halakha* of this **entire portion** of a maidservant who had been designated to cohabit with a Hebrew slave **is linked to,** i.e., dependent on, only the phrase **"nor was freedom** given her" by means of a bill of manumission. The reason she does not have the status of a free woman is that she was not granted her freedom by means of a bill of manumission. If the *halakha* of this portion would be dependent upon the phrase "and not at all redeemed," this would indicate that the reason she does not have the status of a free woman is that she was not redeemed with money. This is in order **to say to you: A bill** of manumission **completes her** emancipation entirely, **and money does not complete her** emancipation. According to Rabbi Shimon, a slave can be emancipated only via a bill of manumission but not by paying money.

*Rabbi Shimon*, citing *Rabbi Akiva*, challenges the position that *kesef* constitutes an independent mechanism of manumission at all. The *Gemara* frames this as a *drasha*: the *pasuk* says *v'hipadeh lo nifdasa* — she has not been redeemed — followed by *o chufsha lo nitan lah* — or her freedom has not been given to her. *Rabbi Shimon* reads the entire passage as being drawn back to the conclusion — *chufsha lo nitan lah* — meaning that even if there is an act of redemption, what ultimately changes the slave's status is the giving of freedom, a formal act of *shichrur* through a *shtar*. The *kesef* does not independently accomplish the status change; *shtar gomer bah v'ein ha-kesef gomer bah* — it is the document that completes her manumission, not the money.

### Nachman vs. Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וְרַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama says** that **Rav Naḥman says: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. And Rav Yosef bar Ḥama says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** says: **The *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.**

*Rami bar Chama* said in the name of *Rav Nachman*: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon* — we rule like *Rabbi Shimon*, and only a *shtar* frees a slave, not *kesef*. But *Rabbi Yosef bar Chama* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *ein halacha k'Rabbi Shimon* — we do not rule like him, and *kesef* can also serve as a mode of manumission. The tension between these two positions is then explored through a series of transmitted rulings.

### Hat Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק לְרָבָא בַּר שְׁאֵילְתָא, דַּהֲוָה קָאֵי אַפִּיתְחָא דְּבֵי תְפִלָּה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲלָכָה אוֹ אֵין הֲלָכָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנִי אוֹמֵר – אֵין הֲלָכָה; וְרַבָּנַן דַּאֲתוֹ מִמָּחוֹזָא אָמְרִי, אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה; וְכִי אֲתַאי לְסוּרָא, אַשְׁכַּחְתֵּיהּ לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין, אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: אֵימָא לִי אִיזִי, גּוּפָא דְעוֹבָדָא הֵיכִי הֲוָה? אֲמַר לִי: דְּהַהִיא אַמְתָּא דַּהֲוָה מָרַהּ שְׁכִיב מְרַע, אָתְיָא בָּכְיָא קַמֵּיהּ, אָמְרָה לֵיהּ: עַד אֵימַת תִּשְׁתַּעְבֵּיד וְתֵיזִיל הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא? שְׁקַל כּוּמְתֵּיהּ שְׁדָא בַּהּ, אֲמַר לַהּ: זִיל קְנִי הָא וּקְנִי נַפְשִׁיךְ. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak found Rava bar She'eilta when he was standing at the entrance to a house of prayer. He said to him,** with regard to this issue: Is the ***halakha*** in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, **or** is the ***halakha* not** in accordance with his opinion? **He said to him: I say** that the ***halakha*** is **not** in accordance with his opinion in this matter, **and the Rabbis who came from Meḥoza say** that **Rabbi Zeira said in the name of Rav Naḥman:** The ***halakha*** is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon. **And when I came to Sura, I found Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin,** and **I said to him: Tell me, my friend [*izi*], the incident itself. What happened,** and what exactly did Rav Naḥman say? **He said to me that** there was **a certain maidservant whose master was on his deathbed. She came crying before him** and **said to him: Until when will that woman,** i.e., I, **continue to be subjugated? He took his hat** [***kumtei***], **threw it to her,** and **said to her: Go acquire this** hat **and** thereby **acquire yourself** as a free woman. **They came before Rav Naḥman** for a ruling, and **he said to them: He did nothing.**

The *Gemara* presents a poignant narrative to clarify how *Rav Nachman*'s ruling came to be understood. There was a slave woman whose master lay dying. She came before him weeping, saying: how long must I remain enslaved? She had served him for seventy years; he was dying — could he not free her now? The master took his hat, threw it at her, and said: *zili kni ha u-kni nafshach* — go, take this hat, and through it acquire your own freedom. They came before *Rav Nachman*, who ruled: *lo asa v'lo klum* — he accomplished nothing; she is not free. From this ruling, observers concluded that *Rav Nachman* held *kesef* does not free a slave.

### Exchange Direction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דַּחֲזָא, סָבַר מִשּׁוּם דַּהֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן; וְלָא הִיא, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ כֵּלָיו שֶׁל מַקְנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **One who saw** this incident **thought** that he ruled in this manner **because** he holds **that the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** that a maidservant can be emancipated only via a bill of manumission. **But** that **is not so. Rather,** it was **because this was** a legal act of acquisition formalizing the transfer of ownership that was performed with **the items of the one who transfers ownership,** and this mode of acquisition takes effect only when the one who transfers ownership acquires an item belonging to the one to whom the ownership is being transferred. Since the item of the owner was used, the maidservant was not emancipated.

The *Gemara* immediately corrects this inference. The ruling had nothing to do with whether *kesef* can free a slave. The problem was directional: in the formal act of *kinyan kesef*, it is the slave who must pay the master, not the master who gives something to the slave. The logic is analogous to *kinyan chalipin* — the handkerchief must travel from the one seeking to acquire toward the one who holds what is being acquired. Here, the master was transferring an object *to* the slave woman; this is *keli shel makneih* — the object belongs to the one doing the giving — and that is not the correct direction for a redemption transaction. The ruling does not demonstrate that *kesef* is categorically invalid; it demonstrates only that this particular transaction was structured incorrectly.

### Final Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַחִיתַאי, אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא סָבָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אַשְׁיָאן, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וְלָא הִיא, אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Aḥittai says** that **Rav Hamnuna the Elder says** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Ashyan says** that **Rav Huna says** that **Rav Hamnuna says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** The Gemara concludes: **And** that **is not so,** as the ***halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.**

Several further traditions are cited: *Rav Shmuel bar Achisfei* transmitting through *Rav Yitzchak bar Ashin* transmitting through *Rav Huna* in the name of *Rav Hamnuna* — an impressively long chain — that *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon*, we rule like *Rabbi Shimon* that only a *shtar* frees and not *kesef*. The *Gemara* then closes: *v'lav hi* — this is not the *halacha*. We do not, in the end, rule like *Rabbi Shimon*; *kesef* can also accomplish manumission.

## Evidence of Freedom

### Marriage Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אָמַר רַבִּי: עֶבֶד שֶׁנָּשָׂא אֶת בַּת חוֹרִין בִּפְנֵי רַבּוֹ – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says** that **Rav Ashi says** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** In the case of **a slave who marries a free woman in the presence of his master,** he is emancipated.

*Rav Zeira* transmitted in the name of *Rabbi Chanina* in the name of *Rav Assi* in the name of *Rav*: if a slave marries a free woman in the presence of his master — *eved she-nasa bat chorin bifnei rabo* — he goes free, *yatza l'cherut*. The near-universal understanding of this ruling is that it does not describe an act of manumission in itself. Where is the *shtar*? Where is the *kesef*? Rather, the master's silence in the face of this marriage is taken as *evidence* that the slave had already been freed. A master would never countenance his slave marrying a free woman — for a slave to do so would be a grave violation — and therefore the master's passive presence is proof that he must have already freed the slave.

### Dual Purpose Shtar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כָּל כָּךְ יֵשׁ בְּיָדְךָ?! וַאֲנִי שׁוֹנֶה: הַכּוֹתֵב שְׁטַר אֵירוּסִין לְשִׁפְחָתוֹ, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to** Rabbi Zeira: **You possess such** an extreme *halakha*, **but I teach** this *halakha*: With regard to **one who writes a document of betrothal for his maidservant,** stating: You are hereby betrothed to me, **Rabbi Meir says: She is betrothed, and the Rabbis say: She is not betrothed,** as even this, when he betroths her directly, does not serve as proof that he emancipates her.

*Rabi Yochanan* challenges this teaching by pointing to the following *braisa*. If a man writes a *shtar eirusin* to his own female slave — declaring *harei at mekudeshet li* — one might expect this to function as proof that he had freed her, since he certainly would not attempt to marry his own slave without first freeing her, and he is directly participating in the act. Yet this is the subject of a dispute between *Rabbi Meir* and *Chachamim* — it is not simply assumed that such an act constitutes proof of prior freedom.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא – כְּשֶׁרַבּוֹ הִנִּיחַ לוֹ תְּפִילִּין; הָכָא נָמֵי – כְּשֶׁרַבּוֹ הִשִּׂיאוֹ אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Just **as that** which **Rabba bar Rav Sheila says** in a different context, that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is referring to a case **where** a slave's **master placed phylacteries on him, here too,** the context of Rabbi Zeira's statement is not that of a slave who married a woman in his master's presence but a case **where** the slave's **master** himself **provided a wife for him,** as this is certainly proof that he had emancipated him.

The *Gemara* explains this case on the basis of a teaching of *Rav Abba bar Rav Huna Sheila*: the cases where we infer freedom from the master's conduct are those where the master is *directly and actively involved* in performing the act together with the slave — not merely present. This resolves the apparent tension. In the case of the *shtar eirusin*, the context makes clear that the master has not previously freed his slave; rather, he is attempting to make the *shtar kiddushin* simultaneously serve as a *shtar shichrur*. The formula *harei at mekudeshet li* is being offered as a dual-purpose document — both a deed of marriage and a deed of manumission.

### Meir's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דִּלְעַבְדֵּיהּ – לָא מִעֲבַד לֵיהּ אִיסּוּרָא, וְאִיהוּ – עָבֵד אִיסּוּרָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this answer: **Is there anything** like this, **where for his slave he would not violate a prohibition,** and by providing a wife for his slave he indicates that he must have emancipated the slave, **but he** himself might **violate the prohibition,** as he is suspected of marrying his maidservant without having freed her?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּאָמַר לָהּ: ״צְאִי בּוֹ וְהִתְקַדְּשִׁי בּוֹ״; רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: יֵשׁ בַּלָּשׁוֹן הַזֶּה לְשׁוֹן שִׁחְרוּר, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין בַּלָּשׁוֹן הַזֶּה לְשׁוֹן שִׁחְרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: With what are we dealing here?** This is a case **where he said to** the maidservant when he gave her the document of betrothal: **Become emancipated with this and become betrothed** to me **with this. Rabbi Meir holds that this formulation** written in the document of betrothal: You are hereby betrothed to me, **contains a formulation of emancipation** and therefore serves both as a bill of manumission and a document of betrothal. **And the Rabbis hold: This formulation is not a formulation of emancipation.** That is why the Rabbis hold she is not betrothed in this case. However, according to everyone, a master is not suspected of marrying his maidservant without first freeing her.

*Rabbi Meir* holds that *yesh bi-lashon hazeh lashon shichrur* — the phrase *harei at mekudeshet li* contains within it an implicit language of manumission. Since one cannot be married to a master, saying "you are betrothed to me" necessarily implies "and I am freeing you." Moreover, since this is written in a *shtar*, the document itself carries the force of the act. *Rabbi Meir* would allow the *shtar kiddushin* to function simultaneously as a *shtar shichrur* because the implication of freedom is carried within the language of betrothal, and it is operative *because* it is a document. *Chachamim* disagree: *ein bi-lashon hazeh lashon shichrur* — the language is not explicit enough to constitute a deed of manumission. The implication is insufficient; a *shtar shichrur* requires its own language.

In either case, this scenario is distinct from the evidentiary question. Evidence of prior freedom requires direct, active involvement by the master — not merely his presence. When the *shtar kiddushin* is offered explicitly as a dual instrument, it is clear from the context that the slave has *not* yet been freed; the question is only whether the document can accomplish both acts simultaneously.

### Master's Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: עֶבֶד שֶׁהִנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין בִּפְנֵי רַבּוֹ – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת. מֵיתִיבִי: לָוָה הֵימֶנּוּ רַבּוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ רַבּוֹ אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס, אוֹ שֶׁהִנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין בִּפְנֵי רַבּוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁקָּרָא שְׁלֹשָׁה פְּסוּקִים בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת בִּפְנֵי רַבּוֹ, הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יָצָא לְחֵירוּת!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this issue, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: A slave who dons phylacteries in the presence of his master is emancipated,** as this is unusual behavior for a slave because slaves are not obligated in this mitzva. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If a slave's **master borrowed** money **from him; or if his master appointed him as a steward** over his possessions; **or if** the slave **donned phylacteries in the presence of his master; or if he read three verses** of the Torah reading **in the synagogue in the presence of his master,** although all of these activities are ordinarily performed only by freemen, **this** slave **is not emancipated.** This seems to contradict the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא: כְּשֶׁרַבּוֹ הִנִּיחַ לוֹ תְּפִילִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Sheila says:** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi was referring to a case **where** the slave's **master placed phylacteries on him.** In that case it is clear that the slave is donning phylacteries with the consent of his master, and a master would not place phylacteries on his slave unless he had already emancipated him.

A *braisa* is then adduced: if a slave puts on *tefillin* in the presence of his master, this is cited as evidence of freedom. But the *braisa* itself lists several acts that one might think would constitute such evidence — the master lending money from his slave, appointing the slave as *apotropos*, the slave receiving an *aliyah* in *shul* in the presence of the master — and rules that *none* of these suffice: *harei ze lo yatza l'cheirus*. How then can *tefillin* constitute proof?

*Rabbah bar Rav Sheila* resolves the difficulty: *keshe-rabo hiniyach lo tefillin* — the case where we accept this as proof is specifically where the master himself placed the *tefillin* on the slave's arm. The master's direct, active involvement in an act socially associated exclusively with free Jewish men — he himself wrapping the *tefillin* — is the evidence. Mere passive presence is insufficient; what is required is that the master actively participated in doing something that no master would do for a slave, thereby demonstrating that he has already freed him.

The distinction between the cases in the *braisa* and the conclusive case parallels the distinction established earlier: it is not enough that the master is present (*bifnei rabo*); what matters is whether the master is the active agent in an act categorically inconsistent with the slave's continued servitude.

### Tosafot & Tefillin

*Tosafot* raises a subtle point regarding the *tefillin* case specifically. One might have thought that a master would hesitate to put *tefillin* on his slave because slaves are exempt from *tefillin*, and if the slave recites a *beracha* on them it would constitute a *beracha levatalah* — a blessing recited in vain. *Tosafot* dismisses this concern: *delo ma'avid lei issura de-mivareich aleihen de-ika beracha levatalah* — this is not the reason. Women are similarly exempt from many time-bound positive commandments and yet, according to the *Ashkenazic* practice, may recite blessings upon them; the same reasoning would apply to slaves. The real reason that a master would not put *tefillin* on his slave is purely societal — *ein avadim regilin b'tefillin* — slaves do not customarily wear *tefillin*, and therefore when the master directly performs this act, it is conclusive societal evidence that the slave has been freed. It is a matter of public status, not of any specific halakhic prohibition.

## Deathbed Directives

### Al Yishtabedu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִי שֶׁאָמַר בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָתוֹ ״פְּלוֹנִית שִׁפְחָתִי אַל יִשְׁתַּעְבְּדוּ בָּהּ לְאַחַר מוֹתִי״ – כּוֹפִין אֶת הַיּוֹרְשִׁים וְכוֹתְבִין לָהּ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר. אָמְרוּ לְפָנָיו רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי: רַבִּי, אִי אַתָּה מוֹדֶה שֶׁבָּנֶיהָ עֲבָדִים?
>
> **Translation:** § **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia he reported that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In a case of **one who says at the moment of his death:** With regard to **so-and-so, my maidservant,** my heirs **should not treat her as a slave after my death,** the court **compels the heirs and they write for her a bill of manumission. Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi said before him: My teacher, don't you admit that her children are slaves?** The master meant only that his heirs should not subjugate her excessively. He did not intend to free her, and her children remain slaves. Why, then, are the heirs compelled to free the maidservant?

When *Rav Dimi* came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported in the name of *Rabi Yochanan*: if a person says at the moment of his death — *ploni shifchasi al yishtabedu bah achar mosi* — "My heirs should not enslave this slave woman of mine after my death," then *kofin es ha-yorshim ve-chosvin lah get shichrur* — we compel the heirs to write her a *get shichrur*. The reasoning: the dying owner's words appear to constitute a *mafkir* declaration, a renunciation of property ownership. Since a *shtar shichrur* is still required, and he is no longer alive to provide it, the heirs write it in his stead.

The *Gemara* records a challenge to this ruling: *Rabi Ami* and *Rabi Assi* asked — do you not concede that her children would be slaves? If the woman is freed, her future children will be free. But if she is merely told she will not be worked, while remaining a slave in personal status, her future children remain slaves and the heirs retain a financial interest in keeping her enslaved. This challenge suggests that the language *al yishtabedu* — "do not enslave her" — may not be a renunciation of ownership at all, but simply a directive not to compel her labor. Under that reading, she remains a slave and no *shtar shichrur* is owed.

### Koras Ruach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִי שֶׁאָמַר בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָתוֹ ״פְּלוֹנִית שִׁפְחָתִי קוֹרַת רוּחַ עָשְׂתָה לִי; יֵעָשֶׂה לָהּ קוֹרַת רוּחַ״ – כּוֹפִין אֶת הַיּוֹרְשִׁין וְעוֹשִׂין לָהּ קוֹרַת רוּחַ. מַאי טַעְמָא? מִצְוָה לְקַיֵּים דִּבְרֵי הַמֵּת.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he reported a different version of what **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In a case of **one who says at the moment of his death: So-and-so, my maidservant, gave me satisfaction** and **one should do for her** something that gives her **satisfaction,** the court **compels the heirs to give her satisfaction,** and if she will be satisfied only by being emancipated, they must do so. **What is the reason** for this? **It is a mitzva to fulfill the statement of the dead.**

A different version of *Rabi Yochanan*'s ruling was transmitted by *Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda*. In this version, the dying master says: *ploni shifchasi koras ruach astah li, ya'asu lah koras ruach* — "My slave woman brought me comfort; you should do for her what she wishes." The *halacha* here is also that *kofin es ha-yorshim la'asos lah koras ruach* — we compel the heirs to fulfill her wishes. Since her wish is to be freed, the heirs are compelled to free her.

### Divrei Hameis

The *Gemara* explains the principle underlying this second version: *mitzvah l'kayem divrei ha-meis* — it is a *mitzvah* to fulfill the wishes of one who expressed them on his deathbed. This is a distinct category from *divrei shechiv mera*, a deathbed bequest, in which ownership transfers automatically at death with the force of a *kinyan*. Here, the master is not making a legally operative property transfer; he is issuing a directive — a request — that his heirs carry out his final wishes. The question of when *beis din* compels compliance with such a directive, and under what halakhic theory the compulsion operates, is left with these interesting parameters.

### Amimar's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הַמַּפְקִיר עַבְדּוֹ – אוֹתוֹ עֶבֶד אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? גּוּפֵיהּ לָא קָנֵי לֵיהּ – אִיסּוּרָא הוּא דְּאִיכָּא גַּבֵּיהּ, וְאִיסּוּרָא לָא מָצֵי מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar says:** With regard to **one who renounces ownership of his slave,** there is **no** halakhic **remedy for that slave,** and he cannot marry a Jewish woman. **What is the reason** for this? The slave **himself does not belong to** his master. However, **there is the prohibition** against marrying a Jewish woman that remains **in** the master's possession, **and** the master **cannot transfer ownership of the prohibition to** the slave, as this is not something that can be transferred. A bill of manumission is not effective in this case because the slave already does not belong to him.

*Amimar* then articulates a stark position: *ha-mafkir avdo, oso eved ein lo takana* — if an owner renounces ownership of his slave while alive, that slave has no remedy. *Amimar*'s reasoning: *gufei lo kani lei* — the owner no longer holds a proprietary claim over the slave's body. What remains is the *issura* — the personal-status dimension. But — *issura lo matzi makni lei* — the owner is no longer in a position to transfer or resolve that personal-status dimension, because he has relinquished the ownership that grounded his authority to do so. The slave needs a *shtar shichrur* but cannot receive one, because the only person who could give it no longer has the standing to do so. This is the worst of all possible outcomes.

### Rav Ashi's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: וְהָאָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה יָצָא לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״צָרִיךְ״ – וְאֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Ameimar: But didn't Ulla say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says, and** similarly **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin** says that **Rav says: Both** in **this** case of one who betroths his slave, **and** in **that** case of one who renounces ownership of his slave, the slave **is emancipated but** nevertheless **requires a bill of manumission?** This demonstrates that when one renounces ownership of his slave, the slave is no longer in his possession but can still become a freeman upon receiving a bill of manumission. Ameimar **said to him:** They meant that **he requires** a bill of manumission to be considered a freeman and to marry a Jewish woman, **but** there is **no remedy for him,** as the master cannot issue one.

*Rav Ashi* challenges *Amimar* from the tradition transmitted by *Ulla* in the name of *Rabi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Chiyya bar Avin* in the name of *Rav*: when an owner is *mafkir* his slave, the slave goes free but requires a *get shichrur* — implying that the *get shichrur* can and should be given. *Amimar* responds: you have misread the tradition. *Tzarich v'ein lo takana* — when they said he "requires a *get shichrur*," they meant: he is in a state in which his resolution depends on receiving a *get shichrur*, and he is never going to get one. The tradition was not offering a path to resolution; it was describing an impasse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הַמַּפְקִיר עַבְדּוֹ וָמֵת – אוֹתוֹ הָעֶבֶד אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? גּוּפֵיהּ לָא קָנֵי לֵיהּ – אִיסּוּרָא הוּא דְּאִיכָּא גַּבֵּיהּ, וְאִיסּוּרָא לִבְרֵיהּ לָא מוֹרֵית. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: וְהָא כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן!
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** a different version of this exchange: **Ameimar says:** With regard to **one who renounces ownership of his slave and dies,** there is **no** halakhic **remedy for that slave** and he cannot marry a Jewish woman. **What is the reason** for this? The slave **himself does not belong to** his master; however, **there is the prohibition** against marrying a Jewish woman that remains **in** the master's possession, **and** the master **cannot bequeath the prohibition to his son. Rav Ashi said to Ameimar: But when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, he reported that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that if one says that his heirs should not treat his maidservant as a slave, the heirs are compelled to write a bill of manumission. This demonstrates that the heirs can write a bill of manumission although they do not own the maidservant.

*Rav Ashi* returns with a second challenge: but the story of *Rav Dimi* — the case of *al yishtabedu bah* — ended with the ruling that the heirs write her a *get shichrur*. Does this not demonstrate that even after the owner dies, having in effect renounced his claim over the slave, the heirs can provide the *shtar shichrur*?

### Heirs Can Free

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַב דִּימִי טָעוּתָא הִיא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טָעוּתָא – דְּלָא אַמְרַהּ בִּלְשׁוֹן שִׁחְרוּר; הָא אַמְרַהּ בִּלְשׁוֹן שִׁחְרוּר – הָכִי נָמֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא כִּדְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה סְבִירָא לִי.
>
> **Translation:** Ameimar answered: The report **of Rav Dimi** quoting Rabbi Yoḥanan **is erroneous,** and Rabbi Yoḥanan never stated that *halakha*. **He said to him: What** is **erroneous** about Rav Dimi's statement? The reason it is erroneous is **that** the master **did not state this using a formulation of emancipation, but** if **he** had **stated this using a formulation of emancipation,** then **indeed** they would be able to write for her a bill of manumission? Why then do you not concede to his opinion? **He said to him: I hold in accordance with** the report **of Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda,** that Rabbi Yoḥanan was discussing a case where the master said that his heirs should give the maidservant satisfaction. Therefore, the master never stated that the heirs would not have ownership over the maidservant, only that they should fulfill his dying instruction to grant her satisfaction.

*Amimar* responds: *gabei d'Dimi ta'us hi* — that ruling was in error. The entire *psak* in the *Rav Dimi* version was called into question precisely because *Rabi Ami* and *Rabi Assi* pointed out that *al yishtabedu* was not a renunciation of ownership — the owner was merely saying "don't make her work," not "I renounce all claim to her." Since the language was ambiguous, the principle of *mafkir avdo u-meis* was never actually tested in that case. Had the owner said explicit language of renunciation — *b'lashon shichrur* — everyone would have accepted that he had renounced ownership and died, and the heirs could then provide the *shtar shichrur*. *Amimar* insists: *ana k'Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda sevira li* — I hold like the version of *Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda*, where the case turned on *koras ruach* — a deathbed directive rooted in *mitzva l'kayem divrei ha-meis* — not on a *mafkir* scenario at all.

### Non-Jewish Owners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דִּסְקַרְתָּא דְּעַבְדֵי דְּאִזְדַּבַּן לְגוֹי. כְּלוֹ מָרְווֹתָא בָּתְרָאֵי, אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבִינָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: זִילוּ אַהְדַּרוּ אַבְּנֵי מָרְווֹתָא קַמָּאֵי, וְיִכְתְּבוּ לְכוּ גִּיטָּא דְחֵרוּתָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרָבִינָא, וְהָאָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הַמַּפְקִיר עַבְדּוֹ וָמֵת – אוֹתוֹ הָעֶבֶד אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was an incident involving **a certain city** [***diskarta***] **of slaves that was sold to gentiles.** When **their final** gentile **masters died,** the slaves **came before Ravina** and said that since they had no masters, they wanted to be considered full-fledged Jews. **He said to them: Go and return to the children of your first masters and** have them **write for you bills of manumission** so that you will be considered freemen in every respect. **The Rabbis said to Ravina: But didn't Ameimar say** that with regard to **one who renounces ownership of his slave and dies,** there is **no remedy for that slave?** Similarly, if one sells his slave to a gentile, he no longer has the right to the slave's labor and can no longer write a bill of manumission for him.

The *Gemara* presents a practical case that frames the stakes of this dispute. There was a certain region (*diskara*) of slaves who had all been sold to non-Jews — a sale which, halakhically, obligates the original Jewish owners to redeem them and issue a *shtar shichrur*. The non-Jewish owners then died. The original Jewish owners had also died. The slaves came before *Ravina*. He ruled: *ziloo hadiru a-b'nei mar bar Rav Ashi kamai* — go to the sons of the original Jewish masters, and *v'yichtevu lach gita d'cheruta* — they will write you a *shtar shichrur*. As *Ravina* himself recognizes, this is essentially a case of *mafkir avdo u-meis*: the moment the original masters sold the slaves to non-Jews, they relinquished property ownership; they then died without having issued a *shtar shichrur*; and *Ravina* is ruling that the sons — the heirs — can now write it.

The *Rabbanan* challenged *Ravina*: but *Amimar* said that in a case of *mafkir avdo u-meis*, the slave has no remedy! *Ravina* replied: *ana k'Rav Dimi sevira li* — I hold like the principle of the *Rav Dimi* case, which demonstrates that heirs can free a slave after the owner has died. The *Rabbanan* pushed back: but was the *Rav Dimi* case not a *ta'ut*? *Ravina* responded with the same resolution that emerged earlier: *d'lo amar b'lashon shichrur* — the *ta'ut* was only because in that specific case the owner did not use clear language of renunciation. The principle — that the heirs can write a *shtar shichrur* after the owner renounced ownership and died — was never in dispute. *Ha amar b'lashon shichrur*, had he used such language, no one would have challenged it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא כְּרַב דִּימִי סְבִירָא לִי. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: דְּרַב דִּימִי טָעוּתָא הִיא! אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי טָעוּתָא – דְּלָא אַמְרַהּ בִּלְשׁוֹן שִׁחְרוּר; הָא אַמְרַהּ בִּלְשׁוֹן שִׁחְרוּר – הָכִי נָמֵי! וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרָבִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to them: I hold in accordance with** the report of **Rav Dimi,** who said that Rabbi Yoḥanan says that the heirs can give a bill of manumission. **They,** the Rabbis, **said to him:** That **which Rav Dimi** said **is erroneous. He said to them: What** is **erroneous** about Rav Dimi's statement? The reason it is erroneous is **that he did not state this using a formulation of emancipation, but** if **he** had **stated this using a formulation of emancipation,** then **indeed** they would be able to write a bill of manumission. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Ravina,** that the original masters can write a bill of manumission for the slaves.

### Ravina's Ruling

The *Gemara* closes with the definitive ruling: *v'hilkheta k'Ravina* — the *halacha* follows *Ravina*. In a case of *mafkir avdo u-meis*, the slave is not permanently stuck; the heirs retain the capacity to write a *shtar shichrur* and free the slave.

## Chatzi Eved

### Two Owners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא עַבְדָּא דְּבֵי תְרֵי, קָם חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ וְשַׁחְרְרֵיהּ לְפַלְגֵיהּ, אֲמַר אִידַּךְ: הַשְׁתָּא שָׁמְעִי בִּי רַבָּנַן וּמַפְסְדוּ לֵיהּ מִינַּאי; אֲזַל אַקְנְיֵיהּ לִבְנוֹ קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain slave who** belonged **to two** partners. **One of them arose and emancipated his half** of the slave. **The other** master **said: Now the rabbis** of the local court **will hear** that my slave is half emancipated **and they will cause me to lose him,** i.e., they will force me to release him, as is stated in the mishna (41b) that the court forces a master to release his slave who has been half emancipated. **He went** and **transferred ownership** of the slave **to his minor son,** who could not be forced by the court to emancipate him, so that the slave would remain in his possession.

The *Gemara* now turns to the well-known problem of the *chatzi eved v'chatzi ben chorin* — a slave who is half-owned by two masters, one of whom frees his share. As the *Mishnah* elsewhere teaches, this person is caught in an impossible situation: he cannot marry a free woman because he is half slave, and he cannot marry a slave woman because he is half free. The *tikkun olam* requires that the remaining owner be compelled to free his half as well, so that the slave can live as a complete person.

A case arose in which one owner sought to circumvent this compulsion. Knowing that the *Rabbanan* would force him to free his half of the slave, he transferred his half-ownership to his minor son. A *beis din* will not compel a minor to do anything; the minor cannot be made to write a *shtar shichrur*. As long as the son remains a minor, the slave remains trapped in his half-free, half-slave status — and the father has effectively protected his family's interest in the slave's labor and in any children the slave might produce.

The case was sent by *Rav Yosef the son of Rava* to *Rav Papa* for resolution.

### Guardian Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁלְחַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה כֵּן יֵעָשֶׂה לּוֹ, גְּמוּלוֹ יָשִׁיב לוֹ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ״ – אֲנַן קִים לַן בְּיָנוֹקָא דִּמְקָרְבָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ לְגַבֵּי זוּזֵי; מוֹקְמִינַן לֵיהּ אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס, וּמְקַרְקֵישׁ לֵיהּ זוּזֵי, וְכָתֵב לֵיהּ גִּיטָּא דְחֵירוּתָא עַל שְׁמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef, son of Rava, sent before Rav Pappa** the question of what the *halakha* is in this circumstance. **He sent to him** a response that paraphrased biblical verses: **As he has done, so shall it be done to him, his dealing shall return upon his own head** (see Leviticus 24:19 and Obadiah 1:15). In other words, since the master acted deceitfully to circumvent the ruling of the Sages, one should deal with him deceitfully. **We know that a child is attracted to money. We** will **appoint a steward for** the child, who will clarify the slave's market value, **and** the slave **should jingle the dinars** before **him.** The child will want the money and he will decide to emancipate him, **and they should write him a bill of emancipation in his name.**

*Rav Papa* responded with a creative solution: *ka'asher asah ken ye'aseh lo* — we will counter his maneuver with one of our own. Since children are susceptible to being won over by money — *d'mikarva datei l'gabei zuzei* — we appoint a *guardian*, an *apotropos*, and arrange for money to be jingled before the child, so that he does not object to the proceedings. A *shtar shichrur* is then written on behalf of the minor owner, freeing the slave.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

A debate between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* concerns precisely what role the *apotropos* plays. *Rashi* explains that the child himself writes the *shtar shichrur* — that a minor of sufficient age has the capacity to engage in such transactions — and the *apotropos* is appointed to ensure that a fair valuation is placed on the slave, since the slave will owe payment for his freedom and someone must protect the minor's financial interest in that transaction. *Tosafot*, citing *Rabbenu Shmuel* (the *Rashbam*), disagrees. According to *Tosafot*, a minor cannot affect personal status (*issur*), even if he has some capacity to engage in property transactions; the *apotropos* is therefore the one who actually writes the *shtar shichrur*, acting in the minor's stead. The money jingled before the child serves to prevent him from objecting or interfering. *Tosafot* further raises the question whether this authority of the *apotropos* to free a slave on behalf of the minor is grounded in the ordinary law of *apitropus*, or whether it is a special measure — *hefker beis din hefker* — ordained by *beis din* for this specific circumstance.

### Minor & Status

The dispute between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* crystallizes the core conceptual distinction. If manumission is primarily a property transaction, a minor of sufficient maturity might have enough legal capacity to accomplish it. If, however, manumission is fundamentally an act affecting personal status — an *issur* dimension rather than a *mamon* dimension — then a minor, who lacks the legal standing to affect another person's personal status, cannot write the *shtar shichrur* at all. *Tosafot*'s position that the *apotropos* writes the *shtar* reflects the view that manumission operates primarily in the domain of *issur*, and therefore requires an adult with full legal standing.

## Shtar Language

### Valid Formulas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, הָאוֹמֵר: ״עָשִׂיתִי פְּלוֹנִי עַבְדִּי בֶּן חוֹרִין״; ״עָשׂוּי בֶּן חוֹרִין״; ״הֲרֵי הוּא בֶּן חוֹרִין״ – הֲרֵי הוּא בֶּן חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** that with regard to **one who says: I made so-and-so my slave a freeman,** or: My slave **was made a freeman,** or: **Behold he is a freeman,** in all of these cases **he is a freeman** as a result.

A *braisa* is cited: *asiti ploni avdi ben chorin* — "I have made my slave free" (past tense); *asui ben chorin* — "he is made free" (present); *harei hu ben chorin* — "behold he is free" — all of these are valid formulations in a *shtar shichrur*. Even past tense, which might seem to describe something already done rather than to accomplish anything new, is read as: "through this document, I have made him free." All are operative.

### Future Tense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶעֱשֶׂנּוּ בֶּן חוֹרִין״ – רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: קָנָה, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא קָנָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְכוּלָּן – בִּשְׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** If he says: **I will make him a freeman, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The slave has **acquired** himself, **and the Rabbis say: He** has **not acquired** himself. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And all of these** *halakhot* apply only when the formulation was written **in a document** that was transferred to the slave. A verbal statement alone is not effective in emancipating the slave.

The dispute concerns the future tense: *a'asenu ben chorin* — "I will make him free." *Rebbi* holds that this works: the intent is that through this *shtar* he becomes free, even if expressed in the future tense. *Chachamim* disagree: this is merely a promise of future intent, not a present act of manumission, and therefore does not take effect.

### Language Irrelevant

*Rabi Yochanan* resolves the underlying issue with a sweeping principle: *v'kullan bi-shtar* — all of these are evaluated as language within a document. The document itself is the operative act; the linguistic tense within it is secondary. In a *shtar*, what matters is that the document formally accomplishes the manumission — not whether the verb is conjugated in the past, present, or future.

### Field Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, הָאוֹמֵר: ״נָתַתִּי שָׂדֶה פְּלוֹנִית לִפְלוֹנִי״; ״נְתוּנָה לִפְלוֹנִי״; ״הֲרֵי הִיא שֶׁלּוֹ״ – הֲרֵי הִיא שֶׁלּוֹ. ״אֶתְּנֶנָּה לִפְלוֹנִי״ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: קָנָה; וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא קָנָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְכוּלָּן – בִּשְׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **the Sages taught** that with regard to **one who says: I gave such and such a field to so-and-so** as a gift; or: This field is **given to so-and-so;** or: **Behold it is his,** in all of these cases **it belongs to** the recipient. If he says: **I will give it to so-and-so,** then **Rabbi Meir says:** That person has **acquired** the field. **And the Rabbis say: He** has **not acquired** it. With regard to this, **Rabbi Yoḥanan** also **says: And all of these** *halakhot* apply only when the formulation was written **in a document** that was transferred to the recipient. A verbal statement alone is not effective in transferring the field.

The identical analysis applies to a *shtar* transferring a field as a gift. *Nasati sadeh ploni l'ploni* — "I have granted this field" (past); *nesunah l'ploni* — "it is granted" (present); *harei hi shelo* — "behold it is his" — all are valid. *Etenenah l'ploni* — "I will give it" (future): *Rebbi* says it takes effect, *Chachamim* say it does not. And *Rabi Yochanan*'s principle applies here too: *v'kullan bi-shtar* — in the context of a document, the language accomplishes what it intends, regardless of tense.

## Delivery Disputes

### Secret Freeing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, הָאוֹמֵר: ״עָשִׂיתִי פְּלוֹנִי עַבְדִּי בֶּן חוֹרִין״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא עֲשָׂאַנִי״ – חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא זִיכָּה לוֹ עַל יְדֵי אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** that with regard to **one who says: I made so-and-so my slave a freeman, and** the slave **says: He did not make me** a freeman, then **we are concerned** that **perhaps** the master **transferred** the slave's emancipation **to him through another** person without the slave being aware of this, and the slave is assumed to be emancipated.

The *Gemara* turns to disputes over whether manumission actually occurred. If the owner says, "I have freed you," and the slave says, "You did not free me" — *chashinan shema zika lo al yedei acher* — we are concerned that the owner may in fact have freed him through a third party. Since delivering a *shtar shichrur* through an intermediary counts as delivery — receiving freedom is presumed to be a benefit, and a person can accept a benefit for another without the other's knowledge — the slave may have been freed without being aware of it. The slave's protestation cannot therefore be taken at face value.

### Direct Delivery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כָּתַבְתִּי וְנָתַתִּי לוֹ״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כָּתַב לִי וְלֹא נָתַן לִי״ – הוֹדָאַת בַּעַל דִּין כְּמֵאָה עֵדִים דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** However, if the master said: **I wrote and I gave to him** a bill of manumission, **and** the slave **says: He did not write** it **for me and he did not give me** a bill of manumission, then he remains a slave, as the legal status of **the admission of a litigant is similar to** the testimony of **one hundred witnesses.** Just as the testimony of witnesses is deemed credible by the court, so is the admission of a litigant deemed credible.

If the owner specifies: *kasavti v'nasati lo* — "I wrote a *shtar shichrur* and gave it directly to him" — and the slave responds: *kasav li v'lo nasan li* — "he wrote it but did not give it to me," or *lo kasav li v'lo nasan li* — "he neither wrote it nor gave it to me" — the *Gemara* invokes the principle:

### Slave Believed

*Hoda'as ba'al din k'me'ah edim dami* — the admission of a litigant counts as the testimony of one hundred witnesses. In this case, the slave is the litigant whose admission controls: he is admitting that he is not free, which hurts his own interest. The question of *whose* admission is operative here is subtle — both parties might seem to be making admissions against their own interest — but the *Gemara*'s ruling is that it is the slave who is believed. *Tosafot* explains: one can construct a plausible scenario in which the owner attempted to give the *shtar shichrur* but the slave did not truly consent to accept it, rendering the delivery ineffective. There is no comparable scenario in which the owner wrote and delivered the *shtar* without genuinely intending to do so. Since the slave's account is consistent with a plausible set of facts, he is credited, and the slave remains in his current status.

### Field Escrow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר: ״נָתַתִּי שָׂדֶה פְּלוֹנִית לִפְלוֹנִי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא נָתַן לִי״ – חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא זִיכָּה לוֹ עַל יְדֵי אַחֵר. ״כָּתַבְתִּי וְנָתַתִּי לוֹ״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כָּתַב וְלֹא נָתַן לִי״ – הוֹדָאַת בַּעַל דִּין כְּמֵאָה עֵדִים דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to **one who says: I gave such and such a field to so-and-so, and** the supposed recipient **says: He did not give me** it, then **we are concerned that perhaps he transferred** the field **to him through another** person without the recipient being aware of this, and the field becomes his. However, if one says: **I wrote and gave to him** a document stating that I am giving him the field, **and** the supposed recipient **says: He did not write** it **and did not give me** a document stating that he is giving me the field, then he does not take possession of the field, as the legal status of **the admission of a litigant is similar to** the testimony of **one hundred witnesses.**

The parallel case: if a person says, "I gave this field to so-and-so," and the recipient says, "You never gave it to me" — again *chashinan shema zika lo al yedei acher*, we are concerned that perhaps it was delivered through a third party. If the owner then specifies that he gave it directly, and the recipient denies it — *hoda'as ba'al din k'me'ah edim dami* — the recipient's denial is credited and the gift is not binding.

### Fruit Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת? רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: נוֹתֵן אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת; וְרַבָּה אָמַר: מְשַׁלְּשִׁין אֶת הַפֵּירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In this case, where the original owner states that he gave the field to his friend, and the supposed recipient states that he did not receive it, **who consumes the produce** of the field? **Rav Ḥisda says:** The **giver consumes the produce,** as the field remains in his possession, **and Rabba says: The produce is deposited with a third party** until it can be determined who the rightful owner is.

A further question arises in cases of uncertainty: *mi ochel peiros* — who benefits from the fruit of the field in the interim? *Rav Chisda* holds that the original owner (*nosen*) continues to eat the fruit — since we have concluded that the gift did not take effect, the field remains his. *Rabbah* holds that the fruit is placed in escrow — *meshalshein es ha-peiros* — held by a third party pending resolution.

### Father vs. Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: הָא בְּאַבָּא, הָא בִּבְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And they do not disagree.** Rather, they are referring to different cases: **This** case pertains **to the father,** the one who is the supposed recipient of the field. As long as he is alive and states that he did not receive the field, the produce is consumed by the original owner. **That** case pertains **to the son** of the supposed recipient of the field, who claims that his father did not receive it. Since it is possible that the father received it without the son's knowledge, the produce is deposited with a third party until it can be determined who the rightful owner is.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent dispute by distinguishing between cases. *Ha b'abba ha b'bra* — the two rulings refer to different scenarios. If it is the *father* — the original owner — who denies having received or accepted the gift, his denial is fully credited: he is the one most likely to know, and his admission against interest is entirely reliable. The field remains the gifter's, and the gifter retains the fruit. But if it is the *son* — an heir — who claims his father never received the gift, the son's knowledge is not as certain. Perhaps his father did receive it and never told him. In that case we cannot simply credit the son's denial, and the fruit is therefore held in escrow until the matter is clarified.
