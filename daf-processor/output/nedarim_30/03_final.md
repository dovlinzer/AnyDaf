# Nedarim 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bar Padda Doctrine

### Opening Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְתֵיב רַבִּי אָבִין וְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרַבִּי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, וְקָא מְנַמְנֵם רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, יָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: לְבַר פְּדָא דְּאָמַר פְּדָאָן חוֹזְרוֹת וְקוֹדְשׁוֹת,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Avin and Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rabbi, sat before Rabbi Yirmeya, and Rabbi Yirmeya was dozing [*menamnem*].** While he was dozing, **they sat and said: According to bar Padda, who said** that if he **redeems them** they **become consecrated again,**

We pick up at the bottom of 29b, in the middle of the fascinating *sugia* concerning the status of *konamot*. When one makes a *neder* using the word *konam* — a *kinuy*, a substitute term for *korban* — the object acquires something of the status of *korbanot*, at least according to Rabbi Meir, with respect to both *me'ilah* and the possibility of *pidyon*, redemption, whereby the sanctified status can be transferred to another object. The preceding discussion established that when one uses the word *konam* to render something forbidden, it is *assur* to all, not merely to a single individual.

The discussion that immediately precedes our passage concerns what happens when that sanctified status is assigned for a limited period of time. *Bar Padda* holds that the status cannot expire automatically; it can only be removed through an active act of *pidyon*. What, then, does a time-limiting phrase accomplish? According to *Bar Padda*, it means that the object is to be continuously and perpetually re-sanctified throughout that period, such that even a successful redemption is immediately overridden by the original statement.

### Continuous Re-sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם לֹא אָמַר ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו שְׁלָמִים״ — לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם עוֹלָה הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** If one says: This ox, after thirty days, is a burnt-offering, then **if he does not say: From now** it is **a peace-offering,** then **after thirty days it is a burnt-offering.**

*Bar Padda*'s principle is that *pade'an chozerot vekodshot* — if you redeem them, they re-acquire their sanctified status. A person cannot simply limit a *neder* to a fixed period and expect the status to dissolve automatically when that period ends, because sanctified status can only be removed through the act of *pidyon*. Accordingly, the phrase "until such-and-such a time" is interpreted not as a terminal condition but as an ongoing directive: I want these objects to continuously hold this status throughout that entire period. Any act of redemption during that interval causes the status to be immediately reinstated by virtue of the original declaration. The *dibbur* — the original statement — is understood as perpetually re-sanctifying the object for as long as the designated period has not elapsed.

### Saplings Example

The application is as follows: if one declared saplings *konam* until they are cut down, the phrase *ad sheyikatztzu* does not mean that the status dissolves at the moment of cutting. Rather, it means that throughout the entire period until they are cut down, the object is to maintain its *konam* status without interruption. Even if during that interval someone redeems the saplings and removes their sanctified status, the original declaration immediately reinstates that status. The act of redemption works conceptually — the status is genuinely removed for a moment — but the original statement simultaneously kicks the status right back in. Thus *ad sheyikatztzu* delineates the duration of this ongoing re-sanctification, not a terminal point at which the status naturally lapses.

## Two Rings Query

### Rav Oshaya Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּפְשׁוֹט דְּבָעֵי רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: הַנּוֹתֵן שְׁתֵּי פְרוּטוֹת לְאִשָּׁה וְאָמַר לָהּ: ״בְּאַחַת הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי הַיּוֹם, וּבְאַחַת הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי לְאַחַר שֶׁאֲגָרְשֵׁיךְ״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְהָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **resolve** the dilemma from here, **as Rav Hoshaya asked:** In the case of **one who gives two *perutot* to a woman and says to her: With one** of them **be betrothed to me today and with one be betrothed to me after I divorce you,** what is the *halakha*? Rav Hoshaya was uncertain whether the second betrothal is effective after the divorce. Bar Padda holds that if he redeems the consecrated saplings, they again become consecrated. Apparently, he holds that upon the redemption, the second consecration immediately goes into effect. From bar Padda's opinion, one could say: **So too,** here, after the first marriage is ended by the bill of divorce, the second betrothal that was previously performed takes effect, and **it should be** a valid **betrothal.**

At the top of 30a, the Gemara introduces the question that *Rav Oshaya* had posed. The case: a man gives two coins — two rings — to a woman, and says to her, "With this one, be betrothed to me today" (*be'achat hitkadshi li hayom*), and "With this other one, be betrothed to me after I divorce you" (*uve'achat hitkadshi li le'achar she'agareshich*). The question is whether the second act of *kiddushin* would take effect automatically upon the delivery of the *get*, based on the original declaration made at the time of the first *kiddushin*.

### Saplings Parallel

The two students sitting before *Rabbi Yirmiyah* — *Rabbi Avin* and *Rabbi Yitzchak berebi* — were discussing this question while their teacher dozed. They proposed that it be resolved from *Bar Padda*'s principle. Just as the saplings are continuously re-sanctified by virtue of the original declaration whenever the sanctified status is removed, so too here: when the man gives the woman her *get* and changes her status to that of a single woman, the act performed a week earlier should take effect. He gave her a second ring and stipulated that it serve as an act of *kiddushin* taking effect after the divorce. When that condition is met, why should the earlier act of *kiddushin* not now come into force?

### Single Act, Dual Effect

The logic of the parallel is this: just as one may say "let this ring take effect in thirty days" — and if neither party has changed position, *kiddushin* takes effect at that future time — so too one may designate a specific future circumstance as the triggering condition. The second ring is given to a woman not currently his wife — because one cannot perform *kiddushin* on one's existing wife — and the declaration is: let this be an act of *kiddushin* that takes effect after the divorce. This is structurally parallel to saying "after thirty days." What makes it equivalent to the saplings case is that one single *dibbur*, one statement made here and now, declares that at a future moment — after a change of status — the *kiddushin* should take hold. The statement re-sanctifies the saplings whenever they lose their status, just as this statement would re-betroth the woman whenever she loses the status of *eshet ish*. It is one act with a dual and temporally extended effect.

## Yirmiyah Distinction

### Yirmiyah Wakes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּעַר בְּהוּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי קָא מְדַּמֵּיתוּן פְּדָאָן הוּא לִפְדָאוּם אֲחֵרִים? הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פְּדָאָן הוּא — חוֹזְרוֹת וּקְדוֹשׁוֹת, פְּדָאוּם אֲחֵרִים — אֵין חוֹזְרוֹת וּקְדוֹשׁוֹת. וְאִשָּׁה כִּפְדָאוּהָ אֲחֵרִים דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya,** who had been dozing, **woke up** when he heard their conversation and **said to them:** For **what** reason **are you comparing** where **he redeemed them to where others redeemed them?** The *halakhot* are not similar. **This is what Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If **he redeemed** the saplings, **they become consecrated again,** but if **others redeemed them** before they were cut **they do not become consecrated again,** since they are not in his possession anymore, **and** the case of **a woman** given a bill of divorce from her husband **is considered as if others redeemed her.** This is because upon divorce she is completely independent, and the second marriage can therefore take effect only with her consent. But if she refuses, the betrothal is not valid.

At this point the Gemara delivers one of its memorable narrative moments: *itar behu Rabbi Yirmiyah* — Rabbi Yirmiyah woke up. Evidently hearing his students reason their way into what he considered an error was sufficient to rouse him. He challenged them: how can you compare this to the case of the saplings without distinguishing between *peda'am hu* — the owner redeeming them himself — and *peda'am acherim* — a third party redeeming them?

### Owner vs. Third Party

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁפְּדָאָן הוּא, אֲבָל פְּדָאוּם אֲחֵרִים — אֵין חוֹזְרוֹת וּקְדוֹשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated** that **Rabbi Ami said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: They taught only** that bar Padda holds that the saplings become consecrated again **when he redeemed them** himself, **but** when **others redeemed them they do not become consecrated again** for he cannot consecrate them after they have been in the possession of others, and it no longer depends on his intent.

*Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling, as transmitted by Rabbi Yirmiyah, draws precisely this distinction: *peda'am hu chozrot vekodshot* — if he himself redeems them, they become re-sanctified; *peda'am acherim ein chozrot vekodshot* — if others redeem them, they do not become re-sanctified.

### Control Determines Effect

The underlying logic is as follows. When one declares these objects sanctified and later redeems them, the act of redemption transfers the objects back into his own possession — he buys them back, as it were, from the *Beis HaMikdash*, and they come directly under his control once more. The moment they leave their sanctified status they return to his domain. His original statement, which declared that they should perpetually hold this status, can then take effect continuously, because he never loses control over them. His *dibbur* is operative at every moment and can govern the object's destiny because he owns it.

But if a third party — call him Michael — redeems the sanctified object, then at that moment of redemption the object passes into Michael's ownership. The original owner's statement cannot sanctify something belonging to someone else. Even if Michael subsequently sells it back to the original owner, it does not matter: there was a break, a period during which the object was entirely outside the original owner's control, and that break severs the chain. The original *dibbur* can no longer govern the object's status because its continuity was interrupted by an interval of third-party ownership.

### Woman as Third Party

The Gemara now applies this distinction to the woman: *veha'ishah kepeda'uhu acherim damya* — the woman is like one who was redeemed by a third party. When the husband gives the *get*, she is no longer under his control. The moment the *get* is delivered, she can marry whomever she chooses. Even if she continues to desire the reinstatement of the original *kiddushin*, even if she never expressed any wish to withdraw from the original agreement — it makes no difference. She is no longer under his control at that moment, and the object of the original act of *kiddushin* is therefore not within his dominion when the triggering condition is met. His original statement cannot determine her status because she is, at that moment, a fully independent entity — like an object that has passed into the possession of a third party. Just as Michael's subsequent willingness to return the object, or his declaration that he intends to give it back, cannot cause the original sanctification to re-activate at that moment, so too the woman's own continued consent cannot substitute for the legal requirement that the object remain within the original owner's sphere of control.

The point is that conceptually, *Bar Padda*'s mechanism of continuous re-sanctification works precisely because the owner redeems the object and it flows back into his control instantaneously — there is no actual temporal gap, though conceptually one act follows the other. The moment a third party intervenes and takes the object out of that chain, the chain is broken, and the original *dibbur* cannot regenerate itself. Since the wife, upon receiving the *get*, occupies exactly that position, the original act of *kiddushin* cannot reassert itself.

The Gemara then notes: *itmar nami* — this same ruling was stated independently: *amar Rabbi Ami amar Rabbi Yochanan* — *lo shanu ela shepedao hu*, the re-sanctification applies only when he himself redeems it; *aval peda'u acherim, ein chozeret lekidushatah* — if others redeemed it, it does not return to its sanctified status.

## Ran's Challenge

### Ran's Question

The Gemara's conclusion is analytically clean, but the *Ran* raises a significant difficulty that deserves careful attention — particularly given how widely it is cited in discussions of *kiddushin*. The *Ran* asks: I understand that after the *get* is delivered, the woman is no longer under the husband's control. But why should that matter? Two people are parties to the act of *kiddushin*. She is one of those parties. She agreed here. Why is her continued consent — her continued status as an active participant — not sufficient to keep the original act operative?

### Dual Participation

The *Ran* frames the objection this way: if both the man and the woman are understood as parallel participants in *kiddushin* — both agreeing to be married, both actors in a joint agreement — then even after the *get*, she remains an active subject who still wants the *kiddushin* to take effect. She is not in some third party's control; she is in her own control and she still wants the marriage to be reinstated. Why is she treated as though she were an object that has been redeemed by an outsider, rather than as a co-participant whose ongoing consent keeps the chain intact?

### Object vs. Subject

The *Ran* resolves his own question, and in doing so, exposes the conceptual framework underlying the Gemara's entire comparison. The Gemara looks at the woman as the *object* upon whom the act of *kiddushin* is performed, not as a *subject* who performs it. It is, in the Gemara's framework, the man's act — something done *to* her or *upon* her — and the relevant question is always whether that object remains within the actor's control. Since the question is framed entirely from the man's perspective, the moment she exits his domain, it is irrelevant that she herself remains under her own control and continues to consent.

The *Ran* states this explicitly. The Torah says *ki yikach ish isha* — "when a man takes a woman" — and not *ki teilakach isha le'ish* — "when a woman takes herself to a man." She does not have the legal capacity to enact *kiddushin* upon herself. She can consent or withhold consent, and her consent is necessary, but she is not the actor. The man takes her; she is taken. *Lav kol heimena she-tachnis atzmah le-reshut ha-ba'al* — it is not within her power to bring herself into the husband's domain through her own act. She can agree to his act, she can refuse it, but the act itself is his and his alone. For this reason, even though *kiddushin* requires her consent, she is conceptually positioned as the object of his act rather than an equal actor alongside him.

Consequently, the comparison to the saplings is fully apt. The saplings are the object; as long as they remain in the owner's control, his original *dibbur* governs their status. The woman is the object; as long as she remains in the husband's domain, his original *dibbur* can govern her status. Once the *get* removes her from his domain, she is exactly like the sanctified object that has passed into a third party's hands — and the chain is severed.

### Evolution of Status

It is worth noting that this framework has evolved significantly over time. In the Torah's original structure, the woman was entirely an object — she required no consent whatsoever, and the operative legal actor was her father, who transferred her. After *bat mitzvah*, once she passed beyond her father's control, the woman could not be given in marriage without her own agreement; but conceptually, as the *Ran* makes clear, the model remained that the man is the actor and the woman is the one acted upon. She must agree, but she does not marry; he marries her.

By the era of the *Chatam Sofer*, within the last two centuries, even a traditionalist posek felt the need to reconceptualize the institution: he framed *kiddushin* as a bilateral contractual agreement in which both parties take one another, entering as equal actors into a mutual exchange. This represents a meaningful shift in the underlying model, even if the formal legal acts remain structurally unchanged.

## Seafarers Category

### Mishnah Seafarers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַנּוֹדֵר מִיּוֹרְדֵי הַיָּם — מוּתָּר בְּיוֹשְׁבֵי הַיַּבָּשָׁה. מִיּוֹשְׁבֵי הַיַּבָּשָׁה — אָסוּר מִיּוֹרְדֵי הַיָּם, שֶׁיּוֹרְדֵי הַיָּם בִּכְלַל יוֹשְׁבֵי הַיַּבָּשָׁה. לֹא כְּאֵלּוּ שֶׁהוֹלְכִים מֵעַכּוֹ לְיָפוֹ, אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לְפָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who takes a vow** that he will not derive benefit **from seafarers,** he is **permitted** to benefit **from those who live on dry land.** But if he takes a vow not to derive benefit **from those who live on dry land,** he is also **prohibited** from deriving benefit **from seafarers, because seafarers are included** within the category of **those who live on dry land.** The mishna now defines seafarers: **Not like those that travel** by ship **from Akko to Jaffa,** which is a short trip, **but rather one who customarily departs [*lefaresh*]** to distant locations, e.g., foreign countries.

The Gemara now shifts to a different and more linguistically focused portion of the *perek*, concerning the scope of generic terms used in *nedarim*. The *Mishnah* states: *ha-noder mi-yordei ha-yam mutar beyoshvei hayabashah* — one who vows against *yordei ha-yam*, seafarers, is permitted to derive benefit from *yoshvei hayabashah*, those who dwell on the dry land. Conversely, *mi-yoshvei hayabashah assur mi-yordei ha-yam* — one who vows against land-dwellers is forbidden from seafarers as well, since seafarers are a subset of land-dwellers. The Mishnah then adds: *lo ka'elu sheholchin me'Akko le'Yafo* — this does not refer to those who travel from *Akko* to *Yafo*, but *ela bemi shedarko lefaresh* — only to those who undertake far journeys.

### Two Readings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא, חַד מַתְנֵי אַרֵישָׁא וְחַד מַתְנֵי אַסֵּיפָא. מַאן דְּתָנֵי אַרֵישָׁא, מַתְנֵי הָכִי: הַנּוֹדֵר מִיּוֹרְדֵי הַיָּם — מוּתָּר בְּיוֹשְׁבֵי יַבָּשָׁה, הָא בְּיוֹרְדֵי הַיָּם — אָסוּר, וְלֹא כְּאֵלּוּ
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the mishna's definition of seafarers, there is a dispute between **Rav Pappa and Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika. One teaches** this statement **with regard to the first clause** of the mishna, **and one teaches** it **with regard to the latter clause.** The Gemara explains: **The one who teaches** it **with regard to the first clause teaches** it **like this: One who takes a vow** not to derive benefit **from seafarers** is **permitted** to derive benefit **from those who live on dry land. But** he is **prohibited** from deriving benefit **from seafarers, and** seafarers are **not like those**

*Rav Pappa* and *Rav Acha brei de-Rav Ika* dispute to which clause of the Mishnah this final line attaches. *Chad matni areisha*, one applies it to the opening clause; *chad matni aseifa*, the other applies it to the closing clause.

### Short-trip Sailors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוֹלְכִים מֵעַכּוֹ לְיָפוֹ, דְּהָלֵין יוֹשְׁבֵי הַיַּבָּשָׁה נִינְהוּ, אֶלָּא מִמִּי שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לְפָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **who travel from Akko to Jaffa, for they are** treated like **those who dwell on the land. Rather,** the term seafarers means he took a vow that deriving benefit **from those who customarily depart** out to sea is forbidden to him.

The one who reads the final line as a gloss on the opening clause understands it as follows: when the Mishnah says that a vow against seafarers does not include land-dwellers, it also specifies what counts as a seafarer in the first place. Short-distance maritime travelers — those who regularly make the crossing from *Akko* to *Yafo* — are not seafarers in the legally meaningful sense, even if they travel by water daily. Only those who undertake genuinely far-reaching ocean journeys qualify as *yordei ha-yam*. Accordingly, the local ferry passenger remains in the category of *yoshvei hayabashah*, and a vow against seafarers does not reach him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּמַתְנֵי אַסֵּיפָא, מַתְנֵי הָכִי: הַנּוֹדֵר מִיּוֹשְׁבֵי יַבָּשָׁה — אָסוּר בְּיוֹרְדֵי הַיָּם, וְלֹא בְּאֵלּוּ הַהוֹלְכִים מֵעַכּוֹ לְיָפוֹ בִּלְבַד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ בְּמִי שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לְפָרֵשׁ, הוֹאִיל וְסוֹפוֹ לְיַבָּשָׁה סָלֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** **And the one who teaches it with regard to the latter clause** of the mishna **teaches** in **this** manner: **One who takes a vow** not to derive benefit **from those who dwell on dry land is prohibited from** deriving benefit from **seafarers, and** this is the *halakha* **not only with regard to those who travel from Akko to Jaffa,** who are certainly not considered seafarers, **but even with regard to one who customarily departs** to great distances. Why is such a person also considered a dweller on dry land? **Since eventually he will go up onto dry land.** No one lives his entire life at sea. Eventually, one will reach dry land, so all people are called dwellers on dry land.

The one who reads the final line as a gloss on the closing clause understands it differently. When the Mishnah says that *yoshvei hayabashah* includes *yordei ha-yam*, one might think this applies only to those who make short trips and effectively live on land. The Mishnah therefore comes to teach: do not think that only the *Akko*-to-*Yafo* traveler is subsumed under land-dwellers — even those who make extended ocean voyages are ultimately *yoshvei hayabashah*, since *sofo leyabashah salik*, eventually they return to land. On this reading, the scope of *yoshvei hayabashah* is maximally inclusive, encompassing even long-distance seafarers. The two readings thus work in opposite directions: one restricts the category of *yordei ha-yam* to far-journey travelers only, while the other expands the category of *yoshvei hayabashah* to absorb even those same long-journey travelers. The *Rishonim* note further that even where the Mishnah establishes that far-journey sailors qualify as *yordei ha-yam*, it does not fully specify the required regularity of travel — a separate question that the authorities address.

This discussion also touches on a deeper methodological issue that will become explicit shortly: when a person uses a term without specifying his meaning, what criterion determines its legal scope? One possibility is to look to *lashon Torah* — the way the Torah uses the term, functioning as a kind of authoritative lexicon. Another is to follow *lashon bnei adam* — the way people actually use the word in common speech, regardless of what the formal or biblical usage would dictate.

## Sun Seers

### Blind Included

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַנּוֹדֵר מֵרוֹאֵי הַחַמָּה — אָסוּר אַף בַּסּוֹמִין, שֶׁלֹּא נִתְכַּוֵּון זֶה אֶלָּא לְמִי שֶׁהַחַמָּה רוֹאָה אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who takes a vow** not to derive benefit **from those who see the sun** is **prohibited** from deriving benefit **even from** the **blind,** although they see nothing. This is **because he meant only** to include all **those that the sun sees,** i.e., shines upon with light.

The next *Mishnah* presents a striking example of non-literal interpretation. *Ha-noder me-ro'ei ha-chamah* — one who vows against "those who see the sun" — *assur af ba-sumin*, is forbidden from deriving benefit even from blind people. On the surface, a blind person plainly does not see the sun. Nevertheless, the vow applies to them.

### Reinterpreted Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? מִדְּלָא קָאָמַר ״מִן הָרוֹאִין״. לְאַפּוֹקֵי דָּגִים וְעוּבָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara explains why the mishna states that blind people are included: **What is the reason** for this? **Since he did not say: From those who see,** which would exclude blind people. Instead, he employed the phrase: Those who see the sun, which comes **to exclude fish and fetuses,** who do not see the sun. Consequently, the vow is interpreted to refer to those who are exposed to the sun, including the blind.

The explanation is that the person did not intend the phrase literally. *Shelo nitkaven zeh ela lemi shehachamah ro'ah otan* — what he meant was not "those who see the sun" but "those whom the sun sees." The Gemara notes: *midela ka'amar min haro'im* — had he meant only those with the faculty of sight, he would simply have said "those with sight." The fact that he invoked the sun suggests that the operative criterion is exposure to sunlight, not the possession of vision. Accordingly, the phrase is reinterpreted: the sun sees them, they are in the world illuminated by the sun, and therefore the vow applies regardless of whether they themselves can perceive it.

*Tosafot* adds a precision: since the phrase still contains the word "those who see," it operates within the universe of beings with sight. Inanimate objects like fruit and plants are also "seen" by the sun, but they are excluded because the language of seeing implies reference to animate creatures. Within the universe of people and animals, the criterion is not whether they literally see the sun, but whether they are exposed to it.

### Fish and Fetuses

*La'apokei dagim ve-ubarim* — the interpretation serves to exclude fish and fetuses. Fish, submerged in water, are not exposed to the sun in the relevant sense; and fetuses, not yet born into the world, are similarly not "seen" by the sun. These two categories therefore fall outside the vow even on the reinterpreted understanding of the phrase.

## Black-headed Men

### Mishnah Black-headed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַנּוֹדֵר מִשְּׁחוֹרֵי הָרֹאשׁ — אָסוּר בַּקֵּרְחִין, וּבַעֲלִי שֵׂיבוֹת. וּמוּתָּר בַּנָּשִׁים וּבַקְּטַנִּים, שֶׁאֵין נִקְרָאִין ״שְׁחוֹרֵי הָרֹאשׁ״ אֶלָּא אֲנָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who takes a vow** not to derive benefit **from** those that have **dark heads [*sheḥorei harosh*]** is **prohibited** from deriving benefit **from those that are bald,** although they have no hair at all, **and** from the **elderly** who have white hair. This is because the term is not to be understood in its simple meaning but rather in a broader manner. **But** he is **permitted** to derive benefit **from women and from children, because only men are called:** Those with **dark heads.**

The following *Mishnah* addresses another term whose conventional usage diverges from what a literal reading would suggest. *Ha-noder mi-shcharei ha-rosh* — one who vows against "the black-headed" — *asur be-kerachim u-va'alei seivot*, is forbidden from deriving benefit even from bald men and from men with white hair. But *mutar be-nashim u-ve-ktanim* — he is permitted to derive benefit from women and from minors. Why? *She-ein nikra'im shchorei ha-rosh ela anashim* — because the term *shchorei ha-rosh*, "black-headed," is not a literal description of hair color but rather a generic designation for men, as opposed to women and children.

### Men Not Hair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא — מִדְּלָא קָאָמַר ״מִבַּעְלֵי שֵׂעָר״. וּמוּתָּר בְּנָשִׁים וּבִקְטַנִּים, שֶׁאֵין נִקְרָאִין ״שְׁחוֹרֵי הָרֹאשׁ״ אֶלָּא אֲנָשִׁים. מַאי טַעְמָא — אֲנָשִׁים זִימְנִין דְּמִיכַּסּוּ רֵישַׁיְיהוּ וְזִימְנִין דְּמִגַּלּוּ רֵישַׁיְיהוּ. אֲבָל נָשִׁים לְעוֹלָם מִיכַּסּוּ, וּקְטַנִּים לְעוֹלָם מִיגַּלּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What is the reason** that the term dark heads does not exclude those that are bald? **Because it does not say: From those with hair.** The mishna states: **But** he is **permitted** to derive benefit **from women and from children, because only men are called:** Those with **dark heads.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? **Men sometimes cover their heads and sometimes uncover their heads.** They can be called dark heads since, for the most part, they have dark hair which is often uncovered. **But women's** heads **are always covered, and children's** heads **are always uncovered,** and the expression dark heads is referring to men whose hair is sometimes seen.

This is initially counterintuitive. If the term meant what it literally says — people with black hair — then it would include women and girls with dark hair, and exclude men who happen to be bald or gray. Yet the Mishnah says exactly the opposite: bald men and white-haired men are included, while women and children are excluded. Clearly, the Gemara explains, *shchorei ha-rosh* is not functioning as a descriptor of hair color at all. It is functioning as a category label for men.

### Coverage Pattern

The Gemara explains why this term came to mean "men." *Anashim zimna dimichasu reisha'eihu ve-zimna dimiglu reisha'eihu* — men sometimes cover their heads and sometimes leave them uncovered. When their heads are uncovered, their hair is visible; and since the hair of Jewish men tends, more often than not, toward black or dark brown, the visible hair of men became associated with the category of men itself. The phrase *shchorei ha-rosh* thus came to denote those whose hair is sometimes visible — i.e., men.

*Aval nashim le'olam michasu* — women's hair is always covered, so one would not refer to its color as a categorical descriptor. *Ve-ktanim le'olam miglu* — minors' hair is always uncovered. If one wanted to refer to minors, one would say "those with uncovered heads," not "black-headed." If one wanted to refer to women, one would say "those with covered heads." The category where hair is *sometimes* visible — sometimes covered, sometimes not — is the category of adult men. And so the phrase *shchorei ha-rosh* becomes a term of art for that category, not a statement about pigmentation. A bald man and a white-haired man are both men whose hair is sometimes visible; they fall within the category. Women and children are defined by an invariant covering pattern and thus fall outside it.

### Minor Girls Hair

One question the Gemara leaves open is whether the *ktanim* whose hair is *le'olam miglu* — always uncovered — refers only to young boys, or includes young girls as well. *Tosafot* addresses this directly: *ktanim le'olam miglu bein zacharim bein nekeivot* — even young girls, before marriage, have their hair uncovered as a regular practice. *Tosafot* states that *nekeivot ktanot she-ein nesuot* — unmarried minor girls — customarily keep their heads uncovered. On this reading, young girls fall into the "always uncovered" category alongside boys, and both are excluded from the designation *shchorei ha-rosh*, which remains specific to adult men. The question of precisely how to read the Gemara here — whether *ktanim* encompasses girls whose hair is also uncovered — is one the *Rishonim* engage with, and *Tosafot*'s position is that it does.

## Birth Categories

### Yeludim Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַיְּלוּדִים — מוּתָּר בְּנוֹלָדִים. מִן הַנּוֹלָדִים — אָסוּר מִן הַיְּלוּדִים. רַבִּי מֵאִיר מַתִּיר אַף בַּיְּלוּדִים. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא נִתְכַּוֵּון זֶה אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לְהִוּוֹלֵד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who takes a vow** not to derive benefit **from those that are born [*yeludim*]** is **permitted** to derive benefit from **those who will be born [*noladim*]** after the time of the vow. But if one takes a vow not to derive benefit **from those who will be born,** he is also **prohibited** from deriving benefit **from those that are** already **born** at the time of the vow. **Rabbi Meir permits** deriving benefit **even from those that are** already **born** at the time of the vow because he holds that the one taking the vow was precise in prohibiting only those that will be born. **And the Rabbis say: He intended** to include with this expression **only one whose nature is to be born.** Therefore, both those who will be born and those who were already born are included in the vow.

The final *Mishnah* on the daf addresses a different categorical question: the distinction between *yeludim* — those who have already been born — and *noladim* — a term whose scope is disputed. The Mishnah opens: *ha-noder min ha-yeludim mutar ba-noladim* — one who vows against *yeludim*, those who have been born, is permitted to derive benefit from *noladim*, those yet to be born. *Yeludim* thus refers unambiguously to the past — those currently alive by virtue of having already been born.

### Noladim Mishnah

The Mishnah continues: *min ha-noladim assur af ba-yeludim* — one who vows against *noladim* is forbidden from both the past-born and the future-born. This represents the position of the *Tanna Kamma*: the word *noladim* is broad enough to encompass all those who come into the world through birth, whether they have already done so or will do so in the future.

### Rabbi Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא נוֹלָדִים. אֶלָּא מִמַּאן אָסוּר?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara comments: **According to Rabbi Meir,** in the case of one who takes a vow that deriving benefit from those who will be born is forbidden to him, the *halakha* is that he is permitted to derive benefit even from those who are already born at the time of the vow. **And** the mishna's use of the term: Even, indicates that **it is not necessary** to say that those who **will be born** are permitted to him. The Gemara asks: **However,** if that is the case, **from whom is he prohibited** to derive benefit? The vow appears to have no effect.

*Rabbi Meir* disagrees. He holds that *noladim* refers exclusively to those who will be born in the future. Just as *yeludim* means "born in the past," *noladim* means "to be born in the future," and the two terms are strictly symmetrical and mutually exclusive. On his reading, a vow against *noladim* does not reach those who have already been born.

### Chachamim Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַיְּלוּדִים — מוּתָּר בַּנּוֹלָדִים, מִן הַנּוֹלָדִים — אָסוּר בַּיְּלוּדִים, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אַף הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַנּוֹלָדִים מוּתָּר בַּיְּלוּדִים, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנוֹדֵר מִן הַיְּלוּדִים מוּתָּר בַּנּוֹלָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna **is incomplete and is teaching like this: One who takes a vow** not to derive benefit **from those that are born** is **permitted** to derive benefit from **those who will be born** after the time of the vow. But if one takes a vow not to derive benefit **from those who will be born,** he is also **prohibited** from deriving benefit **from those that are** already **born** at the time of the vow. **Rabbi Meir says: Even one who takes a vow** not to derive benefit **from those who will be born** is **permitted** to derive benefit **from those who are** already **born, just as one who takes a vow** not to derive benefit **from those who are born** is **permitted** to derive benefit **from those who will be born,** because Rabbi Meir claims that the one taking the vow was precise in his words.

The *Chachamim* maintain that *lo nitkaven zeh ela bemi shedarko lehivaled* — the word *noladim* denotes those whose nature it is to come into the world through birth, without temporal limitation. Whether one has already been born or will be born, one falls under this description. The *Chachamim* thus treat *noladim* as a term that transcends tense, encompassing all those who enter the world through the act of birth, past and future alike.

### Rav Pappa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: לְמֵימְרָא דְּ״נוֹלָדִים״ דְּמִתְיַילְּדָן מַשְׁמַע? אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה ״שְׁנֵי בָנֶיךָ הַנּוֹלָדִים לְךָ בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְאִיתְיַילְדָן הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the distinction between the terms in the mishna, **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: Is this to say that** the word ***noladim* means those who will be born** in the future? **But if that is so,** it says in the verse: **"Your two sons who were born [*noladim*] to you in the land of Egypt"** (Genesis 48:5), **does it also** mean **those who will be born?** The verse is referring to Manasseh and Ephraim, who were already alive.

*Rav Pappa* challenged *Abaye* with a question directed at the premise underlying *Rabbi Meir*'s position. If *noladim* means only those who will be born — *de-miya'aldin* — then how do we explain the verse *shenei vanecha ha-noladim lecha be'eretz mitzrayim* — "your two sons who were born to you in the land of Egypt"? There, *noladim* clearly refers to children already born in the past. This seems to refute *Rabbi Meir*'s contention that *noladim* means only future-born.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי דִּיילִידוּ מַשְׁמַע, אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה דִּכְתִיב ״הִנֵּה בֵן נוֹלָד לְבֵית דָּוִד יֹאשִׁיָּהוּ שְׁמוֹ״, הָכִי נָמֵי דַּהֲוָה? וְהָא עֲדַיִין מְנַשֶּׁה לֹא בָּא? אֶלָּא מַשְׁמַע הָכִי וּמַשְׁמַע הָכִי, וּבִנְדָרִים הַלֵּךְ אַחַר לְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **But rather, what** should one say; **that** the expression **means those already born? However, if that is so,** that **which is written: "Behold, a son shall be born [*nolad*] to the house of David, Josiah by name"** (I Kings 13:2), is the meaning **also that he is already** born? **But Manasseh had not yet come** into this world, and certainly not his grandson Josiah. **Rather,** sometimes the word **means this,** those already born, **and** sometimes **means that,** those who are not yet born, **and with regard to vows, follow the colloquial language,** in which the word *noladim* is used to mean those who are not yet born, so the vow is interpreted in this manner.

The Gemara pushes back: perhaps *noladim* means only the past? That too cannot be sustained, for the verse *hineh ben nolad le-veit David, Yoshiyahu shemo* — "behold, a son will be born to the house of David, Yoshiyahu is his name" — uses *nolad* to refer to a birth that has not yet occurred, a prediction made before even *Menashe* had been born. The Gemara thus demonstrates elegantly that *nolad* in the Torah carries neither an exclusively past nor an exclusively future meaning. *Ela mashma hachi u-mashma hachi* — it means both, depending on context.

### Common Usage Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים, לֹא נִתְכַּוֵּין זֶה אֶלָּא מִמִּי שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לְהִוּוֹלֵד. לְאַפּוֹקֵי מַאי — לְאַפּוֹקֵי דָּגִים וְעוֹפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: **And the Rabbis say: He intended** to include with this expression **only one whose nature is to be born.** The Gemara asks: **What does** this term **exclude?** The Gemara answers: It serves **to exclude fish and birds,** which are not born but are hatched from eggs, whereas the word *noladim* means those born from their mother's womb.

This demonstration supports the *Chachamim*'s position that *noladim* encompasses both past and future. But it raises the question of how *Rabbi Meir* can maintain that *noladim* means only the future. The Gemara's answer is the ruling principle: *u-vindarim halach achar lashon bnei adam* — in matters of *nedarim*, we do not follow the biblical or formally authoritative meaning of a word, but rather the way that word is actually used by people in ordinary speech.

### Usage Comparison

The *Chachamim* hold that *lashon bnei adam* here tracks *lashon ha-Torah*: people use *noladim* in the same broad, tenseless way the Torah does, meaning both those who have been born and those who will be born. *Rabbi Meir* holds that *lashon bnei adam* has diverged from *lashon Torah*: in ordinary spoken usage, *noladim* means only those who will be born — the future tense — while the past is expressed by *yeludim*. People use the two terms as a clean past/future distinction, regardless of how the Torah deploys them. The debate, at its core, is an empirical one about how speakers actually use these words.

### Fish and Birds

Finally, the Gemara asks what the word *noladim* — on the *Chachamim*'s broad reading — comes to exclude. *La'apokei dagim ve-ofot* — fish and birds. These creatures do not come into the world through live birth; they hatch from eggs. The act of hatching is categorically distinct from *leivaled*, from being born, and therefore fish and birds fall entirely outside the scope of *noladim*, whichever temporal interpretation one adopts.
