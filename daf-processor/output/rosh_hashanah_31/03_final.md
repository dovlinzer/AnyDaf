# Rosh Hashanah 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tamid Service Structure

### Morning Tamid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה קִלְקוּל קִלְקְלוּ הַלְוִיִּם בַּשִּׁיר? הָכָא תַּרְגִּימוּ: שֶׁלֹּא אָמְרוּ שִׁירָה כׇּל עִיקָּר. רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: שֶׁאָמְרוּ שִׁירָה שֶׁל חוֹל עִם תָּמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What error did the Levites make with regard to the song** they were supposed to recite? The Gemara answers: **Here,** in Babylonia, **they interpreted that they did not recite any song at all,** as they did not know which psalm should be sung, the one for an ordinary weekday or the special one for the Festival. **Rabbi Zeira said:** Their mistake was **that they recited the song of** an ordinary **weekday with the daily afternoon offering.** After the witnesses testified, it became clear that they should have recited the psalm of the Festival.

The *daf* before us is 31b, picking up approximately fourteen lines from the bottom of the page with the word *mizeh*. The *mishnah* had established that once — when witnesses had not arrived on *Rosh Hashanah* — the *Levi'im* said the wrong *shira* at the afternoon *tamid*. As a result, the Rabbis instituted a *takana* not to accept witnesses after *mincha* time, so that if they had not arrived by then, everyone would know that the day would not be sanctified. Even so, people refrained from *melacha* for the remainder of the day, so that the day would not be treated lightly in subsequent years — the concept of *kedusha achat* — though that is not our present focus.

Before proceeding, it is worth noting that with this *mishnah*, we have established that the topic of *Kiddush HaChodesh* appears in every single *perek* of *Rosh Hashanah*: it concluded the first *perek*, ran through the entirety of the second, opened the third (*ra'u beit din v'chol Yisrael*), and now in the fourth *perek* it resurfaces again. This is almost certainly not coincidental. Calendar control was one of the central points of contention between the *Perushim* and the *Tzedukim*, and whoever controlled the calendar controlled the entire liturgical life of the community. The repeated prominence of *Kiddush HaChodesh* throughout the tractate reflects how high the stakes were.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּמִיד שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה שַׁחֲרִית קָרֵב כְּהִלְכָתוֹ, בְּמוּסָף מַהוּ אוֹמֵר — "הַרְנִינוּ לֵאלֹהִים עוּזֵּנוּ הָרִיעוּ לֵאלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב", בְּמִנְחָה מַהוּ אוֹמֵר — "קוֹל ה׳ יָחִיל מִדְבָּר".
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to the daily offering on Rosh HaShana,** in **the morning it is sacrificed in accordance with its** regular ***halakhot*,** i.e., the Levites recite the regular psalm for that day of the week. When it comes **to the additional offering** of Rosh HaShana, **what** psalm **does one recite?** The psalm that includes the verse: **"Sing aloud to God our strength; shout to the God of Jacob"** (Psalms 81:2). With regard **to the** daily **afternoon** offering, **what** psalm **does one recite?** The psalm that includes the verse: **"The voice of the Lord shakes the wilderness"** (Psalms 29:8).

The *Gemara* opens this passage with a challenge from Rav Acha bar Huna. The *baraita* he cites describes precisely what would be done with the *shir shel yom* — the daily psalm — on *Rosh Hashanah*. At the morning *tamid*, when witnesses had almost never yet arrived to testify to the new moon, the practice was to recite the ordinary *shir shel yom* for that day of the week, exactly as recorded in our *siddur* for each weekday. The *yom tov* identity of *Rosh Hashanah* was not yet reflected in the morning *tamid*.

### Musaf Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִזְמַן שֶׁחָל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לִהְיוֹת בַּחֲמִישִׁי בַּשַּׁבָּת, שֶׁהַשִּׁירָה שֶׁלּוֹ "הַרְנִינוּ לֵאלֹהִים עוּזֵּנוּ" — לֹא הָיָה אוֹמֵר בְּשַׁחֲרִית "הַרְנִינוּ", מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחוֹזֵר וְכוֹפֵל אֶת הַפֶּרֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **And when Rosh HaShana occurs on a Thursday, whose** regular **psalm** even on an ordinary weekday **is: "Sing aloud to God our strength,"** and the witnesses came before the daily morning offering was sacrificed, **one would not recite: "Sing aloud** to God our strength; shout to the God of Jacob" **with the** daily **morning** offering, **because one goes back and repeats that section** at the time of the additional offering.

Once witnesses arrived and it became clear that the day was indeed *Rosh Hashanah*, a *musaf* offering would be brought, and the *shir* sung at *musaf* was *Harninu l'Elohim uzenu* — the opening of Psalm 81. The association is evident: the *mizmor* contains the verse *tik'u vachodesh shofar bakese l'yom chagenu ki chok l'Yisrael hu mishpat l'Elohei Yaakov* — "Blow the *shofar* at the new moon, at the appointed time for our festival, for it is a statute for Israel, a judgment of the God of Jacob." This psalm was the fixed *shir* for *musaf* on *Rosh Hashanah*.

### Afternoon Tamid

At the afternoon *tamid*, the *shir* recited was *Kol Hashem yachil midbar* — Psalm 29. The running theme of that psalm is the voice of God reverberating through all of creation: *kol Hashem bakoach, kol Hashem behadar, kol Hashem shover arazim* — the voice of God in power, in splendor, shattering cedars. *Rashi* adds that it evokes *zechut kol shofar shel matan Torah* — the merit of the *shofar*'s voice at Sinai, the mountain trembling as all of nature trembles before the divine voice. The thematic connection to *Rosh Hashanah* is thus twofold: the motif of *kol* and *teruah*, and perhaps even an echo of *ma'aseh bereshit* imagery, the sound of God causing all creation to shudder.

### Thursday Rosh Hashanah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַהוּ אוֹמֵר — "הֲסִירוֹתִי מִסֵּבֶל שִׁכְמוֹ". וְאִם בָּאוּ עֵדִים אַחַר תָּמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר, אוֹמֵר "הַרְנִינוּ" אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחוֹזֵר וְכוֹפֵל אֶת הַפֶּרֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what does one recite? "I removed his shoulder from the burden"** (Psalms 81:7), which is referring to Joseph, who was set free from prison on Rosh HaShana. In other words, the second half of Psalm 81 was recited with the morning offering, while the first half was recited with the additional offering. **And if the witnesses came** on a Thursday **after the daily morning offering** had already been sacrificed, **one recites: "Sing aloud** to God" at the additional offering, **even though** this means **that one goes back and repeats that section** again. This concludes the *baraita*.

A more complex situation arises when *Rosh Hashanah* falls on Thursday — which does occur in our calendar, and is precisely why one can encounter a three-day *yom tov* sequence in *Tishrei*. The difficulty is that the regular *shir shel yom* for Thursday is *Harninu l'Elohim uzenu* — the very same psalm designated as the fixed *shir* for *musaf* on *Rosh Hashanah*. If that psalm is recited at *shacharit* as the ordinary weekday *shir*, what is there left to say at *musaf*?

The *baraita* resolves this by dividing Psalm 81 into two halves. The *musaf* retains the first half — the section containing *tik'u vachodesh shofar* — which is the essential *Rosh Hashanah* *shir* and therefore must remain fixed at *musaf*. The *shacharit* then uses the second half of the psalm, beginning *hasiroti misevel shichmo* — "I removed the burden from his shoulder" — which speaks of *Yosef* going out over the land of Egypt, a resonance with *Rosh Hashanah* in a different register: the tradition that *Yosef* was released from prison on *Rosh Hashanah*, as discussed in the first *perek*. So in the morning one recites the second half, concerning *Yosef's* liberation; at *musaf* one recites the first half, concerning the *shofar* blast of the new moon.

This, of course, presupposes that one knows by *shacharit* that it is *Rosh Hashanah*. A student raises the pointed question: how could one know by the time of the morning *tamid*, given that the court would not have been examining witnesses before sunrise? The answer is that in practice one almost never would know at that point — and that is precisely the point. The default practice was always to say the ordinary *shir shel yom* at *shacharit* even on *Rosh Hashanah*, since the witnesses would virtually never arrive that early. The division of the psalm into two halves applies when one happens to know in advance, or when witnesses are processed with exceptional speed. The *baraita* addresses the case where witnesses arrive after *shacharit* has already concluded: *ve-im ba'u eidim achar tamid shel shachar* — if witnesses came after the morning *tamid*, then at *musaf* one says *Harninu* again, even though it was already said at *shacharit*. The *baraita* explicitly permits this repetition: *af al pi shechozer v'chofla b'musaf*.

### Pre-Takana Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּשִׁירָה דְּיוֹמֵיהּ הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **It is different there, as** in any case "Sing aloud" **is the psalm of the day,** either because it was an ordinary Thursday or because it was Rosh HaShana. However, there is no proof from here that in all uncertain cases they would recite the psalm for an ordinary weekday, as it is possible that they did not recite any psalm at all.

The *Gemara* now uses this case to address a question left open from the *mishnah*: in the period before the *takana*, when witnesses had not yet arrived at *mincha* time and there was genuine uncertainty about whether the day was *Rosh Hashanah*, did the *Levi'im* recite nothing, or did they say the ordinary weekday *shir shel yom*?

The *Gemara* proposes that an inference can be drawn from the Thursday scenario. If when one does not yet know at *shacharit*, one says the ordinary weekday *shir shel yom*, this explains how the situation of *af al pi shechozeil v'chofla b'musaf* can arise at all: one recited the ordinary Thursday *shir* at *shacharit* not knowing it was *Rosh Hashanah*, then witnesses arrived, and so one repeated *Harninu* at *musaf*. This implies that when one does not know, one says the weekday *shir* — which would resolve our question about *mincha*.

The *Gemara* deflects this inference: *shani hatam de-shir de-yoma hu* — that case is different, because saying the ordinary weekday *shir* at *shacharit* was entirely standard practice even on days when one *did* know it was *Rosh Hashanah*. The weekday *shir shel yom* was always the norm at the morning *tamid* on *Rosh Hashanah*, regardless of one's knowledge. So there is no difficulty in saying it when one does not yet know; it is what one would say anyway. At *mincha*, however, the situation is the reverse: on any *Rosh Hashanah* where one knew it was the holiday, one would never say the weekday *shir shel yom* at *mincha*. Accordingly, when there was uncertainty at *mincha* time, one might indeed say nothing rather than something categorically inappropriate. The inference thus fails: the two contexts are not parallel.

## Weekly Shir Shel Yom

### Sunday–Friday Shir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: בָּרִאשׁוֹן מָה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים — "לַה׳ הָאָרֶץ וּמְלוֹאָהּ", עַל שֵׁם שֶׁקָּנָה וְהִקְנָה וְשַׁלִּיט בְּעוֹלָמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda said in the name of Rabbi Akiva: On the first** day of the week, Sunday, **what** psalm **would** the Levites **recite?** The psalm beginning with the phrase: **"The earth is the Lord's, and its fullness"** (Psalms 24:1), in commemoration of the first day of Creation, **because** on that day **He acquired** the world **and transferred** it to man, **and** He **was** the only **ruler in His world,** as the angels were not created until the second day.

The *Gemara* now pivots from the specific *Rosh Hashanah* practice to the broader question of what was said as the *shir shel yom* throughout the week in the *Beit HaMikdash*. A *baraita* is cited: *Rabbi Yehuda omer mishum Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Akiva — and it provides the foundation for the *shir shel yom* as preserved in our *siddur* to this day, parallel to a *mishnah* in *Tamid*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּשֵּׁנִי מָה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים — "גָּדוֹל ה׳ וּמְהוּלָּל מְאֹד", עַל שֵׁם שֶׁחִילֵּק מַעֲשָׂיו וּמָלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **On the second** day of the week **what** psalm **would** the Levites **recite?** The psalm that begins: **"Great is the Lord, and highly to be praised** in the city of our God, His sacred mountain" (Psalms 48:2). This is **because** on the second day of Creation **He separated His works,** dividing between the upper waters and the lower waters, **and ruled over them** as King; and this psalm speaks of Jerusalem as "The city of a great King" (Psalms 48:3).

On the first day, Sunday, they would say *Lashem ha'aretz umlo'ah* — Psalm 24 — because God *kanah v'hikna v'shalit b'olamo*: he acquired the world, transferred dominion to mankind, and ruled over it all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: "אֱלֹהִים נִצָּב בַּעֲדַת אֵל", עַל שֵׁם שֶׁגִּילָּה אֶרֶץ בְּחׇכְמָתוֹ, וְהֵכִין תֵּבֵל לַעֲדָתוֹ. בָּרְבִיעִי הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: "אֵל נְקָמוֹת ה׳", עַל שֵׁם שֶׁבָּרָא חַמָּה וּלְבָנָה, וְעָתִיד לִיפָּרַע מֵעוֹבְדֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **On the third** day of the week **they would recite** the psalm beginning: **"God stands in the congregation of God"** (Psalms 82:1), **because** on the third day of Creation **He revealed the land in His wisdom and** thereby **prepared the world for His assembly** that could now live on the dry land. **On the fourth** day of the week **they would recite** the psalm beginning: **"O Lord God, to Whom vengeance belongs"** (Psalms 94:1), **because** on the fourth day of Creation **He created the sun and the moon, and in the future He will punish** and take vengeance upon **those who worship them.**

On the second day, Monday, they said *Gadol Hashem u'mehulal me'od* — Psalm 48 — because God *chilek ma'asav umalach aleihem*: he divided his creations — splitting the waters above from the waters below on the second day — and ruled over them. *Rashi* explains the connection via the end of that psalm, *be'elokeinu har kodsho*, the holy mountain of God — the separation of the firmament from the waters creates a domain, as it were, from which God rules.

On the third day, Tuesday, they said *Elokim nitzav b'adat El* — Psalm 82 — because God revealed the land and established it for habitation: *shegila eretz b'chochmato v'hichin tevel la'adato*. The language is somewhat poetic, and one senses a liturgical resonance in these explanations themselves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּחֲמִישִׁי הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: "הַרְנִינוּ לֵאלֹהִים עוּזֵּנוּ", עַל שֵׁם שֶׁבָּרָא עוֹפוֹת וְדָגִים לְשַׁבֵּחַ לִשְׁמוֹ. בַּשִּׁשִּׁי הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: "ה׳ מָלָךְ גֵּאוּת לָבֵשׁ", עַל שֵׁם שֶׁגָּמַר מְלַאכְתּוֹ וּמָלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן. בַּשְּׁבִיעִי הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: "מִזְמוֹר שִׁיר לְיוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת", לְיוֹם שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **On the fifth** day of the week the Levites **would recite** the psalm beginning: **"Sing aloud to God our strength"** (Psalms 81:2), **because** on the fifth day of Creation **He created birds and fish to praise His name. On the sixth** day of the week **they would recite** the psalm beginning: **"The Lord reigns, He is clothed with majesty"** (Psalms 93:1), **because** on that day **He completed His labor and ruled over** all of creation in full glory. **On the seventh** day of the week, Shabbat, **they would recite** the psalm beginning: **"A psalm, a song for the day of Shabbat"** (Psalms 92:1), **as** the future world will be **a day that is all Shabbat.**

On the fourth day, Wednesday, they said *El nekamot Hashem* — Psalm 94 — because that was the day the luminaries were created, and God will ultimately exact punishment from those who worship them. It is striking that what was created to provide light and warmth is filtered here through the lens of *avodah zarah* — idolatry — and that the *shir* for the day of creation of the sun and moon speaks of divine vengeance against those who worship them. This is reinforced by the verse in *Devarim*: *asher chalak Hashem Elokecha otam l'chol ha'amim asher tachat kol hashamayim* — which the tradition reads as God apportioning the celestial bodies to the nations, as an implicit warning.

On the fifth day, Thursday, they said *Harninu l'Elohim uzenu* — Psalm 81 — because God created birds and fish to praise his name: *shebara ofot v'dagim leshabeach lishmo*. The connection is somewhat loose — many psalms speak more explicitly of praising God through the beauty and diversity of nature — and the lengthy reference in the psalm itself to *edut biYosef samo b'tzeto al eretz Mitzrayim* seems remote from the theme of creation's fifth day. The *Gemara* does not resolve this, and one is left noting that the selection is not always a perfect thematic match.

On the sixth day, Friday, they said *Hashem malach ge'ut lavesh* — Psalm 93 — because God completed his work and ruled over it: *shegamar melachto umalach aleihem*. With the creation of human beings, there is now a kingdom over which to reign.

### Shabbat Shir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: מָה רָאוּ חֲכָמִים לְחַלֵּק בֵּין הַפְּרָקִים הַלָּלוּ? אֶלָּא: בָּרִאשׁוֹן — שֶׁקָּנָה וְהִקְנָה וְשַׁלִּיט בְּעוֹלָמוֹ. בַּשֵּׁנִי — שֶׁחִילֵּק מַעֲשָׂיו וּמָלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶם. בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי — שֶׁגִּילָּה אֶרֶץ בְּחׇכְמָתוֹ וְהֵכִין תֵּבֵל לַעֲדָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Neḥemya said: What did the Sages see** that led them **to distinguish between these chapters,** as they interpret the psalms recited on the six weekdays as referring to the past, whereas the psalm recited on Shabbat is referring to the future. **Rather,** all of the psalms refer to the past. The first six are as explained above: **On the first** day, the reason is **that He acquired** the world **and transferred** it to man, **and** He **was** the only **ruler in His world; on the second** day, the reason is **that He separated His works and ruled over them** as King; **on the third** day, the reason is **that He revealed the land in His wisdom and** thereby **prepared the world for His assembly.**

On the seventh day, *Shabbat*, they said *Mizmor shir l'yom haShabbat* — Psalm 92 — which is self-evidently appropriate. However, the explanation given reframes it rather strikingly: *l'yom shekulo Shabbat*, for a day that will be entirely *Shabbat*, namely the seventh millennium of history, when the world will cease to exist entirely — a day of complete cessation, modeled on the seventh day of creation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרְבִיעִי — שֶׁבָּרָא חַמָּה וּלְבָנָה וְעָתִיד לִיפָּרַע מֵעוֹבְדֵיהֶן. בַּחֲמִישִׁי — שֶׁבָּרָא עוֹפוֹת וְדָגִים לְשַׁבֵּחַ לִשְׁמוֹ. בַּשִּׁשִּׁי — שֶׁגָּמַר מְלַאכְתּוֹ וּמָלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶם. בַּשְּׁבִיעִי — עַל שֵׁם שֶׁשָּׁבַת.
>
> **Translation:** **On the fourth** day, the reason is **that He created the sun and the moon, and in the future He will punish those who worship them; on the fifth** day, the reason is **that He created birds and fish to praise His name; on the sixth** day, the reason is **that He completed His labor and ruled over** all of creation. However, **on the seventh** day, the reason is **that He rested** from His work, as the phrase "A psalm, a song for the day of Shabbat" is referring to the first Shabbat of Creation.

At this point Rav Nechemia raises an objection: *mah ra'u chachamim l'chalek bein ha'perakim halalu*? Why does the explanation for *Shabbat* shift from creation-past to eschatology-future, when all the preceding days were anchored in what God did at creation? Why not simply say: on *Shabbat*, God rested — *al shem sheshavat*? The answer is that what is being debated here reflects a disagreement with the position of Rav Kattina.

### World Duration Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּדְרַב קַטִּינָא, דְּאָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: שִׁיתָּא אַלְפֵי שְׁנֵי הָוֵה עָלְמָא וְחַד חָרוּב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְנִשְׂגַּב ה׳ לְבַדּוֹ בְּיוֹם הַהוּא". אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תְּרֵי חָרוּב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "יְחַיֵּינוּ מִיּוֹמָיִם".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** these *tanna'im* **disagree with** regard to a statement **of Rav Ketina, as Rav Ketina said: The world will exist for six thousand years, and** for **one** thousand years it will be **destroyed, as it is stated: "And the Lord alone shall be exalted on that day"** (Isaiah 2:11), and one day for God is a thousand years, as indicated in the verse: "For a thousand years in Your sight are but as yesterday when it is past" (Psalms 90:4). Rav Ketina's statement is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva. Conversely, **Abaye said:** The world will be **destroyed** for **two** thousand years, **as it is stated: "After two days He will revive us"** (Hosea 6:2). According to the opinion of Abaye that the destruction will be for two days, there is no connection between the future world and the day of Shabbat, which is only one day.

Rav Kattina teaches: *shita alfei shenei havei alma, v'chad charuv* — the world will exist for 6,000 years, and one millennium it will be destroyed. The proof-text is *v'nisgav Hashem levado bayom hahu* — God alone will be exalted on that day — and a divine "day" equals a thousand years. On this view, *mizmor shir l'yom haShabbat* refers forward to that single millennium of destruction, which parallels the seventh day of rest.

Abaye disagrees: *trei charuv* — the world will be destroyed for two millennia. His source is *yechayenu miyomayim* — after two days he will revive us — understanding the *techiyat hameitim* as occurring after a two-thousand-year hiatus. On Abaye's view, the millennium of Psalm 92 cannot refer to the future destruction (which lasts two millennia, not one), and the *shir* must be understood as referring to the *Shabbat* of creation in the past. Thus the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rav Nechemia maps onto the dispute between Abaye and Rav Kattina: those who read the future into the *shir* follow Rav Kattina's schema of a single millennium of destruction; those who read the past follow Abaye.

One might ask why it would be appropriate to sing about a period when the world no longer exists, when there are no human beings and no history — a "day" of cosmic emptiness. The answer is not entirely clear. *Shabbat* as *me'ein olam haba* typically refers to something spiritually elevated and living, not to the silence of a destroyed cosmos. Nor does the connection between the first six days and creation obviously demand that the seventh day gesture toward destruction rather than divine rest. The *Ramban* in *Bereishit* develops the idea that each millennium of history models a day of creation, tracing the parallels across all six thousand years — and that broader framework is the backdrop for this entire discussion.

## Shabbat Services

### Musaf Ha'azinu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמוּסְפֵי דְשַׁבְּתָא מָה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים? אָמַר רַב עָנָן בַּר רָבָא אָמַר רַב: "הַזִּיו לָךְ".
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara further asks: When it came **to the additional offerings of Shabbat, what would** the Levites **recite? Rav Anan bar Rava said** that **Rav said:** They would recite in accordance with the mnemonic ***hei*, *zayin*, *yod*, *vav*, *lamed*, *kaf*.** They would divide the song of *Ha'azinu* into six sections, each of which began with a letter of the mnemonic: "Give ear [*ha'azinu*], you heavens" (Deuteronomy 32:1); "Remember [*zekhor*] the days of old" (Deuteronomy 32:7); "He made him ride [*yarkivehu*] on the high places of the earth" (Deuteronomy 32:13); "The Lord saw it [*vayar*] and spurned" (Deuteronomy 32:19); "Were it not [*lulei*] that I dread the enemy's provocation" (Deuteronomy 32:27); "For *[ki]* the Lord will judge His people" (Deuteronomy 32:36).

Having established the *shir shel yom* for each weekday and the morning *tamid* of *Shabbat*, the *Gemara* turns to the *musaf* of *Shabbat*. Rav Chanan bar Rava says in the name of Rav: the *shir* at *musaf* on *Shabbat* followed the mnemonic *HaZi"V La"Kh* — standing for the opening words of six sections of *Ha'azinu* (Deuteronomy 32). The six divisions are: *Ha'azinu hashamayim* (the opening), *Zechar yemot olam*, *Yarkivehu al bamotei aretz*, *Vayar Hashem vayina'atz*, *Lulei ka'as oyev agur*, and *Ki yadin Hashem amo*. The first four sections comprise six verses each; the final two are eight verses each.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא אָמַר רַב: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁחֲלוּקִים כָּאן — כָּךְ חֲלוּקִין בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥanan bar Rava said** that **Rav said: In the manner that** the verses of the song of *Ha'azinu* **are divided here** for the recitation of the additional offerings of Shabbat in the Temple, **so too are they divided** when they are read **in the synagogue** on Shabbat.

Rav Chanan bar Rava adds that this same division governs the *aliyot* when *Ha'azinu* is read as the weekly Torah portion in the synagogue — *kederach shechalukim kan kach chalukim b'veit haknesset*.

It is admittedly somewhat puzzling that *Ha'azinu* — a poem of rebuke, warning, national sin, and the specter of divine retribution — would be the *shir* of *musaf* on *Shabbat*. The poem is not, by any measure, uplifting in the conventional sense. Yet it is one of the great *shirot* of the *Torah*, and as we will see, the overall plan was to incorporate all the *shirot* of the *Torah* into the Shabbat services.

### Mincha Songs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמִנְחֲתָא דְשַׁבְּתָא מָה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: "אָז יָשִׁיר", וּ"מִי כָמוֹךָ", וְ"אָז יָשִׁיר".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question: When it came **to the** daily **afternoon offering on Shabbat, what would** the Levites **recite? Rabbi Yoḥanan said: "Then sang** Moses" (Exodus 15:1), **and: "Who is like You"** (Exodus 15:11), the two halves of the Song of the Sea, **and: "Then Israel sang** this song" (Numbers 21:17), the entire Song of the Well.

Rabbi Yochanan teaches that at *mincha* on *Shabbat* — the afternoon *tamid* — they would say *az yashir Moshe* (the *Shirat Hayam*, Exodus 15), followed by *mi kamocha* (the second half of that same *shira*), and then *az yashir* from the *shira* of the well, *ali be'er enu lah* (Numbers 21). Combined with *Ha'azinu* at *musaf*, the result is that between *musaf* and *mincha* on *Shabbat*, all of the *shirot* of the *Torah* are recited: *Ha'azinu*, *Az Yashir*, and *Ali Be'er*. The systematic completeness of this arrangement helps explain why *Ha'azinu* was selected for *musaf* despite its sobering tone — it is part of a comprehensive program to encompass all the *Torah's* songs on the day of *Shabbat*.

### Cycle or Complete

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הָנֵי כּוּלְּהוּ בְּחַד שַׁבְּתָא אָמְרִי לְהוּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֹּל שַׁבְּתָא וְשַׁבְּתָא אָמְרִי חַד? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: עַד שֶׁהָרִאשׁוֹנָה אוֹמֶרֶת אַחַת — שְׁנִיָּה חוֹזֶרֶת שְׁתַּיִם. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כׇּל שַׁבְּתָא וְשַׁבְּתָא אָמְרִי חַד, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does **one recite all these** sections of the song of *Ha'azinu* **on each Shabbat, or perhaps on each and every Shabbat** they would **recite one** section? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei said: By** the time **that** those who recite **the first** set, i.e., the verses for the additional offerings brought on Shabbat, **recite** it **once,** those who recite **the second** set, for the daily afternoon offering, would **repeat** their cycle **twice,** as the first set was comprised of six sections, whereas the second set included only three sections. **Learn from here** that **each and every Shabbat they would recite** only **one** section. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from here** that this is correct.

A question is raised: *hani kulhu b'chad shabta amrinhu* — are all six sections of *Ha'azinu* recited on a single *Shabbat* (with distinct musical breaks between them), or does each *Shabbat* receive one section, cycling through the six over the course of six weeks? Similarly for the *mincha shirim*.

The *Gemara* resolves the question from a *baraita* in which Rabbi Yosi states that by the time the *musaf* has gone through one cycle, the afternoon *tamid* has gone through two. This clearly implies a rotating schedule: one section per *Shabbat*. After six weeks one completes one cycle of *Ha'azinu* at *musaf*, and after three weeks one completes one cycle of the *mincha shirim* — hence two cycles of the *mincha shirim* for every one cycle of *Ha'azinu*.

This is rather startling in its implications. On any given *Shabbat*, the section of *Ha'azinu* assigned to *musaf* might be something as stark as *vayar Hashem vayana'atz mikinas banav uvnotav vayomer astira panai mehem* — God saw and was provoked by his sons and daughters, and said, I will hide my face from them. To recite just a fragment of *Ha'azinu*, stripped of even the limited consolation that comes at the poem's conclusion, seems deeply unusual as a *musaf shir* on *Shabbat*. Yet the *halakha* appears to accept precisely this.

## Shechina's Exile

### Ten Journeys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר אִידֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֶשֶׂר מַסָּעוֹת נָסְעָה שְׁכִינָה, מִקְּרָאֵי, וּכְנֶגְדָּן גָּלְתָה סַנְהֶדְרִין, מִגְּמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda bar Idi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The Divine Presence traveled ten journeys,** i.e., it left the Temple and Eretz Yisrael in ten stages at the time of the destruction of the First Temple, as derived **from verses. And corresponding to them the Sanhedrin was exiled** in ten stages at the end of the Second Temple period and after the destruction of the Temple, and this is known **from tradition.**

The *Gemara* now shifts its frame entirely. Having discussed the *shir shel yom* in the context of the *Beit HaMikdash*, and with *Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's* role in the post-*churban* order on the horizon, the *Gemara* offers a remarkable teaching. Rav Yehuda bar Idi says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *eser masaot nasa Shechina* — the *Shechina* moved through ten stages of departure from the *Beit HaMikdash* at the time of the *churban*, and this is established from verses. In parallel — *uchnegdam galta Sanhedrin* — the *Sanhedrin* went into exile from its seat in the *Beit HaMikdash*, and this is known from tradition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶשֶׂר מַסָּעוֹת נָסְעָה שְׁכִינָה, מִקְּרָאֵי: מִכַּפּוֹרֶת לִכְרוּב, וּמִכְּרוּב לִכְרוּב, וּמִכְּרוּב לְמִפְתָּן, וּמִמִּפְתָּן לְחָצֵר, וּמֵחָצֵר לְמִזְבֵּחַ, וּמִמִּזְבֵּחַ לְגַג, וּמִגַּג לְחוֹמָה, וּמֵחוֹמָה לָעִיר, וּמֵעִיר לְהַר, וּמֵהַר לְמִדְבָּר, וּמִמִּדְבָּר עָלְתָה וְיָשְׁבָה בִּמְקוֹמָהּ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "אֵלֵךְ אָשׁוּבָה אֶל מְקוֹמִי".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates. **The Divine Presence traveled ten journeys,** as derived **from verses.** The ten journeys are: **From the Ark cover to the cherub; and from** one **cherub to** the other **cherub; and from** the second **cherub to the threshold** of the Sanctuary; **and from the threshold to the courtyard; and from the courtyard to the altar; and from the altar to the roof; and from the roof to the wall** of the Temple Mount; **and from the wall to the city; and from the city to a mountain** close to Jerusalem; **and from** that **mountain to the wilderness; and from the wilderness it ascended and rested in its place** in Heaven, isolated from humanity, **as it is stated: "I will go and return to My place"** (Hosea 5:15).

The ten journeys of the *Shechina* are as follows. First, from the *kaporet* — the cover of the *aron* — to the *keruvim*: from the place between the wings where the divine voice would speak (as in *v'noadeti l'cha sham v'dibarti itcha me'al hakaporet*) to atop one of the large freestanding *keruv* statues that stood in the *Heikhal* of Solomon's Temple. Second, from one *keruv* to the other. Third, from the *keruvim* to the threshold of the *Heikhal* — the transition point between the inner sanctuary and the entrance hall, the *ulam*. Fourth, from the threshold into the *chatzer*, the open courtyard. Fifth, from the *chatzer* to the *mizbeach*.

At this point the sequence moves upward and outward. Sixth, from the *mizbeach* to the roof. Seventh, from the roof to the surrounding wall. There is some uncertainty in the text here — *Rashi's* reading has the *Shechina* going to the roof of the *Heikhal*, which does create a slight impression of doubling back; the *Gra* has a somewhat different version. In any case, the trajectory is consistently outward. Eighth, from the wall into the city of Jerusalem. Ninth, from Jerusalem to *Har Hazeitim*, the Mount of Olives, the mountain to the east of the city. Tenth, from the mountain into *Midbar Yehudah*, the wilderness, and from there *altah v'yashvah bimkomah* — it ascended and returned to its place, as Scripture says: *eilech ashuva el mekomi* — I will go and return to my place (Hosea 5:15).

### Verse Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכַּפּוֹרֶת לִכְרוּב, מִכְּרוּב לִכְרוּב, וּמִכְּרוּב לְמִפְתָּן — דִּכְתִיב: "וְנוֹעַדְתִּי לְךָ שָׁם וְדִבַּרְתִּי אִתְּךָ מֵעַל הַכַּפּוֹרֶת", וּכְתִיב: "וַיִּרְכַּב עַל כְּרוּב וַיָּעֹף", וּכְתִיב: "וּכְבוֹד אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל נַעֲלָה מֵעַל הַכְּרוּב אֲשֶׁר הָיָה עָלָיו אֶל מִפְתַּן הַבָּיִת".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the sources for each of these stages: **From the Ark cover** the Divine Presence traveled **to the cherub,** and **from** one **cherub to** the other **cherub, and from the** second **cherub to the threshold, as it is written** with regard to Moses in the Tabernacle: **"And there I will meet with you, and I will speak to you from above the Ark cover,** from between the two cherubs" (Exodus 25:22). **And it is written: "And He rode upon a cherub, and flew"** (II Samuel 22:11), which indicates that the glory of the Divine Presence can rest upon one cherub. **And it is written: "And the glory of the God of Israel had ascended from the cherub, on which it was, to the threshold of the House"** (Ezekiel 9:3), i.e., the Divine Presence moved from the cherub to the threshold.

The *Gemara* now systematically provides the scriptural basis for each stage of this departure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמִּפְתָּן לְחָצֵר, דִּכְתִיב: "וַיִּמָּלֵא הַבַּיִת אֶת הֶעָנָן וְהֶחָצֵר מָלְאָה אֶת נֹגַהּ כְּבוֹד ה׳". מֵחָצֵר לְמִזְבֵּחַ, דִּכְתִיב: "רָאִיתִי אֶת ה׳ נִצָּב עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ". וּמִמִּזְבֵּחַ לְגַג, דִּכְתִיב: "טוֹב לָשֶׁבֶת עַל פִּנַּת גָּג". מִגַּג לְחוֹמָה, דִּכְתִיב: "וְהִנֵּה ה׳ נִצָּב עַל חוֹמַת אֲנָךְ". מֵחוֹמָה לָעִיר, דִּכְתִיב: "קוֹל ה׳ לָעִיר יִקְרָא".
>
> **Translation:** **And from the threshold** of the Sanctuary the Divine Presence went **to the courtyard, as it is written: "And the House was filled with the cloud and the courtyard was full of the brightness of the Lord's glory"** (Ezekiel 10:4). **From the courtyard to the altar, as it is written: "I saw the Lord standing on the altar"** (Amos 9:1). **And from the altar to the roof, as it is written: "It is better to dwell in a corner of the roof** than in a house together with a contentious woman" (Proverbs 21:9). **From the roof to the wall, as it is written: "And behold, the Lord stood upon a wall made by a plumb line"** (Amos 7:7). **From the wall to the city, as it is written: "The Lord's voice cries to the city"** (Micah 6:9).

The first three stages — *kaporet* to *keruv*, *keruv* to *keruv*, *keruv* to threshold — are supported by two verses. The initial location on the *kaporet* is established from *v'noadeti l'cha sham v'dibarti itcha me'al hakaporet* (Exodus 25:22). The movement to the *keruvim* is implicit in *vayirkav al keruv vayauf* — God rode upon a *keruv* and flew (Psalm 18:11) — though some editions of the text excise this verse because it is not explicitly set in the context of the exile of the *Shechina*. The transition from *keruv* to threshold is from *uchvod Elokei Yisrael naalah me'al hakruv asher hayah alav el miftan habayit* — the glory of the God of Israel lifted from the *keruv* upon which it had rested, to the threshold of the house (Ezekiel 9:3).

It bears noting that these verses are drawn from across a wide span of the biblical corpus. The *Gemara* is not reading a continuous narrative; it is weaving together texts from different contexts to construct this progression. The rabbis understood the various prophetic visions — particularly in *Yechezkel* — as windows into a coherent process of divine withdrawal.

For the stage from threshold to *chatzer*: *vayamalei habayit et he'anan v'hachatzer malah et nogah kvod Hashem* — the house was filled with the cloud and the court was filled with the radiance of God's glory (Ezekiel 10:4). The word *nogah*, radiance or shining, is significant. The *Torah* and prophets frequently describe the *kavod Hashem* as something that shines — a visible luminosity, perceived through the cloud because it is too intense to behold directly. When the texts speak of the *Shechina* departing, they are likely describing the departure of this physical-phenomenological shining as well, not merely an abstract theological absence.

From *chatzer* to *mizbeach*: *va'ere et Hashem nitzav al hamizbeach* — I saw God standing upon the altar (Amos 9:1).

From *mizbeach* to roof: *tov lashevet al pinat gag* — it is better to sit on a corner of the roof (Proverbs 25:24). The verse is read as though God declares: better to ascend to the roof, to remove from this place.

From roof to wall: *hinei Hashem nitzav al chomat anach* — behold, God standing on a wall of *anach* (Amos 7:7).

From wall to city: *kol Hashem la'ir yikra* — the voice of God calls out to the city (Micah 6:9).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵעִיר לְהַר, דִּכְתִיב: "וַיַּעַל כְּבוֹד ה׳ מֵעַל תּוֹךְ הָעִיר וַיַּעֲמֹד עַל הָהָר אֲשֶׁר מִקֶּדֶם לָעִיר". וּמֵהַר לְמִדְבָּר, דִּכְתִיב: "טוֹב שֶׁבֶת בְּאֶרֶץ מִדְבָּר". וּמִמִּדְבָּר עָלְתָה וְיָשְׁבָה בִּמְקוֹמָהּ, דִּכְתִיב: "אֵלֵךְ אָשׁוּבָה אֶל מְקוֹמִי וְגוֹ׳".
>
> **Translation:** **And from the city** the Divine Presence arose **to the mountain** nearest the Sanctuary, i.e., the Mount of Olives, **as it is written: "And the glory of the Lord went up from the midst of the city, and stood upon the mountain, which is on the east side of the city"** (Ezekiel 11:23). **And from the mountain to the wilderness, as it is written: "It is better to live in the wilderness** than with a contentious and fretful woman" (Proverbs 21:19). **And from the wilderness it ascended and rested in its place** in Heaven, **as it is written: "I will go and return to My place** until they acknowledge their guilt" (Hosea 5:15).

From city to *Har Hazeitim*: *vaya'al kevod Hashem me'al toch ha'ir vaya'amod al hahar asher mikedem la'ir* — the glory of God lifted from the midst of the city and stood on the mountain to the east of the city (Ezekiel 11:23).

From the mountain to the wilderness: *tov shevet b'eretz midbar* — better to dwell in a wilderness land (Proverbs 21:19).

And finally the departure from earth to the heavenly realm: *eilech ashuva el mekomi* — I will go and return to my place (Hosea 5:15).

### Six Months Wait

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים נִתְעַכְּבָה שְׁכִינָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר, שֶׁמָּא יַחְזְרוּ בִּתְשׁוּבָה. כֵּיוָן שֶׁלֹּא חָזְרוּ — אָמַר: תִּיפַּח עַצְמָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְעֵינֵי רְשָׁעִים תִּכְלֶינָה וּמָנוֹס אָבַד מִנְהֶם וְתִקְוָתָם מַפַּח נָפֶשׁ".
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** For **six months the Divine Presence lingered in the wilderness,** waiting **for the Jewish people,** hoping that **perhaps they would repent** and it would be able to return to its place. **When they did not repent,** the Divine Presence **said: Let them** despair and **be lost, as it is stated: "But the eyes of the wicked shall fail, and they shall have no way to flee, and their hope shall be the drooping of the soul"** (Job 11:20). This concludes the discussion of the ten stages of the exile of the Divine Presence from the Holy of Holies.

The *Gemara* adds a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan that deepens the pathos of this entire account: *sheshah chodashim nitkavah Shechina l'Yisrael bamidbar* — the *Shechina* waited in the wilderness for six months, *shema yachzeru b'teshuvah* — hoping that Israel would repent, so that it would not have to leave. When *teshuvah* did not come, God declared, as it were: *tipach atzmam* — let their very bones be blasted — as the verse concludes: *v'einei resha'im tichlena umanos avad mihem v'sikvasam mapach nafesh* — the eyes of the wicked will be consumed, escape will be lost to them, and their hope will be the gasping of a soul (Job 11:20).

The image is profoundly moving. The *Torah* itself speaks of God's commitment to dwell *b'toch tumotam* — in the midst of Israel's impurity — the divine decision to remain present even amid human sinfulness. Here that commitment reaches its limit, but even so, the departure is slow, stage by stage, each transition carrying the possibility that things might turn around. Six months of waiting. Ten stages of withdrawal. The *Shechina* is not swept away; it reluctantly retreats, holding on as long as possible before the final departure.

The *Maharsha* notes that the six months corresponds to the period from *Asarah B'Tevet* — when the siege of Jerusalem began — to the *Tisha B'Av* of the following year when the Temple was burned: in fact that is eighteen months, not six, but the six-month interval marks the distance from *Asarah B'Tevet* to the breach of the walls on the ninth of *Tammuz* of the same year.

## Sanhedrin's Exile

### Jerusalem Moves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְנֶגְדָּן גָּלְתָה סַנְהֶדְרִין, מִגְּמָרָא: מִלִּשְׁכַּת הַגָּזִית לַחֲנוּת, וּמֵחֲנוּת לִירוּשָׁלַיִם, וּמִירוּשָׁלַיִם לְיַבְנֶה,
>
> **Translation:** **And corresponding to** these ten stages, **the Sanhedrin was exiled** in ten stages at the end of the Second Temple period and after the destruction of the Temple, and this is known **from tradition: From the Chamber of Hewn Stone,** its fixed seat in the Temple, **to *Ḥanut*,** literally, shop, a designated spot on the Temple Mount outside the Temple proper; **and from *Ḥanut* to Jerusalem; and from Jerusalem to Yavne;**

The *Gemara* now describes the parallel exile of the *Sanhedrin*, which is known not from biblical verses but from tradition: *galta Sanhedrin migemara*. The *Sanhedrin* moved from *Lishkat HaGazit* — the Chamber of Hewn Stone within the *azarah* of the *Beit HaMikdash* — to *chanut*, the shops. These were the commercial stalls lining the base of *Har Habayit*, evocative of the *Cardo* corridor in Jerusalem that archaeologists have excavated. From *chanut* they moved further into central Jerusalem proper.

### Post-Churban Moves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִיַּבְנֶה לְאוּשָׁא, וּמֵאוּשָׁא לְיַבְנֶה, וּמִיַּבְנֶה לְאוּשָׁא, וּמֵאוּשָׁא לִשְׁפַרְעָם, וּמִשְּׁפַרְעָם לְבֵית שְׁעָרִים, וּמִבֵּית שְׁעָרִים לְצִפּוֹרִי, וּמִצִּפּוֹרִי לִטְבֶרְיָא. וּטְבֶרְיָא עֲמוּקָּה מִכּוּלָּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְשָׁפַלְתְּ מֵאֶרֶץ תְּדַבֵּרִי".
>
> **Translation:** **and from Yavne to Usha; and from Usha** it returned **to Yavne; and from Yavne** it went back **to Usha; and from Usha to Shefaram; and from Shefaram to Beit She'arim; and from Beit She'arim to Tzippori; and from Tzippori to Tiberias. And Tiberias is lower than all of them,** as it is in the Jordan Valley. A verse alludes to these movements, **as it is stated: "And brought down, you shall speak out of the ground"** (Isaiah 29:4).

After the *churban*, the *Sanhedrin* left Jerusalem for *Yavne* — this is the famous transition effected by Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai. From *Yavne* they moved to *Usha*, then back to *Yavne*, then back to *Usha* — *Rashi* explains that the *Sanhedrin* followed the *Nasi*, and the *Nasi* — whether Rabban Gamliel or his successors — moved back and forth between the two cities. Thereafter: from *Usha* to *Shefaram*, from *Shefaram* to *Beit Shearim* (where the extensive necropolis excavations have uncovered sarcophagi associated with the house of the *Nasi*), from *Beit Shearim* to *Tzippori*, and finally from *Tzippori* to *Tverya*. The verse cited for the final stage is *v'shachotam me'eretz tedaberi* — brought low from the earth, you will speak — and indeed *Tverya* lies below sea level, the lowest of all these locations.

One should note that calling these movements an "exile" in the same sense as the departure of the *Shechina* is somewhat forced. After leaving Jerusalem, the relocations are not exile in any straightforward sense — the *Sanhedrin* is moving around within the land, following the *Nasi*. The imagery of exile serves primarily to create the structural parallel: as the *Shechina* moved through ten stages and finally departed, so the *Sanhedrin* moved through ten stages and eventually ceased to function as a central body.

There is, however, a deeper parallel that is worth dwelling on. The *churban* of the *Beit Rishon* was associated most prominently with religious sin — *avodah zarah*, *gilluy arayot*, *shefichat damim* — and accordingly what departed was the *Shechina*, the manifest divine presence. The *churban* of the *Beit Sheni* was attributed to *sinat chinam*, and what that phrase seems to capture in the tradition is a catastrophic failure of communal leadership and unity — the *Kamtza and Bar Kamtza* stories in *Gittin*, the inability of the Rabbis to exercise the leadership the moment required. Accordingly, what went into exile in the *Beit Sheni* period was not the *Shechina* but the *Sanhedrin*, the institutional seat of Jewish communal and halachic authority. The parallel is not accidental: each destruction exiled what was most central to the spiritual life of that era.

### Six Exiles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: שֵׁשׁ גָּלוּת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כִּי הֵשַׁח יוֹשְׁבֵי מָרוֹם קִרְיָה נִשְׂגָּבָה יַשְׁפִּילֶנָּה יַשְׁפִּילָהּ עַד אֶרֶץ יַגִּיעֶנָּה עַד עָפָר". אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וּמִשָּׁם עֲתִידִין לִיגָּאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "הִתְנַעֲרִי מֵעָפָר קוּמִי שְּׁבִי".
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** There are **six exiles,** if you count only the places, not the number of journeys, and a different verse alludes to this, **as it is stated: "For He has brought down those who dwell high, the lofty city laying it low, laying it low, to the ground, bringing it to the dust"** (Isaiah 26:5). This verse mentions six expressions of lowering: Brought down, laying it low, laying it low, to the ground, bringing it, and to the dust. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: And from there,** i.e., from their lowest place of descent, **they are destined to be redeemed** in the future, **as it is stated: "Shake yourself from the dust, arise, sit,** Jerusalem" (Isaiah 52:2).

Rabbi Eliezer offers a different count: not ten but six exiles of the *Sanhedrin*. His proof-text is *ki heishach yoshvei marom kiryah nisgavah yashpilena yashpila ad aretz yagi'ena ad afar* — counting the verbs and clauses yields six, not ten. Rabbi Yochanan adds: *umisham atidin l'iga'el* — and from that place they are destined to be redeemed, as Scripture says *hitna'ari mei'afar kumi shvi* — arise, shake off the dust, return to your seat (Isaiah 52:2). Just as the *Shechina*, after its departure, would ultimately return to dwell among Israel in the time of redemption, so too the *Sanhedrin*, after its wandering, would ultimately be restored.

## Fixed Court Location

### Takana Established

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה: וְעוֹד זֹאת הִתְקִין רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ רֹאשׁ בֵּית דִּין בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ הָעֵדִים הוֹלְכִין אֶלָּא לִמְקוֹם הַוַּעַד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa said: And this, too, Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai instituted, that even** if **the head of the court** of seventy-one **is in any** other **place,** not where the Great Sanhedrin is in session, **the witnesses should** nevertheless **go only to the place** where the Great Sanhedrin **gathers** to deliver testimony to determine the start of the month. Although the date of the month is dependent on the head of the Great Sanhedrin, as it is he who declares that the month is sanctified (see 24a), nevertheless, Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai instituted that the members of the Great Sanhedrin may sanctify the month in the absence of the head of the court.

Returning now to the *Rosh Hashanah* context of *Kiddush HaChodesh*, the *mishnah* records: *amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha*, and yet this additional *takana* was enacted by Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai. Even if the *rosh beit din* was located elsewhere on any given day, the witnesses for the new moon would not be required to seek him out — they would go only to the fixed location of the *vaad*, the court's regular seat. The practical motivation is clear: if witnesses find the court is in one place one month and somewhere else the next, they will eventually stop making the effort to come at all. The entire system of testimony for *kiddush hachodesh* depends on making the process as predictable and welcoming as possible.

Beyond the practical logic, this *takana* connects to a broader shift we have been observing throughout this *perek*: the move from *Mikdash* to *Torah*, from hierarchy to a somewhat more decentralized form of religious authority. The willingness to say that the *beit din* can function without its head being physically present, that witnesses need only arrive at a fixed location, reflects the same spirit that produced the other *takanot* of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai — building a functioning, sustainable Jewish community in a post-*Mikdash* reality.

### Woman's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַהִיא אִיתְּתָא דְּאַזְמְנוּהָ לְדִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּאַמֵּימָר בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא. אֲזַל אַמֵּימָר לְמָחוֹזָא, וְלָא אֲזַלָה בָּתְרֵיהּ. כְּתַב פְּתִיחָא עִילָּוַהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר, וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: אֲפִילּוּ רֹאשׁ בֵּית דִּין בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ הָעֵדִים הוֹלְכִין אֶלָּא לִמְקוֹם הַוַּעַד!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara relates: There was **a certain woman who was called to judgment before Ameimar in Neharde'a. Ameimar** temporarily **went to Meḥoza, and she did not follow him** to be judged there. **He wrote a document of excommunication [*petiḥa*] concerning her,** for disobeying the court. **Rav Ashi said to Ameimar: Didn't we learn** in the mishna: **Even** if **the head of the court** of seventy-one **is in any** other **place, the witnesses should go only to the place** where the Great Sanhedrin **gathers?** This shows that one must appear in the court itself, rather than follow the head of the court.

The *Gemara* now adduces a case study. A certain woman received a summons to appear before *Amemar* in *Nehardea*. On the day appointed, *Amemar* happened to be in *Mechuza*. The woman refused to travel to *Mechuza*, and the court in *Nehardea* wrote a *pticha* — a document placing her in contempt — against her. Rav Ashi challenged *Amemar*: how could you do this? Does our *mishnah* not teach that even if the *rosh beit din* is elsewhere, the witnesses — and by logical extension, litigants — need only go to the fixed location?

### Amemar's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן עֵדוּת הַחֹדֶשׁ, דְּאִם כֵּן נִמְצֵאתָ מַכְשִׁילָן לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא. אֲבָל הָכָא — "עֶבֶד לֹוֶה לְאִישׁ מַלְוֶה".
>
> **Translation:** Ameimar **said to him: This applies only to testimony** to determine the start **of the month,** for which it is necessary to have a fixed place. The reason is **that if so,** if the witnesses come to court when the head of the court is absent and they will have to go to another place, **consequently you will be obstructing them for future** occasions, as they will consider it too much trouble and perhaps they will not come the next time. Therefore, the Sages said that these witnesses should go to the regular place where the Great Sanhedrin meets. **However, here,** with regard to monetary claims, the verse states: **"The borrower is servant to the lender"** (Proverbs 22:7), i.e., the defendant must act as is convenient to the claimant and the court.

*Amemar* replied: *hani mili* — that principle applies in *eidut hachodesh*, testimony for the new moon. In that context, the concern is institutional: if witnesses are sent on a chase after the *rosh beit din*, they will stop coming altogether, undermining the entire system. But here — *abda lova l'ish malva* — the borrower is a slave to the creditor. In a monetary dispute, the debtor is obligated to present herself before the creditor's chosen forum. The lender did the borrower a favor by extending credit; the debtor cannot claim the convenience protections designed to encourage voluntary witnesses to come forward. The policy underlying the *takana* simply does not apply in the same way to ordinary litigation, where the parties are in an adversarial relationship and the creditor has legitimate interests of his own.

## Nine Takanot

### Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין כֹּהֲנִים רַשָּׁאִין לַעֲלוֹת בְּסַנְדְּלֵיהֶן לַדּוּכָן, וְזוֹ אֶחָד מִתֵּשַׁע תַּקָּנוֹת שֶׁהִתְקִין רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי. שֵׁית דְּהַאי פִּירְקָא, וַחֲדָא דְּפִירְקָא קַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Priests are not allowed to ascend with their sandals to the platform** to recite the Priestly Blessing in the synagogue. **And this is one of the nine ordinances that Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai instituted. Six** are mentioned **in this chapter:** Sounding the *shofar* on Shabbat in Yavne, taking the *lulav* all seven days, the prohibition against eating new grain the entire day of waving, accepting testimony to determine the start of the month all day, having the witnesses to the New Moon go to the place of meeting, and reciting the Priestly Blessing without sandals. **And one** is stated **in the first chapter,** that the witnesses to the New Moon may desecrate Shabbat only for the months of Tishrei and Nisan.

A *baraita* is cited: the *Kohanim* may not ascend the *duchan* wearing their sandals — this is the practice in the *beit knesset*, corresponding to the practice of going barefoot in the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *Gemara* there explains that if a *kohen* bends down to tie a loose sandal, onlookers might think he is disqualified from *Birkat Kohanim* for some reason. The *baraita* identifies this as one of the nine *takanot* of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai.

The nine *takanot* deserve enumeration. Together they represent Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's comprehensive reorganization of Jewish life in the aftermath of the *churban*, and one of their most striking features is how consistently they embrace the new reality rather than attempting to preserve the old one. They are not primarily about memorializing the *Mikdash*; they are about building a functioning *Torah*-centered community for the long exile.

### Six in This Chapter

Six of the nine *takanot* appear in this *perek*. They include the five enacted by Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai that are recorded in the *mishnayot* of this chapter: blowing *shofar* in *Yavne* even when *Rosh Hashanah* falls on *Shabbat*; taking the *lulav* for all seven days; not eating *chadash* (new grain) on the entire sixteenth of *Nisan* rather than only until *musaf*; accepting witnesses for the new moon after *mincha* time (the original practice that preceded the *takana* we have been discussing); and the rule that witnesses always go to the fixed location of the court regardless of where the *rosh beit din* is. Plus the sixth: the *duchan* without sandals, which the *Gemara* notes is mentioned here even though it is formally at home elsewhere (*sota*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: גֵּר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר בַּזְּמַן הַזֶּה, צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּפְרִישׁ רוֹבַע לְקִינּוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: כְּבָר נִמְנָה עָלֶיהָ רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן וּבִיטְּלָהּ, מִפְּנֵי הַתַּקָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A convert who converts nowadays is required to set aside a quarter**-shekel **for his nest,** i.e., his pair of doves. By Torah law a convert must bring two burnt-offerings of birds, in addition to his immersion and circumcision. After the destruction, it was instituted that he must set aside the value of two young pigeons in anticipation of the rebuilding of the Temple. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar said: Rabban Yoḥanan** ben Zakkai **already** assembled a majority who **voted and rescinded** the ordinance **due to** a potential **mishap.** If a convert is obligated to set aside money, someone might unwittingly use this money, thereby violating the prohibition against misuse of consecrated property.

One might wonder how the sandal *takana* fits within the overarching theme of the others. A suggestion raised in the course of the *shiur* is illuminating: this *takana* places Rabbinic authority squarely over *Kohanim* in their performance of *Birkat Kohanim* outside the *Mikdash*. It is the Rabbis dictating the protocol of *Kohanic* ritual in a decentralized, non-*Mikdash* context — thus reinforcing Rabbinic authority over priestly practice, which is itself a hallmark of the post-*churban* realignment.

### Nisan/Tishrei

The seventh *takana* — the first in the order of the *perakim*, found in the opening chapter — is that after the *churban*, witnesses desecrating *Shabbat* to travel and testify to the new moon would be permitted to do so only for *Nisan* and *Tishrei*: the months that govern *Pesach* and *Rosh Hashanah*/*Yom Kippur*, the pillar festivals whose dates most profoundly affect all of Israel. In the time of the *Mikdash*, precision in the calendar for every month mattered greatly because of the *korbanot* and the Temple calendar. Post-*churban*, the urgency is limited to the months that matter for the community's observance of the great festivals. This *takana* is, again, a perfect expression of the same spirit: recognizing the changed reality and calibrating practice accordingly.

### Convert's Sacrifice

The eighth *takana* concerns the convert's bird-sacrifice obligation. The *baraita* teaches: a convert who undergoes conversion nowadays is required to set aside a *rova* — a quarter-*dinar* — for his *kinim* (bird-offerings), so that when the *Beit HaMikdash* is rebuilt he can complete his conversion process by bringing the obligatory sacrifice. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, however, reports that Rabbi Yochanan already convened a vote and nullified this requirement *mipnei hatakala* — because of the danger of misuse. Money set aside for *hekdesh* in one's home is sanctified; one might inadvertently misappropriate it, incurring *me'ila*.

Beyond the technical concern, the thrust of this ruling is striking. To require the convert to set aside this money is to tell him: your conversion is incomplete; you remain in a kind of suspended state, awaiting the rebuilding of the Temple to finish what you started. The psychological and theological burden of that message — you are a *ger* but not quite fully one — is enormous. Rabbi Yochanan's ruling removes that burden. Do not set aside the money; do not hold onto the idea that your conversion is pending. You are fully a *ger*. Here too, the look is forward, not backward.

### Ninth Takana Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַב פָּפָּא וְרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: כֶּרֶם רְבָעִי, רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: לָשׁוֹן שֶׁל זְהוֹרִית.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** ordinance, the ninth, **is** the subject of **a dispute between Rav Pappa and Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak. Rav Pappa said:** The ordinance concerned the fruit of a **fourth-year grapevine. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** It was with regard to **the strip of crimson** wool.

The ninth and final *takana* is itself a matter of dispute, debated by Rav Pappa and Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak — and with that, the *daf* reaches its limit. The resolution of that debate, and the identification of the ninth *takana*, will be taken up in the next session.
