# Keritot 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Cases: Mezid & Shog…

### Intro: Intentional vs Un…

The ninth daf of *Keritot* opens a new *mishnah* that introduces a distinct category of *chatat*: sin offerings that are brought not only for unintentional violations but for intentional ones as well. Normally, the *chatat* is the exclusive domain of the *shogeg* — the person who transgresses unknowingly or negligently. Here, however, the *mishnah* identifies four cases that blur or override that fundamental distinction entirely: *eilu mevi'in al hazadon kishogeg* — the following bring an offering for even willful sins just as they would for unintentional ones.

It is worth noting at the outset that the *mishnah* is organized numerically rather than thematically. We have just finished discussing the four cases of *mechusrei kapparah* — those who lack atonement and must complete a purification process — and now we move to four cases in which the *mezid/shogeg* distinction is dissolved. These will be followed, as we shall see, by five cases in which one *korban* covers multiple transgressions, and five cases where the *korban* is of variable value based on the offerer's means. The organizing principle is the number, not the underlying legal category.

### Haba al Hashifchah

The first of the four cases is *haba al hashifchah* — one who has sexual relations with a female slave. The *pasuk* in question reads: *ve'ish ki yishkav et ishah shichvat zera, vehi shifchah necheret le'ish* — a man who lies with a woman carnally, and she is a *shifchah* designated to a man — *vehapdeh lo nifdetah o chufshah lo nitan lah* — she has not been fully redeemed nor fully freed. The *mishnah* and *Chazal* understand this to refer to a woman who is half-slave and half-free, quasi-betrothed to a man who is himself half-slave. Because she is not fully free, the betrothal is not fully binding as a proper *kiddushin* would be; yet she is not entirely without status either. It is this liminal state — neither full adultery nor an ordinary relationship — that defines this unique category.

The *pasuk* continues: *vehevi et ashamo l'Adonai el petach ohel moed eil asham, vechiper alav hakohen be'eil ha'asham lifnei Adonai al chatato asher chata venislach lo mechatato asher chata* — he shall bring his guilt offering before God, and the priest shall atone for him, and he shall be forgiven for the sin that he has sinned. The Torah mandates both *malkot* (lashes) and a *korban*, which makes this a striking and unusual case — ordinarily lashes are imposed for *mezid* and a *korban* for *shogeg*, yet here both are applied simultaneously. The important consequence is that both intentional and unintentional acts within this category require the same *korban*.

### Derivation: Redundancy i…

How does the *Gemara* derive that a single *korban* covers multiple acts of this sin, and that the *korban* is brought regardless of whether the act was intentional or unintentional? The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *vechiper alav hakohen be'eil ha'asham al chatato asher chata* — "for the sin that he has sinned." The phrase *chatato asher chata* is somewhat redundant; one might have simply written *al chatato*. The *baraita* derives from this redundancy that you bring one *korban* for multiple transgressions: *shemevi korban echad al aveirot harbeh*. And then the *pasuk* continues with a second redundant phrase — *venislach lo mechatato asher chata* — again "the sin that he has sinned." This second repetition teaches *la'asot mezid keshogeg* — to treat intentional sin like unintentional sin.

The *Gemara* raises a subtlety: one might have assumed that the baseline case of the *pasuk* is the *shogeg*, and that the derivation extends it to *mezid*. But the *Gemara* points out, based on *Rashi*'s reading, that the *peshat* of the *psukim* about *shifchah charufa* is actually the *mezid* case — because the *pasuk* of *bikoret tihyeh* is interpreted by *Chazal* as mandating *malkot*, and *malkot* is never administered for an unintentional act. Therefore, the baseline here is *mezid*, and the derivation from *mechatato asher chata* extends the obligation to *shogeg* as well. The *Gemara* reformulates accordingly: *ela eima la'asot shogeg kemezid* — we are extending the rule to treat *shogeg* like *mezid*. Either way, the *halacha* is identical: for *shifchah charufa*, one brings a *korban* across the full spectrum, and if one is a *mezid*, one receives both *malkot* and a *korban* — an altogether unusual combination.

### Nazir Shenitma

The second case is *venazir shenitma* — a *nazir* who became *tamei*. A *nazir* is prohibited from contracting ritual impurity, but if he does, he brings special *korbanot* — and this is so even if he became *tamei bemezid*, intentionally. The *Gemara* asks *minalan* — where do we know this?

The source is the *pasuk*: *vechi yamut met alav befeta pitom* — "and if a corpse should die upon him suddenly." At first glance, this language seems to describe an accident, perhaps even an *ones* — a completely unavoidable circumstance entirely beyond the *nazir*'s control. How, then, does the *Gemara* extract from it a case of *mezid*?

### Pitom: Three-Fold Meaning

The word *peta* — "suddenly" in the simpler sense — is understood to indicate *shogeg*, ordinary unintentional transgression, as in the *pasuk*: *im befeta belo eivah hadafo* — one who pushed another without premeditation or enmity. So *peta* means a normal case of unintended action.

The word *pitom*, however, carries multiple meanings. On one hand, *pitom zeh ones* — *pitom* can indicate something completely beyond one's control, as in *vayomer Hashem pitom el Moshe* — God spoke suddenly to Moshe, and when God addresses someone, it is entirely outside of that person's agency. On the other hand — and this is the striking teaching — *tanya idach*: another *baraita* teaches *pitom zeh mezid*, that *pitom* can mean the exact opposite: an intentional act. The source is the verse in *Mishlei*: *arum ra'ah ra'ah venistar ufetayim avru vena'anashu* — a shrewd person perceives danger and hides, while *petayim* — foolish people — transgress and are punished. The connection between *petayim* and *pitom* teaches that *pitom* can refer to a willful transgression as well.

### Petayim Derivation

The formal derivation relies on the linguistic relationship between *pitom* and *petayim*. The word in *Mishlei* is *petayim* — foolish ones who knowingly transgress — and this connects semantically to *pitom* in the *pasuk* about the *nazir*. Thus *pitom* is shown to encompass the full range from *ones* to *mezid*, with *peta* covering the middle ground of *shogeg*.

### Pitom Range Resolution

The *Gemara* then asks: why does the *pasuk* not simply say *pitom* alone, since as we have established, *pitom* by itself can mean *shogeg*, *mezid*, and *ones* — the entire spectrum? If *pitom* already implies all three, what does *peta* add?

The *Gemara* answers: *ei kasav kra pitom hava amina ki maysei korban be-shogeg* — if it had only said *pitom*, I would have interpreted it in the most natural way for a *korban*, which is *shogeg*. The classic case that obligates a *korban* is the middle ground: not *ones*, where there is no fault and no *korban* is expected, and not *mezid*, where the sin is too grave for a *korban* to serve as atonement. The standard *korban* is for *shogeg*. So I would have read *pitom* as referring to *shogeg*, just as all other cases in the Torah.

Therefore the Torah writes both *peta* — which unambiguously designates *shogeg* — and *pitom*. Since *peta* already covers *shogeg*, the word *pitom* cannot mean *shogeg*, and must therefore teach the other two ends: *ones* and *mezid*. *Ksav Rachmana pesa* — the Torah writes *peta* to signal *shogeg*, *legilluyei alah de-pitom*, to reveal that *pitom* refers specifically to *ones* and *mezid*. And *ve'afilu hachi hiyveih Rachmana* — even so, the Torah obligates the *korban* across that entire range.

The *Gemara* adds that one cannot argue from *ones* to *mezid* or vice versa by a *kal vachomer*, because each can be excluded on its own grounds: one might say *ones* requires no *korban* because the person has no guilt, or that *mezid* requires no *korban* because the sin is too weighty for it to provide *kapparah*. The *pasuk* must explicitly include both.

### Nazir: Metaphysical Fram…

This leads to a deeper conceptual observation. Why would it make sense that a *nazir* brings a *korban* even by *ones* — when he has no fault whatsoever — and even by *mezid* — when his guilt is at the highest level? The answer may lie in how we understand the *nazir*'s *korban* fundamentally. It is not primarily about atoning for the personal guilt of the act of becoming *tamei*. Rather, it is about the process of ending a *nezirut* of *tumah* and initiating a new *nezirut* of *taharah*. The *korban* is part of a transition, a changing of state, not a response to personal culpability.

Another formulation: the sin offering framework can be understood either in terms of personal fault — which is why *shogeg* and *mezid* normally matter — or in terms of a metaphysical reality that has been altered. When a *nezirut* is violated, something real has happened at the level of the *nezirut* itself, independent of the *nazir*'s state of mind. It is that metaphysical reality that the *korban* addresses and rectifies. This is why the categories of *ones*, *shogeg*, and *mezid* collapse here — personal blame is simply not the operative framework.

### Shvuat Eidut

The third case is *shvuat ha'edut* — swearing falsely that one has no testimony to offer. This is addressed explicitly in *Vayikra* 5: *ve-nefesh ki techeta ve-shama kol alah ve-hu eid o ra'ah o yada ve-im lo yagid ve-nasa avono* — a person who hears an oath of testimony, and who saw or knew, and does not testify — shall bear his sin. Critically, this *pasuk* does not contain the phrase *ve-ne'elam mimmennu* — "it was hidden from him" — which appears in adjacent cases involving *tumah* and a forgotten oath (*shvuat bitui*). In those cases, the Torah explicitly conditions the obligation on the transgression having been unintentional. Here, no such condition is stated.

The *Gemara* derives from the absence of *ve-ne'elam* that for *shvuat ha'edut*, intentionality is irrelevant — *lechayyev al hamezid keshogeg*. Since the unintentional criterion is simply not invoked, the obligation spans the full range of intent.

Moreover, this is one of the cases that brings a *korban oleh veyored* — a *korban* of variable value depending on the offerer's financial capacity. The same passage in *Vayikra* 5 covers multiple violations, and the distinction between those that include *ve-ne'elam* and the one that does not is precisely the lesson the *Gemara* draws here.

### Shvuat Hapikadon

The fourth case is *shvuat hapikadon* — taking a false oath to deny that one holds another's object in trust. The *Gemara* derives that this too obligates a *korban* even when intentional. The derivation works by way of a *gezerah shavah*: both *shvuat ha'edut* and *shvuat hapikadon* use the root *tacheta* — "shall sin" — and from that shared language we learn that just as *shvuat ha'edut* obligates across the spectrum of intent, so too does *shvuat hapikadon*: *yalif tach tach mishvuat ha'edut*.

## One Korban, Many Sins

### Five Cases Overview

The *mishnah* now moves to a second category of special cases: *chamishah mevi'in korban echad al aveirot harbeh* — five cases in which one *korban* covers multiple transgressions. Ordinarily, one *korban* addresses one sin; here, the rule is suspended. Alongside this, the *mishnah* notes that five cases bring a *korban oleh veyored* — a *korban* whose value varies with one's economic means. As noted, the structure is numerical: the organizing logic is the count, not the shared underlying principle.

The first *mishnah* in this section names only two of the five cases, with the remaining three appearing in subsequent *mishnayot*. The first two — *haba al hashifchah* and *nazir shenitma* — appear on both lists, the list of *mezid keshogeg* and the list of one *korban* for many sins. This overlap is itself instructive.

### Shifchah: One Korban, Ma…

For *haba al hashifchah*, the *mishnah* states: *haba al hashifchah bi'ot harbeh* — one who has sexual relations with a *shifchah charufa* multiple times brings only a single *korban*. The derivation is the same redundancy we saw earlier: *al chatato asher chata* teaches one *korban* for multiple violations, while *mechatato asher chata* teaches that even *mezid* is treated like *shogeg*. So the same textual redundancy in the *pasuk* generates both rules simultaneously.

The *Gemara* discusses the precise framing of the derivation — does the *pasuk* start from *shogeg* and extend to *mezid*, or start from *mezid* (given the presence of *malkot*) and extend to *shogeg*? As we saw earlier, because *bikoret tihyeh* implies *malkot*, the baseline of the *pasuk* is *mezid*, and the extension is to *shogeg*. The *halacha* remains: whether *shogeg* or *mezid*, whether once or many times, it is one *korban*.

### Gufin Mechulakim Distinc…

One might then ask: if the rule is that multiple acts of this sin require only one *korban*, does it matter whether those acts were with the same woman or with different women?

### R. Chanina's Question

This precise question was posed by *Rabi Chanina Tirna'ah* to *Rabi Yochanan*: *haba al chamesh shifchot charufot be'he'elam echad* — if a person had relations with five different *shifchot charufot* in one state of forgetfulness, *mahu*? Does the single-*korban* rule apply, or does he bring five separate *korbanot*? The case is pressed further: normally, five separate acts with the same woman across five states of awareness would require five *korbanot*. Here, the *chiddush* of the *pasuk* is that even five such acts constitute only one *korban*. Why, then, should five different women be treated differently if there is only one state of forgetfulness?

### Different Bodies Princip…

*Rabi Yochanan* responded: *ein shifchah achat gufin mecholakin* — one *shifchah* repeated multiple times does not constitute *gufin mechulakim* — different bodies. Five different *shifchot* are *gufin mechulakim* — they are fundamentally distinct entities. This is a recurring principle throughout *Keritot*: the distinctness of an act is determined far more by whether it involves different objects or persons than by the number of occasions or states of awareness. Multiple acts with the same person are essentially one thing played out through different states of awareness; multiple acts with different people are fundamentally distinct, each requiring its own *korban*.

The *Gemara* then asks: where do we know that the principle of *gufin mechulakim* applies specifically to the *shifchah* case, given the single-*korban* rule? *Rabi Yochanan* answers: just as by *arayot* generally, the word *ve'isha* in the *pasuk* teaches that each distinct woman creates a separate obligation — *lechalek al kol isha ve'isha* — so too by *shifchah*, the phrase *asher yishkav et isha...vehi shifchah* contains a seemingly redundant word that teaches *lechalek al kol shifchah veshifchah* — each *shifchah* constitutes a distinct category. Thus the rule of one *korban* for many acts applies only within a single *shifchah*; different women always require separate *korbanot*.

### Nazir Multiple Impurities

The second case on the list is *nazir shenitma tumot harbeh* — a *nazir* who became *tamei* many times. As with the *shifchah* case, this appears on both lists. The *Gemara* raises an important structural question about what the *chiddush* here actually is. It would be obvious, the *Gemara* implies, that if a *nazir* entered a cemetery five times on the same day — violating a single *nezirut* five times — he would bring only one *korban*. Once the *nezirut* has been violated, subsequent violations of that same *nezirut* add no new *korban* obligation; one *korban* wraps up the violated *nezirut* and initiates the new one. That is not a *chiddush*.

The *Gemara* therefore understands the *mishnah* to be speaking about a more radical case: a *nazir* who violated one *nezirut*, underwent the purification process, began a new *nezirut*, violated that *nezirut* as well, began yet another, and so on — multiple distinct sequential *neziruyot*, each independently violated. In that case, you would expect each violated *nezirut* to generate its own *korban* obligation. Yet the *mishnah* teaches that it is still only one *korban*. The question is: how is that possible?

### Nezirut Beginning: Day 7…

The answer depends on a *tannaitic* dispute about precisely when a new *nezirut detahara* begins. The *pasuk* says *vekidash et rosho bayom hahu* — he shall sanctify his head on that day. What is "that day"? *Rabbi* holds: *biyom havat korbanotav* — on the eighth day, when the *nazir* brings his *korbanot*. That is when his new *nezirut* begins. *Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda* holds: *biyom tiglachto* — on the seventh day, when he shaves; that is when the new *nezirut* commences.

### Day Seven Solution

*Rav Chisda* explains: the *mishnah*'s ruling — one *korban* for multiple violated *neziruyot* — is the opinion of *Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda*. Under his view, the new *nezirut* begins on day seven, before the *korban* is brought on day eight. Therefore, the following scenario is possible: a *nazir* becomes *tamei*, completes his seven-day purification, and on day seven — the moment his new *nezirut* begins — becomes *tamei* again. He has now violated a new *nezirut*, but he has not yet entered the period when he was obligated in a *korban* for the previous one. The cycle repeats: again on the seventh day, a new *nezirut* begins and is immediately violated. Since he never passed through a moment of being obligated in a *korban* before starting the next *nezirut*, all these violations coalesce into a single *korban* obligation at the end.

Under *Rabbi*'s view, this cannot work. *Rabbi* holds that the new *nezirut* begins only on day eight — the same moment the *korban* obligation crystallizes. As soon as day eight begins, he is simultaneously obligated in his previous *korban* and beginning his new *nezirut*. Therefore, if he becomes *tamei* on day eight and violates the new *nezirut*, that is a separate *korban* obligation, because he had already entered the time of obligation for the previous one. If he became *tamei* only on day seven, then under *Rabbi*'s view, he has never yet begun a new *nezirut* at all — it is all still one continuous *nezirut* that has been repeatedly violated. That is not a *chiddush*, and so the *mishnah* adds nothing. The *Gemara* concludes: *shema mina Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda* — this *mishnah* reflects the opinion of *Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda* alone.

This case is a conceptual midpoint between a *korban* brought for sin and a *korban* brought for *taharah*. The *nezirut*'s *korban* is about ending a *nezirut shel tumah* and beginning a *nezirut shel taharah*; it is part of a state-change rather than purely an act of atonement. This is why, as with the *mezid/shogeg* question, the number of violations is not the operative variable: what matters is whether the process has been formally closed and a new obligation has crystallized, not how many times the *nezirut* was transgressed.

## Sotah, Metzora, Yoledet

### Hamakane L'Ishto

The *mishnah* continues with additional cases of one *korban* for multiple events. The third case is *hamakane le'ishto al yedei anashim harbeh* — a husband who warned his wife about multiple men, and she was secluded with each of them. The *sotah* process involves the wife being brought to the *Beis HaMikdash* and drinking the *mei hamarim hamarerim*, and she brings a *minchat sotah* as part of that process. The ruling is that even if there were multiple warnings and multiple instances of seclusion, she brings only one *minchah* — not a separate offering for each suspicion.

### Sotah Derivation

The *Gemara* derives this from the verse *zot torat hakenaot* — this is the *Torah* for jealousy. The word *kenaot* is plural — jealousies — yet only one *Torah*, one law, governs all of them: *torah achat lekinuyim harbeh*. This is the source for treating multiple jealousy-episodes as a single case requiring a single *korban*.

The underlying logic, as with the *nazir*, is that the *korban* here is not focused on punishing or atoning for discrete acts of seclusion. It is about addressing the cloud of suspicion and determining the woman's status. Since it is one status question — regardless of how many occasions of suspicion arose — it is one process and one offering.

### Metzora Multiple Lesions

The fourth case is *metzora shenitna benegaim harbeh* — a *metzora* who developed many lesions of *tzara'at*. The *Gemara* derives this from *zot torat hametzora* — this is the law of the *metzora*: *torah achat lemetzoraim harbeh*, one law covering multiple instances. But the application requires clarification: what does it mean to have multiple *tzara'ot*? Simply having additional spots appear while one is already a *metzora* would not constitute distinct cases — that is just one continuous state of being *metzora*.

### Metzora Purification Sta…

The *Gemara* explains by analogy to the *nazir* case. The purification process of a *metzora* is multi-stage. On an early day, the *metzora* brings two birds: one is slaughtered and its blood used in the purification rite, while the other is released. This begins the purification. The *metzora* then waits seven days, shaves on day seven, and on day eight brings the full set of *korbanot* — an *asham*, a *chatat*, and an *olah* — completing the purification process.

The relevant scenario is: a *metzora* completes the birds stage and becomes *tahor* on day seven, but before day eight — before becoming obligated in the *korban* — he contracts *tzara'at* again. The cycle repeats. Since at no point did a *korban* obligation crystallize before the next *tzara'at* began, the *Gemara* holds that the entire accumulated purification process yields only one *korban* at the end. This is structurally identical to the *nazir* case under *Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda*: the new period begins before the *korban* obligation from the previous period is activated.

### Rich vs Poor Status

This leads to a separate legal question about the *metzora*'s *korban*, which is also a *korban oleh veyored* — one whose content varies with the offerer's financial means. If a wealthy *metzora* brings a full sheep as *chatat* and *olah*, while an impoverished *metzora* may substitute birds, at what moment is his status — rich or poor — determined? The *mishnah* addresses this: *ule'akvuei be'aniyut uve'ashirut lo mikva ad sheyavi chatat* — the status is not fixed until the moment of bringing the *chatat*. There is a separate *mishnah* elsewhere (*metzora shehevi ashamo vehe'ashir* — a *metzora* who brought his *asham* and then became wealthy) that confirms: *hakol holech achar chatat* — everything follows the *chatat*. That is the view of *Rabbi Shimon*.

### Three Tahara Views

Three *Tannaim* each interpret the operative phrase *betaharato* — "for his purification" — differently, and that interpretation determines which moment fixes the rich/poor status.

*Rabbi Shimon* holds *davar hamachapro* — *betaharato* means the act that atones for him, which is the *chatat*. Status is therefore determined at the time of the *chatat*. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds *davar hamachshiro* — *betaharato* means the act that renders him fit, that fully cleanses him, and that is the *asham*, which plays the central role in completing the purification ritual itself. Status is determined at the time of the *asham*. *Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* holds *davar hagotem lo tahara* — it refers to the act that initiates the purification for him, and that is the birds, which begin the entire process seven days earlier. Status is therefore fixed at the time of the birds.

All three base themselves on the same *pasuk* — *asher lo tasig yado betaharato* — and disagree only about which stage the word *betaharato* refers to.

### Sin vs Purification

This three-way debate exemplifies a recurring theme throughout this section of *Keritot*: which of the *metzora*'s *korbanot* is about sin and which is about purification? The *metzora* brings multiple distinct offerings — an *asham* whose blood is used in the physical purification rite, a *chatat* that seems more focused on atonement, and an *olah*. The *asham* is arguably the most distinctive, since its blood is applied to the *metzora*'s ear, thumb, and toe as part of the cleansing ceremony — suggesting it is primarily a purification mechanism rather than an atonement. The *chatat*, by contrast, connotes personal sin.

This ambiguity — between the *korban* as response to personal guilt and the *korban* as part of a metaphysical or ritual cleansing process — is the underlying tension running through all the cases in this *mishnah*. As was noted in the *nazir* context, the *chatat* framework does not map neatly onto personal culpability alone. Here, by the *metzora*, the *halacha* about multiple lesions yielding one *korban* fits naturally when we understand the *korban* as part of the purification process: if the process has not yet concluded, multiple episodes of *tzara'at* are all part of one ongoing purification trajectory, and one *korban* concludes it.

### Yoledet Cases

The fifth and final case is *ha'ishah sheyaldah veladot harbeh* — a woman who gave birth many times. The *Gemara* derives this from *zot torat hayoledet lezachar ule'nekevah* — this is the *Torah* of the woman who gives birth, for male or female: *melamed shemevia korban echad al veladot harbeh*, teaching that she brings one *korban* for multiple births.

The mechanism works as follows. A woman who gives birth to a female is *tamei* for 14 days and has a purification period extending to day 80, at which point — on day 81 — she brings her *korban*. For a male birth, the periods are 7 and 33 days, with the *korban* on day 41. For the rule of one *korban* for multiple births to apply, a subsequent birth must occur before the *korban* obligation from the prior birth has been activated — that is, before day 41 or day 81, as the case may be.

Given the timing, such scenarios require miscarriage: for instance, after a female birth, a woman became pregnant, and the fetus developed at least 40 days — the threshold at which a miscarriage constitutes a *leidah* — before she reached day 81. If this recurred multiple times, all those births occurred within one extended, uninterrupted purification window, obligating only one *korban* at the end. The *Gemara* also mentions the case of *hamapelet te'omim* — a woman whose twins were born days apart. If the second twin emerged before the obligation for a *korban* from the first birth crystallized, it too would be subsumed into a single *korban*.

### Yoledet vs Ziva

The *Gemara* asks whether the single-*korban* rule might extend even further — perhaps a woman who both gave birth and became a *zavah* (exhibiting *zivah*-class discharge) would also bring only one *korban*? The answer is no: *talmud lomar zot* — the word *zot* limits the rule. This is a qualitatively different *tumah* with its own *korban* obligation, and it cannot be collapsed into the *yoledet* process.

The *Gemara* presses the question: is it even meaningful to entertain such a *hava amina*? *Zivah* and *leidah* are entirely distinct categories. The *Gemara* reframes the question more precisely: the real *hava amina* is whether a birth that occurs after day 80 — after the *korban* obligation has crystallized — might nonetheless be subsumed in the single-*korban* rule, as in the *shifchah charufa* case where distinct acts nonetheless generate one *korban*. The answer is no: *talmud lomar zot* — the one-*korban* rule for *yoledet* applies only when the births are genuinely sequential and overlapping, not when they are fully distinct with a completed obligation in between. The *shifchah charufa* case is unique in that respect; the cases of *nazir*, *metzora*, and *yoledet* are grounded in the purification process and require that the new event occur before the prior obligation has closed.

## Rabbi Yehuda vs Chachamim

### R. Yehuda: Odd Births On…

Having established the general rule, the *mishnah* now records a dispute. *Rabbi Yehuda* takes a more restrictive position than the *Chachamim*: *mevia al harishon ve'eina mevia al hasheni, mevia al hashlishi ve'eina mevia al harevi'i* — she brings a *korban* for the first birth, not the second; for the third, not the fourth; and so on, alternating through odd and even births. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, the rule of one *korban* for multiple births applies only to pairs, not to an indefinite chain.

### Incorporation Logic

The logic behind *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is the concept of incorporation. The second birth, having occurred within the 80-day window of the first, is incorporated into and subsumed by the first — they count as one, requiring one *korban*. But the third birth is not within the 80-day window of the first birth; it may fall within the window of the second. However, once the second birth has been incorporated into the first and they are treated as a unit, the second birth loses its independent identity and cannot serve as an anchor to draw the third into its window. The third is therefore a new, independent event and requires its own *korban*. In turn, the fourth is incorporated into the third, the fifth stands alone, and so forth. *Vlad rishon gorem umivlad rishon moneh* — the first birth is the operative anchor, and counting proceeds from that first birth.

### Chachamim: Endpoint Focus

The *Chachamim* disagree. Their approach is endpoint-focused: *vlad sheni gorem umivlad sheni moneh* — or more precisely, they look to the last event in the chain. The *Chachamim* do not require that everything be incorporated into the first birth; rather, so long as the chain has not yet arrived at a point where a *korban* obligation has crystallized and been completed, all births remain part of one extended, ongoing process. The question is simply: have we reached an endpoint yet? Until a *korban* obligation is formally activated and discharged, the process continues, and all births within it yield only one *korban*. The *Chachamim* thus allow an indefinite extension of the chain, while *Rabbi Yehuda* limits the incorporation strictly to the first birth's 80-day window.

### Twins Clarification

The *Gemara* asks whether *Rabbi Yehuda*'s logic would be modified in the case of twins. One might think that since twins clearly share one pregnancy — one continuous gestational process — *Rabbi Yehuda* would acknowledge that even the second twin is simply a continuation of the first, and therefore that whatever follows the second twin would still be incorporated under the first birth's window, as though the first and second twins were truly one birth. If so, *Rabbi Yehuda* might agree with the *Chachamim* in the twins case, allowing a third birth following the second twin to be incorporated as well.

### Twins Confirm Rule

The *Gemara* answers that *Rabbi Yehuda*'s rule holds even for twins: *ka mashma lan* — the *mishnah* teaches this precisely to exclude that possibility. Even in the case of twins, where the shared gestational origin is obvious, *Rabbi Yehuda* insists that only the first birth is the anchor, and only events within the 80-day window of that first birth are incorporated. The second twin, once incorporated into the first, does not independently extend the window further. A third birth — whether from a subsequent pregnancy or a third triplet emerging separately — cannot be incorporated via the second, because the second has lost its independent standing by being subsumed into the first. *Rabbi Yehuda*'s principle of incorporation is strictly first-birth-focused, and no amount of biological continuity between subsequent births changes that calculus.

The *Gemara* notes the contrast: the *Chachamim*'s endpoint-focused approach would handle the twins case simply — since no *korban* obligation has been activated before the process concludes, it all remains one extended situation. *Rabbi Yehuda*'s approach requires a more formal accounting of which birth serves as anchor and which subsequent births fall within its precise window. The *sugya* continues on to the following *daf*.
