# Menachot 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Abraham & Churban

### Opening Dialogue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, מְצָאוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְאַבְרָהָם שֶׁהָיָה עוֹמֵד בְּבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, אָמַר לוֹ: ״מֶה לִידִידִי בְּבֵיתִי״?
>
> **Translation:** **At the time when the** First **Temple was destroyed, the Holy One, Blessed be He, found Abraham standing in the Temple. He said to** Abraham: **"What has My beloved to do in My house?"**

The *aggadata* under discussion was introduced somewhat earlier in the tractate in connection with a different sage, but it is brought here to connect with the *kodshim* context, touching on the destruction of the *Beit Hamikdash* and linking to a *pasuk* cited the previous day. The passage reads: *be-sha'ah shecharav Beit Hamikdash*, when the Temple was destroyed, God found Abraham standing within it. God said to him, *ma liydidi b'veisi* — "What is my beloved doing in my house?" This invokes the *pasuk* we had encountered in which Abraham is called *yedidi*, "my beloved." Abraham replied: *al iskei banai bati* — "I have come on account of my children; their Temple has been destroyed and they have been exiled." God responded bluntly: *banecha chatu v'galu* — they sinned and they were punished with exile.

### Abraham's Arguments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ עַל עִיסְקֵי בָּנַי בָּאתִי, אָמַר לוֹ: בָּנֶיךָ חָטְאוּ וְגָלוּ, אָמַר לוֹ: שֶׁמָּא בְּשׁוֹגֵג חָטְאוּ? אָמַר לוֹ: ״עֲשׂוֹתָהּ הַמְּזִמָּתָה״, אָמַר לוֹ: שֶׁמָּא מִיעוּטָן חָטְאוּ? אָמַר לוֹ: ״הָרַבִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** Abraham **said to** God: **I have come over matters** concerning **my children,** to discover why God is destroying the Temple and exiling them from Eretz Yisrael. God **said to** Abraham: The reason is that **your children sinned, and** therefore they are being **exiled** from the land. Abraham **said to** God: **Perhaps they sinned unwittingly,** and they do not deserve such a terrible punishment. God **said to** him: **"Seeing that she has performed lewdness [*hamzimmata*],"** i.e., her evil actions were intentional. Abraham further **said to** God: **Perhaps** only **a minority** of Jews **sinned,** and the rest of the people should be spared punishment. God **said to** him: **"With many,"** i.e., the majority of the people are culpable.

Abraham did not relent. He pressed forward with a series of increasingly focused arguments, each met by a divine rejoinder. First he proposed: *shema bishogeg chatu* — perhaps they sinned unintentionally and did not deserve so severe a punishment. God replied with a *pasuk* from Yirmiyahu indicating that their deeds were willful and premeditated — *mezimah*, a calculated act.

Abraham then shifted to a quantitative argument: *shema mi'utam chatu* — perhaps it was only a minority who sinned, not the whole people. God replied: *harabim* — it was the majority. Abraham then invoked the covenant: *haya lecha lizkor brit milah* — You should have remembered the *brit milah* they observed; surely that merit should have counted in their favor. God responded with a *pasuk* suggesting that the holy flesh — the *brit milah* — had effectively been abandoned, whether through the Hellenistic practice of surgically reversing the circumcision, or simply through non-observance. In either reading, the argument from *brit milah* is deflected.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה לְךָ לִזְכּוֹר בְּרִית מִילָה, אָמַר לוֹ: ״וּבְשַׂר קֹדֶשׁ יַעַבְרוּ מֵעָלָיִךְ״
>
> **Translation:** Abraham continued to contend: Even so, **You should have remembered** the merit of the **covenant of circumcision,** which would have protected them from retribution. God **said to** him: **"And the hallowed flesh is passed from you,"** as they neglected the mitzva of circumcision.

There is something striking about this entire interchange. Abraham is arriving, as it were, after the fact — the Temple is already destroyed, the people already in exile. What precisely is he trying to accomplish? He is not interceding to prevent a punishment; he is contesting the justice of one already executed. This is very much the posture Abraham adopted at Sodom and Gomorrah: standing for justice, demanding an accounting, refusing to simply accept the outcome.

Even more remarkable is the implicit bracketing of *zekhut avot*. At no point does Abraham say: remember what Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob did — *haya lecha lizkor l'Avraham Yitzchak v'Yaakov*. Even when Abraham mentions *brit milah*, the context is not his own keeping of the covenant but whether *they*, his descendants, kept it. The entire appeal is on their own terms. This connects to a theme noted elsewhere — Rav Preada's position that what matters is not your ancestors' accomplishments but your own, and that even lineage from *talmidei chakhamim* does not protect one who has not lived up to their example. The *gemara* here seems to give powerful expression to that view: even with Abraham himself as the advocate, the argument is entirely about judging Israel by their own deeds, not by ancestral merit. The *brit* of the patriarchs — *v'izkarti et briti Yaakov v'af et briti Yitzchak v'af et briti Avraham ezkor* — is conspicuously absent from the exchange.

### Heavenly Voice & Olive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: שֶׁמָּא אִם הִמְתַּנְתָּ לָהֶם הָיוּ חוֹזְרִין בִּתְשׁוּבָה, אָמַר לוֹ: ״כִּי רָעָתֵכִי אָז תַּעֲלֹזִי״. מִיָּד הִנִּיחַ יָדָיו עַל רֹאשׁוֹ, וְהָיָה צוֹעֵק וּבוֹכֶה, וְאָמַר לוֹ: שֶׁמָּא חַס וְשָׁלוֹם אֵין לָהֶם תַּקָּנָה? יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לוֹ: ״זַיִת רַעֲנָן יְפֵה פְרִי תֹאַר קָרָא ה׳ שְׁמֵךְ״
>
> **Translation:** Abraham persisted and **said to** God: **Perhaps if You would have waited for them, they would have returned in repentance.** God **said to** him: **"When you do evil then you rejoice."** That is, it is proper for them to be punished without delay, and they should not be given time to repent, since when they sin and are not punished they rejoice and live at ease, and rather than repent they are encouraged to do more evil. Once all his arguments had been refuted, Abraham **immediately placed his hands on his head** in an act of mourning, **and was screaming and crying. And** he **said to** God: Is it **conceivable, Heaven forbid,** that the Jewish people **have no** further opportunity for **remedy? A Divine Voice emerged and said to him** the continuation of the verse: **"The Lord called your name a leafy olive tree, fair with goodly fruit."**

Abraham then offered one final plea: *shema himtanta lahem hayu chozrim b'teshuvah* — perhaps had You waited, they would have repented; the punishment came too swiftly. God replied with a *pasuk* indicating that they rejoiced in their evil — Rashi understands this to mean their wickedness was entrenched and return was not forthcoming. At this point, *miyad haniach yado al rosho vehayah tzo'ek uvoche* — Abraham placed his hands on his head and broke into weeping, crying out: perhaps, *chas v'shalom*, there is no remedy for them at all, and they will never return.

It is worth pausing to note how unusual this imagery is. In the vast landscape of poetry, prayer, and *midrash* surrounding the destruction of the Temple, one typically encounters Rachel weeping for her children, the prophets lamenting — but not Abraham cast in this role of anguished intercessor crying over the *churban*. Yet here he is.

What follows is equally striking: *yatzta bat kol v'amra lo* — a heavenly voice emerged and spoke to him. Notably, it is no longer God who responds directly; it is the *bat kol*. This shift in voice might reflect the tension in the narrative between *middat hadin* and something softer — just as at the *akeidah* it was God who commanded and the angel who saved the day, here the heavenly voice steps in at the moment of crisis.

The *bat kol* quoted: *zayit ra'anan yafeh pri to'ar kara Hashem shmecha* — "A flourishing olive tree, beautiful of fruit, God has called your name." The interpretation given: *mah zayit zeh achrito b'sofo* — just as an olive tree yields its benefit at the end, after a long period of waiting (whether because the fruit ripens all at once or because the harvest must wait until the season arrives), so too *af Yisrael achritan b'sofan* — for Israel, the ultimate good will come at the end. The present moment is one of terrible difficulty, but the comparison to the olive tree promises eventual flourishing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה זַיִת (זוֹ) [זֶה] אַחֲרִיתוֹ בְּסוֹפוֹ, אַף יִשְׂרָאֵל אַחֲרִיתָן בְּסוֹפָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as** with regard to **this olive tree, its final** purpose is fulfilled **at its end,** when its fruit is picked, **so too,** with regard to **the Jewish people, their final** purpose will be fulfilled **at their end,** i.e., they will ultimately repent and return to Me.

### Spies & Merglim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְקוֹל הֲמוּלָּה גְדוֹלָה הִצִּית אֵשׁ עָלֶיהָ וְרָעוּ דָּלִיּוֹתָיו״, אָמַר רַבִּי חִינָּנָא בַּר פָּפָּא: לְקוֹל מִילֵּיהֶן שֶׁל מְרַגְּלִים נִיתְרוֹעֲעוּ דָּלִיּוֹתֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִינָּנָא בַּר פָּפָּא: דָּבָר גָּדוֹל דִּבְּרוּ מְרַגְּלִים בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה, ״כִּי חָזָק הוּא מִמֶּנוּ״ – אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״מִמֶּנוּ״ אֶלָּא ״מִמֶּנּוּ״, כִּבְיָכוֹל, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ בַּעַל הַבַּיִת אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא כֵּלָיו מִשָּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **"With the sound of a great tumult [*hamulla*] He has kindled fire upon it, and its branches [*daliyyotav*] are broken"** (Jeremiah 11:16). **Rabbi Ḥinnana bar Pappa says: From the sound of the words [*milleihen*] of the spies** that Moses sent to Eretz Yisrael and who brought back an evil report, **the branches of the Jewish people were broken. As Rabbi Ḥinnana bar Pappa says: The spies said a serious statement at that moment: "They are stronger than us"** (Numbers 13:31). **Do not read** the phrase as: "Stronger **than us [*mimmenu*],"** but **rather** read it as: Stronger **than Him [*mimmennu*],** meaning **that even the Homeowner,** God, **is unable to remove His belongings from there, as it were.** The spies were speaking heresy and claiming that the Canaanites were stronger than God Himself.

The *gemara* continues with the earlier part of the *pasuk*, which describes a *hamulah gedolah*, a great tumult, with fire set upon it and its young shoots shaken. Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa reads *hamulah* as an allusion to the words of the *meraglim* — the spies — and *nitra'u daliyoseihen shel Yisrael*, the foundational shoots of Israel were shaken to their roots and the future of the nation endangered. Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa elaborates: *davar gadol dibru meraglim b'otah sha'ah* — the spies said a terrible, weighty thing. The verse they uttered was *ki chazak hu mimennu* — "they are stronger than we." Reading *mimennu* not as "from us" but "from Him," the *gemara* invokes the principle of *al tikri* and understands the spies to have been saying, implicitly, that even God cannot drive them out — *kivyachol she'afilu ba'al habayit eino yachol l'hotzi eilav misham* — as though even the owner of the house cannot remove the squatters from within it. This blasphemy was so profound it placed the entire future of Israel in jeopardy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בְּרַבִּי חִינָּנָא: הַאי ״לְקוֹל הֲמוּלָּה גְדוֹלָה״, ״לְקוֹל מִלָּה״ מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא, אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְאַבְרָהָם: קוֹלְךָ שָׁמַעְתִּי וְחָמַלְתִּי עֲלֵיהֶם, אֲנִי אָמַרְתִּי יִשְׁתַּעְבְּדוּ בְּאַרְבַּע מַלְכִיּוֹת, כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת כְּשִׁיעוּר אַרְבַּע מַלְכִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya, son of Rabbi Ḥinnana, objects to this** interpretation. **This** phrase: **"With the sound of a great tumult [*hamulla*],"** is problematic according to your claim that it is a reference to the words of the spies. According to your interpretation, **it should have** stated: **With the sound of a word [*mila*]. Rather,** Rabbi Ḥiyya interprets this phrase in accordance with the earlier explanation that these verses are referring to Abraham's discussion with God at the time of the destruction of the Temple. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Abraham: I heard your voice, and I took pity [*ḥamalti*] on** the Jewish people and will punish them less harshly. **I** had previously **said** that the Jews **will be subjugated by four kingdoms:** Babylonia, Persia, Greece, and Rome, and **each and every one** of these kingdoms will subjugate them **for the measure** of time I had originally set for their subjugation to all **four kingdoms** put together.

Rabbi Chiya bar Chanina objects: if the allusion were to the words of the spies, the text should have used *milah* (word) rather than *hamulah* (tumult), so the reading is linguistically strained. The *gemara* therefore returns to the primary narrative and reads *hamulah* differently: the *bat kol* told Abraham that God said *kolcha shamati*, "I have heard your voice," and *chamulah*, playing on the letters, *vachimalti aleihem* — "I have had great compassion upon them." The shift between *heh* and *chet* underlies this reading; the *gemara* notes that in manuscripts of this period the two letters were sometimes written in ways difficult to distinguish, which facilitated such plays on the text.

### Four Kingdoms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא, כֹּל חֲדָא וַחֲדָא מַאי דִּפְסִיק לַהּ. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲנִי אָמַרְתִּי בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, עַכְשָׁיו בְּבַת אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** But **now** that you have prayed for them, the Jewish people will be subjugated to **each one** of these four kingdoms only for the amount of time **stipulated for** each one separately. **And there are** those **who say** that God said the following to Abraham: **I** initially **said** that these kingdoms will rule over the Jews **one after the other,** each of them for a separate period. **Now** I decree that their dominion shall occur **simultaneously** in different geographical regions, which will shorten the overall duration of the subjugation.

What then was the fire set upon the olive tree? The *gemara* understands God to be telling Abraham: *ani amarti sheyishta'adu b'arba malchuyot* — I decreed that they would be subjected to the four kingdoms, each for a full measure of servitude, on the model of the exile in Egypt, which lasted its full span. The four kingdoms understood by *chazal* are Egypt, Babylonia — corresponding to the destruction of the First Temple — Persia and Media, Greece, and Rome, which in *chazal*'s framework extends through Christianity to the present. Not all these kingdoms lasted equal durations: Media was brief, lasting only a few years, which illustrates the divine concession that follows. God says: *hashta kol chad v'chad mai d'pasik lei* — each kingdom will receive only its allotted portion, not the full weight originally intended for each.

There is also a second version: *ani amarti b'zeh v'zeh* — I originally meant for them to serve these kingdoms sequentially, one after another; *achshav b'vat achas* — now I am allowing them to serve concurrently, so that some Jews are under Greece while others are under Persia, the exiles overlapping, and the total duration thereby shortened. This is the meaning of God's compassion in response to Abraham's plea.

### Eternal Survival

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: לָמָּה נִמְשְׁלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְזַיִת? לוֹמַר לְךָ: מָה זַיִת אֵין עָלָיו נוֹשְׁרִין, לֹא בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה וְלֹא בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, אַף יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵין לָהֶם בְּטֵילָה עוֹלָמִית, לֹא בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה וְלֹא בָּעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: Why were the Jewish people likened to an olive tree?** It is **to tell you** that **just as the leaves of an olive tree never fall off, neither in the summer nor in the rainy season, so too, the Jewish people will never be nullified, neither in this world nor in the World-to-Come.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asks: why is Israel compared to an olive tree? To teach that *mah zayit ein alav noshrim lo b'yamot hachamah v'lo b'yamot hageshamim* — just as an olive tree's leaves do not fall whether in summer or in the rainy winter season, it is a perennial that retains its foliage year-round — *af Yisrael ein lahem beteilah olamit* — so too the Jewish people will never be destroyed, *lo b'olam hazeh v'lo b'olam haba*, neither in this world nor in the world to come. There will be suffering, oppression, and catastrophic tragedy, but ultimate survival is assured.

### Suffering & Teshuvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָמָּה נִמְשְׁלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְזַיִת? לוֹמַר לְךָ: מָה זַיִת אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא שַׁמְנוֹ אֶלָּא עַל יְדֵי כְּתִיתָה, אַף יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵין חוֹזְרִין לְמוּטָב אֶלָּא עַל יְדֵי יִסּוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Why were the Jewish people likened to an olive tree?** It is **to tell you** that **just as an olive tree brings forth its oil only by** means of **crushing** and breaking, **so too, the Jewish people,** if they sin, **return to good** ways **only by means of suffering.**

Rabbi Yochanan then offers a parallel reading of the same comparison. Why is Israel likened to an olive tree? Because *mah zayit eino motzi shemano ela al yedei ketisha* — an olive yields its oil only through pounding — *af Yisrael ein chozrim l'mutav ela al yedei yissurim* — so too, Israel returns to the right path only through suffering.

This is a jarring note on which to conclude what had seemed to be building toward consolation. The *gemara* invokes a well-known *midrash* from Tractate Megillah: all the admonitions of the prophets across centuries could not accomplish what the single handing of Achashveirosh's ring to Haman accomplished. The threat of catastrophe — the crisis hanging over the people — moves them to *teshuvah* in a way that words alone cannot. Rabbi Yochanan's point is realistic rather than comforting: yes, Israel survives, but the mechanism of return is often suffering itself. The concluding image is not a gentle one. This *aggadata* ends not with the softness of the heavenly voice and the olive tree parable but with Rabbi Yochanan pulling the reader back into a sober reckoning with how the world actually works.

## Making Chametz

### R. Meir vs. R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מַחְמִיצִין בְּתַפּוּחִים. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אָמְרוּ: מַחְמִיצִין. רַב כָּהֲנָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not leaven** the meal offerings that come as leavened bread **with** the juice of **apples,** as the dough will not leaven properly. It was **said in the name of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel** that **one may leaven** these meal offerings with juice from apples, as this is considered proper leavening. The Gemara notes that **Rav Kahana would teach this** *halakha* in the name **of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon,** not in the name of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel.

It is something of a relief to turn from the *aggadata* to the *halakhic* discussion, and, notably, to a discussion not of how to prevent *chametz* but of how to create it. Most *menachot* must be *matzah*; even those that are brought as *chametz* — most prominently the *shtei halechem* on *Shavuot* and certain loaves of the *todah* — may not be offered on the *mizbe'ach* in that state. But they must be made as *chametz*. How is this done?

Rabbi Meir says: you take some of the flour from within the measure, fashion it into sourdough (*se'or*), and reintroduce it to leaven the rest. Rabbi Yehuda objects that this will produce weak sourdough and prefers instead to take already-prepared sourdough from elsewhere, introducing it into the flour and then filling up the measure with the remaining flour.

### Density & Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: עִיסַּת הַשְּׂאוֹר עָבֶה – נִמְצֵאת יְתֵירָה מִדַּת הָעִשָּׂרוֹן, רַכָּה – נִמְצֵאת חֲסֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** If the **leavening dough** that was brought from elsewhere is **stiff,** as its flour was mixed with a small amount of water, and it is relatively small in volume, when this stiff leaven is measured with the fine flour, the amount of fine flour for the meal offering **is found** to be **greater** than **a tenth** of an ephah when **the measuring** vessel is filled. This is because it will be necessary to add more flour to make up for the small volume of leavening dough. Conversely, if the leavening dough is **soft,** i.e., its flour was mixed with a larger amount of water, its volume will be larger than it should be, which will mean that the measuring vessel will be filled with less fine flour than normal, and the amount of flour **is found** to be **lacking.**

The problem Rabbi Yehuda faces is one of accurate measurement. Sourdough differs in density from plain flour — it may be more compact or more airy. If you place sourdough into the measuring vessel and then fill the rest with flour, the total amount of flour will not accurately reflect one *issaron*, because the sourdough occupies volume differently than flour would. Rav Chisda articulates this precisely: if the sourdough is dense, it compresses into less space, leaving more room for flour than normal, resulting in more flour than the *issaron* calls for; if it is loose and airy, it takes up more space, resulting in less flour than required.

### Original vs. Current

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, כִּי קָא כָיֵיל לְעִשָּׂרוֹן קָא כָיֵיל. רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהֵן (הָיְתָה) מְשַׁעֲרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with the interpretation of Rav Ḥisda: Why does it matter if the leavening dough is stiff or soft? **Ultimately, when** the one preparing the meal offering **measures** the leaven brought from elsewhere together with the fine flour used for the meal offering, **he measures** and reaches the requisite amount **of a tenth** of an ephah, as the measure is full either way. **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say:** In order to achieve the appropriate measure it is necessary to know how much flour the substance contained before water was added and it became dough. The reason is that **one measures** the tenth of an ephah **in accordance with** the amount there **was** of the flour of the leavening dough before it was mixed with water, together with the fine flour of the meal offering, and not in accordance with their present volume.

One might ask: why not simply fill the measure to the top and accept whatever we have? Rabba and Rav Yosef both say: *likemot shehein hayu mesharinan* — you measure not according to what you now have in front of you but according to what it should have been originally. The Torah specifies an *issaron solet* — an *issaron* of flour, not of dough. The measuring standard is the original ingredient, the flour, not the current mixture.

One proposed solution is to take a portion of flour outside the *Beit Hamikdash*, prepare it as sourdough there, and then bring it back in — either following Rabbi Meir's approach over a longer period to produce stronger sourdough, or using the outside preparation as a test run to determine how much flour would fill the *issaron* at the relevant density, thereby enabling accurate approximation.

### Gezeira & Appearance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִישְׁקוֹל פּוּרְתָּא מִינֵּיהּ, וְלַיחְמְצֵיהּ מֵאַבָּרַאי, וְלַיְתְיַהּ וְנִילוּשֵׁיהּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ? גְּזֵירָה, דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מֵעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** even according to the opinion of Rabbis, who hold that Rabbi Yehuda's method may not be used because the amount might be lacking or be greater than it should be, **let him take a little** fine flour **from** the tenth of an ephah after it has been measured and found to be the requisite amount, **and leaven it** thoroughly **outside** the rest of the dough, **and** afterward **bring it and knead it together with** the rest of the dough. In this way it is possible to bring leaven from the outside and to be certain that the meal offering contains exactly the correct measure. The Gemara answers: One cannot proceed in this manner, due to a rabbinic **decree.** The reason for this decree is that people who would see this practice might mistakenly think that the leavened portion was not part of the original fine flour, and **perhaps they will come to bring** leavening dough for their meal offerings **from elsewhere,** i.e., leavening dough that has not been consecrated for the meal offering.

The *gemara* rejects this approach on account of a *gezeira*: *dilma ati l'ashvuyei me'alma* — people will see sourdough being brought in from outside and placed into the measure after the initial measuring, and they will conclude that one may add sourdough to the flour after measuring, as though the sourdough need not constitute part of the measured quantity. This misleading appearance is unacceptable. The result is that one must measure everything together at once. According to Rabbi Meir, the sourdough is fashioned quickly from part of the flour itself; according to Rabbi Yehuda, the outside sourdough is placed in first and the remaining flour is used to fill the measure, without ever removing and reintroducing material.

## Fruit Juices & Water

### Mei Peirot Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דִּתְנַן: תַּפּוּחַ שֶׁרִיסְּקוֹ, וּנְתָנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ הָעִיסָּה, וְחִימְּצָה – הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this dispute. **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 10:2): In the case of **an apple** of *teruma* **that one mashed and placed in** non-sacred **dough, and** the juice of the apple **leavened** the dough, **this** dough **is prohibited** to be consumed by anyone who may not partake of *teruma*.

The *gemara* now addresses a further dimension of *chametz* in the context of *menachot*: the principle that *mei peirot* — fruit juices — do not create *chametz*. Water is the agent required for leavening; other liquids, including fruit juices and, as the *gemara* will discuss, egg, do not cause real *chametz*. This principle has significant implications both for the question of how certain *menachot* become *chametz* and for the well-known discussions about egg *matzah* on *Pesach*.

### R. Chanina vs. Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, נְהִי דְּחָמֵץ גָּמוּר לָא הָוֵי, נוּקְשֶׁה מִיהָא הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this suggestion: **You** may **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. Granted,** the Rabbis hold **that** dough leavened by the juice of apples does **not become full-fledged leavened bread,** but **in any event it becomes hardened [*nukshe*]** leaven. Consequently, dough leavened by the juice of *teruma* apples is prohibited to non-priests.

The *mishnah* rules that one may not use apple juice to make the *chametz* required for a *minchah*. However, in the name of Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel, the view is stated that apple juice (*tapuchim*) does create *chametz* — so that, according to this view, apple juice could in principle be used for a *minchah* requiring *chametz*. Rav Kahana transmits this ruling in the name of Rabbi Chanina ben Teradyon rather than Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel. There is an additional question lurking here: even if apple juice could create *chametz*, on what basis would one be permitted to introduce an ingredient not specified by the Torah? The flour and oil are given; water is not mentioned at all. The implicit understanding is that since the Torah requires these *menachot* to be *chametz*, the process of leavening — whatever it entails — is part of fulfilling the Torah's requirement, granting some latitude in the choice of leavening agent. Still, the question is notable, particularly since, as will emerge, water itself is similarly unmentioned in the Torah's text.

### Trumos Evidence

The *gemara* attempts to determine which position — Rabbi Chanina's or the *rabbanan*'s — is supported by a *mishnah* in Tractate Trumos (perek 3, mishnah 2). The case: an apple that is *terumah* is crushed and placed into dough. The general rule is that *terumah* becomes *battel* — nullified — when mixed with a much larger quantity of non-*terumah* material. However, one exception is when the *terumah* causes the dough to ferment: in that case its effect is felt throughout and it cannot be considered nullified. The *mishnah* rules that the dough is *asur* — forbidden — meaning the apple is considered still present, because it caused fermentation. This seems to prove that apple juice is *machmitz* — it creates leavening — which would support Rabbi Chanina's position.

The *gemara* responds that this proof can be deflected even by the *rabbanan*. The *rabbanan* who prohibit apple juice for a *minchah* may acknowledge that apple juice creates *chametz nuksha* — a quasi-*chametz*, the intermediate state between full leavening and true *matzah* that was discussed previously. This quasi-*chametz* would be sufficient to prevent *bittul* in the *trumos* context, since the apple's fermenting effect is still operative, even if it does not rise to the level of *chametz gamur* required for the *minchah*.

This raises a further question about the definition of *chametz* and *matzah* in the context of *menachot*. When the Torah requires a *minchah* to be *matzah*, we established previously that it must be genuine *matzah* — *chametz nuksha* will not satisfy the requirement. By the same logic, when the Torah requires *chametz*, presumably one needs *chametz gamur*, real leavening — not this intermediate quasi-*chametz* produced by fruit juices, at least according to the *rabbanan*.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot engages at length with the broader question of *mei peirot* and *chametz*, both in this tractate and in Tractate Pesachim. The difficulty is that different *gemarot* seem to point in different directions: some suggest that *mei peirot* can produce real *chametz* — *chametz gamur* for which one would be liable to *karet* on *Pesach* — which would represent the position of Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel; others seem to indicate that fruit juices pose no problem at all, not even *rabbinically*, and that one could leave flour kneaded with fruit juices unattended without concern that it would become *chametz*.

Tosafot's general resolution — though he works through it with considerable back-and-forth — is that without water, fruit juices will never produce real *chametz*. At most, one might arrive at *chametz nuksha*, and only in certain circumstances. Real *chametz*, or *chametz* that arises faster than normal, occurs when fruit juices are combined *with* water. A separate *gemara* states that *mei peirot* can cause *chametz* to develop faster than water alone; Tosafot understands this to refer to the combination of fruit juices and water together. Without water, fruit juices alone never create real *chametz* according to this analysis.

This position underpins the entire discussion about egg *matzah* on *Pesach*. The *minhag* not to eat egg *matzah* on *Pesach* — except for the elderly or ill who cannot eat ordinary *matzah* — arises precisely because it is possible to read the *gemara* that says fruit juices accelerate leavening as referring to fruit juices without water. If fruit juices alone can produce *chametz* faster than water does, one would have very little confidence in the *chametz*-free status of egg *matzah*. But according to Tosafot, that concern only applies when water is also present.

### Mishnah & Rambam

This brings the discussion to the question of water in the *menachot* generally. The Torah specifies flour and oil; it does not mention water. Yet a later *mishnah* — which will be reached in approximately two days — states that *kol haminchot niloshot b'poshrim*, all *menachot* are kneaded with lukewarm water. The *gemara* there raises an objection: what about the *minchat nesachim* — the flour offerings that accompanied animal *korbanot* — where the *gemara* seems to assume no water was used? This then opens a renewed discussion of *mei peirot* and *chametz*.

Tosafot addresses the apparent inconsistency. If all *menachot* had water, why does the *gemara* assume the *minchat nesachim* did not? Tosafot explains that the *minchat nesachim* was distinguished by having a much larger proportion of oil mixed into the flour. Because of this, Tosafot reasons, it is assumed that the oil functioned in place of water in the kneading process, and therefore the *minchat nesachim* may not have required water and may have had no capacity to become *chametz*. According to this reading, all other *menachot* did include water — hence the relevance of the *chametz* prohibition — with the *minchat nesachim* as the lone exception due to its oil-heavy composition.

The Rambam reads the same *mishnah* differently. Rather than *kol haminchot niloshot b'poshrim*, the Rambam's version is *kol haminchot hane'afot niloshot b'poshrim* — only those *menachot* that are *baked* are kneaded with lukewarm water. Certain *menachot* are not baked but are rather fried in oil — essentially flour cooked in oil — and for those, no water is involved and no issue of *chametz* arises. According to the Rambam, water enters the picture only for *menachot* that take the form of bread: either unleavened bread (*matzah*) or leavened bread (*chametz*). The *shtei halechem* and similar *chametz* loaves are baked and thus require water; the question of whether they must be *matzah* or *chametz* is then precisely a question about what is done with that water — whether one allows leavening or prevents it. The Rambam's approach neatly contains the relevance of water to the baking context, explaining why the Torah does not treat it as a primary ingredient: it is a means to an end in bread-making, not an offering in its own right.

The practical upshot is that, according to Tosafot, *mei peirot* alone are insufficient for *chametz* — water is essential. This in turn explains the entire halakhic framework of *chametz* versus *matzah* in the *menachot*: the *chametz*-or-*matzah* question only arises where water is present, whether in all *menachot* (Tosafot) or only in the baked ones (Rambam).

## Minchat Chotei

### Kemitzah Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אִילָא: אֵין לְךָ הַקָּשָׁה לִקְמִיצָה יוֹתֵר מִמִּנְחַת חוֹטֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ila says:** Of all the meal offerings, **you do not have** a meal offering **whose removal of** the **handful is more difficult than** that of **the meal offering of a sinner.** This particular meal offering is dry, as no oil is added to it. Therefore, it is very difficult to remove precisely a handful, as when the priest takes a handful with his thumb and little finger, a large amount of flour is apt to fall out.

The *gemara* turns now to the *minchat chotei* — the sinner's meal offering. Rav Ila states: *ein lecha kasheh likmitzah yoter miminchat chote* — there is nothing more difficult to perform *kemitzah* on than the *minchat chotei*. The *kemitzah* — the taking of a precisely measured handful — is the central act of the *minchah* service, and it must be done from the flour of the offering. The problem with the *minchat chotei* is that it contains nothing but plain flour: *lo yasim aleha shemen velo yiten aleha levonah* — no oil and no *levonah* are added, because it is a *chatat*-type offering and must be treated accordingly. Without oil to bind the flour together, taking a precise *kemitzah* from loose dry flour is extraordinarily difficult — particles spill, it is nearly impossible to hold an exact quantity in one's hand.

### Adding Water Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי אָמַר: מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא מְגַבְּלָהּ בַּמַּיִם, וּכְשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi says:** The removal of the handful in the case of the meal offering of a sinner is no more difficult than its removal in other meal offerings. The reason is that although the Torah prohibited the addition of oil to **the meal offering of a sinner,** nevertheless the priest **may knead it in water, and** it is **fit** to be offered.

Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi proposes a solution: *minchat chote megablah bemayim uksheirah* — one may knead the *minchat chotei* with water, and it remains *kasher*. Adding water binds the flour together sufficiently that *kemitzah* becomes practicable. This is a striking ruling, since water is not an ingredient prescribed by the Torah for this offering. Here, as with the earlier discussion of apple juice for leavening, the *gemara* seems unconcerned about the introduction of an unlisted ingredient. One might ask further: even if the addition does not invalidate the *minchah*, does placing this water-and-flour mixture on the *mizbe'ach* not constitute *chullin ba'azara* — a non-sacred substance being brought into the Temple precincts? The *gemara* appears to set this concern aside, perhaps because the water is wholly incidental and functional rather than something presented as part of the offering itself. The *gemara* elsewhere seems to operate on the implicit assumption that a *minchah*, as a constructed offering, allows for a measure of tolerance regarding ancillary ingredients.

This also clarifies an important point raised in connection with the earlier discussion. According to Tosafot, all *menachot* except the *minchat chotei* included water. The *minchat chotei* and the *minchat sotah* — which similarly consists of plain dry flour — would be the exceptions that never had water. The present passage reveals that even here, water can be added *post facto* to enable the *kemitzah* procedure.

### Chareivah Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר: כְּמוֹת שֶׁהֵן מְשַׁעֲרִינַן, וּמָר סָבַר: לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ מְשַׁעֲרִינַן. לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כְּמוֹת שֶׁהֵן מְשַׁעֲרִינַן, וּבְהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר מַאי ״חֲרֵיבָה״? חֲרֵיבָה מִשֶּׁמֶן, וּמָר סָבַר חֲרֵיבָה מִכׇּל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara reiterates the question: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this mishna? **Shall we say** that it **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel,** who maintains that the juice of apples causes the dough to leaven properly, **and not** in accordance with the ruling of **the Rabbis,** the majority opinion that disputes this ruling? The Gemara refutes this suggestion: **You** may **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. Granted,** the Rabbis hold **that** dough leavened by the juice of apples does **not become full-fledged leavened bread,** but **in any event it becomes hardened [*nukshe*]** leaven. Consequently, dough leavened by the juice of *teruma* apples is prohibited to non-priests. ...  No, as **everyone** agrees that **one measures** meal offerings **as they** currently **are. And** it is **with regard to this** that **they disagree: As** one **Sage,** Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi, **holds: What** is the meaning of **"dry"** written in the verse discussing a meal offering: "And every meal offering, mixed with oil, or dry" (Leviticus 7:10)? It means **dry of oil,** but one may add water. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Ila, **holds** that the meal offering of a sinner must be **dry of all substances,** i.e., it may not contain even water.

The *gemara* then frames the underlying dispute. The position of Rav Ila — who did not hold that water may be added — is explained as follows: the Torah uses the term *chareivah* to describe this *minchah*, meaning dry. The question is what *chareivah* excludes. One view is *chareivah mishemen* — dry means without oil; since the Torah says dry, it excludes oil, but water would be permissible. The other view is *chareivah mikol davar* — dry means without any liquid whatsoever, and therefore water is equally forbidden. This is the actual debate, and it is a straightforward question of how to read the Torah's descriptive term. If *chareivah* only excludes oil — the liquid specifically associated with *menachot* — water remains available as a practical tool. If it excludes all liquids, the *minchat chotei* must remain entirely dry, leaving the difficulty of *kemitzah* unresolved.

## Measurement Principle

### Uktzin Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: בְּשַׂר הָעֵגֶל שֶׁנִּתְפַּח, וּבְשַׂר זְקֵנָה שֶׁנִּתְמַעֵךְ, מִשְׁתַּעֲרִין לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Okatzin* 2:8): **Meat of a calf that swelled** due to cooking, as the volume of calf flesh increases when it is cooked in water, **or meat of an old** animal **that shrank** due to cooking, which is what happens to meat of this type, are to be **measured as they are** in order to determine if they are the volume of an egg-bulk, in which case they can contract ritual impurity and transfer impurity of food to other items.

The question concerning whether a meal offering is to be measured in its current, mixed state or as it was before it was mixed relates to a fundamental issue that also arises in other areas of *halakha*. The broader *halakhic* issue is: when an object changes in size, do we assess its status based on its current size or its original size? A *mishnah* in Tractate Uktzin provides the launching point. The case involves *basar ha'eigel* — flesh of a calf — which, when left around, tends to expand and bloat, and *basar zekeinah* — flesh of an old animal — which tends to shrivel and contract. The minimum quantity required to transfer *tumah* is generally a *kebeitzah* — the size of an egg. So: if calf meat started below a *kebeitzah* and bloated to a *kebeitzah*, or if aged meat started at a *kebeitzah* and shriveled to below a *kebeitzah*, does one measure based on the current state or the original state?

The *mishnah* states *mishta'arin kemot shehen* — we measure according to the way they are. The difficulty is whether this phrase means *as they are now* or *as they were then*, and two groups of *amora'im* read it differently.

Tosafot notes, parenthetically, that whatever conclusion is reached in this *tumah*-related discussion need not apply mechanically to the *minchah* context. There, the Torah explicitly says *issaron solet* — an *issaron* of flour. The logic of measuring by the original ingredient is independently compelling from the wording of the verse itself, regardless of how we resolve the general principle for *tumah*.

### Amoraim Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמְרִי: מִשְׁתַּעֲרִין כְּמוֹת שֶׁהֵן, שְׁמוּאֵל וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמְרִי: מִשְׁתַּעֲרִין לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav, Rabbi Ḥiyya, and Rabbi Yoḥanan** all **say** that it means the items are to be **measured as they are** currently, after having been cooked. **Shmuel, Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and Reish Lakish** all **say** it means they are to be **measured according to** their volume **as they are,** before having been cooked. In other words, even if the meat of a calf is the volume of an egg-bulk after it was cooked, if it was less than this before cooking it cannot contract ritual impurity. Conversely, even if the meat of an old animal was less than an egg-bulk after cooking, if it was an egg-bulk before it was cooked it can contract ritual impurity.

Rabbi Chiya and Rabbi Yochanan read the *mishnah* as *mishta'arin kemot shehen* — we measure according to the way they are *right now*. Shmuel, Rabbi Shimon b'Rabbi, and Reish Lakish read it as *l'kemot shehayu* — we measure according to the way they *were* originally. The textual difference hinges on whether one reads *kemot shehen* (as they are) or *l'kemot shehen* / *l'kemot shehayu* (toward what they were), with the addition of a *lamed* directing the phrase backward in time. The *gemara* notes that the distinction between *kemot shehen* and *l'kemot shehen* is subtle enough that the clearer formulation *l'kemot shehayu* was perhaps the original reading, or alternatively that some manuscripts already carried this more explicit version.

### Calf Meat Braitah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: בְּשַׂר הָעֵגֶל שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בּוֹ כְּשִׁיעוּר, וְתָפַח וְעָמַד עַל כְּשִׁיעוּר – טָהוֹר לְשֶׁעָבַר, וְטָמֵא מִיכָּן וּלְהַבָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Shmuel, Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, and Reish Lakish from a *baraita*: With regard to **meat of a calf that** came into contact with a source of ritual impurity but that **was not of** a volume equivalent **to** the minimum **measure** for contracting impurity, i.e., an egg-bulk, if it was then cooked, **and** as a result it **swelled** until **it stood at** the requisite **measure** for ritual impurity, this meat is **pure with regard to the past, but** can become **impure** and render other items impure **from here on.** In other words, the previous contact with a source of impurity did not render the meat impure, as it was of insufficient volume at the time. This indicates that the status of an item with regard to ritual impurity depends on its volume at the current moment.

A *braitah* is then cited that appears quite explicit: *basar ha'eigel shelo haya bo kishiur v'tapach* — calf meat that did not have the requisite measure and then expanded. The *braitah* rules: *tahor l'she'avar v'tamei mikan ul'hava* — it was *tahor* until now and is *tamei* from this point forward. The ruling is unambiguous: once the meat has reached the size of a *kebeitzah*, it now communicates *tumah*, regardless of having started below that threshold. This appears to straightforwardly support the view that current size governs.

Those who hold that original size governs respond that this *braitah* reflects only a Rabbinic stringency — *mi'derabbanan*. Biblically, they would insist, you go by what the object's size was originally, and since it started too small, it never attained the proper status; but *rabbinically* the ruling is that if it now reaches the threshold, it will communicate *tumah* going forward.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּרַבָּנַן. אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וְכֵן בְּפִיגּוּל, וְכֵן בְּנוֹתָר. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, הַיְינוּ דְּאִיכָּא פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּרַבָּנַן, פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר בִּדְרַבָּנַן מִי אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is possible that by Torah law this meat is in fact not susceptible to ritual impurity even from here on, as the *halakha* defines it by its volume prior to cooking. Nevertheless, the *baraita* teaches that it is impure **by rabbinic law.** The Gemara asks: **If so, say the latter clause** of that *baraita*: **And similarly,** the same principle applies **to an offering that was sacrificed with the intent to consume it after its designated time [*piggul*], and similarly with regard to** food **left over from an offering after the time allotted for its consumption [*notar*].** The Gemara analyzes this statement: **Granted, if you say** that the *baraita* is discussing *halakhot* that apply **by Torah law, this is** why the cases of ***piggul* and *notar* are** included in the *baraita*, as they also apply by Torah law. **But if you say** that the *halakhot* in the *baraita* apply **by rabbinic law, are there *piggul* and *notar* by rabbinic law?**

The *gemara* presses: if this is only Rabbinic, how does the *braitah* proceed to apply the same logic to *pigul* and *notar*? One does not become *chayav* — subject to *karet* — for a Rabbinic prohibition. If eating a re-expanded piece of *pigul* or *notar* makes one *chayav*, that liability must be biblical. The *gemara* resolves this by reinterpreting the *braitah*'s reference to *pigul* and *notar*: it does not mean one who *eats* them is *chayav*, but rather that *pigul* and *notar* can themselves *communicate tumah*, since these categories of invalidated sacrificial meat carry a Rabbinic *tumah*. The *braitah* is saying that once calf meat reaches the required size, even if it is *pigul* or *notar* — and thus a source of Rabbinic *tumah* — it will communicate that *tumah*. The concern was that piling a Rabbinic *tumah* status onto a Rabbinic rule about measurement would be excessive; *kol hai b'derabbanan lo avid rabbanan* — the Rabbis would not compound Rabbinic ruling upon Rabbinic ruling to that degree. The *braitah* teaches that they did.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: וְכֵן בְּטוּמְאַת פִּיגּוּל, וְכֵן בְּטוּמְאַת נוֹתָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Say** that the *baraita* is not referring to the prohibitions of *piggul* and *notar*. Rather, it means the following: **And similarly,** the same principle applies **with regard to ritual impurity** imparted by ***piggul*, and similarly with regard to ritual impurity** imparted by ***notar*.** There is a rabbinic decree that meat which is *piggul* or *notar* imparts impurity to one's hands even if the meat itself was not impure. The *baraita* teaches that if the meat is at least the volume of an egg-bulk after it was cooked, it renders the hands impure in this manner.

### Old Meat Shrinkage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בְּשַׂר זְקֵנָה שֶׁהָיָה בּוֹ כְּשִׁיעוּר, וְצָמַק פָּחוֹת מִכְּשִׁיעוּר – טָמֵא לְשֶׁעָבַר, וְטָהוֹר מִיכָּן וּלְהַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to analyze this dispute. **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: With regard to **meat** of **an old** animal **that** initially **was** of a volume equivalent **to** the requisite **measure** for contracting ritual impurity and became impure, after which it was cooked **and** as a result **shrank** until it was **less than** the requisite **measure** for contracting impurity, it is considered **impure with regard to** items that touched it in **the past,** when it was large enough, **and** it is **pure** with regard to items that it may touch **from here on.** This *baraita* indicates that the current state of the item is decisive.

The *gemara* then examines the reverse case from the same *braitah*: *basar zekeinah shehaya bo kishiur v'tzamak* — aged meat that had the requisite measure and then shriveled. The ruling: *tamei l'she'avar v'tahor mikan ul'hava* — it was *tamei* before and is *tahor* from this point forward. This case cuts in the opposite direction from the calf meat. Here, the ruling is explicitly lenient: the meat started at the right size, shriveled below it, and is now treated as *tahor* based on its current state. One cannot argue this is merely a Rabbinic leniency; no Rabbinic principle makes things *tahor* that are biblically *tamei*. This appears to be a clear, binding ruling that current size governs, and that the original size cannot sustain *tumah* status once the meat has shriveled below the threshold.

## Rava's Framework

### Three Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא הֲוָה בֵּיהּ, וְהַשְׁתָּא לֵית בֵּיהּ – הָא לֵית בֵּיהּ, וְכֹּל הֵיכָא דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָא הֲוָה בֵּיהּ וְהַשְׁתָּא הֲוָה בֵּיהּ – מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said,** in explanation of the dispute between the *amora'im*: **Anywhere,** i.e., with regard to any item, **that was initially of** a volume equivalent to the requisite measure, **but now it is not of** such a volume, the *halakha* is determined according to the item's current volume, and **it is not of** the requisite volume. Conversely, **anywhere,** i.e., with regard to any item, **that initially was not of** the requisite measure **but now it is of** sufficient volume, the item contracts impurity **by rabbinic law.**

Rava steps in to reconcile the various positions and minimize the scope of the dispute. His analysis proceeds through three logical scenarios. First: an object that originally had the required size — there is no question that it had the status corresponding to that size. Second: an object that currently retains the required size — there is similarly no question that whatever consequences flow from that size apply now. Third: an object that both originally had and currently has the required size — again, unambiguous.

### The Real Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיָה בּוֹ כְּשִׁיעוּר וְצָמַק וְחָזַר וְתָפַח, דְּמָר סָבַר: יֵשׁ דִּיחוּי בְּאִיסּוּרָא, וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין דִּיחוּי בְּאִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **When they disagree** is in a case **where** the food initially **had** the requisite **measure** for ritual impurity, **and it shrank** until it was less than this measure, **and** subsequently it **again swelled** to the requisite measure for contracting impurity. The dispute is **that** one **Sage,** i.e., Shmuel, Rabbi Shimon, and Reish Lakish, **holds: There is disqualification with regard to a ritual matter,** including impurity. In other words, if at a certain point the food was less than the requisite measure it becomes entirely disqualified from contracting ritual impurity, even if it subsequently swells again. **And** one **Sage,** i.e., Rav, Rav Ḥiyya, and Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds: There is no disqualification with regard to a ritual matter.** Even if at a certain stage the food lost its ability to contract impurity, if it later swells it can once again become impure.

The case where opinions diverge is far narrower: an object that *originally* had the required size (*deikara havei keshiur*), then *shriveled* below it (*v'tzamak*), and subsequently *re-expanded* back to its original size (*v'chazar v'tipach*). It started at the threshold, temporarily fell below it, and is now back at the threshold. Rava emphasizes that both camps — those who say you measure by current state and those who say you measure by original state — would agree on the easy cases. Nobody would say that something currently below the threshold communicates *tumah* because it once was large enough; that position would be indefensible. And conversely, nobody would deny that something currently at the proper size has the requisite status if it *also* originally had that status. The genuinely contested case is only when there has been an intermediate period of loss.

### Dichuiy Debate

Rava frames the dispute in terms of *dichuiy* — the concept of an object being "pushed off" or losing a status it once had. The term appears most prominently in the laws of *korbanot*, where an animal that became invalid at some point can in certain cases be considered permanently disqualified even if the condition that invalidated it later resolves. Rava applies this concept more broadly to *issurin* — forbidden items and their associated statuses. *Yesh dichuiy b'issura*: once the object temporarily lost the minimum size and with it the capacity to communicate *tumah* or the status of a forbidden food, that status was permanently expelled; re-expansion cannot restore what was once lost. *Ein dichuiy b'issura*: the intermediate shrinkage is inconsequential; since the object began at the required size and has returned to it, its original status governs.

## Forbidden Foods Proof

### Eggs in Sun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וְהָתְנַן: כְּבֵיצָה אֳכָלִין שֶׁהִנִּיחָן בַּחַמָּה וְנִתְמַעֲטוּ, וְכֵן כְּזַיִת מִן הַמֵּת, כְּזַיִת מִן הַנְּבֵלָה, וְכַעֲדָשָׁה מִן הַשֶּׁרֶץ, וּכְזַיִת פִּיגּוּל, וּכְזַיִת נוֹתָר, וּכְזַיִת חֵלֶב – טְהוֹרִין, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר וָחֵלֶב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is there one who says** that **there is disqualification with regard to ritual matters? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Teharot* 3:6): In the case **of an egg-bulk** of a ritually impure **food that one placed in the sun and** that therefore **shrank** to less than an egg-bulk; **and similarly** in the case of **an olive-bulk** of flesh **of a corpse,** or **an olive-bulk of an animal carcass, or a lentil-bulk of a creeping animal,** all of which impart impurity; **or an olive-bulk** of ***piggul*, or an olive-bulk** of ***notar*, or an olive-bulk** of forbidden **fat,** if any of these were placed in the sun and shrank, they **are pure,** i.e., they do not impart impurity to other items, **and one is not liable** to receive *karet* **for them due to** the prohibitions of ***piggul*, *notar*, or** forbidden **fat.**

The *gemara* now challenges the view that *yesh dichuiy b'issura* — that temporary loss of size permanently negates status — by citing a *mishnah*. The case: *kebeitzah ochlin shehinicha bachamah v'nitmaatu* — an egg's worth of food was left in the sun and shriveled to less than a *kebeitzah*. The food can only receive or transmit *tumah* if it is of the requisite size. Similarly, *kezayit min hamet* — an olive's worth from a human corpse; *kezayit min haneveilah* — an olive's worth from the carcass of an animal that was not properly slaughtered; *ka'adasha min hasheretz* — the size of a lentil from certain creeping things; and *kezayit pigul, kezayit notar, kezayit chelev* — an olive's worth of *pigul*, *notar*, or forbidden fat (*chelev*), which are forbidden foods that Rabbinically also communicate *tumah*. If any of these shriveled below the required measure, the ruling is *tehorin* — they are *tahor*, meaning they no longer communicate *tumah*. And one would not be *chayav* for eating the *pigul*, *notar*, or *chelev* if they have shriveled below a *kezayit*.

### Pigul & Notar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִנִּיחָן בַּגְּשָׁמִים וְתָפְחוּ – טְמֵאִין, וְחַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶם מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר וָחֵלֶב. תְּיוּבְתָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יֵשׁ דִּיחוּי בְּאִיסּוּרִין! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If, after they shrank in the sun, one took these foods and **placed them in the rain,** as a result of which **they** again **swelled** to the minimum volume for ritual impurity, **they are impure,** as was the case before they shrank. This applies to the impurity of a corpse, the impurity of an animal carcass, and the impurity of foods, **and one is** also **liable** to receive *karet* **for them due to *piggul*, *notar*, or** forbidden **fat.** This demonstrates that the food is not permanently disqualified. Therefore, **the refutation of** the opinion of **the one who says** that **there is disqualification with regard to ritual matters** is **a conclusive refutation.**

The *mishnah*'s next clause is decisive: *henicham b'geshamim v'taphachu* — if one then left these items out in the rain and they re-expanded. *Teme'in* — they become *tamei* again. *V'chayavin aleihem mishum pigul v'notar v'tamei* — and one is *chayav* for eating them under the categories of *pigul*, *notar*, and *chelev*.

### Re-expansion Logic

What makes this case remarkable is that we understand precisely how the re-expansion occurred: the items absorbed water from the rain. This is not an internal biological or chemical process restoring the food to its prior state — it is external liquid being absorbed, causing the items to return to their original volume. And yet the *mishnah* rules unambiguously that they are once again *tamei* and one is *chayav* for eating them. The intermediate state of shrinkage is treated as inconsequential. The food's original status — as a source of *tumah* and as a forbidden item for the purposes of *karet* liability — is fully restored.

### Status Restoration

This constitutes a decisive refutation — *tiyuvta* — of the position that *yesh dichuiy b'issura*. The case described is precisely the scenario Rava identified as genuinely contested: the item began at the required size, shriveled below it, and returned to the required size. If *dichuiy* applied in this domain, the item would remain permanently *tahor* and one would not be *chayav* for eating it. The *mishnah* rules to the contrary: current size, restored to the original threshold, carries the original status, and *dichuiy b'issura* does not apply.

Rava's framework, combined with this proof, yields the following resolution: everyone agrees that current size below the threshold means no active status. Everyone agrees that if something never had the threshold size, it does not magically acquire the status later. The genuinely debated question is whether temporary loss of size during an intermediate period can permanently negate a status that both originated and was ultimately restored. The *braitah* here answers that it does not — *ein dichuiy b'issura*, the status is restored when the size is restored, even when the re-expansion results from the absorption of external liquid. The analogy to reconstituted foods — grape juice from concentrate, for instance — raises genuinely parallel questions about whether something that has had its liquid extracted and then replaced with other liquid retains its original identity. The *braitah* here suggests that at least for these purposes of *tumah* and *issurin*, the restoration of original size is what governs, and the source of that restoration is not determinative.
