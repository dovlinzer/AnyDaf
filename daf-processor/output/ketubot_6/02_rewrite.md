# Ketubot 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ketubot Overview

### Topics Survey

The daf we take up today — 6a — finds us in the middle of a challenging *sugya* concerning first-time intercourse on *Shabbat*. Before proceeding, it is worth pausing to appreciate just how much ground has been covered in the opening pages of this *masechet*. Beginning with the question of when a *besulah* gets married and whether the groom must pay support when the marriage is delayed, the *masechet* quickly moved far afield of its core theme. On 2b we encountered the laws of conditions in a *get* and whether an unfulfillable condition is nonetheless considered fulfilled — a major question in contract law and *gittin*. On daf 3 came the topic of *hafka'as kiddushin*, the rabbinic power to retroactively dissolve a marriage. Then, on 3b, a case of a woman compelled to submit to intercourse with a local ruler under threat of death, bringing in questions of *yaharog v'al ya'avor*. From there the *masechet* turned to *aveilus* and *aninus* — whether a funeral may be postponed to permit a scheduled wedding, the laws of *devarim she'betzina noheg*, and related questions. On daf 5 came the question of what types of matters may be discussed on *Shabbat* and the permissibility of doing *cheshbonos* on *Shabbat*, before returning to the themes of *ketubot* proper: the date of the wedding, the *beracha she'ne'emra bo l'adam*, the *beracha she'ne'emra bo l'dagim*, and an *agadata* about why one should not listen to *lashon hara*.

### Mini-Shas Structure

The range of topics traversed in four *daf* — *niddah*, *aveilus*, *gittin*, *Shabbat*, and more — is precisely why *Ketubot* is famously described as a *Shas Katan*, a mini-tractate of the entire Talmud. And it is not merely the breadth of subject matter that earns it this title. The *sugyot* in *Ketubot* tend to arrive at the very heart of each conceptual question rather than working around its periphery for many sessions. Each discussion reaches the core of a central halakhic principle, which is one reason why this *masechet* is studied so extensively in *yeshivos*.

### Pedagogy Issues

That said, a word must be said about the pedagogical difficulties this particular *sugya* presents. The questions being analyzed here concern first-time intercourse, whether the act creates a wound, whether it creates an opening, what the groom's intent is, whether he wishes to see blood to confirm his bride's virginity, whether making her a non-virgin is seen as an act of desire or as damaging something pristine — all of this is typically taught to teenage boys in *yeshiva*. The difficulty is not the explicitness of the discussion per se; frank conversation about the body is appropriate. The deeper challenge is the orientation of the entire discussion: it is conducted entirely from the man's perspective. The woman's body is analyzed as an object of halakhic scrutiny. Nobody in the *gemara* asks whether the woman is permitted to have intercourse on *Shabbat*. That question is treated as simply irrelevant. This framing — focused on what the man is doing, what the man intends, what the man sees — and the way it objectifies the woman's body and renders her something of a passive subject in the analysis, creates real challenges for how this material ought to be taught. Those challenges, in the experience of most *yeshivos*, are never actually raised.

## First-Time Sex Issue

### Prohibitions

To distill the halakhic structure: there are two potential prohibitions associated with first intercourse on *Shabbat*. The first is *chaburah* — the act of wounding — depending on how the *gemara* understands the underlying physiology. If the blood emerges because the hymenal tissue tears away from the wall and blood is released from vessels within that tissue, that constitutes wounding. The second possible prohibition is *petach* — making an opening — which would presumably fall under the category of *binyan*, an interesting application of the concept of building to a human body, though we do encounter that elsewhere in the *gemarot*. With these two possible prohibitions established, the *gemara* then asks about the groom's intent: is he *mechaven* for this outcome, or is he *eino mechaven*? Does his intent matter, and does the principle of *mekalkel* apply — the notion that since wounding is inherently a somewhat destructive act, perhaps there is a special *halakha* regarding *chaburot* such that even a *mekalkel* is *chayav*?

### Mekalkel

*Tosafot* raises a sharp conceptual question about the category of *chaburah* and its relationship to *melachehet machshevet*. *Chaburah* is categorized under *netilat neshamah* — the same prohibition that forbids *shechting* an animal. Making a wound is like a miniature act of taking life. But how precisely is it a taking of life? *Tosafot* suggests two possibilities: either wounding weakens the living being and thus represents a step toward death, or the very extraction of blood is itself the halakhic act, since blood represents life regardless of the physical harm caused. *Tosafot* argues that it must be the latter. If wounding were prohibited because it harms or weakens the person, there would be no basis for the *gemara* to consider it an intentional *melacha* in this context, since no one desires to wound the woman or cause her pain or weakness. The only way the *gemara* can speak of the groom being *mechaven* is if the blood itself — as a symbol of life — is the object of his intention: he wants to see the blood to know that his bride is a *besulah*. This frames *netilat neshamah* in an almost symbolic sense, independent of physical harm.

### Shabbat Criteria

*Tosafot* then presses a further question. If *mekalkel bechabura chayav* — if even a destructive act of wounding incurs *chiyuv* — then why does the *gemara* treat *kavanah* as relevant at all? If *Rabbi Shimon* holds you are *chayav* for *mekalkel bechabura*, why would *eino mitkaven* provide an exemption according to *Rabbi Shimon*? *Tosafot* answers with an important conceptual distinction: *mechaven* — having intent — is not a uniquely *Shabbat* requirement. It is a general requirement applicable to all prohibitions. What *Rabbi Shimon* is saying when he rules *chayav* by *mekalkel bechabura* is that he waives the special *Shabbat* criterion of *melachehet machshevet* — the requirement that a prohibited act be constructive — in the context of wounding. But the general principle that a person must intend the prohibited act in order to be liable is not a *Shabbat* criterion; it applies universally. Accordingly, *eino mitkaven* remains an exemption even by *chaburah* according to *Rabbi Shimon*, because that exemption derives not from the *Shabbat* requirement of *melachehet machshevet* but from a general principle of halakhic liability.

*Tosafot* makes this point explicitly at the top of 6a. He writes that the reason both *mekalkel* and *misasek* are exempt in other *melachos Shabbat* is: *detam sheneihem depeturin beshaar melachos Shabbos havei mishum debe'inan meleches machsheves* — because we require *meleches machsheves*, and that is the specifically *Shabbat* criterion. But *aval she-eino miskavein klal le-melachah* — if one is not intending to perform the prohibited act at all — *lo mishum debe'inan meleches machsheves mister* — that exemption does not derive from the *Shabbat* requirement of *meleches machsheves* — *deshaar mekomos nami piter aino miskavein* — because *eino miskavein* is an exemption in other prohibitions as well, such as *kilayim*, where those who sell garments may do so in their normal manner provided they do not intend a violation, and similarly a *nazir* may comb his hair even if strands may come out. *Hilkach be-chaburah nami shari* — therefore, *eino miskavein* remains an exemption by wounding as well, even where *mekalkel* does not.

This distinction is not merely relevant to the present *sugya*. It applies practically wherever *mekalkel bechabura* might come up — for example, in the context of an injection on *Shabbat* that draws blood. Does *eino miskavein* still exempt in such a case even if *mekalkel bechabura* is *chayav*? *Tosafot* establishes clearly from this *gemara* that yes, *eino miskavein* remains an exemption, because it is a general halakhic principle, not a specifically *Shabbat* criterion.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Dispute Versions

The *gemara* now presents the central *amoraic* dispute. In the *yeshiva* of Rav, the tradition was: *Rav shari ve-Shmuel assur* — Rav permits first intercourse on *Shabbat* and Shmuel forbids it. In *Neharde'a*, the city of Shmuel, the tradition was reversed: Rav forbids it and Shmuel permits it. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak provides a *siman* — a mnemonic — to remember the correct version: *eilu mikilin le-atzman ve-eilu mikilin le-atzman* — each *yeshiva* attributed the lenient position to their own master.

### Wine Rag Proof

The *gemara* then challenges Rav's permissive position. Does Rav truly permit first intercourse on *Shabbat*? But Rav Shimi bar Chizkiya transmitted in the name of Rav that a *mesukhraya d'nazeita* — a rag stuffed into the bunghole of a wine barrel — is forbidden to be tightened on *Yom Tov*. The implicit reasoning, as Rashi explains, is that tightening the rag wrings out wine absorbed in it, constituting *sechita*. And this ruling, the *gemara* assumes, indicates that Rav holds *eino miskavein* is *assur* — since the wine being squeezed is unintended — which would in turn make first intercourse on *Shabbat* far more likely to be forbidden.

## First-Time Sex Issue (II)

### Tosafot Analysis

A major *Tosafot* engages this passage and raises a series of layered difficulties. If the prohibition of tightening the rag is rooted in *sechita*, the nature of that *sechita* must be clarified. If the *issur* of *sechita* is like laundering — where one soaks a garment and wrings it out — then, *Tosafot* argues, the prohibition would apply only to water, since the wringing of water is part of the laundering process. It would not extend to wine, which does not belong to that category. *Tosafot* therefore identifies a second category of *sechita*, analogous to *dash* — threshing. Just as threshing removes grain from its husk, squeezing a lemon removes juice from its natural container. When wine has been absorbed into a cloth, wringing it out would similarly constitute removing liquid from its place of containment. This would be *d'Oraisa*.

However, *Tosafot* immediately raises a further difficulty: even granting this second category of *sechita*, the wine being squeezed out in this case is *holeich le-ibud* — it falls to the ground and is lost. An act that is *holeich le-ibud* is at least *mekalkel*, if not even less than *mekalkel* from the standpoint of halakhic liability, since there is no constructive purpose achieved even in the most basic sense. Why then is it prohibited?

But before reaching this difficulty, *Tosafot* raises another that is arguably more fundamental: even if we say this is a *pesik reisha* — something that will inevitably occur — if the person derives no benefit from the result, it is a *pesik reisha d'lo nicha lei*. Is such a case *mutar lechatchila* or *assur lechatchila*? This question, which is central to *hilchos Shabbat*, receives its primary treatment in this very *Tosafot*. *Rabbeinu Tam* argues that the act should be prohibited by virtue of *mefarek* — the *dash*-like *sechita* — even in a case of *lo nicha lei*. *Tosafot* then cites the *Aruch*, who writes: *dechol psik reisha d'lo nicha lei shari* — any *pesik reisha d'lo nicha lei* is permitted. The *Ri* disagrees and maintains that *psik reisha d'lo nicha lei* remains *assur*. This debate among the *rishonim* — whether *psik reisha d'lo nicha lei* is *mutar lechatchila* or forbidden at least *m'derabbanan* — is one of the most consequential discussions in practical *hilchos Shabbat*, and it is this *Tosafot* that is the primary locus of that question.

One further note from *Tosafot*: the distinction between *mekalkel* and *misasek* on one side, and *eino miskavein* on the other, clarifies why *psik reisha* matters. The *gemara*'s discussion of *eino miskavein* as permissible according to *Rabbi Shimon* refers to cases where the prohibited outcome is not inevitable. Once the outcome is inevitable — *pesik reisha* — *eino miskavein* no longer exempts, as *Abaye* and *Rava* both rule: *modei Rabbi Shimon be-pesik reisha ve-lo yamut* — even *Rabbi Shimon* concedes that if one chops off an animal's head one cannot claim not to have intended its death. The exemption of *eino miskavein* is suspended by *pesik reisha*. The further question, introduced here by the *Aruch* and the *Ri*, is whether *pesik reisha d'lo nicha lei* — where the outcome is inevitable but unwanted — restores the exemption at the level of *d'Oraisa*, and whether it remains forbidden *m'derabbanan*.

## Rav vs. Shmuel (II)

### Rav's Resolution

The *gemara* returns to the challenge against Rav from the case of the wine rag. Rav Chiya bar Ashi transmitted in the name of Rav that the *halakha* follows *Rabbi Yehuda* — that *eino miskavein* is *assur*. Rav Chanan bar Ami transmitted in the name of Shmuel that the *halakha* follows *Rabbi Shimon* — that *eino miskavein* is permitted. And Rav Chiya bar Avin teaches the same positions without the chain of transmission, stating directly: Rav rules like *Rabbi Yehuda*, Shmuel rules like *Rabbi Shimon*. So the very same dispute between Rav and Shmuel on *eino miskavein* maps precisely onto their positions on first intercourse on *Shabbat*.

But this creates an apparent contradiction: if Rav rules like *Rabbi Yehuda* that *eino miskavein* is *assur*, how can Rav permit first intercourse on *Shabbat*, given that the allowances discussed in the preceding analysis were largely predicated on *eino miskavein*?

The *gemara* resolves this: *l'olam Rav ke-Rabbi Yehuda sevira lei* — Rav indeed holds like *Rabbi Yehuda* that *eino miskavein* is *assur*. His permissive ruling on first intercourse, however, does not depend on *eino miskavein*. According to the position that *dam mizkar pakid* — that the blood is pooled behind the hymen and its release does not constitute a wound — the relevant prohibition is *petach*, making an opening. And here, *mekalkel hu etzel pesach* — it is *mekalkel* with respect to the opening, since from one perspective the husband regards his wife's prior virginal state as something to be preserved, and its transformation is therefore a form of destruction. According to the other position, *dam chiburei miptar* — that there is indeed a wound involved — *mekalkel bachabura hu* — it is similarly a *mekalkel* act of wounding, in the sense that causing the wound is not constructive from his perspective. In both cases the exemption rests on *mekalkel*, not on *eino miskavein*. *Tosafot* adds the observation that being *mekalkel* does not make the act *mutar lechatchila*; there are other considerations at play, namely that this is a *mitzvah* — but that is a separate question.

## Tinokes Case

### Beis Shammai vs. Hillel

Rav Chisda raises a challenge from a *Mishnah* in *hilchos niddah* concerning the case of a *tinokes she-lo higia zemana liros* — an underage girl who has not yet reached the age of menstruation — who is married and has intercourse. The *Mishnah* operates on the principle that *dam besulim* — hymenal blood — exempts from concern about *dam niddah*; bleeding in the days following the wedding night is attributed to the hymen rather than to menstruation. The question is how long this exemption extends.

*Beis Shammai omrim nosna lo arba leilos* — Beis Shammai holds that the first four nights are covered; any bleeding within those four nights is not treated as *niddah*. *U-Veis Hillel omrim ad she-tiyeh hamakah* — Beis Hillel holds that the exemption is indefinite, until one is confident the wound has healed.

Where the girl has reached the age of menstruation, *Beis Shammai omrim nosna lo laila harishon* — Beis Shammai limits the exemption to the first night only. *U-Veis Hillel omrim ad motzei Shabbos arba leilos* — Beis Hillel says until *motzei Shabbat*, four nights — reflecting the institution that virgins marry on Wednesday, so that Wednesday night, Thursday night, Friday night, and Saturday night constitute the four nights, after which *motzei Shabbat* arrives.

### Four Nights Implication

The mention of *motzei Shabbat* and four nights implies that the couple is having intercourse on Friday night — *Shabbat* eve — and that the resulting bleeding is not a concern. This seems to bear directly on the question of whether first intercourse is permitted on *Shabbat*. Even if by the third or fourth night the hymen has already been ruptured, the *gemara* understands that continued intercourse can still produce hymenal bleeding, so the scenario remains analogous to the central question.

### Rava vs. Abaye

The *gemara* asks: *mai lav de-ilu bo'al matzi bo'al* — does this not mean that if he has not yet completed intercourse he is permitted to do so, implying even on *Shabbat*? *Amar Rava: lo, levar mi-Shabbos* — Rava says no; the four nights extend until *motzei Shabbat* but exclude *Shabbat* itself; they are not having intercourse on Friday night. *Amar lei Abaye: veha ad motzei Shabbos arba leilos katani* — but the *Mishnah* explicitly states four nights until *motzei Shabbat*, which implies every one of those nights, including Friday. *Ella amar Rava keshe-baal* — Rava responds: the case is that he has already had intercourse. By that rereading, the *chiddush* is not that they are having first intercourse on *Shabbat* but rather something else. *Veki ba'al mai ka mashma lan?* — but then what is the *chiddush* in mentioning *motzei Shabbat* and four nights at all? The *gemara* answers: *ka mashma lan deshary lemiv'al beShabbos kedishmuel* — it teaches that intercourse on *Shabbat* is permitted even where it might entail some continued bleeding, in accordance with Shmuel's teaching. *De'amar Shmuel pirtzah dechukah mutteres likannes bah beShabbos ve'af al pi shemeshir tzeroros* — Shmuel taught with the image of a narrow breach in a stone wall: one may pass through it on *Shabbat* even if a few stones fall away in the process. The ongoing friction of intercourse that produces some residual bleeding is analogous. However, this does not straightforwardly prove permissibility of first intercourse on *Shabbat*, since the situation after the hymen has already been ruptured is meaningfully different.

## Chatan Shema

### RY Question

Rav Yosef raises a further challenge. The question is whether the four-night framework in the context of the *chatan*'s exemption from *Shema* includes *Shabbat* night — and what this might imply about the permissibility of first intercourse on *Shabbat*.

### Chatan Exemption

The *Mishnah* states: *chassan patur mikrias shema laylah harishon* — a groom is exempt from reciting *Shema* on the first wedding night, because his mind is occupied by the fact that he is going to have intercourse. This exemption extends *ad motzei Shabbos* — until *motzei Shabbat* — *im lo asa ma'aseh* — if he has not yet had intercourse. The structure here differs from the earlier *Mishnah*: there, bleeding continued for four nights after intercourse had occurred; here, the exemption applies for four nights because he is still in the process of attempting first intercourse. If they did not succeed the first night, they will be trying again; the second night he is still planning it; and so on for four nights, after which a certain *ye'ush* — a giving up — may set in. That intercourse may not have been consummated even after several attempts is a real phenomenon, with causes that may be psychological, physiological, or medical, and the *gemara* acknowledges it here matter-of-factly.

The *gemara* generally understands the *chatan*'s exemption in one of two ways. The plain reading of the *Mishnayos* is that someone who is distracted — particularly by sexual anticipation, but perhaps more broadly — is exempt from *davening* and from *Shema*. The *gemara* in *Sukkah*, and the present sugya as well, frames it in terms of *osek bemitzvah* — being occupied with a *mitzvah*. Since the groom is mentally engaged in planning to fulfill the *mitzvah* of first intercourse, he is considered involved in the *mitzvah* and therefore exempt. This is an unusual form of *osek bemitzvah*, since the engagement is purely mental rather than active, but the *gemara* treats it as sufficient.

### Rava Proof

Rava argues that the four-night exemption — which explicitly includes Friday night — proves that the groom intends to have first intercourse on *Shabbat* night. If first intercourse were forbidden on *Shabbat*, there would be no reason to extend the exemption to Friday night; the *chatan* would know he cannot have intercourse that night and his mind would not be occupied with it. The exemption on Friday night is thus evidence that first intercourse on *Shabbat* is permitted.

### Abaye Counter

Abaye counters: *lo de-tarid de-lo ba'al* — the groom is not planning to have intercourse on Friday night; he remains distracted nonetheless because he is anguished over not having succeeded the previous two nights. His mind cannot focus on *Shema* not because he is about to engage in a *mitzvah* that night but because the distress of not having accomplished it yet has not left him.

## Osek Distinction

### Abaye Reframe

Abaye's counter reframes the entire discussion. He is not arguing about whether first intercourse on *Shabbat* is permitted. He is arguing that the Tannaitic debate about the *chatan*'s exemption from *Shema* concerns the grounds for that exemption — not whether intercourse on *Shabbat* is allowed. One view holds that the exemption is grounded in *osek bemitzvah* — active involvement in a *mitzvah* that one is about to perform — and on Friday night, if he cannot have intercourse, there is no such *osek*. The other view holds that the exemption is rooted in distraction, and even without intercourse planned for that night, his ongoing distress about not having yet succeeded is sufficient to exempt him.

### Avel Problem

Rava challenges Abaye's position: *u-mishum tirda patur?* — does mere distraction exempt someone from *Shema*? If so, a person whose boat sank in the sea, or who suffered a catastrophic financial loss, would also be exempt from *Shema* due to his inability to concentrate. *Hachi nami de-patur?* The implications would be far-reaching. And if you were to say yes — that such a person is also exempt — *ve-ha amar Rabbi Abba bar Zavda amar Rav: avel chayav be-chol ha-mitzvot ha-amurot ba-Torah chutz min ha-tefillin* — an *avel* is obligated in all *mitzvot* enumerated in the Torah except for *tefillin*, because *tefillin* are called *pe'er* and it is not fitting for a mourner to adorn himself with glory during *shiva*. But the *avel* is obligated in *Shema*. Yet surely an *avel* is profoundly distracted by grief and anguish over the deceased. If distraction exempts, why is an *avel* obligated?

### Rashi Solution

Rashi addresses this problem at a point further along in the *gemara* and his answer is instructive. He explains: *amar lach hacha nami betirda pligi* — and the *Tanna* who exempts does so for the following reason: *lav mishum deshurei lemeyal* — it is not because he is going to have intercourse that night, *ella kasavar mista'er tza'ar shel mitzvah patur mikriyas shema* — but rather because one who is suffering a *tza'ar shel mitzvah* — a distress that is rooted in a *mitzvah* — is exempt from *Shema*. The groom on Friday night, even if not having intercourse, is anguished because he has failed for two nights to fulfill a *mitzvah*. That *mitzvah*-rooted anguish is a *tza'ar shel mitzvah*, and it is that specific category of distress — not generalized distraction — that exempts him.

This explains why the *avel* is not exempt. Rashi writes: *velo dami le-ovel detarid tirda d'reshus* — it is not similar to an *avel*, whose distraction is not *mitzvah*-related. *She'ein tza'aro mitzvah* — his anguish is not itself a *mitzvah* — *af al pi she'aveiluso mitzvah* — even though his *aveilut* is a *mitzvah*. This is a striking claim. Rashi appears to be distinguishing between the *mitzvah* of *aveilut* — its external observances — and the internal experience of grief itself. The *mitzvah* of *aveilut* consists of behavioral expressions of mourning; the anguish one feels, while natural and perhaps even appropriate, is not itself commanded. The groom's distress, by contrast, is directly caused by his not yet having fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *beilat mitzvah*; his suffering is a consequence of an unperformed *mitzvah*, and that renders it a *tza'ar shel mitzvah*. This is genuinely difficult: even granting Rashi's distinction between *aveilut* as a *mitzvah* and grief as an emotional state, it is not clear why the *avel*'s very real distress would be any less of a legitimate exemption than the groom's frustration. The reason the *gemara* favors the *osek bemitzvah* framing over the distraction framework is precisely this: once you allow distraction to exempt, you have to explain why only certain distractions qualify, and that boundary is difficult to draw cleanly.

## Original Sugya

### Baraita Debate

The *gemara* now arrives at what is likely the original *sugya* underlying the entire discussion. A *baraita* states explicitly: *ha-koneh et ha-besulah lo yavo betchilah be-Shabbat* — one who marries a virgin may not have intercourse for the first time on *Shabbat*. *Ve-chachamim matirin* — and the *chachamim* permit it. Here at last is a direct Tannaitic dispute on the question that has occupied the entire passage.

### Chachamim ID

Rava identifies the *chachamim*: *amar Rava Rabbi Shimon hi* — it is *Rabbi Shimon*, *de'amar davar she-eino mitkaven mutar* — who holds that an unintended act is permitted. This *baraita* is almost certainly the original *sugya*. The elaborate logical framework constructed over the preceding pages — the decision tree of different scenarios and their *issur* or *heter* — is the work of the *stam*, the later anonymous redactional layer. The underlying Tannaitic source is this single *baraita*, a debate attributed to *Rabbi Shimon* on the permissibility of first intercourse on *Shabbat* based on the principle of *eino mitkaven*.

## Baki Argument

### Pesik Reisha

Abaye immediately presses: *ve-modeh Rabbi Shimon be-pesik reisha ve-lo yamut* — and *Rabbi Shimon* concedes that *eino miskavein* does not exempt in a case of *pesik reisha*. If the hymen will inevitably rupture during intercourse, how can the *chachamim* permit it on the basis of *eino miskavein*? Is this not a *pesik reisha* — an inevitable consequence — and therefore no different from chopping off a chicken's head while claiming not to have intended its death?

### Baki Can Avoid

Rava responds: *lo ka-elu Bavlayim she-ein beki'in be-hataya* — unlike those Babylonians who do not know how to incline, *ela yesh beki'in be-hataya* — there are those who know how to incline, meaning it is possible to engage in intercourse in a manner — at a more horizontal or angled approach — that does not rupture the hymen. If such a technique exists, then rupture is not inevitable, and the case is not a *pesik reisha*. The exemption of *eino miskavein* therefore remains applicable.

### Baki Tensions

This answer generates its own difficulties. *Im kein tarud lamah* — if men are capable of having intercourse without rupturing the hymen, why is the *chatan* so distracted? The whole basis of the exemption from *Shema* is that his mind is consumed by the difficulty of what he is attempting. If the *baki* performs it smoothly without rupture, why is there such distress? Conversely, if he is distracted because he is *she-eino baki* — not an expert — how can we permit the act on *Shabbat* on the grounds that it is not a *pesik reisha*? The permission rests on the assumption that a *baki* can avoid rupture; but the distraction argument seems to assume the person is *she-eino baki*. These cannot both apply to the same person. The *gemara* responds: *tarud lamah l'she-eino baki* — the distraction applies to the non-expert. But this generates yet another difficulty: *yomru baki mutar she-eino baki assur* — are we going to rule that an expert may have intercourse on *Shabbat* and a non-expert may not? This seems practically untenable, and conceptually bizarre. The *gemara* responds: *rov baki'in hen* — the majority of men are in fact *baki'in*. Yet this answer is deeply puzzling. How do most men, expected to arrive at the wedding night without prior sexual experience, come to possess such expertise? And even granting that the majority are *baki'in*, one would expect the rule to follow the *individual* before us rather than the statistical majority. The *gemara* here is genuinely difficult, and *Tosafot*'s attempt to reconcile the tensions is strained.

### Groomsmen Custom

Rava bar Rav Chanan raises a final challenge to Abaye: if men can and typically do have intercourse without rupturing the hymen, *shushbinin lamah mapah lamah* — why do the groomsmen wait outside the room and why is a pre-verified sheet prepared? The practice of *parsu hasimlah lifnei ziknei ha'ir* — spreading the sheet before the elders of the city — is described in the *Torah* itself as a means of verifying the bride's virginity through evidence of hymenal blood. Why establish an elaborate verification system if rupture is not reliably expected? The answer given is: *hasam shema yireh ve-yoveid* — the concern is not that rupture will fail to occur but that the evidence, once produced, may be lost before the groomsmen can confirm it. The custom of the *shushbinin* and the pre-checked *mapah* exists to ensure that if there is blood it is found and preserved, not to verify that rupture occurred. Rashi adds that if rupture did not occur and there is therefore no blood, the wife simply says the hymen has not yet been ruptured and they will repeat the act — and this time evidence will be present. The larger concern driving the institution is thus the preservation of evidence, not the occurrence of rupture.
