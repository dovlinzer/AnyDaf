# Bava Metzia 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shomrim Overview

### Basic Categories

The daf opens with a *mishna* that marks a transition of sorts within the broader discussion of *shomrim*. The preceding *sugyot* had addressed special cases — most recently, the circumstances under which a *shomer* may liquidate an object he is watching when it faces deterioration, and the related question of *shlichus yad*, taking unauthorized personal use of a watched object and when one's special liability thereby ends. That final topic was somewhat puzzling, because while the *gemara* developed the category of *shlichus yad* extensively, the *mishna* itself did not spell it out directly. A fuller treatment of the four foundational categories — *shomer chinam*, *shomer sachar*, *sho'el*, and *socher*, each with their respective levels of liability — awaits us some four *perakim* hence. But at least here, at the end of this *perek*, we arrive at something approaching a basic discussion of the standard of care, focused specifically on money.

### Mishna Cases

The *mishna* opens: *HaMafkid ma'ot etzel chaveiro* — one who entrusts money to his friend to watch. The *mishna* then enumerates three situations that render the *shomer* liable: (1) *tzaran v'hifshilan l'achorav* — he bundled the money and placed it behind his back, as in a knapsack or back pocket; (2) *o shemasram livno ulevito haktanim* — he gave it to his minor children to watch; (3) *v'na'al bifneihem shelo karauy* — he locked them in the house but not securely. In all these cases, *shebechol elu chayav*, he is liable, because *shelo shamar k'derech hashomrim* — he did not watch it in the manner that *shomrim* normally do. Conversely, *v'im shamar k'derech hashomrim patur* — if he did follow the normal standard, he is exempt.

It is worth noting that the *mishna* does not specify whether the *shomer* in question is a *shomer chinam* or a *shomer sachar*. The default reading of *stam mafkid* — a deposit without further arrangement — is typically a *shomer chinam*, though the point is not entirely free of ambiguity.

### Derech Hashomrim

The *mishna*'s conclusion begs an immediate question: what precisely is *derech hashomrim*? Who defines it, and is it the same standard for a *shomer chinam* as for a *shomer sachar*? The answer, it emerges, is thoroughly contextual — the standard is shaped by prevailing convention, by what is socially and practically reasonable in the given circumstances. There is no single fixed threshold; it is all a function of what responsible people in that situation would do. This contextual nature of *derech hashomrim* will prove crucial as the *gemara* develops the discussion.

## Money in Hand

### Challenge to Mishna

The *gemara* probes the *mishna*: *bishlama kulehu shelo shamar k'derech hashomrim* — it is understandable why giving the money to one's minor children, or failing to lock the house properly, constitutes a failure of care. But the first case — bundling the money and putting it behind one's back — raises a pointed question: *mai hava leh l'me'avad?* What else should he have done? What is the affirmative standard that bundling in a knapsack fails to meet?

### In Your Hand

The answer comes from *Rava* citing *Rav Yitzchak* citing a verse: *v'tzarta hakesef b'yadecha* — "and you shall bind the silver in your hand" (Deuteronomy 14:25). This verse, in its original context, addresses *ma'aser sheni* — the second tithe that must be redeemed in money and brought to Jerusalem. Yet *Rav Yitzchak* derives from it a principle applicable to the guardianship of money: *af al pi shetzrurin, b'yadecha* — even if the money is bundled up, it must be *b'yadecha*, literally in your hand. The implication for *shomrim* is that money demands the highest degree of attentiveness — not a back pocket or knapsack, but something more like a front pocket, where it remains immediately within one's grasp and awareness. Money is uniquely attractive to thieves because it concentrates high value in a small package, and the standard of care must reflect that reality.

### Pocket Checking

This ruling of *Rav Yitzchak* resonates with an earlier statement of his from the second *perek* of *Bava Metzia*: *adam asui lemashmesh b'kiso b'chol sha'a* — a person habitually checks his pocket at all times to make sure his money is still there. When that statement appeared earlier, it was offered as a descriptive claim to explain how one can assume *yiush* — that a person knows when money has dropped from his pocket. One might have questioned whether this is truly universal behavior. In light of the present passage, however, it appears that *Rav Yitzchak*'s statement carries a prescriptive dimension as well: if you are responsible for money, constant awareness of its whereabouts is not merely typical behavior but the required standard of care.

## Money & Blessing

### Cash Availability

The *gemara* continues with a series of *aggadic* teachings attributed to *Rav Yitzchak*, all touching on money management. The first: *l'olam yehei kaspah shel adam matzui b'yado* — a person should always have cash readily available. The practical connection to the earlier *halacha* is natural: if one is obligated to keep money actively in hand, that presupposes having liquid funds accessible. If you never carry significant amounts of money, the question of where to keep it hardly arises. But if, as sound financial practice demands, one should always have a measure of cash available for unexpected opportunities, then one must correspondingly take greater care about where and how that money is held. The verse is again invoked: *v'tzarta hakesef b'yadecha* — "let it be in your hand," meaning at hand and ready.

### Portfolio Advice

*Rav Yitzchak* continues: *l'olam yashlish adam et ma'otav* — a person should always divide his assets into thirds. *Shlish b'karka* — a third in land, meaning real estate, which retains value over time, appreciates, and cannot be stolen or misplaced. *Ushlish b'prakmatia* — a third in commerce, buying and selling, which represents higher risk but also higher return. *V'shlish tachat yado* — a third in liquid assets, cash readily available. The advice has a strikingly modern ring: diversify, maintain a real estate position, remain active in commerce, and keep liquid reserves.

### Hidden Blessing

*Rav Yitzchak* then states: *ein habracha metzuya ela b'davar hasamui min ha'ayin* — blessing is found only in something hidden from the eye. This is a striking teaching. The idea is that if one constantly monitors an investment — counting today's value and comparing it to yesterday's — it cannot be blessed. Several dimensions of this principle are possible. One reading is metaphysical: God's blessing operates through *nes nistar*, hidden miracle, and constant scrutiny forecloses the space in which that operates. Another, raised by the *Maharsha*, invokes *ayin hara* — when something is publicly known and tallied, others' envious attention affects it adversely. There is also a purely psychological dimension: if one does not check frequently, the aggregate gain discovered upon eventually looking is far more gratifying than the same gain witnessed day by day.

### Hidden Sources

The verse cited in support is: *yetzav Hashem itcha et haberacha be'asamecha* — "God will command the blessing upon you in your storehouses" (Deuteronomy 28:8). *Rav Yitzchak* reads *be'asamecha* as connected to *samui*, hidden. A parallel formulation is brought from the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*: *ein haberacha metzuya ela bedavar she'ein ha'ayin sholetet bo* — blessing is found only in what the eye does not have dominion over, again citing *be'asamecha*, which *Rashi* renders as *lashon genazim*, the language of stored and treasured things. The two formulations subtly differ: *Rav Yitzchak*'s language of *samui* emphasizes hiddenness as enabling divine increase; the *tanna devei Rabbi Yishmael*'s language of *she'ein ha'ayin sholetet bo* more directly invokes the idea of protecting the object from the gaze of an envious eye. The latter reading fits more naturally with *ayin hara* concerns; the former with the idea of leaving room for blessing to accumulate unobserved.

### Measuring Grain

The *gemara* then presents a *baraita* that applies this principle practically. One who goes to measure the grain in his threshing floor should first say: *yehi ratzon milfanecha Hashem eloheinu shetishlach bracha bema'aseh yadeinu* — may it be Thy will, Lord our God, that You send blessing into the work of our hands. This is a *bakashah*, an explicit request before the measuring begins. When the measuring itself commences, one says: *baruch hashole'ach bracha bekari hazeh* — blessed is He who sends blessing into this pile of grain. This formulation uses the classic *matbe'a haberacha* form, framing the request as a blessing.

The relationship between these two formulations is worth exploring. The *yehi ratzon* is future-oriented — one has not yet begun to count, so the request looks forward. The *baruch hashole'ach* is present-tense and is recited as the measuring begins. One way to understand the progression is that the *baraita* moves from future to present: first anticipating God's blessing, then invoking it in the moment of action. The *Rama* suggests that the *bracha* expresses a kind of *bitachon* — confidence that the earlier prayer was heard — though it may simultaneously function as a present-tense petition.

The crucial point comes next: *madad v'achar kach beirach* — if one measured the grain first and only afterward recited *baruch hashole'ach bracha bekari hazeh*, *harei zo tefillat shav* — this is a vain prayer. Once you have counted and established the precise quantity, what are you asking for? That there will somehow be more tomorrow than there is today? That would require a *nes galuy*, a manifest miracle, and God does not do *nes galuy* on demand. The *baraita* concludes with the underlying principle: *ein haberacha metzuya lo bidvar hashakul velo bidvar hamadud velo bidvar hamannuy ela bidvar hasamuy min ha'ayin* — blessing is not found in what is weighed, or measured, or counted, but only in what is hidden from the eye. The verse is again cited: *yetzav Hashem itcha et haberacha be'asamecha*.

## Burial in Ground

### Shmuel's Rule

Returning now to the *halachic* question of proper *shmirah* for money, *Shmuel* takes matters considerably further than the *mishna*: *kesafim ein lahem shmirah ela be'karka* — money can only be properly protected by burying it in the ground. The logic is clear: money is uniquely concentrated in value within a tiny, easily concealed package. In a house, no matter how many hiding places one devises — the mattress, the cabinet, behind a painting — a determined thief will eventually search them all. But thieves will not undertake to dig up an entire yard or floor. Burial in the ground is thus, according to *Shmuel*, the *only* adequate protection for money.

*Rashi* makes the implication explicit: *ein lahem shmirah de'im lo shamran v'nignevu peshia hi* — anything short of burial in the ground constitutes *peshia*, negligence. This is a significant ruling, because it means that even a *shomer chinam*, who is ordinarily exempt from liability for theft, would be liable if the money was not buried, since his failure to bury it would be deemed negligence, not mere lack of precaution.

### Shabbat Exception

*Rava* immediately qualifies *Shmuel*'s ruling: *u'modeh Shmuel b'erev Shabbat bein hashemashot* — even *Shmuel* concedes that on *erev Shabbat*, near nightfall, *delo atrachuhu rabanan* — the Rabbis did not burden the *shomer* with the requirement to dig and bury. If someone deposits money with you just before *Shabbat*, and digging a hole at that moment would mean either violating *Shabbat* or requiring you to abandon all *Shabbat* preparations at the last minute, the Rabbis did not demand it. Hiding the money by other means would be acceptable under those circumstances.

The *gemara* extends this: if *Shabbat* has ended and enough time has elapsed for the *shomer* to have buried the money, yet he failed to do so, *mechayav* — he is now liable if it is stolen. Once the constraint disappears, the obligation resumes immediately. There is one exception: if the owner is a *talmid chacham* who is scrupulous about *havdala al hakos*, the *shomer* might reasonably wait a short time, anticipating that the owner may come to retrieve some of the money to purchase wine for *havdala*. In that case, a brief delay is excused.

### Norms Shape Care

This exception illuminates something fundamental about the entire framework of *derech hashomrim*. The standard of care is not purely a function of risk assessment — it is shaped by social norms and reasonable expectations. The risk that thieves will find unburied money is the same on *erev Shabbat* as on any other day. Yet the duty to bury it does not apply on *erev Shabbat* because under those circumstances it is not what reasonable people would demand. This connects to a parallel point from the preceding day's *sugya*: a shepherd who returns to town during his lunch break is arguably exposing the flock to the same dangers as if he had abandoned them entirely — wolves and lions do not observe lunch hours — and yet such a break can fall within *derech hashomrim*. The content of one's duty is partly constituted by prevailing conventions, not solely by objective risk.

### Thief Escalation

The *gemara* then traces an arms race between hiding places and increasingly sophisticated thieves. Initially, burial in the ground was the safest option — *Shmuel*'s ruling. But *v'haidna d'sh'chichi gashushei* — nowadays there are people who bang on the ground and listen for the sound of freshly disturbed earth. Against them, *leit lehu shmira ela b'shmei kora* — the only protection is hiding money in the rafters of the house. But then, *idna d'sh'chichi frumai* — there are those who break through the roof and search the rafters. Against them, *leit lehu shmira ela beini orbei* — one must hide the money within the bricks of the wall.

Yet even this is no longer fully secure: *d'sh'chichi tapuchai* — there are those who knock against walls, listening for the hollow sound of a removed brick. The solution: *leit lehu shmira ela b'tefcha samuch l'karka* — a course of bricks near the base of the wall, or alternatively, *b'tefcha samuch l'shmei kora* — near the ceiling, where the sound test is less reliable.

### Burial Depth

*Rav Acha the son of Rav Yosef* poses a question to *Rav Ashi*: the *mishna* in *Pesachim* rules that *chametz* buried under rubble is considered destroyed (*harei hu k'mevuar*) — *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* specifying that it must be deep enough that a dog cannot dig it out — and a *tanna* teaches that this threshold is three *tefachim*. So when *Shmuel* requires burial in the ground, how deep must the money be buried? Is three *tefachim* required here as well?

The answer is no, and the reasoning is elegant. *Hatam mishum reicha* — there, with *chametz*, the concern is smell: food has an odor, dogs are attracted to it, and one must bury it deep enough that no dog can detect and excavate it. *Hacha mishum iksuyei me-eina* — here, the concern is simply concealment from sight. Money has no odor; one need only ensure it cannot be seen. *Lo ba'inan gimmel tefachim* — three *tefachim* is not required. *V'kama?* How deep then? *Amar Rav Safra: nesicha tefach* — one *tefach* is sufficient.

## Tosafot's Question

### Buried & Stolen

*Tosafot* pauses at *Shmuel*'s ruling to raise a question: *tzarich iyun* — it requires examination. We have established that burying money in the ground is the proper *shmirah*, and *Rashi* explicitly states that anything less is *peshia*. The obvious corollary would seem to be that if the money *is* properly buried, and a thief nonetheless manages to find and steal it, that should constitute an *oness* — a genuinely unforeseeable misfortune — for which the *shomer* is exempt. But is that actually the case? Does burying it in the ground represent the floor of one extreme (below which is *peshia*) and the ceiling of the other (above which is *oness*), with no middle ground?

### Liability Conflict

*Tosafot* pursues this question by examining conflicting *gemarot*. A *shomer sachar* is normally liable for theft — *v'nignav* is explicitly among his obligations. But that should mean ordinary theft. If the theft is entirely unpreventable — if it genuinely constitutes an *oness* — should a *shomer sachar* still be liable? *Tosafot* finds *gemarot* that seem to suggest that a *shomer sachar* is liable even for *geneiva be'oness* — theft that occurred despite the *shomer*'s inability to prevent it — which seems deeply counterintuitive. Liability for theft by a *shomer sachar* ought to track negligence, not simply the category label of *geneiva*.

### Armed Robbers

*Tosafot* works toward a resolution: the case of *listim mezuyan*, an armed gang, is clearly an *oness*, and a *shomer sachar* is unquestionably exempt there. That case represents a *geneiva* that is unambiguously beyond the *shomer*'s control. *Tosafot* struggles to explain why other forms of *geneiva be'oness* might be treated differently, finding the distinction difficult to justify. The simplest reading may indeed be the cleanest: burying the money in the ground is *peshia* on one side and *oness* on the other, with no middle ground. Anything less is negligence; once it is properly buried and still stolen, that is a complete *oness* for which the *shomer sachar* is exempt. The tension in the various *gemarot* remains, but this binary reading has considerable force.

## Hunting Hut Case

### Duck Blind

The *gemara* presents a case: *hahu gavra de-afkid zuzei gabei chavreih* — a person deposited money with his friend. *Otvinhu b'tzrifa de-urbani* — the *shomer* placed the money in a hunting blind, a small hut out in the fields used for trapping or hunting birds. *Ignuv* — and the money was stolen from there.

### Negligence & Oness

*Rav Yosef* rules: *af al gav de-le-inyan ganavei netiruta hi* — it is true that a remote field hut is actually quite well protected against thieves; no one would ordinarily know to look there. But *le-inyan nura peshiuta hi* — with respect to fire, placing the money in a field hut is negligence. Brush fires can break out; a small wooden hut in a field is obviously vulnerable.

One might object: the money was stolen, not burned. Why should negligence regarding fire render the *shomer* liable for theft, an *oness* he could not have prevented? The answer lies in the principle of *tchilato bifshiya vesofo b'ones* — negligence at the outset followed by an unforeseeable mishap at the end. The key is that a causal argument can be constructed: the money was placed in the wrong location due to negligence. Had it been properly stored, it would not have been in that hut to be stolen. The act of theft, while itself an *oness*, is connected to the initial negligent placement. As long as that argument is available — that the loss would not have occurred but for the earlier negligence — liability follows.

### Opposite View

Some authorities (*ikha d'amri*) formulate it the opposite way: even though the hut is negligent with respect to fire, and protected with respect to theft — *tchilato bifshiya vesofo b'ones patur* — where the negligence is in one domain and the *oness* occurs in a different domain, one is exempt. The logic here is that the negligence with respect to fire is not what caused the theft; the theft was independent. One should not be penalized for a failure in one area when the actual loss came from an entirely different direction.

### Final Ruling

The *gemara* rules definitively: *v'hilcheta tchilato bifshiya vesofo b'ones chayav* — the *halacha* is that negligence at the outset, followed by an *oness* at the end, renders the *shomer* liable. *Tosafot* notes that there was textual uncertainty about whether the *gemara*'s ruling was *chayav* or *patur*, and some versions read *patur*; but *Tosafot* marshals evidence from other passages to confirm that the correct ruling is *chayav*.

## Forgotten Money

### Buried & Forgot

The *gemara* brings another case: *hahu gavra d'afkid zuzei gabei chavreih* — someone deposited money with a friend. When the owner later requests his money back, the *shomer* responds: *lo yadana heicha otvinhu* — I don't remember where I put it. He may have followed *Shmuel*'s advice entirely faithfully: he buried it in the ground, covered it carefully so as to leave no trace, and now genuinely cannot locate it.

### Rava's Ruling

*Rabbah* rules without hesitation: *kol lo yadana p'shiyuta hi* — any claim of "I don't know where it is" constitutes negligence. It does not matter that the money may be perfectly preserved underground. The fact that the *shomer* cannot locate an item in his care is itself a failure of custodial responsibility. *Zil shleim* — go pay. If the *shomer* eventually finds the money, it is his to keep, since he will have already compensated the owner. But at the moment of the claim, the loss is attributed to negligence and he must pay.

## Son & Mother

### Mother's Box

A new case: *hahu gavra d'afkid zuzei gabei chavreih* — a person deposited money with his friend. *Ashalminhu l'imei* — the *shomer* gave the money to his mother for safekeeping. *V'otvinhu b'kartsilita* — she placed it in a box. *V'ignov* — and it was stolen. She had not buried it in the ground.

*Rava* asks: *heichi nadaynu dayanei l'hai dina* — how should the judges rule? One might say to the son: you are liable; you should have buried this money and instead handed it off to your mother who kept it in a box. But the son has a response.

### Own Money Theory

The son argues: *kol hamafkid al daat ishto uvanav hu mafkid* — whoever deposits something with a *shomer* implicitly understands that the *shomer* may hand it over to his family members to watch, and this is tacitly accepted. Giving money to one's mother (presumably within the framework of one's household) is not a violation of the rules of *shomer she-masar l'shomer*; it falls within normal, accepted practice. So the act of transferring the money to the mother was not itself negligent. The application of *kol hamafkid* extends not only downward to children but also upward to parents, at least in cases of household proximity.

But then why did the mother not bury it? *Lo amar li d'lav deidei ninhu d'akvinhu* — he did not tell me it was not his own money. I assumed my son was handing me his personal funds to keep safe. For my own son's money, I put it in a box — a perfectly reasonable precaution for money I believed belonged to a family member. Had I known it was someone else's money, entrusted to my son with a full duty of care, I would have buried it in the ground.

So was the son negligent for not telling her? He responds: *kol shekein d'ki amina lah d'didi ninhu tfei mizdahara behu* — on the contrary, I deliberately did not tell her it was someone else's money, because I believed that if she thought it was *my* money, she would take even greater care of it. A mother is more protective of her own child's property than a stranger's. The son gambled on this assumption and believed his silence actually *increased* the level of care.

One might reasonably question whether this reasoning exonerates the son. He had a duty of care, and he discharged it based on an assumption rather than by ensuring the facts were communicated. If a reasonable person would not have made the same assumption, the son's reliance on it should be considered negligent. The *gemara* appears to entertain the possibility that nobody was negligent here — that each party acted on reasonable assumptions.

### Both Swear

*Rava* resolves the case: *mishtaba ihu de-hanei zuzei asminhu l'imei* — let the son swear that he indeed handed the money over to his mother; *u-mishtaba imei de-hanei zuzei asvinhu b'kartilya v'ignu* — and let the mother swear that she placed them in the box and they were stolen from there. *U-patur* — and then both are exempt.

The structure of the oaths is noteworthy. Rather than requiring them to swear to the reasoning behind their choices — that the son believed his mother would be more careful if she thought it was his money, or that the mother genuinely believed it was her son's — the *gemara* requires them to swear only to the facts. This likely connects to the broader principle established by *Rav Nachman* that a *shomer* must swear that the item is *eino bireshuso* — no longer in his possession. The son swears he transferred the money to his mother; she swears it was stolen from the box. Given those facts, the legal conclusion is that neither party was negligent — a resolution that may strike one as generous, but that is the *gemara*'s ruling.

### Tosafot Concern

*Tosafot* raises a significant question about the law of *shomer she-masar l'shomer* in this case. *Rabbeinu Tam* explains the ruling of *kol hamafkid al daat ishto uvanav hu mafkid* as follows: the implicit permission to hand the deposit to family members means that the *shomer* does not automatically become liable for the mere act of transfer. Normally, *shomer she-masar l'shomer* without authorization renders the first *shomer* liable for everything that subsequently happens, even *oness*. Here, because the transfer is implicitly authorized, that automatic liability does not apply.

But *Tosafot* insists: *aval im pash'u* — if the second party, the family member, was actually negligent, *afilu ein lahem mah l'shalem* — even if they have no assets from which to collect, *yeshalem hanifkad* — the original *shomer* remains fully liable to the owner. The reasoning is stark: *d'im lo ken, kol mah she-hiphkid etzel baal ha-bayis tavo ishto v'sochal v'tipater* — otherwise one could devise an obvious scheme: accept a deposit, hand it to one's wife, she consumes it, she has no independent assets since *kani isha kanah baalah* applies, and neither party is collectible. Such a result would be absurd. Therefore, even when the transfer to family is authorized, the original *shomer* retains full responsibility for any negligence on the part of whoever he transfers to. The family member may also bear direct liability to the owner, but so does the original *shomer* — he cannot use the permitted transfer as a shield against liability for their failures.

## Toothless Cow

### Cow Setup

A new case involving an *apotropos* — an overseer managing the estate of orphans: *hahu apotropos d'yasmi d'zaven lehu tora l'yasmi* — the overseer purchased an ox for the orphans with their funds. *U'mashlei l'bakra* — he gave the ox over to a cowherd to tend. *Lo hava lei kichi v'shinei l'meichal* — it turned out the ox had no incisors or molars and could not eat. *U'meis* — the ox died. The cowherd had been throwing food to a large herd; this particular animal did not eat, was not noticed, and starved.

### Liability Split

*Rami bar Chama* poses the question: how should the judges rule? Tell the *apotropos* to pay the orphans — he was negligent for purchasing a defective animal without inspection. The *apotropos* responds: *ana l'bakra masartei* — I handed it over to the cowherd; the ongoing care was his responsibility. Tell the cowherd to pay: *ana bahadei tori ukmintei v'shadai lehu* — I put him together with all the other cattle and fed them all; *lo yadan d'lo achal* — I had no way of knowing this particular animal wasn't eating.

### Cowherd Liable

The *gemara* cuts through the ambiguity: *michdi bakra shomer sachar d'yasmi hu* — the cowherd is the orphans' *shomer sachar*, being paid for his work. *Itba'ei lei l'iyunei* — a *shomer sachar* cannot discharge his duty merely by performing the mechanical acts of care; he must exercise active attention. Simply throwing food to the herd is not sufficient when one is being paid precisely to ensure the animals are thriving. If this were the complete picture — the orphans suffer a loss because their paid guardian failed to notice the ox was not eating — the cowherd would clearly be liable.

### Mekach Taus

But there is a complicating twist: *d'leka pseda d'yasmi* — the orphans in fact suffered no loss. How? *D'ashkechu l'marei d'tora v'shakal yasmi zuzai minei* — the orphans located the original seller of the ox and demanded a refund on the grounds that the animal lacked molars. The seller conceded — this was a *mekach taus*, a defective sale — and refunded their money. This retroactive *mekach taus* means the ox was, in effect, never the orphans' property; ownership reverted to the original seller. So the ox that died belonged to the original seller. Who is the cowherd liable to?

He was hired by the orphans, not by the original seller. They had no contractual relationship. Yet the cowherd was, in fact, watching the original owner's cow, even if neither party knew it at the time. This generates the question of whether *de facto* possession and care of someone's property creates legal obligations toward that person even without a direct contract — a question *Tosafot* will address explicitly.

### Middleman

A further complication: the *gemara* identifies the seller as a *safsera*, a middleman — *d'zabin me-hacha u-mazbin le-hacha* — he purchased the ox from a farmer and resold it to the orphans for profit, without inspecting it. He did not know about the missing teeth, and the farmer from whom he bought it has presumably disappeared. The *safsera*, acting in good faith, returns the orphans' money because the ox was defective — he acknowledges the *mekach taus* even though it was unwitting on his part. But now he turns to the cowherd: *ibaye leh le-hoduan* — you should have informed me of the problem so I could have retrieved the animal or done something about it.

The *gemara* asks: why does the *safsera* have any claim? He knew he was selling a cow; if it had a defect, he should have known. The answer: because he is a middleman who acquired the animal from someone else without knowing of the defect, his ignorance is genuine and his grievance against the cowherd is real. He would have acted differently had he known.

### Meat Value

The *gemara* rules: *mishtava ihu de-la havah yada* — let the *safsera* swear that he genuinely did not know the animal lacked teeth. Once he swears to his ignorance, *u-mashlim bakra dmei basar bezol* — the cowherd pays him the wholesale value of the meat.

Why the wholesale value of the meat, rather than the full value of the cow? *Rashi* suggests it reflects a kind of sliding scale of partial negligence, which is conceptually difficult — liability normally operates as a binary, either you are liable for the full amount or you are not liable at all. *Tosafot* offers a more analytically satisfying answer: what should the cowherd have done upon noticing the ox was not eating? The responsible action would have been to slaughter it immediately before it died entirely, preserving whatever value could be salvaged. The most that could have been recovered at an off-market slaughter is the wholesale price for the meat. Therefore, the cowherd is liable for exactly what his timely intervention would have saved: *dmei basar bezol*. He failed to take the action that would have protected that much value, so he must compensate for it.

### De Facto Shomer

*Tosafot* then addresses the fundamental question this case raises: why is the cowherd liable to the original owner at all, given that he never contracted with him? *Tosafot* ties this to the *mishna* earlier in the *perek*: when one person rents an animal from another and lends it to a third party, *Rabbi Yosi* objects — *keitzad halah oseh s'chora be-parato shel chaveiro?* How can one make business with another's animal? — and rules that the *sho'el* must pay the original owner.

*Tosafot* reads this not merely as a moral objection but as a legal principle: the *sho'el*'s actual use of the original owner's animal may create a borrower relationship *de facto*, regardless of whether any contract was concluded directly between them. Applied here: the orphans purchased the cow (or so everyone thought), the cowherd was hired by the orphans to watch it, and it turned out to be the original owner's cow. The cowherd's being paid to tend this animal may constitute a *de facto* guardianship relationship with whoever the true owner is — even without any direct agreement. This is a far-reaching principle: legal obligations arising from *shomrim* relationships can be generated by the factual reality of whose property one is caring for, not solely by whose name appears in the contract.

## Mixed Hops

### Wrong Pile

A final case: *hahu gavra de'afkid kishuta gabei chavreih* — a person deposited a quantity of hops with his friend. *Hava lei ledidei nami karya dekishuta* — the *shomer* was himself in the beer-making business and had his own pile of hops. *Amar lei lesarsayeih meihai rami* — he instructed his employee to put hops into the brewing vessel, pointing to his own pile. *Azal rami me'idach* — the employee went and took hops from the other pile — the one belonging to the owner of the deposit — and used those instead.

### Negligence Split

*Rav Papa* poses the question: how should the judges rule? Tell the *shomer* to pay the owner: *ana amari lei meihai rami* — I told my attendant to take from this pile (mine), and he took from that one (yours). Tell the attendant to pay: *lo amar li meihai rami meihai rami* — he did not specify *only* this pile and *not* that one; I did not understand there to be a meaningful distinction.

The underlying question is one of reasonable expectation. If the attendant's interpretation — that both piles were interchangeable — was a reasonable one to hold, then the *shomer* should have been more explicit and bears the responsibility for the confusion. If, on the other hand, one should assume that explicit instructions to take from a specific pile exclude all other piles, then the attendant who deviated bears the responsibility. The *gemara* has not yet resolved which assumption governs.

One clarifying factor: *ve'i deshaha shahi lei le'aituyei velo aiti lei* — if the two piles were at different distances, and the attendant was late in returning, the delay itself is evidence that he went to the farther pile. *Gali da'ateih denicha lei* — the *shomer*'s silence upon observing the delay would constitute implicit acquiescence, and the *shomer* would bear the loss. *Bedela shaha* — but in the case where the piles were adjacent and there was no delay, no such inference is available.

### Benefit Principle

The *gemara* cuts through the negligence analysis with a shortcut: *sof sof mai psida ika, ha ka mishtarshi lei* — ultimately, where is the loss? The *shomer*'s beer was improved by the owner's hops. His hops ended up in the *shomer*'s beer. Whatever the question of negligence, the *shomer* benefited from the owner's property without authorization. The remedy is simple: pay back the value of what you benefited from. The technical legal concept here is *mishtarshi lei* — the benefit has taken root in the *shomer*'s property, an idea familiar from *Bava Kamma*'s principle of *meshalem ma she'nehenit*: one who derives benefit from another's property, even absent tortious conduct, must compensate for that benefit.

If the hops improved the *shomer*'s beer, the question of who was negligent becomes largely irrelevant — simply return the value of the hops, and the matter is resolved without needing to adjudicate fault.

### Damage Case

*Rav Sama the son of Rava* introduces a variation: *dehavah shikhra chala* — what if the owner's hops turned the *shomer*'s beer to vinegar? Now there is no benefit to repay; indeed, the *shomer* suffered a loss. The benefit-based shortcut is unavailable. The *shomer* cannot claim compensation from the owner for ruining his beer — that was the result of the employee's actions, for which the *shomer* bears at least some responsibility. But by the same token, the owner cannot invoke *mishtarshi lei* because his hops did not benefit anyone. So we are back to the fundamental question of who was negligent, and the resolution of that question cannot be bypassed.

### Inferior Hops

*Rav Ashi* introduces yet another variation: *bekisi umashlim lei d'mei kisi* — what if the owner's hops were of inferior quality? In that case, the *shomer*'s beer was neither improved nor ruined but perhaps slightly degraded, or the "benefit," such as it is, represents inferior value compared to what the *shomer*'s own hops would have produced. This muddies the *mishtarshi lei* analysis, since the benefit received is not straightforwardly equivalent to the loss suffered.

### Unresolved

The *gemara* ultimately leaves the core negligence question unresolved. It may be suggesting that where *mishtarshi lei* applies — where the *shomer* genuinely benefited from the owner's hops — negligence is irrelevant and repayment is owed regardless. Where there is no benefit — when the hops soured the beer — one must determine who was negligent, and the *gemara* does not deliver a clean ruling. What runs through this case, and indeed through many of the cases on this *amud*, is a recurring pattern: the *gemara* repeatedly imagines situations where multiple parties each acted on reasonable assumptions and neither was clearly negligent. From one perspective, this seems lenient toward *shomrim* — the *gemara* is willing to construct scenarios in which no one bears primary fault. From another perspective, one might argue that the *shomer*, having accepted the fundamental responsibility of custody, ought to bear the consequences when things go wrong even through others' actions, unless a truly clear *oness* is present. That tension between the reasonable-assumptions framework and the *shomer*'s primary responsibility remains alive and unresolved as the *daf* concludes.
