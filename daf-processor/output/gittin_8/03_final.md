# Gittin 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eretz Yisrael Borders

### Boat/Planter Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא חֲדָא: הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט בִּסְפִינָה – כְּמֵבִיא בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ – כְּמֵבִיא בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara presents a contradiction between two *baraitot*. **It was taught** in **one** *baraita*: **One who brings a bill of divorce** that was written **on a boat** in Eretz Yisrael is considered to be **like one who brings** it **in Eretz Yisrael,** and therefore he is not required to say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence. **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita* (*Tosefta* 1:1) that one who brings a bill of divorce written on a boat in Eretz Yisrael is **like one who brings** a bill of divorce **outside of Eretz** Yisrael.

The daf picks up in the middle of a *gemara* discussing the parameters of *Eretz Yisrael* for the purposes of *gittin*. The question concerns someone who brings a *get* while on a boat traveling on a river in *Eretz Yisrael* — is that considered bringing it from within *Eretz Yisrael*, or does it have the status of *chutz la'aretz*, thereby requiring the messenger to declare *bifnei nichtav ubifnei nechtam*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְרְמְיָה: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הָא רַבָּנַן. דִּתְנַן: עֲפַר חוּצָה לָאָרֶץ הַבָּא בִּסְפִינָה לָאָרֶץ – חַיָּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר וּבִשְׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya says:** This is **not difficult,** as one can say that **this** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** and **that** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. As we learned** in a mishna (*Ḥalla* 2:2): With regard to **soil from outside of Eretz** Yisrael **that is brought on a boat to Eretz** Yisrael, anything that grew in that soil **is obligated in tithe and in** the mitzvot of **the Sabbatical** Year. According to this opinion, soil is considered part of Eretz Yisrael once it arrives there, which means that the plants growing in it are considered to be in Eretz Yisrael.

The *gemara* ties this debate to a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Chachamim* regarding a boat carrying soil on which crops are growing while the boat travels through a river in *Eretz Yisrael* — are those crops obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot*? The *Chachamim* say yes; Rabbi Yehuda says only when the boat is actually scraping against the riverbed. The *gemara* also draws a comparison to the case of an *atzitz*, a flowerpot with a hole in it. Rashi understands the boat to be made of pottery, while Tosafot explains it is a wooden boat, which is considered porous. Regardless, the key question is why this agricultural consideration — whether the boat is touching the riverbed — has any bearing on the entirely separate question of *bifnei nichtav*, which concerns different practical matters such as *lishma* and the ability to validate signatures.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַסְּפִינָה גּוֹשֶׁשֶׁת, אֲבָל אֵין הַסְּפִינָה גּוֹשֶׁשֶׁת – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: When** did the Sages say this ruling? **When the boat is skimming [*gosheshet*]** the sea floor, as it is weighed down in the water. **However,** when **the boat is not skimming** the sea floor, the soil is **exempt.** The Sage who maintains that the boat is considered to be like Eretz Yisrael with regard to a bill of divorce holds in accordance with the Rabbis of this mishna, and the one who maintains that the boat is not like Eretz Yisrael for the purposes of a bill of divorce holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.

### Tosafot: Formal Definition

The answer becomes clear from Tosafot's explanation. The *gemara* is not examining whether the pragmatic concerns underlying the *takana* of *bifnei nichtav* are actually satisfied in any given case. Rather, it is drawing on a pre-existing, formal definition of what constitutes *Eretz Yisrael* — derived from the laws of *terumot u'ma'asrot* — and importing that definition wholesale into the context of *gittin*.

Tosafot, in the *tosafot* entitled *atzitz* toward the end of 7b, makes this point explicitly. He writes: *de'nehei de'hacha de'eina gosheses le'Rabbi Yehuda mi'de'hava lei Chutz La'aretz le'inyan ma'aser chashivnan Chutz La'aretz le'inyan get* — if the boat is not scraping the bottom, then since under Rabbi Yehuda's view the produce would not be obligated in *ma'aser*, it is treated as *chutz la'aretz* for *get* purposes as well. The logic is that the place itself is formally labeled *chutz la'aretz*, and Tosafot explains: *keivan desheim Chutz La'aretz al hamakom* — since that place is called *chutz la'aretz* in the formal sense, one must say *bifnei nichtav ubifnei nechtam*, even though from a purely practical standpoint, the *edim* are right there on the river, easily accessible, and presumably familiar with the laws of *lishma*. Tosafot acknowledges this practical observation and addresses it: *kedei shelo tachalok midinas hayam* — for reasons of formal consistency, the determination follows the label, not the practical reality.

Tosafot further clarifies a potentially confusing point. For something to be *chayav* in *terumot u'ma'asrot*, two separate conditions must be met: first, the produce must be growing in *Eretz Yisrael*; and second, the soil must be connected to the ground. For the purposes of *bifnei nichtav*, however, the second condition — soil connectivity — is irrelevant. That is a technical *terumot u'ma'asrot* concern. The only question that matters for *gittin* is the first: is this place formally considered part of *Eretz Yisrael* or not? We therefore borrow the *terumot u'ma'asrot* definition not to resolve questions about dirt connectivity, but as a formal indicator of whether one is standing inside or outside the land. And that formalistic determination, in turn, governs whether *bifnei nichtav* must be said.

One might ask about the upper floor analogy: does a person writing a *get* on a second floor in Israel need to say *bifnei nichtav* simply because dirt on an upper story would not be *chayav* in *terumot u'ma'asrot*? Tosafot addresses this: there at least the soil beneath the building is *chayav*, whereas on a boat in a river perhaps nothing below would be *chayav* — yet even then, Tosafot's point stands, because the question about the boat is not really about the dirt at all. It is about whether the location itself is formally designated as *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Rav Nachman: Rivers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: בִּנְהָרוֹת דְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי. אֶלָּא כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בַּיָּם הַגָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: With regard to** the **rivers that are in Eretz Yisrael** on which a boat is sailing, **everyone agrees** that a bill of divorce written on that boat is considered to be written in Eretz Yisrael. **However, when they disagree** it is **with regard to the Great Sea,** i.e., the Mediterranean Sea. In other words, is a boat located in the Mediterranean Sea considered to be in Eretz Yisrael or not?

The *gemara* resumes on 8a. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak states: *benaharot Eretz Yisrael kuli alma lo pligi* — when it comes to rivers within *Eretz Yisrael*, nobody disagrees. A river flowing through the land of Israel is unambiguously within the borders of Israel. Rashi confirms this: *kuli alma lo pligi de'Eretz Yisrael ninhu*, and even if the boat is not scraping the riverbed, that is irrelevant because, as Rashi explains, *ta'ama deget lav mishum yanika min hakarka hu* — the rationale for *gittin* is not about whether the soil is drawing nourishment from the ground of Israel, which is a *ma'aser* and *shvi'it* concern. Therefore, whether it is a river or dry land, *lo shna nahar velo shna yabasha*.

What, then, is the subject of the debate? *Ela ki pligi beyam hagadol* — the dispute concerns the Mediterranean Sea. If one is carrying a *get* on a boat in the Mediterranean, off the coast of Israel, at what point is that boat considered within *Eretz Yisrael* and at what point is it in international waters? This is where the *gemara* once again borrows a pre-existing formal definition from *terumot u'ma'asrot* — but now it is a more appropriate borrowing, because this is not about the technical question of soil connectivity. It is a genuine question about what constitutes the territorial boundaries of *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Turei Amnon Border

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: אֵיזֶהוּ אֶרֶץ וְאֵיזֶהוּ חוּצָה לָאָרֶץ? כֹּל שֶׁשּׁוֹפֵעַ וְיוֹרֵד מִטּוּרֵי אַמְנוֹן וְלִפְנִים – אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל; מִטּוּרֵי אַמְנוֹן וְלַחוּץ – חוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in the *Tosefta* (*Terumot* 2:12): **What is Eretz** Yisrael **and what is outside of Eretz** Yisrael? **Any** slope **that descends at an angle from Turei Amnon** in Syria **and inward** toward Eretz Yisrael is part of **Eretz Yisrael. From Turei Amnon and outward,** northward, is considered **outside of Eretz** Yisrael.

A *baraita* is cited: *eizehu Eretz Yisrael ve'eizehu Chutz La'aretz?* — which areas belong to *Eretz Yisrael* and which to *chutz la'aretz* for purposes of *terumah*? *Kol she'shofei'a v'yored mi'Turei Amnon v'lifnim Eretz Yisrael; mi'Turei Amnon v'lachutz Chutz La'aretz* — anything that slopes down from *Turei Amnon* inward is *Eretz Yisrael*; from *Turei Amnon* outward is *chutz la'aretz*. Rashi identifies *Turei Amnon* as the Aramaic equivalent of *Hor HaHar*, the mountain ridge mentioned in the Torah as part of the western border of the Land. The image is that of a mountain ridge: whatever slopes toward the interior of the land is *Eretz Yisrael*, and whatever slopes away is outside it.

### Island Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנִּסִּין שֶׁבַּיָּם – רוֹאִין אוֹתָן כְּאִילּוּ חוּט מָתוּחַ עֲלֵיהֶם מִטּוּרֵי אַמְנוֹן עַד נַחַל מִצְרַיִם; מִן הַחוּט וְלִפְנִים – אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל; מִן הַחוּט וְלַחוּץ – חוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **The islands [*nissin*] that are in the sea, one views them as though a string were pulled taut over them from Turei Amnon** in the north **to the River of Egypt,** Wadi el-Arish, in the south. **From the string and inward,** i.e., east, is **Eretz Yisrael; from the string and outward,** west, is considered **outside of Eretz** Yisrael. This is the opinion of the Rabbis.

The *baraita* continues with the case of islands: *nasin she'ba'yam ro'in otan k'ilu chut matuach aleihen mi'Turei Amnon ad Nachal Mitzrayim* — for islands in the sea, one imagines a string drawn from *Turei Amnon* to *Nachal Mitzrayim* (understood as Wadi el-Arish at the southern coastal border). Anything within the arc between that string and the coastline is considered *Eretz Yisrael*; everything outside that string is *chutz la'aretz*. This is the position of the *Tanna Kamma*. Under this view, the northern and southern coastal borders are connected by a diagonal line, and the enclosed area of Mediterranean waters is considered part of the Land.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁכְּנֶגֶד אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – הֲרֵי הוּא כְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּגְבוּל יָם, וְהָיָה לָכֶם הַיָּם הַגָּדוֹל וּגְבוּל, זֶה יִהְיֶה לָכֶם גְּבוּל יָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: Any** place **that** is directly **across from Eretz Yisrael,** including the sea itself, **is considered** part **of Eretz Yisrael, as it is stated: "And for the western border, you shall have the Great Sea for a border, this shall be your west border"** (Numbers 34:6). According to this opinion, the entire territory directly across from Eretz Yisrael is considered part of Eretz Yisrael.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and expands this definition significantly. He derives from the verse *u'gvul yam v'hayah lachem haYam HaGadol u'gvul* — the redundancy of the word *u'gvul* teaches that more of the Mediterranean is to be included. His formulation: *kol she'keneged Eretz Yisrael harei hu k'Eretz Yisrael* — anything directly opposite Israel is counted as Israel. And for islands that fall to the sides, rather than drawing the diagonal connecting line of the *Tanna Kamma*, Rabbi Yehuda draws two parallel horizontal lines — one extending westward from *Turei Amnon* (which he terms *Kaflurya*) to the Atlantic, and one extending westward from *Nachal Mitzrayim* to the Atlantic. Everything between those two parallel lines is *Eretz Yisrael*, and everything outside them is *chutz la'aretz*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַנִּסִּין שֶׁבַּצְּדָדִין, רוֹאִין אוֹתָן כְּאִילּוּ חוּט מָתוּחַ עֲלֵיהֶן מִקַּפְלוּרְיָא וְעַד יָם אוֹקְיָינוֹס, וּמִנַּחַל מִצְרַיִם וְעַד יָם אוֹקְיָינוֹס; מִן הַחוּט וְלִפְנִים – אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִן הַחוּט וְלַחוּץ – חוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **the islands that are on the sides,** which do not exactly line up on the north or the south, **one views them as though a string were pulled taut over them** in the north **from Kefalorya,** west of Turei Amnon, **to the Atlantic Ocean, and** in the south **from the River of Egypt** westward **until the Atlantic Ocean.** Those islands that lie **from the string and inward** are part of **Eretz Yisrael,** whereas those **from the string and outward** are **outside of Eretz** Yisrael.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״וּגְבוּל״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְנִסִּין. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, נִסִּין לָא צְרִיכִי קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis, what do they do with this** verse: **"And for the border"?** Rabbi Yehuda's opinion that the border of Eretz Yisrael extends into the sea is apparently supported by this verse. The Gemara answers: **They require it to** teach that the **islands** themselves are considered to be within Eretz Yisrael. **And Rabbi Yehuda** would respond that an additional **verse is not required** to teach the *halakha* concerning the **islands,** as it is clear that they are part of Eretz Yisrael.

The *gemara* asks: what does the *Tanna Kamma* do with the word *u'gvul* that Rabbi Yehuda uses for his expansion? Answer: the *Tanna Kamma* needs it to include islands at all — without it, one might not know islands are counted. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, derives the inclusion of islands from the word *yam* itself, and therefore the additional *u'gvul* is available to teach the parallel-line rule.

Under Rabbi Yehuda's map, the territory between the two parallel lines extending across the entire Mediterranean would be extraordinarily vast — encompassing islands such as Sicily, Cyprus, and potentially parts of Greece. The debate between the *Chachamim* and Rabbi Yehuda about whether a boat is in *Eretz Yisrael* or *chutz la'aretz* therefore pertains to boats sailing in the farther regions of the Mediterranean: the *Chachamim* would call those waters *chutz la'aretz*, while Rabbi Yehuda would say they remain *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Tosafot: France Question

This leads to a question posed by Rabbeinu Peter to Rabbeinu Tam, cited in Tosafot: given Rabbi Yehuda's position, should communities in France be obligated in *ma'aser*, since they lie to the west of *Eretz Yisrael* and might fall within Rabbi Yehuda's expanded zone? Tosafot records three responses.

The first answer is simply that we do not rule according to Rabbi Yehuda — *delo kaima lan k'Rabbi Yehuda*. Tosafot then raises an additional question on the side: even granting Rabbi Yehuda's expansion, what does this have to do with *gittin*? Did Rabbi Yehuda really mean to say that all those regions — including apparently distant lands — had communities that were experts in *lishma* and that would not require *bifnei nichtav*? This question itself reinforces the point that this *gemara* is working with purely formal, pre-packaged definitions rather than the substantive concerns of the *takana*.

The second answer — which Tosafot identifies as closer to the plain sense of the *gemara* and as consistent with Rashi's commentary — is that Rabbi Yehuda is speaking only about islands in the Mediterranean, not about major continental land masses. *Delo havay beyabasha ad Kunas ela banasim shebaYam HaGadol davka* — his ruling applies only to islands in the sea. Sicily and certain Greek islands might be included, but France, Spain, and other major territories would not be. Tosafot supports this from a series of verses — *ad HaYam HaGadol mevo hashemesh yiheye gevulchem*, *veshatati gevulcha miYam Suf ve'ad Yam Pelishtim*, and so on — which indicate that the borders are defined in terms of surrounding seas and the territories they enclose, not unlimited projection across entire continents.

The third answer maintains that Rabbi Yehuda did actually know, in his time, that *lishma* requirements were being satisfied even in those regions, and that he knew specifically which communities would qualify. This third response, like the first, returns to the practical considerations of *lishma* and signature validation. Tosafot's most substantive resolution remains the middle one: the discussion concerns islands in the sea, and once the formal definition of which islands belong to *Eretz Yisrael* is established, that definition is imported wholesale into the context of *gittin* — not because it perfectly mirrors the needs of the *takana*, but because the *gemara* is working from a formalistic geographic definition.

## Three Definitions

### Rabbi Meir: Akko

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: עַכּוֹ – כְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְכוּ׳
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Meir says: Akko is like Eretz Yisrael** with regard to bills of divorce.

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishna*: *Rabbi Meir omer Akko k'Eretz Yisrael l'Gittin* — Rabbi Meir rules that Akko counts as *Eretz Yisrael* for *gittin* purposes. This is a more specifically tailored definition — for *gittin* only, not for other purposes — which already reflects a different approach from the broad formalistic borrowing of the earlier discussion.

### Slave to Syria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְסוּרְיָא, כְּמוֹכֵר בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **raised a dilemma before Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba: One who sells his slave to** a master in **Syria,** is **he considered like one who sells** his slave **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, in which case the seller is penalized by the emancipation of his slave, **or not?**

The *gemara* then records a question posed to Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba: *hamocher avdo l'Surya k'mocher b'chutz la'aretz dami o lo?* — if someone sells his slave to *Surya* (Syria), is it treated like selling him to *chutz la'aretz*? This question introduces a third legal context in which the definition of *Eretz Yisrael* is relevant. There is a *halacha* that if a master sells his slave outside of *Eretz Yisrael*, the slave goes free as a *knas*, because an *eved kena'ani* is obligated in certain *mitzvot* tied to the land, and by selling him away the master is effectively determining that the slave will be unable to fulfill those obligations and will be cut off from the Jewish community's center of life. The question is whether *Surya* — understood as the territory broadly called Syria, which includes *Aram Tzova* and which was conquered by King David — is considered *chutz la'aretz* for this purpose.

### Graduated Definitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: תְּנֵיתוּהָ, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: עַכּוֹ – כְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְגִיטִּין; לְגִיטִּין – אִין, לַעֲבָדִים – לָא, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן סוּרְיָא דִּמְרַחֲקָא טוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to them: You learned** a resolution for this dilemma from the mishna: **Rabbi Meir says** that **Akko** is **like Eretz Yisrael with regard to bills of divorce,** from which it may be inferred: **With regard to bills of divorce, yes,** but **with regard to slaves, no,** it is not considered part of Eretz Yisrael, **and all the more so Syria, which is far more distant** than Akko from the main areas of Eretz Yisrael. Therefore, this owner has sold his slave outside of Eretz Yisrael.

The *gemara* answers by invoking Rabbi Meir's ruling: *l'Gittin in, l'avadim lo* — Akko is like *Eretz Yisrael* for *gittin* purposes but not for slave purposes. And if even Akko, which is immediately adjacent to the land, is considered *chutz la'aretz* for slave purposes, then *kol sheken Surya d'merachaka tuva* — how much more so Syria, which is much more distant from the central population of the land.

This establishes three distinct and graduated definitions of *Eretz Yisrael*. The first is the *terumot u'ma'asrot* definition, which turns on the formal question of which land was sanctified through conquest. The second is the *gittin* definition, which reflects where there is sufficient Jewish presence, knowledge of *lishma*, and ability to validate documents. The third is the *avadim* definition, which concerns where the primary Jewish community actually lives — the *ikkar yishuv* — such that selling a slave away from that center is genuinely disadvantaging him.

Conceptually, *Surya* may be *chayav* in *terumot u'ma'asrot* — because it was formally sanctified through conquest — yet it is treated as *chutz la'aretz* for both *gittin* and for the law of slaves. This is because the *gittin* definition asks where there is Jewish travel and communal life sufficient to ensure *lishma*, and the *avadim* definition asks where the primary Jewish population resides. *Surya* satisfies the criterion of formal sanctification but not the criteria of actual Jewish settlement and communal life. The three concentric definitions therefore correspond to three different underlying concerns, and each produces a different geographic result.

## Syria's Status

### Syria: Three Chutz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּרָכִים שָׁוְותָה סוּרְיָא לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וּבִשְׁלֹשָׁה לְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ. עֲפָרָהּ טָמֵא – כְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, וְהַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְסוּרְיָא – כְּמוֹכֵר בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ. וְהַמֵּבִיא גֵּט מִסּוּרְיָא – כְּמֵבִיא מֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned the status of Syria, the Gemara cites a related *halakha*. **The Sages taught:** **In three ways Syria is equal to Eretz Yisrael, and in three** ways it is similar **to outside of Eretz** Yisrael. Syria has the status of land that is outside of Eretz Yisrael in the following respects: First, **its soil is ritually impure like** that of land **outside of Eretz** Yisrael. **And** the second is that **one who sells his slave to** a master in **Syria is like one who sells** him to a master **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, and the second master is obligated to emancipate the slave. **And** third, **one who brings a bill of divorce from Syria is like one who brings** it **from outside of Eretz** Yisrael, in that he must say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence.

A *baraita* articulates the multi-layered status of *Surya* systematically. *Tanu Rabbanan bishlosha drachim shavsa Surya l'Eretz Yisrael u'vishlosha l'Chutz La'aretz* — in three ways *Surya* resembles *Eretz Yisrael*, and in three ways it resembles *chutz la'aretz*.

The three ways it is like *chutz la'aretz*: first, *afrah tamei k'Chutz La'aretz* — its soil is *tamei* like that of *chutz la'aretz*. The Rabbis decreed that the soil of lands outside *Eretz Yisrael* is *tamei*, based on the concern that non-Jews buried their dead throughout the land without demarcation, posing a risk of *tumah* to *kohanim* who traveled there. Beyond the practical concern, this decree also served as a communal mechanism to encourage Jews — and particularly *kohanim* — to remain in *Eretz Yisrael* rather than emigrate, giving *halachic* reality to the sanctity of living in the Land. *Surya*, despite its formal *kedusha* for *terumot u'ma'asrot*, is treated like *chutz la'aretz* in this regard. Second, *v'hamocher avdo l'Surya k'mocher b'Chutz La'aretz* — selling a slave there is like selling to *chutz la'aretz*. Third, *v'hameivi get mi'Surya k'meivi mi'Chutz La'aretz* — one who brings a *get* from *Surya* must declare *bifnei nichtav*, as it is treated as *chutz la'aretz* for *gittin*.

### Kibush Yachid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִשְׁלֹשָׁה לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל: חַיֶּיבֶת בְּמַעֲשֵׂר וּבִשְׁבִיעִית – כְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל
>
> **Translation:** **And in three** ways Syria is similar **to Eretz Yisrael:** Its produce is **obligated in tithe and in** the mitzvot of **the Sabbatical** Year **like Eretz Yisrael.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיֶּיבֶת בְּמַעֲשֵׂר וּבִשְׁבִיעִית כְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – קָסָבַר כִּיבּוּשׁ יָחִיד שְׁמֵיהּ כִּיבּוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: The *tanna* who says Syria is **obligated in tithe and** the mitzvot of **the Sabbatical** Year **like Eretz Yisrael holds** that the **conquest of an individual is called a conquest.** Once Syria was conquered by King David, who is considered an individual in this regard, the sanctity of Eretz Yisrael applied to it and its residents became obligated in the mitzvot of Eretz Yisrael.

Three ways *Surya* is like *Eretz Yisrael*: *chayavet b'ma'aser u'bishvi'it k'Eretz Yisrael* — it is obligated in *ma'aser* and *shvi'it* like *Eretz Yisrael*. The *gemara* explains the underlying principle: *kasavar kibuish yachid shmo kibuish* — it holds that a conquest by an individual counts as a valid conquest for purposes of sanctifying land with the *kedusha* of *Eretz Yisrael*. It is this principle that makes *Surya chayav* in *terumot u'ma'asrot*, since the territories were conquered by David — an individual acting as king rather than through a full national enterprise.

### Rashi: Kibush Yachid

Rashi explains *kibuish yachid* as follows: *shelo hayu kol Yisrael b'yachad k'derech shehayu b'kibuish Yehoshua* — unlike Joshua's conquest, which was a fully national undertaking, David's campaigns were not carried out together with all of Israel. Rashi adds two related points: David's conquest lacked the participation of all twelve tribes as a national army, and it also lacked *Sanhedrin* authorization, which is what would have transformed a military campaign into a nationally sanctioned act of conquest. Additionally, Joshua's conquest was executed *l'tzorech kol Yisrael* — for the benefit of all Israel, for the purpose of division among the tribes. David's conquests, by contrast, were undertaken *l'tzorcho* — for his own purposes as king, not to serve an obvious national goal. That is what makes them *kibuish yachid*.

The *gemara*'s position here, that *kibuish yachid* does confer *kedusha* for *terumot u'ma'asrot*, means David's conquest of these territories sanctified them even without a full national enterprise. A striking question arises in this context: *terumot u'ma'asrot* as practiced is based on the sanctity established during the Second Temple period — we do not generally hold that the sanctity of the First Temple period persists for *terumot u'ma'asrot* purposes. Why, then, should what David did be the operative consideration? This question is worth noting, though the *gemara* proceeds on the assumption that it is.

### Tosafot: Kibush Yachid

Tosafot offers a fundamentally different understanding of *kibuish yachid*, derived from the *Sifrei* at the end of *Parshat Va'yihei Eikev*. For Tosafot, the problem is not that David's conquest was a private rather than national enterprise. Rather, the issue is that one cannot formally annex territory to *Eretz Yisrael* and endow it with *kedusha* until the primary land of Israel has itself been fully conquered. The *Sifrei* derives this from the verse *samuch l'pator yadcha shelo horashta* — you have not yet taken possession of that which is near you. Rashi there explains that this refers to the Jebusite enclave near Jerusalem, which had not yet been subdued. If the land adjacent to the capital had not been fully conquered, one cannot go off and conquer *Aram Naharayim* and *Aram Tzova* and incorporate those territories into the sacred land of Israel.

However, *aval achar shekol Eretz Yisrael kvusha* — once the entire primary land of Israel is conquered, *kol makom asher tidroch kaf raglchem lachem* — any territory subsequently conquered, even by an individual, attains the sanctity of *Eretz Yisrael*. So for Tosafot, *kibuish yachid* is not primarily about the absence of national participation; it is about the sequence of conquest. David conquered *Surya* before completing the conquest of the primary land, and that timing is what prevented full annexation. The conquered territories received some *kedusha* but not the full *kedusha* that would have come with a conquest occurring after *Eretz Yisrael* itself was fully in hand.

### Tahara in Syria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָרוֹצֶה לִיכָּנֵס לָהּ בְּטׇהֳרָה נִכְנָס
>
> **Translation:** **And one who wishes to enter it** and remain **in** a state of **ritual purity may** so **enter,**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמְרַתְּ עֲפָרָהּ טָמֵא? בְּשִׁידָּה, תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't you say** that **its soil is ritually impure?** How then is it possible for one to enter it in a state of ritual purity? The Gemara answers: The *baraita* means that one enters it **in a chest, a box, or a cabinet.** In this case he remains pure, as he did not come into contact with the ground itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הַנִּכְנָס לְאֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים בְּשִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל – רַבִּי מְטַמֵּא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְטַהֵר. וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי לָא קָא מְטַמֵּא אֶלָּא בְּאֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים, דְּגָזְרוּ עַל גּוּשָׁהּ וְעַל אֲוִירָהּ, אֲבָל סוּרְיָא, עַל גּוּשָׁהּ גָּזְרוּ, עַל אֲוִירָהּ לֹא גָּזְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who enters the land of the nations,** i.e., any territory outside of Eretz Yisrael, **in a chest, a box, or a cabinet, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **deems** him ritually **impure,** and **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, deems** him **pure. And even Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **deems** one who did not touch the ground itself **impure only in the land of the nations,** concerning **which they decreed** impurity **upon** both **its clumps** of soil **and upon its air. However,** with regard to **Syria,** everyone agrees that **they decreed** impurity **upon its clumps** of soil, but **they did not decree** impurity **upon its air.** Therefore, it is possible to enter Syria and remain in a state of ritual purity if one does not touch the ground itself.

The second way *Surya* resembles *Eretz Yisrael* in its unique status: *v'harotzeh l'hikanes lah b'tahara nichnas* — if one wishes to enter *Surya* while remaining *tahor*, it is possible to do so. The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: one who enters *Eretz Ha'amim* (foreign lands) while enclosed in a box, chest, or tower — *Rebbe* says he becomes *tamei*; Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda says he remains *tahor*. The *gemara* then clarifies that even Rebbe only rules stringently in normal *chutz la'aretz*, where the Rabbis decreed *tumah* not only on the soil itself but on the very air — *al gusha gazru, avira lo gazru*. In Syria, however, the decree applies only to touching the soil, not to the air above it or to simply being present over the land. Therefore, everyone agrees that in *Surya* one can maintain *tahara* by avoiding direct contact with the ground, for example by being carried in an enclosed conveyance — though Tosafot notes that one riding a horse might similarly argue the same point.

The irony of this ruling is notable: the soil of *Surya* is formally *tamei*, and yet its *tumah* is categorically less severe than that of ordinary *chutz la'aretz*. This precisely reflects *Surya*'s middle position — more connected to *Eretz Yisrael* than the rest of the world, yet not fully part of it.

### Purchasing Fields

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַקּוֹנֶה שָׂדֶה בְּסוּרְיָא כְּקוֹנָהּ בְּפַרְוָארֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם. חַיֶּיבֶת בְּמַעֲשֵׂר וּבִשְׁבִיעִית כְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – קָסָבַר כִּיבּוּשׁ יָחִיד שְׁמֵיהּ כִּיבּוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further teaches: **And one who purchases a field in Syria is like one who purchases** a field **in the outskirts of Jerusalem.** The Gemara clarifies: The *tanna* who says Syria is **obligated in tithe and** the mitzvot of **the Sabbatical** Year **like Eretz Yisrael holds** that the **conquest of an individual is called a conquest.** Once Syria was conquered by King David, who is considered an individual in this regard, the sanctity of Eretz Yisrael applied to it and its residents became obligated in the mitzvot of Eretz Yisrael.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַקּוֹנֶה שָׂדֶה בְּסוּרְיָא כְּקוֹנָהּ בְּפַרְוָארֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם – לְמַאי הִילְכְתָא?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further teaches: **And one who purchases a field in Syria is like one who purchases** a field **in the outskirts of Jerusalem.** The Gemara asks: With regard **to which *halakha*** was this stated? What practical ruling is taught by this statement?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לוֹמַר שֶׁכּוֹתְבִין עָלָיו אוֹנוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says:** This serves **to say that one writes a bill of sale [*ono*] for this** purchase, **and** one may write a bill of sale **even on Shabbat.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשַׁבָּת סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ?! כִּדְאָמַר רָבָא: אוֹמֵר לְגוֹי וְעוֹשֶׂה; הָכָא נָמֵי אוֹמֵר לְגוֹי וְעוֹשֶׂה. וְאַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲמִירָה לְגוֹי שְׁבוּת, מִשּׁוּם יִשּׁוּב אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – לָא גְּזוּר רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Can it **enter your mind** that one may write this bill of sale **on Shabbat?** Writing on Shabbat is a prohibited labor for which one is liable to receive court-imposed capital punishment. The Gemara explains: This is **as Rava says** with regard to a similar issue, that **one tells a gentile** that he should do it, **and he does** so. **Here too,** it is referring to a situation where **one tells a gentile** that he should write a bill of sale, **and he does** so. **And even though** the *halakha* generally is **that telling a gentile** to perform an action that is prohibited for a Jew on Shabbat violates **a rabbinic decree,** since the Sages prohibited instructing a gentile to perform prohibited labor on behalf of a Jew on Shabbat, here **the Sages did not** impose this **decree, due to** the mitzva of **settling Eretz Yisrael.**

The third way *Surya* resembles *Eretz Yisrael*: *v'hakoneh sadeh b'Surya k'koneh b'parvarei Yerushalayim* — one who buys a field in *Surya* is like one who purchases property in the environs of Jerusalem itself. The *gemara* asks: *l'mai hilchasa* — what is the practical implication of this? Rav Sheshes answers: *lomar shekosvin alav ono va'afilu b'Shabbos* — it means that the deed of sale may be drawn up even on *Shabbat*. The *gemara* immediately asks: surely you do not mean that the Jew himself writes on *Shabbat*? The answer, as derived from Rava's ruling elsewhere, is *omer l'Akum v'oseh* — a non-Jew is instructed to draw up the document. Even though *amira l'Akum shvut* is normally prohibited as a Rabbinic prohibition, *mishum yishuv Eretz Yisrael lo gazru rabanan* — the Rabbis did not apply this prohibition when the purpose is settling the Land of Israel.

The tension here is striking. On one hand, the very same *Surya* whose dirt is *tamei*, whose slaves gain freedom if sold there, and from which a *get* requires *bifnei nichtav* — that same territory is valued so highly as an extension of *Eretz Yisrael* that purchasing fields there overrides the Rabbinic prohibition against *amira l'goy* on *Shabbat*. Perhaps the purchase of agricultural fields (*sadeh* suggests planting) in *Surya* serves the larger project of *yishuv Eretz Yisrael* by providing agricultural resources for the Jewish community in the Land proper. Or perhaps the *kedusha* inherent in the sanctified soil, even if Jews do not presently live there in large numbers, is sufficient to make Jewish ownership of that land a religious priority.

Tosafot notes the limits of this ruling. *Mishum yishuv Eretz Yisrael lo gazru rabanan* — only for the specific *mitzvah* of *yishuv Eretz Yisrael* is *amira l'goy* permitted even for a *melacha d'Oraisa*. For any other *mitzvah* purpose, *amira l'goy* remains permitted only for a *d'rabbanan* prohibition, not for a *d'Oraisa*. Tosafot does note that the *Halachot Gedolot* takes a broader view, permitting *amira l'goy* even for a *d'Oraisa* prohibition whenever it serves a *mitzvah* purpose — citing the case of *milah* as an example. We do not rule according to this position, though the Rema is prepared to rely on it *b'sha'at hadechak*. For *yishuv Eretz Yisrael*, however, the ruling of the *gemara* is clear and undisputed: *amira l'goy* is permitted.

## Slave's Get Shichrur

### Eved Brings Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֶבֶד שֶׁהֵבִיא גִּיטּוֹ, וְכָתוּב בּוֹ: ״עַצְמְךָ וּנְכָסַיי קְנוּיִין לָךְ״, עַצְמוֹ – קָנָה, נְכָסִים – לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** With regard to **a slave who brought his bill** of manumission to a court, **and it is written in it: You and my property are transferred to you, he acquires himself** via this document, and he is emancipated. However, **he does not acquire** the **property** unless the document is confirmed in court through its witnesses, like other documents.

Since the discussion has introduced the laws of slavery and manumission, the *gemara* turns to a related *halacha* found in the *mishna*: *eved she'heivi gito v'kasuv bo atzmecha u'nechasai knuyim lach* — a slave who brings his own writ of manumission containing the language "you and all my property are given to you." The ruling: *atzmo kanah, nechasim lo kanah* — he acquires his freedom but does not acquire the property.

### Get vs. Gift

Rashi explains the underlying reasoning. The slave's declaration of *bifnei nichtav* — the very statement that the document was written and signed in his presence — functions as a *kiyum*, a validation of the signatures on the document. But it serves as a *kiyum* only in its capacity as a *get shichrur*, a writ of manumission, and not in its capacity as a *matan*, a gift instrument. The *gemara* introduced a parallel *takana* for *gitei shichrur* analogous to the *takana* for *gitei nashim*: the slave declaring *bifnei nichtav* is a special leniency granted to him with respect to validating his own document of freedom. There is, however, no such leniency for gift documents. A gift document requires standard *kiyum shtarot* — validation of its signatures by two witnesses. Since the single document performs two legal functions simultaneously — manumitting the slave and gifting him property — the *kiyum* achieved through *bifnei nichtav* is effective only for the manumission aspect. For the property transfer, the document must be independently validated through normal means.

### Abaye vs. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״כֹּל נְכָסַיי קְנוּיִין לָךְ״, מַהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁקָּנָה עַצְמוֹ, קָנָה נְכָסִים.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If the bill of manumission stated: **All of my property is transferred to you, what is** the *halakha*? **Abaye said: Since he acquired himself** as a freeman, as he is included in the property mentioned in the document, **he acquires** the rest of the **property** as well.

A question is then raised: the case described above involves two statements — "you are given to yourself" and "my property is given to you" — phrased as two distinct phrases within one document. The *bifnei nichtav* can therefore potentially be understood as applying to one phrase and not the other, since there are two separable statements. But what if the document uses a single phrase: *kol nechasai k'nuim lach* — all my property is given to you? Here, "all my property" includes the slave himself, so the freedom and the gift are expressed in a single unified statement rather than two discrete ones. Does the *kiyum* achieved by *bifnei nichtav* then apply to the entire sentence — including the property — or only to the freedom element?

Abaye rules: *mitoch she'kanah atzmo kanah nechasim* — since the *kiyum* validates the sentence for the purpose of acquiring himself, it validates the sentence in its entirety, including the property. The sentence is one undivided legal act; either the *kiyum* applies to the whole sentence or it does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא עַצְמוֹ לִיקְנֵי, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַגֵּט אִשָּׁה, אֶלָּא נְכָסִים לָא לִיקְנֵי, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַקִּיּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת דְּעָלְמָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Abaye: **Granted, he should acquire himself, just as it is in** the case of **a bill of divorce** of **a woman,** who is divorced when she brings the document herself. **However, he should not acquire** the **property, just as it is in** the case of the **ratification of typical** legal **documents.** If someone brings a typical document that deals with monetary matters that has not been ratified, the court will not rely on that document. So too here, as the bill of manumission, which includes a transfer of property, has not been ratified, he should not acquire the property.

Rava disagrees: *bishlama atzmo liknei, midi d'hava a'get isha* — it is understandable that the *kiyum* works for his own freedom, just as *bifnei nichtav* works for a *get isha*. *Ela nechasim lo liknei, midi d'hava a'kiyum shtarot d'alma* — but for the property, from the vantage point of a gift document, the sentence requires standard validation of signatures, and *bifnei nichtav* does not supply that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁלֹּא קָנָה נְכָסִים – לֹא קָנָה עַצְמוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא נְכָסִים לָא לִיקְנֵי, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַקִּיּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת דְּעָלְמָא, אֶלָּא עַצְמוֹ לִיקְנֵי, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַגֵּט אִשָּׁה!
>
> **Translation:** After hearing Rava's objection, **Abaye then said** the opposite: **Since he did not acquire the property, he does not acquire himself** either. **Rava said to him: Granted, he does not acquire the property, just as it is in** the case of the **ratification of typical** legal **documents; however, he should acquire himself, just as it is in** the case of **a bill of divorce** of **a woman,** who can bring her own bill of divorce and testify about it.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה, עַצְמוֹ – קָנָה, נְכָסִים – לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava says:** With regard to **both this and that,** both in the case when the bill of manumission states: You and my property, and when it says: All of my property, **he acquires himself** but **he does not acquire** the **property.**

The debate continues as Abaye reconsiders. Unwilling to abandon the principle that the sentence is a single unified act, Abaye reverses the logic: *mitoch she'lo kanah nechasim lo kanah atzmo* — if the *kiyum* is insufficient to transfer the property, then by the same token it should not be sufficient to free him either. If the sentence stands or falls as a whole, then either it is fully validated or it is not validated at all.

Rava responds with equal firmness: *bishlama nechasim lo liknei* — I agree the property is not transferred, since that aspect of the document functions as a gift document requiring ordinary validation. *Ela atzmo liknei, midi d'hava a'get isha* — but he should acquire himself. The *kiyum* provided by *bifnei nichtav* is effective for the manumission aspect of the document, since that is precisely what the *takana* of *bifnei nichtav* was established to achieve.

In Rava's final ruling, *echad zeh ve'echad zeh atzmo kanah nechasim lo kanah* — whether the document phrases the two elements in two separate clauses or in a single unified clause, the result is identical: he is freed but does not acquire the property. Rava's position is that the legal effects of a document can be analyzed and split according to the distinct functions the document performs, even when those functions are expressed in a single sentence. A *get shichrur* and a *matan* are two categorically different types of legal instruments governed by different rules of validation. The fact that they happen to be expressed together — even in one sentence — does not merge them into a single legal act for the purposes of *kiyum*. Abaye, by contrast, insists that the physical sentence is the irreducible unit: either the *kiyum* validates the sentence or it does not, and one cannot selectively apply the *kiyum* to some of its legal ramifications while denying it to others.
