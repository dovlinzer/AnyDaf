# Eruvin 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eruv Types & Mishna

### Mishna Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּכֹּל מְעָרְבִין וּמִשְׁתַּתְּפִין, חוּץ מִן הַמַּיִם וּמִן הַמֶּלַח.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may establish a joining** of houses in courtyards [*eiruv ḥatzerot*] in order to permit carrying on Shabbat in a courtyard shared by two or more houses, and one may establish a joining of Shabbat borders [*eiruv teḥumin*] in order to extend the distance one is permitted to walk on Shabbat; **and** similarly, **one may merge** courtyards in order to permit carrying in an alleyway shared by two or more courtyards. This may be done **with all** kinds of food **except for water and salt,** as they are not considered foods and therefore may not be used for these purposes.

The *daf* opens with the beginning of a new *perek*, and the *mishna* at the bottom of 26b sets the stage for the entire chapter. *Bechol me'arvin u'mishtatfin* — one may make an *eruv* and a *shituf* with any food. The *shituf* refers to the joining of the different *chatzerot* — the various courtyards or streets onto which houses open — to form a unified domain for carrying purposes within the *mavoi*, the alleyway. This is the higher-level structure. The more basic building block is *eruv chatzerot* itself, by which the houses opening into a common courtyard are unified so that one may carry freely between them. Both mechanisms require participants to contribute a quantity of food that symbolizes shared ownership and residence.

### Tosafos Reading

Rashi reads *bechol me'arvin* as encompassing *eruvei chatzerot* as well as *eruvei techumin*. Tosafos, however, argues that the *gemara* subsequently makes clear that *eruv chatzerot* may only be performed with bread — *b'pat* exclusively. Therefore, Tosafos understands *me'arvin* in this *mishna* as referring specifically to *eruvei techumin*, while *mishtatfin* covers *shitufei mevo'ot*. The practical upshot is striking: *eruv chatzerot*, which is the counterpart to *shitufei mevo'ot*, is limited to bread, whereas the broader category of food that may be used for any purpose applies to *shitufei mevo'ot* and *eruvei techumin*. One should be attentive to this distinction throughout the chapter.

### Water & Salt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַכֹּל נִיקָּח בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר, חוּץ מִן הַמַּיִם וּמִן הַמֶּלַח. הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַמָּזוֹן — מוּתָּר בְּמֶלַח וּבְמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues with two similar principles: **All** types of food **may be bought with** second-**tithe money,** which must be taken to Jerusalem and used to purchase food (Deuteronomy 14:26), **except for water and salt.** Similarly, **one who vows that nourishment** is prohibited to him **is permitted** to eat **water and salt,** as they are not considered sources of nourishment.

Both uses of food enumerated in the *mishna* — *eruv* and *shituf* — exclude water and salt: *chutz min hamayim u'min hamelach*. The same exclusion recurs in two further *halakhot* stated in the *mishna*: *v'hakol nikach b'kesef ma'aser chutz min hamayim u'min hamelach* — one may redeem *ma'aser sheni* funds to purchase anything except water and salt — and *hanoder min hamazon mutar b'mayim u'melach* — one who vows to abstain from food remains permitted to drink water and eat salt. The shared thread is that water and salt do not carry the legal status of *mazon*, food. This is paradoxical in a sense, since water and salt are among the most essential substances for sustaining life, yet the *halakha* declines to classify either as food proper.

### Techumin Definition

The *mishna*'s emphasis on *eruvei techumin*, in light of Tosafos's reading, calls for a brief orientation to that institution. One is ordinarily prohibited from traveling beyond 2,000 *amot* — approximately a kilometer — outside city limits on *Shabbat*. There is a dispute among the *rishonim* whether this restriction has a biblical basis. According to the Rambam, there is an underlying biblical prohibition, but it applies only to a far greater distance; the 2,000-*amot* limitation is in any case a rabbinic enactment. *Al yetzei ish mimkomo b'yom hashabbat* is cited by some as the biblical source, though its precise application is debated.

### Eruv Placement

The *eruv techumin* operates by depositing food — sufficient for two meals, and comprising any foodstuff except water or salt — at a designated location outside the city before *Shabbat* begins. That location then functions as the person's virtual place of residence for purposes of *Shabbat* travel, so that his 2,000-*amot* radius is measured from there rather than from the city. Critically, this does not merely extend one's range in the desired direction; it shifts one's entire starting point. One gains distance in one direction but loses it in the other, since the city itself may now lie at the far edge of one's 2,000-*amot* boundary. The technicalities of how the 2,000 *amot* are measured — how overlapping city boundaries interact, what constitutes a qualifying placement, and precisely how the shift operates — will occupy much of the subsequent discussion in this *perek*.

### Eruv Protection

One might reasonably ask: since the food must be placed outside the city, in what is by definition an unoccupied area, is it not vulnerable to loss or consumption by animals before *Shabbat*? This is indeed a concern addressed by *halakha*, and the answer is that the food must be deposited in a protected location. The details of what constitutes adequate protection will be treated more fully in the *gemara* ahead.

### Special Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעָרְבִין לַנָּזִיר בְּיַיִן וּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל בִּתְרוּמָה. סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר: בְּחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** It was further stated with regard to the laws of joining courtyards that **one may establish an *eiruv*** *teḥumin* **for a nazirite with wine,** even though he is prohibited to drink it, because it is permitted to others. **And** similarly, one may establish an *eiruv teḥumin* **for an Israelite with *teruma*,** even though he may not eat it, because it is permitted to a priest. The food used for an *eiruv teḥumin* must be fit for human consumption, but it is not essential that it be fit for the consumption of the one for whom it is being used. **Summakhos,** however, **says:** One may only establish an *eiruv teḥumin* for an Israelite **with unconsecrated** food.

The *mishna* continues with a set of cases that sharpen the definition of the food requirement. *Me'arvin le-Nazir be-yayin u'le-Yisrael bi-Trumah* — one may make an *eruv* for a *nazir* using wine, even though a *nazir* is prohibited from drinking it, and for a non-*kohen* using *trumah*, even though a non-*kohen* may not eat it, since in both cases the food is fit to be consumed by someone — a non-*nazir* may drink the wine, and a *kohen* may eat the *trumah*. Sumchus disagrees, holding that the food must be fit for the person on whose behalf the *eruv* is made; it must represent a potential meal for him specifically, making *chullin* the required standard.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַכֹּהֵן בְּבֵית הַפְּרָס. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ בֵּין הַקְּבָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** It was additionally stated that one may establish an *eiruv teḥumin* **for a priest in a *beit haperas*,** a field containing a grave that was plowed over. There is doubt as to the location of bone fragments in the entire area. A priest is prohibited to come into contact with a corpse, and therefore may not enter a *beit haperas*. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** An *eiruv teḥumin* may be established for a priest **even between the graves** in a graveyard, an area which the priest may not enter by Torah law,

The *mishna* further states that one may place an *eruv* for a *kohen* in a *beit ha-pras* — a field in which graves have been plowed, creating a rabbinic concern of bone-contamination — since the prohibition is only rabbinic and the food remains accessible. Rabbi Yehuda adds that one may even place it in a graveyard, since a *mechitzah* can be constructed enabling the *kohen* to enter without transgressing.

Tosafos raises a pointed question in this context. Given that the *mishna*'s dominant opinion permits food the person himself cannot eat — as in the case of wine for a *nazir* — why should the location of the food matter? Why not say: even if the *kohen* personally cannot access the food because it lies atop a grave, someone else could eat it, and the rule of accessibility should be no stricter than the rule of personal edibility? Tosafos concludes that there is in fact a meaningful distinction: while the food need not be personally edible by the individual, it must at minimum be personally accessible. The *stam* of the *mishna* permits food the person cannot eat, but it maintains that the food must be retrievable by the person making the *eruv*. This distinction will be developed further in the *gemara*.

## Ein Lomedim Principle

### Rabbi Yochanan's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין לְמֵידִין מִן הַכְּלָלוֹת, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ חוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One may not learn from general statements,** i.e., when a general statement is made in a mishna using the word all, it is not to be understood as an all-inclusive, general statement without exceptions. This is true **even in a place where it says** the word **except.** Even in that case, there may be other exceptions to the rule that are not listed.

At the top of 27a, Rabbi Yochanan states a foundational principle of *halakhic* methodology: *ein lemedin min haklalos va'afilu b'makom shene'emar bo chutz* — one cannot draw legal conclusions from general rules, even in cases where the rule explicitly enumerates its exceptions. When a *mishna* or *beraita* declares *zeh haklal* — this is the rule — one cannot assume that the rule is exhaustive and iron-clad, even if exceptions are spelled out. There may always remain additional, unenumerated exceptions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָאָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ חוּץ, מִכְּלָל דְּלָאו הָכָא קָאֵי, הֵיכָא קָאֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **From** the fact that Rabbi Yoḥanan **said: Even in a place where it says except,** this proves **by inference that he was not relating to** the general statement made **here** in the mishna, which uses the word except. **To which** mishna, then, **was he relating** when he formulated his principle?

The *gemara* notes the precision of Rabbi Yochanan's language. The fact that he said "even where it states *chutz*" implies that he originally formulated this principle in a context where no exceptions were listed at all — since that would be the more obvious case. This prompts the question: in what context did Rabbi Yochanan first say this?

### Mitzvot Asei Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם קָאֵי כׇּל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא, אֲנָשִׁים חַיָּיבִין וְנָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת. וְשֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא, אֶחָד נָשִׁים וְאֶחָד אֲנָשִׁים חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **He was relating** to a mishna found **there:** With regard to **all time-bound, positive commandments,** i.e., mitzvot that can only be performed at a certain time of the day, or during the day rather than at night, or on certain days of the year, **men are obligated** to perform them **and women are exempt.** But positive commandments **that are not time-bound, both women and men are obligated** to perform.

The *gemara* answers that Rabbi Yochanan's primary reference was the well-known rule: *kol mitzvot asei shehazman grama anashim chayavim venashim peturos* — all positive time-bound commandments obligate men but exempt women; and correspondingly, those that are not time-bound obligate both equally. This formulation contains no enumerated exceptions — it is a pure *zeh haklal* — and it is precisely here that Rabbi Yochanan's caveat applies most forcefully.

### Torah Obligation Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְלָלָא הוּא דְּכׇל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת? הֲרֵי מַצָּה שִׂמְחָה וְהַקְהֵל, דְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא הוּא, וְנָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Is it** a general **principle** that **women are exempt from all time-bound, positive commandments** without exception? But **there is** the commandment to eat ***matza*** on Passover, the commandment of **rejoicing** on a Festival, and the commandment of **assembly** in the Temple courtyard once every seven years during the festival of *Sukkot* following the Sabbatical Year, all of which **are time-bound, positive commandments, and** nevertheless, **women are obligated** to perform them.

The *gemara* challenges both halves of the rule with concrete counterexamples. Against the claim that women are exempt from all time-bound positive commandments: *harei matzah, simchah, ve-hakhel* — *matzah* is time-bound, yet women are obligated, derived from the juxtaposition of the prohibition of *chametz* with the obligation to eat *matzah* (*lo tochal alav chametz — shivat yamim tochal alav matzot*), which applies to anyone bound by the *chametz* prohibition. *Simchah* — the obligation of festive joy — derives from *vesamachta ata u'veitecha*, "you and your household," which includes women. *Hakhel* — the septennial assembly — explicitly enumerates *anashim, venashim, vehataf*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת? הֲרֵי תַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה, פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה, וּפִדְיוֹן הַבֵּן, דְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא וְנָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת. אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין לְמֵידִין מִן הַכְּלָלוֹת וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ חוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **are women obligated in all positive commandments that are not time-bound?** But **there is** the commandment of **Torah study,** the commandment to **be fruitful and multiply,** and the commandment of **redemption of the firstborn,** all of which are **positive commandments that are not time-bound, and** nevertheless, **women are exempt** from them. **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One may not learn from general statements, even in a place where it says except,** because it is always possible that there other exceptions to the rule.

Against the claim that women are obligated in all non-time-bound positive commandments: *harei Talmud Torah, peru urvu, u'Pidyon HaBen*. Women are exempt from *Talmud Torah*, derived from *velimaditem otam et beneichem* — "your sons," implying not your daughters. Women are exempt from *peru urvu* — procreation — derived from the concluding phrase of the commandment, *umilu et ha'aretz vechivshuha*, "and conquer," which implies the verse addresses men. And women are exempt from *Pidyon HaBen*, derived from a principle connecting the obligation to redeem with the theoretical capacity to have been redeemed — an obligation structured around a paternal relationship.

### Rule Paradox

This lengthy catalogue of exceptions invites genuine reflection on the explanatory power of the rule. Rabbi Saul Berman, in a well-known article on women and *mitzvot*, observed that when one counts the total number of positive commandments that are actually time-bound and those that are not, the exceptions on each side approach, and perhaps equal, the number of cases that actually conform to the rule. If the exceptions are approximately as numerous as the conforming cases, one must ask: in what sense is the rule truly explanatory? The rule *mitzvot asei shehazman grama* is often invoked as though it provides a principled reason for why women are exempt from particular *mitzvot* — but if the exceptions are equally numerous, the rule loses much of its descriptive and normative force. This does not render Rabbi Yochanan's principle incorrect; rather, it illustrates precisely his point that *klalos* are never to be trusted as exhaustive.

### Zavim Merkav Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, עוֹד כְּלָל אַחֵר אָמְרוּ: כׇּל שֶׁנִּישָּׂא עַל גַּבֵּי הַזָּב — טָמֵא, וְכֹל שֶׁהַזָּב נִישָּׂא עָלָיו — טָהוֹר, חוּץ מִן הָרָאוּי לְמִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב, וְהָאָדָם. וְתוּ לֵיכָּא? וְהָא אִיכָּא מֶרְכָּב?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said, and some say** it was **Rabbi Yirmeya** who said: **We, too, have also learned** a proof for Rabbi Yoḥanan's principle from a mishna: **They stated** yet **another** general **principle: Anything** that is **carried upon a *zav* is** ritually **impure. And anything on which a *zav* is carried is** ritually **pure, except for** an object **suitable for lying or sitting upon and a human being,** which become defiled if a *zav* is borne on them. The following objection may be raised: **And is there nothing else? But there is** an object upon which a person **rides** that becomes impure, as explained in the Torah itself.

Abaye — or, as the *gemara* alternatively attributes, Rabbi Yirmiya — cites a further example: *od klal echad amru* — another general rule was taught — in *Zavim*. *Kol she'nisa al gabei hazav tamei* — anything that a *zav* carries becomes *tamei*. *Vekol hazav nisa alav tahor* — anything that carries a *zav* is *tahor*.

Some background is necessary here. The ordinary rules of *tumah* involve *tumas massa* — contracting impurity by carrying a *tamei* object. In the case of a *zav*, an unusual dynamic obtains: the *zav* is himself the *tamei* entity, yet he is a person capable of the act of carrying. Accordingly, the Torah establishes a special category — *elyono shel zav*, "what is above the *zav*" — whereby objects the *zav* carries or wears themselves become *tamei*. Rashi notes this is derived from the verse *vekol asher yihyeh tachatav*, which is read creatively to mean "anything beneath which the *zav* is" — i.e., anything that the *zav* is carrying.

The converse rule — that what carries a *zav* is *tahor* — has important qualifications. A human being who carries a *zav* contracts *tumas massa* in the ordinary way. And if the object beneath the *zav* is designed for sitting or lying — a *mishkav* or *moshav* — it becomes an *av hatumah*, even without direct contact, since its *halakhic* identity is determined by its function as the *zav*'s designated seat or bed. But an object not made for sitting or lying that happens to support a *zav* does not become *tamei*. These are the stated exceptions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֶרְכָּב הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּיָתֵיב עֲלֵיהּ — הַיְינוּ מוֹשָׁב. אֲנַן הָכִי קָאָמְרִינַן: הָא אִיכָּא גַּבָּא דְאוּכָּפָא, דְּתַנְיָא: הָאוּכָּף — טָמֵא מוֹשָׁב, וְהַתְּפוּס — טָמֵא מֶרְכָּב. אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֵין לְמֵידִין מִן הַכְּלָלוֹת וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ חוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara first asks: **What are the circumstances** of an object upon which **one rides? If he sat upon it, it is a seat.** If not, how does it become defiled? What is there that is suitable for riding upon but does not fall into the category of something upon which one lies or sits? The Gemara answers: **We say as follows: There is the upper part of a saddle,** which becomes ritually impure as a riding accessory and not as a regular seat. **As it was taught** in the *Tosefta*: **A saddle** upon which a *zav* sat **is impure** as **a seat** of a *zav*, **and the pommel,** which is attached to the front of the saddle and used by the rider to maintain his position or to assist in mounting, **is impure** as **a riding** accessory. Therefore, we see that the general statement found in the mishna omits that which is suitable for riding upon. **Rather, conclude from this** that **one may not learn from general statements, even in a place where it says except.**

The *gemara* then asks: *v'tov leka* — are those the only exceptions? What about *merkav* — a saddle, something one rides on? The *gemara* responds that a saddle upon which the *zav* sits is itself in the category of *moshav* and would already be included. The real question concerns *gavva de'ukfa* — the pommel of the saddle, the raised front horn. The pommel supports the act of riding but is not the surface directly sat upon. The *gemara* establishes that the saddle itself (*ha'ukaf*) has the *tumah* of *moshav*, while the pommel (*hatapus*) has the *tumah* of *merkav* — a parallel but slightly lesser category. This distinction suggests that even the part of an object not directly sat upon may carry a form of *tumah* if it functionally enables the riding, which was not enumerated in the original general rule. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *shema mina ein lemedin min haklalos va'afilu b'makom shene'emar bo chutz*.

### Fungi Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב נַחְמָן: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, עוֹד כְּלָל אַחֵר אָמְרוּ: בַּכֹּל מְעָרְבִין וּמִשְׁתַּתְּפִין, חוּץ מִן הַמַּיִם וְהַמֶּלַח. וְתוּ לֵיכָּא? וְהָא אִיכָּא כְּמֵיהִין וּפִטְרִיּוֹת?! — אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אֵין לְמֵידִין מִן הַכְּלָלוֹת וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ חוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said, and some say** it was **Rav Naḥman** who said: **We, too, have also learned** a proof for Rabbi Yoḥanan's principle from the mishna, which states: **One may establish an *eiruv* and merge** alleyways **with all** kinds of food, **except for water and salt. And is there nothing else? But there are truffles and mushrooms,** which also may not be used for an *eiruv* because they are not regarded as food. **Rather, conclude from this** that **one may not learn from general statements, even in a place where it says except.**

Ravina — or, alternatively, Rav Nachman — then adduces the case closest to the present *daf*: *af anan nami tanina* — we too have taught this in our *mishna*. The *mishna* states one may use any food for *eruv* and *shituf*, *chutz min hamayim u'min hamelach* — yet there are additional exceptions. *Ve'ika kmeihim u'pitriot* — what about fungi and mushrooms? Those too cannot be used, yet they are not listed among the exceptions. Thus, *ein lomedim min haklalos va'afilu b'makom shene'emar bo chutz*.

Two distinct understandings emerge regarding why fungi are excluded, and they differ on which category the exception applies to. Tosafos reads the *gemara* as excluding fungi from *eruvei chatzerot*, *shitufei mevo'ot*, and *eruvei techumin*, on the grounds that fungi are not fit to be eaten in their raw state. This, Tosafos argues, reflects an important distinction: while *ma'aser* money may be used to purchase raw meat that is not yet fit for consumption, the *eruv* requirement demands that the food be presently edible, since the *eruv* functions to establish a legal residence and the food must plausibly represent an actual meal. Mushrooms in their raw state do not meet that standard, at least in Tosafos's view.

The *Gra*, however, reads the *gemara* differently. Noting a textual annotation (*nichtav batziddo ve-chulu* — "written alongside: etc.") indicating that the full text of the *mishna* is being referenced, the *Gra* argues that the exception applies specifically to *ma'aser sheni*, not to *eruv*. His comment on *ve'ika kmeihim u'pitriot* cites *Torat Kohanim*, *perek* 1, *piska* 8, in the context of the *ma'aser* rules. The reason fungi are excluded from *ma'aser* is that they are not *gidulei karka* — they do not genuinely grow from the ground in the manner required by the verse governing *ma'aser sheni* purchases. Thus, while Tosafos locates the exclusion in *eruv* and grounds it in a standard of present edibility, the *Gra* locates it in *ma'aser* and grounds it in the criterion of *gidulei karka*. The significance of this distinction will carry into the discussion of *ma'aser* that follows.

## Kesef Ma'aser

### Eruv vs. Ma'aser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל נִיקָּח בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר כּוּ׳. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא, חַד מַתְנֵי אַעֵירוּב וְחַד מַתְנֵי אַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **All** types of food **may be bought with** second-**tithe money,** except for water and salt. **Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina** both had the same tradition, but **one teaches** it **with regard to *eiruv*, and one teaches** it **with regard to** the second **tithe.**

The *gemara* now turns directly to the *halakhot* of *kesef ma'aser* — what may be purchased with the redeemed funds of *ma'aser sheni*. A dispute is recorded between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina. *Chad masni a'eruv* — one transmits the following clarification as pertaining to *eruv* — *vichad masni a'ma'aser* — and the other transmits it as pertaining to *ma'aser*.

### Salt Water Dip

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַד מַתְנֵי אַעֵירוּב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מַיִם בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ וּמֶלַח בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ דְּאֵין מְעָרְבִין. אֲבָל בְּמַיִם וּמֶלַח — מְעָרְבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **One teaches** this *halakha* **with regard to** the issue of ***eiruv*,** as follows: **They only taught** that **one may not establish an *eiruv*** with water or salt in the case of **water by itself or salt by itself. But** with **water and salt** together, **one may** indeed **establish an *eiruv*.**

The clarification in question is: *lo shanu ela mayim bifnei atzman u'melach bifnei atzman* — the prohibition against using water or salt applies only when each is used alone. *Aval bemayim u'melach me'arvin yachad* — but salt water, water and salt combined, one may use. The combination, functioning as a dipping condiment, acquires the status of food in a way that neither ingredient does on its own.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַד מַתְנֵי אַמַּעֲשֵׂר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מַיִם בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ וּמֶלַח בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, דְּאֵין נִיקָּחִין. אֲבָל מַיִם וּמֶלַח — נִיקָּחִין בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the other **one teaches** this *halakha* **with regard to** the issue of the second **tithe: They only taught** that water or salt **may not be bought** with second-tithe money in the case of **water by itself or salt by itself. But water and salt** mixed together **may** indeed **be bought with** second-**tithe** money.

### Scope Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּמַתְנֵי אַמַּעֲשֵׂר, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אַעֵירוּב. וּמַאן דְּמַתְנֵי אַעֵירוּב, אֲבָל אַמַּעֲשֵׂר לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא — פֵּירָא בָּעִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The one who teaches** this law **with regard to** the second **tithe, all the more so** would he apply it **to an *eiruv*,** i.e., he would certainly maintain that water and salt together are suitable to be used for an *eiruv*. However, according to **the one who teaches** this law **with regard to an *eiruv*,** it applies only to an *eiruv*; **but with regard to** the second tithe, **no,** it does not apply. **What is the reason** for this distinction? For the second tithe, **we require produce,** as stated in the Torah, and even when water and salt are mixed together they do not have the status of produce.

The one who applies this ruling to *ma'aser* implies an even broader permission for *eruv*: *kol shekein a'eruv* — if combined salt water qualifies even for the stricter category of *ma'aser*, it certainly qualifies for *eruv*. The one who applies it to *eruv* does not necessarily extend it to *ma'aser*, since *ma'aser* may have more restrictive criteria. What are those criteria? *Pirei ba'inan* — by *ma'aser*, we require something in the conceptual category of *peri*, a fruit or product, meaning something generated by another living thing, whether animal or plant. Water and salt, as minerals and liquid, do not meet this standard, even when combined. Thus, the *ma'aser* category may be narrower: not merely any food, but specifically food that qualifies as a *peri* in the abstract sense.

Tosafos introduces a nuance that runs in the opposite direction: there is one area in which *eruv* is actually more demanding than *ma'aser*. As noted above, the food for an *eruv* must be presently edible — fit to be eaten in its current state — whereas *ma'aser* money may be used to purchase raw meat and other foods not yet ready to eat. This is the foundation of Tosafos's understanding of why fungi are excluded from *eruv* but not from *ma'aser*. Setting this aside, the general pattern holds: *ma'aser* is the narrower category, defined by the requirement of *peri*, and possibly by *gidulei karka* as well.

### Fish Brine Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַבִּי יִצְחָק, מַתְנֵי אַמַּעֲשֵׂר. מֵיתִיבִי: הֵעִיד רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן גָּדִישׁ לִפְנֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: שֶׁל בֵּית אַבָּא הָיוּ לוֹקְחִין צִיר בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר. אָמַר לוֹ: שֶׁמָּא לֹא שָׁמַעְתָּ אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁקִּרְבֵי דָגִים מְעוֹרָבִים בָּהֶן. וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן גָּדִישׁ לָא קָאָמַר אֶלָּא בְּצִיר, דְּשׁוּמְנָא דְפֵירָא הִיא, אֲבָל מַיִם וּמֶלַח — לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rabbi Yitzḥak came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he taught** this law **with regard to** the second **tithe.** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the following *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish testified before Rabbi Eliezer: In Father's house they would buy** fish **brine with** second-**tithe money. He said to him: Perhaps you only heard** this in a case **where the fish's innards were mixed with** the brine. Since this mixture contains a small portion of the fish, the brine becomes significant enough to be purchased with second-tithe money. **And even Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish only said** his statement **with regard to brine, which is the fat of produce,** i.e., because a certain amount of fish fat, which itself may be purchased with second-tithe money, is mixed in with the brine; **but** a mixture of **water and salt** alone may **not** be bought with it.

Rav Yitzchak, upon returning from *Eretz Yisrael*, transmitted this ruling as applying to *ma'aser*. The *gemara* challenges him with a *beraita*: Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish testified before Rabbi Eliezer that in his father's household, *tzir* — fish brine — was purchased with *ma'aser* money. The *gemara* responds to the challenge:

### Fish in Brine

The *gemara* responds that a saddle upon which the *zav* sits is itself in the category of *moshav* and would already be included. The real question concerns *gavva de'ukfa* — the pommel of the saddle, the raised front horn. The pommel supports the act of riding but is not the surface directly sat upon. The *gemara* establishes that the saddle itself (*ha'ukaf*) has the *tumah* of *moshav*, while the pommel (*hatapus*) has the *tumah* of *merkav* — a parallel but slightly lesser category. This distinction suggests that even the part of an object not directly sat upon may carry a form of *tumah* if it functionally enables the riding, which was not enumerated in the original general rule. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *shema mina ein lemedin min haklalos va'afilu b'makom shene'emar bo chutz*.

### Oil Addition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא שֶׁנָּתַן לְתוֹכָן שֶׁמֶן.
>
> **Translation:** In response to this difficulty, **Rav Yosef said:** Rabbi Yitzḥak's ruling that water and salt mixed together may be bought with second-tithe money **was only necessary** in a case **where one added oil to them.** But a mixture of water and salt alone may not be purchased with second-tithe money.

Rav Yosef offers a resolution at the top of 27b: *lo nitzrecha ela shenasan lesochan shemen* — the permission to use salt water for *ma'aser* applies specifically when oil has been added to the mixture. In that case, it is the oil — a legitimate *ma'aser* product — that grounds the permission, and the salt water is permitted along with it.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם שֶׁמֶן! לֹא צְרִיכָא, שֶׁנָּתַן דְּמֵי מַיִם וּמֶלַח בְּהַבְלָעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: If so, **let him derive** that the mixture may be bought with second-tithe money **because of the oil** alone. The Gemara refutes this argument: **No, it was necessary** for a case in which **one paid the value of the water and salt by including** it in the payment for the oil. Although ostensibly the money that he paid was for the oil, he added to the price of the oil in order to include payment for the water and salt that were mixed with it.

Abaye immediately objects: *vetifok lei mishum shemen* — if the entire basis of the permission is the oil, why mention salt water at all? Simply say one may spend *ma'aser* money on oil. What is the *chiddush* of including the salt water in the discussion?

### Havla'ah Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְהַבְלָעָה מִי שְׁרֵי? אִין — וְהָתַנְיָא, בֶּן בַּג בַּג אוֹמֵר: ״בַּבָּקָר״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלּוֹקְחִין בָּקָר עַל גַּב עוֹרוֹ. ״וּבַצֹּאן״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלּוֹקְחִין צֹאן עַל גַּב גִּיזָּתָהּ. ״וּבַיַּיִן״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלּוֹקְחִין יַיִן עַל גַּב קַנְקַנּוֹ. ״וּבַשֵּׁכָר״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלּוֹקְחִין תֶּמֶד מִשֶּׁהֶחְמִיץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if something may not be bought with second-tithe money, **is one permitted** to buy it **by including** it in the payment for something which may be bought with second-tithe money? The Gemara answers: **Yes, and so it was taught** in the following *baraita*: **Ben Bag Bag says** in exposition of the verse: "And you shall bestow that money on all that your heart desires, on oxen, on sheep, on wine, on strong drink, on whatever your soul requests" (Deuteronomy 14:26): **"On oxen" teaches that one may buy an ox** and include in its price payment for **its hide.** Although the hide cannot be eaten, it may be bought together with the ox, and it does not acquire the sanctity of the second tithe. **"On sheep" teaches that one may buy a sheep** and include in its price payment for **its fleece,** which is used for purposes other than eating. **"On wine" teaches that one may buy wine** and include in its price payment for **its jug. "On strong drink" teaches that** one is permitted to buy not only actual wine, but **one may buy** even **mead,** water in which grape seeds are soaked, **once it has fermented** and acquired the flavor of wine.

The *gemara* answers: *tzricha* — it is indeed necessary, because the case teaches the principle of *havla'ah*: *shenasan dmei mayim u'melach b'havla'ah* — that one may pay for salt water using *ma'aser* money when it is absorbed (*bilvua*) into the larger purchase. If one spends ten coins of *ma'aser sheni* funds on a composite dip consisting of oil, water, and salt, part of the purchase price is factually going toward the salt water. The principle of *havla'ah* holds that when a non-qualifying ingredient is acquired as part of a unified purchase whose primary component is legitimate, the portion of the price attributable to the non-qualifying element is absorbed and permitted along with the rest.

The *gemara* confirms this from a *beraita*: Ben Bag Bag says that the verse *vekol asher tishalkha nafshecha*, in its context alongside *bakar, tzon, yayin, u'shechar*, teaches through each word that *havla'ah* applies to various composite units. When one buys cattle, some of the cost goes toward the hide, which is not food; when one buys sheep, some goes toward the wool; when one buys wine, some goes toward the bottle; and when one buys *temed* — a fermented mead made by soaking grape lees in water — the primary ingredient is water, with only a trace contribution from the grape product. In each case, *ma'aser* money may be used for the entire purchase. This is the *halakhic* principle of *havla'ah* — secondary and otherwise non-qualifying components are absorbed into, and covered by, the primary legitimate purchase.

## Ben Bag Bag Exegesis

### Cattle Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאן דִּמְתַרְגֵּם לִי ״בַּבָּקָר״ אַלִּיבָּא דְּבֶן בַּג בַּג, מוֹבֵילְנָא מָאנֵיהּ אַבָּתְרֵיהּ לְבֵי מַסּוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Having cited ben Bag Bag's exposition of the verse, the Gemara continues: **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Whoever interprets** the words **"on oxen" for me in accordance with** the opinion of **ben Bag Bag, I will carry his clothes after him into the bathhouse,** i.e., I will honor him to such an extent that I will be prepared to treat him as a servant treats his master.

Ben Bag Bag derives *havla'ah* from the verse *babakar* — cattle. *Melamed shelokchin bakar agav oro* — one may purchase cattle with *ma'aser* money even though some of the cost goes toward the hide, which is not food. The justification is *mishum degufei hu* — the hide is literally part of the same body. It is physically inseparable from the animal and constitutes a genuine unity with it.

### Bakar Necessity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? כּוּלְּהוּ צְרִיכִי לְבַר מִ״בַּבָּקָר״, דְּלָא צְרִיךְ. מַאי צְרִיכִי? דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בַּבָּקָר״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בָּקָר הוּא דְּמִזְדַּבַּן עַל גַּב עוֹרוֹ, מִשּׁוּם דְּגוּפֵיהּ הוּא. אֲבָל צֹאן עַל גַּב גִּיזָּתָהּ, דְּלָאו גּוּפֵיהּ הוּא — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for Rabbi Yoḥanan's difficulty? **All** parts of the verse cited above **are necessary, except for** the expression **"on oxen," which is not necessary.** The Gemara clarifies: For **what** purpose are all the other words **necessary? As, if the Torah had written** only **"on oxen," I might have said** that **it is** only an **ox that may be bought** together **with its hide** with second-tithe money **because** the hide **is** an inseparable part of **its body,** and therefore it is not considered an independent entity from its flesh. **But** as for buying a **sheep** together **with its fleece, which is not** an inseparable part of **its body** because the fleece can be removed from the sheep while it is alive, you might **say no,** second-tithe money may not be spent in this manner. It was therefore necessary to state "on sheep."

Rabbi Yochanan famously declares: *man de'mitargem li b'vakar aliba d'Ben Bag Bag* — whoever can explain to me why Ben Bag Bag requires the word *bakar* in the verse, *movilna manei avatrei l'vei masuta* — I will carry his clothes after him to the bathhouse. In other words, why is *bakar* necessary at all? Given that *tzon* — sheep — already teaches *havla'ah* in a harder case (since wool, unlike skin, can be detached), does not *bakar* follow all the more readily? The *gemara* develops this challenge carefully.

### Sheep Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בַּצֹּאן״ עַל גַּב גִּיזָּתָהּ, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מִשּׁוּם דִּמְחוּבָּר בָּהּ. אֲבָל יַיִן עַל גַּב קַנְקַנּוֹ — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the Torah had** also **written "on sheep,"** teaching that one may buy even a sheep with second-tithe money together **with its fleece, I might have said** that it is only a sheep that may be bought together with its fleece **because** the fleece **is attached to it,** and therefore it is considered part of the animal. **But** as for buying **wine** together **with its jug,** you might **say no,** second-tithe money may not be spent this way. It was therefore necessary to state "on wine."

*Tzon* could not have been inferred from *yayin* alone. Wine comes with a bottle — *agav kankanno* — which is clearly a separate object from the contents it contains. One might have thought *havla'ah* applies only because the bottle is the container without which the wine cannot exist or be transported — *hainu netiruteih*, that is its means of preservation. But extending this to wool, which is not necessary for the sheep's continued existence, would not follow. Hence *tzon* must be stated separately.

### Wine Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בַּיַּיִן״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּהַיְינוּ נְטִירוּתֵיהּ. אֲבָל תֶּמֶד מִשֶּׁהֶחְמִיץ דְּקִיּוּהָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, אֵימָא לָא. כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״שֵׁכָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the Torah had** also **written "on wine,"** indicating that one may even buy wine together with its jug with second-tithe money, **I might** still **have said** that this is **because** the jug **is** needed for the wine's **preservation,** as there is no way to carry wine without some sort of container. **But** as for **mead that has fermented, which is mere acidity,** you might **say no,** it should not be included among the items that may be bought with second-tithe money. Therefore, **the Torah wrote:** "On **strong drink."**

*Shechar* — mead or the fermented *temed* — teaches the most extreme application of *havla'ah*. *Temed mishehichmitz*: wine lees were soaked in water until the mixture became slightly sharp, and then the water was poured off and drunk. In this preparation, the actual grape contribution is minimal — *dikpaya d'alma hu* — it is essentially just the sharpness, a faint trace of fruit character in what is fundamentally water. One might have thought *ma'aser* money could not be spent on something so predominantly water-based. Therefore *shechar* must be stated: even this qualifies.

### Each Word Distinct

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא שַׁחַר עַל גַּב קַנְקַנּוֹ — דְּהַיְינוּ נְטִירוּתֵיהּ. אֲבָל צֹאן עַל גַּב גִּיזָּתָהּ — אֵימָא לָא. כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״צֹאן״, דַּאֲפִילּוּ עַל גַּב גִּיזָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the Torah had** also **written** "on wine," indicating that one may buy even **wine** together **with its jug** with second-tithe money, I might have said that this is because the jug **is** needed for the wine's **preservation. But** as for buying a **sheep** together **with its fleece,** you might **say no,** this may not be done with second-tithe money. Therefore, **the Torah stated** "on **sheep,"** to teach **that** a sheep may be bought with second-tithe money **even** together **with its fleece.**

The *gemara* works through the logic in both directions, demonstrating that no word in the list could have been omitted. Had only *shechar* been written, one might have understood it to mean *dveilah ke'ilit* — a particular type of intoxicating date-product, which is genuine fruit — and derived nothing about wine with its bottle or sheep with its wool. Had only *yayin* been written, one would know the bottle is included because of *netirutei*, but not have extended *havla'ah* to wool. Had only *tzon* been written, one might have read *agav gizatah* as referring to a sheep already shorn — meaning only the skin qualifies — and not to the wool as such. Therefore *bakar* is needed: from *bakar* we derive the skin, from *tzon* we derive the wool, from *yayin* the bottle, and from *shechar* the case of a predominantly non-food component. Each word performs irreplaceable work.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בַּבָּקָר״ לְמָה לִי?! וְכִי תֵּימָא: אִי לָא כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בַּבָּקָר״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: צֹאן עַל גַּב עוֹרָהּ אִין, עַל גַּב גִּיזָּתָהּ לָא. כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בַּבָּקָר״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי עוֹרוֹ. אִיַּיתַּר לֵיהּ ״צֹאן״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי גִּיזָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **why do I** need the words **"on oxen"?** If one may purchase a sheep together with its fleece with second-tithe money, it should certainly be permitted to buy an ox together with its hide. **And if you say** that **if the Torah had not written "on oxen," I might have said** that **a sheep** together **with its hide, yes,** it may be bought with second-tithe money, but together **with its fleece, no,** it may not be bought; therefore, **the Torah wrote "on oxen," to include its hide,** and so "on **sheep" remains** available for interpretation **to include its fleece,** i.e., that a sheep may be bought even together with its fleece. Therefore, the words "on oxen" are necessary, for without them I would have understood "on sheep" differently.

The ultimate resolution to Rabbi Yochanan's challenge, then, is as follows: *aval tzon agav gizoteiha mishteban, bakar agav ora miba'ei?* — if we know that sheep may be purchased together with their wool, is there any question that cattle may be purchased together with their skin? The answer is that without the word *bakar*, we would have misread *tzon* as referring only to a shorn sheep, leaving the skin of cattle unaddressed. *Tzon* is needed to teach wool; *bakar* is needed to establish that the skin-inclusion principle exists so that *tzon* can be read to include the wool. Each word is thus indispensable, and the system is fully coherent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לָא כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בָּקָר״, לָא הֲוָה אָמֵינָא צֹאן עַל גַּב עוֹרָהּ — אִין, עַל גַּב גִּיזָּתָהּ — לָא. דְּאִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״בָּקָר״, דְּמִמֵּילָא אִיַּיתַּר לֵיהּ ״צֹאן״.
>
> **Translation:** This argument can, however, be refuted: Even **if the Torah had not written "on oxen," I would not have said** that a **sheep** together **with its hide, yes,** it may be bought with second-tithe money, but together **with its fleece, no,** it may not. For **if** it were **so, the Torah should have written** "on **oxen," in which case** the words "on **sheep" would remain** available for interpretation. "On sheep" can be explained in two ways, both in reference to its hide as well as in reference to its fleece. Had the Torah wanted to teach only that an animal may be bought together with its hide but not with its fleece, it would have written "on oxen" only, which would have left no room for error, as oxen do not have fleece.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״צֹאן״, דַּאֲפִילּוּ עַל גַּב גִּיזָּתָהּ. ״בַּבָּקָר״ לְמָה לִי? הַשְׁתָּא צֹאן עַל גַּב גִּיזָּתָהּ מִיזְדַּבְּנָא, בָּקָר עַל גַּב עוֹרוֹ מִיבַּעְיָא? הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאן דִּמְתַרְגֵּם לִי ״בַּבָּקָר״ אַלִּיבָּא דְּבֶן בַּג בַּג, מוֹבֵילְנָא מָאנֵיהּ לְבֵי מַסּוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And since the Torah writes** "on **sheep,"** teaching that a sheep may be bought with second-tithe money **even** together **with its fleece, why do I need** the words **"on oxen"?** These words are **now** entirely superfluous. If a **sheep may be bought** together **with its fleece, is it necessary** to state that an **ox** may be bought together **with its hide? This is what Rabbi Yoḥanan** meant when he **said: Whoever interprets** the words **"on oxen" for me in accordance with** the opinion of **ben Bag Bag, I will carry his clothes after him into the bathhouse.**

The *chiddush* of the entire passage for the *halakha* of *ma'aser sheni* is therefore: *havla'ah* is a genuine principle. The composite salt-water-and-oil dip may be purchased with *ma'aser sheni* funds, even though part of the price is going toward water and salt that could not be purchased alone, because those elements are absorbed into the primary legitimate purchase. The same principle underlies every composite acquisition — the animal with its hide, the sheep with its wool, the wine with its bottle, the mead with its water base.

## Hermeneutical Approaches

### Ribbuy U'Miu'ut Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן גָּדִישׁ וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי דִּלְקַמַּן? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן גָּדִישׁ וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דָּרְשִׁי רִבּוּיֵי וּמִיעוּטֵי, וְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי דָּרְשִׁי כְּלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now returns to the tannaitic disagreement pertaining to buying fish brine with second-tithe money: **With regard to what** principle **do Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish, and Rabbi Eliezer, and these *tanna'im*** whose views will be cited **below, disagree?** The Gemara explains: **Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish and Rabbi Eliezer expound** the verse based on the principle of **amplifications and restrictions, and these *tanna'im* expound** it based on the principle of **generalizations and details,** which is a different approach to biblical exegesis.

The *gemara* now turns to a foundational question: on what hermeneutical basis do the *tannaim* disagree about what may be purchased with *ma'aser sheni* funds? *B'mai kamiflagi Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish v'Rabbi Eliezer v'hani tannai d'lekaman?* — what is the methodological dispute between Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish and Rabbi Eliezer on one side, and the other *tannaim* on the other?

The answer is that they employ different hermeneutical schools. Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish and Rabbi Eliezer interpret the verse using *ribbuy u'mi'ut* — the method of inclusive and exclusive language. The relevant verse is: *V'nasatah hakesef b'chol asher te'aveh nafshecha* — *riba*, inclusive; *babakar uvatzon uvayayin uvashechar* — *mi'et*, limiting terms; *uvechol asher tishalkha nafshecha* — *chazar v'riba*, returning to inclusion. This produces a structure of *ribbuy, mi'ut, ribbuy*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן גָּדִישׁ וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דָּרְשִׁי רִיבּוּיֵי וּמִיעוּטֵי. ״וְנָתַתָּה הַכֶּסֶף בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר תְּאַוֶּה נַפְשְׁךָ״ — רִיבָּה, ״בַּבָּקָר וּבַצֹּאן וּבַיַּיִן וּבַשֵּׁכָר״ — מִיעֵט, ״וּבְכֹל אֲשֶׁר תִּשְׁאׇלְךָ נַפְשֶׁךָ״ — חָזַר וְרִיבָּה. רִיבָּה וּמִיעֵט וְרִיבָּה — רִיבָּה הַכֹּל. מַאי רַבִּי? רַבִּי כׇּל מִילֵּי. וּמַאי מִיעֵט? לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מִיעֵט צִיר, לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן גָּדִישׁ מִיעֵט מַיִם וּמֶלַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish and Rabbi Eliezer expound** the verse based on the principle of **amplifications and restrictions.** When the verse states: **"And you shall bestow the money on all that your heart desires"** (Deuteronomy 14:26), **it has amplified.** When it then states: **"On oxen, on sheep, on wine, and on strong drink," it has restricted** its discussion to certain specific items. When it concludes with the phrase: **"On whatever your soul requests," it has** once **again amplified.** According to this exegetical approach, we conclude that since **it amplified and restricted and** once again **amplified, it has amplified** the general category to include **everything. What has it amplified** the category to include? **It has amplified** the category to include **everything. And what has it restricted** from inclusion in the category? Only one thing: **According to Rabbi Eliezer, it restricted brine** from inclusion because brine is not at all similar to the items listed in the verse; **according to Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish, it restricted water and salt.**

Under the *ribbuy u'mi'ut* methodology, the structure *ribbuy–mi'ut–ribbuy* yields the conclusion *riba hakol* — virtually everything is included. The limiting middle term (*mi'ut*) does not narrow the category to items similar to the list; rather, it serves only to exclude that which is most dissimilar from all the items mentioned. *U'mai mi'et?* — what does the limiting term exclude? For Rabbi Eliezer, it excludes *tzir* — fish brine alone; for Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish, it excludes *mayim u'melach* — plain water and salt. Fish is certainly included by both, since fish, as a living creature producing offspring, qualifies as a *peri* in the abstract sense, and the brine is arguably a product of the fish as well.

### Klal U'Prat Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי דָּרְשִׁי כְּלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְנָתַתָּה הַכֶּסֶף בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר תְּאַוֶּה נַפְשְׁךָ״ — כָּלַל, ״בַּבָּקָר וּבַצֹּאן וּבַיַּיִן וּבַשֵּׁכָר״ — פָּרַט, ״וּבְכֹל אֲשֶׁר תִּשְׁאׇלְךָ נַפְשֶׁךָ״ — חָזַר וְכָלַל. כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט. וּמָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ: פְּרִי מִפְּרִי, וְגִידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע, אַף כֹּל פְּרִי מִפְּרִי וְגִידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** **And these** other ***tanna'im* expound** the verse according to the principle of **generalizations and details, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: The phrase: **"And you shall bestow the money on all that your heart desires"** is **a generalization,** as no particular type of food is specified. The phrase: **"On cattle, on sheep, on wine, and on strong drink"** is **a detail,** as specific types of food are mentioned. When the verse concludes: **"On whatever your soul requests," it has generalized again,** as no specific type of food is mentioned. Since the verse is formulated as **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization, you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail. Just as** the items mentioned in **the detail** are clearly **defined as the produce of produce,** i.e., not only the produce itself but also things that come from it, such as the calf that comes from a cow or grapes from a seed, **and** they are also things **grown from the ground,** as all of these items grow from the ground or receive their main sustenance from it, **so** it includes **all** things that are **the produce of produce and are grown from the ground.**

*V'hani tannai darshi klal u'prat* — the other *tannaim* employ the school of *klal u'prat*. The identical verse is now read as: *bechol asher te'aveh nafshecha* — *klal*, a general term; *babakar uvatzon uvayayin uvashechar* — *prat*, specific terms; *uvechol asher tishalkha nafshecha* — *chazar v'klal*, a return to the general. This three-part structure — *klal, prat u'klal* — yields the conclusion *ein ata dan ela k'ein haprat* — you may only include items similar to the specific terms.

What makes the specific items in the *prat* distinctive? Two criteria are identified: *pri mi'pri* — they are a product produced from another product, in the sense of a fruit or living-generated item — and *gidulei karka* — they grow from the ground. *Af kol pri mi'pri v'gidulei karka* — anything that satisfies both criteria falls within the scope of the verse.

### Fish Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ: וְלַד וַלְדוֹת הָאָרֶץ, אַף כֹּל וְלַד וַלְדוֹת הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was taught in another** *baraita*: **Just as** the items mentioned in **the detail** are clearly **defined as the offspring of the offspring of the earth,** i.e., things that come from items that came from the ground, **so** it includes **all** things that are **the offspring of the offspring of the earth.**

The consequence of the *klal u'prat* method is that fish are excluded. Fish do not grow from the ground — they live in water and reproduce in water. However clearly they satisfy *pri mi'pri* (since fish are born from other fish), they fail the criterion of *gidulei karka*, and under *klal u'prat u'klal* both criteria must be met. Fish brine, accordingly, also falls outside the permitted category.
