# Eruvin 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eruv Types & Mishna

### Mishna Review

The *daf* opens with the beginning of a new *perek*, and the *mishna* at the bottom of 26b sets the stage for the entire chapter. *Bechol me'arvin u'mishtatfin* — one may make an *eruv* and a *shituf* with any food. The *shituf* refers to the joining of the different *chatzerot* — the various courtyards or streets onto which houses open — to form a unified domain for carrying purposes within the *mavoi*, the alleyway. This is the higher-level structure. The more basic building block is *eruv chatzerot* itself, by which the houses opening into a common courtyard are unified so that one may carry freely between them. Both mechanisms require participants to contribute a quantity of food that symbolizes shared ownership and residence.

### Tosafos Reading

Rashi reads *bechol me'arvin* as encompassing *eruvei chatzerot* as well as *eruvei techumin*. Tosafos, however, argues that the *gemara* subsequently makes clear that *eruv chatzerot* may only be performed with bread — *b'pat* exclusively. Therefore, Tosafos understands *me'arvin* in this *mishna* as referring specifically to *eruvei techumin*, while *mishtatfin* covers *shitufei mevo'ot*. The practical upshot is striking: *eruv chatzerot*, which is the counterpart to *shitufei mevo'ot*, is limited to bread, whereas the broader category of food that may be used for any purpose applies to *shitufei mevo'ot* and *eruvei techumin*. One should be attentive to this distinction throughout the chapter.

### Water & Salt

Both uses of food enumerated in the *mishna* — *eruv* and *shituf* — exclude water and salt: *chutz min hamayim u'min hamelach*. The same exclusion recurs in two further *halakhot* stated in the *mishna*: *v'hakol nikach b'kesef ma'aser chutz min hamayim u'min hamelach* — one may redeem *ma'aser sheni* funds to purchase anything except water and salt — and *hanoder min hamazon mutar b'mayim u'melach* — one who vows to abstain from food remains permitted to drink water and eat salt. The shared thread is that water and salt do not carry the legal status of *mazon*, food. This is paradoxical in a sense, since water and salt are among the most essential substances for sustaining life, yet the *halakha* declines to classify either as food proper.

### Techumin Definition

The *mishna*'s emphasis on *eruvei techumin*, in light of Tosafos's reading, calls for a brief orientation to that institution. One is ordinarily prohibited from traveling beyond 2,000 *amot* — approximately a kilometer — outside city limits on *Shabbat*. There is a dispute among the *rishonim* whether this restriction has a biblical basis. According to the Rambam, there is an underlying biblical prohibition, but it applies only to a far greater distance; the 2,000-*amot* limitation is in any case a rabbinic enactment. *Al yetzei ish mimkomo b'yom hashabbat* is cited by some as the biblical source, though its precise application is debated.

### Eruv Placement

The *eruv techumin* operates by depositing food — sufficient for two meals, and comprising any foodstuff except water or salt — at a designated location outside the city before *Shabbat* begins. That location then functions as the person's virtual place of residence for purposes of *Shabbat* travel, so that his 2,000-*amot* radius is measured from there rather than from the city. Critically, this does not merely extend one's range in the desired direction; it shifts one's entire starting point. One gains distance in one direction but loses it in the other, since the city itself may now lie at the far edge of one's 2,000-*amot* boundary. The technicalities of how the 2,000 *amot* are measured — how overlapping city boundaries interact, what constitutes a qualifying placement, and precisely how the shift operates — will occupy much of the subsequent discussion in this *perek*.

### Eruv Protection

One might reasonably ask: since the food must be placed outside the city, in what is by definition an unoccupied area, is it not vulnerable to loss or consumption by animals before *Shabbat*? This is indeed a concern addressed by *halakha*, and the answer is that the food must be deposited in a protected location. The details of what constitutes adequate protection will be treated more fully in the *gemara* ahead.

### Special Cases

The *mishna* continues with a set of cases that sharpen the definition of the food requirement. *Me'arvin le-Nazir be-yayin u'le-Yisrael bi-Trumah* — one may make an *eruv* for a *nazir* using wine, even though a *nazir* is prohibited from drinking it, and for a non-*kohen* using *trumah*, even though a non-*kohen* may not eat it, since in both cases the food is fit to be consumed by someone — a non-*nazir* may drink the wine, and a *kohen* may eat the *trumah*. Sumchus disagrees, holding that the food must be fit for the person on whose behalf the *eruv* is made; it must represent a potential meal for him specifically, making *chullin* the required standard.

The *mishna* further states that one may place an *eruv* for a *kohen* in a *beit ha-pras* — a field in which graves have been plowed, creating a rabbinic concern of bone-contamination — since the prohibition is only rabbinic and the food remains accessible. Rabbi Yehuda adds that one may even place it in a graveyard, since a *mechitzah* can be constructed enabling the *kohen* to enter without transgressing.

Tosafos raises a pointed question in this context. Given that the *mishna*'s dominant opinion permits food the person himself cannot eat — as in the case of wine for a *nazir* — why should the location of the food matter? Why not say: even if the *kohen* personally cannot access the food because it lies atop a grave, someone else could eat it, and the rule of accessibility should be no stricter than the rule of personal edibility? Tosafos concludes that there is in fact a meaningful distinction: while the food need not be personally edible by the individual, it must at minimum be personally accessible. The *stam* of the *mishna* permits food the person cannot eat, but it maintains that the food must be retrievable by the person making the *eruv*. This distinction will be developed further in the *gemara*.

## Ein Lomedim Principle

### Rabbi Yochanan's Rule

At the top of 27a, Rabbi Yochanan states a foundational principle of *halakhic* methodology: *ein lemedin min haklalos va'afilu b'makom shene'emar bo chutz* — one cannot draw legal conclusions from general rules, even in cases where the rule explicitly enumerates its exceptions. When a *mishna* or *beraita* declares *zeh haklal* — this is the rule — one cannot assume that the rule is exhaustive and iron-clad, even if exceptions are spelled out. There may always remain additional, unenumerated exceptions.

The *gemara* notes the precision of Rabbi Yochanan's language. The fact that he said "even where it states *chutz*" implies that he originally formulated this principle in a context where no exceptions were listed at all — since that would be the more obvious case. This prompts the question: in what context did Rabbi Yochanan first say this?

### Mitzvot Asei Exceptions

The *gemara* answers that Rabbi Yochanan's primary reference was the well-known rule: *kol mitzvot asei shehazman grama anashim chayavim venashim peturos* — all positive time-bound commandments obligate men but exempt women; and correspondingly, those that are not time-bound obligate both equally. This formulation contains no enumerated exceptions — it is a pure *zeh haklal* — and it is precisely here that Rabbi Yochanan's caveat applies most forcefully.

### Torah Obligation Examples

The *gemara* challenges both halves of the rule with concrete counterexamples. Against the claim that women are exempt from all time-bound positive commandments: *harei matzah, simchah, ve-hakhel* — *matzah* is time-bound, yet women are obligated, derived from the juxtaposition of the prohibition of *chametz* with the obligation to eat *matzah* (*lo tochal alav chametz — shivat yamim tochal alav matzot*), which applies to anyone bound by the *chametz* prohibition. *Simchah* — the obligation of festive joy — derives from *vesamachta ata u'veitecha*, "you and your household," which includes women. *Hakhel* — the septennial assembly — explicitly enumerates *anashim, venashim, vehataf*.

Against the claim that women are obligated in all non-time-bound positive commandments: *harei Talmud Torah, peru urvu, u'Pidyon HaBen*. Women are exempt from *Talmud Torah*, derived from *velimaditem otam et beneichem* — "your sons," implying not your daughters. Women are exempt from *peru urvu* — procreation — derived from the concluding phrase of the commandment, *umilu et ha'aretz vechivshuha*, "and conquer," which implies the verse addresses men. And women are exempt from *Pidyon HaBen*, derived from a principle connecting the obligation to redeem with the theoretical capacity to have been redeemed — an obligation structured around a paternal relationship.

### Rule Paradox

This lengthy catalogue of exceptions invites genuine reflection on the explanatory power of the rule. Rabbi Saul Berman, in a well-known article on women and *mitzvot*, observed that when one counts the total number of positive commandments that are actually time-bound and those that are not, the exceptions on each side approach, and perhaps equal, the number of cases that actually conform to the rule. If the exceptions are approximately as numerous as the conforming cases, one must ask: in what sense is the rule truly explanatory? The rule *mitzvot asei shehazman grama* is often invoked as though it provides a principled reason for why women are exempt from particular *mitzvot* — but if the exceptions are equally numerous, the rule loses much of its descriptive and normative force. This does not render Rabbi Yochanan's principle incorrect; rather, it illustrates precisely his point that *klalos* are never to be trusted as exhaustive.

The *gemara* then restates Rabbi Yochanan's conclusion: *ein lemedin min haklalos va'afilu b'makom shene'emar bo chutz* — even in cases where exceptions are explicitly listed, one cannot be certain the list is complete. The more obvious case (a *klal* with no exceptions listed) having been demonstrated, the *gemara* now turns to demonstrate the rule in the context of an explicit *chutz*.

### Zavim Merkav Case

Abaye — or, as the *gemara* alternatively attributes, Rabbi Yirmiya — cites a further example: *od klal echad amru* — another general rule was taught — in *Zavim*. *Kol she'nisa al gabei hazav tamei* — anything that a *zav* carries becomes *tamei*. *Vekol hazav nisa alav tahor* — anything that carries a *zav* is *tahor*.

Some background is necessary here. The ordinary rules of *tumah* involve *tumas massa* — contracting impurity by carrying a *tamei* object. In the case of a *zav*, an unusual dynamic obtains: the *zav* is himself the *tamei* entity, yet he is a person capable of the act of carrying. Accordingly, the Torah establishes a special category — *elyono shel zav*, "what is above the *zav*" — whereby objects the *zav* carries or wears themselves become *tamei*. Rashi notes this is derived from the verse *vekol asher yihyeh tachatav*, which is read creatively to mean "anything beneath which the *zav* is" — i.e., anything that the *zav* is carrying.

The converse rule — that what carries a *zav* is *tahor* — has important qualifications. A human being who carries a *zav* contracts *tumas massa* in the ordinary way. And if the object beneath the *zav* is designed for sitting or lying — a *mishkav* or *moshav* — it becomes an *av hatumah*, even without direct contact, since its *halakhic* identity is determined by its function as the *zav*'s designated seat or bed. But an object not made for sitting or lying that happens to support a *zav* does not become *tamei*. These are the stated exceptions.

The *gemara* then asks: *v'tov leka* — are those the only exceptions? What about *merkav* — a saddle, something one rides on? The *gemara* responds that a saddle upon which the *zav* sits is itself in the category of *moshav* and would already be included. The real question concerns *gavva de'ukfa* — the pommel of the saddle, the raised front horn. The pommel supports the act of riding but is not the surface directly sat upon. The *gemara* establishes that the saddle itself (*ha'ukaf*) has the *tumah* of *moshav*, while the pommel (*hatapus*) has the *tumah* of *merkav* — a parallel but slightly lesser category. This distinction suggests that even the part of an object not directly sat upon may carry a form of *tumah* if it functionally enables the riding, which was not enumerated in the original general rule. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *shema mina ein lemedin min haklalos va'afilu b'makom shene'emar bo chutz*.

### Fungi Exception

Ravina — or, alternatively, Rav Nachman — then adduces the case closest to the present *daf*: *af anan nami tanina* — we too have taught this in our *mishna*. The *mishna* states one may use any food for *eruv* and *shituf*, *chutz min hamayim u'min hamelach* — yet there are additional exceptions. *Ve'ika kmeihim u'pitriot* — what about fungi and mushrooms? Those too cannot be used, yet they are not listed among the exceptions. Thus, *ein lomedim min haklalos va'afilu b'makom shene'emar bo chutz*.

Two distinct understandings emerge regarding why fungi are excluded, and they differ on which category the exception applies to. Tosafos reads the *gemara* as excluding fungi from *eruvei chatzerot*, *shitufei mevo'ot*, and *eruvei techumin*, on the grounds that fungi are not fit to be eaten in their raw state. This, Tosafos argues, reflects an important distinction: while *ma'aser* money may be used to purchase raw meat that is not yet fit for consumption, the *eruv* requirement demands that the food be presently edible, since the *eruv* functions to establish a legal residence and the food must plausibly represent an actual meal. Mushrooms in their raw state do not meet that standard, at least in Tosafos's view.

The *Gra*, however, reads the *gemara* differently. Noting a textual annotation (*nichtav batziddo ve-chulu* — "written alongside: etc.") indicating that the full text of the *mishna* is being referenced, the *Gra* argues that the exception applies specifically to *ma'aser sheni*, not to *eruv*. His comment on *ve'ika kmeihim u'pitriot* cites *Torat Kohanim*, *perek* 1, *piska* 8, in the context of the *ma'aser* rules. The reason fungi are excluded from *ma'aser* is that they are not *gidulei karka* — they do not genuinely grow from the ground in the manner required by the verse governing *ma'aser sheni* purchases. Thus, while Tosafos locates the exclusion in *eruv* and grounds it in a standard of present edibility, the *Gra* locates it in *ma'aser* and grounds it in the criterion of *gidulei karka*. The significance of this distinction will carry into the discussion of *ma'aser* that follows.

## Kesef Ma'aser

### Eruv vs. Ma'aser

The *gemara* now turns directly to the *halakhot* of *kesef ma'aser* — what may be purchased with the redeemed funds of *ma'aser sheni*. A dispute is recorded between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina. *Chad masni a'eruv* — one transmits the following clarification as pertaining to *eruv* — *vichad masni a'ma'aser* — and the other transmits it as pertaining to *ma'aser*.

### Salt Water Dip

The clarification in question is: *lo shanu ela mayim bifnei atzman u'melach bifnei atzman* — the prohibition against using water or salt applies only when each is used alone. *Aval bemayim u'melach me'arvin yachad* — but salt water, water and salt combined, one may use. The combination, functioning as a dipping condiment, acquires the status of food in a way that neither ingredient does on its own.

### Scope Comparison

The one who applies this ruling to *ma'aser* implies an even broader permission for *eruv*: *kol shekein a'eruv* — if combined salt water qualifies even for the stricter category of *ma'aser*, it certainly qualifies for *eruv*. The one who applies it to *eruv* does not necessarily extend it to *ma'aser*, since *ma'aser* may have more restrictive criteria. What are those criteria? *Pirei ba'inan* — by *ma'aser*, we require something in the conceptual category of *peri*, a fruit or product, meaning something generated by another living thing, whether animal or plant. Water and salt, as minerals and liquid, do not meet this standard, even when combined. Thus, the *ma'aser* category may be narrower: not merely any food, but specifically food that qualifies as a *peri* in the abstract sense.

Tosafos introduces a nuance that runs in the opposite direction: there is one area in which *eruv* is actually more demanding than *ma'aser*. As noted above, the food for an *eruv* must be presently edible — fit to be eaten in its current state — whereas *ma'aser* money may be used to purchase raw meat and other foods not yet ready to eat. This is the foundation of Tosafos's understanding of why fungi are excluded from *eruv* but not from *ma'aser*. Setting this aside, the general pattern holds: *ma'aser* is the narrower category, defined by the requirement of *peri*, and possibly by *gidulei karka* as well.

### Fish Brine Testimony

Rav Yitzchak, upon returning from *Eretz Yisrael*, transmitted this ruling as applying to *ma'aser*. The *gemara* challenges him with a *beraita*: Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish testified before Rabbi Eliezer that in his father's household, *tzir* — fish brine — was purchased with *ma'aser* money. The *gemara* responds to the challenge:

### Fish in Brine

*Shema lo shamata ela k'she'irvei dagim me'uravin bahen* — perhaps what Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish testified was only valid when actual pieces of fish were mixed into the brine. Plain brine, without fish, would not qualify. Even Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish's permission extends only to *tzir*, and he grounds it in *shumnah depeirah hu* — the brine carries the "fat" of the fish, understood as a product of the fish itself. Fish, as a living creature producing offspring, qualifies as a *peri* in the abstract sense, and the brine that exudes from the fish partakes of that status. But plain salt water, with no fish component, would not.

### Oil Addition

Rav Yosef offers a resolution at the top of 27b: *lo nitzrecha ela shenasan lesochan shemen* — the permission to use salt water for *ma'aser* applies specifically when oil has been added to the mixture. In that case, it is the oil — a legitimate *ma'aser* product — that grounds the permission, and the salt water is permitted along with it.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye immediately objects: *vetifok lei mishum shemen* — if the entire basis of the permission is the oil, why mention salt water at all? Simply say one may spend *ma'aser* money on oil. What is the *chiddush* of including the salt water in the discussion?

### Havla'ah Principle

The *gemara* answers: *tzricha* — it is indeed necessary, because the case teaches the principle of *havla'ah*: *shenasan dmei mayim u'melach b'havla'ah* — that one may pay for salt water using *ma'aser* money when it is absorbed (*bilvua*) into the larger purchase. If one spends ten coins of *ma'aser sheni* funds on a composite dip consisting of oil, water, and salt, part of the purchase price is factually going toward the salt water. The principle of *havla'ah* holds that when a non-qualifying ingredient is acquired as part of a unified purchase whose primary component is legitimate, the portion of the price attributable to the non-qualifying element is absorbed and permitted along with the rest.

The *gemara* confirms this from a *beraita*: Ben Bag Bag says that the verse *vekol asher tishalkha nafshecha*, in its context alongside *bakar, tzon, yayin, u'shechar*, teaches through each word that *havla'ah* applies to various composite units. When one buys cattle, some of the cost goes toward the hide, which is not food; when one buys sheep, some goes toward the wool; when one buys wine, some goes toward the bottle; and when one buys *temed* — a fermented mead made by soaking grape lees in water — the primary ingredient is water, with only a trace contribution from the grape product. In each case, *ma'aser* money may be used for the entire purchase. This is the *halakhic* principle of *havla'ah* — secondary and otherwise non-qualifying components are absorbed into, and covered by, the primary legitimate purchase.

## Ben Bag Bag Exegesis

### Cattle Example

Ben Bag Bag derives *havla'ah* from the verse *babakar* — cattle. *Melamed shelokchin bakar agav oro* — one may purchase cattle with *ma'aser* money even though some of the cost goes toward the hide, which is not food. The justification is *mishum degufei hu* — the hide is literally part of the same body. It is physically inseparable from the animal and constitutes a genuine unity with it.

### Bakar Necessity

Rabbi Yochanan famously declares: *man de'mitargem li b'vakar aliba d'Ben Bag Bag* — whoever can explain to me why Ben Bag Bag requires the word *bakar* in the verse, *movilna manei avatrei l'vei masuta* — I will carry his clothes after him to the bathhouse. In other words, why is *bakar* necessary at all? Given that *tzon* — sheep — already teaches *havla'ah* in a harder case (since wool, unlike skin, can be detached), does not *bakar* follow all the more readily? The *gemara* develops this challenge carefully.

### Sheep Example

*Bakar* could not have been inferred from *tzon* alone. Had the Torah only mentioned sheep, one might have thought the permission regarding *gizatah* — its wool — applied only because the wool is still physically attached to the body at the time of purchase (*mishum de'mechubar*). From *tzon* alone, one might not have inferred that cattle's hide may be included, since the hide might be treated as a mere *tofel* without even the argument of *guf echad* — one unified body — which does apply to the skin of cattle in a way distinct from wool. Therefore, *bakar* is needed to teach the paradigmatic case of skin as literally part of the body.

### Wine Example

*Tzon* could not have been inferred from *yayin* alone. Wine comes with a bottle — *agav kankanno* — which is clearly a separate object from the contents it contains. One might have thought *havla'ah* applies only because the bottle is the container without which the wine cannot exist or be transported — *hainu netiruteih*, that is its means of preservation. But extending this to wool, which is not necessary for the sheep's continued existence, would not follow. Hence *tzon* must be stated separately.

### Mead Example

*Shechar* — mead or the fermented *temed* — teaches the most extreme application of *havla'ah*. *Temed mishehichmitz*: wine lees were soaked in water until the mixture became slightly sharp, and then the water was poured off and drunk. In this preparation, the actual grape contribution is minimal — *dikpaya d'alma hu* — it is essentially just the sharpness, a faint trace of fruit character in what is fundamentally water. One might have thought *ma'aser* money could not be spent on something so predominantly water-based. Therefore *shechar* must be stated: even this qualifies.

### Each Word Distinct

The *gemara* works through the logic in both directions, demonstrating that no word in the list could have been omitted. Had only *shechar* been written, one might have understood it to mean *dveilah ke'ilit* — a particular type of intoxicating date-product, which is genuine fruit — and derived nothing about wine with its bottle or sheep with its wool. Had only *yayin* been written, one would know the bottle is included because of *netirutei*, but not have extended *havla'ah* to wool. Had only *tzon* been written, one might have read *agav gizatah* as referring to a sheep already shorn — meaning only the skin qualifies — and not to the wool as such. Therefore *bakar* is needed: from *bakar* we derive the skin, from *tzon* we derive the wool, from *yayin* the bottle, and from *shechar* the case of a predominantly non-food component. Each word performs irreplaceable work.

The ultimate resolution to Rabbi Yochanan's challenge, then, is as follows: *aval tzon agav gizoteiha mishteban, bakar agav ora miba'ei?* — if we know that sheep may be purchased together with their wool, is there any question that cattle may be purchased together with their skin? The answer is that without the word *bakar*, we would have misread *tzon* as referring only to a shorn sheep, leaving the skin of cattle unaddressed. *Tzon* is needed to teach wool; *bakar* is needed to establish that the skin-inclusion principle exists so that *tzon* can be read to include the wool. Each word is thus indispensable, and the system is fully coherent.

The *chiddush* of the entire passage for the *halakha* of *ma'aser sheni* is therefore: *havla'ah* is a genuine principle. The composite salt-water-and-oil dip may be purchased with *ma'aser sheni* funds, even though part of the price is going toward water and salt that could not be purchased alone, because those elements are absorbed into the primary legitimate purchase. The same principle underlies every composite acquisition — the animal with its hide, the sheep with its wool, the wine with its bottle, the mead with its water base.

## Hermeneutical Approaches

### Ribbuy U'Miu'ut Method

The *gemara* now turns to a foundational question: on what hermeneutical basis do the *tannaim* disagree about what may be purchased with *ma'aser sheni* funds? *B'mai kamiflagi Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish v'Rabbi Eliezer v'hani tannai d'lekaman?* — what is the methodological dispute between Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish and Rabbi Eliezer on one side, and the other *tannaim* on the other?

The answer is that they employ different hermeneutical schools. Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish and Rabbi Eliezer interpret the verse using *ribbuy u'mi'ut* — the method of inclusive and exclusive language. The relevant verse is: *V'nasatah hakesef b'chol asher te'aveh nafshecha* — *riba*, inclusive; *babakar uvatzon uvayayin uvashechar* — *mi'et*, limiting terms; *uvechol asher tishalkha nafshecha* — *chazar v'riba*, returning to inclusion. This produces a structure of *ribbuy, mi'ut, ribbuy*.

### Three-Term Rule

Under the *ribbuy u'mi'ut* methodology, the structure *ribbuy–mi'ut–ribbuy* yields the conclusion *riba hakol* — virtually everything is included. The limiting middle term (*mi'ut*) does not narrow the category to items similar to the list; rather, it serves only to exclude that which is most dissimilar from all the items mentioned. *U'mai mi'et?* — what does the limiting term exclude? For Rabbi Eliezer, it excludes *tzir* — fish brine alone; for Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish, it excludes *mayim u'melach* — plain water and salt. Fish is certainly included by both, since fish, as a living creature producing offspring, qualifies as a *peri* in the abstract sense, and the brine is arguably a product of the fish as well.

### Klal U'Prat Method

*V'hani tannai darshi klal u'prat* — the other *tannaim* employ the school of *klal u'prat*. The identical verse is now read as: *bechol asher te'aveh nafshecha* — *klal*, a general term; *babakar uvatzon uvayayin uvashechar* — *prat*, specific terms; *uvechol asher tishalkha nafshecha* — *chazar v'klal*, a return to the general. This three-part structure — *klal, prat u'klal* — yields the conclusion *ein ata dan ela k'ein haprat* — you may only include items similar to the specific terms.

### Inclusion Criteria

What makes the specific items in the *prat* distinctive? Two criteria are identified: *pri mi'pri* — they are a product produced from another product, in the sense of a fruit or living-generated item — and *gidulei karka* — they grow from the ground. *Af kol pri mi'pri v'gidulei karka* — anything that satisfies both criteria falls within the scope of the verse.

### Fish Exclusion

The consequence of the *klal u'prat* method is that fish are excluded. Fish do not grow from the ground — they live in water and reproduce in water. However clearly they satisfy *pri mi'pri* (since fish are born from other fish), they fail the criterion of *gidulei karka*, and under *klal u'prat u'klal* both criteria must be met. Fish brine, accordingly, also falls outside the permitted category.

### Tomorrow Preview

The *gemara*'s analysis of these hermeneutical approaches bears directly on the question, raised earlier, of mushrooms and fungi under the *Gra*'s reading. If fungi are excluded from *ma'aser sheni* because they are not *gidulei karka* — as the *Gra* argues from *Torat Kohanim* — then their exclusion fits neatly within the *klal u'prat u'klal* methodology: they fail the criterion of growth from the ground. This will be developed further in the following *daf*, where the *Gra*'s understanding of fungi as *ein lomedim min haklalos* exceptions will receive fuller treatment alongside the broader debate about what qualifies under the *klal u'prat* approach.
