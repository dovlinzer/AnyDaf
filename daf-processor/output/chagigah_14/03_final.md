# Chagigah 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Faces & Wings

### Kerub vs. Human Face

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״וּדְמוּת פְּנֵיהֶם פְּנֵי אָדָם וּפְנֵי אַרְיֵה אֶל הַיָּמִין לְאַרְבַּעְתָּם וּפְנֵי שׁוֹר מֵהַשְּׂמֹאל לְאַרְבַּעְתָּן וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאַרְבָּעָה פָנִים לְאֶחָד פְּנֵי הָאֶחָד פְּנֵי הַכְּרוּב וּפְנֵי הַשֵּׁנִי פְּנֵי אָדָם וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי פְּנֵי אַרְיֵה וְהָרְבִיעִי פְּנֵי נָשֶׁר״, וְאִילּוּ שׁוֹר לָא קָא חָשֵׁיב! אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: יְחֶזְקֵאל בִּיקֵּשׁ עָלָיו רַחֲמִים, וַהֲפָכוֹ לִכְרוּב. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! קָטֵיגוֹר יֵעָשֶׂה סָנֵיגוֹר?
>
> **Translation:** **One verse states: "As for the likeness of their faces, they had the face of a man; and the four had the face of a lion on the right side; and the four had the face of an ox on the left side"** (Ezekiel 1:10). **And it is also written: "And each one had four faces: The first face was the face of the cherub, and the second face was the face of a man, and the third the face of a lion, and the fourth the face of an eagle"** (Ezekiel 10:14), **but it does not include the face of an ox** in this second list. **Reish Lakish said: Ezekiel requested mercy with regard to it,** i.e., the face of the ox, **and had it turned into a cherub. He said before Him:** **Master of the Universe. Shall an accuser become a defender?**

The daf opens in the middle of 13b, where the Gemara has been addressing apparent contradictions in the prophetic visions of the Divine throne. In Yechezkel's vision, the faces surrounding the throne include a lion, an eagle, an ox, and a human face. Yet in another passage within Yechezkel himself, the ox is replaced by a *keruv* — a cherub — which the Gemara explains represents the face of an infant. The change occurred, the Gemara relates, because Yechezkel prayed that the image of the ox be removed from the heavenly throne, lest it serve as a reminder of the *ma'aseh egel*, the sin of the Golden Calf. This is a striking parallel to an earlier passage where David HaMelech prays to affect what exists in the heavenly realm — a profound tension between God's utter transcendence and the reality that human prayer can alter the very appearance of things above.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״כְּרוּב״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: כְּרָבְיָא, שֶׁכֵּן בְּבָבֶל קוֹרִין לְיָנוֹקָא רָבְיָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״פְּנֵי הָאֶחָד פְּנֵי הַכְּרוּב וּפְנֵי הַשֵּׁנִי פְּנֵי אָדָם וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי פְּנֵי אַרְיֵה וְהָרְבִיעִי פְּנֵי נָשֶׁר״, הַיְינוּ פְּנֵי כְרוּב הַיְינוּ פְּנֵי אָדָם? אַפֵּי רַבְרְבֵי וְאַפֵּי זוּטְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the meaning of "cherub"? Rabbi Abbahu said: Like a baby** [***keravya***], **for in Babylonia they call a baby *ravya*. Rav Pappa said to Abaye: However, if that is so, what is the meaning of that which is written: "The first face was the face of the cherub, and the second face was the face of a man, and the third the face of a lion, and the fourth the face of an eagle"? The face of a cherub is the same as the face of a man;** what is the difference between them? He replied: The difference is that the face of a man is referring to **a large face, whereas the face of a cherub means the small face** of a baby.

Rav Pappa raises an objection to Abaye: if a *keruv* is simply an infant's face, then the verse in Yechezkel that lists both *pnei ha-keruv* and *pnei adam* — the face of a cherub and the face of a human — presents a redundancy. Are they not both human faces? The Gemara resolves this: *appei ravrivi ve-appei zutrei* — there is a large face and a small face. One is the face of an adult and one is the face of a child. These become significant technical terms in *Kabbalah* as well.

### Six vs. Four Wings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״שֵׁשׁ כְּנָפַיִם שֵׁשׁ כְּנָפַיִם לְאֶחָד״, וְכָתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״וְאַרְבָּעָה פָנִים לְאֶחַת וְאַרְבַּע כְּנָפַיִם לְאַחַת לָהֶם״? לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים, כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּם. כִּבְיָכוֹל, שֶׁנִּתְמַעֲטוּ כַּנְפֵי הַחַיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **One verse states: "Each one had six wings; with two it covered its face and with two it covered its feet, and with two it flew"** (Isaiah 6:2), **and another verse states: "And every one had four faces, and every one of them had four wings"** (Ezekiel 1:6). The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult, as here,** when the verse states they each had six wings, it is referring **to the time when the Temple is standing, while there,** where four wings are described, it is referring **to the time when the Temple is not standing, for it is as if the wings of the animals were diminished.**

The Gemara turns from the faces of the throne to the wings of the heavenly angels, noting an apparent contradiction between two prophetic visions. Yeshayahu describes each angel with *shesh knafayim*, six wings, while Yechezkel describes the *chayot* as having four wings each. The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya* — when the *Beis HaMikdash* was standing, the angels had six wings; when it was not standing, they had four. Yechezkel received his vision in exile, yet the *Gra* notes that this was anticipatory — the destruction was coming and the vision already reflected what was about to occur.

The Gemara then states, *kivyakhol nismatu kanfei ha-chayot* — it is as if the wings of the heavenly *chayot* were diminished by the destruction of the Temple. Rashi clarifies that the word *kivyakhol* here does not suggest this did not actually happen; it did happen. Rather, *kivyakhol* is a rhetorical hedge because one speaks cautiously about any lack or diminishment being attributed to things related to God. Importantly, one student noted that *kivyakhol* is normally used in relation to God Himself, and yet here the Gemara deploys it in the context of angels — a remarkable extension of the term.

This discussion reflects the Gemara's broader thematic concern: on the one hand, God is supremely transcendent — we read earlier about the vast distances separating the heavenly realms — but on the other hand, what transpires below directly shapes what transpires above, for good and for ill.

### Lost Wings Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ אִימַּעוּט? אָמַר רַב חֲנַנְאֵל אָמַר רַב: אוֹתָן שֶׁאוֹמְרוֹת שִׁירָה בָּהֶן. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וּבִשְׁתַּיִם יְעוֹפֵף וְקָרָא זֶה אֶל זֶה וְאָמַר״, וּכְתִיב: ״הֲתָעִיף עֵינֶיךָ בּוֹ וְאֵינֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Which of the wings were diminished? Rav Ḥananel said** that **Rav said: Those with which they recite song.** The proof is that **it is written here: "And with two it flew [***yeofef***]. And one called to the other and said"** (Isaiah 6:2–3), **and it is written: "Will you set [***hata'if*] your eyes upon it? It is gone"** (Proverbs 23:5), implying that the flight of these wings had ceased.

The Gemara now asks: *hai minaihu imtu?* — which two of the original six wings were lost? Two opinions are presented.

The first is that of Rav Chananel in the name of Rav: the wings that were lost are *otan she-omrot shirah ba-hen*, those with which the angels would sing to God. The verse in Yeshayahu says *u-vish-tayim ye'ofef*, with two they would fly, and separately describes the angels calling out *kadosh kadosh kadosh* to one another. The verb *ye'ofef*, to fly or flutter, connects through wordplay to another verse, *hat'if einecha bo ve-einenu* — if you flutter your eyes at it, it will be gone. So these are the singing/flying wings that have disappeared. The rationale is entirely fitting: with the *Beis HaMikdash* destroyed, the *shira* sung below has been silenced, and correspondingly, the *shira* above has been diminished. As Tosafot note, and as the Gemara will elaborate, there is a deep connection between the song Israel offers and the song of the angels — *vekara zeh el zeh*, one calling to another. A student raised the evocative suggestion that *vekara zeh el zeh* — one calling to the other — might refer to one wing calling to the next, which is an intriguing reading of the imagery.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: אוֹתָן שֶׁמְּכַסּוֹת בָּהֶן רַגְלֵיהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְרַגְלֵיהֶם רֶגֶל יְשָׁרָה״, וְאִי לָאו דְּאִימַּעוּט — מְנָא הֲוָה יָדַע? דִּלְמָא דְּאִיגַּלַּאי וְחַזְיָא לֵיהּ. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, ״וּדְמוּת פְּנֵיהֶם פְּנֵי אָדָם״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּאִימַּעוּט?! אֶלָּא דְּאִיגַּלַּאי וְחַזְיָא לֵיהּ, הָכָא נָמֵי — דְּאִיגַּלַּאי וְחַזְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say** that the wings they lost are **those with which they cover their feet, for it is stated: "And their feet were straight feet"** (Ezekiel 1:7). **Now if these wings had not been diminished, how would he know** what their feet looked like? Clearly their feet were no longer covered. The Gemara rejects this: This is no proof, for **perhaps they were momentarily revealed,** allowing him to **see them. Because if you do not say so,** that he saw them for a moment, then with regard to the verse: **"And the likeness of their faces, they had the face of a man"** (Ezekiel 1:10), **so too will you say that these** the wings covering their faces **were diminished** as well? **Rather,** it must be **that they were revealed and he saw them. Here too, they were revealed and he saw them.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! בִּשְׁלָמָא אַפֵּיהּ — אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְגַלּוֹיֵי קַמֵּיהּ רַבֵּיהּ. כַּרְעֵיהּ — לָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְגַלּוֹיֵי קַמֵּיהּ רַבֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **How can these cases be compared? Granted, it is logical that his face** was revealed, as **it is proper conduct for an angel to reveal** his face **before his Master,** and therefore it is possible that they would have revealed their faces at certain times; but with regard to **his feet,** it is **not proper conduct to reveal** them **before his Master.** Therefore, they must have lacked wings to cover their feet.

The second opinion is that of the *rabbanan*: the wings that were lost are *otan shemechassot bahen ragleihem*, those used to cover the angels' legs. The proof is from Yechezkel's vision: he saw *regel yeshara*, a straight leg, meaning the legs were visible to him. If the covering wings still existed, he would only have seen the legs if the wings happened to be open at that moment. But, the Gemara continues, perhaps the legs were simply visible when the wings opened and closed — *degali ve-chazi leh*. Indeed, he also saw their faces, which the wing-of-the-face covered — does that mean those wings were gone too? No, because *bishlama appei orach ar'a legalluyei kamei rabbei* — it is entirely appropriate to uncover one's face in the presence of one's master. But *karei lav orach ar'a legalluyei kamei rabbei* — the legs, by contrast, would never be uncovered before one's master. Therefore, if Yechezkel saw the legs, it must be because those covering wings were simply gone, not merely momentarily open. The imagery is powerful: the angels are, in a sense, standing partially unclothed before God — a vivid expression of what the exile and destruction have wrought both below and above.

## River of Fire

### Angels from Dinur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״אֶלֶף אַלְפִין יְשַׁמְּשׁוּנֵּיהּ וְרִבּוֹ רִבְבָן קָדָמוֹהִי יְקוּמוּן״, וְכָתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״הֲיֵשׁ מִסְפָּר לִגְדוּדָיו״! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים, כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים, כִּבְיָכוֹל שֶׁנִּתְמַעֲטָה פָּמַלְיָא שֶׁל מַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One verse states: "A thousand thousands ministered to Him, and ten thousand times ten thousand stood before him"** (Daniel 7:10), **and another verse states: "Is there a number to His troops?"** (Job 25:3), implying that they are even more numerous than "ten thousand times ten thousand." The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult, for here,** when they are without number, the verse is referring **to the time when the Temple is standing; there,** the other verse is referring **to the time when the Temple is not standing, for it is as though the heavenly entourage [*pamalya*] were diminished.**

The Gemara presents another contradiction. One verse in Daniel states that *elef alfin yishameshuney* — a thousand thousands, i.e., a million, attend to God — while a verse in Iyov states that his troops are beyond number, infinite. The Gemara resolves it: *lo kashya* — one applies when the *Beis HaMikdash* stood and one applies when it did not. The verse in Iyov was presumably when the Temple still existed; the verse in Daniel, which was written clearly after the *churban*, reflects the diminished retinue. This too is characterized as *kivyakhol she-nismatu pamalya shel ma'ala* — as if God's great celestial retinue, His *pamalya*, had become smaller.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם אַבָּא יוֹסֵי בֶּן דּוֹסַאי: ״אֶלֶף אַלְפִין יְשַׁמְּשׁוּנֵּיהּ״ — מִסְפַּר גְּדוּד אֶחָד, ״וְלִגְדוּדָיו אֵין מִסְפָּר״. וְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: ״אֶלֶף אַלְפִין יְשַׁמְּשׁוּנֵּיהּ״, לִנְהַר דִּינוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נְהַר דִּינוּר נָגֵד וְנָפֵק מִן קֳדָמוֹהִי אֶלֶף אַלְפִין יְשַׁמְּשׁוּנֵּיהּ וְרִבּוֹ רִבְבָן קָדָמוֹהִי יְקוּמוּן״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught in a *baraita*: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says in the name of Abba Yosei ben Dosai: "A thousand thousands ministered to Him"** is referring to the **number** of angels in **a single troop,** but with regard to the number of his troops, it can be said: **"And to his troops, there is no number". And Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba said:** There is no contradiction, since with regard to the phrase **"a thousand thousands ministered to Him,"** the pronoun "Him" can be literally translated as: It, referring not to those who serve God Himself, but to those who administer **to the River Dinur, as it is stated: "A fiery [*dinur*] river issued and came forth from before him; a thousand thousands ministered to it, and a myriad myriads stand before it"** (Daniel 7:10). The ministers of God, however, are indeed too numerous to count.

A *braisa* is cited in the name of Rebbi, citing Abba Yosi ben Dosai: the million who attend to God is the count of a single *gdud*, a single legion. The verse in Iyov saying His legions are infinite means He has an infinite number of such legions, each containing a million.

A further *braisa* is then cited: Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba taught that the *elef alfin yishameshuney* — the thousands upon thousands — refers to those that emerge from the *Nehar Dinur*, the River of Fire. The word *dinur* is Aramaic for fire, and it is drawn from the very passage in Daniel that the Gemara is now expounding.

### River & the Wicked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵהֵיכָן נָפֵיק? מִזֵּיעָתָן שֶׁל חַיּוֹת. וּלְהֵיכָן שָׁפֵיךְ? אָמַר רַב זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה אָמַר רַב: עַל רֹאשׁ רְשָׁעִים בְּגֵיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִנֵּה סַעֲרַת ה׳ חֵמָה יָצְאָה וְסַעַר מִתְחוֹלֵל עַל רֹאשׁ רְשָׁעִים יָחוּל״. וְרַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: עַל אֲשֶׁר קוּמְּטוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר קוּמְּטוּ וְלֹא עֵת נָהָר יוּצַק יְסוֹדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where does this river flow?** The Gemara answers: **From the perspiration of the divine creatures. And where does it flow to? Rav Zutra bar Toviya said** that **Rav said: Upon the heads of the wicked in Gehenna, as it is stated: "Behold, a storm of the Lord has gone forth in fury, a whirling storm; it shall whirl upon the head of the wicked"** (Jeremiah 23:19). **And Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** The river flows **over those who were snatched away,** i.e., the generations that were never created, **as it is stated: "Who were snatched away before their time, whose foundation was poured out as a stream"** (Job 22:16), implying that the River Dinur flows over them.

The relevant passage in Daniel reads as follows: *chazei haveis ad di chorsavan rmiu* — I was watching until thrones were set in place; *v'Atik Yomin yesiv* — and the Ancient of Days took his seat. His garment was like white snow, the hair of His head like clean wool, *korseih shvivin dinur* — His throne was tongues of flame; *galgalohi nur dolik* — the wheels were blazing fire; *nehar dinur naged v'nafek min kodamohi* — a river of fire stretched out and came forth from His presence; *elef alfin yishameshuney v'ribo ribvan kodamohi yekumun* — thousands upon thousands attended Him, myriads of myriads stood before Him; *dina yesiv v'sifrin p'sichu* — the court sat and the books were opened. This vision in Daniel — the Ancient of Days sitting in judgment with open books — is evidently a source for the imagery of Rosh Hashanah.

So the million who attend God emerge from this *Nehar Dinur*: *nehar dinur naged v'nafek min kodamohi, elef alfin yishameshuney*. The Gemara then asks where this river itself comes from: *mizei'asan shel chayot* — from the sweat of the heavenly *chayot*. Their exertion produces this river of fire from which the angels are born.

Where does the river pour out? Rav Zutra bar Tuvya in the name of Rav: *al rosh reshaim b'Gehinnom* — it pours upon the heads of the wicked in *Gehinnom*, as the verse in Yirmiyahu says, the whirlwind of God's anger shall fall on the head of the wicked.

### Lost Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הֶחָסִיד: אֵלּוּ תְּשַׁע מֵאוֹת וְשִׁבְעִים וְאַרְבַּע דּוֹרוֹת שֶׁקּוּמְּטוּ לְהִיבָּרְאוֹת קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּבְרָא הָעוֹלָם, וְלֹא נִבְרְאוּ. עָמַד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וּשְׁתָלָן בְּכׇל דּוֹר וָדוֹר, וְהֵן הֵן עַזֵּי פָנִים שֶׁבַּדּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught in a *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon HeḤasid said** in explanation of this verse: **These people "who were snatched away" are those nine hundred and seventy-four generations that were snatched away; they were to have been created** **before the creation of the world, but they were not created.** The Torah was supposed to have been given a thousand generations after the world was created, as it is written: "He commanded His word for a thousand generations" (Psalms 105:8), but God gave it earlier, after only twenty-six generations, so that nine-hundred and seventy-four generations should have been created but were not. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, acted by planting them in each and every generation, and they are the insolent ones of the generation,** as they belonged to generations that should not have been created at all.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov offers another reading: the river pours upon *asher kumtu b'lo eis* — those that were decreed to exist but whose time did not come. The Gemara elaborates through a *braisa* in the name of Rabbi Shimon Hachasid: these are the nine hundred and seventy-four generations that were decreed to be created before the world was established but were not. The source is the verse *davar tziva l'elef dor* — God commanded His word for a thousand generations. If the Torah was to be given after a thousand generations of human history, but only twenty-six generations elapsed from Adam to *Matan Torah*, then nine hundred and seventy-four generations that were supposed to precede *Matan Torah* were never created. God saw that the world could not wait a thousand generations and accelerated the giving of the Torah.

What happened to those nine hundred and seventy-four? *Amar HaKadosh Baruch Hu veshatlan bechol dor vador* — God distributed them across all subsequent generations. *Veheinan azai panim shebedor* — these are the brazen, shameless people found in every generation. They are the residue of those who were so wicked they contributed to the urgency of giving the Torah earlier.

Tosafot is troubled by this: can God punish souls with fire when they were never created and therefore never sinned? Tosafot resolves this by saying the two opinions are really one — they were distributed into all subsequent generations, and they can only be judged for evil they actually commit once created, not for what they were decreed to be beforehand.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר קוּמְּטוּ״ — לִבְרָכָה הוּא דִּכְתִיב. אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁמְּקַמְּטִין עַצְמָן עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְגַלֶּה לָהֶם סוֹד לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נָהָר יוּצַק יְסוֹדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that the verse: **"Who were snatched [*kumtu*]"** (Job 22:16), **is written for a blessing,** as the verse is not referring to lowly, cursed people, but to the blessed. **These are Torah scholars, who shrivel [*mekamtin*],** i.e., humble, **themselves over the words of Torah in this world. The Holy One, Blessed be He, reveals a secret to them in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "Whose foundation [*yesodam*] was poured out as a stream"** (Job 22:16), implying that He will provide them with an abundant knowledge of secret matters [*sod*].

Rav Amram bar Yitzchak offers the positive counterpart reading: *asher kumtu* refers to *talmidei chachamim* who dedicate themselves to *divrei Torah* in this world — those who press themselves, so to speak, into Torah. For them, God will reveal secrets in the world to come, as the verse concludes: *v'nahar yutzak yesodam* — and the poured river is their *yesod*, their foundation, and also their *sod*, their secret. This river in Iyov, distinct from the *Nehar Dinur* of Daniel, is for the righteous.

### Daily Angel Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב: בַּר אַרְיָא! תָּא אֵימָא לָךְ מִילְּתָא מֵהָנֵי מִילֵּי מְעַלְּיָיתָא דַּהֲוָה אָמַר אֲבוּךְ: כֹּל יוֹמָא וְיוֹמָא נִבְרָאִין מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת מִנְּהַר דִּינוּר, וְאָמְרִי שִׁירָה וּבָטְלִי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חֲדָשִׁים לַבְּקָרִים רַבָּה אֱמוּנָתֶךָ״. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל דִּיבּוּר וְדִיבּוּר שֶׁיּוֹצֵא מִפִּי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא נִבְרָא מִמֶּנּוּ מַלְאָךְ אֶחָד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בִּדְבַר ה׳ שָׁמַיִם נַעֲשׂוּ וּבְרוּחַ פִּיו כׇּל צְבָאָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said to Ḥiyya bar Rav: Son of great ones, come and I will tell you something of the great things that your father would say: Each and every day, ministering angels are created from the River Dinur, and they recite song** to God **and then immediately cease** to exist, **as it is stated: "They are new every morning; great is Your faithfulness"** (Lamentations 3:23), indicating that new angels praise God each morning. The Gemara comments: **And this opinion disagrees with** that **of Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani, as Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said:** With **each and every word that emerges from the mouth of the Holy One, Blessed be He, an angel is created, as it is stated: "By the word of the Lord the heavens were made, and by the breath of His mouth all their hosts"** (Psalms 33:6). The hosts of heaven are the angels, who, he claims, are created from the mouth of God, rather than from the River Dinur.

Shmuel said to Chiyya bar Rav — addressing him as *bar arya*, son of the lion, meaning his father was of the stature of a great scholar — come, let me tell you one of the great things your father used to teach. *Kol yoma v'yoma nivraim malachei hashareit min Nehar Dinur* — every single day, ministering angels are created from the River of Fire. *Ve-amri shirah u-vatli* — they say their song and then they cease to exist. The verse in Eichah supports this: *chadashim labekarim rabbah emunasecha* — new every morning is Your faithfulness. The *Maharsha* reads this as: each day God demonstrates His faithfulness by recreating the world — and not just the world, but the angels themselves are recreated daily, brought into existence for the single purpose of singing *shira* to God, after which they pass out of existence. The analogy was made to a spark flying up from a fire and then immediately extinguishing — the angels are like sparks, brief and luminous, created for one radiant moment of praise.

### Timing of Song

Tosafot on the previous *daf*, at the passage *mizei'at chayot*, develops this theme further and connects it to the liturgy of *Kedushah*. Tosafot writes: from this river of *dinur* angels emerge, say *shira*, and are immediately destroyed — as attested in the *midrash* on *chadashim labekarim*. Why are they destroyed? Because there is a sign among the permanent angels, *hamalachim hakvuim*, the regular ones who have always been present — these angels know the proper moment to begin singing to God and wait for one another. The newly created angels, however, *einam yodim hadas* — they do not know the protocol. Eager and freshly created, they begin singing prematurely, and for singing out of turn, *mitchayvim kilaya* — they are destroyed.

This, Tosafot continues, is why the *Anshei Knesses HaGedolah* instituted the language of *Kedushah* the way they did. In the prayer of *Yotzer*, we say *venosnim reshus zeh lazeh* — the angels give permission to one another before saying *Kedushah*, *kulam ke-echad onim ve-omrim*. They must wait for the proper signal. Correspondingly, *Yotzer mesharsim* — You create attendants — refers to the daily new angels whom God creates; *va-asher mesharsav kulam omdim berum olam*, but the fixed attendants stand on high and endure. The newly created ones are the disposable, daily angels; the permanent ones continue their eternal service.

This is a fascinating window into the theology behind familiar liturgical language. The Gemara then notes a dissenting view: Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonatan that angels are not created from the river of fire at all — rather, *kol dibur v'dibur sheyotze mi-pi HaKadosh Baruch Hu nivra mimenu malach*, every utterance that proceeds from God's mouth creates an angel. The supporting verse: *bidvar Hashem shamayim na'asu u-vi-ruach piv kol tzeva'am* — with God's word the heavens were made and with the breath of His mouth all their hosts. The disagreement touches on a deep question: how directly associated with God are the angels — created from His immediate speech, or emerging at one remove through a river of fire born from the sweat of the celestial *chayot*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״לְבוּשֵׁיהּ כִּתְלַג חִיוָּר וּשְׂעַר רֵישֵׁיהּ כַּעֲמַר נְקֵא״. וּכְתִיב: ״קְוֻצּוֹתָיו תַּלְתַּלִּים שְׁחוֹרוֹת כָּעוֹרֵב״! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בִּישִׁיבָה, כָּאן בַּמִּלְחָמָה. דְּאָמַר מָר: אֵין לְךָ נָאֶה בִּישִׁיבָה אֶלָּא זָקֵן, וְאֵין לְךָ נָאֶה בַּמִּלְחָמָה אֶלָּא בָּחוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to reconcile verses that seem to contradict each other: **One verse states: "His raiment was as white snow, and the hair of his head like pure white wool"** (Daniel 7:9), **and it is written: "His locks are curled, black as a raven"** (Song of Songs 5:11). The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here** the verse in Daniel is referring to when He is **in the heavenly academy,** while **there** the verse in Song of Songs speaks of when He is **at war, for the Master said: There is no finer** individual to study Torah **in an academy than an old man, and there is no finer** individual to wage war **than a youth.** A different metaphor is therefore used to describe God on each occasion.

The Gemara then addresses a further tension in Daniel's vision: one verse describes God's appearance as white-haired and snow-cloaked, while another verse describes *kvutzosav taltalim shechoros ka-orev* — His locks layered and black as a raven. *Lo kashya: kan bi-yeshiva, kan be-milchama* — one is when God sits in council as the elder-sage, the other when He goes out to war. As the saying goes, *ein lecha na'eh bi-yeshiva ela zaken* — nothing is more fitting for one sitting in deliberation than the appearance of age, *ve-ein lecha na'eh be-milchama ela bachur* — and nothing more fitting for battle than youth. This is precisely the idea embedded in *An'im Zemirot*: *zikna be-yom din u-vacharut be-yom kerav* — aged on the day of judgment, youthful on the day of battle.

## Two Thrones

### God & David

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״כׇּרְסְיֵהּ שְׁבִיבִין דִּינוּר״, וְכָתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״עַד דִּי כׇרְסָוָן רְמִיו וְעַתִּיק יוֹמִין יְתִיב״! לָא קַשְׁיָא: אֶחָד לוֹ וְאֶחָד לְדָוִד. כִּדְתַנְיָא: אֶחָד לוֹ וְאֶחָד לְדָוִד, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: עֲקִיבָא, עַד מָתַי אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה שְׁכִינָה חוֹל?! אֶלָּא: אֶחָד לְדִין וְאֶחָד לִצְדָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses another question: **One verse states: "His throne was fiery flames"** (Daniel 7:9), **and another phrase in the same verse states: "Till thrones were placed, and one who was ancient of days sat,"** implying the existence of two thrones. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. One throne is for Him and one is for David, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to this issue: **One throne for Him and one for David; this is the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yosei HaGelili said to him: Akiva, how long shall you make the Divine Presence profane,** by presenting it as though one could sit next to Him? **Rather,** the two thrones are designated for different purposes: **One for judgment and one for righteousness.**

Returning to Daniel's vision, the Gemara notes another tension. One verse says *korseih shvivin dinur* — His throne is flames of fire — while another in the same passage says *ad di chorsavan rmiu*, thrones (in the plural) were set in place, and the Ancient of Days sat upon them. Throne or thrones?

*Lo kashya: kan echad lo ve-kan echad le-David* — one is for God and one is for David, the earthly king. A *braisa* confirms: *echad lo ve-echad le-David, divrei Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva holds that one throne is for God and one for David.

### Justice & Compassion

Rabbi Yose HaGelili rebukes Rabbi Akiva sharply: *Akiva, ad matai ata oseh Shechina chol?* — Akiva, how long will you make the Divine Presence into something mundane? You are placing a human being on a throne alongside God, which is entirely inappropriate. Instead: *echad le-din ve-echad le-tzedakah* — one throne represents God's attribute of *din*, strict justice, and one represents *tzedakah*, which here means compassion or loving-kindness. This is especially fitting given that Daniel's vision is explicitly a scene of divine judgment, resonant with the imagery of Rosh Hashanah — one might say this is the very source for the concept of God removing Himself from the throne of *din* and moving to the throne of *rachamim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ, אוֹ לָא קַיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע: אֶחָד לְדִין וְאֶחָד לִצְדָקָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: עֲקִיבָא, מָה לְךָ אֵצֶל הַגָּדָה! כְּלָךְ מִדַּבְּרוֹתֶיךָ אֵצֶל נְגָעִים וְאֹהָלוֹת. אֶלָּא: אֶחָד לְכִסֵּא וְאֶחָד לִשְׁרַפְרַף. כִּסֵּא — לֵישֵׁב עָלָיו, שְׁרַפְרַף — לַהֲדוֹם רַגְלָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַשָּׁמַיִם כִּסְאִי וְהָאָרֶץ הֲדוֹם רַגְלָי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Did Rabbi Akiva **accept** this rebuff **from him, or did he not accept it from him?** The Gemara offers a proof: **Come and hear** the following teaching of a different *baraita*: **One throne is for judgment and one is for righteousness; this is the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya said to him: Akiva, what are you doing occupying yourself with** the study of ***aggada*?** This is not your field of expertise. **Take [*kelakh*] your words to** the topics of **plagues and tents.** Meaning, it is preferable that you teach the *halakhot* of the impurity of leprosy and the impurity of the dead, which are within your field of expertise. **Rather,** with regard to the two thrones: **One throne is for a seat and one is for a small seat.** The **seat** is **to sit on,** and the **small seat** is **for His footstool, as it is stated: "The heavens are My seat, and the earth My footstool"** (Isaiah 66:1).

The Gemara asks whether Rabbi Akiva accepted the critique. A subsequent *braisa* records Rabbi Akiva himself saying *echad le-din ve-echad le-tzedakah* — so he clearly accepted the correction and adopted this formulation.

One might also note, as a subtext, the possibility that these discussions implicitly address concerns about *gnostic* or *Christian* readings of such passages — particularly those involving a Davidic king figure sharing divine space, given the theological weight such imagery carried in late antiquity.

### Throne & Footstool

Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya was still unsatisfied. Even two thrones for God's two attributes suggests some sense of plurality or lack of perfect singularity in the Divine presence. He said to Rabbi Akiva: *ma lecha etzel aggadah?* — what are you doing with *aggadah*? This is not your domain. *Kelach etzel negaim ve-oholot* — go to Negaim and Oholot, the intricate *halachic* areas where no one can match you. Leave *aggadah* to us. His own resolution: *kan le-kisei ve-kan le-sharpraf* — one is a throne and one is a footstool, as the verse says, *ha-shamayim kisi ve-ha-aretz hadom raglai* — heaven is My throne and the earth is My footstool. Not two thrones, only one throne and a footstool beneath — a formulation that preserves total Divine singularity.

It is worth noting that while Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya tells Rabbi Akiva that *aggadah* is not his domain, there is strong evidence elsewhere — including what the Gemara will discuss regarding Rabbi Akiva entering the *Pardes* — that Rabbi Akiva was in fact a genuine mystic. Whether mystical experience and the ability to interpret these texts as *aggadah* are the same skill is a separate question; not every *kabbalist* is necessarily a master expositor.

## Eighteen Curses

### Ten Losses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה קְלָלוֹת קִילֵּל יְשַׁעְיָה אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְלֹא נִתְקָרְרָה דַּעְתּוֹ עַד שֶׁאָמַר לָהֶם הַמִּקְרָא הַזֶּה: ״יִרְהֲבוּ הַנַּעַר בַּזָּקֵן וְהַנִּקְלֶה בַּנִּכְבָּד״.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Israel to Babylonia, **he said: Isaiah cursed Israel with eighteen curses, and his mind was not calmed,** i.e., he was not satisfied, **until he said to them the** great curse of the **following verse: "The child shall behave insolently against the aged, and the base against the honorable"** (Isaiah 3:5).

Rav Ami states: *shmoneh esreh klalot kilel Yeshayahu et Yisrael* — Yeshayahu pronounced eighteen curses against Israel, predicting the terrible losses that would accompany the destruction. *V'lo nitkarerah da'ato* — and his distress was not settled *ad she-amar lahem ha-mikra hazeh* — until he reached this verse: *yirhavu ha-na'ar ba-zaken ve-ha-nikleh ba-nichbad* — the youth will bully the elder and the despised will lord over the honored. For whatever reason, that final verse — the collapse of social and moral hierarchy — was the culminating anguish of the prophecy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה קְלָלוֹת, מַאי נִינְהוּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי הִנֵּה הָאָדוֹן ה׳ צְבָאוֹת מֵסִיר מִירוּשָׁלִַם וּמִיהוּדָה מַשְׁעֵן וּמַשְׁעֵנָה כֹּל מִשְׁעַן לֶחֶם וְכֹל מִשְׁעַן מָיִם גִּבּוֹר וְאִישׁ מִלְחָמָה שׁוֹפֵט וְנָבִיא וְקוֹסֵם וְזָקֵן. שַׂר חֲמִשִּׁים וּנְשׂוּא פָנִים וְיוֹעֵץ וַחֲכַם חֲרָשִׁים וּנְבוֹן לָחַשׁ. וְנָתַתִּי נְעָרִים שָׂרֵיהֶם וְתַעֲלוּלִים יִמְשְׁלוּ בָם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are these eighteen curses?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "For behold, the Master, the Lord of hosts, shall take away from Jerusalem and from Judah support and staff, every support of bread, and every support of water; the mighty man, and the man of war; the judge, and the prophet, and the diviner, and the elder; the captain of fifty, and the man of favor, and the counselor, and the cunning charmer, and the skillful enchanter. And I will make children their princes, and babes shall rule over them"** (Isaiah 3:1–4). The eighteen items listed in these verses shall be removed from Israel.

The Gemara proceeds to enumerate these eighteen from the passage in Yeshayahu that begins *ki hineh ha-Adon Hashem Tzvaot mesir mi-Yerushalayim u-meYehuda mishen u-mishenah* — the Lord will remove from Jerusalem and Judah all support. The Gemara counts as follows:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מַשְׁעֵן״ — אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלֵי מִקְרָא. ״מַשְׁעֵנָה״ — אֵלּוּ בַּעְלֵי מִשְׁנָה, כְּגוֹן רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן תֵּימָא וַחֲבֵירָיו. פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב פָּפָּא וְרַבָּנַן, חַד אָמַר: שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת סִדְרֵי מִשְׁנָה, וְחַד אָמַר: שְׁבַע מֵאוֹת סִדְרֵי מִשְׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **"Support"; these are masters of the Bible. "Staff"; these are masters of Mishna, such as Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima and his colleagues.** The Gemara interjects: **Rav Pappa and the Rabbis disagreed with regard to this. One** of them **said:** They were proficient in **six hundred orders of Mishna, and** the other **one said:** In **seven hundred orders of Mishna,** only six of which remain today.

*Mishen* — support — these are *ba'alei mikra*, masters of Scripture. *U-mishenah* — the feminine form — these are *ba'alei mishnah*, masters of Mishnah, and the near-homophony with *mishna* is noted. There is a dispute between Rav Pappa and the sages: one says that in the time of Yeshayahu there were six hundred *sidrei mishnah* and one says seven hundred — an almost unimaginable loss of Torah knowledge, a hundred times what we now possess.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כֹּל מִשְׁעַן לֶחֶם״ — אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלֵי תַלְמוּד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְכוּ לַחֲמוּ בְלַחֲמִי וּשְׁתוּ בְּיַיִן מָסָכְתִּי״. ״וְכֹל מִשְׁעַן מָיִם״ — אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלֵי אַגָּדָה, שֶׁמּוֹשְׁכִין לִבּוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כַּמַּיִם בְּאַגָּדָה. ״גִּבּוֹר״ — זֶה בַּעַל שְׁמוּעוֹת. ״וְאִישׁ מִלְחָמָה״ — זֶה שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לִישָּׂא וְלִיתֵּן בְּמִלְחַמְתָּהּ שֶׁל תּוֹרָה. ״שׁוֹפֵט״ — זֶה דַּיָּין שֶׁדָּן דִּין אֱמֶת לַאֲמִיתּוֹ. ״נָבִיא״ — כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ. ״קוֹסֵם״ — זֶה מֶלֶךְ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״קֶסֶם עַל שִׂפְתֵי מֶלֶךְ״. ״זָקֵן״ — זֶה שֶׁרָאוּי לִישִׁיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **"Every support of bread"; these are masters of Talmud, as it is stated: "Come, eat of my bread, and drink of the wine that I have mingled"** (Proverbs 9:5). **"And every support of water"; these are the masters of *aggada*, who draw people's hearts like water by means of *aggada*. "The mighty man"; this is the master of halakhic tradition,** one who masters the *halakhot* transmitted to him from his rabbis. **"And the man of war"; this is one who knows how to engage in the discourse of Torah,** generating novel teachings **in the war of Torah. "A judge"; this is a judge who judges a true judgment truthfully. "A prophet"; as it literally indicates. "A diviner"; this is a king.** Why is he called a diviner? **For it is stated: "A divine sentence is on the lips of the king"** (Proverbs 16:10). **"An elder"; this is one fit for the position of head of an academy.**

*Kol mishen lechem* — all support of bread — these are *ba'alei Talmud*, masters of Talmudic analysis, for it is the staff of life; the verse *lechu lachamu be-lachmi* — come eat of my bread — supports this, with a possible wordplay between *lachmi* and *masachti* (*masechet*). *Kol mishen mayim* — support of water — these are *ba'alei aggadah*, masters of homily and lore, for *aggadah* draws the heart like flowing water, though notably not characterized as a necessity of life in the way bread is. *Gibbor* — a mighty person — this refers to one who is a *ba'al shmu'ot*, a master of many traditions. *V-ish milchama* — a man of war — this is one who knows how to conduct *milchamta shel Torah*, the intellectual battle of Torah, skilled in argument and analysis. *Shofet* — a judge — *she-dan din emet le-amito*, one who renders truly just decisions. *Navi* — a prophet — as its plain meaning. *Kosem* — literally a diviner — this is a *melech*, a king, because *kesem al siftei melech*, a kind of charm is on the lips of the king. *Zaken* — an elder — one who is worthy of sitting in the council of sages.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שַׂר חֲמִשִּׁים״ — אַל תִּקְרֵי ״שַׂר חֲמִשִּׁים״, אֶלָּא ״שַׂר חוּמָּשִׁין״. זֶה שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לִישָּׂא וְלִיתֵּן בַּחֲמִשָּׁה חוּמְשֵׁי תוֹרָה. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״שַׂר חֲמִשִּׁים״ — כִּדְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מִכָּאן שֶׁאֵין מַעֲמִידִין מְתוּרְגְּמָן עַל הַצִּבּוּר פָּחוֹת מֵחֲמִשִּׁים שָׁנָה. ״וּנְשׂוּא פָּנִים״ — זֶה שֶׁנּוֹשְׂאִין פָּנִים לְדוֹרוֹ בַּעֲבוּרוֹ. לְמַעְלָה, כְּגוֹן רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן דּוֹסָא. לְמַטָּה, כְּגוֹן רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ בֵּי קֵיסָר.
>
> **Translation:** **"A captain of fifty," do not read it as "*sar ḥamishim*," rather read it as "*sar ḥumashin*"; this is one who knows how to engage in discourse with regard to the five books of [*ḥamisha ḥumshei*] the Torah. Alternatively, "a captain of fifty"** should be understood **in accordance with Rabbi Abbahu, for Rabbi Abbahu said: From here we learn that one may not appoint a disseminator over the public to transmit words of Torah or teachings of the Sages if he is less than fifty years of age. "And the man of favor"; this is one for whose sake favor is shown to his generation.** The Gemara provides different examples of this: Some garner favor **above, such as Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa,** whose prayers for his generation would invariably be answered. Others gain favor **below, for example: Rabbi Abbahu,** who would plead Israel's case **in the house of the emperor.**

*Sar chamishim* — an officer of fifty — this is reread as *sar chumashim*, a master of the five books of Torah, one who can truly analyze and debate the *chamisha chumshei Torah*. An alternative reading from Rabbi Abbahu: one may not appoint a *meturgeman*, a public translator of Torah, who is less than fifty years old — this is a position of great communal responsibility, making the Torah intelligible to the people. *U-nesu fanim* — one of lifted countenance — someone for whose sake heaven or earth shows favor to the entire generation. In heavenly terms: Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa, through whose merit the whole world was sustained. In earthly terms: Rabbi Abbahu, who was honored in the imperial court.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יוֹעֵץ״ — שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לְעַבֵּר שָׁנִים וְלִקְבּוֹעַ חֳדָשִׁים. ״וְחָכָם״ — זֶה תַּלְמִיד הַמַּחְכִּים אֶת רַבּוֹתָיו. ״חֲרָשִׁים״ — בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁפּוֹתֵחַ בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה הַכֹּל נַעֲשִׂין כְּחֵרְשִׁין. ״וְנָבוֹן״ — זֶה הַמֵּבִין דָּבָר מִתּוֹךְ דָּבָר. ״לָחַשׁ״ — זֶה שֶׁרָאוּי לִמְסוֹר לוֹ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה בְּלַחַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **"The counselor";** this is referring to one **who knows how to intercalate years and determine months,** due to his expertise in the phases of the moon and the calculation of the yearly cycle. **"The cunning"; this is a student who makes his rabbis wise** through his questions. **"Charmer [*ḥarashim*]";** this is referring to one so wise **that when he begins speaking matters of Torah, all those listening are as though deaf [*ḥershin*],** as they are unable to comprehend the profundity of his comments. **"The skillful"; this is one who understands something new from something else** he has learned. **"Enchanter [*laḥash*]"; this** is referring to one **who is worthy of having words of the Torah that were given in whispers [*laḥash*],** i.e., the secrets of the Torah, **transmitted to him.**

*Ve-yo'etz* — a counselor — one who knows how to intercalate the years and establish the months, a role requiring deep specialized knowledge. *Ve-chakham* — a wise man — a student who can sharpen his rabbis through his insights and questions. *Charashim* — when he opens his mouth in *divrei Torah* everyone else is silenced, becoming like a *cheresh*, one who cannot speak. *Ve-navon* — understanding — one who can infer one thing from another. *Lachash* — whispered incantations or secrets — one qualified to receive *divrei Torah she-nitnu be-lachash*, the secret Torah transmitted in whispers.

### Torah Interpretation

It is significant that the Gemara interprets nearly every item in this catalogue through the lens of Torah knowledge and leadership. The verse that specifies who may receive *sitrei Torah* — secrets of Torah — as cited earlier in the Gemara (*ein mosrin sitrei Torah ela le-mi she-yesh bo chamisha devarim: sar chamishim ve-nesu fanim ve-yo'etz ve-chakham charashim ve-navon lachash*) uses this exact list. The person worthy of receiving the *lachash*, the esoteric, is the one who possesses all of these qualities.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְנָתַתִּי נְעָרִים שָׂרֵיהֶם״. מַאי ״וְנָתַתִּי נְעָרִים שָׂרֵיהֶם״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁמְּנוֹעָרִין מִן הַמִּצְווֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to interpret this verse: **"And I will make children their princes"** (Isaiah 3:4). The Gemara asks: **What is the meaning of "And I will make children [*ne'arim*] their princes"? Rabbi Elazar said: These are people who are devoid [*meno'arin*] of mitzvot;** such people will become the leaders of the nation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְתַעֲלוּלִים יִמְשְׁלוּ בָּם״ — אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: תַּעֲלֵי בְּנֵי תַעֲלֵי. וְלֹא נִתְקָרְרָה דַּעְתּוֹ, עַד שֶׁאָמַר לָהֶם: ״יִרְהֲבוּ הַנַּעַר בַּזָּקֵן וְהַנִּקְלֶה בַּנִּכְבָּד״ — אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁמְּנוֹעָרִין מִן הַמִּצְווֹת, יִרְהֲבוּ בְּמֵי שֶׁמְּמוּלָּא בְּמִצְווֹת כְּרִמּוֹן. ״וְהַנִּקְלֶה בַּנִּכְבָּד״ — יָבֹא מִי שֶׁחֲמוּרוֹת דּוֹמוֹת עָלָיו כְּקַלּוֹת, וְיִרְהֲבוּ בְּמִי שֶׁקַּלּוֹת דּוֹמוֹת עָלָיו כַּחֲמוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **"And babes [*ta'alulim*] shall rule over them"; Rav Pappa bar Ya'akov said:** *Ta'alulim* means **foxes [*ta'alei*], sons of foxes.** In other words, inferior people both in terms of deeds and in terms of lineage. **And** the prophet Isaiah's **mind was not calmed until he said to them: "The child shall behave insolently against the aged, and the base against the honorable"** (Isaiah 3:5). "The child" [*na'ar*]; **these are people who are devoid of mitzvot,** who **will behave insolently toward one who is as filled with mitzvot as a pomegranate. "And the base [*nikleh*] against the honorable [*nikhbad*]";** this means that **one for whom major [*kaved*] transgressions are like minor ones [*kalot*] in his mind will come and behave insolently with one for whom even minor transgressions are like major ones in his mind.**

The final verses of the catalogue: *ve-natati ne'arim sareihem* — I will place youths as their rulers — these are *benei adam she-meno'arim min ha-mitzvot*, people who are emptied and bare of *mitzvot*, as opposed to the Torah scholars and righteous leaders who were removed. *Ve-ta'alulim yimshelu bam* — and babes will rule over them. The Aramaic word *ta'ala* means fox; Rav Pappa bar Yaakov reads *ta'alulim* as *benei ta'ala*, sons of foxes — lowly people descended from the lowly.

*Ve-lo nitkarerah da'ato* — and still Yeshayahu's anguish was not quieted — until he said: *yirhavu ha-na'ar ba-zaken ve-ha-nikleh ba-nichbad*. The *na'ar* here is again *she-meno'ar min ha-mitzvot*, emptied of *mitzvot*, and *yirhavu be-mi she-memale be-mitzvot ke-rimon* — they will lord over one who is filled with *mitzvot* like a pomegranate. *Ve-ha-nikleh ba-nichbad*: the *nikleh*, the one who treats *chumrot* as *kalot*, the weighty matters of halacha as trivial — that person will lord over *mi she-kalot domot alav ke-chamurot*, the one so pious that even the lightest matters weigh heavily upon him.

### Honesty in Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת כִּשְׁלוֹנָהּ שֶׁל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם — לֹא פָּסְקוּ מֵהֶם בַּעֲלֵי אֲמָנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יִתְפֹּשׂ אִישׁ בְּאָחִיו בֵּית אָבִיו שִׂמְלָה לְךָ קָצִין תִּהְיֶה לָנוּ״. דְּבָרִים שֶׁבְּנֵי אָדָם מִתְכַּסִּין כְּשִׂמְלָה, יֶשְׁנָן תַּחַת יָדֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ketina said: Even at the time of Jerusalem's downfall, trustworthy men did not cease to exist** among its people, **as it is stated: "For a man shall take hold of his brother of the house of his father, You have a cloak, be our ruler"** (Isaiah 3:6). The Gemara explains that they would approach someone and say to him: **Things that people are careful to keep covered as with a cloak,** i.e., words of Torah that are covered and concealed, **are under your hand,** as you are an expert with regard to them.

Rav Ketina then states: *va-afilu be-sha'at kishlonah shel Yerushalayim lo paseku mehem ba'alei emunah* — even at the very moment of Jerusalem's downfall, trustworthy people never ceased from Israel. He derives this from the verse in Yeshayahu describing how, in the depths of the collapse, one man will grab his brother and say: you have a cloak, you should be our ruler. The Gemara reads this as: you have Torah knowledge — things people hide like a garment, not wanting to lend out what is precious — you should be our leader. Even in the catastrophe, they are seeking whoever still retains some Torah, because all the great masters have been removed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַמַּכְשֵׁלָה הַזֹּאת״. מַאי ״וְהַמַּכְשֵׁלָה הַזֹּאת״ — דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין בְּנֵי אָדָם עוֹמְדִין עֲלֵיהֶן אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נִכְשָׁל בָּהֶן — ״יֶשְׁנָן תַּחַת יָדֶךָ״. ״יִשָּׂא בַיּוֹם הַהוּא לֵאמֹר לֹא אֶהְיֶה חוֹבֵשׁ וּבְבֵיתִי אֵין לֶחֶם וְאֵין שִׂמְלָה לֹא תְשִׂימוּנִי קְצִין עָם״. ״יִשָּׂא״ — אֵין יִשָּׂא אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן שְׁבוּעָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תִשָּׂא אֶת שֵׁם ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״. ״לֹא אֶהְיֶה חוֹבֵשׁ״ — לֹא הָיִיתִי מֵחוֹבְשֵׁי בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. ״וּבְבֵיתִי אֵין לֶחֶם וְאֵין שִׂמְלָה״ — שֶׁאֵין בְּיָדִי לֹא מִקְרָא וְלֹא מִשְׁנָה וְלֹא גְּמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the meaning of the end of that verse: "And this stumbling block"** (Isaiah 3:6)? **Things that people cannot grasp unless they have stumbled over them,** as they can be understood only with much effort, **are under your hand. Although they will approach an individual with these statements, he "shall swear that day, saying: I will not be a healer, for in my house there is neither bread nor a cloak; you shall not make me ruler of a people"** (Isaiah 3:7). When the verse states: **"Shall swear [*yissa*]," *yissa* is none other than an expression of an oath, as it is stated: "You shall not take [*tissa*] the name of the Lord your God** in vain" (Exodus 20:6). Therefore, the inhabitant of Jerusalem swears: **"I will not be a healer [*ḥovesh*]"** (Isaiah 3:7), which means: **I was never one of those who sit [*meḥovshei*] in the study hall; "for in my house there is neither bread nor a cloak," as I possess knowledge of neither the Bible, nor Mishna, nor Gemara.** This shows that even at Jerusalem's lowest spiritual ebb, its inhabitants would admit the truth and own up to their complete ignorance.

*U-ve-ha-machshelah ha-zot tachtecha* — and this stumbling under you — *devarim she-ein benei adam omdim aleihem ela im ken nichshal bahem*: areas of Torah one does not truly master unless one has stumbled in them, made mistakes, and thereby truly internalized them. You have even that. Be our leader.

But the man in the verse refuses. He swears — the Gemara reads *va-yisa* as an oath-taking, parallel to *lo tisa et shem Hashem* — and he says: *lo hayiti mi-khoshvei Beit ha-Midrash* — I was never among those who labored in the study hall. I have no Torah. *U-ve-veiti ein lechem ve-ein simla* — I have no bread and no garment — *she-ein be-yadi lo mikra ve-lo mishna ve-lo Gemara*. I know no Scripture, no Mishnah, no Gemara. Do not make me a leader of the people.

### Torah vs. Business

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִי אָמַר לְהוּ גְּמִירְנָא — אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: אֵימָא לַן! הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר גְּמַר וּשְׁכַח. מַאי ״לֹא אֶהְיֶה חוֹבֵשׁ״ — לֹא אֶהְיֶה חוֹבֵשׁ כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But perhaps it is different there, for if he had said: I have learned, they would have said to him: Tell us,** and people do not lie about things that can be easily verified. The Gemara rejects this claim: If he were a liar, **he would have said** that **he learned and forgot,** thereby avoiding shame. **What is the meaning of "I will not be a healer," which seems to imply that he had learned in the past? It means: I will not be a healer at all,** as I have never learned. Consequently, there were trustworthy men in Jerusalem after all.

The Gemara notes that this refusal demonstrates remarkable honesty — and then immediately questions it. Perhaps he had no choice: if he had claimed to know Torah, they would have said *eima lan*, tell us something, and he would have been exposed. But, the Gemara counters, *hava leih lemeimar gamar ve-shakhakh* — he could have said he once learned and forgot. The fact that he says instead *I never learned at all* — that already reflects a deeper level of honesty, to admit one had never studied rather than claiming the dignified excuse of forgetting what was once known.

The comparison is natural and self-evident to anyone who has experienced the difference between being able to say *I don't remember* versus *I never knew*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רָבָּא: לֹא חָרְבָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם עַד שֶׁפָּסְקוּ מִמֶּנָּה בַּעֲלֵי אֲמָנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוֹטְטוּ בְּחוּצוֹת יְרוּשָׁלִַם וּרְאוּ נָא וּדְעוּ וּבַקְשׁוּ בִרְחוֹבוֹתֶיהָ אִם תִּמְצְאוּ אִישׁ אִם יֵשׁ עוֹשֶׂה מִשְׁפָּט מְבַקֵּשׁ אֱמוּנָה וְאֶסְלַח לָהּ״! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה, הָא בְּמַשָּׂא וּמַתָּן. בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה — הֲווֹ, בְּמַשָּׂא וּמַתָּן — לָא הֲווֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **Is that so? But didn't Rava say: Jerusalem was not destroyed until trustworthy men ceased to exist in it, as it is stated: "Run to and fro through the streets of Jerusalem, and see now and know, and seek in its broad places, if you can find a man, if there is any that acts justly, that seeks truth, and I will pardon her"** (Jeremiah 5:1), implying there were no trustworthy people at that time? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** case is referring **to words of Torah,** while **that** case is referring **to commerce. With regard to words of Torah, they were trustworthy; with regard to commerce, they were not.**

Rav Ami had earlier stated that Jerusalem was destroyed only when *ba'alei emunah* completely ceased from it. The Gemara reconciles: *lo kashya — ha be-divrei Torah, ha be-masa u-matan*. There is no contradiction. When it comes to Torah — to honestly representing one's Torah knowledge — people will always remain honest even in the darkest times. When it comes to business and monetary dealings, however, no honest person is left. A striking and sobering distinction.

## Elazar ben Arach

### Teaching Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי שֶׁהָיָה רוֹכֵב עַל הַחֲמוֹר, וְהָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲרָךְ מְחַמֵּר אַחֲרָיו. אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, שְׁנֵה לִי פֶּרֶק אֶחָד בְּמַעֲשֵׂה מֶרְכָּבָה. אָמַר לוֹ, לֹא כָּךְ שָׁנִיתִי לָכֶם: וְלֹא בַּמֶּרְכָּבָה בְּיָחִיד אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הָיָה חָכָם מֵבִין מִדַּעְתּוֹ? אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, תַּרְשֵׁינִי לוֹמַר לְפָנֶיךָ דָּבָר אֶחָד שֶׁלִּמַּדְתַּנִי. אָמַר לוֹ: אֱמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the topic of the Design of the Divine Chariot. **The Sages taught: An incident occurred involving Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai, who was riding on a donkey and was traveling along the way, and his student, Rabbi Elazar ben Arakh, was riding a donkey behind him.** Rabbi Elazar **said to him: My teacher, teach me one chapter in the Design of the Divine Chariot. He said to him:** Have **I not taught you: And one may not expound the Design of the Divine Chariot to an individual, unless he is a Sage who understands on his own accord?** Rabbi Elazar **said to him: My teacher, allow me to say before you one thing that you taught me.** In other words, he humbly requested to recite before him his own understanding of this issue. **He said to him: Speak.**

The Gemara presents a *baraisa* about teaching *Ma'aseh Merkavah*. It concerns Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, who was riding on a donkey while Rabbi Elazar ben Arach — who is known otherwise mainly from *Pirkei Avot* as someone like *ma'ayan hamitgaber*, an ever-strengthening spring — walked behind him. Rabbi Elazar requested: *Rabbi, sheneili perek echad be-Ma'aseh Merkavah* — teach me even one section of the Merkavah. Rabban Yochanan replied: have I not taught you — *lo be-merkava be-yachid*, one may not teach *Merkavah* even to a single individual, *ela im kein haya chakham meivin mida'ato* — unless that person is a sage who can understand on his own?

Rabbi Elazar responded: *tarsheini lomar lefanecha davar echad she-limadtani* — permit me to tell you something that you yourself taught me. The implication is subtle: *I will demonstrate that I am in fact that sage who can understand independently — I will show you what I was able to perceive from what you hinted to me.*

### Elazar's Discourse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד יָרַד רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי מֵעַל הַחֲמוֹר, וְנִתְעַטֵּף, וְיָשַׁב עַל הָאֶבֶן תַּחַת הַזַּיִת. אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, מִפְּנֵי מָה יָרַדְתָּ מֵעַל הַחֲמוֹר? אָמַר: אֶפְשָׁר אַתָּה דּוֹרֵשׁ בְּמַעֲשֵׂה מֶרְכָּבָה, וּשְׁכִינָה עִמָּנוּ, וּמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת מְלַוִּין אוֹתָנוּ, וַאֲנִי אֶרְכַּב עַל הַחֲמוֹר?! מִיָּד פָּתַח רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲרָךְ בְּמַעֲשֵׂה הַמֶּרְכָּבָה וְדָרַשׁ. וְיָרְדָה אֵשׁ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם, וְסִיבְּבָה כׇּל הָאִילָנוֹת שֶׁבַּשָּׂדֶה, פָּתְחוּ כּוּלָּן וְאָמְרוּ שִׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately, Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai alighted from the donkey, and wrapped his head in his cloak in a manner of reverence, and sat on a stone under an olive tree.** Rabbi Elazar **said to him: My teacher, for what reason did you alight from the donkey? He said:** Is it **possible that while you are expounding the Design of the Divine Chariot, and the Divine Presence is with us, and the ministering angels are accompanying us, that I should ride on a donkey? Immediately, Rabbi Elazar ben Arakh began to discuss the Design of the Divine Chariot and expounded, and fire descended from heaven and encircled all the trees in the field, and all the trees began reciting song.**

Rabban Yochanan immediately dismounted from the donkey, wrapped himself in his garment, and sat upon a stone under an olive tree. When Rabbi Elazar asked why he dismounted, Rabban Yochanan replied: *efshar ata doreish be-Ma'aseh Merkavah u-Shechina imanu u-malachei ha-shareit melavin otanu, va-ani erkhav al ha-chamor?* — Is it possible that you will expound *Ma'aseh Merkavah* while the *Shechina* is with us and the ministering angels accompany us, and I should remain riding on the donkey? He had to show proper respect for the Divine Presence that would attend the teaching. Interestingly, Rabbi Elazar had been wise enough to comprehend what Rabban Yochanan was teaching, yet had not fully grasped the power of what was behind that teaching — the degree to which the *Shechina* itself would be drawn to attend.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה שִׁירָה אָמְרוּ — ״הַלְלוּ אֶת ה׳ מִן הָאָרֶץ תַּנִּינִים וְכׇל תְּהוֹמוֹת ... עֵץ פְּרִי וְכׇל אֲרָזִים ... הַלְלוּיָהּ״. נַעֲנָה מַלְאָךְ מִן הָאֵשׁ, וְאָמַר: הֵן הֵן מַעֲשֵׂה הַמֶּרְכָּבָה. עָמַד רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי וּנְשָׁקוֹ עַל רֹאשׁוֹ, וְאָמַר: בָּרוּךְ ה׳ אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּתַן בֵּן לְאַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לְהָבִין וְלַחְקוֹר וְלִדְרוֹשׁ בְּמַעֲשֵׂה מֶרְכָּבָה. יֵשׁ נָאֶה דּוֹרֵשׁ וְאֵין נָאֶה מְקַיֵּים, נָאֶה מְקַיֵּים וְאֵין נָאֶה דּוֹרֵשׁ. אַתָּה נָאֶה דּוֹרֵשׁ וְנָאֶה מְקַיֵּים. אַשְׁרֶיךָ אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ, שֶׁאֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲרָךְ יָצָא מֵחֲלָצֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **What song did they recite? "Praise the Lord from the earth, sea monsters and all depths…fruit trees and all cedars…praise the Lord"** (Psalms 148:7–14). **An angel responded from the fire, saying: This is the very Design of the Divine Chariot,** just as you expounded. **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai stood and kissed Rabbi Elazar ben Arakh on his head, and said: Blessed be God, Lord of Israel, who gave our father Abraham a son like you, who knows how to understand, investigate, and expound the Design of the Divine Chariot. There are some who expound the Torah's verses well but do not fulfill its imperatives well,** and there are some **who fulfill its imperatives well but do not expound its verses well,** whereas **you expound its verses well and fulfill its imperatives well. Happy are you, our father Abraham, that Elazar ben Arakh came from your loins.**

Rabbi Elazar immediately began to expound *Ma'aseh Merkavah*, and as he did, fire came down from heaven and encompassed all the trees of the field; *pitchu kulam ve-amru shira* — they all opened and sang, quoting *Hallelu et Hashem min ha-aretz taninim ve-chol tehomot... eitz pri ve-chol arazim, Halleluyah*. Then an angel from within the fire responded and declared: *hein hein ma'aseh merkavah* — this, precisely this, is *Ma'aseh Merkavah*. The vision is confirmed from above.

This is a striking moment in the Gemara: *Ma'aseh Merkavah* is not merely a matter of correctly interpreting the prophetic visions — it is a practice that brings heaven down to earth, that causes the *Shechina* to descend, fire to appear, and creation itself to burst into song. Just as earlier we saw that human prayer can alter the appearance of the heavenly throne, here the teaching of *Ma'aseh Merkavah* causes the earthly realm to erupt with heavenly fire and praise.

### Yochanan's Praise

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai rose and kissed Rabbi Elazar on his head. He said: *Baruch Hashem Elohei Yisrael she-natan bein le-Avraham Avinu* — Blessed is God who gave such a son to Abraham our father — *she-yodei'a le-havin u-lakhkor ve-lidrosh be-Ma'aseh Merkavah* — who knows how to understand, to investigate, and to expound in *Ma'aseh Merkavah*. The praise continues: *yesh na'eh doreish ve-ein na'eh mekayeim, ve-yesh na'eh mekayeim ve-ein na'eh doreish* — there are those who expound beautifully but do not fulfill beautifully, and those who fulfill but do not expound beautifully — *ata na'eh doreish ve-na'eh mekayeim*, but you do both. The *Maharsha* suggests that *na'eh mekayeim* here means that his exposition was true — it corresponded to reality, to the actual nature of the *Merkavah* — and that is itself the fulfillment.

*Ashrecha Avraham Avinu she-Elazar ben Arach yatza me-chalatzecha* — praised are you, Abraham our father, that Elazar ben Arach came from your loins. The invocation of Abraham is somewhat puzzling; one suggestion made in the discussion is a connection to *Sefer Yetzirah*, associated with Abraham in the tradition of mystical literature. Whatever the precise association, the praise is striking — Rabbi Elazar is being compared favorably not just to his own teacher but placed in the lineage of the first patriarch.

### Yehoshua's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁנֶּאֶמְרוּ הַדְּבָרִים לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, הָיָה הוּא וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַכֹּהֵן מְהַלְּכִים בַּדֶּרֶךְ. אָמְרוּ: אַף אָנוּ נִדְרוֹשׁ בְּמַעֲשֵׂה מֶרְכָּבָה. פָּתַח רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְדָרַשׁ. וְאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם תְּקוּפַת תַּמּוּז הָיָה. נִתְקַשְּׁרוּ שָׁמַיִם בְּעָבִים וְנִרְאֶה כְּמִין קֶשֶׁת בֶּעָנָן, וְהָיוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת מִתְקַבְּצִין וּבָאִים לִשְׁמוֹעַ, כִּבְנֵי אָדָם שֶׁמִּתְקַבְּצִין וּבָאִים לִרְאוֹת בְּמַזְמוּטֵי חָתָן וְכַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **And when these matters,** this story involving his colleague Rabbi Elazar ben Arakh, **were recounted before Rabbi Yehoshua, he was walking along the way with Rabbi Yosei the Priest. They said: We too shall expound the Design of the Divine Chariot. Rabbi Yehoshua began expounding. And that was the day of the summer solstice,** when there are no clouds in the sky. Yet the **heavens became filled with clouds, and there was the appearance of a kind of rainbow in a cloud. And ministering angels gathered and came to listen, like people gathering and coming to see the rejoicing of a bridegroom and bride.**

When the account of this episode was told over to Rabbi Yehoshua, he was walking together with Rabbi Yosei HaKohen. The two of them said: we too have been given an opportunity, let us expound *Ma'aseh Merkavah*. *Patach Rabbi Yehoshua ve-darash* — Rabbi Yehoshua opened and expounded. It was the season of *Tammuz*, the height of summer, yet the sky filled with clouds and a rainbow appeared. *Ve-hayu malachei ha-shareit mitkabtzim u-va'im lishmoa* — and the ministering angels gathered and came to hear, *kivnei adam she-mitkabtzim u-va'im lir'ot be-mazmutei chatan ve-kallah* — like people who gather to see the rejoicing at a wedding celebration.

Why would angels come to hear a human discourse on *Ma'aseh Merkavah* if they presumably know better than any mortal what transpires above? The comparison to *mazmutei chatan ve-kallah* — to wedding celebrations — may offer a partial answer: people attend a wedding celebration not necessarily to learn something new but to witness and share in another's joy, even if they are not themselves experiencing it. So too, the angels come not because they need instruction, but to witness the joy and wonder of human beings accessing and articulating the divine mysteries.

### Third Group Vision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָלָךְ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַכֹּהֵן וְסִיפֵּר דְּבָרִים לִפְנֵי רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, וְאָמַר: אַשְׁרֵיכֶם וְאַשְׁרֵי יוֹלַדְתְּכֶם, אַשְׁרֵי עֵינַי שֶׁכָּךְ רָאוּ. וְאַף אֲנִי וְאַתֶּם, בַּחֲלוֹמִי מְסוּבִּים הַיְינוּ עַל הַר סִינַי, וְנִתְּנָה עָלֵינוּ בַּת קוֹל מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם: עֲלוּ לְכָאן, עֲלוּ לְכָאן! טְרַקְלִין גְּדוֹלִים וּמַצָּעוֹת נָאוֹת מוּצָּעוֹת לָכֶם, אַתֶּם, וְתַלְמִידֵיכֶם, וְתַלְמִידֵי תַלְמִידֵיכֶם מְזוּמָּנִין לְכַת שְׁלִישִׁית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei the Priest went and recited these matters before Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai, who said** to him: **Happy are** all of **you, and happy are** the mothers **who gave birth to you; happy are my eyes that saw this,** students such as these. **As for you and I,** I saw **in my dream** that **we were seated at Mount Sinai, and a Divine Voice came to us from heaven: Ascend here, ascend here, for large halls [***teraklin***] and pleasant couches are made up for you. You, your students, and the students of your students are invited to the third group,** those who will merit to welcome the Divine Presence.

Rabbi Yosei HaKohen then went and reported these events to Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai — the remarkable session that Rabbi Yehoshua had conducted. Rabban Yochanan said: *ashreichem ve-ashrei yoledetchem* — praised are you and praised are those who gave birth to you. *Ashrei einai she-kach ra'u* — praised are my eyes that have seen this, for I saw this same wonder when I engaged in *Ma'aseh Merkavah* with Rabbi Elazar ben Arach, and now I see that you too have had that experience.

He added: *va-af ani va-atem ba-chalomi, mesubim hayinu al Har Sinai* — and even you and I in my dream, I saw us reclining at Har Sinai. The image of *mesubim*, reclining, connects to the verse *va-yechezu et ha-Elokim va-yochlu va-yishtu* — they saw God and ate and drank — referring to those at Sinai who had a vision of God. He is placing himself and his students in that same category of those who ascended and beheld. *Ve-nitnah aleinu bat kol min ha-shamayim* — and a heavenly voice went forth from heaven saying: *alu le-chan, alu le-chan* — come up here, come up here. *Traklin gedolim u-mutzaot na'ot mutzaot lachem* — great halls and beautiful furnishings are laid out for you. *Lachem va-atem ve-talmideichem ve-talmidei talmideichem* — for you, your students, and your students' students. *Mezumanin le-chat shelishit* — you are appointed to be in the third group in God's presence.

What the first and second groups consist of the Gemara does not say — the third is evidently as high as the vision could articulate for them, and even so, it is an extraordinary destination.

It is fitting to close by returning to Rabbi Elazar ben Arach himself, who is known otherwise only for the statement in *Pirkei Avot* that he was like a *ma'ayan hamitgaber* — a spring that continuously strengthens. That characterization fits this narrative precisely: Rabban Yochanan believed he had taught nothing explicitly about the *Merkavah*, and yet Rabbi Elazar drew from whatever had been hinted to him, producing a discourse that brought down heavenly fire and caused the very trees to sing. He was the spring that gathers and strengthens from within, giving back more than it received — the paradigmatic student of *Ma'aseh Merkavah*.
