# Yevamot 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Slaves of Chalalah

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, גְּרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, הִכְנִיסָה לוֹ עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג וְעַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל, עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ בִּתְרוּמָה, עַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל — יֹאכֵלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A widow** married **to a High Priest,** and **a divorcée or a *yevama* who performed *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*]** married **to a common priest** are all unions prohibited by Torah law. If one of these women **brought** with her **into** the marriage **slaves of usufruct [*melog*]** property **or slaves of guaranteed investment,** then the **slaves of usufruct** property **do not partake of *teruma*** but the **slaves of guaranteed investment do partake** of *teruma*.

The *mishnah* at the opening of our discussion addresses the status of slaves belonging to a woman married to a *kohen*. Two categories of property are relevant here. *Nichsei milug* refers to property over which the wife retains title, though the husband enjoys its use; *nichsei tzon barzel* refers to property that has been assessed and formally transferred to the husband's ownership, leaving the wife with only a monetary claim. The *mishnah* focuses on the more interesting case — the slaves she owns through *nichsei milug* — and introduces a complicating factor: if she is an *almanah*, a *gerushah*, a *chalutzah*, or an *almanah l'kohen gadol*, she becomes a *chalalah* through the marriage. As a *chalalah*, she cannot eat *terumah* even by virtue of being married to the *kohen*. The question then becomes: what is the status of her slaves? Can they eat *terumah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג: אִם מֵתוּ — מֵתוּ לָהּ, וְאִם הוֹתִירוּ — הוֹתִירוּ לָהּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב בִּמְזוֹנוֹתָן הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ לֹא יֹאכְלוּ בִּתְרוּמָה. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן עַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל: אִם מֵתוּ — מֵתוּ לוֹ, וְאִם הוֹתִירוּ — הוֹתִירוּ לוֹ, הוֹאִיל וְהוּא חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ יֹאכְלוּ בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And these are slaves of usufruct** property: They are those with regard to whom the couple stipulated that **if** the slaves **die, their death** is **her** loss, **and if they increase** in value, **their increase** is **her** gain. **Although** the husband **is obligated in their sustenance, they do not partake of *teruma*,** as they belong to her, not to him. He owns only the right of their use while he is married to her. **And these are slaves of guaranteed investment:** They are those with regard to whom the couple stipulated that **if they die, their death** is **his** loss, **and if they increase** in value, **their increase** is **his** gain. **Since he bears** financial **responsibility** for compensating her in the event of **their** loss, **they partake of *teruma*,** as they are considered his property.

The *mishnah* rules that they cannot. The *gemara* explains that this follows from the principle governing *kinyano she-kanah kinyan* — that is, the wife herself, who is his *kinyan* of a kind and who owns these slaves, is the conduit through which they eat. If she herself cannot eat *terumah*, the question is whether she can nonetheless enable her slaves to eat.

### Eating Through Whom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְעַבְדֵי מְלוֹג לֹא יֹאכְלוּ בִּתְרוּמָה, אַמַּאי? לֶהֱוֵי כְּקִנְיָנוֹ שֶׁקָּנָה קִנְיָן! דְּתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לְכֹהֵן שֶׁנָּשָׂא אִשָּׁה וְקָנָה עֲבָדִים שֶׁיֹּאכְלוּ בִּתְרוּמָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְכֹהֵן כִּי יִקְנֶה נֶפֶשׁ קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ הוּא יֹאכַל בּוֹ".
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that if a priest married a woman forbidden to him, his wife's **slaves of usufruct** property **do not partake of *teruma*.** The Gemara asks: **Why** is this so? **Let** this case **be like** that of **his acquisition who acquired an acquisition, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived with regard **to a priest who married a woman and acquired slaves that they partake of *teruma*? As it is stated: "But if a priest buys any soul, the purchase of his money, he may eat of it"** (Leviticus 22:11).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן לְאִשָּׁה שֶׁקָּנְתָה עֲבָדִים, וַעֲבָדָיו שֶׁקָּנוּ עֲבָדִים, שֶׁיֹּאכְלוּ בִּתְרוּמָה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְכֹהֵן כִּי יִקְנֶה נֶפֶשׁ קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ הוּא יֹאכַל בּוֹ", קִנְיָנוֹ שֶׁקָּנָה קִנְיָן — אוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived with regard **to** both the **wife** of a priest **who acquired slaves and** a priest's **slaves who acquired slaves, that** the acquired slaves **may** also **partake of *teruma*? As it is stated: "But if a priest buys any soul, the purchase of his money, he may eat of it,"** which is interpreted to mean: If **his acquisition acquired an acquisition,** the latter **partakes** of *teruma*. Here too, since the **slaves of usufruct** property belong to his wife, it should be permitted for them to partake of *teruma*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הָאוֹכֵל — מַאֲכִיל, כֹּל שֶׁאֵין אוֹכֵל — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The principle is that **anyone who** is fit to **partake** of *teruma* can **enable** others **to partake** of *teruma*, and **anyone who does not partake** of *teruma* **cannot enable** others **to partake.** Since the priest's wife in this case does not partake of *teruma*, as her marriage is forbidden, her slaves do not partake of *teruma* either.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וַהֲרֵי עָרֵל, וְכׇל הַטְּמֵאִים שֶׁאֵינָן אוֹכְלִין, וּמַאֲכִילִין! הָתָם, פּוּמַּיְיהוּ כָּאֵיב לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that one who does not partake of *teruma* **cannot enable** others **to partake? But aren't** there the cases of a priest who is **uncircumcised** because it was considered too dangerous for him **and all impure** priests, **who do not partake** of *teruma*, **and** yet **they enable** their wives and slaves **to partake** of *teruma*? The Gemara answers: The difference is that **there,** in those cases, there is no inherent disqualification rendering them unfit to partake of *teruma*. The hindrance to their partaking of *teruma* is tantamount to a situation where **their mouths hurt,** and that is why they refrain from eating *teruma*. They retain, however, the fundamental right to partake of *teruma*, and therefore they can enable others to partake as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי מַמְזֵר, שֶׁאֵין אוֹכֵל, וּמַאֲכִיל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't** there the case of **a son born from an incestuous or adulterous relationship [*mamzer*]**, **who does not partake** of *teruma* yet **enables** others to partake? If an Israelite woman was married to a priest and was subsequently widowed or divorced, and a child from that union married a *mamzer* and then had a child, in that case, even if the woman's child is dead, she partakes of *teruma* due to her grandchild, as she has a living descendant from a priest, although that descendant is a *mamzer*. Although this child does not partake of *teruma*, he enables his grandmother to partake of it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: קִנְיָן אוֹכֵל קָאָמַר, קִנְיָן אוֹכֵל — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said** that the above principle **is referring** to the case of **an acquisition who partakes** of *teruma*. If the **acquisition** of a priest **partakes** of *teruma*, he **enables** others **to partake,** whereas an acquisition **who does not partake,** e.g., his forbidden wife, **cannot enable** others **to partake.**

The *gemara* raises an apparent challenge to the principle that those who eat do so through her. The case involves a *mamzer* — and the word *mamzer* is used here to make the example as extreme as possible, though the core case could involve any non-*kohen*. Suppose a *kohen* has a daughter who marries a *mamzer* or a *yisrael*, and they have a child. That child is not a *kohen* — the father being a non-*kohen* — and would never himself eat *terumah*. Yet if the *kohen* dies and there remains such a grandchild descended from the marriage, the widow may continue eating *terumah* for as long as that grandchild is alive, even after she has collected her *ketubah*. The grandchild enables her to eat even though the grandchild itself cannot eat and is, in an extreme case, even a *mamzer*.

This seems to challenge the rule that only one who eats can enable others to eat. The *gemara* cites this case with the phrase *va-harei mamzer she-ein ochel u-maachil* — "behold, the *mamzer* who does not eat yet enables." Should this not demonstrate that someone who cannot eat can still enable others to?

### Kinyan Ochel

Ravina responds sharply: *kinyan ochel ka'amar* — the relevant principle concerns specifically a *kinyan* that eats. The case of the grandchild enabling the grandmother to eat is categorically different: the grandmother eats not because the grandchild "enables" her in the technical sense relevant to property ownership, but because the grandchild's existence keeps her connected to the *kohen*'s family. That is a distinct mechanism entirely. The operative principle for our *mishnah* remains: *kinyan ochel ma'achil, kinyan she'eino ochel eino ma'achil* — only a *kinyan* that itself eats can feed others. Since the wife is called a *kinyan* in this context, and since she cannot eat *terumah* as a *chalalah*, her slaves cannot eat through her. This is the first explanation of the *mishnah*.

### Rava's Gezerah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא מֵיכָל אָכְלִי, וְרַבָּנַן הוּא דְּגָזְרִי בְּהוּ, כְּדֵי שֶׁתֹּאמַר: אֲנִי אֵינִי אוֹכֶלֶת, עֲבָדַי אֵינָן אוֹכְלִין — זוֹנָה הִיא אֶצְלוֹ?! הִלְכָּךְ אָתֵי לְאַפּוֹקַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said** a different solution. **By Torah** law, the forbidden wife's slaves indeed **partake** of *teruma*, as they are included in the category of: His acquisition who acquired an acquisition. **And it was the Sages who issued a decree** prohibiting **them** from partaking of *teruma*, **so that** the woman unlawfully married to a priest **would say: I do not partake** of his *teruma* and **my slaves do not partake** of it either, so that she will realize that she is not a valid wife, but rather **she is** like **a prostitute to him.** Her husband will **therefore come to divorce her,** which is the desired outcome.

Rava offers a second explanation. He acknowledges that in principle, the slaves of the *chalalah* ought to be entitled to eat *terumah*, because indirectly they are connected to the *kohen* and their eating flows through him rather than purely through her. *Mide'oraisa me'achlei* — by Torah law they would be permitted to eat. However, *verabbanan hu de-gazru behu* — the Rabbis enacted a prohibition against their eating in this case, as an incentive to dissolution of the forbidden marriage.

The logic is as follows: because the *kohen* is married to a *gerushah*, *chalutza*, or the *kohen gadol* to an *almanah*, a situation the Rabbis find deeply objectionable, they sought to create pressure toward divorce. The wife can be expected to say: *ani eini ochelet* — I myself cannot eat *terumah*; *avadai einam ochlim* — my slaves cannot eat *terumah*. She is, as it were, like a *zonah* to him, *zonah hi etzlo* — not fully a wife in the sense of enjoying the privileges of wifehood. This sense of exclusion and non-belonging, says Rava, was designed by the Rabbis — *va'ati lefuka* — to bring about a divorce. The slaves could in principle eat; the Rabbis withheld that entitlement as a form of social and legal pressure.

### Rav Ashi's Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא תַּאֲכִיל לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** a different reason for the prohibition: It is a rabbinic **decree lest she have** those slaves **partake** of *teruma* even **after** the **death** of her husband the priest. As long as he is alive, they are permitted to partake of *teruma*, as she is considered his acquisition and they belong to her. Once he dies, she is no longer his acquisition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְכֹהֵן לֹא תַּאֲכִיל, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא תַּאֲכִיל לְאַחַר מִיתָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However, if** that is **so,** that the decree is lest she have those slaves partake of *teruma* after her husband's death, any **Israelite woman who marries a priest should not enable** her slaves of usufruct property **to partake** of *teruma* either, due to the same rabbinic **decree, lest she have them partake** of *teruma* **after** her husband's **death.**

Rav Ashi agrees with the premise that the slaves could in principle eat, but offers a different reason for the rabbinic prohibition: *gezerah shema ta'achilah achar misah* — a decree lest she continue feeding them *terumah* even after the *kohen* dies. Her entitlement to feed them *terumah* is entirely contingent on her husband's status; once he dies, that entitlement ceases. If we permit her to feed them during the marriage, we fear she will continue the practice after his death.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: that concern should apply universally. Every Jewish woman married to a *kohen* might continue feeding her slaves *terumah* after his death — should we therefore prohibit all such wives from feeding their slaves? Rav Ashi withdraws that formulation and offers a refined version.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בְּאַלְמָנָה כֹּהֶנֶת, דְּאָתְיָא לְאוֹרוֹיֵי: מֵעִיקָּרָא אֲכַלִי בִּתְרוּמָה דְּבֵי נָשָׁא, אִינְּסֵבִי לֵיהּ לְהַאי — אֲכַלִי בִּתְרוּמָה דְגַבְרַאי, וְהַשְׁתָּא הֲדַרִי לִי לְמִילְּתַאי קַמַּיְיתָא. וְלָא יָדְעָה דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָא שַׁוֵּיתַהּ לְנַפְשַׁהּ חֲלָלָה, הַשְׁתָּה שַׁוֵּיתַהּ לְנַפְשַׁהּ חֲלָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said** that the decree is dealing **with a widowed priestess,** the daughter of a priest, who then married the High Priest, **as she is likely to rationalize** enabling her slaves to partake of *teruma* after the death of the High Priest as follows: **Initially,** my slaves **partook of the *teruma* of my father's house. I** then **married this** man, **and they partook of the *teruma* of my husband. And now** that my husband died, **I have returned to the original circumstance,** and therefore they may once again partake of my father's *teruma*. **And she does not realize that** this is not so, as **initially she did not render herself a woman disqualified from marrying a priest [*ḥalala*],** but **now,** by marrying a High Priest unlawfully, **she rendered herself a *ḥalala*,** and both she and her slaves do not partake of *teruma* even upon returning to her father's house.

### Almanah Kohenes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח אַלְמָנָה כֹּהֶנֶת, אַלְמָנָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? בְּאַלְמְנוּתַהּ לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This **works out well** as an explanation of the mishna, with regard to **a widowed priestess.** However, if that **widow** who married a High Priest was **an Israelite woman, what can be said?** There is no reason for the decree in that case. The Gemara answers: **With regard to widowhood, the Sages did not distinguish** between one type of widow and another. Once they issued a decree due to one widow, they applied it to all widows.

The *gemara* asks: this reasoning accounts for the case of the *almanah kohenes*, the *bas kohen* who married the *kohen gadol*. But what of the *almanah bas yisrael* — the woman who is not a *bas kohen*? She has no prior entitlement from her father's house; why should the same prohibition apply to her? The *gemara* answers: *be-manusa lo pligi rabbanan* — where there is a specific case that genuinely warrants the decree, the Rabbis did not distinguish among cases. *O lo plug* — they enacted a uniform prohibition across all these situations rather than creating distinctions that might be confused in practice.

The upshot is that we have two general approaches. The first, associated with Ravina, is that the slaves eat only through the wife, and since she cannot eat, they cannot either — this is the *de'oraisa* position. The second, associated with Rava and Rav Ashi, acknowledges that in principle the slaves eat through the *kohen*, but a rabbinic *gezerah* was imposed for the reasons described. In either case the practical ruling is that the slaves of a *chalalah* do not eat *terumah*.

## Tzon Barzel Debate

### Property Assessment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמַּכְנֶסֶת שׁוּם לְבַעְלָהּ, הִיא אוֹמֶרֶת: כֵּלַי אֲנִי נוֹטֶלֶת, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: דָּמִים אֲנִי נוֹתֵן, הַדִּין עִם מִי? רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר:
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated:** With regard to a woman **who brings appraised,** guaranteed **property into her** marital contract with her **husband,** he is obligated to return it at the conclusion of the marriage. Upon collection of her marriage contract, e.g., following divorce, if **she says: I am taking my belongings, and he says: I am** willing to **give you** only their **monetary** value, **the *halakha*** favors **whom? Rav Yehuda said:**

The *gemara* introduces a new topic with the word *itmar* — "it was stated" — signaling an *amoraic* dispute. The subject is *nichsei tzon barzel*: property a woman brings into the marriage whose value is formally assessed — *shum* — and recorded in the *ketubah*. The *ketubah* lists the items and assigns them a total monetary value, and the husband accepts full financial responsibility for that amount. If the property appreciates, he benefits; if it depreciates or is lost, he bears the loss. What the wife is guaranteed is the return of the assessed monetary value.

The practical question arises when the husband dies or the marriage is dissolved and the actual property is still available — the original field, the original slaves, the original vessels. Can the wife demand the return of the specific items? Or has the husband taken absolute title, leaving her only a monetary claim against the estate?

### Title vs. Debt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַדִּין עִמָּהּ, וְרַב אַמֵּי אָמַר: הַדִּין עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The *halakha*** favors **her;** she may take the belongings. **And Rav Ami said: The *halakha*** favors **him;** he may retain the items and return their value.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר הַדִּין עִמָּהּ — מִשּׁוּם שֶׁבַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ דִּידַהּ הָוֵי. רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר: הַדִּין עִמּוֹ — כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר מָר: אִם מֵתוּ — מֵתוּ לוֹ, וְאִם הוֹתִירוּ — הוֹתִירוּ לוֹ, הוֹאִיל וְחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן — יֹאכֵלוּ. אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: מִי קָתָנֵי "וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ"? "הוֹאִיל וְחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן" קָתָנֵי, וּלְעוֹלָם לָאו דִּידֵיהּ נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rav Yehuda said** that **the *halakha*** favors **her because** they are **the assets of her paternal family,** whose prestige will suffer if they aren't returned. Therefore, **they are hers. Rabbi Ami said that the *halakha*** favors **him, since the Master said** in the mishna, with regard to guaranteed property: **If they die, their death** is **his** loss, **and if they increase** in value, **their increase** is **his** gain. **Since he bears** financial **responsibility for their** loss, **they partake** of *teruma*. Apparently, the slaves belong to the husband. Therefore, he is obligated to return only their monetary value. **Rav Safra said** in rejection of Rabbi Ami's reasoning: **Does** the mishna **teach** that **they are his?** It **teaches** only that **he bears** financial **responsibility for their** loss, **but actually they are not his.**

This is precisely the dispute between Rav Yehuda and Rav Ami. Rav Yehuda rules *hadin immah* — the law is with her; she may demand the return of the specific property. Rav Ami rules *hadin immo* — the law is with him; she holds only a monetary debt and cannot compel return of the items themselves.

Rav Yehuda's reasoning is formulated as *mishum shevach beis aviha* — on account of the benefit of her father's house. This is the property that came with her from her father as a dowry; she ought to be entitled to reclaim it. Rav Ami reasons from the *mishnah* itself, which states explicitly regarding *nichsei tzon barzel*: *im mesu mesu lo, ve-im hokiru hokiru lo* — if they die, it is his loss; if they appreciate, it is his gain. This language, he argues, demonstrates that the property entered his possession absolutely, *bi-rshusei kayami* — it stands in his domain, *u-ch'didei damu* — and is treated as fully his.

### Rental Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הֵיכָא דְּחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן אָכְלִי בִּתְרוּמָה? וְהָתְנַן: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁשָּׂכַר פָּרָה מִכֹּהֵן — הֲרֵי זֶה יַאֲכִילֶנָּה כַּרְשִׁינֵּי תְרוּמָה. כֹּהֵן שֶׁשָּׂכַר פָּרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמְּזוֹנוֹתֶיהָ עָלָיו — לָא יַאֲכִילֶנָּה כַּרְשִׁינֵּי תְרוּמָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do they partake of *teruma* wherever he bears** financial **responsibility for their** loss, even if they are not his actual possession? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Terumot* 11:9): **An Israelite who rented a cow from a priest may feed it vetches [*karshinin*] of *teruma*,** since the animal belongs to a priest? With regard to **a priest who rented a cow from an Israelite, although** its feed **is** incumbent **upon him, he may not feed it vetches of *teruma*,** as it does not belong to him. This indicates that the requirement for enabling an acquisition to eat *teruma* is possession, not responsibility.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא? נְהִי נָמֵי דִּמְחַיֵּיב בִּגְנֵבָה וַאֲבֵדָה, בְּאוֹנְסֶיהָ בְּכַחְשָׁהּ וּבְנִפְחַת דָּמֶיהָ — מִי מִיחַיַּיב? הָא לָא דָּמְיָא אֶלָּא לְסֵיפָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁשָּׁם פָּרָה מִכֹּהֵן, לָא יַאֲכִילֶנָּה כַּרְשִׁינֵּי תְרוּמָה. אֲבָל כֹּהֵן שֶׁשָּׁם פָּרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל — יַאֲכִילֶנָּה כַּרְשִׁינֵּי תְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **And** how can **you understand** that the case of the rented cow is parallel to the case of guaranteed property? **Though** the renter **is indeed liable for theft and loss, is he liable for unavoidable accidents, for emaciation,** i.e., the cow became thinner or weaker for any reason, **or for** any other **decrease in its value?** Certainly he is not. In fact, the case of guaranteed property **is similar only to the latter clause** of that mishna: In the case of **an Israelite who appraised a cow** upon renting it **from a priest** under an arrangement where he guaranteed its value to the owner, he **may not feed it vetches of *teruma*,** as it is considered his own. **However, a priest who appraised a cow** upon renting it **from an Israelite may feed it vetches of *teruma*.** This indicates that guaranteed property is considered the acquisition of its recipient with regard to enabling it to eat *teruma*.

The *gemara* attempts to test whether formal liability (*chiyuv achrayus*) alone is sufficient to give a person such a relationship to property that the property's slaves could eat *teruma* on his account — without necessarily establishing outright ownership. If we take seriously the claim that *chayav be-achrayusan* establishes this relationship without full title, we should be able to find a parallel. The *gemara* brings a *mishnah*: a *yisrael* who rents a cow from a *kohen* may feed it *teruma* fodder, because the cow remains the *kohen*'s and he has the relevant interest in it. But a *kohen* who rents a cow from a *yisrael* — even though *mezonoseha alav*, he is responsible for feeding it and bears liability for certain losses — nevertheless *lo ya'achilennah karshinei terumah*, he may not feed it *teruma* fodder. The cow is still the *yisrael*'s. Rental liability is simply insufficient to confer the kind of interest that enables feeding *teruma*.

### Middle Zone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף בְּשִׁילְהֵי פִּרְקֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן וְיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, וְתַנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי. תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי: עַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל יוֹצְאִין בְּשֵׁן וָעַיִן. לָאִישׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לָאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba and Rav Yosef sat at the conclusion of Rav Naḥman's sermon, and they sat and said: It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda, and it is taught** in another *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami. It is taught in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami** in the following *baraita*: If a slave's owner strikes him and knocks out his tooth or blinds him in an eye, he is set free. **Slaves of guaranteed investment go** free **at** the loss of **a tooth or an eye** caused **by the husband, but not** at such loss caused **by the wife.** This indicates that they belong to the husband.

The *gemara* responds that the husband's relationship to the *tzon barzel* property is not comparable to a renter's. A renter bears liability only for theft and loss; he is not responsible for depreciation due to market changes, normal wear, or reduction in market value. The *tzon barzel* relationship is categorically different: no matter what happens — whether the market changes, whether the property deteriorates through ordinary use, whether the decrease is anyone's fault or not — the husband must restore the full assessed value. Conversely, any increase in value belongs entirely to him.

This sliding scale of liability raises the question acutely. The *gemara* is exploring whether there exists a legal middle zone: the wife retains formal title, but the husband has such comprehensive rights and obligations with respect to the property — all gains his, all losses his regardless of fault — that the slaves can eat *teruma* through his relationship to them, even without his having acquired outright ownership. The *gemara* presses: *v'chol heicha de-chayav be-achrayusan achli bi-terumah?* — does any degree of comprehensive liability trigger the right to eat? The rental cases show low-level liability is insufficient. The question is whether this uniquely intensive relationship suffices to permit *teruma* eating without constituting full ownership.

### Two Braitot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: הַמַּכְנֶסֶת שׁוּם לְבַעְלָהּ, אִם רָצָה הַבַּעַל לִמְכּוֹר — לֹא יִמְכּוֹר. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ הִכְנִיס לָהּ שׁוּם מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, אִם רָצָה הַבַּעַל לִמְכּוֹר — לֹא יִמְכּוֹר. מָכְרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם לְפַרְנָסָה, זֶה הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וְאָמַר: הַבַּעַל מוֹצִיא מִיַּד הַלָּקוֹחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, **it is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda:** When a woman **brings appraised,** guaranteed **property** into her marriage **to her husband, if the husband wishes to sell** it, **he may not sell** it, as it belongs to her. **And not only** that, but **even** when her husband **brought** property **into** the marriage and added it to **her** dowry as **an appraised,** guaranteed gift **of his own,** even **if the husband wishes to sell** that gift **he may not sell** it. With regard to a case in which **either** the husband or wife unlawfully **sold** this property **for subsistence,** there **was an incident** like **this** that came **before Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and he said:** Although **the husband** executed the sale, **he may repossess** the property **from the purchasers,** as the sale is void.

Rava and Rav Yosef were sitting together at the conclusion of Rav Nachman's *drasha*, and in their discussion they noted that the sources appear to point in both directions. One *braita* supports Rav Ami: *avdei tzon barzel yotzin be-shen va-ayin la-ish, aval lo la-ishah* — slaves brought in as *tzon barzel* go free if the husband knocks out their eye or tooth, but not if the wife does the same. This strongly implies the husband is considered their master — he is the one whose act of violence triggers the law of *shen va-ayin*. If she retained genuine title, why would her striking not also trigger their release?

The second *braita* supports Rav Yehuda: *ha-machneses shum le-ba'alah im ratza ba'al limkor lo yimkor* — if a wife brings in assessed property and the husband wishes to sell it, he may not do so. If he held absolute title, he would plainly be entitled to sell his own property. The fact that he cannot sell it — and that the *ma'aseh* before Rabban Gamliel affirmed that any such sale is void and the husband must retrieve the property from the purchasers — demonstrates that she retains rights in the objects themselves, not merely a monetary claim.

## Rava's Ruling

### She Retrieves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַב יְהוּדָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: וְהָתַנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי! אַף עַל גַּב דְּתַנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, מִסְתַּבְּרָא טַעְמָא דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁבַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that **Rav Naḥman said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda. Rava said to Rav Naḥman: But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami?** He replied: **Although it is taught in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami, Rav Yehuda's rationale,** that the wife may take the objects in question **because** they are **assets of her paternal family** and their complete removal from her domain would hurt the family's prestige, **is** more **reasonable.**

Rava, citing Rav Nachman, rules: *halacha ke-Rav Yehuda* — the law follows Rav Yehuda. The wife is entitled to retrieve the specific property she brought in. The *braita* showing that the husband cannot sell the property without her consent was evidently persuasive.

### Shen V'Ayin

Rava acknowledges the counter-*braita*, which seemed to prove Rav Ami's position by stating that only the husband's violence triggers the slave's freedom. He addresses this directly: *af al gav de-tanya ke-vassa de-Rabbi Ami, mistabra ta'ama de-Rav Yehuda mishum shevach beis aviha* — even though the *braita* appears to favor Rav Ami, the underlying rationale of Rav Yehuda's position is more compelling, because this property came from her father's house and she ought to be entitled to reclaim it. Tosafot, for their part, find the *shen va-ayin braita* less than a decisive proof for Rav Ami in any case: just as the slaves eat *terumah* by virtue of the husband's comprehensive relationship to them, so too the *shen va-ayin* law might operate through him without that implying full title.

### Cloak Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דְּעַיִּילָה לֵיהּ לְגַבְרַאּ אִיצְטְלָא דְמֵילָתָא בִּכְתוּבְּתַהּ. שְׁכֵיב, שַׁקְלוּהָ יַתְמֵי וּפַרְסוּהָ אַמִּיתָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates an incident: **A certain woman brought into** her marriage to her **husband a robe [*itztela*] of fine wool [*meileta*],** which was deemed guaranteed property **by her marriage contract.** Her husband subsequently **died,** and **the orphans took** that robe **and spread it over the corpse** as a shroud. The woman demanded that the robe be returned to her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר רָבָא: קַנְיַיהּ מִיתָנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ נָאנַאי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: וְהָאָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַב יְהוּדָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי לָא מוֹדֶה רַב יְהוּדָה דִּמְחוּסַּר גּוּבְיָינָא. וְכֵיוָן דִּמְחוּסַּר גּוּבְיָינָא — בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: The dead has acquired it,** as deriving benefit from anything consecrated for the dead is prohibited. **Nanai, son of Rav Yosef, son of Rava, said to Rav Kahana: But didn't Rava say** that **Rav Naḥman said** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda?** Accordingly, the woman's robe must be returned. **He said to him: Doesn't Rav Yehuda admit that** the robe **has not yet been collected? And since it has not yet been collected, it remains in his possession,** and his inheritors can render its use as a burial shroud prohibited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הֶקְדֵּשׁ, חָמֵץ, וְשִׁחְרוּר — מַפְקִיעִין מִידֵי שִׁעְבּוּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that in this regard **Rava** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rava said: Consecration** of property, the prohibition against benefiting from **leavened bread** on Passover, **and** the **manumission** of a slave **release** the property **from a lien.** If someone placed an asset under a lien for his debt and subsequently consecrated it; or if the asset under lien is leavened bread and the festival of Passover arrived; or if the asset is a slave and he freed him, the lien is released, and the creditor must claim his debt from the debtor's other property. In the case of the robe as well, because it was placed over the corpse, it was consecrated for the dead. Consequently, it is prohibited to derive benefit from it. Therefore, it is released to the woman from under the lien.

A *ma'aseh* illustrates the practical limits of Rav Yehuda's ruling. A woman brought into her marriage a fine wool cloak as part of her *nichsei tzon barzel*, recorded in her *ketubah*. Her husband died. Before she had the opportunity to claim the cloak, the orphans seized it and spread it over the dead body, using it as a shroud. Once an object is designated and used as a shroud for a deceased, it becomes *assur be-hana'ah* — prohibited from benefit.

Rava ruled: *kanya misna* — the dead body acquires it. The cloak has changed its identity and she loses any right to that specific object, though she remains entitled to its monetary value from the estate. A grandson of Rava challenged: do we not rule like Rav Yehuda, that the property is hers? If so, the orphans had no right to use her property as a shroud, and their doing so should not affect its status. The response is decisive.

### Requires Collection

The reply: *mi lo modeh Rabbi Yehuda de-mechusar gubyanya?* — even Rav Yehuda concedes that the property requires active collection before it reverts to her control. Rav Yehuda's ruling does not mean that the property is automatically, at every moment, fully under her title and control. He holds that she has a right to reclaim it — but that right must be exercised; it must be collected. *De-kivun de-mechusar gubyanya bi-rshusei kai* — since it still requires collection, it remains under the control of the husband's heirs.

The conceptual picture that emerges is subtle: she may hold some form of title, or she may hold only a superior claim of retrieval, but in neither case does the property automatically revert to her domain upon the husband's death. Until she acts to collect it, it remains under the heirs' effective control. And because of that, the heirs were in a position to use it as a shroud, triggering *asur be-hana'ah* and extinguishing her specific rights in that object.

Rava's reasoning invokes his broader principle: *hekdesh, chametz, u-shichrur motzi'in mi-yedei shibud* — that sanctification of property (*hekdesh*), the transformation of *chametz* during Passover into something *assur be-hana'ah*, and the emancipation of a slave all operate to dissolve existing liens or encumbrances upon property by fundamentally transforming the identity of the object. Here, using the cloak as a shroud similarly transforms its identity — just as *chametz* that becomes forbidden during Passover loses its character as encumbered property, so too this cloak, once assigned to the dead, loses whatever lien or claim she held over it.

## Appreciation Cases

### Two Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הִכְנִיסָה לוֹ שְׁנֵי כֵלִים בְּאֶלֶף זוּז, וְשָׁבְחוּ וְעָמְדוּ עַל שְׁנֵי אֲלָפִים. אֶחָד נוֹטַלְתּוֹ בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ, וְאֶחָד נוֹתֶנֶת דָּמִים וְנוֹטַלְתּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁבַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** If the wife **brought** with her **into** the marriage **two belongings** of guaranteed investment worth **one thousand dinars, and they appreciated** until **they stood at two thousand, one** of them **she collects as** payment of **her marriage contract,** as it is now worth her dowry of one thousand dinars. **And** as for the other **one, she pays its monetary** value **and takes it** from her husband **because** it is **an asset of her paternal family.**

Rav Yehuda presents the following scenario at the top of 67a: a wife brings into the marriage two vessels assessed at one thousand *zuz*. By the time of divorce or the husband's death, they have appreciated and are now worth two thousand.

### Shevach Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן — שֶׁבַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ דִּידַהּ הָוֵי? הָא אַמְרַהּ רַב יְהוּדָה חֲדָא זִימְנָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּמָטְיָא לְמִשְׁקַל בִּכְתוּבְּתַהּ, אֲבָל מִיתָּן דְּמֵי וּמִישְׁקָל — לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is he teaching us?** Is it that **assets of her paternal family are hers? Rav Yehuda** already **said this once,** in his previous statement. The Gemara answers: The latter statement was necessary as well, **lest you say** that **this applies** only **where she comes to collect her marriage contract,** which is rightfully hers, **but to give money and take** assets that are worth beyond what her husband owes her, you might say that she may **not** do so, although the property in question is an asset of her paternal family. Rav Yehuda therefore **teaches us** that she may take all of the assets of her paternal family and pay for what they are worth beyond her husband's debt to her.

The ruling is: *echad notalah bi-chtuvtasah* — she takes one vessel back as part of her *ketubah* entitlement. *Ve-echad nosenet damim ve-notalah mipnei shevach beis aviha* — and the second vessel she is also entitled to demand, paying the difference in value, on account of *shevach beis aviha*, the benefit of her father's house.

The *gemara* asks: what is the novelty here? Didn't Rav Yehuda already establish that she can retrieve her property? The answer: one might have thought that her right to retrieve extends only up to the original assessed value — that is, she can demand the vessels back insofar as they satisfy her monetary entitlement, but not insofar as they have appreciated beyond that value. Perhaps she should be entitled to only one of the two vessels, since one is now worth the full original thousand. The ruling teaches that *mipnei shevach beis aviha* she is entitled to retrieve both the objects she brought in and to pay the appreciated difference — not merely to receive the monetary equivalent. The same logic applies in reverse: if the property has lost value, she retrieves all of it and the estate compensates her for the shortfall.

## Fetus & Eating

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנִּיסֵּת לְכֹהֵן וּמֵת, וְהִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ עֲבָדֶיהָ בִּתְרוּמָה, מִפְּנֵי חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר, שֶׁהָעוּבָּר פּוֹסֵל. וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **an Israelite woman who married a priest and he died and left her pregnant, her slaves** of guaranteed investment **may not partake of *teruma*** during her pregnancy, **due to the share of the fetus,** as an inheritor of his father, in the ownership of the slaves. In the opposite case, **where** the Israelite husband of a priest's daughter died and left her pregnant, **the fetus disqualifies** her from partaking of *teruma*. However, in the current case, the fetus **does not enable** its mother or the slaves **to partake** of *teruma*, despite the fact that it is the child of a priest. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מֵאַחַר שֶׁהֵעַדְתָּ לָנוּ עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן — אַף בַּת כֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן וּמֵת, וְהִנִּיחָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ עֲבָדֶיהָ בִּתְרוּמָה, מִפְּנֵי חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him: Since you testified before us about** the case of **an Israelite woman** who was married **to a priest,** in the case of **the daughter of a priest** who was married **to a priest and he died and left her pregnant, her slaves should not partake of *teruma* either, due to the fetus's share.** The same *halakha* should apply whether the woman is an Israelite or the daughter of a priest.

The *mishnah* turns to a related but distinct problem. A *bas yisrael* — a non-*kohen* woman — was married to a *kohen*, and the *kohen* dies leaving her pregnant. When a *kohen* dies, the general rule is that if he leaves behind a child from that marriage, the widow continues to eat *terumah* through the child. But here the child is not yet born. What is the status of the slaves?

The *mishnah* rules *lo yokhlu avadeha bi-terumah mipnei chelko shel ubar* — the slaves may not eat *terumah* on account of the portion of the *ubar* (the fetus). The reasoning requires unpacking. The *kohen* died leaving his estate to his heirs. If he left sons, those sons are the heirs and are co-owners of the slaves; the slaves eat *terumah* by virtue of those sons. But here, the *ubar* is among the heirs — indeed, if the child is born and is male, he will inherit a portion. The *ubar* is thus a partial owner of the slaves. Since the *ubar* cannot yet cause the slaves to eat *terumah* — he is unborn — his partial ownership prevents the slaves from eating, because that portion of the slaves that is owned by him is not entitled to eat. Ten percent owned by the *ubar* is not entitled; and since the slaves are partially owned by one who cannot enable their eating, none of them may eat.

Furthermore, the *mishnah* notes that the *ubar* also does not enable his mother to eat: she too cannot eat *terumah* on account of an unborn child. This produces the striking result that the *ubar* *posel* — he disqualifies — and yet *eino ma'achil* — he does not feed. He is the worst of both legal worlds: his existence as a partial owner creates a disqualification, but his unborn status prevents him from conferring any entitlement.

### Rabbi Yosi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אוֹכְלִים. הִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אֵין אוֹכְלִים. הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים, וְהִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג אוֹכְלִים כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהִיא אוֹכֶלֶת, עַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ, מִפְּנֵי חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר. שֶׁהָעוּבָּר פּוֹסֵל וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If the priest who was married to an Israelite woman and died **left children,** both the slaves of usufruct property **and** the slaves of guaranteed investment **may partake** of *teruma*. The slaves of guaranteed investment are owned by the children, who are priests, and the slaves of usufruct property are owned by the woman, who partakes of *teruma* due to her children. If **he left** his wife **pregnant** and did not leave children, both **these** slaves **and those** slaves **may not partake** of *teruma*. If **he left children and left her pregnant,** the **slaves of usufruct** property who belong to her **partake** of *teruma* **just as she partakes** due to her children. However, the **slaves of guaranteed investment,** who are inherited by the children, **may not partake, due to the fetus's share,** as it too inherits them, **as a fetus can disqualify** one from partaking of *teruma* **but it cannot not enable** one **to partake.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.**

*Divrei Rabbi Yosi* — this is the ruling of Rabbi Yosi. Rabbi Yosi formulates the principle: *ha-ubar posel ve-eino ma'achil* — the fetus invalidates and does not feed. He prevents the slaves from eating by virtue of his partial ownership, while himself being unable to enable anyone to eat.

### Kohenes Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מֵאַחַר שֶׁהֵעַדְתָּ לָנוּ עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן — אַף בַּת כֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן וּמֵת, וְהִנִּיחָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ עֲבָדֶיהָ בִּתְרוּמָה, מִפְּנֵי חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him: Since you testified before us about** the case of **an Israelite woman** who was married **to a priest,** in the case of **the daughter of a priest** who was married **to a priest and he died and left her pregnant, her slaves should not partake of *teruma* either, due to the fetus's share.** The same *halakha* should apply whether the woman is an Israelite or the daughter of a priest.

The Sages pose a challenge to Rabbi Yosi: *me'achar she-eidasta lanu al bas yisrael le-kohen, af bas kohen le-kohen* — if this is true when the mother is a *bas yisrael* married to a *kohen*, the same logic should apply equally when the mother is a *bas kohen* married to a *kohen*. Why would the mother's lineage matter? The fetus still holds a portion of the estate and is still unborn. The Sages appear to be making the point that according to Rabbi Yosi's reasoning, the same ruling should hold in both cases.

### Two Theories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר עוּבָּר בִּמְעֵי זָרָה — זָר הוּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא: יָלוּד — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָלוּד — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the scholars: Is **the reason for** the ruling of **Rabbi Yosei because he holds that a fetus in the womb of a non-priest is a non-priest,** as it is considered part of its mother's body and it becomes a priest only upon birth, and therefore the slaves in which it owns a share will be allowed to eat *teruma* only at that stage? **Or,** is Rabbi Yosei **perhaps** of the opinion that only **one who was born enables** others **to partake** of *teruma*, whereas **one who is not** yet **born does not enable** others **to partake,** although it is considered a priest?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ — לְעוּבָּר בִּמְעֵי כֹהֶנֶת, מַאי? אָמַר רַבָּה, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: דְּקָסָבַר עוּבָּר בִּמְעֵי זָרָה זָר הוּא. רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: יָלוּד — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵין יָלוּד — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the **practical difference** between the two possible reasons? It is the case of **a fetus in the womb of the priestess,** the daughter of a priest. If Rabbi Yosei's rationale is that the fetus in the womb of a non-priest is a non-priest, that is not the case here, and therefore the slaves should partake of *teruma*. **What** is the *halakha* in this case? **Rabba said** that **this is Rabbi Yosei's reasoning: He holds** that **a fetus in the womb of a non-priest is a non-priest. Rav Yosef said:** His rationale is that only **one who was born enables** others **to partake** of *teruma*, whereas **one who is not** yet **born does not enable** others **to partake.**

The *gemara* raises a fundamental question about what Rabbi Yosi's reasoning actually is. Two possible explanations are offered. The first — associated with Rabba — is: *ta'ama de-Rabbi Yosi mishum de-kasavar ubar be-mei zarah zar hu* — Rabbi Yosi's reason is that the fetus, while still in the womb of a non-*kohen* mother, is considered a *zar*, a non-*kohen*. He has a portion of the estate, but because his mother is not a *kohen* and he has not yet achieved independent identity, he is classified as a *zar*. The slaves thus have a partial *zar*-owner, and therefore cannot eat. On this reading, the critical variable is the mother's status, not the fact of being unborn.

The second explanation — associated with Rav Yosef — is: *yalud ma'achil, she-eino yalud eino ma'achil* — one who is born enables feeding; one who is not yet born does not enable feeding. On this reading, it has nothing to do with the mother being a non-*kohen*. Even if the mother were a *bas kohen*, the result would be identical: being unborn is itself the disqualifying factor.

### No Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מֵאַחַר שֶׁהֵעַדְתָּ לָנוּ עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן, בַּת כֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן מַהוּ? אָמַר לָהֶם: זוֹ שָׁמַעְתִּי וְזוֹ לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav Yosef's opinion from a *baraita* that continues the last clause of the mishna: The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Yosei: Since you testified before us about** the case of **an Israelite woman** married **to a priest,** in the case of **the daughter of a priest** married **to a priest, what is** the *halakha*? **He said to them:** With regard to the former case, **I heard** from my teachers that the slaves do not partake of *teruma*, **but** with regard to **this** one, **I did not hear** such a thing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא: עוּבָּר בִּמְעֵי זָרָה זָר הוּא — הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ: זוֹ שָׁמַעְתִּי וְזוֹ לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: יָלוּד — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵין יָלוּד — אֵין מַאֲכִיל, מַאי זוֹ שָׁמַעְתִּי וְזוֹ לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי? אִיהִי הִיא קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that Rabbi Yosei's reasoning is that **a fetus in the womb of a non-priest is a non-priest, this is** the reason **that he said to them: This** case **I heard but this** case **I did not hear.** There is a logical distinction between the two cases, as in the latter case the fetus is not in the womb of a non-priest. **However, if you say** that his rationale is that only **one who was born enables** others **to partake** of *teruma*, whereas **one who is not** yet **born does not enable** others **to partake, what** does he mean by saying: **This** case **I heard but this** case **I did not hear? It is the same** case with regard to this principle. The Gemara concludes: This is **a difficult** objection.

The *nafka mina* between these two theories is precisely the case of an *ubar* whose mother is a *bas kohen*. On Rabba's reading, since the mother is a *kohen*, the *ubar* is not a *zar*, and therefore his partial ownership creates no problem — the slaves could eat. On Rav Yosef's reading, being unborn is categorically disqualifying regardless of the mother's identity, so the result would be identical: the slaves cannot eat.

The *braita* presses Rabbi Yosi directly on this: *zo shamati ve-zo lo shamati* — this I have received as a tradition, and that I have not. He has a tradition that a *bas yisrael*'s slaves cannot eat on account of the *ubar*; whether the same applies when the mother is a *bas kohen*, he declines to rule. If his reasoning were simply that an unborn person cannot enable feeding regardless of the mother's identity, then the Sages' challenge would be unanswerable and he would have had to respond in kind. His silence on the *bas kohen* case suggests that his actual reasoning was the first — that the relevant factor is the *ubar*'s status as a *zar* by virtue of his mother being a *bas yisrael*. But this is not resolved definitively, and the *gemara* leaves the ambiguity in place.

## Fetus Inheritance

### Fetus Title

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים — אוֹכְלִים מִשּׁוּם בָּנִים. אֵין לוֹ בָּנִים — אוֹכְלִים מִשּׁוּם אַחִים. אֵין לוֹ אַחִים — אוֹכְלִים מִשּׁוּם מִשְׁפָּחָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: This is the statement of Rabbi Yosei. However, the Rabbis say** that if the dead husband **has children,** the slaves **partake** of *teruma* **due to** the **children,** as they inherit the slaves. If **he does not have children, they partake** of *teruma* **due to** his **brothers,** who inherit his property. If **he does not have brothers** either, **they partake due to** the **entire family,** which inherits his property. The fetus does not disqualify them, as it does not yet own its share of the inheritance.

The *gemara* now examines the deeper theoretical issue beneath the surface of the *mishnah*: does a fetus acquire title to an inheritance before birth? Rabbi Yosi's ruling in the *mishnah* rests on the premise that the *ubar* already holds a portion of the estate — that he is, in some sense, already a partial heir from the moment of his father's death. This is assumption number one. Assumption number two is that the *ubar*, despite holding this partial title, cannot cause slaves to eat *terumah* because he is unborn.

The *chachamim* who challenge Rabbi Yosi appear to reject assumption number one entirely. Their position is that *yesh lo banim, okhlin mishum banim* — if the deceased left sons, the slaves eat through those sons; *ein lo banim, okhlin mishum achim* — if there are no sons, the slaves eat through the brothers. In other words, the *ubar* is simply not counted until he is born. Before birth, the existing relatives — sons, brothers, or any *mishpachah* — are treated as the heirs for purposes of determining who can enable *terumah* eating. The *ubar* creates no disruption because he has, in the view of the *chachamim*, no present title to the estate.

This produces a sharp disagreement: Rabbi Yosi holds that the *ubar* acquires a portion of the inheritance immediately upon his father's death, even before birth. The *chachamim* hold that no such acquisition occurs until birth. The *gemara* asks whether Shmuel, who reports Rabbi Yosi's position, personally agrees with it or merely reports it neutrally.

### Shmuel's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זוֹ״ וְלָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ. הָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב חָנָא בַּגְדָּתָאָה: פּוֹק אַיְיתִי לִי בֵּי עַשְׂרָה, דְּאֵימָא לָךְ בְּאַנְפַּיְיהוּ: הַמְזַכֶּה לְעוּבָּר — קָנָה. אֶלָּא: ״זוֹ״ וּסְבִירָא לֵיהּ. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן — דִּפְלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: By saying that **this** is only Rabbi Yosei's stance, Shmuel seemingly indicates that **he** himself **does not maintain** that opinion. However, **Shmuel said to Rav Ḥana of Baghdad: Go and bring me an assembly of ten** men **and I will say to you before them** a *halakha* that I seek to disseminate: **One who transfers ownership** of an object **to a fetus,** the fetus **acquires** it. Consequently, according to Shmuel, a fetus can own property, which is the premise of Rabbi Yosei's stance that a fetus shares the inheritance even before he is born. The Gemara answers: **Rather,** although Shmuel said that **this** is only Rabbi Yosei's stance, **he holds** likewise. **What is** Shmuel **teaching us** by saying so? He is teaching us **that the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Yosei.**

The answer emerges from Shmuel's dramatic declaration to Rav Chana Bagdata — identified by Rashi as either a master of *aggadah* or, more likely given the name, a resident of Baghdad in Babylonia: *puk ayti li bei asara* — go and bring me a group of ten people — *ve-eima lach be-anpaihu* — and I will declare publicly before them: *ha-mezakeh le-ubar kanah* — one who transfers title to a fetus, the fetus acquires it. This is not a quiet, tentative ruling; Shmuel wants to make a public declaration of principle. A fetus can receive a transfer of title — *da'at acheret makneh oto*, through the mechanism of another person acting on its behalf — and presumably, if it can receive a transfer, it also inherits by operation of law while still unborn. This aligns Shmuel clearly with Rabbi Yosi on assumption number one.

### Shmuel vs. Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי פְּלִיגִי? מֵתִיב רַבִּי זַכַּאי: זוֹ עֵדוּת הֵעִיד רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מִפִּי שְׁמַעְיָה וְאַבְטַלְיוֹן, וְהוֹדוּ לוֹ! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִי קָתָנֵי "וְקִבְּלוּ"? "וְהוֹדוּ לוֹ" קָתָנֵי, דְּמִסְתַּבַּר טַעְמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But do they** really **disagree? Rabbi Zakkai raised an objection** to this statement from a *baraita*: **This** was **a testimony** that **Rabbi Yosei testified** that he heard **from the mouths of Shemaya and Avtalyon, and** the Rabbis **acknowledged** his testimony. Apparently, they accepted his opinion. **Rav Ashi said: Does** that *baraita* **state: And** the Rabbis **accepted** his testimony? **It states: And they acknowledged** his testimony, which indicates **that his opinion is reasonable.** However, they did not accept his ruling.

The *gemara* then revisits the phrase *zu divrei Rabbi Yosi* — "this is the position of Rabbi Yosi" — which might have implied that Shmuel was merely reporting a minority view without endorsing it. In light of Shmuel's public declaration, the *gemara* corrects course: *ela zu u-sevira lei* — it means both "this is Rabbi Yosi's position" and "this is how Shmuel himself holds." Shmuel endorses Rabbi Yosi's first principle that the *ubar* acquires a portion of the inheritance before birth. He notes, however, that *chachamim* disagree with this, so students of the tradition should be aware that the matter is not uncontested.

### Shemaya Testimony

The *gemara* introduces a report: Rabbi Zakai testified that Rabbi Yosi transmitted this ruling *mipi Shemaya ve-Avtalyon* — in the name of Shemaya and Avtalyon. This clarifies an apparent anachronism: Rabbi Yosi did not testify *before* the *tanna'im* of earlier generations but rather passed on a tradition *in their name*. The question then becomes whether this transmission constitutes a definitive ruling. *Ve-hodu lo* — and they conceded to him, or agreed that his reasoning made sense.

Rav Ashi clarifies the force of that concession: *mi katani de-kiblu?* — the text does not say they *accepted* his position. *Ve-hodu lo katani* — it says they *conceded* or acknowledged that the reasoning was compelling. *De-mistabra ta'ameih* — it made great sense to them. But acknowledging that an argument is compelling is not the same as issuing a formal ruling in its favor. The *chachamim* were impressed but did not formally commit to the position. This leaves the debate in a nuanced state: Shmuel holds like Rabbi Yosi; the *chachamim* of the *mishnah* appear to disagree; and even the great authorities of earlier generations, while finding the argument persuasive, stopped short of a definitive acceptance.

## Eating Scenarios

### Sons Present

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אוֹכְלִים. הִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אֵין אוֹכְלִים. הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים, וְהִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג אוֹכְלִים כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהִיא אוֹכֶלֶת, עַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ, מִפְּנֵי חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר. שֶׁהָעוּבָּר פּוֹסֵל וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If the priest who was married to an Israelite woman and died **left children,** both the slaves of usufruct property **and** the slaves of guaranteed investment **may partake** of *teruma*. The slaves of guaranteed investment are owned by the children, who are priests, and the slaves of usufruct property are owned by the woman, who partakes of *teruma* due to her children. If **he left** his wife **pregnant** and did not leave children, both **these** slaves **and those** slaves **may not partake** of *teruma*. If **he left children and left her pregnant,** the **slaves of usufruct** property who belong to her **partake** of *teruma* **just as she partakes** due to her children. However, the **slaves of guaranteed investment,** who are inherited by the children, **may not partake, due to the fetus's share,** as it too inherits them, **as a fetus can disqualify** one from partaking of *teruma* **but it cannot not enable** one **to partake.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.**

The *gemara* now presents a systematic *braita* working through the various scenarios. The first case: *heniah banim* — the *kohen* dies leaving sons. *Elu va-elu okhlin* — both these slaves and those slaves eat *terumah*. The *nichsei tzon barzel* slaves eat because they are part of the estate now inherited by the sons. The *nichsei milug* slaves eat because the wife herself is entitled to eat *terumah* — there are living children from the marriage who keep her connected to the *kohen*'s family, and through her the slaves eat.

### Only Pregnancy

The second case: *heniah meuberet* — he leaves her pregnant, with no born children. *Elu va-elu einan okhlin* — neither set of slaves may eat. This is the teaching of the *mishnah* itself, following Rabbi Yosi. The wife cannot eat *terumah* through an unborn child, so her *milug* slaves cannot eat through her. And the *tzon barzel* slaves are now partially owned by the *ubar*, who cannot enable them to eat. *Ubar posel ve-eino ma'achil*.

### Sons & Pregnancy

The third and most complex case: *heniah banim ve-heniah meuberet* — the *kohen* leaves both born sons and a pregnant wife. *Avdei melog okhlin ke-derekh she-hi okhelet* — the *milug* slaves eat, just as the wife herself eats. Since there are born children from the marriage, she is entitled to eat *terumah*, and they eat through her. *Avdei tzon barzel lo yokhlu mipnei chelko shel ubar* — but the *tzon barzel* slaves may not eat, because the *ubar*'s partial ownership of the estate prevents them. The *ubar* thus *posel* — he disqualifies the *tzon barzel* slaves — while the born sons enable the wife and through her the *milug* slaves. The *ubar* does not enable anything; he only disqualifies. *Divrei Rabbi Yosi* — this is Rabbi Yosi's position.

### Daughter vs. Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם אָבִיו: הַבַּת — מַאֲכֶלֶת, הַבֵּן — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: זְכָרִים יֹאכְלוּ כּוּלָּן, נְקֵבוֹת לֹא יֹאכֵלוּ, שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא עוּבָּר זָכָר, וְאֵין לַבָּנוֹת בִּמְקוֹם הַבֵּן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, says in the name of his father:** If the priest left behind **a daughter,** she **enables** the slaves **to partake** of *teruma*; however, **a son does not enable** them **to partake. Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says:** If among the priest's children there are **males,** the slaves **partake** of *teruma*. But if they are **all females, they do not partake, lest the fetus be found** to be **a male, and daughters do not** receive **any** of the inheritance **where** there is **a son.** The male fetus would be the sole inheritor, and it does not enable slaves to partake of *teruma*.

Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yosi reports a position *mishum aviv* — in his father's name: *ha-bat ma'achelet, ha-ben eino ma'achil* — the daughter enables feeding but the son does not. The *gemara* leaves this to be unpacked further.

### Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִירְיָא שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא עוּבָּר זָכָר? תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דִּנְקֵבָה נָמֵי פָּסְלָה! חֲדָא וְעוֹד קָאָמַר: חֲדָא, דִּנְקֵבָה נָמֵי פָּסְלָה. וְעוֹד, שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא עוּבָּר זָכָר, וְאֵין לַבָּנוֹת בִּמְקוֹם הַבֵּן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why** does Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai **specifically** explain that if the children are females, the slaves may not partake of *teruma*, **lest the fetus be found** to be **a male? Derive** the ruling that the slaves do not partake of *teruma* from the *halakha* **that a female** fetus **also disqualifies** its slaves from partaking of *teruma*. Since the priest has only daughters, they inherit from him, and the female fetus receives a share in the inheritance too. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai **stated one** reason **and another. One** reason is **that a female also disqualifies** its slaves from partaking of *teruma*, **and another** reason is **lest the fetus be found** to be **a male, and daughters have no** share in the inheritance **at all in a place** where there is **a son.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai offers a further position: *zekharim yokhlu kullan, nekeivot lo yokhlu, shema yimatzei ubar zeh* — males eat, all of them, but females do not, out of concern that this *ubar* may turn out to be male. The discussion of these positions and their underlying reasoning extends into the following *daf*, where we will continue the analysis tomorrow.
