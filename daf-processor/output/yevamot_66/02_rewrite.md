# Yevamot 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Slaves of Chalalah

### Mishnah Setup

The *mishnah* at the opening of our discussion addresses the status of slaves belonging to a woman married to a *kohen*. Two categories of property are relevant here. *Nichsei milug* refers to property over which the wife retains title, though the husband enjoys its use; *nichsei tzon barzel* refers to property that has been assessed and formally transferred to the husband's ownership, leaving the wife with only a monetary claim. The *mishnah* focuses on the more interesting case — the slaves she owns through *nichsei milug* — and introduces a complicating factor: if she is an *almanah*, a *gerushah*, a *chalutzah*, or an *almanah l'kohen gadol*, she becomes a *chalalah* through the marriage. As a *chalalah*, she cannot eat *terumah* even by virtue of being married to the *kohen*. The question then becomes: what is the status of her slaves? Can they eat *terumah*?

The *mishnah* rules that they cannot. The *gemara* explains that this follows from the principle governing *kinyano she-kanah kinyan* — that is, the wife herself, who is his *kinyan* of a kind and who owns these slaves, is the conduit through which they eat. If she herself cannot eat *terumah*, the question is whether she can nonetheless enable her slaves to eat.

### Eating Through Whom

The *gemara* raises an apparent challenge to the principle that those who eat do so through her. The case involves a *mamzer* — and the word *mamzer* is used here to make the example as extreme as possible, though the core case could involve any non-*kohen*. Suppose a *kohen* has a daughter who marries a *mamzer* or a *yisrael*, and they have a child. That child is not a *kohen* — the father being a non-*kohen* — and would never himself eat *terumah*. Yet if the *kohen* dies and there remains such a grandchild descended from the marriage, the widow may continue eating *terumah* for as long as that grandchild is alive, even after she has collected her *ketubah*. The grandchild enables her to eat even though the grandchild itself cannot eat and is, in an extreme case, even a *mamzer*.

This seems to challenge the rule that only one who eats can enable others to eat. The *gemara* cites this case with the phrase *va-harei mamzer she-ein ochel u-maachil* — "behold, the *mamzer* who does not eat yet enables." Should this not demonstrate that someone who cannot eat can still enable others to?

### Kinyan Ochel

Ravina responds sharply: *kinyan ochel ka'amar* — the relevant principle concerns specifically a *kinyan* that eats. The case of the grandchild enabling the grandmother to eat is categorically different: the grandmother eats not because the grandchild "enables" her in the technical sense relevant to property ownership, but because the grandchild's existence keeps her connected to the *kohen*'s family. That is a distinct mechanism entirely. The operative principle for our *mishnah* remains: *kinyan ochel ma'achil, kinyan she'eino ochel eino ma'achil* — only a *kinyan* that itself eats can feed others. Since the wife is called a *kinyan* in this context, and since she cannot eat *terumah* as a *chalalah*, her slaves cannot eat through her. This is the first explanation of the *mishnah*.

### Rava's Gezerah

Rava offers a second explanation. He acknowledges that in principle, the slaves of the *chalalah* ought to be entitled to eat *terumah*, because indirectly they are connected to the *kohen* and their eating flows through him rather than purely through her. *Mide'oraisa me'achlei* — by Torah law they would be permitted to eat. However, *verabbanan hu de-gazru behu* — the Rabbis enacted a prohibition against their eating in this case, as an incentive to dissolution of the forbidden marriage.

The logic is as follows: because the *kohen* is married to a *gerushah*, *chalutza*, or the *kohen gadol* to an *almanah*, a situation the Rabbis find deeply objectionable, they sought to create pressure toward divorce. The wife can be expected to say: *ani eini ochelet* — I myself cannot eat *terumah*; *avadai einam ochlim* — my slaves cannot eat *terumah*. She is, as it were, like a *zonah* to him, *zonah hi etzlo* — not fully a wife in the sense of enjoying the privileges of wifehood. This sense of exclusion and non-belonging, says Rava, was designed by the Rabbis — *va'ati lefuka* — to bring about a divorce. The slaves could in principle eat; the Rabbis withheld that entitlement as a form of social and legal pressure.

### Rav Ashi's Concern

Rav Ashi agrees with the premise that the slaves could in principle eat, but offers a different reason for the rabbinic prohibition: *gezerah shema ta'achilah achar misah* — a decree lest she continue feeding them *terumah* even after the *kohen* dies. Her entitlement to feed them *terumah* is entirely contingent on her husband's status; once he dies, that entitlement ceases. If we permit her to feed them during the marriage, we fear she will continue the practice after his death.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: that concern should apply universally. Every Jewish woman married to a *kohen* might continue feeding her slaves *terumah* after his death — should we therefore prohibit all such wives from feeding their slaves? Rav Ashi withdraws that formulation and offers a refined version.

### Rav Ashi's Concern

*Ela amar Rav Ashi: be-almanah kohenes* — the particular case that generates a real risk is when the woman married to the *kohen gadol* is herself a *bas kohen*, a daughter of a *kohen*. Here is the chain of reasoning she might follow after her husband dies: *me'ikara achli bi-terumah debei nasha* — originally, my slaves ate *terumah* because of my father's house, by virtue of the fact that I was a *bas kohen*. Then *inseiva le-hai kohen* — I married this *kohen*, *achli bi-terumah de-gavrai* — and they continued eating *terumah* by virtue of my husband. Now that my husband has died, she may reason, *hadri leh milsa ke-me'ikara* — things return to their original state and my slaves may continue eating *terumah* as they did before.

The flaw in this reasoning is that she may be unaware — *delo yade'a* — that in the course of this marriage she became a *chalalah*. *De-me'ikara lo shavisah le-nafshah chalalah* — originally, when the slaves ate by virtue of her father's house, she was not yet a *chalalah*. *Hashta shavisah nafshah chalalah* — but now, by virtue of this forbidden marriage, she is a *chalalah*, and therefore her slaves cannot eat even after her husband dies. The concern, then, is not general but specific: she will assume she may revert to her original entitlement without recognizing that the marriage itself permanently altered her status.

### Almanah Kohenes

The *gemara* asks: this reasoning accounts for the case of the *almanah kohenes*, the *bas kohen* who married the *kohen gadol*. But what of the *almanah bas yisrael* — the woman who is not a *bas kohen*? She has no prior entitlement from her father's house; why should the same prohibition apply to her? The *gemara* answers: *be-manusa lo pligi rabbanan* — where there is a specific case that genuinely warrants the decree, the Rabbis did not distinguish among cases. *O lo plug* — they enacted a uniform prohibition across all these situations rather than creating distinctions that might be confused in practice.

The upshot is that we have two general approaches. The first, associated with Ravina, is that the slaves eat only through the wife, and since she cannot eat, they cannot either — this is the *de'oraisa* position. The second, associated with Rava and Rav Ashi, acknowledges that in principle the slaves eat through the *kohen*, but a rabbinic *gezerah* was imposed for the reasons described. In either case the practical ruling is that the slaves of a *chalalah* do not eat *terumah*.

## Tzon Barzel Debate

### Property Assessment

The *gemara* introduces a new topic with the word *itmar* — "it was stated" — signaling an *amoraic* dispute. The subject is *nichsei tzon barzel*: property a woman brings into the marriage whose value is formally assessed — *shum* — and recorded in the *ketubah*. The *ketubah* lists the items and assigns them a total monetary value, and the husband accepts full financial responsibility for that amount. If the property appreciates, he benefits; if it depreciates or is lost, he bears the loss. What the wife is guaranteed is the return of the assessed monetary value.

The practical question arises when the husband dies or the marriage is dissolved and the actual property is still available — the original field, the original slaves, the original vessels. Can the wife demand the return of the specific items? Or has the husband taken absolute title, leaving her only a monetary claim against the estate?

### Title vs. Debt

This is precisely the dispute between Rav Yehuda and Rav Ami. Rav Yehuda rules *hadin immah* — the law is with her; she may demand the return of the specific property. Rav Ami rules *hadin immo* — the law is with him; she holds only a monetary debt and cannot compel return of the items themselves.

Rav Yehuda's reasoning is formulated as *mishum shevach beis aviha* — on account of the benefit of her father's house. This is the property that came with her from her father as a dowry; she ought to be entitled to reclaim it. Rav Ami reasons from the *mishnah* itself, which states explicitly regarding *nichsei tzon barzel*: *im mesu mesu lo, ve-im hokiru hokiru lo* — if they die, it is his loss; if they appreciate, it is his gain. This language, he argues, demonstrates that the property entered his possession absolutely, *bi-rshusei kayami* — it stands in his domain, *u-ch'didei damu* — and is treated as fully his.

### Rental Comparison

The *gemara* attempts to test whether formal liability (*chiyuv achrayus*) alone is sufficient to give a person such a relationship to property that the property's slaves could eat *terumah* on his account — without necessarily establishing outright ownership. If we take seriously the claim that *chayav be-achrayusan* establishes this relationship without full title, we should be able to find a parallel. The *gemara* brings a *mishnah*: a *yisrael* who rents a cow from a *kohen* may feed it *terumah* fodder, because the cow remains the *kohen*'s and he has the relevant interest in it. But a *kohen* who rents a cow from a *yisrael* — even though *mezonoseha alav*, he is responsible for feeding it and bears liability for certain losses — nevertheless *lo ya'achilennah karshinei terumah*, he may not feed it *terumah* fodder. The cow is still the *yisrael*'s. Rental liability is simply insufficient to confer the kind of interest that enables feeding *terumah*.

### Middle Zone

The *gemara* responds that the husband's relationship to the *tzon barzel* property is not comparable to a renter's. A renter bears liability only for theft and loss; he is not responsible for depreciation due to market changes, normal wear, or reduction in market value. The *tzon barzel* relationship is categorically different: no matter what happens — whether the market changes, whether the property deteriorates through ordinary use, whether the decrease is anyone's fault or not — the husband must restore the full assessed value. Conversely, any increase in value belongs entirely to him.

This sliding scale of liability raises the question acutely. The *gemara* is exploring whether there exists a legal middle zone: the wife retains formal title, but the husband has such comprehensive rights and obligations with respect to the property — all gains his, all losses his regardless of fault — that the slaves can eat *terumah* through his relationship to them, even without his having acquired outright ownership. The *gemara* presses: *v'chol heicha de-chayav be-achrayusan achli bi-terumah?* — does any degree of comprehensive liability trigger the right to eat? The rental cases show low-level liability is insufficient. The question is whether this uniquely intensive relationship suffices to permit *terumah* eating without constituting full ownership.

### Two Braitot

Rava and Rav Yosef were sitting together at the conclusion of Rav Nachman's *drasha*, and in their discussion they noted that the sources appear to point in both directions. One *braita* supports Rav Ami: *avdei tzon barzel yotzin be-shen va-ayin la-ish, aval lo la-ishah* — slaves brought in as *tzon barzel* go free if the husband knocks out their eye or tooth, but not if the wife does the same. This strongly implies the husband is considered their master — he is the one whose act of violence triggers the law of *shen va-ayin*. If she retained genuine title, why would her striking not also trigger their release?

The second *braita* supports Rav Yehuda: *ha-machneses shum le-ba'alah im ratza ba'al limkor lo yimkor* — if a wife brings in assessed property and the husband wishes to sell it, he may not do so. If he held absolute title, he would plainly be entitled to sell his own property. The fact that he cannot sell it — and that the *ma'aseh* before Rabban Gamliel affirmed that any such sale is void and the husband must retrieve the property from the purchasers — demonstrates that she retains rights in the objects themselves, not merely a monetary claim.

## Rava's Ruling

### She Retrieves

Rava, citing Rav Nachman, rules: *halacha ke-Rav Yehuda* — the law follows Rav Yehuda. The wife is entitled to retrieve the specific property she brought in. The *braita* showing that the husband cannot sell the property without her consent was evidently persuasive.

### Shen V'Ayin

Rava acknowledges the counter-*braita*, which seemed to prove Rav Ami's position by stating that only the husband's violence triggers the slave's freedom. He addresses this directly: *af al gav de-tanya ke-vassa de-Rabbi Ami, mistabra ta'ama de-Rav Yehuda mishum shevach beis aviha* — even though the *braita* appears to favor Rav Ami, the underlying rationale of Rav Yehuda's position is more compelling, because this property came from her father's house and she ought to be entitled to reclaim it. Tosafot, for their part, find the *shen va-ayin braita* less than a decisive proof for Rav Ami in any case: just as the slaves eat *terumah* by virtue of the husband's comprehensive relationship to them, so too the *shen va-ayin* law might operate through him without that implying full title.

### Cloak Story

A *ma'aseh* illustrates the practical limits of Rav Yehuda's ruling. A woman brought into her marriage a fine wool cloak as part of her *nichsei tzon barzel*, recorded in her *ketubah*. Her husband died. Before she had the opportunity to claim the cloak, the orphans seized it and spread it over the dead body, using it as a shroud. Once an object is designated and used as a shroud for a deceased, it becomes *assur be-hana'ah* — prohibited from benefit.

Rava ruled: *kanya misna* — the dead body acquires it. The cloak has changed its identity and she loses any right to that specific object, though she remains entitled to its monetary value from the estate. A grandson of Rava challenged: do we not rule like Rav Yehuda, that the property is hers? If so, the orphans had no right to use her property as a shroud, and their doing so should not affect its status. The response is decisive.

### Requires Collection

The reply: *mi lo modeh Rabbi Yehuda de-mechusar gubyanya?* — even Rav Yehuda concedes that the property requires active collection before it reverts to her control. Rav Yehuda's ruling does not mean that the property is automatically, at every moment, fully under her title and control. He holds that she has a right to reclaim it — but that right must be exercised; it must be collected. *De-kivun de-mechusar gubyanya bi-rshusei kai* — since it still requires collection, it remains under the control of the husband's heirs.

The conceptual picture that emerges is subtle: she may hold some form of title, or she may hold only a superior claim of retrieval, but in neither case does the property automatically revert to her domain upon the husband's death. Until she acts to collect it, it remains under the heirs' effective control. And because of that, the heirs were in a position to use it as a shroud, triggering *asur be-hana'ah* and extinguishing her specific rights in that object.

Rava's reasoning invokes his broader principle: *hekdesh, chametz, u-shichrur motzi'in mi-yedei shibud* — that sanctification of property (*hekdesh*), the transformation of *chametz* during Passover into something *assur be-hana'ah*, and the emancipation of a slave all operate to dissolve existing liens or encumbrances upon property by fundamentally transforming the identity of the object. Here, using the cloak as a shroud similarly transforms its identity — just as *chametz* that becomes forbidden during Passover loses its character as encumbered property, so too this cloak, once assigned to the dead, loses whatever lien or claim she held over it.

## Appreciation Cases

### Two Vessels

Rav Yehuda presents the following scenario at the top of 67a: a wife brings into the marriage two vessels assessed at one thousand *zuz*. By the time of divorce or the husband's death, they have appreciated and are now worth two thousand.

### Shevach Rule

The ruling is: *echad notalah bi-chtuvtasah* — she takes one vessel back as part of her *ketubah* entitlement. *Ve-echad nosenet damim ve-notalah mipnei shevach beis aviha* — and the second vessel she is also entitled to demand, paying the difference in value, on account of *shevach beis aviha*, the benefit of her father's house.

The *gemara* asks: what is the novelty here? Didn't Rav Yehuda already establish that she can retrieve her property? The answer: one might have thought that her right to retrieve extends only up to the original assessed value — that is, she can demand the vessels back insofar as they satisfy her monetary entitlement, but not insofar as they have appreciated beyond that value. Perhaps she should be entitled to only one of the two vessels, since one is now worth the full original thousand. The ruling teaches that *mipnei shevach beis aviha* she is entitled to retrieve both the objects she brought in and to pay the appreciated difference — not merely to receive the monetary equivalent. The same logic applies in reverse: if the property has lost value, she retrieves all of it and the estate compensates her for the shortfall.

## Fetus & Eating

### Mishnah Setup

The *mishnah* turns to a related but distinct problem. A *bas yisrael* — a non-*kohen* woman — was married to a *kohen*, and the *kohen* dies leaving her pregnant. When a *kohen* dies, the general rule is that if he leaves behind a child from that marriage, the widow continues to eat *terumah* through the child. But here the child is not yet born. What is the status of the slaves?

The *mishnah* rules *lo yokhlu avadeha bi-terumah mipnei chelko shel ubar* — the slaves may not eat *terumah* on account of the portion of the *ubar* (the fetus). The reasoning requires unpacking. The *kohen* died leaving his estate to his heirs. If he left sons, those sons are the heirs and are co-owners of the slaves; the slaves eat *terumah* by virtue of those sons. But here, the *ubar* is among the heirs — indeed, if the child is born and is male, he will inherit a portion. The *ubar* is thus a partial owner of the slaves. Since the *ubar* cannot yet cause the slaves to eat *terumah* — he is unborn — his partial ownership prevents the slaves from eating, because that portion of the slaves that is owned by him is not entitled to eat. Ten percent owned by the *ubar* is not entitled; and since the slaves are partially owned by one who cannot enable their eating, none of them may eat.

Furthermore, the *mishnah* notes that the *ubar* also does not enable his mother to eat: she too cannot eat *terumah* on account of an unborn child. This produces the striking result that the *ubar* *posel* — he disqualifies — and yet *eino ma'achil* — he does not feed. He is the worst of both legal worlds: his existence as a partial owner creates a disqualification, but his unborn status prevents him from conferring any entitlement.

### Rabbi Yosi

*Divrei Rabbi Yosi* — this is the ruling of Rabbi Yosi. Rabbi Yosi formulates the principle: *ha-ubar posel ve-eino ma'achil* — the fetus invalidates and does not feed. He prevents the slaves from eating by virtue of his partial ownership, while himself being unable to enable anyone to eat.

### Kohenes Challenge

The Sages pose a challenge to Rabbi Yosi: *me'achar she-eidasta lanu al bas yisrael le-kohen, af bas kohen le-kohen* — if this is true when the mother is a *bas yisrael* married to a *kohen*, the same logic should apply equally when the mother is a *bas kohen* married to a *kohen*. Why would the mother's lineage matter? The fetus still holds a portion of the estate and is still unborn. The Sages appear to be making the point that according to Rabbi Yosi's reasoning, the same ruling should hold in both cases.

### Two Theories

The *gemara* raises a fundamental question about what Rabbi Yosi's reasoning actually is. Two possible explanations are offered. The first — associated with Rabba — is: *ta'ama de-Rabbi Yosi mishum de-kasavar ubar be-mei zarah zar hu* — Rabbi Yosi's reason is that the fetus, while still in the womb of a non-*kohen* mother, is considered a *zar*, a non-*kohen*. He has a portion of the estate, but because his mother is not a *kohen* and he has not yet achieved independent identity, he is classified as a *zar*. The slaves thus have a partial *zar*-owner, and therefore cannot eat. On this reading, the critical variable is the mother's status, not the fact of being unborn.

The second explanation — associated with Rav Yosef — is: *yalud ma'achil, she-eino yalud eino ma'achil* — one who is born enables feeding; one who is not yet born does not enable feeding. On this reading, it has nothing to do with the mother being a non-*kohen*. Even if the mother were a *bas kohen*, the result would be identical: being unborn is itself the disqualifying factor.

### No Ruling

The *nafka mina* between these two theories is precisely the case of an *ubar* whose mother is a *bas kohen*. On Rabba's reading, since the mother is a *kohen*, the *ubar* is not a *zar*, and therefore his partial ownership creates no problem — the slaves could eat. On Rav Yosef's reading, being unborn is categorically disqualifying regardless of the mother's identity, so the result would be identical: the slaves cannot eat.

The *braita* presses Rabbi Yosi directly on this: *zo shamati ve-zo lo shamati* — this I have received as a tradition, and that I have not. He has a tradition that a *bas yisrael*'s slaves cannot eat on account of the *ubar*; whether the same applies when the mother is a *bas kohen*, he declines to rule. If his reasoning were simply that an unborn person cannot enable feeding regardless of the mother's identity, then the Sages' challenge would be unanswerable and he would have had to respond in kind. His silence on the *bas kohen* case suggests that his actual reasoning was the first — that the relevant factor is the *ubar*'s status as a *zar* by virtue of his mother being a *bas yisrael*. But this is not resolved definitively, and the *gemara* leaves the ambiguity in place.

## Fetus Inheritance

### Fetus Title

The *gemara* now examines the deeper theoretical issue beneath the surface of the *mishnah*: does a fetus acquire title to an inheritance before birth? Rabbi Yosi's ruling in the *mishnah* rests on the premise that the *ubar* already holds a portion of the estate — that he is, in some sense, already a partial heir from the moment of his father's death. This is assumption number one. Assumption number two is that the *ubar*, despite holding this partial title, cannot cause slaves to eat *terumah* because he is unborn.

The *chachamim* who challenge Rabbi Yosi appear to reject assumption number one entirely. Their position is that *yesh lo banim, okhlin mishum banim* — if the deceased left sons, the slaves eat through those sons; *ein lo banim, okhlin mishum achim* — if there are no sons, the slaves eat through the brothers. In other words, the *ubar* is simply not counted until he is born. Before birth, the existing relatives — sons, brothers, or any *mishpachah* — are treated as the heirs for purposes of determining who can enable *terumah* eating. The *ubar* creates no disruption because he has, in the view of the *chachamim*, no present title to the estate.

This produces a sharp disagreement: Rabbi Yosi holds that the *ubar* acquires a portion of the inheritance immediately upon his father's death, even before birth. The *chachamim* hold that no such acquisition occurs until birth. The *gemara* asks whether Shmuel, who reports Rabbi Yosi's position, personally agrees with it or merely reports it neutrally.

### Shmuel's Rule

The answer emerges from Shmuel's dramatic declaration to Rav Chana Bagdata — identified by Rashi as either a master of *aggadah* or, more likely given the name, a resident of Baghdad in Babylonia: *puk ayti li bei asara* — go and bring me a group of ten people — *ve-eima lach be-anpaihu* — and I will declare publicly before them: *ha-mezakeh le-ubar kanah* — one who transfers title to a fetus, the fetus acquires it. This is not a quiet, tentative ruling; Shmuel wants to make a public declaration of principle. A fetus can receive a transfer of title — *da'at acheret makneh oto*, through the mechanism of another person acting on its behalf — and presumably, if it can receive a transfer, it also inherits by operation of law while still unborn. This aligns Shmuel clearly with Rabbi Yosi on assumption number one.

### Shmuel vs. Sages

The *gemara* then revisits the phrase *zu divrei Rabbi Yosi* — "this is the position of Rabbi Yosi" — which might have implied that Shmuel was merely reporting a minority view without endorsing it. In light of Shmuel's public declaration, the *gemara* corrects course: *ela zu u-sevira lei* — it means both "this is Rabbi Yosi's position" and "this is how Shmuel himself holds." Shmuel endorses Rabbi Yosi's first principle that the *ubar* acquires a portion of the inheritance before birth. He notes, however, that *chachamim* disagree with this, so students of the tradition should be aware that the matter is not uncontested.

### Shemaya Testimony

The *gemara* introduces a report: Rabbi Zakai testified that Rabbi Yosi transmitted this ruling *mipi Shemaya ve-Avtalyon* — in the name of Shemaya and Avtalyon. This clarifies an apparent anachronism: Rabbi Yosi did not testify *before* the *tanna'im* of earlier generations but rather passed on a tradition *in their name*. The question then becomes whether this transmission constitutes a definitive ruling. *Ve-hodu lo* — and they conceded to him, or agreed that his reasoning made sense.

Rav Ashi clarifies the force of that concession: *mi katani de-kiblu?* — the text does not say they *accepted* his position. *Ve-hodu lo katani* — it says they *conceded* or acknowledged that the reasoning was compelling. *De-mistabra ta'ameih* — it made great sense to them. But acknowledging that an argument is compelling is not the same as issuing a formal ruling in its favor. The *chachamim* were impressed but did not formally commit to the position. This leaves the debate in a nuanced state: Shmuel holds like Rabbi Yosi; the *chachamim* of the *mishnah* appear to disagree; and even the great authorities of earlier generations, while finding the argument persuasive, stopped short of a definitive acceptance.

## Eating Scenarios

### Sons Present

The *gemara* now presents a systematic *braita* working through the various scenarios. The first case: *heniah banim* — the *kohen* dies leaving sons. *Elu va-elu okhlin* — both these slaves and those slaves eat *terumah*. The *nichsei tzon barzel* slaves eat because they are part of the estate now inherited by the sons. The *nichsei milug* slaves eat because the wife herself is entitled to eat *terumah* — there are living children from the marriage who keep her connected to the *kohen*'s family, and through her the slaves eat.

### Only Pregnancy

The second case: *heniah meuberet* — he leaves her pregnant, with no born children. *Elu va-elu einan okhlin* — neither set of slaves may eat. This is the teaching of the *mishnah* itself, following Rabbi Yosi. The wife cannot eat *terumah* through an unborn child, so her *milug* slaves cannot eat through her. And the *tzon barzel* slaves are now partially owned by the *ubar*, who cannot enable them to eat. *Ubar posel ve-eino ma'achil*.

### Sons & Pregnancy

The third and most complex case: *heniah banim ve-heniah meuberet* — the *kohen* leaves both born sons and a pregnant wife. *Avdei melog okhlin ke-derekh she-hi okhelet* — the *milug* slaves eat, just as the wife herself eats. Since there are born children from the marriage, she is entitled to eat *terumah*, and they eat through her. *Avdei tzon barzel lo yokhlu mipnei chelko shel ubar* — but the *tzon barzel* slaves may not eat, because the *ubar*'s partial ownership of the estate prevents them. The *ubar* thus *posel* — he disqualifies the *tzon barzel* slaves — while the born sons enable the wife and through her the *milug* slaves. The *ubar* does not enable anything; he only disqualifies. *Divrei Rabbi Yosi* — this is Rabbi Yosi's position.

### Daughter vs. Son

Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yosi reports a position *mishum aviv* — in his father's name: *ha-bat ma'achelet, ha-ben eino ma'achil* — the daughter enables feeding but the son does not. The *gemara* leaves this to be unpacked further.

### Shimon View

Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai offers a further position: *zekharim yokhlu kullan, nekeivot lo yokhlu, shema yimatzei ubar zeh* — males eat, all of them, but females do not, out of concern that this *ubar* may turn out to be male. The discussion of these positions and their underlying reasoning extends into the following *daf*, where we will continue the analysis tomorrow.
