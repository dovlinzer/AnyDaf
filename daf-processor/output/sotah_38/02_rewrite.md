# Sotah 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Birkat Kohanim

### Daf 38 Overview

Daf 38 marks a transition away from the *agadata* and returns to the *mishnayot*, specifically to the category of things that must be said in any language — *ne'emarin bechol lashon* — as well as those requiring *lashon hakodesh*. The daf now turns its attention to *birkat kohanim*.

### Why in Sotah

It is worth pausing to note how much of the *halachot* of *birkat kohanim* come to be discussed precisely here, in Masechta Sotah. The reason is structural: *birkat kohanim* appears in the *mishna* as one of the items requiring *lashon hakodesh*, and so Sotah becomes the occasion for a full treatment of the subject. The same dynamic explains why the laws of *egla arufa* — which might more naturally have found a home in Kodshim — end up receiving an entire *perek* here: because *egla arufa* is mentioned in the *mishna*'s discussion of *lashon hakodesh* versus *bechol lashon*, and one topic pulls the other in its wake.

### Mishna: Birkat Kohanim

The *mishna* asks: *birkat kohanim keitzad* — how is *birkat kohanim* performed? The *keitzad* here is not asking how to recite the blessing in *lashon hakodesh*; that requirement is taken for granted. Rather, the *mishna* uses this as an opportunity to address the broader laws of how *birkat kohanim* is conducted.

### Three Issues

Three issues are addressed. First, the number of blessings: *bamedina omer osa shalosh berachos* — outside the *mikdash*, the blessing is divided into three, meaning the congregation responds *amen* after each of the three verses (*Yevarechecha*, *Ya'er*, *Yisa*), treating each as a separate unit. *Ubamikdash bracha achat* — in the *Beis HaMikdash*, they do not interrupt with *amen* between the verses, and so the entire blessing constitutes one. In the *mikdash*, the response was *baruch shem* rather than *amen*, and since this does not interrupt the flow of the three verses, they are treated as a single unified blessing.

Second, the divine name: *bamikdash omer es Hashem kichsavov* — in the *mikdash*, God's name is pronounced as written, that is, the *shem hameforash*, the four-letter name that is not sounded outside the *mikdash*. *Ubamedina bekinuyov* — outside the *mikdash*, the name is pronounced in its customary substitute form. Rashi explains: *kichsavov be-Yud-Heh*, *bekinuyov be-Aleph-Dalet*.

Third, the position of the hands: *bamedina kohanim nosim es yedeihen kneged kitfeihen* — outside the *mikdash*, the *kohanim* lift their hands to shoulder height. *Ubamikdash al gabei rasheihen* — in the *mikdash*, the hands are raised above the head. The reason given is that the *shechina* rests in the *mikdash*, and the hands should therefore be raised high enough that the divine presence is, in a sense, above them. An exception is made for the *kohen gadol*, *chutz mikohen gadol she'eino magbiya yadav lema'la min hatzitz* — his hands should not rise above the *tzitz* on his forehead. *Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees, holding that even the *kohen gadol* raises his hands above the *tzitz*, citing the verse *vayisa Aharon es yadav el ha'am vayivarchaim* — and since Aharon was a *kohen gadol*, the word *vayisa* implies a particularly high elevation.

### Mishna Structure

The *mishna*'s organization is notable. The pattern alternates between *medina* and *mikdash*: the first ruling goes *medina-mikdash*, the second goes *mikdash-medina*, and the third returns to *medina-mikdash*. This may simply be a mnemonic device — each clause flows into the next through an ABBA-like alternation. There may also be a more deliberate reason: when it comes to the issue of pronouncing God's name as written, the *mishna* places the *mikdash* practice first, because pronouncing the *shem hameforash* is a matter of such gravity that it cannot be mentioned as an afterthought. The other issues are more historically remote and so their secondary mention is less problematic.

## Language Requirement

### Koh Tevarchu

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita*: *koh tevarchu* — the word *koh* indicates that the blessing must be given in *lashon hakodesh*. The *baraita* asks: perhaps it may be said in any language? Perhaps *koh* does not carry this implication?

### Gezerah Shava

The *gemara* resolves this through a *gezerah shava*. The word *koh* appears here in *koh tevarchu*, and the word *eileh* appears elsewhere: *eileh ya'amdu levarech et ha'am* — the blessing pronounced on Har Gerizim. Just as the blessing at Har Gerizim was given in *lashon hakodesh*, so too *birkat kohanim* must be in *lashon hakodesh*: *mah lehalan bi-lashon hakodesh af kan bi-lashon hakodesh*.

### Tosafot Question

Tosafot raises an incisive question: why derive the requirement from Har Gerizim? One could equally argue the opposite — learn from the verse *ve'achalta ve'savata u'verachta*, which governs *birkat hamazon*, and which we know may be said in any language. The *gezerah shava* could just as well have pointed toward *bechol lashon*.

### Text vs. Non-Text

Tosafot answers: it is more compelling to derive *birkat kohanim* from the blessings of Har Gerizim, because both are instances where the Torah provides an explicit text — *dil'shteihen meforash ba'Torah*. The text of the *kohanim*'s blessing is fully spelled out in the Torah; similarly, the blessings and curses at Har Gerizim are spelled out. By contrast, the text of *birkat hamazon* is not specified in the Torah — the verse *ve'achalta ve'savata u'verachta* simply requires expressing gratitude, and any adequate formulation suffices. Since there is no prescribed text, the question of original versus translation does not arise, and of course it may be said in any language.

This points to a deeper conceptual distinction. The requirement to speak in *lashon hakodesh* is, in many cases, really a requirement to recite the original text rather than a translation. When the Torah provides a specific text, the question becomes: must you say the original, or may you say it in translation? Things permitted in any language may simply be those for which no specific text is mandated — in which case the concept of "original versus translation" is inapplicable, and naturally any language will do.

### Categorization

Examining the lists in the *mishna* bears this out. Those items requiring *lashon hakodesh* — *mikra bikkurim*, *chalitzah*, *berachot ukelalot*, *birkat kohanim*, *parshat hamelech*, *parshat egla arufa*, and *mashuach milchamah* — nearly all have a specific Torah text. Those permitted *bechol lashon* that have no formal text — like *tefilla* and *birkat hamazon* — are of course open to any language. And some things with a formal text — like *parshat sotah*, *vidui ma'aser*, and *kriat shema* — are permitted in any language, meaning translation is acceptable. The one exception in the *lashon hakodesh* list is *birkat kohen gadol* on Yom Kippur, which has no fixed text, making it the outlier. On this reading, the emphasis of *lashon hakodesh* is less about the Hebrew language per se and more about the obligation to use the original text rather than a translation.

## Two Approaches

### Rabbi Yehuda

*Rabbi Yehuda* holds that no derivation from Har Gerizim is necessary. *Koh* itself — "exactly like this" — is sufficient to teach that the blessing must be recited in precisely this form, in this language. No external source is needed.

## Standing Requirement

### Koh Tevarchu

A further *baraita* derives from *koh tevarchu* that the blessing must be given while standing. Perhaps, the *baraita* asks, the *kohanim* may be seated? The verse provides the initial opening.

### Eileh Ya'amdu

A *gezerah shava* resolves the question. *Eileh ya'amdu levarech* — "these shall stand to bless" — at Har Gerizim makes clear that standing is required: *mah lehalan be'amida af kan be'amida*.

---

## Standing Requirement…

### Lesharto Ulevarech

*Rabbi Natan* holds that the standing requirement need not be derived from Har Gerizim. The verse *lesharto ulevarech bishmo* — the *kohanim* are chosen to serve God and to bless in His name — is itself sufficient. This verse powerfully links the priestly role of service with the role of blessing.

### Avodah Connection

The derivation proceeds as follows: just as priestly service (*sheirut*) must be performed while standing, so too the blessing must be given while standing — *mah mesharet be'amida af mevarech be'amida*. And how do we know that priestly service itself requires standing? From the verse *la'amod lesharet* — "to stand and to serve."

---

## Two Approaches…

### Rabbi Natan

As the discussion develops, a clear structural pattern emerges. One approach — that of *Rabbi Yehuda* and those who derive requirements from *koh tevarchu* combined with *eileh ya'amdu levarech* — situates *birkat kohanim* within the category of a *beracha*. The relevant constellation is the nature of blessing itself: *koh* points us to the blessing text, and Har Gerizim provides a parallel instance of blessing. The priestly identity is not the hermeneutic focus.

*Rabbi Natan* adopts an entirely different orientation. He consistently derives the requirements of *birkat kohanim* from *lesharto ulevarech bishmo* — framing *birkat kohanim* as a form of *avodah*, priestly service. The model is not "how does one give a blessing" but "what do *kohanim* do in their role as servants of God."

### Conceptual Dispute

This is not merely a technical dispute about which verse to use. The two approaches reflect a deeper disagreement about what *birkat kohanim* fundamentally is. Is it a *beracha* — an act of blessing that happens to be performed by *kohanim* — or is it an *avodah* — a form of priestly service that happens to take the form of a blessing? Both approaches reach the same practical conclusions so far, but the underlying framework diverges significantly.

### Different Conclusions

The stakes of this dispute become visible when one asks whether *birkat kohanim* is a *mitzvah de'oraita* outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. If one learns everything from how *berachot* work — and especially from Har Gerizim, which itself took place outside any *mikdash* — then there is no inherent reason to confine the *mitzvah* to the *mikdash*. The practice outside the *mikdash* is straightforwardly a *mitzvah de'oraita*. But if one learns everything from *sheirut* — from priestly service in the *mikdash* — then one might well conclude that *birkat kohanim* is fundamentally a *mikdash*-centered *avodah*, and whatever is practiced outside is something of a lesser status. This question will continue to resonate throughout the subsequent discussions.

## Hand Position

### Nesi'as Kapayim

A further *baraita* derives from *koh tevarchu* that the blessing requires *nesi'as kapayim* — the lifting of the hands. The verse itself might not make this obvious, so the *baraita* turns to a *gezerah shava*: *ne'emar kan koh tevarchu* and *ne'emar lehallan vayisa Aharon es yadav el ha'am vayivarchaim* — Aharon lifted his hands toward the people and blessed them. Just as there the blessing was given with raised hands, so too here.

### Rabbi Yonatan Challenge

*Rabbi Yonatan* challenges this derivation. If we are going to learn from Aharon's blessing, we are obligated to learn all the contextual circumstances of that blessing: Aharon was the *kohen gadol*, the occasion was *Rosh Chodesh*, and it followed a series of *korbanos tzibbur* at the time of the *miluim*. If we learn from there, perhaps *nesi'as kapayim* is only required when all these circumstances converge — when it is a *kohen gadol*, on *Rosh Chodesh*, after public offerings.

### Hu Uvanav Kol Hayamim

*Rabbi Natan* responds: the derivation from Aharon's blessing is unnecessary. The verse *lesharto ulevarech bishmo hu uvanav kol hayamim* — "to serve and to bless in His name, he and his sons, all the days" — provides the needed answer. From *hu uvanav*, we learn that just as Aharon performed *nesi'as kapayim*, so too his sons do the same — eliminating the restriction to *kohen gadol*. And from *kol hayamim* — "all the days" — we learn that the practice is not limited to special occasions like *Rosh Chodesh*.

### Eliminating Circumstances

This disposes of two of the three limiting circumstances: the *kohen gadol* restriction falls away through *hu uvanav*, and the *Rosh Chodesh* restriction falls away through *kol hayamim*. The question of the structural link is then addressed: the verse *hu uvanav kol hayamim* speaks of *la'amod lesharet* — priestly service — and we connect the *beracha* to the *sheirut* through the other verse, creating the *hekesh* between *beracha* and *sheirut* that makes the *avodah* comparison operative. The general principle is then stated: just as *avodah* is performed the same way by ordinary *kohanim* as by the *kohen gadol*, and under ordinary circumstances as under special ones, the same applies to *birkat kohanim*.

### Tosafot: Korban Type

Tosafot raises a further question. Even granting all of the above — even accepting that *nesi'as kapayim* is not limited to the *kohen gadol* and not limited to *Rosh Chodesh* — how do we know it applies in the absence of a *korban tzibbur*? In the case of Aharon, the setting was a *korban tzibbur*, and *kol hayamim* could itself be satisfied by the *korban tamid*, which is itself a *korban tzibbur* offered every single day. So perhaps *nesi'as kapayim* is required every day — but only because every day there is a *tamid*, a public offering. How do we know it would apply by a *korban yachid*?

### Tosafot Answer

Tosafot resolves this by accepting the implicit premise: in fact, *birkat kohanim* in the *mikdash* was only performed in the context of *korban tzibbur*, not *korban yachid*. This is documented explicitly in the fifth *perek* of Masechta Tamid, which describes the *kohanim* descending after placing the limbs on the ramp, gathering in the *lishkat hagazit*, reciting the *aseret hadibrot*, *shema*, and other prayers, and then blessing the people — and the same practice applied at *musaf* and at the afternoon *tamid*. *Rabbi Yonatan*'s challenge about *korban yachid* was therefore not truly a challenge at all: *nesi'as kapayim* was always tied to *korban tzibbur*.

But this answer carries a consequence that Tosafot makes explicit: if *birkat kohanim* in its full *halachic* form was tied to *korban tzibbur*, then the question of whether the *mitzvah de'oraita* applies outside the *Beis HaMikdash* becomes acute. The *avodah*-centered approach has now established that the practice is inherently bound to the context of communal sacrifice in the *mikdash*.

## God's Name

### Vesamu Et Shemi

The *gemara* turns to the question of the divine name. A *baraita* teaches: *koh tevarchu es benei Yisrael beshem hameforash* — since the verse says *koh*, and the word *koh* specifies the exact form, one reads the name as it is written, yielding the *shem hameforash*. But perhaps one may use only a *kinuy*, a substitute pronunciation?

The *baraita* resolves this from the verse *vesamu es shemi al bnei Yisrael* — "and they shall place My name upon the children of Israel." *Shemi hameyuchad li* — my distinctive, unique name. That name is the *shem hameforash*.

### Outside Mikdash Question

But one might ask: if that is the source, perhaps the *shem hameforash* is to be used even outside the *Beis HaMikdash*? After all, the verse *vesamu es shemi* does not itself specify a location.

The *baraita* answers through a verbal analogy: *ne'emar kan vesamu* and *ne'emar lehallan lasum et shemo sham* — "the place that God has chosen to place His name there." The word *vesamu* here echoes *lasum et shemo sham* there. Just as *lasum et shemo sham* refers to the *Beis Habechira*, the chosen place, so too *vesamu es shemi* applies only in the *Beis Habechira*. Outside the *mikdash*, only the *kinuy* is used.

### Bechol Hamakom

*Rabbi Yoshiya* holds that no such derivation is necessary. He cites the verse *bechol hamakom asher azkir es shemi avo eilecha uverachticha* — "in every place where I will cause My name to be mentioned, I will come to you and bless you." From this verse he derives that wherever God's name is mentioned — meaning wherever the *kohanim* invoke it — God will come and bless.

### Gemara Resistance

The *gemara* resists the straightforward reading of this verse. *Bechol makom salka deitach?* — does it truly mean everywhere? Rashi explains the difficulty on two levels. First, there is a circular problem: the verse *ze shemi leolam veze zichri* already teaches that God's written name is not pronounced as written — *lo keshe'ani nichtav ani nikra* — so the idea that the *shem hameforash* would be used everywhere already runs against an established principle. Second, and more substantively, Rashi notes: *vechi bechol makom shechina ba'ah vesharah* — does the *shechina* truly descend and rest in every place? The verse says *avo eilecha* — "I will come to you" — which implies divine presence descending. That is not a universal phenomenon.

### Reading Out of Order

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ella mikra ze mesuras hu* — this verse must be read out of sequence. The correct reading is: *bechol hamakom asher avo eilecha uverachticha* — wherever I choose to come and bless you, wherever I cause My *shechina* to dwell — *sham azkir es shemi*, there I will cause My name to be mentioned. The location of *avo eilecha uverachticha* is the *Beis Habechira*, and it is there that *azkir es shemi* occurs. Rashi adds that *bechol hamakom* refers to the various sacred sites — such as *Shilo* — that served as the chosen location before the permanent *Beis HaMikdash* was established.

### Theological Balance

Placing the *gemara*'s rearrangement alongside the plain meaning of the verse reveals a profound theological tension. On one side stands *hamakom asher bachar Hashem Elokecha lasum es shemo sham* — one specific, chosen place where God places His name. On the other stands *bechol hamakom asher azkir es shemi* — which, on its face, sounds like God's presence can be invoked anywhere. These two verses pull in opposite directions: one speaks of radical particularity, the other of apparent universality. The *gemara* resolves this tension by rearranging the universal verse so that it, too, points back to the one chosen place. But the tension itself is theologically significant.

### Kohanim's Role

There is a reading of the *psukim* that preserves both dimensions rather than collapsing the universal into the particular. *Lasum es shemo sham* — God places His name in the *Beis HaMikdash*. But the *kohanim* are *vesamu es shemi al bnei Yisrael* — they place God's name upon the people. They do not live only in the *mikdash*; their role, as stated in *koh tevarchu es benei Yisrael*, extends to all of Israel. When they perform *birkat kohanim* and invoke the divine name, they are causing God's name to be mentioned — *azkir es shemi* — and God responds: *avo eilecha uverachticha*, *va'ani avarchem*. On this reading, the verse is not rearranged but understood as a description of the full dynamic of *birkat kohanim*: God concentrates His presence in one place (*lasum es shemo sham*), but commissions the *kohanim* to carry that name outward (*vesamu es shemi*), and promises that wherever they do so, He will bless the people (*bechol hamakom asher azkir es shemi avo eilecha uverachticha*). The universal and the local are both real — the *kohanim* are the bridge between them.

Returning to the structural debate: it is noteworthy that the second approach — the *avodah*-centered one — is precisely the one that resists the universal reading and rearranges the verse. The first approach, less invested in tying *birkat kohanim* to *mikdash*-based service, is more compatible with the universalist implication of *bechol hamakom*. The *avodah* approach needs to pull the verse back toward the *Beis Habechira*; the *beracha* approach is less compelled to do so.

Both approaches, it should be noted, seem to presuppose that *birkat kohanim* applies outside the *mikdash* as a *mitzvah de'oraita* — the *baraitot* in this section treat the distinction between *mikdash* and *medina* as a question of *how* the *mitzvah* is performed (with or without the *shem hameforash*), not *whether* it exists outside. Yet the internal logic of the *avodah* approach — with its emphasis on *sheirut*, *korban tzibbur*, and *mikdash* — creates real hermeneutical pressure toward the conclusion that the full *mitzvah de'oraita* belongs in the *mikdash* alone.

## Additional Requirements

### Who Receives

A further *baraita*: *koh tevarchu es benei Yisrael* — one might think this limits the blessing to native-born Israelites. The verse *emor lahem* — "say to them" — extends it to all, including *geirim* and freed slaves.

### Face to Face

Another *baraita*: *koh tevarchu* — the blessing is given *panim keneged panim*, face to face. Perhaps the *kohanim* could face away from the congregation? The verse *emor lahem* teaches otherwise: just as one speaks to a friend — *ke'adam she'omer lachaveiro* — so here, the *kohanim* must face those they bless.

### Audible Voice

Another *baraita*: *koh tevarchu bekol ram* — the blessing must be given in a loud, fully audible voice. Perhaps a whispered recitation suffices? Again, *emor lahem* teaches: one speaks to a friend in an ordinary, audible manner.

### Calling Kohanim

*Abaye* teaches: *naktinan* — we hold the position that when there are two *kohanim*, the *chazzan* calls out *kohanim* to prompt them to begin. When there is only one *kohen*, the *chazzan* does not call out *kohen*; the single *kohen* begins on his own. The source is *emor lahem* — here read in the liturgical sense, as the caller addressing those who will bless. *Lahem* implies a plural audience; one person alone does not require the call.

### Rav Chisda View

*Rav Chisda* adds a further position: *naktinan*, we hold that a *kohen* may call out *kohanim* to prompt his fellow *kohanim*, but a *Yisrael* leading the service does not call out *kohanim*. His source: *amira mishelachem* — the calling must come from among their own. It may be that having a *Yisrael* instruct *kohanim* to perform their sacred role is considered inappropriate.

### Ruling

The *halacha* follows *Abaye* and not *Rav Chisda*. The *chazzan* calls *kohanim* when there are two, not when there is one — but the caller need not himself be a *kohen*.

It is worth noting that these discussions about the *chazzan* calling *kohanim* are evidently conducted in a contemporary *tefilla* context entirely outside the *mikdash*, which retrospectively confirms that the earlier statements about *oleh laduchan* and the obligations of *birkat kohanim* are also addressed to the *shul* reality — not exclusively to the *mikdash* setting.

## Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi

### God's Desire

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* teaches: how do we know that God desires *birkat kohanim* to be performed? From the verse *vesamu es shemi al bnei Yisrael va'ani avarchem* — "they shall place My name upon the children of Israel, and I will bless them." The phrasing suggests not merely that if the name is placed God will bless, but that God needs this to happen — that the *kohanim* placing His name enables God to bless Israel. God, so to speak, desires to bless His people and requires the *kohanim*' invocation as the vehicle for that blessing.

### Kohen Blessed

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* further teaches: any *kohen* who gives the blessing is himself blessed — *kol kohen hamivarech misbarech*. But a *kohen* who does not give the blessing is himself not blessed. The source: *va'avaracha mevarachecha* — "I will bless those who bless you." The *kohen* who blesses the people places himself within the covenant blessing promised to Avraham, receiving blessing in return.

### Three Mitzvot

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* adds: any *kohen* who does not go up to the *duchan* transgresses three positive commandments — *koh tevarchu*, *emor lahem*, and *vesamu es shemi*. This is striking: alongside the encouragement that the blessing kohen is himself blessed, there is a hard obligation — failure to go up is an active transgression of three *aseis*.

### Ben Gerusha

However, a *kohen* who does not go up on every occasion does not necessarily arouse suspicion on each absence. The *gemara* distinguishes: if a *kohen* goes up *leprakim* — occasionally — then people understand he simply did not go up on this particular occasion, and no one suspects he is a *ben gerusha* or *ben chalutza* (a *kohen* rendered unfit by illegitimate lineage). But if a *kohen* never goes up at all, he arouses suspicion that his lineage disqualifies him. These are two separate concerns — the halachic transgression attaches each time he fails to go up; the social suspicion attaches only to one who never goes up. Rashi explains *leprakim* as referring to *chagim umoadim*, which may reflect the *Ashkenazi* practice of performing *birkat kohanim* only on *yom tov* — though the plain meaning of the *gemara* is simply "sometimes," without specifying which occasions.

## Rabbi Yehoshua ben Lev…

### Timing Restriction

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* further teaches: a *kohen* who does not go up *ba'avoda* — while the *shliach tzibur* is in the midst of the *retzei* blessing — may no longer go up afterward. *Shuv eino oleh*. The source: *vayisa Aharon es yadav el ha'am vayivarchaim vayered me'asos hachatat veha'olah vehashlamim* — Aharon blessed the people while still in the context of the *korbanot*, before descending from the *mizbeach*. The blessing must take place while the *avodah* is still in active progress. Translated into the *shul* setting: the *kohen* must begin his ascent to the *duchan* while the *retzei* is still being recited — not after the *beracha* is complete.

## Timing in Tefilla

### Aaron's Context

This principle — that the blessing must occur within the context of the *avodah* — is itself a remarkable statement about the structure of *tefilla* as it corresponds to the *mikdash*. Even when *birkat kohanim* is performed entirely outside any *mikdash*, the *gemara* insists on situating it within the *avodah* context, represented in *tefilla* by the *retzei* blessing. The verse *vayered me'asos hachatat* makes clear that Aharon had not yet descended — he blessed the people while still above, in the very midst of the sacrificial service.

### Beginning Movement

The *gemara* then asks: *Rav Ami* and *Rav Assi* — who were themselves *kohanim* — apparently went up to perform *birkat kohanim* after the *beracha* of *retzei* was complete. Does this not contradict the ruling just stated? The *gemara* resolves this: *meikara havu akiri kora'aihu matu lo havu matu* — from the beginning they were lifting their legs, they had begun the physical movement, they simply had not yet arrived at the *duchan* by the time the *beracha* was completed. The process of going up was already underway during *retzei*.

### Leg Lifting

This is given a more general formulation by *Rabbi Oshaya*: *lo shanu ela shelo akar raglav* — the rule that one may not go up after the *beracha* ends applies only when the *kohen* has not yet lifted his leg at all during *retzei*. But once he has lifted his leg — even slightly — he may go up even after the *beracha* has concluded.

### Shliach Tzibur

This same principle resolves a related question: if the *shliach tzibur* is himself a *kohen*, may he interrupt his role to perform *birkat kohanim* and then return? The answer is: if he is certain he can do so without losing his place — *im havtachto she-nosei kapav ve-chozer litfilato* — he is permitted. But the difficulty is that as *shliach tzibur*, he was standing at the *amud* reciting *shemoneh esrei* during *retzei*, not moving toward the *duchan*. How can he be considered to have begun the movement?

### Movement Suffices

The *gemara* answers: *hacha nami de'akar porta* — here too, the slightest movement suffices. Even the faintest physical beginning — even a shift of weight, a barely perceptible forward motion — is enough to situate the *kohen* within the *avodah* context and permit the subsequent ascent. The principle is that the connection to *avodah* does not require completed movement; intention expressed in even the most minimal physical act is sufficient.

## Character & Generosity

### Tov Ayin

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* teaches: *ein nosnim kos shel bracha levarech ela letov ayin* — the cup of blessing for *birkat hamazon* is given only to one who is *tov ayin*, of a generous and benevolent disposition. The reasoning: a *beracha* is not only words. The spirit in which a blessing is given matters. A person with a generous heart blesses from abundance; such a blessing carries weight.

### Generous Disposition

The source verse: *tov ayin hu yevorach ki nasan milachmo ladal* — the one of good eye will be blessed, for he gave of his bread to the poor. The *gemara* reads this *midrashically*: do not read *yevorach* (he will be blessed) but *yevarech* (he will bless) — the same quality of generosity that leads one to give to the poor qualifies one to bestow blessings upon others.

### Birds Know

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* further teaches: even birds recognize the stingy person — *afilu ofos makirim betzarei ayin*. A person's character is communicated not only through words but through every dimension of his presence. Someone who is stingy broadcasts that quality non-verbally, and even creatures who do not understand speech can sense it. The source: *ki chinam mezorah hareshes be'einei chol ba'al kanaf* — "for in vain is the net spread in the sight of every winged creature." The birds see someone scattering seed and they know it is a stingy person who will take and not give; they do not come to him. The deeper point is about the *kohanim*: the *beracha* is not merely a verbal formula. A *kohen* of generous spirit communicates that generosity through the blessing itself, and the non-verbal dimension of the blessing is as real as the words.

### Stingy Transgression

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* concludes: *kol haneheneh mitzarei ayin over belav* — one who benefits from a stingy person transgresses a negative commandment.

### Ra Ayin

The source: *al tilcham es lechem ra ayin* — do not eat the bread of one who has a bad eye. *Ki kemo sha'ar benafsho hu* — his soul is pained by it. He may say *echol ushesah* — "eat and drink" — but he does not mean it. His outward hospitality is empty; inwardly, he resents the giving. To accept from him is to take what was never truly offered, which is a form of wrongful acquisition.

### Two Lavin

*Rav Nachman* adds: in fact, such a person transgresses not one but two negative commandments — *al tilcham* and *al sis'av*, the second derived from the continuation of the verse: "do not desire his delicacies." Both the act of eating and the cultivating of desire for what a stingy person offers constitute separate transgressions.
