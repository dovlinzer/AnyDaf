# Nedarim 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hafarah Overview

### Transition to Hafarah

The present daf opens at the very last line of 66b, marking a shift in the tractate's focus. While the preceding *perek* was devoted largely to *hatarah* — the mechanism by which a *beis din* retroactively releases a vow — *hatarah* itself was never the subject of a direct, foundational exposition. The *gemara* never paused to ask: from where do we derive that *hataras nedarim* is possible at all? That question remains, in a sense, suspended. What the Torah does address explicitly is a related but distinct institution: *hafarah*, the annulment or voiding of vows. It is this phenomenon that forms the subject of the new *perek*.

### Basic Halachot

The Torah establishes that a woman is always, conceptually, under the authority of some man. If she is in her father's house, her father has the power to annul her vows. If she is in her husband's house, her husband has that power. The Torah assumes no independent space for a woman between these two domains. Even if she never marries and remains in her father's home well into adulthood, she is conceptually under his authority. The passage from one domain to the other is precisely *nisuin* — the physical transfer from the father's house to the husband's house.

The exception the Torah carves out is explicit: *v'neder almanah u'grushah kol asher asra al nafsha yakum aleha* — the vow of a widow or divorcee stands, because she has left both domains and no man holds authority over her.

Several additional *halachos* govern this institution. First, annulment must occur *b'yom sham'o* — on the day the relevant party hears of the vow; the precise parameters of "that day" will be discussed as the *gemara* develops. Second, alongside the act of voiding, the Torah also recognizes *hakamah* — an affirmative declaration that the vow stands. If the husband or father actively establishes the vow, the opportunity for *hafarah* is lost even within that same day.

It bears noting that Chazal, in their reading of the Torah, do introduce a concept the Torah itself does not explicitly formulate: that once a girl reaches twelve and a half (*bogeres*), she is conceptually independent of her father. The Torah knows no such temporal boundary — in the Torah's framework, a woman moves from father to husband with no intervening independent stage. That independent stage is a rabbinic conceptual development.

### Hafarah vs. Hatarah

A third foundational point concerns the structural difference between *hafarah* and *hatarah*. *Hatarah* is retroactive. It is modeled on the concept of *ta'os* — the premise that had the person genuinely understood the implications of the vow, they would never have taken it. Undoing that misunderstanding retroactively dissolves the vow from its inception, as though it had never bound. *Hafarah*, by contrast, operates *me'kan u'laba* — from this point forward. It does not reach back. The mechanism is not intellectual dissolution but the assertion of authority: the husband or father exercises dominion over the woman's commitments and declares them void. The vow was binding until that moment; it is no longer binding from that moment onward. Accordingly, if a woman violated her vow prior to her husband's *hafarah*, she has genuinely transgressed — *hafarah* cannot reach back to undo that.

### Orphan Question

One might ask: according to the Torah's framework, what of a girl whose father has died and who has not yet married? In principle, she would be independent, since she has no father to place her under authority. Chazal, however, open up an entirely different conceptual landscape. Once the category of *bogeres* is introduced — independent adult status beginning at twelve and a half — the question of the orphaned girl largely loses its urgency. A girl who has passed twelve and a half is already independent regardless of whether her father is living. The large gap that opens up between *bas mitzvah* and *nisuin*, especially as *kiddushei ketanah* became uncommon, creates an expansive zone of female independence that the Torah itself does not map.

## Na'arah Me'orasah

### Mishnah Statement

The *mishnah* opens: *na'ara ham'urasa aviha u'va'alah mefirin nedareha* — the adolescent betrothed girl: her father and her husband annul her vows. This is a case of a woman occupying a genuinely intermediate status. She is a *na'ara* — between twelve and twelve and a half — and thus not yet a *bogeres* who has left her father's authority. At the same time, she is *m'urasa*, betrothed through *kiddushin*, though *nisuin* has not yet occurred. Critically, *nisuin* is the act of transition, the physical movement from the father's house to the husband's house. Until that transition occurs, she remains, in the most concrete sense, in her father's domain.

The *mishnah*'s language — "her father and her husband" — is itself the subject of the entire legal and textual discussion that follows.

### Ran's Analysis

The Ran opens his commentary with a foundational observation: *afilu nedarim she-nadra b'odah penuyah* — even vows she took before the *kiddushin* can be annulled in this framework. The *arus* (groom) can participate in annulling vows that predated his entry into her life entirely — but only *b'shutfuta d'av*, in partnership with the father. Furthermore, the *mishnah*'s use of the term *na'ara* is noted as excluding the *bogeres*: once she is twelve and a half, *d'ein aviha rishus bah* — her father no longer holds authority over her, and accordingly the *arus* too cannot annul, *b'lo shutfuta d'av lo matzi meifer* — without the partnership of the father he has no capacity to act.

This is a striking conceptual point. One might have expected that the *arus*, now that the father is out of the picture, would simply step into that role. But no: the *arus*'s capacity to annul is not an independent power. It is derivative of the father's authority. Where the father has no standing, neither does the *arus*.

### Groom Annuls Pre-Betroth…

This leads directly to an important conceptual clarification. Normally, a husband who enters *nisuin* cannot annul vows his wife took before the marriage. The logic is clear: his domain begins at the moment of *nisuin*, and what preceded it was outside his purview. Why, then, can the *arus* annul vows from before the *erusin*?

The answer follows directly from the principle just established: because it is fundamentally the father who annuls. The father's authority over his daughter is not bounded by the moment of *erusin*. He was always in authority. Since the *arus* acts in partnership with the father, and since it is really the father's power that drives the *hafarah*, the temporal boundary that would limit a husband acting alone does not apply here. The *arus* is, as the Ran puts it, tagging onto the father's participation. The father is primary; the groom is secondary.

The implication is sharp: when the father is absent — whether because the girl is a *bogeres* or because the father has died — the *arus* has no role at all. His power exists only in the shadow of the father's.

### Vows Age 11+1

The category is not limited to a *na'ara* in the strict sense. The Ran notes that the same *halacha* applies to a *ketanah* who is in her twelfth year — after eleven years and one day — because there is a separate principle in *hilchos nedarim* according to which vows taken in the year preceding *bar* or *bas mitzvah* are binding. For a girl who takes a vow in that year, she is still a *ketanah*, but her vow has force, and in that case, too, both the father and the *arus* together would annul it. This demonstrates, again, that the fundamental dynamic is not the girl's maturity but the structure of whose authority she is under.

### Joint Requirement

The *mishnah* continues: *heifer ha'av v'lo heifer ha'baal, heifer ha'baal v'lo heifer ha'av, eino mufar* — if the father annulled without the husband, or the husband without the father, it is not annulled. Neither party acting alone is sufficient. The *mishnah* adds: *v'ein tzarich lomar she'kiyam echad mehem* — and it goes without saying that if one of them performed *hakamah*, actively affirmed the vow, the annulment fails.

### Gemara Parsing

The *gemara* parses this *mishnah* carefully. The statement that if the father annulled alone or the groom annulled alone it does not work is necessary, because one might have thought that either party acting alone suffices. The *gemara* therefore notes: *mahu d'teima o aviha o va'alah* — lest one read the *mishnah* disjunctively, thinking either the father or the husband may act. The *mishnah* teaches that both must act together.

But then the *gemara* questions why the *mishnah* needs to state that *hakamah* by one of them ruins the process. If neither can annul alone, how much more so when one of them actively affirms! *Hashet'a yesh lomar heifer zeh belo zeh belo klum* — if one annuls without the other it accomplishes nothing, then *kiyam echad mehem lamah li* — why state the *hakamah* case at all? The *gemara* acknowledges this and says *tzaricha* — it is necessary to teach — and proceeds to explain in what unusual case the *hakamah* provision adds something new.

## Affirm vs. Void

### One Voids, One Affirms

The case the *gemara* constructs is this: party A performed *hafarah*, party B performed *hakamah*. Now, ordinarily, that is the end of the story — the *hakamah* has affirmed the vow and the prior *hafarah* of A is rendered ineffective, since the two must act together and B has instead gone the opposite direction. But the *gemara* introduces a twist.

### Hatarah on Hakamah

A *hakamah* is a verbal act — a declaration that the vow stands. As such, it is subject to *hatarah*: just as a vow itself can be retroactively released through a *chacham* who demonstrates it was made in error, so too a *hakamah* made in error (*hakamah b'ta'us*) can be retroactively released. The mechanism of *hatarah* applies to the *hakamah* just as it applies to any other consequential verbal commitment.

### Hakamah Reversal Problem

So the case is: A performed *hafarah*, B performed *hakamah*, and then B went to a *chacham* and had the *hakamah* released retroactively. The obvious intuition is: fine, the *hakamah* is undone as though it never happened, so A's *hafarah* still stands — now let B add his *hafarah* and it is complete. But the *gemara* rejects this.

### Gemara Conclusion

*V'chazar hamekayem v'nish'al al hakamaso* — and the one who affirmed went and sought release from his affirmation. *Mahu d'teima mai d'meifer me'ikara hashta v'eima* — one might think that what he voided initially, he has now reversed, so we are back to square one and can proceed. *Ka mashmalan d'ba'inan sh'neihem b'vas achas* — no, the *gemara* teaches that we need both parties to perform *hafarah* together, meaning A must now perform a new *hafarah*. The *hafarah* A performed before the *hakamah* cannot combine retroactively with B's new *hafarah* now that the *hakamah* has been undone.

The logic, though terse in the *gemara*, is roughly as follows. At the moment B performed *hakamah*, A's *hafarah* became effectively inert — not merely incomplete, but drained of any halachic potential. Even if that *hakamah* is later retroactively dissolved, the *hafarah* A performed had already passed through a period in real time during which it was entirely powerless. That passage is irreversible.

The Rambam takes this even further: not only can A's prior *hafarah* not combine with B's subsequent one, but A cannot even now perform a new *hafarah*. Once A's initial *hafarah* lost its effectiveness — even if for a reason later retroactively undone — A has exhausted his single opportunity. The rationale is that you cannot perform *hafarah* a second time after the first was already done, and since the first is now spent, A is out.

### Ran's Logical Question

The Ran raises a sharp objection to the *gemara*'s ruling. The entire power of *hatarah* is retroactivity. When we say the *hakamah* has been released retroactively, we are saying that in retrospect there was never a *hakamah*. It is as though it never happened. If so, why does A's *hafarah* suffer? Looking back, there was no *hakamah* intervening. The comparison the Ran invokes: if a man betroths a woman on condition that she has no outstanding vows, and she has vows at the time, and then she subsequently obtains a retroactive *hatarah* — the *kiddushin* takes effect retroactively, because retroactively she had no vows at the moment of betrothal. Why cannot the same retroactive logic restore A's *hafarah* to full effectiveness?

### Ran's Answer

The Ran offers two lines of response. The first is conceptual: even when *hatarah* dissolves a verbal act retroactively, it does not undo the fact that the act occurred. What it undoes is the binding force of the words. The act of *hakamah* — the physical speaking of words of affirmation — remains a historical fact, even if its legal consequences are erased. That act, having occurred, may be sufficient to create an obstacle, even after its effects are dissolved.

The second line of response reflects a structural asymmetry. *Hafarah* is a peculiarly fragile kind of act. Unlike *kiddushin*, which is a concrete act with immediate independent legal force, *hafarah* is purely verbal and, when only one party has acted, is inherently incomplete — it has no standing effect until the other party joins. It is waiting, in a state of suspended potential, for its partner. An act of such tenuous and inchoate character, which passed through a period in real time where it was not merely incomplete but actively foreclosed by the *hakamah*, loses that potential permanently. The Ran acknowledges he does not fully resolve the theoretical mechanism, but the *halacha* stands: even retroactively undoing the *hakamah* does not restore A's *hafarah*.

### Why Mention Hakamah

This returns us to the *gemara*'s question: why does the *mishnah* mention *hakamah* at all, given that it "goes without saying"? The Ran answers: the simple case — where one party does *hakamah* and that is the end of the story — indeed goes without saying. But the *mishnah* introduces *hakamah* specifically to teach that *hakamah* can create an obstacle even in more complex scenarios: specifically, the case where a *hakamah* is made and then retroactively dissolved, yet still manages to block the earlier *hafarah* from combining with a later one. That *chiddush* is not obvious, and it required the *mishnah* to mention *hakamah* in order to convey it.

## Scriptural Sources

### Reading the Pesukim

The *gemara* now turns to the scriptural grounding of *hafarah*, and we read through the relevant *pesukim* of Parashat Matot. The section opens: *vayedaber Moshe el rashei hamatot livnei yisrael lemor zeh hadavar asher tziva Hashem* — God spoke to Moses to tell the heads of the tribes. The phrase *rashei hamatot* is noted by Chazal as a small textual peg: normally the address would be *bnei yisrael*, so the mention of tribal heads, figures of authority, provides a hint in the direction of *hatarah* — though the text itself is entirely about *hafarah*. The textual basis for *hataras nedarim* itself is a separate discussion.

The first *pasuk* addresses the general case of vowing: *ish ki yidor neder la-Hashem o hishava shevua le-esor issar al nafsho lo yachel devaro ke-chol ha-yotze mipiv ya'aseh* — a man who takes a vow or an oath to prohibit something upon himself may not violate his word; he must do as he said. This *pasuk* establishes both the positive obligation and the prohibition of *bal yachel* for vows in general. It is worth noting that both *neder* and *shevua* are mentioned here, though in the framing of making something forbidden — which has been the consistent assumption throughout the tractate, where *nedarim* are understood primarily as prohibitions placed on objects.

### Father's Role

The text then shifts: *v'isha ki tidor neder la-Hashem v'asra issar b'veit aviha b'neureiha* — a woman who takes a vow while in her father's house in her youth. The father hears the vow and the prohibition she has placed upon herself. Notably, from this point the *shevua* drops from the text entirely — a fact consistent with the tractate's sustained analysis that *nedarim* are fundamentally about making objects forbidden, which is the core function of a *neder* rather than a *shevua*.

*V'hecherish lah aviha* — if the father is silent — *v'kamu kol nedareha v'chol issar asher asra al nafsha yakum* — all her vows and prohibitions stand. His silence functions as passive *hakamah*. *V'im hini aviha osa b'yom sham'o* — but if her father *hini* (annulled) it on the day he hears — *kol nedareha v'essareha asher asra al nafsha lo yakum* — they are not established. The word *hini* is an unusual term for annulment, but the content is clear: the father has the power to void his daughter's vows, and he must exercise it on the day he hears.

*V'Hashem yislach lah* — and God will forgive her — *ki hini aviha osa*. The phrase "God will forgive her" is unexpected: she has not necessarily violated anything, since the father annulled the vow. Chazal explain: the father annulled it without her knowledge, and she subsequently violated it, believing the vow to still be in force. On the level of objective action she committed no transgression — the vow was already void. But subjectively, in her own mind and intention, she experienced herself as transgressing. It is for that psychological act, that *kavanah* of transgression, that she requires divine forgiveness even in the absence of an objective *ma'aseh aveira*.

### Husband's Role

The second paragraph begins: *v'im hayo tihye l'ish* — if she comes to be to a man. The plain sense of *tihye* in many contexts in the Torah is *nisuin*, full marriage. *V'nedareha aleha* — and her vows are upon her from before. The husband hears, is silent, and the vows stand. If he annuls on the day he hears, they are dissolved, and *V'Hashem yislach lah*.

### Widow/Divorcee

At this point the Torah inserts the case of the widow and divorcee: *v'neder almanah u'grushah kol asher asra al nafsha yakum aleha*. Having left her father's house and her husband's house, she stands under no man's authority, and her vows stand unconditionally. No one can annul on her behalf.

### Vows During Marriage

The Torah then adds a third paragraph: *v'im beis isha nadra* — if she took the vow while already in her husband's house. This is a fresh vow, taken during the marriage itself. *O asra issar al nafsha b'shavua* — or she prohibited something upon herself by oath. The husband hears, is silent, and the vow stands. *V'im haifer yaifer osam isha b'yom sham'o* — if the husband annuls them on the day he hears — *kol motza sefaseha l'nedareha v'l'issar nafsha lo yakum* — all that issued from her lips will not stand. *Isha haiferam v'Hashem yislach lah*.

## Redundancy Problem

### Two Nisuin Cases?

The *gemara* now confronts a fundamental problem. We have two separate paragraphs about a husband annulling his wife's vows. The second paragraph explicitly concerns vows taken *b'veit isha*, during the marriage. The first paragraph, which says *v'nedareha aleha* — her vows were already upon her — seems to describe vows predating the marriage. If both paragraphs concern a *nesuah*, a fully married woman, and if the first establishes that the husband can annul even prior vows, then the second paragraph is entirely superfluous: the case it covers is less novel and less demanding than the first.

The redundancy is what forces Chazal to read the *pesukim* otherwise. If reading them both as *nisuin* creates an impossible redundancy, then one of them must be about something different.

The Ramban, in his commentary on the *parashah*, takes note of this problem explicitly. His position is that the *peshat* of the text reads both paragraphs as referring to a *nesuah* — a fully married woman. The repetition, on a *peshat* level, is a genuine difficulty, but it is the plain sense of the words. Nevertheless, he acknowledges, Chazal have read it otherwise, and he concludes *v'hakabalah tachria* — the received tradition adjudicates. That closing phrase is telling: the Ramban is not fully satisfied, but accepts the rabbinic reading as authoritative.

### Both Nisuin Theory

The *gemara* entertains the possibility that both paragraphs address *nisuin*. If so, why would the Torah need two paragraphs? Perhaps, the *gemara* suggests, the second paragraph — *v'im beis isha nadra* — comes to teach a *chiddush* about the first: specifically, *she'ein ha'baal meifer b'kodmin* — the husband cannot annul vows that predate the marriage. The first paragraph contains the phrase *v'nedareha aleha* — her vows were upon her — and one might have thought this means the husband can reach back to prior vows. The second paragraph, by emphasizing *beis isha*, "in her husband's house," would then be a corrective: his power of annulment extends only to vows made within the marriage, not before it.

This reading attempts to make both paragraphs work together around *nisuin* without redundancy. But the *gemara* refuses to accept it. Even if the second paragraph adds the *chiddush* that prior vows cannot be annulled, one is still left asking why the first paragraph is necessary at all. If the second paragraph already tells us everything — the husband can annul vows made during marriage, and cannot annul prior ones — what does the first paragraph add? *Sof sof lamah li* — ultimately, why do I need it? The redundancy is not dissolved; it is only shifted.

### Havayah = Erusin

The *gemara* then proposes a different approach: *havayah k'kiddushin mashma* — the word *tihye* (becoming) in the phrase *v'im hayo tihye l'ish* connotes *kiddushin*, betrothal, not *nisuin*. The *gemara* in tractate *Kiddushin* draws interpretive connections between the language of *havayah* and the language of betrothal, and on that basis Chazal read the first paragraph as describing an *arusah*, a betrothed woman, not a *nesuah*.

The Ran notes that this reading is not entirely straightforward. *Havayah* can mean *nisuin* as well — it can mean both. But since it can also be read as *erusin*, and since reading it as *erusin* resolves the redundancy, Chazal adopt that reading. With the first paragraph now understood as the case of an *arusah*, and the second as the case of a *nesuah*, the two paragraphs address different situations and each is independently necessary.

### Father-Only Theory

But even granting that the first paragraph addresses an *arusah*, a question remains: the paragraph mentions only the husband (or groom). How does Chazal's conclusion — that both father *and* groom must act — emerge from a text that seems to discuss only one party?

The *gemara* entertains a position consistent with the principle that the groom has no real authority before *nisuin*: *eima av lechudei meifer* — perhaps the father alone annuls. The mention of the groom in the *pasuk* would then serve a different purpose: not to give him a positive role, but to signal that if he performs *hakamah* he can block the process. The groom's only function is as a potential obstacle, not as a necessary co-participant.

This reading has a certain logical appeal. If we know that the father is the primary authority before *nisuin* and the groom has no genuine independent standing, why should we read the *pasuk* as requiring the groom's participation? Perhaps his mention is only cautionary — beware, if he affirms the vow, the father's annulment will fail.

### Father-Only Fails

The *gemara* rejects this reading, however, by noting that it makes the first paragraph of the *parashah* — the section about a girl in her father's house before she has any *arus* — completely unnecessary. If the father can annul alone even when there is an *arus*, how much more obviously can he annul when there is no *arus* at all. *Hashta yesh lomar bimkom arus meifer av lechudei* — if in the presence of a groom the father acts alone — *shelo bimkom arus mibaya* — is it necessary to tell me about the case without a groom? Of course the father's authority is established in the simpler case; you would never need to state it first.

The Ran explains what the *gemara* is driving at: the Torah clearly intends the opening section about the father to be a necessary teaching, not a redundant prelude. If the father-alone theory were correct, that opening section would be logically dependent on — and derivable from — the more complex *arus* case, which is backwards. You establish simple cases before complex ones; you do not require a complex case to be stated first in order to understand a simple one.

## Partnership Model

### Groom-Only Reading

The *gemara* now reverses course entirely and tests the opposite extreme: *eima arus lechudei meifer* — perhaps the groom alone annuls, with no need for the father. This is actually far closer to the *peshat* of the *pesukim*. The text speaks of the husband's role; if we are reading the first paragraph as *erusin*, then the plain sense is that the groom holds the authority. Perhaps the mention of the father in the structure of the *parasha* is merely contextual, and the groom, now that she is *m'urasa*, is the sole relevant authority.

### Husband Case Objection

But this reading too generates a redundancy problem. If the groom can annul entirely without the father, then the case of the *nesuah* — the fully married woman, *beis isha* — follows *kal vachomer*: if the groom can do it even when she is still conceptually in her father's house, then certainly the husband can do it after *nisuin* when she is fully under his authority. There would be no need for the Torah to state the husband's case separately.

*U'mah bimkom av arus meifer lechudei* — if in the presence of a father the groom annuls alone — *shelo bimkom av mibaya* — when the father is entirely out of the picture after *nisuin*, is it even a question? The *nesuah* paragraph would be entirely derivable by *kal vachomer* and would serve no independent purpose.

### Husband Limitation

The *gemara* attempts to rescue the groom-only reading by suggesting that the *nesuah* paragraph adds a genuine *chiddush* even in this framework: *she'eino meifer b'kodmin* — the husband cannot annul prior vows, only those made during the marriage. This would distinguish the *nesuah* case from the *arus* case, since the *arus* — acting in the context of a woman still fundamentally in her father's house — can reach prior vows.

But this response, the *gemara* observes, actually creates a new difficulty. If the *nesuah* paragraph's function is to teach that the husband cannot annul prior vows, then implicitly the *arus* paragraph teaches the opposite — that the *arus* can. But that implication of the first paragraph would then itself be informative in a way that makes the reader wonder: why can the *arus* do something the husband cannot? The answer, as we know, is *shutfuta d'av* — it is really the father doing it. But if that is the answer, the groom-only reading cannot be correct, because the father's involvement is the very explanation for the *arus*'s extended capacity. *Ela lav mishum shutfuta d'av* — is this not precisely because of the father's partnership?

### Shutfuta Resolution

The only reading that resolves all the tensions simultaneously is the *shutfuta* model: neither the father alone nor the groom alone, but both together. Each paragraph then serves an independent purpose: the first establishes the unique joint requirement for the *na'ara me'urasa*, and the second establishes the husband's independent but limited authority after *nisuin*. Neither is redundant, and neither is derivable from the other.

### Father's Fundamentality

Even within the *shutfuta* framework, it is essential to understand that the partnership is not between equals. The father remains the primary authority. The evidence is structural: when the father is absent — when the girl is a *bogeres* or the father has died — the *arus* can accomplish nothing at all. The *hafarah* is fundamentally the father's act; the groom participates but does not initiate. The reason the *arus* can annul vows predating the *erusin* is precisely this: since it is really the father's authority being exercised, temporal boundaries linked to the groom's entry into her life are irrelevant.

The final word of *b'yom sham'o* governs the entire structure. Anything heard more than a day before — whether heard by the father before the *kiddushin* or by both parties at different times — has already achieved its finalized status: either it was passively established by the father's silence or actively annulled. The groom's role pertains only to matters still within the window of *yom sham'o* at the time both parties are in a position to act. These complexities of timing — whose clock governs, what happens when the father hears on one day and the groom hears a week later — will be developed as the *perek* continues.

What this *perek* establishes at its outset is a new halachic reality, one the *pesukim* do not state plainly and that required Chazal to work carefully through the verses' apparent redundancy to uncover. The *arus* is given any role at all only because of his connection to the father's authority — and that is why the *parasha* cannot be reduced either to the father alone or to the groom alone. It is the *shutfuta*, the partnership, that the Torah encodes — with the father as the fundamental and primary partner, and the groom as the necessary but secondary one.
