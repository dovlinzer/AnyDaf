# Bava Batra 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Umka Veruma Review

### Opening & Dedication

Today's learning of daf 64 is dedicated as a *refuah sheleimah* for Aryeh Menachem ben Raizel Gila, who is undergoing delicate surgery. We pick up at the *mishnah* and continue the broad discussion of what is implicitly included — or not included — when one sells a house or other property.

### Recap: House Sales

The preceding discussion centered on how much a standard sale implicitly conveys: specifically, whether selling a house automatically transfers air rights and rights to the ground beneath it, or whether the seller must use the emphatic phrase *umka veruma* — "its depth and its height" — to convey those rights. The conclusion reached was that *umka veruma* conveys the property rights beneath the ground. However, if one wishes to include things already physically constructed beneath the surface — such as an existing well — even more emphatic language is required: *mitehom ha'ara ad rom rakia*, "from the depths of the earth to the height of the heavens."

### Rights vs. Structures

The critical distinction underlying this discussion is between abstract rights — the right to dig, to use the airspace, to access what lies beneath — and existing physical structures. A sale of the house, even accompanied by the phrase *umka veruma*, conveys the former but not the latter. The ground rights are transferred; the structure is not.

### Well in House Floor

This leads to a striking detail that the *Rashbam* emphasizes throughout: the *bor* (well) and *dus* (cistern) under discussion are not located in a yard adjacent to the house. They are dug directly into the floor of the house itself. This makes the ruling considerably more surprising. One would naturally assume that a well embedded in the floor of a house, used to supply water to that very house, would be included in a sale of the house — especially when the seller explicitly conveys *umka veruma*. Yet the *mishnah* rules otherwise: the well is a separate, independent structure and is not conveyed by the sale. To include it, the seller would need to use the more expansive formula of *mitehom ha'ara ad rom rakia*.

At this point, a terminological difficulty in the *mishnah* prompted brief discussion. The *mishnah* lists both a *bor* and a *dus* as not being sold. *Ravina* was troubled by this: what distinguishes the two? *Rav Tosefa'ah* cited a *braita*: both are dug into the ground, but a *bor* is dug into firm earth whose walls hold on their own, while a *dus* is dug into softer ground and therefore requires a built stone lining to prevent collapse. *Rav Ashi* independently posed the same question, and *Mar Kashisha bereih deRav Chisda* supplied him with the same *braita* in reply.

## Access Rights Debate

### Seller Retains Well

Having established that the well is not sold when the house is sold, the *mishnah* raises a practical question of real consequence: if the seller retains ownership of a well embedded in the floor of the house he just sold, how does he get to it? The *mishnah* frames this as a debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim*.

### R. Akiva: Generous Sale

*Vetzarich likach lo derech divrei Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva rules that the seller, having retained the well, must nonetheless purchase access rights from the buyer in order to reach it. In other words, although the seller kept ownership of the well, the sale of the house did not preserve for him a right of passage through that house.

### Chachamim: Seller Retains

The *Chachamim* disagree: *eino tzarich likach lo derech* — the seller need not purchase access. Having retained the well, he implicitly retained the right of passage to reach it. On this view, the buyer of the house acquires the house under the condition that the seller may continue to enter and use his well. This is, as noted, quite a remarkable outcome: the buyer pays for a house and finds that the previous owner retains not only a well in the basement but an ongoing right to enter the premises.

The *mishnah* adds one qualification on which Rabbi Akiva concurs: if, in the deed of sale, the seller explicitly stated "I am selling the house *except for* the well" — *chutz me-ha-bor* — then Rabbi Akiva agrees that the seller does retain access rights. Since retaining the well was already the default rule, the fact that the seller went out of his way to state it explicitly is interpreted by Rabbi Akiva as signaling an intention to retain something beyond the mere ownership of the well — namely, the access rights to it.

### Seller Sells Well

The *mishnah* then presents the inverse case: *machar le-acher* — the seller sells the well but retains the house. Here the positions of Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim* reverse. Rabbi Akiva rules that the buyer of the well does not need to purchase access rights — they come automatically with the purchase. The *Chachamim* rule the opposite: even having bought the well, the buyer must separately acquire a right of passage.

### Generous Seller Principle

What accounts for Rabbi Akiva's position across both cases? The *Gemara* identifies it as the principle of *mocher be-ayin yafah hu mocher* — a seller sells with a generous eye. Crucially, the access rights are not treated as an attribute that automatically accompanies whoever owns the well at any given moment. Rather, Rabbi Akiva's principle concerns the direction of generosity in a transaction: the seller, whoever he is, is assumed to be giving the buyer the fullest possible deal. Abstract rights that are not explicitly reserved — such as rights of access — are presumed to have been granted to the buyer.

This means that in both scenarios — selling the house while retaining the well, and selling the well while retaining the house — the access rights flow to the buyer. When the seller retains the well, he nonetheless gave up his access rights in favor of the buyer of the house. When the seller retains the house, he gave up his access rights in favor of the buyer of the well. The *Chachamim*, by contrast, follow the principle of *mocher be-ayin ra'ah hu mocher* — the seller holds back as much as he can — and therefore the access rights always remain with the seller, regardless of who holds the well.

One participant noted that this is consistent with a broader Talmudic instinct — *ein adam mocher* — the general reluctance attributed to sellers to fully divest themselves of their property. That framing would suggest the default should favor the seller, which aligns with the *Chachamim*. Yet Rabbi Akiva's principle cuts the other way when it comes to abstract access rights specifically. It is worth noting, however, that the debate here concerns *access*, not physical property — a distinction that may explain why the principle can operate differently here than in other contexts.

## Competing Claims

### Generous vs. Stingy Sale

The *Gemara* now attempts to characterize the debate of Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim* more precisely. The initial framing is straightforward: Rabbi Akiva holds *mocher be-ayin yafah mocher* — a seller sells generously — while the *Chachamim* hold *mocher be-ayin ra'ah mocher* — a seller holds back. The *Gemara* further notes that wherever in *Shas* Rabbi Akiva's position of *mocher be-ayin yafah mocher* appears, it must ultimately trace back to this *mishnah* as its source.

### Privacy Protection Theory

The *Gemara* then challenges this broad reading. Perhaps the debate of Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim* here is not a general principle about how sellers conduct transactions, but a specific, situational judgment. According to this reading, Rabbi Akiva is sensitive to the psychology of the buyer: *eino rotzeh sheyitein ma'osav veyihyu acherim dorsim* — a person does not pay money for a house and then expect strangers to be trampling through it. Rabbi Akiva's ruling is therefore not a universal principle of generosity but a particular recognition that no buyer would consent to purchasing a house under such conditions.

### Power Position Theory

The *Chachamim*, on this alternative reading, reason from the position of the seller: if the seller is receiving payment and retaining the well, he is in a position of strength and would naturally expect to retain access to his own property. The phrase the *Gemara* uses — *shita motz* — is somewhat obscure, but the general point is that the seller, having gotten value from the transaction, is not simply going to relinquish his ability to access what he still owns.

### Positions Reverse

The *Gemara* immediately dismantles both of these local readings by pointing to the *seifa* — the latter half of the *mishnah*. If Rabbi Akiva were exclusively protecting the homeowner's privacy, and if the *Chachamim* were exclusively protecting the well owner's access, then their positions should remain fixed across both scenarios. Yet they do not: when the seller sells the well and retains the house, their positions reverse entirely. Rabbi Akiva, who in the first case protected the homeowner by denying the seller access, now protects the buyer of the well by granting him access. The *Chachamim*, who in the first case protected the seller's access, now deny access to the buyer of the well, thereby protecting the seller-homeowner.

The reversal demonstrates that neither position is simply about protecting one fixed party. Rather, the consistent logic across both cases is: Rabbi Akiva always favors the buyer, and the *Chachamim* always favor the seller. This brings the debate squarely back to *batar da'ata de-lokei'ach azlinan* versus *batar da'ata de-mocher azlinan* — and whether that is meaningfully different from the broad formulation of *mocher be-ayin yafah* versus *mocher be-ayin ra'ah*.

## Tree Case Analysis

### Tosafot Limitation

*Tosafot* draws an important methodological boundary here. The question of whether one can generalize from this debate of Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim* to all cases of ambiguity in a sale depends on a key distinction. *Tosafot* states: *heikha de-ika ta'ama de-drisha u-frisha* — the principle of Rabbi Akiva that favors the buyer applies specifically where there are legitimate competing claims on both sides, each of whom can articulate a concrete grievance about their rights being compromised.

### Tree Sale Parallel

The *Gemara* introduces a parallel case to test the limits of the principle: the sale of a tree. If one sells a tree while retaining the surrounding land, a question arises about the plot of earth directly beneath the tree. The *Gemara* applies the same debate — Rabbi Akiva would say, *be-ayin yafah hu mocher*, the seller included the ground beneath in the sale; the *Chachamim* would say the seller retained it.

### Future Replanting Rights

The practical stakes become clear when the tree eventually dies: does the buyer have the right to replant another tree in that spot? That depends on whether he owns the ground directly beneath. If Rabbi Akiva is correct, the buyer received the ground with the tree and may replant. If the *Chachamim* are correct, that ground remained with the seller, and the buyer has no right to replant.

### Rights vs. Package

The contrast between the tree case and the well-and-house case is instructive. In the well-and-house scenario, the moment the sale is complete, both parties already have a concrete complaint: the seller who retained the well cannot get to it, and the buyer who purchased the house faces the prospect of the seller barging in. Each side's rights are immediately and tangibly compromised. The question of who prevails is a question of adjudicating between two active, ongoing grievances.

In the tree case, by contrast, no such immediate conflict exists. The buyer uses his tree; the seller uses his surrounding land; no one is trampling anywhere or being denied access to anything. The only question is prospective — if the tree dies, did the sale package include the ground for future replanting? This is purely a question of what was encompassed in the transaction, not a question of whose existing rights are being violated right now. *Tosafot* therefore argues that the generalization of the debate to cases like the tree — where there are no immediate competing grievances — may not follow automatically. The principle of *batar da'ata de-lokei'ach* versus *batar da'ata de-mocher* operates in its clearest form when each side has a live complaint; it is a separate and independent question whether the same principle determines what was "included in the package" when no such competing grievances exist.

## Mishnah Repetition

### Field Sale Parallel

The *mishnah* repeats the same structural debate in the context of a field sale: when one sells a field, the wells within it are not included — *lo et ha-bor velo et ha-gat* — neither the well, nor the threshing floor, nor the pigeon coop (*shovach*), whether they are currently in use or lying fallow. These are treated as separate units that must be explicitly conveyed. And once again the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim* emerges: Rabbi Akiva holds that if the seller retains the well in the field he sold, he must purchase access rights from the buyer; the *Chachamim* hold he need not.

### Why Repeat?

The *Gemara* asks: *ha su lamma li?* Why state this identical debate again in the context of a field when it was already established for a house? The fact of repetition demands explanation.

### Different Complaints

One answer the *Gemara* considers is that the repetition is necessary to teach two concrete cases, not to generalize the principle. The concerns animating the debate differ between house and field. By a house, the complaint of the homeowner is one of *tzni'ut* — privacy — a qualitatively distinctive grievance that might be considered especially weighty. By a field, the complaint of the field owner is that someone traversing the field to reach his well damages the crop underfoot — an economic harm, but of a different character. One might have thought that the privacy concern in the house gives the homeowner's claim special standing, or conversely that the trampling harm in the field makes it more compelling. Therefore, the *mishnah* needed to state both cases to teach that the same rule applies in each, despite the different character of the underlying complaints.

### Broad Application

Yet the *Gemara* ultimately concludes that the repetition does intend to broaden the debate as generally as possible. The fact that the same dispute is stated twice — first for a house, then for a field — signals that Rabbi Akiva's principle of *mocher be-ayin yafah mocher* and the *Chachamim*'s principle of *mocher be-ayin ra'ah mocher* are not confined to specific scenarios but constitute a broad framework for resolving ambiguity in sales wherever competing claims arise.

### Field Sale Flip

The *mishnah* also presents the inverse case for the field — *mocher le-acher* — where the seller sells the well but retains the field. Again Rabbi Akiva rules that the buyer of the well does not need to purchase access (*ein tzarich likach lo derech*), and the *Chachamim* rule that she does. The *Gemara* asks: *ha su lamma li?* We already know from the house case and the field case that Rabbi Akiva always favors the buyer and the *Chachamim* always favor the seller. Why state the flip scenario for the field as well?

The answer: the fourfold repetition — house with retained well, house with sold well, field with retained well, field with sold well — establishes beyond doubt that the debate is a general and broadly applicable one. *Ela lav ha kamashma lan de-Rabbi Akiva savar mocher be-ayin yafah mocher ve-rabbanan savri mocher be-ayin ra'ah mocher* — *shema mina*. The abstract principle is now fully established.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Shmuel's Division

The *Gemara* introduces a parallel dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerning the division of a parental estate between brothers. *Rav Nachman amar Shmuel*: when brothers divide an inherited estate — *ha-achim she-chalku* — they acquire no reciprocal rights in each other's portions.

### No Cross-Rights

Shmuel enumerates the consequences: *ein lahem lo derech zeh al zeh* — no right of passage across the other's portion to reach a well; *velo sulam zeh al zeh* — no right to place a ladder on the other's portion to access an upper story; *velo chalonot zeh al zeh* — no right to demand that the other refrain from building in a way that blocks one's windows. To illustrate: if brothers divide a property in which one inherits the ground floor with the yard and the other inherits the upper story, the one with the upper story has no right to place his ladder in the other's yard. If one inherits the house and the other inherits the surrounding land, the land owner may build immediately adjacent to the house and block the windows. And if one inherits a field that had relied on a well now located in the other's portion, he has no claim to water from that well. *Ve-zaharu ba-hem halachot kevuot hem* — be careful with these, they are fixed and established rules.

### Rav's Position

Rav disagrees: *yesh lahem* — the brothers do retain those necessary usage rights across each other's portions. The question immediately arises how this debate maps onto that of Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim*. The *Gemara* notes that when brothers divide an estate, each is simultaneously seller and buyer: ראובן is, in effect, selling his half to שמעון and שמעון is selling his half to ראובן. If *mocher be-ayin yafah mocher* — if each is generous in what he conveys to the other — then what each receives, he receives completely and without residual claims from the other. שמעון received his field fully and completely, which means ראובן has no right of access to the well in it. That is Shmuel's position, paralleling Rabbi Akiva. Rav, who says the brothers do retain cross-rights, parallels the *Chachamim*: *mocher be-ayin ra'ah mocher* — each seller holds back what he needs to make his own property viable.

### Parental Usage Principle

The *Gemara* notes, however, a distinctive argument available in the context of a parental estate. שמעון might argue: *be-ina lemeidar bah ke-heichi de-dar bah aba* — I intend to use this property the way our father used it. The implicit understanding of any division of a parental estate is that the resulting portions remain usable in the way they functioned during the father's lifetime. *Tachat avotecha yihyu vanecha* — children stand in place of their fathers. When our father was alive, this field was irrigated from that well; that attic was reached by a ladder placed in that yard. The division should therefore be understood as preserving those conditions. This reasoning is specific to the case of a parental estate and does not apply to an ordinary commercial sale, which is why it cannot simply be derived from the debate of Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim*.

### Why Both Cases Needed

The *Gemara* therefore concludes that both disputes — the one between Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim* about ordinary sales, and the one between Rav and Shmuel about division of a parental estate — must each be stated independently. Had only the case of ordinary sales been stated, one might have thought that Shmuel, in a case of a parental estate, would concede that the brothers retain cross-rights because of the principle of *be-ina lemeidar bah ke-heichi de-dar bah aba*. And had only the case of the parental estate been stated, one might have thought that Rav, in a case of an ordinary sale, would concede to the buyer's generosity argument. Each case has a distinctive internal logic, and both must be stated in order to establish that the debate runs consistently across both contexts.

## Halachic Ruling

### Follow Shmuel

*Rav Nachman* asked *Rav Huna* directly: *hilchasa kevasin o hilchasa kevasaichu?* — do we rule like your camp (Rav) or like ours (Shmuel)? *Rav Huna* replied: *hilchasa kevasaichu* — the ruling is like you, like Shmuel. The *halacha* follows Shmuel, which means it follows Rabbi Akiva's position of *mocher be-ayin yafah hu mocher*.

### Practical Authority

The reason given is striking: *di-mekarvu lei l'vava de-reish galusa de-shachichi dayani* — because you (Shmuel and his circle) are close to the gate of the *Reish Galusa*, where judges are regularly convened. The word *bava* — gate — is itself the root of the name *Bava Batra*. The point is not merely geographic proximity but epistemic: being immersed in the actual adjudication of real monetary cases, observing how disputes unfold in practice *le-ma'aseh*, gives an authority a deeper and more reliable grasp of how the law should operate. One who has seen countless real transactions — who knows what buyers and sellers actually expect, how ambiguity actually plays out in lived experience — is better positioned to rule on the law governing those transactions than one who reasons solely from texts.

### Rav-Shmuel Rule

This also reflects the well-known general principle in *dinei mamonot*: wherever Rav and Shmuel dispute a question of monetary law, we rule like Shmuel. That rule itself is presumably grounded in the same consideration — Shmuel's unparalleled exposure to practical judgment in monetary matters. It is important to note that *mocher be-ayin yafah mocher* is not a term of praise for any party: the ruling means, for instance, that if ראובן retains the well in the field he sold שמעון, ראובן has no access to it. And when brothers divide a parental estate, שמעון does not get to put his ladder in ראובן's yard. *Be-ayin yafah mocher* yields real and sometimes harsh consequences for the seller — it simply means that what was conveyed, was conveyed fully.

## Two Houses Case

### Physical Setup

The *mishnah* introduces a final scenario: *shnei batim ze lifnei mi-ze* — two houses, one inside the other, where the inner house can only reach the *reshut ha-rabim* by passing through the outer house. The question is what access rights, if any, attach to each house when the owner disposes of them.

### Both Sold

If the owner sells both houses to different buyers — one to each — there are no implied access rights between them: *ein lahem derech ze al ze*. The buyer of the inner house cannot automatically traverse the outer house to reach the street. As discussed throughout this *sugya*, when the seller has divested himself of both properties, the framework of generosity versus stinginess cannot operate: one cannot simultaneously be fully generous to two buyers whose interests conflict. The buyer of the inner house receives only the inner house and must negotiate access separately.

### Both Gifts

If both houses are given as gifts to different recipients, the ruling is the same: *ein lahem derech ze al ze*. No access rights are implied, notwithstanding the greater generosity typically associated with a gift. When generosity must be allocated between two parties with competing interests, it cannot resolve the tension.

### Gift Gets Access

The case changes when the transactions are of different types. If the outer house is sold and the inner house is given as a gift simultaneously, the recipient of the gift — the one with the inner house — acquires access rights through the outer house, even at the expense of the buyer of the outer house. This is the ruling the *Gemara* endorses after initially entertaining the opposite conclusion.

### Gift Generosity

The reason: *man de-yahiv matana be-ayin yafah yahiv* — one who gives a gift gives with a generous eye. A later *mishnah* establishes that when one gives a well as a gift rather than selling it, the access rights come automatically with the gift, even though they would not come automatically with a sale. The principle that a gift carries more generous implications than a sale is thus already grounded in the tradition. Applying it here: when the giver disposed of these two houses simultaneously — selling one and gifting the other — his act of gifting is treated as the more generous disposition, and the generosity of the gift carries with it the access rights needed to make the inner house usable, even if that comes at some cost to the buyer of the outer house.

One participant noted that *tachat avotecha yihyu vanecha* — the verse invoked earlier in the context of a parental estate — is one of the rare *psukim* that appears as a grounding for one of these rulings, given that most of *Bava Batra* proceeds on pure *sevara*. The *Gemara* ultimately treats that verse as a *hava amina* rather than the operative derivation, but it remains a noteworthy moment in which a *pasuk* enters the largely reason-driven discourse of this *masechta*.
