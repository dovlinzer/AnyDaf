# Yoma 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Inuy & Yom Kippur

### Three Sources

We begin at the bottom of 74b, turning from the discussion of *chatzi shiur* to the proper concerns of *Yom Kippur*. The Gemara presents three distinct derivations for why *inuy et nafshoteichem* — the Torah's command to "afflict your souls" — means specifically fasting, given that the expression is quite generic in the *pesukim*.

### Three Approaches

The three derivations proceed as follows: the first learns *inuy* from *melacha*, understanding it as *nefesh* of refraining — a form of disengagement; the second anchors it by comparison to other things the Torah prohibits in different contexts, such as forbidden foods; and the third derives it from *ibud nefesh*, the notion of destroying the self. These three sources correspond to meaningfully different models of what fasting is supposed to accomplish.

### Rambam & Disengagement

The first approach corresponds to the *Rambam*'s emphasis on *shvita* — *shvita* from *melacha*, *shvita* from *achila v'shtiya* — a disengagement from the physical world. On this reading, fasting is not really about hurting or afflicting oneself; it is about withdrawal. The second approach treats fasting as analogous to *ma'achalot asurot* in the Torah: there are times one may eat *chazir*, times one may eat *chametz*, and there are times when all food is forbidden. This doesn't explain the inner logic of the prohibition but anchors it structurally within the body of Torah law. The third approach, by contrast, seems most closely related to the model of self-flagellation — the idea that pain and suffering, *yissurim*, constitute a necessary element of the *teshuva* process.

### Manna Comparison

The Gemara now introduces a *gezeira shava* via the school of *Derei Rabbi Yishmael*: *ne'emar kan inuy* — it says "afflict" here on *Yom Kippur* — *ve'ne'emar la'halan inuy* — and it says "afflict" in another context, namely by the *mahn*. The verse there reads *va'ya'anchecha va'yarivecha*, that God afflicted you and made you go hungry, and then fed you the *mahn*. The parallel is clear: *mah la'halan inuy ra'avon* — there it plainly means going hungry — *af kan inuy ra'avon* — here too it refers to hunger.

### Divine Affliction

The Gemara raises an objection: why not derive from the verse *im te'aneh et benotai*, where Lavan says to Yaakov, "if you afflict my daughters"? This *inuy* could plausibly refer to sexual deprivation or abstention — which would suggest that the primary *inuy* of *Yom Kippur* is sexual abstention rather than fasting. The Gemara responds: *dani inuy d'rabim me'inuy d'rabim, v'ein dani inuy d'rabim me'inuy d'yachid* — we derive a communal *inuy* from a communal *inuy*, and we do not derive a communal *inuy* from an individual one. In the case of Lavan and Yaakov, the concern was about individuals. The *mahn* and *Yom Kippur*, by contrast, both speak about the entire community.

## Communal vs. Private

### Community Focus

The Gemara presses further: there is also an *inuy* recorded in connection with Egypt. As the *Haggadah* expounds the verse *va'yar et onyenu* — "God saw our affliction" — *zu prishut derech eretz*, that the Egyptians forced the Jewish people to abstain from marital relations. This too was a communal *inuy*, applying to the entire people. Why not derive from there?

The Gemara's final answer is: *ela danin inuy bidei shamayim me'inuy bidei shamayim, v'ein danin inuy bidei shamayim me'inuy bidei adam* — we derive an *inuy* decreed from Heaven from another *inuy* decreed from Heaven, and we do not derive a Heaven-decreed *inuy* from a human-imposed one. The affliction in Egypt was imposed by human masters; the *mahn* and *Yom Kippur* are both divinely ordained. That is the more technically grounded criterion for the *gezeira shava*.

### Private vs. Public

Yet the earlier distinction — communal versus individual — is also worth pausing over. Is this purely a technical point about which verse grammatically qualifies for comparison? Or is there something substantively true about the communal nature of fasting that the Gemara is drawing out? One might observe that eating is naturally a public, shared activity — families and communities eat together — whereas sexual activity is inherently private. When an entire community fasts together, there is something meaningfully communal about that experience; everyone is in *shul* together, not eating. But if every household were refraining from marital relations, one would not describe this as a communal abstention from sex in the same organic sense. The experience of the day as a *communal* fast, as opposed to a collection of individual fasts happening simultaneously, may be precisely what is being communicated here. *Rashi* reinforces this by noting that the community in the *mahn* narrative is *Klal Yisrael* as a whole — all of Israel ate the *mahn*, all of Israel went hungry without it on a given day — just as all of Israel does not eat on *Yom Kippur*.

## Eating & Religion

### Manna Discussion

With the *gezeira shava* established, the *mahn* becomes the operative point of comparison. And having mentioned the *mahn*, the Gemara now pivots entirely — spending the better part of a *daf* on an extended discussion of the *mahn*, the *slav*, and related topics. This is, in one sense, a digression: somewhere in *Shas* the Gemara wants to discuss the *mahn*, and this passage furnishes the occasion. But the juxtaposition is striking and deserves attention. We have just been discussing fasting as a religious experience. We are now about to encounter what amounts to a sustained disquisition on eating — on the conditions, textures, visuals, and dependencies that constitute the experience of food. The interplay of these two discussions is not incidental. How do we think religiously about food? How do we think religiously about fasting? Both questions are live as we enter this material.

### No Storage

The Gemara cites the verse *hamachilcha mahn bamidbar lema'an anosecha* — God fed you *mahn* in the desert in order to afflict you. What was the affliction of receiving the *mahn*? *Rav Ami* and *Rav Assi* disagree.

One says: *eino domeh mi sheyesh lo pas besalo lemi she'ein lo pas besalo* — it is not the same for one who has bread in his basket as for one who does not. Even if you receive food every single day, there is a qualitative difference between having provisions stored and facing an empty cupboard each morning. The sense of dependence, the absence of security — that is the *inuy*. And of course it instills something spiritually significant: daily recognition of one's dependence upon God.

### Visual Deprivation

The other opinion says: *eino domeh mi shero'eh ve'ocheil lemi she'eino ro'eh ve'ocheil* — it is not the same for one who sees what he eats as for one who does not. When you ate the *mahn*, it all looked the same. You did not have the aesthetic, visual experience of eating. We know that the way food appears affects how good it tastes and how satisfied you feel from the meal. Presentation matters — you arrange it nicely on the plate, you color it, you create a fully sensory experience. Approximately half of eating, it has been said, is visual. The *Gemara*'s emphasis on why one lights *Shabbos* candles includes precisely the idea of what it means to eat in light versus to eat in the dark.

So the *inuy* of the *mahn* was this: you received the taste, you were filled, but you were deprived of the full sensory experience of eating. The *mahn* may have tasted like anything — as we shall see — but it looked like the same white substance every morning. Whatever taste it produced inwardly, the visual experience matched none of it. If it tasted like an apple, you wanted to see an apple. If it tasted like a hamburger, you wanted to see the meat. The connection between olfactory and visual experience reinforces this point: the two senses are deeply intertwined in the eating experience, and the absence of visual stimulus undermined even the full realization of taste.

### Blind Eating

*Rav Yosef* — who was himself blind — says: *mikan remez l'sumin she'ochlin ve'einam svei'im* — this is an allusion to blind people, who eat and are never fully satisfied, precisely because they lack the visual component of the meal. This ratchets up the earlier point: it is not merely that you lose the pleasure of seeing your food. Without the visual experience, you are not even fully satiated. *Bnei Yisrael* in the desert, eating the *mahn*, may thus have experienced something of what blind people experience at every meal.

### Day Eating

*Abaye* draws a practical lesson: *man d'is lei se'udasa, lo leichlei ela biyemama* — if you have a meal to eat, eat it during the day, so that you can fully experience and enjoy it. The comparison to hospital patients on intravenous drips or to eating arrangements that supply all necessary calories while bypassing the experiential act of eating is apt: you can receive complete nutrition and still feel hungry, because the experience of eating has been bypassed.

### Vision > Appetite

*Rav Zeira* asks: what is the verse that supports this principle? *Tov mareh einayim mehaloch nafesh* — better is the vision of the eyes than the going — or the satisfaction — of the *nafesh*. The word *nafesh* here carries the meaning it sometimes carries in Semitic cognates: appetite, or throat. This also connects back to the phrase *te'anu et nafshoteichem* — some argue that *nafesh* in that context has a particularly grounded meaning tied to fasting, given its association with appetite and hunger. The verse in *Kohelet* thus supports the idea that the visual experience of eating is not merely supplementary; it is in some ways prior to the appetite's satisfaction.

*Reish Lakish* extends this: *tov mareh einayim b'isha yoter migufo shel ma'aseh* — the visual encounter with a woman is, from the male perspective, in some ways more powerful than the act itself. This connects to the earlier Talmudic observation that *hirhurei aveira kasha m'aveira* — the mental experience can be more intense than the deed. The verse — *tov mareh einayim mehaloch nafesh* — thus applies across domains: the visual, anticipatory dimension of an experience frequently exceeds in intensity the satisfying of the appetite that follows.

## Wine & Indulgence

### Wine & Sex

The Gemara moves to another verse — *al tireh yayin ki yisa'dam, ki yitein bekoso eino, yishalech bemeisharim* — do not be drawn after wine because of its redness; you will put your eye upon the cup, and you will go straight away after it. The phrase *yishalech bemeisharim* is now interpreted.

*Rav Ami* and *Rav Assi* debate. One says: *kol hanosein eino bekoso, arayos kulan domos alav k'mishor* — whoever fixes his eye upon his cup, focusing too intensely on his drinking, all the *arayos* will appear to him as flat, as permissible. He will lose his sense of restriction; he will tell himself there are no limits. While there is nothing in the verse itself about *arayos*, the association between drinking and sexual license is the interpretive move being made here.

### Theft Justification

The other opinion says: *kol hanosein eino bekoso, kol ha'olam kula doma alav k'mishor* — not only do the *arayos* appear permissible, but the entire world does. The *Maharsha* distinguishes these two readings precisely: the first is that intoxication gives you license to act on your desires; the second is that over-indulgence in drink creates a need to support the habit, and so one justifies taking other people's money — the world's property becomes flat, ownerless, accessible. These are meaningfully different pathological outcomes of excess.

### Anguish Response

The Gemara then turns to the verse *de'agah belev ish yashchena* — anguish in the heart of a person will subdue it. *Rav Ami* and *Rav Assi* again disagree. One says *yashchena mida'ato* — you should remove the anguish from your mind, distract yourself, engage in something else. The other says *yashchena l'acherim* — you should speak of it to others. If you have anguish, find someone to talk to. Do not try to suppress it through distraction; give voice to it.

### Serpent Curse

The series of disputes between *Rav Ami* and *Rav Assi* continues with a verse from *Yeshayahu*: *v'nachash afar lachmo* — and the serpent's food is dust. One says: *afilu ochel kol ma'adanei olam to'eim bahem ta'am afar* — even if the serpent eats the greatest delicacies in the world, they all taste like dust to him. This is, in a sense, the inverse of the *mahn*: with the *mahn*, one plain-looking substance could produce any taste; for the serpent, any delicacy reduces to the same undifferentiated blandness. The curse is total sensory nullification.

The other opinion says: *afilu ochel kol ma'adanei olam, ein da'ato meyushevet alav ad she'yochal afar* — even if the serpent eats every delicacy, he is never truly satisfied until he eats dust. Only dust provides the serpent with genuine satiation. These are two distinct readings of the same curse: one is about the experience of food being reduced to tastelessness; the other is about an inversion of desire in which only the base and degraded can ever satisfy.

## Mercy in Curses

### God's Mercy

A *baraita* is introduced. *Rabbi Yosei* says: *bo u're'ei shelo kemiddat Hakadosh Baruch Hu middat basar vadam* — come and see how God's ways differ from the ways of flesh and blood. When a human being holds a grievance, he goes straight to his adversary's livelihood — he tries to undermine him, to cause him suffering in the thing that matters most. God does not operate this way. Even when God curses, there is *rachmanus* embedded within the curse.

### Blessings Hidden

Four examples are enumerated. God cursed the serpent — and yet, wherever the serpent goes, its food is there: *oleh lagag mezonosav imo, yored lemata mezonosav imo* — it goes up to a roof, and its food is there; it descends to the ground, and its food is there. The curse is real, but it is livable.

God cursed *Kena'an* — and yet: *ochel mah sherabo ochel, v'shoseh mah sherabo shoseh* — he eats what his master eats and drinks what his master drinks. He is a slave, but he benefits from his master's table. He is not utterly destroyed.

God cursed the woman — the travails of childbirth — and yet *v'el isheich teshukateich*, she is beloved, she is desired: *hakol ratzin achareha* — people pursue her. The curse does not eliminate her desirability or her place.

God cursed the ground — *vekotz vedardar tatzmia'ch lach* — yet: *hakol nizonin heimena* — all creatures are sustained by it. Even the cursed earth remains the source of universal sustenance. In each case, God curses but simultaneously ensures that the curse does not extinguish the life of its recipient.

## Egypt Complaints

### Fish vs. Sex

The Gemara returns to the *mahn* narrative and the complaint of *Bnei Yisrael*: *zacharnu et hadagah asher nochal b'Mitzrayim chinam, et hakishuim ve'et ha'avatichim* — we remember the fish (*dagah*) that we ate in Egypt for free, the cucumbers and the melons. *Rav* and *Shmuel* dispute what *dagah* means. One says: fish, literally. The other says: it is a euphemism for sexual relations — as in *veyidgu larov*, the language of procreation — meaning the people were lamenting a loss of sexual freedom.

The Gemara works through the textual difficulty in each direction. If *dagah* means fish, the word *nochal* — "that we ate" — presents no problem, but what does *chinam*, "for free," mean? If it means sex, the word *nochal* can be defended by analogy: *achlah u'macha piha* (Proverbs 30:20) is a well-known verse using eating as a euphemism for sexual activity, so *nochal* is *lishon ma'alya*, a euphemistic expression. But if it means sex, what then does *chinam* mean? *Rashi* understands it as freedom — they were free of restrictions, they had latitude they no longer have.

### Free Provision

If the simpler reading — literal fish — is adopted, then *chinam* is explained by a tradition: *k'she'hayu Yisrael sho'avim mayim, Hakadosh Baruch Hu mazmin lahem betoch hamayim dagim ketanim bechadeihem* — when the Jewish people would draw water in Egypt for the bricks, small fish would rise up into their buckets. The sustenance was miraculously provided at no cost, from *hefker*, from unowned abundance. So *chinam* means quite literally free of charge.

### Noahide Laws

The Gemara asks: *bishlama le'man d'amar dagim* — if *dagah* means literal fish, good. But if it means sexual relations, does this not imply that the people were sexually unrestrained in Egypt? The verse *gan na'ul achoti kallah* — "a locked garden is my sister, my bride" — is adduced from *Shir HaShirim*, read in the context of the redemption from Egypt, as testimony to *Klal Yisrael*'s sexual propriety. The Gemara resolves this: *mehanach d'asiri lehu lo pritzei behu* — those that were forbidden to them as *Bnei Noach*, they did not transgress. They were not sexually unrestrained before receiving the Torah; they observed the *sheva mitzvot Bnei Noach*.

### New Restrictions

What, then, were they bemoaning? The new restrictions that came with receiving the Torah. As *Bnei Noach*, certain relatives who later become *arayot* under Torah law were permissible. Now they were forbidden. And the laws of *niddah* — which impose separation between husband and wife — were newly binding. The famous teaching that *Moshe* heard *ha'am bocheh lemishpechotav* — the people weeping *al issei mishpechotav*, about matters relating to their families — is invoked: *shenasru lahem lishkav etzlam*, that they could no longer sleep alongside certain relatives, or sleep alongside their wives during *niddah*. They had not acted improperly before. They had simply now been given additional constraints that felt like a loss.

### Multiple Complaints

The Gemara concludes: *ha da v'ha da* — it was both. They were lamenting both the fish and the sexual restrictions. Even those who hold that the primary subject of *zacharnu et hadagah* was not the sexual dimension acknowledge that the people were crying about *mishpechotav* — their family restrictions. And those who understand *dagah* as referring to sexual relations still must account for the fact that the people were also crying about their diet. The complaints were layered: a more restrictive lifestyle across multiple domains, in food, in family life, in freedom.

## Five Missing Flavors

### Taste Only

The Gemara returns to the list in the verse: *et hakishuim ve'et ha'avatichim ve'et hachatzir ve'et habetzalim ve'et hashumim* — the cucumbers, melons, leeks, onions, and garlic. Why specifically these five vegetables? And how do they relate to the *mahn*, which was said to contain all flavors?

*Rav Ami* and *Rav Assi* again disagree. One says: *ta'am kol haminin ta'amu baman* — you could taste any flavor in the *mahn*. These five, however — *ta'am chamisha minin halalu lo ta'amu bo* — the taste of these five specifically could not be experienced in the *mahn*. The absence of their taste is precisely what the people were lamenting.

### Texture Missing

The other opinion says: *ta'am kol haminin ta'amu* — in fact, you could taste anything, including these five. But *ta'aman u'mamashan* — everything else you received both the taste and the *mamash*, the physical substance of the thing — the texture. For these five alone, *ta'aman velo mamashan* — you had the taste but not the textural experience.

### Texture Role

This connects back to the earlier discussion about the dimensions of the eating experience that go beyond mere flavor. Taste alone, it turns out, is insufficient for full satisfaction. When you bite into a pretzel, you want the crunch. You want the resistance, the body, the physical encounter with the food. For all other foods, the *mahn* provided this. For these five, it did not. It is worth noting the slight humor in the list: onions and garlic are not foods for which most people would say texture is the primary value. Yet even here, something was missing. The complaint was not irrational; it was about the totality of sensory experience, and even the loss of texture in an onion could constitute genuine deprivation.

## Manna Description

### Round & White

The Gemara now addresses the physical description of the *mahn*: *vehaman kezera gad hu, lavan* — the *mahn* was like a *zera gad*, a coriander seed, and white. But coriander seeds are not white. *Rav Assi* resolves this: *agol kegida* — it was round like a coriander seed — *velavan kemargalit* — and white like a pearl. The verse is encoding two separate descriptions in the comparison.

### Flax & Story

A *baraita* adds: *gad*, the word for coriander, can be read as suggesting resemblance to *zera pishtan begivolin*, flax seed in its husks, which is very round, very spherical — a different but compatible comparison to the roundness of the *mahn*. *Acherim omrim* — others say — *gad shedomeh lahaggadah*, the *mahn* was like a *haggadah*, like a story: *shemoshechét libo shel adam*, that draws the heart of a person. Just as a story captures everyone's attention — when someone says "I have a story to tell you," everyone wakes up — so too the *mahn* had a magnetic pull, drawing people toward it. Precisely how this manifests is not fully spelled out, but perhaps the smell, appearance, or some ineffable quality made it irresistible. *Shemoshechét libo shel adam kemayim* — it drew people after it like flowing water.

### Secret Telling

Another *baraita*: *gad shemagid lahem leYisrael i ben tisha larishon ve'i ben sheva le'acharon* — the *mahn* would tell the Jewish people whether a child was born after nine months (from the first husband) or seven months (from a second). Because the *mahn* accumulated in each household proportional to the number of members — *omer lagulgoleth* — when collected and measured at home, it would reflect the exact head count of that household. In contested cases of paternity, or in cases where a woman had remarried quickly after divorce, the *mahn* would resolve the question: if the extra *omer* appeared in the first man's house, the child belonged to him; if it appeared in the second man's house, the child belonged there.

### Sin Whitening

The white color — *lavan* — the Gemara says *shemalbin avonoseihem shel Yisrael*, that it whitened, purified, the sins of Israel. *Rashi* connects this most simply to the verse's own framing: *shema lo yereid mahn lemachor* — the daily anxiety that perhaps the *mahn* would not descend the next morning — *hayu meshab'dim libam laMakom* — caused the people to subjugate their hearts to God. Every morning's dependency was a renewal of recognition. And as we are about to see, the *mahn* also forced hidden sins into the open — because when a household's *omer* count did not match its expected membership, the reason would become clear. Transgressions that could otherwise remain concealed were revealed by the mechanism of the *mahn* itself.

## Character Revealed

### Prophet Function

A *baraita* states: *Rabbi Yosei* says — *keshem shehanavi haya magid lahem leYisrael mah shebachorin umah shebasidakim* — just as a prophet would reveal to the Jewish people what was hidden in their nooks and crannies, the skeletons they were trying to hide — *kach haman magid lahem leYisrael mah shebachorin umah shebasidakim* — so too the *mahn* would reveal precisely those things. The *mahn* functioned as a kind of moral prophet, making visible what people most wished to conceal.

### Slave Case

Two cases illustrate this. *Shnayim sheba'u lifnei Moshe ledin* — two people came before *Moshe* for judgment. One says: you stole my slave. The other says: you sold him to me. *Moshe* says: *laboker mishpat* — wait until the morning. If the next day the extra *omer* was found in the first master's house, the slave belonged to him and was stolen; if it was found in the second man's house, the sale was legitimate and the second man was the genuine owner.

### Divorce Case

The second case: a husband and wife come before *Moshe* for judgment. The husband says she committed an offense against him — presumably adultery or some grounds for divorce without *ketubah*. The wife says he committed an offense against her — grounds entitling her to a *ketubah*. Again, *Moshe* says: wait until morning. The *omer*'s location would determine the outcome.

The *girsa* of our text is: if the *omer* was found in the husband's house, she had sinned against him; if found in her father's house, he had sinned against her. This is somewhat difficult: if she sinned against him, why is her *omer* still in his house, as they are presumably headed for divorce regardless? An alternative *girsa* reverses it: if it is his fault, the *omer* remains in his house — because since the divorce is under his control, if he is at fault he will not choose to divorce her, and she remains his. If it is her fault, her *omer* appears at her father's house, because she is being sent away. This alternative reading has a tighter logical coherence.

### Distance Proportional

The Gemara then reconciles three apparently contradictory verses about where the *mahn* was gathered. One verse implies it was in the camp; another says the people went out to gather it; a third says they scattered far. *Ha keitzad?* — how are all three true? *Tzaddikim*: the *mahn* fell at their doorstep — home delivery. *Beinonim*: they had to go out of the camp to gather it. *Reshaim*: they had to scatter far. Every morning, the distance a person had to travel to find his *mahn* was itself a public announcement of his spiritual standing.

### Forms by Merit

A similar structure applies to the form in which the *mahn* arrived. The verse calls it *lechem* in one place, *ugah* (cakes) in another, and yet another verse implies the people had to grind it. *Ha keitzad?* For the *tzaddikim*, it came as finished bread, ready to eat. For the *beinonim*, it came as dough or unbaked cakes — requiring some preparation. For the *reshaim*, it came as wheat grain: *tahanu barechayim, o dachu bamdocha* — they had to grind it in a mill or pound it in a mortar. The eating experience itself — how much effort one had to invest before eating — was calibrated to one's moral standing. The broader point is clear: eating, in the context of the *mahn*, was inextricably a religious experience. Distance, form, and effort all encoded spiritual information.

## Divine Abundance

### Spices & Perfume

The Gemara asks: if the *mahn* came already as bread for the *tzaddikim*, why do the verses speak of grinding and cooking? *Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Rav*, and some attribute it to *Rabbi Chama bar Chanina*: *melamed sheyarad lahem leYisrael im haman tachshitei nashim davar shenidoch bamdocha* — along with the *mahn* came items used as women's jewelry and cosmetics, aromatic things that required grinding. It was not the *mahn* itself being ground; it was these additional gifts — spices and perfumes — that came down with it.

### Pot Seasonings

Similarly, *Rabbi Chama* teaches: *melamed sheyarad lahem leYisrael im haman tziki kdeira* — with the *mahn* also came pot-seasonings, flavorings for cooking. *Rashi* explains *tziki kdeira* as *tavlin letavshil arev umevusam* — flavorings that make a cooked dish fragrant and delicious. Even if the *mahn* itself had all these tastes, apparently there was also a desire to cook with enhancing flavors. The larger sensory experience of cooking was accommodated. It is notable, given that we earlier said the *mahn* had all these tastes except for five things, that one would still want to cook with seasonings — perhaps because the experiential dimension of cooking, not merely the taste of the result, matters to people.

### Cloud Jewels

The verse records that *hanesi'im hevi'u et avnei hashoham* — the *nesi'im* brought the *shoham* stones. A *baraita* teaches: *nesi'im mamash* — but *nasi* can mean a cloud. The clouds brought the precious stones. Because the *mahn* fell from the clouds, and with the *mahn* came jewels, pearls, and exotic wealth. The verse *nesi'im v'ruach v'geshem ayin* — clouds and wind and no rain — is cited to establish that *nesi'im* indeed denotes clouds. The picture being painted is one of extraordinary abundance: not merely daily food, but perfume, spices, pot-flavorings, and precious gems, all descending daily from Heaven.

## Multiple Tastes

### Breast Milk

The verse says the *mahn* tasted *k'ta'am l'shad hashamen* — like a smear of oil, or *leshad*, which can be read as *shad*, a breast. *Rabbi Abbahu* expounds: *mah shad zeh, tinok to'eim bah kamah ta'amim* — just as a nursing infant tastes many flavors from the breast — influenced, *Rashi* notes, by what the mother eats — *af haman kol zman sheYisrael ochlin oto, motzi'in bo kamah ta'amim* — so too whenever the Jewish people ate the *mahn*, they would draw from it many different flavors. The *mahn* was not static; it was responsive, dynamic, producing different tastes for different people or at different moments.

### Color Change

An alternative reading: *leshed mamash* — the comparison is quite literally to the breast: *mah shed zeh mithapeich l'kamah g'vanei* — just as a breast has varied coloring — the skin, the nipple — *af haman mithapeich l'kamah ta'amim* — so too the *mahn* would shift and change into many tastes. Some identify yet another reading in which the comparison is to a *shed*, a demon, which can shift into many forms and colors. In any case, the underlying point is consistent: the *mahn* was capable of producing a spectrum of flavors, not a single uniform taste.

### Meat & Bread

*Moshe* announces to the people: *b'teit Hashem lachem ba'erev basar le'echol, velachem baboker lisbo'a* — God will give you meat in the evening to eat, and bread in the morning to satisfy. This verse sets the framework for the discussion that follows.

### Improper Meat

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha* teaches: *basar she'sha'alu shelo k'hogen* — the meat they asked for improperly, since meat is a luxury, not a necessity, and they complained and demanded it in a way that was not appropriate. Accordingly, *nitan lahem shelo k'hogen* — it was given to them improperly — towards evening, when there was little time to prepare it, especially without electric light. *Lechem she'sha'alu k'hogen* — bread, which they rightly needed — *nitan lahem k'hogen* — was given to them properly, first thing in the morning.

### Meal Timing

From this, *mikan limdah Torah derech eretz* — the Torah teaches a standard of proper behavior: *shelo yochal adam basar ela balailah* — one should eat meat specifically at night. This is somewhat counterintuitive, since it means eating the heavier food right before sleep. But perhaps the point is that meat ought to remain a delicacy, something consumed sparingly, not a staple of the daytime meal. One might object that this contradicts the earlier *Gemara* which says to eat your meal in the day to enjoy it fully. The *Gemara* resolves this: *ke'ein yimama ka'amina* — what was meant is that you should eat in a well-lit setting; it need not be literally during daylight, provided there is adequate light.

## Derech Eretz

### Chicken Behavior

*Abba bar Yaakov* provides a vivid baseline: *betichila hayu Yisrael domim ketarnagolim shemenakrim ba'ashpah* — originally, the Jewish people were like chickens pecking in a garbage dump, eating at random, constantly grazing with no structure or regularity.

### Regular Times

*Ad sheba Moshe vekava lahem zman se'udah* — until *Moshe* came and established fixed meal times: bread in the morning, meat in the evening. No constant snacking, no nibbling throughout the day. This is the *derech eretz* of eating: a person eats at defined times, not perpetually. The image of the refrigerator in every home, the constant availability of food, the culture of snacking — all of this is, from the *Gemara*'s perspective, sub-optimal. A *mentsch* eats at a morning meal and an evening meal, not continuously. The contrast with the chicken-like pecking is the contrast between structure and appetite run without governance.

## Quail Death

### Teeth Question

The verse states *habasar odenu bein shineihem* — the meat was still between their teeth. Yet another verse says they ate for a full month: *ad chodesh yamim*. Did they eat for a month, or did they die immediately while still eating? *Ha keitzad?* — how can both be true?

*Beinonim le'altar metu* — the *beinonim*, the average sinners, died immediately, even as the meat was still in their teeth. Their death was quick. *Reshaim mitsta'arin veholechin ad chodesh yamim* — the *reshaim* suffered with a stomach ache for the entire month and died at the end of it. The *beinonim* had enough *zechut* that they received a swift death; the *reshaim* suffered the full month before dying.

### Deserving Death

*Reish Lakish* offers a dramatic *al tikrei*: *al tikrei vayishtahu ela vayishchatu* — do not read "they spread out" but "they were slaughtered." *Melamed shenitkayevu soneihem shel Yisrael shechitah* — this teaches that they were deserving of actual *shechita*. The expression *soneihem shel Yisrael* is, as always, a euphemism for *Yisrael* itself — we do not attribute such things directly. The use of their lust for meat as a vehicle of their own punishment carries a grim irony.

### Shechita Source

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha* takes the same textual variation and derives a different point: *al tikrei shatuach ela shachut* — do not read "spread out" but "slaughtered": *melamed sheyarad lahem leYisrael im haman davar hata'un shechitah* — the *slav* was something requiring *shechitah*. This is presented as a basis for the requirement to perform *shechitah* on fowl.

*Rabbi* objects: is this really the source for *shechitah* on fowl? There is another verse: *vayamter aleihem ke'afar she'er uchechol yamim of kanaf* — God rained down flesh like dust and birds like the sand of the sea. And the obligation to perform *shechitah* on fowl is derived elsewhere: *detanya, Rabbi omer, vezavachta ka'asher tziviticha* — you shall slaughter as I commanded you — *melamed shenitztavah Moshe al havashet ve'al hakaneh, al rov echad be'of, ve'al rov shenayim bivehemah* — Moshe was commanded on the trachea and esophagus, that for a bird the majority of one suffices, and for a land animal the majority of both is required. That is where the laws of *shechitah* for fowl are derived.

What then is the point of the word *shatuach*? *Melamed sheyarad lahem mashtichain mashtichain* — it teaches that the *slav* fell in layers upon layers. That is the simple *pshat* of the verse: the birds descended in spreading, layered flocks across the ground.

## Age-Based Tastes

### Taste by Age

The Gemara returns to the competing descriptions of the *mahn*'s taste: one verse calls it *lechem*, another *shemen*, another *dvash*. *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina* resolves this: *linearim lechem* — for the young people it tasted like bread, which is what young people want; *lazkeinim shemen* — for the elderly it tasted like oil, which is more appropriate to their palate; *latinokot dvash* — for the children it tasted like honey, like candy.

### Tranquility vs. Thorns

The word *slav* is written with a *shin* but read with a *samech* — the two letters being interchangeable in sound. *Rabbi Chanina* plays on the dual reading: read with a *shin*, *slav* suggests *shalvah*, tranquility. *Tzaddikim ochlin oso bishalvah* — the *tzaddikim* ate it in peace and ease. *Reshaim ochlin oso vedomeh lahem kesilon* — for the *reshaim*, eating it was like thorns — *silon*, read with a *samech*. The same food, in the same moment, produced opposite experiences depending on the character of the one consuming it.

### Quail Types

*Rabbi Chanan bar Rava* teaches that there are four varieties of quail: *shichli, kivli, pasyoni*, and *slav*. *Ma'alya dechuhu shichli* — the finest is *shichli*. *Garya dechuhu slav* — the lowest quality is *slav*. And yet even *slav*, the worst of the four, when roasted in an oven — *ve'hava ke'tziporta, umosvinan lah betanura* — would expand from its fat until it filled the oven, and could be spread across thirteen slices of bread, with even the bottom slice so saturated with fat that it could not be eaten plain but only mixed with something else. The abundance and richness of the *slav* was extraordinary, even at its lowest grade.

## Finding Quail

### Yehuda's Barrels

The *Gemara* now transitions to a series of anecdotes about *Amoraim* who miraculously found quail in their everyday surroundings. *Rebbi Yehuda* would find quail among his barrels — they would appear there, available for him to eat, a small daily miracle provided to a Torah scholar.

### Chisda's Chips

*Rav Chisda* would find his quail among the chips of wood. Again, the bird would appear in an unlikely location, a quiet ongoing gift.

### Sharecropper Gift

*Rava* — who was *Rav Chisda*'s son-in-law and student — had a sharecropper who worked his field. Every day, *Rava meiti leh arisei kol yoma*, his sharecropper would bring him *slav* that had fallen into the field. The connection between *Rava*'s merit and the ongoing provision of quail is implicit: it was by virtue of his Torah learning and his relationship to his teacher that this gift continued.

### Death & Blessing

*Yoma chad lo aiti* — one day the sharecropper did not bring any quail. *Rava* wondered what had happened. He went up to the roof and heard a child reciting the verse: *shamati va'tirgaz bitni* — I have heard, and my stomach trembled. *Shma mina nach nafsheih de'Rav Chisda* — from this *Rava* understood that *Rav Chisda*, his father-in-law and *Rebbi*, had died. *Uvidil Rava achal talmida* — the student eats by virtue of his teacher. With the teacher gone, the ongoing flow of blessing that sustained the student was interrupted, and no quail fell that day.

## Eating & Religion

### Fasting & Eating

It is striking to step back and observe the full arc of this *daf*. We began with a discussion of *Yom Kippur* fasting — with the question of what *inuy et nafshoteichem* means and how we derive that it refers specifically to abstaining from food. That discussion led, via a *gezeira shava*, to the *mahn*. And the *mahn* opened into this extended exploration of eating: its sensory dimensions, its social structures, its moral valences, its miraculous forms.

### Complaint Despite

One thread running through the discussion is the extraordinary ease with which people complain even in the midst of abundance. *Bnei Yisrael* received *mahn* daily, received spices and perfumes and jewels from the clouds, received *slav* so delicious that even its lowest grade would expand to fill an oven — and still they wept. They wept about the five vegetables whose texture they missed. They wept about the fish they remembered from Egypt. They wept about the family restrictions that had been placed upon them. The warning implicit in this is not subtle: the capacity for complaint is nearly limitless, and abundance does not automatically generate gratitude.

### Eating Significance

At the same time, something more constructive is being communicated. The *mahn* created a context in which eating was unavoidably a religious act. Dependency on God was built into the structure of daily nourishment: you did not have bread stored for tomorrow; you did not know whether the *mahn* would fall. The distance you walked to gather it announced your spiritual standing. The form in which it arrived reflected your moral condition. The mechanism of the *omer* exposed hidden sins and concealed truths. Eating under these conditions was not merely caloric intake; it was encounter with the divine order.

How that translates into the present — when the refrigerator is full, when the store is always open, when food is neither scarce nor miraculously provided — is a question the *Gemara* does not answer directly. But the juxtaposition with which the *daf* began — fasting as the *inuy* of *Yom Kippur*, eating as the *inuy* of the desert — suggests that the relationship between food and religious experience runs in both directions. The fast and the miraculous meal are, in their different ways, both structured encounters with dependence, with limit, and with the recognition that sustenance, in the end, comes from somewhere beyond ourselves.
