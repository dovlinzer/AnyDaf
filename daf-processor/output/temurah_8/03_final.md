# Temurah 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Ownership of Bechor

### Ba'al HaKorban Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: בְּכוֹר מוֹכְרִין אוֹתוֹ תָּם חַי, וּבַעַל מוּם חַי וְשָׁחוּט, וּמְקַדְּשִׁין בּוֹ הָאִשָּׁה. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה, הוֹאִיל וְאִית לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן זְכִיָּיה בְּגַוֵּויהּ (הוֹאִיל וְיֵשׁ לַכֹּהֵן שׁוּתָּפוּת בּוֹ), אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים, כֵּיוָן דְּתָם לְהַקְרָבָה הוּא דְּקָאֵי — אֵין מוֹכְרִין אוֹתוֹ תָּמִים חַי.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Ma'aser Sheni* 1:2): With regard to **a firstborn** offering, **one may sell it** if it is **unblemished** only while it is still **alive,** as after it is slaughtered outside the Temple one may not derive benefit from it, and if it was offered in the Temple, one may not sell sacrificial meat. **And** if it is **blemished,** one may sell it while it is **alive or** after it has been **slaughtered. And** a priest **can betroth a woman with it,** as it is his property. **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: They taught** this *halakha* **only with regard to the present,** when there is no Temple, **since the priest has** the ability to **acquire** the firstborn offering. **But when the Temple is standing, since** the **unblemished** firstborn animal **stands for sacrifice,** one may **not sell it unblemished** while it is **alive.**

The Gemara on daf 8 opens by resuming the analysis of who is considered the *ba'al hakorban* — the owner of the sacrifice — for purposes of performing *temurah*. The general rule is that the *kohen*, despite eating the meat of a *chatat* or *asham*, is not the owner of that offering; it belongs to the individual for whom it provides atonement. The *kohen* may eat the flesh, but it is the Yisrael who brought the *korban*, and only the Yisrael who can perform *temurah*. When a *kohen* brings his own *korban*, of course, he is the owner and performs *temurah* accordingly.

### Mishnah Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַכֹּהֲנִים מְמִירִין בְּשֶׁלָּהֶן, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְמִירִין בְּשֶׁלָּהֶן. אֵין הַכֹּהֲנִים מְמִירִין לֹא בְּחַטָּאת וְלֹא בְּאָשָׁם וְלֹא בִּבְכוֹר. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין מְמִירִין בִּבְכוֹר? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֵן, וּבְכוֹר מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֵן. מָה חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם אֵין מְמִירִין בּוֹ, אַף בְּכוֹר אֵין מְמִירִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The priests substitute for their** own offerings **and Israelites substitute for their** own offerings. **The priests substitute neither for a sin offering, nor for a guilt offering, nor for a firstborn** offering that they received from an Israelite, as those animals are not their property, and one does not substitute an animal that is not his. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said: For what** reason can priests **not substitute for a firstborn** offering that they received from an Israelite? Does it not belong to them? **Rabbi Akiva said to him: A sin offering and a guilt offering** are **a gift to the priest, and the firstborn** offering is likewise **a gift to the priest. Just as** in the cases of **a sin offering and a guilt offering,** priests that receive one of them from an Israelite **cannot substitute for it, so too** with regard to **a firstborn** offering, priests that receive it from an Israelite **cannot substitute for it.**

The focus of the Mishnah, and now of the Gemara's discussion, concerns the *bechor* — the firstborn animal. The Mishnah appears to reflect the position of Rabbi Akiva, over the resistance of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, that the *kohen* is not the owner of the *bechor*. When a Yisrael possesses a *bechor*, the animal must be given to a *kohen*, who then brings it as a *korban* and eats its meat. Yet the *kohen* is not considered the *ba'al hakorban*. He receives it, as the Gemara will later articulate, *mishulchan gavo'ah* — as one eating from God's table — just as he eats the meat of a Yisrael's *chatat* or *asham* without being that *korban*'s owner. According to this position, it is the Yisrael who can perform *temurah* prior to transferring the animal to the *kohen*; once it is in the *kohen*'s possession, no one performs *temurah* at all.

### Bechor's Intrinsic Kedushah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: מָה לוֹ אִם אֵינוֹ מֵמִיר בְּחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם, שֶׁאֵין זָכִין בָּהֶן בְּחַיֵּיהֶן, תֹּאמַר בִּבְכוֹר שֶׁזָּכִין בּוֹ בְּחַיָּיו! אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״וְהָיָה הוּא וּתְמוּרָתוֹ יִהְיֶה קּוֹדֶשׁ״, הֵיכָן קְדוּשָּׁה חָלָה עָלָיו? בְּבֵית הַבְּעָלִים, אַף תְּמוּרָה בְּבֵית הַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said: What** is this comparison **for him? If** a priest **does not substitute for a sin offering and a guilt offering, which** priests **do not acquire during** the animals' **lifetimes, will you say** the same **with regard to a firstborn, which** priests **do acquire during** the animal's **lifetime? Rabbi Akiva said to him: But isn't it already stated: "Then both it and its substitute shall be sacred"** (Leviticus 27:10), which juxtaposes the consecration of the consecrated animal with that of its non-sacred substitute? **Where is** the consecrated animal **imbued with sanctity?** It is **in the house of the owner. So too,** the **substitute** animal is consecrated **in the house of the owner.** Therefore, the priest cannot substitute for the firstborn that he received because he is not the owner that initially consecrated it.

This raises a striking point worth pausing on. A *bechor* is fundamentally different from every other *korban*: it is never actively sanctified by a person. Its *kedushah* arises automatically by virtue of being the firstborn. One might have imagined, therefore, that no one could perform *temurah* on a *bechor*, since *temurah* would naturally be associated with the one who instills the *kedushah*. The Mishnah itself uses the phrase *chalah alav* — the sanctity took effect — in the owner's house, establishing that the Yisrael in whose possession the *kedushah* first took hold is the relevant party for *temurah*, even though no deliberate act of sanctification was performed.

### Owner vs. Sanctifier

The deeper principle at stake here, already established by the Gemara earlier in the tractate, is that the performer of *temurah* is not the one who instilled the *kedushah* but the one for whom the *korban* operates — the *ba'al hakorban* in the functional sense. Even when one person consecrates an animal as a *korban* on behalf of another, it is the beneficiary who performs *temurah*, not the one who pronounced the consecration. Given this principle, one might have expected the *kohen* to be the *ba'al hakorban* of the *bechor*, since the animal is ultimately brought by him and serves, in a sense, for his benefit. Rabbi Akiva's position, however, maintains that the *kohen* is nonetheless not considered the owner, and Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri appears to dispute this. The Gemara now proceeds to examine this question at length.

## Bechor Sales & Beit HaMikdash

### Bechor Sale Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: מוֹכְרִין אוֹתוֹ תָּמִים חַי — חַי אִין, שָׁחוּט לָא. אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה — מִי אִיכָּא תָּם שָׁחוּט? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים, וְקָתָנֵי נָמֵי: מוֹכְרִין אוֹתוֹ תָּמִים חַי, (אַלְמָא אִית לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן זְכִיָּיה בְּגַוֵּויהּ)!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection to Rav Naḥman:** The mishna teaches that **one may sell an unblemished** firstborn offering while it is **alive.** One can infer that while it is **alive, yes,** one may sell it, but once it has been **slaughtered,** one may **not** sell it. **When** is this the *halakha*? **If we say** that this mishna is speaking **in the present, is there an unblemished, slaughtered,** firstborn offering? Offerings are not slaughtered in the present day. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the mishna is speaking of the time **when the Temple is standing. And** yet, the mishna **still teaches** that **one may sell an unblemished** firstborn offering while it is **alive,** which contradicts the statement of Rav Naḥman.

The Gemara opens this discussion by citing a Mishnah from Maseches Ma'aser Sheni: *bechor mochrin oto tam chai uva'al mum chai veshachut* — a *bechor* may be sold unblemished while alive, and if it is blemished, it may be sold alive or slaughtered. The one case excluded is an unblemished animal after it has been slaughtered in the *Beit HaMikdash*. Once the *korban* has been offered, its meat may not be treated as an ordinary commercial commodity; the flesh of *kodashim* is to be eaten by its proper recipients, not sold in the marketplace. But prior to that — while the animal is alive — the *kohen* may sell it, even to another *kohen* who will be the one to bring it to the *Beit HaMikdash* and eat its meat.

### Redemption vs. Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה, מִי קָתָנֵי: מוֹכְרִין אוֹתוֹ תָּמִים חַי — אִין, שָׁחוּט — לָא? הִא גּוּפַאּ אֲתָא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן, דְּבִזְמַן הַזֶּה נָמֵי מוֹכְרִין אוֹתוֹ תָּמִים חַי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman responds: **No; actually** the mishna is referring to the *halakha* **in the present.** I disagree with the above inference. **Does** the mishna explicitly **teach: One may sell an unblemished** firstborn offering while it is **alive, yes,** but once it has been **slaughtered, no?** The mishna does not mean to rule out selling a slaughtered animal; rather, **it is coming to teach us the** matter itself, **that even in the present, one may sell it unblemished** while it is **alive.**

The Torah states regarding the *bechor*, *lo yipadeh* — it may not be redeemed. Since its *kedushah* was never instilled through a human act, it cannot be dissolved through a human act either; there is no mechanism for transferring its sanctity to money and rendering the animal ordinary. But the prohibition is specifically against redemption, not against sale. A *bechor* can be sold. If blemished, it retains its identity as a *bechor* with all accompanying restrictions but may be sold to anyone, eaten by *kohanim* and their households when unblemished, and by anyone once it bears a permanent blemish (*mum kavua*). The Gemara notes further that the *kohen* may even use a *bechor* as *kesef kiddushin* — as a means of betrothing a woman. This is significant because the ability to effect *kiddushin* with an object is a strong indicator of full ownership. While one might argue that selling rights to an object merely transfers *tovas hana'ah* rather than actual ownership, using something to betroth a woman demonstrates that the object is genuinely and fully one's property.

### Kiddushin Test

The point about *kiddushin* is worth dwelling on. The Gemara and its commentators sometimes use the question of whether an object may serve as *kesef kiddushin* as a litmus test for ownership. Can a *kohen* say, *harei at mekudeshes li be'bechor zeh* — "you are hereby betrothed to me with this *bechor*"? If he can, the animal is unambiguously his property, not merely an entitlement or a right. The Mishnah's ruling that he may sell it suggests as much, but the explicit mention of *kiddushin* reinforces the point.

### Time Period Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיבֵיהּ: בִּבְכוֹר נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא תִפְדֶּה״ וְנִמְכָּר הוּא, בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה — אֵימָא סֵיפָא: ״וְאֶת דָּמָם תִּזְרוֹק עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״, בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה מִי אִיכָּא מִזְבֵּחַ? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** § Rava **raised an objection to** the statement of Rav Naḥman from a *baraita*: **With regard to a firstborn** offering, it **is stated:** "But the firstling of an ox, or the firstling of a sheep, or the firstling of a goat, **you shall not redeem;** they are holy: And you shall sprinkle their blood against the altar" (Numbers 18:17). **But it may be sold** to another priest for consumption after its sacrifice. **What** era **are we dealing with** in this *baraita*? **If we say** it is **in the present, say the latter clause** of the verse: **"And you shall sprinkle their blood against the altar." Is there an altar in the present? Rather,** it is **obvious** that it is referring to the time **when the Temple is standing.**

The key distinction in this entire discussion is introduced by Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *lo shanu ela bizman hazeh* — this ruling applies only nowadays, in the absence of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The reason is that *de'ho'il ve'is lei le'Kohen zechiyah begavei* — nowadays the *kohen* has effective and comprehensive ownership of the *bechor*, because even an unblemished animal will never be brought as a *korban*. Since there is no *Beit HaMikdash*, the animal must wait until it develops a blemish before being eaten, and in the interim it is unambiguously the *kohen*'s property to manage, sell, or use as he sees fit.

But *aval bizman she'Beis HaMikdash kayam* — when the *Beit HaMikdash* stands, the situation is different. An unblemished *bechor* is headed for the altar. The *kohen* receives this animal and brings it as a *korban*, but in doing so he is acting, as it were, *mishulchan gavo'ah* — just as a *kohen* who eats the meat of a Yisrael's *chatat* is eating from God's table and is not considered that *korban*'s owner, so too a *kohen* who brings a *bechor* in the time of the *Beit HaMikdash* is not considered its owner. Consequently, *ein mochrin oso tamim chai* — an unblemished *bechor* may not be sold, because the *kohen* does not have the ownership rights that would authorize such a sale.

### Time Period Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּבַעַל מוּם — הַיְינוּ ״בְּהֶמְתָּהּ״! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בְּתָם. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא אִית לֵיהּ זְכִיָּיה בְּגַוֵּויהּ — שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לֵית לֵיהּ — לְמָה לִי מִבְּהֵמָה? תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִ״שְּׁלָלָהּ״, וְלֹא שְׁלַל שָׁמַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** **And to what** type of animal is the *baraita* referring? **If you say** that it is referring **to a blemished** firstborn animal, which belongs to its owners, **this is** included in the term **"its animals,"** which one is commanded to destroy. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the *baraita* is referring **to an unblemished** firstborn offering. **And granted, if you say** that the priest **has** the right of **acquisition to** an unblemished firstborn offering when the Temple is standing, it is **well,** as although it belongs to an individual, the Torah nevertheless exempts it from destruction. **But if you say** that a priest **does not have** the right of acquisition, **why do I** need to derive its exemption **from** the term "its **animals"? One should derive its** exemption **from** the term **"its spoil,"** from which one can infer: **But not the spoils of Heaven.**

The Gemara immediately challenges this position. Rava cites the very Mishnah that permits selling the *bechor* unblemished and alive. The Mishnah also refers to a situation where the animal is *tam veshachut* — unblemished and slaughtered — as a case where sale is prohibited. But, Rava argues, when does the scenario of an unblemished *shechutah* arise? Only when there is a *Beit HaMikdash* and the animal is actually brought as a *korban*. The Mishnah is therefore implicitly set in the time of the *Beit HaMikdash*, and yet it permits selling the *bechor* unblemished while alive — which would contradict Rav Nachman's position.

### Mishnah Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ: מַאי שְׁנָא דִּידַן וּמַאי שְׁנָא דִּידְהוּ? אֲנַן יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן בְּמוּמַיְיהוּ, אִינְהוּ — כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא בְּהוּ מִקְצָת כֹּהֵן, מַפְקַע לֵיהּ לְכֹהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna is speaking **of the present. And** as for **that which** is **difficult for you: What is different** about **our** firstborn offerings **and what is different** about **their** firstborn offerings, there is in fact a difference, as **we** must **give** ours **to a priest once they are blemished.** Although it is permitted for Israelites to eat blemished firstborn offerings, they are still required to give them to priests. By contrast, **they,** the offspring mentioned in the mishna, are different, **since there is part of a priest in** each of **them** due to the uncertainty of their parentage. And this is sufficient to **dislodge the** claims of all other **priests** to the animal.

The Gemara deflects this challenge. The Mishnah, it explains, need not be read as contrasting the case of unblemished-alive against unblemished-slaughtered in order to teach that the latter is forbidden. Rather, the Mishnah is discussing the present time, *bizman hazeh*, and the *chiddush* is precisely that even nowadays, when an unblemished animal cannot be brought to the *Beit HaMikdash*, the *kohen* may sell it. The scenario of unblemished-slaughtered simply does not arise nowadays; there is no altar, no *korban*, and therefore no case to discuss. The Mishnah's formulation — focusing on cases available in the present — tells us something meaningful about today's law without implying anything about the status of a *kohen* vis-à-vis an unblemished *bechor* when the *Beit HaMikdash* stood.

## Pasuk Analysis & Proof

### Bayit Aleinu Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיב רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: כֹּהֶנֶת שֶׁנִּתְעָרֵבָה וְלָדָהּ בִּוְלַד שִׁפְחָתָהּ, הִגְדִּילוּ הַתַּעֲרוֹבוֹת — מְשַׁחְרְרִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, וּשְׁנֵיהֶם אוֹכְלִין בִּתְרוּמָה, וְחוֹלְקִין חֵלֶק אֶחָד עַל הַגּוֹרֶן. בְּכוֹרָן יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ, וְיֵאָכְלוּ בְּמוּמָן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Mesharshiyya raises an objection** from a mishna (*Yevamot* 99a): In the case of **a priestess's offspring who was comingled with her maidservant's offspring,** when **the comingled** children **have grown up, they free each other,** and therefore whichever one was a slave will be emancipated. **And** even beforehand, **they both** may **partake of *teruma*,** as both a priest and the slave of a priest may partake of *teruma*. **And they receive one share** of *teruma* **in the granary,** because the slave of a priest receives no share. And **their firstborn** animals must **graze until they become unfit** for sacrifice by virtue of a blemish, **and** then **they may be eaten with their blemish.**

The Gemara now presses the question more sharply with a *braita* citing the verse about *bechor*: *lo tipadeh* — it may not be redeemed — from which the inference is drawn that while it may not be redeemed, it may be sold. Rav Mesharshiya asks: in what context is this *braita* operating? If we say it is discussing the present time, the verse itself concludes — *ve'es damam tizrok al ha-mizbe'ach, ve'es chelbam taktir* — "their blood shall be sprinkled on the altar and their fat shall be burned." This reference to the altar and the offering makes clear that we are discussing the time of the *Beit HaMikdash*, where the *bechor* is actually being brought as a *korban*. And in that context, the *braita* teaches that the *bechor* may be sold. This appears to prove Rav Nachman wrong — it seems that even when the *Beit HaMikdash* stands, and the unblemished *bechor* is to be brought as a *korban*, it may nonetheless be sold by the *kohen*, which would imply that the *kohen* is its owner.

### Pasuk Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי אִירְיָא? רֵישָׁא — בְּבַעַל מוּם, סֵיפָא — בְּתָם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman responds: **Are** the two clauses of the verse **comparable? The first clause** is stated **with regard to a blemished** animal, and it is teaching that it may be sold, and **the latter clause** is stated **with regard to an unblemished** animal, and it teaches that it is sacrificed as an offering. Unblemished animals may not be sold when the Temple is standing.

The Gemara resolves this by reading the verse in two parts, each applying to a different scenario. The beginning of the verse — *lo tipadeh*, it may not be redeemed but may be sold — refers to a blemished animal. A blemished *bechor* cannot be redeemed, since *bechor* admits no redemption regardless, but it may be sold. The end of the verse — sprinkling the blood on the altar, burning the fat — refers to an unblemished animal that is brought as a *korban*. On this reading, the *drasha* permitting sale applies only to the blemished case, not to the unblemished one. The *braita* therefore offers no proof that an unblemished *bechor* in the time of the *Beit HaMikdash* may be sold, and Rav Nachman's position stands undisturbed.

This reading is internally elegant. What type of *bechor* would its owner wish to redeem? Precisely one that has a blemish — a *kohen* with a blemished *bechor* might prefer to redeem it for money and use the proceeds freely. The verse therefore addresses that very impulse: even if your *bechor* has a blemish, you cannot redeem it; you may sell it, but you may not redeem it. The unblemished *bechor*, by contrast, is headed for the altar.

## Taruva Case

### Mixed Identity Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה — מַאי שְׁנָא דִּידַן וּמַאי שְׁנָא דִּידְהוּ? דִּידַן נָמֵי יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ! אֶלָּא לָאו בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא אִית לְהוּ לְכֹהֵן זְכִיָּיה בְּגַוֵּיהּ — שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לֵית לְהוּ — לֵיתֵי גִּזְבָּר וְלִישְׁקְלֵהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **What** era **are we dealing with** in this mishna? **If we say** it is **in the present, what is different** about **our** firstborn offerings, i.e., those of Israelites, **and what is different** about **their** firstborn offerings, those of priests? **Ours also require** that they have **blemishes** before they may be eaten. Why is the *halakha* stated specifically with regard to priests? **Rather, is it not** speaking of the time **when the Temple is standing? Granted, if you say** that **the priest has** the right of **acquisition to** an unblemished firstborn offering when the Temple is standing, it is **well. But if you say** that a priest **does not have** the right of acquisition, **let** the Temple **treasurer [*gizbar*] come and take** the firstborn offerings for sacrifice. Why are they left in the offspring's possession until they develop blemishes?

The Gemara introduces a *braita* involving a *ta'aruva* — a case of mixed identity. A *kohenes* gave birth to a son, but the infant was mixed up at the hospital with the newborn son of her *shifchah* — her slave woman. The children grow up without knowing which of the two is the *kohen* and which is the slave belonging to the *kohen*. This uncertainty creates a series of unusual *halachic* situations.

Once they reach maturity, the first step is *meshachrerin zeh es zeh* — they free one another. Each one writes a document of manumission declaring that if I am the master and you are my slave, you are hereby freed, and they do so mutually. This resolves the question of personal status as between the two of them, though the one who was in fact the slave is now a freedman rather than a *kohen*.

Before the freeing takes place, both may eat *terumah*: one is a *kohen*, the other is an *eved* of a *kohen*, and both categories permit consumption of *terumah*. However, when they go to the threshing floor to receive their *terumah*, they receive only a single portion. There is a concern about giving *terumah* directly to the *eved* of a *kohen* independently, since this could create the impression that he is a *kohen* himself. They therefore go together as a pair and receive one combined allocation.

### Waiting for Blemish

The more analytically interesting scenario concerns a *bechor* that one of these two individuals owns. If he were known to be a *kohen*, he would keep the *bechor*, bring it as a *korban* when it is unblemished, and eat it. If he were known to be a slave, he would have to give it to a *kohen* — specifically his master. But since neither knows which he is, the *halacha* is *yiru ad sheyistavu veyochlu bemuman* — the animal grazes until it develops a blemish, and then it is eaten with the blemish.

Why this resolution? Once the animal has a blemish, the question of bringing it as a *korban* no longer applies. It becomes a straightforward question of monetary ownership, governed by the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* — the burden of proof lies with the one seeking to extract from another's possession. Neither of the two individuals can prove that the other is the *kohen* entitled to the animal, and therefore neither can be compelled to hand it over. Without the blemish, however, the question is not merely monetary. An unblemished *bechor* in the time of the *Beit HaMikdash* must be brought as a *korban*, and only the *kohen* who owns it may do so. Since we do not know who is the *kohen*, the animal cannot simply be brought by any *kohen* who steps forward.

### Seizure Argument

The Gemara draws a significant inference from this case. Why, it asks, does the *Beit HaMikdash* not simply send its treasurer to seize the animal and ensure that it is brought as a *korban*? After all, if the *kohen* is not truly the owner of an unblemished *bechor* — if he merely holds the right to bring it but the *bechor* fundamentally belongs to God — then the *Beit HaMikdash* should be able to compel its offering. The very fact that we instead allow these two individuals to retain the animal until it develops a blemish demonstrates that the *kohen* does have genuine ownership rights over his *bechor*, even while it is unblemished. Because one of the two individuals may be the *kohen* owner, the *Beit HaMikdash* cannot simply appropriate the animal.

### Nowadays Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ, אֲפִילּוּ דִּילַן נָמֵי יִרְעוּ? לָא (מָצִינוּ) [מָצֵינַן] מְדַחֵינַן לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן, דְּלָא אִית כָּאן סְפֵק כְּהוּנָּה. הָנֵי סְפֵיקוֹת מְדַחוּ לְכֹהֵן, כֹּל חַד וְחַד אָמַר לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן: דַּאֲנָא כֹּהֵן וַאֲנָא כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Actually,** the mishna is referring **to the present. What** is **difficult for you,** the fact that **even our** firstborn offerings should **graze** until they are blemished? **We** Israelites **are unable to deny the priest** who claims the firstborn offering, **as there is no** member of the **priesthood,** however **uncertain, here** among the owners of the animal. By contrast, in **these** cases of **uncertain** parentage described in the mishna, they can **deny the priest,** as **both of them** can **say to the priest: I am a priest, and I am a priest.** Since the burden of proof rests upon the claimant, both may keep their animals until they are blemished.

The Gemara declines this proof. It maintains that the *braita* is discussing the present time, *bizman hazeh*, and that the *chiddush* of the case is not the ruling to wait for a blemish — since nowadays every *bechor* must wait for a blemish anyway — but rather the monetary *halacha* that follows. Everyone today waits for a blemish; that is simply the baseline. The novel teaching of this *braita*, according to the Gemara, is that since each of the two individuals might be the *kohen*, neither is obligated to give the animal to the other. The principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* operates in their favor: no other *kohen* can come and demand the animal either, since each of the two will declare, "I am the *kohen* and this animal is mine." Tosafos makes explicit what the Gemara is implying: the *chiddush* here is purely monetary, not the instruction to wait for a blemish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה, מַאי אִירְיָא בְּכוֹרוֹת דְּהָלֵין סְפֵיקוֹת? אֲפִילּוּ דִּידַן נָמֵי יִרְעוּ! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים, וְאִי בְּבַעַל מוּם — יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ? הָא מְסוֹאָבִין וְקָיְימִין! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בְּתַמִּין, וְהָכִי הוּא דְּלָא מְזַבְּנִי וַדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents **an alternative version** of the previous discussion: If the mishna is referring **to the present, why specifically** discuss the **firstborn** offerings **of these** cases of **uncertain** parentage? **Even our** firstborn offerings **should** be left to **graze** until they are blemished. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the mishna is referring **to** the time **when the Temple is standing. And if** the mishna is **speaking of a blemished** animal, does it make sense to say: **They** should **graze until they become unfit** for sacrifice? **They are already unfit. Rather,** it is **obvious** that the mishna is referring **to unblemished** animals, **and it is** only in a case **such as this,** when the owners might not be priests, **that they definitely may not sell** the animal. One can infer that a priest may sell an unblemished firstborn offering when the Temple is standing.

The Gemara even offers a second formulation (*lashon acher*) to reinforce this reading. Nowadays, the *braita*'s instruction to "let it graze until it develops a blemish" is unremarkable — that is what one does with every *bechor*. The novel point is that these two individuals of uncertain identity may collectively prevent any other *kohen* from claiming the animal. Each can say to a third-party *kohen*: "I may be the *kohen* owner; you cannot take this from me."

It is worth appreciating the intellectual texture of this exchange. The attempt to prove from the *ta'aruva* case that the *kohen* is the owner of his unblemished *bechor* was elegant: if the *kohen* were not the owner, the *Beit HaMikdash* would simply seize the animal and ensure it was brought. The Gemara's response — that we are in fact discussing the present time and the *chiddush* is merely monetary — is technically adequate but, as the Gemara itself acknowledges, less sweeping. The *hava amina* (the initial assumption) powerfully illustrates what it would mean to say the *kohen* is not the owner: his personal preference to wait for a blemish would be legally irrelevant, and the *Beit HaMikdash* would simply take what it regards as its own.

## Ir HaNidachat

### Ir HaNidachat Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״בְּהֶמְתָּהּ״ — פְּרָט לְבֶהֱמַת בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֶׁבָּהּ, ״שְׁלָלָהּ״ — פְּרָט לְכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁבָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Sanhedrin* 4:5): A verse discussing an idolatrous city states: "You shall smite the inhabitants of that city with the edge of the sword, destroying it utterly, and all that is therein and its animals, with the edge of the sword. And you shall gather all its spoil into the midst of the broad place thereof, and shall burn with fire the city, and all the spoil thereof" (Deuteronomy 13:16–17). **Rabbi Shimon says:** The term **"its animals"** serves **to exclude firstborn** kosher **animals and** the animal **tithe that are in** the city, as they are sacred. The term **"its spoil"** serves **to exclude money** used to redeem the **second tithe that is** found **in** the city, as it too is sacred.

The Gemara turns to yet another case in its ongoing effort to resolve the question of *kohen* ownership. An *ir hanidachat* — an idolatrous city whose inhabitants were led to worship *avodah zarah* — is subject to a special law requiring the destruction of all its property. The Torah specifies *shlalah* — its booty — indicating that what must be destroyed is the private property of the city's inhabitants. Excluded, however, is *shlal shamayim* — things that belong to Heaven. Sacred objects such as *Sifrei Torah*, *mezuzos*, and sanctified animals do not belong to the city and are therefore not destroyed with it.

### Bechor Status Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה — מִי אִיכָּא עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת? וְהָתְנַן: אֵין עוֹשִׂין עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין שֶׁל שִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** **What** era **are we dealing with** in this *baraita*? **If we say** it is **in the present, can there be an idolatrous city** in the present day? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Sanhedrin* 2a): A city **may be designated** as **an idolatrous city only in** accordance with the ruling of **a court of seventy-one** judges, i.e., the Great Sanhedrin? **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the mishna is referring **to** the period **when the Temple is standing.**

A *braita* cites Rabbi Shimon, who derives from a separate word in the verse — *behemtah*, "its animals" — that *bechor* and *ma'aser behemah* are excluded from the destruction of the *ir hanidachat*. The *drasha* reads: its animals are destroyed, excluding the animals of *bechor* and *ma'aser*. The Gemara asks: in what context is this *braita* operating? An *ir hanidachat* requires a *beis din* of seventy-one, which functioned only when the *Beit HaMikdash* stood. We are therefore squarely in the world of the *Beit HaMikdash*. Furthermore, if the *bechor* in question were blemished, it would be so thoroughly the *kohen*'s property that it would seemingly be destroyed together with the rest of his belongings — why would we need a special verse to exclude it? The Gemara therefore concludes that we must be discussing an unblemished *bechor*, one that in theory would be brought as a *korban*.

### Kedushah Prevents Trade

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְמַאי? אִי נֵימָא בְּבַעַל מוּם — הַיְינוּ ״בְּהֶמְתָּהּ״! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בְּתָם. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא אִית לֵיהּ זְכִיָּיה בְּגַוֵּיהּ — שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לֵית לֵיהּ — לְמָה לִי מִבְּהֶמָה? תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִ״שְּׁלָלָהּ״, וְלֹא שְׁלַל שָׁמַיִם! לְעוֹלָם בְּבַעַל מוּם, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ הַיְינוּ ״בְּהֶמְתָּהּ״ — מִי שֶׁנֶּאֱכָל בְּתוֹרַת ״בְּהֶמְתָּהּ״, יָצְאוּ בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר (שֶׁאֵינוֹ בִּכְלַל) [שֶׁאֵין נֶאֱכָלִין בְּתוֹרַת] ״בְּהֶמְתָּהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And to what** type of animal is the *baraita* referring? **If you say** that it is referring **to a blemished** firstborn animal, which belongs to its owners, **this is** included in the term **"its animals,"** which one is commanded to destroy. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the *baraita* is referring **to an unblemished** firstborn offering. **And granted, if you say** that the priest **has** the right of **acquisition to** an unblemished firstborn offering when the Temple is standing, it is **well,** as although it belongs to an individual, the Torah nevertheless exempts it from destruction. **But if you say** that a priest **does not have** the right of acquisition, **why do I** need to derive its exemption **from** the term "its **animals"? One should derive its** exemption **from** the term **"its spoil,"** from which one can infer: **But not the spoils of Heaven.** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the *baraita* is speaking **of a blemished** animal. **And** as for **that which** is **difficult for you,** that **this is** considered **"its animals,"** one can answer that this term includes only **that which is eaten in the manner of its animals,** without additional restrictions. This **excludes** even a blemished **firstborn** offering **and** animal **tithe** offering, **which is not** included **in the category of "its animals."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: כׇּל פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין נִמְכָּרִין בָּאִיטְלִיז, וְנִשְׁחָטִין בָּאִיטְלִיז, וְנִשְׁקָלִין בְּלִיטְרָא, חוּץ מִן הַבְּכוֹר וּמִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר, שֶׁהֲנָאָתָן לַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** And which restrictions apply to the consumption of blemished firstborn animals? The restrictions are **as we learned** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 31a): **All disqualified consecrated** animals are redeemed and then **sold in the butchers' market [*ba'itliz*],** where they fetch a higher price, in order to benefit the Temple treasury. **And they are slaughtered in the butchers' market, and** their meat **is weighed** and sold **by the *litra*,** as is done with non-sacred meat, **with the exception of a firstborn** offering **and** animal **tithe** offering, **as their benefit** is accrued **to their owners.** The money will benefit their owners rather than the Temple treasury, and private individuals may not disgrace disqualified offerings by treating them as one would non-sacred meat.

The Gemara again finds a way out. It insists that the *braita* is discussing a blemished *bechor* after all. The question then becomes: why was a special verse needed? If the *bechor* has a blemish and the *kohen* can eat it freely, is it not simply his property, subject to destruction like any other? The answer is that even a blemished *bechor* is not treated as entirely ordinary property. The Mishnah establishes that all sanctified animals whose blemishes have been redeemed may be sold in the marketplace and weighed out by the pound as *chullin*, except for *bechor* and *ma'aser behemah*, which are not redeemed and retain an element of *kedushah* in the way they are handled. Even with a blemish, they cannot be sold in the open marketplace like ordinary meat. Their *kedushah* continues to express itself, preventing them from being treated as fully *behemtah* — as completely ordinary animals. Therefore the word *behemtah* in the verse excludes them: since they do not function as ordinary *behemtah*, they are not included among the city's ordinary property to be destroyed.

On this reading, the verse was not needed because the *kohen* is the owner; it was needed because the *bechor*'s *kedushah*, even after a blemish, renders it categorically distinct from normal property. Rav Nachman's position remains intact.

## Ashám & Me'ilah

### Kodashim Kalim Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״וּמָעֲלָה מַעַל בַּה׳״ — לְרַבּוֹת קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים, שֶׁהֵן מָמוֹן בְּעָלִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If one takes an oath falsely denying possession of another's property, he must pay the value of that property and an additional one-fifth to the owner. The passage stating this *halakha* begins: "If anyone sin, and commit a trespass against the Lord, and deal falsely with his neighbor in a matter of deposit, or of pledge, or of robbery" (Leviticus 5:21). By stating: **"And commit a trespass against the Lord,"** the verse serves **to include** one who denies possession of **offerings of lesser sanctity** belonging to another, **as they are the property of** their **owner,** who has the right to consume their meat after the sacrifice. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.**

The Gemara now brings another challenge, this time from the laws of *me'ilah* — trespass against God. The Torah describes a situation where someone is entrusted with his fellow's property and falsely denies it under oath, committing *ma'al b'Hashem* — a trespass involving God's name. The inclusion of the phrase *l'Hashem* beyond the plain meaning of the oath's sanctity prompted a *drasha*: the verse comes to include *kodashim kalim* — lighter sacrifices — which are owned by their bearers yet possess a dimension of divine sanctity. This is the classic position of Rabbi Yose HaGlili, who teaches *l'rabot kodashim kalim she'hem mamon ba'alim* — the lighter sacrifices are the property of their owners, and one who falsely denies holding his fellow's *shelamim* has committed *ma'al b'Hashem*.

### Ben Azzai Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: לְרַבּוֹת שְׁלָמִים. אַבָּא יוֹסֵי בֶּן דּוֹסַאי אוֹמֵר: לֹא אָמַר בֶּן עַזַּאי אֶלָּא בִּבְכוֹר בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Ben Azzai says:** The verse serves **to include** only one who denies possession of **a peace offering** of another, not other offerings of lesser sanctity. **Abba Yosei ben Dosai says: Ben Azzai stated** his *halakha* **only with regard to a firstborn** offering, that it is considered the property of the owner, i.e., the priest, but not with regard to other offerings of lesser sanctity.

A variant tradition (*acherim omrim*) limits the inclusion to *shelamim* alone, and then Abba Yosi ben Dosai transmits a further restriction in the name of Ben Azzai: only *bechor* — and not all *kodashim kalim* — is truly owned in this sense.

### Bechor Distinction

Why would Ben Azzai exclude *shelamim* and *ma'aser* while retaining *bechor*? Rashi explains that *shelamim* carry numerous ritual trappings — *semichah*, *tenufah*, the bringing of *nesachim* — that continuously emphasize its status as a *korban* and distance it from the owner's sphere of personal property. *Ma'aser behemah* carries the restriction of *lo yimacher* — it may not be sold — which similarly marks it as something other than fully owned property. *Bechor* alone lacks these extra encumbrances and restrictions. There is no *semichah*, no *tenufah*, no libation, and no prohibition on sale. It is therefore the one *korban* that Ben Azzai regards as sufficiently the owner's property to fall under the *ma'al* category.

### Context Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה — הָא דּוּמְיָא דִּשְׁלָמִים קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים. וּבְמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בְּבַעַל מוּם — הָא דּוּמְיָא דִּשְׁלָמִים קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו בְּתָם, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אִית לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן זְכִיָּיה בְּגַוֵּויהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** era **are we dealing with** in this *baraita*? **If we say** it is **in the present, doesn't** the *baraita* **teach** the *halakha* of a firstborn offering as **similar to** the *halakha* of **a peace offering?** Peace offerings are not a relevant category in the present day. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the *baraita* is referring **to** the period **when the Temple is standing. And what** type of animal **are we dealing with? If we say** that the *baraita* is referring specifically **to a blemished** animal, **doesn't it teach** the *halakha* of a firstborn offering as **similar to** the *halakha* of **a peace offering?** Peace offerings are the property of their owners even when they are unblemished. **Rather, is it not** referring even **to an unblemished** firstborn animal? **And** the *baraita* teaches that this animal is the property of its owner. **Learn from it** that **the priest has** the right of **acquisition to** an unblemished firstborn offering when the Temple is standing.

Having cited Ben Azzai's position, the Gemara immediately asks the standard pair of questions: what time period and what state of the animal are we discussing? Since *shelamim* is mentioned as a contrast case, we are clearly in a context where animals are being brought as *korbanos* — i.e., the time of the *Beit HaMikdash*. And if we are discussing blemished animals, the case is not comparable to *shelamim* in the way the *drasha* requires. We must therefore be discussing an unblemished *bechor* in the time of the *Beit HaMikdash*. And the very fact that Ben Azzai considers it *mamon ba'alim* in that context would appear to prove that the *kohen* is the owner of his unblemished *bechor* even when it would be brought as a *korban*.

### Chutz La'aretz Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא, לְעוֹלָם בְּתָם, וּבִבְכוֹר בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא דְּאָמַר: אִם בָּאוּ תְּמִימִין יִקְרְבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: No, actually,** the *baraita* is referring **to an unblemished** animal when the Temple is standing, but it is speaking **of a firstborn** offering **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **and it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said: If unblemished** firstborn offerings **came** from outside of Eretz Yisrael, **they may be sacrificed** on the altar. This language indicates that there is no obligation to sacrifice such an animal, as people did not generally bring their firstborn offerings from outside Eretz Yisrael. Therefore, a priest is able to acquire it even when it is unblemished. Firstborn offerings from Eretz Yisrael, by contrast, cannot be acquired by the priests.

Abaye responds with the third avenue of escape that has now entered the discussion: *l'olam b'tam u'be'bechor b'chutz la'aretz* — granted that it is unblemished and granted that there is a *Beit HaMikdash*, but we are discussing a *bechor* that arose outside of *Eretz Yisrael*. Rabbi Shimon, as noted earlier, holds that *bechor* in *chutz la'aretz* is ideally left to develop a blemish before being brought — or consumed — since *bechor* and *ma'aser behemah* are linked to the agricultural and communal life of the Land. *Bedi'eved*, if an unblemished *bechor* from *chutz la'aretz* is brought to the *Beit HaMikdash*, it is offered. But *lechatchila*, it should wait for a blemish, much like the situation *bizman hazeh*. In such a case, the *kohen* is effectively the owner, because the animal is not headed to the altar; hence it is *mamon ba'alim*, as Ben Azzai teaches.

This completes the set of three defenses available to Rav Nachman's position: the animal has a *mum*; or it is *bizman hazeh*; or it is in *chutz la'aretz*.

## Yochanan vs. Akiva

### Yochanan's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: מָה לִי אִם אֵינוֹ מֵימֵר בְּחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם, שֶׁהֲרֵי אֵין זָכִין בָּהֶן בְּחַיֵּיהֶן, תֹּאמַר בִּבְכוֹר שֶׁזָּכִין בְּחַיָּיו. בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בְּבַעַל מוּם — הָא דּוּמְיָא דְּחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם קָאָמַר, אֶלָּא לָאו בְּתָם, וְקָתָנֵי זָכִין בּוֹ בְּחַיָּיו!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara **raises an objection** from the mishna: **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said to him: What** is this comparison **for me? If** a priest **does not substitute for a sin offering and a guilt offering, which** priests **do not acquire during** the animals' **lifetimes, will you say** the same **with regard to a firstborn, which** priests **acquire during** the animal's **lifetime? What are we dealing with** in the mishna? **If we say** that it is referring **to a blemished** animal, **isn't** the mishna **saying** that the firstborn offering is **similar to a sin offering and a guilt offering,** which the priests acquire only unblemished? **Rather, is it not** referring **to unblemished** animals? **And it** nevertheless **teaches:** Which priests **acquire during** the animal's **lifetime.**

The Gemara at last returns to the Mishnah itself — the debate between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and Rabbi Akiva with which the entire discussion began. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri challenges Rabbi Akiva: I understand why a *kohen* cannot perform *temurah* on a *chatat* or *asham* brought by a Yisrael — *she'harei ein zachin bahem b'chayeihem*, because the *kohen* has no rights whatsoever to those animals while they are alive. He merely eats the meat after *shechitah*. But *chomer b'bechor she-zachin b'chayav* — what do you say about a *bechor*, where the *kohen* has genuine rights to the animal while it is still alive? How can you say he cannot perform *temurah*? The argument is that the *kohen*'s living ownership of the *bechor* — the fact that this animal is actually his while it breathes — distinguishes it fundamentally from a *chatat* or *asham*.

### Chutz La'aretz Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: הָא נָמֵי בִּבְכוֹר בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אִם בָּאוּ תְּמִימִים — יִקְרְבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: This** mishna **too** is referring **to a firstborn** offering **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **and it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said: If unblemished** firstborn offerings **came** from outside of Eretz Yisrael, **they may be sacrificed** on the altar. The priests do not acquire firstborn offerings from Eretz Yisrael.

Abaye responds, applying the framework already established: the discussion in the Mishnah concerns a *bechor* in *chutz la'aretz*, where Rabbi Shimon rules that one should ideally wait for a blemish. In *chutz la'aretz*, the *kohen* is the owner, and Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri correctly observes that the *kohen* has rights to the living animal. The question of whether he may therefore perform *temurah* is precisely what Rabbi Akiva disputes, even there, based on the *pasuk* about *beis ba'alim*.

### Debate Reframing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: בְּכוֹר, בְּבֵית הַבְּעָלִים — עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, בְּבֵית הַכֹּהֵן — אֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁבָּא לִידֵי כֹהֵן — אֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that acquisition of the firstborn offerings by the priests is **the subject of** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** The *baraita* states: In the case of **a firstborn** offering, when it is **in the house of** its **owner,** who did not yet give it to a priest, its owner **can render** another animal **a substitute** for it. Once it is **in the house of the priest,** its owner **cannot render a substitute** for it. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: Once it has entered the possession of the priest,** its owner **cannot render a substitute** for it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא! מַאי לָאו הָכִי קָאָמַר: בְּבֵית כֹּהֵן — הוּא עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה, וְאֵין בַּעַל עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה, אַלְמָא אִית לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן זְכִיָּיה בְּגַוֵּויהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Apparently, the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar **is** identical to that of **the first *tanna*.** What is their dispute? **What, is it not** correct that **this** is what the first *tanna* **is saying:** Once it is **in the house,** i.e., possession, **of the priest,** the priest **can render a substitute** for it, **but** the **owner cannot render a substitute** for it, as the priest has acquired it; while Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds that once it enters the possession of the priest, neither of them can render a substitute with it. **Apparently,** the first *tanna* **holds** that **the priest has** the right of **acquisition to** an unblemished firstborn offering when the Temple is standing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי, הָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The opinion of the first *tanna* is **not difficult;** one can say that the dispute in the *baraita* concerns a firstborn offering outside of Eretz Yisrael, while those from Eretz Yisrael are not acquired by the priests. And **this** opinion of the first *tanna* accords with the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri** in the mishna above, and **that** opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar accords with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva** in that mishna.

The Gemara then steps back to reflect on how this debate maps onto a broader *tannaitic* dispute. A *braita* formulates the rule as follows: *bechor b'veis ba'alim osim temurah, b'veis kohen ein osim temurah* — for a *bechor* in the owner's house, *temurah* is performed; once it is in the *kohen*'s house, *temurah* is not performed. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar disagrees and says: once the animal comes into the *kohen*'s possession, no one performs *temurah*.

At first glance, these positions might seem identical — after all, if only the owner can perform *temurah* and the animal is now with the *kohen*, the owner no longer has it and clearly cannot act. The Gemara therefore re-reads the *Tanna Kamma*: *b'veis kohen hu oseh temurah ve'ein ba'al oseh temurah* — once it is in the *kohen*'s house, the *kohen* performs *temurah*, and the original owner may not. This is the position aligned with Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, who holds that the *kohen* is the owner of his *bechor* and may perform *temurah*. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar then takes the position of Rabbi Akiva: once the *kohen* has it, no one performs *temurah*, because the *kohen* is not the owner and the original owner has relinquished possession.

The Gemara concludes simply: *ha Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, ha Rabbi Akiva* — the two positions in the *braita* parallel the two positions of the Mishnah exactly. This entire discussion of whether the *kohen* is the owner of his *bechor*, and all the attempts to prove it from *bayit aleinu*, *ta'aruva*, *ir hanidachat*, and the *me'ilah* case, ultimately circle back to that foundational Mishnaic debate, which remains in the form of a *machloket* between these two Tannaim, with the resolution anchored to the case of *chutz la'aretz* and the opinion of Rabbi Shimon.

## Sale Restrictions

### Kohen-to-Kohen Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא כֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן, אֲבָל כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — אָסוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּלְמָא אָזֵיל יִשְׂרָאֵל וְשָׁדֵי בֵּיהּ מוּמָא, וּמַמְטֵי לְחָכָם, וְאוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״בְּכוֹר זֶה נָתַן לִי כֹּהֵן בְּמוּמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara earlier (7b) cited a mishna (*Ma'aser Sheni* 1:2) which states that a firstborn offering may be sold unblemished only while it is alive. **Rav Ḥisda says:** The Sages **taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to **a priest** selling **to a priest, but** with regard to **a priest** selling it **to an Israelite,** it is **prohibited** even when the animal is alive. **What is the reason?** Since an Israelite can consume a firstborn animal only if it is blemished, there is a concern that **perhaps the Israelite will go and inflict a blemish on** the firstborn offering **and bring** it **to a halakhic authority** to attain permission to eat it, **and** he will **say to him: A priest gave me this firstborn** offering **when it** was already **blemished.**

Having explored the theoretical dimensions of *kohen* ownership, the Gemara pivots to an eminently practical question. Rav Chisda rules: *lo shanu ela kohen le-kohen, aval kohen le-Yisrael assur* — the ruling permitting a *kohen* to sell his *bechor* applies only when he sells it to another *kohen*, but it is prohibited to sell it to a Yisrael.

The reason, explains Rav Chisda, is the concern of *dilma azil Yisrael ve'shadi bei muma* — lest the Yisrael who now possesses an unblemished *bechor* go and inflict a blemish on it himself. The Yisrael has every incentive to do so: without a blemish, the animal cannot be eaten; with a blemish, it can be consumed. If the Yisrael creates the blemish artificially, that is itself a transgression. But the further concern is that when he brings the animal to a sage to have the blemish certified, he will claim *bechor zeh natun li Kohen be-mumo* — "this *bechor* was given to me by the *kohen* already bearing this blemish" — thereby deflecting suspicion and falsely implicating the *kohen* in the transgression.

### Ruling Safeguard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי שָׁרֵי חָכָם הָכִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַב: אֵין רוֹאִין בְּכוֹר לְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן כֹּהֵן עִמּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And may a halakhic authority permit** the animal in a case **such as this? But didn't Rav say:** A halakhic authority **may not look at a firstborn** offering brought **by an Israelite** to determine if it has the type of permanent blemish allowing it to be slaughtered **unless a priest** is **with him,** out of concern that the Israelite will keep the blemished animal for himself without performing the mitzva of giving it to a priest? If so, one can demand that the priest who sold him the animal must be present, and this should prevent the Israelite from lying about the blemish.

The Gemara raises a counterargument: would a sage actually rule in favor of the Yisrael under these circumstances? Rav is cited: *ein ro'in bechor le-Yisrael ela im ken Kohen immo* — sages will not render a ruling on a *bechor* in a Yisrael's possession without the *kohen* being present. This rule exists as a broader safeguard against *kochot* withholding *bechor* from *kohanim* — since a Yisrael might claim falsely that he received it back from a *kohen*, sages require the *kohen* to be present and to testify directly. If the *kohen* is present, the sage can ask directly when the blemish arose — before or after the sale — and the scheme of blaming the *kohen* for an artificially induced blemish collapses.

### Mar Zutra Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהוּ: לָא סְבִירָא לְכוּ הָא דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה כְּכֹהֵן הַמְסַיֵּיעַ בְּבֵית הַגֳּרָנוֹת? אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ: לָא סְבִירָא לַן, דַּאֲנַן מִיזְבָּן קָא זָבְנִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Mar Zutra **said to them: Do you not hold** like **that which Rav Huna, son of Rabbi Yehoshua, said,** that Israelites may not receive an unblemished firstborn offering from a priest **because it looks like a priest who assists at the threshing floor? They said to** Mar Zutra: **We hold** that this is **not** a concern, **as we purchased** it for its fair market value, and did not receive it at a discount. Therefore, there is no appearance of impropriety.

The Gemara illustrates the texture of these concerns with a brief narrative. Mar Zutra visited the household of Rav Ashi and was invited to eat. Meat was brought before him, and those present told him it was the meat of a *bechor* — considered especially healthful — and he should partake. Mar Zutra asked how they had obtained *bechor* meat, since they were not *kohanim*. They replied that a certain *kohen* had sold them his *bechor*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: וְלָא סְבִירָא לְכוּ הָדִתְנַן: עַד כַּמָּה יִשְׂרָאֵל חַיָּיב לִיטַּפֵּל בַּבְּכוֹר? בַּדַּקָּה — שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, ובגסה — חמשים יום. וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״תְּנֵהוּ לִי בְּתוֹךְ זְמַנּוֹ״ — הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִתְּנֶנּוּ לוֹ. וְאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מָה טַעַם? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה כְּכֹהֵן הַמְסַיֵּיעַ בְּבֵית הַגֳּרָנוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** Mar Zutra **said to them: But do you not hold** like **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 26b): **Until when** is **an Israelite obligated to tend to a firstborn** animal before giving it to the priest? In the case **of a small** animal, a sheep or goat, **thirty** days, **and** in the case **of a large** animal, one of a herd of cattle, **fifty days. And if** the priest **said to** the owner: **Give it to me within its period,** the owner **may not give it to him. And Rav Sheshet says: What is** the **reason** for this? **Because it looks like a priest who assists at the threshing floor,** as the priest is assisting the Israelite by assuming the responsibility of tending to the firstborn.

Mar Zutra then pivoted to a second challenge, citing the Mishnah that requires a Yisrael to raise his *bechor* for thirty days (small animals) or fifty days (large animals) before transferring it to the *kohen*. If a *kohen* offers to relieve the Yisrael of that financial burden and take the animal early, the Yisrael may not hand it over — because such a favor from the *kohen* would constitute an implicit exchange for the *bechor* he is about to receive. Mar Zutra's point was that even when a Yisrael transfers a *bechor* to a *kohen* to which the *kohen* is legally entitled, if that transfer is entangled with a favor received from the *kohen*, it is inappropriate. A fortiori, one might argue that a purchase from a *kohen* is similarly suspect if the price is artificially low.

### Profit Motive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ: הָתָם מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא, הָכָא מִזְבָּן קָא זָבְנִינַן. לָשׁוֹן אַחֵר, אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ: הָכָא לָא יָהֵיב דְּמֵי, הָכָא קָא יָהֵיב דְּמֵי. מַאי אָמְרַתְּ? מוֹזֵיל כֹּהֵן גַּבֵּיהּ, דְּסָבַר כֹּהֵן דְּכִי הָוֵי לֵיהּ בְּכוֹר אַחֲרִינָא יָהֵיב נִיהֲלֵיהּ? לָא, דְּמַסֵּיק אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ: בּוּצִינָא טָב מִקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to** Mar Zutra: **There** the circumstances of **the matter prove** that the priest accepted responsibility to care for the animal in return for some benefit, and it has the appearance of impropriety. **Here, we purchased it** for its fair market value, and there is no appearance of impropriety. The Gemara presents **an alternative version** of this final response. **They said to** Mar Zutra: **There** the Israelite **does not give payment** to the priest for accepting responsibility to care for the animal. **Here,** the Israelite **gives payment** to the priest, as we purchased the animal. And **what** would **you say** to this claim? Would you say that perhaps the **priest lowered** the price of the animal **for** us, **as the priest thinks that when** we **have another firstborn** offering we **will give it to him,** and therefore it has the appearance of impropriety? This is **not** a concern, **as he thinks:** **A zucchini** now **is better than** the possibility of **a gourd** later. There is no reason to give up an animal now for the hope of receiving another later.

The response is that the *Mishnah*'s case is obviously problematic — the favor and the *bechor* are directly linked, with the *kohen* doing a service and the immediate benefit being the receipt of the *bechor*. A straightforward purchase is entirely different.

The Gemara finally addresses the underlying concern about a discounted sale: *mouzil kohen gabei* — perhaps the *kohen* sold the meat at a discount, hoping that the Yisrael would in turn give him his future *bechor* in exchange. This is the *maris ayin* concern — not that the transaction is prohibited on its face, but that a below-market price signals an implicit deal and creates the appearance of impropriety. The Gemara's response is pithy and psychologically astute: *butzina tav mikara* — a small gourd now is worth more than a large gourd later; a bird in the hand is worth two in the bush. A *kohen* who is selling his *bechor* will extract as much money as he can in the present transaction and will not reduce his price on the speculative hope that this same Yisrael will someday give him a *bechor* in return. The profit motive of the immediate sale rules out any assumption of a discounted exchange.

With this practical resolution, the daf concludes its long and intricate examination of the *kohen*'s relationship to the *bechor* — from the theoretical question of ownership and *temurah*, through the time-sensitive distinctions between the era of the *Beit HaMikdash* and the present day, and finally to the concrete commercial regulations governing how a *kohen* may and may not dispose of the sacred property entrusted to him.
