# Bava Batra 112 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Husband Inherits Wife

### Intro: Husband Rights

The daf opens two lines from the top with the words *mah v'omer*, situating us in the middle of a discussion carried over from the previous day: from where do we know that a husband inherits from his wife? The operative principle is that if a wife dies, her estate passes first to her husband, and only in the absence of a husband does it descend to her children. The *gemara* attempts to derive this rule from various *pesukim*, but the textual basis turns out to be deeply problematic.

### Sheiro Zo Ishto

One candidate *pasuk* reads: *v'natan et hanachala l'sheiro hakarov eilav mimishpachto v'yarash otah* — "you shall give his inheritance to his flesh that is closest to him from his family, and he shall inherit it." The difficulty is that this language sounds like a blood relationship, describing a man passing his estate to his nearest kin. To the extent that we apply the principle *sheiro zu ishto* — that the word *she'er*, meaning flesh, also refers to one's wife on account of the concept of *basar echad*, that husband and wife become one flesh — the *pasuk* would seem to indicate that the *wife* inherits the *husband*, not the reverse. The *gemara* therefore attempted to work with this *pasuk*, in particular seizing on the two words *v'yarash otah* — "he shall inherit *her*" — to derive, somewhat out of context, that the husband inherits the wife. That reading was attributed to Rebbi Akiva.

### Rebbi Yishmael Pesukimim

Rebbi Yishmael took a different approach, marshalling a chain of *pesukim* centered on the concern that a woman's inherited property would pass out of her *shevet*. The straightforward reading of these *pesukim* is that a woman who inherits from her father must marry within her *shevet* so that the land does not pass to a different tribe through her son — since the son follows his father's *shevet*, and if the husband belongs to a different *shevet*, so will the son. This was the central issue with Bnot Tzelofchad and the requirement that they marry from within their tribe. Rebbi Yishmael, however, argues that those *pesukim* collectively demonstrate that if a woman were to die, her estate would pass to her husband — meaning the concern about tribal diversion is animated by the husband's inheritance right, not only by the son's. He further adduced two *pesukim* from *Nach* in which a son appears to hold property that his father never possessed, from which Rebbi Yishmael infers that the son must have inherited through his mother — that is, the father inherited the mother's property upon her death and then passed it to the son.

### Rambam Sofrim

It is worth pausing here to note that the very weakness and non-*pshat* character of all these *pesukim* led the Rambam to a striking conclusion: the husband's right to inherit from his wife is *d'vrai sofrim* — rabbinic in nature — and is not a *d'raita* law at all. A parallel example the Rambam cites is the rule that a *kohen* husband may become *tamei* for his deceased wife, even though the plain reading of the *pasuk* *lo yitama ba'al b'amav l'heichalo* — which can be read as prohibiting a *kohen* from defiling himself — would cut against this. Nevertheless, *Chazal* read *l'sheiro, sheiro zu ishto*, and permitted the *kohen* to become *tamei* for his wife. The Rambam holds that this too is rabbinic, grounded in the category of *meis mitzvah*: since the husband alone bears the obligation to bury his wife, he is treated like a *meis mitzvah* case and permitted to become *tamei*. Throughout, the *gemara* here speaks in the register of *d'raita*, but the force of the Rambam's observation should be kept in mind as we watch the *gemara* struggle to sustain any of these derivations.

## Pesukimim Challenge

### Mah V'omer

The *gemara* now turns to scrutinize Rebbi Yishmael's entire chain of *pesukim* by asking *mah v'omer*: why were multiple *pesukim* needed at all? Beginning with the *pasuk* cited by Rebbi Yishmael — *v'chol bat yoreshet nachala mimatot bnei Yisrael* — the *gemara* notes that the *Torah*'s instruction that the generation of Bnot Tzelofchad marry within the *shevet* so that land not be diverted, while suggestive, is not a clean proof that the husband inherits. The concern about tribal diversion could be explained entirely by the son's inheritance: the son follows his father's *shevet*, and if the husband is from a different *shevet*, so is the son. The husband's own inheritance right would not be necessary to explain this concern.

### B'sibat Haven

The *gemara* therefore raises the objection directly: *v'chi teima b'sibat haben ko kapid kra, aval ba'al lo yarit* — perhaps the entire concern of this *pasuk* is that the inheritance will be diverted through the son, and the husband simply does not inherit at all. That objection forces the *gemara* to seek a different *pasuk*.

### Lo Tisov Nachala

The *gemara* brings *ta shma*: the *pasuk* *v'lo tisov nachala livnei Yisrael mimateh el mateh* — that the inheritance shall not be diverted from one tribe to another. The argument is that since we already knew the concern about tribal diversion from the previous *pasuk*, this additional *pasuk* must come to teach a new mode of diversion — not through the son but through the husband, who actually takes precedence over the son as an heir. The *gemara*, however, deflects: *v'chi teima la'avor alav b'lav v'asei* — perhaps this *pasuk* is not introducing a new halachic mechanism at all, but is simply adding a negative prohibition to the positive commandment already established. The generation of Bnot Tzelofchad was warned: if a woman who inherited land marries out of her tribe, she will transgress the positive command — *v'chol bat yoreshet nachala mimatot avoteiha tihyeh le'ishah* — and she will transgress the negative prohibition — *velo tisov* — because she will have caused the inheritance to be diverted. The *pesukim* thus may be about layering prohibitions, not about establishing the husband's inheritance right. Accordingly, the *gemara* introduces yet another *pasuk: velo tisov nachalah mimateh lemateh acher* — that the inheritance shall not go to a different tribe. Once again the inference is that this additional language must be teaching a new mode of diversion.

The *gemara* pushes back yet again: *v'chi teima lav velav bishnei lavin v'aseh* — perhaps this is still only about the son, and we are merely establishing that this single concern — diversion through the son — now carries the weight of two negative prohibitions and a positive commandment, not just one.

### Tosafot Extra Pesuk

Tosafot register a pointed observation here: normally, when a *pasuk* appears to be duplicating something already stated, we prefer to read the extra *pasuk* as teaching something substantively new rather than as simply stacking additional *lavin*. The expectation would therefore be that the doubled language of *lo tisov nachala* is coming to introduce a new case — namely, diversion through the husband — rather than to merely multiply the prohibitions surrounding diversion through the son. It is therefore somewhat surprising that the *gemara* does not deploy this logic here and instead continues to entertain the possibility that both *pesukim* address only the son. The resolution to this difficulty will come later, on *amud bet*, where the *gemara* does in fact insist that the doubling teaches two separate cases: one concerning the son and one concerning the husband.

## Elazar & Pinchas

### Elazar Pinchas

Since the *pesukim* from Bnot Tzelofchad have proven insufficient to cleanly establish the husband's inheritance right, the *gemara* turns to *Nach*. The *pasuk* reports: *Ve'Elazar ben Aharon met* — Elazar the son of Aharon died — and states that he was buried in a valley belonging to Pinchas his son. The *gemara* is puzzled: how did Pinchas come to possess this property? If he did not inherit it from his father Elazar, and yet the valley bears his name, the inference is that he must have acquired it through his wife — that is, Pinchas's wife died and Pinchas inherited her estate.

### Wife vs Mother

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *v'chi teima Elazar hu denasiv iteta umeta veyartah Pinchas* — perhaps it was not Pinchas who married and inherited from his wife, but rather Elazar who married a woman, she died, and *she* passed the property to *her son* Pinchas. In other words, the property in question may have come to Pinchas through his mother rather than through his own wife. The *gemara* therefore looks for a second proof.

### Segov & Yair

The *gemara* brings *ta shma*: the *pasuk* tells us that Segov fathered Yair, and that Yair possessed extensive property in the land of Gilad. The question is the same: how did Yair come to hold property his father Segov apparently did not have? By the same reasoning, he must have inherited it from his wife. The *gemara* argues that the existence of two parallel cases — Pinchas and Yair — eliminates the possibility that either is simply an incidental story about property acquisition. Since both situations describe a son possessing what his father did not, and they cannot both be explained away, the *gemara* concludes that the *Torah* is teaching a *halacha*: one can acquire property through a deceased wife.

One might ask why two cases are needed. The *gemara*'s logic is *im ken terei karai lamah li* — if both are just incidental stories, there would be no reason for the *Torah* to mention both. The duplication implies that the *Torah* is establishing a rule. As the Rashbam notes, however, this reasoning is strained: there is no inherent reason to assume these narratives are halachic rather than historiographical. The stories of Pinchas and Yair and Segov are simply telling us what happened. Nothing in the text compels us to read them as conveying a legal principle, and the mere fact that the *Torah* tells two such stories does not automatically transform them into a redundancy that must be teaching a *halacha*.

### Rav Pappa Objection

Rav Pappa poses this challenge directly to Abaye. *Mimay* — who says Yair inherited through his wife? *Dilma le'olam eima lach ba'al lo yarit* — perhaps a husband never inherits at all — *v'karai besibat haben k'deshninan* — and the earlier *pesukim* about Bnot Tzelofchad are entirely about diversion through the son. As for Yair: *Yair d'zavin mazbin* — perhaps he simply purchased the property. And similarly for Pinchas: *uPinchas nami d'zavin mazbin* — perhaps Pinchas too merely purchased his holdings. There is no need to invoke inheritance from a wife at all. The Rashbam adds further that in both cases one could equally have said the sons received the property from their mothers — no *halacha* need be inferred; the *pesukim* may simply be recording facts about individual circumstances.

### Abaye Defense

Abaye responds to Rav Pappa's objection. He acknowledges that it is a strong challenge to Rebbi Yishmael's position, but argues that at least in the case of Pinchas, the purchase theory cannot stand: *Pinchas d'zavin mazbin lo matzit amrat*.

### Yovel Property

The reason Abaye gives is rooted in the law of *yovel*: *ve'im ken nimtza sadeh chozeret biyovel venimtza kover b'kever she'eno shelo*. If Pinchas purchased the field from someone else and then buried his father Elazar in that land, the field would revert to its original owner at the *yovel*, and Elazar HaKohen would be interred in a burial site belonging to another person — a valley that would no longer belong to the family of Pinchas. That is untenable. Accordingly, the field cannot have been a purchase. Yet this still does not establish that Pinchas inherited from his wife; it remains possible that he received the land from his mother.

### Cherem Alternative

Rav Pappa returns with a third alternative. Even granting that the field was not purchased, Pinchas could have acquired it through *cherem*: when a person dedicates a field as *cherem* and fails to redeem it, the field passes to the *kohanim* at the *yovel* — and crucially, property received through *cherem* would not itself revert in a subsequent *yovel*. So Pinchas, as a *kohen*, could have received the field through this mechanism, and the valley would remain his without reverting. The *gemara* thus leaves unresolved the question of how precisely one demonstrates from these narratives that the husband inherits from his wife according to Rebbi Yishmael. The discussion about the *pesukim* from *Nach* has effectively been neutralized, and the *gemara* signals that it will return to the doubling of *lo tisov nachala* on *amud bet* for the real resolution.

## Abaye: Two Shevat

### Two Shevatim Problem

Before arriving at that resolution, the *gemara* introduces a self-contained sugya built around a statement of Abaye — one the lecturer notes may seem somewhat wordy and repetitive on first encounter, but which the Rashbam reads as addressing a distinct question about the *pesukim* of *lo tisov nachala* rather than about the proof of the husband's inheritance. The scenario Abaye contemplates is one where a woman has inherited from *two* tribes: she has received property from her father, who belongs to, say, the tribe of Reuven, and also from her mother, who belongs to, say, the tribe of Shimon.

### Sof Sof Diverted

Abaye's concern is expressed in the phrase *sof sof ha ka mitakra nachala misheivta d'ima l'sheivta d'abba*: ultimately, the inheritance will be diverted from her mother's tribe to her father's tribe. The Torah commands this woman to marry within her father's tribe — within Reuven — so that the land she received from her Reuvenite father not be diverted away from Reuven. But what about the Shimon property she inherited from her mother? If she marries a man from Reuven, the Shimon property passes with her into Reuven hands. The Shimon inheritance has been diverted from Shimon to Reuven. Why is the Torah not equally troubled by this?

### Already Diverted

The *gemara* attempts an answer: *umimai v'dilma shani hatam shek'var husevah* — perhaps the Torah is not concerned about the Shimon property because, at the moment she inherited it from her mother, it was already diverted. She, the inheriting woman, belongs to the tribe of Reuven — that is her tribal identity — and when the Shimon property fell into her possession, it immediately moved into Reuven's sphere of control. The further diversion that will occur when she marries a Reuvenite man is not a new crossing of a tribal line; the diversion already happened the moment she received the inheritance. Accordingly, the Torah is focused exclusively on preserving the Reuven property, which is at risk of being diverted, and says nothing more about the Shimon property, which has already crossed tribal lines.

### Abaye Rejects

Abaye rejects this reasoning: *shek'var huspah lo amrinan*. He is not satisfied that the property's having been somewhat diverted already is sufficient to relieve the Torah of concern about its further diversion. It still troubles him that the Torah instructs her to marry from Reuven — which will result in the Shimon property becoming fully and definitively embedded in Reuven — without offering any remedy for the diversion of the mother's tribal inheritance.

### Same Mixed Heritage

Rav Yeimar then addresses Rav Ashi, and the *gemara* articulates what Abaye's implicit concern was pointing toward: if the approach of *shek'var husevah* — that already-diverted property no longer concerns the Torah — is rejected, then what possible solution could there be for a woman who has inherited from two tribes? If she marries someone from Reuven, the Shimon property is diverted; if she marries someone from Shimon, the Reuven property is diverted. There is no clean answer unless we posit a third option.

The *gemara* provides precisely that: *dimenasvinan lah legavra de-avuh mishevetah de-avuh ve-imeih mishevetah de-imeih* — perhaps she must marry a man who mirrors her exact dual lineage. She holds property from a Reuven father and a Shimon mother; she should marry a man whose own father is from Reuven and whose own mother is from Shimon. In such a marriage, she brings her dual inheritance to a husband who reflects the same composite identity.

### Property Preservation

The logic of this solution is intricate. When the Reuven property was in her hands, it was held by someone from the tribe of Reuven. After she dies and her husband inherits, the Reuven property remains in the hands of someone from the tribe of Reuven — his father is from Reuven. No diversion has occurred. As for the Shimon property: when it was in her hands, it was held by someone whose father was Reuven but whose mother was Shimon. After her husband inherits, the situation is exactly the same — the property is now held by someone whose father is Reuven and whose mother is Shimon. The Shimon property has not moved; it remains at precisely the same remove from its tribal origin as it was before. This is what Abaye was suggesting the Torah may have had in mind: that the requirement to marry within one's father's tribe carried with it, implicitly, a further refinement that the mother's tribe should also be matched — ensuring that each inherited property tracks back through the same composite lineage.

### Husband Inherits Proof

There is also a relevance here to our overarching discussion about whether the husband inherits. If we posit that the Torah is concerned about the Shimon property maintaining its level of connection to Shimon even after it passes to the husband — and if that concern makes sense — this works only on the assumption that the husband inherits. If only the son inherited, the son's father would be from Reuven and his mother would also be from Reuven (since she is the woman who inherited from her Reuven father), and the Shimon connection would be entirely lost. The fact that the Torah might be articulating a solution in which the property stays at the same Reuven-Shimon level thus implicitly assumes that the husband is the one inheriting, not the son.

### Father's Tribe Core

The *gemara* on *amud bet* raises an immediate objection to the proposed solution: if the Torah truly intended that the woman marry someone who mirrors both her father's and mother's tribal origins, why does the *pasuk* say only *mimateh aviha* — only from her father's tribe? Why is the mother not mentioned? The *gemara* answers: *ichtiv hachi hava amina afilu ipecha* — had the Torah written "from her father's and her mother's tribe," one might have supposed that the direction of the match does not matter. It would not matter whether the man's father is from Reuven and his mother from Shimon, or his father from Shimon and his mother from Reuven. But tribal identity is determined patrilineally. The core concern is that the father's tribe be the same, because that is what ultimately defines one's *shevet*. The Reuven property she holds, held as she is a member of the tribe of Reuven, must pass to someone who is also tribally Reuven — and that is determined by the father's line. The Torah therefore emphasizes *mimateh aviha*: the father's tribe is the essential anchor. That the mother's tribe should also match is an additional refinement, parenthetically serving the Shimon property — but since the primary obligation concerns the father's tribal identity, and since specifying only the father's tribe captures the essential rule, the Torah's formulation is appropriate. With this, Abaye's extended discussion concludes.

## Lo Tisov Doubled

### Two Beraitot

The *gemara* now returns to the central derivation of Rebbi Yishmael and resolves the impasse that had been left hanging throughout *amud alef*. The resolution, signaled earlier in passing, is that the doubled language of *lo tisov nachala* is not redundant and is not merely adding an additional *lav*. Rather, the two *pesukim* are addressing two distinct modes of tribal diversion — one through the son and one through the husband. The *gemara* presents two *beraitot*, each making the same fundamental point but starting from opposite directions.

### Son: Mimatteh Lematteh

The first *baraita* begins with the *pasuk* *v'lo tisov nachala livnei Yisrael mimateh el mateh* — it should not be diverted from one tribe to another. The *baraita* states: *besivah haben hakasuv medaber* — this *pasuk* is speaking about diversion through the son. One might ask whether this *pasuk* in fact concerns diversion through the son or diversion through the husband.

### Husband: Matteh Acher

The *baraita* resolves the question by invoking the companion *pasuk*: *keshehu omer v'lo tisov nachala mimateh el mateh acher* — when the *Torah* says it should not be diverted to a *different* tribe, *harei besivah habaal emur* — that one is clearly referring to diversion through the husband. The term *mateh acher* — a different tribe — captures the husband's situation precisely: he is from a tribe that is genuinely *other* relative to the wife's tribe. Once she has married him and he has become part of her world, her son does not come from a genuinely *acher* tribe in the same sense — the son is born into the merged reality of the household. The husband, however, comes from outside, from a tribe that was *acher* from the start. The first *pasuk*, without *acher*, therefore speaks of the son's inheritance, while the second, with *acher*, speaks of the husband's. Since the first *pasuk* has thus been assigned to the son, the *baraita* concludes that the second *pasuk* — which says *mateh acher* — is entirely about the husband: *harei besivah habaal emur*.

### Second Baraita

The second *baraita* covers the identical ground but reverses the order of presentation, opening with the *pasuk* that includes *acher*.

### Matteh Lematteh Son

Having assigned the *acher* formulation to the husband, the second *baraita* then turns to the *pasuk* without *acher* — *v'lo tisov nachala livnei Yisrael mimateh el mateh* — and confirms: *harei simas haben emur* — that is the one speaking about diversion through the son.

### Both Diversions

The conclusion is clear and symmetric: the two *pesukim* of *lo tisov nachala* together establish both modes of tribal diversion. The *pasuk* without *acher* — from one tribe to another — teaches that a woman's inheritance can be diverted through her son, who follows his father's tribe. The *pasuk* with *acher* — to a *different* tribe — teaches that her inheritance can be diverted through her husband, who is genuinely from another tribe. Together, they are what Rebbi Yishmael had been building toward all along: a *d'raita* basis for the principle that the husband inherits from his wife. The *gemara* will continue on 113a.
