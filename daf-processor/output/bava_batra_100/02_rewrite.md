# Bava Batra 100 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Road Swap & Proximity

### Landowner's Swap

The daf opens two lines from the top of 100a, picking up the case of a landowner through whose field a major thoroughfare passed. Wishing to reclaim that road for himself, he offered the public a different route. The *mishna* rules that the *rabbim* retain both roads — the original and the one he offered. The situation is reminiscent of *tmura*, the exchange of sanctified animals, where one attempts to substitute one animal for another and the result is that the first remains *kadosh* and the second becomes *kadosh* as well: the landowner loses on both counts. The obvious difficulty is that this seems internally inconsistent. Either the swap was valid, in which case the *rabbim* should accept the new road and surrender the old one, or it was invalid, in which case they should keep only the old one. Why should they have both?

The *gemara*'s answer is that the swap was fundamentally invalid from the outset, because it was made unilaterally, with no one representing the *rabbim*. Since no authorized party agreed on their behalf, there is reason to fear that whatever road the landowner provides will be less convenient, more circuitous, or simply out of the way.

### Rav Ashi's Principle

Rav Ashi sharpens this point with a sweeping principle: *kol min hatzad derech akalkalot hi* — any road that is off to the side, even a perfectly straight one, is by definition an indirect, out-of-the-way path. The reason is *karov lezeh verachok lezeh*: any shift in a road's location benefits those living on one side and disadvantages those living on the other. Since the swap was unilateral and no one represented all the affected parties, it is inherently invalid, regardless of how geometrically straight the new road may be.

### Why Both Roads?

This explanation accounts for why the *rabbim* retain their original road, but it raises a further question. Once we establish that the swap was invalid, why should the *rabbim* also keep the new road that the landowner offered them? The *gemara* poses this precisely: *lema lehu shkulu didchu vehavvei didi* — why cannot the landowner now tell the *rabbim* to keep their old road and return his field to him, since the entire exchange was void? This question drives the discussion forward.

## Rabbi Eliezer Lost Path

### Rabbi Eliezer Cited

The *gemara* answers: *ha meni Rabbi Eliezer hi* — our *mishna*, which awards the *rabbim* both roads, follows the position of Rabbi Eliezer. A *braita* is cited: Rabbi Yehuda said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer that *rabbim sheberu derech leatzmam* — if the masses simply chose a path for themselves through someone's field, *ma sheberu beru*, their selection stands and they may take possession of it. On its face this is a striking position. Does a community simply begin walking through someone's property and thereby acquire it?

### Communities as Robbers?

The *gemara* is immediately troubled: *Rabbi Eliezer rabbanan gazlanim ninhu?* — according to Rabbi Eliezer, are the masses simply robbers? On what basis could a community seize a path through private property, and on what basis would such a seizure be legitimated?

### Rav Gidel's Lost Path

Rav Gidel said in the name of Rav that the case must be understood carefully — and his reading illuminates the precise language *beru* (selected). The scenario is not that the *rabbim* arbitrarily began cutting through someone's yard. Rather, the community had always possessed an established path through that field. At some point the path became lost — for instance, a rainy season rendered the ground muddy and swampy, and no one could any longer identify where the original road had been. Since the community retains a legal entitlement to *a* path through the property, they are now entitled to *select* a new one. The word *beru* thus means exactly what it says: you have a right to one path, and you are choosing among the available options. This is not seizure but selection among legitimate alternatives.

This reading also explains why the rule might apply differently to a *yachid* than to the *rabbim*. For an individual, one might invoke *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya*: the burden of proof falls on the claimant, and perhaps the landowner would get to designate which path the individual may use, rather than the individual choosing for himself. But the authority of the *rabbim* suffices to give them the prerogative of selection.

### Two Versions of Rav

However, this resolution creates a difficulty. If Rav himself explains Rabbi Eliezer as referring to a lost path and a lawful selection, then Rabbi Eliezer's position is entirely reasonable. Why, then, does another tradition record Rav bar Rav Huna in the name of Rav as ruling *ein halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer* — that we do not rule in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer?

The *gemara* resolves this by noting that there are two independent versions of Rav's position: *ma'an d'masni ha lo masni ha*. One version of Rav is precisely the reading of Rav Gidel — Rabbi Eliezer is addressing only the intelligible case of a lost path from which the community selects a replacement, and according to this version Rav rules in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer. The other version of Rav takes Rabbi Eliezer's statement at face value: that the *rabbim* can begin using any path through private property and thereby acquire it with no prior entitlement — a radical position. According to this second reading, Rav rules against Rabbi Eliezer. The two traditions are thus not in conflict; they simply reflect two different understandings of what Rabbi Eliezer said.

## Rav Yehuda Chazaka

### Better Explanation Needed

Having worked through the discussion of Rabbi Eliezer, the *gemara* circles back to ask: *v'ta'ama mai* — what is actually the underlying rationale of our *mishna*? The *mishna* deals with a case where the *rabbim* already have their original road intact; it is not a lost-path scenario. Tosafos notes that this is precisely why the *gemara* now asks for a better explanation: once it becomes clear that Rabbi Eliezer's ruling only makes sense in the lost-path scenario, it is no longer applicable to our *mishna*. The *gemara* therefore seeks a different basis for the ruling.

### Rav Yehuda's Seizure

The *gemara* answers: *mishum d'Rav Yehuda* — the rationale derives from the principle stated by Rav Yehuda: *meitzar she'hecheziku bo rabbim asur l'kalkelo* — a path in which the *rabbim* have established *chazaka*, taken possession, may no longer be destroyed. The *rabbim*, by having de facto seized rights to it, have made it theirs. Strikingly, this sounds very close to exactly what the *gemara* rejected when it objected to Rabbi Eliezer — *rabbim gazlanin ninhu?* If the *rabbim* simply start using a path and that suffices to make it theirs, are they not simply acting as robbers?

### Rashbam Tones Down

The Rashbam, without explicitly addressing this tension, attempts to mitigate it. In his commentary to the phrase *she'hecheziku bo rabbim*, the Rashbam explains: *she'hishvuhu v'tiknuhu l'hiluch* — the community did not merely walk through the property; they actively went in and improved the path, clearing debris, leveling the way. This is the classic form of *chazaka* — an act of improvement that manifests ownership. Furthermore, he adds: *v'haba'alim yadu v'shatku* — the owners saw what was being done and remained silent. When townspeople send workers to clear a path through your field, and you see them working and say nothing, that silence constitutes an implicit *mechila*, a waiver of your rights. The Rashbam then extends this point: *devadei lerabbim mechila* — even silent acquiescence that would not rise to the level of *mechila* vis-à-vis a single individual does constitute *mechila* when the party acting is the *rabbim*. The public nature of the act, combined with the owner's silence, is sufficient.

The Rashbam then applies this — though with some difficulty — to our *mishna*: *vechol sheken* — and certainly when the landowner actively handed over the road with his own hands, even though he did so under a misapprehension that a valid swap was occurring, that voluntary conveyance to the *rabbim* is interpreted in the *rabbim*'s favor as having been intended regardless. He concludes, however, that even under this analysis the *rabbim* would only keep the new road *ukegon shehechiziku* — where they actually took physical possession of it. A mere declaration or a failed swap would not suffice; there must be some act of de facto possession, whether actively improving the path or, as the *gemara* will now discuss, walking upon it.

There is something genuinely surprising about this conclusion. The *gemara* was offended by the idea that the *rabbim* could acquire a path simply by using it, yet ends up affirming something remarkably similar, now framed under Rav Yehuda's statement. The Rashbam's effort to tone it down — by introducing active improvement and owner complicity — softens the edge somewhat, but the basic structure remains: because it is the *rabbim*, we interpret the owner's conduct in their favor. Notably, this differs from the situation of an individual who acts under a misapprehension; here, because the owner did not explicitly articulate his assumptions, we do not treat the invalidity of those assumptions as sufficient to undo what he conveyed.

There is a real-world parallel here. Just as in certain legal systems a landowner who consistently allows others to use a path through his property may find that an easement arises — which is precisely why property owners post signs warning that trespassers will be prosecuted, making clear that any use is not consented to — so too the *gemara* is tracking the idea that unchallenged use, especially by a community, can ripen into legal entitlement. The *gemara* does not, however, fully specify the precise threshold: how long, how openly, how actively must the *rabbim* act before we assume the owner's acquiescence? That is left without complete resolution.

## Walking as Chazaka

### Walking Acquires

The *gemara* now turns to Rabbi Eliezer's underlying mechanism: once the *rabbim* have the right to select a new path in a lost-path case, how exactly do they take possession of it? The *gemara* answers: *behilucha* — by walking on it. The very act of walking through a space constitutes *chazaka* for a path. A *braita* is cited in support: *halach bah le'orka u'lerochba kana mekom hilucho divrei Rebbi Eliezer* — one who walked a field its length and its width acquires the place he walked. This is the same Rabbi Eliezer, now in a completely separate context: not the *rabbim* and not a lost path, but a straightforward case of one person buying a field from another, where the buyer walks the perimeter. Rabbi Eliezer holds that even though walking the perimeter is not a *kinyan* on the entire field, it is a *kinyan* on the area walked, because walking is itself an act of *chazaka*. The *chachamim*, by contrast, maintain that *ein hiluch mo'il klum ad she'yachazik* — walking alone accomplishes nothing; one must perform a classic act of improvement to effect *chazaka*.

The Rashbam notes that in the context of the *rabbim* seizing a path, he understands *chazaka* to mean they fixed it, as classic *chazaka* requires. But where Rabbi Eliezer uses only the language of selection (*beru*), the *gemara* infers that for Rabbi Eliezer even mere walking suffices.

### Abraham's Walking

Rav Elazar — the *amora*, not Rabbi Eliezer — is asked: *mai ta'ama deRebbi Eliezer* — what is Rabbi Eliezer's source? He answers from a verse: *kum hishalech ba'aretz le'orka u'lerochba ki lecha etnenah* — the command to Abraham to walk the land its length and its width, for it would be given to him. The implication is that Abraham's act of walking through the land was itself a form of taking possession. If so, walking is indeed a recognized act of *chazaka*.

The broader theme this invokes is *ma'aseh avot siman labanim*: the deeds of the patriarchs prefigure and, more than merely foreshadow, help bring about what will happen to their descendants. Abraham walks through the land, and that act lays some form of metaphysical groundwork making the eventual conquest more achievable for his children. In the case of a path, the principle applies with even greater directness: if walking the length and width of an entire field as a *chazaka* is derived from Abraham, then walking a path — whose very function is to be walked — is certainly an act of *chazaka*.

### Sages' Objection

The Sages respond to this proof: *hasam mishum chavivusa deAvraham hu* — there, God told Abraham to do this *kedei she'yei noach lichvosh lifnei banav*, so that the land should be easy for his descendants to conquer. In other words, Abraham's walking was not a standard legal act of *chazaka*; it was a special divine command motivated by God's love for Abraham and was directed toward facilitating the future conquest, not toward establishing a legal precedent for *hiluch* as *chazaka*. Therefore, one cannot derive a general rule from that episode.

The Rashbam's reading of this passage is particularly interesting. He explains the *chavivusa* dimension not merely in metaphysical terms — though the classic formulation of *ma'aseh avot siman labanim* would apply — but in terms of legal standing: *dehavu lehu kiyorshim velo kegazlanim, velo yehei reshus leSatan lekatreg, velo pis'chon peh leba'al middas hadin* — so that the Jewish people would relate to the land as heirs and not as robbers, giving no foothold to the Adversary to bring charges, and closing the mouth of the attribute of strict judgment. This reading carries considerable weight in contemporary political context: it is not simply that Abraham's walking made the conquest easier in some instrumental sense, but that it established the character of the relationship to the land — as rightful inheritance rather than seizure. The irony the Rashbam himself implicitly acknowledges is that if Abraham's walking was not a legally cognizable act of *chazaka*, it is somewhat paradoxical to describe it as conferring the status of *yorshim*. But the point seems to be that it functions symbolically and spiritually to create that status, even if not through the formal mechanism of *chazaka*.

### Vineyard Path Exception

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina introduces a mediating position: *modim chachamim l'Rabbi Eliezer b'shvil shel kramim* — the *chachamim* concede to Rabbi Eliezer that walking is a form of *chazaka* specifically for a narrow vineyard path. The reason is *ho'il v'na'aseh l'hiluch nikneh b'hiluch*: since the path's entire function is to be walked, the act of walking upon it is the appropriate and natural form of taking possession. The Rashbam notes that this connects to an earlier *gemara* principle: when something cannot be subjected to the classic forms of *chazaka* — just as the *gemara* earlier discussed hard rock, where planting and plowing are impossible — then using the thing in the way it is meant to be used constitutes *chazaka*. A path through a vineyard has no purpose other than to be walked; therefore, walking it is the definitive act of possession, even according to the *chachamim*. The implication is that for a path through a wheat field, since the field's primary character is agricultural and the path is secondary, walking alone would still not suffice for the *chachamim*. But for something that is unambiguously and exclusively a path, the concession holds.

## Path Width Standards

### Vineyard Path Width

When people came before Rabbi Yitzchak bar Ami with a dispute over a vineyard path, he ruled: *havu lei k'heicha d'dari tuna d'shavishta v'hadar* — give him the width of a person carrying a bundle of sticks on his back, with enough room to turn around. The Rashbam explains that this case concerns a vineyard path specifically, which may be narrower than the standard private path in the *mishna*. This ruling is then qualified: *lo amran ela d'mesaymi mechitza* — this width applies only where there are walls or defined enclosures on both sides. When there are tall walls, the width of the path must be sufficient for a person carrying a wide bundle to pass through; if the walls are too close, the person simply cannot get through despite the bundle extending beyond him. But if there are no walls — *ela d'shakil kara u'manach kara* — then an even narrower path suffices, just enough to place one foot in front of the other in single file, since the load protruding to the sides is unobstructed.

### Path Measurements

The *mishna* itself states: *derech hayachid arba amot* — a private path, sold by one individual to another, has a minimum width of four *amot*, approximately six to eight feet. A *baraita* records a dissenting view: *acheirim omrim k'dei sheyavor chamor b'masa'o* — others say it is the width sufficient for a donkey to pass with its load. Rav Huna rules: *halacha k'acheirim*. The *dayyanei gola* — the judges of the diaspora — give a measurement of two and a half *gmadim*, which appears to be a variant unit roughly equivalent to the *amah*, though the Rashbam notes uncertainty as to whether it represents a slightly larger or slightly smaller *amah*; the Rambam simply rules in standard *amot*. In any event, Rav Huna rules *halacha k'dayyanei gola* as well, and the *gemara* observes that the two rulings are consistent: *hai v'hai shiura chad hu* — two and a half *amot* is the width a donkey with its load requires.

For a *derech me'ir l'ir* — a road connecting two towns, used only by residents of those two towns to pass between them — the *baraita* prescribes eight *amot*. For a *derech harabim*, a true public road that will be used by anyone passing through the region, the standard is *shesh esrei ama* — sixteen *amot*, roughly twenty-four feet. One begins to appreciate why such roads cut through agricultural fields: towns were surrounded by farmland, and there was no other terrain through which to build them.

For roads leading to the *arei miklat*, the cities of refuge, the *pasuk* states *tachin lecha haderech* — you shall prepare the way — conveying a special obligation of accessibility. Accordingly, the prescribed width is *shloshim ushtayim amot* — thirty-two *amot*, approximately fifty feet, twice the standard public road. Rav Huna asks *mai kra* — what is the textual derivation? He reads it from the doubling: *derech haderech*, the extra *heh* of *haderech* being interpreted to indicate that this road requires double the standard measure. This width is comparable to a modern four-lane highway with a shoulder.

### King & Grave Roads

*Derech hamelech ein lo shiur* — a king's road has no limit. *Shehamelech poretz geder la'asot lo derech ve'ein mochin beyado* — the king may break through any fence or wall to make his road, and no one may prevent him. Reading this in the context of the *mishna* — which is discussing sales of paths — the Rambam understands this as addressing someone who has sold a path to the king. Since the king's road has no fixed measure, the sale cannot be specified, and this creates a problem of indeterminate subject matter. The Rambam does not himself state the conclusion, but the *nosei keilim* note that he would apparently rule the king gets nothing, since when a sale's subject matter cannot be identified there is nothing to convey — a somewhat extraordinary result. The Ra'avad, by contrast, understands it to mean the king himself sets the measure, which practically means one should never make such a deal without spelling out the terms precisely.

*Derech hakever ein lah shiur* — a grave path also has no limit. The Rambam and Ra'avad again read this in the context of a sale. Alternatively, it simply states a standalone *halacha*: people carrying a body have the right to cut through private property to reach the burial place, without limit, *mishum yikara deshichvi* — out of honor to the dead. The larger entourage, the greater the honor. Rabbeinu Gershom adds a further dimension: *zivluta deshichva v'ikara* — the path must also be wide enough so that those surrounding the body are not overwhelmed by its odor as it decomposes, a matter of basic dignity that requires additional space.

## Grave Rights & Ma'amad

### Grave Revocation

The *mishna* states: *hamocher kivro derech kivro mekom ma'amado beis hespedo* — one who sold his grave, or the path leading to it, or the place designated for *ma'amadot*, or the location set aside for *hespedim*. All of this property might be owned and maintained by a family in anticipation of eventual burial, and a person in financial straits might sell it. The *halacha* is that *bain bnei mishpacha vekovrin oso al korcho* — the family members come and bury their relative there by force, against the will of the buyer. The reason is *mishum pgam mishpacha* — because it would be a disgrace to the family for their member to have no proper burial place. The buyer is compensated, but the family's right takes precedence.

This ruling makes intuitive sense when the grave site is part of a larger family burial compound, where the whole extended family has been interred over generations. If one family member has sold his designated plot within the compound to a stranger, so that a stranger now occupies the space while the family member has no place among his kin, the *pgam mishpacha* is vivid and concrete. If the grave were simply an isolated plot with no family context, the reasoning would be less obvious — the family could take the proceeds and purchase a different grave. The *pgam mishpacha* logic is most powerful precisely because of the collective nature of the family burial site.

The *mishna* also introduces the space designated for *ma'amadot*, described as *sadeh beit peras hi beit arba kavin* — an area of four *kavin*. Since a standard *beit se'ah* is a space of fifty by fifty *amot* (2,500 square *amot*) and equals six *kavin*, four *kavin* represents two-thirds of a *beit se'ah*, approximately 1,666 square *amot* — something like forty by forty *amot*, or roughly sixty feet by sixty feet. That is a substantial area.

### Ma'amad Ritual

A *braita* defines the core ritual: *ein pochtin mishiva ma'amadot umoshavot lames* — one must not perform fewer than seven *ma'amadot* and *moshavot* for the deceased. The number seven is derived from *hevel havalim amar Kohelet hevel havalim hakol havel*: the word *hevel* appears five times in that verse from Kohelet, but two appearances are in the plural *havalim*, yielding a total count of seven. The ritual thus numbers seven standings and sittings.

The Rashbam describes the practice: *b'chazirasam min hakever holchin me'at v'yoshvin lenachem ha'avel* — upon returning from the grave, those performing the ritual would walk a short distance and then sit to console the mourner, then rise and walk again, and repeat this seven times. This has an obvious parallel in contemporary practice, where funeral processions pause seven times on the way to burial, though the *gemara* here clearly frames it as occurring on the return from the cemetery. David notes that Rashi in *Megillah* records a version where the practice was performed on the way to the burial rather than on the return, and the Rambam also situates it on the return; both are thus attested in the sources.

### Ma'amad Purposes

Three explanations are offered for this ritual. First, the Rashbam suggests it was a form of *nichum aveilim*, consoling the mourners. Second, it was meant *le-harbos tzar le-meis she-meis u-le-harbos be-vechi* — to multiply grief for the deceased and to multiply weeping, functioning as a form of ritualized lamentation, closer to the *hesped* tradition of wailing than to polite condolence. The *hesped* in classical form — *spod al yerech*, slapping the thigh and wailing — is a visceral, communal act of grief, not a celebration of life. Third, the Rashbam says: *v'nasna isha el libah el libah lashuv be-teshuva, ki chayei adam hevel* — it was meant to move those present to *teshuva*, recognizing the vanity of human life. The standing and sitting, repeated against the words of Kohelet, impresses upon participants that human life is fleeting and that repentance is called for.

The connection to *hevel havalim* could be read in a consoling direction — this world is not where ultimate meaning resides, and the deceased has passed to their true reward. The Rashbam, however, does not emphasize that register. As for the explanation involving *shedim*, demons that cling to those returning from a cemetery and for whom the repeated standing and sitting would provide an opportunity to depart: the Rashbam himself dismisses this: *v'lo nehira* — it is not convincing. If the purpose were to dislodge demons, the practice would be universal, whereas the *gemara* makes clear that it was only observed where there was an established *minhag* to do so.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Account

Rabbi Yehuda describes the actual conduct of the ritual in Judea: *be-rishona lo hayu pochtim mi-shiva ma'amados u-moshavos* — they never performed fewer than seven standings and sittings. The participants would be directed: *amdu yekarim, amdu — shivu yekarim, shevu* — honored ones, stand; honored ones, sit. This gave the signal for each movement, though it says nothing about what was said or done while in place. The *chachamim* pointed out that if the ritual consisted entirely of standing and sitting — nothing more — then it could equally well be performed on *Shabbat*, since standing and sitting involves no prohibited labor. The challenge for Rabbi Yehuda is to explain why *ma'amad u'moshav* is prohibited on *Shabbat*. Presumably the answer is that the ritual necessarily included wailing and formal lamentation, which are not appropriate on *Shabbat*, even though technical *nichum aveilim* would be permissible.

The *gemara* further addresses how the *Shabbat* scenario even arises, given that burial on *Shabbat* is prohibited. The answer is: *be-ir ha-smucha le-veis hakvaros de-mitu bein ha-shmashos* — the town was adjacent to the cemetery, the burial took place just before *Shabbat* began, and those returning found themselves in the cemetery context as *Shabbat* commenced. This also reinforces the understanding that the ritual occurred on the return from the cemetery, not on the way there.

### Rav Avya's Mistakes

The sister of Rami bar Papa was married to Rav Avya. When she died, Rav Avya performed a *ma'amad u'moshav* for her. Rav Yosef said: *ta ba-tartei* — he made two mistakes. First, *she-ein osim ela bi-krovim* — the *ma'amad u'moshav* is performed only by relatives of the deceased, and Rav Avya included non-family members, apparently friends who participated in the arrangements. Second, *she-ein osim ela be-yom rishon* — the practice is only for the day of burial itself, and Rav Avya performed it on the second day, the day after the burial. Abaye added a third mistake: *she-ein osim ela be-veis hakvaros* — it must be done in the context of the cemetery, and Rav Avya conducted it in the town itself, having waited until everyone had already returned. Rava added a fourth: *she-ein osim ela be-makom she-nahagu* — it is only practiced where there is an established local custom, and in that community there was no such custom. Rav Avya apparently attempted to institute the practice without understanding that all four conditions — relatives only, first day only, at the cemetery, and only where it is an established *minhag* — must be satisfied.

## Cave Burial Specs

### Cave Dimensions

The closing *mishna* of the daf addresses the physical specifications of a family burial cave. Whether one has sold a plot for use as such a cave or taken a contracting job to construct one, the standard dimensions are: *arba amot al shesh* — four *amot* by six *amot* — with eight niches (*kuchin*) opening into it: three along one wall, three along the opposite wall, and two facing the entrance. These burial caves were a feature of wealthy families; the archaeological record has preserved impressive examples with grand facades, columns, and elaborate entranceways, though such structures represent only a small fraction of those who died in antiquity. Ordinary burial was in the ground.

The Rashbam works out the geometry: each niche is one *amah* wide, with half an *amah* at each edge and one *amah* between adjacent niches. Along the six-*amah* wall accommodating three niches: three *amot* for the niches themselves, two for the spaces between them, and half at each edge accounts for the full six. Along the four-*amah* wall with two niches: the same logic applies, fitting neatly within four *amot*. The interior space of four by six *amot* is thus fully accounted for.

### Niche Measurements

The niches themselves are specified as follows: *orchan arba amot* — four *amot* in length, corresponding to the full height of a human body, approximately six feet. *Veruman shivah* — seven *tefachim* in height, which is one *amah* and one *tefach*, or roughly twenty-one inches. *Verachban shishah* — six *tefachim* wide, which is exactly one *amah*, or approximately eighteen inches.

These dimensions are the dimensions of a body. Four *amot* at six feet accommodates the average stature of the ancient world, somewhat shorter than today. Seven *tefachim* at roughly twenty-one inches reflects the height — or perhaps the depth — of the body as placed. The six-*tefachim* width of eighteen inches raises an immediate question: how does a body fit into a niche only eighteen inches wide, given that the average shoulder width is close to that or wider? One possibility is that the body was placed on its side. The fuller answer, however, emerges from understanding the actual burial practice: the body was initially laid in the cave on a shelf and allowed to decompose. Once decomposition was complete, the bones were gathered and transferred to the niches for longer-term storage. At the stage of secondary burial — with bones rather than a full body — the six-*tefachim* width would be adequate, particularly if the bones were arranged rather than left in skeletal alignment. This two-stage process of primary deposition followed by ossuary interment is consistent with what has been found archaeologically in the region, and it is the most coherent explanation of how the niche dimensions correspond to a human body. The question of the exact arrangement of the bones within the niche remains open, and whether they were placed in skeletal form or more compactly gathered is a matter that warrants further investigation.
