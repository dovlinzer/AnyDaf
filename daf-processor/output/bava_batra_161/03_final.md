# Bava Batra 161 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Get Mekushar Intro

### Get Forms Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה פָּשׁוּט. ״וְחָתוֹם״ – זֶה מְקוּשָּׁר. ״וְהָעֵד״ – שְׁנַיִם, ״עֵדִים״ – שְׁלֹשָׁה. הָא כֵּיצַד? שְׁנַיִם לְפָשׁוּט, שְׁלֹשָׁה לִמְקוּשָּׁר. וְאֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא! מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנִּתְרַבָּה בִּקְשָׁרָיו, נִתְרַבָּה בְּעֵדָיו. וְהָנֵי לְהָכִי הוּא דְּאָתוּ? כׇּל חַד וְחַד לְמִילְּתֵיהּ הוּא דַּאֲתָא – לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״שָׂדוֹת בַּכֶּסֶף יִקְנוּ, וְכָתוֹב בַּסֵּפֶר וְחָתוֹם״ – עֵצָה טוֹבָה קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. ״וָאֶקַּח אֶת סֵפֶר הַמִּקְנָה״ – הָכִי הֲוָה מַעֲשֶׂה. ״עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם עֵדִים אוֹ עַל פִּי שְׁלֹשָׁה עֵדִים״ – לְהַקִּישׁ שְׁלֹשָׁה לִשְׁנַיִם, בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְרַבָּנַן! אֶלָּא מְקוּשָּׁר מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּקְרָאֵי אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא. וְטַעְמָא מַאי תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן מְקוּשָּׁר? אַתְרָא דְכָהֲנֵי הֲווֹ, וַהֲווֹ קָפְדִי טוּבָא וּמְגָרְשִׁי נָשַׁיְיהוּ; וְעָבְדִי רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא, אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי מִיַּתְּבָא דַּעְתַּיְיהוּ. הָתִינַח גִּיטִּין, שְׁטָרוֹת מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּחַלֵּק בֵּין גִּיטִּין לִשְׁטָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **This** is referring to **an ordinary** document. When the verse states: **"And seal them," this** is referring to **a tied** document. The next phrase, "and call witnesses," is interpreted as: **"And have bear witness," this** indicates the need for **two** witnesses. As to the word **"witnesses," this** additional term indicates the need for **three** witnesses. **How so?** How can the verse call for both two witnesses and three witnesses? Rabbi Ḥanina explains: **Two** witnesses are required **for an ordinary** document, and **three** are required **for a tied** document. The Gemara questions this explanation: **But I** can just as well **reverse** it, requiring two witnesses for a tied document and three for an ordinary one. The Gemara answers: **Since** the tied document requires **more** to be done **with regard to its ties,** it stands to reason that it requires **more** to be done **with regard to its witnesses,** requiring three rather than two. The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that **these** verses **are coming for this** purpose, to teach that there are two types of documents? But **each and every one** of them **comes for its** own **purpose.** [The Gemara provides alternative sources.] **Rather,** the entire institution of the **tied** document is **rabbinic** in origin, **and** all these **verses** that were cited above by various *amora'im* were intended as **mere support** for the concept of a tied document, as opposed to actual sources. The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **the Sages instituted** the **tied** document? The Gemara explains: There was **a place where there were** many **priests, and they were very quick tempered, and they would** seek to **divorce their wives** impetuously. **And** therefore, **the Sages instituted an ordinance** that the bill of divorce for these people should be of the tied format, which is a long, drawn-out process, hoping that **meanwhile, their composure would be regained** and they would reconsider their decision to divorce. The Gemara asks: This **works out well** for **bills of divorce,** but **what can be said** with regard to other **documents?** Why is this procedure used for other documents as well? The Gemara answers: This was instituted **so that you should not differentiate between bills of divorce and** other **documents.**

### Tosafot: Mekushar Today

Having established the rationale for the *get mekushar*, Tosafot on 160b raises an obvious and pointed question: if this form was enacted specifically to protect *Kohanim* from hasty divorces, why is it no longer practiced today? No one in living memory has heard of a *Kohen* being required to use a knotted *get* when divorcing his wife.

### Tosafot Explanations

Tosafot offers three distinct answers. The first is that in principle the requirement remains in force, but since we are no longer sufficiently expert in how the *get mekushar* was written and executed — *mispaklan bizman hazeh* — it is better to use the standard *get pashut* that we know how to perform correctly than to attempt the knotted form and risk invalidating it.

The second answer is that perhaps the requirement was never universally binding; it may have applied only in that particular locale where *Kohanim* were especially prone to rash behavior, and was only an option elsewhere.

The third answer, attributed to *v'ri*, is the most conceptually interesting: *tiknu Rabbanan mekushar lo shehovah la'asot* — the Rabbis never actually required the *mekushar* form even in its original context. Rather, they created it as a legitimate form of *get* so that *Kohanim* who recognized their own impulsiveness could voluntarily opt into a process that would slow them down. It was a tool of self-restraint, not a compulsion. Once the form existed as an option, a *Kohen* who knew his own weaknesses — or a *sofer* advising him — could choose it for precisely that purpose.

## Signatures on Mekushar

### Rav Huna: Bein Kesher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָן עֵדִים חוֹתְמִין? רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: בֵּין קֶשֶׁר לְקֶשֶׁר. וְרַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: אֲחוֹרֵי הַכְּתָב – וּכְנֶגֶד הַכְּתָב מִבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Where do the witnesses sign** on a tied document? **Rav Huna says:** They sign **between each tied** fold. **And Rav Yirmeya bar Abba says:** They sign **on the back of the written** side, taking care that the signatures are exactly **opposite the writing, on the outside.**

The *gemara* turns to the technical question of where the witnesses' signatures are placed on a *get mekushar*. To understand the discussion, one must picture the physical structure of the document. The text is written across a parchment, which is then folded over and knotted repeatedly, producing a fan-like layered document. It should be noted that the Rashbam holds that the portion which gets folded over remains blank, while Tosafot maintains that writing appears on both the upper and lower portions before folding.

Rav Huna's position is that the signatures are placed *bein kesher l'kesher* — in the white space on the back of the document, between one knot and the next. According to this arrangement, the witnesses sign on the reverse side, each signature appearing in the exposed strip between consecutive knots.

### Adding After Signatures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא לְרַב חִסְדָּא: לְרַב הוּנָא דְּאָמַר בֵּין קֶשֶׁר לְקֶשֶׁר – קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין בֵּין קֶשֶׁר לְקֶשֶׁר מִגַּוַּאי – וְהָא הָהוּא מְקוּשָּׁר דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי, וְאָמַר רַבִּי: אֵין זְמַן בָּזֶה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי לְרַבִּי: שֶׁמָּא בֵּין קְשָׁרָיו מוּבְלָע? פַּלְיֵיהּ, וְחַזְיֵיהּ. וְאִם אִיתָא, ״אֵין זְמַן בָּזֶה וְאֵין עֵדִים בָּזֶה״ מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ בֵּין קֶשֶׁר לְקֶשֶׁר – מִגַּוַּאי? לָא, בֵּין קֶשֶׁר לְקֶשֶׁר – מֵאַבָּרַאי. וְנֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא זַיֵּיף וְכָתֵב מַאי דְּבָעֵי, וַחֲתִימִי סָהֲדִי! דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said to Rav Ḥisda: According to Rav Huna, who says** that the witnesses sign **between each tied** fold, it **enters our mind** that he meant **between each tied** fold **on the inside** of the document. **But** this is difficult, as there was **a certain tied** document **that came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, not realizing it was tied, **said: There is no date on this** document, so it is not valid. Then, **Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **said to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **Perhaps** the date is **hidden between the tied** folds. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **opened it and saw** that the date was in fact between the tied folds. **And if it is so** that the witnesses sign between each tied fold on the inside of the document, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **should have** had two objections, and said: **There is no date on this** document, **and there are** also **no witnesses** signed **on this** document. Rav Ḥisda **said to him: Do you maintain** that Rav Huna meant that the witnesses sign **between the tied** folds **on the inside? No,** he meant **between the tied** folds **on the outside** of the document. The Gemara questions Rav Huna's opinion: **But let** there be **a concern** that **perhaps** the party holding the document **will write whatever he wants** on the inside, adding to the text, **and** then **have extra witnesses sign on the outside. In** an ordinary document the witnesses sign immediately following the text, so there is no possibility of adding to the text. A tied document has part of its text written in the folds, but also has a part written on the face of the document on the unfolded paper, before or after the text in the folded part. If the witnesses sign between the folds there is the possibility of writing additional text in the unfolded section. The Gemara explains: The case is one **where it is written in** the document: Everything is **confirmed and established.** That is, every folded document must contain this formula at the end of the text, to prevent forgery, as any writing after this formula would be disregarded.

This immediately raises a concern: if the signatures appear on the back of the document, what prevents someone from turning to the front side and adding text below the original content after the document has already been signed? In a standard *get pashut*, the signatures appear directly beneath the text, leaving no room for subsequent additions. But here, since the signatures are on the reverse, the front of the document could in principle be extended.

### Hakol Sharir V'Kayam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא כָּתֵב מַאי דְּבָעֵי, וַהֲדַר כָּתֵב ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״ אַחֲרִינָא! חַד ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״ כָּתְבִינַן, תְּרֵי ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״ לָא כָּתְבִינַן. וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא מָחֵיק לֵיהּ לְ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״, וְכָתַב מַאי דְּבָעֵי, וַהֲדַר כָּתֵב ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״! הָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תְּלוּיָה; מְקוּיֶּימֶת – כְּשֵׁרָה, מְחָק – פָּסוּל, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמְּקוּיָּם. וְלֹא אָמְרוּ מְחָק פָּסוּל – אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״, וּכְשִׁיעוּר ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **But let** there be **a concern** that **perhaps** the holder of the document **wrote whatever he wanted and afterward wrote another** time: Everything is **confirmed and established.** The Gemara explains: **We write** only **one** declaration of: Everything is **confirmed and established; we do not write two** declarations of: Everything is **confirmed and established.** Therefore, anything written after the first declaration would be rejected, even if followed by a repetition of the declaration. The Gemara questions further: **But let** there be **a concern** that **perhaps** the holder of the document **erased** the declaration: Everything is **confirmed and established, and** then **wrote whatever he wanted** over the erasure, **and afterward wrote** the declaration: Everything is **confirmed and established.** The Gemara responds: How could this happen? **Doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** A document that includes **a suspended** correction of text inserted between lines of the document, which is **verified** at the end of the document, **is valid;** but a document with a reference to words written over **an erasure is not valid, even if it is verified** at the end of the document. At the end of a document, before the formula: Everything is confirmed and established, is written, any corrections made in the document are verified by adding to the text: On line so-and-so, such and such a word has been added, or some similar formulation. This may be done only for inserted corrections, not for erasures. The Gemara clarifies this statement: **And they said that an erasure** on a document renders the document **not valid only** if it is **in a place** on the document where the declaration: Everything is **confirmed and established,** should have been written, **and** only if the erasure **is the measure** of space in which the declaration: Everything is **confirmed and established,** can be written. The only concern with erasures is that the crucial formula: Everything is confirmed and established, might have been erased, as this would allow for unlimited forgery. If the erasure is such that this formula could not possibly have been erased, the document is valid.

The *gemara* answers that the phrase *hakol sharir v'kayam* — "all is confirmed and established" — serves precisely this function. This closing formula signals the definitive end of the document; nothing may be added beyond it. The *gemara* then considers a further challenge: what if someone erases *hakol sharir v'kayam*, adds fraudulent content, and rewrites the formula? To this the *gemara* responds that erasures near the end of a document are themselves inadmissible — any erasure in that region would be treated with suspicion. The combination of *hakol sharir v'kayam* and the rule against accepted erasures after that point effectively closes the document against tampering.

## K'Neged HaKtav Method

### Rebbi Yirmiya bar Abba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא, דְּאָמַר: אֲחוֹרֵי הַכְּתָב – וּכְנֶגֶד הַכְּתָב מִבַּחוּץ; לֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא כָּתֵיב מִגַּוַּאי מַאי דְּבָעֵי, וּמַחְתִּים סָהֲדֵי יַתִּירֵי מֵאַבָּרַאי, וְאָמַר: אֲנָא לְרַבּוֹת בְּעֵדִים הוּא דַּעֲבַדִי!
>
> **Translation:** § Rami bar Ḥama asked Rav Ḥisda: **And according to Rav Yirmeya bar Abba, who says** that the witnesses sign **on the back of the written** side, taking care that the signatures are exactly **opposite the writing, on the outside let** there be **a concern** that **perhaps** the party holding the document **will write whatever he wants on the inside,** i.e., the front of the document, adding to the text, **and** then **have extra witnesses sign on the outside, and** he will **say** to anyone questioning the number of witnesses being more than the minimum: **I did** this in order **to increase** the number of **witnesses,** the more to publicize the matters written in the document.

The *gemara* presents an alternative view: that of Rebbi Yirmiya bar Abba, who holds that the signatures on a *get mekushar* run *achorei haktav u'k'neged haktav mibachutz* — on the outside of the document, parallel to the writing on the inner side. On this approach, the signatures on side B are aligned horizontally with the corresponding lines of text on side A, so that the endpoint of the signatures naturally corresponds to the endpoint of the writing.

### Forging Signatures Risk

Under this method, the concern about adding text after the fact is largely resolved, since the signatures already run parallel to — and thus visually "cap off" — the full extent of the writing. However, a different concern arises: *leichosh dilma kativ miguai mai d'ba'ei u'machtim sahadei yiteiri milvar* — perhaps one could write additional content on the inside and then obtain extra signatures on the outside, making it appear as though those signatures were part of the original document. The fraudster might then claim, *ana l'ravcha b'eidim hu d'avdi* — "I simply wanted the extra security of additional witnesses."

### Tosafot: Three Witnesses

Tosafot addresses this concern directly. The *halacha* is that for a *shtar mekushar* requiring three signatures, one need only validate (*lekayem*) three of them — not all of them. This means that if a forger adds extra lines of text and procures additional signatures alongside them, he gains nothing, because the court will simply validate the original three genuine signatures and disregard the rest. The forged extra signatures, lacking validation, will not strengthen the fraudulent additions. This asymmetry between genuine and forged signatures effectively neutralizes the scheme.

### Bottom-Up Signing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ עֵדִים כְּסִדְרָן חֲתִימִי? עֵדִים מִמַּטָּה לְמַעְלָה חֲתִימִי. וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא מִתְרַמְיָא רֵיעוּתָה בְּשִׁיטָה אַחֲרוֹנָה, וְגָיֵיז לַיהּ לְשִׁיטָה אַחֲרוֹנָה – וְגָיֵיז לֵיהּ לִרְאוּבֵן בַּהֲדֵיהּ, וּמִתַּכְשַׁר בְּ״בֶן יַעֲקֹב עֵד״; דִּתְנַן: ״בֶּן אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי, עֵד״ – כָּשֵׁר! דִּכְתִיב ״רְאוּבֵן בֶּן״ בְּחַד דָּרָא, וְ״יַעֲקֹב עֵד״ עִלָּוֵויהּ. וְלִיחוֹשׁ דִּלְמָא גָּיֵיז לֵיהּ לִ״רְאוּבֵן בֶּן״, וּמִתַּכְשַׁר בְּ״יַעֲקֹב עֵד״ – דִּתְנַן: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי עֵד״, כָּשֵׁר! דְּלָא כְּתִיב ״עֵד״. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם דִּכְתִב ״עֵד״, דְּיָדְעִינַן בַּהּ דְּהָא חֲתִימוּת יְדָא –
>

This is not correct; rather, the **witnesses sign from bottom to top.** The witnesses sign in a perpendicular direction relative to the text of the document. The first signature begins at the reverse side of the last line of the document, and it continues upward toward the first line. There is therefore no possibility of adding to the text of the document, as, if the text extended beyond the beginning of the signatures, it would be recognized as a forgery. For instance, if the witness's name is Reuven, son of Ya'akov, he will excise **Reuven along with** the final line of the document, **and** the document **will be rendered valid with** the remaining part of the signature: **"Son of Ya'akov, witness." That is, a tied document is valid only if the witnesses sign in this manner. In this case, if the final line of the document is excised, all that will remain of the signature will be: "Ya'akov, witness." ### Horizontal Name Integrity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו דְּיַעֲקֹב הוּא. וְדִלְמָא בִּשְׁמֵיהּ דַּאֲבוּהּ חֲתַם! לָא שָׁבֵיק אִינִישׁ שְׁמֵיהּ דִּידֵיהּ, וְחָתֵים בִּשְׁמֵיהּ דַּאֲבוּהּ. וְדִלְמָא סִימָנָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּשַׁוְּויֵהּ! דְּהָא רַב צָיֵיר כְּווֹרָא, רַבִּי חֲנִינָא צָיֵיר חֲרוּתָא, רַב חִסְדָּא סָמֶךְ, רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא – עַיִן, רָבָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא – מָכוּתָא! לָא חֲצִיף אִינָשׁ לְשַׁוּוֹיֵהּ לִשְׁמֵיהּ דַּאֲבוּהּ סִימָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **is not Yaakov's** signature. That is, it is known for a fact that there is no one living in the city where the document was written who is named Ya'akov and whose signature matches the signature on this document. Therefore, the court will recognize that the signature must have originally stated: "So-and-so, son of Ya'akov, witness," and that the last line had been excised, and they will invalidate it. Rami bar Ḥama challenges: **But perhaps** this witness **signed using the name of his father** instead of his own name, as a gesture of respect toward his father. The Gemara answers: This is not done; **a person does not discard his own name and sign using** only **the name of his father.** Rami bar Ḥama challenges further: **But perhaps** the witness **made** this name **into a mere distinguishing mark** that he uses as his signature, **as** it is known that **Rav** used to **draw a fish** as his signature mark, rather than signing his name, and **Rabbi Ḥanina** used to **draw a palm branch** as his signature mark, and **Rav Ḥisda** used to sign just the letter ***samekh*,** and **Rav Hoshaya** used to sign just the letter ***ayin*,** and **Rava bar Rav Huna** used to sign his name by drawing a ship's **mast [*makhota*].** The Gemara answers: **A person is not** so **insolent** as **to use his father's name as a distinguishing mark.**

The *gemara* raises a related concern: *leichosh dilma misramia reiusa b'shita achrona* — perhaps a damaging clause appears in the last line of the document. The interested party might then simply cut off that last line. If the witnesses have signed lengthwise, cutting away a line from the bottom of the text might also sever part of a signature — say, removing the word *Reuven ben*, leaving only *Yaakov eid*. Since *ben ish ploni eid* is a valid signature according to the *Mishna*, this truncated signature might still be accepted, and the fraudulent cut would go undetected.

The *gemara*'s response is that *Reuven ben* is written together in one column and *Yaakov eid* in the adjacent column, so that cutting away *Reuven ben* necessarily leaves the signature beginning with *Yaakov eid* — but the court would recognize that this is not Yaakov's signature at all; it is Reuven's handwriting. The *kiyum shtarot* process, which validates signatures by comparing them to known exemplars, would reveal the discrepancy.

The *gemara* pushes further: perhaps the witness himself signs using his father's name as a kind of symbol or family crest? After all, the *gemara* records that Rav would draw a fish as his signature, Rabbi Chanina would draw a palm tree, Rav Chisda would write the letter *samech*, Rav Hoshaiah an *ayin*, and Rabba bar Rav Huna a ship's mast. If rabbis could sign with such symbolic marks, perhaps a witness might use his father's name as his symbol? The *gemara* dismisses this: *lo khatzif inish v'shavi l'shmei d'avuh simana* — a person does not have the audacity to adopt his father's name as his own signature symbol. The social and familial inhibition is a sufficient safeguard.

### Mar Zutra: Justification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר זוּטְרָא אָמַר: לְמָה לָךְ כּוּלֵּי הַאי? כׇּל מְקוּשָּׁר שֶׁאֵין עֵדָיו כָּלִין בְּשִׁיטָה אַחַת – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra said: Why do you need all this?** Why go to such lengths to answer the question posed above? There is a simpler answer: **Any tied** document **whose witnesses do not end on a single line** is **not valid.** The Gemara had previously assumed that the witnesses sign one after the other, beginning at the document's bottom line and going upward toward the first line; this arrangement leaves open the possibility that the signature of the first witness could be truncated by an unscrupulous party. Mar Zutra explains that this is not so; rather, the signatures are written with each one beginning opposite the bottom line and heading upward toward the beginning of the document. Therefore, if a line of text is excised from the bottom of the document, the names of all the witnesses on the reverse side will be truncated, and the forgery will become apparent.

Having worked through a series of increasingly technical answers, Mar Zutra cuts through all of them with an elegant and straightforward solution: *mekushar she'ein yadav kalin b'shita achat pasul* — a *mekushar* document in which the signatures do not all conclude on the same line is invalid.

What this means, according to the Rashbam and Rabbeinu Gershom, is that all signatures must be fully justified — spread out so that each one begins and ends at precisely the same left and right margins. When names of different lengths are fully justified in this way, the spacing within each name will differ. Any fraudulent cut would therefore have to pass cleanly through all the signatures simultaneously, but since the internal letter spacing varies from name to name, a cut that aligns perfectly with one name will necessarily pass through the middle of letters in another. The irregularity makes the tampering immediately visible. This answer is so clean and obvious that one wonders why the *gemara* did not begin with it.

The sustained concern threading through this entire discussion is a single underlying problem: how does one prevent alterations to a document after it has been duly signed? The *gemara* has addressed this from multiple angles — the role of *hakol sharir v'kayam* in marking the document's terminus, the illegitimacy of erasures near the end, the physical arrangement of signatures, and the structural requirement that signatures be fully justified.

## Corrections & Erasures

### Listing Corrections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַמְּחָקִין כּוּלָּן, צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב ״וְדֵין קִיּוּמֵיהוֹן״. וְצָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּחֲזוֹר מֵעִנְיָנוֹ שֶׁל שְׁטָר בְּשִׁיטָה אַחֲרוֹנָה; מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** There are two *halakhot* with regard to documents: For **any erasures** in a document, the scribe **must write** at the end of the document: **And this is their verification.** That is, he must list the erasures, stating that on line so-and-so there is an erasure and a correction stating such and such, for each erasure. **And** the second *halakha* is that the scribe **must review** some **of the details of the document in the final line** of the document. **What is the reason** for this second requirement?

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosef said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol hamichakim kulam tzarich sheyichtov v'den kiyumeihon* — all erasures in a document must be enumerated and confirmed at the document's end. The question of whether the phrase *v'den kiyumeihon* — "and these are their validations" — must appear explicitly as a heading, or whether one need only list the corrections without that introductory phrase, is disputed. The version without *v'den kiyumeihon* is the practice endorsed by both Tosafot and the Rashbam: rather than a formal heading, one writes the substance of the corrections directly, noting in which line a *machak* occurred, whether a word was suspended (*tluya*), and so forth. This list appears before *hakol sharir v'kayam*, which then closes the document entirely.

Since no erasures are permitted after *hakol sharir v'kayam* has been written, and since that phrase itself signals the absolute terminus of the document, the listing of corrections before it carries full legal force and cannot be tampered with thereafter.

### Last Line Summary

The second requirement established by Rabbi Yochanan is *v'tzarich she'yachzor me'inyano shel shtar b'shita achrona* — the last line of the document must contain a recapitulation of the document's main substance. This does not mean rewriting the entire *shtar* in miniature. According to the Rashbam, the operative formula is something like *v'kanina min* so-and-so *al kol ma d'chtiv u'meforash le'eil u'mana de'kashrana le'makanya bei* — "we acquired from him regarding everything written and specified above, with an instrument fit for acquisition." This refers back to the whole of the *shtar* without restating it in full.

The rationale for this requirement becomes clear when one considers that witnesses, when signing, will inevitably leave some white space between the end of the text and their signatures. That gap creates an opportunity for fraud — a bad actor could insert an additional clause in that space. The solution is to fill the last substantive line with a formulaic summary that (a) leaves no ambiguity about where the document's content ends, and (b) is sufficiently standardized that any deviation from the expected boilerplate would be immediately apparent.

## Ein Lamedim Rule

### No Learning from Last

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין לְמֵדִין מִשִּׁיטָה אַחֲרוֹנָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְרַב עַמְרָם: מְנָא לָךְ הָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: הִרְחִיק אֶת הָעֵדִים שְׁנֵי שִׁיטִין מִן הַכְּתָב – פָּסוּל, שִׁיטָה אַחַת – כָּשֵׁר. מַאי שְׁנָא שְׁנֵי שִׁיטִין – דִּלְמָא מְזַיֵּיף וְכָתֵב; שִׁיטָה אַחַת נָמֵי, מְזַיֵּיף וְכָתַב! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֵין לְמֵדִין מִשִּׁיטָה אַחֲרוֹנָה? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram says:** It is **because** one **may not learn** any new details **from** the **final line** of a document. There is a concern that the holder of the document may have written in an extra line of text to his advantage, between the text and the witnesses' signatures. Therefore, any new information contained in the last line is disregarded. Since the final line is disregarded, it is necessary that the final line of a document contain only a review of what is already written in it. **Rav Naḥman said to Rav Amram: From where do you** know **this?** Rav Amram **said to him: As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 11:1): If one writing a document **distanced the witnesses'** signatures **two lines from the text** of the document, leaving two lines blank, the document is **not valid,** as it would be possible for the holder of the document to fill in false information in those lines. But if one distanced the signatures **one line** from the text, the document is **valid.** Rav Amram analyzes the *baraita*: **What is different** in the case of **two** blank **lines** that the document is not valid? If you say that **perhaps** the holder of the document will take advantage of those two blank lines, and **forge** unverified information **and write** it in those lines, then in the case of **one line** left blank **as well,** he can **forge** information **and write** it in that line. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** the *baraita* that one **may not learn** any new details **from** the **final line** of a document? Therefore, only when two lines are left blank is there a concern that false information would be added to the penultimate line. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** this *baraita* that it is so.

This requirement leads directly to the principle articulated by Rav Amram: *lefi she'ein lamedim mishita achrona* — no legally operative content may be derived from the last line of a document. The last line exists solely to close and summarize; it is structurally boilerplate. If someone manages to insert a substantive clause there — whether fraudulently or inadvertently — it carries no legal weight.

There is a dispute among the *rishonim* about how to understand this rule. The Rashbam holds that it is purely advisory: it instructs the drafter not to place anything of legal consequence in the last line, since the court will disregard it regardless. On this reading, the rule is about drafting practice — a warning, not a formal prohibition. Tosafot, drawing on other *Gemarot*, understands the requirement more stringently, suggesting that there is an affirmative obligation to include a substantive summary in that final line — and that the *ein lamedim* rule is then the logical corollary, since that line is inherently suspect as a potential site of tampering. The practical upshot is the same: the last line before the signatures must be standardized summary language, and nothing written there can serve as a source for independent legal inference.

### White Space Rule

The *gemara* raises a related concern: *leichosh dilma misramia reiusa b'shita achrona* — perhaps a damaging clause appears in the last line of the document. The *gemara*'s response is that erasures near the end are themselves inadmissible. The rule about white space and the principle of *ein lamedim mishita achrona* thus prove and reinforce each other.

## Modern Practice Explained

### Tosafot Observation

A substantial Tosafot at the end of the *sugya* confronts what is, on the face of it, a glaring discrepancy between the *gemara*'s framework and contemporary practice. The *gemara* operated in a world where *hakol sharir v'kayam* was not a standard feature of all documents — indeed, from the earlier discussion about the *mekushar*, one infers that it was the exception. Precisely because most *shtarot* lacked that closing formula, the entire architecture of the *sugya* — the prohibition on white space, the requirement that the last line be boilerplate, the rule of *ein lamedim mishita achrona* — was necessary to protect document integrity.

### Hakol Sharir Solves It

Tosafot observes: *b'yameihem lo haya katuv sharir v'kayam* — in their time, *sharir v'kayam* was not written in *peshutim*. It was precisely this absence that made all the protective measures necessary. But today, *sharir v'kayam* has become not merely conventional but obligatory — a *shtar* lacking it would be *pasul*. Once that phrase is universally required and its absence invalidates the document, all the concerns driving the *gemara*'s rulings dissolve. *Hakol sharir v'kayam* definitively seals the document; nothing written after it carries any force, and no erasure after that point is accepted. The fraudster has no opening.

### Corrections Before Final

This resolution also explains our practice regarding the placement of *kiyum machakim*. The *gemara* as analyzed by Tosafot would seem to require that corrections be validated before the summarizing last line — *kodem shita achrona* — so that nothing substantive appears after that boilerplate. Yet contemporary scribal practice places the *kiyum machakim* after the *v'kanina* formula, making the validation of corrections the last substantive element before the signatures, not something preceding the summary line.

Tosafot frames this as a genuine difficulty: *v'yeish litmoha — eich hurgalnu lichtov kiyum machakim achar v'kanina minei* — how have we become accustomed to writing the validation of corrections after the *v'kanina* formula? There is nothing more substantive than confirming erasures, and yet we place it precisely where the *gemara* says nothing of substance should appear. Tosafot's answer is that this concern evaporates entirely once *hakol sharir v'kayam* is universally mandated. Since the phrase is now required in every *shtar*, and since its presence absolutely bars any subsequent addition, the entire rationale for *ein lamedim mishita achrona* no longer applies. We can and do learn from the last line; we can place substantive content — including *kiyum machakim* — there, because *hakol sharir v'kayam* following it ensures the document is airtight. The *gemara*'s protective architecture was built for a world without that formula; in a world where it is obligatory, the rules governing the last line shift accordingly.
