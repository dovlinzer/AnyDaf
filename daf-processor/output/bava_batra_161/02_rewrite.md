# Bava Batra 161 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Get Mekushar Intro

### Get Forms Review

The final *perek* of Bava Batra opens with a discussion of two fundamental forms of legal documents: the *get pashut*, a standard flat *shtar*, and the *get mekushar*, a document that is folded and knotted. The *gemara* explains that the *get mekushar* was originally enacted for *Kohanim*, who were prone to divorcing their wives in a sudden burst of anger and then regretting it — since once a *Kohen* divorces his wife, he cannot remarry her. The elaborate knotting process was designed to slow them down and give them time to reconsider. Over time, this form was extended as an option for writing general *shtarot*, though the precise reason anyone would prefer this more cumbersome format is not entirely clear — perhaps it lent greater weight or formality to the document.

### Tosafot: Mekushar Today

Having established the rationale for the *get mekushar*, Tosafot on 160b raises an obvious and pointed question: if this form was enacted specifically to protect *Kohanim* from hasty divorces, why is it no longer practiced today? No one in living memory has heard of a *Kohen* being required to use a knotted *get* when divorcing his wife.

### Tosafot Explanations

Tosafot offers three distinct answers. The first is that in principle the requirement remains in force, but since we are no longer sufficiently expert in how the *get mekushar* was written and executed — *mispaklan bizman hazeh* — it is better to use the standard *get pashut* that we know how to perform correctly than to attempt the knotted form and risk invalidating it.

The second answer is that perhaps the requirement was never universally binding; it may have applied only in that particular locale where *Kohanim* were especially prone to rash behavior, and was only an option elsewhere.

The third answer, attributed to *v'ri*, is the most conceptually interesting: *tiknu Rabbanan mekushar lo shehovah la'asot* — the Rabbis never actually required the *mekushar* form even in its original context. Rather, they created it as a legitimate form of *get* so that *Kohanim* who recognized their own impulsiveness could voluntarily opt into a process that would slow them down. It was a tool of self-restraint, not a compulsion. Once the form existed as an option, a *Kohen* who knew his own weaknesses — or a *sofer* advising him — could choose it for precisely that purpose.

## Signatures on Mekushar

### Rav Huna: Bein Kesher

The *gemara* turns to the technical question of where the witnesses' signatures are placed on a *get mekushar*. To understand the discussion, one must picture the physical structure of the document. The text is written across a parchment, which is then folded over and knotted repeatedly, producing a fan-like layered document. It should be noted that the Rashbam holds that the portion which gets folded over remains blank, while Tosafot maintains that writing appears on both the upper and lower portions before folding.

Rav Huna's position is that the signatures are placed *bein kesher l'kesher* — in the white space on the back of the document, between one knot and the next. According to this arrangement, the witnesses sign on the reverse side, each signature appearing in the exposed strip between consecutive knots.

### Adding After Signatures

This immediately raises a concern: if the signatures appear on the back of the document, what prevents someone from turning to the front side and adding text below the original content after the document has already been signed? In a standard *get pashut*, the signatures appear directly beneath the text, leaving no room for subsequent additions. But here, since the signatures are on the reverse, the front of the document could in principle be extended.

### Hakol Sharir V'Kayam

The *gemara* answers that the phrase *hakol sharir v'kayam* — "all is confirmed and established" — serves precisely this function. This closing formula signals the definitive end of the document; nothing may be added beyond it. The *gemara* then considers a further challenge: what if someone erases *hakol sharir v'kayam*, adds fraudulent content, and rewrites the formula? To this the *gemara* responds that erasures near the end of a document are themselves inadmissible — any erasure in that region would be treated with suspicion. The combination of *hakol sharir v'kayam* and the rule against accepted erasures after that point effectively closes the document against tampering.

## K'Neged HaKtav Method

### Rebbi Yirmiya bar Abba

The *gemara* presents an alternative view: that of Rebbi Yirmiya bar Abba, who holds that the signatures on a *get mekushar* run *achorei haktav u'k'neged haktav mibachutz* — on the outside of the document, parallel to the writing on the inner side. On this approach, the signatures on side B are aligned horizontally with the corresponding lines of text on side A, so that the endpoint of the signatures naturally corresponds to the endpoint of the writing.

### Forging Signatures Risk

Under this method, the concern about adding text after the fact is largely resolved, since the signatures already run parallel to — and thus visually "cap off" — the full extent of the writing. However, a different concern arises: *leichosh dilma kativ miguai mai d'ba'ei u'machtim sahadei yiteiri milvar* — perhaps one could write additional content on the inside and then obtain extra signatures on the outside, making it appear as though those signatures were part of the original document. The fraudster might then claim, *ana l'ravcha b'eidim hu d'avdi* — "I simply wanted the extra security of additional witnesses."

### Tosafot: Three Witnesses

Tosafot addresses this concern directly. The *halacha* is that for a *shtar mekushar* requiring three signatures, one need only validate (*lekayem*) three of them — not all of them. This means that if a forger adds extra lines of text and procures additional signatures alongside them, he gains nothing, because the court will simply validate the original three genuine signatures and disregard the rest. The forged extra signatures, lacking validation, will not strengthen the fraudulent additions. This asymmetry between genuine and forged signatures effectively neutralizes the scheme.

## Witness Signature Direct…

### Bottom-Up Signing

The *gemara* continues by introducing another protective mechanism: *eidim milmata l'mala chatmi* — the witnesses sign from the bottom upward. Based on the *Yerushalmi*, as understood by Tosafot and the Rashbam, this means the witnesses sign *lengthwise* — that is, each witness writes his name vertically along the side of the document rather than horizontally across it. If signatures run lengthwise in this fashion, there is no way to append an additional line of text below them, since any addition would have to cut across the vertical signature lines and would be immediately apparent.

Rabbeinu Gershom understands the phrase differently — that witnesses begin signing from the bottom line rather than the top — but this interpretation is difficult to sustain, since it would still leave the possibility of adding one more line below the last signature. The more convincing reading is the lengthwise interpretation, which physically forecloses any subsequent addition.

### Horizontal Name Integrity

The *gemara* raises a related concern: *leichosh dilma misramia reiusa b'shita achrona* — perhaps a damaging clause appears in the last line of the document. The interested party might then simply cut off that last line. If the witnesses have signed lengthwise, cutting away a line from the bottom of the text might also sever part of a signature — say, removing the word *Reuven ben*, leaving only *Yaakov eid*. Since *ben ish ploni eid* is a valid signature according to the *Mishna*, this truncated signature might still be accepted, and the fraudulent cut would go undetected.

The *gemara*'s response is that *Reuven ben* is written together in one column and *Yaakov eid* in the adjacent column, so that cutting away *Reuven ben* necessarily leaves the signature beginning with *Yaakov eid* — but the court would recognize that this is not Yaakov's signature at all; it is Reuven's handwriting. The *kiyum shtarot* process, which validates signatures by comparing them to known exemplars, would reveal the discrepancy.

The *gemara* pushes further: perhaps the witness himself signs using his father's name as a kind of symbol or family crest? After all, the *gemara* records that Rav would draw a fish as his signature, Rabbi Chanina would draw a palm tree, Rav Chisda would write the letter *samech*, Rav Hoshaiah an *ayin*, and Rabba bar Rav Huna a ship's mast. If rabbis could sign with such symbolic marks, perhaps a witness might use his father's name as his symbol? The *gemara* dismisses this: *lo khatzif inish v'shavi l'shmei d'avuh simana* — a person does not have the audacity to adopt his father's name as his own signature symbol. The social and familial inhibition is a sufficient safeguard.

### Mar Zutra: Justification

Having worked through a series of increasingly technical answers, Mar Zutra cuts through all of them with an elegant and straightforward solution: *mekushar she'ein yadav kalin b'shita achat pasul* — a *mekushar* document in which the signatures do not all conclude on the same line is invalid.

What this means, according to the Rashbam and Rabbeinu Gershom, is that all signatures must be fully justified — spread out so that each one begins and ends at precisely the same left and right margins. When names of different lengths are fully justified in this way, the spacing within each name will differ. Any fraudulent cut would therefore have to pass cleanly through all the signatures simultaneously, but since the internal letter spacing varies from name to name, a cut that aligns perfectly with one name will necessarily pass through the middle of letters in another. The irregularity makes the tampering immediately visible. This answer is so clean and obvious that one wonders why the *gemara* did not begin with it.

The sustained concern threading through this entire discussion is a single underlying problem: how does one prevent alterations to a document after it has been duly signed? The *gemara* has addressed this from multiple angles — the role of *hakol sharir v'kayam* in marking the document's terminus, the illegitimacy of erasures near the end, the physical arrangement of signatures, and the structural requirement that signatures be fully justified.

## Corrections & Erasures

### Listing Corrections

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosef said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol hamichakim kulam tzarich sheyichtov v'den kiyumeihon* — all erasures in a document must be enumerated and confirmed at the document's end. The question of whether the phrase *v'den kiyumeihon* — "and these are their validations" — must appear explicitly as a heading, or whether one need only list the corrections without that introductory phrase, is disputed. The version without *v'den kiyumeihon* is the practice endorsed by both Tosafot and the Rashbam: rather than a formal heading, one writes the substance of the corrections directly, noting in which line a *machak* occurred, whether a word was suspended (*tluya*), and so forth. This list appears before *hakol sharir v'kayam*, which then closes the document entirely.

Since no erasures are permitted after *hakol sharir v'kayam* has been written, and since that phrase itself signals the absolute terminus of the document, the listing of corrections before it carries full legal force and cannot be tampered with thereafter.

### Last Line Summary

The second requirement established by Rabbi Yochanan is *v'tzarich she'yachzor me'inyano shel shtar b'shita achrona* — the last line of the document must contain a recapitulation of the document's main substance. This does not mean rewriting the entire *shtar* in miniature. According to the Rashbam, the operative formula is something like *v'kanina min* so-and-so *al kol ma d'chtiv u'meforash le'eil u'mana de'kashrana le'makanya bei* — "we acquired from him regarding everything written and specified above, with an instrument fit for acquisition." This refers back to the whole of the *shtar* without restating it in full.

The rationale for this requirement becomes clear when one considers that witnesses, when signing, will inevitably leave some white space between the end of the text and their signatures. That gap creates an opportunity for fraud — a bad actor could insert an additional clause in that space. The solution is to fill the last substantive line with a formulaic summary that (a) leaves no ambiguity about where the document's content ends, and (b) is sufficiently standardized that any deviation from the expected boilerplate would be immediately apparent.

## Ein Lamedim Rule

### No Learning from Last

This requirement leads directly to the principle articulated by Rav Amram: *lefi she'ein lamedim mishita achrona* — no legally operative content may be derived from the last line of a document. The last line exists solely to close and summarize; it is structurally boilerplate. If someone manages to insert a substantive clause there — whether fraudulently or inadvertently — it carries no legal weight.

There is a dispute among the *rishonim* about how to understand this rule. The Rashbam holds that it is purely advisory: it instructs the drafter not to place anything of legal consequence in the last line, since the court will disregard it regardless. On this reading, the rule is about drafting practice — a warning, not a formal prohibition. Tosafot, drawing on other *Gemarot*, understands the requirement more stringently, suggesting that there is an affirmative obligation to include a substantive summary in that final line — and that the *ein lamedim* rule is then the logical corollary, since that line is inherently suspect as a potential site of tampering. The practical upshot is the same: the last line before the signatures must be standardized summary language, and nothing written there can serve as a source for independent legal inference.

### White Space Rule

Rav Nachman challenges Rav Amram: what is the source for this principle? Rav Amram replies from a *baraita*: *hirchik et ha'edim shnei shitin min hakatav pasul; shita achat kasher* — if the witnesses sign leaving two empty lines of white space between the text and their signatures, the document is invalid; one line of space is acceptable. The reason two lines is invalid is transparent: *dilma mezayef v'chatav* — the fraudster has room to insert an additional line of content in that space. But why is one line also not disqualifying, since one could theoretically insert something there as well? The *gemara* answers: *ela lav shema mina ein lamedim mishita achrona* — this must be because we rule that nothing written in that last line is legally operative. A forger gains nothing by inserting a clause there if the court will refuse to derive any ruling from it. The rule about white space and the principle of *ein lamedim mishita achrona* thus prove and reinforce each other.

## Modern Practice Explained

### Tosafot Observation

A substantial Tosafot at the end of the *sugya* confronts what is, on the face of it, a glaring discrepancy between the *gemara*'s framework and contemporary practice. The *gemara* operated in a world where *hakol sharir v'kayam* was not a standard feature of all documents — indeed, from the earlier discussion about the *mekushar*, one infers that it was the exception. Precisely because most *shtarot* lacked that closing formula, the entire architecture of the *sugya* — the prohibition on white space, the requirement that the last line be boilerplate, the rule of *ein lamedim mishita achrona* — was necessary to protect document integrity.

### Hakol Sharir Solves It

Tosafot observes: *b'yameihem lo haya katuv sharir v'kayam* — in their time, *sharir v'kayam* was not written in *peshutim*. It was precisely this absence that made all the protective measures necessary. But today, *sharir v'kayam* has become not merely conventional but obligatory — a *shtar* lacking it would be *pasul*. Once that phrase is universally required and its absence invalidates the document, all the concerns driving the *gemara*'s rulings dissolve. *Hakol sharir v'kayam* definitively seals the document; nothing written after it carries any force, and no erasure after that point is accepted. The fraudster has no opening.

### Corrections Before Final

This resolution also explains our practice regarding the placement of *kiyum machakim*. The *gemara* as analyzed by Tosafot would seem to require that corrections be validated before the summarizing last line — *kodem shita achrona* — so that nothing substantive appears after that boilerplate. Yet contemporary scribal practice places the *kiyum machakim* after the *v'kanina* formula, making the validation of corrections the last substantive element before the signatures, not something preceding the summary line.

Tosafot frames this as a genuine difficulty: *v'yeish litmoha — eich hurgalnu lichtov kiyum machakim achar v'kanina minei* — how have we become accustomed to writing the validation of corrections after the *v'kanina* formula? There is nothing more substantive than confirming erasures, and yet we place it precisely where the *gemara* says nothing of substance should appear. Tosafot's answer is that this concern evaporates entirely once *hakol sharir v'kayam* is universally mandated. Since the phrase is now required in every *shtar*, and since its presence absolutely bars any subsequent addition, the entire rationale for *ein lamedim mishita achrona* no longer applies. We can and do learn from the last line; we can place substantive content — including *kiyum machakim* — there, because *hakol sharir v'kayam* following it ensures the document is airtight. The *gemara*'s protective architecture was built for a world without that formula; in a world where it is obligatory, the rules governing the last line shift accordingly.
