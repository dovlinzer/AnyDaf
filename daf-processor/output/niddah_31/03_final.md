# Niddah 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fetus & Torah

### Torah Learning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְלַמְּדִין אוֹתוֹ כָּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיּוֹרֵנִי וַיֹּאמֶר לִי יִתְמׇךְ דְּבָרַי לִבֶּךָ שְׁמוֹר מִצְווֹתַי וֶחְיֵה״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״בְּסוֹד אֱלוֹהַּ עֲלֵי אׇהֳלִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** a fetus **is taught the entire Torah** while in the womb, **as it is stated: "And He taught me and said to me: Let your heart hold fast My words; keep My commandments, and live"** (Proverbs 4:4). **And it** also **states:** "As I was in the days of my youth, **when the converse of God was upon my tent"** (Job 29:4).

The *daf* opens in the middle of a beautiful *aggadata* discussing what happens to the fetus while it is in the mother's womb and as it comes into the world. The *Gemara* returns to a passage that was read at the close of the previous *daf*: that while in the womb, the baby learns the entire *Torah*. *V'cheivan sheba l'avir ha'olam* — and when it comes out into the air of the world — *ba malach v'satro al piv, umeshachacho kol hatorah kulah* — an angel comes and strikes it on its mouth, and causes it to forget the entire *Torah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן שֶׁבָּא לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם, בָּא מַלְאָךְ וְסֹטְרוֹ עַל פִּיו, וּמְשַׁכְּחוֹ כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לַפֶּתַח חַטָּאת רוֹבֵץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And once** the fetus **emerges into the airspace of the world, an angel comes and slaps it on its mouth, causing it to forget the entire Torah, as it is stated: "Sin crouches at the entrance"** (Genesis 4:7), i.e., when a person enters the world he is immediately liable to sin due to his loss of Torah knowledge.

The verse adduced is *lapetach chatat rovetz* — "sin crouches at the opening." The precise meaning of *chatat* here is ambiguous: it may refer to the imperfection of the world the infant is entering, a world where *Torah* does not come easily and must be acquired through struggle, or it may refer to the angel itself — though that reading would be unusual. In any case, the image is of a transition from a state of completeness into an imperfect world where *Torah* must be striven for anew.

### Angel's Strike

One might wonder why the *malach* strikes the infant on its mouth rather than on its brain, where the *Torah* presumably resides. This *midrash* does not say. Yet the connection between speech and *Torah* is deep — *v'shinantam l'vanecha v'dibarta bam* — and so the mouth as the locus of *Torah* expression is perhaps not so strange. There is also a possible wordplay in *lapetach*, "at the opening," understood as the *petach* of the mouth. In any event, the *midrash* presents the forgetting not as a deprivation but as a condition of human existence in an imperfect world: *Torah* must now be earned.

### The Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא מִשָּׁם עַד שֶׁמַּשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לִי תִּכְרַע כׇּל בֶּרֶךְ תִּשָּׁבַע כׇּל לָשׁוֹן״. ״כִּי לִי תִּכְרַע כׇּל בֶּרֶךְ״ — זֶה יוֹם הַמִּיתָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְפָנָיו יִכְרְעוּ כׇּל יוֹרְדֵי עָפָר״. ״תִּשָּׁבַע כׇּל לָשׁוֹן״ — זֶה יוֹם הַלֵּידָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נְקִי כַפַּיִם וּבַר לֵבָב אֲשֶׁר לֹא נָשָׂא לַשָּׁוְא נַפְשׁוֹ וְלֹא נִשְׁבַּע לְמִרְמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** a fetus **does not leave** the womb **until** the angels **administer an oath to it, as it is stated: "That to Me every knee shall bow, every tongue shall swear"** (Isaiah 45:23). The verse is interpreted as follows: **"That to Me every knee shall bow"; this is** referring to **the day of** one's **death, as it is stated: "All those who go down to the dust shall kneel before Him"** (Psalms 22:30). **"Every tongue shall swear"; this is** referring to **the day of** one's **birth, as it is stated** in description of a righteous person: **"He who has clean hands, and a pure heart, who has not taken My name in vain, and has not sworn deceitfully"** (Psalms 24:4), i.e., he has kept the oath that he took before he was born.

The *Gemara* continues: *v'eino yo'tzei misham ad she'mashbi'ino oto* — the fetus does not leave the womb until it is administered an oath. The source is the verse *ki li tichra kol berech tishava kol lashon* — "to Me shall every knee bow, every tongue shall swear." The *Gemara* reads this verse on two registers. *Ki li tichra kol berech* — every knee bows — this refers to *yom hamitah*, the day of death, as in the verse *lifanav yichre'u kol yordei afar*, all who descend to the dust shall bow before Him. *Tishava kol lashon* — every tongue has sworn — this refers to *yom haledah*, the day of birth. Support comes from the verse *neki chapayim u'var levav asher lo nasa lashav nafshi v'lo nishba l'mirmach* — the one of clean hands and pure heart who has not borne My soul in vain and has not sworn deceitfully.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה הִיא הַשְּׁבוּעָה שֶׁמַּשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתוֹ? תְּהִי צַדִּיק וְאַל תְּהִי רָשָׁע, וַאֲפִילּוּ כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ אוֹמְרִים לְךָ ״צַדִּיק אַתָּה״ — הֱיֵה בְּעֵינֶיךָ כְּרָשָׁע. וֶהֱוֵי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא טָהוֹר, וּמְשָׁרְתָיו טְהוֹרִים, וּנְשָׁמָה שֶׁנָּתַן בְּךָ טְהוֹרָה הִיא. אִם אַתָּה מְשַׁמְּרָהּ בְּטׇהֳרָה — מוּטָב, וְאִם לָאו — הֲרֵינִי נוֹטְלָהּ מִמְּךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is the oath that** the angels **administer to** the fetus? **Be righteous and do not be wicked. And even** if **the entire world says to you: You are righteous, consider yourself wicked. And know that the Holy One, Blessed be He, is pure, and His ministers are pure, and the soul that He gave you is pure. If you preserve it in** a state of **purity,** all is **well, but if** you do **not** keep it pure, **I,** the angel, **shall take it from you.**

The content of the oath itself is: *tehe tzaddik v'al tehe rasha* — be righteous and do not be wicked. And even if the entire world were to say to you, "you are righteous," you should regard yourself as though you still have far to go — *heye b'einecha k'rasha*. Whether framing this as "regard yourself as wicked" is psychologically helpful may be debated; perhaps it is better rendered as: never assume you have arrived. At the same time, *v'al tehe rasha bifnei atzmecha* — do not be wicked in your own eyes either. The oath is double-edged: it obligates constant striving while prohibiting self-contempt.

### Pure Soul

The passage continues: *she'hakadosh baruch hu tahor u'meshartav tehorim v'neshamah shenatan b'cha tehorah hi* — God is *tahor*, pure, those who attend God are pure, and the soul He has placed within you is pure. This is, of course, the source of the *Elokai neshamah* prayer recited each morning: *Elokai neshamah shenatata bi tehorah hi*. The conditional follows: if you guard that soul in its purity, well and good. If not — *hareni notlah mimcha* — I will take it back from you. Do not assume the soul is yours unconditionally.

### Kohen & Trumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: מָשָׁל לְכֹהֵן שֶׁמָּסַר תְּרוּמָה לְעַם הָאָרֶץ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: אִם אַתָּה מְשַׁמְּרָהּ בְּטׇהֳרָה — מוּטָב, וְאִם לָאו — הֲרֵינִי שׂוֹרְפָהּ לְפָנֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught a parable:** This matter is comparable **to a priest who gave *teruma*,** the portion of the produce designated for the priest, **to one who is unreliable with regard to ritual impurity [*am ha'aretz*],** and therefore it is suspected that he might not maintain the purity of the *teruma*. **And** the priest **said to him: If you keep it in** a state of **ritual purity,** all is **well, but if** you do **not** keep it pure, **I shall burn it before you.**

A *mashal* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: this is comparable to a *kohen* who entrusts his *terumah* to an *am ha'aretz*. The *kohen* says: if you guard it in its purity, good; if not, *harei hi nisrefet l'fanecha* — it will be burned before you. I will not hesitate to destroy it in your presence. The entire human race is here cast in the role of the *am ha'aretz* relative to God — the recipient of something precious and holy that does not inherently belong to them, held in trust under condition of care.

### Sin & Purity

There is a striking tension in this passage that deserves careful attention. The *Gemara* describes God as *tahor* — not *kadosh*, the more typical divine epithet, but *tahor*. This choice seems deliberate. The point appears to be a direct counterstatement to the doctrine of original sin. *Lapetach chatat rovetz* is not ignored — the *Gemara* is aware that sin crouches at the threshold of the world. But entering a world where sin exists, a world of struggle and incompleteness in which the *Torah* one was born knowing must now be laboriously reacquired, is categorically different from being born sinful or impure.

The soul comes from God. God is *tahor*. Therefore the soul is *tahor*. If one were to say that the soul enters the world already stained or corrupted, one would be saying something about God — and that is precisely what this passage refuses to say. The mention of *meshartav tehorim* — God's attendants are likewise pure — may carry an additional implication: it is perhaps a quiet rejoinder to any notion of a sinful or evil divine being, a *satan* who is ontologically corrupt. Everything in the heavenly retinue is pure; the corruption of this world is not traced to some primordial divine evil.

### Being vs. Becoming

This connects to a theme from the week's *parshah*. Korach declares *ki kol ha'edah kulam kedoshim* — all of the congregation is holy. And is that not true? The *Torah* indeed says *v'hayitem li mamlechet kohanim v'goy kadosh*, *kedoshim tihyu*, *anshei kodesh tihyun li* — you shall be, you shall become, a holy people. But every one of those formulations is in the future tense or imperative: you shall become holy, be holy. Korach says: we *are* holy. The difference is enormous. There is a profound distinction between asserting that one has arrived at holiness — that one's existing status renders further striving unnecessary — and recognizing that holiness is an ongoing obligation, a state of perpetual *becoming*. The oath *tehe tzaddik v'al tehe rasha* encodes this very dynamic: it commands continuous striving and warns against the complacency of assuming the work is done.

## Fetal Development

### Oath Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי קְרָא? ״מִמְּעֵי אִמִּי אַתָּה גוֹזִי״. מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״גּוֹזִי״ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאִשְׁתְּבוֹעֵי הוּא? דִּכְתִיב: ״גׇּזִּי נִזְרֵךְ וְהַשְׁלִיכִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the verse** from which it is derived that a fetus is administered an oath on the day of its birth? "Upon You I have relied from birth; **You are He Who took me out [*gozi*] of my mother's womb"** (Psalms 71:6). **From where may** it **be inferred that this** word: **"*Gozi*," is a term of administering an oath? As it is written: "Cut off [*gozi*] your hair and cast it away"** (Jeremiah 7:29), which is interpreted as a reference to the vow of a nazirite, who must cut off his hair at the end of his term of naziriteship.

Rav Elazar asks: *mai kra* — what is the verse? The *Gemara* has spoken of an oath taken in the womb, but has not yet identified the biblical locus for where exactly in the womb this occurs. The verse offered is *mi'ma'ai imi atah gozi* — "from my mother's womb You are my *gozi*." The word *gozi* is normally translated as "You who shaped me," but the *Gemara* reads it as a term of oath-taking. The proof that *gozi* can connote an oath is from the verse *gazi nizrech vehashlichi* — a *nazir* takes a vow, *nezirut*, and the word *gazi* is related to that act of vowing.

### Walnut in Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לְמָה וָלָד דּוֹמֶה בִּמְעֵי אִמּוֹ? לֶאֱגוֹז מוּנָּח בְּסֵפֶל שֶׁל מַיִם, אָדָם נוֹתֵן אֶצְבָּעוֹ עָלָיו — שׁוֹקֵעַ לְכָאן וּלְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar says: To what is a fetus in its mother's womb comparable?** It is comparable **to a nut placed in a basin** full **of water,** floating on top of the water. If **a person puts his finger on top of** the nut, **it sinks** either **in this direction or in that direction.**

Rav Elazar offers a second teaching: *lama havlad domeh bimei imo* — what is the fetus like in its mother's womb? *Le'egoz munach bisafal shel mayim* — like a walnut resting in a dish of water. If one presses a finger upon it, the walnut shifts and bobs in every direction, *shokeia lechan u'lechan*, yet remains protected. The image conveys how the fetus, suspended in amniotic fluid, can be moved from the outside while still being sheltered within.

### Three Chambers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים — וָלָד דָּר בְּמָדוֹר הַתַּחְתּוֹן, אֶמְצָעִיִּים — וָלָד דָּר בְּמָדוֹר הָאֶמְצָעִי, אַחֲרוֹנִים — וָלָד דָּר בְּמָדוֹר הָעֶלְיוֹן, וְכֵיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ זְמַנּוֹ לָצֵאת — מִתְהַפֵּךְ וְיוֹצֵא, וְזֶהוּ ״חֶבְלֵי אִשָּׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: During **the first three months** of pregnancy, the **fetus resides in the lower compartment** of the womb; in the **middle** three months, the **fetus resides in the middle compartment;** and during the **last** three months of pregnancy the **fetus resides in the upper compartment. And once its time to emerge arrives, it turns upside down and emerges; and this is** what causes **labor pains.**

The *Baraita* teaches: *shlosha chadashim harishonim velad dar bimador hatachton* — during the first trimester, the fetus resides in the lower chamber of the womb, closest to the birth canal. In the second trimester, it rises to the middle chamber. In the final trimester, it ascends to the upper chamber — *bimador ha'elyon*. This is the reverse of what one might expect intuitively, but the *Gemara* supports it from the verse in Tehillim: *asher asiti baseter rukamti betachtiyot aretz* — "You fashioned me in secret, You wove me in the lowest depths." The word *rukamti*, "You wove me," is used rather than *darti*, "I dwelt," which indicates that the fashioning — the early formation — occurs in the lower chamber. From there the fetus ascends.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי קְרָא? ״אֲשֶׁר עֻשֵּׂיתִי בַסֵּתֶר רֻקַּמְתִּי בְּתַחְתִּיּוֹת אָרֶץ״. ״דַּרְתִּי״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״רֻקַּמְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar says: What is the verse** from which it is derived that a fetus initially resides in the lower part of the womb? **"When I was made in secret, and I was woven together in the lowest parts of the earth"** (Psalms 139:15). Since it **is not stated: I resided** in the lowest parts of the earth, **but rather: "I was woven together** in the lowest parts of the earth," this teaches that during the initial stage of a fetus's development, when it is woven together, its location is in the lower compartment of the womb.

### Birth Pangs

When the time arrives for the fetus to emerge — *mitehafech veyotze* — it turns and comes out. This turning, the *Gemara* will explain, is the source of *chevlei isha*, the birth pangs of a woman. A *Baraita* states: *chevlei shel nekeivah merubin mishel zachar* — the birth pangs of giving birth to a girl are greater than those of giving birth to a boy. Rav Elazar asks: *mai kra* — what is the textual basis?

### Girl Birth Pangs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא חֶבְלֵי נְקֵבָה מְרוּבִּין מִשֶּׁל זָכָר? זֶה בָּא כְּדֶרֶךְ תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ, וְזֶה בָּא כְּדֶרֶךְ תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ, זוֹ הוֹפֶכֶת פָּנֶיהָ, וְזֶה אֵין הוֹפֵךְ פָּנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **the labor pains experienced by** a woman who gives birth to **a female,** that they **are greater than those experienced by** a woman who gives birth to **a male?** The Gemara answers: **This** one, a male fetus, **emerges in the manner in which it engages in intercourse.** Just as a male engages in intercourse facing downward, so too, it is born while facing down. **And that** one, a female fetus, **emerges in the manner in which it engages in intercourse,** i.e., facing upward. Consequently, **that** one, a female fetus, **turns its face around** before it is born, **but this** one, a male fetus, **does not turn its face around** before it is born.

The explanation proceeds from an understanding of how conception shapes fetal orientation. The *Gemara* reasons that during conception the man is typically on top, and the child is formed in the orientation of its parent: *zeh ba k'derech tashmisho v'zeh ba k'derech tashmisho*. A male fetus is fashioned face-down, as the father was, and emerges face-down — which is indeed the normal presentation at birth — without needing to flip. A female fetus, fashioned face-up as the mother was, must turn over in order to be born face-down: *vezehu chevlei isha*, and this turning is what produces the additional birth pangs. The *Gemara* states it directly: *ve-ze ofech paneha ve-ze ein ofech paneha* — the female fetus must flip, the male need not.

## Sex in Pregnancy

### Trimesters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים — תַּשְׁמִישׁ קָשֶׁה לָאִשָּׁה, וְגַם קָשֶׁה לַוָּלָד. אֶמְצָעִיִּים — קָשֶׁה לָאִשָּׁה וְיָפֶה לַוָּלָד. אַחֲרוֹנִים — יָפֶה לְאִשָּׁה וְיָפֶה לַוָּלָד, שֶׁמִּתּוֹךְ כָּךְ נִמְצָא הַוָּלָד מְלוּבָּן וּמְזוֹרָז.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: During **the first three months** of pregnancy, **sexual intercourse is difficult** and harmful **for the woman and is also difficult for the offspring.** During the **middle** three months, intercourse is **difficult for the woman but is beneficial for the offspring.** During the **last** three months, sexual intercourse is **beneficial for the woman and beneficial for the offspring; as a result of it the offspring is found to be strong and fair skinned.**

The *Baraita* addresses the impact of marital relations during pregnancy, broken down by trimester. *Shlosha chodashim harishonim tashmish kasheh l'ishah vegam kasheh lavalad* — in the first trimester, relations are difficult and potentially harmful for both the woman and the fetus. In the second trimester, *kasheh l'ishah v'yafeh lavalad* — they are difficult for the woman but beneficial for the fetus. In the final trimester, *yafeh l'ishah v'yafeh lavalad* — they are beneficial for both. Rashi notes that he is not certain of the underlying reasoning for the effect on the woman, but explains the differential impact on the fetus as follows: since in the first trimester the fetus resides in the lower chamber, it is more directly affected by penetration; as the fetus ascends through the second and third trimesters, it becomes progressively less vulnerable and the semen can even be beneficial. The precise mechanism remains unclear, but this trimester-based framework was, notably, widely accepted not only in Rabbinic literature but in broader medical culture well into the late medieval period.

### Day 90

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: הַמְשַׁמֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ לְיוֹם תִּשְׁעִים — כְּאִילּוּ שׁוֹפֵךְ דָּמִים. מְנָא יָדַע? אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְשַׁמֵּשׁ וְהוֹלֵךְ, וְ״שׁוֹמֵר פְּתָאיִם ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who engages in intercourse** with his wife **on the ninetieth day** of her pregnancy, **it is as though he spills** her **blood.** The Gemara asks: **How does one know** that it is the ninetieth day of her pregnancy? **Rather, Abaye says: One should go ahead and engage in intercourse** with his wife even if it might be the ninetieth day, **and** rely on God to prevent any ensuing harm, as the verse states: **"The Lord preserves the simple"** (Psalms 116:6).

The *Gemara* adds: *tana hameshammesh mitato l'yom tish'im* — if a husband has relations with his wife on exactly the 90th day of pregnancy — the cusp between the first and second trimesters — *k'ilu shofech damim*, it is as though he has spilled blood, as this is the moment of greatest vulnerability and risk of miscarriage. The practical difficulty is immediately raised: *hashta mana yadei* — how would one know exactly which day is the 90th? Abaye resolves this pragmatically: *meshammesh veholech v'shomer peta'im Hashem* — one should simply proceed, and God watches over the simple. The *Gemara* also assumes that in many cases a woman may not even know she is pregnant during the first trimester, which further renders the concern largely theoretical. It should be noted that the *Gemara* appears to count from the moment of conception, whereas the contemporary convention counts weeks from the last menstruation, adding approximately two weeks to the baseline.

## Three Partners

### Father's White

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה שׁוּתָּפִין יֵשׁ בָּאָדָם — הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, וְאָבִיו, וְאִמּוֹ. אָבִיו מַזְרִיעַ הַלּוֹבֶן, שֶׁמִּמֶּנּוּ עֲצָמוֹת, וְגִידִים, וְצִפׇּרְנַיִם, וּמוֹחַ שֶׁבְּרֹאשׁוֹ, וְלוֹבֶן שֶׁבָּעַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: There are three partners in** the creation of **a person: The Holy One, Blessed be He, and his father, and his mother. His father emits the white seed, from which** the following body parts are formed: The **bones,** the **sinews,** the **nails,** the **brain that is in its head, and** the **white of the eye.**

The *Gemara* introduces one of its most celebrated passages: *shlosha shutafim yesh b'adam* — there are three partners in a human being: *HaKadosh Baruch Hu v'aviv v'imo* — God, the father, and the mother. *Aviv mazria haloven* — the father provides the white matter. From this white matter come the bones, the sinews, the fingernails — which are translucent and whitish — the brain, and the white of the eye.

### Mother's Red

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִמּוֹ מַזְרַעַת אוֹדֶם, שֶׁמִּמֶּנּוּ עוֹר, וּבָשָׂר, וּשְׂעָרוֹת, וְשָׁחוֹר שֶׁבָּעַיִן. וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא נוֹתֵן בּוֹ רוּחַ, וּנְשָׁמָה, וּקְלַסְתֵּר פָּנִים, וּרְאִיַּית הָעַיִן, וּשְׁמִיעַת הָאוֹזֶן, וְדִבּוּר פֶּה, וְהִלּוּךְ רַגְלַיִם, וּבִינָה, וְהַשְׂכֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **His mother emits red seed, from which** are formed the **skin,** the **flesh,** the **hair, and** the **black of the eye. And the Holy One, Blessed be He, inserts into him a spirit, a soul,** his **countenance [*ukelaster*], eyesight, hearing of the ear,** the capability of **speech** of **the mouth,** the capability of **walking** with **the legs, understanding, and wisdom.**

*Imo mazra'at odem* — the mother contributes the red matter, associated with her blood and with the menstrual cycle that the Rabbis observed. From this red matter come the skin, the flesh, the blood, the hair, and the black of the eye. The *Gemara* thus maps the visible components of the body onto two color registers: the father's white and the mother's red. One might wonder whether lighter-skinned populations are imagined here, but it is worth noting that this *Baraita* originates in *Eretz Yisrael*.

Of particular interest among God's contributions — which are enumerated next — is the *klaster panim*, the distinctive configuration of the face. Even if a child has the physical eyes of the father and the skin of the mother, the individual character of how those features cohere into a particular human face — what Rashi describes as *ziv*, the radiance or expression — that belongs to the divine contribution.

### God's Gifts

While the father and mother supply the raw materials, none of it functions until God animates it. God contributes *reiyat ha'ayin* — sight, *shemiat haozen* — hearing, *dibur peh* — speech (*mi sam peh l'adam*), *hilukh raglayim* — the ability to walk, and *deah u'vinah v'sechel* — knowledge, understanding, and wisdom. These may correspond to different levels of the soul: what is more purely spiritual (*neshamah*) versus what is shared with the animal world (*ruach*) — a distinction developed extensively in medieval Jewish philosophy, though the *Gemara* does not elaborate on it here. What is clear is that *klaster panim*, appearing among these divine gifts, occupies a peculiar middle position: it is physical in nature, yet it is somehow the most distinctly human aspect of the body, the visible mark of the individual soul.

### Death & Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ זְמַנּוֹ לְהִפָּטֵר מִן הָעוֹלָם, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא נוֹטֵל חֶלְקוֹ וְחֵלֶק אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ, מַנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶם. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי ״פּוּץ מִלְחָא וּשְׁדִי בִּשְׂרָא לְכַלְבָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **And when** a person's **time to depart from the world arrives, the Holy One, Blessed be He, retrieves His part, and He leaves the part of** the person's **father and mother before them. Rav Pappa said: This** is in accordance with the adage **that people say: Remove the salt** from a piece of meat, **and** you may then **toss the meat to a dog,** as it has become worthless.

*V'kivun she'higia zemano l'ipater min ha'olam* — when the time comes for departure from this world, *HaKadosh Baruch Hu notel chelko* — God takes back His portion, namely the soul, *v'chelek avi v'imo maniakh lifneihem* — and the portions of the father and mother are left below. This echoes the verse in Kohelet: *v'haruach tashuv el ha'Elokim asher netanah* — the spirit returns to God who gave it, while the body returns to the earth from which it came.

Rav Pappa adds a folk saying: *pot milkhakh v'shadi l'visra* — once you remove the salt that preserves the meat, you might as well throw the meat to the dogs. The coarser image drives home the point: it is not merely that the soul animates the body spiritually; the soul is in some sense what preserves the body even physically. Once the soul departs, decomposition begins. The two things are more intertwined than a simple body-soul dualism would suggest.

## Miracles

### Pouch Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַב חִינָּנָא בַּר פָּפָּא: מַאי דִכְתִיב ״עוֹשֶׂה גְּדוֹלוֹת עַד אֵין חֵקֶר וְנִפְלָאוֹת עַד אֵין מִסְפָּר״? בֹּא וּרְאֵה שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִדַּת הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מִדַּת בָּשָׂר וָדָם. מִדַּת בָּשָׂר וָדָם, נוֹתֵן חֵפֶץ בְּחֵמֶת צְרוּרָה וּפִיהָ לְמַעְלָה — סָפֵק מִשְׁתַּמֵּר, סְפֵק אֵין מִשְׁתַּמֵּר. וְאִילּוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, צָר הָעוּבָּר בִּמְעֵי אִשָּׁה פְּתוּחָה וּפִיהָ לְמַטָּה, וּמִשְׁתַּמֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥinnana bar Pappa taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Who does great deeds beyond comprehension, wondrous deeds without number"** (Job 9:10)? **Come and see that the attribute of flesh and blood is unlike the attribute of the Holy One, Blessed be He. The attribute of flesh and blood** is that if one **puts an article in a flask,** even if the flask is **tied and its opening** faces **upward, it is uncertain whether** the item **is preserved** from getting lost, **and it is uncertain whether it is not preserved** from being lost. **But the Holy One, Blessed be He, forms the fetus in a woman's open womb, and its opening** faces **downward, and** yet the fetus **is preserved.**

Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa expounds: *mai dikhtiv oseh gedolot ad ein kheker v'niflaot ad ein mispar* — "Who performs great deeds beyond comprehension and wonders beyond number." *Bo ur'eh shelo k'midat HaKadosh Baruch Hu midat basar vedam* — come and see how God's ways differ from the ways of human beings. A person places a valuable object in a bound leather pouch — *cheimas tzerurah* — with the mouth of the pouch facing upward, and even so it is doubtful whether the contents will remain secure. God, by contrast, fashions the fetus *bimei ishah pesuchah ufia lematah* — in the open womb with the mouth facing downward — and yet *umishtameir* — it remains protected. Rashi emphasizes that the fetus, residing in the lower chamber, is already in closest proximity to the opening, making the miracle of retention all the more striking.

### Scale Rises

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: אָדָם נוֹתֵן חֲפָצָיו לְכַף מֹאזְנַיִם, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּכְבִּיד יוֹרֵד לְמַטָּה, וְאִילּוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּכְבִּיד הַוָּלָד עוֹלֶה לְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Another matter** that demonstrates the difference between the attributes of God and the attributes of people is that when **a person places his articles on a scale** to be measured, **the heavier** the item **is,** the more **it descends. But** when **the Holy One, Blessed be He,** forms a fetus, **the heavier the offspring gets,** the more **it ascends upward** in the womb.

A second comparison: a person places objects on a balance scale, and the heavier the load, the further down that side descends — *kol zeman shemachbid yoreid lematah*. But with God, *kol zeman shemachbid havlad oleh lema'alah* — as the fetus grows heavier, it rises higher in the womb. This corresponds to the movement through the three trimesters: the fetus ascends from the lower chamber through the middle to the upper, even as it increases in weight — the inverse of ordinary physical expectation.

### Seeds Merge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: מַאי דִכְתִיב ״אוֹדְךָ (ה׳) עַל כִּי נוֹרָאוֹת נִפְלֵיתִי נִפְלָאִים מַעֲשֶׂיךָ וְנַפְשִׁי יוֹדַעַת מְאֹד״? בֹּא וּרְאֵה שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִדַּת הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מִדַּת בָּשָׂר וָדָם. מִדַּת בָּשָׂר וָדָם, אָדָם נוֹתֵן זֵרְעוֹנִים בַּעֲרוּגָה כׇּל (אַחַת וְאַחַת) [אֶחָד וְאֶחָד] עוֹלֶה בְּמִינוֹ, וְאִילּוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא צָר הָעוּבָּר בִּמְעֵי אִשָּׁה, וְכוּלָּם עוֹלִין לְמִין אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "I will give thanks to You, for I am fearfully and wonderfully made; wonderful are Your works, and that my soul knows very well"** (Psalms 139:14)? **Come and see that the attribute of flesh and blood is unlike the attribute of the Holy One, Blessed be He. The attribute of flesh and blood** is that when **a person plants seeds** of different species **in** one **garden bed, each and every one** of the seeds **emerges** as a grown plant **according to its species. But the Holy One, Blessed be He, forms the fetus in a woman's womb, and all of** the seeds, i.e., those of both the father and the mother, **emerge** when the offspring is formed **as one** sex.

Rabbi Yose HaGlili expounds the verse *odkha al kol nora'os niflesi nifla'im ma'asekha venafshi yoda'as me'od* — "I will thank You for the awesome wonders You have done for me; wondrous are Your works and my soul knows it well." *Adam nosein zir'onim be'arugah* — when a person plants seeds in a garden bed, *kol achas ve'achas olah beminah* — each seed grows true to its own kind. Plant a cucumber seed and you get a cucumber; plant a tomato seed and you get a tomato. Even if planted side by side, they remain distinct and do not merge. *Ve'ilu HaKadosh Baruch Hu tzar ubar b'mei ishah vechulam olim lemin echad* — but God fashions the fetus in the mother's womb, and the contribution of the father, the contribution of the mother, and the divine contribution all merge into a single, beautiful, unified human being — something that would never occur if one merely planted the seeds next to one another.

It is worth noting that in this *drasha*, the three components are treated as genuine equals. One could have made the point about divine involvement by simply contrasting the human partners with God, but instead the *drasha* builds a three-way partnership in which all three are necessary and none is dispensable. This is significant in light of where the *sugyah* began: a discussion of viable and non-viable birth, rooted in the recognition that God is a genuine *shutaf* in every human life.

### Dyer's Colors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: צַבָּע נוֹתֵן סַמָּנִין לַיּוֹרָה — כּוּלָּן עוֹלִין לְצֶבַע אֶחָד, וְאִילּוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא צָר הָעוּבָּר בִּמְעֵי אִשָּׁה, כׇּל (אַחַת וְאַחַת) [אֶחָד וְאֶחָד] עוֹלֶה לְמִינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** when **a dyer puts herbs in a cauldron [*leyora*], they all emerge as one color** of dye, **whereas the Holy One, Blessed be He, forms the fetus in a woman's womb,** and **each and every one** of the seeds **emerges as its own type.** In other words, the seed of the father form distinct elements, such as the white of the eye, and the seed of the mother forms other elements, such as the black of the eye, as explained above.

A final *davar acher*: *tzava noten samanin leyoreh* — a dyer places different colored dyes into a vat, *kulam olim letzeva echad* — and they all merge into one undifferentiated color. But God, *tzar hu bimei ishah*, fashions the fetus in the womb, *kol echad ve'echad oleh lemino* — and each component retains its own character within the whole. The white matter of the father remains distinct as bone and sinew; the red matter of the mother remains distinct as skin and blood; the divine gifts of sight, speech, and *klaster panim* remain distinguishable. They do not homogenize.

This is the salad-bowl model rather than the melting-pot model, to use a contemporary analogy: the component elements cohere into a greater whole while preserving their distinctiveness. And this, as it happens, is precisely how modern genetics understands the situation — maternal and paternal genetic imprints are preserved as such, with dominant and recessive expressions, rather than being fused into a single undifferentiated code.

## Divine Oversight

### Yosef Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי דִכְתִיב ״אוֹדְךָ ה׳ כִּי אָנַפְתָּ בִּי יָשׁוֹב אַפְּךָ וּתְנַחֲמֵנִי״, בַּמֶּה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written:** "And on that day you shall say: **I will give thanks to You, Lord, for You were angry with me; Your anger is turned away, and You comfort me"** (Isaiah 12:1)? **With regard to what** matter **is the verse speaking?**

The *Gemara* shifts slightly from the immediate topic of fetal formation to a series of *drashot* on related themes of divine involvement. The *drasha* attributed to Yosef expounds: *odkha Hashem ki anafta bi yashov apecha utnachameni* — "I will thank You, God, because You were angry with me; Your anger has turned away and You have comforted me." The plain meaning is probably gratitude for the removal of divine anger, but the *Gemara* asks: *bameh hakatuv medaber* — what is this verse speaking about?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁיָּצְאוּ לִסְחוֹרָה. יָשַׁב לוֹ קוֹץ לְאֶחָד מֵהֶן, הִתְחִיל מְחָרֵף וּמְגַדֵּף. לְיָמִים שָׁמַע שֶׁטָּבְעָה סְפִינָתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ בַּיָּם, הִתְחִיל מוֹדֶה וּמְשַׁבֵּחַ. לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר: ״יָשׁוֹב אַפְּךָ וּתְנַחֲמֵנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** It is referring, for example, **to two people who left** their homes to go **on a business** trip. **A thorn penetrated** the body **of one of them,** and he was consequently unable to go with his colleague. **He started blaspheming and cursing** in frustration. **After a period of time, he heard that the ship of the other** person **had sunk in the sea,** and realized that the thorn had saved him from death. He then **started thanking** God **and praising** Him for his delivery due to the slight pain caused to him by the thorn. This is the meaning of the statement: I will give thanks to You, Lord, for You were angry with me. **Therefore, it is stated** at the end of the verse: **"Your anger is turned away, and You comfort me."**

The answer is a parable: two people set out together on a business trip. One of them gets a splinter and, unable to travel, is forced to stay behind. He curses and blasphemes — *mecharef umegadef* — furious at his misfortune. Then, days later, he hears that the ship his companion was sailing on has sunk. *Hitchil modeh umeshabeach* — he begins to offer thanks and praise. The bad thing was, in retrospect, the deliverance. *Vekach ne'emar yashov apecha utnachameni* — your anger has turned away and become comfort. One becomes grateful even for what appeared at the time to be a calamity.

There is a genuine theological difficulty in these stories: the gratitude of the one who survived raises uncomfortable questions about the hundreds who did not. The *Gemara* does not resolve that question. But the *gam zu letovah* message — the teaching of Nachum Ish Gam Zu — is present here: that even bad things can ultimately contain hidden good. What is particularly striking is that the figure in the parable is not a righteous man — he is blaspheming God. Yet even such a person can, in the end, arrive at gratitude and recognition. Perhaps that too is part of the point: the capacity to ultimately recognize God's hidden goodness is not reserved for the *tzaddik*.

### Miracles Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי דִכְתִיב ״עוֹשֶׂה נִפְלָאוֹת (גְּדוֹלוֹת) לְבַדּוֹ וּבָרוּךְ שֵׁם כְּבוֹדוֹ לְעוֹלָם״? אֲפִילּוּ בַּעַל הַנֵּס אֵינוֹ מַכִּיר בְּנִסּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this** statement **is** identical to **that which Rabbi Elazar said: What** is the meaning of that **which is written:** "Blessed be the Lord God, the God of Israel, **Who does wondrous things alone; and blessed be His glorious name forever"** (Psalms 72:18–19)? What does it mean that God "does wondrous things alone"? It means that **even the one for whom the miracle was performed does not recognize the miracle** that was performed for **him.**

Rabbi Elazar expounds: *mai d'chsiv oseh niflaot levado uvaruch shem kevodo le'olam* — "Who alone performs wonders, and blessed is His glorious name forever." The word *levado* — alone — means that the miracle is known only to God. *Afilu ba'al haness eino makir benisso* — even the recipient of the miracle does not recognize the miracle that has been done for him. There are moments when great salvations occur, and the very person saved cannot perceive them — perhaps because being at the center of events obscures one's perspective, or because the miracle presents itself under the guise of an ordinary inconvenience, like a splinter. This connects back to the *drasha* of Yosef: the blasphemer does not know, in the moment, that he is the beneficiary of a miracle.

### Winnowing Seed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵישׁ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא: מַאי דִכְתִיב ״אׇרְחִי וְרִבְעִי זֵרִיתָ וְכׇל דְּרָכַי הִסְכַּנְתָּה״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלֹּא נוֹצַר אָדָם מִן כׇּל הַטִּפָּה, אֶלָּא מִן הַבָּרוּר שֶׁבָּהּ. תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: מָשָׁל לְאָדָם שֶׁזּוֹרֶה בְּבֵית הַגְּרָנוֹת, נוֹטֵל אֶת הָאוֹכֶל וּמַנִּיחַ אֶת הַפְּסוֹלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "You measure [*zerita*] my going about [*orḥi*] and my lying down [*riv'i*], and are acquainted with all my ways"** (Psalms 139:3)? This verse **teaches that a person is not created from the entire drop** of semen, **but from its clear** part. *Zerita* can mean to winnow, while *orḥi* and *riv'i* can both be explained as references to sexual intercourse. Therefore the verse is interpreted homiletically as saying that God separates the procreative part of the semen from the rest. **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught a parable:** This matter is comparable **to a person who winnows** grain **in the granary; he takes the food and leaves the waste.**

Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa expounds the verse *odkha al kol nora'os niflesi nifla'im ma'asekha venafshi yoda'as me'od* — "I will thank You for the awesome wonders You have done for me; wondrous are Your works and my soul knows it well." The word *riv'i* is read in a sexual register — *rove'a* denotes an act of intercourse — and *zerita* is read as *zoreh*, to winnow. The teaching: *melo notzar adam mikol hatipa ela min habarur shebah* — a person is not created from the entirety of the drop of semen, but from the clearest, most refined part of it.

A *mashal* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: a winnower in a threshing floor removes the food grain and casts aside the chaff — *notel et haohel umeniach et hapsoles*. So too God winnows the semen, taking only the most refined element for the formation of life. This raises an interesting observation: while the earlier teaching assigned the physical components of the body entirely to father and mother, with God contributing only the spiritual gifts, here God is implicated in the fashioning of the physical as well — it is God who selects which element of the seed will form the child.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״וַתַּזְרֵנִי חַיִל״, וּכְתִיב ״הָאֵל הַמְאַזְּרֵנִי חָיִל״. אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, זֵירִיתַנִי וְזֵרַזְתַּנִי.
>
> **Translation:** This is **in accordance with** a statement **of Rabbi Abbahu, as Rabbi Abbahu raises a contradiction: It is written** in one of King David's psalms: **"For You have girded me [*vatazreni*] with strength for battle"** (II Samuel 22:40), without the letter *alef* in *vatazreni*; **and it is written** in another psalm: **"Who girds me [*hame'azreni*] with strength"** (Psalms 18:33), with an *alef* in *hame'azreini*. What is the difference between these two expressions? **David said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, You selected me [*zeiritani*],** i.e., You separated between the procreative part and the rest of the semen in order to create me, **and You have girded me [*zeraztani*] with strength.**

Rabbi Abahu resolves an apparent contradiction between two verses. One verse uses the form *vatizreni chayil* — "You have winnowed me with strength" — without an *alef*, yielding the sense of winnowing (*zoreh*). Another uses *ha'el hame'azreni chayil* — "God who has girded me with strength" — with an *alef*, yielding the sense of girding. Rabbi Abahu says: David was saying before the Holy One, Blessed be He — *zeritani vezirzatani* — by winnowing the seed that led to my conception, You have thereby girded me with strength. The process of refinement is itself a source of strength.

### God Counts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מַאי דִכְתִיב ״מִי מָנָה עֲפַר יַעֲקֹב וּמִסְפָּר אֶת רוֹבַע יִשְׂרָאֵל״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא יוֹשֵׁב וְסוֹפֵר אֶת רְבִיעִיּוֹתֵיהֶם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, מָתַי תָּבֹא טִיפָּה שֶׁהַצַּדִּיק נוֹצָר הֵימֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written** in Balaam's blessing: **"Who has counted the dust of Jacob, or numbered the stock [*rova*] of Israel"** (Numbers 23:10)? The verse **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, sits and counts the times that the Jewish people engage in intercourse [*revi'iyyoteihem*],** anticipating the time **when the drop from which the righteous person will be created will arrive.**

Rabbi Abahu expounds: *mi manah afar Yaakov umispar et rova Yisrael* — "Who has counted the dust of Yaakov and numbered the *rova* of Israel?" The word *rova* — normally meaning "myriad" — is read through the cognate *rove'a*, an act of sexual intercourse. *Melamed sheHaKadosh Baruch Hu yoshev vesoper et riviosaihem shel Yisrael* — this teaches that God sits and counts the sexual encounters of Israel. Rashi specifies that it is perhaps the *onas tashmishan* — the appointed times of their relations — that is being counted.

The question is: to what end? *Matai tavo tipa shetzadik notzar mimenah* — God is watching for when the conception of a *tzadik* will take place. This requires care: the famous principle states *hakol bidei shamayim chutz miyiras shamayim* — everything is in the hands of Heaven except the fear of Heaven — meaning that righteousness itself is beyond divine predetermination and remains in human control. A related *midrash* observes that while God determines whether a child will be wealthy or wise, it pointedly does not say whether the child will be *tzadik* or *rasha*. What the *Gemara* may mean here, then, is not that God is waiting to predetermine a *tzadik*, but that God is deeply attentive to the moment when someone who will *choose* to become a *tzadik* is conceived.

### Bilam Blind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל דָּבָר זֶה נִסְמֵית עֵינוֹ שֶׁל בִּלְעָם הָרָשָׁע, אָמַר: מִי שֶׁהוּא טָהוֹר וְקָדוֹשׁ, וּמְשָׁרְתָיו טְהוֹרִים וּקְדוֹשִׁים, יָצִיץ בְּדָבָר זֶה?! מִיָּד נִסְמֵית עֵינוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״נְאֻם הַגֶּבֶר שְׁתוּם הָעָיִן״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it was **due to this matter** that **the eye of wicked Balaam went blind. He said: Should** God, **who is pure and holy, and whose ministers are pure and holy, peek at this matter? Immediately his eye was blinded** as a divine punishment, **as it is written: "The saying of the man whose eye is shut"** (Numbers 24:3).

The punishment of Bilam flows from this very teaching. Bilam, upon hearing that God sits and counts the sexual encounters of Israel in anticipation of righteous conception, was outraged: *mi shehu tahor vekadosh umeshartav tehorim vekdoshim yatzitz bidavar zeh* — He who is holy and pure, whose attendants are holy and pure — this is a fitting preoccupation for Him? *Miyad nismit eino* — immediately one of his eyes was blinded. As the Gemara puts it: you think you see properly? You do not see at all. The verse reads Bilam as *shtum ha'ayin* — of the sealed eye. Tosafot notes that *shtum* in fact means an *open* eye, singular — meaning one eye was open and the other sealed shut.

### Yissachar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי דִכְתִיב ״וַיִּשְׁכַּב עִמָּהּ בַּלַּיְלָה הוּא״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא סִיֵּיעַ בְּאוֹתוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִשָּׂשׂכָר חֲמוֹר גָּרֶם״ — חֲמוֹר גָּרַם לוֹ לְיִשָּׂשׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And this** statement **is** the same as that **which Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What** is the meaning of that **which is written,** with regard to Leah's conceiving Issachar: **"And he lay with her that night"** (Genesis 30:16)? The verse **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, contributed to that act.** The manner in which God contributed to this act is derived from another verse, **as it is stated: "Issachar is a large-boned [*garem*] donkey"** (Genesis 49:14). This teaches that God directed Jacob's **donkey** toward Leah's tent so that he would engage in intercourse with her, thereby **causing [*garam*]** Leah's conceiving **Issachar.**

Rabbi Yochanan expounds the verse *vayshkav imah balaila hu* — "and he lay with her that night, *hu*." The word *hu* — "he" — is taken as an allusion to God. *Melamed sheHaKadosh Baruch Hu siyaya be'oto ma'aseh* — this teaches that God assisted in that encounter. Leah had acquired the *dudaim* and said to Yaakov: come to me tonight. The *Gemara* adds that the verse *Yissachar chamor garem* — "Yissachar, a bony donkey" — is read as *chamor garam lo l'Yissachar*: the donkey caused it to happen for Yissachar. Rashi explains: Yaakov was traveling home on his donkey, perhaps hesitating, and God caused the donkey to walk toward Leah's tent, so that the encounter — and the conception of Yissachar — took place through divine involvement in the physical circumstances.

This sits in deliberate contrast to anything like a Christian theological reading. The *Gemara* is not saying that God impregnates in any supernatural sense. It is saying that God is genuinely invested in the conception of righteous people, and acts within the natural order — through the direction of a donkey, through the winnowing of seed — to bring such conceptions about. And just as *lapetach chatat rovetz* is met not by denying sin's existence in the world but by insisting on the purity of the soul, so here the response to divine withdrawal from human biology is not adopted but rather rejected: God is present, deeply present, in the most physical aspects of human generation.

## Male vs Female

### Ancient Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אִשָּׁה מַזְרַעַת תְּחִילָּה — יוֹלֶדֶת זָכָר, אִישׁ מַזְרִיעַ תְּחִילָּה — יוֹלֶדֶת נְקֵבָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִשָּׁה כִּי תַזְרִיעַ וְיָלְדָה זָכָר״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yitzḥak says** that **Rabbi Ami says:** The sex of a fetus is determined at the moment of conception. If the **woman emits seed first, she gives birth to a male,** and if the **man emits seed first, she gives birth to a female, as it is stated: "If a woman bears seed and gives birth to a male"** (Leviticus 12:2).

The *Gemara* now records: *amar Rabbi Yitzchak amar Rabbi Ami* — Rabbi Yitzchak said in the name of Rabbi Ami — *ishah mazra'at techilah yoledet zachar* — if the woman gives forth seed first, she gives birth to a male. *Ish mazria techilah yoledet nekevah* — if the man gives forth seed first, she gives birth to a female. The verse is: *ishah ki tazria veyaldah zachar* — "a woman who gives forth seed and gives birth to a male" — meaning: when she does so first, it is a male.

### Tarfon Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים — אִשָּׁה מַזְרַעַת תְּחִילָּה יוֹלֶדֶת זָכָר, אִישׁ מַזְרִיעַ תְּחִלָּה יוֹלֶדֶת נְקֵבָה, וְלֹא פֵּירְשׁוּ חֲכָמִים אֶת הַדָּבָר, עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבִּי צָדוֹק וּפֵירְשׁוֹ: ״אֵלֶּה בְּנֵי לֵאָה אֲשֶׁר יָלְדָה לְיַעֲקֹב בְּפַדַּן אֲרָם וְאֵת דִּינָה בִתּוֹ״ — תָּלָה הַזְּכָרִים בַּנְּקֵבוֹת וּנְקֵבוֹת בַּזְּכָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: At first,** people **would say** that if the **woman emits seed first she gives birth to a male,** and if the **man emits seed first, she gives birth to a female. But the Sages did not explain** from which verse this **matter** is derived, **until Rabbi Tzadok came and explained** that **it** is derived from the following verse: **"These are the sons of Leah, whom she bore to Jacob in Paddan Aram, with his daughter Dinah"** (Genesis 46:15). From the fact that the verse **attributes the males to the females,** as the males are called: The sons of Leah, **and** it attributes **the females to the males,**in that Dinah is called: His daughter, it is derived that if the woman emits seed first she gives birth to a male, whereas if the man emits seed first, she bears a female.

A *Baraita* elaborates: the ancients taught this rule — *barishonim hayu omrim* — but they did not explain its source, *velo pirshu Chachamim et hadavar*, until Rabbi Tarfon came and explained it. His proof text is not from the verse quoted above but from: *eleh bnei Leah asher yaldah l'Yaakov b'Padan Aram v'et Dinah bito* — "these are the sons of Leah that she bore Yaakov in Padan Aram, and Dinah his daughter." The verse *attaches* the sons to the mother — *talah hazacharim binkevot* — and attaches the daughter to the father — *vehankevot bazacharim*. This demonstrates that when the mother's seed comes first, the result is male, and when the father's seed comes first, the result is female.

### Female Orgasm

It is at this point that the *sugyah* makes explicit what was implicit above: the *Gemara* is referring, in its discussion of *ishah mazra'at*, to something akin to female orgasm or arousal resulting in the release of fluids. The *Gemara* never states this baldly, but no other biological phenomenon is analogous to the male *zera* in a way that the *Gemara* could have observed, and the subsequent discussion makes the point unmistakable.

This understanding has a striking implication in the cultural context the *Gemara* is addressing. The *Gemara* assumes — and states plainly — that male offspring were strongly preferred: sons were valued and the birth of a daughter was, in certain settings, a source of disappointment. Given the typical dynamics of marital relations, in which a husband might seek his own satisfaction without attending to his wife's, this biological teaching — whether accurate or not — creates a powerful incentive for husbands to ensure their wives' satisfaction. The man who seeks sons must, on this model, concern himself with *ishah mazra'at techilah*.

It is a historical fact, documented by scholars such as Laqueur (*The Making of Sex*), that prior to the discovery of the ovum roughly 150 years ago, a widespread belief held that female orgasm was necessary for conception. This belief, whether arising from the two-seed theory or from other reasoning, correlated historically with significant concern for female sexual satisfaction. Once the egg was discovered and it became clear that female arousal played no role in fertilization, interest in the topic sharply declined in medical and popular literature. Whether the *Gemara* means orgasm specifically or something closer to arousal is a question that cannot be resolved with certainty from the text, but the weight of analogy points toward orgasm.

### Archers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּהְיוּ בְנֵי אוּלָם אֲנָשִׁים גִּבּוֹרֵי חַיִל דּוֹרְכֵי קֶשֶׁת וּמַרְבִּים בָּנִים וּבְנֵי בָנִים״ — וְכִי בְּיָדוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם לְהַרְבּוֹת בָּנִים וּבְנֵי בָנִים? אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁמְּשַׁהִין עַצְמָן בַּבֶּטֶן כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּזְרִיעוּ נְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן תְּחִלָּה, שֶׁיְּהוּ בְּנֵיהֶם זְכָרִים — מַעֲלֶה עֲלֵיהֶן הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ הֵם מַרְבִּים בָּנִים וּבְנֵי בָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** This statement is also derived from the following verse: **"And the sons of Ulam were mighty men of valor, archers, and had many sons and sons' sons"** (I Chronicles 8:40). **Is it in a person's power to have many sons and sons' sons? Rather, because** they delay while **in** their wives' **abdomen,** initially refraining from emitting semen **so that their wives will emit seed first,** in order **that their children will be male, the verse ascribes them** credit **as though they have many sons and sons' sons.**

The *Gemara* turns to the verse about the sons of Ulam: *u'vnei ulam anashim giborei chayil dorkhei keshet umarbim banim u'vnei banim* — "and the sons of Ulam were men of valor, archers, who had many sons and sons of sons." The question is raised: can one truly control whether one's children will be male? The *Gemara* answers: *ela mitokh she-mash'him atzmam ba-beten kedei she-yazri'u nishoteihem techilah* — because they withheld themselves during intercourse in order that their wives should give forth seed first. The archery metaphor is apt: archers hold the tension of the drawn bow before releasing. *Hainu de-skharam she-yihyu bneihem zakharim* — this is their reward: that their children were male.

There is another dimension here worth noting. If the birth of a daughter can be attributed to a father's failure to ensure his wife's satisfaction, then a girl's birth carries an implicit social criticism of the father — that he acted selfishly. In a society already inclined to prefer sons, this compounds the pressure on husbands to attend to their wives. Whether this framing is fair or generous is another question; but it is the *Gemara*'s framing, and it has real social consequences.

### Rav Katina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: יָכוֹלְנִי לַעֲשׂוֹת כׇּל בָּנַי זְכָרִים. אָמַר רָבָא: הָרוֹצֶה לַעֲשׂוֹת כׇּל בָּנָיו זְכָרִים — יִבְעוֹל וְיִשְׁנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **And this** statement **is** the same as that **which Rav Ketina said: I could have made all of my children males,** by refraining from emitting seed until my wife emitted seed first. **Rava says** another method through which one can cause his children to be males: **One who wishes to make all of his children males should engage in intercourse** with his wife **and repeat** the act.

Rav Katina declares: *yacholni la'asot kol banai zakharim* — I could ensure that all my children would be male, for I could always withhold until my wife reached satisfaction first. Why did he not? Apparently because he ruled like Beit Hillel: the obligation of *pru u-rvu* requires one to have at least one son and one daughter, and making all children male would preclude fulfilling the female part of that obligation. Rava then offers a more refined strategy: *ha-rotzeh la'asot kol banav zakharim yiv'ol ve-yishneh* — one who wishes all his children to be male should have relations twice in the same night. The reasoning, spelled out by Rashi, is that a man may not always be able to withhold himself on the first occasion; but having achieved satisfaction the first time, on the second he will be less urgently driven, giving his wife the opportunity to reach satisfaction first — *ishah mazra'at techilah* — thus increasing the likelihood of male offspring.

## Conception Timing

### Near Period

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֵין אִשָּׁה מִתְעַבֶּרֶת אֶלָּא סָמוּךְ לְוִסְתָּהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״הֵן בְּעוֹוֹן חוֹלָלְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says** that **Rabbi Ami says: A woman becomes pregnant only** by engaging in intercourse **close to the onset of her** menstrual **cycle, as it is stated: "Behold, I was brought forth in iniquity"** (Psalms 51:7). This iniquity is referring to intercourse close to the woman's menstrual cycle, when intercourse is prohibited.

Rabbi Yitzchak says in the name of Rabbi Ami: *ein ishah mit'aberet ela samukh le'vistah* — a woman becomes pregnant only near the time of her period. The scientific accuracy of this claim is, of course, inverted — fertility is highest at ovulation, which is near the midpoint of the cycle, not near the period. But that is not the direction the *Gemara* is pursuing.

### Cleansing Sin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: סָמוּךְ (לטבילה) [לִטְבִילָתָהּ], שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְחֵטְא יֶחֱמַתְנִי אַמֵּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** A woman becomes pregnant only by engaging in intercourse **near** the time of her **immersion** in a ritual bath, through which she is purified from her status as a menstruating woman, **as it is stated** in the continuation of the same verse: **"And in sin [*uvḥet*] did my mother conceive me"** (Psalms 51:7).

The verse brought is *hen be-avon cholalti* — "behold, in iniquity I was fashioned." One might read this verse as implying that the sexual act itself is sinful, or that conception carries an inherent moral taint — a reading that would dovetail with certain Christian interpretations of original sin. The *Gemara* firmly rejects this reading. The word *avon* here does not mean moral sin; it refers to the fact that conception occurs near the time of the woman's period, which is when relations carry the risk of *niddah* status. That is the only *issur* in view.

Rabbi Yochanan reads the verse differently and more accurately in practical terms: *samukh l'tvilah* — it is near the time of her immersion in the *mikvah* that conception is most likely. This corresponds much more closely to the actual peak of fertility following the completion of the *niddah* period. The verse he uses is *uv'cheit yechematni immi* — "and in sin my mother heated for me." Here *cheit* is read not as moral transgression but as *cleansing*, cognate with *v'chitteh et habayit* — "he shall cleanse the house" — which appears in the *Torah* as a term of ritual purification. The *Targum* renders that verse *v'idaki yat beita* — he cleansed the house — confirming the lexical point. And the verse *techatani b'eizov v'et'har* — "cleanse me with hyssop and I will be pure" — makes clear that the biblical Hebrew root *chet-tet-alef* carries the meaning of purification and cleansing at least as naturally as it carries the meaning of sin. So the verse is not saying "in sin my mother fashioned me" but "in the cleansing — the *mikvah* — my mother became heated and conceived me." The optimal moment of conception is thus located precisely at the *mikvah*, the moment of tahorah following the *niddah* period.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״חֵטְא״ לִישָּׁנָא דְּדַכּוֹיֵי הוּא? דִּכְתִיב ״וְחִטֵּא אֶת הַבַּיִת״, וּמְתַרְגְּמִינַן ״וִידַכֵּי יָת בֵּיתָא״. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״תְּחַטְּאֵנִי בְּאֵזוֹב וְאֶטְהָר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains this derivation: **From where may** it **be inferred that this term "*ḥet*" is** a reference **to purity?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written** with regard to leprosy of houses: **"*Veḥittei* the house"** (Leviticus 14:52), **and we translate** the verse into Aramaic as: **And he shall purify the house. And if you wish, say** that the interpretation is derived **from here: "Purge me [*teḥatte'eni*] with hyssop, and I shall be pure"** (Psalms 51:9). Evidently, the root *ḥet*, *tet*, *alef* refers to purification.
