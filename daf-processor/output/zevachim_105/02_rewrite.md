# Zevachim 105 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Adayin Lo Higia Zmano

### Daf Location

We begin on 105a, on the second line, picking up from the sugya introduced at the end of 104a where the *mishna* opened the discussion. The broader context concerns the rules governing when a sacrifice becomes subject to *metamei begadim* — impurifying the clothing of those who handle it — and specifically the question of when the obligation to remove and burn certain offerings takes legal effect.

### Timing & Obligation

Rabbi Yirmiah bar Abba raises the following question: according to the opinion that holds *adayin lo higi'a zmano latzeit* — the sacrifice has not yet reached the time when it is obligated to be taken outside — does this rule apply specifically to meat that is not destined to be taken outside as a *chova*, a mandatory obligation? In that case, the restriction might reflect the absence of any future burning obligation. But what about offerings whose very nature requires that they ultimately be taken outside and burned? Is such an offering also subject to the limitation, or does its future *chova* status change the assessment even now?

The question is whether the threshold for *metamei begadim* tracks the current stage of processing or anticipates the ultimate obligation. Put differently: if an offering is destined to leave and be burned, does that future status already confer upon it some of the legal weight of having left?

### Psul & Incompleteness

Underlying this question is a general principle that runs through several areas of *hilchot kodashim*: incomplete processing prevents full legal consequences from taking effect. Just as a defect in the proper sequence of *avodah* can prevent *pigul* from attaching, so too, where a sacrifice has not yet completed the stage of *yetzia* — departure from the *azarah* — that incompleteness may insulate it from transmitting *tumah* to clothing. The rule is not merely formal; it reflects a substantive position that each stage of the sacrificial process must reach its threshold before subsequent legal effects can cascade from it.

## Levi Psul Beyetziato

### Baraita Source

The Gemara attempts to resolve the question from a *baraita*: *she'eira bo psul beyetziato* — a *Levi* in whom a *psul* occurred *beyetziato*, upon his leaving. The question is what exactly this phrasing indicates. Does it mean that the *psul* consists in the act of leaving itself — *psul yetzia* — implying that departure from the *Beit HaMikdash* is itself the disqualifying event?

### Psul Tuma not Yetzia

The Gemara answers: no. The *baraita* means rather that the *psul* occurred *while* the Levi was leaving, but the *psul* itself was not the act of leaving. Rather it was *psul tuma* — impurity — or *psul lina* — being left over — that happened to occur at the moment of departure. The phrasing *beyetziato* is circumstantial, not causal: the *psul* struck him as he was exiting, but the legal disqualification is grounded in *tumah* or *lina*, not in the act of *yetzia* per se. This reading defuses what initially appeared to be support for the view that departure itself triggers disqualification.

## Rov Behema vs. Rov Evarim

### Mixed Majority Query

Rabbi Elazar poses a question concerning the *parim hanisrafim* and *se'irim hanisrafim* — the bulls and goats that are burned outside the Temple precinct. The law holds that once the majority of the animal has exited the boundary, the departure is legally complete and the *metamei begadim* consequences attach. But what if the majority of the animal has exited only because of a minority of a single limb? The case is: *sheyatza rubo bemiut ever* — the majority of the animal's mass has crossed the boundary, but only because a minority portion of one particular limb pushed the total to more than half.

To visualize the problem: imagine the animal positioned precisely at the boundary, with exactly half its mass on each side. The only reason the outside portion counts as a *rov* is that the majority of a particular limb — say, the tail — lies outside, even though a minority of the overall animal is equally distributed. The question then is how we assess *rov* in this context: do we evaluate the animal as a single undivided mass, or do we subdivide it into its constituent *evarim* and assess the majority of each limb separately?

The underlying conceptual tension is something like the difference between a popular vote and an electoral college system. Does the animal's crossing threshold get measured holistically — fifty-one percent of total mass — or limb by limb, where the majority of each *ever* determines whether that limb counts as having exited?

### Half by Limb Majority

The Gemara initially suggests that the answer should be obvious: surely we assess the animal limb by limb, and thus we follow the majority of each *ever* rather than the majority of the whole animal. But on reflection, the harder case emerges. What if the animal is positioned such that it is *precisely* half on each side — neither side can claim a true majority of the overall mass — except that the majority of one particular limb happens to fall outside? In that scenario, the borderline between inside and outside cuts through the middle of a single limb, and the animal is otherwise exactly fifty-fifty. Does the majority of that one limb tip the legal balance and count the whole animal as having exited?

### Assessment Standard

The Gemara considers: do we say that the minority portion of that limb follows the majority of the limb it belongs to — *batar rov ever shadinan leh* — and therefore the animal is treated as having exited? Or do we assess the whole animal as a unit — *batar beheimah shadinan* — and since it is precisely fifty-fifty, we hold that it has not yet crossed the majority threshold?

### Teiku Case

This question is left unresolved — *teiku*. The case of exactly half the animal with the legal result turning on the majority of a single borderline limb has no conclusive answer within this sugya.

## Matni Lah Begavrei

### Workers Departure

Rabba bar Rav Huna offers a variant formulation of the majority question, but now applied not to the animal itself but to the people working on it. He teaches the rule *matni lah begavrei* — he frames it in terms of the workers. The case: five people are engaged in processing and transporting the sacrifice. Three of them depart from the *azarah*, while two remain inside.

### Workers vs. Animal

The question: do we follow the majority of the workers involved — *batar rov mit'askin azlinan* — so that since three of the five have exited, we treat the sacrifice as having legally departed? Or do we follow the status of the animal itself — *batar beheimah azlinan* — in which case, since the animal is still physically within the *azarah*, it has not yet achieved the status of *yotzei*? Rabba bar Rav Huna's framing implies a willingness to consider the sacrifice as *yotzei* even while the animal remains inside, based solely on the departure of a majority of those handling it — a striking and perhaps counterintuitive proposition.

### Teiku Dispute

This too is left unresolved — *teiku*. Whether the legal assessment of departure follows the workers or the animal's physical location receives no definitive answer.

## Yatzeu V'Chazaru

### Return Scenario

Rabbi Elazar poses another question: what happens in the case of *parim use'irim hanisrafim* that left the boundary and then returned — *sheyatzu vechazaru*? They exited, and then someone brought them back inside. What is their legal status at that point?

### Contamination Trigger

The question has two layers. First: does the act of leaving suffice to cause *metamei begadim* — that is, do the handlers who were involved in bringing the sacrifice outside now carry *tumah* that impurifies their clothing? And second: does the departure of the sacrifice itself give it the status of an *av hatumah* such that it becomes a source of *tumah* upon return? The fact that it was able to return — *de'i hadar hadar* — might indicate that the departure was not legally complete, or it might be entirely irrelevant once the threshold of departure was crossed. This question likewise does not receive an immediate answer within the sugya.

## Carrying on Poles

### Front Exit Scenario

Rabbi Abba bar Memel introduces a case intended to shed light on these questions. The sacrifice is being carried on wooden poles — *bemotot* — by four workers, two in front and two behind. As they carry the animal toward the exit of the *azarah*, the two front carriers cross the boundary of the *azarah* wall while the two rear carriers remain inside.

### Person vs. Animal

The ruling in this case is explicit: *harishonim sheyatzu chutz lechomat ha'azarah metamei begadim* — the front carriers who exited become *tamei* to the degree that they impurify their clothing. *Veha'acharonim ein metamei begadim ad sheyatzu* — the rear carriers who remain inside do not become *metamei begadim* until they themselves exit. The animal, meanwhile, is still in process — partially inside, partially outside — and yet the legal distinction applies entirely based on whether the individual person has crossed the boundary.

### Proof from Mishna

This ruling appears to support the position that the assessment follows the person, not the animal. If the legal consequence of *metamei begadim* depended on the animal having departed, then the rear carriers — who are also handling the same animal — should also become *tamei* once the front carriers cross the boundary with it. Yet they do not. The implication seems to be: we go by whether *the person* has exited, not by whether the animal has.

### Ravina's Answer

Ravina, however, pushes back on this inference. He argues that the reason the rear carriers are not yet *metamei begadim* is not because we assess by the person rather than the animal. Rather, it is because the *pasuk* explicitly requires *ve'achar yavo el hamachaneh* — afterward one shall enter the camp — meaning the *tumah* is triggered by the act of personally departing and then re-entering. If you have not yet left the *machaneh*, there is no applicable verse to make you *tamei*, regardless of what the animal has done. This is a textual criterion, not a statement about whether we track the animal or the workers.

One might then ask: does this resolve Rabba bar Rav Huna's *teiku* about the five workers? Ravina's answer addresses a different dimension — whether the person must personally exit — but Rabba bar Rav Huna's question was about whether a *majority* of workers having exited could legally stand in for the animal's departure. Those remain distinct questions, and Ravina's answer does not collapse them. The case of three workers out of five is not a question about whether those workers individually became *tamei* — obviously they did, having exited — but whether their majority departure confers *yotzei* status on the sacrifice for other legal purposes.

Ravina then clarifies the proper framing of Rabbi Elazar's original question about *yatzu vechazaru*. He suggests the case must be reconfigured: *kegon denakit leh bechulveta* — that the *kohanim* were positioned entirely outside the *azarah* and lifted the sacrifice out using a long implement, a kind of spear or hook. In this scenario, the handlers themselves were never inside, and therefore the verse's requirement of personally exiting and re-entering cannot apply to them. The question then becomes: is there any way in this configuration to ever achieve the status of *tamei*? It sounds as though, if done this way, the handlers might entirely avoid becoming *tamei* — a clever procedural workaround, even if somewhat impractical.

## Metamei Begadim Rules

### Types of Offerings

A new *baraita* is introduced: *parim ufarah useir hamishtaleach*. The *baraita* addresses three categories: the *parim hanisrafim* and *se'irim hanisrafim* — the bulls and goats burned outside; the *parah adumah* — the red heifer; and the *seir hamishtaleach* — the scapegoat sent to *Azazel* on *Yom Kippur*.

### Handler Impurity

The rule: *hamishale'ach vehasorfan vehamotzi'an metamei begadim* — the one who sends off the scapegoat, those who burn the offerings, and those who carry them out, all become *tamei* to the degree that they impurify their clothing. The act of processing these offerings confers upon the handler a high enough degree of *tumah* that even his garments become *tamei*.

### Animal Status Lower

A striking detail follows: *ve'hein atzman ein metamei'im begadim* — the animals themselves do not impurify clothing. That is, if a garment merely makes contact with the carcass of one of these animals, the garment does not become *tamei* to the degree of *metamei begadim*. The source of the *tumah* that rises to the level of *av hatumah* is the *person* involved in the process, not the animal itself. The animal occupies a lower rung in the *tumah* hierarchy.

### Food Impurity Only

*Aval metamei'im ochlin umashkin* — but the animals themselves can impurify foods and liquids. This places them at the level of *rishon l'tumah* in the food domain: they can make *ochlin* and *mashkin tamei* by contact, but they cannot impurify persons or vessels in the manner of an *av hatumah*. The person doing the work is the *av hatumah*; the animal is merely a *rishon*.

### Why Impurity Here

One might ask: why do these particular offerings convey *tumah* to the handlers at all? After all, these animals were properly slaughtered or processed — they are not *neveilah* in the ordinary sense. The *parim hanisrafim* and *se'irim hanisrafim* were slaughtered within the Temple; the *parah adumah* was slaughtered outside at *Har HaZeitim*; the *seir hamishtaleach* was pushed alive off a cliff and never slaughtered at all. None of them became *tamei* through the normal avenue of *neveilah*.

The answer lies in the nature of these *korbanot*. All of these offerings share the designation of *chatat*. The *chatat* is intrinsically bound up with the removal and absorption of *tumah* and sin. If one thinks in concrete terms about the mechanism of these rituals, these animals really are carrying a concentrated load of *tumah* — whether it is the *tumah* of *chet* borne by the *parim* and *se'irim* of *Yom Kippur*, the *tumah* of death that the *parah adumah* counteracts, or the sins explicitly loaded onto the head of the *seir hamishtaleach* before it is sent to *Azazel*. The Torah prescribes that involvement in transporting and processing this concentration of *tumah* makes the person himself *tamei* at the level of *metamei begadim*. This is not a consequence of touching a dead animal; it is a consequence of being the agent of this particular *avodah*.

## Derasha on Receptivity

### Meir's Rule

The Gemara now examines the doctrinal underpinning for this structure of *tumah* according to Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir relies on the *drasha* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael, derived from the verse *al kol zera zarua* — concerning all planted seed. The *drasha* reads: just as seeds and foods cannot become *tamei* at the level of *tumah chamurah* — they can never become an *av hatumah* — and therefore they require *hechsher*, they must be wetted by liquid before they can become susceptible to *tumah* at all, so too anything else that can never become an *av hatumah* also requires *hechsher* before it can become *tamei* or transmit *tumah* to other things.

The logic of the *drasha* is a *mah matzinu*: since the paradigmatic case of *ochlin* that can only ever reach *rishon l'tumah* requires prior wetting to enter the *tumah* system, anything else similarly capped at below *av hatumah* also requires that prior wetting. This becomes a general hermeneutic rule: the *hechsher* requirement tracks the ceiling of *tumah* that something can reach.

### Kosher Bird Exception

The one case explicitly excluded from this rule is *neveilat of tahor* — the carcass of a kosher bird that was not properly slaughtered. Such a carcass is *metamei b'veit hablia'ah* — it conveys *tumah* specifically in the throat, when someone swallows a *kezayit* of it. This is one of the most sui generis cases in all of *hilchot tumah*. The person who swallows it becomes *tamei* to a degree that makes his clothing *tamei* — that is, he becomes an *av hatumah*.

The *neveilat of tahor* is therefore excluded from the *hechsher* requirement because *sofah litamei tumah chamurah* — ultimately it does produce *tumah chamurah*, it does yield an *av hatumah* (namely, the person who swallows it). Since that ceiling of *tumah chamura* is reachable, the *neveilat of tahor* does not need prior *hechsher* to enter the *tumah* system. The capacity for *tumah chamurah* is the criterion, and this carcass meets it.

## Rabbanan Position

### Consistency Challenge

The Gemara challenges the *Rabbanan* who reject Rabbi Meir's *drasha*. If they do not accept the hermeneutic of *tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael*, then they face a consistency problem. On one hand, they would seem obligated to say that even the *seir hamishtaleach* should require *hechsher* from an external source of *tumah* before it can transmit *tumah* — since it is a live goat that can never become an *av hatumah* in the normal sense. On the other hand, if they dispense with the *drasha* entirely, why should the *parah adumah* and *parim* be *metamei ochlin umashkin* at all, given that they also have not yet been contacted by an external *tumah* source?

### Rav Dimi's Answer

When Rav Dimi arrived from *Eretz Yisrael* to *Bavel*, he conveyed the teaching of the western academies: *tzrichin hechsher tumah mimakom acher* — these offerings require *tumah* from an outside source in order to then transmit *tumah* to other things. The *Rabbanan*'s position is that the *parim* and *parah*, in order to be *metamei ochlin umashkin*, must first have become *tamei* themselves from some external source — be it contact with a *sheretz*, a *neveilah*, or something similar. Their capacity to then make *ochlin* and *mashkin tamei* is derivative of their having first received *tumah* from outside.

### Meat Status Difference

Rashi explains the underlying distinction. The *parim* and *parah* are dead animals — they are meat. And meat, as a category of food (*ochlin*), is susceptible to *tumah* in principle once it has been contacted by a *tumah* source. So their capacity to be *metamei ochlin umashkin* is not intrinsic to their status as these specific *korbanot*; it is a consequence of their being beef — flesh in the category of food — that happens to have received *tumah* from an external source. They can then pass that *tumah* along to other foods and liquids in the normal way that foods do.

The *seir hamishtaleach*, by contrast, is a *ba'al chaim* — a living animal. A living animal does not belong to the *tumah* system in the same way as meat. It has no inherent susceptibility to *tumah* as an *ochel*, because it is not food. Therefore, the *seir hamishtaleach* itself cannot be *metamei* other things even if it contacts a *tumah* source — it simply is not in the relevant legal category. The *hamishale'ach*, the person who escorts it, does become *tamei*, but that is because of the *avodah* itself and the *pasuk* that prescribes it, not because the goat conveyed *tumah* to him.

## Neveilat Of Tahor

### Inside Impurity Status

Rabbi Elazar poses another question in this vein: do the *parim hanisrafim* and *se'irim hanisrafim* have the capacity to be *metamei ochlin umashkin bifnim* — even while they are still inside the *azarah*, before they have been taken outside? The operative question is whether the incomplete stage of processing — the fact that they have not yet been removed — prevents them from functioning as a *tumah* source even at the lower level of *metamei ochlin umashkin*.

### Incomplete = No Impurity

Rabbi Elazar then answers his own question: *mechusar yetzia kimechusar ma'aseh dami* — the fact that the offering has not yet completed the stage of departure is treated like the absence of a required act. Since the offering is still missing its essential processing stage — *yetzia* — it is as though the process is incomplete, and therefore it does not yet have the capacity to transmit *tumah* even to foods and liquids. The incompleteness of the *yetzia* is not merely technical; it forestalls the legal effect.

### Parallel Case

This resolution prompts an analogy. Rabbi Abba bar Shmuel asked Rabbi Chiya bar Abba: what is the rule for *neveilat of tahor* according to Rabbi Meir — at what minimum *shiur* does it convey *tumah*? The question is whether the *shiur* should be *kezayit*, as for regular *neveilah*, or *kabeitza*, as for *ochlin* that have become *tamei* secondarily. The parallel question: does the *neveilat of tahor*, since it only becomes *metamei* through swallowing (*beit hablia*), perhaps have rules that track the *ochlin* system rather than the *neveilah* system?

The question is then refined. If the bird is *demachta le'ara* — lying on the ground — there is no question, because there is no risk of *tumah* at all: the bird becomes *metamei* only through being swallowed, not through touch or carriage. Equally, if the bird is already *bepumei* — in the person's mouth — there is no question about the *shiur*, because at that point whatever other food is also in the mouth becomes *tamei* by contact, and those foods then make the person *tamei*, regardless of *shiur*. The hard case is *denakit leh bidei* — when the person has picked it up in his hand and is on the way to eating it.

## Bird Carcass Rules

### Timing of Impurity

The difficult question concerns the moment at which the *neveilat of tahor* becomes legally operative as a *tumah* source according to Rabbi Meir. When the bird is still on the ground — on the plate, so to speak — everyone agrees it conveys no *tumah*. When it is already being swallowed, everyone agrees it does. The question is the intermediate stage: what is the status of the bird when it has been picked up in hand and is about to be eaten?

### Stages of Impurity

If the bird is still on the ground, the question cannot even arise — there is not yet any *tumah* in play. If the bird is already in the mouth, the answer is clear in a different direction: the other food present in the mouth becomes *tamei* by contact, and that secondary *tumah* then makes the person *tamei*. The unresolved case is the hand: having picked up the bird with the clear intention to eat it, does any aspect of the *neveilat of tahor*'s *tumah* already apply?

### Incomplete Action

The question is framed as: *mechusar kreiva kimechusar ma'aseh dami o lo* — is the fact that the eating has not yet been completed analogous to an incomplete action (*mechusar ma'aseh*), such that it blocks the *tumah* from taking effect? Or is *mechusar kreiva* — the mere absence of the final approach to the mouth — not comparable to *mechusar ma'aseh*?

Abaaye resolves the question: *mechusar kreiva lav kimechusar ma'aseh dami* — the incompleteness of the *kreiva*, the final approach to swallowing, is *not* comparable to a missing act. This means, according to Rabbi Meir's position, that the bird in hand already has some operative *tumah* status. The reasoning connects to Rabbi Meir's general *drasha*: since the *neveilat of tahor* is *sofah litamei tumah chamurah* — its trajectory culminates in producing *tumah chamurah* — that capacity is not suspended merely because the final act of swallowing has not yet occurred. Rabbi Meir does not require the act to be complete before some aspects of the *tumah* scheme engage.

This is a striking and somewhat troubling position. The entire point of the *neveilat of tahor* rule is that *tumah* is conveyed only *beit hablia'ah* — specifically through swallowing. To then say that picking it up already activates some prior-stage *tumah* seems to undercut that premise. One might argue that since the person is in the process of doing something they are not supposed to do — eating *neveilah* — perhaps the *tumah* should only attach once the sin is actually committed, not in anticipation of it. But Rabbi Meir appears to hold that once the trajectory toward *tumah chamurah* is underway, the intermediate stage already falls within the orbit of that eventual *tumah* effect. It is also worth noting that this analysis concerns a rejected opinion being applied analogically to another case — an *amora* inferring what Rabbi Meir's rejected *shita* might say about a case where we also would not rule that way. There is no practical *halachic* difference on either side.

### Thirteen Rules Summary

A new *baraita* is introduced: *shelosha asar devarim ne'emru beneveilat of tahor* — thirteen *halachot* were stated about the carcass of the kosher bird, and this is one of them. The thirteen include, among others: *tzericha machshava* — it requires *machshava*, intent that the bird is being treated as food; *ve'einah tzericha hechsher* — it does not require *hechsher*, prior wetting with liquid; and *umetamei tumat ochlin bekabeitza* — it conveys *tumah* to other foods at the *shiur* of a *kabeitza*, the size of an egg.

The question arises: whose position does this *baraita* reflect? The middle clause — *metamei tumat ochlin bekabeitza* — which describes the bird as *metamei ochlin* even before swallowing, seems to align with Rabbi Meir's position. But it is claimed that it could equally be the position of the *Rabbanan*. The opening clause — *tzericha machshava ve'einah tzericha hechsher* — is however specifically the hermeneutic of Rabbi Meir, who holds that things which are *sofan litamei tumah chamurah* do not require *hechsher*. So the opening aligns with Rabbi Meir.

### Baraita Structure

This creates a structural puzzle: the *reisha* of the *baraita* reflects Rabbi Meir's position, the *seifa* also reflects Rabbi Meir (*shechita umelikatah metaheret terefatah mitumatah* — slaughter or *melika* removes the *neveilah* status from a *tereifa* — is specifically Rabbi Meir's view), and yet the middle clause appears to represent the *Rabbanan*. The Gemara is compelled to accept: *reisha veseifa Rabbi Meir umitziata Rabbanan* — the opening and closing of this *baraita* are Rabbi Meir's position, while the middle section represents the *Rabbanan*. This is an unusual and somewhat convoluted structure, and the Gemara arrives at it only because it has read each clause with strict fidelity to its attributed opinion.

## Rishon V'Sheni Rules

### Counting Levels

Rav Hamnuna addresses Rabbi Zeira directly and with some urgency: do not sit down until you explain this to me. The question: according to Rabbi Meir, do we count *rishon vesheni* — first and second degree *tumah* — for *neveilat of tahor*? That is, if the *neveilat of tahor* is an *av hatumah* that makes someone who swallows it into an *av hatumah*, does that person then transmit *tumah* to things he touches, creating a *rishon*, and does that *rishon* then create a *sheni*, in the ordinary cascading fashion?

### Contact Rule

Rabbi Zeira's answer establishes a general principle: *kol heicha demetamei adam bemaga, monin bo rishon vesheni* — wherever an *av hatumah* can make a person *tamei* by physical contact, we count outward from that *av* through the normal chain of *rishon vesheni*. The chain of *tumah* levels is grounded in and patterned after the *sheretz*, which is the paradigmatic *av hatumah* that conveys *tumah* by contact: you touch it, you become *tamei*; the vessel you then touch becomes *rishon*; the food that vessel touches becomes *sheni*.

### Sui Generis Status

*Neveilat of tahor*, however, does not impurify a person *bemaga* — by contact or carrying. It only conveys *tumah bevet hablia'ah* — through swallowing. Since the mechanism of *tumah* transmission is entirely different from the contact-based paradigm, the normal framework of counting *rishon vesheni* simply does not apply. The *neveilat of tahor* is genuinely *sui generis* in the *tumah* system, operating under its own rules, and the cascading *tumah* levels that flow from every other *av hatumah* do not cascade from it in the same way.

## Chibur Ochlin

### Connection Levels

Rabbi Zeira then poses a question to Rabbi Ami bar Chiyya — or, in another version, to Rabbi Avin bar Kahana — based on a *mishna* concerning *chiburei ochlin al yedei mashkin*. The case: two foods are connected by liquid — the same liquid touches both of them. The rule from the *mishna* is that this liquid connection counts as a *chibur* for *tumah kala*, light *tumah*, but does not count as a *chibur* for *tumah chamura*, severe *tumah*. That is: if a secondary source of *tumah* — a *vlad hatumah* — touches one food, the second food connected to it by liquid will also become *tamei*, because the connection is sufficient for purposes of this lesser *tumah*. But if an *av hatumah* touches one food, making it a *rishon*, the second food connected by liquid does not automatically become *tamei* at a *rishon* level — the liquid connection is insufficient for the purpose of the more severe degree of *tumah*.

This is somewhat counterintuitive. One would expect that the more powerful the *tumah* source, the more readily it would spread through any connection. Yet the opposite appears: for the lesser *tumah*, the liquid connection counts; for the more severe *tumah*, it does not. The resolution is that the lower levels of *tumah* in this domain are in many cases *derabbanan*, and the Sages were willing to extend *tumah* through the liquid connection for purposes of their own protective *gezeirot* without demanding the same stringency of physical connection that *tumah chamura* requires.

### Counting Question

The question Rabbi Zeira poses: in a case where the liquid connection counts only for *tumah kala* and not for *tumah chamura* — do we then count *rishon vesheni* from the connected food? That is, the second food became *tamei* through the connection, but only at the level of *tumah kala*. Can that food then go on to transmit *tumah* one further step down the chain — making something else *tamei* as a *sheni* — or does the unusual pathway by which it became *tamei* preclude the normal counting of *rishon vesheni*?

### General Principle

The answer again invokes Rav Hamnuna's principle: *kol heicha demetamei adam, monin bo rishon vesheni* — wherever the underlying *av hatumah* is of a type that can make a person *tamei* by contact, we count *rishon vesheni* from what that *av* touches. *Ein metamei'in adam* — if the *av* in question cannot make a person *tamei* by contact, then we do not count *rishon vesheni* from what it touches. The capacity of the originating *av hatumah* to impurify persons by contact is the threshold criterion. Where that capacity is present, the full cascade of *rishon vesheni* follows; where it is absent, the chain does not extend in the same manner.
