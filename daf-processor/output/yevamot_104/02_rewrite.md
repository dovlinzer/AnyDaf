# Yevamot 104 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sandal & Tzara'at

### Bayit Hamusgar

Today's daf is 104, and we pick up somewhere in the middle of 103b, where the *gemara* raises a challenge — *meitivei* — concerning the status of a *bayit hamusgar*, a sealed house whose *tzara'at* status is being determined. The broader question under discussion is whether a sandal that has been afflicted with *tzara'at* may be used for *chalitza*. The relevant distinction is between *musgar* — a house or object whose status is being evaluated but has not yet been finalized — and *muchlat* — one whose status has been finalized and which is destined for destruction.

The question is whether something destined for destruction can still function as a meaningful halachic object. There is no per se quantitative *shiur* at stake, but the concern is whether the sandal retains its identity as a shoe. Can it be considered a significant shoe if it is slated for destruction?

### Kitutei Mechatat

This brings us to the principle of *kitutei mechatat shiurei* — the idea that something destined for destruction has already, in a conceptual sense, lost its identity as a functional object. It is not merely that a quantitative measure has been diminished, but that the very status of the object as a coherent, purposeful thing has been undermined. The *gemara* will use this principle repeatedly, and it is important to keep it clearly in view.

### Rav Pappi/Pappa

The practical question is framed through two competing traditions in the name of *Rava*. *Rav Pappi* said in *Rava*'s name that a *sandal muchlat* is not valid for *chalitza*, while *Rav Pappa* said in *Rava*'s name that it is valid. The *gemara* then attempts to challenge and resolve these positions through a series of cases from the laws of *tzara'at*.

The *gemara* challenges: a *bayit hamusgar*, once sealed, is *tamei* from the inside; a *bayit muchlat*, once its status is finalized and it is destined for demolition, conveys *tumah* from both inside and outside, and even causes *tumah* simply by entering — as the *pasuk* says *haba el habayit*, one who enters the house. If we apply *kitutei mechatat shiurei* — if the house scheduled for destruction is as if already ground up — how can it still function as a house for the purpose of the *tumah* of entry? Should it not lose its identity as a house, just as we are suggesting it loses its identity as a shoe?

### House Identity

The *gemara* answers: *shani hacha damar kra* — here the verse itself is different, because it says *venatatz et habayit*, "you shall demolish the house." The *Torah* teaches that even in the act of demolition, the structure is still called a *bayit*. The *Torah* explicitly preserves the object's identity within the framework of *tzara'at* law, even as it is being destroyed. This, however, is an internal ruling confined to the laws of *tzara'at*. When one wishes to apply the object's identity to some other *halachic* context — such as using it for *chalitza* — that is an entirely different matter.

## Matlit & Kezayit

### Matlit Garment

The *gemara* then says *tashma* — come and hear a further challenge. If a piece of cloth (*matlit*) is three finger-widths by three finger-widths — the minimum size that defines it as a *beged* — and it is a garment afflicted with *tzara'at*, it conveys *tumah* to a pure house when brought inside. Crucially, this holds even if the garment does not have the volume of a *kezayit*, a detail that will become significant momentarily.

### Majority Entry

The operative rule: *keivan shenichnas rubo leveit tahor tim'ahu* — once the majority of the garment has entered a pure house, it has rendered that house *tamei*. The majority entering is treated as if the whole object entered. This implies that the garment retains its identity as a unified *beged*. As *Rashi* explains, the *gemara* is not suggesting that a *muchlat* object loses its *shiur* and therefore fails to cause *tumah* — obviously, if that were true, nothing with *tzara'at* could ever cause *tumah*, since we would always say it has no *shiur*. Rather, the argument is about *coherence of identity* as a unified object: we said that something whose *shiur* is compromised loses its identity as a coherent object. Yet here, even a fractional majority is treated as representing the whole.

### Muchlat Question

The *gemara* asks: are we not discussing a *muchletet* — a garment that is *muchlat* and destined for destruction? And yet it retains sufficient identity to cause *tumah*? The *gemara* answers: *lo* — *musgeret*. No, we are actually discussing a *musgar* garment, one whose status is still being evaluated, not yet finalized for destruction. So this does not prove anything about a *muchlat* object.

*If* that is so, says the *gemara*, *eima seifa* — let us look at the end of this *braita*.

### Seifa Analysis

The *seifa* discusses a thick garment whose volume amounts to several *zeitim* of material. The ruling there is: *keivan shenichnas mimenu kezayit* — once a *kezayit* worth of the garment enters the house, even if it is not a three-by-three piece, it renders the house *tamei*. Why would a *kezayit* be the operative measure for a garment? Normally, *kezayit* is not a relevant measure for cloth at all.

### Meit Parallel

The *gemara* explains: *ei amrat bishlama muchletet* — if we are indeed discussing a *muchlat* garment, this makes sense. *Hainu de-itkash le-meit* — it is because a *metzora* is compared to a dead person, as Moshe prayed for Miriam: *al na tehi ke-meit asher bezeito me-rechem imo*. The comparison to a *meit* brings the measure of *kezayit* into play, since a *kezayit* of a corpse conveys *tumah*. But this *itkash la-meit* — this comparison to a *meit* — only applies in the *muchlat* state. If we were discussing a *musgar* garment, there would be no basis for the *kezayit* measure. Therefore, the *seifa* must be discussing a *muchlat* garment.

And yet, even in the *muchlat* state, the garment retains a coherent identity such that a majority entering is treated as the whole entering. So the challenge to *kitutei mechatat shiurei* remains.

## Object Identity

### Shani Hataim

The *gemara* responds with the same logic it used for the house: *shani hatam de-amar kra ve-saraf et habeged* — the *Torah* says "and he shall burn the garment," teaching that *afilu bish'at sreifa karui beged* — even in the moment of its burning, it is still called a *beged*. Just as the *Torah* explicitly preserves the house's identity as a house throughout the process of its destruction, it preserves the garment's identity as a *beged* throughout the process of its destruction. This preservation of identity, however, operates within the laws of *tzara'at* internally — it does not follow that the object carries its identity into other domains of *halacha*.

### Issur Tumah

The *gemara* then raises an obvious question: *ve-nigmor mineh* — why not derive from this that the *sandal muchlat* retains its identity for *chalitza* as well? Since within *tzara'at* we see the object is still called by its name even when destined for destruction, let us apply that to *chalitza*!

The *gemara* answers: *issur mi-tumah lo gamrinan* — we do not derive *issur* rulings from *tumah* laws. As *Rashi* explains, the entire category of *hilchot tumah* is a *chidush* — it operates by its own internal logic, which is somewhat arbitrary and not necessarily generalizable to other *halachic* domains. The preservation of identity within *tzara'at* is internal to those laws; it does not flow outward into other categories.

### Rava Hilchasa

This gives us the framework for the two versions of *Rava*. The position that *bedieved* the *sandal muchlat* is not valid — *Rav Pappi*'s version — holds that we do not learn *issur* from *tumah*, and therefore the preservation of identity within *tzara'at* cannot be applied to *chalitza*. The position that *bedieved* it is valid — *Rav Pappa*'s version — holds that once we see the object retains its identity within *tzara'at*, it retains its identity for other *halachic* purposes as well.

What is striking is that the *gemara* now quotes *Rava* directly: *amar Rava hilchasa* — the ruling is that both a sandal that is *muskav* (set aside) and one that is *muklat* (afflicted with *tzara'at* and destined for destruction) are valid for *chalitza*. This corresponds to *Rav Pappa*'s version. One cannot help but note the irony: if *Rava* himself ruled explicitly, why did we need two competing traditions transmitted in his name?

## Avodah Zarah

### AZ Distinctions

*Rava* continues: *ve'echad sandal hamuklat ve'echad sandal shel avodat kochavim* — and even a sandal used as an object of idolatrous worship, *lo tachlotz* — one should not use it *lechatchila*, but *ve'im chaltzah chalitzatah kesherah* — if one did use it *bedieved*, the *chalitza* is valid.

### Ir Nidachas

However, *she-teikroves avodat kochavim* — if the sandal was offered as a sacrifice to an idol rather than being worshipped directly — or if it belonged to an *ir nidachas*, a city condemned to complete destruction — *lo tachlotz*, and *ve'im chaltzah chalitzatah pesulah* — even *bedieved* it is invalid. For an *ir nidachas*, this is because *Rava* acknowledges that *kitutei mechatat shiurei* applies when something is genuinely destined for total destruction outside the special framework of *tzara'at*.

### Zakein Issue

The *gemara* then introduces the case of *zakein asui lichvodo* — a euphemism for a shroud shoe, a shoe prepared specifically to be placed on a corpse and not made for actual walking. Such a shoe, *Rava* rules, is invalid even *bedieved*. Why? The obvious answer: *delav lehilocha avid* — it is not made for walking. It does not qualify as a shoe in the functional sense required for *chalitza*.

Before proceeding to the challenge this ruling raises, it is worth pausing to appreciate a remarkable detail that emerges here. It becomes clear that already in the *talmudic* era, the *beit din* maintained a dedicated *chalitza* shoe — a special shoe set aside specifically for performing *chalitza*. This is far from the straightforward scenario in which two people simply appear before a court wearing their ordinary shoes. The existence of a communal *beit din* shoe already reflects an institutional practice of considerable sophistication.

What is the difference between *avodat kochavim* and *tikroves avodat kochavim* — why is one valid *bedieved* and the other not? *Rava* offers no explicit distinction in the text, so *Rashi* supplies one: a sandal used as an object of *avodah zarah* by a non-Jew could potentially have its status annulled — a non-Jew can be *mevatel* it by renouncing belief in it, after which it ceases to be *avodah zarah*. Because it has the potential of not being destroyed, we do not fully apply the principle of *kitutei mechatat shiurei*. *Tosafot* disagrees, understanding the case as referring to *avodah zarah* that has already been annulled, and argues on the basis of a related *sugya*. *Tosafot* further raises the question of whether using an object associated with *avodah zarah* is problematic for a *mitzvah* object — drawing a comparison to the *shofar* of *avodah zarah* — and treats the *chalitza* shoe in a similar conceptual category.

*Rashi*, by contrast, invokes the principle of *mitzvot lav leihanos nitnu* — the fulfillment of *mitzvot* does not constitute *hana'ah* — to explain why using an *avodah zarah* sandal for *chalitza* would not violate the prohibition against deriving benefit from *avodah zarah*. This framing is itself significant: *Rashi* is conceptualizing *chalitza* primarily as a *kiyum mitzvah*, a ritual act, rather than as a quasi-transactional act of releasing the woman. Whether *chalitza* is more like a *mitzvah* or more like a legal transaction — analogous to *get* — has real *halachic* implications, and *Rashi*'s deployment of *mitzvot lav leihanos nitnu* reveals his position clearly.

### Rav Papa Q

*Rav Papa* poses a challenge to *Abaye*: what distinguishes the shroud shoe? Even granting that *delav lehilocha avid* — it is not made for walking — the same would seem to be true of the *beit din*'s *chalitza* shoe. Who walks around in the *beit din*'s special *chalitza* shoe? It too is kept exclusively for the performance of *chalitza* and is never used for ordinary walking.

### Beit Din Shoe

The *gemara* responds: *ilu mash'il lei shulcha debei dina mi kapid alei dayana?* — if the court's attendant wanted to borrow the *chalitza* shoe, would the judges object? Of course not. The *beit din* shoe is available in principle for ordinary use; it simply is not used that way in practice. The shroud shoe, by contrast, is intrinsically defined by its purpose of accompanying a corpse — it is not made for walking by design, not merely by convention. The answer is logically coherent, though one cannot help noting the irony: the *gemara* is essentially defending the validity of the *beit din*'s *chalitza* shoe on the grounds that, in theory, anyone could borrow it for a stroll.

## Mishna Chalitza

### Night & Left

The *Mishna* teaches: *chaltzah balaylah* — if she performed *chalitza* at night, *chalitzatah kesheira* — it is valid. *Rabbi Eliezer posul* — Rabbi Eliezer rules it invalid. Similarly, if the *chalitza* was performed on his left foot, *chalitzatah pesulah* — it is invalid; *Rabbi Eliezer machshir* — Rabbi Eliezer rules it valid.

The *gemara* proposes that the dispute about whether *chalitza* may be performed at night turns on the question of whether we link court proceedings to the laws of *negaim*. The *pasuk* says *al pihem yihyeh kol riv vechol nega* — on their word shall be every litigation and every *nega*. Since *tzara'at* declarations by the *kohen* must be made by day — *bayom hira'os bo hakohen yitma* — one might extend this requirement to court proceedings as well.

The *gemara* immediately rejects a sweeping application of this link: *d'chulei alma lo makshinan rivim lenegaim* — everyone agrees we do not make a global equation between litigation and *negaim*. Were we to do so fully, we could not even conclude a court case at night, which contradicts established *halacha*: while *dinei nefashot* must begin and be conducted entirely in the daytime, *dinei mamonot* may continue into the night once proceedings have begun.

The debate is then reframed: *mar savar chalitza k'techilas din damya* — one holds that *chalitza* is analogous to the beginning of a court proceeding, while *umar savar chalitza k'gmar din damya* — the other holds it is analogous to the conclusion of proceedings. The *gemara* itself provides no explanation of this distinction. One might suggest that viewing *chalitza* as the *gmar din* is natural because, like a verdict, it is the moment at which the legal change of status is effected — it is simultaneously the beginning and end of the proceeding, and the act itself produces the definitive outcome.

The question of whether *chalitza* is more like a *mitzvah* or more like a *din* proves directly relevant here. *Rashi* notes that *chalitza* has a *din* element because the woman collects her *ketubah* as a result. More broadly, framing *chalitza* as a *din* explains why it would be placed in the category of *riv* at all, and the *machloket* over whether it is the beginning or end of that *din* flows naturally from there.

One might have thought to derive the requirement of daytime from the word *le-einei*, since the witnesses must see the act — but this cannot be the source, as we have seen that even a *suma* poses complications, and the *gemara* does not pursue that derivation.

The narrative then introduces *Rabba bar Chiya Katuspa'ah*, who performed a *chalitza* using a *mok* — a hard felt shoe, previously established as technically valid though not ideal — alone (*bi-yechidi*), without a *beit din*, and at night. *Shmuel* responded with what *Rashi* reads as sarcasm: "How great is this man," meaning: look how freely he relies on minority opinions. The *gemara* analyzes which element actually troubled *Shmuel*. The *mok* was established as valid by an anonymous *braita*, not a minority view. The nighttime *chalitza* corresponds to the *stam* position of our *Mishna*, which overrides *Rabbi Eliezer*'s lone dissent. The genuinely troubling element, says the *gemara*, is the absence of a *beit din* — *yechidai katani lah* — that position is sourced in a minority opinion.

The *Mishna* on the following *amud* records that *chalitza* performed before only two judges, or before three where one turned out to be a relative or otherwise disqualified — effectively making it two — is invalid. *Rabbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Yochanan ha-Sandlar* validate it. And there is the famous case of one who performed *chalitza* privately in prison, before no court at all, which *Rabbi Akiva* nonetheless upheld. This is the *da'as yachid* upon which *Rabba bar Chiya* relied.

*Rav Yosef bar Minyumi* quoted *Rav Nachman*: *ein halacha ke-osah ha-zug* — we do not rule like *Rabbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Yochanan ha-Sandlar*, the pair who validated two-judge *chalitza*. Thus *Rabba bar Chiya* was going even further, relying on the even more isolated view of *Rabbi Akiva*. The *gemara* adds: and if you prefer, you could say that even all three leniencies — the *mok*, the nighttime, and the absence of a *beit din* — ultimately trace back to a single chain of minority tradition, as recorded in a *braita* reporting that *Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha* himself once performed *chalitza* using felt, alone, and at night. *Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yosi* reports this — characteristically, as he is consistently the tradent of eyewitness accounts throughout *hazal*. Whether this means the other two leniencies should now be revisited is a separate question for the *poskim*.

### Metzora Source

The *Mishna* teaches that *chalitza* performed on the left foot is invalid, and the *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama d'rabbanan?* What is the Rabbis' source for requiring the right foot when the *Torah* does not say "right" anywhere in the *chalitza* passage? *Ulla* answers: *yalfinan regel regel mimitzora* — we derive "foot" from "foot" by the *metzora*. In the purification ritual of the *metzora*, the text specifies the right foot explicitly. We learn from there to here: *mah lehalan yamin af kan yamin*.

### Position Reversal

*Rabbi Eliezer*, who validates left-foot *chalitza*, does not accept this *gezeira shava* from *metzora*. Yet a *braita* reports *Rabbi Eliezer* himself deriving "right ear" for the case of ear-piercing of a slave from the *metzora* — *ne'emar kan ozen ve-ne'emar lehallan ozen*, a *gezeira shava* of "ear" to "ear." If he accepts that *gezeira shava*, he might as well accept the one from *metzora* to *chalitza*. *Rabbi Yosef bar Yosef* said in the name of *Yochanan*: *muchlefet hashita* — reverse the positions, and restore consistency to *Rabbi Eliezer*.

### Rava Gezeira

*Rava* says: *le'olam lo tehipach* — do not reverse the positions. There is no need to. *Ozen ozen mufni* — the word "ear" appears redundantly in the slave passage, which makes it available to serve as one end of a *gezeira shava*. *Regel regel lo mufni* — the word "foot" does not appear redundantly in the *chalitza* passage, so *Rabbi Eliezer* has no available endpoint to anchor the *gezeira shava* from *metzora* to *chalitza*. Hence there is no inconsistency in *Rabbi Eliezer* accepting one *gezeira shava* but not the other.

### No Redundancy Q

The *gemara* presses further: *ve-chi lo mufni mai pircha ika?* — even granting that "foot" is not redundant by *chalitza*, what specific logical challenge would defeat the *gezeira shava*? After all, a *gezeira shava* need not have redundancy at both endpoints to function; the presence or absence of redundancy affects only whether it can withstand a challenge, not whether it can be made at all.

The answer: *mah le-metzora sheken ta'un erez eitz erez ve-eizov ve-shani tola'as* — *metzora* involves an elaborate ritual with cedar wood, hyssop, and crimson thread. Precisely because *metzora* is accompanied by such a detailed and demanding set of ritual criteria, its requirement of the right foot is contextually justified. *Chalitza* lacks these elaborate requirements, so one cannot assume its rules must mirror those of *metzora* in this respect. This challenge cannot be refuted, and therefore the *gezeira shava* from *metzora* to *chalitza* remains unavailable to *Rabbi Eliezer*.

This allows us to articulate a taxonomy of *gezeira shava* strength: the most robust form has redundancy at both endpoints and is immune to challenge; a somewhat weaker form has redundancy at one endpoint; the weakest has redundancy at neither endpoint, which means it may still be proposed but can be defeated by any contextual objection. This is precisely the structure the *gemara* is working with here.

## Components

### Chaltzah Verakeka

The *Mishna* now turns to the three components of *chalitza*: removal of the shoe, spitting, and recitation of the verses. *Chaltzah verakeka aval lo kara* — she removed the shoe and spat but did not recite — *chalitzatah kesheira*, it is valid. Recitation is not *me'akev*.

### No Removal

*Kara verakeka aval lo chaltza* — she recited and spat but did not remove the shoe — *chalitzatah pesulah*, it is invalid. The act of removal is the core of the entire proceeding; there is a reason it is called *chalitza*.

### No Spitting

*Chaltza vekara aval lo rakeka* — she removed the shoe and recited but did not spit — *Rabbi Eliezer omer chalitzatah pesulah*, invalid; *Rabbi Akiva omer chalitzatah kesheira*, valid. The debate concerns whether spitting is *me'akev*.

### Acts Essential

*Rabbi Eliezer*'s reasoning: *kkah yeaseh* — "thus shall be done" — means *kol davar shehu maaseh me'akev*, anything that is an act is indispensable. Spitting is an act; therefore it is *me'akev*. By the same logic, recitation is not an act in the operative sense, and therefore is not *me'akev*. This raises the philosophically rich question of whether *dibbur* constitutes *maaseh* — a question *Tosafot* engages with at length.

### To the Man

*Rabbi Akiva* responds: *misham raya?* — you want to bring proof from there? Read the verse carefully: *kacha yeaseh la-ish* — this shall be done *to the man*. *Kol davar shenaaseh ba-ish* — only what is done *to* the man is *me'akev*. Removal of the shoe is done to him. Spitting in front of him is not done to him — she spits before him, not upon him. Therefore spitting is not *me'akev*.

## Disqualified

### Cheresh

*Hacheresh she-nichlatz veha-chereshet she-chaltzah* — if a deaf-mute man had the *chalitza* performed, or a deaf-mute woman performed it, *chalitzatah pesulah* — it is invalid.

### Ketanah

*Ketanah she-chaltzah* — a minor girl who performed *chalitza* — *tachlotz mishetagdil*, she must redo it when she reaches adulthood. *Ve'im lo chaltzah chalitzatah pesulah* — if she does not redo it, it remains invalid.

### Court Size

*Chaltza bishnayim o bishloshoh ve-nimtza echad mehem karov o pasul* — if she performed *chalitza* before two, or before three where one proved to be a relative or otherwise disqualified, *chalitzatah pesulah* — it is invalid. *Rabbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Yochanan ha-Sandlar* validate such a *chalitza* after the fact.

### Private Case

*U-ma'aseh be-echad she-chalatz beino le-veinah be-veis ha-asurim* — there was an incident of a man who performed *chalitza* privately in prison, with no *beit din* present whatsoever — *va ma'aseh lifnei Rabbi Akiva ve-hichshir* — and when it came before *Rabbi Akiva*, he declared it valid. This is a striking ruling. The *psukim* repeatedly emphasize *le-einei ha-zekeinim* — in the presence of the elders — yet *Rabbi Akiva* validates *chalitza* with literally no court at all. *Rav Nachman* rules we do not follow this *da'as yachid*.

The situation of the *chereshet* raises a deeply troubling practical question. If the *yavam* is a *cheresh* and cannot have *chalitza* performed with him, and cannot do *yibum* properly either, what becomes of the *yevamah*? *Rashi* acknowledges explicitly that a *chereshet* who needs *chalitza* has no remedy through *chalitza* — *ein la takana* — and her only possible relief is *yibum*, and if they subsequently wish to separate, he may give her a *get*. The *halacha* does contemplate situations in which *chalitza* is simply impossible and *yibum* is the only path, even when that outcome is profoundly difficult. This follows from the structure of the *Torah*'s framework: because *yibum* remains an alternative, the impossibility of *chalitza* does not automatically release the woman from the *levirate* bond.

## Cheresh Analysis

### Ileim Intellect

The *gemara* now probes the grounds for the *cheresh*'s disqualification. *Rava* opens with the observation: since the *Mishna* unanimously agreed that *kriyah* — recitation — is not *me'akev*, the *cheresh*'s disability need not be the cause of disqualification. Therefore *Rava* reasons that a mute person — *ileim* — who is fully hearing and of full intellect, merely unable to speak, should be perfectly valid for *chalitza*. *Hashta de-amarta kri'ah lo me'akva, lefichach ileim ve-ilemet she-chaltzah chalitzatam kesherah* — since recitation is not *me'akev*, a mute person may perform *chalitza* and it is valid.

### Intellect Challenge

The *gemara* challenges: but then what is the reason the *cheresh* and *chereshet* are disqualified? *Mai ta'ama?* If it is because they cannot recite — *lav mishum delav benei kri'ah ninhu* — then a mute person would be equally disqualified by the same reasoning, since a mute person also cannot recite. So is the *cheresh*'s disqualification on account of the inability to recite, or on account of lacking full *da'at*?

The *gemara* answers: the *cheresh* is disqualified *mishum delav benei da'ah ninhu* — because they are not of full intellect, not because of the inability to speak. The category of *cheresh* in *halachic* discourse reflects the reality that, in a society without schools for the deaf, an individual who could neither hear nor speak often developed without acquiring the conceptual framework of normal social and legal life. This is distinct from a mute person who can hear — who processes language, understands the world, and simply cannot produce speech. *Ufumaihu hu de-che'av lehu* — their mouth causes them difficulty, not their minds.

### Recitation Ability

However, the *gemara* then raises a further challenge from the school of *Rabbi Yannai*: *lefi she-eino be-omar ve-omrah* — the *cheresh* and *chereshet* are disqualified precisely because they are not in the category of "he says and she says," meaning the inability to recite is itself the disqualifying factor, not the lack of *da'at*. This reading is actually more favorable to the dignity of the *cheresh* — it treats them as intellectually capable — but it has the practical consequence of excluding the *ileim* as well.

The *gemara* concedes the point: *ela ki itmar de-Rava aseifa itmar* — *Rava*'s statement must actually have been directed at the end of the *Mishna*, and his ruling is reversed. A mute person cannot perform valid *chalitza*, because although recitation is not *me'akev* as an act that must be performed, the person performing *chalitza* must at least have the capacity to recite. Even if the act is not required, the potential must exist.

### Potential Test

This brings us to the principle articulated by *Rabbi Zeira*: *kol ha-ra'uy le-bilah ein bilah me'akevet bo, ve-chol she-eino ra'uy le-bilah bilah me'akevet bo.* By the case of the *mincha*, the *Torah* describes the flour as *belulah ba-shemen* — mixed with oil. If one fails to mix it, *bedieved* the *mincha* is valid. But if it was impossible to mix — because the volume of flour was too great — the *mincha* is entirely invalid. The principle: one does not actually have to perform the act, but the act must be performable in principle.

### Mincha Parallel

*Tosafot* illuminates the conceptual basis: where the *Torah* frames a requirement as a descriptor — *belulah ba-shemen*, "mixed with oil," an adjective rather than a verbal command — we read it as characterizing the nature of the object rather than mandating a specific act. Therefore, it suffices that the object has the *potential* to exhibit that characteristic, even if the act is not performed. By contrast, where the *Torah* says *ve-amar ve-omrah* — "and he shall say and she shall say" — one might think this demands actual performance. *Rava* resolves this by reading the verbal form as a characterization of the *gavra*, the person: the participants must be of the type of person who can say — they must have the capacity, even if the act is waived. A mute person, lacking that capacity, is therefore excluded, even though the act of recitation itself is not *me'akev*.

## Spitting Power

### Spitting Alone

The *gemara* records a ruling sent to the father of *Shmuel*: *yevamah she-rakakah tachlotz* — a *yevamah* who spat alone, without performing any other component of *chalitza*, must subsequently perform full *chalitza*. The implication is that the mere spitting alone is enough to disqualify her from *yibum* with any of the brothers, leaving *chalitza* as her only path.

### Akiva Question

This raises an immediate question: whose opinion does this reflect? If it is *Rabbi Akiva*, who rules that spitting is not *me'akev* even when one is in the midst of performing *chalitza*, then spitting alone — performed outside the context of *chalitza* entirely — should carry even less weight. *Rabbi Akiva* does not even require spitting when everything else is being done properly. How then could spitting alone, stripped of any further act, be sufficient to disqualify her from *yibum*? Surely spitting should be entirely trivial for *Rabbi Akiva*.

### Two Acts

The *gemara* resolves: this must be *Rabbi Eliezer*'s view, for whom spitting is *me'akev*. But then a further difficulty arises: *Rabbi Eliezer* requires two acts — removal of the shoe and spitting. The principle of *shenei devarim ha-matirin* is that when two elements are jointly required to produce a permitted status, neither element alone carries any legal weight. If you have not done both, you have done nothing. How then does spitting alone carry enough weight to disqualify her?

### Eimurim

The *gemara* draws an analogy to the *emurim* — the internal sacrificial parts that must be burned on the altar before the rest of the animal may be consumed. If the *emurim* are lost or become *tamei* and cannot be burned, the meat may nonetheless be eaten. But if the *emurim* are present, the meat may not be eaten until they are burned. This suggests a nuanced position: when an element that would otherwise be *me'akev* is unavailable, its absence does not block the permitted act; but when it is present and available, it must be performed. By analogy, one might say that spitting, when present and possible, is *me'akev* within *chalitza* — yet its lone occurrence outside *chalitza* should still carry no weight, since the removal of the shoe has not been performed.

### Half Status

The *gemara*'s resolution invokes *Rabbi*'s position in an unrelated case of *kivsei atzeret* — the lambs brought on *Shavuot*. Sanctification of the accompanying loaves requires both the *shechita* and the *zerakat hadam* of the lambs, performed with proper intent. If the *shechita* was proper but the *zerakat hadam* was not, *Rabbi* rules *kadosh ve-eino kadosh* — it is half-consecrated. There exists an intermediate state of partial sanctification. *Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon* disagrees: it is either fully sanctified or not at all; there is no halfway.

Applied to our case: according to *Rabbi Eliezer*, *chalitza* requires two acts. By analogy to *Rabbi*'s view, if only one of the two required acts has been performed, the result is not zero — it is a partial state. The woman has done enough to be in an intermediate status: she has not completed *chalitza*, but she has done enough to sever the *yibum* bond. It is, in *Rabbi*'s terms, a kind of fifty percent — enough to block *yibum* even though full *chalitza* has not been achieved. This is a genuinely striking *gemara*: the halachic consequence of spitting alone, for *Rabbi Eliezer*, flows from the recognition that a partially performed multi-element act can produce a real, if incomplete, legal effect.
