# Yevamot 104 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sandal & Tzara'at

### Bayit Hamusgar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: בַּיִת הַמּוּסְגָּר — מְטַמֵּא מִתּוֹכוֹ. מוּחְלָט — מִתּוֹכוֹ וּמֵאֲחוֹרָיו. וְזֶה וָזֶה מְטַמְּאִין בְּבִיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav Pappi's version, from a mishna (*Nega'im* 13:4): **A quarantined house,** i.e., one in which a discoloration appeared and was then quarantined by a priest until it could be determined whether it would be deemed a confirmed house of leprosy or not, **renders** one who touches it **ritually impure from within,** i.e., if one touches the inside of the house he becomes ritually impure; a house with **confirmed** leprosy renders one impure not only **from** touching it inside from **within,** but additionally **from** touching it **behind,** by touching it on the outside; **and** both **this and that,** i.e., both a quarantined house and a confirmed house, **impart ritual impurity through entering** it, as one who enters into either house becomes ritually impure, even if he does not touch the walls.

Today's daf is 104, and we pick up somewhere in the middle of 103b, where the *gemara* raises a challenge — *meitivei* — concerning the status of a *bayit hamusgar*, a sealed house whose *tzara'at* status is being determined. The broader question under discussion is whether a sandal that has been afflicted with *tzara'at* may be used for *chalitza*. The relevant distinction is between *musgar* — a house or object whose status is being evaluated but has not yet been finalized — and *muchlat* — one whose status has been finalized and which is destined for destruction.

The question is whether something destined for destruction can still function as a meaningful halachic object. There is no per se quantitative *shiur* at stake, but the concern is whether the sandal retains its identity as a shoe. Can it be considered a significant shoe if it is slated for destruction?

### Kitutei Mechatat

This brings us to the principle of *kitutei mechatat shiurei* — the idea that something destined for destruction has already, in a conceptual sense, lost its identity as a functional object. It is not merely that a quantitative measure has been diminished, but that the very status of the object as a coherent, purposeful thing has been undermined. The *gemara* will use this principle repeatedly, and it is important to keep it clearly in view.

### Rav Pappi/Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פַּפִּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: סַנְדָּל הַמּוּסְגָּר — לֹא תַּחֲלוֹץ בּוֹ, וְאִם חָלְצָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה. סַנְדָּל הַמּוּחְלָט — לֹא תַּחֲלוֹץ בּוֹ, וְאִם חָלְצָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה. רַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: אֶחָד סַנְדָּל הַמּוּסְגָּר וְאֶחָד סַנְדָּל הַמּוּחְלָט — לֹא תַּחֲלוֹץ בּוֹ, וְאִם חָלְצָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappi said in the name of Rava: One should not perform *ḥalitza*** on a *yavam* wearing **a quarantined sandal,** i.e., a sandal examined by a priest who found its signs of leprosy to be inconclusive, and places the sandal in isolation for a waiting period of up to two weeks to see if clear indications of leprosy develop. **But if she did perform *ḥalitza*** while the *yavam* was wearing it, **her *ḥalitza* is** nevertheless **valid** after the fact. On the other hand, if the sandal with leprosy is **a confirmed sandal,** i.e., a sandal that was definitively ruled to have leprosy, **one may not perform *ḥalitza* with it, and if she did perform *ḥalitza*** while the man was wearing it, **her *ḥalitza* is disqualified.** As an object with confirmed leprosy must be burned, it is considered halakhically as if it were already burnt, and is consequently considered to lack the qualities of a shoe necessary for *ḥalitza*. In contrast, **Rav Pappa said in the name of Rava:** With regard to **both a quarantined sandal and a confirmed sandal** the same *halakha* applies: **She may not perform *ḥalitza* with it** *ab initio*, **but if she did perform *ḥalitza*** with it, **her *ḥalitza* is valid.**

The practical question is framed through two competing traditions in the name of *Rava*. *Rav Pappi* said in *Rava*'s name that a *sandal muchlat* is not valid for *chalitza*, while *Rav Pappa* said in *Rava*'s name that it is valid. The *gemara* then attempts to challenge and resolve these positions through a series of cases from the laws of *tzara'at*.

The *gemara* challenges: a *bayit hamusgar*, once sealed, is *tamei* from the inside; a *bayit muchlat*, once its status is finalized and it is destined for demolition, conveys *tumah* from both inside and outside, and even causes *tumah* simply by entering — as the *pasuk* says *haba el habayit*, one who enters the house. If we apply *kitutei mechatat shiurei* — if the house scheduled for destruction is as if already ground up — how can it still function as a house for the purpose of the *tumah* of entry? Should it not lose its identity as a house, just as we are suggesting it loses its identity as a shoe?

### House Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כְּדִמְכַתַּת דָּמֵי — וְהָא בָּעֵינַן: ״וְהַבָּא אֶל הַבַּיִת״, וְלֵיכָּא! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְנָתַץ אֶת הַבַּיִת״, אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת נְתִיצָה קָרוּי בַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **And if it enters your mind** to say **that** an item with confirmed leprosy **is considered as if it were crushed** and not intact due to the requirement to burn it, **then** in order to contract ritual impurity through entering a house with leprosy, **we require** that there be a house, as the verse states: **"Moreover, one who enters the** house…shall be ritually impure" (Leviticus 14:46)? **And this** requirement **is not** satisfied here, as the house confirmed with leprosy must be burned and consequently should be considered as if it is crushed and not intact. The Gemara answers: **There it is different, as the verse states: "And he shall break down the house"** (Leviticus 14:45), which teaches that **even while it is being broken down it is** still **called a house,** until it is totally destroyed. Therefore, although for other purposes, objects that are required to be burned are considered crushed, the verse explicitly teaches that a house confirmed with leprosy is considered intact with regard to its ability to transmit ritual impurity to those who enter it.

The *gemara* answers: *shani hacha damar kra* — here the verse itself is different, because it says *venatatz et habayit*, "you shall demolish the house." The *Torah* teaches that even in the act of demolition, the structure is still called a *bayit*. The *Torah* explicitly preserves the object's identity within the framework of *tzara'at* law, even as it is being destroyed. This, however, is an internal ruling confined to the laws of *tzara'at*. When one wishes to apply the object's identity to some other *halachic* context — such as using it for *chalitza* — that is an entirely different matter.

## Matlit & Kezayit

### Matlit Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מַטְלֵית שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ שָׁלֹשׁ עַל שָׁלֹשׁ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ כְּזַיִת, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּכְנָס רוּבָּהּ לְבַיִת טָהוֹר — טִמְּאַתְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof from the *Tosefta* (*Nega'im* 7:3): If **a rag that is three** fingerbreadths **by three** fingerbreadths has a spot of leprosy, then **even if it does not contain** the minimum volume of **an olive-bulk, once most** of the rag **enters a pure house it renders** the house **ritually impure.**

The *gemara* then says *tashma* — come and hear a further challenge. If a piece of cloth (*matlit*) is three finger-widths by three finger-widths — the minimum size that defines it as a *beged* — and it is a garment afflicted with *tzara'at*, it conveys *tumah* to a pure house when brought inside. Crucially, this holds even if the garment does not have the volume of a *kezayit*, a detail that will become significant momentarily.

### Majority Entry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו, מוּחְלֶטֶת? לָא, מוּסְגֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **What, is it not** referring to a rag with **confirmed** leprosy, implying that one should calculate its measurements even when its leprosy is confirmed and it is destined to be burned? The Gemara rejects this assumption: **No,** this is referring to a **quarantined** rag, which does not have to be burned and is consequently considered intact. It can be measured to determine if it has the minimum measurements required for imparting ritual impurity.

The operative rule: *keivan shenichnas rubo leveit tahor tim'ahu* — once the majority of the garment has entered a pure house, it has rendered that house *tamei*. The majority entering is treated as if the whole object entered. This implies that the garment retains its identity as a unified *beged*. As *Rashi* explains, the *gemara* is not suggesting that a *muchlat* object loses its *shiur* and therefore fails to cause *tumah* — obviously, if that were true, nothing with *tzara'at* could ever cause *tumah*, since we would always say it has no *shiur*. Rather, the argument is about *coherence of identity* as a unified object: we said that something whose *shiur* is compromised loses its identity as a coherent object. Yet here, even a fractional majority is treated as representing the whole.

### Muchlat Question

The *gemara* asks: are we not discussing a *muchlelet* — a garment that is *muchlat* and destined for destruction? And yet it retains sufficient identity to cause *tumah*? The *gemara* answers: *lo* — *musgeret*. No, we are actually discussing a *musgar* garment, one whose status is still being evaluated, not yet finalized for destruction. So this does not prove anything about a *muchlat* object.

*If* that is so, says the *gemara*, *eima seifa* — let us look at the end of this *braita*.

### Seifa Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הָיוּ בָּהּ כַּמָּה זֵיתִים, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּכְנַס מִמֶּנָּה כְּזַיִת לְבַיִת טָהוֹר — טִמְּאַתְהוּ, אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא מוּחְלֶטֶת, הַיְינוּ דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ לְמֵת. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ מוּסְגֶּרֶת, אַמַּאי אִיתַּקַּשׁ לְמֵת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If** you interpret it **so, say the latter clause: If** the rag **was the volume of several olives,** i.e., it was very thick and even a small section of the cloth was equal to an olive-bulk, **when one olive-bulk of** the rag **enters a ritually pure house,** even if it is a small portion of the rag, **it renders** the house **ritually impure.** **Granted, if you say** that the entire *baraita* is referring to a rag with **confirmed** leprosy, **this is** so **because** a confirmed leper is **juxtaposed with** and thereby compared to **a dead person** (Numbers 12:12), and therefore an olive-bulk of the rag causes ritual impurity just as an olive-bulk of a corpse does. **But if you say** it is speaking about a **quarantined** rag, **why should it be juxtaposed to a dead person?** There is no biblical source comparing a quarantined leper to a corpse, and therefore, there is no reason to think that an olive-bulk of a rag transmits ritual impurity. Only if the rag is of the minimum dimensions and the entire rag enters the airspace does it transmit ritual impurity.

The *seifa* discusses a thick garment whose volume amounts to several *zeitim* of material. The ruling there is: *keivan shenichnas mimenu kezayit* — once a *kezayit* worth of the garment enters the house, even if it is not a three-by-three piece, it renders the house *tamei*. Why would a *kezayit* be the operative measure for a garment? Normally, *kezayit* is not a relevant measure for cloth at all.

### Meit Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְשָׂרַף אֶת הַבֶּגֶד״, אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת שְׂרֵיפָה קָרוּי בֶּגֶד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: That is correct; the *baraita* is referring to a rag with confirmed leprosy, but nevertheless it is not difficult for Rav Pappi. Although generally objects that are required to be burnt are considered crushed even before they are burnt, this is not true with regard to a garment with leprosy. **There** the *halakha* for a garment that has confirmed leprosy **is different, as the verse states: "And he shall burn the garment"** (Leviticus 13:52), which indicates that **even when it is being burned, it is still called a garment.**

The *gemara* explains: *ei amrat bishlama muchletet* — if we are indeed discussing a *muchlat* garment, this makes sense. *Hainu de-itkash le-meit* — it is because a *metzora* is compared to a dead person, as Moshe prayed for Miriam: *al na tehi ke-meit asher bezeito me-rechem imo*. The comparison to a *meit* brings the measure of *kezayit* into play, since a *kezayit* of a corpse conveys *tumah*. But this *itkash la-meit* — this comparison to a *meit* — only applies in the *muchlat* state. If we were discussing a *musgar* garment, there would be no basis for the *kezayit* measure. Therefore, the *seifa* must be discussing a *muchlat* garment.

And yet, even in the *muchlat* state, the garment retains a coherent identity such that a majority entering is treated as the whole entering. So the challenge to *kitutei mechatat shiurei* remains.

## Object Identity

### Shani Hataim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיגְמַר מִינֵּיהּ! אִיסּוּר מִטּוּמְאָה לָא גָּמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If your interpretation above is correct, that a garment with confirmed leprosy can still transmit ritual impurity even though the object is to be burned and should be considered crushed, **let us learn from this** concerning a sandal that even after it has been definitively determined to have leprosy, it is still considered a shoe and should be eligible for *ḥalitza*, at least after the fact, in accordance with Rav Pappa's opinion. The Gemara answers: **We cannot derive** a *halakha* of **a prohibition from** a *halakha* of **ritual impurity,** as these different areas of *halakha* cannot be compared. Therefore, although a garment with leprosy is considered intact with regard to the transmission of ritual impurity, that status cannot act as a source to teach that it is intact for the purpose of ruling that *ḥalitza* performed with a shoe with confirmed leprosy is valid.

The *gemara* responds with the same logic it used for the house: *shani hatam de-amar kra ve-saraf et habeged* — the *Torah* says "and he shall burn the garment," teaching that *afilu bish'at sreifa karui beged* — even in the moment of its burning, it is still called a *beged*. Just as the *Torah* explicitly preserves the house's identity as a house throughout the process of its destruction, it preserves the garment's identity as a *beged* throughout the process of its destruction. This preservation of identity, however, operates within the laws of *tzara'at* internally — it does not follow that the object carries its identity into other domains of *halacha*.

### Issur Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, הִלְכְתָא: אֶחָד סַנְדָּל הַמּוּסְגָּר, וְאֶחָד סַנְדָּל הַמּוּחְלָט, וְאֶחָד סַנְדָּל שֶׁל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — לֹא תַּחְלוֹץ, וְאִם חָלְצָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites Rava's final ruling: **Rava said** that **the *halakha* is the same for a quarantined sandal, a confirmed sandal, and a sandal of idolatrous worship,** i.e., a sandal that was placed on a statue of idolatrous worship; **one should not perform *ḥalitza*** with it, **and if she did perform *ḥalitza*** with it, **her *ḥalitza* is valid.**

The *gemara* then raises an obvious question: *ve-nigmor mineh* — why not derive from this that the *sandal muchlat* retains its identity for *chalitza* as well? Since within *tzara'at* we see the object is still called by its name even when destined for destruction, let us apply that to *chalitza*!

The *gemara* answers: *issur mi-tumah lo gamrinan* — we do not derive *issur* rulings from *tumah* laws. As *Rashi* explains, the entire category of *hilchot tumah* is a *chidush* — it operates by its own internal logic, which is somewhat arbitrary and not necessarily generalizable to other *halachic* domains. The preservation of identity within *tzara'at* is internal to those laws; it does not flow outward into other categories.

### Rava Hilchasa

This gives us the framework for the two versions of *Rava*. The position that *bedieved* the *sandal muchlat* is not valid — *Rav Pappi*'s version — holds that we do not learn *issur* from *tumah*, and therefore the preservation of identity within *tzara'at* cannot be applied to *chalitza*. The position that *bedieved* it is valid — *Rav Pappa*'s version — holds that once we see the object retains its identity within *tzara'at*, it retains its identity for other *halachic* purposes as well.

What is striking is that the *gemara* now quotes *Rava* directly: *amar Rava hilchasa* — the ruling is that both a sandal that is *muskav* (set aside) and one that is *muklat* (afflicted with *tzara'at* and destined for destruction) are valid for *chalitza*. This corresponds to *Rav Pappa*'s version. One cannot help but note the irony: if *Rava* himself ruled explicitly, why did we need two competing traditions transmitted in his name?

## Avodah Zarah

### AZ Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁל עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת, וְשֶׁל זָקֵן הֶעָשׂוּי לִכְבוֹדוֹ — לֹא תַּחְלוֹץ, וְאִם חָלְצָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **or** a sandal **belonging to** an inhabitant of **an idolatrous city,** a city the majority of whose inhabitants committed idolatry, which stands to be destroyed with all of the city's property; **and** likewise, the sandal **of an Elder made in accordance with his dignity** to be worn upon his death as part of his shroud, i.e., it is not meant for walking as a normal shoe, the *yevama* **may not perform *ḥalitza*** using any of these shoes. **And if she did perform *ḥalitza*, her *ḥalitza* is invalid** even after the fact, as these are not halakhically considered shoes.

*Rava* continues: *ve'echad sandal hamuklat ve'echad sandal shel avodat kochavim* — and even a sandal used as an object of idolatrous worship, *lo tachlotz* — one should not use it *lechatchila*, but *ve'im chaltzah chalitzatah kesherah* — if one did use it *bedieved*, the *chalitza* is valid.

### Ir Nidachas

However, *she-teikroves avodat kochavim* — if the sandal was offered as a sacrifice to an idol rather than being worshipped directly — or if it belonged to an *ir nidachas*, a city condemned to complete destruction — *lo tachlotz*, and *ve'im chaltzah chalitzatah pesulah* — even *bedieved* it is invalid. For an *ir nidachas*, this is because *Rava* acknowledges that *kitutei mechatat shiurei* applies when something is genuinely destined for total destruction outside the special framework of *tzara'at*.

### Zakein Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מַאי שְׁנָא זָקֵן הֶעָשׂוּי לִכְבוֹדוֹ — דְּלָאו לְהִילּוּכָא עֲבִיד, דְּבֵי דִּינָא נָמֵי — לָאו לְהִילּוּכָא עֲבִיד! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִילּוּ מְסַגֵּי בֵּיהּ שְׁלוּחָא דְּבֵי דִּינָא, מִי קָפֵיד עֲלֵיהּ דַּיָּינָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: What is different** about the sandal of **an Elder that is made in accordance with his dignity** that one should say that even though it is the proper size for his foot, it is not valid **because it was not made for walking,** but merely for him to wear after his death. But **the court's** sandal **also was not made for walking,** as the court kept a sandal that met all the other necessary qualifications for a sandal for *ḥalitza* and gave it to the *yavam* to be worn during the *ḥalitza* procedure. Since the *yavam* would return the *ḥalitza* sandal after the conclusion of the *ḥalitza*, therefore, the sandal was never used for walking and should be invalidated for *ḥalitza* just as the sandal made for the dignity of an Elder? He said to him: **If a messenger of the court had walked in** the *ḥalitza* shoe used by the court, **would the judge reprimand him?** Although the court's sandal was designed for the express purpose of *ḥalitza*, it may also be used for walking. A shoe designed for a dead person, on the other hand, is forbidden for any other use and is not made for walking at all, and it is consequently disqualified.

The *gemara* then introduces the case of *zakein asui lichvodo* — a euphemism for a shroud shoe, a shoe prepared specifically to be placed on a corpse and not made for actual walking. Such a shoe, *Rava* rules, is invalid even *bedieved*. Why? The obvious answer: *delav lehilocha avid* — it is not made for walking. It does not qualify as a shoe in the functional sense required for *chalitza*.

Before proceeding to the challenge this ruling raises, it is worth pausing to appreciate a remarkable detail that emerges here. It becomes clear that already in the *talmudic* era, the *beit din* maintained a dedicated *chalitza* shoe — a special shoe set aside specifically for performing *chalitza*. This is far from the straightforward scenario in which two people simply appear before a court wearing their ordinary shoes. The existence of a communal *beit din* shoe already reflects an institutional practice of considerable sophistication.

What is the difference between *avodat kochavim* and *tikroves avodat kochavim* — why is one valid *bedieved* and the other not? *Rava* offers no explicit distinction in the text, so *Rashi* supplies one: a sandal used as an object of *avodah zarah* by a non-Jew could potentially have its status annulled — a non-Jew can be *mevatel* it by renouncing belief in it, after which it ceases to be *avodah zarah*. Because it has the potential of not being destroyed, we do not fully apply the principle of *kitutei mechatat shiurei*. *Tosafot* disagrees, understanding the case as referring to *avodah zarah* that has already been annulled, and argues on the basis of a related *sugya*. *Tosafot* further raises the question of whether using an object associated with *avodah zarah* is problematic for a *mitzvah* object — drawing a comparison to the *shofar* of *avodah zarah* — and treats the *chalitza* shoe in a similar conceptual category.

*Rashi*, by contrast, invokes the principle of *mitzvot lav leihanos nitnu* — the fulfillment of *mitzvot* does not constitute *hana'ah* — to explain why using an *avodah zarah* sandal for *chalitza* would not violate the prohibition against deriving benefit from *avodah zarah*. This framing is itself significant: *Rashi* is conceptualizing *chalitza* primarily as a *kiyum mitzvah*, a ritual act, rather than as a quasi-transactional act of releasing the woman. Whether *chalitza* is more like a *mitzvah* or more like a legal transaction — analogous to *get* — has real *halachic* implications, and *Rashi*'s deployment of *mitzvot lav leihanos nitnu* reveals his position clearly.

### Rav Papa Q

*Rav Papa* poses a challenge to *Abaye*: what distinguishes the shroud shoe? Even granting that *delav lehilocha avid* — it is not made for walking — the same would seem to be true of the *beit din*'s *chalitza* shoe. Who walks around in the *beit din*'s special *chalitza* shoe? It too is kept exclusively for the performance of *chalitza* and is never used for ordinary walking.

### Beit Din Shoe

The *gemara* responds: *ilu mash'il lei shulcha debei dina mi kapid alei dayana?* — if the court's attendant wanted to borrow the *chalitza* shoe, would the judges object? Of course not. The *beit din* shoe is available in principle for ordinary use; it simply is not used that way in practice. The shroud shoe, by contrast, is intrinsically defined by its purpose of accompanying a corpse — it is not made for walking by design, not merely by convention. The answer is logically coherent, though one cannot help noting the irony: the *gemara* is essentially defending the validity of the *beit din*'s *chalitza* shoe on the grounds that, in theory, anyone could borrow it for a stroll.

## Mishna Chalitza

### Night & Left

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חָלְצָה בַּלַּיְלָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר פּוֹסֵל. בִּשְׂמֹאל — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מַכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a woman **performed *ḥalitza* at night, her *ḥalitza* is valid, but Rabbi Elazar invalidates it.** If she performed *ḥalitza* **on** the **left foot, her *ḥalitza* is invalid, but Rabbi Elazar validates it.**

The *Mishna* teaches: *chaltzah balaylah* — if she performed *chalitza* at night, *chalitzatah kesheira* — it is valid. *Rabbi Eliezer posul* — Rabbi Eliezer rules it invalid. Similarly, if the *chalitza* was performed on his left foot, *chalitzatah pesulah* — it is invalid; *Rabbi Eliezer machshir* — Rabbi Eliezer rules it valid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר מַקְּשִׁינַן רִיבִים לִנְגָעִים, וּמָר סָבַר לָא מַקְּשִׁינַן רִיבִים לִנְגָעִים? לָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא מַקְּשִׁינַן רִיבִים לִנְגָעִים, דְּאִי מַקְּשִׁינַן — אֲפִילּוּ גְּמַר דִּין בַּלַּיְלָה נָמֵי לָא, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר: חֲלִיצָה כִּתְחִלַּת דִּין דָּמְיָא, וּמָר סָבַר: חֲלִיצָה כִּגְמַר דִּין דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Let us say that they disagree about this** issue: One **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, **holds that we compare** the *halakhot* governing monetary **disputes,** which category includes *ḥalitza*, as *ḥalitza* carries with it monetary ramifications and requires payment of the marriage contract to the *yevama*, **with** the *halakhot* of **leprosy.** Just as leprosy cases are judged only during the day (see Leviticus 13:14), likewise, monetary cases may take place only during the day. **And** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds that we do not compare** monetary **disputes with leprosy.** The Gemara responds: **No, everyone** holds **that we do not compare** monetary **disputes with leprosy, for if we would compare** them fully, then **even** delivering the **verdict** of the court **case** could **not** be done **at night,** but it is permitted to complete monetary judgments and deliver the verdict at night, provided the proceedings began during the day. **And here,** with respect to performing *ḥalitza* at night **they disagree about this** issue: One **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, **holds** that ***ḥalitza* is considered like the commencement of judgment** of monetary cases, **and** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that ***ḥalitza* is considered like the verdict** of a monetary judgment, and therefore it may also be conducted at night.

The *gemara* proposes that the dispute about whether *chalitza* may be performed at night turns on the question of whether we link court proceedings to the laws of *negaim*. The *pasuk* says *al pihem yihyeh kol riv vechol nega* — on their word shall be every litigation and every *nega*. Since *tzara'at* declarations by the *kohen* must be made by day — *bayom hira'os bo hakohen yitma* — one might extend this requirement to court proceedings as well.

The *gemara* immediately rejects a sweeping application of this link: *d'chulei alma lo makshinan rivim lenegaim* — everyone agrees we do not make a global equation between litigation and *negaim*. Were we to do so fully, we could not even conclude a court case at night, which contradicts established *halacha*: while *dinei nefashot* must begin and be conducted entirely in the daytime, *dinei mamonot* may continue into the night once proceedings have begun.

The debate is then reframed: *mar savar chalitza k'techilas din damya* — one holds that *chalitza* is analogous to the beginning of a court proceeding, while *umar savar chalitza k'gmar din damya* — the other holds it is analogous to the conclusion of proceedings. The *gemara* itself provides no explanation of this distinction. One might suggest that viewing *chalitza* as the *gmar din* is natural because, like a verdict, it is the moment at which the legal change of status is effected — it is simultaneously the beginning and end of the proceeding, and the act itself produces the definitive outcome.

The question of whether *chalitza* is more like a *mitzvah* or more like a *din* proves directly relevant here. *Rashi* notes that *chalitza* has a *din* element because the woman collects her *ketubah* as a result. More broadly, framing *chalitza* as a *din* explains why it would be placed in the category of *riv* at all, and the *machloket* over whether it is the beginning or end of that *din* flows naturally from there.

One might have thought to derive the requirement of daytime from the word *le-einei*, since the witnesses must see the act — but this cannot be the source, as we have seen that even a *suma* poses complications, and the *gemara* does not pursue that derivation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר חִיָּיא קְטוֹסְפָאָה עֲבַד עוֹבָדָא בְּמוּק, וּבִיחִידִי, וּבַלַּיְלָה. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כַּמָּה רַב גּוּבְרֵיהּ דְּעָבֵיד כִּיחִידָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is told: **Rabba bar Ḥiyya Ketosfa'a,** from Ctesiphon, **conducted** *ḥalitza* **using a slipper** that was not made of leather, **and** he did so **in private,** as he was the only judge, **and by night. Shmuel said** disparagingly: **How great is the power** of this master **who follows an individual** opinion, as in his practice he relied on individual opinions that are not accepted as *halakha*.

The narrative then introduces *Rabba bar Chiya Katuspa'ah*, who performed a *chalitza* using a *mok* — a hard felt shoe, previously established as technically valid though not ideal — alone (*bi-yechidi*), without a *beit din*, and at night. *Shmuel* responded with what *Rashi* reads as sarcasm: "How great is this man," meaning: look how freely he relies on minority opinions. The *gemara* analyzes which element actually troubled *Shmuel*. The *mok* was established as valid by an anonymous *braita*, not a minority view. The nighttime *chalitza* corresponds to the *stam* position of our *Mishna*, which overrides *Rabbi Eliezer*'s lone dissent. The genuinely troubling element, says the *gemara*, is the absence of a *beit din* — *yechidai katani lah* — that position is sourced in a minority opinion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קַשְׁיָא? אִי מוּק — סְתָמָא תַּנְיָא. אִי לַיְלָה — סְתָמָא תַּנְיָא. אֶלָּא יְחִידִי קָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ: הֵיכִי עָבֵיד בִּיחִידִי, דִּיחִידָאָה קָתָנֵי לַהּ. דִּתְנַן: חָלְצָה בִּשְׁנַיִם אוֹ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה וְנִמְצָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הַסַּנְדְּלָר מַכְשִׁירִים. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁחָלַץ בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִים, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְהִכְשִׁיר. וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי [אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן]: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּאוֹתוֹ הַזּוּג. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כּוּלְּהוּ נָמֵי יְחִידָאָה קָתָנֵי לְהוּ. דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֲנִי רָאִיתִי אֶת רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֶּן אֱלִישָׁע שֶׁחָלַץ בְּמוּק, בִּיחִידִי, וּבַלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is difficult** for Shmuel about Rabba bar Ḥiyya's actions? **If it was** the fact that he conducted *ḥalitza* using **a slipper, an unattributed opinion is taught** in a *baraita* stating that this is valid. As the opinion is unattributed, this indicates that it is not the view of one individual, but rather the opinion of the majority. **If it was** the fact that he performed *ḥalitza* at **night, an unattributed opinion is taught** in the mishna stating that it is valid as well. **Rather, it was difficult for him** that Rabba bar Ḥiyya conducted *ḥalitza* **in private,** as the sole judge: **How could he do so in private, as that is taught only in accordance with an individual** opinion, **as we learned** in a mishna: **If she performed *ḥalitza* before two or three** judges **and one of them is found to be a relative or disqualified, her *ḥalitza* is invalid. And Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yoḥanan the Cobbler validate** the judge who is not a relative or disqualified. If there are two judges who are not relatives, then they hold that the *ḥalitza* is valid, as they validate a *ḥalitza* performed before two judges. **And an incident** occurred **involving one who performed *ḥalitza* between him and her** alone **in prison,** as there was no judge present at all, **and the incident came before Rabbi Akiva and he validated** it. **And Rav Yosef bar Minyumi said** that **Rav Naḥman said: The *halakha* does not follow that pair,** i.e., Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yoḥanan the Cobbler. Evidently, Rabba bar Ḥiyya relied on the individual opinion of Rabbi Akiva, who permitted *ḥalitza* in private, and therefore Shmuel commented regarding Rabba bar Ḥiyya's power to rule based on an individual's opinion. **And if you wish, say** that not only does this detail follow an individual opinion, but rather **all of these** details **are taught by an individual** opinion. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: I saw that Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha** performed **a *ḥalitza* using a slipper, in private, and at night.** This statement implies that these details are all according to his individual opinion, contrary to the opinion of the rest of the Sages.

The *Mishna* on the following *amud* records that *chalitza* performed before only two judges, or before three where one turned out to be a relative or otherwise disqualified — effectively making it two — is invalid. *Rabbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Yochanan ha-Sandlar* validate it. And there is the famous case of one who performed *chalitza* privately in prison, before no court at all, which *Rabbi Akiva* nonetheless upheld. This is the *da'as yachid* upon which *Rabba bar Chiya* relied.

*Rav Yosef bar Minyumi* quoted *Rav Nachman*: *ein halacha ke-osah ha-zug* — we do not rule like *Rabbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Yochanan ha-Sandlar*, the pair who validated two-judge *chalitza*. Thus *Rabba bar Chiya* was going even further, relying on the even more isolated view of *Rabbi Akiva*. The *gemara* adds: and if you prefer, you could say that even all three leniencies — the *mok*, the nighttime, and the absence of a *beit din* — ultimately trace back to a single chain of minority tradition, as recorded in a *braita* reporting that *Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha* himself once performed *chalitza* using felt, alone, and at night. *Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yosi* reports this — characteristically, as he is consistently the tradent of eyewitness accounts throughout *hazal*. Whether this means the other two leniencies should now be revisited is a separate question for the *poskim*.

### Metzora Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׂמֹאל חֲלִיצָתָהּ כּוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן? אָמַר עוּלָּא: יָלְפִינַן ״רֶגֶל״ ״רֶגֶל״ מִמְּצוֹרָע. מָה לְהַלָּן דְּיָמִין, אַף כָּאן דְּיָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that if *ḥalitza* was performed **using** his **left foot, her *ḥalitza*** is invalid, while Rabbi Elazar validates it. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of the Rabbis'** opinion? **Ulla said: We derive** a verbal analogy from the word **"foot"** stated here, and the word **"foot" stated regarding the leper. Just as there,** with respect to the leper, **it is the right** foot (Leviticus 14:14), **so too here,** with respect to *ḥalitza*, **it is the right** foot that must be used.

The *Mishna* teaches that *chalitza* performed on the left foot is invalid, and the *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama d'rabbanan?* What is the Rabbis' source for requiring the right foot when the *Torah* does not say "right" anywhere in the *chalitza* passage? *Ulla* answers: *yalfinan regel regel mimitzora* — we derive "foot" from "foot" by the *metzora*. In the purification ritual of the *metzora*, the text specifies the right foot explicitly. We learn from there to here: *mah lehalan yamin af kan yamin*.

### Position Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לָא יָלֵיף ״רֶגֶל״ ״רֶגֶל״ מִמְּצוֹרָע? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לִרְצִיעָה שֶׁהִיא בְּאֹזֶן הַיְמָנִית? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״אֹזֶן״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״אֹזֶן״. מָה לְהַלָּן יָמִין, אַף כָּאן יָמִין! אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מוּחְלֶפֶת הַשִּׁיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** this would seem to indicate that **Rabbi Elazar does not derive "foot"** with regard to *ḥalitza* from the word **"foot" from the leper. But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Elazar says: From where** is it derived **that piercing** a Hebrew slave's ear with an awl when the slave chooses to remain in servitude **is done** specifically **on the right ear? "Ear" is stated here** in the *halakhot* pertaining to a pierced slave, **and "ear" is stated there** in the *halakhot* of the leper. **Just as there,** with regard to leprosy, **it is the right** ear, as it is stated explicitly there, **so too here,** with regard to piercing the ear, **it is the right** ear. This statement of Rabbi Elazar implies that he does learn a verbal analogy about the word "right." **Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The** attribution of **the opinions is reversed,** meaning that it is Rabbi Elazar who invalidates *ḥalitza* on the left foot, as he learns an analogy from the *halakha* with regard to piercing the ear that the right must be used.

*Rabbi Eliezer*, who validates left-foot *chalitza*, does not accept this *gezeira shava* from *metzora*. Yet a *braita* reports *Rabbi Eliezer* himself deriving "right ear" for the case of ear-piercing of a slave from the *metzora* — *ne'emar kan ozen ve-ne'emar lehallan ozen*, a *gezeira shava* of "ear" to "ear." If he accepts that *gezeira shava*, he might as well accept the one from *metzora* to *chalitza*. *Rabbi Yosef bar Yosef* said in the name of *Yochanan*: *muchlefet hashita* — reverse the positions, and restore consistency to *Rabbi Eliezer*.

### Rava Gezeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, ״אֹזֶן״ ״אֹזֶן״ מוּפְנֵי, ״רֶגֶל״ ״רֶגֶל״ לָא מוּפְנֵי. וְכִי לָא מוּפְנֵי, מַאי פִּירְכָא אִיכָּא? אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לִמְצוֹרָע, שֶׁכֵּן טָעוּן עֵץ אֶרֶז וְאֵזוֹב וּשְׁנִי תוֹלָעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Actually, do not reverse** the opinions. The words **"ear"** and **"ear" are free** terms, i.e., they are superfluous in their context and therefore it is clear that the Torah included those terms for the express purpose of establishing the verbal analogy. A verbal analogy that is based on otherwise extraneous terms cannot be logically refuted. Therefore, the superfluous "ear" teaches that piercing is done on the right ear. However, the words **"foot"** and **"foot" are not free,** because the word "foot," written with regard to the *yavam*, is necessary in its context and is not superfluous. Therefore, the verbal analogy of the word "foot" is incomplete. The Gemara asks: **And even if they are not** both **free** to be used for exposition, **what refutation is there?** A verbal analogy that is free to be used for exposition in only one place is still valid, provided there is no reason to refute the comparison. The Gemara explains: It **can be refuted, as the leper** is unique in that there is a very specific process necessary for his purification: He requires that the blood of the offering be sprinkled upon him, and **he requires cedarwood, hyssop, and scarlet thread.** Therefore, it is possible to say that the Torah also specified the use of the right foot in the case of the leper. However, this would not necessarily be required with respect to *ḥalitza*, which lacks such specific *halakhot*. Consequently, *ḥalitza* performed using the left foot could be valid.

*Rava* says: *le'olam lo tehipach* — do not reverse the positions. There is no need to. *Ozen ozen mufni* — the word "ear" appears redundantly in the slave passage, which makes it available to serve as one end of a *gezeira shava*. *Regel regel lo mufni* — the word "foot" does not appear redundantly in the *chalitza* passage, so *Rabbi Eliezer* has no available endpoint to anchor the *gezeira shava* from *metzora* to *chalitza*. Hence there is no inconsistency in *Rabbi Eliezer* accepting one *gezeira shava* but not the other.

### No Redundancy Q

The *gemara* presses further: *ve-chi lo mufni mai pircha ika?* — even granting that "foot" is not redundant by *chalitza*, what specific logical challenge would defeat the *gezeira shava*? After all, a *gezeira shava* need not have redundancy at both endpoints to function; the presence or absence of redundancy affects only whether it can withstand a challenge, not whether it can be made at all.

The answer: *mah le-metzora sheken ta'un erez eitz erez ve-eizov ve-shani tola'as* — *metzora* involves an elaborate ritual with cedar wood, hyssop, and crimson thread. Precisely because *metzora* is accompanied by such a detailed and demanding set of ritual criteria, its requirement of the right foot is contextually justified. *Chalitza* lacks these elaborate requirements, so one cannot assume its rules must mirror those of *metzora* in this respect. This challenge cannot be refuted, and therefore the *gezeira shava* from *metzora* to *chalitza* remains unavailable to *Rabbi Eliezer*.

This allows us to articulate a taxonomy of *gezeira shava* strength: the most robust form has redundancy at both endpoints and is immune to challenge; a somewhat weaker form has redundancy at one endpoint; the weakest has redundancy at neither endpoint, which means it may still be proposed but can be defeated by any contextual objection. This is precisely the structure the *gemara* is working with here.

## Components

### Chaltzah Verakeka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חָלְצָה וְרָקְקָה, אֲבָל לֹא קָרְאָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה. קָרְאָה וְרָקְקָה, אֲבָל לֹא חָלְצָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה. חָלְצָה וְקָרְאָה, אֲבָל לֹא רָקְקָה — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: חֲלִיצָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If she, i.e., the *yevama*, **removed** the shoe **and spat** in accordance with the *halakha* **but did not recite** the necessary text, **her *ḥalitza* is valid.** If **she recited** the text **and spat but did not remove** the shoe, **her *ḥalitza* is disqualified.** If **she removed** the shoe **and recited** the text **but did not spit, Rabbi Elazar says: Her *ḥalitza* is disqualified,** while **Rabbi Akiva says: Her *ḥalitza* is valid.**

The *Mishna* now turns to the three components of *chalitza*: removal of the shoe, spitting, and recitation of the verses. *Chaltzah verakeka aval lo kara* — she removed the shoe and spat but did not recite — *chalitzatah kesheira*, it is valid. Recitation is not *me'akev*.

### No Removal

*Kara verakeka aval lo chaltza* — she recited and spat but did not remove the shoe — *chalitzatah pesulah*, it is invalid. The act of removal is the core of the entire proceeding; there is a reason it is called *chalitza*.

### No Spitting

*Chaltza vekara aval lo rakeka* — she removed the shoe and recited but did not spit — *Rabbi Eliezer omer chalitzatah pesulah*, invalid; *Rabbi Akiva omer chalitzatah kesheira*, valid. The debate concerns whether spitting is *me'akev*.

### Acts Essential

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: ״כָּכָה יֵעָשֶׂה״ — כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא מַעֲשֶׂה מְעַכֵּב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה? ״כָּכָה יֵעָשֶׂה לָאִישׁ״ — כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא מַעֲשֶׂה בָּאִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said to him:** The verse states: **"So shall it be done** to the man who does not build his brother's house" (Deuteronomy 25:9). "So" is an exclusionary term indicating that only precisely in this fashion is *ḥalitza* valid. Therefore, **any term that constitutes an action** for *ḥalitza* **is indispensable. Rabbi Akiva said to him:** You derive **proof from there?** But it states: **"So shall it be done to the man"** indicating that only **a term constituting an action toward the man,** namely any aspect of *ḥalitza* that concerns the man's body, such as removal of his shoe, is indispensable. But spitting, which does not involve the man, although it takes place in his presence, is not indispensable.

*Rabbi Eliezer*'s reasoning: *kkah yeaseh* — "thus shall be done" — means *kol davar shehu maaseh me'akev*, anything that is an act is indispensable. Spitting is an act; therefore it is *me'akev*. By the same logic, recitation is not an act in the operative sense, and therefore is not *me'akev*. This raises the philosophically rich question of whether *dibbur* constitutes *maaseh* — a question *Tosafot* engages with at length.

### To the Man

*Rabbi Akiva* responds: *misham raya?* — you want to bring proof from there? Read the verse carefully: *kacha yeaseh la-ish* — this shall be done *to the man*. *Kol davar shenaaseh ba-ish* — only what is done *to* the man is *me'akev*. Removal of the shoe is done to him. Spitting in front of him is not done to him — she spits before him, not upon him. Therefore spitting is not *me'akev*.

## Disqualified

### Cheresh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַחֵרֵשׁ שֶׁנֶּחְלַץ, וְהַחֵרֶשֶׁת שֶׁחָלְצָה, וְחוֹלֶצֶת לְקָטָן — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה. קְטַנָּה שֶׁחָלְצָה — תַּחְלוֹץ מִשֶּׁתַּגְדִּיל, וְאִם לֹא חָלְצָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה. חָלְצָה בִּשְׁנַיִם אוֹ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, וְנִמְצָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הַסַּנְדְּלָר מַכְשִׁירִין. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁחָלַץ בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִים, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְהִכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna lists additional *halakhot* with regard to *ḥalitza*. **If a deaf-mute man underwent *ḥalitza*, or a deaf-mute woman performed *ḥalitza*, or if an** adult **woman performs *ḥalitza* with a male minor, her *ḥalitza* is invalid** and the woman may not marry. **If a female minor performed *ḥalitza*, she must perform *ḥalitza*** a second time **once she becomes an adult, and if she does not perform** the second ***ḥalitza*, her** first ***ḥalitza* is invalid. If she performed *ḥalitza* before two or three** judges **and one of them is found to be a relative or disqualified** as a judge for some other reason, **her *ḥalitza* is invalid. Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yoḥanan the Cobbler validate** the *ḥalitza* in this case. **And an incident** occurred **involving a certain** person **who performed *ḥalitza* between him and her alone in prison,** i.e., not in the presence of others, **and the case came before Rabbi Akiva and he validated** it.

*Hacheresh she-nichlatz veha-chereshet she-chaltzah* — if a deaf-mute man had the *chalitza* performed, or a deaf-mute woman performed it, *chalitzatah pesulah* — it is invalid.

### Ketanah

*Ketanah she-chaltzah* — a minor girl who performed *chalitza* — *tachlotz mishetagdil*, she must redo it when she reaches adulthood. *Ve'im lo chaltzah chalitzatah pesulah* — if she does not redo it, it remains invalid.

### Court Size

*Chaltza bishnayim o bishloshoh ve-nimtza echad mehem karov o pasul* — if she performed *chalitza* before two, or before three where one proved to be a relative or otherwise disqualified, *chalitzatah pesulah* — it is invalid. *Rabbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Yochanan ha-Sandlar* validate such a *chalitza* after the fact.

### Private Case

*U-ma'aseh be-echad she-chalatz beino le-veinah be-veis ha-asurim* — there was an incident of a man who performed *chalitza* privately in prison, with no *beit din* present whatsoever — *va ma'aseh lifnei Rabbi Akiva ve-hichshir* — and when it came before *Rabbi Akiva*, he declared it valid. This is a striking ruling. The *psukim* repeatedly emphasize *le-einei ha-zekeinim* — in the presence of the elders — yet *Rabbi Akiva* validates *chalitza* with literally no court at all. *Rav Nachman* rules we do not follow this *da'as yachid*.

The situation of the *chereshet* raises a deeply troubling practical question. If the *yavam* is a *cheresh* and cannot have *chalitza* performed with him, and cannot do *yibum* properly either, what becomes of the *yevamah*? *Rashi* acknowledges explicitly that a *chereshet* who needs *chalitza* has no remedy through *chalitza* — *ein la takana* — and her only possible relief is *yibum*, and if they subsequently wish to separate, he may give her a *get*. The *halacha* does contemplate situations in which *chalitza* is simply impossible and *yibum* is the only path, even when that outcome is profoundly difficult. This follows from the structure of the *Torah*'s framework: because *yibum* remains an alternative, the impossibility of *chalitza* does not automatically release the woman from the *levirate* bond.

## Cheresh Analysis

### Ileim Intellect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ קְרִיאָה לָא מְיעַכְּבָא, לְפִיכָךְ אִלֵּם וְאִלֶּמֶת שֶׁחָלְצוּ — חֲלִיצָתָן כְּשֵׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava said: Now that you have said** that **recitation** of the text **is not indispensable** in order for the *ḥalitza* to be valid, **therefore** if **a mute man or a mute woman performed *ḥalitza*, their *ḥalitza* is valid.** Although the statements should be recited *ab initio*, since the recitation is not indispensable, mute individuals can perform *ḥalitza*.

The *gemara* now probes the grounds for the *cheresh*'s disqualification. *Rava* opens with the observation: since the *Mishna* unanimously agreed that *kriyah* — recitation — is not *me'akev*, the *cheresh*'s disability need not be the cause of disqualification. Therefore *Rava* reasons that a mute person — *ileim* — who is fully hearing and of full intellect, merely unable to speak, should be perfectly valid for *chalitza*. *Hashta de-amarta kri'ah lo me'akva, lefichach ileim ve-ilemet she-chaltzah chalitzatam kesherah* — since recitation is not *me'akev*, a mute person may perform *chalitza* and it is valid.

### Intellect Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: חֵרֵשׁ שֶׁנֶּחְלַץ, וְהַחֵרֶשֶׁת שֶׁחָלְצָה, וְהַחוֹלֶצֶת מִן הַקָּטָן — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה. מַאי טַעְמָא — לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא בְּנֵי קְרִיָּיה נִינְהוּ! לָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָאו בְּנֵי דֵעָה נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But **we learned** in the mishna above: **If a deaf-mute man underwent *ḥalitza*, or a deaf-mute woman performed *ḥalitza*, or if a woman performs *ḥalitza* with a male minor, her *ḥalitza* is invalid. What is the reason** that the *ḥalitza* is invalid? **Is it not because they are not competent to recite** the text, thereby indicating that the recitation is necessary even after the fact? The Gemara rejects this assertion: **No,** the reason for disqualifying a deaf-mute man and woman is **because they are not** considered **to have intellectual capacity,** and therefore their actions do not have halakhic significance.

The *gemara* challenges: but then what is the reason the *cheresh* and *chereshet* are disqualified? *Mai ta'ama?* If it is because they cannot recite — *lav mishum delav benei kri'ah ninhu* — then a mute person would be equally disqualified by the same reasoning, since a mute person also cannot recite. So is the *cheresh*'s disqualification on account of the inability to recite, or on account of lacking full *da'at*?

The *gemara* answers: the *cheresh* is disqualified *mishum delav benei da'ah ninhu* — because they are not of full intellect, not because of the inability to speak. The category of *cheresh* in *halachic* discourse reflects the reality that, in a society without schools for the deaf, an individual who could neither hear nor speak often developed without acquiring the conceptual framework of normal social and legal life. This is distinct from a mute person who can hear — who processes language, understands the world, and simply cannot produce speech. *Ufumaihu hu de-che'av lehu* — their mouth causes them difficulty, not their minds.

### Recitation Ability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אִלֵּם וְאִלֶּמֶת נָמֵי! אָמַר רָבָא: אִלֵּם וְאִלֶּמֶת בְּנֵי דֵעָה נִינְהוּ, וּפוּמַּיְיהוּ הוּא דְּכָאֵיב לְהוּ. וְהָא אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּ״אָמַר״ ״וְאָמְרָה״! אֶלָּא, כִּי אִתְּמַר דְּרָבָא אַסֵּיפָא אִתְּמַר:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** let us **also** say that **a mute man and woman** do not have intellectual capacity. **Rava said: A mute man and woman do have intellectual capacity.** Rather, **it is their mouth that hurts them.** Mute individuals have full intellectual capacity; they merely lack a means of expression. The deaf-mute, on the other hand, is not considered to have the mental capacity to speak. The Gemara challenges again: **But didn't** the scholars from the **house** of study **of Rabbi Yannai say** that a deaf-mute man and woman are disqualified from participation in *ḥalitza* **because they cannot** fulfill the requirements **of "he says"** (Deuteronomy 25:8) **and "she shall say"** (Deuteronomy 25:9), and not due to insufficient intellectual capacity? **Rather, when that** first statement **of Rava was said, it was stated** concerning **the latter clause** of the mishna:

However, the *gemara* then raises a further challenge from the school of *Rabbi Yannai*: *lefi she-eino be-omar ve-omrah* — the *cheresh* and *chereshet* are disqualified precisely because they are not in the category of "he says and she says," meaning the inability to recite is itself the disqualifying factor, not the lack of *da'at*. This reading is actually more favorable to the dignity of the *cheresh* — it treats them as intellectually capable — but it has the practical consequence of excluding the *ileim* as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ קְרִיָּיה מְעַכְּבָא, לְפִיכָךְ אִלֵּם וְאִלֶּמֶת שֶׁחָלְצוּ — חֲלִיצָתָן פְּסוּלָה. וּמַתְנִיתִין כְּרַבִּי זֵירָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כׇּל הָרָאוּי לְבִילָּה — אֵין בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ. וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לְבִילָּה — בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: You have now said** that **recitation is indispensable,** as can be inferred from the mishna disqualifying the deaf-mute. **Therefore, if a mute man or woman performed *ḥalitza*, their *ḥalitza* is invalid.** And **the mishna,** which indicates in its first clause that recitation is not indispensable, but later states that *ḥalitza* performed by someone incapable of recitation is invalid, is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Zeira** with regard to a meal-offering. A meal-offering is generally a mixture of flour and oil. If flour is added to the oil but they are not mixed, they are considered to be fitting to be mixed so long as there is not an excessive amount of flour. This is that **which Rabbi Zeira said: Whatever is fitting to be mixed, mixing is not indispensable to it,** and it is valid even if it is not mixed. **And whatever is not fit to be mixed,** e.g., if the quantity of flour is so great that the ingredients cannot be properly mixed, **mixing is indispensable for it** and it is invalid if it has not been mixed. From here one may learn a general *halakhic* principle: There are some actions for which their actual performance is not indispensable, provided they are capable of being performed. An action becomes indispensable only if one is unfit or unable to perform it. With regard to recitation of the verses, although it is not indispensable, a mute person is disqualified from performing *ḥalitza* because he is not capable of reciting the verses.

The *gemara* concedes the point: *ela ki itmar de-Rava aseifa itmar* — *Rava*'s statement must actually have been directed at the end of the *Mishna*, and his ruling is reversed. A mute person cannot perform valid *chalitza*, because although recitation is not *me'akev* as an act that must be performed, the person performing *chalitza* must at least have the capacity to recite. Even if the act is not required, the potential must exist.

### Potential Test

This brings us to the principle articulated by *Rabbi Zeira*: *kol ha-ra'uy le-bilah ein bilah me'akevet bo, ve-chol she-eino ra'uy le-bilah bilah me'akevet bo.* By the case of the *mincha*, the *Torah* describes the flour as *belulah ba-shemen* — mixed with oil. If one fails to mix it, *bedieved* the *mincha* is valid. But if it was impossible to mix — because the volume of flour was too great — the *mincha* is entirely invalid. The principle: one does not actually have to perform the act, but the act must be performable in principle.

### Mincha Parallel

*Tosafot* illuminates the conceptual basis: where the *Torah* frames a requirement as a descriptor — *belulah ba-shemen*, "mixed with oil," an adjective rather than a verbal command — we read it as characterizing the nature of the object rather than mandating a specific act. Therefore, it suffices that the object has the *potential* to exhibit that characteristic, even if the act is not performed. By contrast, where the *Torah* says *ve-amar ve-omrah* — "and he shall say and she shall say" — one might think this demands actual performance. *Rava* resolves this by reading the verbal form as a characterization of the *gavra*, the person: the participants must be of the type of person who can say — they must have the capacity, even if the act is waived. A mute person, lacking that capacity, is therefore excluded, even though the act of recitation itself is not *me'akev*.

## Spitting Power

### Spitting Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ לַאֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: יְבָמָה שֶׁרָקְקָה — תַּחְלוֹץ. מִכְּלָל דְּאִיפַּסְלָא לַהּ מֵאַחִין.
>
> **Translation:** **They sent** the following teaching from Eretz Yisrael **to the father of Shmuel:** Once **a *yevama* has spat she must perform** a complete ***ḥalitza*** and may not enter into levirate marriage. The Gemara comments: **By inference,** you may learn **that** even though the spitting did not permit her to marry outside the family, **she is disqualified from** entering into levirate marriage with any of **the brothers,** and must therefore complete the *ḥalitza*.

The *gemara* records a ruling sent to the father of *Shmuel*: *yevamah she-rakakah tachlotz* — a *yevamah* who spat alone, without performing any other component of *chalitza*, must subsequently perform full *chalitza*. The implication is that the mere spitting alone is enough to disqualify her from *yibum* with any of the brothers, leaving *chalitza* as her only path.

### Akiva Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם מִצְוָה, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַאֵימוּרִים, דְּכִי לֵיתַנְהוּ — לָא מְעַכְּבִי, וְכִי אִיתַנְהוּ — מְעַכְּבִי, אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לָא מְעַכְּבָא, מֵאַחִין אִיפַּסְלָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is** this *halakha* taught? **If we say it is** in accordance with **Rabbi Akiva,** who stated in the mishna that spitting is not required after the fact, it is difficult, as the following *a fortiori* reasoning indicates: **Now if** even in a case **where there is a mitzva** of *ḥalitza*, and the *yevama* is still around and capable of fulfilling the Torah command of spitting, **and one might say** that spitting should be treated just **as it is with** regard to **the** fatty **portions** of offerings that are to be consumed on the altar, where the *halakha* is **that when they are not** present for offering, i.e., they were lost or became ritually impure, they are **not** considered **indispensable** to permit the consumption of the offering. **When the fats of the offering are no longer present for offering, it is permitted for the sacrificial meat to be eaten by the priests through the sprinkling of blood alone, even without offering the fatty portions on the altar. But when** the fatty portions **are present** and extant, then they are considered **indispensable,** and the priests are not permitted to eat their portions of the offering until the fatty portions are burned on the altar. Based on an analogy to the fatty portions of offerings, one would have said regarding the spitting of a *yevama* that even if it is not indispensable after the fact, so long as the *yevama* is around, one would expect that her spitting is indispensable. Yet even so, **Rabbi Akiva said** in the mishna that spitting is **not indispensable** to *ḥalitza* even in such a case, and the *ḥalitza* is validated by the removal of the shoe alone. Since Rabbi Akiva seems to accord little significance to the spitting, he cannot be the basis for this *halakha*, as if spitting is never indispensable, could spitting alone **disqualify her from** entering into levirate marriage with one of **the brothers?**

This raises an immediate question: whose opinion does this reflect? If it is *Rabbi Akiva*, who rules that spitting is not *me'akev* even when one is in the midst of performing *chalitza*, then spitting alone — performed outside the context of *chalitza* entirely — should carry even less weight. *Rabbi Akiva* does not even require spitting when everything else is being done properly. How then could spitting alone, stripped of any further act, be sufficient to disqualify her from *yibum*? Surely spitting should be entirely trivial for *Rabbi Akiva*.

### Two Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר — וְהָא שְׁנֵי דְּבָרִים הַמַּתִּירִין נִינְהוּ, וּשְׁנֵי דְּבָרִים הַמַּתִּירִין — אֵין מַעֲלִין זֶה בְּלֹא זֶה. אֶלָּא כְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **But rather,** one might say that this teaching is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** who disagrees with Rabbi Akiva and states in the mishna that spitting is indispensable for validating *ḥalitza* and is necessary alongside the removal of the shoe. Since he accords significance to the spitting, one might have thought that the teaching prohibiting the *yevama* from levirate marriage is in accordance with his view. **But** that is difficult as well, as according to Rabbi Elazar, removal of the shoe and spitting are both necessary, and therefore, they **are** considered **two permitting factors** that must be completed in order to fulfill an obligation. **And** there is a principle accepted by Rabbi Elazar with regard to offerings that should be applicable here: Whenever **there are two permitting factors** that are indispensable for an offering to be valid, **one** of those factors **cannot elevate one** of the subordinate components of the offering to consecrated status **without the other.** Similarly, since Rabbi Elazar holds that *ḥalitza* and spitting are both indispensable, if she performs only one of these actions, such as spitting, it is only one of two permitting factors and therefore should not disqualify her for levirate marriage with one of the other *yevamin*. **Rather,** one must say that this teaching is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who is of the opinion that even one of two permitting factors can effect a change in status without performance of the other factor, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The lambs** sacrificed on the festival **of Assembly,** i.e., *Shavuot*, **consecrate the loaves** that accompany them **only by** means of their **slaughter,** as the slaughtering of the lambs consecrates the bread.

The *gemara* resolves: this must be *Rabbi Eliezer*'s view, for whom spitting is *me'akev*. But then a further difficulty arises: *Rabbi Eliezer* requires two acts — removal of the shoe and spitting. The principle of *shenei devarim ha-matirin* is that when two elements are jointly required to produce a permitted status, neither element alone carries any legal weight. If you have not done both, you have done nothing. How then does spitting alone carry enough weight to disqualify her?

### Eimurim

The *gemara* draws an analogy to the *emurim* — the internal sacrificial parts that must be burned on the altar before the rest of the animal may be consumed. If the *emurim* are lost or become *tamei* and cannot be burned, the meat may nonetheless be eaten. But if the *emurim* are present, the meat may not be eaten until they are burned. This suggests a nuanced position: when an element that would otherwise be *me'akev* is unavailable, its absence does not block the permitted act; but when it is present and available, it must be performed. By analogy, one might say that spitting, when present and possible, is *me'akev* within *chalitza* — yet its lone occurrence outside *chalitza* should still carry no weight, since the removal of the shoe has not been performed.

### Half Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? שְׁחָטָן לִשְׁמָן, וְזָרַק דָּמָן לִשְׁמָן — קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם. שָׁחַט שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן, וְזָרַק לִשְׁמָן — לֹא קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם. שְׁחָטָן לִשְׁמָן, וְזָרַק דָּמָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן — קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁחוֹט לִשְׁמָן וְיִזְרוֹק דָּמָן לִשְׁמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** If **one slaughtered** the lambs **for their own sake,** i.e., as lambs for *Shavuot*, **and** the priest **sprinkled their blood for their own sake, the loaves are consecrated.** However, if **one slaughtered** them **not for their own sake, and** the priest **sprinkled their blood for their own sake, the loaves are not consecrated,** as the factors indispensable in rendering the offering valid were not properly performed. If **one slaughtered them for their own sake, and he sprinkled their blood not for their own sake,** the fact that the lambs were properly slaughtered renders the loaves partially consecrated. Therefore, the loaves **are consecrated** to the extent that they cannot be redeemed, **but they are not consecrated** to the extent that they may be eaten. **This is the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: Actually,** the loaves **are consecrated only when one slaughters** the offerings **for their own sake and sprinkles their blood for their own sake,** i.e., only if both factors indispensable in rendering the offering valid were properly performed. If so, the teaching sent from Eretz Yisrael seems to be in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion that even if only one of the two permitting factors of slaughter and sprinkling was performed, the loaves will still be consecrated. Likewise, with respect to *ḥalitza*, where there is a need for two permitting factors, spitting and removal of the shoe, performing one factor such as spitting is sufficient to disqualify the *yevama* from subsequently entering into levirate marriage.

The *gemara*'s resolution invokes *Rabbi*'s position in an unrelated case of *kivsei atzeret* — the lambs brought on *Shavuot*. Sanctification of the accompanying loaves requires both the *shechita* and the *zerakat hadam* of the lambs, performed with proper intent. If the *shechita* was proper but the *zerakat hadam* was not, *Rabbi* rules *kadosh ve-eino kadosh* — it is half-consecrated. There exists an intermediate state of partial sanctification. *Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon* disagrees: it is either fully sanctified or not at all; there is no halfway.

Applied to our case: according to *Rabbi Eliezer*, *chalitza* requires two acts. By analogy to *Rabbi*'s view, if only one of the two required acts has been performed, the result is not zero — it is a partial state. The woman has done enough to be in an intermediate status: she has not completed *chalitza*, but she has done enough to sever the *yibum* bond. It is, in *Rabbi*'s terms, a kind of fifty percent — enough to block *yibum* even though full *chalitza* has not been achieved. This is a genuinely striking *gemara*: the halachic consequence of spitting alone, for *Rabbi Eliezer*, flows from the recognition that a partially performed multi-element act can produce a real, if incomplete, legal effect.
