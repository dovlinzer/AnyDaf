# Hullin 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oto Ve'et Beno & Unfit S…

### Yesterday's Review

The previous session concluded the discussion of the father's contribution in the animal kingdom and moved into the end of the Mishnah's analysis. The Mishnah discusses cases in which one is *chayav* for *oto ve'et beno* even when the first or second *shechitah* does not render the meat permissible — a situation the Gemara terms *shechitah she'einah re'uyah*. The Gemara notes that this position is in tension with that of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* does not count as a *shechitah* at all. The Mishnah to be studied later in this session spells out Rabbi Shimon's position explicitly.

Two points were established previously. First, *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* applies even to *Kodashim*, because the *shechitah* alone does not immediately render the meat permissible — a somewhat surprising result that will be revisited. Second, the relevance of *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* is not limited to the second *shechitah* when assessing liability for *oto ve'et beno*; it applies equally to the first *shechitah*. Since the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* requires that both animals have been *shachted*, if the first *shechitah* was a *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* — whether because it involved *Kodashim* or *Kodashim chutz lamikdash* or some other disqualifying circumstance — the person would be exempt from the prohibition entirely.

### Rabbi Shimon vs. Sages

The Mishnah under discussion does not reflect Rabbi Shimon's position. The broader question that had been introduced was why, when one *shachts* a *Kodashim* animal as the second animal in an *oto ve'et beno* sequence, there is no additional prohibition triggered from the verse *lo yeratzeh* — understood by the Gemara as a prohibition against *shachting* any *korban* that will not be accepted. Since the second animal cannot be brought today, as it is *mechussar zman*, one might expect that the very act of bringing it should constitute a transgression of that prohibition in addition to the transgression of *oto ve'et beno*.

The first answer offered by the Gemara was that in fact one does incur *malkos* for that prohibition as well; the Mishnah simply did not find it necessary to mention it since one set of *malkos* was already being discussed. A second answer is now taken up.

## Mechussar Zman & Lashes

### Rabbi Zeira's Position

At the very bottom of 81b, Rabbi Zeira offers his answer: *hanach limechussar zman dekativ nitko le'asei*. The prohibition against bringing a sacrifice before its designated time — or more broadly, bringing any animal that is unfit — does not incur *malkos*, because it is a prohibition that derives from a positive commandment. The positive commandment in question is the verse *miyom hashmini vahal'ah yeratzeh* — "from the eighth day onward it shall be acceptable as a *korban*." The same verse that can be read as an implicit prohibition (*miyom hashmini in, me'ikara lo* — only from day eight, not before) is framed as a positive *mitzvah*. Therefore, the prohibition against bringing a *korban* before its time has the status of a positive commandment, and the rule of *lav haba miklal aseh aseh* applies: a negative prohibition that is derived from a positive framing has the force of a positive *mitzvah*, not a standard prohibition for which one receives *malkos*. This applies equally to an animal that cannot be brought because its mother was *shachted* that day — it, too, falls under the category of *mechussar zman*.

### Lav Haba Miklal Aseh

One might ask why this is called *nitko le'asei* — a prohibition "connected to" or "severed to" a positive commandment. Normally, the concept of a negative prohibition *nitko le'asei* refers to cases where a positive commandment serves to remedy or rectify the transgression of the negative. Classic examples include: do not steal — and if you do, return the object; do not take the mother bird together with the young — and if you do, send the mother bird free; do not leave meat of a *korban* overnight — and if you do, burn it. In each of these cases the positive *mitzvah* somewhat ameliorates the consequences of the transgression, and for that reason the negative prohibition alone does not generate *malkos*.

That logic does not apply here at all. If one brought this *korban* on the seventh day, or on the same day the mother was *shachted*, there is no way to retroactively fix the transgression. Being told one must wait until the eighth day does not rectify anything — if anything, it compounds the sin. Not only did one transgress the negative prohibition, one also transgressed the positive *mitzvah* to wait.

### Tosafot's Explanation

*Tosefos* raises all of these difficulties and concludes that the phrase *nitko le'asei* here does not carry its standard meaning of the positive *mitzvah* as a remedy. Rather, *lenatek* can mean to remove or detach. *Tosefos* explains that the effect of the positive *mitzvah* here is to remove the generic prohibition of *lo yeratzeh* from applying to this case. There is a general prohibition: do not bring a *korban* that will not be accepted. There is also, in the specific sub-case of *mechussar zman*, an explicit positive formulation from the Torah: wait until the eighth day. Where the Torah speaks so explicitly to a particular situation through a positive commandment, that positive commandment governs, and the generic prohibition does not apply. The violator transgresses only the explicit provision — which has the force of a positive *mitzvah* — and not the overarching negative prohibition. That is why no *malkos* are incurred. This is the meaning of the Gemara's phrase: the prohibition of *mechussar zman* is prohibited by virtue of a positive, not by the generic negative.

*Rashi* attempts to read this as the classic formulation where the positive *mitzvah* fixes the negative, but for all the reasons stated — it does not fix anything, and the very language of the Gemara emphasizes the transgression of the positive *mitzvah* rather than any remedial function — this reading is difficult to sustain.

### Rabbi Avtolkai's Contrad…

The Gemara then notes that the verse cannot simply be used to establish this prohibition-as-positive-commandment because one needs it to resolve the contradiction raised by Rabbi Avtolkai: *d'Rabbi Avtulki rami*. One verse states *vehaya shivat yamim tachat imo* — "it shall be seven days under its mother." From this one might infer that by nightfall of the seventh day, once seven full days have passed, the animal is eligible to be brought as a *korban*. Yet another verse states *miyom hashmini vahal'ah yeratzeh* — "from the eighth day onward it shall be acceptable" — implying that the preceding night does not suffice; only the day itself qualifies.

### Night vs. Day Distinction

The resolution is: *laila likdusha, yom l'hartza'ah* — the night can be used for sanctification (*hekdesh*), while bringing the *korban* itself must wait for the day. The *chiddush* is not simply that one may not bring an animal at night — that is a general rule. The *chiddush* is that one may sanctify the animal the preceding night. One might have thought that since the animal cannot be brought that night, it still has the status of *mechussar zman* and therefore cannot be sanctified. The verse teaches otherwise: since the night is legally connected to the following day, and on that day the animal will be fit, it is already sufficiently "in its time" to be sanctified. The nighttime is *lo mechussar zman* — not considered outside its designated time. The animal cannot be brought because of the general prohibition against night-time Temple service, but it is already temporally fit.

### Tosafot's Alternative Vi…

*Tosefos* is troubled by this explanation, because the Gemara elsewhere seems to permit sanctifying (*making hekdesh*) even a fetus still in the womb, suggesting that one can generally be *makdish* an animal long before it is fit to be brought. If so, what is the novel ruling that one may be *makdish* on the preceding night? *Tosefos* notes a parallel Gemara in *Zevachim* and concludes that, at least in this context, the *chiddush* is the opposite: the night is itself an instance of *mechussar zman*. The verse is teaching that the night before the eighth day is still considered *mechussar zman* — which matters in the specific context of a *bamah* (a private altar), where *korbanot* could generally be brought at night, yet an animal that is *mechussar zman* could not.

This reading runs in the opposite direction from the other Gemara on the subject, which views the night as not *mechussar zman*. The tension stands. But regardless of which *chiddush* is the operative one, the verse is needed for Rabbi Avtolkai's resolution and therefore cannot simultaneously serve to establish the positive prohibition of *mechussar zman*.

### Ken Ta'aseh Verse

The Gemara resolves this by pointing to another verse: *ken ta'aseh lishorcha v'litzonecha* — "so shall you do for your ox and your sheep." This verse, appearing in the context of the law of the eighth day, restates the obligation as a freestanding positive commandment: wait until the eighth day. It is from this verse that one derives the positive *mitzvah* requiring one to refrain from bringing the animal before its time. And as *Tosefos* explained, it is this explicit positive commandment that governs *mechussar zman* in place of the generic *lo yeratzeh*, which is why no *malkos* apply.

## Does Oto Ve'et Beno Appl…

### Rav Hamnuna's Report

The Gemara now returns to the central question of whether *oto ve'et beno* applies to sacrifices. Rav Hamnuna reports in the name of Rabbi Shimon: *oto ve'et beno* does not apply to *Kodashim*. This follows a point raised earlier: if every *shechitah* of a *korban* is considered a *shechitah she'einah re'uyah*, then the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* can never apply to sacrifices. Whether it is the first or second animal that is a *korban*, neither *shechitah* would qualify as a *shechitah* in the halakhic sense.

### No Valid First Slaughter

The reasoning is spelled out: *keivan d'amar Rabbi Shimon shechitah she'einah re'uyah lo shma shechitah* — since Rabbi Shimon holds that an unfit *shechitah* does not count as a *shechitah*, and *shechitat Kodashim* is similarly a *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* (because the meat does not become permissible through the *shechitah* alone but only through the subsequent *zerikat hadam*), it follows that one can never have a qualifying *shechitah* when the animal is a *korban*.

One might object: with *chullin*, the meat becomes permissible through *shechitah* itself — even if one would have to drain the blood before eating, and even with the various prohibitions of *chelev* and the like. The meat is halakhically permitted by the act of *shechitah*. With a *korban*, by contrast, the meat remains forbidden after *shechitah*; it only becomes permissible through the *zerikat hadam*. Therefore, the *shechitah* of a *korban* does not of itself make the meat permissible, rendering it a *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* according to Rabbi Shimon.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava challenges this from a *baraita*. The *baraita* deals with the prohibition of *shechutei chutz* — slaughtering a *korban* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* — in a case that also involves *oto ve'et beno*. One is *chayav* for *shechutei chutz* only when the animal could have been brought inside the *Beit HaMikdash*; if it could not have been brought today, one does not incur *karet*, though according to Rabbi Shimon one may still transgress a *lav*.

The *baraita* states: if one *shachts* two sacrifices outside the *Beit HaMikdash* in an *oto ve'et beno* sequence, Rabbi Shimon says the second person transgresses a *lo ta'aseh* — a negative prohibition — because Rabbi Shimon holds *kol hara'uy lavo l'achar zman harei hu b'lo ta'aseh*: anything that will be eligible to be brought at a later time falls within the negative prohibition, even if not today, though one does not incur *karet* (*ve'ein bo karet*). The Sages disagree: *kol she'ein bo karet eino b'lo ta'aseh* — if there is no *karet*, there is no negative prohibition either.

Now, implicit in this *baraita* is that Rabbi Shimon accepts that *oto ve'et beno* applies to *Kodashim*. The reason the second animal cannot be brought today is precisely the restriction of *oto ve'et beno*. If *oto ve'et beno* did not apply to *Kodashim*, the second animal would be entirely unrestricted, and one bringing it outside the *Beit HaMikdash* should incur full *karet*. This directly contradicts the assertion that *oto ve'et beno* does not apply to *Kodashim* according to Rabbi Shimon.

### Both Outside (Kodashim B…

Rava (or, in another formulation, an anonymous tradition) resolves the difficulty by invoking the principle of *chasurei mechasra vehachi katani* — the *baraita* is incomplete and must be read with additional material. The *baraita* must be understood as addressing two distinct scenarios.

The first scenario: both animals are slaughtered outside the *Beit HaMikdash* (*shneihem bachutz*). According to the Sages (*lerabbanan*): the first animal — *rishon anush karet* — incurs *karet* for *shechutei chutz*. The second animal — *sheini pasul u'patur* — is disqualified by *oto ve'et beno* from being brought today and therefore does not incur *karet* for *shechutei chutz*; it does incur a *lav* for *oto ve'et beno*, but the Sages, following the rule that no *karet* means no *lav* for *shechutei chutz*, exempt the person from lashes on the *shechutei chutz* count. What would Rabbi Shimon say in this scenario? *Shneihem anushim karet* — both incur *karet*. Because, if *shechitat Kodashim* does not count as a *shechitah*, the first animal's *shechitah* is no *shechitah* at all. Consequently, *oto ve'et beno* does not restrict the second animal, which can therefore be brought inside the *Beit HaMikdash* today. Since it is eligible to be brought inside, slaughtering it outside makes one *chayav karet*. Both animals, then, yield *karet*.

### One In, One Out

The second scenario: one animal is slaughtered inside the *Beit HaMikdash* and the other outside (*echad bachutz ve'echad bifnim*). The Gemara works through both orderings.

First ordering — the first *korban* is slaughtered outside, the second inside. According to the Sages: the first person incurs *karet* for *shechutei chutz*. The second person, restricted by *oto ve'et beno* (since the Sages do not apply *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* to disqualify the first *shechitah*), brings his *korban* inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, which is *pasul u'patur* — the animal is invalid, but there is no further punishment; having been restricted by *oto ve'et beno*, there is no *shechutei chutz* liability. According to Rabbi Shimon: the first animal's *shechitah* outside does not count as a *shechitah* at all, so *oto ve'et beno* is not triggered. The second animal, being unrestricted, is a perfectly valid *korban kasher* when brought inside the *Beit HaMikdash*.

Second ordering — the first *korban* is slaughtered inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, the second outside. According to the Sages: the first is a valid *korban kasher*, and the second is restricted by *oto ve'et beno*, so *sheini pasul u'patur* — exempt from *shechutei chutz*. According to Rabbi Shimon: since *oto ve'et beno* does not apply to *Kodashim* (under the initial premise), the first *shechitah* does not trigger the restriction, and the second animal slaughtered outside incurs the full *lo ta'aseh* of *shechutei chutz* — *sheini b'lo ta'aseh*.

And yet — even after this elaborate reconstruction of the *baraita*, the same difficulty remains. If Rabbi Shimon truly holds that *oto ve'et beno* does not apply to *Kodashim* because *shechitat Kodashim* is a *shechitah she'einah re'uyah*, then whenever the first *korban* is a valid *shechitah kasher* inside the *Beit HaMikdash* (meaning no *oto ve'et beno* restriction operates), the second animal brought outside should incur full *karet* — not merely a *lav*. But the *baraita* attributes only a *lav* to Rabbi Shimon. The rewriting of the *baraita* has not resolved the core tension. We are left exactly where we started.

## Rava's Resolution on Kod…

### Rava Reinterprets

In light of the unresolved difficulty, the Gemara declares: *ela amar Rava* — rather, Rava offers a fundamentally different reading. This rejects the earlier premise entirely. It is not the case that *shechitat Kodashim* is categorically excluded from counting as a *shechitah*. Instead, Rava explains what Rav Hamnuna was actually reporting in the name of Rabbi Shimon: *ein malkus oto ve'et beno noheg b'Kodashim* — there are no *malkos* for *oto ve'et beno* by *Kodashim*, but only under specific circumstances. The prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* does apply to *Kodashim* in principle; what Rabbi Shimon was saying is that *malkos* will typically not be available.

### Meat Permissibility Key

The operative principle is: *keivan d'kamah d'lo zarik dam lo mishtarei basar* — as long as the blood has not been sprinkled, the meat is not yet permissible. Whether any given *shechitah* of a *korban* counts as a *shechitah* will depend entirely on whether the blood is subsequently sprinkled. If one *shachts* the first *korban* and then *shachts* the second *korban* before sprinkling the blood of the first, the first *shechitah* has not yet been "confirmed" as a full *shechitah*. Therefore, no *oto ve'et beno* restriction attaches to the second *shechitah*, and there is no liability.

If, however, one sprinkled the blood of the first *korban* and only then *shachted* the second, then the first *shechitah* is retrospectively confirmed as a full *shechitah*, and the question of whether one has transgressed *oto ve'et beno* becomes live. Yet even in this second case, whether the second *shechitah* constitutes a *shechitah re'uyah* remains conditional on what will happen next: will the blood of the second animal be sprinkled or not? This uncertainty creates the problem of *hasra'at safek*.

### Hasra'at Safek

The Gemara states explicitly: *u-ve-idan de-ka-shachit havei hasra'at safek, ve-hasra'at safek lo shema hasra'ah* — at the moment of the *shechitah*, the warning is a doubtful warning, and a doubtful warning does not count as a warning. To incur *malkos*, one must receive a valid warning (*hasra'ah*) immediately prior to the transgression. Here, at the moment of the transgression — the *shechitah* — it is not yet known whether that act will constitute a halakhic *shechitah*, because that determination will only be made retroactively, depending on whether the blood is subsequently sprinkled. No definitive warning can therefore be given, and without a valid *hasra'ah*, *malkos* cannot be administered.

This is a more coherent position than the earlier one. Rather than declaring that *shechitat Kodashim* never counts as a *shechitah*, Rava says it does count — conditionally. And the reason one never receives *malkos* for *oto ve'et beno* by *Kodashim* is not that the prohibition does not apply, but that the technical conditions for administering *malkos* can never be met at the moment of the transgression.

### Application to Oto Ve'et…

With this framework in place, the Gemara applies Rava's reasoning. *Oto ve'et beno* does apply to *Kodashim* according to Rabbi Shimon. However, *malkos* will not be available whenever the second animal is a *korban*, because at the moment of *shechitah* it is unknown whether the blood will be sprinkled and whether the act will retroactively qualify as a *shechitah*. By contrast, if the first animal was a *korban* and the blood was sprinkled, by the time one comes to *shecht* the second animal, one knows retroactively that the first *shechitah* counted. If the second animal is *chullin*, then upon *shechitah* of the second animal one does transgress *oto ve'et beno* fully, and since the *shechitah* of *chullin* immediately renders the meat permissible, there is no *hasra'at safek* problem for the second act. This now also explains the *baraita*: when Rabbi Shimon says the second animal (*korban* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*) incurs a *lav* but not *karet*, this is because the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* does apply, limiting the eligibility of the second animal. The *Beit HaMikdash* is closed to it today by virtue of *oto ve'et beno*, and therefore while one transgresses a *lav* by slaughtering it outside, one does not incur *karet* — entirely consistent with Rabbi Shimon's own framework.

## Rava's Cases: Valid Slau…

### Chullin Then Olah

Rava further develops the analysis through a series of cases. The first case: one first *shachts* *chullin* and then *shachts* an *olah* inside the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *chullin* *shechitah* is unquestionably a *shechitah re'uyah*, since the meat becomes permissible immediately. Now one proceeds to *shecht* the *olah*. Has one transgressed *oto ve'et beno*? The transgression may have occurred, but — as established — one will never receive *malkos*. At the moment of *shechitah* of the *olah*, it is not yet known whether the blood will be sprinkled. If it is not, the act will not retroactively count as a *shechitah*, and therefore the warning given before the *shechitah* is a *hasra'at safek*. Exempt.

### Olah Then Chullin

The second case: first one *shachts* the *olah* inside the *Beit HaMikdash* and sprinkles the blood, and only then *shachts* the *chullin*. Now the question: is the first *shechitah* — that of the *olah* — a *shechitah re'uyah*? At first glance, this looks more promising. The blood has been sprinkled, confirming that the first act was a *shechitah*. And when one comes to *shecht* the *chullin*, the act of *shechitah* itself renders the meat permissible — so there is no *hasra'at safek* problem for the second animal. One might therefore expect full liability.

### Olah Never Counts

But Rava rules otherwise: *patur* — exempt. The first *shechitah* of the *olah*, even with blood sprinkling, never constitutes a *shechitah re'uyah* in the sense relevant to *oto ve'et beno*. The criterion for *shechitah re'uyah* is whether the *shechitah* renders the meat fit for *achilah* — eating. By an *olah*, the meat is never eaten. An *olah* is entirely burned on the *mizbe'ach*; no part of it is consumed by a person. Therefore, even after blood sprinkling, the meat of an *olah* has never become permissible for eating. The *shechitah* of an *olah* is thus permanently *shechitah she'einah re'uyah*, and no *oto ve'et beno* restriction flows from it.

### Achilas Mizbe'ach

One might object: the altar "consumes" the *olah* — is not the burning on the *mizbe'ach* itself a form of *achilah*? This is precisely the question Rava addresses by citing the ruling of Rav Yaakov in the name of Rav Yochanan: *achilas mizbe'ach shema achilah* — consumption by the altar is considered eating. The proof is from a verse regarding *piggul*: *v'im he'achol ye'achel mibesar zevach shlamav* — "if the meat will be eaten." The Gemara derives from this verse *bishtei achilos hakatuv m'daber* — the verse speaks of two types of eating: *achilas adam* (human consumption) and *achilas mizbe'ach* (altar consumption). The *piggul* prohibition applies equally whether one intends to eat the meat at the wrong time or to burn the forbidden parts on the altar at the wrong time. Both constitute *achilah*.

So the principle of *achilas mizbe'ach shema achilah* is established. Yet this means that the *shechitah* of an *olah* does render the meat "fit for eating" — eating by the altar. Why then does Rava exempt from *oto ve'et beno*? The resolution, as Rava himself has now established, is that *shechitah re'uyah* for purposes of *oto ve'et beno* requires that the *shechitah* render the meat permissible for human *achilah*, not merely altar consumption. Since the meat of an *olah* can never be eaten by a person, its *shechitah* never qualifies as a *shechitah re'uyah*, and *oto ve'et beno* is never triggered by it — in either direction.

To summarize Rava's framework: *oto ve'et beno* does apply to *Kodashim* in principle, but *malkos* will typically not result. If the second animal is a *korban*, there is always *hasra'at safek* at the moment of *shechitah*. If the first animal is a *korban* (other than an *olah*) and the blood is sprinkled, then by the time one reaches the second *shechitah* the first is confirmed as having counted — and liability for the second depends on whether the second *shechitah* itself immediately renders the meat permissible. An *olah* is categorically excluded from ever counting as a *shechitah re'uyah* for these purposes, since its meat never becomes permissible for human consumption.

## Mishnah: Unfit Slaughter

### Mishnah's Cases

The next Mishnah now addresses Rabbi Shimon's position directly. The cases enumerated are: one who *shachts* and the animal is found to be a *tereifah* (fatally flawed, forbidden for eating though permitted in benefit); one who *shachts* for *avodah zarah* (idolatrous worship, which renders the animal forbidden in benefit); one who *shachts* a *parah adumah* (the Red Heifer, from which no benefit may be derived); a *shor haniskal* (an ox condemned to be stoned for goring, forbidden in benefit); and an *eglah arufah* (the calf whose neck is broken in the valley, which attains a certain status of being forbidden in benefit even before the rite is performed, if *shachted* at that stage).

### Shimon vs. Sages Dispute

In all of these cases, Rabbi Shimon exempts from *oto ve'et beno*, because in each instance the *shechitah* does not render the meat permissible — each is a *shechitah she'einah re'uyah*. The Sages obligate in all these cases, rejecting Rabbi Shimon's application of *shechitah she'einah re'uyah*.

### Agreed Cases

There are, however, cases upon which everyone agrees one does not transgress *oto ve'et beno*. If one *shachts* and the animal is found to be a *neveilah* — meaning the *shechitah* itself was botched, so that the act was not even a *shechitah* at all — all agree one is exempt. Similarly, *hanocher* (one who kills the animal without engaging the *simanim*) and *hame'aker* (one who tears out the *simanim* rather than cutting them) — in all such cases where the killing was not performed through *shechitah*, everyone agrees: *patur mishum oto ve'et beno*. One may have violated *tzar ba'alei chayim*, but *oto ve'et beno* requires a *shechitah*, and these acts do not qualify as one.

## Slaughter for Idolat (II)

### Idolatry First

Having covered *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* extensively, the Gemara now turns to a distinct and fascinating question raised by the case of *shechitah* for *avodah zarah*: does one's participation in idolatrous worship affect the analysis of *oto ve'et beno*? Reish Lakish establishes the basic parameters. When the Sages say that *shechitah* for *avodah zarah* still renders one *chayav* for *oto ve'et beno*, this applies specifically when *rishon la'avodah zarah v'sheni l'shulchano* — the first animal was *shachted* for idolatry and the second for ordinary consumption. In that case, the act of idolatrous worship is complete before the second *shechitah*, and the second *shechitah* for consumption fully triggers *oto ve'et beno*.

### Eating First

However, if the first animal was *shachted* for consumption and the second for *avodah zarah*, Reish Lakish rules *patur* — exempt from *oto ve'et beno*. The reason is *kam lei bidrabba minei* — one's liability is absorbed by the greater punishment. The act of *shachting* the second animal for idolatrous worship constitutes both a transgression of *oto ve'et beno* and an act of worshipping *avodah zarah*, which carries the death penalty. When a single act encompasses two punishments, one receives only the greater: *risha achas atem mechayvo v'ee attah mechayvo bishtei risha'uyos*. Since *avodah zarah* carries *misah*, the *oto ve'et beno* liability — which carries only *malkos* — is absorbed.

### Yochanan's Objection

Rabbi Yochanan's response to Reish Lakish is sharp: *zu afilu tinokos shel beis rabban yodim* — this even schoolchildren know. *Kam lei bidrabba minei* is a basic principle; the case Reish Lakish described is too obvious to require articulation by an *Amora* of his stature.

### Yochanan's Exception

Rabbi Yochanan therefore argues that Reish Lakish should have focused on the genuinely novel case: *pe'amim she'afilu shachat rishon l'shulchano v'sheni la'avodah zarah chayav* — there are circumstances in which even when the second *shechitah* is for *avodah zarah*, one is still *chayav* for *oto ve'et beno*. This is the *chiddush* worth stating.

### Partial Warning Exception

When is one *chayav* for *oto ve'et beno* even when the second *shechitah* was for idolatry? *Kegon de'asru bei mishum oto ve'et beno v'lo asru bei mishum avodah zarah* — when witnesses warned him only for the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* and not for the prohibition of *avodah zarah*. If the witnesses did not properly warn him about *avodah zarah*, then the death penalty cannot be administered — not because of *kam lei bidrabba minei* (since that would only operate if one were actually being punished for *avodah zarah*), but because the *hasra'ah* for *avodah zarah* was absent. Without a valid *hasra'ah* for the greater sin, *kam lei bidrabba minei* does not operate to absorb the lesser. The warning for *oto ve'et beno* was valid, and since no death penalty is in play, the *malkos* for *oto ve'et beno* stand.

## Slaughter for Idolat (II)

### Lakish's Position

The Gemara asks why Reish Lakish did not state this exception. The answer is that Reish Lakish holds a more expansive view of *kam lei bidrabba minei*: *keivan dechi asru bei patur, ki lo asru bei nami patur* — since were one warned for *avodah zarah* one would be exempt from *oto ve'et beno*, even without that warning, one is also exempt. According to Reish Lakish, the exemption is not a function of whether punishment is actually administered for the greater sin; it is a function of whether the sin itself — of sufficient severity — was committed. The very presence of the capital sin overshadows the lesser transgression, regardless of whether warning was given for it.

## Greater Sin Overshadows…

### Rav Dimi's Report

This dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is not limited to the case of *avodah zarah*. Rav Dimi, upon arriving from *Eretz Yisrael*, reported a parallel dispute that makes the underlying principle explicit. The question: if one commits *chayvei misos shogegin* — an act deserving the death penalty, performed unintentionally — or *chayvei malkiyos shoggegin* — an act deserving *malkos*, performed unintentionally — together with *davar acher*, some other liability (here a civil/monetary liability, *mammon*), is the lesser liability absorbed by the greater sin?

### Yochanan: Punishment-Bas…

Rabbi Yochanan rules *chayav* — the civil liability is not absorbed. Since the person was not warned for the greater sin and in fact did not even perform it intentionally, there is no prospect of the greater punishment being administered. The principle of absorbing the lesser punishment only operates when one is actually being given the greater punishment. Here, where no greater punishment is forthcoming, the lesser liability applies in full.

### Lakish: Sin-Based

Reish Lakish rules *patur* — exempt from the civil liability as well. His reasoning: *keivan de-ee asru bei patur, ki lo asru bei nami patur* — since were warning given for the capital offense one would be exempt from the civil liability, even without warning one is equally exempt. And even more strikingly: the case of *chayvei misos shoggegin* involves not merely the absence of a warning, but the absence of intent altogether. The person was never deserving of the death penalty. Yet Reish Lakish still holds that the gravity of the act — independently of any question of punishment — overshadows the civil liability. For Reish Lakish, it is the sin itself, not the punishment actually imposed, that does the overshadowing.

This is a striking Gemara. For Rabbi Yochanan, *kam lei bidrabba minei* is fundamentally a procedural principle: we do not apply two punishments simultaneously, so we apply the greater. If the greater is not being applied, the lesser operates normally. For Reish Lakish, it is a substantive principle about the nature of sin: the presence of a weighty sin, even uncommitted with full culpability, eclipses a lesser transgression entirely.

### Why Both Cases Needed

The Gemara notes that it is necessary to state the dispute in both cases — the case involving *malkos* alongside *mammon*, and the case involving *misah* alongside the lesser liability. Were only one case stated, one might think Reish Lakish's position is limited to that case alone and that he would agree with Rabbi Yochanan in the other. The explicit articulation of both cases forecloses the possibility of such limitation: the positions of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish are consistent across both scenarios.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

Which case is more obvious and which is the one that needed to be stated? This is the subject of a classic debate between Rashi and *Tosefos*.

### Rashi's Approach

Rashi holds that the case of civil liability (*mammon*) is the less obvious one that more needed to be explicitly stated. The reasoning: when comparing a death penalty or lashes with civil liability, the two categories are qualitatively different. Court-administered corporal punishment and civil obligations to another party belong to entirely separate domains. One might have thought that *kam lei bidrabba minei*, which governs the relationship among criminal punishments, could not extend to absorb a civil liability — since it is not even a "punishment" in the same sense. Therefore, Rashi argues, I might have thought Reish Lakish would agree with Rabbi Yochanan in the civil liability case: the absence of actual punishment for the capital sin means there is nothing to overshadow a civil obligation. It must therefore be stated explicitly that Reish Lakish extends his principle even to this case.

### Tosafot's Approach

*Tosefos* reaches the opposite conclusion. Payment of money is, by definition, the lightest form of consequence — it is relatively trivial compared to *malkos* or *misah*. Precisely because of how light it is, it seems entirely intuitive that a grave sin would overshadow it; no explicit statement is needed to teach that. The less obvious case is the one involving *malkos*. One might have supposed that Reish Lakish only held that a capital sin overshadows a civil liability — since the gap in severity is so vast — but would concede to Rabbi Yochanan that if one is not being punished for the capital sin, one should still receive the lesser punishment of *malkos*. It must therefore be stated explicitly that Reish Lakish's position extends to the *malkos* case as well.

In either reading, the positions of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish are clear and consistent. Rabbi Yochanan: no punishment for the greater means full liability for the lesser. Reish Lakish: the gravity of the sin itself overshadows, whether the lesser consequence is monetary or corporal.
