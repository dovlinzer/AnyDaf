# Zevachim 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Blood Services

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁנִּשְׁחְטוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָם – כְּשֵׁרִים, אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשֵׁם חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **All offerings that were slaughtered not for their own sake are fit,** but **they do not fulfill the obligation of the owner who brought them.**

The opening *mishnah* of *Masechet Zevachim* establishes the foundational principle governing the entire tractate: *kol hazvachim shenizbechu shelo lishmam kesherim ela shelo alu labaalim leshem chovah* — all sacrifices that were slaughtered not for their own sake are valid, yet they do not fulfill the obligation of the owner who brought them. This is the backdrop against which the Gemara on daf 4 operates. The task before us is to trace the textual proofs establishing that each of the four blood services must be performed *lishma* — with proper intent.

### Dimensions of Intent

The requirement of *lishma* operates along two distinct axes. The first concerns the *kodesh* of the offering — that is, the type of sacrifice. If a *kohen* sets out to bring a *korban shelamim*, the intent throughout must remain fixed on it being a *shelamim* and not drift toward some other category, such as an *olah* or a *chatat*. The second axis concerns the *ba'alei hakorban*, the owners of the sacrifice. The person for whom the offering is brought must be the one in whose name it is performed; one may not redirect the offering toward a different individual. Both dimensions of intent — *shinui kodesh* (change in the type of offering) and *shinui ba'alim* (change in the owner) — must be examined separately for each of the four services.

### Four Services

The four *avodot hadam* — the blood services — are *shechitah* (slaughter), *kabbalah* (collection of the blood), *zrikah* (sprinkling of the blood), and *holachah* (carrying the blood to the altar). Each service must be shown, through its own textual derivation, to require *lishma*. The services share certain features with one another, but each also possesses unique characteristics that prevent a simple inference from one to the others. The analysis of the *daf* proceeds through this comparison methodically, examining each service in turn for both the *kodesh* and *ba'alim* dimensions.

## Shechitah & Kodesh

### Vayikra 3:1 Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן דְּבָעֵינַן זְבִיחָה לִשְׁמָהּ? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִם זֶבַח שְׁלָמִים קׇרְבָּנוֹ״ – שֶׁתְּהֵא זְבִיחָה לְשֵׁם [שְׁלָמִים].
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that we require** that an offering's **slaughter** be performed **for its own sake?** It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "And if his offering is a sacrifice [*zevaḥ*] of peace offerings"** (Leviticus 3:1), teaching **that slaughter [*zeviḥa*] must be** performed **for the sake of a peace offering.**

The Gemara opens with the question: *menalan d'ba'inan zvicha lishma?* — from where do we derive that the *shechitah* must be performed *lishma*, for the sake of the offering? The answer is drawn from Vayikra 3:1: *ve'im zevach shelamim korbano* — and if his offering is a sacrifice of peace offerings. The verse contains what appears to be a redundancy: it might have said simply *ve'im shelamim korbano*, or alternatively *ve'im zevach korbano*. The presence of both terms — *zevach* and *shelamim* together — is the source of the *derashah*.

### Redundancy Analysis

From the double expression *zevach shelamim*, the Gemara derives *shetehei zvicha leshem hashelamim* — that the slaughtering must be performed for the sake of the *shelamim*. The word *zevach* is superfluous unless it comes to teach that the act of slaughter itself must be oriented toward the identity of the offering.

### Zevach as Name

One might object: perhaps *zevach shelamim* is simply the proper name of this category of sacrifice, just as we speak of a *korban olah* or a *chatat*. If so, there is no redundancy to analyze — the Torah is merely using the offering's full title.

### Not the Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדִּכְתִיב ״הַמַּקְרִיב אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים״, ״הַזּוֹרֵק אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים״ – וְלָא כְּתִיב ״זֶבַח״; וְהָכָא כְּתִיב ״זֶבַח״; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: שֶׁתְּהֵא זְבִיחָה לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **From** the fact **that it is written** in other verses: **"Who offers the blood of the peace offering"** (Leviticus 7:33), and: **"Who sprinkles the blood of the peace offering"** (Leviticus 7:14), **and** the term: **A sacrifice [*zevaḥ*], is not written,** whereas **here,** in this verse, the term: **"A sacrifice [*zevaḥ*]** of peace offerings," **is written, learn from it that** the **slaughter [*zeviḥa*] must be** performed **for the sake of a peace offering.**

This objection is refuted by examining other verses. In Vayikra 7:33, the Torah writes *hamakriv et dam hashelamim* — the one who brings the blood of the *shelamim* — and in Vayikra 7:14, *hazorek et dam hashelamim* — the one who sprinkles the blood of the *shelamim*. In neither case does the Torah write *dam zevach hashelamim*. If *zevach shelamim* were truly the formal name of the offering, it would appear consistently whenever the offering is referenced. The absence of the word *zevach* in these parallel contexts demonstrates that it is not a fixed name.

### Shechitah Conclusion

Since *zevach shelamim* is not merely a title but a meaningful locution, the presence of *zevach* in the verse teaches something substantive: *shema mina shetehei zvicha leshem shelamim* — we derive from here that the *shechitah* must be performed for the sake of the *shelamim*. The first of the four blood services has been anchored in a textual proof.

## Kabbalah & Kodesh

### Learn from Shechitah?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן זְבִיחָה, שְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת מְנָלַן? וְכִי תֵּימָא לֵילַף מִזְּבִיחָה; מָה לִזְבִיחָה – שֶׁכֵּן נִפְסְלָה שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם אוֹכְלִין בַּפֶּסַח!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that **slaughter** must be performed for the sake of the offering being sacrificed. **From where do we** derive that the **other** sacrificial **rites,** i.e., collecting the blood, conveying it to the altar, and sprinkling it on the altar, must also be performed for the sake of the offering? **And if you would say: Let us derive from** the fact that **slaughter** must be performed for the sake of the offering that the other sacrificial rites must be performed for the sake of the offering as well, this derivation can be refuted, as slaughter has a unique element of stringency: **What** is unique **about slaughter?** It is unique **in that with regard to a Paschal offering,** the offering is **disqualified** if it is slaughtered **not for the sake of those who eat** it. If the Paschal offering is slaughtered for the sake of people who are incapable of eating it, such as one who is too ill to eat, it is disqualified. By contrast, performing other rites with this intent does not disqualify the offering. Therefore, one cannot assume that a *halakha* that applies to slaughter applies to other rites as well.

Having established that *shechitah* requires *lishma*, the Gemara asks: *ve'chi teima leilaf mizvicha* — perhaps we can simply derive all the other services from *shechitah*? If slaughter must be *lishma*, surely collection, sprinkling, and carrying must be as well?

The Gemara introduces the principle that each service possesses its own unique stringencies that prevent a straightforward inference from one to another. The services are, in a sense, like a Venn diagram: they share certain features but not all, and these differences block the extension of any single derivation across the board.

### Pesach Limitation

The *shechitah* carries a special stringency that does not apply to the other services: *sheken nifsela shelo leshem ochlav bepesach* — it is disqualified in the case of the *Pesach* offering if it is not slaughtered with intent toward those who will eat it. If the person bringing the *korban Pesach* is somehow unable to eat the requisite amount — whether due to illness or any other incapacity — the offering is disqualified. This unique *chumra* of *shechitah* with regard to *Pesach* means that *shechitah* is not a neutral template from which we can simply derive the other services.

### Vayikra 7:33 Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר קְרָא: ״הַמַּקְרִיב אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים״ – שֶׁתְּהֵא קַבָּלָה לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather, the verse states: "Who sacrifices [*hamakriv*] the blood of the peace offering"** (Leviticus 7:33), referring to the one who collects the blood of the offering, as the Gemara will explain. This phrase indicates **that** the **collection** of the blood **must be** performed **for the sake of a peace offering.**

A separate derivation is therefore required for *kabbalah*. The Gemara cites Vayikra 7:33: *hamakriv et dam hashelamim* — the one who brings near the blood of the *shelamim*. The verb *hamakriv*, from the root *karov* (to be near, to bring close), is understood as indicating the act of collecting the blood — *kabbalah*. From the fact that this verse specifically identifies the blood as being of the *shelamim*, the Gemara derives *shetehei kabbalah leshem shelamim* — that the collection of the blood must also be performed for the sake of the *shelamim*. In other contexts the Torah simply says *dam*, blood, without reattaching the identity of the offering; here the repetition of *shelamim* teaches that *kabbalah* must be intentional regarding the type of offering.

### Distinction Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בְּקַבָּלָה, וְלֵילַף שְׁחִיטָה מִינַּהּ! מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ – מָה לְקַבָּלָה שֶׁכֵּן פְּסוּלָה בְּזָר וְאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And let the Merciful One write** this *halakha* in the Torah only **with regard to** the **collection** of the blood, **and we would derive from it** that **slaughter** must also be performed for the sake of the offering. The Gemara answers: **Because** such a derivation **can be refuted** as follows: **What** is unique **about collection** of the blood? It is unique **in that it is not valid** if performed **by a non-priest or a woman,** whereas slaughter can be performed by any competent Jew. Therefore, one cannot assume that a *halakha* that applies to collection applies to slaughter as well.

One might then ask: since we now have a verse for *kabbalah*, why not derive *shechitah* from *kabbalah* rather than the other way around? The Gemara addresses this by noting that the traffic of inference cannot simply be reversed. Each derivation is anchored in a specific textual excess, and the question of which service teaches which cannot be settled by reversing the direction of the argument. The Gemara's framing — *velichtov rachmana bakkabbalah veneilef shechita minnah* — presents this as a challenge: let the Torah have written the rule only for *kabbalah*, and we would learn *shechitah* from it. The response is *mishum deika lemeifrach* — because there is a refutation. The *kabbalah* has its own unique stringency: *sheken pesulah bezar ve'ishah* — it is disqualified if performed by a non-*kohen* or a woman. This stringency does not apply to *shechitah*, and therefore we cannot derive *shechitah* from *kabbalah* any more than we can derive *kabbalah* from *shechitah*. Each requires its own independent proof.

### Kohen Requirement

This last point warrants emphasis. While *shechitah* may be performed by virtually anyone — a *zar* (non-*kohen*), a woman, even in principle the owner of the animal — *kabbalah* is categorically restricted to a male *kohen*. This is a dimension of stringency entirely absent from *shechitah*, and it is precisely this difference that blocks the inference between them.

## Zrikah & Kodesh

### Learn from Others?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן שְׁחִיטָה וְקַבָּלָה, זְרִיקָה מְנָלַן? וְכִי תֵּימָא לֵילַף מֵהָנֵי – מָה לְהָנֵי שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנוֹת צָפוֹן, וְיֶשְׁנָן בְּחַטָּאוֹת הַפְּנִימִיּוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that **slaughter and collection** of the blood must be performed for the sake of the offering. **From where do we** derive that this *halakha* applies to the **sprinkling** of the blood as well? **And if you would say: Let us derive from these** rites, i.e., slaughter and collection, that this *halakha* applies to the sprinkling of the blood as well, this derivation can be refuted: **What** is unique **about these** rites? They are unique **in that** for offerings of the most sacred order, it is **required** that they be performed in the **north** of the Temple courtyard. **And** furthermore, these rites **are** performed **in the** sacrifice of **inner sin offerings** as well as standard sin offerings. The sprinkling of the blood on the external altar, by contrast, is performed in all portions of the Temple courtyard, and it is not performed in the sacrifice of inner sin offerings, whose blood is sprinkled only in the Sanctuary.

With *shechitah* and *kabbalah* both established, the Gemara proceeds to *zrikah*: *zrikah menalan?* — from where do we derive that the sprinkling of the blood must be *lishma*? And as before, the Gemara first considers whether we might simply derive it from the two already-established services.

### Location Distinction

The proposed derivation is rejected on familiar grounds. What is unique about *shechitah* and *kabbalah* that does not apply to *zrikah*? The Gemara notes: *mah hanni sheken te'unot tzafon* — these services are unique in that they require a specific location. For *kodshei kodashim*, *shechitah* and *kabbalah* must be performed in the northern section of the Temple courtyard. The *zrikah*, by contrast, may be performed from any position in the courtyard. This locational stringency that characterizes *shechitah* and *kabbalah* is absent from *zrikah*, and therefore we cannot extend the rule automatically.

### Vayikra 7:14 Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר קְרָא: ״הַזּוֹרֵק אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים״ – שֶׁתְּהֵא זְרִיקָה לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather, the verse states: "Who sprinkles the blood of the peace offering"** (Leviticus 7:14), indicating **that** the **sprinkling** of the blood **must be** performed **for the sake of a peace offering.**

A fresh prooftext is therefore required. The Gemara finds it in Vayikra 7:14: *hazorek et dam hashelamim* — the one who sprinkles the blood of the *shelamim*. Just as with the *kabbalah* verse, the explicit attachment of *shelamim* to the act of *zrikah* teaches *shetehei zrikah leshem shelamim* — that the sprinkling must be performed with intent toward the *shelamim*.

### Death Penalty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בִּזְרִיקָה, וְלֵילַף מִינַּהּ! מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ – מָה לִזְרִיקָה, שֶׁכֵּן חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ זָר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And let the Merciful One write** this *halakha* only **with regard to sprinkling, and we would derive from it** that it applies to slaughter and collection as well. The Gemara answers: That would not be sufficient, **because** such a derivation **can be refuted,** as sprinkling has a unique element of stringency. **What** is unique **about sprinkling?** It is unique **in that a non-priest is liable to** be punished with **death** at the hand of Heaven **for** performing **it.**

Could we reverse the inference and derive *shechitah* and *kabbalah* from *zrikah* instead? No — because *zrikah* possesses its own unique stringency: *mah lezrikah sheken chayav aleha zar mitah* — a *zar* who performs *zrikah* is liable to death (or *karet*). This is a severity that does not attach to *shechitah* and is likewise absent from *kabbalah* in the same degree. The unique stringency of each service thus both prevents upward inference from it and justifies the need for its own independent derivation.

## Holachah & Kodesh

### Learn from Others?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן כּוּלְּהוּ, הוֹלָכָה מְנָלַן? וְכִי תֵּימָא לֵילַף מִכּוּלְּהוּ; מָה לְכוּלְּהוּ – שֶׁכֵּן עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לְבַטְּלָהּ; תֹּאמַר בְּהוֹלָכָה – שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לְבַטְּלָהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for this *halakha* with regard to **all** four rites save **conveying** the blood to the altar. **From where do we** derive that this latter rite must also be performed for the sake of the offering? **And if you would say: Let us derive from all** the other rites that conveying the blood must also be performed for the sake of the offering, this derivation can also be refuted: **What** is unique **about all of** the other rites? They are unique **in that** each of them is **an indispensable rite. Would you say** that any requirement that applies to those rites necessarily applies also **with regard to conveying** the blood, **which is dispensable?** If an offering is slaughtered by the side of the altar, it is unnecessary to convey its blood to the altar.

Three services established, the Gemara turns to *holachah*, the carrying of the blood from the site of slaughter to the altar: *ashkacha kulhu holachah menalan?* — we have found sources for all the others, where do we derive *holachah*?

### Dispensability

The Gemara immediately anticipates the attempt to derive *holachah* from the other three, and preempts it: *mah lekulhu sheken avodah she-i efshar levatlah tomar beholachah she-efshar levatlah?* — what is unique about all the other services? Each of them is indispensable — the offering cannot be completed without them. *Holachah*, by contrast, is dispensable. If the *shechitah* takes place adjacent to the altar, there is nowhere to carry the blood — it is already where it needs to be. One does not perform *holachah* in such a case. An obligation that can be bypassed entirely under certain circumstances cannot automatically be assimilated to obligations that are always mandatory.

### Vayikra 1 Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהִקְרִיב אֶת הַכֹּל הַמִּזְבֵּחָה״, וְאָמַר מָר: זוֹ הוֹלָכַת אֵבָרִים לַכֶּבֶשׁ; וְתַנְיָא: ״וְהִקְרִיבוּ״ – זוֹ קַבָּלַת הַדָּם; וְאַפְּיִק רַחֲמָנָא בִּלְשׁוֹן הוֹלָכָה – לְמֵימְרָא דְּהוֹלָכָה לָא תַּפְּקַהּ מִכְּלַל קַבָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather, the verse states: "And** the priest **shall sacrifice [*vehikriv*] the whole,** and make it smoke **upon the altar"** (Leviticus 1:13), **and the Master said** that **this** is referring to **conveying** the **limbs** of the offering **to the ramp** of the altar. **And it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"And** Aaron's sons, the priests, **shall present [*vehikrivu*]** the blood" (Leviticus 1:5), that **this** is referring to the **collection of the blood. And** one can infer that **the Merciful One expresses** collection of the blood **in the** same **language** used for **conveying, to say that you should not exclude conveying from the category of collection** of the blood. Therefore, the *halakhot* of collection apply to conveying.

The prooftext for *holachah* is derived from the first chapter of Vayikra, where the verb *vehikriv* — and he shall bring near — appears in the context of the *olah*. The Sages interpret *vehikriv hamizbecha* as referring to the carrying of the limbs up the *kevesh*, the ramp leading to the altar. The language of *lehakriv* — bringing close — generates the *derashah* for *holachah lishma*.

### Holachah & Kabbalah

A deeper connection emerges from the parallel usage. The same expression, *lehakriv*, is used for both *holachah* and *kabbalah* in adjacent verses: *vehikriv* refers to carrying, and *ve'hikrivu* is understood as *kabbalat hadam*. The Gemara derives from this parallel that *holachah* is linked to *kabbalah*: *ve'afik rachmana bilshon holacha lemeimra deholacha lo tafka miklal kabala* — the Torah expressed the collection of the blood using language associated with *holachah* to teach that *holachah* should not be excluded from the category of *kabbalah* in terms of the *lishma* requirement. The two are connected, and what is demanded of one is demanded of the other in this respect.

## Shechitah & Owner

### Owner Intent Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַשְׁכְּחַן שִׁנּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ, שִׁנּוּי בְּעָלִים מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב פִּנְחָס בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַמֵּי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּבְשַׂר זֶבַח תּוֹדַת שְׁלָמָיו״ – שֶׁתְּהֵא זְבִיחָה לְשֵׁם תּוֹדָה. אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְשִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ – דְּנָפְקָא לַן מֵהָתָם; תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְשִׁינּוּי בְעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **And we found** a source for the *halakha* concerning **deviation** with regard to the type of **offering,** i.e., that an offering may not be slaughtered for the sake of a different type of offering. **From where do we** derive the *halakha* concerning **deviation** with regard to the **owner,** i.e., that an offering may not be slaughtered for the sake of one who is not the owner? **Rav Pinḥas, son of Rav Ami, says: The verse states** with regard to a thanks offering: **"And the meat of the sacrifice [*zevaḥ*] of his thanksgiving peace offering** shall be eaten on the day of his offering" (Leviticus 7:15), apparently indicating **that** its **slaughter [*zeviḥa*] must be** performed **for the sake of a thanks offering.** And **if** this language **is not** needed **for the matter of deviation** with regard to the type of **offering,** and in fact it is not, **as we** already **derived** this *halakha* **from there,** i.e., the verse cited concerning peace offerings, **apply it** to the **matter of deviation** with regard to the **owner.** It is thereby derived that an offering must be slaughtered for the sake of its owner.

Having established that all four blood services must be performed *lishma* with respect to the type of offering (*shinui kodesh*), the Gemara turns to the second dimension: the owner. *Ashkechan shinui kodesh, shinui be'alim minalan?* — we have found the sources for change-of-offering-type; from where do we derive the rules regarding change of owner?

### Todah Meat Law

Rav Pinchas brei deRav Ami cites Vayikra chapter 7: *uvasar zevach todat shelamav* — and the meat of the sacrifice of his thanksgiving peace offering. The *todah*, the thanksgiving offering, has a uniquely stringent eating restriction: it must be eaten on the day of the offering and the following night. The Gemara reads this verse as teaching two things. First, *shetehei zvicha leshem todah* — that the *shechitah* must be performed for the sake of the *todah*. Second, it raises the question: does the word *zevach* here come to teach the *shinui kodesh* requirement, or the *shinui ba'alim* requirement? Since the *shinui kodesh* requirement for *shechitah* has already been derived elsewhere, the Gemara applies the *im eino inyan* principle — if a teaching is unnecessary for one matter, apply it to another. Here, *zevach* is redirected to teach the *shinui ba'alim* requirement: the *shechitah* must be performed for the sake of the owner.

The purpose of this mechanism is worth pausing over. The *todah* offering is brought by someone in gratitude — for surviving illness, a sea voyage, imprisonment, or crossing a desert. Its meat must be eaten quickly, forcing the owner to invite others to share it, thereby publicizing his gratitude and, in turn, the *shem Hashem*. When the Torah says *zevach todat shelamav*, it is associating the act of slaughter with the identity and purpose of the owner's offering.

### Todah vs Shelamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?! הָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״וּבְשַׂר זֶבַח תּוֹדַת שְׁלָמָיו״ – אַבָּא חָנִין אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: בָּא לְלַמֵּד, תּוֹדָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים – כְּשֵׁרָה, שְׁלָמִים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן לְשֵׁם תּוֹדָה – פְּסוּלָה. מָה הֶפְרֵשׁ בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה? תּוֹדָה קְרוּיָה שְׁלָמִים, וְאֵין שְׁלָמִים קְרוּיִין תּוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But does this** verse **come to** teach **this** *halakha*? **Isn't it necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: Concerning the phrase **"and the meat of the sacrifice of his thanksgiving peace offering," Abba Ḥanin says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer:** It **comes to teach** that **a thanks offering that one slaughtered for the sake of a peace offering is fit;** whereas **a peace offering that one slaughtered for the sake of a thanks offering is unfit. What** is the **difference between this** offering **and that** offering? **A thanks offering is called a peace offering** in that verse, **but a peace offering is not called a thanks offering.**

This leads to a teaching of Abba Chanin in the name of Rabbi Eliezer, cited from a *beraita*: *todah she-shechatah le-shem shelamim, kesherah. Shelamim she-shechatan le-shem todah, pesulah.* A *todah* slaughtered with the intent of a *shelamim* is valid; a *shelamim* slaughtered with the intent of a *todah* is disqualified. The asymmetry is explained: *todah keruyah shelamim ve-ein shelamim keruyim todah* — a *todah* may be called a *shelamim*, but a *shelamim* may not be called a *todah*. The *todah* is a subspecies of *shelamim*, so directing it toward the broader category is permissible. The reverse, however, takes the *shelamim* outside its own category and into a more specific one it has not been designated for — and that disqualifies it. The relationship is one-directional, like a set and its subset.

### Zevach Teaches Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲנַן מִ״זֶּבַח״ קָאָמְרִינַן. וְאַכַּתִּי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״זֶבַח״! אִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא: ״וּבְשַׂר תּוֹדַת שְׁלָמָיו [זֶבַח]״; מַאי ״זֶבַח״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **We are saying** that it is derived **from** the superfluous term: **"Sacrifice [*zevaḥ*],"** that the slaughter [*zeviḥa*] must be performed for the sake of the owner. The term "peace offering" teaches Abba Ḥanin's ruling. The Gemara disputes this: **But** the term "sacrifice [*zevaḥ*]" is **still necessary** to serve as the source for another halakhic midrash: **From where** is it derived that the meat of **a sin offering and a guilt offering** may be eaten only on the day the animal is sacrificed and the following night, like a thanks offering? **The verse states:** "And the meat of the **sacrifice [*zevaḥ*]** of his thanksgiving peace offering shall be eaten on the day of his offering." The phrase teaches that not only thanks offerings, but any slaughtered offering [*zevaḥ*] is subject to this time limit unless the Torah specifies otherwise. The Gemara answers: **If that were so,** i.e., if the term "sacrifice" indicated only that a sin offering and a guilt offering may be eaten only for the period of time for which a thanks offering may be eaten, **let the verse write: And the meat of his thanksgiving peace offering sacrifice. What** is indicated by the mention of the word **sacrifice** before the phrase "his thanksgiving peace offering"? Rather, **conclude two** conclusions **from it.**

A further nuance: the word *zevach* in this verse is also needed to extend the time restriction of the *todah* to other offerings, such as the *chatat* and *asham*. One might therefore argue that the word is already occupied and cannot be reassigned to teach *shinui ba'alim*. The Gemara responds that if the verse had wanted only to teach the eating restriction, it could have written *uvasar todat shelamav zevach* — placing *zevach* differently. Since it is written as *uvasar zevach todat shelamav*, the term *zevach* here carries multiple teachings. *Mah zevach? Shema mina tarti* — what does *zevach* teach? We derive two conclusions from it: both the *todah*'s extended rules and the requirement of *lishma* with regard to the owner.

## Owner Intent Others

### Learn All from Shechitah?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן זְבִיחָה, שְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת מְנָלַן? וְכִי תֵּימָא לֵילַף מִזְּבִיחָה – מָה לִזְבִיחָה שֶׁכֵּן פּוֹסֵל שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם אוֹכְלִין בַּפֶּסַח!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that **slaughter** must be performed for the sake of the owner. **From where do we** derive that the **other** sacrificial **rites** must also be performed for the sake of the owner? **And if you would say: Let us derive from slaughter** that the other rites must be performed for the sake of the owner as well, this derivation can be refuted: **What** is unique **about slaughter?** It is unique **in that with regard to a Paschal offering,** slaughter **not for the sake of those who eat** it **disqualifies** the offering, whereas performing the other rites not for the sake of the owner does not.

The Gemara now replicates the same analytical structure used for *shinui kodesh*, this time for *shinui ba'alim*. We have established that *shechitah* must be performed for the sake of the owner. Can we derive the same requirement for all four services from *shechitah* alone?

### Pesach Limitation

The answer is no, for the same reason encountered before: *mah lizevichah sheken posel shelo lishmo ochlim uvapesach* — the *shechitah* is uniquely stringent in the case of *Pesach*, where slaughtering with intent toward those who will eat it is critical. If the person who is to eat the *Pesach* is unable to eat the requisite amount at the time of *shechitah* — for instance, if the owner becomes *tamei* between the time of slaughter and the time of *zrikah* — the offering may be disqualified. This *chumra* is particular to *shechitah* and does not map onto the other services. Therefore *shechitah* cannot serve as a universal template for *shinui ba'alim* either.

### Four Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֶמְרָה זְבִיחָה בְּשִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ, וְנֶאֶמְרָה זְבִיחָה בְּשִׁינּוּי בְעָלִים; מָה זְבִיחָה הָאֲמוּרָה בְּשִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ – לֹא חִלַּקְתָּ בֵּין זְבִיחָה לִשְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת, אַף זְבִיחָה הָאֲמוּרָה בְּשִׁינּוּי בְעָלִים – לֹא תְּחַלֵּק בָּהֶן בֵּין זְבִיחָה לִשְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת. אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְשִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ – שֶׁכֵּן פְּסוּלָהּ בְּגוּפָהּ, וְיֶשְׁנוֹ בְּאַרְבַּע עֲבוֹדוֹת, וְיֶשְׁנוֹ לְאַחַר מִיתָה, וְיֶשְׁנוֹ בְּצִיבּוּר כִּבְיָחִיד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: A term of **slaughter is stated with regard to deviation** from the type of **offering, and** a term of **slaughter is stated with regard to deviation** with regard to the **owner. Just as** concerning the term **slaughter stated with regard to deviation** from the type of **offering, you did not differentiate between slaughter and** the **other** sacrificial **rites,** and all four rites must be performed for the sake of the offering, **so too,** concerning the term **slaughter stated with regard to deviation** in the context of the **owner, you should not differentiate between slaughter and** the **other** sacrificial **rites.** The Gemara counters: This derivation **can be refuted: What** is unique **about deviation** from the type of **offering,** as opposed to deviation with regard to the owner? It is unique with regard to four matters: First, it is unique **in that its disqualification concerns** the essence of the offering **itself. And** second, it is unique in that deviation from the type of **offering applies to** the **four** sacrificial **rites,** whereas performing a rite for the sake of the owner is relevant only to the sprinkling of the blood, which atones for the owner. **And** third, it is unique in that deviation from the type of offering **applies** even **after** the **death** of the owner, when the owner's heir brings the offering, whereas intent for the sake of the owner is irrelevant after the owner has died. **And** fourth, it is unique in that deviation from the type of offering **applies with regard to communal** offerings **as well as with regard to individual** offerings, whereas employing the term owner is irrelevant with regard to communal offerings, which are owned by the public.

The Gemara then identifies four ways in which *shinui kodesh* (change of offering type) differs from *shinui ba'alim* (change of owner), each of which independently blocks the inference from one to the other: first, *sheken psulah begufah* — the *shinui kodesh* disqualifies the offering itself; second, *v'yeshno b'arba avodot* — the *shinui kodesh* applies to all four services; third, *v'yeshno l'achar mitah* — the *shinui kodesh* requirement persists even after the death of the owner; and fourth, *v'yeshno b'tzibbur k'veyachid* — the *shinui kodesh* applies equally to communal and individual offerings.

### Communal vs Individual

The fourth distinction bears elaboration. For communal offerings, the concept of an individual owner does not apply in the same way — the *tzibbur* as a whole is the owner. The *shinui ba'alim* concern is therefore relevant primarily to individual offerings and does not extend naturally to communal ones. The *shinui kodesh* requirement, however, governs all offerings regardless of whether they are individual or communal. This asymmetry is itself a reason we cannot derive *ba'alim* rules from *kodesh* rules.

### Animal vs Thought

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּתַרְתֵּי לָאו דַּוְקָא, תַּרְתֵּי מִיהָא דַּוְקָא. מָה שְׁנָא שִׁינּוּי בְעָלִים דְּלָא הָוֵי פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ – מַחְשָׁבָה בְּעָלְמָא; שִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ נָמוּ מַחְשָׁבָה בְּעָלְמָא הוּא! אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן דְּאַחְשְׁבַהּ – פַּסְלַהּ; הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּאַחְשְׁבַהּ – פַּסְלַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And although two** of these distinctions **are not precise,** the other **two are in any event precise** distinctions, based upon which a distinction can be drawn. The Gemara explains why two of the distinctions are not precise: **What is different** about **deviation** with regard to the **owner,** such **that its disqualification does not concern** the offering **itself?** It is that this **merely** constitutes a change of **thought. Deviation** from the type of **offering is also merely** a change of **thought;** the offering is not physically affected. **Rather,** this distinction between deviation with regard to the owner and deviation from the type of offering is false; one should claim that if deviation from the type of offering **disqualifies it once** the one who slaughtered it **had** that **intent, here too, once** the one who slaughtered it **had intent** to deviate from its owner, **he disqualified it.**

The first distinction is elaborated further. When one asks whether *shinui ba'alim* disqualifies the animal itself — *psulah begufah* — or whether it operates only as a matter of thought, the Gemara notes: *machshava be'alma* — it is a matter of thought alone. The *shinui kodesh* also begins as a thought; however, once one entertains the wrong intent, the disqualification attaches to the animal itself. *Kevan d'achshava psala* — once the wrong thought arises, the offering is disqualified through that thought. The same logic applies to *shinui ba'alim*: once the intent is directed toward the wrong person, the disqualification is effected by thought. In this respect the two are more similar than they initially appear, which is why the Gemara acknowledges that this distinction may not be fully precise.

### Zrikah Primacy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב פִּנְחָס בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי, דְּאָמַר יֵשׁ שִׁינּוּי בְעָלִים לְאַחַר מִיתָה – תַּרְתֵּי מִיהָא אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty with the second distinction, the case where the owner died: **And according to Rav Pineḥas, son of Rav Mari, who says** that disqualification by **deviation** with regard to the **owner applies** even **after** the owner's **death,** there is no distinction in this regard between deviation from the type of offering and deviation with regard to the owner. **In any event, there are two** remaining distinctions based on which the comparison between a deviation from the type of offering and a deviation with regard to the owner **can be refuted.**

The second distinction — that *shinui ba'alim* applies to *zrikah* specifically — reflects the centrality of *zrikah* in the sacrificial process as the act through which atonement is principally achieved. The *zrikah* is the culminating moment of the blood service, and the owner's intent at that stage is paramount. The rules of *shinui ba'alim* are therefore anchored in *zrikah* in a way that does not parallel the broader spread of *shinui kodesh* across all four services.

### After Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְנִרְצָה לוֹ לְכַפֵּר עָלָיו״ – וְלֹא עַל חֲבֵירוֹ. וְהַאי לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?! הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״וְנִרְצָה לוֹ לְכַפֵּר עָלָיו״ – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֶת שֶׁעָלָיו – חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ, וְאֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ עָלָיו – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ. וְאָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״עָלַי״ – כְּמַאן דְּטָעוּן לֵיהּ אַכַּתְפֵּיהּ דָּמֵי. רַב אָשֵׁי מִ״וְּנִרְצָה לוֹ לְכַפֵּר״ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi says** that the *halakha* that it is prohibited to sprinkle the blood for the sake of one other than the owner is derived from a verse. **The verse states: "And it shall be accepted for him to atone for him"** (Leviticus 1:4), teaching that the sacrificial rite that atones for the owner, namely, the sprinkling of the blood, must be performed specifically for him, **and not for another.** The Gemara asks: **But does this** verse **come to** teach **this** *halakha*? Isn't **it necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the verse: **"And it shall be accepted for him to atone for him [*alav*]," Rabbi Shimon says:** This indicates that in a case where one consecrated an animal as an offering and it died or was lost, if it was initially incumbent **upon him [*alav*]** to bring an offering, i.e., if he had accepted upon himself a personal obligation to bring an offering, **he bears responsibility for it,** i.e., he is obligated to bring another offering in its stead. **But** if it was **not** incumbent **upon him [*alav*]** to bring an offering, i.e., if he consecrated a specific animal without accepting upon himself any personal obligation, **he does not bear responsibility for it** if it dies or is lost. **And Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi says: What is the reasoning** behind this distinction? **Once** a person **said:** It is incumbent **upon me** to bring an offering, it is considered **as though** the offering is **loaded upon his shoulders.** He does not discharge his obligation until he brings some offering. The Gemara answers: **Rav Ashi says** that the *halakha* that it is prohibited to sprinkle the blood for the sake of one other than the owner is derived **from** the phrase **"And it shall be accepted for him,"** not from the term "for him," on which the *baraita* is based.

The third distinction concerns what happens after the owner's death. The Gemara notes that *shinui kodesh* — the disqualification arising from performing the offering for the wrong type — continues to be relevant even posthumously, whereas *shinui ba'alim* is tied to the living owner. The Gemara cites Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi in connection with the verse *venirtza lo lekhaper alav*: the word *lo* — for him — is apparently superfluous, since the verse could have simply said the offering is accepted to atone. This extra word teaches *velo al chavero* — for him and not for another. The verse requires that the offering be performed for the benefit of its designated owner and not redirected to someone else.

A *beraita* further elaborates: Rabbi Shimon teaches that whoever bears responsibility for the offering — *et she'alav hayav be'achrayuto* — is obligated in its accountability, but whoever does not bear that responsibility is not. Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi explains the logic: once a person says *alai* — this is upon me — it is considered as if the offering is loaded upon his shoulders. He carries it with him as an obligation until he discharges it through the actual sacrifice. Rav Ashi connects this specifically to the *zrikah*, deriving from *mi-venirtza lo lekhaper* that the *zrikah* must be performed for the sake of the specific owner — the extra *lo* excludes performance on behalf of another.

## Nazir Ram Proof

### Kabbalah Owner Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן זְבִיחָה וּזְרִיקָה, קַבָּלָה מְנָלַן? וְכִי תֵּימָא לֵילַף מִזְּבִיחָה וּזְרִיקָה – מָה לִזְבִיחָה וּזְרִיקָה שֶׁכֵּן עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ בַּחוּץ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַתְיָא מֵאֵיל נָזִיר; דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת הָאַיִל יַעֲשֶׂה זֶבַח שְׁלָמִים״ – שֶׁתְּהֵא עֲשִׂיָּיתוֹ לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים; וְאִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְשִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ – דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵהָתָם, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְשִׁינּוּי בְעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that **slaughter and** the **sprinkling** of the blood must be performed for the sake of the owner. **From where do we** derive that the **collection** of the blood must also be performed for the sake of the owner? **And if you would say: Let us derive from slaughter and sprinkling** that collection must also be performed for the sake of the owner, this derivation can be refuted: **What** is unique **about slaughter and sprinkling?** They are unique **in that** each is **a rite for which one is liable** to be punished with death at the hand of Heaven if he performs it **outside** the Temple. **Rather, Rav Ashi says** that the *halakha* that the collection of the blood must also be performed for the sake of the owner **is derived from** the case of **a nazirite's ram. As it is written: "And he shall offer the ram for a sacrifice [*zevaḥ*] of peace offerings** unto the Lord" (Numbers 6:17), indicating **that its offering should be** performed **for the sake of a peace offering. And if** the verse **is not** necessary for the **matter of deviation** from the type of **offering, as we derived that from there,** i.e., the verses cited earlier (4a), **apply it** to the **matter of deviation** with regard to the **owner.** It is thereby derived that its sacrificial rites must be performed for the sake of its owner.

The Gemara now returns to the structural question: *ashkachan zvicha u'zerika, kabbalah mina lan?* — we have found prooftexts for *shechitah* and *zrikah* with respect to *shinui ba'alim*, but where do we derive the same requirement for *kabbalah*?

The same reflexive challenge is posed: can we simply derive *kabbalah* from *shechitah* and *zrikah*? The answer is no — *mah le-zvicha u'zerika, sheken avodah she-chayavim alei b'choutz* — *shechitah* and *zrikah* are unique in that performing them outside the Temple carries the punishment of *karet*. The *kabbalah* does not carry this penalty for performance *chutz limkomo*, and therefore the inference cannot be made.

### Nazir Offering

Rav Ashi therefore seeks a different derivation. He points to the *eil Nazir* — the ram of the *Nazir* — whose offering is described in *Parshat Nasso*: *ve-et ha-ayil ya'aseh zevach shelamim* — and the ram he shall make into a sacrifice of peace offerings. From this verse Rav Ashi derives *she-tehei asiyato leshem shelamim* — that the performance of this sacrifice must be for the sake of the *shelamim*. Since the *shinui kodesh* requirement has already been established from other sources, this verse is superfluous on that count — and by the principle of *im eino inyan*, it is redirected to teach *shinui ba'alim*.

### Klal U'Prat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר אַבָּא לְרָבָא, אֵימָא: ״יַעֲשֶׂה״ – כָּלַל, ״זֶבַח״ – פָּרַט; כְּלָל וּפְרָט – אֵין בַּכְּלָל אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁבַּפְּרָט; זְבִיחָה אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Abba said to Rava,** in objection to this derivation: **Say** that the term: **"Shall offer," is a generalization** and: **"A sacrifice [*zevaḥ*]," is a detail.** According to the principles of biblical hermeneutics, where there is **a generalization and a detail, the generalization includes only that which is** specified **in the detail.** Accordingly, **slaughter [*zeviḥa*], yes,** must be performed for the sake of its owner; but **no other** rite must be performed for the sake of its owner in order for the offering to be fit.

Rav Acha bar Abba then challenges Rava with a pointed objection rooted in the hermeneutical principle of *klal u'prat* — generalization and detail. The verse reads *ya'aseh zevach shelamim*. The word *ya'aseh* — he shall make/perform — is a general term; *zevach* is the specific detail. By the rule of *klal u'prat*, when a generalization is followed by a particularization, the law encompasses only what is specified in the detail. Here, *zevach* specifies *shechitah*. If so, the derivation would teach only that *shechitah* of the *Nazir's* ram must be *lishma* with respect to the owner — *zvicha in, midei achrina lo* — slaughter yes, nothing else no. The other services would remain unaddressed.

### Incomplete Generalization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי כְּתִיב ״יַעֲשֶׂה שְׁלָמִים זֶבַח״ – כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ; הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב ״יַעֲשֶׂה זֶבַח שְׁלָמִים״ – הָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּלָל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מָלֵא, וְכׇל כְּלָל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מָלֵא – אֵין דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If** it had been **written: Shall offer** the ram **for peace offerings as a sacrifice [*zevaḥ*],** it would be **as you say.** The clause: Shall offer the ram for peace offerings, would be considered a generalization, and: Sacrifice [*zevaḥ*], would be a detail. **Now that it is written: "Shall offer** the ram **for a sacrifice [*zevaḥ*] of peace offerings,"** the term "shall offer" is **an incomplete generalization,** as the term "for a sacrifice" interrupts between "shall offer" and "peace offerings." **And one cannot derive** a *halakha* from **any incomplete generalization by** employing the principle of **a generalization and a detail.**

One might respond that the order could be rearranged: had the verse written *ya'aseh shelamim zevach*, then *shelamim* would serve as the *klal* and *zevach* as the *prat*, and the derivation would function differently. But the verse as it stands reads *ya'aseh zevach shelamim* — and in this order, *zevach* interrupts before *shelamim* has been fully articulated. This renders the putative *klal* (*ya'aseh*) incomplete — *klal she'eino malei*. And the principle is: *kol klal she'eino malei, ein danim oto biklal u'prat* — a generalization that is not fully formed cannot serve as the basis for the *klal u'prat* interpretive method. Whether this ultimately resolves or merely displaces the difficulty — and how the Gemara eventually establishes the *ba'alim* requirement for *kabbalah* on firm ground — is where the *daf* breaks off, to be taken up in the continuation.
