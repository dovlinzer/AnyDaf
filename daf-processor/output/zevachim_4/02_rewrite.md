# Zevachim 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Blood Services

### Mishnah Overview

The opening *mishnah* of *Masechet Zevachim* establishes the foundational principle governing the entire tractate: *kol hazvachim shenizbechu shelo lishmam kesherim ela shelo alu labaalim leshem chovah* — all sacrifices that were slaughtered not for their own sake are valid, yet they do not fulfill the obligation of the owner who brought them. This is the backdrop against which the Gemara on daf 4 operates. The task before us is to trace the textual proofs establishing that each of the four blood services must be performed *lishma* — with proper intent.

### Dimensions of Intent

The requirement of *lishma* operates along two distinct axes. The first concerns the *kodesh* of the offering — that is, the type of sacrifice. If a *kohen* sets out to bring a *korban shelamim*, the intent throughout must remain fixed on it being a *shelamim* and not drift toward some other category, such as an *olah* or a *chatat*. The second axis concerns the *ba'alei hakorban*, the owners of the sacrifice. The person for whom the offering is brought must be the one in whose name it is performed; one may not redirect the offering toward a different individual. Both dimensions of intent — *shinui kodesh* (change in the type of offering) and *shinui ba'alim* (change in the owner) — must be examined separately for each of the four services.

### Four Services

The four *avodot hadam* — the blood services — are *shechitah* (slaughter), *kabbalah* (collection of the blood), *zrikah* (sprinkling of the blood), and *holachah* (carrying the blood to the altar). Each service must be shown, through its own textual derivation, to require *lishma*. The services share certain features with one another, but each also possesses unique characteristics that prevent a simple inference from one to the others. The analysis of the *daf* proceeds through this comparison methodically, examining each service in turn for both the *kodesh* and *ba'alim* dimensions.

---

## Shechitah & Kodesh

### Vayikra 3:1 Proof

The Gemara opens with the question: *menalan d'ba'inan zvicha lishma?* — from where do we derive that the *shechitah* must be performed *lishma*, for the sake of the offering? The answer is drawn from Vayikra 3:1: *ve'im zevach shelamim korbano* — and if his offering is a sacrifice of peace offerings. The verse contains what appears to be a redundancy: it might have said simply *ve'im shelamim korbano*, or alternatively *ve'im zevach korbano*. The presence of both terms — *zevach* and *shelamim* together — is the source of the *derashah*.

### Redundancy Analysis

From the double expression *zevach shelamim*, the Gemara derives *shetehei zvicha leshem hashelamim* — that the slaughtering must be performed for the sake of the *shelamim*. The word *zevach* is superfluous unless it comes to teach that the act of slaughter itself must be oriented toward the identity of the offering.

### Zevach as Name

One might object: perhaps *zevach shelamim* is simply the proper name of this category of sacrifice, just as we speak of a *korban olah* or a *chatat*. If so, there is no redundancy to analyze — the Torah is merely using the offering's full title.

### Not the Name

This objection is refuted by examining other verses. In Vayikra 7:33, the Torah writes *hamakriv et dam hashelamim* — the one who brings the blood of the *shelamim* — and in Vayikra 7:14, *hazorek et dam hashelamim* — the one who sprinkles the blood of the *shelamim*. In neither case does the Torah write *dam zevach hashelamim*. If *zevach shelamim* were truly the formal name of the offering, it would appear consistently whenever the offering is referenced. The absence of the word *zevach* in these parallel contexts demonstrates that it is not a fixed name.

### Shechitah Conclusion

Since *zevach shelamim* is not merely a title but a meaningful locution, the presence of *zevach* in the verse teaches something substantive: *shema mina shetehei zvicha leshem shelamim* — we derive from here that the *shechitah* must be performed for the sake of the *shelamim*. The first of the four blood services has been anchored in a textual proof.

## Kabbalah & Kodesh

### Learn from Shechitah?

Having established that *shechitah* requires *lishma*, the Gemara asks: *ve'chi teima leilaf mizvicha* — perhaps we can simply derive all the other services from *shechitah*? If slaughter must be *lishma*, surely collection, sprinkling, and carrying must be as well?

The Gemara introduces the principle that each service possesses its own unique stringencies that prevent a straightforward inference from one to another. The services are, in a sense, like a Venn diagram: they share certain features but not all, and these differences block the extension of any single derivation across the board.

### Pesach Limitation

The *shechitah* carries a special stringency that does not apply to the other services: *sheken nifsela shelo leshem ochlav bepesach* — it is disqualified in the case of the *Pesach* offering if it is not slaughtered with intent toward those who will eat it. If the person bringing the *korban Pesach* is somehow unable to eat the requisite amount — whether due to illness or any other incapacity — the offering is disqualified. This unique *chumra* of *shechitah* with regard to *Pesach* means that *shechitah* is not a neutral template from which we can simply derive the other services.

### Vayikra 7:33 Proof

A separate derivation is therefore required for *kabbalah*. The Gemara cites Vayikra 7:33: *hamakriv et dam hashelamim* — the one who brings near the blood of the *shelamim*. The verb *hamakriv*, from the root *karov* (to be near, to bring close), is understood as indicating the act of collecting the blood — *kabbalah*. From the fact that this verse specifically identifies the blood as being of the *shelamim*, the Gemara derives *shetehei kabbalah leshem shelamim* — that the collection of the blood must also be performed for the sake of the *shelamim*. In other contexts the Torah simply says *dam*, blood, without reattaching the identity of the offering; here the repetition of *shelamim* teaches that *kabbalah* must be intentional regarding the type of offering.

### Distinction Analysis

One might then ask: since we now have a verse for *kabbalah*, why not derive *shechitah* from *kabbalah* rather than the other way around? The Gemara addresses this by noting that the traffic of inference cannot simply be reversed. Each derivation is anchored in a specific textual excess, and the question of which service teaches which cannot be settled by reversing the direction of the argument. The Gemara's framing — *velichtov rachmana bakkabbalah veneilef shechita minnah* — presents this as a challenge: let the Torah have written the rule only for *kabbalah*, and we would learn *shechitah* from it. The response is *mishum deika lemeifrach* — because there is a refutation. The *kabbalah* has its own unique stringency: *sheken pesulah bezar ve'ishah* — it is disqualified if performed by a non-*kohen* or a woman. This stringency does not apply to *shechitah*, and therefore we cannot derive *shechitah* from *kabbalah* any more than we can derive *kabbalah* from *shechitah*. Each requires its own independent proof.

### Kohen Requirement

This last point warrants emphasis. While *shechitah* may be performed by virtually anyone — a *zar* (non-*kohen*), a woman, even in principle the owner of the animal — *kabbalah* is categorically restricted to a male *kohen*. This is a dimension of stringency entirely absent from *shechitah*, and it is precisely this difference that blocks the inference between them.

## Zrikah & Kodesh

### Learn from Others?

With *shechitah* and *kabbalah* both established, the Gemara proceeds to *zrikah*: *zrikah menalan?* — from where do we derive that the sprinkling of the blood must be *lishma*? And as before, the Gemara first considers whether we might simply derive it from the two already-established services.

### Location Distinction

The proposed derivation is rejected on familiar grounds. What is unique about *shechitah* and *kabbalah* that does not apply to *zrikah*? The Gemara notes: *mah hanni sheken te'unot tzafon* — these services are unique in that they require a specific location. For *kodshei kodashim*, *shechitah* and *kabbalah* must be performed in the northern section of the Temple courtyard. The *zrikah*, by contrast, may be performed from any position in the courtyard. This locational stringency that characterizes *shechitah* and *kabbalah* is absent from *zrikah*, and therefore we cannot extend the rule automatically.

### Vayikra 7:14 Proof

A fresh prooftext is therefore required. The Gemara finds it in Vayikra 7:14: *hazorek et dam hashelamim* — the one who sprinkles the blood of the *shelamim*. Just as with the *kabbalah* verse, the explicit attachment of *shelamim* to the act of *zrikah* teaches *shetehei zrikah leshem shelamim* — that the sprinkling must be performed with intent toward the *shelamim*.

### Death Penalty

Could we reverse the inference and derive *shechitah* and *kabbalah* from *zrikah* instead? No — because *zrikah* possesses its own unique stringency: *mah lezrikah sheken chayav aleha zar mitah* — a *zar* who performs *zrikah* is liable to death (or *karet*). This is a severity that does not attach to *shechitah* and is likewise absent from *kabbalah* in the same degree. The unique stringency of each service thus both prevents upward inference from it and justifies the need for its own independent derivation.

## Holachah & Kodesh

### Learn from Others?

Three services established, the Gemara turns to *holachah*, the carrying of the blood from the site of slaughter to the altar: *ashkacha kulhu holachah menalan?* — we have found sources for all the others, where do we derive *holachah*?

### Dispensability

The Gemara immediately anticipates the attempt to derive *holachah* from the other three, and preempts it: *mah lekulhu sheken avodah she-i efshar levatlah tomar beholachah she-efshar levatlah?* — what is unique about all the other services? Each of them is indispensable — the offering cannot be completed without them. *Holachah*, by contrast, is dispensable. If the *shechitah* takes place adjacent to the altar, there is nowhere to carry the blood — it is already where it needs to be. One does not perform *holachah* in such a case. An obligation that can be bypassed entirely under certain circumstances cannot automatically be assimilated to obligations that are always mandatory.

### Vayikra 1 Proof

The prooftext for *holachah* is derived from the first chapter of Vayikra, where the verb *vehikriv* — and he shall bring near — appears in the context of the *olah*. The Sages interpret *vehikriv hamizbecha* as referring to the carrying of the limbs up the *kevesh*, the ramp leading to the altar. The language of *lehakriv* — bringing close — generates the *derashah* for *holachah lishma*.

### Holachah & Kabbalah

A deeper connection emerges from the parallel usage. The same expression, *lehakriv*, is used for both *holachah* and *kabbalah* in adjacent verses: *vehikriv* refers to carrying, and *ve'hikrivu* is understood as *kabbalat hadam*. The Gemara derives from this parallel that *holachah* is linked to *kabbalah*: *ve'afik rachmana bilshon holacha lemeimra deholacha lo tafka miklal kabala* — the Torah expressed the collection of the blood using language associated with *holachah* to teach that *holachah* should not be excluded from the category of *kabbalah* in terms of the *lishma* requirement. The two are connected, and what is demanded of one is demanded of the other in this respect.

## Shechitah & Owner

### Owner Intent Source

Having established that all four blood services must be performed *lishma* with respect to the type of offering (*shinui kodesh*), the Gemara turns to the second dimension: the owner. *Ashkechan shinui kodesh, shinui be'alim minalan?* — we have found the sources for change-of-offering-type; from where do we derive the rules regarding change of owner?

### Todah Meat Law

Rav Pinchas brei deRav Ami cites Vayikra chapter 7: *uvasar zevach todat shelamav* — and the meat of the sacrifice of his thanksgiving peace offering. The *todah*, the thanksgiving offering, has a uniquely stringent eating restriction: it must be eaten on the day of the offering and the following night. The Gemara reads this verse as teaching two things. First, *shetehei zvicha leshem todah* — that the *shechitah* must be performed for the sake of the *todah*. Second, it raises the question: does the word *zevach* here come to teach the *shinui kodesh* requirement, or the *shinui ba'alim* requirement? Since the *shinui kodesh* requirement for *shechitah* has already been derived elsewhere, the Gemara applies the *im eino inyan* principle — if a teaching is unnecessary for one matter, apply it to another. Here, *zevach* is redirected to teach the *shinui ba'alim* requirement: the *shechitah* must be performed for the sake of the owner.

The purpose of this mechanism is worth pausing over. The *todah* offering is brought by someone in gratitude — for surviving illness, a sea voyage, imprisonment, or crossing a desert. Its meat must be eaten quickly, forcing the owner to invite others to share it, thereby publicizing his gratitude and, in turn, the *shem Hashem*. When the Torah says *zevach todat shelamav*, it is associating the act of slaughter with the identity and purpose of the owner's offering.

### Todah vs Shelamim

This leads to a teaching of Abba Chanin in the name of Rabbi Eliezer, cited from a *beraita*: *todah she-shechatah le-shem shelamim, kesherah. Shelamim she-shechatan le-shem todah, pesulah.* A *todah* slaughtered with the intent of a *shelamim* is valid; a *shelamim* slaughtered with the intent of a *todah* is disqualified. The asymmetry is explained: *todah keruyah shelamim ve-ein shelamim keruyim todah* — a *todah* may be called a *shelamim*, but a *shelamim* may not be called a *todah*. The *todah* is a subspecies of *shelamim*, so directing it toward the broader category is permissible. The reverse, however, takes the *shelamim* outside its own category and into a more specific one it has not been designated for — and that disqualifies it. The relationship is one-directional, like a set and its subset.

### Zevach Teaches Both

A further nuance: the word *zevach* in this verse is also needed to extend the time restriction of the *todah* to other offerings, such as the *chatat* and *asham*. One might therefore argue that the word is already occupied and cannot be reassigned to teach *shinui ba'alim*. The Gemara responds that if the verse had wanted only to teach the eating restriction, it could have written *uvasar todat shelamav zevach* — placing *zevach* differently. Since it is written as *uvasar zevach todat shelamav*, the term *zevach* here carries multiple teachings. *Mah zevach? Shema mina tarti* — what does *zevach* teach? We derive two conclusions from it: both the *todah*'s extended rules and the requirement of *lishma* with regard to the owner.

## Owner Intent Others

### Learn All from Shechitah?

The Gemara now replicates the same analytical structure used for *shinui kodesh*, this time for *shinui ba'alim*. We have established that *shechitah* must be performed for the sake of the owner. Can we derive the same requirement for all four services from *shechitah* alone?

### Pesach Limitation

The answer is no, for the same reason encountered before: *mah lizevichah sheken posel shelo lishmo ochlim uvapesach* — the *shechitah* is uniquely stringent in the case of *Pesach*, where slaughtering with intent toward those who will eat it is critical. If the person who is to eat the *Pesach* is unable to eat the requisite amount at the time of *shechitah* — for instance, if the owner becomes *tamei* between the time of slaughter and the time of *zrikah* — the offering may be disqualified. This *chumra* is particular to *shechitah* and does not map onto the other services. Therefore *shechitah* cannot serve as a universal template for *shinui ba'alim* either.

### Four Differences

The Gemara then identifies four ways in which *shinui kodesh* (change of offering type) differs from *shinui ba'alim* (change of owner), each of which independently blocks the inference from one to the other: first, *sheken psulah begufah* — the *shinui kodesh* disqualifies the offering itself; second, *v'yeshno b'arba avodot* — the *shinui kodesh* applies to all four services; third, *v'yeshno l'achar mitah* — the *shinui kodesh* requirement persists even after the death of the owner; and fourth, *v'yeshno b'tzibbur k'veyachid* — the *shinui kodesh* applies equally to communal and individual offerings.

### Communal vs Individual

The fourth distinction bears elaboration. For communal offerings, the concept of an individual owner does not apply in the same way — the *tzibbur* as a whole is the owner. The *shinui ba'alim* concern is therefore relevant primarily to individual offerings and does not extend naturally to communal ones. The *shinui kodesh* requirement, however, governs all offerings regardless of whether they are individual or communal. This asymmetry is itself a reason we cannot derive *ba'alim* rules from *kodesh* rules.

### Animal vs Thought

The first distinction is elaborated further. When one asks whether *shinui ba'alim* disqualifies the animal itself — *psulah begufah* — or whether it operates only as a matter of thought, the Gemara notes: *machshava be'alma* — it is a matter of thought alone. The *shinui kodesh* also begins as a thought; however, once one entertains the wrong intent, the disqualification attaches to the animal itself. *Kevan d'achshava psala* — once the wrong thought arises, the offering is disqualified through that thought. The same logic applies to *shinui ba'alim*: once the intent is directed toward the wrong person, the disqualification is effected by thought. In this respect the two are more similar than they initially appear, which is why the Gemara acknowledges that this distinction may not be fully precise.

### Zrikah Primacy

The second distinction — that *shinui ba'alim* applies to *zrikah* specifically — reflects the centrality of *zrikah* in the sacrificial process as the act through which atonement is principally achieved. The *zrikah* is the culminating moment of the blood service, and the owner's intent at that stage is paramount. The rules of *shinui ba'alim* are therefore anchored in *zrikah* in a way that does not parallel the broader spread of *shinui kodesh* across all four services.

### After Death

The third distinction concerns what happens after the owner's death. The Gemara notes that *shinui kodesh* — the disqualification arising from performing the offering for the wrong type — continues to be relevant even posthumously, whereas *shinui ba'alim* is tied to the living owner. The Gemara cites Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi in connection with the verse *venirtza lo lekhaper alav*: the word *lo* — for him — is apparently superfluous, since the verse could have simply said the offering is accepted to atone. This extra word teaches *velo al chavero* — for him and not for another. The verse requires that the offering be performed for the benefit of its designated owner and not redirected to someone else.

A *beraita* further elaborates: Rabbi Shimon teaches that whoever bears responsibility for the offering — *et she'alav hayav be'achrayuto* — is obligated in its accountability, but whoever does not bear that responsibility is not. Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi explains the logic: once a person says *alai* — this is upon me — it is considered as if the offering is loaded upon his shoulders. He carries it with him as an obligation until he discharges it through the actual sacrifice. Rav Ashi connects this specifically to the *zrikah*, deriving from *mi-venirtza lo lekhaper* that the *zrikah* must be performed for the sake of the specific owner — the extra *lo* excludes performance on behalf of another.

## Nazir Ram Proof

### Kabbalah Owner Source

The Gemara now returns to the structural question: *ashkachan zvicha u'zerika, kabbalah mina lan?* — we have found prooftexts for *shechitah* and *zrikah* with respect to *shinui ba'alim*, but where do we derive the same requirement for *kabbalah*?

The same reflexive challenge is posed: can we simply derive *kabbalah* from *shechitah* and *zrikah*? The answer is no — *mah le-zvicha u'zerika, sheken avodah she-chayavim alei b'choutz* — *shechitah* and *zrikah* are unique in that performing them outside the Temple carries the punishment of *karet*. The *kabbalah* does not carry this penalty for performance *chutz limkomo*, and therefore the inference cannot be made.

### Nazir Offering

Rav Ashi therefore seeks a different derivation. He points to the *eil Nazir* — the ram of the *Nazir* — whose offering is described in *Parshat Nasso*: *ve-et ha-ayil ya'aseh zevach shelamim* — and the ram he shall make into a sacrifice of peace offerings. From this verse Rav Ashi derives *she-tehei asiyato leshem shelamim* — that the performance of this sacrifice must be for the sake of the *shelamim*. Since the *shinui kodesh* requirement has already been established from other sources, this verse is superfluous on that count — and by the principle of *im eino inyan*, it is redirected to teach *shinui ba'alim*.

### Klal U'Prat

Rav Acha bar Abba then challenges Rava with a pointed objection rooted in the hermeneutical principle of *klal u'prat* — generalization and detail. The verse reads *ya'aseh zevach shelamim*. The word *ya'aseh* — he shall make/perform — is a general term; *zevach* is the specific detail. By the rule of *klal u'prat*, when a generalization is followed by a particularization, the law encompasses only what is specified in the detail. Here, *zevach* specifies *shechitah*. If so, the derivation would teach only that *shechitah* of the *Nazir's* ram must be *lishma* with respect to the owner — *zvicha in, midei achrina lo* — slaughter yes, nothing else no. The other services would remain unaddressed.

### Incomplete Generalization

One might respond that the order could be rearranged: had the verse written *ya'aseh shelamim zevach*, then *shelamim* would serve as the *klal* and *zevach* as the *prat*, and the derivation would function differently. But the verse as it stands reads *ya'aseh zevach shelamim* — and in this order, *zevach* interrupts before *shelamim* has been fully articulated. This renders the putative *klal* (*ya'aseh*) incomplete — *klal she'eino malei*. And the principle is: *kol klal she'eino malei, ein danim oto biklal u'prat* — a generalization that is not fully formed cannot serve as the basis for the *klal u'prat* interpretive method. Whether this ultimately resolves or merely displaces the difficulty — and how the Gemara eventually establishes the *ba'alim* requirement for *kabbalah* on firm ground — is where the *daf* breaks off, to be taken up in the continuation.
