# Ketubot 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ones & Early Marriage

### Sakana vs. Ones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִסַּכָּנָה וְאֵילָךְ נָהֲגוּ הָעָם לִכְנוֹס בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי, וְלֹא מִיחוּ בְּיָדָם חֲכָמִים. וּבַשֵּׁנִי לֹא יִכְנוֹס. וְאִם מֵחֲמַת הָאוֹנֶס — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And from** the time of **danger and onward, the people adopted the custom to marry on Tuesday** as well, **and the Sages did not reprimand them. And on Monday one may not marry** even in time of danger. **However, if it is due to the coercion,** it is **permitted.**

The daf picks up near the bottom of 3b, where a *braita* — essentially a *Tosefta* — discusses the established practice of getting married on Wednesdays. The *braita* records that during a period of *sakana*, a genuine risk to life, the community shifted weddings to Tuesday. The Gemara explains this as a response to a local ruler who was forcing brides to sleep with him on their wedding night, so that making the wedding day less predictable offered some protection. The *braita* then states that in a time of *ones* — a circumstance beyond one's control, but short of a life-threatening *sakana* — one may marry on another day, using Monday as the example.

The Gemara immediately distinguishes between these two terms. If the prior case was already called a *sakana*, why is this one now called an *ones*? The change in terminology signals that we are dealing with an altogether different kind of circumstance. Rashi adds a further nuance: the earlier formulation described a communal *minhag* that developed organically, whereas here the language *mutar* — it is permitted — carries the flavor of a formal, proactive rabbinic allowance, perhaps even a *l'chatchila* ruling for an individual case.

### Military Threat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׂר צָבָא בָּא לָעִיר. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאָתֵי וְחָלֵיף — לִיעַכַּב! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאָתֵי וְקָבַע. בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי מִיהָא לִכְנוֹס? אִסְפַּרְווֹא דִידֵיהּ בִּשְׁלִישִׁי קָאָתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A general** and his army **are coming to the city** on Wednesday, and the concern is that the troops will appropriate the supplies for the feast. **What are the circumstances? If** it is a situation **where** the general **comes and passes** through, **let** them **postpone** the wedding until the following week. **Rather, it is necessary** to teach the *halakha* with regard to the general only in a case **where he comes and establishes** himself there. **In any case, let one marry on Tuesday.** Why does the *baraita* permit marrying on Monday? **His entourage [*asperava*] arrives on Tuesday.**

Rava identifies the *ones* in question: word spreads that a military commander — a *sar tzava* — is coming to the city with his troops, who will requisition all available food and supplies. Since the wedding feast has already been prepared and will be seized if left for Wednesday, the couple has grounds to move the wedding earlier. The financial disruption of having all the food confiscated constitutes a sufficient *ones* to justify departing from the standard Wednesday date.

### Duration of Threat

The Gemara raises a challenge: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If the commander is simply passing through and will leave after a short time, Rashi reads the Gemara's response as: just wait, and hold the wedding the following Wednesday or the one after. There is no need to act early if the disruption is temporary. Tosafos reads the case differently, suggesting that if the commander is leaving on that same Wednesday, one should simply schedule the feast for later in the day — but this reading is difficult, since the problem is that once the food is gathered it will be seized regardless of timing. The Gemara accordingly sets the case as *de-ati ve-kava*: the commander is arriving and settling in indefinitely. Under those circumstances, there is no later Wednesday to wait for, and moving the wedding earlier is warranted.

### Advanced Retinue

This raises a follow-up question: if the commander is arriving on Wednesday, why not simply hold the wedding on Tuesday? The answer is that in this case, there would be no *chiddush* — no novel ruling — since one is merely moving back one day to avoid the exact day of the disruption. The Gemara therefore frames the case more pointedly: the commander's *asafrava* — his advance retinue — is arriving on Tuesday, ahead of his own arrival on Wednesday. This means the food could be seized even earlier, justifying moving the wedding all the way back to Monday. That is the genuine novelty: the disruption threatens not merely Wednesday but the days leading up to it, and we permit the wedding as early as necessary to avoid the loss.

## Death & Wedding

### Setup: Food & Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה פִּתּוֹ אָפוּי, וְטִבְחוֹ טָבוּחַ, וְיֵינוֹ מָזוּג, וּמֵת אָבִיו שֶׁל חָתָן אוֹ אִמָּהּ שֶׁל כַּלָּה — מַכְנִיסִין אֶת הַמֵּת לַחֶדֶר, וְאֶת הֶחָתָן וְאֶת הַכַּלָּה לַחוּפָּה,
>
> **Translation:** **If one's bread was baked, and his animal slaughtered, and his wine diluted,** and all preparations for the wedding feast were complete, **and the father of the groom or the mother of the bride died** before the wedding, then before burying the deceased, which would trigger the onset of mourning, **one moves the corpse into a room, and the bride and groom** are ushered **to the wedding canopy** and they are married.

Having outlined the *ones* of military requisition, the Gemara now turns to what may have been the original referent of the word *ones* in the first *braita*, or at minimum a distinct and important case that several *braisos* address in their own right. The scenario: *harei she-haya pito afui* — the bread has been baked, fresh; *u-tivcho tavuach* — the meat has been slaughtered, freshly; *ve-yaino mazug* — the wine has been mixed; everything is prepared for the wedding feast and will spoil if not consumed imminently. And at that moment, *u-mais aviv shel choson o imah shel kallah* — the father of the *chosson* or the mother of the *kallah* dies.

This is not merely a theoretical case. It combines two pressing concerns: the catastrophic financial loss of irreplaceable prepared food, and the death of the very parent who was instrumental in preparing for the wedding. If they wait a month until after *shiv'ah* and *shloshim*, not only will the food be lost, but the father will not be there to oversee the *chosson*'s preparations, nor will the mother be there to prepare the *kallah* — the people uniquely responsible for making this wedding what it is. That combination drives the entire *halacha* that follows.

### Postponing Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבוֹעֵל בְּעִילַת מִצְוָה וּפוֹרֵשׁ, וְנוֹהֵג שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי הַמִּשְׁתֶּה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹהֵג שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי אֲבֵילוּת. וְכׇל אוֹתָן הַיָּמִים, הוּא יָשֵׁן בֵּין הָאֲנָשִׁים וְהִיא יְשֵׁנָה בֵּין הַנָּשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the groom then **engages in intercourse** with the bride to fulfill the **mitzva, and** then he **withdraws** from his wife, and the corpse is buried. **And** the groom then **observes the seven days of** the wedding **feast,** which are a personal festival for him, when the obligation of mourning rites does not take effect, **and thereafter he observes the seven days of mourning. And throughout those days** of rejoicing and mourning, the groom **sleeps among the men, and** the bride **sleeps among the women,** and they are not permitted to enter into seclusion.

The practical opportunity created by this moment is that the body has not yet been buried. *Avelus* does not begin until burial — and that creates room to maneuver. The *braita* prescribes: *machnisin es ha-meis la-cheder* — bring the corpse into a room — *ve-es ha-chosson ve-es ha-kallah la-chuppah* — and bring the *chosson* and *kallah* to the *chuppah*. The wedding proceeds while the body remains unburied and before *avelus* has begun, while the couple are still technically *onanim*.

What *chuppah* means in this context deserves attention. It may refer to a *yichud* room, in which case the act of *bi'ah* would follow immediately within it; or it may refer to the formal wedding canopy, with the act of *bi'ah* occurring afterward. Since *kiddushin* has already been performed, there is no question about *bircas erusin*, and the *sheva brachos* are recited by the *tzibbur*. The precise mechanics of what constitutes *chuppah* here can be addressed separately.

### Why Sex Needed

The *braita* continues: *u-vo'el bi'as mitzvah u-poresh* — he has relations once, the act of *mitzvah*, and then separates. Why is this required? Two explanations present themselves. One is a kind of *rachmanus*: they have prepared for this night, they anticipated it, and it would be deeply cruel to deny it entirely. The second, and perhaps more structural, reason is that the act of *bi'ah* concretizes and actualizes the status of *chosson va-kallah* — it makes the marriage genuinely real, not merely formal. Since the entire enterprise depends on establishing that the *shivas yemei ha-mishteh* status of *chosson va-kallah* precedes the onset of *avelus*, having the *bi'ah* makes that status more robust and unambiguous.

### Celebration Before Mourn…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹהֵג שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי הַמִּשְׁתֶּה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹהֵג שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי אֲבֵילוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the groom then **observes the seven days of** the wedding **feast, and thereafter observes the seven days of mourning.**

Having had *bi'as mitzvah*, the *braita* continues: *ve-nohag shivas yemei ha-mishteh, ve-achar kach nohag shivas yemei avelus* — the full seven days of the wedding feast are observed, and only afterward does the week of mourning begin.

This is the crux of the entire arrangement, and it is worth dwelling on. The logic parallels the laws of *avelus* during a *regel*. Ordinarily, if a burial occurs before a festival, the *regel* cancels the remaining days of *shiv'ah*, because the public joy of the *regel* cannot coexist with the public mourning of *avelus*. Here, the *shivas yemei ha-mishteh* of *chosson va-kallah* functions as a kind of *regel*: because it began before the *avelus* — because the burial takes place after the *chuppah* — the status of the wedding week precedes and displaces the onset of *avelus* during that entire week. The *avelus* does not kick in until the wedding week concludes.

This is a more significant leniency than it might first appear. Had *avelus* already begun, perhaps we would have permitted a single wedding night — but we would not have allowed the *shivas yemei ha-mishteh* to run its full course. By manipulating the timing to ensure that *avelus* had not yet commenced, the couple gains not just the first night but the entire week of celebration. The *avelus* then follows in full.

Several practical advantages flow from acting before burial: first, the status of *onen* may be less restrictive than that of *avel* regarding *nissuin* and regarding *bi'ah* — since there is a *machlokes* whether sex is even prohibited for an *onen*, making it less severe than the explicit prohibition during *avelus*; second, and more fundamentally, the *chuppah* itself and the *simcha* associated with *nissuin* are deeply bound up with that status of *chosson va-kallah*, and it is far less problematic to initiate *nissuin* during *aninus* than during *avelus*.

Tosafos raises a striking point: the Gemara is in effect being more lenient regarding *aninus d'oraisa* — since *aninus* has a Torah root, at least with respect to *korbanot* — than it is regarding *avelus d'rabbanan*. Tosafos explains that although *aninus* may have a *d'oraisa* anchor, the practical obligations and restrictions of an *onen* are structured very differently from those of an *avel*: an *onen* does not sit on the ground, does not rend garments, does not observe the other symbolic mourning practices. The status of *onen* is simply a different kind of status, and within that status, the space for *nissuin* and even *bi'ah* is greater.

### Sleeping Separate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל אוֹתָן הַיָּמִים, הוּא יָשֵׁן בֵּין הָאֲנָשִׁים וְהִיא יְשֵׁנָה בֵּין הַנָּשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **And throughout those days** of feast and mourning, the groom **sleeps among the men, and his wife sleeps among the women,** and they are not permitted to be alone together.

Although the *shivas yemei ha-mishteh* precedes *avelus*, the Gemara is clear that once the burial takes place — which occurs after the *bi'as mitzvah* — *avelus* begins. Even while the wedding week is in full swing, the private dimension of *avelus* obtains. The principle is that *devarim she-be-tzina* — private matters — apply even when *avelus* is otherwise suspended, just as they apply when a burial occurs during a *regel*. The most classic example of *devarim she-be-tzina* is the prohibition on marital relations. Accordingly, although the public celebrations of the week continue, the couple may not continue to have sex.

To enforce this, the *braita* prescribes: *hu yashen bein ha-anashim ve-hi yeshena bein ha-nashim* — he sleeps among the men and she sleeps among the women. This is stronger than merely sleeping in separate beds; they are placed in entirely separate company to prevent them from acting on the very powerful impulse to be together — having just gotten married — in violation of the *issur* that is now in effect. The reason this extreme measure is required, as the Gemara will explain through Rav Ashi, is that the rabbis were so lenient in enabling all of this manipulation in the first place that the couple might not take the residual *avelus* obligations seriously. Since the *avelus* has been pushed aside and only the *devarim she-be-tzina* remain, the sole live prohibition is the most invisible one. Extra measures are needed to ensure it is honored.

One might observe, as an interesting point, that requiring the couple to sleep in conspicuously separate quarters — surrounded by others of the same gender — turns what was meant to be a private matter into a very public one. The very act of enforcing the private prohibition makes it publicly visible. This tension remains unresolved in the Gemara.

### Bride's Beautification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מוֹנְעִין תַּכְשִׁיטִין מִן הַכַּלָּה כׇּל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in the event of mourning, **one does not withhold jewels from the bride** for the **entire thirty-day** period after the wedding, so that she not be undesirable to her husband.

The *braita* adds: *ve-ein monin tachshitin min ha-kallah kol shloshim yom* — one does not prevent the *kallah* from wearing jewelry and beautifying herself throughout the first thirty days, even though she is an *aveilah* and even though *shloshim* normally restricts such beautification. The rationale is clear: this is the first month of marriage, and doing everything possible to encourage mutual attraction between the new couple takes precedence over the standard requirements of *avelus* in this context.

### Father/Mother Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדַוְקָא, אָבִיו שֶׁל חָתָן אוֹ אִמָּהּ שֶׁל כַּלָּה, דְּלֵיכָּא אִינִישׁ דְּטָרַח לְהוּ. אֲבָל אִיפְּכָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the wedding takes place and is followed by seven days of feasting and seven days of mourning, **specifically** if it is **the father of** the **groom or the mother of** the **bride** who died, **as** in that case **there is no** other **person who** would **exert** themselves **for them.** They are the ones responsible for the wedding preparations, and therefore the preparations that were completed must be utilized. **However,** if **the opposite** takes place, i.e., the mother of the groom or the father of the bride dies, **no,** the practice is different. The corpse is buried immediately, the seven-day mourning period is observed, and only afterward is the couple married.

The *braita* specifies: *ve-davka aviv shel chosson o imah shel kallah* — specifically the *chosson*'s father or the *kallah*'s mother, not the reverse. The reason is *d'ika inish d'tarach lehu*: these are the people who were actively engaged in preparing for the wedding. The father oversaw and coordinated his son's preparations; the mother prepared her daughter. If we were to say simply postpone the wedding until after the *avelus*, the argument that "it can be replicated later" fails — because what cannot be replicated is the preparation that parent performed. The *kallah*'s mother prepared her for this wedding, and then died moments before it took place. The emotional and practical dimensions of that are real; it is that irreplaceability that drives the ruling. *Aval ipcha lo* — the reverse configuration does not qualify, because that presumption of active personal preparation does not apply.

Contemporary *poskim* have at times been less strict about insisting on the exact formulation of *aviv shel chosson*, particularly given the enormous financial losses involved in modern weddings — deposits, caterers, halls — that are also irretrievable. The Gemara does say *ve-davka*, which implies the specification is meaningful. Whether the financial loss alone suffices, or whether it requires both the financial factor and the irreplaceable-preparation factor together, is a genuine question. One could read the Gemara as requiring both: a purely financial loss, however large, may not be sufficient without the specific circumstance of the irreplaceable parental role.

### Food Spoilage Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַפְרָם בַּר פָּפָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנָּתַן מַיִם עַל גַּבֵּי בָּשָׂר, אֲבָל לֹא נָתַן מַיִם עַל גַּבֵּי בָּשָׂר — מִזְדַּבַּן. אָמַר רָבָא: וּבִכְרַךְ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנָּתַן מַיִם עַל גַּבֵּי בָּשָׂר — מִזְדַּבַּן. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וּבִכְפָר, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן מַיִם עַל גַּבֵּי בָּשָׂר — לָא מִזְדַּבַּן. וְאֶלָּא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כְּגוֹן מָתָא מַחְסֵיָא, דְּמַפְּקָא מִכְּרַךְ, וּמַפְּקָא מִכְּפַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rafram bar Pappa said** that **Rav Ḥisda said:** The Sages **taught** that they are married immediately **only if one** already **placed water on the meat.** In that case, it will be impossible to sell it to others, and if it is not cooked immediately it will spoil and a significant loss will be incurred, potentially resulting in cancellation of the wedding feast. **However, if he did not place water on the meat, it** can be **sold.** No significant loss will be incurred, so the mourning period need not be postponed. **Rava said: And in a city,** where there are typically many buyers, **even if he placed water on the meat it** can be **sold,** and the mourning period need not be postponed. **Rav Pappa said: And in a village, even if he did not place water on the meat, it cannot** be **sold,** because no buyers can be found to purchase a quantity of meat that great. Based on the statements of Rava and Rav Pappa, whether or not water was placed on the meat is irrelevant both in a large city and in a village. The Gemara asks: **Where do you find** a case where the statement **of Rav Ḥisda** applies? **Rav Ashi says:** It can be found in a place **like** his city of **Mata Meḥasya, which is removed from** the category of **a city,** as it is too small, **and removed from** the category of **a village,** as it is too large.

Rav Chisda introduces a qualification: *lo shanu ela she-natan mayim agabi basar* — this leniency applies only when water has been applied to the meat, i.e., the meat has been rinsed in preparation for *melichah* to remove the blood. In that state, it cannot be preserved or resold; if not consumed immediately it is lost. But if the meat has not yet been rinsed and is still in a more preserved state, it can be sold and the financial loss can be recouped — and therefore the wedding should be postponed.

Rava qualifies this further: in a large city, *u-va-krach*, even rinsed meat can be sold quickly — enough buyers exist that one can set up and sell it all within hours. The financial loss is therefore recoverable, and the wedding should be postponed. Rav Pappa offers the counter-qualification: in a village, *u-vi-chfar*, even unrinsed meat cannot be sold, because the population is too small to absorb it. So the wedding should always proceed in a village, and never needs to be postponed in one on account of rinsed versus unrinsed meat.

This leaves Rav Chisda's original distinction — rinsed versus unrinsed — without an apparent case. Rav Ashi resolves it: Rav Chisda's case is a medium-sized city, like *Masa Machasya*, which is neither a full metropolitan center nor a village. In such a town, rinsed meat cannot be sold fast enough to prevent loss, but unrinsed meat can still be preserved and sold. The *tosefta kevasei de-Rav Chisda* — the *braita* that accords with Rav Chisda — then repeats the full scenario, including the condition *ve-natan mayim agabi basar*, confirming that the operative halacha requires the meat to have been rinsed. The remainder of the *braita*'s formulation is essentially identical to what we have already seen: the corpse into the room, the couple to the *chuppah*, *bi'as mitzvah*, the seven days of wedding feast, then seven days of *avelus*, and the sleeping-separate requirement for all those days.

## Chuppat Niddah

### Niddah Sleeping Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן מִי שֶׁפֵּירְסָה אִשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה — הוּא יָשֵׁן בֵּין הָאֲנָשִׁים וְהִיא יְשֵׁנָה בֵּין הַנָּשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise,** a groom **whose wife began to menstruate** at the time of the wedding, **he sleeps among the men and she sleeps among the women,** until she becomes ritually pure.

The *braita* continues with a second scenario: *ve-chen mi she-pirsa ishto niddah* — similarly, a man whose wife becomes a *niddah* on the wedding night — *hu yashen bein ha-anashim ve-hi yeshena bein ha-nashim*, he sleeps among the men and she among the women. At first glance, this is startling, since the Gemara elsewhere is quite clear that a husband and wife share a room even when she is a *niddah*; isolating the woman to a separate dwelling is associated with practices of ancient cultures that maintained strict menstrual taboos, not with *halacha*. The key is context: the entire *braita* is addressing the wedding night specifically. Here, the couple has not yet had relations. When a *chuppat niddah* occurs — when the wife becomes a *niddah* before *bi'ah* on the wedding night — there has been no established marital intimacy, the desire is at its most intense, and the concern that they will not be able to withstand the temptation is acute. The requirement to sleep in separate company therefore applies specifically in this pre-*bi'ah* context. This is in fact a real and practically significant *halacha*, and the question of how to implement the requirement of sleeping *bein ha-anashim* and *bein ha-nashim* in contemporary circumstances is one that *poskim* address.

### Devarim Shebetzina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הוּא יָשֵׁן בֵּין הָאֲנָשִׁים וְהִיא יְשֵׁנָה בֵּין הַנָּשִׁים, מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ אֵין אֲבֵילוּת בַּמּוֹעֵד, אֲבָל דְּבָרִים שֶׁל צִינְעָא — נוֹהֵג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to analyze the *baraita*. **The Master said: He sleeps among the men and she sleeps among the women.** This **supports** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Although they stated that there is no mourning** observed **on a Festival, yet one observes matters of privacy,** i.e., mourning practices not apparent to onlookers. Therefore, the groom and the bride may not engage in relations during the seven days of rejoicing, as the legal status of those days is like that of a Festival for them.

The Gemara notes that this *braita* — specifically the requirement of sleeping separate even after the *bi'as mitzvah* in the death case — provides strong support for Rabbi Yochanan's position: *af al pi she-amru ein avelus ba-moed, aval devarim she-be-tzina noheg* — even though *avelus* does not apply during the *regel*, the private dimensions of *avelus* are nonetheless observed. Here, the *shivas yemei ha-mishteh* functions as the *regel*, and yet they must sleep separate — demonstrating that the *devarim she-be-tzina* prohibition of *bi'ah* applies even within that week. The sleeping-separate requirement is precisely the *braita*'s way of enforcing that private prohibition.

### Before vs. After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא בָּעַל, אֲבָל בָּעַל — אִשְׁתּוֹ יְשֵׁנָה עִמּוֹ. וְהָא הָכָא, דִּבְבָעַל עָסְקִינַן, וְקָתָנֵי: הוּא יָשֵׁן בֵּין הָאֲנָשִׁים וְהִיא יְשֵׁנָה בֵּין הַנָּשִׁים! כִּי קָאָמַר, אַפֵּירְסָה אִשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef, son of Rava, taught in the name of Rava: They taught** the *halakha* that if the bride began menstruating, the bride and groom may not be alone together, **only if he did not engage in intercourse** with her. **However, if he engaged in intercourse** with her, and afterward she begins menstruating, **his wife sleeps with him,** and there is no concern that this will lead to their engaging in forbidden relations. The Gemara asks: **But here,** with regard to mourning, **we are dealing** with a case **where he** already **engaged in intercourse** that was permitted prior to the funeral, **and** yet the *tanna* **teaches: He sleeps among the men and she sleeps among the women.** The Gemara answers: **When he states** the ruling that if they already engaged in intercourse she sleeps with him, it was **concerning** only a case where **his wife began menstruating,** and it is not a case of mourning.

Rav Yosef the son of Rav Ami in the name of Rava then introduces a distinction: *lo shanu ela she-lo ba'al, aval ba'al, ishto yeshena imo* — this separation requirement applies only where they have not yet had *bi'ah*; once they have had *bi'ah*, the wife sleeps with him normally.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: in the death case, the whole point is that they did have *bi'ah* — *bi'as mitzvah* — and still they are required to sleep separate. So how can Rava say that *bi'ah* removes the separation requirement? The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction by re-reading the *braita*: the *ve-chen* — "similarly" — introducing the *niddah* case does not indicate identical circumstances. Rather, it indicates that the same *halacha* of sleeping separate applies. The *niddah* case itself, however, is specifically one where *bi'ah* has not occurred; it is *mi she-pirsa ishto niddah ve-lo ba'al* — she became a *niddah* and they never had *bi'ah*. In that situation — *chuppat niddah* before any *bi'ah* — the separation requirement applies. Once *bi'ah* has taken place and the woman subsequently becomes a *niddah* in the normal course of marriage, the couple continues to share a room.

## Avelus vs. Niddah

### Niddah Duties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כׇּל מְלָאכוֹת שֶׁאִשָּׁה עוֹשָׂה לְבַעְלָהּ — נִדָּה עוֹשָׂה לְבַעְלָהּ, חוּץ מִמְּזִיגַת הַכּוֹס וְהַצָּעַת הַמִּטָּה, וְהַרְחָצַת פָּנָיו יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't Rav Yitzḥak bar Ḥanina say** that **Rav Huna said: All tasks that a woman** typically **performs for her husband, a menstruating woman performs for her husband, except for pouring** his drink into the **cup; arranging** his **bed; and washing his face, hands, and feet,** as these actions are particularly intimate.

The Gemara now turns to a broader comparison, framing it as a question: does *avelus* exhibit greater stringency than *niddah* in this regard, such that the sleeping-separate requirement would make sense as a routine feature of any case of *avelus*? The Gemara cites Rabbi Yitzchak bar Chanina in the name of Rav Huna: *kol melachot she-ha-ishah osah le-va'alah, niddah osah le-va'alah* — all the domestic duties that a wife performs for her husband, a *niddah* continues to perform. The extensive list of domestic responsibilities that will be enumerated later in the tractate applies equally during *niddah*.

### Three Harchakot

The exceptions are three specific acts designated as intimate: *chutz mi-mezigat ha-kos* — she does not mix his drink, which is seen as an act of personal and affectionate service; *ve-hatza'as ha-mitah* — she does not make his bed in the final decorative, presentational sense (Tosafos clarifies this refers specifically to the finishing touches, not the basic spreading of sheets); *ve-harchatzat panav yadav ve-raglav* — she does not wash his face, hands, and feet. These constitute the *harchakot*, the specific intimate acts that a *niddah* refrains from performing for her husband as a form of distancing.

### Avelus Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִלּוּ גַּבֵּי אֲבֵילוּת תַּנְיָא: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ אֵין אָדָם רַשַּׁאי לָכוֹף אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ לִהְיוֹת כּוֹחֶלֶת וְלִהְיוֹת פּוֹקֶסֶת, בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ: מוֹזֶגֶת לוֹ הַכּוֹס, וּמַצַּעַת לוֹ הַמִּטָּה, וּמַרְחֶצֶת לוֹ פָּנָיו יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Whereas with regard to mourning, it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Although they said that a man may not compel his** mourning **wife, to paint** her eyes **blue or to rouge [*pokeset*]** her face, **in truth they said that she may pour** his drink into **the cup; arrange his bed; and wash his face, hands, and feet.** Apparently, the concern lest they come to engage in relations while in mourning is less pressing than the concern while she is menstruating.

By contrast, when a woman is an *aveilah*, a *braita* teaches: *af al pi she-amru ein adam rashai lichof ishto lihyot kochelet lihyot pokeset* — a husband cannot compel his *aveilah* wife to beautify herself; he cannot insist she apply eye makeup (*kochelet*, which Rashi may read as rouge) or braid her hair (*pokeset*). Even so, *be-emet amru* — the halakhic truth is — she still performs all the intimate domestic acts, including mixing his drink, making his bed, and washing his face, hands, and feet. The *harchakot* that apply by *niddah* do not apply by *avelus*. An *aveilah* may perform all of these acts.

### Beautification Prohibiti…

The asymmetry is noteworthy. During *niddah*, certain intimate domestic acts are prohibited to preserve appropriate boundaries; during *avelus*, those same acts are permitted — the restriction runs the other way: the husband cannot compel the *aveilah* wife to beautify herself against her will. Her state of mourning is hers to observe, and he may not override it by demanding she present herself in a way that contradicts her *avelus*.

## Avel Sleeping Rules

### General Avel Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בַּאֲבֵילוּת דִּידֵיהּ, כָּאן — בַּאֲבֵילוּת דִּידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** where the Sages required them to sleep apart, it is **in** a case of the husband's **mourning; there,** where the Sages allowed her to pour his drink and perform other intimate activities, it is **in** a case of the wife's **mourning.** The prohibition against relations when one is mourning is not perceived to be as severe as the prohibition against relations with a menstruating woman. However, when his wife is mourning, even were he unable to restrain himself, his wife would not be complicit. Therefore, the Sages did not restrict their interaction.

Having established that *avelus* is in fact *more lenient* than *niddah* when it comes to intimate acts, the Gemara confronts its earlier assumption. It had been reading the *braita*'s sleeping-separate requirement as a general feature of any *avelus* situation. But if *avelus* is less stringent than *niddah* in terms of *harchakot*, why would we impose a more severe sleeping arrangement — separate rooms, sleeping among members of the same gender — in any ordinary case of *avelus*? This requires explanation. The Gemara's initial answer: *yetzer hara*. A man is assumed to be less in control of his sexual impulses, more capable of pressuring his wife even if she is reluctant, and more likely to override even his own *avelus* obligation. Therefore, when *he* is the *avel*, more protective measures are required.

### Husband vs. Wife

This leads the Gemara to distinguish between two configurations: *kan be-avelus didei, kan be-avelus didah* — in one case he is the *avel*, in the other she is the *aveilah*. When he is the *avel*, his *yetzer hara* may lead him to violate the prohibition on marital relations — and to bring his wife along, whether she wants to or not. When she is the *aveilah*, the same dynamic does not hold: she is in mourning, she will resist, and he will be unable to override her refusal. The Gemara therefore proposes that the sleeping-separate requirement applies specifically when the husband is the *avel*, and not when only the wife is the *aveilah*.

### Amended Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָבִיו שֶׁל חָתָן אוֹ אִמָּהּ שֶׁל כַּלָּה קָתָנֵי! כִּי קָתָנֵי, אַשְּׁאָרָא. וּמִי שָׁאנֵי בֵּין אֲבֵילוּת דִּידֵיהּ לַאֲבֵילוּת דִּידַהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: מִי שֶׁמֵּת חָמִיו אוֹ חֲמוֹתוֹ — אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לָכוֹף אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ לִהְיוֹת כּוֹחֶלֶת וְלִהְיוֹת פּוֹקֶסֶת, אֶלָּא כּוֹפֶה מִטָּתוֹ, וְנוֹהֵג עִמָּהּ אֲבֵילוּת. וְכֵן הִיא שֶׁמֵּת חָמִיהָ אוֹ חֲמוֹתָהּ — אֵינָהּ רַשָּׁאָה לִהְיוֹת כּוֹחֶלֶת וְלִהְיוֹת פּוֹקֶסֶת, אֶלָּא כּוֹפָה מִטָּתָהּ וְנוֹהֶגֶת עִמּוֹ אֲבֵילוּת. תְּנִי בַּאֲבֵילוּת דִּידֵיהּ: הוּא יָשֵׁן בֵּין הָאֲנָשִׁים וְאִשְׁתּוֹ יְשֵׁנָה בֵּין הַנָּשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **isn't it taught** in that *baraita*: **The father of the groom and the mother of the bride?** This indicates that it makes no difference which of them was in mourning. **When** the *tanna* **teaches** that there is no distinction between which of them was in mourning, it was **concerning the rest** of the mourning practices cited there, not with regard to prohibiting their seclusion. **And do** the Sages **distinguish between his mourning and her mourning? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one whose father-in-law or mother-in-law died, he may not compel his wife to paint** her eyes **blue or to rouge** her face. **Rather, he overturns his bed,** as was the practice among mourners, **and observes** the **mourning** period **with her. And likewise, if** a wife's **father-in-law or mother-in-law dies, she is not allowed to paint** her eyes **blue or to rouge** her face. **Rather, she overturns her bed and observes** the **mourning** period **with him.** There is no mention in the context of his mourning that he must not be alone with his wife. **Teach with regard to his mourning: He sleeps among the men and his wife sleeps among the women.**

The Gemara faces an objection: the original *braita* specifies *aviv shel chosson o imah shel kallah* — either his father or her mother. That means both cases — he as *avel* and she as *aveilah* — result in the same sleeping-separate arrangement. How then can we say the requirement applies only when he is the *avel*? The Gemara resolves this by proposing that the *braita* be read differently: the *ve-davka* formulation of the father-of-the-*chosson* / mother-of-the-*kallah* applies to most of the *halachos* discussed, but the specific sleeping-separate rule should be understood as inserted only for the case where he is the *avel*. The *braita* is to be read — *teni* — with that clarification built in.

### Spouse Respect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא ״וְכֵן״ קָתָנֵי! כִּי קָתָנֵי, אַכִּיחוּל וְאַפִּירְכּוּס. וְהָא ״עִמּוֹ״ קָתָנֵי, מַאי לָאו עִמּוֹ בַּמִּטָּה! לֹא, עִמּוֹ בַּבַּיִת. וּכְדַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְחִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ: בְּאַפַּהּ נְהוֹג אֲבִילוּתָא, דְּלָא בְּאַפַּהּ לָא תִּנְהוֹג אֲבִילוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **doesn't** the *tanna* **teach: And likewise?** This indicates that there is no difference between the two cases. The Gemara answers: **When** the *tanna* **teaches** that there is no distinction between which of them is mourning, it is with regard to **painting and rouge.** The Gemara asks: But **doesn't** the *tanna* **teach: With him? What,** does **it not** mean **with him** together **in bed,** and there is no concern that it will lead to sexual relations? **No,** it means **with him at home, and** it is **as that which Rav said to Ḥiyya, his son,** when his wife's father died: **Before her, observe mourning** practices; **not before her, do not observe mourning** practices. Understood in this context, the term: With him, means in his presence.

The discussion of who is the *avel* raises a further question from a second *braita*: when a man's father-in-law or mother-in-law dies — his wife's parent, not his own — he cannot compel his wife to beautify herself, and moreover, *kofeh mitaso ve-noheg imah avelus* — he overturns his own couch and himself observes *avelus* together with her. The same applies in reverse: if his parents die, she cannot beautify herself and she too turns over her couch and observes *avelus* with him. Neither of those *braisos* mentions any sleeping-separate requirement in its description of the spousal-respect dimension of *avelus*. This seems to undermine the Gemara's claim that sleeping separate is a feature of *avelus* when the husband is the *avel*.

The Gemara's response is to amend the *braita* once more — *teni be-avelus didei hu yashen bein ha-anashim ve-ishto yeshena bein ha-nashim* — inserting the sleeping-separate rule into the *braita* where the husband is the *avel*. The Gemara also resolves the phrase *kofeh mitaso ve-noheg imo avelus* — "observing *avelus* with him" — not as meaning sharing his bed, but rather as meaning that in his presence she too practices *avelus* out of respect. This is grounded by the tradition in Rav's name to his son Chiya: when in your wife's presence during her *shiv'ah*, observe *avelus*; when not in her presence, you need not. The word *imo* — "with him" — refers not to physical proximity in bed but to observing *avelus* when together in the house.

## Special Avelus

### Normal Avelus Serious

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מִי קָמְדַמֵּית אֲבִילוּתָא דְהָכָא לַאֲבִילוּתָא דְעָלְמָא? אֲבִילוּתָא דְעָלְמָא חֲמִיר, וְלָא אָתֵי לְזַלְזוֹלֵי בֵּיהּ. אֲבֵילוּת דְהָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאַקִּילוּ רַבָּנַן, אָתֵי לְזַלְזוֹלֵי בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that the question was based on a mistaken premise: **Can you compare the mourning here with mourning in general? Mourning in general is stringent, and one will not come to take it lightly.** However, with regard to **mourning here,** immediately following the wedding, **since the Sages were lenient, one will come to take it lightly.**

Rav Ashi then intervenes with a resolution that reframes the entire discussion: *mi kamadmis avelus de-hacha le-avelus de-alma*? How can you compare the *avelus* in this specific case to ordinary *avelus*? The two are fundamentally different situations, and the sleeping-separate requirement in the *braita* does not indicate anything about routine *avelus*. *Avelus de-alma chamir* — ordinary *avelus* is treated seriously. People do not take it lightly, do not disparage it, and do not need additional safeguards to ensure the prohibitions are respected.

### This Avelus Lenient

*Avelus de-hacha*, however — this particular *avelus* — is a completely different matter. *Keivan d'akilu rabbanan* — since the rabbis themselves were so lenient in this case, enabling the entire arrangement to proceed — *asu lizilzuli bei* — the person may come to treat it lightly. When the *avelus* has been pushed aside and manipulated so that the wedding week could come first, the psychological weight of the mourning obligation is diminished. The person may not feel fully bound by even what remains of the *avelus* during the wedding week.

### Leniency Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קוּלָּא? אִילֵּימָא דְּקָתָנֵי בּוֹעֵל בְּעִילַת מִצְוָה וּפוֹרֵשׁ — הָתָם מִשּׁוּם דְּלֹא חָל עָלָיו אֲבִילוּתָא, אִי לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא מִפֶּתַח הַבַּיִת, אִי לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עַד שֶׁיִּסָּתֵם הַגּוֹלֵל. אֶלָּא דְּקָתָנֵי: נוֹהֵג שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי הַמִּשְׁתֶּה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹהֵג שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי אֲבֵילוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **What leniency** did the Sages enact in this case? **If we say** it is that **which** the *tanna* **teaches:** The groom **engages in** the initial **intercourse** with the bride to fulfill the **mitzva** and then he **withdraws** from his wife, then **there,** where the corpse is placed into a room in the house, it is **due to** the fact **that mourning has not yet taken effect upon him.** Consequently, there is no leniency with regard to mourning practices. **If** it is according to the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** mourning does not take effect **until the** corpse **emerges from the entrance of the house** for burial. **If** it is according to the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** mourning does not take effect **until the covering of the grave is sealed.** **Rather,** the leniency is that **which** the *tanna* **teaches:** And the groom then **observes the seven days of feast** following the wedding, **and thereafter he observes the seven days of mourning.**

The Gemara then clarifies what the specific leniency was. It was not the act of *bi'ah* itself — the Gemara states *hatam mishum de-lo chala alei avelus*: *avelus* had not yet begun when they had *bi'ah*, because the *meis* was still unburied. According to Rabbi Eliezer, *avelus* begins when the body is removed from the house on the way to burial; according to Rabbi Yehoshua, it begins when the *golel* — either the tombstone or, according to Rashi, the cover of the *aron* — is put in place. Either way, at the time of *bi'as mitzvah*, *avelus* had not yet commenced, and so permitting *bi'ah* then was no leniency at all. The leniency — *mai kula* — is precisely what the *braita* states: *nohag shivas yemei ha-mishteh ve-achar kach noheg shivas yemei avelus* — that the entire week of wedding celebration is permitted to precede the entire week of mourning. That is the rabbinic manipulation, the pushing-aside of *avelus* to enable the *simcha* to run its course first. And it is because of that extraordinary leniency — not a violation of *halacha*, but a deliberate restructuring of its onset — that the person may come to treat the residual obligations lightly.

### Private Laws Only

Once the burial occurs, *avelus* begins; but since the *shivas yemei ha-mishteh* has already started and that status came first, most of the outward and public dimensions of *avelus* are suspended during that week. The only operative dimension of *avelus* during the week is *devarim she-be-tzina* — the private prohibition on marital relations. That prohibition is, by its very nature, invisible to others. And since the entire frame has been one of leniency — you were allowed to have your *bi'as mitzvah*, the wedding week is proceeding — there is a real concern that this one remaining, private prohibition will be taken lightly or ignored. It is precisely because *devarim she-be-tzina* is all that remains that we must go to the extreme of having him sleep among the men and her among the women, making the enforcement of a private prohibition through very public means.
