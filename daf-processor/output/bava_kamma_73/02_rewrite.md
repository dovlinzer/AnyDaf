# Bava Kamma 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Edim Zomemim Retroactive

### Intro: Edim Zomemim

We find ourselves squarely in the middle of one of the most analytically intense *sugyot* in *Shas* concerning *edim zomemim*. The preceding discussion established the great debate between Abaye and Rava over whether *edim zomemim* are disqualified *lemafre'a hu nifsul* — retroactively from the time of their false testimony — or only *mikan ule-haba* — prospectively from the moment they are exposed.

### Abaye: Lemafrea

Abaye's position is in many ways the intuitive one. Once the *zomemim* are proven to have lied, and we accept the testimony of those who contradict them, we can conclude that back at the moment they testified they were already lying. This retrospective recognition stamps them as *resha'im* — or more precisely, as *edim zomemim* — which formally disqualifies their witness status even for that earlier period. Importantly, this does not mean that a person who has lied once will necessarily lie again; rather, the act of bearing false testimony categorically labels one a *rasha* and invalidates one's standing as a witness, even if, as a matter of fact, what was said in some instance happened to be true.

### Rava: Mikan Ule-Haba

Rava's response is that disqualification takes effect only from this point forward. One compelling explanation he offers rests on a fundamental conceptual point about the nature of *edim zomemim*: logically speaking, there is no compelling reason to believe the later group over the original witnesses. If the *Torah* instructs us to accept the *hazama* process, we comply — but the degree to which we can say that this *proves* the original witnesses were lying is limited. We do not know as a fact that they lied; the *Torah* imposes a legal status upon them. That status, therefore, properly attaches from the moment it is declared, not retroactively. On another reading, Rava may genuinely agree with Abaye that the disqualification is logically retroactive — but introduces a sweeping practical consideration: if *lemafre'a* applies without qualification, then anyone who has ever testified in court is potentially a liability. Witnesses who signed a *get* two years ago might later be proven *zomemim*, rendering the *get* retroactively invalid. The practical consequences, particularly for monetary documents, would be catastrophic.

### Practical Consequences

The *halakha* is one of the well-known cases in which we rule like Abaye: *lemafre'a hu nifsul*, the disqualification is retroactive. Even Rava's pragmatic concession extends only to monetary documents — for matters of personal status, the retroactive invalidation stands with full force. The possibility that witnesses trusted for years might be unmasked as *zomemim* long after the fact is genuinely disquieting. Rava's position limits the damage for monetary instruments but cannot wholly resolve it for *issurei* matters.

### Teshuvah Discussion

One might ask whether an *ed zomem* can ever restore his witness status through *teshuvah*. The *Gemara* in Masechet Sanhedrin addresses this directly: the answer is yes, but the standard is demanding. It is not sufficient to *klop al chet* on Yom Kippur. What is required is visible, practical change — communal recognition that the person has genuinely altered his conduct. Abstract inner repentance does not suffice; the community must be able to observe tangible evidence of transformation before its confidence in the witness is restored.

## Ganav, Tavach, Machar

### Mishna Overview

The *Gemara* turns to a *mishna* that presents a case directly relevant to the Abaye-Rava debate. The *mishna* reads: *ganav al pi shenayim* — someone was convicted of burglary on the testimony of two witnesses; *ve-tavach u-machar al pihen* — the very same two witnesses testified that he subsequently slaughtered or sold the stolen animal; *ve-nimtzu zomemim* — and they were proven to be *edim zomemim* on the entire matter. The ruling is *meshalmim et hakol* — they pay the full amount corresponding to the fourfold or fivefold penalty they had sought to impose.

The natural reading of this scenario assumes a sequence: the witnesses first testified about the theft, and subsequently testified about the slaughter or sale. This makes procedural sense — no one simply walks into a *beit din* and testifies that someone sold an animal without first establishing that the animal was stolen.

### The Kashe

The *Gemara* now raises a sharp difficulty against this natural reading. If we rule like Abaye that disqualification is retroactive, a serious problem emerges. Let us place these events on a timeline. Suppose the witnesses testified about the theft at noon on Sunday and about the slaughter at noon on Monday. Later, a group of *hazama* witnesses came and declared: you could not have seen the theft on Sunday, for you were with us that day. Then another group — or the same group at a different point — declared: you could not have seen the slaughter on Monday either.

Now: according to Abaye, the witnesses became *psulei edim* retroactively from the moment of their false Sunday testimony. That means that by Monday at noon, when they testified about the slaughter, they were already disqualified witnesses. But if they were already *pasul* at the time they gave the *tavichah* testimony, that testimony was never validly received — not as false testimony but as *invalid* testimony, testimony that should never have been credited at all. A disqualified witness cannot become an *ed zomem* for that very act, since the status of *ed zomem* presupposes a legally valid act of testimony that is then proven false. Consequently, they should pay only *kefel* — the twofold penalty associated with the theft alone — but not the additional payment for slaughter or sale, since that testimony was void from the outset. This is the *Gemara*'s *kashe*.

The *Gemara* attempts a first answer: perhaps the *hazama* came first on the *tavichah* testimony, so the witnesses became *pasul* only from that later point, leaving the original *geneivah* testimony intact. But the *Gemara* immediately rejects this, noting that ultimately the *geneivah* testimony will also be undone, and *igalai milta* — it will retroactively emerge — that even at the moment they testified about the *tavichah*, they were already *pasulim*. The order in which the *hazama* groups come to court is immaterial; at the end of the day, both testimonies will be nullified.

### Bevat Achat Solution

The answer, which in retrospect may seem obvious, is that the testimony was given *b'vat achat* — all at once. The witnesses did not come on Sunday to testify about the theft and return on Monday to testify about the slaughter. They delivered the entire testimony — *ganav ve-tabach* — in a single court session.

This requires some unpacking. Even within one court session, testimony takes time to process. Witnesses do not speak simultaneously; there is cross-examination, clarification of dates and locations, and so on. So the question is not merely whether they entered the building on the same day, but whether the entire testimony is legally regarded as a single unit. The *Gemara* affirms that it is, because it was delivered within *toch k'dei dibbur* — within the halakhic span of a single speech act.

The key insight is this: if the entire testimony is one unit, then the retroactive *pesul* that attaches when the *zomemim* are exposed attaches to the whole unit simultaneously. One cannot say that "at the first instant of the testimony the witnesses were kosher, and only at the second instant they became *pasul*," because halachically there is no first and second instant — it is all one moment. Therefore, the same testimony that renders them *pasul* is also the testimony upon which they are judged as *edim zomemim*, and they pay for the full amount.

### Toch K'dei Dibbur

This solution raises the further question of what exactly *toch k'dei dibbur* means and why it renders the entire testimony a single unit. Tosafot explains the connection: within *toch k'dei dibbur*, one retains the ability to retract or correct a statement. If you pronounce a *bracha* over the wrong food and correct yourself within *toch k'dei dibbur*, the correction stands. This is why the entire speech act — even if it spans several sentences — is considered legally undivided: it remains open to self-correction and therefore constitutes one coherent act of speech rather than a series of distinct acts.

One might ask: even if all the witnesses arrived in court together, surely the testimony about the theft and the testimony about the slaughter take more than a single breath to deliver. Is it truly the case that a testimony spanning perhaps a minute of description counts as *toch k'dei dibbur*? The answer, as we shall see, involves a more nuanced examination of what *toch k'dei dibbur* actually measures — but the *Gemara*'s position at this stage is that it indeed encompasses the relevant span of time, and therefore the entire testimony is one unit.

An interesting adjacent question arises: if witnesses had their testimony accepted by the court, and it were subsequently discovered that they were relatives or otherwise disqualified — not *zomemim* — yet it is also known that their testimony was false, would their disqualification as valid witnesses carry any moral or legal consequence of its own? They would not be *chayav* for *hazama*, since *hazama* requires valid testimony that is proven false, and their testimony was never legally valid. Whether the bare fact of knowingly bearing false witness, even under a technical procedural invalidity, carries independent consequences is a genuinely difficult question.

## R. Yosi: Part Negates Wh…

### Sh'tei Eduyot vs. Edut A…

The *Gemara* now introduces a *tanna'ic* debate and asks whether it maps onto the Abaye-Rava dispute. The *baraita* presents two scenarios. In the first, two witnesses testify that someone stole an animal (*geneivah*) and that he subsequently slaughtered it (*tavichah*). They are then proven *zomemim* only on the *geneivah* — but not on the *tavichah*. In this case, *edut she-batlah miktzatah batlah kullah* — because the theft testimony is void, the slaughter testimony becomes meaningless (one cannot be liable for the enhanced penalty of four or five without the predicate theft), and nobody pays four or five. The witnesses pay *kefel* for the false theft testimony alone.

In the reverse scenario, the witnesses are found *zomemim* only on the *tavichah*. Here the *ganav* pays *kefel* for the theft, since that testimony stands, and the witnesses pay three additional parts — the difference between fourfold or fivefold and twofold — because they were *zomemim* on the slaughter.

Rabbi Yosi then introduces his qualification: *bameh devarim amurim* — when does this hold? Only *bi-shtei eduyot*, when the testimony was delivered in two distinct testimonies. But *b'edut achat*, when it was one unified testimony, *edut she-batlah miktzatah batlah kullah* — if any part of the testimony is void, the whole is void.

The novel implication here is that Rabbi Yosi applies this principle in the reverse direction from what one might expect. The *Rabbanan* already established that invalidating the *geneivah* portion nullifies the *tavichah* portion — that is the straightforward reading of the principle. Rabbi Yosi extends it: invalidating the *tavichah* portion also nullifies the *geneivah* portion, even though the *geneivah* testimony logically preceded it in time. This is the surprising move — because even under *lemafre'a*, the *geneivah* testimony was delivered *before* the false *tavichah* testimony. How does a later falsehood retroactively poison an earlier valid statement?

### Gemara Challenges

The *Gemara* seeks to clarify what Rabbi Yosi means by *shtei eduyot* versus *edut achat*. One reading: *shtei eduyot mamash* refers to two separate groups of witnesses — Group A testifies about the theft, Group B testifies about the slaughter. *Edut achat* would then mean one group, testifying on two occasions.

But this reading immediately creates a problem. If *edut achat* means one group testifying first about the *geneivah* at noon and then about the *tavichah* an hour later, how can finding them *zomemim* on the *tavichah* void their earlier *geneivah* testimony? *Me-heicha teisi hach* — where does that come from? They testified about the theft at noon and were valid witnesses. Their subsequent falsehood about the slaughter happened an hour later. There is no obvious mechanism by which a later falsehood retroactively corrupts an earlier valid testimony.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes that *shtei eduyot* means one group testifying sequentially — first about the theft and then, some time later, about the slaughter. *Edut achat* means one group testifying about both in a single utterance, *b'vat achat*, within *toch k'dei dibbur*. The dispute between Rabbi Yosi and the *Rabbanan* must therefore be about whether *toch k'dei dibbur k'dibbur dami* — whether something stated within the span of *toch k'dei dibbur* is treated as a unified single statement.

### Debate Focus Refined

The *Gemara* now proceeds carefully. It first assumes that *kulei alma* — both Rabbi Yosi and the *Rabbanan* — would agree that the disqualification of *edim zomemim* is *lemafre'a*, retroactive. Given this shared assumption, the question of whether invalidating the *tavichah* throws out the *geneivah* turns on whether we treat the testimony as one unified statement or two. If it is one unified statement (because *toch k'dei dibbur k'dibbur dami*), then retroactive invalidation reaches back through the entire statement and voids the *geneivah* portion too, since at the moment of the unified act of testimony, the witnesses were *pasulim*. If, however, the two parts are treated as distinct statements — even if delivered within *toch k'dei dibbur* — then the *geneivah* portion was validly given and is not undermined by the later *pesul*.

On this reading, the *Rabbanan* hold *toch k'dei dibbur lav k'dibbur dami* — even within that brief span, the two conceptually distinct testimonies about theft and slaughter are treated as separate. Thus only the portion upon which they are *zomemim* is void; the prior portion stands. Rabbi Yosi holds *toch k'dei dibbur k'dibbur dami* — the entire utterance is one statement, one moment in time, and voiding any part of it voids the whole.

The *Gemara* then revises this picture. It notes that if *kulei alma* held *lemafre'a* and *kulei alma* held *toch k'dei dibbur k'dibbur dami*, everyone would agree to throw out everything, and there would be no dispute. The debate must therefore itself be about *toch k'dei dibbur*. The *Rabbanan* deny that *toch k'dei dibbur* can unify two conceptually distinct testimonies into one statement; Rabbi Yosi affirms it. And since everyone by this point is understood to hold *lemafre'a*, it follows that Rabbi Yosi's position that it is all one statement also entails retroactive invalidation of the *geneivah* testimony. This is how Rabbi Yosi's ruling maps cleanly onto Abaye: *lemafre'a hu nifsul*, retroactively they were *pasul*, and therefore the unified testimony is entirely void.

The *Gemara* then raises a striking challenge against Rabbi Yosi himself. Does Rabbi Yosi truly hold *toch k'dei dibbur k'dibbur dami*? The *mishna* in the laws of *tmura* records a dispute: if someone says *harei zo tmurat olah tmurat shelamim* — "this animal shall be an exchange for a burnt-offering, an exchange for a peace-offering" — Rabbi Meir holds that we follow the first part of the statement; the animal becomes a *tmurat olah*. Rabbi Yosi says: if the person intended from the outset to designate it for both, since it is impossible to pronounce two words simultaneously, we do not coerce the statement to follow only the first word — *devarav kayyamim*, both designations are upheld and the animal is treated as half of each. But if the person said *tmurat olah* and then *nimlach* — changed his mind — and said *tmurat shelamim*, then *harei zo tmurat olah*: he is bound by his first declaration.

This seems to say that Rabbi Yosi *does not* hold that you can amend a statement within *toch k'dei dibbur* — if you clearly changed your mind, you are bound by the first word. But then how does he say, in the *edut* case, that the entire testimony is one unified statement and voiding the *tavichah* voids the *geneivah*? He seems to be treating the two halves as bound together, which should require him to hold *toch k'dei dibbur k'dibbur dami*. Yet the *tmura* case suggests he does not allow a statement made within *toch k'dei dibbur* to undo a prior statement. The cases seem to pull in opposite directions.

One could draw various distinctions: between amending a statement and appending to one; between the speech-act context of a *korban* designation, where speech creates immediate metaphysical change, and the context of court testimony, which unfolds through a process of examination; or between a correction that goes backward and a continuation that adds forward. There is also the issue of *devarim she'ba-lev* — Rabbi Yosi's ruling in the *tmura* case appears to depend on what the speaker intended, raising questions about when we rely on the speaker's declared intent.

The *Gemara*, however, resolves the tension with a technical rather than conceptual distinction, introduced by Rav Pappa.

## Two K'dei Dibur Measures

### Rav Pappa's Distinction

Rav Pappa explains that there are in fact two distinct measures subsumed under the term *toch k'dei dibbur*. The first is *k'dei she'elat talmid le-rav* — the time it takes a student to greet his teacher: *shalom alecha rabbi u-mori*. This is the longer measure, equivalent to roughly a full breath's worth of speech. The second is *k'dei she'elat ha-rav le-talmid* — the time it takes a teacher to greet a student: simply *shalom alecha*, which is half the length.

On this basis, Rav Pappa explains Rabbi Yosi's apparently contradictory positions. In the *tmura* case, where Rabbi Yosi says that changing one's mind within *toch k'dei dibbur* does not help — *nimlach* within *toch k'dei dibbur* still leaves you bound by your first statement — he is referring to the longer measure, *k'dei she'elat talmid le-rav*. That longer span is long enough that a change of mind partway through constitutes a genuine interruption and a distinct act of speech. But in the *edut* case, where he treats the entire testimony as a unified statement, he refers to the shorter measure, *k'dei she'elat ha-rav le-talmid* — within that briefer interval, he does hold *toch k'dei dibbur k'dibbur dami*.

This is an admirably economical solution, though one may be forgiven for finding it somewhat mechanical. There are many richer distinctions one might have drawn — between amending and extending a statement, between the immediate performative effect of a *korban* designation and the processual nature of court testimony, between a corrective reversal and a continuous elaboration. The *Gemara* sets all of these aside and resolves the tension on the basis of a quantitative difference in the span of *toch k'dei dibbur*.

### Rabbeinu Tam's Theory

Tosafot cites Rabbeinu Tam, who addresses a more foundational question: why is *toch k'dei dibbur* measured by a greeting at all? What is the relationship between the measure of a salutation and the legal unification of speech acts?

Rabbeinu Tam's answer is distinctively pragmatic. When a person is in the middle of a transaction or giving testimony, it can happen that a friend or teacher greets him. The *Gemara* in Berakhot establishes that one who is greeted and fails to return the greeting is called a *gazlan* — he has withheld something owed. Therefore, anyone in the course of a transaction or testimony may find himself compelled to interrupt and respond to a greeting. Since this interruption is not voluntary but socially and halachically obligatory, the Sages ruled — using the language of *tiknu chachamim* — that such an interruption shall not be counted as a *hefsek*, a legal break in the speech act. The measure of the greeting thus defines the maximum obligatory interruption that is considered invisible to halacha.

### Two Conceptions

Tosafot itself notes a tension in Rabbeinu Tam's formulation. The language *tiknu chachamim* — "the Sages enacted" — is an unusual way to describe what might seem like a definitional or conceptual ruling. Rabbeinu Tam acknowledges that this explanation sits uneasily with the *Gemara* in the final chapter of Nedarim, where *toch k'dei dibbur* is applied to the laws of *kriah* — a mourner who tears his garment believing his father has died, but the father actually dies a moment later: if the death occurs within *toch k'dei dibbur* of the tearing, the *kriah* is valid. This has nothing to do with social interruptions or obligatory greetings. Rabbeinu Tam offers only a tentative answer to this difficulty.

The deeper theoretical issue is this: there are really two distinct conceptions of *toch k'dei dibbur* operating in halachic literature. One conception, which resonates with Rabbeinu Tam's framing, treats *toch k'dei dibbur* as defining the natural unit of a *speech act* — the period during which an utterance remains open, correctable, still part of the living breath of a single declaration. The other conception, perhaps better suited to the *kriah* case, treats *toch k'dei dibbur* as defining a *moment in time* — the halachic precision with which simultaneity is measured. Two events that occur within *toch k'dei dibbur* of one another are treated as having occurred *at the same time*, regardless of their content or any speech involved.

Both of these conceptions are at play in our *sugya*, and distinguishing which applies in a given case is itself a significant analytical task.

## Hachasha v. Hazama

### Rava's Principle

Rava now introduces a separate but related ruling. *Edim she-huchkhashu u-le-vasof huzmu* — witnesses who were first subjected to *hachasha* (contradictory testimony) and who were subsequently made *zomemim* — *neheragin*, they can still be executed. The *hachasha* does not prevent the *hazama* from taking legal effect. The reason: *de-hachasha techilat hazama hi* — a contradiction is considered the *beginning* of the process of *hazama*, not an independent competing process that forecloses it.

To understand this, one must recall the distinction between *hachasha* and *hazama*. *Hachasha* occurs when another group of witnesses contradicts the testimony — saying, for instance, that Reuven was not at the scene, or offering a different account of events. This does not prove that the first group lied; it creates an impasse, and the court cannot rely on either group. *Hazama* is categorically different: the contradicting witnesses say "you could not have seen what you claim to have seen, because you were *with us* at a different location at that time." This is not merely contradictory — it is *disproof*. The *Torah* applies specific consequences to *hazama* that do not apply to mere *hachasha*.

Rava's ruling is that if witnesses are first *mochachash* — their testimony is contradicted and thrown out — and then *later* the same witnesses are *muzzam* (proven *zomemim* by a subsequent group who says they were with them elsewhere), they are still liable as *edim zomemim*. One might have thought that because the testimony had already been discarded, there was no longer a legally operative testimony against which the *hazama* could function. Rava says no: *hachasha* is the opening stage of the same process that culminates in *hazama*. The testimony is attacked, not resolved, by *hachasha*, and the *hazama* completes the attack.

### Eye and Tooth Case

Rava then asks: *mena amina lah* — from where do I derive this? He cites a *baraita*. The case involves a slave owner who is said to have blinded his slave's eye and then knocked out his tooth. The background rule is that a master who destroys any limb of his slave must free him — *ayin tachat ayin*. Crucially, if the tooth was knocked out first, the slave becomes free from that moment, and then the destruction of the eye entitles the now-free slave to the full monetary value of an eye as compensation. If the eye was blinded first, the slave becomes free from that point, and the later loss of the tooth entitles him only to the (much smaller) value of a tooth. The order thus dramatically affects the financial consequences.

The *baraita* describes witnesses who testify: first the eye was blinded, then the tooth was knocked out. And the *baraita* notes: *harei ha-rav omer kein* — the master is satisfied with this order of testimony. The *Gemara* asks: why would the master be satisfied? If this group of witnesses were the *first* to come to court, their testimony would be making the master *liable* — they are forcing him to free the slave and then pay the value of the tooth. That is worse for the master than if no testimony had been given.

The only way the master is *satisfied* with this testimony is if there was already a different testimony in place — specifically, the *opposite* order. If a prior group of witnesses had testified that the tooth came first (freeing the slave) and then the eye was blinded (now entitling the free man to the full value of an eye), the master faced a vastly larger payment. The *second* group then comes and says: no, it was the eye first, then the tooth — which means the slave became free only when the eye was blinded, and the tooth was knocked out while he was still a slave, entitling him only to the value of a tooth. The master pays far less under this order. That is why *harei ha-rav omer kein* — relative to the first testimony, the master benefits enormously from this second testimony.

### Hachasha = Beginning

Now the crucial point. The second group testified in a context where the first group had already testified to the opposite order. The second group's testimony is therefore *mochachash* — contradictory — from the very moment it is given. There is no stage at which the second testimony was simply accepted by the court; it entered into an immediate conflict with the prior testimony. And yet, the *baraita* says *ve-nimtzu zomemim* — and they were found to be *zomemim*. A third group came and said: you couldn't have witnessed what you claimed, for you were with us. And the ruling is *meshalmin dmei ayin le-eved* — the *metziai*, the middle group of false witnesses, must pay the slave the value of an eye (net of the tooth).

This proves Rava's principle. The middle witnesses began their career as *mochachashim* — their testimony was in immediate conflict with the prior group and could not be simply accepted. Yet they were ultimately processed as *edim zomemim* and paid accordingly. The *Gemara* confirms: *shema mina hachasha techilat hazama* — this case demonstrates that contradiction is the beginning of the *hazama* process, not a bar to it.

### Abaye's Counter

Abaye, however, challenges this reading of the *baraita*. He argues that the case does not require a backdrop of prior contradictory testimony. Rather, the sequence was: the middle group came first and testified that the eye was blinded and then the tooth was knocked out. *On this original testimony alone*, the master was being made liable — forced to free the slave and pay for the tooth. The master was *not* happy; they were hurting him.

Then a later group came and made the middle group *zomemim* — saying you were with us, you could not have seen what you claim. But they simultaneously testified to the true order of events: in fact, it was the tooth first and then the eye. Only now, with this later testimony in court, do we understand the true state of affairs. Retroactively, we see that what the middle witnesses had actually accomplished — had their false testimony been relied upon — was not to hurt the master but to hurt the slave, preventing him from recovering the much more valuable compensation for the eye. Their *kavana* aside, the *objective effect* of their false testimony, once the true facts emerge, was to deprive the slave.

On Abaye's reading, there is no *hachasha* at all — the *hazama* stands alone. The later witnesses do double duty: they prove that the middle witnesses were *zomemim*, and they supply the true testimony that reframes the financial impact of the falsehood. Abaye thus rejects Rava's proposed proof, denying that the *baraita* involves a case of *hachasha* preceding *hazama*. Rava's principle may or may not be correct, but this particular proof text, Abaye argues, does not establish it. The *sugya* will continue on the next *daf*.
