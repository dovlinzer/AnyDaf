# Bava Kamma 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hoda'ah & Kenas Debate

### Rav's Position

The Gemara on 75b is engaged with the question of *modeh b'knas v'achar kach ba'u eidim* — what is the legal effect of admitting to a *knas* when witnesses subsequently arrive to testify to the same offense? Three positions have been presented, or more precisely, two positions together with a significant nuance on one of them.

Rav holds that if a person admits to a *knas* and witnesses come afterwards, he remains exempt. His admission not only fails to obligate him — since the principle of *modeh b'knas patur* means that one who admits to a fine does not pay — but it actively exempts him, so that even if witnesses later appear, the *knas* cannot be imposed.

### Shmuel's Position

Shmuel disagrees. In his view, the admission does not obligate the defendant, but neither does it exempt him in a durable way. If witnesses subsequently arrive, they do obligate him, and he must pay the *knas*. The Gemara's discussion appears to come down strongly in favor of Rav's position.

### Principal Requirement

Within Rav's position, however, there is a further nuance: not every type of admission is sufficient to exempt a person when witnesses come later. The qualifying condition is *chiyev atzmo b'keren* — the admission must be one that simultaneously obligates the defendant in the *keren*, the principal amount owed. That constitutes a genuine admission. But if the admission relates only to the *knas* element, without any corresponding self-obligation in the principal, then it lacks the legal weight to produce exemption. In short, no admission ever creates an obligation in a *knas*, but the admission that exempts from a *knas* is one that carries with it a real obligation in *keren*.

## Brayta d'Tanya

### Scenario Setup

The Gemara now seeks to demonstrate this position from a *brayta*. The *brayta* in question describes the following scenario: a person sees witnesses making their way toward the *beis din*, moving in to testify against him, and decides to get ahead of them by admitting. He declares *ganafti* — I committed the theft — but adds *aval lo tavachti v'lo macharti*, I did not slaughter or sell the animal. The rule is *eino meshalem ela keren*: he pays only the principal.

The *chiddush* of this *brayta* is that even though the defendant admitted only because he saw witnesses approaching — hardly a spontaneous or entirely voluntary admission — it is still legally treated as an admission. And because it is treated as an admission, even if the witnesses actually arrive in court, he is exempt from the *kefel*. Since *kefel* is one component of the four-and-five payment (four for a sheep, five for an ox), his exemption from *kefel* carries with it exemption from the additional payments as well.

### Brayta Logic

How does this *brayta* support the position that admission must come with an obligation in *keren* in order to exempt? The Gemara asks: why does the *brayta* specifically present the example of admitting to the theft (*ganafti*) while denying the slaughter and sale? Why not simply give one of two cleaner examples — either admitting to the *gneivah* alone, which would exempt from *kefel* and thus automatically from four-and-five, or admitting to the *tevicha u'mechira* alone, which would exempt from that component?

The answer, the Gemara suggests, is that the second option simply does not work. An admission only to the *tevicha u'mechira*, made after witnesses to the *gneivah* have already appeared, does not obligate the person in anything — it obligates him in no *keren*, since the *gneivah* has already been established by others. Therefore the *brayta* specifically gives the example of admitting to the *gneivah*, because that admission obligates him in the principal and thereby qualifies as the kind of admission that can exempt. This seems to be a sound proof: *hoda'at tevicha lav hoda'ah hi* — an admission to the slaughtering alone is not a real admission, because it comes with no self-obligation.

### Gemara's Challenge

The Gemara, however, rejects this inference. The reason the *brayta* chose the example of admitting to *gneivah* rather than admitting to *tevicha* is not to teach that only the former counts as a valid admission. Rather, the *brayta* chose this case in order to teach a remarkable *chiddush* about the case itself.

### The Novelty

The *chiddush* is this: the defendant admitted to the theft but denied the slaughter and sale. He was lying about the denial, as witnesses later came to testify to the *tevicha u'mechira*. Yet despite having denied it and despite witnesses proving the *tevicha u'mechira*, he is still exempt from paying the additional penalty. *Uva'u eidim she'tavach u'machar — patur*: even when witnesses come to prove the slaughter and sale, he pays nothing beyond the *keren*. This is a remarkable and extreme result: an admission made only because witnesses were already on their way, accompanied by a lie about the *tevicha u'mechira*, still suffices to remove the obligation for everything beyond the principal.

### Torah's Language

Why? *Tashlumei arba'a v'chamisha rachmana*, the Torah declared that one pays four or five — not four and three, not some other combination. Once the defendant admitted to the *gneivah* and thereby obligated himself in the *keren*, one unit of the *kefel* payment is removed. And the Torah's language of "four and five" means that the payments stand together: remove one component, and the entire structure of the penalty collapses. You cannot have a payment of four without the full complement of components, and you cannot have a payment of five without it either. So the reason the *brayta* chose this example was to teach the dramatic *chiddush* of this specific case — not to exclude the possibility that an admission to *tevicha* alone might also work. The *brayta*, on this reading, is silent about that question, and so it does not prove our position.

## Alternative Brayta Readi…

### Tana'im Debate

Since the *brayta* fails to resolve the question definitively, the Gemara proposes a different approach. Perhaps the debate about whether an admission that does not obligate in the *keren* is a valid admission is itself a *tannaitic* dispute — that is, perhaps the positions of the *amora'im* reflect earlier debates between *tanna'im*. All may agree with Rav that an admission can exempt a person; the question is whether that exemption extends even to an admission that carries no obligation in *keren*. To explore this, the Gemara turns to a *brayta* involving *edim zomemim*.

### Witnesses Discredited

The *brayta* presents the following scenario. Two witnesses testify that a defendant committed *gneivah*, and two additional witnesses testify that he performed the *tevicha u'mechira*. If the witnesses to the *gneivah* are subsequently proven to be *zomemim* — their testimony is discredited — then by the principle of *eidut shebatla miktzatah batla kulah*, once part of the testimony falls, it all falls. Since one cannot establish that the defendant was a *ganav*, the testimony to the *tevicha u'mechira* becomes meaningless. Perhaps he was slaughtering his own animal. Accordingly, the discredited witnesses pay him *kefel*, and the defendant pays nothing, even though there remain witnesses to the *tevicha u'mechira*.

### Slaughter Witnesses Discredited

In the reverse case, where the witnesses to the *tevicha u'mechira* are proven *zomemim*, the result is different. The *gneivah* still stands, so the defendant pays the owner *tashlumei kefel*, and the discredited witnesses pay the defendant three (the extra component beyond *kefel* that constitutes the "four" for a sheep or "five" for an ox).

### Sumchus Position

Then the *brayta* adds: in the name of Sumchus, the witnesses pay *tashlumei kefel* (two) and the defendant pays *tashlumei shlosha* (three). This is a perplexing position on its face. Why would the witnesses pay two and the defendant pay three? This formulation makes sense only in the first case — where the witnesses to the *gneivah* are discredited — since at least numerically the figures of two and three correspond. But in the first case, it seems impossible: if you cannot establish that there was a *gneivah*, how can the defendant be paying three to the owner?

The Gemara works through the possibilities. *Ileima reisha* — if Sumchus is addressing the first case, the problem is clear: he would be contradicting himself by holding that only part of the testimony is discredited while the rest remains. *Ella seifa* — perhaps Sumchus is addressing the second case, where the witnesses to the *tevicha* are discredited. But in that scenario, it is the *Rabbanan's* position — that the defendant pays *kefel* and the witnesses pay three — that makes the most sense, and Sumchus's reversal seems inexplicable.

Because neither reading of Sumchus coheres naturally with the cases described, the Gemara concludes that a completely different underlying scenario must be constructed to render Sumchus intelligible.

## Zomemim Scenario

### Setup & Proof

The Gemara reconstructs the case as follows. Two witnesses, call them Michael and David, come to court and testify that the defendant committed *gneivah*. The defendant responds: yes, I admit — *ganafti v'tavachti u'macharti*, I stole and I slaughtered and I sold — however, *lo bifneichem ganavti*, I did not steal it in your presence. You were not there. You are lying about having witnessed it. The defendant then produces his own witnesses who prove that Michael and David were elsewhere at the time — they are *edim zomemim*. They must therefore pay him *kefel*.

Now, in the meantime, the owner of the stolen animal brings ראובן and שמעון (*Reuven* and *Shimon*), who come to court and testify that they witnessed the defendant commit the entire *gneivah*, *tevicha*, and *mechira*. The question is: what does the defendant now owe the owner?

### Rabbanan View

The *Rabbanan* hold that the defendant's admission — *ganafti v'tavachti u'macharti* — counts as a valid *hoda'ah*, even though it was made in response to witnesses who subsequently turned out to be *zomemim*. It was a double admission: an admission to the *gneivah* and an admission to the *tevicha u'mechira*. Since it counts as a genuine admission, and since *modeh b'knas patur*, the defendant is exempt from the *kenas* component. He pays the owner only the *keren*, and no more, even when *Reuven* and *Shimon* come to confirm the *tevicha u'mechira*. The admission stands as a valid *hoda'ah* that prevents the *kenas* from being imposed. The *zomemim* pay the defendant two, and he remains exempt beyond the *keren*.

### Sumchus View

Sumchus holds that this admission does not qualify. His reasoning is subtle and precise. The defendant's admission to the *gneivah* — *ganafti* — was made in direct response to Michael and David's accusation. Even though they turned out to be *zomemim*, the defendant did not know at the moment of admission that he would be able to disprove them. His *ganafti* was not a free and spontaneous *hoda'ah*; it was a reaction to their presence in court. An admission made under that kind of pressure, *machmat eidim*, is not a genuine *hoda'ah*.

Furthermore, Sumchus identifies a critical asymmetry within the double admission. The defendant said *ganafti v'tavachti u'macharti*. Michael and David accused him only of *gneivah* — they said nothing about *tevicha u'mechira*. So when the defendant added *tavachti u'macharti*, that portion of the admission was entirely volunteered and not driven by their testimony. It was, in that sense, a sincere *hoda'ah* to the *tevicha u'mechira*. But according to Sumchus, that admission to *tevicha u'mechira* is precisely the kind of admission that does not obligate in any *keren*, because at the time of that admission, the *gneivah* itself had not been established — the *ganafti* portion was driven by the *eidim* and therefore does not count. An admission to *tevicha u'mechira* in isolation, without a corresponding obligation in *keren*, is not a valid *hoda'ah*.

Therefore, Sumchus concludes, the defendant's admission affords him no protection. When *Reuven* and *Shimon* come to confirm the entire sequence of events, the defendant is obligated for the full amount. Since Michael and David are *zomemim* and must pay him two, and since he owes the owner the full four or five, the practical result is that Michael and David's payment of two flows to the owner via the defendant, and the defendant pays the remaining three directly from his own funds. Hence: they pay two, he pays three.

The *Rabbanan*, by contrast, hold that even though the *ganafti* was in reaction to the *eidim*, the admission to *tevicha u'mechira* added on top of it remains a valid *hoda'ah* and exempts from the *kenas*.

### Rav Acha's Reading

Rav Acha bar Rav Ika objects to this entire construction. He argues that it is not necessary to build the case around *eidim* who are subsequently proven *zomemim* in order to explain Sumchus's position. On his reading, *dechulei alma hoda'at tevicha lav hoda'ah hi* — everyone agrees that an admission to *tevicha* alone, without an accompanying obligation in *keren*, does not constitute a valid *hoda'ah*. This is not the point of the *tannaitic* dispute. Rather, the real dispute concerns *edut she'ei atah yachol lehazimah* — testimony that structurally cannot be subjected to *hazamah*, the procedure by which witnesses are discredited.

### Real Witnesses

The scenario Rav Acha constructs is closely parallel in its opening but diverges decisively in what follows. Two witnesses accuse the defendant of *gneivah*. He again says *ganafti v'tavachti u'macharti* while denying that those particular witnesses were present. He again proves them *zomemim*. But this time, rather than the owner producing entirely independent witnesses, the defendant himself has named *Reuven* and *Shimon* — saying, in effect, "it was before them that I stole." The owner then calls *Reuven* and *Shimon*, who duly testify to the *gneivah*, *tevicha*, and *mechira*.

---

## Undisproven Testimony

### Rabbanan Position

The *Rabbanan* hold that the testimony of *Reuven* and *Shimon* in this scenario is disqualified, because it constitutes *edut she'ei atah yachol lehazimah*. Ordinarily, the principle that testimony incapable of *hazamah* is inadmissible exists because witnesses who cannot be proven *zomemim* have no fear of lying — the threat of being found *zomemim* is what makes testimony trustworthy. Here, the defendant himself has asserted that *Reuven* and *Shimon* witnessed his actions, which means he is effectively supporting their testimony. Testimony that the accused himself corroborates cannot be subjected to *hazamah* in the ordinary way, for it would be bizarre to go ahead and try to prove that *Reuven* and *Shimon* were elsewhere when the defendant himself said they were present. This creates a situation where the testimony is formally protected against *hazamah*, and therefore, paradoxically, it is inadmissible under the principle of *edut she'ei atah yachol lehazimah*.

The practical consequence, according to the *Rabbanan*, is that since *Reuven* and *Shimon's* testimony is invalid, it cannot obligate the defendant. The *zomemim* pay him two, and he pays the owner nothing beyond the *keren* — not because of any *hoda'ah* exemption, but because the testimony that would have obligated him is inadmissible.

### Sumchus Position

Sumchus holds that *edut she'ei atah yachol lehazimah* is still valid testimony. He maintains that such testimony, though it cannot be subjected to *hazamah*, is not thereby disqualified. On this view, *Reuven* and *Shimon's* testimony stands, and the defendant is obligated for the full four or five. The *zomemim* pay him two, and he pays the owner the remaining three.

### Conclusion

The Gemara then moves to dismiss this entire reconstruction by invoking the accepted principle: *v'kayma lan* — it is established that *edut she'ei atah yachol lehazimah lo havya edut*, that testimony which cannot be subjected to *hazamah* is not valid testimony. Since this is the accepted ruling, Sumchus's position — which requires that such testimony be valid — cannot stand as the *Gemara* has described it. Any reading of the *brayta* that forces us to attribute to Sumchus the view that such testimony is valid is therefore untenable. One should note that this principle applies where the testimony is rendered incapable of *hazamah* because of a structural defect — not merely because it happens to be difficult to disprove, as when witnesses are vague about the time or place of the event.

## Sanctification Issue

### Mishnah Case

The Gemara now moves forward to the *Mishnah*. The case presented is: *ganav v'hikdish v'achar kach tavach u'machar* — a person stole an animal, sanctified it as a *korban*, and then subsequently slaughtered or sold it. The *Mishnah*'s ruling is that he does not pay four and five for the *shechita* or *mechira* performed after the *hekdesh*. The reason is straightforward: when he slaughtered or sold it, he was dealing with an animal that belonged to *hekdesh*, not to the original owner. *De'ki tavach de'hekdesh tavach, v'lo de'marei tavach* — he slaughtered *hekdesh's* animal, not the owner's.

### Gemara Question

The Gemara, however, raises a pointed question about the *Mishnah*. Granted, once the animal has been sanctified and the defendant then slaughters it, there is no liability — he is not slaughtering the owner's animal. But what about the act of sanctification itself? Why is *hekdesh* not treated as an act of *mechira*? *Mah li machar l'hedyot mah li machar l'shamayim* — what difference does it make whether you sold it to a private individual or to *shamayim*? The thief took this stolen animal and transferred it to *hekdesh*, to the ownership of the *Beis HaMikdash*. Why does that not constitute a sale that would obligate him in the four-and-five payment?

### Rabbi Shimon Answer

The Gemara answers: *ha manni Rebbi Shimon hi* — this is the view of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *kodshim she'chayav b'achrayutan birshutei de'marei kamei*, that *kodshim* for which the owner bears liability for replacement are still considered to remain under the owner's domain — in the present context, under the *ganav's* domain. What does this mean in practice? If a person has accepted upon himself a *neder* of an *olah* or is obligated to bring a *chatat*, and he designates this stolen animal to fulfill that obligation, then if the animal is lost, he must replace it. Because its loss causes him a financial loss, it is treated as though the animal is still under his control. This is analogous to the principle of *davar hagorim l'mamon k'mamon dami* — something whose loss generates a monetary obligation is treated as though it remains within one's possession.

Accordingly, in Rabbi Shimon's framework, the act of sanctification in such a case is not a full *mechira*, because the animal is treated as still under the *ganav's* control. It is, however, no longer the owner's animal — it belongs to *hekdesh*. This creates an interesting intermediate status: the act of sanctification does not constitute a *mechira* that would obligate in four-and-five, because the animal is considered still under the *ganav's* auspices; yet when the *ganav* subsequently slaughters it, he is not liable, because at that point he is slaughtering *hekdesh's* animal, not the owner's. Rashi notes the irony: the sanctification did not transfer the animal far enough away from the *ganav* to create liability for *mechira*, yet it transferred the animal far enough away from the owner that subsequent *shechita* creates no liability.

### Types of Kodashim

The Gemara then proposes an alternative approach that does not require relying on Rabbi Shimon. The *Mishnah's* ruling could reflect the position of *kodashim kalim* according to Rabbi Yose HaGalili. Rabbi Yose HaGalili holds that *kodashim kalim*, lighter sacrifices such as *shelamim*, are considered *mamon ba'alim* — still the property of the owner — because the owner retains the right to eat the meat. Since the animal is still considered the *ganav's*, the act of sanctification is not a *mechira*. Yet because it is *hekdesh*, subsequent *tevicha* does not create liability. This is another pathway to the same intermediate result.

The Gemara then asks: what would the rule be for *kodshei kodshim*, the most sacred sacrifices, where neither Rabbi Shimon's *chayav b'achrayutan* nor Rabbi Yose HaGalili's *kodashim kalim* reasoning applies? Would the act of *hekdesh* then constitute a *mechira* and obligate in four-and-five? If so, the *Mishnah* should have made a distinction: for *kodashim kalim* no liability for *hekdesh*, but for *kodshei kodshim* liability for *hekdesh*. The fact that the *Mishnah* makes no such distinction suggests that neither type of *hekdesh* is ever treated as a *mechira*.

## Status vs. Ownership

### Association Remains

The Gemara, pressed to explain why the act of *hekdesh* is universally not considered a *mechira* regardless of the type of *korban*, offers the following principle: *me'ikara tora d'Reuven v'hashta tora d'Reuven* — when one sells to a private individual, the ox that was Reuven's is now Shimon's. The association of the animal with the original person is severed. But when one sanctifies an animal as a *korban*, it is still referred to as Reuven's *olah* or Reuven's *chatat*. The animal may be sanctified, it may belong to *hekdesh*, but it is still associated with the person who sanctified it. This persistent association is what distinguishes *hekdesh* from *mechira*: a genuine *mechira* severs the connection entirely, whereas sanctification as a *korban* does not.

### Kedusha vs. Ownership

This observation points to a deeper distinction. When a person sells an animal to another person, the transaction is fundamentally about transferring ownership. The question is: who is the owner? The answer changes cleanly from seller to buyer. But when a person sanctifies an animal as a *korban*, the transaction is not primarily about transferring ownership — it is about conferring *kedusha*, about changing the animal's status so that it may now function as a vehicle for the *avodah*. The *korban* is still referred to as the person's *korban*. The purpose of sanctification is not to give the animal to some other legal entity but to invest the animal with holiness, with a capacity for sacred service. That is why even *kodshei kodshim*, where the owner has no remaining proprietary interest in the meat, still does not constitute a *mechira*: the question of ownership is simply not what sanctification is about.

### Types of Sanctity

This distinction carries important implications when one considers *kodshei bedek habayit* — donations made to the Temple treasury for the upkeep of the *Beis HaMikdash*. Tosafot, as noted below, identifies *kodshei bedek habayit* as a category of *hekdesh* that does function like a *mechira*. The conceptual difference is precisely this: when one donates an animal to *bedek habayit*, the animal passes into the ownership of the *Beis HaMikdash* as an institution. The *gizbar* takes possession; the animal is now the *Beis HaMikdash*'s asset. No one refers to it as the donor's animal — at most, they say the donor gave it. By contrast, a *korban* remains associated with the person who brought it, because the entire purpose of a *korban* is precisely this personal relationship between the *makriv* and the *avodah*.

This also maps onto two distinct ways of conceptualizing *me'ilah* — the prohibition of deriving benefit from *hekdesh*. One can understand *me'ilah* as a form of *geneivah* or *gezeilah*, taking something that belongs to another (in this case, *hekdesh*). That conception fits naturally with *kodshei bedek habayit*, where the animal is genuinely owned by the Temple. Alternatively, one can understand *me'ilah* as an *issur hana'ah* — a prohibition against using a sanctified object for non-sanctified purposes, based not on ownership but on the object's sacred status. That conception fits naturally with *kodshei korbanos*, where the prohibition is rooted in the animal's *kedusha* rather than in a transfer of title. The two ways of thinking about *me'ilah* thus reflect two fundamentally different concepts of *kedusha*: one about ownership, and one about status.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot addresses an apparent difficulty in this framework. If the act of sanctifying an animal as a *korban* leaves the animal still associated with the *ganav*, one might think that no *shinui* at all has occurred — and if no *shinui* has occurred, the entire discussion of *hekdesh* as a potential *mechira* would never have gotten off the ground in the first place. Tosafot therefore clarifies that a *shinui hashem* — a change of name or designation — does unquestionably occur when an animal is sanctified as a *korban*, since it is now called a *korban* rather than simply an animal. Moreover, Tosafot invokes the possibility of *shinui reshut*, noting that once something becomes a *korban*, it is indeed considered under the auspices of *hekdesh* and is not purely and entirely the *ganav's*. It is not, as one might oversimplify, that sanctification leaves the animal wholly within the *ganav's* domain with only its *kedusha* added on. Rather, the animal genuinely passes to some degree into the domain of *hekdesh*. But because it remains associated with the *ganav* — it is still his *korban* — this *shinui reshut* is incomplete, and it is precisely this incompleteness that prevents the act of sanctification from rising to the level of a full *mechira*.

## Rabbi Shimon's Rule

### Seeming Contradiction

The *Mishnah* at the end cites Rabbi Shimon's statement that *kodashim she'chayav b'achrayutan* — sacrificial animals for whose replacement the owner is liable — do obligate in four-and-five, while those for which the owner is not liable do not. This seems, at first glance, paradoxical. Rabbi Shimon himself, as quoted above, holds that *kodashim she'chayav b'achrayutan* are still considered under the owner's control. If that is true, then *chayav b'achrayutan* means the animal is still considered the *ganav's*, and therefore sanctification is not a *mechira*. But then why would Rabbi Shimon say that in the case of *chayav b'achrayutan* one must pay four-and-five? And if *she'eino chayav b'achrayutan* means the animal has left the *ganav's* control entirely and passed to *hekdesh*, that would seem to be more like a *mechira* — yet Rabbi Shimon says in that case one is exempt.

### Different Case

The resolution is that Rabbi Shimon in the *Mishnah* is not addressing the same scenario that the earlier discussion addressed. He is not speaking of a case where the *ganav* stole an animal and then sanctified it himself. Rather, Rabbi Shimon is addressing a different case entirely: someone steals a *korban* that was already sanctified from the owner's house.

### Liability = Ownership

In this scenario, the question is whether the law of *v'gunav mibeis ha'ish* — theft from the house of a person — applies to a sanctified animal. Rabbi Shimon holds: if the owner is *chayav b'achrayutan*, meaning that if this *korban* is lost he must replace it at his own expense, then the animal is considered his in a meaningful economic sense. Its loss is his loss. Therefore, the law of *v'gunav mibeis ha'ish* does apply, and a *ganav* who steals such a *korban* from the owner's house and slaughters it must pay four-and-five. *Karina bei v'gunav mibeis ha'ish* — it qualifies as being stolen from the house of that person.

### No Liability = Exemption

Conversely, if the owner is not *chayav b'achrayutan* — if this particular *korban* is not his financial responsibility should it be lost — then the animal is not considered his in the relevant sense. Stealing it from his house is like stealing from *hekdesh* itself, and the rule of *v'gunav mibeis ha'ish* does not apply. The *ganav* pays no four-and-five. *D'lo karina bei v'gunav mibeis ha'ish.*

## Shechicha Outside Beis H…

### Problem Setup

Having established that in the case where a *ganav* steals a *korban* that is *chayav b'achrayutan* he must pay four-and-five, the Gemara identifies a serious difficulty. Rabbi Shimon himself holds that *shechita she'einah re'uyah lo shma shechita* — a *shechita* that does not render the animal permissible for consumption is not considered a *shechita* for the purposes of the four-and-five obligation. But if the *ganav* steals this *korban* from the owner's house and slaughters it in his backyard, outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, this is a classic case of *shchutei chutz* — slaughtering a *korban* outside the Temple — and the animal is *assur b'hana'ah*. One cannot benefit from it. How then can such a *shechita* be the basis for a four-and-five payment?

### First Answer: Brought to…

Rav Dimi, citing Rabbi Yochanan, provides the first answer. The case is one where the *ganav* felt remorseful and, recognizing that the animal is a *korban*, brought it to the *Beis HaMikdash* and slaughtered it there on the owner's behalf — *l'shem ba'alim*. In this setting, the *shechita* is entirely proper and the animal could be eaten. It qualifies as *shechita re'uyah*. The *ganav* has, in effect, brought the owner's *korban* on the owner's behalf — which might seem like an act of restoration rather than an act of *geneivah*. How can this be the predicate for a four-and-five payment?

The Gemara resolves this: *she'harei chazar keren l'ba'alim* — it would appear that the *ganav* has returned the principal, the animal itself, to the owner's account. And indeed, at the moment of *shechita*, had everything proceeded normally, it would have been credited to the owner. But then the blood was spilled — *k'she'nishpach hadam* — the *kohen* spilled the blood before it could be thrown on the altar, and the *korban* was never actually credited to the owner's account. In hindsight, therefore, this was not an act of restoration — the owner received nothing. We now see that the *shechita* was an act of furthering the *geneivah*. Yet the *shechita* itself, at the moment it was performed, was fit and proper: the animal was inside the *Beis HaMikdash*, slaughtered in the correct manner, and the meat would have been permissible had the blood been sprinkled. It was therefore a *shechita re'uyah*, and the four-and-five obligation applies.

There is something striking in this analysis: we assess the validity of the *shechita* as of the moment it was performed — at which point it was indeed *re'uyah* — while we assess the *geneivah* character of the act retrospectively, based on the spilled blood revealing that the owner received no benefit. The two assessments are, in a sense, made at different moments in time.

### Second Answer: Not For O…

Ravin, also citing Rabbi Yochanan, offers a second answer that does not require the *ganav* to be so well-intentioned. In this version, the *ganav* brings the animal to the *Beis HaMikdash* and slaughters it inside — but *shelo l'shem ba'alim*, not on the owner's behalf. He claims it as his own *korban*. The owner therefore receives no credit — the *korban* is not attributed to him — so there is no act of restoration. At the same time, the *shechita* is performed inside the *Beis HaMikdash* and the meat is fit to be eaten, either by the *kohanim* or, if it is a *shelamim*, by others as well. It is thus unambiguously a *shechita re'uyah*, and the four-and-five obligation follows. The *ganav* did not return the animal to the owner and did advance the *geneivah*, and the *shechita* was a proper one — both conditions for liability are satisfied.

### Third Answer: Animal Had…

Reish Lakish proposes a third approach. The animal had a *mum* — a blemish — and was slaughtered outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. An animal with a *mum* cannot be brought as a *korban* in any case, and therefore slaughtering it outside the *Beis HaMikdash* does not violate the prohibition of *shchutei chutz*, which applies only to animals that could have been offered inside. Accordingly, the *ganav* has not transgressed *shchutei chutz*, and the animal is not *assur b'hana'ah* on that basis.

However, a question remains: even though the *ganav* has not violated *shchutei chutz*, the animal still carries *kedushat korban* — it was sanctified as a *korban* even if it could not be offered in its current state. Does slaughtering it outside, while it still has *kedushat haguf*, render it a *shechita she'einah re'uyah*? After all, a blemished *korban* that has not been redeemed cannot be eaten — it retains its sanctity even in its disqualified state. If the meat cannot be eaten, then by Rabbi Shimon's own principle, this *shechita* should not qualify as *shechita re'uyah* for the purposes of four-and-five. The Gemara leaves this as an opening for further discussion, noting the three distinct proposed answers and their respective resolutions — or unresolved difficulties — in accounting for how a *shechita* of a stolen *korban* can qualify as *shechita re'uyah* and give rise to the four-and-five obligation.
