# Nazir 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nazir Tamei: Yochanan vs…

### Nazir in Cemetery

The opening discussion of this *daf* centers on a case that the *mishna* introduces: a *nazir* who accepts *nezirut* while standing in a cemetery (*beis hakevarot*). This scenario raises several interrelated questions, and before proceeding to the *gemara*'s dialectic, it is worth placing the broader issue on the table.

If someone is *tamei* and accepts *nezirut*, what are the implications? When does the *nezirut* take effect? And there is an additional question lurking beneath the surface, one relevant not only to a *nazir* but to a *kohen* as well: if a person is already *tamei*, can they become *tamei* again? Practically speaking, this question bears on *kohanim* today, given the widespread assumption that people are born in hospitals containing morgues, such that nearly everyone is considered *tamei meis* from birth. If a *kohen* is already *tamei*, what is the status of becoming *tamei* a second time?

The real difficulty in this question arises only on the very day the original *tumah* began. Any subsequent day, one day later or more, there is a nearly universal assumption that further *tumah* adds a layer and is therefore equally prohibited. But on the very first day, the question is genuinely interesting and has practical relevance for *kohanim*.

### Mishna Framework

The *mishna* addresses the case of someone who accepts *nezirut* while in a cemetery. The principle established is as follows: the person's *nezirut* does not begin while they remain *tamei*. They must first become *tahor*, which requires going through the *taharah* process — receiving the ashes of the *parah adumah* on day three and day seven — and their thirty-day count of *nezirut* begins only once they emerge as *tahor*. Crucially, however, such a person does *not* bring a *korban tumah*.

The reason is structural: the *korban tumah* is brought only when a *nazir* began in a state of *taharah* and subsequently became *tamei*. Here, the person began their *nezirut* already in a state of *tumah*, so the specific *korban* associated with violating a previously clean *nezirut* does not apply.

### R. Eliezer Position

*Rabbi Eliezer* adds a further qualification regarding the *korban*. He holds that even in the standard case — a *nazir tahor* who becomes *tamei* — a *korban* is required only if the person became *tamei* on the *second* day of their *nezirut*, not on the very first day. The *torahitic* phrase about "earlier days falling away" implies that there must be earlier days to fall away in the first place. If someone became *tamei* on the very day they accepted *nezirut*, *Rabbi Eliezer* holds they would not bring a *korban*. However, this debate does not directly affect the case of the person who began as *tamei* in the cemetery, who does not bring a *korban* for an entirely independent reason.

### Nezirut Timing

The *gemara* initially frames the dispute between *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Reish Lakish* as a question about *when* the *nezirut* takes effect. On one reading, the core question is whether the *nezirut* begins at all while the person is still *tamei*, or whether it is entirely suspended until after the person achieves *taharah*. This framing would have sweeping consequences for what obligations and prohibitions apply during the *tumah* period.

### Reish Lakish View

*Reish Lakish*'s initial position, as the *gemara* first presents it, is that the *nezirut* is entirely suspended while the person is *tamei*. Since the *nezirut* did not get a chance to begin in a state of *taharah*, it is in abeyance, and therefore none of the prohibitions of *nezirut* apply during this period. The striking consequence: if the person were to drink wine, get a haircut, or incur further *tumah* while remaining in the cemetery, they would receive no *malkus*.

This is counterintuitive when contrasted with the case of a *nazir tahor* who subsequently becomes *tamei*. That person, who started their *nezirut* properly, remains a full *nazir* throughout — even while *tamei*, they are prohibited from wine, shaving, and contracting *tumah*, and violations are punishable with *malkus*. The asymmetry that *Reish Lakish* is apparently proposing is that when the *nezirut* never began in *taharah*, it does not rise to the level of full force even to impose *malkus*.

### Tosafot on Re-Acceptance

*Tosafot* introduces an important additional inference on 16b. The passage there reads: *Reish Lakish amar ein neziros chalah alav be-odo tamei ad she-yit-hare ve-az yit-starech lachazor u-lekabel alav neziros* — that according to *Reish Lakish*, the *nezirut* does not take effect while the person is *tamei*, and when they become *tahor*, they would need to re-accept the *nezirut* explicitly. *Tosafot* cites *Rashi*'s view that a minimal re-acceptance (*kabalah mu'etes*) suffices — one need not perform a full acceptance anew, merely indicate that the *nezirut* should now begin.

Why does *Tosafot* introduce this? Because it helps explain the *gemara*'s otherwise puzzling question. The *gemara* asks: if *Reish Lakish* holds that the *nezirut* is suspended while the person is *tamei*, why does the *mishna* say that "day seven counts" as the beginning of the thirty-day period? The force of the question depends on the assumption that, since it was suspended, it should not begin automatically — it should require active re-triggering. Without that assumption, the question is weak; once *tahor*, why *wouldn't* it begin? So *Tosafot* reads into *Reish Lakish*'s position the assumption that automatic resumption is insufficient, which is what gives the *gemara*'s challenge its teeth.

### Gemara Self-Correction

The *gemara* then fundamentally recasts the dispute. *Mar bar Rav Ashi* clarifies: *meheicha kaamrat lo pligi d'chayil* — actually, *Reish Lakish* does not disagree that the *nezirut* takes effect even while the person is *tamei*. Everyone agrees that accepting *nezirut* in a state of *tumah* creates *nezirut* immediately. *Ela ki pligi l'milki* — the dispute is only about whether the transgressions carry *malkus*.

*Rabbi Yochanan* holds that since the *nezirut* took effect, it carries full force and violations are punishable with *malkus*. *Reish Lakish* holds that while it took effect, because it was accepted in a compromised state — a *tumah* state — the full force of *nezirut* has not yet been reached, and therefore violations during this period do not carry *malkus*.

This revised framing is important: it is not that the *nezirut* does not exist. It is that the *nezirut* that began in *tumah* has not yet attained full legal force, and therefore the question is whether all of its prohibitions come with the consequence of *malkus*.

## Full Effect While Tamei

### Yochanan Challenge

*Rabbi Yochanan* challenges *Reish Lakish* from the *mishna* itself. The *mishna* states that someone who accepts *nezirut* in a cemetery — even if they remained there for thirty days — does not begin counting their days and does not bring a *korban tumah*. *Rabbi Yochanan* reads this as listing only a single difference: the *korban*. If the *nezirut* had another significant distinction — namely, that violations carry no *malkus* — the *mishna* should have mentioned it. The silence of the *mishna* about *malkus* implies that there is no difference in that domain; the person is a full *nazir* in every sense except for the *korban* and the timing of the count.

### Reish Lakish Defense

*Reish Lakish* responds: *b'din hu d'litni eino lokei* — you are correct that it should have said there is no *malkus*. But the *mishna*'s focus is entirely elsewhere. The *mishna* is structured around the *sof*, the later case, which discusses what happens if the person left the cemetery, became *tahor*, and then re-entered. In that case — *yatza v'nichnas oleh lo min haminyan u'meivi korban tumah* — they do bring a *korban tumah*. This is the ruling the *mishna* is building toward, against the position of *Rabbi Eliezer*, who requires the *tumah* to occur on the second day. Since the *mishna*'s entire interest is in the *korban* question, it mentions nothing about *malkus*.

This defense, while not without force, is strained. The gap the *mishna* leaves unmentioned — namely that a person who is fully prohibited from wine, shaving, and *tumah* would receive no *malkus* for any of these — is no small omission. If that were truly the *halacha*, one would expect it to be taught.

The discussion then turns to *Tosafot*'s analysis of a phrase that appears in the *baraita* on 17a: *v'tiglachas zeh v'zeh shavin* — "and for shaving, they are the same." *Tosafot* offers two interpretations. The first, cited in the name of *yesh mefarshim*, reads this as meaning that both persons — the one who started *tamei* and the one who began *tahor* and became *tamei* — would receive *malkus* for shaving. On this reading, *Reish Lakish*'s limitation of "no *malkus*" applies only to the specific act of becoming *tamei*, since that is the particular aspect compromised by the way the *nezirut* began. For all other prohibitions — wine, shaving — *malkus* would apply even to the *nazir tamei* who started that way.

*Tosafot* rejects this interpretation: *ve-lo nehira* — it does not seem correct. If the *gemara* meant that *Reish Lakish* was distinguishing only *tumah* from the other prohibitions, it should have said "for shaving *and* wine, they are the same." Since it mentions only shaving, a different reading must be intended. *Tosafot* therefore concludes that the phrase about shaving refers not to *malkus* for the prohibition of shaving, but to the *mitzvah* of *tiglachat* — the shaving done to restart the *nezirut*. The *gemara* is saying that both persons, whether they started *tamei* or became *tamei*, must shave to restart their counting. But when it comes to *malkus* for transgressions, *Reish Lakish* would say there is a difference, and that difference might extend to all prohibitions, not just *tumah*.

On *Reish Lakish*'s view, therefore, even wine and shaving would not carry *malkus* during the period the *nezirut* has not fully taken force. The *nezirut* is not *muttar* — it is forbidden — but it is in the category of *patur aval assur*: prohibited but not punishable with *malkus*. The *gemara* is quite clear on this: even under *Reish Lakish*'s reading, the acts are transgressions; they simply do not carry corporeal punishment until the *nezirut* attains full force.

### Baraita Proof

A *baraita* then resolves the question definitively against *Reish Lakish*: *mi she-haya tamei ve-nazar, assur legaleiach velishtos yayin, ve-im gilach ve-shata yayin ve-nitma, harei zeh sofeig arba'im* — a person who was *tamei* and accepted *nezirut* is forbidden to shave, drink wine, or become *tamei*, and if they did any of these, they receive forty *malkus*. This is a direct, explicit statement that the full *nezirut* applies with full *malkus* even when it was accepted in a state of *tumah*.

*Tiyufta* — a complete refutation. Though the *gemara* had previously attempted a weak answer to earlier challenges, *Tosafot* notes that the same answer cannot rescue *Reish Lakish* here. The simple meaning of this *baraita* stands as a conclusive proof that the *nezirut* takes full effect from the moment of acceptance, even in *tumah*.

### Full Nezirut Applies

The *gemara* thus concludes: accepting *nezirut* while *tamei* creates a full *nezirut* immediately, with all its prohibitions and consequences, including *malkus*. The *only* difference between such a person and a *nazir tahor* who became *tamei* is structural: the former begins counting on day seven, the latter on day eight, and the *korban tumah* applies only to the latter. Everything else — the prohibitions, the *malkus*, the full range of *nezirut* obligations — is identical.

## Tarrying Question

### Unintentional Tumah Fram…

With the *nezirut* question settled, the *gemara* turns to a new and subtle inquiry raised by *Rava*: when a *nazir* is in a cemetery, is *shehiya* — continued tarrying — necessary in order to become liable for *malkus*, or does liability attach immediately?

The question requires some setup. The general framework involves situations where a person incurs *tumah* unintentionally, through no fault of their own. In such cases, no *malkus* attaches to the initial act since it was not willful. The question is whether the *act of remaining* — after becoming aware of one's situation — can serve as the operative transgression that generates liability.

### Beit Hamikdash Model

The model *Rava* has in mind comes from the laws of *tumah* in the *Beis Hamikdash*. It is forbidden for a *tamei* person to be present in the *Azara*. If someone was in the *Beis Hamikdash* and accidentally became *tamei* — for example, an animal was slaughtered improperly and became a *neveila* and the person touched it inadvertently — no *malkus* attaches to the initial act of becoming *tamei*, since it was entirely unintentional. But if someone then warns this person that they are *tamei* and cannot remain, and the person stays anyway, the *act of staying* is treated as an active, willful transgression equivalent to entering the *Beis Hamikdash* in a state of *tumah*. The tarrying becomes the operative act.

*Rava*'s question is whether this same principle applies to a *nazir* in a cemetery. The case he envisions: someone in a cemetery accepts *nezirut*, or is in a cemetery and becomes a *nazir* — and the question is whether *shehiya* can serve as the qualifying act of transgression.

### With Warning Given

The *gemara* immediately clarifies that if someone warned the person — "do not accept *nezirut* here, you are in a cemetery" — and the person did so anyway, *shehiya* is entirely irrelevant. The willful act of accepting *nezirut* in the cemetery, done deliberately after receiving a warning, is itself the transgression. There is no need for any subsequent tarrying to create liability. Just as a *nazir* who deliberately enters a cemetery incurs *malkus* through the act of entering (not through staying), here the act of accepting *nezirut* while warned is the operative act.

### Ones Case

The genuine case where the question of *shehiya* arises is when the initial situation was *ones* — entirely beyond the person's control. The *gemara* gives a striking example: a person being transported in a sealed container — *shidah, teivah, u'migdal*, a large enclosed box — through a cemetery. Since the container is large enough not to become *tamei* itself and is sealed above and below, the person inside is protected from *tumat meis* by its *ohel*-like enclosure. But then a companion comes along and removes the covering — *para me'alav maziva* — exposing the person to *tumah* against their will. This is the paradigmatic *ones*: the person became *tamei* in the cemetery through no fault of their own.

Now someone warns them: "You are *tamei* and you are a *nazir* — you must leave." Do they incur *malkus* by remaining? Is the act of staying the operative transgression, as it would be in the *Beis Hamikdash*?

The *gemara* frames this as a straight question: *ki gamrinan shehiya b'veis hamikdash, aval a'barai lo* — perhaps the principle of *shehiya* as the operative act was taught specifically for the *Beis Hamikdash*, and does not apply outside it, as in the case of a *nazir* in a cemetery. Or perhaps — *o dilma lo shna* — it makes no difference, and *shehiya* can constitute the transgression anywhere. The *gemara* concludes: *teiku*. The question remains undecided.

### Sacred Space vs Sacred P…

The unresolved status of this question points to something conceptually significant, and the *gemara*'s hesitation is not merely technical. There is a profound structural difference between the two cases.

In the *Beis Hamikdash*, the problem with *tumah* is that a *tamei* person is defiling a sacred *space*. The *Beis Hamikdash* retains its *kedushah* regardless — its sanctity is not diminished by the presence of *tumah*, it simply does not belong there. The continued presence of a *tamei* person in that space is an ongoing, active violation of its *kedushah*. Each moment of tarrying is another moment of maintaining *tumah* in a place that cannot tolerate it. The *shehiya* is therefore a natural extension of the initial violation, and treating it as the operative act makes intuitive sense.

With a *nazir* in a cemetery, however, the dynamic is inverted. The *nazir* is the *kadosh* — the sacred person. The cemetery is not a place whose *kedushah* is being violated; it is a profane space that the *nazir*, as a sanctified person, should not be in. But once the *nazir* is *tamei*, their own *kedushah* has already been compromised. The *nazir tamei* is not the same as the *Beis Hamikdash* with *tumah* inside it — the *Beis Hamikdash* does not itself become *tamei*, whereas the *nazir* has already become *tamei*. One might therefore argue that remaining in the cemetery does not continue to violate anything at an escalating rate, since the *kedushah* of the *nazir* has already been compromised. It is less clear that staying, rather than leaving, constitutes a fresh or ongoing act of transgression.

This distinction — between a sacred *space* being violated by an impure *object*, and a sacred *person* whose sanctity has been compromised in a profane space — is what the *gemara* leaves as *teiku*. It is a genuinely difficult conceptual question.

## Shaving Requirement

### Shaving Package Question

*Rav Ashi* raises yet another question about the *nazir* who began in a state of *tumah*: *teun giluach o lav* — is such a person required to shave on day seven (when they become *tahor* and begin counting) or not?

The question requires some background. Normally, when a *nazir tahor* becomes *tamei* and must restart their *nezirut*, the restart involves two things: bringing a *korban* and shaving. Both are part of the ritual by which the *nazir* transitions from the *tumah* state back to the *taharah* state and begins counting again. We have already established that a *nazir* who began as *tamei* does not bring a *korban*. But does that mean the *giluach* — the shaving — also does not apply? Or is the shaving independently required to restart the *nezirut*, even without a *korban*?

### Mishnah Evidence

The first attempt to answer comes from our *mishna* itself: *korban tumah hu d'lo maity* — the *mishna* says only that such a person does not bring a *korban tumah*. It does not explicitly say there is no shaving. One might infer from the *mishna*'s silence on shaving that shaving is still required. Perhaps the *mishna* only addressed the *korban* because that was the *halacha* under discussion, and the shaving — which is also part of restarting — still applies.

### Baraita Day Seven vs Eig…

The *baraita* returns: *ein bein tamei nazir l'nazir tahor shenitma* — there is no difference between a person who was *tamei* and accepted *nezirut* and a person who was *tahor* and became *tamei*, except that the *tamei* person starts counting on day seven while the *tahor* person starts on day eight. The *baraita* enumerates this as the *only* difference.

*Rav Ashi* presses: if there is also a difference regarding shaving — one must shave and the other need not — why does the *baraita* not mention it? The *baraita* seems to say the *only* difference is day seven versus day eight.

### Gemara Package Response

The *gemara* responds: *ma ta'am eino meivi korban tumah? Mishum dela ba'ei giluach* — why does the person not bring a *korban tumah*? Because they do not need to shave. The reasoning works both ways and establishes that the two — *korban* and *giluach* — are a single package. If you are not bringing the *korban*, you are not doing the associated shaving. The *baraita*'s statement that the *only* difference is the timing (day seven versus day eight) implicitly conveys that this person does not have the whole ritual of day eight: no *korban* and no shaving. They simply become *tahor* on day seven and begin counting. The *mishna*'s mention of the *korban* alone is sufficient to establish both, since *giluach* is bundled into the *korban* ritual.

## Metzora vs Nazir

### Tumat Meit Destroys

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* relating to a *nazir* who becomes a *metzora* — a person with *tzaraat*. The *tannaitic* rule is as follows: only *tumat meis* (corpse-*tumah*) destroys the earlier days a *nazir* has already counted. If someone was ten days into their *nezirut* and became *tamei meis*, those ten days are lost and they must begin again from zero. But if that same *nazir* became a *metzora*, those ten days are *not* lost. Being a *metzora* puts the *nezirut* on hiatus — the days of *metzora* status do not count toward the thirty — but the earlier days already banked remain intact.

The rationale is straightforward: the prohibition that destroys earlier days is specifically *tumat meis*. Since the *nazir* did not contract *tumat meis*, the earlier days survive. The *nazir* who becomes a *metzora* finishes the *metzora* process and then counts however many remaining days are needed to complete thirty.

One complicating factor is worth noting: becoming *tahor* from *tzaraat* requires shaving. If the *nazir-metzora* must shave to exit *metzora* status, the hair that was growing is lost, raising the question of whether the *nazir* can finish their thirty days without thirty days of continuous hair growth. *Tosafot* raises this but the *gemara* does not resolve it here.

### Shaving Analogy

The *baraita* attempts to establish this *halacha* through a *kal va-chomer* argument. First it asks: how do I know that the days of being a *metzora muchlat* (sealed off) do not count toward a *nazir*'s thirty days? Taking it for granted that those days do not *destroy* the earlier period (since there was no *tumat meis*), perhaps they *do* count, since the *metzora* period is not inherently antithetical to *nezirut*.

The argument from analogy proceeds: just as when a *nazir* is *tamei*, the *tumah* ends by shaving and bringing a *korban* — *megalei'ach u'meivi korban* — and those days do not count; so too when the *nazir* is a *metzora*, the *metzora* period also ends with shaving and bringing a *korban* — *af yimei chaluto megalei'ach u'meivi korban* — and therefore those days should similarly not count.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this analogy. *Im amarta b'imei tumah* — the case of *tumat meis* is fundamentally different. When a *nazir* becomes *tamei meis*, those days destroy the earlier period: *she'ken mevatel bahen es hakodmin*. The *tumah* was so antithetical to *nezirut* that it wiped out everything preceding it. Of course those days do not count. But *tomar bimei chaluto* — can you say the same of the *metzora* period? *She'eino mevatel es hakedomim* — being a *metzora* does not destroy the earlier days. Since the earlier days survive, the argument runs the other direction: the *metzora* period is not so antithetical to *nezirut*, and therefore one might say the days *can* count.

### Rav Pappa Interpretation

The *baraita* then introduces a new comparison involving the *nazir* in the cemetery — the case of *nazir bakever* — and the phrase *she'searo raui letiglachas*: "his hair is fit for shaving." *Rav Pappa* interprets this as referring to *tiglachat tumah* — the shaving done by a *nazir tamei* to restart their *nezirut*.

On this reading, the *baraita* is establishing the following comparison: the *nazir* in the cemetery, even though he began as *tamei* and brings no *korban*, is still required to shave his hair (on this reading, to restart his *nezirut*). He is therefore still operating within the framework of *nezirut* law in a meaningful sense. His hair is "fit for shaving" because the *nezirut* laws still actively govern him. A *metzora nazir*, by contrast, has no shaving of the hair specifically in the context of *nezirut*; the shaving that occurs is about exiting *metzora* status, not about *nezirut*. Since the *nazir* in the cemetery remains more squarely within the *nezirut* mode — his days still do not count — a *metzora nazir*, who is less connected to active *nezirut* governance, should certainly have his days not count either.

### Hair Safety Interpretati…

The second interpretation rejects this reading: *lo, tiglachat taharah* — no, the phrase refers not to shaving at the *beginning* of the restart, but to shaving at the *end* of the *nezirut* period.

On this reading, the *baraita* is not assuming that the *nazir bakever* shaves to restart. Rather: the *nazir* in the cemetery does *not* shave to restart (as the *gemara* concluded earlier — the shaving is part of the *korban* package and this person does not shave). His hair, therefore, remains intact as he begins counting on day seven. That hair is "fit for shaving" in the sense that it will eventually be shaved at the *end* of the thirty-day *nezirut* — it is preserved for the final *tiglachat* of *tahara*. The hair is safe; his *nezirut* is fully on track.

The *metzora nazir*, by contrast, will have to shave his hair midway through — to exit *metzora* status — and that hair will not be present at the conclusion of the *nezirut*. His hair is *not* safe for the ultimate *tiglachat*. This makes his *metzora* period a more significant contradiction to the *nezirut* — the hair, which is central to the identity and process of *nezirut*, will be lost before the period concludes. If the *nazir* whose hair remains intact and whose *nezirut* is comparatively less disrupted still cannot count those days, the *metzora nazir* whose hair will be lost before the end of the period certainly cannot count those days.

The *gemara* supports this second interpretation: *hachi nami mistabra* — this is the more sensible reading — *de-i salka daitach tiglachat tumah*: if the *baraita* were talking about *tiglachat tumah*, how could it contrast the *nazir tamei* (who shaves for *tumah*) with the *metzora* (who does not shave for *tumah*), since a *metzora* also shaves as part of the *tumah*-*taharah* process? *Yemei chaluto mi lo ba'ei tiglachat* — the days of being a *metzora muchlat* also require shaving. So the contrast cannot be about which type of *tumah* requires shaving, since both do. Therefore: *lo tiglachat di-nezirut katani* — the *baraita* is comparing the shaving done as part of *nezirut* itself, not the general shaving associated with *tumah* processes.

### Baraita Ambiguity

The *gemara* reaches the end of this analysis in a state of unresolved ambiguity. The *baraita* attempted to prove that the days of *metzora* do not count toward a *nazir*'s thirty days, and the comparison it draws involves the shaving of the *nazir bakever*. But as the two interpretations show, the *baraita* can be read either as assuming that the *nazir bakever* does shave to restart (the *Rav Pappa* reading), or as assuming that he does not shave and his hair remains safe for the final *tiglachat* (the second interpretation).

This means the original question of *Rav Ashi* — does a *nazir* who began as *tamei* require shaving on day seven to restart his *nezirut*? — remains unanswered by this *baraita*. The *baraita* is genuinely ambiguous on the point, and the *halacha* is not yet determined.
