# Zevachim 119 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Corrections & Intro

### Shaul Affiliation

Before proceeding into the body of the *daf*, two corrections from the previous lecture are in order. In the course of discussing the geography of the tribal territories — Binyamin, Yehuda, and Ephraim, and the way the *Beis Hamikdash* and Shilo were distributed among the children of Yosef — it may have been stated that Shaul was from Ephraim. That is incorrect: Shaul was from Binyamin. The broader observation remains interesting, namely how the *malchut* and the *Mikdash* were navigated among the children of Yosef and Yehuda — with Reuven cut out entirely, though he at least receives *Ever Hayardein*.

### Nob & Givon Status

The second correction concerns the status of Nob and Givon. We have been emphasizing that the reason Nob and Givon do not have the status of a *Mikdash* is because they lack *makom hashechina* — the locus of God's concentrated presence — and therefore there is no singular place of *avodah*. Since God's presence is not more concentrated there than anywhere else, there is a *heter bamot*, and one may offer sacrifices outside the central *bamah*. This is the logic that undergirds the *heter bamot* during the Nob and Givon period.

A student raised the question about the *aron* at Nob and Givon. When Shilo was destroyed, the *aron* was taken captive by the Philistines. After it was returned, it resided in Kiryat Yearim, and eventually David moved it — but the *aron* was never reunited with the central *mizbeach*. The central altar and the *aron* were simply not in the same place during the Nob and Givon period, and that is precisely the point.

### Gilgal Transition

The situation at Gilgal during the first fourteen years of the conquest is somewhat different. Gilgal did have the *aron*, yet it also had a *heter bamot*. The reason there is distinct: the *Bnei Yisrael* had just entered the land, had not yet conquered and settled it, and everything remained in a state of transition. One might note a certain irony: in the *midbar*, the nation was wandering with no fixed destination, yet they maintained a central *Mishkan* with an *issur bamot*. Here, at the threshold of settlement, the very goal was to establish permanence — and yet the situation was still too unsettled, too provisional, to anchor a singular place of *avodah*. It is like having just moved into a new home with all the furniture still unplaced. So while the *heter bamot* at Gilgal and the *heter bamot* at Nob and Givon are both real, they rest on different conceptual foundations.

## Heter Bamot Source

### Ki Lo Basem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ לִירוּשָׁלַיִם [וְכוּ׳]. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי לֹא בָּאתֶם עַד עָתָּה אֶל הַמְּנוּחָה וְאֶל הַנַּחֲלָה״;
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that when Shiloh was destroyed and they arrived at Nov and Gibeon, private altars were permitted and offerings of lesser sanctity could be eaten in any city in Eretz Yisrael. The Gemara asks: From where are these matters derived? they are derived as the Sages taught: The Jewish people were told that when they enter Eretz Yisrael they would be permitted to sacrifice on private altars, **"for you have not as yet come to the rest and to the inheritance"** (Deuteronomy 12:9), during which time those altars would be prohibited.

Four lines into the *daf*, the *Gemara* asks: *minan hani mili* — from where do we derive the *heter bamot* during the interim periods? The source is the *pasuk* in Devarim: *ki lo basem ad ata el hamenucha v'el hanachala* — "for you have not yet come to the resting place and to the inheritance." During the period of entering the land, it is *ish hayashar b'einav*, each person acts as seems right in his own eyes, and one may offer on the *bamot*. The logic is that so long as the nation has not arrived at the *menucha* and the *nachala*, the state of transition renders *bamot* permissible.

### Menucha/Nachala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶל הַמְּנוּחָה״ – זוֹ שִׁילֹה, ״נַחֲלָה״ – זוֹ יְרוּשָׁלַיִם. לָמָּה חִלְּקָן? כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן הֶיתֵּר בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets the verse: **"To the rest"; this** is a reference to **Shiloh.** "The **inheritance"; this** is a reference to **Jerusalem.** One may ask: **Why** does the verse **divide them** into two terms, i.e., "rest" and "inheritance"? It is **in order to give permission** to sacrifice on private altars during the period **between this** one **and that** one.

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *ki lo basem ad ata el hamenucha v'el hanachala — el hamenucha zo Shilo, nachala zu Yerushalayim*. The *menucha* is Shilo and the *nachala* is Yerushalayim. But how do we derive from this that *bamot* were permitted in the interim between Shilo and Yerushalayim? *Lama chilkan* — why were they divided? The answer: *kedai litten heter bein zeh lazeh* — to signal that there is a *heter* between the two. The *nachala* has a connotation of perpetuity: once the *Bnei Yisrael* inherit Yerushalayim, it remains their permanent inheritance; even when the *Beis Hamikdash* is destroyed, the sanctity does not migrate elsewhere.

### Et Repetition Gap

There is something noteworthy in the formulation of the *pasuk*. The phrase is *el hamenucha v'el hanachala* — with the prefix *el* repeated before each term. Had the *pasuk* said simply *el hamenucha v'hanachala*, we might read the two as a single unified concept. But the repeated *el* signals two distinct, separate referents with a space between them. This extra *vav-el* implies a gap — the very gap occupied by Nob and Givon — during which *bamot* become permissible once again. Rashi, by contrast, understands *lama chilkan* as asking why the *pasuk* needed to mention Yerushalayim at all: if the *issur bamot* had commenced at Shilo and continued from that point forward in Jewish history, the *pasuk* could simply have said *ki lo basem el hamenucha*. The additional mention of Yerushalayim tells us that there was a *heter* between the two. Both readings converge on the same conclusion, though the precise mechanism differs.

## Ma'aser Sheni

### Reish Lakish Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי נָמֵי לִיתְנֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַעֲשֵׂר – ״שָׁם״–״שָׁם״ מֵאָרוֹן קָא יָלְפִי; כֵּיוָן דְּאָרוֹן לָא הֲוָה, מַעֲשֵׂר נָמֵי לָא הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: Let** the *tanna* of the mishna **teach** the *halakha* with regard to **second tithe as well.** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** Second **tithe is derived from** what was written with regard to **the Ark,** by means of the verbal analogy between **"there"** and **"there."** With regard to second tithe, the verse states: "And there you shall eat before the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 12:7), while with regard to the Ark, the verse states: "There you shall place the Ark of the Testimony" (Exodus 40:3). Due to the analogy between second tithe and the Ark, one can infer that **since there was no Ark** in Nov and Gibeon, as at that time it was in Kiriath Jearim, **there was also no** second **tithe** eaten there.

The *Mishna* teaches that when the *Bnei Yisrael* came to Nob and Givon, *kodshim kalim* were eaten *bechol arei Yisrael* — throughout all the cities of Israel. Since one could bring *shelamim* anywhere during the *heter bamot* period, *kodshim kalim* were accordingly eaten wherever they were offered. *Kodshei kodashim*, by contrast — such as a *chatas* — were eaten only within the defined courtyard-like space of the central *bamah*, assuming they were brought there at all.

Reish Lakish posed a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan: *ma'aser sheni nami lisni* — why does the *Mishna* not mention *ma'aser sheni*? The significance of this question depends on the *girsa* of the *Mishna*. Our *girsa* does not mention *ma'aser sheni* at all, which implies that *ma'aser sheni* was simply not consumed during the Nob and Givon period — that there was no relevant context for it. Rashi's *girsa*, however, reads that *kodshim kalim* and *ma'aser sheni* were eaten everywhere, which raises the question from the other direction: why are we not instead bringing *ma'aser sheni* specifically to Nob and Givon?

The core issue is that the concept of *ma'aser sheni* is bound up with eating *lifnei Hashem*. If there is no *makom hashechina* — no place of concentrated divine presence — during the Nob and Givon period, then there is no place to which *ma'aser sheni* can be brought. Two consequences are possible: either one does not eat it at all, or, since there is no uniquely designated place, one may eat it anywhere. This symmetry parallels the logic for *korbanot*: the permissibility of *korbanot* everywhere during *heter bamot* follows from the principle *bechol makom asher azkir et shemi* — wherever I cause My name to be mentioned — since no central location has greater claim than any other. Whether the same logic applies to *ma'aser sheni* — yielding universal permissibility rather than total suspension — is precisely what is under discussion.

### Aron Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֵׂר – ״שָׁם״–״שָׁם״ מֵאָרוֹן קָא יָלְפִי; כֵּיוָן דְּאָרוֹן לָא הֲוָה, מַעֲשֵׂר נָמֵי לָא הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** Second **tithe is derived from** what was written with regard to **the Ark,** by means of the verbal analogy between **"there"** and **"there."** ...Due to the analogy between second tithe and the Ark, one can infer that **since there was no Ark** in Nov and Gibeon, as at that time it was in Kiriath Jearim, **there was also no** second **tithe** eaten there.

The *Gemara* offers an initial answer: *ma'aser sham sham me-aron ka-yalfi* — the word *sham* ("there") appears in connection with *ma'aser sheni*, and the same word appears in connection with the *aron*. By *ma'aser* it says *ve-achaltem sham*, and by the *aron* it says *ve-nasata sham et ha-aron*. The *gezerah shavah* teaches that eating *ma'aser sheni* requires the same *lifnei Hashem* presence associated with the *aron*. Since the *aron* was not in Nob and Givon, *ma'aser sheni* has no operative context there. The *Gemara* then states: *keivan de-aron lo hava, ma'aser nami lo hava* — since the *aron* was absent, *ma'aser sheni* was likewise inapplicable.

### Pesach Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, פֶּסַח וְקָדָשִׁים נָמֵי – דְּ״שָׁם״–״שָׁם״ מֵאָרוֹן יָלְפִי; דְּכֵיוָן דְּאָרוֹן לָא הֲוָה, אִינְהוּ נָמֵי לָא הֲווֹ!
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish asked: **If** it is **so** that this is the source, then with regard to the **Paschal offering and** other **sacrificial** animals, which everyone agrees were consumed in Nov and Gibeon, it can **also** be said **that they are derived from the Ark** by means of the verbal analogy between **"there"** and **"there,"** and **since the Ark was not** in Nov and Gibeon **they too were not** there.

The *Gemara* immediately presses further: *ei hachi, Pesach ve-kodashim nami* — if that is so, then by the same logic, the *Pesach* offering and all *korbanot* should also be excluded. After all, the *pasuk* says *ve-zavachta shelamim ve-achalta sham* — "you shall slaughter *shelamim* and eat there" — and the word *sham* appears there as well, linking all *korbanot* to the same *aron*-presence requirement. The *Gemara* presses to its conclusion: *de-keivan de-aron lo hava, inhu nami lo hava* — since the *aron* was absent, they too should be inapplicable.

One might ask: why should *ma'aser sheni* be more bound to *lifnei Hashem* than *korbanot* are? Conceptually the distinction is compelling — *korbanot* are inherently offerings wherever offered, but *ma'aser sheni* involves a pilgrimage, a carrying-up to a place of divine presence, analogous to *olat re'iah*, which similarly would not apply to Nob and Givon. Nevertheless, once one invokes the *sham-sham* derivation, the logic extends to everything — and the *Gemara* acknowledges as much.

### R. Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דַּאֲמַר לָךְ הָא, מַנִּי – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אַף צִבּוּר לֹא הִקְרִיבוּ אֶלָּא פֶּסַח וְחוֹבוֹת הַקָּבוּעַ לָהֶן זְמַן, אֲבָל חוֹבוֹת שֶׁאֵין קָבוּעַ לָהֶם זְמַן – הָכָא וְהָכָא לָא קְרוּב. מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה – חוֹבוֹת שֶׁאֵין קָבוּעַ לָהֶן זְמַן הוּא, וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן לְמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** With regard to the one **who said this to you,** i.e., the *tanna* of the mishna, from which you inferred that second tithe was not eaten in Nov and Gibeon, in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** statement of his? **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says: Even** the **public sacrificed** upon a great public altar, e.g., in Nov and Gibeon, **only Paschal offerings and compulsory public offerings that have a set time. But compulsory** offerings **that do not have a set time were not sacrificed here or there,** i.e., on a great public altar or on a small private altar. This includes **an animal tithe** offering, which **is a compulsory offering that has no set time, and grain tithe is juxtaposed to animal tithe.** Since animal tithe offerings were not brought at that time, second tithe was not eaten there either.

The *Gemara* resolves the challenge by identifying whose position this *Mishna* represents: *da-amar lach ha-mani Rabbi Shimon hi* — the *Mishna* follows Rabbi Shimon's approach. Rabbi Shimon holds that what was practiced in Nob and Givon was highly restricted: *af tzibbur lo hikrivu ela Pesach ve-chovot she-kavu'a la-hem zman* — even the community brought nothing there except the *Pesach* and communal obligations that are fixed to a specific time.

Tosafot is careful to note that the exclusion of *ma'aser sheni* is not so much because of the specific logic of Rabbi Shimon regarding communal fixed obligations, but rather because of the view of Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* who dispute Rabbi Yehuda. Their position is that even when an individual comes to the *bamah gedolah*, he brings only *nedavot* — voluntary offerings — and not obligatory ones. A private *bamah* is for an individual like his own rooftop. On this view, *ma'aser behemah*, which is an individual obligation but not a communal fixed obligation, would not be brought to the *bamah gedolah*. And since *ma'aser dagan* is linked to *ma'aser behemah* by *hekesh* — *ve-itkish ma'aser dagan le-ma'aser behemah* — *ma'aser sheni* is excluded as well.

The reason, then, that *ma'aser sheni* is absent from Nob and Givon has nothing to do with the *lifnei Hashem* framework introduced via the *aron* — that avenue was abandoned by the *Gemara* because it would have swept away all *korbanot* as well. Instead, the exclusion flows from a systemic principle: during the period of *bamot*, the framework of individual obligation simply does not operate. Just as there is no *chatas* for an individual at that *bamah*, no *ma'aser sheni*.

The open question — what the individual actually does with *ma'aser sheni* he cannot bring — remains. By a *korban* without a valid venue, the *korban* simply is not brought. But *ma'aser sheni* is not a *korban*; it is produce. Does it therefore spoil unused, or may it be consumed in any location since there is no operative central site? This is the long discussion in Tosafot engaging Rashi's position, and it remains unresolved.

### R. Yehuda Implications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דִּלְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קְרוּב? אִין; דְּהָאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָה: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וּמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה נֶאֱכָלִין בְּנוֹב וְגִבְעוֹן לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וְהָא בָּעֵי בִּירָה! וְלָאו תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: שָׁלֹשׁ בִּירוֹת הֵן – שִׁילֹה, וְנוֹב וְגִבְעוֹן, וּבֵית עוֹלָמִים? הוּא תָּנֵי לַהּ וְהוּא אָמַר לַהּ: לַאֲכִילַת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Based on this, may it be concluded **by inference that according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** that compulsory offerings for which there is no set time were sacrificed upon a great public altar, second tithe was eaten there and animal tithe offerings were **sacrificed?** The Gemara responds: **Yes, as Rav Adda bar Mattana says: Second tithe and animal tithe** offerings **were consumed in Nov and Gibeon according to the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.** The Gemara challenges: **But** these **require** the **Temple,** not a tent, to which people will come to eat tithes. The Gemara responds: **But didn't Rav Yosef teach** a *baraita* that states: **There** were **three temples: Shiloh, Nov and Gibeon, and the Eternal House. He teaches it and he says it:** This is referring **to consuming second tithe, and** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *Gemara* then notes: *michlal de-Rabbi Yehuda kareiv* — by implication, according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that an individual may bring obligatory *korbanot* at the *bamah gedolah*, one would indeed eat *ma'aser sheni* at Nob and Givon. This is stated explicitly: Rabba bar bar Chana teaches that *ma'aser sheni u-ma'aser behemah* were eaten at Nob and Givon according to Rabbi Yehuda. The *bechor* is implicit in this as well, and Tosafot addresses it directly.

## Heter Bamot Duratio (III)

### Birah Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בָּעֵי בִּירָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** these **require** the **Temple,** not a tent, to which people will come to eat tithes.

The *Gemara* then raises a further challenge against Rabbi Yehuda's position: *ve-ha ba'ya birah* — does it not require a *birah*? The term *birah* functions as a way of expressing the concept of *lifnei Hashem* — a site worthy of bearing God's presence before which *ma'aser sheni* may be consumed. Rashi explains: *de-chtiv lifnei Hashem Elokeichem* — the *pasuk* says "before the Lord your God," which implies that *ma'aser sheni* requires a place of concentrated divine presence, not merely any *bamah*.

The *Gemara* resolves this by citing a teaching of Rav Yosef: there are three places that qualify as *birah* — Shilo, Nob and Givon (counted together as one), and *beit olamim*, the permanent *Beis Hamikdash*. Rav Yosef both taught and explained this *baraita*: the designation of *birah* is for the purpose of eating *ma'aser sheni*, and this is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.

This is a striking conclusion. Throughout the discussion, the working premise has been that Nob and Givon lack *makom hashechina* — that the absence of the *aron* and the presence of a *heter bamot* combine to deprive them of any concentrated divine presence. Yet here, at least for the purpose of *ma'aser sheni*, Nob and Givon are designated as a *birah* and deemed to qualify as *lifnei Hashem*. The tension is real: in a matter of moments, the *Gemara* will enumerate a lengthy list of rituals — *semichah*, *tenufah*, *hagashah*, blood application, and more — that do not apply in Nob and Givon because the location is not considered *lifnei Hashem*. And yet for *ma'aser sheni* it counts. The *Gemara* offers no explicit resolution of this asymmetry; it simply states it as fact.

One clarification follows from this analysis: the *birah* designation of Nob and Givon works for Rabbi Yehuda because he holds that obligations may be brought to the *bamah gedolah*. Those who disagree with him regarding *chovot* would not necessarily contest the *birah* status of Nob and Givon itself — they would simply say that the prohibition on individual obligatory *korbanot* prevents bringing *ma'aser sheni* regardless.

## Pasuk Interpretation (II)

### R. Yehuda Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי לֹא בָּאתֶם עַד עָתָּה אֶל הַמְּנוּחָה וְאֶל הַנַּחֲלָה״ – ״מְנוּחָה״ זוֹ שִׁילֹה, ״נַחֲלָה״ זוֹ יְרוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse that discusses the permissibility of private altars states: **"For you have not as yet come to the rest and to the inheritance"** (Deuteronomy 12:9). The Gemara interprets: With regard to **"rest," this** is a reference to **Shiloh,** and with regard to **"inheritance," this** is a reference to **Jerusalem.**

Returning to the *pasuk ki lo basem ad ata el hamenucha v'el hanachala*, the *Gemara* now surveys four distinct ways in which *tannaim* understand the referents of *menucha* and *nachala*. The first, already introduced above, is that of Rabbi Yehuda: *menucha zo Shilo, nachala zu Yerushalayim* — the *menucha* is Shilo and the *nachala* is Yerushalayim. This reading is sequential and tracks the chronology. The verse mentions the two resting places in order, and the repeated *el* signals the gap between them during which *bamot* are permissible.

### R. Shimon Reverse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״מְנוּחָה״ זוֹ יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, ״נַחֲלָה״ זוֹ שִׁילֹה. וְאוֹמֵר: ״זֹאת מְנוּחָתִי עֲדֵי עַד פֹּה אֵשֵׁב כִּי אִוִּיתִיהָ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״כִּי בָחַר ה׳ בְּצִיּוֹן אִוָּהּ לְמוֹשָׁב לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **Rabbi Shimon says:** With regard to **"rest," this** is a reference to **Jerusalem,** and with regard to **"inheritance," this** is a reference to **Shiloh. And** this is evident from the verse that **says: "This is My resting place forever; here will I dwell, for I have desired it"** (Psalms 132:14). **And it states** in the previous verse: **"For the Lord has chosen Zion; He has desired it for His habitation"** (Psalms 132:13), which indicates that the verses are referring to Jerusalem.

Rabbi Shimon reverses the identification: *menucha zo Yerushalayim, nachala zo Shilo*. This reversal is not chronological — Shilo preceded Yerushalayim — and the *Gemara* accordingly asks why the *pasuk* does not simply say *el hanachala v'el hamenucha*, which would at least match the inverted order. The *Gemara* answers: *hachi ka'amar* — this is what the verse is saying. *Lo mibei menucha d'lo matisu* — not only have you not yet reached the ultimate resting place, namely Yerushalayim; *ella afilu nachala nami lo basem* — you have not even reached the temporary resting place of Shilo. It is a *kal va-chomer* formulation embedded in the *pasuk*. The *pasuk* leads with the more exalted destination to underscore that the lesser one has not been reached either. The rationale for calling Yerushalayim *menucha* is drawn from the *pasuk* in Tehillim: *zot menuchati adei ad, po eshev ki ivisiha* — "this is My resting place forever; here I will dwell for I have desired it." The rationale for calling Shilo *nachala* is that it was the site where Yehoshua divided the land: *vayechalek lahem Yehoshua vayapel lahem goral be-Shilo al pi Hashem*.

As for the designation of *nachala* for Shilo: the *Gemara* notes that it is slightly ironic, given the principle that *Yerushalayim lo nitchalkah la-shvatim* — Yerushalayim was never apportioned among the tribes. It is indeed a national *nachala*, belonging to no single tribe individually, which is precisely why it is the permanent inheritance.

### R. Yishmael Both=Shilo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: זוֹ וְזוֹ שִׁילֹה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the words "rest" and "inheritance" in the aforementioned verse, **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that **this and that,** i.e., both terms, are a reference to **Shiloh.**

The school of Rabbi Yishmael (*tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael*) offers yet another reading: *zo v'zo Shilo* — both terms, *menucha* and *nachala*, refer to Shilo alone. The *pasuk* says nothing about Yerushalayim. On this reading, the *heter bamot* continues after Shilo's destruction, and there is no scriptural marker for the resumption of *issur bamot* when Nob and Givon functioned. Rashi is compelled to explain that this opinion would have to hold that there was a *heter bamot* even after Yerushalayim is destroyed — since Yerushalayim enjoys no greater status than Shilo on this reading, and the destruction of Shilo reinstated *heter bamot*, so too would the destruction of Yerushalayim. This is quite a radical implication.

## Heter Bamot Duratio (III)

### Shilo Alone Issue

The *Gemara* raises a pointed difficulty against the *zo v'zo Shilo* reading: in Sefer Shmuel there is clear evidence that people were offering *korbanot* on *bamot* throughout the land during the period of Nob and Givon, without any record of objection or censure. This evidence is precisely what compels Rashi to say that the *zo v'zo Shilo* position must be holding that *bamot* remained permissible even after the destruction of Shilo — otherwise there would be no way to account for the unquestioned practice attested in Shmuel. The *Gemara* does not resolve this cleanly and moves on.

## Pasuk Interpretation (II)

### R. Shimon bar Yochai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: זוֹ וְזוֹ יְרוּשָׁלַיִם. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר זוֹ וָזוֹ שִׁילֹה, אֲבָל שִׁילֹה הֲווֹ (שריא) [שַׁרְיָאן] בָּמוֹת – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקַּח מָנוֹחַ אֶת גְּדִי הָעִזִּים וְאֶת הַמִּנְחָה וַיַּעַל עַל הַצּוּר לַה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai **says: This and that** are a reference to **Jerusalem.** The Gemara comments: **Granted, according to the one,** i.e., Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai, **who says** that **this and that** are references to **Jerusalem,** and consequently private altars were forbidden only following the establishment of the Temple in Jerusalem, **but** during the period of the Tabernacle in **Shiloh** private **altars were permitted, this is as it is written: "So Manoah took the kid with the meal offering, and offered them upon the rock to the Lord"** (Judges 13:19), i.e., he sacrificed them upon a private altar and not in Shiloh.

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai states: *zo v'zo Yerushalayim* — both *menucha* and *nachala* refer to Yerushalayim alone. The *pasuk* does not speak of Shilo as a resting place. The consequence is the mirror image of the *zo v'zo Shilo* position: if the verse is pointing exclusively to Yerushalayim, then *bamot* were permissible throughout the entire 369-year period of Shilo. The *Gemara* states this explicitly — *aval Shilo havay shari b'bamot* — and cites as evidence the episode in Sefer Shoftim where Manoach slaughtered a kid and brought a *minchah* upon the rock, without being corrected. If *bamot* were forbidden during the Shilo period, how could Manoach have done this? The *Gemara* answers that for the positions that hold *bamot* to have been forbidden during Shilo, this episode constitutes a *hora'at sha'ah* — a one-time exceptional ruling. The *Gemara* adds: *tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael ke-Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai* — the school of Rabbi Yishmael follows Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's position, and the mnemonic is *mashkei gavra l'gavri* — that one scholar drew an entire school to follow his view. The *Gemara* then consolidates: *bishlama le-man d'amar menucha zo Shilo nachala zu Yerushalayim*, or even the reverse, at least the *pasuk* is mentioning two distinct locations, which accounts for the repeated *el*. But for those who say *zo v'zo Shilo* or *zo v'zo Yerushalayim*, the verse should have simply said *menucha ve-nachala* without the repeated *el* — and the *Gemara* simply states *kashya*, it remains a difficulty.

## Shchutei Chutz

### Rav Kahana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁחִיטָה, אֲבָל בְּהַעֲלָאָה – כָּרֵת נָמֵי מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana says: They taught** that **only with regard to slaughter** of these animals outside the designated area is one not liable to receive *karet*. **But with regard to offering up,** one would **also** be **liable** to receive ***karet*.**

The *Gemara* now returns to the topic of *shchutei chutz*. The *Mishna* teaches that if an animal was sanctified during the period of *heter bamot* and then slaughtered outside the proper location during the period of *issur bamot*, one is not liable for *karet*. One transgresses, but *karet* does not apply.

Rav Kahana qualifies this: *lo shanu ela bi-shchita* — this leniency applies only to *shchita*, the slaughter. But *ha'alah* — the actual offering up of the animal on the *mizbeach* — is different. Even if the animal was sanctified during *heter bamot*, if one performs *ha'alah* outside the proper place during *issur bamot*, *karet nami mechayev* — one is liable for *karet*. His reasoning is based on the *pasuk*: *va'aleihem tomar* — "tell them," which he reads as demarcating the earlier discussion of *shchita* as distinct from the subsequent discussion of *ha'alah*. The leniency derived from the timing of sanctification applies only *al hasmuchim*, the earlier section on *shchita*, and not to the laws of *ha'alah*.

### Rava 4 Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד תַּנְיָא, אַרְבָּעָה כְּלָלוֹת הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר בְּקָדָשִׁים: הִקְדִּישָׁן בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת, וְשָׁחַט וְהֶעֱלָה בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת בַּחוּץ – הֲרֵי הֵן בַּעֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, וְיֵשׁ בָּהֶן כָּרֵת. הִקְדִּישָׁן בִּשְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר הַבָּמוֹת, וְשָׁחַט וְהֶעֱלָה בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת – הֲרֵי הֵן בַּעֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, וְאֵין בָּהֶן כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And additionally, it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon would say four principles with regard to sacrificial** animals: With regard to offerings that one **consecrated during a period of prohibition of** private **altars, and slaughtered and offered up outside** their designated area **during a period of prohibition of** private **altars,** for **these** one is **in** violation of **a positive mitzva and a prohibition, and they carry** the punishment of ***karet*.** With regard to one who **consecrated them during a period of** the **permitting of** private **altars, and slaughtered and offered** them **up during a period of prohibition of** private **altars,** for **these** one is **in** violation of **a positive mitzva and a prohibition, but they do not carry** the punishment of ***karet*.**

Rava challenges this position on two grounds. First, *mi ktiv va'aleihem tomar? aleihem ktiv* — the verse actually says *aleihem*, "to them," referring to the *Bnei Yisrael*, not *va'aleihem*; the word cannot be parsed so as to create the division Rav Kahana requires. Second, Rava cites a *baraita* in which Rabbi Shimon enumerates four categories concerning the timing of sanctification and the timing of slaughter or offering:

The first category: sanctified during *issur bamot*, slaughtered and offered outside during *issur bamot* — one violates a positive commandment and a negative commandment and is liable for *karet*. The second: sanctified during *heter bamot*, slaughtered and offered outside during *issur bamot* — one violates a positive and a negative commandment, but there is no *karet*. The third: sanctified during *issur bamot*, slaughtered and offered outside during *heter bamot* — one violates an *aseh*, the obligation of *ve-havaysem shammah* (since the animal was sanctified when a central place existed and was not brought there), but no negative commandment is violated. The fourth: sanctified during *heter bamot* and slaughtered and offered outside during *heter bamot* — *patur mi-klum*, entirely exempt.

### Ha'alah No Karet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִקְדִּישָׁן בִּשְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר הַבָּמוֹת, וְשָׁחַט וְהֶעֱלָה בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת – הֲרֵי הֵן בַּעֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, וְאֵין בָּהֶן כָּרֵת. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one who **consecrated them during a period of** the **permitting of** private **altars, and slaughtered and offered** them **up during a period of prohibition of** private **altars,** for **these** one is **in** violation of **a positive mitzva and a prohibition, but they do not carry** the punishment of ***karet*.**...The *baraita* states explicitly that there is no distinction between slaughtering and offering up on a private altar: In both cases, one who consecrated the offering during a period of the permitting of private altars and slaughtered or offered it up during a period of prohibition of private altars is exempt from the punishment of *karet*. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Rav Kahana,** who distinguished between slaughter and offering up, is **a conclusive refutation.**

The second category is decisive: it states explicitly that even when the animal was sanctified during *heter bamot* and subsequently offered (*ha'alah*) during *issur bamot*, there is no *karet* — directly contradicting Rav Kahana's position. *Tiyuvta de-Rav Kahana tiyuvta* — this is a definitive refutation of Rav Kahana.

### Issur to Heter

The third category also merits attention. If the animal was sanctified during *issur bamot* — for example, during the Shilo period when a central place existed — and then Shilo was destroyed and the owner offered it in his backyard during the now-reinstated *heter bamot*, the owner violates an *aseh*. The *aseh* is derived from the verse *ve-havaysem shammah*: since the animal was sanctified at a time when a central location was available, one was obligated to bring it there. That the destruction of Shilo was not the owner's fault is irrelevant; the *aseh* was activated at the moment of sanctification. Nevertheless, since *ha'alah* was performed during a time of *heter bamot*, no negative commandment is violated. One might note the curious use of the word *patur* for the fourth category — the *Gemara* could have said *muttar* — and this may reflect an open question about the theological valence of *sha'at heter bamot*: was offering on *bamot* then a positive religious act, or merely a concession?

## Lifnei Hashem Rituals

### Semicha/Tzafona

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְמִיכָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״לִפְנֵי ה׳ וְסָמַךְ״. שְׁחִיטַת צָפוֹן – דִּכְתִיב: ״צָפוֹנָה לִפְנֵי ה׳״. תְּנוּפָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהֵנִיף הַכֹּהֵן לִפְנֵי ה׳״. הַגָּשָׁה – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִגִּישָׁהּ אֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the source of each ritual on the list: The source for the *halakha* concerning **placing hands** on the head of an offering is **as it is written:** "If his offering be a burnt offering…he shall bring it to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, that he may be accepted **before the Lord. And he shall lay** his hand upon the head of the burnt offering" (Leviticus 1:3–4). The source for the *halakha* concerning **slaughtering** offerings of the most sacred order in the **north** is **as it is written:** "And he shall slaughter it on the side of the altar **northward before the Lord"** (Leviticus 1:11). The source for the *halakha* concerning **waving** of those offerings that require waving is **as it is written: "And the priest** shall take one of the lambs, and offer it for a guilt offering, and the *log* of oil, and **wave** them for a wave offering **before the Lord"** (Leviticus 14:12). The source for the *halakha* concerning **bringing** meal offerings to the corner of the altar before the removal of the handful is **as it is written: "And he shall bring it to the altar"** (Leviticus 2:8). The definite article before the word "altar" indicates that this applies only to the altar in the Tabernacle, and not on a private altar.

The *Mishna* enumerates the differences between a *bamas yachid*, a private altar, and a *bamas tzibur*, the public communal *bamah* at Nob and Givon. As noted above, this requires an important correction of what may have been stated in a previous session: all the rituals about to be listed did apply at Nob and Givon. The divide is not between Shilo/Yerushalayim on one side and Nob and Givon on the other, but between Nob and Givon (and Shilo and the *Beis Hamikdash*) on one side and the private *bamah* on the other. Nob and Givon, despite the absence of the *aron* and despite the *heter bamot*, are nonetheless considered *lifnei Hashem* for these ritual purposes — consistent with what was established above regarding *ma'aser sheni* and the *birah* concept.

*Semichah* — the laying of hands upon an offering — is derived from the phrase *lifnei Hashem v'samach*; it requires *lifnei Hashem* and therefore applied only at a central *bamah*. *Shechitas tzafona* — the requirement that an *olah* be slaughtered on the northern side of the altar — derives from *tzafona lifnei Hashem*; it likewise requires the context of *lifnei Hashem*. *Tenufah* — the waving of an offering — derives from *ve-henif ha-kohen lifnei Hashem* by the *minchah*; again, *lifnei Hashem* is explicit.

### Blood Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתָּנוֹת סָבִיב – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְזָרַק עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ סָבִיב (סָבִיב)״. וּכֹהֵן – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְזָרַק הַכֹּהֵן״. בִּגְדֵי שָׁרֵת – ״לְשָׁרֵת בַּקֹּדֶשׁ״. וּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת – ״אֲשֶׁר יְשָׁרְתוּ בָם בַּקֹּדֶשׁ״. לְרֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ – דִּכְתִיב: ״לְרֵיחַ נִיחֹחַ לַה׳״. מְחִיצָה בְּדָמִים – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיְתָה הָרֶשֶׁת עַד חֲצִי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״. רִיחוּץ יָדַיִם – דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבְקׇרְבָתָם אֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ יִרְחָצוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The source for the *halakha* concerning **placement** of the blood of a burnt offering **around,** i.e., on all four sides of, the altar, is **as it is written: "And** Aaron's sons, the priests, **shall…sprinkle** the blood **roundabout against the altar** that is at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 1:5). [Additional ritual sources...]

*Matanos saviv* — the specific manner of blood application, whether around the altar or at two opposite corners — derives from *ve-zarak al ha-mizbeach saviv*. While *lifnei Hashem* does not appear directly in this verse, Rashi cites a proximate verse that does contain the phrase, and the word *ha-mizbeach* — with the definite article — implies the central communal *mizbeach* specifically. *Hagashah* — the bringing of the *minchah* to the altar — derives from *higishah el ha-mizbeach*, again pointing to the central *mizbeach*.

### Kohen Ritual

The requirement that the *avodah* be performed by a *kohen* is derived from *ve-zarak ha-kohen et ha-dam al mizbach Hashem* — *mizbach Hashem*, the altar of Hashem, which implies the context of the central *bamah*. *Bigdei sharet* — the priestly vestments — must be worn *le-shares bakodesh*, in the service of the holy precincts; and *klei sharet*, the service vessels, likewise must be used *ba-kodesh*. The concept of *bakodesh* thus constitutes a third operative category alongside *lifnei Hashem* and *ha-mizbeach* — and it applies to the central communal *bamah* but not the private one.

### Odor & Washing

*Reiach nichoach* — the concept that an offering rises as a pleasing aroma — is tied to the phrase *l'reiach nichoach l'Hashem*. This is a striking point: one would naturally assume that any offering to God, wherever made, constitutes a *reiach nichoach*. Yet the *Gemara* treats this as a function of the central *bamah* specifically, not merely of any *korban*. The *kodesh* designation, it seems, belongs to the place rather than solely to the offering. *Mechitza badamim* — the line marking the midpoint of the *mizbeach*, whereby some bloods are applied above and others below — derives from *ve-asah ha-reshes ad chatzi ha-mizbeach*, a law specific to the central *mizbeach*. *Richutz yadayim* — the washing of hands before the *avodah* — derives from *u-ve-korvam el ha-mizbeach yirchatzu*, once again specifying the central *mizbeach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵין מִנְחָה בְּבָמָה. אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר יֵשׁ מִנְחָה בְּבָמָה – יֵשׁ עוֹפוֹת בְּבָמָה. לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר אֵין מִנְחָה בְּבָמָה – אֵין עוֹפוֹת בְּבָמָה. ״זְבָחִים״ – וְלֹא מְנָחוֹת, ״זְבָחִים״ – וְלֹא עוֹפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the question of whether a meal offering was sacrificed upon an altar outside the Temple, the mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yehuda says: There is no meal offering** sacrificed **on an altar** outside the Temple, even a great public altar, e.g., the Tabernacle in Gilgal, Nov, and Gibeon. **Rav Sheshet says: According to the statement** of **the one who says there is a meal offering** sacrificed **on an altar** outside the Temple, **there are** also **bird** offerings sacrificed **on an altar** outside the Temple. **According to the statement of the one who says there is no meal offering** sacrificed **on an altar** outside the Temple, **there are** also **no bird** offerings sacrificed **on an altar** outside the Temple. The reason for this is that the Torah is referring to offerings that were sacrificed during the period of the permitting of private altars as: Slaughtered offerings [*zevaḥim*], in the verse: "And sacrifice them for sacrifices of [*vezaveḥu zivḥei*] peace offerings" (Leviticus 17:5), from which it may be inferred: **Slaughtered offerings but not meal offerings; slaughtered offerings but not bird** offerings, i.e., neither meal offerings nor bird offerings were sacrificed on an altar outside the Temple, including a great public altar.

A parenthetical dispute is interjected: Rabbi Yehuda holds that there was no *minchah* brought in a private *bamah* at all — only animal sacrifices qualify, based on the verse *ve-zavachta shelamim*, which speaks of *zevachim* and not *menachos*. Rav Sheshes links *menachos* and *ofor* offerings together: according to whoever holds that there was a *minchah* in a private *bamah*, there were also bird sacrifices; and according to whoever holds there was no *minchah*, there were no bird sacrifices either, since *melikah* is not a form of *shechita* and the verse speaks of *zevachim* specifically.

## Private vs. Public

### Rami: How Sanctified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בָּמָה קְטַנָּה, וְהִקְרִיבוּם בְּבָמָה קְטַנָּה; אֲבָל בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בָּמָה קְטַנָּה דְּקָרְבִינְהוּ בְּבָמָה גְּדוֹלָה – יֵשׁ חִיצּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** § Concerning the *halakha* requiring a partition for the blood, and other *halakhot* that did not apply to a private altar but did apply to a great public altar, **Rami bar Ḥama said:** The Sages **taught** that there is no requirement for a partition **only with regard to** consecrated **sacrificial** items to be used on **a small** private **altar, and which** one **sacrificed upon a small** private **altar. But in** the case of consecrated **sacrificial** items to be used on **a small** private **altar that were sacrificed upon a great** public **altar, there is a partition.**

Having enumerated the list of rituals that applied at the central *bamah* but not the private, the *Gemara* now turns to a nuanced question raised by Rami bar Chama. Are these distinctions determined by *where* the offering was brought, or by *how* it was sanctified — that is, for what type of *bamah* was it originally designated? Rami bar Chama's initial position (*ikka d'amri*) is that the distinctions follow the manner of sanctification. An animal sanctified for a *bamah gedolah* and brought there follows the full complement of rules; an animal sanctified for a *bamah ketanah* would not be subject to those rules even if it happened to be brought to the *bamah gedolah*.

### Chaza v'Shoke

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹתֵיב רַבָּה: חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק, תְּרוּמַת לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה – נוֹהֲגִין בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בָּמָה גְּדוֹלָה, וְאֵין נוֹהֲגִין בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בָּמָה קְטַנָּה. אֵימָא: נוֹהֲגִין בְּבָמָה גְּדוֹלָה, וְאֵין נוֹהֲגִין בְּבָמָה קְטַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba raised an objection** from a *baraita*: The *halakha* of waving the **breast and** the **thigh** of a peace offering and **the *teruma* of the loaves of a thanks offering applies with regard to sacrificial** items **of a great** public **altar and does not apply with regard to sacrificial** items **of a small** private **altar,** even if they were sacrificed upon a great public altar. The Gemara responds: **Say** that the text of the *baraita* should be the following: The *halakha* of waving the breast and thigh and the *teruma* of the loaves of a thanks offering **applies to a great** public **altar,** even with regard to a sacrificial item of a small private altar, **but does not apply to a small** private **altar.**

Rami bar Chama brings a *baraita* in support: *chaze v'shoke* — the breast and thigh portions given to the *kohanim* — and *trumat lachmei todah* — the bread portions separated from a *todah* offering — apply to *kodshei bamah gedolah* and do not apply to *kodshei bamah ketanah*. The language of the *baraita* — *kodshei bamah gedolah* and *kodshei bamah ketanah*, "sanctifications of the great bamah" and "sanctifications of the small bamah" — appears to emphasize how the item was sanctified, not where it was ultimately offered. This would seem to support the view that status tracks sanctification rather than location.

The *Gemara*, however, deflects: *eima noheig b'vama gedolah v'eino noheig b'vama ketanah* — read the *baraita* as saying it applies *at* the great *bamah* and does not apply *at* the small *bamah*. The word *kodshei* may be imprecise, and the *baraita* may simply be specifying location. This reading neutralizes the proof.

## Private vs. Public (II)

### R. Elazar Absorption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בָּמָה גְּדוֹלָה, וְהִקְרִיבָן בְּבָמָה גְּדוֹלָה; אֲבָל בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בָּמָה קְטַנָּה – אַף עַל גַּב דְּקָרְבִינְהוּ בְּבָמָה גְּדוֹלָה, אֵין חִיצּוּי. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עוֹלַת בָּמַת יָחִיד שֶׁהִכְנִיסָהּ בִּפְנִים – קְלָטוּהָ מְחִיצוֹת לְכׇל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **Rami bar Ḥama said:** The Sages **taught** that there is a requirement for a partition **only with regard to sacrificial** items of **a great** public **altar** that **were sacrificed upon a great** public **altar. But in** the case of **sacrificial** items of **a small** private **altar, even though they were sacrificed upon a great** public **altar, there is no partition.**...The Gemara notes: **And** this latter version of Rami bar Ḥama's statement **disagrees with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar says: A burnt offering of a private altar that one brought inside,** i.e., into the area of a great public altar, is **absorbed,** i.e., sanctified, by the **partitions for all matters.**

Rava then cites the position of Rabbi Elazar, which bears directly on this question: *olat bamas yachid she-hichnisa bifnim kaltu ha-mechitzot* — if one had an *olah* that had been sanctified as a *bamas yachid* offering and brought it inside to Nob and Givon, the *mechitzot* — the enclosures of the central *bamah* — absorb it. The animal is now treated as belonging to the category of the central *bamah*, not the private one. This affirms that location, not sanctification, determines status — at least once the animal has been brought inside.

The second version of Rami bar Chama's position is in direct tension with Rabbi Elazar: Rami holds that even if a *kodshei bamah ketanah* is brought to the *bamah gedolah*, the rules pertaining to blood application — *mechitza badamim* — do not apply, because status tracks sanctification. Rabbi Elazar holds the opposite: once inside, it is fully absorbed. This remains a genuine dispute.

## Removal Reversion

### Zeira Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: עוֹלַת בָּמַת יָחִיד, שֶׁהִכְנִיסָהּ לִפְנִים וְהוֹצִיאָהּ לַחוּץ – מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דַּעֲיַילָא – קָלְטָה לַהּ מְחִיצְתָּא; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דַּהֲדַר – הֲדַר?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the previous matter, **Rabbi Zeira raises a dilemma:** With regard to **a burnt offering of a private altar** **that one brought inside and** subsequently **took outside, what is** the *halakha*? Does it have the status of a sacrificial item of a public altar? The Gemara clarifies the question: **Do we say** that **once** it was **brought in the partition** has already **absorbed it,** and all *halakhot* of sacrificial items of a public altar apply; **or perhaps once it returns,** i.e., was taken outside again, **it returns** to its prior status as an offering of a private altar?

Rabbi Zeira poses the final question of the *daf*, left unresolved: *olat bamas yachid she-hichnisa bifnim ve-hotzia la-chutz* — what if the animal sanctified as a *bamas yachid* offering was brought inside Nob and Givon and then removed? *Mai?* — what is the law? Two possibilities present themselves. *Mi amrinan keivan de-ayela kalta le-mechitzta* — do we say that the moment it was brought inside, the *mechitzot* absorbed and defined it, such that removing it constitutes an invalidation? Or perhaps — *keivan de-hadar, hadar* — once it is removed, it reverts to its prior status as a *bamas yachid* offering and the stricter rules no longer apply? The *daf* closes with this question unresolved, to be taken up in the final *daf* of the *masechet*.
