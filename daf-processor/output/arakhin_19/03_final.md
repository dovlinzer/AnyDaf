# Arakhin 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Weight Donation

### Pledging Weight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר: ״מְשַׁקְּלִי עָלַי״ נוֹתֵן מִשְׁקָלוֹ, אִם כֶּסֶף כָּסֶף, וְאִם זָהָב זָהָב. מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאַמָּה שֶׁל יַרְמַטְיָא שֶׁאָמְרָה: ״מִשְׁקַל בִּתִּי עָלַי״, וְעָלְתָה לִירוּשָׁלַיִם וְשַׁקְלַהּ מִשְׁקָלָהּ זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who says:** It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my weight, gives his weight** to the Temple treasury; **if** he specified **silver** he donates **silver, and if** he specified **gold** he donates **gold.** There was **an incident involving the mother of Yirmatya, who said:** It is incumbent **upon me** to donate the **weight of my daughter, and she ascended to Jerusalem and paid her** daughter's **weight** in **gold** to the Temple treasury.

We begin a new *perek* today — Daf 19a — and with it a shift in focus. Although we are still in *Masechet Arakhin*, this *perek* is less concerned with *erchin* proper and deals instead with more traditional *nedarim*: the various ways a person may donate money to the *Beit HaMikdash*. The opening *Mishnah* states: *ha'omer mishkali alai* — if someone says "my weight is upon me," pledging their body weight to the *Beit HaMikdash*, then *noten mishkalo*, they must give their weight. And the *Mishnah* adds: *im kesef kesef, im zahav zahav* — if silver, then silver; if gold, then gold. The *Gemara* will clarify precisely what this means.

### Yarmatya Story

The *Mishnah* then introduces a narrative: *maaseh be'imo shel Yarmatya* — a story involving the mother of someone named Yarmatya, a figure of apparent importance. The mother declared *mishkal biti alai* — the weight of my daughter should be upon me. She went up to Jerusalem, they weighed her daughter, and the court required her to give that weight in gold. The reason this story is brought — and why gold specifically was demanded — will become clear from the *Gemara*'s discussion below.

### Hand Weight Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִשְׁקַל יְדֵי עָלַי״, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְמַלֵּא חָבִית מַיִם, וּמַכְנִיסָהּ עַד מַרְפֵּיקוֹ, וְשׁוֹקֵל מִבְּשַׂר חֲמוֹר וַעֲצָמוֹת וְגִידִים וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹכָהּ עַד שֶׁתִּתְמַלֵּא. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: וְכִי הֵיאַךְ אֶפְשָׁר לְכַוֵּין בָּשָׂר כְּנֶגֶד בָּשָׂר וַעֲצָמוֹת כְּנֶגֶד עֲצָמוֹת? אֶלָּא שָׁמִין אֶת הַיָּד כַּמָּה הִיא רְאוּיָה לִשְׁקוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate the **weight of my forearm,** how does he ascertain the weight of his forearm? **Rabbi Yehuda says: He fills a barrel** with **water and inserts** his arm **up to his elbow** into the water. **And** in order to measure the displacement, **he weighs donkey flesh, and bones, and sinews and places** it **into** the barrel **until it fills,** and the water level reaches the top of the barrel. He then donates the weight of the meat and the bones to the Temple treasury. **Rabbi Yosei said:** Displacement is according to volume not according to weight, **and how then is it possible to match** the amount of the donkey **flesh with the flesh** of a person **and** the volume of the donkey's **bones with** his **bones? Rather,** the court **appraises how much the forearm is likely to weigh.**

The *Mishnah* continues with a further case. If someone says *mishkal yadi alai* — I want to donate the weight of my hand — Rabbi Yehuda says: you fill a barrel completely with water and insert the arm until *marpeko*. *Rashi* understands *marpeko* as the shoulder; *Tosafot* understands it as the elbow, and *Tosafot*'s reading fits more smoothly across the relevant *sugyas* in *Shas*. You observe how much water is displaced, then take donkey flesh, bones, and sinews — *besar chamor va'atzamot vegidin* — and displace the same amount of water. The density of donkey flesh is assumed to be approximately equal to that of human flesh, so you weigh the donkey material needed to reach the same displacement, and that is the weight owed to the *Beit HaMikdash*.

Rabbi Yosi objects: *heich efshar lechaven basar keneged basar, gidim keneged gidim, atzamot keneged atzamot?* — How is it truly possible to match muscle for muscle, sinew for sinew, bone for bone in exact proportion? Rather, says Rabbi Yosi, *shammin et hayad kamah hi re'uyah lishkol* — we simply estimate approximately how much the hand would weigh. The *Mishnah* thus presents both approaches, and the *Gemara* will develop the dispute further.

## Conditional Pledges

### Silver vs. Gold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״אִם כֶּסֶף כֶּסֶף אִם זָהָב זָהָב״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: פֵּירַשׁ כֶּסֶף — כֶּסֶף, פֵּירַשׁ זָהָב — זָהָב. פְּשִׁיטָא! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: טַעְמָא דְּפֵירַשׁ, הָא לֹא פֵּירֵשׁ — פָּטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּכֹל דְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** is the meaning of the mishna's statement: **If silver, silver,** and **if gold, gold? Rav Yehuda said:** If **one specified** that he vows to donate his weight in **silver** he donates **silver,** and if **he specified gold** he donates **gold.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **This** is what the mishna **is teaching us: The reason** he donates silver or gold is **that he specified** silver or gold, from which it may be inferred that if **he did not specify** the means of payment, he may **exempt himself with any** material.

The *Gemara* opens by asking: *mai im kesef kesef?* — what does the *Mishnah* mean when it says "if silver, silver; if gold, gold"? Rav Yehuda explains: *peireish kesef kesef, peireish zahav zahav* — if you specified that you want to give silver, you must give silver; if you specified gold, you must give gold. The *Gemara* immediately objects: *peshitah* — that is obvious. What new information does the *Mishnah* convey?

The answer is that the *Mishnah* is teaching by implication: *ta'ama depeireish, ha lo peireish patar nafshih bechol dehu* — the reason you must give the specified metal is because you specified it. Had you not specified, you could give your weight in any substance — or rather, in nearly any substance, as Rachba qualifies. Rachba teaches: *ba'asra detakli kufra, patar nafshih afilu bekufra* — in a locale where pitch or tar is sold by weight, you may discharge your obligation by giving your weight in pitch. The substance need not be a precious metal; it must simply be something sold by weight in your locale.

### Pitch & Onions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּרְחָבָה, דְּאָמַר רַחֲבָה: בְּאַתְרָא דְּתִקְלֵי כּוֹפְרָא — פָּטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ אֲפִילּוּ בַּכּוֹפְרָא. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִיכָּא דִּתְקֵל וְאִיכָּא דְּכָיֵיל. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּכוּלְּהוּ לָא תִּקְלֵי — לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: And this is **in accordance with** a statement of **Raḥava, as Raḥava says: In a place where** merchants **weigh pitch** when selling it, one who vows his weight **may exempt himself** by donating his weight **even in pitch.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **No,** the statement of Raḥava is **necessary** in a place **where there are** merchants **who weigh** pitch **and there are** others who **measure** its volume. **Lest you say: Since not all** merchants **weigh** pitch one may **not** fulfill his vow by donating his weight in pitch, Raḥava **teaches us** that as there are merchants there who sell pitch by weight, one can fulfill his vow in that manner.

The *Gemara* again objects: *peshitah* — why shouldn't pitch work if it is sold by weight? The *Gemara* answers: *lo tzerichah de'ika detakal ve'ika dekayal* — the novelty is in a case where some merchants sell pitch by weight and others sell it by volume. One might have thought that since not all merchants sell it by weight it cannot qualify; the teaching tells us it is nonetheless sufficient.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּאַתְרָא דְּתִקְלֵי שַׁמְכֵי, פָּטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁמְכֵי. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּבָתַר דְּשָׁקְלִי שָׁדוּ תְּרֵי תְּלָתָא. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: בְּטִיל תּוֹרַת מִשְׁקַל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: In a place where** merchants **weigh onions** when selling them, one who vowed his weight **may exempt himself** by donating his weight **even in onions.** The Gemara again asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **No,** the statement of Rav Pappa is **necessary** in a place **where after they weigh** the onions the merchants **throw** in **two** or **three** extra onions to the buyer. **Lest you say** that its **status** as a place where onions are sold by **weight** is **void** due to the additional onions, Rav Pappa **teaches us** that it is still considered a place where onions are sold by weight.

Rav Papa then adds a parallel ruling: *ba'asra detakli shamchei, patar nafshih afilu beshamchei* — in a place where onions are sold by weight, you may discharge your pledge by giving your weight in onions (*shamchei*, as *Rashi* explains, meaning *betzalim*, onions). Again the *Gemara* challenges: *peshitah*. And again the answer identifies the novelty: *debasar deshakli shadu trei platei* — the typical practice when selling onions was to weigh them out and then throw in two or three extra as a bonus, like a baker's dozen. One might have thought that since the transaction is not purely by weight — given the extra portion added — the substance is no longer considered *sold by weight*. The *Gemara* teaches that it nonetheless qualifies.

### Wealthy Assessment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאַמָּה שֶׁל יַרְמַטְיָא וְכוּ׳. מַעֲשֶׂה לִסְתּוֹר?! חַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי, וְאִם אָדָם חָשׁוּב הוּא, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא פָּרֵישׁ — לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ אָמְרִינַן, וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאַמָּה שֶׁל יַרְמַטְיָא שֶׁאָמְרָה ״מִשְׁקַל בִּתִּי עָלַי״, וְעָלְתָה לִירוּשָׁלַיִם וְשַׁקְלוּהּ וְנָתְנָה מִשְׁקָלָהּ זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: There was **an incident involving the mother of Yirmatya,** who said: It is incumbent upon me to donate the weight of my daughter, without specifying silver or gold, and she ascended to Jerusalem and paid her daughter's weight in gold to the Temple treasury. The Gemara asks: Was **an incident** cited **to contradict** the previous ruling of the mishna? The mishna had stated: If silver, silver, and if gold, gold, which indicates that if one did not specify the means of payment he may exempt himself with any material that merchants sell by weight, whereas it can be inferred from the incident that one must pay the weight in gold. The Gemara answers: The mishna **is incomplete and this** is what **it is teaching: And if** the one who vowed **is a distinguished person, even though he did not specify** silver or gold **we say** he must fulfill his vow **in keeping with his** socioeconomic **status. And** likewise, there was **an incident involving the mother of Yirmatya,** a very wealthy woman, **who said:** It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **the weight of my daughter, and she ascended to Jerusalem and gave her** daughter's **weight** in **gold** to the Temple treasury.

The *Gemara* now returns to the story of Yarmatya's mother. It asks: *maaseh listor?* — is the story coming to contradict what we just said? We explained that if you do not specify a substance you may give anything sold by weight. Yet in the story, Yarmatya's mother did not specify, and the court required her to give gold. The *Gemara* resolves this with *chasuri mechasra vehachi kasani* — our text of the *Mishnah* is incomplete, and here is what it should have read: *ve'im adam chashuv hu, af al gav delo peireish, lefi kevodo amdinan* — if the person is a person of great means, then even if they did not specify, we assess what would be fitting for someone of their standing. The story of Yarmatya's mother is thus brought to illustrate this principle: she was a wealthy woman, she said merely that she was pledging her daughter's weight, and because of her stature the court assessed that the appropriate payment was gold.

## Height & Limbs

### Height Rods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָאוֹמֵר ״קוֹמָתִי עָלַי״ נוֹתֵן שַׁרְבִיט שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִכְפָּף, ״מָלֵא קוֹמָתִי עָלַי״ נוֹתֵן שַׁרְבִיט הַנִּכְפָּף. מֵיתִיבִי: ״קוֹמָתִי עָלַי״, ״מָלֵא קוֹמָתִי עָלַי״ — נוֹתֵן שַׁרְבִיט שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִכְפָּף.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **one who says:** It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my height, gives** a thick **rod that cannot be bent** equivalent to his height. One who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my full height,** may **give** even a thin **rod that** can be **bent,** provided it is equivalent to his height. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 3:1): With regard to one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my height,** or: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my full height, he gives** a thick **rod that cannot be bent** and that is equivalent to his height.

The *Gemara* now takes up a related question. Rav Yehuda asks: what is the *din* if one says *komasi alai* — I want to donate my height? The ruling is: *nosein sharvit she'eino nichpaf* — you must give a rod of the appropriate substance equal to your height, and the rod must be thick enough that it cannot bend. However, if one says *melo komasi alai* — the fullness of my height — then *nosein sharvit hanichpaf*, you may give even a flexible rod.

The *Gemara* challenges this from a *Tosefta*: the *Tosefta* explicitly states that whether you say *komasi alai* or *melo komasi alai*, in both cases you must give a rigid rod — *sharvit she'eino nichpaf*. The *Gemara* resolves this by saying that Rav Yehuda follows the *shita* of Rabbi Akiva, as indicated by the phrase *dedayek lishnei yeseira* — Rabbi Akiva pays close attention to every extra word and interprets it as introducing a meaningful distinction.

### Teiku Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּדָיֵיק לִישָּׁנָא יַתִּירָא, דִּתְנַן: לֹא אֶת הַבּוֹר וְלֹא הַדּוּת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכָּתַב עוֹמְקָהּ וְרוּמַהּ, וְצָרִיךְ לִיקַּח לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בִּזְמַן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ ״חוּץ מֵאֵלּוּ״ שֶׁאֵין צָרִיךְ לִיקַּח לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ. אַלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא צְרִיךְ וְקָאָמַר — לְטַפּוֹיֵי מִילְּתָא קָאָתֵי, הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא צְרִיךְ וְקָאָמַר — לְטַפּוֹיֵי מִילְּתָא קָאָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Yehuda **says** his statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who** holds that one can **draw an inference** from **superfluous language. As we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 64a): If one sold his house without specification, he has sold **neither the pit nor the cistern [*dut*]** with it, **even if he wrote** in the document of sale: With **its depth and its height.** This is because anything that is ancillary to the house, e.g., pits and cisterns, must be mentioned explicitly in the contract. **And** the seller **must purchase for himself a path** through to the pit or cistern that he kept back, as he sold his rights to the area surrounding the house along with the house, and therefore he may no longer walk through that area. This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.** **And the Rabbis say: He need not** purchase a path, as the seller clearly did not intend to keep the pit or cistern without maintaining access to it. **And Rabbi Akiva concedes** that **when** the seller **states to** the buyer in the document of sale: **Excluding these,** the pit and the cistern, **that he need not purchase for himself a path** through to the pit or cistern. **Evidently,** Rabbi Akiva's reasoning is that **since** the seller **need not** specify that the pit and cistern are excluded from the sale, **and** yet **he says** that they were excluded, **he is coming** with this statement **to add an element** to the agreement, i.e., the right of access. **Here too,** when one says: It is incumbent upon me to donate my full height, **since** it is a case where **he need not** add the word full, **and** yet **he says** it, **he is coming to add an element** to his vow, i.e., the ability to exempt himself with a thin rod.

We know this principle from a *Mishnah* in *Bava Batra*, in *perek* on selling a house (*perek hamocher et habayit*): *velo et habor velo et hadus* — when selling a house or field, the sale does not automatically include cisterns or cellars, even if the deed specifies dimensions. Rabbi Akiva rules, moreover, that if the seller must now pass through sold land to reach his retained cistern, he must purchase a path back from the buyer — because the path is not assumed to be included in what was retained. The *Chachamim* disagree, holding that since the cistern was not sold, the path necessary to access it was implicitly retained as well.

The part relevant to our case is *modeh Rabbi Akiva bizman she'amar lo chutz me'elu she'ein tzarich likach lo derech* — Rabbi Akiva concedes that if the seller said "I am selling my field with the exception of the cistern," he does not need to repurchase the path, even though he normally would. Why? Because he did not need to specify the exception — under the ordinary rule the cistern is not included. Since he specified it anyway, *alma keivan delo tzarich veka'amar litfuyei milta ka'ati* — since he did not need to say it and he said it regardless, he must have been adding something, and what he was adding was his retention of the path as well. From this we see Rabbi Akiva's general interpretive method: any extra word a person uses carries legal weight.

Applying this to our case: *hacha nami keivan delo tzarich veka'amar litfuyei milta ka'ati* — here too, the person did not need to say *melo komasi*; the ordinary formulation *komasi* would have sufficed. Having added the word *melo*, he must have intended to give something different — namely, the more lenient option of a flexible rod.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״עוֹמְדֵי״ מַהוּ? ״רוֹחְבּוֹ״ מַהוּ? ״יְשִׁיבָתוֹ״ מַהוּ? ״עוֹבְיוֹ״ מַהוּ? ״הִיקִּיפוּ״ מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages. If one says: It is incumbent upon me to donate **my stature, what is** the *halakha*? If one vowed that it is incumbent upon him to donate **his width, what is** the *halakha*? Likewise, if one vowed to donate **his sitting, what is** the *halakha*? If he referred to **his thickness, what is** the *halakha*? Finally, if he spoke of **his girth, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara states that these dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

The *Gemara* then raises a series of unresolved questions. *Iba'ya lehu*: what is the *din* if someone says *omdi* — my stature? What if they say *rochvi* — my breadth? What about *yeshivati* — the space I occupy when seated? What about *ovyi* — my width? Or *heikefi* — my circumference? The *Gemara* leaves all of these at *teiku* — unresolved. These edge cases may simply be too rare to require definitive resolution.

## Limb Weight

### Displacement Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשְׁקַל יָדִי עָלַי וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מִשְׁקָל יְדֵי״ וּ״מִשְׁקַל רַגְלֵי עָלַי״, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מֵבִיא חָבִית וּמְמַלֵּא מַיִם, וּמַכְנִיס בְּיַד עַד הָאַצִּיל, וּבָרֶגֶל עַד הָאַרְכּוּבָּה. וְשׁוֹקֵל בְּשַׂר חֲמוֹר גִּידִים וַעֲצָמוֹת, וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹכָהּ עַד שֶׁתִּתְמַלֵּא, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לְדָבָר, זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר בְּשַׂר חֲמוֹרִים בְּשָׂרָם״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that there is a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei concerning one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate the **weight of my forearm,** as to how he ascertains the weight of his forearm. **The Sages taught** this dispute in greater detail in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 3:2): With regard to one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate the **weight of my forearm, and** with regard to one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate the **weight of my leg, Rabbi Yehuda says: He brings a barrel and fills** it with **water, and** he **inserts** his **forearm up to the elbow or** his **leg up to the knee,** into the water. **And** in order to measure the displacement, **he weighs donkey flesh, sinews, and bones, and places** it **into the barrel until it fills,** and the water reaches the same level as it was when his arm or leg was inserted. **And even though there is no proof for the matter,** that donkey flesh weighs the same as human flesh, nevertheless there is **an allusion to the matter, as it is stated: "Whose flesh is as the flesh of donkeys"** (Ezekiel 23:20).

We now return to the question of how to weigh a specific limb. The *Gemara* quotes a *baraita*: *tanu rabbanan* — if someone pledges *mishkal yadi alai* or *mishkal ragli alai* — the weight of their hand or foot — Rabbi Yehuda rules as follows. Fill a barrel with water. For the hand, insert the arm *ad ha'atzil*, until the elbow. For the leg, insert it *ad ha'arkuva*, until the knee. Then take donkey flesh, bones, and sinews, and displace that same volume of water — *noten letocho ad she'titmalei*. The donkey material used to achieve equivalent displacement is then weighed, and that is the amount owed.

The *baraita* acknowledges: *ve'af al pi she'ein re'ayah la'davar zecher la'davar* — even though there is no *pasuk* that proves this method, there is a *pasuk* in *Yechezkel* that alludes to it: *asher besar chamorim besaram* — their flesh is like the flesh of donkeys. This verse is taken to confirm that donkey flesh is a reasonable analogue for human flesh in density, making it a valid substitute for this measurement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: הֵיאַךְ אֶפְשָׁר לְכַוֵּין בָּשָׂר כְּנֶגֶד בְּשַׂר, גִּידִים כְּנֶגֶד גִּידִים, עֲצָמוֹת כְּנֶגֶד עֲצָמוֹת? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אוֹמְדִין. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: עַד שֶׁאוֹמְדִין, יָמוֹדּוּ אֶת הַיָּד. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כַּמָּה דְּאֶפְשָׁר עָבְדִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said to** Rabbi Yehuda: Displacement is according to volume, not according to weight, and **how** then **is it possible to match** the amount of the donkey **flesh with the flesh** in one's forearm, **the sinews with the sinews, and the bones with the bones? Rabbi Yehuda said to him:** One **estimates. Rabbi Yosei said to him: If one estimates, let one estimate** the weight of **the forearm** directly. **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda** respond to Rabbi Yosei? Rabbi Yehuda holds that **we do whatever is possible** in order to be more precise.

Rabbi Yosi objects, as in the *Mishnah*: *hei efshar lechaven basar keneged basar, gidim keneged gidim, atzamot keneged atzamot?* — Can one truly replicate the exact proportional composition of muscle, sinew, and bone? Rabbi Yehuda responds: *umdana* — you estimate where necessary. Rabbi Yosi presses the point: *ad she'umdana yamodu et hayad* — once you have to estimate anyway, simply estimate the total weight of the hand directly, without the elaborate donkey-flesh procedure. Rabbi Yehuda's position, in turn, is *kama de'efshar avdinan bah* — we do everything possible to be precise, and only where an exact match is impossible do we rely on estimation.

### Hand Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּיָּד עַד הָאַצִּיל, וּרְמִינְהוּ: קִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם בְּמִקְדָּשׁ עַד הַפֶּרֶק. דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא עַד הַפֶּרֶק, בִּנְדָרִים הַלֵּךְ אַחַר לְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם, וּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא עַד הַפֶּרֶק? וְהָא גַּבֵּי תְּפִילִּין דִּכְתִיב ״יָדְךָ״, וְתַנָּא דְבֵי מְנַשֶּׁה: ״יָדְךָ״ — זוֹ קִיבּוֹרִית! דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — קִיבּוּרִית כּוּלַּהּ, בִּנְדָרִים הַלֵּךְ אַחַר לְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם, וְקִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ — הִילְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *Tosefta* teaches with regard to one who says: It is incumbent upon me to donate the weight of my forearm [*yad*], that Rabbi Yehuda says: He brings a barrel and fills it with water and inserts his **forearm up to the elbow.** This indicates that the term *yad* denotes the forearm until the elbow. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Yadayim* 2:1): Since it is written: "And Aaron and his sons shall wash their hands [*yedeihem*] and their feet" (Exodus 30:19), the priests are obligated to **sanctify** their **hands and feet in the Temple up to the wrist.** The Gemara answers: The term *yad* written **in the Torah** indicates **up to the wrist,** but **with regard to vows** one **follows** the ordinary **language of people,** in which the word *yad* is referring to the forearm until the elbow. Consequently, the vow is interpreted in this manner. The Gemara asks: **And** does the word *yad* that is written **in the Torah** mean **up to the wrist? But with regard to phylacteries it is written:** "And it shall be for a sign to you upon **your hand [*yadkha*]"** (Exodus 13:9), **and the school of Menashe taught: *Yadkha*; this** is referring to the bulge of the **biceps [*kibborit*]** on the upper arm. The Gemara answers: Generally, the term *yad* that is written **in the Torah** includes the **entire biceps** area of the upper arm. But **with regard to vows** one **follows** the ordinary **language of people,** in which the word *yad* means the forearm until the elbow; **and** with regard to **sanctifying the hands and feet in the Temple** the ***halakha* is learned** as a tradition that the word *yad* is referring only to the hand up to the wrist.

The *baraita* states that for the hand one inserts the arm *ad ha'atzil*, until the elbow. The *Gemara* challenges this from a *Tosefta* in *Masechet Yadayim*: *kidush yadayim veraglayim bamikdash ad haperek* — when a *kohen* sanctifies his hands in the *Mikdash*, he sanctifies until the *perek*, the wrist. This implies that the halakhic definition of *yad* is only until the wrist, not the elbow.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *binedarim halach achar lashon benei adam* — in *nedarim* we follow not formal halakhic definitions but ordinary speech. When people in those times used the word *yad*, they meant the entire arm up to the elbow, and therefore a pledge using the word *yad* is interpreted accordingly.

But the *Gemara* then asks whether the assumption itself is correct — is it really true that *deoraita* the definition of *yad* extends only to the wrist? After all, regarding *tefillin*, the *pasuk* says *yadcha*, and a *baraita* (*Tana devei Menashe*) teaches: *yadcha zo kiboret* — the Torah's use of *yad* by *tefillin* refers to the bicep. No one wraps *tefillin* on the hand itself; it goes on the upper arm. So the *Gemara* revises its assumption: *deoraita kiboret kulah* — the Torah's definition of *yad* actually extends all the way to the end of the bicep.

The principle *binedarim halach achar lashon benei adam* now works in the opposite direction. Previously, *lashon benei adam* was *machmir* relative to the halakhic definition — people say *yad* and mean more than the wrist. Now it is *mekil* — because the halakhic definition extends to the end of the bicep, while *lashon benei adam* stops at the elbow.

As for the earlier ruling about hand-washing in the *Mikdash* going only to the wrist: *vekidush yadayim veraglayim bamikdash hilchata gemiri lah* — that is a special transmitted tradition, not an expression of the Torah's general definition of *yad*, and does not affect our analysis.

### Foot Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרֶגֶל עַד הָאַרְכּוּבָּה, וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״רְגָלִים״ — פְּרָט לְבַעֲלֵי קַבִּין! בִּנְדָרִים הַלֵּךְ אַחַר לְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם, וּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא — פְּרָט לְבַעֲלֵי קַבִּין? וְהָא גַּבֵּי חֲלִיצָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״רַגְלוֹ״, וְתָנָא: חָלְצָה מִן הָאַרְכּוּבָּה וּלְמַטָּה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״מֵעַל רַגְלוֹ״. אִי הָכִי, לְמַעְלָה מֵהָאַרְכּוּבָּה נָמֵי? ״מָעַל״, וְלֹא מָעַל דְּמֵעַל.
>
> **Translation:** It is further taught in the *Tosefta* cited above: His **leg [*regel*] until the knee,** which indicates that the term *regel* is defined as the foot until the knee. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: It is written in the Torah with regard to the obligation to ascend to Jerusalem for the pilgrimage Festivals: "Three **times [*regalim*]** you shall keep a feast for Me in the year" (Exodus 23:14). The term *regalim*, legs, serves to **exclude people with artificial legs [*ba'alei kabbayin*]** from this obligation. According to the *baraita*, one who has a stump in place of his foot is exempt from the pilgrimage. This indicates the term *regel* is referring to the foot, up to the ankle, not to the entire area below the knee. The Gemara explains: The term *regel* that is written in the Torah is referring to the foot below the ankle, but **with regard to vows,** one **follows** the ordinary **language of people,** in which the word *regel* means the leg until the knee. The Gemara asks: **And** does *regel* written **in the Torah** mean the foot up to the ankle, and therefore it serves to **exclude people with artificial legs?** **But with regard to the ritual by which the *yavam* frees the *yevama* of her levirate bonds [*ḥalitza*], it is written:** "Then his brother's wife shall approach…and loose his shoe from upon **his foot [*raglo*]"** (Deuteronomy 25:9), **and the Sages taught** in a mishna (*Yevamot* 101a): In the case of a *yavam* who was missing part of his leg, if the *yevama* **performed the *ḥalitza*** on any part of the leg **from the knee and below her *ḥalitza* is valid.** This indicates that the term *regel* in the Torah means the leg from the foot up to the knee. The Gemara answers: The term *regel* in the Torah actually is referring to the foot, below the ankle, and it **is different there,** with regard to *ḥalitza*, **as the verse states: From upon his foot [*raglo*],** instead of simply stating: From his foot. This indicates that the area that is upon, i.e., above, the foot is also valid for the performance of *ḥalitza*. The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** then if the *yevama* performed *ḥalitza* **above the knee** it should **also** be valid. The Gemara answers that it is written: **From upon,** to include only the section directly above the foot, i.e., until the knee, **and** the area above the knee is considered **from upon that** area **that is from upon** the foot.

Parallel to the hand, the *baraita* states that for the foot one inserts *ad ha'arkuva*, until the knee. The *Gemara* challenges this from a *davar* in the laws of *aliyah leregel*. The *Gemara* derives from the word *regalim* — the pilgrimage festivals, which contains the word *regel* — that *prat lebaalei kabin*: those who walk on wooden feet, i.e., amputees with prosthetics, are exempt from the obligation to appear in Jerusalem and bring the *olat reiyah*. This implies that the definition of *regel* relevant to this *mitzvah* is the foot alone, not the entire leg up to the knee.

The *Gemara* resolves this the same way: *binedarim halach achar lashon benei adam* — in *nedarim* we follow how people speak, and people refer to the entire lower leg up to the knee as their *regel*. It is only for the purpose of *aliyah leregel* that the Torah's narrower definition applies.

### Chalitza Anatomy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, הַאי אִיסְתְּוֵירָא עַד אַרְעָא נָחֵית, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מִיפְסָק פְּסֵיק, הֲוָה לֵיהּ אִיסְתְּוֵירָא ״מֵעַל״, וְשָׁקָא ״מֵעַל״ דְּ״מֵעַל״. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא מִיפְסָק פְּסֵיק, כֹּל דְּבַהֲדֵי כַּרְעָא כְּכַרְעָא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Conclude from** this discussion that **the ankle** bone **[*istaveira*] descends to the ground** and is not separated from the foot. The reason is **that if it enters your mind** to say it is **separated, then the ankle** bone is the section of the leg referred to by the phrase: **From upon** his foot, and the lower **leg,** i.e., the section of the leg from the ankle up to the knee, is the section of the leg that is called: **From upon** that **which** is **from upon** the foot, and would therefore be invalid for the performance of *ḥalitza*. **Rav Ashi says: Even** if **you say** the ankle is **separated** from the foot it would still not be referred to as the section that is upon the foot, as **anything that is adjacent to the foot is considered like the foot,** i.e., it is part of the same section of the leg as the foot.

The *Gemara* then asks whether it is really accurate that the Torah's definition of *regel* is limited to the foot alone. Is there not the case of *chalitza*, where the *pasuk* reads *vechaltzah na'alo me'al raglo* — she removes his shoe from his *regel* — and a *tanna* teaches that if the *chalitza* shoe was tied anywhere below the knee, the *chalitza* is *keshera bedieved*? This seems to indicate that the Torah's definition of *regel* extends all the way up to the knee, not merely the foot.

The *Gemara* responds: *shani hatam de'amar me'al raglo* — by *chalitza* the *pasuk* adds the word *me'al*, "from above the foot," and it is this extra word that comes to include the calf. One might then ask: if *me'al* expands the definition, why not extend it above the knee as well? The *Gemara* answers: *me'al velo me'al deme'al* — "above," but not above that which is above the foot. The word *me'al* adds one extension above the foot — the calf — but not a second extension beyond that, which would include the thigh. The thigh therefore does not qualify for *chalitza*.

Rav Papa then derives an anatomical observation from this *sugya*. Rav Sheshet notes: *shma mina hai istavira ad ara nachit* — one can infer that the ankle bone extends all the way down to the ground. His reasoning: if the ankle bone ended before reaching the ground, then the ankle itself would be *me'al* the foot, and the calf would be *me'al deme'al*, leaving the calf outside the valid range. Since the *Mishnah* permits *chalitza* up the entire length of the calf, it follows that the ankle must descend all the way to the floor, placing the calf — not the ankle — as the single extension *me'al* the foot.

Rav Ashi disagrees: *afilu teima mafsek pasik* — even if you say the ankle bone does end before the ground, the rule is *kol de'bahadei kara ke'kara dami* — whatever occupies the same horizontal plane as the foot is considered part of the foot. Therefore the ankle, though not physically continuous to the ground, counts as part of the foot because it enters the same horizontal plane. The calf is then *me'al* the foot, and the thigh is *me'al deme'al* — and therefore invalid for *chalitza*.

## Limb Value

### Slave Market Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״דָּמֵי יְדֵי עָלַי״ — שָׁמִין אוֹתוֹ כַּמָּה הוּא שָׁוָה בַּיָּד וּבְלֹא יָד. זֶה חוֹמֶר בַּנְּדָרִים מִבַּעֲרָכִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one vows: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate the **assessment of my forearm,** the court **appraises him** to determine **how much he is worth with a forearm and** how much he is worth **without a forearm,** and he pays the difference. **This is** a *halakha* that is more **stringent with regard to vows** of assessment **than with regard to valuations,** as one who says: It is incumbent upon me to donate the valuation of my forearm, is exempt from paying.

The *Mishnah* now addresses a related but distinct case: *de'mei yadai alai* — I pledge the *value* of my hand to the *Beit HaMikdash*, not its weight. The *Mishnah* rules: *shamin oso kama hu shaveh be'yad u'velo yad* — we assess how much he is worth with a hand, how much without, and the difference is the value of the hand. The *Mishnah* notes this is *chomer binedarim mi'be'erchin* — more stringent in *nedarim* than in *erchin*, because saying *erech yadi* — the *erech* of my hand — obligates nothing, since *erchin* only applies to a limb *she'ha'neshama teluya bo*, a limb without which one cannot live. *Demai*, by contrast, assigns real monetary value to the limb.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכִי שָׁיְימִינַן לֵיהּ? אָמַר רָבָא: אוֹמְדִין אוֹתוֹ אוֹמֶד שֶׁל נִזָּקִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם גַּבְרָא זִילָא הוּא, הָכָא גַּבְרָא שְׁבִיחַ הוּא. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אוֹמְדִין כַּמָּה אָדָם רוֹצֶה לִיתֵּן בְּעֶבֶד הָעוֹשֶׂה מְלָאכָה בְּיָדוֹ אַחַת לָעוֹשֶׂה בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו. יָדוֹ אַחַת מַאי נִיהוּ, וְאִידַּךְ פְּסִיקָא? הַיְינוּ הָךְ! אֶלָּא, יָדוֹ מוּכְתֶּבֶת לְרַבּוֹ רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **How do we assess him? Rava said:** The court **assesses him** by means of the same process of **assessment** used with regard to the *halakhot* **of damages.** If one's hand was severed, the court considers the injured party as though he is a slave being sold in the slave market and appraises how much he was worth before the injury and how much he is worth after the injury. The difference between these two sums is the amount that the guilty party must pay for damages. **Abaye said to** Rava: **Are these** two assessments **comparable? There,** with regard to damages, **the person has depreciated** in value because his hand was severed, and therefore the decline in the assessment is greater for someone with a severed hand than for someone who merely lacks the utility of his hand. **Here,** with regard to one who vowed to donate the assessment of his forearm, **the person** himself **has good** value, as his hand is intact, and if the assessment used with regard to damages is applied to him he would be obligated to pay more than that which he vowed. Rather, **Abaye said: One assesses how much less** **a person is willing to pay for a slave who works with one hand than for** a slave **who works with two hands.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the case of a slave who works with **one hand?** Isn't this a situation where **the other** hand **is severed?** If so, then **this** assessment is identical to **that** which Rava proposed. **Rather,** one assesses how much more a person is willing to pay for a slave who belongs exclusively to him than for a case where it is **written** in his bill of sale **that** his **first owner** reserves ownership of one **hand** of the slave.

The *Gemara* asks: *heichi sham'inan* — how precisely do we carry out this assessment? Rav Ada says: *omden oso omed shel nezikin* — we assess him the same way we assess for tort damages. In a *nezikin* case where someone's hand was severed, the assessment involves estimating what the person would have been worth on the slave market with the hand, versus what they are worth now without it, and the difference is the hand's value. This appears to be simply the plain reading of the *Mishnah*.

Abaye challenges this: *mi dami?* — are the cases truly comparable? *Hasam gavra zila hu, hacha gavra shvi'ach hu* — in the *nezikin* case, the person stands before us as he currently is, namely without a hand, and we estimate his value *with* the hand. In the *nedarim* case, the person currently *has* the hand, and we must estimate what he would be worth *without* it. Abaye's concern is that the direction of estimation matters: even if we accept that estimating upward from an incomplete state is reliable, we cannot assume the same reliability when estimating downward from a complete state.

Abaye therefore proposes a different method: *omden kama adam rotze litein be'eved ha'oseh melacha be'yad achat le'oseh bi'shtei yadayim* — we assess how much more a buyer would pay for a slave who works with both hands versus one who works with only one. One might ask: is this not essentially the same as going to the slave market and comparing the prices of people with and without a hand? The *Gemara* raises exactly this point: if we are talking about a slave who is actually missing a hand, *hainu hach* — that is the same method as Rav Ada's. Rather, Abaye specifies: *yado muchtevet lerabo rishon* — we are dealing with a case where one hand of the slave is sold to a different master. One person therefore owns the entire slave except one hand. The differential in market value between owning a complete slave versus owning this slave-minus-one-hand yields, according to Abaye, a more precise and less speculative measure of that hand's value than estimating the price of someone who has lost the hand entirely.

### Assessment Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: אֲמָדוּהוּ אוֹמֶד שֶׁל נִזָּקִין, וְאָמַר ״דְּמֵי עָלַי״, מַהוּ? מִי אָמַר: הָא אֲמָדוּהוּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא, אוֹ דִילְמָא שָׁאנֵי אוּמְדָּנָא דְּבֵי עַשְׂרָה מֵאוּמְדָּנָא דְּבֵי תְלָתָא? אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר: שָׁאנֵי אוּמְדָּנָא דְּבֵי עֲשָׂרָה מֵאוּמְדָּנָא דְּבֵי תְּלָתָא, אָמַר: ״דְּמֵי עָלַי״ וַאֲמָדוּהוּ, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר: ״דְּמֵי עָלַי״, מַהוּ? הָכָא וַדַּאי אֲמָדוּהוּ בֵּי עֲשָׂרָה, אוֹ דִילְמָא שָׁבַח בֵּינֵי וּבֵינֵי? אָמַר: ״דְּמֵי עָלַי״, וְלֹא אֲמָדוּהוּ, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר: ״דְּמֵי עָלַי״, מַהוּ? הָכָא וַדַּאי בְּחַד זִימְנָא אָמְדִינַן לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דִּבְזֶה אַחַר זֶה נָדַר, תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי אָמְדִינַן לֵיהּ? וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר: כֵּיוָן דִּבְזֶה אַחַר זֶה נָדַר תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי אָמְדִינַן לֵיהּ, אָמַר: ״שְׁנֵי דָּמַי עָלַי״ בְּבַת אַחַת, מַהוּ? הָכָא וַדַּאי בְּבַת אַחַת נָדַר, בְּבַת אַחַת אָמְדִינַן לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּקָאָמַר ״שְׁנֵי״, כְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה דָּמֵי? אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר: כֵּיוָן דְּקָאָמַר ״שְׁנֵי״ כְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה דָּמֵי, אֲמָדוּהוּ מֵאֵלָיו מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: הָא אֲמִיד מֵאֵלָיו וְקָאֵי, אוֹ דִלְמָא בָּעֵינַן כַּוָּונָה לְאוּמְדָּנָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna in *Sanhedrin* 2a states that the valuation of a person for the purpose of a vow must be performed by nine judges and one priest. By contrast, the valuation of someone for the purpose of damages is performed by three judges. With this in mind **Rava raises a dilemma:** If one was damaged by another and the court **assesses him** by the **assessment of damages,** i.e., with three judges, **and** then **he says: My assessment** is incumbent **upon me, what is** the *halakha*? **Does** one **say** that as his assessment was already **assessed one time** for damages, this is the amount he must donate? **Or perhaps an assessment** performed **by ten is different from an assessment** performed **by three,** and therefore it is necessary to repeat the assessment with ten judges. **If you say an assessment** performed **by ten is different from an assessment** performed **by three,** then in a case where one **says: My assessment** is incumbent **upon me, and** the court **assessed him, and** then **he again says: My assessment** is incumbent **upon me, what is** the *halakha*? Does one say that **here he was certainly assessed by ten** and there is no need for another assessment, **or perhaps** there is a concern that **in the interim** there might have been **an enhancement** in his assessment? Furthermore, if you say in that case there is a concern that in the interim his assessment might have increased and it is therefore necessary to assess him a second time, then with regard to one who **says: My assessment** is incumbent **upon me, and** the court **did not** yet **assess him, and** then before the court assesses him **he again says: My assessment** is incumbent **upon me, what is** the *halakha*? Do we say that **here certainly** **we assess him once** and he donates twice the value of that single assessment, **or perhaps since he vowed sequentially we assess him twice?** **And if you say** in that case that **since he vowed sequentially we assess him twice,** if **he says at the same time: My assessment** is **twice** incumbent **upon me, what is** the *halakha*? Do we say that **here certainly he vowed at the same time,** and therefore **we assess him once, or perhaps since he says** the word: **Twice, it is** considered **as though** he vowed **sequentially** and therefore he must be assessed twice? Finally, **if you say** that **since he says** the word: **Twice, it is** considered **as though** he vowed **sequentially** and he must be assessed twice, if a court of ten **assessed him on its own,**i.e., not for the purpose of assessing a vow, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say that** he has already been **assessed on his own and** now **stands** before us, and therefore there is no need for another assessment, **or perhaps we require an assessment** to be performed **with intent** for the sake of consecration to the Temple treasury.

The *Gemara* then enters a series of *sha'alot* posed by Rava regarding estimation in *nedarim*. The first: suppose someone had their hand severed and their value was already assessed for *nezikin* purposes — and then they say *demai alai*, pledging their value to the *Beit HaMikdash*. Can that prior assessment be used for the *nedarim* obligation? *Mi amrinan ha amduhu chada zimna* — do we say it has already been assessed once, and that is sufficient? *O dilma shani umdena devei asara me'umdena devei tlata* — or perhaps the two assessments are categorically different, since for *nezikin* a *beit din* of three suffices, while for *nedarim* the *Mishnah* in *Sanhedrin* requires a *beit din* of ten. If so, the assessment performed for *nezikin* cannot discharge the requirement for *nedarim*.

A follow-up question: assuming it does matter that the *nedarim* assessment requires ten — *amar demai alai ve'amduhu vechazar ve'amar demai alai mahu* — if someone was already assessed with a *beit din* of ten for a prior *neder*, and then makes the same *neder* again, do we require a new assessment? *Hacha vadai amduhu bei asara* — this time he certainly was assessed by ten, so perhaps it counts again. *O dilma shevach beini uveini* — or perhaps his value has changed in the intervening period, so the first assessment can no longer serve for the second *neder*.

Yet another question: *amar demai alai velo amduhu vechazar ve'amar demai alai* — if someone made a pledge of their value, was never assessed, and then made the same pledge again before any assessment occurred, must they now be assessed twice? This question probes whether the assessment is purely fact-finding — in which case one determination of the value serves for both *nedarim* — or whether it constitutes a distinct component of the *mitzvah* of *nedarim*, requiring a separate act for each *neder*. *Hacha vadai b'chad zimna amdinan* — perhaps one assessment clearly suffices since the value will be identical. *O dilma keivan d'v'zeh achar zeh nadar trei zimnei amdinan* — or perhaps since two separate *nedarim* were taken, two separate assessments are required.

A further extension: *amar shnei damai alai b'vat achat* — what if someone says "twice my value should be upon me," taking both pledges simultaneously? *Hacha b'vat achat nadar b'vat achat amdinan* — perhaps since it was said at one moment, one assessment suffices. *O dilma keivan d'ka'amar shnei k'zeh achar zeh dami* — or perhaps since the person said *two*, it is legally treated as if the *nedarim* were taken one after the other, requiring two assessments.

### Self-Assessment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשׁוֹט מִיהָא חֲדָא, דִּתְנַן: ״דְּמֵי עָלַי״ וּמֵת — לֹא יִתְּנוּ יוֹרְשִׁין, שֶׁאֵין דָּמִים לַמֵּתִים. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אֲמָדוּהוּ מֵאֵלָיו הָוֵי אוּמְדָּנָא, הָא אֲמִיד וְקָאֵי, מִי אִיכָּא גַּבְרָא דְּלָא שָׁוֵי אַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי? אֲמָדוּהוּ מֵאֵלָיו — נְחֵית לְאוּמְדָּנָא, ״דָּמַי עָלַי״ — לָא נְחֵית לְאוּמְדָּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Resolve at least one** of these dilemmas, **as we learned** in the mishna below: In the case of one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my assessment, and** then **dies,** his **heirs** need **not give** his assessment to the Temple treasury, **as there is no monetary** value **for the dead,** and his assessment at the time of his vow is unknown. The Gemara explains the resolution: **And if it enters your mind** that in a case where a court of ten **assessed him on its own it is** a valid **assessment** with regard to his vow, why does the mishna state that his heirs do not have to donate to the Temple treasury? **Isn't he** considered as **standing assessed** from the outset, as **is there a person who is not worth** at least **four *zuz*?** This proves that an assessment is required to be performed with intent for the sake of consecration to the Temple treasury. The Gemara rejects this resolution: In a case where a court of ten **assessed him on its own** before he took his vow, **he has entered into an assessment,** i.e., an assessment has actually been performed. By contrast, in the case of one who said: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my assessment,** and then dies, **he has not entered into an assessment,** i.e., no actual assessment has been conducted at all.

Finally, the *Gemara* asks: *amduhu me'elav mahu* — what if a person was assessed entirely on their own initiative, not in connection with any *neder*, simply out of curiosity as to their slave-market value, and ten people were present — does this count for a subsequent *neder*? *Mi amrinan ha amid me'elav v'ka'i* — do we say the value is already established? *O dilma ba'inan kavana l'umdana* — or do we require that the assessment be performed with specific *kavanah* for this *neder*? If specific *kavanah* is required, this strongly suggests that assessment is itself part of the *mitzvah* of *nedarim*, not merely a factual inquiry.

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve at least this final question from a *Mishnah*: *damai alai v'met lo yitnu yorshim she'ein damim l'metim* — if someone pledged their value and then died, the heirs need not pay, because *ein damim l'metim* — there is no way to assess the value of a dead person. One might suggest: even if we require *kavanah* for a formal assessment, at least we know that every living person is worth some minimum value on the slave market. The *Gemara* notes *mi ika gavra d'lo shaveh arba zuzei* — is there anyone not worth at least four *zuzim*? So why not make the heirs liable for four *zuzim* as a kind of *umdana me'elav*, an informal assessment derived from general knowledge?

The *Gemara* rejects this proof. *Amduhu me'elav nachit l'umdana* — a person who had their value assessed, even informally, has at least undergone an act of assessment. *Damai alai lo nachit l'umdana* — someone who merely pronounced *demai alai* has performed no assessment whatsoever. General knowledge that no person sells for less than four *zuzim* is not equivalent to an *umdana*. Even if we were to hold that no specific *kavanah* is required, an affirmative act of assessment is still necessary — and that was never done here. Accordingly, the *Gemara* leaves all of these questions unresolved.
