# Arakhin 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Weight Donation

### Pledging Weight

We begin a new *perek* today — Daf 19a — and with it a shift in focus. Although we are still in *Masechet Arakhin*, this *perek* is less concerned with *erchin* proper and deals instead with more traditional *nedarim*: the various ways a person may donate money to the *Beit HaMikdash*. The opening *Mishnah* states: *ha'omer mishkali alai* — if someone says "my weight is upon me," pledging their body weight to the *Beit HaMikdash*, then *noten mishkalo*, they must give their weight. And the *Mishnah* adds: *im kesef kesef, im zahav zahav* — if silver, then silver; if gold, then gold. The *Gemara* will clarify precisely what this means.

### Yarmatya Story

The *Mishnah* then introduces a narrative: *maaseh be'imo shel Yarmatya* — a story involving the mother of someone named Yarmatya, a figure of apparent importance. The mother declared *mishkal biti alai* — the weight of my daughter should be upon me. She went up to Jerusalem, they weighed her daughter, and the court required her to give that weight in gold. The reason this story is brought — and why gold specifically was demanded — will become clear from the *Gemara*'s discussion below.

### Hand Weight Method

The *Mishnah* continues with a further case. If someone says *mishkal yadi alai* — I want to donate the weight of my hand — Rabbi Yehuda says: you fill a barrel completely with water and insert the arm until *marpeko*. *Rashi* understands *marpeko* as the shoulder; *Tosafot* understands it as the elbow, and *Tosafot*'s reading fits more smoothly across the relevant *sugyas* in *Shas*. You observe how much water is displaced, then take donkey flesh, bones, and sinews — *besar chamor va'atzamot vegidin* — and displace the same amount of water. The density of donkey flesh is assumed to be approximately equal to that of human flesh, so you weigh the donkey material needed to reach the same displacement, and that is the weight owed to the *Beit HaMikdash*.

Rabbi Yosi objects: *heich efshar lechaven basar keneged basar, gidim keneged gidim, atzamot keneged atzamot?* — How is it truly possible to match muscle for muscle, sinew for sinew, bone for bone in exact proportion? Rather, says Rabbi Yosi, *shammin et hayad kamah hi re'uyah lishkol* — we simply estimate approximately how much the hand would weigh. The *Mishnah* thus presents both approaches, and the *Gemara* will develop the dispute further.

## Conditional Pledges

### Silver vs. Gold

The *Gemara* opens by asking: *mai im kesef kesef?* — what does the *Mishnah* mean when it says "if silver, silver; if gold, gold"? Rav Yehuda explains: *peireish kesef kesef, peireish zahav zahav* — if you specified that you want to give silver, you must give silver; if you specified gold, you must give gold. The *Gemara* immediately objects: *peshitah* — that is obvious. What new information does the *Mishnah* convey?

The answer is that the *Mishnah* is teaching by implication: *ta'ama depeireish, ha lo peireish patar nafshih bechol dehu* — the reason you must give the specified metal is because you specified it. Had you not specified, you could give your weight in any substance — or rather, in nearly any substance, as Rachba qualifies. Rachba teaches: *ba'asra detakli kufra, patar nafshih afilu bekufra* — in a locale where pitch or tar is sold by weight, you may discharge your obligation by giving your weight in pitch. The substance need not be a precious metal; it must simply be something sold by weight in your locale.

### Pitch & Onions

The *Gemara* again objects: *peshitah* — why shouldn't pitch work if it is sold by weight? The *Gemara* answers: *lo tzerichah de'ika detakal ve'ika dekayal* — the novelty is in a case where some merchants sell pitch by weight and others sell it by volume. One might have thought that since not all merchants sell it by weight it cannot qualify; the teaching tells us it is nonetheless sufficient.

Rav Papa then adds a parallel ruling: *ba'asra detakli shamchei, patar nafshih afilu beshamchei* — in a place where onions are sold by weight, you may discharge your pledge by giving your weight in onions (*shamchei*, as *Rashi* explains, meaning *betzalim*, onions). Again the *Gemara* challenges: *peshitah*. And again the answer identifies the novelty: *debasar deshakli shadu trei platei* — the typical practice when selling onions was to weigh them out and then throw in two or three extra as a bonus, like a baker's dozen. One might have thought that since the transaction is not purely by weight — given the extra portion added — the substance is no longer considered *sold by weight*. The *Gemara* teaches that it nonetheless qualifies.

### Wealthy Assessment

The *Gemara* now returns to the story of Yarmatya's mother. It asks: *maaseh listor?* — is the story coming to contradict what we just said? We explained that if you do not specify a substance you may give anything sold by weight. Yet in the story, Yarmatya's mother did not specify, and the court required her to give gold. The *Gemara* resolves this with *chasuri mechasra vehachi kasani* — our text of the *Mishnah* is incomplete, and here is what it should have read: *ve'im adam chashuv hu, af al gav delo peireish, lefi kevodo amdinan* — if the person is a person of great means, then even if they did not specify, we assess what would be fitting for someone of their standing. The story of Yarmatya's mother is thus brought to illustrate this principle: she was a wealthy woman, she said merely that she was pledging her daughter's weight, and because of her stature the court assessed that the appropriate payment was gold.

## Height & Limbs

### Height Rods

The *Gemara* now takes up a related question. Rav Yehuda asks: what is the *din* if one says *komasi alai* — I want to donate my height? The ruling is: *nosein sharvit she'eino nichpaf* — you must give a rod of the appropriate substance equal to your height, and the rod must be thick enough that it cannot bend. However, if one says *melo komasi alai* — the fullness of my height — then *nosein sharvit hanichpaf*, you may give even a flexible rod.

The *Gemara* challenges this from a *Tosefta*: the *Tosefta* explicitly states that whether you say *komasi alai* or *melo komasi alai*, in both cases you must give a rigid rod — *sharvit she'eino nichpaf*. The *Gemara* resolves this by saying that Rav Yehuda follows the *shita* of Rabbi Akiva, as indicated by the phrase *dedayek lishnei yeseira* — Rabbi Akiva pays close attention to every extra word and interprets it as introducing a meaningful distinction.

We know this principle from a *Mishnah* in *Bava Batra*, in *perek* on selling a house (*perek hamocher et habayit*): *velo et habor velo et hadus* — when selling a house or field, the sale does not automatically include cisterns or cellars, even if the deed specifies dimensions. Rabbi Akiva rules, moreover, that if the seller must now pass through sold land to reach his retained cistern, he must purchase a path back from the buyer — because the path is not assumed to be included in what was retained. The *Chachamim* disagree, holding that since the cistern was not sold, the path necessary to access it was implicitly retained as well.

The part relevant to our case is *modeh Rabbi Akiva bizman she'amar lo chutz me'elu she'ein tzarich likach lo derech* — Rabbi Akiva concedes that if the seller said "I am selling my field with the exception of the cistern," he does not need to repurchase the path, even though he normally would. Why? Because he did not need to specify the exception — under the ordinary rule the cistern is not included. Since he specified it anyway, *alma keivan delo tzarich veka'amar litfuyei milta ka'ati* — since he did not need to say it and he said it regardless, he must have been adding something, and what he was adding was his retention of the path as well. From this we see Rabbi Akiva's general interpretive method: any extra word a person uses carries legal weight.

Applying this to our case: *hacha nami keivan delo tzarich veka'amar litfuyei milta ka'ati* — here too, the person did not need to say *melo komasi*; the ordinary formulation *komasi* would have sufficed. Having added the word *melo*, he must have intended to give something different — namely, the more lenient option of a flexible rod.

### Teiku Cases

The *Gemara* then raises a series of unresolved questions. *Iba'ya lehu*: what is the *din* if someone says *omdi* — my stature? What if they say *rochvi* — my breadth? What about *yeshivati* — the space I occupy when seated? What about *ovyi* — my width? Or *heikefi* — my circumference? The *Gemara* leaves all of these at *teiku* — unresolved. These edge cases may simply be too rare to require definitive resolution.

## Limb Weight

### Displacement Method

We now return to the question of how to weigh a specific limb. The *Gemara* quotes a *baraita*: *tanu rabbanan* — if someone pledges *mishkal yadi alai* or *mishkal ragli alai* — the weight of their hand or foot — Rabbi Yehuda rules as follows. Fill a barrel with water. For the hand, insert the arm *ad ha'atzil*, until the elbow. For the leg, insert it *ad ha'arkuva*, until the knee. Then take donkey flesh, bones, and sinews, and displace that same volume of water — *noten letocho ad she'titmalei*. The donkey material used to achieve equivalent displacement is then weighed, and that is the amount owed.

The *baraita* acknowledges: *ve'af al pi she'ein re'ayah la'davar zecher la'davar* — even though there is no *pasuk* that proves this method, there is a *pasuk* in *Yechezkel* that alludes to it: *asher besar chamorim besaram* — their flesh is like the flesh of donkeys. This verse is taken to confirm that donkey flesh is a reasonable analogue for human flesh in density, making it a valid substitute for this measurement.

Rabbi Yosi objects, as in the *Mishnah*: *hei efshar lechaven basar keneged basar, gidim keneged gidim, atzamot keneged atzamot?* — Can one truly replicate the exact proportional composition of muscle, sinew, and bone? Rabbi Yehuda responds: *umdana* — you estimate where necessary. Rabbi Yosi presses the point: *ad she'umdana yamodu et hayad* — once you have to estimate anyway, simply estimate the total weight of the hand directly, without the elaborate donkey-flesh procedure. Rabbi Yehuda's position, in turn, is *kama de'efshar avdinan bah* — we do everything possible to be precise, and only where an exact match is impossible do we rely on estimation.

### Hand Definition

The *baraita* states that for the hand one inserts the arm *ad ha'atzil*, until the elbow. The *Gemara* challenges this from a *Tosefta* in *Masechet Yadayim*: *kidush yadayim veraglayim bamikdash ad haperek* — when a *kohen* sanctifies his hands in the *Mikdash*, he sanctifies until the *perek*, the wrist. This implies that the halakhic definition of *yad* is only until the wrist, not the elbow.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *binedarim halach achar lashon benei adam* — in *nedarim* we follow not formal halakhic definitions but ordinary speech. When people in those times used the word *yad*, they meant the entire arm up to the elbow, and therefore a pledge using the word *yad* is interpreted accordingly.

But the *Gemara* then asks whether the assumption itself is correct — is it really true that *deoraita* the definition of *yad* extends only to the wrist? After all, regarding *tefillin*, the *pasuk* says *yadcha*, and a *baraita* (*Tana devei Menashe*) teaches: *yadcha zo kiboret* — the Torah's use of *yad* by *tefillin* refers to the bicep. No one wraps *tefillin* on the hand itself; it goes on the upper arm. So the *Gemara* revises its assumption: *deoraita kiboret kulah* — the Torah's definition of *yad* actually extends all the way to the end of the bicep.

The principle *binedarim halach achar lashon benei adam* now works in the opposite direction. Previously, *lashon benei adam* was *machmir* relative to the halakhic definition — people say *yad* and mean more than the wrist. Now it is *mekil* — because the halakhic definition extends to the end of the bicep, while *lashon benei adam* stops at the elbow.

As for the earlier ruling about hand-washing in the *Mikdash* going only to the wrist: *vekidush yadayim veraglayim bamikdash hilchata gemiri lah* — that is a special transmitted tradition, not an expression of the Torah's general definition of *yad*, and does not affect our analysis.

### Foot Definition

Parallel to the hand, the *baraita* states that for the foot one inserts *ad ha'arkuva*, until the knee. The *Gemara* challenges this from a *davar* in the laws of *aliyah leregel*. The *Gemara* derives from the word *regalim* — the pilgrimage festivals, which contains the word *regel* — that *prat lebaalei kabin*: those who walk on wooden feet, i.e., amputees with prosthetics, are exempt from the obligation to appear in Jerusalem and bring the *olat reiyah*. This implies that the definition of *regel* relevant to this *mitzvah* is the foot alone, not the entire leg up to the knee.

The *Gemara* resolves this the same way: *binedarim halach achar lashon benei adam* — in *nedarim* we follow how people speak, and people refer to the entire lower leg up to the knee as their *regel*. It is only for the purpose of *aliyah leregel* that the Torah's narrower definition applies.

### Chalitza Anatomy

The *Gemara* then asks whether it is really accurate that the Torah's definition of *regel* is limited to the foot alone. Is there not the case of *chalitza*, where the *pasuk* reads *vechaltzah na'alo me'al raglo* — she removes his shoe from his *regel* — and a *tanna* teaches that if the *chalitza* shoe was tied anywhere below the knee, the *chalitza* is *keshera bedieved*? This seems to indicate that the Torah's definition of *regel* extends all the way up to the knee, not merely the foot.

The *Gemara* responds: *shani hatam de'amar me'al raglo* — by *chalitza* the *pasuk* adds the word *me'al*, "from above the foot," and it is this extra word that comes to include the calf. One might then ask: if *me'al* expands the definition, why not extend it above the knee as well? The *Gemara* answers: *me'al velo me'al deme'al* — "above," but not above that which is above the foot. The word *me'al* adds one extension above the foot — the calf — but not a second extension beyond that, which would include the thigh. The thigh therefore does not qualify for *chalitza*.

Rav Papa then derives an anatomical observation from this *sugya*. Rav Sheshet notes: *shma mina hai istavira ad ara nachit* — one can infer that the ankle bone extends all the way down to the ground. His reasoning: if the ankle bone ended before reaching the ground, then the ankle itself would be *me'al* the foot, and the calf would be *me'al deme'al*, leaving the calf outside the valid range. Since the *Mishnah* permits *chalitza* up the entire length of the calf, it follows that the ankle must descend all the way to the floor, placing the calf — not the ankle — as the single extension *me'al* the foot.

Rav Ashi disagrees: *afilu teima mafsek pasik* — even if you say the ankle bone does end before the ground, the rule is *kol de'bahadei kara ke'kara dami* — whatever occupies the same horizontal plane as the foot is considered part of the foot. Therefore the ankle, though not physically continuous to the ground, counts as part of the foot because it enters the same horizontal plane. The calf is then *me'al* the foot, and the thigh is *me'al deme'al* — and therefore invalid for *chalitza*.

## Limb Value

### Slave Market Value

The *Mishnah* now addresses a related but distinct case: *de'mei yadai alai* — I pledge the *value* of my hand to the *Beit HaMikdash*, not its weight. The *Mishnah* rules: *shamin oso kama hu shaveh be'yad u'velo yad* — we assess how much he is worth with a hand, how much without, and the difference is the value of the hand. The *Mishnah* notes this is *chomer binedarim mi'be'erchin* — more stringent in *nedarim* than in *erchin*, because saying *erech yadi* — the *erech* of my hand — obligates nothing, since *erchin* only applies to a limb *she'ha'neshama teluya bo*, a limb without which one cannot live. *Demai*, by contrast, assigns real monetary value to the limb.

The *Gemara* asks: *heichi sham'inan* — how precisely do we carry out this assessment? Rav Ada says: *omden oso omed shel nezikin* — we assess him the same way we assess for tort damages. In a *nezikin* case where someone's hand was severed, the assessment involves estimating what the person would have been worth on the slave market with the hand, versus what they are worth now without it, and the difference is the hand's value. This appears to be simply the plain reading of the *Mishnah*.

Abaye challenges this: *mi dami?* — are the cases truly comparable? *Hasam gavra zila hu, hacha gavra shvi'ach hu* — in the *nezikin* case, the person stands before us as he currently is, namely without a hand, and we estimate his value *with* the hand. In the *nedarim* case, the person currently *has* the hand, and we must estimate what he would be worth *without* it. Abaye's concern is that the direction of estimation matters: even if we accept that estimating upward from an incomplete state is reliable, we cannot assume the same reliability when estimating downward from a complete state.

Abaye therefore proposes a different method: *omden kama adam rotze litein be'eved ha'oseh melacha be'yad achat le'oseh bi'shtei yadayim* — we assess how much more a buyer would pay for a slave who works with both hands versus one who works with only one. One might ask: is this not essentially the same as going to the slave market and comparing the prices of people with and without a hand? The *Gemara* raises exactly this point: if we are talking about a slave who is actually missing a hand, *hainu hach* — that is the same method as Rav Ada's. Rather, Abaye specifies: *yado muchtevet lerabo rishon* — we are dealing with a case where one hand of the slave is sold to a different master. One person therefore owns the entire slave except one hand. The differential in market value between owning a complete slave versus owning this slave-minus-one-hand yields, according to Abaye, a more precise and less speculative measure of that hand's value than estimating the price of someone who has lost the hand entirely.

### Assessment Scenarios

The *Gemara* then enters a series of *sha'alot* posed by Rava regarding estimation in *nedarim*. The first: suppose someone had their hand severed and their value was already assessed for *nezikin* purposes — and then they say *demai alai*, pledging their value to the *Beit HaMikdash*. Can that prior assessment be used for the *nedarim* obligation? *Mi amrinan ha amduhu chada zimna* — do we say it has already been assessed once, and that is sufficient? *O dilma shani umdena devei asara me'umdena devei tlata* — or perhaps the two assessments are categorically different, since for *nezikin* a *beit din* of three suffices, while for *nedarim* the *Mishnah* in *Sanhedrin* requires a *beit din* of ten. If so, the assessment performed for *nezikin* cannot discharge the requirement for *nedarim*.

A follow-up question: assuming it does matter that the *nedarim* assessment requires ten — *amar demai alai ve'amduhu vechazar ve'amar demai alai mahu* — if someone was already assessed with a *beit din* of ten for a prior *neder*, and then makes the same *neder* again, do we require a new assessment? *Hacha vadai amduhu bei asara* — this time he certainly was assessed by ten, so perhaps it counts again. *O dilma shevach beini uveini* — or perhaps his value has changed in the intervening period, so the first assessment can no longer serve for the second *neder*.

Yet another question: *amar demai alai velo amduhu vechazar ve'amar demai alai* — if someone made a pledge of their value, was never assessed, and then made the same pledge again before any assessment occurred, must they now be assessed twice? This question probes whether the assessment is purely fact-finding — in which case one determination of the value serves for both *nedarim* — or whether it constitutes a distinct component of the *mitzvah* of *nedarim*, requiring a separate act for each *neder*. *Hacha vadai b'chad zimna amdinan* — perhaps one assessment clearly suffices since the value will be identical. *O dilma keivan d'v'zeh achar zeh nadar trei zimnei amdinan* — or perhaps since two separate *nedarim* were taken, two separate assessments are required.

A further extension: *amar shnei damai alai b'vat achat* — what if someone says "twice my value should be upon me," taking both pledges simultaneously? *Hacha b'vat achat nadar b'vat achat amdinan* — perhaps since it was said at one moment, one assessment suffices. *O dilma keivan d'ka'amar shnei k'zeh achar zeh dami* — or perhaps since the person said *two*, it is legally treated as if the *nedarim* were taken one after the other, requiring two assessments.

### Self-Assessment

Finally, the *Gemara* asks: *amduhu me'elav mahu* — what if a person was assessed entirely on their own initiative, not in connection with any *neder*, simply out of curiosity as to their slave-market value, and ten people were present — does this count for a subsequent *neder*? *Mi amrinan ha amid me'elav v'ka'i* — do we say the value is already established? *O dilma ba'inan kavana l'umdana* — or do we require that the assessment be performed with specific *kavanah* for this *neder*? If specific *kavanah* is required, this strongly suggests that assessment is itself part of the *mitzvah* of *nedarim*, not merely a factual inquiry.

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve at least this final question from a *Mishnah*: *damai alai v'met lo yitnu yorshim she'ein damim l'metim* — if someone pledged their value and then died, the heirs need not pay, because *ein damim l'metim* — there is no way to assess the value of a dead person. One might suggest: even if we require *kavanah* for a formal assessment, at least we know that every living person is worth some minimum value on the slave market. The *Gemara* notes *mi ika gavra d'lo shaveh arba zuzei* — is there anyone not worth at least four *zuzim*? So why not make the heirs liable for four *zuzim* as a kind of *umdana me'elav*, an informal assessment derived from general knowledge?

The *Gemara* rejects this proof. *Amduhu me'elav nachit l'umdana* — a person who had their value assessed, even informally, has at least undergone an act of assessment. *Damai alai lo nachit l'umdana* — someone who merely pronounced *demai alai* has performed no assessment whatsoever. General knowledge that no person sells for less than four *zuzim* is not equivalent to an *umdana*. Even if we were to hold that no specific *kavanah* is required, an affirmative act of assessment is still necessary — and that was never done here. Accordingly, the *Gemara* leaves all of these questions unresolved.
