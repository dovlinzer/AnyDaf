# Kiddushin 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Converts & Yichus

### Opening & Daf

Today's daf is 68, though we begin at the very end of 67b, in the last three narrow lines of that amud.

### Mishnah: Yichus Cases

The *mishnah* under discussion addresses four distinct categories regarding *yichus* — lineage and personal status — examining how the status of children is transmitted when parties of differing legal standing attempt to marry one another. The *mishnah* uses rather striking descriptive language to organize these cases, grouping them by the nature of the union and the resulting *yichus* of the offspring.

### Restricted Nations

One of the cases treated by the *mishnah* concerns individuals who have converted from nations subject to special restrictions on their marital prospects. The nations in question are *Mitzri* (Egyptian), *Ammoni* (Ammonite), and *Moavi* (Moabite). These groups are permitted to marry converts and *mamzerim*, but face a higher standard before they may enter into the fully accepted Jewish community by marriage. The principle stated is *nitgayru, halach achar hapagum shebishneihem* — when both parties convert, one follows the most *pagum* (flawed or restricted) status between the two of them.

The *gemara* immediately asks: *bimai* — what case exactly are we discussing? If we say it is a *Mitzri* man who married an *Amonit* woman, there is a problem: the restriction for Ammonites, as established by *Torah she-be'al peh* and as the background for the entire discussion in *Masechet Yadayim* and for much of *Sefer Rut*, applies only to males (*Amoni velo Amonit*). The female Ammonite is not restricted from marrying into the Jewish people at all — which is precisely why *Rut* was able to marry *Boaz* without any halachic difficulty. Therefore, if an Egyptian man married an Ammonite woman, there is no competing *pagum* on her side, and the formulation *hapagum shebishneihem* — the more restricted of the two — cannot apply.

Rather, the *gemara* redirects: *ela be'Ammoni shenasa Mitzrit* — we are discussing a male Ammonite convert who married a female Egyptian convert. Here, the husband's restriction as an *Ammoni* is perpetual for male descendants, while the wife's restriction as a *Mitzrit* is not gender-specific and applies for three generations. The *gemara* then clarifies how the *mishnah*'s rule operates: *ve'im zachar havei, hai shadia batrei didei* — if they have a male child, that child follows the father's status, meaning the child is treated as a male Ammonite convert, whose restriction is perpetual. *Ve'im nekeivah hava, shadia batar didah* — if they have a female child, that daughter follows the mother's status, treated as a second-generation *Mitzrit*. The children are thus allocated by gender to the respective parental status — not a simple "take the worst of both" rule, but a gender-based bifurcation of *yichus*.

### Three Generations

One might ask: does the three-generation restriction for an Egyptian convert date from *Matan Torah*, applying only to those three generations after the Exodus? The traditional understanding, however, as reflected in *halacha*, is that *mitzvot* are binding for all generations. Any *Mitzri* who converts at any time in history still faces a three-generation barrier before full integration into the mainstream Jewish community. The convert's children are born Jewish, but are second-generation *Mitzri*; their children are third-generation *Mitzri*; and only thereafter are the descendants fully unrestricted. That represents a span of perhaps seventy-five years — a significant ongoing tracking of *yichus*.

## Mamzerut & Kiddushin

### Mamzer Definition

The *mishnah* now introduces the third category: *kol she-ein lah alav kiddushin* — any woman for whom there is no *kiddushin* possible with this particular man, but who could in principle marry someone else — *ha-valad mamzer*. The child of such a union is a *mamzer*.

It is important to emphasize at the outset what *mamzerut* is and is not. A child born out of wedlock to two people who are halachically permitted to marry one another is not a *mamzer*. *Mamzerut* is fundamentally linked — at least according to most *tanna'im* — to the prohibition itself upon the marriage. The connection between the prohibited nature of the union and the *mamzer* status of the offspring is what the *gemara* now seeks to establish.

### Four Schools

There is a four-way *machloket* among *tanna'im* on this question. *Rabbi Shimon HaTimni* holds that *mamzerut* arises specifically from *chayavei krisos* — those forbidden relationships (*arayot*) for which the Torah prescribes *karet* or *mitat beit din* (capital punishment). In those cases, the *kiddushin* does not take effect and the children are *mamzerim*. *Rabbi Akiva* takes a far more expansive position: *chayavei lavin* — any prohibited relationship backed by a negative commandment — likewise produces *mamzerut*, because *kiddushin* cannot take hold. *Rabbi Yehoshua* is actually narrower than *Rabbi Shimon HaTimni*, holding that only those *arayot* punishable with *mitat beit din* — actual capital crimes — generate *mamzerut*; *karet* alone is insufficient.

### Sexual Relation

An important clarification: in order for a child to be a *mamzer*, there must be an actual sexual relationship. A child conceived through *in vitro* fertilization or artificial insemination, even from a prohibited union, would not be a *mamzer* in the same sense. This is precisely the axis of a famous dispute among *poskim* regarding advanced reproductive technologies. *Rav Moshe Feinstein* was the permissive opinion on artificial insemination and *in vitro* fertilization, marshaling many *mekorot* in his argument. The *Satmar Rav* was strongly opposed, maintaining that a doctor administering such procedures might be regarded as the active male party in the fertilization and thus the act could be considered adulterous. The consensus among contemporary authorities has generally followed *Rav Moshe*'s reasoning.

### Divorce Pasuk

The *gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — what is the source for the rule that prohibited unions produce *mamzerut*? *Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*, transmitted through *Rabbi Yannai* and *Rabbi Acha bereih de-Rabba* in the name of *Rabbi Yossi HaGlili*, that the source is the *pasuk* concerning divorce: *ve-yatzah mi-beisah ve-halchah ve-haysah le-ish acher* — the divorced woman leaves and may go become the wife of another man. The *drasha* derives: *le'acherim velo likrovim* — she may marry others, but not close relatives of her ex-husband. This teaches not merely a prohibition but a substantive legal consequence: the *kiddushin* would not take effect at all between a divorced woman and her ex-husband's relatives.

### Rabbi Abba Query

*Matkif lah Rabbi Abba* — Rabbi Abba challenges this derivation. Why not read the *pasuk* more narrowly: she may marry someone else, but specifically not this man's *son* — not *krovim* in general? The *gemara* answers: the prohibition against a son marrying his father's ex-wife is stated explicitly elsewhere — *lo yikach ish et eshet aviv* — and that *pasuk* already teaches that the *kiddushin* would not work in that case. Therefore the phrase *le-ish acher* must be serving an additional purpose, and from it we derive *le'acherim velo likrovim* — she may marry other people generally, but not his relatives.

The *gemara* then considers: perhaps both *pesukim* refer to the son, one teaching the *lechatchila* prohibition and the other the *bedi'eved* invalidity of any attempted *kiddushin*? The response is that we already know from *achot isha* — a wife's sister — that the prohibition itself is established. Therefore the second *pasuk* must be extending the rule of invalid *kiddushin* to *krovim* more broadly.

### Achot Isha Paradigm

*Achot isha* — the wife's sister — becomes the paradigmatic case for the inability of *kiddushin* to take effect among *arayot*. This is the same *achot isha* that appears at the very opening of *Yevamot* and forms the basis for the exemption from *yibum* in the case of *tzarat ervah*. Here it serves as the foundation for the broader rule: just as one cannot legally marry his wife's sister — not merely that it is prohibited but that any attempted *kiddushin* is legally null — so too should the same apply across the spectrum of *arayot*.

### Kal Vachomer

From *achot isha*, the *gemara* attempts a *kal vachomer*: if *achot isha*, which carries the punishment of *karet*, disables *kiddushin*, then *chayavei mitat beit din* — those *arayot* punishable with capital punishment, which are in some ways more severe — should certainly (*lo kol shekein*) also disable *kiddushin*. One questioner raises the point that *achot isha* becomes permitted after the death of the wife, which might seem to reduce the severity of that prohibition. Yet this observation applies specifically to the *yesh la heter b'makom mitzvah* discussion that follows and does not fully undermine the *kal vachomer* as presented here.

### Kal Vachomer Weak

The *kal vachomer* is not without its difficulties. One might argue that the reason the Torah prescribes a harsher punishment for certain *arayot* is precisely because *kiddushin does* take effect there — the severity of the violation is greater because a genuine legal bond was formed. That is, there is no clear intuition about whether it is more *chamur* that *kiddushin* should work or that it should not work. The relationship between severity of punishment and validity of *kiddushin* is not self-evident, and this undercuts the *kal vachomer*.

### Binyan Av

The *gemara* therefore pivots to a *binyan av* from *achot isha*: just as *achot isha* is distinguished by the fact that she is an *ervah*, the violation carries *karet*, and a *korban chatat* if done *be-shogeg* — and *kiddushin* does not take effect — so too any *ervah* sharing those same features (punishment of *karet*, *chatat* for inadvertent violation) should likewise disable *kiddushin*. The formulation is: *mah achot isha miyuchedet she-hi ervah ve-chayavin al zdonah karet ve-shigegata chatat lo tafsei bah kiddushin, af kol she-hi ervah ve-chayavin al zdonah karet ve-shigegata chatat lo tafsei bah kiddushin*.

### Exceptions

Two cases threaten to break this *binyan av*: *eshet ish* (a married woman) and *eshet ach* (a brother's wife). The *gemara* articulates why each is different.

Regarding *eshet ach*: the prohibition against a brother's wife is in a different category because there exists a situation in which marrying her is not merely permitted but obligatory — namely *yibum*, the levirate marriage commanded when a brother dies childless. This *heter b'makom mitzvah* marks *eshet ach* as qualitatively distinct from other *arayot* and prevents us from using the *binyan av* to conclude that *kiddushin* with her never takes effect.

Regarding *eshet ish*: this prohibition can be lifted during the lifetime of the one who created the *issur* — the husband — through divorce. This *yesh lah heter b'chayei osra* makes *eshet ish* a lesser level of *ervah* in one respect, since there is a mechanism for removal. Therefore the *binyan av* from *achot isha* — which has no such escape mechanism in the normal case — cannot straightforwardly extend to *eshet ish*. Notably, the *acharonim* observe that this entire analysis frames the problem of marrying a married woman in terms of *ervah* — the relational prohibition — rather than from the *kinyan* perspective of whether a woman can be legally acquired by two men simultaneously. This appears to stand in some tension with material treated elsewhere in the tractate and generates significant discussion among the *acharonim*.

### Rabbi Yona Drasha

*Rabbi Yona* — or as the *gemara* alternatively identifies him, *Rav Huna bereih de-Rav Yehoshua* — proposes an entirely different approach. Rather than a *binyan av*, he derives the rule from the concluding *pasuk* of the *parasha* of *arayot*: *kol asher ya'aseh mikol ha-to'evot ha-eleh ve-nichreitu* — whoever does any of these abominations shall receive *karet*. This *pasuk* creates a *hekesh* — a textual equation — linking all of the *arayot* together with *achot isha*: *hukshu kol ha-arayot kulan le-achot isha*. Just as *achot isha* disables *kiddushin*, so too, by virtue of this encompassing equation, all *arayot* similarly disable *kiddushin*.

## Niddah & Sotah

### Niddah Question

The *binyan av* and the *hekesh* of *Rabbi Yona* both confront a striking difficulty: *i hachi afilu niddah nami* — if this logic holds, then it should also apply to *niddah*. A *niddah* — a menstruant woman — appears in the list of *arayot* and her violation carries *karet*. The *hava amina* would therefore be that if one performed *kiddushin* with an unmarried woman while she is a *niddah*, the *kiddushin* would not take effect and any child born of that relationship would be a *mamzer*.

This is a significant challenge. *Tosafot*, already in the context of the first *daf* of *Yevamot*, grapples with the same issue in a parallel form: if *niddah* is among the *arayot*, then if one of the *tzarot* of the deceased brother was in a state of *niddah* at the time of his death, she might count as a *tzarat ervah*, thereby exempting all the *co-wives* from *yibum*. *Tosafot* resolves this, and *Tosafot HaRosh* on *Yevamot daf 2* goes further, arguing that *niddah* is not fundamentally called an *ervah* in the same sense as the other *arayot*. A similar point is raised by the *Tosafot* in *Sanhedrin* regarding why the laws of *yichud* do not apply with equal stringency between married partners during *niddah* as they do in other *arayot* contexts. The *Brisker Rav*, in his commentary on the *Rambam*, and several other *acharonim* develop the distinction between *niddah* and other *arayot* into a fundamental categorical difference.

One might also note that using *achot isha* as the paradigmatic *ervah* for this entire *drasha* invites a similar objection: *achot isha* becomes permitted upon the death of the wife, which arguably makes it less severe than other *arayot*. The *gemara* does address the analogous concern somewhat earlier, in the framework of *ein lah heter b'chayei osra* vs. *yesh lah heter b'chayei osra*, but *Rabbi Yona*'s formulation reopens the same question and the *gemara* does not fully resolve it.

The case of *kiddushin* with a *niddah* that *Rashi* addresses is *im kidesh pnuya bimei nidutah* — what if a man performed *kiddushin* with an unmarried woman during her period of *niddah*? The *hava amina* is that *kiddushin* would not take effect and the child would be a *mamzer*. Separately, there is also the question of *chuppat niddah* — what happens if the *nisuin* ceremony takes place while the woman is in *niddah*? Most *rishonim* do not view this as a fundamental problem for the *nisuin* itself. The *Rambam* cites an opinion that the *nisuin* does not take full effect and must be redone, though in practice this is addressed through various means — principally by ensuring that the couple avoid *yichud* until after *niddah* concludes.

### Agreement Niddah

*Amar Abaye*: *hakol modim beva'al ha-niddah ve-al ha-sotah she-ein ha-valad mamzer* — everyone agrees that in the case of *niddah* and *sotah*, the child is not a *mamzer*. This means that *Rabbi Shimon HaTimni*, despite holding that all *chayavei krisos* produce *mamzerut*, agrees that *niddah* is an exception. And *Rabbi Akiva*, despite his sweeping position regarding *chayavei lavin*, agrees that *sotah* is an exception. *Sotah* here refers to the situation where a woman has had an adulterous relationship and her husband subsequently has relations with her — that relationship is an *issur lav* for the husband, yet the child of that relationship is not a *mamzer*.

In cases where adultery has occurred and a child is born shortly thereafter, the *chazakah* of *rov beilos achar ha-ba'al* — that most marital relations are with the husband — generally serves to prevent the child from being declared a *mamzer*.

### Chizkiyah Drasha

*Amar Chizkiyah*: the exemption of *niddah* is derived from a specific *pasuk*. The verse *u-tehi nidatah alav* describes the consequence for one who has relations with a *niddah* — he acquires the same seven-day impurity she carries. *Chizkiyah* derives from the word *u-tehi* — the root *lihyot*, which the Torah elsewhere uses to denote the establishment of marriage — that even during her period of *niddah*, *havayah* (a marriage bond) can be established. The *pasuk* thus explicitly permits *kiddushin* to take hold despite the prohibition of *niddah*, notwithstanding the *karet* penalty.

### Chumra Chumra Rule

The *gemara* asks: given that we have a *hekesh* connecting all *arayot* to one paradigm, why did we choose to connect them all to *achot isha* rather than to *niddah*? If we connected them to *niddah*, the result would be permissive — *kiddushin* could take effect even with *arayot*. We could construct an argument in either direction, so why favor the stricter outcome?

The *gemara* answers: *kula b'chumra l'chumra makshinan* — when a *hekesh* can be drawn in either direction, we always draw it toward the stricter (*machmir*) option. This is presented as a methodological rule of *drashot*. The *gemara* does not appear to ground this in the general principle of *safek de'oraita l'chumra*; rather, it seems to be an independent rule governing how textual equations are resolved when there is a choice. The *gemara* suggests it as a rule worth flagging, though its consistent application across *Shas* is a question worth investigating further.

### Rav Acha Kal V.

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* offers a different approach altogether: the exclusion of *niddah* can be derived through *kal vachomer* from *yevamah*. A *yevamah* — a widow required to undergo *yibum* — who goes and attempts to marry a stranger faces only an *issur lav*, the least severe category of prohibition, yet her *kiddushin* with that stranger does not take effect. This is because of the principle of *yesh zikah* — the bond between the *yavam* and *yevamah* begins automatically at the moment of the brother's death, creating something almost akin to a proto-*eshet ish*. If even an *issur lav* can disable *kiddushin*, then *chayavei mitat beit din* and *chayavei krisos* certainly should as well — *lo kol shekein*. 

The *gemara* then challenges: *i hachi she'ar chayavei lavin nami* — if this *kal vachomer* holds, then all other *chayavei lavin* should equally disable *kiddushin*, which would force us to rule like *Rabbi Akiva* that *kiddushin* never takes effect by *chayavei lavin* — a conclusion rejected by the *rabbanan*.

### Two Wives Pasuk

*Amar Rav Papa*: *chayavei lavin* are explicitly proven to allow *kiddushin* to take hold from a *pasuk* in *Parshat Ki Teitzei* — *ki tihyena le-ish shtei nashim ha-achat ahuvah ve-ha-achat senuah* — when a man has two wives, one beloved and one hated. Since God is described as designating one wife as *ahuvah* and one as *senuah*, and we do not say that God plays favorites among permissible relationships, this must mean that the *senuah* wife represents a marriage that was halachically disfavored — i.e., a prohibited relationship — yet the *kiddushin* nonetheless took effect. The *pasuk* thus explicitly contemplates a case where *kiddushin* is valid despite a prohibition: *ahuvah b'nisueha* and *senuah b'nisueha* — one whose marriage is favored in the eyes of heaven and one whose marriage is not, yet both are married.

### Akiva vs Rabbanan

The *rabbanan* — those who disagree with *Rabbi Akiva* — understand this *pasuk* as providing explicit proof that *kiddushin* is effective with *chayavei lavin*. *Rabbi Akiva*, however, holds that *kiddushin* never takes hold by *chayavei lavin*. To what situation, then, does he apply this *pasuk*? *Rabbi Akiva* must restrict it to the case of *almanah le-Kohen Gadol* — a widow marrying the High Priest — which involves not a *lav* but an *issur asei*. The High Priest is commanded *ki im betulah me-amav yikach ishah* — he must marry a virgin from his people. Marrying a widow therefore constitutes a *lav ha-ba mi-chlal asei*, a prohibition derived from a positive commandment, which is regarded as a lower-level prohibition. According to *Rabbi Akiva*, this is the only case in which *kiddushin* takes effect despite a prohibition — because it is merely an *issur asei*.

### Four Categories

At this point the full analytic structure comes into view. There are four categories of prohibited relationships: *issur asei* (positive commandment violation), *chayavei lavin* (negative commandment violations), *chayavei karet*, and *chayavei mitat beit din*. The positions of the *tanna'im* can be charted accordingly. *Rabbi Akiva* holds there is no valid *kiddushin* for *chayavei lavin*, *chayavei karet*, and *chayavei mitat beit din*, but *kiddushin* is effective for *issur asei*. *Rabbi Shimon HaTimni* holds that *kiddushin* is disabled for *chayavei karet* and *chayavei mitat beit din* only. *Rabbi Yehoshua* restricts this further to *chayavei mitat beit din* alone.

In addition, various exceptions apply within these categories. *Yevamah*, despite being merely an *issur lav*, disables *kiddushin* — universally agreed upon. *Eshet ach* has a special *heter* in the context of the *mitzvah* of *yibum*. *Eshet ish* can be released from her status through divorce during the husband's lifetime. *Niddah*, despite carrying *karet*, is universally agreed to allow *kiddushin* to take effect. And *sotah* is universally agreed not to produce *mamzerut* even under *Rabbi Akiva*'s expansive framework.

### Rabbi Simai

*De-tani Rabbi Simai*: Rabbi Simai articulates what he understands *Rabbi Akiva*'s full position to be. *Min hakol hayah Rabbi Akiva oseh mamzer chutz me-almanah le-Kohen Gadol* — *Rabbi Akiva* creates *mamzerut* from all prohibited relationships, with one exception: *almanah le-Kohen Gadol*. The reason is that the Torah explicitly uses the word *lo yechalel* — "he shall not profane" — indicating that in this case the result is *chillul*, a disqualification to *kehunah* status, producing a *chalal* rather than a *mamzer*.

The category of *chalal* is significant. The child of a *kohen* who marries a woman he is forbidden to marry becomes a *chalal* — not a full *kohen*, but not a *mamzer* either. A *chalal* cannot serve in the Temple and cannot eat *terumah*, but may otherwise marry any Jewish woman he wishes. Furthermore, there is a path back: a *chalal*'s daughter who marries a legitimate *kohen* can have sons who are *kohanim* once again. This contrasts sharply with *mamzerut*, from which there is essentially no exit — at least not in a world without slavery, where the one technical mechanism of exit no longer functions.

The *gemara* also notes that this discussion extends naturally to a regular *kohen* who marries a *gerushah* (divorced woman). The child of such a union, on the same logic, would be a *chalal* — a step down from *kohen*, but not as catastrophic as *mamzer*. This distinction — that violations within the priestly framework produce *chalal* status rather than *mamzer* status — is important both practically and conceptually.

### Rabbi Shaveiv

*Rabbi Shaveiv* disagrees sharply with *Rabbi Simai*'s account of *Rabbi Akiva*'s position. *De-amar bo unitzvach al Rabbi Akiva* — let us go and protest loudly at *Rabbi Akiva*, who said *kol she-ein lo biah be-Yisrael ha-valad mamzer* — anyone whom you are forbidden to have relations with, the child is a *mamzer*. *Rabbi Shaveiv* contends that *Rabbi Akiva*'s position creates *mamzerut* even in the case of *almanah le-Kohen Gadol*, without the exception that *Rabbi Simai* introduced. The implication is that *Rabbi Akiva*'s rule, as *Rabbi Shaveiv* understands it, is sweeping and unqualified — and *Rabbi Shaveiv* finds this alarming, as it dramatically expands the population of *mamzerim*. The *gemara* notes this objection carries an almost rhetorical, polemical character — this is a *beit midrash* in which strong disagreement is sometimes expressed with open exasperation, as we have seen elsewhere, such as *Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish*'s famous outburst about the *hekesh* of *havayah le-yitziah*.

The question of how many *Kohanim Gedolim* exist at any given time is relevant here: the *Kohen Gadol* is a singular figure, and the case of *almanah le-Kohen Gadol* affects a vanishingly small number of people. It is therefore striking that this particular case becomes the crux of the dispute between *Rabbi Simai* and *Rabbi Shaveiv*.

### Reconciliation

The *gemara* then works through the reconciliation. If *Rabbi Shaveiv* intends to refute *Rabbi Simai*, his approach is coherent — he is arguing that even *almanah le-Kohen Gadol* produces *mamzerut* and that *Rabbi Simai*'s exception is wrong. But if *Rabbi Shaveiv* is stating his own independent position, what is the *issur asei* case he would apply the *pasuk* of *ki tihyena* to?

The resolution involves a careful re-examination of which category the prohibition of *almanah le-Kohen Gadol* properly belongs to. According to *Rabbi Simai*, *Rabbi Akiva*'s one exception applies to *almanah le-Kohen Gadol* — which involves a *chayavei lav*-level prohibition — and therefore the exception operates within *chayavei lavin*. But the *gemara* ultimately concludes that *Rabbi Simai*'s exception is better understood as applying only to *issur asei*, not to *chayavei lavin* proper.

The deeper point about *issur asei* is that it is an extremely weak level of prohibition — *bitul asei* is the least severe form of violation in the rabbinic hierarchy — and accordingly the case of a *beulah le-Kohen Gadol*, a non-virgin who marries the High Priest, is the paradigm of *issur asei* here. Even *Rabbi Akiva* agrees that *kiddushin* takes effect in such a case and no *mamzerut* results. The *rabbanan*, for their part, apply *ki tihyena* broadly to *chayavei lavin*, establishing that *kiddushin* is effective across that entire category. The contrast between *Rabbi Akiva* and the *rabbanan* thus turns on whether *chayavei lavin* falls on the side of valid or invalid *kiddushin*, with *Rabbi Akiva* placing it in the invalid column and the *rabbanan* placing it in the valid column — and the *pasuk* of *ki tihyena* being interpreted accordingly.

The *sotah* exception under *Rabbi Akiva* similarly remains in place: even according to *Rabbi Akiva*, *sotah* — the adulterous woman whose husband subsequently has relations with her — does not produce *mamzerut*, because the *issur* in her case is specifically constructed around the marital relationship itself and carries its own textual basis for exemption.

## Shifcha & Nochrit

### No Kiddushin Ever

The *mishnah* delineates a third major category: *kol mi she-ein lah alav lo kiddushin ve-lo al acherim kiddushin ha-valad k'motah* — any woman for whom there is no possibility of *kiddushin* with this man and no possibility of *kiddushin* with any Jewish man. The two paradigmatic examples are *shifcha Kenaanit* (a Canaanite slave woman) and *nochrit* (a non-Jewish woman). In both cases the child follows the mother's status.

### Shifcha Donkey

*Amar Rav Huna*: the derivation that a *shifcha Kenaanit* cannot contract *kiddushin* is learned from the *pasuk* in *Bereishit* in which *Avraham*, on his way to the *Akeidah*, instructs *Eliezer* and his companion: *shevu lachem po im ha-chamor* — stay here with the donkey. The *midrash* reads *im ha-chamor* as *am ha-domeh la-chamor* — a people similar to a donkey. Since slaves do not have *kiddushin*, they are compared, in this *drasha*, to animals. This is a strikingly harsh comparison, and the *gemara* is clearly aware of the gravity of this formulation. It must be situated within a broader legal framework in which the status of an *eved Kenaani* (Canaanite slave) is treated in certain respects as analogous to property rather than as a fully autonomous legal person with respect to personal-status law. Similar *drashot* appear elsewhere — for example, the discussion in *Avodah Zarah* about whether a non-Jewish corpse conveys *tumah be-ohel*, derived from *adam ki yamut be-ohel* with the *drasha atem k'ruyim adam ve-ein ovdei avodah zarah k'ruyim adam* — and in each case the *gemara* uses a sharp formulation to establish a specific legal category.

### Mother's Status

The *gemara* establishes that *kiddushin* does not apply to a slave. But from where do we know that the child of a *shifcha Kenaanit* follows the mother's status? From the *pasuk*: *ha-ishah vi-yladeha tihyeh le-adoneha* — the woman and her children shall belong to her master. This verse appears in the context of an *eved Ivri* (Hebrew slave) who marries a *shifcha Kenaanit* that his master provided for him; when the *eved Ivri* goes free, his children from the *shifcha Kenaanit* remain with the master as slaves. The explicit language of the *pasuk* establishes that the children follow the mother's status — they remain enslaved because she is a slave. The economic logic here is plain: the capacity to have one's slaves procreate was, within the institution of slavery, a means of generating additional slaves without capture or purchase.

### Non-Jew Marriage

*Nochrit minalan* — how do we know that a non-Jewish woman cannot contract *kiddushin* with a Jewish man and that her children follow her status? *Amar kra lo titchaten bam* — you shall not intermarry with them. The *gemara* derives from *lo titchaten bam* that *kiddushin* cannot take effect with non-Jews.

However, *Tosafot* raises an objection: the *pasuk* of *lo titchaten bam* is specifically addressed to the seven Canaanite nations — the *Zayin Amamim* — for whom there is a distinct and heightened prohibition rooted in their extreme devotion to idolatry. *Rashi* in a long comment further down in the *sugya* — when he discusses *Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai*'s approach of *darish ta'ama de-kra* — distinguishes between these seven nations and other non-Jews precisely on this basis, citing the *gemara* in *Chullin*: *umot she-b'chutz la-aretz lav ovdei avodat kochavim heim, ela minhag avoteihem b'yadeihem* — non-Jews outside of *Eretz Yisrael* are not considered genuine idol worshippers; they simply follow inherited custom. The seven Canaanite nations, by contrast, were deeply and genuinely committed to *avodah zarah*, which is the underlying reason for the heightened prohibition against marrying them. *Tosafot* leverages this distinction in several places to permit closer commercial and social relations with medieval European Christians.

### Severs Jewishness

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai*: the derivation for the children's status follows the mother is taken from *ki yasir et bincha me-acharei* — for he will turn your son away from Me. The *pasuk* addresses the case of a Jewish man who marries a non-Jewish woman: the non-Jewish father-in-law will lead the Jewish son astray into idolatry. From this the *gemara* derives: *bincha ha-ba mi-Yisraelit karuy bincha* — your son born from a Jewish woman is called your son, *ve-ein bincha ha-ba min ha-ovedet kochavim karuy bincha* — but your son born from a non-Jewish woman is not called your son. One generation down, the grandchildren of a Jewish man through a non-Jewish woman are no longer recognized as his children within the Jewish people.

*Rabbi Yishmael* draws a further inference: *ben bitcha ha-ba min ha-oved kochavim karuy bincha* — the son of your daughter born from a non-Jewish man is called your son. That is, the grandchildren of a Jewish woman, even through a non-Jewish father, are still Jewish. The *gemara* then notes that *Ravina* holds that a child born from a non-Jewish man or a slave who had relations with a Jewish woman is not fully *kasher* — *nehi de-kasher lo havi, mamzer lo havi* — meaning the child is Jewish but carries a disqualification (*pasul*), specifically being *pasul le-kehunah*, ineligible for the priesthood. The child is neither fully unrestricted nor a *mamzer*.

### All Nations

The *gemara* presses the point that *lo titchaten bam* applies specifically to the seven Canaanite nations. *She'ar umot minalan* — what is the source for extending the rule to all other nations? *Amar kra ki yasir et bincha lerabot kol ha-mesirim* — the phrase *ki yasir*, "for he will lead astray," is understood to encompass anyone who would draw a Jewish child away from the Jewish people. Any non-Jewish woman who marries a Jewish man creates the same risk, and therefore the same legal consequence — invalid *kiddushin* and children following the mother — extends to all nations.

### Drasha vs Pshat

*Hanicha le-Rabbi Shimon de-darish ta'ama de-kra* — this extension works according to *Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai* because he applies *darish ta'ama de-kra*, interpreting the contextual purpose and meaning of the verse and applying it broadly to all cases that share the same underlying rationale. *Ela le-Rabbanan* — the *rabbanan*, however, do not employ *darish ta'ama de-kra*; they do not generalize from the contextual reason of a verse to expand its legal application. They therefore require a separate source.

### Yefat Toar

For the *rabbanan*, the source is the *pasuk* describing *yefat to'ar* — the captive woman taken in war — which states *ve-achar ken tavo eleha u-ve'altah*, "and only afterward may you come to her and marry her." The phrase *ve-achar ken* — only after a certain process — implies that initially, before this process is completed, *kiddushin* cannot take effect with her. *Michlal de-mei-ikara lo tafsei bah kiddushin* — it is implicit that at the outset she was not subject to *kiddushin*. This establishes, for the *rabbanan*, that *kiddushin* does not apply to a non-Jewish woman.

### Concluding Rule

How do we then know that the child follows the mother? From the *pasuk*: *ki tihyena le-ish ve-yaldu lo* — when a man's wives give birth to him children. *Ke-heicha de-karinan bei ki tihyena karinan bei ve-yaldu lo* — wherever we apply the rule of *ki tihyena* (i.e., wherever a valid marriage bond exists), we also apply *ve-yaldu lo* (the children are attributed to the father). And wherever we cannot apply *ki tihyena* — wherever *kiddushin* does not take effect — the children follow the mother instead.

This concluding principle unifies the entire discussion: *kiddushin* validity and paternal attribution of children are two sides of the same coin. Wherever *kiddushin* works, children follow the father. Wherever it does not, children follow the mother. This is the foundational rule underlying the *mishnah*'s entire framework of *yichus* in these cases.
