# Beitzah 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wood from Field/Karpef

### Muktzah & Wood Theme

Daf 31 returns to one of the dominant themes of this *perek*: gaining access to food and materials that have been set aside, with the ever-present specter of *muktzah* hovering over nearly every discussion. The *mishnah* opens with a relatively accessible case: *mevi'im etzim min hasadeh min hamikhunas* — one may bring wood from the field if it has been gathered into a pile. Since the wood has been collected and stacked, it is evident that the owner intends to use it for firewood or a similar purpose, and it therefore does not carry *muktzah* status. The *mishnah* adds: *umin hakarfaf* — one may also bring wood from a *karfaf*, which, as we shall see, is a fenced enclosure closer in character to a courtyard than to a distant open field.

### Karpef Definition

A *karfaf* is not simply any outdoor space. It occupies an intermediate category — neither one's home nor a remote agricultural field. It is a fenced enclosure that bears enough of a relationship to the domestic sphere to render its contents more readily "on one's radar screen." This concept will be immediately familiar to anyone who learned the relevant *sugya* in Shabbat, where *karfaf* featured prominently in the laws of *hukaf ledira*. The *mishnah* further specifies that if the wood is brought from a *karfaf*, it is permitted *afilu min hamifuzarin* — even if the sticks are scattered and not gathered into a pile — since the nature of the *karfaf* itself suffices to establish that these items are intended for human use.

### R. Yehuda vs. R. Yosi

The *mishnah* then raises the definitional question: *eizu karfaf*? Rabbi Yehuda says: *kol shesamuch la'ir* — any enclosure that is near the city qualifies as a *karfaf*. Rabbi Yosi, by contrast, introduces an additional or alternative criterion: *kol shenichnasin lo bepotachat* — any enclosure that one enters with a key, meaning it is fenced and locked. Rabbi Yosi adds that this definition applies *afilu betoch techum Shabbat* — even if the enclosure is situated at the far edge of the *techum Shabbat*, which on *Yom Tov* extends two thousand *amot* from the city. The force of Rabbi Yosi's position is that even an enclosure located as far away as one may travel on *Yom Tov* retains the status of a *karfaf* if it is properly fenced and locked, because that physical security signals human intention and control over the contents within.

### Tosafot on Imur

Before proceeding to the *gemara*'s analysis, *Tosafot* raises a striking question that deserves careful attention. The *mishnah* speaks of taking wood from the *karfaf* even when the sticks are scattered, and of gathering sticks from the field when they are piled. But if one is gathering scattered sticks, is that not the *melachah* of *me'amer* — the agricultural labor of bundling, which figures in the Torah's account of the *mekoshish etzim*? Indeed, *me'amer* is an *av melachah de'oraita*, and the concern is entirely real.

*Tosafot* resolves this by limiting the prohibition of *me'amer* to its natural agricultural context: *imur* applies specifically where the produce is gathered in the place where it grew — the wheat lying in the field after the harvest, the apples that have fallen from the tree. The act of bundling produce *in situ* as part of the harvest process constitutes *me'amer*. By contrast, sticks that lie scattered in a *karfaf* — which functions more like a lot or a park than a cultivated agricultural field — are not being gathered as part of a harvesting process, and therefore do not implicate *me'amer*. *Tosafot* acknowledges, however, that in certain field scenarios, gathering scattered sticks that fell from trees could theoretically raise a *de'oraita* concern. The point is important: the discussion in the *mishnah* is not so simple that it can be read without awareness of this potential biblical dimension.

### Rav Yehuda's Position

The *gemara* now introduces a ruling from Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *ein mevi'im etzim ela min hamikhunasin she-be-karfaf* — one may bring wood only from sticks that are both gathered *and* located within a *karfaf*. This immediately stands in tension with our *mishnah*, which appears to require only one of the two criteria: either gathered (in the field) or in the *karfaf* (even if scattered). The *gemara* responds that our *mishnah* represents a *da'at yachid* — a minority position — and that there exists an opposing view requiring both criteria simultaneously.

### Beit Shammai/Hillel

To support this claim, the *gemara* cites a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: *lo necheleku Beit Shammai ve-Beit Hillel al hamefuzarin sheba-sadot she-ein mevi'im* — both schools agree that scattered wood in an open field is forbidden; it is *muktzah*, and as *Tosafot* noted, gathering it might even pose a *de'oraita* problem. Similarly, both agree that gathered wood within a *karfaf* — the case with both criteria — is permitted. *Al mah necheleku?* What was the actual dispute? It was the intermediate cases: either scattered wood in a *karfaf*, or gathered wood out in the field. *Beit Shammai* says one criterion is insufficient; *Beit Hillel* says one criterion suffices.

### R. Shimon Interpretation

The *gemara* draws its conclusion: according to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, *Beit Hillel*'s position is that one criterion suffices, and this is consistent with our *mishnah*. But the attribution to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar is itself significant. It implies that others disagree with his reading of the dispute — they hold that *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* never debated the intermediate cases at all, and that everyone agrees both criteria are necessary. This is precisely the position of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: the *mishnah* follows only Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's lenient reading, which is itself a minority position, while the authoritative ruling requires both that the wood be gathered *and* that it be located in a *karfaf*.

### Rava on Easy-Scatter Items

Rava introduces a further refinement. Even where gathered wood is found in the appropriate location, not every physical pile qualifies as *mukhunas*. *Alei kanei ve-alei gefanim* — leaves of reeds or grapevine leaves — *af al gav de-mekhansei ve-motvi*, even if they are piled up, *keivan de-im madli zika mevader lehu, ke-mefuzarin damu* — since a wind could easily scatter them, they are treated as if they were scattered to begin with. A pile of leaves is, in Rava's view, not a genuine pile, because the owner had no reasonable expectation that the pile would remain intact. Without that expectation, there is no *da'at* — no cognitive presence — that would classify the material as set aside for use. The principle is that *muktzah* is fundamentally about what is "on one's radar screen," and something whose stability cannot be assumed does not make it onto that screen.

### Rava's Tarp Solution

Rava then offers the remedy: *ve-ee itnach mana me-etmol la-layeh* — if, on the day before *Yom Tov*, one placed a vessel or some covering over the pile to protect it from the wind, *shapir dami* — that is entirely acceptable. By taking a concrete action before *Yom Tov* to ensure the pile's stability, one demonstrates genuine intention to use the material and overcomes the wind-scattering problem. The practical upshot is that someone who intends to use a pile of lightweight material — leaves, reed leaves, and the like — should place a covering over it before *Yom Tov* commences.

### Karpef Criteria Debate

The *gemara* now returns to the definitional dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi regarding what constitutes a *karfaf*, and raises a pointed question about how to read their exchange: *heichi ka'amar* — precisely what is each one claiming?

One reading: Rabbi Yehuda assumes that a *karfaf* by definition has a fence and lock, and adds that it must *also* be near the city. Rabbi Yosi then comes to say: *keivan de'it lei potachat afilu betoch techum Shabbat* — once it has the fence and lock, proximity to the city is irrelevant. On this reading, Rabbi Yosi is being lenient relative to Rabbi Yehuda, who requires both proximity and a lock.

An alternative reading: Rabbi Yehuda is not requiring two criteria but rather insisting that proximity to the city is the *only* relevant criterion — *kol shesamuch la'ir, bein de'it lei potachat bein deleit lei potachat*. Whether or not it is locked, if it is near the city it is a *karfaf*. Rabbi Yosi, in response, says the only determinative criterion is the fence and lock — *davka de'it lei potachat* — and proximity to the city is neither necessary nor sufficient. On this reading, Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi are not arguing about how many criteria are required but about *which* criterion is the operative one.

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this question from the language of the *mishnah* itself — specifically from Rabbi Yosi's formulation, which includes the phrase *afilu betoch techum Shabbat*. The *gemara* concludes: *shema mina, tartei lekula ka'amar* — from the fact that Rabbi Yosi says "even within the *techum Shabbat*," one can infer that Rabbi Yosi is being lenient in two directions simultaneously: either criterion is sufficient on its own. If it has a lock, distance from the city does not matter; if it is near the city, the absence of a lock does not disqualify it. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, requires both. The resolution hinges on the logical force of the word *afilu* in Rabbi Yosi's formulation, though the precise reasoning is itself the subject of a lengthy discussion in *Rashi* and *Tosafot*, who grapple with exactly how the *gemara* derives this conclusion from the *mishnah*'s language.

### Resolution: One vs. Two

The *gemara* concludes, based on the above analysis, that Rabbi Yehuda demands both criteria to define something as a *karfaf*, while Rabbi Yosi holds that either criterion independently suffices. Our *mishnah*, which required only one criterion (either gathered or in a *karfaf*), aligns with Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's reading of *Beit Hillel*'s position; and the more stringent position of Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel aligns with those who read the debate as one in which both criteria are universally required.

### Practical Halacha

Rav Sala in the name of Rabbi Yirmeyah rules: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yosi lehakel* — the law follows Rabbi Yosi's lenient position in *defining* what constitutes a *karfaf*, such that either proximity to the city or a fence-and-lock arrangement suffices for that definitional question. However, when it comes to the separate question of what is required to *permit use* of the sticks themselves, one follows Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel's stricter standard, which demands both that the wood be gathered *and* that it be located in a *karfaf*.

It is worth pausing to note what makes this class of *muktzah* conceptually distinctive from others encountered earlier in the *masechta*. Most prior examples of *muktzah* involved items that are naturally within the human sphere — food, vessels — that were somehow pushed out of that sphere by being set aside, stored away, or designated for another purpose. Here the situation is the reverse: scattered sticks and stones are naturally *outside* the human sphere, and the question is what brings them *into* it. Gathering them and placing them in a controlled enclosure are the mechanisms by which these objects of the natural world become integrated into the domain of human intention and anticipated use.

## Chopping Wood Issue

### Chopping Prohibition

The next *mishnah* states: *ein mevak'in etzim lo min hakorot velo min hakorah shenishberah beYom Tov* — one may not chop wood from beams, nor from a beam that fell on *Yom Tov* itself. The case of the beam that fell on *Yom Tov* is straightforwardly a *muktzah* problem: when *Yom Tov* began, the beam was part of the building's rafters and entirely outside one's contemplated use. Once it crashes down on *Yom Tov*, it does not automatically become available firewood, since it was not designated as such at the onset of *Yom Tov*.

### Muktzah Beam Status

But what of simple beams — logs of wood that were already present before *Yom Tov*? The *mishnah*'s prohibition on chopping from *korot* (beams) seems at first puzzling: if they were there before *Yom Tov*, why are they *muktzah*? The *gemara* will address this directly, and the resolution involves reconsidering exactly what the *mishnah* means by *korot*.

### Tools Permitted/Forbidden

The *mishnah* then addresses the act of chopping itself. When chopping is permitted, it may not be done *lo bekardom velo bemgirah velo bemagal ela bekupitz* — not with an axe, not with a crosscut saw, not with a scythe, but only with a *kupitz*, which *Rashi* identifies as *sakin shel katzavim* — a butcher's cleaver.

### Avoda Dechol Concept

*Rashi*'s explanation for why the axe and the saw are forbidden illuminates an important principle: *avoda dechol* — the appearance of weekday labor. Chopping wood with an axe, going out to the yard with a full-sized crosscut saw — these evoke the image of someone at work, out in the field, engaged in the kind of labor associated with professional woodcutting. The prohibition on *Yom Tov* against activities that appear as *avoda dechol* is not about the specific *melachah* being performed, but about the overall experiential reality being created. Using a meat cleaver to reduce a log into usable pieces for the fire is a different experience altogether — it places the activity squarely within the domestic context, the preparation of one's home for the needs of the day. The concern, in other words, is not merely technical compliance but the *character* of the act.

### Gemara Reframe

The *gemara* initially reads the first part of the *mishnah* — the prohibition on chopping from *korot* — as also relating to the *avoda dechol* issue, i.e., as a general prohibition on the act of chopping. But this creates a contradiction with the *mishnah*'s own conclusion, which permits chopping with a *kupitz*. The *gemara* therefore says: *ha'amarta reisha ein mevak'in klal* — how can you permit chopping at the end if the beginning seems to prohibit chopping altogether?

### Savar Definition

The resolution comes from Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *chisuri mechasra vehachi katani* — the *mishnah* is elliptical and must be read with an implied context. The prohibition in the first clause is not about the act of chopping per se but about *muktzah*: *ein mevak'in min hasavar shel korot* — one may not chop from a *savar* of beams, meaning a pile of beams that has been set aside specifically for construction purposes. Neatly stacked construction lumber — what we might picture as pre-cut 2×4s arranged on a building site — is designated for construction, not for fuel. Using it for firewood on *Yom Tov* therefore constitutes a violation of *muktzah*, because such materials were mentally assigned to a different purpose entirely.

A *baraita* confirms this reading explicitly, using the word *savar* rather than the ambiguous *korot*, making clear that the context is a pile of construction materials. The *baraita* states the reason: *l'fi she'eino min hamukhan* — because these items are not set aside for *Yom Tov* use. Alongside this, it confirms the prohibition against chopping with an axe for the separate reason of *avoda dechol*. The two prohibitions — *muktzah* applying to construction lumber, and *avoda dechol* applying to the use of professional woodcutting tools — are thus properly distinguished.

### Axe Gender Parts

The *gemara* cites Rav Chinena bar Shamaya in the name of Rav: *lo shanu ela be-nekeivot shelo aval be-zachrot shelo mutar* — when the axe is prohibited, the prohibition applies only to the *feminine* part of the axe; the *masculine* part remains permitted. The terminology here maps onto the distinction between the broader, more robust chopping surface of the axe — associated with the heavy, professional woodcutting work that constitutes *avoda dechol* — and the narrower, more refined edge that functions similarly to a cleaver. The *masculine* part, being narrower and more precise in its cutting action, resembles the *kupitz* in its domestic character and is therefore permitted.

The *gemara* initially objects: *pshita* — this seems obvious. If the *mishnah* permits using a *kupitz*, and this part of the axe functions like a *kupitz*, what is the novel teaching? The answer is that one might have thought: *migu de-hai gisa asur* — since one part of the implement is forbidden, the presence of the forbidden component contaminates the entire implement, making even the permissible part forbidden by association. The ruling comes to teach that this is not so: one may use the cleaver-like portion of the implement even if the axe-like portion of the same tool would be forbidden.

### Cleaver Use Debate

A parallel discussion is then introduced regarding the *kupitz* itself. The *mishnah* permits using a *kupitz*, but the *kupitz* too has component parts that differ in character. The ruling is stated again by Rav Chinena bar Shamaya: when the *kupitz* is permitted, that refers specifically to its *masculine* — narrower — portion, which is the genuine cleaver-like edge. The *feminine* — broader — portion of the *kupitz* more closely resembles an axe and is therefore forbidden for the same *avoda dechol* reason.

Again the *gemara* says *pshita* — the *mishnah* forbids axes, so of course the axe-like portion of the *kupitz* should be forbidden. And again the novelty requires articulation: one might have thought that since the *kupitz* is generically permitted, the permissibility extends to all its functions — *migu de-hai gisa shari, hai gisa nami shari* — since one part of the *kupitz* is permitted, perhaps the other part is permitted as well. The ruling comes to teach that only the genuinely cleaver-like function of the *kupitz* is permitted; the axe-like function remains forbidden.

One might note that the more internally consistent position would be to forbid even the cleaver-like part of the *kupitz* when the *kupitz* itself also has an axe-like component, since the totality of the implement evokes professional woodcutting. The *gemara* acknowledges this intuition and presents it as the view of an alternative version of the tradition — *ve-ika de-matni la asayfa* — which applies the principle in the stricter direction: even some cleavers will be forbidden depending on their dual-function character and the overall experiential reality they create.

## Locked House Fruit

### Storage House Scenario

The next *mishnah* presents a scenario that at first glance might seem straightforward but opens onto a rich discussion of *muktzah*, *soter*, and the special latitude granted on *Yom Tov* for food access. The case: *bayis she-malei peiros* — a house filled with fruit, understood here not as a living room stocked with apples but as a storage facility — a shed, a granary — packed with produce. Before *Yom Tov*, the house was locked and there was no access to the fruit. The owner intended to use the fruit but found no way in. Then *nifchas* — the wall was breached, an opening appeared — and access became possible.

The word *nifchas* itself, as distinct from *niftach* (simply opened), suggests a structural collapse or a section of wall falling away, rather than a door swinging open. One might read it as the wall having been diminished — something fell away and created an opening. The connection to the earlier discussion of *matchilin be-otzar techilah* is real: this scenario also involves gaining access to stored produce, and one might have thought the fruit itself was *muktzah* as undisturbed stored goods.

### Standard Permission

The *mishnah* rules that *notel mimakom hapachas* — one may take fruit from the place of the breach. The fruit itself is accessible, and the opening that has appeared may be used.

### R. Meir Leniency

The significant *chiddush* of the *mishnah* is Rabbi Meir's position: *af poches lechatchilah venotel* — one may actively break open the wall and then take the fruit. Rabbi Meir thus permits the deliberate demolition of a section of the storage structure in order to gain access to the food within.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

Why, before reaching Rabbi Meir's ruling, is the fruit not *muktzah*? If the house was locked and the fruit inaccessible, why is the fruit available at all once the wall is breached? *Rashi* answers that the only obstacle to accessing the fruit was the need to break the wall, and since — as the *gemara* will demonstrate — breaking an unplastered brick wall may constitute only a rabbinic prohibition, that alone is insufficient to render the fruit *muktzah*. A rabbinic barrier to access does not place the fruit outside one's anticipated reach in the way that a biblical prohibition would.

*Tosafot* finds this unsatisfying. Why should the biblical or rabbinic character of the obstacle matter to the question of *muktzah*? If the fruit is out of reach, it is out of reach, and the *muktzah* status should follow. *Tosafot* therefore argues that the *mishnah* must be understood as following the position of Rabbi Shimon, who holds a significantly narrower conception of *muktzah* — one that would not extend to fruit that is physically present and intended for use, even if access to it happened to be blocked by a structural obstruction.

### Soter Problem

The *gemara* focuses its analysis on Rabbi Meir's ruling. *Amai? Veha kasater ahala* — why does Rabbi Meir permit this? Is one not demolishing a structure — engaging in *soter*, which is a *melachah*?

### Avira Delivnei

The answer comes from Rav Nechumi bar Ada in the name of Shmuel: *be'avira delivnei* — the case involves bricks stacked without mortar. Since there is no mortar binding the bricks together, dismantling the wall does not constitute genuine *soter*, as there is no truly permanent structure being deconstructed. *Nifchas* — the wall's having collapsed — is also consistent with this explanation, since an unplastered wall of stacked bricks is inherently unstable and prone to collapse. One might object that even unmortered walls can be extraordinarily stable — as the ancient stone fences of England and Ireland demonstrate, skilled construction without mortar can last centuries. Still, a wall of regularly-shaped bricks without mortar lacks the structural permanence of a plastered wall, and the *gemara*'s position is that this falls below the threshold of biblical *soter*.

### Rav Nachman Bricks

But even granting that *soter* is not a biblical issue here, the *gemara* raises an independent problem: *muktzah*. Does Rav Nachman not teach that *hani livni deyasmi mibinyana* — leftover bricks from a construction site — are not *muktzah*, since they can be used as seating (*hо'il vechazi lemizga aleihu*)? So far so good: leftover bricks, no longer designated for construction, are available. However, *shargineihu* — if one then stacks those bricks neatly, *aktsineihu* — one has re-designated them for construction use and rendered them *muktzah* once more. A neatly stacked pile of bricks signals that they are being reserved for a building project.

Apply this to our case: the bricks of the storage house were arranged and stacked to form a wall before *Yom Tov*. That arrangement constitutes a designation for structural use. Even if the wall has no mortar, the bricks, by virtue of being stacked to form a structure, are *muktzah*. How, then, can Rabbi Meir permit one to dismantle the wall and use the bricks — or at least, to move and handle them in the course of breaching the wall — if those bricks are themselves *muktzah*?

### Yom Tov Limited

Rabbi Zeira resolves this: *be-Yom Tov amru aval lo be-Shabbat* — the *mishnah*'s permission is stated specifically for *Yom Tov*, not for *Shabbat*. The question then becomes: what does *Yom Tov* change? If the bricks are *muktzah* and the act of dismantling is *soter*, how does the *Yom Tov* context alter the analysis?

### Okhel Nefesh Waiver

*Tosafot* makes explicit what the *gemara* implies: *she-hitiru muktzah mishum okhel nefesh* — the concern of *muktzah* is waived on *Yom Tov* when the *muktzah* item constitutes an obstacle to food access, because the Torah's permission for *okhel nefesh* on *Yom Tov* extends to removing impediments to food preparation and access. *Rashi* appears to take the same position, though it is *Tosafot* that formulates it as a principle, citing it in the name of the *Ritzba* who had stated it earlier.

This is a striking conceptual claim. *Muktzah* has throughout the *masechta* been treated as a robust rabbinic framework that applies across many contexts. To say that *Yom Tov*'s *okhel nefesh* license can waive *muktzah* concerns is to significantly expand the reach of that leniency. The question of how to integrate this principle with the earlier discussions — *matchilin be-otzar*, the egg of the first *mishnah*, and all the other *muktzah* scenarios explored throughout the *perek* — remains genuinely complex, and the *gemara* does not fully resolve it here. One observation that helps: if the food itself is *muktzah*, the *okhel nefesh* rationale cannot rescue it, because the food's *muktzah* status effectively disqualifies it from being food-for-*Yom Tov* in the relevant sense. The *okhel nefesh* waiver applies when the food is legitimate but the *muktzah* concern is an external obstacle preventing access. That said, threading this through all the earlier cases is not without difficulty.

## Wells & Seals Issue

### Shmuel Seals

The *gemara* now pivots to a broader discussion of *boneh* and *soter* as they apply to ground-connected structures and to vessels, a discussion triggered by Shmuel's teaching but extending into a wide-ranging analysis of the distinctions between *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, between structures and vessels, and between different modes of undoing a fastening.

Shmuel's teaching: *khotamot she-ba-karka, matir* — one may undo the knots on ground-connected seals on *Shabbat*. The image to have in mind is a well covered by something analogous to a manhole cover, which is roped or chained to the well to prevent it from being lost or stolen. The rope or chain is tied with a knot designed to be undone and retied — it is not a *kesher shel kayama*, a permanent knot — and so untying it raises no concern of *matir kesher*. Nor does simply undoing the knot constitute *soter*, the deconstruction of a structure. Why not? Because this is simply how the well is used: the cover is opened by undoing the knot, and the expectation is that the cover will be replaced afterward. The act of opening the well is part of the well's normal functioning; it does not structurally alter anything in a lasting way. Just as opening a door is not *soter* even though one momentarily breaks the connection between the door and the doorpost, so too undoing the well cover's rope is not *soter*.

### Matir/Mafki'a/Chotech

However, *aval lo mafki'a velo chotech* — one may not *unravel* the rope (*mafki'a*: to unbraid or unwind the braiding of the rope itself) nor cut it. The distinction here is fundamental. In all three actions — undoing the knot, unraveling the rope, and cutting — the physical result is the same: the cover is detached from the well. But the character of the acts differs entirely. Undoing the knot is the intended mode of operation; it is reversible and does not structurally change the connection. Unraveling the rope destroys the rope's integrity and makes it very difficult or impossible to restore to its previous state. Cutting severs the connection permanently. Both unraveling and cutting, therefore, constitute genuine structural deconstruction — *soter* of the well-as-structure — and are prohibited on *Shabbat*.

### Soter vs. Vessels

A critical principle is now stated: *ein boneh vesoter bekeilim* — the prohibitions of *boneh* (building) and *soter* (demolishing) do not apply to vessels. This parallels the intuitive distinction between knocking down a house to access food and tearing open a bag of potato chips to access food. *Soter* is a concern for structures connected to the ground; it is not a concern for moveable vessels.

### Vessel Exception

Consequently, if the same scenario — a rope holding a cover in place — involves not a ground-connected well but a vessel (imagine a covered box in one's home, secured by a rope), then on *Shabbat* one may undo the knot, unravel the rope, and even cut the rope. Since the prohibition of *soter* does not apply to vessels, all modes of detaching the cover are permitted. The analogy to tearing open a bag of potato chips captures the principle well.

### Braitah on Ground

The *gemara* then cites a *braitah*: *khotamot she-ba-karka, be-Shabbat matir aval lo mafki'a velo chotech* — confirming the ruling for ground-connected seals on *Shabbat*. Significantly, the *braitah* then states: *be-Yom Tov matir mafki'a ve-chotech* — on *Yom Tov*, one may even cut the rope securing the well cover. This introduces an apparent inconsistency with Shmuel's ruling, which gave no distinction between *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* for ground-connected seals.

### R. Meir Attribution

The *gemara* resolves this by attributing the permissive *braitah* to Rabbi Meir: *ha mani Rabbi Meir hi* — this is Rabbi Meir, who, as established in the previous *mishnah*, permits *soter* of a structure to gain access to food. Just as Rabbi Meir permits breaking down the wall of the storage house, he permits cutting the rope securing the well cover in order to access the water.

### Rabbis Agree

The *gemara* then notes a *braitah* stating that the Rabbis agree with Rabbi Meir regarding ground-connected seals on *Shabbat* — that one may undo the knot (*matir*) but may not cut. Shmuel's position aligns with the Rabbis, not Rabbi Meir: one may undo the knot on *Shabbat*, but the Rabbis dispute Rabbi Meir's permission to cut on *Yom Tov* in the case of a ground-connected structure.

### Braitah Vessels

For vessels, a separate *braitah* rules: *be-Shabbat matir aval lo mafki'a velo chotech; be-Yom Tov matir u-mafki'a ve-chotech* — on *Shabbat*, one may undo the knot on a vessel's rope but may not unravel or cut it; on *Yom Tov*, one may do all three. This is more stringent than what Shmuel stated for vessels, since Shmuel appeared to permit even cutting the rope of a vessel on *Shabbat*.

### R. Nechemya Position

The *gemara* attributes this stricter *braitah* on vessels to Rabbi Nechemya: *ha mani Rabbi Nechemya hi*. Rabbi Nechemya's concern is unrelated to *soter*; he operates from a completely different principle: *kol ha-kelim ein nitalin ela derech tashmishan* — all vessels may only be used in their designated manner. Rabbi Nechemya holds that even an ordinary kitchen knife, which carries no *muktzah* status, may not be used for purposes other than its normal function. One may not use a kitchen knife to cut a rope, scratch an itch, or pick one's teeth — it is a knife for cutting food, and that is the extent of its permitted use on *Shabbat*.

### R. Nechemya Strictness

Consequently, according to Rabbi Nechemya, the prohibition on cutting the rope securing a vessel on *Shabbat* has nothing to do with *soter*. The act of dismantling the vessel's closure is perfectly acceptable in principle (since *ein boneh vesoter bekeilim*); the problem is that cutting a rope is not what knives are for, and no available vessel is designated for rope-cutting. The restriction, in other words, targets the *instrument* of the action, not the act itself.

### R. Nechemya Yom Tov

The *gemara* raises the obvious question: if Rabbi Nechemya's principle — that vessels may only be used in their designated manner — is a general rule about how vessels may be employed, why is it limited to *Shabbat*? The *braitah* permits cutting on *Yom Tov*, implying that Rabbi Nechemya's restriction does not apply on *Yom Tov*. Does Rabbi Nechemya truly distinguish between the *shevut* of *Shabbat* and the *shevut* of *Yom Tov*?

### Firewood Vessels

To test whether Rabbi Nechemya makes such a distinction, the *gemara* turns to a set of *braitot* concerning which items may be used as firewood on *Yom Tov* — a context that is unambiguously *Yom Tov*. The first *braitah*: *masikin be-kelim ve-ein masikin be-shivrei kelim* — one may use whole vessels as firewood but not broken vessels. The reasoning is that a vessel which broke on *Yom Tov* is *muktzah* — it was not designated as firewood when *Yom Tov* began, and its breakage on *Yom Tov* created a *nolad* (a newly-created status) that renders it *muktzah*. The whole vessel, by contrast, was available before *Yom Tov* and has not undergone a status change. This is the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds of *muktzah*.

The second *braitah*: *masikin bein be-kelim bein be-shivrei kelim* — one may use both whole and broken vessels as firewood. This is the position of Rabbi Shimon, who does not hold of *muktzah* in its broad form and therefore does not treat a vessel that breaks on *Yom Tov* as newly *muktzah*.

The third *braitah*: *ein masikin lo be-kelim velo be-shivrei kelim* — neither whole vessels nor broken vessels may be used as firewood. This is Rabbi Nechemya's position. Whole vessels may not be burned as firewood because vessels are not designated for burning — *ein nitalin ela derech tashmishan* — and this principle applies on *Yom Tov* as well as on *Shabbat*. Since this third *braitah* is unambiguously about *Yom Tov* and yet Rabbi Nechemya's restriction applies within it, the *gemara* has established that Rabbi Nechemya does indeed apply his principle on *Yom Tov*. The *gemara* therefore resolves: *trei tanna'ei aliba de-Rabbi Nechemya* — there is a debate among *tannaim* about Rabbi Nechemya's position: one *tanna* holds that he extends his restriction to *Yom Tov*, while another holds that he distinguishes *Shabbat*'s *shevut* from *Yom Tov*'s *shevut* and is lenient on *Yom Tov*.

### Unresolved Soter Q

It is worth noting in closing that the *gemara* never fully resolves the foundational *soter* question with which this entire discussion began. When the *gemara* first asked how Rabbi Meir could permit breaking into the storage house, it reframed the case as involving unmortered bricks (*avira delivnei*) — thereby reducing or eliminating the *soter* concern. But when the discussion later moved to the well covers and the permission to cut the rope on *Yom Tov* even on a ground-connected structure, the *gemara* seemed to attribute this to Rabbi Meir's general willingness to permit *soter* for food access — without again invoking the *avira delivnei* limitation. One could offer a principled resolution: perhaps the authentic *de'oraita* prohibition of *soter* applies only to *soter al menat livnot* — demolition undertaken as part of a construction project — and destructive acts undertaken purely to gain access to food, with no reconstructive purpose, constitute *mekalkel* (a purely destructive act) rather than *soter*, and thus remain at most rabbinically forbidden. On *Yom Tov*, Rabbi Meir would then be willing to waive the rabbinic concern in the interest of food access. But the *gemara* leaves this reasoning implicit, and the question of precisely how *soter* and *mekalkel* interact in these cases — and how Rabbi Meir's principle is properly delimited — remains, as the *gemara* concludes, without explicit clarification.
