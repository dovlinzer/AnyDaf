# Yevamot 28b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Achot Zekukato

### Four Brothers Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הָיְתָה אַחַת מֵהֶן אֲסוּרָה עַל הָאֶחָד אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — אָסוּר בָּהּ וּמוּתָּר בַּאֲחוֹתָהּ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי אָסוּר בִּשְׁתֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: If **one of** the sisters **was forbidden to one** of the brothers due to a **prohibition against forbidden relatives** because she was a relative of his wife or a relative from his mother's side, then **he is prohibited from** marrying **her but permitted to** marry **her sister.** But **the second** brother, who is not a close relative of either sister, **is prohibited from** marrying **both of them.**

The daf we take up begins at the very last line of 28a, with the word *issur mitzvah*. We are completing a *mishnah* that presented the case of four brothers, two of whom died having been married to two sisters. The question was whether the surviving brothers face the problem of *achot zekukato* — the sister of one's *zekukah*, a woman to whom one stands in a quasi-marital bond through the *mitzvah* of *yibbum*. The *mishnah* established that if one of the sisters is an *ervah* to one of the surviving brothers, that resolves the problem: since he has no *zikah* to her, she is entirely outside the picture, and his fellow brother may perform *yibbum* with the other sister without triggering the prohibition of *achot zekukato*.

### One Ervah Solution

To make the case concrete: suppose Reuven and Shimon died, having been married to Rachel and Leah, who are sisters. The surviving brothers are Levi and Yehudah. If Rachel is Levi's daughter — from a different mother than Leah — then Levi has no *zikah* to Rachel at all, since she is a forbidden relative. With no *zikah* connecting Levi to Rachel, Leah is not the sister of Levi's *zekukah*, and Yehudah may therefore perform *yibbum* with Rachel. This is the logic of the *ervah* solution.

### Issur Mitzvah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: לֵיקוּם אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה בִּמְקוֹם אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — וְתִתְיַיבֵּם, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **It might enter your mind to say: Let the prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva stand in** the same **place,** i.e., level of severity, **as the prohibition against forbidden relatives.** Consequently, the woman who is forbidden to the *yavam* is considered a forbidden relative and her sister is permitted to him. Despite the fact that the prohibition resulting from a mitzva is not as severe, here it is given equal status, **and** we might say: **Let** her sister **enter into levirate marriage.** Therefore, **this comes to teach us** that this is not the case.

The question then arises: what if Rachel is not a biblical *ervah* to Levi, but is instead forbidden to him through a rabbinic prohibition — an *issur mitzvah*, such as being a *grushah* to a *kohen*, or some other *issur derabbanan*? The entire problem of *achot zekukato* is itself rabbinic in origin, since the *zikah* that creates the quasi-marital bond is a *derabbanan* construct. One might therefore reason that since we are operating in a *derabbanan* world — the *zikah* is *derabbanan*, the problem of *achot zekukato* is *derabbanan* — perhaps a *derabbanan ervah* should suffice to dissolve a *derabbanan* problem. Let the rabbinic prohibition against Rachel push her out of Levi's picture, thereby permitting Yehudah to perform *yibbum* with her.

### Issur Mitzvah Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִתְיַיבֵּם? כֵּיוָן דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא רַמְיָא קַמֵּיהּ, קָא פָגַע בַּאֲחוֹת זְקוּקָתוֹ. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: מִשּׁוּם מִצְוָה עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why in fact **does** her sister not **enter into levirate marriage?** The Gemara answers: Her sister is forbidden to him **because by Torah** law the prohibited woman **is** still **set before** *the yavam* for levirate marriage. Were he to take the sister in levirate marriage **he would** essentially **be encountering the sister of the woman with whom he has** a levirate bond. **It might enter your mind to say** that **due to the mitzva** of levirate marriage, **the Sages did as they did** and nullified their decree prohibiting the sister of a woman with whom the *yavam* has a levirate bond from entering levirate marriage, when the original sister is forbidden only as the result of a mitzva. Therefore, it **teaches us** that they did not cancel their decree in this situation.

This is the *hava amina* the *gemara* entertains at the top of 28b. The *gemara* cites the teaching: *issur mitzvah ve-issur kedushah — choletzet velo mityabemet* — a woman who is rabbinically forbidden performs *chalitzah* but not *yibbum*. The *gemara* distinguishes between that simpler case, where a single brother dies and the *yevamah* is forbidden to the surviving brother through a rabbinic prohibition alone, and the more complex case here, where we have both the *issur mitzvah* and the additional complication of *achot zekukato*.

The *gemara* responds: *kevan de-de'oraita ramya kammei* — since at the biblical level there still is a *zikah* connecting Levi to Rachel, the problem of *achot zekukato* persists. The reasoning that a *derabbanan ervah* might dissolve a *derabbanan* problem is rejected. Even though *achot zekukato* is rabbinic in character, the *zikah* that underlies it is a real *de'oraita* relationship, and no rabbinic-level prohibition on Rachel is sufficient to sever that connection. The *mishnah* thus teaches us — *kamashma lan* — that an *issur mitzvah* does not solve the *achot zekukato* problem, and *chalitzah* is still required.

This was a genuine and reasonable *hava amina*: if everything is *derabbanan*, let a *derabbanan ervah* resolve a *derabbanan* difficulty. The *gemara* rejects it cleanly, but it was not an unreasonable idea.

### One vs. Two Ervas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה אַחַת מֵהֶן כּוּ׳. הָא תְּנֵינָא חֲדָא זִימְנָא: אֲחוֹתָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא יְבִמְתָּהּ — אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated: If **one of** the sisters **was** forbidden to one of the brothers due to a prohibition against forbidden relations, then he is prohibited from marrying her but permitted to marry her sister. The Gemara asks: **We** already **learned** this **on one occasion: When her sister,** who is a forbidden relative to the *yavam*, **is her *yevama*** as well, **she either performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage.**

The *mishnah* then presents a further case: *haysah achas meihen zo ervah v'zu ervah* — if each sister is a forbidden relative to one of the surviving brothers. For instance, Rachel is Levi's daughter and Leah is Yehudah's daughter. In this scenario, each brother may perform *yibbum* with the sister who is not forbidden to him: Levi does *yibbum* with Leah, and Yehudah does *yibbum* with Rachel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְועִינַן הָתָם: מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיכָּא לְמִיגְזַר מִשּׁוּם שֵׁנִי, אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיגְזַר מִשּׁוּם שֵׁנִי — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **necessary** that this be said here as well, **for if it taught us** this *halakha* only **there** in its more general formulation (*Yevamot* 20a), then I might have said: The *yavam* is permitted to marry the sister **because there is no** reason **to issue** a rabbinic **decree due to a second** brother. There, there is only one *yavam*, to whom one sister is permitted and the other is forbidden. **Here, however,** in the case of two brothers, **where there is** reason **to issue** a rabbinic **decree due to the second** brother, lest he consummate the levirate marriage as well, **I would say** that we do **not** allow even the first brother to perform levirate marriage, and both sisters-in-law should be forbidden to both brothers.

The *gemara* asks: *ha tu lama li? hainu hach* — why is this case necessary? It is essentially the same principle stated twice. What is added by doubling it? The *gemara* answers: *tzricha* — both cases are needed. If we only knew the case of one *ervah*, we might not appreciate that it is truly a case of *ervah* at work, because there is only one brother performing *yibbum* and the asymmetry is not fully evident. But because Yehudah is present in that scenario and *does not* perform *yibbum*, his restriction makes it clear that Levi's *yibbum* is a special case enabled by the *ervah* status. Conversely, if we only knew the case of two *ervas*, where both brothers each perform *yibbum* with the opposite sister, one might think this is simply the general rule and not a consequence of *ervah* status at all. Each case is therefore necessary to make the full picture clear to the reader.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמוּעִינַן הָכָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא שֵׁנִי דְּקָא מוֹכַח, אֲבָל הָתָם, דְּלֵיכָּא שֵׁנִי — אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the mishna **had taught us** the *halakha* only **here,** it would have been possible to say that the permissibility of levirate marriage here is **because there is** a second brother who **indicates,** by refraining from performing levirate marriage, that the sister of a woman bound by a levirate bond is forbidden. **However, there,** where there **is not a second** brother, **I would say no,** this *halakha* would not apply, due to a concern that people might wrongly conclude that the sister of a woman bound by a levirate bond is permitted. Therefore, it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* in both places.

### Final Mishnah Line

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ וְכוּ׳. ״זוֹ הִיא״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לְמַעוֹטֵי אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה לָזֶה וְאִיסּוּר מִצְוָה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: **And this is** the case that was referred to when **they said:** When her sister is also her *yevama*, she either performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage. The Gemara asks: **What does** the expression: **This is,** come to **exclude?** The Gemara answers: **It excludes** the case where there is **a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva for this** one **and a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva for that** one.

The final line of the *mishnah* reads: *zu hi she'amru* — "this is what the Sages meant when they said" that if a sister is also one's *yevamah*, and one of the sisters is an *ervah*, then the other may either perform *chalitzah* or *yibbum*. The *gemara* asks: *zu hi l'me'utei mai?* — what does this phrase come to exclude?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי, הַיְינוּ הָךְ: מָה לִי לְחַד, מָה לִי לִתְרֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I need this as well? This is** identical to **that** which was taught previously, that if one of the sisters was forbidden to the *yavam* due to a prohibition resulting from a mitzva, then he is not permitted to consummate the levirate marriage with either of them. **What** difference **is** there **to me** if she happened before **one** brother **or two?**

The answer: *l'me'utei issur mitzvah l'zeh v'issur mitzvah l'zeh* — to exclude the case where each sister is a *derabbanan ervah* to one of the brothers. The *gemara* already established that a single *derabbanan ervah* does not dissolve the *achot zekukato* problem. But might one think that if *both* sisters carry a *derabbanan* prohibition — each to one of the brothers — then perhaps together they are sufficient? Perhaps when each brother faces a *derabbanan* obstacle to one sister, we treat it as though both are effectively *ervas* to their respective brothers, and allow *yibbum* with the alternate sisters?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כִּי לָא אָמְרִינַן אוֹקֵי אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה בִּמְקוֹם אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיגְזַר מִשּׁוּם שֵׁנִי, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא לְמִיגְזַר מִשּׁוּם שֵׁנִי, אֵימָא: לְהַאי אוֹקֵימְנָא אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה בִּמְקוֹם אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה, וּלְהַאי [אוֹקֵימְנָא] אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה בִּמְקוֹם אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה, וְלִיַּיבְּמוּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say:** There is only one instance **when we do not say: Let the prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva stand in** the same **place,** i.e., level of severity, **as a prohibition against forbidden relatives.** It occurs in a case **where it is** appropriate **to issue** a rabbinic **decree due to the second** brother, who is prohibited from marrying both sisters. **But** in a case **where it is not** appropriate **to issue** a rabbinic **decree due to the second** brother, as the second sister is permitted to the second brother as well, I might **say: For this** brother, **let us stand the prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva in** the same **place as a prohibition against forbidden relatives, and for this** brother **let us stand the prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva in** the same **place as a prohibition against forbidden relatives, and** as a result both brothers may **consummate the levirate marriage.** Therefore, **it teaches us** that this is not so.

The *gemara* again asks: *mah li chad mah li l'trei* — what difference does doubling it make? And again the *gemara* provides a genuine *hava amina*: in the single case, a concern arises that we might confuse others if we allow *yibbum* based on a *derabbanan ervah*, since only one brother benefits and the second brother will not understand why. But in the symmetric case, where each brother does *yibbum* with one sister, there is no concern about the second brother — perhaps here we should allow the *derabbanan ervah* to function. The *mishnah* teaches us that even in this symmetric case we do not permit it. The *gezeirah* concern — that people will come to think a *derabbanan ervah* generally dissolves the *achot zekukato* problem — remains in force regardless.

## Fifteen Arayot

### Rav Yehuda Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְכֵן תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: בְּכוּלָּן אֲנִי קוֹרֵא בָּהֶן, הָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה, וַאֲחוֹתָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא יְבִמְתָּהּ — אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § Incidental to this *halakha*, the Gemara cites the following statement: **Rav Yehuda said that Rav said, and similarly Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** in a *baraita*: **With regard to all of those** women enumerated in the first mishna of the tractate, who are forbidden to the *yevamin* as forbidden relatives, the following situation could arise: These women could also be two sisters who were married to two brothers who happen before their *yevamin* for levirate marriage while each one is a forbidden relative to one of the *yevamin*. In these situations, **I could apply** the ruling that **she who is forbidden to this** brother **is permitted to that** brother, **as well as** the ruling that **when her sister is also her *yevama* she may either perform *ḥalitza* or enter into levirate marriage.**

The *gemara* now turns to a famous teaching that readers may recall from much earlier in the *masechet*: applying all fifteen *arayot* of the opening *mishnah* to the scenario of sisters married to brothers. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav, and so too taught Rabbi Chiya: *v'chulan ani korei bahen* — in all fifteen cases of the *arayot* listed in the first *mishnah*, one can find a scenario where the rule applies: *asurah l'ze muteret l'ze v'achotah k'she-hi yevimtah o choletzet o mityabemet* — she is forbidden to this one and permitted to that one, and her sister, who is also her *yevamah*, either performs *chalitzah* or *yibbum*.

### Sisters Asymmetry

The puzzle embedded in this teaching is like a mind-bender: one needs to construct a case where two women are sisters, both share the same type of *ervah* relationship, yet each is forbidden only to one of the surviving brothers and permitted to the other. The women must be sisters, they must both carry the same category of *ervah*, and the *ervah* must fall asymmetrically — this one to that brother, that one to this brother. The claim is that for all fifteen categories listed in the opening *mishnah*, one can engineer such a scenario.

### Mother-in-Law Example

The easy case to begin with is *chamot* — mother-in-law. Leah is Yehudah's wife's mother, which obviously has no bearing on Levi. Rachel is Levi's wife's mother, equally irrelevant to Yehudah. Both women are sisters — through their father in such a scenario — and each carries the *ervah* of mother-in-law, but only to one brother. The *ervah* falls asymmetrically, and the sisters remain sisters. The teaching thus works straightforwardly for the mother-in-law case, and the claim is that one can similarly construct working cases for all fifteen categories.

### Rav Yehuda Excludes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יְהוּדָה מְתַרְגֵּם מֵחֲמוֹתוֹ וְאֵילָךְ, אֲבָל שִׁיתָּא בָּבֵי דְרֵישָׁא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But Rav Yehuda interprets** these principles as applying only to those cases found in the list **from** the **mother-in-law of** the *yavam* **and onward, but not to the six cases at the beginning,** which include his daughter, and his wife's daughter, and their descendants.

However, Rav Yehuda restricts his own teaching: *metargem me-chamoso v'eilech* — he will only apply it starting from *chamot*, mother-in-law, the ninth entry in the list. *Aval shita bavei de-reisha* — the first six cases, which are all derived from the category of daughter, he does not wish to apply. The first six *arayot* of the opening *mishnah* are: one's daughter, one's granddaughter through one's daughter, one's granddaughter through one's son, one's wife's daughter, one's wife's granddaughter through her son, and one's wife's granddaughter through her daughter. All six are rooted in the daughter relationship.

### Daughter Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא — כֵּיוָן דְּבִתּוֹ בְּאוּנְסִין הוּא דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, בְּנִשּׂוּאִין לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for Rav Yehuda's distinction? **This is because you find** the scenario where two women who are candidates for levirate marriage are both sisters as well as the daughters of the two *yevamin* only in a case where the women are the daughters of the *yevamin* **through rape, but you cannot find it** in a case where they are his daughters **through marriage.** If one brother married a woman and had a daughter with her, then this woman, the wife of a brother who has children, is forbidden to all of his brothers. Therefore, it would be impossible for another brother to have a daughter with that same woman, and therefore the two daughters could never be sisters. The scenario of daughters who are also sisters is possible only when the first brother raped a woman and gave birth to a daughter, such that the woman is not forbidden to his brothers. If one of the brothers then had a daughter with this woman and both daughters married other brothers who then died, it is possible that these daughters would happen before their fathers for levirate marriage.

Why does Rav Yehuda exclude the first six? The difficulty becomes clear when one tries to construct the case of daughter. Suppose Rachel is Levi's daughter. For Rachel and Leah to be sisters, they must share the same mother — call her Chana. Since they have different fathers, Levi is Rachel's father and another man is Leah's father, while Chana is mother to both. So far so good: Leah is not Levi's daughter.

But here is the problem: if Chana was ever Levi's wife — even if he later divorced her — then Leah, as Chana's daughter, becomes Levi's wife's daughter, which is itself an *ervah*. Both sisters would then be *ervas* to Levi, not just one, and the asymmetry collapses. For the case to work — where Rachel is Levi's daughter but Leah is not any kind of *ervah* to him — Chana could never have been Levi's wife. Rachel must be a daughter born outside of marriage altogether.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנִשּׂוּאִין קָמַיְירֵי, בְּאוּנְסִין לָא קָא מַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda maintains that the current mishna **deals with** cases of **marriage but does not deal with** cases of **rape,** and therefore he does not apply the principles governing sisters to those cases.

The *gemara* states this explicitly: *kevan de-bita ba-onsin hu de-mishkachas lah* — the case of daughter only works when the daughter was born through an out-of-wedlock union. *Bi-nisuyin lo mishkachas lah* — through marriage one cannot find this case. And since Rav Yehuda says *bi-nisuyin kamairi, ba-onsin lo kamairi* — I want to speak of scenarios that arise through marriage, not through out-of-wedlock birth — he excludes the entire first six cases from his teaching.

### Abaye Includes All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי מְתַרְגֵּם אַף בִּתּוֹ (בָּאָה) מֵאֲנוּסָתוֹ, כֵּיוָן דְּאַשְׁכּוֹחֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, אִי בָּעֲיָא — בְּאוּנְסִין תֶּיהְוֵי, אִי בָּעֲיָא — בְּנִשּׂוּאִין תִּהְוֵי. אֲבָל אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ — לָא. כֵּיוָן דְּאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הוּא דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ — בִּפְלוּגְתָּא לָא קָמַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye interprets** these principles as applying **even to his daughter from a woman he raped. Since you** can **find this** scenario, **if** the statement **wants** to deal with cases of **rape, let it; if it wants** to deal with cases of **marriage, let it.** It is preferable to explain this matter with regard to cases of marriage, but if that is not possible, it can still be explained as dealing with cases of rape. **However, it cannot** be explained as referring to **the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist.** Even according to the opinion of Abaye, this case cannot be included, **since you can find** a situation where two sisters happen before two brothers for levirate marriage while each woman is the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist only **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, while** such a case **cannot be found according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis.** And Rabbi Ḥiyya **does not deal with** cases that are subject to **dispute.**

Abaye disagrees: *metargem af bita be-anusato* — Abaye extends the teaching even to the case of daughter, even though it only arises through an out-of-wedlock scenario. *Kevan de-ashkochei mishkachas lah* — since one *can* find the case, it does not matter whether it arises through marriage or not: *ei ba-onsin tehvi, ei bi-nisuyin tehvi* — whether through an out-of-wedlock union or through marriage, either way the case is findable, and Abaye is willing to include it. Working out all fifteen cases is a genuine intellectual puzzle, and according to Abaye all fifteen yield valid scenarios.

### Brother's Wife Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב סָפְרָא מְתַרְגֵּם אַף אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ, וּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּשִׁיתָּא אַחֵי וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וְסִימָנָיךְ: מֵת נוֹלַד וְיִיבֵּם, מֵת נוֹלַד וְיִיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Safra interprets** these principles as applying to the **wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist, and you** can **find this** scenario **in the case** of the **six brothers and according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. And your mnemonic** to remember how this might come about **is** as follows: **Died, born, consummated the levirate marriage; died, born, consummated the levirate marriage.**

However, Abaye himself draws a line at one case: *aval eishet achiv she-lo haya be-olamo* — the *ervah* of one's brother's wife in the case where the brother was not yet alive when she was married. This is the principle that a woman who was married to a brother who subsequently died, and then a new brother was born after the first brother's death — that new brother never coexisted with his deceased brother, and therefore the widow has a special *ervah* status toward him. Abaye says one cannot apply this category to the puzzle of the fifteen *arayot*, because *kevan de-aliba de-Rabbi Shimon hu de-mishkachas lah* — the case only works according to Rabbi Shimon. *Aliba de-rabbanan lo mishkachas lah* — according to the Rabbanan the case cannot be constructed. And since this is a matter of *machlokes*, *be-pilugta lo kamairi* — Abaye does not want to build his teaching on a position disputed among the authorities.

Rav Safra goes further still: *metargem af eishet achiv she-lo haya be-olamo* — even this final case can be made to apply, and one can find it with six brothers according to Rabbi Shimon. The mnemonic for the construction is: *mes, nolad, ve-yibem, mes, nolad, ve-yibem* — died, was born, performed *yibbum*, died, was born, performed *yibbum*. The *gemara* will now spell out this elaborate scenario in full.

## Six-Brother Scenario

### Six Brother Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְאוּבֵן וְשִׁמְעוֹן נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, לֵוִי וִיהוּדָה נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי נׇכְרִיּוֹת. מֵת רְאוּבֵן, נוֹלַד יִשָּׂשכָר, וְיִיבֵּם לֵוִי. מֵת שִׁמְעוֹן, נוֹלַד זְבוּלֻן, וְיִיבֵּם יְהוּדָה. מֵתוּ לֵוִי וִיהוּדָה בְּלֹא בָּנִים, וְנָפְלוּ לְהוּ קַמֵּי יִשָּׂשכָר וּזְבוּלֻן.
>
> **Translation:** How could this come about? There were two brothers, **Reuven and Shimon,** who were **married** to **two sisters.** And there were two other brothers, **Levi and Yehuda,** who were **married** to **two unrelated women. Reuven died, Yissakhar was born, and Levi consummated the levirate marriage** with Reuven's wife. Afterward, **Shimon died, Zevulun was born, and Yehuda consummated the levirate marriage** with Shimon's wife. Later, **Levi and Yehuda died childless and** these two sisters **happened before Yissakhar and Zevulun for levirate marriage.**

The *gemara* sets the stage: Reuven and Shimon are married to the two sisters, Rachel and Leah. Levi and Yehudah are also brothers, each married to unrelated women — Devorah and Chana respectively. These are four of the six brothers. Two more brothers, Yissachar and Zevulun, do not yet exist; they will be born in the course of the scenario.

### First Death-Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת רְאוּבֵן, נוֹלַד יִשָּׂשכָר, וְיִיבֵּם לֵוִי.
>
> **Translation:** Afterward, **Shimon died, Zevulun was born, and Yehuda consummated the levirate marriage** with Shimon's wife.

Step one: Reuven dies. Step two: Yissachar is born. Since Yissachar was born only after Reuven had already died, Reuven and Yissachar never coexisted. Rachel, who was Reuven's wife and is now his widow, was never married to a man who was alive at the same time as Yissachar. She is therefore *eishet achiv she-lo haya be-olamo* with respect to Yissachar — the wife of a brother who was not in the world when he was born — and she stands as an *ervah* to him.

### First Yibum

Step three: Levi performs *yibbum* with Rachel. Levi was already married to Devorah, and now he takes Rachel in *yibbum*. At this moment Rachel becomes Levi's wife. Crucially, Rachel had already become an *ervah* to Yissachar at step two — she was *eishet achiv she-lo haya be-olamo* to him — and this status remains even after Levi performs *yibbum* with her. An *ervah* relationship, once established, persists.

Now, looking ahead: when Zevulun will later be born, Levi will already be married to Rachel. Rachel will therefore not be *eishet achiv she-lo haya be-olamo* with respect to Zevulun, because by the time Zevulun enters the world she is already a married woman — Levi's wife — and not a free widow. Rachel is thus an *ervah* to Yissachar but not to Zevulun.

### Second Death-Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת שִׁמְעוֹן, נוֹלַד זְבוּלֻן, וְיִיבֵּם יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Afterward, **Shimon died, Zevulun was born, and Yehuda consummated the levirate marriage** with Shimon's wife.

Step four: Shimon dies. Step five: Zevulun is born. Since Zevulun was born only after Shimon had already died, Leah — Shimon's widow — is *eishet achiv she-lo haya be-olamo* with respect to Zevulun. Leah is therefore an *ervah* to Zevulun.

### Crossed Ervah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה — מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה. וְהָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה — מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה. וַאֲחוֹתָהּ שֶׁהִיא יְבִמְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In this scenario, the woman who had been Reuven's wife **is forbidden to this** brother, Yissakhar, as the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist, because he was not alive at the same time as Reuven, and yet **she is** nevertheless **permitted to this** brother, Zevulun, because when Zevulun was born this woman was already the wife of Levi, a brother with whom he did coexist. According to Rabbi Shimon, the fact that she had previously been the wife of Reuven, a brother with whom he did not coexist, is not taken into account. Similarly, with regard to Shimon's wife, we find that **she who is forbidden to this** one **is permitted to that** one. Shimon's wife is forbidden to Zevulun as the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist, but she is nevertheless permitted to Yissakhar. Accordingly, the situation of **her sister who is her *yevama*,** i.e., two sisters who are *yevamot* and yet are allowed to perform levirate marriage, can be applied to this case as well.

Step six: Yehudah performs *yibbum* with Leah. Now consider the position of Yissachar and Zevulun. Rachel is an *ervah* to Yissachar but permitted to Zevulun; Leah is an *ervah* to Zevulun but permitted to Yissachar. The two sisters are crossed *ervas* — each forbidden to one of the brothers and permitted to the other.

When Levi and Yehudah subsequently die without children and their wives — Rachel and Leah — fall for *yibbum* before Yissachar and Zevulun, the situation is precisely: *asurah lazo muteret lazo va'asurah lazo muteret lazo achotah k'she-hi yevamtah* — each is forbidden to one and permitted to the other, and they are sisters who are each other's *yevamah*. The *gemara* concludes: *mes Levi vi-Yehudah belo banim venaflu lifnei Yissachar u-Zevulun*.

### Why Yehuda Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי יִיבֵּם יְהוּדָה? בְּלָא יִיבֵּם יְהוּדָה נָמֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need to assume that this is referring to a scenario where **Yehuda consummated the levirate marriage? You can find this** possibility **in** a case where **Yehuda did not consummate the levirate marriage as well.** This scenario can also take place with only five brothers and without Yehuda: Reuven and Shimon were married to two sisters, Reuven died, Yissakhar was born, and Levi consummated the levirate marriage with Reuven's wife. Then Shimon died and Zevulun was born. If Levi then dies, Levi's wife is forbidden to Yissakhar as the wife of his brother Reuven, with whom he did not coexist, but she would be permitted to Zevulun because she was already the wife of Levi when Zevulun was born. Shimon's wife would be forbidden to Zevulun but permitted to Yissakhar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם צָרָה. הָא תִּינַח צָרָה, צָרָה דְצָרָה מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is **due to the rival wife.** It was taught in this way in order to make the principle applicable not only to the wives, but also to their rival wives. In this scenario, Levi and Yehuda were previously married to two sisters who then became the rival wives of a wife of a brother with whom one did not coexist. This allows the ruling to apply to the case of rival wives as well. The Gemara notes: **This works out well** if the mishna comes to include only **a rival wife** herself, but **what can be said** about **the rival wife of a rival wife?** How can this case explained? If one aims to include all of the details of the mishna in Rabbi Ḥiyya's principle, then the mishna must be referring not only to a case involving the brothers' rival wives, but also to the rival wives of those rival wives mentioned in the mishna.

The *gemara* then asks: *lama li yibem Yehudah?* — why was it necessary to include Yehudah performing *yibbum* with Leah? Could one not achieve the same scenario with fewer brothers — simply having Shimon die, Zevulun be born, and let the two surviving original brothers both die, leaving Yissachar and Zevulun to face both widows? The *gemara* answers: *mishum tzarah* — because the purpose is also to generate a case of *tzarat ervah*, the co-wife of a forbidden relative. If Yehudah had not performed *yibbum* with Leah, and Shimon had had a co-wife, that co-wife too would have been *eishet achiv she-lo haya be-olamo* to Zevulun, making her also an *ervah* rather than merely a *tzarat ervah*. By including Yehudah's *yibbum*, Chana — Yehudah's original wife — becomes only a *tzarat ervah* rather than an *ervah* herself, and the scenario captures that additional case.

### Rabbi Shimon Only

This entire construction, the *gemara* notes, works only according to Rabbi Shimon. The dispute is this: once Rachel became Levi's wife, does Yissachar's *ervah* status toward her persist? According to Rabbi Shimon, it does: the *ervah* of *eishet achiv she-lo haya be-olamo* was established when Yissachar was born and Rachel was a free widow, and that status is not dissolved when Levi later takes her in *yibbum*. According to the Rabbanan, however, even they would say that once Rachel is Levi's wife when Zevulun is born, she stands as *eishet ach* — a brother's wife — to Zevulun through a current, ordinary marriage, and the *ervah* relationship of *eishet achiv she-lo haya be-olamo* that Yissachar has to her persists in their view as well. The precise contours of the dispute run through the whole elaborate scenario, and the conclusion is that this six-brother construction operates *aliba de-Rabbi Shimon* — according to Rabbi Shimon's position.

## Three Brothers Case

### Three Brother Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶן נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, אוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ, אוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבַת בִּתָּהּ, אוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבַת בְּנָהּ — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **three brothers, two of whom were married to** close relatives, e.g., **two sisters; or a woman and her daughter; or a woman and her daughter's daughter; or a woman and her son's daughter,** if the two brothers who were married to two close relatives died and their wives happened before a third brother for levirate marriage, **then these** two women must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage,** as each of them is a relative of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond. **And Rabbi Shimon exempts** them even from the obligation to perform *ḥalitza*.

The *mishnah* now presents a new scenario: three brothers, two of whom are married to two sisters, and one who is unattached — *mufneh*, literally empty, unoccupied. This is the *mishnah* of *sheloshah achim, shenayim mehem nesuim shetei achayot, ve-echad mufneh*. The case builds on everything established before but introduces a new complication: *maamar*, the rabbinic form of *kiddushin* performed in the context of *yibbum*.

The broader point of this *mishnah* is largely to establish the ruling of Rabbi Shimon. The *mishnah* teaches that in all related cases — two sisters, or a woman and her daughter, or a woman and her granddaughter, or a woman and the daughter of her son — *harei elu choltzot velo mityabmot*, they all perform *chalitzah* and not *yibbum*. But Rabbi Shimon goes further: *aval Rabbi Shimon poter* — Rabbi Shimon exempts them even from *chalitzah*. Because when a *zikah* would, if consummated, create a forbidden union of two sisters or mother and daughter, Rabbi Shimon treats it as a complete *ervah* situation, deriving from the verse *ve-isha el achotah lo tikach litzror* — do not take a woman together with her sister to create friction — that when the sisters become co-*yevamot*, there is no bond of *yibbum* at all, not even one requiring *chalitzah*. This is a striking position: what the Rabbanan treat as a rabbinic prohibition, Rabbi Shimon regards as a complete biblical exemption from the *mitzvah* entirely.

### Death and Maamar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה אַחַת מֵהֶן אֲסוּרָה עָלָיו אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — אָסוּר בָּהּ, וּמוּתָּר בַּאֲחוֹתָהּ. אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה אוֹ אִיסּוּר קְדוּשָּׁה — חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** If **one of them was forbidden to him,** the third brother, due to **a prohibition against forbidden relatives,** then **he is prohibited from** marrying **her but is permitted to** marry **her sister.** Because the woman who is forbidden to him is not considered to be a woman who requires him for levirate marriage, there is only one woman who happens before him for levirate marriage. However, if one of the women was forbidden due to **a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva or a prohibition** stemming from **sanctity,** then **they** must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.** This is because these prohibitions do not completely cancel the levirate bond.

The *mishnah*'s specific case: Reuven and Shimon are married to Rachel and Leah; Levi is unattached. Reuven dies. At that point, with Shimon still alive, there is no problem of *achot zekukato* — Levi could perform *yibbum* with Rachel without difficulty. Instead, Levi performs *maamar* with Rachel — the rabbinic act of *kiddushin* that a *yavam* may perform with his *yevamah* in lieu of or as a prelude to *yibbum*. Then, after the *maamar*, Shimon dies.

### Maamar Marriage Status

Now the question crystallizes. If Levi had performed full *yibbum* — actual consummation — with Rachel, then when Shimon dies and Leah falls before him, Leah would simply go free as *achot ishto*, his wife's sister. There would be no issue. But Levi performed only *maamar*, not *yibbum*. Is *maamar* sufficient to transform Rachel into his wife at the biblical level, such that Leah is truly considered *achot ishto* and may leave without *chalitzah*? Or does the *maamar* leave his relationship with Rachel in a kind of intermediate state — enough to matter rabbinically, but insufficient to achieve the full biblical transformation that would fully exempt Leah?

Two questions arise: first, may Levi now consummate the relationship with Rachel despite the new *zikah* to Leah — that is, does the *maamar* dissolve the *achot zekukato* concern even at the rabbinic level? Second, and more strikingly, may Leah simply walk away without *chalitzah* at all — does the *maamar* transform Rachel into Levi's wife sufficiently at the biblical level to make Leah *achot ishto* in the full sense?

### Beit Shammai Allow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אִשְׁתּוֹ עִמּוֹ, וְהַלֵּזוּ תֵּצֵא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** In this case, **Beit Shammai say: His wife remains with him.** The woman he betrothed is considered like his wife, and he is not required to divorce her. **And this other** woman **leaves** the *yavam* and is exempt from levirate marriage **as the sister of a wife.**

Beit Shammai rule: *ishto imo, ve-halazu teitzei mishum achot isha* — his wife is with him, meaning he may consummate the relationship with Rachel, and Leah goes free on account of being his wife's sister. Beit Shammai treats the combination of *zikah* plus *maamar* as sufficient to establish Rachel as his wife even at the biblical level. Leah does not require even *chalitzah*.

### Beit Hillel Stringent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט וּבַחֲלִיצָה, וְאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו בַּחֲלִיצָה. זוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ: אוֹי לוֹ עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְאוֹי לוֹ עַל אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו.
>
> **Translation:** Being as he had not yet entered into marriage with the first woman, he is required to perform levirate marriage with both women. Therefore, **he divorces his wife,** i.e., the woman to whom he performed levirate betrothal, **with a bill of divorce,** which nullifies levirate betrothal, **and by *ḥalitza*,** which nullifies the levirate bond. **And,** he sends away **the wife of his** second **brother with *ḥalitza*** as well. They comment: **This is** the case **that** was referred to when the Sages **said: Woe unto him for his wife and woe unto him for the wife of his brother.** Due to the combination of circumstances, he loses them both.

Beit Hillel take the opposing stringent position: *motzi et ishto b'get u-v'chalitzah ve-et eshet achiv b'chalitzah*. Beit Hillel hold that *maamar* alone is not sufficient to dissolve the *achot zekukato* problem. Once Shimon dies, Leah falls into *zikah* with Levi, and since Levi has not fully consummated with Rachel through *yibbum*, his relationship with Rachel has not fully transformed. Leah is still enough of a *zekukah* to make Rachel *achot zekukato*, and therefore Levi may not consummate with Rachel either.

The result for Beit Hillel is that Levi must: first, give Rachel a *get* on account of the *maamar* — since *maamar* is a rabbinic *kiddushin* and the rabbinic dissolution of *maamar* requires a *get*; second, perform *chalitzah* with Rachel on account of the original *zikah* that was never resolved through *yibbum*; and third, perform *chalitzah* with Leah for her *zikah*. Everything requires formal dissolution.

### Consequences Stated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ: אוֹי לוֹ עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ וְאוֹי לוֹ עַל אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו.
>
> **Translation:** They comment: **This is** the case **that** was referred to when the Sages **said: Woe unto him for his wife and woe unto him for the wife of his brother.** Due to the combination of circumstances, he loses them both.

*Zu hi she'amru: oy li al ishto ve-oy li al eshet achiv* — this is what the Sages meant when they said, woe for my wife and woe for my brother's wife. According to Beit Shammai everyone benefits: Rachel and Levi consummate their relationship, and Leah goes free without *chalitzah*. According to Beit Hillel, both women — Rachel and Leah — lose out: neither gains a proper resolution, and both require formal proceedings to extricate themselves. Notably, even in this lament, Rachel is already called *ishto*, his wife — Beit Shammai calls her that, reflecting that *kiddushin* through *maamar* did take effect at the rabbinic level.

## Maamar Analysis

### Maamar Not Complete

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לָא תֵּימָא מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי קוֹנֶה קִנְיָן גָּמוּר, דְּאִי בָּעֵי לְאַפּוֹקֵי סַגִּי לַהּ בְּגִיטָּא, אֶלָּא מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה אֶלָּא לִדְחוֹת בַּצָּרָה בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the main issue of levirate betrothal, it would seem from this mishna that Beit Shammai hold that levirate betrothal has the legal force of levirate marriage. However, **Rabbi Elazar said: Do not say** that **levirate betrothal acquires** the woman **as a full-fledged acquisition according to** the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** such that the first woman becomes his wife to the extent **that if he wants to divorce** her **a bill of divorce would suffice** without an act of *ḥalitza*. **Rather, for Beit Shammai, levirate betrothal acquires** the woman **only** insofar **as it precludes a rival wife** from entering levirate marriage. The legal force of levirate betrothal is limited to preventing the rival wife of the sister from performing levirate marriage; she is exempt from both levirate marriage and *ḥalitza*.

Rabbi Elazar clarifies the precise position of Beit Shammai regarding *maamar*: *lo teima maamar l'Beit Shammai koneh kinyan gamur* — do not say that *maamar* for Beit Shammai is a complete *kinyan* that fully replaces *yibbum* in every respect.

### Divorce Requirements

The test case: if after performing *maamar* Levi wished to dissolve the relationship with Rachel, would a *get* alone suffice, or would he also need to perform *chalitzah*? If *maamar* were a complete *kinyan* equivalent to *yibbum*, then since consummated *yibbum* would require only a *get* upon divorce, *maamar* treated as complete *yibbum* should likewise require only a *get*. But Rabbi Elazar implies that even Beit Shammai would require *chalitzah* in addition to the *get* if the relationship were to be dissolved.

### Partial Transformation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה אֶלָּא לִדְחוֹת בַּצָּרָה בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, for Beit Shammai, levirate betrothal acquires** the woman **only** insofar **as it precludes a rival wife** from entering levirate marriage. The legal force of levirate betrothal is limited to preventing the rival wife of the sister from performing levirate marriage; she is exempt from both levirate marriage and *ḥalitza*.

The formulation is: *maamar l'Beit Shammai eino koneh ella lidchot b'tzarah bilvad* — *maamar* for Beit Shammai only achieves the effect of pushing away the co-wife. It is sufficient to treat Leah as *achot ishto* so that she need not perform *chalitzah*, and it is sufficient for Levi to consummate with Rachel without the obstacle of *achot zekukato*. But it does not fully transform the relationship with Rachel such that *chalitzah* is no longer relevant if they were to separate. The relationship stands in an intermediate state: enough to push Leah away, but not a complete *kinyan*.

### Rabbi Avin Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יְקַיְּימוּ. יְקַיְּימוּ אִין — לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Avin said: We, too, learn** in the mishna a proof that levirate betrothal is not a full-fledged act of acquisition, even according to Beit Shammai. The mishna at the beginning of this chapter (26a) teaches that when two sisters who are *yevamot* happen before two brothers for levirate marriage, if the brothers married their wives before consulting the court, then **Beit Shammai say: They may maintain them** as their wives. From here it can be deduced: If they already married them, **yes, they may maintain them.** However, marrying them ***ab initio*, no,** this is prohibited due to the prohibition against marrying the sister of a woman with whom one has a levirate bond.

Rabbi Avin adds: *af anan nami tanina* — we too have taught this from an earlier *mishnah*. In the previous case involving four brothers and two sisters, if the brothers went ahead and performed *yibbum* despite the *achot zekukah* situation — that is, in violation of the rabbinic prohibition — Beit Shammai says *yekayemu*, they may remain married. This is *be-di'avad* — after the fact, they did something impermissible but the marriage holds. The *achot zekukah* prohibition is rabbinic, and once *yibbum* was performed, the marriage stands.

### Why Not Maamar First

This raises an obvious question: given that Beit Shammai holds *maamar* pushes away the co-wife and lets the remaining *yibbum* proceed, why not do the following? In the original four-brother case where *achot zekukah* applies, have Levi perform *maamar* with Rachel first — not *yibbum*, just *maamar*, which nobody prohibited — and once *maamar* transforms Rachel into his wife (pushing Leah off the scene), then perform *yibbum* with Rachel legitimately. If *maamar* is enough to push Leah off entirely, why can one not do *maamar* first and then *yibbum*, thereby achieving a clean and fully permissible outcome?

The fact that the *mishnah* does not permit this, and instead says that doing *yibbum* in such a case is only permissible *be-di'avad*, must mean that this trick does not work.

### Maamar Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאֲמָר דְּהֵיתֵּירָא דַחִי; מַאֲמָר דְּאִיסּוּרָא לָא דַחִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Permissible betrothal effective; prohibited betrothal ineffective.**

The answer: *maamar de-heiteira dachi; maamar de-issura lo dachi* — *maamar* that is performed permissibly works; *maamar* performed in the presence of a competing *zikah* does not work. In the case of the *mishnah* — the three-brother case — the *maamar* was done while Shimon was still alive and before any problem of *achot zekukato* arose. At the moment of the *maamar*, it was entirely permissible, and when Shimon subsequently dies, Leah falls before a man who already has a legitimate *maamar* with Rachel. The *maamar* was *de-heiteira* — done in a state of full permissibility.

But in the four-brother case, attempting to do *maamar* after both brothers have died and the *zikah* to both sisters already exists — that is a *maamar de-issura*. Since Levi is not permitted to perform *yibbum* with Rachel in the face of the *achot zekukato* problem, the *maamar* with Rachel cannot carry any legal weight. If *yibbum* itself is unavailable, *maamar* — which is a rabbinic shadow of *yibbum* — loses its significance. The trick is therefore unavailable.

### Timing Makes Difference

Having resolved that question, the analysis returns to whether *maamar* is a full *kinyan* or only a partial one. Whichever position one takes — whether *maamar* for Beit Shammai is a hundred percent or only ninety-five percent — the *maamar de-heiteira / maamar de-issura* distinction already disposes of the question of why the trick was not available. What remains is the separate, unresolved question: when *maamar* does work, how strong is it? Is it complete enough that if Levi and Rachel then separate, only a *get* is required — or does *chalitzah* remain necessary as well? That is the question Rabbi Elazar raised, and it continues to drive the analysis forward.
