# Shabbat 142 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Muktzah with Permitted I…

### Intro: Child/Basket Cases

Today's daf is 142, and we pick up on the last line of 141b. The *mishna* there spoke about three related cases: picking up one's son when the child is holding a stone, moving a basket that has a stone inside it, and carrying *terumah teme'ah* together with *terumah tehorah*. The common thread running through all three cases is that *muktzah* is being moved together, as a single unit, with permitted items.

It is worth distinguishing this arrangement from *tiltul min hatzad*. *Tiltul min hatzad* — the indirect motion discussed previously — describes a one-time push, where one moves a *muktzah* item out of the way incidentally in order to reach something else. The cases in our *mishna* are different: the movement is sustained and ongoing, and although one is primarily moving a permitted item, the *muktzah* is along for the ride — a stone in the child's hand, a stone in the basket, and so on. The *mishna* indicates that this is permitted.

As we will see, however, much of today's daf — particularly the first *amud* — proceeds on the assumption that this is fundamentally *not* permitted, and therefore sets about redefining and severely limiting each case in the *mishna*.

### Rava's Secondary Items

Part of what drives the *gemara*'s restrictive reading is Rava's ruling, with which we concluded yesterday: if one carries an infant with a money pouch around its neck into a *reshut harabbim*, one is exempt regarding the infant but *chayav* regarding the money pouch. The pouch is not considered secondary to the infant — unlike clothing, or a chair on which the person sits. Since it is not *tafel* (secondary), one is fully liable for carrying it.

The *gemara* proceeds on the assumption that the same parameters that define liability for carrying also govern the prohibition of moving *muktzah*. If that is correct, then moving a basket with a stone inside, or picking up a child holding a stone, should be treated no differently from carrying the stone directly. This inference will drive a wholesale reinterpretation of the *mishna*.

### Gemara's Challenge

The *gemara* opens with the first case: *notel adam et bno ve-even be-yado* — one may pick up his son while the child holds a stone. But how does this square with Rava's ruling that one would be *chayav* for the money pouch in an analogous carrying case? If the same logic applies here, picking up the child should be treated as directly moving the stone.

### Rabbi Yannai's Exception

The house of Rabbi Yannai answers: *betinok she-yesh lo ga'agu'in al aviv* — we are speaking of an infant who has a particular emotional longing for his father, such that if the father does not pick him up, the child will fall into acute distress. Under those circumstances, the rabbis allowed it — Rashi describes it as approaching a situation of *choleh*, someone whose welfare is at stake — and therefore it is a special dispensation, not a general rule. Needless to say, this is a strained reading of the *mishna*, which states the allowance without any such qualification.

### Stone vs. Coin Question

Proceeding to the top of 142a, the *gemara* asks: *mai irya even afilu dinar nami* — if we are granting a special allowance because the child is in emotional distress, why does it matter whether the child holds a stone or a *dinar*? The allowance should apply equally in either case. Yet Rava himself rules that while a stone is permitted, a *dinar* is *assur*. What is the basis for this distinction, whatever the underlying theory of the *mishna* may be?

The *gemara* answers: *even ee nafal lo ati avuye le-atuyei* — if the child drops the stone, the father will not bother to pick it up. But *dinar ee nafal ati avuye le-atuyei* — if the child drops a coin, the father will come to retrieve it. The concern, then, is that permitting the coin will lead to a direct act of moving *muktzah*. It functions as a kind of *gezeirah* on *muktzah*: we allow the emotional-distress dispensation, but not in a case where there is a realistic risk that the *muktzah* will be directly handled. By extension, anything sufficiently valuable that the father would retrieve it falls into the same prohibition.

### Garment Baraita

The *gemara* then brings a *baraita* in support of Rava's general principle — returning now to the domain of carrying rather than *muktzah*. If someone takes his garments folded on his shoulder, or carries his sandals and rings in his hand rather than wearing them, he is *chayav*. If, however, he is wearing all these items, he is exempt. The *baraita* then addresses a compound case: if someone carries another person outside, and that person is wearing his clothes, with sandals on his feet and rings on his fingers, the carrier is exempt — for the person (*chai nosei et atzmo*, the living being carries himself), and for the clothes (secondary to the person). Rashi notes that the word *tab'otav b'yadav* in the second scenario implies the rings are on the fingers, especially given the parallel with *sandalo b'raglo*, and that the plural form reinforces this reading.

The critical clause follows: *ve-ilu hotzian k'mot she-hen* — but if the person being carried is holding his clothing rather than wearing it, the carrier is *chayav*. This directly supports Rava: something a person holds is not *tafel* (secondary) to that person. Clothing one wears, or a chair one sits in, is genuinely secondary — but an item one merely holds in one's hand remains independent.

The *gemara* then notes that this principle of carrying is being applied to the laws of *muktzah*, which is striking. The laws of *muktzah* have their own distinct parameters — one can perform *tiltul b'gufo*, moving *muktzah* with one's body, as we learned recently, something entirely inapplicable to carrying. Clearly the two domains operate differently. Yet the *gemara* insists on the parallel, and this insistence will drive it to reinterpret every case in the *mishna* as a special circumstance. Let us now turn to those cases.

## Basket/Stone/Fruit Case

### Basis (Basis) Principle

The *gemara* turns to the next case: *kalkalah ve-ha-even b'tochah* — a basket with a stone inside. According to the *mishna*, one may move the basket. But the *gemara* raises an immediate difficulty even before the question of directly moving the stone: *ve-havei kalkalah basis l'davar ha-asur* — the basket itself becomes a *basis l'davar ha-asur*, a base for a prohibited item. Since the stone was resting in the basket when *Shabbat* began, the basket acquires *muktzah* status along with the stone, and cannot be moved at all.

### Fruit Requirement

Rabba bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *hacha b'kalkalah m'lei'ah peirot askinan* — we are dealing with a basket that contains not only a stone but also fruit. A *basis l'davar ha-asur v'davar ha-mutar* — a base for both a prohibited and a permitted item — does not acquire *basis* status. This is the source of the familiar practice of placing *challah* on a candelabrum tray before *Shabbat*, so that the tray does not become a *basis* solely for the candles. Similarly, a drawer containing both *muktzah* and permitted items does not become a *basis*.

The additional detail that there is fruit in the basket also strengthens the *mishna*'s case in another way: one can now say that one is primarily moving the fruit, just as one is primarily picking up the child — the *muktzah* is merely incidental. The *gemara*, however, having already established its restrictive approach, remains dissatisfied.

### Shake-Out Objection

The *gemara* objects: *v'lishdinhu l'peirei v'lishdyeih l'even v'ninktinhu b'yadeih* — why not simply tip the basket out, allow the fruit and stone to fall together, and then pick up only the fruit? If there is a way to avoid moving the stone, that alternative must be pursued. The *gemara* is entirely consistent in its approach: whenever one has another option, the blanket allowance of the *mishna* does not apply. Rabbi Ilai in the name of Rav answers — as originally stated in connection with the following case in the *mishna* but here applied earlier — that we are dealing with *peirot ha-mitnapnim*, fruit that is easily soiled. Emptying out the basket would ruin the fruit, so that option is not available.

The *gemara* presses further: *v'liernahu niurei* — why not at least shake the basket to dislodge the stone, perhaps turning it at an angle so the stone slides out while the fruit remains?

### Stone as Plug Solution

Rav Chiya bar Ashi said in the name of Rav: *hacha b'kalkala p'chuta askinan* — we are dealing with a basket that has a hole in it, *d'even gufa na'asis dofen l'kalkala* — and the stone itself is serving as a plug for that hole. Shaking the basket to remove the stone would cause all the fruit to pour out through the hole. One cannot be expected to do that. Therefore, there is no viable alternative, and moving the basket together with the stone is permitted.

This is a remarkable reading of the *mishna*. The upshot of the *gemara*'s entire analysis of this case is that the *mishna*'s seemingly general allowance — move the basket with the stone inside — is in fact limited to a very narrow scenario: one where there is genuinely no other way to avoid moving the *muktzah*. The first case of the *mishna*, the child with the stone, is similarly limited to an infant in acute emotional distress. Far from a general rule, each case becomes a barely-recognizable exception.

One might observe that this also has implications for the *basis* question raised by students during the shiur: if the *gemara* demands that one shake out or separate the *muktzah* whenever possible before moving the *heter*, that same logic would seem to require removing the permitted item from the *basis* whenever possible rather than simply moving the whole together. The *gemara* is quite consistent in pressing this conclusion.

## Mixed Terumah Case

### Terumah Mixing Rules

The *gemara* now turns to the third case in the *mishna*: *metaltlin terumah teme'ah im hatehorah* — one may carry *terumah teme'ah* together with *terumah tehorah*. The *terumah tehorah* is permitted and not *muktzah*; the *teme'ah* is prohibited and effectively *muktzah*. The *mishna* permits moving them together.

### Rav Chisda's Conditions

Rav Chisda refuses to accept this as a blanket allowance. He rules: *lo shanu ela shetehorah lemata utme'ah lemala* — this applies only when the *tehorah* grain is on the bottom and the *teme'ah* grain is on top, so that there is no practical way to extract the *tehorah* grain by itself. In that case, one has no option but to move everything together.

*Aval tehorah lemala utme'ah lemata* — but if the *tehorah* grain is on top and the *teme'ah* is below, *shakil lei l'tahor v'shavik lei l'tamai* — one should simply remove the *tehorah* grain and leave the *teme'ah* behind. To move the entire mixture in that case is impermissible, because an alternative exists.

### Baraita Challenge

The *gemara* objects from a *baraita*: *metaltlin terumah teme'ah im hatehorah v'im hachullin* — one may carry *teme'ah terumah* together with *tehorah terumah* or with *chullin*. The *baraita* then explicitly addresses both positions: *bein shehatahorah lemala utme'ah lemata, bein sheteme'ah lemala vehatahorah lemata* — whether the *tehorah* is above and the *teme'ah* below, or the *teme'ah* is above and the *tehorah* below. Either way, it is permitted. This appears to directly contradict Rav Chisda.

### Purpose Determines Oblig…

Rav Chisda responds: *matnisin letzorech gufo, braita letzorech mekomo* — the *mishna* addresses the case where one needs the grain itself; the *baraita* addresses the case where one needs the space occupied by the basket.

The distinction is significant. If one's purpose is to access the grain — specifically the *tehorah* grain — and the *tehorah* grain happens to be on top, then one has a viable alternative: simply remove the *tehorah* grain without disturbing the *teme'ah*. In that case, one must do so. But if one's purpose is to clear the location — to move the entire basket out of the way — then removing only the *tehorah* grain would leave one with a basket of *teme'ah* grain still sitting in place. That does not accomplish the goal. Since no viable alternative exists, one may move the whole basket regardless of which grain is on top.

The *gemara* then raises a counter-question against Rav Chisda's own framing: the case of needing the grain could have simply been resolved by saying one always needs to move the basket — in which case there would be no distinction between above and below, since one always needs the whole basket moved. Why did Rav Chisda assume the *mishna* spoke specifically about needing the grain?

### Rava's Reading Context

Rava answers: *matnisin kevasei dika* — the precise language of the *mishna* itself supports Rav Chisda's reading. The indication comes from the end of the *mishna* — which for us is the next *mishna*: one who has money on a pillow may shake the pillow and let the coins fall off in order to use the pillow. Rabba bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *lo shanu ela letzorech gufo* — this is only when one needs the pillow itself. *Aval letzorech mekomo* — if one needs to clear the space, then shaking off the coins will not help, since the coins will still be lying on the floor in the same spot. In that case, *metaltelo ve'odan alav* — one may move the pillow with the coins still on it.

Since the *mishna*'s case of the pillow is one where one needs the pillow itself, Rava reasons that the corresponding case of the grain at the beginning of the *mishna* is likewise one where one needs the grain. *Umi-de-seifa letzorech gufo reisha nami letzorech gufo* — since the end of the *mishna* deals with needing the item itself, so too the beginning. This lends retroactive support to Rav Chisda's distinction.

The pattern is by now entirely consistent: the *gemara* permits moving a *heter* together with *muktzah* only when no viable alternative exists. Where one has a way to access the permitted item without touching the *muktzah*, that alternative must be pursued.

## Terumah Separation on Sh…

### Rabbi Yehudah's Allowance

After addressing the *muktzah* dimensions of the *mishna*, the *gemara* turns to a different ruling at the end of the same *mishna*: *Rabbi Yehuda omer af ma'alin* — Rabbi Yehudah says one may also perform *ha'ala'ah*, the act of removing a unit of grain from a *dimua* mixture (a mixture of *terumah* and *chullin* that has lost its *bitul*) on *Shabbat*, in order to permit the remainder.

### Why Special Case?

The *gemara* asks: *v'amai, ha ka metaken* — why is this permitted? One is effectively fixing the food, rendering it fit for consumption, which is normally prohibited on *Shabbat* just as separating *terumos u'ma'asros* is forbidden.

The *gemara* first suggests: *Rabbi Yehuda k'Rabbi Eliezer sevira leh* — Rabbi Yehudah holds like Rabbi Eliezer, who maintains *terumah b'eini machata* — that in a *dimua* situation, the unit of *terumah* to be removed is considered as if it were already in a distinct, removed state.

The *gemara* derives this from a *mishna*: if one *se'ah* of *terumah* fell into fewer than a hundred units of *chullin*, rendering the whole mixture *dimua* (prohibited because the *terumah* is not *batel*), and then one unit from that mixture fell into a different container of *chullin*, Rabbi Eliezer says that fallen unit is treated as if it were entirely *terumah* — *vada'i* — and creates a *dimua* in its new location according to its full quantity, not proportionally. The sages, by contrast, say the unit *medama* only according to its proportion: since only 1/90th of it is *terumah*, only that fraction creates a problem in the new mixture. Rabbi Eliezer's position implies that the moment one unit needs to be removed, it is conceptually already removed — and any unit that subsequently departs from the mixture is treated as that designated unit, entirely *terumah*.

The *gemara* then asks whether this reasoning could be applied *le'kula* as well: if the unit is considered already removed, perhaps separating it on *Shabbat* involves no real act of fixing? *Eimar de'shamas leh l'chumra* — we only have evidence that Rabbi Eliezer applies this reasoning stringently, never leniently.

### Mental Designation Method

The *gemara* then proposes a different alignment: *ela hu de'amar k'Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — Rabbi Yehudah holds like Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. A *baraita* records: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says, *nosen einav b'tzad zeh v'ochel mitzad acher* — one may mentally designate a unit in one corner of the pile and then eat from the rest. No physical separation is required; mental designation suffices.

The *gemara* connects the two positions: Rabbi Yehudah's allowance to physically separate the unit, and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's allowance to merely designate it mentally, share the same underlying logic — if mental designation is sufficient to render the grain permitted, then the *dimua* is not really in need of a full act of *tikkun*. The physical separation is not seen as a formal act of fixing, because the *terumah* in a *batel* *dimua* is already considered conceptually removed. Rabbi Yehudah simply goes one step further and permits the actual physical act as well.

The *gemara* verifies this alignment from a *baraita* on 142b: *Rabbi Yehuda omer ma'alin es hamiduma b'echad u'me'ah* — Rabbi Yehudah permits physical separation. *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar omer nosen einav b'tzad zeh v'ochel mitzad acher* — Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar permits only mental designation. They are thus in debate. The *gemara* concludes: *Rabbi Yehudah azil k'Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — he follows the same underlying principle but takes it one step further, reasoning that if even the act of mental designation does not constitute fixing, then actual physical separation — which is no more substantial in terms of *tikkun* given that the *terumah* is already *batel* — should equally be permissible on *Shabbat*.

### Fixing vs. Bitul Distinc…

A crucial conceptual distinction underlies this entire discussion. There is a difference between saying that the grain is *fully fixed* and saying that the *terumah* is already *batel*. Under the principle of *kama kama batel*, the *terumah* becomes *batel* sequentially as each unit falls in; one does not need to posit that the designated unit has already been removed. Rather, the *terumah* is genuinely *batel* — though the rabbinic obligation to remove a unit remains. This means that in a second-degree mixture, when a unit from the *dimua* falls into a new container of *chullin*, one need not treat that unit as full *terumah*, because its *terumah* component was already *batel* in the original mixture. The *kama kama batel* principle operates only so long as the proportions remain such that *bitul* applies; once the mixture is detectably *tasted* or a contrary *rov* emerges, one can no longer invoke it.

The point for our discussion is that saying the *terumah* is *batel* does not mean the grain is fully fixed — there is still an obligation to remove a unit. But the prohibition against *tikkun* on *Shabbat* requires an act of real fixing; if the *terumah* is already conceptually *batel*, the act of separation is not a full *tikkun* in the halachic sense, and Rabbi Yehudah permits it.

## Barriers to Removal

### Stone on Barrel

The *gemara* now returns to the core *muktzah* theme, turning to the next *mishna*: *ha'even she'al pi hachavis* — a stone sitting atop a barrel. *Mataltah v'hi nofeles* — one tips the barrel and the stone falls off, allowing access to the barrel or its contents.

*Hayta bein hachaviyos* — but if the barrel is positioned among other barrels, so that tipping it would risk knocking them over (Rashi) or there is simply insufficient room, then one cannot tip it in place. In that case, *magbiya u'mataltah v'hi nofeles* — one lifts the barrel, carries it to a suitable location, and then tips it so the stone falls off.

The case of money on a pillow follows: *ma'os she'al hakar, m'na'er hakar vehen noflos* — one shakes the pillow and the coins fall off, allowing use of the pillow. *Hayta alav lishleshes* — if the pillow has a stain or spittle on it, *m'kanchesah b'smartut* — one wipes it with a rag. *Hayta shel or* — if it is made of leather, one may apply water, since leather is not subject to the prohibition of *libbun* in the same way as cloth. Rashi does note, however, that with soft leather there could in principle be a concern of *kibus*, and he addresses the parameters there.

This *mishna* — particularly its instruction to shake the stone off the barrel whenever possible — is presumably what informed the *gemara*'s restrictive analysis on 142a. The *mishna* there seemed to be a blanket allowance; this *mishna* implies that wherever removal is possible it is required, and moving the *heter* together with the *muktzah* is only a last resort.

### Focus on Heter Principle

*Man tana d'kol haycha d'ika issur veheter beheter tarchinan be'issur lo tarchinan* — who is the *tanna* who holds that whenever there is both an *issur* and a *heter*, we focus our effort on the *heter* and not on the *issur*?

The question is abstract in a way that may seem surprising. After all, directly moving the stone would be a straightforward violation of *muktzah*, while moving the barrel (the *heter*) and tipping the stone off involves no direct handling of *muktzah*. One might think the reasoning is self-evident. Yet the *gemara* elevates this into a general principle — whenever one faces a combination of an *issur* and a *heter*, one must focus on the *heter* even if it is more cumbersome — and asks where else we find this principle.

Rabba bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel hi* — it is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

### Sorting Majority vs. Min…

*D'tenan, haborer kitnit beyom tov* — if one sorts legumes on *Yom Tov*: Beit Shammai says *borer ochel v'ochel* — one selects the food to eat it immediately, much as one performs *borer* on *Shabbat*. Beit Hillel says *borer kedarko* — one may sort in the normal manner. Normally, the most efficient method is to remove the minority element — typically the *psoiles* (chaff or contaminants) — rather than the food itself. Beit Hillel permits this on *Yom Tov*, and further permits using one's lap (*becheyko*) or a vessel (*vetamchui*) to assist.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel then qualifies Beit Hillel's ruling in a *baraita*: *bameh devarim amurim* — when does Beit Hillel permit removing the *psoiles* in the normal manner? *She-ha'ochel merubeh al ha-psoiles* — when the food is more abundant than the refuse, making it easier to remove the minority *psoiles* rather than the majority food. But *aval psoiles merubeh al ha'ochel* — if the refuse is more abundant than the food, *divrei hakol borer ochel* — everyone agrees one must focus on the food. The reason Beit Hillel allows removing the *psoiles* is that it is the more efficient method when it constitutes the minority; if the *psoiles* is the majority, efficiency considerations no longer justify departing from the rule that one focuses on the *heter*.

This is the general principle: we focus on the *heter* unless focusing on the *issur* is significantly easier. The *gemara* then asks: why, then, must one lift and carry the barrel in our case? It would be far easier simply to pick up the stone — which is focusing on the *issur* — and remove it directly. By the logic of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, should we not permit that?

The *gemara* answers: *hacha nami kivan d'ibaei lemishkal lo mishtaklei yayin ad d'shaklei* — in this case too, since one would need to lift the barrel in order to extract all the wine from the bottom, it is not really more laborious to lift the barrel in any case. Focusing on the barrel (the *heter*) is not a significant additional burden, because one would need to lift it to access the contents regardless. Therefore, this is comparable to the case where the *psoiles* is the majority — one must focus on the *heter* and not on the *issur*, since no real imposition is involved in doing so.

It must be acknowledged that the fundamental comparison throughout this passage — between the *borer* question of which category to sort first and the *muktzah* question of whether to handle the stone directly — is puzzling. To pick up the stone is a direct violation of *muktzah*; no one would have imagined this was permitted. To tip the barrel, thereby moving the stone without handling it, is a form of indirect motion entirely distinct from the *borer* analysis. The *gemara* nonetheless draws the comparison and resolves it on the *gemara*'s own terms.

### Money on Pillow Rules

Returning to the case of money on a pillow: Rav Huna said in the name of Rav — *lo shanu ela beshoche'ach* — the allowance to shake the pillow applies only when the money was left there inadvertently. *Aval bemaniach* — if one intentionally placed the money on the pillow before *Shabbat*, *na'asa basis ledavar ha'asur* — the pillow becomes a *basis l'davar ha-asur*, and cannot be touched at all. This ruling applies equally to the earlier case of the barrel and the stone: the barrel is not a *basis* only because the stone was forgotten there, not deliberately placed.

Rabba bar bar Chana, again in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, reiterates: *lo shanu ela l'tzorech gufo* — the instruction to shake off the coins is only when one needs the pillow itself. *Aval l'tzorech mekomo, metaltelo ve'odan alav* — if one needs to clear the space entirely, one may move the pillow with the coins still on it. Chiya bar Rav mi-Difeti teaches similarly in a *baraita*: the allowance to move the pillow with coins on it in the case of needing the space is confirmed.

## Placing Permitted on Muk…

### Wallet Trick

*Shachach arnaki b'chatzer* — if one forgot one's wallet in the courtyard, Rabbi Oshaya rules: *meniach aleha kikar o tinok u'metaltela* — one places a loaf of bread or an infant on top of the wallet and then carries it. This case differs materially from everything discussed before. There, one was moving a *heter* with *muktzah* incidentally along for the ride, and the fundamental object of attention was the *heter*. Here, the object being directly handled is the *muktzah* itself, and the purpose of moving it is the *muktzah*. The bread or infant is added precisely to create the appearance of moving a *heter*. Nevertheless, Rabbi Oshaya permits this because of *hefsed mamon* — concern about financial loss.

### Brick Generalization

Rabbi Yitzchak extends this further: *shachach levana b'chatzer, meniach aleha kikar o tinok u'metaltela* — if one forgot a brick in the courtyard, one may equally place a loaf or infant on it and move it. The trick is not limited to items of high monetary value — even an ordinary brick, if one is concerned about its being damaged or lost, qualifies.

### Sack in Reshut Harabbim

Rav Yehuda bar Shila said in the name of Rav Assi: *paam achat shachechu daskia meleah maot b'istatya* — once they left a sack filled with coins in a *reshut harabbim*. They came and asked Rabbi Yochanan, who instructed them: *henichu aleha kikar o tinok* — place a loaf of bread or an infant on it. Now the difficulty is compounded: besides the *muktzah* problem of the coins, there is also the *d'Oraita* prohibition of carrying in a *reshut harabbim*. Rashi explains that the solution involves moving the sack in increments of fewer than four *amot* at a time — *pachot pachot m'arba amot* — thereby avoiding any single act of carrying that would cross the threshold of liability. Together with the bread or infant solution for the *muktzah*, this produces a multi-layered workaround. As we will see in a coming *mishna*, when one is caught outside with money and faces a *Shabbat* violation, the *gemara* is quite willing to sanction approaches that come close to the edge rather than allow an outright violation.

### Rav Ashi's Restriction

Mar Zutra rules: *hilchata k'chol hanei shematata b'shocheach* — the ruling is in accordance with all of the above positions only when the item was forgotten. If one intentionally left it there before *Shabbat*, this dispensation does not apply.

Rav Ashi goes further: *afilu shachach nami lo* — even if one forgot it, none of this is permitted. *V'lo amru kikar o tinok ela l'met bilvad* — the only context in which the earlier authorities ever sanctioned the bread-or-infant trick was in the case of a *meit*, a dead body: out of respect for the dead, when one needs to move the body out of the sun to prevent decomposition, one may place a permitted item on it and carry it. Outside of that specific case, the technique has no application.

### Abaye/Rava on Shekaach

Abaye and Rava, who preceded Rav Ashi, had practiced differently. *Abaye manach kafa akifi* — Abaye would place a ladle on bundles of wheat and carry them. *Rava manach sakina abar yona umataltela* — Rava would place a knife on a slaughtered pigeon and carry it. Both appear to have used the bread-or-infant technique even in cases other than a *meit*.

Rav Yosef, who was their teacher, responded mockingly: *kama charifa shma'atecha d'dardaki* — how sharp, these young ones. He was not objecting to the principle entirely, but to the specific application: *eima d'amri Rabbanan b'shochei'ach, l'chatchila mi amri* — when the Rabbis permitted this trick, it was only *b'shochei'ach*, for something forgotten. Did they say it may be done *l'chatchila*? Here, one had knowingly left the bundles of wheat or the pigeon outside before *Shabbat*, making this a case of *meniach*, not *shachach*.

Abaye replied: *ilav d'adam chashuv ana, kafa kefi lama li* — were it not that I am a person of distinction, I would not have needed to put the ladle on the bundles at all, because the bundles are not even *muktzah* — *d'chazu l'mizga*, one can recline on them, making them a usable item. He only added the ladle to avoid misleading onlookers, given his standing as an *adam chashuv*. Rava said likewise: *ana sakina abar yona lama li, d'chazi l'umtza* — I would not have needed to put the knife on the pigeon at all, because the pigeon can be eaten raw, and therefore is not *muktzah* to begin with. Again, his care with the knife was a measure appropriate to an *adam chashuv*.

The practical *halacha* follows Rav Ashi's restrictive ruling. The voices of Rav Yosef, Abaye, and Rava represent an earlier and more lenient position, permitting the technique at least in cases of *shachach*; but Rav Ashi, as the later authority who gave the *gemara* its final form, rules that the trick is reserved solely for a *meit*.

## Pigeon & Intended Use

### Raw Pigeon Edibility

The *gemara* now examines Rava's claim that the pigeon is not *muktzah* because *d'chazi l'umtza* — it can be eaten raw. The *gemara* probes: *ta'ama d'chazi l'umtza* — the reason it is not *muktzah* is that it can be eaten raw. *Ha lo chazi l'umtza lo* — implying that if it could *not* be eaten raw, it would be *muktzah*. But why? It could still be given to a dog — and dog food is not *muktzah*.

### Dog Food & Intended Use

The answer the *gemara* draws from this is that Rava holds like Rabbi Yehudah, who maintains a stricter position on *muktzah* than Rabbi Shimon. The critical point is this: if an item was designated as *ma'achal adam* — intended for human consumption — and on *Shabbat* it becomes fit only for *ma'achal behema* (animal consumption), it is *muktzah*. One still intends to use it eventually for people; the fact that right now it can only be given to animals does not change its intended status. It is therefore classified as *muktzah* because it is not presently fit for the use for which it was designated.

This is precisely why Rava needed to say the pigeon *can* be eaten raw: if the pigeon were entirely inedible by humans in its raw state, its status as *ma'achal adam* would be momentarily suspended, and it would be considered *muktzah*.

### Duck Intestines Precedent

To confirm this, the *gemara* cites Rava's own practice: *tavi li bar avza* — he instructed his attendants on *Yom Tov* to roast a duck for him, *ve-shadi maya li-shunra* — and to throw the intestines to the cat. This seems problematic: the intestines of a duck are in principle edible for humans (in the fashion of sweetbreads), and by assigning them to the cat, Rava appears to be treating *ma'achal adam* as *ma'achal behema*, which should create a *muktzah* problem.

The *gemara* answers: *hasam kivan di-masrach daito alavei mei-etmol* — there, from the outset of *Yom Tov*, it was already clear that the intestines would spoil if saved for later. They had to be disposed of during *Yom Tov*, and since leaving them would cause them to spoil, they were never genuinely designated as *ma'achal adam* to begin with. One knew from the very beginning that the meat would serve the people and the intestines the cat. An item that was never intended as *ma'achal adam* is not *muktzah* when given to animals. Only an item initially set aside as *ma'achal adam* that then becomes edible only for animals falls into the *muktzah* category according to Rabbi Yehudah.

*Hachi nami mistavra, de-Rava ke-Rabbi Yehuda sevira lei* — and indeed this is the logical conclusion: Rava holds like Rabbi Yehudah.

### Nolad & Broken Vessels

The *gemara* adduces further support from a teaching of Rava himself: *isha lo tikanes le-veis ha-eitzim litol me-hen ud* — a woman may not enter the woodshed to take a poker on *Yom Tov*. *Ve-ud she-nishbar asur le-hasiko be-Yom Tov* — and a poker or spit that breaks on *Yom Tov* may not be used as firewood. The basis: *lefi she-masikin be-keilim ve-ein masikin be-shivrei keilim* — while one may use whole vessels as firewood on *Yom Tov*, one may not use broken vessels, because the broken state is *nolad* — a new reality that came into being on *Yom Tov* itself. This demonstrates that Rava holds of *nolad*, which is the position of Rabbi Yehudah.

The *gemara* thus concludes from this: *shma mina de-Rava k'Rabbi Yehuda sevira lei* — one may infer that Rava holds like Rabbi Yehudah. The *gemara* concedes that holding like Rabbi Yehudah on *nolad* does not automatically entail holding like him on the *ma'achal adam / ma'achal behema* distinction — the two issues are logically independent — but the *gemara* groups them together as dimensions of Rabbi Yehudah's more stringent approach to *muktzah*, and reasons that since Rava holds like Rabbi Yehudah on *nolad*, it makes sense that he likewise holds like him on the *ma'achal adam* question. This is why Rava needed to establish that the pigeon was fit for raw human consumption: operating within Rabbi Yehudah's framework, an item intended as *ma'achal adam* that is currently fit only for *ma'achal behema* would be *muktzah*, and Rava had to demonstrate that no such problem arose.
