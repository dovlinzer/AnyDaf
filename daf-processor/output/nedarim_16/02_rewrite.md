# Nedarim 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Neder vs. Shevuah

### Previous Daf Review

The present *daf* picks up from the *mishnah* at the bottom of 15b, which addressed a range of fundamental conceptual issues. Among them: the husband's and wife's respective rights over one another's person, and how that mutual quasi-ownership bears on the enforceability of a *neder*. The sugya also treated the concept of *bal yachel* — specifically, a rabbinic *bal yachel* applying even to abstract matters such as sleeping or walking, where no physical object is directly involved. Finally, it examined whether a *minhag* of treating certain things as forbidden can rise to the level of a *neder*, particularly in the context of *mitzvah*-related acts, which have their own relationship to the more biblical dimensions of the prohibition on vow violation.

### Ran vs. Rambam

One critical point from the previous discussion deserves brief review: the dispute between the Ran and the Rambam regarding who receives lashes for violating a *neder*. The Ran holds that lashes follow the violation of the object's status — once the object has been rendered forbidden, anyone who violates that status receives lashes. The Rambam disagrees. He maintains that *lo yachel devaro* — the biblical prohibition — is violated only by the person who both made the vow and then broke it. It is not that the Rambam permits a third party to violate the *neder*; rather, in his view, the biblical lashes are reserved for the person whose own word is being desecrated. The Ran focuses exclusively on the object's status; the Rambam focuses on the act of personal betrayal of one's own oath.

### Shevuah Actions

The *mishnah* now states: *shevuah she'aini yashen, she'aini medaber, she'aini mehalech — assur*. If someone takes a *shevuah* that he will not sleep, not speak, or not walk, it is forbidden. This is relatively straightforward, since a *shevuah* concerning actions — committing oneself not to perform a particular act — is the classic form of a *shevuah*. The notable *chiddush* was actually in the prior *mishnah*, which established that even a *konam* formulation applied to abstract actions like sleeping or speaking is binding, albeit only rabbinically.

### Neder Language

There is also an important linguistic distinction between the two instruments. A *shevuah* is articulated in the negative: *she'aini mehalech* — "I will not walk." A *neder*, by contrast, is articulated in the positive: *she'ani mehalech* — "my walking is forbidden," rendering the act itself a kind of *korban*-like prohibition. Furthermore, as the earlier analysis established, when the *neder* concerns an abstract action rather than a concrete physical object, its force is only rabbinic, whereas the *shevuah* in the same case operates at the biblical level.

## Korban Formulations

### Korban Lo Ochal

The *mishnah* now turns to a series of *neder* formulations involving the word *korban*, contrasting those that are binding with those that are not. Three formulations are presented as non-binding: *korban lo ochel lecha* ("a *korban* is my not-eating of yours"), *ha-korban she-ochel lecha*, and *lo korban lo ochel lecha*.

The first case is relatively clear. When someone says *korban lo ochel lecha*, they are framing the *neder* around the non-eating — essentially saying "a *korban* is my not eating your food." But a *neder* operates by rendering the object forbidden; the classic formulation is *korban she-ochel lecha* — "that which I would eat of yours is a *korban*." Framing the *neder* around the negative does not accomplish this. At most it could be read as an attempt to swear in the name of a *korban* that one will not eat — which is *lashon shevuah*, not *lashon neder* — and in any case one cannot take a *shevuah* in the name of a *korban*. So this formulation fails entirely.

### Ha-Korban Pause

The middle case is the most interesting. *Korban she-ochel lecha* — without the *heh* — would indeed be a binding *neder*: "that which I eat of yours is a *korban*." But if one says *ha-korban she-ochel lecha*, the addition of the *heh* followed by a pause transforms the meaning. Rather than using *korban* as the predicate describing the object's status, the speaker is now using *ha* as an interjection — "Behold! By a *korban*, I will eat of yours!" — which shifts the formulation into the realm of *lashon shevuah*: an oath taken in the name of a *korban*. And since one cannot take a *shevuah* in the name of a *korban*, this formulation also fails to bind.

### Klal Lav Atah

The third formulation — *lo korban lo ochel lecha* — might seem promising. If it is not a *korban* that I will not eat, one might reason by *miklal lav atah shomea hen* that what I will eat is indeed a *korban*. However, the *mishnah* does not accept this inference. Our *mishnah* follows the view of Rabbi Meir, who does not apply the principle of *miklal lav atah shomea hen* in this context. The double negative does not suffice to constitute a positive *neder*, and therefore the formulation is *mutar*.

### Gemara Mani

The *gemara* asks: *mani matnisin?* — whose view does our *mishnah* represent? The answer is Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda would not distinguish between *korban* and *ha-korban* at all, because in his view the very word *korban* without a prefix such as *ke-* does not constitute a valid *neder* formulation. Rabbi Yehuda requires *ke-korban lo ochel lecha* — without the *kaf*, neither formulation works. The *mishnah*, however, implies that *korban she-ochel lecha* would be sufficient, which reflects Rabbi Meir's position that prefixes are not required.

The *gemara* then turns to the end of the *mishnah* and notes an apparent tension. Elsewhere we have learned *li-korban lo ochel lecha* — Rabbi Meir says it is forbidden. This is explained by Rabbi Abba: *li-korban* is read as a sentence with a pause — "Let it be a *korban*" — full stop — "and therefore I will not eat of yours." But if that is so, why does the *mishnah* here rule that *lo korban lo ochel lecha* is permitted? The *gemara* answers: there is no difficulty. When one says *li-korban*, the prefix *lamed* signals an affirmative commitment — it should be a *korban*, therefore I will not eat. But when one says *lo korban*, one is saying it is not a *korban* — and to then infer that what I will eat is a *korban* would require applying *miklal lav atah shomea hen*, which Rabbi Meir rejects. The two rulings are thus fully consistent.

## Shevuah Works

### Shevuah Classic

Having established which *neder* formulations fail, the *mishnah* now contrasts these with the parallel *shevuah* formulations — all of which are binding. *Shevuah lo ochel lecha* — a *shevuah* that I will not eat of yours — is the straightforward case, a classic prohibitory oath.

### Ha-Shevuah

The more complex cases are *ha-shevuah she-ochel lecha* and *lo shevuah lo ochel lecha*, both of which the *mishnah* rules are *assur*. This is striking because the exact same linguistic structures that rendered the *neder* formulations invalid are now said to produce binding obligations. The puzzle is particularly sharp regarding *she-ochel lecha* — "that I will eat." Why should saying one will eat be interpreted as a prohibition against eating? And regarding *lo shevuah lo ochel lecha*, even if one reverses the double negative, one arrives at *shevuah she-ochel lecha* — and the same question recurs.

The *gemara* immediately seizes on this: *miklal d'ha shevuah she-ochel lecha — de-lo achilna mashma?* Are we really saying that a *shevuah* formulated as "I will eat" means one may not eat? The *gemara* cites the well-known *mishnah* in *Shevuot* — *shevuot shtayim she-hein arba* — where the four categories of *shevuah* include both *she-ochel* ("I will eat") and *she-lo ochel* ("I will not eat") as distinct and opposite types. Clearly, saying *she-ochel* in that context means one is obligating oneself to eat — not forbidding it. So how can our *mishnah* treat *shevuah she-ochel lecha* as a prohibition?

### Abaye Context

Abaye resolves this by appealing to context. The words *she-ochel* carry two potential meanings depending on the situation in which they are uttered. If people were pressing someone to eat — insisting again and again — and he finally relented and said *shevuah she-ochel* ("I swear I will eat"), then indeed *de-achilna mashma* — it means he is committing to eat. But if the person had been refusing — *lo achilna, lo achilna* — "I'm not eating, I'm not eating" — and then declared *shevuah she-ochel lecha*, the phrase functions differently. In that context, *she-ochel* is understood as a conditional: "Should I eat of yours, let me have violated a *shevuah*" — meaning, were I to eat, I would be transgressing. This is a refusal reinforced by oath, not a commitment to eat. The *she-ochel*, in context, means *de-lo achilna*.

The default meaning, as established by the *mishnah* in *Shevuot*, is that *she-ochel* means one will eat. Context of refusal can reverse this, but the reversal is entirely context-dependent.

### Rav Ashi Text

Rav Ashi takes a different approach entirely. Rather than appealing to contextual interpretation, he proposes an emended reading of the *mishnah*: the *mishnah* does not actually say *she-ochel* but rather *she-ei ochel* — with a prolonged *alef*, signifying "that I do not eat." This would make the *mishnah*'s ruling entirely straightforward — *shevuah she-ei ochel* is transparently prohibitory — without requiring any appeal to context.

The *gemara* then asks: if that is what the *mishnah* means, what is the *chiddush*? The answer is that one might have thought: perhaps someone who says *she-ei ochel* has simply mispronounced *she-ochel* — slipping up — and should be understood as intending to eat. The *gemara* therefore teaches that we do not reinterpret the words on that basis; the unusual phrasing *she-ei ochel* is taken at face value and treated as binding.

### Abaye Rejects

However, Abaye does not accept Rav Ashi's approach. The *gemara* notes, in strikingly unusual language — *Abaye lo amar ta'am k'Rav Ashi* — that Abaye distanced himself from Rav Ashi's reading. The reason is that Rav Ashi is changing the *girsa* of the *mishnah*: the *mishnah* as transmitted says *she-ochel*, and Rav Ashi emends it to *she-ei ochel*. Abaye refuses to alter the text.

Rav Ashi, in turn, does not say like Abaye — *nadi min ta'am d'Abaye* — because he rejects the entire framework of context-dependent meaning. His objection is principled: if *she-ochel* can mean "I will not eat" depending on context, then equally *she-lo ochel* could mean "I will eat" in the right circumstances. One could imagine a scenario where someone is rhetorically asked, "Should you not eat?" and responds *shevuah she-lo ochel* — meaning, were I not to eat I would have violated a *shevuah*, thus implying he must eat. If every formulation is context-dependent, nothing means anything with certainty, and the *mishnah* cannot be teaching reliable rules. The *mishnah* must therefore be taken to establish fixed, context-independent meanings: *she-ochel* means "I will eat" and *she-lo ochel* means "I will not eat." Abaye's contextual approach, however sensible in theory, cannot serve as the basis for a *mishnah* whose purpose is to teach definitive rules, since a teacher cannot transmit context along with a ruling.

### Tosafot Gezeira

One further difficulty with the *mishnah*'s ruling remains: why is *lo shevuah lo ochel lecha* binding? Even granting that one reverses the double negative to derive *shevuah she-ochel lecha*, we are back at the question of why that formulation is prohibited. Tosafot addresses this with a distinct argument: the binding force of *lo shevuah lo ochel lecha* rests not on logical inference but on a rabbinic *gezeira*. The concern is that a person might accidentally drop the first *lo*, transforming *lo shevuah lo ochel lecha* into *shevuah she-lo ochel* — a classically binding prohibitory oath. To prevent this slippage, the rabbis treated even the full *lo shevuah lo ochel lecha* as binding. This stands in contrast to the *neder* parallel — *lo korban lo ochel lecha* — where even if one drops the first *lo* and arrives at *korban lo ochel lecha*, that formulation is also non-binding. Hence there was no reason to decree stringency in the *neder* case, but there was such a reason in the *shevuah* case.

## Shevuah Stricter

### Zeh Chomer

The *mishnah* summarizes: *zeh chomer b'shevuot mib'nedarim* — this is the stringency of *shevuah* over *neder*. The cases just discussed — where the same linguistic formulations are binding when expressed as a *shevuah* but not when expressed as a *neder* — illustrate one dimension of this stringency. But there is also an earlier and more fundamental contrast. When the *mishnah* earlier taught that *shevuah she'aini yashen, she'aini medaber, she'aini mehalech — assur*, this too reflects a stringency of *shevuah* over *neder* — not because a *neder* on such matters fails entirely (for *konam she'ani mehalech* is indeed binding), but because the *shevuah* is biblically binding, whereas the *neder* on abstract actions — on walking, speaking, sleeping, things that are not physical objects — is only rabbinically binding. Biblical versus rabbinic: that is the weight of the contrast in the earlier formulations.

## Neder Stricter

### Mitzva Objects

Having shown how a *shevuah* is stricter than a *neder* in certain respects, the *mishnah* now turns to the reverse: *v'chomer b'nedarim mib'shevuot*. The example given: *amar konam sukkah she'ani oseh, lulav she'ani notel, tfillin she'ani maniach* — one declares the *sukkah*, *lulav*, or *tfillin* as *konam* upon him, whether for building, taking, or wearing.

### Neder Works

By a *neder*, such a formulation is binding. The person has not directly contradicted his biblical obligation to sit in a *sukkah* or take a *lulav*. He has instead spoken about the status of the object — rendering it forbidden to himself. When *Sukkot* arrives and he cannot use the object, his failure to fulfill the *mitzvah* is a downstream consequence, not a direct countermanding of God's command.

### Shevuah Fails

By a *shevuah*, however, this does not work — *she'ein nishba'in la'avor al ha-mitzvot*. One cannot take a *shevuah* to transgress a *mitzvah*. A *shevuah* operates in the domain of direct personal obligation: one commits to do or refrain from doing something. To take a *shevuah* not to sit in a *sukkah* is to directly pit one's oath against God's command — operating in *cheftzei Shamayim*, in God's domain rather than one's own. Such a *shevuah* does not take effect and remains non-binding. The person may sit in the *sukkah* without any violation of the oath. However, the act of having taken such a *shevuah* is itself a transgression: it constitutes a *shevuat shav* — an oath taken in vain — for which one receives lashes. The *shevuah* was stillborn from the moment of its utterance, but the utterance itself was a biblical violation.

---

## Shevuah Stricter (II)

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

The *gemara* notes a possible ambiguity in the *mishnah*'s phrasing *zeh chomer b'shevuot mib'nedarim*. Does this refer to the cases just discussed regarding *korban* formulations, or to the earlier cases about sleeping and walking? The *gemara* concludes that it refers to the earlier section — *aseifa d'eidach baba katani* — the language about *shevuah she'aini yashen, she'aini medaber, she'aini mehalech*. In those cases, both a *shevuah* and a *neder* are binding, but the *shevuah* is weightier: it is a biblical prohibition, whereas the *neder* on abstract actions that are not physical objects is only rabbinic. That is the stringency: identical subject matter, but different levels of normative force.

## Cheftzei Shamayim

### Rav Gidel Source

The *gemara* now takes up the basis for the rule that one cannot take a *shevuah* to countermand a *mitzvah* obligation. Rav Kahana teaches in the name of Rav Gidel in the name of Rav; and Rav Tavyomi teaches the same in the name of Rav Gidel in the name of Shmuel. The question is: *minayin she'ein nishba'in la'avor al ha-mitzvot?* From where do we know this?

### Lo Yachel Devaro

The answer is *lo yachel devaro*. This verse applies both to *neder* and to *shevuah*, as the full *pasuk* reads: *ki yidor neder l'Hashem o hishava shevuah le'esor issar al nafsho lo yachel devaro* — whether he vows a *neder* to God or swears a *shevuah* to bind himself, he shall not desecrate his word. The prohibition of *lo yachel devaro* thus governs both instruments.

### Personal vs. Divine

The *gemara* derives from this verse: *devaro lo yachel aval meichalu hu l'cheftzei Shamayim* — regarding his own matters, he shall not violate his word; but regarding *cheftzei Shamayim* — God's matters — it may be violated, because it never took effect. The verse's application of *lo yachel* is to *devaro* — his word, in his domain. When a person attempts to make a binding commitment in God's domain, the commitment does not attach, and what was never binding can be set aside without transgression.

This distinction — *cheftzei* as a division between personal and divine domains — appears elsewhere in halachic literature as well. Regarding *Shabbat*, the verse *mimtzo cheftzecha v'daber davar* gives rise to the rule: *cheftzecha asurim ve-cheftzei Shamayim mutarim* — one's personal affairs are restricted on *Shabbat*, while matters of *cheftzei Shamayim* are permitted, which is why a *shul* may make announcements about communal Torah activities. Similarly, the principle of *osek b'mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah* flows from *beshivtecha b'veitecha uvelachtecha vaderech* — *beshivtecha didach uvelachtecha didach* — when you are in your own sphere of action, the obligations of *mitzvot* apply; but when already engaged in God's domain, the calculus shifts.

### Gemara Question

A sharp question then arises. Both *neder* and *shevuah* appear in the same *pasuk* under the same rubric of *lo yachel devaro*, and both are said *l'Hashem*: *ki yidor neder l'Hashem* and *o hishava shevuah l'Hashem*. If the *l'Hashem* of the *shevuah* signals that one cannot operate in God's domain and thereby removes the binding force of such a *shevuah*, why does the *l'Hashem* of the *neder* not produce the same result? Why is a *neder* on *mitzvah* objects binding while a *shevuah* on *mitzvah* acts is not?

---

## Abaye Distinction

### Benefit vs. Use

Abaye answers that the difference lies not in the wording of the verse but in the inherent structure of the two acts. The critical formulations are: *hanat sukkah alai* — the benefit of the *sukkah* is forbidden to me — versus *shevuah she-lo ehena min ha-sukkah* — a *shevuah* that I will not derive benefit from the *sukkah*. In the *neder* formulation, one is not directly forbidding the act God commanded; one is rendering the object's benefit forbidden to oneself. When *Sukkot* comes, one cannot execute the *mitzvah* because the object is unavailable in the relevant sense — but one has not directly said "I will not sit in a *sukkah*." The *shevuah*, by contrast, directly targets the act itself and places a personal oath against the fulfillment of a divine command. That is why it fails.

### Rava Objection

Rava accepts the logical structure of Abaye's distinction but objects to the specific articulation. *V'chi mitzvot lehanot nitnu?* — Are *mitzvot* given in order to derive benefit from them? Performing a *mitzvah* is not classified as *hana'ah* in halachic terms. Therefore, saying *hanat sukkah alai* — the benefit of the *sukkah* is forbidden to me — would not prevent one from sitting in the *sukkah*, since sitting in a *sukkah* to fulfill the *mitzvah* does not constitute *hana'ah*. The *neder* on *hana'ah* would be ineffective in preventing the *mitzvah* in those terms.

---

## Hana'ah Definition

### Mitzvot Framework

The principle at stake here is *mitzvot lav lehanot nitnu* — *mitzvot* are not given for the purpose of deriving pleasure. This is not a sweeping theological statement about our attitude toward the commandments; it is a halachic classification of what counts as *hana'ah*. Halachic *hana'ah* is defined in this-worldly, physical terms. Fulfilling a religious obligation — accumulating, as it were, merit or spiritual satisfaction — does not qualify as physical benefit for the purposes of *neder* law.

### Knowledge Examples

A parallel illustration: may a non-*kohen* study a cadaver for the purpose of medical and anatomical education? The *poskim* generally permit it, despite the general prohibition against deriving *hana'ah* from a corpse. The reason is that intellectual expansion — gaining knowledge — is not classified as *hana'ah* in the operative halachic sense, even though from a broader perspective it may represent the highest form of human fulfillment. Similarly, *mitzvah* performance is not *hana'ah* in halachic terms, however central it is to Jewish life. This has a further wrinkle in contexts like *Tisha B'Av*, where Torah study is prohibited — but there the reason is *simcha*, joy, not *hana'ah*, pleasure, and the distinction between the two is meaningful.

---

## Abaye Distinction (II)

### Rava Solution

Given that *mitzvot lav lehanot nitnu*, Rava reformulates the operative *neder* language. Instead of *hanat sukkah alai* — which would be ineffective because *mitzvah* performance is not *hana'ah* — one must say *yeshivat sukkah alai* — the sitting in this *sukkah* is forbidden to me. This targets not the benefit derived from the object but a specific use of it. A *neder* can render an object forbidden with respect to particular actions or uses, not only with respect to conventional *hana'ah*. This is an important expansion of the concept of *neder*: it can prohibit certain modes of engagement with an object even where no conventional physical benefit is involved.

---

## Object Mention

### Non-Benefit Actions

The Ran draws out an important implication of this principle. Since a *neder* can operate on actions rather than only on *hana'ah*, one can in principle make a *neder* on a completely non-benefit action — his example is *konam zerikat tzor le-yam alav* — declaring this rock as *konam* upon him with respect to throwing it into the ocean. Even though there is no *hana'ah* in throwing a pebble into the sea, the *neder* is binding: *af al pi she'ein lo bi-zerikah zo hana'ah, assur le-zorko*.

### Explicit Requirement

However, the Ran adds a critical qualification: this works only if one is explicit. *Davka bi-meforesh* — one must specify the forbidden action. If one says *konam sukkah alai le-yeshivatah*, or *konam zerikat tzor* with the explicit action named, it is binding on that specific use.

### Default Hana'ah

But if one simply says *konam sukkah alai* or *konam tzor alai* — a generic declaration without specifying the forbidden mode of engagement — the default operative meaning of *konam* is *assur b'hana'ah*. The person would be forbidden to derive conventional benefit from the object but would be permitted to throw the rock into the ocean or to sit in the *sukkah* for *mitzvah* purposes (to the extent that is itself problematic). The word *konam*, standing alone, defaults to forbidding *hana'ah*. If the intention is to forbid something other than *hana'ah*, it must be made explicit.

### Tosafot Object

Tosafot offers a different position on a related question. The Ran holds that saying *yeshivat sukkah alai* — "the sitting in a *sukkah* is upon me as forbidden" — without mentioning the *sukkah* as a concrete object might be only a rabbinic *neder*, since the thing being forbidden is a *davar she'ein bo mamash* — an action without physical substance. Tosafot disagrees. In their view, the *neder* becomes fully binding, and is not classified as a *davar she'ein bo mamash*, as long as one mentions the name of some object in the formulation. Since *yeshivat sukkah alai* includes the word *sukkah*, even though the explicit subject is the act of sitting rather than the object itself, the mention of the object is sufficient to give the *neder* the status of one bearing on a physical thing, and it is therefore binding at the biblical level.
